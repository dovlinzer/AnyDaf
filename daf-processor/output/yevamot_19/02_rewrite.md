# Yevamot 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eishes Achiv Intro

### Opening & Zika Review

The previous discussion centered on the concept of *zika* — whether it creates a genuine prohibition extending to a woman's relatives, and whether it persists even after *misa*. With that analysis as backdrop, we now turn to the next *mishna*, which brings us back to the central theme of *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*. It will come as no surprise that the question of *zika* will resurface prominently here as well.

### Three Brothers Scenario

The *mishna* takes up the case of three brothers: Reuven, Shimon, and Levi. Reuven is married to Rachel. Reuven dies, and then Levi is born. The critical variable the *mishna* wishes to examine is the precise timing of Shimon's *yibum* with Rachel: did it occur before Levi was born, or after?

### Yibum Timing Question

The significance of this question is as follows. In all that preceded, we assumed that Shimon performed *yibum* with Rachel after Levi was born. Under those circumstances, when Levi entered the world, he encountered Rachel already in the status of *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* — an *erva* to him. But if Shimon performed *yibum* with Rachel before Levi was ever born, then by the time Levi came into existence, perhaps her prior identity as Reuven's wife is simply irrelevant. Perhaps Rachel relates to Levi only as Shimon's wife, her earlier history having been entirely superseded. If so, when Shimon dies, it might be a full *mitzva* of *yibum* for Levi with Rachel. That is precisely the question our *mishna* addresses.

---

## Mishna Two Cases

### Reisha: Birth Before

The *mishna* opens: *shnei achim shemet echad meihem* — two brothers, Reuven and Shimon. Reuven dies. Then Levi is born. At that point, Rachel becomes *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* in relation to Levi. And then, in the *reisha*, Shimon performs *yibum* with Rachel — the wedding occurring after Levi's birth. When Shimon subsequently dies, the *mishna* rules: *harishona yotzeis mishum eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*. It makes no difference that Rachel was already married to Shimon when Levi was born. She carries with her the *erva* status she held before the marriage, and that does not change for Levi. If Shimon also had a second wife, *hashniya hareizo tzaras eishes ach*, she would be exempt from *yibum* as a *tzaras erva*.

### Seifa: Yibum Before

The *seifa* reverses the sequence. *Veyibam hasheini es eishes achiv ve'achar kach nolad lahem ach* — Shimon performs *yibum* with Rachel, and only afterward is Levi born. Now when Levi comes into the world, Rachel is already Shimon's wife. Her prior connection to Reuven is, as it were, past history. The *tanna kamma* nonetheless holds that this does not matter — she remains *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* even in this case, and Levi may not perform *yibum* with her. The *mishna* also addresses the scenario of *maamar* without *yibum*: if Shimon performed *maamar* with Rachel but did not complete *yibum* before dying, then Rachel is not truly Shimon's wife *de'oraisa*, though she is treated as such *derabbanan*. In that case, Levi certainly does not perform *yibum* with Rachel — she is *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* — but the *co-wife*, who is the only genuine wife *de'oraisa*, neither falls away without anything nor is permitted for *yibum*: *hashniya choletzes velo misyabemes*, she performs *chalitza* but does not enter *yibum*.

### Tanna Kamma v Shimon

Rabbi Shimon, however, disputes the *tanna kamma*'s position in the first case — the *reisha* without *maamar*. *Rabbi Shimon omer miyabem le'eizo meihen sheyirtzeh* — he may perform *yibum* with whichever of the women he wishes, whether with Rachel or with the co-wife. Alternatively, *o cholets le'eizo meihen sheyirtzeh* — he performs *chalitza* with whichever he chooses. According to Rabbi Shimon, the relevant fact is that by the time Levi was born, Rachel was already Shimon's wife. Her status as *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* has been transformed; it no longer obtains, and when Shimon dies she is simply available for *yibum* like any other *yevama*. The dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon, then, turns on precisely this question: does the prior *erva* status persist through *yibum*, or does it dissolve once Levi enters a world in which Rachel is already married to his brother?

---

## Rav Oshaya Theory

### Superfluous Reisha

*Amar Rav Oshaya: cholek alav Rabbi Shimon af le-reisha.* Rav Oshaya now advances a striking claim: Rabbi Shimon's position that the *erva* status dissolves holds even in the *reisha* — even when Levi was born before Shimon performed *yibum* with Rachel. The structure of the *mishna* itself, Rav Oshaya argues, compels this reading.

His argument runs as follows. *Mimai? Mide'katani mishna yeseirah bava de-reisha* — the *reisha* is an entirely superfluous *mishna*. For whom was it taught? *Eleima le-rabbanan* — if it was taught for the *rabbanan*, then consider: in the *seifa*, the case where *yibum* had already occurred and Levi was born afterward — *deki ashkach bah be-heiteira ashkach* — Levi found Rachel already in a state of permissibility, already Shimon's wife. If even in that case the *rabbanan* hold that Rachel is forbidden, *lo choletzes velo mityabemes*, then in the *reisha* where Levi was born first and *yibum* came afterward — *mibaya?* — is there even a question? Of course she is forbidden to him. The *chiddush* for the *rabbanan* lies entirely in the *seifa*. The *reisha* adds nothing according to the *rabbanan*.

### Reisha Showcases Shimon

It follows, Rav Oshaya argues, that the *reisha* must have been included exclusively to showcase Rabbi Shimon's position. *Ela natru le-rabbanan ad demesaymi le-milsaihu ve-hadar palig aleihu* — Rabbi Shimon allowed the *rabbanan* to complete their statement and then voiced his opposition. But his opposition is relevant even to the *reisha*. *Ve-tana reisha lehodeia kocho de-Rabbi Shimon* — the *reisha* reveals the full force of Rabbi Shimon's view, that even when Levi was born before *yibum*, Rachel becomes permitted once Shimon has married her. *Ve-tana seifa lehodeia kocho de-rabbanan* — the *seifa* reveals the full force of the *rabbanan*'s view, that even when Levi was born after *yibum*, Rachel remains forbidden.

### Board Diagram

To make this visually clear, consider the two scenarios side by side.

In the *reisha* (scenario one): First, Reuven is married to Rachel, with Shimon present. Second, Reuven dies. Third, Levi is born — at this point, Rachel is an *erva* in relation to Levi. Fourth, Shimon marries Rachel. Even at step four, Levi has attended the wedding, as it were, but Rachel's *erva* status vis-à-vis Levi has already been established.

In the *seifa* (scenario two): The same opening, but the order reverses. After Reuven dies, Shimon performs *yibum* with Rachel. Only then is Levi born. When Levi enters the world, Rachel is already Shimon's wife. The question is whether Levi perceives Rachel as *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* or simply as his brother's wife.

### Rachel's Status Change

The question now becomes: what is the mechanism by which Rachel's status could be said to change? The intuitive answer — and the one most naturally read into the *mishna* — is that Shimon's *yibum* with Rachel transforms her status. She begins as *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*, but once *yibum* occurs, that status is superseded. Levi subsequently enters a world in which Rachel is straightforwardly Shimon's wife.

However, what Rav Oshaya is actually claiming — as the *gemara* will develop — is subtler. It is not the act of *yibum* that transforms Rachel's status for Levi. Rather, it is the *zika* that already exists between Rachel and Shimon from the moment of Reuven's death. Even before *yibum* is completed, and even before Levi is born, Rachel stands in a relationship with Shimon by virtue of *zika*. The *gemara*'s position will therefore be: when Levi is born, Rachel is not encountered as *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* because she is already, at the moment of Levi's birth, in a relationship with Shimon through *zika*. She was, in a sense, never unattached from a brother's household when Levi arrived. That is the mechanism behind Rav Oshaya's reading of Rabbi Shimon.

It is important to note, however, that Levi is born before *yibum* has taken place and before Rachel is formally Shimon's wife. So you might have said: yes, at stage three Rachel is technically *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*, but the *yibum* that follows transforms that status. But the *gemara* resists that formulation. Instead, it insists that no transformation occurs — because from the moment of Reuven's death, *zika* already binds Rachel to Shimon in a meaningful way, and so when Levi is born, the *zika* is already operative, and Rachel is already, in that sense, in Shimon's domain.

## Zika Kkenussa Damya

### Gemara's Question

*Ella eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo le-Rabbi Shimon heichi mashkachas lah?* The *gemara* now presses: if Rav Oshaya's reading of Rabbi Shimon is correct, when would one ever encounter a genuine case of *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* according to Rabbi Shimon? Even if Levi is born before *yibum*, and even if Rachel appears to be *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* at that moment, Rabbi Shimon says she is permitted once Shimon's *zika* has taken hold. So in any scenario with three brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — where Reuven dies and his *zika* passes to Shimon, Rachel is already bound to Shimon by *zika* from the moment of Reuven's death. When Levi is then born, Rachel encounters him as effectively Shimon's wife, not as Reuven's widow. There would seem to be no room for *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* at all.

The *gemara* clarifies: *bishlama yibem ve'achar kach nolad* — the *seifa* is intelligible, because *ki asya be-heitera asya*, Levi found Rachel in a state of permissibility, already in a recognized relationship with Shimon. But in the *reisha*, if Levi was born first and *yibum* afterward, *mai ta'ama?* — how is Rachel not an *erva* to Levi? The *gemara* answers: *kasavar yesh zika ve-zika ke-knusa damya*. Rabbi Shimon holds two propositions: first, *yesh zika* — *zika* is a real and operative phenomenon. Second, and crucially, *zika ke-knusa damya* — the *zika* is treated as though the *yibum* has already been consummated. *Knusa* evokes the language of formal entry into marriage, of *nisuin*. So not only is there *zika*, but the *zika* is powerful enough that Rachel is regarded as already fully in Shimon's household. Therefore, when Levi is born, she is not *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* — she is *eishes Shimon*, and when Shimon subsequently dies, the *mitzva* of *yibum* applies in full.

### Two Brothers No Yibum

The *gemara* provides two scenarios in which *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* could still arise according to Rabbi Shimon. First: *be-chad acha u-meis ve-nolad lo ach* — there is only one brother. Reuven dies and then Levi is born. There is no Shimon in the picture, no *zika* to anyone, and Rachel is simply *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* to Levi. Second: *inami be-trei ve-lo yibem ve-lo meis* — Shimon is present but has neither performed *yibum* nor died. As long as Shimon has not acted — has not completed *yibum* — his *zika* to Rachel has not yet transformed her status in the decisive way, and were Levi to be intimate with Rachel, it would constitute *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*. It is specifically Shimon's *yibum* — or, under Rav Oshaya's expanded reading, the operative *zika* itself — that achieves the transformation. Without Shimon acting at all, the transformation does not occur.

---

## Zika Kkenussa Damya (II)

### Zika Definition

The *gemara* now articulates the underlying principle with precision. *Kasavar yesh zika ve-zika ke-knusa damya* — Rabbi Shimon holds that *zika* exists as a genuine halachic phenomenon and, further, that *zika ke-knusa damya*, the *zika* renders the woman as though she has already entered into marriage with the prospective *yavam*. This is not a rabbinic enactment but a characterization of the fundamental nature of *zika*: it is not merely a legal obligation pending action, but a quasi-marital bond powerful enough to redefine the woman's status. When Levi is born, Rachel is therefore already, by virtue of the *zika*, in Shimon's domain — and there is no basis for the *issur* of *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* to take hold.

## Rav Yosef Challenge

### Rav Yosef Objects

*Masiv lei Rav Yosef.* Rav Yosef challenges the claim that Rabbi Shimon holds *zika ke-knusa damya* by demonstrating that Rabbi Shimon himself is uncertain about this. *Hashta zika u-ma'amar mispeka lei le-Rabbi Shimon ei ke-knusa damya ei lo ke-knusa damya* — Rav Yosef shows that Rabbi Shimon has a genuine *safek* whether the combination of *zika* and *maamar* is sufficient to render the woman as *ke-knusa*. If even *zika plus maamar* leaves Rabbi Shimon in doubt, then *zika le-chuda mibaya?* — *zika* alone is certainly not something Rabbi Shimon would declare definitively equivalent to full *knusa*. Rav Yosef then presents the case that demonstrates this.

### Three Brothers Case

The scenario: *shlosha achim nesu'im shalosh nashim nochrivot* — three brothers married to three unrelated women. In our terms: Reuven is married to Rachel, Shimon is married to Sarah, and Levi is present. Reuven dies. Shimon now has a *zika* to Rachel. Shimon then performs *maamar* with Rachel but does not complete *yibum*. Shimon then dies.

At this point, Levi faces a dual situation with respect to Rachel. On one level, there is a *de'oraisa* *zika* to Rachel as Reuven's brother. On another level, there is a *derabbanan* quasi-marital bond to Rachel as a result of Shimon's *maamar*. Rachel therefore stands in relation to Levi with what appears to be a double *zika* — connected to him via two separate brothers simultaneously.

### Cannot Yibum Both

Can Levi perform *yibum* with both Rachel and Sarah? He cannot. *Dilma yesh zika ve-havei shtei yevamos ha-ba'os mi-beis echad* — perhaps the *zika* and *maamar* together render Rachel fully Shimon's wife, in which case Rachel and Sarah are both coming from the same brother's household, and one cannot perform *yibum* with two wives of a single brother. Therefore he cannot do *yibum* with both.

### Dual Zika Problem

Can he perform *yibum* with Rachel alone and release Sarah? He cannot do that either. *Dilma ein zika ke-knusa* — perhaps the *maamar* and *zika* do not render Rachel fully Shimon's wife, and she remains primarily Reuven's wife. In that case, Rachel and Sarah come from two separate brothers, and the act of *yibum* with one does not release the other. Sarah still requires her own *yibum* or *chalitza*. Therefore Levi must perform *chalitza* with both women. Though *de'oraisa* he has an independent obligation toward each woman separately, the dual-status problem with Rachel prevents him from acting on either obligation through *yibum*, and the result is *chalitza* with both. The *pasuk* is invoked: *mi she-yesh lah zikas yavam echad* — a woman may be taken in *yibum* when she is bound to only one *yavam*, *ve-lo she-yesh lah zikas shnei yevamim* — not when she is bound to two *yavamin* simultaneously.

### Shimon Denies Dual

Rabbi Shimon, however, does not accept the premise of a *double zika* problem. *Meyabem le-eizo meihen she-yirtzeh ve-cholets la-shniya* — he performs *yibum* with whichever of the two women he chooses and *chalitza* with the other. Rabbi Shimon denies that Rachel possesses a genuinely dual status. But he does not therefore say one may perform *yibum* with both women, for a different reason: *dilma yesh zika*, and the combination of *zika* and *maamar* may render Rachel fully Shimon's wife, in which case she and Sarah are both from the same household. Therefore he cannot do *yibum* with both. But he can do *yibum* with one and *chalitza* with the other, because in his view Rachel does not have a double status — she has a *doubtful* status.

## Safek Status Analysis

### Abaye: One vs Two

*Amar lei Abaye: ve-lo shani lach bein zikas yavam echad le-zikas shnei yevamim?* Abaye attempts to preserve Rav Oshaya's framework by drawing a distinction. Perhaps Rabbi Shimon's *safek* about whether *zika ke-knusa damya* applies only when two *yevamin* are simultaneously in the picture — when Rachel has a *zika* to both Shimon and Levi at once, making it unclear to whom the *zika* primarily attaches. In that scenario of competing *zikot*, one cannot say with certainty that the *zika* has rendered her any particular man's wife. But *ki amar Rabbi Shimon zika ke-knusa damya*, Abaye suggests, that is in the case *be-yavam echad* — when there is only one *yavam*, and the *zika* is clear and unambiguous. In that case, the *zika* alone suffices to render her *ke-knusa*, fully as though she has entered the *yavam*'s household.

This would preserve Rav Oshaya's position: in our *mishna*, when Levi has not yet been born, the *zika* is exclusively between Rachel and Shimon. There is only one *yavam* in the picture. In that context, *zika ke-knusa damya* operates cleanly, and when Levi is then born, Rachel is already fully regarded as Shimon's wife. The *safek* that Rabbi Shimon evinces in the three-brother case is a product of the dual *zika* problem, not of any inherent weakness in *zika* itself.

### Zika Maamar Strength

The *gemara* presses further. Perhaps one might argue that Rabbi Shimon indeed holds *zika ke-knusa damya* in a thoroughgoing *de'oraisa* sense — that Rachel is fully considered Shimon's wife *de'oraisa* — and the reason Levi must perform *chalitza* with Sarah is only a *derabbanan* enactment. *Mide'oraisa*, Levi could simply do *yibum* with Rachel and Sarah could be released without anything. But *miderabbanan hu de'asur*, the rabbis required *chalitza* with Sarah as well — *gezeira mishum she-yomru shtei yevamos ha-ba'os mi-shnei batim chada miyabema ve-idach mifatra be-la klum* — out of concern that people will say: if you have two *yevamot* from two different brothers, you need only perform *yibum* with one and may simply ignore the other. To avoid this appearance, the *rabbanan* imposed the requirement of *chalitza* with Sarah. Under this reading, Rabbi Shimon does hold *zika ke-knusa damya de'oraisa*, and the *chalitza* with Sarah is only a rabbinic safeguard against a misleading impression.

### Safek vs Dual Status

The *gemara* rejects this construction and establishes the correct understanding of Rabbi Shimon's position. *Ta'amei de-Rabbi Shimon mishum ma'amar ve-lav ma'amar hu* — the mechanism at work for Rabbi Shimon is genuinely whether the *maamar* constitutes a real *maamar* or not. It is a *safek*, a true legal doubt, not a *gezeira*. The proof comes from a *baraita* in which Rabbi Shimon articulates his reasoning directly: *amar lahem Rabbi Shimon la-chachamim, im ma'amaro shel sheini ma'amar, eishet sheini hu bo'el* — if Shimon's *maamar* with Rachel is legally operative, then Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel as Shimon's wife. *Ve-im ma'amaro shel sheini eino ma'amar, eishet rishon hu bo'el* — if the *maamar* is not operative, then Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel as Reuven's wife. Either way, Levi may perform *yibum* with Rachel. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold that Rachel's status is genuinely dual — she carries both relationships simultaneously — and therefore no *yibum* is possible with her at all. Rabbi Shimon denies the dual status: for him, Rachel is either Reuven's wife or Shimon's wife, and the *safek* is which of the two she is. It is therefore clear from Rabbi Shimon's own formulation that he harbors a genuine *safek* about Rachel's status, even when *zika* and *maamar* are both present. This confirms Rav Yosef's challenge: if *zika plus maamar* leaves Rabbi Shimon in doubt, how could *zika alone* definitively make Rachel *ke-knusa* and resolve the problem for Levi?

## Baraita Evidence

### Birth Before Nisuin

*Kulah amar Rabbi Shimon: kol she-ha-leida kodemes le-nisuin, lo choletzes ve-lo mityabemes.* Rabbi Shimon articulates a general principle: whenever the birth of Levi precedes the *nisuin* — here, the *yibum* — Rachel requires neither *chalitza* nor *yibum*. She is forbidden to Levi as *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* and simply exits without any act.

### Nisuin Before Birth

Conversely, *nisuin kodmin le-leida, o choletzes o mityabemes* — if first there was *yibum* with Rachel and only then Levi was born, then Levi has a full obligation toward Rachel: either *chalitza* or *yibum*, as in a standard case. This *baraita* appears to state Rabbi Shimon's position with crystalline clarity: the only variable that matters is whether Levi was born before or after *yibum*. If before, Rachel is forbidden; if after, she is fully permitted.

### Two Brothers Problem

The *gemara* now asks: *mai lav be-yavam echad?* — presumably this *baraita* is speaking of a case with only one surviving brother (Shimon) at the time of Reuven's death, and Levi is then born afterward. If so, and Rabbi Shimon declares Rachel forbidden when Levi is born before *yibum*, this directly contradicts Rav Oshaya's claim that Rabbi Shimon would permit Rachel even in that *reisha* scenario on the basis of *zika ke-knusa damya*. One might have thought that *zika* alone — with only one *yavam* in the picture — suffices to render Rachel as Shimon's wife. Yet this *baraita* suggests Rabbi Shimon makes no such ruling.

### Two Brothers Resolution

The *gemara* attempts to salvage Rav Oshaya's framework: *lo, bi-shnei yevamin* — the *baraita* is not speaking of a scenario with only Shimon present before Levi's birth. Rather, it assumes two brothers (say, Shimon and Issachar) were both present when Reuven died. Because two *yevamin* simultaneously hold a *zika* to Rachel, the *zika* is ambiguous and cannot render Rachel clearly bound to either one. That is why Rachel remains a problem even according to Rabbi Shimon. But if only Shimon were present, his *zika* would operate unambiguously and Rachel would be *ke-knusa*.

### Birth Timing Only

The *gemara* ultimately finds this reading untenable. If the *baraita*'s point were really about the number of *yevamin*, it would have said so explicitly. Moreover, the *baraita* opens *klal amar Rabbi Shimon* — Rabbi Shimon stated a general principle. A *klal* should encompass all cases cleanly. The *klal* Rabbi Shimon announces is: birth before *nisuin* means forbidden, *nisuin* before birth means obligated. That is the only variable. Had Rabbi Shimon's ruling depended on whether one or two brothers were present, the *baraita* would have specified this condition. Its silence strongly indicates that the sole determinant is the timing of Levi's birth relative to *yibum*, regardless of how many brothers were in the picture. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the attempt to read Rav Oshaya's expanded position into Rabbi Shimon through this *baraita* has failed.

## Competing Wives

### Setup Two Sisters

A new *baraita* presents a distinct but related complication. *Shlosha achim: shnayim meihem nesu'im shtei achayot, o isha u-vita, o isha u-vat bita, o isha u-vat bina* — three brothers, two of whom are each married to one of two women who stand in a relationship of *ervah* to each other: sisters, a mother and her daughter, a woman and her granddaughter, and so on. These are women who cannot themselves be married to the same man simultaneously. The two brothers then die.

### Cannot Yibum Either

Levi now faces both women as *yevamot*. The difficulty is that he cannot simply perform *yibum* with one and release the other. If he has a *zika* to both, and then consummates with one of them, he has thereby become intimate with *achot zekukato* — the sister of a woman to whom he is legally bound — or with *em zekukato* or *bat zekukato*, depending on the specific relationship. This renders the consummation itself an act with a relative, even though he has not yet performed *yibum* with the second woman. The *zika* to the second creates the problem.

### Chalitza Both

The *tanna kamma* therefore rules: *harei eilu choltzos ve-lo mityabemos* — both women perform *chalitza* and neither enters *yibum*. Both are released through *chalitza*, one from each brother's household.

### Shimon: Both Exempt

*Rabbi Shimon poter* — Rabbi Shimon exempts. His position is that when the two women are in a situation of mutual competition — *tzarot zo la-zo* — neither may be taken at all, and both are exempt from both *chalitza* and *yibum*. The *baraita* that later explains this derives it from the verse *ve-isha el achotah lo tikach li-tzror* — do not take a woman alongside her sister to create rivalry. *Be-sha'ah she-na'asu tzarot zo la-zo, lo yihiyu lekuchin afilu be-achat meihen* — at the moment when the two women stand as competing claimants, none may be taken, even one of them. This is Rabbi Shimon's principle: when the situation itself is one of competing *yevamot* who cannot both be taken, the entire *yibum* structure falls away and both women exit without any act.

### Rav Amram Reading

*Amar Rav Amram: mai poter nami, poter bi-shniya* — Rav Amram proposes a narrower reading of *Rabbi Shimon poter*. Rather than exempting both women entirely, Rabbi Shimon exempts only the second wife. Under this reading, Levi performs *yibum* with the first wife — the wife of whichever brother died first — and the second wife is simply released.

## Rava Reinterpretation

### Rava Multiple Pairs

*Amar Rava: shniya she-be-zug zeh ve-ha-shniya she-be-zug zeh* — Rava offers a strikingly different reading of the *baraita*'s phrase *Rabbi Shimon poter bi-shteihen*. He proposes that the two brothers are not each married to one of a single problematic pair, but rather each is married to multiple pairs of wives. So Reuven and Shimon are simultaneously married to two sisters *and* to a mother and daughter *and* to further problematic combinations enumerated in the *baraita*. The phrase *be-zug zeh* refers to the second member of each pair, and Rabbi Shimon's exemption applies to the second woman from each pairing. Under this reading, *shteihen* — both — refers to both of the second wives, one from each of the two problematic pairs.

### Or Not And

*Katai Rava* — the *gemara* immediately rejects this. *Chada, de'o o katani* — the *baraita* uses "or, or, or": *sisters, or mother and daughter, or mother and granddaughter*. It is plainly a list of alternatives, not a cumulative conjunction. The two brothers are married to one of these problematic pairs, not all of them simultaneously.

### Should Say Four

A further objection reinforces this rejection: if Rava were correct that the brothers are married to multiple pairs — say, to two sisters *and* to a mother and daughter — then when Rabbi Shimon exempts *both*, he should have said all four women are exempt, not merely *shteihen*, two. The *baraita*'s language of exempting *two* women only makes sense if there are two women in the scenario, not four.

## Tzarot Principle

### Tzarot Cannot Be Taken

*Ve-od tanya: Rabbi Shimon poter bi-shteihen min ha-chalitza u-min ha-yibum, she-ne'emar, ve-isha el achotah lo tikach li-tzror.* The additional *baraita* makes explicit that Rabbi Shimon exempts both women from both *chalitza* and *yibum*, deriving this from the verse prohibiting taking a woman alongside her sister to create rivalry. *Be-sha'ah she-na'asu tzarot zo la-zo, lo yihiyu lekuchin afilu be-achat meihen* — at the moment when the women become competitors, the entire framework of acquisition — whether through *yibum* or even *chalitza* — does not apply to either of them.

### Rav Ashi: Simultaneous

*Ella amar Rav Ashi: i de-naful be-zeh achar zeh, hachi nami* — if Reuven died and then Shimon died sequentially, Rav Ashi concedes, one could indeed argue that Levi should follow the logic of *zika ke-knusa damya* and simply perform *yibum* with the first wife, releasing the second. *Hacha be-mai askinan de-naful be-vat achat* — the *baraita* is dealing with the case where both brothers died simultaneously, at exactly the same moment.

### Like Yossi Glili

*Rabbi Shimon savar lah ke-Rabbi Yossi ha-Galili de-amar efshar le-tzamtzem* — Rabbi Shimon holds in accordance with the position of Rabbi Yossi ha-Galili, who maintains that truly simultaneous events are halachically possible and must be reckoned with. When both brothers die at the exact same moment, the question of which *zika* "came first" is literally unanswerable, and the competing status of the two women crystallizes instantaneously. It is in that uniquely simultaneous scenario that Rabbi Shimon applies the *tzarot* principle and exempts both women entirely.

### Sequential Death

When the deaths are sequential — one brother dying before the other — Rabbi Shimon would follow the logic of the first wife. The *zika* to the first wife is established first; if *zika ke-knusa damya*, she is already as though bound to Levi from that moment. When the second brother then dies, his wife encounters Levi in a status in which the first wife's *zika* already effectively occupies his household, rendering the second wife an *achot zekukato* or its equivalent. The *tzarot* exemption, with its simultaneous-death premise, is therefore not in tension with Rabbi Shimon's holding of *zika ke-knusa damya* in the sequential case.

## Rav Papa Conclusion

### Yibum Then Birth

*Rav Papa amar: be-yibum ve-achar kach nolad, palig Rabbi Shimon* — Rav Papa states clearly where Rabbi Shimon's dispute is actually located. Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the *tanna kamma* only in the case where *yibum* occurred first and Levi was born afterward — the *seifa*. In that case, Rabbi Shimon holds that Rachel is no longer *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* by the time Levi is born, and *yibum* or *chalitza* applies normally.

### Birth Then Yibum

*Be-nolad ve-achar kach yibem, lo palig* — in the case where Levi was born first and *yibum* occurred afterward, Rabbi Shimon does not disagree. He concedes that Rachel is forbidden to Levi as *eishes achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*. This is precisely what the *baraita*'s *klal* stated, and it is the straightforward *peshat* of the *mishna*.

### Rejecting Rav Oshaya

With Rav Papa's formulation, the *gemara* definitively sets aside Rav Oshaya's elaborate reading. Rav Oshaya had argued that the *reisha* was taught solely to showcase Rabbi Shimon's position, implying that Rabbi Shimon would permit Rachel even when Levi was born before *yibum* — on the basis of *zika ke-knusa damya*. This argument, while initially clever, cannot survive the *baraita*'s explicit statement that Rabbi Shimon's own *klal* turns entirely on the timing of birth relative to *nisuin*, with no special claim about *zika* overriding the *erva* status when Levi is born first. Rav Papa's *peshat* is the accepted reading: Rabbi Shimon's singular *chiddush* is the *seifa*, where *yibum* predates Levi's birth, and in that case alone does he say Rachel is permitted.
