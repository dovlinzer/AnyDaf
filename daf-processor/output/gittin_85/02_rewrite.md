# Gittin 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reregiving & Framing

### Reregiving Question

The daf opens in the middle of 84b, with the *gemara* asking *keitzad ya'aseh* — what should a man do if he gave his wife a *get* with a *chutz* clause, declaring her permitted to all men except Reuven? Assuming that such a *get* is invalid, as the rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer hold, what is the remedy? The *gemara* answers: *yitlena heimena* — he takes it back from her and gives it again, this time simply declaring *harei at muteret l'chol adam*, without any exclusion.

### Rebbe vs. R. Shimon

The *gemara* then asks: who is the *tanna* behind this requirement to physically redeliver the *get*, rather than simply reframing the original giving with a new verbal declaration? Chizkiya answers that this position belongs to Rabbi Shimon Elazar, as taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon Elazar holds that one must take the *get* back and give it anew, declaring *ha'geiteich*. This connects to a prior dispute: if a man gives a document to a woman framing it as a promissory note (*shtar chov*), and she opens it to discover it is a *get*, the *chachamim* hold that he may simply declare *ha'geiteich* while it remains in her hand, whereas Rabbi Shimon Elazar requires that he take it back and redeliver it. The *chutz* case, where he must redeliver and declare *harei at muteret l'chol adam* without exclusion, seems to align with Rabbi Shimon Elazar's more demanding position.

Rabbi Yochanan, however, says: *afilu teima Rebbi* — one can maintain that even Rebbi, who disagrees with Rabbi Shimon Elazar, would require regiving here. How so?

### Rav Kahana's Framework

Rabbi Yochanan invokes the position of Rav Kahana — referred to here as "that one of yours" (*dichon amar*), reflecting Rav Kahana's origins in Bavel when Rabbi Yochanan addressed his Eretz Yisrael audience — who offers a principled distinction. In the *chutz* case, the woman has already acquired a degree of *pesul* for *kehunah* even from the invalid giving, on account of *rei'ach haget*. This is the crux of the conceptual framework: was the original act of giving *not a giving of a get at all*, or was it *a giving of a get, but a bad one*?

### Rei'ach Haget

This distinction carries significant analytical weight. If the original giving was not a giving of a *get* at all — as in the *shtar chov* case, where the husband explicitly framed the document as something entirely different — then the act of giving a *get* was never performed. In that circumstance, a subsequent declaration of *ha'geiteich* may suffice to frame the holding of the document as an act of giving a *get*, without requiring a new physical delivery. But if the original giving *was* an act of giving a *get*, albeit a defective one — as in the *chutz* case, where the husband said *harei at muteret l'chol adam chutz mi'Reuven* — then the act of giving was already performed, and performed badly. To correct it, one cannot merely reframe; the entire act must be redone from the start.

This framework also maps onto the question of *rei'ach haget*: where the original giving was not a giving of a *get* at all, there is no *rei'ach haget* and no residual *pesul* for *kehunah*. Where it was a defective giving of a *get*, the *rei'ach haget* takes effect. The two phenomena — whether a restatement suffices, and whether *rei'ach haget* applies — are expressions of the same underlying question about the nature of the original act.

One might consider that the opposite conclusion would be equally defensible: if it was not a giving of a *get* at all, one should have to redo everything from scratch; whereas if it was a bad giving, perhaps one can simply correct it. The *gemara* runs the other way: a bad giving, precisely because it was an act of giving a *get*, cannot be patched over by words alone — the act must be re-executed. This is the position of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rav Kahana, and it provides a coherent account of why even Rebbi would require redelivery in the *chutz* case, while remaining consistent with Rebbi's more lenient stance in the *shtar chov* case.

## Erasing Conditions

### Chutz Cannot Be Erased

The *gemara* turns to a related problem: if the *chutz* exclusion was written inside the body of the *get*, erasing it does not remedy the defect. Even if the text is subsequently corrected and no trace of the *chutz* remains, the *get* remains invalid. The reason is that the *get* was never written as a valid instrument of divorce — at the moment of its composition, it lacked the *da'as gerushin* necessary to function as a *get krisus*. No later correction to the text can retroactively supply what was absent at the time of writing.

### Tosafot on Erasures

*Tosafot* uses this ruling as a springboard to clarify the general rule regarding erasures in a *get*. The implication of the *gemara* — *davka chutz* — is that the prohibition on erasure applies specifically to the case of *chutz*, because *chutz* fundamentally compromises the *krisus* of the *get* and cannot be undone. For other erasures, however, correction is in principle valid, provided procedural requirements are met. The reason we are not concerned that an ordinary erasure represents a tampering with the document is that such corrections can be properly authenticated.

### Before Sharir V'Kayam

*Tosafot* specifies the mechanism: the correction must be documented before the words *sharir v'kayam*, which serve as the closing formula of the document. Any alteration to the text — whether in a *get* or a *ketubah* — must be noted within the body of the document, ahead of the final *hakol sharir v'kayam*, so that the attesting witnesses can validate the correction. For example, if a name was written incorrectly and then changed, a notation such as "in line two, there is an erasure" would be written before *sharir v'kayam*, and the *edim* would sign with awareness of that correction. Alternatively, if the notation appears separately, the *edim* must themselves authenticate what was written there.

*Tosafot* acknowledges the practical concern: without such safeguards, there would be no barrier to altering a document after the fact — changing a sum, a name, or any other material detail. The requirement that corrections precede *sharir v'kayam* or be independently attested by *edim* is precisely what prevents such abuse. *Tosafot* further notes that while erasure is technically permissible in a *get* under these conditions, the practice (*nahagu ha'olam*) is to be stringent, and in practice a *get* with an erasure would ordinarily be rewritten. *Ketubot*, by contrast, are not treated with the same stringency.

The important caveat, however, remains: none of this applies to *chutz*. Where *chutz* was written into the *get*, erasing it does nothing, because the defect is not in the text but in the fundamental nature of the instrument as written.

One might ask: if it is the signatures of the *edim* that give the *get* its legal force — whether one holds *edei mesirah karsei* or *edei chasimah karsei* — why can we not say that as long as the *chutz* is erased before the *edim* sign, their signature would then ratify a valid instrument? The *gemara*'s focus on the act of writing, and Tosafot's emphasis on the *sofer*'s commission from the *ba'al*, suggests that the writing itself — and the frame of mind and instruction under which it was composed — carries independent significance, at least according to the view that *edei mesirah karsei*, where even the writing must reflect the direct *tzivuy* of the husband. But the question is not entirely without force.

## Verbal vs. Written Chutz

### Rav Safra: Written Only

The *gemara* now addresses a fundamental question about the scope of the ruling. Rav Safra notes pointedly: *k'tavo b'tocho tnan* — "we taught: if it was written inside." His emphasis is deliberate: only if the *chutz* is physically written within the *get* does it compromise the instrument. One might think this is obvious — after all, that is precisely what the *mishna* says — but Rav Safra is forestalling an expansive reading.

### Objection: Before Toref

The *gemara* raises the objection: one might have thought that the rule *k'tavo b'tocho* — that writing *chutz* in the *get* invalidates it even after erasure — applies specifically when the *chutz* was written *after* the *toref*, the key operative line of the *get* (i.e., *harei at muteret l'chol adam* and its equivalent). In that case the core line was written without the taint of *chutz*, and the invalidation comes from what surrounds it. But if the *chutz* instruction was given *before* the *toref* was written — even if only verbally, communicated to the *sofer* before he composed the central line — perhaps even that verbal framing would invalidate the *get* as an instrument, since the *toref* itself was written in the context of a *chutz* intent.

This is not a frivolous concern. If the husband instructed the *sofer* — "write *harei at muteret l'chol adam*, but understand that my intention is to divorce her *chutz mi'Reuven*" — and the *sofer* then wrote the *toref* under that directive, there is a reasonable argument that the *toref* was never written as an expression of *da'as gerushin*. The key operative line of the *get* would have been composed without the proper intent, even if nothing problematic appears in the text.

Rav Safra's point is: do not say this. A verbal instruction — even one given before the *toref* was written — does not affect the validity of the instrument. The *get* as an object is defined by what is written in it, not by what was said to the *sofer* in the course of its composition. If nothing invalid appears in the document, the document is valid.

### Rava: Before Toref

Rava disagrees sharply. He holds: *lo shanu ela l'achar hatoref* — the ruling that writing *chutz* inside the *get* invalidates it even after erasure applies only when the *chutz* was written *after* the *toref*. But if the *chutz* instruction was given *before* the *toref* was written, even a verbal instruction invalidates the *get*. In that case, the *toref* itself was composed without proper *da'as gerushin* — the husband's framing of the entire act was as a *chutz* divorce, and the *sofer* wrote the key line under that direction. The instrument is therefore invalid, even though the words *chutz mi'Reuven* never appear in the text.

This is a striking position, and at first blush it seems to contradict the plain sense of the *mishna*, which focuses on what is written. Rava's reading is admittedly interpretive, locating the *mishna*'s ruling in the context of *l'achar hatoref* rather than treating it as a general statement. This is not the simple *pshat* of the *mishna*, but the *gemara* accepts it as Rava's position.

### Silence the Husband

Rava's practical corollary follows from this: *d'amar lehu Rava l'hanei d'chasvei gitei* — Rava instructed the *soferim* who write *gittin*: keep the husband silent (*shatku shutku l'ba'al*) until the *toref* of the *get* has been written. If the husband is going to say something problematic before the *sofer* reaches the key line, he can retroactively compromise the validity of the *get* as an instrument. The practical solution is to keep the husband occupied and quiet — distracted from making any declarations or conditions — until the *toref* has been composed, at which point the core of the instrument is already established and any subsequent statements can be managed separately.

This teaching also opens onto the broader concern that follows: even instructions that are not technically *chutz* may be problematic if given before the *toref*. The reason Rava counsels general silence, rather than simply warning against *chutz* specifically, anticipates the discussion of *gezeirah atu chutz* — a protective decree extending the concern about *chutz* to all conditions stated before the *toref* is written.

## Conditions & Chutz

### Rebbe: No Erasure

A *baraita* is cited: *kol hatna'im poslin baget* — all conditions invalidate a *get*. This is the position of Rebbi. The *chachamim* respond differently.

### Chachamim: Chutz Only

The *chachamim* state: *kol shepasul al peh pasul bichtav* — anything that is problematic when stated orally is problematic when written, and anything that is not problematic orally is not problematic when written. The application: *chutz*, which invalidates the *get* when stated verbally at the moment of delivery, likewise invalidates it when written into the document. An *al mnat* condition — a standard conditional clause — which does not invalidate the *get* when stated verbally, does not invalidate it when written either.

Before the *gemara* explains how Rebbi is to be understood, it is worth noting that one might have read Rebbi's position as a principled claim about the *cheftza* of the *get* itself: a *get* that is written without reservation expresses an absolute *krisus* in its very text, whereas a *get* that incorporates a condition — even a standard *al mnat* — is itself a qualified instrument, and the instrument as an object no longer expresses absolute severance. That reading has genuine force as a *svara*. However, as *Tosafot* explains, this is not how the *gemara* actually understands the dispute.

*Tosafot* frames the issue as follows: the practical question is whether a condition *written* into a *get* and subsequently *erased* before delivery can be treated as though it never existed. The *chachamim* hold that only *chutz* is irredeemably problematic — *chutz* written into the *get* compromises the instrument fundamentally, and erasing it does not help. Any other condition, however, if written and then erased, simply goes away; it does not taint the instrument, because the *chachamim* hold that the equivalence between verbal and written applies symmetrically, and a standard condition stated verbally is not a problem. Rebbi, by contrast, extends the logic of *chutz* to all written conditions: just as erasing a written *chutz* does not help, so too erasing any other condition that was written into the *get* does not help. This is not because all conditions are intrinsically as severe as *chutz*, but because of a *gezeirah atu chutz* — a protective decree that, once anything has been written and erased, we do not permit, lest it lead to doing the same with *chutz*.

### R. Zeira: Before/After

Rabbi Zeira offers a further refinement. He holds that the debate between Rebbi and the *chachamim* applies specifically to the case of a condition written *before the toref* — the key operative line — and subsequently erased. In that zone, Rebbi is concerned: *gazminan al mnat atu chutz* — we fear that permitting the erasure of a standard condition will lead to erasing a *chutz* written before the *toref*, or will otherwise be too close to scenarios where verbal *chutz* before the *toref* also causes problems. The *chachamim* are not worried and permit the erasure of any non-*chutz* condition.

But after the *toref* — *divrei hakol kasher* — everyone agrees that a standard condition written after the key line and then erased is entirely valid. The whole debate, on Rabbi Zeira's reading, is confined to the pre-*toref* zone.

The *gemara* cites a teaching presented before Rabbi Avin before Rabbi Zeira: if a *get* was written conditionally (*al tnai*), it is invalid — *divrei hakol*. The *gemara* challenges this: surely there is a recorded dispute? After correction, the teaching is reformulated: *divrei hakol kasher* — everyone agrees it is valid. The context must be a condition written and erased *after* the *toref*, where Rabbi Zeira's framework places consensus. The *gemara* explains that the tradition bearer remembered that the ruling was *divrei hakol* on something, but may have confused whether it was *pasul* or *kasher* — *kasher b'pasul michelef* — whereas confusing *divrei hakol* with *Rebbi alone* is not the kind of mistake one makes.

### Rava on Toref

Rava disagrees with Rabbi Zeira's limitation. Rava holds that Rebbi's concern applies even *after* the *toref*: *de'Rebbi savar gazrinan atu lifnei hatoref* — Rebbi worries that permitting erased conditions after the *toref* will lead to doing the same before the *toref*, and so Rebbi prohibits erased written conditions throughout the *get*. The *chachamim* are untroubled by that slippery slope and allow erased conditions after the *toref*.

But before the *toref*, both Rava and Rabbi Zeira agree: *divrei hakol pasul*. The difference between them is only in how far back they locate the consensus zone. For Rabbi Zeira, before the *toref* is where the debate lies; after the *toref* all agree it is valid. For Rava, before the *toref* all agree it is invalid; after the *toref* is where the debate lies.

### Gezeirah Concern

The concluding summary of this entire passage identifies the layered structure of the analysis. At the core is *chutz*, which fundamentally compromises the identity of the *get* as an instrument of *gerushin* — whether stated verbally at the moment of giving or written into the document. Around that core sits a series of protective extensions. Rava's instruction to keep the husband silent before the *toref* is written reflects the concern that even a verbal *chutz* before the *toref* would invalidate the instrument. The debate between Rebbi and the *chachamim* about erasing conditions reflects the concern that permitting erased conditions will lead people to treat a written *chutz* as erasable. And the zone before the *toref* is treated with particular severity — *divrei hakol* — because that is where the *chutz* concern is most acute.

The two fundamental positions to keep distinct are those of Rav Safra and Rava. Rav Safra holds that verbal instructions to the *sofer*, even if framed in terms of *chutz*, do not affect the validity of the *get* as an instrument — only what is written inside matters. Rava holds that verbal instructions given before the *toref* was composed do affect the instrument, because the key line was written without proper *da'as gerushin*. Everything else in this section — the debates about erasing conditions, the *gezeirah atu chutz*, the scope of Rebbi's concern — is secondary to that foundational disagreement.

## Categories of Exclusions

### Relatives & Slaves

A new *mishna* opens with a series of cases in which a husband declares *harei at muteret l'chol adam* followed by an *ela* — "except for" — excluding specific categories of men. The first group of exclusions: except for my father, your father, my brother, your brother, a *eved*, a non-Jew (*ovdei kochavim*), and anyone with whom *kiddushin* would not be binding — all of these exclusions leave the *get* valid (*kasher*). The reason, which the *mishna* will clarify through its concluding general principle, is that none of these men are people she could have entered into a binding marriage with. Excluding them from the scope of the *get* therefore does not genuinely limit the practical effect of the *gerushin*. The *kiddushin* with relatives and with *ovdei kochavim* or a *eved* would not take effect even without the exclusion — the *get* is not withdrawing anything real.

### Kohanim & Gerusha

The *mishna* then presents the contrasting category: except for a *almanah* vis-à-vis a *Kohen Gadol*, a *gerushah* or *chalutzah* vis-à-vis a *Kohen hedyot*, a *mamzeret* or *netinah* vis-à-vis a Jew, or a *bas Yisrael* vis-à-vis a *mamzer* or *nasin* — in all of these cases, the *get* is invalid (*pasul*).

The key question is why these exclusions are different. In the case of the *eved* or non-Jew, there is also a prohibition, so why is excluding the *Kohen* worse? The answer is not simply the severity of the prohibition. Even an *eved* or non-Jew may involve a *lo ta'aseh*; the distinction does not lie in the weight of the prohibition. The critical difference is that *kiddushin* with a *Kohen* would be *binding* even though prohibited. If a *gerushah* — a divorced woman — were to marry a *Kohen hedyot* in violation of the prohibition, the marriage would nonetheless take effect as a valid *kiddushin*, because the prohibition is an *issur lav*, not one that negates the binding force of the *kiddushin*. By contrast, *kiddushin* with a non-Jew or an *eved* does not take effect at all.

Accordingly, when the husband says "this is your *get* except with respect to *Kohanim*," he is doing something real: without that exclusion, if she had chosen to marry a *Kohen* in violation of the prohibition, the marriage would have been binding. By carving out the *Kohen*, he is preventing a binding *kiddushin* from taking effect — that is a genuine limitation on the effect of the *gerushin*. In the case of the *eved* or non-Jew, there is nothing real to limit; the *kiddushin* was never going to be binding in any case.

### Binding Kiddushin

The *mishna* states its general principle explicitly: *kol mi she'yesh lo alav kiddushin* — anyone with whom *kiddushin* would be binding — even if that *kiddushin* would only be binding through a transgression (*afilu ba'aveirah*) — excluding such a person from the *get* invalidates it. And conversely, anyone with whom *kiddushin* is not binding at all, excluding that person leaves the *get* valid.

### Minor's Future Status

The *gemara* records a question posed by Rava to Rav Nachman: *chutz mi'kiddushei katan mahu*? If the husband says "this is your *get* except with respect to a minor" — what is the ruling? The *kiddushin* of a *katan* cannot take effect right now; he is simply not in the category of one who can effect *kiddushin* at present. So perhaps this exclusion does not limit the *get* at the moment of giving. Or perhaps, since he will eventually reach the age at which *kiddushin* can take effect — *ati l'khlal havayah* — the exclusion is real even now.

Rav Nachman responds: we have already been taught this, from the case of *ketanah misgarshet b'kiddushei aviha*. A minor girl whose father had married her off can be divorced even before she reaches adulthood. One might have questioned this on the grounds that *gerushin* should parallel *kiddushin* — just as *kiddushin* without a father requires adulthood, so too perhaps *gerushin* without a father should require adulthood. But the *gemara* answers: *ati l'khlal havayah* — since she will naturally mature and enter the category of those who can be given in marriage, she is already treated as within that category. The same logic applies to the *katan*: since he will naturally mature and enter the category of those who can effect *kiddushin*, he is considered within that category now, and excluding him is a real exclusion that invalidates the *get*.

### Unborn Children

The *gemara* next asks: *chutz min hanoladim mahu*? If the husband says this is your *get* except with respect to anyone born from today onward, is that a real exclusion? Right now there are no such people. Or do we say that since people will inevitably be born — *asidei d'miyalid* — this is an anticipated future that renders the exclusion real even now?

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishna*'s ruling that excluding an *eved* or *oved kochavim* does not invalidate the *get*. If future status were always relevant, one should worry even about the *eved* or non-Jew, since either might eventually convert or be freed, after which *kiddushin* would be binding. Since the *mishna* is not concerned about that future, perhaps future status is never relevant.

The *gemara* rejects this inference: *hanahu lo l'giyurei kaimei* — a non-Jew cannot be assumed to be on a path toward conversion; *hani l'ileyudei kaimei* — but children will be born, that is simply the natural course of events. The future arrival of new people in the world is an anticipated certainty; the conversion of a particular non-Jew is not. Accordingly, the question of *chutz min hanoladim* remains unresolved.

A conceptual point follows from this entire discussion. The *chachamim* who dispute Rabbi Eliezer generally hold that one cannot be *muteret* to one person and *asurah* to another — the *gemara* earlier invoked the principle *lo matzinu muteret lazeh va'asurah lazeh*. Yet in these *teku* cases, the *gemara* is entertaining the possibility that a *chutz* clause directed at people who are not currently present may in fact leave the *get* valid — which would mean the woman could be a *gerushah* vis-à-vis most people and something more like an *eishes ish* vis-à-vis those specified in the *chutz*. This is a more permissive stance toward conditional *gerushin* than one might have expected from the *chachamim*'s position — the key criterion being not an absolute bar on partial divorce but whether the exclusion limits the effect of the *get* in the here and now.

### Sister's Husband

The *gemara* poses a further question: *chutz miba'al achosah mahu*? If the husband says this is your *get* except with respect to your sister's husband — right now she cannot marry her sister's husband in any case, since her sister is alive. But if her sister were to die, he would become available. Does the possibility of the sister's death render this a real exclusion? Or does the uncertainty of whether that will occur mean it is not a genuine limitation right now?

The *gemara* attempts to draw an analogy to the *eved* and *oved kochavim* — they too might convert or be freed — and the *mishna* is not concerned about that future. But the *gemara* distinguishes: *geirus lo shchicha* — conversion is uncommon and cannot be assumed; *misah shchicha* — death is common. Death is an anticipated event; the question is only who dies first. Accordingly, the possibility that the sister will predecease makes the exclusion of the sister's husband potentially real, and the question of whether it constitutes an invalidating *chutz* is left unresolved.

## Marginal Exclusions

### Sexual Exclusion

The *gemara* now turns to exclusions that concern not the act of marriage (*nisuin*) but something more marginal. The *mishna*'s entire framework has focused on whether the husband is excluding someone from the scope of *kiddushin* — from the possibility of a binding marriage. What if the exclusion is directed not at marriage but at something else?

### Anal Intercourse

*Chutz miznus'ayich mahu*? If he says this is your *get* except with respect to extramarital sex with a certain man — is that a real *chutz*? The *mishna*'s framework seems to focus on marriage: the question has always been whether certain men are excluded from the scope of the *get* in terms of their ability to marry her. If no marriage is excluded, perhaps this is not a real limitation on the *gerushin*. Or perhaps even limiting certain acts of sex constitutes a holding back on the full effect of the divorce.

The *gemara* then asks the parallel question: *chutz mishelo kedarkah mahu*? If the only thing excluded is *biah shelo kedarkah* — anal intercourse — while *biah kedarkah* is not excluded, is that a limiting exclusion? The Torah considers both forms of intercourse to constitute *mishkevei ishah*, and therefore perhaps even excluding one form of sex constitutes a limitation on the completeness of the *gerushin*. The *gemara* envisions — strikingly — the possibility that a *gerushin* could take partial effect with respect to one form of sexual union and not another.

### Annulling Vows

*Chutz mehafarat nedarayich mahu*? If he says this is your *get* except that he retains the right to annul her vows — is that a real *chutz*? In terms of marriage, she is entirely free: she may marry anyone. But the Torah states *ishah yekimenu v'ishah yeferenu* — it is the husband who upholds or annuls her vows. If he is still exercising that prerogative, perhaps the Torah considers him still to be her husband, and the *get* is therefore not complete.

### Terumah Rights

*Chutz mitrumasayich mahu*? If he says this is your *get* except that she may continue to eat his *terumah* — perhaps the *get* is valid since she remains free to marry anyone. Or perhaps the right to eat *terumah* is rooted in the verse *kinyan kaspo* — she is still considered his *kinyan*, his wife, and therefore the *get* is not genuine.

### Inheritance Rights

*Chutz meyerushasayich mahu*? If he says this is your *get* except that when she dies he will still inherit her — she may marry anyone she chooses, but he retains the right of inheritance. Perhaps this is fine since no marriage is excluded. Or perhaps, since the verse establishes *lishero yirash osah* — his flesh inherits her — if he inherits her she remains considered his, and the severance is not real.

### Document Kiddushin

*Chutz mikidushayich beshtar mahu*? If he says this is your *get* except that if someone tries to effect *kiddushin* with her through a *shtar* — a document — it will not take hold, though *kiddushin* by money or by *biah* remains possible. She can in fact enter a new marriage by other means; perhaps this is sufficient. Or perhaps, since all three modes of *kiddushin* are equated — *huksha havayah leyetziah*, the modes of marriage are parallel to the modes of divorce — she must be fully free in all modes simultaneously, and any limitation on even one mode prevents the *get* from being effective.

The *gemara* leaves all of these questions as *teku* — unresolved. What emerges from this remarkable sequence is that the *gemara* is genuinely entertaining the possibility that a *get* could be valid while leaving some marginal aspect of the marital relationship intact, as long as the core function of the divorce — freeing her to enter a new binding marriage with any man — remains intact. At the same time, the *gemara* is uncertain whether even these marginal retentions might, on close reading of the relevant verses, indicate that the marital bond has not been fully severed.

## Core Language

### Standard Punchline

A new *mishna* states: *gufo shel get* — the essential text of the *get* — is *harei at muteret l'chol adam*: you are hereby permitted to marry any man. This is the minimal statement required at the core of the instrument of divorce.

### Testify About Itself

Rabbi Yehudah adds a further requirement. He cites the *Aramaic* text: *v'dein dei l'hevei lichi minai sefer teiruchin v'igert shivuchin v'get piturin l'meihach l'hitnasba l'chol gevar d'titzabyan* — "and this shall be from me a document of dismissal, a writ of separation, and a letter of release, to go and marry any man you desire." The *gemara* will explain that Rabbi Yehudah's primary contribution is the word *v'dein* — "this." Rabbi Yehudah holds that the *get* must testify about itself as the instrument of divorce. If the *get* merely declares *harei at muteret l'chol adam*, one might suppose that the divorce was effected through some other means and that this document merely records or proves the fact — that the *get* demonstrates she is divorced, rather than being itself the cause. Rabbi Yehudah requires that the document identify itself — *v'dein*, this very document — as the instrument through which the divorce is effected.

The *mishna* concludes by noting the parallel text for a *get shichrur*: *harei at bat chorin, harei at l'atzmech* — you are a free woman, you belong to yourself — which constitutes the essential language of a writ of emancipation for a *shifchah* or *eved*.
