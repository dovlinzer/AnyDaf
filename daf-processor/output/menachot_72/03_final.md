# Menachot 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fodder Cutting Rules

### Fodder Cutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹצֵר לְשַׁחַת וּמַאֲכִיל לַבְּהֵמָה. תְּנַן הָתָם: וְאֵלּוּ מַפְסִיקִין לַפֵּאָה – הַנַּחַל, וְהַשְּׁלוּלִית, וְדֶרֶךְ הַיָּחִיד, וְדֶרֶךְ הָרַבִּים, וּשְׁבִיל הָרַבִּים, וּשְׁבִיל הַיָּחִיד הַקָּבוּעַ בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וּבִימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְהַבּוֹר, וְהַנִּיר, וְזֶרַע אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One may** reap crops in any field **for fodder** and **feed it to an animal** even before the *omer* offering. The Gemara notes that **we learned** in a mishna **there** (*Pe'a* 2:1): **And these divide** a field **for** the purpose of ***pe'a*,** i.e., the presence of any of these separates a field so that each section constitutes a distinct field from which *pe'a* must be allocated independently: **A stream** that passes through the field, **and a canal [*vehashelulit*], and a private road** that is four cubits wide, **and a public road** that is at least sixteen cubits wide, **and a permanent public trail or a private trail** that is used **whether in the summer or in the rainy season,** i.e., winter, **and an uncultivated field, and a plowed field, and a seed** of **a different** kind of plant, e.g., a section of barley seed in a field full of wheat.

We pick up on the bottom of 72a, completing a brief carry-over from the previous session. The discussion had centered on what types of cutting are permitted before the *omer*, and specifically on the case of cutting grain as *shachat* — fodder for animals. The *Mishnah* debated whether such cutting must be performed when the grain is less than one-third ripe (clearly animal food) or even when it has reached one-third ripeness. This question was then connected to a parallel ruling of Rabbi Meir regarding *pe'ah*.

### Rabbi Meir & Pe'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹצֵר לְשַׁחַת מַפְסִיק, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מַפְסִיק אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן חָרַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** In all of the aforementioned instances a field is considered divided into two distinct fields. Another type of separation is subject to dispute: **And** in the case of one who **reaps** crops in a field **for fodder,** this action also **divides** a field in two. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** It **does not divide** a field **unless one** also **plowed** the area that he reaped. Only then is the field divided, as it is a plowed field.

Rabbi Meir holds that if one cuts for fodder in the middle of a field in a spotted pattern — the practice called *menamér* — it divides the field into separate sections for purposes of *pe'ah*, since it is not considered the beginning of a harvest but rather a different type of cutting. The *Gemara* is attempting to identify precisely what Rabbi Meir holds, because our *Mishnah* emphasized that the cutting must be done explicitly for animal consumption, whereas Rabbi Meir did not specify this. Since there was apparently a category of fodder that could also serve human purposes — where one is interested not in the grain itself but in the straw or the like — Rabbi Meir's position appears to diverge from the *Mishnah*'s framing.

### Rav Dimi Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לִבְהֵמָה, לְאָדָם מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? דְּאִם כֵּן, הָווּ לְהוּ תְּלָתָא תַּנָּאֵי. אֶלָּא, כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: רַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא רַבּוֹ אָמַר, אַף לְאָדָם נָמֵי לָא הָוְיָא קְצִירָה. דִּתְנַן: הַמְנַמֵּר שָׂדֶה וְשִׁיֵּיר בּוֹ קְלָחִים לַחִים, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: פֵּאָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מֵאֶחָד עַל הַכֹּל, וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא חִיֵּיב רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אֶלָּא בִּמְנַמֵּר לִקְלָיוֹת, אֲבָל בִּמְנַמֵּר לָאוֹצָר – לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: You can **say that you heard Rabbi Yehuda** express his opinion with regard to fodder that is reaped for the purposes of feeding an animal, but **did you hear him** say so with regard to a case where the reaping is performed **for human** consumption? **That** cannot be his opinion, since **if** it were **so,** then **there would be three** disputing opinions among the ***tanna'im*:** The opinion of the first *tanna*, who holds that reaping fodder for animal consumption is permitted; the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that it may be reaped even for human consumption provided that it has not grown one-third; and the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that it is permitted for human consumption even in a case where it had grown one-third. This is problematic, as the Gemara in tractate *Sanhedrin* (25a) states a principle that whenever Rabbi Yehuda says in a mishna: When [*eimatai*], he is clarifying, rather than disagreeing with, the opinion of the previous *tanna*. **Rather, when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael **he said: Rabbi Meir stated** his opinion in accordance **with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, his teacher.** Rabbi Akiva holds that **even** harvesting **for human** consumption **is not** considered **reaping** with regard to the *halakhot* of *pe'a*, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Pe'a* 3:2): With regard to **one who** reaps **alternate** rows of his **field, and he leaves in it moist stems** that are not yet fully grown, **Rabbi Akiva says:** One must separate ***pe'a* in each and every** row, as each one is considered a separate field. **And the Rabbis say:** One separates *pe'a* **from one** row **for the whole** field, as they are all considered a single field. **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: Rabbi Akiva** maintained that one is **obligated** to separate *pe'a* from each row **only when** he reaps **alternate** rows and the grain is unripe kernels used **for** making **roasted grains. But when** one reaps **alternate** rows and the grain is fully grown produce **for** his **storehouse,** there is **no** obligation to separate *pe'a* from each row, as all the rows are considered part of one field. Rav Dimi is suggesting that Rabbi Meir holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, and therefore the discrepancy between the mishna and the *baraita* can be resolved: The mishna is discussing unripe grains that have not yet grown one-third, similar to unripe kernels used for roasted grains, whereas the *baraita* is discussing fully grown, ripened grains.

The *Gemara* now moves to identify the source of Rabbi Meir's view. The relevant passage begins with the word *tani*, nine lines from the bottom of the page. *Ela ki ata Rav Dimi* — when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported: *Rebbi Meir beshitat Rebbi Akiva rabbo amrah* — Rabbi Meir followed the position of Rabbi Akiva, his teacher. It should be noted that Rabbi Akiva was in fact the *rebbi* of all the major *Tannaim* of the *Mishnah* — Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Shimon — they were all *talmidei Rebbi Akiva*.

What is Rabbi Akiva's position? *De'afilu adam nami la havei ketzirah* — even if one cuts *shachat* for human consumption, it is not considered a harvesting. The source is the *Mishnah* dealing with *hamenamer sadeh* — one who "leopards" a field, cutting it in spots. If *kelachim* — stalks that are not yet ready for harvest — are left standing, the implication is that what was harvested was also not fully ripe, and therefore this was effectively a fodder cutting. Rabbi Akiva rules: *pe'ah lechol echad ve'echad* — each remaining section is treated as a separate field for *pe'ah*, meaning the cutting is not considered an act of harvesting that unifies the field. The *chachamim* disagree: *me'echad al hakol* — it is still one field and a single *pe'ah* suffices.

This ruling was qualified, however, by a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *lo chiyev Rebbi Akiva ela bemenamer lekaliyot* — Rabbi Akiva's ruling applies only when the spotting was done for *kaliyot*, roasted grain, which according to Rashi means the grain had not yet reached one-third ripeness. *Aval bemenamer le'otzar lo* — but when one is cutting for *otzar*, storage, which implies the grain is already ripe, Rabbi Akiva would agree that it constitutes the beginning of harvest and the field remains unified.

### Ravin's Report

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אַף בִּמְנַמֵּר לָאוֹצָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he reported that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Akiva deems** one **obligated** in the separation of *pe'a* from each row **even where** he reaped fully grown fodder in **alternative** sections of his field **for** the purposes of storing in **a storehouse.** According to Ravin, Rabbi Akiva holds that reaping even fully grown fodder is not considered the start of the entire field's reaping process, but only of the individual row. Therefore, one must separate *pe'a* from each row. This is inconsistent with Rabbi Meir's opinion that the harvesting of fodder that has grown one-third is considered reaping.

The *Gemara* challenges this: is it truly the case that everyone agrees that cutting for storage does not divide the field? *Ve-ha ki ata Ravin amar Rabbi Yochanan* — when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mechayev hayah Rabbi Meir af be-mana le-otzar* — Rabbi Meir maintained his position even when cutting for storage, even when the grain is ripe. The *Gemara* resolves this: *savar lah kavateih be-chada u-falig aleih be-chada* — Rabbi Meir agrees with Rabbi Akiva in one respect but disagrees in another. Rabbi Akiva went so far as to say that any fodder cutting, regardless of the grain's state of ripeness, is a non-harvesting act. Rabbi Meir agrees when the grain is less than one-third ripe, but does not go as far as Rabbi Akiva when the grain is already ripe.

The practical upshot, to wrap up this thread, is that cutting for fodder is not considered a harvesting and may therefore be done before the *omer* is cut. The question of how precisely to define "for fodder" — whether by the cutter's intention, by the objective ripeness of the grain, or by whether it is destined for animals or humans — is exactly what these various positions have been working out.

## Mitzvah Harvesting

### Cutting Purposes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּוָותֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא. קוֹצְרִין מִפְּנֵי הַנְּטִיעוֹת, וּמִפְּנֵי בֵּית הָאֵבֶל, וּמִפְּנֵי בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״קְצִירְכֶם״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא קְצִיר מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: He **holds in accordance with** his opinion **with regard to one** issue, **and disagrees with him with regard to one** issue. In other words, Rabbi Meir holds in accordance with Rabbi Akiva's opinion that the harvesting of fodder that has not yet reached one-third of its growth is not considered the start of the reaping of the entire crop, and he disagrees with Rabbi Akiva's opinion that the harvesting of fodder that has reached one-third of its growth is also not considered the start of the reaping process, as Rabbi Meir maintains that this is considered the start of the reaping process even when it is performed for animals, and therefore it does not divide the field with regard to *pe'a*. § The mishna teaches: **And one may reap** crops prior to the *omer* **due to** potential damage to **saplings** growing alongside the crops; and **due to the place of mourning,** to create room for those consoling the mourners, who would bless them upon their return from the cemetery; and **due to** the need to create room for students to study, as failure to do so would lead to **dereliction of** Torah study in **the study hall.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** one is permitted to reap prior to the *omer* offering in these instances? The Gemara answers that **the Merciful One states:** "You shall bring the sheaf of the first fruits of **your harvest** to the priest" (Leviticus 23:10). The use of the term "your harvest" indicates that the *omer* offering's reaping must precede any personal harvest, **but** it does **not** need to precede **reaping for** the purpose of **a mitzva.**

We now continue with a new but related topic, at the top of 72b. The *Mishnah* states: *ha-kotzer mipnei ha-netiot u-mipnei beit ha-avel u-mipnei beit ha-midrash* — one who harvests on account of saplings, on account of a mourning house, or on account of a study hall. These are types of harvesting that are permitted before the *omer*.

What do these cases mean? *Mipnei ha-netiot* refers to a situation where grain is growing together with tree shoots, creating a *kilayim* problem, and one cuts the grain down so that the prohibited mixture should not persist. *Beit ha-avel u-beit ha-midrash* — one wishes to clear space in a wheat field so that mourners may gather there after a funeral, sitting in an open *chatzer*, or so that a group may convene for study. In these cases, the person is not interested in the grain as grain; the goal is to clear the space. Since this is not a harvesting for eating purposes but for a *mitzvah* purpose, it is permitted before the *omer*.

### Talmud Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כְּרִיכוֹת, אֲבָל מַנִּיחָן צְבָתִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּכַמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר לָא טָרְחִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: After reaping the crops for any of these reasons, **one may not fashion them** into **sheaves, but he leaves them unbound.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** The Gemara answers: Although reaping is technically permitted, one should limit his involvement with the new grain. Therefore, **as much as possible** to avoid exerting effort in involvement with the grain, **we do not exert** effort.

*Mai ta'ama* — what is the reason? The *Gemara* answers with elegant brevity: *ketzirchem amar Rachmana ve-lo ketzir mitzvah* — the verse says "the first of *your* cuttings" (*reshit ketzirchem*), implying that the prohibition on cutting before the *omer* applies to cuttings that serve one's own mundane purposes, but not to cuttings performed for the sake of a *mitzvah*, for a divine purpose.

This type of *derashah* is familiar from elsewhere. A parallel principle appears on *Erev Shabbat*: *cheftzecha asur cheftzei shamayim muttarim* — your own affairs are restricted on Shabbat, but affairs of Heaven are permitted. This is why a *shul* may make announcements about events scheduled for after Shabbat. Another parallel is *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah*, derived from the verse by *Keri'at Shema* — *u-ve-lechtecha va-derech*, when you go about your own purposes, implying that when you are engaged in *mitzvah* purposes, the exemption applies.

That said, one could argue that the *mitzvah* framing is not even strictly necessary in our case. Since the person has no interest in obtaining the grain — the goal is simply to clear the space or eliminate *kilayim* — one might argue that it does not qualify as a "cutting" in the relevant sense at all, regardless of whether it is a *mitzvah*. The exemption may follow simply from the fact that this is not a grain-harvesting act. Both rationales are at work here.

One might also raise the question — and it is a genuinely interesting one — of whether it would matter if someone cut grain entirely for eating but then donated the entire yield to the poor. Would that transform it into a *mitzvah* cutting? The answer likely depends on which of the two rationales one emphasizes. Similarly, if the field were owned by *hekdesh* — by the *Beit HaMikdash* — the grain would not be "yours" in the relevant sense, and there might be grounds to permit cutting on an entirely different basis. These questions are not addressed by the *Gemara* here.

As for whether the prohibition on *katzirah* applies *b'zman ha-zeh* — today when there is no *omer* — one would presume there is no *issur katzirah* at all, since the entire prohibition of cutting is defined by the *omer's* status as *reshit ketzirchem*. The *issur chadash* on eating new grain does apply today — the sixteenth of Nisan is the time that permits it even without an actual *omer* being brought — but the cutting prohibition, which is entirely a function of defining the *omer* as the first cutting, presumably has no application *b'zman ha-zeh*.

### No Bundles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִצְוַת הָעוֹמֶר לְהָבִיא מִן הַקָּמָה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאִם תַּקְרִיב מִנְחַת בִּכּוּרִים״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁמִּצְוַת הָעוֹמֶר לְהָבִיא מִן הַקָּמָה, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא מָצָא מִן הַקָּמָה יָבִיא מִן הָעֳמָרִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תַּקְרִיב״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **the mitzva of the *omer*** is **to bring** the barley **from the standing grain. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **What** is the meaning when **the verse states:** "And if you bring a meal offering of first fruits to the Lord, you shall bring for the meal offering of your first fruits grain in the ear parched with fire, even groats of the fresh ear" (Leviticus 2:14)? The *baraita* explains: **Since the mitzva of the *omer*** is **to bring** the barley **from the standing grain, from where** is it derived **that if one does not find** barley **from the standing grain,** he should **bring** it **from the** harvested and gathered **sheaves? The verse states: "You shall bring,"** to include this scenario.

The *Gemara* adds one qualification: *v'lo ya'aseh krichot ela manicham tzvatim* — even when these various pre-*omer* cuttings are permitted, one should not make formal bundles out of the cut grain; rather, one should leave them in small informal piles. *D'chama d'efshar lo tarchinan* — as much as possible, we do not invest effort in the full harvesting process before the *omer*. Some things are necessary and we permit them, but we do not want the full harvesting apparatus to be set in motion before the *omer* is brought.

## Omer Source Rules

### Standing Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״תַּקְרִיב״ – לְפִי שֶׁמִּצְוָה לְהָבִיא מִן הַלַּח, וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא מָצָא מִן הַלַּח יָבִיא מִן הַיָּבֵשׁ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תַּקְרִיב״. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״תַּקְרִיב״ – לְפִי שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ לִקְצוֹר בַּלַּיְלָה, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם נִקְצַר בַּיּוֹם כָּשֵׁר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תַּקְרִיב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the *baraita* suggests another reason why the verse states: **"You shall bring." Since it is a mitzva to bring** the *omer* **from the moist** grain, one can ask: **From where** is it derived that if one does not find barley from the moist grain, he should **bring** it **from the dry** grain? **The verse states: "You shall bring,"** to include this case. **Alternatively,** the term **"you shall bring"** teaches the following: **Since** the **mitzva** of the *omer* is for it to be **reaped at night, from where** is it derived **that** if it was reaped **during the daytime, it is fit? The verse states: "You shall bring."**

*Mitzvat haOmer l'havi min hakama* — the *mitzvah* of the *omer* ideally requires that it be brought from standing grain: one harvests grain that is still in the field, specifically for the sake of the *omer*. This is the optimal fulfillment. However, the *Mishnah* goes on to rule that if standing grain was not cut for the *omer* — whether permissibly or not — and one has already-cut grain on hand, that grain may be used.

### Alternatives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תַּקְרִיב״, ״תַּקְרִיב״ – כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, ״תַּקְרִיב״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, ״תַּקְרִיב״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, ״תַּקְרִיב״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds more *halakhot* that are derived from this same verse. From where is it derived that the *omer* offering **overrides Shabbat? The verse states: "You shall bring."** Also, this term: **"You shall bring,"** teaches that the *omer* is brought in **any** manner that it is found, even from gathered sheaves. Furthermore, the term **"you shall bring,"** teaches that the *omer* crop may be brought **from anywhere** in Eretz Yisrael, if none is found near Jerusalem. Additionally, **"you shall bring"** teaches that it may be brought **even on Shabbat.** Lastly, **"you shall bring"** teaches that it may be brought **even in** a state of **ritual impurity.**

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *v'im takriv minchat bikurim* — the verse "if you bring a *minchah* of the first fruit," understood here as referring to the *omer* (and not to the *Shtei HaLechem*). The verse teaches that the ideal *mitzvah* is to bring it from standing grain. How do we know that if standing grain cannot be found one may bring from sheaves, from already-cut grain? *Talmud lomar takriv* — the repetition of the word *takriv* at the end of the verse ("you shall bring") teaches: just bring it, regardless of how you obtained it.

*Davar acher* — another application of the same verse: the *omer* ideally should be fresh. How do we know that if fresh grain cannot be found, dry grain may be used? *Talmud lomar takriv* — just bring it. Another *davar acher*: since the *mitzvah* is to cut the *omer* at night — as this creates *temimot*, ensuring the count of seven full weeks begins from the night — how do we know it is valid if cut during the day? *Talmud lomar takriv*.

Before arriving at that last point, the *baraita* also teaches that the *omer* overrides Shabbat: *talmud lomar takriv*, bring it under any circumstances. *Takriv kol shehu* — bring whatever you can find, even grain still in sheaves. *Takriv mikol makom* — Rashi understands this phrase literally: from any location in Eretz Yisrael, not only from the fields near Yerushalayim. *Takriv afilu b'Shabbat* — bring it even if it is Shabbat; and by extension, *takriv afilu b'tumah* — bring it even if there is a *tumah* problem, since this is a fixed-time communal sacrifice and such sacrifices override Shabbat.

## Day Cutting Debate

### Megilla Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִקְצַר בַּיּוֹם – כָּשֵׁר. וְהָתְנַן: כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה כָּשֵׁר לִקְצִירַת הָעוֹמֶר, וּלְהַקְטִיר חֲלָבִים וְאֵבָרִים. זֶה הַכְּלָל: דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ כׇּל הַיּוֹם – כָּשֵׁר כׇּל הַיּוֹם, דָּבָר שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ בְּלַיְלָה – כָּשֵׁר כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **it was reaped during the day,** it is **fit.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Megilla* 20b): All mitzvot that must be performed at night may be performed anytime during that night. Therefore, **the entire night is valid** for **reaping the *omer*** on the night following the first day of Passover, **for burning the fats** of offerings that had been brought during the preceding day, **and** for burning **the limbs** of burnt offerings. **This is the principle: A matter that it is a mitzva** to perform **during the entire day is valid** if performed anytime during **the entire day,** and likewise **a matter that it is a mitzva** to perform **at night is valid** if performed anytime during **the entire night.**

The *baraita* has stated that if the *omer* was cut during the day it is *kasher*. This is challenged from a *Mishnah* in Megilla that lists *mitzvot* categorized by when they may be performed. The *Mishnah* there states: *kol halaila kasher l'ktzirat ha'omer* — the entire night is valid for cutting the *omer* — and *u'lehaktir chalavin ve'evarim* — and for burning the fats and limbs of a *korban*. The *Mishnah* there then articulates a general principle: *davar shemitzva'to bayom kasher kol hayom* — anything whose *mitzvah* is by day may be done the entire day; *davar shemitzva'to balaila kasher kol halaila* — anything whose *mitzvah* is by night may be done the entire night.

### Day/Night Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי לַיְלָה דּוּמְיָא דְּיוֹם, מָה דְּיוֹם – בַּלַּיְלָה לָא, אַף דְּלַיְלָה – בַּיּוֹם נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this mishna: The mishna **teaches** the principle of mitzvot performed **at night** as being **similar to** the principle of those performed during **the day.** From this one can infer that **just as** in the case of a mitzva whose prescribed time is **by day,** if it is performed **at night** it is **not** valid, **so too** with regard to a mitzva whose prescribed time is **at night,** if it is performed **by day** it is also **not** valid. If so, why does the mishna here teach that if the *omer* was reaped during the day it is fit?

The *Gemara* draws an inference: *katani laila dumya d'yom* — the night category is presented as parallel to the day category. Just as day *mitzvot* may not be performed at night — one cannot take a *lulav* at night — so too night *mitzvot* may not be performed during the day: *af laila bayom nami lo*. If the cutting of the *omer* is a night *mitzvah*, it follows that it cannot be done during the day. How then can our *Mishnah* rule that it is *kasher* if cut during the day?

A subtle but important question arises: does the mention of "day" in our *Mishnah* refer to the day before, or to the day after the night of cutting? Tosafot clarifies that since the night of cutting is the earliest valid time — the day before is clearly too early — the question must concern the day after. The *Mishnah* in Megilla, with its *diyuk* that night *mitzvot* are restricted to the night, would then exclude the following day as well.

The *Gemara* is aware that one might argue that night *mitzvot* are rare and perhaps begin at night but continue into the day. The *Gemara* declines to read the *Mishnah* in Megilla that way. The question then is how we rule (*pasken*) — and the answer, as the *Gemara* will show, is that this is a dispute between *Tannaim*.

As for the practical rationale for cutting the *omer* at night: while it may not have been logistically simple, the *Gemara* derives it from *temimot* — the requirement for seven complete weeks — which demands that the count begin from the night. Tosafot, notably, connects this entire question to *Sefirat HaOmer* itself: if the ruling of the *Mishnah* in Megilla is normative — meaning the *omer* is exclusively a night *mitzvah* and invalid if done during the day — then *bedi'avad* one cannot fulfill *Sefirat HaOmer* during the day either. Tosafot acknowledges that missing a day does not disqualify one from continuing to count, but the principle that the count is a night *mitzvah* is what he derives from here.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי, וְהָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא: הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב מִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר וְנִטְמֵאת, אִם יֵשׁ אַחֶרֶת – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הָבֵא אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ״, וְאִם לָאו – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֱוֵי פִּקֵּחַ וּשְׁתוֹק״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** It is **not difficult. This** mishna, which teaches that the *omer* reaped during the day is valid, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and that** mishna in *Megilla*, which states that any nighttime mitzva performed during the day is not valid, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon. As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 3:9): In a case where a priest **was standing and sacrificing the *omer* meal offering and it became ritually impure** in his hand, **if there is another** measure of barley grain that is ready to be reaped, then one **says to** the priest: Reap the barley and **bring another** meal offering **in its stead. And if** there is **no** alternative meal offering available, one **says to him: Be shrewd and keep silent;** i.e., do not tell anyone that it is impure. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

Rava resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, ha Rebbi v'ha Rebbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon* — there is no difficulty; one source reflects the position of Rebbi and the other reflects the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon. The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* that makes their dispute explicit.

### Tumah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ, אוֹמַר לוֹ: ״הֱוֵי פִּקֵּחַ וּשְׁתוֹק״, שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹמֶר שֶׁנִּקְצַר שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתוֹ – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: In either case,** one **says to him: Be shrewd and keep silent, as any *omer*** offering **that is harvested not in accordance with** the procedure dictated by **its mitzva is unfit.** Likewise, one may not reap the barley during the daytime, as its prescribed time is at night.

The *baraita* presents a case: a *kohen* was in the process of offering the *Minchat HaOmer* when it became *tamei* just before being placed on the *mizbe'ach*. What is to be done? *Im yesh acheres* — if other grain is available, *omer lo havei acheres tachte'ha* — he is told to go and bring another *omer*; do not use the *tamei* one. *Ve'im lav* — but if it is not possible to obtain replacement grain in time, *omer lo havei pike'ach ushtok* — he is told to be prudent and keep it to himself — that is, proceed with the *tamei* grain without drawing attention to the fact.

This is the position of Rebbi. Rashi explains the instruction to remain quiet as a concern about misleading people: if it becomes known that a *tamei* *minchah* was offered, observers might conclude that *minchos* in general may be brought in a state of *tumah*, without realizing that this applies specifically to the *Minchat HaOmer* as a fixed-time communal *korban*. It is somewhat unclear why this concern is more acute here than in other cases of *tumah* in communal sacrifices — the *omer* ceremony is quite distinct from a private *minchah* — but that is Rashi's reasoning.

The position of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon differs sharply: *bein kach uvein kach, omer lo havei pike'ach ushtok* — regardless of whether replacement grain is available or not, he is told to proceed quietly with the current grain. The reason: *shekol haomer sheniktzar shelo kemitzvaso pasul* — any *omer* that was cut not in accordance with its proper manner is invalid. Going back to the field during the day would produce grain cut *shelo kemitzvasah* — and since the cutting was done at the wrong time, the grain would be *pasul*. According to Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, the *mitzvah* requires cutting specifically at night, and grain cut during the day cannot serve as an *omer* at all.

This is the crux of the dispute: according to Rebbi, grain cut during the day is *kasher bedi'avad*, so one should attempt to obtain valid grain; according to Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, daytime cutting is *pasul*, so there is no point in trying.

One might ask whether the *Mishnah* in Megilla — which treats cutting the *omer* as a night *mitzvah* exclusively — could still be distinguished from the question of whether the grain is *kasher bedi'avad* for the *korban*. One could theoretically hold that the *mitzvah* of cutting was not fulfilled and yet the grain remains usable for the offering. The *Gemara's* framing, however, places the question squarely in terms of *shelo kemitzvaso*: the debate is not merely about whether the *mitzvah* was properly performed but about whether the grain cut outside the designated time is *pasul* for the *korban* itself.

A further question the *Gemara* leaves open: according to Rebbi, who holds that *bedi'avad* daytime cutting is valid, how should one prioritize among the various *bedi'avad* alternatives the *baraita* listed — from sheaves rather than standing grain, dry rather than fresh, day rather than night? The degrees of *bedi'avad* are not ranked.

### Intellectual Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא רַבּוֹ שֶׁל אָבִיו אֲמָרָהּ, דִּתְנַן: כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹתָהּ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת אֵינָהּ דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, that barley for the *omer* offering that is reaped by day is unfit, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, said** his statement **in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, the teacher of his father. As we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 130a): **Rabbi Akiva stated a principle: Any prohibited labor that can be performed on Shabbat eve,** i.e., before Shabbat begins, **does not override Shabbat.**

The *Gemara* then traces the intellectual genealogy of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's position. *Rabbah bar bar Chanah* said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon b'shitat Rabbi Akiva rabo shel aviv amrah* — Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon stated his position following the approach of Rabbi Akiva, who was the teacher of his father. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — whose yahrtzeit falls around this time of year, at Lag BaOmer — was a student of Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Elazar is his son.

## Shabbat Override

### Akiva Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּאָמַר קְצִירַת הָעוֹמֶר מִצְוָה, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: מָה חָרִישׁ רְשׁוּת, אַף קָצִיר רְשׁוּת, יָצָא קְצִיר הָעוֹמֶר שֶׁהִיא מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** furthermore, Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who said** that the **reaping of** barley for **the *omer*** offering **is a mitzva. As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 1:4) that **Rabbi Yishmael says:** The verse: "In plowing time and in harvest you shall rest" (Exodus 34:21), is not referring to the prohibition against farming the land during the Sabbatical Year, as one might have thought. Rather, it is referring to the prohibition against performing labor on Shabbat. And the reason that the verse mentions these two particular forms of labor is to teach that **just as** the **plowing** that is prohibited on Shabbat is an otherwise **voluntary** act, as plowing is never required by the Torah, **so too,** the **harvesting** that is prohibited on Shabbat **is voluntary.** Therefore, **the harvesting of the *omer* is excluded** from the prohibition, **as it is a mitzva.** Consequently, the barley for the *omer* is harvested on the sixteenth of Nisan, even if it occurs on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* now transitions to the question of whether the *omer* overrides Shabbat. We established from the *baraita* that it does, but the precise reasoning requires careful analysis. The *Gemara* invokes a principle of Rabbi Akiva: *kol melachah she'efshar lah l'asot m'erev Shabbos einah docha es haShabbos* — any *melachah* that could have been performed before Shabbat does not override Shabbat. Rabbi Akiva articulated this in the context of *milah* and its *machshirim* — preparatory acts like sharpening the scalpel or transporting the knife, which could have been done before Shabbat and therefore should not be done on Shabbat itself.

The *Gemara* now applies this principle to the *omer*. If the 16th of Nisan falls on Shabbat, why do we cut the *omer* on Shabbat? If it would be *kasher bedi'avad* if cut earlier, why not cut it before Shabbat? The obvious answer would seem to be that the *mitzvah* is specifically to cut it on *mimacharat haShabbat* — the sixteenth — and therefore even if an earlier cutting would be *kasher bedi'avad*, the *mitzvah* demands it be done on Shabbat, just as the *mitzvah* of *milah* on the eighth day overrides Shabbat precisely because the *mitzvah* is tied to that day.

### Elazar's Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ נִקְצַר שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתוֹ כָּשֵׁר, אַמַּאי דָּחֵי שַׁבָּת? נִקְצְרֵיהּ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת! אֶלָּא מִדְּדָחֵי שַׁבָּת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: נִקְצַר שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתוֹ – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan explains why Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael: **And** Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, reached his opinion by the following reasoning: **If it enters your mind** to say that barley for the *omer* offering that is **reaped not** in accordance with the procedure dictated by **its mitzva** is nevertheless **fit, why** would it **override Shabbat? Let one reap it on Shabbat eve. Rather, from** the fact that the reaping **overrides Shabbat, learn from** here that if it **was reaped not** in accordance with **its mitzva,** it is **unfit.**

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon provides what the *Gemara* treats as a compelling proof for his position. *Ve-i salka da'atcha niktzar she-lo ke-mitzvato kasher* — if one were to say that grain cut at the wrong time (*shelo kemitzvaso*) is still *kasher*, then *amai dachi Shabbat*? Why would the cutting of the *omer* override Shabbat at all? One could simply cut it before Shabbat — not on *erev Shabbat* itself which is *Yom Tov*, but two days before — and it would be valid. *Ella mi-de-dachi Shabbat* — since we know the cutting does override Shabbat, the implication is that cutting it at any other time would be invalid. And if it is invalid when done too early, it must equally be invalid when done too late — specifically, when cut during the day rather than at night.

This argument has real force, but it is not without counterarguments. One might distinguish between "too early" and "too late" — the daytime of the same *halachic* day is not obviously comparable to a completely different day. Nevertheless, this is Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's position, and the *Gemara* treats it as a serious argument.

### Shimon Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי, לָאו תַּלְמִידֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הוּא? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: כְּשֶׁהָיִינוּ לְמֵדִין תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בִּתְקוֹעַ, הָיִינוּ מַעֲלִין לוֹ שֶׁמֶן וַאֲלוּנְטִית מֵחָצֵר לְגַג, וּמִגַּג לְקַרְפֵּיף, וּמִקַּרְפֵּיף לְקַרְפֵּיף אַחֵר, עַד שֶׁאָנוּ מַגִּיעִין לְמַעְיָין שֶׁאָנוּ רוֹחֲצִין בּוֹ. סָבַר לַהּ כְּאִידַּךְ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה חֲבִיבָה מִצְוָה בִּשְׁעָתָהּ, שֶׁהֲרֵי הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים וְאֵבָרִים כְּשֵׁרִים כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה, וְלֹא הָיָה מַמְתִּין לָהֶן עַד שֶׁתֶּחְשַׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Isn't Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi also a **student of Rabbi Shimon,** who was a student of Rabbi Akiva? Since Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was a student of Rabbi Shimon, and by extension of Rabbi Akiva, he should accept Rabbi Akiva's principle that any prohibited labor that can be performed on Shabbat eve does not override Shabbat. If so, as Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that if the *omer* was not performed at night it should be done by day, why does its harvest override Shabbat? Let it be harvested on Shabbat eve. The Gemara cites a proof for its claim that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was a student of Rabbi Shimon. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: When we would study Torah with Rabbi Shimon in Tekoa, we would carry to him oil and a towel [*aluntit*] from the courtyard to the roof and from the roof into an enclosure** similar to a courtyard, **and from** one **enclosure to another enclosure, until we reached the spring in which we would bathe,** without passing through a public domain. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi concedes that reaping barley for the *omer* offering overrides Shabbat, in accordance with Rabbi Akiva. But this is not because it is unfit if reaped at an improper time. Rather, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with the other** opinion **of Rabbi Shimon, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon said: Come and see how dear is a mitzva** performed **in its** proper **time. As burning the fats and limbs is valid all night,** and therefore it is possible to wait until the conclusion of Shabbat and burn them at night, **but** nevertheless **one would not wait with them until nightfall;** rather, one burns them immediately, even on Shabbat. Likewise, when it comes to the reaping of the *omer*, although it is fit if reaped during the previous day, reaping at night still overrides Shabbat because a mitzva is dear when performed in its proper time.

The *Gemara* then notes that Rebbi was also a student of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, establishing this through a *baraita* in which Rebbi himself describes how they would carry oil and towels from courtyard to roof to *karfif* on Shabbat in order to reach the spring where Rabbi Shimon would bathe. (This incidentally demonstrates that rooftops, courtyards, and enclosed yards were considered a unified domain for purposes of carrying — a matter that will be treated extensively in Eruvin.) The point here is simply to confirm that Rebbi was a direct student of Rabbi Shimon, and therefore the same chain of tradition — Rabbi Akiva → Rabbi Shimon → Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon — also reaches Rebbi.

Why then does Rebbi not adopt the same conclusion? The *Gemara* answers: *savar lah ke-idach de-Rabbi Shimon* — Rebbi follows a different ruling of Rabbi Shimon himself.

### Rebbi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, not know** of this? He must have known of his father's statement that a mitzva performed at its proper time overrides Shabbat. If so, his ruling that reaping at an improper time is unfit cannot be proved from the fact that reaping the *omer* overrides Shabbat, as claimed earlier.

Given such a compelling argument, why does Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon not accept it? *Lo shmiah lei* — the *Gemara* says he had not heard of this principle of his father regarding performing *mitzvot* at their proper time.

But Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon has a rejoinder of his own: *dachsah shechitah es hashabbos* — the case of burning the *imurim* on Shabbat is not comparable. In that case, one began with a license to violate Shabbat: the slaughter and sprinkling of blood of the *korban* were already performed on Shabbat — and once the initial *avodah* is underway, it follows naturally that one completes the *korban* on the same day. The burning of the fats overrides Shabbat only because the process was already set in motion by acts that themselves legitimately overrode Shabbat. From there, one cannot derive that one may *initiate* a violation of Shabbat for a *mitzvah* that could alternatively have been performed without any Shabbat violation at all.

### Evidence Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, שֶׁהֲרֵי דָּחֲתָה שְׁחִיטָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. וּלְרַבִּי, הָכִי נָמֵי דָּחֲתָה שְׁחִיטָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת! אֶלָּא קָסָבַר רַבִּי: קְצִירַת הָעוֹמֶר לָא דָּחֲיָא שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, was aware of that principle, and nevertheless he maintains that the requirement to perform a mitzva at its optimal time does not apply to the case of the reaping of the *omer*. This is because **there,** in the case of the burning of the fats and limbs, it **is different, as the slaughter** of the animal already **overrode Shabbat.** Therefore, it is dear to perform the burning of its fats and limbs at the proper time, even on Shabbat. One cannot apply this reasoning to the reaping of the *omer*, as there is no prior labor performed on Shabbat before the reaping. Consequently, the reason it may be performed on Shabbat must be that it would be unfit if reaped any other time. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **according to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, he should agree that the case of the burning of the sacrifice's fats and limbs is different from the *omer*, since **also** there, **the slaughter** of the animal already **overrode Shabbat.** Therefore, the Gemara gives a new explanation: **Rather, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that in fact the **reaping of the *omer*** crop **does not override Shabbat,** in contrast to the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael. For this reason Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's ruling that the *omer* is fit if reaped at an improper time does not conflict with the principle of his teacher, Rabbi Akiva, that any prohibited labor that can be performed on Shabbat eve does not override Shabbat.

Given the force of this counterargument, the *Gemara* turns to an even more striking conclusion: *kasavar Rebbi lo dachya shabbos* — the *Gemara* posits that Rebbi holds the cutting of the *omer* does *not* override Shabbat. This requires explaining away an array of evidence. Our *Mishnah* states that whether on Shabbat or the weekday the *omer* is cut — *d'lo k'Rebbe*, this is not Rebbi's view. Another *Mishnah* (involving the three-person procedure with three baskets and three sickles, performed whether on Shabbat or the weekday) — also *d'lo k'Rebbe*. Another *Mishnah* depicting the public ceremony in which the cutter asks "shall I cut on Shabbat?" and the gathered crowd responds affirmatively — *d'lo k'Rebbe*.

This is a striking turn. The *Gemara* is creating an entirely new position — that Rebbi holds the *omer* does not override Shabbat — which is not stated anywhere explicitly, but follows logically from the intersection of his rulings: that *bedi'avad* daytime cutting is *kasher*, and that anything which can be performed without violating Shabbat must be performed without violating Shabbat.

### Rebbi Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָתְנַן: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, אֶחָד שַׁבָּת וְאֶחָד חוֹל – מִשָּׁלֹשׁ הָיָה בָּא. דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד שַׁבָּת וְאֶחָד חוֹל – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, בְּשָׁלֹשׁ קוּפּוֹת וּבְשָׁלֹשׁ מַגָּלוֹת – דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי. וּבְשַׁבָּת יֹאמַר לָהֶם ״שַׁבָּת זוֹ?״ – דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי! אֶלָּא, נִקְצַר בַּיּוֹם כָּשֵׁר, וְדוֹחֶה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: נִקְצַר בַּיּוֹם כָּשֵׁר? רַבִּי, וְקָתָנֵי: וְדוֹחֶה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. מַאי לָאו קְצִירָה? לֹא, לְהַקְרָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **not** maintain that the reaping of the *omer* overrides Shabbat? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (63b) in response to Rabbi Yishmael's opinion that when the sixteenth of Nisan occurred during the week, five *se'a* of barley were reaped, and when it occurred on Shabbat only three *se'a* were reaped: **And the Rabbis say: Both on Shabbat and** during the **week,** the *omer* offering **would come from three** *se'a*? In any case, according to both opinions, the *omer* was reaped on Shabbat. The Gemara answers: **That** mishna **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara raises a difficulty from the latter clause of that mishna. Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, says: On Shabbat the barley was reaped by an individual, with one sickle and one basket into which the barley was placed, whereas during the week it was reaped by three people, with three baskets and three sickles. **And the Rabbis say: Both on Shabbat and** during the **week** it is reaped **by three** people, **with three baskets and three sickles.** According to both opinions the *omer* was reaped on Shabbat. The Gemara again responds: **That** clause of the mishna **is** also **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara raises another difficulty from a mishna (65a) that describes the series of questions posed by the court emissary who reaps the grain to those assembled to witness the process: **And** if the sixteenth of Nisan occurs **on Shabbat,** the court emissary **says to** those assembled: Shall I cut the sheaves on **this Shabbat?** The assembly says in response: Yes. The Gemara answers: **That** clause of mishna **is** also **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara raises a difficulty from the mishna (71a): If the *omer* **was reaped during the day,** it is **fit, and** it **overrides Shabbat.** Now, **whom did you hear who said: If the *omer* was reaped during the day,** it is **fit?** It is **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And** yet **it is taught** in the mishna: **And it overrides Shabbat. What, is it not** referring to **reaping?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is referring **to** the **sacrifice** of the *omer* offering.

Adding to the complexity, the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in the name of Rebbi himself: *Rebbi* derives from the verse *vayidaber Moshe et mo'adei Hashem* — "Moses spoke the appointed times of God to Israel" — that the *omer* and the *Shtei HaLechem* with their associated *korbanot* override Shabbat and *tumah*. The source for communal fixed-time *korbanot* generally overriding Shabbat comes from the verse *eleh ta'asu la-Hashem b'mo'adeichem* in *Parashat Pinchas*, which establishes *mo'ed* as a fixed-time designation analogous to the *Tamid* and the *Pesach* — both of which are explicitly stated to override Shabbat and *tumah* via the word *b'mo'ado*, "in its appointed time, even on Shabbat, even in *tumah*." The *omer* and *Shtei HaLechem*, however, appear in *Parashat Emor* and are not included in the *Pinchas* passage; the concluding verse of *Emor* — *vayidaber Moshe et mo'adei Hashem* — thus extends the override to these as well.

### Baking Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לִקְצִירָה לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וַיְדַבֵּר מֹשֶׁה אֶת מֹעֲדֵי ה׳״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא לָמַדְנוּ אֶלָּא לְתָמִיד וּפֶסַח, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ״, ״בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, ״בְּמוֹעֲדוֹ״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּטוּמְאָה. שְׁאָר קׇרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּבּוּר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֵלֶּה תַּעֲשׂוּ לַה׳ בְּמוֹעֲדֵיכֶם״. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת עוֹמֶר, וְהַקָּרֵב עִמּוֹ? שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, וְהַקָּרֵב עִמָּהֶן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַיְדַבֵּר מֹשֶׁה אֶת מֹעֲדֵי ה׳״ – הַכָּתוּב קָבַע מוֹעֵד לְכוּלָּן. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְהַקְרָבָה – שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם בְּנֵי הַקְרָבָה נִינְהוּ? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לִטְחִינָה וְהַרְקָדָה, וְדִכְוָתַהּ גַּבֵּי עוֹמֶר לִקְצִירָה, וְקָא דָחֵי אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת. אֶלָּא, עוֹמֶר לְהַקְרָבָה, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם לַאֲפִיָּה, וְקָסָבַר רַבִּי תַּנּוּר מְקַדֵּשׁ, אִי אָפֵי לַהּ מֵאֶתְמוֹל – אִיפְּסִילָה לַהּ בְּלִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues with an apparent contradiction. **But with regard to reaping,** does it **not** override Shabbat, according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi? **As, isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi said the following *halakha* based upon the verse: **"And Moses declared the appointed times of the Lord** to the children of Israel" (Leviticus 23:44): **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** this phrase? This phrase is necessary **because we** had **learned only that the daily offering and the Paschal offering** override Shabbat and ritual impurity, **as it is stated concerning them: "In its appointed time"** (see Numbers 9:2, 28:2), from which it is derived that each of them must be sacrificed **in its appointed time and even on Shabbat; in its appointed time and even in ritual impurity.** The *baraita* continues: With regard to **the rest of the communal offerings, from where** is it derived that they likewise override Shabbat and ritual impurity? **The verse states** with regard to additional offerings that are brought on the Festivals: **"These you shall sacrifice to the Lord in your appointed times"** (Numbers 29:39). The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived **to include** in this *halakha* the ***omer* and** the lambs that are **sacrificed with it, the two loaves** sacrificed on *Shavuot*, **and** the communal peace offerings that are **sacrificed with them? The verse states: "And Moses declared the appointed times of the Lord** to the children of Israel," after it lists Shabbat and the Festivals. This indicates that **the verse established one time for all of them.** All of these days are considered appointed times, and their offerings are not deferred. **With regard to what** ritual does the *baraita* state that these offerings override Shabbat? **If we say for** actual **sacrifice,** this cannot be correct, as **are the two loaves fit for sacrifice?** The two loaves are not sacrificed on the altar at all. Rather, they are waved and later eaten by priests. The Gemara concludes its interpretation of the *baraita*, which leads to the question about the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the two loaves override Shabbat with regard **to grinding and sifting. And** if so, **similarly, with regard to the *omer*** offering, it overrides Shabbat **with regard to** the process of **reaping** the grains. **And** as the *baraita* is the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, evidently he maintains that the reaping of the *omer* **overrides Shabbat. Rather,** one must say that the *baraita* is not referring to comparable cases. As far as the ***omer*** is concerned, it overrides Shabbat only **with regard to** its actual **sacrifice, but** when it comes to the **two loaves,** it overrides Shabbat **with regard to** its **baking. And** the reason why the two loaves must be baked on Shabbat is because **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that the **oven consecrates** that which is baked inside it. Therefore, **if** one were to **bake from the previous day,** on the eve of *Shavuot*, he would effectively be **disqualifying it** from being brought on *Shavuot* day, as it would be disqualified by being **left overnight.**

The *Gemara* now presses: *l'mai* — to what *melachah* does Rebbi's ruling refer? If overriding Shabbat means bringing the *omer* or *Shtei HaLechem* as a *korban* on the *mizbe'ach*, the *Shtei HaLechem* cannot be burned on the *mizbe'ach* since it is *chametz*. So the Shabbat override must refer to preparation. But what preparation of the *Shtei HaLechem* cannot be done before Shabbat?

The *Gemara* answers: *le-afiyah* — the baking. Everything else — grinding, sifting — could be done beforehand. Only the baking cannot, because — *ve-savar Rebbi tanur mekadesh* — Rebbi holds that the *tanur*, the oven, sanctifies the bread. Once baked, the bread is *kadosh*, and if it were to remain overnight it would become *pasul bi-linah*. Therefore, baking on the day before would produce bread that was already invalid by the time it was needed. Since there is literally no alternative but to bake on the day itself, the baking overrides Shabbat.

By analogy, this clarifies Rebbi's position on the *omer* as well: only that which cannot be done in advance may override Shabbat. Since according to Rebbi the grain is *kasher bedi'avad* even if cut at other times, the cutting can in principle be performed in advance, and therefore it does not override Shabbat. This is entirely consistent with Rabbi Akiva's principle that anything which can be done before Shabbat must be done before Shabbat.

### Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי תַּנּוּר מְקַדֵּשׁ? וְהָתַנְיָא: כִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁחִיטָה. כֵּיצַד? שְׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן לִשְׁמָן – קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָן לִשְׁמָן, וְזָרַק דָּמָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – הַלֶּחֶם קָדוֹשׁ וְאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחוֹט לִשְׁמָן וְיִזְרוֹק דָּמָן לִשְׁמָן. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הוּקְבְּעוּ וְלֹא הוּקְבְּעוּ קָא אָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi really **hold** that the **oven consecrates** that which is baked inside it? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The communal peace offering of two **lambs** that accompanies the two loaves sacrificed on the festival of *Shavuot* **consecrate the loaves** that accompany them **only by** means of their **slaughter. How so?** If **one slaughtered** the lambs **for their** own **sake,** i.e., as lambs for *Shavuot* in the appropriate manner, **and** the priest **sprinkled their blood for their** own **sake, the loaves are consecrated.** The *baraita* continues: But if **one slaughtered them not for their** own **sake, and** the priest **sprinkled their blood not for their** own **sake, the loaves are not consecrated,** as the factors indispensable in rendering the offering fit were not properly performed. If **one slaughtered them for their** own **sake and he sprinkled their blood not for their** own **sake,** the fact that the lambs were properly slaughtered renders the loaves partially consecrated. Therefore, **the loaves are consecrated** to the extent that they cannot be redeemed, **but they are not consecrated** to the extent that they may be eaten. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: Actually, the loaves are consecrated only when one slaughters** the offerings **for their** own **sake and sprinkles their blood for their** own **sake,** i.e., only if both factors indispensable in rendering the offering fit were properly performed. Apparently, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that it is the slaughter of the lambs that consecrates the loaves, not baking the loaves in the oven. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** Actually, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi does hold that the oven consecrates the loaves. What the *baraita* **is saying** when it uses the categories of consecrated and not consecrated with regard to the slaughter of the lambs is that either the two loaves **are fixed** as *Shavuot* meal offerings and cannot be used for any other offering, **or** they **are not fixed** as *Shavuot* meal offerings. In other words, if the lambs were slaughtered and the blood sprinkled in the proper manner, one cannot change the designation of the loaves, whereas if they were not slaughtered and their blood was not sprinkled properly, one can change their designation. If the lambs were slaughtered properly but their blood was sprinkled improperly, the status of the loaves is subject to a dispute. This has nothing to do with when the loaves are consecrated.

The deeper point that the *Gemara* has been building toward throughout this entire discussion is a precise and remarkable principle: in the *Mikdash*, even for a *mitzvah* that is designated for a specific day, even when the full context of *Beit HaMikdash* *avodah* is operative, one may not initiate a Shabbat violation at the start of a process if that process could have been commenced without violating Shabbat. Only where there is truly no alternative — as with baking the *Shtei HaLechem*, which would be invalidated if done the day before — may one begin the process on Shabbat. Once a process has been legitimately initiated through an act that overrides Shabbat — such as the *shechita* of a *korban* — the entire completion of that *korban* may follow on Shabbat. But to start fresh on Shabbat when an alternative existed: that the *Gemara*, at least in Rebbi's view, does not permit.

It remains worth noting that this conclusion could have been avoided more simply: one might have argued that grain cut before the sixteenth is by definition not *omer* grain at all — just as *milah* before the eighth day is entirely invalid regardless of whether it would be valid later — and therefore there was never any alternative to cutting on the sixteenth itself. The *Gemara* does not take this path, and its insistence on deriving Rebbi's position through the logic of Shabbat-avoidance, with all of its attendant complexity, is what makes this a particularly striking *sugya*.

*Hadran alach Rabbi Yishmael* — with this we conclude the chapter, and we will begin a new *perek* on Shabbat.
