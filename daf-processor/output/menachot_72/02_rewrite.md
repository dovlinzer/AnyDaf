# Menachot 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fodder Cutting Rules

### Fodder Cutting

We pick up on the bottom of 72a, completing a brief carry-over from the previous session. The discussion had centered on what types of cutting are permitted before the *omer*, and specifically on the case of cutting grain as *shachat* — fodder for animals. The *Mishnah* debated whether such cutting must be performed when the grain is less than one-third ripe (clearly animal food) or even when it has reached one-third ripeness. This question was then connected to a parallel ruling of Rabbi Meir regarding *pe'ah*.

### Rabbi Meir & Pe'ah

Rabbi Meir holds that if one cuts for fodder in the middle of a field in a spotted pattern — the practice called *menamér* — it divides the field into separate sections for purposes of *pe'ah*, since it is not considered the beginning of a harvest but rather a different type of cutting. The *Gemara* is attempting to identify precisely what Rabbi Meir holds, because our *Mishnah* emphasized that the cutting must be done explicitly for animal consumption, whereas Rabbi Meir did not specify this. Since there was apparently a category of fodder that could also serve human purposes — where one is interested not in the grain itself but in the straw or the like — Rabbi Meir's position appears to diverge from the *Mishnah*'s framing.

### Rav Dimi Report

The *Gemara* now moves to identify the source of Rabbi Meir's view. The relevant passage begins with the word *tani*, nine lines from the bottom of the page. *Ela ki ata Rav Dimi* — when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported: *Rebbi Meir beshitat Rebbi Akiva rabbo amrah* — Rabbi Meir followed the position of Rabbi Akiva, his teacher. It should be noted that Rabbi Akiva was in fact the *rebbi* of all the major *Tannaim* of the *Mishnah* — Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Shimon — they were all *talmidei Rebbi Akiva*.

What is Rabbi Akiva's position? *De'afilu adam nami la havei ketzirah* — even if one cuts *shachat* for human consumption, it is not considered a harvesting. The source is the *Mishnah* dealing with *hamenamer sadeh* — one who "leopards" a field, cutting it in spots. If *kelachim* — stalks that are not yet ready for harvest — are left standing, the implication is that what was harvested was also not fully ripe, and therefore this was effectively a fodder cutting. Rabbi Akiva rules: *pe'ah lechol echad ve'echad* — each remaining section is treated as a separate field for *pe'ah*, meaning the cutting is not considered an act of harvesting that unifies the field. The *chachamim* disagree: *me'echad al hakol* — it is still one field and a single *pe'ah* suffices.

This ruling was qualified, however, by a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *lo chiyev Rebbi Akiva ela bemenamer lekaliyot* — Rabbi Akiva's ruling applies only when the spotting was done for *kaliyot*, roasted grain, which according to Rashi means the grain had not yet reached one-third ripeness. *Aval bemenamer le'otzar lo* — but when one is cutting for *otzar*, storage, which implies the grain is already ripe, Rabbi Akiva would agree that it constitutes the beginning of harvest and the field remains unified.

### Ravin's Report

The *Gemara* challenges this: is it truly the case that everyone agrees that cutting for storage does not divide the field? *Ve-ha ki ata Ravin amar Rabbi Yochanan* — when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mechayev hayah Rabbi Meir af be-mana le-otzar* — Rabbi Meir maintained his position even when cutting for storage, even when the grain is ripe. The *Gemara* resolves this: *savar lah kavateih be-chada u-falig aleih be-chada* — Rabbi Meir agrees with Rabbi Akiva in one respect but disagrees in another. Rabbi Akiva went so far as to say that any fodder cutting, regardless of the grain's state of ripeness, is a non-harvesting act. Rabbi Meir agrees when the grain is less than one-third ripe, but does not go as far as Rabbi Akiva when the grain is already ripe.

The practical upshot, to wrap up this thread, is that cutting for fodder is not considered a harvesting and may therefore be done before the *omer* is cut. The question of how precisely to define "for fodder" — whether by the cutter's intention, by the objective ripeness of the grain, or by whether it is destined for animals or humans — is exactly what these various positions have been working out.

## Mitzvah Harvesting

### Cutting Purposes

We now continue with a new but related topic, at the top of 72b. The *Mishnah* states: *ha-kotzer mipnei ha-netiot u-mipnei beit ha-avel u-mipnei beit ha-midrash* — one who harvests on account of saplings, on account of a mourning house, or on account of a study hall. These are types of harvesting that are permitted before the *omer*.

What do these cases mean? *Mipnei ha-netiot* refers to a situation where grain is growing together with tree shoots, creating a *kilayim* problem, and one cuts the grain down so that the prohibited mixture should not persist. *Beit ha-avel u-beit ha-midrash* — one wishes to clear space in a wheat field so that mourners may gather there after a funeral, sitting in an open *chatzer*, or so that a group may convene for study. In these cases, the person is not interested in the grain as grain; the goal is to clear the space. Since this is not a harvesting for eating purposes but for a *mitzvah* purpose, it is permitted before the *omer*.

### Talmud Rationale

*Mai ta'ama* — what is the reason? The *Gemara* answers with elegant brevity: *ketzirchem amar Rachmana ve-lo ketzir mitzvah* — the verse says "the first of *your* cuttings" (*reshit ketzirchem*), implying that the prohibition on cutting before the *omer* applies to cuttings that serve one's own mundane purposes, but not to cuttings performed for the sake of a *mitzvah*, for a divine purpose.

This type of *derashah* is familiar from elsewhere. A parallel principle appears on *Erev Shabbat*: *cheftzecha asur cheftzei shamayim muttarim* — your own affairs are restricted on Shabbat, but affairs of Heaven are permitted. This is why a *shul* may make announcements about events scheduled for after Shabbat. Another parallel is *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah*, derived from the verse by *Keri'at Shema* — *u-ve-lechtecha va-derech*, when you go about your own purposes, implying that when you are engaged in *mitzvah* purposes, the exemption applies.

That said, one could argue that the *mitzvah* framing is not even strictly necessary in our case. Since the person has no interest in obtaining the grain — the goal is simply to clear the space or eliminate *kilayim* — one might argue that it does not qualify as a "cutting" in the relevant sense at all, regardless of whether it is a *mitzvah*. The exemption may follow simply from the fact that this is not a grain-harvesting act. Both rationales are at work here.

One might also raise the question — and it is a genuinely interesting one — of whether it would matter if someone cut grain entirely for eating but then donated the entire yield to the poor. Would that transform it into a *mitzvah* cutting? The answer likely depends on which of the two rationales one emphasizes. Similarly, if the field were owned by *hekdesh* — by the *Beit HaMikdash* — the grain would not be "yours" in the relevant sense, and there might be grounds to permit cutting on an entirely different basis. These questions are not addressed by the *Gemara* here.

As for whether the prohibition on *katzirah* applies *b'zman ha-zeh* — today when there is no *omer* — one would presume there is no *issur katzirah* at all, since the entire prohibition of cutting is defined by the *omer's* status as *reshit ketzirchem*. The *issur chadash* on eating new grain does apply today — the sixteenth of Nisan is the time that permits it even without an actual *omer* being brought — but the cutting prohibition, which is entirely a function of defining the *omer* as the first cutting, presumably has no application *b'zman ha-zeh*.

### No Bundles

The *Gemara* adds one qualification: *v'lo ya'aseh krichot ela manicham tzvatim* — even when these various pre-*omer* cuttings are permitted, one should not make formal bundles out of the cut grain; rather, one should leave them in small informal piles. *D'chama d'efshar lo tarchinan* — as much as possible, we do not invest effort in the full harvesting process before the *omer*. Some things are necessary and we permit them, but we do not want the full harvesting apparatus to be set in motion before the *omer* is brought.

## Omer Source Rules

### Standing Grain

*Mitzvat haOmer l'havi min hakama* — the *mitzvah* of the *omer* ideally requires that it be brought from standing grain: one harvests grain that is still in the field, specifically for the sake of the *omer*. This is the optimal fulfillment. However, the *Mishnah* goes on to rule that if standing grain was not cut for the *omer* — whether permissibly or not — and one has already-cut grain on hand, that grain may be used.

### Alternatives

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: *v'im takriv minchat bikurim* — the verse "if you bring a *minchah* of the first fruit," understood here as referring to the *omer* (and not to the *Shtei HaLechem*). The verse teaches that the ideal *mitzvah* is to bring it from standing grain. How do we know that if standing grain cannot be found one may bring from sheaves, from already-cut grain? *Talmud lomar takriv* — the repetition of the word *takriv* at the end of the verse ("you shall bring") teaches: just bring it, regardless of how you obtained it.

*Davar acher* — another application of the same verse: the *omer* ideally should be fresh. How do we know that if fresh grain cannot be found, dry grain may be used? *Talmud lomar takriv* — just bring it. Another *davar acher*: since the *mitzvah* is to cut the *omer* at night — as this creates *temimot*, ensuring the count of seven full weeks begins from the night — how do we know it is valid if cut during the day? *Talmud lomar takriv*.

Before arriving at that last point, the *baraita* also teaches that the *omer* overrides Shabbat: *talmud lomar takriv*, bring it under any circumstances. *Takriv kol shehu* — bring whatever you can find, even grain still in sheaves. *Takriv mikol makom* — Rashi understands this phrase literally: from any location in Eretz Yisrael, not only from the fields near Yerushalayim. *Takriv afilu b'Shabbat* — bring it even if it is Shabbat; and by extension, *takriv afilu b'tumah* — bring it even if there is a *tumah* problem, since this is a fixed-time communal sacrifice and such sacrifices override Shabbat.

## Day Cutting Debate

### Megilla Mishna

The *baraita* has stated that if the *omer* was cut during the day it is *kasher*. This is challenged from a *Mishnah* in Megilla that lists *mitzvot* categorized by when they may be performed. The *Mishnah* there states: *kol halaila kasher l'ktzirat ha'omer* — the entire night is valid for cutting the *omer* — and *u'lehaktir chalavin ve'evarim* — and for burning the fats and limbs of a *korban*. The *Mishnah* there then articulates a general principle: *davar shemitzva'to bayom kasher kol hayom* — anything whose *mitzvah* is by day may be done the entire day; *davar shemitzva'to balaila kasher kol halaila* — anything whose *mitzvah* is by night may be done the entire night.

The *Gemara* draws an inference: *katani laila dumya d'yom* — the night category is presented as parallel to the day category. Just as day *mitzvot* may not be performed at night — one cannot take a *lulav* at night — so too night *mitzvot* may not be performed during the day: *af laila bayom nami lo*. If the cutting of the *omer* is a night *mitzvah*, it follows that it cannot be done during the day. How then can our *Mishnah* rule that it is *kasher* if cut during the day?

A subtle but important question arises: does the mention of "day" in our *Mishnah* refer to the day before, or to the day after the night of cutting? Tosafot clarifies that since the night of cutting is the earliest valid time — the day before is clearly too early — the question must concern the day after. The *Mishnah* in Megilla, with its *diyuk* that night *mitzvot* are restricted to the night, would then exclude the following day as well.

The *Gemara* is aware that one might argue that night *mitzvot* are rare and perhaps begin at night but continue into the day. The *Gemara* declines to read the *Mishnah* in Megilla that way. The question then is how we rule (*pasken*) — and the answer, as the *Gemara* will show, is that this is a dispute between *Tannaim*.

As for the practical rationale for cutting the *omer* at night: while it may not have been logistically simple, the *Gemara* derives it from *temimot* — the requirement for seven complete weeks — which demands that the count begin from the night. Tosafot, notably, connects this entire question to *Sefirat HaOmer* itself: if the ruling of the *Mishnah* in Megilla is normative — meaning the *omer* is exclusively a night *mitzvah* and invalid if done during the day — then *bedi'avad* one cannot fulfill *Sefirat HaOmer* during the day either. Tosafot acknowledges that missing a day does not disqualify one from continuing to count, but the principle that the count is a night *mitzvah* is what he derives from here.

### Rava's Resolution

Rava resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya, ha Rebbi v'ha Rebbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon* — there is no difficulty; one source reflects the position of Rebbi and the other reflects the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon. The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* that makes their dispute explicit.

### Tumah Case

The *baraita* presents a case: a *kohen* was in the process of offering the *Minchat HaOmer* when it became *tamei* just before being placed on the *mizbe'ach*. What is to be done? *Im yesh acheres* — if other grain is available, *omer lo havei acheres tachte'ha* — he is told to go and bring another *omer*; do not use the *tamei* one. *Ve'im lav* — but if it is not possible to obtain replacement grain in time, *omer lo havei pike'ach ushtok* — he is told to be prudent and keep it to himself — that is, proceed with the *tamei* grain without drawing attention to the fact.

This is the position of Rebbi. Rashi explains the instruction to remain quiet as a concern about misleading people: if it becomes known that a *tamei* *minchah* was offered, observers might conclude that *minchos* in general may be brought in a state of *tumah*, without realizing that this applies specifically to the *Minchat HaOmer* as a fixed-time communal *korban*. It is somewhat unclear why this concern is more acute here than in other cases of *tumah* in communal sacrifices — the *omer* ceremony is quite distinct from a private *minchah* — but that is Rashi's reasoning.

The position of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon differs sharply: *bein kach uvein kach, omer lo havei pike'ach ushtok* — regardless of whether replacement grain is available or not, he is told to proceed quietly with the current grain. The reason: *shekol haomer sheniktzar shelo kemitzvaso pasul* — any *omer* that was cut not in accordance with its proper manner is invalid. Going back to the field during the day would produce grain cut *shelo kemitzvasah* — and since the cutting was done at the wrong time, the grain would be *pasul*. According to Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, the *mitzvah* requires cutting specifically at night, and grain cut during the day cannot serve as an *omer* at all.

This is the crux of the dispute: according to Rebbi, grain cut during the day is *kasher bedi'avad*, so one should attempt to obtain valid grain; according to Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon, daytime cutting is *pasul*, so there is no point in trying.

One might ask whether the *Mishnah* in Megilla — which treats cutting the *omer* as a night *mitzvah* exclusively — could still be distinguished from the question of whether the grain is *kasher bedi'avad* for the *korban*. One could theoretically hold that the *mitzvah* of cutting was not fulfilled and yet the grain remains usable for the offering. The *Gemara's* framing, however, places the question squarely in terms of *shelo kemitzvaso*: the debate is not merely about whether the *mitzvah* was properly performed but about whether the grain cut outside the designated time is *pasul* for the *korban* itself.

A further question the *Gemara* leaves open: according to Rebbi, who holds that *bedi'avad* daytime cutting is valid, how should one prioritize among the various *bedi'avad* alternatives the *baraita* listed — from sheaves rather than standing grain, dry rather than fresh, day rather than night? The degrees of *bedi'avad* are not ranked.

The *Gemara* then traces the intellectual genealogy of Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's position. *Rabbah bar bar Chanah* said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon b'shitat Rabbi Akiva rabo shel aviv amrah* — Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon stated his position following the approach of Rabbi Akiva, who was the teacher of his father. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai — whose yahrtzeit falls around this time of year, at Lag BaOmer — was a student of Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Elazar is his son.

## Shabbat Override

### Akiva Principle

The *Gemara* now transitions to the question of whether the *omer* overrides Shabbat. We established from the *baraita* that it does, but the precise reasoning requires careful analysis. The *Gemara* invokes a principle of Rabbi Akiva: *kol melachah she'efshar lah l'asot m'erev Shabbos einah docha es haShabbos* — any *melachah* that could have been performed before Shabbat does not override Shabbat. Rabbi Akiva articulated this in the context of *milah* and its *machshirim* — preparatory acts like sharpening the scalpel or transporting the knife, which could have been done before Shabbat and therefore should not be done on Shabbat itself.

The *Gemara* now applies this principle to the *omer*. If the 16th of Nisan falls on Shabbat, why do we cut the *omer* on Shabbat? If it would be *kasher bedi'avad* if cut earlier, why not cut it before Shabbat? The obvious answer would seem to be that the *mitzvah* is specifically to cut it on *mimacharat haShabbat* — the sixteenth — and therefore even if an earlier cutting would be *kasher bedi'avad*, the *mitzvah* demands it be done on Shabbat, just as the *mitzvah* of *milah* on the eighth day overrides Shabbat precisely because the *mitzvah* is tied to that day.

### Elazar's Argument

Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon provides what the *Gemara* treats as a compelling proof for his position. *Ve-i salka da'atach niktzar she-lo ke-mitzvato kasher* — if one were to say that grain cut at the wrong time (*shelo kemitzvaso*) is still *kasher*, then *amai dachi Shabbat*? Why would the cutting of the *omer* override Shabbat at all? One could simply cut it before Shabbat — not on *erev Shabbat* itself which is *Yom Tov*, but two days before — and it would be valid. *Ella mi-de-dachi Shabbat* — since we know the cutting does override Shabbat, the implication is that cutting it at any other time would be invalid. And if it is invalid when done too early, it must equally be invalid when done too late — specifically, when cut during the day rather than at night.

This argument has real force, but it is not without counterarguments. One might distinguish between "too early" and "too late" — the daytime of the same *halachic* day is not obviously comparable to a completely different day. Nevertheless, this is Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon's position, and the *Gemara* treats it as a serious argument.

### Shimon Counterargument

The *Gemara* then notes that Rebbi was also a student of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, establishing this through a *baraita* in which Rebbi himself describes how they would carry oil and towels from courtyard to roof to *karfif* on Shabbat in order to reach the spring where Rabbi Shimon would bathe. (This incidentally demonstrates that rooftops, courtyards, and enclosed yards were considered a unified domain for purposes of carrying — a matter that will be treated extensively in Eruvin.) The point here is simply to confirm that Rebbi was a direct student of Rabbi Shimon, and therefore the same chain of tradition — Rabbi Akiva → Rabbi Shimon → Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon — also reaches Rebbi.

Why then does Rebbi not adopt the same conclusion? The *Gemara* answers: *savar lah ke-idach de-Rabbi Shimon* — Rebbi follows a different ruling of Rabbi Shimon himself. *Dé-tanya*, in a *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon says, come and see how dear is a *mitzvah* in its proper time — *bо u-re'eh kama chaviva mitzvah be-sha'atah*. For example, one is permitted to burn the innards and fats of *korbanot* the entire night, and yet we do not wait until night; when a *korban* is brought on Shabbat, the burning of the *imurim* is done on Shabbat itself even though it could technically be deferred to the night. Why? Because it is important to perform the *mitzvah* at its proper time rather than postpone it. And this is exactly the logic that applies to cutting the *omer*: even if it would be *kasher bedi'avad* if cut earlier or later, the right time is the night of the sixteenth, and therefore it overrides Shabbat.

This is also precisely what distinguishes the *omer* from the preparatory acts of *milah* — sharpening the scalpel, transporting the knife. Those have no *mitzvah* component that designates Shabbat as the correct time to perform them. There is no reason to do them on Shabbat. Here, by contrast, the *mitzvah* is to cut the *omer* at night on the sixteenth, and that designation is reason enough to override Shabbat.

### Rebbi Position

Given such a compelling argument, why does Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon not accept it? *Lo shmiah lei* — the *Gemara* says he had not heard of this principle of his father regarding performing *mitzvot* at their proper time.

But Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon has a rejoinder of his own: *dachsah shechitah es hashabbos* — the case of burning the *imurim* on Shabbat is not comparable. In that case, one began with a license to violate Shabbat: the slaughter and sprinkling of blood of the *korban* were already performed on Shabbat — and once the initial *avodah* is underway, it follows naturally that one completes the *korban* on the same day. The burning of the fats overrides Shabbat only because the process was already set in motion by acts that themselves legitimately overrode Shabbat. From there, one cannot derive that one may *initiate* a violation of Shabbat for a *mitzvah* that could alternatively have been performed without any Shabbat violation at all.

### Evidence Review

Given the force of this counterargument, the *Gemara* turns to an even more striking conclusion: *kasavar Rebbi lo dachya shabbos* — the *Gemara* posits that Rebbi holds the cutting of the *omer* does *not* override Shabbat. This requires explaining away an array of evidence. Our *Mishnah* states that whether on Shabbat or the weekday the *omer* is cut — *d'lo k'Rebbe*, this is not Rebbi's view. Another *Mishnah* (involving the three-person procedure with three baskets and three sickles, performed whether on Shabbat or the weekday) — also *d'lo k'Rebbe*. Another *Mishnah* depicting the public ceremony in which the cutter asks "shall I cut on Shabbat?" and the gathered crowd responds affirmatively — *d'lo k'Rebbe*.

This is a striking turn. The *Gemara* is creating an entirely new position — that Rebbi holds the *omer* does not override Shabbat — which is not stated anywhere explicitly, but follows logically from the intersection of his rulings: that *bedi'avad* daytime cutting is *kasher*, and that anything which can be performed without violating Shabbat must be performed without violating Shabbat.

### Rebbi Teaching

Adding to the complexity, the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in the name of Rebbi himself: *Rebbi* derives from the verse *vayidaber Moshe et mo'adei Hashem* — "Moses spoke the appointed times of God to Israel" — that the *omer* and the *Shtei HaLechem* with their associated *korbanot* override Shabbat and *tumah*. The source for communal fixed-time *korbanot* generally overriding Shabbat comes from the verse *eleh ta'asu la-Hashem b'mo'adeichem* in *Parashat Pinchas*, which establishes *mo'ed* as a fixed-time designation analogous to the *Tamid* and the *Pesach* — both of which are explicitly stated to override Shabbat and *tumah* via the word *b'mo'ado*, "in its appointed time, even on Shabbat, even in *tumah*." The *omer* and *Shtei HaLechem*, however, appear in *Parashat Emor* and are not included in the *Pinchas* passage; the concluding verse of *Emor* — *vayidaber Moshe et mo'adei Hashem* — thus extends the override to these as well.

### Baking Logic

The *Gemara* now presses: *l'mai* — to what *melachah* does Rebbi's ruling refer? If overriding Shabbat means bringing the *omer* or *Shtei HaLechem* as a *korban* on the *mizbe'ach*, the *Shtei HaLechem* cannot be burned on the *mizbe'ach* since it is *chametz*. So the Shabbat override must refer to preparation. But what preparation of the *Shtei HaLechem* cannot be done before Shabbat?

The *Gemara* answers: *le-afiyah* — the baking. Everything else — grinding, sifting — could be done beforehand. Only the baking cannot, because — *ve-savar Rebbi tanur mekadesh* — Rebbi holds that the *tanur*, the oven, sanctifies the bread. Once baked, the bread is *kadosh*, and if it were to remain overnight it would become *pasul bi-linah*. Therefore, baking on the day before would produce bread that was already invalid by the time it was needed. Since there is literally no alternative but to bake on the day itself, the baking overrides Shabbat.

By analogy, this clarifies Rebbi's position on the *omer* as well: only that which cannot be done in advance may override Shabbat. Since according to Rebbi the grain is *kasher bedi'avad* even if cut at other times, the cutting can in principle be performed in advance, and therefore it does not override Shabbat. This is entirely consistent with Rabbi Akiva's principle that anything which can be done before Shabbat must be done before Shabbat.

### Sanctification

The *Gemara* then challenges the premise that Rebbi holds the *tanur* sanctifies: *ve-hatanya, kivshei atzeret ein makdishin et halechem ela bishchita* — a *baraita* teaches that the lambs of *Shavuot* sanctify the *Shtei HaLechem* through their slaughter. The *baraita* details the various combinations: if both the *shechita* and the *zerikah* were performed *l'shman* — for the proper purpose — the bread is sanctified. If both were done *shelo l'shman*, it is not sanctified. If the *shechita* was *l'shman* but the *zerikah shelo l'shman*, Rebbi says it is in an intermediate state — *kadosh ve'eino kadosh* — partly sanctified. Rabbi Elazar ben Rabbi Shimon holds there is no sanctification at all until both stages are performed correctly: *le'olam eino kadosh ad sheyishchat l'shman v'yizrok daman l'shman*.

This seems to contradict the claim that the *tanur* sanctifies — if slaughter sanctifies, how can baking also sanctify?

Rav Nachman resolves this: *hukva v'lo hukva ka'amar* — the language "sanctifies" as applied to the slaughter was imprecise. What the slaughter of the lambs does is *hukva* — it establishes and binds the lambs and the bread as a unified entity. The actual sanctity — from which the bread cannot be redeemed, and after which it becomes *pasul* if left overnight — was conferred already at the moment of baking in the oven. The sprinkling of the blood then renders the bread permissible for consumption. The three stages — baking, slaughter, and *zerikah* — are thus distinguished: the *tanur* confers *kedushah*, the *shechita* establishes the link between the lambs and the loaves, and the *zerikah* permits eating.

The deeper point that the *Gemara* has been building toward throughout this entire discussion is a precise and remarkable principle: in the *Mikdash*, even for a *mitzvah* that is designated for a specific day, even when the full context of *Beit HaMikdash* *avodah* is operative, one may not initiate a Shabbat violation at the start of a process if that process could have been commenced without violating Shabbat. Only where there is truly no alternative — as with baking the *Shtei HaLechem*, which would be invalidated if done the day before — may one begin the process on Shabbat. Once a process has been legitimately initiated through an act that overrides Shabbat — such as the *shechita* of a *korban* — the entire completion of that *korban* may follow on Shabbat. But to start fresh on Shabbat when an alternative existed: that the *Gemara*, at least in Rebbi's view, does not permit.

It remains worth noting that this conclusion could have been avoided more simply: one might have argued that grain cut before the sixteenth is by definition not *omer* grain at all — just as *milah* before the eighth day is entirely invalid regardless of whether it would be valid later — and therefore there was never any alternative to cutting on the sixteenth itself. The *Gemara* does not take this path, and its insistence on deriving Rebbi's position through the logic of Shabbat-avoidance, with all of its attendant complexity, is what makes this a particularly striking *sugya*.

*Hadran alach Rabbi Yishmael* — with this we conclude the chapter, and we will begin a new *perek* on Shabbat.
