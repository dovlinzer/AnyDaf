# Menachot 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bikkurim Debate Resolved

### RY vs RL: Bikkurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, קַשְׁיָא? תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: שֶׁבְּגַג וְשֶׁבְּחוּרְבָּה – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא, שֶׁבְּעָצִיץ וְשֶׁבִּסְפִינָה – אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** Having reconciled all the apparent contradictions between the two *baraitot* according to the opinion of Reish Lakish, the Gemara asks: **But according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who holds inferior produce cannot be consecrated as first fruits, the first *baraita* is **difficult,** as it states that inferior produce can be consecrated. The Gemara answers: The *baraitot* cited above contradict Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion. But there **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** with regard to this issue, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to produce **that** grew **on a roof, or that** grew **in a ruin,** the owner **brings** it **and recites** the accompanying passage. With regard to produce **that** grew **in a flowerpot, or that** grew **on a ship,** the owner **does not bring** it **at all.** Rabbi Yoḥanan holds in accordance with that *baraita*.

The daf opens at its very first word with the continuation of a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and *reish lakish* concerning the validity of bikkurim that come from less than ideal growing conditions. The question was whether such bikkurim are patur bedi'avad — that is, whether they fail to discharge the obligation even after the fact. *reish lakish* maintained that they are valid bedi'avad, drawing an analogy to a scrawny animal. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, held that they are not valid, invoking a special pasuk and arguing that the nature of bikkurim demands something more — that the fruit represent the genuine choice of one's produce, grown under fitting conditions.

The dispute was complicated by two conflicting baraitot. One braita ruled that bikkurim brought from rooftops and boats are valid and require the korei, the formal declaration — which is to say the bringer is biblically obligated. The other braita ruled that one brings but does not make the korei, implying only a rabbinic obligation at most. *reish lakish* could resolve this readily: the case where one brings and makes the korei reflects his position that bikkurim from non-ideal conditions are valid, while the case where one does not make the korei refers to produce grown on an ordinary rooftop — not a cave roof — meaning it is not considered to be rooted in the ground of Eretz Yisrael at all, and thus falls below even the threshold of bedi'avad validity.

### Tannai Hi Resolution


The difficulty, as the Gemara opens on 85a, is for Rabbi Yochanan: ela le'Rabbi Yochanan kashia. How does he account for the braita that says one brings from a rooftop and makes the full declaration? The Gemara's answer is tannai hi — it is a Tannaitic dispute. Rabbi Yochanan cannot escape the braita, but he aligns with those tannaim who invalidate bikkurim from non-ideal conditions. The braita that validates them reflects a dissenting tannaitic view.

### Rooftop Bikkurim Cases


The Gemara then cites a braita that refines the picture further. *shebegag shebachur vemevi vekorei* — from a cave roof and from an open area, one brings and makes the declaration, even though these are non-ideal growing conditions. However, shebakis shebesfina eino mevi kol ikkar — when it comes from a flowerpot on a boat, one cannot bring bikkurim at all. The reason is either that such pots have no holes and the produce is not considered to be growing from the land of Eretz Yisrael, or that these growing conditions are so profoundly substandard that even bedi'avad the bikkurim would not be acceptable in any sense.

### Bikkurim vs. Menachot


The upshot is as follows. *reish lakish* holds that bikkurim are valid bedi'avad even from non-ideal conditions. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. The baraitot that appear to support *reish lakish* reflect a dissenting tannaitic opinion, and even those tannaim who permit bikkurim from non-ideal conditions would distinguish between varying degrees of inadequacy.

This stands in striking contrast to what the previous Mishnah had established regarding flour for menachot. Throughout the entire discussion of grain quality, there was never a suggestion that the offering is invalid bedi'avad. The only debate about bedi'avad invalidity arose in the context of bikkurim — and this is understandable, since bikkurim by their very concept must represent the choicest of one's produce. As was noted, this does not necessarily mean the fruit itself must be at its physical peak — the very first fruits of the season are sometimes somewhat scrawny — but it does mean the produce must represent the best of what one's land can offer, grown under proper conditions. The Mishnah concludes accordingly: vekulan ein ba'in ela min hamuvchar — all of these offerings come only from the finest sources. Yet the implication — and the next Mishnah will make it explicit — is that if they are not ideal, they are still valid bedi'avad.

## Straw to Ofrayim Aggada

### Afurayim Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן אֵינָן בָּאוֹת אֶלָּא מִן הַמּוּבְחָר כּוּ׳. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ יוֹחָנָא וּמַמְרֵא לְמֹשֶׁה: תֶּבֶן אַתָּה מַכְנִיס לְעֶפְרַיִים! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי ״לְמָתָא יַרְקָא – יַרְקָא שְׁקוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And all** meal offerings **come only from the optimal** produce. One of the places the mishna mentions as having good-quality produce is Aforayim. The superior quality of its produce was so well known that Aforayim was used as an example in colloquial aphorisms. In Moses and Aaron's first meeting with Pharaoh, Aaron cast his staff to the ground, whereupon it turned into a serpent. Pharaoh's necromancers then duplicated the feat using their incantations, only to then be confounded when Aaron's staff swallowed up all of theirs (see Exodus 7:10–12). The Gemara relates the conversation that took place: Pharaoh's two leading necromancers, **Yoḥana and Mamre, said to Moses:** Are **you bringing straw to Afarayim?** Performing necromancy in Egypt, the world leader in sorcery, is like bringing straw to Afarayim, which is rich in the finest grains. Moses **said to them:** It is as **people say: To a city** rich in **herbs, take herbs.** If you want to guarantee that people will appreciate your merchandise, bring it to a place where they are familiar with it.

Among the places named in the Mishnah as premier sources of solet, Michmas and Zanoach are listed as the top-grade sources, with Afurayim ranked just beneath them — the second-finest wheat. This mention of Afurayim gives the Gemara occasion for an aggadic digression.

### Yochna & Mamre Taunt


Amru lei Yochna u-Mamre le-Moshe — two figures named Yochna and Mamre, identified from context as *chartumei mitzrayim*, Egyptian sorcerers whom Moshe had to contend with, said to him: teven ata machnis l'Ofrayim — you are bringing straw to Ofrayim. Rashi, it should be noted, cites the phrase as l'Mitzrayim in his version, though the substance is the same. The taunt is the equivalent of "coals to Newcastle." Since Afurayim was renowned as a source of abundant grain and wheat, it was naturally a place overflowing with straw as well. The sorcerers were mocking Moshe: who needs your tricks and wonders here? You are bringing what we already have in abundance.

### Moshe's Comeback


Moshe replied: amar lehu — amri inshi, l'mata yarka yarka shakil — people say, in the city of vegetables, that is precisely where you bring your vegetables. There is a market for the finest goods precisely where people know quality. As one of the commentators explains, in a place where everyone is buying vegetables, the best vegetables rise to the top. Moshe's point, then, is: precisely here, where Egypt is full of sorcery, is where it will become clear whose power is genuine. If what I do surpasses what you are doing in your own domain, that will prove beyond doubt that what I perform is not sorcery at all, but the hand of God.

## Grain Quality & Crop Rot…

### Non-Ideal Fields Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מְבִיאִין לֹא מִבֵּית הַזְּבָלִים, וְלֹא מִבֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִים, וְלֹא מִבֵּית הָאִילָן, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Even when selecting grain for meal offerings from the locations mentioned in the previous mishna, **one may not bring** as a meal offering grain **from a fertilized field, nor from an irrigated field, nor from a field of trees,** as such fields do not produce grain of optimal quality. **But if** one **did bring** a meal offering of grain from such fields, it is **fit.**

The Gemara now turns to the next Mishnah, which makes explicit what had only been implied before: bedi'avad, if one has not brought ideal flour, the offering is still valid. *ein mevi'im lo mibeit hazevalim* — one may not bring flour from grain grown in a manured field. Rashi offers two explanations for why manure disqualifies the grain. His first explanation is that the field requires external fertilization and thus did not grow sufficiently on its own — suggesting the grain is of lesser quality. His second explanation is that shezevela mav'isha umekalkel ta'am haperot — heavy dung affects the taste and quality of what grows in it.

V'lo mibeit hashlachim — and not from a field that is heavily irrigated. The underlying principle is that if the grain cannot flourish on natural rainfall alone, it is not considered of the highest quality. Velo midei ilan — and not from a grain field that had trees growing within it, since the competition between grain and trees means neither is receiving the full nourishment it needs. It is worth noting that trees interspersed with grain need not constitute kilayim if they are spaced appropriately or otherwise configured to avoid the prohibition; the concern here is purely one of grain quality.

Ve'im hevi kasher — but if one brought from any of these, it is valid bedi'avad. This is the clear contrast with the bikkurim debate: for menachot, even grain from these non-ideal conditions will discharge the obligation after the fact.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot Scope


A significant disagreement between Rashi and Tosafot concerns the scope of this Mishnah's requirements. Rashi reads the Mishnah — possibly influenced by its plural language and its placement at the end of the previous perek — as referring specifically to the communal menachot, namely the Omer and the *shtei halechem*. These offerings are akin to bikkurim in character: they represent the choice of the crops on behalf of the entire community, and therefore the higher standards apply to them in particular. Tosafot, by contrast, understands the Mishnah to be speaking of grain for all menachot without exception, not limiting the requirements to the Omer and *shtei halechem*.

This ambiguity persists throughout the subsequent discussion and is not fully resolved. Every source that might seem to settle the question carries some element that reintroduces uncertainty.

### Nira & 70-Day Planting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? נָרָהּ שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, וּבְשָׁנָה שְׁנִיָּה זוֹרְעָהּ קוֹדֶם לַפֶּסַח שִׁבְעִים יוֹם, וְהִוא עוֹשָׂה סוֹלֶת מְרוּבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **How does one produce** optimal-quality grain? **He plows** the field during the **first year,** but he does not sow it, **and in the second year, he sows it seventy days before Passover, and** in that manner **it produces** grain that will provide **an abundance of fine,** high-quality, **flour.**

The Mishnah then describes the ideal method for producing the finest grain. Nira hashana — the first year, one overturns the soil but leaves the field fallow. The word nir does not mean merely leaving the field unplanted; it means actively turning over the soil. *uvashana shniya* — in the second year, zar'ah kodem lePesach shiv'im yom — one plants the grain seventy days before Pesach. The timing is deliberate: the grain grows during the period when the sun is strengthening into the spring, which produces the finest yield. Vehu oseh solet meruba — and this produces an abundance of fine flour, with each stalk yielding a high quantity of grain.

Here again the question of scope is genuinely ambiguous. The reference to seventy days before Pesach need not be specifically about the Omer; it may simply reflect the agronomic principle that early spring planting produces the best grain. So the Mishnah may well be speaking about grain for all menachot, and Rashi and Tosafot are in dispute about precisely this question.

### Gizbar Flour Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד בּוֹדֵק הַגִּזְבָּר? מַכְנִיס יָדוֹ לְתוֹכָהּ. עָלָה בָּהּ אָבָק – פְּסוּלָה, עַד שֶׁיְּנַפֶּנָּה. וְאִם הִתְלִיעָה – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **How does** the Temple treasurer **inspect** the flour to determine whether it is of sufficiently high quality? **The treasurer inserts his hand into** the flour. If, when he removes his hand, flour **powder covers it,** the flour is **unfit, until one sifts it** with a fine sifter, so that no powder will remain. **And if** the flour **became wormy,** it is **unfit** for use in a meal offering.

Keitzad bodek et hakemach — how does one check that the flour has been properly sifted? Hagizbar machnis yado letocha — the gizbar, the Temple treasurer, inserts his hand into the flour. Ala be'avak psula — if dust or fine dirt clings to his hand when he withdraws it, the flour is invalid ad sheyinafe — until it has been properly resifted. Once sifted to the requisite standard it becomes kasher. *ve'im hitli'a*, psula — but if the flour has become wormy, it is invalid outright; one cannot simply remove the worms and use the rest.

This raises a question about the context. If the Mishnah is speaking about the Omer, the involvement of the gizbar is readily understood, since the Omer is produced within the *beit hamikdash* itself. But if we are speaking of private menachot — which may in some cases have been prepared at home — how would the gizbar have the opportunity to inspect the flour? This question reinforces the ambiguity about whether the discussion is limited to the Omer or extends to all menachot.

What is particularly striking about the flour inspection is the severity of the standard. Even dust — avak — is treated as a disqualifying impurity. The language used is psula, invalid, even though the flour can be resifted and thereby validated. This is noteworthy precisely because the same Mishnah says that grain from a manured or irrigated field is valid bedi'avad — yet flour that has not been perfectly sifted is psula until remedied. The threshold for physical purity of the final flour is remarkably high.

### Once or Twice Plowing?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כֵּיצַד עוֹשֶׂה? נָרָהּ שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הֵיכִי קָאָמַר? נָרָהּ שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה נָרָהּ וְזוֹרְעָהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא נָרָהּ שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וּשְׁנִיָּה זוֹרְעָהּ בְּלֹא נָרָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states: **How does one produce** optimal-quality grain? **He plows** the field during the **first year,** and in the second year he sows it. The Gemara clarifies what should be done in the second year: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What** is the *tanna* **saying?** Is he saying that **he plows** the field during the **first** year, **and** in the **second** year **he plows it and sows it? Or perhaps** he is saying that **he plows** the field during the **first** year, **and** in the **second** year **he sows it without plowing it.**

The Gemara now focuses its attention on the crop rotation method described in the Mishnah. *keitzad oseh* — nira shana rishona — one overturns the soil the first year. The question the Gemara raises is: how precisely should the phrase be parsed? *ibayah lehu*, heichi ka'amar — does it mean: nira shana rishona u'shniya nira u'zra — you overturn the soil in year one, and in year two you overturn the soil again and then plant? Or does it mean: nira shana rishona, u'shniya zra belo nira — you overturn the soil in year one, and in year two you simply plant without any additional overturning?

The second reading is what the Mishnah appears on its surface to say. But the Gemara wonders whether the first year's overturning suffices, or whether the second year requires its own overturning before planting. Rashi notes an important clarification: one should not conflate overturning the soil with the routine plowing done at the time of planting. When one plants, one always makes furrows — that is inherent in the word zra. The question here is whether, prior to the planting season in year two, there would be a separate and substantial overturning of the soil, analogous to what was done in year one. The agricultural logic of overturning is that it aerates the soil and allows the seed to penetrate more effectively — so there is a compelling reason to do it even in year two, in addition to the furrows made at the time of planting.

### Southern Fields Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אַף חִיטֵּי כָּרָזִיִּים וּכְפַר אַחִים, אִלְמָלֵא סְמוּכוֹת לִירוּשָׁלַיִם – הָיוּ מְבִיאִין מֵהֶן, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מְבִיאִין אֶת הָעוֹמֶר אֶלָּא מִן הַשָּׂדוֹת הַמּוּדְרָמוֹת וְהַמְנוֹנָרוֹת לְכָךְ, שֶׁבָּהֶן חַמָּה זוֹרַחַת, וּמֵהֶן חַמָּה שׁוֹקַעַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says: Also** concerning **grain** grown in **Kerazayim and Kefar Aḥim, if only** those places **had been close to Jerusalem, they would have brought** barley **from them** for the *omer*. **As** the *halakha* is **that one brings the *omer* only from the southern fields and** those **that were plowed for that** purpose, **as upon such** fields, the **sun rises** and shines, **and from those** fields, the **sun** also **sets.** In other words, those fields are exposed to a lot of sunlight, so they produce a superior-quality crop. Kerazayim and Kefar Aḥim were such fields, but they were too far from Jerusalem for their barley to be used for the *omer* offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד עוֹשֶׂה? נָרָהּ שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, וּשְׁנִיָּה חוֹרֵשׁ וְשׁוֹנֶה, וְזוֹרְעָהּ קוֹדֶם לַפֶּסַח שִׁבְעִים יוֹם, כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא סְמוּכָה לַחַמָּה, וְעוֹשָׂה קָנֶה זֶרֶת וְשִׁיבּוֹלֶת זִרְתַּיִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **How does one produce** optimal-quality grain? **He plows** the field during the **first year, and** in the **second** year, he **plows** it once **and** then **repeats** the plowing a second time, **and he sows it seventy days before Passover.** It is done this way **in order that** the sowing **will be** done **close to** the time when the strength of the **sun** reaches its climax, **and** will thereby **produce** a high-quality crop in which the length of **a stalk** is **a handspan and** the **ear** itself is **two handspans.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹצֵר וּמְעַמֵּר, וְדָשׁ, וְזוֹרֶה, וּבוֹרֵר, וְטוֹחֵן, וּמְרַקֵּד, וּמֵבִיא אֵצֶל גִּזְבָּר, וְגִזְבָּר מַכְנִיס יָדוֹ לְתוֹכָהּ. אִם עָלְתָה בָּהּ אָבָק – אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״חֲזוֹר וְנַפֵּה אוֹתָהּ שְׁנִיָּה״. מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי נָתָן אָמְרוּ: גִּזְבָּר סָךְ יָדוֹ שֶׁמֶן, וּמַכְנִיס לְתוֹכָהּ עַד שֶׁמַּעֲלֶה כׇּל אֲבָקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** then one **reaps** the grain **and gathers** it together into a pile, **and** then he **threshes** the grain **and winnows** it, **and** then he **sorts** the inedible admixture from the edible grain, **and** then he **grinds** the grain **and sifts** it **and brings** the flour produced **to the** Temple **treasurer. And** the **treasurer inserts his hand into it** in order to check its quality. **If** upon removing his hand **powder covers it,** the treasurer **says to** the owner: **Go back and sift it a second** time. The Sages **say in the name of Rabbi Natan** that **the treasurer** would perform a more thorough examination of the flour's quality. He **douses his hand** with **oil and** then **inserts it into** the flour **until all of its powder will be brought up.**

Ta shema — the Gemara brings a braita to address this question. Amar Rabbi Yose: af *chitei karzayim* u'kfar achim — even the places called *chitei karzayim* and the village of Achim — ilmalei smuchos l'Yerushalayim, hayu mevi'im mehem — had they been closer to Yerushalayim, they would have brought the Omer and the *shtei halechem* from them. The principle established here is that ideally the Omer is brought from the closest available source to Yerushalayim. These places were close enough to be ideal in terms of grain quality but were passed over because of distance.

The braita then states: lefi she'ein mevi'im et ha'omer ela min hasados hamidromos — the Omer is brought only from the southern fields, ve'hamenunarot — and from fields where the soil has been overturned. Rashi interprets "southern" as meaning south of Yerushalayim. Tosafot, however, argues that what matters is not geographic direction relative to Yerushalayim but rather southern exposure — that is, the field faces south and therefore receives the full force of the sun throughout the day, particularly in the spring and early summer when the sun travels along a more southerly arc. The sun shines on these fields from the moment it rises and departs from them only at sunset, meaning they receive the maximum possible daily sunlight. Tosafot's reading is strengthened by the Gemara's own subsequent elaboration: *shebehen chama zorachat*, umehem chama shoka'at — the sun rises on them and sets from them.

The braita then describes the full process for these southern fields: *nirash shana rishona* — the soil is overturned in year one. *uvashana shniya* — in year two, choresh, veshoneh — one plows and then plows again, vezara — and then plants, kodem lePesach shiv'im yom — seventy days before Pesach, *kedei shetehei smucha lechama* — so that the growing grain is close in time to the onset of the warm season.

This braita is explicit: in the second year, there is both a choresh — a plowing — and a shoneh — a second plowing — before the planting. The soil is turned twice in the year of planting, in addition to the overturning done in year one.

Tosafot raises a difficulty with this braita's attribution of the entire process to the Omer. In the braita's description of the production process, it mentions desh — threshing. Yet in earlier discussions of the Omer, the grain was not threshed in the conventional sense; the stalks were beaten rather than threshed in the standard manner. Tosafot therefore concludes that this discussion is not limited to the Omer — it encompasses grain for menachot more broadly. This is the ongoing ambiguity: the Omer is mentioned explicitly, yet certain details do not fit exclusively an Omer context. One of the places is even named *chitei karzayim*, "the wheat of Karzayim" — and of course the Omer is brought from barley, not wheat, though Tosafot notes that this may simply be the name of the locality rather than a description of what is brought from it.

Regarding the southern field requirement — the Gemara later records Rabbi Yochanan stating this as an Amoraic ruling as well: *ein mevi'in* et ha'Omer ela min hasadot hamudramot shebe'Eretz Yisrael, *shebehen chama zorachat* umehem chama shoka'at. It is not uncommon for Amoraic statements and baraitot to duplicate one another in this fashion.

Abba Shaul is also cited: Omer haya ba mibik'at *beit makle* — the Omer would come from the Valley of *beit makle*. Kevat gimmel se'in — this place was three se'in in size, with se'ah here being used as an area measurement rather than a volume measurement. It is perhaps worth noting that the Omer offering requires beginning with what amounts to three se'in of barley (in volume), which is then processed down to one-tenth of that quantity in flour. That the Omer field is itself measured in three se'in of land may reflect the assumption that one se'ah of land yields roughly one se'ah of grain. *sadeh mudramot haita* — it was a southern exposure field. Uva chama zorachat umimena chama shoka'at — the sun rose upon it and set from it. Nir chetzya vezoreia chetzya — they would alternate: one half of the field would be overturned in a given year while the other half was planted, and in the following year they would reverse.

### Zeret & Shibbolet Yield

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת חוֹרֵשׁ וְשׁוֹנֶה. וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, מַתְנִיתִין לָא קָתָנֵי ״שׁוֹנֶה״, בָּרַיְיתָא קָתָנֵי ״שׁוֹנֶה״. הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בַּעֲבוּדָה, כָּאן בְּשֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲבוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that the *baraita* provides a resolution to the dilemma: **In any event, the** *baraita* **teaches** that during the second year, the owner of the field **plows** it once **and** then **repeats** the plowing. It is explicit, then, that during the second year the field should be plowed. The Gemara rejects this proof: **But** even **according to your reasoning,** ultimately **the mishna does not teach** that he plows the field and then **repeats** the plowing; it is only **the *baraita*** that **teaches** that he plows and then **repeats** the plowing. It would appear, then, that the mishna and *baraita* express different opinions, and it may be that according to the mishna one does not need to plow in the second year at all. The Gemara dismisses this suggestion: **This** is **not difficult;** it is possible that the mishna and the *baraita* do not disagree, and **here,** the mishna, which does not require plowing a second time, **is referring to a cultivated** field, whereas **there,** the *baraita* **is referring to an uncultivated** field, and therefore it requires that the field be plowed a second time.

Having established from the braita that in year two one plows and plows again — *choresh veshoneh* — the Gemara confronts the apparent discrepancy with our Mishnah, which mentions only an overturning in year one and planting in year two without mentioning a second overturning. Tani hatam *choresh veshoneh* — the braita indeed says you plow twice. But matnitin lo tani shona — our Mishnah does not say shoneh. So at best one might say this is a debate between the braita and the Mishnah, but the braita does not help determine what the Mishnah itself intended.

The Gemara resolves this: ha lo kashya — there is no difficulty. Kan ba-avoda, kan beshe-eina avoda — here we are speaking of a field that was already worked, there of a field that was not previously worked. Rashi explains: if the field had already been plowed in earlier years — avoda — then when I begin the rotation, there is already one overturning in its history. Therefore in year one I turn it over once more, and in year two I plow and plant, achieving a total of two overturnings. But if the field had not been previously worked — she-eina avoda — then I need to count two overturnings myself: once in year one and once in year two before planting. The goal in either case is to achieve two substantial overturnings of the soil before planting. The Mishnah describes the former scenario; the braita, the latter.

The Gemara then cites an additional source that discusses rotating the field: nir chatziya vezoreia chatziya — one overturns half the field while planting the other half, then in the following year reverses. This passage describes an ongoing alternation without any mention of a double overturning. The Gemara acknowledges that this adds little beyond the Mishnah's own description, and the question of double plowing remains resolved through the distinction of avoda versus she-eina avoda.

### Full Production Steps


The question naturally arises: can one plow a field without planting in a Shmitta year? This was addressed in the previous session. The answer offered was that there are two possible grounds for permitting it. The first is that the *beit hamikdash* can simply override the prohibition of Shmitta, as it can override Shabbat. The second — and arguably more intellectually interesting — explanation is that some of the prohibitions of Shmitta are rooted in the idea of asserting private ownership over the land. When the labor is carried out not for private benefit but for communal purposes, and not as an exercise of personal ownership — especially when *shluchei Beit Din* are the ones who go out to protect the field and plow it — the prohibition may not apply in the same way. The great theme of Shmitta is precisely that one does not exert private ownership; when *beit din* has, in effect, taken the field over, that condition is satisfied even if the field is technically in private hands.

### Avoda vs. Lo Avoda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מְבִיאִין אֶת הָעוֹמֶר אֶלָּא מִן הַשָּׂדוֹת הַמּוּדְרָמוֹת שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁבָּהֶן חַמָּה זוֹרַחַת, וּמֵהֶן חַמָּה שׁוֹקַעַת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One brings the *omer* only from** the **southern fields of Eretz Yisrael, as upon those** fields, the **sun rises** and shines, **and from those** fields, the **sun** also **sets.** Those fields are exposed to abundant sunlight, and so they produce a superior-quality crop. **This is also taught** in a *baraita* similarly, giving this a dual support from both Amoraic and Tannaitic tradition. As noted, such duplication between an Amoraic statement and a baraita does occur.

The Gemara records Rabbi Yochanan stating as an Amoraic ruling: *ein mevi'in* et ha'Omer ela min hasadot hamudramot — one brings the Omer only from the southern fields of Eretz Yisrael, *shebehen chama zorachat* umehem chama shoka'at — on which the sun shines from rising to setting. Tanya nami hachi — a braita teaches similarly, giving this a dual support from both Amoraic and Tannaitic tradition. As noted, such duplication between an Amoraic statement and a baraita does occur.

### Shmitta & Plowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: עוֹמֶר הָיָה בָּא מִבִּקְעַת בֵּית מִקְלָה, כְּבַת שָׁלֹשׁ סְאִין הָיְתָה, וְשָׂדֶה מוּדְרֶמֶת הָיְתָה, וּבָהּ חַמָּה זוֹרַחַת וּמִמֶּנָּה חַמָּה שׁוֹקַעַת. נָיר חֶצְיָהּ וְזוֹרֵעַ חֶצְיָהּ, נָיר חֶצְיָהּ וְזוֹרֵעַ חֶצְיָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abba Shaul says** that **the *omer* would come from** grain grown in **the valley of Beit Mikle.** The field there **was about three *se'a*, and it was a southern field, and** the **sun would rise** and shine **upon it, and** the **sun would set from it.** During the first year, the farmer **plowed** the entire field during the first **half of** the year, **and** he then **sowed half of** the field, leaving the other half fallow. During the second year, he again **plowed** the entire field during the first **half of** the year, **and** he then **sowed** the **half of** the field that was left fallow in the previous year.

Abba Shaul omer — Abba Shaul says the Omer would come from Bik'at *beit makle*, a valley of that name. Kevat gimmel se'in — it was three se'in in area. *sadeh mudramot haita* — it was a southern exposure field. Uva chama zorachat umimena chama shoka'at — the sun rose and set upon it. Nir chetzya vezoreia chetzya — one half was overturned while the other half was planted, and the years alternated, as described above.

### RY: Southern Field Omer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִלְקִיָּה בַּר טוֹבִי הֲוָה לֵיהּ קַרְנָא דְּאַרְעָא, נָר חֶצְיָהּ וְזָרַע חֶצְיָהּ, נָר חֶצְיָהּ וְזָרַע חֶצְיָהּ, וַעֲבַדָה עַל חַד תְּרֵי, וּמְזַבֵּין לְהוּ לְחִיטֵּי לִסְמִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara demonstrates the efficacy of this method: **Rav Ḥilkiya bar Tovi had a tract of land. He plowed** the field during the first **half of** the year **and** then **sowed half of** it. The next year, **he plowed** the field during the first **half of** the year **and** then **sowed** the other **half of it. And** this method was so effective that his field **produced twice** as much wheat as other fields its size, **and** it was of such a superior quality that **he sold** the **wheat** to be used **as fine flour [*lismida*]** for the meal offerings in the Temple.

Rav Hilkiya bar Tuvi hava lei karna de'ar'a — Rav Hilkiya bar Tuvi had a small corner of a field. He applied this alternating regimen — nir chetzya vezoreia chetzya, nir chetzya vezoreia chetzya — and ve'avda chad terei — it produced double the normal yield, precisely as the Gemara described: an ear twice the size of the stalk. Umazbin lehu lechitei desmida — he would sell this wheat for smida, which Rashi explains means for use in menachot.

This story introduces a final layer of ambiguity. The entire preceding discussion appears to be about the Omer, yet here a private individual practices the rotation and sells the resulting grain for menachot generally. This may simply mean that any extraordinarily high-quality grain, whatever its original purpose, would naturally be sought for use in menachot broadly — or it may be another indication that the entire discussion of ideal grain production was never exclusively about the Omer to begin with. The ambiguity is not resolved; it is simply the nature of this sugya.

## Wormy Flour & Ba'al Mum

### Abba Shaul: Beit Makle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: סוֹלֶת שֶׁהִתְלִיעָה רוּבָּהּ – פְּסוּלָה, וְחִיטִּין שֶׁהִתְלִיעוּ רוּבָּן – פְּסוּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Fine flour the majority of which became wormy** is **unfit. And** similarly, **wheat** kernels **the majority of which became wormy** are **unfit,** and they may not be used to produce fine flour for meal offerings.

The Gemara had already established in the Mishnah that wormy flour is pasul outright and cannot be remedied simply by removing the worms. The Gemara now clarifies the standard. Tanu Rabbanan: solet she-hitlia ruba psula — if the majority of the flour is wormy, it is invalid. Ve-chittin she-hitliau rubam psula — similarly, if the majority of the wheat itself has become wormy before grinding, it too is invalid. The critical qualifier is ruba — the majority. If only a minority of the flour or wheat is wormy, one discards the affected portion and uses the rest.

### Rav Hilkiya's Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: בְּרוֹב חִטָּה, אוֹ בְּרוֹב סְאָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya asks:** What is the meaning of this latter ruling? Is it saying only that if the **majority of** an individual **wheat** kernel becomes wormy the flour produced from it is unfit, **or** is it saying that when the **majority of a *se'a*** of kernels becomes wormy the entire *se'a* is **unfit?** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Having established that majority-wormy flour is pasul, Rabbi Yirmiya raises a precise question: baya Rebbi Yirmiya: *be-rov chittin o be-rov se'ah*? How does one measure the "majority"? Does it mean that each individual grain must itself be majority wormy — that is, more than half of each kernel has been eaten through — or does it mean that a majority of the grains in the se'ah are wormy, even if each wormy grain is not fully consumed?

The practical difference is significant. If the standard is the former — each grain majority wormy — then one could in principle remove the fully wormy grains and preserve any grain that is less than majority affected. If the standard is the latter — a majority of grains in the batch are wormy at all — then even if each individual grain is only slightly wormy, once the majority of grains are touched, the entire batch is invalid and the remaining minority of unaffected grains cannot be preserved separately. The Gemara answers: teiku — the question stands unresolved.

### Majority Wormy = Pasul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: הִקְדִּישָׁן, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּלְקֶה עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם בַּעַל מוּם? כֵּיוָן דְּפָסֵיל – כְּבַעַל מוּם דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין בַּעַל מוּם אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asks:** If **one consecrated** grains of wormy wheat for use in a meal offering, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he should be flogged for** consecrating **them due to** the prohibition against consecrating **a flawed** item as an offering? One is flogged for consecrating a blemished animal as an offering (see *Temura* 6b); does the same apply to consecrating wormy wheat? Does one say that **since** the wheat is **unfit,** it is **comparable to a blemished** animal? **Or perhaps,** the prohibition against consecrating **a flawed** item **applies only to an animal.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Baya Rava: hiktishan mahu she-yilke aleihen mishum *ba'al mum*? — if one sanctifies this wormy grain, does he receive lashes as one would for consecrating a blemished animal? The relevance of the question turns on the conceptual underpinning of the grain's disqualification. The Torah does not explicitly enumerate standards for grain quality; the invalidity of substandard grain appears to derive by analogy from the prohibition of *ba'al mum* — the prohibition against offering or even consecrating an animal with a physical blemish. If the analogy to *ba'al mum* is what drives the invalidation, the question is how far that analogy extends: does it reach all the way to the prohibition's penal consequence, namely lashes for the act of consecration?

*Keivan de-pasul ke-*Ba'al Mum* dami* — one could argue that since wormy grain is pasul, it must be that we are comparing it to a *ba'al mum*. O dilma — or perhaps ein *ba'al mum* ela be-behemah — the formal category of mum applies only to animals. On this view, the analogy tells us the grain cannot be used, but it does not make the grain a mum in the legal sense, and therefore one does not incur lashes for consecrating it. The act is disrespectful and improper, but not a punishable transgression. The Gemara answers: teiku.

### Rov Chitin or Rov Se'ah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: כׇּל עֵץ שֶׁנִּמְצָא בּוֹ תּוֹלַעַת – פָּסוּל לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לַח, אֲבָל יָבֵשׁ – גּוֹרְרוֹ וְכָשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Middot* 2:5) with regard to the wood logs that are burned on the altar: Priests inspect them before they are used and **any log in which a worm is found** is **unfit for** use **on the altar.** In reference to this mishna, **Shmuel says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **a wet** log, as a wormy section cannot be removed. **But** if a wormy section is found in **a dry** log, the priest **scrapes** the wormy spot **away, and** the log is **fit** for use.

The Gemara now applies the same analysis to a different type of offering. Tnan hatam: kol etz she-nimtza vo tola'at, pasul legabei Mizbeach — any piece of wood found to contain a worm is disqualified for use on the Mizbeach. Amar Shmuel: lo shanu ela lach — this applies only to moist wood, because in moist wood the worm infestation spreads and cannot be fully removed. Aval beyavesh — but if the wood is dry, gorero vechasher — one can scrape out the wormy portion and the rest remains valid. For use as firewood, then, the standard may be somewhat more lenient than for a mincha — a practical distinction reflecting the different nature of the offering.

### Rava: Lashes for Worm?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: הִקְדִּישׁוֹ, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּלְקֶה עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם בַּעַל מוּם? כֵּיוָן דְּפָסוּל – כְּבַעַל מוּם דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין בַּעַל מוּם אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asks:** If **one consecrated** a wormy log to be used on the altar, **what is** the *halakha* with regard to whether **he should be flogged for** consecrating **it due to** the prohibition against consecrating **a flawed** item as an offering? Does one say that **since** the log is **unfit,** it is **comparable to a blemished** animal? **Or perhaps,** the prohibition against consecrating **a flawed** item **applies only to an animal.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Ba'i Rava: hikdisho, mahu sheyilke alav mishum *ba'al mum*? — the same question is asked of wormy wood: does sanctifying it incur lashes for *ba'al mum*? *keivan depasul kiba'al mum dami* — since it is pasul, perhaps we compare it to a mum. O dilma ein *ba'al mum* ela bebehema — or perhaps the concept of mum is categorically limited to animals. Teiku — here too the question is left unresolved.

Tosafot explains why Rava asks the question separately for wood rather than simply extending the answer from solet. Even if one were to conclude that wormy solet carries some analogy to *ba'al mum*, solet is clearly a type of korban — its relationship to the Mizbeach is through consecration as an offering. Wood is a more ambiguous case: some hold that donated wood functions as a korban in its own right, requiring other wood beneath it; others see it as simply the fuel. If wood is not considered a true korban, the analogy to *ba'al mum* may not apply even where it would for solet. Hence the question must be posed independently. Teiku — it remains unresolved.

## Oil: Tekoa & Aggadot

### Wormy Wood for Mizbe'ach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תְּקוֹעַ – אַלְפָּא לַשֶּׁמֶן. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: שְׁנִיָּה לָהּ רֶגֶב בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן. כׇּל הָאֲרָצוֹת הָיוּ כְּשֵׁרוֹת, אֶלָּא מִיכָּן הָיוּ מְבִיאִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Olive trees in **Tekoa** are the **primary** source **of oil** to be used in meal offerings. **Abba Shaul says: Secondary to** Tekoa **is Regev on the** east **bank of the Jordan** River. **All the regions were valid** for oil to be brought from them, **but** it was **from here** that **they would bring** it.

The Mishnah now turns from grain to oil. Tekoa — spelled with a kuf-vav-ayin — is designated as the premier source of olive oil: Tekoa alfa leshemen, A-grade oil. Abba Shaul omer sheniya lah — second to it is Regev in Ever HaYarden. Kol ha'aratzot hayu kesherot ela mikan hayu mevi'im — all lands were technically valid but they would bring from these preferred locations.

### Wood Ba'al Mum Teiku


The mention of Regev in Ever HaYarden immediately raises a question. We know that the Omer must come from grain grown in Eretz Yisrael — it represents the *reishit ktzirchem*, the first of the harvest. The Mishnah in Kelim enumerates ten levels of kedushah in Eretz Yisrael, stating: *shemevi'im mimenah ha'omer vehabikkurim veshtei halechem mah she'ein* mevi'im mikol ha'aratzot — it is from Eretz Yisrael that one brings the Omer, the bikkurim, and the *shtei halechem*, which cannot be brought from other lands. Similarly, we know one cannot bring bikkurim from Ever HaYarden. The question, which deserves further examination, is whether the same restriction applies to the Omer.

But this Mishnah concerns the oil of the menachot, not the grain. Even if the Omer grain must come from Eretz Yisrael, the question of whether the oil for the Omer is subject to the same requirement is a separate and less obvious one. This gives rise to two distinct questions: first, whether the oil for the Omer must come from Eretz Yisrael at all; and second, assuming it must, whether Ever HaYarden qualifies as Eretz Yisrael for this purpose, given that bikkurim cannot be brought from there. The broader question also remains — whether this Mishnah is speaking specifically about the Omer or about menachot in general — and that ambiguity has continued throughout the daf. These questions are noted but not resolved here.

### Tekoa Premier Oil Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבִיאִין לֹא מִבֵּית הַזְּבָלִים, וְלֹא מִבֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִים, וְלֹא מִן מַה שֶּׁנִּזְרַע בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר. אֵין מְבִיאִין אַנְפַּקְטָן, וְאִם הֵבִיא – כָּשֵׁר. אֵין מְבִיאִין מִן הַגַּרְגְּרִין שֶׁנִּשְׁרוּ בְּמַיִם, וְלֹא מִן הַכְּבָשִׁים, וְלֹא מִן הַשְּׁלוּקִים, וְאִם הֵבִיא – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not bring** a meal offering containing oil from olives taken **from a fertilized** olive **grove, nor** from olives taken **from an irrigated** olive **grove, nor** from olives taken **from** an olive grove where grain **was sown between** the trees. **But if one did bring** a meal offering containing oil from such groves, it is **valid. One may not bring** a meal offering containing oil from **unripe olives [*anpiktan*], but if one did bring** it, it is **valid. One may not bring** a meal offering containing oil **from olives that were soaked in water, nor from pickled** olives, **nor from boiled** olives, **and** even if **one did bring** it, it is **not valid.**

The Mishnah then enumerates the disqualified sources of oil, in direct parallel to the disqualified sources of grain discussed earlier. *ein mevi'in lo mibeit hazvalim velo mibeit hashlehin* — one may not bring oil from olive trees that were heavily fertilized or that required irrigation. *velo mima shenizra beinehem* — and not from groves where grain was planted among the olive trees. Just as grain quality suffers when trees compete with it for nutrients, so olive quality suffers when grain is planted among the olive trees. The competition works both ways.

Ve'im hevi kasher — but if one brought from any of these, the oil is valid bedi'avad. So far the parallel to grain is exact.

The Mishnah then adds prohibitions with no exact parallel in the grain discussion. *ein mevi'in* anpakton — one may not bring oil pressed from unripe olives. Ve'im hevi kasher — bedi'avad it is valid.

*Ein mevi'in* min hagargerin shenishru bamayim — one may not use olives that were soaked in water before having their oil extracted, since the soaking dilutes the oil. *velo min hakvashim* — and not from olives that were pickled before extraction. *velo min hashlikim* — and not from olives that were boiled. Ve'im hevi pasul — if one brought oil from any of these last three categories, it is invalid, with no bedi'avad leniency. This is a notable departure from the earlier pattern. The first set of disqualified sources — manured groves, irrigated groves, grain-interspersed groves, and unripe olives — are valid bedi'avad. The latter set — soaked, pickled, and boiled olives — render the oil pasul outright. The practical distinction likely reflects the degree to which the oil itself is physically compromised or adulterated.

The question of how we know these standards — since the Torah does not enumerate them explicitly for oil any more than it does for grain — is similar to the issues that arose regarding the grain. The prohibition by the Menorah is *shemen zayit zach katit lama'or*, but the standards for other uses of oil in the *beit hamikdash* are derived by extension and tradition.

### Oil from Ever HaYarden?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״וַיִּשְׁלַח יוֹאָב תְּקוֹעָה וַיִּקַּח מִשָּׁם אִשָּׁה חֲכָמָה״, מַאי שְׁנָא תְּקוֹעָה? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁרְגִילִין בְּשֶׁמֶן זַיִת, חָכְמָה מְצוּיָה בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara notes the effect of Tekoa's oil on those living there: The verse states: **"And Joab sent to Tekoa, and fetched from there a wise woman"** (II Samuel 14:2). **What is different** about **Tekoa** that Joab chose to bring a woman from there? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Since** the residents of Tekoa **are accustomed to** use **olive oil, wisdom is prevalent there.**

The Gemara departs from halacha for an aggadic observation prompted by the Mishnah's mention of Tekoa. Vayishlach Yoav Tekoa vayikach misham isha chachama — and Yoav sent to Tekoa and took a wise woman from there. This episode, in which Yoav arranged for a woman from Tekoa to approach David with a fabricated story of mourning in order to persuade David to reconcile with Avshalom, raises the question: mai shna Tekoa — why specifically Tekoa?

Amar Rabbi Yochanan: mitoch sheragilim beshemen zayit, chochma metzuya bahen — because the people of Tekoa were accustomed to olive oil, wisdom was found among them. Rashi cites other passages in the Gemara that speak of olive oil sharpening the mind: deshemen zayit mefake'ach et haleib. Olive oil, in the Gemara's view, was a kind of brain food — and since Tekoa was the premier source of the finest olive oil, its inhabitants partook of it in abundance and were accordingly blessed with exceptional wisdom.

### Disqualified Oil Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְטֹבֵל בְּשֶׁמֶן רַגְלוֹ״ – זֶה חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל אָשֵׁר, שֶׁמּוֹשֵׁךְ שֶׁמֶן כְּמַעְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara digresses to discuss the tribal portion of Asher, in which the city of Tekoa is located: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In his blessing to the tribe of Asher, Moses said: "He will be pleasing to his brothers, **and immerse his foot in oil"** (Deuteronomy 33:24). **This** is referring to **the portion of Asher, as the oil flows** there **like a spring.**

Tanu rabanan: vetovel bashemen raglo — and he dipped his foot in oil. Ze chelko shel Asher — this is the portion of Asher, *shemoshech shemen kema'ayan* — where oil flows like a wellspring. The territory of Asher, identified as being in the north of the land (in the vicinity of Gush Chalav and Meron), was extraordinarily abundant in olive oil.

### Yoav to Tekoa: Oil=Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ פַּעַם אַחַת נִצְרְכוּ לָהֶן אַנְשֵׁי לוּדְקִיָּא לְשֶׁמֶן, מִינּוּ לָהֶן פּוּלְמוֹסְטוּס אֶחָד, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֵךְ וְהָבֵא לָנוּ שֶׁמֶן בְּמֵאָה רִיבּוֹא. הָלַךְ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֵךְ לְצוֹר, הָלַךְ לְצוֹר, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֵךְ לְגוּשׁ חָלָב, הָלַךְ לְגוּשׁ חָלָב, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֵךְ אֵצֶל פְּלוֹנִי לְשָׂדֶה הַלָּז, וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁהָיָה עוֹזֵק תַּחַת זֵיתָיו. אָמַר לוֹ: יֵשׁ לְךָ שֶׁמֶן בְּמֵאָה רִיבּוֹא שֶׁאֲנִי צָרִיךְ? אָמַר לוֹ: הַמְתֵּן לִי עַד שֶׁאֲסַיֵּים מְלַאכְתִּי, הִמְתִּין עַד שֶׁסִּיֵּים מְלַאכְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught** that **once, the people of Laodicea were in need of oil. They appointed** a gentile **messenger [*polmostos*]** and **said to him: Go and bring us one million** *maneh* worth **of oil.** **He** first **went to Jerusalem** to procure the oil, but residents there did not have that quantity of oil. **They said to him: Go to Tyre,** which was a commercial city. **He went to Tyre,** but they also did not have enough oil. **They said to him: Go to Gush Ḥalav,** which is located in the portion of Asher. **He went to Gush Ḥalav,** and **they said to him: Go to so-and-so, to that field.** He went there **and found** someone **hoeing [*ozek*] under his olive trees.** The messenger **said to** that man: **Do you have** the **one million** *maneh* worth **of oil that I need?** The man **said to him: Wait for me until I complete my labor,** i.e., hoeing. The messenger **waited until** the man **completed his labor.**

Amru pa'am achat nitzrechu lahem anshei Ludkaya — one time the people of Ludkaya, a non-Jewish city, had a pressing need for oil. Minu lahen pulmostos echad — they appointed an officer, the word being a Greek loanword for a military or administrative official. Amru lo: lech ve-havi lanu shemen be-me'ah ribwo — they instructed him to go and procure oil worth a million maneh, a sum of staggering proportions, sufficient to acquire either an enormous quantity or the finest quality of oil available — or both.

*halach li-Yerushalayim* — he went to Yerushalayim, the natural first destination for anyone seeking goods from the Jews. They directed him: lech le-Tzor — go to Tzor. Tosafot notes that this cannot be Tyre, since that is outside Israel; it refers to a different place of that name. From there he was directed to Gush Chalav, and from there he was sent to a particular individual — lech etzel ploni, la-sadeh ha-laz — go to so-and-so, in that field over there.

He went and found the man working under his olive trees — u-matza'o she-hayah ozek tachat zeito. The precise meaning of the word ozek is debated: he was either removing stones, collecting fallen olives, building fences around his trees, or engaged in some other form of ordinary fieldwork. The officer asked him: yesh lecha shemen be-me'ah ribwo she-ani tzarich — do you have a million maneh's worth of oil for me? The man replied: hamten li ad she-asayem melachti — wait for me to finish what I am doing.

Himtin ad she-siyem melachto — the officer waited. When the man finished his fieldwork, he put his implements in his knapsack and, as he walked home, continued removing stones from the path — vehayah mesakel u-va ba-derech. This small detail is telling: even after completing the day's labor, the man was cleaning up the road, a picture of simple, humble industry. The officer, seeing all this, began to doubt. He said to himself — or perhaps aloud — *ki medumanni she-sechok se-chaknu bi ha-yehudim*: I think the Jews are mocking me. They send me from place to place and finally direct me to this field hand, who is supposed to have a million maneh's worth of oil?

### Asher's Oil Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר שֶׁסִּיֵּים מְלַאכְתּוֹ, הִפְשִׁיל כֵּלָיו לַאֲחוֹרָיו, וְהָיָה מְסַקֵּל וּבָא בַּדֶּרֶךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: יֵשׁ לְךָ שֶׁמֶן בְּמֵאָה רִיבּוֹא? כִּמְדוּמֶּה אֲנִי שֶׁשְּׂחוֹק שָׂחֲקוּ בִּי הַיְּהוּדִים. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְעִירוֹ, הוֹצִיאָה לוֹ שִׁפְחָתוֹ קוּמְקְמוֹס שֶׁל חַמִּין, וְרָחַץ בּוֹ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו. הוֹצִיאָה לוֹ סֵפֶל שֶׁל זָהָב מְלֵיאָה שֶׁמֶן, וְטָבַל בּוֹ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְטוֹבֵל בַּשֶּׁמֶן רַגְלוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **After he completed his labor,** the man **slung his tools** over his shoulders **behind him,** a manner typical of poor laborers, and started walking, **and he was removing stones** from his orchard **as he went along the path.** Upon seeing this behavior, which suggested the man was merely a laborer, the messenger questioned whether the man was truly able to provide him with the oil. **He said to** the man: Can it be that **you** really **have** the **one million** *maneh* worth **of oil that I need? It seems to me that the Jews** of Gush Ḥalav **are making a laughingstock of me** by sending me here. **When he reached his city,** the man's **maidservant brought out to him a kettle [*kumkemos*] of hot** water, **and he washed his hands and his feet.** Afterward, **she brought out to him a golden basin filled with oil, in which he immersed his hands and feet, in fulfillment of that** which **is stated** with regard to the Tribe of Asher: **"And immerse his foot in oil"** (Deuteronomy 33:24).

*keivan shehigi'a le'iro* — when they arrived at the man's home, his maidservant brought out a large urn of hot water — kumkum shel chamin — and he washed his hands and feet from the dust of the journey and the day's labor. Then hotzi'ah lo safal shel zahav malei shemen — she brought out a gold bowl filled with oil, vetaval bo yadav veraglav — and he dipped his hands and his feet in it, *lekayeim mah shene'emar* — to fulfill the verse: vetovel bashemen raglo — and he dipped his foot in oil. This was the portion of Asher made manifest in one man's household.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר שֶׁאָכְלוּ וְשָׁתוּ, מָדַד לוֹ שֶׁמֶן בְּמֵאָה רִיבּוֹא. אָמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לְיוֹתֵר? אָמַר לוֹ: הֵן, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לִי דָּמִים. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם אַתָּה רוֹצֶה לִיקַּח – קַח, וַאֲנִי אֵלֵךְ עִמְּךָ וְאֶטּוֹל דָּמָיו. מָדַד לוֹ שֶׁמֶן בִּשְׁמוֹנָה עָשָׂר רִיבּוֹא. אָמְרוּ: לֹא הִנִּיחַ אוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ לֹא סוּס וְלֹא פֶּרֶד וְלֹא גָּמָל וְלֹא חֲמוֹר בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא שְׂכָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **After they ate and drank,** the man **measured out** for the messenger **one million** *maneh* worth **of oil.** The man **said to him:** Are you sure that **you do not need any more** oil? The messenger **said to him: Yes,** I do need more, **but I do not have** the **money** for it. The man **said to him: If you wish to take** more oil, **take** it **and I will go** back to Laodicea **with you and collect the money** for the extra oil there. The messenger agreed and the man **measured out** an additional **180,000** *maneh* worth **of oil.** Concerning this incident, people **said:** The messenger had such an enormous burden of oil that **he left neither a horse, nor a mule, nor a camel, nor a donkey in** all of **Eretz Yisrael that he did not rent** in order to help transport the oil back to Laodicea.

They ate and drank, and then madad lo shemen beme'ah ribo — he measured out a million maneh's worth of oil for him. And then: klum atah tzarich leyoter — is that all you need? Do you want more? The officer said he did want more — ela she'ein li damim, I simply have no money left. The man replied: im atah rotzeh likach kach, take as much more as you need, *va'ani imcha ve'etol damav* — and I will accompany you home and collect the balance there.

Madad lo shemen bishmonah asar ribo — he measured out another 180,000 in oil beyond the million. And lo heniach oto ish lo sus velo fered velo gamal *velo chamor* be'eretz yisrael *shelo secharo* — the officer was forced to hire every horse, mule, camel, and donkey in Eretz Yisrael to transport all of this oil back to Ludkaya.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לְעִירוֹ, יָצְאוּ אַנְשֵׁי עִירוֹ לְקַלְּסוֹ. אָמַר לָהֶם: לֹא לִי קַלְּסוּנִי, אֶלָּא לָזֶה שֶׁבָּא עִמִּי, שֶׁמָּדַד לִי שֶׁמֶן בְּמֵאָה רִיבּוֹא, וַהֲרֵי נוֹשֶׁה בִּי בִּשְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה רִיבּוֹא, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יֵשׁ מִתְעַשֵּׁר וְאֵין כֹּל מִתְרוֹשֵׁשׁ וְהוֹן רָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **When** the messenger finally **reached his city, the people of his city came out to praise him [*lekaleso*]** for achieving this tremendous feat. The messenger **said to them: Do not praise me. Rather,** praise **this** man **who has come with me, as** it is **he** who **measured for me one million** *maneh* worth **of oil, and he extended a debt to me for 180,000** *maneh* worth **of oil.** This incident was **in fulfillment of that** which **is stated: "There is one who seems to be rich, yet has nothing; there is one who seems to be poor, yet has great wealth"** (Proverbs 13:7).

*keivan shehigi'a le'iro*, yatz'u anshei iro lekalso — when the officer finally arrived home, the people of his city came out to praise him for having procured the vast quantity of oil they needed. He told them: *lo li kalsuni* — do not praise me. Ela zeh sheba imi shemadad li shemen beme'ah ribo, veharei nosheh bi bishmonah asar ribo — praise this man who came with me, who gave me a million maneh's worth of oil, and to whom I now owe another 180,000 — so much oil did he have.

*lekayeim mah shene'emar*: *yesh mitasher ve'ein kol*, mitroshesh vehon rav — to fulfill the verse: there is one who acts rich but has nothing, and one who appears poor yet possesses great wealth. The man worked in his field like a simple laborer, cleaned stones from the path as he walked home, lived without ostentation — and yet possessed more oil than an entire city could purchase for any price. The wealth of Asher in olive oil, and the humble greatness of those who held it, was thus fulfilled to the letter.
