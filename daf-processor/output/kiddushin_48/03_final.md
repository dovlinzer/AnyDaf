# Kiddushin 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Milveh Le'hotza'ah Nitnah

### Marrying with Milveh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא דְּמָר סָבַר: מִלְוָה, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וּמָר סָבַר: נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – אִין, וְאִי לָא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר הֵימֶנָּה שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – לָא. אֲבָל דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת!
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree only with regard to** this: There is one **Sage who holds** that one can betroth a woman with **a loan, even though** the **value of one *peruta* does not remain of it. And** one **Sage holds** that if the **value of one *peruta* remains from it, yes,** he can betroth her with it, **but if** the **value of one *peruta* does not remain of it,** he **cannot. But everyone agrees** that if **one betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is betrothed.**

The daf opens in the middle of an extended discussion that began on 47b: can a man betroth a woman using a *milveh* — not by waiving a loan she owes him, but by in some sense "returning" the money she borrowed? The complication is that the money may no longer be considered his at all. This is the principle of *milveh le'hotza'ah nitnah*: once a lender hands over cash, that cash belongs to the borrower, who merely owes an abstract debt in return. If so, when the man says "be betrothed to me with this money," he is attempting to betroth her with something that is already hers — and that cannot constitute *kesef kiddushin*.

This is Rav's position: *mekadesh bimilveh* does not work. The Gemara has been defending Rav by arguing that the principle of *milveh le'hotza'ah nitnah* is actually undisputed, and that the only open question is precisely *when* the money ceases to be the lender's — whether immediately upon transfer or only once the borrower begins to spend it.

### Money Becomes Hers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמָר: עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בְּמִלְוָה, דְּלָא הָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ. אֲבָל בִּשְׁאֵלָה, דְּהָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – אִין, לֹא בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – לֹא קְנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree only with regard to a loan, which is not returned intact.** Since there is no need for the debtor to return the same money to the lender, this means that the money has been transferred to the debtor's domain, and the first *tanna* holds that the lender may not retract the loan. **But with regard to** the **borrowing** of an item, for example the ax, **which is returned intact, everyone agrees** that if **one chopped with it, yes,** he has acquired it and is responsible for it. If **he has not chopped with it, he has not acquired it.**

The Gemara's resolution is that everyone agrees the money becomes the borrower's property once it is handed over. What the *tannaim* may disagree about is a narrower question: at what point is the transaction irreversible from the lender's perspective, and at what point can the money be said to have fully passed out of his domain? The practical consequence of *milveh le'hotza'ah nitnah* is that by the time the man attempts to use the loan as *kesef kiddushin*, the money is already hers. He cannot marry her with what is already in her possession.

### Nafka Minas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ: לָאוֹנָסִים – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבִרְשׁוּת לֹוֶה קָיְימִי, מַאי טַעְמָא – לָא גָּרְעָא מִשְּׁאֵלָה, מָה שְׁאֵלָה דְּהָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ חַיָּיב בָּאוֹנָסִים, מִלְוָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. אֶלָּא הָכָא מִלְוָה בִּרְשׁוּת בְּעָלִים לַחֲזָרָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba continues: **And I said to them: With regard to accidents, everyone agrees** that it **stands in the possession of the debtor** and he is responsible for the money. **What is the reason?** A loan of money is **no worse than borrowing** an item. **Just as** in the case of **borrowing** an item, **whereby** the item is **returned** to its owner **intact** and yet the debtor **is liable for accidents,** as explicitly stated in the Torah, is it **not all the more so** that with regard to **a loan,** which the debtor spends and repays with other money, it should be considered in the debtor's possession and he should bear responsibility for it? **Rather, here** the practical difference **between them** concerns the question of **a loan in the possession of the owner with regard to** the possibility of **retraction** of the loan.

Beyond the question of *kiddushin* itself, the Gemara identifies two *nafka minas* — practical differences — between the position that the money passes immediately upon transfer and the position that it passes only when she begins to spend it. The first *nafka mina* is liability for losses: if a wind blows the money out of her hand before she has spent any of it, whose loss is it? The second *nafka mina* is *chazarah* — whether the lender can retract and demand his money back before she begins spending it.

### Liability vs. Ownership

The Gemara argues that on the question of liability for losses, both positions would actually agree that she becomes liable immediately upon receiving the money. The reasoning is drawn from the laws of *she'elah*: when one borrows an object, one does not become its owner — the object remains the lender's — yet the moment it passes into the borrower's physical control, the borrower becomes a *sho'el* and is fully liable for any damage or loss. The same logic applies here: even if the money does not yet fully belong to her, once she has taken physical possession she bears the status of a *sho'el* and absorbs any losses. The genuine point of disagreement, then, is only the second *nafka mina*: whether the lender retains the right to retract before she begins spending the money.

## She'elah - Borrowed Objects

### Rav Huna's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַשּׁוֹאֵל קוּרְדּוֹם מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – קְנָאוֹ. לֹא בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – לֹא קְנָאוֹ. לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But rather,** with regard to **that which Rav Huna says:** In the case of **one who borrows an ax from his friend** for a certain period of time, if **he chops** wood **with it he has acquired it** in the sense that the lender cannot demand its immediate return. If **he has not chopped** wood **with it he has not acquired it. Let us say** that **this *halakha*** that Rav Huna **stated** is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.

This analysis leads directly to a statement of Rav Huna: *hasho'el kardom me'chavero — bika bo kena'o; lo bika bo — lo kena'o*. If one borrows an axe from a friend, once one begins chopping with it, one "takes possession"; if one has not yet begun chopping, one has not taken possession. The Gemara here uses the word *kana* — acquisition — but it is worth clarifying what this means, since the axe obviously remains the lender's property throughout. What Rav Huna means by "taking possession" is, at minimum, the point from which the owner can no longer retract — and possibly also the point of full liability, though as we will see, liability begins earlier.

The question the Gemara raises is whether Rav Huna's position represents only one side of a tannaitic debate. After all, we just established that in the context of loans, there are two views on when the lender loses the right to retract — either immediately upon handing over the money, or only once the borrower begins spending. Is Rav Huna, who says the borrower of the axe does not "take possession" until he begins using it, simply adopting one side of that debate?

### Loans vs. Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בְּמִלְוָה, דְּלָא הָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ. אֲבָל בִּשְׁאֵלָה, דְּהָדְרָה בְּעֵינַהּ, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – אִין, לֹא בִּיקַּע בּוֹ – לֹא קְנָאוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, they disagree only with regard to a loan, which is not returned intact.** Since there is no need for the debtor to return the same money to the lender, this means that the money has been transferred to the debtor's domain, and the first *tanna* holds that the lender may not retract the loan. **But with regard to** the **borrowing** of an item, for example the ax, **which is returned intact, everyone agrees** that if **one chopped with it, yes,** he has acquired it and is responsible for it. If **he has not chopped with it, he has not acquired it.**

The Gemara rejects this framing: *ad kan lo peligi ela bemilveh* — the tannaitic debate applies only to a monetary loan, not to a borrowed object. The reason is rooted in the fundamental nature of each transaction. A monetary loan is structured precisely so that the borrower will spend the specific coins and return different ones; from the moment the lender hands over the cash, he has in some sense relinquished his hold on those particular coins. One could therefore argue that his proprietary claim dissolves immediately. A borrowed object, by contrast, must be returned in its original form — *dehadra be'eina*, it comes back as itself. The lender's sense of ownership over that specific object remains intact throughout the loan period. Consequently, *divrei hakol* — by universal agreement — the borrower of a physical object does not extinguish the lender's right to retract until the borrower actually begins using it.

This also helps explain why the Gemara ruled the way it did on the monetary loan: the position that the lender loses his claim *immediately* upon handing over cash is grounded in the uniqueness of money, not in a general principle applicable to all forms of lending.

### Immediate Liability

Tosafot raises a complementary point. The Gemara earlier justified immediate liability for losses in the context of a monetary loan by analogy to borrowed objects — as soon as the object passes into the borrower's control, the *sho'el* is liable. Tosafot notes that the same logic must apply in the case of the borrowed axe: the moment it passes into the borrower's physical control, he is liable for any damages, even before he begins using it. This is in fact the source of the principle in the loan case. So in both the monetary loan and the borrowed object, liability for *onsin* — accidents and losses — attaches immediately upon transfer, even though the question of *chazarah* and full proprietary transfer may differ. The Gemara does not spell this out explicitly in this context, but Tosafot makes clear that the cases are parallel in this respect.

## Tannaitic Debate?

### Shtar Chov Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי? ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בִּשְׁטַר חוֹב״, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ מִלְוָה בְּיַד אֲחֵרִים וְהִירְשָׁהּ עֲלֵיהֶם, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that Rav's statement that one cannot betroth a woman with a loan **is subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** The *baraita* teaches: If one says to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with a promissory note, or if he had a loan in the possession of others and he authorized her to** collect the money for herself, **Rabbi Meir says she is betrothed and the Rabbis say she is not betrothed.**

Having settled the internal logic of *milveh le'hotza'ah nitnah* and its comparison to *she'elah*, the Gemara pivots to the central project of this sugya: can the ruling that *mekadesh bimilveh* is ineffective be shown to reflect a tannaitic debate, or is it universally agreed upon? The Gemara now attempts — in several stages — to locate a *machloket tannaim* that maps onto this question.

The first attempt introduces a *baraita* with two cases. In the first, a man betroths a woman by handing her a *shtar chov* — a document of debt. In the second, others owe him money and he authorizes her to collect on his behalf, thereby transferring the debt to her. Rabbi Meir holds she is betrothed (*mekudeshet*); the Sages hold she is not (*einah mekudeshet*). At first glance, particularly in the second case — where a concrete right of collection is being conferred — this looks like a genuine debate about whether a debt can function as *kesef kiddushin*, and thus a debate about *mekadesh bimilveh*.

### Whose Debt?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי שְׁטַר חוֹב הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא שְׁטַר חוֹב דַּאֲחֵרִים – הַיְינוּ מִלְוָה בְּיַד אֲחֵרִים! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁטַר חוֹב דִּידַהּ, וּבִמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of **this promissory note? If we say** it is **a promissory note of others** who owe him money, **this is** the same as **a loan in the possession of others,** and why would the *baraita* state it twice? **Rather, is it not** referring to **her promissory note,** i.e., a loan she has taken from him, and **they disagree with regard to one who betroths** a woman **with a loan,** whether forgiving the debt by returning the promissory note counts as betrothal money?

The Gemara zeroes in on the first case: what exactly is the *shtar chov* being handed over? If it is a document recording a debt that others owe the man — *shtar chov de'acheirim* — then that is simply a restatement of the second case, and the two cases collapse into one. The Gemara therefore entertains the possibility that the first case involves a *shtar chov deidah* — a document recording a debt that *she* owes *him*. In that case, by handing her the IOU, the man is effectively telling her to keep the money she owes him. That *would* be *mekadesh bimilveh*, and the tannaitic debate would map directly onto the question of whether such betrothal works.

The Gemara ultimately rejects this reading — it is unlikely that the *baraita* would use a single list to debate two logically distinct categories (her debt to him versus third parties' debt to him) without distinguishing between them. Instead, the Gemara concludes that both cases involve debts owed to the man by *others*, and the debate concerns entirely different sub-questions about how such a debt is transferred. The debate, in other words, is not really about *kiddushin* at all.

### Document Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם שְׁטַר חוֹב דַּאֲחֵרִים, וְהָכָא בְּמִלְוָה בִּשְׁטָר וּבְמִלְוָה עַל פֶּה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. בְּמִלְוָה בִּשְׁטָר בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי – בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי וְרַבָּנַן קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּתַנְיָא: אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין שֶׁכָּתַב וְלֹא מָסַר, בֵּין שֶׁמָּסַר וְלָא כָּתַב – לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב וְיִמְסוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Actually,** the *baraita* is referring to **a promissory note of others, and here** they **disagree** in two cases: **In** the case of **a loan with** a promissory **note and in** the case of **a loan by oral** agreement. The Gemara explains: **With regard to a loan with** a promissory **note, concerning what** matter **do they disagree? They disagree concerning the dispute between Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and the Rabbis, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Letters,** i.e., the content of a promissory note, **are acquired by** merely **transferring** the document. If the lender hands over a promissory note to a third party, the latter can collect the debt. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: Whether one wrote** a document of sale for the promissory note **but did not transfer** the promissory note itself, or **whether he transferred** the promissory note **but did not write** a document of sale for it, the recipient **has not acquired** the promissory note. The recipient acquires it only **once** the other **writes** a document of sale **and transfers** the promissory note.

With the *baraita* now read as involving only third-party debts, the Gemara asks: what exactly do Rabbi Meir and the Sages disagree about? When there is a written *shtar chov*, the mechanism of transfer is *otiyyot niknot bimesirah* — the rights embedded in a debt document are acquired by physically handing over the document. Rabbi says this is sufficient; the Sages (*Rabbanan*) say that mere transfer of the document is not enough — one must *both* write a supporting document (formally recording the new creditor) *and* hand over the *shtar*: *ad she'yichtov ve'yimsor*.

On this reading, the case involves a man who handed her the *shtar* but did not write supporting documentation. Rabbi Meir, following the opinion of *Rabbi* (Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi) that handing over the document suffices, holds she is *mekudeshet*. The Sages, who require both acts, hold she is not. This is a debate about the mechanics of *kinyan* in document transfers — not about *kiddushin*.

### Rav Pappa's Line

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לֵית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי, וְהָכָא בִּדְרַב פָּפָּא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַאן דְּזָבֵין שְׁטָרָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ, צָרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב לֵיהּ: ״קְנִי לָךְ הוּא וְכׇל שִׁעְבּוּדֵיהּ״. מָר אִית לֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, וּמָר לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead the following answer: **No one is of** the opinion that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and here** they **disagree with regard to** the statement **of Rav Pappa, as Rav Pappa says: This one who sells** a promissory **note to his friend must write** the following formula **for him: Let it be acquired by you, it and all its liens.** Otherwise, the debt discussed in the promissory note is not transferred. One **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **is of** the opinion that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Rav Pappa. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **is not of** the opinion that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Rav Pappa.** He holds that the debt is acquired even if one did not write this phrase, and a man can betroth a woman by giving a promissory note to her.

A second resolution is offered: perhaps everyone rejects Rabbi's position and agrees that both handing over and writing are required. On this reading, the man performed *both* acts — he handed her the document *and* wrote supporting documentation — but the question is whether the supporting documentation contained the precise formula required by Rav Pappa. For Rav Pappa rules: *hai man de'zavin shtar le'chavrei tzarich le'michtav lei — knei lach ihu ve'chol shibudei* — when transferring a debt document, the supporting text must explicitly state "take possession of it and all its liens." One side of the debate holds that this precise formulation is indispensable; the other holds it is not. Once again, the dispute has nothing to do with the law of *kiddushin*.

### Shmuel's Mechilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא וְהָכָא בְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמּוֹכֵר שְׁטַר חוֹב לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְחָזַר וּמְחָלוֹ – מָחוּל, וַאֲפִילּוּ יוֹרֵשׁ מוֹחֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Everyone is of** the opinion that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Rav Pappa,** and even Rabbi Meir agrees that she is betrothed only if he wrote in the document: Let it be acquired by you, it and all its liens. **And here** they **disagree with regard to** the statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel says:** In the case of **one who sells a promissory note to another, and** he, the seller, **went back and forgave** the debtor his debt, **it is forgiven,** since the debtor essentially had a non-transferable obligation to the creditor alone. **And even** the creditor's **inheritor can forgive** the debt.

A third resolution involves an entirely different dimension of the problem. Assume everyone agrees with Rav Pappa that the required formula was written, and that the transfer was fully executed. The Gemara now invokes Shmuel: *ha'mocher shtar chov le'chaveiro ve'chazar u'machalo — machul*. If one sells a debt document to another person, and the original creditor subsequently forgives the debt — *mochel* it — the forgiveness is legally effective, and the purchaser can no longer collect. The reasoning, at its core, is that a debt is fundamentally a personal obligation between debtor and original creditor. What passes to the buyer is not the debt itself in some fully abstracted sense, but rather the right to step into the creditor's position — perhaps as a kind of agent or substitute. Since the original creditor's claim never fully dissolves, he retains the power to be *mochel*.

Tosafot offers a related but differently framed explanation: *kinyan shtarot lav mi'de'orayta* — the transfer of debt documents has no biblical basis, only rabbinic. Therefore the rabbinic mechanism of transfer is not strong enough to fully uproot the original creditor's power (*lo alim le'afkuei koach harishon*). The conceptual formulation offered in the shiur — that the debtor fundamentally still owes the original creditor, and the buyer merely acquires some right to collect through or in place of that creditor — captures the same insight.

How is Shmuel's rule relevant to *kiddushin*? Even if the woman receives a fully transferred *shtar chov* of real market value, the original creditor retains the power to forgive the debt and leave her with nothing. This affects the *quality* of what she receives as *kesef kiddushin*.

### Samchat Da'ata

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, וְהָכָא בְּאִשָּׁה קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: אִשָּׁה סָמְכָה דַּעְתַּהּ, מֵימָר אָמְרָה: לָא שָׁבֵיק לֵיהּ לְדִידִי וּמָחֵל לֵיהּ לְאַחֲרִינֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: אִשָּׁה נָמֵי לָא סָמְכָה דַּעְתַּהּ
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion **of Shmuel, and here** they **disagree with regard to a woman** who is becoming betrothed. One **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that **a woman relies** on a promissory note she receives, since **she says** to herself: **He would not leave** aside **my** benefit **and forgive** the debt **of others. And** one **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **even a woman** who received the promissory note for betrothal **does not rely** on being able to collect the debt, since she is concerned he might forgive it.

This leads to the concept of *samchat da'ata* — settled confidence in the object of betrothal. The Gemara raises the possibility that even though the transferred debt has genuine market value (discounted, of course, for the risk of *mechilah*), the woman's lack of confidence in her ability to collect may itself be a disqualifying factor for *kesef kiddushin*. This is a striking argument: that something with real monetary worth, which could be sold on the open market for a price reflecting its discounted value, might nevertheless fail to operate as *kesef kiddushin* because of an anxiety about its ultimate collectibility.

The Gemara presents two levels of debate. The first is simply whether one holds like Shmuel at all — whether the original creditor genuinely retains the power to forgive. If not, the transferred debt is fully hers and *samchat da'ata* is unproblematic. The second level, introduced as an alternative framing, accepts that everyone holds like Shmuel, and asks instead whether the specific context of betrothal is different. Here the Gemara distinguishes between selling a field with a transferred debt — where no one, presumably, would feel confident, since the seller might turn around and forgive the debt to benefit the debtor at her expense — and the case of betrothal. The argument for *samchat da'ata* in betrothal is romantic but legally grounded: *meimar amra — lo shavik leididi u'machil le'achrinei* — she reasons that he would not deprive her, his new wife, by doing a favor for a third party. One opinion holds that this confidence is real and legally operative; the other holds that even in betrothal, the woman cannot be assumed to feel truly secure, and therefore the anxiety remains. Either way, the underlying debate is not about *kiddushin* per se but about the transferability of debt instruments and the reliability of *mechilah* — and the tannaitic *machloket* has not yet been established as a debate about *mekadesh bimilveh*.

## Oral Loans Transfer

### Ma'amad Shloshtan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמִלְוָה עַל פֶּה. בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? בִּדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ, תְּנֵהוּ לִפְלוֹנִי בְּמַעֲמַד שְׁלָשְׁתָּן – קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Having enumerated several possibilities for the dispute in the case of a loan with a promissory note, the Gemara explains the dispute in the case of a loan by oral agreement: In the case of one who betroths a woman **with a loan** by **oral** agreement, **with regard to what do they disagree?** They disagree **with regard to** the statement **of Rav Huna,** who quoted a statement that **Rav says, as Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** If one said to another: **I have one hundred dinars in your possession, give it to so-and-so,** if he stated this **in the presence of** all **three** parties, i.e., the one who had the money, the one who was the owner of the money, and the intended recipient, the intended recipient **has acquired** it.

The Gemara turns to the second case in the *baraita*: the oral loan (*milveh al peh*) — a debt owed by a third party that is not recorded in any document. Here even the mechanism of *otiyyot bimesirah* is unavailable. The Gemara invokes Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *maneh li be'yadcha — tenahu li'ploni bema'amad shloshtan* — if all three parties are present — the original creditor, the debtor, and the intended recipient — and the creditor says "that hundred you owe me, give it to so-and-so," the recipient (*koneh*) acquires the debt. This is a special form of *kinyan* — *ma'amad shloshtan* — enacted by the Sages for precisely this situation. The Gemara further notes, drawing on Tosafot's analysis, that this mechanism has an important advantage over the written transfer: Shmuel's rule of *mechilah* may not apply. When all three stand together and the creditor explicitly steps out of the picture, the new recipient is not merely a collector on behalf of the original creditor — the original creditor has, in some conceptual sense, fully substituted the recipient into his own position. He is out; the new party is in. Accordingly, the original creditor may no longer be *mochel* the debt.

### Oral vs. Physical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: כִּי קָאָמַר רַב הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּפִקָּדוֹן, אֲבָל מִלְוָה – לָא. וּמָר סָבַר: לָא שְׁנָא מִלְוָה וְלָא שְׁנָא פִּקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** One **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **when Rav said** that it can be acquired in the presence of all three parties, **this applies in** the case of **a deposit,** since the item is intact. **But** in the case of **a loan,** which is meant to be spent, he did **not** rule that it can be transferred this way, since there is no actual money but merely a debt. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds:** There **is no difference** whether it is **a loan or a deposit.** The third party acquires it in both cases. A woman is therefore betrothed if he gave her a loan in the above manner.

If *ma'amad shloshtan* is a fully effective transfer, why would it not work as *kesef kiddushin*? The debate, the Gemara says, is whether one accepts Rav Huna's ruling at all — and, if one does, whether it applies to oral debts as well as to objects in safekeeping (*pikadon*). One view holds that *ma'amad shloshtan* works only for a *pikadon* — a physical object being held — because it is easier to conceptualize the transfer of a physical thing when all parties are present and in agreement. A monetary debt, being an abstract obligation, cannot be transferred by this mechanism. The other view holds that there is no distinction: *lo shna milveh velo shna pikadon* — whether it is a debt or a physical deposit, *ma'amad shloshtan* works, and therefore she can be betrothed with it.

Once again, what appears to be a debate about betrothal is in reality a debate about the laws of transfer. The Gemara has now exhausted the *baraita*'s two cases — the *shtar chov* and the *milveh al peh* — without finding a genuine tannaitic *machloket* about *mekadesh bimilveh* itself.

## Shtar Kiddushin

### Document Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בִּשְׁטָר״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שָׁמִין אֶת הַנְּיָיר, אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again suggests: **Let us say** that Rav's statement that one cannot betroth a woman with a loan is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** A *baraita* teaches: If one says to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with a document, Rabbi Meir says she is not betrothed, and Rabbi Elazar says she is betrothed, and the Rabbis say** the court **appraises the paper** the document is written on: **If** the paper itself **has the value of one *peruta*, she is betrothed. But if not, she is not betrothed.**

The Gemara regroups and tries a different *baraita*. The case now involves *shtar kiddushin* — betrothal by document — specifically the scenario of *hiskadshi li beshtar*: a man says to a woman, "be betrothed to me with this document." The document is not a *shtar chov* at all; rather, it is the classic *shtar kiddushin*, the written declaration *harei at mekudeshet li*. The *baraita* records a three-way dispute. Rabbi Meir says she is not betrothed; Rabbi Eliezer says she is betrothed; the Sages evaluate the monetary worth of the paper itself — if the paper is worth a *perutah*, she is *mekudeshet*; if not, she is not.

The Gemara asks: what exactly is the *shtar* in this case? If it is a *shtar chov* of money others owe him, there is an immediate contradiction: in the earlier *baraita*, Rabbi Meir said she *is* betrothed; here Rabbi Meir says she is *not*. The positions would be reversed. Therefore, the Gemara concludes, the case here cannot be a third-party debt document. It must be something else — and that opens the door to Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's reading.

### Witness Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן כְּגוֹן שֶׁקִּדְּשָׁהּ בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים. וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי, וְרַבָּנַן מְסַפְּקָא לְהוּ אִי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אִי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, הִלְכָּךְ שָׁמִין אֶת הַנְּיָיר, אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says** that this is not necessarily so. **With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where he betrothed her with a document** of betrothal **that had no witnesses to it. And Rabbi Meir** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** with regard to a bill of divorce: **Signatory witnesses** on the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce, and the same applies to a document of betrothal. Since no witnesses signed the document, it cannot be used for betrothal. **And Rabbi Elazar** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says: Witnesses to the transmission** of the bill of divorce **effect** the divorce, and she is betrothed if the document was handed over in the presence of witnesses. **And the Rabbis are uncertain if** the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** or **if** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar. Therefore,** they rule that the betrothal is not effected by means of the document, but it is betrothal effected by means of giving an item worth money. The court **appraises the paper: If it has the value of one *peruta*, she is betrothed. But if not, she is not betrothed.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes: *keivan shekidsha beshtar she'ein lo eidim* — the case is where the man betrothed her with a *shtar kiddushin* that has no signed witnesses. This is a classic debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer — not about loans, but about the law of *shtarot*. Rabbi Meir holds *edei chasimah karsi* — the signed witnesses on a document are what give it legal force; without their signatures, it is not a valid *shtar*. Rabbi Eliezer holds *edei mesirah karsi* — it is sufficient that witnesses observed the handing over of the document; signatures are not required.

On this reading, Rabbi Meir says she is not betrothed because the unsigned *shtar* has no legal standing. Rabbi Eliezer says she is betrothed because the witnesses were present at the moment of delivery. The debate is the identical *machloket* these two *tannaim* conduct throughout the laws of *gittin* and *shtarot* — it has been transplanted here into *kiddushin*, but it is not a debate unique to betrothal.

### Paper's Value

Rashi notes a difficulty: if the Sages are uncertain whether to follow Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Eliezer, then without a *perutah*'s worth of paper, she should be *safek mekudeshet* (doubtfully betrothed), not definitively *einah mekudeshet*. Rashi therefore emends the conclusion: with a *perutah*-worthy paper she is *vadai mekudeshet*; without it, the matter is unresolved.

A further question arises: why would the Sages evaluate the paper at all, given that the man was not attempting to betroth her with the value of the paper — he was attempting *kiddushei shtar*? The answer appears to be that because there is a real possibility that the *shtar* worked as a genuine *shtar kiddushin* (following Rabbi Eliezer), and because the paper itself has monetary value, the Sages treat it as potentially doubling as *kiddushei kesef*. If the *shtar kiddushin* route was possibly effective, then the paper's monetary value provides an additional basis for betrothal. But if the *shtar* was definitively invalid and the paper was worth less than a *perutah*, there would be no betrothal on either ground.

### Shelo Lishmah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, כְּגוֹן שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, וּבִדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּבָעֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שְׁטַר אֵירוּסִין שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, מַהוּ? הֲוָיָה לִיצִיאָה מַקְּשִׁינַן, מָה יְצִיאָה בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ, אַף הֲוָיָה נָמֵי בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ. אוֹ דִלְמָא: הֲוָיוֹת לַהֲדָדֵי מַקְּשִׁינַן, מָה הֲוָיָה דְכֶסֶף לָא בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ, אַף הֲוָיָה דִשְׁטָר לָא בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that this *baraita* is referring to a case **where he wrote** the document of betrothal **not for her sake,** i.e., not for this particular woman's sake. **And they disagree with regard to** a statement **of Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish asks: What is** the *halakha* of **a document of betrothal that** the scribe **wrote not for her sake?** The Gemara clarifies the question: Do **we juxtapose becoming** a wife through betrothal **to leaving** a marriage through divorce and say: **Just as** in the document that causes her to **leave** the marriage **we require** that it be written **for her sake, so too,** in the document that causes her to **become** betrothed **we require** that it be written **for her sake as well. Or perhaps we juxtapose** different ways of **becoming** betrothed **to each other: Just as** in the act of **becoming** betrothed by means **of money we do not require** that money be minted **for her sake** and he can use any money, **so too,** with regard to the act of **becoming** betrothed by means **of a document, we do not require** that it be written **for her sake.**

The Gemara offers an alternative framework for the same dispute. Setting aside the witness question, the debate may instead concern *lishmah* — whether a *shtar kiddushin* must be written specifically with *this* woman in mind. The scenario: a man writes out a *harei at mekudeshet li* document in advance, before identifying his intended bride, and later produces it on a date. Is such a document valid?

This question was raised by Reish Lakish.

### Lishmah Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דְּבַעְיַהּ הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: ״וְיָצְאָה... וְהָיְתָה״, מַקִּישׁ הֲוָיָה לִיצִיאָה. מָר אִית לֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, וּמָר לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **After** Reish Lakish **raised the dilemma,** he **then resolved it:** The verse states: **"And she departs** out of his house and goes **and becomes** another man's wife" (Deuteronomy 24:2). The verse thereby **juxtaposes** the verb **becoming to** the verb **leaving,** so a bill of divorce and a document of betrothal must be written for her sake to be valid. **One Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **is of** the opinion that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Reish Lakish,** and maintains that she is not betrothed if the document was not written for her sake. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, **is not of** the opinion that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Reish Lakish.**

Reish Lakish formulated the question as a *beia*: does the principle of *havayah liyetzi'ah makishna* — comparing betrothal to divorce — require that a *shtar kiddushin* be written *lishmah*, just as a *get* must be written *lishmah* (derived from *ve'chasav lah*, "and he shall write for her")? Or does one instead compare one form of *kiddushin* to another — *havayah le'hadadi makishna* — in which case, since *kiddushei kesef* (monetary betrothal) requires no *lishmah* (money is not minted with a specific woman in mind), *kiddushei shtar* similarly requires none?

The Gemara reports that after posing the question, Reish Lakish himself resolved it: *veyatze'ah ve'hayetah — makish havayah liyetzi'ah* — the comparison to divorce is the operative one, and *lishmah* is required for a *shtar kiddushin*. The tannaitic debate in the *baraita* would then be between one who accepts Reish Lakish's conclusion and one who does not. Rabbi Meir, requiring *lishmah*, rules she is not betrothed when the document was written *shelo lishmah*. The other opinion, presumably Rabbi Eliezer, does not require *lishmah* and holds she is betrothed. This reading has an additional advantage: it preserves the exact same Rabbi Meir / Rabbi Eliezer pairing as the previous explanation, lending it structural coherence.

### Lishmah Lo Mida'atah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ לִשְׁמָהּ וְשֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ. וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרָבָא וְרָבִינָא, וְרַב פָּפָּא וְרַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּאִיתְּמַר: כְּתָבוֹ לִשְׁמָהּ וְשֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ, רָבָא וְרָבִינָא אָמְרִי: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא אָמְרִי: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone is of** the opinion that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion **of Reish Lakish, and with what are we dealing here?** With a case **where he wrote** the document **for her sake but unbeknownst to her. And** they **disagree concerning the dispute between Rava and Ravina, and Rav Pappa and Rav Sherevya, as it was stated** that these *amora'im* engaged in a dispute with regard to the following issue: If **he wrote** a document of betrothal **for her sake but unbeknownst to her** and gave it to her for betrothal, **Rava and Ravina say she is betrothed; Rav Pappa and Rav Sherevya say she is not betrothed.**

The Gemara pushes further. What if everyone agrees with Reish Lakish that *lishmah* is required, and the document *was* written with this specific woman in mind — but without her knowledge or awareness? This is the case of *lishmah ve'shelo mida'atah*: he writes *harei at mekudeshet li* while thinking of her specifically, perhaps months before proposing, without her having any idea.

Would this satisfy the *lishmah* requirement? The question has conceptual depth. A *get* need not be written with the wife's knowledge — divorce is a unilateral act and she is entirely passive. But *kiddushin* is different: she must consent, and she is, in some sense, a party to the transaction. Moreover, there is a subtle structural point: in a standard sale, the *shtar* is written by the *seller*. In betrothal, the man writes *harei at mekudeshet li* — yet it is the woman who is in the position of the seller (she is "selling" herself into the marital bond). Since she is the functional *ba'al hashtar*, one could argue that the document must emerge with her awareness, if not by her hand.

This question is the subject of a dispute among *Amoraim*: Rabbah and Ravina hold she is *mekudeshet* — the document is valid even without her awareness. Rav Papa and Rav Sharvya hold she is *einah mekudeshet* — the lack of her awareness is disqualifying. The tannaitic *baraita* may reflect this very debate, with Rabbi Meir and the Chachamim divided on whether the woman's awareness at the time of writing is a precondition for a valid *shtar kiddushin*.

In any case, the Gemara has now demonstrated that the tannaitic debate in the *baraita* is about the internal requirements of *kiddushei shtar* — witness signatures, *lishmah*, or the woman's awareness — and not about *mekadesh bimilveh*. The attempt to locate *mekadesh bimilveh* in a tannaitic dispute has again come up empty.

## Labor as Kiddushin

### Jewelry Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״עֲשֵׂה לִי שִׁירִים נְזָמִים וְטַבָּעוֹת, וְאֶקַּדֵּשׁ אֲנִי לָךְ״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ מָמוֹן לְיָדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again suggests: **Let us say** that Rav's statement that one cannot betroth a woman with a loan is **subject to** a dispute between **these *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a woman gave gold to a goldsmith, instructing him: **Make bracelets, earrings, and rings for me, and I will be betrothed to you** as payment for your work, **once he has made them she is betrothed;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: She is not betrothed until money enters her possession.**

The Gemara makes one final attempt to root *mekadesh bimilveh* in a tannaitic debate, this time using a case involving labor. A woman approaches a man who is a jeweler and says: fashion for me earrings, rings, and bracelets from my gold and silver — *ve'ekadesh ani lach* — and in exchange for your service, I will be betrothed to you. He performs the work and hands the finished jewelry back to her. Has he given her anything? The answer requires careful analysis. Her gold is still her gold — it was never his. The increase in value that his workmanship produced belongs, in the ordinary case, to the owner of the raw material. All that he has rendered is his labor. The question is how to characterize the legal relationship between that labor and the proposed betrothal.

### When Debt Incurs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מָמוֹן הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן, מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אֲפִילּוּ אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן נָמֵי לָא! אֶלָּא בְּמַאי כּוּ מִקַּדְּשָׁא? אֶלָּא לָאו בְּמָמוֹן אַחֵר, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of **this money** mentioned by the Rabbis? **If we say** it means **that** very **money,** i.e., the jewelry she requested, then **by inference the first *tanna*,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that **even that** same **money is also not** required to be given for her to be betrothed, but she is betrothed as soon as he made the jewelry. **But with what** does he betroth her? It is only **with** the jewelry that she **is betrothed,** since he has given her nothing but the jewelry. **Rather, is** the statement of the Rabbis **not** referring to when he betroths her **with** the **other money,** i.e., the payment she owes him for his service, and they hold that she is not betrothed? **And conclude from it** that Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to one who betroths** a woman **with a loan,** since the payment she owes him for making the rings is like a loan.

A three-way structure emerges in the *baraita*. Rabbi Meir says: *keivan she'asah — mekudeshet* — once he completes the work and presumably hands over the jewelry, she is betrothed. The Sages say she is not betrothed *until money reaches her hands* (*ad sheyagia mamon leyadah*). The Gemara immediately interrogates what "money" refers to. If it means the jewelry itself — the physical objects now completed and returned — then does Rabbi Meir's position imply she is betrothed even *before* the jewelry is handed back? That would be startling: what exactly has she received at that earlier moment that could serve as *kesef kiddushin*?

### Schirus Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבְרִי דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף וְהָוֵה מִלְוָה. מַאי לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמָר סָבַר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וּמָר סָבַר: הַמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **And** it must be **they hold that everyone agrees** that the obligation to pay **a wage is** incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end,** i.e., the obligation to pay for a service begins when the hired party starts to work, and the sum owed increases as he proceeds. **And it is** therefore **a loan,** as when he gives her the finished article she was already obligated to pay for the work he had performed earlier. **What, is it not** the case that they **disagree with regard to this,** i.e., **that** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is betrothed, and** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed?**

The Gemara's resolution introduces a crucial conceptual distinction: *yeshnah li'skhirus mitechilah ve'ad sof* versus *einah li'skhirus ella besof*. Does the wage debt accrue continuously from the very beginning of the work — so that by the time the job is done, what exists is a loan (the accumulated debt for services already rendered)? Or does no debt come into being at all until the work is completed and handed over — in which case, at the moment of completion, the craftsman is giving her the *service itself* rather than forgiving a debt?

This is the pivot on which the entire case turns. If the debt was accruing all along (*yeshnah mitechilah ve'ad sof*), then at the moment of completion, the only thing the man can do is forgive the debt she owes him — which is *mekadesh bimilveh*, and which everyone agrees does not work. If the debt arises only at the end (*einah ella besof*), then at the moment of completion the man is giving her his service as a gift — not forgiving a pre-existing debt but providing something new and concrete — and *that* could work as *kesef kiddushin*.

On this reading, Rabbi Meir holds *einah li'skhirus ella besof*: the service is given at the end and is a genuine gift of value. The Sages hold *yeshnah mitechilah ve'ad sof*: by the time he is done, it has always been a debt, and *mekadesh bimilveh einah mekudeshet*. The tannaitic debate, then, is not about *mekadesh bimilveh* per se — everyone may agree that does not work — but about when the wage obligation crystallizes.

### Artisan's Share

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְהָכָא – בְּיֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף קָמִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: אֵינָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת אֶלָּא בַּסּוֹף. וּמָר סָבַר: יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it is possible that **everyone** agrees that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed, and here** they in fact **disagree** over the question of whether or not the obligation to pay **a wage is** incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds:** The obligation to pay **a wage is** incurred **only at the end** of the labor, when he returns the item to her. Since it is at this stage that he forgives the money due him and converts it to money for betrothal, it was never considered to be a loan. **And** one **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds:** The obligation to pay **a wage is** incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end,** at which point the debt is viewed as a loan that has accumulated throughout the period of hire.

The Gemara offers yet another reading. Assume everyone holds *yeshnah li'skhirus mitechilah ve'ad sof* — the debt accrues from the beginning — and assume everyone agrees that *mekadesh bimilveh* is invalid. The remaining debate concerns *uman koneh be'shvach keli*: does the craftsman acquire a proprietary share in the enhanced object by virtue of his labor?

This principle holds that when an artisan improves an object and is being paid for the job rather than by the hour, he does not merely become a creditor of the owner. Instead, he acquires a partial ownership stake in the improved object proportional to the enhancement his work produced. He must then, as it were, "sell" that stake back to the owner upon completion. If *uman koneh be'shvach keli* applies, then what the jeweler transfers to the woman at the end of the job is not simply a forgiven debt but a tangible proprietary interest — his partial ownership in the finished ring — and *that* is a real and concrete object of value that can serve as *kesef kiddushin*.

Rabbi Meir holds *uman koneh be'shvach keli*: the artisan owns a share in the ring, and what he gives her is that ownership stake — genuine *kesef kiddushin*. The Sages hold *eino uman koneh be'shvach keli*: he owns nothing in the ring, all he has is money owed to him, and giving it up is *mekadesh bimilveh*.

### Milveh U'Perutah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֵין אוּמָּן קוֹנָה בִּשְׁבַח כְּלִי, וְיֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִלָּה וְעַד סוֹף, וּמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּמִלְוָה אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוֹסִיף לָהּ נוֹפֶךְ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ. דְּמָר סָבַר: מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה, דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַפְּרוּטָה, וּמָר סָבַר: דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַמִּלְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **a craftsman does not acquire** ownership rights **through enhancement of the vessel, and** also that the obligation to pay **a wage is** incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end. And** everyone also agrees that in the case of **one who betroths** a woman **with a loan, she is not betrothed. And with what are we dealing here?** A case **where he added a jewel [*nofekh*] of his own for her, as** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that if a man betroths a woman with **a loan and one *peruta*, his mind** is focused **on the *peruta*. And** one **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **his mind** is focused **on the loan.** Here too, the question is whether her intention is to become betrothed with the payment for the work, which is a loan, or with the jewel he added.

The Gemara adds one further layer. Assume everyone agrees *uman koneh be'shvach keli* does not apply, and *yeshnah li'skhirus mitechilah ve'ad sof* does apply, so the artisan has only a monetary debt and cannot betroth her with it. Nevertheless: what if, out of affection, he added a small precious stone of his own — something not contracted for, not part of the original agreement? Can he betroth her with that addition?

This is the case of *milveh u'perutah*: a large loan being forgiven (which cannot work) combined with a small concrete addition of actual value (which potentially could). The debate is whether *da'atei aperutah* — his intention, and halachic characterization, is focused on the concrete *perutah*-sized addition — or whether *da'atei amilveh* — the dominant element is the loan he is forgiving, and the small addition is legally trivial, swept up in and overshadowed by the invalid larger transaction. This parallels, though does not perfectly replicate, the earlier question of whether the paper in a *shtar kiddushin* can function as *kesef kiddushin* when the man's primary intent was betrothal by document.

### Past/Future Labor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמָּךְ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁאֶעֱשֶׂה עִמָּךְ״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁאֶעֱשֶׂה עִמָּךְ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמָּךְ״. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא אוֹמֵר: בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ: בֵּין ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי״, בֵּין ״בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁאֶעֱשֶׂה עִמָּךְ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאִם הוֹסִיף לָהּ נוֹפֶךְ מִשֶּׁלּוֹ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** they disagree **in the dispute** between **these *tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one says to a woman: Be betrothed to me **with the payment** for that **which I have worked for you, she is not betrothed** because it is a loan, since she already owes him the money. But if he said: Be betrothed to me **with the payment** for that **which I will work for you, she is betrothed,** as at the moment he becomes entitled to the money it is considered as though he gives it to her for her betrothal. **Rabbi Natan says:** If he says: **With the payment** for that **which I will work for you, she is not betrothed,** as Rabbi Natan holds that the obligation to pay a wage is incurred continuously from the beginning of the period he was hired to its end, which means that by the end of the job it is a loan. **And** this is **all the more so** if he says: **With the payment** for that **which I have worked for you. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says: Actually, they said** that the *halakha* is that **whether** he said: **With the payment** for that **which I have worked for you, or: With the payment** for that **which I will work for you, she is not betrothed. But if he added a *nofekh* of his own for her, she is betrothed.**

The tannaitic parallel is now made explicit. A *baraita* records: *bischar she'asasi imach — einah mekudeshet*: "with the wages you owe me for labor I have already performed" — she is not betrothed. This is straightforwardly *mekadesh bimilveh*. *Bischar she'e'eseh imach — mekudeshet*: "with the wages you will owe me for labor I will perform" — she is betrothed, according to the *Tanna Kamma*. This corresponds to the view that *einah li'skhirus ella besof* — no debt exists yet, and the service to be rendered is a genuine future gift.

Rabbi Natan disagrees: even *bischar she'e'eseh imach* does not work — once performed, it will always have been a debt. Rabbi Yehuda Nasi goes further and rules that *both* formulations fail, whether past or future labor. However, Rabbi Yehuda Nasi adds: *ve'im hosif lah nofach mishelo — mekudeshet* — if he added something of his own, she is betrothed.

The Gemara maps out the internal structure. Between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Natan, the debate is *yeshnah li'skhirus mitechilah ve'ad sof* versus *einah ella besof*. Between Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Yehuda Nasi, the debate is *milveh u'perutah*: Rabbi Natan holds *da'atei amilveh* even when a *perutah* is added; Rabbi Yehuda Nasi holds *da'atei aperutah* and the small addition rescues the transaction.

### Final Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּין תַּנָּא קַמָּא לְרַבִּי נָתָן – אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ שְׂכִירוּת, בֵּין רַבִּי נָתָן לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא – אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה. מָר סָבַר: מִלְוָה וּפְרוּטָה, דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַמִּלְוָה, וּמָר סָבַר: דַּעְתֵּיהּ אַפְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dispute: The difference **between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Natan** concerns **a wage:** Is the obligation incurred continuously or only at the end? The difference **between Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi** is the issue of **a loan and one *peruta*.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Natan, **holds** that if a man betroths a woman with **a loan and one *peruta* his mind** is focused **on the loan,** and his jewel is disregarded. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **his mind** is focused **on the *peruta*,** and she is betrothed with the jewel.

In each stage of the *baraita*, the Gemara works to show that *nobody* is actually debating *mekadesh bimilveh* on its own terms. The *Tanna Kamma* who permits future-labor betrothal is not saying *mekadesh bimilveh* works — he is saying no debt existed yet, so this is not *milveh* at all. Rabbi Yehuda Nasi who permits when a *nofach* is added is not saying *mekadesh bimilveh* works — he is saying the small concrete addition is what effects the *kiddushin*. The Gemara thus arrives at its conclusion: *mekadesh bimilveh einah mekudeshet* — a man cannot betroth a woman with an existing monetary debt she owes him. This ruling is undisputed. All of the tannaitic debates that appeared to bear on the question turn out, upon careful analysis, to concern something else entirely — and Rav's position stands as universally accepted *halacha*.
