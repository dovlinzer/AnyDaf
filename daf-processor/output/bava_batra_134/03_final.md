# Bava Batra 134 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Disinheritance Ethics

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכּוֹתֵב אֶת נְכָסָיו לַאֲחֵרִים וְהִנִּיחַ אֶת בָּנָיו – מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי, אֶלָּא אֵין רוּחַ חֲכָמִים נוֹחָה הֵימֶנּוּ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אִם לֹא הָיוּ בָּנָיו נוֹהֲגִים כַּשּׁוּרָה – זָכוּר לַטּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who wrote** a document granting **his property to others** as a gift **and left his sons** with nothing, **what he did is done,** i.e., it takes effect; **but the Sages are displeased with him. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: If** he did so because **his sons were not acting properly, he is remembered positively.**

The *daf* opens in the middle of a sustained discussion about a dying person's power to redistribute property among his heirs, a theme that has occupied the preceding pages. The immediately prior material dealt with cases in which a father can identify which of his sons is the *bechor*, relying on the *pasuk* of *ki es habechor ben hasnuah yakir* — the father must recognize and declare who the firstborn is. Even a child presumed to be younger can be declared the *bechor* through the father's authoritative identification. The *mishna* now before us continues in this spirit, though it pivots toward a different and more dramatic scenario: the complete disinheritance of one's children.

### Writing Away Property

This ruling is, on its surface, striking in its internal tension. The preceding discussion labored to develop the mechanisms by which a person *can* redistribute property: as long as the language of *matanah* (gift) appears somewhere in the document, even alongside the word *yerusha* (inheritance), the rabbis interpret the transaction as a gift and thereby grant the person the power to allocate his estate as he sees fit. The institution of *matnas shechiv meira* — the deathbed gift — represents an even more expansive expression of rabbinic willingness to empower a dying person, dispensing with the ordinary requirement of a formal *kinyan* and treating a mere verbal declaration as legally binding.

### Redistribution Tension

Given all of this, the *mishna*'s expression of rabbinic displeasure is puzzling. One way to reconcile these two currents is to draw a distinction: the mechanisms of redistribution were designed for cases where a person adjusts proportions among his children or channels some property to a worthy recipient, whereas this *mishna* addresses the extreme case of complete disinheritance — leaving the children with nothing at all. That reading preserves both voices within a coherent framework. Alternatively, one might read these as genuinely competing voices: one more pragmatic and empowering, the other registering a moral reservation even about the very mechanisms that were created. It would be unusual for the *halacha* to simultaneously construct an institution and express displeasure at its exercise, yet both positions would be possible, and it is not impossible that the legal tradition is holding both in tension.

### Shimon ben Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם לֹא הָיוּ בָּנָיו נוֹהֲגִים כַּשּׁוּרָה – זָכוּר לַטּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** If **his sons were not acting properly, he is remembered positively.**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds a counterpoint: *im lo hayu banav nohagim kashura zachur latov* — if his sons were not behaving properly, the father who disinherited them should actually be remembered for good. That is, the displeasure of the rabbis is not absolute; it yields when the children have proven themselves undeserving. Indeed, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel seems to endorse the act of disinheritance under such conditions as the morally correct course.

### Worthiness Theme

This position evokes a tension that runs throughout the *Torah* narrative but is not resolved in its formal *halachos*. In the stories of the *Torah* — from *Kayin* and *Hevel* through *Avraham*, *Yitzchak*, and *Yaakov*, and culminating in *bnei bechori Yisrael* — the status of *bechor*, the one who inherits the family's legacy, belongs not to the biological firstborn but to the deserving child, the chosen one. Yet the formal *halacha* of the *Torah* states explicitly *lo yuchal levaker es ben ha'ahuva al pnei ben hasnuah habechor* — a father may not reassign the *bechor* status away from the actual firstborn. The rabbinic mechanisms of *matnas shechiv meira* and *matanah* create a workaround that effectively vindicates the narrative value, even if they cannot touch the formal law. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel goes further: he articulates the underlying principle explicitly. Property should go to those who are deserving of it, and if the children have disqualified themselves through their behavior, directing the inheritance elsewhere is not merely permissible but praiseworthy.

## Yosef ben Yoezer

### Case Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מִי פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Do the Rabbis disagree with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** maintaining that depriving one's children of their inheritance is inappropriate in any event, **or not?**

The *gemara* now raises a question: do the *rabanan* dispute Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, or do they fundamentally agree with him — conceding that while disinheritance is generally disapproved of, it is vindicated when the sons are unworthy? The *gemara* attempts to resolve this through a narrative case.

### Yoezer's Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּיוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר הָיָה לוֹ בֵּן שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה נוֹהֵג כַּשּׁוּרָה. הֲוָה לֵיהּ עִילִּיתָא דְּדִינָרֵי, קָם אַקְדְּשַׁהּ. אֲזַל נְסֵיב בַּת גָּאדֵיל כְּלִילֵי דְּיַנַּאי מַלְכָּא. אוֹלִידָה דְּבֵיתְהוּ, זַבֵּין לַהּ בִּינִיתָא. קַרְעַהּ, אַשְׁכַּח בָּהּ מַרְגָּלִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear, as Yosef ben Yo'ezer had a son who was not acting properly.** Yosef ben Yo'ezer **had a vessel [*illiyyata*]** full **of dinars,** and **he arose and consecrated it** to the Temple treasury, depriving his son of his inheritance. His son **went and married the daughter of King Yannai's crown weaver.** After the son's **wife gave birth, he bought her a fish [*binita*]. He tore its** stomach open and **found a pearl in it.** He decided to sell it.

*Ta shema* — come and hear. Yosef ben Yoezer had a son *shelo haya noheg kashura* — who was not walking the straight and narrow, using the same phrase as the *mishna*. Yosef ben Yoezer possessed an *alita* of gold *dinarei*. Tosafos notes that *alita* ordinarily means an attic-full, which would be an exaggeration, and therefore reads it as a basketful. In any case, this represented a significant portion of his estate, and Yosef ben Yoezer went and sanctified it — *kam akdeshei* — to the *beis hamikdash*, effectively keeping it out of his son's hands.

It is worth pausing to note that the story does not necessarily imply total disinheritance. There is no indication that this basket of *dinarei* constituted Yosef ben Yoezer's entire estate. The *mishna*'s criticism applied to complete disinheritance; this story may represent a more partial redirection. Whether the *mishna*'s displeasure extends to partial disinheritance of this kind is a question the story leaves open.

### Son's Pearl Story

The narrative continues. After the father's act of sanctification, the son went and married a woman — the daughter of the person who wove the ceremonial wreaths for Yannai the king — a position of considerable prestige. When his wife gave birth, the son purchased a fish as a celebratory gift. Opening the fish, he found within it a *margalita* — a precious jewel or gem. The story carries echoes of the tale of Yosef Mokir Shabbos, where a similar miraculous find occurs within a fish.

### Wife's Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לָא תַּמְטְיַיהּ לְמַלְכָּא, דְּשָׁקְלִי לַהּ מִינָּךְ בִּדְמֵי קַלִּילֵי; זִיל אַמְטְיַיהּ לְגַבֵּי גִּזְבָּרֵי. וְלָא תְּשַׁיְּימַהּ אַתְּ, דַּאֲמִירָתוֹ לְגָבוֹהַּ כִּמְסִירָתוֹ לְהֶדְיוֹט, אֶלָּא לְשַׁיְּימוּהָ אִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** His wife **said to him: Do not bring it to** the treasury of **the king** to sell it, **as they will take it from you for an insignificant** sum of **money.** Rather, **go bring it to the** Temple **treasurers. And do not appraise it yourself, as declaration to the Most High is equivalent to transfer to an ordinary** person, and if you offer to sell it for an amount less than its worth, you will not be able to change your mind. **Rather, let them appraise it.**

The son's wife counseled him with considerable practical wisdom. Do not bring the gem to the king, she said — *de-shaklei lei minach bi-dmei kelilei* — the royal officers will lowball you, and once you are negotiating with the palace, you will find yourself unable to refuse whatever offer they make. Her father, after all, worked in the king's service and she knew how these transactions went.

Instead, she advised: go to the *gizbari* — the treasurer — of the *beis hamikdash*, and crucially, *v'lo teshaymi at* — do not be the one to suggest a price. The principle underlying her advice is *amiraso l'gavo k'mesireso l'hedyot*: your verbal declaration to *hekdesh* is like a physical transfer in the secular domain. If you name a figure, you may find yourself bound by it in ways you did not anticipate.

Tosafos raises an objection here: why would naming a price bind him? After all, a verbal statement alone does not create a *kinyan* with *hekdesh* — there has been no *meshicha*, no transfer of funds. Tosafos resolves this by reframing the concern. The problem is not that naming a price creates a binding sale. Rather, if the gem is worth one hundred thousand and the son declares forty thousand, he has implicitly *donated* the sixty-thousand difference to the *beis hamikdash*. The laws of *ona'ah* that would ordinarily void such a transaction do not apply to *hekdesh*. So the wife's concern was not a technical one about contractual commitment but about inadvertent donation through undervaluation. Her practical advice — let *them* name the price — reflects both financial prudence and a substantive legal awareness. The Rashbam adds that the *gizbari* of the *beis hamikdash* were honest assessors who would give a fair valuation.

### Son's Character

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְטְיַיהּ, שָׁמוּהָ בִּתְלֵיסְרֵי עִלִּיָּאתָא דְּדִינָרֵי. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: שְׁבַע אִיכָּא, שֵׁית לֵיכָּא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׁבַע הַבוּ לִי, שֵׁית הֲרֵי הֵן מוּקְדָּשׁוֹת לַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **He brought it** to the Temple treasury, and **they appraised it as** having the value of **thirteen vessels [*illiyyata*]** full **of dinars.** The treasurers **said to him: There are seven** *illiyyata* of dinars at our disposal to pay you for the pearl, but **there are not** an additional **six. He said to** the treasurers: **Give me** the **seven** in exchange for the pearl, and as for the additional **six** that you owe me, **they are hereby consecrated to Heaven.**

The *gizbari* assessed the gem at thirteen baskets of gold *dinarei* — recall that his father had sanctified only one basket. They told him: *sheva ika, sheis leka* — we have seven available to pay you now, six we do not have on hand. The son replied: give me the seven you have, and the six you lack — *harei mukdashim l'shamayim* — I am donating back to the *beis hamikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמְדוּ וְכָתְבוּ: יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר הִכְנִיס אַחַת, וּבְנוֹ הִכְנִיס שֵׁשׁ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: יוֹסֵף בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר הִכְנִיס אַחַת, וּבְנוֹ הוֹצִיא שֶׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** The treasurers **arose and wrote: Yosef ben Yo'ezer bestowed one** *illiyyata* to the Temple treasury, **and his son bestowed six. And there are** those **who say** that they wrote: **Yosef ben Yo'ezer bestowed one** *illiyyata* to the Temple treasury, **and his son removed seven,** which he received for the pearl.

The *gemara* records two versions of how the treasurers then entered this transaction in their ledgers. In the first version, they wrote: *Yosef ben Yoezer hichnis achas* — Yosef ben Yoezer brought in one basket — *uveno hichnis sheish* — and his son brought in six. This reading casts the son as a *tzaddik*, one who returned six baskets to the *beis hamikdash*. In the second version — *ika d'amri* — they wrote: *Yosef ben Yoezer hichnis achas, uveno hotzi sheva* — the son *took out* seven. The Rashbam explains that according to this second reading, the son would have taken all thirteen had the *beis hamikdash* been able to produce them; he donated back only what they could not pay. Far from being generous, he emptied out all of their available funds.

### Ambiguity Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּקָא אָמְרִי ״הִכְנִיס״, מִכְּלָל דְּשַׁפִּיר עֲבַד! אַדְּרַבָּה, מִדְּקָא אָמְרִי ״הוֹצִיא״, מִכְּלָל דְּלָאו שַׁפִּיר עֲבַד! אֶלָּא מֵהָא לֵיכָּא לְמִשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **From** the fact **that they said** approvingly that Yosef ben Yo'ezer's son **bestowed** six, **by inference, he acted well** when he left him out of his inheritance. The Gemara responds: **On the contrary; from** the fact **that** according to the second account, **they said** disparagingly that he **removed** seven, **by inference,** Yosef ben Yo'ezer **did not act well** when he left him out of his inheritance, as he caused money to be removed from the Temple treasury. **Rather, no** inference is **to be learned from this** story with regard to the dilemma as to whether the Rabbis agree with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, as the two accounts contradict each other on this matter.

The *gemara* now attempts to extract a conclusion about the original question — whether the father's decision to disinherit was vindicated — from either reading of the story. If the son turned out to be a *tzaddik*, one might naturally conclude that the father misjudged his son and the disinheritance was unjustified. The *gemara*, however, reads it differently: perhaps the father's very act of cutting the son off caused the son to do *teshuvah* and improve. If that is so, then the son's goodness is itself evidence that the father's decision was correct.

Conversely, if the son is read as a bad actor who took everything he could from the *beis hamikdash*, one might conclude that the father was right to disinherit him. But the *gemara* reads even this in reverse: perhaps the bad son is evidence that the father did something wrong to begin with — the father stripped the son of his inheritance, and the son in turn stripped the *beis hamikdash* of its funds; the father set a pattern of wrongful taking that the son continued.

The *gemara* concludes: *me'ha leka lemishma mina* — we cannot draw any clear lesson from this case. Either reading of the son's character can be turned to support or undermine the father's original decision, and the case therefore proves nothing as a matter of policy about whether disinheritance of an unworthy child is appropriate.

## Shmuel's Stance

### Shmuel's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, לָא תְּיהַוֵּי בֵּי עַבּוֹרֵי אַחְסָנְתָּא; וַאֲפִילּוּ מִבְּרָא בִּישָׁא לִבְרָא טָבָא, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן מִבְּרָא לְבַרְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? **Come** and **hear, as Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: *Shinnana*, do not be in a house where inheritance is transferred** away from its rightful heir, **even** if it is transferred **from a wicked son to a good son, and all the more so** if it is transferred **from a son to a daughter.** Evidently, the Rabbis hold that inheritance should not be transferred in any case.

Having failed to resolve the question through the narrative of Yosef ben Yoezer, the *gemara* turns to a direct teaching. *De'amar lei Shmuel le'Rav Yehuda: shinana* — Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda, calling him "sharp one" — *lo tehei be'avurei achsanta*: do not be part of a household that transfers over an inheritance.

### Transfer Scope

Shmuel's ruling is notably expansive in its scope: *va'afilu mibra bisha le'bra tava* — even from a bad son to a good son. Even when the redistribution seems morally justified — redirecting inheritance from an unworthy heir to a worthy one — Shmuel disapproves. And *vechol sheken mibra le'varta* — certainly one should not take property from a son and redirect it to a daughter, which removes the estate entirely from its proper heirs. The phrase *avurei achsanta* is itself evocative, echoing the *pasuk* in the *Torah* — *veha'avartem es nachalaso le'visso* — which describes the transfer of inheritance to daughters when there are no sons.

### Broader Implications

The question of what it means to "not be part of" such a transfer is left somewhat open — presumably it means, as a communal leader or rabbi, one should censure those who do this and decline to facilitate it — but the person's legal ability to effectuate such a redistribution is not taken away. The law stands; the moral disapproval is registered alongside it.

This creates a notable irony, as Shmuel is also the authority who, in yesterday's discussion, made it possible to effectively disinherit a wife through the right legal maneuver. Here he is working to discourage the disinheritance of sons. The common thread is that Shmuel acknowledges the legal power while trying to orient moral expectations.

## Yonatan ben Uziel

### Gift Situation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ בָּנָיו נוֹהֲגִין כַּשּׁוּרָה, עָמַד וְכָתַב נְכָסָיו לְיוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל. מָה עָשָׂה יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל? מָכַר שְׁלִישׁ, וְהִקְדִּישׁ שְׁלִישׁ, וְהֶחֱזִיר לְבָנָיו שְׁלִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident involving one man whose children did not act properly. He arose and wrote** a document transferring all **his property to Yonatan ben Uzziel,** one of the Sages, as a gift. **What did Yonatan ben Uzziel do? He sold a third** of the property for his needs, **and consecrated a third** of the property, **and returned** the remaining **third to** the man's **children.**

The *gemara* continues with a direct illustration. *Ma'aseh b'adam echad shelo hayu banav nohagim kashura* — there was a case of a person whose sons were not behaving properly. He went and wrote his entire estate to Yonatan ben Uziel. Whether this was because Yonatan ben Uziel was a great Torah scholar, or for some other reason, the *gemara* will clarify. The essential point is that the father wished to keep the property out of his sons' hands while entrusting it to someone he respected.

### Yonatan's Method

Yonatan ben Uziel was not comfortable with the complete disinheritance of the sons. He therefore took a deliberate and carefully structured course of action: *machar shelish vehikdish shelish vehechzir l'vanav shelish* — he sold a third for his own purposes, consecrated a third to the *beis hamikdash*, and returned a third to the sons.

The purpose of the first two transactions was not primarily self-interest. Had Yonatan simply received the estate and immediately written it back to the sons, someone could argue — as indeed Shammai will argue — that the gift to Yonatan was never real, that he was merely a conduit, and therefore that the property never truly left the family. By first selling a portion and consecrating another portion, Yonatan demonstrated concretely that the gift was genuine — he truly owned it and disposed of it as an owner would. Only once that was established could he freely gift the remaining third to whomever he chose, including the sons.

### Shammai Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא עָלָיו שַׁמַּאי בְּמַקְלוֹ וְתַרְמִילוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ: שַׁמַּאי, אִם אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא אֶת מַה שֶּׁמָּכַרְתִּי וּמַה שֶּׁהִקְדַּשְׁתִּי – אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא מַה שֶּׁהֶחְזַרְתִּי, אִם לָאו – אִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא מַה שֶּׁהֶחְזַרְתִּי. אָמַר: הֵטִיחַ עָלַי בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל! הֵטִיחַ עָלַי בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** **Shammai came to** Yonatan ben Uzziel **with his staff and traveling bag** to protest his giving part of the property to the man's children against the deceased's wishes. Yonatan ben Uzziel **said to him: Shammai, if you can repossess** the property **that I sold** from the purchasers **and** the property **that I consecrated** from the Temple treasury, **you can repossess what I returned** to the man's children as well; but **if not,** as the property is mine and I have the right to do with it whatever I want, **you cannot repossess what I returned** to the man's children either. Shammai then **said: Ben Uzziel reprimanded me; ben Uzziel reprimanded me,** and I have no response.

Shammai came to Yonatan ben Uziel *bemaklo uvetarmilo* — with his staff and his pouch — and challenged him. Shammai's objection, though not spelled out in full in the text, was essentially that by returning property to the sons, Yonatan was subverting the father's intent, and that this demonstrated the gift to Yonatan had never been a genuine transfer of ownership — just as in the famous case of *Beit Choron*.

Yonatan ben Uziel's response was direct: *im ata yachol l'hotzi es ma shemacharti uma shehikdashti, ata yachol l'hotzi es ma shehechizarti* — if you are prepared to reverse my sale and my *hekdesh*, then you may also reverse what I returned to the sons. If those transactions were valid — if I truly owned this estate and disposed of portions of it freely — then the gift to the sons is equally valid. And since overturning an already-completed sale and a dedication to *hekdesh* would be practically unthinkable, the logical conclusion is that the gift to me was genuine, and my gift to the sons is therefore equally valid.

Shammai conceded: *hitiach alai ben Uziel, hitiach alai ben Uziel* — you have gotten the better of me, son of Uziel, you have gotten the better of me.

### Beit Choron Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי סָבַר? מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה דְּבֵית חוֹרוֹן. דִּתְנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבֵית חוֹרוֹן, בְּאֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה אָבִיו מוּדָּר הֵימֶנּוּ הֲנָאָה, וְהָיָה מַשִּׂיא בְּנוֹ; וְאָמַר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: הֲרֵי חָצֵר וּסְעוּדָה נְתוּנִין לְךָ בְּמַתָּנָה, וְאֵינָן לְפָנֶיךָ אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁיָּבֹא אַבָּא וְיֹאכַל עִמָּנוּ בַּסְּעוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did** Shammai **hold initially,** causing him to protest Yonatan ben Uzziel's behavior? The Gemara answers: He protested **due to the incident** that happened **in** the city of **Beit Ḥoron.** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 48a): **An incident** occurred **involving someone in** the city of **Beit Ḥoron whose father had vowed not to derive benefit from him, and** the son **was marrying off his** own **son** and wanted his father to be able to participate in the wedding meal. **And** he therefore **said to another:** The **courtyard** where the wedding will take place **and** the wedding **meal are hereby given to you as a gift, but they are** given **to you only so that my father will come and eat with us at the meal.** The son wanted to circumvent the prohibition imposed by the vow and enable his father to participate in the meal, so he transferred ownership to someone else for that purpose.

The *gemara* then inquires: what was Shammai's original reasoning? *Mishum ma'aseh d'Beit Choron* — because of the story of Beit Choron. The principle of Beit Choron is that a gift is not a genuine gift if the recipient has no real freedom to use it. In that story, a man whose father was prohibited by oath from deriving any benefit from him wished to invite his father to his son's wedding. He told his friend Michael: this banquet hall and all the food I have prepared are a *matanah* to you — and since it is now Michael's, the father would be eating Michael's food, not his son's food, and the oath would not be violated.

### Wedding Banquet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: אִם שֶׁלִּי הֵן – הֲרֵי הֵן מוּקְדָּשִׁין לַשָּׁמַיִם! אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא נָתַתִּי לְךָ אֶת שֶׁלִּי שֶׁתַּקְדִּישֵׁם לַשָּׁמַיִם! אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא נָתַתָּ לִי אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ אֶלָּא שֶׁתְּהֵא אַתָּה וְאָבִיךְ אוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין וּמְרַצִּין זֶה לָזֶה, וִיהֵא עָוֹן תָּלוּי בְּרֹאשׁוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The recipient **said to him: If they are mine, they are** all **hereby consecrated to Heaven,** i.e., the Temple, and are forbidden to everyone. The son **said to him** in anger: **I did not give you my** property **so that you should consecrate them to Heaven.** The recipient **said to him: You gave me your** property **only** so **that you and your father would eat and drink and** thereby **appease each other, and the sin** of transgressing the vow would **be hung on his,** i.e., my, **head,** as I enabled the transgression.

Michael refused to play along. He responded: if it is truly mine, *harei hem mukdashin lashamayim* — I will donate it to the *beis hamikdash*. The son protested: I did not give you all this so you could donate it! Michael replied: exactly — you did not give it to me at all. You gave it to me only so that you and your father could eat together and be reconciled; I would be violating the spirit of the entire arrangement if I used it for any other purpose, and the burden of that would fall on my head.

### Gift Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: כׇּל מַתָּנָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁאִם הִקְדִּישָׁהּ מוּקְדֶּשֶׁת – אֵינָהּ מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: In reference to this incident, **the Sages said: Any gift that is not** so absolute **that if** the recipient **were to consecrate it,** the gift **would be consecrated, is not a gift.** In other words, in order for it to be a gift the recipient must have the ability to consecrate it. Similarly, Shammai had initially reasoned that the gift to Yonatan ben Uzziel was not a valid gift, as its sole purpose was so that the property should not fall into the possession of the man's children. Once he discovered that Yonatan ben Uzziel consecrated part of the gift, he realized that it was, in fact, a valid gift, with which the recipient could do whatever he pleased.

The question this raises is whether Michael was right to be so scrupulous, or whether the larger good of reconciling father and son should have outweighed his concern for the technical spirit of the *nedarim*. But that is not the direction the *gemara* pursues. Instead, the rabbis state the governing principle: *kol matanah she'einah kedei she'im hikdishah mekudeshes einah matanah* — any gift that is not genuinely the recipient's to the extent that he could consecrate it to *hekdesh* is not a real gift.

Tosafos raises an important qualification here, drawing on the *Yerushalmi*. One might think this principle means that *any* gift in which the donor stipulates "do not consecrate it to *hekdesh*" is thereby invalid — but that cannot be right. A donor can attach any condition he wishes to a gift: he can say "on the condition you do not give this to your wife" or "on the condition you do not donate it." Those are valid conditions and the gift remains a good gift. What the Beit Choron story actually demonstrates is something subtler: not that the recipient literally could not be *makdish* the gift, but that the *totality* of the circumstances made clear the supposed gift was never genuinely transferred. The donor had given Michael no freedom whatsoever over the property — not to swap the guests, not to donate it, not to cancel the event. The "gift" was entirely a sham in which Michael was simply being asked to serve as a legal fiction. The *hekdesh* was merely the particular example Michael chose to illustrate the underlying reality: that he did not truly own anything.

Applied to the Yonatan ben Uziel case, Tosafos reads the circumstances as essentially parallel to Beit Choron. According to Tosafos, the father in this case had actually pronounced all of his property *assur behana'ah* on his son — forbidden for the son to derive any benefit from — and then had a change of heart. Rather than reversing the prohibition directly, he gifted the estate to Yonatan ben Uziel with the implicit intention that Yonatan would find a way to return some of it to the son. This would make the transaction virtually identical to Beit Choron: a nominal gift to an intermediary for the purpose of circumventing a prior restriction. Under these circumstances, Shammai's concern that the gift was not genuine was well-founded.

Yonatan ben Uziel, however — knowing the precedent of Beit Choron — structured his handling of the estate carefully. By actually selling a third and consecrating a third for his own purposes before returning anything to the sons, he demonstrated that he *did* treat the gift as genuine property, that it was not a sham, and that he exercised real dominion over it. It was on this basis that he rebutted Shammai: Shammai had not yet known that Yonatan had already sold and consecrated portions of the estate, and assumed the entire estate was being returned directly to the sons. Once Yonatan revealed what he had done, Shammai acknowledged he had no further argument.

### Resolution

The *gemara* thus arrives at a principle that synthesizes the legal and moral concerns of this entire discussion. A gift must constitute a genuine transfer of ownership — the recipient must have real freedom over the property — or it is no gift at all. The institutional mechanisms the rabbis created to allow redistribution of estates presuppose real transfers of ownership. Where the "gift" is merely a device to achieve an ulterior purpose while keeping the property under the effective control of the original owner or his directed intent, the rabbis will not give it legal effect. At the same time, once genuine ownership is demonstrated, the new owner may do with the property as he wishes — including directing some of it back to family members whom the original owner wished to exclude. Yonatan ben Uziel's structured response to the father's gift navigated these requirements with precision, satisfying both the legal demand for genuine ownership and his own moral commitment to not leaving the sons entirely disinherited.

## Hillel's Students

### Student Tiers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁמוֹנִים תַּלְמִידִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְהִלֵּל הַזָּקֵן; שְׁלֹשִׁים מֵהֶן רְאוּיִם שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁכִינָה כְּמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ, שְׁלֹשִׁים מֵהֶן רְאוּיִם שֶׁתַּעֲמוֹד לָהֶן חַמָּה כִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן, עֶשְׂרִים בֵּינוֹנִיִּים. גָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן – יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל, קָטָן שֶׁבְּכוּלָּן – רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos Yonatan ben Uzziel, the Gemara cites that **the Sages taught: Hillel the Elder had eighty students. Thirty of them** were sufficiently **worthy that the Divine Presence should rest upon them as** it did upon **Moses our teacher, thirty of them** were sufficiently **worthy that the sun should stand still for them as** it did for **Joshua bin Nun,** and **twenty** were on **an intermediate** level between the other two. **The greatest of all** the students was **Yonatan ben Uzziel, and the least of them** was **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai.**

Having mentioned Yonatan ben Uziel, the *gemara* takes the opportunity to describe his place among the students of Hillel. *Shmonim talmidim hayu lo l'Hillel ha-zaken* — Hillel the Elder had eighty students. The *gemara* divides them into three tiers.

The top thirty were *re'uyim shetishre aleihem shechina ke-Moshe Rabbeinu* — fit for the *shechina* to rest upon them as it rested upon Moshe Rabbeinu. The Rashbam notes, as do others, that this did not actually happen because the generation was not sufficiently worthy, but those thirty students had personally achieved that level. The Maharsha adds that another *gemara* ascribes this same distinction to Hillel himself, suggesting these thirty had matched the level of their teacher.

The bottom thirty were *re'uyim sheta'amod lahem chama ke-Yehoshua bin Nun* — fit for the sun to stand still for them as it did for Yehoshua bin Nun. The Maharsha, citing another *gemara*, notes that the sun also stood for Moshe, and reads the imagery of *ta'amod lahem chama* as reflecting the relationship between Moshe and Yehoshua: Moshe was like the sun itself, Yehoshua like the moon that reflects the sun's light. The bottom thirty of Hillel's students had achieved a level analogous to Yehoshua's vis-à-vis Moshe — great in their own right but standing in reflected light.

### Yonatan's Rank

In the middle were twenty — the *beinonim* — and of these twenty, *gadol shebekulan Yonatan ben Uziel*: the greatest was Yonatan ben Uziel.

### Yochanan's Modesty

And the smallest of all — *katan shebekulan Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai* — was at the very bottom of the middle group. This gives some sense of the range under discussion.

### Yochanan's Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי, שֶׁלֹּא הִנִּיחַ מִקְרָא, וּמִשְׁנָה, תַּלְמוּד, הֲלָכוֹת, וְאַגָּדוֹת, דִּקְדּוּקֵי תוֹרָה, וְדִקְִדּוּקֵי סוֹפְרִים, וְקַלִּין וַחֲמוּרִין, וּגְזֵרוֹת שָׁווֹת, וּתְקוּפוֹת, וְגִמַטְרִיָּאוֹת, וּמִשְׁלוֹת כּוֹבְסִים, וּמִשְׁלוֹת שׁוּעָלִים, שִׂיחַת שֵׁדִים, וְשִׂיחַת דְּקָלִים, וְשִׂיחַת מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, וְדָבָר גָּדוֹל, וְדָבָר קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said about Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai** that **he did not neglect Bible and Mishna; Talmud; *halakhot* and *aggadot*; minutiae of the Torah and minutiae of the scribes; and** the hermeneutical principles of the Torah with regard to ***a fortiori*** inferences; **and verbal analogies; and** the calculation of the calendric **seasons; and numerical values** of Hebrew letters **[*gimatriyot*]; and parables of launderers,** which are folktales that can be used to explain the Torah, **and parables of foxes.** In addition, he did not neglect esoteric matters, including **the conversation of demons, and the conversation of palm trees, and the conversation of ministering angels, and** more generally, **a great matter and a small matter.**

The *gemara* elaborates on Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's knowledge as a way of measuring, however indirectly, the staggering height of those above him. *Shelo hiniach mikra u-mishna gemara halachot ve-agadot dikdukei torah ve-dikdukei sofrim ve-kalim ve-chamurim gezeirot shavot ve-tekufot ve-gimatriot ve-mishlot kovsim ve-mishlot shualim sichat sheidim ve-sichat dekalim ve-sichat malachei ha-shareit* — there was not a single one of all these domains that he left unmastered.

Working through the list: *mikra* is the biblical text; *mishna* is the apodictic rabbinic teaching; *gemara* is the discursive legal discussion surrounding the *mishna*. *Halachot* — the Rashbam reads this as *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, the oral traditions received at Sinai; Rabbeinu Gershom reads it as the *midreshei halacha* such as the *Sifra* and the *Mechilta*. *Agadot* are the narrative and homiletical traditions, what we call *midrash aggadah*. *Dikdukei torah* involves the precise reading of the biblical text — extra letters and their significance; *dikdukei sofrim*, according to Rabbeinu Gershom, refers to the rabbinic use of enumeration to organize law, such as *arba'ah avot nezikin* or *chamesh esrei nashim potrot tzaroteihen*. *Kalim va-chamurim* and *gezeirot shavot* are the hermeneutical principles for deriving law. *Tekufot* refers to astronomical knowledge of the seasons. *Gimatriot*, according to the Rashbam, has less to do with numerical equivalences and more with *notarikon* — reading letters of a word as acronyms or abbreviations for other words. *Mishlot kovsim* and *mishlot shualim* are parables — from launderers and from foxes — forms of popular wisdom deployed in teaching. *Sichat sheidim* is knowledge of incantations relating to demons; *sichat dekalim*, according to Rabbeinu Gershom, is the sort of speech by which a sage could cause a field to fill with trees. *Sichat malachei ha-shareit* is knowledge of the speech of the ministering angels.

What is conspicuously absent from this list — and therefore must belong to the *davar gadol* and *davar katan* mentioned elsewhere — is *ma'aseh merkavah*, the secrets of the divine chariot, and *havayot d'Abaye v'Rava*, the great unresolved dialectical disputes of classical Talmudic learning.

### Rambam's Theory

The Rambam draws on precisely this passage, and specifically on the distinction between *davar gadol* and *davar katan*, to articulate his vision of the hierarchy of religious knowledge. For the Rambam, *ma'aseh merkavah* — which he interprets not in mystical terms but as the study of metaphysics and theology, the rational contemplation of God's existence and the nature of the heavenly realm — represents the ultimate goal of human religious life. *Havayot d'Abaye v'Rava*, the entire enterprise of Talmudic dialectics, is the *davar katan* — not trivial, but subsidiary to the larger aim.

The Rambam therefore instructs that a person should not devote himself to the *davar gadol* — the philosophical-theological enterprise — until he has first filled himself with *davar katan*: learning all of *halacha*, mastering how one is obligated to live. Only then can he proceed to the pursuit of *ma'aseh merkavah*. This is a somewhat paradoxical formulation: the lesser thing must be completed before the greater thing can be pursued, and yet *havayot d'Abaye v'Rava* has become in later rabbinic usage the standard shorthand for the highest and most demanding form of Torah learning. The irony that the Rambam classifies the whole enterprise of Talmudic learning as *davar katan* is one that later authorities found significant.

The verse applied to Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai — *lehan'chil ohavai yesh v'otzroteihem amale*, to bequeath to those who love Me true existence and to fill their treasuries — carries a resonance given the surrounding discussion about inheritance and bequeathing property. The true wealth, the text implies, is Torah knowledge, and those who love wisdom are bequeathed something far more enduring than dinars.

### Yonatan's Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּקָטָן שֶׁבְּכוּלָּם כֵּן, גָּדוֹל שֶׁבְּכוּלָּם – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל יוֹנָתָן בֶּן עוּזִּיאֵל, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה – כׇּל עוֹף שֶׁפּוֹרֵחַ עָלָיו נִשְׂרָף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And if the least of them** was **so** prolific, **the greatest of them** was **all the more so** prolific. The Gemara relates that the Sages **said of Yonatan ben Uzziel,** the greatest of Hillel's students, that **when he would sit and engage in Torah** study, the sanctity that he generated was so intense that **any bird that would fly over him would be incinerated.**

If the *katan shebekulam* of the middle group, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai, had achieved all of the above, one can only imagine what the *gadol*, Yonatan ben Uziel, had attained. The *gemara* offers one vivid illustration: *besha'ah sheyoshev v'osek baTorah, kol of sheporei'ach alav nisraf* — when he sat and engaged in Torah study, any bird that flew overhead was burned. The image is of such an intense concentration of spiritual fire, generated by the gathering of angels above his head, that birds in flight were incinerated.

The Kotsker Rebbe offered a different reading of this image. The bird that is burned is not a literal bird, but a metaphor for thought. Each thought Yonatan ben Uziel generated in his learning was immediately overtaken and consumed by an even higher thought, which in turn yielded to yet another. His mind was in a state of perpetual ascent, each level of insight dissolving into the next.

## Son Identification

### Son Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר ״זֶה בְּנִי״ – נֶאֱמָן, ״זֶה אָחִי״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן, וְיִטּוֹל עִמּוֹ בְּחֶלְקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says: This is my son,** is **deemed credible.** One who says: **This is my brother,** is **not deemed credible** with regard to his other brothers' obligation to share the inheritance with the subject of his statement. When one claims that this man is his brother, this claim is accepted with regard to the speaker's own portion, **and** the man in question **takes** a share of their father's inheritance **with him,** i.e., **from his portion.**

The *gemara* now moves to a topic previewed at the very opening of the *daf*: *ha'omer zeh beni ne'eman* — if a person says "this is my son," he is believed. This is the converse of the *bechor* cases, which concerned identifying which of one's known sons held the status of firstborn. Here the question is about recognizing as a son someone who is not publicly known to be one's child at all.

### Brother vs. Son

By contrast, *zeh achi eino ne'eman* — if a person says "this is my brother," he is not believed. If Reuven declares that Levi is a long-lost brother, Shimon — the other known heir — need not accept that claim. Reuven is, however, bound by his own declaration: *veyitol imo bechelko* — Levi receives from Reuven's portion. In practical terms: if the estate consists of three fields, Reuven and Shimon would each ordinarily receive one and a half. If Reuven declares Levi a brother, Levi should in theory receive one field, with half coming from each brother. But Shimon refuses to accept the claim, invoking *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* — the burden of proof rests on the one making the claim. Reuven, however, having made the claim, must honor it from his own portion.

### Property Reversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת – יַחְזְרוּ נְכָסִים לִמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** If the man in question **dies,** the **property** he received from the father's inheritance **shall return to its place,** i.e., to the possession of the brother who testified on his behalf, from whose portion he received a share.

If Levi subsequently dies, *yachziru nechasim lemekoman* — the property returns to its source. Even if Shimon never accepted that Levi was a brother, he does not now benefit from Levi's death by claiming a share of whatever Reuven had already conceded to Levi. The half-field Levi received from Reuven reverts to Reuven; Shimon cannot suddenly assert brotherhood now that it would benefit him.

### Selective Heir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְלוּ לוֹ נְכָסִים מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר, יִירְשׁוּ אֶחָיו עִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **property came into** the man in question's possession **from somewhere else,** other than from the father, and the man in question died, all of **the brothers of** the one who testified **shall inherit with** the one who testified, as according to his claim they too are the heirs of the deceased.

The reverse scenario, however, works differently. If Levi comes into great wealth from an external source, Shimon may now reverse his earlier position and affirm that yes, Levi is indeed a brother. This may seem unfair, but it follows from the structure of the claims: Reuven has always maintained that Levi is a brother, and on that basis, Shimon is in any event entitled to half of whatever Levi inherits. If Shimon also confirms the fraternal relationship, that changes nothing with respect to what he receives from the estate — and he stands to gain from Levi's new wealth.

### Paternity Functions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ ״זֶה בְּנִי״ – נֶאֱמָן. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ, וְלִפְטוֹר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִן הַיִּבּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one who says: **This is my son,** is **deemed credible. With regard to what *halakha*** is this stated? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** It is stated **with regard to inheriting** from **him,** i.e., the son inherits from the speaker, **and with regard to rendering his wife exempt from levirate marriage.** Because he claims that the person in question is his son, his wife is not required to enter into levirate marriage after his death, as he has a child.

The *mishna* having established that a father is believed to say "this is my son," the *gemara* asks: *lemai hilcheta* — for what halachic purpose is he believed? This is not a trivial question. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *leyorsho* — the son inherits from him. And second: *ulifetur ishto min hayibum* — to exempt the father's wife from *yibum*. If the father had no publicly known children and declares "this is my son" before his death, his wife is thereby exempt from *yibum* with the father's brother.

### Property Control

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ – פְּשִׁיטָא! לִפְטוֹר אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ מִן הַיִּבּוּם אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that his claim is deemed credible **with regard to** someone **inheriting** from **him?** Since he could have given this person his property as a gift, it need not be stated that his claim is accepted with regard to his inheritance. The Gemara responds: **It was necessary for** the mishna to state that his claim is deemed credible **with regard to rendering his wife exempt from levirate marriage** despite the fact that it is not in his power to render her exempt, but the *halakha* of inheritance is not a novelty.

The first ruling — that the son inherits — is intuitive, and the *gemara* declares it *peshita*, obvious. Both the Rashbam and Tosafos explain that the reason the father is believed is that he could have given the property to this person as a *matanah* even without invoking the claim of paternity. Since he could effect the same result through a gift, he is believed to do it through a declaration of kinship. But this also defines the *limit* of the belief: just as a gift can only convey property currently in the donor's possession, the father's declaration of "this is my son" is credited only with respect to property he presently controls. Property acquired after the declaration would not automatically pass to the declared son; a renewed declaration would be necessary to bring such subsequently acquired property within the son's inheritance.

### Brother/Son Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא – מִי שֶׁאָמַר בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָתוֹ: ״יֵשׁ לִי בָּנִים״ – נֶאֱמָן. ״יֵשׁ לִי אַחִים״ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן! הָתָם – דְּלָא מוּחְזָק לַן בְּאָח, הָכָא – אַף עַל גַּב דְּמוּחְזָק לֵיהּ בְּאָח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We** already **learned this** in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 64a) **as well: One who said at the time of his death: I have children,** is **deemed credible,** and his wife is thereby exempt from levirate marriage. If he said: **I have brothers,** and his wife therefore must enter levirate marriage, **he** is **not deemed credible.** The Gemara answers: **There,** in that mishna, it is a case **where he is not presumed by us to** have **a brother.** Therefore, his wife is already presumed to be exempt from levirate marriage, and his claim that he has a son merely substantiates this presumption. **Here,** the mishna adds a novelty that **even if he is presumed to** have **a brother,** his claim that he has a son is accepted, and his wife is thereby exempted from levirate marriage.

That the father is believed about a son for purposes of *yibum* exemption is *not* obvious, since the ability to gift property does not obviously translate into the ability to affect one's wife's *yibum* status. The *gemara* therefore notes that this ruling required specific articulation: *itztricha leh*.

The *gemara* also contrasts this ruling with a parallel case. If a person says at the moment of death *yesh li banim* — I have sons — he is believed and his wife is exempt from *yibum*. But if he says *yesh li achim* — I have brothers — he is *not* believed to thereby obligate his wife in *yibum*. The reason is asymmetrical: in the default state, where no brothers are known, the wife is presumed exempt from *yibum*. A declaration of sons reaffirms the exemption from another angle; a declaration of brothers seeks to impose a burden that contradicts the default. The *gemara* cannot accept the latter on mere say-so.

Our *mishna* concerns a different situation: one where the brother is *known* — *af al gav de-muchzak lan be-ach* — and the wife is therefore already presumed *chayevet be-yibum*. In that case, can the dying man's declaration of "this is my son" override the presumption of *yibum* obligation?

### Divorce Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ: ״זֶה בְּנִי״ – נֶאֱמָן? הוֹאִיל וּבַעַל שֶׁאָמַר: ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says** that **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that one who says: **This is my son,** is **deemed credible? Since a husband who says: I divorced my wife,** is **deemed credible,** and his wife is thereby exempt from levirate marriage, he is deemed credible with regard to this claim as well.

Rav Yosef initially cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: the husband is believed to say "this is my son" — and by analogy, also believed to say "I divorced my wife" — because *ho'il uva'al she'amar gerashti et ishti ne'eman*, a husband who says he divorced his wife is believed. But this immediately meets with Rav Yosef's own sharp objection: *tali tanya bedelo tanya* — you are grounding an explicit ruling in something that has never itself been stated! We have not yet established anywhere that a husband is believed to say he divorced his wife; you cannot use that as the premise for explaining why he is believed to declare his son.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מָרֵיהּ דְּאַבְרָהָם! תָּלֵי תַּנְיָא בִּדְלָא תַּנְיָא?! אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרוּ: ״זֶה בְּנִי״ נֶאֱמָן? הוֹאִיל וּבְיָדוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** As Rav Yosef forgot some of his Torah knowledge due to an illness, he questioned the accuracy of his citation of Rav Yehuda. **Rav Yosef said: Master of Abraham!** This reasoning **makes** that which **is taught** in the Mishna **dependent upon that which is not taught,** as the credibility of one who claims: This is my son, is stated in the mishna, while the *halakha* that a husband's claim that he divorced his wife is accepted is the statement of an *amora*. **Rather, if** this explanation **was stated, it was stated like this: Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: For what** reason **did** the Sages **say** that one who says: **This is my son,** is **deemed credible** with regard to rendering his wife exempt from levirate marriage? **Since it is in his power to divorce her** and thereby render her exempt from levirate marriage, he is deemed credible with regard to this claim as well.

The corrected formulation is therefore: *mipnei mah amru zeh beni ne'eman? Ho'il u'veyado lgareshah* — why is a father believed to say "this is my son"? Because it is *within his power to divorce her*. If the husband wished to exempt his wife from *yibum*, he could do so directly by divorcing her before he dies. Because he has this power over her *yibum* status, he is believed when he makes a declaration that achieves the same result — namely, that he has a son.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״, בַּעַל שֶׁאָמַר ״גֵּרַשְׁתִּי אֶת אִשְׁתִּי״ – נֶאֱמָן, הוֹאִיל וּבְיָדוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** in addition: **Now that you said** that **we say** that the husband is deemed credible **since** he has the power to divorce her, **a husband who says: I divorced my wife,** is also **deemed credible, since it is in his power to divorce her** at any time.

This is not precisely a *migo* in the classic sense. A *migo* argues: believe me because if I wanted to lie I could have done so more effectively through another available claim. Here there may well be reasons the husband would not wish to divorce his wife — it would render her *asurah* to a *kohen*, he might still expect to recover his health and live longer, and so forth. Rather, the principle is one of *beyado lgareshah*: because he has power over this legal status, he is believed regarding it. Control over a legal status generates credibility in that domain.

This principle, the *gemara* notes, then extends: *hashta d'amart amrinan ho'il u-ba'al she'amar gerashti et ishti* — once we have established that a man is believed because it is *beyado lgareshah*, we can now also say that a husband who declares he divorced his wife is likewise believed, for the same reason. He could have divorced her; therefore he is believed to say that he did. Both rulings — belief in "this is my son" and belief in "I divorced my wife" — rest on the same foundation of *beyado lgareshah*. That discussion will be taken up on the following *daf*.
