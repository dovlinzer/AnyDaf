# Bava Kamma 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## David & Water

### David's Water Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיִּתְאַוֶּה דָוִד וַיֹּאמַר: מִי יַשְׁקֵנִי מַיִם מִבֹּאר בֵּית לֶחֶם אֲשֶׁר בַּשָּׁעַר. וַיִּבְקְעוּ שְׁלֹשֶׁת הַגִּבּוֹרִים בְּמַחֲנֵה פְלִשְׁתִּים, וַיִּשְׁאֲבוּ מַיִם מִבֹּאר בֵּית לֶחֶם אֲשֶׁר בַּשַּׁעַר [וְגוֹ׳]"
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues with another statement of *aggada* on a related topic: The verse states: **"And David longed, and said: Oh, that one would give me water to drink of the well of Bethlehem, which is by the gate! And the three mighty men broke through the host of the Philistines, and drew water out of the well of Bethlehem, that was by the gate,** and took it, and brought it to David; but he would not drink it, but poured it out to the Lord" (II Samuel 23:15–16). The Sages understood that David was not simply asking for water, but was using the term as a metaphor referring to Torah, and he was raising a halakhic dilemma.

The *daf* opens in the middle of the final section of the sixth *perek*, which addresses the *chiyuv* of *eish* — the liability for fire damage. We arrive in the midst of a remarkable *agadata* drawn from verses in Shmuel and Divrei HaYamim, concerning a strange episode in the life of David HaMelech. David declared, "Who would give me water to drink?" — whereupon some of his soldiers broke through the camp of the *Plishtim* to retrieve water. When they returned, David refused to drink it and instead poured it as a libation to God. The episode is puzzling on its face: what was actually going on?

### Three Inquiries

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, אִי כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אִי כְּרַבָּנַן, וּפְשַׁטוּ לֵיהּ מַאי דִּפְשַׁטוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the dilemma** that David **is raising? Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says: He was asking** about the *halakha* with regard to **a concealed** article damaged by **a fire.** He wanted to know whether the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who holds that one is liable to pay for such damage, or **whether** the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that one is exempt from liability for damage by fire to concealed articles. **And** the Sages in Bethlehem **answered him what they answered him.**

One version of the story mentions that the *Plishtim* were hiding in a field filled with lentils; another says barley. The *Gemara* interprets the "water" as a metaphor for the *Sanhedrin*, and David's request as a quest for a *psak halacha*. Three possible versions of what he sought are offered. First, he wanted to know the *halacha* concerning property that was buried or hidden — *tamun* — in a fire, which is precisely why this *agadata* is cited here in the context of fire liability. Second, he wanted to know whether one is permitted to burn down stacks of grain in which *Plishtim* are hiding in order to save oneself, even though this means destroying another person's property — *ba lehatzil atzmo b'mamon shel chavero*. Third, he wanted to know whether he could take one pile of grain to feed his animals with the intention of repaying with another pile.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: גְּדִישִׁים דִּשְׂעוֹרִים דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל הֲווֹ, דַּהֲווֹ מִטַּמְּרִי פְּלִשְׁתִּים בְּהוּ, וְקָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לְהַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna stated** a different explanation of the verse: **There were stacks of barley belonging to Jews in which the Philistines were hiding, and** David wanted to burn down the stacks to kill the Philistines and save his own life. **He raised the dilemma: What is** the *halakha*? Is it permitted **to save oneself** by destroying **the property of another?**

According to the latter two versions, the ruling he received was that technically a commoner is not permitted to save himself using another's property, nor may one steal with the intention to replace — but as a king, David would be permitted, since a king may exercise prerogative over people's property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ: אָסוּר לְהַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ; אֲבָל אַתָּה מֶלֶךְ אַתָּה, [וּמֶלֶךְ] פּוֹרֵץ לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ דֶּרֶךְ וְאֵין מוֹחִין בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They sent** the following answer **to him: It is prohibited to save oneself** by destroying **the property of another. But you are king, and a king may breach the fence** of an individual **in order to form a path for himself, and none may protest his** action, i.e., the normal *halakhot* of damage do not apply to you since you are king.

This raises a genuinely difficult question. In Judaism the general principle is that one may transgress virtually any prohibition to save a life, with the sole exceptions of *avodah zarah*, *gilui arayot*, and *shefichat damim*. Theft is not among those three. Why, then, would saving one's life by damaging another's property be considered problematic? This prompts *Tosafot* and many *rishonim* to reframe the question: of course one may damage another's property to save a life, but one is obligated to compensate afterward. The question, then, is whether one may do so without having to pay. This can perhaps be reframed further as involving a distant rather than immediate threat to life.

There is, however, a deeper issue. When we normally speak of violating a prohibition to save life, the question concerns only the *sin* dimension. But here we are speaking of actually injuring someone else — and that may operate by different logic. We do say one cannot commit murder even to save oneself, so there must be some threshold. Would one be permitted to blind someone to save one's own life? Property, being entirely replaceable, would seem to be the clearest case where we should say yes — which is why the simple reading of this *Gemara* that forbids it is so strange, and why *Tosafot* feels compelled to reread it.

A further dimension worth noting is the question of whether normal rules apply at all during wartime. War is, by definition, an enterprise of destroying property and person. If David was fighting the *Plishtim*, does the question of compensating one's countrymen for their grain stacks even arise? When an army drives through a field or confiscates property to feed its troops, it seems anachronistic to read that as a simple question of personal liability. Perhaps the scenario was understood as a local skirmish rather than a full-scale war, but it is striking that the *Gemara* treats it at such an intimate, personal level rather than as a question of war powers.

### King's Prerogative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְאַחְלוֹפֵי – הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב חַד קְרָא: "וַתְּהִי שָׁם חֶלְקַת הַשָּׂדֶה מְלֵאָה עֲדָשִׁים", וּכְתִיב חַד קְרָא: "וַתְּהִי חֶלְקַת הַשָּׂדֶה מְלֵאָה שְׂעוֹרִים"; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְמִקְלֵי, מַאי אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ לְהָנֵי תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the different explanations: **Granted, according to the one who says** that David was asking whether he could take the stacks of barley and **exchange** them, i.e., repay the owners of the barley, with stacks of lentils, **this is as it is written** in **one verse:** "And the Philistines were gathered together into a troop, **where was a plot of ground full of lentils;** and the people fled from the Philistines" (II Samuel 23:11), **and it is written** in **one** other **verse:** "He was with David at Pas Dammim, and there the Philistines were gathered together to battle, **where was a plot of ground full of barley;** and the people fled from before the Philistines" (I Chronicles 11:13). This apparent contradiction can be reconciled by saying that there were two fields, one of barley and one of lentils.

The *Gemara* now says: *bishlama l'man de'amar hani tartei* — according to those who say David's question was one of the two other matters (either the saving-oneself-with-another's-property question or the taking-of-one-pile-to-repay-another question), the story works well. The ruling was that technically such acts are forbidden, but David as king was permitted. The verse then records that *ve-lo ava David lishtotam* — David refused to drink the water, meaning he refused to act upon the permissive ruling. He said: *keivan de'ika issura, lo nikha li* — since the act is fundamentally forbidden for a commoner, I have no desire to avail myself of the king's prerogative.

But according to the version in which David's only question was about *tamun* — buried property destroyed by fire — what does the phrase "he refused to drink it" mean? If it was merely a legal inquiry with no personal stake in the ruling, why would he refuse?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ קָא מִבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ – מִכְּדִי גְּמָרָא הוּא דִּשְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ, מַאי "לֹא אָבָה (דָּוִד) לִשְׁתוֹתָם"?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to the one who says** one of **these two** explanations, that David was asking either about repaying barley with lentils or burning the stacks of barley, **this is as it is written** of David: **"But he would not drink it"** (II Samuel 23:16). David **said** to himself: **Since there is a prohibition** involved in this action, **it is not satisfactory to me** to act in this manner, even though technically it is permitted for a king. **But according to the one who says** that David **was raising a dilemma** with regard to the *halakha* of **a concealed** article damaged by **a fire, since they sent him** an answer that was **a tradition** with regard to the *halakha*, **what** is the meaning of: "But **he would not drink it"?**

### Anonymous Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלָא אַמְרִינְהוּ מִשְּׁמַיְיהוּ. אָמַר, כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִבֵּית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שְׁמוּאֵל הָרָמָתִי: כׇּל הַמּוֹסֵר עַצְמוֹ לָמוּת עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה – אֵין אוֹמְרִים דְּבַר הֲלָכָה מִשְּׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This means **that he did not say the *halakha* in their names.** He did not transmit the ruling in the name of those who went in the time of battle to ask the Sages what the *halakha* is. David **said** to himself: **This** is the tradition that **I received from the court of Samuel of Rama:** With regard to **anyone who hands himself** over **to die for** the sake of **words of Torah,** the Sages **do not say a matter of *halakha* in his name,** so that others will not follow this ruling and endanger their lives.

The *Gemara* explains: *zu amrinuhu mishmaya hi* — in this version, the "water" is a Torah teaching brought back to David by his valorous soldiers, who literally broke through the *Plishtim* encampment to reach the *Sanhedrin* and retrieve the ruling. David then chose not to transmit the *psak* in the names of those men. One would think this deprives them of deserved honor. Why did David not attribute the ruling to them?

### Martyrdom Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיַּסֵּךְ אֹתָם לַה׳ – בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבַד לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ, מַאי "וַיַּסֵּךְ אֹתָם לַה׳"? דְּאַמְרִינְהוּ מִשְּׁמָא דִגְמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question: The verse states: **"He poured it out to the Lord"** (II Samuel 23:16), which indicates that David acted stringently and did not rely on the lenient ruling that he received. **Granted, according to the one who says** either of **these two** explanations, that David asked either about burning the stacks of barley or about replacing their value with lentils, he poured out the water to God **due to** the fact **that he acted for the sake of Heaven** and did not rely on the lenient ruling he had received. **But according to the one who says** that David asked about **a concealed** article damaged by **a fire, what** is the reason that **he poured out** the water **to the Lord?** The reason is **that they said** this *halakha* **in the name of the tradition,** without associating it with any specific individual.

David said: *kach mekubalni mi-beit dino shel Shmuel HaRamati* — this is the tradition I received from the court of Shmuel HaRamati (not to be confused with the *amora* Shmuel): *kol hammoser atzmo lamut al divrei Torah, ein omrin devar halacha mishmo* — anyone who risks their life for the sake of Torah is not cited as the source of a *halacha*.

This is striking. One would expect such a person to receive all the more honor. But the *Maharsha* reads this as a critique. The phrase *zot hatorah adam ki yamut ba'ohel* — this is the Torah, a person who "dies" in the tent — and the principle that *ein hatorah niknet ela b'mi shemeimet atzmo aleha* — Torah is only acquired by one who "kills himself" over it — are meant as metaphor, not literally. One is not supposed to actually risk one's life simply to obtain a *psak halacha*. Torah study is not among the things for which one is literally required to give one's life. If the honor of having one's teaching cited *maivi geula la'olam* — brings redemption to the world — then someone who risked their life for an unnecessary purpose is not worthy of that merit.

This reading actually fits remarkably well with the plain sense of the Biblical narrative: David made an offhand wish for water, his soldiers took it literally and risked their lives, and David's pouring it out as a libation was his way of expressing that he felt they had done something pointless and even reckless. The *Gemara* maps this onto a discussion of Torah — *vayisech osam l'Hashem*, he poured it to God, meaning he gave the teaching to the anonymous tradition, making it "God's water" rather than theirs.

## Barriers & Exemptions

### Exemption Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עָבְרָה גָּדֵר שֶׁהוּא גָּבוֹהַּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, אוֹ דֶרֶךְ הָרַבִּים, אוֹ נָהָר – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one kindled a fire that **crossed a fence that is four cubits high, or** if the fire crossed **the public thoroughfare, or** if the fire crossed **a river,** and in each case it caused damage on the other side, he is **exempt** from liability.

With that striking *agadata* concluded — a discussion of water interleaved into a section about fire — the *Gemara* turns to the next *Mishnah*, which addresses the technical question of what constitutes a sufficient protective barrier against spreading fire.

The *Mishnah* states: *avar geder sheguvo'ah arba amot* — if a fire crossed a wall that is four *amot* high — or if it crossed a *derech harabbim*, a public thoroughfare (understood to be sixteen *amot* wide), or *o nahar*, a river, *patur* — the person who started the fire is exempt. The underlying principle is *shmira*: if one has made an adequate protective measure such that a *ruach metzuya* — an ordinary wind — cannot be expected to cause the fire to spread, then the hazard has been adequately contained.

Four *amot* is roughly six feet. Whether a six-foot wall actually contains a large fire is a fair question, and the *Gemara* will address the details of how and from where that measurement is taken.

### Baraitot Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָתַנְיָא: עָבְרָה גָּדֵר שֶׁהוּא גָּבוֹהַּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if a fire **crossed a fence that is four cubits in height,** the one who kindled the fire is **liable?** This appears to contradict the mishna.

A *baraita* appears to contradict the *Mishnah*: *avar geder sheguvo'ah arba amot chayav* — if the fire crossed a wall four *amot* high, one is *chayav*. This seems to say that four *amot* is never sufficient for exemption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תַּנָּא דִּידַן קָא חָשֵׁיב מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה: שֵׁשׁ אַמּוֹת – פָּטוּר, חָמֵשׁ אַמּוֹת – פָּטוּר, עַד אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – פָּטוּר. תַּנָּא בָּרָא מִלְּמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה קָא חָשֵׁיב: שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת – חַיָּיב, שָׁלֹשׁ אַמּוֹת – חַיָּיב, עַד אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** There is no dispute between the *tanna* of the mishna and the *tanna* of the *baraita*; there is merely a difference of how they stated their rulings. **The *tanna* of our** mishna **counts** downward **from above to below.** In other words, if the fire crossed a fence **six cubits** high, the one who kindled the fire is **exempt;** if it crossed a fence **five cubits** high, he is **exempt;** and this is the *halakha* **until** the fire crosses a fence of a minimum of **four cubits** high, where the one who kindled the fire is still **exempt.** Conversely, **the *tanna* of the *baraita* counts** up **from below to above.** The meaning is that if the fire crossed a fence **two cubits** high, the one who kindled the fire is **liable;** if the fire crossed a fence **three cubits** high, he is **liable;** and this is the *halakha* **until** the fire crosses a fence of a maximum of **four cubits** high, where the one who kindled the fire is still **liable.** Accordingly, there is no contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita*.

Rav Pappa resolves the tension: *tanna didan k'chashi mil'ma'ala l'mata* — our *Mishnah*'s *tanna* counts from the top downward. That is, starting from a very tall wall, once you get down to four *amot*, you are still exempt — *patur* applies all the way down to four *amot*, and only below four *amot* does liability begin. *Tanna bara*, the outer *tanna* of the *baraita*, counts *mil'mata l'ma'ala* — from the bottom upward. Saying you are *chayav* at four *amot* means you are *chayav* up to four *amot*, and above four *amot* you are *patur*. Both sources agree that more than four *amot* exempts and less than four *amot* creates liability — the ambiguity only concerns exactly at four *amot*, and each source reads that borderline case from its own direction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ דְּפָטוּר – אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׂדֵה קוֹצִים. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וּמִשְּׂפַת קוֹצִים וּלְמַעְלָה אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says: When they said** in the mishna with regard to a fire crossing a fence **four cubits** high **that** the one who kindled the fire is **exempt,** this is **even in a field of thorns. Rav Pappa says: And** Rava's statement is referring to a case where the height of the fence **is four cubits** counting **from above the** upper **limit of the thorns.**

Rava then adds: *arba amot she'amru d'patur afilu b'veit kotzim* — even if your field is filled with thorns and thistles, which constitute excellent kindling — dry, thin, and highly flammable — you are still exempt if the wall is four *amot* high. This seems counterintuitive: if there is more fuel, shouldn't the standard be higher? Rav Pappa refines this: *umisfas kotzim ul'ma'ala arba amot* — the four *amot* must be measured not from the ground but from the top of whatever fuel material sits on the ground. If there is a pile of thorns, the wall must be four *amot* above the top of the thorns. If there is a woodpile, the four *amot* is measured from the top of the woodpile.

*Tosafot* confirms this reading explicitly in a note at the bottom of the sugya (*s.v. avra*): the *Mishnah*'s four *amot* is measured from the height of the wood used for the fire. This reflects a practical logic: the concern is where the base of the fire would be when it reaches the wall. One cannot measure from the height of the flame itself, because no one can predict how high the flame will be by the time the fire reaches the wall. The fuel source on the ground is the more determinate and measurable baseline.

### Fire Orientation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּקוֹלַחַת, אֲבָל בְּנִכְפֶּפֶת – אֲפִילּוּ עַד מֵאָה אַמָּה חַיָּיב. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַתְנִיתִין בְּנִכְפֶּפֶת, אֲבָל בְּקוֹלַחַת – אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: They taught** in the mishna that one is exempt from liability if the fire crosses a public thoroughfare **only in** a case where the flame **blazes high [*bekolaḥat*]. But in** a case where the flame **blazes low [*benikhpefet*]** and therefore spreads easily along the ground, the one who kindled the fire is **liable even** if the space that the fire crossed was **up to one hundred cubits. And Shmuel said: The mishna** exempts one from liability if the fire crosses a public thoroughfare **in** a case where the flame **blazes low, but in** a case where the flame **blazes high, even any** minimal gap between where the fire was kindled and where it caused damage renders the one who kindled the fire **exempt.**

Rav introduces an important distinction. All of the foregoing — the four-*amot* wall, the exemptions, the measurements from the fuel source — applies only to a *kolachas*, a fire that rises straight upward. *Aval b'menasheves* — but if the fire is slanted, bending in the wind — *afilu ad me'ah ama chayav* — even if the distance is a hundred *amot*, one is still *chayav*.

*Rashi* understands this as referring to the horizontal distance the fire traverses: a fire that bends and moves at an angle can spread across an enormous area. *Tosafot* understands it as referring to the height of the wall: even a wall a hundred *amot* high will not stop a slanting fire, because the fire bends over it. A subsequent *baraita* (*tani kivatei de-rav*) supports the reading in terms of distance, using the phrase *afilu ad me'ah mil chayav* — even up to a hundred *mil* one is *chayav*, and a *mil* is approximately a kilometer. No wall is a hundred kilometers thick, so Tosafot's reading that this refers to the thickness of the wall is clearly an exaggeration used for rhetorical effect, even by Tosafot's own admission. The distance reading is more naturally supported by the language.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּקוֹלַחַת, אֲבָל בְּנִכְפֶּפֶת וְעֵצִים מְצוּיִין לָהּ – אֲפִילּוּ עַד מֵאָה מִיל חַיָּיב. עָבְרָה נָהָר אוֹ שְׁלוּלִית שֶׁהֵם רְחָבִים שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav: In what** case **is this statement said** that one is exempt if the fire crosses a public thoroughfare? It is **in** a case where the flame **blazes high. But in** a case where the flame **blazes low and there is wood** to keep **it** burning, one is **liable even** if it causes damage at a distance of **up to one hundred *mil*.** If the fire **crossed a river or a pool [*shelulit*]** of water **that is eight cubits wide,** he is **exempt** from paying for the damage caused, regardless of whether the fire blazed high or low.

The *baraita* also specifies an additional condition for the *menasheves* case: *ha-etzim metzuyim lo* — there must be available fuel for the fire to spread along. This makes physical sense: a slanting, wind-driven fire will continue indefinitely as long as it has fuel, but it cannot cross a gap where there is nothing to burn.

A question arises naturally: when do we assess whether a fire is *kolachas* or *menasheves*? When you are still tending the fire, you may not yet know which it will become. The implication seems to be that if one is intentionally setting a fire that is already of the *menasheves* type — perhaps deliberately burning brush — then one is already on notice that no distance will exempt them. If you set a fire with the potential to become *menasheves*, the question of what standard governs it is precisely the critical unresolved issue.

Shmuel offers an alternative formulation: our *Mishnah* actually describes the *menasheves* case, and says that in that case, even a small wall exempts one if the fire is straight — *aval b'kolachat, afilu kol shehu patur* — because a straight-rising fire is not expected to spread. Shmuel thus inverts Rav's formulation, taking the *Mishnah* as speaking of the slanted case and the leniency as applying to the straight one.

### Water Barriers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרָה נָהָר אוֹ שְׁלוּלִית שֶׁהֵם רְחָבִים שְׁמוֹנֶה אַמּוֹת – פָּטוּר. דֶּרֶךְ הָרַבִּים. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא – דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמּוֹת כְּדֶרֶךְ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים (פָּטוּר).
>
> **Translation:** If the fire **crossed a river or a pool [*shelulit*]** of water **that is eight cubits wide,** he is **exempt** from paying for the damage caused. § The mishna teaches: If the fire crossed **the public thoroughfare,** he is exempt. The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who holds this opinion? **Rava said: It is Rabbi Eliezer, as we learned** in a mishna (61b) that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** One is liable up to a distance of **sixteen cubits, like** the width of **the public domain.** By inference, one is exempt if a fire crosses a greater distance, i.e., across a public thoroughfare.

The *Mishnah* continues: *avar nahar o shelulit she-hem rechavim shmoneh amot, patur* — if the fire crossed a river or a *shelulit* (a water channel) that is eight *amot* wide, one is exempt. The *Gemara* then asks whose opinion underlies the *Mishnah*'s reference to *derech harabbim* as an exempting factor. Rabbah answers: it is Rabbi Eliezer, *detnan rabbi eliezer omer shesh esrei amot kederech reshut harabbim* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that the width of a *reshut harabbim* is sixteen *amot*, and that this width constitutes a sufficient break to exempt the fire's originator. We will see this figure again in the next *Mishnah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ נָהָר. רַב אָמַר: נָהָר מַמָּשׁ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲרִיתָּא דְּדַלָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Or** if the fire crossed **a river,** he is exempt. **Rav says:** The term stream means **an actual river. And Shmuel says:** This term means **a water channel.**

Regarding the river: Rav says *nahar mamash* — an actual river, even if it contains no water at the time, presumably provides sufficient width or residual moisture to constitute a break. Shmuel says even an *arisa de-diluy* — an irrigation canal — suffices, but only *if it beit maya in* — if it actually contains water at the time, since otherwise there is nothing stopping the fire.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר נְהַר מַמָּשׁ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא מַיָּא; וּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֲרִיתָּא דְּדַלָּאֵי, אִי אִית בֵּיהּ מַיָּא – אִין, אֲבָל לֵית בֵּיהּ מַיָּא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains their dispute: **The one who says** that it is referring to **an actual river,** Rav, deems exempt one whose fire crosses a riverbed **even when there is no water** in it, since it is sufficiently deep and wide to prevent a typical fire from crossing it. **But the one who says** that it is referring to **a water channel,** Shmuel, holds that **if** the fire crosses a water channel that **has water in it, yes,** the one who kindled the fire is exempt. **But if** the fire crosses a water channel that **does not have water in it,** he is **not** exempt.

### Shelulit Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: וְאֵלּוּ מַפְסִיקִין לַפֵּאָה – הַנַּחַל, וְהַשְּׁלוּלִית, וְדֶרֶךְ הַיָּחִיד, וְדֶרֶךְ הָרַבִּים. מַאי שְׁלוּלִית? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְקוֹם שֶׁמֵּי גְשָׁמִים שׁוֹלְלִין שָׁם. רַב בִּיבִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַמַּת הַמַּיִם שֶׁמְּחַלֶּקֶת שָׁלָל לַאֲגַפֶּיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Pe'a* 2:1): **And these,** the following list of features, **divide** a field **for the purpose of *pe'a*,** i.e., it is no longer considered a single field, but instead *pe'a* must be given from each separate section: **A stream, a *shelulit*, a private road, and a public thoroughfare.** The Gemara asks: **What** is **a *shelulit*? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** It is **a place where rainwater gathers [*sholelin*]. Rav Beivai says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says: It is **a water channel that distributes** its **spoils [*shalal*] to its banks,** since the water spreads to the adjacent cisterns.

The *Gemara* explores the term *shelulit*, citing its usage in the tractate of *Pe'ah*, where the question is what features divide a single field into two, each requiring its own *pe'ah* contribution. The list includes *ha-nachal* (a wadi), *ha-shelulit*, and both a private path and a *derech harabbim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר מְקוֹם שֶׁמֵּי גְשָׁמִים שׁוֹלְלִין שָׁם – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַמַּת הַמַּיִם; וּמַאן דְּאָמַר אַמַּת הַמַּיִם – אֲבָל מְקוֹם שֶׁמֵּי גְשָׁמִים שׁוֹלְלִין שָׁם לָא מַפְסְקִי, דְּהָנְהוּ בָּאגָנֵי דְאַרְעָא מִקְּרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that according to the **one who says** that it means **a place where rainwater gathers,** Shmuel, since gathered rainwater divides a field, **all the more so** does **a water channel,** which is both larger and permanent, divide a field. **But** according to the **one who says** that only **a water channel** divides a field, Rabbi Yoḥanan, he holds that only that divides a field, **but a place where rainwater gathers does not divide** the field, **since these are** simply **called pools of the land,** and are not considered significant enough to divide the field.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel defines *shelulit* as *makom she-mei geshamim sholelim sham* — a place where rainwater streams or collects. This might indicate a naturally low area where runoff flows, or a shallow ditch worn over time by repeated drainage. For it to function as a divider in the field — either for *pe'ah* or for fire liability — it must be some semi-permanent feature of the terrain, not merely a transient puddle that appears during rain.

Rav Bibi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan defines it differently: *amat ha-mayim she-mechaleket shalal le-agafeha* — a water channel that distributes its overflow to its banks, like a ditch that fills with water and spills over. The word *shelulit* on this reading derives from *shalal*, meaning booty or spoil — the water "distributes its spoil" to the surrounding banks.

The two opinions diverge in their implications for *pe'ah*: if a *shelulit* means merely a place where rainwater naturally collects, then *a fortiori* a man-made channel would also divide the field. But if *shelulit* means specifically a man-made channel, then a natural low-lying area where rain puddles would *not* be considered a sufficient division — *dehanu bagnei de'ara mikru* — those are simply called puddles in the ground, and do not rise to the level of a field boundary.

## Distance Standards

### Beit Kor Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ, עַד כַּמָּה תַּעֲבוֹר הַדְּלֵיקָה? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא בְּאֶמְצַע בֵּית כּוֹר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמּוֹת, כְּדֶרֶךְ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: "שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם הַמַּבְעִר אֶת הַבְּעֵרָה" – הַכֹּל לְפִי הַדְּלֵיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **one who kindles** a fire **on his own** premises, **up to what** distance **may the fire travel** within his property for him to still bear liability for damage caused? **Rabbi Elazar ben Azaria says:** The court **views his** location where he kindled the fire **as if it were in the center of a *beit kor*.** Therefore, if the fire spreads and causes damage farther away than half a *beit kor*, the one who kindled the fire is exempt, since he could not anticipate that the fire would spread so far. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** One is liable up to a distance of **sixteen cubits, like** the width of **a public thoroughfare. Rabbi Akiva says:** One is liable up to a distance of **fifty cubits. Rabbi Shimon says:** The verse states: **"The one who kindled the fire shall pay [*shallem yeshallem*] compensation"** (Exodus 22:5), to teach that **everything is according to the fire.**

The next *Mishnah* takes up the question of open-field fire: if you light a fire in your own property and there is no wall — just flat, open land between your field and your neighbor's — how far must the fire travel before you are exempt from liability for what it destroys? The *Mishnah* presents four positions.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: *ro'in oto ke'ilu hu be'emtza beit kor* — we view the person as if standing in the center of a *beit kor*, a field measuring 274 by 274 *amot*. If placed at the center, that means 137 *amot* of open land in every direction. Only after traversing that distance would the fire be considered beyond the range of normal accountability, and anything past that threshold would render the fire-starter exempt.

### 16-Cubit Rule

Rabbi Eliezer says: *tet-zayin amot kederech reshut harabbim* — sixteen *amot*, corresponding to the width of a *reshut harabbim*, is sufficient. This now explains why, in the previous *Mishnah*, the ruling that a *derech harabbim* constitutes an exempting barrier was attributed to Rabbi Eliezer: because Rabbi Eliezer equates the width of a *reshut harabbim* with sixteen *amot*, and holds that this distance is adequate to break liability for spreading fire. Once the fire has traveled sixteen *amot* beyond where it was set, it is no longer the originator's legal responsibility.

This is a notably lenient position. Sixteen *amot* is roughly twenty-four feet — a rather short distance for an open-field fire. One would intuitively feel that only Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's 137-*amot* threshold reflects the real potential of fire to spread. The width of a road, however wide it may be for foot traffic, does not feel like a natural boundary against fire in an open field the way a river or a high wall does. Yet Rabbi Eliezer anchors his position in that standard.

### 50-Cubit Rule

Rabbi Akiva says *chamishim ama* — fifty *amot*, roughly seventy-five feet. This represents a middle position between Rabbi Eliezer's sixteen and Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's 137.

### Context-Dependent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְלֵית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שִׁיעוּרָא בִּדְלֵיקָה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Rabbi Shimon appears to hold that there is no maximum distance which would exempt one from liability for the spreading of a fire. The Gemara asks: **But isn't Rabbi Shimon** of the opinion that there is a maximum **limit** concerning liability **for a fire,** beyond which one is exempt?

Rabbi Shimon says: *shallem yeshallem hamav'ir et hab'era, hakol lefi hadleika* — the verse simply says "the one who kindled the fire shall pay," and everything depends on the particulars of the fire. He rejects all arbitrary numerical thresholds. How can one say sixteen *amot* or fifty *amot* without knowing how large the fire was, how much fuel was on the ground, what the wind conditions were? The only rational standard is case-by-case assessment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: לֹא יַעֲמִיד אָדָם תַּנּוּר בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ עַל גַּבּוֹ גּוֹבַהּ דְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. הָיָה מַעֲמִידוֹ בַּעֲלִיָּיה – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא תַּחְתָּיו מַעֲזִיבָה שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים. וּבַכִּירָה – טֶפַח. וְאִם הִזִּיק – מְשַׁלֵּם מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 20b): **A person may not stand an oven inside the house unless there is a height of four cubits** to the ceiling **above it,** out of concern that the ceiling might catch fire. Similarly, if **he stood it in the attic,** he should not do so **unless there is plaster [*ma'aziva*] underneath it,** above the ceiling of the floor below, **three handbreadths** in thickness, out of concern that the floor might catch fire. **And in the** case of **a stove,** which is smaller and does not reach temperatures as high as those of an oven, a thickness of one **handbreadth** is sufficient. **And** even though he may place his oven or stove in this manner, **if** the fire from the oven or stove **causes damage, he must pay** for **what it damaged.**

The *Gemara* observes that one reason the *rishonim* may have preferred fixed numbers is practical: how does a court retroactively determine wind conditions, fuel volume, and flame size? There is something to be said for a workable legal standard, even an imperfect one. And yet Rabbi Shimon's critique stands — those numbers are simply arbitrary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֶמְרוּ שִׁיעוּרִין הַלָּלוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם הִזִּיק – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Shimon says: These measurements were stated only** to teach **that if** the fire from the oven or stove **causes damage** after the owner takes these precautions, he is **exempt from paying** compensation. Evidently, Rabbi Shimon does have a maximum distance beyond which one is not liable for fire.

Notably, the *psukim* on fire never include the formula of exemption for adequate *shmira* that appear by other *nezikin*. By the *shor* it says *velo yishmerennu* — if he did not guard it; by the *bor*, *velo yechassennu* — if he did not cover it. But by fire, the verse says only *ki tetzei esh* — fire goes out — *shalaim yeshalem* — he shall pay. No exemption is stated. This could support reading Rabbi Shimon as saying that one always pays for fire, no matter what. The *Gemara* does initially read him that way, before concluding otherwise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: הַכֹּל לְפִי גּוֹבַהּ הַדְּלֵיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says:** This is how Rabbi Shimon's statement in the mishna should be understood: **Everything is according to the height of the fire** he initially kindled. If it was a small fire he is not liable to pay for damage caused by it if it traveled far, whereas if he started a large fire, he is liable even if it traveled a great distance.

When the *Gemara* pushes back and asks whether Rabbi Shimon holds that there is truly no level of *shmira* that can exempt one from fire liability, it cites the subsequent *Mishnah* about oven placement, in which Rabbi Shimon himself says that the precautionary measures prescribed are precisely what *create* the exemption if damage occurs. Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha therefore explains Rabbi Shimon's position in the field case as follows: *hakol lefi govah hadleika* — everything depends on the height and character of the fire itself. Rabbi Shimon is not saying you always pay; he is saying the correct standard is the particular fire, not an arbitrary number. Rav Yosef in the name of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: *halacha k'Rebbe Shimon*. Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel likewise: the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says** that **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And so Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

This ruling is in one sense relieving. The fixed numbers of the other opinions always had an arbitrary quality. At the same time, it leaves unresolved the practical question of exactly how the fire is to be assessed. Is it measured based on the fire's current state when one lights it, or based on its potential — what it could become under foreseeable conditions? By analogy, recall the discussion of a well cover: if the cover can protect against oxen but not camels, and camels are commonly found in the area, the cover is inadequate. Similarly, a *shmira* adequate for the fire as it currently stands may be inadequate for the fire as it is likely to develop given prevailing winds and available fuel. The *halacha* demands anticipation of the reasonably foreseeable, not merely the present state.

## Oven Safety Rules

### Oven Clearances

The *Mishnah* here addresses the placement of ovens and hearths in shared residential structures — a question not of after-the-fact liability (*bava kamma*) but of before-the-fact prevention (*bava batra*). This distinction is important: *Bava Batra* addresses the right of neighbors to demand that you configure your property so as not to create hazards for them. The rules of *harchakat nezikin* — distancing to prevent damage — allow a neighbor to compel you to move a hazardous installation even before any damage occurs, whereas Bava Kamma governs what you owe after damage has been done.

### Post-Damage Liability

If full compliance with all these precautionary standards is nevertheless insufficient to prevent liability, the *Mishnah* establishes that the one who kindled the fire nonetheless remains liable. Compliance with the preventive requirements does not automatically generate an exemption after the fact.

### Shimon's Distinction

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. *Lo ne'emru shiurim halalu ela she'im hizik patur milshalem* — the entire reason these specific measurements were prescribed is precisely to tell you that if damage occurs after you have complied with them, you are exempt. This is why the *Gemara* earlier cited this *Mishnah* against the suggestion that Rabbi Shimon holds one always pays for fire: Rabbi Shimon himself establishes that adequate protective measures create exemption from liability. The difference between this case and the open-field discussion is that here we are speaking of a contained fire with defined precautions, whereas there the debate concerned what measures constitute adequate *shmira* for a fire burning in an open field.

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha, as noted above, reads Rabbi Shimon's open-field position as consistent with this: Rabbi Shimon is not eliminating the possibility of exemption, but insisting that the standard for exemption must track the actual characteristics of the fire in question. Both rulings of Shmuel — transmitted through Rav Yosef and through Rav Nachman — confirm: *halacha k'Rebbe Shimon*.

## Buried Property

### Core Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדִישׁ, וְהָיוּ בּוֹ כֵּלִים וְדָלְקוּ, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְשַׁלֵּם מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא גָּדִישׁ שֶׁל חִטִּין אוֹ שֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who kindles a stack** of wheat or barley **and there were vessels** concealed **inside** the stack **and they** caught fire and **burned** together with the stack, **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The one who kindled the fire also **pays** compensation **for what was inside** the stack, **but the Rabbis say: He pays** compensation **only** for **the stack of wheat or barley,** as the case may be, and he is not responsible for that which was concealed within it.

After the extended discussion of *shmira* and measurement, the *Gemara* finally turns to the question of *tamun* — buried or hidden property destroyed by fire — which was the first thing raised in the David narrative at the opening of the *daf*. The *Mishnah* addresses a case where someone lit a stack of grain (*gedish*) on fire, and there were vessels (*keilim*) concealed within it that were destroyed.

Rabbi Yehuda holds: *meshalem mah shebetokho* — one must pay for whatever was in the stack, including things that were hidden there. The *Chachamim* disagree: *eino meshalem ela gedish chitim o shel seorim* — one pays only as if the stack contained what would normally be expected — wheat, or barley, as the case may be. One does not pay for unexpected hidden objects.

At first glance, it is tempting to read the *Chachamim* as saying there is simply no exemption based on what could or could not have been expected — just an arbitrary rule that *tamun ba'eish*, hidden property in a fire, creates no liability. But the simpler reading of the *Mishnah* resists that. The *Chachamim* seem to be saying something eminently sensible: if you could not reasonably have known that something precious was buried in that haystack, you cannot be held accountable for it. If you were making a fire in the middle of a field surrounded by what appeared to be wheat, and it turns out someone had buried something valuable in that wheat — that is not a foreseeable consequence of your negligence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּמַדְלִיק אֶת הַבִּירָה, שֶׁהוּא מְשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָהּ; שֶׁכֵּן דֶּרֶךְ בְּנֵי אָדָם לְהַנִּיחַ בַּבָּתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda and exempt one from payment for vessels concealed inside the stack in the field, **concede to Rabbi Yehuda** that if one **sets fire to a building,** he **pays** compensation **for everything that was** burned **inside it, since it is the** normal **way of people to place** items **in houses.**

The *Mishnah* also records that the *Chachamim* agree with Rabbi Yehuda in the case of *madlik et habirah* — one who burns down a large house. In that case, *meshalem kol mah shebetokho* — one pays for all the contents, *shekein derekh bnei adam lehaniach b'vatim* — because people normally keep things in their houses. Here the rationale is plain: burning someone's house makes you responsible for all ordinary contents, because that is entirely foreseeable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה גְּדִי כָּפוּת לוֹ, וְעֶבֶד סָמוּךְ לוֹ וְנִשְׂרַף עִמּוֹ – חַיָּיב. עֶבֶד כָּפוּת לוֹ, וּגְדִי סָמוּךְ לוֹ וְנִשְׂרַף עִמּוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **there was a goat tied to** the stack of grain, **and** there was a Canaanite **slave nearby** who was not tied to it, **and** both the goat and the slave **were burned** together **with** the stack and killed, the one who kindled the fire is **liable** to pay compensation for both. Conversely, if the **slave was tied** to the stack **and** there was **a goat nearby** that was not tied to it, **and** they were both **burned together with it,** the one who kindled the fire is **exempt** from payment for damage because he is liable to receive capital punishment for murder, and he is punished only for the greater transgression.

Before the *Gemara* explores the detailed mechanics of this debate, it pauses on another clause of the *Mishnah* — the case of a goat (*gedi kafut lo*) bound near the stack, and a slave (*eved samukh lo*) positioned nearby. If both caught fire and burned, one is *chayav*. The reason is subtle: one is liable despite the slave's death because the principle of *kim leih bid'raba minei* — by which a greater punishment subsumes a lesser monetary one — does not apply, since the slave was not bound and therefore could have fled. The *Gemara* is careful to specify: one is *chayav* only if the *gedi* was bound (so it could not escape) and the *eved* was unbound (so *kim leih* does not apply). If the *eved* was bound — effectively meaning one committed an act of murder — then *kim leih* does apply, and one is *patur* on everything. Likewise, if the *gedi* was unbound and should have run away, one is *patur* for the *gedi* on grounds that it should have escaped.

### Own vs. Friend's

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּמַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהָלְכָה וְאָכְלָה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיַּיב אַנִּזְקֵי טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ, וְרַבָּנַן פָּטְרִי; אֲבָל בְּמַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Kahana says:** This **dispute** between the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda concerning vessels concealed in a stack is referring specifically to a case **where one kindled** a fire **on his own** premises **and** the fire **spread and consumed** the stack **on another's** property. In that case, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** the one who kindled the fire **liable for damage** to **concealed** articles damaged by **a fire, but the Rabbis exempt** him. **But in** a case of one who **kindles** a fire **on another's** premises, **all agree** that **he pays** compensation for **everything that is** contained **within it.**

Rav Kahana introduces a crucial structural observation: *machlokes bemadlik betoch shelo vehalcha betoch shel chaveiro* — the entire debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Chachamim* about *tamun* applies only when the fire was started in one's own domain and spread into another's. When it starts on one's own property, there is a question whether one must pay for hidden objects in the neighbor's domain that the fire reaches. Rabbi Yehuda says *chayav*; the *Rabanan* say *patur*.

*Aval bemadlik betoch shel chaveiro* — but if one started the fire in the friend's property, *dekuli alma meshalem kol mah shebetocha* — everyone agrees one pays for everything. The act of originating a fire on another's property is fundamentally more culpable, and no exemptions apply.

### Spreading Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּמַדְלִיק אֶת הַבִּירָה – שֶׁמְּשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָהּ, שֶׁכֵּן דֶּרֶךְ בְּנֵי אָדָם לְהַנִּיחַ בַּבָּתִּים; לִפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהָלְכָה וְאָכְלָה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ; אֲבָל מַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁהָיָה בְּתוֹכוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so,** that the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Yehuda in the case of one who kindles the fire on the premises of another, then **rather than teaching the latter clause** that states: **The Rabbis concede to Rabbi Yehuda** that if one **sets fire to a building he pays** compensation **for everything** that was burned **inside it, since it is the** normal **way of people to place** items **in houses, let** the *tanna* instead **distinguish and teach** the concession of the Rabbis **in** the context of **the same** case of one setting fire to a stack: **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where he kindled the fire on his** premises **and it spread and consumed** a stack **on** the premises of **another. But if he kindled** the fire **on** property belonging to **another,** all agree that **he pays** compensation for **everything that was inside** the stack. Since the mishna is not worded in this manner, it seems that this is not the opinion of the Rabbis.

Rava challenges Rav Kahana's reading with a pointed textual argument. If the debate is only about fires that start on one's property and spread, why does the *Mishnah* settle the house-burning case by appealing to the general principle that *people normally leave things in homes*? If someone is burning their friend's house from the outside, the exemption or liability should not hinge on what people generally store in homes — it should simply hinge on the fact that you set fire to your friend's property, in which case everyone agrees you pay for everything. The appeal to normalcy only makes sense if we are distinguishing between expected and unexpected contents in a case where the fire *originated* on one's own property and spread to the house. But in that case, the *Mishnah* should have made that distinction explicitly within the haystack case, rather than introducing the house as a separate illustration.

### Started in Friend's

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בְּתַרְתֵּי פְּלִיגִי; פְּלִיגִי בְּמַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהָלְכָה וְאָכְלָה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיַּיב אַטָּמוּן בָּאֵשׁ, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי לָא מִחַיַּיב. וּפְלִיגִי נָמֵי בְּמַדְלִיק בְּשֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: מְשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ אַרְנְקִי, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: כֵּלִים שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לְהַטְמִין בְּגָדִישׁ, כְּגוֹן מוֹרִיגִּין וּכְלֵי בָּקָר – הוּא דִּמְשַׁלֵּם; כֵּלִים שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן לְהַטְמִין בְּגָדִישׁ – לָא מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda **disagree with regard to two** issues: **They disagree** with regard to the case of one who **kindles a fire on his own** premises **and** it then **spreads and consumes** a stack **on** property belonging to **another, as Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **liable** even **for a concealed** article damaged by **a fire, but the Rabbis hold** that he is **not liable. And they disagree also** in the case of one **who kindles a fire on** premises belonging to **another, as Rabbi Yehuda holds** that he must **pay** compensation **for everything within it, even a purse** of money if it was concealed within the stack, **but the Rabbis hold** that it is only for **vessels that are typically concealed inside a stack, such as threshing tools or yokes for cattle, that he must pay** compensation, but for **vessels** or other items **that are not typically concealed inside a stack** he is **not** liable to **pay** compensation.

*Ella amar Rava bitartei pligi* — Rava therefore concludes that the debate operates on two separate fronts simultaneously, and this reading preserves both the notion of an arbitrary *tamun* exemption and the role of reasonable expectation.

### Permission Limits

The first front is when the fire starts on one's own property and spreads. There, Rabbi Yehuda holds one is *chayav* for *tamun ba'eish*, and the *Rabanan* hold one is *patur* — and this exemption is *arbitrary*. When it spreads from your domain, the *Chachamim* exempt you for anything hidden, regardless of whether it was something one could have expected or not. This is a genuinely striking ruling: it means that if one is negligent with a fire and it spreads to a neighbor's property and burns down the neighbor's house, one would — according to the *Chachamim* — be liable for the structure but potentially exempt from paying for its contents, since those contents are *tamun* relative to the fire-starter who was in a different domain.

The second front of the debate is when the fire starts in the friend's property. There too Rabbi Yehuda and the *Chachamim* disagree, but the operative principle shifts. Rabbi Yehuda says one pays for everything — *afilu arneki*, even a wallet. The *Chachamim* say one pays for *keilim she'darkan lehatmin b'gadish* — objects that are normally stored in or near a grain stack, such as farm implements and animal equipment — because those were reasonably foreseeable. But *keilim she'ein darkan lehatmin b'gadish* — objects not normally stored in a stack — *lo meshalem*, one does not pay for those.

This is why the *Mishnah*'s ruling about the burned house makes perfect sense: when you burn your friend's house, you started the fire in his domain. You pay for everything that could normally be expected to be in a house — because that is the standard that applies when the fire originates on the friend's property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדִישׁ, וְהָיוּ בּוֹ כֵּלִים וְדָלְקוּ, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁהָיָה בְּתוֹכוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא גָּדִישׁ שֶׁל חִטִּין אוֹ גָדִישׁ שֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִין. וְרוֹאִים מְקוֹם כֵּלִים כְּאִילּוּ הוּא מָלֵא תְּבוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to one **who kindles a stack and there were vessels inside it** that were **burned, Rabbi Yehuda says:** The one who kindled the fire **pays** compensation **for everything that was inside it, but the Rabbis say: He pays** compensation only for the value of **a stack of wheat or a stack of barley,** and he does not pay compensation for the vessels. **And** the court **views the place** where **the vessels** were **as if it were filled with grain,** and calculates the amount of compensation accordingly.

A supporting *baraita* confirms Rava's analysis. *Bameh devarim amurim bemadlik betoch shelo v'halcha v'dalka betoch shel chaveiro* — the exemption for *tamun* applies when one lit on one's own property and the fire spread. *Aval madlik betoch shel chaveiro, meshalem kol mah shehayah betocha* — if one started in the friend's property, one pays for everything (meaning: everything that was reasonably foreseeable to be there).

### Grain Swap Cases

The *Mishnah* then records a case in which Rabbi Yehuda agrees with the *Chachamim*. If one granted a neighbor permission to stack grain in one's field — *mashreh makom le-chaveiro le-hagdish gadish* — and the neighbor stacked grain and also buried objects within it, one is liable only for the grain itself, not for the buried objects. Here the property owner can say: I gave you permission for your grain. I did not authorize you to hide your vessels here. Since one's permission extended only to grain, the presence of hidden objects in excess of that permission means those objects are, from the property owner's perspective, there without authorization. Just as a person who enters one's property without permission cannot recover for injuries that occur there, items stored beyond the scope of permission fall outside the scope of liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהָלְכָה וְדָלְקָה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, אֲבָל מַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁהָיָה בְּתוֹכוֹ. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לַחֲכָמִים בְּמַשְׁאִיל מָקוֹם לַחֲבֵירוֹ לְהַגְדִּישׁ גָּדִישׁ, וְהִגְדִּישׁ וְהִטְמִין, שֶׁאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא דְּמֵי גָדִישׁ בִּלְבָד. לְהַגְדִּישׁ חִטִּין וְהִגְדִּישׁ שְׂעוֹרִין; שְׂעוֹרִין וְהִגְדִּישׁ חִטִּין; חִטִּין וְחִיפָּן בִּשְׂעוֹרִין; שְׂעוֹרִין וְחִיפָּן בְּחִטִּים – שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא דְּמֵי שְׂעוֹרִין בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is in a case where one **kindled a fire on his** premises **and** the fire **spread and burned** items **in** premises belonging to **another. But** with regard to one who **kindles a fire on** premises belonging to **another** that destroys a stack of wheat, **all agree** that **he pays** compensation for **everything that was inside** the stack. **And Rabbi Yehuda concedes to the Rabbis that in** a case where one **lends space to another** in his field **to stack grain, and** this person **stacked** grain in the field **and concealed** items inside the stack, if the owner of the field causes the stack to be burned down, **he pays compensation for the stack alone,** since the other person did not have permission to conceal items inside his stack. Similarly, if one received permission **to stack wheat** in the field of another **and he stacked barley;** or conversely, if he received permission to stack **barley and he stacked wheat;** or similarly, if he stacked **wheat and covered** the stack **with barley,** or stacked **barley and covered** the stack **with wheat,** in all these cases, Rabbi Yehuda concedes **that he pays compensation for** the **barley alone,** which is less than the value of wheat.

The *Mishnah* then works through a series of *grain swap* cases. If one granted permission to store wheat and the neighbor stored barley, one pays only for barley — that is the easy case. If one granted permission for barley and the neighbor stored wheat, one also pays only for barley — the more valuable wheat was stored without permission, so one is liable only up to the value of the permitted barley. The rationale is analogous to the rule that if an ox gores someone on one's property whom the owner told not to enter, the owner is *patur*. Since the wheat was present without authorization, the loss of wheat is treated as if only barley had been there.

What of wheat covered with a layer of barley, or barley covered with a layer of wheat? In every such case, *eino meshalem ela dmei seorim bilvad* — one pays only the value of barley. In every permutation, the liability is capped at whatever the explicitly permitted grain was worth. A subtle point emerges: even in the case where one had permission only for barley and the neighbor brought wheat, one is not entirely *patur* — one still pays the equivalent of barley, because the neighbor *could* have brought barley, and at minimum one bears liability as if they had. Just as when the *keilim* are present and one does not pay for them but does pay *as if the space were filled with grain*, the principle here is that the scope of permission defines the ceiling of liability, but not a complete exemption. As one student observes, the relative flammability of wheat versus barley — one grain may burn more readily than the other — could also factor into the practical calculation, though the *Gemara* does not explicitly develop this point.
