# Zevachim 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Azarah Dimensions

### Intro: 187 x 135

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹרֶךְ מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים וָשֶׁבַע עַל רוֹחַב מֵאָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְחָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** an area whose **length** is **187** cubits **by 135** cubits in **width.**

The daf opens by returning to the final line of 55b, where Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Shmuel: *ein chayavin mishum tum'ah ela al orech me'ah u'shmonim v'sheva al rochav me'ah u'shloshim v'chamesh* — one is liable for *tum'ah* only within the space measuring 187 *amot* in length by 135 *amot* in width. This is the *azarah* proper — the *ezrat kohanim* together with the *ezrat Yisrael* — which constitutes the *makom hamikdash* in the technical sense. The *ezrat nashim*, situated to the east of *Sha'ar Nicanor*, carries the *kedushah* of *Har HaBayit* but is not the *azarah* proper. The entire *azarah*, however, shares a uniform *kedushah*, and because of the way multiple doors were constructed opening into the *heichal*, the entire space is considered *petach ohel moed*.

### Azarah for Offerings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: כׇּל הָעֲזָרָה הָיְתָה מֵאָה שְׁמוֹנִים וָשֶׁבַע עַל רוֹחַב מֵאָה שְׁלֹשִׁים וְחָמֵשׁ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר לִי אַבָּא: כְּגוֹן זֶה – כֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְשָׁם וְאוֹכְלִין שָׁם קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְשׁוֹחֲטִין שָׁם קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וְחַיָּיבִין מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman: The entire** Temple **courtyard was 187** cubits in length **by 135** cubits in **width.** Rav Naḥman **said to** the *tanna*: **My father said this to me:** In an area **such as this,** the **priests enter there and eat offerings of the most sacred order there, and slaughter offerings of lesser sanctity there, and are liable due to** entering in a state of **ritual impurity.**

This zone — 187 by 135 — has been the operative frame throughout the preceding *mishnayot*: it is the place where *kodshei kodashim* are eaten *lizichrei kehunah*, the phrase *lifnim min hakla'im* encompassing the entire *azarah*. Alongside eating *kodshei kodashim*, this is where *kodashim kalim* are slaughtered. Now a further dimension is added: it is also the zone in which one who enters while *tamei* incurs *karet*. While there are prohibitions against *tum'ah* on *Har HaBayit* and in Yerushalayim for higher degrees of *tum'ah*, within the *azarah* proper all *tum'ah* is forbidden and carries a *chiyuv karet*.

### Rav Nachman's Teaching

A *tanna* taught before Rav Nachman: *kol ha'azarah haytah* 187 *orech* by 135 — 187 by 135. Rav Nachman responded with the tradition he had received from his father: *kegon zeh kohanim nichnasim lesham ve'ochlim sham kodshei kodashim ve'shochtim sham kodashim kalim* — in that space, priests enter, eat *kodshei kodashim*, and slaughter *kodashim kalim*, adding now explicitly that this is also the space in which one is *chayav* for *tum'ah*.

## Lishkot Exclusion

### Wall Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אִילֵּימָא לְמַעוֹטֵי חַלּוֹנוֹת, דְּלָתוֹת וְעוֹבִי הַחוֹמָה – תְּנֵינָא: הַחַלּוֹנוֹת וְעוֹבִי הַחוֹמָה כְּלִפְנִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: These specific dimensions serve **to exclude what? If we say** they are stated **to exclude** the **windows, doors, and thickness of the wall, we learn** the opposite in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 85b): **The windows** in the wall **and the thickness of the wall** are considered **as though** they are **inside** the Temple courtyard.

The Gemara immediately asks: *limu'tei mai?* — what is the precise point of specifying 187 by 135? If we simply want to convey where one is *chayav mishum tum'ah*, why not just say "in the *azarah*"? The specificity implies that there is some space one might have thought was included that is in fact excluded. What is being excluded?

The first possibility raised is that the measurements are inner dimensions, and the specification comes to exclude the thickness of the surrounding wall — its windows, doors, and mass — from the *azarah* proper. This would be a natural reading: perhaps 187 by 135 measures the interior, and sitting atop or within the wall is legally "outside." But the Gemara immediately rejects this: a *beraita* teaches *hachalonot ve'ovei hachomah kilifnim* — the windows and thickness of the wall are treated as inside. One could eat *kodshei kodashim* while positioned within the wall's thickness or a windowsill, and one would be *chayav* for *tum'ah* there. The 187 by 135 cannot be meant to exclude the wall itself. A fascinating tangential question arises: does the *issur yeshivah* — the prohibition against sitting in the *azarah* — apply to the top of the wall, which has *kedushat Azarah* but is not the floor of the *azarah* itself? This is left as an intriguing open question.

### Chambers Built

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי לְשָׁכוֹת? וְאִי בְּנוּיוֹת בַּחוֹל וּפְתוּחוֹת לַקֹּדֶשׁ – וְהָתְנַן: תּוֹכָן קוֹדֶשׁ! מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** they are stated **to exclude** the **chambers** in the Temple courtyard. The Gemara asks: **And if** it is referring to chambers that are **built in the non-sacred** area, which is outside this designated space, **and open to the sacred** area, **but didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Ma'aser Sheni* 3:8): The inside of chambers that are built in the sacred area but are open to the non-sacred area is non-sacred, but their roofs are sacred. If they are built in a non-sacred area and open to a sacred area, **their interior** is **sacred** and their roofs are non-sacred? The Gemara answers: They have sanctity **by rabbinic law.**

The Gemara then advances to the second possibility: the specification comes to exclude *lishkot* — chambers built within or off the wall. The scenario under consideration is *lishkot habonuyot bechol ufetuchot lekodesh*: chambers whose floor rests on ground that has not been consecrated with *kedushat Azarah* (it has the sanctity of *Har HaBayit* rather than the *azarah*), but whose doorway opens directly into the Sanctuary. One might compare such a chamber to a walk-in closet accessible only through the bedroom — the only access is via the *azarah* itself. The question is whether such a space is treated as part of the *azarah* or not, and consequently whether one is *chayav* for *tum'ah* there, whether one may slaughter *kodashim kalim* there, and whether one may eat *kodshei kodashim* there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: לְשָׁכוֹת בְּנוּיוֹת בַּחוֹל, וּפְתוּחוֹת לַקֹּדֶשׁ. מִנַּיִן שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְשָׁם וְאוֹכְלִים שָׁם קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים וּשְׁיָרֵי מִנְחָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּחֲצַר אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד יֹאכְלוּהָ״ – הַתּוֹרָה רִיבְּתָה חֲצֵירוֹת הַרְבֵּה אֵצֶל אֲכִילָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** do they **not** have sanctity **by Torah law? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the **chambers** that are **built in the non-sacred** area of the Temple Mount **and open to the sacred** area, **from where** is it derived **that the priests enter there and eat there offerings of the most sacred order and the remainders of meal offerings? The verse states** with regard to a meal offering: "It shall be eaten without leaven in a holy place; **in the courtyard of the Tent of Meeting they shall eat it."** (Leviticus 6:9). The verse could have simply stated: "In a holy place," which indicates the courtyard. By also stating: "In the courtyard of the Tent of Meeting," **the Torah amplified many courtyards with one** type of **eating,** i.e., of the meal offering. If the chambers do not have sanctity by Torah law, the Sages would not have permitted the priests to eat offerings of the most sacred order there.

The Gemara initially suggests that a *Mishnah* in *Ma'aser Sheni* teaches that these chambers — built on unconsecrated ground but opening into the Sanctuary — are treated with the *kedushah* of the *azarah*, implying one could eat *kodshei kodashim* there. The Gemara responds: that ruling is only *miderabbanan*. Biblically, such a chamber is not fully equivalent to the *azarah*. One is not *chayav* for *tum'ah* there *mide'oraita*, and one cannot eat *kodshei kodashim* there as a matter of biblical law.

### Eating in Chambers

The Gemara pushes back: but a *beraita* explicitly teaches — *lishkot habonuyot bechol ufetuchot lekodesh, minayin shehakohanim nichnasim lesham ve'ochlim sham kodshei kodashim veshirei menachot?* — how do we know that priests may enter and eat *kodshei kodashim* and *shirei menachot* there? *Talmud lomar: bechatzer ohel moed yochluha.* The verse says to eat it "in the courtyard of the *ohel moed*." The *drasha* follows: the verse uses one word for eating — *yochluha* — but three words to define where. This generates the principle *hatorah ribta chatzerot harbeh etzel achila achat*: the Torah multiplied courtyards in connection with a single act of eating — implying that there are multiple courtyards, not only the *azarah* floor itself, where eating is permitted.

The conceptual move here is important. The question "does this chamber have *kedushat Azarah*?" and the question "may one eat *kodshei kodashim* there?" are not necessarily answered by the same criterion. The chamber may lack *kedushat Azarah* proper — its ground is *chol* — and yet remain a *chatzer ohel moed*, a space connected to the *ohel moed*, and that connection suffices for the purposes of eating.

### Rava: Le'achila Shani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לַאֲכִילָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The *halakha* **concerning eating** is **different.** The Torah specifically includes these chambers in addition to the Temple courtyard itself for eating offerings of the most sacred order. By contrast, with regard to slaughtering offerings of lesser sanctity and the punishment for entering the courtyard in a state of ritual impurity, these chambers are not considered sacred by Torah law.

Rava articulates the resolution: *le'achila shani* — eating is different. When we say that one is *chayav mishum tum'ah* only within 187 by 135, we are excluding these chambers. Similarly, one may not slaughter *kodashim kalim* in these chambers. But the eating of *kodshei kodashim* is governed by a separate rule, and these chambers remain valid locations for it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה לָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: לְשָׁכוֹת הַבְּנוּיוֹת לַחוֹל וּפְתוּחוֹת לַקּוֹדֶשׁ – כֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְשָׁם וְאוֹכְלִין שָׁם קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְאֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין שָׁם קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וְחַיָּיבִין מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But with regard to ritual impurity,** is one **not** liable to receive *karet* for entering these chambers? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the **chambers that are built in the non-sacred** area **and** that **open to the sacred** area, **priests enter there and eat offerings of the most sacred order there, but may not slaughter offerings of lesser sanctity there, and are liable** to receive *karet* **due to ritual impurity** if they enter there while ritually impure?

The Gemara, however, challenges the claim that *tum'ah* liability is excluded from these chambers. Another *beraita* seems to teach the opposite: *lishkot habonuyot bechol ufetuchot lekodesh — kohanim nichnasim lesham ve'ochlim sham kodshei kodashim* (eating: yes), *ve'ein shochtim sham kodashim kalim* (slaughtering: no), *vechayavim mishum tum'ah* (liable for *tum'ah*: yes). If one is *chayav mishum tum'ah* there, that sounds like these chambers truly are temple ground — which contradicts the claim that 187 by 135 is meant to exclude them for *tum'ah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו אָמְרַתְּ ״אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין״? תְּנִי נָמֵי ״אֵין חַיָּיבִין״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava could answer: **Did you not say** in the *baraita* that **they may not slaughter** offerings of lesser sanctity in those chambers? If so, emend the text of the *baraita* and **teach also: They are not liable** to receive *karet* for entering in a state of ritual impurity.

The Gemara resolves this by reading the *beraita* differently. Since the *beraita* uses the word *ein* — "one does not slaughter there" — that negation, says the Gemara, should be read as extending to the *tum'ah* clause as well: *tani nami ein chayavin mishum tum'ah*. The *beraita* should be understood as saying one is also *not* liable for *tum'ah* in those chambers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין – בָּעֵינָא כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח, וְלֵיכָּא; אֶלָּא אֵין חַיָּיבִין – אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Granted,** the priests **may not slaughter** there, because slaughtering an offering **requires** that it be performed **before the entrance** of the Temple courtyard, **and** if he slaughters it inside a chamber, it **is not. But why** would they **not** be **liable** for entering there in a state of ritual impurity?

But why, then, can one not slaughter *kodashim kalim* there? The Gemara explores two possible rationales. One possibility is that it is a matter of *petach ohel moed*: the chamber is not considered *lifnei petach ohel moed* — even with the door open — because one is in one's own enclosed space rather than facing the opening of the *heichal*. This is the concept of *tzidei tzdadim* developed earlier in the tractate. Alternatively, the reason may be more fundamental: since the *beraita* says *ein shochtim*, the explanation must be that the chamber simply lacks *kedushat Azarah* entirely — not a refinement about *petach ohel moed* but a basic disqualification. And if it lacks *kedushat Azarah*, then the *tum'ah* liability equally does not apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין – מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּאִיכָּא שְׁחִיטָה כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח?! דְּאִי לֵיכָּא, לְמַאי אִיצְטְרִיךְ? אֶלָּא אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא שָׁחֵיט כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח – תְּנִי ״אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין״ מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קָדֵישׁ; תְּנִי נָמֵי: ״אֵין חַיָּיבִין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **And according to your reasoning,** when it teaches that they **may not slaughter** there, **are we not dealing** even with a case in **which there is** an act of **slaughter** performed **before the entrance,** i.e., the entrance to the chamber directly faced the entrance of the Temple? **As if not, for what** purpose **was** it **necessary** to teach this at all? **Rather, even though he slaughters** the offering **before the entrance,** the *baraita* **teaches** that a priest **may not slaughter** offerings in the chambers **because** that area **is not sacred.** If so, **teach also** that they **are not liable** to receive *karet* for entering the chambers in a state of ritual impurity, as they are not sacred.

The reason eating remains permitted despite the absence of *kedushat Azarah* is the *ribui* of *bechatzer ohel moed*: even without *kedushat Azarah*, the connection to the *ohel moed* suffices for *achila*. The three halachic categories thus operate under genuinely distinct criteria: *tum'ah* liability and *shechitat kodashim kalim* require *kedushat Azarah* proper; eating *kodshei kodashim* requires only being within a *chatzer* connected to the *ohel moed*.

### Eating & Petach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַאֲכִילָה לָא בָּעֵינַן כְּנֶגֶד הַפֶּתַח?! וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי פִּשְׁפָּשִׁין הָיוּ בְּבֵית הַחֲלִיפוֹת, גּוֹבְהָן שְׁמוֹנֶה; כְּדֵי לְהַכְשִׁיר אֶת הָעֲזָרָה לַאֲכִילַת קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, וְלִשְׁחִיטַת קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים! אָמַר רָבִינָא: סְמִי מִכָּאן אֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And for eating** an offering, **do we not require** that this take place **before the entrance** of the Temple courtyard? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: There were two wickets in the Chamber of Knives,** and **their height** was **eight** cubits; the function of these openings was **in order to render fit the** entire Temple **courtyard for the eating of offerings of the most sacred order, and** to render fit all of the Temple courtyard **for the slaughter of offerings of lesser sanctity? Ravina said: Remove from here** the clause concerning **eating.** The eating of offerings does not have to take place before the entrance of the Temple courtyard.

The Gemara now presses further on the eating question. Earlier in the tractate, *beraitot* discussing the opening of additional doors into the *heichal* — to extend the concept of *petach ohel moed* throughout the *azarah* — appeared to tie the permissibility of eating *kodshei kodashim* to the concept of *petach ohel moed* as well. A *beraita* is cited: *Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Yehuda* taught that two *pishpashim* — small doorways — were opened in the *beit hachalifot* to make the *azarah* valid for eating *kodshei kodashim* and slaughtering *kodashim kalim*. If eating *kodshei kodashim* is mentioned alongside the rationale of *petach ohel moed*, it seems to require *petach ohel moed* just as slaughtering does. But if so, how can these chambers — which presumably are not always *petach ohel moed* — be valid for eating?

### Ravina: Achila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״בַּשְּׁלוּ אֶת הַבָּשָׂר פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, וְשָׁם תֹּאכְלוּ אוֹתוֹ״! קׇדְשֵׁי שָׁעָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written** with regard to the offerings brought at the inauguration of the Tabernacle: "And Moses said to Aaron and to his sons: **Cook the meat at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting; and eat it there"** (Leviticus 8:31). This indicates that the eating of offerings had to be at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting. The Gemara answers: **Offerings** that were sacrificed only one **time are different.** Since they were specifically commanded for just that time, one cannot derive from there any *halakhot* with regard to offerings in general.

Ravina resolves the difficulty: *sami mikan achila* — remove eating from the equation in the earlier *beraitot* where it appears alongside *petach ohel moed*. Eating was mentioned in those contexts incidentally, because it is typically discussed together with slaughtering, not because it actually requires *petach ohel moed*. In truth, no *pasuk* ever conditions the eating of *kodshei kodashim* on *petach ohel moed*; that concept appears repeatedly in connection with *shechita* but never with *achila*. This distinction reflects a deep logic: when the *korban* is being slaughtered, it is being offered to God — the imagery of the king's doors being open is apt. When the *kohanim* eat it, they are on sanctified ground, but the act of eating no longer requires the same orientation toward the opening of the Sanctuary.

This means: even without these doors being open, one may eat *kodshei kodashim* in the *azarah*; and one may eat *kodshei kodashim* in a chamber with a closed door that is not facing *petach ohel moed* — provided the chamber is connected to the *azarah*. The *nafka mina* between eating and slaughtering is thus real and structurally significant.

A *pasuk* from the *miluim* is raised as a potential counterexample: *bashlu et habasar petach ohel moed... vesham tochlu oto* — cook the meat at the opening of the *ohel moed* and eat it there. This seems to require eating specifically *petach ohel moed*. The Gemara answers: *kodshei sha'ah shani* — the *miluim* offerings were unique, a one-time event. Moreover, *Tosafot* explains: during the *miluim*, the entire week of consecration was conducted *petach ohel moed* — the priests were instructed to remain at the entrance for seven days — so naturally the eating, the cooking, the entire ceremony was framed in terms of *petach ohel moed*. This is not a general rule for all *kodshei kodashim*. The elegant result is that the *halachah* accurately mirrors the *pesukim*: *shechita* requires *petach ohel moed* throughout, eating requires only the grounds of the *azarah*, and even a connected chamber without *kedushat Azarah* suffices for eating.

## Blood of Shelamim

### Blood Notar Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אֲבוּדִימִי: מִנַּיִן לְדָם – שֶׁנִּפְסָל בִּשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַקְרִיבוֹ אֶת זִבְחוֹ יֵאָכֵל״ – בְּיוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ אַתָּה מַקְרִיב, בְּיוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ אִי אַתָּה מַקְרִיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avudimi says: From where** is it derived **that the blood** of offerings **becomes disqualified at sunset** and can no longer be presented on the altar? This is **as it is stated** in the verse: "But if the sacrifice of his offering be a vow, or a gift offering, **it shall be eaten on the day that he sacrifices his offering [*zivḥo*],** and on the morrow" (Leviticus 7:16). This means that **on the day that you slaughter [*zove'aḥ*]** the offering **you sacrifice** it on the altar and present its blood. But **on the day that you do not slaughter** the offering **you may not sacrifice** it and present its blood.

The Gemara shifts to a new topic. Rav Yitzchak bar Avudimi asks: *minayin ledam shenifsal beshki'at hachamah?* — how do we know that the blood of a *shelamim* becomes invalid at sunset? This might seem obvious, but there is a genuine *hava amina*: since a *shelamim* may be eaten for two days, perhaps its blood is similarly valid for two days and need not be applied on the day of slaughter. One might imagine, as Rashi suggests, that the blood was placed at the top of the *mizbe'ach*, where it arguably does not become *notar*; or, as *Tosafot* suggests, perhaps blood is excluded from *notar* the way it is excluded from *piggul* — since it is the *mechaper*, the agent of atonement, it may have a special status. The question is: where do we derive that even for a *shelamim*, the blood must be applied on the day of slaughter?

### Shuchteta Makriv

The answer comes from the verse: *b'yom hakrivo et zivcho ye'achel* — on the day that one brings [the blood of] one's offering, it shall be eaten. The word *hakrivo* refers to the blood application and the word *zivcho* refers to the slaughtering. Their juxtaposition teaches: *b'yom she'atah zove'ach atah makriv, b'yom she'ee atah zove'ach ee atah makriv* — on the day you slaughter you apply the blood; on a day when you do not slaughter, you do not apply the blood. Blood and slaughter must occur on the same day.

### Counting from Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ! אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא: ״בְּיוֹם זִבְחוֹ יֵאָכֵל״; ״הַקְרִיבוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בְּיוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ אַתָּה מַקְרִיב, בְּיוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ אִי אַתָּה מַקְרִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **he requires this** verse **for** the matter **itself,** to teach that a peace offering may be eaten for only two days? The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse state: On the day he slaughters it shall it be eaten,** and the following day. **Why do I** need the verse to add the term **"that he sacrifices it"? Conclude from** the wording of the verse that **on the day that you slaughter** the offering **you sacrifice** the blood on the altar, but **on a day that you do not slaughter** the offering **you do not sacrifice** the blood on the altar.

The Gemara objects: this derivation is needed for the *pasuk*'s own basic content — teaching that a *shelamim* is eaten for two days. Can one use it simultaneously to derive the same-day requirement for blood? Furthermore, a potential alternative reading is proposed: perhaps the verse is telling us not that slaughter and blood must be on the same day, but that the two-day eating window is counted from when the blood is applied rather than from when the animal is slaughtered. On this reading, the reason both *hakrivo* and *zivcho* appear together is to anchor the eating period to the moment of blood-bringing. If that were so, blood could be applied the day after slaughter, and the two days of eating would then be counted from that later day.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: אִי קָרֵיב דָּם הָאִידָּנָא – נִיתְאֲכֵיל בָּשָׂר הָאִידָּנָא וְלִמְחַר, אִי קָרֵיב דָּם לִמְחַר – נִיתְאֲכֵיל בָּשָׂר לִמְחַר וּלְיוֹמָא אוּחְרָא? אִם כֵּן, נֵימָא קְרָא: ״בְּיוֹם הַקְרִיבוֹ יֹאכַל״; ״זִבְחוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בְּיוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ אַתָּה מַקְרִיבוֹ, בְּיוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה זוֹבֵחַ אִי אַתָּה מַקְרִיבוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this proof: **But perhaps this** is what **the Merciful One is saying: If** the **blood is sacrificed today,** i.e., the day the offering was slaughtered, the **meat** may **be eaten today and tomorrow. If** the **blood is sacrificed tomorrow,** the **meat may be eaten tomorrow and the following day.** The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse state: On the day it is sacrificed it shall be eaten,** and the following day. **Why do I** need the verse to state: "That he sacrifices **his offering"? Conclude from** the wording of the verse that **on the day that you slaughter** the offering **you sacrifice** the blood on the altar, but **on a day that you do not slaughter** the offering **you do not sacrifice** the blood on the altar.

The Gemara rejects this: if the sole purpose of mentioning both *hakrivo* and *zivcho* were to indicate when to begin counting the two days, the verse should simply use the relevant term — either *zivcho* (count from slaughter) or *hakrivo* (count from blood application) — not both. The fact that both terms appear together, each in some sense redundant if the other is present, teaches that slaughtering and blood application must occur on the same day. The *shelamim*'s blood cannot be applied the day after slaughter; even if stored atop the *mizbe'ach*, the same-day requirement applies.

## Piggul/Notar Night 2

### Machshevet Night 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַמְחַשֵּׁב לְאוֹר שְׁלִישִׁי – חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: כָּשֵׁר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: פָּסוּל. חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר כָּשֵׁר – דְּהָא לָא אִינְּתִיק לִשְׂרֵיפָה; רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר פָּסוּל – דְּהָא אִידְּחִי לֵיהּ מֵאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to **one who** slaughters a peace offering and **intends** to eat it **on the evening** preceding the **third** day, **Ḥizkiyya says:** The offering is **valid,** and it is not disqualified due to his intention to eat it after its designated time [*piggul*]. And **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is **disqualified.** The Gemara explains: **Ḥizkiyya says** that the offering is **valid because** on the evening preceding the third day, the peace offering has **not** yet **been removed** from its previous status and designated **for burning.** A peace offering is not burned until the third morning, as the verse states: "It shall be eaten the same day you offer it, and on the following day; and if anything remain until the third day, it shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 19:6). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** it is **disqualified, because it has been rejected from** being **eaten.**

Having established the basic timeline of *shelamim* — slaughtered and blood applied on Sunday, eaten Sunday and Monday — the Gemara turns to a related question: what is the status of the meat on Monday night? The *shelamim* is eaten for two days and the intervening night; it is burned only on Tuesday morning. During Monday night, it is neither eaten nor burned. What is the halachic status of that interstitial period?

The question is sharpened through the lens of *machshevet*: if a *kohen* slaughters the *shelamim* while intending to eat the meat on Monday night — *l'or shlishi*, the night before the third day — does that constitute a *piggul* intent? For a thought to constitute *piggul*, it must contemplate an act performed at an improper time. If Monday night is already outside the time of eating, planning to eat then would render the *korban* *piggul*. If Monday night is some intermediate zone — the time of eating has ended but the time of burning has not yet arrived — perhaps it is not yet fully "outside" and the *piggul* thought would be ineffective.

### Chizkiyah vs R. Yochanan

Two Amoraim disagree. Chizkiyah says the *korban* is *kasher*: *d'lo mta l'sreifah* — since Monday night has not yet reached the time of burning, the meat is not yet *notar*, and planning to eat it then does not constitute a *piggul* thought. Rabbi Yochanan says it is *pasul* — indeed *piggul* — because *d'ha idchei lei me'achilah*: the meat has already been excluded from the time of eating, and that suffices to render a thought of eating it at that time a *piggul* intent. The parallel dispute applies to one who actually eats the meat on Monday night: Chizkiyah says one is *patur* — it is not yet *notar* — while Rabbi Yochanan says one is *chayav*, because it is already *notar* once it has been excluded from the time of eating, regardless of when the burning is scheduled.

### Braita: Sunset Day 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹכֵל לְאוֹר שְׁלִישִׁי – חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: פָּטוּר, דְּלָא אִינְּתִיק לִשְׂרֵיפָה; רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, דְּהָא אִידְּחִי לֵיהּ מֵאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** These two *amora'im* have the same dispute concerning a similar matter: With regard to **one who eats** a peace offering **on the evening** preceding the **third** day, **Ḥizkiyya says:** He is **exempt** from *karet*, **because** the offering has **not** yet **been removed** from its previous status and designated **for burning.** And **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He is **liable** to receive *karet*, **because it has been rejected from** being **eaten.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קָדָשִׁים הַנֶּאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד – מְחַשְּׁבִין בְּדָמָן מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁקַע הַחַמָּה, וּבִבְשָׂרָן וּבְאֵימוּרֵיהֶן מִשֶּׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר. קָדָשִׁים הַנֶּאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אַחַת – מְחַשְּׁבִין בְּדָמָן מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁקַע הַחַמָּה, וּבְאֵימוּרֵיהֶן מִשֶּׁיַּעֲלֶה עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, וּבִבְשָׂרָן מִשֶּׁתִּשְׁקַע הַחַמָּה שֶׁל שְׁנֵי יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **sacrificial** animals **that may be eaten for one day,** if one **intends to** present **their blood after sunset, or** if one intends **to** eat **their meat or to** sacrifice **their sacrificial portions after the first rays of dawn,** the offering is considered to be *piggul* and is disqualified. With regard to **sacrificial** animals **that may be eaten for two days and one night,** if one **intends to** present **their blood after sunset, or** if one intends **to** sacrifice **their sacrificial portions after the first rays of dawn, or** if one intends **to** eat **their meat after sunset** following the second **of** the **two days,** the offering is considered to be *piggul* and is disqualified.

The Gemara rules in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan from a *beraita*. The *beraita* distinguishes between *kodashim* eaten for one day and those eaten for two days. For one-day *kodashim* — such as a *chatat* — a *piggul* thought regarding the blood is effective if contemplated for after sunset; for the entrails and meat, the improper time begins at daybreak. For *shelamim* — two days and an intervening night — the *beraita* specifies: a *piggul* thought for the blood is effective after sunset (the same as one-day offerings), a *piggul* thought for the *imurim* is effective after the daybreak of Monday morning (since the *imurim* are burned only through Sunday night), and — explicitly — a *piggul* thought for the meat is effective *mishetishka hachamah shel shnei yamim* — after the sunset of the second day, i.e., Monday night. This confirms Rabbi Yochanan's position: the meat becomes *notar* at Monday's sunset, and a thought to eat it after that point constitutes *piggul*.

## No Eating Night 2

### Third Night Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל יְהוּ נֶאֱכָלִין לְאוֹר שְׁלִישִׁי? וְדִין הוּא – זְבָחִים נֶאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד, וּזְבָחִים נֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים; מָה זְבָחִים הַנֶּאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד – לַיְלָה אַחֲרֵיהֶן, אַף זְבָחִים הַנֶּאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים – לַיְלָה אַחֲרֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *baraita* on this topic. **The Sages taught:** One **might** have thought that peace offerings, which may be eaten for two days, **may** also **be eaten on the evening** preceding the **third** day. **And this** is the conclusion of **a logical inference:** Other **offerings,** e.g., thanks offerings, **are eaten for one day, and** peace **offerings are eaten for two days. Just as** with regard to **offerings that are eaten for one day,** the **night follows** the previous day, i.e., the offering may be eaten during the day and the subsequent night, **so too,** with regard to peace **offerings that are eaten for two days,** say that the **night follows** the day, and rule that they may be eaten on the night after the second day.

A further *beraita* addresses a more fundamental question: how do we know that *shelamim* may not be eaten on Monday night at all? The presumption throughout the preceding discussion has been that Monday night is outside the eating window, but this needs derivation.

### Kal Vachomer

The *beraita* raises a *kal vachomer*: by one-day *kodashim* such as a *chatat*, the following night is included in the eating period — that is, the night follows the day. If that is so for one-day offerings, surely the same should apply to two-day offerings: Sunday, Monday, and then Monday night as the night that follows the second day.

### Pasuk Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהַנּוֹתָר עַד יוֹם״ – בְּעוֹד יוֹם הוּא נֶאֱכָל, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל לְאוֹר שְׁלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** To counter this logic, **the verse states:** "And when you sacrifice a peace offering to God, you shall sacrifice it of your own will. It shall be eaten the same day you sacrifice it, and on the next day; **and if any remains until** the third **day,** it shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 19:5–6). This teaches that **it may be eaten while** it is still **daytime,** i.e., during the second day, **but it may not be eaten on the evening** preceding the **third** day.

The *kal vachomer* is refuted from the *pasuk*. The verse in Kedoshim (19:6) reads: *beyom zivchachem ye'achel umimacharat vehanotar ad yom hashlishi ba'esh yisaref*. By reading the phrase *vehanotar ad yom* as standing on its own — "and what remains, until the day" — the *drasha* teaches: *be'od yom hu ne'echal ve'eino ne'echal le'or shlishi* — it is eaten only throughout the day, and may not be eaten on Monday night. The verse cuts off the eating period at the end of Monday's daylight, generating the intermediate zone that gave rise to the dispute between Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan.

### Burning Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד? וְדִין הוּא – זְבָחִים נֶאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד, וּזְבָחִים נֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים; מָה זְבָחִים הַנֶּאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד – תֵּיכֶף לַאֲכִילָה שְׂרֵיפָה, אַף זְבָחִים הַנֶּאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים – תֵּיכֶף לַאֲכִילָה שְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If a peace offering may not be eaten beyond the second day, one **might** have thought that it should **be burned immediately** after the conclusion of the second day, **and this** too is the conclusion of **a logical inference:** Other **offerings are eaten for one day, and** peace **offerings are eaten for two days. Just as** with regard to **offerings that are eaten for one day, immediately** after the conclusion of their permitted time **for eating** should their **burning** commence, on the morning of the second day, **so too,** with regard to peace **offerings that are eaten for two days** say that **immediately** after the conclusion of their permitted time **for eating** should their **burning** commence, at night after the second day.

Having established that Monday night is not a time of eating, the *beraita* considers whether it might nevertheless be the time to burn the remaining meat — perhaps immediately when the eating period ends at Monday's sunset. A *kal vachomer* from the *chatat* is proposed: by one-day offerings, the *sreifah* follows immediately upon the end of the eating period (the meat is eaten through the night and burned the next morning). Perhaps here too *sreifah* should follow immediately after the eating period closes — meaning burning Monday night. This is refuted from the explicit *pasuk*: *bayom hashlishi ba'esh yisaref* — on the third day it shall be burned. *Bayom atah sorfo ve'ee atah sorfo balaylah* — you burn it during the day, not at night. The *sreifah* is reserved for Tuesday's daytime only, leaving Monday night as a space in which the meat is neither to be eaten nor burned — the very liminal period at the heart of the Chizkiyah–Rabbi Yochanan debate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי בָּאֵשׁ יִשָּׂרֵף״ – בַּיּוֹם אַתָּה שׂוֹרְפוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה שׂוֹרְפוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** To counter this logic, **the verse states:** "And if any remains of the flesh of the sacrifice **on the third day, it shall be burned with fire"** (Leviticus 7:17), meaning: **You** must **burn it during the day, but you may not burn it during the night.**

## Bechor/Ma'aser/Pesach

### Bechor Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר וְהַפֶּסַח – קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים – שְׁחִיטָתָן בְּכׇל מָקוֹם בָּעֲזָרָה; וְדָמָן טָעוּן מַתָּנָה אֶחָת, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן כְּנֶגֶד הַיְסוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The firstborn** offering, **the** animal **tithe** offering, **and the Paschal offering are offerings of lesser sanctity. Their slaughter is anywhere in the** Temple **courtyard, and their blood requires one placement, provided that** the priest **places it** so that the blood goes **on the base** of the altar.

The *Mishnah* now turns to the final category: *habechor vehama'aser vehapesach* — the firstborn animal, the animal tithe, and the Paschal offering. All three are *kodashim kalim*. Their slaughter is permitted anywhere within the *azarah* — *shechitatan bechol makom ba'azarah*. Most significantly, their blood requires only a single application anywhere on the *mizbe'ach* — *vedaman ta'un matanah achat* — provided it is applied where the *yesod* (the protruding base of the *mizbe'ach*) runs beneath. This is a notably simpler blood requirement than those of other offerings, which typically require either two applications amounting to four or a specified manner and location.

### Ma'aser Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁינָּה בַּאֲכִילָתָן. הַבְּכוֹר – נֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים, וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר – לְכׇל אָדָם; וְנֶאֱכָלִין בְּכׇל הָעִיר, בְּכׇל מַאֲכָל, לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד. הַפֶּסַח – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא עַד חֲצוֹת, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא לִמְנוּיָו, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא צָלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* **differs with regard to their consumption. The firstborn** offering **is eaten by the priests, and the** animal **tithe** offering is eaten **by any person,** i.e., any ritually pure Jew. **And they are eaten throughout the city** of Jerusalem, prepared **in any** manner of **food** preparation, **for two days and one night. The Paschal offering is eaten only at night, and it is eaten only until midnight, and it is eaten only by its registrants,** i.e., those who registered in advance to partake of the offering, **and it is eaten only roasted,** not prepared in any other manner.

When it comes to eating, the three offerings diverge. *Habechor ne'echal lakohanim* — the *bechor* is eaten exclusively by the *kohanim* and their families, throughout Jerusalem, in the manner of the *chazeh* and *shok* of the *shelamim*: it is as if the entire animal is a priestly portion. *Vehama'aser lechol adam* — the animal tithe, by contrast, is eaten by any person. None of it is given to the *kohanim*; the owner brings the *ma'aser behemah* and eats it himself in Jerusalem. Both are eaten for two days and the intervening night throughout the city.

### Pesach Rules

The Paschal offering is more restricted in every dimension. *Vehapesach eino ne'echal ela balaylah* — it may only be eaten during the night following the slaughter; it does not carry over to the next day. *Ve'eino ne'echal ela ad chatzot* — even within the night, it is limited to *chatzot* (midnight). Whether this *chatzot* is the standard rabbinic provision applying to all night-offerings (a protective fence against inadvertent delay until morning) or a special limitation rooted in the verse *vayehi bachatzi halailah* is debated; in context, the *beraita* appears to treat it as the standard rabbinic *chatzot*. *Ve'eino ne'echal ela limnuyav* — it may only be eaten by those who pre-registered for that particular offering. *Ve'eino ne'echal ela tzali* — and it may only be eaten roasted, not prepared in any other manner.

### Yose HaGalili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: ״חֶלְבּוֹ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״חֶלְבָּם״; ״דָּמוֹ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא ״דָּמָם״; לִימֵּד עַל בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר וּפֶסַח, שֶׁטְּעוּנִין מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that the blood of these offerings is placed, not poured, so that it goes on the base of the altar? **Rav Ḥisda says** that **it is Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says:** The verse states: "But the firstborn of an ox, or the firstborn of a sheep, or the firstborn of a goat, you shall not redeem; they are sacred; you shall sprinkle their blood upon the altar and you shall burn their fat for an offering made by fire, for a pleasing aroma to the Lord" (Numbers 18:17). **It is not stated: Its fat, but** rather: **"Their fat."** Similarly, **it is not stated: Its blood, but** rather: **"Their blood."** This **teaches with regard to a firstborn** offering, which is mentioned explicitly in the verse, **and** an animal **tithe** offering, **and a Paschal offering,** which are similar in their sanctity to a firstborn offering, **that they** all **require placement of their blood and** the burning of their **sacrificial portions on the altar.**

The Gemara asks: *man tanna?* — who is the *tanna* who rules that all three of these offerings require only a single blood application? Rav Chisda answers: it is Rabbi Yose HaGalili. The derivation works as follows. By *bechor*, the Torah writes: *ve'et damam tizrok al hamizbe'ach ve'et chelbam taktir* — throw their blood on the altar and burn their fat. The word *damam* — "their blood" — is plural, not "its blood" (*damo*); similarly, *chelbam* — "their fat" — rather than *chelbo*. Rabbi Yose HaGalili reads these plurals as encompassing *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *pesach* together: *limed al habechor uma'aser vapesach shete'unin matan damim ve'imurim legabei hamizbe'ach* — they all require blood application and burning of the *imurim* on the *mizbe'ach*. Since the Torah provides no further specification, a single application (*matanah achat*) suffices.

A striking observation: by *korban Pesach*, the Torah never explicitly states what is to be done with the blood at the Temple. This is particularly noteworthy given the nature of the offering. The *korban Pesach* is fundamentally oriented toward eating — it is referred to as *korban Hashem* but never as *korban laHashem*, and the phrase *lerei'ach nicho'ach* never appears in connection with it. In Egypt, the blood was placed on the doorposts rather than offered on an altar; to some degree, the offering is less about presenting something to God and more about sanctifying the meat for the purposes of the commanded meal. It is therefore quite significant that the *halachah* of what to do with the blood in the Temple is learned not from any explicit verse about *pesach* but by *gezerah shavah* from *bechor*.

### Yesod Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּנֶגֶד הַיְסוֹד מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אָתְיָא ״זְרִיקָה״–״זְרִיקָה״ מֵעוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that their blood must be placed so that it goes **on the base** of the altar? **Rabbi Eliezer says:** This is **derived** by way of a verbal analogy, with the meaning of the word **"sprinkling"** stated here derived **from** the meaning of the word **"sprinkling"** stated with regard to **a burnt offering.**

How do we know that the single blood application must be *keneged hayesod* — in a location where the base of the *mizbe'ach* protrudes beneath? The Torah's verse simply says *tizrok al hamizbe'ach* — throw it on the altar — without specification. Rabbi Eliezer derives the *yesod* requirement via a *gezerah shavah*: *atya zerika zerika me'olah* — the same word *zerika* appears by the *olah*, and by the *olah* we know the blood must be applied *el yesod mizbe'ach ha'olah*. But how do we know that the *yesod* requirement for the *olah* does not also import the requirement of two applications that amount to four (*shetei matanot she'hein arba*)? If the *gezerah shavah* connects these offerings to the *olah*, perhaps it should bring along the *olah*'s fuller blood-application requirement.

### Abiay: One Matanah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְמָה לִי לְמִכְתַּב ״סָבִיב״ בְּעוֹלָה, ״סָבִיב״ בְּחַטָּאת? הָווּ שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִין הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Why do I** need the Torah **to write:** "And Aaron's sons, the priests, shall present the blood, and sprinkle the blood **around** against the altar" (Leviticus 1:5), **with regard to a burnt offering,** to teach that the blood must be placed on all four sides of the altar, in addition to writing: "And Moses took the blood, and put it upon the corners of the altar **around** with his finger" (Leviticus 8:15), **with regard to a sin offering? They are two verses that come as one,** i.e., they teach the same matter; **and any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common aspect to apply to other cases. Therefore, a firstborn offering, an animal tithe offering, and a Paschal offering do not require two placements that are four.

Abaye resolves this. The Torah explicitly says *saviv* — "all around" — by the *olah* and also by the *chatat*. The rule of *shenei ketuvim haba'im ke'echad ein melamdin* — when the same teaching appears in two separate verses, it cannot serve as a basis for applying the rule elsewhere — limits *saviv* to those cases where it is explicitly stated. Since it is explicitly stated by *olah* and *chatat*, those two cases cannot be used as a model to import *saviv* into cases where the Torah was silent. Accordingly, the *gezerah shavah* from *olah* establishes only the *yesod* requirement, not the requirement of *saviv*.

### Hermeneutics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין מְלַמְּדִין, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מְלַמְּדִין מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? הָוֵי אָשָׁם שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה וַדַּאי אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that two verses that come as one **do not teach** their common aspect with regard to other cases. **But according to the one who says** that two verses that come as one do **teach** their common aspect with regard to other cases, **what is there to say?** The Gemara answers: Together with the verse stated with regard to **a guilt offering, there are three** verses, as it is written: "And its blood shall be sprinkled around the altar" (Leviticus 7:2), **and three** verses which come as one **certainly do not teach** their common aspect with regard to other cases.

For those who hold that *shenei ketuvim haba'im ke'echad* do in fact teach elsewhere (the minority position), the Gemara notes that *saviv* appears in three cases — *olah*, *chatat*, and *asham* — and three cases (*sheloshah vadai ein melamdin*) certainly cannot serve as a basis for applying the rule elsewhere.

Rashi adds an observation that enriches the hermeneutic picture. Normally, the principle of *shenei ketuvim haba'im ke'echad* means that when a rule appears in two (or more) places, you cannot extend it further by analogy. Here, however, the principle is working in the opposite direction: the fact that *saviv* is explicitly stated in those cases restricts the scope of the *gezerah shavah* of *zerika zerika*. Even though the *gezerah shavah* would otherwise connect *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *pesach* to the *olah* and might seem to import all the characteristics of *olah*'s blood-service, the explicit limitation of *saviv* to *olah*, *chatat*, and *asham* signals that this feature is deliberately confined to those three. The *gezerah shavah* therefore carries over only the *yesod* requirement — a single application in a location where the base protrudes — and nothing more. The Torah's silence regarding *pesach* and *ma'aser* is thus deliberate, and the halachic result is both elegant and precisely calibrated.
