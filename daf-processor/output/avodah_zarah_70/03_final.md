# Avodah Zarah 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Crack in Door

### Locked Door Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בֵּיתָא דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב בֵּיהּ חַמְרָא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, עָל גּוֹי אַחְדַּהּ לְדַשָּׁא בְּאַפֵּיהּ, וַהֲוָה בִּיזְעָא בְּדַשָּׁא, אִישְׁתְּכַח גּוֹי דְּקָאֵי בֵּינֵי דַּנֵּי. אֲמַר רָבָא: כֹּל דְּלַהֲדֵי בִּיזְעָא — שְׁרֵי, דְּהַאי גִּיסָא וְהַאי גִּיסָא — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain house where Jews' wine was stored. A gentile entered** the house, and **he locked the door before** the Jew, **but there was a crack in the door,** and **the gentile was found standing between the barrels. Rava said: All** the barrels **that were opposite the crack** through which the gentile could be seen are **permitted,** because he would have been wary about being seen tampering with them. Barrels **on this side and that side** of the crack, where the gentile could not be seen, are **forbidden,** as perhaps the gentile used them for a libation.

The daf opens with a case that tests the limits of supervision over Jewish wine. A house contained wine belonging to a Jew. A non-Jew entered, locked the door — closing it in the Jew's face — and was subsequently found standing among the barrels when the Jew gained entry. The locking of the door is significant: one of the standard concerns about leaving a non-Jew unattended in a store is precisely that he might lock the door, thereby gaining unsupervised access to the wine. Here, he has done exactly that.

Rava rules: *kol delehadi biza shari* — any barrel situated directly opposite the crack in the door is permitted. The barrels on either side of that crack, where there was no line of sight from outside, are forbidden.

### Line of Sight

The operative principle is that the non-Jew's awareness that he might be observed is what restrains him from handling the wine. Barrels positioned opposite the crack could, in principle, be seen from outside. Whether or not anyone was actually looking through the crack at the moment is beside the point; the non-Jew's knowledge that the crack existed and that those barrels were visible is sufficient to establish that he would have refrained from touching them. Barrels beyond the line of sight enjoy no such protection.

One might ask what the ruling would be had the non-Jew not been found among the barrels at all — had he been found, say, elsewhere in the house upon entry. It is not entirely clear how much his presence *among* the barrels is the generating concern here, as opposed to the mere fact that he locked the door. What the ruling establishes, in any case, is that even given the locked door and the discovery among the barrels, proximity to the crack rehabilitates those particular barrels.

## Housemates

### Upper/Lower Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא חַמְרָא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב בְּבֵיתָא, דַּהֲוָה דָּיַיר יִשְׂרָאֵל בָּעֶלְיוֹנָה וְגוֹי בַּתַּחְתּוֹנָה. שְׁמַעוּ קָל תִּיגְרָא, נָפְקִי, קְדֵים אֲתָא גּוֹי אַחְדַּהּ לְדַשָּׁא בְּאַפֵּיהּ. אֲמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי, מֵימָר אָמַר: כִּי הֵיכִי דִּקְדֵים אֲתַאי אֲנָא, קְדֵים וַאֲתָא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיָתֵיב בָּעֶלְיוֹנָה וְקָא חָזֵי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain Jew's wine that was stored in** the lower story of **a house,** in **which the Jew was living in the upper** story **and a gentile in the lower** story, and the wine could be supervised from the upper story. One day the residents **heard a sound of quarreling** and **went outside. The gentile came** back in **first** and **locked the door before** the Jew. **Rava said: The wine** is **permitted,** because the gentile presumably **said** to himself: **Just as I came** back in **early,** perhaps my neighbor the **Jew came** back in **early and is sitting in the upper** story **and watching me,** and therefore he would not use the wine for a libation.

The Gemara continues with a related scenario. A Jew and a non-Jew shared a house: the Jew resided in the upper story, the non-Jew in the lower, with the wine — belonging to the Jew — stored in the lower story. Rashi explains that the arrangement itself constituted effective supervision: the Jew could look down at any moment, perhaps from a balcony or an interior overlook, and the non-Jew knew this. The possibility of being observed at any time kept the wine safe.

### Fighting Interruption

The normal arrangement is then disrupted. Both the Jew and the non-Jew heard the sound of a fight outside (*shamu kol tigra*) and ran out to see what was happening. When things settled, the non-Jew returned first and locked the door — he is now alone with the wine, behind a locked door, and the Jew is outside.

### Fear of Return

Rava rules that the wine is still permitted. The reasoning: the non-Jew will say to himself, *ki heichi dekadim ata'i ana, kadim ve'ata Yisrael* — just as I rushed back first, the Jew may well have rushed back too and is already sitting upstairs watching me. The non-Jew cannot be certain that his quick return gave him any exclusive advantage. He must assume, plausibly enough, that the Jew may be back in the upper story and observing.

This parallels the logic of the crack in the door: it is not actual observation that matters, but the reasonable possibility of it. If the non-Jew had good reason to think the Jew might be present and watching, the wine is protected. One might wonder whether the result would differ if the circumstances made it obvious to the non-Jew that the Jew was nowhere near — for instance, had the Jew visibly gone to summon the authorities. Rava's ruling does not address that scenario; it addresses the case as given, where the non-Jew simply cannot be certain.

## Nitpas K'Ganav

### Inn Barrels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אוּשְׁפִּיזָא דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב בֵּיהּ חַמְרָא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, אִישְׁתְּכַח גּוֹי דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב בֵּי דַנֵּי. אֲמַר רָבָא: אִם נִתְפָּס עָלָיו כְּגַנָּב — שְׁרֵי, וְאִי לָא — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain inn [*ushpiza*] where a Jew's wine was stored,** and **a gentile was found sitting among the barrels. Rava said: If he was caught as a thief,** i.e., if the gentile seemed startled and did not have a good explanation for being there, the wine is **permitted,** as the gentile was presumably afraid about being caught and would not have used it for a libation. **But if not,** the wine is **forbidden.**

The Gemara presents a third scenario. At an inn containing Jewish wine, a non-Jew is discovered among the barrels. There is no locked door, no interrupted arrangement — simply a non-Jew found in the middle of the barrels. Rava issues a conditional ruling.

### Consequences Test

*Im nitpas alav k'ganav shari* — if, had he been discovered mishandling the wine, he would have faced consequences, the wine is permitted. The concern that he would be caught and punished is what would have restrained him. An earlier comment from Rashi suggests that a person in a position of power or social protection — someone who knows that people would look the other way even if he were caught — falls into a different category, as does someone who could offer a plausible excuse for what he was doing.

### No Consequences

If, however, being discovered handling the wine would carry no consequences — whether because he has sufficient social cover, a ready excuse, or some other reason — the wine is forbidden. In that case, the deterrent does not operate, and one must assume he did handle the wine.

This is, as the Gemara frames it, a clarification and elaboration of the principle underlying the *mishnah*: the cases in the *mishnah* where wine left unattended remains permitted are precisely cases where the non-Jew fears the Jew might return unexpectedly. *Nitpas alav k'ganav* is the articulation of that fear: what holds him back is the prospect of being caught and facing consequences.

## Excuse vs Consequences

### House Barrels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בֵּיתָא דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב בֵּיהּ חַמְרָא, אִישְׁתְּכַח גּוֹי דַּהֲוָה קָאֵים בֵּי דַנֵּי. אֲמַר רָבָא: אִי אִית לֵיהּ לְאִישְׁתְּמוֹטֵי — חַמְרָא אֲסִיר, וְאִי לָא — חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי. מֵיתִיבִי: נִנְעַל הַפּוּנְדָּק, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״שְׁמוֹר״ — אָסוּר. מַאי לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ לְאִישְׁתְּמוֹטֵי? לָא, בִּדְאִית לֵיהּ לְאִישְׁתְּמוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain house where wine was stored. A gentile was found standing among the barrels. Rava said: If he has** a way **to excuse** his entrance to where the wine was stored, **the wine** is **forbidden, but if not, the wine** is **permitted.** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this ruling from a *baraita*: If **an inn was locked** and a gentile was inside, **or** if the Jew **said to** the gentile: **Safeguard** my wine, the wine is **forbidden. What, is it not** forbidden **even if** the gentile **does not have** a way **to excuse** his entrance? The Gemara answers: **No,** the *baraita* is referring to a situation **where he does have** a way **to excuse** his entrance; otherwise the wine is permitted.

The Gemara presents a new case: a house — not an inn — containing wine, and a non-Jew found among the barrels. Rava rules conditionally.

### Has Excuse

*Ei it lei l'ishtamutei, chamra assur* — if he has an excuse for why he is in this room at all, the wine is forbidden.

### No Excuse

*Ve'ei lo, chamra shari* — if he has no excuse for being there, the wine is permitted.

### Rashi Explains

This seems, at first, precisely backwards. Surely a non-Jew found among the barrels without any excuse should be the *more* suspicious case? Rashi's explanation clarifies the distinction. If the non-Jew has an excuse for being in the room — some legitimate or semi-legitimate reason he can offer for his presence — then he feels at ease. He does not feel rushed, he does not feel exposed, and he therefore has the leisure to linger and handle the wine. It is precisely his sense of security that makes the situation more dangerous. If he has no excuse at all for being in the room, he knows he is entirely out of place. He is anxious, he feels the pressure of potential discovery at any moment, and that anxiety prevents him from tarrying long enough to handle the wine.

The Gemara confirms this reading by testing it against a related teaching: if a store was locked or a non-Jew was told to stand guard, the wine inside is forbidden. The apparent challenge is that this is a case where the non-Jew has no excuse for being inside — and yet it is forbidden. The Gemara answers: *lo, bede'it lei l'ishtamutei* — no, it is precisely a case where he *does* have an excuse. He has been stationed there with a role; he has reason to believe he is permitted to be present. It is that legitimizing context that creates the concern.

### Right to Be

The deeper structural distinction between *nitpas alav k'ganav* and *aishtemutei* now comes into relief. *Nitpas alav k'ganav* applies in cases where the non-Jew has a right to be in the location — he is at an inn, he shares a building, he is in a store where he belongs. The question in those cases is entirely about whether he would face consequences for reaching into the wine itself. As long as he would face consequences for that act, everything is fine. *Aishtemutei* applies in cases where he has no right to be in the room at all. There, the question shifts to his overall willingness to be in a compromised position. If he is willing to be in a room where he has no business being — and he has no excuse to offer — that very brazenness is a sign of anxiety and exposure, and paradoxically the wine is safer. If, however, he has a cover story that explains his presence, he has already demonstrated a readiness to enter compromised territory with a plan, and the wine is at risk.

## Companions

### Mincha Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי דַּהֲווֹ יָתְבִי וְקָא שָׁתוּ חַמְרָא, שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל קָל צַלּוֹיֵי בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא, קָם וַאֲזַל. אֲמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי, מֵימָר אָמַר: הַשְׁתָּא מִדְּכַר לֵיהּ לְחַמְרֵיהּ וְהָדַר אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain Jew and** a certain **gentile who were sitting and drinking wine.** The **Jew heard the sound of praying at the synagogue. He got up and went** to pray. **Rava said: The wine** is **permitted,** because the gentile presumably **said** to himself: Any moment **now he will remember his wine and come back.**

The Gemara turns to a series of cases involving a Jew and a non-Jew who are companions, where the Jew leaves wine unattended — and the question is whether the non-Jew believes the Jew will return. In the first case, the non-Jew is presumed to reason that his Jewish companion will remember he left wine behind and come back to check on it.

### Mincha Service

A Jew and a non-Jew are sitting and drinking wine together. The Jew hears the congregation beginning to pray in the synagogue — it is *mincha* time — and he gets up and leaves, leaving his wine at the table with the non-Jew. Rava rules that the wine is permissible. The reasoning: *meimar amar, hashta midkar lehu lechamra ve-hadar atei* — the non-Jew says to himself, my companion is not so scrupulous that he will forget about his wine; he will remember it is sitting here and he will duck out of *shemoneh esreh* to come back and check on me. The Jew's departure to *mincha* is not analogous to a declared absence with a known duration. It is an interruption, and the non-Jew has reason to think it will be a short one.

### Shabbat Preparation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי דַּהֲווֹ יָתְבִי בְּאַרְבָּא, שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל קָל שִׁיפּוּרֵי דְּבֵי שִׁימְשֵׁי, נְפַק וַאֲזַל. אֲמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי, מֵימָר אָמַר: הַשְׁתָּא מִדְּכַר לֵיהּ לְחַמְרֵיהּ וְהָדַר אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain Jew and** a certain **gentile who were sitting on a ship.** The **Jew heard the sound of the shofar of twilight** indicating the beginning of Shabbat. **He disembarked and went** into town to spend Shabbat there. **Rava said: The wine** is **permitted,** because the gentile presumably **said** to himself: Any moment **now he will remember his wine and come back.**

A Jew and a non-Jew are on a boat together. The Jew hears the shofar blast at evening time — Rashi notes elsewhere that the sounding of the shofar at nightfall signaled the onset of *Shabbat*, as is still the practice in Jerusalem — and he disembarks to prepare for *Shabbat*. Now the non-Jew might well feel safe: the Jew has to prepare his *Shabbat* meal, attend synagogue, observe the day — surely he will not return to the boat on *Shabbat*. Rava again rules the wine is permissible.

### Shabbat Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מִשּׁוּם שַׁבְּתָא, הָאָמַר רָבָא: אֲמַר לִי אִיסּוּר גִּיּוֹרָא, כִּי הֲוֵינַן בְּאַרְמָיוּתַן אָמְרִינַן: יְהוּדָאֵי לָא מְנַטְּרִי שַׁבְּתָא, דְּאִי מְנַטְּרִי שַׁבְּתָא כַּמָּה כִּיסֵי קָא מִשְׁתַּכְחִי בְּשׁוּקָא. וְלָא יָדַעְנָא דִּסְבִירָא לַן כְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: הַמּוֹצֵא כִּיס בְּשַׁבָּת מוֹלִיכוֹ פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** And if one might object that the gentile is presumably not concerned **because** he knows that the Jew will not return until the end of **Shabbat, didn't Rava say: Issur the Convert told me: When we were** still **gentiles,** before converting, **we** used to **say: Jews do not** actually **observe Shabbat, as, if they observe Shabbat, how many wallets would be found in the marketplace** that the Jews could not take on Shabbat? **And I did not know that we maintain** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: One who finds a wallet on Shabbat may carry it** in increments of **less than four cubits.** Evidently, gentiles assume that a Jew would violate Shabbat for monetary gain.

Why? Rava cites a remarkable teaching in the name of Issur Giyora, a convert who shared with Rava what non-Jews used to say about Jews when he was still among them: *yudai lo mantri Shabta* — Jews do not really observe *Shabbat*. The basis for this perception: *kama kissay mishtakchay b'shuka* — if they really kept *Shabbat*, you would find wallets lying all over the marketplace, because Jews trade in the marketplace right up until the moment *Shabbat* begins, and if they were genuinely committed to the day, they would simply drop whatever they were carrying the instant darkness fell. Since they do not, non-Jews concluded that Jewish *Shabbat* observance dissolves when money is at stake.

### Wallet Precedent

The Gemara acknowledges the uncomfortable accuracy lurking in this perception. The rabbis were aware that Jews would be tempted to violate *Shabbat* to protect their money, and so they instituted a dispensation: one may carry a wallet home on *Shabbat* by taking steps of less than four *amos* at a time, never technically performing a full *carrying* act in the public domain, following the ruling of Rabbi Yitzchak. The very existence of this dispensation is a concession to the reality that anxiety about money creates pressure on *Shabbat* observance. The non-Jewish perception was not unfounded.

In the context of the boat case, this means the non-Jew on the boat reasons: my Jewish companion left to keep *Shabbat*, but he also left all his wine here with me. He knows that Jews, when it comes to protecting their assets, are not inclined to let *Shabbat* stand in the way. So he expects the Jew to come back and check on his wine even on *Shabbat*. That ongoing apprehension keeps the non-Jew from handling the wine, and therefore it remains permitted.

## Thieves & Troops

### Lion Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אַרְיָא דַּהֲוָה נָהֵים בְּמַעְצַרְתָּא, שְׁמַע גּוֹי, טְשָׁא בֵּינֵי דַּנֵּי. אָמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי, מֵימָר אָמַר: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּטָשֵׁינָא אֲנָא, אִיטְשָׁא נָמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲחוֹרַיי וְקָא חָזֵי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain lion who roared in a winepress. A gentile heard** the roar and was frightened, and **he hid among the barrels** of wine. **Rava said: The wine** is **permitted,** because the gentile presumably **said** to himself: **Just as I am hiding, a Jew** might **also** be **hiding behind me and see me.**

The Gemara presents a sequence of cases in which non-Jews are found among wine barrels under unusual circumstances. In the first, a lion was roaring in the winepress. A non-Jew, hearing the lion, fled and hid among the barrels for protection. Should one be concerned that, while hiding there, he drank some wine or performed some act of *nisuch*?

### Mutual Fear

Rava rules the wine is permitted. *Meimar amar ki heichi d'tashina ana, itshu nami Yisraeli achorei v'chazu li* — the non-Jew reasons: the same way I came running to hide among the barrels, so did the Jews, and they are watching me right now. The same emergency that drove the non-Jew to the barrels would equally have driven the Jewish owners there, and the non-Jew cannot be confident he is alone.

### Thieves Pumbedita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ גַּנָּבֵי דְּסָלְקִי לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, וּפְתַחוּ חָבְיָתָא טוּבָא. אֲמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? רוּבָּא גַּנָּבֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל נִינְהוּ. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא, וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **certain thieves who came to Pumbedita and opened many barrels** of wine. **Rava said: The wine** is **permitted. What is the reason? Most of the thieves** in Pumbedita **are Jews,** and the *halakha* follows the majority, and therefore the wine is not rendered forbidden. **There was** a similar **incident in Neharde'a, and Shmuel said: The wine** is **permitted.**

A gang of thieves (*ganavim*) came to Pumbedita and broke open many barrels of wine. Rava rules the wine is permitted.

### Thieves Jewish

Why? *Ruba ganvay Yisrael ninhu* — the majority of thieves in this context are Jewish. A note clarifies what Rava means: he is speaking of Mechuza, his own city, where the population was predominantly Jewish. In Mechuza, the majority of residents — and therefore the majority of thieves — were Jews. One should not assume that thieves who broke into Jewish wine storage were non-Jews; more likely they were Jews, in which case there is no concern of *yayin nesech*. Rava, who was known to have a complicated relationship with the people of Mechuza, was evidently not above characterizing his city's majority Jewish population as the primary source of local theft.

### Nehardea Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאָמַר: סְפֵק בִּיאָה טָהוֹר. דִּתְנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לְבִקְעָה בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְטוּמְאָה בְּשָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית, וְאָמַר: הָלַכְתִּי בַּמָּקוֹם הַלָּז, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם נִכְנַסְתִּי לְאוֹתָהּ שָׂדֶה אִם לֹא נִכְנַסְתִּי, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: סְפֵק בִּיאָה — טָהוֹר, סְפֵק מַגָּע — טָמֵא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this? Perhaps it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says** with regard to cases of uncertainty concerning ritual purity that if the **uncertainty** is with regard to a person's **entry** into a certain place, he is deemed **pure.** This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 6:5): With regard to **one who enters into a valley during the rainy season,** i.e., winter, when people generally do not enter this area, **and** there was **ritual impurity in such and such a field, and** he **said:** I know **I walked to that place,** i.e., I walked in the valley, **but I do not know whether I entered that field** where the ritual impurity was **or whether I did not** enter, **Rabbi Eliezer says:** In a case of **uncertainty** with regard to **entry,** i.e., it is uncertain whether he entered the area where the ritual impurity is located, he is **ritually pure.** But if he certainly entered the area where the ritual impurity is located and the **uncertainty** pertains to **contact** with the source of ritual impurity, he is **ritually impure.** Apparently, the ruling of Shmuel, that in a case where it is uncertain whether gentile thieves entered the house at all the wine is permitted, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer.

The Gemara then records a case in Nehardea, where the population was not majority Jewish. Thieves opened barrels of wine, and Shmuel ruled the wine permitted. This is more striking, since in Nehardea one cannot simply appeal to a majority of Jewish thieves.

### Double Doubt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא דְּפָתְחִי לְשׁוּם מָמוֹנָא, הָוֵה לֵיהּ סְפֵק סְפֵיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No,** it **is different there,** with regard to the wine barrels. **Since there are** thieves **who open** barrels **for the sake of** perhaps finding **money** in them and are not interested in the wine, **it is** a case of **compound uncertainty,** as it is uncertain whether the thieves were gentiles or Jews, and even if they were gentiles, it is uncertain whether or not they touched the wine. In a case of compound uncertainty, everyone agrees that the wine is not forbidden.

The Gemara asks whether Shmuel is following Rabbi Eliezer, who holds *safek bi'ah tahor* — that a doubtful entry into a domain where *tumah* is present does not render one *tamei*. The parallel is drawn from the teaching about one who entered a field during the rainy season (*yemot ha-geshamim*), when fields are considered *reshut ha-yachid* because their owners do not want them trampled, and a body is buried in one of the fields. If the person does not even know which field he entered, Rabbi Eliezer holds he is *tahor*, since there is a doubt about the entry itself (*safek bi'ah*), as distinct from a doubt about contact (*safek maga*) — the latter being *tamei* in a private domain.

The Gemara declines to rest the ruling on Rabbi Eliezer alone. Instead, it invokes a *sefek sefeka* — a double doubt. Even granting that the thieves were non-Jewish, there are two independent reasons to think they did not handle the wine: first, perhaps they did not touch it even after opening the barrel; and second — which Rashi identifies as the operative structure — their motivation was not wine but money. Thieves who systematically break open barrel after barrel are looking for hidden cash, not wine. Seeing wine, they likely moved on. Because the non-Jew's purpose was to search for valuables rather than to consume or handle the wine, there is a second layer of doubt even once his presence is established. The Gemara notes that Tosafot connects this to the structure of *safek bi'ah* itself: Rabbi Eliezer's ruling is also, in effect, a double doubt — whether one entered the right field at all, and whether one then walked over the body.

## Froth Case

### Froth Discovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא רְבִיתָא דְּאִישְׁתְּכַח דַּהֲוָת בֵּי דַנֵּי, וַהֲוָת נְקִיטָא אוּפְיָא בִּידַהּ, אָמַר רָבָא: חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי, אֵימַר מִגַּבַּהּ דְּחָבִיתָא שְׁקַלְתֵּיהּ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיכָּא תּוּ, אֵימַר אִתְרְמוֹיֵי אִתְרְמִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain** gentile **girl who was found among** wine **barrels and she was holding** wine **froth in her hand. Rava said: The wine** is **permitted,** as it is reasonable to **say** that **she took it from the outside of the barrel** and not from inside the barrel. **And even if there is no more** of the froth on the outside of the barrel, it is reasonable to **say** that **she happened upon** the froth while it was still there, even though it is no longer there.

A non-Jewish girl was found among the wine barrels with wine froth on her hand. The concern: did she insert her hand into a barrel, producing the froth as evidence of contact with the wine?

### Outside Froth

Rava rules the wine is permitted. *Eimor migaba de'chavita shaklata* — assume she took the froth from the outside of the barrel, not from inside. Even if no froth remains visible on the exterior of the barrel — which might seem to eliminate that explanation — Rava adds: *eimor isrumei isramila* — assume it simply dried or disappeared by chance, and what was on her hand is all that remained. This reasoning is, as the discussion acknowledges, somewhat generous. The assumption that the froth came from the exterior rather than the interior, especially when none is visible on the exterior, does require a measure of interpretive charity.

### Child Awareness

Rashi adds a further consideration: she is a young girl. There was a prior discussion in the *masechet* about children who are not yet aware of the context of *avodah zarah*. For such a child, even actual contact with wine does not create a concern of *yayin nesech*, since the prohibition is predicated on the act being meaningful within an idolatrous framework — which a young child has not yet internalized.

## Thieves & Troops…

### Troop Barrels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא פּוּלְמוּסָא דִּסְלֵיק לִנְהַרְדְּעָא, פְּתַחוּ חָבְיָתָא טוּבָא. כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: עוֹבָדָא הֲוָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וּשְׁרָא. וְלָא יָדַעְנָא, אִי מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּאָמַר: סְפֵק בִּיאָה טָהוֹר, אִי מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר: רוּבָּא דְּאָזְלִי בַּהֲדֵי פּוּלְמוּסָא יִשְׂרָאֵל נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain army [*pulmusa*] that entered Neharde'a** and **opened many barrels** of wine. **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: There was** a similar **incident** that was brought **before Rabbi Elazar, and he deemed** the wine **permitted. But I do not know whether** he permitted it **because he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, who says:** Concerning **uncertainty** with regard to **entry,** the person or item is **ritually pure,** or **whether** he permitted it **because he maintains** that **most** of those **who went with** that **army were Jews,** i.e., that although it was a gentile army, the ancillaries were mostly Jews.

A *polmusa* — a troop, or as Rashi explains, perhaps referring to a military commander — came to Nehardea and opened many barrels. Rav Dimi reported that a similar case came before Rabbi Elazar, who permitted the wine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַאי סְפֵק בִּיאָה? סְפֵק מַגָּע הוּא! כֵּיוָן דְּמִפַּתְחִי טוּבָא, אֵימָא אַדַּעְתָּא דְּמָמוֹנָא פְּתַחוּ, וְכִסְפֵק בִּיאָה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** if he holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, why did he permit the wine? **Is this** a case of **uncertainty** with regard to **entry?** It is clear that the ancillaries came and opened the barrels, so **it is** a case of **uncertainty** with regard to **contact,** i.e., whether they touched the wine or not, and Rabbi Eliezer agrees that such a case is treated stringently. The Gemara answers: **Since they opened many** barrels, it is reasonable to **say** that **they opened** the barrels only **with the intention of** finding **money** and had no interest in the wine itself. **And** therefore it is **similar to** a case of **uncertainty** with regard to **entry.**

The Gemara considers two possible grounds for the ruling: perhaps Rabbi Elazar holds like Rabbi Eliezer (*safek bi'ah tahor*), or perhaps he holds that the majority of those accompanying such troops were Jewish, making it more likely that the barrels were opened by Jews. The Gemara then tests this: if the majority were not Jewish and the barrels were nonetheless opened, is that not a *safek maga* — a straightforward single doubt about contact — rather than a *safek bi'ah*?

The answer lies in the number of barrels opened. If only one or two had been opened, one might assume the troops were simply thirsty and took what they needed, making the question one of contact. But because they opened many barrels (*miftachi tuva*), it becomes apparent they were searching for money. That purpose — looking for hidden cash rather than wanting the wine — reintroduces the second layer of doubt even when the soldiers are presumed non-Jewish. They saw wine, but wine was not what they came for. Therefore, even though non-Jews definitively opened the barrels, one cannot assume they handled the wine itself, and the double doubt structure applies: the wine is permitted.

This clarifies the earlier discussion about the Pumbedita case. The critical variable is the searchers' motivation. When motivation is ambiguous — wine or money — that ambiguity produces the second doubt necessary to permit the wine. When thieves or troops open many barrels systematically, that pattern of behavior is itself evidence that money, not wine, was the goal.

## Tumah Parallels

### Key Guardian

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא מָסוֹבִיתָא דִּמְסַרָה לַהּ אִיקְּלִידָא (מַפְתְּחָה) לְגוֹיָה, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עוֹבָדָא הֲוָה בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, וַאֲמַרוּ: לֹא מָסְרָה לָהּ אֶלָּא שְׁמִירַת מַפְתֵּחַ בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** There was an incident involving **a certain female owner of a wine shop who transferred the key [*iklida*]** to the door of her wine shop **to a gentile woman. Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that **Rabbi Elazar said: There was** a similar **incident** that was brought before the Sages in **the study hall, and they said: She transferred to her** the responsibility for **safeguarding the key alone** but did not authorize her to enter the tavern, so there is no concern that she entered there.

The Gemara presents a case with a different structure. A female wine seller (*musavisa*, as Rashi explains it) gave the key to her wine cabinet to a non-Jew. Having handed him the key, must one now be concerned that he accessed the wine? Rav Yitzchak citing Rav Elazar reported that this very case was discussed in the *beit midrash*, and the ruling was: *lo masrah ella shemirat hamafteach bilvad* — she handed him the job of watching the key, not the right to access the cabinet. His mandate was custodial, not accessory. Since he was not authorized to enter the cabinet and he would face consequences for doing so without authorization — a clear instance of *nitpas alav k'ganav* — the wine is permitted.

### Tahara Keys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: הַמּוֹסֵר מַפְתְּחוֹת לְעַם הָאָרֶץ — טׇהֳרוֹתָיו טְהוֹרוֹת, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא מָסַר לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׁמִירַת מַפְתֵּחַ בִּלְבָד. הַשְׁתָּה טׇהֳרוֹתָיו טְהוֹרוֹת, יֵין נֶסֶךְ מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We learn** this *halakha* in a mishna **as well** (*Teharot* 7:1): In the case of **one who transferred keys to one who is unreliable with regard to ritual impurity [*am ha'aretz*],** even though contact with an *am ha'aretz* renders pure items impure, **his pure items are pure, because he transferred to** the *am ha'aretz* the responsibility for **safeguarding the key alone** and did not authorize him to enter. **Now** that the mishna has determined that **his pure items are pure, is it necessary** to state this principle with regard to the *halakhot* of **wine** used for **a libation?**

Abaye notes that this ruling has a precise parallel in the laws of *taharah*. A *mishnah* teaches: if one handed over keys to a storehouse containing *tahor* fruit to an *am ha'aretz*, the fruit remains *tahor* — *taharotav tehorot* — because the *am ha'aretz* was given custodianship of the key, not access to the fruit (*shemirat hamafteach bilvad*). The parallel is exact.

### Weights Compare

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דִּטְהָרוֹת אַלִּימִי מִיֵּין נֶסֶךְ? אִין, דְּאִיתְּמַר: חָצֵר שֶׁחִלְּקָהּ בִּמְסִיפָס, אָמַר רַב: טׇהֳרוֹתָיו טְמֵאוֹת, וּבְגוֹי אֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה יֵין נֶסֶךְ, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף טׇהֳרוֹתָיו טְהוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to Abaye's reasoning: **Is this to say that** the *halakhot* of **ritually pure items are** more **stringent than** those concerning **wine** used for **a libation?** The Gemara answers: **Indeed,** that is so. **As it was stated** that there was a dispute with regard to **a courtyard** whose owners **divided it** among themselves **with a low partition [*meseifas*]. Rav says:** If one's neighbor on the other side of the partition is an *am ha'aretz*, **one's pure items** that he leaves in the courtyard **are** rendered **impure, but in** the case of **a gentile** neighbor, this **does not render** his **wine** an idolatrous **libation. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: His pure items** remain **pure as well.** Evidently, Rav considers the *halakhot* of purity more stringent than those of wine used for a libation.

Abaye then draws a *kal va-chomer*: if this leniency applies in the domain of *tumah ve-taharah*, where we are more readily inclined to identify a problem, then *yein nesech* concerns — which Abaye treats as the less weighty category — certainly should not be more stringent. If guarding the key without the right of access is sufficient to protect *tahor* fruit, it is certainly sufficient to protect wine.

This comparative claim — that *tumah ve-taharah* is the weightier concern — is taken up explicitly by the Gemara as a substantive question.

### Adjacent Yards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַפְּנִימִית שֶׁל חָבֵר וְהַחִיצוֹנָה שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ, אוֹתוֹ חָבֵר שׁוֹטֵחַ שָׁם פֵּירוֹת וּמַנִּיחַ שָׁם כֵּלִים, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיָּדוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ מַגַּעַת לְשָׁם. קַשְׁיָא לְרַב! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, שֶׁנִּתְפָּס עָלָיו כְּגַנָּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rav from a *baraita*: If **the inner** courtyard **belongs to a *ḥaver*,** i.e., one devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot, especially the *halakhot* of ritual purity, *teruma*, and tithes, **and the outer** courtyard **to an *am ha'aretz*, that *ḥaver* may lay out** his **produce there,** in the inner courtyard, **and place** his **vessels there,** without concern that the *am ha'aretz* will touch them and render them impure. **And** this applies **even if the hand of the *am ha'aretz* can reach there.** This poses **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rav.** The Gemara answers that **Rav** could have **said to you:** It **is different there, as** were the *am ha'aretz* to tamper with the produce, **he could be caught** and accused **as a thief,** as he has no business being in the inner courtyard. Therefore, there is no concern that he will tamper with it.

Rav addresses a case of two adjacent yards separated by a low hedge or demarcating row of stones (*maspis*). On one side is a *chaver*, careful in matters of *tumah ve-taharah*; on the other, either a non-Jew or an *am ha'aretz*. Rav rules: if the neighbor is an *am ha'aretz*, the *chaver*'s fruit is *tamei* — the low boundary line is insufficient to prevent concern that the *am ha'aretz* crossed over and touched the fruit. But if the neighbor is a non-Jew, the wine is not rendered *yayin nesech* — the same boundary line is adequate protection in that domain. This is Rav's concrete expression of the hierarchical claim: we treat the possibility of *tumah* more seriously and are more ready to find a problem there than we are in the domain of wine.

What accounts for the asymmetry is not entirely spelled out. It may relate to the respective weights the tradition assigns these bodies of law, or to differing assessments of likelihood — an *am ha'aretz* neighbor may be considered more likely to casually cross a boundary line than a non-Jew is to cross into a neighbor's yard specifically to handle wine.

### Boundary Sufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: גַּגּוֹ שֶׁל חָבֵר לְמַעְלָה מִגַּגּוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ, אוֹתוֹ חָבֵר שׁוֹטֵחַ שָׁם פֵּירוֹת וּמַנִּיחַ שָׁם כֵּלִים, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא יָדוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ מַגַּעַת לְשָׁם. קַשְׁיָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן! אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִית לֵיהּ לְאִישְׁתְּמוֹטֵי, מֵימָר אָמַר: אִימְּצוֹרֵי קָא מִמְּצַרְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** support for Rav's opinion from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Teharot* 9:11): **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If **the roof of a *ḥaver* is above the roof of** his neighbor who is **an *am ha'aretz*, that *ḥaver* may lay out produce there and place vessels there, provided that the hand of the *am ha'aretz* cannot reach there;** but if it is within his reach, the pure items of the *ḥaver* are rendered impure. This poses **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who deems permitted pure items in a courtyard divided by a low partition. The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said to you:** It **is different there, as** were the *am ha'aretz* to be discovered reaching up to the upper roof, **he has** a way **to excuse** his behavior by **saying: I** merely **stretched myself;** I was not intending to tamper with anything.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. He rules *af taharotav tehorot* — the boundary line is sufficient protection even for *taharah* purposes. For Rabbi Yochanan, there is no meaningful hierarchy between the two domains at this level of analysis; the same logic of *ein lo lishtatmutei* (he has no excuse for crossing) applies equally in both. He has no right to be in the other yard; the demarcation is clear; and therefore one does not assume he crossed over.

### Rooftop Reach

The Gemara brings further cases to test the limits of these positions. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: if a *chaver*'s rooftop is higher than the adjacent *am ha'aretz*'s rooftop, the *chaver* may spread fruit and place vessels there — provided the *am ha'aretz*'s hand cannot reach up to that level. If the hand *can* reach, it is a problem even for *taharah* purposes.

### Excuse Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: גַּגּוֹ שֶׁל חָבֵר בְּצַד גַּגּוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ, אוֹתוֹ חָבֵר שׁוֹטֵחַ שָׁם פֵּירוֹת וּמַנִּיחַ שָׁם כֵּלִים, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיָּדוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ מַגַּעַת לְשָׁם. קַשְׁיָא לְרַב! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: לָאו אִיכָּא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּקָאֵי כְּוָותִי? אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** that which is taught in that same *baraita*: If **the roof of a *ḥaver* is beside the roof of an *am ha'aretz*, that *ḥaver* may lay out produce there and place vessels there, even if the hand of the *am ha'aretz* can reach there.** This poses **a difficulty to** the statement of **Rav.** The Gemara answers that **Rav** could have **said to you: Isn't there** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel, which stands in accordance with my** opinion with regard to roofs that are next to one another? What **I say** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel.**

A subsequent teaching presents the case of adjacent side-by-side rooftops, where the *am ha'aretz*'s hand can reach the *chaver*'s fruit — and yet the *chaver* may place fruit and vessels there. This appears to contradict Rav's position, since it suggests a boundary line (or in this case, rooftop demarcation) is sufficient even for *tumah ve-taharah* when there is no excuse for crossing.

The Gemara works through the apparent contradictions between these sources, distributing them between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan. Rav responds to the side-by-side rooftop case by aligning himself with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who takes the stricter position: if the hand can physically reach, it is a concern regardless. Rabbi Yochanan responds to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling about the higher rooftop by invoking *aishtemutei*: even there, the person stretching his arm up to a higher rooftop has an excuse — *meimar amri im mitzrei ka memtzina*, I was measuring how tall your building is, or as Rashi alternatively reads it, I was simply stretching, exercising, yawning. The excuse is admittedly weak, but Rashi's point is that some minimally plausible excuse exists, and that is sufficient to create concern.

When one distills the underlying agreement, both authorities accept the same framework: if there is a legitimate excuse for the reaching or crossing, the concern is real; if there is no excuse, the boundary is protected. Their dispute concerns only specific cases — whether, in a given configuration, a minimally colorable excuse exists. This same principle of *nitpas k'ganav* and *aishtemutei* that drove the wine cases applies throughout the laws of *tumah ve-taharah* as well.

## Peace War Troops

### Peacetime

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בּוֹלֶשֶׁת שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה לְעִיר, בִּשְׁעַת שָׁלוֹם — חָבִיּוֹת פְּתוּחוֹת אֲסוּרוֹת, סְתוּמוֹת מוּתָּרוֹת; בִּשְׁעַת מִלְחָמָה — אֵלּוּ וְאֵלּוּ מוּתָּרוֹת, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין פְּנַאי לְנַסֵּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a military unit [*boleshet*] that entered a city,** if it entered **during peacetime,** then after the soldiers leave the **open barrels** of wine **are forbidden,** but the **sealed** barrels **are permitted.** If the unit entered **in wartime,** both **these** barrels **and those** barrels **are permitted, because** in wartime **there is no time to pour** wine for **libations,** and one can be certain that the soldiers did not do so.

The *mishnah* with which the daf concludes presents the case of *boleshet* — a troop — entering a city. Rashi explains the term derives from a root meaning to search out; these are soldiers moving through a town, confiscating food and supplies. If they enter *bi-sh'at shalom* — in peacetime — then open barrels are forbidden and sealed barrels are permitted. If they were already open, one assumes the troops helped themselves; sealed barrels they would have had no reason to open.

### Wartime

But if the troops are engaged in active combat — *bi-sh'at milchamah* — then both open and closed barrels are permitted, *lefi she-ein penai lenasech* — because they have no leisure to perform *nisuch*. This is a striking ruling. Even if the troops are non-Jewish and they are in the middle of the wine, the wine remains permitted because the context of active warfare leaves no room for the deliberate act of idolatrous libation.

One might ask: even if they have no time for *nisuch*, surely they have time to drink, and surely a non-Jew who handles wine and drinks it creates a concern even without explicit *nisuch*? The *mishnah*'s ruling seems to imply that absent a *nisuch* context — absent the intent and leisure associated with idolatrous ritual — even non-Jewish handling of wine is not forbidden. This would be a significant statement about the nature of the prohibition.

### Distraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהִי: עִיר שֶׁכְּבָשׁוּהָ כַּרְקוֹם — כׇּל כֹּהֲנוֹת שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ פְּסוּלוֹת! אָמַר רַב מָרִי: לְנַסֵּךְ אֵין פְּנַאי, לִבְעוֹל יֵשׁ פְּנַאי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to the assumption that soldiers during wartime do not have time to commit transgressions from that which is taught in another mishna (*Ketubot* 27a): With regard to **a city that was conquered by** an army laying **siege, all the women** married to **priests** located **in** the city **are unfit** and forbidden to their husbands, due to the concern that they were raped. **Rav Mari** resolved the contradiction and **said: They do not have time to pour** wine for **libations,** as their passion for idolatry is not pressing at that time, but **they have time to engage in intercourse,** because their lust is great even during wartime.

The Gemara challenges from a separate ruling: if a city is besieged, all the wives of *kohanim* within it are rendered forbidden (*pesulot*), because one must assume that the besieging soldiers, when they breached the city, engaged in acts of rape — and a *kohen*'s wife who has had relations with a non-Jew, even under compulsion, is forbidden to her husband. So even in the midst of war, soldiers apparently find the opportunity for sexual violence. Why then would we say they have no time for *nisuch*?

Rav Mari resolves this directly: *lenasech ein penai, livol yesh penai* — for *nisuch* there is no leisure; for sexual violence there is. Rashi explicates: *d'yeitzer ha-nisuch lo takif lehu ve-trudei b'milchamah* — the impulse toward *nisuch* is not so powerful as to break through the distraction of combat. *Livol yesh penai d-takif lehu yitzraihu* — for the other, a more overpowering drive is at work. The act of sexual violence is driven by a *yetzer* that overwhelms even the pressure of battle; the ritual act of libation is not. Soldiers distracted by fighting will not stop to perform a deliberate religious rite, even if they would stop for other things.

This distinction carries an implicit but important principle: the concern of *yayin nesech* is not simply about a non-Jew physically contacting wine. It is about contact in the context of, or with the capacity for, deliberate idolatrous intention. Troops in the chaos of active warfare, whatever else they may be doing, are not in a frame of mind that produces *nisuch*. The wine therefore remains permitted even if they have unquestionably touched it.
