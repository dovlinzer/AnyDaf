# Zevachim 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tadir vs. Kadosh

### Chattat & Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַחַטָּאוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה קוֹדְמוֹת לָאֲשָׁמוֹת, חוּץ מֵאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא בָּא עַל הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All the sin offerings** mandated **by the Torah take precedence over the guilt offerings,** as explained in the previous mishna (89a), **except for the guilt offering of a leper, because it comes to** render one **fit.** One who has been cured of leprosy must undergo a process through which he is rendered ritually pure before coming into contact with consecrated items. Although he must also bring a sin offering, his guilt offering is more central to that process of purification and therefore it takes precedence over the sin offering.

The *mishna* at the bottom of 90b opens with a general principle of precedence among *korbanos*: *kol hachataot shebatorah kodmin la'ashamot* — all *chataot* take precedence over *ashamot*. The reason given is that the *chattat* involves four applications of blood whereas the *asham* involves only two. As a rule, then, the *chattat* goes first.

### Asham Metzora

There is, however, a notable exception: the *asham metzora*. If one must choose between the *asham* of a *metzora* and a *chattat*, the *asham metzora* is brought first, *mipnei shehu ba al hachshair* — because it comes to restore the *metzora* to a functional status, granting him access to the *Beit HaMikdash* and reintegrating him into the community.

This is a striking ruling. A *chattat* atones for personal sin — it addresses the metaphysical relationship between the individual and God. The *asham metzora*, by contrast, remedies a condition that has excluded this person from communal and sacred life in a thoroughly practical sense. The *metzora* is pushed outside the community in a way that a normal sinner is not. This *halacha* thus expresses a powerful recognition that the urgency of restoring someone's practical role within the community can take precedence over atoning for private sin.

### Asham Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הָאֲשָׁמוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה בָּאִין בְּנֵי שְׁתַּיִם וּבָאִין בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים, חוּץ מֵאֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע – שֶׁהֵן בָּאִין בְּנֵי שֶׁנָּתָן, וְאֵין בָּאִין בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **All the guilt offerings** mandated **by the Torah come** as rams **in their second year, and come worth** two **silver shekels, except for the guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper, as they come** from sheep **in their** first **year, and do not** need to **come worth** two **silver shekels,** as they have no fixed value.

The *mishna* then notes a parenthetical specification regarding *ashamot* generally: *kol ha'ashamot shebatorah ba'in bnei shtayim* — all *ashamot* in the *Torah* must be two years old, and must cost a minimum of two *shekalim*. The exceptions are the *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*, which are derived from specific *pesukim* and must be only one year old, without the two-*shekel* minimum.

### Eating Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁקּוֹדְמִין בְּהַקְרָבָתָן, קוֹדְמִין בַּאֲכִילָתָן. שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ וּשְׁלָמִים שֶׁל יוֹם – שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ קוֹדְמִין לְשֶׁל יוֹם. שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ, חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם שֶׁל הַיּוֹם – שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ קוֹדְמִין. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חַטָּאת קוֹדֶמֶת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as** the more sacred offerings **precede** other offerings **with regard to their sacrifice,** as taught in the previous mishna (89a), **they** also **precede** the others **with regard to their consumption.** If one has **a peace offering from yesterday and a peace offering from today,** the peace offering **from yesterday precedes** the peace offering **from today.** If one has **a peace offering from yesterday** and **a sin offering or a guilt offering from today,** the **peace offering from yesterday precedes** the others; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** The **sin offering precedes** the peace offering, **due to** the fact **that it is an offering of the most sacred order.** Likewise, the guilt offering precedes the peace offering, as it is also of the most sacred order.

Returning to the main theme of precedence, the *mishna* establishes a parallel principle regarding consumption: *kesheim shekodmin behakravatan kodmin be'achilatan* — just as certain *korbanos* take priority in being offered, the same order of priority governs the order of eating them. Whatever is *kadshei kodashim* is eaten before *kadashim kalim*, and all the distinctions established for *hakrava* carry over into *achila*.

### Shelamim Emesh

The *mishna* then presents a case: *shelamim shel emesh veshelamim shel yom* — a *shelamim* brought yesterday and a *shelamim* brought today. Yesterday's comes first, *shelamim shel emesh kodmin leshelamim shel yom*. The simplest reading is that yesterday's animal was slaughtered yesterday, creating urgency because it must be finished before the time limit expires.

*Rashi*, however, based on a later *gemara*, interprets the case differently: the animals were brought to the *azarah* yesterday but had not yet been slaughtered — they simply remained waiting overnight. This raises some difficulty in imagining the practical scenario — did the owner leave his animal behind? And why specifically *shelamim*? One might argue that since the *mishna* just introduced eating, it is specifically discussing the owners eating the *shelamim* at home. But that introduces its own difficulty, since the owners eat *shelamim* while the *kohanim* eat *chattat* and *asham* — so there would be no unified scenario in which the same people are eating all these *korbanos* and facing the same choice. This point perhaps favors reading the passage as focused on the *hakrava* itself rather than consumption.

The *mishna* then addresses a more complex case: *shelamim shel emesh ve'chattat ve'asham shel hayom* — yesterday's *shelamim* versus today's *chattat* and *asham*. On one side stands the urgency or disrespect of keeping the animal waiting since yesterday; on the other, the higher *kedusha* of the *chattat* and *asham*. *Rabbi Meir* rules that yesterday's *shelamim* comes first, while *Chakhamim* rule that the *chattat* takes precedence *mipnei shehi kodesh kodashim* — because it is *kodesh kodashim*. This is precisely the tension the *gemara* will be exploring: what happens when *tadir* (or urgency) competes with *kadosh*?

### Meat Preparation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן הַכֹּהֲנִים רַשָּׁאִין לְשַׁנּוֹת בַּאֲכִילָתָן – לְאָכְלָן צְלוּיִן וּשְׁלוּקִין וּמְבוּשָּׁלִין, לָתֵת לְתוֹכוֹ תַּבְלֵי חוּלִּין וְתַבְלֵי תְרוּמָה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִתֵּן לְתוֹכוֹ תַּבְלֵי תְרוּמָה, שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא הַתְּרוּמָה לִידֵי פְסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **all of** the offerings that are eaten, **the priests are permitted to alter** the manner of **their consumption** and eat them as they choose. Therefore, the priests are permitted **to eat them roasted, or boiled, or cooked,** and **to place in** the cooking pot **non-sacred spices or *teruma* spices.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Meir says: One may not place *teruma* spices in it,** so **that he will not bring the *teruma* to** a state **of disqualification.** Consumption of consecrated foods is permitted only for a limited period, after which they are disqualified and burned. Adding *teruma* spices might cause those spices to be similarly disqualified.

A final clause applies broadly to eating: *vechulan hakohanim resha'in leshanot be'achilatan* — the *kohanim* may eat *korbanos* in any manner they wish, whether roasted, boiled, or cooked.

## Tadir Precedence

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תָּדִיר וּמְקוּדָּשׁ, אֵיזֶה מֵהֶם קוֹדֵם? תָּדִיר קוֹדֵם – מִשּׁוּם דִּתְדִיר, אוֹ דִלְמָא מְקוּדָּשׁ קָדֵים – דְּקַדִּישׁ? תָּא שְׁמַע: תְּמִידִין קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסָפִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: The *mishnayot* (89a) taught that a frequent offering precedes other offerings, and also that an offering of greater sanctity precedes others. If there is **a frequent** offering **and** an offering **of** greater **sanctity** to be sacrificed, **which of them precedes** the other? Does the **frequent** offering **take precedence, due to** the fact **that** it is **frequent, or perhaps** the offering **of** greater **sanctity takes precedence, as** it is **of** greater **sanctity?** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the ruling of the first mishna in this chapter, that the **daily offerings precede** the **additional offerings** because they are frequent.

The *gemara* now raises its central question: *tadir u'mekudash eizeh meihem kodem* — when a more frequent obligation conflicts with a holier one, which takes precedence? Does *tadir* go first because of its regularity, or does *mekudash* go first because of its greater *kedusha*? The *gemara* will now bring a series of cases to resolve this.

### Tamid & Musaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמוּסָפִין קַדִּישִׁי! אַטּוּ שַׁבָּת לְמוּסָפִין אַהֲנַאי, לִתְמִידִין לָא אַהֲנַאי?!
>
> **Translation:** **And even though** the **additional offerings** are **of** greater **sanctity,** as they are sacrificed due to the sanctity of Shabbat, the frequent offering precedes the offering of greater sanctity. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Is that to say** that the sanctity of **Shabbat affects** the sanctity of the **additional offerings** but does **not affect** the **daily offerings** brought on Shabbat? Rather, the sanctity of Shabbat elevates the sanctity of the daily offerings as well, and as both are of equal sanctity, the frequent daily offering precedes the additional offerings.

The first evidence: *temidim kodmin le'musafin* — the *tamid* offering precedes the *musaf*, even though the *musaf*, say of *Shabbat*, carries a greater *kedusha* by virtue of its association with the day. The *olat Shabbat* carries the identity of *Shabbat* in a way that the *tamid* does not.

### Kedusha Attachment

The *gemara*'s response, however, is telling: *atu Shabbat le'musafin ahani, le'temidim lo ahani?* Is it really the case that the *kedusha* of *Shabbat* attaches only to the *musaf* and not equally to the *tamid*? Today, the *tamid* is also the *tamid* of *Shabbat*. The *gemara* is essentially arguing that all *korbanos* brought on a given day share in that day's *kedusha*, so one cannot distinguish between the *musaf* and the *tamid* on the grounds of *kedusha* alone. The answer, it must be said, is less than fully convincing — there is clearly a sense in which the *musaf* is more specifically identified with the holy day than a generic *tamid* — but the *gemara* will deploy this same response throughout the following cases.

### Shabbat Musaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מוּסְפֵי שַׁבָּת קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ! אַטּוּ רֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ לְמוּסָפִין דִּידֵיהּ אַהֲנִי, לְמוּסְפֵי שַׁבָּת לָא אַהֲנִי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another proof: **Come** and **hear** the continuation of this mishna: The **additional Shabbat offerings precede** the **additional New Moon offerings** because they are more frequent, despite the fact that the New Moon elevates the sanctity of its additional offerings. The Gemara rejects this proof in a similar manner: **Is that to say** that the sanctity of the **New Moon affects** the sanctity of **its additional offerings** but **does not affect the additional offerings of Shabbat?** These additional offerings are also imbued with the sanctity of the New Moon.

Second evidence: *musafi Shabbat kodmin le'musafi Rosh Chodesh* — when *Shabbat* and *Rosh Chodesh* coincide, the *musaf* of *Shabbat* is brought before that of *Rosh Chodesh*, even though, as *Rashi* notes, *Rosh Chodesh* is called a *moed* and might be considered holier. The *gemara* responds identically: *atu Rosh Chodesh le'musafin dideih hani, le'musaf Shabbat lo hani?* The *kedusha* of *Rosh Chodesh* attaches equally to the *musaf of Shabbat* offered on that day.

One might ask why the *tefila* of *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh* is structured around *Rosh Chodesh* with *Shabbat* inserted, rather than the reverse — a question that seems to favor precisely the opposite rule. The *gemara* does not address this directly, but the principle of *tadir* would seem to argue for *Shabbat* as the primary framework.

### Rosh Chodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ קוֹדְמִין לְמוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה. אַף עַל גַּב דְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה קַדִּשָׁה! אַטּוּ רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לְמוּסְפֵי דִּידֵיהּ אַהֲנִי, לְמוּסְפֵי רֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ לָא אַהֲנִי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another proof: **Come** and **hear** the continuation of this mishna: The **additional New Moon offerings precede** the **additional New Year offerings** because they are more frequent, **even though the New Year** is **of** greater **sanctity.** The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **Is that to say** that the sanctity of **the New Year affects** the sanctity of **its additional offerings** but **does not affect the additional offerings of** the **New Moon?**

Third evidence: *musafi Roshei Chodashim kodmin le'musafi Rosh Hashana* — on *Rosh Hashana*, which always falls on *Rosh Chodesh*, the *musaf* of *Rosh Chodesh* precedes the *musaf* of *Rosh Hashana*, despite *Rosh Hashana* being clearly holier. *V'af al gav d'Rosh Hashana kadsha* — and even though *Rosh Hashana* has greater *kedusha*. The *gemara*'s answer is the same: the *kedusha* of *Rosh Hashana* attaches to all the *korbanos* of that day, including the *musaf of Rosh Chodesh*.

The pattern across all three cases is consistent: the *gemara* produces compelling evidence that *tadir* takes precedence, and then answers that the distinction in *kedusha* is illusory because the *kedusha* of the day applies equally to all *korbanos* brought that day.

## Tadir Beyond Korbanos

### Kiddush Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דָּבָר אַחֵר – בִּרְכַּת הַיַּיִן תְּדִירָה וּבִרְכַּת הַיּוֹם אֵינָהּ תְּדִירָה, וְתָדִיר וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּדִיר – תָּדִיר קוֹדֵם. אַף עַל גַּב דְּבִרְכַּת הַיּוֹם קַדִּישָׁה! אַטּוּ שַׁבָּת לְבִרְכַּת הַיּוֹם אַהֲנַאי, לְבִרְכַּת הַיַּיִן לָא אַהֲנַאי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another proof: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Berakhot* 5:25) that discusses the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel with regard to the order of blessings in *kiddush*. After stating one reason for the opinion of Beit Hillel that one recites the blessing on the wine before the blessing of the sanctity of the day, the *Tosefta* adds: **Alternatively,** Beit Hillel say: **The blessing over wine** is recited **frequently, and the blessing over the day is not** recited **frequently, and** there is a principle: When **a frequent** practice **and an infrequent** practice clash, the **frequent** practice **takes precedence** over the infrequent practice. This applies **even though the blessing of the day** is **of** greater **sanctity,** as it is recited due to the sanctity of Shabbat. The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **Is that to say** that the sanctity of **Shabbat affects** the sanctity of **the blessing of the day** but **does not affect** the sanctity of **the blessing on the wine?**

The *gemara* now extends the analysis beyond *korbanos* to *tefila* and *kiddush*. *Birkat hayayin tedira u'virkat hayom eina tedira* — the blessing over wine, *borei pri hagafen*, is said more frequently than *mekadesh hashabbat*, which is recited only on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. Accordingly, *borei pri hagafen* precedes *mekadesh hashabbat*, despite the latter's direct association with the sanctity of *Shabbat*. This is the ruling reflected in *Beit Hillel*'s position in the famous *machloket* with *Beit Shammai*.

The *gemara* is again offering this as evidence that *tadir* precedes *kadosh*. And again the response is: *atu Shabbat le'virkat hayom ahani le'virkat hayayin lo ahani?* The *kedusha* of *Shabbat* attaches equally to *borei pri hagafen* recited on *Shabbat*. Your drinking wine on *Shabbat* is itself a holier act than drinking wine during the week — even the ordinary *borei pri hagafen* takes on the character of *Shabbat*. The answer remains as before: no differential in *kedusha* between the two, so *tadir* is the operative rule — but the *gemara* will not concede that this *proves* the principle, since it keeps dissolving the *kedusha* distinction.

### Mincha & Musaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה – מִתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם שֶׁל מִנְחָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ שֶׁל מוּסָפִין! אַטּוּ שַׁבָּת לִתְפִלַּת מוּסָפִין אַהֲנַאי, לִתְפִלַּת מִנְחָה לָא אַהֲנַאי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another proof: **Come** and **hear, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one did not recite the additional prayer on Shabbat until the time of the afternoon prayer arrived, the ***halakha*** is that **a person prays the afternoon prayer and afterward the additional prayer,** as the afternoon prayer is more frequent. This ruling applies despite the fact that the additional prayer is of greater sanctity. Once again the Gemara rejects the proof: **Is that to say** that the sanctity of **Shabbat affects** the sanctity of **the additional prayer** but **does not affect** the sanctity of **the afternoon prayer?**

The *gemara* cites *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling: *halacha mispalel adam shel Mincha ve'achar kach shel Musafin* — if a person arrives at *Mincha* time having not yet davened *Musaf*, the *halacha* follows that *Mincha* is davened first and *Musaf* afterward. *Mincha* is *tadir* — it occurs every day — whereas *Musaf* is associated with the particular sanctity of the day. This scenario arises practically, for instance, on *Shemini Atzeret* when *davening* extends past *chatzot*, or when one wakes in the afternoon on *Rosh Chodesh* having missed both *Shacharis* and *Musaf*. In such cases, *Mincha* takes priority.

The *gemara*'s answer: *atu Shabbat le'tfilat Musafin ahani, le'tfilat Mincha lo ahani?* The *kedusha* of the day applies equally to the *Mincha* prayer, which on *Shabbat* itself contains *mekadesh hashabbat* in its *nusach*. So again, no distinction in *kedusha*, and *tadir* is therefore the governing principle.

## Tadir vs. Matzui

### Raba's Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מָצוּי קָאָמְרַתְּ?! תָּדִיר קָמִיבַּעְיָא לַן, מָצוּי לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response: **Are you speaking** of **a common** offering? Although peace offerings are sacrificed more often than sin offerings, there is no obligation to sacrifice them at any particular frequency. **We raise the dilemma** only with regard to a clash between **a frequent** offering and one of greater sanctity, but **we do not raise the dilemma** with regard to **a common** offering.

At this point the *gemara* pauses to examine the premise underlying the *mishna*'s case of *shelamim shel emesh* versus today's *chattat* and *asham*. The *gemara* had been treating the *shelamim* as the *tadir* element, but *Rava* objects: *shelamim* is not *tadir* — it is *matzui*. The distinction is crucial. *Matzui* means something that is statistically common or prevalent — on any given day in the *Beit HaMikdash*, there will probably be more *shelamim* brought than *chataot* or *ashamot*, simply because *shelamim* is the most voluntarily offered *korban*. But prevalence is not regularity. *Tadir* refers to obligatory regularity — a structure of recurrence built into the obligation itself. A *shelamim* brought frequently by chance, even daily, lacks that intrinsic system of regularity. *Tadir kamibaya lan, matzui lo kamibaya lan* — our question is about *tadir*, not about what is more common. If something is merely prevalent, that does not override the holier *korban*; only genuine *tadir* does.

One might ask whether *shelamim* brought during the *Shalosh Regalim*, where there is a *mitzvah* of *simcha* and a degree of obligatory meat consumption, might approach *tadir*. This is an interesting question — something that occurs three times a year with a degree of regularity might have some claim to *tadir*. But the *gemara* is not exploring that nuance here, and it is worth noting that in any case, the voluntariness of *shelamim* during the week is precisely what removes it from the *tadir* category.

### Pesach & Milah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אוֹצִיא אֶת הַפֶּסַח שֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּדִיר, וְלֹא אוֹצִיא אֶת הַמִּילָה שֶׁהִיא תְּדִירָה!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to positive mitzvot whose intentional neglect results in the punishment of *karet* but whose unwitting transgression does not entail the sacrifice of a sin offering: **I should exclude** the neglect of **the Paschal offering** from the obligation to sacrifice a sin offering, **as it is not frequent, and I should not exclude** the neglect of **the** mitzva of **circumcision, as it is frequent?** Circumcision is considered a frequent mitzva, as it is performed more often than the Paschal offering, despite the fact that there is no obligation to perform circumcisions at any particular frequency.

A *baraita* is cited from *Torat Kohanim* that appears to complicate this picture. The *baraita* notes that we do not bring a *chattat* for failure to fulfill *korban Pesach* or *brit milah*, despite both carrying a penalty of *karet*. The reason is that a *chattat* is brought only for an act of commission, not for non-performance. However, the *baraita*'s language implies that *milah* is weightier than *Pesach*, and it describes *milah* as *tadirah* — more regular. The *gemara* asks: does this not suggest that *matzui* — something that happens frequently, even without a fixed schedule — counts as *tadir*?

### Tadir Contexts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תְּדִירָה – תְּדִירָה בְּמִצְוֹת. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִילָה לְגַבֵּי פֶּסַח – כִּי תָדִיר דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava answers: **What** is the meaning of **frequent** in that context? It means that circumcision is **frequent in** terms of the numerous **mitzvot** commanded with regard to its fulfillment. **And if you wish, say** instead that **circumcision in relation to** the **Paschal offering is considered like a frequent** obligation, as it occurs far more often, whereas peace offerings are brought only somewhat more often than sin offerings. In sum, one cannot infer from the mishna that an offering of greater sanctity precedes a frequent offering.

The *gemara* responds that the use of *tadirah* in that *baraita* is loose and does not carry the technical meaning relevant to the question of precedence. *Mai tadira? Tadira be'mitzvos* — *Rashi* understands this to mean that *milah* is more pervasive in its implications, connected to multiple *mitzvot* (the *brit* applies to *pesach* and *terumah* as well), or that the *Torah* emphasizes the word *brit* thirteen times in connection with *milah*. In that *baraita*, *tadirah* simply means weightier, more significant — not regularly recurrent in the technical sense. *Ve'ibba'it eima*, the *gemara* adds, one could even say that *milah* is *tadir* relative to *Pesach*, but this is a loose comparative usage and not the *tadir* of our halakhic framework.

The conclusion: all the evidence strongly favors *tadir kodem* — *tadir* takes precedence — even if the *gemara*'s answers to each proof are less than fully compelling. The principle stands.

## Out of Order Shechita

### Pause Blood Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: תָּדִיר וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּדִיר, וּקְדֵים וּשְׁחַט לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּדִיר, מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁחְטֵיהּ – מַקְרֵיב לֵיהּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, יָהֵיב לְאַחֵר דִּמְמָרֵס בְּדָמוֹ עַד דְּמַקְרֵיב לֵיהּ לְתָדִיר, וַהֲדַר מַקְרֵיב לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ תָּדִיר?
>
> **Translation:** § An additional **dilemma** with regard to precedence **was raised before** the Sages: If the priest had two offerings to sacrifice, **a frequent** offering **and an infrequent** offering, **and** although he should have initially sacrificed the frequent offering **he slaughtered the infrequent** offering **first, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **since** he already **slaughtered** the infrequent offering he also proceeds to **sacrifice it? Or perhaps** he does not yet sacrifice it but **gives** it **to another** priest, **who stirs its blood** to prevent it from congealing, **until** he **sacrifices** the **frequent** offering; **and then** he **sacrifices the infrequent** offering.

Having established the principle that *tadir* takes precedence, the *gemara* raises a variation of the question: *tadir ve'she'eino tadir ve'kodem ushachat she'eino tadir* — if you had a *tadir* and a non-*tadir* before you, and you went ahead and *shachted* the non-*tadir* first, what do you do? The specific case: it is *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh*, and you have slaughtered the *Rosh Chodesh musaf* first, when *Shabbat* should have gone first. Do you pause — have someone stir the blood to prevent it from congealing — while you return to the correct order and bring the *Shabbat musaf* first? Or, since the *shechita* is already done, do you finish what you started?

### Shelamim Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִסּוּרָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ, חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם שֶׁל יוֹם – שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ קוֹדֵם לְשֶׁל יוֹם. הָא דְּיוֹם דּוּמְיָא דְּאֶמֶשׁ –
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna from Sura said: Come** and **hear** an answer from the mishna: If one has **a peace offering from yesterday** and **a sin offering or a guilt offering from today,** the peace offering **from yesterday precedes** the sin offering **from today.** It can be assumed that the mishna is not discussing a case where none of the offerings have been slaughtered, as the peace offering would not take precedence in this situation. Rather, it is discussing a peace offering from yesterday that was slaughtered but its blood has not yet been presented. One can infer from this that only a peace offering from yesterday takes precedence in this situation, **but** in the case of a peace offering **from today** that is **similar** in other aspects **to** a peace offering **from yesterday,** the peace offering does not take precedence.

The *gemara* first attempts to infer the answer from the *mishna*'s case of *shelamim shel emesh* alongside today's *chattat* and *asham*. The *mishna* rules *shelamim shel emesh kodmin*. If both were brought today but the *shelamim* was *shachted* first — making today's case analogous in some way to the *emesh* scenario — the *gemara* infers that you would push aside the *shelamim* and bring the *chattat* and *asham* first, since *chattat ve'asham kodmin*. This would suggest that once the wrong order has begun, you still correct course and restore priority.

### Both Shechted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי, דִּקְדֵים שַׁחְטֵיהּ לִשְׁלָמִים – חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם קָדְמִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And what are the circumstances** of this case? This is a situation **where** one **first slaughtered the peace offering** from today. In this case the **sin offering or guilt offering takes precedence,** although the peace offering is already slaughtered, as both of them are of greater sanctity. The same should apply to an infrequent offering that was slaughtered before a frequent offering: The frequent offering is slaughtered before the blood of the infrequent offering is presented.

However, the *gemara* immediately limits this inference. The scenario comparable to *emesh* — where *shelamim* has its foot in the door — can only arise if both the *shelamim* and the *chattat* have already been *shachted*. In that situation, one blood will necessarily have to be held while the other is brought; you are merely choosing which to postpone, and you restore the correct order by postponing the *shelamim*. But if only the *shelamim* has been *shachted* and the *chattat* has not yet been *shachted* at all, the *mishna* offers no inference. That case — whether you pause with the *shelamim* blood already in hand in order to *shecht* the *chattat* fresh and restore order — remains unresolved.

### Kiddush Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא שְׁלָמִים דְּאֶמֶשׁ וְחַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם דְּיוֹם הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ – דְּשַׁחְטִינְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ; אֲבָל לָא שַׁחְטִינְהוּ לְתַרְוַיְיהוּ – תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this answer: **Perhaps** when the mishna makes reference to **a peace offering from yesterday and a sin offering or a guilt offering from today,** the circumstances should be understood differently. **How can you find these** other circumstances? The mishna discusses a case **where** the priest already **slaughtered both of them,** the peace offering and the sin or guilt offering, and the blood of both awaits presentation on the altar. **But** had he **not** yet **slaughtered both of them,** but only the peace offering, **you** can still **raise the dilemma** of whether the priest should set aside the blood of the peace offering in order to slaughter the sin offering first, due to its greater sanctity.

The *gemara* looks to the *kiddush* case for further evidence. In *kiddush*, one might note that *Shabbat* is in some sense "encountered" first — you become obligated in *Shabbat* before you pour the wine. Yet we push aside the acknowledgment of *Shabbat* in order to introduce *borei pri hagafen* first. The *gemara* explains: *keivan d'asyan k'man d'shachtinhu le'travaihu dami* — once the wine has been poured and the cup filled, you have encountered the wine obligation as well. So both have been "encountered," the situation is analogous to both *korbanos* having been *shachted*, and you are simply choosing which to postpone. Thus the *kiddush* case supports restoring correct order, but only maps onto the situation where both elements are already fully before you.

### Mincha Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה, מִתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם שֶׁל מִנְחָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ שֶׁל מוּסָפִין! הָכָא נָמֵי, דְּכֵיוָן (דְּאָתְיָין) [דַּאֲתָא יַיִן] – כְּמַאן דִּשְׁחִיטִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites yet another proof: **Come** and **hear, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is that **a person prays the afternoon prayer and afterward the additional prayer,** despite the fact that the obligation of the additional prayer applies first, which is similar to an offering slaughtered first. The Gemara rejects this proof: **Here too, since the time of the afternoon prayer has** now **arrived,** one is obligated in both prayers, and again this is **comparable to** a situation **where one** already **slaughtered both** animals.

Similarly, *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling that *Mincha* precedes *Musaf* when both are pending: the *gemara* explains that once it is *Mincha* time, the obligation of *Mincha* has been encountered. Now both *Mincha* and *Musaf* are before you simultaneously, so you are choosing which to postpone — and you postpone *Musaf*. Again, *keivan d'mata zman tfilat Mincha, k'man d'shachtinhu le'travaihu dami*. The case where only one has been shachted and the other has not remains unresolved.

## Pesach Before Tamid

### Pesach Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִסּוּרָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ, חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם שֶׁל יוֹם – שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ קוֹדֵם לְשֶׁל יוֹם. הָא דְּיוֹם דּוּמְיָא דְּאֶמֶשׁ – וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי, דִּקְדֵים שַׁחְטֵיהּ לִשְׁלָמִים – חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם קָדְמִי!
>
> **Translation:** From the mishna (Pesahim 61a): **Pesach** offerings **slaughtered before** the afternoon **tamid** are **valid** (though technically the Pesach should be slaughtered after the tamid).

The *gemara* next turns to the specific case of *korban Pesach* slaughtered before the afternoon *tamid*. Ordinarily, the *Pesach* is slaughtered after the afternoon *tamid*, since the *Torah* says *bein ha'arbayim* twice in connection with *Pesach*, indicating the latest part of the afternoon. However, *shachtu kodem letamid kasher* — if the *Pesach* was *shachted* before the *tamid bedieved*, it is valid.

### Stirring Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִסּוּרָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ, חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם שֶׁל יוֹם – שְׁלָמִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ קוֹדֵם לְשֶׁל יוֹם. הָא דְּיוֹם דּוּמְיָא דְּאֶמֶשׁ – וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי, דִּקְדֵים שַׁחְטֵיהּ לִשְׁלָמִים – חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם קָדְמִי!
>
> **Translation:** 

The prescribed remedy: *yema'em bedam ad sheyizrok hadam* — have someone stir the blood of the *Pesach* so it does not congeal, until the blood of the *tamid* can be thrown on the *mizbeach*. This appears to be direct evidence for the principle that when the wrong order has occurred, you restore the correct order by having someone hold the blood of the one already *shachted* while you attend to the one that should have gone first.

### Abiyas Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן דִּקְדֵים שַׁחְטֵיהּ לְתָמִיד בְּרֵישָׁא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא סָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא – דְּקָתָנֵי ״עַד שֶׁיִּזָּרֵק הַדָּם״ (תָּמִיד), וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחוֹט וְיִזְרֹק דָּם״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** he **gave precedence to the daily offering** and **slaughtered it first,** and then slaughtered the Paschal offering before sprinkling the blood of the daily offering. Since the blood of both offerings requires sprinkling on the altar, the blood of the daily offering takes precedence. **Rav Aḥa the Elder said to Rav Ashi** that the wording of **the mishna is also precise, as it teaches: Until the blood of the daily offering is sprinkled, and does not teach: Until he slaughters and sprinkles the blood.** This indicates that the daily afternoon offering has already been slaughtered. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** its wording that this is the meaning of the mishna.

The *gemara*, however, resists accepting this as proof for the broader *sha'alah*. *Hacha bemai askinan — kegon sheshachat letamid bereisha* — the case we are dealing with, says the *gemara*, is one where the *tamid* was slaughtered second, after the *Pesach*. So both bloods are now in play, and someone must wait. In that situation, restore the order — but this tells us nothing about the case where only the *Pesach* has been *shachted* and the *tamid* has not yet been *shachted* at all.

### Mishna Reading

*Abaye* adds that a close reading of the *mishna* itself supports this limitation. The *mishna* says *ad sheyizrok hadam* — stir the blood of the *Pesach* until you throw the blood of the *tamid*. It does not say *ad sheyishchot veyizrok dam* — until you *shecht* and then throw the blood. The absence of the *shechita* from this formulation suggests the *tamid* has already been *shachted* — the *mishna* is describing a situation where both *korbanos* are already in the blood stage. Once again, the question of what to do when only one has been *shachted* remains without resolution.

## Meat Preparation and Spices

### Teruma Spices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לִתְנוּ לְתוֹכוֹ תַּבְלֵי חוּלִּין וְתַבְלֵי תְרוּמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לְמׇשְׁחָה״ – לִגְדוּלָּה, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהַמְּלָכִים אוֹכְלִין. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִתֵּן לְתוֹכוֹ תַּבְלֵי תְרוּמָה, שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא הַתְּרוּמָה לִידֵי פְסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: One may place in it non-sacred spices and *teruma* spices, as it is stated: "For greatness,"** indicating that the flesh of the offerings should be eaten **in** the **manner that kings eat** their food, i.e., prepared in any way they want. **Rabbi Meir says: One may not place *teruma* spices in it, so** **that he will not bring the *teruma* to** a state **of disqualification.**

Regarding the use of spices in the preparation of sacrificial meat, *Rabbi Shimon* permits adding any spices, whether ordinary *chullin* spices or *teruma* spices, as the *kohanim* may prepare the meat as befits persons of important status. *Rabbi Meir* disagrees specifically about *teruma* spices: *lo yiten letochan tavlin terumah* — one should not add *teruma* spices to the meat of *korbanos*.

### Absorption Issue

*Rabbi Meir*'s concern is *mevi et haterumah lidei pesul*: the *teruma* absorbs the taste of the *kodashim*, and since the *kodashim* is subject to a time limit for consumption, the *teruma* spices — now having absorbed the flavor of *kodashim* — become similarly constrained. One who uses those spices after the permitted time will have brought *teruma* to a state of *pesul*. This concern is more easily visualized not merely with small amounts of pepper but with whole pieces — an onion, a clove of garlic — that absorb the *kodashim* juices and then become subject to the same restricted time window.

## Cooking Freedom

### Royal Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכוּלָּן כֹּהֲנִים רַשָּׁאִין כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא ״לְמׇשְׁחָה״ – לִגְדוּלָּה, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהַמְּלָכִים אוֹכְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And with regard to all of** the offerings that are eaten, **the priests are permitted** to alter the manner of their consumption and eat them as they choose. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: The **verse states,** with regard to the gifts of the priesthood: "Of all the consecrated items of the children of Israel to you have I given them ***lemoshḥa*"** (Numbers 18:8). *Targum Onkelos* renders this term as: **For greatness,** indicating that the flesh of the offerings should be eaten **in** the **manner that kings eat** their food, i.e., prepared in any way they want.

Returning to the theme of *korban* preparation: *uvechulan kohanim resha'im* — in all these *korbanos*, the *kohanim* are permitted to cook the meat in any fashion and to add any spices they choose. The question of why *chullin* ingredients may be brought into the *azarah* for this purpose is noted — and while *tosfos* elsewhere engages the issue of *chullin ba'azarah*, the *gemara* treats it as permissible in the context of *hakrava* preparation.

The source for this latitude is the verse *le'moshchah* — interpreted as *le'gdulah*, denoting status and importance. *Kederech shehamelachim ochlin* — the *kohanim* eat as kings eat. The significance of this formulation is that it is not "fancy" in a particular prescribed mode — kings eat however they wish, and no one may dictate otherwise. This means the *kohanim* are not limited to a specific preparation method and may eat *korbanos* however befits them.

## Oil Offering

### Two Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִם רָאִיתָ שֶׁמֶן שֶׁהוּא מִתְחַלֵּק בַּעֲזָרָה – אֵין צָרִיךְ לִשְׁאוֹל מַהוּ; אֶלָּא מוֹתַר רְקִיקֵי מְנָחוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע. אִם רָאִיתָ שֶׁמֶן שֶׁהוּא נִיתָּן עַל גַּבֵּי הָאִישִּׁים – אֵין צָרִיךְ לִשְׁאוֹל מַהוּ; אֶלָּא מוֹתַר רְקִיקֵי מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, שֶׁאֵין מִתְנַדְּבִין שֶׁמֶן. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִתְנַדְּבִין שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Shimon said: If you saw oil that is being distributed in the Temple courtyard** for consumption by the priests and you seek to ascertain its nature, you **do not need to ask what it is. Rather,** it is **left over** from the oil **of the wafers of the meal offerings of Israelites** after they smeared a bit of oil on them, **or** it is left over from the ***log* of oil of a leper** after a small amount of the oil was placed on him. **If you saw oil that is placed on the flames** of the altar in the manner of an offering, you **do not need to ask what it is. Rather,** it is **left over** from the oil **of the wafers of the meal offering of priests, or** it is the leftover oil from **the meal offering of the anointed priest,** which requires a great deal of oil and which is burned in its entirety on the altar. The mishna adds: One cannot say that the oil distributed to priests or burned on the altar was brought as a gift offering, **as one may not contribute oil** as a gift offering. **Rabbi Tarfon says: One may contribute oil** as a gift offering.

*Rabbi Shimon* offers a teaching about oil that appears in the *azarah*: if you see *kohanim* dividing oil in the *azarah*, or adding oil to the fire, *ein tzarich lishol mahu* — you need not ask what it is. There are a defined set of contexts in which oil appears in the *Beit HaMikdash*, and *Rabbi Shimon* organizes them into two categories corresponding to two distinct dispositions of the oil.

### Oil Eaten

The first scenario: oil being divided and eaten by *kohanim*. This arises from *motar rekikei minchos Yisrael* — leftover oil from the *rekik* type of *mincha* offering. When a *mincha* is prepared as a wafer (*rekik*), a small amount of oil — just an X-shaped marking — is placed on the wafer, and the rest of the oil that was brought is not incorporated into the baking. That remainder is eaten by the *kohanim*. Similarly, *ve'log shemen shel metzora* — the *log* of oil brought for the *metzora* purification rite, of which only small amounts are applied to the ear and other locations, with the remainder eaten by the *kohanim*. These are the two ways oil is consumed straight in the *azarah*.

### Oil Burned

The second scenario: *im ra'ita shemen shenitan al gabei ishim* — if you see oil being placed on the fire. This comes from *motar rekikei minchos kohanim* — leftover oil from the wafer-type *mincha* of a *kohen*. The difference from the first case is that a *kohen*'s *mincha* is entirely burned; nothing is eaten. So the same surplus oil that would be eaten in the Israelite case is here placed on the fire. Additionally, *u'minchat kohen mashiach* — the *mincha* of the *kohen gadol*, which he brings daily and which involves a disproportionately large quantity of oil relative to the flour, such that much of the oil cannot be incorporated into the baking. Since it is a *kohen*'s *mincha*, it all goes on the fire.

### Nedava Oil

*Rabbi Shimon* closes by stating *she'ein misnadvim shemen* — it is not possible to bring oil as a voluntary *nedava* offering on its own. The *Torah* does not recognize oil as an independent *korban*.

*Rabbi Tarfon* disagrees: *misnadvim shemen* — one may indeed bring oil as a *nedava*. The *gemara* will now explore where this is learned and what its practical implications are.

## Oil Source

### Mincha Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, הַמִּתְנַדֵּב שֶׁמֶן – קוֹמְצוֹ וּשְׁיָרָיו נֶאֱכָלִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״קׇרְבַּן מִנְחָה״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמִּתְנַדְּבִין שֶׁמֶן; וְכִי מִנְחָה – מָה מִנְחָה קוֹמְצָהּ וּשְׁיָרֶיהָ נֶאֱכָלִין, אַף שֶׁמֶן קוֹמְצוֹ וּשְׁיָרָיו נֶאֱכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says: According to the statement of Rabbi Tarfon** that oil may be sacrificed as a gift offering, in the case of **one who contributes oil,** a priest **removes a handful of** the oil and sacrifices it on the altar, **and its remainder is eaten** by the priests. **What is the reason** for the ruling of Shmuel? The **verse states:** "And when one brings **a meal offering [*korban minḥa*]"** (Leviticus 2:1). The superfluous word *korban* **teaches that one may contribute oil, and** its status is **like** that of **a meal offering: Just as** with regard to **a meal offering** the priest **removes a handful and its remainder is eaten, so too** with regard to **oil,** the priest **removes a handful and its remainder is eaten.**

*Shmuel* explains *Rabbi Tarfon*'s position: *lidivrei Rabbi Tarfon hamisnadeiv shemen, komtzo* — if one brings a *nedava* of oil, one takes a *kometz* from the oil, burns it, *ve'shiyurav ne'echalim* — and the remainder is eaten. The model is thus the *mincha*: just as a *mincha* has a *kometz* burned on the *mizbeach* and the *shirayim* eaten by the *kohanim*, the same applies to oil as a *nedava*.

### Korban Mincha

The source: the verse says *korban mincha* — the word *korban* is superfluous. The *Torah* could have simply said *mincha*. The addition of *korban* teaches that there is something else which can constitute its own *korban* in the manner of a *mincha* — and from this, the *gemara* derives that oil may be brought as a *nedava*. *Melamed shemisnadvim shemen.* The *binyan av* then follows: just as a *mincha* involves a *kometz* burned and the remainder eaten, so too oil — *mah mincha kometz ve'shiyurav ne'echalim, af shemen kometz ve'shiyurav ne'echalim*.

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אִם רָאִיתָ שֶׁמֶן שֶׁמִּתְחַלֵּק בַּעֲזָרָה – אִי אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לִשְׁאוֹל מַהוּ; אֶלָּא מוֹתַר רְקִיקֵי מִנְחוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע, שֶׁאֵין מִתְנַדְּבִין. מִכְּלָל דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִתְנַדְּבִין – מִתְחַלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that **we learn** this *halakha* in the mishna **as well: Rabbi Shimon said: If you saw oil that is being distributed in the Temple courtyard, you do not need to ask what it is; rather,** it is **left over** from the oil **of the wafers of the meal offerings of Israelites, or** it is left over from the ***log* of oil of a leper, as one does not contribute** oil as a gift offering. Rabbi Zeira learns **by inference** from the mishna **that** according to **the one who says** that **one may contribute** oil, it is **distributed** to the priests for consumption and it is not sacrificed entirely.

*Rav Zeira* adds that this is already implicit in the *mishna* itself. *Rabbi Shimon*'s statement that the oil eaten by *kohanim* comes specifically from the *rekik* surplus or the *metzora*'s *log* — and not from a *nedava* — carries the implicit *miklal* that according to *Rabbi Tarfon*, who allows a *nedava* of oil, such a *nedava* would indeed be eaten. For *Rav Zeira*, both scenarios in the *mishna* — oil eaten and oil burned — map onto *Rabbi Tarfon*'s view: the *kometz* is burned and the *shirayim* are eaten.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אִם רָאִיתָ שֶׁמֶן שֶׁנִּיתָּן עַל גַּבֵּי אִישִּׁים – אִי אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לִשְׁאוֹל מַהוּ; אֶלָּא מוֹתַר רְקִיקֵי מִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, שֶׁאֵין מִתְנַדְּבִין שֶׁמֶן. מִכְּלָל דִּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִתְנַדֵּב – כּוּלָּן לָאִישִּׁים. לְאַבָּיֵי קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא, לְרַבִּי זֵירָא קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rabbi Zeira: **Say the last clause** of the mishna: **If you saw oil that is placed on the flames** of the altar, **you do not need to ask what it is; rather,** it is **left over** from the oil **of the wafers of the meal offering of priests or** it is left over from **the meal offering of the anointed priest, as one does not contribute oil** as a gift offering. Abaye learns **by inference** from the mishna **that according to the one who says** that **one may contribute** oil, it is burned in its **entirety in the flames** on the altar. This contradicts Shmuel's statement that according to Rabbi Tarfon only a handful of the oil is burned on the altar. The Gemara comments: **For Abaye** the inference from **the first clause** of the mishna is **difficult,** while **for Rabbi Zeira** the inference from **the last clause** is **difficult.**

*Abaye* challenges this by pointing to the second scenario: the *mishna* also says that oil placed on the fire is not from a *nedava*, carrying the implication that according to *Rabbi Tarfon*, a *nedava* of oil would go on the fire entirely. If so, *Rabbi Tarfon* is only contesting the second scenario (burning), and his *nedava* of oil is entirely burned — not a case of *kometz* and *shirayim*. This is *Abaye*'s position.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי זֵירָא – רֵישָׁא בְּשִׁירַיִם, סֵיפָא בְּקוֹמֶץ. אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי – קַשְׁיָא! תְּנָא רֵישָׁא אַטּוּ סֵיפָא. בִּשְׁלָמָא סֵיפָא – תָּנֵי מִשּׁוּם רֵישָׁא; אֶלָּא רֵישָׁא מִשּׁוּם סֵיפָא מִי תָּנֵי?! אִין; אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: תְּנָא רֵישָׁא מִשּׁוּם סֵיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Granted,** according **to Rabbi Zeira,** the inference from **the first clause** that the oil is distributed for consumption by the priests can be explained as referring **to** the **remainder** of the oil, whereas the inference from **the last clause** that the oil is burned on the altar is referring **to** the **handful** removed from the oil. **But** according **to Abaye,** the contradictory inferences pose **a difficulty.** The Gemara answers: One cannot infer anything from the first clause, as the mishna **taught the first clause due to the last clause.** That is, as the *tanna* of the mishna wishes to teach the last clause in a certain manner, he teaches the first clause in a similar style, despite the fact that one might come to an erroneous conclusion from the wording of the first clause. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** a *tanna* may **teach the last clause** of a mishna **due to the first clause,** i.e., a *tanna* might teach in a similar formulation to one he had already used. **But would** a *tanna* **teach the first clause** of a mishna **due to the last clause?** The Gemara answers: **Yes; they say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, that a *tanna* **taught the first clause due to the last clause.**

The *gemara* then resolves the tension for *Rav Zeira*: the *mishna* lists both scenarios — eaten and burned — and *Rabbi Tarfon*'s *nedava* in his view covers both: some is burned (the *kometz*) and the rest is eaten (*shirayim*). The *mishna* reads naturally for *Rav Zeira*. For *Abaye*, however, there is a difficulty with the first scenario, since his *Rabbi Tarfon* would not have a *nedava* that is eaten. The *gemara* resolves this by saying *tani reisha atu seifa* — the *mishna* introduces the first scenario as background and context for the second, which is where the actual debate lies. *Amrei be-ma'arava*: in *Eretz Yisrael* it was said that *tani reisha mishum seifa* — sometimes a *mishna* leads with the undisputed case in order to set up the disputed one. This is actually coherent: here is scenario one, about which everyone agrees; now here is scenario two, about which *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Tarfon* disagree.

The debate between *Rav Zeira* and *Abaye*, then, is about the character of *Rabbi Tarfon*'s *nedava*: is it like a *mincha* (partial burning, partial eating), or is it like an *olah* (fully burned)?

### Wine Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: יַיִן כְּדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – לַסְּפָלִים, שֶׁמֶן כְּדִבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן – לָאִישִּׁים. מַאי, לָאו מִדְּיַיִן כּוּלּוֹ לַסְּפָלִים, שֶׁמֶן כּוּלּוֹ לָאִישִּׁים? מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? הָא כִּדְאִיתַהּ וְהָא כִּדְאִיתַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: If one contributes **wine, according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva** that one may contribute wine, it is poured **into the basins** adjacent to the corner of the altar. If one contributes **oil, according to the statement of Rabbi Tarfon** that one may contribute oil, it is burned **in the flames** of the altar. **What, is it not** possible to infer **from** the fact **that** the **wine** is poured in **its entirety into the basins** that the **oil** is likewise burned in **its entirety in the flames** of the altar, contrary to Shmuel's statement? The Gemara rejects this proof: **Are the cases comparable? This** case is **as it is and that** case is **as it is,** i.e., the donations of wine and the oil are separate cases, and the two statements of the *baraita* need not accord with each other.

The *gemara* then introduces the question of whether wine may also be offered as a *nedava*, citing *Rabbi Akiva*'s position that *yayin ke-divrei Rabbi Akiva le-sfalim* — according to *Rabbi Akiva*, wine may be brought as a *nedava* and is poured down the *sefalim* (the temple drain funnels). This prompts the question: if wine as a *nedava* goes entirely *le-sefalim*, does that mean oil as a *nedava* goes entirely onto the fire? *Mai lav mi-de-yayin kulo le-sfalim shemen kulo la-ishim?* The *gemara* rejects this inference: *mide irya? Ha ke-dita ve-ha ke-dita* — each case operates by its own rules. The fact that wine all goes through the funnels does not compel the conclusion that all the oil goes on the fire. Some of the oil going on the fire is fully compatible with the rest of the oil being eaten.

## Rav Papa Framework

### Source Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, כְּתַנָּאֵי: שֶׁמֶן לֹא יִפְחוֹת מִלּוֹג. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Shmuel's statement is **like** one side of a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: One who contributes **oil** should **not** bring **less than a *log*. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Three *log*.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **disagree? The Sages said this before Rav Pappa:** They disagree with regard to the nature of an inference by means of verbal analogy or juxtaposition: Is the secondary case equated to the primary case in all aspects, in accordance with the exegetical principle: **Infer from it and** again **from it;** or does the comparison extend only to one specific issue derived from the primary case, in accordance with the principle: **Infer from it but interpret** the *halakha* according **to its** own **place,** i.e., in all other aspects the cases are not equated?

*Rav Papa* thus frames the dispute: *Rabbanan* derive the *nedava* of oil from the *mincha* context and maintain that context throughout — one *log*, *kometz* burned, remainder eaten. *Rabbi* derives the quantity from the liquid-offering context and applies the rules of liquid offerings — three *log*, all burned. The *machloket* is fundamentally about which source governs not only the fact of the *nedava* but the entire structure of how it is offered.

### Baraita Reexamined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן לְרַב פָּפָּא: מִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי?! וְהָתַנְיָא: ״קׇרְבַּן מִנְחָה״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמִּתְנַדְּבִין שֶׁמֶן. וְכַמָּה? שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין. וּמַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּין – רַבִּי הִיא; וְקָא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מִ״קׇּרְבָּן״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי תַּנְיָא תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said to Rav Pappa: How can you say that,** i.e., that according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi the source of the gift offering of oil is not from the meal offering? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And when one brings **a meal offering [*korban minḥa*]"** (Leviticus 2:1), that the superfluous word *korban* **teaches that one may contribute oil?** The *baraita* continues: **And how much** must one contribute? **Three *log*.** The Gemara explains the question: **And whom did you hear who says** the gift offering of oil is **three *log*? This is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and** yet he **cites** the source of the gift offering of oil **from** the word ***korban*,** which is referring to a meal offering. Rav Pappa **said to him: If** this *baraita* **is taught, it is taught;** and I cannot take issue with it.

A challenge to *Rav Papa*'s framework comes from a *baraita*: *korban mincha melamed she-nitnadav im shemen ve-chamah shloshah login* — a *baraita* states that we learn from *korban mincha* that oil may be brought as a *nedava*, and specifies three *log* as the amount. *Man tana de-amar shloshah login? Rabbi* — it is *Rabbi* who says three *log*. But this *baraita* shows *Rabbi* learning from *korban mincha* as the source — yet arriving at three *log*. If *Rav Papa*'s framework is correct, *Rabbi*'s three *log* should be derived from wine/nesachim, not from *mincha*. This appears to contradict *Rav Papa*'s analysis.

*Rav Papa* concedes the point from the *baraita*: *tanya tanya* — you have a *baraita*, I cannot argue with that. The *baraita* shows that even *Rabbi* learns from *korban mincha* as the textual source, and therefore *Rav Papa*'s original framing — that *Rabbi* learns directly from wine — cannot be correct. Rather, the debate is not about *where* one learns the *nedava* of oil (both learn it from *korban mincha*), but about whether, once learned, the framework of *mincha* is maintained (*don mina u'mina*, yielding one *log* and *kometz* with remainder eaten), or whether it assimilates into the context of liquid offerings (*don mina u'uki be'atrah*, yielding three *log* and all burned). *Rabbi* learns from *mincha* but then applies the law *ba'atrah* — in the place of liquid offerings — and thus arrives at three *log* and full burning. *Rabbanan* maintain the *mincha* context throughout and arrive at one *log* with *kometz* and *shirayim*.

### Framework Elaboration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מָה מִנְחָה מִתְנַדְּבִין, אַף שֶׁמֶן מִתְנַדְּבִין [וּמִינַּהּ – מָה מִנְחָה לוֹג שֶׁמֶן, אַף כָּאן לוֹג שֶׁמֶן. וּמָה מִנְחָה קוֹמְצָהּ וּשְׁיָרֶיהָ נֶאֱכָלִין, אַף שֶׁמֶן קוֹמְצוֹ וּשְׁיָרָיו נֶאֱכָלִין].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that this **is** the difference **between them, as the Rabbis hold** by the principle: Infer from it and again from it. The Gemara explains the application of this principle: **Just as a meal offering is contributed, so too, oil is contributed,** as inferred from the verse addressing the meal offering. **And** again one infers **from** this source: **Just as a meal offering** requires **a *log* of oil, so too here,** an offering of oil alone must be **a *log* of oil. And just as** with regard to **a meal offering** the priest **removes a handful and its remainder is eaten, so too** with regard to **oil,** the priest **removes a handful and its remainder is eaten.**

The *gemara* continues with the debate about whether wine may also be offered as a *nedava*, citing *Rabbi Akiva*'s position that *yayin ke-divrei Rabbi Akiva le-sfalim* — according to *Rabbi Akiva*, wine may be brought as a *nedava* and is poured down the *sefalim* (the temple drain funnels). This prompts the question: if wine as a *nedava* goes entirely *le-sefalim*, does that mean oil as a *nedava* goes entirely onto the fire? The *gemara* will continue tomorrow with a debate about whether wine offered as a *nedava* goes through the *sefalim* or is placed directly on the fire.
