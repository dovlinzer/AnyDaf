# Zevachim 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tadir vs. Kadosh

### Chattat & Asham

The *mishna* at the bottom of 90b opens with a general principle of precedence among *korbanos*: *kol hachataot shebatorah kodmin la'ashamot* — all *chataot* take precedence over *ashamot*. The reason given is that the *chattat* involves four applications of blood whereas the *asham* involves only two. As a rule, then, the *chattat* goes first.

### Asham Metzora

There is, however, a notable exception: the *asham metzora*. If one must choose between the *asham* of a *metzora* and a *chattat*, the *asham metzora* is brought first, *mipnei shehu ba al hachshair* — because it comes to restore the *metzora* to a functional status, granting him access to the *Beit HaMikdash* and reintegrating him into the community.

This is a striking ruling. A *chattat* atones for personal sin — it addresses the metaphysical relationship between the individual and God. The *asham metzora*, by contrast, remedies a condition that has excluded this person from communal and sacred life in a thoroughly practical sense. The *metzora* is pushed outside the community in a way that a normal sinner is not. This *halacha* thus expresses a powerful recognition that the urgency of restoring someone's practical role within the community can take precedence over atoning for private sin.

### Asham Requirements

The *mishna* then notes a parenthetical specification regarding *ashamot* generally: *kol ha'ashamot shebatorah ba'in bnei shtayim* — all *ashamot* in the *Torah* must be two years old, and must cost a minimum of two *shekalim*. The exceptions are the *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*, which are derived from specific *pesukim* and must be only one year old, without the two-*shekel* minimum.

### Eating Order

Returning to the main theme of precedence, the *mishna* establishes a parallel principle regarding consumption: *kesheim shekodmin behakravatan kodmin be'achilatan* — just as certain *korbanos* take priority in being offered, the same order of priority governs the order of eating them. Whatever is *kadshei kodashim* is eaten before *kadashim kalim*, and all the distinctions established for *hakrava* carry over into *achila*.

### Shelamim Emesh

The *mishna* then presents a case: *shelamim shel emesh veshelamim shel yom* — a *shelamim* brought yesterday and a *shelamim* brought today. Yesterday's comes first, *shelamim shel emesh kodmin leshelamim shel yom*. The simplest reading is that yesterday's animal was slaughtered yesterday, creating urgency because it must be finished before the time limit expires.

*Rashi*, however, based on a later *gemara*, interprets the case differently: the animals were brought to the *azarah* yesterday but had not yet been slaughtered — they simply remained waiting overnight. This raises some difficulty in imagining the practical scenario — did the owner leave his animal behind? And why specifically *shelamim*? One might argue that since the *mishna* just introduced eating, it is specifically discussing the owners eating the *shelamim* at home. But that introduces its own difficulty, since the owners eat *shelamim* while the *kohanim* eat *chattat* and *asham* — so there would be no unified scenario in which the same people are eating all these *korbanos* and facing the same choice. This point perhaps favors reading the passage as focused on the *hakrava* itself rather than consumption.

The *mishna* then addresses a more complex case: *shelamim shel emesh ve'chattat ve'asham shel hayom* — yesterday's *shelamim* versus today's *chattat* and *asham*. On one side stands the urgency or disrespect of keeping the animal waiting since yesterday; on the other, the higher *kedusha* of the *chattat* and *asham*. *Rabbi Meir* rules that yesterday's *shelamim* comes first, while *Chakhamim* rule that the *chattat* takes precedence *mipnei shehi kodesh kodashim* — because it is *kodesh kodashim*. This is precisely the tension the *gemara* will be exploring: what happens when *tadir* (or urgency) competes with *kadosh*?

A final clause applies broadly to eating: *vechulan hakohanim resha'in leshanot be'achilatan* — the *kohanim* may eat *korbanos* in any manner they wish, whether roasted, boiled, or cooked.

## Meat Preparation

### Teruma Spices

Regarding the use of spices in the preparation of sacrificial meat, *Rabbi Shimon* permits adding any spices, whether ordinary *chullin* spices or *teruma* spices, as the *kohanim* may prepare the meat as befits persons of important status. *Rabbi Meir* disagrees specifically about *teruma* spices: *lo yiten letochan tavlin terumah* — one should not add *teruma* spices to the meat of *korbanos*.

### Absorption Issue

*Rabbi Meir*'s concern is *mevi et haterumah lidei pesul*: the *teruma* absorbs the taste of the *kodashim*, and since the *kodashim* is subject to a time limit for consumption, the *teruma* spices — now having absorbed the flavor of *kodashim* — become similarly constrained. One who uses those spices after the permitted time will have brought *teruma* to a state of *pesul*. This concern is more easily visualized not merely with small amounts of pepper but with whole pieces — an onion, a clove of garlic — that absorb the *kodashim* juices and then become subject to the same restricted time window.

## Tadir Precedence

### Question Posed

The *gemara* now raises its central question: *tadir u'mekudash eizeh meihem kodem* — when a more frequent obligation conflicts with a holier one, which takes precedence? Does *tadir* go first because of its regularity, or does *mekudash* go first because of its greater *kedusha*? The *gemara* will now bring a series of cases to resolve this.

### Tamid & Musaf

The first evidence: *temidim kodmin le'musafin* — the *tamid* offering precedes the *musaf*, even though the *musaf*, say of *Shabbat*, carries a greater *kedusha* by virtue of its association with the day. The *olat Shabbat* carries the identity of *Shabbat* in a way that the *tamid* does not.

### Kedusha Attachment

The *gemara*'s response, however, is telling: *atu Shabbat le'musafin ahani, le'temidim lo ahani?* Is it really the case that the *kedusha* of *Shabbat* attaches only to the *musaf* and not equally to the *tamid*? Today, the *tamid* is also the *tamid* of *Shabbat*. The *gemara* is essentially arguing that all *korbanos* brought on a given day share in that day's *kedusha*, so one cannot distinguish between the *musaf* and the *tamid* on the grounds of *kedusha* alone. The answer, it must be said, is less than fully convincing — there is clearly a sense in which the *musaf* is more specifically identified with the holy day than a generic *tamid* — but the *gemara* will deploy this same response throughout the following cases.

### Shabbat Musaf

Second evidence: *musafi Shabbat kodmin le'musafi Rosh Chodesh* — when *Shabbat* and *Rosh Chodesh* coincide, the *musaf* of *Shabbat* is brought before that of *Rosh Chodesh*, even though, as *Rashi* notes, *Rosh Chodesh* is called a *moed* and might be considered holier. The *gemara* responds identically: *atu Rosh Chodesh le'musafin dideih hani, le'musaf Shabbat lo hani?* The *kedusha* of *Rosh Chodesh* attaches equally to the *musaf of Shabbat* offered on that day.

One might ask why the *tefila* of *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh* is structured around *Rosh Chodesh* with *Shabbat* inserted, rather than the reverse — a question that seems to favor precisely the opposite rule. The *gemara* does not address this directly, but the principle of *tadir* would seem to argue for *Shabbat* as the primary framework.

### Rosh Chodesh

Third evidence: *musafi Roshei Chodashim kodmin le'musafi Rosh Hashana* — on *Rosh Hashana*, which always falls on *Rosh Chodesh*, the *musaf* of *Rosh Chodesh* precedes the *musaf* of *Rosh Hashana*, despite *Rosh Hashana* being clearly holier. *V'af al gav d'Rosh Hashana kadsha* — and even though *Rosh Hashana* has greater *kedusha*. The *gemara*'s answer is the same: the *kedusha* of *Rosh Hashana* attaches to all the *korbanos* of that day, including the *musaf of Rosh Chodesh*.

The pattern across all three cases is consistent: the *gemara* produces compelling evidence that *tadir* takes precedence, and then answers that the distinction in *kedusha* is illusory because the *kedusha* of the day applies equally to all *korbanos* brought that day.

## Tadir Beyond Korbanos

### Kiddush Order

The *gemara* now extends the analysis beyond *korbanos* to *tefila* and *kiddush*. *Birkat hayayin tedira u'virkat hayom eina tedira* — the blessing over wine, *borei pri hagafen*, is said more frequently than *mekadesh hashabbat*, which is recited only on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. Accordingly, *borei pri hagafen* precedes *mekadesh hashabbat*, despite the latter's direct association with the sanctity of *Shabbat*. This is the ruling reflected in *Beit Hillel*'s position in the famous *machloket* with *Beit Shammai*.

The *gemara* is again offering this as evidence that *tadir* precedes *kadosh*. And again the response is: *atu Shabbat le'virkat hayom ahani le'virkat hayayin lo ahani?* The *kedusha* of *Shabbat* attaches equally to *borei pri hagafen* recited on *Shabbat*. Your drinking wine on *Shabbat* is itself a holier act than drinking wine during the week — even the ordinary *borei pri hagafen* takes on the character of *Shabbat*. The answer remains as before: no differential in *kedusha* between the two, so *tadir* is the operative rule — but the *gemara* will not concede that this *proves* the principle, since it keeps dissolving the *kedusha* distinction.

### Mincha & Musaf

The *gemara* cites *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling: *halacha mispalel adam shel Mincha ve'achar kach shel Musafin* — if a person arrives at *Mincha* time having not yet davened *Musaf*, the *halacha* follows that *Mincha* is davened first and *Musaf* afterward. *Mincha* is *tadir* — it occurs every day — whereas *Musaf* is associated with the particular sanctity of the day. This scenario arises practically, for instance, on *Shemini Atzeret* when *davening* extends past *chatzot*, or when one wakes in the afternoon on *Rosh Chodesh* having missed both *Shacharis* and *Musaf*. In such cases, *Mincha* takes priority.

The *gemara*'s answer: *atu Shabbat le'tfilat Musafin ahani, le'tfilat Mincha lo ahani?* The *kedusha* of the day applies equally to the *Mincha* prayer, which on *Shabbat* itself contains *mekadesh hashabbat* in its *nusach*. So again, no distinction in *kedusha*, and *tadir* is therefore the governing principle.

## Tadir vs. Matzui

### Raba's Definition

At this point the *gemara* pauses to examine the premise underlying the *mishna*'s case of *shelamim shel emesh* versus today's *chattat* and *asham*. The *gemara* had been treating the *shelamim* as the *tadir* element, but *Rava* objects: *shelamim* is not *tadir* — it is *matzui*. The distinction is crucial. *Matzui* means something that is statistically common or prevalent — on any given day in the *Beit HaMikdash*, there will probably be more *shelamim* brought than *chataot* or *ashamot*, simply because *shelamim* is the most voluntarily offered *korban*. But prevalence is not regularity. *Tadir* refers to obligatory regularity — a structure of recurrence built into the obligation itself. A *shelamim* brought frequently by chance, even daily, lacks that intrinsic system of regularity. *Tadir kamibaya lan, matzui lo kamibaya lan* — our question is about *tadir*, not about what is more common. If something is merely prevalent, that does not override the holier *korban*; only genuine *tadir* does.

One might ask whether *shelamim* brought during the *Shalosh Regalim*, where there is a *mitzvah* of *simcha* and a degree of obligatory meat consumption, might approach *tadir*. This is an interesting question — something that occurs three times a year with a degree of regularity might have some claim to *tadir*. But the *gemara* is not exploring that nuance here, and it is worth noting that in any case, the voluntariness of *shelamim* during the week is precisely what removes it from the *tadir* category.

### Pesach & Milah

A *baraita* is cited from *Torat Kohanim* that appears to complicate this picture. The *baraita* notes that we do not bring a *chattat* for failure to fulfill *korban Pesach* or *brit milah*, despite both carrying a penalty of *karet*. The reason is that a *chattat* is brought only for an act of commission, not for non-performance. However, the *baraita*'s language implies that *milah* is weightier than *Pesach*, and it describes *milah* as *tadirah* — more regular. The *gemara* asks: does this not suggest that *matzui* — something that happens frequently, even without a fixed schedule — counts as *tadir*?

### Tadir Contexts

The *gemara* responds that the use of *tadirah* in that *baraita* is loose and does not carry the technical meaning relevant to the question of precedence. *Mai tadira? Tadira be'mitzvos* — *Rashi* understands this to mean that *milah* is more pervasive in its implications, connected to multiple *mitzvot* (the *brit* applies to *pesach* and *terumah* as well), or that the *Torah* emphasizes the word *brit* thirteen times in connection with *milah*. In that *baraita*, *tadirah* simply means weightier, more significant — not regularly recurrent in the technical sense. *Ve'ibba'it eima*, the *gemara* adds, one could even say that *milah* is *tadir* relative to *Pesach*, but this is a loose comparative usage and not the *tadir* of our halakhic framework.

The conclusion: all the evidence strongly favors *tadir kodem* — *tadir* takes precedence — even if the *gemara*'s answers to each proof are less than fully compelling. The principle stands.

## Out of Order Shechita

### Pause Blood Query

Having established the principle that *tadir* takes precedence, the *gemara* raises a variation of the question: *tadir ve'she'eino tadir ve'kodem ushachat she'eino tadir* — if you had a *tadir* and a non-*tadir* before you, and you went ahead and *shachted* the non-*tadir* first, what do you do? The specific case: it is *Shabbat Rosh Chodesh*, and you have slaughtered the *Rosh Chodesh musaf* first, when *Shabbat* should have gone first. Do you pause — have someone stir the blood to prevent it from congealing — while you return to the correct order and bring the *Shabbat musaf* first? Or, since the *shechita* is already done, do you finish what you started?

### Shelamim Inference

The *gemara* first attempts to infer the answer from the *mishna*'s case of *shelamim shel emesh* alongside today's *chattat* and *asham*. The *mishna* rules *shelamim shel emesh kodmin*. If both were brought today but the *shelamim* was *shachted* first — making today's case analogous in some way to the *emesh* scenario — the *gemara* infers that you would push aside the *shelamim* and bring the *chattat* and *asham* first, since *chattat ve'asham kodmin*. This would suggest that once the wrong order has begun, you still correct course and restore priority.

### Both Shechted

However, the *gemara* immediately limits this inference. The scenario comparable to *emesh* — where *shelamim* has its foot in the door — can only arise if both the *shelamim* and the *chattat* have already been *shachted*. In that situation, one blood will necessarily have to be held while the other is brought; you are merely choosing which to postpone, and you restore the correct order by postponing the *shelamim*. But if only the *shelamim* has been *shachted* and the *chattat* has not yet been *shachted* at all, the *mishna* offers no inference. That case — whether you pause with the *shelamim* blood already in hand in order to *shecht* the *chattat* fresh and restore order — remains unresolved.

### Kiddush Analogy

The *gemara* looks to the *kiddush* case for further evidence. In *kiddush*, one might note that *Shabbat* is in some sense "encountered" first — you become obligated in *Shabbat* before you pour the wine. Yet we push aside the acknowledgment of *Shabbat* in order to introduce *borei pri hagafen* first. The *gemara* explains: *keivan d'asyan k'man d'shachtinhu le'travaihu dami* — once the wine has been poured and the cup filled, you have encountered the wine obligation as well. So both have been "encountered," the situation is analogous to both *korbanos* having been *shachted*, and you are simply choosing which to postpone. Thus the *kiddush* case supports restoring correct order, but only maps onto the situation where both elements are already fully before you.

### Mincha Evidence

Similarly, *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling that *Mincha* precedes *Musaf* when both are pending: the *gemara* explains that once it is *Mincha* time, the obligation of *Mincha* has been encountered. Now both *Mincha* and *Musaf* are before you simultaneously, so you are choosing which to postpone — and you postpone *Musaf*. Again, *keivan d'mata zman tfilat Mincha, k'man d'shachtinhu le'travaihu dami*. The case where only one has been shachted and the other has not remains unresolved.

## Pesach Before Tamid

### Pesach Kosher

The *gemara* next turns to the specific case of *korban Pesach* slaughtered before the afternoon *tamid*. Ordinarily, the *Pesach* is slaughtered after the afternoon *tamid*, since the *Torah* says *bein ha'arbayim* twice in connection with *Pesach*, indicating the latest part of the afternoon. However, *shachtu kodem letamid kasher* — if the *Pesach* was *shachted* before the *tamid bedieved*, it is valid.

### Stirring Solution

The prescribed remedy: *yema'em bedam ad sheyizrok hadam* — have someone stir the blood of the *Pesach* so it does not congeal, until the blood of the *tamid* can be thrown on the *mizbeach*. This appears to be direct evidence for the principle that when the wrong order has occurred, you restore the correct order by having someone hold the blood of the one already *shachted* while you attend to the one that should have gone first.

### Abiyas Concern

The *gemara*, however, resists accepting this as proof for the broader *sha'alah*. *Hacha bemai askinan — kegon sheshachat letamid bereisha* — the case we are dealing with, says the *gemara*, is one where the *tamid* was slaughtered second, after the *Pesach*. So both bloods are now in play, and someone must wait. In that situation, restore the order — but this tells us nothing about the case where only the *Pesach* has been *shachted* and the *tamid* has not yet been *shachted* at all.

### Mishna Reading

*Abaye* adds that a close reading of the *mishna* itself supports this limitation. The *mishna* says *ad sheyizrok hadam* — stir the blood of the *Pesach* until you throw the blood of the *tamid*. It does not say *ad sheyishchot veyizrok dam* — until you *shecht* and then throw the blood. The absence of the *shechita* from this formulation suggests the *tamid* has already been *shachted* — the *mishna* is describing a situation where both *korbanos* are already in the blood stage. Once again, the question of what to do when only one has been *shachted* remains without resolution.

## Meat Preparation (II)

### Cooking Freedom

Returning to the theme of *korban* preparation: *uvechulan kohanim resha'im* — in all these *korbanos*, the *kohanim* are permitted to cook the meat in any fashion and to add any spices they choose. The question of why *chullin* ingredients may be brought into the *azarah* for this purpose is noted — and while *tosfos* elsewhere engages the issue of *chullin ba'azarah*, the *gemara* treats it as permissible in the context of *hakrava* preparation.

### Royal Status

The source for this latitude is the verse *le'moshchah* — interpreted as *le'gdulah*, denoting status and importance. *Kederech shehamelachim ochlin* — the *kohanim* eat as kings eat. The significance of this formulation is that it is not "fancy" in a particular prescribed mode — kings eat however they wish, and no one may dictate otherwise. This means the *kohanim* are not limited to a specific preparation method and may eat *korbanos* however befits them.

## Oil Offering

### Two Scenarios

*Rabbi Shimon* offers a teaching about oil that appears in the *azarah*: if you see *kohanim* dividing oil in the *azarah*, or adding oil to the fire, *ein tzarich lishol mahu* — you need not ask what it is. There are a defined set of contexts in which oil appears in the *Beit HaMikdash*, and *Rabbi Shimon* organizes them into two categories corresponding to two distinct dispositions of the oil.

### Oil Eaten

The first scenario: oil being divided and eaten by *kohanim*. This arises from *motar rekikei minchos Yisrael* — leftover oil from the *rekik* type of *mincha* offering. When a *mincha* is prepared as a wafer (*rekik*), a small amount of oil — just an X-shaped marking — is placed on the wafer, and the rest of the oil that was brought is not incorporated into the baking. That remainder is eaten by the *kohanim*. Similarly, *ve'log shemen shel metzora* — the *log* of oil brought for the *metzora* purification rite, of which only small amounts are applied to the ear and other locations, with the remainder eaten by the *kohanim*. These are the two ways oil is consumed straight in the *azarah*.

### Oil Burned

The second scenario: *im ra'ita shemen shenitan al gabei ishim* — if you see oil being placed on the fire. This comes from *motar rekikei minchos kohanim* — leftover oil from the wafer-type *mincha* of a *kohen*. The difference from the first case is that a *kohen*'s *mincha* is entirely burned; nothing is eaten. So the same surplus oil that would be eaten in the Israelite case is here placed on the fire. Additionally, *u'minchat kohen mashiach* — the *mincha* of the *kohen gadol*, which he brings daily and which involves a disproportionately large quantity of oil relative to the flour, such that much of the oil cannot be incorporated into the baking. Since it is a *kohen*'s *mincha*, it all goes on the fire.

### Nedava Oil

*Rabbi Shimon* closes by stating *she'ein misnadvim shemen* — it is not possible to bring oil as a voluntary *nedava* offering on its own. The *Torah* does not recognize oil as an independent *korban*.

*Rabbi Tarfon* disagrees: *misnadvim shemen* — one may indeed bring oil as a *nedava*. The *gemara* will now explore where this is learned and what its practical implications are.

## Oil Source

### Mincha Model

*Shmuel* explains *Rabbi Tarfon*'s position: *lidivrei Rabbi Tarfon hamisnadeiv shemen, komtzo* — if one brings a *nedava* of oil, one takes a *kometz* from the oil, burns it, *ve'shiyurav ne'echalim* — and the remainder is eaten. The model is thus the *mincha*: just as a *mincha* has a *kometz* burned on the *mizbeach* and the *shirayim* eaten by the *kohanim*, the same applies to oil as a *nedava*.

### Korban Mincha

The source: the verse says *korban mincha* — the word *korban* is superfluous. The *Torah* could have simply said *mincha*. The addition of *korban* teaches that there is something else which can constitute its own *korban* in the manner of a *mincha* — and from this, the *gemara* derives that oil may be brought as a *nedava*. *Melamed shemisnadvim shemen.* The *binyan av* then follows: just as a *mincha* involves a *kometz* burned and the remainder eaten, so too oil — *mah mincha kometz ve'shiyurav ne'echalim, af shemen kometz ve'shiyurav ne'echalim*.

*Rav Zeira* adds that this is already implicit in the *mishna* itself. *Rabbi Shimon*'s statement that the oil eaten by *kohanim* comes specifically from the *rekik* surplus or the *metzora*'s *log* — and not from a *nedava* — carries the implicit *miklal* that according to *Rabbi Tarfon*, who allows a *nedava* of oil, such a *nedava* would indeed be eaten. For *Rav Zeira*, both scenarios in the *mishna* — oil eaten and oil burned — map onto *Rabbi Tarfon*'s view: the *kometz* is burned and the *shirayim* are eaten.

*Abaye* challenges this by pointing to the second scenario: the *mishna* also says that oil placed on the fire is not from a *nedava*, carrying the implication that according to *Rabbi Tarfon*, a *nedava* of oil would go on the fire entirely. If so, *Rabbi Tarfon* is only contesting the second scenario (burning), and his *nedava* of oil is entirely burned — not a case of *kometz* and *shirayim*. This is *Abaye*'s position.

The *gemara* then resolves the tension for *Rav Zeira*: the *mishna* lists both scenarios — eaten and burned — and *Rabbi Tarfon*'s *nedava* in his view covers both: some is burned (the *kometz*) and the rest is eaten (*shirayim*). The *mishna* reads naturally for *Rav Zeira*. For *Abaye*, however, there is a difficulty with the first scenario, since his *Rabbi Tarfon* would not have a *nedava* that is eaten. The *gemara* resolves this by saying *tani reisha atu seifa* — the *mishna* introduces the first scenario as background and context for the second, which is where the actual debate lies. *Amrei be-ma'arava*: in *Eretz Yisrael* it was said that *tani reisha mishum seifa* — sometimes a *mishna* leads with the undisputed case in order to set up the disputed one. This is actually coherent: here is scenario one, about which everyone agrees; now here is scenario two, about which *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Tarfon* disagree.

The debate between *Rav Zeira* and *Abaye*, then, is about the character of *Rabbi Tarfon*'s *nedava*: is it like a *mincha* (partial burning, partial eating), or is it like an *olah* (fully burned)?

The *gemara* then introduces the question of whether wine may also be offered as a *nedava*, citing *Rabbi Akiva*'s position that *yayin ke-divrei Rabbi Akiva le-sfalim* — according to *Rabbi Akiva*, wine may be brought as a *nedava* and is poured down the *sefalim* (the temple drain funnels). This prompts the question: if wine as a *nedava* goes entirely *le-sefalim*, does that mean oil as a *nedava* goes entirely onto the fire? *Mai lav mi-de-yayin kulo le-sfalim shemen kulo la-ishim?* The *gemara* rejects this inference: *mide irya? Ha ke-dita ve-ha ke-dita* — each case operates by its own rules. The fact that wine all goes through the funnels does not compel the conclusion that all the oil goes on the fire. Some of the oil going on the fire is fully compatible with the rest of the oil being eaten.

### Context Question

*Rav Papa* then frames the debate as a *machloket tannaim*, introducing the famous hermeneutical distinction of *don mina u'mina* versus *don mina u'uki be'atrah*. When one learns a *halacha* from a source, does one take the entire contextual framework of the source (*don mina u'mina* — learn from it and maintain its context), or does one apply the single derived *halacha* to the new case and then allow the new case's own context to determine all further implications (*don mina u'uki be'atrah* — learn from it and establish it in its own place)? This is the question of whether, once *Torah* learning bridges from one domain to another, the import retains its origin or assimilates into its destination — a choice between what one might call the preservation of original identity versus assimilation into the new context.

### One Log

The *Rabbanan* hold: since the *nedava* of oil is learned from *korban mincha* (derived from the superfluous word *korban* in that verse), the entire framework of *mincha* governs. The amount of oil in a *mincha* is one *log*; therefore the minimum for an oil *nedava* is one *log*. And since one maintains the original *mincha* context, the mode of offering is also that of *mincha*: a *kometz* is burned and the *shirayim* are eaten. This is *don mina u'mina* — take the source and keep its context.

### Three Logs

*Rabbi* holds that the minimum is three *log*. Three *log* is the minimum amount of oil associated with *minchat nesachim*, since a *revi'it ha-hin* of wine accompanies that offering, and a *hin* is twelve *log*, making *revi'it ha-hin* equal to three *log*. If the operative comparison is to liquids offered on the *mizbeach*, then like wine — which all goes through the *sefalim* — oil would all go on the fire. This is *don mina u'uki be'atrah*: you learn the principle of oil as *nedava* from *mincha*, but then the oil assimilates into the context of liquid offerings, and liquid offerings are offered in their entirety.

## Rav Papa Framework

### Source Debate

*Rav Papa* thus frames the dispute: *Rabbanan* derive the *nedava* of oil from the *mincha* context and maintain that context throughout — one *log*, *kometz* burned, remainder eaten. *Rabbi* derives the quantity from the liquid-offering context and applies the rules of liquid offerings — three *log*, all burned. The *machloket* is fundamentally about which source governs not only the fact of the *nedava* but the entire structure of how it is offered.

### Baraita Evidence

A challenge to *Rav Papa*'s framework comes from a *baraita*: *korban mincha melamed she-nitnadav im shemen ve-chamah shloshah login* — a *baraita* states that we learn from *korban mincha* that oil may be brought as a *nedava*, and specifies three *log* as the amount. *Man tana de-amar shloshah login? Rabbi* — it is *Rabbi* who says three *log*. But this *baraita* shows *Rabbi* learning from *korban mincha* as the source — yet arriving at three *log*. If *Rav Papa*'s framework is correct, *Rabbi*'s three *log* should be derived from wine/nesachim, not from *mincha*. This appears to contradict *Rav Papa*'s analysis.

### Mincha Source

*Rav Papa* concedes the point from the *baraita*: *tanya tanya* — you have a *baraita*, I cannot argue with that. The *baraita* shows that even *Rabbi* learns from *korban mincha* as the textual source, and therefore *Rav Papa*'s original framing — that *Rabbi* learns directly from wine — cannot be correct. Rather, the debate is not about *where* one learns the *nedava* of oil (both learn it from *korban mincha*), but about whether, once learned, the framework of *mincha* is maintained (*don mina u'mina*, yielding one *log* and *kometz* with remainder eaten), or whether it assimilates into the context of liquid offerings (*don mina u'uki be'atrah*, yielding three *log* and all burned). *Rabbi* learns from *mincha* but then applies the law *ba'atrah* — in the place of liquid offerings — and thus arrives at three *log* and full burning. *Rabbanan* maintain the *mincha* context throughout and arrive at one *log* with *kometz* and *shirayim*.
