# Sotah 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Egla Arufa & Sota

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָרוֹצְחִין כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָרוֹצְחָנִין — בָּטְלָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָהּ בָּאָה אֶלָּא עַל הַסָּפֵק. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָרוֹצְחָנִין בְּגָלוּי — בָּטְלָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when murderers proliferated,** the ritual of the heifer whose neck is broken was nullified. **The Sages taught: From** the time **when murderers proliferated,** the ritual of **the heifer whose neck is broken was nullified, because it comes only for** a case involving **uncertainty** with regard to the identity of the murderer. Therefore, **when there was an increase of murderers** acting **openly** so that their identities were known, the ritual of **the heifer whose neck is broken was nullified.**

The daf opens with a *mishnah* establishing that when murderers increased, the practice of *egla arufa* — the beheaded heifer brought in cases of an unsolved murder — was discontinued. The *Tanna* explains the reasoning directly: *egla arufa* was instituted only for cases of doubt, where the killer was unknown. Once murderers were acting openly and brazenly, killing with impunity in full public view, the entire institutional logic of the ritual collapsed. Even if a particular case remained technically unsolved, a society in which murder was rampant and unpunished could no longer sustain the moral outrage and communal responsibility that *egla arufa* was designed to express.

### Sota Discontinued

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הַנּוֹאֲפִין כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְנִקָּה הָאִישׁ מֵעָוֹן״, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָאִישׁ מְנוּקֶּה מֵעָוֹן — הַמַּיִם בּוֹדְקִין אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, אֵין הָאִישׁ מְנוּקֶּה מֵעָוֹן — אֵין הַמַּיִם בּוֹדְקִין אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. וְאוֹמֵר: ״לֹא אֶפְקוֹד עַל בְּנוֹתֵיכֶם כִּי תִזְנֶינָה כּוּ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also taught that **from** the time **when adulterers proliferated,** the performance of the ritual of the bitter water of a *sota* was nullified. **The Sages taught:** It states: **"And the man shall be cleared of transgression,** and that woman shall bear her transgression" (Numbers 5:31), which indicates that **when the man is clear of transgression the waters evaluate** if **his wife** was unfaithful, but **if the man is not clear of transgression the waters do not evaluate** if **his wife** was unfaithful. **And it states: "I will not punish your daughters when they commit harlotry,** nor your daughters-in-law when they commit adultery; for they consort with lewd women, and they sacrifice with prostitutes; and the people that is without understanding is distraught" (Hosea 4:14).

Similarly, the *mishnah* records that when adulterers multiplied, the *sota* water ordeal was discontinued — tying the discussion back to the central theme of the tractate. A *beraita* is cited: *venizka ha'isha me'avon bizman she'ish menuke me'avon* — the waters test the wife only when the husband himself is clean from sin.

### Husband's Purity Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא עָוֹן דִּידֵיהּ — אִין, דִּבְנֵיהּ וְדִבְנָתֵיהּ — לָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״לֹא אֶפְקוֹד עַל בְּנוֹתֵיכֶם כִּי תִזְנֶינָה וְעַל כַּלּוֹתֵיכֶם כִּי תְנָאַפְנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is** the purpose of the addition of: **And it states?** What is lacking in the exposition from the verse of the Torah? The Gemara explains: **And if you would say** that based on the verse: "And the man shall be cleared of transgression," the *halakha* would be that with regard to **his transgression, yes,** it will cause the waters to be ineffective, but the transgression **of his sons and daughters** does **not** impact the effectiveness, **come** and **hear** the verse: **"I will not punish your daughters,"** i.e., I will not punish your wives, due to your daughters, **"when they commit harlotry, nor your daughters-in-law when they commit adultery."**

What is striking here is the scope of "clean from sin." When this principle was discussed earlier in the tractate, the focus was specifically on the husband having violated the laws of *sota* — for example, by continuing to live with his wife after she had been warned. Here, however, the *Gemara* broadens the requirement to encompass any type of sin. The *beraita* cites the verse: *lo efkod al benoseichem ki tiznena* — "I will not punish your daughters when they fornicate" — with the continuation of the verse making clear that the reason God withholds punishment is that the men themselves are running after prostitutes.

### Sexual Sin Affects Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: עֲוֹן אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ — אִין, עָוֹן דִּפְנוּיָה — לָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״כִּי הֵם עִם הַזֹּנוֹת יְפָרֵדוּ וְעִם הַקְּדֵשׁוֹת יְזַבֵּחוּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say:** With regard to the **transgression of** adultery with **a married woman, yes,** it will cause the waters to be ineffective, but the **transgression of** one who engaged in sexual intercourse with **an unmarried woman** does **not** impact the effectiveness, **come** and **hear** the continuation of the verse: **"For they consort with lewd women, and they sacrifice with prostitutes."**

The *Gemara* then anticipates a series of potential limitations. One might argue that only the husband's own sexual transgression disqualifies the water test, but not the sins of his sons or daughters. The verse, however, speaks of both daughters and daughters-in-law — *ve'al kaloseichem ki tena'afena* — extending the husband's responsibility to encompass the sexual conduct of his household members. A further objection suggests that perhaps only adultery (*avon eshet ish*) disqualifies the test, not general sexual immorality with an unmarried woman. The verse again responds: *ki heim im hazonot yepareidu* — "for they go off with prostitutes" — which is ordinary sexual violation rather than technical adultery, yet it is precisely this that renders the *mayim ha-marim* inoperative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְעָם לֹא יָבִין יִלָּבֵט״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמַר לָהֶם נָבִיא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: אִם אַתֶּם מַקְפִּידִין עַל עַצְמְכֶם — מַיִם בּוֹדְקִין נְשׁוֹתֵיכֶם, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין הַמַּיִם בּוֹדְקִין נְשׁוֹתֵיכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara turns its attention to the end of the verse. **What is** the meaning of: **"And the people that is without understanding is distraught"? Rabbi Elazar says: The prophet said to the Jewish people: If you are particular about yourselves, the water evaluates your wives; but if not, the water does not evaluate your wives.** This would make people distraught, as they would not know how to overcome their suspicion if they are concerned that their wives have been unfaithful.

The *Gemara* then cites Rabbi Elazar's reading of the prophet's message to Israel: if you are careful about your own conduct, the waters will test your wives; if not, they will not. It is worth noting that *Rashi* reads *yepareidu* as deriving from *pered*, a mule — signifying habitual, animal-like fornication.

## Justice Perverted

### Pleasure & Perversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ בַּעֲלֵי הֲנָאָה, נִתְעַוְּתוּ הַדִּינִין וְנִתְקַלְקְלוּ הַמַּעֲשִׂים וְאֵין נוֹחַ בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when those who accept benefit** from others **proliferated, the laws became twisted and deeds became corrupted, and there was no comfort in the world.**

The *mishnah* shifts to a broader catalogue of societal decline and its institutional consequences. When *ba'alei hana'ah* increased — people who sought ease and pleasure, who preferred comfort to rigorous effort — justice became perverted. *Rashi* clarifies that this does not refer to extreme hedonism, but simply to people who preferred to take life a little too easy, who were unwilling to do the hard work that genuine adjudication demands. From this complacency, *nit'avu hadinim* — judgments were distorted — and *venitkalkelu hama'asim* — actions in general became corrupted — since people sensed they could act with impunity. The result: *ve'ein no'ach ba'olam* — there is no pleasantness in the world, which *Rashi* interprets as God no longer taking pleasure in the world.

What is striking about this entire catalogue is that it focuses overwhelmingly on the corruption of justice and the courts, not on ritual violations or even general moral failures. The point seems to be that once the justice system is compromised — once judges can be influenced, flattered, or financially entangled — the entire fabric of society unravels. The *dayan* and the *beit din* send the decisive signal to society both practically and symbolically, and their failure reverberates through every domain.

### Partiality in Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ רוֹאֵי פָנִים בַּדִּין, בָּטַל ״לֹא תָגוּרוּ״, וּפָסַק ״לֹא תַכִּירוּ״, וּפָרְקוּ עוֹל שָׁמַיִם וְנָתְנוּ עֲלֵיהֶם עוֹל בָּשָׂר וָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when those who look at** the **faces** of the litigants **in judgment,** in order to rule based on the appearance of the litigants, **proliferated,** the fulfillment of the verse: **"You shall not fear** the face of any man" (Deuteronomy 1:17), **ceased, and** the fulfillment of the verse: **"You shall not respect** faces in judgment" (Deuteronomy 1:17), **halted, and they removed the yoke of Heaven** from themselves, **and placed upon themselves the yoke of flesh and blood.**

When *roei panim* increased in judgment — judges who showed favor to litigants, not necessarily through outright bribery but through personal recognition and partiality — the prohibition *lo taguru mipnei ish* ceased to operate, and the injunction *lo takiru panim* was abandoned. Judges became responsive to who stood before them rather than to the demands of *din emet*. The consequence: they cast off the yoke of heaven and took upon themselves the yoke of *basar vadam* — flesh and blood — since the faces of the litigants now exerted more influence on their rulings than the awareness that they were conducting *din shamayim* and would answer to God.

### Lawyers' Influence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ לוֹחֲשֵׁי לְחִישׁוֹת בַּדִּין — רָבָה חֲרוֹן אַף בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְנִסְתַּלְּקָה הַשְּׁכִינָה, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים יִשְׁפֹּט״.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when those who whisper whisperings in judgment,** advising judges surreptitiously, **proliferated, fierce anger proliferated in Israel, and the Divine Presence departed, because it is stated:** "God stands in the congregation of God; **in the midst of the judges He judges"** (Psalms 82:1). The Divine Presence that dwells among judges leaves if they judge improperly.

When *lochashei lechishot b'din* increased — those who whispered in court — divine anger intensified among Israel and the *Shechinah* withdrew. *Rashi* here clarifies that these are not practitioners of incantation but rather the legal advocates, *orchei dayanim*, who would whisper into the ears of the judges, attempting to find them an angle of acquittal for their client. The verse is invoked: *b'kerev Elohim yishpot* — judgment is conducted in the presence of God — and when it is subverted through this kind of behind-the-scenes manipulation, God is, as it were, removed from the proceeding.

### Profit Over Truth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ ״אַחֲרֵי בִצְעָם לִבָּם הֹלֵךְ״ — רַבּוּ ״הָאוֹמְרִים לָרַע טוֹב וְלַטּוֹב רָע״. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ ״הָאוֹמְרִים לָרַע טוֹב וְלַטּוֹב רָע״ — רַבּוּ ״הוֹי הוֹי״ בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when** those who are referred to in the verse: **"Their heart goes after their covetousness"** (Ezekiel 33:31), **proliferated, "Those who say to evil good, and to good evil"** (Isaiah 5:20) **proliferated,** i.e., those who treat wicked people as though they were righteous proliferated as a result. **From** the time **when** the fulfillment of the verse: **"Those who say to evil good, and to good evil," proliferated,** the cry of: **Woe, woe, proliferated in the world.** There was an increase in troubles that cause people to cry out.

When people's hearts followed after gain — *acharei bitzam libam holech* — the *Gemara* records that *rabu ha'omrim lara tov velatov ra*: people began calling bad good and good bad. The dynamic is recognizable: when profit is the dominant motivation, truth becomes a casualty, flattery replaces honest speech, and people say whatever is needed to get ahead. The *pasuk* in the *navi* continues with *hoy hoy ba'olam* — woe, woe to the world — and this, the *mishnah* observes, became the prevailing condition.

### Haughtiness & Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ מוֹשְׁכֵי הָרוֹק — רַבּוּ הַיְּהִירִים וְנִתְמַעֲטוּ הַתַּלְמִידִים, וְהַתּוֹרָה חוֹזֶרֶת עַל לוֹמְדֶיהָ. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הַיְּהִירִים — הִתְחִילוּ בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהִנָּשֵׂא לִיהִירִים, שֶׁאֵין דּוֹרֵינוּ רוֹאֶה אֶלָּא לַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when those who** show their arrogance by **drawing** out **spittle proliferated,** the number of **haughty people** in general **proliferated, and the** number of **students decreased,** as they would say haughtily that there was nothing left for them to learn, **and the Torah** needs to **go around to** seek **those who study it,** as people do not learn of their own initiative. Furthermore, **from** the time **when haughty people proliferated, the daughters of Israel began marrying haughty men, as our generation looks only at the face,** i.e., the external aspects of a person, and ignores the inner aspects of a person.

When *moshchei harok* increased — those who draw out their speech in an act of self-important haughtiness — *rabu hayehirim*, the arrogant multiplied, and *venitma'atu hatalmidim*, Torah scholars decreased. *Rashi* invokes the principle that haughtiness is a sign of Torah poverty: *siman legasut haruach aniyut shel Torah*. Perhaps the connection is that when people are focused on external performance and on projecting status, the genuine internal investment that Torah learning demands is neglected. Consequently, *vehaTorah chozeret al lomdeha* — the Torah goes searching for those who will truly devote themselves to it.

The social consequence follows directly: when the haughty multiplied, *hitchilu bnot Yisrael lehinase layehirim* — women began marrying such men — because *she'ein dorenu ro'eh ela lifnim*, the generation sees only the external presentation and cannot perceive whether the status on display has been genuinely earned.

### Haughtiness Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר מָר: הַאי מַאן דְּמִיַּהַר — אֲפִילּוּ אַאִינָשֵׁי בֵּיתֵיהּ לָא מִיקַּבַּל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גֶּבֶר יָהִיר וְלֹא יִנְוֶה״, לֹא יִנְוֶה אֲפִילּוּ בַּנָּוֶה שֶׁלּוֹ! מֵעִיקָּרָא קָפְצָה עֲלֵיהּ, לְסוֹף מִיתְּזִיל עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Is that so?** Do women wish to marry arrogant men? **But didn't the Master say: One who is haughty is not even accepted by the members of his household, as it is stated: "The haughty man abides not"** (Habakkuk 2:5)? **"Abides [*yinaveh*] not"** means that **even in his abode [*naveh*],** he is not accepted. The Gemara explains: **Initially, she jumps at** the chance to marry **him,** because he appears to be a great person to her, but **in the end,** once she gets to know him, **he is demeaned in her eyes.**

The *Gemara* raises an obvious objection. We have a principle: *hai man demayahir afilu inish beiteih lo mikabli leih* — someone who is haughty is not even tolerated by the members of his own household. The verse is cited: *gever yahir velo yanveh* — the haughty man cannot even dwell in his own home, because his household sees through him and will not abide him. How then can it be said that women are attracted to such men?

The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *me'ikara kaftza leih* — initially, before the man is known, he appears distinguished and is eagerly sought in marriage. *Ulevasof mitzla leih* — but once he is married and his wife comes to know him for what he truly is, he becomes lowly in her eyes. The attraction and the disillusionment are sequential, not contradictory.

### Judges' Investments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ מַטִּילֵי מְלַאי עַל בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים — רָבָה הַשּׁוֹחַד וְהַטָּיַית מִשְׁפָּט, וּפָסְקָה טוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From** the time **when there was an increase** in those who **placed upon homeowners** the obligation to designate the profits from **merchandise** for the upkeep of judges, **bribery and corruption of judgment proliferated and good ceased.**

When *matilei melai al ba'alei batim* increased — when judges began placing their money with businessmen to invest on their behalf — bribery increased and justice was perverted. The problem, it should be noted, is not anonymized investment in a general fund; it is the specific entanglement between a judge and particular businessmen who offer to handle his money as a personal favor. The judge who benefits from such an arrangement cannot adjudicate impartially in any dispute touching those businessmen or their interests. The result: *raba hashochar*, bribery increased, *vehatayas mishpat*, justice was tilted, *vepaska tova*, and all good ceased.

### Acceptance of Favors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ ״מְקַבְּלֵנִי טוֹבָתְךָ״ וּ״מְחַזְּקֵנִי טוֹבוֹתֶיךָ״ — רַבּוּ ״אִישׁ הַיָּשָׁר בְּעֵינָיו יַעֲשֶׂה״. שְׁפָלִים הוּגְבְּהוּ, וְהַגְּבוֹהִים הוּשְׁפְּלוּ, וּמַלְכוּתָא אָזְלָא וְנָוְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when** those judges and leaders who say: **I accept your favor, and: I hold your favor, proliferated,** the fulfillment of the verse: **"Every man did that which was right in his eyes"** (Judges 17:6), **proliferated. Lowly ones were raised and lofty ones were lowered, and the monarchy is increasingly on the decline.**

When people multiplied who would say — in a context of judges and community members — "thank you for the favor you did for me," acknowledging and accepting kindnesses from those who might come before them in court, the consequence was *ish hayashar be'einav ya'aseh* — everyone acted as they saw fit, secure in the knowledge that the judges were indebted to them. *Shfalim hugbehu vehagvohim hushpelu* — the low were raised up and the high brought low — and *malchusa azla venavla*, governance collapsed entirely.

### Stinginess & Robbery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ צָרֵי עַיִן וְטוֹרְפֵי טֶרֶף — רַבּוּ מְאַמְּצֵי הַלֵּב וְקוֹפְצֵי יָדַיִם מִלְּהַלְווֹת, וְעָבְרוּ עַל מַה שֶּׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה ״הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ פֶּן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when misers and those greedy for profit proliferated, those hardened of heart and who closed their hands from lending proliferated, and they transgressed that which is written in the Torah:** "You shall not harden your heart, nor shut your hand from your needy brother…**Guard yourself in case** there is a base thought in your heart…and you do not give him" (Deuteronomy 15:7, 9).

When the stingy (*tsarei ayin*) and those who seize what belongs to others (*torfei teref* — *Rashi* reads this simply as *gazlanim*, robbers) increased, *rabu ma'amitsei halev* — those of hardened heart multiplied — and *vekofsei hayadayim*, those who close their hands against lending, increased. The *Gemara* connects this explicitly to the Torah's warning: *hishamer lecha pen yihyeh im levavcha* — be careful that your heart does not harbor the miserly thought that withholds a loan, especially as *shemita* approaches. Whether stinginess produces robbery or the two are manifestations of the same underlying character, the *Gemara* groups them together as forces that produce a society of closed hearts and closed hands.

### Seductive Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ ״נְטוּיוֹת גָּרוֹן וּמְשַׂקְּרוֹת עֵינָיִם״ — רַבּוּ מַיִם הַמָּרִים, אֶלָּא שֶׁפָּסְקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when** women with **"stretched-forth necks and wanton eyes"** (Isaiah 3:16) **proliferated, the bitter waters** of a *sota* **proliferated,** as more people were suspected of committing adultery; **but they** eventually **ceased** when licentiousness became too widespread.

When *netuyot garon umeshakrot einayim* increased — women of outstretched necks and beckoning eyes, acting in a manner calculated to attract male attention — *rabu mayim hamarim* should, in theory, have increased as well, since the prevalence of such behavior would presumably generate more cases of suspected adultery warranting the *sota* ordeal. Instead, *pasku* — the ordeal ceased — because by that point the practice had already been suspended: the husbands themselves were not clean from sin, rendering the bitter water inoperative in any case.

### Gift-Taking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ מְקַבְּלֵי מַתָּנוֹת — נִתְמַעֲטוּ הַיָּמִים וְנִתְקַצְּרוּ הַשָּׁנִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשׂוֹנֵא מַתָּנוֹת יִחְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when those who accept gifts proliferated, the days decreased and the years shortened, as it is written: "And he who hates gifts will live"** (Proverbs 15:27).

When those who accept gifts (*mekablei matanos*) increased, *nitma'atu hayamim veniktseru hashanim* — days diminished and years shortened. People lived shorter lives. The proof-text is direct: *veson'eh matanos yichyeh* — only one who hates gifts will live. It is notable that this consequence — shortened lifespan — is among the more overtly divine punishments in this catalogue, most of which operate through more recognizable social and political mechanisms.

### Arrogance & Disputes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ זְחוּחֵי הַלֵּב — רַבּוּ מַחֲלוֹקֹת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי שַׁמַּאי וְהִילֵּל שֶׁלֹּא שִׁימְּשׁוּ כׇּל צוֹרְכָּן — רַבּוּ מַחְלוֹקוֹת בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְנַעֲשֵׂית תּוֹרָה כִּשְׁתֵּי תוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **From** the time **when those with boastful [*zeḥuḥei*] hearts proliferated, dispute proliferated in Israel. From** the time **when the students of Shammai and Hillel who did not serve** their Rabbis **sufficiently proliferated, dispute proliferated in Israel, and the Torah became like two Torahs.**

When the arrogant of heart (*zechuchei halev*) increased, *rabu machlokes b'Yisrael* — disputes among Israel multiplied — because students no longer devoted their full energies to attending upon their teachers and mastering what they were being taught. The *mishnah* then makes this explicit with the famous statement: when the disciples of Shammai and Hillel who had not adequately served their teachers (*shelo shimshu kol tzorkan*) increased, debates multiplied and *na'asu Torah kishetei Toros* — the Torah became as though it were two Torahs. Until that moment, there had been virtually nothing debated in *Torah she-ba'al peh*; after it, the great controversies that characterize Talmudic literature took hold.

## Yosi ben Yoezer

### Eshkol Ceased

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁמֵּת יוֹסֵי בֶּן יוֹעֶזֶר כּוּ׳. מַאי ״אֶשְׁכּוֹלוֹת״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, אִישׁ שֶׁהַכֹּל בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **from** the time **when Yosei ben Yo'ezer died** the clusters ceased. The Gemara poses a question: **What is** the meaning of **clusters [*eshkolot*]? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** It means **a man who contains all [*ish shehakol bo*]** elements of Torah and mitzvot.

When Yosi ben Yoezer died, *batlu ha-eshkolos* — the *eshkolot* ceased. The *Gemara* asks: what is an *eshkol*? Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel explains: *ish she-hakol bo* — a person in whom everything is contained, a completely rounded *talmid chacham*. This connects directly to what was just said: the era of undisputed transmission ended, the unbroken *Mesorah* was disrupted — or even if not every *halacha* had been transmitted explicitly by *Mesorah*, there had been sufficient unanimity and consensus that any given question could be settled. After Yosi ben Yoezer's death, the great debates began.

### Yochanan Abolishes Viduy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹחָנָן כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל הֶעֱבִיר הוֹדָיַית הַמַּעֲשֵׂר כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further taught that **Yoḥanan the High Priest took away the declaration of the tithe.** The Gemara poses a question: **What is the reason** he did this?

The *mishnah* turns to the reforms of Yochanan the High Priest. He abolished the *viduy ma'aser* — the declaration made when presenting the tithe distribution after the three-year cycle, in which one attests to having distributed correctly.

### Ma'aser to Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ כְּתִיקּוּנוֹ. דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר דְּיָהֲבִי לִלְוִיִּם, וַאֲנַן קָא יָהֲבִינַן לְכֹהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Because they did not give** the tithe **in the proper manner** as stated by the Torah. In what way is that? **As the Merciful One states** in the Torah **that they should give** the first tithe **to the Levites,** **and we give** it **to the priests.**

The *Gemara* asks: what was his reason? One might have assumed the answer fits naturally into the theme of societal decline — perhaps people were no longer distributing correctly and the declaration had become a fiction. But Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina gives a different reason entirely: the problem was not individual dishonesty but a systemic departure from the Torah's prescription. The Torah requires *ma'aser rishon* to be given to *Levi'im* — *de-rachmana amar di-yehavi le-Levi'im* — but the practice had become to give it to *kohanim* instead. This shift had come about as a *kenas*, a penalty, against the *Levi'im* who had not returned to the land in the time of Ezra. Since *kohanim* are themselves *Levi'im*, the technical obligation was technically satisfied — but the declaration *vegam netativ la-Levi* could no longer be recited honestly, because the *ma'aser* was manifestly not going to the *Levi'im* as the Torah had specified.

What is remarkable about this juxtaposition is the way it complicates the preceding narrative of decline. Throughout that narrative, societal corruption produces institutional failure. Here, however, it is a *takkanah* of *Chazal* that caused the divergence from the Torah's ideal — and the reason for that *takkanah* was itself rooted in an earlier failure, the *Levi'im*'s failure to return with Ezra. Even five hundred years before the events under discussion, cracks were already appearing in the edifice of ideal Torah observance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹדֵי אַשְּׁאָר מַעַשְׂרוֹת? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כׇּל בַּיִת שֶׁאֵין מִתְוַדֶּה עַל מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן — שׁוּב אֵין מִתְוַדֶּה עַל שְׁאָר מַעַשְׂרוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הוֹאִיל וּפָתַח בּוֹ הַכָּתוּב תְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let him** at least **declare** that he donated **the other tithes** in the proper manner. **Reish Lakish said: Any house that does not state the declaration about** the **first tithe can no longer state the declaration of the other** types of **tithes.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? **Abaye said: Because the written** verse **began** the declaration **with** the tithe given to the Levites: "And I also gave it to the Levite, and to the stranger, to the orphan, and to the widow, in accordance with all Your mitzvot that You commanded me" (Deuteronomy 26:13). If he cannot say the first part of the declaration, he cannot say the rest.

The *Gemara* raises a further question: if the *viduy* was blocked only by the first clause — *vegam netativ la-Levi* — why not recite the rest of the *viduy* formula while omitting that clause? Reish Lakish explains: a *viduy* that cannot begin with *ma'aser rishon* cannot continue with the rest of the *ma'asrot*. Abaye identifies the reason: since the *katuv* opens with *ma'aser rishon*, a declaration that cannot include it cannot proceed at all.

### Demai Ordinance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְלָל דְּאַפְרוֹשֵׁי הֲווֹ מַפְרְשִׁי? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: אַף הוּא בִּיטֵּל אֶת הַוִּידּוּי, וְגָזַר עַל הַדְּמַאי. לְפִי שֶׁשָּׁלַח בְּכׇל גְּבוּל יִשְׂרָאֵל וְרָאָה שֶׁאֵין מַפְרִישִׁין אֶלָּא תְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה בִּלְבַד, וּמַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי — מִקְצָתָן מְעַשְּׂרִין וּמִקְצָתָן אֵין מְעַשְּׂרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: The fact that Rabbi Yoḥanan canceled the declaration of tithes proves **by inference that they would separate** tithes in his days. **But isn't it taught** (*Tosefta* 13:10): **He,** Yoḥanan the High Priest, **also canceled the declaration** of tithes **and decreed with regard to doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*]?** Why did he issue this decree? **Because he sent** messengers **throughout** the land, to **all the borders of** Eretz **Yisrael** to investigate, **and saw that they would separate only *teruma gedola*, and** as for **first tithe and second tithe, some** people **would tithe and some** people **would not tithe.**

The *Gemara* adds that Yochanan also instituted the law of *demai* — the requirement that produce purchased from an *am ha'aretz* be treated as doubtful with respect to tithe separation.

### Am Ha'aretz Practices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: בָּנַי, בּוֹאוּ וְאוֹמַר לָכֶם: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁתְּרוּמָה גְּדוֹלָה יֵשׁ בָּהּ עֲוֹן מִיתָה — כָּךְ תְּרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר וְטֶבֶל יֵשׁ בָּהֶן עֲוֹן מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: My sons, come and I will tell you** something: **Just as** the *halakhot* of ***teruma gedola*** include **a transgression punishable by death** at the hand of God, as one who ate produce from which *teruma* has not been separated is punished with death from Heaven, **so too,** the ***teruma* of the tithe,** the portion the Levites must separate from their first tithe and give to priests, **and untithed produce,** these include **a transgression punishable by death** at the hand of God, if the produce is eaten without the tithes having been taken.

The reason was empirical. Yochanan sent agents throughout all the borders of Israel and observed that *ammei ha'aretz* were routinely separating only *terumah gedolah*, while *ma'aser rishon* and *ma'aser sheni* were observed inconsistently — some separating them, some not. They assumed that *terumah gedolah* required attention, since eating it untithed is punishable by death at divine hands, but regarded the other obligations as less urgent.

### Heavenly Warnings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַד וְהִתְקִין לָהֶם: הַלּוֹקֵחַ פֵּירוֹת מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ — מַפְרִישׁ מֵהֶן מַעֲשֵׂר רִאשׁוֹן וּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Realizing that it was uncertain with regard to whether or not people were separating tithes, **he arose and instituted an ordinance for them** with regard to doubtfully tithed produce: **One who purchases produce from an *am ha'aretz*,** which may or may not have been tithed, **must separate from** the produce **first tithe and second tithe** due to the uncertainty as to whether or not the *am ha'aretz* separated them.

Yochanan gathered the people and delivered a striking teaching: just as *terumah gedolah* carries the sin of death, so too does *terumat ma'aser* and *tevel* — grain from which *terumat ma'aser* has not been taken. The underlying logic is precise: if one does not separate *ma'aser rishon*, the *terumat ma'aser* that the *Levi* would have been obligated to separate from that *ma'aser* remains embedded within the produce — and that *terumat ma'aser* renders the entire quantity *tevel* subject to the *chiyyuv mitah*. So the *am ha'aretz* who neglects *ma'aser rishon* is not merely failing to give the *Levi* his due; he is leaving the buyer's produce in a state of genuine *tevel*.

The question then arises: to whom exactly was Yochanan delivering this message? A close reading of *Rashi* suggests it was addressed not to the *ammei ha'aretz* themselves but to the *chaverim* — the observant scholars who were purchasing from them. They were the ones who needed to understand that when they bought produce from an *am ha'aretz*, they could not assume proper tithe separation had occurred; the obligation to complete the separation rested with them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְתֵּי תַּקֵּן: בִּיטֵּל וִידּוּי דַּחֲבֵירִים, וְגָזַר עַל דְּמַאי שֶׁל עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** In any case, the Gemara proves from the *baraita* that not all people would separate tithes in the time of Yoḥanan the High Priest. The Gemara answers: **He instituted two ordinances: He canceled the declaration of tithes of those devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot [*ḥaverim*],** especially *halakhot* of *teruma* and tithes, **and he decreed with regard to doubtfully tithed produce** purchased **from *amei ha'aretz*,** because they may not have separated tithes at all.

The *Gemara* then harmonizes the apparently contradictory accounts — one suggesting that *ammei ha'aretz* were in fact separating all required *ma'asrot*, another suggesting they were not — by concluding that Yochanan established two separate measures: first, he abolished the *viduy* even for *chaverim* separating from their own grain, because the first clause could not honestly be recited; second, he imposed the *demai* ordinance on produce purchased from *ammei ha'aretz*, requiring buyers to complete whatever tithe separations might have been omitted.

## Yochanan's Reforms

### Abolished Wake-Upers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף הוּא בִּיטֵּל אֶת הַמְעוֹרְרִים. מַאי מְעוֹרְרִים? אָמַר רַחֲבָה: בְּכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹמְדִים לְוִיִּם עַל דּוּכָן וְאוֹמְרִים ״עוּרָה לָמָּה תִישַׁן ה׳״, אָמַר לָהֶן: וְכִי יֵשׁ שֵׁינָה לִפְנֵי הַמָּקוֹם? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״הִנֵּה לֹא יָנוּם וְלֹא יִישָׁן שׁוֹמֵר יִשְׂרָאֵל״!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further taught: **He also nullified** the actions of **the awakeners.** The Gemara poses a question: **What** are **awakeners? Raḥava says: On each and every day when the Levites stood on the platform** in the Temple **they** would **say: "Awake, why do you sleep, O Lord?"** (Psalms 44:24). Therefore, they were called awakeners. Yoḥanan the High Priest **said to them: Does the Omnipresent sleep,** that you call upon Him to awaken? **But isn't it already stated: "Behold, He that keeps Israel neither slumbers nor sleeps"** (Psalms 121:4)?

Yochanan the High Priest also abolished the practice of the *me'orerim* — the "wake-upers." Every day the *Levi'im* would ascend the *duchan* and, before their singing, would call out: *urah lamah tishan Hashem* — "Awake, why do You sleep, O God?" Yochanan put an end to this. He argued: *vechi yesh sheinah lifnei hamakom*? Is there sleep before God? Has it not already been stated: *hinei lo yanum velo yishan shomer Yisrael* — "Behold, the Guardian of Israel neither slumbers nor sleeps"?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל שְׁרוּיִין בְּצַעַר וְאוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם בְּנַחַת וְשַׁלְוָה, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״עוּרָה לָמָּה תִישַׁן ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, when the Jewish people are in a state of suffering, and the nations of the world are in** a state of **calm and serenity,** it is **with regard to this** that **it is stated: "Awake, why do you sleep, O Lord?"** If the verse were to be recited every morning it would be interpreted in the wrong way, so Yoḥanan the High Priest therefore canceled the daily recitation of this verse.

His response was not to deny that the verse had a legitimate metaphorical meaning — the *Gemara* acknowledges that *urah lamah tishan* is appropriate language when Israel is suffering and the nations are at ease, as a way of saying, "How can You allow this to continue?" But that is not appropriate as a daily declaration in the *Beit HaMikdash*, and the fact that Yochanan needed to explain it as metaphor implies that the *Levi'im* had been reciting it in something closer to its literal sense. How this practice ever began in the middle of the Second Temple period, when the situation was not necessarily one of acute national tragedy, is a striking and somewhat disturbing question.

### Abolished Scratching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶת הַנּוֹקְפִים. מַאי ״נוֹקְפִים״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שֶׁהָיוּ מְסָרְטִין לָעֵגֶל בֵּין קַרְנָיו, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּפּוֹל דָּם בְּעֵינָיו. אֲתָא אִיהוּ בַּטֵּיל, מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כִּי מוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna also taught that Yoḥanan the High Priest canceled **the strikers.** The Gemara asks: **What** are **strikers? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** They are those **who would scratch the calf** being prepared for slaughter as an offering **between its horns, in order that blood should fall in its eyes,** so that the animal would not see and resist being slaughtered. **He came** and **nullified** this practice, **because it looked like** they are causing **a blemish.**

Yochanan also abolished the *nokfim* — the "bangers." Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel explains: they would scratch the animal between its eyes so that blood would run into them, confusing the animal and making it easier to slaughter without it thrashing. Yochanan abolished this because *demechazi kib'al mum* — it resembles a *mum*, a disqualifying blemish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: שֶׁהָיוּ חוֹבְטִין אוֹתוֹ בְּמַקְלוֹת כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ לִפְנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אָמַר לָהֶם: עַד מָתַי אַתֶּם מַאֲכִילִים נְבֵילוֹת לַמִּזְבֵּחַ! נְבֵילוֹת?! הָא שָׁחֵיט לְהוּ! אֶלָּא טְרֵיפוֹת — שֶׁמָּא נִיקַּב קְרוּם שֶׁל מוֹחַ. עָמַד וְהִתְקִין לָהֶם טַבָּעוֹת בַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** A different explanation of strikers **was taught in a *baraita*** (*Tosefta* 13:9): They are those **who would beat** the calf **with sticks,** to stun it before it was slaughtered, **in the manner that they do it before idols.** Yoḥanan the High Priest **said to them: Until when will you feed unslaughtered animal carcasses to the altar?** The Gemara asks: Are these animal carcasses actually **unslaughtered animal carcasses? They were slaughtered,** i.e., they did not die of their own accord. **Rather,** he said that the beatings would cause them to become **like animals with a wound that will cause them to die within twelve months [*tereifot*],** as **perhaps** these beatings will **perforate the membrane surrounding the brain,** which would make the calf a *tereifa*. **He** therefore **arose and instituted an ordinance for them** to put **rings in the ground** with which they could secure the animals, thereby making it easier to slaughter the animals without having to scratch them between the horns or hit them with sticks.

A *beraita* teaches a related practice: some would strike the animal with sticks — in the manner performed before pagan worship — to stun it before slaughter. Yochanan objected to this as well, decrying it with the words: "How long will you feed *neveilot* to the altar?" The *Gemara* objects: a properly slaughtered animal is not a *neveilah*. The *Gemara* corrects: not *neveilah* but *tereifot* — by striking the animal violently you may have perforated the membrane of the brain (*nikev krum shel moach*), rendering it a *tereifah*. It is worth noting that the *beraita* describes the striking as resembling pagan practice, connecting this concern back to the earlier abolition of the *me'orerim* — both practices had acquired an uncomfortably pagan appearance.

### Rings for Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמַד וְהִתְקִין לָהֶם טַבָּעוֹת בַּקַּרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** He therefore **arose and instituted an ordinance for them** to put **rings in the ground** with which they could secure the animals.

In place of the striking with sticks, Yochanan instituted *tabaot bakarka* — rings fixed in the ground through which the animals' heads could be placed, holding them steady during *shechita* without any risk of injury.

### Blacksmith Hammer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד יָמָיו הָיָה פַּטִּישׁ מַכֶּה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated that **until the days** of Yoḥanan the High Priest **the hammer** of smiths **would strike in Jerusalem.** The Gemara explains: This is referring **to the intermediate days of a Festival.**

He also stopped the sound of the blacksmith's hammer in Jerusalem on *Chol HaMoed*. Until his time, *devar ha'avad* — the principle that work which would cause irretrievable loss may be performed on *Chol HaMoed* — had been applied liberally enough that the hammer of the smithy was audible throughout the city. Yochanan found this too public and too permissive a signal, and he put an end to it.

## Sanhedrin & Song

### Sanhedrin Gone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִשֶּׁבָּטְלָה סַנְהֶדְרִין — בָּטַל הַשִּׁיר מִבֵּית הַמִּשְׁתָּאוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּשִּׁיר לֹא יִשְׁתּוּ יָיִן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna continues with the list of items that were nullified. **From** the time **when the Sanhedrin ceased song was** also **nullified from the places of feasts,** i.e., it was no longer permitted to sing at a feast where wine was served, **as it is stated: "With song they shall not drink wine"** (Isaiah 24:9).

The *mishnah* declares: *mishebatlah Sanhedrin batel hashir mibeit hamishtaot* — when the *Sanhedrin* ceased, song ceased from the drinking houses. The proof-text: *bashir lo yishtu yayin* — "they shall not drink wine with song." This is a striking formulation. The cessation of song is usually associated in the tradition with the *Churban Beit HaMikdash*, but here it is connected specifically to the *Sanhedrin*. The connection is not arbitrary: the entire arc of this section of the tractate has been about the corrupted justice system and the failure of the courts and their authority. The *Sanhedrin* is the apex of that system, and its disappearance extinguishes the institutional structure that had kept society intact — and with it, the capacity for genuine joy.

### Biblical Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גְּמָ׳ וּמִמַּאי דְּמִשֶּׁבָּטְלָה סַנְהֶדְרִי כְּתִיב? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״זְקֵנִים מִשַּׁעַר שָׁבָתוּ בַּחוּרִים מִנְּגִינָתָם״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara poses a question with regard to the first clause of the mishna: **And from where** is it derived **that** the verse: "With song they shall not drink wine" (Isaiah 24:9) **is written** about the period **from** the time **when the Sanhedrin was nullified? Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** From **that** which **the verse states: "The Elders have ceased from the gate, the young men from their music"** (Lamentations 5:14). This indicates that the dissolution of the Sanhedrin, who are the Elders from the gate, is linked to the end of the young men singing.

The *Gemara* then raises the question of whether this statement is purely descriptive — recording that joy ceased when the *Sanhedrin* fell — or also prescriptive, directing that song should not be practiced today.

The verse establishing the connection between the *Sanhedrin* and song is supplied by Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua: *zkeinim misha'ar shavatu bachurim miginatam* — "the elders have ceased from the gate, and the young men from their music." The elders in the gate are the *beit din*, and their departure takes the young men's music with it.

### Song Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אוּדְנָא דְּשָׁמְעָא זִמְרָא — תִּעֲקַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** The **ear that hears song should be uprooted,** as it is prohibited to listen to music after the destruction of the Temple.

Rav then rules prescriptively: *ozna d'shama zimra te'aker* — an ear that hears song nowadays should be uprooted. One should not be listening to music at all, as an expression of mourning and loss. Whether this applies to all song or specifically to instrumental music is not spelled out here, though the term *zemer* could carry connotations of instrumental performance.

### Song Brings Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: זִמְרָא בְּבֵיתָא — חוּרְבָּא בְּסִיפָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קוֹל יְשׁוֹרֵר בַּחַלּוֹן חֹרֶב בַּסַּף כִּי אַרְזָה עֵרָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** If there is **song in a house** there will be **destruction on the threshold, as it is stated: "Voices shall sing in the windows; desolation shall be in the doorposts; for its cedar work shall be uncovered"** (Zephaniah 2:14). The word "uncovered" [*era*] could be read to mean: Its city [*ira*].

Rava adds: *zimra beveita churba beseifa* — song in the household brings destruction at its threshold. The proof is from the verse: *kol yeshorer bachalon chorev basaf* — the sound of singing at the window, destruction at the threshold.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״כִּי אַרְזָה עֵרָה״? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: בַּיִת הַמְסוּבָּךְ בַּאֲרָזִים עִיר הוּא! אֶלָּא: אֲפִילּוּ בַּיִת הַמְסוּבָּךְ בַּאֲרָזִים מִתְרוֹעֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of: **For its cedar work shall be its city?** Furthermore, **Rabbi Yitzḥak** wondered when he **said: Is a house interlaced with cedars** not as strong as **a city,** and therefore not threatened by desolation? **Rather,** it means that **even a house interlaced with cedars will become unstable [*mitroe'a*]** if song is heard there.

Rabbi Yitzchak draws out the full force of this: even a house surrounded by cedars — seemingly impregnable and protected — will not be spared. *Mitro'eia*, it will be shattered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כִּי מַתְחֵיל חוּרְבָּא — בְּסִיפָּא מַתְחֵיל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חֹרֶב בַּסַּף״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Learn from it that when the destruction starts** it **starts with the threshold, as it is stated: "Desolation shall be in the posts."**

Rav Ashi adds: from this verse we also learn that destruction, when it comes, begins at the threshold — *ki matchali churba beseifa matchali*. An additional verse supports this: *vesha'iyah yukat sha'ar* — its desolation is smitten at the gate.

## Song Ban Failed

### Rav Huna's Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: זִמְרָא דְּנַגָּדֵי וּדְבַקָּרֵי — שְׁרֵי. דְּגַרְדָּאֵי — אֲסִיר. רַב הוּנָא בַּטֵּיל זִמְרָא, קָם מְאָה אֲווֹזֵי בְּזוּזָא וּמְאָה סָאֵה חִיטֵּי בְּזוּזָא וְלָא אִיבְּעִי. אֲתָא רַב חִסְדָּא זַלְזֵיל בֵּיהּ, אִיבְּעַאי אֲווֹזָא בְּזוּזָא וְלָא אִשְׁתְּכַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** The **song of those who pull ships and lead the herd is permitted,** for their singing assists them to establish a rhythm in their work. However, that **of weavers is forbidden,** as they sing only for their own enjoyment. The Gemara relates that subsequently, **Rav Huna nullified** all types of **song,** and this led to a general blessing: The price of **one hundred ducks stood at a dinar, and one hundred *se'a* of wheat at a dinar, and there was no desire** for them even at such a cheap price, due to their great abundance. Later, when **Rav Ḥisda came and belittled** this prohibition, people began to sing again. As a result, prices increased greatly, and this led to a situation whereby one **wanted** a single **duck for** one **dinar and it could not be found.**

Rav Huna attempted to move from the theoretical to the practical and actually annul song within his community. The result was economic devastation. A hundred geese could be had for a *zuz* and a hundred *sa'im* of wheat for a *zuz* — and yet no one wanted to buy. Prices collapsed not because supply had increased but because the prohibition on song had drained all joy from life, leaving people without the will to engage in normal commerce and consumption.

### Rav Chisda Reverses

Rav Chisda came and overturned the prohibition — disparaging it, or perhaps himself demonstrating that it was not to be enforced. The economic reversal was immediate: a single goose now cost a *zuz* and could not be found. Joy returned, demand surged, and prosperity was restored. The *Gemara* does not explicitly endorse either Rav Huna's stringency or Rav Chisda's reversal, but the narrative arc makes the practical lesson clear: however principled the mourning behind the song prohibition may be, its full enforcement produces consequences that are themselves destructive. Society cannot function in a state of enforced joylessness.

## Song Distinctions

### Permitted vs. Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: זִמְרָא דְּנַגָּדֵי וּדְבַקָּרֵי — שְׁרֵי. דְּגַרְדָּאֵי — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said:** The **song of those who pull ships and lead the herd is permitted,** for their singing assists them to establish a rhythm in their work. However, that **of weavers is forbidden,** as they sing only for their own enjoyment.

Rav Huna himself, even while seeking to restrict song, draws a distinction: *zimra denagdei udevakrei shari* — song used to lead cattle is permitted, because it serves a functional purpose in the work. *Degarda'ei asir* — but song sung by weavers for their own entertainment is forbidden, because it is simply *stam* pleasure with no connection to any necessary task. This distinction also supports the observation that the prohibition is not limited specifically to musical instruments — the issue seems to be the nature and purpose of the singing rather than its medium.

### Mixed-Gender Singing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: זָמְרִי גַּבְרֵי וְעׇנְיָ[ין] נְשֵׁי — פְּרִיצוּתָא. (זָמְרִי) [זָמְרָן] נְשֵׁי וְעָנַיִ[ין] גַּבְרֵי — כְּאֵשׁ בִּנְעוֹרֶת. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְבַטּוֹלֵי הָא מִקַּמֵּי הָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** If **men sing and women respond,** this is **licentiousness.** If **women sing and men respond,** it causes the evil inclination to burn **as if** one were setting **fire to chips** of kindling. The Gemara poses a question: **What difference is there?** Rav Yosef indicates that in any case both are prohibited. The Gemara answers: **To nullify one before the other,** i.e., if it is impossible to ban singing entirely, they should at least stop the most problematic form.

Rav Yosef introduces a distinction that is widely cited in *halachic* literature in discussions of *kol isha* and related matters. If men are singing and women respond to them responsively — *zimrei gavrei ve'anei nashei* — this is *pritzuta*, general sexual looseness. But if women are singing and men are responding — *zimrei nashei ve'anei gavrei* — the situation is far more serious: *ke'esh bene'oret*, like fire in flax. The men, waiting for their cue, focus intensely on the women's singing; the potential for genuine transgression is far greater.

### Priority Elimination

The *Gemara* then asks: what is the practical difference between the two levels? They are both problematic. The answer: *levatulei hai mikamei hai* — to tell us which to eliminate first if one cannot eliminate both. The *Gemara*'s realism here is notable. Not every wrong in society can be stamped out simultaneously; sometimes one must prioritize. If forced to choose, eliminate the women-led responsive singing, since it is the more combustible of the two. This principle — that practical *halacha* must account for what can and cannot be achieved — resonates directly with the earlier episode of Rav Huna and Rav Chisda.

## Five Punishments

### Music & Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹתֶה בְּאַרְבָּעָה מִינֵי זֶמֶר, מֵבִיא חָמֵשׁ פּוּרְעָנִיּוֹת לְעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי מַשְׁכִּימֵי בַבֹּקֶר שֵׁכָר יִרְדֹּפוּ מְאַחֲרֵי בַנֶּשֶׁף יַיִן יַדְלִיקֵם וְהָיָה כִנּוֹר וָנֶבֶל תֹּף וְחָלִיל וָיַיִן מִשְׁתֵּיהֶם וְאֵת פֹּעַל ה׳ לֹא יַבִּיטוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Anyone who drinks** wine **with** the accompaniment of **four types of instruments brings five** types of **retribution to the world, as it is stated: "Woe to them who rise early in the morning, that they may follow strong drink; who tarry late into the night, until wine inflames them. And the harp and the psaltery, the drum and the pipe and wine, are at their feasts, but they do not regard the work of the Lord"** (Isaiah 5:11–12).

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *kol hashoteh be'arba'ah minei zemer meivi chamesh puraniyot la'olam* — whoever drinks with four types of musical instruments brings five punishments upon the world. The verse is from the *navi*: *vehaya kinor vanevel tof vachalil veyayin mishteihem* — it was the *kinor* (harp), *nevel* (lyre), *tof* (cymbal), *chalil* (flute), and wine their drink — *ve'et po'al Hashem lo yabitu*, and the acts of God they do not see. It should be noted that the people described in these verses are not simply listening to music at a religious celebration; they are rising early in the morning to run after *shechar* and staying late into the night inflamed by wine. The music is a feature of a thoroughgoing lifestyle of excess and indifference.

### Exile & Famine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה כְּתִיב אַחֲרָיו: ״לָכֵן גָּלָה עַמִּי מִבְּלִי דָעַת״ — שֶׁגּוֹרְמִין גָּלוּת לָעוֹלָם, ״וּכְבוֹדוֹ מֵתֵי רָעָב״ — שֶׁמְּבִיאִין רָעָב לְעוֹלָם, ״וַהֲמוֹנוֹ צִחֵה צָמָא״ — שֶׁגּוֹרְמִין לַתּוֹרָה שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּכַּח מִלּוֹמְדֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** After listing the sin of those who drink wine with musical accompaniment, the verse states their punishment: **What is written afterward? "Therefore, My people have gone into captivity, for want of knowledge"** (Isaiah 5:13), meaning **that they cause exile to the world; "and their honorable men are famished"** (Isaiah 5:13), **as they bring famine to the world; "and their multitude are parched with thirst"** (Isaiah 5:13), **that they cause the Torah,** which is compared to water, **to be forgotten by those who learn it.**

What follows from this? *Mah ketiv achrav* — what does the text say afterward? *Lachen gala ami mibli da'at* — therefore My people will go into exile for lack of knowledge: *she-gormim galut la'olam*, this behavior causes exile. *Ukhevodo metei ra'av* — and their honored ones will die of hunger: *she-mevi'im ra'av la'olam*, it brings famine. *Vehamono tzecheih tzamah* — and its multitude is parched with thirst — read metaphorically: *she-gormim la-Torah she-tishtakach mi-lomdeiha*, it causes Torah to be forgotten by those who learn it, since Torah is likened to water.

### Righteous Diminished

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּשַּׁח אָדָם וַיִּשְׁפַּל אִישׁ״ — שֶׁגּוֹרְמִין שִׁפְלוּת לְשׂוֹנְאוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. וְאֵין ״אִישׁ״ אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ אִישׁ מִלְחָמָה״, ״וְעֵינֵי גְבֹהִים תִּשְׁפַּלְנָה״ — שֶׁגּוֹרְמִין שִׁפְלוּת שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And mankind is bowed down, and man is humbled"** (Isaiah 5:15), **that they cause the enemy of the Holy One, Blessed be He,** i.e., God Himself, **to be brought down, as "man"** in the phrase "and man is humbled" means **nothing other** than **the Holy One, Blessed be He, as** it **is stated: "The Lord is a man of war"** (Exodus 15:3). The verse continues: **"And the eyes of the lofty are humbled"** (Isaiah 5:15), **that they cause the Jewish people to be brought down.** These are the five retributions.

*Vayishach adam vayishpel ish* — a person is humbled and a man is brought low: *she-gormim shiflut le-son'o shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — it even brings humiliation to the One Who is called the enemy of sin, which is to say God Himself, since *ein ish ela HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — the term *ish* alludes to God, as the verse says *Hashem ish milchama*. And *veinei gevohim tishpalna* — the eyes of those held high will be brought low: *she-gormim shiflut shel Yisrael*, it diminishes Israel. The final consequence: *hirchiva she'ol nafsha ufa'ara piha livli chok* — the depths have opened their mouth without limit, consuming all glory.

## Urim & Tumim

### First Prophets Died

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁמִּתוּ נְבִיאִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים. מַאן ״נְבִיאִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים״? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: זֶה דָּוִד וּשְׁמוּאֵל וּשְׁלֹמֹה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **From** the time **when the early prophets died,** the *Urim VeTummim* was nullified. The Gemara poses a question: **Who** are **the early prophets? Rav Huna says: This is** referring to **David, and Samuel, and Solomon,** and after their death the *Urim VeTummim* was no longer used.

The *mishnah* records: *mishemetu nevi'im rishonim batlu urim vetumim* — when the first prophets died, the *urim vetumim* ceased to function. The phrasing is precise: it was not that the physical *urim vetumim* disappeared, but that they ceased to deliver answers.

### First Prophets Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: בִּימֵי דָּוִד, זִימְנִין סְלֵיק, וְזִימְנִין לָא סְלֵיק, שֶׁהֲרֵי שָׁאַל צָדוֹק — וְעָלְתָה לוֹ, שָׁאַל אֶבְיָתָר — וְלֹא עָלְתָה לוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַל אֶבְיָתָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: In the days of David** there were **times** an answer **rose up** for them from the *Urim VeTummim* **and** there were **times** an answer **did not rise up,** i.e., they did not receive an answer. The proof for this is **that Tzadok,** the High Priest in David's time, **asked** the *Urim VeTummim* **and** an answer **rose up for him,** whereas **Abiathar asked and** an answer **did not rise up for him, as** it **is stated: "And Abiathar went up"** (II Samuel 15:24), and he was removed from serving as the High Priest as a result.

Rav Huna identifies the "first prophets" as David, Solomon, and Samuel — an early and distinguished group. According to this view, the *urim vetumim* lost its efficacy already in that era. Rav Nachman qualifies: in the time of David it was not entirely defunct — *zimen solek vezimen lo solek*, sometimes an inquiry would receive a response and sometimes not. When Tzadok the *kohen* consulted it, he received an answer; when Avyatar did, he did not — as the verse implies when it records that Avyatar was removed from his position.

One might wonder whether David himself qualifies as a *navi*, since the *mishnah* uses the term *nevi'im rishonim*. It is a legitimate question; while David is known for his close association with prophets, he is not typically listed among them.

### First Destruction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״וַיְהִי לִדְרֹשׁ אֱלֹהִים בִּימֵי זְכַרְיָהוּ הַמֵּבִין בִּרְאֹת הָאֱלֹהִים״, מַאי לָאו, בְּאוּרִים וְתוּמִּים? לֹא, בִּנְבִיאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Shmuel raises an objection:** The verse states concerning Uzziah: **"And he set himself to seek God in the days of Zechariah, who had an understanding of the vision of God"** (II Chronicles 26:5). **What, is** the verse **not** stating that Uzziah would seek God by asking questions **of** the ***Urim VeTummim*,** despite the fact that he lived after the time of Solomon? The Gemara rejects this claim: **No,** he would seek God by asking questions **of** the **prophets,** but not of the *Urim VeTummim*.

The *Gemara* tests the claim that the *urim vetumim* ceased so early against a verse: *vayehi lidrosh Elokim kol yemei Zecharya hameivin bire'ot Elokim* — it was there to inquire of God all the days of Zecharya who understood the visions of God. This Zecharya — *Rashi* identifies him as the son of Yehoyada — lived very late in the period of the kings, well after David and Solomon. Does this not show the *urim vetumim* was operative until much later? The *Gemara* deflects: *lo, bineviim* — no, they would inquire of God through prophets, not through *urim vetumim*. The verse is about prophetic consultation, not the priestly oracle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מִשֶּׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן בָּטְלוּ עָרֵי מִגְרָשׁ, וּפָסְקוּ אוּרִים וְתוּמִּים, וּפָסַק מֶלֶךְ מִבֵּית דָּוִד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 13:2) with regard to when the *Urim VeTummim* ceased: **From** the time **when the First Temple was destroyed, the cities with fields** that were allocated to the Levites **were nullified, and the *Urim VeTummim* ceased, and the monarchy ceased from the house of David.**

A second *tanna'itic* source proves more decisive: *mishecharav Beit HaMikdash rishon batlu arei migrash, upasku urim vetumim, upasak melech mibeit David* — from the time the First Temple was destroyed, the cities of the *Levi'im* ceased, the *urim vetumim* ceased, and kingship from the house of David ended. This connects the *urim vetumim* definitively to the destruction of the First Temple, not to the earlier era of David and Solomon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לְחָשְׁךָ אָדָם לוֹמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַתִּרְשָׁתָא לָהֶם אֲשֶׁר לֹא יֹאכְלוּ מִקֹּדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים עַד עֲמֹד כֹּהֵן לְאוּרִים וּלְתֻמִּים״, אֱמוֹר לוֹ: כְּאָדָם שֶׁאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״עַד שֶׁיִּחְיוּ מֵתִים וְיָבֹא מָשִׁיחַ בֶּן דָּוִד״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if a person would whisper to you saying** that the *Urim VeTummim* was still extant, as it states with regard to when the Second Temple first stood: **"And the Tirshatha said to them that they should not eat of the most sacred things, until there stood a priest with the *Urim VeTummim*"** (Ezra 2:63), which seems to indicate that they merely had to wait until the Second Temple was built for the reappearance of the *Urim VeTummim*; you should **say to him** that this is not referring to an expectation of a short-term development, but it is **like a person who says to his friend,** with regard to something that will occur in the distant future: **Until the dead live and the Messiah,** the **son of David, comes.** In any case, the *baraita* indicates that the *Urim VeTummim* ceased only from the time when the First Temple was destroyed, and not in the time of Solomon.

A final text from the Second Temple period — the *tirshata's* declaration that certain *kohanim* of uncertain lineage must refrain from eating sacred food *ad amod kohen le-urim vetumim*, until a *kohen* arises who can consult the *urim vetumim* — might seem to suggest that restoration was imminent. The *Gemara* firmly rejects this reading: that phrase means nothing of the sort. It is like one person saying to another: "until the dead are revived and *Mashiach ben David* comes." It points to the eschaton, not to the near term.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאן ״נְבִיאִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים״ — לְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵחַגַּי זְכַרְיָה וּמַלְאָכִי, דְּאַחֲרוֹנִים נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Who** are **the early prophets,** with regard to whom it states that use of the *Urim VeTummim* ceased immediately after their death? This term early prophets serves **to exclude Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi,** who **are** the **latter prophets.**

In view of all this, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak redefines "first prophets" in the *mishnah*: the phrase means to the exclusion of the *last* prophets — Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi. These three prophesied at the very beginning of the Second Temple period, and the *urim vetumim* ceased no later than their time.

### Last Prophets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִשֶּׁמֵּתוּ חַגַּי זְכַרְיָה וּמַלְאָכִי — נִסְתַּלְּקָה רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל. וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן, הָיוּ מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁים בְּבַת קוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 13:3): **From** the time **when Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi died the Divine Spirit departed from the Jewish people,** as these three were considered to be the last prophets. **And** even after the Urim VeTummim ceased to exist, they would **nevertheless** still **make use of a Divine Voice** to receive instructions from Above, even after this time.

A *beraita* confirms: when Chaggai, Zechariah, and Malachi died, *nistalka ruach hakodesh miYisrael* — divine inspiration departed from Israel. These were the last of the *nevi'im*. After them, there was no more *nevuah*.

And yet — *ve'af al pi chen hayu mishtamshim bebat kol* — even after prophecy ceased, they would make use of a *bat kol*, a heavenly voice. This was not *ruach hakodesh* proper; it was a more attenuated form of divine communication, and its precise phenomenological character invites reflection. It is worth noting that while in the *Bavli* a *bat kol* is consistently presented as a miraculous heavenly voice, Saul Lieberman has demonstrated that in the *Yerushalmi* the term more often means something like a widely circulating report — a *shemuah* that spreads and acquires the status of near-certain information. The degree to which the *Bavli*'s *bat kol* was experienced as a literally miraculous auditory phenomenon or as a form of divinely inspired collective intuition is itself a question the text leaves open.

### Jericho Bat Kol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁפַּעַם אַחַת הָיוּ מְסוּבִּין בַּעֲלִיַּית בֵּית גּוּרְיָא בִּירִיחוֹ, נִתְּנָה עֲלֵיהֶן בַּת קוֹל מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם וְאָמְרָה: יֵשׁ בָּכֶם אָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁרָאוּי שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה עָלָיו, אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין דּוֹרוֹ רָאוּי לְכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **For on one occasion** the Sages **were reclining in the upper story of the house of Gurya in Jericho. A Divine Voice from Heaven was issued to them, and it said: There is one person among you** for **whom it is fitting that the Divine Presence should rest upon him** as a prophet, **but his generation is not fit for it;** they do not deserve to have a prophet among them.

Once, the Sages were gathered at a meal in the upper room of the house of Gurya in Jericho, when a *bat kol* issued from heaven declaring: *yesh bachem adam echad sherauy shetishre Shechinah alav* — there is among you one person upon whom the divine presence could rest. The declaration itself is poignant in its implications: there is someone here worthy of *nevuah*, but the *bat kol* goes on to add — *ela she'ein doro rauy lekach* — the generation is not fit for it. The very vehicle of the announcement, the *bat kol*, thus serves simultaneously to identify prophetic potential and to explain why prophecy cannot be restored.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתְנוּ עֵינֵיהֶם בְּהִלֵּל הַזָּקֵן, וּכְשֶׁמֵּת הִסְפִּידוּהוּ: הִי חָסִיד, הִי עָנָיו, תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל עֶזְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages present **directed their gaze to Hillel the Elder. And when he died, they eulogized him** in the following manner: **Alas pious one, alas humble one, student of Ezra.**

All eyes turned to Hillel the Elder — the assembled Sages recognized immediately who was being described. When Hillel died, the eulogy over him invoked the language of loss: *hei chasid, hei anav, talmido shel Ezra* — woe to the pious man, woe to the humble man, disciple of Ezra. The designation *talmido shel Ezra* is not biographical but typological: as Ezra had embodied the transmission of *Torah she-ba'al peh* in his generation, so Hillel embodied it in his — the continuation of an unbroken spiritual lineage.

### Samuel the Small

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוּב פַּעַם אַחֶרֶת הָיוּ מְסוּבִּין בַּעֲלִיָּיה בְּיַבְנֶה, נִתְּנָה (לָהֶן) [עֲלֵיהֶן] בַּת קוֹל מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, וְאָמְרָה לָהֶן: יֵשׁ בָּכֶם אָדָם אֶחָד שֶׁרָאוּי שֶׁתִּשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה עָלָיו אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין דּוֹרוֹ זַכָּאִין לְכָךְ, נָתְנוּ עֵינֵיהֶם בִּשְׁמוּאֵל הַקָּטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And again, on another occasion** several generations later, the Sages **were reclining in an upper story** of a house **in Yavne,** and **a Divine Voice from Heaven was issued to them, and said: There is one person among you** for **whom it is fitting that the Divine Presence should rest upon him, but his generation is not fit for it.** The Sages present **directed their gaze to Shmuel HaKatan.**

A second *bat kol* came, this time at a gathering in Yavneh, with the identical declaration: one person among them is worthy of the *Shechinah*'s resting upon him, but the generation is not fit. This time all eyes turned to *Shmuel haKatan* — Samuel the Small — a student of Hillel in the same typological sense, spiritually his disciple even though Hillel had lived roughly a hundred and fifty years earlier.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁמֵּת, הִסְפִּידוּהוּ: הִי עָנָיו, הִי חָסִיד, תַּלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And when he died, they eulogized him** in the following manner: **Alas humble one, alas pious one, student of Hillel.**

When Shmuel haKatan died, his eulogy echoed Hillel's: *hei anav, hei chasid, talmido shel Hillel*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף הוּא אָמַר בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָתוֹ: שִׁמְעוֹן וְיִשְׁמָעֵאל — לְחַרְבָּא, וְחַבְרוֹהִי — לִקְטָלָא, וּשְׁאָר עַמָּא — לְבִיזָּא, וְעָקָן סַגִּיאִין עֲתִידִין לְמֵיתֵי עַל עַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And he too,** Shmuel HaKatan, **said** the following statement of divinely inspired prediction **at the time of his death: Shimon,** i.e., Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **and Yishmael,** i.e., Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha the High Priest, are slated **for the sword, and their colleagues for killing, and the rest of the people for plunder, and great troubles are destined to befall the people.**

What is particularly remarkable is that at the moment of his death, Shmuel haKatan uttered a prophecy: *Shimon veYishmael lecharba, vachavro leketala, usha'ar ama leviza, ve'akan sagi'in atidin lemeti al ama* — Shimon and Yishmael are destined for the sword, their colleagues for slaughter, the rest of the nation for despoilment, and great travails will come upon the people. This refers to Rabban Shimon haNasi and Rabbi Yishmael ben Elisha, among the *asara harugei malchut* — the ten martyrs of the Hadrianic persecutions, placing Shmuel haKatan at or near the time of the *Churban*. The *bat kol* had identified him as worthy of *nevuah*; this dying prophecy represents, it seems, at least a partial fulfillment of that verdict — something approaching, if not fully achieving, the *hashraat haShechina* that was declared his due.

### Yehuda ben Bava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא בִּקְּשׁוּ לוֹמַר ״הִי חָסִיד הִי עָנָיו״, אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה שָׁעָה, שֶׁאֵין מַסְפִּידִין עַל הֲרוּגֵי מַלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **And they also sought to say about Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava,** when eulogizing him: **Alas pious one, alas humble one, but the moment was disturbed** and they could not do so. That is because **eulogies are not given for those killed by the monarchy,** which was Rabbi Yehuda ben Baba's fate, in order not to arouse the monarchy's wrath.

A similar *hesped* was sought for Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava — they wished to say the same *hei chasid, hei anav* over him — but *nitrefa hasha'ah*, the opportunity was lost. Since he was among the *harugei malchut*, the martyrs executed by the Roman government, they could not deliver a public eulogy over him without incurring dangerous attention from the authorities.

## Shamir & Miracles

### Shamir Worm

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ בָּטַל הַשָּׁמִיר כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁמִיר שֶׁבּוֹ בָּנָה שְׁלֹמֹה אֶת בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַבַּיִת בְּהִבָּנֹתוֹ אֶבֶן שְׁלֵמָה מַסָּע נִבְנָה״ — הַדְּבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **From** the time **when the** First **Temple was destroyed the *shamir* ceased** to exist. **The Sages taught:** This ***shamir*** is the creature **with which Solomon built the Temple, as it is stated: "For the house, when it was built, was built of whole stone from the quarry"** (I Kings 6:7). Now **these words** should be understood exactly **as they are written,** that King Solomon took whole stones and shaped them by having the *shamir* do the cutting. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

With the destruction of the First Temple, the *shamir* was lost. A *beraita* records: *shamir shebo bana Shlomo et Beit HaMikdash* — the *shamir* was the means by which Solomon built the Temple.

### Metal Vessels Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה: וְכִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר כֵּן? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״כׇּל אֵלֶּה אֲבָנִים יְקָרוֹת וְגוֹ׳ מְגֹרָרוֹת בַּמְּגֵרָה״! אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא נִשְׁמַע בַּבַּיִת בְּהִבָּנֹתוֹ״ — שֶׁהָיָה מְתַקֵּן מִבַּחוּץ, וּמַכְנִיס מִבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥemya said to him: And is it possible to say so? But isn't it already stated: "All these were costly stones,** according to the measures of hewn stones, **sawed with saws"** (II Kings 7:9), which indicates that saws, which are iron implements, were used to shape the stones? **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states:** "And hammer, ax, and any tool of iron **were not heard in the house when it was being built"** (I Kings 6:7)? It means **that he would prepare** the stones **outside** the Temple Mount using tools, **and bring** them **inside** already cut.

The basis for this claim is the verse: *vehabayit behibanoto even shlemah masa nivnah* — the Temple was built with complete stones brought from the quarry. Rabbi Yehuda reads this literally: no metal tools were used at all; the stones were hewn from the quarry in their final form and brought as they were. Rabbi Nechemya objects: can this really be maintained? Another verse states clearly that the stones were *meGuzorot baMegeirah* — hewn with a metal saw. What then is the meaning of the prohibition on metal in the *Beit HaMikdash*? Rabbi Nechemya explains: *shehaya matkin mibachutz umachnis mibifnim* — the metal work was done off-site, and only the finished stones were brought to the Temple Mount, where no sound of metal was heard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּאַבְנֵי מִקְדָּשׁ, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה בְּאַבְנֵי בֵיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The statement of Rabbi Yehuda** that no iron tools were used **appears** to be correct **with regard to the Temple stones, and the statement of Rabbi Neḥemya** that tools were used appears to be correct **with regard to the stones of the** king's own **house.**

Rav attempts a synthesis: Rabbi Yehuda's position applies to the stones of the *Mikdash* proper — no metal was used on them at any stage — while Rabbi Nechemya's position, permitting off-site metal work, applies to the stones of Solomon's palace. On this reading, Rabbi Nechemya has no need of the *shamir* for the Temple stones at all.

### Shamir & Breastplate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, שָׁמִיר לְמַאי אֲתָא? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: אֲבָנִים הַלָּלוּ אֵין כּוֹתְבִין אוֹתָן בִּדְיוֹ, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פִּתּוּחֵי חוֹתָם״, וְאֵין מְסָרְטִין עֲלֵיהֶם בְּאִיזְמֵל, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּמִלּוּאֹתָם״, אֶלָּא כּוֹתֵב עֲלֵיהֶם בִּדְיוֹ, וּמַרְאֶה לָהֶן שָׁמִיר מִבַּחוּץ, וְהֵן נִבְקָעוֹת מֵאֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **And** according to **Rabbi Neḥemya,** who maintains that they used iron tools even in the cutting of the stones for the Temple, **for what** purpose **did the *shamir* come?** The Gemara answers: **It** was **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **These stones** in the breastplate and ephod, upon which were inscribed the names of the tribes, **they may not be written on with ink, because it is stated: "Like the engravings of a signet"** (Exodus 28:21), which means the names must be engraved onto the stones. **And they may not be scratched on with a scalpel [*izemel*], because it is stated: "In their full settings"** (Exodus 28:20), indicating that the stones must be complete and not missing any of their mass. **Rather,** one **writes** the letters **on them in ink, and shows them,** i.e., he places the ***shamir*** close to the ink markings **from outside,** without having it touch the stones, **and they split** open along the lines of the ink **of their own accord.**

But according to Rabbi Nechemya, who permits metal work off-site, why was the *shamir* needed at all? The answer comes from a *beraita*: the stones of the *efod* — the breastplate of the High Priest, upon which the twelve names of the tribes were engraved — presented a unique set of constraints. They could not be inscribed with ink, because the verse says *pituchei chotam*, carved engraving. But they also could not be cut with a metal awl, because the verse says *bimlu'otam*, they must be complete — nothing may be removed. The solution: the name was written in ink on the surface of the stone, the stone was then shown to the *shamir* — *umar'eh lahem shamir mibachutz* — and the *shamir*, following the path of the ink, caused the stone to split open precisely along those lines. *Vehen nivka'ot me'aleihen* — they would crack of their own accord, like a fig splitting in summer heat or a crevice opening in the earth during the rainy season, so that nothing was removed and the stone remained *malu'ot*, complete.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּתְאֵינָה זוֹ שֶׁנִּבְקַעַת בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וְאֵינָהּ חֲסֵירָה כְּלוּם, וּכְבִקְעָה זוֹ, שֶׁנִּבְקַעַת בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, וְאֵינָהּ חֲסֵירָה כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** like this fig that splits in the summer without losing anything** of its mass, **and like this** field in **a valley that cracks in the rainy season without losing anything** of its mass.

### Shamir Properties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁמִיר זֶה, בְּרִיָּיתוֹ כִּשְׂעוֹרָה, וּמִשֵּׁשֶׁת יְמֵי בְּרֵאשִׁית נִבְרָא, וְאֵין כׇּל דָּבָר קָשֶׁה יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בְּפָנָיו. בַּמֶּה מְשַׁמְּרִין אוֹתוֹ — כּוֹרְכִין אוֹתוֹ בִּסְפוֹגִין שֶׁל צֶמֶר, וּמַנִּיחִין אוֹתוֹ בְּאִיטְנִי שֶׁל אֲבָר מְלֵיאָה סוּבֵּי שְׂעוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: This *shamir*, its size is that of a barleycorn, and it was created in the six days of creation, and nothing hard can withstand it. In what is it kept,** so that it will not break everything in the vicinity? **They wrap it in tufts [*sefogin*] of wool and place it in a leaden vessel [*itenei*], full of barley bran,** which is soft and will not be broken by the *shamir*.

The *shamir* was created at twilight on the eve of the first Sabbath — among the items listed in *Pirkei Avot* as created at *bein hashemashot*. *Ve'ein kol davar kashe yakhol la'amod befanav* — nothing hard can withstand it. This raises the obvious question: what vessel can contain it? If it eats through everything, what does one keep it in? *Korkhin oto bisfogin shel tzomer, uminichin oto be'itanei shel avar, mele'ah suvei se'orin* — it is wrapped in wool and placed in a lead container filled with barley bran. The logic is the same as — and apparently analogous to — the use of soft or pliable materials to contain highly corrosive or penetrating substances: the very softness of the material is what neutralizes the *shamir*'s destructive capacity.

## Lost Blessings

### Silk & Glass Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מִשֶּׁחָרַב מִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן — בָּטְלָה שִׁירָא פְּרַנְדָּא וּזְכוּכִית לְבָנָה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מִשֶּׁחָרַב מִקְדָּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן — בָּטְלָה שִׁירָא פְּרַנְדָּא וּזְכוּכִית לְבָנָה וְרֶכֶב בַּרְזֶל, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף יַיִן קָרוּשׁ הַבָּא מִשְּׂנִיר, הַדּוֹמֶה כְּעִיגּוּלֵי דְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ami says: From** the time **when the First Temple was destroyed, shiny [*peranda*] silk [*shira*] and white glass ceased** to exist. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **From** the time **when the First Temple was destroyed, shiny silk, white glass, and iron chariots ceased; and some say** that **even congealed wine that comes from Senir,** the Hermon, **which is similar to round fig cakes** after it congeals, ceased to exist as well.

Rav Ami teaches: *mishecharav mikdash rishon batla shira paranda uzkhukhit levana* — from the destruction of the First Temple, *shira paranda* (a type of fine silk) and clear white glass were lost. A *beraita* confirms this and adds further losses: *rekhev barzel*, iron chariots, were also lost. Some say even *yayin karush haba mi-Shanir* — a congealed wine from Mount Senir that resembled pressed fig rounds — was lost. It could be used a little at a time and reconstituted with water, making it a kind of ancient concentrated wine.

### Nofet Tzufim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹפֶת צוּפִים. מַאי ״נוֹפֶת צוּפִים״? אָמַר רַב: סוֹלֶת שֶׁצָּפָה עַל גַּבֵּי נָפָה, וְדוֹמָה לְעִיסָּה שֶׁנִּילּוֹשָׁה בִּדְבַשׁ וָשֶׁמֶן. וְלֵוִי אָמַר: שְׁתֵּי כִּכָּרוֹת הַנִּדְבָּקוֹת בַּתַּנּוּר וְתוֹפְחוֹת וּבָאוֹת, עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught: **And** the **sweetness of the honeycomb [*nofet tzufim*]** also ceased when the First Temple was destroyed. The Gemara asks: **What** is ***nofet tzufim*? Rav says: Fine flour that floats** up and remains **on the top of the sieve [*nafa*], which is similar** in taste **to dough kneaded with honey and oil. And Levi says** that *nofet tzufim* is the term for **two loaves stuck together in an oven, which keep swelling until they reach each other.**

Also lost: *nofet tzufim*. Rav explains: *solet shetzafa al gabei nafa* — a finest flour so rich that it would adhere to the top of the sieve rather than passing through, and it resembled dough kneaded with honey and oil.

Levi offers a different interpretation: *shtei kikkarot hanidbakot batanur* — two loaves placed at opposite walls of the oven that would expand and rise until they touched each other in the middle. This recalls the discussion of the original blessing of dough, and the implication is clear: once the blessing departed from bread, loaves no longer expanded with such abundance.

### Wildflower Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: זֶה דְּבַשׁ הַבָּא מִן הַצִּיפְיָא. מַאי מַשְׁמַע — כְּדִמְתַרְגֵּם רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: ״כְּמָא דְּנָתְזָן דַּבְרָיָאתָה וְשָׁיְיטָן בְּרוּמֵי עָלְמָא וּמַתְיָין דּוּבְשָׁא מֵעִישְׂבֵי טוּרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: This is honey that comes from elevated areas [*tzipiyya*].** The Gemara explains: **From where may** it **be inferred** that this is what *nofet tzufim* is? **As Rav Sheshet would translate** the words: "As the bees do" (Deuteronomy 1:44): **Like the bees spread out and fly all over the world and bring honey from mountainous plants.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi identifies *nofet tzufim* as honey coming from *tzufim* — the highlands, the mountains. The word *nofet* suggests dripping, and *tzufim* here is read as the high places — hence wildflower honey gathered by bees from the mountain grasses. Rav Sheshet's *targum* of a verse about bees — *kema denitzan devyata veshaitan beruma alma, umaskan duvsha me'ishbei tura* — supports this: the finest honey comes from bees that soar to the heights and bring down honey from the mountain grasses.

### Zifim Honey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: כׇּל הַנִּצּוֹק טָהוֹר, חוּץ מִדְּבַשׁ זִיפִים וְהַצַפִּיחִים. מַאי ״זִיפִים״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דְּבַשׁ שֶׁמְּזַיְּיפִין בּוֹ. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: עַל שֵׁם מְקוֹמוֹ, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״זִיף וָטֶלֶם וּבְעָלוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Makhshirin* 5:9): **Anything that is poured** remains **ritually pure.** In other words, even if a liquid is poured into a ritually impure utensil, the stream of the liquid does not defile the contents that remain in the ritually pure utensil from which they were poured, **apart from *zifim* honey and wafer** batter. These substances are too viscous to be considered liquids. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***zifim*? Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Honey** of such rare quality that **they** could **falsify [*mezayyefin*] it,** by diluting it with other substances, and it would not be noticed. **And Reish Lakish says:** It is named **after its place, as it is written: "Ziph and Telem and Bealoth"** (Joshua 15:24).

The *Gemara* then connects this to a *mishnah* elsewhere: *kol hanitzok tahor* — anything poured from one vessel to another does not create a link (*tziruf*) that would transmit impurity back to the upper vessel. The exception is honey of *Zifim* and *tzapichin* — pressed honey cakes so thick and congealed they form a solid mass, so that if the lower portion becomes *tamei*, the upper portion is *tamei* as well. What is *dvash Zifim*? Rabbi Yochanan says it is honey so potent that one can adulterate other honey with it without the blend being detected — it can be used for "cutting" lesser honey. Reish Lakish maintains that *Zifim* is simply a place name, as the verse *Zif veTelem uVe'alot* confirms — a town in the territory of Yehuda, and the same place mentioned in the incident when the people of Zif informed Saul of David's whereabouts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בַּדָּבָר אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״בְּבוֹא הַזִּיפִים וַיֹּאמְרוּ לְשָׁאוּל הֲלֹא דָוִד וְגוֹ׳״. מַאי ״זִיפִים״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּנֵי אָדָם הַמְזַיְּיפִין דִּבְרֵיהֶם. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: עַל שֵׁם מְקוֹמָן, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״זִיף וָטֶלֶם וּבְעָלוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, you** can **say** with regard to the verse: **"When the *zifim* came and said to Saul, does not David** hide himself with us" (Psalms 54:2). **What is** the meaning of ***zifim*,** mentioned in this verse? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It means **people who** would **falsify [*hamzayyefin*] their words. And Rabbi Elazar says:** They are called **after their place, as it is written: "Ziph and Telem and Bealoth."**

Rabbi Yochanan, separately, interprets *anshei haZifim* in that narrative as people who falsify their words — *mezayefim divreihem* — while Rabbi Elazar maintains it is simply the place name.

## People of Faith

### Faith Ceased

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפָסְקוּ אַנְשֵׁי אֲמָנָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁהֵן מַאֲמִינִים בְּהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that from the time when the Second Temple was destroyed **men of faith ceased. Rabbi Yitzḥak says: These are people who believe in the Holy One, Blessed be He,** and place their trust in Him in all their ways.

*Upasku anshei amanah miYisrael* — the people of faith ceased from Israel. Rabbi Yitzchak explains: these are people who genuinely, fully believe in God — *ma'aminim b'HaKadosh Baruch Hu* in the deepest sense.

### High Faith Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַגָּדוֹל אוֹמֵר: כָּל מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ וְאוֹמֵר ״מָה אוֹכַל לְמָחָר״ — אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מִקְּטַנֵּי אֲמָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer the Great says** that **whoever has bread in his basket** to eat today **and says: What shall I eat tomorrow,** meaning he does not know how he will acquire bread for tomorrow, **he is nothing other** than **from those of little faith.** One must trust in God to provide him with his sustenance.

What level of faith did they possess? Rabbi Eliezer the Great articulates the standard: *kol mi she-yesh lo pat besalo ve'omer mah ochal lemachaR* — whoever has bread in his pantry and says "what will I eat tomorrow?" — that person is among the *katnei emunah*, those of small faith. The bar is extraordinarily high: if you have food today and are already worrying about tomorrow, you lack complete trust in divine providence.

### Righteous Lack Faith

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי מִי בַז לְיוֹם קְטַנּוֹת״ — מִי גָּרַם לַצַּדִּיקִים שֶׁיִּתְבַּזְבֵּז שׁוּלְחָנָן לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא — קַטְנוּת שֶׁהָיְתָה בָּהֶן, שֶׁלֹּא הֶאֱמִינוּ בְּהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is what Rabbi Elazar said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "For who plunders the day of small things"** (Zechariah 4:10)? **What caused the table,** i.e., the reward, **of the righteous to be plundered,** meaning wasted, **in the future?** It was **the** small-mindedness **they possessed.** And what is this small-mindedness? **That they did not believe in the Holy One, Blessed be He,** with a complete faith.

The consequence reaches even the *tzadikim* of later generations. Rabbi Elazar expounds the verse *ki mi vaz l'yom katanot* — who has despised the day of smallness? — as a question: what caused the righteous to have their table diminished in the world to come, not receiving the full reward they deserve? *Katanut she-hayah bahen* — the smallness that was in them; even the righteous of our time lack complete *emunah*, worrying about their material provision in this world. *She-lo he'eminu b'HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — they did not fully trust that God would provide. Measure for measure, their heavenly table in the world to come will not be complete either.
