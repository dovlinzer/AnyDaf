# Bekhorot 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Categories

### Framework Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין מוּמָא לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן? אִיכָּא מִשּׁוּם אַחוֹלֵי עֲבוֹדָה: מוּמָא מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״מוּם בּוֹ וְלֹא יְחַלֵּל״, שֶׁאֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן לָא מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** difference **is there between** a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service due to a full-fledged **blemish** that disqualifies both people and animals, and a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service because **he is not equal among the seed of Aaron?** The Gemara responds: **There is** a difference between them **with regard to profaning** the Temple **service.** A priest with a full-fledged **blemish** who performs rites in the Temple **profanes** the **service** he has performed, **as it is written: "He has a blemish; that he not profane** My Sanctuaries" (Leviticus 21:23). A priest **who is not equal among the seed of Aaron** does **not profane** the **service** he performs.

Daf 44 picks up with the *mishna* on 43b, approximately ten lines down, continuing the discussion of the *mumim* of a *kohen* as distinct from the *mumim* of a *bechor*. Three conceptual categories have emerged from the preceding material. The first is the classic *mum*, which invalidates the *avoda* outright. The second — and the category around which the *gemara* has effectively framed the entire *perek* — is *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon*: not an actual blemish or disfigurement, but a characteristic that places the individual outside what one might call the middle of the bell-shaped curve of normal human variation. Within this second category, the further question is which characteristics are under consideration. The third, and most minor, category is Rabbinic: the *avoda* performed by such a person is not invalid, and the *gemara* characterizes the violation as at most an *aseh*. Beyond even that lies a purely Rabbinic concern of *maris ayin* — the problem of appearances that distract — which is neither a formal *mum* nor a violation of the *eino shaveh* category.

### Rambam Approach

Before turning to the text, a conceptual point regarding the Rambam is worth noting. The Rambam does not use the phrase *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon*. Instead, much as the *mishnayos* themselves do, the Rambam subsumes the entire middle category under the heading of *mum* — blemish — and explains the distinction by noting that these conditions are primarily found in humans and not in animals. For the Rambam, the only operative difference from the standard *mum* is that the *avoda* performed is not *mechulal* — not retroactively invalidated. He also maintains that a transgression of a *lo sa'aseh* applies to anyone with a blemish who serves. This framing aligns more naturally with the language of the *mishna*, but stands in tension with the *gemara*'s reconceptualization, which treats *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* as a genuinely distinct and broader category — one that is not fundamentally about physical blemish at all.

### Aesthetics vs Objective

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן, וּמִשּׁוּם מַרְאִית הָעַיִן? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **What** difference **is there between** a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service because **he is not equal among the seed of Aaron,** e.g., one whose head is pointed, **and** a priest disqualified from performing the Temple service **due to the** mere **appearance of** a blemish, such as one whose eyelashes have fallen out, as stated in the *mishnayot* further on in this chapter? The Gemara responds: **There is** a difference **between them** with regard to the violation of the prohibition, which is stated as **a positive mitzva,** i.e., that priests who are equal shall serve in the Temple. A priest who is not equal among the seed of Aaron who performs the Temple service transgresses this prohibition. By contrast, a priest who is disqualified due to the appearance of a blemish is disqualified by rabbinic law, and he does not transgress a Torah prohibition by performing the Temple service.

A question arises about the underlying rationale for excluding a *baal mum* from the *avoda*: is the concern aesthetic — about the experience of worship in the *Beis Hamikdash* — or is there something more objective at work? This question is complicated by the *gemara* in *Zevachim* 107a, which states that a *baal mum* is *pasul* even on a *bama*. There is nothing in that *gemara* that contradicts this ruling, and while a *Tosefta* exists that could arguably be read as referring to a *baal mum* in the *korban* rather than the *kohen*, the *gemara* in *Zevachim* is clear: even on a *bama*, where no *kohen* is required at all and even a non-*kohen* may serve, a *baal mum* remains invalid. This does not sit easily with a purely aesthetic explanation rooted in the sanctity and splendor of the *Beis Hamikdash*, since on a *bama* that institutional context is absent.

The question of moral failings is related. One might ask whether known sinners — people with serious ethical violations on their record — are similarly disqualified. The only case where this appears is the *kohen* who worshipped *avoda zara*, who is *pasul*, but that is because *avoda zara* categorically recategorizes the *kohen*. Separately, the *gemara* holds that a *kohen* who committed murder cannot perform *nesias kapav* — the raising of the hands for the priestly blessing — even after *teshuva*, but this is specific to the hands: *yadeichem damim male'u*, "your hands are filled with blood." As a general rule, moral failings are not relevant; the disqualification framework is concerned with the physical, not the ethical.

### Birchas Kohanim Distinct…

The contrast with *birchas kohanim* is instructive and actually sharpens the conceptual question. For *nesias kapav*, there is no formal *mum* requirement as such. The entire concern is distraction: anything likely to draw the congregation's gaze or attention is problematic, but only to the extent it is visible — specifically, things on the hands or face. Moreover, if the townspeople are already familiar with the *kohen*'s condition, that very familiarity eliminates the problem, since there is nothing left to distract. Similarly, a *kohen* who does not pronounce words correctly may be excluded from *birchas kohanim* on distraction grounds, where there is no formal blemish involved at all. For *birchas kohanim*, then, the standard is entirely functional and contextual — what will actually distract people, and whether habituation has neutralized that effect. For the *avoda*, by contrast, the standard is formal and categorical: it concerns a defined blemish, not a degree of distraction. The aesthetic explanation works better for *birchas kohanim* than for the *avoda*, but given that the *avoda* disqualification applies even on a *bama* and is not reducible to a distraction criterion, a purely aesthetic theory does not fully hold.

### Rambam's 140 Blemishes

For those curious about the scope of the category: the Rambam, in his comprehensive enumeration, counts one hundred and forty blemishes for humans. He is explicit that the blemishes mentioned in the *Torah* are not exhaustive but merely illustrative examples, and that the rest must be derived by analogy.

## Gibben Analysis

### Gibben Definitions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין לוֹ גְּבִינִין, אוֹ אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא גְּבִין אֶחָד — זֶהוּ גִּבֵּן הָאָמוּר בְּתוֹרָה. רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר: (שֶׁגְּבִינִין) [שֶׁגְּבִינָיו] שׁוֹכְבִין. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּין וּשְׁתֵּי שִׁדְרָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a priest **has no eyebrows, or** if **he has only one eyebrow, that is the *gibben* that is stated in the Torah** in the list of blemished priests (see Leviticus 21:20). **Rabbi Dosa says:** A *gibben* is one **whose eyebrows** are so long that they **lie** flat and cover his eyes. **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says:** A *gibben* is one **who has two backs and two spines.**

The *mishna* states: *ein lo gavinim* — if a person does not have eyebrows — or *ein lo ela gavin echad* — only one eyebrow. Rashi explains that the case of one eyebrow could refer to one that fell out over only one eye, but also interprets it as a single continuous eyebrow extending over both eyes. Either way, this is identified as *zehu giben* — the *giben* mentioned in the *Torah*. Rabbi Dosa interprets *giben* differently: the eyebrows are very long and the hair hangs down considerably over the eyes. Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos takes an entirely different approach, deriving *giben* not from *gavinim* (eyebrows) but from *gav* (back), and defining it as a person who has two separate spinal cords and two backs — a rather extraordinary condition.

### Linguistic Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְגִבֵּן — דְּאֵין לוֹ מַשְׁמַע? וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״גִּבֵּן״ — שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ גְּבִינִין הַרְבֵּה, אֵין לוֹ גְּבִינִין, אוֹ אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא גְּבִין אַחַד — מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ גִבֵּן״. אָמַר רָבָא: זֶהוּ מִדְרָשׁ ״אוֹ גִּבֵּן״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the *gibben* stated in the Torah is one who lacks eyebrows. The Gemara asks: **But** does ***gibben* mean** one **who does not have** eyebrows? **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: When it is stated that a priest who is **a *gibben*** is disqualified from performing the Temple service (see Leviticus 21:20), this is referring to one **who has many eyebrows.** With regard to one **who has no eyebrows or who has only one eyebrow, from where** is it derived that he is disqualified as well? **The** same **verse states: "Or a *gibben*."** Clearly, the disqualification of one lacking eyebrows is not derived from the mention of a *gibben*, but from the term "or." **Rava said** the mishna should be understood as follows: One who has no eyebrows is disqualified, and **this is** derived from **the exposition** of the phrase **"or a *gibben*."**

The *gemara* raises an immediate difficulty: *ha-giben de'eino mashma* — the word *giben* does not obviously convey the meaning of "lacking eyebrows." In fact, from its plain sense one might expect *giben* to mean especially long or prominent eyebrows. The linguistic situation is genuinely paradoxical: the word is used in one direction to mean an absence and in the other to mean an excess — a situation not entirely unlike certain English words that can mean opposite things depending on context. The *gemara* resolves this by pointing to a *braita* which uses *giben* explicitly to mean *sheyesh lo gavinim harbe* — one who has many eyebrows. So how does the *mishna* derive "no eyebrows" from the same word?

### Rava's Etymology

Rava explains: *zehu midrash o giben* — when the *mishna* says "this is the *giben* mentioned in the *Torah*," it means this is the *o giben*, the "or *giben*" — the teaching is derived from the word *o* (or) in the *Torah*'s verse. The word *giben* fundamentally means "many," and the *o* comes to extend the disqualification to the opposite extreme: too few, or none at all.

### Two Backs Viability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר כּוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דְּחָיֵי? וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: הַמַּפֶּלֶת בְּרִיָּה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ שְׁנֵי גַּבִּין וּשְׁתֵּי שִׁדְרָאוֹת, וְאָמַר רַב: בָּאִשָּׁה — אֵינוֹ וָולָד, בַּבְּהֵמָה — אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Dosa says** a *gibben* is one whose eyebrows are so long that they lie flat and cover his eyes, and Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says that a *gibben* is one who has two backs and two spines. The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that** a person with two backs and two spines is capable of **living? But it was stated** that the Sages discussed the case of **one who miscarries an entity that has two backs and two spines, and Rav said** that **in** the case of **a woman** who miscarries such an entity, it **is not** considered **an offspring,** as it cannot survive, and therefore she does not have the ritual impurity caused by childbirth, and **in** the case of **an animal** who miscarries, the entity is **prohibited for consumption.**

Turning to Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos's interpretation — two backs and two spinal cords — the *gemara* asks whether such a person could in fact live. A *braita* is cited: if a woman miscarries a fetus that had two backs and two backbones, she is not *teme'a leida*, because it is not considered to have ever been viable. Rav adds: *be'isha eino valad* — in a woman it is not considered a child at all; *be'veheima assur b'achilah* — in an animal it cannot even be eaten, since the animal was never viable and is therefore essentially like a *neveilah*.

### Bent Spine Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּבָר רְמָא נִיהֲלֵיהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא לְרַב, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: שִׁימִי אֶת! אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁשִּׁדְרָתוֹ עֲקוּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya already raised this contradiction to Rav,** his grandfather, **and** Rav **said to him:** Are **you** really **Shimi?** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus is not referring literally to someone with two backs and two spines, **but to** a person **whose spine is crooked,** causing it to appear as though he has two spines. Such a person can survive but is disqualified from performing the Temple service.

This creates a problem: the *mishna* appears to discuss a person with two backs as a living individual capable of performing *avoda*, yet the *braita* and Rav indicate such a being is never viable. Rav Shimi bar Chiya had in fact already raised this very question to Rav — the *gemara* notes that Rav's response was introduced with the phrase *Shimi at*, a phrase the *gemara* debates: is it an expression of affectionate exasperation ("Oh, Shimi, such a good question!") or gentle rebuke ("Is this really a question worthy of you, Shimi?"). Either way, Rav's answer is: *ela she'shidraso akuma* — it does not literally mean two separate spines. The phrase "two backs" refers to a spine so severely bent and curved that it appears as though there are two. So extreme a curvature that it looks like a double back — that is the condition being described, and that is what constitutes the *giben* according to Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos. The *mishna* is not describing conjoined twins or a creature with two literal spines; it is describing a person with a profoundly deformed spine.

## Nose Eye Blemishes

### Charum & Eye Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֶחָרוּם פָּסוּל. אֵיזֶהוּ חָרוּם? הַכּוֹחֵל שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו כְּאַחַת. שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַעְלָה, וּשְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַטָּה, עֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְעֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַטָּה, רוֹאֶה אֶת הַחֶדֶר וְאֶת הָעֲלִיָּיה כְּאֶחָד, סָכֵי שֶׁמֶשׁ, וְהַזַּגְדָּן, וְהַצַּיְרָן, וְשֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ רִיסֵי עֵינָיו — פָּסוּל מִפְּנֵי מַרְאִית הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The *ḥarum* is disqualified** from performing the Temple service. **What is a *ḥarum*?** It is **one who** can **paint both of his eyes as one,** with one brushstroke, because he has a sunken nose. If **both of one's eyes** are **above or both of his eyes** are **below;** or if **one of his eyes** is **above and one of his eyes** is **below;** or if both eyes are in the proper place but **he sees** both **the room** on the ground floor **and the upper story as one,** at the same time; and likewise those unable to **look at the sun; and one whose eyes are different; and one whose eyes tear constantly,** these are disqualified from performing the Temple service. **And one whose eyelashes have fallen out** is **disqualified** from performing the Temple service **due to the appearance** of a blemish. Unlike the others listed in this mishna, who are disqualified by Torah law, one with this condition is not disqualified by Torah law. Rather, the Sages issued a decree prohibiting a priest with such a condition to perform the Temple service.

The next *mishna* begins: *ha-charum pasul* — one who is *charum* is disqualified. *Charum* is one of the conditions enumerated in the *Torah*, and the *mishna* proceeds to define it: *she-chotmo she-bein einav k'achat* — that his nose bridge between the eyes is absent, so depressed that one could paint both eyes with makeup simultaneously without any barrier interrupting. The *mishna* then enumerates a series of additional conditions: *shtei einav l'ma'ala* or *l'mata* — both eyes placed too high or too low in the skull; *eino achat l'ma'ala ve'eino achat l'mata* — one eye high and the other low; *ro'eh et ha-chadar ve'et ha-aliya k'echad* — cross-eyed vision, where one eye looks at the ground-floor room and the other at the attic simultaneously. *Sachif shemesh* designates someone with extreme sensitivity to sunlight, unable to be outdoors in direct sun.

The *mishna* also names *ha-zagdan* and *ha-tziran*, which the *gemara* will explain. Finally: *she-nashru risei einav pasul mipnei maris ayin* — if the eyelashes have fallen out, the person is disqualified, but only on account of *maris ayin*. It is not a real *mum* and not even a genuine *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* violation. The *gemara* had already addressed how far back this qualification of *mipnei maris ayin* reaches into the *mishna*, and concluded that it explains only the very last case.

### Charum Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״חָרוּם״ — שֶׁחוֹטְמוֹ שָׁקוּעַ, חוֹטְמוֹ סוֹלֵד, חוֹטְמוֹ בּוֹלֵם, חוֹטְמוֹ נוֹטֵף, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹ חָרוּם״. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵין ״חָרוּם״ אֶלָּא הַכּוֹחֵל שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו כְּאַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הִפְלַגְתָּ,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When it is stated that **a *ḥarum*** is considered a blemished priest (Leviticus 21:18), this is referring to a priest **whose nose is sunk.** If **his nose retreats,** i.e., it is very short and contracted upward, or if **his nose** is **blocked,** or if **his nose** is so long that it **droops, from where** is it derived that he is considered blemished? **The** same **verse states: "Or a *ḥarum*."** The term "or" serves to include all blemishes of the nose. **Rabbi Yosei says: A *ḥarum* is only one who** can **paint both of his eyes as one. They said to him: You have gone** too **far** in limiting your definition of a *ḥarum*.

The *gemara* presents a *braita*: *charum she-chotmo shaku'a* — one whose nose is depressed or pushed inward. But what of other nasal abnormalities? *Chotmo soled* — a very narrow, flat nose. *Chotmo bolen* — a sealed or fused nose, with closed nostrils. *Chotmo notef* — a nose that permanently drips. The *gemara* derives the inclusion of all these from the word *o* in the *Torah*'s phrase *o charum*: the *o* extends the disqualification to any significant nasal problem, all of which constitute a genuine *mum*. One might observe that some of these — such as the dripping nose — seem less severe than conditions classified merely as *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon*; the conceptual boundary is not always intuitive.

### Rabbi Yossi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ כּוֹחֵל שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו כְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **even if he does not paint both of his eyes as one,** any priest with an abnormally sunken nose is considered a *ḥarum*.

Rabbi Yossi limits the disqualification: *ein charum ela ha-kocha shtei einav ke-achas* — the only case is where the nose is so depressed that one could paint both eyes in a single stroke without any obstruction. The *chachamim* reject this as an exaggeration — *hiflaga* — and rule that the nose need not be depressed to quite that extreme. Even without reaching the threshold where both eyes can be painted simultaneously, a sufficiently depressed nose bridge is a *mum*.

### Eye Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַטָּה. מָה ״שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַעְלָה״ ״שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַטָּה״? אִילֵּימָא שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַעְלָה — דְּחָזֵי לְמַעְלָה, שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַטָּה — דְּחָזֵי לְמַטָּה, עֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְעֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַטָּה — דַּחֲזַאי עֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַטָּה וְעֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַעְלָה, הַיְינוּ ״רוֹאֶה אֶת הַחֶדֶר וְאֶת הָעֲלִיָּיה כְּאֶחָת״! אֶלָּא, שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַעְלָה — דְּקָיְימָן לְמַעְלָה, שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַטָּה — דְּקָיְימָן לְמַטָּה, עֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְעֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַטָּה — דְּקָיְימִי עֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְעֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַטָּה, וְקָיְימִי כְּסִדְרָן נָמֵי, וְרוֹאֶה אֶת הַחֶדֶר וְאֶת הָעֲלִיָּיה כְּאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if both of one's eyes are above or **both of his eyes** are **below,** he is disqualified from performing the Temple service. The Gemara asks: **What** does the mishna mean when it mentions a priest wherein **both of his eyes** are **above** or **both of his eyes** are **below? If we say** that the phrase: **Both of his eyes** are **above,** means **that he** constantly **gazes upward,** and the phrase: **Both of his eyes** are **below,** means **that** he constantly **gazes downward,** then the case where **one of his eyes** is **above and one of his eyes** is **below** must similarly mean **that he gazes** with **one of his eyes downward and one of his eyes upward.** But if so, **this is** identical to the next case in the mishna, that one **sees** both **the room** on the ground floor **and the upper story as one.**

The *gemara* then turns to the cases of eye placement: *she-einav l'ma'ala* and *she-einav l'mata*. One might initially read these as referring to the direction of the eyes' gaze — that the eyes are constantly looking upward or downward. But this cannot be correct, because the *mishna* itself later addresses visual direction separately, with the case of one eye looking at the room and the other at the attic. The end of the *mishna* already covers the directional issue. Therefore the opening cases must refer to physical location: the eyes are situated abnormally high or abnormally low within the skull itself. *She-einav l'ma'ala de-kayimi l'ma'ala* — both eyes are physically placed too high; *she-einav l'mata de-kayimi l'mata* — both are too low; *eino achat l'ma'ala ve-eino achat l'mata* — one is situated too high and the other too low within the skull. And even when the eyes are in their proper physical locations, if they nonetheless track in opposite directions — one looking toward the floor and the other toward the ceiling — that is also a disqualifying condition.

### Eye Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּעֵינוֹ — כׇּל מָה שֶׁבְּעֵינוֹ, מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַטָּה, שְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו לְמַעְלָה, עֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְעֵינוֹ אַחַת לְמַטָּה, וְרוֹאֶה אֶת הַחֶדֶר וְאֶת הָעֲלִיָּיה כְּאֶחָת, אוֹ שֶׁמְּדַבֵּר עִם חֲבֵירוֹ וְאַחֵר אוֹמֵר: לִי רוֹאֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to blemishes that disqualify priests: "Or a cataract or a *tevallul* in his eye" (Leviticus 21:20). Since it is obvious that a cataract or a *tevallul* can occur only in one's eyes, why does the verse state: In his eye? The term **"in his eye"** indicates that **any** blemish **that is in his eye** disqualifies him from performing the Temple service. **From here** the Sages **stated:** If **both of his eyes** are **below** or **both of his eyes** are **above,** or if **one of his eyes** is **above and one of his eyes** is **below,** or if **he sees the room and the upper story as one, or** if **when he speaks with his friend another** person **says: He is looking at me,** he is blemished and is disqualified from performing the Temple service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עִוֵּר — בֵּין סוֹמֵא בִּשְׁתֵּי עֵינָיו, בֵּין סוֹמֵא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו. חֲוַרְוָור וְהַמַּיִם הַקְּבוּעִין מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אִישׁ עִוֵּר״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When it is stated that **a blind man [*iver*]** is disqualified from performing the Temple service (see Leviticus 21:18), this applies **whether** he is **blind in both of his eyes or blind in one of his eyes.** With regard to a priest who cannot see due to **pale spots** on the eye **or** due to **tears** streaming from the eye **that are constant, from where** is it derived that he is disqualified? **The** same **verse states: "A blind man [*ish iver*]."** The superfluous word "*ish*" serves to include these conditions as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אִישׁ״, ״עִוֵּר״, ״דַּק״, ״תְּבַלּוּל״, ״בְּעֵינוֹ״? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״עִוֵּר״ מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ כְּלָל, אֲבָל חֲוַרְוָור וְהַמַּיִם הַקְּבוּעִין דְּאִיתַנְהוּ בֵּיהּ — לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אִישׁ״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״אִישׁ״ — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קָא חָזֵי כְּלָל, אֲבָל מַחְסוֹרַיְיתָא — לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״דַּק״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״דַּק״ — מִשּׁוּם דִּמְחַסְּרָן, אֲבָל מְבַלְבְּלִיתָא — לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״תְּבַלּוּל״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״תְּבַלּוּל״ — מִשּׁוּם דִּמְבַלְבְּלָן, אֲבָל מִשּׁוּם מְשַׁנְּיוּתָא — לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בְּעֵינוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Why do I** need **the Merciful One to write** the terms **"man," "blind," "cataract," "*tevallul*,"** and **"in his eye"** (Leviticus 21:18, 20)? All these **are necessary, as had the Merciful One written** only **"blind,"** then one might say that a blind person is disqualified **because** his eyes **are not in** their sockets **at all,** i.e., they were enucleated. **But** in the case of a priest with **pale spots** on the eye **or tears** streaming from the eye **that are constant, where** his eyes **are** still **in** their sockets but he cannot see, perhaps he is **not** considered blemished. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote "man"** to include these conditions as well. **And had the Merciful One written** only **"man,"** one might have thought that only total blindness disqualifies a priest, **because he cannot see at all. But** if his condition causes merely **impaired** vision, then he is **not** considered blemished. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote "cataract"** to include conditions of impaired vision as well. **And had the Merciful One written** only that a priest with **a cataract** is disqualified, it might have been thought that such a priest is disqualified **because** his vision is **impaired. But** if his vision is not impaired but his eyes are **mixed,** i.e., the black and white parts of his eyes are mixed together, then he is **not** disqualified. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote "*tevallul*."** **And had the Merciful One written** only **"*tevallul*,"** one might say that this disqualifies a priest **because** his eyes are **mixed. But** the Torah does **not** disqualify a priest **due to a deviation** from normal appearance, e.g., especially large eyes, or eyes situated higher than normal. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote "in his eye."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכָּךְ, כֹּל מֵחֲמַת כַּהְיוּתָא — אָתְיָא מֵ״אִישׁ״, מַחְסוֹרַיְיתָא — מִ״דַּק״, מְבַלְבְּלִיתָא — מִ״תְּבַלּוּל״, מְשַׁנְּיוּתָא — מִ״בְּעֵינוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** In summary, **Rava says: Therefore, all** blemishes of the eyes that disqualify a priest **on account of dimness,** i.e., blindness, **are derived from** the term **"man."** All blemishes of the eyes that are related to vision **impairment** are derived **from "cataract."** All blemishes that are related to parts of the eye being **intermingled** are derived **from "*tevallul*."** Finally, the disqualification of **deviations** is derived **from "in his eye."**

The *gemara* derives the inclusion of all these from the phrase *be-eino* — in his eye — which extends coverage to all conditions affecting the eye. A *braita* then articulates the different categories: *iver* — blind, whether in both eyes or only one; *chivrar* — dots or patches in the eye; *ha-mayim ha-kevu'im* — a fixed, non-ceasing discharge of water from the eye, which causes a blurring or diminishment of vision. The derivation is from the word *ish* in *ish iver*, which extends coverage to conditions analogous to blindness even when vision is not entirely absent.

Rava asks the natural question: *v'lamah li d'katav Rachmana ish iver, dak, tavlul be-eino* — why does the *Torah* need all of these separate categories? Are they not redundant? He answers that each is necessary, because without any one of them the others would not suffice.

Had the *Torah* written only *iver* — blind — one might have thought the disqualification applies only where there is no sight whatsoever — *d'leit lei klal*. Cases like *chivrar* or *mayim ha-kevu'im*, where some vision remains even if impaired, would not be included. The word *ish*, which is an additional, seemingly superfluous word, comes to include even partial blindness and vision-diminishing conditions. Conversely, had the *Torah* written only *ish* — extending to cases of limited vision — without specifying *dak*, one might have concluded only conditions of true blindness or near-blindness are covered, not a film or cataract (*dak*) that causes blurry but not absent vision. Had the *Torah* written only *dak* — the film that blurs vision — one might have excluded *tavlul*, the white streak that crosses from the white of the eye into the pupil, on the grounds that *tavlul* does not necessarily diminish vision; it disrupts the eye aesthetically and structurally but may leave vision intact. And had the *Torah* written only *tavlul* — where the normal segmentation of the eyeball is disrupted — one might have excluded *be-eino*, the category encompassing eyes that are in the wrong location or tracking in mismatched directions, since in those cases the eyes are not themselves internally disordered, only misplaced or misaligned.

Rava concludes with the principle: *hilkhakh* — therefore — *kol machmats kihuta* — anything involving diminished or weakened vision — is derived from *ish*; *machasurita* — lacking vision, blurry vision — from *dak*; *mivulbalita* — the eye being internally mixed or structurally disrupted — from *tavlul*; and *mishniyata* — eyes that are in different places from where they should be, or tracking differently from one another — from *be-eino*. These are genuinely distinct conceptual categories, not mere stylistic variation. The observation is worth pausing on: some of the most visually obvious abnormalities — eyes placed dramatically too high or too low in the skull — fall into the least severe conceptual category, derived from the most general term, while conditions like cataracts or internal eye lesions, which may be nearly invisible to an outside observer, are classified more severely because they actually eliminate vision. This further complicates any simple aesthetic account of *mumim*.

### Zagdayan Mismatch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָכֵי שֶׁמֶשׁ. תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: ״סָנֵי שֶׁמֶשׁ״. זַגְדְּיָין. מַחְוֵי רַב הוּנָא: חַד מִדִּידַן וְחַד מִדִּידְהוּ, וְאִיקְּפַד רַב יְהוּדָה. מֵיתִיבִי: שָׁכְבָנָא — שֶׁגְּבִינָיו שׁוֹכְבִים, זַגְדּוֹם — אֶחָד שָׁחוֹר וְאֶחָד לָבָן. תַּנָּא: כֹּל זוּגָא דְּלָא שָׁוֵי לַהֲדָדֵי, ״זַגְדּוֹם״ קָרֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that those unable to **look at the sun [*sakhei shemesh*]** are disqualified from performing the Temple service. **Rav Yosef taught** that this is a reference to those who **hate the sun [*sanei shemesh*],** i.e., they are incapable of opening their eyes in sunlight. The mishna further states that **one whose eyes are different** is disqualified from performing the Temple service. In order to illustrate this blemish, **Rav Huna pointed** with his finger while saying: The term: Different eyes, is referring to an individual wherein **one** of his eyes is like **ours and one** of his eyes is like **theirs. And** since Rav Huna was pointing at Rav Yehuda, who had abnormal eyes, **Rav Yehuda took offense. The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita* to Rav Huna's interpretation of one whose eyes are different: When the Sages taught that **a *shekhavna*** is disqualified from performing the Temple service, they were referring to one **whose eyebrows lie** flat [***shokhvim***] and cover his eyes. When they taught that **one whose eyes are different [*zagdum*]** is disqualified, they were referring to a case where **one** of his eyebrows is **black and one** is **white.** The Gemara responds: With regard to **any pair** of limbs **that are not identical to one another,** the ***tanna*** of this *baraita* **calls** them **different.**

Rav Huna demonstrates the meaning of *zagdayan* by pointing to two people standing before him and indicating: one of my eyes and one of his. Rav Yehuda, who was apparently standing there, was offended by being pointed to in this way. Rashi explains simply that the eyes of the two people looked different. Tosafos, however, brings evidence from other *Gemaros* — noting that Rav Huna was apparently a somewhat smaller man while Rav Yehuda was larger — to argue that *zagdayan* refers to mismatched eyes, specifically one eye that is small and another that is conspicuously large. The offense taken by Rav Yehuda is consistent with this: he was being singled out as the example of the unusually large eye.

Tosafos also notes that the word *zagdayan* or *zagdum* is etymologically connected to *zug* — the Rabbinic Hebrew word for a pair — and proposes that what appears in the text as a *mem* ending is a regularized Hebrew form of what was originally a Greek-derived *samech* ending: *zagdos*, where *dos* reflects the Greek *duo*, meaning two. The word thus denotes an ill-matched pair — two things that should be equal but are not.

The *gemara* then generalizes: *tana kol zuga delo shavi lahadadi zagdos karei leh* — any pair that is not evenly matched is called *zagdos*. This raises the question of whether the principle extends to all paired limbs or specifically to the eyes. The eyes, being higher on the body and more visible, are the primary case, but a *mishna* shortly to come will address disproportionate limbs more broadly.

### Tziran Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַצַּיְרָן. עֵינָיו תְּרוּטוֹת, וְצִירָנִיּוֹת, דּוֹמְעוֹת, דּוֹלְפוֹת, טוֹרְדוֹת. תַּנָּא: הַזִּדֵּיר וְהַלּוּפְיָן (וְהַתָּמִיר) [וְהַתִּימְיָן]. זִוֵּיר — דִּמְזַוַּר עֵינֵיהּ, לוּפְיָן — דִּנְפִישִׁין זִיפֵיהּ, (תָּמִיר) [תִּימְיָן] — שֶׁתַּמּוּ (זִיפִין) [זִיפָיו]. וְהָנֵי גַּבֵּי מוּמִין תָּנוּ לְהוּ? וְהָתְנַן: שֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ רִיסֵי עֵינָיו פָּסוּל מִפְּנֵי מַרְאִית הָעַיִן! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאִשְׁתְּיוּר גַּרְדּוּמֵי, הָא דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּיוּר גַּרְדּוּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one whose eyes tear constantly** is disqualified from performing the Temple service. The Gemara cites a *baraita* that defines such an individual: **His eyes,** i.e., his eyelids, **droop** and can open only partially, **or** his eyes are **round;** his eyes **tear,** or tears **stream** from his eyes, or tears **flow** intensely from them. The Sages taught in a *baraita*: **The *zadir*, and the *lupin*, and the *tamir*** are disqualified from performing the Temple service. ***Zadir*** is a reference to one **whose eyes are strange. *Lupin*** is a reference to one **with an abundance of eyelashes. *Tamir*** is a reference **to one whose eyelashes are depleted.** The Gemara asks: **And** does the *tanna* **teach these** cases together **with** full-fledged **blemishes? But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One whose eyelashes have fallen out is disqualified** from performing the Temple service **due to the appearance** of a blemish? Clearly, this is not considered an actual blemish. The Gemara answers that it is **not difficult. This** mishna is referring to a case **where the roots** of his eyelashes **remain,** whereas **that** *baraita* is referring to a case **where the roots do not remain.**

The *gemara* teaches that *hatziran* refers to eyes that are *trutot ve-tziraniot* — Rashi explains these as referring to eyes that are overly circular in shape, as opposed to the normal almond shape, with two gradations of circularity. The *gemara* also categorizes several degrees of watery eyes: *dumot* — mildly teary; *lofot* — streaming more significantly; *tzerodot* — gushing. Tosafos questions why it is necessary to enumerate the more extreme degrees once the mildest case has established the principle. Tosafos answers that the range is given to indicate that even at the extreme end of the spectrum — constantly flowing eyes — the condition remains only in the category of *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* and does not rise to the level of an actual *mum* that invalidates the *avoda* retroactively. The enumeration thus defines both ends of a spectrum that remains, throughout its range, within the lesser category.

The *gemara* further enumerates: *ha-zadir* — eyes that are constantly moving and cannot rest (the *Shita Mekubetzes* explains *she-eino yachol lanuach mi-mshotetot*); *ha-lufan* — eyelashes that are excessively long; *ha-tamir* — eyelashes that are entirely absent.

### Eyelash Loss

The last condition, absent eyelashes, creates a tension with the *mishna*, which classified missing eyelashes as only a *maris ayin* problem. The *gemara* resolves this with a precise distinction: *ha de-ishtayer gardumai* — if the roots of the eyelashes remain even though the hair itself has fallen out, then it is only a *maris ayin* problem. *Ha de-lo ishtayer gardumai* — if not even the roots are present, then it rises to an actual *mum*. Even in the latter case, the further question applies whether "actual *mum*" here means a genuine biblical *mum* or merely the intermediate category of *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon*.

## Body Proportions

### Size Disproportions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עֵינָיו גְּדוֹלוֹת כְּשֶׁל עֵגֶל, אוֹ קְטַנּוֹת כְּשֶׁל אַוָּוז. גּוּפוֹ גָּדוֹל מֵאֵבָרָיו, אוֹ קָטָן מֵאֵבָרָיו. חוֹטְמוֹ גָּדוֹל מֵאֵבָרָיו, אוֹ קָטָן מֵאֵיבָרָיו. הַצִומֵּם וְהַצִּומֵּעַ — אֵיזֶהוּ צִומֵּעַ? כֹּל שֶׁאׇזְנָיו קְטַנּוֹת, וְהַצִּומֵּם — כֹּל שֶׁאׇזְנָיו דּוֹמוֹת לִסְפוֹג. שְׂפָתוֹ הָעֶלְיוֹנָה עוֹדֶפֶת עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה, הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה עוֹדֶפֶת עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם. וְשֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ שִׁינָּיו — פָּסוּל מִפְּנֵי מַרְאִית הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna lists additional blemishes that disqualify a priest from performing the Temple service: If a priest's **eyes are large like those of a calf or small like those of a goose;** if **his body is** disproportionately **large** relative **to his limbs or** disproportionately **small** relative **to his limbs;** if **his nose is** disproportionately **large** relative **to his limbs or** disproportionately **small** relative **to his limbs,** he is disqualified. And **the *tzomem* and the *tzome'a*** are also disqualified. **What is a *tzome'a*?** It is **anyone whose ears are small. And** what is **the *tzomem*?** It is **anyone whose ears are similar to a sponge.** If **his upper lip protrudes beyond the lower** lip or his **lower** lip **protrudes beyond the upper** lip, **that is a blemish. And one whose teeth fell out is disqualified due to the appearance** of a blemish.

The *mishna* continues with conditions of disproportion. Eyes that are as large as a calf's or as small as a duck's are disqualifying — the eyes themselves may be paired and matching, but if they are strikingly outside the normal range for human eyes, that is a *mum*. More broadly: *gufo gadol me-evarav* — a body disproportionately large relative to the limbs — or *katan me-evarav* — disproportionately small. Similarly, a nose disproportionately large or small relative to the other features of the face.

### Ear Issues

*Hatzomem* — ears that resemble a sponge, what would today be called cauliflower ear. *Ha-tzome'a* — ears that are particularly small. Both are listed as *mumim*.

### Lip Protrusion

If the upper lip protrudes over the lower or the lower over the upper — *sfatov elyonah odefet al ha-tachtonah* or vice versa — that is a *mum*. Here again, the *mishna* uses the language of *mum*, while the *gemara* earlier reframed at least some such conditions as *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* rather than a full biblical blemish.

### Moses Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּפְרֹשׂ אֶת הָאֹהֶל עַל הַמִּשְׁכָּן״, מִי פְּרָשׂוֹ? מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ פְּרָשׂוֹ, וּכְתִיב: ״עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת אוֹרֶךְ הַקָּרֶשׁ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא לְרַב: אִם כֵּן עֲשִׂיתוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ בַּעַל מוּם! דִּתְנַן: גּוּפוֹ גָּדוֹל מֵאֵבָרָיו אוֹ קָטָן מֵאֵבָרָיו. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שִׁימִי! בְּאַמָּה שֶׁל קֹדֶשׁ קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav says: Moses, our teacher, was ten *ammot*** tall, **as it is stated: "And he spread the tent over the Tabernacle"** (Exodus 40:19). **Who spread** the tent over the Tabernacle? **Moses, our teacher, spread it, and it is written** with regard to the height of the Tabernacle: **"Ten *ammot* shall be the length of a board"** (Exodus 26:16). Moses must have been at least as tall as the Tabernacle itself for him to have spread the tent over it. **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya said to Rav: If so, you have rendered Moses, our teacher, a blemished** person, **as we learned** in the mishna: If **one's body is** disproportionately **large** relative **to his limbs or** disproportionately **small** relative **to his limbs,** then he is blemished. A typical person is no more than three arm-lengths [*ammot*] tall, and yet Moses was apparently ten times taller than the length of his own arm. Rav **said to him: Shimi,** when I said that Moses was ten *ammot* tall, I **said** so **in** reference to the **cubit [*amma*] of a board** used in the building of the Tabernacle. The limbs of Moses were proportional to the rest of his body, but he was indeed ten cubits tall.

The *gemara* introduces a striking aggadic statement: Rav says that *Moshe Rabbeinu* was ten *amot* tall. The proof is from the verse *vayifros et ha-ohel* — "and he spread out the tent" over the *Mishkan*. The beams of the *Mishkan* were ten *amot* tall, and since *Moshe Rabbeinu* spread the covering over them, he must have been at least that tall.

Rav Shimi bar Chiya immediately objects: if so, you have made *Moshe Rabbeinu* a *baal mum*, since the *mishna* disqualifies a person whose body is disproportionately large or small relative to his limbs. The problem is that if one assumes the *amah* referred to for the *keresh* is *Moshe*'s own arm-length, then *Moshe* was ten times his own arm-length tall — whereas a normal person according to the *gemara* is three *amot* to the shoulders by standard measure. Rav answers: *be-amah shel keresh ka-amar* — Moshe's ten *amot* are measured in the standard *amah* used to measure the beams, not in units derived from Moshe's own arm. He was tall, but proportionate to his own limbs.

One might ask: if a midget — who was mentioned earlier as potentially falling under *zera shel Aharon* — is disqualified by being too small, why would a giant not be disqualified for being too large? This tension is acknowledged. It further underscores that the framework is not simply about distraction: a giant is, if anything, more visually striking than a person of short stature, yet the *gemara* does not categorically disqualify exceptional height.

## Animal vs Human

### Non-Blemish Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: עֵז שֶׁאֵין לָהּ קַרְנַיִם, וְרָחֵל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ קַרְנַיִם — כְּשֵׁרִים לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יֵשׁ דְּבָרִים שֶׁהֵן כְּמוּמִין, וְאֵינָן כְּמוּמִין, וְשׁוֹחֲטִין עֲלֵיהֶם בְּמִקְדָּשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא בַּמְּדִינָה. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: עֵז שֶׁאֵין (לוֹ) [לָהּ] קַרְנַיִם, וְרָחֵל שֶׁיֵּשׁ (לוֹ) [לָהּ] קַרְנַיִם, וְהַצִּימֵּחַ, וְהַצִּימֵּם, וְהַצִּימֵּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the *halakha* of a *tzomem*, *tzome'a*, and *tzime'aḥ* with regard to animals. **Rav Ḥisda says: A female goat that has no horns and a female lamb that has horns are fit for** sacrificing **upon the altar,** i.e., they are not considered blemished, despite the fact that they differ from regular female goats and female lambs. The Gemara notes that **this is also taught** in a *baraita*: **There are matters that** appear **like blemishes but they are not** truly **like blemishes, and** one may therefore **slaughter** sacrificial animals **on** account of **them in the Temple,** i.e., they are fit for sacrifice despite these blemishes; **but** if they are firstborn animals, they are **not** permitted for slaughter **in the** rest of the **country** on account of these blemishes, as the unique physical characteristics of these animals are not true blemishes. **And these are** the blemishes: **A female goat that has no horns, and a female lamb that has horns, and the *tzime'aḥ*, and the *tzomem*, and the *tzome'a*.**

Rav Chisda rules: a goat that lacks horns or a sheep that has horns is *kasher le-gabei mizbe'ach* — acceptable as a *korban*. Although these are clearly non-standard physical characteristics for their respective species, they do not constitute *mumim* for animals. A *braita* elaborates: there are things that resemble *mumim* but are not — *yesh devarim she-hem ki-mumin ve-einam ki-mumin* — and an animal with these characteristics may be sacrificed in the *Beis Hamikdash* and does not provide grounds for *shechita* outside. The list includes a hornless goat, a horned sheep, and the *tzimach* and *tzome'ach* — the large-eared or sponge-eared conditions. This is precisely the *gemara*'s broader point: many conditions disqualify humans under the rubric of *eino shaveh b'zaro shel Aharon* without constituting *mumim* for animals at all. The categories are not symmetrical.

### Horn Hoof Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: נִטְּלוּ קַרְנוֹתָן וְזַכְרוּתָן עִמָּהֶן — פְּסוּלָה, וְאֵין פּוֹדִין עֲלֵיהֶם. נִטְּלוּ טְלָפַיִים וְזַכְרוּתָם עִמָּהֶן — פְּסוּלִין, וְנִפְדִּין עֲלֵיהֶם. מֵיתִיבִי: נִטְּלוּ קַרְנַיִין וּטְלָפַיִין וְזַכְרוּתָן עִמָּהֶן — פְּסוּלִין וְנִפְדִּין עֲלֵיהֶן! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִיתְעֲקוּר אִיתְעֲקוֹרֵי, הָא דְּאִיגּוּם אִיגּוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the horns of an animal, **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Ameimar says:** If **the horns** of an animal **were removed, and their bones,** i.e., the bones inside the horns, were removed **with them,** the animal **is unfit** for sacrifice. **But** even so, **one may not redeem** the animal **on account of** such blemishes, as they are not full-fledged blemishes. But if **the hooves were removed, and their bones,** i.e., the portion of the leg to which the hooves are attached, were removed **with them, they are unfit** for sacrifice **and they are** even **redeemed on account of** such blemishes, as they are full-fledged blemishes. Rav Ḥisda stated that the removal of a horn and its bone is not considered a full-fledged blemish that enables the animal to be redeemed. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **the horns or the hooves** of an animal **were removed, and their** respective **bones** were removed **with them, they are unfit and they are redeemed on account of them.** The Gemara explains that it is **not difficult. This** *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the bone **was** entirely **uprooted,** which is a full-fledged blemish on account of which the animal may be redeemed. **That** statement of Rav Ḥisda is referring to a case **where** the bones **were cut** but were not uprooted entirely.

Rav Chisda, citing Ameimar, addresses the case where a horn was removed together with its inner marrow bone — *zachrut*. Rashi explains that a horn grows around a central inner core that contains marrow; the outer horn material does not. If the horn was removed including this inner core, the animal is *pesulah* — disqualified as a *korban* — but may not be redeemed; the blemish is real but not severe enough to permit redemption outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. If the hooves were removed including the inner marrow bone, the condition is more severe and the animal may be redeemed.

A *braita* then creates an apparent contradiction: it states that removal of horns or hooves together with the marrow bone yields an animal that is *pesulah* and may be redeemed — applying the more severe standard to both. The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya* — there is no real contradiction. One case involves the horn being entirely uprooted — torn out from its base — while the other involves being cut down with the base still remaining. In the case of horns, if the base is still present, the defect is not severe enough to permit redemption; only if the horn was fully uprooted is that threshold reached.

### Para Aduma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְאִיגּוּם אִיגּוֹמֵי, פָּסוּל? וּרְמִינְהוּ: פָּרָה שֶׁקַּרְנֶיהָ וּטְלָפֶיהָ שְׁחוֹרִים — יָגוֹד! תַּרְגְּמַהּ זְעֵירִי: מֵעִילָּוֵי זַכְרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in a case **where** the bones of the animal **were cut,** is the animal even rendered **unfit? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Para* 2:2): With regard to a red **heifer whose horns and hooves are black,** the *halakha* is that **one should cut** away the black parts so that the heifer remains entirely red. Evidently, cutting away the horns of the heifer does not inflict a blemish upon it. **Ze'eiri interpreted** the mishna as referring to cutting the horn specifically **from above the bone** inside it. In such a case, the heifer is not disqualified. Cutting the bone itself does disqualify the animal.

A striking illustration of the range of severity: a *parah aduma* with black tips on its horns or hooves has a problem, since it is not *adumah tmimah* — completely red — as required. The solution is simply to cut off the black tip. The *gemara* notes that this cutting-off at the very tip, above the marrow bone — *me'ila de-shachor* — resolves the problem entirely, leaving the animal fully valid. This illustrates the most lenient end of the spectrum: a superficial cut that removes only the discolored tip, without touching the marrow, affects neither the *kedusha* nor the status of the animal at all.

## Systemic Conditions

### Condition List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דַּדִּין שׁוֹכְבִין כְּשֶׁל אִשָּׁה, כְּרֵיסוֹ צָבָה, טִיבּוּרוֹ יוֹצֵא, נִכְפֶּה — אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת לְיָמִים, רוּחַ קְצָרָה בָּאָה עָלָיו, הַמְאוּשְׁבָּן, וּבַעַל גֶּבֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna lists additional blemishes that disqualify a priest from performing the Temple service: One who has **breasts** so large that they **sag like** those **of a woman;** or if **one's belly is swollen** and protrudes; or if **one's navel protrudes;** or if one is **an epileptic, even** if he experiences seizures only **once in** a long **while;** or one **who is afflicted with a melancholy temper;** or **one whose scrotum is** unnaturally **long; or one whose penis is** unnaturally **long** is disqualified from performing the Temple service.

The *mishna* lists a series of conditions disqualifying a *kohen*: *dadin shochvin ke-shel isha* — breasts that hang down like a woman's; *kriso tzava* — a bloated belly; *tiburo yotze* — a protruding navel; *nichpeh* — epilepsy, even if it occurs only rarely; *ruach tzarah ba eilav* — which Tosafos explains as asthma; *ha-mushpan* — excessively large testicles; and *baal gever* — an oversized male member.

Several of these conditions raise a conceptual challenge. An epileptic condition that strikes only rarely is not visible by looking at the person; it is a medical condition that manifests intermittently. Similarly, a protruding navel — statistics suggest approximately ten percent of people have one — is a condition of normal variation that would seem to fall well within the range of ordinary human appearance. And yet both are listed as grounds for disqualification. If the concern were purely aesthetic, neither would obviously qualify. One possible answer in the case of epilepsy is that since a seizure could occur during the *avoda* itself, the condition is considered effectively present in the *avoda*-context, even if invisible in ordinary circumstances. Rashi adds that the *mishna*'s phrase *afilu achat l'yamim* means rare but not necessarily periodic — simply that it occurs even infrequently. A later medical observation is worth noting: evidence from seizure records suggests that truly periodic seizures — occurring on a fixed schedule — essentially do not occur clinically; seizures are either clustered or random. Whether Rashi's precision here has independent support is thus difficult to assess.

### Bloated Belly Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: מַשְׁתִּינִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים, וְאֵין שׁוֹתִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים. וְתַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מַשְׁתִּינִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים, וְאֵין שׁוֹתִין מַיִם בִּפְנֵי רַבִּים. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ לְהַשְׁתִּין מַיִם וְלֹא הִשְׁתִּין, וְנִמְצָא כְּרֵיסוֹ צָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara cites a *halakha* involving a swollen belly. **Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, says** that Torah scholars **may urinate in public** and they need not be concerned with issues of modesty, because holding back from urinating causes bodily harm. **But they may not drink water in public,** as such conduct is unbefitting a Torah scholar. **And this is also taught** in a *baraita*: Torah scholars **may urinate in public, but they may not drink water in public. And there was an incident involving one who sought to urinate, and he did not urinate, and his belly was found to be swollen.**

Rav Abba, son of Rav Chiya bar Abba, connects the condition of a bloated belly to the urgency of urination. A story is cited: a person needed to urinate, refrained from doing so, and as a result his belly became bloated. This grounds the *mishna*'s ruling on *kriso tzava* in a specific etiology — urinary retention leading to physical harm.

### Urination Imperative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ בְּשַׁבְּתָא דְרִגְלָא, נְגַדוּ לֵיהּ גְּלִימָא, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶתֵּן לָךְ אַרְבַּע מְאָה זוּזֵי וְזִיל אַהְדַּר עוֹבָדָא. אַתְּ — דְּאֶפְשָׁר לָךְ, דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר לֵיהּ — לִיסְתַּכַּן. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אִיצְטְרִיךְ אַגּוּדָּא גַּמְלָא, אַשְׁתֵּין. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: חֲמָתָךְ קָאָתְיָא! אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּאוּדְנַהּ. וְתִיפּוֹק לִי מִשּׁוּם עַלְקָא! בְּשׁוֹתֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: It **was necessary for Shmuel** to urinate **on the Shabbat** when everyone came to hear *halakhot* relating **to the** impending **Festival.** In order to afford him privacy, **they spread a sheet for him.** Shmuel then **came before his father,** who **said to him: I will give you four hundred dinars** if you will **go** and **retract** this **incident,** i.e., if you will state publicly that one may not hold back from urinating even at the expense of one's privacy. Since **you** are an important man, **you can** have others spread a sheet around you. But with regard to one **who cannot** have others spread a sheet **for him, should he endanger** himself by seeking privacy? You must therefore teach that one should not hold back from urinating even in public. The Gemara further relates that it **was necessary** for **Mar bar Rav Ashi** to urinate while he was walking **on the beams** of **a bridge,** and therefore he **urinated. They said to him: Your mother-in-law is coming.** Mar bar Rav Ashi **said to them:** I would have done this even **in her ear.** Since holding back from urinating would place him in physical danger, he would not care that his mother-in-law was coming and might see him. The Gemara returns to the incident cited in the *baraita* involving one who held back from urinating and his belly became swollen: **And let me derive,** i.e., can it not be said, that his belly swelled **due to a leech** that entered his intestines? The Gemara responds: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** his urine would thereafter **drip** and no longer flowed in a healthy manner. Evidently, his health problems resulted from damage to the urinary tract.

The *gemara* states: *mashtinin mayim bifnei rabbim* — a person may and indeed should urinate in front of other people rather than withhold urination for the sake of modesty. The medical urgency overrides the normal requirements of privacy. Alongside this, the *gemara* notes: *ve-ein shosin mayim bifnei rabbim* — one should not drink in public. Tosafos finds this pairing curious, noting that in France it was entirely ordinary to eat and drink outdoors. Tosafos offers two possible readings: perhaps the restriction applies only to water but not wine, or alternatively, the *gemara* is saying that even a person of particularly modest disposition who would ordinarily not drink publicly — even that person may go ahead and urinate in public, because the medical imperative overrides the consideration of modesty.

A story is told of Shmuel who needed to urinate during a *shabta de-rigla* — a public lecture delivered in the period before the *regel* to large crowds. He had cloaks spread around him for privacy, urinated, and then continued his lecture. He later reported this to his father, who told him: I will give you four hundred *zuz* — but go reverse what you have done. His father's concern was pedagogical: by arranging for privacy, Shmuel had inadvertently taught people that one needs to secure private conditions before urinating. Anyone who lacked the means to arrange such privacy would now feel compelled to wait — and in waiting would endanger their health. As Shmuel's father put it: *at d-efshar lach* — for you it was possible, and it took only a moment. *Delo efshar lei mistakein* — for someone for whom it is not possible, waiting will cause them genuine harm.

### Gershom's View

The instruction to "reverse the action" is interpreted differently by Rashi and Rabbeinu Gershom. Rashi understands it as an instruction to return and deliver a public lecture telling people they should not wait for privacy. Rabbeinu Gershom, however, reads it more powerfully: what you do is so much more impactful than what you say. A public action teaches in a way that a subsequent verbal correction — like a two-line retraction buried after a major front-page story — can never undo. Rabbeinu Gershom therefore interprets the instruction as: at your next public lecture, make a point of urinating openly in front of the crowd, so that your action negates the lesson of your previous action. This reading has considerable force, even if Rashi did not adopt it.

### Mar bar Rav Ashi

Mar bar Rav Ashi similarly needed to urinate while standing near a riverbank with nowhere private to go. When told that his mother-in-law was approaching, he replied: *be-udna* — I would do it even within her earshot. The response is not elegant, but the message is unambiguous: medical necessity supersedes social convention entirely. One does not wait under any circumstances.

### Male Anatomy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁנֵי נְקָבִים יֵשׁ בּוֹ בָּאָדָם, אֶחָד מוֹצִיא שֶׁתֶן וְאֶחָד מוֹצִיא שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, וְאֵין בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה אֶלָּא כִּקְלִיפַּת הַשּׁוּם. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָדָם נִצְרָךְ, אִם נִקְּבוּ זֶה לְתוֹךְ זֶה — נִמְצָא עָקָר.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this topic **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **There are two orifices in a man. One** of them **expels urine and one** of them **expels semen, and there is only** a partition **about** the thickness of **a garlic peel between them. When a man needs** to urinate and withholds the urine, **if** his urinary duct and seminal duct **were punctured** such that **this** duct leads **into that** one, **he** will be **found** to be **barren,** as his urine will penetrate into his seminal duct.

The *gemara* then offers its physiological understanding: a man has two internal passages — *ekhad motzi sheten ve-ekhad motzi shikhvat zera* — one for urine and one for semen. The two passages are separated by a membrane no thicker than garlic paper — *keli-fat ha-shum* — exceedingly thin. If a person withholds urination and the resulting pressure causes a perforation between these two passages, the seminal tract becomes compromised, and the person will become sterile — *nimtza akar*. The *gemara* thus identifies urinary retention as a potential cause not only of bloating but of permanent infertility. The underlying anatomical model — two distinct passages that converge — is not entirely inconsistent with actual male anatomy, in which the vas deferens feeds into the urethra at the level of the prostate. Whether the *gemara*'s model was derived from Galenic anatomy, from analogy to female anatomy where the urinary and reproductive orifices are separate, or from independent observation, cannot be determined with certainty.

## Theology Medicine

### Reish Lakish View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לֹא יִהְיֶה בְךָ עָקָר וַעֲקָרָה וּבִבְהֶמְתֶּךָ״? אֵימָתַי לֹא יִהְיֶה בְךָ עָקָר? בִּזְמַן שֶׁ״בִּבְהֶמְתֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "There shall not be male or female barren among you, or among your cattle"** (Deuteronomy 7:14)? It means as follows: **When will there not be a barren** male **among you? At a time that** you act as **among your cattle,** i.e., you urinate when the need arises, without hesitation.

Reish Lakish offers an interpretation of the verse *lo yihyeh vekha akar va-akarah u-bi-vehemetekha* — "there shall be no sterile among you, neither man nor woman, nor among your animals" — that reads it as behavioral instruction. When will you not become sterile? When you conduct yourself like an animal — meaning, when you are willing to urinate in public rather than hold back for social reasons, as an animal does. Reish Lakish reads *bi-vehemetekha* — "among your animals" — not as a parallel category but as the behavioral model: act in this respect like an animal, and you will avoid biological sterility.

### Rabbi Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: ״לֹא יִהְיֶה בְךָ עָקָר״ — מִן הַתַּלְמִידִים, ״וַעֲקָרָה״ — שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא תְּפִלָּתֶךָ עֲקוּרָה לִפְנֵי הַמָּקוֹם. אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁאַתָּה מֵשִׂים עַצְמְךָ כִּבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says,** with regard to the same verse, that the phrase **"There shall not be male**…**barren among you"** means you will not be barren **of students,** as one who leaves behind no students is considered barren. The term **"or female barren"** indicates **that your prayers will not be barren,** i.e., unheard, **before the Omnipresent,** but will be heard by Him. And **when** will they be heard? **At a time when you place yourself** in prayer as **an animal,** with humility.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi interprets the same verse in an entirely religious rather than biological register. *Lo yihyeh vekha akar* — do not be sterile among your students: ensure that you have students, who are the spiritual equivalent of children. *Va-akarah* — and do not let your prayer be *akurah*, barren; let your prayers be answered. How does one achieve this? *Bizman she-atah mesim atzmekha ke-vehemah* — by making oneself humble and lowly like an animal when one prays. The two readings of the verse thus sit alongside each other: one physiological and behavioral, the other theological and ethical.

### Rav Papa Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא יַשְׁתִּין אָדָם מַיִם לֹא עַל כְּלֵי חֶרֶס, וְלֹא עַל מְקוֹם קָשֶׁה, דְּאָמַר רַב: הָנֵי מְדוֹרֵי דְּבָבֶל מְהַדְּרִי מַיָּא לְעֵין עֵיטָם. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָא אִיתְּתָא לָא תְּקוּם לְהֶדְיָא בְּאַנְפֵּי יָנוֹקָא, אַגִּיסָא לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites additional statements of the Sages with regard to urination. **Rav Pappa says: A person** should urinate in Babylonia only on the dust, as it absorbs the urine. He **should not urinate either on an earthenware vessel or on a hard place,** as the urine will not be absorbed and will eventually flow into the underwater rivers. **As Rav says: Those inclines of Babylonia return the water** through underground watercourses **to the Spring of Eitam** in Eretz Yisrael, from which water was taken to the Temple. Additionally, **Abaye says: A woman should not stand openly in front of a child** when she urinates. But if she urinates while turning **sideways, we have no** problem **with it.**

Rav Papa adds a practical instruction: one should not urinate into pottery vessels or onto hard ground. The reason: Rav explains that the inclines of *Bavel* channel their water eventually toward *Ein Eitam*, the water source used in *Eretz Yisrael*. Pottery vessels, if used for urination, would be emptied into rivers; hard ground would cause the urine to flow into those same rivers; and those rivers ultimately feed the water supply of *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Tosafot Geography

Tosafos raises a geographical puzzle: the premise seems to be that rivers from *Bavel* flow into *Eretz Yisrael*, i.e., that the Euphrates flows from east to west into the land. Tosafos reports that he asked someone from *Eretz Yisrael* about this and was told the opposite — the Euphrates flows from *Eretz Yisrael* into *Bavel*, not the other way around. Tosafos leaves the question unresolved.

### Women Urination

Abaye introduces a distinction for women: a woman should not urinate even in front of young children. If, however, she steps to the side, the concern is mitigated and there is no problem. The *gemara*'s differentiation between men and women here may reflect either a view that the physiological danger of urinary retention was understood primarily in terms of male anatomy — specifically the risk of perforation between the urinary and seminal passages — or a recognition that social norms around female modesty created a different standard, with the understanding that women need not go to the same extreme as men.

### Retention Illnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: עַמּוּד הַחוֹזֵר מֵבִיא אָדָם לִידֵי הִדְרוֹקָן, סִילוֹן הַחוֹזֵר מֵבִיא אָדָם לִידֵי יֵרָקוֹן. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר רַב קַטִּינָא, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: דָּם רָבֶה — שְׁחִין רָבֶה, שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע רָבָה — צָרַעַת רָבָה, צוֹאָה רָבָה — הִדְרוֹקָן רָבֶה, מֵי רַגְלַיִם רָבִין — יֵרָקוֹן רָבֶה.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A mass** of feces **that is held back** without being discharged **causes a person** to suffer **from dropsy [*hidrokan*],** while **a stream** of urine **that is held back causes a person** to suffer **from jaundice. Rabba bar Rav Huna says** that **Rav Ketina says** that **Reish Lakish says:** If one's **blood** levels **increase** due to not undergoing bloodletting often enough, then **boils** will **increase** on him as well. If one's **semen** levels **increase** due to not engaging in sexual intercourse often enough, then **leprosy** will **increase** on him. If one's **feces** levels **increase** due to not relieving himself enough, **dropsy** will **increase** in him. Finally, if one's **urine** levels **increase** due to not relieving himself enough, then **jaundice** will **increase** as well.

A *braita* quotes Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: holding back defecation leads to *hadrokan* — Rashi identifies this as some form of serious digestive illness; holding back urination leads to *yarkon* — jaundice.

Rava bar Rav Huna, citing Rav — and the same statement is attributed to Reish Lakish — presents a comprehensive account of illnesses caused by the retention of bodily substances: *dam raba shechin raba* — excess blood, not periodically let through bloodletting, leads to boils and blisters; *shikhvat zera raba tzara'at raba* — excess semen, through insufficient marital relations, leads to leprosy; *tzoa raba hadrokan raba* — excess feces, from insufficient defecation, leads to *hadrokan*; *mei raglayim rabim yarkon raba* — excess urine, from holding back urination, leads to jaundice. The *gemara*'s medical framework is systematic: every category of retained bodily substance has its corresponding pathological consequence.
