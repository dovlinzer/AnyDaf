# Bava Metzia 89 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Worker Rights & Ox

### Opening

The daf opens in the middle of a cluster of *mitzvot* from the Torah concerning the rights of workers in the field. The Torah states that when one enters a friend's vineyard, the *Chazal* understand this as applying specifically to a worker who has been granted the right to eat from the produce he is harvesting — but not to store it away in his own vessel. In parallel, the Torah commands *lo tachsom shor bedisho*: one may not muzzle an ox while it is threshing. These two *mitzvot* together establish that those who work with produce in the field may eat from what they are handling, subject to carefully delimited conditions regarding the type of produce and the stage of its development.

The Gemara has been working through a fundamental question: how do we know that these *halachot* apply equally to detached produce? The Torah speaks of one who is actively picking grapes — produce still attached to the vine — or, in the parallel case of the ox, of an animal working with produce in that same attached state. Threshing, by contrast, involves grain already cut from the ground. How do we extend these laws from one context to the other?

### Torah Sources

The discussion that has been running through the preceding amud involves deriving these extensions from *pesukim*, and the Gemara has just concluded an argument via *gezeirah shavah*. We now arrive at an alternative position.

### Ravina's Position

*Ravina* asserts that no *gezeirah shavah* is needed at all. *Lo adam betalush velo shor bemechubar tzerichi k'ra* — neither the rule that a human worker may eat from already-detached produce, nor the rule that an ox may not be muzzled while working with still-attached produce, requires a special derivation. The reason: *lo tachsom shor bedisho* already contains both cases implicitly.

The argument unfolds at the top of 89a. Since we learn via *gezeirah shavah* from *Shabbat* — where the verse says *l'ma'an yanuach shorchecha*, "that your ox may rest," and expands this to *kol behemetecha*, all your animals — the prohibition of *lo tachsom shor* is likewise not limited to an ox. It applies to any animal involved in such work. If that is so, why did the Torah specifically write *shor*, ox? The Gemara answers: *leakushei chosem l'nechsam u'nechsam l'chosem* — to create an equation between the muzzler and the muzzled, between the human agent and the animal. Had the Torah simply written *lo tadush bechasimah* — do not thresh in a state of muzzling — it would have spoken only to one party. By writing *lo tachsom shor*, it places both the one who muzzles (the human) and the one being muzzled (the animal) within the same *pasuk*, forging an explicit equivalence.

The consequence is a double inference: just as the human muzzler (*chosem*) may eat from produce that is *mechubar*, attached to the ground, at the moment of its being detached, so too the muzzled animal (*nechsam*) may eat from *mechubar* produce. And just as the ox may eat from *talush*, already-detached produce — so long as *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser* has not yet occurred — so too the human worker may eat from *talush* produce. The word *shor* thus generates this full bilateral equation without any need for an additional *gezeirah shavah*.

---

## Gezerah Shavah

### Shor from Shabbat

The Gemara establishes that the word *shor* in *lo tachsom shor bedisho* cannot be read as limiting the prohibition to oxen alone. Through a *gezeirah shavah* linking the word *shor* here to the word *shor* in the Shabbat verse — *l'ma'an yanuach shorchecha* — where the context immediately expands to all animals (*kol behemetecha*) — we learn that here too, the prohibition and the associated *halachot* apply to any animal engaged in such work.

### Why 'Ox'?

This raises an immediate question: if the *gezeirah shavah* teaches that *shor* here is not meant to limit the law to oxen, then why did the Torah bother writing *shor* at all? The answer, as articulated above, is that the word serves a different structural purpose entirely. *Im ken lichtov Rachmana lo tadush bechasimah* — had the Torah simply stated "do not thresh in a muzzled state," it would have addressed only one dimension of the act. By writing *lo tachsom shor*, the Torah places both the human who does the muzzling and the animal that is muzzled side by side in a single formulation, enabling the equation between their respective rights and obligations.

### Logical Equation

The resulting logical equation operates in both directions. The *chosem*, the human muzzler, may eat from *mechubar* produce — produce still attached to the ground — at the moment of *gmar melacha* when it is being detached. This applies equally to the *nechsam*, the animal. Conversely, the *nechsam*, the animal, may eat from *talush* produce — produce already cut from the ground — until the *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser*; and the same right extends symmetrically to the *chosem*, the human worker. Ravina therefore derives all these extensions from the word *shor* itself, without recourse to any additional hermeneutic device.

## Threshing Baraitot

### Growth from Ground

The Gemara now introduces a series of *baraitot* that use the paradigm of *dayish* — threshing — to derive the scope and limits of the worker's right to eat and the prohibition of muzzling. The first *baraita* reasons as follows: *mah dayish meyuchad davar she'gidulei karka u'poel ochel bo* — what makes threshing distinct is that it involves things that grow from the ground, and a worker may eat from such produce. Therefore, *af kol she'gidulei karka poel ochel bo* — the same applies to anything that grows from the ground: a worker may eat from it under the relevant conditions.

The implication is restrictive as well as expansive. *Yatza hacholev vehamachbetz vehamagben* — this excludes the one who milks animals, the one who curdles milk, and the one who makes cheese. All of these are legitimate farm activities, but they do not involve *gidulei karka*, produce grown from the ground. Therefore, *ein poel ochel bo* — a worker engaged in milking or cheese-making does not have the Torah-granted right to consume a portion of what he is handling.

### Exclusions

The exclusion of milking, curdling, and cheese-making rests on a single principle: the Torah's entitlement to eat from one's labor applies only where the work is performed on produce that emerged from the ground. Animal products, even when produced on a farm, do not fall within this category. The worker handling milk or cheese may not invoke the prerogative of *lo tachsom shor bedisho* or the parallel *kerem re'acha* verse to claim the right to consume part of his employer's goods.

### Challenge

The Gemara challenges the necessity of deriving this rule from the paradigm of *dayish*. *Miki tavo bekerem re'acha nafka* — does this not already follow from the verse "when you enter your friend's vineyard"? A vineyard produces grapes; the parallel verse speaks of standing grain (*kama*). Both are explicitly agricultural, clearly *gidulei karka*. Why must we look to *dayish* to establish the ground-growth requirement?

### Response

The Gemara responds: *tzericha* — the repetition is necessary. One might have read the broad language of *kama* — standing grain ready for harvest — so expansively as to include even things that do not literally grow from the ground. Since the word *kama* is used to extend the *halacha* beyond vineyards to all manner of produce being gathered, one might suppose that any "standing" product awaiting collection — even animal products, in theory — could be included. The repetition via *dayish* comes to forestall this: no, the right is limited to what actually grows from the ground.

## Gmar Melacha

### Dayish Timing

A second *baraita* addresses a different dimension of *dayish*: its relationship to the concept of *gmar melacha*. The *baraita* reasons: *mah dayish meyuchad davar shebishat gmar melacha poel ochel bo* — what distinguishes *dayish* is that the worker eats from it at the time of *gmar melacha*, the completion of the work. And therefore, *af kol shebishat gmar melacha poel ochel bo* — similarly, for any produce, it is specifically at the time of *gmar melacha* that the worker's right to eat applies.

This formulation is admittedly difficult, since threshing is not itself the moment of *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser*. The full sequence runs: threshing, winnowing, sorting, smoothing the pile — and only that final smoothing (*meruach bakari*) renders grain obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*. Rashi reads *shebishat gmar melacha* as meaning that the worker is completing the work in this moment — *de'achshav hu gomer melachato* — though the precise meaning remains somewhat elusive. One workable reading is that *dayish* is the paradigm case where grain that is in the process of being brought to completion from its raw harvested state remains available for the worker to eat; it is from this paradigm that we derive the general principle that the worker may eat during the process that leads to *gmar melacha*, but not after.

### Weeding Excluded

The *baraita* applies its principle restrictively: *yatza hamenakesh beshumim ube-vatzalim* — this comes to exclude one who is weeding a field of garlic or onions. *Ho'il ve-ein gmar melacha ein poel ochel bahem* — since this weeding activity does not constitute *gmar melacha* — it is not an act of harvesting the produce from the ground — the worker engaged in this task has no right to eat from the garlic or onions.

### Challenge 2

The Gemara again challenges the derivation from *dayish*: *me'el kelyecha lo titein nafka* — does this not already follow from the verse *el kelyecha lo titein*, "do not put it in your vessel"? That verse, in context, addresses the moment when the worker is actively picking produce and detaching it — the moment of *gmar melacha* in the sense of removal from the ground. From there we already know that the right to eat applies specifically when one is engaged in the act of harvesting, not at preparatory stages. Why invoke *dayish* once more?

### Small Onions Case

The Gemara's response is subtle and striking. *Af al gav d'ka mishlaf kitini mibeini alimi* — even in a case where one is removing small, immature onions from among the larger, developing ones. This is a form of pruning: the small onions will never mature and their presence inhibits the growth of the larger ones. By removing them, the worker is directly handling produce — onions — and physically extracting them from the ground. One might think this resembles the act of harvesting and that the worker could therefore eat from the small onions he is removing.

The *baraita* teaches otherwise. The worker's *gmar melacha* consists in completing the intended work on the crop — harvesting the mature product. When he removes the small onions, his action is aimed at the benefit of the large onions, not at bringing the small onions to their own completion. Since this act is not a *gmar melacha* with respect to the small onions, the worker may not eat them, even though he is in direct physical contact with them and is indeed pulling them from the earth. A simple case of weeding might be excluded on the grounds that the worker is not even directly working on the edible produce itself; but this case — where he is directly handling the small onions — required the additional derivation from *dayish* to clarify that it is nonetheless forbidden.

---

## Ma'aser vs. Challa

### Pre-Ma'aser Stage

A third *baraita* sharpens the picture further. *Mah dayish meyuchad davar shelo nigmar melachto l'ma'aser poel ochel bo* — what is distinctive about *dayish* is that the produce has not yet reached *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser*, and during this time the worker may eat from it. After threshing, the grain must still be winnowed, sorted, and piled before it becomes obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*. Until that final stage, the worker retains the right to eat.

The affirmative principle: *af kol shelo nigmar melachto l'ma'aser poel ochel bo* — any produce that has not yet reached *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser* may be eaten by the worker. Once it has passed that threshold, the right lapses.

### Pressed Dates/Figs

The *baraita* illustrates the exclusion: *yatza habodeh bitmarim uvegrogros* — this excludes one who is separating pressed dates or pressed figs (*grogros*). Dates and figs that have been compressed together for storage or sale, and are then being separated out, have already undergone their *gmar melacha*. They were picked from the tree and processed; there is nothing further to be done to render them fit. Therefore *ein poel ochel bo* — the worker engaged in separating them may not eat from them.

### Unripe Dates

A contradiction immediately surfaces: a different *baraita* explicitly states that one who separates pressed dates and figs *may* eat from them. *Amar Rav Papa*: the permissive *baraita* deals with a special case — *bituchlani* — dates or figs that were harvested before ripening and stored in baskets designed to allow them to ripen gradually, a process closely analogous to modern controlled-ripening in shipping. In that case, the process is not yet complete at the time of pressing and storage; the final act of separating them, once they have ripened, constitutes the actual completion of the work — *gmar melacha* has not yet occurred — and therefore the worker may eat. The restrictive *baraita*, by contrast, speaks of produce that was already fully ripe when pressed: there, *gmar melacha* preceded the separation, and the worker has no right.

---

## Challa Extension

### Pre-Challa Stage

A fourth *baraita* pushes the boundary still further. *Mah dayish meyuchad davar shelo nigmar melachto lechalla upoel ochel bo* — what distinguishes *dayish* is that the *melacha* for *challa* has not yet been completed, and the worker may therefore eat. The obligation of *challa* arises at a later stage than the obligation of *ma'aser*: *ma'aser* applies once the grain has been piled and smoothed, whereas *challa* applies once flour has been ground, water added, and dough kneaded. The implication of this *baraita* is that even a worker grinding grain into flour — a stage past the *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser* — retains the right to eat, since *gmar melacha* for *challa* has not yet occurred.

### Kneading Onward

The *baraita* defines the exclusion: *yatza halosh vehamikatef vehaofeh shenigmar melachto lechalla* — this excludes one who kneads, one who shapes the dough, and one who bakes, since at those stages *gmar melacha* for *challa* has been reached. From kneading onward, the worker loses the right to eat freely from the dough.

### Contradiction

This fourth *baraita* creates a direct tension with the third. The third *baraita* stated that the worker may eat only until *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser*. The fourth *baraita* implies that even after *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser* — when the grain is already in a stage that triggers *ma'aser* obligation — the worker continues to eat until *gmar melacha* for *challa*. The Gemara flags this: *vehalo nigmara melachto lemaaser* — is it not already at the stage that obligates it in *ma'aser*?

---

## Resolution

### Abaye: Chutz L'aretz

*Abaye* proposes a solution: *lo kashya bechutza laaretz askinan delika maaser* — there is no difficulty, because the fourth *baraita* is speaking of a case in *chutz la'aretz*, outside the Land of Israel, where there is no obligation of *ma'aser*. Without a *ma'aser* obligation to mark *gmar melacha*, the relevant threshold shifts to the obligation of *challa*, which does apply in *chutz la'aretz* — and the worker therefore eats until that later stage.

### Objection

The Gemara rejects this immediately: *ei hachi challa nami lika* — if there is no *ma'aser* in *chutz la'aretz*, the same should apply to *challa*. The obligation of *challa* in *chutz la'aretz* is at most rabbinic in force, and the worker's Torah-level entitlement to eat should not be governed by a rabbinic threshold. The two *baraitot* therefore cannot be distinguished by geography alone.

### Seven Years

The Gemara proposes an alternative: *ela leolam baaretz velo kashya besheva shekavshu uvesheva shechalku* — in fact, both *baraitot* are speaking of the Land of Israel, and there is no contradiction, because there existed a historical period during which *challa* applied but *ma'aser* did not. During the fourteen years of conquest and division of the land — the seven years of conquest (*sheva shekavshu*) and the seven years of division (*sheva shechalku*) — the land had not yet attained the full *kedushah* of *Eretz Yisrael* required for *ma'aser*. Yet the tradition, as cited by the Gemara, is explicit: *sheva shekavshu vesheva shechalku nitchayvu bechalla velo nitchayvu bemaaser* — during those fourteen years, the people were obligated in *challa* but not in *ma'aser*. This is because the verse introducing *challa* (*bevoachem el haaretz*) triggers from the moment of entry into the land, even before full possession, whereas *ma'aser* required the land to be held as a proper inheritance.

That said, the Gemara itself registers some unease about a *baraita* concerning worker rights being framed around a historical situation from the period of initial conquest. But theoretically, in that narrow window, a worker grinding grain would be past no *ma'aser* threshold (since none applied) yet not past the *challa* threshold — and the fourth *baraita* would apply.

The Gemara then offers a decisive reframing: *midi maaser ka garim gemar melacha ka garim* — is it *ma'aser* itself that matters, or is it *gmar melacha*? The point is that *ma'aser* does not create the worker's obligation or the limit on his right; rather, the stages of *ma'aser* liability serve as *indicators* of completion. The Torah chose the stages of *ma'aser* to demarcate what counts as the end of the work process. It follows that even in a situation where *ma'aser* does not formally apply — whether in *chutz la'aretz* or in the fourteen years — the underlying concept of *gmar melacha* still governs, because that is what the *ma'aser* stages were measuring all along. The worker's right lapses when the produce has reached a stage of completion, not merely when a formal obligation of *ma'aser* has been triggered.

### Ravina Synthesis

*Ravina* resolves the contradiction between the two *baraitot* directly: *kroch v'sani* — combine them into one. *Mah dayish meyuchad davar shelo nigmar melachto l'ma'aser ul'challah upoel ochel bo* — *dayish* is distinctive in that the *melacha* is not yet complete for either *ma'aser* or *challa*, and the worker may eat. *Af kol shelo nigmar melachto l'ma'aser ul'challah poel ochel bo* — anything not yet at *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser* and for *challa* a worker may eat from. Rather than two competing limits, the *baraitot* together describe a single, composite condition.

### Interpretations

The question then becomes: if we require that *gmar melacha* have not yet occurred for *both* *ma'aser* and *challa*, and *challa* is the later stage, does the worker effectively always eat until *challa*? The *Raavad* reads it differently: once you combine the two *baraitot*, *ma'aser* takes precedence as the operative limit. When two thresholds are stated — A and B, with B later than A — the practical limit is A. The worker may eat until *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser*; the mention of *challa* adds nothing beyond that.

Most *Rishonim* — *Rashi* and *Tosafot* among them — understand it differently. They hold that the relevant threshold depends on the intended use of the produce. If the grain will not be made into dough — for example, produce destined to be eaten as parched grain, or fruit being processed in some other way — then the limit is *gmar melacha* for *ma'aser*. If, however, the grain is in the process of becoming dough and ultimately bread — the normative use of grain — then the worker retains his right to eat until *gmar melacha* for *challa*. The reasoning is that when the entire purpose of the process is the production of bread, one cannot view grain in a pile as "done"; it is conceptually incomplete until it has become dough. The *Ramban* offers additional explanations, but the *Rashi*–*Tosafot* position represents the dominant reading.

## Charring Question

### Charring Issue

The Gemara raises a new *she'eilah*: *poel mahu sheyahev ba'or v'yochal* — may a worker go ahead and grill or char the grain in fire before eating it? The issue connects to an earlier ruling that the worker may not eat the produce with *davar acher* — an additional substance — such as salt or bread, which would cause him to eat more than he would otherwise. Grilling is not an additive; there is no foreign substance involved. Yet it renders the grain considerably more palatable and could lead the worker to eat far more than he would in its raw state. Is that permissible or not?

### Wine & Brine

The Gemara seeks an answer from a *baraita*: *rashai baal habayis l'hashkos poalim yayin kedei shelo yochlu anavim harbei* — the owner may provide his workers with wine before they start, so that they will be full and not eat so many grapes while harvesting. *V'rashai poalin litbol pitan batzir kedei sheyochlu anavim harbe* — conversely, workers may dip their bread in brine during their lunch break in order to make themselves thirsty, so that they will be inclined to eat more grapes when they resume. The Gemara infers from this that we are not concerned about actions that make the person more or less inclined to eat, as long as nothing is added to the produce itself. By extension, grilling — which makes no addition — should be permissible.

### Distinction

The Gemara rejects this inference. *Lachshurei gavra lo kamibei lan* — making the person more or less fit to eat is not the question we are asking. The owner can give wine, the workers can eat brine, because all of that affects the person, not the produce. *Ki kamibei lan lachshurei peira mai* — what we are asking is whether one may do something to alter the produce itself, to transform it into a more edible form. The Torah entitles the worker to eat the produce as it comes to him in the field; the question is whether he may perform a further act on the produce — specifically, subjecting it to fire — before eating it.

---

## Head of Rows

### No Charring

The Gemara brings a *baraita* to address this question: *poalin ochlin anavim berashei umaniyot shelahem* — workers may eat grapes at the head of their rows. Rashi explains: in the course of harvesting, there is no opportunity to stop and eat mid-row; only when a worker reaches the end of a row and must pause to turn around does he have a natural moment to eat a few grapes. *U'bilvad shelo yehavvehu ba'or* — but they may not grill them in fire. This appears to resolve the *she'eilah*: charring is prohibited.

### Bitur Melacha

The Gemara, however, deflects this proof. *Hatam mishum bitul melacha* — there, the prohibition is not about the permissibility of altering the produce, but about *bitur melacha*, wasting the owner's time. The workers eat at the head of rows precisely so as not to interrupt the work mid-row and waste the owner's time. The prohibition on grilling is similarly motivated: taking time to grill the grapes would constitute *bitur melacha*, a misuse of the time the owner is paying for, not a violation of any rule about what may be done to the produce itself.

### Wife & Children

This leads the Gemara to refine the question: *ki kamibei lan heicha de'ika ishto uvanav mai* — what we are really asking is the following scenario: what if the worker's wife and children are present, and while he continues harvesting, they prepare the grain or grapes by grilling it, so that when he pauses he can eat it without any loss of time? In that case, *bitur melacha* is not an issue — the worker himself has not stopped working; others have done the preparation. Does the worker then have a right to eat the grilled produce? The Gemara leaves this as an open question.

---

## Prohibited Preps

### Methods Forbidden

A further *baraita* is introduced: *lo yehavvev ba'or v'yochal v'lo yachmor b'adama v'yochal v'lo yafriach al gabei hasela v'yochal* — the worker may not grill the produce in fire and eat it, may not bury it in warm earth to heat it and eat it, and may not break it on a rock and eat it. Three prohibited methods of preparation are enumerated: fire, warm earth, and crushing on a rock.

### Hand Crushing OK

However, *aval mafriach hu al yad al yad v'ochel* — the worker may crush the produce with his hand and eat it. Crushing by hand is permitted because it is essentially part of the act of eating itself, not a distinct preparatory process.

### Question

The Gemara interrogates the rationale of this *baraita*. Are these prohibitions about *davar acher* — about altering the produce to make it more palatable, causing the worker to eat more — or are they about *bitur melacha*, wasting the owner's time?

### Rock Argument

The Gemara pushes on the rock-breaking case specifically: *sela mai mitukei peira ika* — how does breaking produce on a rock sweeten it or make it more edible? If the concern is about improving the produce's palatability, rock-breaking is a difficult fit. *Tosafot* suggests it might refer to a hard-shelled fruit like a walnut, where crushing on a rock releases the interior. The Gemara's response is that this objection does not conclusively prove the rationale is *bitur melacha*; *ei efshar d'lo mimtiki purti* — even rock-breaking might slightly improve the produce's texture or release some of its juices. The question of whether the underlying rationale is *davar acher* or *bitur melacha* remains unresolved from this *baraita* alone.

---

## Comprehensive Rights

### Harvesting Rights

The Gemara now introduces a comprehensive *baraita* summarizing the worker's rights: workers who are harvesting figs, dates, grapes, and olives — the four major agricultural products — may eat from what they are working with and are exempt from *ma'aser* on what they consume. The exemption from *ma'aser* reflects a principle established elsewhere in the Gemara: since the Torah granted the worker the right to eat, his consumption is not considered a form of *mekach*, a purchase, which would otherwise render the produce obligated in *ma'aser*.

### Bread Prohibition

Despite these broad rights, the *baraita* states: *b'pittan lo yochlu ella im ken natlu reshut mi'baal habayit* — workers may not eat the produce together with their bread unless the owner has explicitly given them permission to do so. This is the restriction of *davar acher*: eating with bread enables the worker to consume far more of the produce than he would on its own. Without the owner's permission, this falls outside the Torah's grant.

### Salt Prohibition

Similarly: *v'lo yispot b'melach v'yochal* — they may not dip the produce in salt and eat it. Again, this is the parallel concern: salt, like bread, functions as a *davar acher* that enhances consumption beyond what the Torah permits. Salt is categorized differently from bread — it is absorbed into the produce rather than remaining a distinct substance alongside it — but the Gemara confirms that *melach vadai k'ein davar acher dami*: salt is certainly treated as a form of *davar acher*. If the owner gives permission, both bread and salt are permitted; otherwise, neither is.

## Paid vs. Torah Rights

### Non-Harvest

A subsequent *baraita* draws a comparison between workers operating under the Torah-granted entitlement and those operating under a contractual arrangement. A worker hired for non-harvest labor — for example, *l'ader*, tilling the ground, or *lekaskes*, making a circle in the earth around an olive tree — does not have the right to eat. These tasks are performed in preparation for or in service of the harvest, but they do not constitute the act of harvesting itself, and therefore *gmar melacha* in the relevant sense has not occurred. The worker's Torah right to eat does not apply.

### Harvest Work

By contrast, a worker hired for actual harvesting — *livtzor* (grapes), *limsok* (olives), *lilket* (other produce) — may eat and is exempt from *terumah* and *ma'asrot*. These are the conditions under which the Torah grants the right: the worker is engaged in the act of bringing the produce in from the field, at the very moment of *gmar melacha* in the sense of detachment from the ground. *HaTorah zichsa lahen* — the Torah gave them this right — and because it derives from the Torah's grant rather than from a contractual exchange, it does not constitute *mekach* and does not trigger the obligation of *terumah* and *ma'asrot*.

### Paid Deal

The *baraita* then addresses a worker who has made a deal (*katzatz*) with the owner — a contractual arrangement granting the worker the right to eat. In such a case, *achat achat yochal, shtayim shtayim lo yochal* — he may eat one by one, but not two by two. The reason is that when the worker purchased the right to eat from the owner, this transforms his consumption into a form of *mekach*. Since it is now a purchase, the produce becomes subject to *ma'aser* once the worker has acquired it. And the threshold for that acquisition — which the Gemara will elaborate on — is tied to quantity: one fruit at a time does not constitute a *goren*, a granary-stage gathering, but two fruits together does.

### Salt with Deal

The *baraita* adds: *v'sofeit b'melach v'yochal* — in the context of the paid deal, the worker may dip the produce in salt and eat. But again, even so, *achat achat*, one by one — the salt and the deal together do not permit two at a time, since two items dipped in salt, combined with the purchase arrangement, creates *gmar melacha* and obligates the worker in *terumot u'ma'asrot*.

---

## Case Analysis

### Paid Deal Unrestricted

The Gemara now interrogates which case the latter part of the *baraita* is addressing. If the *baraita*'s final clause — permitting salt — is referring to the case of the *katzatz*, the paid deal, then there is no limitation at all: *kivan de'katzatz, kol heicha de'ba'i leichol* — since the worker paid the owner for the right to eat, he may eat however he wishes. The owner's grant is unrestricted, and any mode of consumption is in principle permitted.

### Contradiction

Since that would make the *baraita*'s rulings trivially true and uninteresting, the Gemara concludes: *ela lav a'reisha* — the latter part of the *baraita* must be addressing the first case, that of the worker eating under the Torah's grant rather than a contractual one. And yet, in that case, the *baraita* seems to permit dipping in salt. This is the contradiction: the earlier *baraita* prohibited salt as *davar acher*, and now a later clause appears to allow it under the same Torah-granted right.

### Abaye's Tfisah

*Abaye* proposes a resolution: *kan ba'aretz, kan be'chutz la'aretz* — the cases differ by geography. *Ba'aretz kava sfisa* — in *Eretz Yisrael*, dipping in salt constitutes *tfisat*, a form of acquisition or *gmar melacha*, and therefore obligates in *terumah* and *ma'asrot*. This is why the earlier *baraita* forbids it: not because of *davar acher* per se, but because in *Eretz Yisrael*, dipping renders the produce *tevel*, obligated in *ma'asrot*, and the worker who has not yet separated *terumot u'ma'asrot* would be eating something forbidden. *Be'chutz la'aretz lo kava sfisa* — in *chutz la'aretz*, the dipping does not create this *gmar melacha* effect, and therefore the latter *baraita* — dealing with *chutz la'aretz* — permits salt.

### Rava's Objection

*Rava* rejects *Abaye*'s distinction. *Mi gamidi de'ba'aretz kava sfisa mide'oraysa uv'chutz la'aretz lo kava sfisa umutar lechatchila* — is it really so that in *Eretz Yisrael* dipping creates a Torah-level *gmar melacha* while in *chutz la'aretz* it creates no obligation at all, making it entirely permitted? The *baraita* that permits salt is plainly discussing a context where *terumah* and *ma'asrot* are at stake — that is the entire framework of the passage. Any *chutz la'aretz* that is relevant here must be one where there is at least a rabbinic obligation in *terumot u'ma'asrot*. And if dipping in salt creates *gmar melacha* in *Eretz Yisrael*, the same act must equally create *gmar melacha* in *chutz la'aretz* where *ma'asrot* apply rabbinically. The geographical distinction therefore does not hold.

## Rava's Factors

### Quantity Factor

*Rava* offers his own resolution. Two independent factors can generate *tfisah* — the obligating acquisition or *gmar melacha* — that subjects the produce to *terumot u'ma'asrot*. The first is quantity: taking a single fruit does not constitute a *goren*, a gathering sufficient to create obligation, but taking two together does. This applies universally, regardless of geography.

### Purchase Factor

The second factor is the contractual purchase (*katzatz*): if the worker has made a deal with the owner and bought the right to eat, then that acquisition operates independently. It creates *tfisah* regardless of quantity and regardless of whether salt is involved. Once the worker purchased the right, even one fruit at a time triggers the concern of *ma'asrot*, which is why even then he eats one by one.

### Two Scenarios

Putting these together, *Rava* identifies two scenarios that create *tfisah*: a purchase deal with the owner (operative at any quantity), and dipping in salt combined with two fruits. Either one is sufficient on its own to obligate in *terumot u'ma'asrot*.

### Normal Worker

A worker who has neither made a purchase deal nor is dipping in salt — the normal Torah-entitled worker eating as he harvests — *ochel shtayim shtayim*: may eat two at a time, or more. Two items without any further act of acquisition do not constitute a *goren* and do not obligate in *ma'asrot*. The worker's right under the Torah is unconstrained by the quantity issue.

### Worker + Salt

However, if that same normal worker — no purchase deal — proceeds to dip the produce in salt: *achas achas ochel, shtayim shtayim lo yochal* — he may eat one by one but not two by two. Salt functions as a form of *tfisah*, akin to acquiring the produce for consumption; when combined with two items, this creates the *gmar melacha* threshold that triggers obligation in *terumot u'ma'asrot*. With only one item, the salt alone does not suffice to create that threshold.

### Even with Permission

Even if the owner grants the worker permission to dip in salt — which removes the *davar acher* objection — the owner's permission cannot override the *ma'aser* obligation that arises from the combination of salt and two fruits. *De'itvu lehu lema'aser vekava sfisa* — the dipping in salt plus two items renders the produce *tevel* with respect to *ma'aser*, and that obligation cannot be waived by the owner's consent. The permission speaks only to the worker's entitlement vis-à-vis the owner; it cannot alter the produce's status with respect to the obligations of *terumot u'ma'asrot*. This synthesis resolves the apparent contradiction between the *baraitot*: the *baraita* that permits salt is addressing the case of one fruit at a time (where *tfisah* has not occurred), while the *baraita* that prohibits salt is addressing two at a time (where salt plus two creates *gmar melacha*).

## Goren Concept

### Sheaf Verse

Having established that two items represent the threshold for *tfisah* while one does not, the Gemara asks for the textual basis. *Rav Matna* provides the source: *ki kavtzam ka-amir gorna* — "for He gathered them like a sheaf into the granary." The concept of *goren*, the granary, represents the stage at which produce becomes obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*. This verse depicts the *goren* as the result of a gathering — *kavtzam* — an act of collecting multiple items together.

### Goren Stage

The *goren* is not merely a physical location but a conceptual stage: it is the moment at which produce has been sufficiently gathered and prepared to be considered complete and ready for formal disposition, triggering the obligations of *terumah* and *ma'aser*. The verse's imagery — sheaves brought together into the granary — captures the essence of what creates this stage: the act of amassing.

### Two-Item Minimum

Gathering, by its very nature, requires more than one item. A single sheaf cannot be "gathered" in the sense the verse describes; gathering implies plurality. Therefore, *Rav Matna* derives that the minimum threshold for creating a *goren*-level obligation — the minimum number that constitutes a qualifying gathering — is two. One item is insufficient to trigger *tfisah*; two items represent the baseline of what counts as a collection. This scriptural grounding from *ki kavtzam ka-amir gorna* provides the foundation for *Rava*'s entire analysis: the two-item minimum is not a rabbinic fence but a principle rooted in the verse's own conception of what it means to bring produce to the stage of a *goren*.
