# Hullin 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chaya & Oph Coverage

### Multiple Animals

The Mishnah on 87b addresses a scenario in which one slaughters multiple animals — multiple *chayot* or multiple birds — at the same time. The ruling is that one covers the blood only once, at the end. There is no obligation to cover between each animal. A dispute arises, however, when one slaughters both a *chaya* and *oph* together, where Rabbi Yehuda holds that the bloods must be covered separately.

### Linguistic Analysis

The *Gemara* opens with a *baraita*: the word *chaya* in the verse is understood as a class term — *kol mashma chaya*, meaning whether one or many. Similarly, *oph* — fowl — *kol mashma oph*, whether many or few. From this linguistic analysis the *baraita* derives its ruling: if one slaughtered a hundred *chayot* in one place, *kisui echad l'kulam* — one covering suffices for all. The same applies to a hundred birds, and even if one slaughtered *chaya* and *oph* together in the same place, a single covering covers them all. Rabbi Yehuda, however, dissents: if one slaughters a *chaya*, one must cover it, and only thereafter slaughter the birds — for the verse states *chaya o oph*, using the word *o* (or) to indicate that they are to be treated separately.

### Scriptural Source

The Sages respond to Rabbi Yehuda by citing the verse *ki nefesh kol basar damo b'nafsho hi* — the soul of all flesh, its blood is in its soul — which appears immediately after the verse commanding the covering of blood, addressed to *bnei yisrael* collectively. The intent is to establish that the obligation of covering blood is addressed to the entire community of Israel — *azhara lechol yisrael* — not merely to a single individual acting in isolation. The communal dimension of the *mitzvah* flows directly from this formulation.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Or

The question immediately raised is: what kind of a response is this? How does citing *ki nefesh kol basar* address Rabbi Yehuda's position? The *Gemara* clarifies that the Sages are challenging his use of the *o*. Rabbi Yehuda reads *chaya o oph* as mandating separate coverings. The Sages counter: that *o* is needed to teach *l'chaleik* — to inform us that the obligation applies even when only a *chaya* is slaughtered and even when only *oph* is slaughtered, not solely when both are present together.

Rabbi Yehuda's rejoinder is that we already know this from the singular *damo* — "its blood" — which teaches that even a single animal requires covering. Since the singular form already handles that lesson, the *o* is freed to teach that *chaya* and *oph* require separate coverings.

The Sages respond that *damo* does not necessarily mean only one animal's blood. The word can function collectively, as demonstrated by the very same verse — *ki nefesh kol basar damo b'nafsho hi* — which uses the singular possessive while referring to all flesh universally. Since *damo* alone could not have taught us that either *chaya* or *oph* individually obligates, the *o* is required for precisely that lesson. Rabbi Yehuda, left without an alternative source for the *o*, uses it to mandate separate coverings.

## One Bracha Question

### Rabbi Chanina

The *Gemara* continues with a teaching of Rabbi Chanina: *modeh hayah Rabbi Yehuda l'inyan beracha she'eino mevareich ela beracha achas* — Rabbi Yehuda concedes that for the purpose of the *beracha*, one recites only a single blessing, even according to his own position. This is striking. One might have assumed that if the nature of the *mitzvah* according to Rabbi Yehuda demands two separate acts of covering, each would require its own *beracha*. Rabbi Chanina teaches otherwise.

This invites a broader question: what precisely defines the relationship between *mitzvos* and the *berachos* recited over them? One might frame it in two ways. Perhaps even when two *mitzvos* are performed sequentially, one immediately after the other, a single *beracha* suffices. Or, more simply, Rabbi Yehuda understands the entire obligation — covering *chaya* blood separately from *oph* blood — as constituting a single *mitzvah*, the nature of which merely requires that the coverings be performed in a particular order. On that reading, there is only one *mitzvah* and therefore only one *beracha*.

### Ravina's Question

The *Gemara* then records that Ravina said to Rav Acha son of Rava — and some say Rav Acha son of Rava said to Rav Ashi — *mai shna me-hamidei de-Rav*, why is this any different from the following story involving the students of Rav? The question is not a conceptual one about whether we view the two coverings as a single *mitzvah*, but a practical one. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the person actually performs an intervening act — covering the blood of the *chaya* before proceeding to slaughter the *oph*. Should that act of covering not constitute a *hefsek*, an interruption, between the two? And if so, even if we call the entire process one *mitzvah*, the first *beracha* cannot carry over past a *hefsek* to cover the second act.

### Hefsek Concern

The comparison is to a case involving students of Rav. Rav Bruna and Rav Chananel were seated at a meal, with Rav Yeba Saba serving as the *shamash*. They said to him *havu nvarech* — come, let us bentch — perhaps intending to include him in the *zimun*. They then changed their minds and said *havu nishrei*, wait, bring us more wine. Rav Yeba Saba told them: this is what Rav ruled — *keivan de-amru havu nvarech*, once you said "come let us bentch," *itzlei le-mishtei chamra* — you may no longer drink wine. The statement constituted a *hefsek*. You cannot simply retract it and resume drinking without consequence.

### Bentching Story

The implication for our case is as follows: here too, *keivan de-itzlei le-kisui ichayev lei be-bracha* — once you were obligated to attend to covering the blood, you have effectively signaled the end of one stage, and the next covering should require a new *beracha*. The *Gemara* presses the comparison: just as declaring "let us bentch" constituted a *hefsek* that precluded drinking wine without a new blessing, so too the first covering should interrupt the sequence and require a new *beracha* for the second.

## Hefsek Resolution

### Simultaneity Argument

The *Gemara* rejects the comparison: *hachi hashta* — what kind of comparison is this? *Hatam, mishtei u-vruchei be-hadei hadadi lo efshar* — there, one cannot drink wine while simultaneously making the *beracha*. One cannot talk while eating. Saying *rabosai nevarech* necessarily sets aside the drinking, because one physically cannot do both at once. *Hacha, efshar de-shachi be-chada u-mechasi be-chada* — here, if one is ambidextrous, one can be slaughtering with one hand while covering with the other. The covering is therefore not a contradiction to the ongoing process of *shechita*, and is not a *hefsek*.

### Ambidextrous Act

Taken literally, the *Gemara*'s reasoning is purely physical: if it is not physically possible to perform two activities simultaneously, then one activity signals the conclusion of the other. Since declaring intent to bentch makes drinking physically impossible at that moment, it constitutes a *hefsek*. Covering blood, however, is theoretically compatible with simultaneous *shechita*, and therefore cannot constitute a decisive break.

### Tosafos Signals End

Tosafos is uncomfortable with this purely physical reading of the *Gemara*, and offers a more conceptual interpretation. He raises a challenge: suppose in the middle of eating one rose to *daven* — to pray. One cannot eat and *daven* simultaneously. Does returning to eating after *davening* require a new *beracha*? According to the physical logic of the *Gemara*, it should. Similarly, if one heard thunder during a meal and paused to make a *beracha* on the thunder, would resuming the meal demand a new *beracha* merely because one cannot eat and speak simultaneously? Tosafos finds this problematic.

Tosafos therefore reframes the *Gemara*'s reasoning. The critical point is not physical simultaneity but what an activity *signals*. Bentching is the formal conclusion of a meal — it is what one does when eating is finished. When one says *rabosai nevarech*, one has declared, in effect, that eating is over. That declaration is what constitutes the *hefsek*, independent of any physical impossibility.

On this reading, one might have thought the same logic applies to covering blood. After all, covering blood comes at the end of *shechita*, just as bentching comes at the end of eating. Tosafos says this is precisely what troubled the *Gemara*, and it is why the *Gemara* made the comparison in the first place. The *Gemara*'s answer is that since covering blood is at least theoretically compatible with ongoing *shechita* — one could cover and slaughter simultaneously — the act of covering does not carry the same decisive finality that bentching carries relative to eating. Bentching admits of no ongoing eating whatsoever, whereas covering does not fully exclude the continuation of *shechita*. Therefore, covering blood is not a *hefsek* of the same order.

### What Is Hefsek

The upshot of Tosafos's reading is that a true *hefsek* — one that demands a new *beracha* — must consist of an activity that genuinely signals the conclusion of the prior process, in the way bentching signals the end of eating. A physical pause alone, or an activity that does not carry such conclusive significance, does not rise to that level. Because covering blood does not fully exclude the continuation of the *shechita* process, it does not constitute an interruption, and a single *beracha* suffices throughout.

This opens onto a broader discussion. Regarding *tefillin*, for instance, if one interrupts between the *shel yad* and the *shel rosh*, a new *beracha* is required. Even Ashkenazim who recite a separate *beracha* on the *shel rosh* — *al mitzvat tefillin* — must nonetheless also recite *lehaniach* on the *shel rosh* if there was an interruption, because the *beracha* of *lehaniach* on the *shel yad* can only extend to the *shel rosh* without interruption. Yet in other contexts — *bedikas chametz*, for example — once one has begun, even a pause or an exchange of words in the service of the *mitzvah* does not demand a new *beracha*. The same *beracha* continues to govern subsequent acts throughout the process.

Tosafos is inclined to treat *tefillin* as the exception: there it is one *mitzvah* where even a small interruption severs the connection between the *beracha* and the second act. In other extended processes or in *mitzvos* that comprise multiple sequential acts, the standard for *hefsek* is considerably higher. The same questions apply to a *shochet* who *shechts* throughout the day: does going to the bathroom, stopping for a meal, or answering a call constitute a *hefsek* sufficient to require a new *beracha*? Tosafos raises these questions but does not resolve them with finality.

One further distinction is worth noting. The *beracha* discussed in the parallel case of the students of Rav was a *beracha* on the drinking, and the bentching was what interrupted and terminated it. Here, by contrast, the *beracha* is not on the *shechita* — it is on the *kisuy* itself. What the *Gemara* would therefore have to establish is not that covering interrupts the *shechita* that preceded it, but rather that the *shechita* between the two coverings does not constitute a *hefsek* between the first covering and the second. The conceptual structure is not perfectly parallel, but the *Gemara*'s distinction still holds.

## Arvus Principle

### One Slaughters

The next Mishnah establishes: *shachat velo kisa* — if one slaughtered and did not cover the blood, *vera'ahu acher chayav lechasot* — and another person saw the exposed blood, that other person is obligated to cover it. The obligation does not reside exclusively with the *shochet*. This raises a fundamental question about the structure of the *mitzvah*: certain obligations are personal, binding the individual who performed the triggering act; others inhere in a state of affairs that demands rectification regardless of who created it. Here, exposed blood that has not been covered creates a condition that obligates the broader community to address it.

### Spiller Must Cover

A *baraita* teaches: *veshafach vechisa* — you shall spill and cover. From the juxtaposition of these two acts, the *Sages* derive *mi sheshafach yechaseh* — the one who spills is the one who should cover. That is the primary obligation. But if he slaughtered and did not cover, and another person saw it, the *baraita* asks: from where do we know that the other person is obligated to cover? The answer derives from the verse *va'omar livnei yisrael* — and I said to the children of Israel — which places the declaration in the context of a collective address to *Klal Yisrael*. From this the *Gemara* establishes *azhara lechol yisrael*: the obligation of *kisuy hadam* carries a communal dimension. The primary obligation rests with the *shochet*; if he fails to discharge it, the obligation devolves upon others.

### Respectful Covering

A second *baraita* derives an additional lesson from *veshafach vechisa*: cover with the same implement used to spill — *shelo yechasenu beregel*, do not cover the blood with one's foot. Kicking dirt over the blood may be physically effective, but it is disrespectful. *Shelo yiheyu mitzvos bezuyos alav* — one should not treat *mitzvos* with contempt. Even when performing a *mitzvah* in a technically sufficient manner, it must be done with appropriate dignity. A shovel would be acceptable; one's foot would not.

### Jumped Ahead Story

A third *baraita* returns to the primary obligation: *mi sheshafach hu yechasenu* — the one who spills is the one who should cover it. The *baraita* then records a case: *ma'aseh be'echad sheshachat vekadam chaveiro vechisa* — someone slaughtered and another person jumped ahead and covered the blood before the *shochet* had a chance to do so. Rabban Gamliel obligated this person to pay ten gold coins to the one from whom the *mitzvah* was seized.

### Fine Question

The *Gemara* raises the question: *sechar mitzvah o sechar beracha* — is the payment for stealing the *mitzvah*, or for stealing the *beracha*? The practical difference is illustrated by analogy to *birchas hamazon*: if someone stole the opportunity to lead the bentching for a *zimun*, that involves four *berachos*. If the fine is per *mitzvah*, the payment is ten gold coins for one *mitzvah*. If the fine is per *beracha*, the calculation shifts — and the *Gemara* implies a figure of forty gold coins.

The *poskim* note that the fine cannot be understood as applying to any *stam* stolen *beracha*, particularly in light of the principle that *gadol ha'oneh yoser min hamevarech* — greater is the one who answers *amen* than the one who makes the *beracha*. Two explanations are offered: either the *beracha* was recited quietly so no one could answer *amen*, making the loss more acute; or, more persuasively, the number of *berachos* associated with a *mitzvah* serves as an index for measuring the weight of the *mitzvah* itself. The fine is not literally about the *beracha* as a discrete act but about the severity of the theft, which is calibrated by how many *berachos* attended the stolen *mitzvah*. This reading avoids the difficulty of imposing a fine merely for stealing a *beracha*, which would seem disproportionate, and frames the *beracha* count as a measure of the *mitzvah*'s weight.

## Min Debate

### Mountains & Wind

The *Gemara* introduces a remarkable aggadic passage. A certain *min* said to Rebbi: *mi she'yatzar harim lo bara ruach* — the one who fashioned the mountains did not create the wind, and the one who created the wind did not fashion the mountains. His proof: the verse reads *ki hinei yotzer harim u'vorei ruach* — behold the fashioner of mountains and the creator of wind — using two distinct verbs with two distinct grammatical constructions, implying two distinct creative agents.

### One Creator

Rebbi's response was immediate: *shoteh* — idiot. *Shfil l'seifa de'krah* — go look at the end of the verse: *Hashem tzevaot shemo* — the Lord of hosts is His name. The verse itself resolves the apparent duality by attributing both acts to a single name. One senses that the theological stakes in this exchange were considerably higher than a grammatical dispute. The use of *borei* — a term associated with creation *yesh mei'ayin*, from nothing — alongside *yotzer* — formation and fashioning — may itself be part of the provocation, suggesting distinctions in the nature or hierarchy of divine acts. The broader context of such debates in the Talmud, particularly involving *minim*, suggests that arguments for divine plurality or a compound godhead were very much in the background.

### Three Days Fast

The *min* responded: *nekos li zimna tlati yomei u'madveina lach tyuvta* — give me three days, and I will come back to you with a challenge. Rebbi, clearly concerned, sat in three consecutive fasts — *yasiv Rebbi tlata ta'anisa* — that this person should not be able to construct an effective rebuttal. The echoes of medieval *vikuach*, religious disputation with all its attendant dangers, are unmistakable here.

### Good News

When Rebbi finally reached the end of his fasts and was about to eat, people came to the door and announced that a *min* was there. Rebbi quoted the verse *vayitnu bivrosi rosh* — they have put poison in my food — expressing his dismay at being interrupted precisely at the moment he was ready to eat. But the *min* at the door was not his adversary. *Mevaser tovos ani lacha* — I bring you good news, he said. *Lo matza teshuvah oyvecha* — your enemy found no answer for you — *v'nafal min hagag v'met* — and he fell from the roof and died.

### Cup of Bracha

Rebbi then extended an invitation: *retzoncha shetis'ad etzli* — would you like to eat with me? The *min* accepted. After they ate and drank, Rebbi offered him a choice: *kos shel beracha ata shoteh* — would you drink the *kos shel beracha* — or *arbaim zuzim ata notel* — would you take forty gold coins? The *min* chose the *kos shel beracha*.

Tosafos notes that Rebbi was not eager to extend the *kos shel beracha* to this individual, but felt a certain obligation to acknowledge the man's good deed. The offer of forty gold coins was an attempt to buy him out of the honor while still showing respect. The man's preference for the *kos shel beracha* over a substantial sum of money demonstrated the depth of his reverence for Jewish practice.

There is also a textual nuance worth noting. The *Gemara* says *kos shel beracha ata shoteh* — will you drink the cup of blessing — rather than *kos shel beracha ata notel*, will you take it. Rashi understands this as an invitation to remain present for the bentching, to hear the *berachos*, and to answer *amen* — *schar arba brachos she'ani achreihem amen*, the reward of the four *berachos* to which he answered *amen*. On this reading, the *min* was not leading the bentching but participating as one who answers. That interpretation, however, creates its own difficulty: would the opportunity to answer *amen* truly be weighed against forty gold coins? And the broader question arises regarding whether it was appropriate to include a *min* in a *zimun* at all. These questions are noted in the discussion, with the observation that exclusion on grounds of theological deviance, while prominent in the *Rambam*, is far less prominent as an operative principle in the *Gemara* itself.

### Heavenly Voice

*Yatzta bat kol v'amra* — a heavenly voice went forth and declared: *kos shel beracha yishveh arbaim zuzim* — the cup of blessing is worth forty gold coins. Rabbi Yitzchak adds that the family of that *min* survives to this day among the prominent families of Rome, known as the house of Bar Lavyanus.

The *bat kol* here is understood not as a one-time supernatural pronouncement but as the articulation of what became the received wisdom — the moral that everyone drew from the story, a principle that entered common discourse. And indeed, the matter is brought *le-halakhah*: the question of what fine is owed to one from whom a *mitzvah* is stolen is discussed in the *Shulchan Aruch*. Some understand the figure of ten gold coins as a fixed fine; others hold it subject to the assessment of a *beit din*. Further questions arise regarding whether contemporary Jewish courts retain authority to impose fines of this punitive nature as distinct from purely compensatory damages.

Tosafos also addresses the scope of the principle: the *mitzvah* must belong to the person from whom it was seized. If a father intended to perform the *milah* on his son himself and another person performed it without authorization, the *mitzvah* was stolen from the father. But if the father assigned a *mohel* to do it and a second *mohel* pushed him aside and performed the *milah*, the father retains a grievance against the interloper, but the first *mohel* does not — for the *mitzvah* never belonged to him in the first place. It was always the father's *mitzvah*, merely delegated. Similarly, Tosafos holds that being called for an *aliyah* does not vest a proprietary claim in the *mitzvah* — if another person takes it, the one called has been denied an honor, but has not had a *mitzvah* stolen from him in the sense that triggers this fine.

## Re-exposure Cases

### Covered Then Exposed

The *Gemara* now turns to several cases involving blood that was covered and subsequently uncovered. The Mishnah rules: *kisa venisgala* — if one covered the blood and it became exposed again, *patur milachasot* — one is exempt from covering it a second time. The *mitzvah* was performed; that it was undone by subsequent events does not reimpose the obligation.

### Lost Objects

Rav Achai son of Rava poses a challenge to Rav Ashi: why should this be different from *hashavat aveida* — returning a lost object? The verse commands *hashev* — return — and from this the *Sages* derive that one must return even *afilu me'ah pe'amim*, even a hundred times over, should the object keep getting lost. Perhaps the same should apply here: if the covered blood becomes exposed again, one must cover it again.

The reply is that by *hashavat aveida* there is no limiting word in the verse. Here, however, the verse reads *vechisahu* — and cover it — which carries an implicit restriction: you cover it once, and once it has been covered the obligation is discharged. The word itself signals that a single performance of the *mitzvah* completes the obligation, and re-exposure does not reimpose it.

### Wind Coverage

By contrast, if the blood was covered by the wind — *kisa'u haruach* — the *mitzvah* was never performed at all, and therefore *chayav lechasot* — one is still obligated to cover. But a natural question arises: if the blood is already covered, what does covering it accomplish? Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo shanu ela shechazar venitkaleh* — the obligation applies only if the wind-covered blood was subsequently revealed again. If it was covered by the wind and remains covered — *lo chazar venitkaleh patur milachasot* — one is exempt, because there is nothing to cover. The obligation arises only when there is exposed blood that has never had the *mitzvah* performed upon it.

### Ein Dichuy

The *Gemara* then pushes further: *vechi chazar venitkaleh mai havei* — so what if it was revealed again? During the period that it was covered by the wind, was it not effectively *nidcheh* — pushed away — from the scope of the *mitzvah*? By analogy to *korbanot*, where an animal that was disqualified and then becomes eligible again remains invalid due to the principle of *dichuy*, perhaps the same should apply here: since there was a period during which the blood could not be the subject of the *mitzvah*, it should remain ineligible even when it is re-exposed.

Rav Pappa draws the decisive distinction: *zot omeret ein dichuy etzel mitzvot* — from this case we learn that the principle of *dichuy* applies to sacrifices but not to *mitzvos*. A *mitzvah* is not pushed away permanently by a period of ineligibility. When the condition that triggers the *mitzvah* returns — exposed blood that has never been covered — the obligation returns with it.

## Blood Mixtures

### Blood & Water

The Mishnah deals with blood mixed with various liquids. The first case: *dam shenitarav bemayim* — blood mixed with water. The rule: *im yesh bo mareh dam chayav lechasot* — if the mixture has the appearance of blood, one must cover it. Tosafos raises the question of what exactly *mareh dam* means in this context. Does the mixture merely need to have a reddish tint, or must it genuinely look like blood — deep in color and viscosity, not merely detectable? Tosafos holds that it must truly look like blood, reasoning that if the non-blood ingredient were in the majority, we would apply the laws of *bittul* and the blood would be nullified. Therefore, these cases assume that the other ingredient is in the minority, and even then the mixture must look not merely reddish but actually like blood.

### Blood & Wine

The next case: *nitarav beyayin* — blood mixed with wine. Since both are red, one cannot simply observe color. The rule is *ro'eh oto ke'ilu hu mayim* — one imagines the wine as if it were water and asks: would the blood still look like blood in that water? If yes, one must cover. If the dilution would be sufficient to render it not blood-looking, one need not cover.

### Blood Mixtures

Two further cases: blood mixed with *dam behemah* — the blood of a domesticated animal, which does not require covering — or with the blood of a living animal. The same test applies: treat the other blood as if it were water. If the *chaya* or *oph* blood would still appear as blood against that background, one must cover.

### Dam Lo Mevatel

Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *ein dam mevatel dam* — blood does not nullify blood. Since both substances are of the same type — *min bemino* — one does not nullify the other. Accordingly, the blood of the *chaya* or *oph* retains its status regardless of how much other blood surrounds it, and the obligation to cover remains.

### Splattered Blood

The final case in the Mishnah: *dam hanitaz veshel hasakin* — blood that has spattered away from the main collection point, or blood remaining on the knife, *chayav lechasot* — must be covered. Rabbi Yehuda qualifies: *eimatai*, when does this apply? *Bizman she'ein sham dam ela hu* — only when there is no other blood present to which the *mitzvah* can be applied. *Aval yesh sham dam shelo hu, patur milachasot* — if there is a main collection of blood to which the *mitzvah* is directed, one need not separately cover the spatters or the knife blood. This raises the question of whether one must cover every drop of blood or whether covering the main collection discharges the obligation — Rabbi Yehuda's position suggests the latter.

## Zevachim Parallel

### Zevachim Mishnah

The *Gemara* observes that nearly the same Mishnah appears in Zevachim, addressing sacrificial blood. *Dam shenitarav bemayim* — blood mixed with water — *im yesh bo mareh dam kasher* — if it has the appearance of blood it is *kasher* and may be sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach*. The parallel is almost word for word with the Mishnah in Chullin, the difference being that there the operative term is *kasher* rather than *chayav lechasot*. The wine case, the *behemah* blood case — all follow the same structure, and Rabbi Yehuda's position of *ein dam mevatel dam* appears there as well.

### Water Into Blood

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo shanu ella shenaflo mayim letoch dam* — all of the above applies only when water fell into the blood, gradually diluting it. In that scenario the blood is the dominant substance into which water is introduced, and the appearance of blood is assessed against that background.

### Blood Into Water

But *aval nafal dam letoch mayim* — if blood dripped into water, drop by drop — a different principle applies. *Rishon rishon battel* — each drop, as it enters, is nullified, because at that moment the blood does not appear as blood against the surrounding water. Even if ultimately enough blood accumulates so that the mixture looks like blood, the principle of *rishon rishon battel* means that each increment was neutralized at the point of entry. This is the concept of *chozer veni'or* — reawakening — a substance that was neutralized and then, as subsequent quantities accumulate, regains its effective status.

### Dichuy Distinction

Rav Pappa rules: *ul'inyan kisuy eino ken* — this rule does not apply when it comes to the *mitzvah* of covering blood. *Ein dichuy etzel mitzvot* — *mitzvos* are not subject to *dichuy*. Even if there was a period when the blood was neutralized — when it had, so to speak, lost its status — now that it has regained that status and the mixture appears as blood, the obligation to cover applies. The principle of *dichuy* that disqualifies a *korban* permanently after a period of ineligibility does not carry over to the domain of *mitzvos*.

The distinction is important on multiple levels. For *korbanot*, sanctity is a formal legal status; once compromised, it is not simply restored. For a *mitzvah* obligation like *kisuy hadam*, what matters is the present condition: is there blood that appears as blood and that has not had the *mitzvah* performed upon it? If the answer is yes, the obligation exists, regardless of any prior period of neutralization.

This principle has implications for *trefos* more broadly. Where prohibited substances accumulate drop by drop and each drop is *battel* upon entry, the question of whether the aggregate *chozer veni'or* — reawakens — is a significant practical question in the laws of *bittul*.

## Tumah Susceptibility

### Red Appearance

The *Gemara* cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *kol mareh admumit mechapperin* — any red appearance suffices for *kapparah*, for the use of sacrificial blood. And *umachshirin* — and such a mixture renders food susceptible to *tumah*, since blood is one of the seven liquids that confer susceptibility upon produce. And *v'chayavin bekisuy* — and one is obligated to cover such a mixture.

### Rainwater Question

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mai ka mashma lan* — what is the novel point here? The rule that such a mixture is *kasher* for *kapparah* was already taught in the Mishnah of Zevachim. The rule that one must cover it was already taught in the Mishnah of Chullin. And as for *hechsher zerayim* — the susceptibility of produce — since the mixture contains either blood or water, either component would render produce susceptible to *tumah* anyway. What has Shmuel added?

The *Gemara* answers: *lo tzricha shenis'arvu b'mei geshamim* — the ruling is necessary for a case where the blood became mixed with rainwater. Rainwater does not confer susceptibility upon produce unless the owner desired that the water fall on the produce, or unless the water was designated for some use. Rainwater that falls naturally and unintentionally does not render produce susceptible.

### Natural vs. Intentional

The *Gemara* initially suggests that even rainwater, once consciously mixed with blood — *keivan d'shakil v'rami*, since one took it and poured it — has been given significance by the intentional act, and would therefore be *machshir*. But Shmuel's ruling is necessary for the case of *lo tzricha shenis'arvu mei'aleihen* — where the mixing happened entirely on its own, without any human intention. A vessel of blood was left out, rainwater fell into it naturally, and the mixture was then knocked onto produce. Treating the liquid as rainwater, one might think it could not render the produce susceptible, since no human intention was involved. Shmuel teaches that since the mixture still has the appearance of blood, it is treated as blood for all purposes — for *kapparah*, for *kisuy*, and for conferring susceptibility upon produce — regardless of whether the mixing was intentional or accidental.
