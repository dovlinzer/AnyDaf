# Bava Kamma 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Shmuel: Modeh B'…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס, וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – רַב אָמַר: פָּטוּר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** with regard to one who **admits** that he is liable **to** pay **a fine, and afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability, that **Rav says** he is **exempt, and Shmuel says** he is **liable.**

### Admission vs. Obligation

The *sugya* opens with a formal statement of the dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning the rule of *modeh b'knas* — the case in which a person admits to an act that carries a *knas*, a monetary penalty, and witnesses subsequently arrive to corroborate that admission. The controlling principle under discussion is whether such an admission actively exempts the defendant from liability, or whether it merely fails to obligate him in the first instance.

Rav's position is stated directly: *modeh b'knas achar kach ba'u edim — patur*. If one admitted to a *knas* and witnesses thereafter came to testify, the defendant is nonetheless exempt. The admission itself generates the exemption. The underlying intuition is that a *knas* functions as a punitive measure for wrongdoing; one who comes forward voluntarily is, in some sense, no longer the proper target of that punishment, whether because the admission demonstrates a reformed disposition or because the legal system seeks to incentivize such confessions.

Shmuel's position is the converse: *modeh b'knas achar kach ba'u edim — chayav*. The witnesses' subsequent testimony renders the defendant liable. According to Shmuel, the admission merely fails to obligate — it does not create an exemption.

### Knas vs. Mammon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר אֲהִילַאי: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? ״אִם הִמָּצֵא״ – בְּעֵדִים, ״תִמָּצֵא״ – בְּדַיָּינִין; פְּרָט לְמַרְשִׁיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Ahilai said: What is the reason for** the ruling of **Rav?** With regard to theft, which is subject to a fine of double payment, the Torah states: "If the theft shall be found in his possession alive, whether it is an ox, or a donkey, or a sheep, he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:3). The verb for "shall be found" is doubled, as the verse states "*himmatze timmatze*." Rav derives from the repetition that there are two matters that are found: The double payment is imposed only **if it is found** [*himmatze*], i.e., it is revealed that he stole the item, **through** the testimony of **witnesses,** and the theft **is found** [*timmatze*], as determined **through judges.** This **excludes one who incriminates himself** through his own admission.

To appreciate Shmuel's position, one must understand the structural distinction between *knas* and *mamon*. An ordinary monetary obligation — say, a loan — exists from the moment the act occurs; admission by the debtor simply confirms what is already legally his burden. *Knas*, by contrast, is not an obligation that inheres in the act itself. It requires *beis din* to impose it. The verse explicitly conditions the payment on a judicial finding: *asher yarshi'un elohim yishalem shnayim lerei'ehu* — those whom the judges declare guilty shall pay double. It follows that one cannot obligate oneself in a *knas* merely by confessing, because the obligation depends on an external determination of guilt by the court. Admission to a *knas* therefore neither obligates the confessor nor — according to Shmuel — exempts him.

### Rabbah bar Ilai on Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי? מֵ״אֲשֶׁר יַרְשִׁיעֻן״ נָפְקָא! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, מוֹדֶה בִּקְנָס וְאַחַר כָּךְ בָּאוּ עֵדִים – פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** Rav asks: But **why do I** need the Torah to teach this here? This principle is already **derived from** a different source: **"The one whom the judges convict** shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:8), which indicates that self-incrimination is insufficient to render one liable for double payment. **Rather, conclude from** the fact that there are two verses to serve as the source for this principle that one who **admits** he is liable **to** pay **a fine is exempt** from paying even if **afterward witnesses come** and testify to his liability. The second verse teaches this additional novelty.

Rabbah bar Ilai undertakes to explain Rav's reasoning from the *pesukim*. The verse concerning the *shomer* who claims *to'en ta'anas gannav* — a watchman who falsely claims that the deposited item was stolen — states: *im himatzei timatzei beyado hagnivah... shnayim yishalem*. Rabbah bar Ilai reads this as encoding two distinct teachings: *im himatzei* — "if it is found in his hand" — refers to discovery through witnesses (*be'edim*), while *timatzei beyad hadayanim* — "found by the judges" — means that the judges impose the double payment on the basis of witness testimony. The exclusion *prat le'modeh b'atzmo* follows: one's own admission will not lead to a *knas* obligation.

But Rabbah bar Ilai presses further. The verse *asher yarshi'un* already teaches that *knas* requires judicial imposition and therefore cannot be self-imposed by admission. If so, why is the exclusion stated a second time in the verse about the *shomer*? The doubling of the teaching, Rabbah bar Ilai argues, can only be explained if the second instance is carrying an additional message — namely, that admission not only fails to obligate but actually exempts. Thus: *shema mina modeh b'knas achar kach ba'u edim patur*.

### Shmuel's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר לָךְ: הָהוּא מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגַנָּב עַצְמוֹ, כִּדְתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** would explain the double expression differently. He could have **said to you: That** verse **is necessary to** teach that **a thief himself** must pay double payment, i.e., the double payment is imposed not only upon a bailee who takes a false oath that the article entrusted to him was stolen, but it is also imposed upon a thief, **as the school of Ḥizkiyya taught** earlier in this chapter (63b).

Shmuel is unpersuaded. He reads the two appearances of the exclusion differently: one verse addresses the *shomer* who claims *to'en ta'anas gannav*, and one addresses the ordinary *gannav*. One might have thought the *Torah* is stricter with an actual thief and that even his admission could obligate him in *knas*; the second verse disabuses us of that notion. Both instances therefore teach only that admission does not obligate — neither teaches that admission exempts. Shmuel associates this reading with the position of the *Tanna de'bei Chizkiyahu* cited earlier, and concludes that the textual argument does not compel Rav's position.

## Baraitah: Admitting Before Witnesses Arrive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב לִשְׁמוּאֵל: רָאָה עֵדִים שֶׁמְּמַשְׁמְשִׁים וּבָאִים, וְאָמַר: ״גָּנַבְתִּי, אֲבָל לֹא טָבַחְתִּי וְלֹא מָכַרְתִּי״ – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא קֶרֶן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁחָזְרוּ עֵדִים לַאֲחוֹרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav raised an objection to Shmuel** from the following *baraita*: If a thief **saw witnesses who were approaching** with the intent to testify against him, **and** at that point **he said:** I admit that **I stole** an animal, **but I did not slaughter or sell** it, **he pays only the principal.** This indicates that even if those witnesses subsequently testify, the thief remains exempt from the double payment as well as the fourfold or fivefold payment. Shmuel **said to him** in response: **With what are we dealing here** in this *baraita*? With a case **where the witnesses returned back,** i.e., ultimately they did not testify.

### Case Setup

Rav then challenges Shmuel from a *baraitah*. The case: a man saw witnesses making their way to court — he recognized them, understood that they were coming to testify against him for theft and subsequent *shechita u'mechira* — and he raced ahead of them. Before the witnesses could testify, he declared: *ganavti* — I admit I stole — *aval lo tavachti ve'lo macharti* — but I did not slaughter or sell the animal. The witnesses then arrived and testified to both the theft and the *shechita u'mechira*.

The *baraitah* rules: *eino meshalem ella keren* — he pays only the principal. He does not pay *kefel*, and consequently he does not pay four or five times, since the Torah's obligation of four and five presupposes *kefel* and cannot be computed without it.

### Rav's Proof

Rav reads this *baraitah* as a direct proof for his position. The witnesses did arrive and testify to the *shechita u'mechira*, yet the defendant's earlier admission to the theft exempts him from *kefel*. Since the removal of *kefel* collapses the four-and-five calculation entirely — the additional multiple is predicated on the double payment — the result is that the defendant pays only the principal. This, Rav argues, demonstrates that *modeh b'knas patur af al pi sheba'u edim*: admission to a *knas* is an exemption even when witnesses subsequently testify.

### Shmuel's Defense

Shmuel deflects the proof with a pointed reinterpretation of the *baraitah*'s facts. He contends: *shechazu edim me'achorav* — when the defendant admitted, the witnesses who had been heading to court simply turned around and went home. Because he had already confessed, they saw no purpose in testifying. The *baraitah* is therefore not describing a case in which witnesses testified despite a prior admission and the admission nonetheless exempted; rather, it is a case in which the admission caused the witnesses to forgo testifying altogether. The *chiddush* is different: that other witnesses who testified to the *shechita u'mechira* do not render him liable once the *kefel* obligation has been removed. This is a notable concession to Rav's framing of the factual narrative, even though it does not constitute a concession to the legal principle.

### R. Elazar's Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יָבוֹאוּ עֵדִים וְיָעִידוּ; מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: לָא!
>
> **Translation:** Rav raised an objection: **But** this interpretation is impossible, as can be seen **from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the *baraita* **teaches: Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: Let the witnesses come and testify.** This means that their testimony will be accepted and the thief will be required to pay the relevant fines. **By inference** it may be deduced **that the first *tanna*** holds that **no,** there is no point in the witnesses testifying, as the thief will be exempt in any case.

The *gemara* challenges Shmuel's reinterpretation from the *seifa* — the concluding clause — of the same *baraitah*. Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon offers a dissenting view within that text: *yavo'u edim ve'ya'idu* — let the witnesses come and testify. In the very case under discussion, where the defendant has raced ahead of the witnesses and confessed, Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon insists that the witnesses should nonetheless proceed to testify and render him liable in *kefel*.

### Tana Kama vs. R. Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל: לָאו אִיכָּא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּקָאֵי כְּווֹתִי? אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי – כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said to** Rav: **Isn't there** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, which stands in accordance with my** opinion? When I said that one who admits that he is liable to pay a fine and afterward witnesses testified that he committed that act is deemed liable, **I said** this ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.**

The implications of this dissent are significant. *Michlal* — by inference from Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon's position — the *tana kama* must hold the opposite: that once the defendant admitted, there would be no point in the witnesses testifying, because the admission has created an exemption. Thus the *baraitah* encodes a dispute between the *tana kama*, who holds *modeh b'knas patur*, and Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon, who holds *chayav* even with a prior admission.

For Shmuel, this is perfectly acceptable: *l'didi k'Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon d'ka'i k'vasi* — he is aligned with Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon. The *tana kama* reflects an opposing tannaitic view, and there is a genuine *machlokes tana'im* on this question, which Shmuel acknowledges comfortably.

The *gemara* then asks whether Rav is similarly compelled to acknowledge a *machlokes tana'im* — whether Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon necessarily represents a view that contradicts Rav's principle. This leads to the next refinement.

## Sincere vs. Fear-Motivated Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי – אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן; עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָתָם, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מוֹדֵי מֵחֲמַת בִּיעֲתוּתָא דְעֵדִים; אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּמוֹדֶה מֵעַצְמוֹ – אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav** could have **said to you: I say** my opinion in accordance with the rulings of both *tanna'im* of the *baraita*, **even according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says** that the testimony of the witnesses obligates the thief to pay a fine even after his admission **only there,** in that specific case, **because** the thief **admitted** his guilt solely **due to** his **fear** of the impending testimony **of the witnesses. But here,** in an ordinary case of admission, **where** there is no impending testimony to incriminate him, and **he admits** his guilt **of his own** volition, **even Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** would **concede** that the thief is exempt from payment even if witnesses subsequently testify that he is liable.

### Rav's Refinement

Rav offers a nuanced response: *ana d'amri afilu k'Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon* — he can hold his position while still being consistent with Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon. The key is that in the *baraitah*'s case, the defendant's admission was made specifically because he saw the witnesses approaching the courthouse. That context matters enormously. Rav argues that Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon's ruling — that witnesses may come and obligate despite the admission — is limited precisely to that scenario.

### Demodei Me'Anfsheih

The distinction Rav draws is between two categories of *hoda'ah*. An admission that is *demodei me'anfsheih* — from one's own initiative, with no external pressure motivating the confession — is a genuine *hoda'ah* and warrants the exemption. An admission made *machmat bisusa d'edim* — out of fear or awareness that witnesses are about to arrive — is a strategically motivated act, not a sincere expression of contrition or legal obligation. Such an admission is aimed at preempting the *kefel* liability and has no genuine character as a confession. It neither obligates the defendant in *knas* nor earns him the exemption that a true *hoda'ah* provides.

The *baraitah*'s case — where the defendant saw the witnesses boarding their transportation to court and raced ahead — is paradigmatically an admission *machmat bisusa d'edim*. That is precisely why Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon says the witnesses should proceed; the admission was not genuine.

### R. Elazar Concedes

On Rav's reading, therefore, Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon is not a dissenter from the general principle of *modeh b'knas patur* but rather an authority who identifies this particular case as falling outside the principle's scope. In any normal case — where a person comes forward *demodei me'anfsheih*, admitting without strategic motive — even Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon would agree that the admission serves as an exemption. The dispute in the *baraitah* is thus confined to the special scenario of fear-motivated admission, and Rav can maintain his general position without conflict.

For Shmuel, by contrast, this resolution is unavailable. He must accept that the *tana kama* and Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Shimon dispute the general principle, even in cases of sincere admission.

## Rav Hamnuna: Chiyuv Atzmo B'Keren

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִילְּתֵיהּ דְּרַב בְּאוֹמֵר ״גָּנַבְתִּי״, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים שֶׁגָּנַב – פָּטוּר, שֶׁהֲרֵי חִיֵּיב עַצְמוֹ בְּקֶרֶן. אֲבָל אָמַר ״לֹא גָּנַבְתִּי״, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים שֶׁגָּנַב, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״טָבַחְתִּי וּמָכַרְתִּי״, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים שֶׁטָּבַח וּמָכַר – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהֲרֵי פָּטַר עַצְמוֹ מִכְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna said: Rav's statement is** more **reasonable** in a case **where** the thief **says: I stole** an item, **and** subsequently **witnesses came** and testified **that he stole** that item. In that case it is logical that the thief is **exempt** from paying the fine despite the witnesses' testimony, because he at least **obligated himself to** pay **the principal** amount via his admission. **But** if **he says: I did not steal** anything, **and witnesses came** and testified **that he did steal** an animal, **and subsequently** the thief **says:** Yes, I did steal the animal, and **I** also **slaughtered** it, **or** I also **sold** it, **and witnesses came** and testified **that he slaughtered or sold** it, he is **liable** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment. The reason he is liable is **that** through his admission he sought to **exempt himself from any** payment whatsoever. In order for an admission to exempt the perpetrator from a fine, it must include an admission that he is liable to pay some payment.

### Obligation in Principal Amount

A further refinement of Rav's position is offered by Rav Hamnuna. Even granting Rav's general principle, Rav Hamnuna argues that *mistabra milta deRav* — the reasoning of Rav's view is most coherent — only in a case where the admission itself obligates the confessor in the *keren*, the principal. The case he specifies is: *omer ganavti u'va'u edim sheganav — patur, dechayev atzmo bekeren*. When someone says *ganavti* — I stole — that admission, while not creating an obligation in *kefel* (which remains a *knas*), does obligate him to restore the principal amount of what was stolen. He has genuinely assumed a monetary burden through his confession.

### Incentive Theory

The logic underlying Rav Hamnuna's position operates on two levels. Conceptually, a true *hoda'ah* involves the confessor incurring an obligation — he is giving something up, acknowledging a real debt. An admission that carries no monetary consequence is not, in the full sense, an admission; it is merely a statement. On an instrumental level, the exemption from *knas* may serve as an incentive for wrongdoers to at least make the victim whole with respect to the principal. That incentive structure only makes sense when the admission entails paying the *keren*. Where the admission carries no monetary obligation whatsoever, neither the conceptual nor the instrumental rationale supports awarding an exemption.

### Theft vs. Shechita U'Mechira

Rav Hamnuna illustrates by contrasting two cases. In the first, a defendant says *ganavti* — I stole — and witnesses come to confirm the theft. This admission obligates him in the *keren* even though it spares him from *kefel*, and therefore, on Rav's principle, the admission exempts him from *kefel* even when witnesses subsequently arrive. In the second case, witnesses have already established the theft — so the *keren* obligation is no longer created by the admission but by the witnesses — and the defendant then separately admits to *tavachti u'macharti*, I slaughtered or sold the animal. At the moment of that second admission, there is nothing of monetary consequence that the confession is adding. The *shechita u'mechira* carries only the additional penalty of four and five times, which is entirely *knas*. Since the admission to *tavachti u'macharti* obligates the defendant in nothing — *depatar nafsheih mikula*, it only exempts him — it does not qualify as an *hoda'ah* that earns the exemption. If witnesses come after and testify to the *shechita u'mechira*, he is *chayav*.

## Rava Challenges Rav Hamnuna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קַפַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְסָבֵי דְּבֵי רַב. דְּהָא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל פּוֹטֵר עַצְמוֹ מִכְּלוּם הֲוָה, וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב הוּנָא – וְלָא קָא מְשַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** In this case **I have gotten the better [*kipaḥti*] of the elders of the school of Rav,** which is a reference to Rav Hamnuna. The reason is **that** in the *baraita* that discusses **Rabban Gamliel** injuring his slave, he **was** seeking to **exempt himself from any payment** whatsoever through his admission, **and** yet earlier in the Gemara it was said that **Rav Ḥisda stated** this *baraita* **to Rav Huna** to challenge Rav Huna's opinion, **and** Rav Huna **did not answer him** that Rabban Gamliel's case was different because his admission served to exempt him entirely.

### Rabban Gamliel & Tavi

Rava challenges Rav Hamnuna's formulation — *kapcheinhu sabei d'vei Rav*, he claims he has bested the elders of Rav's academy — by pointing to the case of Rabban Gamliel discussed on the previous *daf*. Rabban Gamliel admitted that he had blinded the eye of his servant Tavi. The legal framework for injuring a Canaanite servant is that the master must free the servant; this remedy is conceived entirely as a *knas* — there is no *mamon* component, no principal obligation that the master assumes through his admission. His admission was, in Rav Hamnuna's terms, *poter atzmo mikula* — it exempted him from everything while obligating him in nothing.

### Pure Exemption Problem

Yet the discussion on the previous *daf* — in which Rav Chisda challenged Rav Huna — treated that case as potentially relevant to whether Rabban Gamliel's admission served as an exemption from liability or merely failed to obligate him. Rava's argument is that had Rav Hamnuna's principle been operative, Rav Huna would have had a simpler answer available to him in that exchange: he could have said that in the case of Tavi, there was no *chiyuv b'keren* whatsoever, and therefore the admission could never function as an exemption regardless of any other consideration. The fact that Rav Huna did not employ this answer — and instead gave a different response — suggests that Rav Huna, speaking in the tradition of Rav, did not accept Rav Hamnuna's condition.

### Rava's Position

Rava concludes accordingly that Rav's position does not require *chiyuv atzmo b'keren* as a condition for the exemption to apply. Even where an admission purely exempts the confessor — where he assumes no monetary obligation through the confession — Rav would maintain that the admission generates an exemption. Rav Hamnuna's refinement, while logically coherent, does not accurately represent Rav's view.

## R. Yochanan's Middle Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״גָּנַבְתִּי״, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים שֶׁגָּנַב – פָּטוּר, שֶׁהֲרֵי חִיֵּיב עַצְמוֹ בְּקֶרֶן. אֲבָל אָמַר ״לֹא גָּנַבְ[תִּי]״, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים שֶׁגָּנַב, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר ״טָבַחְתִּי וּמָכַרְתִּי״, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים שֶׁטָּבַח וּמָכַר – חַיָּיב, שֶׁהֲרֵי פָּטַר עַצְמוֹ מִכְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It was also stated** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** the same opinion as Rav Hamnuna. If a thief says: **I stole** an item, **and witnesses came** and testified **that he stole** that item, the thief is **exempt** from paying the fine despite the witnesses' testimony, because he at least **obligates himself,** through his admission, **to** pay **the principal. But** if **he says: I did not steal** anything, **and witnesses came** and testified **that he did steal** an animal, **and subsequently** the thief **says:** Yes, I did steal the animal, and **I** also **slaughtered** it, **or** I also **sold** it, and then **witnesses came** and testified **that he slaughtered or sold** it, he is **liable** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, **as** through his admission **he was** seeking to **exempt himself from any** payment whatsoever.

### Admission + Principal

The *gemara* then records a statement transmitted by Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, which precisely tracks the distinction Rav Hamnuna articulated. Rabbi Yochanan rules: *ganavti u'va'u edim sheganav — patur, she-harei chiyuv atzmo b'keren*. If one says *ganavti* and witnesses come to confirm the theft, he is exempt from *kefel* because his admission obligated him in the *keren*. The principal obligation is present; the admission is substantive; the exemption applies.

### Admission Without Principal

In the contrasting case, the defendant denied stealing — *lo ganavti* — witnesses then established the theft, and subsequently the defendant admitted *tavachti u'macharti*. Witnesses then arrived to confirm the *shechita u'mechira*. Rabbi Yochanan rules: *chayav*. In that configuration, the admission to *tavachti u'macharti* — given that the *keren* obligation was already imposed by witnesses to the theft — creates no new monetary burden on the defendant. *She-harei patar atzmo mikolum*: the admission only serves to exempt, not to obligate. Such an admission does not function as an *hoda'ah* warranting exemption from the subsequently corroborated *knas*.

### R. Yochanan's Authority

Even if Rava is correct that Rav himself does not require *chiyuv b'keren* as a condition, Rabbi Yochanan's position is an independently authoritative ruling. His distinction — between an admission that carries a *keren* obligation and one that does not — represents a coherent middle ground between Rav and Shmuel, and remains a live *halachic* principle regardless of whether it precisely tracks Rav's own view.

## Rav Ashi's Textual Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין וּבָרַיְיתָא נָמֵי דַּיְקָא; מַתְנִיתִין – דִּתְנַן: גָּנַב עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם, וְטָבַח וּמָכַר עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** There is **a mishna and a *baraita*** that are **also precisely** formulated in accordance with this opinion. **The mishna** is **as we learned** (74b): If one **stole** an ox or a sheep, as established **based on** the testimony of **two** witnesses, **and he** subsequently **slaughtered or sold** the stolen animal, as established **based on** the testimony of **one witness or based on his own** admission, **he pays** the **double payment, but he does not pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.**

### Mishnah's Specificity

Rav Ashi adds that this same distinction — between an admission that obligates in the principal and one that does not — is actually embedded in the *mishna* and the *baraitah* themselves, as demonstrated by close textual reading. The *mishna* states: *ganav al pi shnayim ve'tavach al pi ed echad o al pi atzmo — meshalem tashlumei kefel ve'eino meshalem tashlumei arba'a ve'chamisha*. He stole as established by two witnesses, and the *shechita u'mechira* was established by one witness or by his own admission — he pays *kefel* but not four or five.

Rav Ashi asks: why did the *mishna* specifically construct the case so that the *geneivah* was established by two witnesses while only the *shechita u'mechira* was admitted to? If the *mishna* simply wanted to convey that one's admission does not obligate in *knas*, it could have presented all the variables together — one who admitted to both the *geneivah* and the *shechita u'mechira* and should pay only the *keren*. Why limit the illustration to admission at the stage of *shechita u'mechira*?

### Admission = One Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי דְּתָנֵי גָּנַב עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם? לִיתְנֵי: גָּנַב וְטָבַח [וּמָכַר] עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא הַקֶּרֶן!
>
> **Translation:** **Why do I** need the mishna to **teach** in the beginning of this case: If one **stole** an ox or a sheep, as established **based on** the testimony of **two** witnesses? **Let** the mishna **teach** the case more simply: If one **stole** an animal **and** then **slaughtered or sold** it, as established **based on** the testimony of **one witness or based on his own** admission, **he pays only the principal.** This would serve to teach the same principle in a less complicated manner, without the need for two additional witnesses.

The *mishna* had earlier paired *al pi atzmo* with *al pi ed echad* — one's own admission alongside the testimony of a single witness — as parallel cases producing the same outcome. This comparison was previously examined by the *gemara* and carried a significant implication: just as a single witness does not exempt but merely fails to obligate (such that a second witness arriving later would render the defendant liable), so too one's own admission may not exempt but merely fail to obligate.

### No Principal = No Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: גָּנַב עַל פִּי שְׁנַיִם וְטָבַח עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – הוּא דְּלָא מְחַיֵּיב עַצְמוֹ בְּקֶרֶן, הוּא דְּאָמְרִינַן עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ דּוּמְיָא דְּעַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד; מָה עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד – כִּי אָתֵי עֵד אֶחָד מִצְטָרֵף בַּהֲדֵיהּ, מִחַיַּיב; עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ נָמֵי – כִּי אָתוּ עֵדִים, מִחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** by citing the more complicated case, **this** is what the mishna **teaches us: It is** only in this case, where it is established that the thief **stole** an animal **based on** the testimony of **two** witnesses, **and** it is established that **he slaughtered** or sold it **based on** the testimony of **one witness or based on his own** admission, and **where he does not obligate himself** through his admission **to** pay **the principal,** that he is obligated to pay the fine. The Gemara elaborates: In other words, it is only in **that** case, where the theft is established based on the testimony of two witnesses, **that we say** that liability **based on his own** admission **is similar to the** testimony **of one witness,** in that **just as when** the slaughter is established **based on** the testimony of **one witness,** if **one** other **witness** later **comes he joins together with** the first witness and together their testimony is rendered a valid testimony of two witnesses and the thief **becomes liable** to pay, so **too,** in a case when the slaughter is established **based on his own** admission, **if witnesses** subsequently **come** and testify, he **becomes liable** to pay the fine, as his admission would not have rendered him liable to pay any payment.

Rav Ashi now explains why the *mishna* constructed the case specifically around *shechita u'mechira*: it is precisely because in that configuration — where witnesses have already established the *geneivah* and the defendant admits only to the *shechita u'mechira* — the admission does not obligate him in the *keren*. That is the scenario where *al pi atzmo* is genuinely parallel to *al pi ed echad*: in neither case is there an exemption, and subsequent witnesses can impose liability. The *mishna* taught the rule specifically where it applies — namely, where the admission carries no *keren* obligation — and used the parallel to *ed echad* to convey that the admission, like a single witness, neither obligates nor exempts.

### With Principal = Exempts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל גָּנַב וְטָבַח וּמָכַר עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – דְּחִיֵּיב עַצְמוֹ בְּקֶרֶן, לָא אָמְרִינַן עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ דֻּומְיָא דְּעַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if it is established that the thief **stole** an animal **and slaughtered or sold** it, all **based on** the testimony of **one witness or based on his own** admission, in **which** case through his admission **he obligates himself to** pay **the principal** amount, **we do not say** that when witnesses testify subsequent **to his own** admission it is **similar** to when they arrive subsequent **to the** testimony **of one witness,** and he is liable to pay the fine in both cases. Rather, since in this case his admission would have rendered him liable to pay the principal due to the fact that the theft was not established based on the testimony of two witnesses, it is considered a proper admission, which exempts him from paying the fine.

By contrast, had the *mishna* constructed a case where the defendant admitted to the *geneivah* itself — *ganav ve'tavach ve'machar al pi ed echad ve'al pi atzmo* — it would not have said that *al pi atzmo* is parallel to *al pi ed echad*, because in that case the admission to the *geneivah* does obligate him in the *keren*. There, his admission would earn him an exemption: if witnesses later arrived to confirm the *geneivah*, he would be *patur* from *kefel*. The admission would not function merely like a single witness awaiting corroboration; it would function as a genuine *hoda'ah* warranting exemption.

The *mishna*'s deliberate choice to illustrate the rule only at the stage of *shechita u'mechira* — and not at the stage of *geneivah* — is thus not incidental. It reflects the principle of Rabbi Yochanan precisely: the comparison between one's own admission and *ed echad* holds, and the absence of exemption applies, only when the admission does not obligate in the *keren*. When the admission does carry a *keren* obligation, the dynamics shift entirely, and the admission warrants the exemption. This, Rav Ashi concludes, is encoded in the *mishna*'s own careful architecture.
