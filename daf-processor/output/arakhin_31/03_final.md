# Arakhin 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sold Fields Overview

### Daf Location

Today's daf is 31, though we pick up in the middle of 30b, approximately fifteen lines from the bottom, beginning with the words *lo yimkor berachok*.

### Lo Yimkor Berachok

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יִמְכּוֹר בְּרָחוֹק כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִשִּׂיגָה יָדוֹ״ — יַד עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא יִלְוֶה וְיִגְאַל.
>
> **Translation:** The rule is that a person **should not sell** his ancestral field that is located **in a distant** area and redeem with that money a field that he sold in a nearby area. Likewise, he may not sell a low-quality field and redeem with that money a high-quality field. And he may not borrow money and redeem the field, nor may he redeem the field incrementally, half now and half at a later date. From where are **these** matters derived? **As the Sages taught** with regard to the verse: "And he prospers and finds sufficient means to redeem it" (Leviticus 25:26), the phrase **"and he prospers"** teaches that the money he uses to redeem the field must be **his own possession,** meaning **that he may not borrow** money **and redeem** the field.

The rule is that a person should not sell distant fields in order to redeem his nearby ones. Even though there may be a compelling personal reason to make such a trade, the *Torah* does not want one engaged in this kind of substitution — the underlying intent is that a person should hold onto all of his fields. The *Torah* is not facilitating a swap; it wants him to become whole.

## Matza & Hermeneutics

### Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמָצָא״ — פְּרָט לְמָצוּי, שֶׁלֹּא יִמְכּוֹר בְּרָחוֹק וְיִגְאוֹל בְּקָרוֹב, בְּרַע וְיִגְאוֹל בְּיָפֶה; ״כְּדֵי גְאוּלָּתוֹ״ — כְּדֵי גְאוּלָּה הוּא גּוֹאֵל, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **"And finds** sufficient means"; this **excludes** the usage of means **that were** previously **available,** meaning **that** one may not sell property that he owned at the time of the sale in order to redeem his field, e.g., **he may not sell** a field that is located **in a distant** area **and redeem** a field that is located **in a nearby** area, nor may he sell **a low-quality** field **and redeem a high-quality** field. **"Sufficient means to redeem it";** this indicates that one **may redeem** his field if he possesses **sufficient means to redeem** the entire field, **but he may not partially redeem** his field.

The *Gemara* opens with the question *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for these limitations on redemption?

### Rabbinic Conditions

*Detanu rabanan*: the rabbis taught a series of conditions derived from the phrase *vehisiga yado* — "his hand reaches," meaning he has achieved the means. The *Gemara* identifies several exclusions embedded in the surrounding language.

*Yad atzmo* — "his own hand" — means he must be using his own funds. *Shelo yilaveh vegoel* excludes borrowed money: he may not borrow in order to redeem. *Umatza*, "and he finds," is read as *prat lematzui* — excluding funds that were already available but tied up elsewhere. If a person sold his field while already possessing assets he simply had not liquidated, those assets are not the kind of "newly found" means that the *Torah* is accommodating.

This raises a genuine question. We understand why one should not sell one field to redeem another — that is merely trading one holding for another, and the *Torah* wants him to possess *all* of his fields. But what if the assets one now liquidates are not fields at all — say, other investments that happened to be tied up at the time of sale? The *drashah* of *prat lematzui* would seem to exclude that scenario as well. Why? One possible reading is that the *Torah* is only sympathetic when a person had absolutely no choice. If the funds were available, even if it was not yet the right moment to liquidate them, the *Torah* withdraws its accommodation. Another reading focuses on the *lo yimkor berachok* principle alone — what is excluded is only the act of selling one field to recover another, and other asset classes remain permissible. The exact scope of the exclusion is not fully resolved.

The final condition is *kedei geulah goel* — one may only redeem if he has enough to buy back the entire field. *Ve'eino goel lachatza'in*: one may not redeem in installments. The reasoning behind this "all or nothing" requirement will be clarified as the *Gemara* proceeds.

### Matza Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּ״מָצָא״ הַשְׁתָּא מַשְׁמַע? וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״וּמָצָא״ — פְּרָט לְמַמְצִיא עַצְמוֹ, מִיכָּן אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אִם מִשֶּׁיָּצְאָה אֶבֶן מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ, הוֹצִיא הַלָּה אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְקִיבְּלָהּ — פָּטוּר; אַלְמָא ״מָצָא״ מִידֵי דְּאִיתֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** the term **"finds" indicates** that the means were found **now** and were not previously available? **But** you can **raise a contradiction** to this premise from a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to an unintentional killer: "And one who goes into the forest with another to hew wood, and his hand fetches a stroke with the ax to cut down the tree, and the blade slips off the wood, and finds another person, and he dies, he shall flee to one of these cities and live" (Deuteronomy 19:5). The term **"and finds" excludes one who introduces himself** into the area of danger. **From here Rabbi Eliezer said: If, after the stone departed from** one's **hand, the other** person **stuck his head out and received** a blow from **it** and died, the killer **is exempt** from exile. **Evidently,** the term **"finds" indicates an item that is** there **from the outset.**

Having established those derivations, the *Gemara* raises a sharp hermeneutic challenge: the word *umatza* — "and he finds" — is being read here to mean that the funds are only *now* coming together, i.e., they were not previously available. But elsewhere, a *drashah* reads *matza* to mean precisely the opposite — that the object was *already there*, present from the outset.

The other case is the *arei miklat* context. When an accidental killer goes into a forest to chop wood and the axe-head flies off and kills someone, the *Torah* uses the phrase *vematza et re'ehu* — "it finds his fellow." The *Gemara* derives from this that the fellow must have already been there — already present in the forest — for liability to attach. If the victim came *after* the axe-head was already in flight, the killer is exempt. So there, *matza* means "it found what was already there."

### Stone Case

This principle is made concrete through a ruling attributed to Rabbi Eliezer. If a stone flew from a person's hand and only then the victim stuck out his head and was struck, the thrower is *patur* — exempt. The reasoning: *matza midei de-ita me-ikara mashma* — *matza* implies that the object found something that was present from the beginning. The victim was not there when the stone left; therefore the condition of *vematza* is not met, and there is no culpability.

This directly challenges the earlier reading, where *umatza* was taken to mean funds not previously available. If *matza* ordinarily means "what was already there," perhaps the redemption verse means the same thing — the funds were there all along and you are merely now making them available.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא מֵעִנְיָנָא דִקְרָא, וְהָכָא מֵעִנְיָנָא דִקְרָא. הָכָא דּוּמְיָא דְּ״הִשִּׂיגָה יָדוֹ״, מָה ״הִשִּׂיגָה יָדוֹ״ דְּהַשְׁתָּא, אַף ״מָצָא״ נָמֵי דְּהַשְׁתָּא. וְהָכָא דּוּמְיָא דְּ״יַעַר״, מָה יַעַר מִילְּתָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, אַף ״מָצָא״ מִילְּתָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Here,** "finds" is interpreted according **to the context of the verse, and here** it is likewise interpreted according **to the context of the verse. Here,** with regard to redeeming a field, one interprets "finds" in a manner **similar to** the phrase **"he prospers." Just as "he prospers"** is referring to funds **that** he acquires only **now, so too "finds"** indicates property **that** he finds only **now,** and not property that he owned from the outset. **And here,** with regard to an unintentional killer, one interprets "finds" in a manner **similar to** the term **"forest." Just as a forest is something that is** there **from the outset, so too, "finds"** indicates **something,** i.e., an individual, **that is** there **from the outset.** This excludes an individual who was not in the vicinity when the ax slipped.

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction by invoking the principle *hacha me-inyana de-kra ve-hacha me-inyana de-kra* — in each case the word must be read in its own literary and legal context.

By the redemption case, the word *matza* sits alongside *hisiga yado* — "his hand reached," which unambiguously describes a newfound capacity. Just as *hisiga yado* means "only now does he have the means," so too *umatza* means "only now has he found the funds." The surrounding language pulls *matza* in the direction of novelty.

By the accidental killer, the word *matza* sits alongside the image of a forest — a setting that is described as already established. Everything in the scene was pre-existing: the forest, the chopper, the victim in proximity. In that context, *matza* naturally reads as finding something that was already in place.

What is interesting here is the question of whether this is a purely literary principle or whether there is also a legal intuition animating it. In the redemption case, the *Torah* is only extending its latitude to someone who genuinely had no means — not someone who had assets and simply chose not to liquidate them. In the accidental killer case, the *Torah* assigns liability only when the entire dangerous scenario was set before the act occurred. In both instances, context shapes the meaning of *matza*, and the resolution is elegant precisely because it does not declare one usage correct and the other wrong, but shows each to be internally coherent.

## Sold vs. Consecrated

### Hekdesh Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהֶקְדֵּשׁ כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאִם גָּאֹל יִגְאַל״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּוֶה וְגוֹאֵל, וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But with regard to** redeeming a field from **the Temple** treasury, it is permitted to redeem the field in any of these ways. This is a *halakha* where greater stringency applies with regard to redeeming a field from an ordinary individual than with regard to redeeming it from the Temple treasury. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** with regard to a verse dealing with one who consecrates his field: **"And if** the one who consecrates the field **will indeed redeem it"** (Leviticus 27:19). The phrase "will indeed redeem" **teaches that** one **may borrow** money **and redeem** his field, **and** one **may partially redeem** his field, despite the fact that one may not do so when redeeming a field from an ordinary individual.

The *Gemara* now turns to a related but distinct category: redeeming a field that one has *sanctified* to *hekdesh*, as opposed to one that was sold. By consecrated fields, the restrictions that applied to sold fields — no borrowing, no partial redemption, no selling other fields — do not apply. The verse *ve'im gaol yigal* uses a doubled form, and the *Gemara* derives from this emphatic language that one may borrow money to redeem a consecrated field and may even redeem it in installments. The question is: why the difference?

### Rabbi Shimon Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ בְּמוֹכֵר שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה שֶׁיִּפָּה כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאִם הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה חוֹזֶרֶת לַבְּעָלִים בַּיּוֹבֵל — הוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason** for this leniency in the case of consecrated fields? It is **because we find with regard to one who sells his ancestral field, that** the Torah **enhanced his power,** in **that if the Jubilee** Year **arrived and** the field **was not redeemed, it returns to** its original **owners in the Jubilee** Year. Therefore, **his power was diminished** with regard to the manner of redemption, in **that he cannot borrow** money **and redeem** the field, **and he cannot partially redeem** it.

Rabbi Shimon provides a *mah ta'am* — a rationale — for the divergence. Regarding a *sedeh achuzah* that was sold, the seller is in a relatively strong position: *she'im higia yovel velo nig'alah chozeret leva'alim bayovel* — even if he never redeems it, the field returns to him automatically at the *yovel*. Because he is protected in the long run, the *Torah* imposes restrictions: *hura kocho she'eino loveh vegoel ve'eino goel lachatza'in* — it limits his latitude to redeem, since he has a safety net.

But one who *sanctifies* a field is in a far weaker position: *she'im higia yovel velo nig'alah yotzah lakohanim* — if it is not redeemed before *yovel*, it goes to the *kohanim* permanently and is lost to the family forever. Precisely because the stakes are higher, the *Torah* grants him greater latitude: he may borrow to redeem, and he may do so in installments.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מַקְדִּישׁ שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, הוּא שֶׁהוֹרַע כֹּחוֹ, שֶׁאִם הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה יוֹצָא לַכֹּהֲנִים, יִפָּה כֹּחוֹ שֶׁלֹּוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **one who consecrates** his **ancestral field, since his power was diminished,** in **that if** the **Jubilee** Year **arrived and** the field **was not redeemed,** the field **leaves** the possession of the Temple treasury and passes **to** the possession of **the priests** and does not return to the original owner, the Torah therefore **enhanced his power,** in **that he may borrow** money **and redeem** his field, **and he may partially redeem** it.

There is also a practical argument embedded in the *Gemara*'s discussion: if someone wishes to sell another field in order to redeem the consecrated one, that is actually a sensible strategy, because the *yovel* will return the sold field anyway, whereas the consecrated field would be lost. In that scenario, the seller gains on both ends.

This explanation is striking because the *Gemara* does not simply invoke *gezeirat hakatuv* — it derives a coherent legal principle from the underlying logic of the *Torah*'s concern for ancestral property.

### Tannaitic Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְגוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵינוֹ לֹוֶה וְגוֹאֵל וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹאֵל לַחֲצָאִין. לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבָּנַן וְהָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a contradiction between two *baraitot* with regard to redeeming a field from the Temple treasury: **It is taught** in **one** *baraita*: One **may borrow** money **and redeem** a field, **and** one **may partially redeem** it. **And** it **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: One **may not borrow** money **and redeem** a field, **nor may** one **partially redeem** it. The Gemara explains: It is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which teaches that one may not borrow money and redeem his field, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis; and that** *baraita*, which states that one may borrow money and redeem his field, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who is lenient with regard to redeeming a field from the Temple treasury.

The *Gemara* notes a conflict between two *baraitot*. One *baraita* states *loveh vegoel vegoel lachatza'in* — one may borrow and may redeem in installments. The other states *eino loveh vegoel ve'eino goel lachatza'in* — one may not. The *Gemara* explains: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction — *ha rabanan ha Rabbi Shimon*. The first *baraita* reflects Rabbi Shimon's view, and the second reflects the *Rabanan*, who maintain that the same restrictions applicable to a sold field apply to a consecrated one as well.

### Tosafot Analysis

Rashi reads this dispute as applying directly to the case of *makdish sedeh achuzah*: Rabbi Shimon would permit borrowing and partial redemption, while the *Rabanan* would restrict it. *Tosafot*, however, takes a different approach. *Tosafot* maintains that by both *mecher* and *hekdesh*, everyone agrees: the *pesukim* themselves distinguish between the two cases. The controversy concerns a third category entirely — *batei arei chomah*, houses sold in a walled city, where the *Torah* does not spell out the rules for redemption.

Here, *Tosafot* identifies a broader principle at stake. The *Gemara* follows Rabbi Shimon's articulation of the underlying logic, and that logic has independent force. Once he explains *why* the *Torah* grants greater latitude when the owner stands to lose the property permanently, that reasoning can be applied to cases the *Torah* does not address explicitly. Since one who fails to redeem a house in a walled city within the year loses it completely — with no *yovel* to rescue him — Rabbi Shimon would apply the same latitude there as well. The *Rabanan*, who do not bother to articulate the underlying principle, treat this as a matter of *gezerat hakatuv* and apply the more restrictive rule by default, since the *Torah* is silent.

What makes *Tosafot*'s reading particularly valuable is the methodological point: explicitly stating the legal rationale is not merely an academic exercise. It enables the *halacha* to travel to cases the *Torah* never directly addressed.

## Batei Arei Chomah

### House Sale Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר בֵּית בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה — הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹאֵל מִיָּד, וְגוֹאֵל כׇּל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, הֲרֵי זֶה כְּמִין רִבִּית, וְאֵינוֹ רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who sells a house** from **among the houses of walled cities may redeem** the house **immediately,** even without the consent of the buyer, **and he may redeem** the house during **the entire twelve months** following the sale, but not after that. When he redeems the house within the twelve-month period, he returns the sale price to the buyer, and **this is** ostensibly **like a form of interest,** as the buyer has effectively resided in the house for free in exchange for the fact that the buyer's money was in the possession of the seller. **It is not** considered **interest,** because the buyer owned the house during the period in which he resided in it.

We now move into the *mishna* on *batei arei chomah*. As noted, the end of *Arachin* covers material that one might have expected to find in *Masechet Shevi'it*, which focuses on the agricultural dimensions; here the *masechta* treats the legal transactions associated with *yovel* and *shemitah*. We have worked through the sanctification of fields from *Bechukotai* and the sale of fields from *Behar*, and now we reach *batei arei chomah* — houses sold in walled cities.

*Hamocher bayit bevatei arei chomah* — one who sells a house in a walled city — *harei zeh goel miyad*: the seller may redeem it immediately upon sale. *Vegoel kol shenem asar chodesh*: he has twelve months in which to exercise that right. This is the Torah's grant: a one-year buyback option, exercisable at the original sale price, which the seller can enforce against the buyer's will.

### Interest Question

*Harei zeh kemin ribit veino ribit* — it is like *ribit* but not actual *ribit*. The concern arises because the *Torah* does not prescribe a proportional reduction of the purchase price based on how long the buyer held the house. If I sell for a million and buy it back six months later for the same million, the buyer has effectively received six months of occupancy for free — he paid a million, got it back, and had the use of the property in between. That closely resembles a loan with interest: lend the money, get it back, keep the benefit.

Yet it is not genuine *ribit* for at least two reasons. First, it was not framed as a loan but as a sale; the transaction was constituted as a purchase and a repurchase option. Second — as will be articulated — it did not have to end this way. Had the seller not redeemed, the buyer would simply have kept the house permanently. That makes it a case of *tzad echad beribit*: only one of the two possible outcomes leads to the appearance of interest. The *Gemara* will examine both of these arguments in detail.

### Succession Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת הַמּוֹכֵר — יִגְאַל בְּנוֹ, מֵת הַלּוֹקֵחַ — יִגְאַל מִיַּד בְּנוֹ. אֵין מוֹנִין שָׁנָה אֶלָּא מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁמָּכַר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַד מְלֹאת לוֹ שָׁנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** If **the seller died, his son may redeem** the house from the buyer. If **the buyer died,** the seller **may redeem** it **from the possession of** the buyer's **son.** If the buyer sold the house to another, **one calculates** the **year only from the time that** the owner **sold** the house to the first buyer, **as it is stated:** "And if it is not redeemed **until the passage of** a full **year for him,** then the house that is in the walled city shall stand in possession of the one who bought it in perpetuity" (Leviticus 25:30). The term "for him" indicates that the year is calculated from when the initial owner sold the house.

*Met hamocher yigal beno* — if the seller dies, his son may still redeem. One might have thought that since the *Torah* uses the language of "a man sells," the right to redeem is personal to that seller. The *Gemara* therefore derives from the phrase *veyasag geulato* — "its redemption shall be achieved" — that redemption is described as a goal in its own right, not merely as an act tied to the specific individual who sold. The result applies *mikol makom*, in all circumstances: even if it is the seller's son, not the seller himself.

*Met halokeach yigal miyad beno* — if the buyer dies, the seller may redeem from the buyer's son. Here too, one might have thought that since the *Torah* speaks of redeeming from "the one who purchased it," only that specific person can be the party from whom it is reclaimed. Again, the *Gemara* derives from *veyasag geulato* that redemption is to be achieved as an overarching aim, and therefore it applies even from the buyer's heirs.

What remains unresolved in the *mishna* is the broader question of who may *assist* in the redemption — whether, as by *sedeh achuzah*, any relative may come and redeem on the seller's behalf, or whether the right is strictly personal. That question is not yet fully addressed.

### Year Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״תְּמִימָה״, לְהָבִיא אֶת חֹדֶשׁ הָעִיבּוּר. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: לִיתֵּן לוֹ שָׁנָה וְעִיבּוּרָהּ. הִגִּיעַ יוֹם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה — הָיְתָה חֲלוּטָה לוֹ. אֶחָד הַלּוֹקֵחַ וְאֶחָד הַנִּיתָּן לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַצְּמִיתוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** **When it says: "A full** year," this serves **to include the intercalated month** in the year calculated from the sale, if it was a leap year. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The word "full" serves **to give** the seller **a year and its addition,** i.e., the year during which the house may be redeemed is not the 354-day lunar year, but the 365-day solar year. If the final **day of the twelve-month** period **arrived and** the house **was not redeemed,** the house has **become** the property **of** the buyer **in perpetuity.** This is the *halakha* with regard to both **one who buys** a house in a walled city **and one to whom it is given as a gift, as it is stated:** "Then the house that is in the walled city shall stand in possession of the one who bought it **in perpetuity"** (Leviticus 25:30).

*Ein monin shanah ela mishaah shemachar* — the year is measured not from a calendar date but from the precise moment of sale, as the verse states *ad melot lo shanah* — "until a full year is completed for him." The word *lo* — "for him" — emphasizes that the year belongs to the seller and is measured from his particular moment of transaction, not from Rosh Hashanah or any fixed calendrical marker.

The phrase *lehavi et chodesh ha'ibur* indicates that in a leap year, the seller receives the full thirteen months: since "a complete year" means returning to the same calendar date on which the sale occurred, even an intercalated month is included.

### Rebbi's Opinion

*Rebbi omer: liten lo shana ve'iburah* — Rebbi holds that the seller receives "the year and its fullness." This sounds like it might mean the same thing, but the *Gemara* will clarify that Rebbi is actually advocating for a solar year. When the *Torah* says *shana tmima* — "a complete year" — Rebbi reads this as pointing to the fuller measure of a year, which is the solar year. The solar year is longer than the lunar year, and in Rebbi's view it more precisely reflects the Earth's return to its original position.

This is a striking position. The solar year functions in *halacha* ordinarily only as a corrective mechanism for the lunar calendar — to ensure the holidays remain in their proper seasons. For a *posek* to assign the solar year *direct halachic weight* independent of the lunar calendar is unusual. According to Rebbi, the seller therefore has 365 days to redeem, not twelve lunar months.

## Year Measurement Gemara

### Mishna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״יָמִים״ — אֵין יָמִים פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one who sells a house in a walled city may redeem it immediately. The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The Torah states: "And if a man sells a dwelling house in a walled city, then he may redeem it until the completion of the year after it is sold; for **days** he shall have the right of redemption" (Leviticus 25:29). The word **"days"** means **no fewer than two** days, i.e., the house cannot be redeemed during the first two days after the sale.

The *Gemara* opens this section by noting: *matnitin d'lo k'Rabbi* — our *mishna* does not follow Rebbi's view.

### Two-Day Wait

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Rebbi derives from the word *yamim* — "days" — that the buyer is entitled to hold the house for a minimum of two days before the seller may demand it back. *Ein yamim pachot mishnayim* — "days" implies at least two. Thus *goel miyad* in the *mishna*, which would suggest immediate redemption on the day of sale, is not Rebbi's position. Rebbi requires a waiting period before the seller's right of redemption becomes operative.

### Exact Moment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״יָמִים״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם. וְרַבִּי, מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״עַד תּוֹם שְׁנַת מִמְכָּרוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis, what do they do with this** word **"days"?** The Gemara answers: The Rabbis **require it** to teach the *halakha* that the year does not conclude with the arrival of Rosh HaShana, at the end of the calendar year; rather, it is calculated **from day to day,** that is, until the arrival of the date of the sale in the subsequent year. The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **from where does he** derive that the year is calculated **from day to day?** The Gemara responds: **He derives it from** the phrase: **"Until the completion of the year after it is sold"** (Leviticus 25:29).

The *Gemara* then asks: according to the *Rabanan*, what do they do with the word *yamim*? They read it as *miyom l'yom* — to teach that the year is measured precisely, day to day. This means, in turn, that the year ends not on the same *calendar date* but at the same *moment* — *me'et l'et* — down to the hour and minute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ שְׁנַת מִמְכָּרוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְלֹא (שְׁנַת) [שָׁנָה] שֶׁל מִנְיַן עוֹלָם. וְיָמִים מִיבְּעֵי לְהוּ לְמֵעֵת לְעֵת, דְּאִי מֵ״עַד תּוֹם שְׁנַת מִמְכָּרוֹ״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם — אִין, מֵעֵת לְעֵת — לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״יָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** what do they do with this phrase? The Gemara responds: Actually, **they require that** phrase to teach that the calculated year is **a year from his sale and not the year of the counting of the world,** i.e., not the calendar year. **And** the Rabbis **require** the word **"days"** to teach that the year is calculated not only from day to day, but also **from hour to hour,** i.e., the year is completed only when the hour of the sale arrives in the subsequent year. **As if** one sought to derive this **from** the phrase **"until the completion of the year after it is sold," I would say** that with regard to calculating the year **from day to day, yes,** it is calculated in this manner, but with regard to calculating the year **from hour to hour, no,** it is not calculated in this manner. Rather, once the beginning of the day arrives the seller can no longer redeem the house. **The Merciful One** therefore **wrote** the word **"days"** to teach that the year is calculated from hour to hour.

To illustrate: if the house was sold on 7 Sivan at noon, then the seller may redeem it until 11:59 AM on 7 Sivan the following year. The year does not expire simply because 7 Sivan has arrived; it expires at the exact moment the sale was completed. The verse *katav Rachmana yamim* — the *Torah* wrote "days" — teaches that the measurement includes not only days but minutes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי, מֵעֵת לְעֵת מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״תְּמִימָה״. וְרַבָּנַן? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְעִיבּוּרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **from where does he** derive that the year is calculated **from hour to hour?** The Gemara answers that **he derives it from** the term **"a full** year" (Leviticus 25:30). The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** what do they derive from the term "a full year"? The Gemara responds: The Rabbis **require that** term **to** teach that **the intercalated month** of a leap year is included in the year of sale.

Rebbi, in turn, derives the *me'et l'et* principle from the word *tmimah* — "complete" — a full year measured to the moment. The *Rabanan* use *tmimah* instead to include the leap month, as discussed above. The *Gemara* works through the resulting exchange of *drashot*: each side assigns the available words to the legal conclusions they require.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי נָמֵי, הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְעִיבּוּרַהּ! הָכִי נָמֵי, מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם וּמֵעֵת לְעֵת מֵ״עַד תּוֹם שְׁנַת מִמְכָּרוֹ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **also requires that** term **to** teach that **the intercalated month** of a leap year is included in the year of sale. The Gemara explains: **Indeed,** i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi derives the inclusion of the intercalated month from the term "a full year." Rather, according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, the fact that the year is calculated **from day to day and from hour to hour** constitutes one *halakha*, which is **derived from** the phrase **"until the completion of the year after it is sold."**

### Bar Mitzvah App.

This precise mode of time-measurement raises a broader question: is *me'et l'et* the exception, limited to *batei arei chomah* by virtue of the specific verse, or is it the general rule applicable wherever *halacha* measures a span of years from a triggering event?

*Tosafot* cites Rabbeinu Chananel, who holds that *me'et l'et* is specific to *batei arei chomah* — *davka b'bayit arei chomah beinan me'et l'et l'sha'ot*, only here does the *Torah* reveal this requirement. All other cases — *bar mitzvah*, *bat mitzvah*, animal age classifications, and similar — follow the day, not the hour: *de'be'bayis areicha davka hu de'gali kra, aval inach lo*.

*Tosafot* itself, however, cites another view among the *ba'alei haTosfot* that *me'et l'et* is the universal rule: *kan hu hadin be'chulhu de'be'inan me'es le'es ve'sha'os* — in all such cases, one must measure to the minute. The practical question is live: if a child was born at 6 PM and his *bar mitzvah* celebration falls in the morning of his thirteenth birthday, has he halachically entered his thirteenth year at that moment? The same applies to questions about *bechorot* — whether an animal is within its first year or has crossed into its second. *Tosafot* leaves the question open, and while the general practice follows the position that days, not minutes, govern in most cases, it is instructive that the debate reaches as far as it does from this passage.

## Ribbit Analysis

### Ribbit Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי זוֹ כְּמִין רִבִּית וְכוּ׳. וְהָתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי זוֹ רִבִּית גְּמוּרָה, אֶלָּא שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה הִתִּירַתּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: When one redeems a house among those of a walled city, **this is** ostensibly **like a form of interest,** as the seller returns the original sale price to the buyer and he does not subtract from it in exchange for the period during which the buyer resided in the house. This is not considered interest because the buyer owned the house during that period. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **This is fully** considered **interest, but** in this case **the Torah permitted it?**

The *mishna* characterized the transaction as *harei zeh kemin ribit veino ribit* — resembling *ribit* but not truly so. A *baraita*, however, states *harei zeh ribit gemura* — it is full-fledged *ribit*, except that *the Torah permitted it in this case*. Rabbi Yochanan resolves the apparent conflict: the *mishna* follows the *Rabanan*, the *baraita* follows Rabbi Yehuda, and the two reflect a genuine *machloket*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְהָא רַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה נוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, וְעָשָׂה לוֹ שָׂדֵהוּ מֶכֶר, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַמּוֹכֵר אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת — מוּתָּר, לוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is **not difficult. This** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: Consider the case of one **who had a debt of one hundred** dinars **against another, and** the borrower **made** a conditional **sale of his field to** the lender, stipulating that if he does not repay the loan on time then the sale shall take effect retroactively from the present moment. **As long as the seller,** i.e., the borrower, **consumes the produce** of that field until the time the loan comes due, this arrangement **is permitted.** But if the **buyer,** i.e., the lender, **consumes the produce** during this time, the arrangement **is prohibited,** as it constitutes interest. The reason is that if the loan is repaid on time, the sale is nullified, which means that the produce consumed by the lender will have been consumed as payment for allowing the loan to remain in the borrower's possession.

The debate is illustrated through a scenario in a separate *baraita*: Reuven owes a hundred *zuz* to Shimon. Rather than framing the arrangement as a collateral loan — which would raise serious *ribit* concerns, as the creditor sitting in collateral property clearly benefits from the loan context — the parties do the following: Reuven *sells* his field to Shimon for a hundred *zuz*, with a contractual right to repurchase within a set period. If Reuven repurchases, Shimon will have held the field, received the fruits of the land during that period, and then returned the field in exchange for getting his hundred *zuz* back. He has, in effect, benefited from occupancy without any additional payment.

How is this different from a collateral arrangement? When property is taken as collateral, the entire transaction is framed as a loan — the creditor's presence in the property is a direct product of the debt relationship. Here, the arrangement is constituted as a *mecher*, a sale. The debt may have been the motivation, but the transaction was formally a purchase. That formal character is one reason the arrangement may be less problematic.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַלּוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת — מוּתָּר. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבַיְתוֹס בֶּן זוֹנִין שֶׁעָשָׂה שָׂדֵהוּ מֶכֶר עַל פִּי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, וְלוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת הָיָה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָיה? מוֹכֵר אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת הָיָה, וְלֹא לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even when the buyer consumes the produce,** such an arrangement is permitted. **Rabbi Yehuda said** in support of his opinion: There was **an incident involving Baitos ben Zunin, who made** a conditional **sale of his field** by a similar arrangement **under the direction of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, and** in this case the **buyer was consuming the produce.** The Rabbis **said to him:** Do you seek to bring **proof from there?** Actually, it was the **seller** who **was consuming the produce, and not** the **buyer.**

*Bizman she'mocher ochel peiros, mutar* — if the original seller continues to eat the fruits during the period, there is no concern. *Lokeach ochel peiros, assur* — if the buyer eats the fruits and the seller then buys it back, that is forbidden. Rabbi Yehuda says even that is permissible, and cites a story involving Baitos ben Zonin, who did precisely this arrangement on the authority of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. The *Rabanan* respond that Rabbi Yehuda has the facts reversed — it was the *seller* who ate the fruits, not the buyer.

### Rivka Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? צַד אֶחָד בְּרִבִּית אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: צַד אֶחָד בְּרִבִּית אָסוּר, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: צַד אֶחָד בְּרִבִּית מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the basis for the dispute **between** Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis? The Gemara responds: The dispute **between them** concerns the permissibility of an agreement involving **an uncertain interest,** i.e., an agreement that will involve interest only under certain circumstances. This is the case here, since if the loan is repaid the produce consumed by the lender constitutes interest, but if the loan is not repaid then the field is acquired retroactively by the lender and no interest is involved. The Gemara elaborates: **The first *tanna*,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that **uncertain interest is prohibited, and Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **uncertain interest is permitted.**

The *Gemara* asks: *mai beinaihu* — what is the substantive disagreement? The answer: *tzad echad beribbit ika beinaihu*. The *Tanna Kamma* holds *tzad echad beribbit assur* — even where only one outcome leads to the buyer having benefited without payment (i.e., only if the seller exercises his repurchase right), the transaction is forbidden as *ribit*. Rabbi Yehuda holds *tzad echad beribbit mutar* — since it did not have to end this way, since the buyer might simply have kept the field permanently, the element of contingency renders it not *ribit*.

### Rav Papa View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא צַד אֶחָד בְּרִבִּית אָסוּר, וְהָכָא בְּרִבִּית עַל מְנָת לְהַחְזִיר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ — מָר סָבַר: אָסוּר, וּמָר סָבַר: מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Everyone** agrees that **uncertain interest is prohibited; and here,** the dispute **between** Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis concerns the permissibility **of interest** given **on the condition that it will be returned.** That is, in addition to the arrangement described in the *baraita*, the parties agreed that the buyer will consume the produce, and if the sale will later be nullified, then the buyer will reimburse the seller for the value of the produce. One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that although the interest is subsequently refunded, this practice is **prohibited, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that this is permitted.

Rava, however, reframes the entire debate. He holds that *dechulei alma tzad echad beribbit assur* — all agree that the one-sided *ribit* issue is a real problem. The dispute rather concerns a case of *ribit al menat lehachazir* — where there is an explicit stipulation that if the repurchase is exercised, the buyer will *pay back* whatever benefit he derived from the property during his occupancy.

In such a case the logic seems compelling: the buyer is in the property as a purchaser, not as a lender. If the sale reverts, he will compensate. Shouldn't that make the whole arrangement entirely permissible? The *Tanna Kamma* says no: the moment the sale effectively reverses, the buyer, retroactively, was holding the property in the context of a debt relationship. Even though he will return the benefit, the interim period created an improper situation. Rav Papa's reading clarifies the precise point of contention.

### Rashi Framing

Rashi brings an important distinction to bear. Our *mishna* involves a straightforward sale with a repurchase option, not a transaction preceded by a loan. The loan context is entirely absent; the parties did not restructure a debt as a sale. That makes our case, in Rashi's view, fundamentally different from and more permissible than the *baraita*'s case. In our *mishna*, the arrangement was constituted as a pure sale from the outset, and therefore even if the buyer holds the fruits and the seller repurchases, there is a stronger argument that no genuine *ribit* has occurred — both because it was framed as a sale and because it did not have to end in repurchase at all.

## Multiple Sales

### Year from First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שָׁנָה — אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם שָׁנָה לָרִאשׁוֹן אִם שָׁנָה לַשֵּׁנִי, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״עַד מְלֹאת לוֹ שָׁנָה תְמִימָה״ — הֱוֵי שָׁנָה לָרִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages taught:** When the verse states **"year," I do not know if** one counts the **year** from when the owner sold it **to the first** buyer, or **if** one counts the **year** from when the first buyer sold it **to** the **second. When** the verse **states: "Until the passage of a full year for him," you must** say that it is **a year** from when the owner sold it **to the first** buyer.

*Tanu rabanan*: the word *shana* — a year — raises the question of how to measure the year when the buyer has already resold the property to a third party. If I sell to James, and James sells to Mordechai, from whose sale do we measure the year? The *Gemara* derives from *ad melot lo shana tmima* — "until a full year is completed *for him*" — that the year is measured from the *original* seller's sale. *Lo* — "for him" — anchors the year to the first transaction.

### Title Succession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמִי חָלוּט? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לָרִאשׁוֹן חָלוּט, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לַשֵּׁנִי חָלוּט. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דִּלְדִידֵיהּ קָא מָנֵינַן, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: מָה מָכַר לוֹ רִאשׁוֹן לְשֵׁנִי — כׇּל זְכוּת שֶׁתָּבֹא לְיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In such a case, if the owner did not redeem the house within one year of the first sale, **to whom** does it belong **in perpetuity? Rabbi Elazar says:** It belongs **in perpetuity to** the **first** buyer. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It belongs **in perpetuity to** the **second** buyer. The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** it is understandable why the house belongs to the first buyer, **as one calculates** the year **according to his** acquisition. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, what is the reason** that the house belongs to the second buyer after the conclusion of one year from the acquisition of the first buyer? **Rabbi Abba bar Memel says: What did** the **first** buyer **sell to the second** buyer? He sold him **any right** to the field **that will come into his possession.** This includes the fact that the house will belong to him in perpetuity after the conclusion of one year from the first purchase.

The *Gemara* then raises a further question: *lemi chalut* — after the year expires, to whom does permanent title pass? This question is not self-evident. One possibility is that Mordechai gets it: he is the current holder, and once the year has expired, the property simply cements with whoever has it. Another possibility is that James receives it: since the year is measured from my sale to James, when that year ends, it is James's interest that crystallizes into permanent ownership — and he retains six months to exercise whatever buyback right he may have from Mordechai.

Rabbi Eliezer says *larishon chalut* — James gets it permanently. Since the year is measured from my sale to James, James is the one whose title solidifies. Rabbi Yochanan says *lashaini chalut* — Mordechai gets it. The year may be measured from the first sale, but the permanent transfer goes to whoever currently holds the property.

The *Gemara* asks: granted that Rabbi Eliezer's position is internally consistent — the year anchors to the first sale, so the first buyer receives permanent title — but what is Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning? Why measure the year from the first sale if the title goes to the second buyer?

### Rights Transfer

The answer is provided by Rabbi Abba bar Memel through a principle that recurs in multiple contexts throughout the *Gemara*: *kol zchus shetavo l'yado* — all rights that would have come to James's hand he transferred to Mordechai when he sold. When James sold to Mordechai, he did not merely sell the physical property; he sold everything he had, including his contingent right to become permanent owner when the year expired. Mordechai therefore holds the title not merely by virtue of physical possession, but as the legal successor to all of James's rights. When the year ends, that right — which originated with James — has already been transferred, and it vests in Mordechai.

A question that the *Gemara* does not explicitly address here, but which follows naturally, is the purchase price for redemption: presumably, since everything flows through the first transaction and Mordechai stands in James's shoes, the redemption price is based on the original sale to James. But the *Gemara* leaves this unresolved.

## Leap Year Calendar

### Adar Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: מָכַר שְׁנֵי בָּתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, אֶחָד בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בַּאֲדָר הָרִאשׁוֹן וְאֶחָד בְּאֶחָד בַּאֲדָר הַשֵּׁנִי; זֶה שֶׁמָּכַר לוֹ בַּאֲדָר הַשֵּׁנִי, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ יוֹם אֶחָד בַּאֲדָר שֶׁל שָׁנָה הַבָּאָה — עָלְתָה לוֹ שָׁנָה; זֶה שֶׁמָּכַר לוֹ בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר שֶׁל אֲדָר הָרִאשׁוֹן — לֹא עָלְתָה לוֹ שָׁנָה עַד חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בַּאֲדָר שֶׁל שָׁנָה הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel says:** If one **sold two houses of walled cities, one on the fifteenth** day **of the first** month of **Adar** in a leap year, **and** the other **one on the first** day **of the second Adar,** then the *halakha* is as follows: With regard to **this** house **that he sold to him** on the first day **of the second Adar, once the first day of Adar of the next year arrives, it is counted as** though a full **year** has elapsed. With regard to **this** house **that he sold to him on the fifteenth** day **of the first Adar, it is not counted as** though a full **year** has elapsed **until the fifteenth** day **of Adar of the next year.**

Rabbi Abba bar Memel presents a case that becomes directly relevant as we enter the month of *Adar*. I sold two houses in a walled city: one on the 15th of *Adar Rishon* and one on the 1st of *Adar Sheni*, in a leap year. The following year is a regular year with only one *Adar*. When does the year expire for each sale?

The answer: *keivan shehigia yom echad b'Adar shel shanah haba'ah* — when the first of *Adar* arrives in the following year, the year is complete for the house sold on 1 *Adar Sheni*. But *lo alsa lo shanah ad chamishah asar b'Adar shel shanah haba'ah* — for the house sold on the 15th of *Adar Rishon*, the year does not expire until the 15th of the following *Adar*.

The irony is immediate: the house sold *earlier* in the leap year — on the 15th of the first *Adar* — has its year expire *later* than the house sold afterward on the 1st of the second *Adar*. The buyer of the earlier house has more time to achieve permanent title, but also more exposure to potential redemption.

### Ravina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא, וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: ״אֲנָא קָדֵים שָׁחֵין נוּרָא מִקַּמֵּא דִּידָךְ״! מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אַתְּ נְחֵית לְעִיבּוּרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina objects to this: But let** the first buyer **say** to the second: **I preceded** you and **kindled a fire before you,** i.e., I bought my house before you acquired yours. How, then, can you gain possession in perpetuity before me? The Gemara responds: This is **due to** the fact **that** the second buyer can **say to him: You descended to** the house during the **intercalated** month, i.e., the first Adar, and as it is taught in the mishna, the seller has the right to redeem the house for an entire year, including the intercalated month.

Ravina surfaces the fairness concern embedded in this result: *ana kadim shachinu ra mikmi didach* — "I lit my fire before you." The buyer of the first house has been occupying it for two weeks longer than the buyer of the second. Why should his year expire later? The *Gemara*'s response is pointed: *at machis l'iburah* — you entered an intercalated year. The year is measured by calendar date, not by number of days. If you sold during a leap year, that is the framework within which your transaction is defined. The asymmetry is a consequence of the formalistic rule, and there is no remedy.

### Lambs Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: נוֹלְדוּ לוֹ שְׁנֵי טְלָאִים, אֶחָד בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר שֶׁל אֲדָר הָרִאשׁוֹן וְאֶחָד בְּאֶחָד בַּאֲדָר הַשֵּׁנִי, זֶה שֶׁנּוֹלַד בַּאֲדָר הַשֵּׁנִי, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ יוֹם אֶחָד בַּאֲדָר שֶׁל שָׁנָה הַבָּאָה — עָלְתָה לוֹ שָׁנָה, זֶה שֶׁנּוֹלַד לוֹ בַּחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בַּאֲדָר הָרִאשׁוֹן — לֹא עָלְתָה לוֹ שָׁנָה עַד חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר בַּאֲדָר שֶׁל שָׁנָה הַבָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abba bar Memel says:** If **two lambs were born to** a single owner, **one on the fifteenth** day **of the first Adar, and** the other **one on the first** day **of the second Adar,** then the *halakha* is as follows: With regard to **this** lamb **that was born** on the first day **of the second Adar, once the first day of Adar of the next year arrives, it is counted as** though a full **year** has elapsed, and if it was a firstborn it should be sacrificed before that time arrives *ab initio*. With regard to **this** lamb **that was born to him on the fifteenth** day **of the first Adar, it is not counted as** though a full **year** has elapsed **until the fifteenth** day **of Adar of the next year.**

Rabbi Abba bar Memel extends the same principle to an entirely different context: two lambs born in a flock, one on the 15th of *Adar Rishon* and one on the 1st of *Adar Sheni*. The question of a year's age matters either for the *mitzvah* of *bechor* — which is ideally brought within the animal's first year, as the verse says *shanah b'shanah lifnei Hashem Elokecha* — or for the classification of the animal as a lamb or a ram, which determines what sacrificial use it can serve.

Rashi reads the case in terms of *bechor*; Rabbeinu Gershom understands it in terms of the animal's halachic status category. In either case, the age is measured by calendar date, not by duration. The lamb born on the 15th of *Adar Rishon* does not complete its year until the 15th of the following *Adar*, and the lamb born on the 1st of *Adar Sheni* completes its year on the 1st of the next *Adar*.

### Ravina Again

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא, וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: אֲנָא קָדֵים אָכֵיל יְרוּקָּא מִקַּמָּךְ דִּידָךְ! מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ נְחֵיתְתְּ לְעִיבּוּרָא, אֲנָא לָא נְחֵיתְנָא לְעִיבּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina** again **objects to this: But let** the lamb that was born first **say** to the other lamb: **I preceded** you and **ate vegetables before you,** i.e., I was born first. The Gemara responds: This is **due to** the fact **that** the second lamb can **say to** the first: **You descended to** the world during the **intercalated** month, which is added to the year, whereas **I did not descend to** the world during the **intercalated** month.

Ravina again raises the apparent absurdity, this time from the lambs' perspective: *ana kadim achli yeroka mikamei didach* — "I was grazing in the field before you." Why does the later-born lamb complete its year first? The *Gemara* gives the same answer: *at nachit ibura v'ana lo nachit ibura* — you were born in the intercalated year, I was not. The calendar governs, not the counting of days.

### Gezeira Shava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הַיְינוּ הָךְ! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב ״תְּמִימָה״, הָכָא, דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״תְּמִימָה״ — לָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּ״שָׁנָה״ ״שָׁנָה״ מֵהֲדָדֵי גָּמְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I also** need **this** second *halakha*? **This** *halakha* with regard to the lambs is identical to **that** *halakha* concerning the houses. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **there,** with regard to houses of walled cities, **where it is written: "A full** year" (Leviticus 25:30), this is indeed the *halakha*, but **here,** with regard to lambs, **where it is not written: A full** year, perhaps this is **not** the case; Rabbi Abba bar Memel therefore **teaches us that** by means of a verbal analogy between the words **"year"** and **"year"** the two cases **derive** their *halakhot* **from each other.** With regard to houses of walled cities, it is written: "And if it is not redeemed until the passage of a full year for him" (Leviticus 25:30), and it is written with regard to lambs: "Your lamb shall be without blemish, a male of the first year" (Exodus 12:5).

The *Gemara* asks why this second case with the lambs was necessary, since it seems to make the identical point as the *batei arei chomah* case. The answer: one might have thought that the word *tmimah* — "complete" — written by the house case is what requires going by the calendar date rather than counting days. *Hacha d'lo ktiv tmimah* — in the animal case, where *tmimah* is not written, perhaps one would count exact months, in which case the animal born on the 15th of *Adar Rishon* would complete its year in *Shevat* the following year, not on the 15th of *Adar*. Therefore the case is needed to establish, via *gezeira shava* — *shana shana me'hadadi gamri* — that both cases are governed by the same principle of measuring by calendar date.

### Solar vs. Lunar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״שָׁנָה תְּמִימָה״, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: מוֹנֶה שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה יָמִים כְּמִנְיַן יְמוֹת הַחַמָּה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מוֹנֶה שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ מִיּוֹם לְיוֹם, וְאִם נִתְעַבְּרָה — נִתְעַבְּרָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **"A full year"; Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** This means that one **counts 365 days, in accordance with the number** of **days** in **a solar** year, which are eleven more than in a lunar year. **And the Rabbis say:** One **counts twelve months from day to day, and if** an additional month **was intercalated** into the year, then the month **was intercalated to** the benefit of the seller, i.e., he has thirteen months to redeem his house.

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* returns to Rebbi's position: *Rebbi omer mone sh'sa yamim k'minyan yemot hachama* — Rebbi counts 365 days, corresponding to the solar year. He holds that *shnat tmima* means the most complete measure of a year, which is the solar year. The *Chachamim* respond: *mone shneim asar chodesh miyom l'yom v'im nitabra nitabra lo* — count twelve lunar months, day to day, and if the year is a leap year, the extra month accrues to the seller.

## Gift & Perpetuity

### Twelve Month Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ יוֹם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא נִגְאָל כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לַצְּמִיתוּת״ — לַחֲלוּטִין. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״לַצְּמִיתוּת״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמַּתָּנָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״צְמִית״ ״צְמִיתוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the final **day of the twelve-month** period **arrived and** the house **was not redeemed,** it becomes the property of the buyer in perpetuity. This is the *halakha* with regard to one who buys a house in a walled city and one to whom it is given as a gift, as it is stated: "In perpetuity [*latzemitut*]" (Leviticus 25:30). With regard to this matter, **the Sages taught: "*Latzemitut*"** means **in perpetuity;** that is, the seller can no longer redeem the house against the buyer's will, nor does it return to his possession in the Jubilee Year. **Another matter** derived from this verse is that **"*latzemitut*"** serves **to include** a house given as **a gift. What is the reason,** i.e., how is this derived from "*latzemitut*"? The verse could have stated ***tzemit*,** but instead it states ***tzemitut*.** The expanded term serves to include a house given as a gift.

*Higia yom shneim asar chodesh velo nigal* — when twelve months arrive and the house has not been redeemed, it passes over permanently to the buyer.

### Perpetuity Clause

The verse uses the word *litzmitut* — for perpetuity — to characterize this transfer.

### Gift Inclusion

*Davar acher l'tzmitut l'rabot et hamatana* — the term *litzmitut* is read as inclusive: it encompasses not only a house that was sold but also one that was *given as a gift*. The logic is that the word could have been written as *tzmit*; the fuller form *litzmitut* signals a broader scope. By including *matana* within this *parasha*, the *Torah* subjects gifts of houses in walled cities to the same rules: if the recipient holds the house for twelve months without the giver redeeming it, the house passes to the recipient permanently.

This creates an interesting reversal. Under ordinary circumstances, a gift of land is permanent — the recipient simply owns it. By drawing a gift into the framework of *batei arei chomah*, the *Torah* actually grants the giver a right to reclaim it within twelve months, something that would not otherwise exist. What price the giver must pay to exercise that right is not spelled out anywhere in the *Gemara* or the *Rishonim*; presumably it would be market value, but the question remains unresolved.

The converse implication is also worth noting: if gift had not been included in this *parasha*, one might have assumed that a gift of a house in a walled city would revert at the *yovel*, just as a sold field does. By including it here under *litzmitut*, the *Torah* may be making the opposite statement — that a gift of such a house is *not* returned at *yovel*, and if not redeemed within the year, it is permanently lost.

### Rabbi Meir View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, כְּמַאן? דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר — הָאָמַר מַתָּנָה אֵינָהּ כְּמֶכֶר! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּרַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא ״לַצְּמִיתוּת״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said** the above *baraita* **before Rav Pappa,** and then asked: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this ruling? Ostensibly, it is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as if** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, doesn't he say** with regard to the return of an ancestral field in the Jubilee Year that **a gift is not like a sale,** i.e., an ancestral field given as a gift does not return to the original owner in the Jubilee Year? Likewise, a house in a walled city given as a gift should not become the perpetual property of the buyer after twelve months. **Rav Pappa said: You may even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** and it **is different here, as the Merciful One includes** a house given as a gift through the term **"*latzemitut*."**

The rabbis before Rav Pappa observe: *keman d'lo k'Rabbi Meir* — this *baraita* cannot follow Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir holds *matana eina k'mecher* — a gift is categorically unlike a sale. The entire framework of *yovel* reversions and redemption rights is built around the language of sale, and Rabbi Meir maintains that gifts fall outside that structure. Accordingly, if the *Torah* is here deriving the inclusion of gifts from the word *litzmitut*, that derivation requires treating a gift as functionally similar to a sale — which Rabbi Meir would not accept.

### Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב פָּפָּא, וַאֲמַר לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: וְהָא גַּבֵּי יוֹבֵל, דִּכְתִיב ״תָּשׁוּבוּ״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמַּתָּנָה, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר לָא קָא מְרַבֵּי! אֶלָּא הָא וַדַּאי דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rav Pappa, and some say** that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** said **to Rav Pappa: But** consider the case **of the Jubilee** Year, **as it is written:** "In this year of Jubilee **you shall return** every man unto his possession" (Leviticus 25:13), and the Sages teach that this verse serves **to include the gift, and** yet **Rabbi Meir does not include** a gift. **Rather, this** *baraita* is **certainly not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

Rav Pappa disagrees: *afilu teima Rabbi Meir* — even Rabbi Meir can accept this *baraita*. *Shani hacha d'rabi rachmana litzmitut* — here the *Torah* explicitly wrote *litzmitut*, which is an independent, broadening term. Rabbi Meir's general position that gifts are not like sales pertains to contexts where the *Torah* has not specifically addressed the gift case. Here, the *Torah* has used language broad enough to include it. Even Rabbi Meir would concede that where the *Torah* expressly widens the category, gifts are included.

The rabbis press back, citing a parallel verse at *yovel* where the word *tashuvu* is similarly expanded to include gifts — yet Rabbi Meir does not accept that expansion there. Rav Pappa is forced to concede: *ella ha vadai d'lo k'Rabbi Meir* — you are correct, this cannot be Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir is simply unmoved by such expansive words, and here too he would limit the *parasha* to literal sales.

## Consecrated Houses

### Consecration Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה — הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹאֵל, וְגוֹאֵל לְעוֹלָם. גְּאָלוֹ אַחֵר מִיַּד הֶקְדֵּשׁ, הִגִּיעַ יוֹם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ וְלֹא נִגְאַל — הָיָה חָלוּט לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who consecrates a house among the houses of walled cities, this** individual **may redeem** it from the Temple treasury, **and he may always redeem** it, even after the first year, unlike a sale. If **another redeemed** the house **from the possession of the Temple** treasury, and **the** final **day of** the **twelve-month** period from its redemption **arrived and** the house **was not redeemed** by its owner, the house **has** become the property of the other individual **in perpetuity.**

*Hamakdish bayit b'batei arei chomah harei zeh goel* — one who consecrates a house in a walled city retains the right to redeem it. This is analogous to the general rule for consecrated fields: the owner may always redeem, and has latitude to do so up until the *yovel*.

### Third Party Buy

*Goalo acher miyad hekdesh* — suppose someone else, a third party, purchases the house from *hekdesh*. Once that purchase occurs, the twelve-month clock begins. If the original owner does not redeem within twelve months of that purchase, *haya chalut lo* — the buyer acquires it permanently. The verse *lakoneh oto* — "to the one who purchased it" — is understood to include even a purchase from *hekdesh*, as Shmuel derives: *v'afilu miyad hekdesh*.

### L'Dorosav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״לַקּוֹנֶה אוֹתוֹ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִיַּד הֶקְדֵּשׁ. וְלַחְלְטֵיהּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ? אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְדוֹרוֹתָיו״, יָצָא הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁאֵין לוֹ דּוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? **Shmuel says: As the verse states:** "And if it is not redeemed until the passage of a full year for him, then the house that is in the walled city shall stand in possession of the one who bought it in perpetuity, throughout his generations; it shall not go out in the Jubilee" (Leviticus 25:30). The verse indicates that the house belongs in perpetuity to **"the one who bought it," even** if he purchased it **from the possession of the Temple** treasury. The Gemara asks: **But** why is the consecrator always capable of redeeming the house from the possession of the Temple treasury? **Let it** belong to the **Temple** treasury **in perpetuity** if it is not redeemed within one year. The Gemara answers that **the verse states: "Throughout his generations." Excluded,** therefore, is the **Temple** treasury, **as** it is not a person and it **does not have generations.**

A natural question arises: why does *hekdesh* itself not receive the property permanently after a year passes without redemption? If someone simply held a consecrated house for a year without buying it from *hekdesh*, why shouldn't *hekdesh* acquire permanent title as a buyer would?

The *Gemara* answers from the verse *l'dorosav* — "for his generations." The permanent transfer to a buyer is conditioned on the possibility of generational succession. *Yatza hekdesh she'ein lo doros* — *hekdesh* is excluded because *hekdesh* has no progeny, no generations. As Rashi explains: there is no procreation for the Temple treasury. Therefore the mechanism that creates permanent title — which is built around the concept of passing down to descendants — simply does not apply to *hekdesh*. The owner may always redeem from *hekdesh*, regardless of how much time has passed.

### Yovel Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יֵצֵא בַּיּוֹבֵל, לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְמוֹכֵר בַּיִת בְּבָתֵּי עָרֵי חוֹמָה, וּפָגַע בּוֹ יוֹבֵל בְּתוֹךְ שְׁנָתוֹ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לִיפּוֹק בְּיוֹבֵל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: ״לֹא יֵצֵא בַּיּוֹבֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the phrase in the above verse: **"It shall not go out in the Jubilee"?** After all, it already stated: "In perpetuity." **Rav Safra said:** This phrase **is necessary only for** the case of **one who sells a house among the houses of walled cities and the Jubilee** Year **arrived during the year** of the sale. It might **enter your mind to say** that the house **should leave** the possession of the buyer to enter the possession of its owner **in the Jubilee** Year. Therefore, the verse **teaches us: "It shall not go out in the Jubilee,"** to teach that this is not the case.

The *Torah* states *lo yeitzei bayovel* — houses in walled cities do not go out at the *yovel*. The *Gemara* asks what specific case this is addressing, since we already know that an unredeemed house in a walled city transfers permanently after twelve months, well before any *yovel* question would arise.

The answer, attributed to Safra, is: *lo nitzrecha ela lemocher bayit bebatei arei chomah upaga bo yovel betoch shenato* — the verse is needed for a case where the *yovel* falls during the twelve-month period. If someone sold a house in a walled city six months before the *yovel*, one might have thought that the *yovel* intervenes and returns the house as it would a *sedeh achuzah*. The verse therefore teaches that no — houses in walled cities are entirely outside the *yovel* framework, and even when *yovel* arrives mid-year, it has no effect on the transaction.

What this leaves open is the status of a consecrated house where no buyer ever stepped in: the house sits with *hekdesh*, *yovel* arrives, and there has been no twelve-month private transaction. The *Gemara*'s ruling seems to imply that *yovel* is simply irrelevant to *batei arei chomah* as a category — the house stays with *hekdesh*, available for redemption at any time, with neither *yovel* nor the passage of twelve months in *hekdesh*'s hands effecting any transfer. A similar open question arises according to Rabbi Meir, who excludes *matana* from this *parasha*: if a house in a walled city was given as a gift, does *yovel* restore it? Or is *yovel* simply irrelevant to all houses in walled cities regardless of how the transfer occurred? These questions remain to be continued.
