# Yoma 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Thread Counts

### Sheish & Six Threads

The *gemara* opens immediately after the final *mishnah* of the *perek*, which catalogued the eight garments worn by the *Kohen Gadol* and the four worn by the ordinary *kohen hedyot*, and concluded with the discussion of the *Urim V'Tumim*. The *tanna'im* now take up the question of thread construction in the priestly garments and Tabernacle vessels.

The *braita* states: *devarim shene'emar bahen sheish* — when the Torah employs the word *sheish* in connection with certain garments, it is to be read literally as six. Specifically, the rule is *chutin kaful sheish*, that each distinct strand of material consisted of six threads twisted together. When the word *meshuzar* appears independently — without the accompanying word *sheish* — it denotes eight threads spun into a single strand.

### Thread Counts

Working from this principle, the *braita* enumerates the thread counts of the major garments and Tabernacle furnishings. The *me'il*, the robe of the *Kohen Gadol*, was woven from strands of twelve threads. The *paroches*, the curtain separating the *kodesh* from the *Kodesh Hakodashim*, required twenty-four. The *choshen* and the *ephod* — the jeweled breastplate and the backwards-worn apron, respectively — each required twenty-eight.

Twenty-eight is, notably, divisible neither by six nor by eight, which immediately raises the question of how garments composed entirely of six- or eight-thread strands can yield a composite of twenty-eight. The resolution, which the *gemara* works through methodically, is that each individual strand woven into the *choshen* and *ephod* was itself a composite of twenty-eight separate threads.

The *gemara* then turns to the first foundational question: *chutin kaful shisha minalan* — where do we derive that the word *sheish*, which literally means linen, should be read as six threads spun together? The proof comes from the verse *vaya'asu es hakotonos sheish* — you shall make the tunics of *sheish* — and the same verse enumerates the turban, the priestly hats, and the breeches, each also described as *sheish* or *sheish meshuzar*. The word *sheish* or its equivalent appears five times in that passage — four as the word *sheish* itself and once as *bad*, a synonym for linen.

The *gemara* accounts for each occurrence. One instance establishes the material itself — *de'kittna nihavei*, it must be linen. A second tells us to read *sheish* as the numeral six, meaning six threads per strand. A third teaches that the threads must be *shizurim*, twisted together — not merely six threads laid parallel but six genuinely spun into a single cord. A fourth extends the rule *leshe'ar begadim*, to garments that do not themselves carry the word *sheish*, so that the default of six applies universally. A fifth establishes *l'akev*, that fewer than six threads invalidates the garment — following the familiar principle, operative in contexts connected to the *korbanos*, that a doubling of the verse signals an invalidating condition.

### Sheish = Linen

The *gemara* now addresses how we know that *sheish* means linen in the first place, beginning with the synonym *bad*. The phrase *davar ha'oleh min hakarka bad b'vad* — something that grows from the ground individually, stalk by stalk — is offered as the etymology: linen grows from a central stalk with small buds branching off it, each unit unto itself, rather than spreading into multiple branches. Hence *bad* connotes linen.

One might ask whether *bad* could refer to wool instead, on the grounds that a single sheep provides a unified, unbranching source of fiber. The *gemara* dismisses this: *imra aftzuchei miftzach* — wool threads naturally separate and splay outward. But then, the *gemara* asks, doesn't flax do the same? The answer is that flax threads spread apart only when beaten; they are naturally more unified than wool. So *bad* means linen.

The *gemara* then pauses over an interesting philological observation. It is remarkable that the *gemara* must resort to etymology at all, rather than simply asserting that the word means linen by tradition. One might note that the word *kitna* (flax) is related to the modern Hebrew *kutna*, and there may be a shared etymological root with words for cotton in other languages — though the Talmudic term for cotton is *tzemer gefen*, wool of the vine, a separate designation entirely.

It is worth noting, by way of broader context, that linen is the primary material of the priestly garments, particularly the *Kohen Gadol*'s white garments on *Yom Kippur*, which are entirely linen. The prohibition of *sha'atnez* — the forbidden mixing of wool and linen — takes on added significance here. One compelling explanation, advanced by scholars of purity systems, is that the Torah organizes the world into distinct categories, and the violation of categorical boundaries is precisely what *sha'atnez* represents. Linen is, uniquely among classical fabrics, entirely from the plant world, while wool is from the animal world; their mixture transgresses an elemental boundary.

### Yechezkel

*Ravina* offers a proof from a different direction. The *gemara* cites the book of *Yechezkel*, which describes the priestly garments using the word *pishtim* — *pe'arei pishtim yihiyu al rosham*, hats of linen shall be on their heads; *umichnesei pishtim yiheyu al matneihem*, and breeches of linen shall be on their loins. Since *Yechezkel* uses *pishtim* to describe the same garments that the Torah calls *bad* and *sheish*, this would seem to confirm that those words mean linen.

*Rav Ashi*, however, objects: *hai mi mikamei d'ati Yechezkel man amrah* — before *Yechezkel* appeared, who would have told us what *bad* meant? One cannot use a later prophetic text to establish the meaning of a Mosaic term.

The *gemara* responds by invoking a parallel principle from *Rav Chisda*. *Rav Chisda* taught that the rule disqualifying a *kohen* who is either an *arel* — uncircumcised — or of uncircumcised heart (an apostate) from serving in the Temple is not derived from the Torah of Moses but from *Yechezkel*: *kol ben nechar arel lev ve'arel basar lo yavo el mikdashi*. And yet, if someone challenged that ruling with the same objection — how did earlier generations know this before *Yechezkel* was written? — the answer would have to be *gemira gemiri lah*: it was an established tradition, and *Yechezkel* came and gave it an anchoring in a verse, *ve'ata Yechezkel ve'asmachah akra*. The same applies here. The meaning of *bad* as linen was known by tradition; *Yechezkel*'s use of *pishtim* for the same garments simply provides an additional scriptural basis.

### Meshuzar = 8

Having established the baseline of six threads per strand, the *gemara* turns to *meshuzar*. *Moshzar shemonah minalan* — how do we know that *meshuzar*, when it stands alone without the word *sheish*, means eight threads?

The answer comes from the verse *vaya'asu al shulei hame'il* — they made on the hem of the robe — *rimonei techeilet ve'argaman vetola'at shani, mashzar*. This is the one instance where *mashzar* appears without the accompanying *sheish*. There are three types of thread in those pomegranates — *techeilet* (blue), *argaman* (purple), and *tola'at shani* (scarlet or crimson) — and the word *mashzar* appears.

The derivation of eight from this verse works by means of a *gezerah shavah*. The *gemara* says *veyalif mashzar mashzar miparoichet* — we learn the meaning of *mashzar* by the pomegranates from the same word by the *paroches*. The *paroches* is woven from four types of material: *techeilet*, *argaman*, *tola'at shani*, and linen — three wools and one linen — each of six threads, totaling twenty-four. By applying *mah lehalan esrim ve'arba*, the same total of twenty-four applies to the pomegranates. But the pomegranates have only three types of thread, not four. Twenty-four divided by three yields eight threads per strand.

### Pomegranates

The *gemara* now asks whether the derivation for the pomegranates should instead come from the *choshen* and the *ephod*, which also use the word *mashzar* and will turn out to require twenty-eight threads. If *mashzar* there yields twenty-eight, perhaps the pomegranates should also be twenty-eight, divided by three, yielding roughly nine or ten per strand.

The *gemara* rejects this on a structural principle: *danim davar shelo ne'emar bo zahav midavar shelo ne'emar bo zahav* — we derive a case where gold is not mentioned from a case where gold is not mentioned, and not from the *choshen* and *ephod* where gold threads are woven in. Gold threads constitute a categorically different element.

The *gemara* then considers the reverse argument. Perhaps it is preferable to learn the pomegranates from the *choshen* and *ephod* because those are garments, like the pomegranates, whereas the *paroches* is part of a structure — *d'ohel hu*. So the *gemara* settles on a third source, the *avneit*, the belt, which is also twenty-four threads, is a garment, and has no gold. *Danim beged mibeged v'davar shelo ne'emar bo zahav* — we derive a garment from a garment and a gold-free item from a gold-free item. Dividing twenty-four by three, each of the three types in the pomegranates carries eight threads.

*Rav Mari* adds a further argument: the verse by the *choshen* says *ta'asenu* — you shall make it — which is taken to mean that this garment's construction is unique to itself and may not serve as a model for others.

*Rav Ashi* adds yet another consideration from the verse by the pomegranates, which also says *ve'asita* — you shall make — implying *she'kol asiyot shavot*, that all the making must be equal. If one were to learn from a model of twenty-eight and apply it to three types, no equal distribution is possible: three times ten yields thirty, and distributing two types at nine and one at ten yields twenty-eight but violates the requirement of equality. Therefore, the model of twenty-four, which divides equally among three, is the only valid one.

### Meíl = 12

How do we arrive at twelve threads for the *me'il*? The robe is *kelil techeilet* — entirely blue, entirely one type of thread. A single type at the standard six threads would yield only six per strand, not twelve.

The *gemara* derives the six as a baseline by means of a *gezeirah shavah*: *veyalif techeilet techeilet miparoichet* — we learn the word *techeilet* by the *me'il* from *techeilet* by the *paroches*, establishing that *techeilet* is six threads. But how do we reach twelve?

*Rashi* explains that the word *kelil* means *gedil*, a braid — and *ein gedil pachot mishnayim*, a braid requires at minimum two. The *me'il* is therefore constructed by braiding two six-thread strands together, producing a twelve-thread composite. Every strand in the *me'il* is itself a six-twisted cord, and two such cords are twisted together, yielding twelve threads per strand.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: why not learn the thread count for the *me'il* not from the *paroches* but from the pomegranates at its own hem — *rimonei techeilet* — which are eight-thread strands? If the *techeilet* in the pomegranates of the *me'il* itself is eight, should not the *me'il*'s own body also be eight?

The *gemara* gives two replies. First: *danin kli mikli v'ein danin kli mitachshit* — we derive a garment from a whole garment, not from decorative ornamentation. The pomegranates are ornamental, not the garment proper. The rejoinder is: *aderaba danin gufo migufo v'ein danin gufo m'alma* — on the contrary, derive the body of a garment from its own body, not from somewhere else. But the *gemara* ultimately resolves this by invoking the general rule that was derived earlier — *hanei milei d'amrinan ushear begadim shelo ne'emar bahem shesh* — for garments that do not carry the word *sheish*, the baseline of six applies. Since the *me'il*'s body has no explicit thread count, it defaults to six, and then *kelil*, meaning braided double, brings it to twelve.

### Paroches = 24

The *paroches* at twenty-four threads requires almost no derivation at all. The *gemara* states simply: *arba deshisha shisha* — four types of material, each at six threads. *Lo dina velo dayana* — there is nothing worth arguing about. The groundwork has already been fully laid: each type is six threads, there are four distinct types, and four times six is twenty-four, straightforwardly and without complication.

### Choshen/Ephod = 28

For the *choshen* and the *ephod*, the verse specifies: *zahav techeilet ve'argaman vetola'at shani vesheish mashzar* — gold, blue, purple, scarlet, and linen. That is five types. Five times six would yield thirty, not twenty-eight. The resolution: the four fabric threads — *techeilet*, *argaman*, *tola'at shani*, and linen — each carry six threads, totaling twenty-four. The gold contributes only four additional threads, yielding twenty-eight.

Why four strands of gold? *Rav Acha bar Yaakov* derives this from the term *psilim*, which appears when the Torah describes the fabrication of the gold thread — hammering the gold flat and cutting it into strands. The word *psil* suggests two, as twisting requires two strands; the plural *psilim* doubles it, yielding four.

*Rav Ashi* offers an alternative: the verse says *la'asos besoch hatecheilet* — to weave the gold into the *techeilet* — and similarly into the *argaman*, *tola'at shani*, and linen. This means a single gold thread is woven into each of the four fabric types. Each strand is then not six threads but seven — six of its own color plus one of gold — and four times seven equals twenty-eight.

The *gemara* closes this line of reasoning by rejecting the alternative of six gold threads: one cannot simply add six, since six divided equally among four types leaves a remainder, violating the requirement that all constructions be equal. One gold thread per type, distributed evenly among the four fabric strands, is the only arithmetically clean solution.

## Prohibitions

### Tearing Garments

*Rachava* said in the name of *Rav Yehuda*: one who tears *bigdei kehunah* — the priestly garments — intentionally receives lashes, as the verse states *lo yikarei*, it shall not be torn. This verse appears specifically by the *me'il*, but the ruling is generalized to all priestly garments.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* challenges this reading: perhaps the Torah is not issuing a prohibition but rather an instruction for construction. Perhaps the meaning is — make a hem around the collar, *kedei delo nikarei*, so that it should not accidentally tear. The verse would then be describing how to make it durable, not commanding a person.

The *gemara* dismisses the challenge on textual grounds: the verse reads *lo yikarei*, it shall not tear, not *shelo yikarei*, so that it should not tear. The phrasing is read as a categorical prohibition addressed to a person, not as a design specification. The objection retains some force as a matter of interpretive intuition, but the *gemara* holds firm.

### Removing Items

*Elazar* similarly rules: one who removes the *choshen* from the *ephod* while the *Kohen Gadol* is actively engaged in the *avodah*, and one who removes the staves of the *aron* from their rings, each receives lashes. The sources are: *ve-lo yizach ha-choshen me-al ha-ephod* — the breastplate shall not be moved from the *ephod* — and *lo yasuru mimenu* — they shall not be removed from it — referring to the staves of the *aron*.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* raises a parallel objection: perhaps the Torah is instructing how the items should be secured — *chadkinuhu*, tighten the breastplate to the *ephod*; do it well, so that it will not slip off — rather than prohibiting any deliberate removal. Again, the *gemara* answers with the same textual observation: the verses do not read *shelo yizach* or *shelo yasuru*, but *lo yizach* and *lo yasuru*, the language of prohibition.

### Ark Staves

*Rabbi Yosi be-Rabbi Chanina* identifies an apparent contradiction within the Torah's own treatment of the staves. One verse states *be-tabba'ot ha-aron yihyu ha-badim lo yasuru mimenu* — the staves shall be in the rings of the *aron* and shall not be removed from it. A second verse states *ve-huva et badav ba-tabba'ot* — the staves shall be brought into the rings — implying that there are times they are placed in and times they are not. If the staves may never be removed, what does the second verse describe?

The resolution: *mishtalfin ve-ein nishmatin* — the staves can be shifted, loosened, and adjusted within the rings, but they can never be fully extracted. *Rashi* explains that the staves were fitted with bulbous heads, allowing them to move back and forth within the rings without ever falling free. The word *ve-huva* in the second verse therefore does not describe an initial installation but rather an adjustment — repositioning the staves within the rings where they permanently reside.

A natural question arises: if the staves were loose in the rings, how would they function when the *aron* was being carried? One answer floats the possibility that the rings were fashioned around the staves after insertion, making removal impossible. Another possibility is that gold, being malleable, might have allowed the staves to be squeezed through initially. The *gemara*'s answer — that they shift but do not slip out — leaves these logistical questions open.

## Ark & Service

### Acacia Wood

*Rabbi Chama be-Rabbi Chanina* asks: what is the meaning of *atzei shitim omdim* — standing acacia wood — as the instruction for building the *Mishkan*? The first explanation offered is *she'omdim derech gedilatan* — the wood is to be oriented in the direction of its natural growth. You do not lay the wood horizontally when it grew vertically; you do not invert the grain. *Rashi* adds a further refinement: even the base of each plank should correspond to the root end of the original tree, and the top of the plank to the crown — maintaining the wood's natural orientation in all respects.

A related question: acacia in the wilderness grows as a relatively short bush, yet the planks of the *Mishkan* were ten *amot* — approximately fifteen feet — tall. How was such lumber available? The *gemara* recalls that the wood was brought out of Egypt. The wilderness does support acacia growth, but not at that height. Egyptian acacia, however — a distinct variety — apparently can achieve that stature, which resolves the difficulty.

### Standing Meanings

*Davar acher omdim* — a second interpretation of *omdim*, they stand. This refers to the gold plating that covers the acacia planks: *shema'amidim et tzipuyan* — the planks sustain and hold up their plating. The plating does not peel or corrode. *Rashi* adds the dimension that the wood itself does not rot despite being encased in gold — a remarkable quality, since one would expect the organic material inside to decay over time, regardless of the exterior plating. This is presented as a miraculous property of the *Mishkan*'s construction.

A third meaning of *omdim*: *shema tomar avad sikuyan* — lest you say that all hope for these sacred objects has been lost — *talmud lomar omdim*, the word *standing* teaches that the promise of the *Mishkan* and its vessels endures forever, *she'omdim le-olam u-le-olamim*. Even in the absence of the *Mishkan*, and later the *Beis Hamikdash*, the hope of their restoration remains, and the acacia planks stand symbolically — they have not perished.

### Garments Serad

*Rabbi Chama bar Chanina* further asks: what is the meaning of *et bigdei ha-serad lesharet ba-kodesh* — the garments of *serad* to serve in the holy? The word *serad* evokes *sarid*, a remnant.

One explanation given: *ilmalei bigdei kehunah lo nishtayer mi-sonaihem shel Yisrael sarid u-falit* — were it not for the *bigdei kehunah*, nothing of the Jewish people would have remained. *Rashi* explains that it was through the priestly garments that the *korbanos* were offered, and those *korbanos* provided atonement; the atonement is what sustained the nation. This is a striking theological claim, situated here in the midst of an extended technical discussion of garment construction — a reminder that all this precision in weaving thread counts and fabric types served the ultimate purpose of the people's spiritual survival.

*Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon* offers a different reading of *serad*: these garments were so perfectly woven that when removed from the loom, they needed almost no finishing work — you would lift them off the frame and the garment would be complete, requiring no subsequent needlework. *U-masardin me-hem klum* — only the smallest amount was left over to stitch. *Resh Lakish* identifies this remnant as *ma'aseh machat*, a small finishing stitch.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: a *baraisa* states explicitly that *bigdei kehunah ein osim osam maaseh machat ela maaseh oreg* — the priestly garments contained no needlework whatsoever, only weaving. *Abaye* resolves this: the sole exception was the sleeves. The cuffs — *veis yad* — were woven separately and then attached to the garment body, reaching to the palm of the hand. That minimal joining stitch is the *serad*, the tiny remnant requiring needlework. Everything else was completed on the loom.

### Survival

The *gemara* then transitions to a discussion of the *aron*, moving between the priestly garments and the vessels of the *Mishkan* in a way that reflects a structural parallel — the *kohen* himself functions as a kind of walking *Mishkan*, carrying the sanctity of the sacred objects within him.

The following passage will be seen as part of a broader thematic current running through the entire section: even while the *gemara* engages in highly technical discussions of thread counts, plating methods, and the dimensions of sacred objects, it consistently introduces a transitional voice gesturing toward the post-*Mikdash* reality. The focus on the *viddui* of the *Kohen Gadol*, the blessings he recited after the *avodah*, and now the assertion that *bigdei kehunah* sustained the people through the atoning power of the *korbanos* — all of these represent the *gemara* building a bridge from the ritual world of the Temple to the spiritual world that must persist without it.

## Ark Boxes & Crowns

### Nested Boxes

*Rachava* in the name of *Rav Yehuda* teaches: *Betzalel* made three *aronos* — not three separate *aronos* in the sense of different arks, but three nested boxes that together composed the single *aron*. Rather than taking a wooden box and laminating it with gold directly, *Betzalel* constructed three discrete containers — one of gold for the interior, one of wood in the middle, and one of gold for the exterior — which fit inside one another like nesting vessels.

The dimensions are as follows. The Torah specifies the *aron* as one and a half *amot* in height, equal to nine *tefachim*. The wooden middle box was therefore nine *tefachim* on the outside. The inner gold box was eight *tefachim*, because the thickness of the wooden base consumes one *tefach* from the interior. The outer gold box was ten *tefachim* and a fraction — ten because the outer box extends one *tefach* above the wood to provide a rim on which the *kaporet* rests, and the additional fraction accounts for the thickness of the base of the outer gold box itself.

A *baraisa* records an alternative figure of eleven and a fraction for the outer box. The *gemara* resolves the discrepancy: *ha keman de'amar yesh bo ovi tefach* — the eleven-and-fraction version holds that the base of the outer gold box is itself a full *tefach* thick, which is a great deal of gold; *ha keman de'amar ein bo ovi tefach* — the ten-and-fraction version assumes the outer gold box has only a minimal base. The *mashu*, the fraction beyond ten or eleven, is identified as the *zer*, the small crown-like rim that protrudes above the *kaporet*.

### Three Crowns

*Rabbi Yochanan* teaches: *shelosha zeirim hem* — there are three crowns: the crown on the *mizbeach* (altar), the crown on the *aron*, and the crown on the *shulchan* (table). This immediately calls to mind the famous *mishnah* in *Pirkei Avot*. The crown of the *mizbeach* — representing the sacrificial service — corresponds to the crown of *kehunah*; it was taken by *Aharon* and his descendants. The crown of the *shulchan* — evoking prosperity and dominion — corresponds to the crown of *malchut*; it was taken by the house of *David*. The crown of the *aron* — representing Torah — *adayin munach*, still rests there, *kol ha-rotzeh likach yavo ve-yitol*, and anyone who wishes to take it may come and take it.

### Torah Crown

One might suppose that the *keter Torah*'s accessibility to all implies it is the lesser of the three crowns. The *gemara* counters with the verse from *Mishlei*: *bi melachim yimlochu* — through me, kings reign. The Torah speaks in the first person: it is through Torah that all other crowns are sustained. The crown of Torah is therefore not lesser but greater — the source from which *kehunah* and *malchut* derive their legitimacy.

*Rav Yochanan* further notes a textual curiosity: the word for crown is written *zer* but vocalized as *zeir*. If one merits, *zacha na'asis lo zeir* — it becomes a crown for him. If one does not merit, *zara heimenu* — it becomes foreign to him. The same written form carries two meanings depending on whether the learner is worthy.

It is worth pausing on the cumulative structure of this passage. Throughout the extended technical material on garment threads and the *aron*'s construction, a persistent counterpoint has emerged: even in the midst of all that ritual precision, the *gemara* returns repeatedly to the Torah as the great equalizer — the one sacred object not inherited by lineage, not restricted by tribe or dynasty, but available to whoever will come and take it.

## Torah & Sincerity

### Inner/Outer Gold

*Rava* draws a moral teaching from the *aron*'s construction: *kol talmid chacham she'ein tocho k'varo eino talmid chacham* — any Torah scholar whose inside is not like his outside is not truly a Torah scholar. The ark was gold within and gold without; a scholar must be the same. The gold plating is not merely decorative — it expresses that the inner reality must match the outer presentation.

The connection is made explicit by the verse *mibayis umichutz titzapenu* — you shall plate it inside and outside. The physical requirement of the *aron* becomes the ethical standard of the *talmid chacham*.

*Abaye*, citing *Rabba bar Ulla*, intensifies the point: such a scholar is not merely less than a *talmid chacham*, he is actively despised — *nikra nisa'av*, as the verse says, *af ki nisa'av ve'ne'elach ish shoseh chamayim avlah* — even more despised and loathsome is a person who drinks iniquity like water. The image is one who consumes Torah — as water is drunk — yet does so in corruption, without inner truth.

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* adds: *lamah zeh mechir b'yad k'sil liknos chochmah v'lev ayin* — what is this purchase in the hand of a fool who seeks to acquire wisdom and has no heart? *Oy lahem l'sonayehem shel talmidei chachamim* — woe to those enemies of scholars, a euphemism meaning woe to those scholars themselves. The charge is *she'oskim baTorah v'ein bahem yiras shamayim* — they engage in Torah and yet have no *yiras shamayim*. The intellect pursues *chochmah* but *v'lev ayin*, the heart is not present.

### Torah Potion

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* teaches from the verse *vezos haTorah asher sam Moshe* — and this is the Torah that Moshe *placed*. The word *sam* — placed — also means a potion or medicine. *Zacha na'asis lo sam chayim*, if one merits, the Torah becomes a potion of life. *Lo zacha na'asis lo sam misa*, if one does not merit — whether through insincerity, exploitation of Torah's prestige, or study untethered from genuine religious purpose — it becomes a poison.

*Rava* restates this in his own formulation: *d'uman lah sam d'chayei* — one who is expert in it, who engages with it correctly, finds life. *D'lo uman lah suma d'mosa* — one who does not engage with it rightly finds death.

*Rabbi Yonatan* shows a similar contradiction between two verses. One reads *pikudei Hashem yesharim m'samchei lev* — God's statutes are straight and gladden the heart. The other reads *imrat Hashem tzerufa* — the word of God is like a smelting fire, a force that purifies but can also destroy. The resolution parallels what came before: *zacha m'samchato*, if one merits, the Torah gladdens; *lo zacha tzarfato*, if not, it scorches.

*Resh Lakish* observes that the same verse can carry both meanings simultaneously: *zacha tzarfato l'chayim* — if one merits, the refining fire purifies and brings life; *lo zacha tzarfato l'misa* — if not, the same fire consumes.

### Fear & Purity

*Rabbi Chanina* applies the verse *yirat Hashem tehora omedet la'ad* — the fear of God is pure and endures forever — to *Talmud Torah*: *zeh lomeid Torah b'tahara*, this describes one who studies Torah in a state of purity. *Mai hi?* What does purity mean in this context? *Nosei isha v'achar kach lomeid Torah* — one who marries first and then studies Torah, so that the preoccupation of the body is settled and the mind can engage the learning without distraction.

*Rabbi Yannai* makes a proclamation of great force: *chaval al d'leis lei darta v'tara l'darta avid* — woe to the person who has no yard but constructs for himself a gate to the yard. Torah is the gateway — but a gateway to what? To *avodas Hashem*, to *yiras Hashem*, to genuine connection with God. If one focuses entirely on the gateway and builds no yard behind it — if the learning produces no *yirah*, no authentic relationship with the divine — the gate leads nowhere.

*Rava* turns to the assembled scholars with a direct and urgent appeal: *b'matusa minaichu, lo tirsu tarti Gehennom* — please, do not inherit two *gehennoms*. The scholar who dedicates his life to Torah study and denies himself ordinary comforts and pleasures has already accepted hardship in this world. If that sacrifice is not accompanied by sincerity and *yiras shamayim*, it will yield no reward in the next world either. The least he can do is make the sacrifice worth something.

*Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* offers a final thought on the verse *edut Hashem ne'emana* — the testimonies of God are trustworthy: *ne'emana hi l'haid b'lomdeha* — the Torah is trusted to testify about those who study it. One can look at those who have truly absorbed their learning and see it — and one can look at those who have not, and see that too. The Torah bears faithful witness to its students.

The discussion closes with a final phrase — *ma'aseh rokeim ma'aseh choshev* — referring to the distinction between types of woven work in the *Mishkan*, which connects back to the construction of the garments and the *aron* discussed throughout the *daf*.
