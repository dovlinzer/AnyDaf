# Yoma 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Thread Counts

### Sheish & Six Threads

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, דְּבָרִים שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בָּהֶן "שֵׁשׁ" חוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה. "מׇשְׁזָר" שְׁמוֹנָה. מְעִיל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר. פָּרוֹכֶת עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה. חוֹשֶׁן וְאֵפוֹד עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to those **items** of the priestly vestments about which **it is stated** they must be made with **linen [*shesh*], their threads** are spun **six-fold,** as suggested by the use of the term *shesh*, which also means six. When the Torah states that certain items are **twined,** it means their threads are spun **eight**-**fold. **Threads used to weave the **robe** were spun from **twelve** strands. The threads of the **curtain** were spun from **twenty-four** strands. The threads used to weave the **breastplate and ephod** were spun from **twenty-eight** strands.

The *gemara* opens immediately after the final *mishnah* of the *perek*, which catalogued the eight garments worn by the *Kohen Gadol* and the four worn by the ordinary *kohen hedyot*, and concluded with the discussion of the *Urim V'Tumim*. The *tanna'im* now take up the question of thread construction in the priestly garments and Tabernacle vessels.

The *braita* states: *devarim shene'emar bahen sheish* — when the Torah employs the word *sheish* in connection with certain garments, it is to be read literally as six. Specifically, the rule is *chutin kaful sheish*, that each distinct strand of material consisted of six threads twisted together. When the word *meshuzar* appears independently — without the accompanying word *sheish* — it denotes eight threads spun into a single strand.

### Thread Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה מְנָא לַן — דְּאָמַר קְרָא: "וַיַּעֲשׂוּ אֶת הַכׇּתְנֹת שֵׁשׁ ... וְאֵת הַמִּצְנֶפֶת שֵׁשׁ וְאֶת פַּאֲרֵי הַמִּגְבָּעֹת שֵׁשׁ וְאֶת מִכְנְסֵי הַבָּד שֵׁשׁ מׇשְׁזָר". חַמְשָׁה קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי: חַד לְגוּפֵיהּ, דְּכִיתָּנָא נִיהְווֹ. וְחַד, שֶׁיְּהֵא חוּטָן כָּפוּל שִׁשָּׁה. וְחַד, שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שְׁזוּרִין. וְחַד, לִשְׁאָר בְּגָדִים שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן שֵׁשׁ. וְחַד, לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: That **the threads** made from *shesh* are spun **six-fold, from where do we** derive this? **The verse states: "And they made the tunics of linen** [***shesh***] of woven work for Aaron and for his sons. **And the mitre of linen, and the adorning mitres of linen, and the linen [*bad*] trousers of twined linen"** (Exodus 39:27–28). **Five mentions** of the word linen are **written;** four times as "*shesh*" and an additional instance of "*bad*," both meaning linen. **One** mention is stated **for that** *halakha* **itself,** to teach **that they should be** made of **linen. And one** mention is written to teach **that the threads should be** spun **six-fold,** *shesh* being interpreted as six. **And one** mention teaches that the six strands **should be spun together** into one. **And one** mention teaches that this also applies **to the other garments,** even though the term ***shesh* is not stated** with regard to them. **And one** mention teaches that this requirement is **indispensable** and that garments not made this way are invalid.

Working from this principle, the *braita* enumerates the thread counts of the major garments and Tabernacle furnishings. The *me'il*, the robe of the *Kohen Gadol*, was woven from strands of twelve threads. The *paroches*, the curtain separating the *kodesh* from the *Kodesh Hakodashim*, required twenty-four. The *choshen* and the *ephod* — the jeweled breastplate and the backwards-worn apron, respectively — each required twenty-eight.

Twenty-eight is, notably, divisible neither by six nor by eight, which immediately raises the question of how garments composed entirely of six- or eight-thread strands can yield a composite of twenty-eight. The resolution, which the *gemara* works through methodically, is that each individual strand woven into the *choshen* and *ephod* was itself a composite of twenty-eight separate threads.

The *gemara* then turns to the first foundational question: *chutin kaful shisha minalan* — where do we derive that the word *sheish*, which literally means linen, should be read as six threads spun together? The proof comes from the verse *vaya'asu es hakotonos sheish* — you shall make the tunics of *sheish* — and the same verse enumerates the turban, the priestly hats, and the breeches, each also described as *sheish* or *sheish meshuzar*. The word *sheish* or its equivalent appears five times in that passage — four as the word *sheish* itself and once as *bad*, a synonym for linen.

The *gemara* accounts for each occurrence. One instance establishes the material itself — *de'kittna nihavei*, it must be linen. A second tells us to read *sheish* as the numeral six, meaning six threads per strand. A third teaches that the threads must be *shizurim*, twisted together — not merely six threads laid parallel but six genuinely spun into a single cord. A fourth extends the rule *leshe'ar begadim*, to garments that do not themselves carry the word *sheish*, so that the default of six applies universally. A fifth establishes *l'akev*, that fewer than six threads invalidates the garment — following the familiar principle, operative in contexts connected to the *korbanos*, that a doubling of the verse signals an invalidating condition.

### Sheish = Linen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי "שֵׁשׁ" כִּיתָּנָא הוּא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא "בַּד" — דָּבָר הָעוֹלֶה מִן הַקַּרְקַע בַּד בַּד. וְאֵימָא עַמְרָא! עַמְרָא אִיפְּצוֹלֵי מִיפַּצְלָא. כִּיתָּנָא נָמֵי אִיפְּצוֹלֵי מִפְּצִיל! כִּיתָּנָא אַגַּב לָקוּתֵיהּ מִפְּצִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** may it **be inferred that this** term ***shesh*** means **linen? Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: As the verse** also **states: "*Bad*,"** and uses it interchangeably with *shesh*. And *bad* refers to **something which sprouts from the ground stalk by stalk [*bad bad*],** each one by itself. This is a fitting description of flax, the plant used to produce linen, as opposed to other plants, such as cotton, whose fibers grow meshed together. The Gemara asks: **And say** that it refers to **wool,** since when it grows from the animal, each hair grows separately. The Gemara answers: **Wool splits,** with each hair dividing into several hairs, so it does not fully fit the description of sprouting stalk by stalk. The Gemara asks: But **flax also splits** into individual hairs. The Gemara answers: **Flax,** unlike wool, **splits** only **when beaten.**

The *gemara* now addresses how we know that *sheish* means linen in the first place, beginning with the synonym *bad*. The phrase *davar ha'oleh min hakarka bad b'vad* — something that grows from the ground individually, stalk by stalk — is offered as the etymology: linen grows from a central stalk with small buds branching off it, each unit unto itself, rather than spreading into multiple branches. Hence *bad* connotes linen.

One might ask whether *bad* could refer to wool instead, on the grounds that a single sheep provides a unified, unbranching source of fiber. The *gemara* dismisses this: *imra aftzuchei miftzach* — wool threads naturally separate and splay outward. But then, the *gemara* asks, doesn't flax do the same? The answer is that flax threads spread apart only when beaten; they are naturally more unified than wool. So *bad* means linen.

The *gemara* then pauses over an interesting philological observation. It is remarkable that the *gemara* must resort to etymology at all, rather than simply asserting that the word means linen by tradition. One might note that the word *kitna* (flax) is related to the modern Hebrew *kutna*, and there may be a shared etymological root with words for cotton in other languages — though the Talmudic term for cotton is *tzemer gefen*, wool of the vine, a separate designation entirely.

It is worth noting, by way of broader context, that linen is the primary material of the priestly garments, particularly the *Kohen Gadol*'s white garments on *Yom Kippur*, which are entirely linen. The prohibition of *sha'atnez* — the forbidden mixing of wool and linen — takes on added significance here. One compelling explanation, advanced by scholars of purity systems, is that the Torah organizes the world into distinct categories, and the violation of categorical boundaries is precisely what *sha'atnez* represents. Linen is, uniquely among classical fabrics, entirely from the plant world, while wool is from the animal world; their mixture transgresses an elemental boundary.

### Yechezkel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: "פַּאֲרֵי פִשְׁתִּים יִהְיוּ עַל רֹאשָׁם וּמִכְנְסֵי פִשְׁתִּים יִהְיוּ עַל מׇתְנֵיהֶם".
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** We have a proof for the matter **from here:** The verse states: **"They shall have linen** [***pishtim***] **mitres upon their heads, and linen trousers upon their loins"** (Ezekiel 44:18). The term *pishtim* used in Ezekiel certainly refers to linen; therefore, it is clear that the terms *shesh* and *bad*, used in the Torah for the same garments, also refer to linen.

*Ravina* offers a proof from a different direction. The *gemara* cites the book of *Yechezkel*, which describes the priestly garments using the word *pishtim* — *pe'arei pishtim yihiyu al rosham*, hats of linen shall be on their heads; *umichnesei pishtim yiheyu al matneihem*, and breeches of linen shall be on their loins. Since *Yechezkel* uses *pishtim* to describe the same garments that the Torah calls *bad* and *sheish*, this would seem to confirm that those words mean linen.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: הָא מִקַּמֵּי דְּאָתֵי יְחֶזְקֵאל, מַאן אַמְרַהּ? וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּבָר זֶה מִתּוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ לֹא לָמַדְנוּ, מִדִּבְרֵי יְחֶזְקֵאל בֶּן בּוּזִי לָמַדְנוּ: "כׇּל בֶּן נֵכָר עֶרֶל לֵב וְעֶרֶל בָּשָׂר לֹא יָבֹא אֶל מִקְדָּשִׁי" — הָא מִקַּמֵּי דְּאָתֵי יְחֶזְקֵאל, מַאן אַמְרַהּ? אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, וַאֲתָא יְחֶזְקֵאל וְאַסְמְכַהּ אַקְּרָא. הָכָא נָמֵי: גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, וַאֲתָא יְחֶזְקֵאל וְאַסְמְכַהּ אַקְּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to him: But before Ezekiel came, who said** this *halakha*? How was it known? Ravina retorted: **And according to your reasoning,** the same question could be asked with regard to **that which Rav Ḥisda said** concerning the disqualification of an uncircumcised priest from serving in the Temple: **This matter we did not learn from the Torah of Moses our teacher,** as it is not written explicitly in the Torah; rather, **we learned it from the words of Ezekiel ben Buzi,** as the verse states: **"No foreigner, uncircumcised of heart or uncircumcised of flesh, shall enter into My sanctuary"** (Ezekiel 44:9). One could ask here as well: **Before Ezekiel came, who said** this *halakha*? **Rather,** perforce, **they learned it as a tradition and** then **Ezekiel came and supported it with a verse. Here, too, they learned it as a tradition and** then **Ezekiel came and supported it with a verse.**

*Rav Ashi*, however, objects: *hai mi mikamei d'ati Yechezkel man amrah* — before *Yechezkel* appeared, who would have told us what *bad* meant? One cannot use a later prophetic text to establish the meaning of a Mosaic term.

The *gemara* responds by invoking a parallel principle from *Rav Chisda*. *Rav Chisda* taught that the rule disqualifying a *kohen* who is either an *arel* — uncircumcised — or of uncircumcised heart (an apostate) from serving in the Temple is not derived from the Torah of Moses but from *Yechezkel*: *kol ben nechar arel lev ve'arel basar lo yavo el mikdashi*. And yet, if someone challenged that ruling with the same objection — how did earlier generations know this before *Yechezkel* was written? — the answer would have to be *gemira gemiri lah*: it was an established tradition, and *Yechezkel* came and gave it an anchoring in a verse, *ve'ata Yechezkel ve'asmachah akra*. The same applies here. The meaning of *bad* as linen was known by tradition; *Yechezkel*'s use of *pishtim* for the same garments simply provides an additional scriptural basis.

### Meshuzar = 8

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "מׇשְׁזָר" שְׁמוֹנָה. מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב: "וַיַּעֲשׂוּ עַל שׁוּלֵי הַמְּעִיל רִמּוֹנֵי תְּכֵלֶת וְאַרְגָּמָן וְתוֹלַעַת שָׁנִי מׇשְׁזָר", וְיָלֵיף "מׇשְׁזָר" "מׇשְׁזָר" מִפָּרוֹכֶת, מָה לְהַלָּן עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה — אַף כָּאן עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה, דַּהֲוָה כׇּל חַד וְחַד תַּמְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to explain the *baraita*. That the use of the term **twined** implies that the thread should be spun from **eight** strands, **from where do we** derive this? **As it is written: "And they made upon the skirts of the robe pomegranates of sky-blue, and purple, and scarlet, twined"** (Exodus 39:24); **and derive** a verbal analogy from the term **"twined"** used in this verse and the term **"twined" from** the verse about **the curtain: Just as there,** with regard to the curtain, there are **twenty-four** strands, as will be explained, **so too here,** there are **twenty-four** strands in total. And since each pomegranate is made of three colors, sky-blue, purple, and scarlet, it must be **that each one of them was** spun from **eight** strands.

Having established the baseline of six threads per strand, the *gemara* turns to *meshuzar*. *Moshzar shemonah minalan* — how do we know that *meshuzar*, when it stands alone without the word *sheish*, means eight threads?

The answer comes from the verse *vaya'asu al shulei hame'il* — they made on the hem of the robe — *rimonei techeilet ve'argaman vetola'at shani, mashzar*. This is the one instance where *mashzar* appears without the accompanying *sheish*. There are three types of thread in those pomegranates — *techeilet* (blue), *argaman* (purple), and *tola'at shani* (scarlet or crimson) — and the word *mashzar* appears.

The derivation of eight from this verse works by means of a *gezerah shavah*. The *gemara* says *veyalif mashzar mashzar miparoichet* — we learn the meaning of *mashzar* by the pomegranates from the same word by the *paroches*. The *paroches* is woven from four types of material: *techeilet*, *argaman*, *tola'at shani*, and linen — three wools and one linen — each of six threads, totaling twenty-four. By applying *mah lehalan esrim ve'arba*, the same total of twenty-four applies to the pomegranates. But the pomegranates have only three types of thread, not four. Twenty-four divided by three yields eight threads per strand.

### Pomegranates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף מֵחֹשֶׁן וְאֵפוֹד: מָה לְהַלָּן עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה, אַף כָּאן עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה! דָּנִים דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ זָהָב, מִדָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ זָהָב, לְאַפּוֹקֵי חֹשֶׁן וְאֵפוֹד שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בָּהֶן זָהָב. אַדְּרַבָּה: דָּנִים בֶּגֶד מִבֶּגֶד, לְאַפּוֹקֵי פָּרוֹכֶת — דְּאֹהֶל הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this derivation: **Let us derive** the number of strands instead **from** the **breastplate and ephod** and say: **Just as there,** with regard to the breastplate and ephod, there are **twenty-eight** strands, **so too, here** there are **twenty-eight** strands. The Gemara answers: It is preferable to **derive** the *halakhot* of **an item,** i.e., the pomegranates, **with regard to which** the use of **gold** threads **is not stated, from** the *halakhot* of **an item,** i.e., the curtain, **with regard to which** the use of **gold** threads **is** also **not stated.** This would come **to exclude** the possibility of deriving them from the **breastplate and ephod, with regard to which** the use of **gold** threads **is stated.** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** it should be preferable to **derive** the *halakhot* of one **garment from** another **garment,** i.e., the *halakhot* of the robe from the *halakhot* of the breastplate and ephod. This would come **to exclude** the possibility of deriving them from the **curtain, which is a tent,** i.e., part of the Temple building, and not a garment. The Gemara accepts that the derivation is flawed.

The *gemara* now asks whether the derivation for the pomegranates should instead come from the *choshen* and the *ephod*, which also use the word *mashzar* and will turn out to require twenty-eight threads. If *mashzar* there yields twenty-eight, perhaps the pomegranates should also be twenty-eight, divided by three, yielding roughly nine or ten per strand.

The *gemara* rejects this on a structural principle: *danim davar shelo ne'emar bo zahav midavar shelo ne'emar bo zahav* — we derive a case where gold is not mentioned from a case where gold is not mentioned, and not from the *choshen* and *ephod* where gold threads are woven in. Gold threads constitute a categorically different element.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: דָּנִים מֵאַבְנֵט, וְדָנִים בֶּגֶד וְדָבָר שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ זָהָב, מִבֶּגֶד וְדָבָר שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ זָהָב, וְאֵין דָּנִים דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ זָהָב מִדָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the need for thread of eight strands **is derived from the belt,** which had twenty-four threads in total, **and a garment and an item with regard to which** the use of **gold** thread **is not stated,** i.e., the pomegranates, **are derived** from **a garment and an item with regard to which** the use of **gold** thread **is not stated. And an item with no gold,** such as the pomegranates and robe, **is not derived from an item that has gold in it,** such as the breastplate and ephod.

The *gemara* then considers the reverse argument. Perhaps it is preferable to learn the pomegranates from the *choshen* and *ephod* because those are garments, like the pomegranates, whereas the *paroches* is part of a structure — *d'ohel hu*. So the *gemara* settles on a third source, the *avneit*, the belt, which is also twenty-four threads, is a garment, and has no gold. *Danim beged mibeged v'davar shelo ne'emar bo zahav* — we derive a garment from a garment and a gold-free item from a gold-free item. Dividing twenty-four by three, each of the three types in the pomegranates carries eight threads.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מָרִי אָמַר: "תַּעֲשֶׂנּוּ" כְּתִיב. תַּעֲשֶׂנּוּ לָזֶה, וְלֹא לְאַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said** another reason not to derive the number of strands in a pomegranate from the breastplate and ephod: "Like the work of the ephod **you shall make it"** (Exodus 28:15) **is written** with regard to the breastplate to indicate that **you shall make it,** i.e., the breastplate, like the ephod, which indicates that **for this,** i.e., the breastplate, you shall use thread of twenty-eight strands, **and not for anything else.**

*Rav Mari* adds a further argument: the verse by the *choshen* says *ta'asenu* — you shall make it — which is taken to mean that this garment's construction is unique to itself and may not serve as a model for others.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: "וְעָשִׂיתָ" כְּתִיב, שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כׇּל עֲשִׂיּוֹת שָׁווֹת. וְהֵיכִי נֶעְבֵּיד? נֶעְבֵּיד תְּלָתָא דְּעַשְׂרָה עַשְׂרָה — הָווּ לְהוּ תְּלָתִין! נֶעְבֵּיד תְּרֵי דְּתִשְׁעָה תִּשְׁעָה וְחַד דְּעַשְׂרָה — אֲמַר קְרָא: "וְעָשִׂיתָ", שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כׇּל עֲשִׂיּוֹתָיו שָׁווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** another reason that there could not be twenty-eight strands in a pomegranate: **"And you shall make** pomegranates of sky-blue, and of purple, and of scarlet" (Exodus 28:33) **is written** to indicate **that all the makings of it must be equal,** i.e., that each color thread should be made from the same number of strands. However, if there are twenty-eight strands in total, the three threads, each of a different color, cannot be made with an equal number of strands, as **how should we do** it? **Let us make three** colored threads **of ten** strands **each;** then **they are thirty** strands in total, which is too many. **Let us make two** colored threads **of nine** strands **and one of ten;** but **the verse states: "And you shall make,"** to indicate **that all the makings of it must be equal.** Perforce, the threads used for the pomegranates and the robe must be derived from an item woven from threads of a number of strands divisible by three, such as the curtain.

*Rav Ashi* adds yet another consideration from the verse by the pomegranates, which also says *ve'asita* — you shall make — implying *she'kol asiyot shavot*, that all the making must be equal. If one were to learn from a model of twenty-eight and apply it to three types, no equal distribution is possible: three times ten yields thirty, and distributing two types at nine and one at ten yields twenty-eight but violates the requirement of equality. Therefore, the model of twenty-four, which divides equally among three, is the only valid one.

### Meíl = 12

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלִיל תְּכֵלֶת", וְיָלֵיף "תְּכֵלֶת" "תְּכֵלֶת" מִפָּרוֹכֶת: מָה לְהַלָּן שִׁשָּׁה, אַף כָּאן שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **spun of sky-blue"** (Exodus 28:31). **And derive** a verbal analogy from the term **"sky-blue"** used here and the same term **"sky-blue" from** the verse about **the curtain: Just as there,** with regard to the curtain, there are **six** strands, **so too here,** there are **six** strands. Then, since the Torah also says the threads are spun, i.e., each strand is made of two thinner strands spun together, each thread must contain twelve strands.

How do we arrive at twelve threads for the *me'il*? The robe is *kelil techeilet* — entirely blue, entirely one type of thread. A single type at the standard six threads would yield only six per strand, not twelve.

The *gemara* derives the six as a baseline by means of a *gezeirah shavah*: *veyalif techeilet techeilet miparoichet* — we learn the word *techeilet* by the *me'il* from *techeilet* by the *paroches*, establishing that *techeilet* is six threads. But how do we reach twelve?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף מִשּׁוּלָיו וְרִמּוֹנָיו — מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁמוֹנָה, אַף כָּאן שְׁמוֹנָה! דָּנִים כְּלִי מִכְּלִי, וְאֵין דָּנִים כְּלִי מִתַּכְשִׁיט כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us derive** the number of strands in the robe **from its hem and pomegranates: Just as there,** each thread is spun from **eight** strands, **so too here,** the threads should be spun from **eight** strands. The Gemara rejects this: It is preferable to **derive** the *halakhot* of **a utensil,** i.e., the robe, **from** the *halakhot* of another **utensil,** i.e., the curtain, **and** one should **not derive** the *halakhot* of **a utensil from** the *halakhot* of something that is merely **an ornament of a utensil,** i.e., the pomegranates of the robe.

*Rashi* explains that the word *kelil* means *gedil*, a braid — and *ein gedil pachot mishnayim*, a braid requires at minimum two. The *me'il* is therefore constructed by braiding two six-thread strands together, producing a twelve-thread composite. Every strand in the *me'il* is itself a six-twisted cord, and two such cords are twisted together, yielding twelve threads per strand.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה: דָּנִים גּוּפוֹ מִגּוּפוֹ, וְאֵין דָּנִים גּוּפוֹ מֵעָלְמָא. הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִינַן: לִשְׁאָר בְּגָדִים שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן "שֵׁשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** it is preferable to **derive** the *halakhot* of **an object from that object itself,** i.e., to assume that the *halakhot* of the robe and its pomegranates are similar; **and** one should **not derive** the *halakhot* of an **object from elsewhere.** The Gemara explains: **This is** precisely **what we said** in the *baraita*: One of the five mentions of *shesh* in the verse is to teach that the requirement that threads be made from six strands applies also **to other garments about which *shesh* is not** explicitly **stated,** such as the robe.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: why not learn the thread count for the *me'il* not from the *paroches* but from the pomegranates at its own hem — *rimonei techeilet* — which are eight-thread strands? If the *techeilet* in the pomegranates of the *me'il* itself is eight, should not the *me'il*'s own body also be eight?

The *gemara* gives two replies. First: *danin kli mikli v'ein danin kli mitachshit* — we derive a garment from a whole garment, not from decorative ornamentation. The pomegranates are ornamental, not the garment proper. The rejoinder is: *aderaba danin gufo migufo v'ein danin gufo m'alma* — on the contrary, derive the body of a garment from its own body, not from somewhere else. But the *gemara* ultimately resolves this by invoking the general rule that was derived earlier — *hanei milei d'amrinan ushear begadim shelo ne'emar bahem shesh* — for garments that do not carry the word *sheish*, the baseline of six applies. Since the *me'il*'s body has no explicit thread count, it defaults to six, and then *kelil*, meaning braided double, brings it to twelve.

### Paroches = 24

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרֹכֶת עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה — אַרְבְּעָה דְּשִׁיתָּא שִׁיתָּא, לָא דִּינָא וְלָא דַּיָּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: Each thread of the **curtain** was made of **twenty-four** strands. The Gemara explains: With regard to each thread being composed of **four** colored threads: White, purple, scarlet, and sky-blue, and **each one** of them being composed **of six** strands, **there is neither judgment nor judge,** i.e., it is absolutely clear that this is how the threads of the curtain are produced.

The *paroches* at twenty-four threads requires almost no derivation at all. The *gemara* states simply: *arba deshisha shisha* — four types of material, each at six threads. *Lo dina velo dayana* — there is nothing worth arguing about. The groundwork has already been fully laid: each type is six threads, there are four distinct types, and four times six is twenty-four, straightforwardly and without complication.

### Choshen/Ephod = 28

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹשֶׁן וְאֵפוֹד עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב: "וְעָשִׂיתָ חֹשֶׁן מִשְׁפָּט מַעֲשֵׂה חוֹשֵׁב כְּמַעֲשֵׂה אֵפוֹד תַּעֲשֶׂנּוּ זָהָב תְּכֵלֶת וְאַרְגָּמָן וְתוֹלַעַת שָׁנִי וְשֵׁשׁ מׇשְׁזָר", אַרְבְּעָה דְּשִׁיתָּא שִׁיתָּא — עֶשְׂרִין וְאַרְבְּעָה, זָהָב — אַרְבְּעָה, הָא עֶשְׂרִין וּתְמָנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: Each thread of the **breastplate and ephod** was made of **twenty-eight** strands. **From where do we** derive this? **As it is written: "And you shall make a breastplate of judgment, the work of the skilled craftsman; like the work of the ephod you shall make it: Of gold, sky-blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine twined linen"** (Exodus 28:15). There are **four** colored threads, **each** composed **of six** strands, which makes **twenty-four.** In addition, **gold** is spun together with each of the **four** colors, giving the total of **twenty-eight.**

For the *choshen* and the *ephod*, the verse specifies: *zahav techeilet ve'argaman vetola'at shani vesheish mashzar* — gold, blue, purple, scarlet, and linen. That is five types. Five times six would yield thirty, not twenty-eight. The resolution: the four fabric threads — *techeilet*, *argaman*, *tola'at shani*, and linen — each carry six threads, totaling twenty-four. The gold contributes only four additional threads, yielding twenty-eight.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא זָהָב נָמֵי שִׁשָּׁה! אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, אָמַר קְרָא: "וְקִצֵּץ פְּתִילִים". "פְּתִיל", "פְּתִילִים" — הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** But couldn't one **say** the **gold** should be made as a thread of **six** strands, like the other colors? **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said** that **the verse states:** "And they beat the gold into thin plates, **and cut it into cords,** to work it into the sky-blue, and into the purple, and into the scarlet, and into the fine linen, the work of a skilled craftsman" (Exodus 39:3). **"Cord"** implies a strand that is doubled over and can be twisted into a cord; **"cords"** is in the plural, meaning at least two of these. **Accordingly, there are four** strands **here.**

Why four strands of gold? *Rav Acha bar Yaakov* derives this from the term *psilim*, which appears when the Torah describes the fabrication of the gold thread — hammering the gold flat and cutting it into strands. The word *psil* suggests two, as twisting requires two strands; the plural *psilim* doubles it, yielding four.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: "לַעֲשׂוֹת בְּתוֹךְ הַתְּכֵלֶת וּבְתוֹךְ הָאַרְגָּמָן". הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? נַעֲבֵיד אַרְבְּעָה דִּתְרֵי תְּרֵי — הָווּ לְהוּ תְּמָנְיָא. נַעֲבֵיד תְּרֵי דִּתְרֵי תְּרֵי, וּתְרֵי דְּחַד חַד — "וְעָשִׂיתָ", שֶׁיִּהְיוּ כׇּל עֲשִׂיּוֹתָיו שָׁווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** This can be seen from that fact that **the verse states** with regard to the gold strands: **"To work it into the sky-blue, and into the purple"** (Exodus 39:3), indicating that the gold strands should be combined with the other colors. **What** should **we do?** If **we make four** gold threads **of two** strands **each** and combine each one with each of the colors, then **there would be eight.** If **we make two** gold threads **of two** strands **each, and two** gold threads **of one** strand **each,** it says: **"And you shall make,"** indicating **that all its makings should be the same.** Perforce, one strand of gold should be combined with each of the colors, producing a total of twenty-eight strands.

*Rav Ashi* offers an alternative: the verse says *la'asos besoch hatecheilet* — to weave the gold into the *techeilet* — and similarly into the *argaman*, *tola'at shani*, and linen. This means a single gold thread is woven into each of the four fabric types. Each strand is then not six threads but seven — six of its own color plus one of gold — and four times seven equals twenty-eight.

The *gemara* closes this line of reasoning by rejecting the alternative of six gold threads: one cannot simply add six, since six divided equally among four types leaves a remainder, violating the requirement that all constructions be equal. One gold thread per type, distributed evenly among the four fabric strands, is the only arithmetically clean solution.

## Prohibitions

### Tearing Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲבָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הַמְקָרֵעַ בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה לוֹקֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לֹא יִקָּרֵעַ". מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב, וְדִילְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: נַעֲבֵד לֵיהּ שָׂפָה כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִיקְּרַע! מִי כְּתִיב "שֶׁלֹּא יִקָּרֵעַ"?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses various *halakhot* concerning the priestly vestments and other sacred vessels: **Raḥava said** that **Rav Yehuda said: One who** intentionally **tears** any of the **priestly vestments** transgresses a prohibition and **is flogged, as it is stated** concerning the robe: "It shall have a hem of woven work around the opening of it, like the opening of a coat of mail, and **it shall not be torn"** (Exodus 28:32). Just as it is prohibited to tear the opening of the robe, so too, it is prohibited to tear any of the priestly vestments. **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov strongly objects to this: But perhaps this is what the Merciful One is saying** in the Torah: **An opening should be made in order that it not tear.** In other words, the Torah was giving an explanation, not a prohibition. The Gemara rejects this: **Is it written: In order that it not be torn?** Clearly, the intention of the verse is to state a prohibition.

*Rachava* said in the name of *Rav Yehuda*: one who tears *bigdei kehunah* — the priestly garments — intentionally receives lashes, as the verse states *lo yikarei*, it shall not be torn. This verse appears specifically by the *me'il*, but the ruling is generalized to all priestly garments.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* challenges this reading: perhaps the Torah is not issuing a prohibition but rather an instruction for construction. Perhaps the meaning is — make a hem around the collar, *kedei delo nikarei*, so that it should not accidentally tear. The verse would then be describing how to make it durable, not commanding a person.

The *gemara* dismisses the challenge on textual grounds: the verse reads *lo yikarei*, it shall not tear, not *shelo yikarei*, so that it should not tear. The phrasing is read as a categorical prohibition addressed to a person, not as a design specification. The objection retains some force as a matter of interpretive intuition, but the *gemara* holds firm.

### Removing Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמַּזִּיחַ חוֹשֶׁן מֵעַל הָאֵפוֹד, וְהַמֵּסִיר בַּדֵּי אָרוֹן — לוֹקֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לֹא יִזַּח" וְ"לָא יָסוּרוּ". מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: וְדִילְמָא כִּי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, חַדְּקִינְהוּ וְעַבְדִינְהוּ שַׁפִּיר כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִזַּח וְלֹא יָסוּרוּ! מִי כְּתִיב "שֶׁלֹּא יִזַּח" וְ"שֶׁלֹּא יָסוּרוּ"?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: One who detaches the breastplate from upon the ephod** or **one who removes the staves of the Ark** from their rings transgresses a Torah prohibition and **is flogged, as it is stated:** "And the breastplate **shall not be detached** from the ephod" (Exodus 28:28),and it is also stated: "The staves shall be in the rings of the Ark; **they shall not be removed** from it" (Exodus 25:15). **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov strongly objects to this:** But **perhaps when the Merciful One said** this in the Torah, the intention was **to strengthen them and make them fast so that** the breastplate **not become detached** from the ephod **and** the staves **not be removed.** He suggests that the Torah was giving an explanation, not a prohibition. The Gemara rejects this: **Is it written: In order that it not become detached, and: In order that they not be removed?** Clearly, the intention of the verse is to state a prohibition.

*Elazar* similarly rules: one who removes the *choshen* from the *ephod* while the *Kohen Gadol* is actively engaged in the *avodah*, and one who removes the staves of the *aron* from their rings, each receives lashes. The sources are: *ve-lo yizach ha-choshen me-al ha-ephod* — the breastplate shall not be moved from the *ephod* — and *lo yasuru mimenu* — they shall not be removed from it — referring to the staves of the *aron*.

*Rav Acha bar Yaakov* raises a parallel objection: perhaps the Torah is instructing how the items should be secured — *chadkinuhu*, tighten the breastplate to the *ephod*; do it well, so that it will not slip off — rather than prohibiting any deliberate removal. Again, the *gemara* answers with the same textual observation: the verses do not read *shelo yizach* or *shelo yasuru*, but *lo yizach* and *lo yasuru*, the language of prohibition.

### Ark Staves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: "בְּטַבְּעוֹת הָאָרוֹן יִהְיוּ הַבַּדִּים לֹא יָסוּרוּ מִמֶּנּוּ", וּכְתִיב: "וְהוּבָא אֶת בַּדָּיו בַּטַּבָּעוֹת"! הָא כֵּיצַד? מִתְפָּרְקִין וְאֵין נִשְׁמָטִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, raised a contradiction.** In one verse **it is written: "The staves shall be in the rings of the Ark; they shall not be removed from it"** (Exodus 25:15), implying the staves should remain there permanently. But in another verse **it is written: "And its staves shall be put into the rings"** (Exodus 27:7), implying that when the Ark is used the staves are inserted, which suggests that they do not remain there permanently. **How is this** contradiction resolved? The staves **could be removed** from their position, i.e., they were loosened, but **did not come out.** The staves were wider at their ends and thinner in the middle. Therefore, once they had been forced into the rings, although they could be shifted, they would not be removed entirely.

*Rabbi Yosi be-Rabbi Chanina* identifies an apparent contradiction within the Torah's own treatment of the staves. One verse states *be-tabba'ot ha-aron yihyu ha-badim lo yasuru mimenu* — the staves shall be in the rings of the *aron* and shall not be removed from it. A second verse states *ve-huva et badav ba-tabba'ot* — the staves shall be brought into the rings — implying that there are times they are placed in and times they are not. If the staves may never be removed, what does the second verse describe?

The resolution: *mishtalfin ve-ein nishmatin* — the staves can be shifted, loosened, and adjusted within the rings, but they can never be fully extracted. *Rashi* explains that the staves were fitted with bulbous heads, allowing them to move back and forth within the rings without ever falling free. The word *ve-huva* in the second verse therefore does not describe an initial installation but rather an adjustment — repositioning the staves within the rings where they permanently reside.

A natural question arises: if the staves were loose in the rings, how would they function when the *aron* was being carried? One answer floats the possibility that the rings were fashioned around the staves after insertion, making removal impossible. Another possibility is that gold, being malleable, might have allowed the staves to be squeezed through initially. The *gemara*'s answer — that they shift but do not slip out — leaves these logistical questions open.

## Ark & Service

### Acacia Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: "עֲצֵי שִׁטִּים עוֹמְדִים", שֶׁעוֹמְדִים דֶּרֶךְ גְּדִילָתָן. דָּבָר אַחֵר: "עוֹמְדִים", שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין אֶת צִפּוּיָין. דָּבָר אַחֵר: "עוֹמְדִים". שֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר אָבַד סִבְרָן וּבָטֵל סִכּוּיָין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "עוֹמְדִים", שֶׁעוֹמְדִין לְעוֹלָם וּלְעוֹלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites other statements concerning the ark. **Rabbi Ḥama, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written:** "And you shall make the boards for the Tabernacle of **acacia wood, standing"** (Exodus 26:15)? This verse teaches **that** the boards of wood used for the Tabernacle should **stand in the** same **direction in which they grew** from the ground as a tree. **Alternatively, "standing"** means **that they supported their** gold **plating** and prevented it from falling. **Alternatively, "standing"** is written to hint at the following: **Perhaps you will say** that now that the Tabernacle is no longer in use, **their hope is lost and their chance is abandoned,** and after being stored away the boards will no longer return to use. Therefore, **the verse states "standing"** to indicate **that they stand forever and ever.**

*Rabbi Chama be-Rabbi Chanina* asks: what is the meaning of *atzei shitim omdim* — standing acacia wood — as the instruction for building the *Mishkan*? The first explanation offered is *she'omdim derech gedilatan* — the wood is to be oriented in the direction of its natural growth. You do not lay the wood horizontally when it grew vertically; you do not invert the grain. *Rashi* adds a further refinement: even the base of each plank should correspond to the root end of the original tree, and the top of the plank to the crown — maintaining the wood's natural orientation in all respects.

A related question: acacia in the wilderness grows as a relatively short bush, yet the planks of the *Mishkan* were ten *amot* — approximately fifteen feet — tall. How was such lumber available? The *gemara* recalls that the wood was brought out of Egypt. The wilderness does support acacia growth, but not at that height. Egyptian acacia, however — a distinct variety — apparently can achieve that stature, which resolves the difficulty.

### Standing Meanings

*Davar acher omdim* — a second interpretation of *omdim*, they stand. This refers to the gold plating that covers the acacia planks: *shema'amidim et tzipuyan* — the planks sustain and hold up their plating. The plating does not peel or corrode. *Rashi* adds the dimension that the wood itself does not rot despite being encased in gold — a remarkable quality, since one would expect the organic material inside to decay over time, regardless of the exterior plating. This is presented as a miraculous property of the *Mishkan*'s construction.

A third meaning of *omdim*: *shema tomar avad sikuyan* — lest you say that all hope for these sacred objects has been lost — *talmud lomar omdim*, the word *standing* teaches that the promise of the *Mishkan* and its vessels endures forever, *she'omdim le-olam u-le-olamim*. Even in the absence of the *Mishkan*, and later the *Beis Hamikdash*, the hope of their restoration remains, and the acacia planks stand symbolically — they have not perished.

### Garments Serad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא בַּר חֲנִינָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: "אֶת בִּגְדֵי הַשְּׂרָד לְשָׁרֵת בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ" — אִלְמָלֵא בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה, לֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר מִשּׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שָׂרִיד וּפָלִיט.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of the priestly vestments: **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "The plaited** [***serad***] **garments, for serving in the Sanctuary"** (Exodus 35:19)? Why does the verse refer to the priestly vestments as "*serad* garments"?
> 
> He offers a homiletic interpretation: **Were it not for the priestly vestments,** which provide atonement for the Jewish people, **there would not remain a remnant** [***sarid***] **or survivor from the haters of the Jewish people,** a euphemism used to refer to the Jewish people themselves. Due to the atonement provided by the priestly vestments, a remnant [*sarid*] of the Jewish people does survive.

*Rabbi Chama bar Chanina* further asks: what is the meaning of *et bigdei ha-serad lesharet ba-kodesh* — the garments of *serad* to serve in the holy? The word *serad* evokes *sarid*, a remnant.

One explanation given: *ilmalei bigdei kehunah lo nishtayer mi-sonaihem shel Yisrael sarid u-falit* — were it not for the *bigdei kehunah*, nothing of the Jewish people would have remained. *Rashi* explains that it was through the priestly garments that the *korbanos* were offered, and those *korbanos* provided atonement; the atonement is what sustained the nation. This is a striking theological claim, situated here in the midst of an extended technical discussion of garment construction — a reminder that all this precision in weaving thread counts and fabric types served the ultimate purpose of the people's spiritual survival.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: דְּבֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן תָּנָא: בְּגָדִים שֶׁגּוֹרְדִין אוֹתָן כִּבְרִיָּיתָן מִכְּלֵיהֶן, וּמְשָׂרְדִין מֵהֶן כְּלוּם. מַאי הִיא? רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה מַחַט.
>
> **Translation:** Another interpretation: **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **the school** of **Rabbi Shimon taught:** The priestly vestments are referred to as "*serad* garments" because they are **garments that are woven in their completed form upon the loom,** as opposed to weaving the material and then cutting and sewing pieces of the material together to create the required form, **and** then just **a small part of them remains** [***masridin***] which is not completed upon the loom. **What is** the remnant, the part that was not woven? **Reish Lakish said: This is the needle-work** required to complete the garment.

*Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon* offers a different reading of *serad*: these garments were so perfectly woven that when removed from the loom, they needed almost no finishing work — you would lift them off the frame and the garment would be complete, requiring no subsequent needlework. *U-masardin me-hem klum* — only the smallest amount was left over to stitch. *Resh Lakish* identifies this remnant as *ma'aseh machat*, a small finishing stitch.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה אֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָן מַעֲשֵׂה מַחַט, אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂה אוֹרֵג, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "מַעֲשֵׂה אוֹרֵג"! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְבֵית יָד שֶׁלָּהֶם. כִּדְתַנְיָא: בֵּית יָד שֶׁל בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה נֶאֱרֶגֶת בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ, וְנִדְבֶּקֶת עִם הַבֶּגֶד, וּמַגַּעַת עַד פִּיסַּת הַיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection to this** from a *baraita*: **Priestly vestments** should **not be made through needle-work but** though **woven work, as it is stated: "Woven work"** (Exodus 28:32). The Gemara answers that **Abaye said:** Reish Lakish's statement **is necessary only for,** i.e., refers only to, **the sleeves. As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **A sleeve** made for the **priestly vestments is woven separately and** then **attached to the garment** by sewing, **and** the sleeve is made to **reach as far as the palm of the hand.** However, the main body of the garment must indeed be made exclusively though weaving.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: a *baraisa* states explicitly that *bigdei kehunah ein osim osam maaseh machat ela maaseh oreg* — the priestly garments contained no needlework whatsoever, only weaving. *Abaye* resolves this: the sole exception was the sleeves. The cuffs — *veis yad* — were woven separately and then attached to the garment body, reaching to the palm of the hand. That minimal joining stitch is the *serad*, the tiny remnant requiring needlework. Everything else was completed on the loom.

### Survival

The *gemara* then transitions to a discussion of the *aron*, moving between the priestly garments and the vessels of the *Mishkan* in a way that reflects a structural parallel — the *kohen* himself functions as a kind of walking *Mishkan*, carrying the sanctity of the sacred objects within him.

The following passage will be seen as part of a broader thematic current running through the entire section: even while the *gemara* engages in highly technical discussions of thread counts, plating methods, and the dimensions of sacred objects, it consistently introduces a transitional voice gesturing toward the post-*Mikdash* reality. The focus on the *viddui* of the *Kohen Gadol*, the blessings he recited after the *avodah*, and now the assertion that *bigdei kehunah* sustained the people through the atoning power of the *korbanos* — all of these represent the *gemara* building a bridge from the ritual world of the Temple to the spiritual world that must persist without it.

## Ark Boxes & Crowns

### Nested Boxes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲבָה אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שָׁלֹשׁ אֲרוֹנוֹת עָשָׂה בְּצַלְאֵל, אֶמְצָעִי שֶׁל עֵץ תִּשְׁעָה, פְּנִימִי שֶׁל זָהָב שְׁמוֹנָה, חִיצוֹן עֲשָׂרָה וּמַשֶּׁהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites statements concerning other Temple vessels: **Raḥava said** that **Rav Yehuda said:** The Torah states that the Ark should be made of wood with gold plating inside and out (Exodus 25:10–11). In order to achieve this **Bezalel made three arks: A middle one** made **of wood,** whose height was **nine** handbreadths; **an inner one** made **of gold,** whose height was **eight** handbreadths; and an **outer one** of gold, whose height was **ten** handbreadths **and a bit.** These arks were nested.

*Rachava* in the name of *Rav Yehuda* teaches: *Betzalel* made three *aronos* — not three separate *aronos* in the sense of different arks, but three nested boxes that together composed the single *aron*. Rather than taking a wooden box and laminating it with gold directly, *Betzalel* constructed three discrete containers — one of gold for the interior, one of wood in the middle, and one of gold for the exterior — which fit inside one another like nesting vessels.

The dimensions are as follows. The Torah specifies the *aron* as one and a half *amot* in height, equal to nine *tefachim*. The wooden middle box was therefore nine *tefachim* on the outside. The inner gold box was eight *tefachim*, because the thickness of the wooden base consumes one *tefach* from the interior. The outer gold box was ten *tefachim* and a fraction — ten because the outer box extends one *tefach* above the wood to provide a rim on which the *kaporet* rests, and the additional fraction accounts for the thickness of the base of the outer gold box itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אַחַד עָשָׂר וּמַשֶּׁהוּ! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ בְּעׇבְיוֹ טֶפַח, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין בְּעׇבְיוֹ טֶפַח. וּמַאי מַשֶּׁהוּ — זֵיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the outer ark was **eleven** handbreadths **and a bit?** The Gemara explains: **This is not difficult: This** statement in the *baraita* **is in accordance with the one who said** that **the thickness** of the gold plating **was one handbreadth.** According to this opinion, the outer ark's base took up one handbreadth of its height, ten handbreadths were then needed to contain the middle ark within it, and then a bit more was needed so it could also contain the Ark's cover. **That** statement of Rav Yehuda **is in accordance with the one who said** that **the thickness** of the gold plating **was not one handbreadth** but was a plate of gold of negligible thickness. According to this opinion, the outer ark needed to be only ten handbreadths and a bit and could still contain the outer ark and have room for the cover. **And what is** this additional **bit?** It is the ornamental **crown** on the edge of the outer ark.

A *baraisa* records an alternative figure of eleven and a fraction for the outer box. The *gemara* resolves the discrepancy: *ha keman de'amar yesh bo ovi tefach* — the eleven-and-fraction version holds that the base of the outer gold box is itself a full *tefach* thick, which is a great deal of gold; *ha keman de'amar ein bo ovi tefach* — the ten-and-fraction version assumes the outer gold box has only a minimal base. The *mashu*, the fraction beyond ten or eleven, is identified as the *zer*, the small crown-like rim that protrudes above the *kaporet*.

### Three Crowns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, שְׁלֹשָׁה זֵירִים הֵן: שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ, וְשֶׁל אָרוֹן, וְשֶׁל שֻׁלְחָן. שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ — זָכָה אַהֲרֹן וּנְטָלוֹ. שֶׁל שֻׁלְחָן — זָכָה דָּוִד וּנְטָלוֹ. שֶׁל אָרוֹן — עֲדַיִין מוּנָּח הוּא, כָּל הָרוֹצֶה לִיקַּח — יָבֹא וְיִקַּח. שְׁמָּא תֹּאמַר פָּחוּת הוּא, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "בִּי מְלָכִים יִמְלוֹכוּ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: There** were **three crowns** on the sacred vessels in the Temple: The crown **of the altar, and of the Ark, and of the table.** The regal appearance they provided symbolized power and authority: The crown **of the altar** symbolized the crown of priesthood; **Aaron was deserving and took it,** and the priesthood continues exclusively through his descendants. The crown **of the table** symbolized the abundance and blessing associated with the crown of kingship; **David was deserving and took it** for himself and his descendants after him. The crown **of the Ark** symbolized the crown of Torah; **it is still sitting** and waiting to be acquired, **and anyone who wishes to take** it may **come and take** it and be crowned with the crown of Torah. **Perhaps you will say it is inferior** to the other two crowns and that is why nobody has taken it; therefore, **the verse states** about the wisdom of Torah: **"Through me kings will reign"** (Proverbs 8:15), indicating that the strength of the other crowns is derived from the crown of Torah, which is greater than them all.

*Rabbi Yochanan* teaches: *shelosha zeirim hem* — there are three crowns: the crown on the *mizbeach* (altar), the crown on the *aron*, and the crown on the *shulchan* (table). This immediately calls to mind the famous *mishnah* in *Pirkei Avot*. The crown of the *mizbeach* — representing the sacrificial service — corresponds to the crown of *kehunah*; it was taken by *Aharon* and his descendants. The crown of the *shulchan* — evoking prosperity and dominion — corresponds to the crown of *malchut*; it was taken by the house of *David*. The crown of the *aron* — representing Torah — *adayin munach*, still rests there, *kol ha-rotzeh likach yavo ve-yitol*, and anyone who wishes to take it may come and take it.

### Torah Crown

One might suppose that the *keter Torah*'s accessibility to all implies it is the lesser of the three crowns. The *gemara* counters with the verse from *Mishlei*: *bi melachim yimlochu* — through me, kings reign. The Torah speaks in the first person: it is through Torah that all other crowns are sustained. The crown of Torah is therefore not lesser but greater — the source from which *kehunah* and *malchut* derive their legitimacy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רָמֵי. כְּתִיב: "זָר", וְקָרֵינַן "זֵיר". זָכָה — נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ זֵיר, לֹא זָכָה — זָרָה הֵימֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara presents a number of statements based on the idea that the Ark symbolizes the Torah: **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised a contradiction:** According to the way the word crown **is written** in the Torah (Exodus 25:11), without vowels, it should be pronounced ***zar*,** meaning strange, **but** according to the traditional vocalization **we read** it as ***zeir*,** meaning crown. These two ways of understanding the word appear to contradict each other. Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: The two understandings apply to two different situations: If one **is deserving** by performing mitzvot, **it becomes a crown [*zeir*] for him;** but if one **is not deserving,** the Torah **will be a stranger [*zara*] to him** and he will forget his studies.

*Rav Yochanan* further notes a textual curiosity: the word for crown is written *zer* but vocalized as *zeir*. If one merits, *zacha na'asis lo zeir* — it becomes a crown for him. If one does not merit, *zara heimenu* — it becomes foreign to him. The same written form carries two meanings depending on whether the learner is worthy.

It is worth pausing on the cumulative structure of this passage. Throughout the extended technical material on garment threads and the *aron*'s construction, a persistent counterpoint has emerged: even in the midst of all that ritual precision, the *gemara* returns repeatedly to the Torah as the great equalizer — the one sacred object not inherited by lineage, not restricted by tribe or dynasty, but available to whoever will come and take it.

## Torah & Sincerity

### Inner/Outer Gold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "מִבַּיִת וּמִחוּץ תְּצַפֶּנּוּ". אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁאֵין תּוֹכוֹ כְּבָרוֹ — אֵינוֹ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states concerning the Ark: **"From within and from without you shall cover it"** (Exodus 25:11). **Rava said:** This alludes to the idea that **any Torah scholar whose inside is not like his outside,** i.e., whose outward expression of righteousness is insincere, **is not** to be considered **a Torah scholar.**

*Rava* draws a moral teaching from the *aron*'s construction: *kol talmid chacham she'ein tocho k'varo eino talmid chacham* — any Torah scholar whose inside is not like his outside is not truly a Torah scholar. The ark was gold within and gold without; a scholar must be the same. The gold plating is not merely decorative — it expresses that the inner reality must match the outer presentation.

The connection is made explicit by the verse *mibayis umichutz titzapenu* — you shall plate it inside and outside. The physical requirement of the *aron* becomes the ethical standard of the *talmid chacham*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא: נִקְרָא נִתְעָב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אַף כִּי נִתְעָב וְנֶאֱלָח אִישׁ שׁוֹתֶה כַמַּיִם עַוְלָה".
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said, and some say** it was **Rabba bar Ulla** who said: Not only is such a person not to be considered a Torah scholar, but he is **called loathsome, as it is stated: "What then of one loathsome and foul, man who drinks iniquity like water"** (Job 15:16). Although he drinks the Torah like water, since he sins, his Torah is considered iniquitous and this makes him loathsome and foul.

*Abaye*, citing *Rabba bar Ulla*, intensifies the point: such a scholar is not merely less than a *talmid chacham*, he is actively despised — *nikra nisa'av*, as the verse says, *af ki nisa'av ve'ne'elach ish shoseh chamayim avlah* — even more despised and loathsome is a person who drinks iniquity like water. The image is one who consumes Torah — as water is drunk — yet does so in corruption, without inner truth.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: "לָמָּה זֶּה מְחִיר בְּיַד כְּסִיל לִקְנוֹת חׇכְמָה וְלֶב אָיִן" — אוֹי לָהֶם לְשׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה וְאֵין בָּהֶן יִרְאַת שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Why is there a price in the hand of a fool to buy wisdom, as he has no heart?"** (Proverbs 17:16)? This expresses the following sentiment: **Woe to them, haters of Torah scholars,** a euphemism for the Torah scholars themselves, **who immerse themselves in Torah and have no fear of Heaven.** They are fools; they try to acquire the wisdom of Torah, but since they have no fear of Heaven in their hearts they lack the ability to do so.

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon* adds: *lamah zeh mechir b'yad k'sil liknos chochmah v'lev ayin* — what is this purchase in the hand of a fool who seeks to acquire wisdom and has no heart? *Oy lahem l'sonayehem shel talmidei chachamim* — woe to those enemies of scholars, a euphemism meaning woe to those scholars themselves. The charge is *she'oskim baTorah v'ein bahem yiras shamayim* — they engage in Torah and yet have no *yiras shamayim*. The intellect pursues *chochmah* but *v'lev ayin*, the heart is not present.

### Torah Potion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: "וְזֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אֲשֶׁר שָׂם מֹשֶׁה", זָכָה — נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ סַם חַיִּים, לֹא זָכָה — נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ סַם מִיתָה. וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רָבָא: דְּאוֹמֵן לַהּ — סַמָּא דְחַיָּיא, דְּלָא אוֹמֵן לַהּ — סַמָּא דְמוֹתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And this is the Torah which Moses put** [***sam***] before the children of Israel" (Deuteronomy 4:44)? The word *sam* is written with the letter *sin* and means put; it is phonetically similar to the word *sam* written with the letter *samekh*, meaning a drug. This use of this word therefore alludes to the following: If one **is deserving,** the Torah **becomes a potion [*sam*] of life for him.** If one **is not deserving,** the Torah **becomes a potion of death for him. And this** idea **is what Rava said: For one who is skillful** in his study of Torah and immerses himself in it with love, it is **a potion of life;** but **for one who is not skillful** in his studies, **it is a potion of death.**

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* teaches from the verse *vezos haTorah asher sam Moshe* — and this is the Torah that Moshe *placed*. The word *sam* — placed — also means a potion or medicine. *Zacha na'asis lo sam chayim*, if one merits, the Torah becomes a potion of life. *Lo zacha na'asis lo sam misa*, if one does not merit — whether through insincerity, exploitation of Torah's prestige, or study untethered from genuine religious purpose — it becomes a poison.

*Rava* restates this in his own formulation: *d'uman lah sam d'chayei* — one who is expert in it, who engages with it correctly, finds life. *D'lo uman lah suma d'mota* — one who does not engage with it rightly finds death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי, רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן רָמֵי, כְּתִיב: "פִּקּוּדֵי ה׳ יְשָׁרִים מְשַׂמְּחֵי לֵב", וּכְתִיב: "אִמְרַת ה׳ צְרוּפָה". זָכָה — מְשַׂמַּחְתּוֹ, לֹא זָכָה — צוֹרַפְתּוֹ. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר, מִגּוּפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא נָפְקָא: זָכָה — צוֹרַפְתּוֹ לְחַיִּים, לֹא זָכָה — צוֹרַפְתּוֹ לְמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan raised a contradiction: It was written: "The precepts of the Lord are upright, gladdening the heart"** (Psalms 19:9), but it is also written: **"The word of the Lord is refining"** (Psalms 18:31), which implies that the study of Torah can be a distressing process by which a person is refined like metal smelted in a smith's fire. He reconciles these verses as follows: For one who **is deserving,** the Torah **gladdens him;** for one who **is not deserving,** it **refines him. Reish Lakish said:** This lesson **emerges from that** second **verse itself:** For one who **is deserving,** the Torah **refines him for life;** for one who **is not deserving, it refines him for death.**

*Rabbi Yonatan* shows a similar contradiction between two verses. One reads *pikudei Hashem yesharim m'samchei lev* — God's statutes are straight and gladden the heart. The other reads *imrat Hashem tzerufa* — the word of God is like a smelting fire, a force that purifies but can also destroy. The resolution parallels what came before: *zacha m'samchato*, if one merits, the Torah gladdens; *lo zacha tzarfato*, if not, it scorches.

*Resh Lakish* observes that the same verse can carry both meanings simultaneously: *zacha tzarfato l'chayim* — if one merits, the refining fire purifies and brings life; *lo zacha tzarfato l'misa* — if not, the same fire consumes.

### Fear & Purity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "יִרְאַת ה׳ טְהוֹרָה עוֹמֶדֶת לָעַד". אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: זֶה הַלּוֹמֵד תּוֹרָה בְּטָהֳרָה. מַאי הִיא? נוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ לוֹמֵד תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"Fear of the Lord is pure, it stands forever"** (Psalms 19:10). **Rabbi Ḥanina said: This** is referring to **one who studies Torah in purity;** for such a person the Torah will remain with him forever. **What is this;** what does it mean to study in purity? **One** first **marries a woman and afterward studies Torah.** Since he is married, his heart will not be occupied with thoughts of sin, which could lead him to become impure.

*Rabbi Chanina* applies the verse *yirat Hashem tehora omedet la'ad* — the fear of God is pure and endures forever — to *Talmud Torah*: *zeh lomeid Torah b'tahara*, this describes one who studies Torah in a state of purity. *Mai hi?* What does purity mean in this context? *Nosei isha v'achar kach lomeid Torah* — one who marries first and then studies Torah, so that the preoccupation of the body is settled and the mind can engage the learning without distraction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: חֲבָל עַל דְּלֵית לֵיהּ דָּרְתָּא, וְתַרְעָא לְדָרְתֵּיהּ עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai declared** that the situation may be expressed by the following sentiment: **Pity** him **who has no courtyard but** senselessly **makes a gate for his courtyard.** Fear of Heaven is like the courtyard, and the study of Torah is the gate that provides entrance to the courtyard. The study of Torah is purposeful only if it leads to fear of Heaven.

*Rabbi Yannai* makes a proclamation of great force: *chaval al d'leis lei darta v'tara l'darta avid* — woe to the person who has no yard but constructs for himself a gate to the yard. Torah is the gateway — but a gateway to what? To *avodas Hashem*, to *yiras Hashem*, to genuine connection with God. If one focuses entirely on the gateway and builds no yard behind it — if the learning produces no *yirah*, no authentic relationship with the divine — the gate leads nowhere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא לְרַבָּנַן: בְּמָטוּתָא מִינַּיְיכוּ, לָא תִּירְתוּן תַּרְתֵּי גֵּיהִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to the Sages** in the study hall: **I beg of you, do not inherit Gehenna twice.** By studying Torah without the accompanying fear of Heaven, not only are you undeserving of the World-to-Come, but even in this world you experience Gehenna, as you spend all your time in study and fail to benefit from worldly pleasure.

*Rava* turns to the assembled scholars with a direct and urgent appeal: *b'matusa minaichu, lo tirsu tarti Gehennom* — please, do not inherit two *gehennoms*. The scholar who dedicates his life to Torah study and denies himself ordinary comforts and pleasures has already accepted hardship in this world. If that sacrifice is not accompanied by sincerity and *yiras shamayim*, it will yield no reward in the next world either. The least he can do is make the sacrifice worth something.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: "וְזֹאת הַתּוֹרָה אֲשֶׁר שָׂם מֹשֶׁה", זָכָה — נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ סַם חַיִּים, לֹא זָכָה — נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ סַם מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** [This passage was previously cited and translated above.]

*Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* offers a final thought on the verse *edut Hashem ne'emana* — the testimonies of God are trustworthy: *ne'emana hi l'haid b'lomdeha* — the Torah is trusted to testify about those who study it. One can look at those who have truly absorbed their learning and see it — and one can look at those who have not, and see that too. The Torah bears faithful witness to its students.

The discussion closes with a final phrase — *ma'aseh rokeim ma'aseh choshev* — referring to the distinction between types of woven work in the *Mishkan*, which connects back to the construction of the garments and the *aron* discussed throughout the *daf*.
