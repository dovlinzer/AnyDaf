# Eruvin 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sleeping & Eruv

### Mishna: Falling Asleep

Today's daf is 46, and we pick up from the bottom of 45b. The *mishna* addresses the case of somebody who falls asleep: if a person is not conscious, can he establish that location as the place of his *eruv*? According to *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*, he can; according to the *Chachamim*, he cannot. Interestingly, if he cannot, where is the place of his *eruv*? The *mishna* gives him the same status as someone who has gone outside the *techum* — he is not defined as being in any particular place and has only his four *amos*.

Some *Rishonim* ask why this is different from the case discussed later in the *Gemara* of rain that falls on *Shabbos* or a boat that lands on *Shabbos* when it was higher than ten *tefachim* — in those cases the object can at least acquire two thousand *amos* from where it lands. Here, however, the *Chachamim* are not so lenient: they reason that in those other cases the object was never yet situated within the zone, whereas here the person was in the zone but simply was not conscious. The distinction is not entirely clear, but the consequence is plain: without an established place, the sleeping person is left with only four *amos*.

### R. Yochanan vs. Chachamim

The *Gemara* then raises the possibility that this debate runs even deeper — that it is not merely about a person who is unconscious but may extend to inanimate objects as well. The question becomes: does an inanimate object that normally follows its owner — but which is currently ownerless — establish its place for *Shabbos* as two thousand *amos* from where it began *Shabbos*, or is it defined by the person who subsequently finds it? The sleeping person serves as an analogy: does he establish his place for two thousand *amos*, or does he lack any place for *Shabbos* and remain limited to four *amos*?

### Hefker Objects

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* turns to examine both the case of ownerless objects and the case of the unconscious person in tandem, setting the stage for the extended discussion that follows.

## Rain & Techumin

### Rain on Yom Tov

The *Gemara* continues: *ki ata lekamei deRav Yosef* — when Abaye came before Rav Yosef, he reported over this entire discussion about the *braisa* regarding rain. The foundational principle being explored is that *chafzei hefker* — ownerless objects — are *koneh shvisa* where they are at the onset of *Shabbos*, and this is treated as parallel to the position of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*.

The *Gemara* now reads: *amar mar* — if rain fell on *Yom Tov*, then clearly its place was not established when *Yom Tov* began (since it was still in the sky and not yet on the ground), and therefore *harei hen ke-ragli kol adam* — it is defined by the person who takes possession of it.

### Ocean & Water Cycle

The *Gemara* then asks: *v'amai* — why is this so? *Likni lei shvisa be-Okianos* — why doesn't the rainwater acquire its *shvisa* in the ocean? The *Gemara* is working with the understanding that there is one great ocean surrounding the world and that, based on the water cycle, all rainwater ultimately originates there. If that is so, when *Yom Tov* began the water was in the ocean — why not say that is where it establishes its two thousand *amos*, such that by the time it has evaporated, condensed, and fallen as rain, it is already far outside its *techum* and would be forbidden to use?

The *Gemara* raises the possibility: *lema d'lo ke-Rebbi Eliezer* — perhaps this contradicts Rabbi Eliezer, since *ha-amar kol olam kulo mi-mei Okianos hu shoteh* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that the entire world drinks from the waters of the ocean. The *Gemara* assumes this process occurs within twenty-four hours, so rain falling on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov* would have originated in the ocean on *erev Shabbos*. That we permit the use of such rain may indicate that this ruling does not follow Rabbi Eliezer.

Rav Yitzchak responds: *hacha b'avim shenitkashru m'erev Yom Tov askinan* — we are dealing here with a case where the clouds had already darkened before *Yom Tov* began. The water was already up in the clouds at the onset of *Yom Tov*; it was not on the ground and was not *koneh shevitah* in the ocean.

### Clouds & Movement

The *Gemara* immediately challenges: *v'dilma hanei azlei v'hanei achrinei ninhu* — perhaps those original dark clouds blew away, and the rain that actually fell on *Yom Tov* came from different clouds, formed from water that had evaporated during *Yom Tov* itself? The *Gemara* responds: *d'it lehu simana b'gavaihu* — the clouds bear an identifying mark, so we can establish that it is the same clouds.

A second, more principled answer then follows: *v'iba'it eima safek d'rabbanan l'kula*. We simply do not know for certain whether the water came from those pre-existing clouds or from newly evaporated water. But since the entire institution of *techumin* is rabbinic in nature, a *safek* in a rabbinic matter is resolved leniently.

The *Gemara* then poses a further question: even granting that the water was in the clouds when *Yom Tov* began, *v'likni shevitah b'avim* — why doesn't it establish its *shevitah* in the clouds? If so, *tifshot minah d'ein techumin l'ma'ala m'asara* — we could derive from here that there are no *techumin* above ten *tefachim*, since otherwise the cloud-water would already have established its zone in the sky. The *Gemara* declines to accept this inference: *le'olam eima lach yesh techumin* — in truth, *techumin* do apply even above ground level. Rather, *u'maya b'avei mivla blei'i* — the water is absorbed within the cloud. It does not yet exist as a discrete entity; it is merely vapor and therefore has no *techum* of its own.

The *Gemara* then raises a further objection: *kol sheken d'havu lehu nolad* — if the water does not yet truly exist, it should certainly be a problem of *nolad* and forbidden on *Yom Tov* entirely. The *Gemara* responds: *ela maya b'avei mida nayedi* — it is not that the water is completely non-existent; it is present in the cloud but is not stationary. Since it is constantly in motion as water vapor, it cannot be *koneh shevitah*. The decisive factor is non-stationarity, not non-existence.

It is worth noting how striking this treatment of *nolad* is. We are generally quite stringent in our definition of *nolad* — some opinions hold that changing water to ice or ice to water constitutes *nolad*, even though the substance itself has not changed, only its state. Yet rain and snow are not considered *muktzeh* or *nolad*. This inconsistency would seem to argue for a more relaxed definition of *nolad* across the board, though the *Gemara* does not address this tension directly.

### Rivers & Flowing Water

The *Gemara* then draws the full conclusion: *hashta d'ateit l'hachi, v'd'Okyanus nami lo tikshei lach* — now that we have established the principle that non-stationary objects cannot be *koneh shevitah*, there is no difficulty even from the starting position of the ocean. *Miha b'Okyanus nami maya nayedi* — the water in the ocean is also in motion. This is now a fully general principle: something that is moving when *Shabbos* begins does not establish a fixed place.

This is confirmed by a *braita*: *neharot hamoshchin u'mayyanot hanovim, harei hen k'raglei kol adam* — rivers that run and springs that flow do not have a fixed place for *Shabbos* and are therefore defined by the person who draws from them, not by where the water happened to be at the onset of *Shabbos*. Notably, in the case of a boat that has not yet docked and is still moving — bracketing the separate issue of ten *tefachim* — it too would not be *koneh shevitah* by reason of motion. Once the boat stops, its place would then be defined by where it docked.

## Lenient in Eruvin

### Psak: R. Yochanan

The *Gemara* now shifts to the question of *psak*. *Amar Rav Yakov bar Idi amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — we rule like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* that a person who falls asleep establishes *Shabbos* where he is, with two thousand *amos* in every direction, and is not treated like someone deposited outside the *techum* with only four *amos*.

### Explicit vs. Inferred

*Rabbi Zeira* then asked Rav Yakov bar Idi: *befeirush shemia lach* — did you hear this ruling explicitly from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, *o m'chlala shemia lach* — or did you infer it from a more general statement he made? Rav Yakov bar Idi replied: *befeirush shemia li* — I heard it explicitly.

Why did Rabbi Zeira think it might have been inferred? Because Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi had also stated a general principle: *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv* — we rule like the lenient position in *eruv*. One might have thought that Rav Yakov bar Idi applied that general principle to the case of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* on his own, since *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s ruling is indeed the lenient one: he permits two thousand *amos* rather than restricting the sleeper to four. But Rav Yakov bar Idi clarified that no inference was necessary — the explicit ruling was transmitted directly.

### General Leniency Rule

The *Gemara* then asks: *v'taryeihu lama li* — why were both statements necessary? Why say both the general principle (*halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv*) and the specific ruling (*halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*)? *Amar Rabbi Zeira tzrichi* — both were necessary.

### Person vs. Object

Had Rav Yakov bar Idi stated only *halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*, we might have thought that this applies not only where *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s position is lenient but even where it is strict. When would that be? The *Gemara* earlier suggested that *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s ruling may be grounded in the principle that inanimate objects are *koneh shevitah* where they are when *Shabbos* begins. Applied to ownerless objects, however, that principle is actually a *chumra* — it restricts the object to its own two thousand *amos* rather than allowing the finder to use it within his own *techum*. Had we simply ruled like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* across the board, we might have extended his ruling to inanimate objects as well, to their detriment.

*Ka mashma lan halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv* — the additional statement that we always rule leniently in *eruv* tells us that we go leniently in both directions: for the sleeping person, we follow *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* (two thousand *amos* rather than four), and for the ownerless object, we rule that it is not *koneh shevitah* on its own but follows the finder.

Both *Rashi* and *Tosafos* raise the obvious objection: earlier in this *masechet* we established that one cannot adopt two contradictory *kulot* — one must be consistent. How can we be lenient in both directions, saying the object establishes its *shevitah* when a person is involved but does not when an object is involved? Both *Rashi* and *Tosafos* resolve this by reverting to an answer proposed earlier in the *Gemara*: *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s ruling regarding a sleeping person is not based on the analogy to ownerless objects. Rather, his reasoning is that since a conscious person would have been *koneh shevitah* where he stood, the sleeping person — who is still a person — retains that status. An inanimate object, by contrast, is a categorically different entity, and even *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* may concede that it is not *koneh shevitah*. There is therefore no contradiction: the person establishes his place even while asleep, while the ownerless object follows its finder.

### Minority Opinion

*Ela v'leima halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv* — conversely, had Rav Yakov bar Idi stated only the general principle of leniency in *eruv*, we might have assumed that this principle applies only where it is one opinion against one opinion (*yachid bimkom yachid*). But *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* stands alone against the *Chachamim*, who represent multiple opinions — *yachid bimkom rabbim*. Under normal rules of *psak* one follows the majority. Therefore, had only the general principle been stated, we might have said it does not apply to a case as lopsided as *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* against the majority. Hence the explicit ruling was necessary: even in this asymmetric fight, we rule like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*.

This has broad implications for the entirety of *hilchos eruvin*: across virtually all of its discussions, we adopt the lenient position — and, as the *Gemara* will shortly establish, this holds even when the lenient position is a single opinion against many.

## General Psak Rules

### Rava's Challenge

*Amar lei Rava l'Abaye* — Rava raised a challenge. He did not find this a surprising *chiddush* at all. Since *eruvin* is rabbinic in origin, what difference should it make whether it is *yachid bimkom yachid* or *yachid bimkom rabbim*? In matters of rabbinic law one should always follow the lenient position, even against a majority. Rav Pappa responded that this assumption is simply incorrect.

### Niddah Example

*U'vidrabonon lo shani lan bein yachid bimkom yachid l'yachid bimkom rabim?* — Do you really think that in all rabbinic matters it makes no difference whether it is one against one or one against many? *V'ha tnan* — consider the following *mishna* in *niddah*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that any woman — not only an elderly woman — who goes three periods (ninety days) without bleeding attains the status of *mesuleket damim*, one whose blood is effectively removed. This is a lenient position, for such a woman need not be concerned that she bled prior to the moment she actually observed blood. This is a *yachid bimkom rabbim* — Rabbi Eliezer stands alone against the majority of *Chachamim*.

A *braita* then records: *maaseh v'asah Rabbi k'Rabbi Eliezer* — there was an actual case in which *Rabbi* (Yehudah HaNasi) ruled like Rabbi Eliezer. A woman who was not elderly had gone three months without bleeding and had handled *trumah*, and *Rabbi* permitted it. *Rashi* explains that this was a period of famine — which also provides a physiological rationale, since malnutrition is itself a cause of amenorrhea. *Tosafos* alternatively suggests that a very large quantity of *trumah* was involved, making the potential loss significant.

### Sha'as HaDchak

The *braita* continues: *l'achar shenizkar, amar, kedai hu Rabbi Eliezer lismoch alav b'sha'as hadachak* — after he remembered something, *Rabbi* said: Rabbi Eliezer is worthy of being relied upon in a time of exigency. This is a critically important halachic principle: one may rely on a *da'as yachid* that has not been definitively rejected *b'sha'as hadachak*.

What did *Rabbi* remember? The *Gemara* considers whether he remembered that the *halacha* had been explicitly ruled against Rabbi Eliezer — but if so, *b'sha'as hadachak heichi avid k'vasei*? How could he have relied on a position that had been formally rejected? Rather, *d'lo itmar hilchesa lo k'Rabbi Eliezer v'lo k'Rabbanan* — no explicit ruling had been issued either way. What *Rabbi* remembered was that Rabbi Eliezer is nonetheless a *da'as yachid* against the majority, and the ordinary rules of *psak* should therefore have directed him toward the *Rabbanan*. The ruling in favor of *trumah* was not reversible since it was already done, but *Rabbi* acknowledged that under normal circumstances he should have followed the majority. Nevertheless, *kedai Rabbi Eliezer lismoch alav b'sha'as hadachak* — in a time of genuine need one may rely on him, since his position has never been formally and definitively silenced.

This principle carries enormous practical weight in later halachic literature. The question of when a *da'as yachid* may be relied upon *b'sha'as hadachak* involves several layers of analysis: how authoritative must the minority position have been to begin with? And, critically — at what point has a position been so thoroughly displaced by subsequent *psak* that it can no longer function as a legitimate *da'as yachid* at all? This question bears directly on the role of the *Shulchan Aruch*: when it first appeared it was one opinion among many, becoming authoritative only gradually. Can one rely on a view in the *Rishonim* that the *Shulchan Aruch* did not adopt? What of authorities the *Shulchan Aruch*'s author was unaware of entirely, such as the *Meiri*? These are all live questions whose answers depend on exactly the same conceptual framework the *Gemara* is developing here.

Rav Kook engages this entire framework at length in his introduction to *Shabbos Aretz*, laying the groundwork for *heter mechirah* by examining the parameters of relying on a *da'as yachid b'sha'as hadachak* in *shemitah*. Those who oppose *heter mechirah* argue that Rav Kook himself framed the leniency as contingent on *sha'as hadachak* — raising the question of whether, if that emergency no longer obtains, the reliance on those minority positions lapses entirely.

Determining what constitutes *sha'as hadachak*, and when a minority opinion has been effectively silenced, are ultimately judgment calls belonging to the *posek*. They are informed by precedent and responsa literature, but the evidence is often contradictory, and the art of weighing it is precisely what distinguishes one *posek* from another.

### Aveilus Leniency

The *Gemara* now draws the broader conclusion from this discussion. *Kol makom she-ata motzei yachid meikel v'rabbim machmirim, halacha k'divrei hamachmirim* — the general rule is that whenever a single opinion is lenient and the majority is stringent, we follow the majority. This applies even in rabbinic matters. However, there are two famous exceptions: *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv* and *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'avel* — in both *eruvin* and *aveilus* we always rule like the lenient position.

The *aveilus* case emerges from the ruling of *Shmuel: d'amar Shmuel halacha k'divrei hameikel b'avel*. The specific case concerns *shemua kerova* — hearing of a relative's death within thirty days — versus *shemua rechoka* — hearing more than thirty days after the death. Rabbi Akiva holds that a *shemua rechoka* requires only one day of *aveilus*, while the *Chachamim* require *shiva* and *sheloshim* even for old news. *Rabbah bar bar Chana* rules in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* that we follow the majority (*Chachamim*) in general, but makes an explicit exception: *halacha k'divrei Rabbi Akiva* — here we rule like the lenient minority, in accordance with *Shmuel*'s principle of *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'avel*.

Thus the *Gemara* on today's daf establishes a clear and important framework: the default rule, even in rabbinic matters, is to follow the majority over the minority. The two exceptions — *eruvin* and *aveilus* — are categorical departures from this default, in which we always follow the lenient position regardless of the numerical weight of the opposing views.

## Eruv Chatzerot vs. Techu…

### Chatzerot vs. Techumin

Rav Pappa now adds a further reason why both statements — the general principle of leniency in *eruv* and the specific ruling like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — were necessary. *Salka daitach amina hani mili be-eiruv chatzeiros* — one might have thought the rule of leniency in *eruv* applies only to *eruv chatzerot*, but *aval be-eiruvi techumin, eima lo* — perhaps it would not apply to *eruvi techumin*. Both statements were therefore needed to establish that the leniency covers *eruvi techumin* as well.

*U-mena teimra de-shani lan bein eiruv chatzeiros ve-eiruv techumin?* — What would have led one to think there is a meaningful distinction between the two? One might naturally suggest that *eruvi techumin* has a *de-oraisa* dimension, making it stricter — but that is not the *Gemara*'s reasoning.

### Alleyway Courtyards

Instead, the *Gemara* cites a *mishnah* that demonstrates a structural difference. *Bameh devarim amurim be-eiruvi techumin* — by *eruvi techumin* one may not establish an *eruv* on behalf of another person without their knowledge, since doing so benefits the person in one direction while restricting them in another. It is a trade-off, not a pure benefit, and therefore requires explicit consent. *Aval be-eiruvi chatzeiros me-arvin bein le-daas bein she-lo le-daas* — by *eruv chatzerot*, by contrast, one may establish the *eruv* on behalf of another even without their knowledge, because *zakin le-adam she-lo be-fanav* — one may act to another's benefit in their absence.

*Tosafos* notes that this reasoning is somewhat strained: there is in fact a minor loss involved in *eruv chatzerot* as well, since one takes a portion of the other person's bread for the *eruv* without their explicit consent. Nevertheless, *Tosafos* reads the *Gemara* as ruling that in *eruv chatzerot* the benefit predominates, while in *eruvi techumin* the trade-off is genuine and therefore requires consent. This structural asymmetry is what might have led one to treat the two types of *eruv* differently for purposes of the leniency principle — hence the necessity of specifying that the principle applies to both.

## Beginning vs. Remnant

### Techillas vs. Shiyurei

Rav Ashi offers yet another reason why both statements were necessary. *Hani mili be-shiyurei eiruv* — one might have thought the leniency applies only to the *remnant* of an existing *eruv*, where the question is whether what remains is sufficient to preserve the *eruv*'s validity. *Aval bi-techillas eiruv, eima lo* — but at the *outset*, when one is first establishing the *eruv*, perhaps we would not apply the leniency.

*U-mena teimra de-shani lan bein shiyurei eiruv le-vein techillas eiruv?* — What grounds would there be for such a distinction? From a *mishnah* that records Rabbi Yose's ruling: *bameh devarim amurim bi-techillas eiruv* — the minimum food requirement applies at the outset of placing the *eruv*. *Aval be-shiyurei eiruv, afilu kol she-hu* — once an *eruv* was valid and the food subsequently diminished, even a minimal remnant suffices to maintain it. One is demonstrably more lenient once the *eruv* was established than at its inception — and one might therefore have thought that the general principle of leniency applies only in the *shiyurei* context.

### Food Minimum

The explicit ruling like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — which concerns the initial establishment of one's place — clarifies that the leniency applies *b'techillas eruv* as well.

The *mishnah* appends: *v'lo amru l'arev chatzeros ella k'dei shelo lishkach toras eruv min hatinokos* — the Rabbis required *eruv chatzeros* in order that children (and by extension the community) not forget the laws of *eruv*. This remark addresses a specific configuration: three courtyards, each feeding into the next, with all of them opening into a shared alleyway. If the courtyards all made a joint *eruv* with one another through their interconnecting doors, they would technically not need to make a separate *shituf mavos* in the alleyway. Nevertheless, the Rabbis required it in order to prevent a misleading inference. If people saw the residents of those courtyards carrying freely in the alleyway without any *shituf*, they might conclude that a *shituf mavo'os* is never required. The concern is not about teaching children incorrectly but about the community inferring a wrong principle from observed practice. Demanding a *shituf mavo'os* even where it is not technically necessary avoids the more consequential error of assuming it is never needed.

## Tannaitic Hierarchy

### Standard Authorities

Having established the general principles of *eruvin* leniency, the *Gemara* now turns to a broader discussion of *klalei psak* — the general principles governing how *halacha* is decided when *Tannaim* disagree. *Rabbi Yaakov v'Rabbi Zrika amru*: we rule like *Rabbi Akiva* when he disputes a single colleague (*mechavero*), but not when he disputes multiple colleagues. We rule like *Rabbi Yose* even when he disputes multiple colleagues (*mechaverav*). And we rule like *Rabbi* (Yehudah HaNasi) when he disputes a single colleague, but not when he disputes multiple colleagues.

### Three Levels

The *Gemara* then asks *l'mai hilchesa* — when we say we "rule like" one of these authorities, what precisely do we mean? Three *Amoraim* offer different formulations. *Rabbi Yochanan amar halacha* — we rule definitively. *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar matim* — we incline in that direction. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina amar nir'im* — it appears that way, or seems to us to be correct.

*Tosafos* explains the distinctions in a way that illuminates the underlying structure. To say *halacha* means the opposing view is entirely illegitimate — if someone ruled in accordance with the rejected position, the ruling would have to be reversed. *Matim* means we incline toward the favored position, but the other view remains a valid *da'as yachid*; a ruling based on it would stand. *Nir'im* is weaker still — the preferred position appears more correct, and one should ideally rule accordingly, but it is fully legitimate to rule *l'chatchila* like the other view as well. These three levels thus represent a hierarchy of exclusivity: from absolute displacement of the opposing view, to its demotion to secondary status, to its retention as a fully equal alternative.

### Tannaitic Ranking

*Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi* then transmits in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* a hierarchy of *Tannaitic* authority relevant to specific pairings. When *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* dispute one another, *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — we follow *Rabbi Yehuda*. When *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Yose* dispute one another, *halacha k'Rabbi Yose* — we follow *Rabbi Yose*.

### Transitive Logic

*Pshita* — this would seem to make obvious by transitive logic that when *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yose* dispute, we also follow *Rabbi Yose*, without needing an explicit statement. The *Gemara* confirms: *ain tzorich lomar Rabbi Meir v'Rabbi Yose halacha Rabbi Yose* — since *Rabbi Meir* cannot prevail against *Rabbi Yehuda*, and *Rabbi Yehuda* cannot prevail against *Rabbi Yose*, it is self-evident that *Rabbi Yose* prevails over *Rabbi Meir*. *Hashta b'makom Rabbi Yehuda leisa, b'makom Rabbi Yose mibaya* — if *Rabbi Meir*'s position is set aside in the presence of *Rabbi Yehuda*, is there any question whether it falls before *Rabbi Yose*?

Rav Assi adds that the same transitive reasoning extends to *Rabbi Shimon*. *D'amar Rav Abba amar Rav Yochanan*: when *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* dispute, *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*. Since *Rabbi Yehuda* beats *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yose* beats *Rabbi Yehuda*, it follows by transitivity that *Rabbi Yose* beats *Rabbi Shimon* as well. Both *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* emerge as the weakest positions in this ranking. One is then left to ask: when *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* dispute each other directly, what is the *psak*? The *Gemara* leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved.

## Challenge to Rules

### Mesharshiya Dissent

The *Gemara* now challenges the universality of these *klalei psak*. *Amar Rav Mesharshiya: leisnehu l'hanei klalei* — we do not hold of these rules. Where does Rav Mesharshiya get this?

### Three Courtyards Case

From the following *mishnah*. The *mishnah* records *Rabbi Shimon*'s ruling about three courtyards opening into one another and also opening to the public domain. If the outer courtyards each made an *eruv* with the middle courtyard, then the middle courtyard may carry with either outer courtyard, and vice versa — but the two outer courtyards are forbidden to one another. *Rav Chama bar Guriya* then states in the name of Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — we rule like *Rabbi Shimon*.

But who is the opposing position? The *Gemara* identifies it as *Rabbi Yehuda*, even though no explicit dispute appears in this *mishnah* (Rashi clarifies how the *Gemara* knows this). The difficulty is immediate: we just established that when *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* dispute, *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — yet here we rule like *Rabbi Shimon*. *V'ha amart Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabbi Shimon halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — does this not contradict the *klal*?

### Conditional Rules

The *Gemara*'s resolution is fundamental: *dilma heicha d'itmar itmar, heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar* — perhaps the answer is that these rules were never intended to be universal. When a specific tradition exists about how to rule in a particular case, that tradition governs — regardless of what the general *klal* would suggest. The *klalei psak* are not absolute rules; they are default principles to be invoked precisely when we lack a specific tradition or explicit determination. *Heicha d'lo itmar* — where no such specific ruling exists, these principles provide guidance. But they cannot override a direct, transmitted *psak* in a given case. The *klalim* fill the gaps; they do not supersede specific knowledge.
