# Eruvin 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sleeping & Eruv

### Mishna: Falling Asleep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בָּעֵי רָבָא: מַאי קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי? מִסְבָּר קָא סָבַר: חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה. וּבְדִין הוּא דְּלִיפְלוֹג בְּכֵלִים. וְהָא דְּקָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּאָדָם — לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כּוֹחָן דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: הוֹאִיל וְנֵיעוֹר קָנָה, יָשֵׁן נָמֵי קָנָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rava raised a dilemma: What does Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri hold?** Does **he hold** that **ownerless objects acquire residence** for Shabbat, i.e., even an article that does not belong to anyone acquires residence at the onset of Shabbat and can therefore be carried two thousand cubits in each direction? **And** according to this understanding, Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri **should by right have disagreed** with the Rabbis even **about utensils** that were left in the field, i.e., that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, ownerless utensils can be moved two thousand cubits in each direction. **And the reason that they disagreed about a person** is **to convey the far-reaching** nature of the stringent ruling **of the Rabbis, that although there is** room **to say: Since** a person who is **awake acquires** for himself two thousand cubits, **he also acquires** them if he is **sleeping,** the mishna nonetheless **teaches us that** the Rabbis did **not** accept this argument, and this is why the dispute is taught specifically with respect to a person.

Today's daf is 46, and we pick up from the bottom of 45b. The *mishna* addresses the case of somebody who falls asleep: if a person is not conscious, can he establish that location as the place of his *eruv*? According to *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*, he can; according to the *Chachamim*, he cannot. Interestingly, if he cannot, where is the place of his *eruv*? The *mishna* gives him the same status as someone who has gone outside the *techum* — he is not defined as being in any particular place and has only his four *amos*.

Some *Rishonim* ask why this is different from the case discussed later in the *Gemara* of rain that falls on *Shabbos* or a boat that lands on *Shabbos* when it was higher than ten *tefachim* — in those cases the object can at least acquire two thousand *amos* from where it lands. Here, however, the *Chachamim* are not so lenient: they reason that in those other cases the object was never yet situated within the zone, whereas here the person was in the zone but simply was not conscious. The distinction is not entirely clear, but the consequence is plain: without an established place, the sleeping person is left with only four *amos*.

### R. Yochanan vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא, קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי בְּעָלְמָא: חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר אֵין קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה, וְהָכָא הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא: הוֹאִיל וְנֵיעוֹר קָנָה, יָשֵׁן נָמֵי קָנָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** we should understand his position differently, that **in general Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **ownerless objects do not acquire residence** of their own. **But here,** with regard to a person, **the reason is as follows: Since** a person who is **awake acquires** for himself two thousand cubits, **he also acquires** them if he is **sleeping.**

The *Gemara* then raises the possibility that this debate runs even deeper — that it is not merely about a person who is unconscious but may extend to inanimate objects as well. The question becomes: does an inanimate object that normally follows its owner — but which is currently ownerless — establish its place for *Shabbos* as two thousand *amos* from where it began *Shabbos*, or is it defined by the person who subsequently finds it? The sleeping person serves as an analogy: does he establish his place for two thousand *amos*, or does he lack any place for *Shabbos* and remain limited to four *amos*?

### Hefker Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: גְּשָׁמִים שֶׁיָּרְדוּ מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב — יֵשׁ לָהֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. בְּיוֹם טוֹב — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּרַגְלֵי כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** a solution to this dilemma from the following *baraita*: **Rain that fell on the eve of a Festival has two thousand cubits in each direction,** meaning that one is permitted to carry the rainwater within a radius of two thousand cubits. But if the rain fell **on the Festival** itself, **it is like the feet of all people,** as it did not acquire residence, and consequently one is permitted to carry this water wherever he is permitted to walk.

With this framework in place, the *Gemara* turns to examine both the case of ownerless objects and the case of the unconscious person in tandem, setting the stage for the extended discussion that follows.

## Rain & Techumin

### Rain on Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: "חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה", הָא מַנִּי — רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הִיא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: "חֶפְצֵי הֶפְקֵר אֵין קוֹנִין שְׁבִיתָה", הָא מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְלֹא רַבָּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** it is understandable **if you say that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **ownerless objects acquire residence;** in accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** *baraita*? **It is** that of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** ben Nuri, and consequently the rain that fell on the eve of the Festival acquired residence in the spot where it fell. **However, if you say** that he maintains that **ownerless objects do not acquire residence,** in accordance with **whose** opinion is **this** *baraita*? **Neither** that of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** ben Nuri, **nor** that of **the Rabbis,** as it clearly indicates that rain acquires a place of residence even though it has no owner. Rather, we must say that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri is of the opinion that ownerless objects acquire residence, and this *baraita* is in accordance with his opinion.

The *Gemara* continues: *ki ata lekamei deRav Yosef* — when Abaye came before Rav Yosef, he reported over this entire discussion about the *braisa* regarding rain. The foundational principle being explored is that *chafzei hefker* — ownerless objects — are *koneh shvisa* where they are at the onset of *Shabbos*, and this is treated as parallel to the position of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*.

The *Gemara* now reads: *amar mar* — if rain fell on *Yom Tov*, then clearly its place was not established when *Yom Tov* began (since it was still in the sky and not yet on the ground), and therefore *harei hen ke-ragli kol adam* — it is defined by the person who takes possession of it.

### Ocean & Water Cycle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? לִיקְנֵי שְׁבִיתָה בְּאוֹקְיָינוֹס. לֵימָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, דְּאִי כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר — הָא אָמַר: כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ מִמֵּי אוֹקְיָינוֹס הוּא שׁוֹתֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: And why should this be? The water **should have acquired residence in the ocean [*okeyanos*],** where it was when the Festival began. And since the water went out on the Festival beyond its limit after it evaporated and formed into clouds, moving the water more than four cubits should be prohibited. **Let us say** that this *baraita* was taught **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. Because if** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, he said: The entire world drinks from the waters of the ocean;** that is to say, evaporated ocean water is the source of rain.

The *Gemara* then asks: *v'amai* — why is this so? *Likni lei shvisa be-Okianos* — why doesn't the rainwater acquire its *shvisa* in the ocean? The *Gemara* is working with the understanding that there is one great ocean surrounding the world and that, based on the water cycle, all rainwater ultimately originates there. If that is so, when *Yom Tov* began the water was in the ocean — why not say that is where it establishes its two thousand *amos*, such that by the time it has evaporated, condensed, and fallen as rain, it is already far outside its *techum* and would be forbidden to use?

The *Gemara* raises the possibility: *lema d'lo ke-Rebbi Eliezer* — perhaps this contradicts Rabbi Eliezer, since *ha-amar kol olam kulo mi-mei Okianos hu shoteh* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that the entire world drinks from the waters of the ocean. The *Gemara* assumes this process occurs within twenty-four hours, so rain falling on *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov* would have originated in the ocean on *erev Shabbos*. That we permit the use of such rain may indicate that this ruling does not follow Rabbi Eliezer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּעָבִים שֶׁנִּתְקַשְּׁרוּ מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Here we are dealing with clouds that were** already **formed on the eve of the Festival.** Since these clouds were already formed before the Festival, the water did not acquire residence in the ocean or travel beyond its limit on the Festival.

Rav Yitzchak responds: *hacha b'avim shenitkashru m'erev Yom Tov askinan* — we are dealing here with a case where the clouds had already darkened before *Yom Tov* began. The water was already up in the clouds at the onset of *Yom Tov*; it was not on the ground and was not *koneh shevitah* in the ocean.

### Clouds & Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא הָנָךְ אָזְלִי, וְהָנָךְ אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ? דְּאִית לְהוּ סִימָנָא בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָוֵי סָפֵק דְּדִבְרֵיהֶם, וְסָפֵק דְּדִבְרֵיהֶם לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps those** clouds that had already been formed on the eve of the Festival **went** away, **and these** clouds, from which the rain fell, **are others** that did acquire residence in the ocean? The Gemara answers: We are dealing here with a case **where there is** an identifying **sign** that these are the same clouds and not others. **And if you wish, say** that there is another reason we are not concerned that these might be other clouds: **This** matter of whether or not they are the same clouds pertains to **an uncertainty** with respect to **a rabbinic law,** and the principle is that with regard to **an uncertainty** concerning **a rabbinic law,** one may follow the **lenient** understanding.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges: *v'dilma hanei azlei v'hanei achrinei ninhu* — perhaps those original dark clouds blew away, and the rain that actually fell on *Yom Tov* came from different clouds, formed from water that had evaporated during *Yom Tov* itself? The *Gemara* responds: *d'it lehu simana b'gavaihu* — the clouds bear an identifying mark, so we can establish that it is the same clouds.

A second, more principled answer then follows: *v'iba'it eima safek d'rabbanan l'kula*. We simply do not know for certain whether the water came from those pre-existing clouds or from newly evaporated water. But since the entire institution of *techumin* is rabbinic in nature, a *safek* in a rabbinic matter is resolved leniently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיקְנִי שְׁבִיתָה בְּעָבִים! תִּיפְשׁוֹט מִינַּהּ, דְּאֵין תְּחוּמִין לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה. דְּאִי יֵשׁ תְּחוּמִין לִיקְנֵי שְׁבִיתָה בְּעָבִים! לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: יֵשׁ תְּחוּמִין, וּמַיָּא בְּעֵיבָא מִיבְלָע בְּלִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let** the water **acquire residence in the clouds,** where it was when the Festival began, and its limit should be measured from there. Since the *baraita* taught that the water is like the feet of all people, if so, **resolve from here** another dilemma, and say that **there is no** prohibition of Shabbat **limits above ten** handbreadths, and one is permitted to travel more than two thousand cubits above this height. **For if there is** a prohibition of Shabbat **limits** above ten handbreadths, **let** the water **acquire residence in the clouds.** The Gemara rejects this argument: **Actually,** I can **say to you: There is** a prohibition of Shabbat **limits** even above ten handbreadths, **and the water** does not acquire residence in the clouds because it **is absorbed in the clouds.** Since water does not exist in its usual state within the clouds, but rather takes on a different form, it does not acquire residence there.

The *Gemara* then poses a further question: even granting that the water was in the clouds when *Yom Tov* began, *v'likni shevitah b'avim* — why doesn't it establish its *shevitah* in the clouds? If so, *tifshot minah d'ein techumin l'ma'ala m'asara* — we could derive from here that there are no *techumin* above ten *tefachim*, since otherwise the cloud-water would already have established its zone in the sky. The *Gemara* declines to accept this inference: *le'olam eima lach yesh techumin* — in truth, *techumin* do apply even above ground level. Rather, *u'maya b'avei mivla blei'i* — the water is absorbed within the cloud. It does not yet exist as a discrete entity; it is merely vapor and therefore has no *techum* of its own.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּהָווּ לְהוּ נוֹלָד, דַּאֲסִירִי! אֶלָּא: מַיָּא בְּעָבִים מֵינָד נָיְידִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: If the water was previously not in its current state, **all the more so should it be** considered as something that **came into being [*nolad*]** on the Festival, and consequently it is **prohibited** to carry it. Something that came into being or assumed its present form on Shabbat or Festivals is considered set-aside [*muktze*] and may not be handled on Shabbat or Festivals. **Rather,** we should say: **The water in clouds** is **in** constant **motion** and therefore does not acquire residence there.

The *Gemara* then raises a further objection: *kol sheken d'havu lehu nolad* — if the water does not yet truly exist, it should certainly be a problem of *nolad* and forbidden on *Yom Tov* entirely. The *Gemara* responds: *ela maya b'avei mida nayedi* — it is not that the water is completely non-existent; it is present in the cloud but is not stationary. Since it is constantly in motion as water vapor, it cannot be *koneh shevitah*. The decisive factor is non-stationarity, not non-existence.

It is worth noting how striking this treatment of *nolad* is. We are generally quite stringent in our definition of *nolad* — some opinions hold that changing water to ice or ice to water constitutes *nolad*, even though the substance itself has not changed, only its state. Yet rain and snow are not considered *muktzeh* or *nolad*. This inconsistency would seem to argue for a more relaxed definition of *nolad* across the board, though the *Gemara* does not address this tension directly.

### Rivers & Flowing Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, אוֹקְיָינוֹס נָמֵי לָא לִיקְשׁוֹ לָךְ, מַיָּא בְּאוֹקְיָינוֹס נָמֵי מֵינָד נָיְידִי, וְתַנְיָא: נְהָרוֹת הַמּוֹשְׁכִין וּמַעְיָינוֹת הַנּוֹבְעִין הֲרֵי הֵן כְּרַגְלֵי כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Now that you have arrived at this** answer, **the ocean should also not be difficult for you,** as the **water in the ocean is also in** constant **motion. And it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Flowing rivers and streaming springs are like the feet of all people,** as their waters do not acquire residence in any particular place. The same law also applies to clouds and seas.

The *Gemara* then draws the full conclusion: *hashta d'ateit l'hachi, v'd'Okyanus nami lo tikshei lach* — now that we have established the principle that non-stationary objects cannot be *koneh shevitah*, there is no difficulty even from the starting position of the ocean. *Miha b'Okyanus nami maya nayedi* — the water in the ocean is also in motion. This is now a fully general principle: something that is moving when *Shabbos* begins does not establish a fixed place.

This is confirmed by a *braita*: *neharot hamoshchin u'mayyanot hanovim, harei hen k'raglei kol adam* — rivers that run and springs that flow do not have a fixed place for *Shabbos* and are therefore defined by the person who draws from them, not by where the water happened to be at the onset of *Shabbos*. Notably, in the case of a boat that has not yet docked and is still moving — bracketing the separate issue of ten *tefachim* — it too would not be *koneh shevitah* by reason of motion. Once the boat stops, its place would then be defined by where it docked.

## Lenient in Eruvin

### Psak: R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** that one who was asleep at the beginning of Shabbat may travel two thousand cubits in every direction.

The *Gemara* now shifts to the question of *psak*. *Amar Rav Yakov bar Idi amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — we rule like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* that a person who falls asleep establishes *Shabbos* where he is, with two thousand *amos* in every direction, and is not treated like someone deposited outside the *techum* with only four *amos*.

### Explicit vs. Inferred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידִי: בְּפֵירוּשׁ שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ אוֹ מִכְּלָלָא שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּפֵירוּשׁ שְׁמִיעַ לִי. מַאי כְּלָלָא? דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמֵּיקֵל בְּעֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi:** Did **you hear** this *halakha* **explicitly** from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, **or did you understand it by inference** from some other ruling that he issued? Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi **said to him: I heard** it **explicitly** from him. The Gemara asks: From **what** other teaching could this ruling **be inferred?** The Gemara explains: From that **which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient** opinion **with regard to an *eiruv*.**

*Rabbi Zeira* then asked Rav Yakov bar Idi: *befeirush shemia lach* — did you hear this ruling explicitly from Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, *o m'chlala shemia lach* — or did you infer it from a more general statement he made? Rav Yakov bar Idi replied: *befeirush shemia li* — I heard it explicitly.

Why did Rabbi Zeira think it might have been inferred? Because Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi had also stated a general principle: *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv* — we rule like the lenient position in *eruv*. One might have thought that Rav Yakov bar Idi applied that general principle to the case of *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* on his own, since *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s ruling is indeed the lenient one: he permits two thousand *amos* rather than restricting the sleeper to four. But Rav Yakov bar Idi clarified that no inference was necessary — the explicit ruling was transmitted directly.

### General Leniency Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי?! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא בֵּין לְקוּלָּא וּבֵין לְחוּמְרָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמֵּיקֵל בְּעֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I need both?** Why was it necessary for Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi to state both the general ruling that the *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient opinion with regard to an *eiruv*, and also the specific ruling that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri on this issue? **Rabbi Zeira said:** Both rulings **were necessary, as had he informed us** only that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, I would have said** that the *halakha* is in accordance with him **whether this is a leniency,** i.e., that a sleeping person acquires residence and may walk two thousand cubits in every direction, **or** whether **it is a stringency,** i.e., that ownerless utensils acquire residence and can be carried only two thousand cubits from that place. **Consequently, he teaches us** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient** opinion **with regard to an *eiruv*,** so that we rule in accordance with Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri only when it entails a leniency.

The *Gemara* then asks: *v'taryeihu lama li* — why were both statements necessary? Why say both the general principle (*halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv*) and the specific ruling (*halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*)? *Amar Rabbi Zeira tzrichi* — both were necessary.

### Person vs. Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא "הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמֵּיקֵל בְּעֵירוּב". הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לְמָה לִי? אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי יָחִיד בִּמְקוֹם יָחִיד וְרַבִּים בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּים, אֲבָל יָחִיד בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּים — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let him state** only that **the *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient** opinion **with regard to an *eiruv*. Why do I need** the statement that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri?** The Gemara answers: This ruling **was necessary** as well, for had he informed us only that the *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient opinion with regard to an *eiruv*, **it might have entered your mind to say** that **this statement applies only** to disputes in which **a single** authority disagrees **with** another **single** authority, **or several** authorities disagree with **several** other authorities. **But** when **a single** authority maintains a lenient opinion against **several** authorities who maintain a more stringent position, you might have **said** that we do **not** rule in his favor. Hence, it was necessary to state that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri although he disputes the Rabbis.

Had Rav Yakov bar Idi stated only *halacha k'Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*, we might have thought that this applies not only where *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s position is lenient but even where it is strict. When would that be? The *Gemara* earlier suggested that *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s ruling may be grounded in the principle that inanimate objects are *koneh shevitah* where they are when *Shabbos* begins. Applied to ownerless objects, however, that principle is actually a *chumra* — it restricts the object to its own two thousand *amos* rather than allowing the finder to use it within his own *techum*. Had we simply ruled like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* across the board, we might have extended his ruling to inanimate objects as well, to their detriment.

*Ka mashma lan halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv* — the additional statement that we always rule leniently in *eruv* tells us that we go leniently in both directions: for the sleeping person, we follow *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* (two thousand *amos* rather than four), and for the ownerless object, we rule that it is not *koneh shevitah* on its own but follows the finder.

Both *Rashi* and *Tosafos* raise the obvious objection: earlier in this *masechet* we established that one cannot adopt two contradictory *kulot* — one must be consistent. How can we be lenient in both directions, saying the object establishes its *shevitah* when a person is involved but does not when an object is involved? Both *Rashi* and *Tosafos* resolve this by reverting to an answer proposed earlier in the *Gemara*: *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri*'s ruling regarding a sleeping person is not based on the analogy to ownerless objects. Rather, his reasoning is that since a conscious person would have been *koneh shevitah* where he stood, the sleeping person — who is still a person — retains that status. An inanimate object, by contrast, is a categorically different entity, and even *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* may concede that it is not *koneh shevitah*. There is therefore no contradiction: the person establishes his place even while asleep, while the ownerless object follows its finder.

### Minority Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי: מִכְּדֵי עֵירוּבִין דְּרַבָּנַן, מָה לִי יָחִיד בִּמְקוֹם יָחִיד, וּמָה לִי יָחִיד בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּים! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: וּבִדְרַבָּנַן לָא שָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין יָחִיד בִּמְקוֹם יָחִיד לְיָחִיד בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּים!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Abaye: Now, since** the laws of ***eiruvin* are rabbinic** in origin, **what** reason is there for **me** to differentiate between a disagreement of **a single** authority **with a single** authority and a disagreement of **a single** authority **with several** authorities? **Rav Pappa said to Rava: Is there no difference with regard to rabbinic** laws **between** a disagreement of **a single** authority **with a single** authority, **and** a disagreement of **a single** authority with **several** authorities?

*Amar lei Rava l'Abaye* — Rava raised a challenge. He did not find this a surprising *chiddush* at all. Since *eruvin* is rabbinic in origin, what difference should it make whether it is *yachid bimkom yachid* or *yachid bimkom rabbim*? In matters of rabbinic law one should always follow the lenient position, even against a majority. Rav Pappa responded that this assumption is simply incorrect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁעָבְרוּ עָלֶיהָ שָׁלֹשׁ עוֹנוֹת — דַּיָּיהּ שְׁעָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Didn't we learn** in a mishna that **Rabbi Elazar says: Any woman who passed three** expected menstrual **cycles** without experiencing bleeding is presumed not to be menstruating. If afterward she sees blood, **it is enough** that she be regarded as ritually impure due to menstruation **from the time** that she examined herself and saw that she had a discharge, rather than retroactively for up to twenty-four hours. The Rabbis, however, maintain that this *halakha* applies only to an older woman or to a woman after childbirth, for whom it is natural to stop menstruating, but not to a normal young woman for whom three periods have passed without bleeding.

*U'vidrabonon lo shani lan bein yachid bimkom yachid l'yachid bimkom rabim?* — Do you really think that in all rabbinic matters it makes no difference whether it is one against one or one against many? *V'ha tnan* — consider the following *mishna* in *niddah*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that any woman — not only an elderly woman — who goes three periods (ninety days) without bleeding attains the status of *mesuleket damim*, one whose blood is effectively removed. This is a lenient position, for such a woman need not be concerned that she bled prior to the moment she actually observed blood. This is a *yachid bimkom rabbim* — Rabbi Eliezer stands alone against the majority of *Chachamim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה וְעָשָׂה רַבִּי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּזְכַּר, אָמַר: כְּדַי הוּא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לִסְמוֹךְ עָלָיו בִּשְׁעַת הַדְּחָק.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught** in a *baraita*: **It once happened that Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **ruled** that the *halakha* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar. After he remembered** that Rabbi Elazar's colleagues disagree with him on this matter and that he had apparently ruled incorrectly, **he** nonetheless **said: Rabbi Elazar is worthy to rely upon in exigent circumstances.**

A *braita* then records: *maaseh v'asah Rabbi k'Rabbi Eliezer* — there was an actual case in which *Rabbi* (Yehudah HaNasi) ruled like Rabbi Eliezer. A woman who was not elderly had gone three months without bleeding and had handled *trumah*, and *Rabbi* permitted it. *Rashi* explains that this was a period of famine — which also provides a physiological rationale, since malnutrition is itself a cause of amenorrhea. *Tosafos* alternatively suggests that a very large quantity of *trumah* was involved, making the potential loss significant.

### Sha'as HaDchak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּזְכַּר"? אִילֵּימָא: לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּזְכַּר דְּאֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֶלָּא כְּרַבָּנַן — בִּשְׁעַת הַדְּחָק הֵיכִי עָבֵיד כְּווֹתֵיהּ! אֶלָּא: דְּלָא אִיתְּמַר הִלְכְתָא לָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְלָא כְּרַבָּנַן. "לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּזְכַּר" דְּלָאו יָחִיד פְּלִיג עֲלֵיהּ אֶלָּא רַבִּים פְּלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ, אָמַר: כְּדַי הוּא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לִסְמוֹךְ עָלָיו בִּשְׁעַת הַדְּחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **What is** the meaning of: **After he remembered? If you say** that it means **after he remembered** that **the *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar but** rather **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** then **how could he rule in accordance with him** even **in exigent circumstances,** given that the *halakha* had been decided against him? **Rather,** it must be that the ***halakha*** had **not been stated** on this matter, **neither in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, nor in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis. And after he remembered that it was not a single** authority who **disagreed with** Rabbi Elazar, **but** rather **several** authorities who **disagreed with him, he** nonetheless **said: Rabbi Elazar is worthy to rely upon in exigent circumstances.** This demonstrates that even with a dispute that involves a rabbinic decree, such as whether a woman is declared ritually impure retroactively, there is room to distinguish between a disagreement of a single authority and a single authority, and a disagreement of a single authority and several authorities.

The *braita* continues: *l'achar shenizkar, amar, kedai hu Rabbi Eliezer lismoch alav b'sha'as hadachak* — after he remembered something, *Rabbi* said: Rabbi Eliezer is worthy of being relied upon in a time of exigency. This is a critically important halachic principle: one may rely on a *da'as yachid* that has not been definitively rejected *b'sha'as hadachak*.

What did *Rabbi* remember? The *Gemara* considers whether he remembered that the *halacha* had been explicitly ruled against Rabbi Eliezer — but if so, *b'sha'as hadachak heichi avid k'vasei*? How could he have relied on a position that had been formally rejected? Rather, *d'lo itmar hilchesa lo k'Rabbi Eliezer v'lo k'Rabbanan* — no explicit ruling had been issued either way. What *Rabbi* remembered was that Rabbi Eliezer is nonetheless a *da'as yachid* against the majority, and the ordinary rules of *psak* should therefore have directed him toward the *Rabbanan*. The ruling in favor of *trumah* was not reversible since it was already done, but *Rabbi* acknowledged that under normal circumstances he should have followed the majority. Nevertheless, *kedai Rabbi Eliezer lismoch alav b'sha'as hadachak* — in a time of genuine need one may rely on him, since his position has never been formally and definitively silenced.

This principle carries enormous practical weight in later halachic literature. The question of when a *da'as yachid* may be relied upon *b'sha'as hadachak* involves several layers of analysis: how authoritative must the minority position have been to begin with? And, critically — at what point has a position been so thoroughly displaced by subsequent *psak* that it can no longer function as a legitimate *da'as yachid* at all? This question bears directly on the role of the *Shulchan Aruch*: when it first appeared it was one opinion among many, becoming authoritative only gradually. Can one rely on a view in the *Rishonim* that the *Shulchan Aruch* did not adopt? What of authorities the *Shulchan Aruch*'s author was unaware of entirely, such as the *Meiri*? These are all live questions whose answers depend on exactly the same conceptual framework the *Gemara* is developing here.

Rav Kook engages this entire framework at length in his introduction to *Shabbos Aretz*, laying the groundwork for *heter mechirah* by examining the parameters of relying on a *da'as yachid b'sha'as hadachak* in *shemitah*. Those who oppose *heter mechirah* argue that Rav Kook himself framed the leniency as contingent on *sha'as hadachak* — raising the question of whether, if that emergency no longer obtains, the reliance on those minority positions lapses entirely.

Determining what constitutes *sha'as hadachak*, and when a minority opinion has been effectively silenced, are ultimately judgment calls belonging to the *posek*. They are informed by precedent and responsa literature, but the evidence is often contradictory, and the art of weighing it is precisely what distinguishes one *posek* from another.

### Aveilus Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא יָחִיד מֵיקֵל וְרַבִּים מֵחֲמִירִין, הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמַּחְמִירִין הַמְרוּבִּים, חוּץ מִזּוֹ, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מֵיקֵל וַחֲכָמִים מֵחֲמִירִין — הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וְסָבַר לַהּ כִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמֵּיקֵל בְּאֵבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** From here the Gemara infers: **Wherever you find** that **a single** authority **is lenient** with regard to a certain *halakha* **and several** other authorities **are stringent, the *halakha* is in accordance with the words of the stringent** authorities, who constitute **the majority, except for here, where despite** the fact that the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva is lenient and the** opinion of the **Rabbis is** more **stringent, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** **And** Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds like Shmuel, as Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient** opinion **with** regard to **mourning** practices, i.e., wherever there is a dispute with regard to mourning customs, the *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient opinion.

The *Gemara* now draws the broader conclusion from this discussion. *Kol makom she-ata motzei yachid meikel v'rabbim machmirim, halacha k'divrei hamachmirim* — the general rule is that whenever a single opinion is lenient and the majority is stringent, we follow the majority. This applies even in rabbinic matters. However, there are two famous exceptions: *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'eiruv* and *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'avel* — in both *eruvin* and *aveilus* we always rule like the lenient position.

The *aveilus* case emerges from the ruling of *Shmuel: d'amar Shmuel halacha k'divrei hameikel b'avel*. The specific case concerns *shemua kerova* — hearing of a relative's death within thirty days — versus *shemua rechoka* — hearing more than thirty days after the death. Rabbi Akiva holds that a *shemua rechoka* requires only one day of *aveilus*, while the *Chachamim* require *shiva* and *sheloshim* even for old news. *Rabbah bar bar Chana* rules in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* that we follow the majority (*Chachamim*) in general, but makes an explicit exception: *halacha k'divrei Rabbi Akiva* — here we rule like the lenient minority, in accordance with *Shmuel*'s principle of *halacha k'divrei hameikel b'avel*.

Thus the *Gemara* on today's daf establishes a clear and important framework: the default rule, even in rabbinic matters, is to follow the majority over the minority. The two exceptions — *eruvin* and *aveilus* — are categorical departures from this default, in which we always follow the lenient position regardless of the numerical weight of the opposing views.

## General Psak Rules

### Tannaitic Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב וְרַבִּי זְרִיקָא אָמְרוּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, וּכְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מֵחֲבֵרָיו, וּכְרַבִּי מֵחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara discussed the principles cited with regard to halakhic decision-making, it cites additional principles. **Rabbi Ya'akov and Rabbi Zerika said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** in disputes **with** any individual **Sage, and** the *halakha* **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** even in disputes **with** other **Sages, and the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi in disputes **with** any individual **Sage.**

Having established the general principles of *eruvin* leniency, the *Gemara* now turns to a broader discussion of *klalei psak* — the general principles governing how *halacha* is decided when *Tannaim* disagree. *Rabbi Yaakov v'Rabbi Zrika amru*: we rule like *Rabbi Akiva* when he disputes a single colleague (*mechavero*), but not when he disputes multiple colleagues. We rule like *Rabbi Yose* even when he disputes multiple colleagues (*mechaverav*). And we rule like *Rabbi* (Yehudah HaNasi) when he disputes a single colleague, but not when he disputes multiple colleagues.

### Three Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: הֲלָכָה. וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: מַטִּין. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: נִרְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard **to what *halakha*** do these principles apply, meaning, to what degree are they binding? **Rabbi Asi said:** This is considered binding ***halakha*. And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said: One is inclined** toward such a ruling in cases where an individual asks, but does not issue it as a public ruling in all cases. **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: It appears** that one should rule this way, but it is not an established *halakha* that is considered binding with regard to issuing rulings.

The *Gemara* then asks *l'mai hilchesa* — when we say we "rule like" one of these authorities, what precisely do we mean? Three *Amoraim* offer different formulations. *Rabbi Yochanan amar halacha* — we rule definitively. *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba amar matim* — we incline in that direction. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Chanina amar nir'em* — it appears that way, or seems to us to be correct.

*Tosafos* explains the distinctions in a way that illuminates the underlying structure. To say *halacha* means the opposing view is entirely illegitimate — if someone ruled in accordance with the rejected position, the ruling would have to be reversed. *Matim* means we incline toward the favored position, but the other view remains a valid *da'as yachid*; a ruling based on it would stand. *Nir'em* is weaker still — the preferred position appears more correct, and one should ideally rule accordingly, but it is fully legitimate to rule *l'chatchila* like the other view as well. These three levels thus represent a hierarchy of exclusivity: from absolute displacement of the opposing view, to its demotion to secondary status, to its retention as a fully equal alternative.

### Tannaitic Ranking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּלָּשׁוֹן הַזֶּה אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. הַשְׁתָּא בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לֵיתָא, בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Idi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In the case of a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda;** in the case of a dispute between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei; and, needless to say,** in the case of a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** As **now,** if **in disputes with Rabbi Yehuda,** the opinion of Rabbi Meir **is not** accepted as law, **need it** be stated that **in disputes with Rabbi Yosei,** Rabbi Meir's opinion is rejected? Rabbi Yehuda's opinion is not accepted in disputes with Rabbi Yosei.

*Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi* then transmits in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* a hierarchy of *Tannaitic* authority relevant to specific pairings. When *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* dispute one another, *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — we follow *Rabbi Yehuda*. When *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Yose* dispute one another, *halacha k'Rabbi Yose* — we follow *Rabbi Yose*.

### Transitive Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אַף אֲנִי לוֹמֵד: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. הַשְׁתָּא בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לֵיתָא, בִּמְקוֹם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִיבַּעְיָא? אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said: I also learn** based on the same principle that in a dispute between **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. As Rabbi Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In cases of dispute between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. Now,** if **where** it is opposed by **Rabbi Yehuda** the opinion of Rabbi Shimon **is not** accepted as law, **where** it is opposed by the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** with whom the *halakha* is in accordance against Rabbi Yehuda, **is it necessary** to say that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei? The Gemara **raises a dilemma:** In a dispute between **Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Shimon, what** is the *halakha*? No sources were found to resolve this dilemma, and it **stands** unresolved.

*Pshita* — this would seem to make obvious by transitive logic that when *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yose* dispute, we also follow *Rabbi Yose*, without needing an explicit statement. The *Gemara* confirms: *ain tzorich lomar Rabbi Meir v'Rabbi Yose halacha Rabbi Yose* — since *Rabbi Meir* cannot prevail against *Rabbi Yehuda*, and *Rabbi Yehuda* cannot prevail against *Rabbi Yose*, it is self-evident that *Rabbi Yose* prevails over *Rabbi Meir*. *Hashta b'makom Rabbi Yehuda leisa, b'makom Rabbi Yose mibaya* — if *Rabbi Meir*'s position is set aside in the presence of *Rabbi Yehuda*, is there any question whether it falls before *Rabbi Yose*?

Rav Assi adds that the same transitive reasoning extends to *Rabbi Shimon*. *D'amar Rav Abba amar Rav Yochanan*: when *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* dispute, *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda*. Since *Rabbi Yehuda* beats *Rabbi Shimon* and *Rabbi Yose* beats *Rabbi Yehuda*, it follows by transitivity that *Rabbi Yose* beats *Rabbi Shimon* as well. Both *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* emerge as the weakest positions in this ranking. One is then left to ask: when *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Shimon* dispute each other directly, what is the *psak*? The *Gemara* leaves this as *teiku* — unresolved.

## Challenge to Rules

### Mesharshiya Dissent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: לֵיתַנְהוּ לְהָנֵי כְּלָלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiya said: These principles** of halakhic decision-making **are not** to be relied upon.

The *Gemara* now challenges the universality of these *klalei psak*. *Amar Rav Mesharshiya: leisnehu l'hanei klalei* — we do not hold of these rules. Where does Rav Mesharshiya get this?

### Three Courtyards Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — לְשָׁלֹשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת הַפְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ וּפְתוּחוֹת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. עֵירְבוּ שְׁתַּיִם הַחִיצוֹנוֹת עִם הָאֶמְצָעִית — הִיא מוּתֶּרֶת עִמָּהֶן וְהֵן מוּתָּרוֹת עִמָּהּ, וּשְׁתַּיִם הַחִיצוֹנוֹת אֲסוּרוֹת זוֹ עִם זוֹ. וְאָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וּמַאן פְּלִיג עֲלֵיהּ — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לֵיתַנְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **If you say** that he derived it **from that which we learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Shimon said: To what is this comparable?** It is like **three courtyards that open into one another, and** also **open into a public domain.** If **the two outer** courtyards **established an *eiruv* with the middle one,** the residents of the middle one **are permitted to** carry to the two outer ones, **and they are permitted to** carry to **it, but the** residents of the **two outer** courtyards **are prohibited to** carry from **one** to the **other,** as they did not establish an *eiruv* with one another. **And Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said** that **Rav said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon; and who disagrees with** Rabbi Shimon on this matter? It is **Rabbi Yehuda. Didn't you say:** In disputes between **Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? Rather, can we not conclude from** this mishna that these principles should **not** be relied upon?

From the following *mishnah*. The *mishnah* records *Rabbi Shimon*'s ruling about three courtyards opening into one another and also opening to the public domain. If the outer courtyards each made an *eruv* with the middle courtyard, then the middle courtyard may carry with either outer courtyard, and vice versa — but the two outer courtyards are forbidden to one another. *Rav Chama bar Guriya* then states in the name of Rav: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon* — we rule like *Rabbi Shimon*.

But who is the opposing position? The *Gemara* identifies it as *Rabbi Yehuda*, even though no explicit dispute appears in this *mishnah* (Rashi clarifies how the *Gemara* knows this). The difficulty is immediate: we just established that when *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Shimon* dispute, *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — yet here we rule like *Rabbi Shimon*. *V'ha amart Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabbi Shimon halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda* — does this not contradict the *klal*?

### Conditional Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא, דִּילְמָא הֵיכָא דְּאִיתְּמַר — אִיתְּמַר, הֵיכָא דְּלָא אִיתְּמַר — לָא אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **What is the difficulty** posed by this ruling? **Perhaps where it is stated** explicitly to the contrary, **it is stated,** but **where it is not stated** explicitly to the contrary, **it is not stated,** and these principles apply.

The *Gemara*'s resolution is fundamental: *dilma heicha d'itmar itmar, heicha d'lo itmar lo itmar* — perhaps the answer is that these rules were never intended to be universal. When a specific tradition exists about how to rule in a particular case, that tradition governs — regardless of what the general *klal* would suggest. The *klalei psak* are not absolute rules; they are default principles to be invoked precisely when we lack a specific tradition or explicit determination. *Heicha d'lo itmar* — where no such specific ruling exists, these principles provide guidance. But they cannot override a direct, transmitted *psak* in a given case. The *klalim* fill the gaps; they do not supersede specific knowledge.

## Eruv Chatzerot vs. Techumim

### Chatzerot vs. Techumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב פָּפָּא אָמַר, אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּעֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת, אֲבָל בְּעֵירוּבֵי תְחוּמִין — אֵימָא לָא צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** a different explanation for the fact that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi made both statements: **It was necessary** for Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi to inform us that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, because had he said only that the *halakha* follows the lenient opinion with regard to an *eiruv*, **it could have entered your mind to say** that **this statement applies only with regard to** the laws governing the ***eiruv* of courtyards,** which are entirely rabbinic in origin. **But with regard to** the more stringent laws governing the ***eiruv* of** Shabbat **limits,** you would have **said** that we should **not** rule leniently, and therefore **it was necessary** to make both statements.

Rav Pappa now adds a further reason why both statements — the general principle of leniency in *eruv* and the specific ruling like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — were necessary. *Salka daitach amina hani mili be-eiruv chatzeiros* — one might have thought the rule of leniency in *eruv* applies only to *eruv chatzerot*, but *aval be-eiruvi techumin, eima lo* — perhaps it would not apply to *eruvi techumin*. Both statements were therefore needed to establish that the leniency covers *eruvi techumin* as well.

*U-mena teimra de-shani lan bein eiruv chatzeiros ve-eiruv techumin?* — What would have led one to think there is a meaningful distinction between the two? One might naturally suggest that *eruvi techumin* has a *de-oraisa* dimension, making it stricter — but that is not the *Gemara*'s reasoning.

### Alleyway Courtyards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין עֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת לְעֵירוּבֵי תְחוּמִין — דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּעֵירוּבֵי תְחוּמִין, אֲבָל בְּעֵירוּבֵי חֲצֵירוֹת מְעָרְבִין בֵּין לְדַעַת וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת, שֶׁזָּכִין לְאָדָם שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, וְאֵין חָבִין לְאָדָם אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do you say that we distinguish between an *eiruv* of courtyards and an *eiruv* of** Shabbat **limits? As we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda said: In what** case **is this statement said,** that an *eiruv* may be established for another person only with his knowledge? It was said **with regard to an *eiruv* of** Shabbat **limits, but with regard to an *eiruv* of courtyards, an *eiruv* may be established** for another person whether **with his knowledge or without his knowledge, as one may act in a person's interest in his absence; however, one may not act to a person's disadvantage in his absence.** One may act unilaterally on someone else's behalf when the action is to that other person's benefit; however, when it is to the other person's detriment, or when there are both advantages and disadvantages to him, one may act on the other person's behalf only if one has been explicitly appointed as an agent. Since an *eiruv* of courtyards is always to a person's benefit, it can be established even without his knowledge. However, with regard to an *eiruv* of Shabbat limits, while it enables one to walk in one direction, it disallows him from walking in the opposite direction. Therefore, it can be established only with his knowledge.

Instead, the *Gemara* cites a *mishnah* that demonstrates a structural difference. *Bameh devarim amurim be-eiruvi techumin* — by *eruvi techumin* one may not establish an *eruv* on behalf of another person without their knowledge, since doing so benefits the person in one direction while restricting them in another. It is a trade-off, not a pure benefit, and therefore requires explicit consent. *Aval be-eiruvi chatzeiros me-arvin bein le-daas bein she-lo le-daas* — by *eruv chatzerot*, by contrast, one may establish the *eruv* on behalf of another even without their knowledge, because *zakin le-adam she-lo be-fanav* — one may act to another's benefit in their absence.

*Tosafos* notes that this reasoning is somewhat strained: there is in fact a minor loss involved in *eruv chatzerot* as well, since one takes a portion of the other person's bread for the *eruv* without their explicit consent. Nevertheless, *Tosafos* reads the *Gemara* as ruling that in *eruv chatzerot* the benefit predominates, while in *eruvi techumin* the trade-off is genuine and therefore requires consent. This structural asymmetry is what might have led one to treat the two types of *eruv* differently for purposes of the leniency principle — hence the necessity of specifying that the principle applies to both.

## Beginning vs. Remnant

### Techillas vs. Shiyurei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא, הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּשִׁיּוּרֵי עֵירוּב, אֲבָל בִּתְחִילַּת עֵירוּב — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's need to issue two rulings can be explained in another manner: **It is necessary** for Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi to inform us that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, as if he had said only that the *halakha* is in accordance with the lenient opinion with regard to an *eiruv*, **it could have entered your mind to say** that **this statement applies only with regard to the remnants of an *eiruv*,** i.e., an *eiruv* that had been properly established, where the concern is that it might subsequently have become invalid. **But with regard to an initial *eiruv*,** i.e., an *eiruv* that is just being established and has not yet taken effect, you might have **said** that we should **not** rule leniently, and therefore it was necessary to issue both rulings.

Rav Ashi offers yet another reason why both statements were necessary. *Hani mili be-shiyurei eiruv* — one might have thought the leniency applies only to the *remnant* of an existing *eruv*, where the question is whether what remains is sufficient to preserve the *eruv*'s validity. *Aval bi-techillas eiruv, eima lo* — but at the *outset*, when one is first establishing the *eruv*, perhaps we would not apply the leniency.

*U-mena teimra de-shani lan bein shiyurei eiruv le-vein techillas eiruv?* — What grounds would there be for such a distinction? From a *mishnah* that records Rabbi Yose's ruling: *bameh devarim amurim bi-techillas eiruv* — the minimum food requirement applies at the outset of placing the *eruv*. *Aval be-shiyurei eiruv, afilu kol she-hu* — once an *eruv* was valid and the food subsequently diminished, even a minimal remnant suffices to maintain it. One is demonstrably more lenient once the *eruv* was established than at its inception — and one might therefore have thought that the general principle of leniency applies only in the *shiyurei* context.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין שִׁיּוּרֵי עֵירוּב לִתְחִילַּת עֵירוּב — דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּתְחִילַּת עֵירוּב, אֲבָל בְּשִׁיּוּרֵי עֵירוּב — אֲפִילּוּ כָּל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do you say that we distinguish between the remnants of an *eiruv* and an initial *eiruv*? As we learned** in a mishna: **Rabbi Yosei said: In what** case **is this statement said,** that the Sages stipulated that a fixed quantity of food is necessary for establishing an *eiruv*? It is said **with regard to an initial *eiruv*,** i.e., when setting up an *eiruv* for the first time; **however, with regard to the remnants of an *eiruv*,** i.e., on a subsequent Shabbat when the measure may have become diminished, **even a minimal amount** suffices.

### Food Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לְעָרֵב חֲצֵירוֹת, אֶלָּא כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְשַׁכֵּחַ תּוֹרַת עֵירוּב מִן הַתִּינוֹקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And they said to establish an *eiruv* for courtyards only** after all the inhabitants of the city merge their alleyways and become like the inhabitants of a single courtyard, **so that the law of *eiruv* should not be forgotten by the children,** who may not be aware of the arrangement that has been made with regard to the alleyways.

The explicit ruling like *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri* — which concerns the initial establishment of one's place — clarifies that the leniency applies *b'techillas eruv* as well.

The *mishnah* appends: *v'lo amru l'arev chatzeros ella k'dei shelo lishkach toras eruv min hatinokos* — the Rabbis required *eruv chatzeros* in order that children (and by extension the community) not forget the laws of *eruv*. This remark addresses a specific configuration: three courtyards, each feeding into the next, with all of them opening into a shared alleyway. If the courtyards all made a joint *eruv* with one another through their interconnecting doors, they would technically not need to make a separate *shituf mavos* in the alleyway. Nevertheless, the Rabbis required it in order to prevent a misleading inference. If people saw the residents of those courtyards carrying freely in the alleyway without any *shituf*, they might conclude that a *shituf mavo'os* is never required. The concern is not about teaching children incorrectly but about the community inferring a wrong principle from observed practice. Demanding a *shituf mavo'os* even where it is not technically necessary avoids the more consequential error of assuming it is never needed.
