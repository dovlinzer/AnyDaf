# Shabbat 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tosafot & Practice

### Courtyard Permission

The daf begins at the very bottom of 64b, continuing the extended discussion of what may and may not be worn on *Shabbat*. Before entering the new Gemara text, it is worth summarizing the crucial ground covered at the close of the previous session, as it bears directly on everything that follows.

The discussion concerns items that women are prohibited from wearing even in a *chatzer*, and *Tosafot* confronts the obvious tension: in practice, women of his era wore precisely these items of jewelry—not only in a *chatzer*, but openly in the public domain. How is this to be reconciled? *Tosafot* offers three answers, all of which are reproduced in the *Shulchan Aruch*.

The first answer is that the Gemara's prohibition applies only to the *reshut ha-rabbim*, whereas wearing such items in a *chatzer* without an *eruv* is permitted. Extending this logic, *Tosafot* adopts the position—significant for the laws of *eruvin* more broadly—that there is no genuine *reshut ha-rabbim* in the present day. That position alone would effectively permit wearing such items in virtually any contemporary context, though the claim that there was a true *reshut ha-rabbim* in Talmudic times but not now is itself difficult to accept at face value.

### Pragmatic Leniency

The second answer is more pragmatic, if less elegant: women are indeed violating the prohibition, but since the transgression is only *d'rabbanan* and they will not heed any objection, it is better to remain silent. The principle of *mutav she-yihyu shogegin* — better that they sin unwittingly than wittingly — underlies this approach.

### Contextual Maris Ayin

The third answer is perhaps the most conceptually striking. *Tosafot* argues that these rabbinic prohibitions were motivated by a specific concern: that a woman would remove her jewelry in the street to show a friend, thereby inadvertently carrying it. That concern, *Tosafot* maintains, is not merely the rationale behind a formal *takana* that remains on the books regardless of circumstances. Rather, the parameters of the prohibition are continuously defined by whether that concern remains a live one in the present social reality. Since women in *Tosafot*'s time no longer engaged in that behavior, the prohibition simply does not apply.

### Takana Permanence

This third approach, while compelling in limited contexts, is potentially dangerous if applied too liberally, since it could be used to dissolve virtually any rabbinic enactment by claiming that realities have changed. The more conservative and generally accepted approach is that once a *beit din* has established a *takana*, another *beit din* cannot uproot it without surpassing the original in wisdom and in numbers — *ad she-yigdal be-chochma u-ve-minyan* — a principle that reflects the system's fundamental resistance to change.

It is important to note that *Tosafot* deploys the contextual argument selectively. He raises it only when confronted with an existing practice that contradicts the Gemara — never proactively, as a grounds for dismantling a *takana* in the abstract. Classic examples include the neglect of *mayim acharonim*, which was instituted as a precaution against *melach sedomit*, a particular harmful salt that is no longer in common use; or the permission to clap and dance on *Shabbat*, where the original concern about inadvertently constructing a musical instrument is no longer realistic. In each case, *Tosafot* uses the contextual argument defensively, to make sense of accepted practice, not offensively, to innovate.

## Maris Ayin Doctrine

### Private Maris Ayin

The Gemara now introduces a formal statement: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav* — Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav — that wherever the *Chachamim* prohibited something on account of *maris ayin*, the concern for appearances, it is forbidden even in the most private of places, *afilu be-chadrei chadarim assur*. The intuitive assumption would be that a prohibition rooted in appearances should only operate where one is visible. Rav rejects that assumption categorically.

### Bell Contradiction

This ruling immediately invites challenge from a *mishnah* discussed earlier in the tractate: an animal may not go out wearing a bell even if the bell is stopped up and making no noise. The rationale given there is *maris ayin* — it looks as though one is leading the animal to market. Yet another *braita* records that if the bell is stopped up, the animal may walk about with it in the *chatzer*. If the concern is only about appearances in the public domain, that is perfectly consistent; if the concern applies even in private, how is use in a *chatzer* permitted? This appears to directly contradict Rav's ruling.

### Tannaim Debate

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction by noting that the question is itself a *tannaitic* dispute. At the top of 65a, a *braita* is cited regarding wet laundry: one who has wet garments — say, caught in the rain — may spread them in the sun to dry, but not in a place where the public can see, lest observers assume the laundry was done on *Shabbat*.

### Wet Laundry Case

This is a *maris ayin* concern, and the *braita*'s *Tanna Kamma* permits it in private precisely because there is no one to observe. This supports the view that *maris ayin* does not apply where one cannot be seen.

### Unresolved Status

Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon, however, prohibit spreading the wet laundry even where no one can see, in keeping with Rav's position that *maris ayin* applies even in private. The Gemara thus identifies Rav's ruling with the minority view of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon, while the *Tanna Kamma* and the ruling about the bell in the *chatzer* reflect the majority view that private settings are exempt from *maris ayin*. The Gemara does not issue a definitive ruling. If one examines *Tosafot* at the top of 65a, on the words *amar Rav kol makom she-asru chachamim*, one finds that *Tosafot* cites opinions — *yesh mefarshim* — who rule that the *halacha* does not follow Rav, while also noting that in other contexts *Tosafot* himself rules that *maris ayin* is indeed a problem even *be-chadrei chadarim*. Some of the *Geonim* explicitly rule that it is not a problem in private. *Le-halacha*, the generally assumed *psak* is that *maris ayin* does apply in private.

### D'oraisa vs D'rabbanan

There is, however, a position in the later *poskim* suggesting that even if *maris ayin* applies in private when something looks like a biblical violation, it may not apply when the act merely resembles a rabbinic transgression. The logic is that concerns about appearances generate concentric protective fences, and when one is already at some remove from a *d'oraisa* prohibition, the outer fence need not extend into completely private settings.

### Sensibility Concern

There is a further way to understand the prohibition of *maris ayin be-chadrei chadarim* that goes beyond the purely sociological concern about what observers might conclude. When the *Chachamim* prohibit an action because it *looks* like a transgression, they may not only be guarding against reputational harm or the risk that onlookers will draw incorrect inferences and become more permissive themselves. There may also be a concern about what performing an action that appears to be an *aveirah* does to one's own religious sensibilities — even in complete privacy, and even when no transgression is technically occurring.

To illustrate: placing a piece of non-kosher imitation food in one's mouth, or ordering a soy cheeseburger, may be technically permissible, but one might ask whether it is the right posture for someone who takes the *kedushah* of *kashrut* seriously. If one understands that laws like *kashrut* and *Shabbat* are not merely legislative constructs but carry some degree of intrinsic significance as *aveiros*, then to perform an act that objectively appears forbidden — even to oneself, even in private — may itself be problematic at the level of religious sensibility. This might explain why *maris ayin* applies *be-chadrei chadarim* specifically in cases that resemble a *d'oraisa* violation, where that sense of intrinsic gravity is most pronounced, while being less pressing when only a rabbinic prohibition is implicated.

It should be noted that this distinction between *d'oraisa* and *d'rabbanan* does not emerge from the present Gemara itself. The cases discussed here — the cow walking to market, which resembles a rabbinic violation, and the laundry drying, which resembles a biblical violation — cut across the proposed distinction rather than supporting it. The distinction is a later suggestion offered by certain *poskim*, and the Gemara does not endorse it explicitly.

### Coffee Rich Example

The Coffee Rich episode is instructive here. When non-dairy coffee creamer first became commercially available, its use raised a genuine *maris ayin* concern: pouring a white liquid into coffee at a dairy-free meal looked exactly like adding milk. Early authorities required that the original container be kept on the table to signal to observers that the substance was *parve*. Over time, as communal awareness of such products became widespread, that requirement lost its force. What constitutes *maris ayin* is not fixed in the abstract; it is a function of what a given action actually appears to be within a specific community at a specific time.

The same dynamic applies to women's trousers. In communities and eras where only men wore trousers, doing so would have raised both a *beged ish* concern and a *maris ayin* concern. In parts of the world where women routinely wore trousers, neither concern was applicable. The *poskim* of Eastern Europe simply were not aware of those communities; the perception that drove the prohibition was locally conditioned.

One might note that a change in communal reality need not be driven by initial violation. When Coffee Rich became universally recognized as *parve*, it was not because people disregarded the original ruling and thereby changed the norm — it was because communal knowledge itself expanded. The item in the box on the table communicated the new reality without anyone having to transgress first. Other normative shifts may have been driven in part by initial non-compliance, but that does not provide license to violate deliberately in order to engineer a change. The point is simply that *maris ayin* remains anchored to social reality, and when that reality genuinely shifts, so do the parameters of the prohibition.

## Medical Pads

### Ear Pad

The Gemara now turns to a series of rulings regarding items worn directly on the body for medical purposes. A *moch* — a wad of cloth — placed in one's ear to absorb discharge may be worn on *Shabbat*, since it functions as a form of bodily care and is therefore considered akin to wearing rather than carrying. However, *Tani Rami bar Yechezkel*: the wad must be tied to the ear. If it is not secured, there is concern that it will fall out and the wearer will pick it up and carry it without replacing it.

### Heel Pad

Similarly, a wad placed in one's sandal to cushion a heel abrasion may be worn. Again, *Tani Rami bar Yechezkel* adds the condition that it must be tied to the sandal; the same concern applies — that it will fall and be picked up and carried rather than replaced.

### Menstrual Pad

A pad worn by a woman on account of *niddah* — menstrual bleeding — is likewise permitted, subject to earlier qualifications in the Gemara: the pad must be serving the body itself, not merely protecting clothing, for in the latter case it would not be considered a bodily garment and its transport would constitute carrying. Rami bar Chama considered ruling that this pad too must be bound — specifically, tied between the thighs — lest it fall and be picked up.

### Rabbi Yochanan Case

Rabbi Yochanan would go out with a pad in his ear to the *beis medrash*. Rashi clarifies that the referent is not the menstrual pad but an ear pad for his own discharge. His colleagues objected, and the source of their objection appears to be that he had not properly bound the pad to his ear, as Rami bar Yechezkel required.

### Rabbi Yannai Case

Rabbi Yannai went further: he wore such a pad into a *karmelis*. This was even more sharply contested — not just by his immediate colleagues in the *beis medrash* but, the Gemara says, by his entire generation — since one may not carry even into a *karmelis*, and without proper binding, the concern about carrying applied there as well.

### Rava's Leniency

Rava disagrees with the requirement to bind the menstrual pad. His reasoning: *keivan de-mais lo ati le-atuyei* — since the pad becomes repulsive when soiled with menstrual blood, a woman will not pick it up if it falls. The revulsion itself provides the functional assurance that she will not carry it. This distinguishes the menstrual pad from the ear wad and the heel wad, both of which are relatively clean and would present no particular aversion to handling if dropped.

This is an interesting point in light of the Gemara's earlier extended discussion about the *tahor* status of a woman who is a *niddah*. The earlier *Tannaim ha-rishonim* had cultivated a comprehensive system of taboos around menstrual blood — a posture that Rabbi Akiva moved away from. Here the Gemara seems to take it as self-evident that a woman will be sufficiently repelled by the soiled pad to leave it where it falls rather than pick it up.

### Pad with Handle

A question is then raised by Rabbi Yirmiyah to Rabbi Abba: what if the pad has been fashioned with a handle? If the woman can pick it up without directly touching the soiled material, the aversion is removed and perhaps the original concern about carrying is reinstated. Rabbi Abba rules that even so, it remains permitted — *mutar*. Even with a handle, people simply do not handle a soiled pad after it has become contaminated, and we are not concerned she will carry it. This ruling is confirmed independently: *itmar nami*, Rav Nachman bar Oshaya said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that a pad made with a handle is nonetheless permitted.

## Mouth Remedies

### Pepper & Salt

The Gemara now addresses items held in the mouth for medical or hygenic purposes. It is permitted to go out on *Shabbat* with pepper or salt in one's mouth. *Pilpel l'reach ha-peh* — pepper is used to counteract bad breath, functioning essentially as an ancient breath remedy. Rashi on the *mishnah* explains that this refers to *pilpel aroch*, a long pepper rather than a peppercorn — there is indeed a plant known as long pepper, related to black pepper, and that appears to be the referent. *Galgal melach* — a ball of salt — is used for a *dorshani*, which Rashi identifies as some form of tooth ailment.

This ruling is a significant *chiddush*. Until this point, the Gemara's cases of items worn for bodily purposes all involved things physically attached to or embedded in the body — bandages, pads, wads bound to the ear or sandal. Here the item is simply loose in the mouth, not attached, not a garment, not a bandage in any conventional sense. Yet it is permitted because it is actively performing a therapeutic function for the body while it is there. This is distinct from carrying medicine in a container: a bottle of pills is not doing anything for the body at the moment of transport; it is merely being moved from one place to another for future use. These cases occupy an intermediate category — the substance is present and functional, but not physically integrated with the body in the way a bandage is.

One might ask: could a breath mint be permitted on this basis? Following the logic of the Gemara, a mint serving to counteract bad breath would appear to qualify. As for the question of whether salt used for a toothache runs into the concern of *sh'chikat sammanim* — the rabbinic prohibition against grinding medicinal substances on *Shabbat*, which extends to taking medicine for minor ailments — the relevant distinction is that salt and pepper are ordinary foods, not exclusively medicinal substances. The question of whether they cross into the category of medicine is a genuine one, but their status as natural foodstuffs provides a basis for leniency.

### Spices

The Gemara adds that *zangvila* — ginger — and *dartzuna* — which Rashi identifies as cinnamon — are similarly permitted when placed in the mouth, presumably serving the same function as the pepper in counteracting bad breath.

### Gold Tooth

The Gemara then addresses the false tooth. A silver false tooth is unanimously permitted. A gold tooth is disputed: *Rebbi matir ve-chachamim osrin* — Rebbi permits it, while the *Chachamim* prohibit it. The concern driving the prohibition appears to be that a gold tooth is sufficiently striking or unusual that the wearer might remove it in the street to show someone — and in so doing, carry it.

### Silver vs Gold

Rav Zeira clarifies the scope of the dispute: it is only the gold tooth that is contested. A silver tooth, by contrast, is *mutar* according to all. Rashi offers a further explanation: the concern with the gold tooth is not only that one might display it to a friend, but that one might feel embarrassed to be wearing something so conspicuous and remove it for that reason. A silver tooth is less visually arresting and thus less likely to prompt removal.

### Embarrassment Principle

Abaye then draws together the shared principle uniting Rebbi, Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in their various rulings: *kol midi d'miganya bah* — anything that would be embarrassing or unbecoming to remove, one will not in fact remove, and therefore we do not fear that it will be carried. Rebbi's ruling about the gold tooth rests on this: even if a gold tooth is conspicuous, removing it would itself be more embarrassing than wearing it, and therefore the concern that one will take it out in the street is unfounded.

Rabbi Eliezer's application of the same principle: he permitted a woman to go out wearing a bottle of perfume around her neck — *lechatchila*, as a practical matter — on the grounds that women who wear perfume bottles do so because of poor body odor. Removing the bottle would expose that fact, and therefore the embarrassment of removal is sufficient to ensure she will not do so.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar formulates a general rule: any item situated beneath a hairnet may be worn out — *yotza bah* — because even momentarily removing such an item would expose the hair, which a woman would be unwilling to do, and thus the concern about carrying it in public is removed.

## Women's Attire

### Coin Wound

The *mishnah* continues listing items women may wear out on *Shabbat*. A woman may go out with a coin placed against a wound — *sela she-al ha-tzinis*. The Gemara discusses what a *tzinit* is: Rashi explains it as a wound on the underside of the foot, in a location vulnerable to irritation. The coin serves as a protective covering. The Gemara then asks why specifically a coin — could not a hard piece of pottery or a plain piece of metal serve equally well? The Gemara ultimately concludes, based on Abaye's analysis, that a coin possesses all three relevant attributes simultaneously: hardness, the capacity to exude moisture beneficial to the wound, and the image embossed on it. Each attribute alone could be replicated by something simpler, but no single alternative combines all three. Abaye thus concludes that all three properties together are what make the coin uniquely therapeutic for this purpose.

### Ear Strings

Young girls — *banot ktanot* — may go out with strings or even with small twigs, like toothpicks, inserted in their pierced ears. Rashi explains the practice: young girls have their ears pierced but do not yet wear earrings; the strings or twigs serve to keep the holes from closing until the girls are old enough to wear jewelry. The ruling permits this even though what is being worn is neither jewelry nor a medical device in any conventional sense.

### Wearing vs Carrying

This raises the fundamental question: why is this not carrying? The item is not attractive — Rashi explicitly notes that the twig is not jewelry, *lav tachshit l'noy hu*. It is not therapeutic in the way that a bandage or pad is. Yet Rashi explains that it is permitted because *orcha behachi velav masoi hu* — this is simply the norm for how such an item is worn on the body, and therefore it is categorized as wearing rather than carrying.

This formulation is worth pausing over. The defining distinction between wearing and carrying does not reduce to a single criterion — not decoration, not therapy, not attachment. Rather, the principle seems to be: if one always wants the item on one's body and has no intention of transporting it to a different location, it is wearing. Carrying, by contrast, is what one does when the purpose is to get something from one place to another — even if the object happens to be placed on or in the body during transit. A recorder held in the mouth, or a backpack worn on the shoulders, are carrying rather than wearing because the purpose is transportation, not continuous bodily use. Items like the strings in pierced ears, the wad in the ear, the pad between the thighs, the pepper in the mouth — all share the quality of being continuously wanted on the body, not moved toward a destination. The Gemara does not always make this principle explicit, but it seems to be what is organizing these rulings.

### Cultural Dress

The *mishnah* turns to two examples of culturally specific dress: Arab women may go out fully veiled — *re'ulot* — and Median women may go out with a cloak fastened in their manner. These are simply their ordinary garments.

### Universal Norm

The *chiddush* is articulated in the immediately following phrase: *ve-chol adam* — and in fact any person may wear these items. The *Chachamim* speak in terms of the common case, but the principle is general. Once something constitutes normal dress for some identifiable group of people, a member of a different group who wears it is wearing, not carrying — even if in their own community the item is exotic. The category of *levush*, of dress, is not community-specific in the restrictive sense.

### Stone/Nut Fastener

The *mishnah* also addresses a method of fastening a garment: attaching a string to one end and a stone, nut, or coin to the other, then wrapping the string around the stone to close the cloak. This is permitted — but with the caveat *u-vilvad she-lo tifrof l'chatchilah be-Shabbat*: one should not do this fastening for the first time on *Shabbat* itself. The reason, as will become clear, turns on the *muktzah* status of the coin.

A stone can be designated in advance for a non-monetary purpose — as a fastener, as a paperweight — and thereby lose its *muktzah* status. A coin, however, retains its monetary identity regardless of how one attempts to repurpose it, and thus remains *muktzah* even when incorporated into a garment. The *mishnah*'s initial permission — *porefet al ha-even ve-al ha-egoz ve-al ha-matbeia* — covers fastening with a stone or nut; the concluding restriction against doing so *le-chatchilah on Shabbat* applies specifically to the coin.

## Mikvah & Rainwater

### Colorful Strings Risk

The Gemara now presents a series of rulings attributed to *Avuha de-Shmuel* — Shmuel's father — regarding his daughters. First: he would not permit his daughters to go out on *Shabbat* with strings in their ears, despite the *mishnah*'s explicit permission for young girls to do so. The Gemara explains that his daughters wore *tzivonin* — colorful strings — and colorful strings are sufficiently attractive that a girl might be tempted to remove them in the street to show to friends.

### Sleeping Separation

Second: he would not permit his daughters to sleep together in the same bed. This was apparently a common arrangement in large families. The Gemara initially considers whether this reflects support for the position of Rav Huna.

### Arousal Concern

Rav Huna had stated that *nashim ha-mesolalot zo be-zo* — women who rub against one another — are *pesulot le-kehuna*, disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. Rashi, apparently seeking to limit the ruling's scope, says this refers to a *kohen gadol*, on the grounds that such contact might compromise the status of *betula*. Rashi also acknowledges, however, that the act carries overtones of improper intimacy. *Tosafot*, drawing on other Gemarot, takes a broader view: this behavior would constitute *zona* in a more expansive sense, disqualifying even for a regular *kohen*.

The Gemara, however, declines to attribute Shmuel's father's ruling to Rav Huna's disqualification. The Gemara rules that Rav Huna's position itself is rejected: we do not regard such contact as constituting *zona*. Shmuel's father's concern was different — *ki heikhi de-lo le-alfan gufa nukhra'ah* — he did not want his daughters to grow accustomed to having another body beside them while sleeping, which might cultivate a premature or misdirected physical need. This is a concern about habit formation and its consequences for young women, not a concern about the formal halachic category of *zona*.

It bears noting that the *d'oraisa* prohibition against homosexual relations applies specifically to male relations, because *bi'ah* halakhically requires penile penetration. The question of how to categorize intimate female contact has no direct *d'oraisa* source. The Gemara raises the issue here in terms of whether it constitutes *zona* for purposes of *kehuna*, and appears to reject that characterization. The *Rambam* attempts to classify it under *ma'aseh Eretz Mitzrayim*, but this is a difficult position given the absence of clear evidence that Egypt specifically practiced this, and the *Rambam*'s categorization is itself debated. This Gemara is thus directly relevant to contemporary discussions, and the trend of the *sugyah* seems to resist categorical prohibition at the formal halachic level, though the behavior is clearly regarded with disapproval.

### Spring Mikvah

Third: Shmuel's father would construct private *mikva'ot* for his daughters in *Nisan* — in the spring — and would lay down mats for them in *Tishrei*, in the autumn. The Gemara first addresses the spring *mikvah*: the Gemara says *mesayeia lei le-Rav* — this supports the position of Rav.

### Rainwater Majority

Rav had stated: *mitra be-ma'arava sahada raba perat* — rain in the land of Israel is evidenced by the Euphrates rising. The implication is that the rivers rise in the rainy season due to an influx of rainwater, and this creates a halachic problem for *tevila* in a river. The problem is as follows: a *mikvah* of rainwater must be stationary — *mei geshamim* that are *zochalin*, flowing, are invalid. A natural river may be used even though its water runs, because the river itself is classified as *mayim chayim*, groundwater. But if a majority of the river's water at a given time is rainwater rather than groundwater, the river is reclassified, as it were, as a rainwater body — and then the flowing water is invalid. One cannot immerse in a river when its majority is rainwater unless the river is standing, which a river by definition is not. Therefore, in the spring — when snowmelt and sustained rainfall cause the rivers to swell dramatically — Shmuel's father was concerned that the majority of the river's water was rainwater, and so he built standalone *mikva'ot* for his daughters rather than permit them to use the river.

### Underground Water

Shmuel, however, disagreed with his father. Shmuel held that *nahara mi-keifei mivrak* — a river rises from its floor. He believed that when the surface level of a river rises due to rain, there is a corresponding rise in underground water feeding the river from below, such that the proportion of true groundwater never actually falls to a minority. On this view, there is no concern about rainwater constituting a majority, and the river remains usable throughout the year.

Yet, the Gemara notes, Shmuel seems to contradict himself: he elsewhere rules that flowing water is only valid for immersion — *ein ha-mayim metaherin be-zochalin* — in the case of the Euphrates during *Tishrei*, the autumn, but not during other seasons. This position aligns with his father's concern during the spring, even though Shmuel's general principle would suggest no concern was warranted. The tension is left in the Gemara without full resolution: all agree that if a river's majority is rainwater it is problematic when flowing; the dispute concerns whether that situation actually arises in practice.

This is an important point for contemporary practice as well. It cannot simply be assumed that any natural body of water constitutes a valid *mikvah*. Beyond the obvious point that some very large lakes are man-made — and thus lack the requisite characteristics of a natural water source — there is the further question of the proportion of rainwater at any given time and season. For rivers and lakes, this requires investigation, and general statistics about seasonal water composition may be consulted, though each situation should be evaluated carefully.

### Mayim Chayim

Rashi introduces an additional dimension by citing his *rabosav*, who raise the possibility that the problem with a river whose majority is rainwater is not only about flowing versus standing water, but about the loss of the category of *mayim chayim* — living water. The Torah specifies that a *zav* must immerse in *mayim chayim*. Rashi's *rabosav* extend this requirement to a *zavah* as well. Given that contemporary halachic practice treats every woman as combining the statuses of *niddah* and *zavah*, this would mean that all women require *mayim chayim* for valid immersion — and that if rainwater becomes the majority in a river, the river no longer qualifies.

Rashi himself rejects this position, and so does *Tosafot*, and *le-halacha* it is definitively rejected. Were it accepted, the entire system of modern *mikva'ot* — which are overwhelmingly constructed using collected rainwater — would be invalidated. One might ask how such a position could have been maintained at all: surely even the *rishonim* who held it needed functional *mikva'ot*. The answer is that in earlier eras, the overwhelming majority of *mikva'ot* were actually wells, drawing on groundwater that unambiguously qualifies as *mayim chayim*. The position was therefore not practically untenable in its historical context, even if it could not survive into the period when rainwater *mikva'ot* became universal.

### Autumn Mats

As for the mats in *Tishrei*: Rashi explains that in the autumn, the river could be used for *tevila* — the rainwater concern was absent — but the riverbed was extremely muddy, and walking through the mud would create a *chatzitzah* problem, an interposition between the body and the water. Shmuel's father would lay down mats so his daughters could enter the water without accumulating mud on their feet. *Tosafot* offers a different explanation: the mats were erected not on the riverbed but as screens providing privacy, without which his daughters would not immerse properly out of embarrassment. These two explanations reflect different understandings of what practical obstacle the mats were designed to solve.

## Women's Attire (II)

### Coin Muktzah

Returning now to the question of the coin as a garment fastener: the *mishnah* had permitted fastening a garment with a string wrapped around a stone, nut, or coin, but had added that one should not do so *le-chatchilah* on *Shabbat*. Abaye explains: the later clause of the *mishnah* — *seifa asan le-matbei'ah* — addresses specifically the coin. Wrapping a string around a stone or nut involves no *muktzah* concern, since those items can be designated for use in the garment before *Shabbat*, changing their functional identity. A coin, however, cannot shed its monetary identity by being incorporated into a garment. It remains *muktzah* regardless. Therefore, handling the coin to fasten the garment on *Shabbat* itself is prohibited, and whatever was not fastened before *Shabbat* cannot be fastened on *Shabbat*.

This also resolves the question of the coin placed on the wound: presumably, this coin too would need to have been in place from before *Shabbat*, since moving the coin into position on *Shabbat* would involve handling *muktzah*.

### Nut Trick

Abaye now poses a question: may a woman use a nut — a legitimate garment fastener — as a ruse to carry food for her infant out into the street on *Shabbat*? The scenario is that she cannot carry the food directly, but she wraps the string of her garment around a nut that happens to also be an edible food item. She walks outside wearing the garment thus fastened, then removes the nut and gives it to the child to eat. Is this permitted?

### No Emergency

*Teivai le-man d'amar ma'arimin, teivai le-man d'amar ein ma'arimin* — the question can be posed on either side of the debate about whether one may employ ruses in cases like a burning building on *Shabbat*. In the case of a fire, one may wish to save garments by wearing many layers at once — effectively carrying them without technically carrying — and the question is whether this trick is permitted. The Gemara draws a critical distinction. In the fire case, there is an urgent emergency: if a permissible outlet is not provided, the person will simply carry the garments in outright violation. Permitting the ruse is thus a concession to human urgency. *Aval hacha, ee lo sharis lei lo asi le-afkukei* — here, by the nut, if the ruse is not permitted, the woman will simply not take the food out. There is no emergency that will cause her to violate regardless. Therefore, no concession is necessary, and the ruse need not be permitted.

The Gemara then entertains a counterargument: even for the opinion that prohibits the fire-garment ruse, the present case might be more permissible — because in the fire case, wearing extra garments is sometimes genuinely a mode of transport rather than dress, and thus the action itself carries the taint of carrying even when technically classified as wearing. Using a nut to fasten a garment, by contrast, is simply what people do with garments — it is unambiguously a mode of dress and never a mode of transport. On this view, even setting aside the emergency consideration, the nut ruse is purer in form and might be permitted.

The Gemara does not resolve the question. *Teiku* — it stands unanswered.
