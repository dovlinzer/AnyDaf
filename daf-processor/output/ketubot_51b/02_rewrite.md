# Ketubot 51b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Min. Ketubah Amount

### 100/200 Zuz Standard

The *mishnah* on this *daf* arrives — somewhat belatedly, as has been noted throughout this tractate — at what one might have expected to be among the very first topics addressed: the baseline *ketubah* amount. The standard is 200 *zuz* for a *betulah* and 100 *zuz* for an *almanah*. The *mishnah* establishes that even if these amounts were not written explicitly in the *ketubah* document, or if the husband attempted to write a lesser figure, this makes no practical difference. These obligations exist as *t'nai beit din* — rabbinic conditions that bind the parties whether or not they appear in writing. The *mishnah* similarly addresses the lien structure of the *ketubah*: the encumbrance applies to all of the husband's property, and any attempt to limit the *shudim* to a particular field is ineffective. These too are mandated by *beit din* regardless of what the document states. The *mishnah* then proceeds to the obligation of redeeming a captive wife — but before reaching the *ketubah*'s financial mechanics, the *gemara* pauses to analyze who authored the *mishnah*'s various clauses.

### Rabbi Meir: Be'ilat Zenut

The *gemara* opens with the question *mani* — who is this *mishnah* attributed to? The answer: *Rabbi Meir hi*. It must be Rabbi Meir, for it is Rabbi Meir who rules that anyone who writes a *ketubah* for a *betulah* for less than 200 *zuz*, or for an *almanah* for less than 100 *zuz*, has rendered the relationship *be'ilat zenut* — akin to fornication. The reasoning is significant: a woman's sense of marital security depends on the *ketubah*'s protection. In a legal regime where the husband may divorce his wife against her will, the *ketubah* functions as both deterrent and guarantee. It deters callous divorce by imposing a financial cost on the husband and provides the wife with protection should divorce occur. If those protections are absent or inadequate, the ongoing marital relationship lacks the essential character of marriage and takes on the quality of casual, unprotected intimacy. That is Rabbi Meir's position: anything below the standard amounts constitutes *be'ilat zenut*.

### Censure vs. Voiding

This principle raises an important distinction. Does Rabbi Meir's characterization of a sub-standard *ketubah* as *be'ilat zenut* mean only that the practice is censured — that writing less than 200 or 100 *zuz* is halachically condemned — or does it mean that writing a lesser amount is entirely void, such that the *ketubah* automatically reverts to the standard figure? The *gemara* treats these as equivalent: Rabbi Meir's categorical opposition to any *ketubah* below the standard implies that whatever lesser figure was written is simply disregarded, and the obligation defaults to 200 or 100 *zuz* as appropriate. On this reading, our *mishnah* — which likewise refuses to honor a sub-standard *ketubah* — follows Rabbi Meir.

### Rabbi Yehuda: Receipt Me…

Against this attribution, the *gemara* considers whether the *mishnah* might reflect Rabbi Yehuda's position instead. Rabbi Yehuda's view, as cited, is that a husband wishing to reduce the effective *ketubah* amount may write the standard 200 *zuz* for a *betulah*, and then have his wife write a receipt acknowledging that she has already received 100 *zuz* — thereby reducing the effective obligation to 100 *zuz*. Similarly, for an *almanah*, one writes the standard 100 *zuz* and she provides a receipt for 50. What is immediately striking about this formulation is that Rabbi Yehuda does not simply say: write 100 *zuz* for a *betulah*. He requires beginning with the standard amount. This implies that according to Rabbi Yehuda, the document must open at the baseline figure the Rabbis established — even if the parties subsequently arrange to reduce it in practice. Therefore, the *mishnah*'s rule that the *ketubah* may not be written for less than the standard amount could reflect Rabbi Yehuda's position: one must start at 200 or 100, even if a receipt later brings the effective amount lower.

### Mishnah = Rabbi Meir

The *gemara* thus concludes that the opening clause of the *mishnah* — the requirement that the *ketubah* not fall below the standard amounts — accords with Rabbi Meir, *mishnah Rabbi Meir hi*.

## Liens & Encumbrances

### Liens Full on All Proper…

The *gemara* turns to the *seifa* of the *mishnah*: if the husband wrote that a particular field worth 100 *zuz* is encumbered as security for a *ketubah* of 200 *zuz* — omitting the standard formula by which *kol nechasin de'is li acharin l'kesuvaseich*, all of his property stands as surety — he is nonetheless *chayav*, and the lien attaches to all of his property. This is *t'nai beit din*.

### Achrayut = Default

Here the *gemara* raises a difficulty. If the opening of the *mishnah* follows Rabbi Meir, can the *seifa* — which refuses to honor the husband's attempt to limit his liens — also follow Rabbi Meir? The *gemara* argues that this clause actually reflects Rabbi Yehuda's principle. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *achrayut ta'us sofer hu* — the absence of an explicit lien clause in a loan document is treated as a scribal error. What this means practically is that any written loan instrument automatically carries liens (*achrayut*) on the borrower's property, whether or not they are written in the document. The rationale is that liens are simply presumed to accompany any written loan; their omission is treated as inadvertent. Accordingly, a husband who explicitly limits his *ketubah*'s lien to a single field has not successfully circumscribed his obligation — because Rabbi Yehuda's principle is that liens attach to all property by virtue of the written instrument itself.

### Lost Document Baraita

The *gemara* supports Rabbi Meir's contrary position — that absent an explicit lien clause, no lien exists — by citing the *mishnah* in *Bava Metzia* dealing with found loan documents. If one finds a *shtar chov* lying in the street, the concern is that the debt may already have been paid, or alternatively that the document could be exploited collusively. The hypothetical scheme is as follows: Reuven borrowed money from Shimon; the debt was repaid, but the document was not destroyed. The document is now found in the street. If it is returned to Shimon, he and Reuven could fraudulently use it to seize property that Reuven had already sold to third parties — Shimon would present the document in court, Reuven would claim insolvency, and Shimon would then collect from the encumbered property held by subsequent purchasers, after which they would divide the proceeds.

Why, it might be asked, is this concern unique to a found document? Generally, we are not suspicious that paid-up documents will be reused fraudulently. The answer is that a *shtar* is only operative as testimony when held by the party it favors. A document lying in the street is not functioning as testimony at all; returning it to Shimon would, in effect, be creating testimony — and that requires a stronger basis of trust than the circumstances provide.

The *mishnah* rules: *im yesh bahem achrayut nechasim, lo yachazir* — if the document contains a lien clause, do not return it, since *beit din* might execute the lien. *Im ein ba achrayut nechasim, yachazir* — if there is no lien clause, return it, since *beit din* will not execute a lien from property in third-party hands. This is the ruling of Rabbi Meir. The Sages, by contrast, rule *echad zeh ve'echad zeh lo yachazir* — do not return it regardless, because in the Sages' view a written instrument automatically carries liens even without an explicit clause.

The *gemara* now notes something striking: this *baraita* itself states explicitly that even a *ketubat isha she'ein ba achrayut* — a *ketubah* lacking a lien clause — carries no lien according to Rabbi Meir. So Rabbi Meir consistently holds that liens must be written; their absence is significant, not merely a scribal oversight. This is the opposite of Rabbi Yehuda. And it applies to *ketubot* and regular loan documents alike.

### Five Unencumbered Items

The *baraita* in question actually enumerates five categories of obligation that can only be collected from *b'nei chorin* — unencumbered property currently in the debtor's possession — and not from property that has passed to third-party purchasers via liens. The five are: *peirot*, fruit; *shevach peirot*, the enhanced value of fruit; obligations to support a stepchild (*hamekabel alav lizon et ben ishto u'vat ishto*); a loan document lacking an *achrayut* clause; and a *ketubah* lacking an *achrayut* clause.

The case of *peirot ve'shevach peirot* requires some elaboration. The scenario involves a *ganev* who steals a field and sells it. The rightful owner subsequently reclaims the field from the purchaser. The purchaser, having lost the field, seeks reimbursement from the *ganev*. If the *ganev* has no liquid assets but did own other fields that he has sold to yet other parties, those sales may be subject to liens in favor of the purchaser — since when the purchaser paid the *ganev* for the stolen field, the *ganev*'s other properties were encumbered as implicit guarantors of the transaction. The purchaser may therefore collect the original purchase price from those encumbered properties.

However, while the purchaser held the stolen field before it was reclaimed, he invested in it: he plowed, fertilized, and planted it, and the field was returned to its owner in an improved state. The purchaser is entitled to reimbursement for that investment — but only from property currently in the *ganev*'s direct possession, not from property the *ganev* has already transferred. The reason is that this reimbursement is not a fixed, predetermined sum. When a third party purchased land from the *ganev*, he could estimate the fixed lien on that property; he could not be expected to account for an open-ended, ongoing liability whose total would depend on how much the purchaser happened to invest over an indeterminate period. Imposing such an indefinite encumbrance on innocent third-party purchasers would be unfair, and so the entitlement to *shevach peirot* is collectible only from *b'nei chorin*.

The same logic governs the obligation to support a stepchild. If a husband undertook in his *ketubah* to provide *mezonot* for his wife's children from a prior marriage, this is an ongoing, indefinite obligation — not a fixed sum. Should he then sell his property, subsequent purchasers cannot be subjected to an encumbrance whose extent is unknown. Collection is therefore limited to property the husband currently holds. The *gemara* also notes the resonance with recent discussions about daughters' support from an estate: because that obligation is similarly open-ended and indefinite, it is likewise not clear that it can be collected from property that has changed hands.

Finally, the fifth category — a loan document or *ketubah* lacking an explicit *achrayut* clause — has no liens at all by Rabbi Meir's reckoning, and therefore collection is necessarily limited to unencumbered property.

The *gemara* asks: *man tana lah de'amar achrayut lav ta'us sofer* — who is the *tanna* who holds that the absence of a lien clause is not a scribal error? The answer: *Rabbi Meir*. Rabbi Meir explicitly treats a document without liens as genuinely lacking liens, and the *baraita* confirms this applies to *ketubot* as well.

What is somewhat surprising is that Rabbi Meir, who is so insistent that the *ketubah* amount never fall below the standard — and characterizes any deviation as *be'ilat zenut* — nonetheless does not insist on the full lien structure. Perhaps his reasoning is that while the minimum amount is necessary to protect the wife, the lien question implicates third-party purchasers as well, and Rabbi Meir feels their interests in predictability and certainty must also be protected.

Conversely, Rabbi Yehuda appears to hold the inverse position: the *ketubah* amount may be effectively reduced below the standard (though one must start at the standard figure), but the liens are always full and comprehensive regardless of what the document says.

### Reconciliation: R. Yehuda

This creates an apparent problem for our *mishnah*, which seems to insist simultaneously on both the full amount *and* the full liens — a position that fits neither Rabbi Meir nor Rabbi Yehuda squarely. The *gemara* offers two possible resolutions.

The first and simpler answer: *ei ba'is eima Rabbi Yehuda*. The *mishnah* follows Rabbi Yehuda throughout. Rabbi Yehuda's formulation — write a *ketubah* of 200 and then have the wife issue a receipt — implies that one must at least begin with the standard amount. If Rabbi Yehuda requires beginning at the base, then the *mishnah*'s rule that one cannot write a *ketubah* for less than the standard reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position: even though the effective amount can later be reduced via a receipt, the document itself must open at 200 or 100 *zuz*. And the lien clause of the *mishnah* is straightforwardly Rabbi Yehuda's *achrayut ta'us sofer* principle.

The second answer: *ei ba'is eima Rabbi Meir*. The entire *mishnah* can be read as Rabbi Meir, but the ruling that one is still *chayav* despite having written the lien as applying to only a single field does not mean that liens extend over all sold property. It means only that the husband's personal liability — for property currently in his possession, *b'nei chorin* — remains full. He cannot cap his personal obligation at the value of one field. But liens on property already transferred to third parties might not exist, consistent with Rabbi Meir's position that an explicit lien restriction (or absence) is effective. This reading, however, is not the simple reading of the *mishnah*, which appears to say that the lien structure cannot be curtailed in any fashion. The simpler reading of the *mishnah* is therefore that it follows Rabbi Yehuda.

## Rape & Captivity

### Father of Shmuel: Forbid…

The *mishnah* distinguishes between the fate of a captive wife married to a *Yisrael* and one married to a *Kohen*. The husband of a *Yisrael* redeems her and takes her back as his wife; the husband of a *Kohen* redeems her but must then divorce her. Before addressing the *ketubah* mechanics of redemption, the *gemara* examines the underlying question of what captivity or rape does to the wife's permitted status.

The father of Shmuel states: *eshet Yisrael she'ne'ensah, asurah l'ba'alah* — the wife of a non-*Kohen* who was raped is forbidden to her husband. The reason: *chayshinan shema techilatah be'ones ve'sofah be'ratzon* — we are concerned that even though it began as rape, it concluded with her willingness. This is a deeply difficult passage. The *gemara* appears to be contemplating that at some point during a rape, the woman might become a willing participant. The halachic consequence, according to the father of Shmuel, is that she would then be forbidden to her husband just as a *zonah* is — having engaged in voluntary intercourse with a man she cannot marry.

It is important to note that the *gemara* is not here speaking primarily of captivity but of a straightforward case of rape. One might have imagined that the father of Shmuel's concern involves a woman held over a long period who eventually, psychologically, identifies with her captors and comes to act willingly — a dynamic that has observable parallels in certain captivity cases. But as the *gemara* will clarify, the case of captivity is actually treated more leniently than rape, because a captive woman may never have been violated at all. The father of Shmuel's concern is about a woman who was directly assaulted.

### Rav's Challenge

Rav challenges the father of Shmuel from the language of the *ketubah* itself: *im tishtavai afarkinnach ve'osvinnach li le'intu* — if you are taken captive, I will redeem you and take you back as my wife. The *ketubah* explicitly contemplates the husband's obligation to redeem a captive wife *and* to take her back. This implies that the captive wife of a *Yisrael* who returns is permitted to her husband. The father of Shmuel had no answer and was silent. Rav remarks — citing the verse *sarim atzru ve'milim ve'kaf yasimu lefihem*, that great men restrained their words and placed their hands over their mouths — that the father of Shmuel's silence was unnecessary.

### Captivity vs. Rape

The answer the father of Shmuel *could* have given is: *bi'shvuyah hekilu* — in the case of captivity, the Rabbis were lenient. The *ketubah* clause refers to a woman taken captive, where we do not know that she was raped. Since there is at least a possibility she was never violated, she is permitted to return to her husband. The father of Shmuel's ruling — that she is forbidden — applies specifically to a case where it is known that she was raped. Captivity and rape are therefore distinct: captivity may permit the wife to return; confirmed rape triggers the concern of *techilatah be'ones ve'sofah be'ratzon*, which the father of Shmuel regards as sufficient grounds to forbid her.

### Rava: Coercion Principle

The *gemara* now notes that this position of the father of Shmuel is directly contradicted by Rava. The *gemara* asks: according to the father of Shmuel, in what circumstances does the Torah's own implication — that a raped woman is permitted to her husband (*ve'hi lo nitpasa asurah*, implying that *nitpasa*, i.e., seized by force, renders her permitted) — ever apply? The answer offered: only when witnesses testify *she'tzvacha mitechilah ve'ad sof* — that she cried out and protested from the very beginning of the assault to the very end. Any cessation of protest raises the concern that she became a willing participant. The discomfort of this position is acknowledged — it demands that we interpret a woman's silence during a violent assault as an expression of consent.

Rava holds the opposite extreme: *kol she'techilatah be'ones ve'sofah be'ratzon, afilu hi omeret hanichu lo, she'ilmaleh nizkak lah hi socharto, muteret* — even if the assault began against her will and she later says not only that she consented but that she would have paid the man to have relations with her, she is still permitted to her husband. The reasoning: *yetzer albasha* — her *yetzer* overcame her. The fact that an act began as *ones* means that any subsequent expression of *ratzon*, no matter how explicit, is understood as resulting from and rooted in the original coercion. It cannot be treated as genuine, independent *ratzon*.

This is a far-reaching principle. It is related to — but distinct from — the parallel concept in torts: *techilatah bifshia ve'sofah be'ones*, where an initial act of negligence determines the character of everything that follows. Here, Rava's principle is that once an intimate violation begins under coercion, all subsequent apparent desire is legally attributed to the original *ones* and cannot constitute *ratzon* sufficient to create a prohibition.

Rava's position also has implications for a case where a woman is held captive over a long period and comes to act cooperatively — bringing food, handing weapons, and so on. If one frames the entire situation as originating in *ones*, perhaps everything that follows remains categorized as resulting from that original coercion.

### Bandits & Fear

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* in support of Rava: *ve'yesh acheres she'af al pi she'lo nitpasa muteret* — there is a woman who, even though she was not seized by force, is nevertheless permitted to return to her husband. Which case is this? *Kol she'techilatah be'ones ve'sofah be'ratzon* — one whose situation began as coercion, even if it concluded with apparent willingness. A second *baraita* addresses the wife of a *Kohen*: even one who *was* raped is forbidden to her husband, because the prohibition of *zonah* applies to any sexual act outside the permitted relationship, regardless of the woman's consent.

Rav Yehuda then rules: women stolen by bandits (*listim*) may return to their husbands, because we presume that any sexual contact was unwilling. The Rabbis challenge this: such women appear to be willing participants — they bring food to their captors, hand them arrows during combat. Rav Yehuda responds: *machmat yira* — all of this is out of fear. The appearance of willingness is entirely a function of the threat environment they inhabit; it does not reflect genuine *ratzon*. This is consistent with the *Rambam*'s analysis in his *Iggeret HaShmad* regarding Jews who outwardly conformed to religious coercion: as long as one has no realistic opportunity to leave the threatening environment, apparent compliance is still *ones*. The limiting case is: *vadai shavkinhu ve'azlu minafshayhu assurun* — if the captors definitively offered them freedom and the women chose to remain, they would then be considered willing and forbidden to their husbands.

### King vs. Bandit

A *baraita* states: women taken captive by the royal government (*malchut*) are considered like captives — i.e., we presume coercion. But another *baraita* appears to contradict this, ruling the opposite. The *gemara* resolves the contradiction: both statements about *malchut* are consistent — the distinction is between *malchut Achashverosh* and *malchut Ben Netzer*.

In the case of Achashverosh, Rashi explains, the king would not ordinarily marry the woman, so there is no realistic prospect of elevation in status that might induce willingness. The encounter remains coercive and the woman is presumed to have acted under *ones*. Ben Netzer, by contrast, was a bandit leader who had carved out his own territory and was building, as it were, his own kingdom. Because he was not of royal blood, a woman seized by him might conceivably *want* to become his consort — there would be no hereditary barrier to her becoming queen. In that case, we may be concerned that the woman was willing.

This explains the apparent contradiction between the two *baraitot* regarding *listim* (bandits) as well: one *baraita*'s reference to *listim* refers to Ben Netzer (treated stringently), while the other refers to ordinary bandits (treated leniently). And Ben Netzer himself is called a *malka* (king) in relation to ordinary bandits, and a *listim* (bandit) in relation to Achashverosh — a fitting illustration of the relativity of status.

## Redemption Obligation

### Widow/Divorcee to KG

The *gemara* now turns to the *ketubah*'s redemption obligation across different types of marriages, including those that are halachically prohibited. Abaye raises the case of an *almanah* who married a *Kohen Gadol*, or a *grushah* who married a *Kohen* — forbidden unions, but ones where the *kiddushin* is nonetheless binding and requires a *get*. Is the husband obligated to redeem her if she is taken captive? Abaye rules: *chayav liftotah* — he is indeed obligated to redeem her, because the relevant line of the *ketubah*, *u'bekohenet adrinach le'adinetech* ("for a *kohenet* I will return you to your city"), applies to her. Even though the marriage was illicit, the *ketubah* clause obligating redemption remains operative.

### Ongoing vs. Redemption

This ruling requires an important clarification. It is a general principle that *kiddushin be'issurei la'avin* — marriage contracted in violation of a negative commandment — is binding and requires a *get*, and obligations associated with the marriage may still apply. However, not all *ketubah* obligations remain in force in an illicit marriage. The obligations that reflect the *ongoing* marital relationship — such as *mezonot* and medical expenses — are not upheld, because the court wishes to discourage the couple from remaining together and to create a financial incentive for the husband to divorce his wife. Maintaining those ongoing obligations would effectively subsidize their continued cohabitation in a forbidden relationship.

The lump-sum *ketubah* payment upon divorce is treated differently: the wife still receives it, both because it represents her financial protection upon separation and because one of the *ketubah*'s functions — deterring casual divorce — is arguably not the operative reason here, where we *want* divorce to occur. That discussion aside, the point is that some *ketubah* obligations survive and some do not.

Redemption occupies a distinct category. In the normal case of a *Kohen* and his wife, redeeming her does not lead to their remaining married — he redeems her and then must divorce her. Since redemption does not sustain the ongoing marital relationship, it is not analogous to *mezonot*; it is not an obligation that maintains a forbidden union. Therefore the obligation to redeem survives even in an illicit marriage.

### Rava: Captivity-Specific

Rava refines this further. The *ketubah* clause that says "I will return you to your city" does not create a general obligation to redeem one's wife under all circumstances where the marriage cannot continue. Rather, Rava holds: *kol she'issur shvuyah gorem lah, chayav lispotah; issur davar acher gorem lah, eino chayav lispotah* — when it is the captivity itself that creates the impediment to remaining married (as in the case of a *Kohen* whose wife becomes a *shvuyah*), the obligation to redeem exists. But when the impediment to the marriage exists for some *other* reason — such as the forbidden nature of the union itself — the obligation to redeem does not apply.

This is why, according to Rava, a *Yisrael* who married a *mamzeret* is not obligated to redeem her if she is taken captive: *de'ein ani korei bo ve'osvinnach li le'intu* — the clause "I will take you back as my wife" does not apply to them at all, since the marriage was fundamentally forbidden. The redemption obligation is grounded in the husband-wife relationship; if that relationship should never have existed, the redemption obligation has no foothold, regardless of the *ketubah* clause's language about captivity.

### Vow & Divorce Baraita

The *gemara* now asks whether this debate between Abaye and Rava parallels a *tannaic* dispute. A *baraita* is cited: if a man vowed that his wife may derive no benefit from him — which obligates him to divorce her — and she is subsequently taken captive, Rabbi Eliezer rules that he must redeem her and then pay out her *ketubah* upon divorcing her. Rabbi Yehoshua rules that he pays out the *ketubah* upon divorce but need not redeem her.

### Vow Timing Question

Rabbi Nassan reports that he posed a question to Sumchus (a student of Rabbi Meir): when Rabbi Yehoshua ruled that the husband need not redeem her, was that ruling limited to a case where the vow *preceded* the captivity (*hedirah ve'levassof nishbeis*) — meaning the couple was already on a trajectory toward divorce before she was taken captive? Or would it apply even if the captivity occurred first and the vow was taken afterward (*nishbeis ve'levassof hedirah*)? Sumchus replied that he had not heard an explicit ruling, but it seemed to him that Rabbi Yehoshua's exemption from redemption applies only when the vow preceded the captivity. If the captivity came first and the vow followed, the husband's motivation for taking the vow would be suspect — it would appear to be a scheme to avoid the expense of ransom. Since redeeming a captive wife can be far more costly than the lump-sum *ketubah* payment, a husband might deliberately take a vow in order to obligate himself only to the *ketubah* payment and escape the redemption obligation. To prevent such manipulation, the exemption is unavailable when the vow postdates the captivity.

### Debate Parallel

The *gemara* proposes that the debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua in this *baraita* parallels the debate between Abaye and Rava. The relevant case for the parallel is that of a *Kohen*'s wife, since only in that situation — where the *ketubah* explicitly says "I will return you to your city" rather than "as my wife" — is there any basis to argue that the obligation continues even when the couple cannot remain married. The *gemara* suggests that Abaye follows Rabbi Eliezer (the obligation to redeem persists even where some other circumstance requires divorce) and Rava follows Rabbi Yehoshua (no obligation to redeem when the impediment to the marriage is independent of the captivity). The question at the heart of the debate is whether the language of the *ketubah* — which contemplates return without continued marriage, in the case of a *Kohen*'s wife — creates a freestanding redemption obligation even when the prior state of the marriage already required divorce, or whether the obligation is only triggered when the captivity itself is what separates the couple.
