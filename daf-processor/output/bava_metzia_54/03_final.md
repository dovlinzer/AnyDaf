# Bava Metzia 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ma'aser Below Prutah

### Ona'ah & Coins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִם גָּאֹל יִגְאַל אִישׁ מִמַּעַשְׂרוֹ״, מִמַּעַשְׂרוֹ – וְלֹא כׇּל מַעַשְׂרוֹ. פְּרָט לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that it is written: **"And if a man will redeem of his tithe, he shall add to it the fifth part thereof"** (Leviticus 27:31), from which it is inferred: **Of his tithe, but not all his tithe.** This serves **to exclude second tithe that does not have the value of one *peruta*,** which cannot be redeemed.

The background is as follows. When one redeems *ma'aser sheni* outside of Yerushalayim, one transfers the sanctity of the food onto money and then uses that money in Yerushalayim to purchase food eaten in a state of *kedushah*. The minimum meaningful unit of monetary value recognized by halachah is a *prutah*. If the *ma'aser sheni* one wishes to redeem does not reach that threshold, the question arises whether a valid act of *pidyon* — redemption — can be performed at all. Hizkiyah taught that if a coin has already been used in redemption and retains residual value from a previous transaction, one may redeem even *ma'aser sheni* worth less than a *prutah* onto that coin. The question under examination here concerns a fresh coin — one that has not yet been used in any redemption — when the *ma'aser sheni* itself is worth less than a *prutah*.

### Rav vs. Rav Asi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר. רַב אַמֵּי אָמַר: אֵין בּוֹ. רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֵין בְּחוּמְשׁוֹ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵין בּוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵין בְּחוּמְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute with regard to this *halakha*. **Rav Ami says:** The second tithe cannot be redeemed in a case where the produce itself **does not have** the value of one *peruta*. **Rav Asi says:** The second tithe cannot be redeemed in a case where **its** additional payment of **one-fifth does not have** the value of one *peruta*. Other *amora'im* dispute the same issue. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The produce itself **does not have** the value of one *peruta*. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: Its** additional payment of **one-fifth does not have** the value of one *peruta*.

The Gemara now cites the *pasuk*: *Im ga'ol yigal ish mi-ma'asro* — "if a man redeems from his *maaser*" — and derives from the phrase *mi-ma'asro*, "from his *maaser*" and not "from all his *maaser*," that there are circumstances in which redemption is not possible. The teaching states: *prat le-ma'aser sheni she-ein bo shaveh prutah* — this comes to exclude *ma'aser sheni* that is not worth a *prutah*, which lacks the minimum value necessary to constitute a valid redemptive act. Of course, as noted, Hizkiyah's ruling allows redemption even below a *prutah* when the coin already bears the imprint of a prior redemption and carries residual value.

An *amoraic* dispute is then recorded. Rav says the problem is *ein bo* — the *ma'aser sheni* itself does not contain a *prutah*'s worth of value. Rav Asi, by contrast, says *ein be-chomsho* — the difficulty arises even when the *ma'aser sheni* does contain a *prutah*'s worth, but its *chomesh* does not. When one redeems one's own *ma'aser sheni*, one is obligated to add a fifth. This fifth, as the Gemara will explain shortly, is a fifth of the final total rather than of the base — what we might call a quarter of the starting value. If the food is worth four *prutot*, the *chomesh* added is one *prutah*, bringing the total to five, and that one *prutah* constitutes a fifth of the whole. But if the *ma'aser sheni* is worth less than four *prutot*, then the *chomesh* — the increment being added — does not itself amount to a *prutah*, and Rav Asi holds that this too disqualifies the redemption. The addition of the *chomesh* is treated, in some sense, as its own discrete redemptive act, requiring its own minimum value. This same dispute is replicated between Rabbi Yochanan, who holds *ein bo*, and Resh Lakish, who holds *ein be-chomsho*.

### New vs. Used Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – דַּיּוֹ שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״הוּא וְחוּמְשׁוֹ מְחוּלָּל עַל מָעוֹת רִאשׁוֹנוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **second-tithe** produce **that does not have the value of one *peruta*,** it is **sufficient that he will say: It and its** additional payment of **one-fifth are desacralized upon the first coins** upon which I already redeemed second-tithe produce.

The Gemara challenges these positions from a *baraita*: *ma'aser sheni she-ein bo shaveh prutah, dayo she-yomar hu ve-chomsho mechulalin al ma'ot rishonim* — if the *ma'aser sheni* is not worth a *prutah*, it is sufficient for one to declare that it and its *chomesh* are redeemed upon the original money, i.e., coins previously used in redemption. This is Hizkiyah's ruling applied in practice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֵין בְּחוּמְשׁוֹ״ – הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״דַּיּוֹ״, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דִּבְדִידֵיהּ אִית בֵּיהּ, כֵּיוָן דִּבְחוּמְשֵׁיהּ לֵיכָּא – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אֵין בּוֹ״, מַאי ״דַּיּוֹ״? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **Granted, according to the one who says:** There is **not** the value of a *peruta* **in its** additional payment of **one-fifth, this** is the reason **that the** *tanna* **teaches: It is sufficient,** which indicates **that even though in** the produce **itself it has** the value of one *peruta*, **since** there is **not** the value of a *peruta* **in its one-fifth** payment, it works out **well,** as it is sufficient if he redeems the produce with other second-tithe produce. **But according to the one who says:** It is in a case where the produce itself **does not have** the value of one *peruta* that the second tithe cannot be redeemed, **what** is the meaning of: **It is sufficient?** From the outset, there was never sufficient value for there to be any element of redemption. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, it is **difficult** to explain the *baraita* according to that opinion.

The Gemara then analyzes how this *baraita* bears on the dispute. If one holds like Rav Asi — that the problem arises when the *chomesh* lacks a *prutah* — then the word *dayo*, "it is sufficient," makes good sense. The general rule requires the *chomesh* to be worth a *prutah*, but in the case of money already used in redemption, as long as the *ma'aser* itself is worth a *prutah*, it is enough even if the *chomesh* is not, because something has already been accomplished — the *ma'aser* has crossed the minimum threshold. The *dayo* therefore indicates that one has achieved a partial satisfaction of the requirement.

But if one holds like Rav — that the issue is simply whether the *ma'aser sheni* itself reaches a *prutah* — then the word *dayo* is difficult. The *baraita* should have stated simply that one may redeem *ma'aser sheni* worth less than a *prutah* upon previously used money. The word *dayo*, implying that something short of ideal is being permitted, does not fit naturally. The Gemara acknowledges: *kashya* — this remains difficult for the position of Rav. Nevertheless, the essential halachic conclusion is that once money has already been used in redemption, everyone agrees that redemption may proceed; the dispute concerns only fresh, previously unused money, and whether the threshold requirement applies to the *ma'aser* itself or to the *chomesh* it generates.

## Chomesh Mi-Legav/Levar

### How Fifth Calculated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חוּמְשָׁא מִלְּגָיו, אוֹ חוּמְשָׁא מִלְּבַר? אָמַר רָבִינָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: הַבְּעָלִים אוֹמְרִים בְּעֶשְׂרִים, וְכׇל אָדָם בְּעֶשְׂרִים – הַבְּעָלִים קוֹדְמִין מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמּוֹסִיפִין חוֹמֶשׁ. אָמַר אֶחָד: הֲרֵי עָלַי בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד –
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the additional payment of one-fifth, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is the payment of **one-fifth** calculated **from within,** i.e., one-fifth of the value of the redeemed item, **or** is the payment of **one-fifth** calculated **from without,** meaning one-quarter of the value of the redeemed item, which is one-fifth of the eventual payment, i.e., the principal plus the additional one-fifth? **Ravina says: Come** and **hear** a resolution of the dilemma from a *baraita*: In a case where **the owner says** he is willing to redeem consecrated property **for twenty** dinars, **and any** other **person** is willing to purchase the property **for twenty** dinars, **the owners take precedence** and redeem the property **due to** the fact **that they** are obligated to **add one-fifth,** and the Temple treasury profits more from the owner than from anyone else. If **one** who is not the owner **said: It is** incumbent **upon me** to desacralize it **for twenty-one** dinars,

Having established the basic framework of the *chomesh*, the Gemara now asks a foundational question: *chumsha mi-legav o chumsha mi-lebar* — is the fifth calculated from the inside or from the outside? That is, does one take a fifth of the base value — *mi-legav* — or does one add an amount such that after the addition the increment constitutes a fifth of the total — *mi-lebar*? If *mi-legav*, then on something worth twenty one adds four, since four is a fifth of twenty. If *mi-lebar*, then on something worth twenty one adds five, since five is a fifth of the resulting total of twenty-five. The latter formulation means that what the Torah calls a "fifth" is in practical terms a quarter of the starting value.

### Be'alim Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבְּעָלִים נוֹתְנִין עֶשְׂרִים וָשֵׁשׁ. עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁנַיִם – הַבְּעָלִים נוֹתְנִין עֶשְׂרִים וָשֶׁבַע. בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְשָׁלֹשׁ – הַבְּעָלִים נוֹתְנִין עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁמוֹנֶה. בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע – הַבְּעָלִים נוֹתְנִין עֶשְׂרִים וָתֵשַׁע. עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ – הַבְּעָלִים נוֹתְנִין שְׁלֹשִׁים, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מוֹסִיפִין חוֹמֶשׁ עַל עִילּוּי שֶׁל זֶה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ חוּמְשָׁא מִלְּבַר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **the owner gives** a payment of **twenty-six** dinars. Were they to enable the other person to purchase it for twenty-one dinars, the Temple treasury would incur a loss, as the principal plus one-fifth paid by the owner is greater than the payment of the other person, who must pay only the principal. Were the owner to pay only twenty for the principal and add one-fifth, the principal paid by the owner is less than the sum offered by the other person. Therefore, the owner pays the principal proposed by the other person and then adds the payment of one-fifth that he was obligated to pay based on his own offer, i.e., five dinars, for a total of twenty-six. Likewise, if the other person offers **twenty-two** dinars, **the owner gives twenty-seven;** if the other person offers **twenty-three, the owner gives twenty-eight;** if the other person offers **twenty-four, the owner gives twenty-nine.** If the other person offers **twenty-five** dinars, **the owner gives thirty, due to** the fact **that** the owner **does not add one-fifth** based **on the raise** in the offer **of this** other person, but only on the principal according to his own offer. It is clear from these calculations that a principal of twenty plus an addition of one-fifth equal twenty-five dinars. **Learn from it** that **one-fifth** is calculated **from without,** meaning one-quarter of the value of the redeemed item. The Gemara affirms: **Learn from it** that this is the *halakha*.

Ravina resolves the question by citing a *baraita* concerning the redemption of *hekdesh*. When a sanctified object is being redeemed and the *be'alim* — the original owners who sanctified it — wish to reclaim it, they enjoy the right of first refusal over other bidders because they are required to add a *chomesh*. The *baraita* presents a series of scenarios: the *be'alim* offer twenty, another person offers twenty. The *be'alim* take precedence because they add a *chomesh*. If a stranger then offers twenty-one, the *be'alim* pay twenty-six — their base of twenty plus five as the *chomesh*, plus the additional one to match the stranger's bid.

What is decisive is that the *chomesh* on a base of twenty is five, not four. That demonstrates conclusively that the fifth is *mi-lebar*: one adds an amount equal to a quarter of the starting value, so that the final result is five parts of which the added increment is one — a fifth of the total. The *baraita* continues through progressive examples: a stranger bids twenty-two and the *be'alim* pay twenty-seven; twenty-three and the *be'alim* pay twenty-eight; twenty-four and the *be'alim* pay twenty-nine; twenty-five and the *be'alim* pay thirty. The owners do not pay a *chomesh* on the stranger's bid — only on their own base of twenty. They pay their *chomesh* of five, and then add whatever premium the competing bid introduced. The Gemara notes a difficulty in the final case — when the stranger bids twenty-five, which equals the *be'alim*'s total of twenty plus five — but refers the reader to the discussion in tractate *Erchin* where that case is addressed.

The conclusion from all this is clear: *shma mina chomesh mi-lebar* — the *chomesh* of the Torah is calculated from the outside, i.e., it is a fifth of the total after addition, which amounts to a quarter of the starting value.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״וְיָסַף חֲמִשִׁתוֹ עָלָיו״, שֶׁיְּהֵא הוּא וְחוּמְשׁוֹ חֲמִשָּׁה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: חֲמִישִׁיתוֹ חוּמְשׁוֹ שֶׁל קֶרֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The two sides of this dilemma are **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita* that it is written: **"And shall add unto it one-fifth part thereof"** (Leviticus 27:27). This means **that** the item **and its** additional **one-fifth** payment **will** total **five** parts; one-fifth is calculated from without; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya. Rabbi Yonatan says: Its one-fifth** means **one-fifth of the principal;** one-fifth is calculated from within.

The Gemara confirms that this point was itself a *tannaic* dispute: the verse states *ve-yasaf chamishito alav* — he shall add its fifth upon it. Rabbi Yoshiya reads this as: together with its fifth, the total is five — meaning you start with four, add one, and arrive at five. This is *mi-lebar*. Rabbi Yonatan, however, reads *chamishito* as a fifth of the principal — *mi-legav*. The established halachah, as confirmed by the *baraita* about the *be'alim*, follows Rabbi Yoshiya's reading: the fifth is *mi-lebar*.

## Fifth Me'akev?

### Four + One or Four on Five…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב, אוֹ אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב? אַרְבְּעָה בְּאַרְבְּעָה פָּרֵיק, וְאַכְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, אַלְמָא חוֹמֶשׁ לָא מְעַכֵּב. אוֹ דִּלְמָא אַרְבְּעָה בְּחַמְשָׁה פָּרֵיק, וְחוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does failure to pay the additional **one-fifth prevent** consumption outside of Jerusalem of second tithe that was redeemed, **or does it not prevent** consumption? The dilemma is: Is second-tithe produce worth **four** dinars **redeemed with four** dinars, **and** the obligation to **add** the payment of **one-fifth** is an obligation incumbent **upon** the owner **himself?** In that case, **apparently,** failure to pay the additional **one-fifth does not prevent** consumption. **Or perhaps** second-tithe produce worth **four** dinars **is redeemed with five** dinars, no less, **and** failure to pay the additional **one-fifth prevents** consumption.

The Gemara now turns to a separate and fundamental question: *chomesh me'akev o ein chomesh me'akev* — does failure to add the fifth invalidate the *pidyon*, the act of redemption? If one redeems *ma'aser sheni* worth twenty outside of Yerushalayim by transferring its sanctity onto twenty coins without adding the requisite five additional coins, has the *ma'aser sheni* in fact been redeemed, such that one may now eat it outside of Yerushalayim?

The conceptual question is framed with precision. One possibility is that the Torah's meaning is: you redeem the four on four — the redemption is transacted at the food's value — and then separately and additionally you add a fifth. Under this reading, the act of redemption and the obligation to add a *chomesh* are distinct. The *pidyon* is complete when you transfer the food's value onto money of equivalent worth; the *chomesh* is an additional financial obligation but does not define or constitute the redemptive act itself. The consequence would be: *alma chomesh lo me'akev* — the fifth does not prevent the redemption from taking effect.

The alternative possibility is that the Torah is telling you to redeem four on five — that is, the proper redemption of an item worth four requires the payment of five. Under this reading, tendering only four is simply not a redemption at the correct value, and the *pidyon* accordingly fails. The *chomesh* is not an add-on but an integral component of the redemptive transaction itself.

### Demai Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: הַדְּמַאי – אֵין לוֹ חוֹמֶשׁ, וְאֵין לוֹ בִּיעוּר. הָא קֶרֶן יֵשׁ לוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? קֶרֶן דִּמְעַכֵּב בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – אִיתַהּ בִּדְרַבָּנַן, חוֹמֶשׁ דְּלָא מְעַכֵּב בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – לֵיתַהּ בִּדְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a mishna (*Demai* 1:2): For second tithe of **doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*],** whose status is that of untithed produce by rabbinic law, **there is no** payment of **one-fifth** if the owner redeems its second tithe, **and there is no** obligation of the **eradication** of tithes after three years, as is the case with tithes taken from untithed produce. Ravina explains: **But** by inference, **there is** payment of **principal.** Although there is no obligation to add one-fifth, second tithe separated from *demai* requires redemption. **What is the reason** for this? Apparently, concerning **the principal,** about **which** failure to pay it **prevents** consumption of second tithe of untithed produce **by Torah law,** it **is** also a sum that must be paid **in** the case of *demai* **by rabbinic law.** Concerning the payment of **one-fifth,** about **which** failure to pay it **does not prevent** consumption of second tithe of untithed produce **by Torah law,** it **is not** a sum that must be paid **in** the case of *demai* **by rabbinic law.**

Ravina offers a proof from the laws of *demai*. *Demai* refers to produce purchased from an *am ha-aretz* whose proper separation of *ma'asrot* is uncertain. At the Torah level, one may assume the separations were performed; rabbinically, one must treat the produce as if they were not and separate again. The Mishnah teaches: *ha-demai ein lo chomesh ve-ein lo bi'ur* — *demai* does not require the addition of a *chomesh* and is not subject to *bi'ur*, the obligation to remove all *ma'aser* from one's home by a certain point in the sabbatical cycle. Yet the *keren* — the principal redemption — does apply. One must still redeem the *ma'aser sheni* of *demai*, even though one omits the *chomesh*.

The Gemara asks: what is the rationale for this distinction? Why would the rabbis require the *keren* but not the *chomesh* for *demai*? The answer is: *keren de-me'akev be-de'oraita ita be-derabbanan; chomesh de-lo me'akev be-de'oraita leita be-derabbanan* — the principal is *me'akev* at the Torah level, and therefore the rabbis preserved its requirement even for *demai*; the *chomesh* is not *me'akev* at the Torah level, and therefore the rabbis did not extend its requirement to *demai*. The logic of the rabbinic extension tracks the Torah-level significance of each component. If one accepts this reasoning, it constitutes a strong proof that the *chomesh* is fundamentally not *me'akev* — its absence does not invalidate the *pidyon* at the Torah level.

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: נָתַן אֶת הַקֶּרֶן, וְלֹא נָתַן אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יֹאכַל. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא יֹאכַל. אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בַּשַּׁבָּת, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בַּחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the two sides of this dilemma are **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: In a case where one **gave** payment to redeem **the principal but did not give** payment of **the** additional **one-fifth, Rabbi Eliezer says: One may eat** it, and **Rabbi Yehoshua says: One may not eat** it. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The statement of Rabbi Eliezer appears** correct **with regard to Shabbat,** when the Sages deemed it permitted for one to eat without the additional payment, in deference to Shabbat, **and the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua** appears correct **with regard to** the days of **the week.**

The Gemara raises the possibility that this issue is in fact a *tannaic* dispute. A *baraita* states: if one gave the *keren* but not the *chomesh* — *natan et ha-keren ve-lo natan et ha-chomesh* — Rabbi Eliezer says *yochal*, one may eat the *ma'aser sheni*, while Rabbi Yehoshua says *lo yochal*, one may not eat it. Rabbi Yehudah ha-Nasi then offers a split ruling: the position of Rabbi Eliezer appears correct on Shabbat, while the position of Rabbi Yehoshua appears correct on a weekday.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאָמַר נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בַּשַּׁבָּת, מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּחוֹל. מִדְּאָמַר נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בַּחוֹל, מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיגִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **From** the fact **that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **says: The statement of Rabbi Eliezer appears** correct **with regard to Shabbat, by inference they disagree even with regard to** the days of **the week.** Likewise, **from** the fact **that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **says: The statement of Rabbi Yehoshua appears** correct **with regard to** the days of **the week, by inference they disagree even with regard to Shabbat.** Apparently, theirs is a fundamental dispute.

The Gemara reads Rabbi's split ruling as revealing something about the underlying dispute. Since Rabbi says Rabbi Eliezer's view applies on Shabbat and Rabbi Yehoshua's applies on a weekday, this implies that each Tanna holds his position universally — Rabbi Eliezer allows eating regardless of the day, and Rabbi Yehoshua prohibits it regardless of the day — and Rabbi is mediating between them by applying each position to a different context. On the face of it, this appears to reflect a substantive disagreement: Rabbi Eliezer holds the *chomesh* is not *me'akev*, while Rabbi Yehoshua holds that it is.

## Fifth Me'akev? (II)

### Rav Pappa Resolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו בְּהָא סְבָרָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר חוֹמֶשׁ לָא מְעַכֵּב, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר חוֹמֶשׁ מְעַכֵּב? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא חוֹמֶשׁ לָא מְעַכֵּב. וְהָכָא בְּחָיְישִׁינַן לִפְשִׁיעוּתָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר חָיְישִׁינַן לִפְשִׁיעוּתָא. וּמַר סָבַר לָא חָיְישִׁינַן לִפְשִׁיעוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What,** is it **not** that **they disagree with regard to this reasoning, that Rabbi Eliezer holds** that failure to pay the additional **one-fifth does not prevent** consumption, as redemption of the principal alone suffices; **and Rabbi Yehoshua holds** that failure to pay the additional **one-fifth prevents** consumption? **Rav Pappa said: No,** perhaps the explanation is that **everyone agrees** that failure to pay the additional **one-fifth does not prevent** consumption, **and here** they **disagree with regard to** whether **we are concerned for** potential **negligence.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehoshua, **holds: We are concerned for negligence,** lest one fail to pay the additional one-fifth and therefore the owner may not eat the second-tithe produce until he adds the one-fifth payment. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Eliezer, **holds: We are not concerned for negligence,** and one will presumably add the one-fifth payment later.

Rav Pappa, however, rejects the conclusion that this is a dispute about the Torah-level status of the *chomesh*. He proposes: *de-kulei alma chomesh lo me'akev* — everyone agrees that at the Torah level the *chomesh* does not invalidate the *pidyon*. This is clearly the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who permits eating even without the *chomesh*. But even Rabbi Yehoshua, Rav Pappa argues, need not hold that the *chomesh* is *me'akev* at the Torah level. Rather, the dispute is whether we impose a rabbinic deterrent — *chashinan le-fshiyuta* — whether we are concerned that a person will be negligent. If there are no consequences to failing to add the *chomesh* and one can simply eat the *ma'aser sheni* after a *pidyon* without the *chomesh*, one will perpetually defer this obligation and never fulfill it. Rabbi Yehoshua therefore says we do not permit eating until the *chomesh* has been paid, not because the *pidyon* failed *de'oraita*, but as a rabbinic safeguard to ensure compliance.

Rabbi Eliezer, in contrast, holds *lo chashinan le-fshiyuta* — we are not concerned about negligence, and so he allows eating immediately.

This interpretation harmonizes elegantly with Rabbi's mediation. The concern for negligence is not absolute; it is a secondary, utilitarian consideration that can be overridden by competing concerns. On Shabbat, where the need for food is pressing and eating is a *mitzvah* dimension of the day — *u-kerasa le-Shabbat oneg* — Rabbi rules that we do not impose the rabbinic deterrent and the person may eat. On a weekday, where no such countervailing consideration exists, Rabbi endorses the deterrent of Rabbi Yehoshua. This works perfectly if the question is one of rabbinic policy, not *de'oraita* validity.

### Hekdesh & Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ – שֶׁחִילֵּל, הוֹאִיל וְגִזְבָּרִין תּוֹבְעִין אוֹתוֹ בַּשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Although the *tanna'im* disagree with regard to second tithe, **all concede with regard to consecrated** property that if the one who consecrated it paid the principal and did not add one-fifth, **that he** successfully **desacralized** the produce, **since** the Temple **treasurers demand** payment from **him in the marketplace,** preventing any potential negligence. Therefore, he may use the property immediately.

Rabbi Yochanan then introduces an important qualification. He states that everyone agrees — *hakol modim* — that when one has redeemed *hekdesh* and the *gizbarim*, the Temple treasurers, will pursue the unpaid *chomesh* in the marketplace, the *pidyon* is effective even without the *chomesh* having been paid. The rationale is that where there exists an institutional creditor who will enforce collection, the rabbinic concern for negligence does not arise. The person cannot simply forget or defer — the Temple treasury will ensure payment. Consequently, even Rabbi Yehoshua would not impose the deterrent in such a case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהֶקְדֵּשׁ לָא פְּלִיגִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: נָתַן אֶת הַקֶּרֶן, וְלֹא נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: חִילֵּל, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא חִילֵּל. אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים בְּמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do they not disagree with regard to consecrated** property? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where one **gave** payment to redeem **the principal but did not give him** payment of **the** additional **one-fifth, Rabbi Eliezer says: He** successfully **desacralized** the produce, and **the Rabbis say: He** did **not desacralize** the produce? **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The statement of Rabbi Eliezer appears** correct **with regard to consecrated** property, **and the statement of the Rabbis** appears correct **with regard to** second **tithe.**

The Gemara challenges this from a different *baraita* where Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* dispute a case that seems to involve *hekdesh*, and Rabbi again splits the vote — this time ruling that Rabbi Eliezer's permissive view makes sense for *hekdesh* and the *Chachamim*'s restrictive view makes sense for *ma'aser*. The Gemara concludes from Rabbi's split that each disputant actually holds his position across both *hekdesh* and *ma'aser*, and Rabbi is distinguishing between the two for policy reasons — not that everyone agrees by *hekdesh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאָמַר נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ – מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיג אֲפִילּוּ בְּמַעֲשֵׂר. מִדְּקָאָמַר נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים בְּמַעֲשֵׂר – מִכְּלָל דְּאִינְהוּ פְּלִיגִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **From** the fact **that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **says** that **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer appears** correct **with regard to consecrated** property, **by inference they disagree even with regard to** second **tithe.** Likewise, **from** the fact **that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said** that the statement of **the Rabbis appears** correct **with regard to** second **tithe, by inference they disagree even with regard to consecrated** property. This contradicts Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that there is no dispute with regard to consecrated property.

The original statement of Rabbi Yochanan must therefore be read more narrowly: it applies specifically to the combined case where *both* the considerations of *hekdesh* (institutional enforcement) *and* Shabbat are present simultaneously.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר, הָכִי אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּשַׁבָּת בְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ שֶׁחִילֵּל. חֲדָא – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְקָרָאתָ לַשַּׁבָּת עֹנֶג״. וְעוֹד, הוֹאִיל וְגִזְבָּרִין תּוֹבְעִין אוֹתוֹ בַּשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** it **was stated,** it **was stated like this: Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All concede** that **concerning Shabbat with regard to consecrated** property **that he** successfully **desacralized** the property. **One** reason for this *halakha* is **as it is written: "And you shall call Shabbat a delight"** (Isaiah 58:13). The Sages sought to facilitate that delight in Shabbat by ruling leniently. **And furthermore,** it is desacralized without payment of the additional one-fifth, **since** the Temple **treasurers demand** payment from **him in the marketplace.** *Hakol modim be-Shabbat be-hekdesh she-chilel* — everyone agrees that if one redeemed *hekdesh* on Shabbat and failed to pay the *chomesh*, the redemption stands: first because there is institutional enforcement, and second because of the honor of Shabbat and the desire to enable one to enjoy food on the holy day.

## Ma'aser Below Prutah (II)

### Fifth Not Me'akev

Before closing this topic, a *Tosafot* at the top of 54b raises a subtle question worth noting. The suggestion that the *chomesh* is *me'akev* rests on the premise that the Torah requires redeeming four on five — i.e., the *pidyon* is only valid when transacted at the total amount including the *chomesh*. But *Tosafot* points out that even an undervalued redemption generally works *bedieved* — if one redeems *hekdesh* worth a hundred on a penny, the *pidyon* is valid after the fact. If so, even if the Torah envisions redeeming four on five, why should paying only four invalidate it, given that an underpayment generally suffices *bedieved*?

*Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing between two types of shortfall. If one explicitly declares, "this item and its *chomesh* are hereby redeemed on this coin" — even if the coin's value is less than the item's value — the *pidyon* works *bedieved* because one has framed the transaction as a deliberate, if undervalued, redemption. But if one says, "I am redeeming this four on four," without mentioning the *chomesh* at all, one is not attempting an undervalued redemption — one is attempting a *full* redemption, but doing so incorrectly, without meeting the Torah's requirement that four be redeemed on five. That formulation does not work even *bedieved*, because the basis of the transaction is wrong. The *chomesh* must be part of the declared redemptive framework.

## Chomesh K'Moto

### Fifth Properties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ אֵינוֹ מִתְחַלֵּל עַל הַקַּרְקַע, דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְנָתַן הַכֶּסֶף וְקָם לוֹ״. חוּמְשׁוֹ מַהוּ שֶׁיִּתְחַלֵּל עַל הַקַּרְקַע?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama says:** The Sages **said** that **consecrated** property **cannot be desacralized with land, as the Merciful One states** with regard to the redemption of a consecrated field: **"And he will give the money and it will be assured to him"** (Leviticus 27:19), indicating that redemption is accomplished with money. The question is with regard to payment of **its** additional **one-fifth: What is** the *halakha* in terms of the possibility **that it will be desacralized with land?**

Having established the basic rules of the *chomesh*, the Gemara proceeds to a series of questions about the nature of the *chomesh* obligation and whether it mirrors the properties of the underlying *hekdesh* or *ma'aser*. Rami bar Chama introduces the inquiry by noting an established rule: *hekdesh eino mitchallel al karka* — *hekdesh* cannot be redeemed onto land; the Torah requires payment in money, as derived from the verse *ve-natan ha-kesef ve-kam lo* — one gives money and it is thereby established. The *chomesh* must follow form: *chumsho mahu she-yischalel al karka* — can the *chomesh* be redeemed onto land, or does it too require chattel?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרוּמָה אֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּלֶּמֶת אֶלָּא מִן הַחוּלִּין. דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְנָתַן לַכֹּהֵן אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ״, דָּבָר הָרָאוּי לִהְיוֹת קֹדֶשׁ. חוּמְשָׁהּ מַהוּ שֶׁיִּשְׁתַּלֵּם שֶׁלֹּא מִן הַחוּלִּין?
>
> **Translation:** If a non-priest unwittingly ate ***teruma*,** his debt **can be repaid** to the priest **only with non-sacred** property, not with *teruma*, **as the Merciful One states:** "And if a man eats that which is sacred unwittingly…**and he shall give that which is sacred to a priest"** (Leviticus 22:14), from which it is derived that repayment must be with **an item fit to become sacred,** i.e., a non-sacred item, not *teruma*, which cannot be consecrated again. The question is with regard to payment of **its** additional **one-fifth: What is** the *halakha* in terms of the possibility **that it will be repaid not from non-sacred** items, but from *teruma*?

Similarly: *terumah einah mishtalemet ela min ha-chullin* — if a *zar* eats *terumah* inadvertently, he must repay with non-consecrated food that thereupon becomes *terumah*, as derived from the verse *ve-natan la-kohen et ha-kodesh* — one gives the *kohen* something capable of becoming holy. The question is: *chumsho mahu she-yishtalem shelo min ha-chullin* — must the *chomesh* paid alongside that repayment also be food capable of becoming *terumah*, or may it be given in a different form entirely?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂר אֵין מִתְחַלֵּל עַל הָאֲסִימוֹן. דְּרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״וְצַרְתָּ הַכֶּסֶף בְּיָדְךָ״, לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו צוּרָה. חוּמְשׁוֹ מַהוּ שֶׁיִּתְחַלֵּל עַל הָאֲסִימוֹן?
>
> **Translation:** Second-**tithe** produce **cannot be desacralized with an unminted coin, as the Merciful One states: "And you shall bind up [*vetzarta*] the money in your hand"** (Deuteronomy 14:25), which the Sages interpret as serving **to include any item that has the imprint [*tzura*]** of a coin **upon it** for desacralization, not an unminted coin. The question is with regard to payment of **its** additional **one-fifth: What is** the *halakha* in terms of the possibility **that it will be desacralized with an unminted coin?**

And further: *ma'aser ein mitchallel al asimon* — *ma'aser sheni* cannot be redeemed onto an *asimon*, an unmarked metal blank that bears no stamped image, as derived from *ve-tzarta ha-kesef be-yadecha* — you shall bind the money in your hand, which requires a properly minted coin bearing an image. The question again is: *chumsho mahu she-yischalel al asimon* — may the *chomesh* be paid with an *asimon*, or does it require a proper stamped coin just as the principal does?

### Rava's Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְגַּלְגַּל מִלְּתָא וּמְטָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אָמַר קְרָא ״עָלָיו״, לְרַבּוֹת חוּמְשׁוֹ כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama was unable to resolve this series of dilemmas, and **the matter proceeded and came before Rava,** who **said to** the people who related the dilemmas to him: **The verse states:** "And shall add **unto it** one-fifth part thereof" (Leviticus 27:27), which serves **to include its** additional **one-fifth** within the same legal status **as** that of **its** principal.

These questions were deliberated and eventually reached Rava. He resolved all of them together with a single textual argument. Every *pasuk* in the Torah dealing with the *chomesh* uses the formulation *ve-asaf chamishito alav* — he shall add its fifth upon it. The word *alav*, "upon it," teaches: *le-rabbos chomsho ke-moto* — to include that the *chomesh* must be like the object itself. The *chomesh* must replicate the properties of the principal. If the principal must be redeemed on money, so must the *chomesh*. If the principal must be repaid in food capable of becoming *terumah*, so must the *chomesh*. If the principal must be redeemed on a stamped coin, so must the *chomesh*. The *chomesh* tracks the nature of the original obligation in every respect.

*Tosafot* notes in this connection that this *chomsho ke-moto* principle does not make the *chomesh* strictly *me'akev* — it defines the proper form of the *chomesh*, not a condition for the validity of the *pidyon* itself.

### Terumah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, הַגּוֹנֵב תְּרוּמָה וְלֹא אֲכָלָהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל דְּמֵי תְרוּמָה. אֲכָלָהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם שְׁנֵי קְרָנִים וָחוֹמֶשׁ. קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ מִן הַחוּלִּין, וְהַקֶּרֶן דְּמֵי תְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: We too learn** this in a mishna (*Terumot* 6:4): **One who steals *teruma* and did not partake of it pays a payment of double** the principal at **the price of** the ***teruma*,** as a thief pays double the value of the item that he stole. Both payments are calculated based on the price of *teruma*, which is lower than the price of non-sacred food, as the demand for it is lower because it is eaten only by priests. If the thief **ate it** unwittingly, **he pays** a payment worth the value of **two principals and** adds **one-fifth** in this manner: He pays one **principal and one-fifth from non-sacred** items, like any non-priest who partakes of *teruma*, **and** with regard to **the** other **principal,** he pays it according to **the price of *teruma*.** **Learn from it** that the legal status of **its one-fifth** payment is **like** that of the principal **itself,** in that it must be paid from non-sacred property.

Ravina offers an independent proof for the same principle from a *mishnah* concerning the repayment of *terumah*. The *mishnah* teaches: if someone stole *terumah* but did not eat it, he pays double — *tashlumei kefel* — but the double is calculated at the market value of *terumah*, which is lower than the value of ordinary produce because *terumah* may only be consumed by *kohanim* in a state of *taharah*. If, however, the thief ate the *terumah*, the repayment is more complex: he owes *shenei karnayim ve-chomesh* — two principal amounts and a *chomesh*. Specifically, the *keren* and *chomesh* are paid from *chullin* food that thereby becomes *terumah*, while the *keren* for the *kefel* is paid as a monetary assessment at *terumah* market value.

What is significant here is that the *chomesh* is paid in kind — in food — just as the principal is paid in food. The *chomesh* follows the nature of the original object. This is the proof: *shma mina chomesh ke-moto* — the *chomesh* must be like the original. The proof from *terumah* supports Rava's general principle derived from the word *alav*, and presumably what is true of *terumah* is equally true of the other categories.

## Fifth of Fifth

### Gezel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, גַּבֵּי גָּזֵל כְּתִיב: ״וַחֲמִשִׁתָיו יֹסֵף עָלָיו״. וּתְנַן: נָתַן לוֹ אֶת הַקֶּרֶן, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ עַל הַחוֹמֶשׁ – הֲרֵי זֶה מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ עַל חוֹמֶשׁ, עַד שֶׁיִּתְמַעֵט הַקֶּרֶן פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said: With regard to robbery, it is written:** "And he shall restore the robbed item that he robbed…**and he shall add its one-fifth payments to it"** (Leviticus 5:23–24), **and we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 103a): If the robber **gave** the robbery victim **the principal and took** a false **oath to him concerning the** additional **one-fifth** payment, asserting that he had already paid it, then the additional one-fifth is considered a new principal obligation. **This** robber **adds** an additional **one-fifth** payment **apart from** the additional **one-fifth** payment about which he had taken a false oath. If he then takes a false oath concerning the second one-fifth payment, he is assessed an additional one-fifth payment for that oath, **until the principal,** i.e., the additional one-fifth payment about which he has most recently taken the false oath, **is reduced to less than the value of one *peruta*.**

The Gemara now turns to the question of whether the obligation to add a *chomesh* applies recursively — that is, whether one may ever owe a *chomesh* on a *chomesh*. Rava introduces the discussion by citing the verse in the context of *gezel*. When one swears falsely that one has not stolen or defrauded someone and then admits the oath was false, one must return the principal and add a fifth: *ve-chamishitav yosef alav* — with his fifths he shall add upon it. The word *chamishitav* is in the plural — "his fifths" — indicating that the obligation of adding a fifth may compound.

The Mishnah cited provides the mechanism: if one gave back the *keren* but then swore falsely regarding the *chomesh* and subsequently admitted it, one now owes a *chomesh* on that *chomesh*. If one then swears falsely on the second *chomesh* and admits it, one owes a *chomesh* on the *chomesh* of the *chomesh*. This process continues — *ad she-yisma'et ha-keren pachos mi-shaveh prutah* — until the amount being sworn upon falls below a *prutah*. As long as each successive amount upon which one takes a false oath exceeds a *prutah*, the cycle of repayment and *chomesh* continues indefinitely. The plural *chamishitav* in the verse explicitly licenses this recursive application of the *chomesh* in the context of *gezel*.

### Terumah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי תְּרוּמָה כְּתִיב: ״אִישׁ כִּי יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ בִּשְׁגָגָה וְיָסַף חֲמִשִׁיתוֹ עָלָיו״. וּתְנַן: הָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמָה בְּשׁוֹגֵג – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ. אֶחָד הָאוֹכֵל וְאֶחָד הַשּׁוֹתֶה וְאֶחָד הַסָּךְ. אֶחָד תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה, וְאֶחָד תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה – מְשַׁלֵּם חוּמְשָׁהּ וְחוּמְשָׁא דְחוּמְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **With regard to *teruma*** too, **it is written: "If a man eats that which is sacred unwittingly, then he shall add its one-fifth payment to it"** (Leviticus 22:14), **and as we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 6:1): **One who partakes of *teruma* unwittingly pays** the **principal and** an additional **one-fifth.** This is the *halakha* **whether** it concerns **one who partakes of** *teruma*, **or one who drinks** it, **or one who applies** oil to himself; or **whether** it is **ritually pure *teruma* or ritually impure *teruma*. He pays its one-fifth** payment, **and** if he partook of that one-fifth, he pays **one-fifth of its one-fifth.**

In the case of *terumah*, the relevant verse uses the singular: *ve-yasaf chamishito alav* — he shall add its fifth upon it. The plural is absent. One might have thought that the *chomesh* on a *chomesh* therefore does not apply to *terumah*. Nevertheless, the Mishnah teaches explicitly: *ha-ochel terumah be-shogeg meshalem keren ve-chomesh* — one who eats *terumah* inadvertently repays the principal and the fifth. And the Mishnah continues: *va-chumsha de-chumsha* — and a fifth of a fifth. If one then ate the food one was repaying as the *chomesh*, one owes a further fifth on that. Thus, even without a plural form in the verse, the Mishnah teaches that the *chomesh* of a *chomesh* applies in *terumah*.

### Ma'aser No Fifth²

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ גַּבֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר, לָא מִכְתָּב כְּתִיב, וְלָא מִיתְנָא תְּנֵא, וְלָא אִיבְּעוֹיֵי אִיבַּעְיָא לַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava concludes: **While with regard to** second **tithe,** it **is neither written** in the Torah, **nor taught** in **a mishna, nor raised as a dilemma before us** by the *amora'im*.

By contrast, with respect to *ma'aser sheni*, neither the verses nor any Mishnah suggests the existence of a *chomesh* on a *chomesh*. The verse says *mi-ma'asro* — from his *maaser* — which led to the conclusion that there are circumstances where redemption cannot occur, but nothing in the text hints at recursive fifths. The Gemara states plainly: *ve-lo ibaya iba'ya lan* — it was never even raised as a question. The silence of both the scriptural sources and the Mishnaic tradition on this point makes clear that no *chomesh* on a *chomesh* applies to *ma'aser sheni*. The recursive obligation arises only from the original food, not from the money onto which it was redeemed. Once the *ma'aser sheni* has been redeemed onto money and the *chomesh* added, there is no further layer of fifths if one subsequently wishes to transfer that money.

### Hekdesh Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי הֶקְדֵּשׁ כְּתִיב: ״וְאִם הַמַּקְדִּישׁ יִגְאַל אֶת בֵּיתוֹ וְיָסַף חֲמִישִׁית כֶּסֶף עֶרְכְּךָ״. וּתְנַן: הַפּוֹדֶה אֶת הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹ – מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ. חוּמְשָׁא תְּנַן, חוּמְשָׁא דְחוּמְשָׁא לָא תְּנַן. מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara pursues a similar line of inquiry: **With regard to consecrated** property **it is written: "And if he who consecrated it will redeem his house, then he shall add one-fifth of the money of your valuation** unto it, and it shall be his" (Leviticus 27:15), **and we learned** in a mishna (55b): **One who redeems his** own **consecrated** property that he consecrated himself **adds one-fifth** to the sum of the redemption. **We learned one-fifth; we did not learn one-fifth of the one-fifth. What** is the *halakha*?

For *hekdesh*, the picture is more complex. The verse reads: *ve-im ha-makdish yigal et beito ve-yasaf chamishit kesef erchecha alav* — if the one who sanctified it redeems his house, he shall add a fifth of the valuation money upon it. The word *chamishit* is in the singular, not the plural. Nor does the Mishnah say *chumsho* and *chumsha de-chumsho* as it does with *terumah*. The Mishnah regarding *hekdesh* says only: *ha-podeh hekdesh mosif chomesh* — one who redeems *hekdesh* adds a fifth. A fifth — singular — with no mention of a fifth of a fifth.

The question therefore arises: what is the basis for saying a *chomesh* on a *chomesh* applies by *terumah*, and does that basis exist by *hekdesh* as well?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבֵּי תְּרוּמָה כְּתִיב ״וְיָסַף״, גַּבֵּי קָדָשִׁים נָמֵי הָא כְּתִיב ״וְיָסַף״. אוֹ דִלְמָא גַּבֵּי תְּרוּמָה כְּתִיב ״וְיָסַף״: אִי שָׁקְלַתְּ לֵיהּ לְוָיו דִּ״וְיָסַף״ וְשָׁדֵית לֵיהּ עַל ״חֲמִשִּׁיתוֹ״, הָוֵה לֵיהּ ״חֲמִישִׁיתָיו״. גַּבֵּי הֶקְדֵּשׁ כְּתִיב ״וְיָסַף חֲמִישִׁית״: אַף עַל גַּב דְּכִי שָׁקְלַתְּ לֵיהּ לְוָיו דִּ״וְיָסַף״ וְשָׁדֵית לֵיהּ עַל ״חֲמִישִׁית״, סוֹף סוֹף הָוֵה לֵיהּ ״חֲמִשִׁתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **With regard to *teruma* it is written: "Then he shall add,"** and **with regard to consecrated** property **too, isn't it written: "Then he shall add"?** Apparently, in a case of consecrated property one also pays one-fifth of the one-fifth. **Or perhaps** we should learn the *halakha* as follows: **With regard to *teruma* it is written: "Then he shall add [*veyasaf* ],"** and the *halakha* of one-fifth of the one-fifth is derived in this manner: **If you take the** letter ***vav* of** the word ***veyasaf*, and cast it to** the end of the word ***ḥamishito*,** its one-fifth payment, **it** then **becomes** the plural ***ḥamishitav*,** its one-fifths payments, as it is written in the case of robbery, indicating that one pays one-fifth of one-fifth. **With regard to consecrated** property, **it is written: "Then he shall add [*veyasaf* ] one-fifth [*ḥamishit*]." Even when you take the *vav* of *veyasaf* and cast it to** the end of the word ***ḥamishit*, ultimately it is** only ***ḥamishito*,** in the singular, indicating payment of only a single one-fifth. What is the *halakha*?

The Gemara initially considers whether the basis for the recursive *chomesh* in *terumah* might be the word *ve-yasaf* — "and he shall add" — read as indicating an ongoing, cumulative addition. Since the same word *ve-yasaf* appears in the *hekdesh* verse, perhaps the same implication carries over. Alternatively, perhaps the basis for *terumah*'s recursive *chomesh* is a more technical textual maneuver: the extra *vav* of *ve-yasaf* is taken from the root and appended to the end of *chamishato*, transforming it from *chamishato* (singular, "its fifth") to *chamishitav* (plural, "its fifths"). By this reading, it is the *vav* of *ve-yasaf* that generates the plural, and only by *terumah* does the word *chamishato* appear to which this *vav* can be attached. By *hekdesh*, the verse says *chamishit* rather than *chamishato*, and even appending the extra *vav* would produce only *chamishito* — still a singular "its fifth" — not a meaningful plural. Under this reading, the basis for a recursive *chomesh* does not transfer from *terumah* to *hekdesh*.

Before resolving this textual question, the Gemara considers whether there is a separate, threshold reason why the *chomesh* on a *chomesh* might not apply to *hekdesh* — namely, the principle of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that the *chomesh* is added only to the original sanctified object, not to transferred *kedushah*. If the *chomesh* itself represents new *kedushah* added to the system, one might argue that it falls into a category of transferred *kedushah* and therefore no additional fifth applies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֵׁנִי. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אַהֶקְדֵּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֵׁנִי אֵין מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And why not derive** the *halakha* of consecrated property from the fact **that it is** tantamount to **a second consecration.** When one redeems consecrated property with another item, although that item is thereby consecrated, not all the *halakhot* of consecrated property apply to it. **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: For** redemption of **first consecration one adds one-fifth; for** redemption of **second consecration one does not add one-fifth.**

### Hekdesh Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב טָבְיוֹמֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְיָסַף חֲמִישִׁית כֶּסֶף עֶרְכְּךָ״, מַקִּישׁ חוּמְשׁוֹ לְכֶסֶף עֶרְכּוֹ. מָה כֶּסֶף עֶרְכּוֹ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ – אַף כֶּסֶף חוּמְשׁוֹ נָמֵי מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this dilemma? **Rav Tavyumei said in the name of Abaye** that **the verse states** with regard to one who redeems a house that he consecrated: **"Then he shall add one-fifth of the money of your valuation** unto it" (Leviticus 27:15). The Torah **juxtaposes its** payment of **one-fifth to the money of its valuation,** i.e., the consecrated house: **Just as** when redeeming **the money of its valuation one adds one-fifth, so too,** when redeeming **the money of its one-fifth, one adds one-fifth as well.**

The resolution is provided by Rav Tivumi in the name of Abaye, citing the verse: *ve-yasaf chamishit kesef erchecha* — he shall add a fifth of the money of your valuation. What is striking here is that the verse places the words for "fifth" and "principal" in immediate proximity: *chamishit kesef erchecha* — a fifth alongside the valuation money. The Gemara reads this as a *hekesh* — an analogy: *mah kesef ercho mosif chomesh, af kesef chomsho nami mosif chomesh* — just as one adds a fifth upon the principal valuation, so too one adds a fifth upon the *chomesh* itself. The structural juxtaposition of the fifth and the principal amount in the same phrase indicates that the fifth is to be treated like the principal, and whatever obligation attaches to the principal — including the recursive addition of a fifth — attaches equally to the *chomesh*.

Thus, the *chomesh* of *chomesh* applies to *hekdesh* through this *hekesh*, even though neither the plural form of the word nor the Mishnah explicitly state it. The summary picture is: the recursive *chomesh* applies to *gezel* (derived from the plural *chamishitav*), to *terumah* (derived from the Mishnah's explicit statement, supported by the textual manipulation of *ve-yasaf* and *chamishato*), and to *hekdesh* (derived from the *hekesh* of *chamishit kesef erchecha*). It does not apply to *ma'aser sheni*, where neither verse nor Mishnah hints at it and the question was never even raised.

## Original vs. Transferred

### RYBLI Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, וְעַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֵׁנִי אֵין מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes **the** matter **itself. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: For** redemption of **first consecration one adds one-fifth; for** redemption of **second consecration one does not add one-fifth.**

The Gemara now formally introduces the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *al hekdesh rishon mosif chomesh; al hekdesh sheni ein mosif chomesh* — one adds a *chomesh* only upon the original sanctified object; upon transferred *hekdesh* — sanctity that has been conveyed from one object to another — one does not add a *chomesh*. If I sanctify my dresser and it is worth eighty, then redeem it by giving eighty coins to the Temple, those coins have received the *kedushah* by transfer. Were I to then redeem those coins, I would not add a fifth.

### Makdish vs. Metfis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִם הַמַּקְדִּישׁ יִגְאַל אֶת בֵּיתוֹ״. הַמַּקְדִּישׁ, וְלֹא הַמַּתְפִּיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi?** It is as **the verse states: "And if he who consecrated it will redeem his house,** then he shall add one-fifth of the money of your valuation unto it" (Leviticus 27:15), from which it may be inferred that when **he who consecrates** the house redeems it, he adds one-fifth, **but** this is **not** so with regard to **one who associates** an item with an existing sanctity, as in this case, where the sanctity of the one-fifth is derived from its association with the sanctity of the house.

Rava provides the textual basis for Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle. The verse states: *ve-im ha-makdish yigal et beito* — if the one who sanctified it redeems his house. The Gemara reads this as: *ha-makdish ve-lo ha-matfis* — the one who directly sanctified the object, not the one who created *hekdesh* through the mechanism of transfer. When one transfers *kedushah* from one object to another, the second object's sanctity comes not from the direct act of *hakdashah* but from the process of *hatfasah* — the apprehension or conveyance of *kedushah*. The verse's use of *ha-makdish* in the definite sense teaches that the *chomesh* belongs only to the act of direct sanctification.

### Non-Kosher Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״וְאִם בַּבְּהֵמָה הַטְּמֵאָה וּפָדָה בְעֶרְכֶּךָ״, מָה בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה מְיוּחֶדֶת, שֶׁתְּחִילָּתָהּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ וְכוּלָּהּ לַשָּׁמַיִם – וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁתְּחִילָּתָהּ הֶקְדֵּשׁ וְכוּלָּהּ לַשָּׁמַיִם – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **the *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall **taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Elazar.** It is written: **"And if it is of a non-kosher animal, then he shall redeem it according to your valuation"** (Leviticus 27:27). This verse teaches that **just as a non-kosher animal** that was consecrated **is unique** in **that it is** an example of **initial consecration and it is** devoted **entirely to Heaven,** as neither the owner nor anyone else may derive benefit from it after its consecration, **and one** violates the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property **by** using **it** after it was consecrated, **so too,** with regard to **any** item that both undergoes **initial consecration and is** devoted **entirely to Heaven, one** violates the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property **by** using **it** after it was consecrated.

A *tanna* taught a *baraita* before Rabbi Elazar presenting a *derasha* connecting the *chomesh* to the case of a *behemah tme'ah* — a non-kosher animal. The verse in the context of *hekdesh* mentions: *ve-im bi-vhemah ha-tme'ah u-fadah ve-erchecha* — and if it is a non-kosher animal and you redeem it at your valuation. The *baraita* draws an analogy: just as a non-kosher animal is characterized by *techilato hekdesh ve-kulo la-shamayim* — its sanctity is original and its entire value goes to heaven, i.e., to the Temple treasury — so too, anything characterized by original *hekdesh* whose entire value belongs to the Temple is subject to the obligation of *me'ilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְתַנָּא: בִּשְׁלָמָא ״כּוּלָּהּ לַשָּׁמַיִם״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לְהוּ לִבְעָלִים בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ – לֵית בְּהוּ מְעִילָה. אֶלָּא ״תְּחִילַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? תְּחִילַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ הוּא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מְעִילָה, סוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ לֵית בֵּיהּ מְעִילָה?! דִּלְמָא לְעִנְיַן חוֹמֶשׁ קָאָמְרַתְּ, וּכְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said to the *tanna*: Granted,** the statement: **It is** devoted **entirely to Heaven,** serves **to exclude offerings of lesser sanctity,** e.g., peace-offerings. **Since the owners have** a share **in them,** as they may partake of those offerings, **they are not subject to** the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property. **But what** does the mention of **initial consecration** in the *baraita* serve **to exclude?** Is it that **initial consecration is subject to** the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property and **ultimate consecration is not subject to** the prohibition against **misuse** of consecrated property? Even the property consecrated last in a series of redemptions is full-fledged consecrated property. **Perhaps** it is with regard **to** the **matter of** the payment of **one-fifth** that **you are saying** this, **and** it is **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi?** The *tanna* **said to him: Yes, that is** what **I am saying.**

Rabbi Elazar challenges the *baraita*. He accepts that the phrase *kulo la-shamayim* — all to heaven — is intelligible: it distinguishes the non-kosher animal, whose full value goes to the Temple, from *kodshim kalim* such as *shelamim*, where the owners retain a portion of the meat. Since *kodshim kalim* involve a share for the owners, the leniency regarding *me'ilah* might apply. This is a meaningful distinction.

But the phrase *techilat hekdesh* — original sanctity — is puzzling. Does it mean to say that only things originally sanctified by the owner are subject to *me'ilah*, while things sanctified by transfer are not? But this is incorrect as a matter of halachah: even objects that receive *kedushah* through transfer are subject to *me'ilah*. So what is *techilat hekdesh* coming to exclude?

Rabbi Elazar suggests: *de-ima le-inyan chomesh ka-marta ke-Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — perhaps when the *baraita* says *me'ilah* it does not really mean the laws of *me'ilah* at all. The word being used is close in sound to *ma'aleh*, meaning to "elevate" or "add upon." What the *baraita* is actually teaching is the principle of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi about the *chomesh*: the fifth is added only when the sanctity is original, not when it is transferred. The *tanna* confirms: *in, hachi kamina* — yes, that is precisely what I meant.

### Ha-Article Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרָבִינָא: בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה בִּתְחִילַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ אִיתַהּ? בְּאֶמְצַע הֶקְדֵּשׁ, לֵיתַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָהּ בְּסוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos that *baraita*, **Rav Ashi said to Ravina:** Is it so that **a non-kosher animal is** subject **to initial consecration,** but it **is not** subject **to** an **intermediate** stage of **consecration** resulting from redemption? Ravina **said to him:** It is **due to** the fact **that** it **is not** subject **to ultimate consecration.** A non-kosher animal does not ultimately remain consecrated, as it is used neither as an offering nor in the upkeep of the Temple. Instead, it is redeemed and its value is consecrated.

Rav Ashi asks Ravina: the premise that a non-kosher animal has only *techilat hekdesh* and not transferred *kedushah* is questionable. Surely one can transfer *kedushah* to a non-kosher animal — if I sanctify my dresser and then transfer its sanctity to a donkey, the donkey now has *kedushah* by transfer. So why does the *baraita* use the non-kosher animal as representative of original-only *kedushah*?

The response is: *lefi she-einah be-sof hekdesh* — because a non-kosher animal is not the type of object that features in the ultimate disposition of *hekdesh*. A non-kosher animal cannot be offered as a *korban*; it cannot be used as a Temple building beam. It always ends up being redeemed; it never serves as the final vessel for *hekdesh*. It is not a *sof hekdesh* — an end-stage *hekdesh* object.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא: מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא, בְּאֶמְצַע הֶקְדֵּשׁ מִיהָא אִיתַהּ, וְלוֹסֵיף נָמֵי חוֹמֶשׁ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי הוּא כְּסוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ, מָה סוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ אֵינוֹ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ – אַף אֶמְצַע הֶקְדֵּשׁ אֵינוֹ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina:** It **is** subject, **at least, to** an **intermediate** stage of **consecration; and** in that case, **let one add one-fifth as well.** Ravina **said to him:** Its legal status is **like that of ultimate consecration: Just as one does not add one-fifth** with regard to a non-kosher animal in **ultimate consecration,** as that category does not exist in a non-kosher animal, **so too, one does not add one-fifth** in a case of **intermediate consecration.**

Rav Acha mi-Difti presses Ravina further: but if a non-kosher animal can have *kedushah* transferred to it — if it can be a middle stage in the chain of *hekdesh* transfers — does that not suggest that a *chomesh* should apply to it by virtue of being a middle-stage *hekdesh*? The response is: *harei hu ke-sof hekdesh* — even though it can theoretically be a middle stage, in its fundamental character it resembles the end stage, since it is not going to be used directly as *hekdesh*. The end-stage *hekdesh* does not generate a *chomesh*; therefore the middle-stage *hekdesh* analogous to the end stage also does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: מַאי חָזֵית דִּמְדַמֵּית לֵיהּ לְסוֹף הֶקְדֵּשׁ? נְדַמְּיֵיהּ לִתְחִילַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina: What did you see that** led **you to liken** the intermediate stage of consecration **to ultimate consecration? Let us liken it to initial consecration.** Ravina **said to him: It stands to reason that he should liken it to ultimate consecration, as** he thereby derives the *halakha* of an item consecrated **by association with** the sanctity of an item consecrated **by association.**

Rav Zutra the son of Rav Mari pushes back: why compare the middle case to the end? Perhaps compare it to the beginning. The beginning case does involve a *chomesh* — something original that is sanctified does get a fifth when redeemed. The middle case, like the beginning, has something further coming after it in the chain. Why not say that the middle case, like the beginning, should have a *chomesh*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה: לִתְחִילַּת הֶקְדֵּשׁ הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְדַמּוֹיֵי, שֶׁכֵּן דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ אַחֲרָיו קְדוּשָּׁה מִדָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ אַחֲרָיו קְדוּשָּׁה! כִּדְאָמַר רָבָא ״הָעֹלָה״ – עוֹלָה רִאשׁוֹנָה. הָכִי נָמֵי ״הַטְּמֵאָה״ – טְמֵאָה רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **On the contrary, he should have likened it to initial consecration,** as he thereby derives the *halakha* of **an item after which there is** another stage of **sanctity,** the intermediate stage of consecration, **from an item after which there is** another stage of **sanctity.** The Gemara answers: It is **as Rava says: "The burnt-offering"** (Leviticus 6:2), employing the definite article, indicates that the reference is to the **first burnt-offering. So too,** when it is written: **The non-kosher** animal, the reference is to the **initial** consecration of the **non-kosher** animal, not the intermediate stage of consecration.

The Gemara initially offers the response that the end-stage comparison is more apt because both the middle and the end share the property of *nitpeset* — having *kedushah* received through transfer — whereas the beginning is unique in representing original *kedushah*. But this line of reasoning is then set aside.

Ultimately the Gemara accepts that the resolution does not lie in reasoning about beginning, middle, and end stages at all. Rather, as Rava teaches, it lies in the *hei* of the definite article. In the verse from *Tzav* — *hi ha-olah al mokdah* — the word *ha-olah* refers to the specific *olah* mentioned earlier in the passage, namely the *olat tamid*, and from this we learn that the *hei* can indicate "the first" or "the original." Similarly, when the verse says *ve-im bi-vhemah ha-tme'ah* — and if with a non-kosher animal — the *hei* of *ha-tme'ah* indicates: the first, the original *behemah tme'ah* that was sanctified, not one to which *kedushah* was subsequently transferred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: ״פָּרָה זוֹ תַּחַת פָּרָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, ״טַלִּית זוֹ תַּחַת טַלִּית שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ – הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹ פָּדוּי, וְיַד הֶקְדֵּשׁ עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi:** If one said that **this cow is in place of** that **cow,** which **belongs to** the **Temple** treasury, or **this garment is in place of that garment,** which **belongs to** the **Temple** treasury, **his consecrated** property **is redeemed, and the** treasurer of **consecrated** property **is at an advantage.** If the replacement item is equal to or more valuable than the original item, it belongs to the treasurer, and if it is less valuable, the one who consecrated it must pay the difference.

The verse's use of the definite article thus teaches that the *chomesh* applies only to the originally sanctified object, not to one that received *kedushah* by transfer. This is the derivation underlying Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle: *al hekdesh rishon mosif chomesh; al hekdesh sheni ein mosif chomesh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״פָּרָה זוֹ בְּחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים תַּחַת פָּרָה שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, ״טַלִּית זוֹ בְּחָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים תַּחַת טַלִּית שֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ – הֶקְדֵּשׁוֹ פָּדוּי. עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ רִאשׁוֹן מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ שֵׁנִי אֵין מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** If he said: **This cow** valued **at five *sela* is in place of this cow,** which **belongs to** the **Temple** treasury, or: **This garment** valued **at five *sela* is instead of this garment,** which **belongs to** the **Temple** treasury, **his consecrated** property **is redeemed.** Even if the second consecrated item is more valuable, it is not considered a consecration done in error. He will have to pay the difference. **He adds one-fifth** when redeeming the item that was the **initial consecration,** but when redeeming the item **for** which the initial consecration was redeemed, the **second consecration, he does not add one-fifth.**
