# Bava Metzia 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ma'aser Below Prutah

### Ona'ah & Coins

The present chapter of the Gemara has been treating questions of *ona'ah* — specifically the degree to which a coin may be physically deficient and still retain its face value for commercial purposes. The Mishnah touched on *ma'aser sheni* in this context, noting that even a somewhat deficient coin may be used to redeem *ma'aser sheni*. This led the Gemara into a sustained discussion of what it means to redeem *ma'aser sheni* whose value is less than a *prutah*.

The background is as follows. When one redeems *ma'aser sheni* outside of Yerushalayim, one transfers the sanctity of the food onto money and then uses that money in Yerushalayim to purchase food eaten in a state of *kedushah*. The minimum meaningful unit of monetary value recognized by halachah is a *prutah*. If the *ma'aser sheni* one wishes to redeem does not reach that threshold, the question arises whether a valid act of *pidyon* — redemption — can be performed at all. Hizkiyah taught that if a coin has already been used in redemption and retains residual value from a previous transaction, one may redeem even *ma'aser sheni* worth less than a *prutah* onto that coin. The question under examination here concerns a fresh coin — one that has not yet been used in any redemption — when the *ma'aser sheni* itself is worth less than a *prutah*.

### Rav vs. Rav Asi

The Gemara now cites the *pasuk*: *Im ga'ol yigal ish mi-ma'asro* — "if a man redeems from his *maaser*" — and derives from the phrase *mi-ma'asro*, "from his *maaser*" and not "from all his *maaser*," that there are circumstances in which redemption is not possible. The teaching states: *prat le-ma'aser sheni she-ein bo shaveh prutah* — this comes to exclude *ma'aser sheni* that is not worth a *prutah*, which lacks the minimum value necessary to constitute a valid redemptive act. Of course, as noted, Hizkiyah's ruling allows redemption even below a *prutah* when the coin already bears the imprint of a prior redemption and carries residual value.

An *amoraic* dispute is then recorded. Rav says the problem is *ein bo* — the *ma'aser sheni* itself does not contain a *prutah*'s worth of value. Rav Asi, by contrast, says *ein be-chomsho* — the difficulty arises even when the *ma'aser sheni* does contain a *prutah*'s worth, but its *chomesh* does not. When one redeems one's own *ma'aser sheni*, one is obligated to add a fifth. This fifth, as the Gemara will explain shortly, is a fifth of the final total rather than of the base — what we might call a quarter of the starting value. If the food is worth four *prutot*, the *chomesh* added is one *prutah*, bringing the total to five, and that one *prutah* constitutes a fifth of the whole. But if the *ma'aser sheni* is worth less than four *prutot*, then the *chomesh* — the increment being added — does not itself amount to a *prutah*, and Rav Asi holds that this too disqualifies the redemption. The addition of the *chomesh* is treated, in some sense, as its own discrete redemptive act, requiring its own minimum value. This same dispute is replicated between Rabbi Yochanan, who holds *ein bo*, and Resh Lakish, who holds *ein be-chomsho*.

### New vs. Used Money

The Gemara challenges these positions from a *baraita*: *ma'aser sheni she-ein bo shaveh prutah, dayo she-yomar hu ve-chomsho mechulalin al ma'ot rishonim* — if the *ma'aser sheni* is not worth a *prutah*, it is sufficient for one to declare that it and its *chomesh* are redeemed upon the original money, i.e., coins previously used in redemption. This is Hizkiyah's ruling applied in practice.

The Gemara then analyzes how this *baraita* bears on the dispute. If one holds like Rav Asi — that the problem arises when the *chomesh* lacks a *prutah* — then the word *dayo*, "it is sufficient," makes good sense. The general rule requires the *chomesh* to be worth a *prutah*, but in the case of money already used in redemption, as long as the *ma'aser* itself is worth a *prutah*, it is enough even if the *chomesh* is not, because something has already been accomplished — the *ma'aser* has crossed the minimum threshold. The *dayo* therefore indicates that one has achieved a partial satisfaction of the requirement.

But if one holds like Rav — that the issue is simply whether the *ma'aser sheni* itself reaches a *prutah* — then the word *dayo* is difficult. The *baraita* should have stated simply that one may redeem *ma'aser sheni* worth less than a *prutah* upon previously used money. The word *dayo*, implying that something short of ideal is being permitted, does not fit naturally. The Gemara acknowledges: *kashya* — this remains difficult for the position of Rav. Nevertheless, the essential halachic conclusion is that once money has already been used in redemption, everyone agrees that redemption may proceed; the dispute concerns only fresh, previously unused money, and whether the threshold requirement applies to the *ma'aser* itself or to the *chomesh* it generates.

## Chomesh Mi-Legav/Levar

### How Fifth Calculated

Having established the basic framework of the *chomesh*, the Gemara now asks a foundational question: *chumsha mi-legav o chumsha mi-lebar* — is the fifth calculated from the inside or from the outside? That is, does one take a fifth of the base value — *mi-legav* — or does one add an amount such that after the addition the increment constitutes a fifth of the total — *mi-lebar*? If *mi-legav*, then on something worth twenty one adds four, since four is a fifth of twenty. If *mi-lebar*, then on something worth twenty one adds five, since five is a fifth of the resulting total of twenty-five. The latter formulation means that what the Torah calls a "fifth" is in practical terms a quarter of the starting value.

### Be'alim Redemption

Ravina resolves the question by citing a *baraita* concerning the redemption of *hekdesh*. When a sanctified object is being redeemed and the *be'alim* — the original owners who sanctified it — wish to reclaim it, they enjoy the right of first refusal over other bidders because they are required to add a *chomesh*. The *baraita* presents a series of scenarios: the *be'alim* offer twenty, another person offers twenty. The *be'alim* take precedence because they add a *chomesh*. If a stranger then offers twenty-one, the *be'alim* pay twenty-six — their base of twenty plus five as the *chomesh*, plus the additional one to match the stranger's bid.

What is decisive is that the *chomesh* on a base of twenty is five, not four. That demonstrates conclusively that the fifth is *mi-lebar*: one adds an amount equal to a quarter of the starting value, so that the final result is five parts of which the added increment is one — a fifth of the total. The *baraita* continues through progressive examples: a stranger bids twenty-two and the *be'alim* pay twenty-seven; twenty-three and the *be'alim* pay twenty-eight; twenty-four and the *be'alim* pay twenty-nine; twenty-five and the *be'alim* pay thirty. The owners do not pay a *chomesh* on the stranger's bid — only on their own base of twenty. They pay their *chomesh* of five, and then add whatever premium the competing bid introduced. The Gemara notes a difficulty in the final case — when the stranger bids twenty-five, which equals the *be'alim*'s total of twenty plus five — but refers the reader to the discussion in tractate *Erchin* where that case is addressed.

The conclusion from all this is clear: *shma mina chomesh mi-lebar* — the *chomesh* of the Torah is calculated from the outside, i.e., it is a fifth of the total after addition, which amounts to a quarter of the starting value.

The Gemara confirms that this point was itself a *tannaic* dispute: the verse states *ve-yasaf chamishito alav* — he shall add its fifth upon it. Rabbi Yoshiya reads this as: together with its fifth, the total is five — meaning you start with four, add one, and arrive at five. This is *mi-lebar*. Rabbi Yonatan, however, reads *chamishito* as a fifth of the principal — *mi-legav*. The established halachah, as confirmed by the *baraita* about the *be'alim*, follows Rabbi Yoshiya's reading: the fifth is *mi-lebar*.

## Fifth Me'akev?

### Four + One or Four on Fi…

The Gemara now turns to a separate and fundamental question: *chomesh me'akev o ein chomesh me'akev* — does failure to add the fifth invalidate the *pidyon*, the act of redemption? If one redeems *ma'aser sheni* worth twenty outside of Yerushalayim by transferring its sanctity onto twenty coins without adding the requisite five additional coins, has the *ma'aser sheni* in fact been redeemed, such that one may now eat it outside of Yerushalayim?

The conceptual question is framed with precision. One possibility is that the Torah's meaning is: you redeem the four on four — the redemption is transacted at the food's value — and then separately and additionally you add a fifth. Under this reading, the act of redemption and the obligation to add a *chomesh* are distinct. The *pidyon* is complete when you transfer the food's value onto money of equivalent worth; the *chomesh* is an additional financial obligation but does not define or constitute the redemptive act itself. The consequence would be: *alma chomesh lo me'akev* — the fifth does not prevent the redemption from taking effect.

The alternative possibility is that the Torah is telling you to redeem four on five — that is, the proper redemption of an item worth four requires the payment of five. Under this reading, tendering only four is simply not a redemption at the correct value, and the *pidyon* accordingly fails. The *chomesh* is not an add-on but an integral component of the redemptive transaction itself.

### Demai Proof

Ravina offers a proof from the laws of *demai*. *Demai* refers to produce purchased from an *am ha-aretz* whose proper separation of *ma'asrot* is uncertain. At the Torah level, one may assume the separations were performed; rabbinically, one must treat the produce as if they were not and separate again. The Mishnah teaches: *ha-demai ein lo chomesh ve-ein lo bi'ur* — *demai* does not require the addition of a *chomesh* and is not subject to *bi'ur*, the obligation to remove all *ma'aser* from one's home by a certain point in the sabbatical cycle. Yet the *keren* — the principal redemption — does apply. One must still redeem the *ma'aser sheni* of *demai*, even though one omits the *chomesh*.

The Gemara asks: what is the rationale for this distinction? Why would the rabbis require the *keren* but not the *chomesh* for *demai*? The answer is: *keren de-me'akev be-de'oraita ita be-derabbanan; chomesh de-lo me'akev be-de'oraita leita be-derabbanan* — the principal is *me'akev* at the Torah level, and therefore the rabbis preserved its requirement even for *demai*; the *chomesh* is not *me'akev* at the Torah level, and therefore the rabbis did not extend its requirement to *demai*. The logic of the rabbinic extension tracks the Torah-level significance of each component. If one accepts this reasoning, it constitutes a strong proof that the *chomesh* is fundamentally not *me'akev* — its absence does not invalidate the *pidyon* at the Torah level.

### Rabbi Eliezer vs. Yehosh…

The Gemara raises the possibility that this issue is in fact a *tannaic* dispute. A *baraita* states: if one gave the *keren* but not the *chomesh* — *natan et ha-keren ve-lo natan et ha-chomesh* — Rabbi Eliezer says *yochal*, one may eat the *ma'aser sheni*, while Rabbi Yehoshua says *lo yochal*, one may not eat it. Rabbi Yehudah ha-Nasi then offers a split ruling: the position of Rabbi Eliezer appears correct on Shabbat, while the position of Rabbi Yehoshua appears correct on a weekday.

The Gemara reads Rabbi's split ruling as revealing something about the underlying dispute. Since Rabbi says Rabbi Eliezer's view applies on Shabbat and Rabbi Yehoshua's applies on a weekday, this implies that each Tanna holds his position universally — Rabbi Eliezer allows eating regardless of the day, and Rabbi Yehoshua prohibits it regardless of the day — and Rabbi is mediating between them by applying each position to a different context. On the face of it, this appears to reflect a substantive disagreement: Rabbi Eliezer holds the *chomesh* is not *me'akev*, while Rabbi Yehoshua holds that it is.

## Fifth Me'akev? (II)

### Rav Pappa Resolves

Rav Pappa, however, rejects the conclusion that this is a dispute about the Torah-level status of the *chomesh*. He proposes: *de-kulei alma chomesh lo me'akev* — everyone agrees that at the Torah level the *chomesh* does not invalidate the *pidyon*. This is clearly the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who permits eating even without the *chomesh*. But even Rabbi Yehoshua, Rav Pappa argues, need not hold that the *chomesh* is *me'akev* at the Torah level. Rather, the dispute is whether we impose a rabbinic deterrent — *chashinan le-fshiyuta* — whether we are concerned that a person will be negligent. If there are no consequences to failing to add the *chomesh* and one can simply eat the *ma'aser sheni* after a *pidyon* without the *chomesh*, one will perpetually defer this obligation and never fulfill it. Rabbi Yehoshua therefore says we do not permit eating until the *chomesh* has been paid, not because the *pidyon* failed *de'oraita*, but as a rabbinic safeguard to ensure compliance.

Rabbi Eliezer, in contrast, holds *lo chashinan le-fshiyuta* — we are not concerned about negligence, and so he allows eating immediately.

This interpretation harmonizes elegantly with Rabbi's mediation. The concern for negligence is not absolute; it is a secondary, utilitarian consideration that can be overridden by competing concerns. On Shabbat, where the need for food is pressing and eating is a *mitzvah* dimension of the day — *u-kerasa le-Shabbat oneg* — Rabbi rules that we do not impose the rabbinic deterrent and the person may eat. On a weekday, where no such countervailing consideration exists, Rabbi endorses the deterrent of Rabbi Yehoshua. This works perfectly if the question is one of rabbinic policy, not *de'oraita* validity.

### Hekdesh & Shabbat

Rabbi Yochanan then introduces an important qualification. He states that everyone agrees — *hakol modim* — that when one has redeemed *hekdesh* and the *gizbarim*, the Temple treasurers, will pursue the unpaid *chomesh* in the marketplace, the *pidyon* is effective even without the *chomesh* having been paid. The rationale is that where there exists an institutional creditor who will enforce collection, the rabbinic concern for negligence does not arise. The person cannot simply forget or defer — the Temple treasury will ensure payment. Consequently, even Rabbi Yehoshua would not impose the deterrent in such a case.

The Gemara challenges this from a different *baraita* where Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* dispute a case that seems to involve *hekdesh*, and Rabbi again splits the vote — this time ruling that Rabbi Eliezer's permissive view makes sense for *hekdesh* and the *Chachamim*'s restrictive view makes sense for *ma'aser*. The Gemara concludes from Rabbi's split that each disputant actually holds his position across both *hekdesh* and *ma'aser*, and Rabbi is distinguishing between the two for policy reasons — not that everyone agrees by *hekdesh*. The original statement of Rabbi Yochanan must therefore be read more narrowly: it applies specifically to the combined case where *both* the considerations of *hekdesh* (institutional enforcement) *and* Shabbat are present simultaneously. *Hakol modim be-Shabbat be-hekdesh she-chilel* — everyone agrees that if one redeemed *hekdesh* on Shabbat and failed to pay the *chomesh*, the redemption stands: first because there is institutional enforcement, and second because of the honor of Shabbat and the desire to enable one to enjoy food on the holy day.

## Ma'aser Below Prutah (II)

### Fifth Not Me'akev

Before closing this topic, a *Tosafot* at the top of 54b raises a subtle question worth noting. The suggestion that the *chomesh* is *me'akev* rests on the premise that the Torah requires redeeming four on five — i.e., the *pidyon* is only valid when transacted at the total amount including the *chomesh*. But *Tosafot* points out that even an undervalued redemption generally works *bedieved* — if one redeems *hekdesh* worth a hundred on a penny, the *pidyon* is valid after the fact. If so, even if the Torah envisions redeeming four on five, why should paying only four invalidate it, given that an underpayment generally suffices *bedieved*?

*Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing between two types of shortfall. If one explicitly declares, "this item and its *chomesh* are hereby redeemed on this coin" — even if the coin's value is less than the item's value — the *pidyon* works *bedieved* because one has framed the transaction as a deliberate, if undervalued, redemption. But if one says, "I am redeeming this four on four," without mentioning the *chomesh* at all, one is not attempting an undervalued redemption — one is attempting a *full* redemption, but doing so incorrectly, without meeting the Torah's requirement that four be redeemed on five. That formulation does not work even *bedieved*, because the basis of the transaction is wrong. The *chomesh* must be part of the declared redemptive framework.

## Chomesh K'Moto

### Fifth Properties

Having established the basic rules of the *chomesh*, the Gemara proceeds to a series of questions about the nature of the *chomesh* obligation and whether it mirrors the properties of the underlying *hekdesh* or *ma'aser*. Rami bar Chama introduces the inquiry by noting an established rule: *hekdesh eino mitchallel al karka* — *hekdesh* cannot be redeemed onto land; the Torah requires payment in money, as derived from the verse *ve-natan ha-kesef ve-kam lo* — one gives money and it is thereby established. The *chomesh* must follow form: *chumsho mahu she-yischalel al karka* — can the *chomesh* be redeemed onto land, or does it too require chattel?

Similarly: *terumah einah mishtalemet ela min ha-chullin* — if a *zar* eats *terumah* inadvertently, he must repay with non-consecrated food that thereupon becomes *terumah*, as derived from the verse *ve-natan la-kohen et ha-kodesh* — one gives the *kohen* something capable of becoming holy. The question is: *chumsho mahu she-yishtalem shelo min ha-chullin* — must the *chomesh* paid alongside that repayment also be food capable of becoming *terumah*, or may it be given in a different form entirely?

And further: *ma'aser ein mitchallel al asimon* — *ma'aser sheni* cannot be redeemed onto an *asimon*, an unmarked metal blank that bears no stamped image, as derived from *ve-tzarta ha-kesef be-yadecha* — you shall bind the money in your hand, which requires a properly minted coin bearing an image. The question again is: *chumsho mahu she-yischalel al asimon* — may the *chomesh* be paid with an *asimon*, or does it require a proper stamped coin just as the principal does?

### Rava's Drasha

These questions were deliberated and eventually reached Rava. He resolved all of them together with a single textual argument. Every *pasuk* in the Torah dealing with the *chomesh* uses the formulation *ve-asaf chamishito alav* — he shall add its fifth upon it. The word *alav*, "upon it," teaches: *le-rabbos chomsho ke-moto* — to include that the *chomesh* must be like the object itself. The *chomesh* must replicate the properties of the principal. If the principal must be redeemed on money, so must the *chomesh*. If the principal must be repaid in food capable of becoming *terumah*, so must the *chomesh*. If the principal must be redeemed on a stamped coin, so must the *chomesh*. The *chomesh* tracks the nature of the original obligation in every respect.

*Tosafot* notes in this connection that this *chomsho ke-moto* principle does not make the *chomesh* strictly *me'akev* — it defines the proper form of the *chomesh*, not a condition for the validity of the *pidyon* itself.

### Terumah Proof

Ravina offers an independent proof for the same principle from a *mishnah* concerning the repayment of *terumah*. The *mishnah* teaches: if someone stole *terumah* but did not eat it, he pays double — *tashlumei kefel* — but the double is calculated at the market value of *terumah*, which is lower than the value of ordinary produce because *terumah* may only be consumed by *kohanim* in a state of *taharah*. If, however, the thief ate the *terumah*, the repayment is more complex: he owes *shenei karnayim ve-chomesh* — two principal amounts and a *chomesh*. Specifically, the *keren* and *chomesh* are paid from *chullin* food that thereby becomes *terumah*, while the *keren* for the *kefel* is paid as a monetary assessment at *terumah* market value.

What is significant here is that the *chomesh* is paid in kind — in food — just as the principal is paid in food. The *chomesh* follows the nature of the original object. This is the proof: *shma mina chomesh ke-moto* — the *chomesh* must be like the original. The proof from *terumah* supports Rava's general principle derived from the word *alav*, and presumably what is true of *terumah* is equally true of the other categories.

## Fifth of Fifth

### Gezel Case

The Gemara now turns to the question of whether the obligation to add a *chomesh* applies recursively — that is, whether one may ever owe a *chomesh* on a *chomesh*. Rava introduces the discussion by citing the verse in the context of *gezel*. When one swears falsely that one has not stolen or defrauded someone and then admits the oath was false, one must return the principal and add a fifth: *ve-chamishitav yosef alav* — with his fifths he shall add upon it. The word *chamishitav* is in the plural — "his fifths" — indicating that the obligation of adding a fifth may compound.

The Mishnah cited provides the mechanism: if one gave back the *keren* but then swore falsely regarding the *chomesh* and subsequently admitted it, one now owes a *chomesh* on that *chomesh*. If one then swears falsely on the second *chomesh* and admits it, one owes a *chomesh* on the *chomesh* of the *chomesh*. This process continues — *ad she-yisma'et ha-keren pachos mi-shaveh prutah* — until the amount being sworn upon falls below a *prutah*. As long as each successive amount upon which one takes a false oath exceeds a *prutah*, the cycle of repayment and *chomesh* continues indefinitely. The plural *chamishitav* in the verse explicitly licenses this recursive application of the *chomesh* in the context of *gezel*.

### Terumah Case

In the case of *terumah*, the relevant verse uses the singular: *ve-yasaf chamishito alav* — he shall add its fifth upon it. The plural is absent. One might have thought that the *chomesh* on a *chomesh* therefore does not apply to *terumah*. Nevertheless, the Mishnah teaches explicitly: *ha-ochel terumah be-shogeg meshalem keren ve-chomesh* — one who eats *terumah* inadvertently repays the principal and the fifth. And the Mishnah continues: *va-chumsha de-chumsha* — and a fifth of a fifth. If one then ate the food one was repaying as the *chomesh*, one owes a further fifth on that. Thus, even without a plural form in the verse, the Mishnah teaches that the *chomesh* of a *chomesh* applies in *terumah*.

### Ma'aser No Fifth²

By contrast, with respect to *ma'aser sheni*, neither the verses nor any Mishnah suggests the existence of a *chomesh* on a *chomesh*. The verse says *mi-ma'asro* — from his *maaser* — which led to the conclusion that there are circumstances where redemption cannot occur, but nothing in the text hints at recursive fifths. The Gemara states plainly: *ve-lo ibaya iba'ya lan* — it was never even raised as a question. The silence of both the scriptural sources and the Mishnaic tradition on this point makes clear that no *chomesh* on a *chomesh* applies to *ma'aser sheni*. The recursive obligation arises only from the original food, not from the money onto which it was redeemed. Once the *ma'aser sheni* has been redeemed onto money and the *chomesh* added, there is no further layer of fifths if one subsequently wishes to transfer that money.

### Hekdesh Question

For *hekdesh*, the picture is more complex. The verse reads: *ve-im ha-makdish yigal et beito ve-yasaf chamishit kesef erchecha alav* — if the one who sanctified it redeems his house, he shall add a fifth of the valuation money upon it. The word *chamishit* is in the singular, not the plural. Nor does the Mishnah say *chumsho* and *chumsha de-chumsho* as it does with *terumah*. The Mishnah regarding *hekdesh* says only: *ha-podeh hekdesh mosif chomesh* — one who redeems *hekdesh* adds a fifth. A fifth — singular — with no mention of a fifth of a fifth.

The question therefore arises: what is the basis for saying a *chomesh* on a *chomesh* applies by *terumah*, and does that basis exist by *hekdesh* as well? The Gemara initially considers whether the basis for the recursive *chomesh* in *terumah* might be the word *ve-yasaf* — "and he shall add" — read as indicating an ongoing, cumulative addition. Since the same word *ve-yasaf* appears in the *hekdesh* verse, perhaps the same implication carries over. Alternatively, perhaps the basis for *terumah*'s recursive *chomesh* is a more technical textual maneuver: the extra *vav* of *ve-yasaf* is taken from the root and appended to the end of *chamishato*, transforming it from *chamishato* (singular, "its fifth") to *chamishitav* (plural, "its fifths"). By this reading, it is the *vav* of *ve-yasaf* that generates the plural, and only by *terumah* does the word *chamishato* appear to which this *vav* can be attached. By *hekdesh*, the verse says *chamishit* rather than *chamishato*, and even appending the extra *vav* would produce only *chamishito* — still a singular "its fifth" — not a meaningful plural. Under this reading, the basis for a recursive *chomesh* does not transfer from *terumah* to *hekdesh*.

Before resolving this textual question, the Gemara considers whether there is a separate, threshold reason why the *chomesh* on a *chomesh* might not apply to *hekdesh* — namely, the principle of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that the *chomesh* is added only to the original sanctified object, not to transferred *kedushah*. If the *chomesh* itself represents new *kedushah* added to the system, one might argue that it falls into a category of transferred *kedushah* and therefore no additional fifth applies. But Rava addresses this below.

### Hekdesh Resolution

The resolution is provided by Rav Tivumi in the name of Abaye, citing the verse: *ve-yasaf chamishit kesef erchecha* — he shall add a fifth of the money of your valuation. What is striking here is that the verse places the words for "fifth" and "principal" in immediate proximity: *chamishit kesef erchecha* — a fifth alongside the valuation money. The Gemara reads this as a *hekesh* — an analogy: *mah kesef ercho mosif chomesh, af kesef chomsho nami mosif chomesh* — just as one adds a fifth upon the principal valuation, so too one adds a fifth upon the *chomesh* itself. The structural juxtaposition of the fifth and the principal amount in the same phrase indicates that the fifth is to be treated like the principal, and whatever obligation attaches to the principal — including the recursive addition of a fifth — attaches equally to the *chomesh*.

Thus, the *chomesh* of *chomesh* applies to *hekdesh* through this *hekesh*, even though neither the plural form of the word nor the Mishnah explicitly state it. The summary picture is: the recursive *chomesh* applies to *gezel* (derived from the plural *chamishitav*), to *terumah* (derived from the Mishnah's explicit statement, supported by the textual manipulation of *ve-yasaf* and *chamishato*), and to *hekdesh* (derived from the *hekesh* of *chamishit kesef erchecha*). It does not apply to *ma'aser sheni*, where neither verse nor Mishnah hints at it and the question was never even raised.

## Original vs. Transferred

### RYBLI Position

The Gemara now formally introduces the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *al hekdesh rishon mosif chomesh; al hekdesh sheni ein mosif chomesh* — one adds a *chomesh* only upon the original sanctified object; upon transferred *hekdesh* — sanctity that has been conveyed from one object to another — one does not add a *chomesh*. If I sanctify my dresser and it is worth eighty, then redeem it by giving eighty coins to the Temple, those coins have received the *kedushah* by transfer. Were I to then redeem those coins, I would not add a fifth.

### Makdish vs. Metfis

Rava provides the textual basis for Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle. The verse states: *ve-im ha-makdish yigal et beito* — if the one who sanctified it redeems his house. The Gemara reads this as: *ha-makdish ve-lo ha-matfis* — the one who directly sanctified the object, not the one who created *hekdesh* through the mechanism of transfer. When one transfers *kedushah* from one object to another, the second object's sanctity comes not from the direct act of *hakdashah* but from the process of *hatfasah* — the apprehension or conveyance of *kedushah*. The verse's use of *ha-makdish* in the definite sense teaches that the *chomesh* belongs only to the act of direct sanctification.

### Non-Kosher Animal

A *tanna* taught a *baraita* before Rabbi Elazar presenting a *derasha* connecting the *chomesh* to the case of a *behemah teme'ah* — a non-kosher animal. The verse in the context of *hekdesh* mentions: *ve-im bi-vhemah ha-tme'ah u-fadah ve-erchecha* — and if it is a non-kosher animal and you redeem it at your valuation. The *baraita* draws an analogy: just as a non-kosher animal is characterized by *techilato hekdesh ve-kulo la-shamayim* — its sanctity is original and its entire value goes to heaven, i.e., to the Temple treasury — so too, anything characterized by original *hekdesh* whose entire value belongs to the Temple is subject to the obligation of *me'ilah*.

Rabbi Elazar challenges the *baraita*. He accepts that the phrase *kulo la-shamayim* — all to heaven — is intelligible: it distinguishes the non-kosher animal, whose full value goes to the Temple, from *kodshim kalim* such as *shelamim*, where the owners retain a portion of the meat. Since *kodshim kalim* involve a share for the owners, the leniency regarding *me'ilah* might apply. This is a meaningful distinction.

But the phrase *techilat hekdesh* — original sanctity — is puzzling. Does it mean to say that only things originally sanctified by the owner are subject to *me'ilah*, while things sanctified by transfer are not? But this is incorrect as a matter of halachah: even objects that receive *kedushah* through transfer are subject to *me'ilah*. So what is *techilat hekdesh* coming to exclude?

Rabbi Elazar suggests: *de-ima le-inyan chomesh ka-marta ke-Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — perhaps when the *baraita* says *me'ilah* it does not really mean the laws of *me'ilah* at all. The word being used is close in sound to *ma'aleh*, meaning to "elevate" or "add upon." What the *baraita* is actually teaching is the principle of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi about the *chomesh*: the fifth is added only when the sanctity is original, not when it is transferred. The *tanna* confirms: *in, hachi kamina* — yes, that is precisely what I meant.

### Ha-Article Drasha

The discussion continues with further attempts to locate the precise textual derivation. Rav Ashi asks Ravina: the premise that a non-kosher animal has only *techilat hekdesh* and not transferred *kedushah* is questionable. Surely one can transfer *kedushah* to a non-kosher animal — if I sanctify my dresser and then transfer its sanctity to a donkey, the donkey now has *kedushah* by transfer. So why does the *baraita* use the non-kosher animal as representative of original-only *kedushah*?

The response is: *lefi she-einah be-sof hekdesh* — because a non-kosher animal is not the type of object that features in the ultimate disposition of *hekdesh*. A non-kosher animal cannot be offered as a *korban*; it cannot be used as a Temple building beam. It always ends up being redeemed; it never serves as the final vessel for *hekdesh*. It is not a *sof hekdesh* — an end-stage *hekdesh* object.

Rav Acha mi-Difti presses Ravina further: but if a non-kosher animal can have *kedushah* transferred to it — if it can be a middle stage in the chain of *hekdesh* transfers — does that not suggest that a *chomesh* should apply to it by virtue of being a middle-stage *hekdesh*? The response is: *harei hu ke-sof hekdesh* — even though it can theoretically be a middle stage, in its fundamental character it resembles the end stage, since it is not going to be used directly as *hekdesh*. The end-stage *hekdesh* does not generate a *chomesh*; therefore the middle-stage *hekdesh* analogous to the end stage also does not.

Rav Zutra the son of Rav Mari pushes back: why compare the middle case to the end? Perhaps compare it to the beginning. The beginning case does involve a *chomesh* — something original that is sanctified does get a fifth when redeemed. The middle case, like the beginning, has something further coming after it in the chain. Why not say that the middle case, like the beginning, should have a *chomesh*?

The Gemara initially offers the response that the end-stage comparison is more apt because both the middle and the end share the property of *nitpeset* — having *kedushah* received through transfer — whereas the beginning is unique in representing original *kedushah*. But this line of reasoning is then set aside.

Ultimately the Gemara accepts that the resolution does not lie in reasoning about beginning, middle, and end stages at all. Rather, as Rava teaches, it lies in the *hei* of the definite article. In the verse from *Tzav* — *hi ha-olah al mokdah* — the word *ha-olah* refers to the specific *olah* mentioned earlier in the passage, namely the *olat tamid*, and from this we learn that the *hei* can indicate "the first" or "the original." Similarly, when the verse says *ve-im bi-vhemah ha-tme'ah* — and if with a non-kosher animal — the *hei* of *ha-tme'ah* indicates: the first, the original *behemah tme'ah* that was sanctified, not one to which *kedushah* was subsequently transferred. The verse's use of the definite article thus teaches that the *chomesh* applies only to the originally sanctified object, not to one that received *kedushah* by transfer from another. This is the derivation underlying Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's principle: *al hekdesh rishon mosif chomesh; al hekdesh sheni ein mosif chomesh*.
