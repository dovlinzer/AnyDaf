# Menachot 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mechameitz Achar

### Defect Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בִּמְחַמֵּץ אַחַר מְחַמֵּץ שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב, דִּכְתִיב ״לֹא תֵעָשֶׂה חָמֵץ״ וְ״לֹא תֵאָפֶה חָמֵץ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses similar cases, including examples involving meal offerings. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All** of the Sages who disagree as to whether one may let the blood of a firstborn animal whose blood circulation is constricted **concede that one who leavens** a meal offering **after** another had already **leavened** it is **liable** to receive lashes for the additional leavening, **as it is written:** "No meal offering that you shall bring to the Lord **shall be made with leaven"** (Leviticus 2:11), **and** it is also stated: **"It shall not be baked with leaven"** (Leviticus 6:10). This indicates that one is liable for every act of leavening performed on a meal offering.

The daf proper begins at the very bottom of 56b. The Gemara had just introduced a debate between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Meir concerning whether one who creates a *mum* in an animal that already possesses a *mum* has transgressed. The conceptual crux of that dispute is how to define the prohibition: is it aimed at the act of making the animal imperfect, in which case a pre-existing *mum* removes any new violation, or is the prohibition focused on the *mum* itself as a state, such that every subsequent act remains problematic? The Gemara drew parallels between that case and other legal contexts where the same question arises, and specifically applied it to *mechameitz achar mechameitz* — the principle that when a *mincha* has already become *chametz*, those who perform subsequent stages in its preparation continue to transgress, not because they caused the leavening, but because they are processing the offering *as chametz*. The prohibition is not merely allowing it to become *chametz* — that can only happen once — but performing the acts of making it into a *mincha* while it exists as *chametz*.

### Sourdough Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הִנִּיחַ שְׂאוֹר עַל גַּבֵּי עִיסָּה, וְהָלַךְ וְיָשַׁב לוֹ, וְנִתְחַמְּצָה מֵאֵלֶיהָ – חַיָּיב עָלֶיהָ כְּמַעֲשֵׂה שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss the leavening of a meal offering. **Rabbi Ami says:** If one **placed leaven,** i.e., dough that has leavened to such an extent that it is no longer used as food but as a leavening agent for other dough, **on top of** the **dough** of a meal offering, **and he went and sat himself** down to wait, meaning that he performed no other action, **and** the dough then **leavened of its own** accord, he is **liable** to receive lashes **for it.** This is **similar to** performing a prohibited **action on Shabbat.**

Rabbi Ami then poses a more nuanced case, stated in the Gemara four lines from the bottom of 56b: *hiniach se'or al gabei issa vehalach veyashav lo venitchamtza* — he placed sourdough on top of the dough, walked away, sat down, and the dough became *chametz* on its own. This is a deliberate and instructive formulation. The case is not one of passive negligence — simply standing by while the dough ferments — because then one could argue there is no *ma'aseh* at all, and in any event it would be difficult to attribute the leavening specifically to this person. Nor is it the case of kneading *chametz* dough, which would fall cleanly into the category of performing a stage of *mincha* preparation as *chametz*. Rather, the person actively introduced sourdough into the dough and then walked away without kneading it in, allowing the *chametz* to develop without any further personal involvement.

The Gemara rules: *chayav aleiha k'maaseh Shabbos* — he is liable, just as one is liable under the laws of Shabbat. The Shabbat comparison will be unpacked shortly, but its immediate import is this: just as baking on Shabbat involves an active initiation — placing food in the oven — followed by a passive completion, so too here. One need not actively be processing the dough at the moment it becomes *chametz* in order to bear liability for having caused that transformation.

A preliminary question arises here: if the dough has not yet had any *avodah* performed upon it, has it even been designated as a *mincha* at all? The answer is that all of this discussion presupposes that the flour has already acquired *kedushas kli* — it has been placed in a sanctified vessel and thus possesses *kedushas mincha*. Once that *kedusha* exists, the prohibition against *chametz* attaches. If the sourdough was introduced before the dough was placed in a *kli shares*, no transgression has occurred. But once it has *kedushas mincha*, the prohibition against leavening it is in effect.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot addresses a conceptual tension in this ruling. The verse governing *chametz* in a *mincha* uses the language *lo teaseh* and *lo te'afeh* — do not make and do not bake — which implies active stages of the bread-making process: *lisha*, *aricha*, *afiya*, *ktifa*. If so, why should placing sourdough and walking away constitute a violation? One might have thought the prohibition is exclusively about performing those production stages *while the offering is chametz*, not about causing the leavening itself.

Tosafot resolves this by noting that *haniach se'or al gabei issa vehalach veyashav lo — lo teaseh karinan bei*. The phrase *lo ta'aseh chametz* can be read as "it shall not become *chametz*," meaning the prohibition encompasses the more passive sense of allowing the dough to ferment, not only the active processing stages. In Tosafot's reading, the simple *peshat* of *lo ta'aseh chametz* is precisely this — do not allow it to become *chametz*.

The conceptual payoff of this ruling is thus bilateral. The earlier discussion established that even after the dough is already *chametz* — and therefore already *pasul* — every subsequent stage of processing incurs an independent violation. Now the Gemara adds that there is also an independent prohibition against *causing* the leavening in the first place, even without performing any stage of the bread-making process. Both endpoints carry liability, and the person who merely neglects to act — doing nothing, allowing sourdough someone else introduced to ferment — is exempt, having committed neither act.

## Shabbat Cooking

### Meat on Coals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הִנִּיחַ בָּשָׂר עַל גַּבֵּי גֶּחָלִים, הִיפֵּךְ בּוֹ – חַיָּיב, לֹא הִיפֵּךְ בּוֹ – פָּטוּר. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִי לָא הִיפֵּךְ בֵּיהּ לָא בְּשִׁיל – פְּשִׁיטָא! אֶלָּא דְּאִי לָא מְהַפֵּיךְ לֵיהּ נָמֵי הֲוָה בְּשִׁיל, אַמַּאי לָא מִיחַיַּיב?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself. Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of one who **placed meat on top of coals** on Shabbat, if he subsequently **turned over** the meat he is **liable** for cooking on Shabbat, and if he **did not turn over** the meat he is **exempt.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say** that this was a situation **where if** he does **not turn over** the meat it would **not cook,** then it is **obvious** that if he does not turn it over he is exempt. **Rather,** it must be referring to a case **where** even **if** he **does not turn over** the meat it would nevertheless **cook.** But if so, **why isn't he liable** for merely placing the meat on the coals, despite the fact that he did not turn it over?

The Gemara now opens a sustained *digression* on the parallel category of cooking on Shabbat, examining what degree of cooking constitutes a *melacha* and under what conditions passive heat application renders one liable. The question is posed *gufa* — returning to the case that was cited parenthetically: Rabba bar bar Chana said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — *hiniach basar al gabei gechalim, hiphcho chayav, lo hiphcho patur* — if one placed meat on coals on Shabbat, he is liable if he flipped it over and exempt if he did not.

The Gemara immediately presses: what exactly is the case? If the meat would not cook at all without being flipped — because coals alone without being fanned do not generate sufficient heat — then of course one is exempt without flipping; the meat will not cook, and this is obvious. Conversely, if the meat would have fully cooked even without being flipped, why should flipping be required for liability? If it was going to cook anyway, why does the act of flipping matter?

This is an interesting juncture, and worth pausing over. One might have expected the Gemara to give its standard response in such cases — namely, that even if the cooking would have occurred on its own, one is liable because the flipping *accelerated* it. The Gemara frequently uses that reasoning. Yet here the Gemara does not deploy it, and this silence is itself significant. There is in fact a debate among the *acharonim* about whether liability for Shabbat cooking requires that the cooking *complete* on Shabbat, or whether it is sufficient that one performed the initiating act on Shabbat even if completion occurs afterward. If one holds that the cooking must be completed on Shabbat for there to be liability, then the answer of "you accelerated it" becomes crucial, because the person who flipped the meat caused it to finish *on Shabbat*, whereas without flipping it would have finished after Shabbat. That the Gemara does not use this reasoning here suggests that, at least in the Gemara's framing of this case, the scenario is not one where the difference is about completion before or after Shabbat.

### Maachal Ben Drusai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי לָא הַפֵּיךְ בֵּיהּ – הֲוָה בְּשִׁיל מִצַּד אֶחָד כְּמַאֲכָל בֶּן דְּרוֹסַאי, וְכִי מְהַפֵּיךְ בֵּיהּ – בְּשִׁיל מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין כְּמַאֲכָל בֶּן דְּרוֹסַאי, וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּכֹל מִצַּד אֶחָד כְּמַאֲכָל בֶּן דְּרוֹסַאי – לָא כְּלוּם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case **where if** he does **not turn over** the meat it would **cook on one side** only partially, roughly one-third of the ordinary process of cooking, **like the food of ben Derosai. And** now **that he turns it over,** it **cooks on both sides like the food of ben Derosai. And** Rabbi Yoḥanan **teaches us that any** meat roasted **on** only **one side like the food of ben Derosai is nothing,** i.e., this is not a violation of the prohibited labor of cooking on Shabbat. If it was roasted on both sides like the food of ben Derosai this is classified as cooking, and he is liable for cooking on Shabbat.

The Gemara's actual answer is: *tzricha* — the case is necessary for a specific scenario. Without flipping, the meat would cook on one side to the level of *maachal Ben Drusai* — the standard attributed to bandits, meaning half-raw, or depending on whether one follows Rashi (a third cooked) or the Rambam (half cooked), in any case a degree of cooking that could be eaten only *b'shaas hadchak*, in extremis, but that most people would not consider properly prepared. It is established that *maachal Ben Drusai* is sufficient to constitute a *melacha* of cooking on Shabbat.

However, the Gemara introduces a critical nuance. *Maachal Ben Drusai* on one side is not sufficient for liability if the other side remains completely raw. Even if theoretically the person could cut out the cooked portion and eat it, in practice no one eats a thick piece of meat where the bottom half is rare and the top half is entirely raw. The test is not theoretical edibility — it is whether the piece of meat, as it stands, is something a person would actually eat. Since the answer is no, it is not considered a completed act of cooking, and one is therefore exempt.

By contrast, if one flipped the meat over, both sides reach the level of *maachal Ben Drusai*, and the piece is now something people would eat — rare on both sides. That constitutes a sufficiently completed *melacha* of cooking, and one is liable.

### Roasting Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְאִם נִצְלָה בּוֹ כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת מִצַּד אֶחָד בִּמְקוֹם אֶחָד – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the case of meat roasted on one side on Shabbat. **Rava says: And if** a quantity of that meat equivalent in volume **to a fig-bulk was** fully **roasted on one side** of the meat and the roasted area was **in one spot** on the piece of meat, while the rest of the meat remained raw, he is **liable** for cooking on Shabbat.

Rava then introduces an important contrast: *ve'im nitzla bo kegrogeret mitzad echad bemakom echad chayav* — if the meat was roasted on one side, not merely to *maachal Ben Drusai* but fully cooked, and the cooked area amounts to a *grogeret* (a pressed fig's worth) and is all concentrated in one place, one is liable — even though the other side of the piece remains raw.

The distinction tracks the logic of the earlier analysis. The question in each case is whether a person would actually eat this piece of meat as it stands. A piece that is fully cooked on the bottom and raw on top is in fact something a person will eat: they will eat the bottom. The raw portion does not make the edible portion uneatable. But a piece that is merely rare throughout — one side rare and the other raw — is not something anyone would eat as a whole piece, and the rare portion cannot be disaggregated from the raw portion in the mind of a normal eater.

### Multiple Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: בִּמְקוֹם אֶחָד – אִין, בִּשְׁנַיִם אוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת – לָא? וְהָתְנַן: הַקּוֹדֵחַ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** It can be inferred from Rava's statement that if the fig-bulk of meat that was roasted was **in one spot, yes,** he is liable, whereas if the amount of the fig-bulk **was** distributed **over two or three** separate **spots** he is **not** liable. **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 102b): **One who drills** a hole of **any** size **is liable** for the labor of building or the labor of striking a blow with a hammer to complete the production process of a vessel?

This brings Rava to his more precise formulation: *kegrogeret mitzad echad bemakom echad* — a *grogeret*'s worth of fully cooked meat, on one side, in one place. Ravina raises a question to Rav Ashi: why must it all be in *one* place? If the cooked portions are distributed across two or three spots on the meat, could they not be combined to reach the requisite *shiur*?

### Hole Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אֶלָּא לָאו בִּשְׁנַיִם אוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת, דַּחֲזוֹ לְצֵירוּף? לָא, לְעוֹלָם בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, דַּחֲזוּ לְבָבָא דְּאַקְלִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes that mishna: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that the mishna is referring to one who drills a hole **in one spot** alone, why is he liable? **For what** purpose is a hole **of any size,** which includes a very small hole, **fit** to be used? **Rather, is it not** referring to one who drills holes **in two or three** different **spots,** and the reason he is liable is **that** these holes **are fit to** be **joined?** Builders who require a large hole will often start by drilling a few small holes which they later enlarge and join together. By the same logic, if the meat was roasted in a few spots he should be liable, as these can join together. The Gemara answers: **No; actually** the mishna is referring to one who drills **in** only **one spot,** as one can say **that** the hole **is fit as an entrance [*levava*] for** the placement of the tooth of **a key [*aklida*],** which is very small.

The attempt to prove that distributed locations can be combined comes from a *mishna* concerning *boneh* on Shabbat: *hakodeach kol shehu chayav* — one who drills even the smallest hole is liable. The Gemara asks: *heichi dami* — what is the case? If the hole is in one place, *kol shehu l'mai chazi* — what can one tiny hole accomplish? It must therefore be, says the Gemara, that the *mishna* is referring to two or three holes in nearby locations, and one is liable *dechazi l'tziruf* — because they can theoretically be combined into something functional.

This would seem to support Ravina's position: if small holes in different locations can be combined to create a significant act of *boneh*, surely cooked portions in different areas of a piece of meat can be combined to meet the *shiur* for cooking. But the Gemara rejects the proof. The case of the hole does not necessarily involve multiple holes at all — *lo l'olam bemakom echad*. A single tiny hole can serve a purpose: *dechazi l'vava d'aklida* — it is suitable for a door bolt, a lock mechanism. One hole is thus meaningful on its own, and the *mishna* need not be read as referring to multiple holes in multiple places. The analogy therefore does not establish that distributed locations can be combined, and the question of whether cooking in multiple spots may be combined remains unresolved.

The upshot of this Shabbat *sugya* is the following: cooking is defined by fitness for eating, not by abstract quantity. *Maachal Ben Drusai* is the minimum level, but the piece must reach that minimum in a way that makes the whole piece — as it actually exists — something a person would eat. One-sided cooking to *maachal Ben Drusai* does not meet that standard; one-sided full cooking does. Whether the required quantity of cooking may be assembled from multiple discrete locations on the meat was raised but left without resolution.

## Chametz Scope

### Scope Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ לֹא תֵעָשֶׂה חָמֵץ״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר אֵין לִי בְּלֹא תֵעָשֶׂה חָמֵץ אֶלָּא קוֹמֶץ בִּלְבָד. מִנְחָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מִנְחָה״. שְׁאָר מְנָחוֹת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כׇּל הַמִּנְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discussing the prohibition against leavening a meal offering. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: Concerning the deep-pan meal offering, the verse states: "No meal offering that you shall bring to the Lord shall be made with leaven; for any leaven, and any honey, you shall not burn any of it as an offering made by fire to the Lord" (Leviticus 2:11). The term "meal offering" is apparently superfluous, and therefore the *baraita* explains: **If** the verse **had stated** only: **Nothing that you shall bring to the Lord shall be made with leaven, I would say: I have** derived **only** that the **handful** removed from the meal offering for burning on the altar **alone** is included **in** the prohibition: **"Shall not be made with leaven,"** as only the handful is burned on the altar. **From where** is it derived that one is liable for the leavening of **a meal offering** whose handful has not yet been removed? **The verse states: "Meal offering,"** to teach that the prohibition includes a meal offering before the removal of its handful. The Gemara asks: Since this verse is stated in the context of the deep-pan meal offering, **from where** is it derived that one is liable for leavening the **other meal offerings,** which are not mentioned in this passage? Therefore, **the verse states** the inclusive phrase: **"No meal offering,"** to apply this *halakha* to other meal offerings.

With the Shabbat discussion complete, the Gemara returns to the *chametz* prohibition in *minchos*. A *baraisa* derives the scope of the prohibition through a careful reading of the verse. Had the verse said only *asher takrivu l'Hashem lo teaseh chametz* — "that which you bring to God you shall not make *chametz*" — one might have thought the prohibition applies only to the *kometz*, the handful that is actually brought on the *mizbeach* as the portion designated *l'Hashem*. From where would I know that the prohibition extends to the entire *mincha* before the *kometz* is separated?

The verse says *kol mincha asher takrivu l'Hashem lo teaseh chametz* — *every mincha* that you bring. The word *mincha* in this phrase teaches that the prohibition covers the whole *mincha*, not just the designated *kometz*. The word *kol* teaches that it is not limited to the one *mincha* where this verse appears — the *marcheshes* — but extends to all the other standard *minchos* as well.

Having established both points from *kol* and *mincha*, the phrase *asher takrivu l'Hashem* remains to be explained.

### Kashrus Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ לַה׳״ – כְּשֵׁרָה, וְלֹא פְּסוּלָה. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הַמְחַמֵּיץ אֶת הַכְּשֵׁירָה – חַיָּיב, וְאֶת הַפְּסוּלָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues to expound the verse. The phrase: **"That you shall bring to the Lord,"** indicates that this prohibition applies only to **a fit** meal offering, **but not** to a **disqualified** meal offering, e.g., a meal offering that was taken outside the Temple or that was rendered ritually impure. **From here** the Sages **stated** that **one who leavens a fit** meal offering is **liable** to receive lashes, **but** one who leavens **a disqualified meal offering** is **exempt.**

The phrase *asher takrivu l'Hashem* teaches: *keshera velo psula* — only if the *mincha* is fit to be brought. If the *mincha* has already become *pasul* for some reason unrelated to *chametz*, and one then makes it *chametz*, one is exempt. The logic is direct: the prohibition against *chametz* exists because a *chametz mincha* cannot be placed on the *mizbeach*. If the offering is already *pasul* for some other reason and cannot be placed on the *mizbeach* in any event, the additional act of making it *chametz* adds nothing to its disqualification, and one bears no liability.

This rule has one important exception, noted from the earlier discussion: the principle of *mechameitz achar mechameitz*. Once an offering has become *chametz* — which itself renders it *pasul* — one is still liable for each subsequent stage of processing, even though the offering is already *pasul*. The *chametz* case is thus self-contained: the *pasul* that arises from *chametz* does not cancel liability for further acts of *chametz* processing, because the entire sequence of violations is bracketed within the category of *chametz*. But if the offering is *pasul* for a reason other than *chametz* — the analogy to *pigul* is apt: liability for *pigul* requires that the offering would otherwise have been *kasher*, and if it was already *pasul* for a different reason, there is no *pigul* — then making it *chametz* generates no liability.

## Removal Case

### Pasul Yotzei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: חִימְּצָהּ וְיָתְזָה וְחָזַר וְחִימְּצָהּ – מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּיָצָאת אִיפְּסִילָה לַהּ בְּיוֹצֵא, וְכִי הָדַר מְחַמֵּיץ לַהּ – לָא מִיחַיַּיב עֲלַהּ מִשּׁוּם מְחַמֵּיץ אַחֵר מְחַמֵּיץ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the ruling that the prohibition against leavening does not apply to a disqualified meal offering, **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** If one **leavened** a meal offering when it was fit, **and** subsequently someone removed the meal offering and it **emerged** from the Temple courtyard and was thereby disqualified, **and he again leavened it, what is** the *halakha*? Is he liable for the second leavening as well? The Gemara explains the possibilities: Does one say that **since** it **emerged it is disqualified in** accordance with the status of a sacred item that **emerges** from its permitted area, **and** therefore **when he again leavens it he is not liable for it due to** the prohibition against **leavening** a meal offering **after** one who already **leavened** it?

Rav Pappa now poses a question that probes the interaction between *mechameitz achar mechameitz* and the rule of *asher takrivu*. The case: *chamtza v'yatza v'chazara v'chamtza mahu* — a *mincha* became *chametz*, then was taken outside the *Beis HaMikdash*, rendering it *pasul* due to *yotzei*, and was then brought back in, and subsequently one performed another stage of *chametz* processing with it. Is one liable?

Two principles appear to be in tension. On one hand, *mechameitz achar mechameitz* teaches that once an offering is *chametz*, further acts of processing it as *chametz* incur independent liability, even though the offering is already *pasul* from the *chametz*. On the other hand, the rule of *asher takrivu* — *keshera velo psula* — says that if an offering is *pasul* and one then makes it *chametz*, one is exempt.

### Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּחִימְּצָהּ, פְּסוּל יוֹצֵא לָא מַהֲנֵי בַּיהּ, וְכִי הֲדַר מְחַמֵּיץ לַהּ – מִיחַיַּיב עֲלַהּ מִשּׁוּם מְחַמֵּיץ אַחֵר מְחַמֵּיץ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps, since** one already **leavened** the meal offering, from this point forward the **disqualification** of a sacred item that **emerges** from its permitted area **is ineffective with regard to** removing it from the prohibition against leavening, as the prohibition against emerging from the Temple itself applies only to a fit meal offering. **And** therefore, **when he again leavens it he is liable for it due to** the prohibition against **leavening** a meal offering **after one who** already **leavened** it. No answer was found, and the Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The question is whether the subsequent *psul yotzei* constitutes a new and independent disqualification that severs the chain of liability established by *mechameitz achar mechameitz*, or whether it does not. The argument for continued liability runs as follows: the offering was already *pasul* when it became *chametz*. Adding a *psul yotzei* does not make it *more pasul*; it was already off the track of permissible use. Since we bracket the original *chametz pasul* and continue to hold the person liable for further acts, why should the introduction of a different *psul* change anything? The *chametz* framework remains operative. The argument for exemption is equally intuitive: once a new *psul* — one independent of *chametz* — enters the picture, the offering no longer fits the category of *asher takrivu*, and liability should terminate.

The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*. The personal inclination expressed is that the person should be exempt — because the *mechameitz achar mechameitz* principle can be understood as treating the entire sequence as a single extended act of introducing *chametz* into this offering, but once a wholly independent *psul* intervenes, the offering is now off the track regardless of the *chametz*, and one cannot be held liable for continuing to process something that was disqualified for reasons independent of one's own prior transgression. But as the Gemara itself notes, this remains unresolved.

## Mizbe'ach Timing

### Placement Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב מָרִי: חִימְּצָהּ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ, מַהוּ? ״אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא אַקְרְבַהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא מְחוּסַּר הַקְטָרָה כִּמְחוּסַּר מַעֲשֶׂה דָּמֵי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari raises** another **dilemma** concerning the leavening of a meal offering. If a priest **leavened** a meal offering while standing **at the top of the altar, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara clarifies the possibilities: **The Merciful One states** in the Torah: "No meal offering **that you shall bring** to the Lord shall be made with leaven" (Leviticus 2:11), which indicates that this prohibition applies only to a meal offering that has not yet been brought to the Lord, i.e., to the altar. **And** therefore, as **this** meal offering **has** already been **brought** to the top of the altar, even though it has not yet been burned, perhaps it is not included in the prohibition. **Or perhaps** any offering whose **burning has not yet** been performed **is considered like** one whose **action** of sacrifice **has not yet** been performed, despite the fact that it has already been brought to the top of the altar. If so, this meal offering is included in the prohibition against leavening. No answer was found, and the Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Rav Mari raises a related question: *chimsa b'rosh ha-mizbeach mahu* — if one made the *kometz chametz* after having placed it at the top of the *mizbeach*, what is the law? The context is the phrase *asher takrivu* — "that which you will bring." Once the offering has been fully brought — i.e., burned on the *mizbeach* — one is obviously no longer liable for making it *chametz*; the verse says "that which you will bring," implying the prohibition is operative only while it is in the process of being brought, not after. But the question is whether placement at the top of the *mizbeach*, before the burning begins, already constitutes a completed act of *karavas* — bringing — that terminates liability, or whether *mechusar haktara k'mechusar ma'aseh dami* — since it still lacks the burning, it is as though the act is incomplete, and one remains liable for *chametz*.

The Gemara reads *takrivu* as potentially meaning "bring close," in which case physical placement at the top of the *mizbeach* could already constitute the completion of *karavas*. Alternatively, *takrivu* means "offer up," in which case burning is required before the process is considered complete. The Gemara again concludes with *teiku* — the question of at what precise moment liability terminates is unresolved.

The Gemara then notes a broader framing question. Since we have derived from *kol mincha* that the prohibition applies to all *minchos*, and from *mincha* alone that it extends to the whole offering rather than only the *kometz*, the phrase *asher takrivu* — having been used to establish *keshera velo psula* and to address the timing questions of *Rav Mari* — is now invoked again to derive an entirely new inclusion.

## Extension Clause

### RYH vs RA

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״כׇּל הַמִּנְחָה״, ״אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ (לַה׳)״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ״ – לְרַבּוֹת מִנְחַת נְסָכִים לְחִימּוּץ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים לְחִימּוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **derives from** the phrase: **"No meal offering,"** that the prohibition includes even a meal offering before the removal of its handful, **why do I** need the phrase: **"That you shall bring,"** in the same verse? The Gemara answers: **It is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"That you shall bring,"** in order **to include the meal offering** brought with the **libations** that accompany certain animal offerings such as burnt offerings, peace offerings, and the sin offerings and guilt offerings of a leper **in** the prohibition against **leavening.** Unlike the standard meal offerings, from which a handful is removed, these are entirely burned upon the altar, and the additional derivation is necessary to include them in the prohibition against **leavening.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** This phrase serves **to include the shewbread in** the prohibition against **leavening.**

The *baraisa* records that *asher takrivu* serves additionally to extend the *chametz* prohibition to non-standard *minchos*. The verse's formulation — "whatever you will bring" — is broad enough to include offerings beyond those explicitly enumerated as standard *minchos* in *Vayikra*. Rabbi Yose HaGelili says: *l'rabos minchas nesachim l'chimutz* — this includes *minchas nesachim*, the flour-and-oil *mincha* accompanying animal offerings. Rabbi Akiva says: *l'rabos lechem hapanim l'chimutz* — this includes the *lechem hapanim*, the showbread.

As a transmission from *Eretz Yisrael* (introduced by the formula *shlach*) will later clarify, the two names in the *baraisa* must be reversed: it is Rabbi Yose HaGelili who includes *lechem hapanim*, and Rabbi Akiva who includes *minchas nesachim*. This reversal, as will become clear, is necessitated by internal consistency with positions attributed to each *tanna* elsewhere.

The need for a special derivation in each case reflects the fact that both *minchas nesachim* and *lechem hapanim* are in various ways non-standard *minchos*. The *minchas nesachim* is entirely burned on the *mizbeach* rather than partially eaten; it lacks *levonah*; it accompanies an animal offering rather than standing alone. The *lechem hapanim* lacks oil; it is not directly offered on the *mizbeach* — its *levonah* is burned but the bread itself is eaten by *kohanim*; it does not have a *kometz* taken from it. Tosafot notes that these structural differences would have sufficed to exclude them from the general category of *mincha* even before any technical problems of *chametz* arise — and it is *asher takrivu* that overrides that exclusion.

### Nesachim Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחַת נְסָכִים? מֵי פֵירוֹת הֵם! וּמֵי פֵירוֹת אֵין מַחְמִיצִין? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, מִנְחַת נְסָכִים מְגַבְּלָהּ בְּמַיִם וּכְשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies these opinions: With regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, the Gemara asks: How is it possible to leaven **the meal offering** brought with **libations?** This type of meal offering is kneaded only with olive oil, which **is** a type of **fruit juice,** **and** there is a principle that **fruit juices do not leaven** dough. **Reish Lakish says** that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili would say:** Even though the oil of **the meal offering** brought with **libations** is plentiful, sometimes one might also **knead** a meal offering **with** some **water,** if he considers it necessary to add it, **and** it is **fit.** If so, it is possible for this meal offering to leaven due to the water, and therefore the verse: "That you shall bring," teaches that the prohibition against leavening also applies to this type of meal offering.

Why might one have thought *minchas nesachim* cannot become *chametz* at all? *Minchas nesachim mei peiros heim* — the liquid used in *minchas nesachim* is oil, which is a *mei peiros* (a fruit-derived liquid rather than water), *umei peiros einam machmitzim* — and fruit-derived liquids do not cause leavening. Without water, dough cannot ferment into *chametz*. The Torah does not mention water as an ingredient in *minchas nesachim* — only oil, and in much greater proportion than in standard *minchos*. One might therefore conclude that *minchas nesachim* is simply incapable of becoming *chametz* and that the prohibition is irrelevant to it.

The Gemara responds in the name of Reish Lakish: Rabbi Yose HaGelili — in the corrected attribution, the one who *includes* the *lechem hapanim* — held that *minchas nesachim migadbelah bemayim ukesheirah*, water may be added to the *minchas nesachim* and it remains valid. The word *ukesheirah* indicates that adding water is permissible rather than mandatory, but the Torah must then address the possibility: if you do add water, do not allow it to become *chametz*.

Rashi and Tosafot both raise the observation that water is nowhere mentioned explicitly by any of the *minchos* in the Torah; the text speaks only of oil. Their answer: by all standard *minchos*, because the quantity of oil specified is relatively small, it is self-evident that water must be added to make the dough workable, and thus water is assumed even where unstated. By *minchas nesachim*, however, the ratio of oil is three times greater than in a standard *mincha*, and therefore the assumption was that no additional water would be needed — the dough could be worked with oil alone. Rabbi Yose HaGelili corrects this assumption: one may add water, the offering remains valid, and therefore the prohibition of *chametz* is applicable if one does so.

## Lechem Hapanim

### Kedusha Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים מִדַּת יָבֵשׁ הִיא, וּשְׁמַעְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר: מִדַּת יָבֵשׁ לֹא נִתְקַדְּשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to Rabbi Akiva's opinion, the vessel into which a tenth of an ephah of flour for baking the **shewbread is** placed in **is a measuring** vessel for **dry** items, **and we have heard that Rabbi Akiva said:** The **measuring** vessels for **dry** items were **not consecrated** as service vessels. This means the shewbread becomes consecrated only when it is placed on the Table in the Sanctuary, after it has been baked. If so, at this stage, the prohibition against leavening is not relevant.

The question regarding *lechem hapanim* is of a different character entirely. *Lechem hapanim midas yavesh* — the *lechem hapanim* is prepared using only dry measures, since it contains no oil and no specified liquid measure of water. And *midas yavesh lo nitkadshu* — Rabbi Akiva holds (in a *sugya* later in the tractate, at 94) that dry measuring vessels were not sanctified. This is the heart of the problem.

The prohibition of *chametz* in a *mincha* requires that the offering possess *kedushas haguf* — intrinsic sanctity. For animals, this sanctity attaches at the moment of *hakdasha*. For *minchos*, however, the established principle is that *kedushas haguf* is conferred by contact with a *kli shares* — a sanctified Temple vessel. Once flour comes into contact with a sanctified vessel, it acquires intrinsic *kedusha* and the prohibition of *chametz* becomes applicable.

But if the dry measuring vessels were not sanctified, then measuring the flour for the *lechem hapanim* does not confer *kedusha*. The water has no specified measure and requires no measuring vessel. The kneading — as will be established shortly — is done on a flat surface, not in a sanctified bowl. The baking oven may or may not be a *kli shares*. If the first moment of contact with a *kli shares* is when the baked loaves are placed on the *shulchan* — the table in the Sanctuary — then *kedushas haguf* is acquired only after baking is complete. At that point, the bread is already fully baked and the possibility of *chametz* is foreclosed. There would therefore be no moment at which the *lechem hapanim* simultaneously possesses *kedushas haguf* and is capable of leavening, making the prohibition of *chametz* inapplicable.

This is why the *baraisa* requires a special derivation — *asher takrivu* — to include the *lechem hapanim*: without it, one might conclude that no transgression is possible given the absence of sanctified contact before baking. And it is this problem that forces the reattribution of positions: whoever rules that *chametz* applies to *lechem hapanim* must hold that dry measures *were* sanctified, since otherwise the prohibition has nothing to attach to at any stage when leavening is still possible.

### Name Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח רַבִּי רְאוּבֵן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּךְ הִיא הַצָּעָה שֶׁל מִשְׁנָה, וְאֵיפוֹךְ – ״אֲשֶׁר תַּקְרִיבוּ״ לְרַבּוֹת לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים לְחִימּוּץ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לְרַבּוֹת מִנְחַת נְסָכִים לְחִימּוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** In answer to this question, the Gemara states that **Rabbi Reuven sent** a letter from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, in which he cited a statement **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: This is the** correct **version [*hetze'ah*] of** this ***baraita*, and** one should **reverse** the names of the Sages in it: The phrase **"that you shall bring"** serves **to include the shewbread in** the prohibition against **leavening;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** This phrase serves **to include the meal offering** brought with **libations in** the prohibition against **leavening.** Consequently, there is no contradiction between Rabbi Akiva's statement here and his statement with regard to measuring vessels for dry items.

The message transmitted from *Eretz Yisrael* — introduced by *shlach* — confirms the structure but corrects the names. The attribution in the *baraisa* as originally read is reversed: *asher takrivu* is invoked by Rabbi Yose HaGelili to include *lechem hapanim* (meaning he holds dry measures were sanctified), while Rabbi Akiva invokes it to include *minchas nesachim* (meaning he, not Rabbi Yose HaGelili, holds that dry measures were *not* sanctified, and therefore the *chametz* prohibition for *lechem hapanim* does not apply). Rabbi Yochanan explicitly states this reversal is necessary to align the *baraisa* with the known positions of each *tanna*.

### Sanctified Dry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי וְאֶחָד מִתַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד, וּמַנּוּ רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. דְּתַנְיָא, ״וַיִּמְשָׁחֵם וַיְקַדֵּשׁ אֹתָם״. רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה אוֹמֵר: מִדַּת הַלַּח נִמְשַׁח בֵּין מִבִּפְנִים בֵּין מִבַּחוּץ, מִדַּת יָבֵשׁ נִמְשְׁחוּ מִבִּפְנִים וְאֵין נִמְשְׁחוּ מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who holds that Rabbi Yosei HaGelili is the one who maintains that the measuring vessels for dry items were consecrated, **follows [*ve'azda*] his** line of **reasoning** stated elsewhere. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and one of the students of Rabbi Yishmael** both **said the same thing. And who is** that student of Rabbi Yishmael? It is **Rabbi Yoshiya.** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the consecration of the Tabernacle and its vessels before their inauguration: "And it came to pass on the day that Moses had made an end of setting up the Tabernacle, and had anointed it and sanctified it, and all its vessels, and the altar and all its vessels, **and had anointed them and sanctified them"** (Numbers 7:1). **Rabbi Yoshiya says:** The **measuring** vessels for **liquid** items, e.g., oil, and wine for libations, were **anointed** and thereby consecrated **both on** the **inside and on** the **outside.** The **measuring** vessels for **dry** items, such as the flour for meal offerings, **were anointed** and consecrated only **on** the **inside, but were not anointed on** the **outside.**

Rabbi Yochanan proceeds to note that Rabbi Yose HaGelili and one of the students of Rabbi Yishmael stated essentially the same position, and identifies that student as Rabbi Yoshiya. In a *baraisa*, the verse *vayimshachem vayikadesh osam* — describing Moshe's anointing and sanctification of the Temple vessels — is parsed carefully. The word *osam* is a limiting term. Rabbi Yoshiya interprets it as limiting, not eliminating: *midas halach nimshachu bein mibifnim bein mibachutz* — liquid measures were anointed both inside and outside; *midas yavesh nimshachu mibifnim ve'ein nimshachu mibachutz* — dry measures were anointed on the inside only, not on the outside. They were therefore sanctified — on the inside — and can confer *kedusha* on what is measured within them. The *kedusha* of the dry measures is thus partial rather than absent. This position aligns with Rabbi Yose HaGelili's ruling that the prohibition of *chametz* applies to *lechem hapanim*.

### Inside Only

Rabbi Yoshiya's position thus reflects a middle path. He does not dispute that there is some difference between the treatment of dry and liquid measures — the verse's limiting force is acknowledged. But the limitation is spatial, not categorical: dry measures lacked the outer anointing but retained the inner anointing that is functionally relevant for sanctifying contents. In this view, *kedushas haguf* would be conferred on the *lechem hapanim* flour when measured in the dry measuring cups of the Sanctuary, making it possible to transgress the *chametz* prohibition during the preparation process.

### Proof Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִדַּת הַלַּח נִמְשְׁחוּ מִבִּפְנִים, וְאֵין נִמְשְׁחוּ מִבַּחוּץ, מִדּוֹת יָבֵשׁ לֹא נִמְשְׁחוּ כׇּל עִיקָּר. תֵּדַע לְךָ שֶׁהֲרֵי אֵין מְקַדְּשׁוֹת, דִּכְתִיב ״מִמּוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם תָּבִיאּוּ לֶחֶם תְּנוּפָה שְׁתַּיִם שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרֹנִים סֹלֶת תִּהְיֶינָה חָמֵץ תֵּאָפֶינָה בִּכּוּרִים לַה׳״. אֵימָתַי הֵן ״לַה׳״? לְאַחַר שֶׁנֶּאֱפוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** The **measuring** vessels for **liquid** items **were anointed on** the **inside and were not anointed on** the **outside,** whereas the **measuring** vessels for **dry** items **were not anointed at all.** Rabbi Yonatan cites a proof for his opinion: **You can know** that the measuring vessels for dry items were not consecrated at all, **because** it is taught that these vessels **do not consecrate** items placed inside them. **As it is written: "You shall bring out of your dwellings two wave-loaves of two tenth-parts of a ephah. They shall be of fine flour; they shall be baked with leaven, for first produce to the Lord"** (Leviticus 23:17). **When** are **they** considered consecrated **to the Lord?** Only **after they are baked** with leaven. This demonstrates that when the flour is placed into the tenth of an ephah measuring vessel the meal offering is not yet consecrated.

Rabbi Yonatan takes the opposing position: *midas yavesh lo nimshechu kol ikar* — dry measures were not sanctified at all. He offers a proof from the verse concerning the *shtei halechem* on Shavuot: *mimoshvoseichem taviu lechem tenufah shtayim shnei esronim solet tihyena chametz teiafena bikurim l'Hashem* — you shall bring from your homes the two loaves, two *esronim* of *solet*, they shall be baked as *chametz*, *bikurim l'Hashem*.

Rabbi Yonatan reads the verse as follows: *eimasi heim l'Hashem l'achar shenaafu* — they are designated *l'Hashem* only after they have been baked. The word *bikurim l'Hashem* does not apply to the loaves before baking, only after. This is because before baking they have not yet acquired intrinsic sanctity — *kedushas haguf* — since no sanctified vessel has been used in their preparation. The *shtei halechem*, like the *lechem hapanim*, contains no oil and thus requires no liquid measuring vessel; the flour would be measured in dry vessels; and since those vessels were not sanctified, no *kedusha* is conferred until the loaves enter the baking oven (which does possess *kedusha*) or are subsequently placed on the *shulchan*.

This proves, in Rabbi Yonatan's view, that the dry measuring vessels do not sanctify their contents, because otherwise the *shtei halechem* would have acquired *kedushas haguf* when measured, long before baking. The fact that the verse locates their status as *l'Hashem* at the point after baking demonstrates that no earlier sanctification occurred.

### Debate Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? ״בְּאֹתָם״. רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה סָבַר ״אֹתָם״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מִדַּת יָבֵשׁ בַּחוּץ, וְרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן סָבַר מִדַּת יָבֵשׁ חוֹל הוּא, וְלָא אִצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי, כִּי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַעוֹטֵי מִדַּת לַח מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rabbi Yoshiya and Rabbi Yonatan **disagree?** They disagree **with regard to** the interpretation of the word "them" in the phrase: "And sanctified **them"** (Numbers 7:1). Both *tanna'im* agree that the term "them" serves to exclude something from the anointing and consecration, but they disagree over what is excluded. **Rabbi Yoshiya maintains** that **"them"** serves **to exclude** the consecration of the **measuring** vessels for **dry** items on the **outside,** i.e., only the inside of the vessels is consecrated. **And Rabbi Yonatan maintains** that the **measuring** vessels for **dry** items **are non-sacred,** i.e., not consecrated at all, **and** therefore **it is not necessary** for the **verse to exclude** them. **Where it was necessary** for the **verse to exclude** something, it was with regard to **measuring** vessels used for **liquid** items, to teach that they are consecrated only on the inside but not **on** the **outside.**

The debate on the sanctification of dry measures thus emerges with three positions. Rabbi Yonatan (aligning with Rabbi Akiva) holds that dry measures were not sanctified at all; the limiting word *osam* excludes the outer anointing of liquid measures but operates against a background assumption that dry measures are simply secular — *midas yavesh chol hu*. Therefore the verse needs no work to exclude them, and *osam* is used instead to limit the liquid measures: even they are anointed on the inside only, not outside. Rabbi Yoshiya (aligning with Rabbi Yose HaGelili) holds that dry measures were sanctified, though only on the inside; *osam* limits their anointing spatially. Rabbi Yochanan notes that Rabbi Yose HaGelili and Rabbi Yoshiya "said the same thing" — both maintain that dry measures do confer *kedusha*, making it possible to transgress the *chametz* prohibition in preparing the *lechem hapanim*. The question of whether one can align Rabbi Akiva with Rabbi Yonatan is answered negatively, because while their practical positions regarding *lechem hapanim chametz* liability are equivalent, they diverge on the status of liquid measures: Rabbi Akiva holds liquid measures were sanctified inside and outside, while Rabbi Yonatan holds inside only.

## Kneading Bowl

### Bisah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְהָא אִיכָּא בִּיסָא דְּלַח הוּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן שֶׁלָּשׁ עַל גַּבֵּי קַטְבֻלְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** How can it be suggested, according to the opinion that the measuring vessels for dry items were not consecrated, that the shewbread is consecrated only when it is placed on the Table? **But** before that **there is** the stage in which the dough is placed into the **receptacle [*bisa*]** in which it is kneaded. Since this vessel **is** used for **liquid** items, the dough should be consecrated at that point. Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa: This is referring to a case **where he kneaded** the dough **on top of** a flat **leather spread [*katavliyya*],** which does not sanctify the dough.

Rav Pappa poses a further challenge to Abaye: *ve-ha ika bisa de-lachma* — there is the *bisa*, the kneading trough. The *bisa* is a vessel used for kneading dough, and unlike the dry measuring cup, it handles a wetter, more liquidy mixture. Should it not be classified as a liquid vessel, and therefore sanctified? If the *bisa* was a *kli shares* that was sanctified, then the *lechem hapanim* dough would come into contact with a sanctified vessel during kneading — before baking — and would thereby acquire *kedushas haguf* at that earlier stage. If so, the argument that *lechem hapanim* has no opportunity for *chametz* liability would collapse even according to those who hold dry measures were not sanctified.

The Gemara responds: *kegon de-lasha al gabei katbalya* — the case is where one kneaded the dough on a flat surface, a butcher block or table, rather than in a kneading bowl. The Torah cannot legislate based on the assumption that the *bisa* will always be used, because there is no requirement to knead in a bowl. Normal bread-making — *lechatchila* — can perfectly well be done on a table. The Torah's prohibitions are addressed to standard, normative practice, and standard practice for kneading bread does not presuppose the use of a *bisa*. Therefore, the possibility of kneading on a table renders the *bisah* argument moot: one cannot assume the sanctified kneading vessel will necessarily be used.

### Standard Practice

### Measure Vs Knead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: תֵּדַע לְךָ שֶׁהֲרֵי אֵינָהּ מְקַדֶּשֶׁת, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן דְּכַיְילָא בְּעִשָּׂרוֹן דְּחוֹל. הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? בִּשְׁלָמָא בִּיסָא – לָא כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא דְּלֶעֱבֵיד בִּיסָא לְמֵילַשׁ בֵּיהּ, כִּי לָשׁ לַהּ עַל גַּבֵּי קַטְבֻלְיָא – לֵית לַן בַּהּ. אֶלָּא עִשָּׂרוֹן, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: עֲבֵיד עִשָּׂרוֹן וּכְיֵיל בֵּיהּ – שָׁבֵיק עִשָּׂרוֹן דְּקוֹדֶשׁ וְכָיֵיל בְּעִשָּׂרוֹן דְּחוֹל?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **How can** these cases **be compared? Granted,** one can say that the shewbread was kneaded on a leather spread rather than in **a receptacle,** as **the Merciful One does not write** in the Torah **that one should** specifically **use a receptacle to knead** the shewbread. Therefore, **when one kneads** the dough of a meal offering **on top of a leather spread, we have no** problem **with it. But** in the case of the two loaves whose flour was measured in a vessel used for measuring **a tenth** of an ephah, **since the Merciful One states** explicitly to **use** a vessel of **a tenth** of an ephah **and measure with it,** can it be suggested that the one preparing the meal offering should **leave aside** the **consecrated** vessel for **a tenth** of an ephah **and measure with a non-sacred** vessel for **a tenth** of an ephah?

The Gemara then raises a follow-up: if this reasoning is accepted, why could Rabbi Yonatan not have responded similarly to the proof from *shtei halechem*? When Rabbi Yonatan argued that *shtei halechem* acquires *kedusha* only after baking because dry measures are not sanctified, one might have countered: perhaps that case involved using an unsanctified household dry measure (*bisa de-chol*) while the sanctified dry measures in the *Mikdash* were in fact sanctified and would have conferred *kedusha*. Why doesn't the "just use a different vessel" argument work there too?

The Gemara's answer establishes a fundamental distinction. When it comes to the *bisa*, the Torah never prescribed its use. Since there is no obligation to knead in a bowl, one cannot assume the Torah's baseline involves a sanctified kneading vessel. *Lo katav rachmana de-leivad be-bisa* — the Torah never said to use a *bisa*. Kneading on a table is entirely normative.

But when it comes to measuring, the Torah explicitly requires *isaron* — a specific measure. *Keivan de-amar rachmana avid isaron* — since the Torah mandates using an *isaron* measure, the Torah's baseline assumption is that one uses the *isaron* of the *Mikdash*, not one brought from home. *Shavik isaron de-kodesh ve-chayal be-isaron de-chol?* — would the Torah simply assume one bypasses the sanctified measuring vessel of the Sanctuary and uses a secular one from home? That assumption is untenable. Since the Torah requires a measured *isaron*, it assumes the standard Sanctuary measuring vessel — and therefore, if that vessel confers no *kedusha*, we learn that the dry measuring vessels are in fact not sanctified.

The lesson is one of asymmetry between prescription and possibility. Where the Torah prescribes a particular act — measuring by *isaron* — we read the Torah as assuming the standard Sanctuary implementation of that act, which makes the sanctity-status of those vessels directly relevant. Where the Torah prescribes no particular implement — kneading — no assumption about which vessel is used can be imposed, and the Torah cannot be read to predicate a prohibition on the contingent use of a *bisa*. This asymmetry resolves the apparent parallel and closes the discussion.
