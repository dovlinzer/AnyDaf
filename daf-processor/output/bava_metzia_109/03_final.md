# Bava Metzia 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Choker & Shatla Intro

### Kablan vs. Choker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְשָׁנִים מוּעָטוֹת – לֹא יִזְרָעֶנָּה פִּשְׁתָּן, וְאֵין לוֹ בְּקוֹרוֹת שִׁקְמָה. קִיבְּלָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ לְשֶׁבַע שָׁנִים – שָׁנָה רִאשׁוֹנָה יִזְרָעֶנָּה פִּשְׁתָּן, וְיֵשׁ לוֹ בְּקוֹרוֹת שִׁקְמָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One **who receives a field from another** to cultivate **for a few years,** i.e., fewer than seven, **may not plant flax in it,** as flax greatly weakens the soil, **and** if a sycamore tree was growing in the field, **he does not have** rights **to** the **beams** fashioned from the branches of the **sycamore** tree. Therefore, he may not cut down its branches for his own use, as it takes many years for new ones to grow. If **he received** the field **from him for seven years,** in **the first year he** may **plant flax in it, and he does have** rights **to** the **beams** fashioned from the branches of the **sycamore** tree.

Daf 109 opens by returning from the discussion of *bar metzra* to the laws of *sechirut* — renting land. This daf introduces not only the *choker* but a new figure who will occupy much of the subsequent discussion: the *shatla*, the planter. We have previously encountered the *kablan*, who rents land at a fixed ratio of the produce, and the *choker*, who rents for a fixed sum regardless of yield. The two figures operate under meaningfully different legal frameworks, and that distinction drives the opening *mishnah*. The *shatla*, by contrast, is not a renter in the traditional sense at all — he is hired specifically to plant, receives compensation in a particular form, and if he breaks his contract, distinct rules govern what he owes and what he may claim.

### Flax & Sycamore Restrict…

The *mishnah* begins: *ha-mekabel sadeh me-chavero le-shanim mu'atot* — one who rents land from another *be-chakirut*, at a fixed cost, for a small number of years. Rashi explains, and the *seifa* of the *mishnah* confirms, that *shanim mu'atot* means fewer than seven years. Such a renter *lo yizra bah pishtan* — may not plant flax. The reason, as Rashi explains, is that *pishtan* damages the soil. This restriction also proves, according to Rashi, that the *mishnah* is discussing a *choker* rather than a *kablan*: a *kablan* who rents at a fixed ratio of produce has already agreed with the landowner on what to plant. As we saw in an earlier *mishnah*, a *kablan* may not substitute a crop that is worse for the land than the one contracted — *ha-mekabel sadeh me-chavero lizroa chitin lo yizra'enah se'orin, lizroa se'orin lo yizra'enah chitin* — but may substitute something less harmful. Flax, being harmful to the soil, would be permissible for a *kablan* who expressly contracted for it. Since the *mishnah* prohibits it without qualification, it must be addressing the *choker* who pays a fixed rent.

The *mishnah* further rules: *ve-ein lo be-kurot shikmah* — this short-term *choker* has no rights to the wood produced by the sycamore trees on the property. The particular sycamore common in *Eretz Yisrael* would regularly regenerate after being cut for its wood, but the regrowth cycle takes seven years. That biological fact underlies the entire framework that follows.

### Seven-Year Rental Rules

If the rental is for a full seven-year period, the calculus changes. *Shanah rishonah yizra'enah pishtan* — in the first year, the renter may plant flax, because over a seven-year lease the soil will recover before the land reverts to the owner. *Ve-yesh lo be-kurot shikmah* — and implicitly, in that first year, the renter may also cut the sycamore and use its wood for building, because by the end of his lease the trees will have regenerated to the state they were in at the outset.

## Sycamore Overgrowth Deba…

### Abaye: Gets Shevach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּקוֹרוֹת שִׁקְמָה – אֵין לוֹ, בִּשְׁבַח שִׁקְמָה – יֵשׁ לוֹ. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁבַח שִׁקְמָה נָמֵי אֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Abaye says:** Although **he does not have** rights **to** the **beams** fashioned from the branches of the **sycamore** tree, **he does have** rights **to** the value of **the enhancement of the sycamore** tree, i.e., the value of its growth that occurred while he was cultivating the field. **And Rava says: He does not even have** rights **to** the value of **the enhancement of the sycamore** tree.

The *gemara* opens with Abaye's comment on the *mishnah*: *be-kurot shikmah ein lo* — correct, the short-term renter has no right to the normal wood of the sycamore. *Be-shevach shikmah yesh lo* — but if the sycamore grows beyond the expected amount, that excess growth is his. The *tenai* of the rental, Abaye reasons, was that he would have no rights to the ordinary growth of the sycamore; the excess, however, was not part of that agreement, and he has a claim to compensation for it.

### Rava: No Shevach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁבַח שִׁקְמָה אֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: He does not even have** rights **to** the value of **the enhancement of the sycamore** tree.

Rava disagrees: *afilu be-shevach shikmah ein lo* — even for the excess growth, the renter receives nothing.

### Vegetables Counterargume…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְהִגִּיעַ זְמַנּוֹ לָצֵאת – שָׁמִין לוֹ. מַאי לָאו: שָׁמִין לוֹ בִּשְׁבַח שִׁקְמָה? לָא, שָׁמִין לוֹ יַרְקָא וְסִילְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rava's opinion from a *baraita*: With regard to **one who receives a field from another** to cultivate **and his time to leave arrives,** the court **appraises** its value **for him. What, is it not** that the court **appraises for him** the value of **the enhancement of the sycamore** or other trees? The Gemara responds: **No,** the court **appraises for him** the value of **the vegetables and beets** left in the field.

The *gemara* attempts to refute Rava from a *baraita*: *ha-mekabel sadeh me-chavero ve-higi'a zemano latzet, shamin lo* — when a renter's time to leave arrives, an evaluation is made of what is owed to him. The *gemara* asks: does this not mean that we evaluate the excess growth of the sycamore trees, refuting Rava? The *gemara* deflects: *lo, shamin lo yarak u-silka* — no, the evaluation is made for vegetables growing on the property. But this prompts an immediate question: why evaluate vegetables at all? Vegetables can simply be taken and sold — *yarka ve-ashla na'akor venishtakar*. Why is an evaluation (*shimin lo*) necessary?

### Unripe Vegetables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַרְקָא וְסִילְקָא – נַעֲקוֹר וְנִשְׁקוֹל! בִּדְלָא מְטָא יוֹמָא דְשׁוּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: If it is referring to **vegetables and beets, let him uproot and take** them. The Gemara explains: It is referring to a case **where the market day has not** yet **arrived,** so that if he uproots them now he will not be able to sell them. He therefore leaves them for the owner of the land and receives money instead.

The answer is *d'lo matei yoma d'shuka* — the vegetables are not yet ready for market, either because market day has not arrived or because they are not yet ripe. Since the renter cannot simply take them along, the *beit din* evaluates their worth and the owner compensates him accordingly. The first attempted refutation of Rava thus fails.

### Shvi'it Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְהִגִּיעַ שְׁבִיעִית, שָׁמִין לוֹ. שְׁבִיעִית מִי קָא מַפְקְעָא אַרְעָא? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הַמְקַבֵּל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְהִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל, שָׁמִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof from a *baraita*: In the case of one **who receives a field from another** to cultivate **and the Sabbatical** Year **arrived,** the court **appraises** it **for him.** The Gemara first expresses puzzlement over the *baraita* itself: **Does** the **Sabbatical** Year **release land** from the one who contracted to cultivate it? The arrangement is in effect it during this time, so why is there a need for an appraisal? The Gemara responds: **Rather, say** that the *baraita* reads as follows: In the case of one **who receives a field from another** to cultivate **and the Jubilee** Year **arrived,** the court **appraises** it **for him.**

A second attempt is made: *ha-mekabel sadeh me-chavero ve-higi'ah Shvi'it, shamin lo* — if the *shemitah* year arrives during a rental, an evaluation is made. This too seems to prove that a renter is owed some assessed value upon having to vacate. But the *gemara* rejects this proof as well: *Shvi'it mi ka mafkat ara'a?* — the sabbatical year does not remove the land from the renter's possession. He still holds the rental; *shemitah* merely prohibits working the land. There is no *din* of *shemitah* that requires the renter to depart, and so this proof cannot establish anything about the evaluation owed to a departing renter.

### Yovel Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, יוֹבֵל מִי מַפְקְעָא קַבְּלָנוּת? ״לִצְמִיתֻת״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְהִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל – שָׁמִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still,** this is difficult; **does** the **Jubilee** Year **release** the term of **a contractor? The Merciful One states: "Permanently"** (Leviticus 25:23), which indicates that only land that was permanently sold returns to its owner, whereas land that was rented does not revert to the owner at the Jubilee Year. **Rather, say** that the *baraita* reads as follows: In the case of **one who purchases a field from another and the Jubilee** Year **arrives,** the court **appraises** it **for him.**

The *gemara* corrects itself: the *baraita* must actually read *ve-higi'ah Yovel* — the Jubilee year — not *Shvi'it*. When *Yovel* arrives, an evaluation is made for the renter. Now the question becomes: *Yovel mi mafkat kablanu?* — does the Jubilee year actually undo a rental agreement? The *gemara* clarifies: *litzmitut amar Rachmana* — the Torah's rule of *Yovel* applies to land sold *litzmitut*, permanently. The verses describing *Yovel* pertain only to outright sales, not to rentals. Therefore the *baraita* cannot be about a *mekabel* at all — it must be about *halokeach sedei chaveiro ve-higi'ah Yovel* — one who purchased a field, whose ownership *Yovel* does undo. And the evaluation made upon *Yovel* must be for the *shevach shikmah* — the excess growth of the sycamore — not for vegetables, because *silka ve-yarka be-Yovel hefkeira hu* — during *Yovel*, vegetables are *hefker*, ownerless, belonging to all; an evaluation of something that belongs to no one is meaningless. The *baraita* thus appears to require compensating the departing buyer for the excess sycamore growth, directly contradicting Rava's position that *afilu shevach ein lo*.

### Yovel Is Exceptional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, שָׁמִין לוֹ בְּיַרְקָא וְסִילְקָא? סִילְקָא וְיַרְקָא בְּיוֹבֵל הֶפְקֵירָא הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁבַח שִׁקְמָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **so too** here it means that the court **appraises** it **for him with regard to vegetables and beets,** this cannot be, as **vegetables and beets in the Jubilee** Year **are ownerless.** This is because the Jubilee is like the Sabbatical Year in that any produce that grows is ownerless and may be taken by anyone. **Rather,** is the *baraita* **not** referring to **the enhancement of** the value of **the sycamore that occurred** during the time he had owned the field? It must be referring to this, and therefore presents a difficulty to Rava.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ אַבָּיֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרָבָא: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְיָצָא מִמְכַּר בַּיִת״. מִמְכָּר – חוֹזֵר, שֶׁבַח – אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר. וְנִגְמַר מִינֵּיהּ? הָתָם זְבִינֵי מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, וְיוֹבֵל אַפְקַעְתָּא דְּמַלְכָּא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye interpreted** the *baraita* so that it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of** his disputant **Rava: There it is different, as the verse states: "Then the house that was sold shall go out**…in the Jubilee" (Leviticus 25:33), which teaches that the house or field that was **sold returns** to its owner, but the value of its **enhancement** does **not return,** but remains with the buyer. The Gemara asks: If so, **let us derive from it** a general *halakha* that the value of enhancement need not be returned. The Gemara answers: The two cases are dissimilar, as **there,** in the *baraita*, it is **a full-fledged sale, and the Jubilee** released it, as it **is a release of the King,** a Divine decree. Since the buyer had been in full ownership of the field, he keeps the value of the enhancement that occurred while it was his. By contrast, one who receives a field to cultivate is not an owner.

Abaye himself resolves the difficulty on behalf of Rava: *shani hatam* — *Yovel* is different. *De-amar kra ve-yatza bi-memkar* — the verse says the land "goes out" from the sale. *Memkar chozer, shevach eino chozer* — the sale itself reverts, but the added value does not revert with it. The Torah establishes a special rule that when *Yovel* undoes a sale, the buyer retains compensation for the *shevach* he caused. This is a particular *din* anchored in explicit verses and in the unique nature of *Yovel* — *afki'ata de-malka hi* — God simply overrides the buyer's ownership — and it is further confined to full sales, *zevina me'alya*.

One might then ask: even if *Yovel* has this particular rule, why can we not derive from it a general principle that applies also to renters? *Ve-nigmar minei* — let us learn from *Yovel* that a renter is also owed *shevach*. The *gemara* rejects this as well: *hatam zevina me'alya hu* — that is a full sale, a fundamentally different transaction. *Yovel* is an exceptional divine decree applicable to a singular category, and one cannot extend its particular rules to the ordinary case of rental. Rava's position — *afilu be-shevach shikmah ein lo* — therefore survives all three attempted refutations.

## Rav Pappa Alfalfa Case

### Trees on Alfalfa Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא קַבֵּיל אַרְעָא לְאַסְפַּסְתָּא. קְדַחוּ בַּהּ תָּאלֵי. כִּי קָא מִסְתַּלַּק, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לִי שְׁבָחָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי לְרַב פָּפָּא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דִּיקְלָא וַאֲלֵים, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּבָעֵי מָר שְׁבָחֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם לָאו אַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי נָחֵית, אֲנָא הָכָא אַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי נְחֵיתְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa received land** as a contractor **for** growing **hay.** During the time he was cultivating it, **palm trees sprouted in** the ground. **When he left** the land **he said to** the owners of the field: **Give me** the value of **the enhancement** to the field from the palm trees. **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said to Rav Pappa: If that is so,** i.e., if your claim is justified, then in a situation where there was **a palm tree and it grew thick, so too would the Master,** i.e., Rav Pappa, **want** to be paid for the value of the tree's **enhancement?** Rav Pappa **said to him: There,** in that theoretical case, the cultivator **did not descend** to the field **with that** possibility **in mind,** as the cultivator considered only the consumption of the date palm's fruit, not its growth. **Here,** in my case, **I did** indeed **descend** to the field **with this in mind,** as I anticipated receiving compensation for any growth of the field.

The *gemara* now shifts to a series of practical cases exploring the boundaries of *shevach* claims. *Rav Pappa kabil ar'a le-asasta* — Rav Pappa once rented land to plant alfalfa, fodder for animals. *Karchu bah talei* — trees sprouted unexpectedly on the land during his tenancy. When his lease ended, *amar lehu havu li shivcha* — he said, give me the excess value for these trees. His argument: he had not contracted for trees, yet they grew during his semi-ownership of the property, and he is entitled to compensation for them.

### Palm Tree Hypothetical

Rav Shisha son of Rav Idi challenged him: *ela me'ata dikla be-alma, hachi nami devayei mar shevcha?* — by that reasoning, if you had rented land to grow palm trees and they grew more than expected, would you also demand *shevach* for the excess growth of the palms?

### Short vs. Long-Term Inte…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? כְּאַבַּיֵּי, דְּאָמַר: בִּשְׁבַח שִׁקְמָה יֵשׁ לוֹ. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא כְּרָבָא, הָתָם לֵית לֵיהּ פְּסֵידָא. הָכָא אִיכָּא פְּסֵידָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion did he make this statement? Is it not **in accordance with** the opinion of **Abaye, who says** that **he does have** rights **to** the value of **the enhancement of the sycamore?** The Gemara refutes this claim: **You** may **even say** that he holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava,** as **there** the cultivator suffers **no loss** when the sycamore grows in the field, so he is not entitled to the value of the enhancement as compensation. By contrast, **here there is a loss,** as the palm trees that sprouted occupied space designated for hay.

Rav Pappa draws a distinction. *Hatam lav ada'ata dehachi nachit ana* — in that case, if I had rented land to grow palms and they grew more than expected, that was never my intent; I rented to get the fruit. *Hacha ada'ata dehachi nachitna* — here, my purpose in renting was to plant things and cultivate growth. The assumption underlying my agreement was that new growth occurring during my tenancy would accrue to me, and these sprouting trees represent exactly that. The *gemara* notes that this position appears consistent with Abaye's, which raises a difficulty — we generally do not rule like Abaye against Rava in matters of *halacha*. The *gemara* deflects: *afilu teima ke-Rava* — one may read Rav Pappa's position as consistent with Rava as well. *Hatam leit leh pseida* — in the original case of the *mishnah*, Rava held that the renter gets no *shevach* because no loss accrued to him from the sycamore's presence; the sycamore was always there. *Hacha ika pseida* — here there is a loss, because land was occupied by these sprouting trees where alfalfa could have been planted.

The *gemara* presses: what exactly is the loss? *Mai pseidatich?* — the land was rented for alfalfa, you planted your alfalfa, what is the damage? *Shkul yada depasti ve-zil* — take whatever alfalfa is on the property now and leave. Alfalfa is apparently planted and harvested monthly; why cannot he simply take what remains?

### Saffron Loss Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי פְּסִידָיךְ יְדָא דְּאַסְפַּסְתָּא, שְׁקוֹל יְדָא דְאַסְפַּסְתָּא וְזִיל! אָמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא כּוּרְכְּמָא רִישְׁקָא רַבַּאי. אָמַר לֵיהּ: גַּלֵּית אַדַּעְתָּךְ דִּלְמִשְׁקַל וְאִסְתַּלּוֹקֵי עֲבַדְתְּ – שְׁקֹל כּוּרְכְּמָא רִישְׁקָא וְזִיל, אֵין לְךָ אֶלָּא דְּמֵי עֵצִים בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheisha **said to Rav Pappa:** But here too, the owner of the field can say: **What loss have I caused you?** I have caused you to lose **a handful of hay. Take a handful of hay and go.** Rav Pappa **said to him:** I claim that **I grew garden saffron** there. He claimed that he lost land that he could have used for the cultivation of expensive produce, not only hay. Rav Sheisha **said to him:** Even so, you admit that you wanted the land for other plantings, not to plant palm trees, and **you** have thereby **revealed your intention that you acted** so as **to take** the produce **and leave. Take** your **garden saffron and leave,** as **you have** rights **only** to **the value of wood alone.** Since you did not mean to grow these trees, you are entitled only to the price you could have received for the palm trees had you had uprooted them and sold them as wood during the time you cultivated the field.

At this point Rav Pappa reveals: *ana karkuma rishka ravai* — I did not actually grow alfalfa; I grew saffron, a high-value crop. The *gemara* responds: *galeita da'atach d'lemishkal u-leistalukei avadt* — by saying that, you have disclosed that your entire intent was to plant something short-lived, something you would harvest and leave. *Shevach* accrues on the assumption that the renter was planting things with longevity, investments that extend beyond the term of his tenancy and yield value that belongs partly to him. But a crop that is grown and taken within a short cycle — where the renter never intended to leave anything of lasting value in the ground — generates no such entitlement. *Shkul karkuma rishka ve-zil, ein lach ela dmei etzim bilvad* — take your saffron and go; you are owed nothing for these new trees except their value as firewood. The distinction matters: the assessed future value of a growing sapling, including its eventual fruit, would be far greater than its firewood value. But since the renter's entire orientation was toward short-term, annually harvested crops, he forfeits any claim to the long-term appreciation and is limited to *dmei etzim*, the value of the trees as raw wood.

### Rav Bibi Hedge Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי קַבֵּיל אַרְעָא וְאַהְדַּר לֵיהּ מְשׁוּנִּיתָא, קְדַחוּ בֵּיהּ זַרְדָּתָא. כִּי קָא מִיסְתַּלַּק אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לִי שְׁבָחַאי. אָמַר רַב פַּפֵּי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתֵיתוּ מִמּוּלָאֵי אָמְרִיתוּ מִילֵּי מוּלְיָיתָא?! אֲפִילּוּ רַב פָּפָּא לָא אֲמַר אֶלָּא דְּאִית לֵיהּ פְּסֵידָא, הָכָא מַאי פְּסֵידָא אִית לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: **Rav Beivai bar Abaye received land** to cultivate **and** he **surrounded it** with **a fence** made **of earth.** In the meantime, **trees sprouted in it. When he left** the field **he said to** the owners: **Give me my** value of the **enhancement** of the trees that sprouted. **Rav Pappi said:** Is it **because you come from unfortunate people** that **you say unfortunate** and unsound **words?** Abaye's family came from the family of Eli, whose descendants were sentenced to die at a young age. Rav Pappi explains: **Even Rav Pappa said only** that he is entitled to the value of the enhancement of the palm trees **when** he **has** suffered **a loss,** as they take up part of the field. **Here,** by contrast, **what loss do you have?** As the trees sprouted in a place that would have been left unplanted, you have not lost anything and you are not entitled to compensation.

A parallel case: *Rav Bibi bar Abaye kabal ar'a ve-hadar lah mishunita* — Rav Bibi bar Abaye rented land and a hedge or stone border formed around it. *Kadchu bei zaradta* — some small plants grew up among the stones along the border. When his tenancy ended, *amar lehu hav li shvachai* — he claimed the excess value of these border plants. Rav Pappa dismisses the claim sharply. Even Rav Pappa's own ruling — that *shevach* is owed when there is a genuine *pseida* — was limited to cases where the new growth actually caused a loss by occupying space where the renter wanted to plant. *Hacha mai pseida is lach?* — here there is no such loss. The plants grew along the stone border, not within the cultivated field, and caused the renter no damage whatsoever. His claim to *shevach* for those plants therefore fails entirely.

## Shatla & Inheritance

### Five Sons-in-Law Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא שַׁתָּלָא, שְׁכֵיב וּשְׁבַק חַמְשָׁה חַתְנָווֹתָא. אֲמַר עַד הָאִידָּנָא חַד, הַשְׁתָּא חַמְשָׁה. עַד הָאִידָּנָא לָא הֲווֹ סָמְכוּ אַהֲדָדֵי וְלָא מַפְסְדוּ לִי, הַשְׁתָּא חַמְשָׁה סָמְכוּ אַהֲדָדֵי וּמַפְסְדוּ לִי. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי שָׁקְלִיתוּ שְׁבָחַיְיכוּ וּמִסְתַּלְּקִיתוּ – מוּטָב, וְאִי לָא – מְסַלֵּיקְנָא לְכוּ בְּלָא שְׁבָחָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rav Yosef had a certain planter** whose job it was to plant trees, similar to a sharecropper. **He died and left** behind **five sons-in-law.** Rav Yosef **said: Until now** I had to deal with only **one** person; **now** there are **five. Until now they did not rely on each other** to plant the trees **and did not cause me a loss,** as the responsibility was their father-in-law's, but **now** that they are **five they will rely on each other** to plant the trees **and cause me a loss.** In light of these considerations, he decided to discontinue the agreement with them. Rav Yosef **said to them: If you take** the value of **your enhancement** that you brought to the field **and remove** yourselves, all is **well, but if not, I will remove you without** giving you the value of **the enhancement.**

The *gemara* presents a case involving Rav Yosef. *Rav Yosef hava lei hahu shatla* — Rav Yosef once employed a *shatla*, a planter. *Shachiv ve-shavik chamsha chatnavata* — the planter died and left behind five sons-in-law as his inheritors. Rav Yosef's reaction captures his practical concern: *ad hashta chad, hashta chamsha* — until now I had one; now I have five. The problem is not merely numerical. *Ad hashta lo havu samchu ahadadi ve-lo masdu li* — with only one worker, that individual relied on himself and a loss to Rav Yosef was unlikely. *Hashta chamsha samchu ahadadi ve-masdu li* — with five sons-in-law, they will rely on one another, no one will take full responsibility, and Rav Yosef anticipates real damage to his property.

### Shevach Offer

Rav Yosef presents them with two options: *ei shaklitu shvacha u-mistalkitu* — if you take the *shevach*, the value that accrued to the land through your father-in-law's work, and leave, *mutav* — that is agreeable to him. *Ve-ei lo, mesalikna lechu bela shvacha* — if not, he claims the right to remove them without paying anything. He frames this as a generous offer: the good option is take the money and go; the bad option, which he claims is also legally available to him, is to evict them empty-handed.

### Rav Yehuda Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב הוּנָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב נַחְמָן: הַאי שַׁתָּלָא דִּשְׁכֵיב – יוֹרְשִׁין דִּילֵיהּ (מִסְתַּלְּקִין) [מְסַלְּקִינַן] לְהוּ בְּלָא שְׁבָחָא. וְלָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef explains his statement: **As Rav Yehuda says, and some say** it was **Rav Huna, and some say** it was **Rav Naḥman:** With regard to **this planter who died, his heirs may be removed without** receiving the value of the **enhancement.** The Gemara comments: **But this is not correct,** as the *halakha* is not in accordance with this opinion.

To support the threat, Rav Yosef invokes an authority: *de-amar Rav Yehuda* — and perhaps it was said in the name of Rav Huna or Rav Nachman — *hai shatla de-shachiv, yorshim dilei mistalkim lehu bela shvacha* — when a planter dies, his heirs are removed without receiving any *shevach*.

### Rav Yehuda Clarified

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *ve-lav milta hi* — that is not actually a correct statement of law. Rav Yehuda apparently never said anything so sweeping. His actual ruling was considerably more limited, and Rav Yosef was extending it beyond its scope in order to intimidate the sons-in-law into accepting the settlement offer. The *rishonim* are explicit: Rivan on 29b explains that Rav Yosef never actually said this as a rule; he said it *le-hafchidan* — to frighten them into accepting the *shevach* and departing quietly. The Rosh in *siman* 37 cites this explanation as well, and the Ramban grounds it in a *tosefta* that circumscribes the scope of Rav Yehuda's actual ruling. Had only one of the five sons-in-law presented himself and asked to continue working, Rav Yosef would have been obligated to accept him; the multiplicity of heirs created a legitimate basis for some form of objection, but not for outright denial of any *shevach*.

## Loss Forfeit Conditions

### Damage Forfeiture Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא שַׁתָּלָא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי מַפְסֵדְינָא מִסְתַּלַּקְנָא. אַפְסֵיד. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מִסְתַּלַּק בְּלָא שְׁבָחָא. רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: מִסְתַּלַּק וְשָׁקֵיל שְׁבָחָא. וּמוֹדֶה רַב כָּהֲנָא דְּאִי אָמַר אִי [מַ]פְסֵידְנָא מִסְתַּלַּקְנָא בְּלָא שְׁבָחָא, מִסְתַּלַּק בְּלָא שְׁבָחָא. רָבָא אָמַר: אַסְמַכְתָּא הִיא, וְאַסְמַכְתָּא לָא קָנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident. There was **a certain planter who said to** the owner: **If I cause a loss** to the vineyard by ruining its plants **I will leave** the field. Ultimately, he indeed **caused a loss** to the plants, but some enhancement from the time he began did still exist. **Rav Yehuda said: He leaves without** receiving payment for **the enhancement,** while **Rav Kahana said: He leaves and takes the** payment for the **enhancement** resulting from his work. **And Rav Kahana concedes that if he said: If I cause a loss I will leave without** receiving payment for **the enhancement, he** indeed **leaves without** receiving payment for **the enhancement.** Conversely, **Rava said:** A promise of this kind, which he does not expect to have to fulfill, **is a transaction with inconclusive intent [*asmakhta*], and** the *halakha* is that **an *asmakhta* does not effect acquisition** and is therefore not legally binding.

The *gemara* turns to a new case. A *shatla* stipulates at the outset: *im afsidehu, mesalkana af afsid* — if I cause a loss, I will leave, having forfeited the *shevach*.

### Yehuda vs. Kahana

Rav Yehuda rules: *mistalek belo shevach* — he leaves without the *shevach* that accrued, but he does receive his base wages. Rav Kahana rules the opposite: *mistalek be-shevach* — he does take the *shevach* despite having caused a loss. Notably, Rav Kahana concedes one narrower case: if the *shatla* explicitly said *im afsidna, mistalakna belo shevach* — if I cause damage, I will leave without *shevach* — then in that case he does indeed leave without the *shevach*. The linguistic precision of the stipulation matters to Rav Kahana.

### Asmachta Unenforceable

Rabba intervenes with a foundational principle: *asmachta hi* — this is an *asmachta*, a verbal condition that goes beyond what the person truly meant to commit to, and *asmachta lo kanyah* — an *asmachta* does not take legal effect. When a worker says "if I cause a loss, it's all on me, I forfeit everything," he cannot genuinely mean to surrender his base wages for labor already performed. Such a declaration is the kind of thing people say loosely — "don't worry, it's all on me" — without a serious legal intention to forfeit their earned compensation. The law therefore does not enforce it as stated.

### Deduction vs. Forfeiture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא מַאי שְׁנָא מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: אִם אוֹבִיר וְלָא אַעֲבֵיד אֲשַׁלֵּם בְּמֵיטְבָא! הָתָם מַאי דְּאַפְסֵיד – מְשַׁלֵּם, הָכָא מַאי דְּאַפְסֵיד – מְנַכֵּינַן לֵיהּ וְאִידַּךְ יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rava,** in **what** way **is** it **different from that which we learned** in a mishna (104a) that the recipient of the field stipulates: **If I let** the field **lie fallow and do not cultivate** it **I will pay with the best**-quality produce, in which case he is obligated to fulfill his promise? The Gemara answers: **There, he pays** for **the loss he caused,** while **here, we deduct from him the loss that he caused, and we give him the other** portion of the money. He does not forfeit all that was due to him just because some of his plantings were unsuccessful.

One might ask: is this not precisely the language used in a well-known *mishnah* — *im ovir ve-lo avid, ashalem be-meitivah* — if I leave the field fallow and fail to work it, I will pay back with the best? Does the *mishnah* not enforce such conditional language? Rabba distinguishes: *hatam mai de-afsid meshallem* — in that *mishnah*, the worker pays only for the damage he actually caused, measure for measure. *Hacha mai de-afsid mankinan leh ve-idach yahvinan leh* — here, the damage caused is deducted from what he is owed, but the remainder of his wages is still paid. The critical difference is between forfeiture of everything and deduction of the actual loss. A condition that amounts to total forfeiture of earned wages is an *asmachta* and is unenforceable; a proportional deduction for actual damage is simply an assessment of net wages owed and requires no special condition to operate.

## Runya Firing Case

### Runya Evicted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רוּנְיָא שַׁתָּלָא דְרָבִינָא הֲוָה אַפְסֵיד. סַלְּקֵיהּ אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ חֲזִי מָר מַאי קָא עָבֵיד לִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר עֲבַיד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא לֹא הִתְרָה בִּי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא (צְרִיכָא לְהַתְרוֹת) [צְרִיכַתְּ לְאַתְרוֹיֵי]. רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: מַקְרֵי דַרְדְּקֵי שַׁתָּלָא טַבָּחָא וְאוּמָּנָא
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Runya was the planter of Ravina.** He **caused a loss,** and Ravina **removed him** from his field. Runya **came before Rava** and **said to him:** Let **the Master see what** Ravina **has done to me.** Rava **said to him:** Ravina **did well,** as you caused him a loss. Runya **said to him: But** Ravina **did not warn me** beforehand. How can he force me to leave without prior warning? Rava **said to him:** In this case it **is not necessary to provide a warning.** The Gemara comments: **Rava** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as Rava said:** With regard to **a teacher of children, a planter, a ritual slaughterer, and a bloodletter,**

The *gemara* presents a case involving a planter named Runya who worked for Ravina. *Afsid salki* — he caused some damage and was dismissed. *Ata lekamei de-Rabba* — Runya came before Rabba to complain. *Amar leh chazi mar mai ka-avid li* — look at what he did to me. *Amar leh shapir avid* — Rabba replied: he acted correctly.

### At-Will Employment

Runya protested: *ha lo hitrah bi* — he gave me no warning before dismissing me. Rabba's response is categorical: *lo tsarich le-hatrah* — no warning was required whatsoever. Rabba then articulates a broader principle: certain categories of workers are at-will hires, and no advance notice is necessary before dismissing them. He lists: *makrei dardakei, shatla, tavcha, umana ve-safra mata* — teachers of young children, planters, butchers, bloodletters, and the town scribe.

### Irrecoverable Loss Doctr…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָפַר מָתָא – כּוּלָּן כְּמוּתְרִין וְעוֹמְדִין דָּמֵי. כְּלָלָא דְּמִילְּתָא: כֹּל פְּסֵידָא דְּלָא הָדַר – כְּמוּתְרִין וְעוֹמְדִין דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **and a town scribe** who drafts documents on behalf on the local residents, **all of these are considered forewarned.** Therefore, any loss incurred due to them is deducted from their wages, and they are fined without the need for prior warning. **The principle of** this **matter** is: With regard to **any loss that is not recoverable they are considered forewarned.**

Rava formulates the underlying rationale as a general rule: *kol pseida de-lo hadar* — any loss which is irrecoverable — that worker is treated as an at-will hire who may be dismissed without warning. A teacher who neglects children, a planter who damages crops, a butcher who renders meat *treif* — in each case, the damage cannot be undone. Because the nature of the work is such that a single instance of carelessness causes permanent, unrecoverable harm, the employer's right to terminate immediately without notice is recognized as an overriding consideration. This rule will be elaborated further in *Bava Batra*.

## Three-Way Shevach Split

### Early Departure Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא שַׁתָּלָא, דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לִי שְׁבָחַאי דְּבָעֵינָא לְמִיסַּק לְאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לֵיהּ שְׁבָחֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִיהוּ אַשְׁבַּח, אַרְעָא לָא אַשְׁבַּח! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא פַּלְגָא דִּשְׁבָחָא קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַד הָאִידָּנָא הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת פַּלְגָא וְשַׁתָּלָא פַּלְגָא. הַשְׁתָּא בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב מְנָתָא לַאֲרִיסָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רִיבְעָא דִשְׁבָחָא קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain planter who said to** the owner of the field: **Give me** the value of **my enhancement because I wish to ascend to Eretz Yisrael.** The owner **came** for a ruling **before Rav Pappa bar Shmuel,** who **said to him: Give** the planter **the value of his enhancement. Rava said to** Rav Pappa bar Shmuel: Did **he** alone **enhance** the field, while **the land did not enhance it?** He cannot be credited with all the improvement. Rav Pappa bar Shmuel **said to** Rava: **I am telling you** that he is entitled to **half** the value of **the enhancement.** Rava **said to him: Until now the owner of the land would take half and the planter** would take **half,** while the planter would work the field. **Now** the owner **also needs to give a portion to the sharecropper,** so that the sharecropper will continue working the field. Rav Pappa bar Shmuel **said to him: I am telling you** to give him **one-quarter of** the value of **the enhancement,** half of the sum to which he is entitled.

The *gemara* introduces a new case involving a *shatla* who wishes to break his contract early. *D'ba'ina l'meisak l'Eretz Yisrael* — he wants to leave and go to *Eretz Yisrael*, a desire the *gemara* treats sympathetically. He accordingly requests: *havu li shivcha* — give me the *shevach* I am owed for the value I added to the land, and release me from the remainder of the agreement.

### Full Shevach Question

The case comes before Rav Papa bar Shmuel, who rules: *havu lei shivchei* — give him his *shevach*. Rava immediately questions the scope of this ruling: *ihu ashpach ara, lo ashpacha?* — was he alone responsible for all the value added to the land? Surely the land itself contributed to its own improvement. Does the *shatla* receive the entirety of the *shevach*?

### Half Only; Aris Factor

Rav Papa bar Shmuel clarifies: *plaga d'shivcha ka'amina lach* — I meant only half the *shevach*. Half reflects the land's own contribution, which belongs to the owner; the *shatla* is entitled to half. However, a further complication arises: *hashta ba'i l'meitav man talei arisa* — now that the *shatla* is departing early, the owner will need to hire a new *aris*, a tenant farmer, to continue working the land. That new tenant will also require compensation. The owner therefore cannot bear the full loss of that hire while simultaneously paying the *shatla* his full half. Since the *shatla's* early departure forces the owner into an additional expense, his share must be reduced accordingly.

### Quarter Not Half

Rav Papa bar Shmuel revises: *riva d'shivcha ka'amina* — he gets only a quarter of the *shevach*, not half, because part of what would have been the *shatla's* half must now cover the cost of the new *aris*.

Rav Ashi objects that even "a quarter" requires precision: *riva d'hu danka* — the quarter in question is actually a sixth. That is, it is not one quarter of the total *shevach*, but one quarter of the portion remaining after the *aris's* share is accounted for — which, mathematically, equals one sixth of the whole.

### Regional Norms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבַר רַב אָשֵׁי לְמֵימַר ״רִיבְעָא״ דְּהוּא דַּנְקָא. דְּאָמַר רַב מִנְיוֹמֵי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נְחוּמִי: בְּאַתְרָא דְּשָׁקֵיל שַׁתָּלָא פַּלְגָא וַאֲרִיסָא תִּילְתָּא, הַאי שַׁתָּלָא דְּבָעֵי לְאִסְתַּלּוֹקֵי – יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ שְׁבָחָא וּמְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא נִמְטְיֵיהּ הֶפְסֵד לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi thought to say** that when he referred to one-quarter he meant **one-quarter that is one-sixth,** i.e., one-quarter of the sum due to the owner, as the sum due to the owner is two-thirds of the entire yield. This payment would therefore amount to one-sixth of the total. **As Rav Minyumi, son of Rav Naḥumi, said: In a location where the planter takes half** the fruit **and the sharecropper** takes **one-third, with regard to a planter who wants to leave, we give him** his share of the value of **the enhancement and remove him** in such a manner **so that the homeowner should suffer no loss.**

To understand this, the *gemara* cites Rav Manyumi son of Rav Nachumi, who reports the regional custom: *b'atra d'shakil shatla palga ve-arisa tulta* — in a place where the *shatla* normally takes half and the *aris* normally takes a third. These are the baseline proportions against which the early-departure scenario is measured. The goal throughout the calculation is that *lo nimtei hefsed le-baal habayit* — no loss should be incurred by the owner.

## Fraction Calculations

### Reva D'Danka Math

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רִיבְעָא דְּהוּא דַּנְקָא – שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רִיבְעָא מַמָּשׁ, קָא מָטֵי לֵיהּ פְּסֵידָא לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת פַּלְגָא דַּנְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that he meant **one-quarter that is one-sixth,** this is **well** and the calculations are in order, **but if you say** he referred to **an actual quarter, the homeowner suffers the loss of half of one-sixth.** This is because if the owner had paid the planter initially he would have given him only one-half, whereas now he must give the planter one-quarter and the sharecropper one-third. He thereby pays an extra twelfth.

The mathematical question now receives close scrutiny. Rav Ashi's position is that what was called "a quarter" is in fact *riva d'hu danka* — a quarter which is a sixth of the total. The reasoning: because the owner must now hire an *aris* who will receive a full third of the total *shevach*, only two thirds of the pie remains to be divided between owner and departing *shatla*. One quarter of that two-thirds remainder equals one sixth of the whole. Assigning one sixth to the *shatla* and one third to the new *aris* yields a total of one sixth plus two sixths, or three sixths — exactly one half — meaning the owner retains his full half and suffers no loss. This is the *riva d'hu danka* solution: the "quarter" is a quarter of the remainder, which is a sixth of the whole.

### Ari Paid from Planter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ: אַנְתְּ מְנָתָא דִּילָךְ – הַב לֵיהּ לַאֲרִיסָא, וַאֲנָא מְנָתָא דִּילִי מַאי דְּבָעֵינָא עָבֵידְנָא בֵּיהּ. אָמַר: כִּי מָטֵית לִשְׁחִיטַת קֳדָשִׁים, תָּא וְאַקְשִׁי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Yosef, said to Rav Ashi: Let** the planter **say to** the owner: **You give your portion to the sharecropper, and I will do what I wish with my portion.** I performed my half of the work properly, so why should I suffer a loss because you want to pay the sharecropper? Rav Ashi **said** to him: **When you reach** the tractate of: **The slaughter of sacrificial** animals, i.e., tractate *Zevaḥim*, **come and ask** this difficulty **to me.** In other words, your question is a good one, worthy of a difficult tractate full of complex reasoning such as *Zevaḥim*.

This alternative makes *riva mamash* — an actual, literal quarter — workable. Under this approach, the *shatla* receives one quarter of the total *shevach*. The owner takes his full half. The new *aris* is then paid out of the *shatla's* quarter, not from the owner's share. Crucially, the *aris* in this scenario receives not one third of the whole but one third of the remainder after the *shatla* has taken his quarter. Since the *shatla* takes one quarter of the total, three quarters remain; the *aris* then receives one third of that three-quarter remainder, which is one quarter of the total. But this is the *shatla's* quarter — the *shatla* pays the *aris* from his own allocation. The *shatla* thus nets zero from his quarter (he gives it entirely to the *aris*), the owner keeps his half, and the *aris* receives a third of the portion that remained — not a full third of the whole, but a third of the three quarters, which is a quarter of the total.

### Owner Protection

The two solutions can be contrasted directly. In the *riva d'hu danka* approach: the *aris* receives a full third of the whole (two sixths), the *shatla* receives one sixth, and the owner retains one half. In the *riva mamash* approach: the owner again retains one half, the *shatla* receives one quarter and remits it to the *aris*, who consequently receives only one quarter rather than one third of the whole. Both arrangements protect the owner fully. The difference lies in whether the *aris* absorbs the cost of the *shatla's* early departure (in the *riva mamash* solution he receives less than his normal third) or whether the *shatla* alone bears that cost (in the *riva d'hu danka* solution, where his share is reduced from a half to a sixth).

### Rav Ashi Praised

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: כִּי מָטֵית לִשְׁחִיטַת קֳדָשִׁים, תָּא וְאַקְשִׁי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said** to him: **When you reach** the tractate of: **The slaughter of sacrificial** animals, i.e., tractate *Zevaḥim*, **come and ask** this difficulty **to me.**

Rav Ashi, upon hearing this resolution, concedes admiringly: *ki mateita li-shchitat kodshim, ta ve-achlah* — when you reach the *masechet* of *Zevachim* in your learning, come and study it with me. Rashi explains this as an expression of praise for the mathematical precision and analytical cleverness that the argument displayed.

## Old Vines Split

### Vine Value Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב מִנְיוֹמֵי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נְחוּמִי: בְּאַתְרָא דְּשָׁקֵיל שַׁתָּלָא פַּלְגָא וַאֲרִיסָא תִּילְתָּא, הַאי שַׁתָּלָא דְּבָעֵי אִיסְתַּלּוֹקֵי – יָהֲבִינַן לֵיהּ שְׁבָחֵיהּ וּמְסַלְּקִינַן לֵיהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיפְסוֹד בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. אָמַר רַב מִנְיוֹמֵי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נְחוּמִי: קוֹפָא סָבָא – פַּלְגָא. שַׁטְפַהּ נַהֲרָא – רִיבְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the** matter **itself, Rav Minyumi, son of Rav Naḥumi, said: In a location where the planter takes half** the fruit **and the sharecropper one-third,** with regard to **a planter who wants to leave, we give him** his share of the value of **the enhancement and remove him** in such a manner **so that the homeowner should suffer no loss. Rav Minyumi, son of Rav Naḥumi,** further **said:** With regard to **an old vine** in a vineyard, the planter receives **half.** Although he did not plant the vine, since he was placed in charge of the entire vineyard, he receives a portion of that which was there before. If **a river flooded** the vineyard and the owner and the planter come to divide the trees that fell down, the planter receives **one-quarter.**

Rav Manyumi son of Rav Nachumi adds a further ruling concerning *kufsa saba* — old, withered vines. The one who cultivates them takes half of their value when they wither in the ordinary course. However, *shtefah nahara* — if the vines are destroyed by a river flooding and carrying them away — he takes only one quarter of their value. The distinction apparently reflects the nature and degree of the loss, though the *gemara* does not elaborate further at this point.

## Mishkon Dried Trees

### Mortgage Dries Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּמַשְׁכֵּין פַּרְדֵּיסָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ לַעֲשַׂר שְׁנִין, וְקַשׁ לַחֲמֵשׁ שְׁנִין. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: פֵּירָא הָוֵי. רָבָא אָמַר: קַרְנָא הָוֵי, וְיִלָּקַח בּוֹ קַרְקַע, וְהוּא אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who mortgaged an orchard to another,** i.e., the creditor, **for ten years** in order that the latter should use the profits gained from the orchard as repayment of the debt. **But** the orchard **aged after five years** and no longer produced quality fruit. Consequently, the only way to proceed was to cut down its trees and sell them as wood. **Abaye said:** This wood **is** considered as **produce** of the orchard, and therefore the creditor is entitled to cut them down and sell them. **Rava said:** The wood **is** classified as **principal** and is therefore viewed as part of the orchard itself. Consequently, the creditor has no rights to the wood itself, **but** other **land** is **purchased with** the profits from the sale of the wood **and** the creditor **consumes the produce** of that land until the debt is repaid.

The *gemara* transitions to a related question concerning *mashkon* — a mortgage arrangement. The case: *hahu gavra de-mashken pardesa le-chavrei le-eser shanin* — a man mortgages his orchard to a creditor for ten years. The produce of the orchard will serve as repayment of the loan over that period. *Ve-kash le-chamesh shanin* — but the orchard dries out and dies after only five years, halfway through the mortgage term.

### Abaye: Pira Status

Abaye rules: *pirei havei* — the dried trees are considered *pira*, produce. They therefore fall within the creditor's rights as the party entitled to benefit from the field's yield during the mortgage period, and he may take the trees and use their firewood value — *dmei etzim*.

### Rava: Karna Status

Rava disagrees: *karna havei* — the dried trees are classified as *karna*, the principal of the loan, not its produce. The orchard itself was the security for the debt; if it withers, what remains is the underlying asset, not a yield from it. Consequently, neither party may simply consume the firewood value. *Ve-zil ve-lizbon ara ve-hu achil peirot* — the wood should be sold, that money used to purchase new land, and the creditor then eats the produce of that replacement land as his ongoing mortgage payment. The replacement land steps into the shoes of the original orchard as the *keren* of the mortgage.

### Baraita Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: יָבַשׁ הָאִילָן אוֹ נִקְצַץ, שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִים בּוֹ. כֵּיצַד יַעֲשׂוּ? יִמָּכְרוּ לְעֵצִים, וְיִלָּקַח בָּהֶן קַרְקַע, וְהוּא אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת. מַאי לָאו, יָבַשׁ דּוּמְיָא דְּנִקְצַץ: מָה נִקְצַץ בִּזְמַנּוֹ, אַף יָבַשׁ בִּזְמַנּוֹ, וְקָתָנֵי: יִלָּקַח בָּהֶן קַרְקַע וְהוּא אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת. אַלְמָא קַרְנָא הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against this from a *baraita* that discusses the *halakhot* of a mortgage: If **the tree dried up or was chopped down,** it is **forbidden** for **both** the creditor and the debtor to take **it** for themselves. **How** should **they proceed?** It **is sold for wood, and land should be purchased with** the proceeds, **and he,** i.e., the creditor, **consumes the produce** from that land. The Gemara clarifies: **What, is it not** referring to **a dry** tree that is **similar** to one **that was chopped down,** in that **just as** the tree **was chopped down at its** proper **time, so too,** the tree **dried at its** proper **time? And** yet the *tanna* **teaches** that **land should be purchased with** the money **and** the creditor **consumes the produce. Apparently,** the wood **is** considered part of the **principal,** not the profits. This supports Rava's opinion and presents a difficulty to Abaye.

A *baraita* is cited that appears to create difficulty: *yavesh ilan o niktzatz, sheneihem asurim bo* — if a tree withers or is cut down, neither the creditor nor the owner has rights to the wood. *Keitzad yaasu? Yimcheru le-etzim, ve-yilkach bahen karka ve-hu ochel peirot* — what should they do? Sell the wood as timber, use the proceeds to buy land, and the creditor eats the produce of that land. This ruling aligns with Rava's position and seems to contradict Abaye's.

### Normal vs. Abnormal With…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא: נִקְצַץ דּוּמְיָא דְּיָבַשׁ, מָה יָבַשׁ בְּלֹא זְמַנּוֹ – אַף נִקְצַץ בְּלֹא זְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **No,** the **chopped-down** tree mentioned in the *baraita* is **similar to** the **dry** one: **Just as** that tree **dried up before its time, so too,** this one **was chopped down before its time.** Since this did not occur naturally, the wood is classified as produce; had the orchard dried up at the expected time, its trees would be considered as part of the land.

The *gemara* examines the *baraita* closely. The initial reading assumed *yavesh dumya de-niktzatz* — that "withering" is analogous to "being cut," both representing trees that have completed their productive cycle and dried out at their proper time. Under that reading, the *baraita* supports Rava comprehensively. But an alternative reading is pressed: *niktzatz dumya de-yavesh* — no, "cut" should be read as analogous to "withered," and *yavesh belo zemano* — the withering described is premature, abnormal death before the tree's expected productive life has ended. If the trees died before their time, *hazkin bizmano* — aged and dried in the normal course — would be described differently. On this reading, the *baraita* speaks only of trees that died abnormally early, and the dispute between Abaye and Rava comes down to whether the withering in our original case — where the orchard died after five years of a ten-year mortgage — was within the normal expected lifespan of those vines or premature. If it was within normal time, Abaye's ruling that it is *pira* may still stand; if it was abnormally early, the *baraita's* rule of *karna* applies. The daf concludes with this question open, to be taken up in the continuation.
