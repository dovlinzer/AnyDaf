# Zevachim 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tamei & Psul

### Mishnah: Tamei Psul

The daf begins on 22b, picking up the next case in the *mishnah*: *tamei pasul* — a *kohen* who is ritually impure renders the *avodah* invalid. Having already treated lesser degrees of impurity, such as *mechusrei kippurim* and *tevul yom*, the *mishnah* now addresses the general case of *tamei* itself.

### Ziknei Darom Position

The *mishnah*'s blanket rule is immediately qualified by a striking opinion. *Amru Ziknei Darom* — the elders of the South — teach: *lo shanu ela tamei sheretz, aval tamei meis, ho'il u-miratzeh betzibur, miratzeh nami beyachid.* The rule that a *tamei kohen* invalidates the *avodah* applies only when he is *tamei* through contact with a *sheretz* — a dead rodent or similar source of contact-based impurity. But if he is *tamei meis* — impure through contact with a human corpse — paradoxically the more severe *tumah*, the *avodah* is nevertheless *kasher*. The reason: since *tumah* of this kind is accepted, i.e., overridden, in cases involving the *tzibur*, it is also accepted *beyachid*, in the individual case.

Before proceeding to the Gemara's analysis, it is worth noting that the constant term of approval for a sacrifice — *ritzuy*, *miratzeh* — is the same root as *Retzei Hashem Elokeinu*, the blessing in *Shemoneh Esrei*. *Tefillah keneged korbanos*, prayer corresponds to sacrifices, yet *korbanos* are remarkably absent from *Shemoneh Esrei* except for the faintest allusion in *Retzei* — *ishei Yisrael u-tefillatam*, their fire offerings. That *ritzuy*, the key verb of sacrificial acceptance, is also the key verb of that blessing is worth pausing over.

## Tumah Hutra

### Ritzuy & Retzei

The *Ziknei Darom* position rests on the principle of *tumah hutra betzibur* — impurity is overridden for communal sacrifices. This is actually a slight misnomer, as both *Tosafos* and the *Rambam* point out: the principle is not strictly about communal sacrifices but about sacrifices that have a fixed time. Most such sacrifices are indeed *korbanot tzibur* — *korban tamid*, *yom tov musafim* — but the fixed-time requirement can apply to an individual offering as well, most notably *korban Pesach*, which is brought at a precise, fixed moment and cannot be deferred. Conversely, there are communal *korbanos* that do not have a fixed time, such as the *korbanos* of *horayos*, and those would not fall under this principle.

### Only Tamei Meis

The principle overrides *tumah*, but critically, not all *tumah* — only *tumei meis*, impurity derived from a human corpse. More severe forms — *zav*, *niddah*, *metzora*, and the like, which are *tumah yotzei m'gufo*, impurity originating within the body — are not overridden. And, strikingly, even less severe contact-based *tumot*, such as *tamei sheretz*, are also not overridden. The principle applies exclusively to *tamei meis*. This is not derived through logical inference but is a fixed *halacha*, and since it is *halacha lemoshe misinai* one does not apply *kal vachomer* to extend it.

### Conditions

Two further conditions govern the principle. First, it applies to a sacrifice with a fixed time. Second, in the case of *korban Pesach* specifically, it applies when the *rov* of the *tzibur* is *tamei*, since otherwise — when only a minority is *tamei* — those individuals are simply pushed off to *Pesach Sheni*. Outside of *korban Pesach*, the more usual application of *tumah hutra betzibur* is when the *kohanim* themselves are *tamei*, since their *tumah* is what prevents the *korban* from being brought.

### Hutra vs Dchuya

There is a significant debate whether *tumah hutra betzibur* — impurity is categorically permitted in this context, as though there were no prohibition at all — or *tumah dchuya betzibur* — impurity is merely overridden by necessity. The practical consequence: if *dchuya*, the override operates only when there is no alternative. If *tahor kohanim* are available from another priestly division, those *kohanim* must be called in and the *tamei kohanim* set aside. Only when no *tahor* alternative exists does the *avodah* proceed in a state of *tumah*. The operative rule, then, is that *tumah* is *dchuya*, overridden in the absence of alternatives, for sacrifices of fixed time, but only for *tamei meis*, and only when no *tahor kohen* is available to substitute.

## Meis vs Sheretz

### Reish Lakish Challenge

Against the *Ziknei Darom*, the *Gemara* presses a *kal vachomer*. *Amar Reish Lakish*: if *tamei meis* — the more severe *tumah*, requiring sprinkling of purification waters on the third and seventh day — yields a valid *avodah bedi'avad* for an individual *kohen*, then *tamei sheretz*, which requires no such seven-day process and is purified through simple immersion, should certainly yield a valid *avodah* by the same logic. Why limit the leniency of *Ziknei Darom* to *tamei meis*?

The *Gemara* responds on behalf of *Ziknei Darom*: *kasavri Ziknei Darom, mechapprim k'mitchapprim* — those who atone are compared to those who are atoned for. The basis of *tumah hutra betzibur* for *tamei meis* derives from the law of *korban Pesach*: when the majority of *Yisrael* is *tamei meis*, they bring the *korban Pesach* on the fourteenth of Nisan rather than being deferred to *Pesach Sheni*. This override applies for *tamei meis*, and emphatically not for *tamei sheretz*, even though *sheretz* is the lesser *tumah*. The *Ziknei Darom* argue: just as this asymmetric rule holds for the *Yisraelim* who are atoned for — *tamei meis* yes, *tamei sheretz* no — so too it holds for the *kohanim* who perform the atonement: *tamei meis* is within the scope of tolerance, *tamei sheretz* is not.

The irony is philosophically striking, as one of the students raised: why should the more severe *tumah* be the one that is overridden, while a lesser one is not? The *drasha* identifying *tamei meis* as the subject of the override derives from the very passage that gave rise to *Pesach Sheni*: *ish ki yihyeh tamei lanefesh* — *tamei lanefesh* means *tumaat meis*, and that is the *tumah* the *Torah* happens to be addressing in that context. It is less an explanation than a textual identification.

### Mechapprim Analogy

On a more reflective level, several dimensions of this asymmetry were explored. The cases in which the *Torah* imposes severe restrictions in the face of death — *Nadav v'Avihu*, the men who could not bring *korban Pesach* — all involve encounter with human death. There is, perhaps, a counterintuitive affirmation at work: precisely because so much of life closes down in the presence of death, the *Torah* creates an opening to bring *korbanos* when most of the community is engaged with death. According to *Chazal*, the men who were *tamei lanefesh* on the first *Pesach* were those who had buried *Nadav v'Avihu*, performing the highest act of *chesed* possible. One participant offered a further insight: if there were no override, people might hesitate to accompany the dying or tend to the dead, lest they forfeit their *avodah*. The override removes that hesitation. Whatever the precise rationale, the textual anchor is clear even if the philosophical explanation remains open.

### Individual Level Issues

*Reish Lakish* then sharpens his challenge further. The *Gemara* frames the problem at the individual level:

*Mai kasavar* — what exactly is the position of *Ziknei Darom* regarding *tamei sheretz*? If they hold *ein shochtin vezorkin al tamei sheretz* — that a *korban Pesach* cannot be slaughtered and its blood sprinkled for an owner who is currently *tamei sheretz*, even if he will be able to eat it that night — then *amay lo avdi tzibbur betumah*: why isn't *tumaat sheretz* overridden at the *tzibur* level? If the majority of the *tzibur* is *tamei sheretz*, one would expect the principle of communal override to apply and allow the *korban Pesach* to be brought for them.

The *Gemara* concludes that *Ziknei Darom* must hold *shochtin vezorkin al tamei sheretz* — one may bring the *korban Pesach* for an owner who is currently *tamei sheretz*, because he will in fact be able to eat it that night. Therefore *tumaat sheretz* was never a genuine obstacle for the *korban* to be brought; it never *needed* overriding at the *tzibur* level. And since it was never the subject of a communal override, no principle of tolerance for *tamei sheretz* was ever established — not for *Yisrael* and not for *kohanim*. Even if an entire generation of *kohanim* were *tamei sheretz*, since the principle of *tumah hutra betzibur* was never articulated for *sheretz*, there is no *heter* to draw upon. The *Ziknei Darom* therefore have a consistent framework: for *tamei meis*, the override was needed and established; for *tamei sheretz*, it was never needed and therefore never established, which is why the *bedi'avad* tolerance applies only to *tamei meis*.

*Reish Lakish* presses the point from a different angle. He notes that the *Torah* tolerates *tumah* more readily at the level of the *Yisrael* than at the level of the *kohen*: when a *Yisrael* is *tamei sheretz*, he simply does not enter the *Beit Hamikdash*, and a *kohen* brings his *korban* on his behalf. The *kohen's* own *tumah*, by contrast, is the obstacle. Therefore, *Reish Lakish* argues, we should reason from what is true at the *yachid* level: since *tamei meis* is worse for the *Yisrael* sending the *korban* — who cannot participate in *korban Pesach* even as an owner when *tamei meis* — so too the *kohen* who is *tamei meis* should not produce a valid *avodah bedi'avad*.

*Ziknei Darom* respond: *tamei meis nami meshale'ach korbanotav* — even an owner who is *tamei meis* can send his *korban* and have it brought for him. This is a radical position. The *Gemara* immediately objects: *ve'haktiv ish ish ki yihyeh tamei*, which implies that a *tamei meis* individual brings on *Pesach Sheni*, not the first *Pesach*. *Ziknei Darom* answer: *le'mitzvah* — that is the ideal, but *bedi'avad*, having it brought on the first *Pesach* even though the owner cannot eat it that night fulfills the obligation. The *Gemara* then raises the requirement of *ish lefi ochlo*, that the *korban Pesach* is slaughtered according to those who will eat it, and *bemichsat nefashot*, that only those enrolled can be the basis for the *shechita*. The *Gemara* derives that eaters and the enrolled group are *huksha* — compared — so if you cannot eat you cannot serve as the basis for the *shechita*. *Ziknei Darom* respond that they do not accept that *hekkesh* — they do not link the eaters requirement to the enrollment requirement.

The *Gemara* then presses further: even if *Ziknei Darom* escape the *hekkesh* objection, there remains an asymmetry. When the owners are *tamei sheretz*, the *korban* can be brought for them *lechatchila* — they simply don't enter the Mikdash and the *kohen* brings it. But when owners are *tamei meis*, even *Ziknei Darom* concede it is *lechatchila* not done on the first *Pesach*. So we still have a *lechatchila* distinction: *tamei sheretz* by the owner is preferable to *tamei meis* by the owner. By parallel, *tamei sheretz* by the *kohen* should be preferable to *tamei meis* by the *kohen*. And if *tamei sheretz* by the *kohen* is invalid *bedi'avad*, then *tamei meis* should certainly be invalid *bedi'avad* — against *Ziknei Darom*'s entire position. The *Gemara* leaves this question unanswered.

### Death & Tuma

The philosophical dimension underlying these discussions is the question of why *tumaat meis* specifically receives this unique status — neither the most severe *tumot* nor the lighter contact ones, but precisely *tumaat meis*. One suggested reading: the *Torah* is responding to a profound human reality. The encounter with death brings with it obligations — *chesed shel emet*, burial, the care of the dying — that are among the highest *mitzvot*. If the cost of performing those obligations were to permanently disqualify one from *avodah*, the community would face an agonizing conflict between human duty and religious service. The override dissolves that conflict, affirming that where the community is most deeply engaged with death, the *avodah* continues. *Aharon*'s standing at his post on the day of *Nadav v'Avihu* — maintaining public function in the midst of private grief — is the iconic expression of the same principle.

## Pesach Sheni

### Scripture Basis

The *Gemara* now focuses on the scriptural basis for when a *tamei meis* individual brings *korban Pesach* on *Pesach Sheni*. The verse *ish ki yihyeh tamei lanefesh o bederech rechoka* — a man who is *tamei lanefesh* or on a distant journey — establishes that the individual who is *tamei meis* defers to *Pesach Sheni*. The *Gemara's* *drasha* is that when an *ish* — an individual — is *tamei*, he defers; when *hatzibur* is *tamei*, the *korban* is not deferred but brought on time. And the *tumah* the *Torah* was addressing in that foundational passage was *tumaat meis*, which is why *tumaat meis* is the *tumah* identified with the override.

### Eaters Requirement

The *Gemara* raises the requirement *ish lefi ochlo tachossu al haseh* — that the *korban Pesach* is apportioned according to those who will eat it. From *bemichsat nefashot* we learn that the *korban Pesach* may be slaughtered only for those enrolled in a group; slaughtering for those not enrolled is invalid. The *Gemara* then reasons *va-hikish ochlim limnu'im* — eaters are compared to those enrolled — so that one who cannot eat from the *korban* that night is as though not properly enrolled, and the *shechita* on his behalf is invalid *bedi'avad*. This creates a direct problem for *Ziknei Darom*, who hold that an owner who is *tamei meis* can still have his *korban Pesach* brought for him even though he cannot eat it.

### Ziknei Darom Radical

*Ziknei Darom* respond by rejecting the *hekkesh* — they do not equate the eater requirement with the enrollment requirement. An owner who is *tamei meis* and who is enrolled in the group has his *korban Pesach* brought validly *bedi'avad*, even though he cannot eat from it that night. *Lechatchila*, the *Torah* directs him to *Pesach Sheni*; *bedi'avad*, having the *korban* brought on the first *Pesach* is sufficient for the obligation. This is described as a radical position — one that severs eating from participation in a way the standard *halacha* does not accept. The *Gemara* continues to mount challenges against this position, noting that even *Ziknei Darom* must concede the *lechatchila* distinction between owners who are *tamei sheretz* — for whom the *korban* is brought *lechatchila* — and owners who are *tamei meis* — for whom even they concede it is not *lechatchila*. If so, *tamei meis* is demonstrably worse at the owner level, and by analogy the *kohen* who is *tamei meis* should not produce a valid *avodah bedi'avad* — which is precisely the point *Reish Lakish* was making.

## Tzitz & Atonement

### Tzitz Function

As if the foregoing were not sufficiently complex, the *Gemara* now introduces another layer: the principle of the *tzitz*, the golden forehead-plate worn by the *kohen gadol*, inscribed with *Kodesh L'Hashem*. The *Torah* states: *ve-nasa Aharon et avon hakodashim asher yakdishu bnei Yisrael l'Hashem* — *Aharon* will bear the sin of the sacred offerings that *Bnei Yisrael* consecrate. *Chazal* understand this to mean that the wearing of the *tzitz* renders acceptable — *miratzeh* — certain *korbanos* that were brought in an irregular state. A *braita* states: *mipnei she-amru nazir ve-oseh Pesach, hatzitz meratzeh al tumat dam, ve-ein hatzitz meratzeh al tumat haguf* — for a *nazir* and one bringing the *korban Pesach*, the *tzitz* atones for the *tumah* of the blood (*dam*) of the sacrifice, but does not atone for the *tumah* of the person (*haguf*).

The reason these two — *nazir* and *oseh Pesach* — are specifically mentioned is that both require the owner to be *tahor* as a prerequisite: the *oseh Pesach* must be able to eat from the sacrifice, and the *nazir* brings his *korban* specifically to terminate his *tumah* and restart his *nezirut*, so he cannot bring it while still *tamei*. For other sacrifices, where the owner's personal *tumah* is not a direct obstacle to the sacrifice's validity, the framework is different.

### Dam vs Haguf

The distinction between *tumat dam* — impurity of the sacrificial blood — and *tumat haguf* — impurity of the person — is central to understanding the scope of the *tzitz*. When the blood of the sacrifice itself became *tamei* and was nonetheless sprinkled, the *tzitz* validates the sacrifice *bedi'avad*. But when the person — whether owner or *kohen* — is *tamei*, the *tzitz* does not remedy the situation.

The *Gemara* presses: how exactly is the person *tamei* in the scenario where *tumat haguf* is the problem? If it is through *sheretz*, then according to *Ziknei Darom* one may bring a *korban* for that person in any event — *shochtin ve-zorkin al tamei sheretz* — so the *tzitz* would be irrelevant. Therefore the case must involve *tamei meis*. And the *braita* then says the *tzitz* does not work — *ein hatzitz meratzeh* — which, if the case is *tamei meis*, would seem to demonstrate that when owners are *tamei meis* the *korban* is invalid even *bedi'avad*, directly against *Ziknei Darom*'s stated position.

The *Gemara* deflects this reading: the case is actually one where the *kohen* is *tamei sheretz*, not the owners. *Hacha be-mai askinan, ke-gon she-nitma kohen be-sheretz* — here we are dealing with a *kohen* who became *tamei sheretz*. When the owners are *tamei*, one does not need the *tzitz*; when the blood itself is *tamei*, the *tzitz* works; when the *kohen* is *tamei sheretz* — which *Ziknei Darom* themselves hold renders the *avodah* invalid — the *tzitz* does not help.

### Tumat Hatehom

The *braita* continues: *nitma tumat hatehom, hatzitz meratzeh* — if the *tumah* was of the variety called *tumat hatehom*, the *tzitz* does work. *Tumat hatehom* — literally the impurity of the depths — refers paradigmatically to walking over ground where a corpse may be buried deep underground, creating a doubtful *tumah*. By extension, *Chazal* apply the concept to other cases of *safek tumah* — doubtful impurity — provided there is a concrete, specific reason to suspect the *tumah* rather than a general theoretical possibility.

*Rabbi Chiya* teaches: *lo amru tumat hatehom ela le-meis bilvad* — the principle of *tumat hatehom* applies only to *tumaat meis*, not to *tumaat zav* or other severe *tumot*. An example of a *tumat hatehom* of a *zav*: if a person experienced a flow throughout *bein hashmashos* and cannot determine whether it began on one day or another, thus not knowing whether it counts as a one-day or two-day flow — that doubtful scenario is analyzed like *tumat hatehom*, though the paradigm remains tied to *tumaat meis*.

What does *tumat hatehom* limited to *meis* exclude? The *Gemara* considers: does it exclude *tumat hatehom de-sheretz* — meaning, a case of doubtful *sheretz*-type contact? Or does it exclude the more severe *tumat hatehom de-zivah* — doubtful *zav*-type impurity? The *Gemara* concludes the latter: the *tzitz* works for *tumat hatehom* of *sheretz* but not for *tumat hatehom* of *zivah*.

Pulling this together: for *tamei meis*, according to *Ziknei Darom*, the *bedi'avad* tolerance operates without needing the *tzitz*. For *tamei sheretz* — if certain, not doubtful — no tolerance exists and the *tzitz* does not help. For doubtful *tamei sheretz* — *tumat hatehom de-sheretz* — the *tzitz* does provide *bedi'avad* validity. And for *zivah*-type impurity, the *tzitz* does not operate even in the *tumat hatehom* form. This leaves the cases that remain fully invalid — certain *tamei sheretz* and *tamei zav* — as the hard boundaries.

One might ask whether the *tzitz* must actually be worn at the time for it to effect *ritzuy*. This is a good reason for the *kohen gadol* to remain consistently at his post. As for the nature of *ritzuy* itself: the verse uses the language of *nesi'at avon* — bearing sin — which in *Torah* usage always implies that something improper occurred but is being overlooked. *Chazal* understand the *tzitz* as actually validating the *korban bedi'avad* rather than merely atoning for the individual's sin while leaving the *korban* invalid. The *nisiat avon* does not mean the end justifies the means — which *halacha* does not accept, save for the narrow doctrine of *aveira lishmah* — but that the *korban* is retroactively rendered *nirtzeh*, acceptable.

### Rami Bar Chama

*Rami bar Chama* poses a pointed question: *kohen ha-miratzeh be-korbanoteihem* — when a *kohen* is bringing the *korbanos* on behalf of a *nazir* or *oseh Pesach* — *hutra lo tumat hatehom o lo hutra lo tumat hatehom?* — is *tumat hatehom* overridden for the *kohen* himself, or not? That is, we have established that *tumat hatehom* of the *nazir* or *oseh Pesach* does not disqualify the *korban* thanks to the *tzitz*. Does the same apply when it is the *kohen* bringing the *korban* who has *tumat hatehom*?

The significance of this question is that it assumes the normative position — against *Ziknei Darom* — that the *tzitz*'s function as discussed in the *braita* concerns the owners of the *korban*, not the *kohen*. *Rami bar Chama* is asking whether the *kohen* can benefit from the same leniency extended to the owner by the *tzitz*. The *Gemara* concludes: *de-Rami bar Chama vadai pligi* — *Rami bar Chama* clearly disagrees with *Ziknei Darom*, because *Ziknei Darom* held that the entire discussion of the *tzitz* in this *braita* was about the *kohen*, not the owner. According to the mainstream view that *Rami bar Chama* is working within, it is the owner's *tumah* that the *tzitz* addresses, and the question of whether the *kohen*'s *tumat hatehom* is remedied remains open.

## Tzitz Scope

### Excluding Pigul

The *Gemara* now turns to establishing precisely which sin the *tzitz* atones for. The verse *ve-nasa Aharon et avon hakodashim* — what *avon* does *Aharon* carry? *Im avon pigul* — if it is the sin of *pigul*, understood here in the sense of improper intention regarding place of consumption, *harei kvar ne'emar lo yeichashev* — the *Torah* already states it shall not be counted: no atonement is possible. *Im avon notar* — and here, following *Rashi*, *notar* refers to true *pigul*, the intention to eat at the wrong time — *harei kvar ne'emar lo yeiratzeh* — it already says it will not be accepted.

### Impurity Only

If the *tzitz* cannot atone for *pigul* in either of its forms, what is left? *Ela avon tumah* — only the sin of *tumah*. The *Gemara* asks: why leap to *tumah* specifically? There are other invalidities — *zar* performing the *avodah*, *onen*, and others discussed throughout this tractate. Why is *tumah* singled out?

### Logic Framework

The answer: *she-hutra miklala betzibur* — because we find that *tumah* is sometimes overridden entirely, namely in the communal sacrifice context. Since the *Torah* itself demonstrates a category of *tumah* that can be tolerated, and since the *tzitz* also makes acceptable things that would otherwise be invalid, we conclude that the *tzitz* operates in the domain of *tumah*. The logic is that because *tumah* has an internal exception — it is sometimes *hutra* — the *tzitz* can function within that same domain. *Pigul* and *notar* have no such internal exception; they are categorically rejected. *Tumah* alone has the characteristic of sometimes being tolerated, which is what makes it the domain where the *tzitz* can operate.

### Which Tumah

But which *tumah*? *Mai tumah, ileima mitumat sheretz* — if it is *tumaat sheretz*, there is a difficulty: *heichi ishtrei*, how was it ever permitted? We never apply *tumah hutra betzibur* to *tumaat sheretz*. *Ela tumat meis* — it must be *tumaat meis*, the only *tumah* for which we find a communal override. And the implication is *nitmeu ba'alim be-meis meshalachin korbanoteihem* — when the owners are *tamei meis* they can send their *korbanos*, which is the position of *Ziknei Darom*.

The *Gemara* complicates this reading: if it concerns a *nazir*, the *Torah* separately teaches that *nazir* who becomes *tamei* must begin his count again — *ve-chi yamut meit alav amar Rachmana* — suggesting the *korban* cannot be brought while he is *tamei*. So the case in the *braita* must concern *oseh Pesach*, and the *Gemara* reads it as support for *Ziknei Darom*'s position that an *oseh Pesach* who is *tamei meis* can still send his *korban*. The *Gemara* immediately pulls back: *le'olam besheretz* — one may say the *tumah* being discussed for the purpose of identifying *tumat hatehom* is actually *tumaat sheretz*, and the logic is merely that since the *Torah* tolerates *some tumah* in some context, the *tzitz* operates in the domain of *tumah* broadly — without requiring that the *specific tumah* being remedied by the *tzitz* be the same *tumah* that was overridden *betzibur*.

### Kodashim vs Makdishim

Some *rishonim* read the sources in the opposite direction, yielding a *kashya* on *Ziknei Darom* rather than a support. *Avon kodashim in, avon makdishim lo* — the *tzitz* bears the sin of the *kodashim*, the sacrifices themselves, but not the sin of the *makdishim*, those who consecrate and bring the sacrifices. *Mai tumah* — what *tumah* is this talking about? If it were *tumaat sheretz* it was never overridden *betzibur*, so it must be *tumaat meis*. But the *braita* says the *tzitz* works for the *kodashim* — the sacrifice itself — and not for the *makdishim*, the persons involved. This demonstrates that even when the *makdishim*, i.e., the owners, are *tamei meis*, the *tzitz* does not work for them — meaning the *korban* is invalid — directly against *Ziknei Darom*'s claim that *tamei meis* owners can have their *korban Pesach* brought for them.

The *Gemara* resolves: *le'olam tumas sheretz, ve-she'ein tumah ba'olam* — the *tumah* that the *tzitz* atones for is *tumaat sheretz* (for the *kodashim*), and the logic is simply that since there is *some tumah* in the world that is sometimes permitted, the domain of *tumah* in general is where the *tzitz* operates. The argument does not require the *same* type of *tumah* to be both the overridden *tumah* and the *tumah* the *tzitz* remediates.

The discussion at this point involves enormous *Tosafos* precisely because the principle throughout seems to turn on the *tzibur* rather than the *yachid* — yet the entire discussion of *Ziknei Darom* has been about individual cases. The interplay between the communal *tumah hutra betzibur* principle and the individual-level *bedi'avad* tolerances, mediated by the *tzitz* and its specific *ritzuy*, remains one of the most intricate topics on this *daf*, and the discussion continues into the following *daf*.
