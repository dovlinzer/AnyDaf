# Makkot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lashes & Karet

### Three Positions

The opening *mishnah* of *Eilu Hein HaLokin* lists a substantial number of sins for which one incurs *karet*, and the *mishnah* presents this list as reflecting the position of Rabbi Akiva. The *gemara* on 13b frames this within a three-way debate recorded in a *braisa*.

### Rabbi Ishmael

Rabbi Ishmael holds that one receives *malkos* for any negative transgression, even if the ultimate punishment is court execution — and certainly if the punishment is *karet*. This is a striking and unfamiliar position. The *gemara* explains it is based on the principle that lashes leading up to a more severe bodily punishment — whether imposed by the court or by the heavenly court — are all considered, in effect, one extended death. One does not receive both a monetary fine and lashes, since those represent two fundamentally different categories of punishment. But lashes administered prior to a harsher bodily consequence are considered part of a single drawn-out continuum.

### Rabbi Akiva

Rabbi Akiva takes a more nuanced position. He agrees that one does receive *malkos* when the accompanying punishment is *karet*, but he disagrees when the accompanying punishment is *misat beit din* — court execution. His reasoning flows from the verse *kedei rishato* (Deuteronomy 25:2): "according to his wickedness" — meaning, the court holds a person liable for one wickedness, not two. Double punishment by the court is impermissible.

Rabbi Ishmael would counter that this prohibition on double punishment applies only when the two punishments are of different types — monetary fine combined with execution, or monetary fine combined with lashes. But execution combined with lashes is all one extended death, and that is not a prohibited doubling. Rabbi Akiva's response to this is what distinguishes his position: he holds that *karet* does not fall within the category of double *beit din* punishment, because the person liable to *karet* might yet do *teshuvah* — and thus *karet* is not an inevitable second punishment.

### Gemara's Question

The *gemara* presses Rabbi Akiva on this reasoning. Even if *teshuvah* theoretically averts *karet*, the individual standing before the court has not yet done *teshuvah*. At this moment, he is deserving of *karet*. How, then, can the fact that he *could* escape it be sufficient reason to treat it as non-existent for purposes of the double-punishment concern? This question drives the *gemara* forward.

---

## Gezeirah Shavah

### L'Einei L'Einecha

Rabbi Abbahu answers that Rabbi Akiva's position is not purely a matter of logical reasoning about the nature of *karet* — it is derived explicitly from a *pasuk*. The Torah itself includes those liable to *karet* within the category of those who receive *malkos*, through a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *einei*. By *karet*, the Torah writes *v'nichresu l'einei bnei amam* — "they shall be cut off before the eyes of their people" (Leviticus 20:17). By *malkos*, the Torah writes *v'niklah achicha l'einecha* — "your brother shall be degraded before your eyes" (Deuteronomy 25:3). The shared formulation *l'einei* / *l'einecha* creates a verbal analogy that explicitly links *karet* offenses to the punishment of *malkos*.

### Abba bar Mammal

Rabbi Abba bar Mammal challenged this derivation. If one can construct a *gezeirah shavah* between the *karet* verse and the *malkos* verse, why not construct an equally valid *gezeirah shavah* and reach the conclusion of Rabbi Ishmael — that even those liable to court execution receive *malkos*? By *misat beit din*, the verse in the context of *avodah zarah* writes *im me'einei ha'eidah ne'estah lishgagah* — "if it was hidden from the eyes of the congregation" (Numbers 15:24). Both that verse and the *malkos* verse contain the word *einei* / *einecha* — why not link those as well?

### Linguistic Distinction

The *gemara* responds that the two links are not equally precise. The *karet* verse uses *l'einei* and the *malkos* verse uses *l'einecha* — both share the prefix *lamed*, making this a close parallel. The *misat beit din* verse, by contrast, uses *me'einei* — with a *mem* prefix rather than a *lamed*. The *gemara* thus rules: *danin l'einei m'l'einei v'ein danin me'einei m'l'einei* — one may construct a *gezeirah shavah* from *l'einei* to *l'einei*, but not from *me'einei* to *l'einei*.

The *gemara* itself questions whether this distinction carries real weight. After all, the school of Rabbi Ishmael taught that *v'shav hakohen* and *u'va hakohen* — "the priest shall return" and "the priest shall come" — are treated as a *gezeirah shavah* in the laws of *tzara'at* of the house, even though these are entirely different words that simply share the same sense. If imprecise wording can be sufficient, why is the *mem*-*lamed* difference decisive? The *gemara* raises this as a standing question.

### No Double Punishment

Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak resolved the underlying question by returning to Rabbi Akiva's own language. The verse *kedei rishato* — understood as "the court imposes one punishment" — establishes that *beit din* may not administer two punishments. This prohibits *misat beit din* together with *malkos*, since both are *beit din* punishments. But *karet* is a divine punishment, not a *beit din* punishment. There is no prohibition on *beit din* imposing *malkos* when God separately imposes *karet*, because these operate on entirely different planes. The question of whether the person will ultimately suffer *karet* — something he can avert through *teshuvah* — is between him and God, not a matter for the court below. The court's concern is exclusively with its own sanctions, and *malkos* stands on its own as the *beit din* response to a violated *lav*.

---

## Rava's Reframe

### Rava's Premise

Rava now significantly reframes the entire debate. He argues that Rabbi Ishmael could not have meant that a person receives both *malkos* and *misat beit din* simultaneously from the court. Everyone would agree that *beit din* does not impose two punishments — *ein lokeh u'mes*. Rava thus eliminates the most radical reading of Rabbi Ishmael's position.

### Warning Mismatch

If everyone agrees that *beit din* does not administer both *malkos* and *misat beit din*, then what exactly is the debate between Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva? According to Rava, the debate concerns a case where witnesses gave an incorrect *hasra'ah* — where they warned the transgressor that his act carries *malkos*, without mentioning *misat beit din*. Perhaps the witnesses had compassion and downplayed the severity, warning him only of lashes. He acknowledged the warning and proceeded anyway. Now, the court cannot impose *misat beit din* because the warning did not match that punishment. The question is: can the court impose *malkos*?

Rabbi Ishmael says yes. Even though the *lav* that this person violated normally carries *misat beit din*, every *lav* has within it an inherent capacity to generate *malkos*. Since *misat beit din* will not be imposed, *malkos* steps in. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: a *lav* that was assigned as the vehicle for *misat beit din* cannot be repurposed to produce *malkos*. The *lav* is dedicated, so to speak, to the heavier punishment, and when that punishment cannot be imposed due to a mismatched warning, the person goes free rather than receiving the lesser sanction.

### Rabbi Akiva Refined

Under Rava's reframing, Rabbi Akiva's concern about *karet* cases takes on a different dimension. If Rabbi Akiva holds that a *lav* assigned to one punishment cannot function to generate a different punishment, then why can a *lav* that accompanies *karet* generate *malkos*? The *lav* is, after all, associated with a *karet* offense. The answer, as Rav Mordechai reported in the name of Avimi of Hagronya in the name of Rava, is: *chayvei kerisos lo tsrichi hasra'ah* — for God to impose *karet*, no *lav* is required. This is demonstrated by the two cases of *karet* that carry no accompanying *lav* at all: failure to bring the *korban pesach* and failure to perform *bris milah*. In both cases, the Torah imposes *karet* for a passive omission — no prohibition was transgressed. This proves that *karet* operates entirely independently of any *lav*.

### Karet & Lav

Since *karet* does not require a *lav*, the *lav* in a *karet* case is not "used up" by the *karet*. It is genuinely free to serve as the basis for *malkos*. This is the economy of the *lo ta'aseh*: for Rabbi Akiva, a *lav* can only serve one purpose, and a *lav* assigned to *misat beit din* cannot generate *malkos*. But a *lav* in a *karet* case is not assigned to *karet* — *karet* exists without it. Therefore, the *lav* is available to produce *malkos* independently.

The *gemara* momentarily considers a challenge to this: perhaps the *lav* in *karet* cases is needed not for *karet* itself, but for the *korban* that accompanies *karet* offenses. After all, we observe that *pesach* and *milah* — the two *karet* offenses that lack a *lav* — also have no *korban* for their omission. Does this not show that the *lav* is part of the *korban* package?

The *gemara* rejects this. The reason *pesach* and *milah* carry no *korban* is not because of any *lav*. The paradigm for *korbanos* is *avodah zarah*, and the rule is *mah avodah zarah shev v'al ta'aseh* — just as *avodah zarah* involves an active transgression, every *korban* arises only from an active violation. *Pesach* and *milah* are sins of omission — of *kum va'aseh*, of failing to act — and therefore they fall outside the category of offenses requiring a *korban*, regardless of the presence or absence of a *lav*. A *korban* is owed whenever there is *karet* combined with an active transgression. The *lav* has no role in generating the *korban* obligation.

The conclusion is clean: *karet* does not need the *lav*. The *korban* does not need the *lav*. The *lav* is therefore entirely free to serve as the basis for *malkos*. Rabbi Akiva's position is consistent: he applies *malkos* to *karet* cases because the *lav* is unencumbered, and he withholds *malkos* from *misat beit din* cases because there the *lav* is dedicated to the court execution.

---

## Ravina's View

### Teshuvah Option

Ravina offers a final account of Rabbi Akiva's reasoning, returning to what he characterizes as the original formulation. Rabbi Akiva's position, at its core, is that *karet* does not constitute a definite second punishment. The person who has violated a *karet* offense and is now before the court has not yet done *teshuvah* — that is true. But *karet* is not a punishment that *beit din* imposes; it is something God may or may not carry out, and crucially, the transgressor retains the ability to escape it entirely through repentance. Since there is no certainty that *karet* will materialize, imposing *malkos* does not constitute giving the person two definite punishments. Double punishment — in the relevant sense — means administering two certain, irrevocable sanctions. *Karet* does not qualify.

It is worth noting that throughout all these opinions, not one of them claims that receiving *malkos* exempts the person from *karet*. Rabbi Ishmael says both are received. Rabbi Akiva says *malkos* is administered alongside *karet*, but the two operate on separate planes and neither cancels the other. Ravina clarifies Rabbi Akiva's reasoning but does not alter the substance: the *malkos* is simply not the mechanism by which *karet* is averted.

## Rabbi Yitzchak

### Karet Exclusion

Rabbi Yitzchak takes the most restrictive position of all: one does not receive *malkos* for any *karet* offense whatsoever. His reasoning, as presented through the *Tosafos* reading, begins from an inclusive premise. Those liable to *karet* were originally included within the general category of *lav* violators who receive *malkos* — so by default, one might have assumed *malkos* applies. The question is therefore: why did the Torah explicitly single out *karet* for the case of one's sister (*achoto*)?

### Achoto Proof

The verse in *Kedoshim* (20:17) contains a notably elaborate formulation: *v'ish asher yikach es achoso bas aviv o vas imo v'ra'ah es ervasah v'hi sir'eh es ervaso chesed hu v'nichresu l'einei v'nei amam ervas achoso gilah avono yisa*. Unlike the other *arayos* offenses, which receive their *karet* from the general concluding verse at the end of *Acharei Mos*, the sister case receives an explicit, dedicated mention of *v'nichresu* in *Kedoshim*. Rabbi Yitzchak reads this singling-out as a deliberate exclusion: it tells us that the *karet* here operates *instead of malkos* — *ladunu b'kares v'lo b'malkos*. The Torah is specifying that one who sleeps with his sister is punished through *karet* and not through *malkos*.

---

## Achoto Interpretation

### Thirty-Six Sins

The *mishnah* in *Kerisus* enumerates thirty-six transgressions for which one incurs *karet*. Rabbi Yochanan explains why the *mishnah* specifies this number: it is to teach that even if a person commits all thirty-six in a single lapse of awareness (*he'elam echad*), he is *chayav* for each one individually — up to a maximum of thirty-six *korbanos*.

### No Malkos

Rabbi Yitzchak uses the singling-out of *karet* in the *achoto* verse for the purpose just described: to establish that *karet* offenses do not carry *malkos*. The explicit *v'nichresu* in the *achoto* passage signals an exclusion from the general rule that *lav* violations incur *malkos*.

### Multiple Statuses

Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Ishmael — the *rabbanan* — who hold that *karet* offenses do carry *malkos*, need a different explanation for why the Torah singled out *karet* by the sister. They use it to teach the principle of *l'chaleik* — division. Even when a person commits multiple *karet* offenses in a single lapse of awareness, each offense counts separately and generates a separate *korban*. The explicit mention of *v'nichresu* by *achoto* establishes that every distinct *karet* transgression is treated independently, rather than collapsed into a single *korban*.

But the *gemara* presses further. The principle that different women and different sins generate different *korbanos* seems obvious: they are separate bodies, separate acts, separate offenses. The deeper question is whether the same principle applies when the *multiple* status categories all converge upon a single individual.

### Rasha Bar Rasha

The *gemara* resolves this by specifying the precise case the verse is addressing: *lachayiv al achoto she'hi achot aviv she'hi achot imo* — to establish liability for a woman who is simultaneously his sister, his father's sister, and his mother's sister. Three overlapping prohibitions, one woman, one act. The *gemara* asks how such a situation is even possible, and answers: *rasha bar rasha* — a wicked person, son of a wicked person. Rashi describes the scenario: a man has relations with his mother and produces two daughters; he then has relations with one of those daughters and produces a son; that son then has relations with his aunt — who is simultaneously his sister (sharing the same father) and his mother's sister (by direct lineage). The result is a single woman who carries three distinct forbidden statuses. It is for precisely this case — one body, multiple statuses — that the *v'nichresu* of *achoto* teaches that each status generates a separate *korban*.

---

## Separate Karbanos

### Five Women

The *gemara* cites a tradition to clarify the broader principle. The foundational case for separate *korbanos* from separate sins in one *he'elam* is a man who has relations with five different *niddah* women in a single lapse of awareness: he is *chayav* a separate *korban* for each one. This is the paradigm case of the same *aveirah* but different women — different bodies, and therefore obviously different *korbanos*.

Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua were once asked by Rabbi Akiva — while they were all on their way to the meat market at Imaum to purchase an animal for Rabban Gamliel's son's wedding feast — what the law would be if a man slept with a woman who was simultaneously his sister, his father's sister, and his mother's sister. Would he bring one *korban* or three? They answered: *zo lo shamanu* — we have no explicit tradition on this. But they did have a tradition regarding five different *niddah* women in a single act of forgetfulness: each one requires a separate *korban*. From that they wished to reason: if *different women* and the *same aveirah* generate separate *korbanos*, then surely *different aveirot* in a *single woman* should generate separate *korbanos* as well — *kal vachomer*.

The other position challenged this *kal vachomer* as *pricha* — refutable. Different bodies are inherently more distinct than different status categories. Since the *kal vachomer* is reversible, it cannot serve as a definitive proof.

### Achoto Seifa

The *gemara* therefore returns to derivation from scripture. Where does Rabbi Yitzchak — who uses *v'nichresu* to exclude *malkos* from *karet* offenses — learn the principle of separate *korbanos* for multiple overlapping status categories? He derives it from *achoto d'seifa* — the word *achoto* that appears at the end of the *achoto* verse: *ervas achoto gilah*. It could have said simply *ervatah gilah*. The repetition of *achoto* signals that the accumulation of *achot*-related statuses in one person generates separate liabilities.

### Full Sister

Rabbi Akiva uses *achoto d'seifa* for something different. The prohibition against sleeping with one's sister, stated in *Acharei Mos* (Leviticus 18:9), explicitly mentions only a half-sister — *bas avicha o vat imecha* — one's sister on the father's side or the mother's side. It never explicitly prohibits a full sister, one who shares both parents. Yet the punishment passage in *Kedoshim* covers *achoto* broadly, and the extra *achoto* at the end — *ervas achoto gilah* — serves to establish that the prohibition extends to a full sister as well. Without the explicit *pasuk*, one might have thought the prohibition could be derived by *kal vachomer* from the half-sister case, but the rule is *ein onshin min hadin* — one does not derive punishments from logical inference. The verse is therefore necessary to ground the prohibition explicitly.

### Pitum & Sach

Rabbi Akiva still must account for the *v'nichresu* at the beginning of the *achoto* passage — which he uses for the three-in-one case of overlapping status categories — and *achoto d'seifa*, which he uses to establish the prohibition for a full sister. There is a third use of an extra *achoto* at the beginning of the passage, which Rabbi Akiva employs to address a distinct question: what is the law when two separate *lav* prohibitions share a single *karet*?

The case is the *pitum ha-ketores* — the incense compounded for the *Beis Hamikdash* — and *sach ha-shemen* — anointing with the *shemen ha-mishcha*. The Torah separately prohibits compounding the incense for private use and anointing oneself with the sacred oil, yet states a single *karet* referring to both violations. If a person committed both in a single *he'elam*, does he bring one *korban* or two? Since the principle of separate *korbanos* follows the *lavin* and not the *karet*, Rabbi Akiva derives from this *achoto* that even when two *lavin* share one *karet*, they each generate a separate *korban*. This is comparable to the principle already established for the *arayos* — one global *karet* does not merge multiple distinct violations.

Rabbi Yitzchak, who does not use *achoto d'seifa* for the multiple-statuses case and does not use *v'nichresu* for the three-in-one, derives the *pitum* and *sach* rule from the position of Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: wherever there are two *lavin* and a single *karet*, they are automatically treated as separate for purposes of *korban* — no special verse is required, as it follows logically from the structure of the *lavin*.

## Isha Dava Case

### Flow Location

Rabbi Yitzchak requires yet another derivation to account for the extra *karet* mentioned by the case of *isha dava* — a woman who is flowing. The verse in *Kedoshim* (20:18) states: *v'ish asher yishkav es isha dava v'gila es ervatah es mekora he-erati v'hi gilta es mekor dameha v'nichretu sheneihem*. The *v'nichretu* here is an extra mention of *karet* for a case already subsumed under the general *karet* rules. Rabbi Yochanan, in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, derives from this verse a fundamental rule: *minayin l'niddah ad she-yeitzei midveih derech ervatah* — how do we know that a woman does not acquire the status of *niddah* unless the blood flows specifically through the genital area? The verse links the flow of *dava* with *ervatah* — connecting the issue of flow to the place of nakedness. This teaches that a nosebleed or blood from another area of the body does not create the status of *niddah*.

The *gemara* presents this as one of several competing uses of the extra *karet* in the *isha dava* passage. Those who do not need it for this *halacha* can use it for the principle of separate *korbanos* in cases analogous to *pitum* and *sach*; those who derive that principle from Rabbi Elazar's logical rule need the *isha dava karet* for Rabbi Yochanan's teaching. Each opinion thus has its verses accounted for, and none is left without a source.

## Tamei Kodesh

### Mishnah's List

After the extended discussion of *arayos* and *karet*, the *gemara* now turns to individual entries in the *mishnah*'s list of those who receive *malkos*. Among them is the case of a *tamei* person who eats *kodesh* — sacrificial meat. The *mishnah* includes this among the violations that carry *malkos*, which requires both a punishment (*onesh*) and a prohibition (*azhara*) in the form of a *lav*.

### Prohibition Source

For the case of a *tamei* person entering the *Beis Hamikdash*, both elements are clearly present. The punishment — *v'nichreta* — appears in the verse *es mishkan Hashem timei v'nichreta* (Numbers 19:20). The prohibition appears in *v'lo yetam'u es machaneihem* (Numbers 5:3). But for a *tamei* person who eats *kodesh*, while the punishment is explicit — *hanefesh asher tochal mi-besar zevach hashlamim asher l'Hashem v'tumato alav v'nichreta* (Leviticus 7:20) — where is the *azhara*, the prohibitory *lav* that grounds the *malkos*?

There is a verse that initially appears to supply this prohibition: *u-vakodashim lo yocheil ad asher yithar* (Leviticus 22:4) — do not eat *kodashim* until you have become *tahor*. This sounds explicit. But the *gemara* will establish that this verse refers to *terumah*, not to *kodshim* proper, and so a different source must be found.

### Resh Lakish

Resh Lakish derives the *azhara* from the verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el hamikdash lo tavo* (Leviticus 12:4), which appears in the context of a woman after childbirth. The word *tiga* — "touch" — Resh Lakish understands to encompass eating as well.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan takes a different approach. He does not learn from *bechol kodesh lo tiga* at all; he derives the prohibition from a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *tumato*. The verse of *karet* for eating *kodesh* while *tamei* says *v'tumato alav v'nichreta*. The verse dealing with *misat beit din* for entering the *Beis Hamikdash* says *tamei yihye od tumato bo* (Numbers 19:13). By linking the two via the shared word *tumato*, Rabbi Yochanan extends the prohibition from the *Beis Hamikdash* context — where both punishment and prohibition exist — to the eating of *kodesh* context.

---

## Terumah vs. Kodesh

### Aharon's Seed

The reason Rabbi Yochanan cannot use *ish ish mi-zera Aharon v'hu tzarua o zav bakodashim lo yocheil* (Leviticus 22:4) as a prohibition against eating *kodesh* while *tamei* — even though that verse seems maximally explicit — is that the verse specifies *mi-zera Aharon*, the seed of Aaron. This phrase includes not only the male *kohanim* but all of Aaron's descendants, male and female alike. The question is: what is the one category of sacred food that all descendants of Aaron, men and women, share equal access to? The answer is *terumah*. *Kodashim* proper can only be eaten by male *kohanim* under specific conditions; *terumah* is the food shared by all of Aaron's household. Therefore, the prohibition in *ish ish mi-zera Aharon* is understood to be addressed to the eating of *terumah* in a state of *tumah*, not to *kodashim*.

### Touching vs. Eating

This forces the question of how Resh Lakish handles the *terumah* prohibition. If Resh Lakish uses *bechol kodesh lo tiga* for eating *kodesh* while *tamei*, where does he derive the prohibition against eating *terumah* while *tamei*? He takes it from *ish ish mi-zera Aharon*. And if Rabbi Yochanan uses *bechol kodesh lo tiga* for the prohibition against touching *terumah* while *tamei* (reading *lo tiga* in its plain sense), then both authorities use the same set of verses but assign them differently. Rabbi Yochanan: *bechol kodesh lo tiga* = prohibition against touching *terumah*; *ish ish mi-zera Aharon* = prohibition against eating *terumah*. Resh Lakish: *bechol kodesh lo tiga* = prohibition against eating *kodesh*; *ish ish mi-zera Aharon* = prohibition against eating *terumah*.

---

## Resh Lakish's Derivations

### Eating Kodesh

Resh Lakish derives the prohibition against a *tamei* person eating *kodesh* from *bechol kodesh lo tiga*. Even though the verse literally says *lo tiga* — "do not touch" — Resh Lakish understands it to be addressing eating. This creates an immediate internal tension: Resh Lakish himself also maintains that a *tamei* person who *touches kodesh* receives *malkos*. How can a single verse serve both purposes?

### Touching Kodesh

The *gemara* states directly: *deitmar tamei shenaga bekodesh — Resh Lakish amar lokeh* — a *tamei* person who touches *kodesh* receives *malkos*, and the source is *bechol kodesh lo tiga*. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *eino lokeh* — one does not receive *malkos* for touching *kodesh*. For Rabbi Yochanan, *bechol kodesh lo tiga* is addressed only to touching *terumah*, and there is no *malkos* for touching *kodesh* at all. *Tosafos* notes the irony in Rabbi Yochanan's position: he derives a prohibition from *lo tiga* for touching *terumah*, which is a lesser sanctity, while holding that there is no such prohibition for touching *kodesh* proper, which is holier. *Tosafos* suggests that for Rabbi Yochanan, *lo tiga* regarding *terumah* may not be a genuine prohibition at all but rather a statement that touching *terumah* while *tamei* renders it impure — a consequence rather than a prohibition.

### Beis HaMikdash Link

The *gemara* works out how Resh Lakish derives *both* the prohibition against eating *kodesh* and the prohibition against touching *kodesh* from a single verse. The answer lies in the structure of *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el hamikdash lo tavo*. The word *tiga* in its plain sense addresses touching — that is the prohibition against touching *kodesh* while *tamei*. The second half of the verse — *v'el hamikdash lo tavo* — creates a *hekesh*, a textual analogy, linking *kodesh* to the *mikdash*. Just as entry into the *mikdash* while *tamei* is a grave violation that carries *karet*, this analogy imports the same gravity for an act involving *kodesh*. And since touching *kodesh* does not carry *karet* — only eating *kodesh* while *tamei* does — the *hekesh* to the *mikdash* must be pointing to eating. Thus *lo tiga* teaches the prohibition against touching, and the *hekesh* to the *mikdash* teaches the prohibition against eating.

### Before Blood

Resh Lakish uses the verse for yet a third purpose. The *gemara* states: *tamei she'achal basar kodesh lifnei zrikat damim — Resh Lakish amar lokeh* — a *tamei* person who eats sacrificial meat *before* the blood of the offering has been sprinkled (*zrikat damim*) receives *malkos*. Rabbi Yochanan again disagrees: *eino lokeh*. Rabbi Yochanan's position is consistent with his overall approach: he derives the prohibition against eating *kodesh* while *tamei* from the *gezeirah shavah* of *tumato tumato*. That *gezeirah shavah* is anchored to the *karet* verse for eating *kodesh*, and that *karet* applies only once the meat is fit to be eaten — that is, after *zrikat damim*. Before the blood is sprinkled, the meat is not yet at the stage where eating it in *tumah* constitutes a fully culpable act.

Resh Lakish, by contrast, derives the prohibition from *bechol kodesh lo tiga*, and the word *bechol* — "every" or "any" — broadens the scope. *Lo shena lifnei zrika v'lo shena achar zrika* — it makes no difference whether it is before or after *zrikat damim*: the prohibition covers *any* act of eating *kodesh* while *tamei*. The word *bechol* is the textual basis for this extension.

### Braisa Support

The *gemara* concludes with a *braisa* that supports Resh Lakish: *tanya k'vaseih d'Resh Lakish* — it was taught in accordance with Resh Lakish. The *braisa* reads: *bechol kodesh lo tiga — azhara le'ochel*. The question is posed: when it says *lo tiga*, does it mean a prohibition against touching, or a prohibition against eating? The *braisa* answers by invoking the *hekesh*: *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el hamikdash lo tavo — makish kodesh l'mikdash*. Just as entry into the *mikdash* while *tamei* involves a *netilas nefesh* — a taking of life, that is, a *karet* offense — so too the act involving *kodesh* that rises to that level of severity must be one that carries *karet*. Touching *kodesh* does not carry *karet*; eating *kodesh* while *tamei* does. Therefore, the prohibition of *lo tiga* is ultimately about eating — *ela ba'achila*. For Resh Lakish, *lo tiga* thus operates on two levels simultaneously: its plain meaning addresses touching *kodesh* while *tamei*, and its contextual meaning — via the *hekesh* to the *mikdash* — addresses eating *kodesh* while *tamei*, with the word *bechol* further extending that prohibition to the period before *zrikat damim*.
