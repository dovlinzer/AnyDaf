# Makkot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lashes & Karet

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ הֵן הַלָּאוִין שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן כָּרֵת: הַבָּא עַל אִמּוֹ, וְעַל אֲחוֹתוֹ, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, וְעַל אֶשֶׁת אָבִיו, וְעַל אֶשֶׁת אָחִיו, וְעַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וְהַנָּשִׁים שֶׁמִּשְׁמְשׁוֹת בִּבְחִינַת הַגְּבָרִים, וּמִי שֶׁשּׁוֹמֵר אֶת־הַשַּׁבָּת, וּמִי שֶׁאוֹכֵל חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח, וּמִי שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן פֶּסַח בִּזְמַנּוֹ, וּמִי שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה מִילָה, וּמִי שֶׁנָּטַע אִילָן בַּחִנּוּךְ, וּמִי שֶׁיִּשְׁמַר מֵעֲבוֹדַת כּוֹכָבִים שְׁלוֹשׁ שָׁנִים בְּתוֹךְ הַשְּׁמִטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA. These are the negative commandments for which one is liable to receive *karet*: one who engages in intercourse with his mother, with his sister, with his paternal aunt, with his maternal aunt, with his father's wife, with his brother's wife, with an animal, women who engage in sexual conduct with animals, one who violates the Sabbath, one who eats *hametz* on Passover, one who does not bring the Paschal offering in its proper time, one who does not perform circumcision, one who plants a tree in the Temple courtyard, and one who refrains from performing *service* [in the context of idolatry] for three years within the Sabbatical year.

The opening *mishnah* of *Eilu Hein HaLokin* lists a substantial number of sins for which one incurs *karet*, and the *mishnah* presents this list as reflecting the position of Rabbi Akiva. The *gemara* on 13b frames this within a three-way debate recorded in a *braisa*.

### Rabbi Ishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֶשְׁנָן בִּכְלַל מַלְקוֹת אַרְבָּעִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **are** included **in the category** of those liable to receive **forty lashes** for violating a Torah prohibition. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

Rabbi Ishmael holds that one receives *malkos* for any negative transgression, even if the ultimate punishment is court execution — and certainly if the punishment is *karet*. This is a striking and unfamiliar position. The *gemara* explains it is based on the principle that lashes leading up to a more severe bodily punishment — whether imposed by the court or by the heavenly court — are all considered, in effect, one extended death. One does not receive both a monetary fine and lashes, since those represent two fundamentally different categories of punishment. But lashes administered prior to a harsher bodily consequence are considered part of a single drawn-out continuum.

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת (יֶשְׁנוֹ) [יֶשְׁנָן] בִּכְלַל מַלְקוֹת אַרְבָּעִים, שֶׁאִם עָשׂוּ תְּשׁוּבָה בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה מוֹחֲלִין לָהֶן. חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין אֵינוֹ בִּכְלַל מַלְקוֹת אַרְבָּעִים, שֶׁאִם עָשׂוּ תְּשׁוּבָה אֵין בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה מוֹחֲלִין לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: Those liable to** receive ***karet* are** included **in the category** of those liable to receive **forty lashes, because if they repented, the heavenly court absolves them** of the punishment of *karet*. Therefore, *karet* does not absolve them of the punishment of lashes. **Those liable to** be executed with **court**-imposed **death** penalties **are not** included **in the category** of those liable to receive **forty lashes, as** even **if they repented, the earthly court does not absolve them** of execution; and one is not punished by the court twice for performing the same transgression.

Rabbi Akiva takes a more nuanced position. He agrees that one does receive *malkos* when the accompanying punishment is *karet*, but he disagrees when the accompanying punishment is *misat beit din* — court execution. His reasoning flows from the verse *kedei rishato* (Deuteronomy 25:2): "according to his wickedness" — meaning, the court holds a person liable for one wickedness, not two. Double punishment by the court is impermissible.

Rabbi Ishmael would counter that this prohibition on double punishment applies only when the two punishments are of different types — monetary fine combined with execution, or monetary fine combined with lashes. But execution combined with lashes is all one extended death, and that is not a prohibited doubling. Rabbi Akiva's response to this is what distinguishes his position: he holds that *karet* does not fall within the category of double *beit din* punishment, because the person liable to *karet* might yet do *teshuvah* — and thus *karet* is not an inevitable second punishment.

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אִי הָכִי חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת נָמֵי! מַאי אָמְרַתְּ, שֶׁאִם עָשׂוּ תְּשׁוּבָה? הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת לָא עָבְדִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Akiva, if that is so,** that one does not receive two punishments for one transgression, **those liable to** receive ***karet* too** should not be flogged. **What did you say** in response, **that if they repented** they are exempt from *karet*, and therefore they are flogged instead? **Now, in any event,** at the point when they are flogged **they have not** yet **done** so, i.e., repented.

The *gemara* presses Rabbi Akiva on this reasoning. Even if *teshuvah* theoretically averts *karet*, the individual standing before the court has not yet done *teshuvah*. At this moment, he is deserving of *karet*. How, then, can the fact that he *could* escape it be sufficient reason to treat it as non-existent for purposes of the double-punishment concern? This question drives the *gemara* forward.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### L'Einei L'Einecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּפֵירוּשׁ רִיבְּתָה תּוֹרָה חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת לְמַלְקוּת, דִּגְמַר ״לְעֵינֵי״ מִ״לְּעֵינֶיךָ״. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: אִי הָכִי חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת בֵּית דִּין נָמֵי, נִגְמַר ״מֵעֵינֵי״ מִ״לְּעֵינֶיךָ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says: The Torah explicitly included with regard to lashes those liable to** receive ***karet*, as is derived** by means of a verbal analogy: "And they shall be excised [*venikhretu*] **before the eyes of [*le'einei*]** the children of their people" (Leviticus 20:17), **from:** "Forty he shall flog him…and your brother shall be dishonored **before your eyes [*le'einekha*]"** (Deuteronomy 25:3). **Rabbi Abba bar Memel objects to this: If so,** the inclusion of **those liable to** be executed with **court**-imposed **death** penalties **should also be learned** by means of a verbal analogy: "If **from the eyes of [*me'einei*]** the assembly it was performed unwittingly" (Numbers 15:24) stated with regard to idol worship, for which one is liable to be executed, **from: "Before your eyes,"** stated with regard to lashes. On that basis, one should derive that those liable to be executed with a court-imposed death penalty should also be flogged.

Rabbi Abbahu answers that Rabbi Akiva's position is not purely a matter of logical reasoning about the nature of *karet* — it is derived explicitly from a *pasuk*. The Torah itself includes those liable to *karet* within the category of those who receive *malkos*, through a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *einei*. By *karet*, the Torah writes *v'nichresu l'einei bnei amam* — "they shall be cut off before the eyes of their people" (Leviticus 20:17). By *malkos*, the Torah writes *v'niklah achicha l'einecha* — "your brother shall be degraded before your eyes" (Deuteronomy 25:3). The shared formulation *l'einei* / *l'einecha* creates a verbal analogy that explicitly links *karet* offenses to the punishment of *malkos*.

### Abba bar Mammal

Rabbi Abba bar Mammal challenged this derivation. If one can construct a *gezeirah shavah* between the *karet* verse and the *malkos* verse, why not construct an equally valid *gezeirah shavah* and reach the conclusion of Rabbi Ishmael — that even those liable to court execution receive *malkos*? By *misat beit din*, the verse in the context of *avodah zarah* writes *im me'einei ha'eidah ne'estah lishgagah* — "if it was hidden from the eyes of the congregation" (Numbers 15:24). Both that verse and the *malkos* verse contain the word *einei* / *einecha* — why not link those as well?

### Linguistic Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִין ״לְעֵינֵי״ מִ״לְּעֵינֶיךָ״, וְאֵין דָּנִין ״מֵעֵינֵי״ מִ״לְּעֵינֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that objection: **One derives** by means of a verbal analogy ***le'einei* from *le'einekha*,** due to the similar prefix, **but one does not derive *me'einei* from *le'einekha*.**

The *gemara* responds that the two links are not equally precise. The *karet* verse uses *l'einei* and the *malkos* verse uses *l'einecha* — both share the prefix *lamed*, making this a close parallel. The *misat beit din* verse, by contrast, uses *me'einei* — with a *mem* prefix rather than a *lamed*. The *gemara* thus rules: *danin l'einei m'l'einei v'ein danin me'einei m'l'einei* — one may construct a *gezeirah shavah* from *l'einei* to *l'einei*, but not from *me'einei* to *l'einei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי נָפְקָא מִינֵּיהּ? וְהָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְשָׁב הַכֹּהֵן״, ״וּבָא הַכֹּהֵן״, זוֹ הִיא שִׁיבָה וְזוֹ הִיא בִּיאָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what** significant **difference is there** between them that prevents derivation by means of a verbal analogy? **But didn't the school of Rabbi Yishmael teach** a verbal analogy with regard to leprosy of houses? The verse states: **"And the priest shall return [*veshav*]** on the seventh day" (Leviticus 14:39), and another verse with regard to the priest's visit seven days later states: **"And the priest shall come [*uva*]** and look" (Leviticus 14:44). **This returning** and **this coming** have the same meaning, and one can therefore derive by verbal analogy that the *halakha* that applies if the leprosy had spread at the conclusion of the first week applies if it had spread again by the end of the following week. Obviously, the less pronounced difference between *me'einei* and *le'einekha* should not prevent the teaching of a verbal analogy.

The *gemara* itself questions whether this distinction carries real weight. After all, the school of Rabbi Ishmael taught that *v'shav hakohen* and *u'va hakohen* — "the priest shall return" and "the priest shall come" — are treated as a *gezeirah shavah* in the laws of *tzara'at* of the house, even though these are entirely different words that simply share the same sense. If imprecise wording can be sufficient, why is the *mem*-*lamed* difference decisive? The *gemara* raises this as a standing question.

### No Double Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: ״כְּדֵי רִשְׁעָתוֹ״ – מִשּׁוּם רִשְׁעָה אַחַת אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁתֵּי רִשְׁעָיוֹת – בְּרִשְׁעָה הַמְּסוּרָה לְבֵית דִּין הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak received** a tradition **from** Rabbi Abbahu that the reason those liable to receive *karet* are flogged and it is not considered two punishments for one transgression is that when the verse states: **"According to the measure of his wickedness"** (Deuteronomy 25:2), from which it is derived: **For one evildoing you can render him liable, but you cannot render him liable for two evildoings, the verse is speaking with regard to an evildoing that is given to** the jurisdiction of **the court,** not to an act of wickedness punishable at the hand of Heaven.

Rabbi Shmuel bar Yitzchak resolved the underlying question by returning to Rabbi Akiva's own language. The verse *kedei rishato* — understood as "the court imposes one punishment" — establishes that *beit din* may not administer two punishments. This prohibits *misat beit din* together with *malkos*, since both are *beit din* punishments. But *karet* is a divine punishment, not a *beit din* punishment. There is no prohibition on *beit din* imposing *malkos* when God separately imposes *karet*, because these operate on entirely different planes. The question of whether the person will ultimately suffer *karet* — something he can avert through *teshuvah* — is between him and God, not a matter for the court below. The court's concern is exclusively with its own sanctions, and *malkos* stands on its own as the *beit din* response to a violated *lav*.

## Rava's Reframe

### Rava's Premise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ לִקְטָלָא – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאֵין לוֹקֶה וָמֵת. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – דְּאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ לְמַלְקוּת, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: לָאו שֶׁנִּיתַּן לְאַזְהָרַת מִיתַת בֵּית דִין – לוֹקִין עָלָיו. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: לָאו שֶׁנִּיתַּן לְאַזְהָרַת מִיתַת בֵּית דִין – אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says** with regard to the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva: If the witnesses or other onlookers **forewarned him** that if he performs the transgression he will be sentenced **to death, everyone,** even Rabbi Yishmael, **agrees that he is not** both **flogged and executed. When they disagree,** it is in a case **where they forewarned him** that he will be sentenced **to lashes. Rabbi Yishmael maintains** that with regard to **a prohibition** in the Torah **that** potentially **serves as a mandate** for **court**-imposed **capital punishment, one is flogged for its** violation in a case where there is no actual death penalty, **and Rabbi Akiva maintains** that with regard to **a prohibition** in the Torah **that** potentially **serves as a mandate** for **court**-imposed **capital punishment, one is not flogged for its** violation even if no death penalty is imposed, as that prohibition is punishable only by death.

Rava now significantly reframes the entire debate. He argues that Rabbi Ishmael could not have meant that a person receives both *malkos* and *misat beit din* simultaneously from the court. Everyone would agree that *beit din* does not impose two punishments — *ein lokeh u'mes*. Rava thus eliminates the most radical reading of Rabbi Ishmael's position.

### Warning Mismatch

If everyone agrees that *beit din* does not administer both *malkos* and *misat beit din*, then what exactly is the debate between Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva? According to Rava, the debate concerns a case where witnesses gave an incorrect *hasra'ah* — where they warned the transgressor that his act carries *malkos*, without mentioning *misat beit din*. Perhaps the witnesses had compassion and downplayed the severity, warning him only of lashes. He acknowledged the warning and proceeded anyway. Now, the court cannot impose *misat beit din* because the warning did not match that punishment. The question is: can the court impose *malkos*?

Rabbi Ishmael says yes. Even though the *lav* that this person violated normally carries *misat beit din*, every *lav* has within it an inherent capacity to generate *malkos*. Since *misat beit din* will not be imposed, *malkos* steps in. Rabbi Akiva disagrees: a *lav* that was assigned as the vehicle for *misat beit din* cannot be repurposed to produce *malkos*. The *lav* is dedicated, so to speak, to the heavier punishment, and when that punishment cannot be imposed due to a mismatched warning, the person goes free rather than receiving the lesser sanction.

### Rabbi Akiva Refined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אִי הָכִי חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת נָמֵי, לָאו שֶׁנִּיתַּן לְאַזְהָרַת כָּרֵת הוּא! אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי אָמַר אֲבִימִי מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת לָא צְרִיכִי הַתְרָאָה, שֶׁהֲרֵי פֶּסַח וּמִילָה עָנַשׁ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא הִזְהִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Rabbi Akiva, if so,** with regard to **those liable to** receive ***karet* as well,** they violated **a prohibition** in the Torah **that serves as a mandate** for ***karet*** and not for lashes. **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: This** is what **Avimi from Hagronya said in the name of Rava: Those liable to** receive ***karet* do not require forewarning, as** in the case of one who fails to fulfill the positive mitzvot of the **Paschal offering and circumcision** the Torah **punished** them with *karet* **even though it did not warn** that it is prohibited to fail to fulfill the mitzva. Therefore, the prohibitions written in cases punishable with *karet* are written to teach that one can receive lashes for their violation, not to mandate the punishment of *karet*.

Under Rava's reframing, Rabbi Akiva's concern about *karet* cases takes on a different dimension. If Rabbi Akiva holds that a *lav* assigned to one punishment cannot function to generate a different punishment, then why can a *lav* that accompanies *karet* generate *malkos*? The *lav* is, after all, associated with a *karet* offense. The answer, as Rav Mordechai reported in the name of Avimi of Hagronya in the name of Rava, is: *chayvei kerisos lo tsrichi hasra'ah* — for God to impose *karet*, no *lav* is required. This is demonstrated by the two cases of *karet* that carry no accompanying *lav* at all: failure to bring the *korban pesach* and failure to perform *bris milah*. In both cases, the Torah imposes *karet* for a passive omission — no prohibition was transgressed. This proves that *karet* operates entirely independently of any *lav*.

### Karet & Lav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא אַזְהָרָה לְקׇרְבָּן. דְּהָא פֶּסַח וּמִילָה דְּלֵית בְּהוּ אַזְהָרָה לָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But perhaps** the **prohibition** is written concerning those liable to receive *karet* with regard to liability **to** bring **an offering** for unwitting violation of the prohibition, not to teach that the transgressor will receive lashes, **as** for failure to fulfill the positive mitzvot of the **Paschal offering and circumcision, for which there is no** biblical **prohibition, one does not bring an offering.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָאו הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ כׇּל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה: מָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה, אַף כֹּל – שֵׁב וְאַל תַּעֲשֶׂה, לְאַפּוֹקֵי הָנֵי דְּקוּם עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that suggestion: **There,** with regard to the Paschal offering and circumcision, **that is not the reason** that one does not bring an offering. **Rather,** it is **due to** the fact **that all** the mitzvot in **the entire Torah** whose unwitting violation requires the transgressor to bring a sin-offering **are juxtaposed to** and likened to **idol worship. Just as idol worship** is a prohibition about which the Torah commands: **Sit and do not perform** the transgression, and one who unwittingly performs the transgression is liable to bring a sin-offering, **so too,** with regard to **any** prohibition about which the Torah commands: **Sit and do not perform** the transgression, one who unwittingly performs the transgression is liable to bring a sin-offering. This serves **to exclude these** mitzvot of the Paschal offering and circumcision, concerning **which** the Torah commands: **Arise** and **perform** the mitzva, and one violates the mitzva by refraining from action. In those cases, one who unwittingly fails to perform these mitzvot is not liable to bring a sin-offering.

Since *karet* does not require a *lav*, the *lav* in a *karet* case is not "used up" by the *karet*. It is genuinely free to serve as the basis for *malkos*. This is the economy of the *lo ta'aseh*: for Rabbi Akiva, a *lav* can only serve one purpose, and a *lav* assigned to *misat beit din* cannot generate *malkos*. But a *lav* in a *karet* case is not assigned to *karet* — *karet* exists without it. Therefore, the *lav* is available to produce *malkos* independently.

The *gemara* momentarily considers a challenge to this: perhaps the *lav* in *karet* cases is needed not for *karet* itself, but for the *korban* that accompanies *karet* offenses. After all, we observe that *pesach* and *milah* — the two *karet* offenses that lack a *lav* — also have no *korban* for their omission. Does this not show that the *lav* is part of the *korban* package?

The *gemara* rejects this. The reason *pesach* and *milah* carry no *korban* is not because of any *lav*. The paradigm for *korbanos* is *avodah zarah*, and the rule is *mah avodah zarah shev v'al ta'aseh* — just as *avodah zarah* involves an active transgression, every *korban* arises only from an active violation. *Pesach* and *milah* are sins of omission — of *kum va'aseh*, of failing to act — and therefore they fall outside the category of offenses requiring a *korban*, regardless of the presence or absence of a *lav*. A *korban* is owed whenever there is *karet* combined with an active transgression. The *lav* has no role in generating the *korban* obligation.

The conclusion is clean: *karet* does not need the *lav*. The *korban* does not need the *lav*. The *lav* is therefore entirely free to serve as the basis for *malkos*. Rabbi Akiva's position is consistent: he applies *malkos* to *karet* cases because the *lav* is unencumbered, and he withholds *malkos* from *misat beit din* cases because there the *lav* is dedicated to the court execution.

## Ravina's View

### Teshuvah Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כִּדְאָמְרִינַן מֵעִיקָּרָא, שֶׁאִם עָשׂוּ תְּשׁוּבָה – בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה מוֹחֲלִין לָהֶן. מַאי אָמְרַתְּ? הָא לָא עֲבוּד תְּשׁוּבָה? לָא פְּסִיקָא מִילְּתָא לְכָרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says: Actually,** the reason Rabbi Akiva rules that those liable to receive *karet* are flogged is **as we stated initially, that if they repented the heavenly court absolves them** of the punishment of *karet*, and therefore this is not a case of two punishments for one transgression, and there is no exemption from lashes. **What do you say** in response, **that they did not** yet **repent?** Nevertheless, since **the matter is not clear-cut with regard to *karet*,** as perhaps they repented, they are therefore not exempt from lashes.

Ravina offers a final account of Rabbi Akiva's reasoning, returning to what he characterizes as the original formulation. Rabbi Akiva's position, at its core, is that *karet* does not constitute a definite second punishment. The person who has violated a *karet* offense and is now before the court has not yet done *teshuvah* — that is true. But *karet* is not a punishment that *beit din* imposes; it is something God may or may not carry out, and crucially, the transgressor retains the ability to escape it entirely through repentance. Since there is no certainty that *karet* will materialize, imposing *malkos* does not constitute giving the person two definite punishments. Double punishment — in the relevant sense — means administering two certain, irrevocable sanctions. *Karet* does not qualify.

It is worth noting that throughout all these opinions, not one of them claims that receiving *malkos* exempts the person from *karet*. Rabbi Ishmael says both are received. Rabbi Akiva says *malkos* is administered alongside *karet*, but the two operate on separate planes and neither cancels the other. Ravina clarifies Rabbi Akiva's reasoning but does not alter the substance: the *malkos* is simply not the mechanism by which *karet* is averted.

## Rabbi Yitzchak

### Karet Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת בַּכְּלָל הָיוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצָאת כָּרֵת בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ – לְדוּנוֹ בְּכָרֵת וְלֹא בְּמַלְקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the *baraita* that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: Those liable** to receive ***karet*** in cases of incest **were included in** the principle: "For anyone who performs any of these abominations, the souls who do so shall be excised from among their people" (Leviticus 18:29). **And why was** the punishment of ***karet*,** when administered to one who engages in intercourse **with his sister, excluded** from this verse and mentioned independently (Leviticus 20:17)? It is **to sentence him** to be punished **with *karet* and not to** be punished **with lashes.**

Rabbi Yitzchak takes the most restrictive position of all: one does not receive *malkos* for any *karet* offense whatsoever. His reasoning, as presented through the *Tosafos* reading, begins from an inclusive premise. Those liable to *karet* were originally included within the general category of *lav* violators who receive *malkos* — so by default, one might have assumed *malkos* applies. The question is therefore: why did the Torah explicitly single out *karet* for the case of one's sister (*achoto*)?

### Achoto Proof

The verse in *Kedoshim* (20:17) contains a notably elaborate formulation: *v'ish asher yikach es achoso bas aviv o vas imo v'ra'ah es ervasah v'hi sir'eh es ervaso chesed hu v'nichresu l'einei v'nei amam ervas achoso gilah avono yisa*. Unlike the other *arayos* offenses, which receive their *karet* from the general concluding verse at the end of *Acharei Mos*, the sister case receives an explicit, dedicated mention of *v'nichresu* in *Kedoshim*. Rabbi Yitzchak reads this singling-out as a deliberate exclusion: it tells us that the *karet* here operates *instead of malkos* — *ladunu b'kares v'lo b'malkos*. The Torah is specifying that one who sleeps with his sister is punished through *karet* and not through *malkos*.

## Achoto Interpretation

### Thirty-Six Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, כָּרֵת בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ לְמָה לִי? לְחַלֵּק, וְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁאִם עֲשָׂאָן כּוּלָּם בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the Rabbis,** i.e., Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva, who maintain that those liable to receive *karet* are flogged, **why do I** need ***karet*** to be written in the case of one who engages in intercourse **with his sister;** what does it teach? The Gemara answers: It teaches **to divide** the various prohibitions of sexual intercourse with forbidden relatives, **and is in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says that if one performed all** the transgressions described in the passage in the Torah enumerating forbidden relatives **during one lapse of awareness,** he is **liable** to bring a separate sin-offering **for each and every one,** as one is liable to receive *karet* for each and every one.

The *mishnah* in *Kerisus* enumerates thirty-six transgressions for which one incurs *karet*. Rabbi Yochanan explains why the *mishnah* specifies this number: it is to teach that even if a person commits all thirty-six in a single lapse of awareness (*he'elam echad*), he is *chayav* for each one individually — up to a maximum of thirty-six *korbanos*.

### No Malkos

Rabbi Yitzchak uses the singling-out of *karet* in the *achoto* verse for the purpose just described: to establish that *karet* offenses do not carry *malkos*. The explicit *v'nichresu* in the *achoto* passage signals an exclusion from the general rule that *lav* violations incur *malkos*.

### Multiple Statuses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, לְחַלֵּק מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִוְּאֶל אִשָּׁה בְּנִדַּת טֻמְאָתָהּ, לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל אִשָּׁה וְאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** concerning **Rabbi Yitzḥak,** who derives the *halakha* that one who is liable to receive *karet* is not flogged from the fact that *karet* for one who engages in intercourse with his sister emerged from the generalization, **from where** does **he** derive the concept **to divide** the various prohibitions? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse written with regard to a menstruating woman: **"And to a woman in the separation of her impurity** you shall not approach" (Leviticus 18:19). From the superfluous expression "and to a woman," Rabbi Yitzḥak derives **that one is liable for** intercourse with **each and every** forbidden **woman.**

Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Ishmael — the *rabbanan* — who hold that *karet* offenses do carry *malkos*, need a different explanation for why the Torah singled out *karet* by the sister. They use it to teach the principle of *l'chaleik* — division. Even when a person commits multiple *karet* offenses in a single lapse of awareness, each offense counts separately and generates a separate *korban*. The explicit mention of *v'nichresu* by *achoto* establishes that every distinct *karet* transgression is treated independently, rather than collapsed into a single *korban*.

But the *gemara* presses further. The principle that different women and different sins generate different *korbanos* seems obvious: they are separate bodies, separate acts, separate offenses. The deeper question is whether the same principle applies when the *multiple* status categories all converge upon a single individual.

### Rasha Bar Rasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, כָּרֵת דַּאֲחוֹתוֹ לְמָה לִי? לְחַיְּיבוֹ עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ וְעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא, הֲרֵי גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין, הֲרֵי שֵׁמוֹת מוּחְלָקִין! אֶלָּא, לְחַיְּיבוֹ עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ. וְהֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ – בְּרַשִּׁיעָא בַּר רַשִּׁיעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **Rather,** the question again arises: **Why do I** need ***karet*** to be written in the case of one who engages in intercourse **with his sister;** what does it teach? The Gemara answers: It teaches **to render him liable** to bring three separate sin-offerings for engaging in intercourse **with his sister, and with his father's sister, and with his mother's sister,** during a single lapse of awareness. The Gemara asks: Isn't the fact that he is liable to bring three sin-offerings **obvious?** The actions were performed with **separate bodies,** i.e., three different women, and those actions violated the **names** of three **separate** prohibitions; no derivation is required. **Rather,** it is **to render him liable** to bring three separate sin-offerings for engaging in intercourse **with his sister who is** also **his father's sister** and **who is** also **his mother's sister.** The Gemara inquires: **And how can you find these** circumstances? The Gemara answers: It is found **in** the case of **a wicked person, son of a wicked person.** How so? If a man engages in intercourse with his mother, and she bears him two daughters, and he then engages in intercourse with one of the daughters and fathers a son, that son could engage in intercourse with the other daughter, who is his half sister through his father, as well as being his father's half sister and his mother's half sister.

The *gemara* resolves this by specifying the precise case the verse is addressing: *lachayiv al achoto she'hi achot aviv she'hi achot imo* — to establish liability for a woman who is simultaneously his sister, his father's sister, and his mother's sister. Three overlapping prohibitions, one woman, one act. The *gemara* asks how such a situation is even possible, and answers: *rasha bar rasha* — a wicked person, son of a wicked person. Rashi describes the scenario: a man has relations with his mother and produces two daughters; he then has relations with one of those daughters and produces a son; that son then has relations with his aunt — who is simultaneously his sister (sharing the same father) and his mother's sister (by direct lineage). The result is a single woman who carries three distinct forbidden statuses. It is for precisely this case — one body, multiple statuses — that the *v'nichresu* of *achoto* teaches that each status generates a separate *korban*.

## Separate Karbanos

### Five Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק, הָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: שָׁאַלְתִּי אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בָּאִיטְלִיז שֶׁל עֵימָאוּם, שֶׁהָלְכוּ לִיקַּח בְּהֵמָה לְמִשְׁתֵּה בְנוֹ שֶׁל רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: הַבָּא עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אָבִיו שֶׁהִיא אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, מַהוּ? [אֵינוֹ] חַיָּיב עַל כּוּלָּן אֶלָּא אַחַת, אוֹ חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **Rabbi Yitzḥak, from where does he** derive this? **He derives it by means of an *a fortiori*** inference, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says: I asked Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua** a question **in the meat market [*itliz*] of** the town of **Emmaus, where they went to purchase an animal for the** wedding **feast of Rabban Gamliel's son.** Rabbi Akiva asked: In the case of **one who engages in intercourse with his sister who is** also **his father's sister** and **who is** also **his mother's sister, what is** the *halakha* with regard to bringing a sin-offering? Is **he liable** to bring **only one** sin-offering **for** engaging in intercourse that violated **all of** the prohibitions, **or** is **he liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each and every** prohibition that he violated when he engaged in intercourse with that woman?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: זוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ, אֲבָל שָׁמַעְנוּ: הַבָּא עַל חָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים נִדּוֹת בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד, שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, וְנִרְאִין דְּבָרִים מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה נִדָּה שֶׁהִיא שֵׁם אֶחָד – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, כָּאן שֶׁשְּׁלֹשָׁה שֵׁמוֹת – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: We did not hear** the *halakha* in **that** case. **But we heard** the *halakha* in a similar case: **One who engages in intercourse with five menstruating women during one lapse of awareness** is **liable** to bring a sin-offering **for each and every one** of the women with whom he engaged in intercourse, **and it appears** with regard to the **matter** of your inquiry that he is liable to bring a sin-offering for each and every prohibition, **by means of an *a fortiori*** inference. How so? **If** in the case of **a menstruating woman, which** involves violation of the **name of one** prohibition, he is **liable for** intercourse with **each and every one, here, where** the woman is forbidden due to the **names** of **three** prohibitions, is it **not all the more so** logical that he is liable to bring three sin-offerings?

The *gemara* cites a tradition to clarify the broader principle. The foundational case for separate *korbanos* from separate sins in one *he'elam* is a man who has relations with five different *niddah* women in a single lapse of awareness: he is *chayav* a separate *korban* for each one. This is the paradigm case of the same *aveirah* but different women — different bodies, and therefore obviously different *korbanos*.

Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua were once asked by Rabbi Akiva — while they were all on their way to the meat market at Imaum to purchase an animal for Rabban Gamliel's son's wedding feast — what the law would be if a man slept with a woman who was simultaneously his sister, his father's sister, and his mother's sister. Would he bring one *korban* or three? They answered: *zo lo shamanu* — we have no explicit tradition on this. But they did have a tradition regarding five different *niddah* women in a single act of forgetfulness: each one requires a separate *korban*. From that they wished to reason: if *different women* and the *same aveirah* generate separate *korbanos*, then surely *different aveirot* in a *single woman* should generate separate *korbanos* as well — *kal vachomer*.

### Achoto Seifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר פְּרִיכָא הוּא: מָה לְנִדָּה, שֶׁכֵּן גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And** according to **the other** opinion of the Rabbis, who maintain that liability for intercourse with each and every one is derived from an explicit verse, **it is a refuted *a fortiori*** inference: **What** is notable **about** the case of **a menstruating woman?** It is notable **in that** the actions were performed with **separate bodies,** i.e., different women, while in the case in question, the person engaged in intercourse with one woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הַאי וַדַּאי קַל וָחוֹמֶר פְּרִיכָא הוּא! אֶלָּא, נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אֲחֹתוֹ״ דְּסֵיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according **to the other** opinion of Rabbi Yitzḥak **too, this is certainly a refuted *a fortiori*** inference, from which no *halakha* can be derived. **Rather,** as for the *halakha* that a person is liable to bring three sin-offerings for engaging in intercourse with his sister who is also his father's sister and who is also his mother's sister, Rabbi Yitzḥak **derives it from** the phrase **"his sister"** written **in the latter clause** of that verse. The verse begins: "And a man who takes his sister, the daughter of his father or the daughter of his mother…and they shall be excised…the nakedness of his sister he has revealed, he shall bear his transgression" (Leviticus 20:17). From the repetition of the term sister, it is derived that he is liable to bring a sin-offering for each and every prohibition that he violated.

The other position challenged this *kal vachomer* as *pricha* — refutable. Different bodies are inherently more distinct than different status categories. Since the *kal vachomer* is reversible, it cannot serve as a definitive proof.

The *gemara* therefore returns to derivation from scripture. Where does Rabbi Yitzchak — who uses *v'nichresu* to exclude *malkos* from *karet* offenses — learn the principle of separate *korbanos* for multiple overlapping status categories? He derives it from *achoto d'seifa* — the word *achoto* that appears at the end of the *achoto* verse: *ervas achoto gilah*. It could have said simply *ervatah gilah*. The repetition of *achoto* signals that the accumulation of *achot*-related statuses in one person generates separate liabilities.

### Full Sister

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ, ״אֲחוֹתוֹ״ דְּסֵיפָא לְמָה לִי? לְחַיְּיבוֹ עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ בַּת אָבִיו וּבַת אִמּוֹ, לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵין עוֹנְשִׁין מִן הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** opinion of the Rabbis, who derive this *halakha* from the term "sister" at the beginning of the verse, **why do I** need the phrase **"his sister"** to be written **in the latter clause** of that verse; what does it teach? The Gemara answers: It teaches **to render him liable for** engaging in intercourse with **his sister** who is also **the daughter of his father and the daughter of his mother,** which comes **to say that one does not administer punishment based on** an *a fortiori* **inference.** Although the Torah specified liability for one who engaged in intercourse with his paternal sister and for one who engaged in intercourse with his maternal sister, an independent derivation is necessary for liability for one who engaged in intercourse with a woman who is both his paternal and his maternal sister.

Rabbi Akiva uses *achoto d'seifa* for something different. The prohibition against sleeping with one's sister, stated in *Acharei Mos* (Leviticus 18:9), explicitly mentions only a half-sister — *bas avicha o vat imecha* — one's sister on the father's side or the mother's side. It never explicitly prohibits a full sister, one who shares both parents. Yet the punishment passage in *Kedoshim* covers *achoto* broadly, and the extra *achoto* at the end — *ervas achoto gilah* — serves to establish that the prohibition extends to a full sister as well. Without the explicit *pasuk*, one might have thought the prohibition could be derived by *kal vachomer* from the half-sister case, but the rule is *ein onshin min hadin* — one does not derive punishments from logical inference. The verse is therefore necessary to ground the prohibition explicitly.

### Pitum & Sach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא גָּמַר עוֹנֶשׁ מֵאַזְהָרָה. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אֲחוֹתוֹ״ דְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** opinion of Rabbi Yitzḥak, who derives another matter from the term "sister" in the latter verse, from where does he derive liability for one who engaged in intercourse with a woman who is both his paternal and his maternal sister? The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say he derives** the **punishment from the prohibition;** just as the prohibition includes intercourse with a woman who is both his paternal and his maternal sister, the same applies to the punishment. **And if you wish, say** instead that **he derives it from** the term **"his sister" at the beginning** of the verse (Leviticus 20:17), as the verse could have been formulated: And a man who takes the daughter of his father or the daughter of his mother, and there is no need to mention a sister at all. Liability for intercourse with a woman who is the daughter of both his father and his mother is derived from the term "his sister."

Rabbi Akiva still must account for the *v'nichresu* at the beginning of the *achoto* passage — which he uses for the three-in-one case of overlapping status categories — and *achoto d'seifa*, which he uses to establish the prohibition for a full sister. There is a third use of an extra *achoto* at the beginning of the passage, which Rabbi Akiva employs to address a distinct question: what is the law when two separate *lav* prohibitions share a single *karet*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, לְחַלֵּק כָּרֵת לַמְפַטֵּם וְלַסָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to **the other** opinion of the Rabbis, since nothing relevant to the topic of intercourse with one's sister is derived from the term "sister," **that** term **is necessary** in order to derive an unrelated matter, i.e., **to divide** the various prohibitions and establish liability **for** either **one who blends** anointing oil **or for one who applies** anointing oil to receive ***karet*,** if he performed either intentionally, and to bring a sin-offering if he performed either unwittingly, even though only a single punishment of *karet* is written in the verse: "A man who blends a mixture like it and who places any of it upon a non-priest, and he shall be excised from his people" (Exodus 30:33).

The case is the *pitum ha-ketores* — the incense compounded for the *Beis Hamikdash* — and *sach ha-shemen* — anointing with the *shemen ha-mishcha*. The Torah separately prohibits compounding the incense for private use and anointing oneself with the sacred oil, yet states a single *karet* referring to both violations. If a person committed both in a single *he'elam*, does he bring one *korban* or two? Since the principle of separate *korbanos* follows the *lavin* and not the *karet*, Rabbi Akiva derives from this *achoto* that even when two *lavin* share one *karet*, they each generate a separate *korban*. This is comparable to the principle already established for the *arayos* — one global *karet* does not merge multiple distinct violations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? סָבַר כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא שְׁנֵי לָאוִין וְכָרֵת אֶחָד – חֲלוּקִין הֵן לְקׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the other,** Rabbi Yitzḥak, **holds** in this regard **in accordance with** that which **Rabbi Elazar** says that **Rabbi Hoshaya says, as Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Hoshaya says** that there is a principle: **Any place where you find two** different **prohibitions and one** punishment of ***karet*** stated concerning them, **they are distinct with regard to** liability to bring **an offering;** if he performed both unwittingly, he is liable to bring two sin-offerings. According to Rabbi Yitzḥak, there is no need for an additional derivation to render one who unwittingly blends and applies anointing oil liable to bring two sin-offerings.

Rabbi Yitzchak, who does not use *achoto d'seifa* for the multiple-statuses case and does not use *v'nichresu* for the three-in-one, derives the *pitum* and *sach* rule from the position of Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: wherever there are two *lavin* and a single *karet*, they are automatically treated as separate for purposes of *korban* — no special verse is required, as it follows logically from the structure of the *lavin*.

## Isha Dava Case

### Flow Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: לָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וְנָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכַּב אֶת אִשָּׁה דָּוָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **he does not hold in accordance with** that which **Rabbi Elazar** says that Rabbi Hoshaya says in this regard, **and he derives** liability to bring two sin-offerings for one who unwittingly blends and applies anointing oil **from** a superfluous term elsewhere: **"And a man who lies with a woman who is afflicted,** and…both of them shall be excised" (Leviticus 20:18). Intercourse with a menstruating woman is already included in the verse: "For anyone who performs any of these abominations, the souls who do so shall be excised from among their people" (Leviticus 18:29). Since this verse does not introduce any nuance concerning the punishment of *karet* for one who engages in intercourse with a menstruating woman, an unrelated matter is derived, which establishes liability for one who blends anointing oil and for one who applies anointing oil to receive *karet* if he performed either act intentionally, and to bring a sin-offering if he performed either act unwittingly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה טְמֵאָה עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מַדְוֶהָ דֶּרֶךְ עֶרְוָתָהּ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכַּב אֶת אִשָּׁה דָּוָה וְגִלָּה אֶת עֶרְוָתָהּ וְגוֹ׳״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵין הָאִשָּׁה טְמֵאָה עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מַדְוֶהָ דֶּרֶךְ עֶרְוָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to **the other** opinion of the Rabbis, what is derived from the verse? **That** verse **is necessary for him to** derive the *halakha* **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: From where** is it derived **that a woman is impure** as a menstruating woman **only if the blood of menstruation is discharged through her vagina?** Blood discharged from any other orifice does not render her impure. It is derived **as it is stated: "And a man who lies with a woman who is afflicted, and uncovers her nakedness"** (Leviticus 20:18), which **teaches that a woman is impure only if the blood of menstruation is discharged through her vagina.**

Rabbi Yitzchak requires yet another derivation to account for the extra *karet* mentioned by the case of *isha dava* — a woman who is flowing. The verse in *Kedoshim* (20:18) states: *v'ish asher yishkav es isha dava v'gila es ervatah es mekora he-erati v'hi gilta es mekor dameha v'nichretu sheneihem*. The *v'nichretu* here is an extra mention of *karet* for a case already subsumed under the general *karet* rules. Rabbi Yochanan, in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, derives from this verse a fundamental rule: *minayin l'niddah ad she'yeitzei midveih derech ervatah* — how do we know that a woman does not acquire the status of *niddah* unless the blood flows specifically through the genital area? The verse links the flow of *dava* with *ervatah* — connecting the issue of flow to the place of nakedness. This teaches that a nosebleed or blood from another area of the body does not create the status of *niddah*.

The *gemara* presents this as one of several competing uses of the extra *karet* in the *isha dava* passage. Those who do not need it for this *halacha* can use it for the principle of separate *korbanos* in cases analogous to *pitum* and *sach*; those who derive that principle from Rabbi Elazar's logical rule need the *isha dava karet* for Rabbi Yochanan's teaching. Each opinion thus has its verses accounted for, and none is left without a source.

## Tamei Kodesh

### Mishnah's List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְטָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ״. בִּשְׁלָמָא הַבָּא לַמִּקְדָּשׁ טָמֵא – כְּתִיב עוֹנֶשׁ וּכְתִיב אַזְהָרָה: עוֹנֶשׁ – דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶת מִשְׁכַּן ה׳ טִמֵּא וְנִכְרְתָה״. אַזְהָרָה – ״וְלֹא יְטַמְּאוּ אֶת מַחֲנֵיהֶם״. אֶלָּא טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ, בִּשְׁלָמָא ״עוֹנֶשׁ״ כְּתִיב – ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר מִזֶּבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים אֲשֶׁר לַה׳ וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו וְנִכְרְתָה״, אֶלָּא אַזְהָרָה מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And a ritually impure person who ate sacrificial** food, and one who entered the Temple while ritually impure, are flogged. The Gemara asks: **Granted, one who entered the Temple** while ritually **impure** is flogged, as **a punishment is written and a prohibition is written.** The Gemara elaborates: There is **a punishment, as it is written: "He has rendered impure the Tabernacle of the Lord, and** that soul **shall be excised"** (Numbers 19:13). And there is **a prohibition,** as it is written: **"And they shall not render their camp impure"** (Numbers 5:3). **But** with regard to **a ritually impure person who ate sacrificial** food, **granted, a punishment is written: "And the soul that eats from the flesh of a peace-offering that pertains to the Lord and his impurity is upon him, and** that soul **shall be excised"** (Leviticus 7:20). **But from where is a prohibition** derived?

After the extended discussion of *arayos* and *karet*, the *gemara* now turns to individual entries in the *mishnah*'s list of those who receive *malkos*. Among them is the case of a *tamei* person who eats *kodesh* — sacrificial meat. The *mishnah* includes this among the violations that carry *malkos*, which requires both a punishment (*onesh*) and a prohibition (*azhara*) in the form of a *lav*.

### Prohibition Source

For the case of a *tamei* person entering the *Beis Hamikdash*, both elements are clearly present. The punishment — *v'nichreta* — appears in the verse *es mishkan Hashem timei v'nichreta* (Numbers 19:20). The prohibition appears in *v'lo yetam'u es machaneihem* (Numbers 5:3). But for a *tamei* person who eats *kodesh*, while the punishment is explicit — *hanefesh asher tochal mi-besar zevach hashlamim asher l'Hashem v'tumato alav v'nichreta* (Leviticus 7:20) — where is the *azhara*, the prohibitory *lav* that grounds the *malkos*?

There is a verse that initially appears to supply this prohibition: *u-vakodashim lo yocheil ad asher yithar* (Leviticus 22:4) — do not eat *kodashim* until you have become *tahor*. This sounds explicit. But the *gemara* will establish that this verse refers to *terumah*, not to *kodshim* proper, and so a different source must be found.

### Resh Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** The prohibition is derived from that which is written with regard to a woman after childbirth who has not yet completed the purification process: **"No consecrated item shall she touch"** (Leviticus 12:4), and the reference is not merely to touching, but to eating.

Resh Lakish derives the *azhara* from the verse *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el hamikdash lo tavo* (Leviticus 12:4), which appears in the context of a woman after childbirth. The word *tiga* — "touch" — Resh Lakish understands to encompass eating as well.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר, תָּנֵי בַּרְדְּלָא: אָתְיָא ״טוּמְאָתוֹ״ ״טוּמְאָתוֹ״, כְּתִיב הָכָא ״וְטֻמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו וְנִכְרְתָה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״טָמֵא יִהְיֶה עוֹד טֻמְאָתוֹ בּוֹ״, מָה לְהַלָּן עוֹנֶשׁ וְאַזְהָרָה – אַף כָּאן עוֹנֶשׁ וְאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that a Sage named **Bardela teaches:** The matter **is derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the term **"his impurity"** written here, and the term **"his impurity"** written there. **Here,** with regard to a ritually impure person who eats sacrificial food, **it is written: "And his impurity is upon him, and** that soul **shall be excised," and there,** with regard to a ritually impure person entering the Temple, **it is written: "He shall be impure, his impurity is yet upon him"** (Numbers 19:13). **Just as there,** with regard to a ritually impure person who entered the Sanctuary, there is **a punishment and** there is **a prohibition,** as noted earlier, **so too here,** with regard to a ritually impure person who ate sacrificial food, it is derived that there is **a punishment and** there is **a prohibition.**

Rabbi Yochanan takes a different approach. He does not learn from *bechol kodesh lo tiga* at all; he derives the prohibition from a *gezeirah shavah* on the word *tumato*. The verse of *karet* for eating *kodesh* while *tamei* says *v'tumato alav v'nichreta*. The verse dealing with *misat beit din* for entering the *Beis Hamikdash* says *tamei yihye od tumato bo* (Numbers 19:13). By linking the two via the shared word *tumato*, Rabbi Yochanan extends the prohibition from the *Beis Hamikdash* context — where both punishment and prohibition exist — to the eating of *kodesh* context.

## Terumah vs. Kodesh

### Aharon's Seed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לָא גְּמִיר. אֶלָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ? אָמַר לָךְ: הַהוּא אַזְהָרָה לִתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the dispute: **Granted, Reish Lakish did not say** that the source for the prohibition is a verbal analogy **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** as **he did not receive** this **verbal analogy** as a tradition from his teachers and one may not derive a verbal analogy on his own. **But what is the reason that Rabbi Yoḥanan did not say** that the source of the prohibition is the verse "No consecrated item shall she touch," **in accordance with** the opinion of **Reish Lakish,** as that appears to be a more straightforward source? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yoḥanan could **say to you: That** is not a prohibition for partaking of sacrificial food in a state of impurity; rather, it is **a prohibition for** partaking of ***teruma*** in a state of impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אַזְהָרָה לִתְרוּמָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אִישׁ אִישׁ מִזֶּרַע אַהֲרֹן וְהוּא צָרוּעַ אוֹ זָב״, אֵי זֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא שָׁוֶה בְּזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זוֹ תְּרוּמָה. וְאִידַּךְ? הַהוּא לַאֲכִילָה, וְהָא לִנְגִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Reish Lakish, from where** does **he** derive **a prohibition against** partaking of ***teruma*** in a state of impurity? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** that which is written: **"Any man from the descendants of Aaron who is leprous or a *zav*** may not partake of the sacred food until he will be pure" (Leviticus 22:4). The phrase "descendants of Aaron" indicates that the prohibition applies to all descendants, including women. **What,** then, **is an item whose** status **is equal** with regard **to all descendants of Aaron,** as opposed to certain sacrificial foods that may be eaten only by males? **You must say it is *teruma*.** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, what is derived from this verse? The Gemara answers: **This** verse serves **to** prohibit **partaking** of *teruma* while impure; **and that** verse: "No consecrated item shall she touch," serves **to** prohibit **touching** *teruma* while impure.

The reason Rabbi Yochanan cannot use *ish ish mi-zera Aharon v'hu tzarua o zav bakodashim lo yocheil* (Leviticus 22:4) as a prohibition against eating *kodesh* while *tamei* — even though that verse seems maximally explicit — is that the verse specifies *mi-zera Aharon*, the seed of Aaron. This phrase includes not only the male *kohanim* but all of Aaron's descendants, male and female alike. The question is: what is the one category of sacred food that all descendants of Aaron, men and women, share equal access to? The answer is *terumah*. *Kodashim* proper can only be eaten by male *kohanim* under specific conditions; *terumah* is the food shared by all of Aaron's household. Therefore, the prohibition in *ish ish mi-zera Aharon* is understood to be addressed to the eating of *terumah* in a state of *tumah*, not to *kodashim*.

### Touching vs. Eating

This forces the question of how Resh Lakish handles the *terumah* prohibition. If Resh Lakish uses *bechol kodesh lo tiga* for eating *kodesh* while *tamei*, where does he derive the prohibition against eating *terumah* while *tamei*? He takes it from *ish ish mi-zera Aharon*. And if Rabbi Yochanan uses *bechol kodesh lo tiga* for the prohibition against touching *terumah* while *tamei* (reading *lo tiga* in its plain sense), then both authorities use the same set of verses but assign them differently. Rabbi Yochanan: *bechol kodesh lo tiga* = prohibition against touching *terumah*; *ish ish mi-zera Aharon* = prohibition against eating *terumah*. Resh Lakish: *bechol kodesh lo tiga* = prohibition against eating *kodesh*; *ish ish mi-zera Aharon* = prohibition against eating *terumah*.

## Resh Lakish's Derivations

### Eating Kodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, הַאי ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְטָמֵא שֶׁנָּגַע בַּקֹּדֶשׁ! דְּאִיתְּמַר: טָמֵא שֶׁנָּגַע בַּקֹּדֶשׁ, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה, ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: אֵין לוֹקֶה, הָהוּא אַזְהָרָה לִתְרוּמָה הוּא דַּאֲתָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **Reish Lakish,** does **this** verse: **"No consecrated item shall she touch"** (Leviticus 12:4), **come to** teach **this** prohibition concerning partaking of sacrificial food? **He requires that** verse in order **to** derive the prohibition of **a ritually impure person who touched sacrificial** food. **As it was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute with regard to **a ritually impure person who touched sacrificial** food. **Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged.** The Gemara elaborates: **Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged,** as it is written: **"No consecrated item shall she touch." Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged,** because it **is** to teach **a prohibition for** touching ***teruma* that this** verse **comes.**

Resh Lakish derives the prohibition against a *tamei* person eating *kodesh* from *bechol kodesh lo tiga*. Even though the verse literally says *lo tiga* — "do not touch" — Resh Lakish understands it to be addressing eating. This creates an immediate internal tension: Resh Lakish himself also maintains that a *tamei* person who *touches kodesh* receives *malkos*. How can a single verse serve both purposes?

### Touching Kodesh

The *gemara* states directly: *deitmar tamei shenaga bekodesh — Resh Lakish amar lokeh* — a *tamei* person who touches *kodesh* receives *malkos*, and the source is *bechol kodesh lo tiga*. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *eino lokeh* — one does not receive *malkos* for touching *kodesh*. For Rabbi Yochanan, *bechol kodesh lo tiga* is addressed only to touching *terumah*, and there is no *malkos* for touching *kodesh* at all. *Tosafos* notes the irony in Rabbi Yochanan's position: he derives a prohibition from *lo tiga* for touching *terumah*, which is a lesser sanctity, while holding that there is no such prohibition for touching *kodesh* proper, which is holier. *Tosafos* suggests that for Rabbi Yochanan, *lo tiga* regarding *terumah* may not be a genuine prohibition at all but rather a statement that touching *terumah* while *tamei* renders it impure — a consequence rather than a prohibition.

### Beis HaMikdash Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמֵא שֶׁנָּגַע בַּקֹּדֶשׁ – מִדְּאַפְּקֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא בִּלְשׁוֹן נְגִיעָה, אַזְהָרָה לָאוֹכֵל – אִתַּקַּושׁ קֹדֶשׁ לְמִקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Reish Lakish derives both prohibitions from this verse. The prohibition concerning **a ritually impure person who touched sacrificial** food is derived **from** the fact **that the Merciful One formulates** the prohibition **with the term touching.** The **prohibition for** a ritually impure person who **partakes** of sacrificial food is derived because the matter of a ritually impure person eating **sacrificial** food **is juxtaposed to** the matter of entering **the Temple** while ritually impure, as the Gemara explains below.

The *gemara* works out how Resh Lakish derives *both* the prohibition against eating *kodesh* and the prohibition against touching *kodesh* from a single verse. The answer lies in the structure of *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el hamikdash lo tavo*. The word *tiga* in its plain sense addresses touching — that is the prohibition against touching *kodesh* while *tamei*. The second half of the verse — *v'el hamikdash lo tavo* — creates a *hekesh*, a textual analogy, linking *kodesh* to the *mikdash*. Just as entry into the *mikdash* while *tamei* is a grave violation that carries *karet*, this analogy imports the same gravity for an act involving *kodesh*. And since touching *kodesh* does not carry *karet* — only eating *kodesh* while *tamei* does — the *hekesh* to the *mikdash* must be pointing to eating. Thus *lo tiga* teaches the prohibition against touching, and the *hekesh* to the *mikdash* teaches the prohibition against eating.

### Before Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְטָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל בְּשַׂר קֹדֶשׁ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים! דְּאִיתְּמַר: טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל בְּשַׂר קֹדֶשׁ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And still, does** the verse **come for that** derivation? Reish Lakish **requires that** verse in order **to** derive the prohibition for **a ritually impure person who ate sacrificial meat before the sprinkling of** the **blood** of the offering on the altar, when it is not permitted to partake of the meat. **As it was stated,** there is an amoraic dispute with regard to **a ritually impure person who ate sacrificial meat before the sprinkling of** the **blood. Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged** for doing so; **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: לוֹקֶה, ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע״, לָא שְׁנָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה וְלָא שְׁנָא לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״טוּמְאָתוֹ״ ״טוּמְאָתוֹ״, וְכִי כְּתִיב ״טוּמְאָתוֹ״ – לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה הוּא דִּכְתִיב! הָהִיא מִ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says:** He is **flogged,** as it is written: **"No consecrated item shall she touch,"** which is stated in general terms, indicating that it **is no different** whether one touches the consecrated item **prior to sprinkling** the blood, **and** it **is no different** if one touches the consecrated item **after sprinkling** the blood. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He is not flogged. Rabbi Yoḥanan** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning,** that the prohibition is derived by means of the verbal analogy cited above, **as the verse states "his impurity,"** written with regard to a ritually impure person who eats sacrificial food, and the meaning of that term is derived from **"his impurity,"** written with regard to a ritually impure person entering the Temple. **And when "his impurity" is written,** it is with regard to partaking of sacrificial meat **after the sprinkling** of the blood **that it is written.** The Gemara explains that according to Reish Lakish, **that** prohibition **is derived from** the term: **"No [*bekhol*] consecrated item,"** as the term *bekhol* is inclusive and prohibits one from partaking of sacrificial meat even before the sprinkling of the blood.

Resh Lakish uses the verse for yet a third purpose. The *gemara* states: *tamei she'achal basar kodesh lifnei zrikat damim — Resh Lakish amar lokeh* — a *tamei* person who eats sacrificial meat *before* the blood of the offering has been sprinkled (*zrikat damim*) receives *malkos*. Rabbi Yochanan again disagrees: *eino lokeh*. Rabbi Yochanan's position is consistent with his overall approach: he derives the prohibition against eating *kodesh* while *tamei* from the *gezeirah shavah* of *tumato tumato*. That *gezeirah shavah* is anchored to the *karet* verse for eating *kodesh*, and that *karet* applies only once the meat is fit to be eaten — that is, after *zrikat damim*. Before the blood is sprinkled, the meat is not yet at the stage where eating it in *tumah* constitutes a fully culpable act.

Resh Lakish, by contrast, derives the prohibition from *bechol kodesh lo tiga*, and the word *bechol* — "every" or "any" — broadens the scope. *Lo shena lifnei zrika v'lo shena achar zrika* — it makes no difference whether it is before or after *zrikat damim*: the prohibition covers *any* act of eating *kodesh* while *tamei*. The word *bechol* is the textual basis for this extension.

### Braisa Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: ״בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע״ – אַזְהָרָה לָאוֹכֵל. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר אַזְהָרָה לָאוֹכֵל, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אַזְהָרָה לַנּוֹגֵעַ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: בְּכׇל קֹדֶשׁ לֹא תִגָּע וְאֶל הַמִּקְדָּשׁ וְגוֹ׳״ – מַקִּישׁ קֹדֶשׁ לַמִּקְדָּשׁ, מָה מִקְדָּשׁ דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה – אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה. וְאִי בִּנְגִיעָה – מִי אִיכָּא נְטִילַת נְשָׁמָה? אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Reish Lakish.** It is written: **"No consecrated item shall she touch";** this is **a prohibition for** a ritually impure person who **eats** sacrificial food. **Do you say** that it is **a prohibition for** a ritually impure person who **eats** sacrificial food, **or** perhaps **it is only a prohibition for** a ritually impure person who **touches** sacrificial food? **The verse states: "No consecrated item shall she touch, and to the Temple** she may not come" (Leviticus 12:4). The verse **juxtaposes** the matter of a ritually impure person eating **sacrificial** food **to** the matter of entering **the Temple** while ritually impure. **Just as** entering **the Temple** is **a matter** that entails a punishment **that involves the taking of a life,** i.e., *karet*, **so too, every matter** in that verse entails a punishment **that involves the taking of a life.** The Gemara explains: **And if** the prohibition is **with regard to touching** sacrificial food, **is there** a punishment that entails **the taking of a life? Rather,** the prohibition is **with regard to eating.**

The *gemara* concludes with a *braisa* that supports Resh Lakish: *tanya k'vaseih d'Resh Lakish* — it was taught in accordance with Resh Lakish. The *braisa* reads: *bechol kodesh lo tiga — azhara le'ochel*. The question is posed: when it says *lo tiga*, does it mean a prohibition against touching, or a prohibition against eating? The *braisa* answers by invoking the *hekesh*: *bechol kodesh lo tiga v'el hamikdash lo tavo — makish kodesh l'mikdash*. Just as entry into the *mikdash* while *tamei* involves a *netilas nefesh* — a taking of life, that is, a *karet* offense — so too the act involving *kodesh* that rises to that level of severity must be one that carries *karet*. Touching *kodesh* does not carry *karet*; eating *kodesh* while *tamei* does. Therefore, the prohibition of *lo tiga* is ultimately about eating — *ela ba'achila*. For Resh Lakish, *lo tiga* thus operates on two levels simultaneously: its plain meaning addresses touching *kodesh* while *tamei*, and its contextual meaning — via the *hekesh* to the *mikdash* — addresses eating *kodesh* while *tamei*, with the word *bechol* further extending that prohibition to the period before *zrikat damim*.
