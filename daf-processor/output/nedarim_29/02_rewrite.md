# Nedarim 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pidyon & Meilah

### Daf Overview

Daf 29 is a relatively compact daf, and while the sheer volume of *Ran* commentary might appear intimidating, the conceptual difficulty is more manageable than many other pages of the tractate. The *gemara* picks up at the bottom of 28b and moves into a *mishna* dealing with the concepts of *pidyon* and, relatedly, *meilah* in the context of *konamot*.

The *mishna* presents two cases. If someone declares that certain saplings (*netiot*) or a garment (*tallit*) are *korban*, that declaration carries with it the possibility of *pidyon* — the object's status can be transferred through redemption. However, if the declaration is temporally limited — "it is *korban* until it is burned" or "until it is cut down" — then *ein la pidyon*: no redemption is possible. Understanding why a time-bound *konam* eliminates *pidyon* is the central problem of the *sugya*.

### Konamot & Korbanot

Before entering the particulars of the *mishna*, it is necessary to establish the foundational framework. The key principle is that *konamot yesh bahen mishum meilah* — *konamot* draw from the world of *korbanot*. This applies specifically when the *konam* formula is used and when the prohibition is directed not merely at a particular individual but at everyone, making the object universally forbidden in the manner of a genuine *korban*. Under these conditions, the *konam* generates two significant legal consequences: first, the concept of *meilah* — misuse of the object, not merely a violation of the *neder*, but an act akin to trespass of *hekdesh*; and second, the concept of *pidyon* — the capacity to transfer that status onto something else, just as one may redeem an object consecrated to the Temple.

### Meilah Concept

The concept of *meilah* in this context is one of trespass and misuse rather than theft. When something is *hekdesh*, using it improperly constitutes *meilah*. The same logic, when imported into the world of *konamot*, means that benefiting from an object declared *konam* to everyone carries this added dimension beyond the mere *neder* violation.

### Pidyon Mechanism

*Pidyon* here operates not as a transfer of ownership but as a transfer of status — and it is important to appreciate the distinction. If one sanctifies an animal as a *shelamim*, the Temple does not own the animal in the usual sense; ownership remains with the individual, but the animal now carries a status. When the animal becomes blemished, that status can be transferred onto money or another object through *pidyon*. The same logic applies here: the *konam* creates a status, and that status — not ownership — is what *pidyon* acts upon. This is the working presumption with which the *gemara* enters its analysis.

## Bar Pada's Reading

### Pidyon Paradox

The *gemara* opens with a challenge: *ule'olam* — is it truly the case that these objects can never be redeemed? Given that the principle is that *konamot* carry the mechanism of *pidyon*, why should a time-limited *konam* eliminate that mechanism entirely?

### Auto-Cessation Problem

Bar Pada's answer is as striking as it is counterintuitive. He argues that it is conceptually impossible for a halachic status to simply dissolve on its own, automatically, without an affirmative act of removal. The *gemara* illustrates this with two analogies: if someone were to say to a woman "you are my wife today but not my wife tomorrow," she would not cease to be married without a *get*; similarly, if one were to say "this animal is an *olah* today but not an *olah* tomorrow," the *olah* status would not simply evaporate. Something that pertains to an object's fundamental identity and status cannot be placed on a clock. You can transfer ownership for a fixed period — a *kinyan le'zman* is possible — but you cannot limit a status that goes to the very nature of what something is.

### Ongoing Mechanism

On the basis of this principle, Bar Pada reinterprets the phrase *ad she'yikatzetz* — "until they are cut down." Rather than reading it as a simple terminus, he reads it as expressing an ongoing, continuous reapplication of the *konam* status. The declaration means: this status will persistently apply until the saplings are cut. The word *ad* does not mean "up to the point at which," after which the status ceases; it means the status is continually being reinstalled throughout the entire period preceding the cutting.

### Cyclical Pidyon

This reading explains the *mishna*'s ruling that *ein lahem pidyon* — not that *pidyon* is categorically inoperative, but that it cannot achieve a lasting effect. If one attempts *pidyon*, the act does transfer the *konam* status onto the object used for redemption — in that sense *pidyon* works — but simultaneously the original object immediately reacquires its *konam* status, because the declaration is an ongoing mechanism running until the cutting. One can repeat the *pidyon* indefinitely; each time the other object picks up the status, and each time the original is immediately re-*konam*-ed. Once the saplings are actually cut, the ongoing mechanism ceases, and a single act of *pidyon* then suffices — *padaan pa'am achat vedayo*.

This is why *ein lahem pidyon* does not mean *pidyon* is impossible in principle; it means that *pidyon* cannot achieve a permanent result in the pre-cutting period. The distinction matters: saying *pidyon* fundamentally does not operate would leave the mechanism unexplained; saying it operates but is immediately reversed by an ongoing reinstallation is the logically coherent reading Bar Pada requires.

## Ulla's Reading

### Automatic Cessation

Ulla reads the *mishna* differently. He holds that *keivan she'niktzetzu shuv ein podan* — once the saplings are cut, there is no need to redeem them at all, because their *konam* status ceases automatically upon cutting. He reads *ad she'yikatzetz* in its most natural sense: these saplings have this status until they are cut, and once they are cut, the status is simply gone.

### Ad Semantics

The entire dispute thus hinges on the semantics of *ad*. Bar Pada maintains that *ad* cannot mean "until," in the sense of automatic cessation, because no status can dissolve without an affirmative removal. *Ad she'yikatzetz* must therefore mean "ongoing until" — an active, continuous application of the status up to the point of cutting, with a permanent result achievable only through *pidyon* after cutting. Ulla, by contrast, holds that *ad* means precisely "until": the status is present and static up to the point of cutting, and then it ends — no act of removal required.

### Ulla's Pidyon

A significant implication follows for Ulla's position regarding the pre-cutting period. The *Ran* presents two approaches to understanding Ulla. One approach holds that even according to Ulla, *pidyon* during the pre-cutting period would repeatedly re-establish the status, similar to Bar Pada's mechanism — though the status then ceases automatically at cutting regardless. The other approach, taken as obvious by *Tosafot*, is simpler: since Ulla reads *ad* as a plain terminus and rejects the idea of any ongoing reinstallation mechanism, if one were to perform *pidyon* during the pre-cutting period, the *pidyon* would be permanently effective. The object would be redeemed and done. Ulla needs no cyclical mechanism. According to this reading, *ein lahem pidyon* for Ulla means only that redemption is unnecessary after cutting — not that it fails during the preceding period.

## Status Identity

### Kedusha Loss

The *gemara* now introduces Rav Hamnuna's challenge to Ulla's position. *Kedusha shebahen lehakan halcha?* — where did the *kedusha* go? How can Ulla countenance a *kedusha* simply vanishing? The challenge is given vivid form: if a man says to a woman "you are my wife today and tomorrow you are not my wife," she does not cease to be married without a *get*. A status that pertains to something's fundamental identity requires an affirmative act to alter it; it does not dissolve by itself.

### Value vs. Essence

Rava responds by drawing a sharp distinction: *mi ka medamit kedushat damim l'kedushat haguf?* — how can you compare sanctity of value to sanctity of essence? *Kedushat damim paka b'chedi* — a *kedusha* rooted in value can cease automatically. *Kedushat haguf lo paka b'chedi* — a *kedusha* rooted in the identity and essence of the thing itself cannot.

### Time-Bound Status

What does this distinction mean in practice? *Kedushat haguf* is exemplified by an animal sanctified as a *korban*: the animal's very identity has changed; it is an *olah*; no clock can be placed on that. *Kedushat damim* is more like a transfer of ownership-interest: one has dedicated the value or proceeds of an object to the Temple, which is closer to a transactional relationship and can, in principle, be time-bound.

The question then arises: into which category do *konamot* fall? Logically, *konamot* are about status, not ownership — the saplings are not owned by the Temple; they simply bear a prohibition. The word *konam* draws on the language of *korban*, which is a status concept rather than a *bedek habayis* concept. Yet because the actual objects — saplings, a *tallit* — will never themselves be offered as *korbanot*, what is really being consecrated is their value or proceeds. It is precisely because these objects can only function in the *kedushat damim* world that Rava can maintain that their status can be time-bound and can cease automatically according to Ulla.

The resolution is thus: Rav Hamnuna's challenge — that *kedusha* cannot simply vanish — applies fully to *kedushat haguf*. Bar Pada accepts this and therefore insists the *konam* status cannot dissolve automatically. Ulla's response, as articulated by Rava, is that *konamot* of this type operate in the domain of *kedushat damim*, and *kedushat damim* can indeed be placed on a clock and can cease without an affirmative act of *pidyon*.

## Ran's Distinction

### Personal vs. Universal

At this point the *Ran*, citing Rabbeinu Yona, raises a fundamental clarification that prevents a misreading of the entire *sugya*. The *gemara*'s discussion — *pidyon*, *meilah*, the impossibility of automatic cessation, *kedushat haguf* versus *kedushat damim* — might seem to apply to all *nedarim*. That would be a mistake. Not every *neder* enters this framework.

The *Ran* draws a distinction between what he calls a *konam prati* — a personal *konam* directed at a specific individual — and a *konam klali* — a universal *konam* that renders the object forbidden to everyone. Only the latter imports the laws of *korbanot* into the analysis.

### Pesach Case

The *Ran* supports this from a *gemara* in the chapter *Ha-Noder Min Ha-Yerek*, which discusses a case where someone says to his wife: "I forbid you from benefiting from me until Pesach if you go to your father's house before Sukkot." The *gemara* there treats this as a straightforward *neder* with a time terminus — before Pesach the prohibition applies, and after Pesach it automatically lapses: *paka issura mimmeila*. No one there raises any concern about *kedusha* dissolving automatically or the need for *pidyon*.

### Korban Application

The explanation is that this case involves a personal *konam* — *konam prati* — directed at one individual. Such a *neder* remains in the ordinary world of *nedarim*: the prohibition is less absolute, the *kedusha* thinner, and it can therefore lapse automatically. Only when one uses the *konam* formula and renders the object forbidden to *everyone* — *assur le-khulei alma* — does the *neder* acquire the weight of a genuine *korban*, attracting the full framework of *meilah* and *pidyon* and the insistence that the status cannot dissolve without affirmative removal. Bar Pada's position, and the entire tension between Bar Pada and Ulla, belongs exclusively to this latter type of *konam*.

## Abaye's Position

### Baraita Challenge

Abaye now presses further, challenging the claim that *kedushat haguf lo paka b'chedi*. A *baraita* is cited: *shor zeh olah kol shloshim yom u-le'achar shloshim yom shelamim* — if one declares that this ox shall be an *olah* for the first 30 days and a *shelamim* after 30 days, the declaration is effective. Within the first 30 days, if one brings it as a *korban*, it is treated as an *olah*; after 30 days, it is treated as a *shelamim*. This is a case of *kedushat haguf* — the very identity of the animal as a specific type of *korban* — and yet that identity changes automatically with the passage of time.

### Radical Extension

Abaye's intent is to extend Ulla's position even further. If even *kedushat haguf* can shift automatically from one form to another, then the entire basis for insisting that *kedusha* cannot dissolve without an act of removal is undermined. Abaye appears prepared to argue that in principle, even a full *kedushat haguf* — an animal genuinely sanctified as a *korban* — could be set on a clock, such that after a defined period it might revert to *chulin* status entirely, with no act of *pidyon* required.

The *Ran* notes that Abaye would still limit this radical position to the world of *kodashim* and *hekdesh*, where everything is effected through speech and the mode of sanctification is fundamentally different from *kiddushin*. No one disputes the *kiddushin* case — no one argues that a woman can cease to be married simply because a time limit was declared. But within the world of *korbanot* and *hekdesh*, Abaye is prepared to say that even *kedushat haguf* can be time-bound.

### Two Cases Logic

Furthermore, Abaye argues from the structure of the *baraita* itself. The *baraita* presents two cases: first, *olah* for 30 days and then *shelamim*; second, *shelamim* now and *olah* after 30 days. If both cases are really about *kedushat damim* — as the *gemara* might wish to argue — why give two cases at all? The second case, moving from *shelamim* to *olah*, is obviously less of a *chiddush* than the first, since it represents an upgrade in sanctity. You would not need two cases to make a point about *kedushat damim* when one would suffice and the second adds nothing. The only reason to give two cases, Abaye argues, is to demonstrate that the principle operates across all scenarios — including *kedushat haguf* — and that even *kedushat haguf* is capable of automatic transformation.

## Baraita Defense

### Value Reading

The *gemara* responds to Abaye's challenge by reinterpreting the *baraita*. *Hacha be-mai askinan de-amar le-damei* — the *baraita* is discussing a case where one has not sanctified the animal as an *olah* or *shelamim* in the full sense of *kedushat haguf*, but rather has declared it for its value: the proceeds of this animal shall go to purchase an *olah* or a *shelamim*. The *rishonim* clarify that this reading requires the animal to have a *mum* — a blemish — because if the animal is unblemished and one says "let it be for an *olah*," it automatically receives *kedushat haguf* since the animal itself could be offered. But a blemished animal cannot itself be brought as a *korban*, and so the declaration necessarily means its monetary value will be used. Under this reading, both cases of the *baraita* involve *kedushat damim*, not *kedushat haguf*, and the *gemara*'s earlier insistence that *kedushat haguf lo paka b'chedi* remains intact.

### Both Directions

Given this reinterpretation, the *gemara* can account for why the *baraita* gives two cases without conceding anything about *kedushat haguf*. Under *kedushat damim*, status can change with time. The *baraita* gives two cases — *olah* to *shelamim* and *shelamim* to *olah* — to demonstrate that this principle operates in both directions.

### Two-Case Necessity

The *gemara* now explains the structural logic. If one wished to argue that the *baraita* deals with *kedushat haguf* in one case and *kedushat damim* in the other, the two cases would be necessary to teach that even *kedushat haguf* can change — lest one think only *kedushat damim* can shift. But if both cases are *kedushat damim*, one might ask: why mention both? The answer is precisely to preempt the inference that you could suffice with one. Had only one case been given — say, *olah* becoming *shelamim* — one might have thought that only *kedushat damim* of a lesser nature (*shelamim* replacing *olah*) can change, but not *kedushat damim* of a more stringent nature. By giving both cases, the *baraita* demonstrates that *kedushat damim* is equally malleable in either direction, and no special inference about *kedushat haguf* need be drawn.

## Bar Pada Explained

### Simple Case Works

The *gemara* now turns to how Bar Pada would explain the first case of the *baraita* — the animal sanctified as *olah* for 30 days and then as *shelamim* afterward. According to Rav Pappa, explaining Bar Pada's position, this case is perfectly intelligible without any automatic status change. If one said *le'achar shloshim yom olah* — after 30 days this animal shall be an *olah* — that declaration takes effect at the 30-day mark. Bar Pada has no difficulty with this: you can sanctify something to take effect in the future, just as a man can say to a woman "be married to me after 30 days" and the *kiddushin* takes effect then. Before day 30, the animal has not yet acquired *olah* status; at day 30, it does. This is a legitimate future-directed sanctification, not an automatic change of an existing status.

---

## Bar Pada Explained (II)

### Added Status Sticks

The problem for Bar Pada arises in the second formulation: one first says *me'achshav shelamim* — from now it is a *shelamim* — and then adds *le'achar shloshim yom olah*. Here the animal has already acquired *shelamim* status from the moment of the declaration. Bar Pada insists: *lo paka bichdi* — that status does not dissolve automatically. Once *shelamim* status is in place, it cannot be replaced by *olah* status simply because a clock runs out. The *shelamim* identity is locked in.

### Marriage Parallel

The parallel to *kiddushin* is instructive. If one says to a woman "be married to me after 30 days," the marriage takes effect at day 30 — a straightforward future-directed *kinyan*. But if one has already established a present status and then attempts to overlay it with a future-directed change, the existing status persists. The same logic governs Bar Pada's reading of the *baraita*: the *seifa* only works — the *olah* after 30 days — if one did not first say *me'achshav shelamim*. Once the *shelamim* status is introduced, it cannot be dislodged by the passage of time alone.

## Amira Lagavoa

### Word Power

The *gemara* now explains why the first case of the *baraita* — sanctifying something for after 30 days — works at all, and introduces the principle of *amira lagavoa k'mesirato lahedyot*: declaring something to belong to *hekdesh* is like physically transferring it to an ordinary person. Normally, if one says to another person "this object is yours," nothing happens — a *kinyan* requires a physical act: lifting, pulling, placing in one's domain. Mere words accomplish nothing. Yet when one says *harei zeh l'hekdesh*, the words alone suffice. The explanation is this principle: speech directed toward Heaven carries the weight of a physical act of transfer.

### No Backtrack

This principle, however, does more than merely dispense with the need for a formal *kinyan*. It generates a *chiddush* regarding the ability to retract. In the context of *kiddushin*, if one says to a woman "be married to me after 30 days" and gives her a ring, there is a dispute among the *rishonim* as to whether she — or he — can retract before the 30 days are up. According to one opinion, retraction is possible during the interim period, because the *kiddushin* has not yet taken effect. The *chiddush* of the *gemara* is that when it comes to *hekdesh*, even the opinion that permits retraction in *kiddushin* would agree that no retraction is possible. The reason: since the relationship with *hekdesh* is mediated entirely by words, and since words directed to *Gavoah* carry the full force of a completed physical transfer, the declaration is equivalent to having said *me'achshav* — from this moment. Once the words are spoken, it is as though the transfer has already occurred, and there is no backing out.

### No Remnant Needed

This principle also generates a second *chiddush*, noted separately by the *gemara*: *afilu nisachlu hamaos* — even if the money used for the sanctification has been consumed or no longer exists, the sanctification takes effect at the appointed time. In *kiddushin*, if a man gives a woman a ring and says "be married to me after 30 days," and the ring is then destroyed before day 30, the *kiddushin* still takes effect — because the woman remains indebted for the value of the ring; the *kinyan* has a residual presence in the form of that debt. But if the *kinyan* were effected by a *shtar* that was subsequently destroyed, the *kiddushin* would not take effect, because the physical instrument of the *kinyan* has no remnant.

### Debt Substitute

When it comes to *hekdesh*, no such remnant is required, because the act of sanctification was never physical to begin with. The "transfer" to *hekdesh* is effected entirely through speech — there is no physical *kinyan* whose remnant must persist. Since the entire relationship is constituted by language and abstraction, it can take effect at the future date without any physical reality needing to survive into that moment. This is why *nishachlu hamaos* — the money being gone — poses no obstacle: there was never a physical dimension to the *kinyan* that needed to remain present.

## Seifa Difficulty

### Reverse Case

The *Ran* observes that Bar Pada's position faces its sharpest challenge from the *seifa* of the *baraita* — the reverse formulation: one first says *me'achshav shelamim* and then adds *le'achar shloshim yom olah*. Here, the animal already bears *shelamim* status from the moment of the declaration. The subsequent specification that it should become an *olah* after 30 days would, on the face of it, seem to represent an attempt to upgrade the sanctity — moving from a lesser *kedusha* to a greater one. One might have thought this should be more achievable: the *shelamim* status is weaker, and the intended transformation elevates the animal, so perhaps there are grounds to allow it. Moreover, one might argue that by specifying the *olah* status in advance — before 30 days have elapsed — one has already "put a stopper" in place, establishing where the status is heading even as the *shelamim* is introduced.

### Status Lock-In

Nevertheless, Bar Pada maintains his position absolutely: *lo paka bichdi*. Once the *shelamim* status is in place — even a weaker *kedusha* — it does not dissolve or transform simply because a clock has been set or because the intended replacement carries greater sanctity. The only scenario in which *le'achar shloshim yom olah* can take effect is the one where no prior status was established — where one said simply *le'achar shloshim yom olah* without the preceding *me'achshav shelamim*. Once any present status is in place, no time-bound declaration can displace it. The *seifa* thus confirms, in Bar Pada's reading, the comprehensive scope of the principle: halachic status, once established, does not change itself.
