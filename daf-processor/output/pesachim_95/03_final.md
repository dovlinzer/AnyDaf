# Pesachim 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pesach Rishon/Sheni

### Tammei/Rechoka Ptur

Before turning to the Gemara proper, it is worth addressing the Rambam's reading of the Mishnah that opened the previous discussion — a reading followed by virtually all the *meforshei hamishna* and which stands, remarkably, against the Gemara's own interpretation.

The Mishnah had asked: what is the difference between *tamei* and *derech rechoka* on one hand, and a case of *shogeg* or *mezid* on the other? Why did the Torah need to specify *tamei* and *derech rechoka* at all? The Mishnah concludes: *she-elu paturim mi-karet ve-elu chayavim be-hikaret* — these are exempt from *karet* and those are liable. The Gemara understood *elu chayavim* as referring to the *mezid*, but this is a strange reading since *mezid* was not the subject of the Mishnah's question.

The Rambam reads the Mishnah according to its plain sense — *peshat* — and his interpretation yields a fundamental conceptual distinction. A person who failed to bring *Pesach Rishon* because he was *tamei* or because he was on a *derech rechoka* falls into a category of *ptur* altogether. For such a person, the obligation of *Pesach Sheni* is an opportunity, not an atonement; even if he ultimately fails to bring *Pesach Sheni*, he is not *chayav karet* — his original exemption stands. By contrast, a person who missed *Pesach Rishon* for any other reason — *shogeg*, *mezid*, or *ones* of a different kind — requires *Pesach Sheni* to discharge his obligation. If he fails to bring *Pesach Sheni*, he is indeed *chayav karet*.

This is a major conceptual distinction in the nature of the *ptur*: for *tamei* and *derech rechoka*, the exemption is categorical; for all others, *Pesach Sheni* is essential for avoiding *karet*.

### Tikkun/Tashlumin

This reading connects, to some degree, to the Gemara's broader discussion of *tikkun* and *tashlumin*, though the Gemara's discussion of those concepts did not present a case in which someone was *mezid* on *Pesach Sheni* itself. Nonetheless, the conceptual relation is clear: where others understand *Pesach Sheni* as a *tashlumin* — a make-up — the Rambam's *chiddush* is that for the categories of *tamei* and *derech rechoka*, the person is in a state of total *ptur*, rendering *Pesach Sheni* fundamentally different in character. Strikingly, on this point all the major *meforshim* — the Bartenura and the others — follow the Rambam against the Gemara's own reading of the Mishnah.

## Cosmology & Mil

### Chazal Science

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַכְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אוֹמְרִים: בַּיּוֹם חַמָּה מְהַלֶּכֶת לְמַטָּה מִן הָרָקִיעַ, וּבַלַּיְלָה לְמַעְלָה מִן הָרָקִיעַ. וְחַכְמֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם אוֹמְרִים: בַּיּוֹם חַמָּה מְהַלֶּכֶת לְמַטָּה מִן הָרָקִיעַ, וּבַלַּיְלָה לְמַטָּה מִן הַקַּרְקַע. אָמַר רַבִּי: וְנִרְאִין דִּבְרֵיהֶן מִדְּבָרֵינוּ, שֶׁבַּיּוֹם מַעֲיָנוֹת צוֹנְנִין, וּבַלַּיְלָה רוֹתְחִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents a dispute: **The Jewish Sages say that during the day the sun travels beneath the firmament and is therefore visible, and at night it travels above the firmament. And the sages of the nations of the world say that during the day the sun travels beneath the firmament, and at night it travels beneath the earth.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said: And the statement of the sages of the nations of the world appears to be more accurate than our statement. A proof to this is that during the day, springs that originate deep in the ground are cold, and during the night they are hot** compared to the air temperature, which supports the theory that these springs are warmed by the sun as it travels beneath the earth.

The Gemara on this *daf* contains a discussion of the *rakia*, the sun's path, and the nature of the cosmological dome — a topic of considerable importance for both historical and *halachic* reasons. The basic picture assumed by Chazal is that the *rakia* is an opaque dome, that the sun travels through its thickness from *alos hashachar* to *hanetz* and again from *shkia* to *tzeis*, which is why there is dim light escaping through the thickness of the dome during those transitional periods. According to Chazal's view, the sun then travels above the dome overnight, while *chachmei umot ha-olam* maintained that it travels below.

The Gemara records the striking statement: *amar Rebbe, nitzchu divreihem mi-divreinu* — Rabbi acknowledged that the position of the non-Jewish sages had prevailed over that of the Jewish sages. This is an important source for the Rambam's broader position that Chazal were not scientists and should not be treated as authorities in scientific matters, such that errors in their science or medicine are not theologically problematic.

There are those who find this threatening to the authority of Chazal and resist the conclusion. Rabbi Akiva Eiger, in his *Gilyon HaShas* on 94b, lower left-hand corner, writes — citing a position in the name of Rabbeinu Tam — that even though *chachmei umot ha-olam* were victorious, *haynu nitzachon beta'anot*: they had better proofs, but the truth still lies with the Jewish sages. As he puts it: *aval ha-emet kachachmei Yisrael* — in the end we are right, proof-shmoof. He supports this from the liturgical formulation *boke'ach chalonei rakia* — the very language of the sun passing through the windows of the *rakia* that we use in prayer shows that Chazal had it right all along. This illustrates the central debate: whether one can accept that Chazal could err in scientific matters.

### Duration Mil

Several important *halachic* conclusions emerge from this Gemara regarding the duration of a *mil*. The Gemara presents effectively three possible readings. One position yields thirty *mil* for twelve hours, or 720 minutes — making a *mil* 24 minutes. Another yields thirty-two *mil*, making a *mil* 22.5 minutes. A third reading understands the Gemara as giving forty *mil* for the twelve hours of the day, making a *mil* 18 minutes. We in practice rule that a *mil* is 18 minutes.

This ruling is highly practically relevant: the 18-minute limit for how long bread may be left before it becomes *chametz* — the time it takes to walk a *mil* — derives from this very Gemara. The matter is disputed: some hold 22.5 minutes and some 24, and indeed the physical distance of a *mil* (approximately one kilometer) and the type of terrain one traverses affect which figure is most accurate, but 18 minutes is the accepted ruling.

### Shkia/Tzeis

The same Gemara states that from *shkia* to *tzeis hakochavim* is four to five *mil*. Working through the arithmetic: if a *mil* is 18 minutes, then four *mil* is 72 minutes and five *mil* is 90 minutes. If a *mil* were 24 minutes, five *mil* would equal 120 minutes. This is the basis for *shitat Rabbeinu Tam*, who takes this Gemara seriously and holds that *Shabbat* is not concluded until 72 minutes after *shkia* — four *mil* at 18 minutes each. Those who are even more *machmir* in both dimensions wait 90 minutes, and some wait 120 minutes, combining Rabbeinu Tam's position with the most stringent reading of the *mil*.

### Cosmology Halacha

Why do we not rule like Rabbeinu Tam in practice? The Gemara in *Shabbat* that addresses the *halachic* question of when *Shabbat* begins contains a direct discussion of *tzeis hakochavim* and speaks of three stars becoming visible — and locates that moment at three-quarters of a *mil* after *shkia*, which at 18 minutes would be approximately 13.5 minutes. The Gra, the Geonim, and others hold that it is that Gemara — being the Gemara explicitly addressing the *halachic* boundary of when the next day begins — that governs. This Gemara, by contrast, is not about *tzeis hakochavim* as a *halachic* matter at all; it is a cosmological discussion about how long it takes the sun to traverse the full thickness of the *rakia*.

The reconciliation offered is elegant: as the sun begins its passage through the thickness of the dome, the stars near the top of the dome begin descending toward visibility. The first three visible stars — the *halachic* *tzeis hakochavim* — appear when only the initial portion of the sun's journey through the thickness has elapsed. By the time the sun has completed its full traversal, all stars, including those that began at the very top, will have descended into view; that full-traversal period corresponds to the four or five *mil* of this Gemara. Thus the 20-to-25-minute practice observed in Israel according to the Gra reflects the *halachic* Gemara in *Shabbat*, while the four-to-five-*mil* figure of our Gemara describes something else entirely.

### Geography Season

Geography and season introduce further complexity. Rabbeinu Tam and the *Baalei HaTosafot*, as well as the Ramban, lived in France and Germany, where twilight extends very late; a 72-minute *tzeis* is plausible — and sometimes even still light — in a northern European summer. The Vilna Gaon, reading the Gemara in *Shabbat* as the controlling source, arrived at a far shorter interval that accords naturally with conditions in Eretz Yisrael, where darkness falls much more quickly.

The question of whether duration from *shkia* to *tzeis* must vary by latitude and season is itself debated. One might also ask whether the cosmological model, which envisions the sun traversing a fixed thickness of the dome, should be thought of as describing a straight-line path or an arcing one, and how the shorter path inside the dome in winter versus the longer path in summer would affect the timing. These questions remain open within the discussion of the Gemara's cosmological framework, and no definitive resolution is offered.

## Mishnah Differences

### Chometz Chametz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָה בֵּין פֶּסַח הָרִאשׁוֹן לַשֵּׁנִי? הָרִאשׁוֹן אָסוּר בְּ״בַל יֵרָאֶה״ וּ״בַל יִמָּצֵא״, וְהַשֵּׁנִי — חָמֵץ וּמַצָּה עִמּוֹ בַּבַּיִת. הָרִאשׁוֹן טָעוּן הַלֵּל בַּאֲכִילָתוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי אֵינוֹ טָעוּן הַלֵּל בַּאֲכִילָתוֹ. זֶה וָזֶה טָעוּן הַלֵּל בַּעֲשִׂיָּיתָן, וְנֶאֱכָלִין צָלִי עַל מַצָּה וּמְרוֹרִים, וְדוֹחִין אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **What is the difference between the Paschal lamb offered on the first *Pesaḥ* and the Paschal lamb offered on the second *Pesaḥ*? On the first *Pesaḥ*, it is prohibited to own leavened bread due to the prohibitions: It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found. And on the second *Pesaḥ* it is permissible for one to have both leavened bread and *matza* with him in the house.** Another difference is that the Paschal lamb offered on **the first *Pesaḥ* requires the recitation of *hallel* as it is eaten and the second does not require the recitation of *hallel* as it is eaten. However, they are the same in that the Paschal lambs sacrificed on both the first and second *Pesaḥ* require the recitation of *hallel* as they are prepared, i.e., as they are slaughtered, and they are both eaten roasted with *matza* and bitter herbs, and they override Shabbat** in that they may be slaughtered and their blood sprinkled even on Shabbat.

The Mishnah now presents the differences between *Pesach Rishon* and *Pesach Sheni* in their actual performance.

*HaRishon assur be-val yeira'eh u-val yimatzei* — the first *Pesach* carries with it the full prohibition of owning *chametz*, connected as it is to *Chag HaMatzos*. There is a prohibition of *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi*, and the day before the *Pesach* the obligation of *tashbitu* already begins. *Ve-hasheini chametz u-matzah imo ba-bayit* — by *Pesach Sheni*, one may have *chametz* in the house alongside it. Indeed, it is intriguing whether one could theoretically eat bread alongside the *Pesach*, given that one is still required to eat it *al matzot u-merorim*. The *matzah* requirement is explicit; whether that itself precludes also having bread present is a question worth noting.

### Hallel Eating

*HaRishon taun Hallel be-achilaso* — the first *Pesach* requires recitation of *Hallel* at the time of eating it; *ve-hasheini ein taun Hallel be-achilaso* — the second does not. Both, however — *zeh ve-zeh te'unim Hallel be-asiyasan* — require *Hallel* at the time of the *korban*'s offering, when it is brought.

### Override Shabbat

*Ve-dochin et ha-Shabbat* — both *Pesach Rishon* and *Pesach Sheni* override *Shabbat*. Since each carries a fixed time obligation — *be-mo'ado* — and overriding *Shabbat* is rooted not specifically in its being a *korban tzibbur* but in the principle of *chiyuv she-kavuah lah zeman*, both are *docheh Shabbat*. The question of *docheh tumah*, however, applies only to *Pesach Rishon*, as will be seen. This is the position of the Mishnah, which the Gemara will note is not in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda.

## Kekhol Chukat

### Gufo Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כְּכׇל חוּקַּת הַפֶּסַח יַעֲשׂוּ אוֹתוֹ״ — בְּמִצְוָה שֶׁבְּגוּפוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. מִצְוָה שֶׁעַל גּוּפוֹ, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרוֹרִים יֹאכְלוּהוּ״. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ מִצְווֹת שֶׁלֹּא עַל גּוּפוֹ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעֶצֶם לֹא יִשְׁבְּרוּ בוֹ״, מָה שְׁבִירַת הָעֶצֶם מְיוּחָד מִצְוָה שֶׁבְּגוּפוֹ — אַף כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁבְּגוּפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught a halakhic midrash pertaining to the Paschal lamb offered on the second *Pesaḥ*. The verse states: "They shall leave none of it to the morning, nor break a bone in it; according to all the entire statute of the Paschal lamb they shall offer it"** (Numbers 9:12). The fact that the verse says "it" indicates that **the verse is speaking of a mitzva applicable to the body of the Paschal lamb, meaning that *halakhot* pertaining to the actual Paschal lamb on the first *Pesaḥ* apply equally to the Paschal lamb on the second *Pesaḥ*.** The midrash continues: **With regard to a mitzva related to the body of the Paschal lamb but not actually performed on the body of the offering, from where is it derived that it applies to the second *Pesaḥ* as well? The verse states: "They shall eat it with *matzot* and bitter herbs"** (Numbers 9:11). One **might have thought that one must fulfill all mitzvot related to the first *Pesaḥ* on the second *Pesaḥ*, even mitzvot not at all related to the body of the Paschal lamb, such as the requirement to destroy all one's leaven. Therefore, the Torah states: "And they shall not break a bone in it"** (Numbers 9:12), which teaches that **just as the prohibition of breaking a bone is notable among the mitzvot related to the Paschal lamb in that it is a mitzva applicable to the Paschal lamb itself, so too, any mitzva applicable to the Paschal lamb itself must be fulfilled on the second *Pesaḥ*. However, other mitzvot pertaining to the first *Pesaḥ* need not be fulfilled on the second *Pesaḥ*.

The Gemara now turns to the underlying derivation of which laws of *Pesach Rishon* apply to *Pesach Sheni*. The verse says: *ke-chol chukat ha-Pesach ya'asu oto* — the verse about *Pesach Sheni* states that one should observe it according to all the statutes of the *Pesach*. The Gemara reads this through a Baraita: *be-mitzvot she-be-gufo ha-katuv medaber* — the verse is speaking about obligations pertaining to the body of the *Pesach* itself, such as *tzli eish*, roasting over fire. 

What then of obligations that accompany the *Pesach* but are not literally the body of the *korban*? For these the verse says *al matzot u-merorim yochluhu* — they are included because they are *al gufo*, directly associated with the body of the *Pesach*. But might one go further and include things entirely extraneous to the body, like *bal yeira'eh u-val yimatzei*? For this the verse repeats by *Pesach Sheni*: *v'etzem lo yishberu vo*. The repetition of the bone-breaking prohibition, applied to *Pesach Sheni*, is not merely restatement; it serves to anchor the analogy. Just as *v'etzem lo yishberu vo* concerns the body of the *Pesach* itself, so all the applied laws must concern the body of the *Pesach* or what is directly *al gufo* — excluding things *chutz me-gufo*, however conceptually linked to the *Pesach* they may be. Even *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi*, though it concerns the *shechitat ha-Pesach*, is considered *chutz me-gufo* because it is not something done with the meat itself; one is simply *shochting* the animal while happening to own *chametz*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״יַעֲשׂוּ אוֹתוֹ״, בְּמִצְווֹת שֶׁבְּגוּפוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אָמַר מָר: יָכוֹל אַף מִצְוָה שֶׁלֹּא עַל גּוּפוֹ. הָא אָמְרַתְּ בְּמִצְוָה שֶׁבְּגוּפוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ ״עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרוֹרִים יֹאכְלוּהוּ״, אַלְמָא ״יַעֲשׂוּ אוֹתוֹ״ לָאו דַּוְקָא הוּא. אֵימָא הָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּפְרָט וּכְלָל, וְנַעֲשָׂה כְּלָל מוֹסִיף עַל הַפְּרָט, וַאֲפִילּוּ כֹּל מִילֵּי נָמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Isi ben Yehuda says: It is unnecessary to derive that *halakha* from the end of the verse quoted, as the phrase in the first half of the verse: "They shall perform it," indicates that the verse is speaking only of mitzvot applicable to the body of the Paschal lamb itself.** The Gemara clarifies the details of the *baraita*. **The Master said:** One **might have thought that one must fulfill even a mitzva not at all related to the body of the Paschal lamb on the second *Pesaḥ*. The Gemara expresses surprise: But you said that the verse is speaking only of a mitzva applicable to the body of the Paschal lamb, so why would one think that unrelated mitzvot are also included?** The Gemara explains that **this is what the *baraita* is saying: Now that you said the additional *halakha* that they shall eat it with unleavened bread and bitter herbs, apparently the phrase: "They shall perform it," is not specific and does not limit the *halakhot* of the second *Pesaḥ* to those applicable to the Paschal lamb itself,** as it first said: "They shall eat it with unleavened bread and bitter herbs" and "They shall leave none of it to the morning," and then it stated a generalization: "According to all the statute of the Paschal lamb they shall perform it." The principles of halakhic midrash state that in that case, **the generalization adds to the detail and even includes everything, such that all the mitzvot of the first *Pesaḥ* would apply equally to the second, including the removal of leaven. Therefore, the verse "They shall not break a bone in it" teaches us that mitzvot unrelated to the Paschal lamb do not apply to the second *Pesaḥ*.

The Gemara then draws an explicit tripartite classification. The three additional verses cited in connection with *Pesach Sheni* correspond neatly to three categories of obligation: *al matzot u-merorim* is a positive *mitzvat asei*; *lo yash'iru mimenu ad boker* — the prohibition of *notar* — is a *lo ta'asei she-nitak la-asei*, paired with the positive obligation of *ve-hanotar mimenu ba-esh tisrofu*; and *v'etzem lo yishberu vo* is a pure *lo ta'asei gamur*. The principle that emerges is: any obligation in any of these three categories applies to *Pesach Sheni* if it is *be-gufo*, and is excluded if it is *chutz me-gufo*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה, הַאי ״עֶצֶם״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְאֶחָד עֶצֶם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מוֹחַ, וְאֶחָד עֶצֶם שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מוֹחַ. וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״יַעֲשׂוּ אֹתוֹ״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ שֶׁאֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַל הַיָּחִיד, דְּכַמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי מַהְדְּרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What does Isi ben Yehuda do with the end of the verse: "And they shall not break a bone in it"? He needs it to teach that the prohibition of breaking a bone applies both to a bone that has marrow and to a bone that does not have marrow. And the Rabbis, what do they do with this phrase: "They shall perform it"? They need it to teach that one does not slaughter the Paschal lamb on the second *Pesaḥ* on behalf of a single individual. Since the verse speaks of people performing the second *Pesaḥ* in the plural, it is derived that as much as it is possible to search for more people to join this individual in his Paschal lamb, we search for them, even if it means causing another individual to become ritually impure to prevent him from performing the first *Pesaḥ*.

The Gemara then asks: what is included by the *klal* of *al matzot u-merorim*? The answer is *tzli eish*, roasting — another *mitzvat asei* that is directly about the body of the *Pesach*. What *mitzvat asei* is excluded? *Tashbitu* — the positive obligation to destroy *se'or* — since it is not *al gufo*.

### Isi ben Yehuda

*Isi ben Yehuda* does not dispute the *halacha* but the derivation. He holds that *ya'asu oto* — the word *oto*, meaning "it" — already suffices to limit the laws to those directly concerning the body of the *Pesach*. The word *oto* is *davka*; it never needed to be expanded in the first place. He therefore has no need for the verse of *v'etzem lo yishberu bo* to perform a limiting function.

This raises the Gemara's counter-question: if Isi holds *oto* was already limiting, and the Rabbis required *v'etzem lo yishberu bo* to limit it back — what forced the Rabbis to think that *oto* was *lav davka* in the first place? The answer is that once the verse explicitly included *matzah* and *maror*, which are not the body of the *Pesach* but are *al gufo*, one might have inferred that *oto* was meant broadly — opening the possibility of including even *bal yeira'eh u-val yimatzei*. Once that inference is entertained, the entire structure becomes a *klal u-prat*, and the verse of *v'etzem lo yishberu bo* is needed to close it back down.

Conversely, having concluded that *v'etzem lo yishberu bo* limits the scope, what do the Rabbis do with *ya'asu oto*? They derive from it: *she-ein shochtin et ha-Pesach al ha-yachid* — ideally one should not bring *Pesach* alone, for an individual, but should seek a *chaburah*. The word *ya'asu* is plural while *oto* is singular — implying that multiple people should be joining together for a single *Pesach*. As the Gemara puts it: *de-kama de-efshar l'hiddurei mahadrinan* — as much as possible, one should seek to do it with a group.

This is admittedly not an absolute prohibition for *Pesach Sheni*. Unlike *Pesach Rishon*, where all of *Klal Yisrael* is bringing together, *Pesach Sheni* by its very nature is for the stragglers and the individuals who were unable to participate. Nevertheless the aspiration encoded in *ya'asu oto* is to recreate the communal character of *Pesach Rishon* as much as possible. The cry of *lamah nigara levilti hakriv et korban Hashem be-mo'ado be-toch bnei Yisrael* — the complaint of those who were left out — is precisely about belonging to the national act, and the ideal of *chaburah* at *Pesach Sheni* recovers something of that sense of communal participation.

Rashi goes so far as to say that *le-hiddurei* — the seeking after a *chaburah* — means that if one would otherwise be the only person bringing *Pesach Sheni*, we would actually render someone else *tamei* deliberately so that there would be a *chaburah* partner.

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כְּכׇל חוּקַּת הַפֶּסַח יַעֲשׂוּ אוֹתוֹ״. יָכוֹל כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהָרִאשׁוֹן אָסוּר בְּ״בַל יֵרָאֶה״ וּ״בַל יִמָּצֵא״ כָּךְ שֵׁנִי אָסוּר בְּ״בַל יֵרָאֶה״ וּ״בַל יִמָּצֵא״ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל מַצּוֹת וּמְרוֹרִים יֹאכְלוּהוּ״. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה, מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יַשְׁאִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר״. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁנִּיתָּק לַעֲשֵׂה. מִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה גָּמוּר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעֶצֶם לֹא יִשְׁבְּרוּ בוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught in a different *baraita*: The verse states: "According to the entire statute of the Paschal lamb they shall offer it"** (Numbers 9:12). **One might have thought that just as at the time of the sacrifice of the Paschal lamb on the first *Pesaḥ* it is prohibited to own leaven due to the prohibitions of: It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found, so too, at the time of the sacrifice of the Paschal lamb on the second *Pesaḥ* it is prohibited to own leaven due to the prohibitions of: It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found. Therefore, the Torah states: "They shall eat it with *matzot* and bitter herbs"** (Numbers 9:11), which indicates that the other mitzvot pertaining to the first *Pesaḥ* do not apply on the second. **And from here I have derived only that positive mitzvot related to the first *Pesaḥ* apply on the second *Pesaḥ*; from where do I derive that the same is true of negative mitzvot? The verse states: "They shall leave none of it to the morning, nor break a bone in it"** (Numbers 9:12). **And from this verse I have derived only that a negative mitzva whose violation can be rectified by the fulfillment of a positive mitzva applies on the second *Pesaḥ*, e.g., the prohibition of leaving over meat from the Paschal lamb until morning, which can be rectified by the positive mitzva of burning the leftovers; from where is it derived that the same is true of a full-fledged negative mitzva? The verse states: "They shall not break a bone in it."**

Returning to the tripartite structure, the Gemara enumerates the categories and their examples in detail. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּכְלָלֵיהּ דְּ״מַצּוֹת וּמְרוֹרִים״, מַאי קָא מְרַבֵּי? צְלִי אֵשׁ. בִּפְרָטֵיהּ מַאי מְמַעֵיט לֵיהּ? הַשְׁבָּתַת שְׂאוֹר. אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא! מִצְוָה דְגוּפֵיהּ עֲדִיף. בִּכְלָלֵיהּ דְּ״לֹא יַשְׁאִירוּ מִמֶּנּוּ עַד בֹּקֶר״ מַאי קָא מְרַבֵּה לֵיהּ? ״לֹא תוֹצִיא מִמֶּנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Now that the generalization has been interpreted as referring to the specific examples mentioned earlier, **what is included through the generalization of unleavened bread and bitter herbs? The Gemara answers: It comes to teach that the mitzva of roasting in fire applies to the Paschal lamb on the second *Pesaḥ* as well as to the first. Given that unleavened bread and bitter herbs is also a qualifying detail, what does it exclude through its detail? It teaches that the mitzva of removal of leaven does not apply on the second *Pesaḥ*.** The Gemara asks: Perhaps **I can reverse it** and say that on the second *Pesaḥ* one is not obligated to roast the offering by fire, but one is obligated to remove all leaven? The Gemara answers: **A mitzva relating to the Paschal lamb itself is preferable.** **What is included through the generalization of: They shall leave none of it to the morning? It comes to teach the prohibition of: You shall not remove any of its meat from one group to another,** which is similar to it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּפְרָטֵיהּ מַאי קָא מְמַעֲטִי? ״לֹא יֵרָאֶה וְלֹא יִמָּצֵא״. אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא! מִצְוָה דְגוּפֵיהּ עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **What does it exclude through its detail? It excludes the prohibitions: It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found.** The Gemara asks: Perhaps **I can reverse it** and say that the generalization is meant to include removal of leaven and the detail excludes the prohibition of leaving over the meat of the offering? The Gemara answers: Including **a mitzva related to the Paschal lamb itself is preferable** to including one that does not relate to the Paschal lamb.

It is significant that the prohibition of *lo totzi mimenu* applies to *Pesach Sheni*. This confirms that the laws of *chaburah* — remaining within the *bayit* associated with one's *korban* — apply to *Pesach Sheni* as well. What exactly *nitak la-asei* means in the case of *lo totzi* is not fully resolved: Rashi notes that unlike other cases discussed in the Talmud, this Gemara assumes one cannot remedy the violation by bringing the meat back inside, since the meat becomes *pasul* upon being removed.

### Lo Totzi/Chaburah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּכְלָלֵיהּ דְּ״עֶצֶם לֹא יִשְׁבְּרוּ בוֹ״ מַאי קָא מְרַבֵּה? ״אַל תֹּאכְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ נָא״. בִּפְרָטֵיהּ מַאי קָא מְמַעֲטִי? ״לֹא תִשְׁחַט עַל חָמֵץ דַּם זִבְחִי״. אֵיפוֹךְ אֲנָא! מִצְוָה דְגוּפֵיהּ עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Through the generalization: They shall not break a bone in it, what is it including?** The Gemara answers that it includes the mitzva: **"Do not eat from it raw"** (Exodus 12:9). The Gemara asks: **What does it exclude through its detail? The Gemara answers that it excludes the mitzva: "You shall not offer the blood of My sacrifice with leavened bread"** (Exodus 34:25). The Gemara asks: Perhaps **I can reverse it** and say the opposite, that the prohibition against eating the offering raw is excluded, and the prohibition against owning leaven is included. The Gemara answers: Including **a mitzva related to the Paschal lamb itself is preferable** to including one that does not relate as directly to the Paschal lamb.

What is excluded as a simple *lo ta'asei gamur be-gufo*? *Al tochlu mimenu na* — the prohibition against eating the *Pesach* raw. That is a *lo ta'asei* directly about the body. And what simple *lo ta'asei* is excluded as *chutz legufa*? *Lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi* — the prohibition against slaughtering the *Pesach* while owning *chametz*. This is the most striking exclusion. One might have thought that since the prohibition pertains to the very act of *shechitas ha-Pesach*, it should be treated as *be-gufo*. But the Gemara holds that it is nonetheless *chutz legufa* — it is not something one is doing *with the meat* but rather a condition of ownership that exists at the time of *shechita*. This distinction between acting upon the meat and circumstances accompanying the *shechita* marks the outer boundary of what counts as *mitzvah she-be-gufo*.

## Hallel Question

### Hallel Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרִאשׁוֹן טָעוּן הַלֵּל בַּאֲכִילָתוֹ וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: אָמַר קְרָא ״הַשִּׁיר יִהְיֶה לָכֶם כְּלֵיל הִתְקַדֶּשׁ חָג״. לַיְלָה הַמְקוּדָּשׁ לֶחָג — טָעוּן הַלֵּל, לַיְלָה שֶׁאֵין מְקוּדָּשׁ לֶחָג — אֵין טָעוּן הַלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that the Paschal lamb on **the first *Pesaḥ* requires the recitation of *hallel* as it is eaten.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters derived? Rabbi Yoḥanan said, citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak,** that **the verse states: "You shall have a song as in the night when a Festival is sanctified"** (Isaiah 30:29). From here it may be derived that **a night sanctified as a Festival, on which labor is prohibited, such as the first night of Passover, requires the recitation of *hallel*; however, a night which is not sanctified as a Festival, such as the night when the Paschal lamb is eaten following the second *Pesaḥ*, does not require the recitation of *hallel*.**

The Mishnah having established that *Pesach Rishon* requires *Hallel be-achilaso* while *Pesach Sheni* does not, the Gemara asks: where is this derived? Rav Yochanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: the verse says *hashir yihyeh lachem ke-leil hiskadesh chag* — a song will be for you like the night consecrated with the festival. The night that is consecrated with a *chag* — the night of *Chag HaMatzos* — is *ta'un Hallel*. A night not consecrated with a *chag* — the night of *Pesach Sheni* — is not *ta'un Hallel be-achilaso*. As for *Hallel be-asiyasan*, both require it when bringing the *korban*, since the verse's exclusion is only of the *lailah* and not of the bringing during the day.

### Maggid Omission

One might ask: the Mishnah discusses *Hallel be-achilaso* and *Hallel be-asiyatan*, but it says nothing about *Maggid* — the recitation of *sippur yetziat Mitzrayim*. This is a striking omission. One might have thought that just as *matzah* and the *Pesach* itself require contextual explanation and *Maggid* gives meaning to *achilas ha-Pesach* — so much so that the Gemara elsewhere says that if *matzah bi-zman ha-zeh* is *de-rabbanan*, then *Maggid* is *de-rabbanan* — perhaps *Maggid* should apply to *Pesach Sheni* as well.

The assumption that *Maggid* is excluded from *Pesach Sheni* follows from the same logic as the exclusion of *Hallel be-achilato*: *Maggid*, like *Hallel*, is connected to *Chag HaMatzos* and not specifically to *achilas ha-Pesach*. The verse *ke-leil hiskadesh chag* ties both *Hallel* and implicitly *Maggid* to the sanctity of the *chag* itself. Just as a *sukka* requires no parallel obligation of *Maggid* despite the verse *le-ma'an yeid'u doroteichem*, so too *Maggid* retains independent status linked to *Chag HaMatzos* rather than to the eating of the *korban*.

That said, it is not entirely *pashut*. One could argue that *Maggid* is integral to how one eats the *Pesach* — that without articulating the symbolism of the *Pesach*, *matzah*, and *maror*, one has not fully performed *achilas ha-Pesach* itself. The statement *kol she-lo amar shlosha devarim eilu be-Pesach lo yatza yedei chovato* raises exactly this question: does failing to say the three things mean one has not fulfilled *Maggid*, or not fulfilled *Pesach*, or both? The Mishnah's silence here is thus significant — and it suggests that even in the Talmudic understanding, *Maggid* is more deeply tied to *Chag HaMatzos* than to *achilas ha-Pesach* as such.

### Philo Jubilees

Relevant here is pre-Mishnaic and extra-Mishnaic evidence. In the book of Jubilees — whose composition predates the Mishnah — the story of the first *Pesach* is told with an account of the night of *achilas ha-Pesach*: "All Israel was eating the flesh of the paschal lamb and drinking the wine and was lauding and blessing and giving thanks to the Lord the God of their fathers." Song and praise accompany the eating of the *Pesach*; there is no mention of *Maggid*.

Similarly, in Philo's account of the *Pesach* — also earlier than the Mishnah — he describes the *Pesach* gathering as unique among feasts: "Those who are to share in the feast come together not as they do to other entertainments to gratify their bellies with wine and meat, but to fulfill their hereditary custom with prayer and songs of praise." Again: *shira*, no *Maggid*.

This pattern of evidence is significant. The Mishnah's focus on *Hallel be-achilato* — rather than on *Maggid* — is not an accident. In an earlier stratum of practice, song and praise with the eating of the *korban Pesach* was the central performative act of the night. *Maggid* may represent a later, more explicitly expository development tied to the *chag* framework, while *Hallel* as sung praise is the more ancient accompaniment to the *Pesach* itself. The observation that the Mishnah is discussing *Hallel* but not *Maggid*, and that the external sources similarly know *shira* but not *Maggid*, is therefore meaningful in tracing the history of the *seder* night.

### Shechirah Hallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה וָזֶה טְעוּנִין הַלֵּל בַּעֲשִׂיָּיתָן כּוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לַיְלָה קָא מְמַעֵט, יוֹם לָא קָא מְמַעֵט. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אֶפְשָׁר יִשְׂרָאֵל שׁוֹחֲטִין אֶת פִּסְחֵיהֶן וְנוֹטְלִין אֶת לוּלְבֵיהֶן, וְאֵין אוֹמְרִים הַלֵּל?!
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna that the Paschal lambs sacrificed on **both the first and second *Pesaḥ* require the recitation of *hallel* as they are prepared.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason that *hallel* must be recited while one prepares the Paschal lamb on the second *Pesaḥ*? If you wish, say** that the verse quoted above: "As in the night when a festival is sanctified," **excludes** laws that apply at **night, but it does not exclude** laws that apply by **day**; therefore, the recitation of *hallel* is required while slaughtering the Paschal lamb on the second *Pesaḥ* just as it is required while slaughtering the Paschal lamb on the first *Pesaḥ*. **And if you wish, say** that this *halakha* simply makes logical sense: **Is it possible that the Jewish people are slaughtering their Paschal lambs or taking their *lulavim* on *Sukkot* and not reciting *hallel*?** It is inconceivable that they would not be reciting *hallel* and there is no need for an explicit biblical source for this *halakha*.

The Gemara then turns to explain why *Hallel be-asiyatan* applies to both *Pesachim*. It offers two approaches. The first: *laila ka-mi-mai, yom lo ka-mi-mai* — the verse *ke-leil hiskadesh chag* excluded only the *Hallel* of the night; the *Hallel* of the day, accompanying the *shechita*, was never excluded.

The second approach is striking: *efshar Yisrael shochtin et pisecheihen ve-notlin lulaveihen ve-ein omrin Hallel?* Could it be that Israel is slaughtering their *pesachim* and taking their *lulavim* and not saying *Hallel*? This is virtually a rhetorical self-evidence argument — a form of reasoning from what is inconceivable. The Ramban derives from this that *Hallel* in the context of performing such *mitzvot* is *de-oraita*, at least in principle, though the precise texts of *Hallel* as we know them were composed by David, raising the same question that arises regarding *tefillah*.

A deeper question remains, however, and is acknowledged without a full resolution: why should *Hallel be-asiyah* — at the *shechita* — be more self-evident than *Hallel be-achilah*? Ordinarily, the *Hallel* of the *korban* in the Temple was associated with the *Levi'im*, whereas what is being described here is a *Hallel* connected to the *mitzvot* of *chag* and *simcha* by the *Yisraelim* themselves. If the argument is that one cannot possibly bring the *Pesach* without *Hallel*, why does that logic not apply equally at the moment of *achilas ha-Pesach*? The verse *ke-leil hiskadesh chag* provides the answer for the night, but the intuition that the *shechita* demands *Hallel* while the *achilah* does not — though derivable from the verse — is not intuitively obvious. This question deserves further investigation.

## Lina Requirement

### Tosefta Lina

The question of whether *Pesach Sheni* requires *lina* — an overnight stay in Jerusalem — is disputed among *Tannaim*. The Tosefta's discussion on this point includes a vivid depiction of what the absence of a *lina* requirement would look like in practice. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *Pesach Sheni* does not require *lina*, deriving this from the juxtaposition of the verse *u-fanita va-voker ve-halachta le-ohalecha* — you may turn in the morning and go to your tents — with *sheishet yamim tochel matzot*: the *lina* requirement applies only to what is eaten across the six days of *Chag HaMatzos*, and since *Pesach Sheni* carries no such *chag* extension, no *lina* is required.

The Tosefta renders this vividly: one who brings *Pesach Sheni* may enter Jerusalem, slaughter the *Pesach* in the *Azarah*, leave, and even get a haircut at Beis Pagi — the district just outside the walls — the same night. If someone has pressing obligations, such as a death in the family requiring him to become *tamei*, he need not remain. Bring the *Pesach*, and then attend to what must be attended to. The image of someone departing Jerusalem immediately after the *shechita* — eating the *korban* in Jerusalem and then leaving that same night — underscores how differently *Pesach Sheni* is structured from *Pesach Rishon*. Note that even absent the *lina* requirement, the *korban* itself must still be eaten within Jerusalem; one may not take it *chutz li-Yerushalayim*.

## Pesach Tumah

### Eating Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפֶּסַח שֶׁבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה, לֹא יֹאכְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ זָבִין וְזָבוֹת נִדּוֹת וְיוֹלְדוֹת. וְאִם אָכְלוּ — פְּטוּרִין מִכָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: When **the Paschal lamb is sacrificed in a state of ritual impurity due to the fact that the majority of the Jewish people are ritually impure, *zavim*, and *zavot*, and menstruating women, and women after childbirth may not eat it,** because the Paschal lamb overrides only ritual impurity imparted by a corpse, but it does not override other forms of ritual impurity. However, **if they violated the *halakha* and ate from the offering, they are exempt from *karet*.** One who eats sacrificial food in a state of ritual impurity is generally liable to receive *karet*; however, since in this case the offering is sacrificed in a state of ritual impurity, there is no punishment of *karet* even for ritually impure individuals who are not permitted to eat it.

A new discussion opens concerning the *Pesach she-ba be-tumah* — a *Pesach Rishon* brought in a state of communal *tumah*, when the majority of Israel is *tamei* and the *korban* is therefore offered in *tumah*. Even in such circumstances, certain categories of *tamei* individuals are excluded from eating it: *lo yochlu mimenu zavim ve-zavot niddot ve-yoldot*. Individuals whose *tumah* emanates from within their bodies — *zavim*, *zavot*, *niddot*, and *yoldot* — may not eat from the *Pesach*, even though it is being brought *be-tumah*. Their status is of a more personal and severe character of *tumah*, one that is not overridden by the communal dispensation that permits *tumah yotzei mi-chutz* — external *tumah* from a *meit* or *sheretz* — to trigger the *Pesach be-tumah* arrangement.

The practical consequence: they may not eat, but *hem pturim min ha-karet* — they are nonetheless exempt from *karet* for failing to eat.

### Eliezer Azarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר פּוֹטֵר אַף עַל בִּיאַת מִקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Eliezer exempts these individuals from *karet* even for entering the Temple** in a state of ritual impurity, despite their not being permitted to enter, because people who are impure due to ritual impurity imparted by a corpse are permitted to enter the Temple in this situation despite their impurity.

Rabbi Eliezer adds a further ruling: not only is there no *karet* for failing to eat, but even if *zavim* and *metzora'im* were to press into the *Azarah* during the time the *Pesach* was being brought *be-tumah*, Rabbi Eliezer holds they would be *patur* from *karet* for entering.

### Pesukim Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: זָבִין וְזָבוֹת נִדּוֹת וְיוֹלְדוֹת שֶׁאָכְלוּ בְּפֶסַח שֶׁבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה, יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל טָהוֹר יֹאכַל בָּשָׂר. וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל בָּשָׂר מִזֶּבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים אֲשֶׁר לַה׳ וְטוּמְאָתוֹ עָלָיו וְנִכְרְתָה״. נֶאֱכַל לִטְהוֹרִים — חַיָּיבִים עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טָמֵא, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל לִטְהוֹרִין — אֵין טְמֵאִים חַיָּיבִים עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught in a *baraita*: With regard to *zavim*, and *zavot*, and menstruating women, and women after childbirth who ate of the Paschal lamb that was sacrificed while the majority of the Jewish people were in a state of ritual impurity, one might have thought that they would be liable to receive *karet*; therefore, the verse states:** "The meat that touches any impure thing shall not be eaten, it shall be burned in fire; **and the meat, every one that is ritually pure may eat the meat. But the soul that eats of the meat of the sacrifice of peace-offerings, that belong to the Lord, having his impurity upon him, that soul shall be cut off [*venikhreta*] from his people"** (Leviticus 7:19–20). **The *baraita* continues: The juxtaposition of these verses teaches that if the offering is eaten only by people who are ritually pure, ritually impure people are liable for eating it due to being ritually impure, but if it is not eaten only by people who are ritually pure, because it was offered when the majority of the Jewish people were impure, those who are ritually impure are not liable for eating it due to being impure.**

The derivation from the verses: the Torah states *kol tahor yokhal basar* — all who are *tahor* may eat meat — and then, *ve-ha-nefesh asher tochhal basar mi-zevach ha-shelamim asher la-Hashem ve-tum'ato alav ve-nikhretah* — the person who eats *korban* meat while *tamei* is *chayav karet*. The Gemara reads these verses as connected: the prohibition generating *karet* applies to meat that is *ne'ekhal le-tahorim* — restricted to *tahor* people. When the *korban* is brought *be-tumah* and is not restricted to *tahor* people, it is *she-eino ne'ekhal le-tahorim*, and in that circumstance *ein tme'im chayavin alav mishum tamei* — the *tamei* person is not *chayav karet* for eating it. The *cheftza* of the *korban* has changed in status; the *karet* applies only to eating meat that belongs categorically to the *tahorim*.

For Rabbi Eliezer's ruling regarding *bi'at ha-mikdash*: the verse *ve-yishalchu min ha-machaneh kol tzarua ve-khol zav ve-khol tamei la-nefesh* — send out from the camp the *metzora*, the *zav*, and the *tamei meit* — is read as establishing the rule by parallel: when *tamei meit* are expelled from the camp, that is also when *zavim* and *metzora'im* are expelled. And conversely, when *tamei meit* are not expelled — when the *Pesach be-tumah* allows them in — *zavim* and *metzora'im* are also not expelled, and therefore cannot be held *chayav karet* for entering.

### Yosef Heikhal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: דָּחֲקוּ טְמֵאֵי מֵתִים וְנִכְנְסוּ לַהֵיכָל בְּפֶסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה, מַהוּ? מִדְּאִישְׁתְּרַי טוּמְאַת עֲזָרָה אִישְׁתְּרַי נָמֵי טוּמְאַת הֵיכָל, אוֹ דִילְמָא מַאי דְּאִישְׁתְּרַי — אִישְׁתְּרַי, מַאי דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּרַי — לָא אִישְׁתְּרַי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef asked a question related to the *halakhot* discussed above: If those who are impure due to ritual impurity imparted by a corpse pushed their way in and entered the Sanctuary, which is an area in which no services are performed for the Paschal lamb and which only priests may enter *ab initio*, and this occurs in a case in which the Paschal lamb is brought when the majority of the Jewish people are in a state of ritual impurity, what is the *halakha*? Is the *halakha* that since ritual impurity was permitted in the courtyard for the sake of the Paschal lamb, ritual impurity in the Sanctuary was also permitted; or perhaps what was permitted was permitted and what was not permitted was not permitted, and consequently they are liable for entering the Sanctuary?**

Rabbi Yosef then raises the question: if *zavim* and *metzora'im* who press into the *Azarah* during a *Pesach be-tumah* are *patur* according to Rabbi Eliezer — what if such individuals pressed further, entering the *Heikhal*? Is the dispensation coextensive? *Mid'ishtaray tumat azarah, ishtaray nami tumat heikhal?* Once we have said the *Azarah* entry does not generate *karet* in this circumstance, does the same follow for the *Heikhal*? Or is the dispensation strictly limited to what was explicitly permitted — *ma d'ishtaray ishtaray, ma d'lo ishtaray lo ishtaray* — the *Azarah* was opened but the *Heikhal* was not?

### Rava Heikhal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה״, אֲפִילּוּ מִקְצָת מַחֲנֶה. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה ... אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה תְּשַׁלְּחוּם״, כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּקָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״אֶל מִחוּץ לַמַּחֲנֶה תְּשַׁלְּחוּם״ — קָרֵינַן בֵּיהּ ״וִישַׁלְּחוּ מִן הַמַּחֲנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said in answer to this question that the verse states: "They send out from the camp"** (Numbers 5:2); the phrase "from the camp" indicates that the *halakha* applies **even to a part of the camp.** Therefore, when the majority of the Jewish people are ritually impure, even though people who are ritually impure are permitted to enter part of the Temple, as they must bring their offerings to the Temple courtyard, they are not permitted to enter everywhere inside the Temple, and the prohibition of entering the Sanctuary remains in place. **Some say that Rava said a different answer: The verse states: "That they send out from the camp any leper and any *zav* and anyone defiled by the dead…outside the camp you shall send them"** (Numbers 5:2–3). This implies that **anywhere that we apply the phrase: "Outside the camp you shall send them," which is referring to a case in which the majority of the Jewish people are ritually pure and indicates that the impure people must be sent outside of the entire camp, we also apply the phrase: "They must send out from the camp,"** meaning it is prohibited for people who are ritually impure to enter the Sanctuary. However, if the majority of the Jewish people are impure and the offering is sacrificed in a state of impurity, those who are impure are not liable if they enter the Sanctuary.

Rava offers a resolution through two formulations from the same verse. One formulation: *ve-yishalchu min ha-machaneh* — *afilu miktzat machaneh*, even a partial part of the camp. The *Heikhal* is its own distinct *machaneh*; even a partial domain remains subject to its own rule. Since the dispensation was only for the *Azarah*, the *Heikhal* — being a distinct domain — retains its prohibition, and entry there would render one *chayav*.

A second formulation of Rava takes the opposite view, reading *ve-yishalchu min ha-machaneh* alongside *u-mi-chutz la-machaneh tishalchum*: the principle is that wherever the verse of expulsion applies, the *yishalchu min ha-machaneh* also applies. Since when a *Pesach be-tumah* is brought, even *tamei meit* need not leave the *Beit HaMikdash* at all, the full dispensation — including for the *Heikhal* — would apply, and one who presses into the *Heikhal* under these circumstances would likewise be *patur*.
