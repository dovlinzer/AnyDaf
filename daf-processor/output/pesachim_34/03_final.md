# Pesachim 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trumah & Chametz Issues

### Trumah Payment Review

The daf begins six lines from the bottom of 33b, at the word *gufa*. The broader sugya has been occupied with a series of interconnected discussions arising from the question of how *trumah* payment works: specifically, whether the obligation is defined by the *damim* — the monetary value of what was destroyed — or by *mida*, the replacement of the fruit itself in terms of quantity. This question connects directly to the case of *chametz b'Pesach*: if the item is essentially worthless, does one return it at all? That question led the *gemara* into a debate among *tannaim* about whether the threshold for payment is measured by a *kezayit* or a *perutah*, which appears to bear directly on the underlying issue.

### Chametz B'Pesach

The *gemara* then moved to the question of whether one can be *mafrish trumah* from *chametz*, and the answer was no, because one cannot separate *trumah* from something that is *assur b'hana'ah* — it gives the *kohen* nothing of value and does not improve the remainder. This, in turn, opened the broader question of *trumah temei'ah* and what use can be made of it.

### Tamei Grapes Extraction

The discussion arrives at the case of *tamei* grapes: is there a way to press them such that the juice that emerges is not *tamei*? This seems directly relevant because in the back-and-forth over whether *trumah* without value creates an obligation, the example of *tamei trumah* arose, and with it the question of why one cannot simply press the grapes in a manner that keeps the juice pure. One answer offered by the *gemara* is that even if there exists a technically acceptable method of extraction, the concern of *atei l'yidei takalah* prevents it — we do not permit the method because one might come to eat the *tamei* grapes in the process.

## Tamei Terumah & Takalah

### Bread & Oil as Fuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: מַדְלִיקִין בְּפַת וּבְשֶׁמֶן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה שֶׁנִּטְמֵאת. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּחִזְקִיָּה, וְרָבָא אָמַר דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר מָרְתָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא פַּת, אֲבָל חִיטֵּי — לֹא, שֶׁמָּא יָבֹא בָּהֶן לִידֵי תַּקָּלָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ חִיטֵּי. וְאַמַּאי? נֵיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא אָתֵי בָּהֶן לִידֵי תַּקָּלָה! כִּדְאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי:
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the** matter **itself,** it was taught: **One** may **light with bread and oil of *teruma* that became ritually impure.** The Sages limited the application of this *halakha*, as **Abaye said in the name of Ḥizkiya and Rava said** in the name of the Sages from **the school of Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Marta** that **Rav Huna said: They taught** that one may use impure *teruma* as firewood **only** with regard to **bread; however,** with regard to **wheat, no,** one may not light a fire **with it, lest one encounter a stumbling block** and eat it, because wheat will not become inedible by being placed among the firewood. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even wheat** may be used as fuel. The Gemara asks: **And why** does he permit this? **Let us be concerned lest he encounter a stumbling block.** The Gemara answers based on what **Rav Ashi said** with regard to a different issue:

The *gemara* then cites the *gufa* of the ruling: *madlikin b'pas u'b'shemen shel trumah she'nitme'it* — one may use *tamei* bread or *tamei* oil of *trumah* as fuel. This is the standard disposition of *trumah teme'iah*: a *kohen* is permitted to burn it and derive benefit from it in the course of its destruction. As soon as bread is thrown into the fire it becomes inedible immediately, so there is no concern that one will come to eat it. Similarly, oil can be placed in a disgusting vessel, ensuring that no one would want to consume it after the process has begun.

### Abaye & Rava on Grain

Both Abaye, transmitting in the name of Chizkiyah, and Rava, transmitting from the school of Rav Yitzchak bar Marta, report the following ruling of Rav Huna: *lo shanu ela pat* — this permission applies only to bread. But by grain — *chiti*, wheat kernels — one may not do so, *shema yavo lidei takalah*, lest one come to eat from it. The same principle that prevents pressing *tamei* grapes applies here: if the grain does not immediately become disgusting in the course of being burned, we are concerned one might eat from it.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

Rabbi Yochanan, however, permits even grain: *afilu chiti*. The *gemara* immediately questions this: should we not be concerned about *takalah*? Is Rabbi Yochanan not bothered by this principle?

### Rav Assi: Boiled Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלִיקָתָא וּמְאִיסָתָא, הָכִי נָמֵי בִּשְׁלִיקָתָא וּמְאִיסָתָא. וְהֵיכָא אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב אָשֵׁי? אַהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר רַב אַחָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אַבָּא שָׁאוּל גַּבָּל שֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי הָיָה, וְהָיוּ מְחַמִּין לוֹ חַמִּין בְּחִיטִּין שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה לָלוּשׁ בָּהֶן עִיסָּה בְּטׇהֳרָה. אַמַּאי? נֵיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא אָתֵי בְּהוּ לִידֵי תַקָּלָה! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בִּשְׁלִיקָתָא וּמְאִיסָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is referring **to boiled and repulsive** wheat, i.e., wheat that one boiled and then placed in a repulsive area, in which case he need not be concerned that this wheat will accidentally be eaten; **so too** here, it is referring **to boiled and repulsive** wheat. The Gemara asks: **Where was Rav Ashi's** explanation **stated?** It was stated **with regard to this: As Rabbi Avin bar Rav Aḥa said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Abba Shaul was the** dough **kneader of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's **house, and they would heat water for him,** to make dough, **with wheat of ritually impure *teruma*,** which was purchased from priests at a low price, **in order to knead dough in ritual purity.** The Gemara asks: **Why** did they do this? **Let us be concerned lest they encounter a stumbling block** by accidentally eating this wheat. With regard to this **Rav Ashi said** that it was only done when the wheat was **boiled and repulsive** and could only be used for lighting a fire.

The *gemara* resolves the question by citing Rav Assi's formulation from the top of 34a: *b'shlikta u'mista* — Rabbi Yochanan's permissive ruling refers specifically to grain that has already been boiled and is therefore revolting. Once boiled, the grain is soft and unappetizing, and as soon as it is thrown into the fire it will become disgusting instantly, just like bread. The concern of *takalah* is simply absent in this circumstance. Had the grain not been boiled — had it remained hard and palatable — Rabbi Yochanan would agree that it would be forbidden.

The *gemara* here also revisits Rabbi Yochanan's earlier statement about *tamei* grapes — the ruling that one may press them a little at a time to produce juice that is *kasher* for *nesachim*. The *gemara* clarifies that this earlier statement was not addressing *trumah* grapes at all; it concerned ordinary *tamei* produce where the fruit itself is not *trumah*. In the *trumah* scenario, even if the technical method would keep the juice pure, *atei l'yidei takalah* prevents it.

## Abba Shaul's Terumah

### Abba Shaul & Firewood

The *gemara* asks: where was the ruling of Rav Ashi — that the *takalah* concern is resolved when the grain is boiled — actually stated? It was stated in connection with the following: Rav Avin bar Rav Acha, in the name of Rav Yitzchak Abba, citing Rav Yitzchak the elder, reports that Abba Shaul was the dough-maker in the household of Rebbi. They would heat the water for his baking using the grain of *trumah teme'iah* as firewood. Rashi notes that *trumah teme'iah* sold for a very low market price, since few could make use of it, and so it was purchased cheaply for use as firewood — which is entirely permitted, as burning *trumah teme'iah* and deriving benefit from it in the course of its destruction is the standard permitted use.

Rashi adds that part of the *chiddush* is that Rebbi was *ochel chullin b'taharah* — he ate *chulin* in a state of ritual purity — yet he was not concerned that the handlers might inadvertently touch the *tamei* grain below and thereby contaminate things above. Tosafot, however, raises the issue from other *gemaros* that only *kohanim* may derive benefit from burning *trumah teme'iah*, just as only *kohanim* may eat *trumah*. According to Tosafot, then, one may have to say that the bread being baked from this process was intended to feed the *kohanim* of Rebbi's household.

### Takalah Concern Here

The *gemara* raises the obvious question: why is Abba Shaul permitted to do this at all? *Neichosh shema yavo lidei takalah* — perhaps in the process of throwing grain down to serve as firewood one might pop a kernel in one's mouth? This is precisely the concern that Rav Huna articulated: by grain, unlike bread, the *takalah* problem is real.

### Rav Ashi's Resolution

Rav Ashi resolves it: *b'shlikta d'mista* — the grain had already been boiled and was therefore disgusting. Once boiled grain is thrown into the fire it is revolting, and no one would think to eat it. The *takalah* concern therefore does not apply. The *gemara* at this point is working with the assumption that the principle of *shema yavo lidei takalah* is universally accepted — it was not merely one optional answer among several, but a genuine operative rule that requires an answer whenever the circumstances seem to trigger it.

## Learning Terumos Dispute

### Rava bar Masnah's Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי בַּר אָבִין וְרַב חֲנַנְיָא בַּר אָבִין תָּנוּ תְּרוּמוֹת בֵּי רַבָּה. פְּגַע בְּהוּ רָבָא בַּר מַתְנָה, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי אָמְרִיתוּ בִּתְרוּמוֹת דְּבֵי מָר? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וּמַאי קַשְׁיָא לָךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ, תְּנַן: שְׁתִילֵי תְרוּמוֹת שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ וּשְׁתָלָן — טְהוֹרִים מִלְּטַמֵּא, וַאֲסוּרִין מִלֶּאֱכוֹל (בִּתְרוּמָה). וְכִי מֵאַחַר דִּטְהוֹרִים מִלְּטַמֵּא, אַמַּאי אֲסוּרִין מִלֶּאֱכוֹל?
>
> **Translation:** After mentioning ways in which impure *teruma* was used, the Gemara mentions other *halakhot* pertaining to this issue. **Abaye bar Avin and Rav Ḥananya bar Avin taught** the tractate of ***Terumot* in the school of Rabba. Rava bar Mattana met them** and **said to them: What** novel idea can **you say** has been taught with regard to ***Terumot* in the school of** our **Master,** Rabba? **They said to him: What** is **difficult for you?** There must be some issue troubling you that has caused you to ask this question. **He said to them:** The following statement that **we learned** in the mishna in *Terumot* is unclear: **Saplings** of ***teruma* that became ritually impure and were planted are pure** such that they **do not impart ritual impurity** once they have been planted, **but they are prohibited to be eaten as *teruma*.** The question arises: **If they do not impart ritual impurity, why is it prohibited to eat them?** If their impurity has been eliminated then it should be permitted to eat them, like other ritually pure *teruma*.

The *gemara* now introduces a new narrative. Abaye bar Avin and Rav Chanina bar Avin — apparently two brothers — were studying the tractate *Terumos* in the house of Rava. Rava bar Masnah encountered them and asked what *chiddushim* they had encountered in their learning. Sensing that he had a specific difficulty in mind, they turned the question around: what was bothering him? What issues did he have with *Terumos*?

### Planted Terumah Mishnah

He responded by citing a *mishnah* in *Terumos*: *shtilei Terumos she'nitme'u* — plantings of *trumah* that had become *tamei*. The case is: one had *trumah* produce — grain, onions, or the like — which became *tamei*. Rather than burn it, one chose to plant it in the ground. The *mishnah* rules that *tehorim mil'tamei* — once planted, the produce ceases to be *tamei*. This follows from the principle that things attached to the ground, or more precisely things that take root and grow within the ground, lose their *tumah*. However, the *mishnah* continues: *asurin mil'echol* — they remain forbidden to eat.

### Rava's Problem

This split ruling is puzzling. The reason the produce could not be eaten was that it was *trumah teme'iah*. If the *tumah* is removed by planting, why does the prohibition to eat remain? This is what troubled Rava bar Masnah, and he asked whether they had come up with a satisfactory explanation.

## Removing Tumah, Keeping…

### Rabbah: Asurin L'Zarim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַבָּה: מַאי אֲסוּרִין — אֲסוּרִין לְזָרִים. וּמַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: גִּידּוּלֵי תְרוּמָה — תְּרוּמָה? תְּנֵינָא: גִּידּוּלֵי תְרוּמָה — תְּרוּמָה!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye bar Avin and Rav Ḥananya bar Avin **said to** Rava bar Mattana: **This is what Rabba said** in explaining this mishna: **What** does it mean that **they are prohibited** to be eaten? It means that **they are prohibited** to be eaten **by non-priests,** but a priest may eat them. Once these saplings are planted, they lose their ritual impurity but retain their status as *teruma*. Rava bar Mattana challenged this answer: If this is the case, **what is** the mishna **teaching us** with this statement? Is it teaching us that **growths of *teruma*** are considered ***teruma*?** It is unnecessary to teach this principle, as **we** already **learned: Growths of *teruma*,** i.e., produce that grows from *teruma* that was planted in the ground, are considered ***teruma*.** Why, then, is it necessary to teach this principle again?

They reported that Rabbah had resolved the *mishnah* as follows: *mai asurin? asurin l'zarim* — what does it mean they are forbidden? It means they are forbidden to non-*kohanim*. In other words, the *mishnah* is not saying that the *kohanim* themselves cannot eat it; rather, it is simply reaffirming that the produce retains its *trumah* status. Planting removes the *tumah* but does not remove the *kedushah* of *trumah*.

### Gidulei Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא גִּידּוּלֵי גִידּוּלִין, וּמַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן — בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין זַרְעוֹ כָּלֶה. הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: הַטֶּבֶל — גִּידּוּלָיו מוּתָּרִין, בְּדָבָר שֶׁזַּרְעוֹ כָּלֶה. אֲבָל בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין זַרְעוֹ כָּלֶה — אֲפִילּוּ גִּידּוּלֵי גִידּוּלִין אֲסוּרִין בַּאֲכִילָה. אִישְׁתִּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** as follows: This case is referring to **the growths of growths** of *teruma*, i.e., plants that grew from the original growths of *teruma*, and **what is it teaching us?** It is teaching that **an item whose seed does not disintegrate** when planted in the ground maintains its *teruma* status. While most seeds will disintegrate, other plants, such as onions and garlic, merely continue growing when planted. In that case, this mishna would be informing us that even the growths of growths of such plants retain their *teruma* status. However, **we** already **learned** that **as well.** As the mishna states: With regard to **untithed produce [*tevel*], its growths,** the produce that grows from it, **are permitted in** the case of **items whose seed disintegrates; however, in** the case of **items where the seed does not disintegrate, it is prohibited to eat even the growths of growths** unless they are tithed. There would be no need for the mishna to teach us this *halakha* a second time. **They were silent** and did not have an answer to this question.

This prompts the obvious question: if that is the *chiddush*, what is novel about it? Rava bar Masnah said precisely this: *mai kamashma lan* — what is the *mishnah* teaching us? And the response was: *gidulei Terumah Terumah* — things that grow from *trumah* retain the status of *trumah*. The *mishnah* is not merely saying that planting does not change the status of the planted item itself; it goes further and teaches that the growth that emerges from a *trumah* seed also carries *trumah* status. If one plants a grain of *trumah* and a full stalk of wheat grows from it, that wheat is *trumah*.

Rava bar Masnah pushes back: but we already know this — *tanina*, we have also learned that *gidulei Terumah Terumah* in another *mishnah*. So this is not a *chiddush* either.

Perhaps the *chiddush* is *gidulei gidulin* — a second generation. According to Tosafot, this means: one plants a *trumah* grain, the resulting stalk grows, one takes a kernel from that stalk and plants it again, and the wheat that grows from that second planting is also *trumah*. According to Rashi, the scenario is slightly different — an onion bulb is planted and additional bulbs cluster around it as an outgrowth, and those secondary bulbs are the *gidulei gidulin*.

The *gemara* specifies that this rule applies *b'davar she'ein zar'o kalah* — in cases where the original seed is not destroyed in the course of growth. When a wheat grain is planted, the original kernel ceases to exist as it transforms into the stalk; there is nothing left. But when an onion bulb is planted, the original bulb remains present throughout as the additional bulbs develop. In the latter case, where the original remains present, even a second-generation growth retains *trumah* status.

Rava bar Masnah says this too is already known — *ha nami tanina* — because the *mishnah* elsewhere teaches a parallel rule about *tevel*.

### Tevel vs. Terumah

The *mishnah* states regarding *tevel* — produce from which *terumos u'maasros* have not yet been separated: *ha'tevel gidulav mutarin b'davar she'zar'o kalah* — the growth of *tevel* is permitted when the original seed has been destroyed. Rashi explains why this should matter at all: the original grain of *tevel* had already been threshed and processed, and once *gemar melachah* was performed on it, one may not even eat from it *achilas arai* — casually, before formal separation. But new grain that grows and has not undergone *gemar melachah* may be eaten casually. If the new growth is considered a continuation of the original *tevel*, it cannot be eaten *achilas arai*; if it is considered an entirely new entity, it may be. By *tevel*, the *gemara* is lenient, and the growth is treated as new.

However, the *mishnah* continues: *aval b'davar she'ein zar'o kalah* — but when the original seed is not destroyed, *afilu gidulei gidulim assurim b'achilah* — even second-generation growth is forbidden. This is a stringency even by *tevel*. Certainly by *trumah* the rule would be at least as strict. This comparison actually suggests that the entire restriction of *gidulei trumah* is rabbinic in origin, since the same logic of continuation versus new growth would apply equally to both — and yet the *halachah* treats them differently without an obvious *d'oraita* basis for the distinction.

In sum, Rava bar Masnah's point stands: all of this is already known from other *mishnayos*, and the *mishnah* in *Terumos* adds nothing new. The two brothers had no response and admitted he had stumped them. They asked if he himself had heard a good explanation. He said: here is what Rav Sheshet says.

## Rav Sheshet & Hesech

### Asurin L'Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ בְּהָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַאי אֲסוּרִין — אֲסוּרִין לְכֹהֲנִים, הוֹאִיל וְאִיפְּסִילוּ לְהוּ בְּהֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to** Rava bar Mattana: **Have you heard something in this** regard? **He said to them: This is what Rav Sheshet said: What** is the meaning of the word **prohibited** in this context? It means that it is **prohibited for priests, since it has been disqualified for them due to the diversion of attention.** *Teruma* and other consecrated property must be guarded, and when one fails to do so, it is treated as though it were impure. Therefore, these *teruma* saplings are treated as though they have become impure once the priest diverts attention from them, and they remain prohibited to him even after another generation grows from them.

Rav Sheshet offers a fundamentally different reading of the *mishnah*: *mai asurin? asurin l'kohanim* — what does it mean they are forbidden? It means they are forbidden even to *kohanim*. The *mishnah* is saying exactly what it sounds like: the planting removes the *tumah*, and yet even *kohanim* cannot eat the produce. The first answer — that *asurin* merely means *asurin l'zarim*, forbidden to non-*kohanim* — collapses the *mishnah*'s statement into something already known. Rav Sheshet insists the *mishnah* is saying something genuinely striking: despite the *tumah* being gone, a *kohen* still cannot eat it.

### Hesech Hadaat Psul

Why not? *Ho'il ve'ifsalu lehu b'hesech hada'as* — because the produce has become *pasul* not through *tumah* but through *hesech hada'as*, lack of attention. *Kohanim* are commanded to guard their *trumah* from becoming *tamei* — *mishmeret terumati*. Once the produce became *tamei*, they ceased paying attention to it. They reasoned: it is already *tamei*, there is nothing left to protect. This neglect itself constitutes an independent *psul* called *hesech hada'as*. Even after the *tumah* is removed by planting, the *psul* of *hesech hada'as* remains, because it is not a *psul* that depends on the *tumah* — it is its own category of invalidity.

This is a striking claim. One would naturally assume that the entire point of guarding *trumah* is to prevent *tumah*, and so once *tumah* is addressed, there is nothing left for *hesech hada'as* to do. Rav Sheshet's position is that *hesech hada'as* has become an independent *psul* in its own right.

## Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish

### Yochanan: Psul Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת פְּסוּל הַגּוּף הָוֵי — שַׁפִּיר, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת פְּסוּל טוּמְאָה הָוֵי — מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?! דְּאִתְּמַר: הֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: פְּסוּל טוּמְאָה הָוֵי, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פְּסוּל הַגּוּף הָוֵי. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר פְּסוּל טוּמְאָה הָוֵי: שֶׁאִם יָבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ וִיטַהֲרֶנָּה — שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר פְּסוּל הַגּוּף הָוֵי: שֶׁאִם יָבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ וִיטַהֲרֶנָּה — אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that a **diversion of attention constitutes an inherent disqualification, it works out well.** According to this opinion, a diversion of attention does not disqualify *teruma* due to a concern that it became impure. Instead, there is an independent rabbinic decree rendering *teruma* that has not been watched impure even when this *teruma* could not possibly have become impure. According to this opinion, one can understand why this growth may not be eaten by a priest. **But according to the one who says** that a **diversion of attention is a disqualification due to** a concern about **ritual impurity, what is there to say?** It is stated in the mishna that by planting these saplings they become pure, even if they were certainly ritually impure prior to being planted. If this is the case with regard to *teruma* that is certainly impure, all the more so should it apply to a case where there is only a chance that the *teruma* is ritually impure.

Apropos the discussion of diversion of attention, the Gemara cites a dispute between *amora'im* with regard to this issue, **as it was stated:** What is the nature of the disqualification of **diversion of attention? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is a disqualification** due to a concern about **ritual impurity** that may have been contracted while one's attention was diverted. And **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: It is an inherent disqualification.**

The Gemara discusses the ramifications of this dispute: According to **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who **said it is a disqualification** due to a concern about **ritual impurity, if Elijah comes and renders it ritually pure** then **we will listen to him,** because it was treated as impure only due to a doubt with regard to its actual status. However, according to **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** who **said** that **it is an inherent disqualification,** even **if Elijah comes and renders it pure we will not listen to him.** The reason for this is that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish believes that this decree is unrelated to the question of whether the object actually became impure.

### Reish Lakish: Psul Hagoof

Reish Lakish disagrees: *psul hagoof havei* — *hesech hada'as* is an intrinsic invalidity, entirely independent of whether *tumah* occurred. *She'im yavo Eliyahu v'yitaharena, ein shomin lo* — even if Eliyahu were to come and testify that nothing happened, we would not accept it. The *kohen* was supposed to pay attention, and the failure to pay attention is itself the problem — regardless of outcome. According to Reish Lakish, the *mishnah* about planted *tamei trumah* is perfectly intelligible: planting it is like Eliyahu coming to declare it *tahor*, but that makes no difference, because *hesech hada'as* remains as an independent *psul* even when the *tumah* itself has been removed.

### Bird Offerings Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא אוֹמֵר: לוּל קָטָן הָיָה בֵּין כֶּבֶשׁ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ בְּמַעֲרָבוֹ שֶׁל כֶּבֶשׁ, שֶׁשָּׁם הָיוּ זוֹרְקִים פְּסוּלֵי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף, וּתְעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן, וְיוֹצְאִין לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא פְּסוּל טוּמְאָה הָוֵי, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי בָּעֵי עִיבּוּר צוּרָה, שֶׁמָּא יָבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ וִיטַהֲרֶנָּה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ פְּסוּל הַגּוּף, לְמָה לִי עִיבּוּר צוּרָה? וְהָתְנַן, זֶה הַכְּלָל:
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** based on what is taught in the *Tosefta*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: There was a small gap between the ramp and the altar on the western** side **of the ramp, where they would throw disqualified birds** that had been designated as **sin-offerings.** If birds became disqualified for any reason, such as a diversion of attention, they were left there **until their form decayed,** i.e., until the next morning, at which point they would be definitively disqualified due to remaining in the Temple overnight and could be **taken out to the place** designated for **burning. Granted, if you say** that a diversion of attention **is a disqualification** due to a concern for **ritual impurity, for this reason it requires decay of form** to ensure that the bird is certainly disqualified. Currently the bird is disqualified only due to uncertainty, **and Elijah may come and render it ritually pure. However, if you say** that **it is an inherent disqualification,** then **why do I** need to leave it until **it has decay of form?** It should be definitively disqualified once there has been a diversion of attention. **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **this is the principle:**

Rabbi Yochanan challenged Reish Lakish with a *mishnah*. Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka taught: there was a small funnel-like recess (*lul*) between the ramp and the altar, on the western side of the ramp, in the southwest corner where *kohanim* would perform *chatas ha'of* — bird sin-offerings. When a bird offering became invalid, it would be placed in this recess. *V'ta'avor tzuratan* — one would wait until its appearance passed over, a term meaning that nightfall would arrive and the offering would become *pasul* as *notar* (leftover sacrifice). It would then be taken out to *beit hasreifah* to be burned.

The assumption of the *gemara* is that the only invalidity that is not sufficient to permit immediate burning — and therefore requires waiting until nightfall — is *hesech hada'as*. If something has a full intrinsic *psul*, the *mishnah* elsewhere rules *yisaref miyad* — burn it immediately. The only reason to wait for *notar* to develop is that the current *psul* is not intrinsic enough. And the only *psul* that is not intrinsic but prevents bringing the offering is *hesech hada'as* — we are uncertain whether it became *tamei*, cannot bring it, but also cannot burn it. So we wait for nightfall, and then the *psul* becomes intrinsic through *linah* (remaining overnight), and we burn it.

Rabbi Yochanan's argument: if *hesech hada'as* were an intrinsic *psul* as Reish Lakish maintains, one should be able to burn the bird immediately upon the *hesech hada'as* occurring, without waiting for nightfall. The fact that one must wait shows that *hesech hada'as* is not intrinsic — it is merely a *tumah* concern.

### Notar & Linah

Tosafot presses a sharper version of this argument: even if we granted that the invalidity in the bird case arose from an external cause such as the blood spilling (*dam*) or the *ba'alim* dying, once the bird is placed in the recess and the *kohen* walks away, *hesech hada'as* has occurred. If *hesech hada'as* were an intrinsic *psul*, one could turn around immediately and burn the bird right then, without needing to wait for nightfall. The fact that the procedure requires waiting until morning shows that *hesech hada'as* alone is never sufficient to permit immediate burning.

Reish Lakish responds: *hai tanna tanna devei Rabba bar Avuha hu* — the author of this *mishnah* is the *tanna* from the school of Rabba bar Avuha, who holds that *even* intrinsically invalid offerings require *ibur tzurah* — one must wait for nightfall before burning them. According to this *tanna*, the rule of *yisaref miyad* for intrinsic *psul* does not apply; everything requires waiting. The argument would only work if one accepted the majority position that intrinsic *psul* permits immediate burning. Reish Lakish concedes that according to that majority position, the argument would indeed hold, but the *mishnah* cited here follows a different school.

## Korban Psul Types

### Psul B'Gufo Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ — יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד. בַּדָּם וּבַבְּעָלִים — תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן וְיוֹצְאִין לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Any** offering **that has a disqualification in the body** of the animal, i.e., it has a definite disqualification with regard to the meat itself, **should be burned immediately.** If it has a disqualification **in the blood** of the animal, e.g., if the blood was spilled, or a disqualification of **its owner,** e.g., if the owner became impure, then it should be left until **its form is decayed and taken out to the place** designated for **burning.**

The *gemara* now articulates the general *mishnah* framework for the two categories of invalidity. *Zeh haklal: kol shepsulo b'gufo, yisaref miyad* — this is the rule: anything whose invalidity is intrinsic — located in the body of the *korban* itself — is burned immediately. No waiting, no *ibur tzurah*; the status is clear, and the burning proceeds at once.

### Dam & Baalim

By contrast, *v'dam u'va'alim ta'avor tzuratan v'yotzin l'veit hasreifah* — if the problem was with the blood or with the owners, one must wait for the appearance to pass before burning. These are cases where the invalidity is external to the meat itself. With *dam*, the blood became *tamei* or was spilled, so *zerikat hadam* cannot be performed, and the meat cannot be eaten — but nothing happened to the meat. With *ba'alim*, the situation is similar: the owner died, for instance in the case of a *chatas* where the owner's death terminates the offering — but again the meat itself is intact. In these cases, the meat cannot be eaten and cannot be burned yet, because its own *psul* is not intrinsic. One waits until the following morning, when the meat becomes *notar*, acquires an intrinsic *psul*, and can then be burned.

This framework is the backdrop for the debate about *hesech hada'as*: Rabbi Yochanan argues that *hesech hada'as* belongs in the second category — external, *tumah*-related — which is why it requires waiting. Reish Lakish's position, that it is *psul hagoof*, would require placing it in the first category and burning immediately, which the *mishnah*'s procedure does not support.

## Tamei Meat & Zrika

### Rabbi Eliezer: Perform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: נִטְמָא אוֹ שֶׁנִּפְסַל הַבָּשָׂר, אוֹ שֶׁיָּצָא חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יִזְרוֹק, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִזְרוֹק. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שֶׁאִם זָרַק — הוּרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **raised a** further **objection to** the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish based on another *baraita*, where it is taught: **If the meat became impure or disqualified, or if it was taken out of the walls** that delineate its permitted area, **Rabbi Eliezer says: He sprinkles** the blood of these offerings nonetheless, as in his opinion the blood may be sprinkled regardless of the status of the meat of the offering. **Rabbi Yehoshua says: He does not sprinkle** the blood unless the meat is fit to be brought as an offering. **And Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that if** the blood **was sprinkled,** the offering **is accepted.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraita* raising the related question of *zerikat hadam* when the meat is invalid. The *baraita* states: if the meat became *tamei*, or became otherwise invalid, or was taken outside the *kla'im* (the Temple precincts, for *kodshei kodashim* that may only be eaten on the Temple grounds), what does one do about *zerikat hadam*? The meat can no longer be eaten; should one still perform the blood-sprinkling?

Rabbi Eliezer says: *yizrok* — yes, perform *zerikat hadam* even without valid meat.

Rabbi Yehoshua says: *lo yizrok* — do not perform it. Nevertheless, *umodi Rabbi Yehoshua she'im zarak hurtzah* — Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that if one did perform *zerikat hadam* in this situation, the *korban* is accepted *b'dieved*.

### Tzitz Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נִפְסַל — לָאו בְּהֶיסַּח הַדַּעַת? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא פְּסוּלֵי טוּמְאָה הָוֵי, הַיְינוּ דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ דִּמְרַצֵּי צִיץ. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ פְּסוּל הַגּוּף הָוֵי, אַמַּאי הוּרְצָה (צִיץ)?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: To **what** type of **disqualification** is the *baraita* referring? **Is it not** disqualification on account of **a diversion of attention?** It cannot be a case where it was disqualified due to impurity or being taken outside of the walls, since these are mentioned explicitly. **Granted, if you say** that a diversion of attention is **a disqualification** based on a concern about **ritual impurity, this is** how **you** can **find** a case that the offering is accepted **because the frontplate atones** for cases where there is a disqualification related to ritual impurity. **But if you say that it is an inherent disqualification, why** is the offering **accepted** according to Rabbi Yehoshua, given that it is a disqualified offering?

Rabbi Yochanan uses this passage in his argument against Reish Lakish. The *baraita* lists three scenarios making the meat invalid: *nitma*, it became *tamei*; *yatza chutz laklaim*, it left the Temple precincts; and *nifsal*, it became *pasul*. The third scenario is the crux. Rabbi Yochanan argues: *nifsal* presumably refers to the *psul* of *hesech hada'as*, and Rabbi Yehoshua concedes that *b'dieved* the *korban* is accepted. Why? Because the *tzitz*, the golden plate worn by the *Kohen Gadol*, atones for *tumah* problems — *nosei avon hakodashim*. The *Kohen Gadol*'s wearing of the *tzitz* retroactively validates *kodashim* that were compromised by *tumah*. This makes sense if *hesech hada'as* is purely a *tumah* concern — the *tzitz* covers it *b'dieved*. But if *hesech hada'as* were an intrinsic *psul*, why should the *tzitz* help? The *tzitz* atones for *tumah*, not for intrinsic invalidity.

### Reish Lakish's Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נִפְסַל, נִפְסַל בִּטְבוּל יוֹם. אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ טָמֵא! תְּרֵי גַּוְונֵי טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish rejects this interpretation of the *baraita*: No, this is not a case where the offering was disqualified due to a diversion of attention. In **what** way **was** the offering **disqualified?** It was **disqualified due to** contact with **one who immersed himself during the day.** One who immersed himself during the day invalidates items due to ritual impurity. Although these items themselves are invalidated, they cannot in turn render other items ritually impure. The Gemara asks: **If that is so, this is** the same as the disqualification of **ritual impurity.** What, then, is the difference between this disqualification and that of ritual impurity previously mentioned by the *baraita*? The Gemara answers that **two types of ritual impurity** are mentioned here: One type of impurity can also impart impurity to other objects, and a second type can invalidate another object but will not impart impurity.

Reish Lakish responds: *hai tanna tanna devei Rabba bar Avuha hu* — the author of this *mishnah* is the *tanna* from the school of Rabba bar Avuha, who holds that *even* intrinsically invalid offerings require *ibur tzurah* — one must wait for nightfall before burning them. According to this *tanna*, the rule of *yisaref miyad* for intrinsic *psul* does not apply; everything requires waiting. The argument would only work if one accepted the majority position that intrinsic *psul* permits immediate burning. Reish Lakish concedes that according to that majority position, the argument would indeed hold, but the *mishnah* cited here follows a different school.

The *gemara* notes that while it is somewhat redundant to list both *nitma* and *nifsal b'tvul yom* since both are *tumah*, the *mishnah* often lists multiple variations of the same category. The *b'dieved* rule regarding *chutz laklaim* — which is not a *tumah* problem — is addressed by both Rashi and Tosafot as derived from a separate source establishing that this external invalidity also falls under the *tzitz*'s protective reach in some manner.

The *gemara* concludes this section by noting that all the evidence marshaled favors Rabbi Yochanan's position that *hesech hada'as* is merely a *tumah* concern, not an intrinsic *psul*. Reish Lakish's position, while conceptually striking, finds no clear support in the sources. And this returns us to the original problem: if *hesech hada'as* is only a *tumah* concern, then planting *tamei trumah* should restore its permitted status fully, and the *mishnah*'s ruling that the planted produce remains *assur* is left unexplained.

## Water Sanctification Pro…

### Mei Chag Hashaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי סְלֵיק רָבִין, אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, וַאֲמַר: בַּבְלָאֵי טַפְשָׁאֵי, מִשּׁוּם דְּיָתְבִי בְּאַרְעָא דַחֲשׁוֹכָא אָמְרִיתוּן שְׁמַעְתָּתָא דִּמְחַשְּׁכוּ. לָא שְׁמִיעַ לְכוּ הָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מֵי הַחַג שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ, הִשִּׁיקָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁן — טְהוֹרִין, הִקְדִּישָׁן וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִשִּׁיקָן — טְמֵאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin ascended** to Eretz Yisrael, **he stated** this ***halakha* of** Rav Sheshet **before Rabbi Yirmeya. And** Rabbi Yirmeya **said: Foolish Babylonians! Because you dwell in a dark land, you state *halakhot* that are dim. Have you not heard this** statement of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish in the name of Rabbi Oshaya?** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Oshaya: With regard to **the water** used for the water libation during the **festival** of Sukkot which was drawn over the course of the day in order to be poured the next day and consequently **became impure,** the following distinction applies: If it **was** brought into **contact** with a ritual bath of pure water and **was then consecrated, it is ritually pure.** However, **if it was consecrated and was then** brought into **contact** with the ritual bath, **it is ritually impure.**

When Rabin went up to Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted this entire unresolved discussion to Rabbi Yirmeya. Rabbi Yirmeya responded sharply: *Bavla'ei tipshai* — foolish Babylonians. Because you dwell in a dark land (*ar'a d'chashoka*), you transmit discussions that lack light. You tried to explain this *mishnah* without having the key piece of information, so you invented explanations. Rashi makes the point explicit: when one does not know the true reason for something, one constructs a reason from one's own reasoning. These answers — that it is still *trumah*, that it is *hesech hada'as* — were improvised because the crucial teaching of Reish Lakish in the name of Rabbi Yoshia had not reached Babylonia.

The teaching is this: *mei chag she'nitme'u* — the water used for *nisuch hamayim* on *Sukkot* that became *tamei*. Suppose it is *Shabbat* and new water cannot be drawn. The only recourse is *hashaka* — literally "kissing" — a process that works specifically for liquids: if *tamei* water is placed in a vessel and that vessel is submerged in the *mikvah* so that the waterline of the vessel's opening is level with and effectively merges into the *mikvah* water, the *tamei* water becomes part of the *mikvah* and loses its *tumah*. This process is called *zri'ah* in the *gemara*'s idiom, by analogy to planting in the ground — both involve the *tamei* item becoming absorbed into something that is inherently *tahor*.

### Sanctify Then Hashaka

*Hishikun v'achar kach hikdishan, tehorin* — if one first performed the *hashaka* and then sanctified the water for *nisuch hamayim*, the water is *tahor* and valid. The process worked.

### Hashaka Then Sanctify

*Hikdishan v'achar kach hishikun, tme'in* — but if one first sanctified the water and then performed the *hashaka*, the water remains *tamei* and is not valid.

### Ein Zri'ah L'Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי זְרִיעָה נִינְהוּ, מָה לִי הִשִּׁיקָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁן, מָה לִי הִקְדִּישָׁן וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִשִּׁיקָן? אַלְמָא: אֵין זְרִיעָה לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ. הָכָא נָמֵי, אֵין זְרִיעָה לִתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The question arises: **Since** this type of purification **is** similar **to planting,** as when the impure water came in contact with the water of the ritual bath, it is considered as though the water was planted in the ground and thereby purified, **what does it matter if it was** brought into **contact and then consecrated or consecrated and then** brought into **contact? Apparently planting is not** effective with regard **to consecrated items,** i.e., such items are not purified through this process. Therefore, **here too, planting is not** effective with regard **to *teruma*.** Despite the fact that planting is generally effective in removing the impure status of the water, the Sages imposed higher standards with regard to consecrated items. Similarly, the Sages imposed higher standards for removing the *teruma* status of the plants. One can explain that the produce grown from *teruma* mentioned in the *baraita* remains prohibited for non-priests because it is still considered *teruma*.

The question is obvious: in both cases the exact same physical process was performed. Why should the order of sanctification relative to *hashaka* make any difference? The answer Reish Lakish derives: *ein zri'ah l'hekdesh* — planting, or its liquid analog *hashaka*, does not work for *kodesh*. Once something has been sanctified and then becomes *tamei*, the process of *zri'ah* cannot restore it. The underlying principle is: when a mundane item becomes *tamei*, its status is simply a neutral condition to be rectified. Remove the *tumah* and you return to square one. But when a *kodesh* item becomes *tamei*, the holiness itself has been violated. To then run a purification process and have the item return to its prior *kodesh* status involves something the *halachah* will not allow. Once *kodesh* has been violated, it remains compromised. *Ein zri'ah l'hekdesh*.

## Terumah Like Kodesh

### Terumah Zri'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב דִּימִי וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הִקְדִּישָׁן בִּכְלִי קָאָמַר, אֲבָל בַּפֶּה לָא עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מַעֲלָה. אוֹ דִילְמָא בַּפֶּה נָמֵי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מַעֲלָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי, כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ שָׁמַעְתִּי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ, דְּרָכָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁן — טְהוֹרִים. הִקְדִּישָׁן וְאַחַר כָּךְ דְּרָכָן — טְמֵאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi sat and said this *halakha*** that was stated by Rav Oshaya with regard to principle of bringing liquid into contact with a ritual bath. **Abaye said to him: Did** Rav Oshaya state his ruling that bringing a liquid into contact with a ritual bath is not effective for consecrated items with regard to a case where **he consecrated** the water by placing it **in a** sacred **vessel, but** if he consecrated it **through speech then the Sages did not impose a higher** standard, in which case the water can be purified by being brought into contact with a ritual bath? **Or perhaps the Sages imposed a higher** standard in a case **where** one consecrates it **through speech as well?** Rav Dimi **said to him: I did not hear** the *halakha* with regard to **this** case; however, **I heard** the *halakha* with regard to **a similar case. As Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to **grapes that became ritually impure, if one trod on them and afterward consecrated them, they are pure.** According to this opinion, the wine inside the grape does not become impure from the grape itself. However, if **he consecrated** the grapes **and afterward pressed them, they are impure,** because the *halakha* is especially stringent with regard consecrated items.

This principle now provides the long-sought explanation for the *mishnah* about planted *tamei trumah*. The parallel is direct: *hacha nami ein zri'ah l'trumah* — just as *zri'ah* does not restore *kodesh* to its pre-violation status, so too *zri'ah* does not restore *trumah* to the status of *trumah tehorah*. The produce is *trumah*, it became *tamei*, the *kedushah* of *trumah* was violated. Planting it in the ground may technically remove the *tumah* in the sense that it will no longer transmit *tumah* to other items, but it does not undo the violation of its sacred status.

This is the *pshat* of the *mishnah*: *tehorim mil'tamei* — yes, planting makes it *tahor* in the sense that it will not render other things *tamei*; *asurin mil'echol* — but it remains forbidden to eat, because the *trumah* status has been irrevocably compromised. There is no need to invoke *gidulei trumah*, no need to invoke *hesech hada'as*. The simple explanation is: once *trumah* becomes *tamei*, planting cannot restore its usable status, because *ein zri'ah l'trumah* just as *ein zri'ah l'hekdesh*. And had the Babylonians possessed this teaching of Reish Lakish in the name of Rabbi Yoshia, they would never have needed to construct the other explanations.

### Zri'ah Effect Limited

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא עֲנָבִים דִּקְדוּשַּׁת פֶּה נִינְהוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מַעֲלָה. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: עֲנָבִים קָאָמְרַתְּ? הָכָא בַּעֲנָבִים שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה עָסְקִינַן, דִּקְדוּשַּׁת פֶּה דִּידְהוּ — כִּקְדוּשַּׁת כְּלִי דָּמְיָא. אֲבָל הָנֵי דְּבָעֵי כְּלִי, בַּפֶּה לָא עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן מַעֲלָה. דְּרָכָן — וַאֲפִילּוּ טוּבָא?! וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ — דּוֹרְכָן פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And yet** with regard to **grapes which are** only **consecrated through speech,** as the wine/grapes offered on the altar are not brought in a sacred vessel, **even so, the Sages imposed a higher** standard such that these grapes become impure after they have been consecrated. **Rav Yosef said:** This case does not serve as a proof since **you spoke of grapes, and here we are dealing with grapes of *teruma*, whose consecration through speech is comparable to consecration in a** sacred **vessel,** as *teruma* cannot be consecrated by being placed in a sacred vessel. **However,** with regard to **those** items **that require a** sacred **vessel** in order to be fully consecrated, such as water used for a libation, **the Sages did not impose a higher** standard in a case where one consecrated it **through speech.** The phrase **if one tread upon them** is stated without qualification, indicating that the wine is ritually pure **even** if he pressed **many** grapes at once. **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this? Didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** With regard to **grapes that became ritually impure grapes, one** should **tread on them less than an egg-bulk at a time.** When there is less than an egg-bulk of grapes, they do not impart ritual impurity.

The *gemara* continues with Abaye pressing further. He asked Rav Dimi: does this principle — that *zri'ah* does not work once something is sanctified — apply when the sanctification was performed merely *b'peh*, verbally, before the item was placed in a *kli sharet*? Or does it apply only once the item has been formally sanctified through a vessel? This is a question specific to the world of *korbanos* and the *Beit Hamikdash*, where sanctification of liquids and produce becomes fully operative only when placed in a *kli sharet*. Perhaps prior to that stage, verbal sanctification alone does not give the item sufficient status to trigger the rule of *ein zri'ah*.

Rav Dimi replied: *zu lo shamati, kiyotzei vah shamati* — I have not heard a ruling specifically on that point, but I have heard something analogous.

## Tamei Grapes Pressing

### Grapes Before Sanctify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ, דְּרָכָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ הִקְדִּישָׁן — טְהוֹרִים. הִקְדִּישָׁן וְאַחַר כָּךְ דְּרָכָן — טְמֵאִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: With regard to **grapes that became ritually impure, if one trod on them and afterward consecrated them, they are pure.** According to this opinion, the wine inside the grape does not become impure from the grape itself. However, if **he consecrated** the grapes **and afterward pressed them, they are impure,** because the *halakha* is especially stringent with regard consecrated items.

Rabbi Abbahu said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *anavim she'nitme'u* — grapes that became *tamei*. *Drachan v'achar kach hikdishan, tehorim* — if one pressed them and then sanctified the resulting wine, they are *tahor*. This is the connection back to the original discussion of *tamei* grapes. The ruling mirrors the water case exactly: press first, sanctify afterward, and the product is valid.

### Sanctify Before Press

*Hikdishan v'achar kach drachan, tme'im* — but if one sanctified the grapes first and then pressed them, the resulting juice is *tamei*. Same reasoning applies: once the grapes have been sanctified, they have *kodesh* status, and the *zri'ah*-like process of pressing — which under ordinary circumstances would yield juice that is not *tamei* — no longer works, because *ein zri'ah l'hekdesh*.

Rav Yosef challenges this reading. He questions whether Rabbi Yochanan's statement about grapes involves *Beit Hamikdash* sanctification at all: *hacha b'anavim shel trumah askinan* — perhaps the entire case concerns *trumah* grapes, not *hekdesh*. And by *trumah*, verbal sanctification is the only mode — it is the full and complete sanctification, equivalent to the *kli sharet* stage in the *Beit Hamikdash*. If so, the grape case teaches nothing new about whether verbal-only sanctification in the *Beit Hamikdash* context triggers the restriction. It only confirms what was already established about *trumah*, where verbal sanctification is the operative act. The question about *beit hamikdash* and *kdushat peh* specifically therefore remains open.

### Pressing Kezayit Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָכָא נָמֵי פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָתָם דְּנָגְעוּ לְהוּ בְּרִאשׁוֹן, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ אִינְהוּ שֵׁנִי. הָכָא דְּנָגְעוּ בְּשֵׁנִי, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ שְׁלִישִׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** this answer: **Here, too,** it is to be understood that one must tread on **less than an egg-bulk at a time. And if you wish, say** this answer instead: **There,** where the Gemara requires less than an egg-bulk, it is a case where the grapes **came into contact** with an item that was impure with **first-**degree ritual impurity, such **that they became** impure with **second-**degree ritual impurity. When a liquid touches an object that is impure with second-degree ritual impurity, it becomes impure by rabbinic decree with first-degree ritual impurity. Therefore, in that case one must be careful to tread only on less than an egg-bulk at a time. **Here,** it is speaking of a case where **they came into contact** with an item that was impure with **second-**degree ritual impurity, such **that they became** impure with **third-**degree ritual impurity. In that case, the liquid that comes out of the grapes would not become ritually impure at all.

The *gemara* raises a further question: when Rabbi Yochanan permitted pressing *tamei* grapes and yielding *tahor* juice, did he mean pressing without restriction? *Dorchan v'afilu tuva* — does he permit pressing even a large amount?

The *gemara* answers: *umi amar Rabbi Yochanan hachi?* — but did Rabbi Yochanan not himself say elsewhere that *tamei* grapes can be pressed to yield *tahor* juice only if one presses *pachot pachot mi-kabi'tzah* — less than the volume of a *kezayit* at a time? This is the statement that connects back to the very beginning of the daf's discussion, and it is precisely why this entire extended passage was developed — to arrive at this statement of Rabbi Yochanan, that *anavim she'nitme'u* can be pressed to yield *tahor* juice if one presses less than a *kezayit* at a time.

The *gemara* offers two possible reconciliations. The first is the straightforward one: *ibayit eima hacha nami pachot pachot mi-kabi'tzah* — the statement here that permits pressing also assumes the rule of less than a *kezayit* at a time, and the two rulings are consistent.

The second resolution is more technical: *hatam denagu lehu b'rishon d'havu lehu inhu sheni, hacha denagu b'sheni d'havu lehu shelishi* — in the case there, the grapes were touched by a *rishon l'tumah*, making the grapes themselves *sheni*; here, the grapes were touched by a *sheni*, making the resulting juice only *shelishi*. The difference in the degree of *tumah* involved may explain why different rules apply in the two scenarios. The full elaboration of this second resolution, however, is left for the following daf.
