# Beitzah 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Fruit Outside Techum

### Intro & Mishnah Setup

We conclude *masechet* Beitzah with Daf 40, opening with the *mishnah* at the bottom of 39b. The case: a person's fruit are located in another city — that is, outside his *techum*. He is in city A; his fruit are in city B, some three thousand *amot* away.

### No Eruv Case

Because he did not make an *eruv techumim*, his *techum* ends before city B. His fruit began *Yom Tov* outside his *techum* and therefore cannot be moved toward him. Even if the residents of city B made an *eruv* allowing them to travel to city A, they still cannot bring him his fruit — because the fruit's *techum* is determined by him, and his *techum* does not reach city B. The *mishnah* states accordingly: *lo yavi'u lo* — they may not bring it to him.

### Owner's Eruv Case

However, *ve-im eirev hu, peirotav kamohu* — if he was the one who made the *eruv*, extending his *techum* into city B, then his fruit follow him. Just as he may travel to city B, so too his fruit may be transported back with him.

### Guests Scenario

The *mishnah* then presents a related case: *mi she-zimen etzlo orchim* — one who invites guests from city B to his home in city A for *Yom Tov*. Those guests made an *eruv* enabling them to walk to city A; however, the host did not make an *eruv*, since he had no need to travel. After the meal, the host cannot give the guests a package of food to take home with them to city B. His food is governed by his *techum*, which does not extend to city B. The guests may bring food *to* him — food that began *Yom Tov* in their possession may travel with them — but he cannot send his food out of his *techum* with them.

### Zikuya on Erev YT

There is, however, a solution: *ela im ken zika lahem manoteihem me-erev Yom Tov* — unless the host had already transferred ownership of the food to his guests before *Yom Tov* began. If, on *erev Yom Tov*, he designated specific food for them — a package of bottles and sandwiches, for instance — and effected a *zikuya*, a legal transfer of ownership through a third party acting on the guests' behalf, then when *Yom Tov* commenced, that food was already theirs. It therefore began *Yom Tov* governed by *their* *techum*, and they may carry it home with them.

### Techum Application

This prompts a clarifying observation: the rule that fruit or objects cannot be moved beyond a certain *techum* is not simply a function of legal title. The object's *techum* is defined by whose domain it is practically associated with when *Yom Tov* begins — and determining that relationship is precisely what drives the *gemara*'s ensuing debate.

## Rav vs Shmuel

### Entrusted Fruit

The *gemara* introduces a new case: *ha-mafkid peirot etzel chaveiro* — one who deposits fruit with a friend before *Yom Tov* for safekeeping. When *Yom Tov* begins, whose *techum* governs those fruit? The fruit are physically in city B with the guardian, while the owner is in city A. Two great *amora'im* disagree.

### Rav's View

*Rav amar keraglei mi she-hupkidu lo* — Rav holds that the fruit follow the *techum* of the one with whom they were deposited, the guardian (*nifkad*). Since the guardian has taken physical possession of the fruit, it is his domain, not the owner's, that governs their movement on *Yom Tov*.

### Shmuel's View

*u-Shmuel amar keraglei ha-mafkid* — Shmuel holds that the fruit follow the owner, the one who entrusted them. The guardian is merely an extension of the owner; it is the owner who truly retains control.

### Lending Contrast

A useful contrast is the case of lending. When one lends an object to a borrower before *Yom Tov* — and the *gemara* elsewhere indicated that even a verbal commitment to lend suffices — the object follows the borrower's *techum*. The borrower takes it for his own use; he is the one who has control and practical dominion over it. That case is therefore clear. The depositum case is more nuanced, because there is a split between who physically holds the object and who retains authority over it. The guardian holds it, but the owner remains the one with ultimate say.

### Guardian Status

The deeper issue here is not merely the technical question of *hilkhot shomrim* — at what legal moment does one become a *shomer* and assume liability — but a different, more conceptual question: to what degree has the guardian assumed a quasi-ownership relationship with the object, the kind of practical control that governs *hilkhot techumin*? Consider the shepherd: even though he is working for the owner, the *gemara* elsewhere holds that when an owner gives his flock to a shepherd before *Yom Tov*, the flock follows the shepherd's *techum*. The shepherd walks the animals, decides where they graze, and exercises genuine control over them. But if an item is merely stored in someone's house — locked away, with the guardian having no real say over it — then despite his legal liability as a *shomer*, it may well remain, for purposes of *techumin*, under the owner's domain. That is precisely where Rav and Shmuel diverge.

## Tannaim Connection

### Ox in Yard

The *gemara* proposes that this dispute between Rav and Shmuel mirrors an earlier *tannaitic* controversy. The *mishnah* discussed in this connection involves a person who brings his cow into someone else's yard. The general rule is that if he entered without permission — in effect trespassing — he bears all risk himself, and any damage done to his cow by the yard owner's animals or property creates no liability for the host. But the *mishnah* states: *im hikhnis bi-reshut ba'al ha-chatzer, chayav* — if he entered with the permission of the yard owner, then the yard owner is liable should something happen to the cow.

The logic is that by welcoming the visitor and his animal onto his property, the yard owner implicitly assumes responsibility for them. This is analogous, in some respects, to the concept of liability in hosting: once you invite someone into your domain, you undertake a degree of responsibility for what befalls them there.

*Rebbi*, however, disagrees: *l'olam eino chayav ad she-yikabel alav ba'al ha-bayit lishmor* — the yard owner is never liable merely by virtue of having extended an invitation. A formal, explicit assumption of guardianship is required.

### Halakhic Mapping

Rav Huna then transmits a ruling: *amar Rav Huna amar Rav, halakha ki-divrei chakhamim* — Rav holds like the *chakhamim*, the majority, who say that the implicit welcome suffices to impose liability. *u-Shmuel amar halakha ke-Rebbi* — Shmuel holds like Rebbi, requiring an explicit acceptance of responsibility. The *gemara* proposes that these rulings map precisely onto the depositum dispute: Rav holds that implicit circumstances create legal and relational consequences, while Shmuel insists on explicitness.

### Rav's Logic

But Rav immediately deflects: *ana de-amri afilu le-Rabbi* — I could say my ruling even according to Rebbi. Rebbi required an explicit assumption of *shemira* only in the case of welcoming someone into one's yard, because the act of invitation carries no inherent implication of assuming guardianship. Someone saying "come visit me" is not saying "I will take responsibility for your animals." The contexts are entirely different, and without more, liability does not follow. *Aval hacha, ha kibel alei netiruta* — but in the depositum case, the context *is* one of safeguarding. When someone says "here, watch my fruit," the very act of handing over the object in that context is itself an implicit assumption of *shemira*. Rav therefore argues that there is no need to say anything explicit: the nature of the interaction is self-evidently one of entrusting.

### Shmuel's Logic

Shmuel responds in kind: *ana de-amri afilu le-Rabbanan* — I could say my ruling even according to the *chakhamim* who hold that an invitation implicitly creates liability in the yard case. Why do the *chakhamim* rule that way? Because, says Shmuel, *nicha lei le-inish de-ne-kamte netirei bi-reshutei de-ba'al ha-chatzer* — it is to the *benefit* of the animal owner that the yard owner should assume responsibility. When I bring my cow into your yard and you welcome me in, I want you to be responsible for my cow. I want to be free of worry; I want the liability to shift. That desire, even when unexpressed, is obvious and reasonable, and that is why the *chakhamim* read the implicit welcome as creating liability. *Aval hacha* — but in the depositum case, says Shmuel, *mi nicha lei le-inish de-leikei peirei bi-reshutei de-chavreih?* Why should we assume the owner wants his fruit to become associated with, to fall under the domain of, his friend? That seems counterintuitive. He wants them stored, not taken over.

This is a genuinely striking argument, because superficially the *mafkid* is asking his friend to watch his fruit — yet Shmuel is saying the owner does not want those fruit to be considered under the friend's domain, even though he has literally placed them in the friend's house. What is happening here is not a dispute about technical *halakhot* of *shomrim* — about at what legal moment the duty of *shemira* attaches — but about a more conceptual question: to what degree does the *shomer*'s relationship to the object constitute a meaningful form of control, a quasi-ownership, of the kind that governs *techumin*? A *shomer* in the ordinary sense is merely an extension of the *ba'al ha-bayit*. For the object to follow the *shomer*'s *techum*, there must be something more — a degree of practical dominance over the object that rises above mere custodianship. Rav maintains this is present in any depositum; Shmuel maintains the owner retains that underlying control even while temporarily placing the object with another.

## Mishnah Proof

### Mishnah Evidence

The *gemara* attempts to bring a proof from our very *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states: *ve-im eirev hu, peirotav kamohu* — if the owner made an *eruv*, his fruit follow him. By implication, if he did not make an *eruv*, his fruit do not follow him — they are stranded. But those fruit are presumably being watched by someone in city B. If the object followed the watcher's *techum*, as Rav holds, then why does the owner's *eruv* make any difference? The fruit would go by the watcher in city B regardless. The *mishnah* seems to indicate that the fruit follow the owner — apparently vindicating Shmuel.

### Keren Zavit

Rav Huna responds on Rav's behalf: *ke-gon she-yichad lo keren zavit* — the *mishnah* speaks of a case where the owner designated a specific space for the fruit, a locked corner or closet. In that case, everyone agrees the fruit follow the owner, because the person watching them has no real control: their entire role is to stand guard over something that has been sealed away, with explicit instructions not to touch it. The practical domain remains entirely the owner's. The dispute between Rav and Shmuel, therefore, concerns only cases where no specific space was designated — where the guardian has genuine discretion over the fruit's handling and placement.

### Control Degrees

This distinction illuminates the underlying principle. When the guardian has discretion — can move the fruit, decide where to keep them, respond to changing circumstances — he exercises a form of control that approaches quasi-ownership, and Rav holds that in such cases the fruit follow his *techum*. When the guardian is merely standing watch over a sealed space, he has no such control, and even Rav concedes the fruit follow the owner. The debate thus hinges on degrees of practical control.

## Guests & Zikuya

### Transfer Problem

Returning to the *mishnah*'s case of guests and the *zikuya* effected on *erev Yom Tov*: a question arises. The host transferred ownership of the food to his guests before *Yom Tov* — but between the moment of transfer and the arrival of the guests, who is watching the food? The host is. He is effectively the guardian of the guests' food when *Yom Tov* begins. According to Rav, who holds that deposited objects follow the guardian's *techum*, why should the *zikuya* accomplish anything? The food is under the host's control when *Yom Tov* commences, so it should still follow *his* *techum* — and the guests would still be unable to take it home.

### Keren Zavit Answer

The *gemara* offers one answer: *keivan she-zika lahem al yedei acher, ke-mi she-yichad lo keren zavit dami* — since the host transferred ownership formally through another party, it is as if he designated a specific corner for the food. He has, in effect, locked it away for the guests' use and relinquished any practical control over it. It therefore follows the guests' *techum* even according to Rav.

### Zikuya Special

The *gemara* then offers a second, more fundamental answer: *zika shani* — a *zikuya* is categorically different. Rashi explains that when the host performs a *zikuya*, his entire intent is to enable the guests to take the food home. That intent itself constitutes a relinquishment of control. He is not watching the food in any meaningful sense of "control" — he is holding it temporarily for someone else, with the express purpose of handing it over. In such a case, there is no ambiguity about whose domain the food belongs to: it is clearly the guests'. The second answer thus distinguishes *zikuya* not merely as analogous to *keren zavit* but as a categorically distinct act in which the transferor explicitly steps out of the picture.

## Meat Hanging Case

### Setup

The *gemara* now presents a vivid practical case involving Rav Chana bar Chanilai. Meat was suspended — hung out to dry — at *ivra de-dasha*, the other side of the door or door-bar, on *erev Yom Tov*. Rav Chana bar Chanilai had traveled from city B to city A; some people in city A hung out meat for him to dry, and now he wishes to take it back with him to city B on *Yom Tov*. Whose *techum* governs the meat?

### Rav Huna Rules

Rav Huna ruled: *ei at talit, tishkol* — if you were the one who hung the meat out to dry, then take it. *Ve-ei inhu talu lach, lo tishkol* — but if they were the ones who hung it and managed it, then do not take it. The ruling implies that whoever had practical control over the meat when *Yom Tov* began — whoever was the active party in handling it — determines whose *techum* governs it.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* presses: even if Rav Chana bar Chanilai was the one who hung the meat, why should he be able to take it? He went back to city B before *Yom Tov* began, and the meat was left in city A, presumably under the watch of those in city A. Even granting Rav Huna's fidelity to Rav's position, the meat should follow the *techum* of those watching it in city A — not of Rav Chana bar Chanilai who is now in city B.

### Keren Zavit Applied

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking *keren zavit*: *shani ivra de-dasha de-kimei she-yikhdu keren zavit dami* — hanging meat out to dry is like designating a locked corner. The meat is suspended specifically to dry; it is not to be touched or moved. Whoever is present in city A has no control over it. Their role is entirely passive — do not disturb the drying meat. It is therefore like a sealed space, and everyone agrees the meat follows the owner, not the passive watchers.

### Ravina Challenges

Ravina then poses a three-part challenge to Rav Ashi: if the others were the ones who hung the meat and controlled it, how can you say Rav Chana bar Chanilai cannot take it?

### Fattened Ox

Ravina's first point invokes the *shor shel pitam* — the fattened ox. Shmuel ruled that an ox being fattened for sale follows *ke-raglei kol adam*, the *techum* of all people — because the entire point of fattening it is to give it to whoever comes to purchase it on *Yom Tov*. The object is not really "belonging" to the owner in the usual sense; its whole purpose is to be transferred to a buyer. Similarly here: those who hung the meat for Rav Chana bar Chanilai did so entirely for his benefit and with the expectation that he would come to collect it on *Yom Tov*. If the meat was prepared with the intent that he would pick it up, it should follow his *techum* regardless of who physically prepared it. Ravina's second point appeals to the *baraita* of Rabbi Dosa: Rabbah bar bar Chanah said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the *halakha* follows Rabbi Dosa, who holds that even if one did not entrust his flock to the shepherd before *Yom Tov*, but the sheep normally go to a shepherd, the sheep follow the shepherd's *techum*. If the normal expectation is that Rav Chana bar Chanilai will come pick up the meat — just as sheep normally go to the shepherd — then the meat should follow him. Ravina's third point is a reading of our *mishnah*'s principle *ha-behemah ve-ha-kelim ke-raglei ha-be'alim* — that it goes by the owners — which he reads through the lens of his previous arguments to suggest that the effective owner, the one for whom the item is destined, determines the *techum*.

### Rav Ashi Response

Rav Ashi responds: *ela shani Rav Chanana bar Chanilai* — you have misread this case entirely. Rav Huna's ruling to Rav Chana bar Chanilai had nothing to do with *techumin* at all. *Gavra raba hu ve-tarid bi-shma'ateta* — he was a great Torah scholar, always absorbed in learning, and characteristically inattentive to practical matters.

### Kashrus Issue

What Rav Huna was actually addressing was a *kashrut* concern: *ei at talit, is lach simana be-gaveih* — if you were the one who hung the meat, you have a *siman*, a distinguishing mark or recognition, and you know it is the same meat you originally handled. You would not have taken your attention off it. But *ve-ei inhu talu lach, maschas da'atach mineih* — if others hung the meat for you and you were not present, your attention was diverted from it; it is a case of *basar she-nis'alem min ha-ayin*, meat that has been out of sight and unattended. In that case, *lo tishkol* — you may not take it, not because of *techumin*, but because of the *kashrut* concern about unattended meat. The entire exchange with Rav Chana bar Chanilai was about whether the meat could be identified as the same meat originally set out — a question of *kashrut*, not *techumin* at all.

## Control Theme

### Ownership to Control

The long discussion across this *daf* reflects a fundamental conceptual shift in how *techumin* operate: the governing criterion is not formal legal ownership but practical control.

### Examples

This emerges from multiple cases. When one promises to lend an object to another — even before physically handing it over — the object follows the borrower's *techum*, because the relationship of control and anticipated use has already taken effect. When one prepares a *shor shel pitam* to be given to a buyer on *Yom Tov*, it follows *ke-raglei kol adam*, not the owner's *techum*, because the owner has effectively relinquished meaningful control in anticipation of the transfer. When food is transferred to guests via *zikuya* on *erev Yom Tov*, it follows the guests' *techum* because the transferor has explicitly stepped aside.

### Core Principle

The core principle that emerges is: *techumin* go by who controls, not merely who owns. Formal ownership is relevant insofar as it tracks real-world control, but where ownership and control diverge — where someone else has meaningful practical dominion over an object — it is that dominion which determines the object's *techum*. The debate between Rav and Shmuel in the depositum case is therefore a debate about when guardianship rises to the level of meaningful control for the purposes of this principle.

## Domestic vs Wild

### Mishnah Rule

The final *mishnah* of *masechet* Beitzah returns us to *muktzah*, the theme that opened the entire tractate. *Ein mashkin ve-shochtin et ha-midbariyot* — one may not water or slaughter wilderness animals on *Yom Tov*. *Aval mashkin ve-shochtin et ha-bayashot* — but one may water and slaughter domestic animals.

### Watering Advice

One might ask what watering has to do with the prohibition: surely the primary question is *shechita*, not watering. The *gemara* explains that the *mishnah*'s mention of watering is incidental practical advice: *mishum sircha de-mishcha* — because of the adhesions of the hide. Giving an animal a great deal of water to drink immediately before *shechita* makes it significantly easier to flay the hide afterward. The *mishnah* is therefore not legislating about watering as a separate category but offering a *milta agav urchiah*, a practical recommendation embedded in the ruling: if you are going to slaughter a domestic animal on *Yom Tov*, water it well first.

### Domestic Def

What counts as a *beheimah bayit*, a domestic animal? The *baraita* defines it as *kol she-yotzot ve-ro'ot chutz la-techum u-va'ot ve-lanot be-tokh ha-techum* — animals that graze outside the city limits during the day but return every night to sleep within the *techum*, within the inhabited area of the town.

### Wilderness Def

*Behemot midbariyot* are defined as *kol she-yotzot be-Pesach ve-ro'ot ba-afar ve-nikhnasot bi-revi'ah rishonah* — animals that leave the home territory at *Pesach* time, graze out in the wilderness for the entire summer, and do not return until the first rains of *Marcheshvan*, the beginning of the winter rainy season. They spend approximately six months of the year entirely away from their owners. These animals, because they are so remote from their owner's domain when *Yom Tov* arrives, are considered *muktzah* and may not be slaughtered on *Yom Tov*.

## Muktzah Debate

### Rebbi & Muktzah

The *gemara* then raises a striking question: *u-mi it lei le-Rebbi muktzah?* — does Rebbi actually hold of the concept of *muktzah*? Rebbi is associated with the position that *muktzah* is an extremely narrow category. Why is he participating in a debate that seems to presuppose *muktzah* as a relevant concern?

### Unripe Dates

To understand Rebbi's position, the *gemara* cites a related exchange. Rabbi Shimon bar Rebbi — Rebbi's own son — once asked Rebbi about the case of *ptzilei tamrah*, unripe dates, as it pertained to the view of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: do the unripe dates constitute *muktzah* according to Rabbi Shimon?

### Rashi vs Tosafot

The precise nature of these *ptzilei tamrah* is itself a matter of dispute between Rashi and Tosafot. Rashi holds they are dates picked before ripening and placed in a wicker basket to ripen; the process requires waiting. Tosafot holds they are dates placed on a rooftop to dry out and become edible — a process of desiccation rather than ripening. Both agree, however, that the dates are not yet edible and are being processed toward edibility.

### R Shimon View

Rebbi's answer to his son was: *ein muktzah le-Rabbi Shimon ela grogrot ve-tzimukim bilvad* — Rabbi Shimon recognizes *muktzah* only in the single case of drying figs and raisins. In that case, one takes fruit that was edible — grapes, figs — and deliberately places it in a state of temporary inedibility in order to transform it into something else. One consciously *pushes the fruit away* from its current edible state. That is the paradigm of *muktzah* for Rabbi Shimon. But the *ptzilei tamrah* are different: they were never edible, and the entire process is oriented toward making them edible as soon as possible. There is no "pushing away" — quite the opposite. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon would not consider them *muktzah* at all. The distinction Tosafot draws is particularly elegant: in the *grogrot* case, one has actively chosen *not* to eat the fruit as fruit, converting it into something else; in the *ptzilei tamrah* case, one is actively working to bring the item into edibility.

### Rebbi Resolution

Given this framework, the *gemara* offers three possible resolutions of the question whether Rebbi holds *muktzah*. First: *hanei nami ki-grogrot u-tzimukin dami* — these wilderness animals are indeed analogous to drying figs and raisins. The owner sends them out at *Pesach* time and they do not return until the rainy season; they are completely removed from his practical domain for half the year. Until they are actually slaughtered and brought to market, they are in a state of being wholly "pushed away" — more so, one might argue, than figs being converted to raisins. In that case, even Rebbi, following Rabbi Shimon's own logic, would consider them *muktzah*. Second: *li-dvarav de-Rabbi Shimon ka-amar ve-lo sevira lei* — Rebbi was not stating his own position but answering the question as put to him: "according to Rabbi Shimon, would the dates be *muktzah*?" Rebbi answered, no, according to Rabbi Shimon they would not be. His own view may differ entirely. Third: *li-dvreihem de-Rabbanan ka-amar* — Rebbi was addressing the *rabbinic* position, not his own. His own view, *le-didi ein muktzah* — in the sense that he holds of an extremely minimal category of *muktzah* — remains fully intact. He was simply telling the *rabbanan*: even granted your broader approach to *muktzah*, you should at least concede that animals which return in the rainy season are domestic, not wilderness animals, and not *muktzah*. The *rabbanan* respond: *lo mi-havei havu* — no, we hold of *muktzah*, and we hold of a broad category, and those animals that spend six months away are wilderness animals, and they are *muktzah*.

### Masechet End

With this exchange — *Rabbanan* insisting on the broad scope of *muktzah*, Rebbi pressing for a more limited application — *masechet* Beitzah comes to its close. *Hadran alakh mashilin peirot u-selikah masekhta.*
