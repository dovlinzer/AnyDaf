# Bava Metzia 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Price Fixing & Ownership

### Yatza HaSha'ar Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין פּוֹסְקִין עַל הַפֵּירוֹת עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא הַשַּׁעַר. יָצָא הַשַּׁעַר – פּוֹסְקִין, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לָזֶה יֵשׁ לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not set** a price with a buyer **for** the future delivery of **produce until the** market **rate is publicized,** as, if he is paid for supplying produce at a later date in advance of the publication of the market rate for that type of produce, he may set a price that is too low. The money paid in advance is deemed a loan, and if the initial payment was lower than the later market value, delivery of the produce will constitute interest on the loan. Once **the** market **rate is publicized,** the seller **may set** a price, even if the produce is not yet in his possession. The reason for this is that **even though this one,** i.e., the seller, **does not have** any of the produce, **that one,** someone else, **has** it, and the seller could theoretically acquire the produce now at the price he set.

The *mishna* here, toward the end of the chapter, returns to themes introduced earlier: the permissibility of pre-purchasing fruit at the early-season low price for delivery later in the season when prices are higher. The underlying concern is *ribbis* — is such an arrangement effectively interest? The *mishna* establishes that this is permitted, provided two conditions are met. First, a market price must have been set — *yatza hashaar*. Second, the fruit must either be owned by the seller or be available in the marketplace. The logic is that since the buyer could in principle purchase the fruit at that lower price today, there is no real benefit conferred by the later delivery; the higher future value merely reflects market movement, not a lending relationship.

The *mishna* opens: *ein poskin al hapeiros ad she-yatza hashaar* — one may not fix an arrangement for fruits until the market price has been established. Once it has been established, *poskin af al pi she-ein lazeh yesh lazeh* — the arrangement may be made even if the seller himself does not have possession of the fruit, since others do, and the seller can readily acquire it.

### Early Harvesters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה הוּא תְּחִילָּה לַקּוֹצְרִים, פּוֹסֵק עִמּוֹ עַל הַגָּדִישׁ, וְעַל הֶעָבִיט שֶׁל עֲנָבִים, וְעַל הַמַּעֲטָן שֶׁל זֵיתִים, וְעַל הַבֵּיצִים שֶׁל יוֹצֵר, וְעַל הַסִּיד מִשֶּׁשִּׁקְּעוֹ בַּכִּבְשָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** If the seller **was first among the reapers,** having harvested his crop before the market rate was set, he **may set** a price **with** a buyer as he wishes **for a stack** of grain that is already in his possession, **or for** a large **basket of grapes** prepared for pressing into wine, **or for a vat [*hama'atan*] of olives** prepared for pressing into oil, **or for the clumps [*habeitzim*]** of clay prepared for use **by a potter, or for plaster** nearing the end of the manufacturing process at the point **after he has sunk it,** i.e., baked it, **in the kiln.**

The *mishna* continues with a case involving those who harvest early, before a general market price has been set. The rule is *posik immo al hagdish* — one may make an arrangement with such a farmer against the pile of wheat he has already harvested. The same applies to a container of wine (*avit shel anavim*), pressed olives (*maatan shel zeitim*), balls of clay ready to be turned into pottery (*beitzim shel yotzer*), and plaster once it has been placed into the kiln (*sid mishekia b'kivshano*). In each of these cases, the product is substantially ready and in the seller's possession, even if the general market price has not yet been established. What permits the arrangement is that the seller actually holds the product — since he could deliver it now, the arrangement for later delivery does not take on the character of a loan.

This raises a subtle question: does the seller's possession of the goods specifically allow this particular transaction, or does it simply provide enough of the formal features of a sale to eliminate the *ribbis* concern — even if the actual product delivered later need not be the identical product now in hand? This question will surface again in later cases.

### Dung Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפוֹסֵק עִמּוֹ עַל הַזֶּבֶל כׇּל יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵין פּוֹסְקִין עַל הַזֶּבֶל אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיְתָה לוֹ זֶבֶל בָּאַשְׁפָּה, וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And he may set** a price **with** a buyer **for manure** on **any** of **the days of the year,** as the manure will certainly be available and it is therefore viewed as if it is ready. **Rabbi Yosei says: One may set** the price **of manure only if he** already **had** a pile **of manure in** his **dunghill** to which the sale can immediately be applied, **but the Rabbis permit it** in all cases.

The *mishna* includes a further rule: *uposik immo al hazavel kol yemot hashana* — one may arrange for the delivery of dung at any time of year, presumably because dung is always available and in unlimited supply. *Rabbi Yosi*, however, dissents: *ein poskin al hazavel ela im ken haytah lo zavel ba-ashpah* — even dung, he holds, requires that the seller actually own the dung himself, located on his property. The debate turns on whether dung falls into the category of *yatza hashaar* — so widely available that no ownership is required — or whether, despite its ubiquity, it remains a case where the seller must have the product in hand. *Vachachamim matirin* — the Sages permit it without requiring personal ownership.

### Sha'ar HaGavoa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפוֹסֵק עִמּוֹ בְּשַׁעַר הַגָּבוֹהַּ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַק עִמּוֹ בְּשַׁעַר הַגָּבוֹהַּ, יָכוֹל לוֹמַר: תֵּן לִי כָּזֶה, אוֹ תֵּן לִי אֶת מְעוֹתַי.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may** also **set** a price **with** a buyer **at the highest rate,** i.e., a large amount of produce sold for the lowest price, stipulating with the seller that the sale price match the lowest market rate for this product during the course of the year. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even if he did not set** a price **with him** beforehand **at the highest rate,** the buyer **may say** to the seller: **Give me** the produce **at this** rate **or give me** back **my money.** Since he did not formally acquire the produce, if the price changed he may withdraw from the transaction.

The *mishna* introduces the concept of *sha'ar hagavoa*, which despite its literal sound ("high price") actually refers to a favorable rate — that is, more units per dollar. The terminology is counterintuitive: a *sha'ar hagavoa* means a lower price per unit, since more is obtained for the same outlay.

The case is as follows. A buyer gives money now, at a time when prices are low (say, four units per dollar), for later delivery. He and the seller arrange not only that delivery will occur at today's low prices, but also that if prices drop further between now and delivery, the buyer will receive the benefit of that lower price as well. In other words, if prices fall to six units per dollar, the buyer gets six. The buyer is therefore insulated from loss: if prices rise, he benefits from having locked in today's rate; if prices fall, he benefits from the even lower rate at delivery. The Gemara elsewhere characterizes such an arrangement as *karov liskar verachok mehefsed* — near to profit, far from loss. Nevertheless, this is permitted.

### Rabbi Yehuda Opinion

*Rabbi Yehuda* goes a step further. Even if no explicit *sha'ar hagavoa* arrangement was made, he holds that the buyer may still demand the lower price at the time of delivery. His reasoning: since the buyer could at any point say, "forget the delivery, just return my money" — because the sale was never fully final — and then use that money to purchase at the lower prevailing price, he is effectively always entitled to the lower price. *Yachol lomar lo ten li kazeh o ten li ma'osai* — he can say either give me goods at today's rate or return my money.

This ruling highlights the somewhat unusual nature of these transactions. Even though they are structured as sales rather than loans, they carry features that push toward the border of *ribbis*: the buyer gives money now, likely profits from rising prices, and under *Rabbi Yehuda*'s view is also protected from loss.

## Market vs Wholesale Price

### Rav Assi Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין פּוֹסְקִין עַל הַשַּׁעַר שֶׁבַּשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One may not set** a price for the future delivery of produce **at the** current **market rate** because the market is not sufficiently stable.

The *Gemara* opens with a statement by Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ein poskin ela besha'ar shebashuk* — one may not make such an arrangement except against the price established in the marketplace. This appears to flatly contradict the *mishna*, which permits the arrangement once *yatza hashaar*. The question is whether Rav Assi is drawing a distinction — perhaps between a price established at the wholesale or farm level, which is what the *mishna* addresses, and a retail market price.

### Village vs Major Markets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי אַסִּי: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֲפִילּוּ כַּדּוֹרְמוּס הַזֶּה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֶלָּא בַּשּׁוּק שֶׁל עֲיָירוֹת, דְּלָא קְבִיעִי תַּרְעַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Asi:** Does **Rabbi Yoḥanan state** this ruling **even with regard to** the rate of **this** large **central market [*dormus*]?** Rabbi Asi **said to him: Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** this ruling **only with regard to the** small-**town markets, since their rates are not fixed,** as smaller markets have greater sensitivity to fluctuations in price.

Rav Zeira presses Rav Assi: would Rabbi Yochanan say this even in the case of a place like *Durmis* — a major, established marketplace whose prices would be widely known and stable? Surely there the price can be relied upon. Rav Assi responds: no — Rabbi Yochanan's restriction was directed only at *shuk shel ayarot*, the small village markets, *delo kvi'i tar'ai hi*, where prices are not fixed and fluctuate from village to village and even within a single village. In such settings, a stated "market price" is not sufficiently stable to serve as the basis for an arrangement. But in a major marketplace with genuinely stable pricing, even Rabbi Yochanan would agree the arrangement is permissible.

### Warehouse Pricing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דִּסְלֵיק אַדַּעְתִּין מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֲפִילּוּ כַּדּוֹרְמוּס הַזֶּה, אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין דְּקָתָנֵי: אֵין פּוֹסְקִין עַל הַפֵּירוֹת עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא הַשַּׁעַר, יָצָא הַשַּׁעַר פּוֹסְקִין, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? מַתְנִיתִין בְּחִיטֵּי דְּאַכְּלַבֵּי וְאַרְבֵּי, דִּמְשִׁוךְ תַּרְעַיְהוּ טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to what we thought initially, that Rabbi Yoḥanan stated** this ruling **even with regard to this large** central **market, but** then there is a difficulty with **the mishna, which teaches: One may not set** a price with a buyer **for** the future delivery of **produce until the** market **rate is publicized.** By inference, once **the** market **rate is publicized, one may set** a price. If Rabbi Yochanan's ruling applies even to large central markets, **how can you find these** circumstances? The Gemara answers: The **mishna** may be speaking **about wheat** that comes from large **warehouses and** from **ships, as their rate lasts longer,** since this merchandise comes to market in very large quantities.

The *Gemara* then asks: if the *mishna*'s permission is not based on major retail market prices, what sort of price does it contemplate? The answer is *bechitti de'achli v'arfi*, grain that is held in warehouses and on shipping vessels, *demashach tar'eihu tfei* — whose prices are more stable over time. This is essentially a wholesale or storage-level pricing, where large quantities are held and prices do not shift with the daily fluctuations of the retail market. This is the kind of stable, accessible pricing on which a legitimate forward arrangement can be based.

## New vs Old Grain

### New-to-Old Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ חֲדָשׁוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע וִישָׁנוֹת מִשָּׁלֹשׁ – אֵין פּוֹסְקִין עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא הַשַּׁעַר לֶחָדָשׁ וְלַיָּשָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** If the **new** grain **was** selling **at** the rate of **four** *se'a* for a *sela* **and the old** grain was selling **at three, one may not set** the price according to the price of the new grain **until the** market **rate is publicized** both **for the new and for the old** grain. By the time payment is made, the new grain will not be entirely new and its price will be the same as that of the old grain.

The *Gemara* presents a further complication. Suppose new grain is selling at four units per dollar and old grain at three units per dollar. Buyers prefer aged grain because it lasts longer and is better suited to grinding; hence old grain commands a premium. Now, a buyer wishes to pay today's price for new grain and take delivery months later — by which time the grain will have aged and become old grain.

The problem is more than a simple price-appreciation concern. When the *mishna* permits buying now at today's price for later delivery, the underlying rationale is that the buyer could have taken possession of the same product today. But here, what will be delivered later is categorically different from what is available now. The product available today is new grain; the product to be delivered is old grain, which today costs more. One cannot say the buyer could simply have bought it himself today at the agreed price and waited — because buying the aged product today would require paying the higher price. The transaction thus cannot be validated by the standard *yatza hashaar* logic. Accordingly: *ein poskin ad she-yatza hashaar lachadash velayashan* — one may not make this arrangement until the price has been established for both new and old grain alike.

### Rashi's Solution

Rashi explains this tersely stated *Gemara* as follows: the arrangement may only be made for new grain with old-grain delivery if the prices of the two are the same, or alternatively if the buyer is willing to pay today's price for old grain — the more expensive product — in which case there is no concern of receiving something worth more than what was paid. What cannot be done is to pay the current lower price of new grain and then receive aged grain worth more. The mismatch between the product priced and the product delivered is what creates the problem.

## Gathered vs Farm Grain

### Gathered Grain Prices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ לָקוֹטוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע, וּלְכׇל אָדָם מִשָּׁלֹשׁ – אֵין פּוֹסְקִין עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא הַשַּׁעַר לַלּוֹקֵט וְלַמּוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **if the** produce sold by **gleaners** who gather wheat from various fields, the quality of which is low, is selling **at** the rate of **four** *se'a* of wheat for a *sela* **and** that **of every** other **person** is selling **at** the rate of **three** *se'a* of wheat for a *sela*, one **may not set** a price at the gleaners' rate **until the** market **rate is publicized** both **for** wheat sold by **a gleaner and for** wheat sold by an ordinary **seller.**

The *Gemara* introduces another distinction. There were individuals who would go from field to field gathering grain — an activity reminiscent of *leket*, gleaning. These gatherers would sometimes collect more than they needed for their own households and sell the surplus. The price of this gathered grain was lower: four units per dollar, versus three units per dollar for grain sold by ordinary householders. The reason for the lower price, as Rashi explains, is quality: grain gathered from the ground tends to be mixed, uneven, and of generally inferior quality compared to grain purchased directly from a farmer.

The question is whether a buyer can make a forward arrangement with a regular householder at the lower price of the gathered grain — reasoning that since he could go buy gathered grain at that rate, the arrangement at four per dollar should be valid. The *Gemara* states: *ein poskin ad she-yatza hashaar lilkot velimocher* — one may not make such an arrangement until the price for gathered grain and farmer's grain are the same.

The reason requires explanation. The *mishna*'s permission to arrange at today's price even without the seller possessing the product rests on the idea that the buyer could purchase that product himself in the market. But which market? The gathered grain market and the householder's grain market are two distinct markets with different price points. If the buyer is contracting with a householder — a person of ordinary standing — the implicit expectation is that he will receive householder-quality grain. He cannot invoke the lower gathered-grain price as though it were interchangeable.

### Rav Nachman Allow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: פּוֹסְקִין לַלָּקוֹטוֹת כְּשַׁעַר הַלָּקוֹטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: One may set** a price **for gleaners** to deliver produce in the future **at the gleaners' rate.**

Rav Nachman draws a limited distinction: *poskin le-lakutos keshaar le-lakutos* — if both parties to the arrangement are themselves gatherers, one may make the deal at the gathered-grain price. The reasoning follows the same logic as the *mishna*'s general permission: a gatherer who doesn't currently have grain can turn around and borrow from a fellow gatherer, so the product is effectively accessible. Within that community and market, the arrangement is valid.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מַאי שְׁנָא לָקוֹט דְּאִי לֵית לֵיהּ יָזֵיף מִלָּקוֹט חַבְרֵיהּ, בַּעַל הַבַּיִת נָמֵי יְזֵיף מִלָּקוֹט!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: What is different** about **a gleaner,** who you hold can immediately set his price at the gleaners' rate? **As, if he has no** produce **he can borrow it from another gleaner,** and therefore it is viewed as though it were in his possession. **A homeowner as well** should be able to set a price at the gleaners' rate, as, if he has no grain he can **borrow from a gleaner.**

Rava challenges Rav Nachman: if that is the rationale — that the seller can obtain the product from others like himself — then why should the same logic not permit a householder-seller to rely on gathered grain prices? Let the householder, who lacks the product, simply turn around and purchase from the gatherers at their lower price and deliver accordingly. *Mai shna lokeit de-ei leis lei yazif milokeit chavrei... baal habayis nami yazif milakutos?* — What is different about the gatherer that if he lacks product he borrows from a fellow gatherer? So too, a householder could borrow from gatherers!

### Quality Expectations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת זִילָא בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא לְמֵיזַף מִלָּקוֹט. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאן דְּיָהֵיב זוּזֵי לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת – אַפֵּירֵי שַׁפִּירֵי יָהֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** It is **degrading** for **a homeowner to borrow from a gleaner.** Consequently, there is a need to establish a market rate for ordinary sellers. And **if you wish, say** instead: **One who gives money to a homeowner** to buy his grain **gives** the money in return **for quality produce,** and he does not want the inferior produce the homeowner could borrow from gleaners.

The *Gemara* offers two answers. The first: *baal habayis zila bei milsa lemizaf milakutos* — a householder would find it beneath his dignity to go purchase from gatherers, who occupied a lower social station. This market, while technically open, is functionally off-limits to him; he would not in practice engage in such a transaction. Therefore he cannot be said to have ready access to that product at that price.

The alternative answer, *iba'is eima*: even if the householder could bring himself to purchase from gatherers, the buyer would not accept it. When a person gives money to a householder for grain, the implicit understanding — *man de-yahiv zuzei le-baal habayis apiri shapir yahiv* — is that he will receive quality, farmer-grade grain. To deliver gathered, mixed-quality grain in its place would violate that implicit expectation. Either the seller's reluctance or the buyer's expectation of quality independently explains why the lower gathered-grain price cannot be used in a transaction between ordinary parties.

## Borrowing & Fixed Prices

### Rav Sheshet Forbids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אֵין לֹוִין עַל שַׁעַר שֶׁבַּשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says** that **Rav Huna says: One may not borrow** produce based **on the market rate,** meaning that one may not purchase produce on credit with an agreement to pay for it later at the future market price, even though there is grain sold at this price in another location.

Rav Sheshet, citing Rav Huna, states: *ein lovin al hashaar she-bashuk* — one may not borrow against the market price. The precise meaning of this prohibition is not immediately obvious, and Rashi offers two distinct explanations.

### First Explanation

The first explanation connects directly to the permitted arrangement discussed throughout: what is forbidden is calling such a transaction a *loan* rather than a *sale*. If Reuven gives Shimon a hundred dollars and they agree that if Shimon cannot return the hundred dollars in cash he will pay back an equivalent quantity of grain at today's prices, they have structured it explicitly as a loan. But when Shimon repays months later with grain, that grain — measured at today's prices — will be worth more than the hundred dollars originally lent. By naming it a loan and denominating the repayment in kind based on today's rates, the extra value becomes *ribbis*. The same transaction structured as a *sale* — I am pre-purchasing grain at today's price — would be permitted. The prohibition thus concerns not the substance of the deal but its framing.

### Yatza HaSha'ar Logic

The second explanation engages the *se'ah be-se'ah* problem directly. Ordinarily, borrowing a measure of grain and repaying a measure later is forbidden because the price may have risen in the interim; you borrowed something worth ten and repaid something worth twelve. One recognized exception is when the borrower already owns the product — *yesh lo* — in which case he could in theory return it immediately, so the later return at a higher price is not considered a gain.

The question Rav Sheshet addresses is whether *yatza hashaar* — the establishment of a public market price — can serve as an equivalent to *yesh lo*. The reasoning would be: since the product is readily available at a fixed price, it is as though the borrower already possesses it; he can go to the market at any moment and acquire it. If so, borrowing a bag of flour and repaying later ought to be permitted whenever there is a stable, fixed public price for flour.

Rav Sheshet and Rav Huna say no — *yatza hashaar* alone does not create the equivalence of ownership. One cannot borrow on the basis of a fixed market price as though that price confers constructive possession.

This second reading has significant practical implications: it would potentially explain why one may ordinarily borrow a cup of flour from a neighbor. If the stores are open and prices are fixed, perhaps *yatza hashaar* effectively renders the borrower a notional owner of that flour — making repayment permissible even if the price has since risen. Rav Sheshet's ruling pushes back against that reasoning.

### Rav Huna Changes Mind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵי בַּר אַבָּא לְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וּמִי אָמַר רַב הוּנָא הָכִי? וְהָא בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא: הָנֵי בְּנֵי בֵּי רַב, דְּיָזְפִי בְּתִשְׁרִי וּפָרְעִי בְּטֵבֵת, שְׁרֵי אוֹ אֲסִיר? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָא חִיטֵּי בְּהִינֵי וְהָא חִיטֵּי בְּשִׁילֵי, אִי בָּעֵי זָבְנִי וּפָרְעִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef bar Ḥama said to Rav Sheshet, and some say that** it was **Rav Yosei bar Abba** who said **to Rav Sheshet: And did Rav Huna say this? But** didn't it occur that the Sages **asked Rav Huna:** With regard to **those students** of Torah **who borrow** food **in** the month of **Tishrei and pay** for it **in Tevet** at the rate in effect then, is this **permitted or prohibited?** Rav Huna **said to them: There is wheat in** the town called **Hini and there is wheat in** the town called **Shili, and if** the students **want** to **they can buy** wheat there **and pay** the lender immediately, and since they can pay at any time, it is permitted.

The *Gemara* then presents a challenge to Rav Huna's position. Rav Yosef bar Chama (others attribute it to Rav Yosei bar Abba) asked Rav Sheshet: did Rav Huna truly say this? For Rav Huna was once asked about *benei bei Rav* — yeshiva students — who would borrow in *Tishrei* and repay in *Tevet*. Rav Huna responded that this was permissible, pointing to the grain readily available in *Hini* and *Shili* — the markets were open and the product was there. That ruling sounds precisely like permitting a loan on the basis of the market price!

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא סָבַר רַב הוּנָא אֵין לֹוִין, כֵּיוָן דְּשַׁמְעַהּ לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹוִין, אָמַר אִיהוּ נָמֵי: לֹוִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Initially, Rav Huna thought** that **one may not borrow** produce in this manner, **but when he heard that Rabbi Shmuel bar Ḥiyya says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: One may borrow** produce in this manner, he retracted his previously stated opinion and **he also said** that **one may borrow** produce in this manner.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *ei ba'u zimni upari'ei lei me-ikara* — if they wanted, they could have repaid immediately. Because the product was so immediately accessible, it was treated as though they already possessed it, and the arrangement was therefore more like a *yesh lo* situation than a pure market-price arrangement. The *Gemara* then reports that Rav Huna originally held the stricter position but changed his mind upon hearing a ruling transmitted in the name of Shmuel that permitted such borrowing — and adopted Shmuel's position. Notably, the *Gemara* does not record that Rav Huna was persuaded by a new argument; the weight of Shmuel's authority was sufficient.

The practical upshot of the second reading of Rashi is particularly instructive for the everyday case of borrowing from a neighbor: one may do so when the stores are open and the price is fixed, because the ready availability of the product at a stable price is sufficient to treat the borrower as a constructive owner.

## Market Arbitrage

### Merchant Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמּוֹלִיךְ חֲבִילָה מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, מְצָאוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ: תְּנָהּ לִי וַאֲנִי אַעֲלֶה לְךָ כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁמַּעֲלִין לְךָ בְּאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who transports a package** of goods **from one place,** where he bought it inexpensively, **to another place,** where the price is higher, in order to sell it at a profit, and **another found him** on the way **and said to him: Give me** the package, **and I will pay you in the manner that they pay you in that place** to which you are going,

The *Gemara* now presents the case of *hamolich chavila mimakom lemakom* — a merchant transporting wares from one location to another, where the price differential is significant. Suppose goods sell for one dollar per unit in Oshkosh but two dollars per unit in Manhattan. I encounter the merchant and say: *tna-li v'ani a-aleh lecha kederech she-ma'alim lecha be-oso makom* — give them to me, and I will pay you at the Manhattan price, i.e., two dollars per unit.

Why would the buyer agree to pay double the current local price? Because by acquiring the goods now, the buyer gains access to funds — he can conduct business in Manhattan, deploy those resources, and pay back the seller at the Manhattan rate later. The seller, meanwhile, is relieved of the labor and risk of transporting the goods himself. The question is how to characterize this arrangement: is it a *purchase* at an agreed price, or is it in substance a *loan* — the seller extending credit (in the form of goods) to the buyer, who pays "interest" in the form of the price premium?

### Liability Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר – מוּתָּר, בִּרְשׁוּת לוֹקֵחַ – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** then, if the package remains **in the possession of the seller,** i.e., the seller accepts upon himself responsibility for any accidental damage that occurs along the way, it is **permitted,** as the transaction is not a loan. But if it is **in the possession of the buyer,** meaning that the buyer accepts responsibility for accidental damage, then the transaction is **prohibited,** as it is considered a loan with interest.

The *Gemara* provides a decisive criterion: *birshus mocher mutar, birshus loke'ach assur* — if the goods remain in the seller's domain and responsibility, the arrangement is permitted; if they pass into the buyer's domain, it is forbidden.

If the seller retains liability — meaning that if the goods are damaged or lost in transit the loss falls on him — then the buyer has not truly purchased them yet. The buyer is in effect serving as the seller's agent, transporting the goods to Manhattan on his behalf, and only upon arrival and sale does the money change hands. At that point the buyer holds two hundred dollars of the seller's money and repays two hundred dollars — no gain, no *ribbis*.

If, however, the buyer takes liability upon himself — meaning he now bears the risk of loss — then he has acquired ownership of goods worth one hundred dollars while agreeing to pay two hundred dollars later. That deferred payment at a higher rate is the paradigm of *ribbis*: he received something worth one hundred, and the additional hundred in the later payment reflects the time value of the seller's money. This is forbidden.

Tosafot raises an immediate difficulty even with the permitted case: even if the buyer never formally acquires the goods, he has done the seller a significant favor by transporting them to the higher-price market. Why would the buyer provide this service for free? Clearly, the seller is compensating him — and that compensation is the buyer's opportunity to use the seller's money while in transit to Manhattan. Does that not itself constitute *ribbis*? The *Gemara* does not address this directly, but Tosafot raises it as a real concern requiring the answer elaborated below.

### Reputation Benefit

Rashi explains the permitted case on the basis of a different logic. The seller pre-commits to a large transaction at the lower local price, which publicly identifies him as a serious merchant with substantial means. When he arrives at the purchasing market flush with committed funds, his creditworthiness and standing are apparent, and sellers are willing to deal with him on favorable terms. This reputational benefit is what the seller provides to the buyer in exchange for the favorable price, and Rashi insists it is not interest in the halakhic sense — because the benefit does not flow from the *delay* in repayment. Even if payment were required immediately, the buyer would still benefit from the public knowledge that he had cash available. *Ribbis* is defined as compensation for waiting; a benefit that accrues independently of the passage of time does not constitute *ribbis*.

This reasoning is admittedly strained. The benefit — having access to the seller's money while traveling — sounds functionally identical to the benefit that interest ordinarily compensates. Nevertheless, this is Rashi's position.

### Tosafot Service Model

Tosafot is unconvinced by Rashi's reasoning and proposes a structurally different reading of the permitted case. On Tosafot's account, the buyer takes the seller's money and goes to the lower-price market — Oshkosh — and purchases the goods *for the seller*, using the seller's funds. From the moment of purchase, the goods belong to the seller; if anything happens to them en route back, the loss falls on the seller. The buyer has effectively provided a purchasing service — acquiring goods in a cheaper market on the seller's behalf — and the seller compensates him for that service by allowing him the use of the funds for the duration of the journey.

This reframes the transaction as a service arrangement rather than a loan: the buyer earns not interest but a fee for labor and logistics. The benefit the buyer receives — temporary access to the funds — is limited and tied to the service rendered, not to an ongoing lending relationship. Tosafot concedes that this reading is not explicit in the *Gemara*, but considers it necessary to avoid the *ribbis* problems that Rashi's account leaves unresolved.

## Donkey Drivers Case

### Pre-Selling Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַחַמָּרִין מַעֲלִים בִּמְקוֹם הַיּוֹקֶר כְּבִמְקוֹם הַזּוֹל, וְאֵינָן חוֹשְׁשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** But **donkey drivers** who transport merchandise from one place to another may accept money and **set** prices **in a place** where goods are sold at **expensive** prices **according to** the rate in effect in **another place,** where goods are sold at **inexpensive** prices, **and need not be concerned,** as this practice is permitted.

The *Gemara* describes a practice of *chamorin* — merchant-transporters who owned donkeys and made their livelihood moving goods between markets. In a high-price market (Manhattan, where goods fetch two dollars per unit), these merchants would pre-sell their wares at the lower price (one dollar per unit, as in Oshkosh). They had not yet gone to acquire the goods but were committing to deliver at the lower price. The *Gemara* states *veinam chosheshin* — and they need not be concerned about *ribbis*.

Why is this not a problem? The buyers in Manhattan are giving money now and will receive goods worth more at the local price. If this were framed as a loan, the buyer would be paying "interest" in the form of a favorable price.

### Rav Papa Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: נִיחָא לְהוּ דִּמְגַלּוּ לְהוּ תַּרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** this is permitted? **Rav Pappa says:** It is **satisfactory to them** to sell merchandise at a discounted rate, because by doing so **the gates** to the new market **are opened for them,** as in this way they begin to do business in this area and gain new customers.

Rav Papa explains: *nicha lehu dimaglu lehu tara* — it benefits the merchants that their status as active businessmen is publicized. By pre-selling at a stated price, they demonstrate publicly that they are merchants with inventory coming. Rashi elaborates: when they arrive at the purchasing market with known committed sales, their standing is clear, sellers are willing to deal with them, and the whole enterprise is facilitated.

### Rav Acha Variation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: נִיחָא לְהוּ דְּמוֹזְלִי גַּבַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said:** It is **satisfactory to them because** the prices **are reduced for them** in the places where they make their purchases. Since the sellers there hear that the donkey drivers will need to resell the merchandise at a lower price, the sellers give a discount to the donkey drivers.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika offers a variation: *nicha lehu demazpi gabaihu* — it benefits the merchants because sellers will offer them a better price. Specifically, once it is known that a merchant pre-sold at one dollar per unit, sellers in Oshkosh feel some sympathy — they know the merchant's margin is thin — and may offer the goods at slightly below the going rate, say 95 cents. According to Rashi, this modest price advantage in acquisition is the concrete benefit the merchant receives in exchange for the favorable pre-sale price in Manhattan.

The difference between Rav Papa and Rav Acha plays out in the case of a new merchant: does a newcomer stand to gain more from the reputational publicity (since he is unknown and exposure is especially valuable), or less from seller sympathy (since sellers do not know his situation)? The *Gemara* notes this as the practical distinction between the two explanations.

### Rav Conservative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא אָזְלִי אַרְבָּעָה אַרְבְּעָה, בְּכַפְרִי אָזְלָן שִׁיתָּא שִׁיתָּא. יָהֵיב רַב זוּזֵי לְחַמָּרֵי וּ[מְ]קַבֵּיל עֲלֵיהּ אוּנְסָא דְאוֹרְחָא, וְשָׁקֵיל מִינַּיְיהוּ חַמְשָׁה. וְנִשְׁקוֹל שִׁיתָּא! אָדָם חָשׁוּב שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **In Sura, four** *se'a* of wheat **were going** for a *sela*, and **in** the nearby town of **Kafri they were going** for **six** *se'a* for a *sela*. **Rav gave money to donkey drivers** to purchase wheat in Kafri **and accepted upon himself** responsibility for any **accident** that might happen **on the way,** rendering it permitted for him to set a price according to the rate in effect in Kafri, **and he accepted five** *se'a* of wheat for one *sela* **from them.** The Gemara challenges: Since he accepted responsibility for damage that might occur as a result of an accident, the produce was his at the time it was purchased, and therefore there was no loan. Consequently, **he should have accepted six** *se'a* for a *sela*. The Gemara explains: **An important person is different,** as he has to be more stringent with himself and more careful to avoid the appearance of interest.

The *Gemara* then records that in Sura goods were priced at four units per dollar, while in Kafri they went at six units per dollar. Rav gave money to the *chamorin* and accepted liability for the journey, and took delivery at five units per dollar — not six, which would have been the full Kafri price. The *Gemara* asks: having accepted all the theoretical justifications for this arrangement, why did Rav not simply take six per dollar? The answer: *adam chashuv shani* — a person of stature sets a more exacting standard for himself. Even where the practice is technically permissible, Rav chose to take less than the full benefit in order to distance himself from any appearance of *ribbis*. The acceptance of liability (*ones*) by Rav is relevant here: Tosafot notes that this element figures into the broader analysis of which party truly bears ownership of the goods.

## Secondary Markets Test

### Scrap Metal Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אַסִּי מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַהוּ לַעֲשׂוֹת בִּגְרוּטָאוֹת כֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi asked Rabbi Yoḥanan: What is** the *halakha* about **doing so with** metal **scraps [*bigerutaot*]?**

Rav Assi posed a question to Rabbi Yochanan: *ma la'asot bi-grutaos kan* — can the same arrangement used by the *chamorin* be applied to scrap metal? The underlying question concerns the basis of the permit. Whether one follows Rashi's reputation theory or Tosafot's service model, the permit appears to depend in part on the existence of a stable, large-scale market — one in which the seller can reliably acquire the product and in which the reputational benefits or purchasing advantages are genuine.

Scrap metal markets are different. They are far less regularized: different sellers appear on different days, inventory is unpredictable, and no large stable supplier base exists. Rashi frames the question as whether the benefit to the merchant — whether reputational or in terms of purchase pricing — can operate meaningfully in a hit-or-miss secondhand market without a fixed community of sellers with whom an ongoing relationship can develop.

### Garments Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּיקֵּשׁ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן בִּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן, וְלֹא הִנִּיחוֹ רַבִּי. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בִּיקֵּשׁ רַבִּי לַעֲשׂוֹת בִּגְרוּטָאוֹת כֵּן, וְלֹא הִנִּיחוֹ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, wanted to do so with linen garments and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **did not allow him** to do so. **There are** those **who say** a different version of this exchange: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **wanted to do so with** metal **scraps, and Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, did not permit him** to do so.

In the same context, Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose wished to conduct such an arrangement with *klei pishtan* — linen garments — which similarly lack a uniform, stable wholesale market. These are handcrafted goods, not standardized commodities. Rabbi (Rebbi) did not permit him to do so. An alternative version of the tradition reverses the parties: Rebbi wished to make such an arrangement with scrap metal, and Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose refused to permit it. Either way, the point is the same: where the market lacks the stability and scale that makes the merchant's transaction genuinely analogous to a commodity purchase, the arrangement cannot rely on the same justifications and is therefore not permitted.

## Vineyard Wine Case

### Grape-to-Wine Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּרְדֵּיסָא, רַב אָסַר וּשְׁמוּאֵל שָׁרֵי. רַב אָסַר, כֵּיוָן דִּלְקַמֵּיהּ שָׁוְיָא טְפֵי – מִתְחֲזֵי כִּי אֲגַר נְטַר לֵיהּ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל שָׁרֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּהָוֵי בֵּיהּ תִּיוְהָא – לָא מִיחֲזֵי כִּי אֲגַר נְטַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who wants to purchase the produce of an entire **orchard,** in advance of the harvest, at a cheaper price, **Rav prohibits** this practice **and Shmuel permits** it. The Gemara explains: **Rav prohibits** it **because in the future** the produce will **be worth more,** so **it appears that** the seller **is paying** interest **to** the buyer **for waiting** before receiving the produce, and that has the appearance of interest. **And Shmuel permits** it, as, since **there can be spoilage** in the produce of the orchard and the buyer took upon himself responsibility for any losses, **it does not appear that** the seller **is paying** interest **to** the buyer **for waiting** before receiving the produce, as the buyer may either gain or lose.

The final case on this *daf* involves *pardisa* — a vineyard. A buyer wishes to pre-purchase wine from the vineyard owner at a time when the fruit is still unripe and no fixed price has yet been established. The price agreed upon reflects the current low value of the unripe grapes. The delivery, however, will be in wine — a transformed product worth considerably more.

This presents a compounded problem. First, unlike the standard *yatza hashaar* case, the market price has not yet been set. Second, and more acutely, the product to be delivered is fundamentally different from what exists at the time of the arrangement: unripe grapes become wine only through an extended process, and wine is worth far more than the grapes from which it comes. The transaction thus resembles both the new-grain/old-grain problem and the general concern of receiving something more valuable than what one paid for.

### Rav vs Shmuel

Rav forbids such an arrangement: *keivan de-lichi mati shavi tfei, mitchazi ki agar natar lei* — since when the time of delivery comes it will be worth more, it has the appearance of payment for waiting. The extra value of the wine over the price of the unripe grapes is treated as though the buyer is being compensated for having extended money earlier — which is precisely the definition of *ribbis*.

Shmuel permits it: *keivan de-hava bi-asuta* — because there is an element of genuine risk. This returns to the question raised earlier in the *daf*: is the buyer purchasing a specific, concrete object, or merely a promise of delivery?

### Risk Justification

Since **there can be spoilage** in the produce of the orchard and the buyer took upon himself responsibility for any losses, the transaction carries real commercial risk, which distinguishes it from a lending arrangement. A loan is repaid regardless of what happens in the interim; this transaction is contingent on the survival of a specific agricultural enterprise. That element of risk — the possibility of total loss — makes the arrangement a genuine purchase rather than a disguised loan, and therefore Shmuel permits it.

### Tori High Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא: וּמוֹדֵי רַב בְּתוֹרֵי דִּנְפִישׁ פְּסֵידַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya said: Rav concedes** to Shmuel that an arrangement like this would be permitted **in** a case where one arranges to purchase young **oxen** at a later date, **as their loss is** likely to be **great.** Since it is common for one to incur a discernible loss when raising oxen, as some may die, this arrangement is regarded as an investment.

The *Gemara* records that Rav Shimi bar Ashi said: *u-modei Rav be-tori de-nafish psida hu* — even Rav would concede in the case of *tori*, land that is plowed with oxen, where the risk of crop failure or damage is considerably higher. In such high-risk agricultural settings, even Rav agrees that the element of risk is sufficient to reframe the transaction as a genuine purchase, and the arrangement is therefore permissible. The threshold for Rav appears to be not merely theoretical risk but a meaningfully elevated probability of loss.

### Land Possession Fix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְהָנְהוּ דְּשָׁבְשִׁי שִׁבְשָׁא, הֲפוֹכוּ בְּאַרְעָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּקְנֵי לְכוּ גּוּפָא דְאַרְעָא, וְאִי לָא – הָוְיָא לְכוּ כְּהַלְוָאָה וְאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said to those who purchase branches of grapevines** and pay in advance for the vine shoots that will be harvested later: Since the risk in this transaction is small, it has the appearance of interest and therefore you should **turn over** a bit of **the land** yourselves, i.e., perform some labor in farming the orchard, **so that you acquire** some of **the land itself for yourselves,** and by doing this you become partners with the owner. **And** this action is necessary because **if** you do **not** do this **it will be like a loan for you and** it will be **prohibited** for you to accept the branches.

Finally, Shmuel offered practical guidance to those engaged in such pre-purchase arrangements for vineyard wine: *hafukhu be-ara ki heikhi de-tiknu lakhu gufa de-ara* — till the soil a little, so that you take some actual possession of the land itself. By performing a small act of *kinyan* on the land, the buyer acquires a stake — however nominal — in the vineyard itself, rather than merely a contractual entitlement to future wine. This transforms the transaction: instead of what might look like a loan secured by future agricultural output, the buyer is now a partial owner of the underlying land, and the delivery of wine is the yield of his own property. *Ve-i lo havei lakhu ke-halva'ah ve-asir* — without this step, it has the character of a loan and is forbidden.

This concludes the *daf*, which has moved through a wide range of practical applications — from forward arrangements for grain, to quality distinctions between market types, to the *se'ah be-se'ah* problem and borrowed goods, to merchant transport and market arbitrage, and finally to agricultural pre-purchases and the role of risk in validating commercial transactions. The range of cases makes this section of *Bava Metzia* unusual: rather than sustained abstract analysis, the *Gemara* is testing the principles of *ribbis* across the full spectrum of commercial life.
