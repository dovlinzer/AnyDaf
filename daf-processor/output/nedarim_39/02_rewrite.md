# Nedarim 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wife, Children, Property

### Feeding Wife & Children

The daf opens with the tail end of the Mishnah on 38b, which addresses a situation in which Reuven is forbidden to derive *hana'ah* (benefit) from Shimon. Despite this prohibition, the Mishnah rules that Shimon may still feed Reuven's wife and children. The underlying logic is that doing so is not considered a direct conferral of benefit upon Reuven himself — it is analogous to paying off Reuven's creditors. Because Reuven bears an obligation to feed his wife and children, Shimon's providing for them discharges what is, in effect, a debt, and that is not classified as direct *hana'ah* to the person who is off-limits.

By contrast, feeding Reuven's animals is prohibited. There, the act of providing food directly increases the monetary value of Reuven's estate — a fatter animal is worth more — and that constitutes a tangible, direct benefit. The Mishnah's discussion of non-kosher animals, whose value is not measured primarily by weight gain but rather by their capacity for labor (a donkey fed to continue working, for example), raises a further question about whether that too constitutes impermissible *hana'ah*. The general principle emerges clearly: feeding the wife is not *hana'ah*, but feeding the animals is.

### Adult Daughter Marrying

The Gemara opens with a teaching cited in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Chananya in the name of Rav Huna: if one is forbidden to benefit from another (*hamudar hana'ah mechaveiro*), it is still permitted for the second party to marry off his daughter to the first. Rabbi Zeira immediately raises a difficulty: in which direction does the prohibition run?

If it is Reuven who cannot benefit from Shimon, then Shimon's marrying his daughter to Reuven would seem to be a straightforward conferral of *hana'ah* upon Reuven — after all, as the Gemara evocatively notes, a wife brings a *shifcha leshamsho*, a maidservant to attend to his needs, which is precisely what the tractate Ketubot establishes as one of a wife's marital obligations. On the other hand, if the direction is reversed and Reuven cannot give benefit to Shimon, then Reuven's marrying off his daughter to Shimon would appear to give Shimon something of value. Either way, the ruling seems problematic.

The Gemara attempts to resolve this by positing that it must be Shimon (the bride's father) whose property is forbidden to Reuven (the groom). Yet the Gemara immediately challenges this resolution: if Shimon can already feed Reuven's daughter even without marrying her off to him — and that is not considered *hana'ah* to Reuven — then it goes without saying (*gedola mizu amru*) that once Reuven has married her and she is now his wife, Shimon can continue to provide for her. There is no *chiddush* there.

The Gemara therefore settles on the opposite formulation: it is Reuven, the bride's father, whose property is forbidden to Shimon, the groom. The case is one in which Reuven's daughter is a *bogeret* — an adult woman of at least twelve and a half years — and she is acting entirely *mida'atah*, of her own volition. Since she is no longer a minor over whom her father exercises custodial authority, and since she is the initiating party in this marriage, Reuven is not the one transferring anything to Shimon. The daughter is marrying herself off independently, and Reuven is not regarded as conferring any benefit upon Shimon through this act.

The Ran adds an important nuance: the *chiddush* of the phrase *umida'atah* is not merely that the father is uninvolved, but that Shimon himself is also not the initiator. If Shimon were to approach Reuven and ask him to persuade his adult daughter to accept his proposal, Reuven would be performing a service for Shimon, which would itself constitute *hana'ah* and would therefore be forbidden. The ruling is thus conditioned on the daughter acting entirely on her own initiative, with no orchestration from either father or groom.

This discussion also touches briefly on the distinction between *kiddushin* and *nisuin*. The verb used in the teaching — *lehasi'o et bito*, to "marry off" his daughter — implies the full *nisuin*, the actual commencement of cohabitation. The obligations of a wife attendant to *shmisha* and domestic life attach only after *nisuin*, not merely after *kiddushin*. The Gemara acknowledges that while the father technically effects the *kiddushin* for a minor daughter, the *nisuin* — actually taking her to live in the husband's home — is a more complex matter, and practically, the father's role in transferring even a minor daughter does seem to extend to that stage as well, as the language of *masra avi hakallah* (the bride's father "hands over" his daughter) in Ketubot suggests.

A *baraita* is then cited that confirms the ruling: if one is forbidden to receive benefit from another, he may nonetheless marry off his adult daughter to that person, provided she is acting of her own accord.

### Father & Son Study Vow

Rabbi Yaakov teaches: *hamadir beno letalmud Torah* — one who takes a vow *regarding* his son in connection with Torah study. The Ran explains this on the basis of a supporting *Tosefta*: the father declares that he will derive no benefit from his son. The purpose of this declaration is to free the son entirely from the burdens of *kibud av va'em* — honoring his father — so that he need not interrupt his Torah study to attend to the father's needs. The *mefaresh* (the commentary in place of Rashi on this tractate) offers an alternative reading: the father vows that all his property will become forbidden to the son if the son does not devote himself to Torah study — a leveraged threat designed to compel the son toward learning. The shiur reads primarily in accordance with the Ran's interpretation.

Given that reading, the Gemara rules that the son may nonetheless still fill a barrel of water for his father (*lemallos lo chavis shemayim*) and light a candle for him (*lehadlik lo et haner*). These would plainly seem to fall within the category of *hana'ah*, yet the Gemara permits them. This is a striking departure from the otherwise formalistic framework the tractate has employed. Until this point the analysis has been essentially structural: does the benefit flow directly, does it pay off an obligation, does it fatten an asset? Here, however, the Gemara deploys context. In the relationship of a father and son, and given the explicit purpose of the vow — to enable Torah study rather than to create estrangement — one must assume that certain basic domestic services were never intended to be included in the declaration, even if the words would technically encompass them.

Rav Yitzchak then goes further, stating that the son may even fry a small fish (*litzlos lo dag katan*) for his father. This only sharpens the question of where the line is drawn. The answer, it emerges, is that no formal line can be drawn by recourse to the words of the vow alone; one must instead ask what the parties could reasonably be presumed to have intended given the context of their relationship and the stated purpose of the vow.

### Context & Implied Exclusions

The preceding cases crystallize a methodological tension. For most of the tractate, the rules governing *hana'ah* prohibitions have been applied formally and categorically. But the Gemara here introduces the principle that context can effectively remove certain actions from the scope of a prohibition, even when the literal language would include them. The operative question becomes: what would the person who took the vow actually have intended to include?

The Ran articulates the governing distinction with precision: no use of context can permit the actual transfer of an object of value from one party to another. If the son cannot give his fish to his father, he cannot. The contextual reasoning operates only at the level of *services* — acts of assistance or labor that, given the nature of the relationship and the purpose of the vow, can be presumed to have been implicitly excluded. Where the vow was taken in the spirit of advancing Torah study rather than imposing a relational rupture, certain elemental services between father and son fall outside its scope.

### Cup of Peace & Consolation

Rabbi Yirmiya citing Rabbi Yochanan teaches: one who is forbidden to benefit from another (*hamudar hana'ah mechaveiro*) may nonetheless fill a cup of greeting for him — a *kos shel shalom*. This ruling is not limited to the father-son relationship but applies generically.

The Gemara asks: what precisely is a *kos shel shalom*? In Babylonia (*hacha targimu*) it is understood as the cup filled during *nichum aveilim* — consoling a mourner. As part of the visit to a house of mourning, one fills the *avel*'s cup for him, and this customary act of comfort is deemed not to constitute prohibited *hana'ah*. The Ran adds the important qualification that in this case, as in the earlier examples, it must be the *avel*'s own water being used. Giving one's own water or food to someone would always constitute a direct transfer of a valuable object and would be forbidden regardless of context; it is only the *service* of pouring and filling that is being exempted. In the West (*b'ma'arava amri*), the *kos shel shalom* is understood even more broadly as the cup of water offered after a bath — the act of handing someone water after they emerge from a *beit hamerchataz*. Even this minimal, customary courtesy falls outside the prohibition.

The rationale, as the Ran frames it, is *darchei shalom* — the ways of peace. To refuse such a basic act of human interaction, given that *nedarim* are so often born of interpersonal conflict, would be so profoundly contrary to basic norms of civil society that one could never have intended to include such things in the vow. This again confirms the governing principle: objects of value may not be transferred, but contextually assumed services of a basic, customary, or humane character remain permitted.

### Servants vs. Animals

Returning to the Mishnah's prohibition on feeding the animals, a *baraita* is cited in the name of Yehoshua Ish Uzza, who draws a further distinction: one may feed another's Canaanite male and female servants (*avadim* and *shfachot*), but one may not feed his animals, whether kosher or non-kosher.

The Gemara asks for the underlying logic of this distinction. The answer is that Canaanite servants are fed in order to perform their tasks (*lemancharusa avdin*) — they do household work, carry out labor, and the feeding simply maintains their capacity for service. It does not appreciably increase their monetary value. Animals, by contrast, exist to be fattened (*behemah lefituma avida*); providing them with food increases their weight and thus directly increases the asset value of the owner's estate. This is a concrete, direct *hana'ah* that is impermissible regardless of whether the animal is kosher or not — since even a non-kosher animal can be sold, e.g. as animal fodder, and its fattened state increases what it commands.

The distinction being drawn is thus analogous to the question of whether changing someone's oil constitutes giving them *hana'ah*: preventive maintenance that forestalls decline is not the same as an act that affirmatively increases value. Feeding the servants falls in the category of maintenance; feeding the animals falls in the category of value-enhancement.

## Visiting the Sick

### Stand vs. Sit Rule

The next Mishnah: *hamudar hana'ah mechaveiro venichnas levakro* — one who is forbidden to receive benefit from another may nevertheless enter and pay him a *bikur cholim* call when he is sick. The Mishnah then qualifies: he may stand (*omed*), but he may not sit (*aval lo yoshev*). The difference between standing and sitting is not immediately self-evident and will occupy the Gemara at length.

### Refuat Nefesh & Mamon

The Mishnah continues: if the visitor is a physician, he may provide *refuat nefesh* — medical care for the sick person's body — but he may not provide *refuat mamon*, care for the person's property or animals (i.e., veterinary care). This distinction is striking: rendering medical assistance to a human being, which plainly produces tangible physical benefit, is permitted, while veterinary care, directed at the person's assets, is forbidden.

The intuition the distinction tracks is the same one underlying the entire tractate: *hana'ah* in the relevant sense is measured in financial and property terms. Care for another person's body, even when medically significant, falls into the category of a *mitzvah* — it is not the kind of benefit the law of *nedarim* contemplates as forbidden. Care for another's animals, however, directly preserves or augments the value of his property and is therefore impermissible. The term *refuat nefesh* (literally, healing of the person) is used here in contradistinction to *refuat mamon* (healing of property) rather than to *refuat haguf* (healing of the body); the liturgical pairing of *refuat hanefesh verefuat haguf* in the *Mi Shebeirach* is a later development and not the framework operative here.

### Fee-Based Services

The Gemara opens the analysis by identifying whose property is forbidden to whom, since the determination controls the resolution. If it is Shimon's property that is forbidden to Reuven (i.e., Reuven cannot benefit from Shimon), then when Shimon comes to pay Reuven a *bikur cholim* visit, Shimon is performing a *mitzvah* and giving Reuven no direct object of value at all. In that case, the Gemara asks: why should it matter whether he stands or sits? He should be permitted to do either. On the other hand, if it is Reuven's property that is forbidden to Shimon, then Shimon's very entry into Reuven's house involves enjoying Reuven's property — a right of access that would itself constitute *hana'ah* — and Shimon should not even be permitted to stand there, let alone sit.

The Gemara therefore has Shmuel resolve the question: the Mishnah is speaking in the first direction — Reuven cannot benefit from Shimon. The distinction between standing and sitting corresponds to whether a fee is conventionally charged. In a place where people are paid for paying a *bikur cholim* call, the fee attaches to sitting — spending real time with the sick person — but not to merely dropping in (*uvemakom shenotnim sachar leyeshiva ve'ein notnim sachar al amidah*). Standing is the basic performance of the *mitzvah* of *bikur cholim*; demanding a fee for it would be like demanding payment for teaching Torah. Sitting represents a more extended, quasi-professional engagement, the kind for which chaplaincy-style compensation would be appropriate, and by not charging for it the visitor effectively donates the monetary equivalent of that fee to Reuven — which constitutes impermissible *hana'ah*.

One might ask: what is the actual distinction, and why does sitting generate a fee obligation? The Gemara suggests that just as one may not forgo the right to charge for teaching *Torah she-bikhtav* when that right exists (since doing so gives the student a monetary benefit), one may not forgo the right to charge for prolonged bedside presence when that right conventionally exists. Standing is the *mitzvah*; sitting goes beyond it and enters the domain of compensable service.

The Gemara then raises an alternative: even according to the approach of Rabbi Shimon ben Yakim (invoked in a closely related context several dapim later), one might argue that even in a place where sitting is not conventionally charged for, sitting would still be forbidden — as a *gezeira* lest one remain too long, at which point the extended stay would be the kind of thing that would normally attract a fee. The underlying logic is the same throughout: the *hana'ah* consists in forgoing a fee that would otherwise be owed, and the line between permitted standing and forbidden sitting tracks the line between that which is conventionally compensable and that which is not.

### Essential Well-Being

Ulla offers an alternative reading of the Mishnah. Rather than saying that the Mishnah speaks in the direction where Reuven cannot benefit from Shimon, Ulla maintains that the Mishnah speaks in the opposite direction — it is Reuven's property that is forbidden to Shimon (*b'nichsei choleh asurin al hamevaker*). On this reading, Shimon in principle ought not to enter Reuven's house at all, since walking on another's property is a form of benefiting from it. Yet the Mishnah permits the visit.

The Ran explains Ulla's position as a further application of the contextual principle introduced earlier. When Reuven declared that Shimon may not benefit from his property, he obviously did not intend to exclude things essential to his own well-being. Just as the father making a vow about his son's Torah study cannot have intended to exclude having a barrel of water filled, and just as the person who made a vow during a conflict cannot have intended to exclude a basic cup of courtesy water after a bath, Reuven cannot have intended to forbid Shimon from entering his home to pay him a *bikur cholim* visit when he is lying ill. That falls so squarely within what anyone in Reuven's position would want that it is implicitly excluded from the vow. Sitting, however, is not essential; that remains forbidden.

### Person vs. Family Visit

A *baraita* is then introduced that distinguishes between visiting the sick person himself and visiting his child. The *baraita* rules: if the sick person falls ill, the visitor may enter and see him. But if the sick person's son falls ill, the visitor may only inquire after the son's health in the marketplace — he may not enter the house to visit the son. The Gemara notes that this distinction fits seamlessly with Ulla's approach: Reuven's property is off-limits to Shimon except insofar as entry is essential to Reuven's own well-being. Shimon entering to see Reuven himself — the person whose property is at stake — is something Reuven would obviously have wanted to carve out of his declaration. Shimon entering to see Reuven's son is not essential to Reuven's well-being, so it remains forbidden, and Shimon is limited to asking after the son's welfare from the public domain.

### Direction of Vow

For Shmuel's reading of the Mishnah, however, this *baraita* poses a difficulty. If the Mishnah speaks in the direction where Reuven cannot benefit from Shimon, then Shimon's visiting the son should be perfectly permissible — he is not giving Reuven any *hana'ah* by doing so, any more than he gives *hana'ah* by feeding Reuven's daughter or paying off Reuven's creditors. Indeed the Ran notes that if anything, visiting the son should be *more* permissible than visiting Reuven himself, since visiting Reuven directly involves providing him a service that might normally be charged for.

The Gemara resolves the tension by distinguishing the two sources: the Mishnah, Shmuel concedes, is indeed best read in his direction (Reuven cannot benefit from Shimon), and on that reading visiting the son would be permitted. The *baraita*, by contrast, is to be read in Ulla's direction (Reuven's property is forbidden to Shimon), and on that reading the son's visit is naturally prohibited since it is not essential to Reuven's well-being. The Mishnah and the *baraita* thus speak to different configurations of the vow, and the apparent contradiction dissolves once the direction of each prohibition is properly identified.

Rava, citing Shmuel, adds that this reading is actually compelled by the language of the Mishnah itself: the Mishnah's specific formulation distinguishing *standing* from *sitting* makes sense only in Shmuel's direction. If the prohibition ran the other way (Reuven's property forbidden to Shimon), the distinction between standing and sitting would be irrelevant — either the entire visit is excluded as essential to Reuven's well-being, in which case sitting too should be permitted, or it is not so excluded, in which case even standing should be forbidden. The graduated distinction the Mishnah draws between standing and sitting is intelligible only on the premise that Reuven is forbidden to benefit from Shimon, and the question is which forms of Shimon's service constitute a benefit being waived.

## Biblical Sources & Theol…

### Korach & Visitation

The Gemara now shifts into a more aggadic register to explore *bikur cholim* as a theological and biblical value. Reish Lakish asks: where does the Torah provide a *remez*, a hint, for the institution of *bikur cholim*? This question is set against the Rambam's formulation that *bikur cholim* is itself a fulfillment of the biblical commandment *ve'ahavta lere'acha kamocha* — love your neighbor as yourself. As the Rambam explains, the rabbis gave very concrete, specific expression to this sweeping commandment through identified obligations such as *bikur cholim*, *nichum aveilim*, *hachnasas orchim*, and *hachnasas kallah*. There is a profound rationale to this concretization: a commandment as all-encompassing as *ve'ahavta lere'acha kamocha* risks becoming so diffuse as to generate no actual sense of obligation. By naming specific, identifiable expressions of it, the rabbis gave the commandment actionable force.

Reish Lakish locates the scriptural anchor for *bikur cholim* in the Korach narrative. Moses declares: *im kemot kol ha'adam yemutun eileh ufekudat kol ha'adam yipaked aleihem, lo Hashem shelachani* — "if these men die as all men die, and the fate of all mankind befalls them, then God has not sent me" (Bamidbar 16:29). The word *ufekudat*, from the root meaning to visit or to attend to, is understood here as referring specifically to the customary visits people receive while lying ill.

### Death & Visiting

Rava elaborates on Moses' meaning. The phrase *kemot kol ha'adam* — dying as all people die — describes the normal arc of human mortality: one falls ill, lies in one's sickbed, and people come to visit. The phrase *ufekudat kol ha'adam yipaked aleihem* then refers to this act of visitation. Moses is thus saying: if the men of Korach's faction die in the ordinary way, lying sick with visitors attending them, then people will say that God did not send me, that this was nothing more than a natural death.

### Seven Pre-World Creations

This leads into the famous aggadah regarding Gehinnom. Moses says, *im briah yivra Hashem* — if God will create a creation, namely that the earth will open its mouth and swallow Korach's company. Rava interprets this conditionally: if Gehinnom has already been created, well and good — let it be used here; and if not, let God create it now.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: is it really conceivable that Gehinnom had not yet been created? A *baraita* teaches that seven things were created before the world itself was made: *Torah, teshuva, Gan Eden, Gehinnom, kisei hakavod, beit hamikdash*, and the name of *mashiach*. The Ran notes that this should not be taken entirely literally — prior to the world's creation, nothing was literally created in a spatial sense — but that these seven were part of the foundational plan and intention of creation.

The theological statement embedded in this list is striking. *Torah* is cited from Mishlei 8:22, where Wisdom — understood as Torah — declares that God acquired it as the first of His paths. *Teshuva* is anchored in Tehillim 90:2-3, where even before the mountains were born, the possibility of human return was already inscribed: *tashev enosh ad daka* — "You return man to contrition," read as God's provision for human fallibility existing before the world itself. The pre-existence of *teshuva* is among the most profound statements in the aggadic literature: free will and the capacity for moral renewal were intrinsic to the design of creation, not afterthoughts.

*Gan Eden* is derived from Bereishit 2:8, where God planted the garden *mikedem* — from the beginning — understood here not merely as the primordial paradise of Adam and Eve but as the eschatological promise of reward. *Gehinnom* is derived from Yeshayahu 30:33, *ki aruch me'etmol Tofteh* — "for yesterday Tofteh is arranged" — establishing that the reality of consequence and judgment preceded the world as well. *Kisei hakavod* — the divine throne — is derived from Tehillim 93:2, *nachon kis'acha mei'az* — "Your throne was established of old." The *beit hamikdash* is derived from Yirmiyahu 17:12, *kisei kavod marom mei'rishon* — "a throne of glory on high from the beginning," understood as the terrestrial expression of divine sovereignty, complementing the heavenly *kisei hakavod*. And the name of the *mashiach* is derived from Tehillim 72:17, *yehi shemo le'olam* — "may his name endure forever" — the assurance that history has a redemptive direction built into it from before the beginning.

### Gehinnom Creation

Given that the *baraita* establishes Gehinnom as one of the seven pre-world creations, what can Moses have meant when he said *yivra Hashem* — let God create it? The Gemara resolves this in two stages. First, it reframes Moses' conditional: what Moses said was that if *Gehinnom's mouth* — the specific opening into which Korach and his company would fall — has already been positioned here, good; and if not, let God create it. This narrows the question from whether Gehinnom exists to whether a mouth of Gehinnom happens to be located at this precise spot.

The Gemara then raises a further objection: *ein kol chadash tachat hashemesh* — there is nothing new under the sun (Kohelet 1:9). How could even the mouth of Gehinnom be newly created? The final resolution: Moses was not asking for a new creation but for a *relocation* — let the mouth of Gehinnom that exists elsewhere be moved to this spot. Moving an existing feature is not the creation of something entirely new.

### Sun & Moon Strike

Rava expounds on the verse from Chavakuk 3:11: *shemesh yarei'ach amad zvula* — the sun and moon stood in their lofty abode. The Gemara asks what the sun and moon were doing in *Zvul*, one of the upper heavens, when they are normally fixed in the *rakia*, the visible firmament. The answer is that they had ascended from the *rakia* to *Zvul* to lodge a protest before God. During Moses' confrontation with Korach, the sun and moon appeared before God and declared: Master of the Universe, if You execute judgment on behalf of the son of Amram, we will continue to give light; and if You do not vindicate Moses' honor, we will not shine.

God's response was to hurl arrows and spears at them (*yara vahen chitzim ve'chanitot*). The divine rebuke follows: every single day the nations bow down to you and you continue to illuminate the world for them without objection, yet for My honor you raise no protest — and only for the honor of a human being do you threaten to go on strike? Return to your posts. The sun and moon had no answer and returned. From that day forward, Rava teaches, God must each day hurl arrows and spears at the sun and moon to compel them to fulfill their function — which is why the verse in Chavakuk describes them: *l'or chitzecha yeileichu* — "at the light of Your arrows they move." The shiur concludes here, with the discussion of *bikur cholim* and its associated aggadot to be continued on the following daf.
