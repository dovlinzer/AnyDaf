# Bava Batra 147 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechiv Mera Principles

### Institution Overview

After a brief excursion into the world of *shushbinot* and *sovlonot* — which arose in the context of determining how reciprocal gifts are handled when a father dies and the question of which brother receives them — the sugya returns to the fundamental issue of inheritance and finally arrives at the *mishnah* that directly introduces *matnat shechiv mera*, the deathbed bequest that functions as the rabbinic institution designed to play the role of a will. This represents the culmination of a long progression: the Torah's laws of inheritance, the modifications permitted to a dying man under the framework of Yochanan ben Broka, the role of inter vivos gifts, and now the full institution of *matnat shechiv mera* as a vehicle for bequeathing property outside the ordinary channels of *yerusha*.

### Words Create Kinyan

The first foundational principle of *matnat shechiv mera* is captured in the phrase *divrei shchiv mera k'chtuvim u'chemesurim dami* — the words of a deathbed declaration are considered as if written in a document and physically handed over. Whether the subject is movable property (*metaltelin*) or real estate, the spoken words of the *shechiv mera* function as a complete *kinyan*. No formal act of acquisition is required. That is the *chiddush* of the institution: the mere verbal expression, under the appropriate circumstances, creates a legally binding transfer.

### Void Upon Recovery

The second foundational principle — which follows almost as a corollary from the nature of the institution — is that the gift takes effect at death. Since the entire premise of the *matnat shechiv mera* is that the person believes he is about to die and is disposing of his property on that assumption, the *gemara* establishes the rule of *im amad chozer*: if he recovers from the illness, the gift is automatically voided. This is not merely a right to revoke; it is automatic nullification. The property reverts without any further act, because the condition underlying the entire transaction — the expectation of imminent death — has been rendered false by the recovery itself.

### Kol vs. Miktzat

A critical distinction governs how these two principles apply, and it turns on whether the *shechiv mera* is giving away all of his property (*kol nechasav*) or only a portion of it (*miktzat nechasav*). If he is dividing all of his property, the implication is unambiguous: he is doing so because he believes he is about to die. The totality of the disposition signals that this is genuinely a deathbed bequest.

But if he is dividing only part of his property, a problem arises. If he truly expects to die, why is he not dividing everything? One could answer that he intends the remainder to pass by ordinary *yerusha* to his children — and indeed we will see that the explicit designation of the remainder for the heirs may well bring such a case under the rubric of *kol nechasav*. But where it is truly *miktzat nechasav* with no such designation, the partiality of the disposition suggests that he does not view this as a final deathbed act, and accordingly the full force of the institution does not apply.

### Kinyan Requirements

The practical consequence of this distinction is as follows. When giving away all his property (*kol nechasav*), no *kinyan* is required — his words alone suffice. When giving away only part (*miktzat nechasav*), a *kinyan* is required, because the rabbinic dispensation that allows words alone to function as a transfer applies only where the context unmistakably signals a genuine deathbed disposition.

### Partial Gift Recovery

The distinction between *kol nechasav* and *miktzat nechasav* also bears on the question of *amad v'chozer*. For a gift of all one's property, recovery automatically voids the gift. For a gift of only part of one's property — where a *kinyan* was in any case required — recovery does not void the gift. Since the partial gift was treated essentially as an ordinary *kinyan* made while the person happened to be ill, it is governed by the normal rules of *kinyanim*: *amad v'eino chozer*, he recovered and the gift stands. The institution of *shechiv mera* in its full force — words without *kinyan* and automatic revocation upon recovery — applies only to *kol nechasav*.

## Mishnah: Shiyer Karka

### Mishnah Text

The *mishnah* addresses the case of a *shechiv mera* who writes away all of his property to others — *shechiv mera she-kasav kol nechasav l'acherim* — but leaves over a small parcel of land, *v'shiyer karka kol shehu*. The reservation of even a minimal piece of land is the operative act. The *mishnah* then pronounces: *matanaso kayemes* — his gift is valid. And crucially, as the *gemara* reads it, this validity holds even if he subsequently recovers (*amad*).

One might ask: why should leaving over a piece of land make any difference to the validity of the gift upon recovery? The answer lies in understanding precisely what issue the *mishnah* is addressing.

### Gift Valid with Land

The *gemara* reads the *mishnah*'s declaration that the gift is valid as speaking to the *amad* scenario — the case where the *shechiv mera* recovers. If he left over a piece of land, the gift remains valid even though he got better. The Rashbam notes explicitly (*v'hu hadin chilek al piv belo kesivah*) that the *mishnah*'s use of the word *she-kasav* does not restrict the case to one where a document was written — the same rule applies when he made his disposition orally. However, since the *mishnah* is discussing *shiyer karka kol shehu* — a case of *miktzat nechasav* — some formal act of *kinyan* is required, and the *kasav* reflects that some such act was performed.

### Invalid Without Land

Conversely, if he did not leave over any land — *aval lo shiyer karka kol shehu* — the gift is not valid in the event of recovery. This is the natural counterpart: a gift of *kol nechasav* with no reservation is subject to *im amad chozer*, and recovery automatically voids it.

### Amad vs. Kinyan

It is important to clarify that the *mishnah* here is addressing the *amad* question — what happens if he recovers — not the *kinyan* question. The *mishnah* is not saying that without *shiyer karka* the gift is categorically invalid; rather, it is saying that without *shiyer karka* the gift does not survive recovery. The two issues are analytically distinct, and the *mishnah* is speaking to the former.

### Inheritance Portion

A natural question arises from an earlier discussion: we had seen that one should not completely disinherit one's heirs, and that there is a principle discouraging the total diversion of one's estate away from the natural inheritors. If that principle is in force, it would seem that one would always retain at least something, and therefore pure *kol nechasav* cases would be rare. The *gemara* will address the precise definition of *kol nechasav* in the coming discussion, and it may well be that an explicit statement that the remainder goes to one's children can itself qualify as a form of *kol nechasav*. The relationship between *shiyer karka kol shehu* and these broader inheritance concerns will become clearer as the sugya unfolds.

## Umdana & Intent

### Who Holds Umdana?

The rule that recovery automatically voids the gift of *kol nechasav* rests on a particular legal principle: *azlinan batar umdana*, we assess the presumed intent of the giver. The rationale for voiding the gift upon recovery is not that the Rabbis simply decreed it as an arbitrary feature of the institution, but rather that we evaluate the *shechiv mera*'s frame of mind — he gave the gift because he thought he was going to die, and had he known he would recover, he never would have given it. The *gemara* now asks: which *tana* holds that we operate on the principle of *azlinan batar umdana*?

### Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya

Rav Nachman attributes this principle to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya, citing a *braita*. The case: a man's son traveled overseas, and the father then heard that his son had died. Subsequently, the father gifted all his property to others. Afterward, the son returned alive. The *tana kama* holds that the gift is valid — *matnaso matana*. The sequence of events establishes no explicit condition; since the father never said his gift was contingent on the son's death, the principle of *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* applies: unstated mental conditions are not legally operative, and the gift stands.

### Tana Kama vs. RSbM

Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya disagrees: *ein matnaso matana* — the gift is not valid. His reasoning: *she-ilu haya yodei she-b'no kayam, lo haya kosvan* — had the father known his son was alive, he would never have written the gift. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya is willing to void the transaction based on a reconstruction of the giver's presumed state of mind, notwithstanding the absence of an explicit condition. This is *azlinan batar umdana* in its purest form.

### Execution & Anticipation

Rav Sheshet offers an alternative attribution, locating the principle of *azlinan batar umdana* in a different *tana* entirely: Rabbi Shimon Shezuri. The *braita* cited involves the case of *hayotzei b'kolar* — a person being led out in chains to be executed by the Roman government — who says *kitvu get l'ishti*, write a *get* for my wife. The halachically precise formulation should have been *kitvu u'snu get l'ishti* — write and give — since a *get* takes effect only upon delivery, not mere writing. Yet because the context makes the intent self-evident, the word *kitvu* is read to include *u'snu*. This was then expanded to cover one who is departing on an overseas voyage or joining a caravan, and Rabbi Shimon Shezuri extends it further: even *af hamiskukan* — one who is critically ill — falls within this rule. Tosafot notes that even the earlier authorities in the *mishnah* agree to the basic principle of reading into the word *kitvu* the word *tnu*; Rabbi Shimon Shezuri simply extends the principle to a broader range of cases.

### Umdana & Devarim

This raises a fundamental tension: how does *azlinan batar umdana* — assessing presumed intent to override or modify a transaction — coexist with the principle of *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*, which holds that unstated mental conditions are legally inoperative? If we need everything to be explicit for a transaction to be binding, how can we void or condition a transaction based on what we presume was in someone's mind?

### Tosafot's Question

Tosafot raises precisely this question: *tzarich iyun l'shavecha hachamirosta b'devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* — it requires analysis how the principle of *azlinan batar umdana* squares with the stringency of *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*. Tosafot refers the reader to the second chapter of Kiddushin, where the question is treated at length. One approach offered there is that the reconciliation depends on how self-evident the presumed intent is — where it is evident to all, it need not be stated; where it is not so evident, explicit statement is required.

### One-Sided Transactions

The best answer, attributed to the Rambam and the Tosafot Rid, is that *azlinan batar umdana* applies specifically to one-sided transactions — a *matana* or a *get*. In both cases, the other party is not giving up anything of their own; they are simply receiving. In a bilateral transaction, the other party cannot be bound by unstated conditions swimming in the mind of the first party — that is precisely why *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* applies with full force. But where the transaction is essentially one-directional, where it is only the giver's or the husband's intent that is at stake, we can legitimately assess and reconstruct that intent. This distinction is reflected in the Rambam's formulations: in *Hilchos Mechira*, governing sales, *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim*; in *Hilchos Zechiya U'Matana*, governing gifts, *l'olam omdin da'as hanosein* — we always assess the intent of the one who gives.

### RN's Preference

The *gemara* then asks: why did Rav Nachman prefer Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya over Rabbi Shimon Shezuri as the source for the *umdana* principle? The answer is that the *get* case (*kitvu get l'ishti*) is structurally different from what we need. In the *get* case, we are using the person's presumed intent to fill in a missing word — to read *kitvu* as *kitvu u'snu*. We are supplementing what he said, not negating what he did. But in the case of the *shechiv mera* — where the gift is voided because of the father's presumed frame of mind — we are using *umdana* to negate and undo an act that was already performed. That is a far more powerful application of the principle, and Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's case provides a much closer precedent.

### Evidence Requirement

Conversely, the *gemara* asks why Rav Sheshet did not use Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya's case as his evidence. The answer: *umdana d'mokeach shani* — an *umdana* that is entirely self-evident is a special case. In the father-and-son scenario, it is absolutely clear to everyone that the father gave the gift only because he thought his son was dead. This is a uniquely obvious *umdana*. Rav Sheshet preferred to derive the principle from a case where the *umdana* is less immediately self-evident — such as the critically ill person who does not use the precise legal formulation when directing that a *get* be written — in order to establish that we apply *azlinan batar umdana* even in contexts where the presumed intent is not quite so transparent.

### Sick Person Braita

The *gemara* then introduces another *braita* to determine which *tana* it follows. The case involves a person who is ill and lying in bed (*choleh u'mutal b'mitah*), whom the people around him ask about his property — *nechasyecha l'mi* — to whom should his property go? He responds by revealing his assumption: he says he had always believed he had a son overseas but now understands he does not, or that he had assumed his wife was pregnant but now learns she is not, and on that basis he directs his property to a third party. Subsequently it emerges that his original assumption was correct — the son was alive, or the wife was indeed pregnant. The *gemara* asks: is this gift voided?

### Explicit Statement

The *gemara* considers whether this *braita* must be attributed to Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya and not to the Rabbis. The answer is: even the Rabbis could agree here, because this case is categorically different from the father-and-son case where the *tana kama* held the gift valid. There, the father said nothing — his frame of mind was purely internal, and *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* applied. Here, the sick person actually stated his assumption out loud: *savur hayiti she-ein li ben* — I believed I had no son. That verbal articulation accomplishes two things simultaneously. First, it removes any doubt about what his frame of mind actually was — we are not guessing; he told us. Second, and more significantly, it transforms what would otherwise be an internal, legally inoperative mental condition into something approaching an explicit statement of condition. It is no longer purely *devarim she-ba-lev*; he said it. The *gemara* reinforces this by noting that one might have thought he was merely expressing his feelings — venting his distress at having no heir — rather than formally conditioning his gift. The *gemara*'s conclusion is that we read his words as contextualizing and conditioning the gift, not as a mere emotional aside.

## Biblical Sources

### Torah Source Query

After establishing the mechanics and doctrinal underpinnings of *matnat shechiv mera*, the *gemara* turns to a foundational question: Rav Zeira asks in the name of Rav — *minayin l'matnat shechiv mera min hatorah* — where in the Torah do we find the basis for this institution? The question is significant because throughout the earlier discussion the institution has been described in rabbinic terms, yet now the *gemara* appears to be reaching for a biblical derivation.

### V'Ha'avartem

Rav Zeira's proposed derivation draws on the verse in the laws of inheritance (*Bamidbar* 27): *v'ha'avartem et nachalato levitto* — you shall transfer his inheritance to his daughter (in the absence of sons). The *gemara* asks: is there another type of active transfer analogous to this one? *Ve'eizo zo — matnat shechiv mera*. The Rashbam explains the *diyuk*: the verse could simply have stated *nachalato levitto* without using any verb, yet it employs the active verb *v'ha'avartem* — you shall transfer — which implies an active, directed conveyance. Since *yerusha* in the normal case requires no active act on anyone's part (the property simply devolves at death), the use of an active verb hints at a context in which a person can take a more active role in directing where his property goes. This, the *gemara* suggests, is the biblical hint for *matnat shechiv mera*.

### V'Natattem

Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, derives the principle from a different verse in the same passage: *v'netatem et nachalato le'achiv* — you shall give his inheritance to his brothers (in the absence of daughters). The same question is posed: is there another form of *netinah* analogous to this one? And again the answer: *matnat shechiv mera*.

### Verse Selection

One might ask why each *amora* chose a different verse. The *gemara* addresses this directly. Why did Rav Nachman not use the verse with *v'ha'avartem*? Because that verse is needed for a different *drashah* — the teaching of Rabbi (Rebi). As the *braita* records: in all other cases where the *parasha* discusses who receives the inheritance, the verb used is *netinah* — giving. But specifically when discussing the inheritance of a daughter in the absence of sons, the verse uses the word *ha'avarah* — transfer. This signals that the transfer to a daughter is peculiar, because a daughter, upon marriage, may bring the property into another tribe through her husband or son who inherits her. The use of *ha'avarah* — implying diversion — alludes to the transfer of *nachalah* from one tribe to another. Accordingly, that verse is occupied with Rabbi's *drashah* and cannot simultaneously serve as the source for *matnat shechiv mera*. Conversely, Rav Zeira did not use *v'netatem* because it is simply the normal way the verse speaks (*orcha dikra hu*) — an unremarkable verb that carries no special implication.

### Chizkiyahu Case

The *gemara* then turns from legal texts to narrative sources in *Tanach*. Rav Menashya bar Yirmeya derives support from the following verse: *bayamim haheim chalah Chizkiyahu lamut vayavo eilav Yeshayahu ben Amot vayomer eilav ko amar Hashem tzav leveitecha ki met atah* — in those days Chizkiyahu fell ill and was about to die, and the prophet Yeshayahu came to him and said: command your household, for you will die and you will not live. The instruction to *tzav leveitecha* — command your household — implies that a verbal directive issued in the context of anticipated death carries binding force. One might object that narrative passages in *Nach* do not ordinarily generate halachic norms. The answer is that they function at least as *giluy milta* — a reflection of existing practice — demonstrating that deathbed verbal commands were understood to be effective.

### Achitofel Case

The *gemara* anticipates a further objection: Chizkiyahu was a king, and the effectiveness of his verbal command might be attributed to royal authority rather than to the legal institution of *matnat shechiv mera*. To address this, Rami bar Yechezkel cites the case of Achitofel: *va'Achitofel ra'ah ki lo na'astah atzato vayachavosh et hachamor vayelech el beito el iro vayetzav el beito vayechaneik* — Achitofel saw that his counsel was not followed, saddled his donkey, went home, commanded his household, and hanged himself. The phrase *vayetzav el beito* — he commanded his household — establishes that a private individual's deathbed verbal command (*betzava'ah be'alma*) is likewise effective.

### Narrative Evidence

However, this case raises its own complications. Achitofel was not on a deathbed in the conventional sense; he was anticipating death because he was about to take his own life. This raises the question of whether the institution of *matnat shechiv mera* requires that the person be actually ill and dying, or whether it extends to anyone who has a reasonable anticipation of imminent death — such as someone entering a dangerous situation from which he may not return. Furthermore, the narrative might reflect only societal practice, not a binding legal institution. The *gemara* notes that Achitofel's case involves only *betzava'ah be'alma* — a verbal command alone, without a formal *kinyan* — which is precisely the *chiddush* of *matnat shechiv mera*. Whether these narrative proofs establish a *d'Oraita* institution or merely reflect an existing rabbinic practice that the *tanna'im* then read back into the verses will be addressed shortly.

## Achitofel & Weather

### Three Commands

A *braita* records three things that Achitofel commanded his sons before his death. First: *al tehyu bemachloket* — do not engage in strife and conflict. Second: *al timr'du b'malchus beit David* — do not rebel against the House of David, though rebellions against other kingships may be permissible. Both of these pieces of advice reflect the lessons of Achitofel's own catastrophic experience: he had joined the rebellion of Avshalom against David and the entire enterprise had collapsed. Third: if the *Yom Tov* of *Atzeres* (Shavuot) is a clear day (*barur*), plant wheat.

What is striking is that the *gemara* is now reading *vayetzav el beito* not as referring to the legal disposition of property — which was the earlier reading in the context of *matnat shechiv mera* — but as an ethical will: these are the pieces of advice and guidance Achitofel wanted to leave his children, not legally binding transfers of property. The tradition of writing or speaking an ethical will before death — conveying one's values and wisdom to one's descendants — is a practice with deep roots, and Achitofel's three commands represent a notable early instance.

### Weather Signs

The third of Achitofel's commands — to plant wheat if *Atzeres* is clear — immediately generates a dispute. One authority says the sign is a clear day; another says it is a cloudy day. The Sages of Nehardea then resolve the apparent contradiction: it is neither a day of perfect clarity nor one of complete cloud cover. Rather, even a partially cloudy day on which a north wind is blowing and scattering the clouds — *bahil ve'ruach tzafonit menasheves bo* — that too qualifies as the favorable sign (*zehu barur*).

### Holy Day Signs

The *gemara* then reports that Rav Papa told Rav Ashi: we do not teach this tradition about planting wheat in the context of Achitofel's commands. We teach it as a teaching of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi. And in that formulation, the relevant day is not Shavuot but rather *motzei Yom Tov acharon shel Chag* — the conclusion of the final *Yom Tov* of Sukkot, which is Shemini Atzeret. On that day, everyone would look to the smoke rising from the wood pile on the altar in the *Beit HaMikdash* to observe in which direction the wind was blowing, since the direction of the wind on that day was understood to forecast the character of the coming year's rainfall and agricultural conditions. The association with Shemini Atzeret is more logically coherent on several levels: it is the time when prayer for rain (*tefillat geshem*) begins, it is when one is approaching the planting season, and the judgment for water — critical for all crops — is associated with the festival of Sukkot.

### Wind & Crops

The *gemara* then elaborates on the significance of each wind direction as observed at the *maarchah* on the *mizbeiach*. If the smoke inclines toward the north (meaning a south wind is blowing it there): *aniyim smeichim u'ba'alei batim atzeivim* — the poor rejoice and the property owners are distressed. The reasoning: *gishmei shana merubim u'peirot markivim* — plentiful rain will cause the fruit to be abundant but prone to rapid spoilage, forcing sellers to lower prices quickly. This benefits the poor who buy cheap, but harms property-owning farmers whose produce deteriorates before it can command a good price.

If the smoke inclines toward the south (a north wind): *aniyim atzeivim u'ba'alei batim smeichim* — the poor are distressed and the property owners rejoice. Rain will be scarce, fruit less abundant but durable, prices high and sustainable — advantageous to the propertied class but harmful to the poor.

If the smoke inclines toward the east (a west wind): *hakol smeichim* — everyone is happy, as this signals a balance: sufficient abundance and good storage conditions. If toward the west (an east wind): *hakol atzeivim* — everyone is distressed, as this apparently signals scarcity combined with poor storage.

### Wind & Crops

A contradiction emerges from another source. That source states: *mizrachit olam yafeh* — the east wind is always good; *maaravit olam kasha* — the west wind is always bad. This directly contradicts the earlier ruling in which the east wind (causing smoke to go west) was described as bad for all. The *gemara* resolves the north-south contradiction by means of the famous mnemonic: *shulchan batzafon u'menorah badarom* — the table (*shulchan*), which held bread made from wheat, was in the north of the *Mishkan*, and the *menorah*, fueled by olive oil, was in the south. Accordingly, the north wind benefits wheat, the south wind benefits olives — *hai marbi dideih v'hai marbi dideih*, each promotes its own. The east-west contradiction is resolved differently: *ha lan v'ha lehu* — one ruling applies in the Land of Israel and the other in Babylonia. The Rashbam notes that Babylonia received ample rainfall, making an excess of rain counterproductive there, while conditions in the Land of Israel were different; which wind direction was favorable therefore depended on where one lived.

### Annual Forecast

A *braita* cited by Abba Shaul generalizes the principle: if the *Yom Tov* of *Atzeres* is a clear day, it is a favorable sign for the entire year. Rav Zevid then adds: if the first day of Rosh Hashana is warm, the entire year will be warm; if cold, the entire year will be cold.

### High Priest Practice

The *gemara* asks: *lemai nafka mina* — what practical difference does this make? The answer: *b'tzeluta d'Kohen Gadol* — it bears on the prayer of the *Kohen Gadol*. The *Kohen Gadol* observes the weather conditions on Rosh Hashana and uses them to calibrate his prayers when he enters the *Kodesh HaKodashim* on Yom Kippur. Knowing what kind of year is anticipated, he can direct his petitions with greater specificity and intention — whether praying for rain to moderate a hot year or for warmth to temper a cold one.

## Rabbinic vs. Biblical

### Rava's Position

With the extended digression on weather signs concluded, the *gemara* returns to the fundamental question of the status of *matnat shechiv mera*. Rava cites Rav Nachman: *matnat shechiv mera miderabanan be'alma hi* — the institution of *matnat shechiv mera* is purely rabbinic, not biblical. The reason: *shema titrof da'ato* — lest his mind become confused, a concern for *tiruf hadaas*.

### Rashbam Explanations

The Rashbam offers two distinct explanations of what *tiruf hadaas* means in this context. The first: if the Rabbis were to require a formal *kinyan* as a condition of the gift's validity, the dying person might not succeed in executing it in time — as people approach death they often lose mental clarity, and by the time the required formalities could be arranged, the person might no longer be capable of performing them. The institution was therefore created to spare him this obstacle.

The second explanation — and the one more widely cited — is a concern not for the person's mental capacity but for his physical welfare. If the Rabbis were to insist that a formal *kinyan* is required and that the dying person's verbal declaration alone is insufficient, the resulting anxiety, distress, and agitation could actually harm his health and hasten his death. The rabbinic institution was therefore created out of compassion for his condition: by making his words legally effective without further formality, the Rabbis ensured that his affairs could be settled without subjecting him to the emotional and physical strain of formal legal proceedings.

### Medical Autonomy

This second explanation has been applied by Rav Moshe Feinstein in several *teshuvot* addressing the question of patient autonomy in medical halachah. The challenge is well known: if there is a *mitzvah* to preserve life (*pikuach nefesh*), on what basis can a patient refuse treatment? Rav Moshe Feinstein finds an opening through this very principle. Just as the Rabbis instituted *matnat shechiv mera* out of concern that frustrating the dying person's wishes would itself constitute a threat to his life through *tiruf hadaas*, so too in certain medical situations, overriding a patient's self-determination and subjecting him to treatment against his will may cause him such distress that it actually hastens his death. In those cases, patient autonomy is supported not by an independent right of self-determination but through the back door of *pikuach nefesh*: granting the patient a measure of control is itself a life-preserving measure.

### Challenge: Debt Transfer

The *gemara* challenges Rav Nachman's position that *matnat shechiv mera* is purely rabbinic by citing a ruling attributed to Rav Nachman himself. The challenge involves a teaching of Shmuel about the sale of a debt document (*hamocher shtar chov l'chavero*).

### Shtar Chov Mechanics

Shmuel's ruling: if Reuven sells to Michael the debt that Shimon owes him, and subsequently Reuven forgives Shimon the debt (*vachazar umachalo*), the debt is indeed forgiven — *machul* — even though Reuven had already sold the document to Michael. The reason lies in the conceptual structure of debt transfer in halachah. A debt is a personal obligation; Shimon does not owe money to Michael directly — he continues to owe it to Reuven. What Michael has purchased is effectively the right to collect the debt and retain the proceeds; Michael is, in essence, Reuven's designated collector. Since the debt is still fundamentally owed to Reuven, Reuven retains the legal capacity to forgive it, even after having sold the document to Michael. The Rashbam notes that Michael's remedy in such a case is to pursue Reuven for damages under the principle of *diney d'garmi* — Reuven sold him something and then rendered it worthless — but Shimon is no longer obligated to pay Michael. Shmuel adds: *va'afilu yoresh mochel* — even if Reuven dies and his son inherits the debt, the son (as the continuation of the father) likewise retains the capacity to forgive it, and Michael would thereby lose his claim.

### Shechiv Mera Power

However, Shmuel himself rules that if a *shechiv mera* gave the debt to Michael as a *matnat shechiv mera* — rather than selling it in the ordinary fashion — then *eino yachol limchol* — Reuven can no longer forgive the debt even while still alive, and his son cannot forgive it either. The gift of the debt through *matnat shechiv mera* transfers it in a fundamentally more powerful way than an ordinary sale.

### Yerusha Model

The *gemara* now draws the implication. The more powerful transfer achieved through *matnat shechiv mera* is explicable only if the institution functions on the model of *yerusha* — inheritance. When a person dies and his son inherits, the debt is not merely assigned for collection; the son becomes the direct obligee, and Shimon owes the money to the son himself. If *matnat shechiv mera* is modeled on *yerusha*, then when the *shechiv mera* gives the debt to Michael, Michael becomes the direct obligee — equivalent to a *yoresh* — and neither Reuven nor his son can forgive it. But if *matnat shechiv mera* is a purely rabbinic institution built on the model of ordinary *kinyan*, it should produce no greater transfer than the ordinary sale of a debt document, in which case Reuven and his son would retain the capacity to forgive.

### Rabbinic as Biblical

The *gemara* resolves this tension with a formulation that is striking in its precision: *eima d'Rabbanan v'asu'uhu k'shel Torah* — it may be rabbinic in origin, yet the Rabbis structured it and gave it the legal mechanics of a biblical institution. Even though *matnat shechiv mera* is *d'Rabbanan*, it was constructed as if it were *d'Oraita* — specifically, as if it were *yerusha*. The gift therefore operates with the force of inheritance: the recipient becomes the direct obligee of the debt, and neither the original creditor nor his heirs can subsequently forgive it away. This formulation — *d'Rabbanan v'asu'uhu k'shel Torah* — reflects a broader phenomenon in halachah where rabbinic institutions are deliberately engineered to replicate the mechanics of biblical law, without being themselves biblical in origin.

### Internal Shmuel

It is important to note the scope of this resolution. The analysis of the debt transfer and the conclusion that *matnat shechiv mera* operates with the mechanics of *yerusha* is advanced specifically to reconcile Shmuel's position with itself — it is an internal resolution of what might otherwise appear to be a contradiction within Shmuel's own rulings. Other *amoraim* may not accept Shmuel's position on debt transfers, and accordingly the broader question of whether *matnat shechiv mera* is *d'Oraita* or *d'Rabbanan* remains a genuine dispute among the *amoraim*, not foreclosed by this particular resolution.
