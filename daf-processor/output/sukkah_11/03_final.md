# Sukkah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sukkah tachat Sukkah

### Canopy in Sukkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַיִת נָמֵי, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה, כֵּיוָן דִּקְבִיעַ — אוּהְלָא הוּא, דְּלָא גָּרַע מִקִּינוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this *halakha* that it is not sufficient to place his head out the window applies **also** to **a house even if it is not ten** handbreadths **high. Since it is a fixed** structure **it is** considered **a tent** in and of itself, **as it is no less** permanent **than** a bed with **four posts,** which is considered a tent even though the netting is less than ten handbreadths higher than the bed.

The Gemara on 11b takes up the case of a *sukkah al gabi ha-sukkah* — a double-decker *sukkah* — but approaches it through the lens of a related problem: whether a canopy erected inside a *sukkah* invalidates the person sleeping beneath it. The *Mishnah* had established that sleeping under a four-posted canopy (*kinofot*) in a *sukkah* is invalid, since such a canopy creates a distinct roof. By contrast, a two-poled tepee-shaped canopy (*naklitin*), which comes to a point rather than forming a flat roof, does not create a separate roofed space and is therefore permitted.

Rav Yehuda then cited a lenient reading in the name of Shmuel: even a true roof-shaped canopy is only a problem when it is also ten *tefachim* high. Only when both criteria are met — a flat roof and a height of ten *tefachim* — is the person considered to be dwelling in a separate enclosed zone rather than under the *schach* of the *sukkah*. On Shmuel's view, either criterion alone is insufficient to invalidate.

### Baraita contradicts Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נַקְלִיטִין שְׁנַיִם, וְקִינוֹפוֹת אַרְבָּעָה, פֵּירַס עַל גַּבֵּי קִינוֹפוֹת — פְּסוּלָה, עַל גַּבֵּי נַקְלִיטִין — כְּשֵׁרָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיוּ נַקְלִיטִין גְּבוֹהִים מִן הַמִּטָּה עֲשָׁרָה. מִכְּלָל דְּקִינוֹפוֹת — אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין גְּבוֹהִים עֲשָׂרָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear:** ***Naklitin* are two** posts **and *kinofot* are four** posts. If **one spread** a sheet **over four posts,** the area in the *sukka* beneath the sheet is **unfit;** if one did so **over two posts** the entire *sukka* is **fit, provided the two posts are not ten** handbreadths **higher than the bed.** This proves **by inference that** a sheet spread over **four posts** renders the area in the *sukka* beneath the sheet unfit **even if it is not ten** handbreadths **high.**

Against this, the Gemara cites a *baraita* that appears to say that either criterion independently is enough to invalidate. The *baraita* distinguishes between *naklitin* — two poles forming a tepee — and *kinofot* — four posts forming a tent with a flat roof. Spreading a covering over the *kinofot* invalidates the *sukkah*, while spreading it over the *naklitin* is valid — provided, however, that the *naklitin* are not ten *tefachim* above the bed. By inference, the *kinofot*, which has a flat roof, invalidates even when it is less than ten *tefachim* high. Either a roof or a height of ten *tefachim* suffices. This directly contradicts Shmuel, who requires both.

### Fixed vs. temporary structures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי קִינוֹפוֹת, דִּקְבִיעִי. וַהֲרֵי סוּכָּה עַל גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה דִּקְבִיעָא, וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּהֶכְשֵׁרָהּ כָּךְ פְּסוּלָהּ! אָמְרִי: הָתָם, דִּלְמִפְסַל סוּכָּה — בַּעֲשָׂרָה. הָכָא, דִּלְשַׁוּוֹיֵי אוּהְלָא — בְּצִיר מֵעֲשָׂרָה נָמֵי הָוֵי אוּהְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Four posts are different because they are fixed** in the bed and constitute a significant space even without the requisite height. The Gemara asks: **But a *sukka* atop** another ***sukka* is fixed, and** yet **Shmuel said: As** the criterion for **its fitness, so too** is the criterion for **its unfitness.** The upper *sukka* renders the lower *sukka* unfit only if it is ten handbreadths high. The Sages **say** in distinguishing between the cases: **There,** in the case of a *sukka* atop another *sukka*, where the measurement is **in order to disqualify** the lower ***sukka*, ten** handbreadths are required to render the upper *sukka* a separate entity. However, **here,** in the case of the four-post bed, **in order to consider** the covering **a tent, less than ten** handbreadths **is also** considered to be **a tent,** as it is fixed.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between fixed structures and temporary ones. The *kinofot* — poles attached to the bed as a permanent fixture — are qualitatively different from a portable pop tent. Because the *kinofot* is a more established feature, the Gemara is stricter with it, and a single criterion suffices to render it an invalidating enclosure. Shmuel's lenient requirement of two simultaneous criteria applies specifically to temporary, improvised tent structures. Once a structure is more fixed in character, either a flat roof or a height of ten *tefachim* independently establishes it as a separate zone.

### Double Sukkah requirements

This framework then addresses the case of *sukkah tachat sukkah* — two stacked *sukkot*, where the upper one invalidates the lower. The Gemara notes that a double *sukkah* is obviously a fixed structure; nothing could be more permanent than one *sukkah* built atop another. Yet Shmuel himself had stated, *ke-hichshara kach psula* — the same measure of ten *tefachim* that validates a *sukkah* is also what causes it to invalidate the *sukkah* below it. So why, in the case of *sukkah tachat sukkah*, does Shmuel require both criteria — roof and ten *tefachim* — when the structure is plainly fixed?

The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing the nature of the invalidity in each case. By *sukkah tachat sukkah*, the upper structure must function as a genuine *sukkah* in its own right in order to negate the lower one. That requires a full ten *tefachim* of height and a proper *schach* roof — both criteria together, because the claim being made is that the upper space constitutes an independent *sukkah*. In the case of a canopy or tent inside a *sukkah*, by contrast, the question is simply whether the person beneath it is considered to be in a separate enclosed space — in a *tent* — rather than under the *sukkah*'s *schach*. For that more modest claim, even less than ten *tefachim* suffices once the structure is sufficiently fixed, because we do not require it to qualify as a full *sukkah*, only as an *ohel* that interposes between the person and the *schach* above.

## Kila tent and tzitzit

### Sleeping in kila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי לַהּ, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מוּתָּר לִישַׁן בְּכִילַּת חֲתָנִים בַּסּוּכָּה לְפִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ גַּג, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁגְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say another version** of the previous discussion: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: It is permitted to sleep inside** a netted **bridal canopy in the *sukka* since it** is inclined and **does not have a roof, even though it is ten** handbreadths **high.**

A further statement in the name of Shmuel is then introduced: one who sleeps in a *kila* — a temporary draped canopy, something like mosquito netting, loosely suspended over a bed and not physically attached to it as a fixture — while undressed, may stick his head outside the *kila* and recite *keryat shema*. The reason is that Shmuel treats such a temporary canopy not as a tent creating a separate halachic space, but merely as a covering draped over the person, analogous to wearing a loose robe. Since it does not constitute a genuine enclosed zone, the person is considered to be in the open, not sealed off from the *sukkah* above. Tosafot notes that in Shmuel's view this also reflects his position that *libo ro'eh et ha-ervah* — the concern about one's heart facing one's own nakedness while reciting *shema* — is not a problem here, consistent with the *kila* being treated as a garment-like covering rather than a separate room.

### Baraita challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַיָּשֵׁן בְּכִילָּה בַּסּוּכָּה — לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּשֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ גַּג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **One who sleeps in** a bed with **netting inside the *sukka* did not fulfill his obligation.** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here** in the *baraita*? It is **with** a bed with netting **in** a case where, unlike a bridal canopy, **it has a roof.**

Against this, the Gemara cites a *baraita* that explicitly states the opposite: one who sleeps undressed in a *kila* may not stick his head outside and recite *keryat shema*.

### Ten tefachim resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: הָא לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה? לְעוֹמֵד בְּבַיִת עָרוֹם, שֶׁלֹּא יוֹצִיא רֹאשׁוֹ חוּץ לַחַלּוֹן וְיִקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **So too, it is reasonable** to understand the *baraita* in that manner **from** the fact **that it is taught** in **the latter clause** of that *baraita*: **To what is this comparable?** It is comparable **to one standing naked in** his **house, that he** may **not move his head out the window and recite *Shema*.** That is certainly ineffective. The fact that the *baraita* likens the bed with netting to a house indicates that it is netting at least ten handbreadths high. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is the correct understanding.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction in the same way as before: the *baraita* refers to a *kila* that is ten *tefachim* high. At that height, Shmuel concedes that the *kila* constitutes a separate enclosed zone and the person is halachically inside a tent, not merely covered by one. The *baraita*'s prohibition therefore does not apply to the lower *kila* Shmuel was discussing.

The Gemara adds a supporting observation: *hachi nami mistabra* — this reading is indeed compelling. The *baraita* concludes with a comparison: what does sticking one's head out of the *kila* resemble? It resembles a person who is standing naked inside a house and sticks his head out of the window — which obviously does not permit reciting *keryat shema*, since the person remains naked inside the house. Since the *baraita* draws this analogy to a house, it is evidently discussing a *kila* that functions comparably to a house — that is, one that is ten *tefachim* high and thus constitutes a genuine halachic enclosure.

### House comparison

The Gemara then adds a clarificatory remark, which Rashi identifies as something of a later gloss tying together the threads of the discussion: when comparing the *kila* to a house, one should not be misled into thinking that a house also requires ten *tefachim* to be problematic. On the contrary, a house is a permanent structure, and because it is fixed — *keivan dekavia, ohala hu* — it constitutes an *ohel* regardless of its height. It is no different in this respect from the *kinofot*. A permanent structure, even if lower than ten *tefachim*, is considered an enclosing space by virtue of its fixed character alone.

## Alternative formulation

### Two sides of criteria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נַקְלִיטִין שְׁנַיִם, וְקִינוֹפוֹת אַרְבָּעָה, פֵּירַס עַל גַּבֵּי קִינוֹפוֹת — פְּסוּלָה, עַל גַּבֵּי נַקְלִיטִין — כְּשֵׁרָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ נַקְלִיטִין גְּבוֹהִים מִן הַמִּטָּה עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. הָא גְּבוֹהִים מִן הַמִּטָּה עֲשָׂרָה — פְּסוּלָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ גַּג!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another question from what we learned: ***Naklitin* are two** posts **and *kinofot* are four** posts. If **one spread** a sheet **over four posts,** the *sukka* is **unfit;** if he spread a sheet **over two posts** the *sukka* is **fit, provided the two posts are not ten** handbreadths **higher than the bed.** It can be inferred from here: **But** if they **are higher than ten** handbreadths the *sukka* **is unfit even though it has no roof,** contrary to the opinion of Shmuel.

The Gemara now presents an alternative formulation of the same discussion. Rav Yehuda states in the name of Shmuel: it is permitted to sleep in a *kilas chasanim* — a bridal canopy — inside a *sukkah*, *lefi she'ein lah gag*, because it has no flat roof, *af al pi shegavoha asara*, even though it is ten *tefachim* high. This is the mirror image of the earlier version, which said: even though it has a roof, it is permitted if it is less than ten *tefachim* high. Together, the two formulations make explicit what Shmuel's position entails: to be problematic, both criteria must be present simultaneously — a flat roof and a height of ten *tefachim*. A tepee-shaped canopy that reaches ten *tefachim* but has no flat roof does not invalidate; and a flat-roofed canopy that is lower than ten *tefachim* does not invalidate. Only the combination of the two makes it a halachically independent tent.

### Posts and poles baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי נַקְלִיטִין, דִּקְבִיעִי. אִי קְבִיעִי, לֶיהֱוֵי כְּקִינוֹפוֹת! לְגַבַּי קִינוֹפוֹת — לָא קְבִיעִי, לְגַבֵּי כִילָּה — קְבִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Two posts are different** from the bridal canopy **because they are fixed** in the bed, and therefore the sheet over them is considered a tent even with an inclined roof. The Gemara asks: **If they are fixed** then **let them be** considered **like four posts** and let them render the *sukka* unfit even when they are less than ten handbreadths high. The Gemara answers: **Vis-à-vis four posts,** two posts are **not** considered **fixed** and therefore, they render the *sukka* unfit only when they are ten handbreadths higher than the bed. However, **vis-à-vis** a bed with **netting,** two posts **are** considered **fixed** and consequently, they render the *sukka* unfit even though they lack a roof.

The Gemara again challenges Shmuel from the *baraita* about *naklitin* and *kinofot*, which, as established earlier, indicates that each criterion independently invalidates when the structure is fixed to the bed. The Gemara asks: if the *naklitin* are fixed to the bed, why aren't they treated as strictly as the *kinofot*, which invalidates even below ten *tefachim*? Why does the *baraita* only invalidate the *naklitin* when they reach ten *tefachim*?

### Fixed vs. portable

The Gemara answers that the degree of fixedness is relative. Compared to a portable *kila*, the *naklitin* are more fixed — and therefore ten *tefachim* alone suffices to invalidate them even without a flat roof. But compared to the *kinofot*, the *naklitin* are less fixed — and therefore they do not rise to the level where a flat roof alone would suffice below ten *tefachim*. The *kinofot*, with four posts solidly attached to the bed and a flat roof spread across them, is more permanently integrated into the bed's structure. The *naklitin*, meeting only at a central bar to form a tepee, are comparatively more portable. Thus the *naklitin* occupy an intermediate position: more fixed than a *kila*, less fixed than *kinofot*, and the threshold for invalidation shifts accordingly.

## Rava's lenient ruling

### Kila permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: מוּתָּר לִישַׁן בְּכִילָּה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ גַּג, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁגְּבוֹהָה עֲשָׂרָה. כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: לָא אָתֵי אֹהֶל עֲרַאי וּמְבַטֵּל אֹהֶל קֶבַע. דִּתְנַן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נוֹהֲגִין הָיִינוּ לִישַׁן תַּחַת הַמִּטָּה בִּפְנֵי הַזְּקֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna taught: It is permitted to sleep in** a bed with **netting even though it has a roof** and **even though it is higher than ten** handbreadths. **In accordance with whose** opinion did Rabba bar Rav Huna teach this *halakha*? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda who said** that in principle, **a temporary tent does not come and negate a permanent tent, as we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda said: We were accustomed to sleep beneath the bed before the Elders.** Since a bed is a temporary tent relative to the more permanent *sukka*, even one sleeping beneath a bed is considered to be sleeping in the *sukka* and he fulfills his obligation in that manner.

Against the entire thrust of the preceding discussion comes a striking ruling from Rava bar Rav Huna: one is permitted to sleep in a *kila* inside a *sukkah* even when it has a flat roof and even when it is ten *tefachim* high. This appears to sweep away both criteria that Shmuel required — not merely because one is absent, but even when both are fully present.

### Rabbi Yehuda principle

The Gemara identifies whose opinion Rava bar Rav Huna is following: *k'Rabbi Yehuda*, like Rabbi Yehuda, *d'amar lo ati ohel arai u'mevatel ohel keva* — a temporary tent does not come and negate a permanent tent. Rabbi Yehuda is known as the opinion that treats a *sukkah* as a permanent dwelling, *dirat keva*. Against that backdrop, any canopy inside the *sukkah* — however well-constructed — is inherently temporary relative to the *sukkah* itself and therefore cannot override it. The person inside the canopy is still considered to be dwelling in the *sukkah*, not in the canopy.

### Under the bed precedent

The Gemara cites a *mishnah* as the basis for Rabbi Yehuda's principle: Rabbi Yehuda himself testified that it was their practice to sleep underneath a bed inside the *sukkah* in the presence of the elders, and the elders never objected. The implication is that sleeping beneath a bed — which forms a kind of overhead covering — was never considered to remove one from the *sukkah*'s space.

### Bed vs. kila distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** if the statement of Rabba bar Rav Huna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, **let him say** simply that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמַר הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי מִטָּה, דִּלְגַבָּהּ עֲשׂוּיָה, אֲבָל כִּילָּה, דִּלְתוֹכָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה — אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּלָא אָתֵי אֹהֶל עֲרַאי וּמְבַטֵּל אֹהֶל קָבַע, לָא שְׁנָא מִטָּה וְלָא שְׁנָא כִּילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If he said the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, I would have said that this applies only to a bed, which is made for** use **atop it** and not beneath it. Perhaps the reason a bed is not considered a tent in and of itself is that its primary purpose is to lie on top of it, not in the space beneath it. **However,** with regard to a bed with **netting, which is made for** use of the space **within it, say** that **no,** it is indeed considered a tent in and of itself and one who sleeps in it does not fulfill his obligation. Therefore, Rabba bar Rav Huna **teaches us** that **the rationale for** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda is that a temporary tent does not come and negate a permanent tent,** and **there is no difference** whether the temporary tent is **a bed** or whether it is the **netting** over a bed.

The Gemara anticipates an objection: perhaps Rabbi Yehuda's leniency applies only to sleeping under a bed, which is not designed to serve as a roof for the space beneath it. A bed is made for sleeping on top of it; the space underneath is incidental. But a *kila* — *letochah asuya*, made for the interior space beneath it — is designed precisely to function as a sheltering roof over those inside it. Perhaps in that case we should be stricter and say the person is genuinely under the *kila*'s roof rather than under the *sukkah*'s *schach*.

### Permanent Sukkah principle

Rava bar Rav Huna's ruling addresses exactly this concern by making explicit the full scope of Rabbi Yehuda's principle: *lo ati arai mevatel leh a-keva* — a temporary structure categorically cannot negate a permanent one. *Lo shena mitah ve-lo shena kilah* — it makes no difference whether we are speaking of a bed or a *kila*. The *sukkah* is always the permanent structure; everything inside it, whether designed for overhead shelter or not, is temporary by comparison, and the person is always halachically beneath the *sukkah*.

Tosafot rules that the *halachah* does not follow this position. We do not rule like Rabbi Yehuda here, and one may not sleep inside a *kila* that has both a flat roof and a height of ten *tefachim* inside a *sukkah*. The practical corollary for a canopy with only one of the two criteria — say, a low table with a *tefach*-wide surface but without ten *tefachim* of clearance below — would depend on whether that single criterion suffices, which in turn depends on whether the object is treated as fixed or temporary in the manner discussed throughout this sugya.

## Bending vines and gourds

### Invalid vines and gourds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִדְלָה עָלֶיהָ אֶת הַגֶּפֶן וְאֶת הַדַּלַּעַת וְאֶת הַקִּיסוֹס וְסִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבָּהּ — פְּסוּלָה. וְאִם הָיָה סִיכּוּךְ הַרְבֵּה מֵהֶן, אוֹ שֶׁקְּצָצָן — כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one trellised** climbing plants such as **a grapevine, or gourd** plant, **or ivy [*kissos*], over** a *sukka* while they were still attached to the ground, **and** then added **roofing atop them,** the *sukka* is **unfit. If** the amount of fit **roofing was greater** than the plants attached to the ground, **or if he cut** the climbing plants so that they were no longer attached to the ground, **it is fit.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה וְאֵין גִּידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ — אֵין מְסַכְּכִין בּוֹ. וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה וְגִידּוּלוֹ מִן הָאָרֶץ — מְסַכְּכִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle** with regard to the roofing of a *sukka*: **Anything that is susceptible to ritual impurity,** e.g., vessels, **or its growth is not from the ground,** e.g., animal hides, **one may not roof** his *sukka* **with it. And anything that is not susceptible to ritual impurity and its growth is from the ground, one may roof** his *sukka* **with it.**

The *Mishnah* presents the case of one who bends (*hidlah*) a grapevine, a gourd, or ivy (*kissom*) over the *sukkah* and uses it as *schach*. This is invalid because the *schach* is still attached to the ground — growth from the ground (*mechubar*) disqualifies material from serving as *schach*. However, if one adds a majority of valid *schach* alongside the attached growth, the overall covering is valid, since when materials are intermingled the majority determines the character of the whole.

The *Mishnah* then states a second path to validity: *o she-ketzatzan* — or if one cuts them. Once the vine is severed from the ground, it is no longer *mechubar*, and the resulting detached material is in principle valid *schach*. The *Mishnah* concludes with the general rule: anything susceptible to *tumah* or that grows from the ground is invalid for *schach*; anything not susceptible to *tumah* and that grew from the ground — once properly detached — is valid.

### Cutting the vine question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ יָתֵיב רַב יוֹסֵף קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, וְיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: אוֹ שֶׁקְּצָצָן כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאָמַר רַב: צָרִיךְ לְנַעְנֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef sat before Rav Huna, and he sat and said,** citing the mishna: **Or if he cut them, it is fit.** He added: **And Rav said** that it is not enough merely to cut the climbing plants; one **is obligated** to **move** the branches, thereby performing an action with the branches in order to render the roofing fit. When he placed the climbing plants atop the *sukka*, they were attached and therefore unfit roofing. When he ultimately cut them, it was as if the *sukka* were roofed by itself. In that case, the *sukka* is unfit due to the principle: Prepare it, and not from that which has already been prepared, derived from the verse: "You shall prepare for you the festival of *Sukkot*" (Deuteronomy 16:13).

The *Mishnah*'s statement that cutting validates the *schach* raises the question of *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy*. The act of placing the vine over the *sukkah* was done when the material was still attached and therefore invalid as *schach*. Once the vine is cut, it becomes valid — but has it been *made* into *schach*? The verse *chag ha-sukkot ta'aseh lecha* — you shall make your *sukkah* — is interpreted to require an active act of making; it is insufficient for valid *schach* to come into existence indirectly without being actively made as such. The classic illustration is digging a cavity in a haystack: the resulting *schach* overhead was never made, it simply remained in place. Similarly here, the vine was placed when invalid; the *making* occurred then, and the subsequent cutting that makes it valid is not itself an act of making.

### Ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy

The principle of *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy* thus stands as the central halachic question raised by the *Mishnah*'s case: does the cutting of the vine — a physical act that transforms the material into valid *schach* — count as the act of making the *schach*, or does it not?

### Rav vs. Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא: הָא שְׁמוּאֵל אַמְרַהּ. אַהְדְּרִינְהוּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְאַפֵּיהּ וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַטּוּ מִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ דְּלָא אַמְרַהּ שְׁמוּאֵל? אַמְרַהּ רַב, וְאַמְרַהּ שְׁמוּאֵל! אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא: הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אַמְרַהּ וְלָא רַב, דְּרַב אַכְשׁוֹרֵי מַכְשַׁר. כִּי הָא דְּרַב עַמְרָם חֲסִידָא רְמָא תְּכֵלְתָּא לְפַרְזוּמָא דְּאִינָשֵׁי בֵּיתֵיהּ. תְּלָאָן, וְלֹא פָּסַק רָאשֵׁי חוּטִין שֶׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said to** Rav Yosef: **Shmuel stated this** *halakha*. **Rav Yosef turned his face away** in anger **and said to him: Did I say to you that Shmuel did not say it? Rav said it, and Shmuel said it** as well. What is your point? **Rav Huna said to him: This is what I am saying to you, that Shmuel said it and not Rav, as Rav deems** the roofing **fit** merely by cutting them, without moving them, **as** in **that** incident **where Rav Amram the Pious cast the sky-blue dye,** i.e., ritual fringes, **upon the garment** [***pirzuma***] **of the people of his household.** However, **he attached them, but did not cut the ends of their strands** prior to tying them, i.e., he took a single string, folded it a number of times, and inserted it into the hole in the garment. Since the fringes were uncut when he tied them, he was uncertain whether they were fit for use in fulfilling the mitzva, due to the principle: Prepare it, and not from that which has already been prepared.

Rav Yosef was sitting before Rav Kahana and cited the *Mishnah*'s ruling — that cutting validates — and added that Rav requires one to also move the *schach* slightly afterward (*tzarich l'na'anea*). Rav Yosef attributed this stricter view to Rav. Rav Huna corrected him: that is Shmuel's position, not Rav's. Rav himself would say that cutting alone is sufficient, because for Rav the cutting is itself the act of making the *schach* valid.

## Rav's position on cutting

### Rav Huna correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב: מְפַסְּקָן וְהֵן כְּשֵׁרִין. אַלְמָא: פְּסִיקָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן. הָכָא נָמֵי: קְצִיצָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Amram **came before Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi** and asked him about the halakhic status of the ritual fringes. **He said to him** that **this** is what **Rav said: One cuts them** into separate strands **and they are fit.** There is no need to remove them, cut them, and reattach them to the garment as separate strands. **Apparently,** according to Rav, **their cutting is their preparation.** Cutting them qualifies as active preparation of the fringes. **Here too,** in the case of the roofing of a *sukka*, Rav holds: **Their cutting is their preparation,** and no further action is required.

Rav Huna's correction establishes that Rav Yosef had misattributed the position. The requirement to physically move the *schach* after cutting belongs to Shmuel, not to Rav. Rav's view is that the physical act of cutting constitutes the halachic making of the *schach*.

### Rav Amram tzitzit precedent

To support Rav's position, the Gemara cites a parallel from the laws of *tzitzit* involving Rav Amram Chasida. He inserted *tzitzit* strings through the corner holes of his wife's *tallis* — threading both corners simultaneously, so that a single long string passed through two holes at once — but did not cut the looped ends of the strings. In this method, what appears to be four strings is actually one continuous string folded over multiple times; only after cutting the loops are genuine separate strings produced. He came before Rav Chiya bar Ashi and was told in the name of Rav: *mifaskun vehein ksheirin* — one may snip them and they will be valid. The act of cutting is the act of making them into *tzitzit*. *Psikasun zu hi asiyasun* — their cutting is their making — even though the physical insertion of the strings through the hole had already been performed. This is considered a valid act of making because it was a physical act that produced the halachic transformation, even if it was not the act of winding or knotting.

### Cutting is making

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל לָא אָמְרִינַן פְּסִיקָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן? וְהָא תָּנֵי שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: הֵטִיל לִשְׁנֵי קְרָנוֹת בְּבַת אַחַת, וְאַחַר כָּךְ פָּסַק רָאשֵׁי חוּטִין שֶׁלָּהֶן — כְּשֵׁרִין. מַאי לָאו — שֶׁקּוֹשֵׁר וְאַחַר כָּךְ פּוֹסֵק! לֹא, שֶׁפּוֹסֵק וְאַחַר כָּךְ קוֹשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Shmuel hold** that **we do not say: Their cutting is their preparation? But didn't Shmuel teach in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya: If one cast** fringes **upon two corners** of a garment **simultaneously** by repeatedly inserting one strand into holes in both corners **and afterward cut the ends of their strands** resulting in two full-fledged fringes, the fringes are **fit. What, is it not** referring to a case **where one ties** the fringes as required **and afterward cuts them?** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is referring to a case where **he cuts** the strands **and afterward ties** them.

The parallel is applied directly to the *sukkah* case: *chisuch tzerichasun zu hi asiyasun* — the cutting of the vine is the making of the *schach*. Rav holds that a physical act which renders valid material that was previously invalid constitutes the halachic act of making, even when the physical placement preceded it. This is also the simpler reading of the *Mishnah* itself, which states plainly that cutting validates.

## Shmuel's stricter view

### Shmuel on cutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵינַן כָּנָף בִּשְׁעַת פְּתִיל — וְלֵיכָּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **we require** that it must be a single **corner at the time of threading** the strand through the hole. **And there is not** a single corner in this case, as although he ties the fringes separately, he threads the two corners simultaneously. Therefore, Shmuel **teaches us** that with regard to threading it is not a concern.

The Gemara then asks whether Shmuel indeed holds that cutting does not count as making — that is, whether Shmuel disagrees with the *psikasun zu hi asiyasun* principle.

### Shmuel vs. Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: תְּלָאָן וְלֹא פָּסַק רָאשֵׁי חוּטִין שֶׁלָּהֶן — פְּסוּלִין. מַאי לָאו: פְּסוּלִין לְעוֹלָם, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב? אָמַר לְךָ רַב: מַאי פְּסוּלִין — פְּסוּלִין עַד שֶׁיִּפָּסְקוּ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: פְּסוּלִין לְעוֹלָם. וְכֵן אָמַר לֵוִי: פְּסוּלִין לְעוֹלָם. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: פְּסוּלִין לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If **one attached** the ritual fringes **and did not** first **cut the ends of their strands, they are unfit. What, is it not** saying that the ritual fringes are **unfit forever** with no way to remedy the situation, **and** this is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav?** The Gemara answers that **Rav** could have **said to you: What** is the meaning of **unfit?** It means they are **unfit until they will be cut;** not that they are unfit forever. **And Shmuel said** that it means **they are unfit forever. And Levi also said: They are unfit forever. And likewise, Rav Mattana said** that **Shmuel said: They are unfit forever.**

Shmuel is found to have taught a ruling in the name of Rabbi Chiya regarding a case analogous to the one Rav Amram Chasida brought: one who inserted the *tzitzit* strings through both corners simultaneously and then, after completing all the knotting and winding, snipped the loops at the very end — the *tzitzit* are invalid. The cutting at the end of the process, after everything else has been done, is not considered an act of making. Shmuel thus squarely rejects the principle that cutting at the conclusion of a completed process can serve as the act of making.

### Alternative reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: בְּדִידִי הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא, וַאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל, וַאֲמַר לִי: פְּסוּלִין לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** that **Rav Mattana said:** There **was an incident** that happened **to me** involving this uncertainty with regard to ritual fringes, **and I came before Master Shmuel and he said to me: They are unfit forever.**

The Gemara, however, offers an alternative reading of Shmuel's statement. Perhaps what Shmuel is teaching is a narrower point: one may insert the strings through both corners simultaneously and then cut immediately at that early stage — before knotting — and only afterward proceed with the knotting and winding. In this reading, the strings are already cut and valid as separate strings before the substantive act of making (*asiyah*) begins. The *chiddush* of Shmuel's ruling on this reading is that there is no requirement of *knaf bish'at petil* — that the string must be in proper form at the very moment of insertion. Rather, one may insert it in an irregular way and cut immediately, so long as all subsequent knotting is done with properly separate strings. On this reading, Shmuel is not necessarily addressing the question of cutting at the very end; he is only addressing the permissibility of an irregular insertion followed by immediate cutting. The proper act of making — the knotting and winding — follows afterward when everything is already in valid form, which is entirely unproblematic.

### Baraita challenge to Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: תְּלָאָן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ פָּסַק רָאשֵׁי חוּטִין שֶׁלָּהֶן פְּסוּלִין. וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה: ״תַּעֲשֶׂה״ — וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי. מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: הִדְלָה עָלֶיהָ אֶת הַגֶּפֶן וְאֶת הַדַּלַּעַת וְאֶת הַקִּיסוֹס וְסִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבָּן — פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav's opinion from a different *baraita*: If **one attached** the ritual fringes **and** only **afterward cut the ends of their strands, they are unfit. And furthermore, it is taught** in another *baraita* **with regard to a *sukka*:** The verse states: "Prepare for you the festival of *Sukkot*" (Deuteronomy 16:13), and from the language of this verse the Sages derived the principle: **Prepare** it, **and not from that which has already been prepared. From here** the Sages **said: If one trellised a grapevine, a gourd** plant, **or ivy** over a *sukka* while still attached to the ground, **and** then he added **roofing atop** the vines, the *sukka* is **unfit.**

A *baraita* is then cited that poses a more direct challenge to Rav. It invokes the principle of *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy* in connection with the *sukkah* case: if one bent a gourd, vine, or ivy over the *sukkah* and covered it as *schach*, it is invalid. The Gemara asks: how does this illustrate *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy*? If the vine is still attached at the time we are discussing, the invalidity derives simply from *mechubar* — attachment to the ground — not from any principle of indirect making. The only way the *baraita* makes sense as an application of *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy* is if we are discussing a case where the vine was already cut — where the vine is no longer *mechubar* — and the invalidity comes from the fact that the physical placement was done when the material was still attached and invalid. The *baraita* then rules this invalid, demonstrating that cutting after the placement does not retroactively constitute the act of making. This is a substantive contradiction to Rav, who holds that the cutting is the making.

### Rav's response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּשֶׁלֹּא קְצָצָן, מַאי אִירְיָא מִשּׁוּם ״תַּעֲשֶׂה״ — וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דִּמְחוּבָּרִין נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁקְּצָצָן, וְקָתָנֵי פְּסוּלָה, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּלָא אָמְרִינַן קְצִיצָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן, וּתְיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב!
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances? If we say** that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he did not** subsequently **cut** the vines, **why does** the *tanna* **particularly** teach that it is unfit **due to** the principle: **Prepare** it, **and not from that which has already been prepared? Let him derive** that the climbing plants are unfit for roofing due to the fact **that they are attached** to the ground, unrelated to the manner in which they were placed. **Rather,** it must be referring to a case **where he cut them and** nevertheless, the *baraita* **is teaching** that the vines are **unfit, and learn from it that we do not say: Their cutting is their preparation; and** this is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — דְּשַׁלְפִינְהוּ שַׁלּוֹפֵי, דְּלָא מִינַּכְרָא עֲשִׂיָּה דִּידְהוּ. מִכׇּל מָקוֹם, תְּלָאָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ פָּסַק, קַשְׁיָא לְרַב! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav** could have **said to you: With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where he pulled** the branches until they broke off the tree. Since, in that case, **their** active **preparation is not conspicuous,** it does not render the climbing plants fit roofing. The Gemara asks: **In any case,** does that which was taught with regard to ritual fringes: If **one attached** the ritual fringes **and** only **afterward cut** their strands, etc., pose **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Rav?** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, it remains **difficult** according to Rav.

Rav's response is notably weak. He claims the *baraita* discusses a case where the vine was not cleanly severed but rather yanked out in such a way that it remains visibly intertwined with and sheathed in the surrounding growth — *d'shalfinehu shlufey* — so that the separation is not visibly apparent. Because the transformation is not manifest — *d'lo minkara asiya didei* — it cannot be considered a genuine act of making. The Gemara notes that this reframing implies a rabbinic level of invalidity: the material is technically detached, but because the change is not visually obvious, the Rabbis will not recognize it as a genuine act of transformation.

The Gemara is not satisfied. Whatever the resolution for the vine case, the *baraita* about *tzitzit* — stating that strings inserted through the hole and then cut remain invalid — poses a problem for Rav that is not addressed by the argument about visible transformation, since a clean snip of string loops is entirely visible. The Gemara concludes: *kashya l'Rav* — this remains difficult for Rav. Although the simple sense of the *Mishnah* supports Rav, the weight of the *baraita* evidence points toward Shmuel: cutting alone, once the physical making has already been completed, does not constitute the act of making.

## Berries in hadas debate

### Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי. עָבַר וְלִיקְּטָן — פָּסוּל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר יְהוֹצָדָק. וַחֲכָמִים מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this dispute is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** If black berries grew on a myrtle branch, one of the four species taken on *Sukkot*, and its berries were more numerous than its leaves, the myrtle branch is unfit for use in fulfilling the mitzva of taking the four species on *Sukkot*. However, if one picked enough berries so that the leaves were more numerous, it is fit, although one may not pick the berries on the Festival itself. If **he transgressed and picked them** on the Festival, **it is unfit;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Shimon bar Yehotzadak. And the Rabbis deem it fit** in that case.

The Gemara now attempts to establish that the debate between Rav and Shmuel over whether cutting constitutes making corresponds to a *tannaic* dispute — which would lend Rav's position some additional support. The candidate *baraita* involves an *hadas* that has too many berries, rendering it invalid for the *arba minim*. One went ahead and plucked the berries off while the *hadas* was already bundled together with the *lulav* and *aravah*. Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak rules: *avar ve-liktan pasul* — it remains invalid after plucking. The *Chachamim* rule it is valid. The question is why anyone would say plucking the berries while in the bundle is insufficient to fix the *hadas*.

### Making through plucking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לוּלָב צָרִיךְ אֶגֶד, וְיָלְפִינַן לוּלָב מִסּוּכָּה. דִּכְתִיב גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה: ״תַּעֲשֶׂה״ — וְלֹא מִן הֶעָשׂוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to explain the basis for the comparison between the dispute with regard to the roofing of the *sukka* and the dispute with regard to the myrtle branch. The Sages initially **thought that everyone,** Rabbi Shimon bar Yehotzadak and the Rabbis, agrees that in fulfilling the mitzva of the four species, the three species, i.e., the ***lulav*,** the myrtle branch, and the willow branch, **require a binding** by Torah law. Therefore, it is relevant to discuss preparation with regard to this binding. **And** the Sages also initially thought that everyone agrees that **we derive** the *halakhot* of ***lulav* from** the *halakhot* of ***sukka*, as it is written with regard to *sukka*: Prepare,** from which is derived the principle: Prepare it, **and not from that which has already been prepared, and** the same applies to the *halakhot* of *lulav* as well.

The Gemara initially proposes that the dispute maps directly onto the Rav-Shmuel debate. On this reading, everyone agrees that a *lulav* requires being bound into a bundle (*tzarich egged*), and that the act of binding the bundle is the halachic act of *asiyah* — the making — for the *lulav*. If so, when one binds the bundle while the *hadas* still has its berries, the bundle is made at a moment when one component is invalid. Subsequently plucking the berries makes the *hadas* valid — but the question is whether that plucking constitutes the act of making the bundle. The *Chachamim* who permit it would hold, parallel to Rav, that the transformative act — plucking — is the making. Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak, who invalidates, would hold, parallel to Shmuel, that the cutting or plucking of an already-assembled object cannot serve as the act of making.

### Ta'aseh applies to lulav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו, בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמַאן דְּמַכְשַׁיר סָבַר: אָמְרִינַן גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה קְצִיצָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן, וְגַבֵּי לוּלָב נָמֵי אָמְרִינַן לְקִיטָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן. וּמַאן דְּפָסֵיל סָבַר: לָא אָמְרִינַן גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה קְצִיצָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן, וְגַבֵּי לוּלָב נָמֵי לָא אָמְרִינַן לְקִיטָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן!
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** that the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to the following? That the one who deems** the myrtle branch whose berries were picked on the Festival **fit, holds** that **with regard to** the branches on **a *sukka* we say: Their cutting is their preparation, and** therefore, **with regard to** berries on the myrtle branch as one of the species bound with the ***lulav* as well, we say: Their picking is their preparation,** and no further action is required. **And the one who deems it unfit holds** that **with regard to** the branches on **a *sukka* we do not say: Their cutting is their preparation, and** therefore, **with regard to *lulav* as well, we do not say: Their picking is their preparation.** Therefore, since the myrtle branch was not prepared for use prior to the Festival, and it was bound together with the other species, it is considered already prepared and picking the fruit off the branch is not active preparation sufficient to render it fit.

The Gemara rejects this mapping and offers an alternative explanation. Perhaps both *tanna'im* agree with Shmuel that cutting does not constitute making, and they also agree that the *lulav* requires a bundle. Their dispute, rather, concerns whether the principle of *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy* applies to *lulav* at all. The principle is textually grounded in the verse *ta'aseh lecha* — you shall make — which appears explicitly by *sukkah* and by *tzitzit*. No parallel verse appears by *lulav*. The *Chachamim* may hold that even though a bundle is required, there is no requirement of an active act of making — the principle of *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy* does not extend to *lulav*, and therefore plucking the berries afterward is permissible. Rabbi Shimon ben Hotzadak holds that *lulav* is learned from *sukkah*, and just as *sukkah* requires an active act of making, so does *lulav*, rendering the post-bundle plucking insufficient.

### Lulav needs binding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא אָמְרִינַן גַּבֵּי סוּכָּה קְצִיצָתָן זוֹ הִיא עֲשִׂיָּיתָן, וְהָכָא בְּמֵילַף לוּלָב מִסּוּכָּה קָמִיפַּלְגִי. מַאן דְּמַכְשַׁר סָבַר: לָא יָלְפִינַן לוּלָב מִסּוּכָּה, וּמַאן דְּפָסֵיל סָבַר: יָלְפִינַן לוּלָב מִסּוּכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that explanation of the dispute. **No,** the fact is **that everyone agrees** that **we do not say with regard to *sukka*: Their cutting is their preparation, and here** in the case of the myrtle branch, it is **with regard to deriving** the *halakhot* of ***lulav* from** the *halakhot* of ***sukka*** that **they disagree. The one who deems** the myrtle branch **fit holds** that **we do not derive** the *halakhot* of ***lulav* from** the *halakhot* of ***sukka*,** and therefore the principle: Prepare it, and not from that which has already been prepared, does not apply to *lulav*. **And the one who deems** the myrtle branch **unfit holds** that **we derive** the *halakhot* of ***lulav* from** the *halakhot* of ***sukka*.**

The Gemara offers yet a third alternative. Perhaps both *tanna'im* agree that *lulav* is learned from *sukkah* and that the principle of *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy* applies. Their dispute concerns an entirely different question: whether the *lulav* requires a bundle (*tzarich egged*) at all. If no bundle is required, there is no act of making, nothing to which *ta'aseh velo min ha-asuy* could attach, and plucking the berries afterward is simply unproblematic — there is no issue of having made the object while it was invalid. If a bundle is required, then the binding creates the moment of making, the *hadas* was invalid at that moment, and subsequent plucking cannot remedy it.

## Lulav binding debate

### Sages vs. Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּלוּלָב צָרִיךְ אֶגֶד, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּיָלְפִינַן לוּלָב מִסּוּכָּה. וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, מָר סָבַר: צָרִיךְ אֶגֶד, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין צָרִיךְ אֶגֶד. וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: לוּלָב, בֵּין אָגוּד בֵּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ אָגוּד — כָּשֵׁר, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אָגוּד — כָּשֵׁר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אָגוּד — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **If we hold that *lulav* requires a binding, everyone agrees** that **we derive** the *halakhot* of ***lulav* from** the *halakhot* of ***sukka*** and the principle: Prepare it, and not from that which has already been prepared, applies to the *halakhot* of the four species as well. **And here** it is **with regard to the following** that **they disagree:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon bar Yehotzadak, **holds** that the *lulav* **requires a binding,** and therefore the myrtle branch is unfit; **and** the other **Sage,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds** that the *lulav* **does not require a binding,** and therefore, preparation is not relevant with regard to *lulav* and it makes no difference whether the berries were picked before or after the myrtle branch was bound together with the *lulav* and the willow branch. **And** they disagree **with regard to** the same topic as **in the dispute between these *tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A *lulav*, whether** it is **bound** with the myrtle and willow **and whether** it is **not bound, is fit. Rabbi Yehuda says: If it is bound it is fit; if it is not bound it is unfit.**

The Gemara now resolves the underlying question about *lulav* bundling by citing the relevant *baraita* directly. It teaches: *lulav bein agud bein she'eino agud kosher* — a *lulav*, whether bound together with the *hadas* and *aravah* or not, is valid. Rabbi Yehuda says: bound is valid, unbound is invalid.

### Verse about hyssop

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? יָלֵיף ״לְקִיחָה״ ״לְקִיחָה״ מֵאֲגוּדַּת אֵזוֹב. כְּתִיב הָתָם: ״וּלְקַחְתֶּם אֲגוּדַּת אֵזוֹב״, וּכְתִיב הָכָא: ״וּלְקַחְתֶּם לָכֶם בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן״. מָה לְהַלָּן בַּאֲגוּדָּה, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי בַּאֲגוּדָּה. וְרַבָּנַן: ״לְקִיחָה״ מִ״לְּקִיחָה״ לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?** From where does he derive this requirement by Torah law? The Gemara answers: By means of a verbal analogy, **he derives** the term **taking,** written with regard to the four species, **from** the term **taking** written with regard to **the bundle of hyssop. It is written there,** in the context of the sacrifice of the Paschal lamb in Egypt: **"Take a bundle of hyssop"** (Exodus 12:22), **and it is written here,** in the context of the four species: **"And you shall take for yourselves on the first day** the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of a date palm, and boughs of a dense-leaved tree, and willows of the brook, and you shall rejoice before the Lord your God seven days" (Leviticus 23:40). **Just as there,** with regard to the Paschal lamb, the mitzva to take the hyssop is specifically **in a bundle, so too here,** the mitzva to take the four species is specifically **in a bundle. And the Rabbis** hold: **We do not derive** the term **taking from** the term **taking** by means of the verbal analogy.

Rabbi Yehuda derives the requirement of bundling through a *gezeirah shavah* — a verbal analogy — on the word *lekichah*, "taking." The Torah says *u'lekachtem lachem* — take for yourselves — regarding the *arba minim*, and also says *u'lekachtem agudat ezov* — take a bundle of hyssop — in a different context. Just as the "taking" there involves a bundle of the relevant species bound together with others, so too the "taking" of the *lulav* requires that the four species be bound together. The connection between *lulav*, *hadas*, and *aravah* but not *etrog* is derived from the syntax of the verse itself: the three are linked by the conjunction *vav*, while the *etrog* — *pri etz hadar* — stands grammatically apart, preceding the others without a connecting *vav*.

### Aesthetic beautification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: לוּלָב מִצְוָה לְאוֹגְדוֹ, וְאִם לֹא אֲגָדוֹ — כָּשֵׁר. אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, כִּי לֹא אֲגָדוֹ אַמַּאי כָּשֵׁר? אִי רַבָּנַן, אַמַּאי מִצְוָה? לְעוֹלָם רַבָּנַן הִיא, וּמִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זֶה אֵלִי וְאַנְוֵהוּ״ — הִתְנָאֶה לְפָנָיו בְּמִצְווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which is taught** in this *baraita*: There is **a mitzva to bind the** myrtle and the willow with the ***lulav*. And if he did not bind it, it is fit. If** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, when one did not bind it, why is it fit? If** it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, why** is there **a mitzva** to bind it at all? The Gemara answers: **Actually, it is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. And** the reason that there is a mitzva to bind it is **due to** the fact **that it is stated: "This is my God and I will glorify Him [*ve'anvehu*]"** (Exodus 15:2), which they interpreted to mean: **Beautify yourself [*hitna'e*] before Him in** the performance of the **mitzvot.** The Rabbis agree that although failure to bind the three species does not render them unfit for the mitzva, the performance of the mitzva is more beautiful when the *lulav* is bound.

The Rabbis reject the *gezeirah shavah* and do not derive a requirement of bundling from the verse about hyssop. A *baraita* that states *lulav mitzvah le'agdo* — it is a *mitzvah* to bind the *lulav* — *ve'im lo agdo kosher* — but if one did not bind it, it is still valid — seems to fit neither Rabbi Yehuda nor the Rabbis cleanly. If Rabbi Yehuda, why would an unbound *lulav* be valid at all? If the Rabbis, what is the source of this *mitzvah* to bind? The Gemara answers: it is the Rabbis, and the *mitzvah* derives from the principle of *zeh keili ve'anveihu* — "this is my God and I will glorify Him" — interpreted as a general obligation to perform *mitzvot* in a beautiful manner. Binding the *lulav* is not a validity requirement but an act of beautification.

### Double knot requirement

This leads to a nuanced practical question about the nature of the *egged* according to the Rabbis. If the binding is purely aesthetic — a matter of making the *lulav* look beautiful — then any arrangement that makes it look attractive would suffice, and the specific halachic form of the knot might be irrelevant. But if the Rabbis understand the *mitzvah* of *egged* at a more conceptual level — not just visual beauty but the halachic coherence of a bundle — then even for the Rabbis the binding must constitute a proper bundle, which some hold requires a double knot. Those who require a double knot reason that without it, the binding does not rise to the conceptual level of *egged* even as a Rabbinic standard of *lechatchila*. Others hold that so long as the three species are firmly held together, the binding functions equivalently to a double knot regardless of its technical form. The underlying question — what precisely *zeh keli ve'anveihu* demands in this context and whether it is limited to the purely aesthetic — remains the crux of this debate and will receive fuller treatment in the third *perek*.
