# Niddah 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## No Blood First Time

### Chanina vs. Assi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: בָּעַל וְלֹא מָצָא דָּם, וְחָזַר וּבָעַל וּמָצָא דָּם, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: טְמֵאָה, וְרַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated** that the *amora'im* engaged in a dispute: If a husband **engaged in intercourse** with a virgin **and did not find blood, and** he **went back** within the first four nights **and** again **engaged in intercourse** with her **and** this time he **found blood, Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The wife is **ritually impure,** as this is menstruation blood. **And Rabbi Asi says: She is ritually pure,** as it is blood from the wound resulting from the act of intercourse.

The daf opens with an *itmar* — a reported dispute — concerning a case in which a man has intercourse with his young wife and finds no blood the first time, but does find blood the second time. Normally, when hymenal bleeding (*dam betulim*) occurs, one attributes subsequent bleeding to the hymen for some defined period. But here the first act produced nothing. How does one classify the blood that appears only upon the second encounter?

Rabbi Chanina rules that she is *teme'ah* — the blood must be treated as menstrual. His reasoning: *de-ee itah de-damei besulim hu me-ikara hava ati* — if hymenal blood were genuinely present, it would have come the first time. Since it did not, the second bleeding cannot be attributed to the hymen. Rav Assi, by contrast, rules *tehorah* — she remains *tahor*, and the blood is still attributable to the hymen. His basis: *dilma ke-de-Shmuel* — perhaps the first act was like what Shmuel describes.

### Shmuel's Ability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: טְמֵאָה, דְּאִם אִיתַהּ דַּהֲוָה דַּם בְּתוּלִים — מֵעִיקָּרָא הֲוָה אָתֵי. וְרַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: טְהוֹרָה, דִּילְמָא אִתְרְמִי לֵיהּ כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״יָכוֹלְנִי לִבְעוֹל כַּמָּה בְּעִילוֹת בְּלֹא דָּם״. וְאִידָּךְ — שָׁאנֵי שְׁמוּאֵל דְּרַב גּוּבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says: She is ritually impure, as if it is so that it is blood** from her hymen, i.e., the blood of her **virginity, it would have come at the outset,** after the first time they engaged in intercourse. **And Rabbi Asi said: She is ritually pure,** as **perhaps it happened for him** that he engaged in intercourse **like Shmuel** described. **As Shmuel said: I can engage in intercourse several times without** the appearance of **blood.** In other words, I can engage in intercourse with a virgin while leaving her hymen intact. **And the other** Sage, Rabbi Ḥanina, does not allow for that possibility, since he maintains that **Shmuel is different, as his strength** was **great.** Shmuel was particularly skilled at this, while others cannot accomplish this.

The Gemara invokes a remarkable statement: *de-amar Shmuel, yecholni liv'ol kama be'ilos belo dam* — Shmuel says, "I am able to have intercourse multiple times with a virgin without it producing blood." The precise mechanism is never fully explained. Tosafot notes that one possibility is intercourse without full penetration, though this seems an odd boast for Shmuel to make. Nonetheless, the theoretical point stands: it is conceivable that a first act of intercourse with a virgin produces no hymenal bleeding, and the second act does. Since the woman is still a virgin at that point, Rav Assi is willing to attribute the second bleeding to the hymen.

The word *yecholni* — "I am able" — is worth pausing over. It is a present-tense statement of capability, not a report of something Shmuel actually did in a specific case. Whether this reflects some abstract self-assessment or something more, the Gemara does not say.

### Shmuel Exceptional

The Gemara closes off Rav Assi's reasoning by noting: *shani Shmuel de-rav guvrei* — Shmuel is different because of his exceptional ability in this domain. For an ordinary person, if intercourse the first time produced no blood, we assume there was no hymenal bleeding to be had, and the blood found the second time cannot be attributed to the hymen.

## Age-Based Attribution

### Bogeret Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: בּוֹגֶרֶת נוֹתְנִין לָהּ לַיְלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי שֶׁלֹּא רָאֲתָה, אֲבָל רָאֲתָה — אֵין לָהּ אֶלָּא בְּעִילַת מִצְוָה וְתוּ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches the *halakha* of a young girl. The Gemara discusses the case of a girl who is older than twelve and a half. **Rav says:** The Sages **give a grown woman,** who engaged in intercourse on her wedding night, **the** entire **first night,** during which she may engage in intercourse with her husband several times. **And this statement** applies only **if she did not see** any blood. **But** if **she saw** blood, **she has only the relations** that consummate a marriage, which are **a mitzva, and nothing more.**

The Mishnah had enumerated the attribution periods for *dam betulim* according to the girl's age — *ketanah* (pre-pubescent), *naarah* (from the onset of puberty until twelve and a half years), and *bogeret* (after twelve and a half) — but the case of the *bogeret* was not addressed there in full. Rav fills the gap: *bogeres nosen lah lailah harishon* — a *bogeret* is given the first night, just as a *naarah* according to Beis Hillel. However, Rav qualifies this immediately: *ve-hani milei she-lo ra'asah* — this applies only if she has not yet had her period. *Aval ra'asah* — if she has already menstruated, then *ein lah ela bi'as mitzvah* — she is given only the first act of intercourse, not the entire night.

### Rebbi Four Nights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: מַעֲשֶׂה וְנָתַן לָהּ רַבִּי (ארבע) [אַרְבָּעָה] לֵילוֹת מִתּוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav's statement from a *baraita*: There was **an incident** involving a virgin who married, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **gave her four nights** in which to engage in intercourse **within twelve months** of her wedding when the blood is considered to be like blood resulting from the torn hymen.

The Gemara challenges Rav's position with a *ma'aseh* — a practical ruling by Rebbi himself: *ve-nasan lah Rebbi arba leilos mitoch shenaim asar chodesh* — Rebbi gave a woman four nights spread across twelve months. The Mishnah had said four nights, apparently assuming consecutive nights, but Rebbi was willing to extend that to four acts of intercourse even when distributed over an entire year. In this case, the woman's husband apparently traveled extensively, and they had intercourse only four times since their marriage; each time there was bleeding, and Rebbi ruled that all four instances were attributable to the hymen.

Now the question becomes: how old was this woman? The Mishnah's four-night allowance, according to Beis Hillel, applies to a *naarah*. A *naarah*, as Shmuel elsewhere states, is a period of only six months — *ein bein naarus le-vagrus ella shisha chodashim bilvad* — from the onset of puberty until the status of *bogeret*. Yet the case here spans twelve months. How does one fit four acts of intercourse into a twelve-month framework while remaining within the *naarah* category?

### Period Spanning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּיָהֵיב לַהּ כּוּלְּהוּ בִּימֵי קַטְנוּת,
> ״עַד שֶׁתִּחְיֶה הַמַּכָּה״ תְּנַן!
> אֶלָּא דְּיָהֵיב לַהּ כּוּלְּהוּ בִּימֵי נַעֲרוּת — נַעֲרוּת שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ מִי אִיכָּא? וְהָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין בֵּין נַעֲרוּת לְבַגְרוּת אֶלָּא שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים בִּלְבָד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If we say that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **gave her all of** those nights of purity **in** her **days** as **a minor,** in accordance with the ruling of the mishna: The Sages give her four nights, then he should have given her longer, as **we learned** in the mishna that according to Beit Hillel the blood may be attributed to the torn hymen **until the wound heals.** **Rather,** you will say **that he gave her** these four nights **all** during her **days** as **a young woman. Are there twelve months** when one has the status of **a young woman? But didn't Shmuel say: The difference** in time **between** becoming **a young woman and** becoming **a grown woman is only six months?**

The Gemara works through the possibilities. If all four acts occurred during *ketanus* — the pre-pubescent period — then the rule of *ad she-tichyeh ha-makkah* applies: one attributes bleeding to the hymen until there is evidence that the wound has healed, with no fixed limit of four nights. The four-night count is irrelevant there. If all four occurred during *naarus*, a *naarah* period lasts only six months, not twelve. The Gemara therefore explores whether the twelve-month span requires the case to have bridged multiple age-categories.

One possibility the Gemara entertains: two acts as a *ketanah* and two as a *naarah*. But this creates a problem: anything that occurred during *ketanus* counts as only one toward the four-night limit, so two acts as a *ketanah* plus two as a *naarah* totals only three, not four.

### Pre-Puberty Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא מֵרַב: הִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לִרְאוֹת תַּחַת בַּעְלָהּ, מַהוּ?
> אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כׇּל בְּעִילוֹת שֶׁאַתָּה בּוֹעֵל — אֵינָן אֶלָּא אַחַת, וְהַשְּׁאָר מַשְׁלִימִין לְאַרְבָּעָה לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** This too is difficult, as **Rav Ḥinnana bar Shelamya asked Rav:** With regard to a young girl who married before she reached puberty, and then **her time to see** menstrual blood **arrived** while she was **under** the authority of **her husband, what is** the *halakha*? Does she have the four nights when the blood is considered to be from her torn hymen? And Rav **said to him: All the acts of intercourse that you engage in** while she is still too young **are** considered as **only one** act of intercourse, **and the remainder,** i.e., three more acts of intercourse, **complete** the total number of **four nights.**

This leads to Rav Chanina bar Shalamya's question to Rav: *higia zman ve-lo nisset tachas ba'alah mahu* — what is the rule for a girl who was married before puberty and then reached puberty while married? How do the multiple acts of intercourse during her *ketanus* count toward the four-night allowance once she becomes a *naarah*?

Rav's answer: *kol bi'ah she-attah bo'el einah ela achas* — all the acts of intercourse during *ketanus* count as only one. The reasoning is that during *ketanus*, the framework is *ad she-tichyeh ha-makkah* — open-ended attribution without a fixed night-count — so all those acts collapse into a single unit. Once she becomes a *naarah*, she has three remaining acts to complete her four. *Ve-ha-shar mashlimin lah arba leilos* — the remainder brings her up to four.

### One-Three Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּיָהֵיב לַהּ אַחַת בִּימֵי קַטְנוּת וּשְׁתַּיִם בִּימֵי נַעֲרוּת, הָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר שֶׁלֶמְיָא מֵרַב...
> אֶלָּא, דְּיָהֵיב לַהּ אַחַת בִּימֵי קַטְנוּת, וּשְׁתַּיִם בִּימֵי נַעֲרוּת, וְאַחַת בִּימֵי בַּגְרוּת...
> לְעוֹלָם, דְּיָהֵיב לַהּ אַחַת בִּימֵי קַטְנוּת וְשָׁלֹשׁ בִּימֵי נַעֲרוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** you will suggest **that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **gave her one** night **during her days as a minor, and two** nights **during her days as a young woman.** This too is difficult, as **Rav Ḥinnana bar Shelamya asked Rav...** **Rather,** you will suggest **that he gave her one** night **during her days as a minor, and two** nights **during her days as a young woman, and one** night **during her days as a grown woman...** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the correct explanation is **that** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **gave her one** night **during her days as a minor and three** nights **during her days as a young woman.**

This still does not resolve how Rebbi's case spans twelve months within the *naarah* framework. The Gemara settles on the following reconstruction: *ella d'yahav lah achas bi-yemei katnus u-shtayim bi-yemei naarus ve-achas bi-yemei bagrus* — one act as a *ketanah*, two as a *naarah*, and one as a *bogeret*, totaling four, spread across twelve months.

But this raises its own difficulty. If the case includes an act as a *bogeret*, and Rav rules that a *bogeret* who has already menstruated receives only the first act of intercourse — not the entire night — then how does Rebbi's framework of four nights accommodate that? The Gemara reasons: if we accept that a *bogeret* normally receives more than one night, then one can say that multiple acts as a *ketanah* or *naarah* reduce the *bogeret*'s allowance by one — just as multiple *ketanah* acts reduce the *naarah* count by all but one. But if Rav holds that a *bogeret* receives at most one act (*bi'as mitzvah*), there is no room for the four-night count to span into *bagrus* at all.

The Gemara resolves this tension by abandoning the *bogeret* component entirely: *le-olam d'yahav lah achas bi-yemei katnus ve-gimel bi-yemei naarus* — in fact, she bled once as a *ketanah* and three times as a *naarah*. There is no need to assume the gap between each act was three months. If the gap between acts was two months, three acts of intercourse as a *naarah* span six months exactly, and with the preceding act as a *ketanah* accounting for the first half of the year, the twelve-month total is accounted for without ever entering *bagrus*.

## Kashrering Digression

### Vat Cleaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיוּ גִּתָּיו וּבֵית בַּדָּיו טְמֵאוֹת, וּבִקֵּשׁ לַעֲשׂוֹתָן בְּטָהֳרָה, כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? הַדַּפִּין וְהַלּוּלָבִין וְהָעֲדָשִׁין — מְדִיחָן,
> הָעֲקָלִים שֶׁל נְצָרִים וְשֶׁל בִּצְבּוּץ — מְנַגְּבָן, שֶׁל שִׁיפָא וְשֶׁל גֶּמִי — מְיַישְּׁנָן. וְכַמָּה מְיַישְּׁנָן? שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מַנִּיחָן מִגַּת לַגַּת וּמִבַּד לְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: In the case of one **whose winepresses or olive presses were impure and he wished to prepare** his grapes and olives **in** a state of **ritual purity, how does he act?** He should **rinse the planks** used to press the grapes in the winepress, **and the palm branches** used as brooms, **and the troughs,** and he should **clean the** pressing **baskets** with ashes and water if they were made **of palm leaves or of hemp [*bitzbutz*], or leave them dormant** if they were made **of bast [*shifa*] or of reeds,** because these absorb more of the wine. **And how long** must one **leave them dormant?** He must leave them for **twelve months. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: He can lay them aside from** one **wine-pressing** season **to** the next **wine-pressing** season, **or from** one **olive-pressing** season **to** the next **olive-pressing** season.

At this point the Gemara introduces what appears at first to be an unrelated topic — the *kashering* of wine vats and olive presses — but does so in order to clarify the meaning of *onah*, a term that has arisen in the context of hymenal bleeding attribution. The Mishnah discusses the case where a person's wine vats (*git*) or olive presses (*beis bad*) became *tamei* — whether because the owner was formerly an *am ha-aretz* and was not careful about *tumah*, or because something *tamei* came into contact with them — and the owner now wishes to use them for *taharos*. How does one clean them?

The Mishnah distinguishes by material. Boards and broom-like implements (*dafin*, *lulavim*) and the vat itself (*adashim*) must be rinsed — *madichan*. Wicker or hemp items (*akalim shel netzarim* or *shel bitzbutz*) need only be dried — *menagvan*. More absorbent materials, such as *shifa* and *gami*, cannot be cleaned by rinsing or drying alone. For these, one must allow them to age — *meyashnan*.

### Twelve Months

The Gemara's resolution is: *im kein libo nokfo ve-poresh* — if that is so, his heart will trouble him and he will separate on his own. The point is not that a *ba'al nefesh* must withdraw mid-act, but that a man of religious sensitivity will naturally be alert and will separate as soon as he becomes aware of bleeding. The Gemara acknowledges the concern but treats the *ba'al nefesh*'s instinct as the mechanism of compliance.

How long must one let these absorbent materials age? *Yod-beis chodesh* — twelve months. This twelve-month rule is notable because some *poskim* invoke it as a model for objects that cannot be *kashered* by *hagalah* in the conventional sense — pottery and the like — reasoning that after twelve months any absorbed liquid has dissipated. The context here involves cold absorption, not hot, which is a meaningful distinction, but the precedent is at least cited in those discussions.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers an alternative formulation: not twelve months, but *mi-gat le-gat u-mi-bad le-bad* — from one pressing season to the next. The Gemara asks whether this amounts to the same thing as twelve months, and concludes that there is a practical difference: *eika beinaihu charfei ve-aflei* — if one harvest comes early and the next late, the gap could be more or less than a year.

### Onah Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה עוֹנָה? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹ יוֹם אוֹ לַיְלָה. רַבִּי חָנָה שְׁאוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי חָנָה בַּר שְׁאוּנָא, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲצִי יוֹם וַחֲצִי לַיְלָה.
> וְאָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: וְלָא פְּלִיגִי — הָא בִּתְקוּפַת נִיסָן וְתִשְׁרִי, הָא בִּתְקוּפַת תַּמּוּז וְטֵבֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And how long is** the **period** of time referred to in the *baraita*? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Either** the **day or** the **night. Rabbi Ḥana She'ona, and some say Rabbi Ḥana bar She'ona, say** that **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Half a day and half a night.** **And Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak said: And** the Sages **do not disagree.** The ruling of **this** one, who says either a day or a night, is stated **with regard to** the equinox of the **season of Nisan or Tishrei,** when the days and night are equal; whereas the ruling of **that** one, who says half a day and half a night, is stated **with regard to** the solstice of the **season of Tammuz,** i.e., summer, **or Tevet,** i.e., winter, which have the longest day or night respectively, so that half the day and half the night together amount to twelve hours.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *o yom o layla* — either a day or a night, which is the standard definition of an *onah* as a twelve-hour period.

A second tradition, attributed to Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, states: *chatzi yom ve-chatzi layla* — half a day and half a night. Rav Shmuel bar Yitzchak reconciles the two: *ve-lo peligi* — they do not actually disagree. *Ha bi-tekufas Nissan ve-Tishrei ha bi-tekufas Tammuz ve-Teves* — one formulation applies to spring or fall, when day and night are roughly equal, and a full day or full night equals twelve hours. The other applies to summer or winter, when days and nights are unequal; in those seasons, twelve hours is best expressed as half the day plus half the night.

### Wedding Night Onah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, אֵימָא גַּבֵּי נִדָּה חֲצִי יוֹם וַחֲצִי לַיְלָה! וְהָא ״לַיְלָה וַחֲצִי יוֹם״ קָאָמַר! אֶלָּא: אִי לַיְלָה דְּנִיסָן וְתִשְׁרִי, אִי חֲצִי יוֹם וַחֲצִי לַיְלָה דְּטֵבֵת וְתַמּוּז.
> וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שָׁאנֵי כְּתוּבָּה — דְּמַגְּהִי בַּהּ טְפֵי, עַד דְּחָתְמִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here too,** one can **say with regard to a menstruating woman** that he meant **half a day and half a night.** The Gemara raises a difficulty. **But** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **said a night and half a day.** The Gemara clarifies: **Rather,** this is what Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel meant: **Either a night during Nisan or Tishrei,** which is twelve hours, **or half a day and half a night during Tevet or Tammuz,** which also equals twelve hours. **And if you wish, say** instead: With regard to the purity of wine, a period is twelve hours. But in the case of marriage the time frame **is different,** due to the **marriage contract,** which is signed before the marriage is consummated, **as** there are those **who delay for a lengthy** period **until they** complete and **sign it.**

This raises the question of how to reconcile the Gemara's statement elsewhere that Beis Hillel allows attribution of hymenal bleeding for a *lailah va-chatzi yom* — a night and half a day — with the standard definition of *onah* as twelve hours. The Gemara first suggests: perhaps here too we should say *chatzi yom ve-chatzi layla*, half a night and half a day — not a full night and then half a day.

The Gemara then offers a second resolution: *shani kesuva de-mashi bah tuvei* — the wedding context is different because the *kesubah* negotiations require extensive time. In the period when the *kesubah* was a live negotiated document, it was not unusual for the *chasan* and *kallah* to be occupied with those details well into the night before they were finally alone. When Beis Hillel says "a night and half a day," the implicit assumption is that the couple does not actually begin their private time until the middle of the night. So "a night and half a day" from the start of nightfall effectively amounts to twelve hours from the time they are actually alone together.

## Major Shift

### Boel Biat Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הֲלָכָה — בּוֹעֵל בְּעִילַת מִצְוָה, וּפוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § Although the mishna provides a certain period of time for both a minor and a young woman during which they may attribute any blood to the torn hymen, nevertheless **Rav and Shmuel both say** that the ***halakha*** is that the groom **engages in relations** that consummate a marriage, which are **a mitzva, and** then he **separates** from his wife.

The Gemara now arrives at what is announced as a fundamental shift in the practical *halacha*. *Rav ve-Shmuel de-amri tarvaihu, halacha, bo'el biat mitzvah u-poresh* — Rav and Shmuel both rule: one has intercourse the first time as an act of *mitzvah*, and then separates. This is the *halacha*, regardless of the circumstances.

At first reading, this might seem continuous with what the Mishnah already said about a *bogeret she-ra'asah* — a mature woman who has already menstruated — who receives only the first act of intercourse. But the crucial difference lies in the added word *u-poresh* — and separates. Until now, the Mishnah's framework was that any bleeding during the permitted period is *not* a problem; it is attributed to the hymen, and the couple may continue. Now Rav and Shmuel are saying: after that first act, separate — because the blood of even the first act is being treated as problematic *niddah* blood. This is the shift: hymenal bleeding is no longer being attributed as a category distinct from *dam niddah*; it is being treated as *niddah* blood, and the couple must separate even after the very first act.

### Ulla's Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: כִּי הָווּ בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בְּתִינוֹקֶת, לָא הֲווֹ מַסְּקִי מִינַּהּ אֶלָּא כִּדְמַסֵּיק תַּעֲלָא מִבֵּי כְרָבָא, וּמְסַיְּימִי בַּהּ הָכִי: בּוֹעֵל בְּעִילַת מִצְוָה וּפוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proves that this ruling of Rav and Shmuel, based on the *baraita*, is also the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish. **Ulla said: When we were** learning the topic **of a young girl** who saw blood on her wedding night with **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish, they brought up from it only** the amount **of** earth **that the fox brings up from a plowed field,** i.e., they did not establish the *halakha* as stated in the mishna. **And they concluded** the discussion in **this** manner: The groom **engages in relations** that consummate a marriage, which are **a mitzva, and** then he **separates** from his wife.

Ulla makes this explicit in striking terms. When Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish would learn the *perek* of *tinoukes* — the chapter now under discussion — *lo havu maskei mineh ela ke-masik ta'ala mi-bei karda* — they would derive from it no more practical law than a fox takes from a plowed field. A plowed field has no crops; the fox finds nothing. The entire chapter, in other words, yields no operative ruling, because the conclusion swallows everything: *bo bi'as mitzvah u-poresh*. Have intercourse the first time, then separate. None of the distinctions — *ketanah*, *naarah*, *bogeret*, four nights, the healing of the wound — matter in practice.

## Stringency Reasons

### Wedding Night Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בַּעַל נֶפֶשׁ לֹא יִגְמוֹר בִּיאָתוֹ! אָמַר לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, לִבּוֹ נוֹקְפוֹ וּפוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to Rav Ashi,** in light of this ruling that the groom must separate from his bride after completing the act of intercourse: **If that is so, a pious person** should **not complete his** act of **intercourse,** lest he unintentionally continue longer than is permitted. Rav Ashi **said to him** in reply: The Sages did not issue such a decree, because **if so, his heart will strike him** with fear that perhaps his wife will begin to bleed while he is engaged in the act of intercourse, **and he will separate** from his wife completely and will not consummate the marriage.

Rav Abba raises an obvious and pointed question to Rav Ashi: *ela mei-ata ba'al nefesh lo yigmor bi'aso* — if that is the rule, then a person of religious sensitivity (*ba'al nefesh*) would not even be able to complete the act of intercourse on the wedding night. If the blood that appears during the first act is treated as *niddah* blood, then even in the middle of consummating the marriage the couple would be required to stop.

## Stringency Reasons…

### Ba'al Nefesh

The Gemara's resolution is: *im kein libo nokfo ve-poresh* — if that is so, his heart will trouble him and he will separate on his own. The point is not that a *ba'al nefesh* must withdraw mid-act, but that a man of religious sensitivity will naturally be alert and will separate as soon as he becomes aware of bleeding. The Gemara acknowledges the concern but treats the *ba'al nefesh*'s instinct as the mechanism of compliance.

The term *ba'al nefesh* here means someone who is religiously scrupulous, inclined to be *machmir* and careful about transgression — not psychological sensitivity in some aesthetic sense. And notably, the Gemara itself acknowledges that in this context such stringency may be misplaced. It is *perishus* — excessive abstention — rather than required conduct.

### Stringency Origins

The underlying logic of this ruling is, frankly, difficult to reconstruct. It is clear from the Mishnah and from the Gemara's own earlier discussions that *dam betulim* is biologically distinct from *dam niddah*: it is *dam makkah*, the blood of a wound, not uterine menstrual blood. Treating it as *dam niddah* is therefore a departure from the strictly logical *halacha* of the Mishnah. What drove this departure?

The most plausible explanation is that it reflects the broader movement of stringency in all matters of *niddah* blood — a movement we will see crystallize later in the tractate in Rabbi Zeira's statement that *benot Yisrael hechmiru atzman* — Jewish women took upon themselves stringencies beyond the letter of the law, treating even the smallest drop of blood as if it created the status of *zavah*, requiring seven clean days. Rav and Shmuel's ruling likely flows from this same spirit: rather than maintaining careful distinctions between types of blood, one simply applies the strictest possible treatment across the board. Whether or not Rav and Shmuel historically preceded Rabbi Zeira, the general direction of the movement is the same.

### Ramban Rejected

The *Ramban* drew a radical conclusion from this principle. If the rabbis ruled that *dam betulim* — a wound-bleed that is known not to be *dam niddah* — is treated as *niddah* blood, does this not indicate that henceforth all *dam makkah* (blood from any wound) should be treated as *dam niddah*? This reading is rejected by all other *rishonim* as untenable. The implications of treating any wound-blood as *niddah* blood would be severe and practically unworkable.

### Rishonim Response

The other *rishonim* respond that the stringency regarding *dam betulim* is categorically different from wound-blood in general. *Dam betulim* is a one-time event, universal, and unavoidable: every bride experiences it. It was therefore possible for the rabbis to apply a blanket stringency in this specific, bounded context. Persistent or recurring wound-blood — which could arise from any number of ongoing medical conditions — cannot be governed by the same logic, and the *Ramban*'s conclusion is accordingly rejected.

### Contamination Fear

One additional rationale does appear in the sources: the Gemara had earlier introduced the concept of *dam chimmud* — bleeding potentially caused by uterine excitement at the moment of arousal. The concern, on this reading, would be that the trauma of the hymen rupturing during the first act of intercourse, combined with the excitement of the moment, may produce some admixture of uterine blood alongside the hymenal blood, making the blood an uncertain compound. Treating the whole as *niddah* blood would then be a precaution against this contamination. This rationale is noted, but it is far from convincing as a primary justification.

### Post-Tumah Stringency

One observation worth pausing over — and it is a striking one — is that this entire layer of stringency regarding hymenal blood is a post-*tumah* development. Throughout the earlier portions of the tractate, the laws of *tumah va-tahara* drove all the discussion, and yet within that framework the rules around hymenal bleeding and marital relations were, comparatively, fairly structured and manageable. Now, in a world no longer concerned with *tumah* in a practical sense, we see profound new stringencies introduced that have no precedent whatsoever in the *tumah* framework. The movement toward strictness intensified precisely after the concern about *tumah* receded — a paradox that is historically significant and underscores that this stringency belongs to a different, later cultural and legal moment.

## Major Shift…

### Rav Chisda Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב חִסְדָּא: מַעֲשֶׂה וְנָתַן לָהּ רַבִּי אַרְבָּעָה לֵילוֹת מִתּוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda raises an objection** from the *baraita*: There was **an incident** involving a virgin who married, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **gave her four nights** to engage in intercourse **within twelve months** of her wedding. This indicates that the husband does not have to separate from his wife immediately after the first act of intercourse is completed.

Rav Chisda presses a formal objection: *mativ ve-nasan lah Rebbi arba leilos mitoch yod-beis chodesh* — he cites the ruling of Rebbi, who gave a woman four nights spread across twelve months. This is a *ma'aseh* — a practical ruling by one of the greatest authorities — and it directly contradicts the principle of *bo bi'as mitzvah u-poresh*. Indeed, the Gemara notes that Rav Chisda's challenge could have been framed even more broadly: why cite Rebbi's specific ruling when the Mishnah itself — in its entirety — contradicts Rav and Shmuel? The whole *perek* proceeds on the assumption that hymenal bleeding is attributed as such, and none of it says *u-poresh*. Rav Chisda chose to cite Rebbi's ruling as a *ma'aseh rav* — evidence that the Mishnah's approach was not merely theoretical but was actually applied in practice.

### Rabbinical Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינְהוּ דַּעֲבַדוּ כְּרַבּוֹתֵינוּ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבּוֹתֵינוּ חָזְרוּ וְנִמְנוּ — בּוֹעֵל בְּעִילַת מִצְוָה וּפוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the objection: **In any case,** this *baraita* is **difficult for Rav and Shmuel.** The Gemara answers that **they acted in accordance with** the opinion attributed to **our Sages, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Our Sages returned and were counted** again, i.e., they voted and decided, that the groom **engages in relations** that consummate a marriage, which are **a mitzva, and** subsequently he **separates** from his wife.

The Gemara's resolution is decisive: *inhu de-avdi ke-raboseinu. De-tanya: raboseinu chazru ve-nimnu* — Rav and Shmuel were ruling in accordance with a subsequent rabbinical consensus. After the Mishnah was finalized, the rabbis convened again and took a vote — *chazru ve-nimnu* — and the ruling that emerged was *bo bi'as mitzvah u-poresh*. Rav and Shmuel were not contradicting the Mishnah through their own authority; they were implementing the later consensus that superseded it.

It should be noted, however, that the historical consolidation of this approach was not instantaneous. The evidence suggests that well into the Geonic period there were divergent practices regarding how strictly to treat hymenal blood as *niddah* blood, and the position that *dam betulim* is fully treated as *dam niddah* — with all that implies for the wedding night — represents a development that took considerable time to become universally accepted.
