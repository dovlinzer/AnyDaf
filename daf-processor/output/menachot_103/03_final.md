# Menachot 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah Intro

### Cases Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחָה מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״ – יָבִיא מִן הַחִטִּים. ״קֶמַח״ – יָבִיא סוֹלֶת. ״בְּלֹא שֶׁמֶן וּבְלֹא לְבוֹנָה״ – יָבִיא שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה.
>
> ״חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן״ – יָבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם, ״עִשָּׂרוֹן וּמֶחֱצָה״ – יָבִיא שְׁנַיִם, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁלֹּא הִתְנַדֵּב כְּדֶרֶךְ הַמִּתְנַדְּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a meal offering from barley, should bring** the meal offering **from wheat,** as voluntary meal offerings are brought exclusively from wheat. One who says: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering from **flour, should bring** the meal offering from **fine flour,** as it is written: "His offering shall be of fine flour" (Leviticus 2:1). If one vows to bring a meal offering **without oil and without frankincense, he should bring** it with **oil and frankincense,** as voluntary meal offerings require oil and frankincense.
>
> If one vows to bring as a meal offering **half a tenth** of an ephah, **he should bring a complete tenth** of an ephah, the minimum measure of a voluntary meal offering. If one vows to bring a meal offering of **a tenth and a half** an ephah, **he should bring two** tenths. **Rabbi Shimon deems** one **exempt** from bringing a meal offering in all these cases. This is because the vow does not take effect, **as he did not pledge in the manner of those who pledge.**

The daf opens at the *mishnah* approximately six lines from the top of 103a. The subject is a person who takes a vow (*neder*) to bring a *minchah* — a meal offering — but formulates the vow in terms that do not match any recognized category of voluntary *minchah*. The question throughout is: what are the consequences? Is the person bound, and if so, to what?

The *mishnah* presents a series of such cases. One who says *harei alai minchah min ha-se'orim* — "I obligate myself with a *minchah* of barley" — must nonetheless bring a standard *minchah* of wheat flour, since there is no voluntary *minchah* of barley (barley *menachot* are limited to the *minchat choteh* and the *minchah* of the *sotah*). Similarly, one who vows a *minchah* of bran (*kemach*) must bring fine flour (*solet*). One who says *belo shemen u-velo levonah* — without oil and without frankincense — must bring it with oil and frankincense, since those are indispensable components. In each of these cases, the impermissible specification is set aside and the person is bound to bring a proper *minchah*.

### Invalid Specifications

What unites these cases is that the person specifies something that cannot be part of a voluntary *minchah* — whether the wrong grain, the wrong grade of flour, or the omission of required ingredients. The *mishnah* in each instance directs that we ignore the defective specification and hold the person to the underlying obligation of a *minchah*. The central question, which the *Gemara* immediately raises, is why: why should we not simply say that the vow, as stated, is meaningless, and release the person entirely?

### Half-Issaron Case

The *mishnah* adds two further cases involving the *issaron*, the standard unit of flour for a *minchah*. One who says *chatzi issaron yavi issaron shalem* — "I obligate myself in half an *issaron*" — must bring a full *issaron*, since *menachot* can only be brought in whole *issaron* units. This is relatively straightforward by analogy to the earlier cases: ignore the "half" and obligate in the minimum valid quantity.

The most striking case, however, is the last: *issaron u-mechtzah yavi shnayim* — if one says "I obligate myself in an *issaron* and a half," one must bring two full *issronot*. This goes well beyond anything the earlier cases suggest. In all the prior examples, one can explain the rule by simply cutting off the defective portion of the vow and relying on what remains — the word *minchah* standing alone. But here, how do we get from "an *issaron* and a half" all the way to two full *issronot*? That is the crux the *Gemara* will grapple with at length.

*Rabbi Shimon* dissents and exempts in all these cases: *shelo hitnadev k'derech ha-mitnadbim* — the person did not take a vow in the manner of those who make vows. Since the complete statement the person uttered does not describe any valid *minchah*, *Rabbi Shimon* treats the entire vow as void.

## Why Binding?

### Neder Upitcho Imo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַמַּאי? נֶדֶר וּפִתְחוֹ עִמּוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why** is the vow to bring a meal offering from barley valid? **It is** seemingly a case of **a vow and its extenuation together.** The statement: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering, is a valid vow, while the next term: From barley, constitutes a retraction, as the speaker knows that a meal offering may not be brought from barley.

The *Gemara* opens with a sharp question: *amai?* — why is the person bound at all? The principle invoked is *neder upitcho imo* — a vow whose very utterance contains within it an opening for annulment. *Rashi* explains: since the person said, for instance, *minchah min ha-se'orim*, and everyone knows there is no *minchah* of barley, we may read the specification as a signal that the person already regrets the vow or never genuinely meant to obligate himself. Either way — whether we understand it as attempted retraction or simply as a meaningless formula — why should it be binding?

### Tafos Lashon Rishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: הָא מַנִּי? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן, דִּתְנַן: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר מִן הַגְּרוֹגְרוֹת וּמִן הַדְּבֵילָה״ – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נָזִיר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ נָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say:** In the case of one who stated a declaration comprising two contradictory statements, **attend** only to **the first statement. As we learned** in a mishna (*Nazir* 9a): If one says: **I am hereby a nazirite from dried figs and from** pressed **figs,** which is a contradictory statement, as figs are not prohibited to a nazirite, **Beit Shammai say:** He is a full-fledged **nazirite,** as one attends only to the first statement, i.e., I am hereby a nazirite, and the second part is discounted. **And Beit Hillel say:** The second part of his statement is not discounted, and therefore **he is not a nazirite,** as he did not accept naziriteship upon himself.

*Chizkiyah* answers: the *mishnah* follows *Beit Shammai*, who hold the principle of *tafos lashon rishon* — seize the first language. The moment the person said *harei alai minchah*, those words bound him. Whatever followed — "of barley," "of bran," "without oil" — is irrelevant. The first word *minchah* was already out of his mouth, and nothing said afterward can redefine or retract it. The second portion of the statement is simply ignored; the obligation that attached at the first word remains.

### Beit Shammai vs Hillel

The *Gemara* cites the *mishnah* in Nazir as the locus of this dispute. One who says *hareini nazir min ha-gerogerot u-min ha-deveilah* — "I am a *nazir* from pressed figs and pressed dates" — is formulating a *neziroot* in terms the *Torah* does not recognize. *Beit Shammai* say he is nonetheless a *nazir*: we ignore everything after the first word and he is bound by *hareini nazir*. *Beit Hillel* say he is not a *nazir* at all.

This makes the structure of our *mishnah* noteworthy: it appears as a *stam mishnah* — an unattributed ruling — aligned with *Beit Shammai*, with *Rabbi Shimon* being the sole dissenting voice. That is *Chizkiyah's* reading.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Assumed Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בֵּית הִלֵּל, בְּאוֹמֵר ״אִילּוּ הָיִיתִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁאֵין נוֹדְרִין כָּךְ, לֹא הָיִיתִי נוֹדֵר כָּךְ אֶלָּא כָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: You** may **even say** that the mishna here is in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Hillel.** It is referring **to** one who, upon being informed that such a vow is not effective, **says: Had I known that** one **cannot vow** in **this** manner to bring barley for a meal offering, **I would not have vowed** in **this** manner **but rather** in **that** manner, by vowing to bring wheat instead.

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees and maintains that the *mishnah* can in fact be read in accordance with *Beit Hillel*. His explanation turns on the following: *b'omer ilu hayiti yodea she'ein nodrin kach lo hayiti noder kach ela kach* — the case is one where the person would say, "Had I known that one cannot make a vow in this form, I would not have vowed this way, but rather in the straightforward way." That is, had the person realized that a *minchah* of barley is impossible, he would simply have obligated himself in a plain *minchah*.

The question naturally arises whether this requires actually asking the person, or whether we simply assume it. The *mishnah* does not say "if he declares that this is what he meant," which suggests that the point is not to query the person but to assume his intent based on the circumstances. *Rabbi Yochanan's* approach is therefore best understood as an objective inference from the nature of the case: when someone says "I want to bring a *minchah* of barley," the specification of barley is a relatively minor modification — perhaps a cheaper alternative — and it is reasonable to infer that, had the person known it was unavailable, he would have accepted the standard obligation. We can therefore treat the vow as if he had said simply *harei alai minchah*, because that is plausibly what he would have meant.

This contrasts with the case of *neziroot*: one who says "I am a *nazir* from figs and dates" cannot be assumed to have intended full *neziroot* had he known the rules, since *neziroot* entails abstinence from wine, haircuts, and corpse impurity — a substantially different and more demanding commitment than what he described. There we cannot make that assumption, and *Beit Hillel* therefore exempts him entirely. But in the *minchah* cases, where the modification is minor and the core intent clear, *Beit Hillel* can bind him on the basis of his presumed intent.

### Binding Without Words

There remains, however, a serious difficulty with *Rabbi Yochanan's* approach — one that is most acute in the last case of the *mishnah*. Even granting that we can infer the person's intent, a *neder* requires actual words to be binding. If the person said *issaron u-mechtzah*, and we conclude that what he would have meant had he known the *halachah* is two full *issronot* — the inference may be entirely correct, but he never actually said "two *issronot*." There is no verbalized *neder* to anchor that obligation. This remains an open question at this stage, to be resolved later in the *Gemara*.

## Barley vs Lentils

### Chizkiyah's Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״מִנְחָה מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״מִנְחָה מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים״ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another disagreement between Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan about the mishna: **Ḥizkiyya says** that the Sages **taught** in the mishna **only** that **where he says:** It is incumbent upon me to bring **a meal offering from barley,** he can bring a meal offering from wheat instead, **but** if he **says:** It is incumbent upon me to bring **a meal offering from lentils,** the vow is **not** valid.

*Chizkiyah* draws a distinction: the rule of the *mishnah* — that the person is bound — applies specifically when he says *minchah min ha-se'orim*, a *minchah* of barley. But if he says *minchah min ha-adashim* — a *minchah* of lentils — he is not bound, because everyone knows one cannot bring a *minchah* of lentils, and the utterance therefore cannot be read as anything other than a meaningless statement.

### Challenge to Chizkiyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי חִזְקִיָּה כְּמַאן אָמַר לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ? כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי מִשּׁוּם תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן הוּא. מָה לִי מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִין, מָה לִי מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים? הֲדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now, Ḥizkiyya said** that **his tradition** of interpreting the mishna is that it is **in accordance with whose** opinion? It is **in accordance with Beit Shammai's** opinion, **and Beit Shammai's** ruling is **due to** the principle that one should **attend** only to **the first statement.** If so, **what** is the difference **to me** whether he vowed to bring a meal offering **from barley** or **from lentils?** In either case, that principle should require him to bring a meal offering from wheat. The Gemara answers: Ḥizkiyya **retracted his** initial explanation that the mishna is in accordance with Beit Shammai, and subscribes to the explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this distinction. *Chizkiyah* explained the *mishnah* according to *Beit Shammai*, whose principle is *tafos lashon rishon* — we simply seize the first word. *Beit Shammai* do not inquire into what the person was thinking; they focus mechanically on the first term uttered. If so, what difference does it make whether the person said "barley" or "lentils"? In either case, he said the word *minchah*, and that word binds him. The subsequent specification — barley or lentils or anything else — is simply ignored.

### Chizkiyah Reverses

Confronted with this objection, *Chizkiyah* reverses his position. He concedes that the barley-versus-lentils distinction does not work within the framework of *Beit Shammai*, and accordingly adopts *Rabbi Yochanan's* reading: the *mishnah* follows *Beit Hillel*, and the operative question is what the person genuinely meant and would have intended had he known the *halachah*.

### Rava's Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי טַעְמָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ, מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״מִנְחָה מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִים״? לִיתְנֵי ״מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים״!
>
> אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, מִשּׁוּם דְּטָעֵי הוּא, בִּשְׂעוֹרִים טָעֵי, בַּעֲדָשִׁים לָא טָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **he retracted** his explanation? **Rava said: The mishna** posed **a difficulty for him: Why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach** this *halakha* using the example of **a meal offering from barley? Let it teach** the *halakha* using the example of a meal offering **from lentils,** which is a greater novelty.
>
> **Rather, learn from** it that the reason the vow takes effect **is because** one may **err. With regard to barley,** it is reasonable that one may **err,** since one of the individual meal offerings, the meal offering of a *sota*, comes from barley. But **with regard to lentils,** one would **not err** in thinking that one may bring a meal offering from them. Therefore, one can presume that by saying: Lentils, he intended to negate his initial statement.

*Rava* identifies what drove *Chizkiyah* to reverse himself: *matnitin kashyateih* — the language of the *mishnah* itself pressed the point. *Rava* asks: why does the *mishnah* choose the example of barley, which is precisely the kind of mistake a person could plausibly make? Why not teach a more striking *chiddush* — that even if one said "a *minchah* of lentils" one is still bound? The fact that the *mishnah* illustrates the rule with barley and not lentils indicates that the rationale is *mishum de-ta'ei* — because the person made a reasonable mistake. This is the framework of *Beit Hillel*: we ask what the person really intended, and we hold him to that intent only when the error was plausible. By barley — a grain associated with other *menachot* — one could genuinely have thought a voluntary *minchah* was possible. By lentils, that is plainly absurd: *bi-se'orim ta'ei, de-adashim lo ta'ei*. Since lentils cannot be explained as a reasonable mistake, there is no presumed underlying intent to bring a *minchah*, and the vow is void. The *mishnah's* choice of example thus implicitly endorses *Beit Hillel's* intent-based approach.

## Lentils Dispute

### Yochanan: Even Lentils

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים. מִכְּדֵי, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כְּמַאן אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ? כְּבֵית הִלֵּל, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מִשּׁוּם דְּטָעֵי הוּא. בִּשְׂעוֹרִין – טָעֵי, בַּעֲדָשִׁים – לָא טָעֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** According to the mishna, **even** if he vowed to being a meal offering **from lentils,** the vow takes effect. The Gemara asks: **Now, Rabbi Yoḥanan states** that **his tradition** in interpreting the mishna is that it is **in accordance with whose** opinion? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel, and Beit Hillel** understand that the reason the meal offering takes effect **is because** one may reasonably **err.** One may **err with regard to barley** being fit for a meal offering, but **with regard to lentils,** he will **not err** that they are fit. Why then, does Rabbi Yoḥanan hold that the meal offering takes effect even if he said: Lentils?

In a striking reversal of expected positions, *Rabbi Yochanan* now asserts that even a vow of *minchah min ha-adashim* — a *minchah* of lentils — would bind the person, at least according to the reading of the *mishnah* he is addressing. This might seem inconsistent with his own intent-based approach, which appears to require that the mistake be reasonable.

### Lentils Obvious

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְבָרָיו דְּחִזְקִיָּה קָאָמַר לֵיהּ, אַתְּ מַאי טַעְמָא הָדְרַתְּ בָּךְ מִשּׁוּום דְּלָא קָתָנֵי ״מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים״.
>
> דִּלְמָא ״לָא מִיבַּעְיָא״ קָאָמַר, לָא מִיבַּעְיָא מִן הָעֲדָשִׁים, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִהְדָּר הוּא דְּהָדַר בֵּיהּ, וּתְפוֹס לָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ מִן הַשְּׂעוֹרִין נָמֵי, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִיטְעָא הוּא דְּקָא טָעֵי, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי תְּפוֹס לָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yoḥanan does in fact hold that when he vows to bring a meal offering from lentils it is not valid. What he said was in response **to the statement of Ḥizkiyya.** Rabbi Yoḥanan is saying to him: **What is the reason** that **you retracted** your explanation of the mishna as being in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai? It is **because** the mishna **does not teach** using the example of one who vowed to bring a meal offering **from lentils,** which would have been a greater novelty.
>
> **Perhaps** the mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary.** (Lit., It is not necessary to say) **It is not necessary** to say that when one vows to bring a meal offering **from lentils,** the vow takes effect and he brings a meal offering from wheat. He brings it **because one has** reason **to say** that he in fact intended to vow and then **retracted** his initial statement, **and** yet the vow takes effect in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai that one should **attend** only to **the initial statement** and his retraction is disregarded. **But even** if he vowed to bring a meal offering **from barley, where one has** reason **to say he made an error,** and had he known that a meal offering is not brought from barley, he would not have vowed at all, **nevertheless,** the vow takes effect and he must bring a meal offering, due to the principle: **Attend** only to **the first statement.**

The *Gemara* resolves this by clarifying that *Rabbi Yochanan* is speaking *li-devarav de-Chizkiyah* — within *Chizkiyah's* original framework, addressing him on his own terms. *Rabbi Yochanan* is saying: you can maintain your original position that the *mishnah* goes like *Beit Shammai*, and even so you need not retract the distinction you drew. According to *Beit Shammai*, even lentils would bind the person — and the *mishnah* does not say lentils precisely because lentils is *obvious* according to *Beit Shammai*. When someone says "a *minchah* of lentils," that is so clearly absurd that it functions as an open retraction or a second statement entirely distinct from the first. *Beit Shammai's* rule of *tafos lashon rishon* applies with full force: the retraction is ignored and the first word *minchah* binds. Of course lentils yields an obligation — that is self-evident. But why does barley yield an obligation? That is less obvious, because one might have thought that when the second part of the statement shows the person's genuine mistaken intent — as opposed to a clear retraction — *Beit Shammai* might treat the entire statement as a unit and invalidate the whole thing.

### Intent vs Retraction

The point thus becomes a refinement of *Beit Shammai's* position. One might have supposed that *tafos lashon rishon* operates only when the second part of the statement is a clear retraction — "I changed my mind" — in which case we ignore the retraction and hold to the first word. But when the second part reveals what the person always had in mind, perhaps *Beit Shammai* would weigh the entire statement together and find it defective. To this *Rabbi Yochanan* responds: the formulation in the Gemara indicates *Rabbi Yochanan* is making the argument that even so, *Beit Shammai* ignores the second half absolutely. Whether the second part is a clear retraction (lentils) or an expression of original mistaken intent (barley), it is equally disregarded. *Beit Shammai's* rule is categorical. The *mishnah* mentions barley rather than lentils only because barley requires the novel ruling; lentils was already obvious.

To summarize the final state of the debate: the *mishnah* can be read either as *Beit Shammai* or as *Beit Hillel*. According to *Beit Shammai*, the binding force of the vow depends entirely on the first word uttered, regardless of what follows or what the person intended — and accordingly lentils would also bind. According to *Beit Hillel*, we ask what the person genuinely would have meant, and only where a reasonable mistake accounts for the mismatch do we impose an obligation — so barley binds but lentils does not.

## Zeriri's Condition

### Mincha Word Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״מִנְחָה״, אֲבָל לָא אָמַר ״מִנְחָה״ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another comment concerning the ruling in the mishna that a vow to bring a meal offering from barley takes effect: **Ze'eiri said:** The Sages **taught** that the first portion of one's statement is accepted **only where he said** in his vow: **Meal offering [*minḥa*],** but not in the Hebrew construct where it is attached to the term: From barley [*minḥat se'orim*]. **But** if he did **not say** the word **meal offering** independently, but either said *minḥat se'orim* or said: It is incumbent upon me to bring barley, the vow does **not** take effect, and he does not bring a meal offering.

At the top of 103b, *Zeriri* introduces a further condition. The principle that the *mishnah* applies — under whichever rationale, *Beit Shammai* or *Beit Hillel* — only holds *lo shanu ela de-amar minchah*: where the person actually said the word *minchah*. If he did not say the word *minchah* — for instance, if he said merely *harei alai min ha-se'orim* or *harei alai min ha-kemach*, leaving *minchah* only implied — he is not bound.

Working within the *Beit Shammai* framework, the logic is clear: *tafos lashon rishon* requires an actual first word to seize. If the person said *minchah min ha-se'orim*, we discard *min ha-se'orim* and grab *minchah*. But if he said only *min ha-se'orim*, there is nothing to grab; the word *minchah* is not present. Similarly, *minchat se'orim* — written in the construct form — does not give us a freestanding *minchah*: *minchat* is the *minchah* of, and it pulls the word *se'orim* along with it as an inseparable unit. Only when *minchah* stands alone as a discrete word can *Beit Shammai* seize it. According to *Beit Hillel*, one could argue that even without the explicit word, if we know the person meant a *minchah*, we could obligate him — but *Zeriri's* condition, at minimum as applied to *Beit Shammai*, requires the word.

### Rava Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב נַחְמָן וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: ״קֶמַח״ – יָבִיא סוֹלֶת, לָאו דְּלֹא אָמַר ״מִנְחָה״? לָא, דְּאָמַר ״מִנְחָה״.
>
> ״בְּלֹא שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה״, יָבִיא שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה. מַאי לָאו דְּלֹא אָמַר ״מִנְחָה״? לָא, דְּאָמַר ״מִנְחָה״.
>
> ״חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן״ – יָבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם, מַאי לָאו דְּלֹא אָמַר ״מִנְחָה״? לָא, דְּאָמַר ״מִנְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman was sitting and reciting this *halakha*** of Ze'eiri. **Rava raised an objection to Rav Naḥman** from the mishna: One who vows to bring a meal offering from regular **flour,** which is not used for a meal offering, **should bring** the meal offering from **fine flour. Is it not** referring to a case **where he did not say:** It is incumbent upon me to bring **a meal offering,** but rather said: It is incumbent upon me to bring regular flour? Apparently, the vow takes effect even if one did not state the term meal offering in an independent form. Rav Naḥman responded: **No,** it is referring to **where he said:** It is incumbent upon me to bring **a meal offering** of regular flour.
>
> Rava raised another, similar objection from the continuation of the mishna: With regard to one who vows to bring a meal offering **without oil and frankincense,** his vow takes effect, and **he shall bring** it with **oil and frankincense. What, is it not** referring to a case **where he did not say** the word **meal offering** in his vow, and yet it still takes effect? Rav Naḥman responds: **No,** it is referring to a case **where he said:** It is incumbent upon me to bring **a meal offering** without oil or frankincense.
>
> Rava asks again based on the mishna: If one vows to bring a meal offering of **half a tenth** of an ephah, **he should bring a complete tenth** of an ephah. **What, is it not** referring to a case **where he did not say** the term **meal offering** in his vow, and yet it still takes effect? Rav Naḥman responds: **No,** it is referring to a case **where he said:** It is incumbent upon me to bring **a meal offering** of half a tenth of an ephah.

*Rava* challenges this by reading through the cases in the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* says *kemach yavi solet* — bran, bring fine flour — without explicitly stating *minchah min ha-kemach*. Is this not a case where the person did not say the word *minchah*? Similarly, *belo shemen u-velo levonah yavi shemen u-levonah* makes no mention of the word *minchah*. And *chatzi issaron yavi issaron shalem* — again, no explicit *minchah*. *Rava* presses: does the *mishnah* not thereby contradict *Zeriri's* requirement that the word *minchah* must be stated?

### Mincha Implied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: עִשָּׂרוֹן וּמֶחֱצָה – יָבִיא שְׁנַיִם, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״מִנְחָה״ אִיחַיַּיב לֵיהּ בְּעִשָּׂרוֹן, כִּי אָמַר ״חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן״ לֹא כְּלוּם קָאָמַר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava asks: **If** it is **so** that in all the cases in the mishna his vow included the term meal offering, **say the last clause:** If one vows to bring a meal offering of **a tenth and a half** of an ephah, **he should bring two** tenths. **Once he said** the term **meal offering, he is obligated in** bringing **a tenth** of an ephah of flour. Therefore, **when he states** the words: **Half a tenth, he is not saying anything,** as he did not say the term meal offering with it, and would not have to bring two tenths. In what case is the ruling in the latter clause relevant?

The *Gemara* deflects each challenge by insisting that in each case the person did in fact say the word *minchah* — the *mishnah* simply abbreviates. *Kemach yavi solet* means he said *minchah min ha-kemach*; the fuller formulation is understood. And so with each case: the *mishnah's* shorthand does not indicate that the word *minchah* was absent; it is present in each instance, as *Zeriri* requires.

## Half-Issaron Crux

### One Word Binds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא דְּאָמַר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחָה חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן וְעִשָּׂרוֹן״, דְּכֵיוָן דְּאָמַר ״מִנְחָה״ אִיחַיַּיב לֵיהּ בְּעִשָּׂרוֹן, כִּי אָמַר ״חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן״ וְלֹא כְּלוּם קָאָמַר, כִּי הָדַר אָמַר ״עִשָּׂרוֹן״ מַיְיתֵי עִשָּׂרוֹן אַחֲרִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach the *halakha* in the case **where he said: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a meal offering of half a tenth and a tenth. Since he said** the term **meal offering, he is obligated in** bringing **a tenth** of an ephah for it. **When he said: Half a tenth, he is saying nothing,** since a meal offering is not brought with that amount of flour. **When he then said** the word **tenth,** he therefore **brings another tenth,** totaling two tenths.

The challenge that remained from the opening discussion now comes into sharp focus. According to *Beit Shammai* — where we work with the words themselves and need the word *minchah* to anchor the obligation — consider the case of *issaron u-mechtzah*. If the person says *minchah issaron u-mechtzah*: the word *minchah* obligates him in one *issaron* (the minimum valid *minchah*). The word *issaron* that follows is consistent with *minchah* and confirms the obligation. But then he says *chatzi* — half — and that word *chatzi* carries no obligating force on its own. So he remains bound to one *issaron*, and we never arrive at two. How, under *Beit Shammai's* logic, does this case yield the obligation of two *issronot*?

### Reversed Order

The *Gemara* resolves this with a creative structural move: *lo tzericha de-amar harei alai minchah chatzi issaron ve-issaron* — the case in the *mishnah* is not where the person said "an *issaron* and a half," but where he said it in reverse order: a *minchah* of "half an *issaron* and an *issaron*." This changes the analysis entirely.

When the person says *minchah*, he is obligated in one *issaron* — the minimum. When he then says *chatzi issaron*, that phrase carries no halakhic weight: *lo klum ka-amar* — it says nothing binding. It is a meaningless specification in this context and is discarded. But then he says the final word *issaron*, standing on its own. That word *issaron* is itself a valid specification and obligates him in an additional *minchah* of one *issaron* — *meisi issaron achrina*, a second *issaron* is brought. The result: two *issronot*, one anchored by the initial word *minchah* and one anchored by the final word *issaron*, with the intervening *chatzi issaron* discarded as inoperative.

### Two Statements

The *Gemara* thus presents this case as structurally comprising two separate obligating statements: the word *minchah* generating one obligation and the concluding word *issaron* generating a second. It is only this reversed-order formulation — *minchah chatzi issaron ve-issaron* — that produces the two-*issaron* result under *Beit Shammai's* framework.

## Rabbi Shimon

### Whole Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר שֶׁלֹּא הִתְנַדֵּב כְּדֶרֶךְ הַמִּתְנַדְּבִין, אַמַּאי? אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֲמָרָהּ, דְּאָמַר: אַף בִּגְמַר דְּבָרָיו אָדָם מַתְפִּיס.
>
> **Translation:** Rava asks: **If so,** then when the mishna continues: **Rabbi Shimon deems** one **exempt** from bringing a meal offering in all these cases, **as he did not pledge in the manner of those who pledge, why** is this his opinion? Once he said the term meal offering, the vow should be valid. **Rava said** in response: **Rabbi Shimon stated** his opinion **according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, who said** that **the conclusion of a person's statement is also accepted.** Therefore, even when he said the term meal offering at the beginning of the vow, since at the end he also made declarations that do not apply to a voluntary meal offering, the vow does not take effect.

This resolution, however, raises a difficulty regarding *Rabbi Shimon's* position. If the formulation is *minchah chatzi issaron ve-issaron* — which sounds like two distinct components, one of which (the final *issaron*) is fully valid on its own — why does *Rabbi Shimon* exempt entirely? The final *issaron* should at least obligate him, even if the *chatzi issaron* in the middle is discarded. *Rabbi Shimon's* stated reason is *shelo hitnadev k'derech ha-mitnadbim* — he looks at the whole statement and finds it does not constitute a valid vow. But when the statement clearly contains what sounds like two components, one of which independently works, should *Rabbi Shimon* not at minimum obligate him in the one valid *issaron*?

### Rabbi Yosi Position

*Rava* explains: *Rabbi Shimon b'shitat Rabbi Yosi amarah* — *Rabbi Shimon* follows the position of *Rabbi Yosi*, who holds *af be-gmar devarav adam nitpas* — a person can be bound even by the conclusion of his statement, by the final words he utters. The implication of *Rabbi Yosi's* position, as *Rava* applies it, is that the entire utterance from beginning to end is treated as a single unit.

### Same Sentence Rule

When *Rabbi Yosi* says that the end of one's statement is also binding, *Rabbi Shimon* derives from this a broader principle: even where a single sentence contains what might appear to be two logically separable sub-statements, they are treated as one integrated unit. One does not parse the sentence into its components and evaluate each independently; rather, the whole is assessed together. Since the whole formulation — *minchah chatzi issaron ve-issaron* — does not constitute a valid voluntary *minchah*, *Rabbi Shimon* invalidates the entire vow. The final *issaron* does not stand alone; it is part of the same utterance as the *chatzi issaron* that preceded it, and together they form an incoherent vow that is wholly rejected.

## Sixty-Issaron Limit

### Sixty-One Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִתְנַדֵּב אָדָם מִנְחָה שֶׁל שִׁשִּׁים עִשָּׂרוֹן, וּמֵבִיא בִּכְלִי אֶחָד. (אִם אָמַר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי שִׁשִּׁים עִשָּׂרוֹן״ – מֵבִיא בִּכְלִי אֶחָד). אִם אָמַר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי שִׁשִּׁים וְאֶחָד״ – מֵבִיא שִׁשִּׁים בִּכְלִי אֶחָד, וְאֶחָד בִּכְלִי אֶחָד, שֶׁכֵּן הַצִּיבּוּר מֵבִיא בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בַּשַּׁבָּת שִׁשִּׁים וְאֶחָד.
>
> דַּיּוֹ לַיָּחִיד שֶׁהוּא פָּחוֹת מִן הַצִּיבּוּר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person** may **pledge a meal offering of sixty tenths** of an ephah of fine flour, **and bring** all sixty tenths **in one vessel. If he says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **sixty tenths** of an ephah, **he brings it in one vessel. If he says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **sixty-one** tenths of an ephah, **he brings sixty** tenths **in one vessel and one** tenth **in another vessel, as** the greatest number of tenths of an ephah that **the community brings** as meal offerings in one day is **on the first festival day of *Sukkot* when** it **occurs on Shabbat,** when **sixty-one** tenths of an ephah of fine flour are brought.
>
> It **is sufficient for an individual that** the maximum amount he can bring at once is **one** tenth of an ephah **less than** that of **the community.** When the first day of *Sukkot* occurs on Shabbat, thirteen bulls, two goats, and fourteen lambs are sacrificed as the additional offerings of *Sukkot*, two lambs are sacrificed as the daily offerings, and two lambs are sacrificed as the additional offering of Shabbat. Three tenths of an ephah are brought for each bull, two tenths for each goat, and a tenth for each lamb. Altogether, that is sixty-one tenths of an ephah.

A new *mishnah* establishes that a person may voluntarily bring a *minchah* of up to sixty *issronot* in a single vessel: *mitnadev adam minchah shel shishim issaron u-meivi bi-khli echad*. If he explicitly vows a *minchah* of sixty, it is brought in one vessel. But if he vows *shishim ve-echad* — sixty-one — the *mishnah* rules: *meivi shishim bi-khli echad ve-echad bi-khli acher* — sixty is brought in one vessel and the additional one in a separate vessel. The vow is not simply fulfilled as a single unit of sixty-one; rather, it is split.

### Communal Sacrifice

The *Tanna Kamma* explains the basis for the sixty-*issaron* limit by reference to the communal sacrifice. There exists one situation in which the communal offerings of a single day involve a combined *minchat nesachim* totaling sixty-one *issronot*: when the first day of *Sukkot* falls on *Shabbat*. *Rashi* works out the calculation in detail. The thirteen bulls of the *Sukkot musaf* each require three *issronot*, yielding thirty-nine. The fourteen sheep each require one *issaron*, yielding fourteen — bringing the total to fifty-three. The two rams each require two *issronot*, yielding four, for a total of fifty-seven. The two *tamidim* add two more, as do the two lambs of the *Shabbat musaf*, bringing the grand total to sixty-one. Since the community can bring a total of sixty-one *issronot* in a single day's sacrifices, the principle is *dayo le-yachid she-yipaches min ha-tzibbur echad* — it suffices for an individual to bring one fewer than the community. The individual's maximum is therefore sixty.

### Not Mixed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וַהֲלֹא אֵלּוּ לַפָּרִים, וְאֵלּוּ לַכְּבָשִׂים, וְאֵינָם נִבְלָלִים זֶה עִם זֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** What is the relevance of the tenths of an ephah sacrificed on *Sukkot* that occurs on Shabbat? **Aren't these** meal offerings **for bulls and those for lambs, and they are not mixed with each other** (see 89a)? **Rather,** the reason that one may not bring more than sixty tenths of an ephah in one vessel is because **up to sixty** tenths of fine flour can **be mixed** with one *log* of oil.

*Rabbi Shimon* objects to this rationale. The sixty-one *issronot* of the communal sacrifice are never brought as a single *minchah*: some are for the bulls, some for the rams, some for the sheep, and some are the *tamid* offerings brought at different times of day. *Velo, harei elu la-parim ve-elu la-kvasim, ve-einan nivlalin zeh im zeh* — these are for bulls and these are for sheep, and they are never mixed together, since the bulls' *minchah* has a different proportion of oil per *issaron* than the sheep's, and the two cannot be combined. The community therefore never in fact brings sixty-one *issronot* as a unified *minchah*; the figure of sixty-one is only an aggregate. Accordingly, it cannot serve as the benchmark for an individual's *minchah*.

## Mixing Requirement

### Cannot Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שִׁשִּׁים נִבְלָלִין, וְשִׁשִּׁים וְאֶחָד אֵין נִבְלָלִין?! אָמַר לָהֶם: כׇּל מִדּוֹת חֲכָמִים כֵּן – בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה הוּא טוֹבֵל, וּבְאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה חָסֵר קֻרְטוֹב אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִטְבּוֹל בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** Is it so that **sixty** tenths of flour **can be mixed** with a *log* of oil, **but sixty-one** tenths **cannot be mixed?** Rabbi Shimon **said to them: All the measures of the Sages** are **so:** For example, **in** a ritual bath containing **forty *se'a*** of water, **one immerses** for purification, **and in** a ritual bath with **forty *se'a* less** the small measure of **a *kortov*, one cannot immerse in it** for purification.

*Rabbi Shimon* offers the real reason for the sixty-*issaron* limit: *ad shishim yecholim le-hibalel* — up to sixty *issronot*, the flour can be properly mixed with the oil. Beyond sixty, it cannot mix well. This explanation requires a specific understanding of the oil requirement. *Rashi* notes that the ruling follows *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*, who holds that no matter how much flour a voluntary *minchah* contains, only one *log* of oil is used. Under that view, one *log* of oil is sufficient to mix with up to sixty *issronot* of flour, but sixty-one *issronot* exceeds what one *log* can properly permeate.

### One Difference

The Sages press *Rabbi Shimon*: *beshishim nivlalin, be-shishim ve-echad ein nivlalin?* — does one *issaron* really make the difference? If sixty can be mixed, surely sixty-one can also be mixed?

### Sages' Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּבֵיצָה – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, כְּבֵיצָה חָסֵר שׁוּמְשׁוּם – אֵין מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין. שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה – מְטַמֵּא מִדְרָס, שְׁלֹשָׁה עַל שְׁלֹשָׁה חָסֵר נִימָא אַחַת – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא מִדְרָס.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, food in the volume of **an egg-bulk transmits the ritual impurity of food,** while food in the volume of **an egg-bulk less** a small amount equal to the volume of **a sesame** seed **does not transmit impurity of food.** Similarly, a garment that is **three by three** handbreadths **is susceptible to ritual impurity** imparted by **treading** if a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*] sits or lies on it, while if it is **three by three** handbreadths **less one thread,** a tiny measurement, it **is not susceptible to ritual impurity** imparted by **treading.**

*Rabbi Shimon* responds: *kol middot chachamim ken* — that is always the nature of the Sages' measures. All *halakhic* thresholds work this way. One must draw a line somewhere, and below that line the rule applies while above it the rule does not, regardless of the fact that the difference at the margin is small.

### Parallel Examples

*Rabbi Shimon* illustrates with two parallel examples. A *mikveh* requires forty *se'ah* of water: with forty *se'ah* one may immerse; with forty *se'ah* minus a *kortov* — a thimbleful — one may not. The difference at the margin is negligible, yet the rule is absolute. Similarly, a piece of cloth three *tefachim* by three *tefachim* is susceptible to *tumat midras* — the impurity transmitted by a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah* sitting or leaning upon it; three *tefachim* by three *tefachim* minus a single thread is not. The *Sages* set precise measures and they are honored as such. The same applies here: sixty *issronot* is the limit of what can be mixed with the prescribed quantity of oil, and that is the cutoff.

## Gemara Analysis

### Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שְׁאִיל שְׁאֵילָה לְמַעְלָה מֵרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִילְעַאי: מִנַּיִן לְאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי שִׁשִּׁים וְאֶחָד״ מֵבִיא שִׁשִּׁים בִּכְלִי אֶחָד, וְאֶחָד בִּכְלִי אֶחָד?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sages **asked a question above,** i.e., in front of, **Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai: From where** is it derived **that** one **who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring a meal offering of **sixty-one** tenths, **brings sixty** tenths **in one vessel and one** tenth **in another vessel?**

The *Gemara* records that the following question was raised in the presence of *Rabbi Yehudah bar Ilai*: what is the source for the rule that one who vows a *minchah* of sixty-one *issronot* must bring sixty in one vessel and one in another — that is, why do we not simply treat a sixty-one-*issaron minchah* as a single valid offering?

### Bar Ilai Speaks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּתַח רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִילְעַאי, רֹאשׁ הַמְדַבְּרִים בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וְאָמַר: שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ צִיבּוּר מְבִיאִים בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בַּשַּׁבָּת שִׁשִּׁים וְאֶחָד, דַּיּוֹ לַיָּחִיד שֶׁיִּפְחוֹת מִן הַצִּיבּוּר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai, the first speaker on every occasion, began** his speech **and said: Since we find** that the greatest number of tenths of an ephah that **the community brings** in one day is **on the first festival day of *Sukkot* when** it **occurs on Shabbat,** when **sixty-one** tenths of fine flour are brought, it **is** therefore **sufficient for an individual that** the maximum amount he can bring at once is **one** tenth of an ephah **less than** that of **the community.**

*Rabbi Yehudah bar Ilai* opened the discussion. The *Gemara* notes that he held the distinction of *rosh ha-medabrim be-khol makom* — the one who had the right to speak first in every gathering. The *Gemara* in Shabbat relates the background: when the Sages convened and *Rabbi Yehudah bar Ilai* praised the achievements of Rome, this apparently secured for him — perhaps at Roman insistence — the privilege of opening all public discussions.

### Communal Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וַהֲלֹא אֵלּוּ פָּרִים וְאֵילִים, וְאֵלּוּ כְּבָשִׂים? אֵלּוּ בְּלִילָתָן עָבָה, וְאֵלּוּ בְּלִילָתָן רַכָּה, אֵלּוּ בְּלִילָתָן שַׁחֲרִית, וְאֵלּוּ בְּלִילָתָן בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, וְאֵין נִבְלָלִין מִזֶּה עַל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said to him: Aren't these** meal offerings brought with **bulls and rams, and those** brought with **lambs?** Don't **these,** the meal offerings brought with the bulls, have **a thick mixture,** as six *log* of oil are mixed with three tenths of an ephah of flour, **and those,** the meal offerings brought with the lambs, have **a thin mixture,** as three *log* of oil are mixed with a tenth of an ephah of flour? Isn't **the mixture** of **these** meal offerings, the daily offering and the additional offerings, performed **in the morning, and the mixture** of **those** meal offerings, brought with the afternoon daily offering, performed in the **afternoon,** and isn't it the case that **they are not mixed with one another?** The communal offerings cannot serve as precedent because they never bring sixty-one tenths in one vessel.

*Rabbi Yehudah bar Ilai* advanced the same argument as the *Tanna Kamma* in the *mishnah*: the community brings sixty-one *issronot* when the first day of *Sukkot* falls on *Shabbat*, and it suffices for the individual to bring one fewer. *Rabbi Shimon* replied with his objection in more elaborate form: some of those *issronot* are for the bulls (*parim*), some for the rams (*eilim*), some for the sheep (*kevasim*). The bulls and rams have a thicker mixture (*belilatan avah*) — two *log* of oil per *issaron* — while the sheep have a thinner mixture (*belilatan rakah*) — three *log* of oil per *issaron*. Moreover, some offerings are brought in the morning (*shacharit*), others in the afternoon (*bein ha-arbayim*), and they are never combined: *ve-ein nivlalin zeh im zeh*. The community's sixty-one *issronot* therefore cannot serve as a model for an individual's single *minchah*.

### Final Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״אֱמוֹר אַתָּה״. אָמַר לָהֶם: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה בְלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן וַחֲרֵבָה״, כְּבָר אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הָבֵא מִנְחָה שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְהִיבָּלֵל.
>
> אָמַר לוֹ: בְּשִׁשִּׁים נִבְלָלִין, בְּשִׁשִּׁים וְאֶחָד אֵין נִבְלָלִין?! אָמַר לוֹ: כׇּל מִדַּת חֲכָמִים כֵּן הוּא – בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה הוּא טוֹבֵל, בְּאַרְבָּעִים סְאָה חָסֵר קֻרְטוֹב אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִטְבּוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said to him: You** should **state** a reason why a meal offering of more than sixty tenths of an ephah must be brought in more than one vessel. Rabbi Shimon **said to them: It says** in the Torah: **"And every meal offering, mixed with oil, or dry,** shall all the sons of Aaron have, one as well as another" (Leviticus 7:10). **The Torah has already stated** here: **Bring a meal offering that is capable of being mixed.**
>
> Rabbi Yehuda **said to him:** Is it so that if the mixture consists **of sixty** tenths of flour, it can **be mixed** with a *log* of oil, **and** if it consists **of sixty-one** tenths, it **cannot be mixed?** Rabbi Shimon **said to** Rabbi Yehuda: **All the measures of the Sages are so: In** a ritual bath containing **forty *se'a*** of water, **one immerses** for purification, and **in** a ritual bath with **forty *se'a* less** the small measure of **a *kortov*, one cannot immerse** and be purified.

The Sages told *Rabbi Shimon*: if you reject the communal argument, you supply the reason. *Rabbi Shimon* responded with the verse: *ve-khol minchah belulah ba-shemen ve-charevah* — any *minchah* mixed with oil or dry. The *Torah* itself demands *bilul* — mixing — and this means there must be a *minchah* that is capable of being mixed with oil. More than sixty *issronot* cannot be mixed properly with the oil. The Sages again challenged him — sixty works but sixty-one does not? — and *Rabbi Shimon* gave the same answer as in the *mishnah*: *kol middot chachamim ken hen*, with the same examples of forty *se'ah* and the cloth of three by three *tefachim*.

## Rabbi Zeira

### Mixing Not Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אֵין נִבְלָלִים, מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָא תְּנַן: אִם לֹא בָּלַל – כָּשֵׁר! אָמַר רַבִּי זְרִיקָא: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לְבִילָּה – אֵין בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי לְבִילָּה – בִּילָּה מְעַכֶּבֶת בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Even **if** sixty tenths **do not mix** with one *log* of oil, **what of it? But didn't we learn** in a mishna that although there is a mitzva to mix the oil with the flour in a meal offering, **if he did not mix** them, it is still **valid?** The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Zeira says:** For **any** measure of flour **that** is **suitable for mixing** with oil in a meal offering, the lack of **mixing does not invalidate** the meal offering. Although there is a mitzva to mix the oil and the flour *ab initio*, the meal offering is fit for sacrifice even if the oil and the flour are not mixed. **And** for **any** measure of flour **that** is **not suitable for mixing** with oil in a meal offering, the lack of **mixing invalidates** the meal offering.

A final question is raised. If the *Torah* requires *bilul* — mixing — the entire edifice of *Rabbi Shimon's* argument depends on that requirement. But we have a tradition that teaches: *im lo balal kasher* — even if one did not actually mix the oil with the flour, the *minchah* is still valid after the fact. If mixing is not strictly required in practice, why should the theoretical capacity to mix determine the valid quantity?

### Potential Mixing

*Rabbi Zeira* resolves this with a fundamental principle: *kol ha-ra'ui le-vilah ein vilah me'akevet bo* — anything that is fit to be mixed, the actual failure to mix does not invalidate it.

### Theoretical Capacity

The principle works as follows: once we know that the quantities are such that mixing is theoretically possible — that is, that the flour and oil are in proportions that could be combined — then the post-facto failure to carry out the mixing does not render the *minchah* invalid. The *Torah's* demand of *bilul* is satisfied by the theoretical capacity for mixing, even if that mixing was not actually performed.

### Cannot Mix Fails

Conversely: *ve-khol she-eino ra'ui le-vilah, vilah me'akevet bo* — if the quantities are such that mixing is not even possible in principle, then the requirement of *bilul* is inherently unmet, and the offering is invalid. In this case, the failure to mix is not a mere procedural omission; it reflects that the offering was never constituted in the manner the *Torah* demands.

*Rabbi Zeira's* principle thus elegantly resolves the apparent tension. The rule that *im lo balal kasher* — non-mixing does not invalidate — applies only where mixing was possible. Where mixing was never possible because the quantities are wrong, the *Torah's* demand of *belulah ba-shemen* remains unsatisfied at the most basic level, and the offering is defective. It is this principle that undergirds the sixty-*issaron* limit: sixty *issronot* are fit for mixing (*ra'uy le-vilah*) and therefore valid even without actual mixing; sixty-one are not fit for mixing, and no amount of post-facto leniency can remedy what is structurally impossible. The *Gemara* notes that while *Rabbi Zeira's* principle is entirely coherent in its original context, the *Gemara* elsewhere sometimes abstracts it as a general rule — that whenever a requirement can be theoretically satisfied, its non-satisfaction in practice is excused. The question of when that abstraction is legitimate and when a requirement must actually be met in practice is its own broad topic, and not one the *Gemara* pursues further here.
