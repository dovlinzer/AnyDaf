# Yevamot 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Safek Gerushin intro

### Case overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁהָיוּ בָּהֶן קִדּוּשִׁין אוֹ גֵרוּשִׁין בְּסָפֵק — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ הַצָּרוֹת חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. כֵּיצַד סְפֵק קִדּוּשִׁין? זָרַק לָהּ קִדּוּשִׁין, סָפֵק קָרוֹב לוֹ סָפֵק קָרוֹב לָהּ — זֶהוּ סְפֵק קִדּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if **any of these** fifteen women who are prohibited as forbidden relatives **had undergone a betrothal or divorce** whose status **is uncertain** with the deceased brother, **then those** women who were their **rival wives** must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage** since they are possibly the rival wives of forbidden relatives. The mishna elaborates: **How** could there be a situation of **uncertainty** with regard to **betrothal?** If in the public domain **he threw her** an item for the purpose of **betrothal** and there were eight cubits between them, and the item **was possibly closer to him** and did not enter into her domain, and **possibly closer to her,** i.e., within four cubits of her, whereby she could acquire the object, **this is** a case of **uncertainty** with regard to **betrothal.**

Today's daf is officially 34, but we continue from where we left off, picking up at the bottom of 31b. The *mishna* deals with a case of *safek kiddushin* and *safek gerushin* — doubtful betrothal and doubtful divorce — and addresses what is done with the *tzaras ervah*, the co-wife of a forbidden relative, in such circumstances: whether she requires *chalitza* or *yibum*.

### Safek vs. Psul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְפֵק גֵּרוּשִׁין — כָּתַב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ וְאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים, יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים וְאֵין בּוֹ זְמַן, יֵשׁ בּוֹ זְמַן וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא עֵד אֶחָד, זֶהוּ סְפֵק גֵּרוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Uncertainty** with regard to **divorce** occurs when, for instance, **he wrote a** bill of divorce **in his handwriting but there are no** signatures of **witnesses on** the document, or **there are** the signatures of **witnesses** on the document **but there is no date** written **in it,** or **the date is** written **in it but there is only** the signature of **a single witness.** Since there is doubt as to whether these three kinds of bills of divorce are valid, a woman who was divorced through them is only possibly divorced, and so **this** case **is** called **uncertainty** with regard to **divorce.**

The *gemara* makes a pointed observation: the case of *safek gerushin* presented in the *mishna* is not, strictly speaking, a genuine *safek gerushin* at all. It is rather a case of a *gerushin* that is *pasul derabanan* — rabbinically invalid divorce. The *gemara* is therefore working from the assumption that a classic *safek gerushin* would carry different consequences entirely. In such a case, the woman would retain her *chazaka* — her presumed status — and remain *eshes ish*, a married woman, with the result that the *tzaras ervah* would retain her status as exempt from *yibum*.

### Chazaka principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ בְּגֵרוּשִׁין, סָפֵק קָרוֹב לוֹ סָפֵק קָרוֹב לָהּ לָא קָתָנֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: אִשָּׁה זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת הֶיתֵּר לַשּׁוּק עוֹמֶדֶת, וּמִסָּפֵק אַתָּה בָּא לְאוֹסְרָהּ — אַל תַּאַסְרֶנָּה מִסָּפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara remarks: **But yet when** discussing **divorce, it does not teach** the case where it is **uncertain** if the bill of divorce **is closer to him, and uncertain if it is closer to her.** It would have been appropriate to describe this case, as it parallels the case involving the object of betrothal. He could have tossed it in such a way that it was not clear to whom the bill was closer. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** the mishna did not present this situation as well? **Rabba said:** When this type of uncertainty exists, the performance of *ḥalitza* is not mandatory, as **this woman,** the rival wife, **has the presumptive status of being permitted** to marry a man **from the** general **public.** At the time of her marriage she was rendered a rival wife of a forbidden relative. **And due to an uncertainty** alone **would you render her forbidden** to the general public until she performs *ḥalitza*, simply because it is unclear to us whether or not the forbidden relative had indeed been divorced? **Do not render her forbidden due to an uncertainty.** This is not, however, the case with the various bills of divorce mentioned in the mishna, for they are all certainly considered effective bills of divorce, even if the circumstances involved raise some questions or doubts.

This is the tension the *gemara* is working through. Being *mechamir* — stringent — sometimes comes without cost, but in this case it would impose a serious burden: demanding *chalitza* where none is halachically required creates its own complications. Therefore the *gemara* leaves the matter on the *d'oraisa* level, holding that such a woman retains her status as *eshes ish*, her co-wife retains her status as *tzaras ervah*, and the co-wife walks away without *chalitza*.

## Gittin case

### Get thrown case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגַבֵּי גֵירוּשִׁין מִי לָא תְּנַן? וְהָתְנַן: הָיְתָה עוֹמֶדֶת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ, קָרוֹב לָהּ — מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, קָרוֹב לוֹ — אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה — מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did we not learn** in a mishna about the case where it is uncertain whether the bill of divorce is closer to him or closer to her **with regard to** situations of **divorce** whose status is uncertain? **And didn't we learn** in a mishna: In a case where his wife **was standing in the public domain and he threw her** the bill of divorce, if the bill landed **closer to her, she is divorced.** If it was **closer to him, she is not divorced.** If it was **half and half,** i.e., if the bill of divorce landed midway between the man and the woman, there is uncertainty whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced.**

The *gemara* now challenges this framework by invoking a parallel case. It asks: is there not a case of genuine *safek gerushin* in the *mishna* in *Gittin*, which would produce the same consequence — namely, that the co-wife requires *chalitza*? The *mishna* there discusses the case of *ha-omedes bereshus harabim* — a woman standing in the public domain — where her husband throws a *get* toward her. If the *get* lands closer to her, she is divorced; if closer to him, she is not divorced; and if it lands exactly at the midpoint — *mechtza al mechtza* — she is in a state of doubt: *megureshes ve-eino megureshes*, divorced and not divorced.

The *gemara* in *Gittin* then asks: what is the practical significance of this doubtful status? Two implications are given. First, if the husband is a *kohen*, he cannot simply decide retrospectively that perhaps no divorce occurred and remain married — since she may in fact be divorced, she would be a *grusha*, whom a *kohen* cannot remarry. Second — and this is the *nafka mina* directly relevant here — if she is an *ervah*, her co-wife is a questionable *tzaras ervah* and therefore cannot simply walk away. She might be *megureshes*, in which case the co-wife is obligated in *yibum*, yet *yibum* cannot be performed because she might equally be *eina megureshes* and thus a true *tzaras ervah*. The ruling is therefore *ba'ei chalitza* — *chalitza* is required.

So the *gemara* asks: we see in *Gittin* that in a genuine case of *safek gerushin*, the *halacha* is *chalitza*, not that the woman simply retains her *chazaka* and walks away without anything. The *gemara* there does not say we fear that demanding *chalitza* will lead to *yibum* — it simply demands *chalitza*.

### Safeka D'Oraisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן: לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? דְּאִי כֹּהֵן הוּא — אֲסִורָה לֵיהּ, וְאִי עֶרְוָה הִיא — צָרָתָהּ בָּעֲיָא חֲלִיצָה. וְלָא אָמְרִינַן: שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר חוֹלֶצֶת, מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת! הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַויְיהוּ: הָכָא בִּשְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים עָסְקִינַן, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: קָרוֹב לָהּ, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: קָרוֹב לוֹ, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ סְפֵיקָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא. וּמַתְנִיתִין דְּהָכָא בְּכַת אַחַת, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ סְפֵיקָא דְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And we say: With regard to what *halakha*** was the ruling said that she is both divorced and not divorced? The Gemara explains that this affects two areas of *halakha*. The first is **that if** the man divorcing his wife **is a priest,** then his wife **is forbidden to him** due to the uncertainty that she may in fact be divorced through that bill of divorce. Consequently, he would then be unable to remarry her. The second ramification is that **if** the woman being divorced **was a forbidden relative** to her husband's brother, and her husband died childless, then **her rival wife would require *ḥalitza*.** The mishna indicates that in this type of divorce whose status is uncertain as well, the Sages require the rival wife to perform *ḥalitza*, and **we do not say that if you say** that **she** must **perform *ḥalitza*,** she may **enter into levirate marriage.** Here there is no such concern. The Gemara responds: **But wasn't it stated with regard to** that mishna that this is referring to a very specific set of circumstances? It is **Rabba and Rav Yosef who both say:** The doubt here does not result from the facts of the case themselves, but from conflicting testimonies and an inability to decide between them. **Here, we are dealing with two sets of witnesses, one** of which **says** that the bill fell **closer to her, and one** of which **says** that it fell **closer to him. This,** then, **is an uncertainty** in matters **of Torah** law, for in this case there are two testimonies, each one complete by itself, yet they contradict one another. Such instances are deemed to have the status of an uncertainty with regard to Torah law, and therefore the ruling is stringent. **But the mishna here is referring to one set** of witnesses who were divided in their testimony or who could not clarify exactly what had occurred. **This is** considered to be an **uncertainty** in matters of **rabbinic** law alone, as there is only a single uncorroborated testimony, and in cases of uncertainty in matters of rabbinic law the ruling is lenient.

To this the *gemara* responds: *haya itmara alah* — it was already said about that case. Both Rabba and Rav Yosef explain: *hacha bishtei kitei eidim askinan* — there we are actually dealing with two pairs of witnesses. One pair testifies that the *get* landed closer to her; another pair testifies that it landed closer to him. The reason the woman does not simply retain her *chazaka* of being married — and the co-wife her *chazaka* of being a *tzaras ervah* — is not because of an abstract doubt but because there is direct testimony opposing the *chazaka*. The testimony is contradicted, to be sure, but there is nevertheless direct testimony working against the presumed status.

This, the *gemara* says, creates a *sefika d'oraisa* — a genuine biblical-level doubt. The distinction is this: when there is merely an abstract doubt and nothing more, the principle is to rely on the *chazaka* and preserve the status quo. But when there is concrete testimony working against the *chazaka* — even if that testimony is itself contradicted — the *chazaka* is displaced, and one enters the domain of genuine *safek*. In such a case it is a real *d'oraisa* doubt, not a rabbinic stringency, and therefore *chalitza* is demanded. *Chalitza nibei bah*.

Our *mishna*, by contrast, is not talking about two pairs of witnesses. As the *gemara* explains: *u'masnisin d'hacha b'kas achas* — our *mishna* is dealing with a single pair of split witnesses, or with an abstract doubt, not with two opposing pairs of witnesses directly testifying against the *chazaka*. That would at most be a rabbinic *safek*, meaning that at the *d'oraisa* level one would still follow the status quo.

The *gemara* then anticipates the question: how do we know that the *mishna* in *Gittin* is discussing two pairs of witnesses, and how do we know that our *mishna*'s case of *kiddushin* is similarly a case of abstract doubt rather than contradictory testimony? The answer is that the cases are parallel: just as the *kiddushin* case involves a single pair whose members disagree — not two full pairs testifying in opposition — so too the *gerushin* case follows the same structure. *Mah kiddushin b'kas achas af geirushin b'kas achas.*

One might press further: how do we actually know the *kiddushin* case is one of abstract doubt? Perhaps it too involves contradictory pairs of witnesses. The *gemara* responds: if the *kiddushin* case involved two full pairs of contradictory witnesses, then how could it have been said earlier that we would not fear the *chalitza* leading to *yibum*? If there are witnesses testifying directly that the ring was clearly closer to her — that is, that she is genuinely married and the co-wife genuinely a *tzaras ervah* — then how could one be unconcerned about the possibility of *yibum* following? Rashi and Tosafot note the somewhat self-referential character of this argument, but the essential logic stands.

### Witnesses cancel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּמַתְנִיתִין דְּהָכָא בְּכַת אַחַת? דּוּמְיָא דְּקִדּוּשִׁין: מָה קִדּוּשִׁין בְּכַת אַחַת, אַף גֵּרוּשִׁין בְּכַת אַחַת. וְקִדּוּשִׁין גּוּפַיְיהוּ, מִמַּאי דִּבְכַת אַחַת? דִּלְמָא בִּשְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים? אִי בִּשְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים — תִּתְיַיבֵּם, וְאֵין בְּכָךְ כְּלוּם. קָיְימִי עֵדִים וְקָאָמְרִי קָרוֹב לָהּ, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ תִּתְיַיבֵּם וְאֵין בְּכָךְ כְּלוּם?! וְתוּ — בִּשְׁתֵּי כִּיתֵּי עֵדִים נָמֵי, סְפֵיקָא דְרַבָּנַן הִיא, דְּאָמְרִינַן: אוֹקֵי תְּרֵי לְבַהֲדֵי תְּרֵי, וְאִשָּׁה אוֹקְמַהּ אַחֲזָקָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it known **that the mishna here** is referring to a case of uncertainty **with one set** of witnesses? The Gemara responds: **It is similar to that of betrothal. Just as** with regard to **betrothal** it is referring to a case of uncertainty **with one set of witnesses, so too,** with regard to **divorce** it is referring to a case **of one set** of witnesses. The Gemara wonders: **And** with regard to **betrothal itself, from where** is it known that the mishna is referring to a case of uncertainty **that** involves **one set** of witnesses? **Perhaps** it is referring to a case **of two sets** of witnesses? The Gemara answers: **If** the mishna is referring to a case **of two sets of witnesses** who contradict one another, then **let her enter into levirate marriage, and** there is **no** problem **with that,** as there are two witnesses testifying that there was never a betrothal. Therefore, both the cases of betrothal and divorce must be referring to a situation where there is one set of witnesses. How can one say that? After all, **there are witnesses who are standing** before us **and saying** that the object of betrothal fell **closer to her.** Accordingly, she was betrothed and her rival wife is the rival wife of a forbidden relative. **And** yet **you say to let her enter into levirate marriage and** there is **no** problem **with that? And furthermore,** with regard to the fundamental difference between two pairs of witnesses and a single pair, the case **of two pairs of witnesses is also** considered an **uncertainty** in matters of **rabbinic** law. This is not considered to be uncertainty with regard to the reality of what actually happened, which would be a case of uncertainty in matters of Torah law, but rather a contradiction between two opposing testimonies. In these cases **we say: Place two** witnesses **against two** witnesses, and let the two testimonies cancel each other out. Therefore, the *halakha* would be to **let the woman remain in her original presumptive status.** Accordingly, this type of uncertainty stems only from rabbinic law and not from Torah law.

The *gemara* then makes a further and crucial point. Even granting the scenario of two contradictory pairs of witnesses, one might have thought that conflicting direct testimony displaces the *chazaka* and leaves a genuine blank-slate doubt. The *gemara* rejects this: *beshnei kitei eidim nami sefeika d'rabbanan* — even with two contradictory pairs of witnesses the result is only a rabbinic *safek*. The reason is that when witnesses contradict one another, one does not say "there is testimony against the *chazaka*, so the *chazaka* is undermined and we are left with a genuine doubt." Rather, one says: the testimonies contradict one another, so we reject all the testimony entirely, and we are restored to the *chazaka*. This is a fundamental principle: *okei terei bahadei terei* — put two against two — and return to the presumed status.

### Bar Shatya case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַנִּכְסֵי דְּבַר שָׁטְיָא. דְּבַר שָׁטְיָא זַבֵּין נִכְסֵי. אֲתוֹ בֵּי תְרֵי וְאָמְרִי: כְּשֶׁהוּא חָלִים זַבֵּין, וְאָתוּ בֵּי תְרֵי וַאֲמַרוּ: כְּשֶׁהוּא שׁוֹטֶה זַבֵּין. וְאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אוֹקֵי תְּרֵי לַהֲדֵי תְּרֵי, וְאַרְעָא אוֹקְמַהּ בְּחֶזְקַת בַּר שָׁטְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof for this: This is **just as it is in** the case **concerning the property of** a man named **Bar Shatya,** who was referred to by this name because he would occasionally go insane. The case is as follows: **Bar Shatya sold property. Two** witnesses **came** forward **and said that he sold** it **when** he was **healthy** and therefore the sale was valid. **And two** others **came** forward **and said that he sold** it **when** he was **insane,** and so the sale was void. **Rav Ashi said** with regard to this matter: **Place two** witnesses **against two** witnesses and let the testimonies cancel each other out, **and let the land remain in the possession of Bar Shatya.** Since no substantiated proof was brought forth, the land remains in the hands of its current possessor. As such, the same should be true with regard to cases of betrothal and divorce whose status is uncertain; the woman should remain in her former presumptive status.

The *gemara* illustrates this principle with the case of the property of Bar Shatya. Bar Shatya was a person who experienced episodes of mental incapacity, and the question arose concerning transactions he conducted: was he of sound mind at the time? He sold some property. Two witnesses came and testified that he sold it while he was of clear mind — *keshu chalim zavin*. Another two witnesses came and said he sold it while he was of unsound mind — *keshu shoteh zavin*. What does one do? Rav Ashi ruled: *okei terei lahadei terei* — place two against two, cancel all the testimony, and restore the property to Bar Shatya's presumed ownership — *ve-ara okma be-chezkas Bar Shatya*. That was the default position. We wipe the testimony from the slate and return to the status quo.

This demonstrates that even in the face of contradictory witnesses, the *halacha* is to restore the *chazaka*, not to treat the situation as an open *safek*. This brings us back to the central difficulty: if the *halacha* in every case of *safek gerushin* — whether abstract doubt or contradictory witnesses — is to rely on the *chazaka*, then why does the *mishna* in *Gittin* rule that *chalitza* is required? And if the answer is that we are nevertheless *mechamir* and demand *chalitza* in such a case even though the *d'oraisa* principle would be to rely on the *chazaka*, why did our *mishna* not present a classic *safek get* to illustrate this rule?

## Safek resolution

### Abaye parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵיעוֹ״. תְּנָא בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְגֵירוּשִׁין. תְּנָא בְּגֵירוּשִׁין, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְקִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabba's understanding of the mishna must be rejected, and **Abaye said:** The mishna should be understood according to that which is written: **"His fellow speaks of him"** (Job 36:33). This principle teaches that a related case can be inferred from the single case cited. The mishna **teaches** the case where it is uncertain whether the item is closer to him or closer to her **with regard to betrothal, and the same is true with regard to divorce** if it is uncertain whether the bill of divorce fell closer to him or closer to her. Similarly, the mishna **teaches** the case of bills that were written in a questionable manner **with regard to divorce, and the same is true with regard to betrothal.**

Abaye responds by invoking the principle *yagid alav rei'o* — let context and juxtaposition illuminate the matter. He argues: *tana be-kiddushin ve-hu hadin le-geirushin* — the *mishna* taught the rule by means of the *kiddushin* case, and the same applies equally to *geirushin*. The reason the *mishna* gave a different type of scenario by *get* was not to exclude the *kiddushin* example from applying to *gerushin*, but simply to add another scenario. Similarly, *tana be-geirushin ve-hu hadin le-kiddushin* — the *mishna* gave an example by *get* of a rabbinically invalid document, and the same would be true if one had a *kiddushin* that was rabbinically defective in a parallel way.

The essential point: do not read the scenarios in the *mishna* as mutually exclusive. The *mishna* gives one set of examples by *kiddushin* and another set by *geirushin*, but both sets of cases are halachically valid in both domains. And in terms of the underlying rule, even though the *d'oraisa* principle is to follow the *chazaka*, at the rabbinic level *chalitza* is demanded — and this is true for a classic *safek get* just as it is for a rabbinically defective one.

### Rava distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי ״יַגִּיד עָלָיו רֵיעוֹ״, מַאי ״זֶהוּ״ דְּקָתָנֵי? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּקִידּוּשִׁין יֵשׁ בְּגֵירוּשִׁין, וְיֵשׁ בְּגֵירוּשִׁין מַה שֶּׁאֵין בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, וְ״זֶהוּ״ דְּגֵירוּשִׁין לָאו דַּוְקָא. אֶלָּא, מִשּׁוּם דִּתְנָא ״זֶהוּ״ בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, תְּנָא נָמֵי ״זֶהוּ״ בְּגֵירוּשִׁין. וְ״זֶהוּ״ דְּקִידּוּשִׁין לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי זְמַן, דְּלֵיכָּא בְּקִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If** you understand that the legal ruling in all of these cases is the same, and the mishna was written in the style of: **His fellow speaks of him,** then **what is** the meaning of the term: **This is, that** the mishna **teaches?** The mishna in fact emphasizes that this is a betrothal whose status is uncertain and this is a divorce whose status is uncertain, which indicates this case alone and no other. **Rather, Rava said: All** of the cases **that exist with regard to betrothal** whose status is uncertain **exist in** cases **of divorce** as well. However, **there are** some cases of uncertainty **with regard to divorce that do not exist with regard to betrothal,** as betrothal performed with a questionable bill is not disqualified. Accordingly, the term: **This is,** utilized in the mishna **with regard to divorce, is not specific** and does not imply exclusion of the case where it is possibly closer to him and possibly closer to her. **Rather, because** the mishna **teaches** the ruling of: **This is, with regard to betrothal,** where it is specific **it teaches** the phrase: **This is, with regard to divorce as well.** The Gemara asks: **And what does** the phrase: **This is,** mentioned **with regard to betrothal,** come **to exclude?** The Gemara answers: **It** comes **to exclude the** matter of the **date, which is not** essential **with regard to betrothal,** as when one betroths a woman by means of a document the date need not be written.

Rava, however, pushes back. If cases can simply be learned from one context to the other, what is the force of the word *zehu* — "this is the case" — which the *mishna* uses? That word implies precision: *these* are the cases, as though to exclude others. *Mai zehu d'katani?* — why does the *mishna* say "this is the case of *safek kiddushin*" and "this is the case of *safek geirushin*" if all cases apply in both directions?

Rava therefore offers a more nuanced formulation: *kol she'yesh be-kiddushin yesh be-geirushin* — every case of *safek* that applies to *kiddushin* applies equally to *geirushin*, but *yesh be-geirushin mah she-ein be-kiddushin* — there are cases in *geirushin* that do not apply to *kiddushin*. The *zehu* by *geirushin* is therefore *lav davka* — it is not exclusive; *geirushin* is a broader category and includes the classic *safek* as well. But the *zehu* by *kiddushin* is precise — it excludes the particular *psulim* of *get* that are not relevant to *kiddushin*.

### Zeman issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא תִּקְּנוּ זְמַן בְּקִידּוּשִׁין? הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם פֵּירֵי — אֲרוּסָה לֵית לַהּ פֵּירֵי. אֶלָּא, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם בַּת אֲחוֹתוֹ — לִיתַקֵּין זְמַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about this matter itself: **And for what** reason **did they not institute** that **the date** must be included **in the betrothal** document? **This** policy **works out well according to the one who says** that the reason the Sages instituted that the date must be written on a bill of divorce **is due to the profits.** As the husband receives the profits from the wife's properties during the period of their marriage, it was necessary to write a date on the bill of divorce in order to know at what point his right to receive or sell these items was terminated. However, it was not necessary to include a date on a deed of betrothal, as this document serves only to create a bond of betrothal, **and there are no profits from a betrothed woman.** A husband does not have the right to receive profits from his betrothed's property until she is his full-fledged wife. **However, according to the one who says** that the Sages instituted the requirement of including the date in the bill of divorce **due to** a case where a man is married to **the daughter of his sister,** then **they should institute** that he must include **the date** in a deed of betrothal as well. Occasionally a man might marry the daughter of his sister, whom he loves all the more because she is his close relative in addition to being his wife. If he knows that she acted licentiously while she was married to him, he might grant her a bill of divorce without a date so as to save her from the death penalty. Were witnesses to come forth and testify to her behavior, she could claim that at the time of her licentious act she was already a divorced woman. If this was indeed the reason for the Sages' instituting the requirement of including the date in a bill of divorce, then the date should be included in a deed of betrothal as well, for an undated document of betrothal could be utilized equally well to prove the innocence of the daughter of his sister. If she acted licentiously in the period prior to her betrothal, she would not be penalized. Therefore, the date should be written on this document as well.

What specifically is excluded? The *psul* of lacking a date — *ein bo zman* — which invalidates a *get* at a rabbinic level, does not apply to a *shtar kiddushin*. The other defects mentioned in the *mishna* — such as the document being written in the husband's handwriting without witnesses, or having only one witness — would equally apply to *kiddushei shtar*, betrothal effected by document. But the absence of a date: that *psul* is unique to *geirushin* and does not render a *shtar kiddushin* invalid. That is why the *mishna* uses *zehu* by *kiddushin* — not to exclude the classic *safek*, but to distinguish the cases that are genuinely unique to *get* from those that apply to both.

## Zeman reason

### Zeman reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּכַסְפָּא וְאִיכָּא דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ בִּשְׁטָרָא, לָא תַּקּוּן רַבָּנַן זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Because there are those who betroth by means of money and those who betroth by means of a deed, the Sages did not institute** that **the date** must be written in the document. As the date of the betrothal has no place in the act of betrothal by means of money, the Sages did not distinguish between the various modes of betrothal.

Having established that the absence of a *zman* is a *psul* in *get* but not in *shtar kiddushin*, the *gemara* now asks directly: *u'mipnei mah lo tiknu zman be-kiddushin?* Why did the Rabbis not require a date in a *shtar kiddushin*, and correspondingly declare one invalid without it?

To answer this, the *gemara* first identifies the reasons for requiring a date in a *get*. Two reasons are given. The first is *mishum peirot*: from the moment a woman is divorced, the husband forfeits his right to benefit from the fruits of her *nichsei milug* — the property she brought into the marriage whose usufruct belonged to him during the marriage. Rashi cites the *gemara* in *Gittin*, in the chapter *Kol ha-Get*, which explains: the date is required so that it can be determined from when she may claim from her husband the fruits he consumed from her property. Since from the moment the husband wrote the *get* — the moment he set his mind to divorce her — he forfeits the right to the fruits, the woman needs to know precisely when that moment was. Without a date, the husband could always claim he had only just now given her the *get*, and dispute her claim to recover what he consumed over the years.

The second reason is *mishum bas achoso* — so that a husband cannot protect his wife from consequences. The scenario is this: a man divorces his wife, perhaps because she committed adultery and faces severe consequences, but he wishes to shield her. He might therefore give her an undated *get*, which she could then present and claim pre-dates the act. Once a date is required, this manipulation is impossible — the witnesses who signed the *get* ensure the date is accurate, and predating is prevented.

### Arusah no Peirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם פֵּירֵי — אֲרוּסָה לֵית לַהּ פֵּירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **This** policy **works out well according to the one who says** that the reason the Sages instituted that the date must be written on a bill of divorce **is due to the profits.** As the husband receives the profits from the wife's properties during the period of their marriage, it was necessary to write a date on the bill of divorce in order to know at what point his right to receive or sell these items was terminated. However, it was not necessary to include a date on a deed of betrothal, as this document serves only to create a bond of betrothal, **and there are no profits from a betrothed woman.** A husband does not have the right to receive profits from his betrothed's property until she is his full-fledged wife.

Now the *gemara* asks: the first reason — *mishum peirot* — clearly does not apply to a *shtar kiddushin*, and this is straightforward. An *arusah* — a woman who is betrothed but not yet fully married — has no *peirot* rights. The husband's entitlement to the fruits of her property begins only from *nissu'in*, not from *kiddushin*. So there is no question of when to date the transition of those rights, and therefore no reason to require a date in the *shtar kiddushin* for this purpose.

### Kesef majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָא עַבְדָּא, דְּאִיכָּא דְּקָנֵי בְּכַסְפָּא וְאִיכָּא בִּשְׁטָרָא, וְתַקּוּן רַבָּנַן זְמַן! הָתָם רוּבָּא בִּשְׁטָרָא, הָכָא רוּבָּא בְּכַסְפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Yosef, said to Rav Ashi: But** with regard to **a slave, where there are** those **who acquire** them **with money and there are** those who acquire them **with a deed, the Sages** nevertheless **instituted** that **the date** must be written in a slave's deed of purchase. He responded: **There,** with regard to slaves, **the majority** of people purchase them **by means of a deed. Here,** with regard to betrothal, **the majority** of people perform betrothal **by means of money.**

But the second reason — *mishum bas achoso*, the concern about covering up adultery — would seem to apply equally to *kiddushin*. If a woman receives an undated *shtar kiddushin*, she could commit adultery and then claim she was only married afterward. Why then do the Rabbis not require a date in a *shtar kiddushin* to prevent this? The *gemara* answers: *mishum de-ika de-mikadesh be-chaspa ve-ika de-mikadesh be-shtara lo tiknu rabbanan zman* — because people perform *kiddushin* both with money and with a document, and since *kiddushei kesef* is far more common, in those cases there is no document and therefore no precise record of the date of *kiddushin* at all. Since we are already not worried about knowing the exact date when *kiddushin* is performed with money, it would be inconsistent to suddenly invalidate a *shtar kiddushin* for lacking a date. We are not going to impose a requirement of *zman* on the document simply because the parties chose to use a *shtar*.

Rav Aha the son of Rav Yosef challenges this reasoning by pointing to the case of slaves: *ve-ha avadim* — slaves can also be acquired either with money or with a document, yet the Rabbis did require a date in a slave acquisition document. Why does the prevalence of non-document acquisition not similarly exempt the slave document from the *zman* requirement? The *gemara* responds: *hasam rova be-shtara* — there, the majority of slave acquisitions are in fact done with a document, since owners naturally want documented proof of property. Given that the majority use a *shtar*, the requirement of *zman* applies, and the minority who use *kesef* are simply not the reference point. *Hacha rova be-chesef* — here, by contrast, the vast majority of *kiddushin* are performed with money, and the document is the exception. The exception does not drive the requirement.

### Document loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר. הֵיכִי לֶיעְבֵּיד? לַינְּחֵהּ גַּבֵּי דִידַהּ — מָחֲקָה לֵיהּ. לַינְּחֵהּ גַּבֵּי דִידֵיהּ — זִמְנִין דְּבַת אֲחוֹתוֹ הִיא וּמְחַפֵּה עֲלַהּ. לַינְּחֵהּ גַּבֵּי עֵדִים — אִי דִּזְכִיִרִי לַיְתוֹ לַיסְהוּד. וְאִי לָא — זִמְנִין דְּחָזוּ מִכְּתָבָא וְאָתוּ מַסְהֲדִי, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: ״מִפִּיהֶם״, וְלֹא מִפִּי כְתָבָם.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** a different reason why the Sages did not institute that the date must be included in a deed of betrothal. This is **due to** the fact that **it is not possible** to institute this in a manner that will ensure that no problems will result. **How would we do** this? If **we leave** the deed of betrothal **with her, she will erase** the date, and so it would remain impossible to prove the juncture at which her licentious behavior took place. If **we leave** the deed **with him,** then **there are times when she is his sister's daughter and he might cover for her** by erasing the date himself. If **we leave it with the witnesses** who signed the document, **if they remember** themselves the date when the deed was given to the woman, the date need not be written in the document itself, for **let them come forth and testify** from their memory. **And if** they do **not** remember by themselves, then **there are times when they see** the date **that is written and come forth to testify** on that basis. **And the Merciful One states:** "By the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15). From this verse it is derived: **From their mouths, and not from their writings,** indicating that testimony is proper only if the individual stated it of himself, and not on the basis of what is written.

A second answer is offered: *ibayis eima mishum d'lo efshar* — or alternatively, it is simply not practically workable to require a date in a *shtar kiddushin*, because it would serve no purpose. *Heichi ne'avid?* — what would you do with such a document? If the *shtar kiddushin* is held by her — *nanchayei gabei didah* — she can simply erase it or claim to have lost it. She has every incentive to do so, since the whole purpose of the date would be to establish when she became married and thereby limit her ability to claim that a subsequent act of adultery predated the marriage. So she is not the right party to hold it. *Lihvei gabei didei* — perhaps the husband should hold it? But the same problem arises in reverse: *zimnin d'basar chosno mechapei lah* — perhaps he will want to protect her, and he too will lose or suppress the document. *Linhvei gabei eidim* — perhaps the witnesses should hold it?

This brings the *gemara* to a fundamental principle: if the witnesses remember the event, *leisavu v'lisahadu* — let them come and testify from memory. And if they do not remember, allowing them to look at the document creates a different problem: *zimnin d'chazu me'hai ksav* — they may see what is written and testify based on it, not on their actual recollection. And the *Torah* says *mipichem v'lo mipi ksavam* — testimony must come from your mouths, from your memory, not from what you read in a document. So having the witnesses hold the document does not help.

### Witness testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בְּגֵירוּשִׁין נָמֵי נֵימָא הָכִי! הָתָם — לְהַצָּלָה דִּידַהּ קָאָתֵי, הָכָא — לְחוֹבָה דִּידַהּ קָאָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, let us say that with regard to divorce as well.** In cases of divorce there should also be a concern lest the woman erase the date on the bill of divorce in her possession. The Gemara responds: **There,** in the case of a bill of divorce, the date **comes to save her,** since the bill of divorce removes her status as a man's wife. She therefore would fear erasing anything lest she disqualify the bill altogether, thereby possibly rendering herself a married woman again (Ramban). **Here,** however, when dealing with a deed of betrothal, the date **comes to her disadvantage,** since until now she was presumed to be a single woman, and if there is no date on the document then she clearly cannot be punished.

The *gemara* then challenges its own reasoning: *ei hachi be-geirushin nami* — if this is the problem, should the same not apply to a *get*? There too, people could lose or erase the document. The answer is obvious: *hasam le-hatzala didah ka'asi* — by a *get*, the document serves to protect her. She needs to produce the *get* to establish her status as a divorced woman eligible to remarry. She therefore has every incentive to preserve it. *Hacha le-chovsa didah ka'asi* — by *kiddushin*, the document is a liability for her, not a protection. So she will dispose of it.

Tosafot raise a pointed question about the *mipichem v'lo mipi ksavam* principle. They note: the entire purpose of a signed *shtar* is that it can be produced as evidence. Why would witnesses need to testify from memory at all? They could simply present the signed document to the court without saying anything, and the document itself would serve as the testimony. Tosafot answer: that would indeed work perfectly well, and if witnesses actually produced the document and refrained from testifying, the signed document would be accepted. The concern is rather that witnesses might not do this — they might read the document and then come to court and testify as though from memory when they do not in fact remember, essentially laundering their testimony through what they read. *Aval chaishinan shema lo ya'idu shekach ra'u bashtar ela shema ya'idu al hama'aseh atzmo k'ilu heim zochrim v'ein heim zochrim ela al pi haksav.*

Tosafot further clarify: if a witness wrote down notes and then re-reads them, such that reading them actually causes him genuinely to recall the event, and he testifies from that authentic recollection, that is permissible. The *gemara* in *Kesubos* (chapter 2) says a person may write down his testimony in a document and testify from it even many years later — *ve-hu shezochra me'atzmo* — provided that he genuinely remembers from himself. But *ein zochra me'atzmo lo* — if he does not actually remember and is merely relaying what he read, that testimony is invalid.

So the problematic case is narrow: testifying about an event one does not remember, not by producing the signed document, but by claiming to remember based on having read the document. Producing the document itself is fine. Testifying from genuine memory prompted by the document is also fine. Only the middle case — purporting to testify from memory when the "memory" is really just what one read — is invalid. Rabbeinu Tam adds that the contemporary practice of witnesses sending their written testimony to court by letter is similarly valid, since there they genuinely do remember — *keivan she-heim zochrim edusam* — and the written form is merely the medium of communication, not a substitute for memory.

## Maamar case

### Scenario setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין נְשׂוּאִין שָׁלֹשׁ נׇכְרִיּוֹת, וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְעָשָׂה בָּהּ הַשֵּׁנִי מַאֲמָר, וּמֵת — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Three brothers** who **were married to three unrelated women, and one of** the brothers **died,** the following occurred: **The second** brother **performed levirate betrothal with** the wife of the deceased brother **and** before he was able to consummate the levirate marriage **he died** as well, leaving behind two women who happen before the third brother for levirate marriage. **Then those** two women must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.**

The *mishna* now presents a new scenario. Three brothers are married to three unrelated women — *gimmel achin nesuin gimmel nochriyos* — and one of them dies. The second brother then performs *maamar* with the first brother's widow, and then he too dies. To illustrate concretely: Reuven, Shimon, and Levi. Reuven is married to Rachel, Shimon to Devorah, Levi to another woman. Reuven dies. Shimon performs *maamar* — a quasi-betrothal in the context of *yibum*, effected like *kiddushin* with money, but not possessing full *d'oraisa* force — with Rachel. Then Shimon dies.

### Double connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן... יְבָמָהּ יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ״ — שֶׁעָלֶיהָ זִיקַת יָבָם אֶחָד, וְלֹא שֶׁעָלֶיהָ זִיקַת שְׁנֵי יְבָמִין. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מְיַיבֵּם לְאֵיזוֹ שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, וְחוֹלֵץ לַשְּׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated:** "If brothers dwell together **and one of them dies** and he has no child, the wife of the dead man shall not be married outside of the family to one not of his kin; **her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her"** (Deuteronomy 25:5). This teaches that a woman eligible for levirate marriage is one **who has one levirate relationship and not one who has a double levirate relationship.** In this case, the wife of the first deceased brother requires levirate marriage due to both the marriage with her first husband as well as the levirate betrothal with the second brother. **Rabbi Shimon says: He** may **consummate the levirate marriage with whichever** woman **he wishes and** then **perform *ḥalitza* with the second.**

Levi now has a *mitzvah* of *yibum* to Devorah, coming from Shimon. He also has a *mitzvah* of *yibum* to Rachel, coming from Reuven. But since Shimon performed *maamar* with Rachel, Shimon has also created a quasi-connection to Rachel. Levi therefore has a dual connection to Rachel: one from Reuven and one from Shimon. He is connected to one woman through two separate deceased brothers.

The *mishna* rules: *harei elu choltzos v'lo mityabmos* — both women, Devorah and Rachel, do *chalitza* and not *yibum*. The *mishna* provides scriptural grounding: the verse states that when one brother dies, *yevamah yavo aleha* — the surviving brother comes upon his *yevama*. This is interpreted to mean *mi she'aleha zikas yavam echad* — only a woman connected to him through one brother, *v'lo she'aleha zikas shnei yevamin* — not a woman connected through two brothers-in-law. Rachel, with her dual connection, falls in the latter category and is therefore excluded from *yibum*.

It is worth noting that most authorities hold *maamar* to be entirely rabbinic in status, and accordingly this entire *derasha* is understood as a rabbinic enactment. But for those who hold, like Beis Shammai, that *maamar* carries some *d'oraisa* weight, Rachel's dual status might indeed have genuine biblical significance.

As for why Devorah cannot receive *yibum* — that will be addressed by the *gemara*.

### Rebbi Shimon

Rebbi Shimon takes a different position: *miyabem le-eizo she-yirtzeh v'choleitz la-shniya* — he may perform *yibum* with whichever one he wishes and *chalitza* with the other. Rebbi Shimon does not accept the intermediate position that *maamar* is "fifty percent." He holds that *maamar* is either fully valid — one hundred percent — or completely void. If *maamar* is fully valid, then Rachel was fully married to Shimon, Reuven is out of the picture, and Rachel's connection to Levi comes only from Shimon; Levi may then do *yibum* with either Rachel or Devorah. If *maamar* is void, then Rachel's only connection is to Reuven, she and Devorah come from different brothers, and Levi may do *yibum* with both. The problematic case only arises if *maamar* is exactly "fifty percent" — creating a dual status — which Rebbi Shimon rejects. Since it is either all or nothing, Levi can do *yibum* with one and must do *chalitza* with the other — because if *maamar* worked, both come from Shimon, and if it did not, only Rachel comes from Reuven. In either scenario one *yibum* and one *chalitza* suffices.

## Gezeira logic

### Doraita exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי זִיקַת שְׁנֵי יְבָמִין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, חֲלִיצָה נָמֵי לָא תִּיבְעֵי! אֶלָּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּגְזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ שְׁתֵּי יְבָמוֹת הַבָּאוֹת מִבַּיִת אֶחָד מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the *halakha* that a woman who has **a double levirate relationship** is exempt from levirate marriage is **by Torah** law, as indicated by the proof offered in the mishna, **she should not require *ḥalitza* as well,** but be completely exempt. **Rather,** it is **by rabbinic** law. The restriction on levirate marriage in this case is not by Torah law, as by Torah law the brother is allowed to consummate the levirate marriage with both of these women since each was the wife of a different brother. The requirement for *ḥalitza* in this case was instituted as a rabbinic **decree lest** people **say** that **two *yevamot* who come from a single household can enter into levirate marriage.** Since the second brother had performed levirate betrothal, people might come to think that both were actually married to him. If the third brother consummates the levirate marriage with both women, it would lead people to think that it is permitted to take two of a brother's wives in levirate marriage, when in fact the Torah allows the *yavam* to marry only a single wife of the deceased.

The *gemara* now reframes the *mishna*'s reasoning. It observes: *ei zikas beit yevamin de'oraisa* — if the problem of Rachel having a dual connection were a genuine *d'oraisa* issue, then *chalitza nami lo tiba'ei* — not only would *yibum* be forbidden, but *chalitza* too would not be required. If the *Torah* is simply excluding a woman with this double status from the household of the surviving brother, she should simply be able to walk away without any ceremony. The very fact that *chalitza* is demanded indicates that the problem is not *d'oraisa*. *Ela miderabbanan* — the entire framework of the double *zikah* as a *psul* is rabbinic, which follows naturally from the fact that *maamar* itself is rabbinic.

### Gezeira Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְיַיבֵּם לַחֲדָא וְנִיחְלוֹץ לַחֲדָא! גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ: בַּיִת אֶחָד, מִקְצָתוֹ בָּנוּ וּמִקְצָתוֹ חָלוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **So let him consummate the levirate marriage with one** woman **and perform *ḥalitza* with** the other **one,** and this would eliminate our concern. The Gemara responds: We do not do this due to a rabbinic **decree lest they say:** When there are two women from **a single household, part of it** must **be built** **and part of it** must **be released by *ḥalitza*.** That is, if two women were married to a single man, one of these women must enter into levirate marriage and the other must perform *ḥalitza*.

Since the problem is rabbinic, why is *chalitza* demanded from Rachel? *Gezeira shema yomru shtei yevamos haba'os mibeit echad mityabmos* — it is a preventive rabbinic decree, lest people say that two *yevamos* from the same household may both be taken in *yibum*. The scenario is this: Reuven may have died years ago, and people have forgotten. Everyone sees Shimon as Rachel's husband — he performed *maamar* with her, a public act of quasi-betrothal. Now Shimon dies, and Levi performs *yibum* with both Devorah and Rachel. Observers will think that a surviving brother performed *yibum* with both of his deceased brother's wives — which is incorrect. The proper rule is that *yibum* is performed with one, and the others receive *chalitza*. To prevent this misunderstanding, the Rabbis enacted that Levi should not perform *yibum* with Rachel. She is the apparent source of the confusion, and so she is designated for *chalitza*. The *d'oraisa* level permits *yibum* with both; it is this *gezeira* that prevents it.

But if that is so, why not permit Levi to do *yibum* with Devorah and *chalitza* with Rachel? That would be accurate *halacha* with no false impression created.

### Wrong order fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֹאמְרוּ! אִי דִּמְיַיבֵּם וַהֲדַר חָלֵיץ — הָכִי נָמֵי. אֶלָּא גְּזֵירָה דִּילְמָא חָלֵיץ בְּרֵישָׁא וַהֲדַר מְיַיבֵּם, וְקָם לֵיהּ בַּ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא יִבְנֶה״, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁלֹּא בָּנָה — שׁוּב לֹא יִבְנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: **So let them say** it. Why would it be problematic if people thought that? Even were they to act upon this mistaken assumption it would cause no harm, as there is no transgression involved in performing *ḥalitza*. The Gemara answers: **If he were to consummate the levirate marriage** with one and then later **proceed to perform *ḥalitza*** with the other, then **indeed** there would be no reason for concern. **Rather,** the requirement to perform *ḥalitza* with both women is a rabbinic **decree** that was instituted **lest he first perform *ḥalitza*** with one of his brother's wives **and subsequently consummate the levirate marriage** with the other. Under such circumstances, he would in fact be violating a prohibition. Once he performs *ḥalitza* with the first woman **he is subject to** the prohibition indicated by the verse "So shall it be done to the man **who does not build** his brother's house" (Deuteronomy 25:9). In this verse **the Merciful One states** that **once he did not build** his brother's house but rather opted to perform *ḥalitza* with one of his brother's wives, **he may not proceed to build** it by consummating the levirate marriage with a different wife.

The *gemara* explains: *gezeira shema yomru beit echad miktzato banuy umiktzato chalutz* — there is a concern that people will observe Levi doing *yibum* with one woman and *chalitza* with the other, and will conclude that whenever a man dies leaving multiple wives, the surviving brother may perform *yibum* with one and *chalitza* with the others. This is a misunderstanding, but it is not without halachic cost.

The *gemara* probes this: why should we care if people draw that mistaken conclusion? The *gemara* responds: *ei de-miyabeim ve-hadar chalitz* — the problem is not the misunderstanding per se, but its practical consequence. If people believe that one performs *yibum* with one wife and *chalitza* with another, they may do them in the wrong order: *dilma chalitz bereisha ve-hadar miyabeim* — perhaps they will first perform *chalitza* with the woman they are less interested in, and only then perform *yibum* with the other. This sequence is halachically fatal. Once *chalitza* has been performed, the surviving brother enters the category of one who has *not* built up his brother's house — *ba'asher lo yivneh* — and *rachmana amar keivan shelo bana shuv lo yivneh*: once he has not built, he may no longer build. The *chalitza* disqualifies him from subsequently performing *yibum* with any of the remaining *yevamos*.

Therefore the Rabbis enacted the full stringency: both women must do *chalitza*, and neither may receive *yibum*. This comes at no halachic cost — an extra *chalitza* performed when not strictly required is far preferable to the risk that someone performs *chalitza* before *yibum* and thereby permanently forecloses the *yibum* option.

## Get negation

### Rava position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: נָתַן גֵּט לְמַאֲמָרוֹ — הוּתְּרָה צָרָתָהּ. אֲבָל הִיא אֲסוּרָה, דְּמִחַלְּפָה בְּבַעֲלַת גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** If the brother who performed levirate betrothal in the case described in the mishna subsequently **gave a bill of divorce** to that wife in order to nullify **his levirate betrothal** before he died, **her rival wife is rendered permitted** to the third brother. The third brother is allowed to consummate the levirate marriage with the wife of the second brother because this wife is no longer considered to be the rival wife of a woman with a double levirate relationship. The bill of divorce serves to nullify the act of levirate betrothal completely. **However,** the woman with whom the brother performed levirate betrothal and then received the bill of divorce **is forbidden** to the third brother. Why is this? **She might be confused with** a woman **who receives a bill of divorce.** In general cases where levirate betrothal was not performed, if a man gives a *yevama* a bill of divorce, he is no longer allowed to consummate the levirate marriage with her. If it were permitted to consummate the levirate marriage in this case, people might mistakenly do so in other cases of divorce as well.

Rava now introduces a further question. Suppose that after performing *maamar* with Rachel, Shimon — before he died — decided he no longer wished to proceed, and gave Rachel a *get*. Does this *get*, which was intended to negate the *maamar*, free Levi to perform *yibum* with Devorah? And might Levi even be free to perform *yibum* with Rachel herself, who would revert to her status as a *yevama* awaiting *yibum* or *chalitza*?

Rava rules: *nosan get le-maamaro, hutra tzorasa, aval hi asurah* — if the second brother gave a *get* to negate his *maamar*, the co-wife Devorah is freed and becomes available for *yibum*, but Rachel herself remains forbidden to *yibum*. She requires *chalitza*, but not *yibum*.

Why? *Demichlifa be-baalas get* — because she is liable to be confused with a woman who received a *get* addressed to the *zika* itself. The relevant background: if no *maamar* was performed and the surviving brother wishes to decline *yibum*, he performs *chalitza*. But there also exists the option of giving the *yevama* a *get* addressed to the *zika* — a *get le-zika*. Such a *get* is a rabbinic mechanism: it does not dissolve the *zika* at the *d'oraisa* level (which only *chalitza* can do), but it rabbinically precludes the possibility of *yibum*, after which *chalitza* is still required. So there are two distinct uses of a *get* in this context: a *get le-maamar*, which only negates the quasi-betrothal, and a *get le-zika*, which precludes *yibum* for anyone. Since Rachel has now received a *get* from one of the brothers, observers cannot know which type of *get* it was. It looks like she received a *get* on the *zika* — and a woman who received such a *get* may not receive *yibum* from any of the surviving brothers. Therefore, even though Shimon's *get* was only intended to negate the *maamar*, Levi cannot perform *yibum* with Rachel, because she appears to be a woman for whom *yibum* has been foreclosed.

### Alternative view

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: נָתַן גֵּט לְמַאֲמָרוֹ — הוּתְּרָה אֲפִילּוּ הִיא. מַאי טַעְמָא? מַאי דַּעֲבַד בַּהּ — שַׁקְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** an alternative formulation of Rava's statement. **Rava said:** If the second brother **gave a bill of divorce** to the wife of the first deceased brother in order to nullify **his levirate betrothal** to her, then **even she** herself **is permitted** to enter into levirate marriage with the third brother. **What is the reason** for this? **That which he performed with her, he removed.** The levirate betrothal was completely nullified by the bill of divorce, and it is as though he had done nothing at all. Therefore, as far as the third brother is concerned, she remains with her original, single levirate bond, as though her status had never been changed by the second brother.

*Ika d'amri* — some say Rava rules differently in this case: *nosan get le-maamaro, hutra, afilu hi* — even Rachel herself is permitted for *yibum*. Why? *Mah d'avad bah shaklei* — what he did to her, the *maamar*, he fully removed. The *get* is seen as precisely canceling the *maamar* and nothing more — it does not alter her status beyond that, and she is not treated as a *baalas ha-get* in any broader sense.

### Practical issue

The practical question underlying the dispute is: how does one distinguish between a *get le-maamar* and a *get le-zika*? Rashi explains that it depends on what is specified at the time the *get* is given. A *get* given without specification — *stama* — is presumed to apply to the *zika* as well and to prevent *yibum* by any of the brothers. Only a *get* explicitly given solely against the *maamar* is treated as limited in its effect.

This ambiguity, as the *gemara* itself makes evident, actually strengthens Rava's first position considerably. Given how unclear it typically is whether a *get* was given *le-maamar* or *le-zika*, it makes abundant sense to treat any *get* received by such a woman as potentially precluding *yibum* — and therefore to forbid Levi from performing *yibum* with Rachel even when the original intent was merely to cancel the *maamar*.

## Sisters case

### Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי אַחִין נְשׂוּאִין שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וּמֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה עָלָיו עוֹלָמִית, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶסְרָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case where **two brothers were married to two sisters, and one of** the brothers **died,** the widow at this point would be exempt from levirate marriage as the sister of his wife. **And afterward the wife of the second** brother **died.** Although the *yevama* is no longer the sister of his wife, **this** woman **is nevertheless forbidden to him forever, since she had** already **been forbidden to him at one time.**

The *mishna* moves to a further case: *shnei achim nesuim shtei achayos u-meis echad mehen* — two brothers married to two sisters, and one of them dies. *V'achar kach meitah ishto shel sheni* — and afterward, the wife of the second brother dies.

The situation is this: Reuven married Rachel, Shimon married Leah. Reuven dies. Rachel's status relative to Shimon is immediately that of *achos isha* — the sister of his wife — which is an *ervah*, a forbidden relation. She walks away without any need for *yibum* or *chalitza*. Then Leah, Shimon's wife, dies. One might wonder: perhaps now that Leah is no longer alive, Rachel is no longer *achos isha* and Shimon could perform *yibum* with her. The *mishna* rules otherwise: *v'achar kach meitah ishto shel sheni harei zo asurah alav olamit* — Rachel remains eternally forbidden to Shimon. *Ho'il v'neasrah alav sha'ah achas* — because at that critical initial moment, when *yibum* first became relevant upon Reuven's death, she was forbidden to Shimon. That status does not dissolve when Leah subsequently dies.

### Obvious case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! הַשְׁתָּא, וּמָה הָתָם דְּלָא מִידַּחְיָא מֵהַאי בֵּיתָא לִגְמָרֵי — אָמְרַתְּ לָא, הָכָא דְּקָא מִידַּחְיָא מֵהַאי בֵּיתָא לִגְמָרֵי — לָא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן! תַּנָּא — הָא תְּנָא בְּרֵישָׁא, וְהָךְ חַזְיַאּ לְהֶיתֵּירָא וְשַׁרְיַאּ, וַהֲדַר חַזְיַאּ לְאִיסּוּרָא. וְאַיְּידֵי דְּחַבִּיבָה לֵיהּ אַקְדְּמַהּ, וּמִשְׁנָה לֹא זָזָה מִמְּקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** this **obvious? Now, just as there,** in the earlier *mishnayot*, where there was a third brother and so the woman **was not disassociated from this household completely,** as the levirate obligation existed between her and the third brother to whom she was permitted, **you said** that **she may not** enter into levirate marriage because she was forbidden at one time, **here,** in the case of only two brothers, **where she is disassociated from this household completely,** is it **not all the more so** clear that it should be prohibited for her to enter into levirate marriage? The Gemara answers: **The *tanna* taught this** mishna **at first,** at which time **he saw it** fitting to rule that **those** other cases are **permitted, and** therefore he **permitted them. And then he** subsequently **saw it** fitting to rule that **they are forbidden. And since** that case was novel, **it was beloved to him, he taught it earlier.** But despite the fact that this mishna was no longer necessary, **the mishna does not move from its place.**

The *gemara* observes: *pshita* — this is obvious. If anything, the case just discussed in the previous *mishna* — where Rachel was not completely excluded from the household, because she was still relevant to Levi even if forbidden to Shimon — presents a harder question, and yet there too she remained forbidden to Shimon. Here, where Rachel is completely pushed away from the family — *d'ka midcheya mei-hai beisa l'gamrei* — there are only two brothers and she is forbidden to both, so she has no connection to the family whatsoever — *lo kol sheken* — is it not all the more obvious that she remains forbidden?

The *gemara* explains why the *mishna* nevertheless teaches this case. *Tana hai tana b'reisha* — the *tana* taught this case first. And initially, when the *tana* was arranging the *mishna*, he considered the case of the more complex three-brother scenario and perhaps thought that after Levi married Rachel, her status relative to Shimon might have changed. *V'sharya* — he thought it might be permitted. *Ve-hadar chaza le-isura* — and then he concluded that no, even there, she was forbidden to Shimon from the moment of Reuven's death, and that initial forbidden status endures.

Once having resolved the complex case, the simpler two-brother case was clearly also forbidden. *V'aydi d'chaviva lei akdama* — but because this simpler case had seemed like a significant *chiddush* worth exploring, it had already been placed first in the arrangement of the *mishna*. *U-mishna lo zaza mimkomah* — and a *mishna* does not move from its place. Once it was included, it remained, even though by the time the full teaching was in its final form the case had become obvious given the other *mishna* that preceded it.
