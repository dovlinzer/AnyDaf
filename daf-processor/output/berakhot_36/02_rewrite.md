# Berakhot 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine's Special Status

### Wine Exception

The *mishnah* establishes a general rule: everything that derives from fruit receives the blessing of *borei pri ha'etz*, with the sole exception of wine, which receives *borei pri hagafen*. Implicit in this rule is a foundational assumption — that even when something undergoes transformation, it retains its original *bracha*. Were wine not exceptional, it would seemingly receive a *borei pri ha'etz* like any other fruit product. The question the *gemara* immediately poses is: why does wine merit its own distinct blessing?

### Transformation Theory

The *gemara* opens by proposing that wine receives a special *bracha* because it has undergone a transformation to a higher state — *ishtani le'iluya* — wine being, in some sense, superior to grapes. But the *gemara* immediately challenges this: olive oil also represents an improvement upon raw olives, yet as *Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Shmuel*, and as *Rav Yitzchak* likewise said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*, the blessing on olive oil is *borei pri ha'etz* — not a distinct blessing of its own. Transformation to a higher state cannot, by itself, be the reason wine receives a unique *bracha*.

The *gemara* answers that the cases are not parallel: the reason no special blessing was formulated for olive oil is a textual impossibility rather than a conceptual one. One could not simply say *borei pri hazayit* — "Who creates the fruit of the olive" — because the word *zayit* denotes the olive itself, not the oil. The fruit of the olive is the olive; God did not directly create olive oil. This reflects a broader principle: the *bracha* we recite must be literally true of what God has directly created, while simultaneously being capable of extension to include the derived product. *Borei pri hagafen* accomplishes this for wine because it refers to the fruit of the vine — technically the grape — but its abstraction allows it to encompass wine as well. A parallel construction for olive oil would require us to say that God created "the fruit of the olive," which is either false (God created the olive, not oil derived from it through human processing) or confusing (the word already names the fruit). One could suggest *borei pri etz hazayit* — "Who creates the fruit of the olive tree" — which is more textually workable, but the *gemara* in the end sets this aside in favor of the deeper answer to follow.

Underlying this discussion is an important conceptual point: *borei pri hagafen* does not say "Who creates wine." Even on wine, we praise God for creating "the fruit of the vine." God created grapes; human beings, in partnership with the natural processes of fermentation, created wine. The blessing acknowledges the natural substrate while extending, through abstraction, to the transformed product. The same move cannot be made gracefully for oil.

### Wine's Dual Function

The *gemara* advances to the substantive reason wine merits its own *bracha*: *Mar Zutra* states that wine satiates — *chamra zayen* — while oil does not. This, in essence, distinguishes wine from oil in terms of importance and functional weight.

But the *gemara* immediately challenges even the claim that oil does not satiate. The *mishnah* in *Nedarim* states that one who vows to abstain from *mazon* — sustaining food — is nonetheless permitted water and salt. This implies that water and salt alone are excluded from the category of *mazon*, while everything else provides sustenance. Does this not include oil? *Rav Huna* resolves the difficulty by noting that the vow in question uses the broader formulation *kol hazan alai* — "anything that provides sustenance to me" — rather than the more restricted term *mazon*. Everything except water and salt provides some degree of sustenance; but not everything shares the identity of *mazon* in the narrower sense. Oil, then, does provide some sustenance — *alma mishcha zayen* — but not to the same degree as wine.

The *gemara* then probes whether wine truly satiates at all. *Rava* would drink wine on *erev Pesach* specifically to whet his appetite so that he could eat *matzah* with greater desire. Wine, on this account, increases appetite rather than suppressing it. The *gemara* resolves this: a large quantity of wine whets the appetite, while a small amount satiates. The question is then pressed further from a verse: *yayin yismach levav enosh* — "wine gladdens the heart of man" — while *lechem levav enosh yisad* — "bread sustains the heart of man." If bread satiates and wine gladdens, how can we say wine satiates?

The *gemara* reframes the position: wine does two things — *sa'id u'mesame'ach* — it satiates and it gladdens. Bread only satiates; it does not gladden. Wine thus combines both functions, and this dual quality is what earns it a unique *bracha*. The combination of biological sustenance and the capacity to elevate one's spirit places wine in a category of its own among foods and drinks.

### Wine Not a Meal

The *gemara* then pushes the reasoning to its logical conclusion: if wine both satiates and gladdens, perhaps one should recite *birkat hamazon* after drinking it, just as after a bread meal. The *gemara* rejects this: *lo kavei inshi sudata alei* — people do not establish a meal around wine. Wine may perform the biological function of filling a person, but it is not the kind of thing around which a meal is organized. One does not sit down to a meal of wine.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* presses *Rava*: what if someone actually declared that they were making a meal of wine — would *birkat hamazon* then apply? *Rava* responds sharply: such a person's individual perception is nullified — *batla deitei etzel kol adam* — no normal person makes a meal of wine, and therefore one's subjective determination cannot transform it into a *seudah* requiring *birkat hamazon*.

This ruling raises a more general question about when subjective context can affect *halachic* categorization. The *gemara* here is clear that establishing something as a *kovi'at seudah* for purposes of *birkat hamazon* is not the kind of determination that can be left to individual perception. By contrast, questions of *ikar* and *tafel* — which component of a mixed dish is primary and which secondary — do carry a significant subjective dimension, since these categories are by their nature contextual. But whether something constitutes a meal at all is a threshold question about shared norms, not individual preference, and *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* shuts off any argument from individual intention.

## Olive Oil Status

### Oil Contradiction

Having concluded the discussion of wine, the *gemara* returns to examine the earlier statement directly: *Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Shmuel*, and *Rav Yitzchak* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*, that the blessing on olive oil is *borei pri ha'etz*. The *gemara* immediately asks: under what circumstances? If one drinks it directly, that causes harm — as the *braita* demonstrates from the laws of *terumah*: one who drinks *terumah* oil pays only the principal, not the added fifth, because drinking oil directly is not the normal mode of deriving benefit. The Torah's obligation of repayment plus a fifth applies only to *ki yochal* — genuine consumption — and pure drinking of oil does not qualify. The oil damages the person; it is not proper consumption. So this cannot be the case for which *borei pri ha'etz* is prescribed.

### Oil in Mixture

Perhaps, then, one eats it through bread — dipping bread into oil. The *gemara* dispatches this quickly: the bread is *ikar* and the oil is *tafel*, and the rule is explicit in the *mishnah*: one makes the *bracha* on the primary item and thereby exempts the secondary. The oil would not receive its own *bracha* at all.

### Ikar and Tafel

The *gemara* therefore identifies the correct case: one drinks the oil by way of *anigron* — a medicinal mixture. *Rabba bar Shmuel* explains that *anigron* is water of beets, a liquid preparation into which oil is mixed. The parallel preparation *asigron* is water from other boiled vegetables. These are drinkable mixtures into which olive oil is combined, and in this context the oil may be consumed in a meaningful way.

But even here the *gemara* objects: normally the *anigron* — the beet liquid — would be *ikar* and the oil would be *tafel*. The principle returns: the *bracha* follows the primary ingredient. The *gemara* therefore narrows the case further: we are speaking of one who has a sore throat — *choshesh bigrono* — and is drinking the *anigron* specifically for the therapeutic benefit of the oil. In this context the oil is the purpose and the primary element. A *braita* confirms the framework: on *Shabbat*, one may not gargle with oil directly, as that is an overt therapeutic act, but one may prepare an *anigron* mixture with a high oil content and swallow it — the act retains the appearance of ordinary eating while providing the curative benefit on the side.

The *gemara* then asks: is this not obvious? If oil in this context is *ikar*, of course one makes *borei pri ha'etz*. The *gemara* answers that there was a reason to think otherwise: since the person's intention is *refuah* — medical benefit — one might have thought no *bracha* is said at all, as it is functioning as medicine rather than food. The *gemara* teaches otherwise: *keivan de'it lei hana'ah minei ba'ei beruchi* — since one derives genuine pleasure and benefit from it, a *bracha* is required.

This leaves open a broader question that the *gemara* does not fully resolve: what is the rule for something that is purely medicinal and provides no food-like pleasure — such as a flavored cough syrup? The *gemara*'s ruling here covers a case where the substance remains fundamentally a food, consumed in a food-like way, even if the motivation is therapeutic. Whether the same applies to preparations that are purely pharmaceutical in character remains an open question.

One further point is implicit in this discussion: if someone were to drink olive oil directly with no therapeutic framework and no *anigron* mixture, purely out of personal preference, would they even make *borei pri ha'etz*? Almost certainly not — *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* would apply. Whether such a person would make *shehakol* or no *bracha* at all is a separate question left open. The key principle established is that *borei pri ha'etz* on olive oil applies in the specific case where oil is the *ikar* in a context where its consumption is recognized as normal.

## Wheat Flour Case

### Flour Debate

The *gemara* now moves into a broader discussion of foods consumed in unusual or altered states, extending the principles opened by the *mishnah*. The case is *kimcha de'chitti* — wheat flour eaten plain. A *braita* elsewhere establishes that one who chews raw wheat kernels says *borei pri ha'adamah*. The question is whether this *bracha* is retained when those same kernels have been ground into flour.

*Rav Yehuda* says one still recites *borei pri ha'adamah*. *Rav Nachman* says one recites *shehakol niheyah bidvaro*. *Rava* urges *Rav Nachman* not to dispute *Rav Yehuda*, since both *Shmuel* and *Rabbi Yochanan* — transmitted through *Rav Yehuda* himself — maintain that olive oil receives *borei pri ha'etz* even though it is not in its natural form. If oil retains its original *bracha* despite transformation, so too should flour.

### Flour vs. Oil

The *gemara* rejects the comparison: *hatam leit lei iluya achrina* — for olive oil, there is no higher state to which it can progress. Oil is what it is; there is no further, superior form of consumption that the olive is "supposed to" become. Flour, by contrast, has a higher state available to it: bread. Flour is not in its ideal form. It is an intermediate stage on the way to becoming something else. This intermediate character undermines its status.

One might have framed the issue differently — not that flour has a higher potential state, but simply that flour is not a normal way of eating wheat. People chew kernels; people eat bread; but eating flour directly is not a recognized mode of consumption. The *gemara*'s emphasis on the "higher state" — *iluya achrina* — points to the same underlying reality: flour is not food in its own right. It is not yet at a stage fit for normal consumption.

### Unfit Foods

The *gemara* then asks: does the *gemara* mean to say that anything in an intermediate state that has a higher form to reach receives only *shehakol*? *Rabbi Zeira* in the name of *Rav Mattana* in the name of *Shmuel* rules that a raw gourd — *akra chaya* — and barley flour — *kimcha de'sa'ari* — both receive *shehakol*, precisely because they are unfit for consumption. This is noteworthy: a raw gourd is in its natural state — it has not been processed at all — yet it receives only *shehakol*. Natural form, then, does not protect a food; what matters is whether it is in a state fit for normal consumption.

One might ask: does the ruling about barley flour apply equally to wheat flour? The *gemara* says yes: if anything, the *gemara* should have stated the rule for wheat flour — which is closer to human food — and we would have derived the more obvious case of barley flour from it by *kal ve'chomer*. The reason barley flour was specified is to teach a different lesson: one might have thought that barley flour, which is not genuinely human food and can even cause harm, receives no *bracha* at all. The *gemara* teaches that even here, some *bracha* applies.

### Blessing Floor

This brings the *gemara* to the question of the floor of *brachot*: is there anything one might eat that receives absolutely no *bracha*? The *gemara* raises salt and *zumes* — a type of brine or salt water. A *braita* rules that on salt and brine one says *shehakol niheyah bidvaro*. These are things people eat in negligible quantities and only rarely; yet they receive *shehakol*. How could one have thought barley flour receives less than this?

The *gemara* answers: salt is something people do sometimes put directly in their mouths, even if rarely. But barley flour — *kimcha de'sa'ari* — actually causes intestinal harm, *tehol ve'kashi le'kukiani*. One might have thought that something actively deleterious to health is so far outside the category of food that it warrants no *bracha* at all. The *gemara* teaches: *keivan de'it lei hana'ah minei ba'ei brukhi* — since one derives some pleasure from it, a *bracha* is required.

The upshot of this extended analysis is a set of interlocking principles. First, natural form does not guarantee a food's standard *bracha* — raw gourd in its natural state gets *shehakol*. Second, changed form does not necessarily reduce a *bracha* — olive oil in the appropriate context retains *borei pri ha'etz*. Third, what determines the *bracha* is primarily whether the item is in a normal, fit-for-consumption state and whether consuming it in that way is a recognized mode of benefit. Fourth, the combination of changed form and an intermediate, non-ideal state — as with wheat flour — reduces the *bracha* further, to *shehakol*. Fifth, the floor of the system is *shehakol*: as long as any pleasure is derived, some *bracha* applies.

*Tosafot* adds an important qualification on the wheat flour case. *Tosafot* notes that the *gemara* is likely not discussing flour that has been ground to a complete powder, because in that case there would never be grounds for *borei pri ha'adamah* to apply at all. Rather, *Tosafot* understands that *borei pri ha'adamah* applies only to a coarser, grainier flour that still retains some quality of the original grain. Once something has been so thoroughly processed that it entirely loses its original form — as with, for instance, a product that has been pulverized, fully reformed, and bears no visual or textural relationship to its source — even *borei pri ha'adamah* is no longer appropriate, and the *bracha* falls to *shehakol*. This principle underlies the practical distinction often drawn between regular potato chips, which retain enough of the original form to merit *borei pri ha'adamah*, and products like extruded chips that are reconstituted from flour, which receive *shehakol*.

## Planting Intent

### Palm Hearts

The *gemara* now turns to a related but distinct principle: the intent of the one who plants a tree and how that intent shapes the *bracha* on what the tree produces. The case is *kora* — the tender tip of the palm leaf, which *Rashi* describes as the soft new growth of the leaf before it hardens. *Rav Yehuda* rules *borei pri ha'adamah*: it is a kind of fruit, growing from a tree, and merits this *bracha*. *Shmuel* rules *shehakol niheyah bidvaro*: since if left alone it would harden and become inedible — *hava sofo lehakshot* — its status as an edible product is incomplete and therefore it receives only *shehakol*.

### Radish Comparison

*Shmuel* then concedes to *Rav Yehuda* — *shinana, kevatach mistabra* — your position actually makes more sense. After all, a radish also hardens and becomes inedible if left in the ground too long, yet in its edible state it receives *borei pri ha'adamah*. By analogy, the soft tip of the palm leaf should also receive *borei pri ha'adamah* while it is still edible.

But the *gemara* concludes: *velo hi* — this comparison does not hold. The radish is planted with the intent of eating it — *ada'ata de'pugla natei inshi*. The palm tree — *dikla* — is not planted with the intent of eating the leaf tips — *lo ada'ata de'kora natei inshi*. The palm is planted for its dates, its wood, its fiber — not for these incidental edible portions. Because this product falls entirely outside the intent of planting, it receives only *shehakol*. Although *Shmuel* acknowledged the logical strength of *Rav Yehuda*'s argument, the *gemara* rules: *hilchata kevata de'Shmuel* — the law follows *Shmuel*.

### Caper Bush

The *gemara* challenges this principle with the case of the *tzalaf* — the caper bush. The caper bush is planted primarily for its fruit — *ada'ata de'parcha natei inshi* — yet the *mishnah* rules that on *minei nitzpa* — various secondary parts of the bush, including leaf buds and capers attached to the leaves — one recites *borei pri ha'adamah*, while on the *evyonot* — the actual fruit — and the *kaphrisin* — which *Rashi* understands as a kind of shell or casing surrounding the fruit — one recites *borei pri ha'etz*. This creates a hierarchy: the primary fruit of the bush gets *borei pri ha'etz*, while secondary products that are not the main reason for planting get *borei pri ha'adamah*. Why, then, should the palm tips receive only *shehakol* rather than *borei pri ha'adamah*?

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* answers: the caper bush is planted with multiple products in mind. Even if the fruit is primary, the other parts — the leaves, buds, and related components — are also among the things one hopes to harvest when planting. *Ada'ata de'shusha natei inshi* — people plant the caper bush with these secondary products part of their expectation. By contrast, *lo natei inshi ada'ata de'kora* — no one plants a palm tree with the leaf tips in mind. These are entirely outside the intent of planting, and therefore the palm tips fall to *shehakol* rather than *borei pri ha'adamah*.

### Blessing Hierarchy

What emerges from this discussion is a clear three-tiered hierarchy governed by the intent of planting. The primary product for which the tree is planted receives *borei pri ha'etz*. Secondary products that are part of the planting intent — things one hopes to harvest even if they are not the main goal — receive *borei pri ha'adamah*. Products that fall entirely outside any planting intent, which just happen to grow from the tree and which some people may occasionally eat, receive only *shehakol*.

This principle has practical implications that extend beyond the classical cases. The *bracha* appropriate for a given food can, in principle, shift over time as agricultural practice and market demand evolve. If a product that was previously incidental to a tree's cultivation becomes the primary reason for planting — or moves from being an occasional curiosity to a commercially cultivated commodity — its *bracha* may change accordingly. Anything available in a market context was presumably planted *ada'ata de'hachi* — with that product in mind — and would therefore qualify at minimum for *borei pri ha'adamah*, assuming it is in a reasonably natural and processed state. The transition from *shehakol* to *borei pri ha'adamah*, and potentially from *borei pri ha'adamah* to *borei pri ha'etz*, is governed by the accumulated norms of cultivation and consumption.

## Modern Applications

### Juice Question

The principles developed throughout this *sugya* bear directly on contemporary practice in ways that remain actively debated. Take apple juice and orange juice. These are clearly transformed from their source fruit — *ishtani* — but they are not in an intermediate state on the way to a better product, and they are not excessively processed. If anything, they represent a highly desirable form of consumption. On the reasoning developed by the *gemara*, one might expect *borei pri ha'etz* to apply. And indeed, there are serious *halachic* opinions that argue exactly this.

However, the prevailing practice is to recite *shehakol*. The historical basis for this practice lies in the reality of the *gemara*'s world: in that era, no one squeezed juice from apples or oranges for direct consumption. Grape juice and olive oil were the only fruits regularly processed into liquid form. Juice from other fruits was considered incidental — a byproduct, almost a sweat of the fruit — not a recognized mode of deriving benefit. It was therefore *ishtani li'greiusa* — a transformation to a worse state — and would have received *shehakol*. Today the situation has changed substantially. Specific varieties of oranges are cultivated expressly for juice production; the juice is the intended product. Many authorities argue that *ada'ata de'hachi natei inshi* — the planting intent has shifted — and that *borei pri ha'etz* is now warranted. Nevertheless, *minhag ha'olam* — the accepted widespread practice — remains *shehakol*, in part because reciting *borei pri ha'etz* on a liquid feels counterintuitive to most people, even though the *gemara* itself does exactly this in the case of olive oil.

### Coffee & Cocoa

Coffee beans and cocoa beans present analogous questions. These are products of trees — the coffee plant and the cacao tree — and they are planted expressly for the derived products: coffee and chocolate. The *ada'ata de'hachi* argument would seem to point toward *borei pri ha'etz* for these as well, and there are those who so rule, particularly for foods with a high concentration of the primary product, such as very dark chocolate. In the case of coffee, there is the additional complication that the beverage is nearly entirely water flavored by the beans, which raises questions of *ikar* and *tafel*.

### Custom Practice

Here too, *minhag ha'olam* is to recite *shehakol*. The tension between a serious *halachic* argument for *borei pri ha'etz* and the prevailing custom of *shehakol* illustrates a recurring dynamic in this area of *halacha*: the principles established in the *gemara* generate expectations that, when applied to modern agricultural and culinary realities, point in one direction, while accepted practice has crystallized around a different norm. The *gemara*'s framework itself provides the tools to argue both ways — the question of *ada'ata de'hachi*, the question of transformation for better or worse, the question of whether a *bracha* appropriate to a liquid differs from one appropriate to solid food — and the debates remain live for those who engage seriously with the underlying reasoning.
