# Ketubot 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Captive Women

### Intro: Knas & Captivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בִּקְדוּשְׁתַּהּ קָיְימָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַגִּיּוֹרֶת שֶׁנִּתְגַּיְּירָה וְרָאֲתָה דָּם, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּהּ שְׁעָתָהּ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הִיא כְּכׇל הַנָּשִׁים, וּמְטַמְּאָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת, וּמִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** does **Rabbi Yehuda maintain** that a captive woman **remains in her** state of **sanctity? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a female **convert who converted and saw** the flow of menstrual **blood** on that same day, **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Deeming **her** impure from the **hour** that she saw the menstrual flow **is sufficient for her.** There is no decree of retroactive impurity on objects that she touched earlier, due to the concern that the blood flow might have started earlier. **Rabbi Yosei says: Her** legal status **is like** that of **all** of **the** Jewish **women, and she** therefore **transmits impurity** retroactively **for a twenty-four hour period** following her conversion, or **from examination to examination,** i.e., from the last time she examined herself.

The daf opens by returning to the *mishnah* introduced on 36b, which presented the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda that a woman taken captive retains the presumption of *bikdushatah* — that she has not been violated. The practical context of that *mishnah* was the law of *knas*, the statutory fine imposed upon a rapist, and the *gemara* had previously explored whether Rabbi Yehuda's position was categorical — applying even to questions of *terumah* or eligibility to marry a *kohen* — or whether it was driven by a specific policy consideration: *lo yehei chotei niskar*, the principle that a sinner should not benefit. That consideration would naturally support the woman's interests in *knas* and *ketubah* contexts, where denying her the presumption would effectively reward the wrongdoer, but might not extend to other domains. The *gemara* now proceeds under the assumption that Rabbi Yehuda's position is categorical, and sets out to test it.

### Giyoret & Niddah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָה לְהַמְתִּין שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר לֵיאָרֵס וְלִינָּשֵׂא מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** a convert **is required to wait three months** after her conversion before marrying a Jew, due to the concern that she is pregnant, leading to confusion whether the child was conceived before or after her conversion; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei permits her to be betrothed and to be married immediately.**

The challenge comes from a *baraita* dealing with a *giyoret* — a woman who converts to Judaism — who sees blood shortly after her conversion. The ordinary rule governing *niddah* status is that impurity begins not when blood exits the body but when it passes internally from the uterus through the cervix into the vaginal canal. Since that internal transition cannot be directly observed, the Rabbis instituted a retroactive concern: unless the woman performed a *bedikah* more recently, she is considered to have been a *niddah* for the preceding twenty-four hours. If she converted twelve hours ago, the question is whether we extend that retroactive concern back through the twelve hours she has been Jewish.

The *baraita* records a dispute. Rabbi Yehuda says *dayah sha'atah* — her moment is sufficient; she is treated as having become a *niddah* only now, and the retroactive window does not apply to her. Rabbi Yose, by contrast, says she is like all other women — *metama'ah mei'eit l'eit u-mipkidah l'fkidah* — and is *tame'ah* retroactively, bounded by either twenty-four hours or the time of conversion, whichever is shorter. The Ramban explains Rabbi Yehuda's position as follows: since the rabbinic institution cannot be applied in its full normal form — the woman was not Jewish for the entire twenty-four-hour window — it is not applied at all, and we revert to the biblical baseline that she became a *niddah* only now.

A related question arises in practice: may a woman convert while she is actively menstruating or in a state of *tumas niddah*? The answer is that it poses no obstacle. *Tumas niddah* is a form of *tumah* like any other, and just as *tumah* from a corpse does not prevent conversion, neither does *niddah* status. Immersion in the *mikvah* for conversion purposes is valid even if the woman's *niddah* period has not yet concluded and the immersion cannot simultaneously serve to purify her from *niddah* status.

### Papa's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גִּיּוֹרֶת אַשְּׁבוּיָהּ קָא רָמֵית? גִּיּוֹרֶת לָא מְנַטְּרָא נַפְשַׁהּ, שְׁבוּיָה מְנַטְּרָא נַפְשַׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to** Rav Pappa bar Shmuel: **Are you raising a contradiction** from the *halakha* of **a convert to** that of **a captive** woman? **A convert does not protect herself** from engaging in sexual relations before conversion, whereas **a captive protects herself,** as she is conscious of the sanctity of the Jewish people and does not want to be violated.

Now Rav Papa bar Shmuel challenges Rav Yosef with a seeming contradiction within Rabbi Yehuda's own positions. The challenge begins with the *niddah* case as a lead-in, but the sharper problem arises from the continuation of the *baraita*, which states that a *giyoret* must wait three months before marrying. This waiting period exists because of concern that she may be pregnant from before her conversion. The *gemara* understands the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose not as a dispute about whether the pre-conversion paternity matters halachically — though that too raises interesting questions about *yuchsin* that will be explored in Kiddushin — but as a dispute about whether we are simply concerned she might be pregnant. Rabbi Yehuda says yes; Rabbi Yose says no.

The apparent contradiction is this: in the *mishnah*, Rabbi Yehuda shows no concern that a *shvuyah* was raped, yet here he expresses concern that a *giyoret* — who may have been sexually active as a non-Jewish woman — might be pregnant. The *gemara* immediately deflects this challenge: *giyoret lumpda nafshah* — a convert and a captive woman are entirely different cases. A non-Jewish woman living freely may well be sexually active by choice; that is a reasonable assumption. A Jewish woman taken captive, on the other hand, is not presumed to have consented, and we are not simply going to assume she was raped by her captors. The cases are not parallel, and the challenge fails.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי שְׁבוּיָה אַשְּׁבוּיָהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: הַגִּיּוֹרֶת וְהַשְּׁבוּיָה וְהַשִּׁפְחָה שֶׁנִּפְדּוּ וְשֶׁנִּתְגַּיְּירוּ וְשֶׁנִּשְׁתַּחְרְרוּ יְתֵירוֹת עַל בְּנוֹת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — צְרִיכוֹת לְהַמְתִּין שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר לֵיאָרֵס וְלִינָּשֵׂא מִיָּד. אִשְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rav Pappa bar Shmuel **raised a contradiction from** one *halakha* with regard to **a captive to** another *halakha* with regard to **a captive, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The convert, or the captive** woman **or the** gentile **maidservant, who were redeemed, converted, or emancipated** when they were **more than three years and one day** old, **are required to wait three months** before marrying; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei permits** these women **to be betrothed and to be married immediately.** Apparently, Rabbi Yehuda is concerned that she engaged in sexual relations prior to her redemption, contradicting his opinion here. Rav Yosef **was silent,** unable to respond.

The *gemara* then attempts a stronger version of the challenge — *rami shvuyah a-shvuyah*, a direct contradiction between two rulings about captive women. The *baraita* states explicitly that a *giyoret*, *shvuyah*, and *shifchah* — a convert, a captive woman, and a non-Jewish slave woman — who were redeemed, converted, or freed after the age of three must wait three months before marrying. This is Rabbi Yehuda's ruling. Rabbi Yose permits immediate marriage. Here Rabbi Yehuda is treating a *shvuyah* as someone who may have been raped and may be pregnant — directly contradicting his position in the *mishnah* that she is presumed *bikdushatah*.

### Three-Month Wait

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵּי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ בְּהָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, שֶׁרָאוּהָ שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Later, Rav Yosef **said to him: Have you heard anything with regard to this** matter? Rav Pappa bar Shmuel **said to him: This** is what **Rav Sheshet said:** Rabbi Yehuda is referring to a captive **whom** witnesses **saw engage in intercourse.**

This challenge is a genuine one. Rav Papa presents it to Rav Yosef, and Rav Yosef is stumped — *ishtik*, he fell silent. He then turns to Rav Papa and asks whether Rav Papa has heard any resolution. Rav Papa responds with the answer of Rav Sheshet: the case in the *baraita* where Rabbi Yehuda requires waiting is specifically one where we know with certainty that the woman had sexual intercourse — *she-ra'uha she-niv'alah*. Under those circumstances, Rabbi Yehuda naturally requires the three-month wait to establish paternity. The *mishnah*, by contrast, speaks of a case where no such concrete knowledge exists, and there Rabbi Yehuda maintains that we presume she was not violated.

This is an admittedly constrained reading — a *dachuk* *ukimta* — but it is the *gemara*'s resolution. The immediate question, however, is: if that is what the *baraita* is discussing, why does it focus specifically on the *giyoret*, *shvuyah*, and *shifchah*? The answer is that Rabbi Yose's position still requires explanation, because even in a case where we know the woman has had intercourse, Rabbi Yose says no waiting is necessary. And that is what the *gemara* now turns to address.

## Birth Control Debate

### Moch & R. Yose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? אָמַר רַבָּה, קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אִשָּׁה מְזַנָּה מְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת בְּמוֹךְ, שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְעַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, what is the rationale** for the ruling of **Rabbi Yosei** that she may marry immediately? Shouldn't he be concerned lest she is pregnant? **Rabba said: Rabbi Yosei holds** that **a woman who engages in** promiscuous **sexual relations has relations with** a contraceptive **resorbent** at the entrance of her womb, so **that she will not become pregnant.**

Rabbah explains Rabbi Yose's position: *isha mezanah meshamashet be-moch she-lo tit'aber* — a sexually active woman will use a *moch*, a contraceptive device, in order not to become pregnant. Under this assumption, even if we know the woman has had intercourse, we are not concerned she is pregnant, because she would have taken precautions. There is a dispute between Rashi and Tosafot as to whether the *moch* was used before intercourse, to block the seed, or after, to absorb it. Rashi's view that it was used after will become significant shortly.

## Birth Control Debate (II)

### Converts Planning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא גִּיּוֹרֶת, כֵּיוָן דְּדַעְתַּהּ לְאִיגַּיְּירוּ מְנַטְּרָא נַפְשַׁהּ. שְׁבוּיָה נָמֵי, דְּלָא יָדְעָה הֵיכָא מַמְטוּ לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, a convert** uses the resorbent; **since it is her intention to convert, she protects herself** from pregnancy. **A captive too** uses the resorbent because **she does not know where they are taking her,** and she does not want to become pregnant.

The *gemara* proceeds to examine how Rabbi Yose's assumption plays out across each of the three cases. The case of the *giyoret* is the most straightforward: *keivan de-da'atah le-igguyerei mi-natra nafshah* — since she was planning to convert, she would have been careful not to become pregnant beforehand, because she would not want to enter her new Jewish life burdened with a pregnancy from her pre-conversion past. The *gemara* therefore accepts that Rabbi Yose can reasonably presume she used contraception.

### Captives Seeking Help

The case of the *shvuyah* is more striking. The *gemara* says: *de-lo yada heicha mamtu lah* — she does not know where she will be taken. Rashi explains this to mean that she would want the Jewish community to redeem her, and she understood that a pregnancy resulting from her captors would diminish their willingness to do so. She therefore would have taken steps to avoid becoming pregnant. This is a striking argument on several levels. It implies both that the woman had access to contraception while in captivity and that she was sufficiently calculating about her circumstances to use it strategically. One might naturally ask: using contraception implies some degree of agency over the sexual encounter, whereas the entire concern is that she was raped. The answer is that even in circumstances of coercion, if a woman anticipates the possibility of violation and has access to contraceptive means, she might still employ them — particularly if, as Rashi understands it, the *moch* is used after the act.

### Slaves Freed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁפְחָה נָמֵי, דְּשָׁמְעָה מִפִּי מָרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A maidservant** uses the resorbent **too, as she heard from her master** that he intends to free her, and she seeks to avoid confusion with regard to the lineage of her offspring.

For the *shifchah*, the reasoning runs similarly: *de-shama'at mi-pi marah* — if she heard from her master that he intended to free her, she would not have wanted to begin her free life pregnant by her master or another man in the household, and so she would have taken precautions. The *gemara* acknowledges that a slave who had no expectation of freedom might not have the same motivation to avoid pregnancy, but posits that when freedom was anticipated, the incentive to use contraception was present.

### Rape & Sudden Changes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל יוֹצְאָה בְּשֵׁן וָעַיִן, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **However,** with regard a maidservant who **emerges** from slavery **with** the extraction by her master of her **tooth or** her **eye, what is there to say?**

The *gemara* then presses the limits of this reasoning. What about *yotza be-shen va-ayin* — a slave woman who is freed suddenly because her master struck out her eye or tooth? That freedom was not anticipated and came entirely without warning. Under those circumstances, what can Rabbi Yose say? One might argue that Rabbi Yose's leniency is limited to cases where the change in status was foreseeable and the woman could have planned accordingly, but does not apply to sudden, unplanned changes — *mimela* — where no such planning was possible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא כֹּל מִמֵּילָא לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הֲרֵי אֲנוּסָה וּמְפוּתָּה, דְּמִמֵּילָא, וְתַנְיָא: אֲנוּסָה וּמְפוּתָּה צְרִיכוֹת לְהַמְתִּין שְׁלֹשָׁה חֳדָשִׁים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר לֵיאָרֵס וְלִינָּשֵׂא מִיָּד!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that with regard to **any** situation that occurs **on its own,** without advance knowledge, **Rabbi Yosei** concedes to Rabbi Yehuda and **did not say** that it is permitted for her to marry immediately, that cannot be so. There is the case of **a raped or seduced** woman, which happens **on its own** without advance knowledge, **and it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A raped woman and a seduced woman must wait three months** before marrying; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda; Rabbi Yosei permits** these women **to be betrothed and to be married immediately.**

The *gemara* tests this by asking about *anusa* — a raped woman — and *mefuta* — a seduced woman. Rape is quintessentially *mimela*: nothing could have been anticipated or planned. Yet a *baraita* explicitly states that a raped woman must wait three months — that is Rabbi Yehuda's position — and Rabbi Yose permits immediate marriage even there. So Rabbi Yose's position cannot be limited to cases of anticipated change; he maintains it even in cases of rape. The marginal note in the text observes that Rashi's text of the *baraita* mentions only *anusa* and not *mefuta*, since seduction, while perhaps sudden in one sense, does involve some degree of the woman's participation and is harder to characterize as purely *mimela*.

This explicit *baraita* is actually clarifying: what had been presented as a constrained *ukimta* — that the earlier *baraita* must have been discussing a case where we know she had intercourse — is now confirmed by this later *baraita*, which is explicit that even a raped woman is subject to Rabbi Yehuda's waiting requirement, and Rabbi Yose permits her to marry immediately. The *ukimta* was not a stretch; it simply reflects what the sources explicitly teach.

### Internal Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אִשָּׁה מְזַנָּה מִתְהַפֶּכֶת כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְעַבֵּר. וְאִידַּךְ? חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא לֹא נֶהֶפְכָה יָפֶה יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabba said:** The rationale for the ruling of Rabbi Yosei is not because the woman uses a contraceptive resorbent that she inserts before engaging in relations. Rather, **Rabbi Yosei holds: A woman** who **engages in** promiscuous **sexual relations turns over** at the conclusion of the sexual act so **that she will not become pregnant.** Therefore, even if she engaged in unplanned sexual relations, she can take steps afterward to prevent unwanted pregnancy. The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yehuda respond to this contention? The Gemara answers: **We are concerned lest** the semen remain in her womb because **she did not turn over properly,** and she will become pregnant.

With the *moch* before intercourse unavailable to a rape victim, and the *moch* after intercourse unavailable unless she has it ready immediately — as Rashi notes, a delay of hours would make it useless — how can Rabbi Yose exempt even a raped woman? Rabbah answers: *kasavar Rabbi Yose isha mezanah mithapechet kedei she-lo tit'aber* — a woman has internal means of preventing pregnancy even without any device, by repositioning her body after the act in a way that expels the semen. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, responds: *chashinan shema lo nithapcha yafeh yafeh* — we cannot rely on this as a sufficiently reliable method; she may not have done it effectively, and we therefore remain concerned that she is pregnant.

The upshot of this entire discussion is a coherent picture of the two positions. Rabbi Yose holds that even when intercourse is known to have occurred — whether consensually, through seduction, or by outright rape — we do not presume pregnancy, because women have means, physiological or otherwise, to prevent it. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: when intercourse is established, we are concerned about pregnancy and require the three-month wait. But where intercourse is not established — as in the case of the *shvuyah* in the *mishnah* — Rabbi Yehuda maintains that she is presumed *bikdushatah*, and for purposes of *knas* at least, she is not presumed to have been violated.

## Captive Women (II)

### Three Cases

With the foregoing analysis in view, the three cases of *giyoret*, *shvuyah*, and *shifchah* can be properly distinguished. The *giyoret*'s sexual activity as a non-Jewish woman is presumed — she was free and potentially active — and so even Rabbi Yehuda's concern about the *shvuyah* and the *giyoret* are driven by different reasoning. The *shvuyah* in the *mishnah* is assumed not to have been violated in the absence of positive evidence; the *shvuyah* in the *baraita* requiring three months is a case where violation is confirmed. The *shifchah* similarly is treated leniently by Rabbi Yose based on the assumption of birth control use. These distinctions resolve what had appeared to be an internal contradiction within Rabbi Yehuda's rulings and clarify the scope of Rabbi Yose's principled leniency.

## Kim Lei Principle

### Death Over Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר וְלֹא יִהְיֶה אָסוֹן עָנוֹשׁ יֵעָנֵשׁ וְכוּ׳. וְהָא מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא: ״כְּדֵי רִשְׁעָתוֹ״, מִשּׁוּם רִשְׁעָה אַחַת אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁתֵּי רִשְׁעָיוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that one liable to receive the death penalty is exempt from payment, **as it is stated: "And yet no harm follow, he shall be punished, etc."** (Exodus 21:22). The Gemara asks: **And is this** principle **derived from here?** Actually, **it is derived from there:** "And to be beaten before his face **according to the measure of his iniquity"** (Deuteronomy 25:2). From the term: His iniquity, it is inferred: **You can hold** one who performs one action **liable for one iniquity,** i.e., punishment for violating one prohibition, **but you do not hold him liable for two iniquities,** i.e., punishments for violating two prohibitions.

The *gemara* now turns to revisit the principle of *kim lei bi-draba minei* — that the greater penalty subsumes the lesser — by examining the scriptural sources that underpin it. The *mishnah* had cited the verse *velo yihyeh ason anosh ye'anesh*: if a man strikes a pregnant woman and there is no fatal outcome, he pays a fine; but if she dies, the monetary obligation falls away because a capital offense is involved. This is the *kim lei* principle in its most familiar form: a capital offense overrides a monetary obligation. The *gemara* now asks whether that verse is the actual source of the rule.

The *gemara* answers that the primary source is a different verse: *kidei rishato* — "according to his wickedness" — from the passage in Devarim governing *malkos*. The Rabbis derive from this verse that a person is held liable for only one form of *rish'ut*, one category of wickedness, at a time: you may not impose both *malkos* and monetary payment simultaneously. The verse from *ason* therefore teaches a separate lesson, and both are needed.

### Multiple Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא בְּמִיתָה וּמָמוֹן, וַחֲדָא בְּמַלְקוֹת וּמָמוֹן. וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מִיתָה וּמָמוֹן, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא אִיבּוּד נְשָׁמָה, אֲבָל מַלְקוֹת וּמָמוֹן, דְּלֵיכָּא אִיבּוּד נְשָׁמָה, אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **One** of these derivations, from the verse "And yet no harm follow" is stated **with regard to** one who performed an action for which he is liable to receive the **death** penalty **and** to pay **money,** and the liability to be executed exempts him from payment. **And one** of these derivations, from the verse "According to the measure of his iniquity," is stated **with regard to** one who performed an action for which he is liable to receive **lashes and** to pay **money,** and he receives only one punishment. The Gemara elaborates: **And** both derivations **are necessary, as if** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **death and money,** one would assert that the exemption from payment is **due to** the fact **that there is loss of life,** the ultimate punishment, leaving no room for additional punishment; **however,** in the case of **lashes and money, where there is no loss of life, say no,** there is no exemption and he is flogged and pays.

The *gemara* asks why both verses are necessary. Each teaches a distinct case: one establishes that the death penalty overrides monetary liability, and the other establishes that *malkos* overrides monetary liability. You cannot derive one from the other.

If only the verse about the death penalty were available, one might reason: the death penalty overrides money because we are taking the man's life — *ibud neshama* — and in the face of that severity, monetary claims become irrelevant. But in the case of *malkos*, where the man is not being executed, perhaps both can coexist: exact the lashes and collect the money. So the *kidei rishato* verse is needed to tell us that *malkos* too overrides monetary liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מַלְקוֹת וּמָמוֹן, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חֲמִיר אִיסּוּרֵיהּ. אֲבָל מִיתָה וּמָמוֹן, דַּחֲמִיר אִיסּוּרֵיהּ, אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **lashes and money,** one would assert that the exemption from payment is **due to** the fact **that** the **prohibition** that he violated **is not severe,** as it is punishable by lashes, and for violating a prohibition that is not severe one does not receive two punishments. **However,** with regard to **death and money, where** the **prohibition** that he violated **is severe, say no,** he is not exempt from receiving two punishments. Therefore, it was **necessary** for the Torah to teach both derivations.

Conversely, if only *kidei rishato* were available, one might reason differently: *malkos* overrides money because the sin involved was not so grave — it did not rise to the level of a capital offense — and the Torah therefore allows it to be discharged by one form of punishment. But for a sin so grave that it carries the death penalty, the greater weight of the offense might seem to argue *against* exempting the offender from additional obligations. So the *ason* verse is needed to confirm that the death penalty too overrides monetary liability.

### Corporal Punishments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר לוֹקֶה וּמְשַׁלֵּם, תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? חֲדָא בְּמִיתָה וּמָמוֹן,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Meir, who said** that one **is flogged and pays** in cases where he violated a prohibition punishable by both, **why do I** require **two** derivations teaching that one does not receive the death penalty and pay? The Gemara answers: **One** derivation is **with regard to death and money,** exempting one liable to be executed from payment,

According to Rabbi Meir, who holds *lokim u-meshalmim* — that both *malkos* and monetary payment may be imposed simultaneously — the *kidei rishato* verse cannot be used for the *malkos* and money case. He therefore must read it differently: both verses concern the death penalty, but they address distinct sub-questions. One tells us that the death penalty overrides monetary liability, and the other tells us that a death sentence overrides *malkos* — that if a person is subject to both execution and lashes, the execution alone is carried out.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲדָא בְּמִיתָה וּמַלְקוֹת. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מִיתָה וּמָמוֹן, מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲדָא בְּגוּפֵיהּ וַחֲדָא בְּמָמוֹנֵיהּ לָא עָבְדִינַן. אֲבָל בְּמִיתָה וּמַלְקוֹת, דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּגוּפֵיהּ, אֵימָא מִיתָה אֲרִיכְתָּא הִיא, וְנַעְבֵּיד בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **and one** derivation is **with regard to death and lashes,** exempting one liable to be executed from lashes. The Gemara comments: **And** both verses **are necessary, as if** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **death and monetary** payment, one would assert that the exemption from payment is **due to** the fact **that we do not administer one** punishment **to his body and one to his money. However, with regard to death and lashes, that both this,** death, **and that,** lashes, are administered **to his body, say it is an extended death** penalty **and let us administer** lashes and then the death penalty **to him** so that his death will ensue from affliction.

The *gemara* again asks why both verses are needed. The upshot is that each handles a different configuration of penalties: the death/money verse handles one category (corporal versus financial punishment), while the death/lashes verse handles another (corporal punishment when both are bodily). The logic of exemption differs in each case, so both verses are required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מִיתָה וּמַלְקוֹת, דְּתַרְתֵּי בְּגוּפֵיהּ לָא עָבְדִינַן. אֲבָל מִיתָה וּמָמוֹן, דַּחֲדָא בְּגוּפֵיהּ וַחֲדָא בְּמָמוֹנֵיהּ, אֵימָא נַעֲבֵיד בֵּיהּ. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **death and lashes,** one would assert that the exemption from lashes is **due to** the fact **that we do not administer two** punishments **to his body. However,** with regard to **death and money, where one** is administered **to his body and one** is administered **to his money, say: Let us administer** both **to him.** Therefore, both verses are **necessary,** to teach that one receives only one punishment in both cases.

### Murder & Ransom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלֹא תִקְחוּ כוֹפֶר לְנֶפֶשׁ רוֹצֵחַ״ לְמָה לִי? דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: לָא תִּשְׁקוֹל מָמוֹנָא מִינֵּיהּ וְתִפְטְרֵיהּ מִקְּטָלָא. ״לֹא תִקְחוּ כוֹפֶר לָנוּס אֶל עִיר מִקְלָטוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: לָא תִּשְׁקוֹל מָמוֹנָא מִינֵּיהּ וְתִפְטְרֵיהּ מִן גָּלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the verse **"And you shall take no ransom for the life of a murderer,** who is guilty of death" (Numbers 35:31), which means that one does not take payment from a person sentenced to death, **why do I** require this verse, if that principle was already derived from another verse? The Gemara explains **that the Merciful One says: Do not take money from him and exempt him from** the **death** penalty. Similarly, the following verse: "And **you shall take no ransom for him that is fled to his city of refuge"** (Numbers 35:32), **why do I** require this verse? The Gemara explains **that the Merciful One says: Do not take money from him and exempt him from exile.**

The *gemara* now addresses a different category of verse: *velo tikchu kofer le-nefesh rotze'ach asher hu rasha lamus ki mos yumas* — do not take a *kofer*, a ransom, for a murderer deserving of death; he must be put to death. At first glance this might seem to be another iteration of the *kim lei* principle — do not impose both death and a monetary payment. But the *gemara* quickly clarifies: what this verse is actually prohibiting is something entirely different. It is prohibiting the use of money as a means of *escape* from the death penalty — allowing a wealthy murderer to buy his way out of execution. In ancient society, this was not an abstraction; powerful individuals could negotiate their way out of capital punishment through payment. The Torah categorically forbids it: *lo tishkol mamon minei ve-tiftarei mi-katol* — do not collect money from him and thereby exempt him from death.

This raises the broader question of what a *kofer* actually represents. There is one place in the Torah where *kofer* is recognized as a legitimate response to a death — the case of the *shor haniskal*, the goring ox. There, the verse says *gam be'alav yumat* — the owner too shall die — and the Torah then provides that a *kofer* may be imposed instead. The *gemara* in Bava Kamma debates whether the *kofer* in that case represents the value of the owner's life or the value of the victim's life — *demei mazik* or *demei nizak* — assessed by the slave-market value of whichever party's life is being measured. In any case, this is the Torah's lone concession to the concept of monetary redemption for a life. The verse about *rotze'ach* is telling us that when a human being deliberately or negligently causes another person's death, that concession does not apply.

### Manslaughter Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְרֵי קְרָאֵי לְמָה לִי! חַד בְּשׁוֹגֵג וְחַד בְּמֵזִיד. וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן מֵזִיד, מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲמִיר אִיסּוּרֵיהּ. אֲבָל שׁוֹגֵג, דְּלָא חֲמִיר אִיסּוּרֵיהּ — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן שׁוֹגֵג, מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיכָּא אִיבּוּד נְשָׁמָה. אֲבָל מֵזִיד, דְּאִיכָּא אִיבּוּד נְשָׁמָה — אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** require **two verses** to teach the same principle? The Gemara explains: **One** verse refers **to** one who killed **unwittingly, and one** verse refers **to** one who killed **intentionally.** The Gemara comments: **And** both verses **are necessary, as if** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **an intentional** murderer, one would assert that payment is not accepted **due to** the fact **that** the **prohibition** that **he** violated **is severe. However,** with regard to **an unwitting** killer, **where** the **prohibition is not severe, say no,** he may pay in lieu of exile. **And if** the Torah **taught us** this *halakha* only with regard to **an unwitting** killer, one would assert that payment is not accepted **due to** the fact **that there is no loss of life,** as the killer is not executed, and therefore, there is no reason to allow payment in lieu of exile. **However,** with regard to **an intentional** killer, **where there is loss of life,** as he will be executed, **say no,** he may pay in lieu of execution. Therefore, both verses are **necessary.**

The *gemara* then turns to a second verse: *velo tikchu kofer lanus el ir miklato* — do not take a ransom for someone who is obligated to flee to the *ir miklat*, the city of refuge. This addresses the case of *shogeg* — unintentional manslaughter — where the killer must go into exile. One might think that since the killing was unintentional, a monetary payment to the victim's family could serve as an adequate substitute for exile. The verse prohibits this: *lo tishkol mamon minei ve-tiftarei mi-galus* — do not collect money from him and thereby exempt him from exile.

Why are both verses needed? If only the verse about intentional murder existed, one might reason: of course you cannot buy your way out of execution — the crime was monstrous and intentional, and justice demands blood for blood. But for unintentional manslaughter — *shogeg*, where the killer did not intend harm — the severity is lower, and a monetary settlement with the family might be seen as adequate. So the second verse is needed to preclude that option. Conversely, if only the verse about *shogeg* existed, one might reason: that verse precludes ransom for exile only because exile itself is not the destruction of life — *leika ibud neshama* — and we are not going to let money substitute for that particular obligation. But for intentional murder, where we must take a life — *de-ika ibud neshama* — perhaps the powerful human impulse to preserve life, even a murderer's, should lead us to allow a monetary settlement. So the verse about intentional murder is equally necessary.

### Egla Arufa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלָאָרֶץ לֹא יְכוּפַּר לַדָּם אֲשֶׁר שׁוּפַּךְ בָּהּ כִּי אִם בְּדַם שׁוֹפְכוֹ״ לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the following verse: **"And no expiation can be made for the land for the blood that is shed therein, but by the blood of him that shed it"** (Numbers 35:33), which also indicates that he cannot be exempted by money, **why do I** require another verse to teach that one cannot absolve himself from the death penalty by means of payment?

The *gemara* introduces another verse: *ule-aretz lo yechupar la-dam asher shupach bah ki im be-dam shofcho* — the land shall not be atoned for the blood spilled upon it except by the blood of the one who spilled it. This verse appears in the context of the *egla arufa*, the heifer whose neck is broken when a corpse is found between cities and the murderer is unknown. The *gemara* asks: what legal point is this verse adding? We already know that a murderer cannot pay a ransom. What does this *egla arufa* context contribute?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם נִתְעָרְפָה עֲגָלָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצָא הַהוֹרֵג, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלָאָרֶץ לֹא יְכוּפַּר לַדָּם אֲשֶׁר שׁוּפַּךְ בָּהּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **It is necessary to** teach **that which is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the matter of the calf that is beheaded. If a corpse whose murderer is unknown is found between two towns, the elders of the town nearest to the corpse bring a heifer and behead it in a riverbed, after which they pray for atonement for this murder. The *baraita* states: **From where** is it derived **that if the calf was beheaded and the murderer was found thereafter,** it is derived **that one does not exempt him** from punishment? It is **as it is stated: "And no expiation can be made for the land for the blood that is shed therein,** but by the blood of him that shed it" (Numbers 35:32), from which it is inferred: And not by the blood of the calf.

The answer comes from a *baraita*: the verse teaches that if the community performed the *egla arufa* ritual and subsequently the murderer was found, he is not exempt from execution. One might have thought that the ritual accomplished an atonement and effectively closed the case. The verse is telling us otherwise: the land is not atoned until the murderer's own blood is shed. The *egla arufa* addresses the community's moral responsibility in the face of an unsolved crime but does not constitute a substitute for actual justice when the perpetrator is identified.

### Execution Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאַתָּה תְּבַעֵר הַדָּם הַנָּקִי מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לְמוּמָתִים בְּסַיִיף שֶׁהוּא מִן הַצַּוָּאר — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאַתָּה תְּבַעֵר הַדָּם הַנָּקִי מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״ — הוּקְשׁוּ כׇּל שׁוֹפְכֵי דָמִים לְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, מַה לְהַלָּן מִן הַצַּוָּאר — אַף שׁוֹפְכֵי דָמִים מִן הַצַּוָּאר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What about the following verse, from the conclusion of the chapter of the heifer: The verse **"And so shall you put away the innocent blood from your midst"** (Deuteronomy 21:9) appears to be teaching the very same *halakha*, that a murderer must be executed. **Why do I** need it? The Gemara answers that **it is necessary to** teach **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** with regard to **those executed by sword,** e.g., murderers, their execution **is** administered **from the neck,** and nowhere else? **The verse states: "And so shall you put away the innocent blood from your midst"** (Deuteronomy 21:9), **likening all spillers of blood to the beheaded calf** brought for an unresolved murder. **Just as there,** the calf is beheaded **from the neck, so too, murderers** are beheaded **from the neck.**

An additional verse follows: *ve-ata tiva'er ha-dam ha-naki mi-kirbecha* — and you shall purge the innocent blood from your midst. This appears at the end of the *egla arufa* passage, and the *gemara* asks what it adds beyond what we already know. The answer, derived from a *baraita*, is that this verse creates an analogy between the *egla arufa* and the execution of murderers: just as the heifer's neck is broken at the throat, so too those executed by the sword are killed at the throat — *min ha-tzavar*. The verse is connecting all cases of bloodshed to the *egla arufa*, teaching that the method of execution for murder should parallel the method used for the heifer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה לְהַלָּן בְּקוֹפִיץ וּמִמּוּל עוֹרֶף, אַף כָּאן בְּקוֹפִיץ וּמִמּוּל עוֹרֶף? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ״, בְּרוֹר לוֹ מִיתָה יָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, just as there,** in the case of the beheaded calf, it is beheaded **with a cleaver [*kofitz*] and at the nape of the neck, here too** the court executes murderers **with a cleaver and at the nape of the neck. Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **the verse says: "And you shall love your neighbor as yourself"** (Leviticus 19:18), from which it is derived: **Choose for him an agreeable death.** It is prohibited to abuse a guilty person while executing him, and chopping off his head with a cleaver is an unseemly death. The murderer is beheaded from the neck, not with a cleaver, and not by the other methods employed in beheading the calf.

One might press further: should the analogy be complete? The heifer is killed with a cleaver — *kufitz* — and at the nape of the neck — *mimul oref* — the back of the neck. Should murderers be executed the same way? Rav Nachman, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, answers by invoking the verse *ve-ahavta le-rei'acha kamocha* — love your neighbor as yourself. Even a condemned man remains your *rei'ach*, your fellow, and the obligation to choose a *mitah yafah*, a humane death, applies. Cutting the throat with a sword is considered more humane than a blow to the nape with a hatchet, and the concern for the dignity of the condemned shapes the specific method of execution. This reflects a broader principle in the laws of capital punishment in Sanhedrin, where the precise form of each execution is calibrated with sensitivity to the humanity of the condemned.

### Cherem & Erech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כׇּל חֵרֶם אֲשֶׁר יׇחֳרַם מִן הָאָדָם לֹא יִפָּדֶה״ לְמָה לִי? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: מִנַּיִן לַיּוֹצֵא לֵיהָרֵג, וְאָמַר אֶחָד ״עֶרְכּוֹ עָלַי״, מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא אָמַר כְּלוּם —
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the following verse: **"Anything dedicated [*ḥerem*], that may be dedicated of men, shall not be redeemed;** he shall surely be put to death" (Leviticus 27:29), which is interpreted here as: Anyone sentenced to be executed shall not be redeemed; this appears to teach the same *halakha* as above, so **why do I need** it? The Gemara explains: **It is necessary** to teach **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **with regard to** one **taken to be executed, and one** person **said: His valuation is upon me** to donate to the Temple, **that he did not say anything** and his vow is not binding?

The *gemara* turns to yet another verse: *kol cherem asher yucharam min ha-adam lo yipadeh mos yumat* — any person placed under the ban shall not be redeemed; he shall be put to death. The *gemara* understands this not as a verse about wartime *cherem* of enemy populations, but as a verse about any individual deserving of a capital sentence. We already know he cannot pay a monetary ransom — *lo tishkol mamon* — so what is this verse adding?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל חֵרֶם אֲשֶׁר יׇחֳרַם מִן הָאָדָם לֹא יִפָּדֶה״. יָכוֹל אַף קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מִן הָאָדָם״ — וְלֹא כׇּל הָאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** It is derived **as it is stated: "Anything dedicated [*ḥerem*], that may be dedicated of men [*yoḥoram*], shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:29). This verse is taken to mean that anything dedicated, through which a man who is condemned [*yoḥoram*] is valuated, shall not be redeemed, as the person in question is already considered dead. One **might** think that **even before his verdict** is issued this should be **so,** and that one who said: The valuation of so-and-so on trial for murder is upon me, said nothing of consequence. Therefore, **the verse states:** "That may be dedicated **of men,"** implying "of men," **but not entire men.** If it is valuation of an entire man, one not yet sentenced to death, it is binding. If it is valuation of a partial man, one sentenced to death, it is not binding.

The *baraita* teaches: if a person is being taken out for execution and someone declares *erchi alai* — I accept upon myself to pay his *erech*, his fixed Torahic valuation — is the pledge valid? The *erech* system in the Torah sets fixed monetary values based on age and sex, independent of market value. One might think that since the *erech* is a fixed number unrelated to market conditions, it still applies even to a condemned man — one simply looks him up on the table and pays. The verse *lo yipadeh* teaches that no such pledge takes effect. The condemned man, at the moment of being led to execution, is regarded as standing outside the normal framework of human transactions; he has no *erech* that can be pledged.

The *gemara* further specifies that this applies from the moment his sentence is finalized — *mi-she-nigmar dino* — but not before. The qualifier *min ha-adam* — "from among mankind" — excludes everyone: meaning only some people, those whose sentence is sealed, fall under this rule. Before judgment is finalized, the pledge would take effect normally.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן עֲקַבְיָא, דְּאָמַר: נֶעֱרָךְ — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדָּמָיו קְצוּבִין, הַאי ״כׇּל חֵרֶם״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akavya,** who **said** that even a person taken to his execution **is valuated,** and the vow is binding, **because** the **money** of **his** valuation **is fixed.** The sum of the valuation established in the Torah is not based on the worth of the individual; rather, there is a fixed sum determined by age and gender. Therefore, one may be valuated as long as he is alive. According to that opinion, the question remains with regard to **this** verse: **"Anything dedicated," what does** he derive from **it?**

Rabbi Chananya ben Akavia disagrees and holds that the condemned man does have an *erech* that can be pledged, reasoning that *damav ketzuvim* — his value is fixed by Torah schedule and does not depend on circumstances. For him, the verse of *kol cherem* must teach something else: it comes to address the position of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who observed that we find cases where those liable to death at the hands of Heaven can achieve atonement through monetary payment — namely, the *shor haniskal*, where the Torah itself authorizes a *kofer* in place of the owner's death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ לְמוּמָתִים בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין מָמוֹן וּמִתְכַּפֵּר לָהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם כּוֹפֶר יוּשַׁת עָלָיו״. יָכוֹל אַף בִּידֵי אָדָם כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חֵרֶם מִן הָאָדָם לֹא יִפָּדֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He requires it** to teach **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: Because we found with regard to those executed at the hand of Heaven,** and not through court-administered execution, **that they give money and their** sins **are atoned, as it is stated:** "The ox shall be stoned, and its owner shall also be put to death. **If there be laid upon him a ransom** then he shall give for the redemption of his life whatsoever is laid upon him" (Exodus 21:29–30). One whose ox kills a person is essentially liable to receive the death penalty at the hand of Heaven, and pays money instead. You **might** think that **even** with regard to those liable to receive the death penalty **at the hands of man** it is **so,** and one can pay in lieu of execution. Therefore, **the verse states: "Dedicated of men shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:29). One who is executed by man cannot be redeemed with money.

One might therefore have thought that the same option exists for human court-imposed capital penalties. *Yachol af bidei adam kein?* The verse *kol cherem min ha-adam lo yipadeh* forecloses this: any person liable to a humanly imposed death sentence cannot be redeemed monetarily. The verse then addresses the full spectrum of capital offenses.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא מִיתוֹת חֲמוּרוֹת שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה שִׁגְגָתָן לְכַפָּרָה, מִיתוֹת קַלּוֹת שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה שִׁגְגָתָן לְכַפָּרָה, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל חֵרֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And I have** derived this restriction **only** with regard to prohibitions punishable by **severe** penalties of **death,** e.g., striking one's father, **for which no atonement is designated** in the Torah **for their unwitting** violation. However, with regard to prohibitions punishable by **less severe** penalties of **death,** e.g., performing labor on Shabbat, **for which atonement,** a sin-offering, **is designated** in the Torah **for their unwitting** violation, **from where** is it derived that there is no payment in lieu of execution? **The verse states: "Anything dedicated,"** to include all prohibitions punishable by court-administered execution.

The *baraita* distinguishes between *misos chamuros* — weightier capital offenses for which even an unintentional violation (*shogeg*) carries no path to atonement through a *korban* (because the Torah never attaches *kares* to them, and therefore no *chatas* is brought for their inadvertent commission) — and *misos kalos*, lighter capital offenses for which inadvertent violation does carry *kares* and therefore obligates a *korban*. From the word *kol* in *kol cherem* — "every" person under the ban — we learn that even for the less weighty capital offenses, no monetary ransom is accepted.

### No Ransom Ever

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא מִמֵּילָא מִ״לֹּא תִּקְחוּ כּוֹפֶר״ שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ — לָא תִּשְׁקוֹל מָמוֹנָא מִינֵּיהּ וְתִיפְטְרֵיהּ? ״כׇּל חֵרֶם״ לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And didn't you incidentally learn** the following conclusion **from** the verse **"And you shall take no ransom** for the life of a murderer, who is guilty of death" (Numbers 35:31): **Do not take money from him and exempt him** from death? **Why,** then, **do I** require the phrase: **Any *ḥerem*? Rami bar Ḥama said:** It is **necessary,** as **it might enter your mind to say**

The *gemara* asks whether this was not already known from the verse prohibiting *kofer* for the *rotze'ach*. The answer given by Rami bar Chama (continued in the following daf) is that you need all three verses together. The verse about *rotze'ach* might have been understood narrowly — only for intentional murder committed in a manner whose inadvertent counterpart carries no atonement, i.e., a killing done with an upward motion, where if done inadvertently the killer would not go into exile. But what of a killing done with a downward motion, where the inadvertent killer *does* go into exile? That case has a *lighter* quality in one sense — it is more amenable to atonement — and one might have thought monetary settlement could be accepted there. The verse *kol cherem* tells us otherwise: regardless of the relative weight of the capital offense, no ransom is ever accepted, and no one condemned to death by the court can purchase release through money.

This synthesis brings the discussion to a close: across all capital offenses, whether more severe or less, whether involving intentional or unintentional killing, whether addressed through the *rotze'ach* verse, the *galus* verse, or the *kol cherem* verse, the governing principle is consistent — no monetary substitute replaces the judgment of the court, and the principle of *kim lei bi-draba minei* is grounded in this uncompromising framework.
