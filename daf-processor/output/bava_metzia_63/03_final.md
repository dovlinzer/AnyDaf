# Bava Metzia 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ribbis Overview

### Neshech & Marbis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא תִשְׁמְרוּ עִם־עַמִּיתְךָ נֶשֶׁךְ כֶּסֶף נֶשֶׁךְ אוֹכֶל נֶשֶׁךְ כָּל דָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׁמְרוּ בְרִבִּית. וַיֹּאמֶר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶן בָּבָא: הַנּוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה חִטִּים בְּחִטִּים, בְּדָמִים בִּדְמֵיהֶם, בְּגָדוֹל בִּגָדוֹל, בְּקָטָן בִּקָטָן, בַּנִּשְׁמַר בַּנִּשְׁמַר וּבִשְׁלֹא נִשְׁמַר בִּשְׁלֹא נִשְׁמַר - לֹא יִשְׁמֹר. אַךְ אִם הִשְׁמִיר - הָיָה מַלְוֶה שֶׁל רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah employs two distinct terms for interest: *neshech* and *marbis*. One might have supposed that these terms refer respectively to monetary loans and food loans, but the distinction, rather, lies in perspective: *neshech* describes the impact on the borrower, from the root *nashach*, to bite — it bites into his resources. *Marbis* describes the impact on the lender, from *marbis*, to increase — it swells his gain unjustly.

We begin at the top of 63a, picking up from the final line of 62b. The subject is the definition and scope of *ribbis*. The Torah employs two distinct terms for interest: *neshech* and *marbis* (rendered in the verse as *ve-es kaspecha lo sitain lo be-neshech u-ve-marbis lo sitain ochlecha*). One might have supposed that these terms refer respectively to monetary loans and food loans, but the Gemara rejects that reading. Both terms apply equally to food and to money — indeed, to anything one might lend upon which interest could be charged. The distinction, rather, lies in perspective: *neshech* describes the impact on the borrower, from the root *nashach*, to bite — it bites into his resources. *Marbis* describes the impact on the lender, from *marbis*, to increase — it swells his gain unjustly, even though no coercion is involved and no theft in the ordinary sense has occurred.

### Separate Prohibitions

This point about the absence of coercion raises a deeper question that the Gemara addresses: why are *gezeila*, *ona'ah*, and *ribbis* maintained as separate prohibitions rather than subsumed under a single category? The question is pointed because one might think that *ribbis* is simply a form of *gezeila* — after all, the lender is extracting money from the borrower. Yet the Gemara makes clear that this cannot be right. The borrower agrees knowingly to pay the interest. Moreover, the prohibition extends to both parties: the borrower is forbidden to pay interest just as the lender is forbidden to receive it. This bilateral structure is unlike theft, which is a unilateral wrong committed against an unwilling victim. *Ribbis* is therefore a prohibition of a fundamentally different character.

### Returning Ribbis

The Gemara's treatment of the obligation to return *ribbis* once received further illuminates this distinction. It is settled that the children of the lender — the heirs — have no obligation to return interest collected by their father. There is even a question whether the lender himself is obligated to return it, since the borrower agreed to the payment. The ruling is that the lender does bear a personal obligation to return it, but the heirs do not — and that is uncontested. This stands in sharp contrast to stolen property, where the obligation to return passes to heirs. It is also noteworthy that the *Shulchan Aruch* places the laws of *ribbis* not in *Choshen Mishpat*, the section governing monetary law, but in *Yoreh Deah*, the section for general ritual prohibitions. This placement itself reflects the Gemara's conclusion that *ribbis*, while it concerns money, is not simply monetary wrongdoing — it is a prohibition of a distinct and different kind.

### Rabbinic Ribbis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ יֵשׁ דְּבָרִים שֶׁהֵם מוּתָּרִין וַאֲסוּרִין מִפְּנֵי הַעֲרָמַת רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA:** There are matters that are intrinsically permitted by Torah law, but are prohibited because they are artifices used to circumvent transgressing the prohibition of interest.

Having established the Torah-level framework, the Gemara turns its focus to rabbinic forms of *ribbis*, which will occupy most of our attention henceforth. These fall into two broad categories. The first consists of cases where no one intends to charge interest, but the structure of a transaction produces an outcome that resembles interest. The second consists of cases where interest is very much intended, but the parties attempt to conceal it through a subterfuge that gives the arrangement the appearance of a legitimate sale.

### iPhone Subterfuge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: הַלְוֵינִי מָנֶה! אָמַר לוֹ: מָנֶה אֵין לִי, חִטִּין בְּמָנֶה יֵשׁ לִי שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לָךְ. נָתַן לוֹ חִטִּין בְּמָנֶה, וְחָזַר וּלְקָחָן הֵימֶנּוּ בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע סֶלַע – מוּתָּר, וְאָסוּר לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן מִפְּנֵי הַעֲרָמַת רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If one said to another: **Lend me one hundred dinars,** and the other said to him: I do not have one hundred dinars but I have wheat worth one hundred dinars that I will give you, and he gave him wheat worth one hundred dinars, and later he went and acquired the wheat back from him for twenty-four *sela*, the equivalent of ninety-six dinars, this is **permitted** by Torah law, as it is considered a double transaction since it included first a loan of the equivalent of one hundred dinars, and then the purchase of the wheat at a discounted rate. **But it is prohibited to do so** by rabbinic law **because** this is **an artifice** allowing the circumvention of transgressing the prohibition **of interest.**

The second category yields a case that the Gemara identifies as forbidden rabbinically. Suppose Reuven wishes to borrow one hundred dollars from Shimon, and Shimon is unwilling to lend outright with interest. Shimon instead sells Reuven his iPhone for one hundred dollars — Reuven now owes Shimon one hundred dollars on account of the sale. Shimon then immediately offers to buy the iPhone back for ninety dollars, which Reuven accepts. The net effect is that Reuven has received ninety dollars in hand while owing Shimon one hundred. This is a loan of ninety with repayment of one hundred, structured as a double sale with a wink and a nod. The Gemara rules this arrangement forbidden rabbinically, because the underlying intent is transparently a loan with interest, dressed in the clothing of a sale.

## Muchar Ba'amana

### Grain Futures

The more common and analytically richer category is the reverse: parties genuinely intend a legitimate sale, but the end result structurally resembles a loan with interest. The paradigmatic case involves the grain market. When grain is first coming to market — not yet at the height of the season — a buyer approaches a grain merchant and pays him the going rate, say ten dollars per pound, committing the merchant to supply one hundred pounds over the course of the year for one thousand dollars. As the year proceeds and market prices rise to twelve dollars per pound, the merchant still delivers at ten dollars per pound — meaning the buyer ultimately receives goods worth more than what he paid. Could this look like a loan? The buyer handed over money; the merchant held it for months; what came back was worth more than what went out. That is the structural concern.

### Available Market

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין פּוֹסְקִין עַל הַפֵּירוֹת עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא הַשַּׁעַר, יָצָא הַשַּׁעַר – פּוֹסְקִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לָזֶה, יֵשׁ לָזֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: When purchasing produce to be collected by the buyer at a later stage, **one may not set** a price for **produce until the** market **rate is publicized.** If **the rate has been publicized, one may set** a price, despite the fact that the seller is not actually in possession of any such produce, as **even though this** seller **does not have** any of that produce, **that** one, i.e., another seller, **does have** some of that produce, which the first seller could purchase at the market rate. Evidently, once the market rate is publicized, the transaction is considered a purchase and not a loan with interest, even if the buyer does not receive the produce immediately.

The *chiddush* — and it is a significant one — is that this arrangement is nevertheless permitted, and it is permitted even when the merchant does not yet have the grain in his possession at the time the money changes hands. The key principle, derived from this Gemara and codified by the Rambam, is that the rule of *ein adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam* — that one cannot sell something not yet in existence or not yet in one's possession — does not apply when the commodity is readily available in the marketplace. If the merchant can simply walk out and buy the grain immediately, the absence of physical possession is no obstacle to a valid sale. The price has been fixed (*yatza ha-sha'ar*), the market is open, and the commodity is accessible. That is sufficient to constitute a genuine sale.

### Two Requirements

Two conditions therefore emerge as necessary for validating such a future-delivery sale. First, money must actually change hands. Second — or alternatively — the seller must currently be in possession of the merchandise. The reason that money changing hands suffices even without possession is that the seller can immediately take that money and purchase the grain he is obligated to deliver; his ability to do so at that moment makes it the equivalent of possessing the product. One might note a further complication: strictly speaking, *maos einan konos* — money does not effect a *kinyan* — and *meshicha*, taking physical possession, is required for a complete acquisition. The Gemara acknowledges this but treats the monetary transfer as at least rabbinically effective, generating a *mi she-para* obligation if either side backs out.

### Debt Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה נוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, וְהָלַךְ וְעָמַד עַל גּוֹרְנוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר: תֵּן לִי מְעוֹתַי, שֶׁאֲנִי רוֹצֶה לִיקַּח בָּהֶם חִטִּים. אָמַר לוֹ: חִטִּים יֵשׁ לִי שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ, צֵא וַעֲשֵׂה עָלַי כְּשַׁעַר שֶׁל עַכְשָׁיו, וַאֲנִי אַעֲלֶה לְךָ כׇּל שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ – אָסוּר, דְּלָאו כְּאִיסָּרוֹ הַבָּא לְיָדוֹ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **If** a person **was owed one hundred dinars by another, and he went and stood by** the other's **granary and said** to him: **Give me my money, as I wish to buy wheat with it.** And the debtor **said to him: I have wheat** in my granary **that I will give you; go and calculate for me** the amount of wheat to which you are entitled **by the current** market **rate, and I will give you** this amount of wheat over the span of **a full twelve months,** even if the price rises in the interim. Such a practice is **forbidden** because of interest, **as** this is **not similar to** a case where **his *issar* has come into his possession.** Since the lender did not give the borrower even a single *issar* as payment for the wheat, this is not a sale, but a loan with interest.

The harder case — and the one that drives the Gemara's central inquiry — is where no money changes hands at all. Instead, the buyer cancels a pre-existing debt owed by the merchant and uses that forgiven debt as the purchase price for future grain delivery. Here the transaction is far more loan-like: nothing tangible is transferred, one party is simply allowed to retain an obligation he already had, and in exchange he agrees to deliver goods later at a price that may, by delivery time, be below market. The Gemara's initial position is that this cannot be permitted under any circumstances. But the discussion now shifts toward a more nuanced position, which will be developed through the *Tosefta* of Rabbi Oshaya.

## Rabbi Oshaya

### Tosefta Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: כִּי שָׁכֵיבְנָא רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא נְפַק לְווֹתִי, דִּמְתָרַצְנָא מַתְנִיתִין כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** another explanation. He prefaced it by commenting: **When I die, Rabbi Oshaya will come out** from the Garden of Eden **to greet me, as I resolve the mishna in accordance with his** opinion, expressed in the *baraitot* he edited.

Rava opens with a striking declaration: *ki shachivna, Rabbi Oshaya nafik l'afai* — when I die, Rabbi Oshaya will come out to greet me. Rabbi Oshaya was one of the editors of the *Tosefta*, and Rava says he earns this reception because he consistently uses Rabbi Oshaya's *Tosefta* to explain difficult *mishnayos*. This is not an isolated claim; Rava says it on multiple occasions. He turns now to deploy exactly this method with respect to the difficult *mishna* before us.

### Debt Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה נוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, וְהָלַךְ וְעָמַד עַל גּוֹרְנוֹ וְאָמַר: תֵּן לִי מְעוֹתַי, שֶׁאֲנִי רוֹצֶה לִיקַּח בָּהֶן חִטִּין. וְאָמַר לוֹ: חִטִּין יֵשׁ לִי שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ, צֵא וַעֲשָׂאֵן עָלַי כְּשַׁעַר שֶׁל עַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **As Rabbi Oshaya teaches** in a *baraita*: One **was owed one hundred dinars by another, and he went and stood by** the other's **granary and said: Give me my money, as I wish to buy wheat with it, and** the other **said to him: I have wheat** in my granary **that I will give you; go and calculate for me** the amount to which you are entitled **at the current** market **rate.**

The *Tosefta* of Rabbi Oshaya presents the following scenario. Reuven is owed one hundred dollars by Shimon (*ha-nosheh b'chaveiro maneh*). Reuven goes to Shimon's threshing floor at the time when grain is first coming to market and demands his money back: *ten li me'osai she-ani rotzeh likach bahen chittin* — give me my hundred dollars, for I want to use it now to buy wheat at the season's low price.

### Debt to Wheat

Shimon responds that he does not have the hundred dollars in hand, but he does have wheat. He proposes: instead of paying you back the hundred dollars, let me sell you the wheat at the current market price and owe you those ten bushels over the course of the year — *tze ve-aseh li ke-sha'ar shel akhshav*. Go ahead and figure it at today's rate. Reuven agrees. No money changes hands; Reuven simply allows Shimon to keep the debt, now converted into an obligation to deliver wheat. The *Tosefta* will address whether this is permitted.

### Wheat to Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ זְמַן חִטִּין לִמְכּוֹר, אָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לִי חִטִּין, שֶׁאֲנִי רוֹצֶה לְמוֹכְרָן וְלִיקַּח בָּהֶן יַיִן. אָמַר לוֹ: יֵשׁ לִי יַיִן שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ, צֵא וַעֲשָׂאֵן עָלַי כְּשַׁעַר שֶׁל עַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** When **the time to sell wheat arrived,** the lender **said to him: Give me wheat, as I want to sell it and acquire wine with** the money received for **it.** The borrower **said to him: I have wine that I will give you; go and calculate for me** the amount of wine to which you are entitled **at the current** market **rate.**

Time passes. The wheat Shimon owes has now appreciated — it was contracted at ten dollars per pound and is now worth twelve dollars per pound. Reuven comes to Shimon and says: *ten li chittin she-ani rotzeh limkor ve-likach bahen yayin* — give me the wheat I bought from you; I want to sell it now and use the proceeds to buy wine, which is currently at the season's low price. Shimon again does not have the wheat to deliver, but he does have wine. He proposes to Reuven: the wheat you are owed is worth one hundred twenty dollars at today's prices; I will instead give you one hundred twenty dollars' worth of wine, priced at today's low rate — *ke-sha'ar shel akhshav*. Reuven agrees, and the transaction is extended further.

### Wine to Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִגִּיעַ זְמַן יַיִן לִמְכּוֹר, וְאָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לִי יֵינִי שֶׁאֲנִי רוֹצֶה לְמוֹכְרוֹ וְלִיקַּח בּוֹ שֶׁמֶן. אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמֶן יֵשׁ לִי שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ, צֵא וַעֲשֵׂהוּ עָלַי כְּשַׁעַר שֶׁל עַכְשָׁיו. כּוּלָּם, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ – מוּתָּר. אֵין לוֹ – אָסוּר. וּמַאי ״לָקַח״ – לָקַח בְּהַלְוָאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Then **the time to sell wine arrived, and** the lender **said to** the borrower: **Give me my wine, as I want to sell it and acquire oil with** the money received for **it.** The borrower **said to him: I have oil that I will give you; go and calculate for me** the amount of oil to which you are entitled **at the current** market **rate.** With regard to **all** such cases, **if** the borrower **has** wine and oil, the transaction is **permitted,** as this is a proper sale. But if **he does not have** these items it is **forbidden.** Since he cannot give him the merchandise at the current moment, if its value appreciates in the interim he is considered to have paid an additional sum for the delay of the repayment of the debt. Rava concludes his explanation: **And what is** the meaning in the mishna of: **Acquired?** It means that **he acquired** it **as** payment for **his loan.**

The same pattern recurs. When the time comes to take delivery of the wine, it too has appreciated. Reuven comes to collect and says: *ten li yayin she-ani rotzeh le-mokhran ve-likach bahen shemen* — give me the wine; I wish to sell it and invest the proceeds in oil, which is now at its seasonal low. Once more, Shimon lacks the wine to deliver, but he has oil. The wine Reuven is owed is now worth one hundred forty dollars; Shimon offers to owe Reuven that amount in oil at today's prices — *ke-sha'ar shel akhshav*. The chain has now run from a one-hundred-dollar debt, through wheat, through wine, to an obligation of one hundred forty dollars in oil — a progression that, as the Gemara observes with a touch of wry humor, has the character of *chad gadya*.

### Possession Rule

The *Tosefta* then states the governing rule. In all of these transactions, if Shimon actually had the product in his possession at the time he agreed to provide it, the arrangement is permitted. If he did not have the product, it is forbidden. This is the key ruling of Rabbi Oshaya, and it diverges from what had been presented in an earlier *beraita*. The earlier *beraita* had held — on the authority of Rabbi Hiyya — that purchasing on the basis of a forgiven debt is not permissible even if the seller has the product in possession; money must actually change hands. Rabbi Oshaya's *Tosefta* takes a more lenient position: if the seller has the product in possession, that suffices to render it a genuine sale even without monetary transfer.

### Hiyya vs. Oshaya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּמַעֲמִידִין מִלְוֶה עַל גַּבֵּי פֵּירוֹת, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן דְּלָא כְּאִיסָּרוֹ הַבָּא לְיָדוֹ הוּא. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הוּא דְּיֵשׁ לוֹ. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אִיתַהּ לִדְרַבִּי יַנַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Conclude from** this *baraita* **of Rabbi Oshaya three** *halakhot*: **Conclude from it that one may establish** repayment of **a loan upon produce,** meaning that a borrower can promise to pay the lender in produce over the course of a year, based on the market rate at the beginning of the year, **and we do not say** that this is **like** a case where **his *issar* has not** already **come into his possession,** as Rabbi Ḥiyya said in the *baraita* on the previous *amud*. Since he owns produce it is as though he has provided it at the present time, and the current lack of payment is not significant. Accordingly, one can **conclude from it** another *halakha*, that this practice is permitted **provided that he has produce** in his possession. **And** further **conclude from it** that the *halakha* **is** in accordance **with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yannai.**

The distinction between the two positions is therefore clear: Rabbi Hiyya requires that money change hands for a debt-based purchase to be valid; Rabbi Oshaya permits the arrangement as long as the seller has possession of the merchandise. Going forward, the Gemara proceeds on the basis of Rabbi Oshaya's ruling.

## Applying to Mishna

### Mishna Case

With Rabbi Oshaya's *Tosefta* in hand, Rava now turns to the *mishna* that had been difficult to understand. The *mishna* describes a case where grain was purchased at a price of twenty-five, and the buyer later seeks to collect at a price of thirty — that is, he bought at the lower price and the market has since risen. The *mishna*'s ruling seemed to hinge on whether wine was or was not in the seller's possession, but the reasoning had not been transparent.

### Mishna Reading

Rava now reads the *mishna* through the lens of Rabbi Oshaya's *Tosefta*. The *mishna* is dealing with exactly the same chain: a debt was converted into wheat, the wheat was then converted into wine, and the wine was to be delivered later. The *mishna* rules the arrangement forbidden in the particular case because the seller did not have the wine (*ve-yayin ein lo*). Had he had the wine, it would have been permitted under Rabbi Oshaya's rule. This reading renders the *mishna* fully coherent: it is not about simple price appreciation on a straightforward purchase, but about a chain of debt conversions where possession at each stage determines permissibility.

### Umai Lakach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״לָקַח״ – לָקַח בְּהַלְוָאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava concludes his explanation: **And what is** the meaning in the mishna of: **Acquired?** It means that **he acquired** it **as** payment for **his loan.**

The Gemara then asks: *u-mai lakach* — what does the *mishna* mean when it says that the buyer "purchased"? If the legitimating condition is that money changes hands, then "purchased" should mean that money was given. But on this reading, the purchase was effected not by money but by extending a pre-existing debt — *lakach be-halvaso*, he purchased through his loan, through allowing the debtor to retain the debt as payment. The reason the arrangement is problematic is that the initial purchase was not grounded in a monetary transfer but in a debt relationship, making the subsequent conversions more loan-like.

### Rashi's View

Rashi, however, reads the case differently and notes that one need not even go so far as to reinterpret "purchased" in this extended sense. According to Rashi, even if the initial wheat purchase involved actual money changing hands — and was therefore unambiguously valid even without the seller having possession — the subsequent conversion of wheat into wine constitutes an entirely new transaction. At that stage, no money changes hands. The wheat that Shimon owes Reuven is simply rolled over into a new obligation for wine. It is this second transaction — the wheat-to-wine conversion — that is the problematic one, and it is problematic precisely because at that stage Shimon does not have the wine. The ruling of the *mishna* therefore attaches to the wine transaction, not the original wheat purchase, and Rashi's reading does not require any reinterpretation of the word "purchased" as it applies to the wheat.

## Two Conditions

### Issuro Ba Le-Yado

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: מָה לִי הֵן וּמָה לִי דְּמֵיהֶן – אָמְרִינַן, מָה לִי דְּמֵיהֶן וּמָה לִי הֵן – נָמֵי אָמְרִינַן. וּפוֹסְקִין עַל שַׁעַר שֶׁבַּשּׁוּק, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Now that Rabbi Yannai** is **saying** that **we say: What** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce, **and what** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce's **value,** meaning that we do not require the payment to be in the form of the produce stipulated, **we** therefore **also say: What** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce's **value, and what** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce, **and** therefore **one may set** a price **by the market rate. And** this is permitted **even though he does not have** produce at the present moment.

The Gemara now consolidates the two conditions that legitimize a future-delivery sale. The first is *issuro ba le-yado* — the money has come into his hand. It is, admittedly, a peculiar phrase: *issar* is not itself the forbidden thing, but the phrase means that money has changed hands in the context of the transaction, and that transfer is what anchors the arrangement as a sale.

### Merchandise Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה נוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, וְעָשָׂה לוֹ שָׂדֵהוּ מֶכֶר. בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַמּוֹכֵר אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת – מוּתָּר. לוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת – אָסוּר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַלּוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת – מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **If** one **was owed one hundred dinars by another, and** the borrower **performed a sale** of **his field for him** in order to repay the one hundred dinars, if they agreed **that the seller,** i.e., the borrower, **consumes the produce** of the field until the debt is repaid, this is certainly **permitted,** but if the **buyer,** i.e., the lender, **consumes the produce,** it is **prohibited,** as he is in effect taking the produce as interest while awaiting the payment that he is owed. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even if the buyer consumes the produce,** it is **permitted.**

The second condition — an alternative to monetary transfer — is that the seller currently holds possession of the merchandise he is selling. This was the ruling of Rabbi Oshaya: if the seller has the product in hand, the absence of a monetary transfer does not render the deal loan-like. The possession of the product itself makes it a transaction in goods, not an extension of a loan.

### Yatza Ha-Sha'ar

One must note that both conditions presuppose a third prerequisite: *yatza ha-sha'ar* — a fixed market price must have been established and the commodity must be readily available for purchase. Without a going market price, neither condition suffices. If either money changes hands or the seller has possession, *and* the market price is established, the sale is valid. We will see more of this requirement as the Gemara develops.

## Agra Natar

### Delayed Delivery

Now the Gemara steps back to examine the conceptual problem that these conditions are designed to address. Why should delayed delivery create any concern about *ribbis* in the first place? After all, a buyer and seller have agreed on a price; goods are to be delivered later; what is wrong with that? The answer lies in a fundamental principle: when an exchange is not immediate — when I hand over money today and receive the commodity only weeks or months later — and when what I ultimately receive is worth more than what I gave, the structure begins to resemble a loan with interest rather than a purchase. I gave out money; time passed; I got back something worth more. That is the core of the concern.

### Hamtanat Ma'ot

The key phrase the Gemara introduces here is *agra natar*, which in Aramaic means *sechar hamtanat ha-ma'ot* — compensation for the time the money was held. If the reason the buyer ultimately receives more than he paid is that the seller held his money for an extended period and delivered late, then what looks like appreciation in the commodity's value might actually be compensation for the use of the buyer's money over time. That is *ribbis* by another name. The Torah's prohibition on interest is not limited to explicit agreements to pay a percentage; any arrangement in which money is extended and more value returns — whether in cash or in goods — may fall within the rabbinic prohibition.

### Opportunity Cost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁקִילָא טֵיבוּתָיךְ וְשַׁדְיָיא אַחִיזְרֵי. מַאי אַהְנֵית לִי? אִי הֲווֹ לִי זוּזֵי בִּידֵי – הֲוָה מְזַבֵּנְינָא בְּהִינֵי וּבְשִׁילֵי בְּזוּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Because** the buyer **says to** the seller: **Your favor is taken and thrown on the thorns.** In other words, I am deriving no benefit from my transaction with you, as **how have you helped me? If I had money in my hands I would buy** the produce myself **in** the towns of **Hini and Shili,** nearby market towns, **at an inexpensive** price.

Here lies the crucial *chiddush* that explains why money changing hands actually permits price appreciation. Once money genuinely changes hands in the context of a sale — meaning the seller receives the funds and the market price is established — one can argue as follows: what did the seller actually do for the buyer? The buyer could have taken that same money and immediately purchased the commodity himself, taking physical possession at the season's low price, and he would have profited from the appreciation just as surely. He chose instead to hand the money to the seller and allow delivery later. Since the buyer could have achieved the same profit entirely on his own by taking possession directly, we do not view the seller's later delivery of an appreciated commodity as compensation for the use of the money over time. The buyer's profit is not *agra natar*; it is simply the profit he could have made regardless. The transaction does not therefore take on the character of a loan.

### Possession Alternative

The same logic extends to the case where merchandise possession substitutes for monetary transfer. Even where no money changes hands — because the buyer converts a pre-existing debt — if the seller has the product in his possession, he could immediately hand it over. There is no real delay; the exchange could be consummated on the spot. Since the seller holds the product and could deliver it right now, it is not as if the buyer is letting the seller hold his money for an extended period during which the seller benefits from the use of those funds. The possession of the product eliminates the *hamtanat ha-ma'ot* concern: there is no delayed holding of value; there is simply a sale of a product currently in hand, to be collected at a later convenient time. That is a legitimate commercial arrangement, not a disguised loan.

## Rava's Deductions

### Loan Supports Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּמַעֲמִידִין מִלְוֶה עַל גַּבֵּי פֵּירוֹת, וְלָא אָמְרִינַן דְּלָא כְּאִיסָּרוֹ הַבָּא לְיָדוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Conclude from** this *baraita* **of Rabbi Oshaya three** *halakhot*: **Conclude from it that one may establish** repayment of **a loan upon produce,** meaning that a borrower can promise to pay the lender in produce over the course of a year, based on the market rate at the beginning of the year, **and we do not say** that this is **like** a case where **his *issar* has not** already **come into his possession.**

Rava proceeds to extract three *shema mina* conclusions from Rabbi Oshaya's *Tosefta*. The first: *ma'amidin malveh al gabi perot* — you may support or underwrite a sale of future produce on the basis of a pre-existing loan. That is, the creditor's pre-existing claim against the debtor can serve as the purchase price for a future commodity transaction. The money he is owed need not literally change hands again; the credit itself can function as the consideration for the sale, provided the seller has possession of the product.

### Goods-Money Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: מָה לִי הֵן מָה לִי דְּמֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as Rabbi Yannai says: What** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce, and **what** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce's **value?** If he stipulated that he would receive a certain amount of produce, he can later take its value in money rather than the produce itself without violating the prohibition of interest.

The second *shema mina*: *ita li-d'Rabbi Yannai* — we hold in accordance with Rabbi Yannai's principle. Who is Rabbi Yannai, and what is his principle? Rabbi Yannai holds *ma li hen ma li dmeihen* — what difference does it make to me whether I receive the thing itself or its monetary equivalent? In the context of the *Tosefta*'s chain, when the wheat Shimon owes Reuven has appreciated from one hundred dollars to one hundred twenty, and Shimon proposes to deliver one hundred twenty dollars' worth of wine instead, the conversion is permitted because Reuven could simply have treated the wheat as worth its current market value and purchased the wine with that value. The thing and its monetary equivalent are fungible for these purposes. This principle will become important shortly.

### Amana in Kind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִתְּמַר, רַב אָמַר: עוֹשִׂין אֲמָנָה בְּפֵירוֹת. וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֲמָנָה בְּדָמִים. וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: מָה לִי הֵן, וּמָה לִי דְּמֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed about this issue: **Rav says** that **one may make** an arrangement of **trust with regard to** the delivery of items such as **produce,** i.e., one may loan the money in advance with the agreement that he will be repaid with produce at a later date, **but one may not make** an arrangement of **trust with regard to money,** i.e., one may not loan the money in advance with the agreement that he will receive the value of the produce at a later date, as this has the appearance of collecting interest. **And Rabbi Yannai says: What** difference is it **to me** if the agreement concerned **the produce, and what** difference is it **to me** if it concerned the produce's **value?** Just as the lender can take the produce and sell it himself, he can likewise accept the value of the produce directly.

The third *shema mina* is a ruling of Rava himself: *osin amana b'perot, ein osin amana b'damim* — one may make a forward-sale arrangement (*amana*) to be repaid in kind, but one may not make such an arrangement to be repaid in cash. What does this mean? If I give a seller one thousand dollars to be repaid in one hundred bushels of wheat later in the year, that is permissible — the delivery is in the commodity itself, even if that commodity has by then appreciated in value. However, if when the time comes for delivery the seller proposes to give back not the wheat but twelve hundred dollars in cash — the current value of the wheat — Rava says that is forbidden. Why? Because at that point, stripped of the commodity framing, one sees that money was handed over and more money came back. The transaction looks unmistakably like a loan with interest: one thousand dollars out, twelve hundred dollars back.

### Rabbi Yannai

Rabbi Yannai disagrees with this restriction. His principle — *ma li hen ma li dmeihen* — leads him to say that if the original arrangement was valid when conceived as a commodity sale, then it remains valid whether repayment comes in the form of the commodity or its cash equivalent. Since the seller legitimately owed the wheat, and could have owed the money instead, repaying in cash does not transform a valid sale into a loan. Rava's ruling and Rabbi Yannai's position are therefore in tension, and the subsequent discussion navigates between them.

## Meitivei Challenge

### Tosefta Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כּוּלָּם, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ מוּתָּר. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: בְּשֶׁמָּשַׁךְ. אִי בְּשֶׁמָּשַׁךְ צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן שֶׁיִּחֵד לוֹ קֶרֶן זָוִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against Rav's opinion from the above *baraita*: With regard to **all of them, if** the borrower **has** the items in his possession, it is **permitted,** apparently even if he actually pays with something else. **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** That *baraita* is stated **with regard to** a case **where** the lender **pulled** the produce itself, thereby performing an act of acquisition. The Gemara questions Rav Huna's statement: **If he pulled** the produce, does it **need to be said?** After all, there was a proper act of acquisition. **Rather,** the *baraita* is discussing a case **where** the borrower **designated a corner for** the lender in which to place the produce that he acquired.

The Gemara now raises a *meitivei* — a challenge against Rava's position that payment in cash is forbidden. The difficulty is that the *Tosefta* of Rabbi Oshaya appears to contemplate exactly such a cash conversion as permitted. When Shimon converts his wheat obligation into a wine obligation, and his wine obligation into an oil obligation, he is in each case treating the prior commodity as equivalent to its cash value and purchasing a new commodity with that value. Yet Rabbi Oshaya permits all of this — *kullam, im yesh lo, mutar*. How can Rava say that converting into cash is forbidden when Rabbi Oshaya seems to endorse the equivalent?

### Quasi-Possession

Rav Huna answers in the name of Rav: the permissibility in Rabbi Oshaya's case rests on a specific quasi-possession mechanism — *k'gon she-yiched lo keren zavis*, that Shimon designated a corner of his storage for Reuven's wheat. When Shimon told Reuven "the wheat in that corner is yours," Reuven did not perform a *kinyan meshicha* and did not technically acquire the wheat. But there was a designation — a *yichud* — that gave Reuven a quasi-possessory relationship with the specific wheat in that corner. It is because of this designation that the subsequent conversion into wine, and from wine into oil, is treated not as a direct cash-for-cash exchange but as the alienation of property that was already, in a meaningful sense, Reuven's.

### Ownership Transfer

The critical distinction, then, is whether there was ever an effective transfer of ownership — even partial — over the object itself. If the wheat was designated for Reuven, so that in some sense it was already his, then when it is converted into wine, Reuven is not receiving money back for his money; he is receiving a new commodity in exchange for property he effectively owned. The *ribbis* concern is thereby neutralized. But where no such designation occurred — where the seller simply owed money and the buyer never acquired any quasi-possessory claim over specific goods — then converting the obligation into cash puts one right back into the mold of: money out, more money in.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Shmuel Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: צַד אֶחָד בְּרִבִּית מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*, which permits the practice if the borrower is in possession of such produce? **It is** in accordance with that of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that **uncertain interest,** a transaction that may or may not result in the payment of interest, is **permitted.**

The Gemara now records Shmuel's alternative resolution of the difficulty. Rather than explaining the *Tosefta* as Rav Huna did — through the mechanism of *yichud* — Shmuel proposes that the *Tosefta* of Rabbi Oshaya may simply reflect the lenient view of Rabbi Yehuda, not the majority position. The general Halacha follows the majority view, and the majority may well prohibit what Rabbi Oshaya's *Tosefta* permits.

### Tzad Echad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה נוֹשֶׁה בַּחֲבֵירוֹ מָנֶה, וְעָשָׂה לוֹ שָׂדֵהוּ מֶכֶר. בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַמּוֹכֵר אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת – מוּתָּר. לוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת – אָסוּר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַלּוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת – מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **If** one **was owed one hundred dinars by another, and** the borrower **performed a sale** of **his field for him** in order to repay the one hundred dinars, if they agreed **that the seller,** i.e., the borrower, **consumes the produce** of the field until the debt is repaid, this is certainly **permitted,** but if the **buyer,** i.e., the lender, **consumes the produce,** it is **prohibited,** as he is in effect taking the produce as interest while awaiting the payment that he is owed. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even if the buyer consumes the produce,** it is **permitted.**

Who is Rabbi Yehuda, and what is his distinctive leniency? Rabbi Yehuda holds the principle of *tzad echad b'ribbis mutar* — if a transaction is structured such that one possible outcome would involve *ribbis* while another possible outcome would not, the transaction is permitted even if the *ribbis* outcome is realized. The reasoning is that the *ribbis* element is not a certainty built into the arrangement; it is contingent on which way events unfold. Since one path leads to a legitimate outcome, Rabbi Yehuda does not condemn the arrangement even when the other path — the one with a *ribbis* appearance — is ultimately taken.

### Field Collateral

The Gemara illustrates Rabbi Yehuda's position with a concrete case. Reuven is owed one hundred dollars by Shimon. Shimon proposes: rather than pay you back immediately, I will sell you my field for the hundred dollars I owe you, on the condition that if I repay you the hundred dollars by the end of the month, the sale is cancelled. This is a conditional sale piggybacking on a pre-existing loan. Crucially, this is not a lien in the conventional sense — it was not part of the original loan's structure; it is a new arrangement entered into after the loan.

### Peiros Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבַיְתוֹס בֶּן זוֹנִין שֶׁעָשָׂה שָׂדֵהוּ מֶכֶר עַל פִּי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה וְלוֹקֵחַ אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת הָיָה! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה?! מוֹכֵר אוֹכֵל פֵּרוֹת הָיָה, וְלֹא לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* relates: **Rabbi Yehuda said to** the Sages: **An incident** occurred **involving Baitos ben Zunin, who performed a sale** of **his field on the basis of** a directive from **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya,** and that **was** a case where the **buyer consumed the produce.** The Sages **said to** the *tanna*: Will you **bring a proof from there?** The reverse was the case: **It was** the **seller, not the buyer, who consumed the produce.**

What happens during the intervening month? Two scenarios arise. In the first, the seller (Shimon) continues to eat the produce of the field during the month (*bizman she-mocher ochel peiros*). If Shimon then fails to repay and Reuven takes the field, there is no difficulty: the sale was completed, Shimon was eating his own field's produce in the interim, and everything is in order. In the second scenario, the buyer (Reuven) benefits from the field's produce during the month (*bizman she-malveh ochel peiros*). If Shimon ultimately pays back the hundred dollars at month's end, what was Reuven doing during that month? He lent one hundred dollars, got it back, and in the interim enjoyed the produce of Shimon's field. That looks very much like benefit derived from a loan — classic *ribbis*.

### Yehuda's Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: צַד אֶחָד בְּרִבִּית אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. רָבָא אָמַר: רִבִּית עַל מְנָת לְהַחֲזִיר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference in opinion **between** the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yehuda? **Abaye said:** The difference **between them** concerns the *halakha* of **uncertain interest.** According to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, if it is uncertain that a transaction will ultimately involve the payment of interest, it is permitted. **Rava said: The** difference **between them** involves the status of **interest** taken **on the condition** that it be **given back.** Rava maintains that Rabbi Yehuda did not mean that the lender can consume the produce without payment, as he must repay the amount he ate if the borrower pays off his debt. The first *tanna* rules that since the sale is not yet final, his consumption of the produce constitutes interest.

The Tanna Kamma — the majority position — forbids the arrangement where the buyer benefits from the produce during the interim period, for precisely the reason just stated: it may resolve as a loan with benefit accruing to the lender. Rabbi Yehuda permits it, invoking *tzad echad b'ribbis*: since it was equally possible that Shimon would not repay and the field would become Reuven's outright — in which case Reuven would simply have been enjoying the produce of his own field — the arrangement is not condemned even when the other outcome obtains.

Rabbi Yehuda even cites a precedent: *ma'aseh b'Beisus ben Zunin* — a case involving Beisus ben Zunin, who entered into exactly such an arrangement and it was endorsed by Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah. The Rabbis counter that the facts of that case were different: it was the seller (*mocher*) who was eating the produce during the interim, not the buyer (*loke'ach*), and that is why it was permitted. The precedent therefore does not support Rabbi Yehuda's broad leniency.

### Ribbis Conditional

Abaye offers a clarification of what the dispute between the Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yehuda actually turns on: *tzad echad d'ribbis ika beineihu* — the dispute is about the permissibility of a transaction where one side of the possible outcomes would constitute *ribbis* and the other would not. Rashi adds that the conditional sale (*mechar*) component is significant here: the transaction is framed as a sale, not purely as a loan, and it is the coexistence of a sale context with the contingent *ribbis* element that allows Rabbi Yehuda to be lenient.

Rava, however, limits Rabbi Yehuda's leniency sharply. He states that Rabbi Yehuda does not simply permit the buyer to benefit from the produce during the interim period without any further obligation. Rather, *ribbis al menas lehachazir ika beineihu* — the dispute is about whether the arrangement is permitted only on the condition that if the loan is repaid, the buyer must also reimburse the seller for the value of the produce he consumed during the interim. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the arrangement is permissible only when the buyer is obligated to repay for the produce he enjoyed if the sale ultimately does not go through. Without that obligation, even Rabbi Yehuda would forbid it. The Tanna Kamma disagrees and holds the arrangement impermissible even with such a repayment condition, because in the interim it continues to have the appearance of *ribbis*, which is itself objectionable.

## Poskin Al Sha'ar

### Joint Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מַאי טַעְמָא אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן פּוֹסְקִין עַל שַׁעַר שֶׁבַּשּׁוּק וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say: What is the reason** that **the Sages said one may set** a price **by the market rate and that** one may do so **even though he does not have** the produce in his possession?

Rava and Rav Yosef now jointly address the fundamental question raised by the principle *ma li hen ma li dmeihen*: if goods and their monetary equivalent are interchangeable, can one go further and permit a buyer to lock in a future delivery price with money alone, even when the seller has no present possession of the merchandise? Their position is that one can — *poskin al sha'ar she-ba-shuk ve-af al pi she-ein lo* — one may fix the price according to the current market rate even though the seller does not have the product.

### Independent Purchase

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁקִילָא טֵיבוּתָיךְ וְשַׁדְיָיא אַחִיזְרֵי. מַאי אַהְנֵית לִי? אִי הֲווֹ לִי זוּזֵי בִּידֵי – הֲוָה מְזַבֵּנְינָא בְּהִינֵי וּבְשִׁילֵי בְּזוּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Because** the buyer **says to** the seller: **Your favor is taken and thrown on the thorns.** In other words, I am deriving no benefit from my transaction with you, as **how have you helped me? If I had money in my hands I would buy** the produce myself **in** the towns of **Hini and Shili,** nearby market towns, **at an inexpensive** price.

The justification is the argument of *shekila tivusayich ve-shadya achzirei* — "take your good deed and throw it to the pigs." The buyer challenges the premise that the seller did him a favor. Had the buyer not entered into this arrangement and had instead retained his money, he could have gone directly to the grain-producing towns of *Hini* and *Shili* in Bavel, purchased the commodity at the season's low price, and realized the same profit through simple possession. The seller therefore has not actually done the buyer any benefit that the buyer could not have achieved independently.

### Counterfactual

To spell out the counterfactual: two months ago, the buyer had one hundred dollars. Instead of committing it to the seller for future delivery, he could have taken that money, gone to market at the season's low, purchased the commodity, and held it. When the price rose to one hundred twenty dollars, he would have profited identically. The fact that he chose instead to advance the money to the seller and receive appreciated goods later does not, under this analysis, create a *ribbis* problem — because the appreciation is not a reward for extending a loan; it is simply the same profit the buyer could have captured by acting unilaterally. The transaction is therefore genuinely sale-like: the buyer, by paying now and receiving later, is doing nothing more than what any forward purchaser does in a functioning commodity market.

### Grain Loan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה מוּתָּר לִלְוֹת סְאָה בִּסְאָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּמָצֵי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁקִילָא טֵיבוּתָיךְ וְשַׁדְיָיא אַחִיזְרֵי, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: חִטֵּי דְּקָדְחִי בַּאֲכַלְבַּאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם הַלְוָאָה, הָכָא – זְבִינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: But if that is so,** according to this same logic it should be **permitted to lend a *se'a*** of produce **for a *se'a*** of the same produce, **as** the lender **can say to** the borrower: **Your favor is taken and thrown on the thorns, since he says to him:** I stand to gain nothing from the *se'a* you will later give me, as the price of my wheat would increase if it were to remain in my granary, and therefore the loan does not benefit me at all, for will the **wheat burn in my warehouse [*be'akalbai*]?** Rav Yosef **said to him:** The two cases are different, as **there** it is referring to **a loan,** and consequently there is a concern about interest, whereas **here** it is **a sale.**

Abaye raises a powerful objection. If the argument of *shekila tivusayich ve-shadya achzirei* is sufficient to permit a money-based forward purchase with price appreciation, why is it not equally sufficient to permit the classic forbidden case of lending a *se'ah* of grain and receiving back a *se'ah* of grain? The borrower could make exactly the same argument: what did I do for you? You could have simply held onto your flour and you would still have it. Why is lending a *se'ah* and receiving a *se'ah* back forbidden? If the "could have made the same profit independently" argument dissolves the *ribbis* concern in forward commodity sales, it should dissolve it in grain loans as well.

### Framing Matters

The answer distinguishes the two cases by framing. When the transaction is framed from the outset as a loan — "lend me a *se'ah* and I will return a *se'ah*" — the *ribbis* concern is activated regardless of whether the lender could theoretically have retained the commodity. The use of loan language (*halva'ah*) triggers heightened scrutiny. Any return of greater value, or even the same value at a time when prices have shifted, becomes suspect. But when the transaction is framed from the outset as a purchase — "I am buying wheat at today's price, to be delivered over the course of the year" — the analysis is fundamentally different. The framing matters: what is structured as a *mekach* — a sale — is evaluated by the logic of commerce; what is structured as a *halva'ah* — a loan — is evaluated by the law of *ribbis*. Since the buyer is not the lender, and the transaction began as a sale, the *shekila tivusayich* argument suffices to remove the *ribbis* concern.

## Broker & Market

### Middleman Fees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַדָּא בַּר אַבָּא לְרָבָא: וְהָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתַּב זוּזֵי לְסַפְסֵירָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּקָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ נָמֵי. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: זוּזֵי דְּאִינָשֵׁי אִינְהוּ עָבְדִי לֵיהּ סַפְסֵירוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Adda bar Abba said to Rava: But** with regard to establishing the repayment of the loan upon produce valued at the market rate, the buyer does stand to gain from the agreement, as, if he would have to go to acquire produce elsewhere, **he would have to give a dinar to a broker,** and therefore he gains a dinar from the early payment. Rava **said to him:** The case is one **where** the buyer **also gives** the seller the broker's fee. **Rav Ashi said:** There is generally no need to add this fee, as **a person's dinars serve as a broker for him.** One who has ready cash has no need for the services of a broker.

Adda bar Ahava raises a further objection to the logic of *shekila tivusayich ve-shadya achzirei*. The argument assumes that the buyer could have gone independently to the grain market and purchased the commodity without any friction. But in practice, an independent purchaser would have incurred middleman fees — payments to brokers or agents who connect buyers with sellers. The seller therefore *did* do the buyer a genuine favor: by entering into the forward sale directly, the buyer avoided the cost of finding and paying a middleman. This means the buyer actually gained something from the arrangement beyond what he could have achieved independently, which undermines the claim that no real benefit flowed from the seller to the buyer.

### Broker Compensation

The first answer is straightforward: the price the buyer pays the seller already incorporates compensation for those brokerage savings. When a buyer commits money in a forward sale, the price he pays reflects not only the commodity's value but also the convenience of dealing directly. The buyer, having paid the full going rate, has in effect already compensated the seller for the service of bypassing the need for a middleman.

### Money as Middleman

Rav Ashi offers a second and more elegant answer: the buyer's money itself serves as his middleman. A person who has money to spend — who is known to be in the market with purchasing power — does not need to hire agents to find sellers. The sellers come to him. The mere fact of having the money and being a known buyer in the market means that sellers seek him out. He need expend no effort and pay no fees to acquire the commodity independently. The *shekila tivusayich* argument therefore holds: the buyer genuinely could have achieved the same result without the arrangement, and the seller has not done him a special favor that would render the price appreciation into something resembling *ribbis*.

## Summary & Conclusion

### Sale vs. Loan

The Gemara's analysis across this entire sugya rests on a single fundamental distinction: whether a transaction is at its core a *mekach u-memkar* — a sale — or a *halva'ah* — a loan. Everything follows from this classification. A sale, even one with deferred delivery and even one where what is ultimately received is worth more than what was given, does not implicate *ribbis*. A loan, even one where the extra value returned was unintended or unanticipated, does implicate *ribbis* whenever more value returns to the lender than he extended.

### Buyer Perspective

The buyer's perspective provides the decisive test in the sale context. If the buyer, by giving money today for deferred delivery of a commodity, could have achieved the same result — and the same profit — by simply taking that money to market and purchasing the commodity directly, then the seller has not benefited at the buyer's expense. The appreciation in value that the buyer ultimately receives is not compensation for time, not compensation for the use of the buyer's money — it is simply the market appreciation that would have accrued to any holder of the commodity. There is no *agra natar*, no payment for patience, no *hamtanat ha-ma'ot*. The legitimizing conditions — money changing hands or the seller being in possession of the merchandise, combined with an established market price — collectively ensure that the transaction retains the character of a genuine commercial sale, not a disguised loan.

### Iska Partnership

The *sugya* closes with an introduction to an entirely different mechanism for avoiding *ribbis* concerns: the *iska*. A *Tosefta* in Bava Metzia, perek 5, describes a case where one person lends money on the condition that he share in the profits of the borrower's business. If the arrangement gives the capital provider a share of profits without any share of losses — so that the capital is guaranteed — that is *ribbis* and is forbidden. However, if the capital provider shares in both the profits and the losses, the arrangement is entirely permitted. The reason is that in such a case the capital is not a loan; it is an investment. The provider owns a portion of the enterprise. His money is genuinely at risk. Any profit that returns to him is not interest on a loan — it is the return on his equity stake in a shared commercial venture. This is the foundation of the *iska* concept and, by extension, of the *hetter iska*: as long as the provider of capital shares genuinely in the risk of loss, the *ribbis* prohibition does not apply. The two parties are not lender and borrower; they are partners.
