# Gittin 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabba vs. Rava Debate

### Debate Overview

The daf begins at the bottom of 4b, still working through the debate between Rabba and Rava concerning the reason the Mishnah requires the messenger who brings a *get* from abroad to declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* — that the *get* was written and signed in his presence. Rabba holds that the requirement addresses *lishmah* concerns, ensuring the *get* was written with proper intent for this specific couple. Rava holds that the requirement addresses *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo* — the practical unavailability of witnesses to validate the signatures. The Gemara has already worked through several *mishnayot* that could neatly have fallen along these lines, and has managed to explain each according to both positions.

### Asasyos Case

The outstanding question at this stage concerned why Eretz Yisrael enjoys a special status — why, within Eretz Yisrael, the messenger need not make this declaration even according to the position that the requirement stems from concerns about witness availability. The answer offered was that travel within Eretz Yisrael is common, whether due to the *Beis Hamikdash* or, in later periods, the prevalence of *batei dinim*.

The Gemara now advances to a fresh challenge. We learn in the Mishnah that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: even from one *hegemonia* to another — that is, from one jurisdiction to another within a single divided city — the messenger must make the declaration. Rabbi Yitzchak explains that there was a city in Eretz Yisrael called Asasyos, which was divided between two governments that were at odds with one another (*makpidim zeh al zeh*), such that travel from one side of the city to the other was effectively impossible — like East and West Berlin. Therefore, even within that single city, moving from one political jurisdiction to the other required the declaration.

The critical point is that this case is situated in Eretz Yisrael. Since Eretz Yisrael is generally exempt from the requirement on Rava's account — witnesses being available there — the reason Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel reintroduces the requirement must be the practical impossibility of travel and the resulting unavailability of witnesses. This is *nichah l'Rava* — it fits Rava's position well — but *kashya l'Rabba*, it is difficult for Rabba. If the concern were *lishmah*, there would be no reason for Eretz Yisrael's general exemption to be overridden by a divided city, since *lishmah* concerns do not depend on the ease of travel.

### Rabba Concedes

The Gemara concludes: *Rabba is lei d'Rava* — Rabba concedes Rava's point. The one who holds *lishmah* as his primary rationale also acknowledges that there is a genuine problem of *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo*. Rabba's position is therefore not simply *lishmah* alone, but rather *lishmah* plus *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo*.

This is, in fact, a coherent and elegant position: the declaration has two components — *bifnei nichtav* and *bifnei nechtam*. One might understand *bifnei nichtav* as addressing the *lishmah* concern — how the *get* was written — and *bifnei nechtam* as addressing the witness-validation concern — how it was signed.

### Two Concerns

With Rabba now accepting both rationales, the question becomes: what remains between Rabba and Rava? The answer is that while Rabba agrees to Rava, Rava does not agree to Rabba. Rava maintains that signature validation is the *sole* concern; *lishmah* adds nothing. The remaining dispute therefore concerns cases where signature validation is not at issue but a *lishmah* concern might still apply.

### Remaining Debate

What would constitute such a case? The Gemara identifies two possibilities. The first is where two witnesses bring the *get* together. The second is when the *get* is brought within the same country in *Medinat Hayam*.

### Two Witnesses Case

If two witnesses bring the *get*, they themselves implicitly testify that the husband dispatched them, thereby eliminating any concern about the signatures being forged. There is no need for further validation. Yet for Rabba, a *lishmah* concern may still exist if the *get* originated in *Chutz La'aretz* — if it came from a place where the laws of proper writing were not well known.

### Lishmah Concerns

Similarly, when the *get* is brought within the same country of *Medinat Hayam*, there is no problem of signature validation since witnesses are accessible within that jurisdiction. But for Rabba, the *lishmah* concern persists wherever a *Chutz La'aretz* problem exists — even within one country, if it is a place where *lishmah* was not carefully observed, such as a distant location where Torah was not studied.

In short: whenever there is a signature-validation concern, everyone agrees the declaration must be made — *Rabba is lei d'Rava*. What Rabba adds is that even where there is no such concern, if the *lishmah* problem of *Chutz La'aretz* applies, he requires the declaration as well.

## Mute Messenger Case

### Aino Yachol Intro

The Gemara now turns to a Mishnah that will serve as further evidence in this debate. The Mishnah states: one who brings a *get* from *Medinat Hayam* and is *eino yachol lomar* — unable to say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* — may rely instead on having the signatures validated: *im yesh alav eidim yiskayem b'chosamav*, if there are witnesses, let it be confirmed through the signatories. The apparent implication is stark: if you cannot make the declaration, simply validate the signatures, and the *get* is valid. This reads as though the entire point of the requirement is signature validation — nothing more.

Before accepting this reading, however, the Gemara must clarify what *eino yachol lomar* — "unable to say" — actually means. The obvious first answer is that the messenger is a *cheresh*, a deaf-mute. But that cannot be right: the Mishnah elsewhere states explicitly that a deaf-mute may not bring a *get* in the first place — *hakol k'sherim l'havi es haget chutz micheresh shoteh v'katan* — since a deaf-mute lacks the legal competence (*bar da'as*) required to serve as a *shaliach*. How then could the case be a deaf-mute?

### Rav Yosef Explanation

Rav Yosef resolves this: *k'gon shenasno lo k'shehu pike'ach* — the *get* was given to a person who was fully competent, but before he could declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* after delivering the *get*, he became a deaf-mute. He had a stroke on the spot. That is why he is unable to say the declaration — not because he was a deaf-mute at the time of appointment as *shaliach*, but because he became one immediately after delivery.

### Tosafot Alternative

This answer strikes Tosafot as unnecessarily extreme. Two far simpler explanations present themselves: the messenger is simply mute (*ilem*) — physically unable to speak but legally competent — or he was not present at the writing and signing and therefore genuinely cannot testify to it. Either of these would make better sense of the phrase *eino yachol lomar*. And if the Gemara wanted to convey a case of physical inability, why not say *v'lo amar* — "and he did not say" — rather than the charged language of *eino yachol*, which seems to emphasize an absolute incapacity?

### Signatures Primary

Whatever the precise identity of the messenger in the Mishnah, the substantive point stands: someone delivered a *get* and was unable or failed to make the declaration, and the solution is simply to validate the signatures. This is a clear statement that the primary — indeed, on this reading, the sole — operative concern is signature validation. The Gemara therefore notes: *l'Rava nicha, l'Rabba kashya* — it is good for Rava, whose entire position rests on the signature concern, but difficult for Rabba, who also insists on a *lishmah* concern. What became of *lishmah* in this case? The Gemara answers: *l'achar shelamdu* — this Mishnah addresses the period after the laws of *lishmah* became widely known in *Chutz La'aretz*, at which point the *lishmah* concern ceased to apply.

## Lishmah to Signatures

### Evidence Conflict

This Gemara is striking in its overall trajectory. What began as a clear, principled debate — is the requirement of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* about *lishmah* or about signature validation? — has evolved in a way that seems to progressively favor Rava. First, Rabba concedes that signature validation is also a concern. Then, confronted with a Mishnah that implies the *lishmah* concern has entirely lapsed, Rabba is forced to concede further: there was a period when the *lishmah* issue ceased to be operative.

The argument weighing in Rabba's favor remains the Mishnah's fundamental distinction between Eretz Yisrael and *Chutz La'aretz*. If the requirement were purely about signature validation, the geographic distinction should track witness availability — and witnesses are available in Eretz Yisrael because of the *Beis Hamikdash* or the *batei dinim*. This indeed explains Eretz Yisrael's exemption, and the Asasyos case showed that even within Eretz Yisrael, the requirement can be reinstated when travel is impossible. All of this fits Rava's account. The emphasis on Eretz Yisrael is thus Rabba's best evidence, and even that is accounted for by Rava.

Yet there is a tension worth noting. The evidence pointing toward *lishmah* is the structural emphasis on Eretz Yisrael as the default domain of exemption. The evidence pointing toward signature validation is the repeated pattern by which *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo* explains case after case with precision. And crucially, when the two accounts come apart — as in the Mishnah of *eino yachol* — the Gemara explains it away as *l'achar shelamdu*, suggesting that the *lishmah* concern is temporally contingent in a way that the signature concern is not. This indicates that the parameters of the requirement are contextual, not purely formalistic: if circumstances change, what was once a concern may cease to be one.

### Gezeirah Yachzor

This contextual flexibility, however, raises a difficulty. If *lishmah* is no longer a concern, why not simply permit the messenger to skip the declaration entirely and rely on signature validation from the outset? Let the Mishnah say: *lechatchila*, if you want to validate the signatures instead, be our guest — that is the better option.

The Gemara answers with the principle of *gezeirah shema yachzor hadavar lekilkulo* — we fear that if we abandon the established practice, conditions may revert to the problematic state that originally necessitated it. Things were bad once; they got better; they can get worse again, as indeed we have witnessed in our own times. Therefore, we do not say *lechatchila* that the declaration is unnecessary. We maintain the original requirement in its place: the messenger should continue to declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*. Only when this becomes impossible will we acknowledge changed circumstances and permit signature validation instead.

### Lechatachila Bedi'eved

This creates a formal distinction between *lechatchila* and *bedi'eved*. *Lechatchila*, the old practice is preserved — the messenger must make the declaration. *Bedi'eved*, if that is not possible, we acknowledge that *lishmah* is no longer an active concern and signature validation suffices. The *gezeirah* is maintained prospectively but not imposed retroactively in impossible cases.

### Unusual Cases Exempt

The Gemara presses further: if we maintain the old practice even when circumstances have changed, we should insist on the declaration even in a case like the *pike'ach shenishtatek* — the person who became a deaf-mute — where it is genuinely impossible. Should there not be no out? The Gemara responds: *milta d'lo shechicha lo gazru bah rabbanan* — the Rabbis do not extend their decrees to bizarre and highly unusual circumstances. The case of a person who was fully competent and then became a deaf-mute immediately after delivery is so extraordinarily rare that the *gezeirah* does not apply to it.

### Woman Get Case

One might think this creates a general exemption for unusual cases. But the Gemara challenges this immediately: what about the woman who brings her own *get*? That too is a bizarre scenario — the Mishnah explains it as a case where the husband appointed her as his *shaliach* to deliver the *get* to herself upon arriving in a particular place. Yet the Mishnah rules that she must still declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*. If unusual cases are exempt from the *gezeirah*, why must she declare it?

The Gemara answers: *shelo tachlok b'shlichus* — we do not wish to introduce distinctions within the framework of *shlichus*. The principle of *lo plug* applies: we establish a uniform rule that every *shaliach* who is able to make the declaration must make it, regardless of whether the particular circumstances are usual or unusual. The *gezeirah* is maintained as a blanket requirement for all functioning *shlichim*, precisely to avoid the confusion that exceptions would generate.

### Resolution Balance

This brings the analysis to its resolution. Tosafot captures it concisely: *shelo tachlok b'shlichus*, *peirush b'shlichus d'yachol* — the rule applies uniformly when the *shaliach* is able to make the declaration. When, however, the *shaliach* is genuinely unable — as in the deaf-mute case — we do not impose an impossible standard. We acknowledge current realities: *lishmah* is no longer an active concern, and signature validation is sufficient. The woman bringing her own *get*, unusual as that scenario is, can still make the declaration, so the *lo plug* applies and she must.

The overall position, then, is this: *lechatchila*, every *shaliach* who can make the declaration must do so — not because *lishmah* is still a live concern, but because we maintain the old practice against the possibility of its recurrence. When it is genuinely impossible, we permit signature validation as a *bedi'eved* alternative. And unusual cases that fall entirely outside the normal *shlichus* paradigm — such as two messengers bringing a *get* together, as will emerge below — are treated differently.

## Husband Delivers Get

### Husband Question

The Gemara now raises a new question. If the principle is *lo plug* — we maintain the declaration uniformly to preserve the established practice — then even the husband himself, when he personally delivers the *get*, should be required to declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*. After all, even if he is personally delivering it, and even if there is no concern about forgery, perhaps we should insist on his declaration as a hedge against the *lishmah* concern that might someday resurface. Yet a *braita* teaches explicitly: *hu atzmo sheheivi gito ein tzarich lomar bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* — if the husband himself brought his *get*, he need not make the declaration. Why not?

### Real Concern

The Gemara answers by clarifying what the underlying concern actually is. One might have thought that the requirement of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* exists because we genuinely worry the *get* was written *shelo lishmah*. In fact, this is not the case. The genuine concern of *shelo lishmah* is a *miuta d'miuta* — a vanishingly small concern. It is not a real fear that *gittin* are being written without proper intent. The actual concern, says the Gemara, is something more social and strategic: *dilma ati ba'al u'mearer u'fasil lei* — we fear that the husband will use *lishmah* as a pretext, a basis for later challenging the *get* and invalidating it. The declaration closes off that pretext: it establishes a record that the writing and signing were done properly.

### Husband Exception

With this clarification, the exemption for a husband who delivers his own *get* becomes entirely logical. *Minka naktei biyadei ve'ati ka'mearer lei?* — if he himself is physically delivering the *get* with his own hands, taking personal responsibility for it in public, is he then going to turn around and challenge it? The personal involvement eliminates both the factual basis and any credible pretext for a challenge. It would be absurd: he is the one who brought it.

This clarification is significant beyond the immediate case. It reveals that the *lishmah* concern was never primarily about the substantive risk that a *get* was actually written without proper intent. It was always about protecting the woman against a husband who might exploit a technical pretext. The requirement of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* is thus oriented toward the woman's interests, not toward a genuine fear that scribal practice was deficient.

## Two Messengers Get

### Shmuel Query

The Gemara introduces a query posed by Shmuel to Rav Huna: if two people bring a *get* from *Medinat Hayam* — *shnayim sheheviu get mimedinas hayam* — must they declare *bifnenu nichtav u'bifnenu nechtam*? Recall that two messengers together constitute one of the key *nafka mina* cases in the Rabba-Rava debate. Since two witnesses deliver the *get*, their combined testimony implicitly establishes that the husband sent them and that the *get* is genuine — there is no concern about forgery, and thus no *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo* issue. The sole remaining concern, according to Rabba, would be *lishmah*.

Rav Huna answers: they need not declare. He reasons: *uma ilu yomru bifnenu gershah mi lo meheimni* — if they had stated that the divorce was carried out in their presence, they would be believed for everything. Certainly, then, they are believed to establish that the *get* was properly dispatched by the husband. This eliminates the signature concern entirely. The Gemara notes: *l'Rabba nicha, l'Rava kashya* — good for Rabba, whose position is driven by *lishmah*, but difficult for Rava, since on Rava's account the concern is signature validation, and here the two messengers eliminate that concern. The Gemara's characteristic response appears once more: *delamdu* — they already knew the laws of *lishmah* by this point, so that concern does not apply.

### Two Unusual

The Gemara then asks: if *lishmah* is no longer a concern, and we rely on *l'achar shelamdu*, why not simply say that even a single messenger need not declare — just let the signatures be validated? The answer returns to *gezeirah shema yachzor hadavar lekilkulo* — the fear that conditions may revert. We keep the old practice in place.

But then, why are two messengers exempt? The Gemara answers: *bei trei d'maisei gita milta d'lo shechicha* — two people bringing a *get* together is an unusual occurrence, and the Rabbis did not apply their decree to unusual circumstances. *Milta d'lo shechicha lo gazru bah rabbanan*.

### Woman Compare

The Gemara immediately challenges this with the familiar case: the woman who brings her own *get* is also unusual — *d'lo shechicha* — yet she must still declare. Why is two messengers different?

### Testimony Paradigm

The resolution, spelled out by Tosafot, rests on a fundamental distinction between *shlichus* and *edus*. In a standard case of one *shaliach*, the paradigm is purely one of agency: a single individual is empowered and sent. We do not want to make distinctions within that *shlichus* paradigm — *shelo tachlok b'shlichus d'yachol* — so we apply the requirement uniformly to all who are capable. The woman who brings her own *get*, unusual as that is, still falls squarely within the *shlichus* paradigm, and therefore *lo plug* applies.

Two people bringing a *get* together is qualitatively different. It is not merely an unusual instantiation of the standard *shlichus* model; it has the character of *edus* — testimony — rather than pure agency. When two people bring a *get*, the *get* delivery functions more like testimonial validation than like an act of *shlichus* alone. They are, to be sure, still *shlichim* in the sense that they are empowered to deliver, but the dual presence transforms the act into something that resembles a testimonial proceeding. It falls outside the standard *shlichus* paradigm entirely, and therefore *gazrinan chad d'lo shechiach atu chad d'shechiach — bei terei atu chad lo gazrinan*: we extend the decree from common cases of one messenger to less common ones, but we do not extend it to the fundamentally different case of two messengers together.

## Yehoshua Lishmah

### Yehoshua Proof

The Gemara now identifies the Rabba-Rava debate as a parallel to the *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi — one holding *lishmah*, the other holding *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo*. The Gemara seeks to determine which *amora* held which position. It concludes that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is the one who said *lishmah*, on the basis of the following incident: Rabbi Shimon bar Abba brought a *get* before Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and asked whether he still needed to declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi replied: you do not need to. *Lo amru ela b'doros harishonim*, it was only required in the earlier generations *she'ein beki'in lishmah* — when people were not expert in the laws of *lishmah*. But in these later generations, *d'beki'in lishmah*, when everyone knows the law, no declaration is required. This is the source of the Gemara's earlier phrase *l'achar shelamdu*.

### Contradiction

This ruling of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi seems to create a problem. We concluded above that everyone — including Rabba — agrees that the signature-validation concern is real and cannot be dismissed. If that is so, then even *l'achar shelamdu*, when the *lishmah* concern lapses, there should still be a requirement to make the declaration on account of *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo*. And we further concluded that even after *l'achar shelamdu*, the old practice is maintained *lechatchila* because of *gezeirah shema yachzor*. Yet Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi told Rabbi Shimon bar Abba simply: you need not declare at all. This sounds like a *lishmah*-only position that recognizes no remaining concern once *lishmah* is resolved — which seems to contradict everything the Gemara established.

### Two Messenger Implicit

The Gemara resolves this in one stroke: *Rav Shimon bar Abba inish achrini havah bahadei* — Rabbi Shimon bar Abba was not alone; there was another person accompanying him. The *get* was being brought by two messengers together. The other person's presence is not mentioned in the account, as the Gemara explains, *mishum kevodo d'Rav Shimon* — out of respect for Rabbi Shimon bar Abba, the narrative focuses on him rather than noting the unnamed companion.

### Resolution Unusual

This resolves everything. When two people bring a *get* together, we already established that this is a *milta d'lo shechicha* — an unusual case falling outside the standard *shlichus* paradigm — and the Rabbis did not apply their decree to unusual cases. Therefore, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's ruling that no declaration was needed does not reflect a *lishmah*-only position that ignores signature concerns. It reflects the specific exemption for two-messenger cases. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is perfectly consistent with the Gemara's overall framework: the signature concern remains valid, the old practice is maintained *lechatchila*, but two messengers are exempt because they constitute an unusual case that the *gezeirah* never covered. The apparent contradiction dissolves.

## Witness Numbers

### Two vs Three

The Gemara now turns to a new question: *bifnei kamah notna lah* — in front of how many people must the *shaliach* deliver the *get* when making his declaration? Since the *shaliach* is testifying, presumably at least two witnesses must be present. But exactly how many? Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Chanina debate the matter, one holding *bifnei shenayim* — before two — and the other holding *bifnei sheloshah* — before three.

### Rachavei Proof

The Gemara determines which *amora* held which position. An incident is cited: Rachavei bar Rav Chisda brought a *get* before Rabbi Yochanan, and Rabbi Yochanan instructed him, *zil havah lah b'apei trei* — go and deliver it to her in the presence of two — and say to them, *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*. This demonstrates clearly that Rabbi Yochanan holds *bifnei shenayim* — two witnesses suffice.

### Theory Initial

The natural inference is that the debate of two versus three maps onto the underlying debate of *lishmah* versus signature validation. One might suggest: the position requiring two witnesses corresponds to the *lishmah* account, since a simple testimony before two witnesses is all that is needed to establish that proper intent was observed. The position requiring three corresponds to the signature-validation account, since validating signatures (*kiyum shtaros*) is done before a *beis din* of three.

### Theory Rejected

This mapping, however, does not survive scrutiny. We established above that Rabbi Yochanan himself is the one who holds *ein eidim metzuyim lekaymo* — signature validation — while Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi holds *lishmah*. Moreover, we concluded that *Rabba is lei d'Rava* — everyone agrees at some level that signature validation is a concern. If signature validation requires a court of three, how can Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that the requirement is about signatures, rule that two suffice?

### Shliach Eid Dayan

The Gemara therefore proposes a different basis for the debate. The question is not about the underlying rationale for the requirement, but about the legal mechanics of validation. Everyone agrees that signature validation is necessary. The debate concerns whether *shaliach na'aseh eid v'eid na'aseh dayan* — whether the same individual can first act as the *shaliach* delivering the *get*, then switch roles to serve as a witness validating the signatures, and then switch roles again to serve as a *dayan* on the *beis din* that adjudicates his own testimony.

This triple role-shifting is the crux. The *shaliach* is delivering the *get*: that is his agency role. He simultaneously declares *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*: that is his testimonial role — *shaliach na'aseh eid*. Now if he can also count as a *dayan* for the purpose of adjudicating his own testimony — *eid na'aseh dayan* — then the three-person *beis din* needed for *kiyum shtaros* can be constituted with only two additional people: the *shaliach* himself counts as one, and the two people before whom he delivers the *get* count as two, totaling three. Hence delivery before two people suffices.

If, however, *ein eid na'aseh dayan* — the witness cannot serve as a *dayan* for his own testimony — then the *shaliach*'s counting as a *dayan* is impermissible, and three additional people are needed.

### Eid Dayan Rabbinic

The Gemara notes that the accepted *halacha* is: *b'drabanan, eid na'aseh dayan* — in the domain of rabbinic enactments, a witness may indeed serve as a *dayan* for his own testimony. Since *kiyum shtaros* for a *get* delivered from abroad is a rabbinic requirement, the ruling should universally permit delivery before two, and the debate should collapse. The Gemara therefore looks for a different basis for the remaining dispute.

### Woman Dayan Risk

The debate, the Gemara concludes, concerns a concern about practical confusion. According to the view that requires three, the reasoning is: since a woman is valid to serve as a *shliach* and bring a *get*, if we establish a universal practice of delivering before two — relying on the *shaliach* himself counting as the third — people may eventually forget the nuance and begin relying on a woman to serve in that same counting role. A woman, however, cannot be a *dayan*, even for rabbinic proceedings. If she brings the *get* and people count her as the *shaliach*-turned-*eid*-turned-*dayan*, the *kiyum* will be defective. To prevent this confusion, the view of three insists that the required three people be present exclusively as witnesses and *dayanim*, separate from the *shaliach*.

The opposing view — Rabbi Yochanan's view of two — holds that *ishah meida yadiah velo samchi alah*: people know that a woman cannot serve as a *dayan* and will not make that error. Therefore, the nuance that the *shaliach* counts toward the three does not risk being misapplied.

## Failure Remedy

### Two Witnesses Accepted

The *braita* teaches the established practice: delivery before two witnesses suffices, in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan. The *braita* presents a case in which the *shaliach* delivered the *get* and gave it to the woman but did not declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*.

### Meir Stringency

Rabbi Meir rules: *yotzi ve-havalad mamzer* — the husband must divorce her and any child born of a subsequent remarriage is a *mamzer*. The failure to declare invalidates the *get*, at least rabbinically, and the consequences are correspondingly severe. Rabbi Meir brooks no flexibility: the absence of the declaration, for whatever reason, is an irremediable defect. Even if she has already remarried and has children, the *mamzer* status follows.

### Sages Leniency

The Sages disagree sharply: *ein havalad mamzer* — the child is emphatically not a *mamzer*. The failure to declare is a *lechatchila* deficiency, not a fundamental invalidation of the *get*. The Sages then address the practical question of how to remedy the situation *lechatchila* for a woman who has received a *get* without the declaration and wishes to remarry: *yitlenu mimenu, v'yachzir v'yitnena lah bifnei shenayim, v'yomar bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* — the *get* is taken back from her, and the *shaliach* redelivers it in the presence of two, this time making the declaration. Since the declaration must accompany the act of delivery, a redelivery with proper declaration remedies the deficiency.

One might ask: if she is already divorced by the first delivery — which is valid *d'oraisa* — what is the point of redelivering? The answer employs a *mima nafshach* logic: if rabbinically the first delivery is not considered fully valid because the declaration was omitted, then rabbinically she is not yet divorced, and a second delivery with the declaration completes the rabbinic requirement. If *d'oraisa* she was already divorced by the first delivery, then certainly the second delivery causes no harm. Either way, the redelivery cures the problem *lechatchila*.

### Timing Declaration

Tosafot raises the question of when precisely the declaration must be made. The *braita*'s remedy of *yitlenu mimenu* — taking the *get* back from her and redelivering it — implies that the declaration must be contemporaneous with the act of delivery itself. One cannot simply say the words the next day while the *get* sits in the woman's possession. But how narrowly must "contemporaneous" be defined?

Tosafot anchors this from the case on the preceding daf: the *pike'ach shenishtatek* case, where the *shaliach* gave the *get* and became a deaf-mute before completing the declaration. That case implies that a moment after delivery — but still *toch kedei dibbur* — is acceptable, since the *shaliach* was in the process of speaking when he was stricken. Tosafot concludes: during delivery, certainly valid; immediately after delivery within *toch kedei dibbur* (*peirush b'shlichus d'yachol*), also valid; any time after *kedei dibbur*, no longer valid as a contemporaneous declaration, and the *get* must be redelivered.

As for the scene suggested by *yitlenu mimenu*, Tosafot observes that the language implies the *get* is still in her hands at the moment of taking it back — the parties are apparently still standing together, still engaged in the transaction. Once they have dispersed and the moment of the giving has fully passed, the declaration cannot be retroactively attached to it.

What remains unresolved, notes Tosafot, is whether the declaration is valid if made somewhat after *kedei dibbur* but while the parties are still actively engaged in discussing the *get*. The *Ri* remains uncertain — *mesapka lei l'Ri* — whether continued engagement with the subject matter of the *get* constitutes a prolonged moment of delivery. And similarly, whether a declaration made *before* the physical act of giving — moments before rather than during — would satisfy the requirement is also left unresolved.

### Meir Rationale

The Gemara explains Rabbi Meir's position by citing a principle attributed to him by Rav Hamnuna in the name of Ulla. Rabbi Meir used to say: *kol hameshaneh mi-matbe'a she-tav'u chakhamim b'gittin, yotzi ve-havalad mamzer* — anyone who deviates from the established mold (*matbe'a*) that the Sages coined for *gittin*, must have the woman divorce and any child of remarriage is a *mamzer*. For Rabbi Meir, the Sages established a precise and non-negotiable form for the *get* process. Any deviation from that form — including the failure to make the required declaration — is treated as a fundamental departure from the mold, with all its consequences. There is no *bedi'eved* leniency that mitigates the severity of the deviation. Any step outside the established *matbe'a* carries the full consequence.
