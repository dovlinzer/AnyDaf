# Tamid 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lottery & Service Roles

### Service Roles Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן הַמְמוּנֶּה בּוֹאוּ וְהָפִיסוּ: מִי הַשּׁוֹחֵט, וּמִי הַזּוֹרֵק, מִי מְדַשֵּׁן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, מִי מְדַשֵּׁן אֶת הַמְּנוֹרָה, מִי מַעֲלֶה אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: 3:1 Four lotteries were conducted in the Temple each day in order to determine which priests would perform which of the Temple rites. After describing the first of the lotteries, for removal of the ashes, the *tanna* describes the second lottery. **The** priest **appointed** to oversee the lotteries **said to** the priests: **Come and participate in the lottery** to determine **who is the** priest **who** will **slaughter** the daily offering; **and who** is the priest **who** will **sprinkle** its blood; and **who** will **remove the ashes from the inner,** golden **altar;** and **who** will **remove the ashes from the Candelabrum;** and **who** will **take** the **limbs** of the daily offering **up to the ramp** to be burned afterward.

The third *perek* opens with the *memuneh* — the appointed supervisor — addressing the assembled *kohanim*: *bo'u vehifisu*, come and take part in the lottery. The *memuneh* then enumerates the roles to be distributed. Who will perform the *shechita* of the *tamid*? Who will do the *zrika* — the throwing of the blood? It is worth noting that *kabalat hadam* and *holachat hadam*, the receiving and conveying of the blood, are not listed separately; those roles belonged to the same *kohen* who also performed the *zrika*. The *mekabel* caught the blood, brought it to the *mizbeach*, and performed the throwing — all as a single assignment.

### Shechita & Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָרֹאשׁ וְהָרֶגֶל שֶׁל יָמִין, וּשְׁתֵּי הַיָּדַיִם, הָעוֹקֶץ וְהָרֶגֶל שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל, הֶחָזֶה וְהַגֵּרָה, וּשְׁתֵּי דְפָנוֹת, הַקְּרָבַיִם, וְהַסּוֹלֶת, וְהַחֲבִיתִּין וְהַיַּיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The limbs of the daily offering taken up to the ramp were divided among the priests in the following manner: One priest took **the head and the right** hind **leg** up to the altar; **and** a second took **the two forelegs;** a third **the haunch,** including the lower spine and the tail, **and the left** hind **leg;** a fourth **the breast and the cud,** i.e., the neck and appended internal organs, including the windpipe, liver, and heart; **and** a fifth **the two flanks;** a sixth **the innards; and** a seventh **the fine flour** from the accompanying meal offering; **and** an eighth **the griddle-cake** offering; **and** a ninth **the wine** for the libation.

The *mishnah* continues: *mi medashen mizbeach hapnimi*, who will remove the ashes from the inner *mizbeach*? And *mi medashen et hamenorah*, who will remove the ashes from the *menorah*? The verb *medashen* here carries the sense of removal — stripping away — rather than adding anything. Similarly, *shechita* was in principle permissible even by a non-*kohen*, though for the *tamid* specifically this was certainly not the practice. The *payisot*, the lotteries, were conducted in the morning and applied primarily to the communal aspects of the service. Individual *korbanot* were handled differently — there was presumably some form of floor management to keep things moving, and a lottery was not conducted every time a private person arrived with an animal. The *mishnah* in *Shekalim* even names a specific individual as the *memuneh al hapayisot*, suggesting this was a recognized, ongoing appointment rather than a one-time role.

### Dishing Altar & Menorah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵפִיסוּ, זָכָה מִי שֶׁזָּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** They **conducted the lottery** to determine the thirteen tasks, i.e., slaughtering, carrying the nine items or pairs of items, sprinkling the blood, removing the ashes from the inner altar, and removing the ashes from the Candelabrum. **And whoever won** that lottery **won** the right to perform the slaughter, and the twelve priests standing to his right won the other privileges.

After the four roles just described — *shechita*, *zrika*, *dishun mizbeach hapnimi*, and *dishun hamenorah* — the *mishnah* turns to those who will bring the limbs of the *olat tamid* to the ramp. The sequence of roles in the *mishnah* does not reflect the actual order of performance. The *shechita* and *zrika* are listed first because the *tamid* itself is the focus, but as will become clear, the *dishun* — cleaning the inner *mizbeach* and the *menorah* — was actually performed first. The listing prioritizes the *tamid* thematically even while the operational sequence begins elsewhere.

### Payis & Allocation

The lottery itself — *hefisu*, they did the *payis* — resulted in assignments, and *zacha mi she-zacha*, whoever won, won. Beyond the daily morning roles, *payisot* were conducted for communal *musafim* as well. The *mishnah* in *Sukkah*, for instance, describes how the seventy *parei hachag* were distributed among the twenty-four *mishmarot*, three bulls each, accounting for seventy-two of the offerings, with the remaining two allocated by a separate arrangement. Similarly, *Shemini Atzeret*, as a *regel bifnei atzmo*, received its own *payis bifnei atzmo* for its *musafim*. Communal offerings generally required a lottery; private ones did not.

### Ma'aleh Evarim

The *mishnah* now enumerates the full list of *kohanim* involved in *ma'aleh evarim*, bringing the limbs of the *tamid* to the ramp. The first *kohen* brings the *harosh veharegel* — the head and the right rear leg. The second brings *shtei hayadayim* — the two forelegs. The third brings the *uketz veharegel shel smol* — the tail and the left rear leg. The fourth carries the *chazeh vegeirah* — the chest along with the trachea, esophagus, and associated thoracic organs including the heart and liver. The fifth brings *shtei defanot* — the two flanks. The sixth carries the *kirvayim* — the inner organs, meaning the stomach and intestines. That gives us six *kohanim* for the limbs alone.

To these six are added three more: *vehasolet*, the one who brings the flour of the accompanying *mincha*; *veha-chavitin*, the one who brings the *chavitei kohen gadol*, the daily *mincha* of the *kohen gadol* prepared concurrently with the *tamid* — the preparation for which, as noted in the previous *perek*, had already begun during the *terumat hadeshen*; and *vehayayin*, the one who brings the wine of the *minchat nesachim*. Combined with the four roles enumerated earlier — *shochет*, *zoreik*, *dishun mizbeach hapnimi*, and *dishun hamenorah* — the total comes to thirteen *kohanim*. It is worth noting that the one who actually flays and dismembers the animal does not appear explicitly in this count, which is curious given that *nikkur* requires considerable skill; it may be that not every role considered prestigious or specialized was subject to lottery in the same way, or that this was folded into the assignments of those handling the limbs.

The *chavitei kohen gadol*, it should be noted, were brought on the *kohen gadol*'s behalf even in his absence. He was not required to be present each morning, though had he wished to perform any part of the service himself he retained the prerogative — the *kohen gadol* may perform any service he chooses.

## Service Order & Timing

### Morning Readiness Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: צְאוּ וּרְאוּ אִם הִגִּיעַ זְמַן שְׁחִיטָה. אִם הִגִּיעַ, הָרוֹאֶה אוֹמֵר: "בַּרְקַאי". מַתְיָא בֶּן שְׁמוּאֵל אוֹמֵר: הֵאִיר פְּנֵי כׇּל הַמִּזְרָח עַד שֶׁהוּא בְּחֶבְרוֹן? וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: "הֵן".
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:2 The appointed one **said to** the priests: **Go out and observe if** it is day and **the time for slaughter** has **arrived. If** the time has **arrived, the observer says: There is light. Matya ben Shmuel says** that the appointed priest phrased his question differently, saying: **Is the entire eastern sky illuminated as far as Hebron? And** the observer **says: Yes.**

Once the lottery was complete, the *memuneh* addressed those assembled: *tze'u ure'u im higia zeman shechita* — go out and see whether the time for *shechita* has arrived. They were gathered indoors, so someone had to go and verify. The requirement is not sunrise precisely but *alot hashachar*, the first appearance of the morning star, for *shechita* to be valid.

### Barkai Signal

The one on watch — *haro'eh* — presumably among the *shomrim* stationed on the rooftops — would call out *barkai*, an exclamation derived from the root of *boker*, meaning something like "it has shone forth." *Matya ben Shmuel*, who according to one version of the *mishnah* held the position of *memuneh al hapayisot*, would then add a verification: *he'ir p'nei kol hamizrach* — has the entire eastern sky lit up? *Ad she-be-Chevron* — all the way to *Chevron*? And the watchman would confirm: *hen*, yes.

The question of who is saying what here is itself a point of discussion. According to one reading, *Matya ben Shmuel* is not being cited as a *Tanna* but quoted as a *kohen* who, during his tenure, introduced this more rigorous formulation. Before and after him, the exchange may have been simpler — just the watchman calling *barkai* and the *memuneh* accepting it. But during his time, he would press for the fuller confirmation: has it lit up all the way to *Chevron*?

According to the version in *Yoma*, it was the watchman on the roof who said the entire east has lit up, and the *memuneh* — or *Matya ben Shmuel* — who responded by asking "All the way to *Chevron*?" eliciting the final *hen*. The reason for specifically mentioning *Chevron*, according to another discussion elsewhere, was to evoke *zechut avot* in the context of the *korbanot* — an interesting resonance given the broader theme of *tefillah keneged avot* and *keneged korbanot*.

### Pre-Dawn Error

The reason for this careful double-verification was rooted in a historical incident: on one occasion they mistook an early glow for *alot hashachar*, slaughtered the *tamid*, and the entire service was rendered *pasul* — invalid — because it had been performed before it was actually light.

### Sheep Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: צְאוּ וְהָבִיאוּ (לִי, וְהוֹצִיאוּ) טָלֶה מִלִּשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים. וַהֲרֵי לִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים נְתוּנָה בְּמִקְצוֹעַ צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית. אַרְבַּע לְשָׁכוֹת הָיוּ שָׁם: אַחַת – לִשְׁכַּת הַטְּלָאִים, וְאַחַת – לִשְׁכַּת הַחוֹתָמוֹת, וְאַחַת – לִשְׁכַּת בֵּית הַמּוֹקֵד, וְאַחַת – לִשְׁכָּה שֶׁעוֹשִׂין בָּהּ לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:3 The appointed priest **said to** the priests: **Go out and bring** me **a lamb from the Chamber of the Lambs,** where lambs that had been examined and found to be unblemished were kept. **And the Chamber of the Lambs is located in the northwestern corner** of the building of the Chamber of the Hearth. **There were four chambers there,** in that building: **One** was **the Chamber of the Lambs; and one** was **the Chamber of the Seals,** located in the northeastern corner, where the priest stored receipts given to those seeking to purchase animal offerings; **and one** was **the Chamber of the Hearth,** where there was a fire burning to warm the priests; **and** the last **one** was **the chamber in which** the priests **prepared the shewbread.**

Following the check, the *memuneh* instructed the *kohanim*: *tze'u vehavi'u taleh me-lishkat hatla'im* — go and bring a sheep from the chamber of sheep, where animals were held for four days of examination to ensure they were without blemishes. The *lishkat hatla'im* is described as being in the northwest — this refers to the location of the *Beit Hamoked* as a whole, within which the chamber occupied its own position. The sheep kept there were checked each day, with one removed as another was introduced on a rolling basis.

## Beit Hamoked Chambers

### Four Chambers

A *Baraita* teaches that the *Beit Hamoked* contained four chambers — *arba lishchot hayu sham*. These were housed within the large structure of the *Beit Hamoked* itself.

### Chamber of Seals

The first chamber was *lishkat hatla'im*, for the sheep. The second was *lishkat hachotamot*, the chamber of seals. This chamber served the needs of those bringing private *korbanot*: if a person needed to provide the *minchat nesachim* — the oil, flour, and wine accompanying his *korban* — he would go to this chamber, pay, and receive a *chotam* or *petek*, a kind of receipt or token. He would then present this token when his offering was being processed, and the *kohanim* would supply the requisite materials on his behalf.

A natural question arises: how could an ordinary Israelite access a chamber that seems to be located deep within the *azarah*, well into the *ezrat kohanim*? The answer is that the chamber was accessible from the outside — through an entrance that did not require the person to enter the sanctified area. The gate associated with this access may well have been *Sha'ar Hakorban* for this very reason. The visitor would remain on the portion of the chamber that was not within the full sanctity of the *azara* and conduct his transaction from there. It is also worth noting, incidentally, that according to the *Gemara* the *Chashmonaim* buried the stones of the defiled *mizbeach* — the *avnei mizbeach* that had been desecrated by the *Yevanim* — beneath the floor of this chamber.

The third chamber was *lishkat beit hamoked* proper, which housed a fire — though since the main structure also had a central fire, the precise function of this subsidiary chamber is not entirely clear. The fourth chamber, situated in the holy interior, was where the *lechem hapanim* was actually baked — *lishka she'osim bah lechem hapanim*. One chamber noted in other sources but not named in this list is the one containing the underground passage down to the *mikvah*, which was discussed in the first *perek*.

### Vessels & Gold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְנְסוּ לְלִשְׁכַּת הַכֵּלִים, הוֹצִיאוּ מִשָּׁם תִּשְׁעִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה כְּלֵי כֶסֶף וּכְלֵי זָהָב, הִשְׁקוּ אֶת הַתָּמִיד בַּכּוֹס
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:4 The priests **entered the Chamber of the Vessels,** where the service vessels required for the daily Temple service were stored. They **took out from there ninety-three silver vessels and gold vessels. They** then **gave** the lamb selected for **the daily offering** water **to drink in a cup**

After retrieving the sheep from the *lishkat hatla'im*, the *kohanim* also went to another chamber — the storage room for the *klei shareit* — and brought out ninety-three gold and silver vessels. According to the *Yerushalmi*, the number ninety-three corresponds to the number of times God's name appears in the prophecies of *Chaggai*, *Zechariah*, and *Malachi* — the *nevi'im* of the return from exile who were present at the building of the second *Beit Hamikdash*.

### Final Inspection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל זָהָב. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא מְבוּקָּר מִבָּעֶרֶב – מְבַקְּרִין אוֹתוֹ לְאוֹר אֲבוּקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **of gold. Although** the lamb **was examined** and deemed unblemished earlier **in the evening,** the priests **examine it** now **by the light of the torches.**

They then gave the *tamid* water to drink from a gold goblet — *hishku et hatamid bechos shel zahav* — apparently to make subsequent flaying easier. Although the animal had been checked for blemishes over the preceding four days, the *mishnah* specifies that they would check it one final time: *mevakrim oto l'or avukot*, by the light of torches. The use of torches raises a question as to timing — was it still dark? The *mefaresh* suggests this checking took place before the full light of dawn, since even once the watchman on the roof could detect the first gleam, the chamber itself would still require artificial light. Another possibility raised is that they brought the animal indoors for this examination precisely to avoid having to return it to the *azara* once taken out if a blemish were subsequently discovered.

## Heichal Door Opening

### Pesach Ohel Moed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁזָּכָה בְּדִישּׁוּן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי וּבְדִישּׁוּן הַמְּנוֹרָה, הָיוּ מַקְדִּימִין, וְאַרְבַּע כֵּלִים בְּיָדָם: הַטְּנִי וְהַכּוּז וּשְׁנֵי מַפְתְּחוֹת. הַטְּנִי – דּוֹמֶה לְתַרְקַב שֶׁל זָהָב מַחֲזִיק קַבַּיִם וָחֵצִי. וְהַכּוּז – דּוֹמֶה לְקִיתוֹן גָּדוֹל שֶׁל זָהָב. וּשְׁתֵּי מַפְתְּחוֹת – אֶחָד שֶׁהוּא יוֹרֵד בְּאַמַּת הַשֶּׁחִי, וְאֶחָד שֶׁהוּא פּוֹתֵחַ כֵּיוָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:5 The priests **who won** the privilege **of the removal of ash from the inner altar and of the removal of ash from the Candelabrum would precede** the other priests **and** would hold **four vessels in their hands: The basket, and the jug, and** the **two keys. The basket is similar to** a large **gold** vessel with a capacity of **three *kav* [*letarkav*],** but it **holds** only **two and a half *kav*. And the jug is similar to a large flask [*lekitton*] of gold. And** as for the **two keys,** with **one** of them the priest **would lower his arm to his armpit** through a small opening in the door and open the lock that was at the bottom of the door on the inside, and he would pass through that door into a compartment. **And** the other **one** is the key with **which** the priest **opened** the lock on the inner door of the compartment, through which he entered the Sanctuary, and that lock he opened **directly.**

One of the fundamental requirements for the daily service is that the *shechita* of the *tamid* may not take place until the doors of the *heichal* have been opened. This is derived from the *pasuk* of *pesach ohel mo'ed* — the opening of the *ohel mo'ed* in the time of the *mishkan*, which represented the covered sanctuary space. Just as the *mishkan* required its entrance to be opened, so too the doors of the *heichal* must be *pasuach* before the service begins. The opening of those doors was, in effect, the moment the *mikdash* opened for business.

### Small Door & Keys

However, before the main gate was opened, the *kohanim* responsible for the *dishun mizbeach hapnimi* and the *dishun hamenorah* needed access to the interior. For this purpose, a small door — a *pishpash* — was built into the large gate, and entry was possible through it prior to the full opening. This is the purpose of the two keys described earlier: one key that would go into the *beit hashachi* — the underarm-length mechanism — and one that opened *keivan*, directly.

Several interpretations of this mechanism exist. One holds that there were two locks requiring simultaneous operation, one from inside and one from outside, with a small aperture in the wall through which one arm was inserted to turn the inner lock while another person operated the outer one from outside. A simpler interpretation understands *beit shachi* from the root *lishoch*, to bend down — meaning one lock was at a lower position and required the *kohen* to stoop, while the other was at normal height. Either way, two locks were involved.

### North/South Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לַפִּשְׁפָּשׁ הַצְּפוֹנִי. שְׁנֵי פִּשְׁפָּשִׁין הָיוּ לוֹ לַשַּׁעַר הַגָּדוֹל, אַחַת בַּצָּפוֹן, וְאַחַת בַּדָּרוֹם. שֶׁבַּדָּרוֹם לֹא נִכְנַס בּוֹ אָדָם. עָלָיו הוּא מְפוֹרָשׁ עַל יְדֵי יְחֶזְקֵאל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי ה׳ הַשַּׁעַר הַזֶּה סָגוּר יִהְיֶה לֹא יִפָּתֵחַ וְאִישׁ אַל יָבֹא בוֹ כִּי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל בָּא בוֹ וְהָיָה סָגוּר".
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:6 The priest **came to the northern wicket. There were two wickets for the large gate, one in the northern** part of the gate **and one in the southern** part. Through the wicket **that was in the southern** part, **no person entered. In its regard,** the wicket's status **is clarified by** the prophet **Ezekiel, as it is stated:** "Then he brought me back the way of the outer gate of the Sanctuary, which looks toward the east; and it was shut. **And the Lord said unto me: This gate shall be shut, it shall not be opened, neither shall any man enter in by it, for the Lord, the God of Israel, has entered in by it; and it shall be shut"** (Ezekiel 44:1–2).

The *mishnah* specifies that there were two *pishpashin* — small doors — built into the main gate of the *heichal*: *achat batzafon ve'achat badarom*, one in the northern portion and one in the southern. The *kohen* who merited the *dishun* would take his key and open the northern *pishpash*, entering first the small inner chamber — the hollow space within the thickness of the gate — and from there passing into the *heichal* proper.

### Ezekiel's Prophecy

As for the southern *pishpash* — *shebadarom lo nichnas bo adam* — no one ever entered through it. The reason is drawn from a *pasuk* in *Yechezkel*, which speaks of the third *Beit Hamikdash* yet was understood to apply to the second as well: *hasha'ar hazeh sagur yihyeh lo yipate'ach ve'ish lo yavo bo ki Hashem elokei Yisrael ba bo* — this gate shall be closed, it shall not be opened, no man shall enter it, for Hashem the God of Israel has entered through it, and it shall remain closed. One of the *mefarshim* explains this not as a straightforward statement of divine entry but as a theological emphasis: God does not require an open door to be present. Humans need doors opened to access a place; this door remains sealed to demonstrate that divine presence operates independently of physical access. The *pasuk*, of course, was said after the *churban* of the first *bayit*, yet the community understood it as binding for the second *Beit Hamikdash* as well.

### Opening Main Gate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָטַל אֶת הַמַּפְתֵּחַ וּפָתַח אֶת הַפִּשְׁפָּשׁ, וְנִכְנַס לְהַתָּא, וּמֵהַתָּא לַהֵיכָל, עַד שֶׁמַּגִּיעַ לְשַׁעַר הַגָּדוֹל. הִגִּיעַ לְשַׁעַר הַגָּדוֹל, הֶעֱבִיר אֶת הַנֶּגֶר וְאֶת הַפּוֹתְחוֹת וּפְתָחוֹ. לֹא הָיָה שׁוֹחֵט הַשּׁוֹחֵט עַד שֶׁשּׁוֹמֵעַ שַׁעַר גָּדוֹל שֶׁנִּפְתַּח.
>
> **Translation:** The priest **took the key, opened the wicket** north of the gate, **and entered the compartment.** He went **from the compartment to the Sanctuary** and continued **until** he would **reach the large gate.** Once **he reached the large gate,** which was locked from the inside, **he moved the bolt and the locks** fixing the bolt in place **and opened** the gate. The priest **who slaughters** the daily offering **would not slaughter** the animal **until he** would **hear that the large gate** had been **opened.**

The *kohen* who entered through the northern *pishpash* made his way through the interior of the gate and then turned to face the main gate itself. He then *he'evir et hanagar* — removed the great bolt — and *ve'et hapotechos* — undid the locks. Only once this was accomplished, once the sound of the great gate opening was heard, could the *shechita* of the *tamid* proceed: *lo hayah shochet hashochet ad sheshome'a sha'ar hagadol sheniftach* — the slaughterer would not slaughter until he heard the main gate open.

## Jericho Sounds

### Gate Heard Far

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל שַׁעַר גָּדוֹל שֶׁנִּפְתַּח. מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל מַגְרֵיפָה. מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל הַשִּׁיר. מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל בֶּן אַרְזָה מַקִּישׁ בַּצִּלְצֵל. מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל הֶחָלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:7 **From Jericho** the people **would hear** the **sound** indicating **that the large gate** had been **opened. From Jericho** the people **would hear** the **sound** produced in the Temple by the instrument that had the form of **a shovel. From Jericho** the people **would hear the sound of the song** of the Levites in the Temple. **From Jericho** the people **would hear the sound of ben Arza clashing the cymbals** in the Temple. **From Jericho** the people **would hear the sound of the flute** that was played in the Temple twelve days each year.

*MiYericho hayu shomein kol sha'ar hagadol sheniftach* — from *Yericho*, they could hear the sound of the great gate opening. The distance from *Yerushalayim* to *Yericho* is approximately seventeen miles, much of it open desert through which sound could travel and echo through the valleys. The *Gemara* never applies the term *guzma* — exaggeration — to this claim, and treats it as literal.

### Temple Sounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל גְּבִינִי כָּרוֹז. מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל הָעֵץ שֶׁעָשָׂה בֶּן קָטִין מוּכְנִי לַכִּיּוֹר. מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל הַשִּׁיר. מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ שׁוֹמְעִין קוֹל הַשּׁוֹפָר. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים אַף קוֹלוֹ שֶׁל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהוּא מַזְכִּיר אֶת הַשֵּׁם בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **From Jericho** the people **would hear the voice of Gevini** the Temple **crier,** who would proclaim in the Temple each day: Arise, priests, to your service, and Levites to your platform, and Israelites to your non-priestly watch. **From Jericho** the people **would hear the sound of the wood that ben Katin crafted** into **a mechanism** of pulleys **for the Basin. From Jericho** the people **would hear the sound of the song** of the Levites in the Temple. **From Jericho** the people **would hear the sound of the shofar** that was sounded several times each day in the Temple. **And some say** that in Jericho the people would hear **even the voice of the High Priest at the moment that he mentioned the** ineffable **name** of God **on Yom Kippur.**

The *mishnah* continues with an extended catalogue of sounds and even a scent, each heard or detected all the way to *Yericho*. *MiYericho hayu shomein kol magreifa* — they heard the sound of the *magreifa*, one of the musical instruments played by the *Leviyim*. According to some textual variants, they also heard *kol hashir* — the actual singing of the *Leviyim*, though this appears in some *girsaot* and not others. *MiYericho hayu shomein kol Ben Arza makish betziltzal* — they heard the sound of *Ben Arza* striking the *tziltzal*, a percussion instrument. *MiYericho hayu shomein kol hachalil* — they heard the flute, which was played on the twelve occasions of *Hallel* in the year. *MiYericho hayu shomein kol Gevini Karoz* — they heard the voice or sound of *Gevini Karoz*, apparently another musical instrument or trumpeter. *MiYericho hayu shomein kol ha'etz she'asa Ben Katin mechona laki'or* — they heard the sound of the wooden mechanical contraption constructed by *Ben Katin* to lower the *ki'or* into the ground overnight so that the water within it would not become *pasul bilina*, invalidated by remaining stationary overnight. *MiYericho hayu shomein kol shir* — the sound of song. *MiYericho haya shomeia kol hashofar* — they heard the *shofar* blown at the time of the *korbanot*. And *veyesh omrim af kolo shel kohen gadol besha'ah shehu mazkir et HaShem beYom HaKippurim* — some say they could even hear the *kohen gadol* pronouncing the Divine Name on *Yom HaKippurim*, though others understand this not as the *kohen gadol*'s voice itself but the sound of the entire assembled congregation responding *baruch shem kevod malchuto l'olam va'ed*.

The rhythmic repetition of *MiYericho hayu* throughout this passage is striking — one wonders whether this formulation may have had some early liturgical character, similar to other repeated refrains found in early *tefillah* compositions.

### Incense Smell

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִירִיחוֹ הָיוּ מְרֵיחִין רֵיחַ פִּיטּוּם הַקְּטוֹרֶת. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן דִּגְלַאי: עִזִּים הָיוּ לְאַבָּא (בְּעָרֵי) [בְּהָרֵי] הַמִּכְווֹר, וְהָיוּ מִתְעַטְּשׁוֹת מֵרֵיחַ פִּיטּוּם הַקְּטוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **From Jericho** the people **would smell the fragrance** emanating from **the preparation of the incense** in the Temple. **Rabbi Elazar ben Diglai said: There were goats** belonging **to my father** that grazed **in the cities of Mikhvar,** located at a distance from Jerusalem, **and they would sneeze from the fragrance of the preparation of the incense** that they smelled.

*MiYericho hayu merichin reiach pitum haktoret* — from *Yericho* they could even smell the burning of the *ketoret*. *Rabbi Elazar ben Diglai* adds: his father had goats in the hills of *Michvar* — a distant location — and those goats would sneeze (*mitatshot*) from the smell of the *pitum haktoret*. This anecdote serves as independent testimony to how far the fragrance of the incense could travel, reinforcing the *mishnah*'s remarkable claim about the reach of the Temple's sights and sounds.

## Slaughter House Area

### Slaughter Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁזָּכָה בַּתָּמִיד – מְשָׁכוֹ וְהוֹלֵךְ בְּבֵית הַמִּטְבָּחַיִם. וּמִי שֶׁזָּכוּ בָּאֵבָרִים הוֹלְכִין עִמּוֹ. בֵּית הַמִּטְבָּחַיִם הָיָה לִצְפוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ, וְעָלָיו שְׁמוֹנָה עַמּוּדִים נַנָּסִים, וּרְבָעִים שֶׁל אֶרֶז עַל גַּבֵּיהֶן, וְאֻנְקְלָאוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל הָיוּ קְבוּעִין בָּהֶן, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה סְדָרִים לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, שֶׁבָּהֶן תּוֹלִין וּמַפְשִׁיטִין עַל שׁוּלְחָנוֹת שֶׁל שַׁיִשׁ שֶׁבֵּין הָעַמּוּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:8 The priest **who won** the lottery **to** slaughter **the daily offering pulled** the lamb, **and** he would **go to the slaughterhouse** to slaughter it as the daily offering. **And** the priests **who won** the right **to** take **the limbs** up to the ramp would **go with him. The slaughterhouse was to the north of the altar. Adjacent to it** there were **eight low** stone **pillars. And cedarwood squares** were affixed **upon them, and iron hooks were fixed in** the wooden squares. **And** there were **three rows** of hooks **on each and every one** of those wooden squares, **upon which** the priests would **suspend** the animal after it was slaughtered. **And they** would **flay** the animal's hide **onto marble tables that** were positioned **between the pillars.**

The *mishnah* turns next to the group that merited the *tamid* itself. *Mi shezacha batamid meshacho vehalach leBeit Hamitbachayim* — the one who won the *tamid* took the sheep and went to the *Beit Hamitbachayim*, the slaughter area, together with *umi shezachu ba'evarim holchim imo* — the *kohanim* who would deal with the limbs went along with him. The assumption is that those assigned to bring the limbs were also the ones who retrieved the sheep from the *lishkat hatla'im*.

*Beit Hamitbachayim hayu tzefono shel mizbe'ach* — the slaughter area was to the north of the *mizbeach*. This is consistent with the requirement of *tzafona lifnei Hashem* — that *kodshei kodashim*, including the *tamid*, had to be slaughtered to the north of the *mizbeach*. The area also contained marble tables for flaying, dismembering, and washing out the intestines, all of which took place in this same space.

### Pillars & Hooks

*Ve'alav shemonah amudim nanasim* — over this area stood eight miniature pillars, short columns, each topped with *revi'im shel erez* — square frames of cedar wood. *Ve'onkla'ot shel barzel hayu kevu'im bahem* — iron hooks were fixed into these cedar frames. *Ve'gimmel sedarim lechol echad ve'echad* — three rows of hooks for each pillar. *Uvahem tolim umafshitim* — on these the *korbanot* were suspended and flayed.

The reason for three rows of hooks on each frame is likely practical: different animals — sheep, goats, and cattle — vary considerably in size, and a single fixed height would not serve all of them. An adjustable arrangement, or at least multiple levels, would accommodate the range of animals brought.

### Marble Tables

*Al shulchanot shel shayish shebein ha'amudim* — between the pillars were marble tables. These are where the flaying and dismembering took place, and where the intestines were washed out. The relationship between the pillars and the tables — whether interspersed or simply in proximity — is illustrated by the layout of the area itself.

## Altar & Menorah Clean

### Altar Ashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁזָּכָה בְּדִישּׁוּן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי – נִכְנַס וְנָטַל אֶת הַטְּנִי, וְהִנִּיחוֹ לְפָנָיו, וְהָיָה חוֹפֵן וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹכוֹ. וּבָאַחֲרוֹנָה, כִּיבֵּד אֶת הַשְּׁאָר לְתוֹכוֹ, וְהִנִּיחוֹ וְיָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA 3:9 The priest **who won** the right **of the removal of ash from the inner altar entered** through the Sanctuary gate, **and** he **took the basket** with him **and placed it before him** on the floor between him and the altar. **And** he **would take handfuls** of ashes from upon the altar **and place** them **in** the basket. **Ultimately,** when only a small amount of ashes remained on the altar, **the priest swept the rest into** the basket, **and placed** the basket back on the Sanctuary floor, **and emerged** from the Sanctuary.

The *mishnah* now addresses the actual performance of the *dishun mizbeach hapnimi*. *Mi shezacha bidishun mizbeach hapnimi nichnas venatal et hateni* — the one who merited the cleaning of the inner *mizbeach* entered and took his gold basket. *Vehinicho lefanav* — he set it down on the floor before him. *Vechofen venoten letocho* — and then, remarkably, he would scoop the ashes off the top of the inner *mizbeach* with his bare hands, placing them into the basket. The choice to use hands rather than an implement is not explained; it is simply the practice.

*Uva'acharono kibed et hash'ar letocho* — and at the end, having scooped what he could by hand, he would sweep the remainder into the basket. Though the *mishnah* describes him as carrying only four vessels with no explicit mention of a brush, he apparently had some means of sweeping the residue. *Vehinicho veyatza* — and he left the basket where it was and exited. The process was not yet complete; as with the *menorah*, the *dishun* of the inner *mizbeach* was performed in stages.

### Menorah Candles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁזָּכָה בְּדִישּׁוּן הַמְּנוֹרָה, נִכְנַס וּמָצָא שְׁנֵי נֵרוֹת מִזְרָחִית מַעֲרָבִית דּוֹלְקִין – מְדַשֵּׁן אֶת הַשְּׁאָר, וּמַנִּיחַ אֶת אֵלּוּ דּוֹלְקִין בִּמְקוֹמָן. מְצָאָן שֶׁכָּבוּ – מְדַשְּׁנָן וּמַדְלִיקָן מִן הַדּוֹלְקִין, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְדַשֵּׁן אֶת הַשְּׁאָר.
>
> **Translation:** The priest **who won** the right **of the removal of ash from the Candelabrum entered** the Sanctuary. **And** if **he found** in the Candelabrum the **two easternmost lamps**, the second of which is called the **western** lamp, still **burning,** he would first **remove the ashes** and the burned wicks **from the rest** of the lamps and place them in the jug, and place new wicks and oil in those lamps. **And** he would **leave these** two lamps **burning in their** own **place.** If **he found that** the two easternmost lamps **were extinguished,** he would **remove the ashes** and the burned wicks from **them and kindle them from the** lamps that were still **burning.** If none were still burning, he would kindle them from the fire on the outer altar. **And afterward,** the priest would **remove the ashes** and the wicks **from the rest** of the lamps.

*Mi shezacha bedishun hamenorah nichnas umatza shtei nerot ma'araviyot dolkot* — the one who merited the cleaning of the *menorah* entered and found the two western candles burning. The question of which candles are designated as the *nerot ma'araviyot* is itself a matter of debate. If the *menorah* was oriented along an east-west axis, then the most natural reading is that the westernmost lamp — the one facing the *kodesh kodashim* — was the *ner ma'aravi*. Others hold that the *menorah* was oriented differently and that the *ner ma'aravi* was the central shaft, which faced west. Still others read the *mishnah* as referring to the easternmost pair, with the *ma'aravi* being the one that was west relative to the outermost candle. The *mishnah*'s own language — two western candles — is at the heart of this discussion.

### Two Stages

The *mishnah* specifies that the *dishun hamenorah* was performed in two stages. The *kohen* would clean out five of the seven lamps, then leave two burning — or make them burning if they had gone out — and depart. The two-stage process is derived from *psukim*: *yaarchenu hakohen*, repeated twice in the Torah, implies that the attending *kohen* would arrange the *menorah* in two separate acts, each involving multiple lamps. *Medashen et hash'ar* — he cleans the remaining five. *Umeniach et elu dolkot bimcoman* — and he leaves these two burning in their place.

If those two had already gone out — *matza shekavu* — then *medashnan umadlikan min hadolkim* — he cleans them and relights them from whatever lamps were still burning, *ve'achar kach medashen et hash'ar* — and only then proceeds to clean the others. The two designated lamps must be lit before the remaining five are extinguished, so that the two-stage sequence is preserved in all circumstances.

### Stone & Steps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶבֶן הָיְתָה לִפְנֵי הַמְּנוֹרָה, וּבָהּ שָׁלֹשׁ מַעֲלוֹת שֶׁעָלֶיהָ כֹּהֵן עוֹמֵד וּמֵטִיב אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת, (וּמַנִּיחַ) [הִנִּיחַ] אֶת הַכּוּז עַל מַעֲלָה שְׁנִיָּה, וְיָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And there was a stone in front of the Candelabrum and in it there** were **three stairs upon which** the **priest** would **stand and prepare the lamps** for kindling. Since the Candelabrum was eighteen handbreadths high, it was necessary for the priest to stand on an elevated surface to reach the lamps. **And** after he placed the ashes and the wicks from the five westernmost lamps in the jug, he would **place the jug on** the **second stair** of that stone, **and** then he **emerged** from the Sanctuary. When the priest later returned to prepare the two easternmost lamps for kindling, he would remove the jug with the ashes from the Candelabrum, and together with the priest removing the basket with the ashes from the inner altar would pour the ashes from the jug and the basket at the side of the altar.

*Ve'even hayta lifnei hamenorah* — there was a stone in front of the *menorah*, *uvah shlosha maalot* — with three steps cut into it. *She'aleha kohen omed umeti'iv et hanerot* — the *kohen* would stand on these steps to service the lamps, since the *menorah* stood at a height that required elevation for proper access, particularly for cleaning and wick replacement.

### Pitcher & Exit

*Umaniach et hakuz al ma'ala shniya* — he placed the pitcher — the vessel he carried for this purpose — on the second step. Into this pitcher went the old wicks and whatever residue had been cleaned from the lamps, in the same way the basket received the ashes from the inner *mizbeach*. *Veyatza* — and he exited. As with the *dishun mizbeach hapnimi*, the process was left in an intermediate state: two lamps burning, five cleaned, and a return visit yet to come. The continuation of these final steps will be addressed at our next session.
