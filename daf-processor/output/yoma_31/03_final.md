# Yoma 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mikvah Requirements

### Two Models

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאַחוֹלֵי עֲבוֹדָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. לְבֶן זוֹמָא, מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה. לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא מַחֵיל עֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is **with regard to** whether the Temple **service is desecrated** and disqualified if the priest failed to immerse before its performance that **they disagree.** According **to** the opinion of **ben Zoma,** this immersion is for the purpose of sanctification and is an integral part of the service; consequently, if the priest failed to immerse **he desecrates the service.** According **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda he does not desecrate** the **service,** because the immersion is merely a precautionary measure.

The daf opens at the very bottom of 30b, continuing the discussion of why a normal person is required to immerse in a *mikvah* before entering the *Beit HaMikdash*. Two explanations have been proposed: one understands the immersion as a ritual preparation for entering the sacred space, learned from the model of the *Kohen Gadol*; the other treats it as a *serach tvilah* — a reminder that one might be *tamei*. As was noted earlier, these two models parallel the two explanations offered for why the *Kohen Gadol* must wait seven days before Yom Kippur.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף (לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: סֶרֶךְ) טְבִילָה (הִיא) זוֹ, חוֹצֵץ, אוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל דְּתַקּוּן רַבָּנַן — כְּעֵין דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא תַּקּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** According to **Rabbi Yehuda, who said** the immersion is not an actual obligation but **it is an ancillary immersion** to remind the individual of old impurity, do items that are generally considered interpositions that invalidate an immersion **interpose** in this immersion, **or** do they **not interpose,** since the immersion is only ancillary and serves as a reminder? Rav Yosef **said to him: All** ordinances **that the Sages instituted, they instituted parallel to Torah law.** Therefore, this immersion by rabbinic law has the same basic parameters as immersion by Torah law. There may be no interposition.

The *Gemara* now records an exchange between Abaye and Rav Yosef. Abaye poses a pointed question: according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the immersion is merely a *serach tvilah* — a precautionary reminder of potential *tumah* — does this *tvilah* carry the full weight of a normal immersion? Specifically, does it require the absence of a *chatzitzah*, an intervening substance? Since the immersion accomplishes nothing in terms of actual *taharah* and serves only as a reminder, one might argue that the ordinary laws of *tvilah* should not apply to it.

### Rabbinic Model

Rav Yosef responds: *kol d'tikun Rabbanan k'ein d'oraita tikun* — whatever the Rabbis enacted, they established in the form of the biblical model. There is a further elegance to this principle in the present context: if the immersion is meant to remind a person that he might be *tamei*, then the more he treats it as a genuine *tvilah* — going through the full procedure as though he were truly becoming *tahor* — the more effectively it will serve that very function.

## Partial Entry

### Thumbs Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: בִּיאָה בְּמִקְצָת, שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה אוֹ לָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּהוֹנוֹת יוֹכִיחוּ, שֶׁהֵן בִּיאָה בְּמִקְצָת, וְתַנְיָא: מְצוֹרָע טוֹבֵל וְעוֹמֵד בְּשַׁעַר נִיקָנוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef:** Is **partial entry,** when one enters a certain place with only part of his body, **considered entry or not?** Rav Yosef **said to him: The thumbs** of the leper **will prove** this point, **as** the leper reaching his thumbs into the Temple **constitutes partial entry, and it was taught** in a *baraita*: **A leper immerses and stands at the Gate of Nicanor,** indicating that immersion is required before even partial entry.

Abaye then raises a second question to Rav Yosef: *biah b'miktzat shema biah o lo* — if a person only partially enters the *Beit HaMikdash*, does that constitute an "entry" for legal purposes? This is a broader question about the laws of *tumah* as well — a *tamei* person who enters the *Beit HaMikdash* is *chayav mitah b'yedei shamayim*, and the question of whether partial entry triggers that liability has practical implications. A related contemporary example involves placing objects in the crevices of the *Kotel*: if the *Har HaBayit* begins at the outer edge of the *Kotel* wall, then inserting one's finger into a niche may constitute partial entry into the zone of the *Har HaBayit*, raising concerns about *tumah hayotzet migufo*, such as *ba'al keri* or *niddah*.

In the present context, however, the question bears specifically on the newly-enacted requirement that every person immerse in a *mikvah* before entering the *azarah*. If a person intends only to insert a finger into the *azarah* — only a *biah b'miktzat* — must he first go to the *mikvah*?

### Metzora Precedent

Rav Yosef responds: *be-hanach yochi'u* — let the case of the *metzora* serve as proof. The *metzora*, who is a *mechusar kippurim* lacking the final *korban* required to complete his purification, is not permitted to enter the *azarah*. Yet the Torah's process requires him to stick his thumbs into the *azarah* for the placement of blood and oil. And the *Baraisa* teaches: *metzora tovel v'omed b'Sha'ar Nikanor* — the *metzora* must immerse in a *mikvah* before entering, even though he is not truly entering, only inserting his thumbs. Rabbi Yehuda's dissent is not over whether the immersion is required, but only over timing, since he holds the *metzora* immersed the previous day. Everyone agrees that the *tvilah* is required even for this partial entry.

There is an ironic quality to this ruling worth pausing over: for the actual laws of *tumah v'taharah*, the *metzora* may insert his thumbs into the *azarah* even though he is technically prohibited from entering in full, because that partial entry is the very mechanism of his purification process. Yet for the separate rabbinic requirement of *mikvah* before entry, even this minimal, thumb-only entry suffices to trigger the obligation.

## Avodah Without Entry

### Shechitah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה סַכִּין אֲרוּכָּה וְיִשְׁחוֹט?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the *halakha*; may an impure person **craft** a very **long knife and slaughter** an animal in the Temple courtyard while remaining outside the courtyard?

The *Gemara* now raises a further question: *mahu she-yaaseh sakin aruchah v'yishchot* — what is the law if a person fashions a long knife and performs *shechitah* on a *korban* in the *azarah* while standing outside it? This is a theoretical scenario — the practical difficulties of controlling a knife at such a distance are considerable — but its legal dimensions are clarifying. The underlying principle is that the *korban* must be *lifnei Hashem*, but this requirement applies to the *korban*, not necessarily to the *shochet* performing the act. We have two ways of testing the outer limits of the *mikvah* requirement: first, a person who enters only partially (*biah b'miktzat*) — only his thumbs cross the threshold; second, a person who performs an *avodah* in the *Mikdash* without entering at all.

This second question is framed about a *tahor* person who has not yet gone to the *mikvah*. *Tosafot* had noted that the actual model of the *Kohen Gadol*, from which the requirement is derived, involves not merely entering the *Mikdash* but entering to perform the *avodah*. One dimension of that model was carved off to establish that even mere entry, without *avodah*, requires *tvilah*. Now the question runs in the other direction: if one performs the *avodah* without entering, does the requirement apply?

### Ben Zoma View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְבֶן זוֹמָא, תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן דִּפְלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. תִּבְּעֵי לְבֶן זוֹמָא: עַד כָּאן לָא מְחַיֵּיב בֶּן זוֹמָא אֶלָּא לְגַוַּאי, אֲבָל לְבָרַאי — לָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא, אָתֵי לְאִימְּשׁוֹכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Raise the dilemma** according **to ben Zoma,** who rules stringently with regard to immersion; and **raise the dilemma** according **to the Rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda** and do not obligate all people in this immersion. The Gemara elaborates: **Raise the dilemma** according **to ben Zoma:** Does **ben Zoma require** immersion **only for** entry **inside** the courtyard; **however, for** standing **outside** the courtyard, **no,** he does not require immersion? **Or perhaps** even for one standing outside the courtyard ben Zoma requires immersion, **lest he** inadvertently **come to be drawn** inside in the course of his service.

The *Gemara* structures the question in two ways, depending on which opinion one follows. *Ibaya lehu le-Ben Zoma* — according to Ben Zoma, who holds that the requirement is derived from the *Kohen Gadol* and applies to everyone who enters, one might say: *ad kan lo mechayev Ben Zoma ela le-gavai* — Ben Zoma's requirement extends only to actual entry inward. If one remains outside, *le-barai*, there is no requirement. Or one might argue the opposite: *ati le-imshuchi* — we are concerned that once a person is performing the *shechitah* and is already engaged with the *avodah*, he may be drawn to step across the line into the *azarah*. Notably, the *Gemara* frames this not as a formal legal equivalence between *avodah* and entry, but as a practical *chashash* — a concern about being drawn in.

### Rabbanan Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן דִּפְלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם, דְּלָא קָא עָבֵיד עֲבוֹדָה, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּקָא עָבֵיד עֲבוֹדָה — לָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא שְׁנָא. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **raise the dilemma** according **to the Rabbis, who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda:** How so? Do **the Rabbis say** that there is no requirement of immersion **only there, where he is not performing** any Temple **service; however, here, where he is performing service, no,** they would require immersion? **Or perhaps there is no difference,** and they would not require immersion under any circumstances. The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The same question is then asked according to the *Rabbanan* who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda — that is, those who hold that normal people do not require *mikvah* immersion before entering the *Mikdash* at all. According to them, *ad kan lo ka-amri Rabbanan hatam* — the Rabbis only said that a normal person entering the *Mikdash* without performing an *avodah* need not immerse, *de-lo ka avid avodah*. But here, *de-ka avid avodah*, where the person is performing an *avodah*, perhaps even the Rabbis would concede that a *tvilah* is required. Or perhaps, *lo shena* — it makes no difference, and even here the Rabbis would not require it. The *Gemara* leaves the matter as a *teiku*, unresolved.

The discussion thus maps neatly onto the competing rationales introduced earlier in the *Gemara*. Is the *mikvah* requirement about preparing to enter the sacred space — in which case performing *avodah* from the outside without entering should not matter? Or is it about something intrinsic to the *avodah* itself — in which case even long-distance *shechitah* might trigger the requirement? This debate also parallels the earlier parallel: just as the *Kohen Gadol*'s seven-day waiting period was explained either as preparation for entry into the space (derived from *Har Sinai*) or as preparation for performing the *avodah* (derived from the *miluim*), so too here the question of whether *avodah* alone — without entry — requires *tvilah* maps onto those same two frameworks.

## Five Immersions

### Teaching Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָמֵשׁ טְבִילוֹת וַעֲשָׂרָה קִידּוּשִׁין טוֹבֵל. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חָמֵשׁ טְבִילוֹת וַעֲשָׂרָה קִידּוּשִׁין טוֹבֵל כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל וּמְקַדֵּשׁ בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם, וְכוּלָּן בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ בְּבֵית הַפַּרְוָה, חוּץ מֵרִאשׁוֹנָה שֶׁהָיְתָה בַּחוֹל עַל גַּבֵּי שַׁעַר הַמַּיִם, וּבְצַד לִשְׁכָּתוֹ הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **Five immersions and ten sanctifications** the **High Priest immerses** and sanctifies his hands and feet, respectively. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Five immersions and ten sanctifications** the **High Priest immerses and sanctifies** his hands and feet, respectively, **on the day** of Yom Kippur. **And all** of these immersions and sanctifications take place **in the sacred** area, the Temple courtyard, **in** the **Hall of *Parva*, except for** this **first** immersion, **which was in the non-sacred** area **on the roof of Gate of the Water, and** that gate **was alongside his chamber.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the detailed account of the *Kohen Gadol*'s five *mikvaot* and ten hand-washings throughout Yom Kippur. A *Baraisa* teaches: *chamesh tevilot va'asarah kiddushin tovel Kohen Gadol umekadesh bo bayom* — the *Kohen Gadol* immerses five times and washes his hands ten times on that day. All of these, the *Baraisa* continues, took place *bekodesh* — in the sanctified space — on top of the *Beit HaParvah* chamber. The sole exception is the first immersion: *chutz merishonah she'asah bechol* — the first one was performed in a non-sanctified location, because one must go to the *mikvah* before entering the sanctified space in the first place.

### First Immersion

The first immersion took place *al gabi Sha'ar HaMayim* — on top of the Water Gate — and *uvetzad lishkato hayitah* — next to his chamber, where he had been residing for the preceding week. This is entirely fitting: we have already discussed at length the question of where the *Kohen Gadol*'s chamber was situated — whether it opened into sanctified or non-sanctified space — and it stands to reason that the *mikvah* he used before entering for the first time would be in close proximity to where he had been staying, located in *chol*, before he began the *avodah*.

### Ein Eitam Spring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּיהּ עֵין עֵיטָם גָּבוֹהַּ מִקַּרְקַע עֲזָרָה עֶשְׂרִים וְשָׁלֹשׁ אַמּוֹת. דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַפְּתָחִים שֶׁהָיוּ שָׁם גּוֹבְהָן עֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה וְרׇחְבָּן עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, חוּץ מִשֶּׁל אוּלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Conclude from that** which was taught in this *baraita* that ***Ein Eitam*,** the spring from which water was supplied to the Temple, was **twenty-three cubits higher than the ground of the Temple courtyard.** **As we learned** in a mishna: **All the entrances that were there** in the Temple, **their height is twenty cubits and their width is ten cubits, except for** the entrance **to the Entrance Hall** of the Sanctuary, which was twice that, i.e., forty cubits high and twenty cubits wide.

Abaye draws an inference from the placement of this *mikvah*: *shema minah she'Ein Eitam gavoha mikarka azarah esrim veshalosh amot* — we may deduce that the *Ein Eitam* spring, which was understood to be the source that fed water to the *Mikdash* and its *mikveh*, was situated twenty-three *amot* higher than the floor of the *azarah*. The reasoning proceeds from a *Mishnah*: all the gates of the *Beit HaMikdash* were twenty *amot* tall and ten *amot* wide, except for the gates of the *Ulam*. The *mikvah* was built on top of the *Sha'ar HaMayim*, which was twenty *amot* high; the *mikvah* itself adds another three *amot* of depth. Therefore the top of the *mikvah* stands twenty-three *amot* above the ground of the *azarah*, and the spring feeding it must be at least that high in order for the water to flow down by gravity.

Modern archaeological research has confirmed the existence of this aqueduct. The *Ein Eitam* spring is located at an altitude of approximately seven hundred and sixty meters above sea level — roughly twenty-five meters higher than the Temple Mount — and a winding aqueduct of approximately twenty kilometers carried water from this spring northward to Jerusalem, passing through tunnels between the mountains. Remnants of this aqueduct have been uncovered. The aqueduct was destroyed at the time of the destruction of the Temple but was later restored.

One might ask how the spring's water, originating far south past *Beit Lechem*, could have been carried uphill to the *Mikdash* on the summit of the mountain. The answer is that at some point the aqueduct must have been elevated above the ground — a Roman-style overground aqueduct — so as to maintain the necessary gradient for gravity flow. This raises interesting questions about the laws of *she'uvin* (drawn water), which can invalidate a *mikveh*: does water carried in an aqueduct constitute "drawn" water? Much depends on whether the aqueduct is considered a *kli* (a vessel with a *beit kibul*), and whether there is direct human agency involved. If the aqueduct is made of stone, as appears to have been the case, then it is not halachically a *kli* and the concern of *she'uvin* would not apply. Similar questions arise in the laws of our own *mikvaot*, where rainwater is channeled from rooftops through pipes — the same principles of agency and *kli* status are operative.

### Mikvah Size

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: ״וְרָחַץ בְּשָׂרוֹ בְּמַיִם״ — בְּמֵי מִקְוֶה, ״כׇּל בְּשָׂרוֹ״ — מַיִם שֶׁכׇּל גּוּפוֹ עוֹלֶה בָּהֶן. וְכַמָּה הֵן? אַמָּה עַל אַמָּה בְּרוּם שָׁלֹשׁ אַמּוֹת. וְשִׁיעֲרוּ חֲכָמִים מֵי מִקְוֶה אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught** in a *baraita* that it is stated: **"And he shall wash his flesh in water"** (Leviticus 15:13), and it is stated in another verse: "And he shall wash all of his flesh in water"(Leviticus 15:16). This refers to water known as **the waters of a ritual bath.** The verse adds the emphasis: **All of his flesh,** to say that immersion must be **in water that one's entire body enters** at the same time. **And how much** water **is it?** It is **one cubit** length **by one cubit** width **by a height of three cubits.** That is sufficient water to cover a person's height. **And the Sages calculated** that the volume of **the waters of a ritual bath** that fit into that space is **forty *se'a*.**

The *Gemara* now derives the minimum dimensions of a *mikvah* from the verse discussed in connection with the *Kohen Gadol*: *verachatz et besaro bamayim* — he shall bathe his flesh in water, interpreted as *bemei mikvah* — in *mikvah* water. The verse specifies *kol besaro* — his entire flesh — from which the law is derived: *mayim shekol gufo oleh bahem* — the *mikvah* must contain enough water that his entire body can be submerged. The *Gemara* quantifies this as *ama al ama b'rom shalosh amot* — one *amah* by one *amah* by three *amot* in height, which yields the standard *shiur* of forty *seah*.

*Tosafot* raises a pointed difficulty: three *amot* is approximately four and a half feet, and even accounting for the fact that people were shorter in antiquity, this measurement reaches only about shoulder height, not the full height of a person. How, then, can a one-by-one-by-three *mikvah* submerge one's entire body, including the head? *Tosafot* offers two answers. The first is straightforward: the person crouches and bends his knees. The second is more elegant: if the walls of the *mikvah* are of standard height, the displacement of water when a person enters raises the water level above shoulder height, sufficient to cover the head entirely. Both solutions converge on the practical result that a *mikvah* of these minimum dimensions does function as required.

As for the proof that the *mikvah* atop the *Sha'ar HaMayim* was built to exactly one-by-one-by-three dimensions — *Tosafot* acknowledges that one might ask why we should not assume it was built larger, like a bathtub. The inference in the *Gemara* proceeds on the assumption that the normal construction of a *mikvah* follows these minimum parameters, and on that basis the calculation yields twenty-three *amot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיכָּא אַמָּה תִּקְרָה וְאַמָּה מַעֲזִיבָה! שְׁעָרִים דְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, כֵּיוָן דְּשֵׁישָׁא נִינְהוּ, בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ עָבְדִי לְהוּ. וְהָא אִיכָּא הַאיְךְ מַשֶּׁהוּ! כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הֲוַאי אַמְּתָא — לָא חָשֵׁיב לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** As the gate is twenty cubits high, and the immersion chamber above it is at least three additional cubits high, the water was twenty-three cubits above the ground of the courtyard. The Gemara asks: How could it be only twenty-three cubits higher? **Isn't there the cubit of** the thickness of **the ceiling** over the gate **and the cubit of the** thickness of the **plaster** sealing of the ritual bath floor for a total of at least twenty-five cubits? The Gemara answers: With regard to **the Temple gates, since they were** constructed **of marble, they constructed them with minimal** thickness. Neither the thickness of the ceiling nor the thickness of the plaster was a cubit. The Gemara asks: **But isn't there that minimal** thickness of the ceiling and the plaster that would nevertheless total more than twenty-three cubits? The Gemara answers: Indeed; however, **since** the ceiling **was not** a whole **cubit he does not consider it** in his calculation.

The *Gemara* then addresses a potential objection: surely the *mikvah* could not begin immediately at the top of the gate — one needs some thickness of roof and a layer of plaster before building a water-retaining structure. If so, the *mikvah* would be higher than twenty-three *amot*, and the spring would need to be correspondingly higher. The *Gemara* responds: *sha'arei d'Beit HaMikdash keivan d'shaish ninhu* — the gates of the *Beit HaMikdash* were made of marble, and being a strong, relatively non-porous material, only a minimal thickness (*b'mashu*) was required before construction could begin. Since this additional amount was less than a full *amah*, the *Gemara* rounds to the nearest *amah* and the figure of twenty-three *amot* stands.

There is also a broader question raised in passing: Jerusalem had its own water sources predating the *Ein Eitam* aqueduct, which were presumably already serving the *Mikdash*. It is possible that the aqueduct supplemented an existing supply as the needs of the *Mikdash* grew. The matter requires further investigation.

### Linen Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּרְסוּ סָדִין שֶׁל בּוּץ וְכוּ׳. מַאי שְׁנָא שֶׁל בּוּץ? כִּדְאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּכִּיר שֶׁעֲבוֹדַת הַיּוֹם בְּבִגְדֵי בוּץ. הָכָא נָמֵי: שֶׁיַּכִּיר שֶׁעֲבוֹדַת הַיּוֹם בְּבִגְדֵי בוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** § We learned in the mishna: **They spread a sheet of fine linen** between the High Priest and the people in the interest of modesty. The Gemara asks: **What is different** that they specifically used a sheet **of fine linen?** The Gemara answers: It is **as Rav Kahana said** with regard to a related issue: **So that he will be aware that the service of the day** of Yom Kippur is performed **in fine linen garments. Here, too,** a linen sheet was used **so that he will be aware that the service of the day** of Yom Kippur is performed **in fine linen garments.**

Before the *Kohen Gadol* actually entered the *mikvah*, a linen garment (*sadin shel butz*) was spread between him and the assembled people, providing privacy since the immersion took place on a rooftop. The *Gemara* asks: *mai shna shel butz* — why specifically linen? Rav Kahana answers: *kedei sheyakir she'avodat hayom b'vigdei butz* — so that he should recognize, or acknowledge, that the special *avodah* of Yom Kippur is performed in linen garments. Half the *avodos* of the day are performed in the ordinary *bigdei zahav*, the gold priestly garments, while those related specifically to Yom Kippur — the *avodot* of the inner sanctum — are performed in linen. The same explanation is later applied to the linen garments used at subsequent changes of clothing during the day.

It is worth pausing over the language used: the text does not say merely "to remind him" but rather *sheyakir* — that he should recognize and acknowledge. One might read this not as a concern that he will forget his garments, as *Tosafot* understands, but rather as a symbolic gesture: if any single fabric is to mark the day, it is linen, because linen is what defines the *avodah* of Yom Kippur. The linen curtain thus underscores the sanctity of the occasion even before the *avodah* begins.

### Immersion Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פָּשַׁט יָרַד וְטָבַל, עָלָה וְנִסְתַּפָּג. הֵבִיאוּ לוֹ בִּגְדֵי זָהָב, וְלָבַשׁ, וְקִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו. הֵבִיאוּ לוֹ אֶת הַתָּמִיד, קְרָצוֹ וּמֵירַק אַחֵר שְׁחִיטָה עַל יָדוֹ. קִבֵּל אֶת הַדָּם וּזְרָקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The High Priest **removed** the white garments that he was wearing, **descended** to the ritual bath, **and immersed. He ascended and dried himself** with a towel. Then **they brought him** the **golden garments** of the High Priest, **and he dressed** in the garments, and **he sanctified his hands and his feet. They brought him the** sheep for **the daily** morning **offering, which he slaughtered** by cutting most of the way through the gullet and the windpipe. **And a different** priest **completed** the **slaughter on his behalf** so that the High Priest could receive the blood in a vessel and proceed with the order of the Yom Kippur service. As soon as the slaughter was completed, the High Priest **received the blood** in a vessel **and sprinkled it** on the altar.

The process itself is described concisely: *pashat yarad v'taval* — he stripped off his garments, descended, and immersed. *Ala v'nistapeig* — he arose and dried himself. *Hevi'u lo bigdei zahav* — they brought him the gold garments. *V'lavash* — he dressed. *V'kidesh yadav v'raglav* — and he washed his hands and feet.

## Hand Washing Debate

### Post-Mikvah Sequence

After the first immersion, the *Kohen Gadol* dressed in the *bigdei zahav* and performed *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*. It is notable that at this first stage the *Mishnah* mentions only one hand-washing — after dressing — and not one before the immersion. This requires explanation, because the total of ten washings must be accounted for across the five changes of garments. The key is that each of the ten washings relates to either putting on or taking off a set of *bigdei kodesh*, not to the *bigdei chol* at the outset. The five *mikvaot* fall between the transitions, and the washings bracket the *bigdei kodesh* before and after — but not the initial transition from weekday clothing. Working through the sequence — *chol*, *zahav*, *butz*, *zahav*, *butz*, *zahav*, then back to weekday garments at the end — the ten washings distribute across the five pairs of dressing and undressing in *kodesh* garments.

### Shechitah Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְנַס לְהַקְטִיר קְטוֹרֶת שֶׁל שַׁחַר, וּלְהֵיטִיב אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת, וּלְהַקְרִיב אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְאֶת הָאֵבָרִים וְהַחֲבִיתִּין וְאֶת הַיַּיִן. קְטוֹרֶת שֶׁל שַׁחַר הָיְתָה קְרֵיבָה בֵּין דָּם לְאֵבָרִים, שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם בֵּין אֵיבָרִים לִנְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **He entered** the Sanctuary **to burn the morning incense and to remove** the ashes from **the lamps** of the candelabrum; **and** he left the Sanctuary and returned to the courtyard **to sacrifice the head and the limbs** of the sheep sacrificed for the daily offering **and the** High Priest's daily **griddle-cake** offering, **and the wine** libation of the daily offering. The Mishna comments: **The morning incense was burned between** the receiving and sprinkling of the **blood** and the burning **of the limbs;** and the **afternoon** incense was burned **between** the taking of the **limbs** up to the altar and the pouring of the **libations** that accompanied the offering.

After dressing in *bigdei zahav* and performing *kiddush yadayim v'raglayim*, the *Kohen Gadol* was brought the *korban tamid*. The *Gemara* uses the word *sirzo* to describe his performance of the *shechitah* — an unusual term that the *Gemara* will analyze. *Umeirek achar shechitah al yado* — another *kohen* completed the *shechitah* for him. He performed enough of the *shechitah* to be *yotzei*, and another *kohen* finished the act.

This arrangement is initially puzzling — it seems more time-consuming to transfer the animal mid-*shechitah* than to simply complete the act. The answer is that the *Kohen Gadol* had to quickly shift in order to receive the *dam*, the blood. It was not a question of time in the abstract; he needed to be the one to perform *kabbolas hadam*, and for that he had to relinquish the animal immediately. One might ask whether pausing mid-*shechitah* constitutes a *shehiyah* that would invalidate it. The answer is that since *rov* — the majority — of the *shechitah* was already completed and he was already *yotzei*, any subsequent action is not halachically part of the *shechitah* itself; one could, at that point, sever the head entirely without affecting the validity of the act. There is an interesting *Rashi* that discusses whether completing the cut after *rov* and after a *pesul* has occurred might retroactively affect the *shechitah*, but that question remains separate from the present case.

### Blood Handling

After the *shechitah*, the *Kohen Gadol* received the blood — *kibel et hadam* — and performed *zerikah*, throwing the blood upon the altar. This follows directly from the need to hand off the animal: as soon as *rov* of the *shechitah* was complete, he transferred the animal to the other *kohen* and positioned himself to be *mekabel hadam*.

### Avodah Sequence

The specific *avodot* performed next are described in the continuation of the Mishna above. With the *dam* of the *tamid* having been thrown, the *Kohen Gadol* then entered to burn the morning incense, *ketoret shel shachar*, and to clean out the *menorah* lamps, *l'heitiv et hanerot*. He then brought the head and the other limbs of the *tamid* — *et harosh v'et ha'evarim* — to the ramp, along with the *chavitin*, the daily *minchah* of the *Kohen Gadol* offered half in the morning and half in the afternoon, and the wine libation, *minchat nesachim*.

The *Mishnah* then restates a general rule about the sequencing of the morning and afternoon *ketoret*: the morning *ketoret* was offered between the throwing of the *dam* of the *tamid* and the placing of the limbs — that is, immediately after *zerikah* and before the limbs went up. The afternoon *ketoret*, *shel bein ha'arbayim*, was offered between the limbs and the *nesachim*: after the limbs of the afternoon *tamid* were placed, then *ketoret*, then the wine and flour.

### Water Warming

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם הָיָה כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל זָקֵן אוֹ אִיסְטְנִיס מְחַמִּין לוֹ חַמִּין, וּמְטִילִין לְתוֹךְ הַצּוֹנֵן כְּדֵי שֶׁתָּפִיג צִינָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the immersion, **if the High Priest was old** and found it difficult to immerse in cold water, **or** if he was **delicate [*istenis*], they** would **heat hot** water **for him** on Yom Kippur eve **and place** it **into the cold** water of the ritual bath **in order to temper its chill** so the High Priest could immerse without discomfort.

At this point the *Gemara* doubles back, somewhat unexpectedly, to the topic of the *mikvah*: *im haya Kohen Gadol zaken o istinis* — if the *Kohen Gadol* was elderly or fastidious, *mechammemin lo chammin umatilin letoch hatzonen* — hot water would be heated and poured into the cold *mikvah* water *keday shetafig tzinatan* — to take the edge off the cold.

This ruling gives rise to two immediate questions. First, on *Yom Kippur* — how does one heat water without violating the prohibition of *bishul*? And second, does adding heated water affect the validity of the *mikvah* itself? On the second question: once a *mikvah* already contains the requisite *arbaim seah* of *kosher* water, adding *she'uvin* — drawn water, even heated drawn water — does not invalidate it. This is precisely the principle underlying the construction of contemporary *mikvaot*, where a *bor* of *mei geshamim* containing a *kosher mikvah* is connected through a small opening to a separate pool into which clean water is regularly added and changed. The valid *mikvah* confers its status upon the additional water, and the use of drawn water does not disqualify the combined body. The question of how the heating was done on *Yom Kippur* — given the prohibition against *bishul* and the special restrictions of the day — is deferred to the relevant *Gemara*.

### Washing Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: הָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר. דְּאִי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר תְּרֵי קִידּוּשֵׁי אַלְּבִישָׁה עָבֵיד לְהוּ — הָכָא נָמֵי לֶיעְבֵּיד תְּרֵי קִידּוּשֵׁי אַלְּבִישָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages said** this matter **before Rav Pappa: This** mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, since he said:** The High Priest **performs two sanctifications** of his hands and his feet **upon** each **change of garments,** once before donning the garments and once after donning them, **here too,** in the mishna, **let him perform two sanctifications upon the change of garments.** However, the description in the mishna indicates that he removes his garments and does not sanctify his hands and feet.

The students of Rav Pappa raise an objection: *ha d'lo k'Rabbi Meir* — this *Mishnah* cannot accord with Rabbi Meir. Their reasoning is as follows: Rabbi Meir holds, they claim, that one performs two washings in conjunction with putting on garments — one before and one after. If so, the *Mishnah* should have recorded two washings at the first dressing in *bigdei zahav*, not one. Since the *Mishnah* records only a single washing at that point, it cannot represent Rabbi Meir's view.

To understand this correctly, it is necessary to step back and lay out the full structure. The *Kohen Gadol*'s day involves alternating between garments in the sequence: *chol* → *zahav* → *butz* → *zahav* → *butz* → *zahav* → *chol*. Each transition involves going to the *mikvah*, and hand-washings bracket the transitions. The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Rabbanan* concerns the relationship between these hand-washings and the acts of dressing and undressing.

### Rav Pappa's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב פָּפָּא: בֵּין לְרַבָּנַן בֵּין לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר חַד אַפְּשִׁיטָה דְּבִגְדֵי קוֹדֶשׁ, וְחַד אַלְּבִישָׁה, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: ״וּפָשַׁט וְרָחַץ (וְרָחַץ) וְלָבַשׁ״, רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: מַקִּישׁ פְּשִׁיטָה לִלְבִישָׁה, מָה לְבִישָׁה — לוֹבֵשׁ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְקַדֵּשׁ, אַף פְּשִׁיטָה — פּוֹשֵׁט וְאַחַר כָּךְ מְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to them: Both** according **to** the opinion of **the Rabbis and** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** there is **one** sanctification **upon removal** of **sacred garments, and one** sanctification **upon donning** other sacred garments. **And here,** it is **with regard to this** issue that **they disagree.** It is written: **"And he shall remove** the linen garments that he put on when he entered the Sanctuary and leave them there. **And he shall wash** his flesh in water in a sacred place **and he shall put on** his garments" (Leviticus 16:23–24). **Rabbi Meir holds:** The Torah **juxtaposes removal** of garments **to donning** of garments for the following reason: **Just as** when **donning** the garments, **he dons the garments and afterward sanctifies** his hands and feet, **so too,** with regard to **removal** of the garments, **he removes** the garments **and afterward sanctifies** his hands and feet.

Rav Pappa rejects the students' framing entirely. *Bein Rabbanan bein Rabbi Meir*, he says — both the Rabbis and Rabbi Meir agree: *chad a-pshitah de-vigdei kodesh ve-chad a-levishah* — one washing relates to the taking off of *bigdei kodesh* and one washing relates to the putting on of *bigdei kodesh*. The entire debate between Rabbi Meir and the *Rabbanan* is not about which garments the washings relate to — both agree they relate to the *bigdei kodesh* being removed and donned — but about the precise timing of each washing relative to those acts.

The verse in Vayikra reads: *u-fashat et bigdei habad asher lavash be-vo'o el hakodesh ve-hini'cham sham verachatz et besaro bamayim umekadesh uvein ha'azarah v'lavash et begadav* — he shall remove the linen garments he wore when he entered the holy place, leave them there, bathe his body in water in a holy place, and put on his garments. The *Gemara* thus has: *u-fashat, rachatz, v'lavash* — take off, immerse, put on.

Rabbi Meir's principle is *makish pshitah le-levishah*: he draws an analogy between taking off and putting on. The same way that, when putting on the garments, the *Kohen* first dresses and then washes his hands — that is, the washing follows the putting on — so too when taking off the garments, he first removes them and then washes his hands. The washing after taking off comes *after* the act of undressing.

### Rabbanan Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מַקִּישׁ פְּשִׁיטָה לִלְבִישָׁה, מָה לְבִישָׁה — כְּשֶׁהוּא לָבוּשׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ, אַף פְּשִׁיטָה — כְּשֶׁהוּא לָבוּשׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis hold** that the Torah **juxtaposes removal** of garments **to donning** of garments for the following reason: **Just as** when **donning** the garments, **when he is dressed, he sanctifies** his hands and feet; **so too,** for **removal** of the garments, **he sanctifies** his hands and feet **when he is dressed.** Therefore, when he completes the service he sanctifies his hands and feet and only then removes the garments. However, the first time that the High Priest dons the priestly vestments on Yom Kippur he certainly does not require two sanctifications, since at that point he does not remove any other garments.

The *Rabbanan* articulate the analogy differently: *mah levishah keshehu lavush mekadesh* — the same way that when he performs the washing associated with *levishah* he is still dressed (he has just put the garments on), *af pshitah keshehu lavush mekadesh* — even when he performs the washing associated with *pshitah* he is still dressed. In other words, the washing for taking off the garments occurs *before* he removes them, while he is still wearing them.

Both opinions thus agree that neither washing relates to the *bigdei chol*: the first washing is about the old *kodesh* garments being removed, not about the new garments being put on. Accordingly, there is no hand-washing at the very first transition, when the *Kohen Gadol* moves from his weekday clothing to the *bigdei zahav* — because at that point there are no *bigdei kodesh* being taken off. The sole debate is the timing: does the washing for taking off precede or follow the act of removal?

This resolves the students' challenge. They had assumed Rabbi Meir's position was that two washings bracket the donning of new garments — one before and one after. Rav Pappa demonstrates that this is a misreading. According to Rabbi Meir, the two washings associated with any given *mikvah* immersion are: one for taking off the previous *bigdei kodesh* (done *after* removing them) and one for putting on the new *bigdei kodesh* (done *after* dressing). Both washings still occur — one flanking the *mikvah* on either side — but their logic is entirely about the garments being changed, not about preparation for the new ones. At the very first transition, since the *Kohen Gadol* is removing *bigdei chol*, not *bigdei kodesh*, even Rabbi Meir would agree there is no hand-washing before the *mikvah*.

### Baraisa Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב פָּפָּא: וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: פֵּרְסוּ סָדִין שֶׁל בּוּץ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵין הָעָם, פָּשַׁט וְיָרַד וְטָבַל, עָלָה וְנִסְתַּפַּג. הֵבִיאוּ לוֹ בִּגְדֵי זָהָב וְלָבַשׁ, וְקִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: פָּשַׁט וְקִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו, וְיָרַד וְטָבַל, עָלָה וְנִסְתַּפַּג, הֵבִיאוּ לוֹ בִּגְדֵי זָהָב, וְלָבַשׁ וְקִידֵּשׁ יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי תַּנְיָא תַּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rav Pappa: And how can you say that** according to Rabbi Meir the first time that the High Priest dons the garments he does not require two sanctifications? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* as follows? **They spread a sheet of fine linen between him and the people, and he removed** his garments **and descended and immersed,** and **he ascended and dried himself. They brought him golden garments, and he donned** them, **and he sanctified his hands and his feet. Rabbi Meir says: He removed** his garments **and sanctified his hands and his feet and descended and immersed. He ascended and dried himself. They brought him golden garments and he donned** them **and sanctified his hands and his feet.** Apparently, Rabbi Meir maintains that there is a special sanctification prior to donning the garments. **He said to them: If it was taught, it was taught.** I will retract my opinion, which was based on logical analysis, in favor of an explicit *baraita* that contradicts that opinion.

The students push back against Rav Pappa's resolution. *Umi matzis amart hachi* — how can you say that? They cite a *Baraisa*: *pirsu sadin shel butz beino uvein ha'am, pashat veyarad v'taval, ala v'nistapeig, hevi'u lo bigdei zahav, v'lavash v'kidesh yadav v'raglav* — this is the view of the *Rabbanan*. Then: *Rabbi Meir omer, pashat v'kidesh yadav v'raglav, veyarad v'taval, ala v'nistapeig, hevi'u lo bigdei zahav, v'lavash v'kidesh yadav v'raglav* — Rabbi Meir says he took off his clothes and *then* washed his hands, descended and immersed, arose and dried himself, was brought the gold garments, dressed, and washed his hands.

The students understand this *Baraisa* to be describing the first change of garments — from *bigdei chol* to *bigdei zahav*. If so, Rabbi Meir explicitly requires a hand-washing before going to the *mikvah* even at this first transition, which contradicts Rav Pappa's claim that no washing would occur at the *chol* → *zahav* transition. This *Baraisa*, they argue, proves that Rabbi Meir's washing *does* relate to the new garments, not merely to the old *kodesh* garments being removed.

### Final Reconciliation

Rav Pappa's response — or rather the *Gemara*'s acceptance of the challenge — is captured in the terse rejoinder: *ei tanya, tanya* — if it is taught in a *Baraisa*, it is taught. The *Baraisa* is authoritative, and if it explicitly places a hand-washing at the first transition according to Rabbi Meir, that must be accepted.

The final picture, then, is as follows. According to Rav Pappa's analysis, both the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Meir agree that the hand-washings relate to the *bigdei kodesh* being removed and donned, not to the *bigdei chol*. The sole dispute is one of timing: the *Rabbanan* hold that the washing for taking off *bigdei kodesh* occurs while one is still dressed, whereas Rabbi Meir holds it occurs after the garments have been removed. The *Baraisa*, however, introduces a further complication by recording that even at the first transition, from weekday clothing to *bigdei zahav*, Rabbi Meir requires a hand-washing before descending to the *mikvah*. Whether this means Rabbi Meir's position is in fact about preparing for the new *bigdei kodesh*, or whether it reflects some other dimension of his reasoning, the *Baraisa* stands as it has been taught.
