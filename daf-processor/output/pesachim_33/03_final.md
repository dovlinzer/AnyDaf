# Pesachim 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Terumah: Size vs. Value

### Yesterday's Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹכֵל כְּזַיִת תְּרוּמָה מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis taught:** A non-priest **who eats an olive-bulk of *teruma*** must **pay the principal** value of the *teruma* itself **and an** additional **fifth. Abba Shaul says:** He is not required to pay **unless** the *teruma* he ate is **worth a *peruta*.** 

The discussion opens with a brief review of the preceding day's material. The fundamental question was: when one must repay *terumah* that he ate inadvertently — since one who ate it *b'meizid* (deliberately) is not considered worthy of the atonement afforded by paying the principal and an added fifth — is the obligation to repay governed by size or by value? If it is size-based, one who ate a requisite volume repays that volume. If it is value-based, the amount consumed must be worth at least a *pruta*, even if it is what would normally constitute the requisite measure.

### Rav Pappa's Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אַבָּא שָׁאוּל, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל תַּרְתֵּי בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to them:** This is no proof, as **even** if **you say** that this *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of **Abba Shaul, Abba Shaul requires two** conditions: That the *teruma* be at least an olive-bulk in volume, and that it be worth at least a *peruta*.

Into this debate, Rav Pappa introduced the possibility that according to Abba Shaul — who had articulated a value-based criterion — the standard is actually a dual one: both the requisite size *and* a minimum value of a *pruta* are required. This position, and the *pasuk* that was subsequently cited to challenge it, forms the backdrop for the discussion that follows.

## Rav Pappa Retraction

### Rav Pappa Retracts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַב פָּפָּא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa** himself **also retracted** this explanation.

The Gemara states *v'af Rav Pappa hadar bei* — Rav Pappa also retracted his position. The mechanism of this retraction, and the proof that he indeed reversed himself, emerges from the extended analysis of a *baraita* that follows.

### Me'ilah: Intentional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְחָטְאָה בִּשְׁגָגָה״ — פְּרָט לְמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "If any one commits a trespass, and sins through error, in the sacred items of the Lord, then he shall bring his guilt-offering to the Lord, a ram without blemish from the flock, according to your valuation in silver shekels, after the shekel of the sanctuary, for a guilt-offering" (Leviticus 5:15), the *baraita* explains: The phrase **"and sins through error" excludes one who** sins **intentionally** through misuse of consecrated property.

The *baraita* opens with the verse *ve-cheit at bishgagah* — the Torah's requirement that one who commits *me'ilah* (misappropriation of consecrated property) brings an *asham* korban only when the act was done *bishgagah*, in error, *prat l'meizid* — thereby excluding one who acted deliberately from the obligation to bring this offering.

### Why Need Verse?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה שְׁאָר מִצְוֹת שֶׁחַיָּיב בָּהֶן כָּרֵת — פּוֹטֵר בָּהֶן אֶת הַמֵּזִיד, מְעִילָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ כָּרֵת — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁפָּטַר אֶת הַמֵּזִיד?
>
> **Translation:** Could this **not** be derived through **an *a fortiori* inference,** as follows: **Just as** with regard to **other mitzvot for which one is liable** to receive ***karet*** the verse **exempts one** from bringing an offering when the transgression was committed **intentionally, is it not right that** with regard to **misuse** of consecrated property, **which does not** incur the punishment of ***karet*,** it should **exempt one who acts intentionally?**

The Gemara immediately questions whether this exclusion required an explicit verse at all. Could it not have been derived by *kal va-chomer*? The reasoning would run: by other *mitzvot* where one incurs *karet* for an inadvertent transgression, the deliberate transgressor is exempt from a *korban*. *Me'ilah*, which carries no *karet* but rather *mita b'yedei shamayim*, should certainly exempt the deliberate transgressor — if anything, *mita b'yedei shamayim* is a more severe punishment than *karet*, so the argument for exemption seems even stronger.

### Karet vs. Mita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת שֶׁכֵּן לֹא חִיֵּיב בָּהֶן מִיתָה, תֹּאמַר בִּמְעִילָה, שֶׁחִיֵּיב בָּהּ מִיתָה! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בִּשְׁגָגָה״ — פְּרָט לְמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* rejects this claim: **No, if you say that** this is true with regard to **the rest of the mitzvot,** even those for which one is liable to receive *karet*, for which **one is not liable** to receive the **death** penalty if he violates them, **shall you** also **say that** this is the case with regard to **misuse** of consecrated items, for which **one is liable** to receive the **death** penalty, as this offense is punishable by death at the hand of Heaven? Since one cannot logically deduce this principle, **the verse states "through error"** to **exclude one** who acted **intentionally.**

The Gemara immediately reverses this logic. Perhaps *mita b'yedei shamayim* is actually the more severe punishment. In that case, from the other *mitzvot* — where the punishment is "only" *karet* — one cannot draw a *kal va-chomer* to *me'ilah*, where the intentional violator might face something worse. The discussion thus produces what Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak points out is a troubling circularity: at one moment the analysis treats *karet* as the more severe standard, and at the next it treats *mita b'yedei shamayim* as more severe — and it flip-flops between the two without resolution.

### Kezayit Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת — שֶׁכֵּן לֹא חִיֵּיב בָּהֶן מִיתָה בְּפָחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת, תֹּאמַר בִּמְעִילָה — שֶׁחִיֵּיב בָּהּ מִיתָה בְּפָחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin **said to him** that it is possible to maintain the accepted position that *karet* is more stringent by explaining **that this is** what **he is saying: No,** these are incomparable for the following reason: **If you say** that one is exempt from an offering when he violates **the rest of the mitzvot, for** which **one is not liable to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven if he eats **less than an olive-bulk** of a forbidden substance, **shall you** also **say** that this is the case **with regard to misuse** of consecrated property, **for** which **one is liable to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven if he eats **less than an olive-bulk?**

The resolution offered reframes the entire argument. The *kal va-chomer* does not founder on uncertainty about which punishment is worse; rather, one should set aside the punishment entirely and focus on the crime — specifically, on the minimum measure required for liability. By other *mitzvot*, one is not liable for *mita* on less than a *kezayit*. *Me'ilah*, by contrast, makes one liable even for an amount less than a *kezayit*. This is why *me'ilah* cannot be derived by *kal va-chomer* from other *mitzvot*: the other *mitzvot* are in fact more lenient (they require a *kezayit*), making them the *kal*, not the *chomer*. Therefore, a verse is genuinely necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: תָּנוּחַ דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁהִנַּחְתָּ אֶת דַּעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak **said to him: May your mind be settled, as you have settled my mind** and put it at ease by answering this question that was troubling me.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, upon receiving this explanation from Rav Chiyya bar Avin, responds warmly: *tanu'ach da'at'cha she-hinachta et da'ati* — may your mind be at ease, for you have put my mind at ease. This had been a genuinely troubling matter.

### Verse Teaches Korbanot

The verse is thus necessary precisely because without it one would have no way of knowing, via *kal va-chomer*, that the deliberate transgressor of *me'ilah* is exempt from the *asham*. The *kal va-chomer* fails because *me'ilah* is actually the more stringent case (liability on less than a *kezayit*), making it impossible to derive leniency for the deliberate transgressor from less stringent categories.

### Rabba & Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי נִיחוּתָא, דְּרַבָּה וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת שָׁדוּ בֵּיהּ נַרְגָּא
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin **said to him: What is settling** about this explanation? **Rabba and Rav Sheshet threw an axe at** my answer; i.e., they reject my explanation, as follows:

This resolution, however, is undermined. Rabba and Rav Sheshet throw an axe into it — *shadu bah narga*. The problem: who exactly holds that the deliberate transgressor of *me'ilah* is liable to *mita b'yedei shamayim*? That view is attributed to Rebbi, as we shall see. But Rebbi, who derives *me'ilah*'s punishment from a *gezeirah shavah* with *terumah*, would also be bound by the *kezayit* standard of *terumah* — and if that is so, the very distinction the Gemara used (that *me'ilah* applies even below a *kezayit*) collapses entirely, taking the resolution with it.

## Rebbi on Me'ilah

### Who Holds Mita?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵזִיד בִּמְעִילָה בְּמִיתָה — רַבִּי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: הֵזִיד בִּמְעִילָה, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּמִיתָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בָּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** that **one who intentionally misuses** consecrated items is liable **to** receive **death** at the hand of Heaven? It is **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who intentionally misuses** consecrated items, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that he is liable to receive **death** at the hand of Heaven. **The Rabbis say:** he violates **a warning,** a standard prohibition, and is flogged.

The Gemara now focuses: *man shamat lei de-amar meizid be-me'ilah be-mita?* — who is it that holds that deliberate *me'ilah* results in *mita b'yedei shamayim*? The answer emerges from a *baraita*: *meizid be-me'ilah Rebbi omer be-mita va-chachamim omrim be-azharah* — Rebbi says the deliberate transgressor is liable to death, while the Sages say he violates only a *lav*, a prohibition, without incurring death. Clearly, only Rebbi holds the position that underlies the entire prior analysis.

### Rebbi's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: גָּמַר ״חֵטְא״ ״חֵטְא״ מִתְּרוּמָה. מָה תְּרוּמָה בְּמִיתָה — אַף מְעִילָה בְּמִיתָה. וּמִינַּהּ: מָה תְּרוּמָה בִּכְזַיִת אַף מְעִילָה בִּכְזַיִת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? From where does he derive his opinion? **Rabbi Abbahu said: He derives** it by means of a verbal analogy between the word **sin** stated with regard to misuse of consecrated items and the word **sin** stated **with regard to *teruma*.** With regard to misuse of consecrated items, the verse states: "If any one commits a trespass, and sins through error, in the sacred items of the Lord" (Leviticus 5:15); with regard to *teruma*, the verse states: "Lest they bear sin for it, and die due to it, if they profane it" (Leviticus 22:9). Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi makes the following comparison: **Just as** eating ***teruma*** is punishable by the **death** penalty, **so too, misusing** consecrated items is punishable by the **death** penalty. **From this** verbal analogy, the comparison between *teruma* and misuse of consecrated property can be extended to other issues as well: **Just as one** is punished only for eating at least **an olive-bulk of *teruma*, so too,** one is punished for **misusing** consecrated items only if there is at least **an olive-bulk** of consecrated items. This indicates that the *baraita* cannot be explained in accordance with the opinion of Abba Shaul, who requires that the item be worth at least a *peruta*.

The Gemara then asks: *mai ta'ama de-Rebbi* — what is Rebbi's reasoning? He derives the punishment for deliberate *me'ilah* via a *gezeirah shavah* of *cheit-cheit* from *terumah*: just as *terumah* carries a penalty of *mita* (the verse *metu vo ki yechalelu'hu* and related *psukim* establish this), so too *me'ilah* carries a penalty of *mita* for the deliberate transgressor.

### The Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: מִמַּאי דְּרַבִּי כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ? דִּילְמָא כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: יֵשׁ בָּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה — אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית בַּהּ כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa strongly objects to** Rav Sheshet and Rabba's rejection of the explanation of the *baraita*: **From where** do you know that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that one is only punished after eating an olive-bulk of *teruma*? **Perhaps he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul, who said** one is liable for eating *teruma* provided the food **contains the value of a *peruta* of *teruma*, even if it is less than an olive-bulk.** As the *halakhot* of misuse of consecrated items are derived from *teruma*, one is liable for eating both *teruma* and consecrated items only if the object is worth at least a *peruta*. As such, Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin's explanation of the *baraita* should not be rejected.

Precisely here lies the difficulty that Rabba and Rav Sheshet have raised. If Rebbi learns *me'ilah* from *terumah* via *gezeirah shavah*, then just as *terumah* requires a minimum measure of a *kezayit* for liability, so too *me'ilah* — according to Rebbi — should require a *kezayit*. The entire resolution of the *kal va-chomer* problem depended on saying that *me'ilah* is more stringent than other *mitzvot* because it applies even below a *kezayit*. But for Rebbi, who derives *me'ilah*'s law from *terumah*, the same *kezayit* standard applies. The distinction collapses, and so does the reason why a verse was deemed necessary.

### Rav Pappa's Challenge

At this point Rav Pappa raises a challenge: *mimay d'Rebbi k'rabanan svira leh* — how do we know that Rebbi holds like the *rabanan* (rather than like Abba Shaul) regarding the measure of *terumah*? The *machloket* between the *rabanan* and Abba Shaul was precisely about whether the operative criterion is size or value. Rebbi says the minimum for *me'ilah* is a *kezayit* — a size. That aligns him with the *rabanan*. But perhaps, Rav Pappa suggests, Rebbi holds like Abba Shaul, who requires a *shaveh pruta* — a monetary value — even without a *kezayit*.

### Abba Shaul Question

The force of Rav Pappa's challenge is that if Rebbi holds like Abba Shaul, then Rebbi's standard for *me'ilah* (derived from *terumah*) would be value-based rather than size-based. On that reading, the *kezayit* distinction — which was essential to the prior resolution — would not exist within Rebbi's framework, and the *kal va-chomer* problem remains unresolved even under the proposed answer.

### Pappa's Retraction Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב פָּפָּא הוּא דְּאָמַר דְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל תַּרְתֵּי בָּעֵי, אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֲדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the issue mentioned previously, the Gemara asks: **But isn't Rav Pappa the one who said** that **Abba Shaul said** that it **requires two** conditions, that the object be worth a *peruta* and that it be an olive-bulk in volume? **Rather, learn from this** that Rav Pappa **retracted** his statement with regard to Abba Shaul's opinion.

The Gemara responds: but Rav Pappa himself is the one who originally said that Abba Shaul requires *tartei* — both a *kezayit* and a *pruta*. If that were correct, then even if Rebbi held like Abba Shaul, a *kezayit* would still be required, and the challenge would dissolve. The very fact that Rav Pappa poses the question — treating Abba Shaul's position as purely value-based, with no *kezayit* requirement — demonstrates that he must have retracted his original theory. *Ela shema mina hadar bei* — from this we infer that Rav Pappa reversed his earlier position.

## Baraita Analysis

### Intent Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת — שֶׁלֹּא עָשָׂה בָּהֶן שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּין כְּמִתְכַּוֵּין, שֶׁאִם נִתְכַּוֵּין לַחְתּוֹךְ אֶת הַתָּלוּשׁ וְחָתַךְ אֶת הַמְחוּבָּר — שֶׁפָּטוּר, תֹּאמַר בִּמְעִילָה, שֶׁאִם נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהִתְחַמֵּם בְּגִיזֵּי חוּלִּין וְנִתְחַמֵּם בְּגִיזֵּי עוֹלָה — שֶׁמָּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above in the *baraita*: **In what** case **is this statement said** that the *tanna'im* disagree with regard to the obligation to pay for *teruma* of leavened bread? It is said with regard to a case where **one separated *teruma*** in a permitted manner **and it became leavened** during Passover. **However, if he separated *teruma* from leavened bread** during Passover, **everyone agrees that it is not consecrated** since it is worthless.

The Gemara now explores additional ways of explaining why the *kal va-chomer* fails — and thus why the verse is indeed necessary. A further basis for distinguishing *me'ilah* from other *mitzvot* concerns the role of intent. By other *mitzvot*, there is a meaningful difference between an act performed with intent and one performed without intent. By *me'ilah*, this distinction does not operate in the same way.

### Shabbat & Me'ilah

The classic case: regarding *Shabbat*, if one intends to take an already-detached fruit (*talush*) but by mistake takes one that is still attached (*mechubar*) — *she'eino miskaven lachtoch et hatallush ve-chatech et hamechubar* — one is exempt, because the act of severing was entirely unintentional. The two cases are distinct, and acting on the wrong one incurs no liability.

*Me'ilah* is far more demanding. If someone wanted to warm himself with a cloak of ordinary *chullin* material but inadvertently wrapped himself in the skin of an *olah* — *she'eino miskaven lehischamem begizei chullin ve-nischamem begizei olah* — he has committed *me'ilah*. The fact that he had no intention of benefiting from consecrated property affords him no protection. Because *me'ilah* makes one liable even without the requisite intention that other *mitzvot* demand, it is more stringent — and therefore the *kal va-chomer* from other *mitzvot* would not work. Hence the verse was necessary.

### Mis'asek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר, הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּשְׁאָר מִצְוֹת, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא מִתְחַיֵּיב בָּהֶן שֶׁאֵין מִתְעַסֵּק כְּמִתְעַסֵּק, שֶׁאִם נִתְכַּוֵּין לְהַגְבִּיהַּ אֶת הַתָּלוּשׁ, וְחָתַךְ אֶת הַמְחוּבָּר — שֶׁפָּטוּר. תֹּאמַר בִּמְעִילָה, שֶׁאִם הוֹשִׁיט יָדוֹ לִכְלִי לִיטּוֹל חֵפֶץ וְסָךְ יָדוֹ בְּשֶׁמֶן שֶׁל קוֹדֶשׁ — שֶׁמָּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that the *baraita* should be understood differently, and **this is** what **it is saying: No, if you say** this leniency **with regard to rest of the mitzvot, where one who is acting unawares is not liable** in the same way **as** one who **is acting aware** of his actions, such as in a case where one intended to perform a permitted act and mistakenly performed a prohibited one, i.e., **if one intended to lift** something that is **detached** from the ground, but his knife happened to **cut** something that is **attached** to the ground, in violation of the act of harvesting on Shabbat, then **he is exempt; shall you** also **say** the same **with regard to misuse** of consecrated items, **where if one places his hand in a vessel to take an object** and unknowingly **places his hand in consecrated oil** then **he has misused** consecrated items? Therefore, misuse of consecrated items is more stringent than other commandments, as one commits the sin of misusing consecrated property even if he uses the consecrated object while attempting to perform a different action and is unaware that he is performing a prohibited act.

Rav Chama bar Yitzchak formulates this in terms of *mis'asek* — an act that is entirely incidental to one's purpose. The principle is: *lo mis'chayev bahen she'ein mis'asek ke-mis'asek* — by other *mitzvot*, one who performs an act in the manner of *mis'asek* is not liable as if he had acted fully intentionally. *Mis'asek* refers to a case where the act occurs entirely as a byproduct of doing something else: the classic example is one who is juggling knives and inadvertently performs *shechitah* on an animal that happened to be in the path, or one who is blowing a *shofar* as a musical exercise and happens to produce the requisite *kolos* on Rosh Hashanah — the action is performed with no awareness of its legal significance.

Similarly: if one intended to lift a *talush* fruit but severed the *mechubar* instead — *she'eino miskaven lehagbi'ah et hatallush ve-chatech et hamechubar* — one is exempt.

### Me'ilah Stringent

*Me'ilah* operates differently. If one reached into a vessel to retrieve an object and in doing so coated one's hand with consecrated olive oil — oil designated for *menachot*, for example — that is *me'ilah*, even though using the oil was entirely incidental to one's purpose. One wanted only to retrieve the object; one's hand passed through the oil; yet the benefit from *kodesh* property constitutes *me'ilah*. This is the quintessential *mis'asek* scenario, and yet liability attaches. *Me'ilah* therefore cannot be derived by *kal va-chomer* from other *mitzvot* in which *mis'asek* does not generate liability.

### Rav Chama's View

Rav Chama bar Yitzchak's formulation thus parallels the first explanation (focused on *kezayit*) in structure but differs in substance: rather than distinguishing the minimum measure of the transgression, it distinguishes the role of intention. In both formulations, the conclusion is the same — *me'ilah* is more stringent than other *mitzvot*, the *kal va-chomer* cannot be constructed in the way initially proposed, and therefore the explicit verse *bishgagah prat l'meizid* was indeed necessary.

## Terumah from Chametz

### Original Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמַפְרִישׁ תְּרוּמָה וְהֶחְמִיצָה, אֲבָל הִפְרִישׁ חָמֵץ תְּרוּמָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵינָהּ קְדוֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above in the *baraita*: **In what** case **is this statement said** that the *tanna'im* disagree with regard to the obligation to pay for *teruma* of leavened bread? It is said with regard to a case where **one separated *teruma*** in a permitted manner **and it became leavened** during Passover. **However, if he separated *teruma* from leavened bread** during Passover, **everyone agrees that it is not consecrated** since it is worthless.

The Gemara now returns to an earlier topic: is it possible to separate *terumah* from *chametz* on *Pesach*? A *baraita* cited previously (on 32a) established that one cannot do so — but the question of sourcing and mechanism remained. The Gemara revisits this: *mafrish terumah ve-hichmitzah* — perhaps the produce was kosher grain at the time of separation and subsequently leavened; or alternatively, one separated *terumah* from *chametz* before *Pesach*, when chametz was still permitted. The most problematic case, however, is when one attempts to separate *chametz* as *terumah* on *Pesach* itself — in that case, *einah kedoshah*, it does not attain the status of *terumah* at all.

### Titen Lo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, אָמַר קְרָא: ״תִּתֵּן לוֹ״ — וְלֹא לְאוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, that a worthless item cannot be designated as *teruma*? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that **the verse states:** "The first fruits of your grain, your wine, your oil, and the first of the fleece of your sheep, **shall you give to him"** (Deuteronomy 18:4), **and not to his fire.** One must give the priest something that he can use for any purpose, and not something that the priest will be forced to burn as fuel. Even those who permit one to derive benefit from leavened bread during Passover agree that it may not be eaten, and therefore leavened bread cannot be consecrated as *teruma* in this case.

The source for this ruling is derived from the verse *reshit degancha tiroshcha ve-yitzharcha u-reshit gez tzoncha titen lo* — the obligation to give *terumah* to the *kohen* encompasses grain (*dagan*), wine (*tirosh*), and oil (*yitzhar*). The operative phrase is *titen lo* — you must give it *to him*, ולא לאורו — and not for his fire. The principle is that what one separates as *terumah* must constitute a full and usable gift to the *kohen*, not something that can only be burned.

### No Benefit

If *chametz terumah* on *Pesach* is separated and given to a *kohen*, the *kohen* cannot eat it (it is *chametz* on *Pesach*) and, even granting the view of Rabbi Yosi HaGlili that one may derive *hana'ah* from *chametz* on *Pesach*, the only *hana'ah* available is burning it. It is not a full giving — not a *titen lo* in the complete sense — and therefore fails to qualify as *terumah* at all.

### Rav Huna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אֵין תּוֹרְמִין מִן הַטְּמֵאָה לַטְּהוֹרָה, וְאִם תָּרַם בְּשׁוֹגֵג — תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה. וְאַמַּאי? לֵימָא: ״לוֹ״ וְלֹא לְאוּרוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, raised an objection** based on that which was taught in a mishna: **One may not separate** *teruma* **from ritually impure** produce **for ritually pure** produce, **but if one unwittingly separated** it in this manner then **his *teruma* is** valid ***teruma*. And why** should this be valid *teruma*? **Let** the priest **say:** The verse requires that the *teruma* be given **"to him" and not to his fire,** and this ritually impure *teruma* must be burned.

Rav Huna berei de-Rav Yehoshua raises an objection. The rule is: *ein tormin min ha-teme'ah al ha-tehorah* — one may not separate *terumah* from *tamei* produce for *tahor* produce. Yet, if one did so inadvertently, *terumato terumah* — the separation is effective *b'dieved*. The challenge: if the same verse (*titen lo ve-lo le'oro*) applies to *terumah teme'ah* — since *terumah teme'ah* can only be burned and cannot be eaten — why does the separation work at all, even *b'dieved*?

### Terumah Teme'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָתָם הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, הָכָא לֹא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **This is** **not difficult. There,** in the case of impure *teruma*, **it had a period of fitness,** and it could have been given as *teruma* before it became impure. **Here,** in the case of leavened bread, **it did not** ever **have a period of fitness,** and therefore it cannot be consecrated as *teruma*.

The resolution: *lo kashya, hatam hayta lo sha'at kosher, hacha lo hayta lo sha'at kosher* — the cases differ fundamentally. *Terumah teme'ah* had a *sha'at kosher* — a moment when it was *tahor* and could have been properly separated. At that prior moment, the produce was fully within the framework of *terumah*. Even though it is now *tamei* and cannot be eaten, the fact that it once had eligibility means that the retroactive *b'dieved* separation is still recognized. *Chametz* on *Pesach*, by contrast, never had a *sha'at kosher* within the operative framework.

### No She'at Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְלֹא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן דְּאַחְמֵיץ בִּמְחוּבָּר. אֲבָל אַחְמֵיץ בְּתָלוּשׁ — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּקָדְשָׁה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in **which** this leavened bread **did not have a period of fitness** even on Passover? This could have happened only in a case **where it** became **leavened** while it was still **attached** to the ground and could not yet become *teruma*. **However,** if **it** became **leavened** after being **detached** from the ground, then can this leavened bread **indeed become consecrated** as *teruma*, even though the *baraita* indicates that no leavened bread can be designated as *teruma* during Passover?

How can one arrive at a case where *chametz* genuinely never had a *sha'at kosher*? The Gemara acknowledges the apparent difficulty: the wheat grew as ordinary grain, which was certainly fit for *terumah* separation. The only way to construct a genuine case of no *sha'at kosher* is *k'gon de-achmetz bi-mechubar* — if the grain fermented while still attached to the ground (*mechubar*). *Terumah* does not apply to produce that is still *mechubar* — the obligation only arises once the grain is harvested, brought in, and the *kri* (the heap) is properly formed. If the grain became *chametz* while still *mechubar*, it was never within the scope of *terumah* obligation at any point, and thus had no *sha'at kosher* whatsoever.

### Rav Huna Disagrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, ״בִּגְזֵירַת עִירִין פִּתְגָמָא וּבְמֵאמַר קַדִּישִׁין שְׁאֵילְתָא״, וְכֵן מוֹרִין בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא כְּווֹתִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzhak **said to** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua: **Yes,** this is indeed the case despite this puzzling limitation, and the biblical expression can be applied homiletically: **"The matter is by the decree of the watchers, and the sentence by the word of the sacred ones"** (Daniel 4:14). The Sages, who are compared to celestial beings, agree with my statement. **And so too, in the study hall, they teach in accordance with my** opinion, despite the puzzling nature of this ruling.

The follow-up question is natural: *aval achmetz b'talush* — but what if the grain became *chametz* only after it was harvested, when it was *talush*? At that point it was, in principle, within the *terumah* framework. Would we then say the separation works *b'dieved*, just as *terumah teme'ah* works *b'dieved*? The response is: yes, if it became *chametz* while *talush* — after it was harvested and subject to *terumah* — it would indeed have had a *sha'at kosher*, and the separation would work *b'dieved*.

Rav Huna berei de-Rav Yehoshua, however, disagrees with this conclusion and holds that even *chametz talush* does not work. His reasoning is not based on *sha'at kosher* but on a separate analysis of the word *reishit* in the verse.

### Reishit Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אָמַר: אָמַר קְרָא ״רֵאשִׁית״ — שֶׁשְּׁיָרֶיהָ נִיכָּרִין לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, יָצְתָה זוֹ שֶׁאֵין שְׁיָרֶיהָ נִיכָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, came,** **he said** that **the verse states:** "The **first fruits** of your grain, your wine, your oil, and the first of the fleece of your sheep, shall you give him" (Deuteronomy 18:4), implying **that its remnants are recognizable for an Israelite,** as the notion of a first part indicates that there is another portion remaining which is fit to be consumed by the Israelite. **This** *teruma* **is excluded as there is no recognizable remnant** left that may be consumed by the Israelite, because it is leavened bread. Since even after this portion has been separated the remainder of the produce may not be eaten, that which was separated does not become *teruma*.

Rav Huna's argument proceeds from the word *reishit* in the verse *reshit degancha... titen lo*. The word *reishit* — "the first" or "the beginning" — implies, by its very nature, that there is something that follows after it. One cannot have a beginning without something coming after it; the "beginning" presupposes a remainder. Applied to *terumah*: *reishit* implies that by separating the *terumah*, one creates a recognizable distinction between what was taken and what remains — *she-shiyareiha nikkarin le-Yisrael*, that the remainder should be recognizably distinct and permitted to Israel. The separation of *terumah* from *tevel* is what renders the rest of the pile permissible to eat. The *terumah* functions as the *reishit* that enables the *shiyarim*, the remainder, to be *muttar*.

### Yatzah Zo

*Yatza zo she-ein shiyareiha nikkarin* — this case is excluded. When one separates *terumah* from *chametz* on *Pesach*, the remainder does not become permitted to the Israelite who owns it. The *chametz* remains *chametz*, and the Israelite still cannot eat it. The separation accomplishes nothing by way of *hetter* for the rest of the pile. Since the defining characteristic of *reishit* — that it renders the *shiyarim* permissible — is entirely absent, this separation cannot be considered a valid *terumah* at all. On this basis, Rav Huna holds that even if the grain became *chametz* while *talush* (with what might otherwise have been a *sha'at kosher*), the separation remains invalid.

## Tamei Grapes Wine

### Ruling Introduced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב אַחָא בַּר רַב עַוְיָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, וְיָתֵיב וְאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ — דּוֹרְכָן פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה, וְיֵינָן כָּשֵׁר לִנְסָכִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Rav Avya sat before Rav Ḥisda, and he sat and said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **grapes that became ritually impure,** one should **tread on them less than an egg-bulk at a time, and the wine** that comes from them is **kosher** even **for libations** because it is ritually pure.

The Gemara now presents a striking ruling transmitted by Rav Acha bar Avya in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. *Anavim she-nitme'u* — grapes that have become *tamei*. One would naturally assume that such grapes are entirely disqualified from any use in the *Beit HaMikdash*. Yet the ruling states: *darchan pachot pachot mi-kebeitzah ve-yeinan kasher le-nesachim* — if one presses them in increments of less than an egg's volume at a time, the resulting juice is *kasher* for *nesachim*, the wine libations offered on the altar. The reaction to this ruling is one of genuine surprise: how is this possible?

### Kebeitzah Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: מַשְׁקִין מִיפְקָד פְּקִידִי, לְאֵימַת קָא מִיטַּמְּאִי — לְכִי סָחֵיט לְהוּ. לְכִי סָחֵיט לְהוּ לֵיתֵיהּ לְשִׁיעוּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Apparently** Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds: The liquid is stored** inside the grape, as the juice is not considered to be part of the grape itself but rather stored in the grape as though contained in a receptacle. According to Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion, **when do** these liquids **become ritually impure?** This occurs only **when one squeezes them,** and prior to this the juice remains pure even if the grape was impure. And **when one squeezes them, there is less than the** minimum **measure** of grape flesh that would transfer ritual impurity, as food can impart ritual impurity only if it is at least an egg-bulk in size.

The ruling specifies *pachot pachot mi-kebeitzah* — less than a *kebeitzah* at each pressing — as the operative condition for validity. The Gemara adds that while *lechatchilah* only less than a *kebeitzah* is permitted, a *baraita* establishes that even *b'dieved*, if a *tamei meit* pressed exactly a *kebeitzah* of olives or grapes, *tehorim* — the result is still *tahor*. The reason only less than a *kebeitzah* is permitted *lechatchilah* is a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic precaution lest one come to press more than a *kebeitzah*, which would clearly be problematic. So the full picture: less than a *kebeitzah* is permitted *lechatchilah*; up to a *kebeitzah* is valid *b'dieved*; more than a *kebeitzah* is not acceptable under any circumstances.

### Juice Separation

To understand how this is possible at all, the Gemara articulates a conceptual distinction: *mashkin mifkad pikidi* — the juice is considered a separate, contained entity within the grape, not simply an aspect of the grape itself. The analogy is to water in a cup: the water and the cup are distinct entities, even though one contains the other. So too, the juice exists inside the grape as a separate entity. When the grape becomes *tamei*, the juice does not automatically become *tamei* along with it.

### Shiur for Tumah

This conceptual separation leads to a further point. Even if the juice were to be considered at risk, the *tamei* status of *food* (*ochel*) can only convey *tumah* to a liquid (*mashkeh*) if the food meets a minimum measure — a *kebeitzah*. If the *ochel* is less than a *kebeitzah*, it lacks the *shiur* necessary to make the liquid *tamei*. Combined with the pressing method described: the presser does not touch the juice directly; the pressing implement is made of plain wood (*pshutei klei eitz*) that is not *mekabel tumah*; and the grapes being pressed are always less than a *kebeitzah* — which means they lack the *shiur* to convey *tumah* to the juice as it emerges. Furthermore, the juice becomes susceptible to *tumah* through *hukshra* — preparation via liquid — only when it exits the grape; but by that point there is no longer sufficient *ochel* to convey *tumah* to it. The result, under this framework, is juice that is *tahor* and fit for *nesachim*.

### Rav Chisda Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאן צָיֵית לָךְ וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רַבָּךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to** Rav Aḥa bar Rav Avya: **Who will listen to you and to Rabbi Yoḥanan your teacher** with regard to this issue?

Rav Chisda is dismissive: *man tza'it lakh u-le-Rabbi Yochanan rabakh* — who is going to listen to you and to Rabbi Yochanan your teacher? This is a stark challenge, reflecting that the ruling is genuinely difficult to accept. The implicit concern: if the grape is *tamei*, how does one simply say the juice is untouched?

### Counter Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתַנְיָא: הָא לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה — לִתְרוּמַת תּוּתִין (זֵיתִים) וַעֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאָה, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ לֹא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה וְלֹא הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה. הָא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה נָמֵי אִית בֵּיהּ, דְּאִי בָּעֵי דָּרֵיךְ לְהוּ פָּחוֹת פָּחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case where **the grapes have not been rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity, since they have not come in contact with liquids. **When do they become susceptible** to ritual impurity? This is only **once one presses them** and they become wet with their own juice. However, **when he presses them,** they decrease in volume **and are lacking the measure** of volume required to become impure. This must be the explanation, **for if you do not say so,** it is difficult to reconcile this mishna with **that which was taught** in the *baraita* cited above: **To what** may **this** case of *teruma* of leavened bread **be compared?** It may be compared **to *teruma* of berries, olives, and grapes that became impure, which can neither be eaten nor burned.** However, according to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement **they may even be eaten, as if one wishes he may tread less than an egg-bulk** at a time.

A counter-challenge is then raised from the case of *terumah* berries, olives, and grapes that became *tamei* (*terumat tutin zeitim va-anavim she-nitme'u*). Of such produce the rule is that it has neither *heter achilah* — it may not be eaten, being *tamei* — nor *heter hasaka* — it cannot even serve as fuel, being too moist. The question: if the juice is indeed a separate entity that can emerge *tahor*, why not simply press the *tamei terumah* fruit in increments of less than a *kebeitzah* and thereby obtain usable *tahor* juice? The fact that we do not do this seems to imply that the juice is not considered a separate entity at all — it is regarded as part of the fruit and therefore equally *tamei*.

### Never Hukhshar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בַּעֲנָבִים שֶׁלֹּא הוּכְשְׁרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case where **the grapes have not been rendered susceptible** to ritual impurity, since they have not come in contact with liquids.

The response: *hakha be-mai askinan be-anavim she-lo hukhsharu* — the case of the Mishnah deals with grapes that were never rendered susceptible to *tumah* (*hukhshar*) in the first place. This introduces the requirement of *hukshra*: food does not become *tamei* automatically upon contact with an *av ha-tumah*; it must first be rendered susceptible through one of the seven liquids. Until food has been *mukhshar*, it is not in the *parsha* of *tumah* for food.

### Hukshra Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאֵימַת מִתַּכְשְׁרִי? לְכִי סָחֵיט לְהוּ. כִּי סָחֵיט לְהוּ — בְּצִיר לְהוּ שִׁיעוּרָא. דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, הָא דְּתַנְיָא: הָא לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה — לִתְרוּמַת תּוּתִין (זֵיתִים) וַעֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאָה, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ לֹא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה וְלֹא הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **when do they become susceptible** to ritual impurity? This is only **once one presses them** and they become wet with their own juice. However, **when he presses them,** they decrease in volume **and are lacking the measure** of volume required to become impure. This must be the explanation, **for if you do not say so,** it is difficult to reconcile this mishna with **that which was taught** in the *baraita* cited above: **To what** may **this** case of *teruma* of leavened bread **be compared?** It may be compared **to *teruma* of berries, olives, and grapes that became impure, which can neither be eaten nor burned.**

The Gemara elaborates. A person who is *tamei meit* can make other people and vessels *tamei* through contact, without any prior preparation being necessary on their part. But for *food* to become *tamei*, it must first be prepared — *mukhshar* — through contact with one of the seven liquids (*shivah mashkin*). Plain grapes that have never had any liquid on them are not *mukhshar* and thus cannot become *tamei* as *ochel* through contact with a *tamei meit*.

When, then, might the grapes become *mukhshar*? Only when the juice is pressed out, since *ve-eimat mechashrei de-chi sachatei de-hu* — they become susceptible at the moment of pressing, when the juice flows. But at that very moment, *batsir lei shiura* — the volume remaining has been compressed to less than a *kebeitzah*, and there is no longer sufficient *ochel* to convey *tumah* to the liquid. The *tumah* chain is thus broken at precisely the point where *hukshra* would have occurred.

This response also addresses why the *terumah* berry case is different: those berries *were* already *mukhshar* and *tamei*, so the juice cannot escape the *tumah* that pervades the fruit as an integrated unit. The resolution is thus that Rav Chisda's challenge from that *baraita* does not undermine Rabbi Yochanan's ruling, which is limited to a specific and carefully constructed case.

### Rava: Takala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: גְּזֵירָה דִּילְמָא אָתֵי בְּהוּ לִידֵי תַקָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is possible to reject this proof, as even if this action is permitted in principle, there is **a rabbinic decree** prohibiting it **lest he encounter a stumbling block.** If one keeps ritually impure fruit of *teruma* in order to press it for its juice, it is possible that he will forget its status and accidentally eat it.

Rava offers an alternative explanation for why the *terumah* berry case does not work — not because the juice shares the *tumah* of the fruit as an integrated whole, but as a *gezeirah*: *de-dilma ati ba de-takala* — lest it lead to a dangerous precedent. If one were permitted to press the berries and use the juice, one might reason that if the juice is acceptable, so too is the fruit itself — and then come to eat the *tamei* fruit, which is forbidden. The prohibition on using the pressed juice from *tamei terumah* berries is thus a protective *gezeirah*, not a statement about the intrinsic *tumah* of the juice.

### Abaye Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּמִי חָיְשִׁינַן לְתַקָּלָה? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: מַדְלִיקִין בְּפַת וּבְשֶׁמֶן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה שֶׁנִּטְמֵאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Are we concerned about** this type of **stumbling block? Wasn't** it **taught** in a *baraita*: **One** may **light** a fire **with bread and oil of *teruma* that became ritually impure?** It can be deduced from this *baraita* that there is no concern that a person will forget and eat these foods.

Abaye challenges Rava's position: *umi khaishinan le-takala* — are we really concerned about *takala*? A *baraita* explicitly permits using *terumah* oil that has become *tamei* as fuel, burning it for light. If we were genuinely concerned about *takala*, we should not permit this either — for one might reason that if the oil is usable, perhaps the olive it came from could be consumed as well.

### Kli Ma'us Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פַּת — זָרֵיק לֵיהּ בֵּין הָעֵצִים. שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה — רָמֵי לֵיהּ בִּכְלִי מָאוּס.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: Bread** is only permitted when **one throws it among the wood** used for fuel so that it is ruined and no longer considered edible. ***Teruma* oil** is permitted only **when one puts it into a repulsive vessel** so that no one will drink it.

The response: we do indeed take *takala* seriously, and the permission to use *tamei terumah* oil or bread as fuel is only granted under conditions that prevent *takala* from materializing. *Pat zarik lei beina eitzim* — the bread is thrown into the woodpile, where it becomes dirty and disgraceful; one will not come to eat it. *Shemen shel terumah ramei lei bi-khli ma'us* — the oil is placed in a disgusting vessel; one will not come to consume it under those circumstances. The *takala* is averted by rendering the *terumah* object repugnant, so that no one would think to eat it.

The concern with the *tamei* berries is different: if one presses the juice and sees it emerge seemingly *tahor*, one may well reason that the fruit itself is fine — there is no parallel mechanism of *kli ma'us* to prevent that inference. The *gezeirah* against pressing thus stands. Rav Chisda's dismissal of Rabbi Yochanan's ruling and Rava's *takala* concern together frame the parameters within which the ruling of *anavim she-nitme'u* must be understood — a carefully bounded halakhic ruling that stands, according to those who accept it (and remarkably, the Rambam *paskens* in accordance with Rabbi Yochanan), only where every element of the protective framework is in place.
