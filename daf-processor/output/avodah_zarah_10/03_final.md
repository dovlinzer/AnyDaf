# Avodah Zarah 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shtar Dating Systems

### Post-dated Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָהוּא שְׁטָרָא דַּהֲוָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״שֵׁית שְׁנִין יַתִּירָתָא״, סְבוּר רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה לְמֵימַר: הַאי שְׁטָר מְאוּחָר הוּא, נִיעַכְּבֵיהּ עַד דְּמָטֵיא זִמְנֵיהּ וְלָא טָרֵיף. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הַאי סָפְרָא דַּוְקָנָא כַּתְבֵיהּ, וְהָנַךְ שֵׁית שְׁנִין דִּמְלַכוּ בְּעֵילָם, דַּאֲנַן לָא חָשְׁבִינַן לְהוּ, הוּא קָחָשֵׁיב (לֵיהּ) [לְהוּ], וּבְזִמְנֵיהּ כַּתְבֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA. The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** promissory **note in which was written** a date that had **six additional years** relative to the correct scribal date, which takes for its starting point the beginning of Greek rule. **The Sages** who studied **before Rabba thought to say: This is a postdated** promissory **note,** which can be used only from the date it specifies. Therefore, **let us hold it until its time arrives** so that the creditor **will not repossess** property that the debtor sold prior to the date that appears in the note. **Rav Naḥman** disagreed and **said: This** promissory note **was written by an exacting scribe, and those six years** are referring to the years **when** the Greeks **ruled** only **in Elam. We do not count them,** as Greek rule had not yet spread throughout the world, but **he does count them. And** therefore **he wrote in** the promissory note the correct **time,** as the date does in fact match the year in which the promissory note was written.

The Gemara opens with a case involving a *shtar* that appeared to be dated six years into the future from the time it was being examined by the court. The rabbis sitting before Rava assumed it was a post-dated document — a *shtar meuchar* — and proposed holding it back until its stated date arrived before permitting collection. Rav Nachman, however, rejected this assumption. He explained that it was a skilled, senior scribe who wrote the document — *hai safra dukina katveih* — and that this scribe had a particular reason for the unusual date. The document was not post-dated at all; it was written for the present moment. The apparent discrepancy arose because the scribe counted from six years earlier than the conventional starting point of *minyan shtarot*. As Rav Nachman put it: *vahanach shit shanin demalku beEilam, deanan lo chashvinan lehu* — those six years during which the Greek empire ruled over Eilam, a region in Mesopotamia, are not normally included in our count; we conventionally begin from a later point in the consolidation of Greek power, specifically after Alexander's heirs established the Seleucid dynasty. This particular scribe chose to include those six years, giving the document what appeared to be a future date but was in fact entirely consistent with the present.

The broader halachic background here is the well-established discussion in the opening chapter of *Gittin* — around daf 17 — concerning the invalidity of a *shtar mukdam*, a pre-dated document. A pre-dated *shtar* is disqualified because it retroactively asserts that liens were in place earlier than they actually were, thereby undermining the legitimate claims of prior creditors. A *shtar meuchar*, by contrast, raises different concerns. The case here ultimately resolves into a question not of fraud but of which dating convention the scribe employed.

### Greek Rule in Eilam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שֵׁשׁ שָׁנִים מָלְכוּ בְּעֵילָם, וְאַחַר כָּךְ פָּשְׁטָה מַלְכוּתָן בְּכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman cites a proof for his resolution: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says:** The Greeks **ruled for six years in Elam** alone, **and afterward their dominion spread throughout the entire world.** It is the later event that serves as the basis for the dating system used by most scribes.

A *Baraita* is cited to support Rav Nachman's explanation: *de-tanya Rabbi Yosei omer shesh shanim malchu be-Eilam, ve-achar kach pashitah malchutan be-chol ha-olam kulo* — Rabbi Yosei teaches that the Greeks ruled for six years specifically in Eilam before their dominion spread across the entire world. The significance of this teaching for our purposes is that there was a genuine historical ambiguity about when to mark the beginning of Greek imperial rule. The Macedonian Greeks fought lengthy wars against the Persians and other Eastern powers before consolidating control. Depending on which milestone one chose — the first assertion of power in Eilam or the later expansion across the whole empire — one might arrive at dates differing by six years. Some scribes, like the one in our case, counted from the earlier moment; the conventional system did not.

### Yetziat Mitzrayim Dating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: מִמַּאי דִּלְמַלְכוּת יְוָנִים מָנֵינַן? דִּלְמָא לִיצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם מָנֵינַן, וְשַׁבְקֵיהּ לְאַלְפָּא קַמָּא וְנַקְטֵיהּ אַלְפָּא בָּתְרָא, וְהַאי מְאוּחָר הוּא! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בַּגּוֹלָה אֵין מוֹנִין אֶלָּא לְמַלְכֵי יְוָנִים בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov objects to** Rav Naḥman's answer: **From where** is it known **that we count** years according **to** the **Greek rule,** and that this promissory note was dated according to a system that uses the Greek rule as a starting point and was written by an exacting scribe? **Perhaps we count** the years using **the exodus from Egypt** as the starting point, which occurred one thousand years before the start of the Greek rule, **and** in this case the scribe **left** out **the first thousand years** from the time of the exodus **and held on** only **to the last thousand years,** omitting the thousands digit and writing merely the hundreds, tens, and single digits. **And** if so, **this** promissory note **is postdated. Rav Naḥman said** in response: The practice is that **in the exile we count** years **only** according **to the Greek kings.**

Rav Acha bar Yaakov then issues a broader challenge to the entire assumption underlying the discussion: *matkif lah Rav Acha bar Yaakov mi-mai de-malchut Yevanim mininan dilma li-Yetziat Mitzrayim mininan* — from where do we know that we count according to the Greek kingdoms at all? Perhaps we count from *Yetziat Mitzrayim* instead! If so, the apparent six-year discrepancy in the *shtar* would be irrelevant, because the entire dating system would rest on a different foundation.

### Thousands Place Debate

This challenge prompts a deeper question about the structure of dating conventions. The Gemara raises the possibility: *ve-shavkei lah alfa kama ve-naktei* — perhaps we dropped the first thousand years and are working with the second. That is, the year written in the document might represent not a count from the Seleucid dynasty but a count from *Yetziat Mitzrayim* with the leading "1,000" omitted. This is structurally analogous to our own practice of writing the year as *taf shin ayin chet* rather than spelling out the full five-thousand-something — we routinely drop the thousands place because it changes only once a millennium.

The underlying assumption of this proposal is that a count from *Yetziat Mitzrayim* to the beginning of the Seleucid dynasty would come to approximately one thousand years, which is historically plausible. *Yetziat Mitzrayim* is generally dated to the fourteenth or thirteenth century BCE, and the Seleucid era begins around 312 BCE, placing the gap in the vicinity of one thousand years.

The question then becomes: if the date in the *shtar* represents the second millennium since *Yetziat Mitzrayim* with the thousands digit omitted, could the document be read as a *shtar meuchar* — ambiguously dated either to the present or to a thousand years hence? The concern is that the ambiguity itself might compromise the *shtar*'s validity.

This discussion naturally raises a broader point about the religious significance, or lack thereof, of year-numbering conventions. The passage of years in halachic contexts is typically important for relative counts — how long a king has reigned, when *shmitta* or *yovel* falls — rather than for any absolute cosmic significance attached to the calendar number itself. The widespread Jewish practice today of dating documents and tombstones *le-vriat ha-olam* was not the universal convention even among medieval authorities, many of whom continued with *minyan shtarot*. A document dated *le-minyan she-anu monin kan* — according to whichever conventional reckoning is currently in use — would presumably be unproblematic, since no absolute significance attaches to the number of years apart from its function as a shared reference point.

### Rav Nachman's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא סָבַר דַּחוֹיֵי קָא מְדַחֵי לֵיהּ, נְפַק דָּק וְאַשְׁכַּח, דְּתַנְיָא: בַּגּוֹלָה אֵין מוֹנִין אֶלָּא לְמַלְכֵי יְוָנִים בִּלְבַד. אָמַר רָבִינָא: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דִּתְנַן: ״בְּאֶחָד בְּנִיסָן רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לַמְּלָכִים וְלָרְגָלִים״, וְאָמְרִינַן: לַמְּלָכִים — לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לִשְׁטָרוֹת. וּתְנַן: בְּאֶחָד בְּתִשְׁרִי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה לַשָּׁנִים וְלַשְּׁמִיטִּין, וְאָמְרִינַן: לַשָּׁנִים — לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לִשְׁטָרוֹת. קַשְׁיָא שְׁטָרוֹת אַהֲדָדֵי! וּמְשַׁנֵּינַן: כָּאן לְמַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, כָּאן לְמַלְכֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם. לְמַלְכֵי אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם מִתִּשְׁרִי מָנֵינַן, לְמַלְכֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִנִּיסָן מָנֵינַן. וַאֲנַן הַשְׁתָּא מִתִּשְׁרִי מָנֵינַן, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לִיצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם מָנֵינַן — מִנִּיסָן בָּעֵינַן לְמִימְנֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְמַלְכֵי יְוָנִים מָנֵינַן? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Upon hearing this reply, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov **thought:** Rav Naḥman **is** merely **deflecting** my legitimate questions with this answer. Afterward, **he went out, examined** the matter, **and discovered** that it was as Rav Naḥman said. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **In the exile we count** years **only** according **to the Greek kings.** **Ravina said: The mishna is also precisely** formulated, as it teaches that we calculate years according to the Greek kings. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Rosh HaShana* 2a): **On the first of Nisan is the New Year for kings and for the Festivals. And we say** about this: **With regard to what *halakha*** is it stated that the first of Nisan is the New Year **for kings? Rav Ḥisda said:** It is said **with regard to** dating **documents** and determining their validity. **And we learned** in the same mishna: **On the first of Tishrei is the New Year for** counting **years and for** calculating **Sabbatical** cycles. **And we say: With regard to what *halakha*** is it stated that the first of Tishrei is the New Year **for** counting **years? And Rav Ḥisda said:** It is said **with regard to** dating **documents.** These two statements with regard to the dating of **documents** are **difficult** in light of **each other,** as according to one statement the dating system is based on Nisan as the first month, whereas according to the other the year begins in Tishrei. **And we resolved** the contradiction by explaining that **here** the dating is according **to kings of Israel,** and **there** the dating is according **to the kings of the** gentile **nations of the world.** That is, when we date years according **to the kings of the nations of the world, we count from** the month of **Tishrei,** whereas when we date years according **to the kings of Israel, we count from** the month of **Nisan. And now** that **we count from** the month of **Tishrei** when dating documents, one can claim as follows: **If it enters your mind** that **we count** and date years using **the exodus from Egypt** as the starting point, while leaving off the first thousand years, then **we should count from** the month of **Nisan,** when the exodus occurred. **Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** the mishna that **we count** years according **to the Greek kings?** The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that the scribal years are in fact calculated according to the Greek kings.

Rav Nachman provides a definitive ruling: *ba-golah ein monin ela l'malchei Yevanim bilvad* — in the diaspora, we count exclusively according to the years of the Greek kings. At first, Rav Acha bar Yaakov thought this was merely a dismissive deflection and was dissatisfied with the answer. But Rav Nachman then pointed to a supporting *Baraita* that says the same thing explicitly — and the Gemara notes that *Ravina* adds that this can be inferred from the *Mishnah* as well.

The Gemara's emphasis that this rule applies specifically *ba-golah* — in the diaspora — is itself worth noting. In the land of Israel, one might maintain a stronger fidelity to distinctly Jewish frameworks of counting. Outside the land, the reality of living within a foreign political and cultural structure means that we adopt the dominant chronological conventions of that structure. Why the Greek count rather than the Roman? Even by the time of the Amoraim, the Greek Hellenistic culture — particularly in the eastern Mediterranean — remained the dominant cultural framework. The Roman Empire absorbed much of Greek civilization, and Greek functioned as the *lingua franca* of the eastern empire while Latin was largely confined to administration. The Seleucid dating system thus retained its practical utility and cultural familiarity long after the Seleucid dynasty itself had ceased to exist.

## Royal Year Counting

### Nisan vs Tishrei

The Gemara now turns to a related but distinct question: when counting the regnal years of a king for purposes of dating documents, from which month does a new year begin? The *Mishnah* in *Rosh Hashanah* states: *be-echad be-Nisan Rosh Hashanah le-melachim ve-laregalim* — the first of Nisan is the new year for kings and for the festivals.

### Jewish Kings Nisan

Rav Chisda interprets the clause *le-melachim* as referring to the halacha of *shtarot*: when we write the year of a king's reign in a legal document, the year advances at Nisan. However, the same *Mishnah* also states that the first of Tishrei is *Rosh Hashanah le-shanim u-li-shmitin* — the new year for years generally and for *shmitta* cycles — and Rav Chisda also interprets that clause as pertaining to *shtarot*. This creates an internal contradiction: Rav Chisda has apparently assigned two contradictory rulings to the same subject.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *kan le-malkhei Yisrael kan le-malkhei ovdei kokhavim* — one ruling refers to Jewish kings, the other to non-Jewish kings. Jewish kings count their regnal years from Nisan, while non-Jewish kings count from Tishrei. This distinction reflects a broader pattern well known from the *Rosh Hashanah* discussions: Nisan is the beginning of the year for the Jewish people as a nation — it marks *Yetziat Mitzrayim* and the formation of Israel as *am* — while Tishrei represents the universal human new year, associated with the creation of the world.

### Seleucid Convention

The Gemara draws the practical conclusion: *ve-anan hashta mi-Tishrei mininan* — we currently count our years from Tishrei, using the Seleucid reckoning. This confirms the earlier ruling of Rav Nachman. And the Gemara uses this to further refute the suggestion of Rav Acha bar Yaakov: *ve-i salka da-atakh mi-Yetziat Mitzrayim mininan* — if we were truly counting from *Yetziat Mitzrayim*, we would need to count from Nisan, because *Yetziat Mitzrayim* is a specifically Jewish historical anchor and Jewish counting runs from Nisan. The fact that we count from Tishrei demonstrates conclusively that we are following the Seleucid, non-Jewish convention.

### Dating Convention Shift

One might ask why contemporary Jewish documents — *ketubot*, *gittin*, and the like — no longer use *minyan shtarot* at all, having shifted to dating *le-vriat ha-olam*. The answer appears to lie in a convergence of historical and intellectual factors. After fifteen hundred or two thousand years, dating documents by reference to the Seleucid dynasty, which had long since vanished from history, became increasingly artificial. Around the turn of the sixth millennium *le-vriat ha-olam* — roughly 1240 CE — there was a notable surge of Kabbalistic interest in cosmic chronology, and the significance attached to the world's entering its sixth thousand years appears to have catalyzed the shift toward the *briat ha-olam* dating convention. The transition was gradual and not uniform across communities, and some *ketubot* continued to include *minyan shtarot* even after the change became widespread.

## Yom Ginussia

### Coronation Day Def.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא שֶׁל מַלְכֵיהֶם וְכוּ׳. מַאי ״וְיוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא שֶׁל מַלְכֵיהֶם״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: יוֹם שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין בּוֹ גּוֹיִם אֶת מַלְכָּם. וְהָתַנְיָא: יוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא וְיוֹם שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין בּוֹ אֶת מַלְכָּם! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דִידֵיהּ, הָא דִּבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § One of the gentile festivals listed in the mishna is **the day of the festival [*geinuseya*] of their kings.** The Gemara asks: **What is** meant by: **The day of *geinuseya* of their kings? Rav Yehuda says:** This is referring to the **day on which the gentiles appoint** and crown **their king.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: Two gentile festivals are **the day of *geinuseya* and** the **day on which the gentiles appoint their king?** This indicates that these are two separate occasions. The Gemara answers that it is **not difficult: This,** the day of *geinuseya*, is referring to the coronation of the king **himself,** whereas **that,** the day on which the gentiles appoint and crown their king, is referring to the coronation **of his son,** when a son is crowned during his father's lifetime as heir apparent.

The *Mishnah* earlier in the tractate had listed *yom ginussia shel malcheihem* — the *yom ginussia* of their kings — among the occasions on which commercial dealings with non-Jews are restricted. The Gemara now asks: *mai yom ginussia*? What is this term? Rav Yehuda offers the first definition: *yom she-ma'amidin bo ovdei kokhavim et malkam* — it is the day on which the non-Jews appoint their king, the coronation day. This definition reflects an important feature of Roman governance: the emperor was not a hereditary dynastic monarch but was officially appointed by the Senate, giving the Roman succession an electoral or quasi-electoral character rather than an automatic dynastic one.

The Gemara immediately objects from a *Baraita* that lists both *yom ginussia* and *yom she-ma'amidin bo et malkam* as two distinct occasions, implying they cannot be the same thing. The response: *lo kashya ha didei ha dibrei* — one refers to the king himself and the other to his son, the prince regent, who might be formally installed during his father's lifetime as heir apparent.

### Obadiah Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מוֹקְמִי מַלְכָּא בַּר מַלְכָּא? וְהָתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: ״הִנֵּה קָטֹן נְתַתִּיךָ בַּגּוֹיִם״ — שֶׁאֵין מוֹשִׁיבִין מֶלֶךְ בֶּן מֶלֶךְ, ״בָּזוּי אַתָּה מְאֹד״ — שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם לֹא כְּתָב וְלֹא לָשׁוֹן. אֶלָּא מַאי יוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא? יוֹם הַלֵּידָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do** the Romans actually **appoint** as **king the son of the king? But didn't Rav Yosef teach:** The verse relating a prophesy about Edom, associated with the Roman Empire: **"Behold, I made you small among the nations"** (Obadiah 1:2), is a reference to the fact **that** the Romans **do not place** on the throne as **king the son of the king.** The continuation of the verse: **"You are greatly despised,"** is a reference to the fact **that** the Romans **have neither** their own **script nor** their own **language,** but use those of other nations. The Gemara therefore rejects the explanation of the *baraita* that distinguishes between coronation of a king and coronation of the king's son: **Rather, what is the day of *geinuseya*?** It is **the** king's **birthday.**

This challenge prompts a deeper question about the structure of dating conventions. The Gemara raises the possibility that *yom ginussia* simply means *yom ha-leda*, the birthday. But now a different *Baraita* creates a parallel problem, listing both *yom ginussia* and *yom ha-leda* separately. The same resolution applies: *lo kashya ha didei ha dibrei* — one is the king's birthday and one is the prince's birthday. The Romans made great public celebration of a young man's first shaving, a coming-of-age ceremony with some analogy to significant Jewish lifecycle moments.

### Birthday Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא וְיוֹם הַלֵּידָה! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דִידֵיהּ, הָא דִּבְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: Two gentile festivals are **the day of *geinuseya* and the birthday.** Once again, these two events cannot be the same. The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult: This,** the day of *geinuseya*, is referring to the birthday of the king **himself,** whereas **that,** the birthday mentioned in the *baraita*, is referring to the birthday **of his son.**

### Asverus Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: יוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא שֶׁלּוֹ, יוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא שֶׁל בְּנוֹ, וְיוֹם הַלֵּידָה שֶׁלּוֹ, וְיוֹם הַלֵּידָה שֶׁל בְּנוֹ! אֶלָּא מַאי ״יוֹם גִּינּוּסְיָא״? יוֹם שֶׁמַּעֲמִידִין בּוֹ מַלְכָּם, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא — הָא דִידֵיהּ, הָא דִּבְרֵיהּ. וְאִי קַשְׁיָא לָךְ דְּלָא מוֹקְמִי מַלְכָּא בַּר מַלְכָּא, עַל יְדֵי שְׁאֵלָה מוֹקְמִי, כְּגוֹן אַסְוִירוּס בַּר אַנְטוֹנִינוּס דִּמְלַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **The day of *geinuseya* of** the king, **the day of *geinuseya* of his son, and** the king's **birthday and the birthday of his son?** If so, the *geinuseya* cannot be either his or his son's birthday. **Rather, what** is meant by the **day of *geinuseya*?** In fact it is referring to the **day on which** the gentiles **appoint** and crown **their king. And** the fact that a *baraita* mentions both the day of *geinuseya* and the day on which the gentiles appoint and crown their king is **not difficult,** as **this,** the day of *geinuseya*, is referring to **his** own coronation, whereas **that,** the day on which the gentiles appoint and crown their king, is referring to the coronation **of his son. And if** it is **difficult for you that** which was stated earlier, **that** the Romans **do not appoint** as **king the son of the king,** in fact **they do appoint** a son of the king as king **through the request** of the king. **For example,** there was **Asveirus, son of Antoninus, who ruled** at the request of Antoninus.

The Gemara eventually returns to the coronation interpretation and resolves the objection about hereditary succession with a qualification: *al yedei she'eila mukminan* — through a special petition, the emperor can request that his son be appointed to succeed him. The example given is *kegon Asverus bar Antoninus* — such as Asverus, the son of Antoninus, who did in fact become emperor through such a request. This is a reference to the arrangement following the death of Antoninus Pius, when his two adopted sons — Marcus Aurelius and Lucius Severus — were designated as co-emperors, requiring a special imperial intervention beyond the normal mechanisms of senatorial appointment.

## Antoninus & Rabbi

### Antoninus Dilemma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַנְטוֹנִינוּס לְרַבִּי: בָּעֵינָא דְּיִמְלוֹךְ אַסְוִירוּס בְּרִי תְּחוֹתַי, וְתִתְעֲבֵיד טְבֶרְיָא קָלָנְיָא, וְאִי אֵימָא לְהוּ חֲדָא — עָבְדִי, תְּרֵי — לָא עָבְדִי. אַיְיתִי גַּבְרָא, אַרְכְּבֵיהּ אַחַבְרֵיהּ, וִיהַב לֵיהּ יוֹנָה לְעִילַּאי (בִּידֵיהּ), וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְתַתַּאי: אֵימַר (לעילא דלמפרח) [לְעִילַּאי דְּנַפְרַח] יוֹנָה מִן יְדֵיהּ. אֲמַר: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הָכִי קָאָמַר לִי: אַתְּ בְּעִי מִינַּיְיהוּ דְּ״אַסְוִירוּס בְּרִי יִמְלוֹךְ תְּחוֹתַי״, וְאֵימָא לֵיהּ לְאַסְוִירוּס דְּתִעֲבֵיד טְבֶרְיָא קָלָנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides the background for this assertion. It is related that **Antoninus said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: **I wish for Asveirus my son to rule instead of me, and that** the city **Tiberias be released** [***kelaneya***] from paying taxes. **And if I tell** the Roman senate **one** of my wishes, **they will do** as I wish, but if I ask for **two** of them **they will not do** as I wish. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi conveyed his answer in the following manner: **He brought a man, placed him on** the shoulders of **another** man, **and put a dove in the hands of the one on top. And he said to the one on the bottom: Tell the one on top that he should cause the dove to fly from his hands.** Antoninus **said** to himself: **Learn from it** that **this** is what Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **is saying to me: You** should **ask** the Senate: **Let Asveirus my son rule instead of me, and say to Asveirus that he should release Tiberias** from paying taxes.

With the mention of Asverus bar Antoninus, the Gemara transitions to the extended aggadic material concerning the friendship between Antoninus and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The first episode establishes the political context of their relationship. Antoninus presents Rabbi with a dilemma: *ba'ina de-yimloch Asverus bri tichotai* — I want my son Asverus to reign after me — *ve-tit'avid Tiveriya koloniya* — and I want Tiberias to be granted a remission of its taxes. The problem: *ve-i eima lehu chada avdi, tartei lo avdi* — if I ask the Senate for one of these two things they will do it, but they will not grant both requests.

Rabbi's response is characteristically oblique. Rather than speaking directly, he arranged a visual parable: *aitei gavra arkibei achavreia* — he brought a man riding piggyback on another man's shoulders — *ve-yahav lei yona le'eila bidei* — and placed a dove in the hands of the one on top — *ve-amar lei le-tata eimar le'eila limifrach midei yona* — and instructed the lower man to tell the upper man to release the dove from his hands. Antoninus correctly decoded this message: ask the Senate to appoint Asverus as emperor — that is the request that requires the cooperation of the body beneath him — and then instruct Asverus, once seated in power, to grant the tax remission to Tiberias. The two requests could be achieved sequentially if one used the successor as the vehicle for the second.

### Dove Parable

### Secret Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ מֵימָר [בְּהֶדְיָא]! אָמַר: שָׁמְעִי בִּי חֲשִׁיבִי דְּרוֹמָאֵי וּמְצַעֲרוּ לִי. וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ בְּלַחַשׁ! מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי עוֹף הַשָּׁמַיִם יוֹלִיךְ אֶת הַקּוֹל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **let him say** his advice **explicitly?** Why did Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi answer in such a circumspect way, which could have been interpreted incorrectly? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said** to himself: If I answer openly, **the important Romans** might **hear me and will cause** me **anguish.** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **let him say** his advice **quietly?** The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi was still worried that they might hear what he had said, **because it is written:** "Curse not the king, no, not in your thought, and curse not the rich in your bedchamber, **for a bird of the air shall carry the voice"** (Ecclesiastes 10:20).

One might ask why Rabbi could not simply have told Antoninus this directly, or at minimum whispered it to him. The Gemara explains that even a whisper was considered too dangerous: *mishum de-chtiv ki of ha-shamayim yolich et ha-kol* — as the *pasuk* in *Kohelet* warns, the birds of the sky will carry the word. The danger of political intelligence leaking, even from a private conversation, is too great. The Gemara adds a parallel teaching: a secret shared between two people is a secret of the heart; shared among three, it is a secret of the street. When political intrigue is involved and powerful parties have an interest in the information, the risk of exposure demands the most indirect possible communication.

### Vegetable Codes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא בְּרַתָּא דִּשְׁמַהּ ״גִּירָא״, קָעָבְדָה אִיסּוּרָא. שַׁדַּר לֵיהּ ״גַּרְגִּירָא״, שַׁדַּר לֵיהּ ״כּוּסְבַּרְתָּא״, שַׁדַּר לֵיהּ ״כַּרָּתֵי״, שְׁלַח לֵיהּ ״חַסָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: Antoninus **had a certain daughter whose name was Gira,** who **performed a prohibited action,** i.e., she engaged in promiscuous intercourse. Antoninus **sent a rocket plant [*gargira*] to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, to allude to the fact that Gira had acted promiscuously [*gar*]. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **sent him coriander [*kusbarta*],** which Antoninus understood as a message to kill [*kos*] his daughter [*barta*], as she was liable to receive the death penalty for her actions. Antoninus **sent him leeks [*karti*]** to say: I will be cut off [*karet*] if I do so. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi then **sent him lettuce [*ḥasa*],** i.e., Antoninus should have mercy [*ḥas*] on her.

The same logic of coded communication governs another episode. Antoninus had a daughter named Gira who was conducting herself improperly — the implication being some form of sexual misconduct. He sent Rabbi a *gargira*, a plant whose name echoed her name. Rabbi sent back *kusbarta* — coriander — punning on *kus barta*, effectively "destroy your daughter." Antoninus then sent *karti*, leeks, playing on *karat*, "cut off," suggesting he would execute her. Rabbi responded with *chasa*, lettuce, playing on *lachus*, to have compassion, counseling mercy instead. This elaborate exchange of vegetables encodes an entire conversation about how to handle a scandalous family situation — a negotiation conducted entirely through culinary puns, both because of the political risks of speaking plainly and because the medium itself allowed plausible deniability.

### Secret Gold Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה (שדר) [מְשַׁדַּר] לֵיהּ דַּהֲבָא פְּרִיכָא בְּמַטְרָאתָא, וְחִיטֵּי אַפּוּמַּיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אַמְטִיוּ חִיטֵּי לְרַבִּי. אֲמַר [לֵיהּ רַבִּי]: לָא צְרִיכְנָא, אִית לִי טוּבָא. אֲמַר: לִיהְווֹ לְמַאן דְּבָתְרָךְ, דְּיָהֲבִי לְבָתְרַאי דְּאָתוּ בָּתְרָךְ, וּדְאָתֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ נִיפּוֹק עֲלַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Every day** Antoninus **would send to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **crushed gold in large sacks, with wheat in the opening of** the sacks. **He** would **say to** his servants: **Bring** this **wheat to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, and they did not realize that the bags actually contained gold. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Antoninus: **I do not need** gold, as **I have plenty.** Antoninus **said:** The gold **should be for those who will come after you, who will give it to the last ones who come after you. And those who descend from them will bring forth** the gold that I now give you, and will be able to pay taxes to the Romans from this money.

In another episode, Antoninus would regularly send Rabbi bags of finely granulated gold concealed under a layer of wheat — *dahava paricha be-matrata ve-chiti apumaihu* — instructing his servants to deliver what appeared to be grain to Rabbi's household. When Rabbi protested that he had no need of the money — *la tzrichna, it li tuva* — Antoninus urged him to accept it anyway: *ve-habu le-man de-batrach de-yehavu le-batrai de-atu batrach* — so that the people who come after you will be able to pay off the people who come after me. The gold was meant to provide a long-term financial cushion for the Jewish community of Tiberias, ensuring that future generations could meet their fiscal obligations to the imperial successors of Antoninus.

### Secret Tunnel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהִיא נְקִרְתָּא דַּהֲוָה עָיְילָא מִבֵּיתֵיהּ לְבֵית רַבִּי, כֹּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה מַיְיתֵי תְּרֵי עַבְדֵי, חַד קַטְלֵיהּ אַבָּבָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי, וְחַד קַטְלֵיהּ אַבָּבָא דְּבֵיתֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּעִידָּנָא דְּאָתֵינָא לָא נַשְׁכַּח גְּבַר קַמָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates anther anecdote involving Antoninus. Antoninus **had a certain** underground **cave** from which there was a tunnel **that went from his house to the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Every day he would bring two servants** to serve him. **He would kill one at the entrance of the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and would kill** the other **one at the entrance of his house,** so that no living person would know that he had visited Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. **He said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **When I come** to visit, **let no man be found before you.**

There was a tunnel connecting Antoninus's residence to Rabbi's house, through which Antoninus would travel daily to meet with Rabbi in private. Each day he would bring two slaves as escorts through the tunnel and then kill one at Rabbi's doorway and the other at his own doorway upon returning, ensuring that no one survived to reveal the secret of their meetings. He had given Rabbi explicit instructions: *be-idana de-atina la nishkach gevar kamach* — when I come to visit, there should be no one present.

The practice of eliminating those who knew about the tunnel was consistent with Antoninus's broader character as portrayed throughout these stories — pragmatic, sometimes brutal, unencumbered by sentimentality about the lives of his slaves. At the same time, the Gemara presents him as genuinely committed to his relationship with Rabbi, which required extraordinary measures of secrecy given the political risks of a Roman emperor being known to consult with the leader of a subject people. One gets the portrait of a man simultaneously Machiavellian and sincere — ruthless in his methods, but authentic in his friendship.

### Rabbi Chanina Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר חָמָא דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב. אָמַר: לָא אָמֵינָא לָךְ בְּעִידָּנָא דְּאָתֵינָא לָא נַשְׁכַּח גְּבַר קַמָּךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵית דֵּין בַּר אִינִישׁ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא עַבְדָּא דְּגָנֵי אַבָּבָא דְּקָאֵים וְלֵיתֵי. אֲזַל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר חָמָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קְטִיל, אֲמַר: הֵיכִי אַעֲבֵיד? אִי אֵיזִיל וְאֵימָא לֵיהּ דִּקְטִיל — אֵין מְשִׁיבִין עַל הַקַּלְקָלָה, אֶשְׁבְּקֵיהּ וְאֵיזִיל — קָא מְזַלְזֵילְנָא בְּמַלְכוּתָא. בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי עֲלֵיהּ וְאַחְיֵיהּ וְשַׁדְּרֵיהּ. אָמַר: יָדַעְנָא זוּטֵי דְּאִית בְּכוּ (מחייה) [מְחַיֵּי] מֵתִים, מִיהוּ בְּעִידָּנָא דְּאָתֵינָא לָא נַשְׁכַּח אִינִישׁ קַמָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **One day,** Antoninus **found that Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama was sitting** there. **He said: Did I not tell you** that **when I come** to visit, **let no man be found before you?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: This is not a human being;** he is like an angel, and you have nothing to fear from him. Antoninus **said to** Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama: **Tell that servant who is sleeping at the entrance that he should rise and come.** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama went** and **found that** the servant Antoninus referred to **had been killed. He said** to himself: **How shall I act? If I go and tell** Antoninus **that he was killed,** this is problematic, as one should **not report distressing** news. If **I leave him and go,** then I would be **treating the king with disrespect. He prayed for** God to have **mercy and revived** the servant, **and he sent him** to Antoninus. Antoninus **said: I know** that even **the least among you** can **revive the dead; but when I come** to visit **let no man be found before you,** even one as great as Rabbi Ḥanina bar Ḥama.

One day when Antoninus arrived through the tunnel, he found Rabbi Chanina bar Chama sitting with Rabbi, violating the standing arrangement. Antoninus reminded Rabbi of his instruction: *amina lach be-idana de-atana la nishkach gevar kamach*. Rabbi replied that Rabbi Chanina bar Chama was not to be considered an ordinary human being — *leit dein bar inish* — meaning that someone of such extraordinary spiritual stature stood in a different category entirely.

Antoninus then tested this claim. He instructed Rabbi to send Rabbi Chanina bar Chama to wake up the slave he had killed at the doorway. Rabbi Chanina bar Chama went and found the slave dead. He faced a genuine dilemma: to return and report the death would be to deliver distressing news — *ein meshivin al ha-kala'ah* — yet to simply leave would be to show disrespect to the king. His resolution was to pray over the dead slave: *ba rachamei alei ve-achayei u-shderei* — he prayed for him, revived him, and sent him in. Antoninus's reaction combined admiration with pragmatism: *yadana zuta de-itchu mechayeh metim* — I see that even the least among your students can perform *techiyat ha-meitim* — but he maintained nonetheless that he did not want visitors present during his meetings with Rabbi.

### Antoninus Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ לְרַבִּי, מַאֲכֵיל לֵיהּ, מַשְׁקֵי לֵיהּ. כִּי הֲוָה בָּעֵי רַבִּי לְמִיסַּק לְפוּרְיָא, הֲוָה גָּחֵין קַמֵּי פּוּרְיָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סַק עִילָּוַאי לְפוּרְיָיךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְזַלְזוֹלֵי בְּמַלְכוּתָא כּוּלֵּי הַאי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי יְשִׂמֵנִי מַצָּע תַּחְתֶּיךָ לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Every day** Antoninus **would minister to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi; **he would feed him** and **give him to drink. When Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **wanted to ascend to his bed,** Antoninus **would bend down in front of the bed** and **say to him: Ascend upon me to your bed.** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said** in response: It is **not proper conduct to treat the king with this much disrespect.** Antoninus **said: Oh, that I were set as a mattress under you in the World-to-Come!**

Every day Antoninus would personally serve Rabbi — bringing him food and drink, acting in the role of attendant. When Rabbi wished to climb into bed, Antoninus would bow before the bed to serve as a step for Rabbi to mount it, in the manner that the Gemara in *Kiddushin* describes children doing for their parents as an expression of *kibbud av va-em*. When Rabbi protested that it was not appropriate to show such disrespect to the Roman emperor — *lav orch arch le-zalzulei be-malchuta kulei hai* — Antoninus responded: *mi yesimeni matza tachteicha le-olam ha-ba* — I only hope that in the *olam ha-ba* I will be worthy to be your mattress. The image is one of total self-abnegation, Antoninus measuring his own spiritual worth against Rabbi's by expressing the desire to serve him even in the world to come.

This led directly to the question that animates the next section: Antoninus asked whether he would actually have any share in *olam ha-ba* at all.

## Edom Olam Ha-Ba

### Esau Actions Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָתֵינָא לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָכְתִיב ״לֹא יִהְיֶה שָׂרִיד לְבֵית עֵשָׂו״! בְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עֵשָׂו. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״לֹא יִהְיֶה שָׂרִיד לְבֵית עֵשָׂו״ — יָכוֹל לַכֹּל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְבֵית עֵשָׂו״, בְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עֵשָׂו.
>
> **Translation:** On another occasion, Antoninus **said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: Will **I enter the World-to-Come?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Yes.** Antoninus **said to him: But isn't it written: "And there shall not be any remaining of the house of Esau"** (Obadiah 1:18)? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi answered: The verse is stated **with regard to** those who **perform actions** similar to those **of** the wicked **Esau,** not to people like you. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: From the verse: **"And there shall not be any remaining of the house of Esau,"** one **might** have thought that this applies **to everyone** descended from Esau, irrespective of an individual's actions. Therefore, **the verse states: "Of the house of Esau,"** to indicate that the verse is stated only **with regard to** those who continue in the way of Esau, and **perform actions** similar to those **of Esau.**

Antoninus asked Rabbi directly: *atena le-alma de-atei* — will I enter *olam ha-ba*? Rabbi affirmed that he would. Antoninus then challenged this: doesn't the *pasuk* in *Ovadia* say *lo yihye sarid le-veit Esav* — there will be no remnant for the house of Esau? Rabbi replied: *be-oseh ma'aseh Esav* — the verse applies only to one who actually performs the deeds of Esau. You, Antoninus, do not. Similarly, Antoninus pressed: does not *Yechezkel* say *shama Edom melacheiha ve-chol nesi'eiha* — there will be destruction for Edom's kings and all its princes? Rabbi replied: *melacheiha ve-lo kol melacheiha, kol nesi'eiha ve-lo kol sareiha* — "its kings" means not all of its kings; "all its princes" means not every single one of its officers.

### Baraita Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָכְתִיב ״שָׁמָּה אֱדוֹם מְלָכֶיהָ וְכׇל נְשִׂיאֶיהָ״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״מְלָכֶיהָ״ — וְלֹא כׇּל מְלָכֶיהָ, ״כָּל נְשִׂיאֶיהָ״ — וְלֹא כׇּל שָׂרֶיהָ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״מְלָכֶיהָ״ וְלֹא כׇּל מְלָכֶיהָ, ״כָּל נְשִׂיאֶיהָ״ וְלֹא כׇּל שָׂרֶיהָ. ״מְלָכֶיהָ״ וְלֹא כׇּל מְלָכֶיהָ — פְּרָט לְאַנְטוֹנִינוּס בֶּן אַסְוִירוּס, ״כָּל נְשִׂיאֶיהָ״ וְלֹא כׇּל שָׂרֶיהָ — פְּרָט לִקְטִיעָה בַּר שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Antoninus **said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **But isn't it written** in the description of the netherworld: **"There is Edom, her kings and all her leaders"** (Ezekiel 32:29)? Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him:** The verse states: **"Her kings," but not: All of her kings,** and likewise it states: **"All her leaders," but not: All of her officers.** Some of them will merit the World-to-Come. **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"Her kings," but not: All of her kings,** and: **"All her leaders," but not: All of her officers.** The inference learned from the wording of the verse: **"Her kings," but not: All of her kings,** serves **to exclude Antoninus the son of Asveirus;** and the inference from the wording: **"All her leaders," but not: All of her officers,** serves **to exclude** the Roman officer **Ketia, son of Shalom.**

A *Baraita* is then cited that confirms and specifies this interpretation. On the clause *ve-lo kol melacheiha* — not all of Edom's kings — the *Baraita* specifies: *perat le-Antoninus bar Asverus* — this excludes Antoninus who served Rabbi. And on *ve-lo kol sareiha* — not every single officer — *perat le-Kti'a bar Shalom* — this refers specifically to Kti'a bar Shalom. The Gemara now turns to tell us who Kti'a bar Shalom was.

### Kti'a Bar Shalom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְטִיעָה בַּר שָׁלוֹם מַאי הָוֵי? דְּהָהוּא קֵיסָרָא דַּהֲוָה סָנֵי לִיהוּדָאֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ לַחֲשִׁיבֵי דְּמַלְכוּתָא: מִי שֶׁעָלָה לוֹ נִימָא בְּרַגְלוֹ, יִקְטָעֶנָּה וְיִחְיֶה אוֹ יַנִּיחֶנָּה וְיִצְטַעֵר? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: יִקְטָעֶנָּה וְיִחְיֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is it** that occurred involving **Ketia, son of Shalom? As there was a certain** Roman **emperor who hated the Jews. He said to the important** members **of the kingdom:** If **one had an ulcerous sore [*nima*] rise on his foot, should he cut it off and live, or leave it and suffer? They said to him: He should cut it off and live.** The ulcerous sore was a metaphor for the Jewish people, whom the emperor sought to eliminate as the cause of harm for the Roman Empire.

*Kti'a bar Shalom mai havah* — what was his story? There was a Roman emperor who harbored intense hatred of the Jews. He assembled the leading dignitaries of his empire and put the following question to them: if a man has a wound on his foot, should he amputate it and live, or leave it and suffer? The answer was obvious: amputate and live. The implication was plain — the Jewish people were the festering wound, and the emperor was proposing to eliminate them so that the empire could thrive. The analogy is chilling precisely because of how many times in subsequent history the same rhetoric recurred: the notion of a minority group as a parasitic drain on the body politic, requiring excision for the host to flourish.

### Four Winds Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ קְטִיעָה בַּר שָׁלוֹם: חֲדָא, דְּלָא יָכְלַתְּ לְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי כְּאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת הַשָּׁמַיִם פֵּרַשְׂתִּי אֶתְכֶם״. מַאי קָאָמַר? אִלֵּימָא (דְּבַדַּרְתְּהוֹן) [דְּבַדַּרִית יָתְכוֹן] בְּאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת, הַאי ״כְּאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת״ — ״לְאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לָעוֹלָם בְּלֹא רוּחוֹת, כָּךְ אִי אֶפְשָׁר לָעוֹלָם בְּלֹא יִשְׂרָאֵל. וְעוֹד, קָרוּ לָךְ מַלְכוּתָא קְטִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ketia, son of Shalom, said to them:** It is unwise to do so, for two reasons. **One** is **that you cannot** destroy **all of them, as it is written: "For I have spread you abroad as the four winds of the heaven,** says the Lord" (Zechariah 2:10). He clarified: **What is it saying? Shall we say that** the verse means that God has **scattered them to the four winds** of the world? If so, **this** phrase: **"As the four winds,"** is inaccurate, since **it should have** said: **To the four winds. Rather,** this is what the verse is saying: **Just as the world cannot** exist **without winds, so too, the world cannot** exist **without the Jewish people,** and they will never be destroyed. **And furthermore,** if you attempt to carry out the destruction of the Jews, **they will call you the severed kingdom,** as the Roman Empire would be devoid of Jews, but Jews would exist in other locations.

Kti'a bar Shalom, however, rose to challenge the emperor's conclusion. He offered two arguments. First, *chada de-lo yachalt lehu le-kuleihu* — you simply cannot kill all of them — *dich-tiv ki ke-arba ruchot ha-shamayim perashti etchem* — for the *pasuk* in *Zecharia* says that God has spread Israel out like the four winds. The Gemara notes that the verse says *ke-arba ruchot*, "like the four winds," not *le-arba ruchot*, "to the four winds." The former does not merely mean that the Jews are dispersed in four directions — it means that just as the world cannot exist without winds, so too the world cannot exist without Israel: *keshem she-iy efshar la-olam belo ruchot, kach iy efshar la-olam belo Yisrael*.

Second, Kti'a bar Shalom warned that attempting to destroy the Jews would result in the empire being called *malchuta keti'a* — a severed, cut-off kingdom. The name *Kti'a bar Shalom* itself encodes this teaching, playing on the root *keta*, to cut off. It is worth noting the recurring observation in the Gemara that certain sages' famous teachings seem to echo their names — whether they were named in anticipation of these ideas or developed the ideas by association with their names is itself a point of scholarly interest.

### Kti'a Execution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵימָר שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרַתְּ, מִיהוּ כֹּל דְּזָכֵי מַלְכָּא שָׁדוּ לֵיהּ לְקָמוֹנְיָא חֲלִילָא. כַּד הֲוָה נָקְטִין לֵיהּ וְאָזְלִין, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ הָהִיא מַטְרוֹנִיתָא: וַוי לֵיהּ לְאִילְפָא דְּאָזְלָא בְּלָא מִכְסָא. נְפַל עַל רֵישָׁא דְּעוּרְלְתֵיהּ קַטְּעַהּ, אֲמַר: יְהַבִית מִכְסַי חֲלֵפִית וַעֲבַרִית. כִּי קָא שָׁדוּ לֵיהּ, אֲמַר: כֹּל נִכְסַאי לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וַחֲבֵירָיו. יָצָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְדָרַשׁ: ״וְהָיָה לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו״ — מֶחֱצָה לְאַהֲרֹן וּמֶחֱצָה לְבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The emperor **said to** Ketia: **You have spoken well** and your statement is correct; **but they throw anyone who defeats the king** in argument **into a house full of ashes [*lekamonya ḥalila*],** where he would die. **When they were seizing** Ketia **and going** to take him to his death, **a certain matron [*matronita*] said to him: Woe to the ship that goes without** paying the **tax.** Ketia **bent down over his foreskin, severed it,** and **said: I gave my tax; I** will **pass and enter. When they threw him** into the house of ashes, **he said: All of my property** is given **to Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues.** How was this inheritance to be divided? The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Akiva went out and taught** that the verse: **"And it shall be for Aaron and his sons"** (Exodus 29:28), means **half to Aaron and half to his sons.** Here too, as Rabbi Akiva is mentioned separately, he should receive half, while his colleagues receive the other half.

The emperor acknowledged the force of Kti'a bar Shalom's argument — *meimar shapir ka'amarta*, you spoke well — but declared that anyone who defeats the king in argument must be thrown into a pit of ashes as punishment. Rashi understands this as a furnace; Rabbeinu Chananel describes it as a burning punishment. As Kti'a bar Shalom was being led away to his death, a Roman noblewoman called out to him: *vai lah le-ilfa de-azla be-lo michsa* — woe to the ship that tries to cross without paying the customs duty. The image was that of a soul departing the world without the proper "payment" to gain entry to the world to come.

Understanding her meaning, Kti'a bar Shalom bent over and circumcised himself on the spot, declaring: *yahavit michsi chalifet va-avarit* — I have paid my customs, now let me cross over. In the shadow of his imminent execution, he performed the sign of the covenant as a final act of commitment to the Jewish people and their God, despite having been born a Roman.

As he was being taken to his death, Kti'a bar Shalom declared: *kol nichsai le-Rabbi Akiva ve-chaverav* — all of my property should go to Rabbi Akiva and his colleagues. Rabbi Akiva then went out and delivered a *drasha* on the *pasuk* that describes the priestly portions of the *lechem ha-panim* and *terumah*: *ve-hayah le-Aharon u-le-vanav*, which is interpreted as splitting the gift half to Aharon and half to all his sons collectively. Accordingly, Rabbi Akiva claimed half of the inheritance for himself alone and the remaining half to be divided among all the other rabbis — a creative and perhaps playful halachic reading that allowed Rabbi Akiva to take a disproportionate share while remaining technically grounded in precedent.

### Bat Kol Approval

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: קְטִיעָה בַּר שָׁלוֹם מְזֻומָּן לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. בָּכָה רַבִּי וְאָמַר: יֵשׁ קוֹנֶה עוֹלָמוֹ בְּשָׁעָה אַחַת, וְיֵשׁ קוֹנֶה עוֹלָמוֹ בְּכַמָּה שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the story of Ketia. **A Divine Voice emerged and said: Ketia, son of Shalom, is destined for life in the World-to-Come.** When **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi heard this, **he wept, saying: There is** one who **acquires his** share in the **World**-to-Come **in one moment, and there is** one who **acquires his** share in the **World**-to-Come only **after many years** of toil.

Immediately following this, a heavenly voice descended and announced: *Kti'a bar Shalom mezuman le-chayei ha-olam ha-ba* — Kti'a bar Shalom has been designated for life in *olam ha-ba*. Rabbi wept upon hearing this and said: *yesh koneh olamo be-sha'ah achat, ve-yesh koneh olamo be-chamah shanim* — there are those who acquire their world in a single moment, and there are those who must acquire it over many years. The teaching is one of the most moving in the tractate. A lifetime of Torah study, *mitzvot*, and ethical striving is of immeasurable value — and yet, in certain extraordinary moments, a single act of self-sacrifice or total commitment can accomplish in an instant what others labor toward over decades. The story of Kti'a bar Shalom, a Roman officer who defended the Jews, circumcised himself as he was being led to execution, and used his final breath to leave his estate to the rabbis, stands as the Gemara's paradigmatic example of that singular transformative moment.

## Adrachan & Rav

### Adrachan Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַנְטוֹנִינוּס שַׁמְּשֵׁיהּ לְרַבִּי, אַדַּרְכָּן שַׁמְּשֵׁיהּ לְרַב. כִּי שְׁכֵיב אַנְטוֹנִינוּס, אָמַר רַבִּי: נִתְפָּרְדָה חֲבִילָה. כִּי שָׁכֵיב אַדַּרְכָּן, אָמַר רַב: נִתְפָּרְדָה חֲבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Antoninus would attend to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, and similarly the Persian king **Adrakan would attend to Rav. When Antoninus died, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The bundle is separated. When Adrakan died, Rav** likewise **said: The bundle is separated.**

The Gemara draws a structural parallel between two great rabbinic leaders and their respective royal admirers. Just as Antoninus would personally serve Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi as his attendant — bringing him food and drink, serving as his step to mount the bed — so too Adrachan, the Persian king, would serve as attendant to Rav.

### Deaths & Eulogies

When Antoninus died, Rabbi delivered what amounted to a eulogy in the form of a terse and profound phrase: *nitpardah chavilah* — the bundle has come undone. The image is of a close bond that had kept two very different things tightly bound together; with Antoninus's death, that bond was released and the bundle fell apart. When Adrachan died, Rav likewise announced *nitpardah chavilah* — the same expression, the same sense of a uniquely close friendship across the boundaries of religion and power having been severed. The parallel structure of these eulogies signals the Gemara's interest in a broader phenomenon: the possibility of genuine, deep friendship between the greatest Torah authorities of their generations and the most powerful rulers of the surrounding world, friendships rooted not in political convenience alone but in mutual respect, personal devotion, and, as the foregoing aggadah has shown, a shared aspiration for something beyond the political.

## Onkelos Story

### Onkelos Conversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּנְקְלוֹס בַּר קְלוֹנִימוּס אִיגַּיַּיר, שַׁדַּר קֵיסָר גּוּנְדָּא דְּרוֹמָאֵי אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, מַשְׁכִינְהוּ בִּקְרָאֵי, אִיגַּיּוּר. הֲדַר שַׁדַּר גּוּנְדָּא דְּרוֹמָאֵי [אַחֲרִינָא] אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא תֵּימְרוּ לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara mentions other Romans who converted to Judaism. It relates: **Onkelos bar Kelonimos converted** to Judaism. The Roman **emperor sent a troop [*gunda*] of Roman** soldiers **after him** to seize Onkelos and bring him to the emperor. Onkelos **drew them** toward him **with verses** that he cited and learned with them, and **they converted.** The emperor **then sent another troop of Roman** soldiers **after him,** and **said to them: Do not say anything to him,** so that he cannot convince you with his arguments. The troops followed this instruction, and took Onkelos with them.

The final section of the daf presents the celebrated story of *Onkelos bar Kolonimus*, who converted to Judaism. *Onkelos ha-Ger* is of course best known as the author of the authoritative Aramaic translation of the Torah that bears his name. His very name is interesting: *Kolonimus* in Greek means "good name" — *shem tov* — and the name appears widely among Italian and Greek Jewish families through the early medieval period, including the Kalonymus dynasty of *paytanim*. Thus *Onkelos bar Kolonimus* might be read as Onkelos who had a good reputation, a *ba'al shem tov*.

The Roman emperor, upon learning of the conversion of so prominent a figure, dispatched a legion of troops — *gunda de-romaye* — to apprehend him. The troops succeeded in taking hold of Onkelos, whereupon he enticed them with *pesukim* from the Torah and they all converted: *igayur*. The emperor sent a second detachment with explicit instructions: *lo temru leh velo midi* — do not say a word to him, do not let him speak.

### Four Winds Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲווֹ שָׁקְלִי וְאָזְלִי, אֲמַר לְהוּ אֵימָא לְכוּ מִילְּתָא בְּעָלְמָא: נִיפְיוֹרָא נָקֵט נוּרָא קַמֵּי אַפִּיפְיוֹרָא, אַפִּיפְיוֹרָא לְדוּכָּסָא, דּוּכָּסָא לְהֶגְמוֹנָא, הַגְמוֹנָא לְקוֹמָא, קוֹמָא מִי נָקֵט נוּרָא מִקַּמֵּי אִינָשֵׁי? אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: לָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא נְקַט נוּרָא קַמֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַה׳ הֹלֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם יוֹמָם וְגוֹ׳״. אִיגַּיּוּר כּוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When they were walking,** Onkelos **said to** the troop of soldiers: **I will say a mere statement to you: A minor official [*nifyora*] holds a torch before a high official [*apifyora*], the high official** holds a torch **for a duke [*dukasa*], a duke for the governor,** and **the governor for the ruler [*koma*]. Does the ruler hold a torch before** the common **people?** The soldiers **said to** Onkelos: **No.** Onkelos **said to them:** Yet **the Holy One, Blessed be He, holds a torch before the Jewish people, as it is written: "And the Lord went before them by day** in a pillar of cloud, to lead them the way, and by night in a pillar of fire, to give them light" (Exodus 13:21). **They all converted.**

When the second group arrived and took hold of Onkelos, he asked permission to say just one brief, non-Torah remark. He pointed to the hierarchical chain of imperial service: a lower official carries the torch for a higher one, who carries it for the duke, the duke for the governor, the governor for the supreme ruler. And at the top? The supreme ruler carries the torch for no one — he is served, not serving. *Amar lehu ha-kadosh baruch hu nakat nura kamei Yisrael* — but the Holy One, blessed be He, carries the torch for Israel, as the *pasuk* states: *ve-Hashem holech lifneihem yomam* — and God went before them by day, providing the pillar of cloud and fire in the desert. All of them converted.

This argument is striking and worth dwelling on. The image Onkelos presents is not of God as distant sovereign but of God as intimate servant of Israel's needs — one who humbles Godself, as it were, to provide for the Jewish people. The relational logic is powerful: if the greatest sign of status in human hierarchies is that one is served rather than serving, then God's having "carried the torch" for Israel — providing them light and guidance in the wilderness — signals a uniquely elevated status for the Jewish people in the divine economy. The Roman soldiers, embedded in a hierarchical culture where this logic was perfectly intelligible, found the argument compelling enough to abandon their mission and join the Jewish people.

### Mezuza Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר שַׁדַּר גּוּנְדָּא אַחֲרִינָא אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא תִּשְׁתַּעוּ מִידֵּי בַּהֲדֵיהּ. כִּי נָקְטִי לֵיהּ וְאָזְלִי, חֲזָא מְזוּזְתָּא דְּמַנְּחָא אַפִּתְחָא, אוֹתֵיב יְדֵיהּ עֲלַהּ, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי הַאי? אָמְרוּ לֵיהּ: אֵימָא לַן אַתְּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם יוֹשֵׁב מִבִּפְנִים, וַעֲבָדָיו מְשַׁמְּרִים אוֹתוֹ מִבַּחוּץ, וְאִילּוּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, עֲבָדָיו מִבִּפְנִים וְהוּא מְשַׁמְּרָן מִבַּחוּץ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ יִשְׁמׇר צֵאתְךָ וּבוֹאֶךָ מֵעַתָּה וְעַד עוֹלָם״. אִיגַּיּוּר תּוּ לָא שַׁדַּר בָּתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The emperor **then sent another troop** of soldiers **after him,** to bring Onkelos, and **said to them: Do not converse with him at all.** The troops followed this instruction, and took Onkelos with them. **While they grabbed him and were walking,** Onkelos **saw a *mezuza* that was placed on the doorway. He placed his hand upon it and said to** the soldiers: **What is this? They said to him: You tell us.** Onkelos **said to them: The** standard **practice** throughout the **world** is that **a king** of **flesh and blood sits inside** his palace, **and his servants** stand **guard,** protecting **him outside; but** with regard to **the Holy One, Blessed be He, His servants,** the Jewish people, sit **inside** their homes **and He guards over them outside. As it is stated: "The Lord shall guard your going out and your coming in, from now and forever"** (Psalms 121:8). Upon hearing this, those soldiers also **converted** to Judaism. After that, the emperor **sent no more** soldiers **after him.**

The emperor, undeterred, sent a third detachment with even stricter orders: *lo tishta-u midi bahadeih* — do not engage with him about anything at all. As this group was escorting Onkelos away, he paused at the doorpost, placed his hand on the *mezuza*, and asked: *mai hai* — what is this? When they said he should explain it to them, he offered his argument: *minhago shel olam, melech basar va-dam yoshev bifnim ve-avadav mishamrim oto mibachutz* — normal human practice has the king sitting within, protected, while his servants stand guard outside. *Va-ilu ha-kadosh baruch hu avadav mibifnim ve-hu mishamram mibachutz* — but the Holy One, blessed be He, has His servants dwelling within while He stands guard outside, as it says *Hashem yishmor tzeitcha u-vo'echa me-atah ve-ad olam* — God will guard your going out and your coming in forever. The *mezuza* placed at the doorpost is the sign of God's watchful presence — not within the protected interior, but at the threshold, guarding those who dwell inside. All of them converted.

### Emperor Surrenders

After this fourth wave of soldiers all converted, the emperor gave up sending any more: *tu lo shadar batre* — he simply stopped dispatching troops. The story has a comic inevitability to it, each successive wave of soldiers falling to the same irresistible intellectual and spiritual force.

The Gemara's account of Onkelos here provides a kind of valediction to the themes of the entire daf. Throughout the daf we have seen the complex relationship between the Jewish people and the empires surrounding them — the halachic question of dating documents according to the regnal calendar of foreign rulers, the political friendship between Antoninus and Rabbi, the courage of Kti'a bar Shalom defending the Jews before the emperor, and now Onkelos demonstrating to successive waves of Roman soldiers that God's relationship with Israel is unlike any other relationship in the world. The practice of touching the *mezuza* upon entering and leaving a room — a *minhag* the Gemara may be connecting to this very episode — embodies that consciousness: the awareness that at the threshold of every doorway stands the divine protection that has accompanied Israel through every exile and every empire.

The *Targum Onkelos* itself, the Aramaic translation of the Torah that Onkelos produced, reflects these same sensibilities. Throughout the translation, Onkelos consistently renders anthropomorphic and anthropopathic descriptions of God in more abstract terms, substituting the *yekara de-Hashem*, the divine glory, or the *shechinta de-Hashem*, the divine presence, wherever the *pasuk* might be read as attributing physical qualities or postures to God. The very verse from Onkelos's *mezuza* argument — the torch carried before Israel in the desert — is itself one that Onkelos's translation handles with characteristic theological care. There is a profound coherence between the convert, the translator, and the teacher: the one who crossed every boundary to join the Jewish people is also the one who spent his life articulating, in the most accessible language, what makes the Jewish people's relationship with God irreducibly singular.
