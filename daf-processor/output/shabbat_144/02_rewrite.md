# Shabbat 144 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Barrel & Squeezing

### Barrel Breaking

The *mishnah* on 144a opens with the case of a barrel that breaks on *Shabbos* and the question of saving wine from it. While this somewhat echoes earlier discussions about saving items from a fire — where there was concern that permitting such activity might lead one to extinguish the flames — the barrel case is harder to understand in terms of any genuine prohibition. The issues raised there involve excessive *tircha* or *uvda d'chol*, though the details need not detain us here.

### Fruit Squeezing

From the barrel, the *mishnah* transitions to the more central concern of squeezing (*sechita*), a topic introduced already at the end of the previous *perek*. The earlier discussion there involved using a sponge to absorb oil — the concern being that one would come to squeeze it out. The *mishnah* then turns to fruit juices: oil and wine that run off from grapes or other fruit, and whether one may benefit from them on *Shabbos*, given the worry that permitting such benefit will lead one to actively squeeze the fruit. On this question, *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Chachamim* disagree. The *Chachamim* appear to prohibit benefit in all cases, while *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that if the fruit was set aside for food (*le'ochalin*), one may benefit from the juices that run off on their own, since the fruit's designation for eating means there is no concern one will come to squeeze it. A simple example: if one is eating grapefruit on *Shabbos* and some juice runs out, may one drink it? According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, since it was set aside for food, yes.

### Three Categories

The *Gemara* distinguishes between three categories of fruit. First, *zeitim va'anavim* — olives and grapes — where the *Gemara*'s working position is that even *Rabbi Yehuda* concedes to the *Chachamim*: even when set aside for food, one may not benefit from the juice that runs off. Second, *tutim v'rimonim* — pomegranates and strawberries — which are squeezed with some frequency, though not as regularly as grapes and olives; this is the category under active debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Chachamim*. Third, *she'ar perot* — all other fruits — which are squeezed only rarely, and here even the *Chachamim* agree that the juice presents no problem at all.

### Frequency vs Status

At first glance, the differences among these three categories appear to be simply a question of frequency: everyone squeezes olives and grapes, pomegranates are squeezed occasionally, and other fruits almost never. The two extremes are agreed upon, and the debate concerns the middle case. However, it is important to note that olives and grapes carry an additional dimension that goes beyond mere frequency.

### Oil & Wine Status

What is produced by squeezing olives and grapes? Olive oil and wine — the paradigmatic liquids of the ancient world, and indeed of *halacha* itself. If one were asked to name the most significant liquids, the answer would be water, olive oil, and wine. The point, then, is not only that squeezing olives and grapes is common, but that *halachically* what is produced carries a recognized status: it has a *shem mashkeh*, a formal identity as a liquid. Notably, olive oil and wine are among the seven liquids that render things susceptible to *tumah v'taharah*.

This is why olives and grapes almost certainly constitute the only category where actually squeezing would violate a *d'oraisa* prohibition. If one were to squeeze an orange, even if it were common to do so and not at all done in an unusual manner, the orange juice would essentially be considered a waste product — and therefore one would not have performed the act of truly producing a *mashkeh*. This is also why the case of olives and grapes is agreed upon not merely because of the heightened concern that one will come to squeeze them, but because of the formal status of what they produce. And crucially, if the distinction among the three categories were purely a matter of frequency, those categories might shift over time or by location — but if the *d'oraisa* turns on the status of olive oil and wine as formal liquids, then that category is stable regardless of changing circumstances.

## Categories Established

### Yehuda Concedes

The *Gemara* had already demonstrated on the previous day that *Rabbi Yehuda* agrees with the *Chachamim* with respect to olives and grapes. Now it turns to the other extreme. Three lines from the bottom of 144a, the *Gemara* states: *ashkechan Rabbi Yehuda d'modei l'Rabanan b'zeitim va'anavim* — we have established that *Rabbi Yehuda* concedes to the *Chachamim* regarding olives and grapes, since there is a heightened concern that one will come to squeeze them (and doing so would constitute a *d'oraisa* violation). Now the *Gemara* asks: *Rabanan d'modei lei l'Rabbi Yehuda b'she'ar peiros minalan?* — how do we know that the *Chachamim* agree with *Rabbi Yehuda* that the juices running off fruits other than pomegranates and strawberries (i.e., the very-rarely-squeezed category) present no problem at all?

### Baraita Permission

The *Gemara* answers from a *baraita*: *sochtin b'fagin u'v'prishin u'v'ozradin* — one may squeeze prunes, quince, and crabapples. According to *Rashi*, *pagin* are prunes, *prishin* are quince, and *ozradin* are crabapples. Not only is there no concern about benefiting from their juices — one may actually squeeze them *lechatchila* on *Shabbos*, since they fall into the category so uncommon as to carry no *issur d'rabbanan* at all. *Aval lo b'rimonim* — but not pomegranates. The reason pomegranates fall into the middle category, at minimum *assur d'rabbanan*, is then illustrated.

### Pomegranate Status

*Shel beis Menashia ben Menachem hayu sochtin rimonim* — in the household of Menashia ben Menachem, they would press pomegranates for juice. This established practice demonstrates that pomegranate-squeezing is not so unusual or strange, and is therefore subject to at least a *d'rabbanan* prohibition.

### D'Oraisa vs Rabbanan

The *baraita* is thus speaking to the *lechatchila* question — not whether one may benefit from juice that ran off, but whether one may perform the act of squeezing at all. The three levels emerge: *zeitim va'anavim* is *d'oraisa*; *rimonim* and *tutim* is *d'rabbanan*; and things that are genuinely uncommon to squeeze are entirely *mutar*.

### Rational Proof

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge: perhaps the *baraita* permitting the squeezing of prunes and crabapples represents only *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position, who is generally more lenient — so the proof that the *Chachamim* agree would fail. The *Gemara* responds: *u'seha nami Rabbi Yehuda* — fine, even if it is *Rabbi Yehuda*, this *baraita* still tells us something new. Until now, we only knew *Rabbi Yehuda*'s leniency with respect to benefiting from juices that ran off on their own (*yatzu me'atzman*); he was never heard to permit squeezing *lechatchila*. So what can one say? *Keivan d'lav b'nei sechita ninhu* — since these fruits are simply not the kinds of things that people squeeze, even *Rabbi Yehuda* permits doing so *lechatchila*. And if that is so, then the *Chachamim* must agree as well, since the entire debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Chachamim* was never about the permissibility of the act of squeezing itself, only about the concern of what the act might lead to. Things that no normal person squeezes — there was never any debate about them at all. *Shema mina Rabanan hi* — this is indeed the *Chachamim*'s position too.

This is a striking demonstration: the proof is established not from any explicit text, but purely from a logical argument. One might have supposed that the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *Chachamim* extended even to the underlying act — that they disagreed about what is *d'oraisa*. But the *Gemara* is convinced otherwise: since these fruits fall into the category of *mutar lechatchila*, this must represent universal agreement.

This raises the important practical question of where to draw the line between the *d'rabbanan* and *mutar* categories. We know the *d'oraisa* category is *zeitim va'anavim* with relative confidence, and perhaps only that — though one might argue that certain strains of fruit grown specifically for juice production (like Valencia oranges) could be treated as *d'oraisa*, since that is their primary purpose. Nevertheless, the prevailing view holds that only olive oil and wine carry *d'oraisa* status. The more interesting line to draw is between *d'rabbanan* and *mutar*, which requires defining how widespread a practice must be before it elevates a fruit into the *d'rabbanan* category. The *Gemara* will address this directly.

## Geographic Prevalence

### Rav Nachman Ruling

Returning to the pomegranate case and the household of Menashia Bar Menachem: *Rav Nachman* rules, *halacha k'shvei Menashe Bar Menachem* — the *halacha* follows the practice of the household of Menashia Bar Menachem, meaning pomegranate-squeezing is treated as *assur d'rabbanan*.

### Rava's Challenge

*Rava* immediately challenges: *Menashe Bar Menachem tana hu?* — is Menashia Bar Menachem a *tana*? He was not issuing a *halachic* ruling; he simply made pomegranate juice. How does one rule "like" a pomegranate-juice producer?

### Braita Author

The *Gemara* clarifies: what *Rav Nachman* meant was that the *halacha* follows the author of the *baraita*, who was concerned with the phenomenon of Menashia Bar Menachem's household and ruled that because of it, pomegranate-squeezing is *assur*. It is a pointed and even somewhat playful exchange — particularly fitting for *Purim* — but the meaning was always clear: the *baraita*'s author took note of that practice as evidence of its prevalence.

### One Person Rule

The *Gemara* now presses harder: *u'mishum d'savar k'Menashe Bar Menachem halacha k'moso?* — just because this *tanna* was concerned about the practice of Menashia Bar Menachem, does that mean we must follow suit? *Menashe Bar Menachem havei ruba d'alma?* — is one individual the majority of the world? If a single unusual person makes pomegranate juice, why should that dictate *halacha* for everyone else?

### Thornbush Precedent

The *Gemara* answers by citing a *mishnah*: *hamkayem kotzim bekerem* — one who maintains thornbushes in his vineyard. Does allowing thornbushes to grow there create a *kilayim* problem, given that thornbushes are not normally cultivated for food? *Rabbi Eliezer* says it does — *kadesh*, the vineyard is sanctified as *kilayim*. The *Chachamim* say it does not: only plants that people normally cultivate are relevant, not things that people routinely uproot.

*Rabbi Chanina* explains *Rabbi Eliezer*'s reasoning: *shebechin be'arvaya mekaymin kotzei sadot legmaleihem* — because in Arabia, people cultivate thornbushes in their fields to feed to their camels. Since it is done in some place, even that creates a concern. This demonstrates that a practice common in some subset of society or some geographic region can have *halachic* implications even elsewhere.

### Arabia Example

The *Gemara* objects: *midi irya?* — is that really analogous? *De'arvaya atra* — Arabia is an entire land, a geographic region where the practice is widespread. Because it is genuinely common in that region, it carries implications beyond. That is not comparable to one individual.

### Region Significance

*Hacha batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — here, a single person who makes pomegranate juice should be entirely irrelevant to everyone else. The *Gemara* thus establishes a key limiting principle: only practices that are truly prevalent in a given geographic region can elevate a fruit into the *d'rabbanan* category. One unusual individual is insufficient. The breadth of the geographic region need not be defined with precision, but the practice must be genuinely widespread there.

### Importing Fruit

This in turn opens a broader discussion. What if the practice is common in a different region, not one's own? What if fruit is imported from that region — so that its juice is in fact common locally, not because people press it locally, but because it arrives in pressed form from elsewhere? And what of a further hypothetical: if a fruit is commonly squeezed in Florida but not locally, and the reason it is not common locally is simply that the fruit is not grown there — if one had access to such fruit, one would certainly squeeze it. Does that theoretical prevalence count?

### Most Fruits D'Rabbanan

Based on these considerations — actual local practice, importation, and what one *would* do if one had access to the fruit — the category of *d'rabbanan* turns out to be extremely broad in practice. Almost everything winds up there. It would take something genuinely obscure — a fruit that virtually no one anywhere ever squeezes for juice — to fall into the *mutar* category. Crabapples may be one such example; it is difficult to think of many others. In practice, we assume grapes and olives are *d'oraisa* and virtually everything else is at minimum *d'rabbanan*.

### Lemon Squeezing

There is, however, an interesting *Beit Yosef* that bears on this. The *Beit Yosef* notes that it was very common to squeeze lemons on *Shabbos* — and not lemons onto fish (squeezing into food, which we will address shortly, and which is *mutar*), but simply squeezing lemon juice to make lemonade or add to tea. He struggles to explain how this could be *mutar* given that lemons are commercially pressed at scale. One argument he attempts — that it is not so common to squeeze lemons for plain lemon juice — is difficult to sustain. The strongest argument he can offer is that no one ever drinks lemon juice straight; it is always mixed with something else, so it never functions independently as a *mashkeh*. Regardless, the practical conclusion remains that almost any fruit, unless genuinely rare, falls into the *assur d'rabbanan* category.

## Water Squeezing

### Libun & Sechita

A question was raised earlier about the *issur* of squeezing water from fabric — why should that present a problem if squeezing certain fruit juices does not? Squeezing water absorbed into fabric involves two distinct concerns. One is *libun* — the act of cleaning. There is a debate among the *rishonim* about whether *libun* applies only when squeezing out water (since the normal way to launder involves soaking in water and then wringing it out) or even when squeezing out other unwanted liquids, such as wine, since ridding the fabric of an undesirable liquid is itself part of the cleaning process.

### Wine vs Others

The second concern is *sechita* proper — extracting liquid that one actually wants, as when fabric has absorbed wine that spilled from the barrel and one then wrings out the fabric to salvage the wine. In that case, there is a genuine act of producing a *mashkeh*. Some hold that if the fabric absorbed a liquid other than wine or olive oil — one that would not carry *d'oraisa* status even if squeezed directly from its original source — then squeezing it from fabric would only be *d'rabbanan*, not *d'oraisa*. This type of squeezing — liquid extracted from cloth or similar material, understood as a form of *mefarek*, severing a desired product from its containing medium — is compared to the fruit-squeezing cases discussed throughout this *daf*, and some authorities apply parallel limits. Since the *mishnah* itself opened with a case involving wine, the comparison is at least apt in that instance.

### Spill Wiping

There are cases where *libun* presents no concern: for example, *derech lichluch* — wiping up a spill, so that rather than cleaning the fabric, one is in fact getting it dirty. Without reaching the full practical *halacha*, the point is that in such circumstances the act cannot be characterized as *libun* at all, and the concern diminishes accordingly.

## Mikvah Invalidation

### Beet Juice Case

The *Gemara* now continues with a case introduced by *Rav Chisda*: *tardin she'sechtan u'nesanan b'mikvah* — one who squeezed beets and placed the beet juice into a *mikvah*. Such beet juice *poslin es ha'mikvah b'shinuy mareh* — it invalidates the *mikvah* by changing its color. The basic rule is that drawn water (*mayim she'uvim*) invalidates a *mikvah* if added before it reaches its full forty *se'ah*, but once the *mikvah* is complete with forty *se'ah*, the problem disappears, and only a quantity of three *log* before completion is problematic. Liquids that are not water, however, invalidate the *mikvah* when they change its color — even when there is already forty *se'ah*, and even in amounts less than three *log*.

### Beet Status

The *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *b'shinuy mareh v'ha lav b'nei sechita ninhu* — why should beet juice invalidate the *mikvah* at all? The color change of the *mikvah* is not itself the problem. If water underwent some chemical reaction and changed color without any foreign substance being introduced, the *mikvah* remains *kasher*. The color change serves only as a measure of the amount of invalid liquid that was introduced. For beet juice to invalidate the *mikvah* by changing its color, it must itself be a halakhically significant liquid — a *mashkeh*. But nobody squeezes beets to make beet juice. This is precisely the point made earlier: it is not merely a question of how common the squeezing is, but whether *halacha* recognizes what is produced as a liquid with formal status.

### Significance Theory

The *Gemara* offers an answer: *keivan d'achshavinehu havu lehu mashkeh* — since one gave this juice significance by choosing to squeeze it, that subjective significance is sufficient to confer upon it the status of a *mashkeh*. And the same applies to *tutim u'rimonim*: *keivan d'achshavinehu*, if one gives it significance by squeezing it, *havu lehu mashkeh*, it thereby acquires *mashkeh* status. *Rashi* notes the striking implication of this position: if subjective significance is what confers *mashkeh* status, then one should be forbidden from squeezing *any* fruit for its juice, since the very act of squeezing constitutes an act of giving significance. On this view, when the *mishnah* permitted squeezing certain fruits, it referred only to squeezing in order to remove excess juice — not to squeezing for the purpose of producing juice. If one squeezes *for the juice*, even unusual fruits would be forbidden, because one's intent has objectively granted the product *mashkeh* status. This is not the *Gemara*'s final position, but it is the position being presented at this stage.

### Rav Papa Answer

*Rav Papa* rejects the underlying premise entirely. Who ever said that something must have a formal *halachic* status as a *mashkeh* in order to invalidate a *mikvah*? *Mishum d'havi davar she'ein osin mimenu mikvah l'chatchilah* — the reason beet juice invalidates the *mikvah* is simply that it is a substance from which one cannot construct a *kosher mikvah* in the first place.

### Mikvah Rule

*Rav Papa*'s principle is: *vechol davar she'ein osin mimenu mikvah l'chatchilah posul es ha'mikvah b'shinuy mareh* — anything that cannot itself serve as a *mikvah* invalidates the *mikvah* when it changes its color. No formal status as a *mashkeh* is required. Even a physically liquid substance, without any *halachic* recognition as a liquid of significance, suffices to invalidate the *mikvah* when it changes its color. The question of whether *Rav Papa* means truly *kol davar* — any substance whatsoever, such as iron rust causing a color change — or whether there must still be some liquid-like character to the substance, remains open. But his point is clear: the formal *halachic* status of *mashkeh* is irrelevant to *mikvah* invalidation.

## Mishnat Mikvah

### Wine & Vinegar

A *mishnah* from elsewhere is now cited: *nafal l'tocho yayin o chometz u'mochal v'shinu mar'av pasul* — if wine, vinegar, or *mochal* (the water that runs off olives, distinct from olive oil itself) fell into the *mikvah* and changed its color, the *mikvah* is invalid. The *Gemara* asks: *man tanna d'mochal mashkeh hu?* — operating still with the assumption that only something with *mashkeh* status can invalidate a *mikvah*, who is the *tanna* who holds that *mochal* has *mashkeh* status?

### Mochal Question

The question is sharp: *mochal* is not olive oil; it is merely the watery runoff from olives. Does *halacha* recognize it as a liquid of significance?

### Abaye Position

*Abaye* answers: the *tanna* in question is *Rabbi Yaakov*, as taught in a *baraita*: *Rabbi Yaakov omer mochal harei hu k'mashkeh* — *mochal* is treated as a *mashkeh*. This ruling is not specifically for *mikvah* purposes; it concerns susceptibility to *tumah*. Only something recognized as a *mashkeh* can render other things susceptible to *tumah*, and *Rabbi Yaakov* holds that *mochal* carries this status.

### Initial Juice

But the same *baraita* immediately qualifies: *u'mah tam amru mochal hayotzei betchilah tahor?* — why then did they say that the initial runoff from an olive is *tahor* and does not become *tamei*? The answer is *l'fi she'eino rotzeh b'kiyumo* — because that initial runoff is something nobody retains; it is simply discarded. (The production of olive oil involves a complex process of crushing and pressing, and the first liquids that emerge in a non-processed way are not treated as a desired product.) What everyone throws away is not accorded formal liquid status.

### Shimon Disagrees

*Rabbi Shimon* takes the opposite position: *mochal eino k'mashkeh* — *mochal* does not have *mashkeh* status at all.

### Three Stages

Yet *Rabbi Shimon* himself must account for the fact that the *mochal* that comes out at the final stage of olive pressing — *mochal hayotzei me'ikar beit habad* — is universally agreed to be *tamei*. He explains: *l'fi she'lo efshar she'lo b'tzitzchei shemen* — at that stage, it is impossible that there are not small droplets of olive oil mixed in. So even *Rabbi Shimon* agrees that the late-stage *mochal* is *tamei* — not because it is itself a *mashkeh*, but because of the olive oil present in it.

### Middle Stage

This yields a clear picture of three stages: the initial runoff (universally *tahor*, because it is discarded and given no significance), the final press-house juice (universally *tamei*, either because it is itself a *mashkeh* per *Rabbi Yaakov*, or because of the olive-oil droplets per *Rabbi Shimon*), and a middle stage. This intermediate stage — *mochal* that comes out after the olives have been mounded and are under pressure but have not yet been formally pressed — would not contain olive oil droplets and is not simply discarded. It is this middle case that separates the positions.

### Mochal Debate

The debate between *Rabbi Yaakov* and *Rabbi Shimon*, then, concerns precisely this intermediate stage. *Rabbi Yaakov* holds that *mochal* has *mashkeh* status and therefore this intermediate juice is *tamei*. *Rabbi Shimon* denies *mochal* any *mashkeh* status and holds this intermediate juice is *tahor*, since it lacks olive oil droplets. *Rava* adds that in any case, to invalidate a *mikvah*, formal *mashkeh* status is not required — anything not suitable for a *mikvah* suffices — so the debate between *Rabbi Yaakov* and *Rabbi Shimon* about *mochal* as a *mashkeh* pertains to *tumah*, not necessarily to *mikvah* invalidation.

## Squeezing Into Food

### Pot vs Bowl

*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel* teaches: *socheit adam eshkol shel anavim letoch hakadera aval lo letoch hake'arah* — a person may squeeze a cluster of grapes into a *kadera* (pot), but not into a *kearah* (bowl). This is striking: grapes and olives are the *d'oraisa* category, yet here the *Gemara* permits squeezing them. The reason is fundamental.

### Liquid to Food

When one squeezes a liquid onto food, the liquid effectively becomes food upon contact. Separating food from food — even if the intermediate form is liquid — is not the same as producing a *mashkeh*. It is analogous to cutting a slice from an apple: one is separating food from food, and that is permissible.

### Absorption Cases

The clearest case is when the juice is entirely absorbed by the food — for instance, squeezing a lemon onto fish without skin, where the juice soaks in completely. There it clearly does not exist independently as liquid once it has merged with the fish. Similarly, squeezing onto couscous that fully absorbs the juice creates a new food, not a separate liquid. Even when some juice runs along the side of the fish rather than being fully absorbed, the *halacha* — though it is not entirely explicit in the *Shulchan Aruch* — is that one may do so.

### Intent Cases

A less obvious case is squeezing onto a salad. The juice sits atop the salad rather than being absorbed into it, and retains its physical liquid form. Nevertheless, the ruling in practice is that anything added to give taste to food is considered part of the food, even if it physically remains liquid. Squeezing lemon onto a salad, because its purpose is to enhance the flavor of the food, is treated as producing food rather than a *mashkeh*. Squeezing into tea, by contrast, is adding flavor to a liquid, and therefore produces a liquid — a problem. One cannot squeeze lemon into soup for the same reason.

### Empty Pot

*Rashi*'s reading of the *Gemara* appears to go even further. The contrast in the text is between *kadera* (pot) and *kearah* (bowl) — not between food-containing and empty vessels. *Rashi* seems to suggest that even an empty pot suffices, because squeezing into a pot — by its very context — indicates that one intends to use the juice as an ingredient in food to be cooked. The context alone defines the product as food rather than liquid, even before any actual food is present. This is a more extreme position, and we do not rule in accordance with it. In practice, the ruling is that the juice must go directly into food. If one squeezes a lemon into a small pouring vessel first and then pours it over the fish, that is *assur* — the intermediate step, even if food is the ultimate destination, breaks the connection required for the juice to be classified as food rather than liquid. The *Chazon Ish*'s rejection of the common workaround — squeezing lemon into a spoonful of sugar before adding it to tea — follows this logic: since the intent is ultimately for the tea (a liquid), the intermediate food step does not help.

## Milking Rules

### Goat Milking

*Rav Chisda* draws an inference from *Shmuel*'s teaching: *cholev adam ez letoch hakadera velo letoch hake'arah* — by analogy to the grape-squeezing case, one may milk a goat directly over a pot but not into a bowl. Squeezing or milking into a pot indicates that the product is destined for food and thus takes on food status; milking into a bowl produces milk as an independent liquid.

### Tosafot Limit

*Tosafot* limits this ruling to *Yom Tov*: on *Shabbos*, one may not milk at all, even into a pot. This is a significant restriction, and it rests on an important conceptual foundation.

### Edible Source

*Tosafot*'s reasoning is as follows: the entire leniency of squeezing-into-food rests on the fact that when one produces *ochel* (food) from *ochel* (food), nothing qualitatively new has been created. The source is edible food, and the product is edible food — the act lacks significance as a creative act. But when one milks a goat on *Shabbos*, the goat is not *ochel*; it cannot be slaughtered and eaten on *Shabbos*. The goat is inedible in its current state. Therefore, whatever one produces from it — whether it ends up as food or liquid — is qualitatively different from the source, which is inedible. That distinction is sufficient to make the act of milking itself a violation on *Shabbos*, regardless of where the milk goes.

### Unripe Fruit

There is a *nafka mina* to *Tosafot*'s position: the case of squeezing unripe, inedible fruit. According to *Tosafot*, if the fruit being squeezed is itself inedible, then even if the juice goes directly into food, one would be *chayav* — because the permissibility of squeezing-into-food rests on the source being *ochel*, and here it is not. The source's inedibility removes the basis for the leniency entirely.

## Mikvah Susceptibility

### Liquid to Food

*Rami Bar Chama* cites the principle: *mashkeh haba le'ochel ochel hu* — liquid that goes into food is food. The question of *haba le'ochel* — whether this means liquid that has actually entered food, or liquid that is intended for food — is a matter of significance that runs through the subsequent cases.

### Zav Milking

*Rami Bar Chama* raises a difficulty against this principle. If a *zav* milks a goat — a *zav* transmits *tumah* not only through touch but through *heset*, moving or shifting an object — then the milk he causes to flow would be *tamei*, since he moves it in the act of milking. This seems straightforward. But the *Gemara* notes the following tension.

### Milk Paradox

If *mashkeh haba le'ochel ochel hu* — if milk destined for food acquires food status — then the milk being milked has *ochel* status, not *mashkeh* status. But then, how did the milk become susceptible to *tumah* in the first place? Food requires contact with a liquid (*mashkeh*) to become susceptible. If the milk is itself food, where is the liquid that rendered it susceptible? The milk can hardly render itself susceptible.

### Initial Drop

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking *Rabbi Yochanan*'s teaching in a parallel case: *betippa meluchlechet al pi hadad* — there is a drop that sits on the surface of the udder before flowing out. *Hacha nami betippa meluchlechet al pi hadad* — here too, there is a small amount of milk on the nipple of the udder before milking begins. This initial drop is not included in what one intends to eat or use as food; it simply sits there at the opening. Since it is not designated for food consumption, it retains its default status as a *mashkeh*. That drop — as a *mashkeh* — renders the surrounding material susceptible to *tumah*. Everything that subsequently flows out passes through or alongside it, and thereby becomes susceptible. Once susceptible, the milk can become *tamei* through the *zav*'s *heset*.

### Impure Squeezing

*Ravina* raises a further case: *temei met shesachat zeitim va'anavim* — someone who is *tamei* from contact with a corpse (*avi hatumah*) is squeezing olives and grapes. Unlike the *zav*, this person transmits *tumah* only through direct contact, not through *heset*. Let us say he is squeezing out exactly a *kebeitzah* (egg's worth) of fruit.

### Juice Tumah

The fruit, while being handled, has not yet come into contact with a liquid. The person cannot make the fruit *tamei* by handling it — food requires liquid contact to become susceptible. He then squeezes out a small amount of liquid. That liquid now touches the remaining fruit, which could thereby become susceptible and then *tamei*. But the remaining fruit, at that point, is less than a *kebeitzah* — the minimum required to transfer *tumah* onward. Therefore, even though the fruit is now susceptible and *tamei* from the *tamei met*'s touch, it cannot make the liquid flowing out *tamei*, because it is too small. The liquid remains *tahor*. If, however, the fruit is more than a *kebeitzah*, then it would be sufficient to make the flowing liquid *tamei*.

But this analysis depends on the juice being classified as a *mashkeh* in the first place. If *mashkeh haba le'ochel ochel hu* applies here as well, the liquid has food status and cannot render the fruit susceptible — so the whole chain of susceptibility would not begin.

### Bowl Squeezing

*Ravina* answers his own question: *besochet letoch hake'arah* — the case under discussion is one where the person is squeezing into a bowl, not a pot. That is, this is a case of ordinary *sechita* to produce juice as a liquid, not squeezing into food. Therefore the juice retains full *mashkeh* status throughout, the chain of susceptibility functions as described, and the question of *mashkeh haba le'ochel* does not apply.

## Bread Gloss

### Grape Gloss

*Rabbi Yannai* (as formulated by *Rabbi Yirmiyah*) cites a tannaitic dispute. The case: one smears the surface of bread with grapes before baking in order to give the bread a shine — analogous to using an egg wash — because the grape juice creates a gloss on the crust as it bakes.

### Not Susceptible

The first opinion: *lo huchshar* — this does not render the bread susceptible to *tumah*. Even though grape juice is a *mashkeh* that normally renders things susceptible, in this case it does not.

### Yehuda Susceptible

*Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees: *huchshar* — this does render the bread susceptible.

### Destroyed Liquid

This debate might appear to mirror the earlier question of whether liquid on food retains *mashkeh* status or becomes food. But *Rav Papa* clarifies that this is not the point of contention. *Dechulei alma mashka al ochel lav ochel hu* — everyone agrees that liquid on food remains a liquid and does not take on food status. The grape juice on the bread is a *mashkeh* in everyone's view, and normally a *mashkeh* renders food susceptible.

### Evaporation Status

The actual dispute, says *Rav Papa*, is *behai mashkeh al ibud kamifligi* — they disagree about a *mashkeh* that is being destroyed. In the course of baking, nearly all of the grape juice evaporates; only a faint gleam remains. One position holds that because the liquid is being destroyed — it will mostly evaporate and leave almost nothing behind — it loses its *mashkeh* status before it can render the bread susceptible. The other position holds that it does not matter: right now, at this moment, it is a liquid, and a liquid renders things susceptible regardless of what will happen to it afterward. The *Gemara*'s analysis of this question will continue.
