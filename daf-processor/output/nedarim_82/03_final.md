# Nedarim 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inuy Nefesh vs. Beino L'…

### Bathing & Makeup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ שׁוּעָל שֶׁמֵּת בַּעֲפַר פִּיר. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ — מֵפֵר, בֵּין בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ, בֵּין בֵּינָהּ לְבֵין אֲחֵרִים. שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ בֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ — מֵפֵר. בֵּינָהּ לְבֵין אֲחֵרִים — אֵינוֹ מֵפֵר. כֵּיצַד? אָמְרָה ״קֻוֽנָּם פֵּירוֹת עָלַי״ — הֲרֵי זֶה יָפֵר. ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֵינִי עוֹשָׂה לְפִי אַבָּא״, ״לְפִי אָחִיךָ״, ״לְפִי אָבִיךָ״, ״לְפִי אָחִי״, וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶתֵּן תֶּבֶן לִפְנֵי בְּהֶמְתְּךָ וּמַיִם לִפְנֵי בְּקָרֶךָ״ — אֵין יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר. ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶכְחוֹל״, ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶפְקוֹס״, וְ״שֶׁלֹּא אֲשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתִי״ — יָפֵר מִשּׁוּם דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** And it cannot be argued that if the woman refrains from bathing or adorning herself, it will negatively impact on her relationship with her husband, **as we do not find a fox dying in the earth of the lair** to which it is accustomed. Similarly, a husband who is accustomed to his wife will not come to avoid engaging in sexual intercourse with her merely because she has not bathed. **It is taught in a *baraita* in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Adda bar Ahava:** Vows taken by a woman concerning **matters that involve affliction** the husband **can nullify, whether** they relate to matters **between him and her or** to matters **between her and others.** As for vows concerning matters **that do not involve affliction,** if they relate to matters **between him and her, he can nullify** them, but if they relate to matters **between her and others, he cannot nullify** them. **How so?** If **she said: Produce is *konam* for me, he can nullify** the vow, as it falls into the category of vows of affliction. If, however, she said: **I will not prepare** anything **for** my **father, as that is *konam*** for me or: **For your brother,** or: **For your father,** or: **For my brother, or: I will not place straw before your animal, or:** I will not place **water before your cattle, he cannot nullify** such vows, as they do not touch upon the relationship between husband and wife, nor do they cause her affliction. A wife said: **I will not paint** my eyes, as **that** is *konam* for me; **I will not rouge [*efkos*]** my cheeks, as **that** is *konam* for me; **or: I will not engage in sexual intercourse,** as **that** is *konam* for me. If she made any of these statements, her husband **can nullify** them, **as** they are **matters that** adversely affect the relationship **between him and her.**

### Rabanan vs. R. Yosi

The Gemara had further examined whether even Rabbi Yosi's category of *devarim shebeino l'veina* would encompass such vows, and concluded that it would. This distinction matters because the two categories carry different halachic weight: *inuy nefesh* allows for full annulment, while *devarim shebeino l'veina* grants the husband only a more circumscribed power.

### Husband's Limited Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָפֵר חֶלְקוֹ, וּמְשַׁמַּשְׁתּוֹ, וּתְהֵא נְטוּלָה מִן הַיְּהוּדִים. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ נֶדֶר עִנּוּי נֶפֶשׁ הָוֵי, אַמַּאי תְּהֵא נְטוּלָה מִן הַיְּהוּדִים? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁבֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ הָוַיִין.
>
> **Translation:** her husband **must nullify his part,** i.e., the part of the vow that affects him, so that she will be permitted to him, **and she may engage in intercourse with him, but she is removed from** all other **Jews,** so that if he divorces her, she is forbidden to everyone. **And if you say that** this **is a vow of affliction, why should she be removed from** all other **Jews?** Wasn't it already established that when a husband nullifies a vow of affliction for his wife, he nullifies it not only with respect to himself but with respect to others as well? Rather, **learn from here** that such vows **are** under the category of **matters that** adversely affect the relationship **between him and her,** and therefore he can nullify it only with respect to himself.

The Gemara underscored an important limitation: even where a vow qualifies as *devarim shebeino l'veina*, the husband can annul only that portion of the vow that directly affects the marital relationship between the two of them. He cannot, by virtue of that annulment, remove the vow's effect on her interactions with all other people. This sets the stage for the Gemara's next inquiry.

## Tashmish HaMita Question

### The Question Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּנַן תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ, מִשּׁוּם דִּ״נְטוּלָה אֲנִי מִן הַיְּהוּדִים״ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי קָתָנֵי לַהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כּוּלֵּיהּ פִּירְקִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא. מִמַּאי — כֵּיוָן דְּקָתָנֵי: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר אֵין אֵלּוּ נִדְרֵי עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ, לְמָה לֵיהּ תּוּ לְמִיתְנֵא ״הֲרֵי זֶה יָפֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **According to the Rabbis, you** still **have the dilemma, because** the mishna dealing with a woman who says: **I am removed from the Jews, was taught by Rabbi Yosei. As Rav Huna said: Our entire chapter is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. From where** do we know this? **Since** the mishna **teaches: Rabbi Yosei says** that **these are not vows of affliction, why does it need to teach further,** at the end of the mishna: **He can nullify** the vow; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei? Learn from this** that **from this** point **forward,** the rest of the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** Accordingly, this mishna teaches us only the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, not that of the Rabbis.

Resuming two lines from the bottom of 81b, the Gemara records that Rava posed a question to Rav Nachman: *tashmish hamita l'Rabbanan* — the denial of marital relations according to the *Rabbanan* — does it constitute *inuy nefesh*, or does it fall instead under *devarim shebeino l'veina*?

The framing of this question is significant. For Rabbi Yosi, we had asked whether the denial of bathing was even sufficient to qualify as *beino l'veina*. The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, operate with a considerably lower threshold for *inuy nefesh* — they are more ready to classify deprivations as genuine self-affliction. The question is therefore whether the *Rabbanan* would regard the denial of sexual relations as crossing into *inuy nefesh*, or whether by its very nature it remains a matter fundamentally about the husband-wife bond and therefore categorized as *beino l'veina*.

### Rav Nachman's Answer

Rav Nachman responded that the answer may be derived from a *mishnah*. The *mishnah* teaches the case of a woman who declares *netulah ani min hayehudim* — that she is "removed from Jews" — meaning she has forbidden herself from receiving sexual benefit from any Jew. In such a case, the husband *yafar chelko*, he annuls his portion of the vow, with the result that *u'meshameshta*, she may have marital relations with him. However, should the couple ever divorce, she remains forbidden to all other Jewish men — an outcome that would make remarriage exceedingly difficult. What the *mishnah* thus demonstrates is that the husband's power extends only to that dimension of the vow that affects him personally. He cannot remove the vow's restriction vis-à-vis the rest of the world. The implication of Rav Nachman's proof is that denial of sexual relations is treated as *beino l'veina* rather than as *inuy nefesh*, since if it were *inuy nefesh* the husband would possess full annulment power.

### Rava's Objection

Rava pushes back: *l'Rabbanan ki ba'ei lach* — that was the question I asked according to the *Rabbanan*. But the *mishnah* Rav Nachman cited is *Rabbi Yosi katani lah* — it reflects the view of Rabbi Yosi. And Rabbi Yosi, as established, holds a far more restrictive definition of *inuy nefesh*. For Rabbi Yosi, bathing and makeup do not constitute *inuy nefesh*, so it follows naturally that sexual relations, too, would fall outside *inuy nefesh* and be classified as *beino l'veina* at most. The question was what the *Rabbanan* hold, and citing a *mishnah* that reflects Rabbi Yosi's position provides no answer.

### Attribution to Rabbi Yosi

This prompts a striking observation: *de'amar Rav Huna kulei pirkan Rabbi Yosi hi* — Rav Huna states that the entire remainder of this *perek* is authored by Rabbi Yosi. The basis for this claim is a textual cue in the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* concludes with the phrase *divrei Rabbi Yosi*, explicitly attributing a statement to Rabbi Yosi. The Gemara reads this as not merely closing out one ruling but as introducing Rabbi Yosi's voice as the governing voice for all subsequent *mishnayos* in the *perek* — *mikan v'eilach Rabbi Yosi hi*. This is a remarkable hermeneutic move: the repetition of *divrei Rabbi Yosi* at the end of a *mishnah* is taken as signaling that Rabbi Yosi is the *stam* author of what follows.

The practical consequence is that Rava's objection stands: the *mishnah* Rav Nachman cited, which shows that *tashmish* qualifies only as *beino l'veina* and not *inuy nefesh*, reflects Rabbi Yosi's narrower standard. It offers no guidance regarding the *Rabbanan*'s position.

### Nature of Marriage

The Gemara therefore leaves the question unresolved. The Ran, in the narrow columns, notes that since the question was not definitively answered — *baya v'lo ipashta* — we adopt the stringent position: *nekitinan l'chumra v'eino meifer ela devarim she'beino l'veina*. The husband cannot perform a blanket annulment of such a vow; his power extends only to its *beino l'veina* dimension.

The Ran, however, adds that some authorities infer from a later statement of Shmuel — which we will encounter shortly — that even without explicit Talmudic resolution, *tashmish* falls within the *inuy nefesh* category according to the *Rabbanan*.

This ambiguity is genuinely striking. The *rishonim* note — in connection with other halachic discussions, such as the question of whether a woman in a dangerous medical situation may use certain forms of birth control — that the *halacha* cannot simply counsel a couple not to have marital relations. Sexual relations are regarded as constitutive of marriage itself. Given that, the notion that a permanent denial of sexual relations might not rise to the level of *inuy nefesh* — while not bathing does — presents an intuitive challenge that the Gemara's inconclusiveness does not fully resolve.

The classification of *inuy nefesh* also raises a related question of method. The Gemara appears to operate with formal, categorical judgments: once a category of deprivation — food, bathing, makeup — is deemed *inuy nefesh*, it applies as a class regardless of individual variation. The *mishnah* does not ask whether a particular woman cares greatly about makeup; it classifies makeup categorically. This formal approach raises questions about scope and duration that the Gemara gestures toward but does not resolve here.

## Shmuel's Principle

### Shmuel's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּלֵוִי: כׇּל נְדָרִים בַּעַל מֵפֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, חוּץ מִן ״הֲנָאָתִי עַל פְּלוֹנִי״, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵפֵר. אֲבָל ״הֲנָאַת פְּלוֹנִי עָלַי״ — מֵפֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said in the name of Levi: A husband can nullify all vows** of affliction **for his wife, except for** the vow: **Benefit** from **me** is *konam* **for so-and-so, which he cannot nullify,** as it is entirely between her and another person. **But** if she says: **Benefit** derived **from so-and-so** is *konam* **for me, he can nullify** the vow, as it considered a vow of affliction, since she might one day need that person and be unable to avail herself of his services due to her vow.

### One Source Suffices

Here is Shmuel's *chiddush*: *hana'as Ploni alai meifer* — if she says "I may not receive benefit from Reuven," the husband *can* annul it. This is categorized as *inuy nefesh*. The innovation is that *inuy nefesh* is not assessed by asking: if this vow stands, will she actually be living in an afflicted state? The answer to that question might well be no — there are many other people who can provide her with fruit, assistance, or whatever benefit she has forsworn. Instead, Shmuel holds that the determination depends on whether the *type* of thing being denied is intrinsically a form of *inuy nefesh*. If fruit in general constitutes *inuy nefesh*, then denial of fruit from even a single source is sufficient to invoke the *inuy nefesh* category, and the husband may annul the vow.

This is a significant *chiddush* — and, as the Gemara immediately observes, one that appears to collide directly with our *mishnah*.

### Mishnah's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: ״פֵּירוֹת מְדִינָה זוֹ עָלַי״ — יָבִיא לָהּ מִמְּדִינָה אַחֶרֶת, אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: דְּקָאָמְרָה שֶׁתָּבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection from that which **we learned** in the mishna: If she said: **The produce of this country** is *konam* **for me,** he cannot nullify the vow, as it does not involve affliction, since **he can** still **bring her** produce **from another country.** This vow is similar to a vow by which she prohibits herself from deriving benefit from another person. Why, then, does Shmuel say that the husband cannot nullify it? **Rav Yosef said:** The mishna is referring to a woman **who said** in her vow: **That you bring.** In other words, she did not prohibit herself from deriving benefit from the produce of that country entirely, but only from the produce that her husband himself would bring her. She may still enjoy that produce if it is brought to her by someone else or if she brings it for herself.

The *mishnah* states: *peirot medinah zo alai* — if a woman declares that the fruit of a particular country is forbidden to her, *yavi lah mimedinah acheret* — her husband should bring her fruit from a different country, and since she retains access to the same type of item through another source, the vow does not constitute *inuy nefesh*, and the husband cannot annul it. This is a direct challenge to Shmuel's position: the *mishnah* explicitly rules that if another source is available, there is no *inuy nefesh*.

### Difficult Reading Propos…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״פֵּירוֹת חֶנְוָנִי זֶה עָלַי״ — אֵין יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר. הָכָא נָמֵי דְּקָא אָמְרָה שֶׁתָּבִיא אַתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection from the next clause of the mishna: **Come** and **hear:** If the woman took a vow saying: **The produce of this storekeeper is** *konam* **for me,** her husband **cannot nullify** the vow. But didn't Shmuel say that if a woman prohibits herself from benefiting from a certain person, her husband can nullify the vow? The Gemara answers: **Here too,** the mishna is referring to a case **where she said** in her vow: The produce **that you bring** from this storekeeper is *konam* for me.

To salvage Shmuel's position, Rav Yosef — and the Gemara working through this — proposes a forced reading of the *mishnah*. The case, on this reading, is not that she simply forbade herself from a country's fruit; rather, she said: "I may not have the fruit of this country *that you, my husband, bring.*" On this interpretation, she has not really denied herself access to that fruit at all — she can still obtain it through anyone else. Because the deprivation is purely contingent on the husband's role as provider, and she retains full access through any other channel, it falls outside *inuy nefesh* and the husband cannot annul it.

The difficulty with this reading is not merely that it strains the plain meaning of the *mishnah*. It also requires reconciling it with a subsequent case in the same *mishnah*: *peirot Chonvoni ze alai* — if she says that the fruit of a specific storekeeper is forbidden to her, the *mishnah* rules that the husband likewise cannot annul it. The Gemara suggests the same forced reading: she is only denying herself the storekeeper's fruit as conveyed by her husband; anyone else may still bring it to her. This reading is admittedly far from *peshat*.

### Sole Provider Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הָיְתָה פַּרְנָסָתוֹ אֶלָּא מִמֶּנּוּ — הֲרֵי זֶה יָפֵר. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּקָא אָמְרָה שֶׁ״תָּבִיא אַתָּה״, אַמַּאי יָפֵר? אֶלָּא מִדְּסֵיפָא דְּלָא מַיְיתֵי בַּעַל הָוֵי, רֵישָׁא דְּקָא מַיְיתָא הִיא! אֶלָּא: רֵישָׁא אֵין יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר, וּדְקָא מַיְיתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this resolution: But the continuation of the mishna states: But **if** the husband can obtain **his sustenance only from him,** i.e., that particular storekeeper, **he can nullify** his wife's vow. **And if you say** that this is referring to a case **where** the woman **said** in her vow: The fruit **that you bring** from this storekeeper is *konam* for me, **why can** the husband **nullify** her vow? Other people can bring her the fruit on his behalf. **Rather, from** the fact that **the latter clause** of the mishna must be dealing with a case where the woman renders all fruit forbidden to herself, even that which **the husband does not bring her, the first clause** must also refer to a case where the woman renders forbidden even the fruit **that she** herself **brings,** and nevertheless the husband cannot nullify the vow. Therefore, the objection raised against Shmuel remains. **Rather,** the Gemara retracts its previous answer and explains the matter as follows: In the **first clause** the husband **cannot nullify** his wife's vow, **and** the case is one where she renders forbidden even the fruit **that she** herself **brings.**

The forced reading collapses under the *mishnah*'s own internal evidence. The *mishnah* continues: *lo hayta parnasato ella mimenu* — if the husband's sole source of food provision is this very storekeeper, then *harei ze yafer*, the husband may annul the vow. The reason is that in such a circumstance, she is genuinely denied access to that fruit regardless of any alternative channel.

But if the entire *mishnah* is about vows she made only contingent on the husband being the one who conveys the fruit — then even in the case of the sole-provider storekeeper, her access is not actually blocked: someone other than the husband could simply go to that same storekeeper and bring the fruit to her. Why, then, does the *mishnah* permit annulment in the sole-provider case? The forced reading cannot account for this.

### Mishnah's Plain Reading

The Gemara concludes: *ela mide'seifa delo maisi ba'al* — since the end of the *mishnah* clearly envisions a case where the husband's conveyance is not the operative variable, it follows that the *reisha* likewise is not conditioned on who brings the fruit. She has forbidden the fruit to herself categorically, regardless of the source. *Harei reisha ein yachol lehafer* — the husband cannot annul in the opening case simply because she can always obtain the same fruit elsewhere.

The plain reading of the *mishnah* thus stands as a straightforward rejection of Shmuel's position. The *mishnah* holds that so long as another source exists, the vow does not constitute *inuy nefesh*, and the husband has no power to annul it.

However, Shmuel has a response: *u'matnitin Rabbi Yosi hi* — as Rav Huna established, the entire *mishnah* in this *perek* reflects Rabbi Yosi's view. For Rabbi Yosi, who operates with a narrow definition of *inuy nefesh*, it makes sense that deprivation must be total — from all sources everywhere — before *inuy nefesh* applies. The *Rabbanan*, who operate with a broader category, would hold that even denial from a single source suffices. Shmuel therefore aligns himself with the *Rabbanan* and is undisturbed by the *mishnah*'s apparent contradiction, since that *mishnah* represents Rabbi Yosi's stricter position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כּוּלֵּיהּ פִּרְקִין רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא. וּמַאי ״אֵין יָכוֹל לְהָפֵר״, מִשּׁוּם עִינּוּי נֶפֶשׁ, אֲבָל מֵפֵר נְדָרִים שֶׁבֵּינוֹ לְבֵינָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who is more restrictive in his definition of affliction. **As Rav Huna said: Our entire chapter is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** Shmuel, on the other hand, rules in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis. **And what** does Rabbi Yosei mean when he says that the husband **cannot nullify** his wife's vows? He means that he cannot nullify them **as** vows of **affliction, but he can nullify** them as **vows that** adversely affect the relationship **between him and her.**

The corollary noted parenthetically by the Gemara — *meifer nedarim she'beino l'veina* — is that even where Shmuel cannot establish full *inuy nefesh*, the *beino l'veina* avenue may yet be available, though the Ran notes this is not a logical continuum: something may simply not bear on the marital relationship at all and therefore not qualify on either ground.

## Two Loaves Case

### Two Loaves Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נָדְרָה מִשְׁתֵּי כִכָּרוֹת, בְּאַחַת מִתְעַנָּה, וּבְאַחַת אֵין מִתְעַנָּה, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהוּא מֵפֵר לַמִּתְעַנָּה — מֵפֵר לְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִתְעַנָּה. וְרַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵפֵר לַמִּתְעַנָּה, וְאֵין מֵפֵר לְשֶׁאֵין מִתְעַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** In a case where a woman **vowed** that **two loaves** are forbidden to her, and if she abstains **from one** of them she would **deprive herself,** as it is a fine-quality loaf, **and** if she abstains **from** the other **one** she would **not deprive herself,** as it is a poor-quality loaf, then, **since** the husband **can nullify** the vow **with regard to the** loaf that would cause her to **deprive herself,** like any other vow of affliction, **he can** also **nullify** the vow **with regard to the** loaf that would **not** cause her to **deprive herself. And Rav Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The husband **can nullify** the vow **with regard to the** loaf that would cause her to **deprive herself, but he cannot nullify** the vow **with regard to the** loaf that would **not** cause her to **deprive herself.**

The Gemara now presents a new case, transmitted by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel. A woman takes a vow forbidding herself from two loaves of bread. One is fine white bread — *b'achat nit'aneh*, a genuine deprivation not to be permitted to eat it. The other is bran bread or a stale inferior loaf — *b'achat ein nit'aneh*, something whose loss imposes no meaningful affliction. Half of her single, unified vow falls within *inuy nefesh*; half does not.

It is worth pausing to note the underlying assumption: that even a vow about a single loaf of bread — a narrow scope — is treated categorically as *inuy nefesh* once food of that quality is in the *inuy nefesh* class. The Gemara does not evaluate the magnitude of the deprivation in context; it reasons formally from the category.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel rules: *mitoch shehu meifer l'mit'aneh, meifer l'she'eino mit'aneh* — because the husband can annul the portion of the vow that involves genuine *inuy nefesh*, he can annul both portions of the vow. The vow was made as a single, unified utterance; and since the husband holds annulment power over part of it, Shmuel extends that power to the whole.

### Ran's Explanation

The Ran explains the logic carefully. The Gemara will establish later that the husband cannot perform a *hafara* on only *part* of a vow: *hafara* is all-or-nothing. He cannot say "I annul this vow for three days but not the rest," or "I annul it for these fruits but not those." The *pasuk* indicates that his act of *hafara* must operate on the vow as a whole.

Given this constraint, the husband confronts two options: either he has no power at all — because he cannot do a complete *hafara* of a vow over which he only has partial authority — or we must extend his authority to cover the whole vow, precisely because only a complete *hafara* is a valid act of *hafara*. Shmuel adopts the latter position: since the husband is entitled to annul the *inuy nefesh* portion, and since *hafara* can only be executed on the vow in its entirety, we conclude that he is therefore empowered to annul the whole. The Ran articulates this not merely as a consequence of *hutar miktzato hutar kulo* — the principle that once part of a prohibition is dissolved the rest dissolves with it — but as a direct requirement of how *hafara* functions: the act itself must engage the entire vow, and so the husband must be regarded as authorized to perform it completely.

### Beino L'Veina Exception

One might wonder how this all-or-nothing logic squares with the earlier case of *netulah ani min hayehudim*, where the husband annuls only *chelko* — his portion — leaving the prohibition with respect to all other men intact. The Ran explains that *beino l'veina* is categorically different in kind from *inuy nefesh* annulment. When a husband annuls a vow on grounds of *beino l'veina*, he is not undoing the *neder* wholesale; he is exercising an override rooted in the fact that the vow impinges upon *their* relationship. The annulment is inherently relational and partial in nature — it removes the vow's force to the degree that it affects him, while leaving the rest untouched. This is why, for instance, if the couple divorces, the prohibition reasserts itself: the *hafara* never dissolved the underlying *neder*, it only suspended it within the bounds of the marriage. *Beino l'veina* annulment is by its very nature targeted and relational, not a complete removal of the *neder*. A *hafara* grounded in *inuy nefesh*, by contrast, is a substantive abrogation of the *neder* itself — and therefore must be complete.

## R. Yochanan's View

### Partial Annulment Only

Rabbi Yochanan — or in one version, Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — disagrees with Shmuel's position on the two-loaves case. His ruling is *meifer l'mit'aneh v'eino meifer l'she'eino mit'aneh*: the husband annuls only that portion of the vow that constitutes *inuy nefesh*, and the part that is not *inuy nefesh* remains in force. Rabbi Yochanan does not accept the all-or-nothing logic that Shmuel invokes. In his view, the husband's authority is precisely calibrated to his domain: he has complete power over *inuy nefesh* and no power beyond it, so the non-*inuy nefesh* component of the vow stands unaffected.

### Nazirite Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּדְרָה בְּנָזִיר, וְהָיְתָה שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן, וּמִטַּמְּאָה לְמֵתִים
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi **raised an objection** against Rabbi Yoḥanan from the following mishna (*Nazir* 23a): With regard to **a woman who vowed** to be **a nazirite, and she** transgressed her vow and **drank wine or became impure** by coming into contact **with the dead,**

The Gemara raises an objection against Rabbi Yochanan's position from a *mishnah* concerning *nezirus*. The *mishnah* teaches: if a woman took a vow of *nezirus* and was going around drinking wine and contracting corpse-impurity — thereby violating her *nezirus* — she receives lashes. If, however, her husband annulled the *nezirus* without her knowledge, and she continued drinking wine and contracting impurity thinking she was violating it, she does not receive lashes — because objectively, the *nezirus* had already been dissolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי זוֹ סוֹפֶגֶת אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים. הֵפֵר לָהּ בַּעְלָהּ וְהִיא לֹא יָדְעָה שֶׁהֵפֵר לָהּ, וְהָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמְּאָהּ לְמֵתִים — אֵינָהּ סוֹפֶגֶת אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **she incurs [*sofeget*] the forty** lashes, the penalty for one who transgresses a Torah prohibition, as she violated the terms of her nazirite vow. If **her husband nullified** the vow **for her, but she did not know that he nullified it for her, and she drank wine or became impure through** contact with **the dead, she does not incur the forty** lashes. She did not commit a transgression, as her nazirite vow was nullified.

The Gemara's challenge focuses on this case. The objection asks: if Rabbi Yochanan is correct that a husband can only annul the *inuy nefesh* portion of a vow, perhaps when the husband annulled the *nezirus* he only annulled the wine prohibition — which is the aspect that causes genuine deprivation, *inuy nefesh* — while leaving intact the prohibition against grape skins and grape pits, and possibly the *tumas meis* prohibition. If so, might she still be liable for violating those remaining prohibitions, even after his annulment?

### Nazirite is Package Deal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מֵפֵר לַמִּתְעַנָּה וְאֵין מֵפֵר לְשֶׁאֵין מִתְעַנָּה, דִּלְמָא מִן יַיִן דְּאִית לַהּ צַעֲרָא — הֵפֵר לָהּ, מִן חַרְצָן וּמִן זַג — לֹא הֵפֵר לָהּ, דְּהָא לָא אִית לַהּ צַעֲרָא. וְתִסְפּוֹג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֵין נְזִירוּת לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that the husband **can nullify** the vow **with regard to the** loaf that would cause her to **deprive herself, but he cannot nullify** the vow **with regard to the** loaf that would **not** cause her to **deprive herself,** the same reasoning should apply to a nazirite vow: **Perhaps** the husband **nullified for her** the vow that rendered **wine** forbidden **to** her, **as she suffers pain** when she refrains from drinking it. But as for her vow that rendered grape **seeds and** grape **skins** forbidden **to** her, **he did not nullify** it **for her, as she suffers no pain** when she abstains from them. And since even grape seeds and grape skins are forbidden to a nazirite, if the woman ate of them, **she should receive the forty** lashes, even if her husband nullified her vow. **Rav Yosef said:** Here it is different, as **naziriteship cannot take effect partially.** Since one cannot be a nazirite and accept only some of the prohibitions of naziriteship, the husband's nullification cancels the entire vow. In the case of an ordinary vow, on the other hand, the husband can nullify only the part that causes his wife suffering.

Rav Yosef resolves the objection: *ein nezirus l'chatzoim* — there is no such thing as a partial *nezirus*. A *nezirus* is a unified, indivisible package. One cannot be a *nazir* only with respect to wine, or only with respect to *tumas meis*. The obligations of *nezirus* — the prohibition of wine and all grape products, the prohibition of corpse-impurity, and the prohibition of cutting one's hair — constitute an all-or-nothing commitment. Therefore, when the husband annulled his wife's *nezirus* by virtue of his power over the *inuy nefesh* aspect — the wine prohibition — the entire *nezirus* necessarily dissolved, because there is no intermediate state of a partial *nezirus* that could remain.

This is what distinguishes the *nezirus* case from the two-loaves case. In the two-loaves case, it is entirely possible for the vow to stand as to one loaf and be annulled as to the other — there is a coherent residual state. In *nezirus*, no such residual state exists. And because *ein nezirus l'chatzoim*, the husband's annulment of the wine aspect brings the entire edifice down. There is therefore no contradiction with Rabbi Yochanan: his *hafara* works precisely as he describes — it annuls the *inuy nefesh* component — and as a consequence, because of the indivisible nature of *nezirus*, the whole vow dissolves.

### Abaye's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הָא קׇרְבָּן לַחֲצִי נְזִירוּת אִיכָּא?! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵין נְזִירוּת לַחֲצָאִין, וְאֵין קׇרְבָּן לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** The wording of your statement suggests that naziriteship cannot take effect partially, **but** that **an offering is** brought **for partial** observance of **naziriteship. Rather, Abaye said** that one should say as follows: **Naziriteship cannot take effect partially, and no offering is** brought **for partial** observance of naziriteship.

Abaye accepts Rav Yosef's resolution but sharpens the formulation. Rav Yosef said *ein nezirus l'chatzoim* — there is no half-*nezirus* — which speaks to the qualitative indivisibility of *nezirus*: one cannot be a *nazir* for only some of the prohibited categories. Abaye adds the parallel point about duration: *ein nezirus l'chatzoim bein korban l'chatzi nezirus* — there is no half-*nezirus* in duration either, meaning that if a woman began a *nezirus* of, say, thirty days and her husband annulled it after fifteen days, she cannot bring the *korban* associated with completing a *nezirus*, because a *korban* requires the full term to have been fulfilled. Abaye wished to make explicit that the indivisibility of *nezirus* applies both to its qualitative scope and to its temporal duration.

The broader debate between Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan regarding the two loaves thus remains intact. Shmuel holds that once the husband possesses authority over the *inuy nefesh* component of a unified vow, he must be understood as empowered to annul the whole — because *hafara* is by nature an act on the complete *neder*, and empowering him to act on part necessarily requires empowering him to act on the whole. Rabbi Yochanan holds that the husband's authority is precisely and exclusively coextensive with *inuy nefesh*: he annuls everything within that domain and leaves everything outside it entirely untouched.
