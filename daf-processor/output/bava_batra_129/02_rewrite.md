# Bava Batra 129 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Matnat Shachiv Mera

### Overview & Context

The daf opens with the topic of *matnat shachiv mera* — the deathbed gift. When a dying person divides his property, the legitimacy of the transfer depends entirely on how the declaration is framed. If he frames it as a *yerusha*, an inheritance, the gift is ineffective, because the laws of inheritance are defined by the Torah and cannot be unilaterally altered. A person cannot, for instance, give the *bechor* a larger share, nor can he redirect the *yerusha* to a younger son. If, however, he frames the bequest as a *matana*, a gift, it takes effect through the rabbinic institution of *matnat shachiv mera*.

What makes this institution striking is precisely how far the Rabbis went with it. On the one hand, the Torah is emphatic that inheritance cannot be reassigned. On the other hand, through devices such as *heter iska*, *pruzbul*, and *matnat shachiv mera*, the Rabbis found legitimate means to achieve, in practical terms, exactly that kind of redistribution — provided the person frames the bequest as a gift rather than as an inheritance. The Rabbis even made it easier by allowing the verbal gift of a *shachiv mera* to take effect without a formal *kinyan*.

### Mishna Principle

The Mishna adds a further liberalization: even if the dying person's declaration sounds predominantly like an attempt to reassign a *yerusha*, as long as the word *matana* appears anywhere in the declaration — at the beginning, middle, or end — the Rabbis will seize upon that word and use it to interpret the entire declaration as a gift. This reflects a clear rabbinic agenda to give the dying person the broadest possible latitude to dispose of his property as he sees fit.

---

## Location of Matana

### Rav Dimi's Examples

The Gemara asks: what exactly does it mean for *matana* to appear at the beginning, middle, or end of a declaration? Rav Dimi, transmitting a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan upon his return from Eretz Yisrael, provides the three cases.

*Matana* at the beginning: "Let this field be given to Reuven, and let him inherit it." Even though the declaration ends with the language of *yerusha*, the opening word of giving is sufficient to define the entire bequest as a gift.

*Matana* at the end: "Let Reuven inherit this field and give it to him." Even though the declaration opened with the language of *yerusha*, the concluding word of giving is sufficient.

*Matana* in the middle: "Let him inherit it, and give it to him, and let him inherit it." Even with the language of *yerusha* appearing both before and after, the single word of *matana* in the middle dominates and defines the entire declaration as a gift.

The underlying principle is clear: no matter how heavily the language of *yerusha* dominates, the presence of the word *matana* anywhere in the statement is enough to render the bequest effective.

### Same Person & Field

This principle, however, comes with a restriction. It applies only when the declaration concerns a single person and a single field. In that case, the word *matana* — wherever it appears — is understood to define the entirety of what the dying person is doing, and the entire bequest is treated as a gift.

### Different People/Fields

The moment the declaration involves either more than one person or more than one field, the word *matana* can no longer be stretched to cover everything. It applies only to the specific person and the specific field to which it directly refers.

Consider the case of one person and two fields: if the dying person says "give Reuven my field on Central Park, and let him inherit my field on Riverside," the word of giving covers only the first field, which is therefore an effective gift. The second field, designated with the language of *yerusha*, does not transfer. Similarly, if one field is given to two people — "give it to Reuven, and let Shimon inherit it" — the word of giving applies only to Reuven, and Reuven alone acquires the field. In neither case does the *matana* language "borrow" across to the other person or the other field.

## Three Positions Debate

### Rav Dimi Version

This is the position attributed to Rabbi Yochanan by Rav Dimi: the word *matana* can define the entirety of the bequest only when there is a single person and a single field. Both conditions must be met simultaneously.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Elazar takes a more lenient view. He holds that even when there is one person and two fields, or one field and two people, the word *matana* is sufficient to render the entire bequest effective. His reasoning is that some common element — either the same recipient or the same property — links all parts of the declaration, and that link is sufficient to allow the word of giving to extend across the entire statement.

Thus: if the dying person says "give Reuven my field on Central Park and let him inherit my field on Riverside," since both fields are going to Reuven and the word *matana* was used for one of them, it applies to both. Or if he says "give Reuven my house and let Shimon inherit it," since both people are receiving the same property and the word *matana* was used for one of them, it applies to both.

Where Rabbi Elazar draws the line is at two different people and two different fields simultaneously — for instance, "give Reuven field A and let Shimon inherit field B." Here there is no common thread between the *matana* and the *yerusha* parts of the declaration, and the word of giving cannot be stretched to cover the portion designated by the language of *yerusha*.

### Ravin Version

When Ravin arrived from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted a version of Rabbi Yochanan's teaching that is the polar opposite of Rav Dimi's. According to Ravin, even the case of two different people and two different fields works — "give Reuven field A and let Shimon inherit field B" — and Rabbi Yochanan holds that Shimon nonetheless acquires his field, because we borrow the *matana* language used for Reuven and apply it to Shimon as well. Correspondingly, in Ravin's version, Rabbi Elazar holds that two different people and two different fields does not work.

### Abaye's Analysis

Abaye, confronting Ravin, identifies the logical structure of the situation with precision. He observes that Ravin's transmission presents no difficulty with respect to Rabbi Elazar: both Rav Dimi's report and Ravin's report of Rabbi Elazar are consistent and complementary. Rav Dimi's version tells us when it does work — one person and two fields, or two people and one field — and Ravin's version tells us when it does not work — two people and two fields. These are simply two halves of the same ruling.

The difficulty lies with Rabbi Yochanan. Rav Dimi says Rabbi Yochanan requires that both person and field be the same; Ravin says Rabbi Yochanan holds that even different person and different field works. These are diametrically opposed positions. The Gemara resolves this by acknowledging the reality: these are simply two *amora'im* reporting contradictory traditions in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, and they cannot be reconciled. One preserved his ruling accurately and one did not.

## Reish Lakish Position

### Reish Lakish Formula

Reish Lakish agrees that two different people and two different fields does not straightforwardly work — that is, if one part of the declaration uses *matana* language and a separate part uses *yerusha* language for different people receiving different fields, the *yerusha* portion is ineffective. However, Reish Lakish identifies a formula through which even this case can be rendered effective: the dying person must package the entire declaration together as a single unit, combining both the *yerusha* and *matana* language in one tightly constructed statement.

The formula Reish Lakish requires is something like: "Let Reuven and Shimon inherit field A and field B, which I am giving them as a *matana*, and let them inherit." The declaration opens with *yerusha* language, states *matana* explicitly in the middle, and closes with *yerusha* language again. By bundling everything together in this way, the word *matana* in the center is understood to define and reinterpret the *yerusha* language on both sides of it, and the entire bequest is treated as an effective gift.

The Rashbam works through the various implications and permutations of Reish Lakish's position in some detail. What is clear is that according to Reish Lakish, the operative principle for cases of two different people and two different fields is tight packaging: both the *matana* and the *yerusha* language must be unified into a single, coherent declaration. Without that unification, the *yerusha* portion fails.

## Amoraic Positions

### Rav Hamnuna

Rav Hamnuna holds the strictest position: the word *matana* can define the entire bequest only when there is a single person and a single field. Any divergence — two fields or two people — breaks the unity of the declaration, and the *matana* language applies only to the specific person and field it directly addresses. This corresponds to Rav Dimi's version of Rabbi Yochanan.

### Rav Nachman

Rav Nachman takes a middle position: as long as at least one element is shared — either the same person receiving two fields, or the same field going to two people — the *matana* language can extend to cover the entire bequest. But when both the person and the field differ, the *matana* language cannot bridge the gap. This corresponds to the unanimous position attributed to Rabbi Elazar.

### Rav Sheshes

Rav Sheshes holds the most lenient position: even two different people receiving two different fields can be covered by a single *matana* word appearing elsewhere in the declaration. We borrow the language of *matana* used for one person and one field and apply it to the other person and other field as well. This corresponds to Ravin's version of Rabbi Yochanan.

## Rav Sheshes Proof

### Braisa Setup

Rav Sheshes supports his position from a *braisa*. The case involves a person who instructs that from a fund he is setting aside, his children should receive a weekly allowance of a *shekel* — which equals half a *sela* — and that after his children die, the remainder of the fund should be inherited by Reuven and Shimon.

The context is somewhat ambiguous: this could be a *shachiv mera* declaration made on his deathbed, or it could be instructions given to an administrator or trustee who will oversee his estate during an absence. What is clear is that the man is establishing a fund for the benefit of his children, with a designated remainder to others.

### Shekel Latitude

The *braisa* addresses the executor's latitude in determining how much to give the children each week. In the standard case — where the man simply says "give my children a *shekel* each week" without specifying a maximum — and where the needs of the children and the scale of the estate make a larger amount appropriate, the executor may give a *sela* rather than a *shekel*. The reasoning is that the man did not say "give them only a *shekel*" — he named a *shekel* as a floor, not a ceiling. If a *sela* is also appropriate, the instruction "give them a *shekel*" is satisfied by giving them a *sela*.

However, if the man explicitly says "do not give them more than a *shekel*," then the executor is bound by that restriction and gives exactly a *shekel*.

Now the *braisa* introduces a further refinement. When the man has designated Reuven and Shimon to receive the remainder after his children die, the dynamic changes. In that case, even if he said nothing more than "give them a *shekel*," the executor gives exactly a *shekel* and no more — regardless of what the children's needs might otherwise warrant. The reason is that the man's having designated other beneficiaries for the remainder signals that he cares about how much is left over. He named a *shekel* as a precise figure, not a minimum, because he wants to protect the interests of those who will inherit the balance. Therefore: whether he said "give them a *shekel*" or "give them no more than a *shekel*," the executor gives exactly a *shekel*.

### Conditional Yerusha

Here is why this *braisa* is relevant to Rav Sheshes's position. The case involves the dying man's children on one side, and Reuven and Shimon on the other. When referring to his children, the man uses language of giving — *matana* language. When referring to Reuven and Shimon, he says "let them inherit the remainder" — *yerusha* language. Yet the *braisa* takes for granted that Reuven and Shimon do in fact acquire the remainder.

This is a case of two different groups of people and, arguably, two different fields — the weekly disbursements to the children and the remaining balance that passes to Reuven and Shimon represent distinct portions of the fund. The Gemara acknowledges that one could describe this as a single fund, but presses the argument that since what the children receive and what Reuven and Shimon receive are materially distinct portions, it is analogous to two fields. And yet the *yerusha* language works. It must be that the *matana* language used when referring to the children is being borrowed and applied to Reuven and Shimon as well — demonstrating that *matana* language can operate across different people and different fields, just as Rav Sheshes claims.

Rav Sheshes himself anticipates the objection that would be raised against this proof, and addresses it. The objection is as follows: perhaps the reason the *yerusha* language works for Reuven and Shimon is not because we are borrowing *matana* language from the children's portion. Perhaps it works because Reuven and Shimon are themselves among the dying man's heirs, and there is a position — that of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka — which permits redistributing an inheritance among those who stand in line to inherit. Under that view, the *yerusha* language is independently effective for anyone who is in the line of inheritance, and no borrowing of *matana* language is required.

Rav Sheshes acknowledges that this defense can be raised and then addresses it — which sets up the discussion that follows.

## Rav Ashi Proof

### Succession Formula

Rav Ashi advances a further proof. The case is that of a *shachiv mera* who declares: "My property goes to you [the first recipient], and after you, Ploni shall inherit, and after Ploni, Almoni shall inherit." This is a three-stage successive gift: the property first goes to Reuven, then upon Reuven's death to Shimon, and upon Shimon's death to Levi.

The first recipient — Reuven — is addressed with the language *lecha*, "to you," which functions as *matana* language. The second and third designations, however, use the explicit language of *yerusha*: "shall inherit." And yet the *braisa* teaching this case takes it as given that Shimon acquires the property after Reuven dies and that Levi acquires it after Shimon dies.

The case clearly involves different people. As for the field: one could argue that it is the same field throughout, since it is the same property passing from one to the next. But the Gemara reasons that since Reuven and Shimon never own the property simultaneously — Shimon acquires only upon Reuven's death — it is treated as if they owned different fields. There is never a period of joint ownership; each successive holder's interest is entirely distinct in time. On this basis the Gemara treats the case as analogous to different people and different fields.

And yet the *yerusha* language works — Shimon and Levi each acquire. It must be that the *matana* language used for Reuven is borrowed and applied to Shimon and Levi as well. This is a proof that *matana* language can reach across different people and what is treated as different fields, in support of Rav Sheshes's position.

### Chain Interruption

The *braisa* also addresses what happens if the chain of succession is broken. If Shimon dies before Reuven — that is, the second designated heir predeceases the first — then upon Reuven's death, the property does not skip over to Levi. The chain cannot jump a link. Once Shimon is gone, the sequential transfer stops. The property then reverts to the heirs of the first recipient, Reuven, because if the stipulated succession cannot be carried out, the property simply remains with the last person who held it, passing to his natural heirs. This is the operative *halacha*, though it is not the central point of our discussion.

## Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka

### RYbB Defense

At this point the Gemara considers whether the proof from Rav Ashi's case can be deflected. Perhaps the reason the *yerusha* language works for Shimon and Levi is not because *matana* language is being borrowed from Reuven's designation. Perhaps it works because Shimon and Levi are themselves among this man's heirs — they stand in line to inherit from him — and we are ruling in accordance with the position of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka holds that a person may reassign his inheritance among those who are eligible to inherit from him. Under ordinary Torah law, one cannot redirect a *yerusha*; it goes where the Torah dictates. But Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka permits the dying person to choose which of his potential heirs will actually receive the estate. If Shimon and Levi are among this man's eligible heirs, then the *yerusha* language in the declaration independently satisfies the requirements under Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's ruling, with no need to borrow *matana* language from Reuven's portion.

### Inheritance Finality

Rav Acha bar Rav Adda dispatched a message articulating a crucial implication of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's position — and this implication is what refutes the proposed defense.

According to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, if the dying person says "my property goes to Reuven, and after Reuven to Shimon," and Reuven is an actual heir, then something remarkable follows: even though the man said "give it to Reuven" — language that everywhere else we would read as *matana* language — here, because Reuven is in the line of inheritance, we interpret the declaration as designating Reuven as an heir, not as a recipient of a gift. And if Reuven receives it as an heir rather than as a donee, the principle of *yerusha ein la hefsek* applies: an inheritance does not interrupt. Once property passes to an heir, it becomes fully and permanently his. The original owner cannot condition or limit what the heir then does with it, and cannot stipulate that it will pass to someone else after the heir dies. It stays within the heir's family.

This means that if the case of the successive gift were indeed governed by Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka — if Shimon were receiving the property as an heir by virtue of being in the line of inheritance — then after Shimon's death the property could not pass on to Levi. It would stay within Shimon's family. Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's framework would make it impossible for the third designation to function.

### Matana Mechanism

But the *braisa* establishes plainly that after Shimon dies, Levi does receive the property. The chain continues. This conclusively rules out the Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka explanation. We are not operating under Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's framework; we are operating under the framework of the Sages (*chachamim*). And the only reason the *yerusha* language works for Shimon and Levi is that we are borrowing the *matana* language used in Reuven's designation and applying it to them as well.

This confirms that *matana* language can indeed bridge across different people — and, by extension, across what the Gemara treats as different fields — in support of Rav Sheshes's position that even two people and two fields can be covered by borrowed *matana* language.

## Toch Kedei Dibbur Rule

### Contradiction Setup

Having established that *matana* language can extend across different people and different fields, a difficulty now arises. Rava rules that the *halacha* follows Reish Lakish in three specific areas, and one of those areas is precisely our case: when a declaration involves different people and different fields, Reish Lakish requires tight packaging — without it, the *yerusha* language fails. Yet the proofs just adduced demonstrate that borrowed *matana* language can cover different people and different fields without any special packaging formula. How are these two rulings to be reconciled?

### Toch Kedei Dibbur Resolu…

The Gemara resolves the contradiction through the concept of *toch kedei dibbur*. The rule is: *kol toch kedei dibbur ke-dibbur dami* — anything stated within the time it takes to say a brief greeting is considered part of a single statement.

The resolution is as follows. The proofs showing that *matana* language bridges different people and different fields all involve cases where the *matana* language and the *yerusha* language appear within one continuous declaration — that is, the two portions of the statement fall within *toch kedei dibbur* of each other. When that is the case, the entire declaration is treated as a single unit, and the *matana* language governs the whole, rendering even the *yerusha* portions effective.

Reish Lakish's ruling, requiring the tight packaging formula, applies to a different situation: where the *matana* statement and the *yerusha* statement are separated by more than *toch kedei dibbur*. When there is a meaningful pause between the two parts, they can no longer be interpreted as a single unit. In that case, the *yerusha* portion stands alone and fails on its own. The only way to save it is through Reish Lakish's packaging formula, which constructs a new unified statement that subsumes both.

### Exceptions to Rule

The Gemara then states the general principle explicitly and notes its exceptions: *toch kedei dibbur ke-dibbur dami* — statements made within *toch kedei dibbur* are treated as simultaneous — except in three areas: *avoda zara*, *kiddushin*, and *geirushin*.

Regarding *avoda zara*: if someone verbally accepts an idol as a deity and then immediately retracts within *toch kedei dibbur*, the retraction is ineffective. The statement of idolatry — and similarly a statement of blasphemy — is so weighty that no reconsideration period is granted. The moment it is uttered, it is complete and carries its full consequences.

Regarding *kiddushin*: if a man betroths a woman and immediately says he was joking or retracts, the retraction within *toch kedei dibbur* does not undo the betrothal. Once the act of *kiddushin* is complete, it is complete. The Gemara does recognize, it should be noted, a different application of *toch kedei dibbur* in the tractate of Kiddushin: where a couple has been engaged in a sustained conversation about marriage, even a subsequent act of giving a ring — not accompanied by the explicit formula *harei at mekudeshet li* — may be interpreted as an act of *kiddushin* under the principle of *asukin be-oto inyan*, since the context of the conversation defines the intent. But that is a use of context to clarify intent, not a retraction. Where the issue is retraction, *kiddushin* is immune to *toch kedei dibbur*.

The same applies to *geirushin*: a *get* once delivered cannot be recalled within *toch kedei dibbur*.

## Toch Kedei Dibbur Logic

### Rabbeinu Tam

Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Tam's explanation of why the Rabbis instituted *toch kedei dibbur* as a period of continuity. The case Rabbeinu Tam envisions is this: a person is in the middle of negotiating a business transaction. At the critical moment when terms are being concluded, his rabbi or a person of distinction walks by. Were there no *toch kedei dibbur* principle, the moment he turns to greet his rabbi — "shalom aleicha, rabbi" — he would be considered to have interrupted and concluded the negotiation, losing his opportunity to reconsider or redefine the terms. In order not to put a person in the position of either being rude to his teacher or forfeiting his legal options, the Rabbis instituted that this brief window of greeting does not constitute a break. He may greet his rabbi and then return to the transaction, and it is all treated as continuous.

Tosafot notes a difficulty with this explanation, however. The concept of *toch kedei dibbur* operates across a wide range of legal contexts that have nothing to do with business transactions or the politeness of greeting a passing rabbi. It seems to reflect a broader definitional principle about when intent is considered finalized and when a statement is considered complete — not merely a social accommodation. So while Rabbeinu Tam's reasoning is elegant, it does not fully account for the breadth of *toch kedei dibbur*'s application.

### Rashbam Analysis

The Rashbam provides a more comprehensive analysis of the range of scenarios governed by *toch kedei dibbur*.

The first scenario is where two statements are both halachically possible and can coexist. For example, if someone says "this animal is an *olah* and a *shlamim*," both designations cannot be fully realized simultaneously, but one can distribute the monetary value accordingly — half toward an *olah* and half toward a *shlamim*. When both things can be satisfied, the fact that they were said within *toch kedei dibbur* means both are honored to the extent possible.

The second scenario is where one statement interprets the other — which is precisely our case. The word *yerusha* and the word *matana* appear within *toch kedei dibbur* of each other, and they are treated as part of a single statement. Because they are a single statement, the question becomes which language governs.

The third scenario is where the two statements are flatly contradictory — for instance, giving the entirety of an estate first to Reuven and then immediately to Shimon. Here the question of which statement prevails is more complex, and various opinions exist regarding *tfos lashon rishon* — seizing the first language — versus *tfos lashon acharon* — seizing the last. At minimum, if the second statement is clearly a retraction, virtually everyone agrees that a retraction within *toch kedei dibbur* is effective.

### Matana Dominates

In our specific case, the Rashbam explains why the *matana* language dominates over the *yerusha* language when the two appear within *toch kedei dibbur*. The reason is that the word *yerusha* has no halachic effect in the context of a *matnat shachiv mera* — it cannot legally transfer property, because the Torah's laws of inheritance cannot be reassigned by the dying person's declaration. The word *matana*, by contrast, does have halachic effect: it is precisely what makes the bequest valid. When one part of a statement is legally operative and another part is legally inert, the operative language governs and interprets the inert language.

Accordingly, when *matana* and *yerusha* appear together within *toch kedei dibbur*, the *matana* language dominates and the entire declaration is read as a gift. This is reinforced by the broader rabbinic tendency, evident throughout this entire sugya, to read every possible declaration in the most favorable light — as a gift — in order to give the dying person the maximum latitude to dispose of his property according to his wishes.
