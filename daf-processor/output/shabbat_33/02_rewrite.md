# Shabbat 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sins & Punishments

### Women in Childbirth

The daf opens in the context of a broader discussion about why women die at the time of childbirth — a topic attributed to particular sins — which in turn opens onto the larger question of how one connects specific deaths or catastrophes to specific types of transgression. This raises, inevitably, serious questions of theodicy, and as we will see, the *gemara* itself does not shy away from pressing on some of those difficulties.

### Theft & Locusts

The *gemara* picks up at the bottom of 33b. *Ba'avon gezel* — for the sin of theft — *hagovai oleh*, the locust rises up, *veharav hoveh*, and there is famine in the land. People are eventually reduced to eating the flesh of their own children, as the *pasuk* states: *shim'u hadavar hazeh parot habashan asher behar shomron ha'oshkot dalim harotzot evyonim* — "Hear this thing, cows of the Bashan who are in the mountains of Shomron, who oppress the poor and crush the destitute."

*Amar Rava*: who are these "cows of the Bashan"? *Kegon hanei neshei deMechuza de'achlan velo avdan* — they are like the women of *Mechuza*, who eat and do not work, like cows that just eat and grow fat and do nothing. Dr. Elman of Revel has written at length — a substantial monograph — on *Rava* of *Mechuza* and on how *Mechuza* was a cosmopolitan city whose residents gave *Rava* considerable trouble. He was constantly frustrated with them and persistently critical of them, which helps explain some of the sharper remarks he makes here. As *Rashi* explains, the connection to theft is that the women overate, forcing their husbands to go out and steal to support them.

How does this lead to the locusts? *Dechtiv*: "I have smitten you with *shidafon*" — a blasting wind — "and *yerakon*," a rot, "and all your vineyards and figs and olives shall be eaten by the *gazam*," the locust. And then: *yeter hagazam achal ha'arbeh veyeter ha'arbeh achal hayelek veyeter hayelek achal hachasil* — whatever one swarm of locusts left over, the next swarm consumed. From there the *pasuk* continues: "And He commanded on the right and there was famine, and they ate on the left and could not be satisfied. *Ish besar zero'o yocheilu* — each man shall eat the flesh of his arm." Here the *gemara* invokes the principle of *al tikrei*: do not read *besar zero'o*, the flesh of his arm, but *besar zar'o*, the flesh of his offspring — which of course corresponds to the explicit language of the *klalos* in the Torah that speaks of parents consuming their children.

What is striking about this entire passage — and this pattern will continue throughout much of the *daf* — is that the *gemara* is not speculating freely about why bad things happen and then reaching for a source. Rather, it seems to be doing something more constrained: taking explicit biblical *pesukim* and extracting from them the precise causal connection between sin and consequence. The *pesukim* of the *klalos* contain both the punishments and, by *derasha*, the sins. The *gemara* is mapping one onto the other.

### Judicial Sins

*Ba'avon inuy hadin ve'ivut hadin vekilkul hadin uvitul Torah* — for the sins of oppressing justice, perverting justice, corrupting justice, and neglecting Torah. *Rashi* distinguishes carefully among the first three: *inuy hadin* is delaying judgment — the *din* is known but the court simply postpones its decision, denying speedy justice; *ivut hadin* is willful perversion; and *kilkul hadin* is corrupt negligence — not intentionally sinful but reckless enough to produce perversion of justice. And then there is *bitul Torah*, the neglect of Torah study.

The consequences: *cherev uviza raba* — sword and plunder — *vedever uvatzoret ba'a*, plague and famine. People eat and are not satisfied, and they must measure out their bread.

The *pasuk* cited is *ve'heiveiti aleichem cherev nokemet nekam berit* — "I will bring upon you a sword that avenges the covenant." The *gemara* establishes: *v'ein berit ella Torah* — *berit* refers to Torah — as the *pasuk* says, *im lo beriti yomam valailah chukkot shamayim va'aretz lo shamti*. And we know *yomam valailah* points to Torah because of *vehagita bo yomam valailah*. The *pasuk* continues: *b'shivri lachem mateh lechem*, "when I break your staff of bread," and *v'afu esser nashim lachmechem b'tanur echad* — ten women will bake their bread in a single oven — *yanu biyan b'mishpatai ma'asu*, because they despised My laws.

One might wonder how an emphasis on *din* — law and justice — gets folded into the theme of *bitul Torah*. It is worth noting that the *gemara* here seems to identify *Torah* with *din*, with its application in lived communal life rather than only as a theoretical pursuit. The verse's language of *mishpatai* — "my laws" — reinforces this, since *mishpat* points toward *ivut hadin* as well. It is also interesting that for us today this language translates more naturally into the domain of personal and private *halakha*, whereas the *gemara*'s framework here is more about the communal institutions of justice — reflecting the very different social reality of the Talmudic period.

### False Oaths

*Ba'avon shvuat shav u'shvuat sheker v'chilul Hashem v'chilul Shabbat* — for the sins of an oath for naught, a false oath, desecrating God's name, and desecrating *Shabbat* — *chaya ra'ah rabbah u'vhemah kalah* — evil beasts multiply and domesticated animals are destroyed — *u'vnei adam mitma'atin*, the human population diminishes, *v'hadrachim mishtomemin*, and the pathways become desolate.

The *pasuk* says: *v'im b'eleh lo tivasru li* — "and if with these you are not chastened by Me." The *gemara* invokes *al tikrei*: do not read *b'eleh* but *b'alah* — with the oath. This gives us the connection to *shvua*. Then: *v'shillachti bachem et chayat hasadeh v'shikkla etchem v'nashamu darcheichem* — "I will send the beasts of the field against you, and they shall bereave you, and your roads shall be desolate." Those are the explicit consequences.

How do we arrive at *chilul Hashem* and *chilul Shabbat*? By *gzeira shava* of *chilul-chilul*. The *pasuk* by *shvuat sheker* states: *lo tishavu bishmi lashaker v'chillalta et shem elokecha* — a false oath constitutes *chilul Hashem*. And the *pasuk* by *Shabbat* states: *mechaleleha mot yumat* — those who desecrate it shall be put to death, the same root of *chilul*. So from *alah* we derive *shvua*; from *shvua* we derive *chilul*; and from *chilul* we derive both *chilul Hashem* and *chilul Shabbat* by *gzeira shava*.

Again, the *gemara* is not primarily engaged in symbolic speculation about the connection between these particular sins and these particular punishments. Its agenda seems to be either to explain why bad things happen in the world, or to convey *mussar* — if you sin, severe punishment awaits — or, most precisely, to show that everything follows from the explicit language of the *klalos*, with the *derasha* only pinpointing the exact sin that the *pasuk* has in view.

## Exile & Destruction

### Murder & Beit HaMikdash

*Ba'avon shfichut damim Beit HaMikdash charev v'shchina mistalleket mi'Yisrael* — for the sin of murder, the *Beit HaMikdash* is destroyed and the Divine Presence departs from Israel. The *pasuk*: *v'lo tachanifu et ha'aretz ki hadam hu yachanif et ha'aretz v'lo tetamme et ha'aretz asher atem yoshvim bah asher ani shochen b'tochah* — "do not defile the land in which you dwell, in which I dwell." The *gemara* reads: if you do defile it, then you will no longer dwell in it — *v'eini shochen b'tochah* — and I will no longer dwell within it either. This connection is direct and the *pasuk* makes it explicit.

### Immorality & Idolatry

*Ba'avon gilluy arayot v'avodah kochavim u'shmitat shmitin v'yovlos* — for the sins of sexual immorality, idolatry, and the violation of *shmitin* and *yovlos* — *galus ba lo-olam*, exile comes to the world, and others come and settle in their place.

The *pasuk* for sexual immorality: *ki es kol ha-to'evos ha-eil asu anshei ha-aretz*, "for all these abominations were done by the people of the land" — and the *to'evos ha-eil* appear immediately after the entire *parsha* of *arayot*, connecting them by juxtaposition. *Vatitma ha-aretz va-efkod avonah aleha* — the land became impure and God visited its sin upon it. *V'lo sakia ha-aretz eschem b'tamacha'chem osah ka'asher ka'ah es ha-goy asher lifneichem* — the land will not vomit you out when you defile it, just as it vomited out the nation before you.

It is worth noting that here the framework shifts slightly — the punishment is not described merely as *ba lo-olam*, a general occurrence, but carries the sense that non-Jews too were exiled for these sins, and that the Jews' own exile follows the same pattern. The non-Jews who previously occupied the land were themselves dispossessed; and the Jews, by committing the same offenses, face the same fate.

For *avodah zarah*: *v'nasati es pigreichem al pigrei gilluleichem* — "I will place your corpses upon the corpses of your idols." For *shmitin*: *az tirtzeh ha-aretz es shabbososeha kol yemei hashamah v'atem b'eretz oyveichem* — this is stated quite explicitly in *Behar*, that the land will finally rest and recover all the *shmitin* that were not observed during the time of settlement. *Kol yemei hashamah tishbos* — throughout all the years of its desolation it will rest, compensating for the *shmitin* that were neglected.

## Foul Speech

### Foul Speech Consequences

Here the *gemara* introduces something rather different from what has come before. All the prior cases involved explicit and serious *aveiros* — theft, murder, sexual immorality, idolatry, and so on. Now the *gemara* turns to *nivlus peh*, foul or degenerate speech. The *Ramban* in his comment on *kedoshim tihyu* famously holds that one must live up to the spirit of Torah even where no explicit *mitzvah* applies, and *nivlus peh* is one of his prime examples — it is not a formal prohibition, yet it falls within the scope of *kedoshim tihyu*. It is striking, then, that in the middle of this very serious discourse about catastrophic punishment the *gemara* introduces this category.

*Ba-avon nivlus peh* — *tzaros rabbos u-gezeiros kashos mis-chadshos* — great afflictions and difficult decrees come into the world. The young men of Israel die — *u-vachurei sonei Yisrael mesim* — orphans and widows cry out and are not answered. The *pasuk*: *al kein al bachurav lo yismach Hashem v'es yesomav v'es almanosav lo yerachem ki kulo chanef u-meira v'chol peh dover nevalah* — "therefore God will not rejoice over His youth, and will not have compassion on the orphans and widows, for all of them are flattering and speaking evil, and every mouth speaks filth." It is worth noting, though, that in its original context in Yeshayahu, the prophet is likely referring to *chanef u-meira* in the sense of idolatry rather than foul speech per se — the *gemara* is applying the verse in a new direction.

The *pasuk* continues: *u-v'chol zos lo shav apo v'od yado netuyah* — "and despite all this His anger has not turned back and His arm is still stretched out."

### Rav Chanan on Speech

*Mai v'od yado netuyah?* What does it mean that His arm is still stretched out? *Amar Rav Chanan bar Rava*: *ha-kol yod'im kallah lamah nichnesah l'chuppah* — everyone knows why a bride enters the *chuppah*. The statement is already notable for focusing on the bride rather than the groom. *Ella kol ha-menavel piv afilu chosmim alav gezar din shel shiv'im shanah l'tovah hofchim alav l'ra'ah* — but anyone who makes his mouth foul, even if they have sealed for him a decree of seventy years of good, they overturn it entirely for evil.

This is a heavy claim. One might ask what precisely connects *v'od yado netuyah* to this teaching about seventy years being reversed. The textual derivation is not immediately transparent, and *Rashi* does not seem to resolve it clearly. The precise exegetical link to *shivim shanah* or to the reversal of a favorable *gezar din* remains somewhat opaque.

What is interesting about the choice of example — the bride entering the *chuppah* — is that it reflects a context where foul speech most naturally arises around the male gaze and objectification of women. But the formulation here is actually talking about the bride as the subject, not merely the object, which is somewhat curious.

*Amar Rabbah bar Shila amar Rav Chisda*: *kol hamenavel piv maamikin lo gehinnom* — for one who speaks foully, *gehinnom* is made even deeper. The *pasuk*: *shucha amuka pi zarot* — a deep pit for the mouth of perversity.

*Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak amar*: not only the speaker, but even *shome'a v'shoteik* — one who hears it and remains silent — *ze'um Hashem yipol sham* — the one whom God is angry with will fall there. *Rashi* explains: the divine displeasure rests near the mouth of perversity, and the one who sits there and hears it is called *ze'umo shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu*. The question of how to respond when one hears inappropriate or offensive speech — especially speech that is not quite *nivlus peh* in the classic sense but still deeply wrong — is genuinely challenging. The *gemara* is offering a powerful principle: silence in the face of such speech is itself a form of culpability.

*Amar Rav Oshaya*: one who pours himself entirely into sin — devotes himself to it, pursues it — *chaburot u'ptzaim yotzin bo*, wounds will come upon him, as the *pasuk* says: *chaburot petza tamruk b'ra'ah* — wounds of smiting will purify in evil. *V'lo od ela shenidon behidrokan* — and moreover, he is judged with *hidrokan*, an intestinal disease. *Shene'emar u'makot chadrei vaten* — plagues of the innermost chambers of the belly.

### Illness as Sign

At this point the *gemara* executes a significant logical move. Until now the discourse has been directional: if you commit sin X, you receive punishment Y. Now *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* reverses the inference: *siman l'aveira hidrokan* — *hidrokan* is a sign that one has sinned. This is a much more theologically fraught claim. It is the logical fallacy of affirming the consequent: the fact that sin leads to illness does not mean that every instance of illness signals sin. The *gemara* is aware of this problem, as we shall see.

### Three Types of Hidrokan

*Tanu Rabbanan*: there are three types of *hidrokan*. The one that comes from sin — *shel aveira* — is *avei*, thick, producing a hardening and bloating of the skin. The one that comes from famine — *shel ra'av* — is *tapuach*, also involving a swelling. And the one that comes from witchcraft — *shel kshafim* — is *dak*, making the skin thin and brittle.

The *gemara* then applies this framework to three prominent *amoraim*. *Shmuel Hakatan* contracted *hidrokan* and said: *Ribono Shel Olam, mi mafis* — who will be able to determine that this is not the type that comes from sin? People will think I am a sinner. And he recovered. *Abaye* contracted it. *Rava* said: I know Nachmani — one of Abaye's names — *d'machpi nafshei*, that he fasts a great deal, and it is from the fasting, which corresponds to the *shel ra'av* variety.

*Rava* himself then contracted it. But Rava had said: *nepishi d'ketili k'dar minepishi de'chafan* — more people die from *k'dar* — which *Rashi* explains as holding back when one needs to relieve oneself — than from actual fasting. So we know *Rava* was acutely aware of this danger and was presumably not himself holding back. And yet there is no obvious physical cause. The *gemara* resolves it: *shani Rava d'aniseh lei rabanan be'idnei b'al korchei* — Rava was a special case because the rabbis would press him with questions at precisely the moments when he needed to go, and so he was compelled against his will, and this brought about his illness.

The discussion makes clear that the *gemara* recognizes the difficulty of inferring sin from illness — hence the careful work done to supply an alternative, non-sinful explanation for each of these *amoraim*'s cases.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: there are four signs. The sign of sin is *hadrokan*. The sign of *sinas chinam* — baseless hatred — is *yerakon*, jaundice. The sign of haughtiness is poverty. And *siman lilashon hara askara* — the sign of evil speech is *askara*, a disease of the throat and upper respiratory tract.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: *askara* comes to the world *al hama'aser* — for failure to give tithes. *Rashi* explains: eating *tevel* carries the penalty of *mita bidei shamayim*, and since it involves eating, the disease appropriately afflicts the upper body, though it presents as respiratory rather than purely digestive. *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose* says: *askara* comes for *lashon hara*. This, too, makes intuitive sense — the disease affects the mouth and throat, the instruments of speech.

*Amar Rava* — and some say *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — *mai kra?* Where is it in the *pasuk*? *Ve'hamelech yismach belohim yithalel kol hanishba bo ki yisacher pi dovrei shaker* — "the mouth of those who speak falsehood shall be stopped up," and *yisacher* is read as cognate with *askara*. It is worth noting here that *lashon hara* in this context carries a broader meaning than the familiar one of slander — it encompasses all forms of wicked speech, including lying. The *Yerushalmi* similarly understands *lashon hara* as a generic category for harmful speech of any kind.

## Kerem b'Yavne

### Askara Question

The *gemara* now asks: *iba'ya lehu* — a question arose among the sages: *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose al lashon hara kama o dilma af al lashon hara kama* — when he said that *askara* comes for *lashon hara*, did he mean exclusively for *lashon hara*, or even for *lashon hara* in addition to other causes? The stakes of this question become clear when the *gemara* introduces its proof.

*Keshenichnesu raboteinu l'Kerem b'Yavne* — when our sages entered the assembly at *Kerem b'Yavne*. This, of course, is the great rabbinic convocation following the *churban*, during which the sages gathered to preserve *Torah she-be'al peh* — the oral tradition — including the landmark testimony compiled in *Masechet Eduyot*. Three sages were present at this gathering in our narrative: *Rabbi Yehuda*, *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose*, and *Rabbi Shimon*. And *nish'alu lish'eilah zu bifneihem* — the following question was brought before them.

The question itself is arresting: *maka zu mipnei mah matchala bivne me'ayim v'gomeres bepeh* — why does this affliction, *askara*, begin in the intestines and end in the mouth? It is striking to contemplate that the sages of *Kerem b'Yavne*, charged with the preservation of the entire oral tradition, apparently also devoted time to questions of this kind — theological and, in their terms, quasi-medical speculation about why a disease follows a particular course in the body. It speaks to how much *aggadata* and theological reflection — what we might call the attempt to discern divine order in the world — was part of their project. Especially in the aftermath of the *churban*, there was comfort in the idea that *hashgacha* was still operative, that God's hand could still be detected in the smallest details of human experience.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Explanati…

*Tanna Rabbi Yehuda berabbi Ilai* — who is, of course, *stam Rabbi Yehuda* throughout the *Mishna*, the foremost student of *Rabbi Akiva*, and *rosh hamedabrim bechol makom* — the one who speaks first everywhere (the significance of this title will be explained in the next section). He answered: *af al pi shekelayot yoatzot velev mevin velashon mechatech peh gomer* — even though the kidneys advise, the heart understands, the tongue enunciates, the mouth finishes. In the ancient understanding of human physiology, the kidneys serve as the seat of inner counsel, the heart of understanding. The tongue and mouth are the final instruments through which thought becomes speech. *Rashi* notes: *peh gomer lashon hara* — the mouth is where *lashon hara* is completed. The disease, therefore, follows the same anatomical trajectory as the sin itself: it begins deep within the body and ascends to conclude in the mouth.

### Three Explanations

*Tanna Rabbi Elazar berabbi Yose* offered a different explanation: *mipnei she'ochlin bidevarim tme'in* — because they eat non-kosher things. The *gemara* immediately challenges: *devarim tme'in salka da'atach?* — is that really conceivable? *Rashi* explains the difficulty: most *issurim* of non-kosher food carry the penalty of *lav*, not *mita*, and the degree of punishment described here seems disproportionate. The *gemara* therefore reads *devarim tme'in* not as forbidden foods in general, but as *devarim she'einam metukanim* — *tevel*, untithed produce, which does indeed carry the penalty of *mita bidei shamayim*, and which also involves the mouth and eating.

*Tanna Rabbi Shimon* offered a third explanation: *ba'avon bitul Torah* — for the neglect of Torah study.

### Theodicy Problem

At this point the *gemara* raises the objection that gives this entire passage its real bite. The sages challenge each explanation in turn on grounds of theodicy: what about those who suffer *askara* but who cannot, by any reasonable logic, be guilty of the attributed sin?

Women, they object, are not obligated in *talmud Torah* — so how do they contract *askara* if it comes for *bitul Torah*? Rabbi Shimon answers: *shemvatlos et ba'aleihen* — they cause their husbands to be neglectful of Torah study, and so they bear that consequence.

*Nochrim yochi'u* — non-Jews will prove this wrong. Non-Jews certainly are not obligated in *talmud Torah*. The answer: *shemvatlin et Yisrael* — through their decrees and impositions they prevent Israel from investing fully in Torah study.

*Tinokot yochi'u* — what about young children? They answer: *shemvatlin et avihen* — they cause their fathers to be neglectful, perhaps by waking them at night and disrupting their learning.

### Righteous Suffer

The *gemara* then presses further: *tinokot shel beit rabban yochi'u* — what about the children who are already sitting in school, actively learning all day? What have they done? *Rashi* distinguishes the two cases: the first *tinokot* are infants, not yet old enough to learn; but the second, the *tinokot shel beit rabban*, are precisely the ones who are learning. How can *bitul Torah* be invoked against them?

Here the *gemara* introduces a profound and theologically weighty answer: *hasam kerabbi goryon* — that follows the view of *Rabbi Goryon* (others attribute it to *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yishmael*). *Bizman she'tzaddikim b'dor tzaddikim nitpasim al hador* — when there are righteous people in the generation, the righteous are sometimes punished in place of the generation; they suffer vicariously for others' sins. *Ein tzaddikim b'dor tinokot shel beit rabban nitpasim al hador* — when there are no such righteous people, then the *tinokot shel beit rabban* — the most innocent members of the community — are seized in their stead.

The *pasuk* cited: *im lo sadi lach hayafa banashim tzei lach b'ikvei hatzon u'rei es gediyosayich al mishkenos haro'im* — "if you do not know, most beautiful among women, go out by the footsteps of the flock and shepherd your young goats by the tents of the shepherds." The *gemara* reads *mishkenos haro'im* through the lens of *mashkon* — collateral. The shepherds, the leaders of the generation, hold these children as collateral for the community. When the shepherds themselves are no longer *tzaddikim*, the innocent children become the pledges that are seized.

This is, to say the least, a striking and deeply unsettling teaching. After Christianity, the idea of vicarious punishment — the innocent suffering for the sins of others — is one that many Jews instinctively resist as a foreign theological category. Yet the notion appears here with force, and it is hardly isolated. The concept of *misas tzaddikim mechaperes* — the death of the righteous as atonement — appears in classical liturgical contexts, including the *asara harugei malchus* on *Yom Kippur*, where the martyrs function as a kind of *korban* for the generation. The idea that innocent children suffer as a form of communal punishment for their fathers is perhaps even more disturbing, and the *gemara* does not resolve the tension — it simply presents it. The *tinokot shel beit rabban* dying for the generation is difficult to absorb, and it sits alongside — without resolving — the broader problem of children suffering for parental sin that the *gemara* has been wrestling with throughout.

## Rabbi Yehuda ben Gerim

### Three Positions

We now understand why *Rabbi Yehuda* was called *rosh hamedabrim bechol makom* — the first speaker everywhere. The *gemara* explains: *Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yose*, and *Rabbi Shimon* were sitting together, and with them sat *Yehuda ben Gerim* — the son of converts. *Rabbi Yehuda* opened and said: *kama na'im ma'aseihem shel uma zu* — how beautiful are the accomplishments of this Roman nation. *Tiknu shvakin* — they made marketplaces. *Tiknu gisharin* — they built bridges. *Tiknu merchaza'os* — they built bathhouses.

*Rabbi Yose* said nothing. *V'na'ana Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai v'amar*: *kol mah shetiknu lo tiknu ela l'tzorech atzman* — everything they built was for their own self-interest alone. *Tiknu shvakin l'hoshiv bahen zonos* — they built the marketplaces to install prostitutes in them. *Marchaza'os* — to beautify themselves. *Gsharim litol mehem meches* — the bridges were built to collect tolls.

These three positions map quite naturally onto broader attitudes toward the non-Jewish world and, by extension, to how Jews have understood their relationship to the larger civilization in which they live. Is gratitude owed? Is appreciation appropriate, even when the builders are an oppressive power? *Rabbi Yehuda* says yes — the accomplishments are genuinely good. *Rabbi Shimon* says no — the motive was purely self-serving, and we are merely incidental beneficiaries. *Rabbi Yose* remains silent — perhaps the most prudent position, and the one that will earn him the least severe consequence.

It is worth noting that this exchange is taken up in the opening *sugya* of *Masechet Avodah Zarah*, where the same argument — that Rome built everything *l'tzorech atzmam* — is placed in the mouth of God Himself, addressing Rome's self-justification at the end of history. The fact that *Rabbi Shimon's* position is there attributed to the divine voice is significant, even as one recognizes that different *gemoros* take different approaches to this question.

### Consequences

*Halach Yehuda ben Gerim v'siper divreihem* — *Yehuda ben Gerim* went and repeated what he had heard. *Rashi* notes that this was not necessarily deliberate informing — he was simply talking, perhaps at home, perhaps in the presence of people with connections to non-Jews — and *v'nishmu l'malchus*, it reached the government's ears.

The government's response was calibrated precisely to each man's position. *Yehuda she'ila* — *Yehuda* who praised them — *yisaleh* — shall be elevated. And so he was honored by the government as the first to speak at every convocation, hence *rosh hamedabrim bechol makom*. *Yose sheshatak* — *Yose* who was silent — *yigaleh l'Tzipori* — shall be exiled to *Tzipori*. *Shimon shegina* — *Shimon* who spoke disparagingly — *yehareg* — shall be put to death. Whatever the precise historicity of this account, it accurately reflects the dangerous environment in which *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* lived and which shaped his deeply adversarial attitude toward Rome.

## Cave & Learning

### Twelve Years

*Azal hu u'vreh* — he and his son fled. It is interesting that the son, *Rabbi Elazar*, was apparently also subject to some threat, or at least *Rabbi Shimon* did not want to be alone. They hid first in the *beis medrash*, sustained by bread and water brought daily by *Rabbi Shimon's* wife. When the Roman edict intensified, *Rabbi Shimon* said to his son: *nashim da'atan kala aleihen* — not that women lack intelligence, but that under torture they may not be able to withstand the pressure and might reveal their location. *Dilma m'tzari lah u'mgalya lan* — perhaps the authorities will torture her and she will disclose where they are.

So they went and hid in a cave. A miracle was wrought for them: a carob tree grew and a wellspring of water appeared. They took off their clothes and sat buried in sand up to their necks in order to preserve their single set of garments, studying all day. When the time came to pray, they dressed, *davened*, and then undressed again so that the clothing would not wear out. This arrangement also preserved their modesty. *Yatvu treisar shani b'mearta* — they remained in the cave for twelve years.

Then *Eliyahu* came and stood at the entrance of the cave. There is something evocative in this detail — the echo of *Eliyahu* in his own cave on *Har Horev* is unmistakable, as is the parallel of miraculous sustenance (the ravens bringing food to *Eliyahu*, the carob tree and spring here). *Eliyahu*'s announcement was indirect: *man lodeyei l'Bar Yochai d'mit Keisar u'batla gzeirateih* — who will inform *Bar Yochai* that Caesar has died and the edict is nullified?

### Burning & Repentance

They emerged from the cave and saw people plowing and planting. *Menichin chayei olam v'oskin b'chayei sha'ah* — they are leaving eternal life and occupying themselves with temporal needs. Twelve years of total immersion in Torah had rendered them incapable of relating to the world of ordinary human labor. *Kol makom shenotnin eineihem miyad nisraf* — wherever they directed their gaze, people were incinerated. This connects directly to a well-known *machloket* in *Berachot* between *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* and other sages about whether one engaged in Torah study should also engage in worldly labor, or whether, if the people merit it, others will take care of their material needs while they devote themselves entirely to Torah. Here, having lived that ideal in the cave, *Rabbi Shimon* and his son could no longer abide the sight of people choosing the temporal over the eternal.

*Yatzta bat kol v'amra lahem*: *l'hachriv olami yetzatem?* — "Did you come out to destroy My world?" *Chizru l'mearatchem* — return to your cave. So they returned. This time they remained for twelve months — and here *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* reasons: *mishpat reshaim b'gehinnom yud-bet chodesh* — even the wicked are punished in *gehinnom* for no more than twelve months. Perhaps this period will serve as a *kapara* for whatever wrong they had done.

When the second *bat kol* came — *tze'u m'mearatchem* — they emerged again. *Kol heicha d'hava machi Rabbi Elazar hava masi Rabbi Shimon* — wherever *Rabbi Elazar* would smite, *Rabbi Shimon* would heal. The son had apparently not fully absorbed the lesson; the father had. *Rabbi Shimon* said to his son: *beni dai l'olam ani v'ata* — you and I together are sufficient to sustain the world in Torah; do not worry about others. This remark carries both a note of genuine reassurance — don't hold the rest of the world to a standard they cannot meet — and perhaps a trace of the ego that the cave experience had also cultivated.

### Lesson of Shabbat

*V'bahadei panya d'ma'alei shabta* — as *Erev Shabbat* was falling, they saw an old man running with two bunches of myrtle. They asked him: why do you have these? *Amar lehu lichvod shabbos* — to honor *Shabbat*. And why two? *Chad kineged zachor v'chad kineged shamor* — one corresponding to *zachor* and one to *shamor*, the two formulations of the *Shabbat* commandment in the Torah.

*Amar lei l'breih* — he said to his son: *chazi kama chavivin mitzvos alei Yisrael* — see how dear the *mitzvos* are to Israel. The old man does not merely fulfill the minimum; he doubles his *mitzvah*, running at *bein hashmashos*, deeply invested in the honor of *Shabbat*. The people do not know *Torah* at the level of *Rabbi Shimon* and his son, but the *mitzvos* that are accessible to them — these they embrace with love. *Yasiv daiteihu* — their minds settled. The rage at the world dissipated. The parallel of the story to the relationship between *Eliyahu* and his encounter with ordinary human life after his cave experience is again present in the background.

*Shama Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair chasnei* — his son-in-law *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* heard that *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* was coming out, went to meet him, and brought him to the bathhouse. The earlier discussion in the *sugya* of *Rabbi Shimon's* critique of Roman bathhouses built for self-indulgence now acquires an ironic undertone — he is himself brought to a bathhouse, and finds comfort in it. One wonders whether the story's redactor intended the connection.

As *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* massaged *Rabbi Shimon's* skin, he saw that it was cracked and furrowed from years of sitting in sand. He wept, and the tears fell on the fissured skin and caused *Rabbi Shimon* pain. *Rabbi Shimon* said to him: *ashrecha she're'isani b'kach* — fortunate are you to have seen me in this state, for *she'ilmalei lo re'isani b'kach lo matzasa bi kach* — had you not seen me this way, you would not have found in me what you now find. The physical mortification had been the price of the Torah he had attained.

The measure of the transformation is expressed in striking terms: before the cave, when *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* posed a difficult question, *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* — his son-in-law and himself a towering *tanna* — would offer twelve answers. After the cave, when *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* posed a question, *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* would give twenty-four answers. He had surpassed his interlocutor by a factor of two, and his son-in-law had previously surpassed him by the same factor.

## Purifying Cemetery

### Decision to Help

*Ho'il v'itrachish nisa* — since a miracle has been performed for me, *eizil askin milsa* — let me go and benefit the community in some way. The model he invokes is *Yaakov Avinu*. *D'ksiv vayavo Yaakov shalem* — and *Yaakov* came whole to the city of *Shechem*. *Rav* explains: *shalem b'gufo shalem b'mamono shalem b'Toraso* — whole in his body after the struggle with the angel, whole in his wealth, whole in his Torah — none of this was damaged by his years with *Lavan*.

*Vayichan es p'nei ha'ir* — "and he encamped before the city." *Rav* says: *vayichan* indicates that he gave *chen*, grace, to the city — he established coinage for them. *Shmuel* says: *shvaki tikein lahem* — he set up markets. *Rabbi Yochanan* says: *merchatzaos tikein lahem* — he built bathhouses. The echo of the Roman infrastructure debate — *shvakim*, *gisharin*, *merchatzaos* — is unmistakable and is clearly intentional on the part of the *gemara*'s redactor. *Yaakov Avinu* built for *Shechem*, a non-Jewish city; the Romans built supposedly for the general public. The significant distinction seems to be directionality: *Yaakov* built for others from a position of generosity, while the Romans built ostensibly for others but actually for themselves.

There is a *machloket* between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* on what exactly *Rabbi Shimon's* identification with *Yaakov* entails. *Tosafot* understands *avad ihu nami hachi* — he did similarly — as meaning that *Rabbi Shimon* modeled himself on *Yaakov Avinu* directly. *Rashi* appears to read it as a precedent for doing something — the parallel is that both emerged from a difficult experience and gave back. The precise scope of the analogy remains contested.

### Cemetery Work

The community presented *Rabbi Shimon* with its problem: *ika duchta d'is bei safek tumah* — there is a place with doubtful *tumah*, likely a burial ground within or adjacent to a public space, *v'is lei tza'ara l'kohanim l'akufi* — causing *kohanim* considerable hardship by forcing them to circumnavigate it. Could the situation be clarified?

*Rabbi Shimon* asked whether anyone could testify that the area had previously been established as *tahor*. A certain elder replied: *kan kitzetz ben zakai turmusei terumah* — here *Ben Zakai* had handled cuttings of *terumah*, which means the space had at some point been confirmed as *tahor*, since *terumah* could not have been handled there otherwise. Based on this precedent, *Rabbi Shimon* proceeded to survey the area: wherever the ground was hard and compact — indicating virgin, undisturbed earth — he declared it *tahor*. Wherever it was soft — indicating the possibility of a burial — he marked it as requiring caution.

### Elder's Death

A certain elder then remarked sarcastically: *teher ben yochai beit hakevarot* — so *ben Yochai* purified a cemetery! The implication was that *Rabbi Shimon* had simply declared everything clean when the true state of affairs was more complicated.

*Rabbi Shimon* responded sharply: *ilmalei hayita imanu v'afilu hayita imanu v'lo nimneita imanu yafeh atah omer* — if you were not part of our deliberations, or if you were present but did not participate in the vote, I could understand your critique. *Achshav shehayita imanu v'nimneita imanu* — but you were part of the process and part of the decision, and you still attack me by name. He invoked the principle: *yomru zonot mefarkesot zo et zo talmidei chachamim lo kol shekein* — if prostitutes adorn one another, how much more so should scholars support rather than undermine one another, even amid competition. *Yahav beih eina v'nach nafsheih* — he gave him the evil eye, and the elder died.

### Yehuda's Death

*Nafak l'shuka* — *Rabbi Shimon* went out to the marketplace. Here the *shuk* reappears — one of the very symbols at the center of the debate about Roman infrastructure, now the setting for his encounter with the man whose careless speech had cost *Rabbi Shimon* twelve years. *Chazyei l'yehudah ben geirim* — he saw *Yehuda ben Gerim*, the man who had inadvertently reported the three sages' conversation to the Roman authorities.

*Amar adayin yesh lazeh ba'olam* — this person is still alive in the world? *Natan bo einav v'na'aseh gal shel atzamot* — he directed his gaze upon him, and he was reduced to a heap of bones.

The *daf* ends here. It is worth pausing over the moral texture of the ending. *Rabbi Shimon* has emerged from the cave having learned to appreciate the *mitzvot* of ordinary Jews and to check his impulse to destroy those engaged in worldly pursuits. He has performed a *tikun* for the community. And yet: an elder who challenges him dies at his hand, and *Yehuda ben Gerim* — who by *Rashi's* account was not even deliberately informing but merely speaking carelessly — is killed for it. The lesson of the myrtle branches has not fully transformed him.

The story's redactor has also carefully woven together the themes of the *daf* at its conclusion. *Yehuda ben Gerim's* offense was, at root, a failure of careful speech — not *lashon hara* in the classic sense of slander, but precisely the kind of careless talk that the *gemara* has been discussing throughout. The great irony is that *Rabbi Shimon's* own story was set in motion by a *tikun* that was itself only partial. Had the narrative ended after his encounter with the old man and the myrtle branches, or after his gift to the community of clarifying the cemetery, the arc would have been one of genuine transformation. The final verses complicate that reading considerably — and one senses that the *gemara* intends us to sit with that complication.
