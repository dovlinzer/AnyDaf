# Shabbat 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sins & Punishments

### Women in Childbirth

The daf opens in the context of a broader discussion about why women die at the time of childbirth — a topic attributed to particular sins — which in turn opens onto the larger question of how one connects specific deaths or catastrophes to specific types of transgression. This raises, inevitably, serious questions of theodicy, and as we will see, the *gemara* itself does not shy away from pressing on some of those difficulties.

### Theft & Locusts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲוֹן גֵּזֶל הַגּוֹבַאי עוֹלֶה, וְהָרָעָב הוֹוֶה, וּבְנֵי אָדָם אוֹכְלִים בְּשַׂר בְּנֵיהֶן וּבְנוֹתֵיהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׁמְעוּ הַדָּבָר הַזֶּה פָּרוֹת הַבָּשָׁן אֲשֶׁר בְּהַר שׁוֹמְרוֹן הָעוֹשְׁקוֹת דַּלִּים הָרוֹצְצוֹת אֶבְיוֹנִים״. אָמַר רָבָא: כְּגוֹן הָנֵי נְשֵׁי דְמָחוֹזָא דְּאׇכְלָן וְלָא עָבְדָן. וּכְתִיב: ״הִכֵּיתִי אֶתְכֶם בַּשִּׁדָּפוֹן וּבַיֵּרָקוֹן הַרְבּוֹת גַּנּוֹתֵיכֶם וְכַרְמֵיכֶם וּתְאֵנֵיכֶם וְזֵיתֵיכֶם יֹאכַל הַגָּזָם״, וּכְתִיב: ״יֶתֶר הַגָּזָם אָכַל הָאַרְבֶּה וְיֶתֶר הָאַרְבֶּה אָכַל הַיָּלֶק וְיֶתֶר הַיֶּלֶק אָכַל הֶחָסִיל״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּגְזוֹר עַל יָמִין וְרָעֵב וַיֹּאכַל עַל שְׂמֹאל וְלֹא שָׂבֵעוּ אִישׁ בְּשַׂר זְרוֹעוֹ יֹאכֵלוּ״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בְּשַׂר זְרוֹעוֹ״ אֶלָּא ״בְּשַׂר זַרְעוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Due to the sin of robbery, locusts emerge, and famine prevails, and people eat the flesh of their sons and daughters,** as **it is stated: "Hear this word, you cows of Bashan, that are in the mountain of Samaria, that oppress the poor, that crush the needy,** that say unto their lords: Bring, that we may feast" (Amos 4:1). Afterward it says: "And I also have given you cleanness of teeth in all your cities, and want of bread in all your places" (Amos 4:6), which refers to famine. **Rava said:** The cows of Bashan; **like those women of** the city of **Meḥoza, who eat and do nothing** to support themselves, and cause their husbands to commit the sin of theft. **And it is written: "I have smitten you with blight and mildew; the multitude of your gardens and your vineyards and your fig trees and your olive trees has the palmerworm devoured"** (Amos 4:9). **And it is written: "That which the palmerworm has left the locust has eaten; and that which the locust has left the cankerworm has eaten; and that which the cankerworm has left the caterpillar has eaten"** (Joel 1:4). **And it is written: "And one snatches on the right hand, and is hungry; and he eats on the left hand, and is not satisfied; they eat every man the flesh of his own arm [*besar zero*'*o*]"** (Isaiah 9:19). **Do not read** it: **The flesh of his own arm [*besar zero*'*o*]; rather, the flesh of his own offspring [*besar zaro*].** All the punishments for theft listed above were explicitly mentioned in the verses.

The *gemara* picks up at the bottom of 33b. *Ba'avon gezel* — for the sin of theft — *hagovai oleh*, the locust rises up, *veharav hoveh*, and there is famine in the land. People are eventually reduced to eating the flesh of their own children, as the *pasuk* states: *shim'u hadavar hazeh parot habashan asher behar shomron ha'oshkot dalim harotzot evyonim* — "Hear this thing, cows of the Bashan who are in the mountains of Shomron, who oppress the poor and crush the destitute."

*Amar Rava*: who are these "cows of the Bashan"? *Kegon hanei neshei deMechuza de'achlan velo avdan* — they are like the women of *Mechuza*, who eat and do not work, like cows that just eat and grow fat and do nothing. Dr. Elman of Revel has written at length — a substantial monograph — on *Rava* of *Mechuza* and on how *Mechuza* was a cosmopolitan city whose residents gave *Rava* considerable trouble. He was constantly frustrated with them and persistently critical of them, which helps explain some of the sharper remarks he makes here. As *Rashi* explains, the connection to theft is that the women overate, forcing their husbands to go out and steal to support them.

How does this lead to the locusts? *Dechtiv*: "I have smitten you with *shidafon*" — a blasting wind — "and *yerakon*," a rot, "and all your vineyards and figs and olives shall be eaten by the *gazam*," the locust. And then: *yeter hagazam achal ha'arbeh veyeter ha'arbeh achal hayelek veyeter hayelek achal hachasil* — whatever one swarm of locusts left over, the next swarm consumed. From there the *pasuk* continues: "And He commanded on the right and there was famine, and they ate on the left and could not be satisfied. *Ish besar zero'o yocheilu* — each man shall eat the flesh of his arm." Here the *gemara* invokes the principle of *al tikrei*: do not read *besar zero'o*, the flesh of his arm, but *besar zar'o*, the flesh of his offspring — which of course corresponds to the explicit language of the *klalos* in the Torah that speaks of parents consuming their children.

What is striking about this entire passage — and this pattern will continue throughout much of the *daf* — is that the *gemara* is not speculating freely about why bad things happen and then reaching for a source. Rather, it seems to be doing something more constrained: taking explicit biblical *pesukim* and extracting from them the precise causal connection between sin and consequence. The *pesukim* of the *klalos* contain both the punishments and, by *derasha*, the sins. The *gemara* is mapping one onto the other.

### Judicial Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲוֹן עִינּוּי הַדִּין, וְעִיוּוּת הַדִּין, וְקִלְקּוּל הַדִּין, וּבִיטּוּל תּוֹרָה — חֶרֶב וּבִיזָּה רָבָה, וְדֶבֶר וּבַצּוֹרֶת בָּא, וּבְנֵי אָדָם אוֹכְלִין וְאֵינָן שְׂבֵעִין, וְאוֹכְלִין לַחְמָם בְּמִשְׁקָל. דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֵבֵאתִי עֲלֵיכֶם חֶרֶב נוֹקֶמֶת נְקַם בְּרִית וְגוֹ׳״. וְאֵין ״בְּרִית״ אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אִם לֹא בְרִיתִי יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״בְּשִׁבְרִי לָכֶם מַטֵּה לֶחֶם וְאָפוּ עֶשֶׂר נָשִׁים וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״יַעַן וּבְיַעַן בְּמִשְׁפָּטַי מָאָסוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, the Sages said that **due to the sin of delay of justice,** i.e., judges delay issuing their rulings due to personal considerations, **and** for **distortion of justice,** i.e., judges intentionally distort their verdicts, **and** for **miscarriage of justice** that results from negligence, **and** for **dereliction in** the study of **Torah, violence and looting abound** in the world, **and pestilence and famine come, and people eat and are not sated, and they eat their bread** measured **by weight. As it is written: "And I will bring a sword upon you, that shall execute the vengeance of the covenant;** and you shall be gathered together within your cities; and I will send the pestilence among you; and you shall be delivered into the hand of the enemy" (Leviticus 26:25). **And covenant means nothing other than Torah, as it is stated: "If My covenant be not with day and night,** if I have not appointed the ordinances of heaven and earth" (Jeremiah 33:25). The study of Torah is the mitzva practiced both day and night. **And it is written** with regard to this punishment: **"When I break your staff of bread, ten women shall bake** your bread in one oven, and they shall deliver your bread again by weight; and ye shall eat, and not be satisfied" (Leviticus 26:26). **And it is written: "Even because they rejected My ordinances,** and their soul abhorred My statutes" (Leviticus 26:43). All of these punishments result from breaching the covenant of the Torah and the perversion of justice.

*Ba'avon inuy hadin ve'ivut hadin vekilkul hadin uvitul Torah* — for the sins of oppressing justice, perverting justice, corrupting justice, and neglecting Torah. *Rashi* distinguishes carefully among the first three: *inuy hadin* is delaying judgment — the *din* is known but the court simply postpones its decision, denying speedy justice; *ivut hadin* is willful perversion; and *kilkul hadin* is corrupt negligence — not intentionally sinful but reckless enough to produce perversion of justice. And then there is *bitul Torah*, the neglect of Torah study.

The consequences: *cherev uviza raba* — sword and plunder — *vedever uvatzoret ba'a*, plague and famine. People eat and are not satisfied, and they must measure out their bread.

The *pasuk* cited is *ve'heiveiti aleichem cherev nokemet nekam berit* — "I will bring upon you a sword that avenges the covenant." The *gemara* establishes: *v'ein berit ella Torah* — *berit* refers to Torah — as the *pasuk* says, *im lo beriti yomam valailah chukkot shamayim va'aretz lo shamti*. And we know *yomam valailah* points to Torah because of *vehagita bo yomam valailah*. The *pasuk* continues: *b'shivri lachem mateh lechem*, "when I break your staff of bread," and *v'afu esser nashim lachmechem b'tanur echad* — ten women will bake their bread in a single oven — *yanu biyan b'mishpatai ma'asu*, because they despised My laws.

One might wonder how an emphasis on *din* — law and justice — gets folded into the theme of *bitul Torah*. It is worth noting that the *gemara* here seems to identify *Torah* with *din*, with its application in lived communal life rather than only as a theoretical pursuit. The verse's language of *mishpatai* — "my laws" — reinforces this, since *mishpat* points toward *ivut hadin* as well. It is also interesting that for us today this language translates more naturally into the domain of personal and private *halakha*, whereas the *gemara*'s framework here is more about the communal institutions of justice — reflecting the very different social reality of the Talmudic period.

### False Oaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲוֹן שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא וּשְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר וְחִילּוּל הַשֵּׁם וְחִילּוּל שַׁבָּת — חַיָּה רָעָה רָבָה, וּבְהֵמָה כָּלָה, וּבְנֵי אָדָם מִתְמַעֲטִין, וְהַדְּרָכִים מִשְׁתּוֹמְמִין. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם בְּאֵלֶּה לֹא תִוָּסְרוּ לִי״ — אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בְּאֵלֶּה״, אֶלָּא ״בְּאָלָה״. וּכְתִיב: ״וְהִשְׁלַחְתִּי בָכֶם אֶת חַיַּת הַשָּׂדֶה וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב בִּשְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר: ״וְלֹא תִשָּׁבְעוּ בִשְׁמִי לַשָּׁקֶר וְחִלַּלְתָּ אֶת שֵׁם אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, וּבְחִלּוּל הַשֵּׁם כְּתִיב: ״וְלֹא תְחַלְּלוּ אֶת שֵׁם קׇדְשִׁי״. וּבְחִלּוּל שַׁבָּת כְּתִיב: ״מְחַלְּלֶיהָ מוֹת יוּמָת״, וְיָלֵיף ״חִילּוּל״ ״חִילּוּל״ מִשְּׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Due to the sin of an oath** taken **in vain and a false oath, and desecration of** God's **name, and desecration of Shabbat, wild beasts abound** in the world, **and** domesticated **animal cease** to exist, **and human beings decrease** in number, **and the roads become desolate, as it is stated: "And if in spite of these [*be*'*eleh*] things you will not be corrected unto Me,** but will walk contrary unto Me casually" (Leviticus 26:23). **Do not read of these [*be*'*eleh*]; rather,** due to a vain **oath [*be*'*ala*]. And it is written** that the punishment for this is: **"And I will send the beast of the field among you,** which shall rob you of your children, and destroy your cattle, and make you few in number; and your ways shall become desolate" (Leviticus 26:22). **And it is written with regard to a false oath: "And you shall not swear by My name falsely, so that you desecrate [*veḥillalta*] the name of your God:** I am the Lord" (Leviticus 19:12). **And it is written with regard to desecrating the name of God: "And you shall not desecrate [*teḥallelu*] My Holy Name"** (Leviticus 22:32). **And it is written with regard to desecrating Shabbat: "Every one that desecrates it [*meḥaleleha*] shall surely be put to death"** (Exodus 31:14). **And derive** by means of a verbal analogy [*gezera shava*] **desecration [*ḥillul*] of Shabbat from desecration [*ḥillul*] of a false oath.** Just as punishment for a false oath is desolation and wild beasts, one receives the same punishment for desecrating Shabbat and the name of God.

*Ba'avon shvuat shav u'shvuat sheker v'chilul Hashem v'chilul Shabbat* — for the sins of an oath for naught, a false oath, desecrating God's name, and desecrating *Shabbat* — *chaya ra'ah rabbah u'vhemah kalah* — evil beasts multiply and domesticated animals are destroyed — *u'vnei adam mitma'atin*, the human population diminishes, *v'hadrachim mishtomemin*, and the pathways become desolate.

The *pasuk* says: *v'im b'eleh lo tivasru li* — "and if with these you are not chastened by Me." The *gemara* invokes *al tikrei*: do not read *b'eleh* but *b'alah* — with the oath. This gives us the connection to *shvua*. Then: *v'shillachti bachem et chayat hasadeh v'shikkla etchem v'nashamu darcheichem* — "I will send the beasts of the field against you, and they shall bereave you, and your roads shall be desolate." Those are the explicit consequences.

How do we arrive at *chilul Hashem* and *chilul Shabbat*? By *gzeira shava* of *chilul-chilul*. The *pasuk* by *shvuat sheker* states: *lo tishvu bishmi lashaker v'chillalta et shem elokecha* — a false oath constitutes *chilul Hashem*. And the *pasuk* by *Shabbat* states: *mechaleleha mot yumat* — those who desecrate it shall be put to death, the same root of *chilul*. So from *alah* we derive *shvua*; from *shvua* we derive *chilul*; and from *chilul* we derive both *chilul Hashem* and *chilul Shabbat* by *gzeira shava*.

Again, the *gemara* is not primarily engaged in symbolic speculation about the connection between these particular sins and these particular punishments. Its agenda seems to be either to explain why bad things happen in the world, or to convey *mussar* — if you sin, severe punishment awaits — or, most precisely, to show that everything follows from the explicit language of the *klalos*, with the *derasha* only pinpointing the exact sin that the *pasuk* has in view.

## Exile & Destruction

### Murder & Beit HaMikdash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲוֹן שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ חָרֵב, וּשְׁכִינָה מִסְתַּלֶּקֶת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא תַחֲנִיפוּ וְגוֹ׳ וְלֹא תְטַמֵּא אֶת הָאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר אַתֶּם יוֹשְׁבִים בָּהּ אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי שׁוֹכֵן בְּתוֹכָהּ״. הָא אַתֶּם מְטַמְּאִים אוֹתָהּ — אֵינְכֶם יוֹשְׁבִים בָּהּ, וְאֵינִי שׁוֹכֵן בְּתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Due to the sin of bloodshed, the Holy Temple is destroyed, and the Divine Presence leaves Israel, as it says: "So you shall not pollute** the land wherein you are; for blood, it pollutes the land; and no expiation can be made for the land for the blood that is shed therein, but by the blood of him that shed it. **And you shall not defile the land which you inhabit, in the midst of which I dwell;** for I the Lord dwell in the midst of the children of Israel" (Numbers 35:33–34). However, if **you defile** the land, **you will not inhabit it, and I will not dwell in it.**

*Ba'avon shfichut damim Beit HaMikdash charev v'shchina mistalleket mi'Yisrael* — for the sin of murder, the *Beit HaMikdash* is destroyed and the Divine Presence departs from Israel. The *pasuk*: *v'lo tachanifu et ha'aretz ki hadam hu yachanif et ha'aretz v'lo tetamme et ha'aretz asher atem yoshvim bah asher ani shochen b'tochah* — "do not defile the land in which you dwell, in which I dwell." The *gemara* reads: if you do defile it, then you will no longer dwell in it — *v'eini shochen b'tochah* — and I will no longer dwell within it either. This connection is direct and the *pasuk* makes it explicit.

### Immorality & Idolatry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲוֹן גִּלּוּי עֲרָיוֹת וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְהַשְׁמָטַת שְׁמִיטִּין וְיוֹבְלוֹת — גָּלוּת בָּא לָעוֹלָם, וּמַגְלִין אוֹתָן, וּבָאִים אֲחֵרִים וְיוֹשְׁבִין בִּמְקוֹמָן. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי אֶת כׇּל הַתּוֹעֵבוֹת הָאֵל עָשׂוּ אַנְשֵׁי הָאָרֶץ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַתִּטְמָא הָאָרֶץ וָאֶפְקוֹד עֲוֹנָהּ עָלֶיהָ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְלֹא תָקִיא הָאָרֶץ אֶתְכֶם בְּטַמַּאֲכֶם אוֹתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Due to the sin of prohibited sexual relations, and idol worship, and** failure to let the land **lie fallow** during **the Sabbatical and Jubilee Years, exile comes to the world and they exile** the Jewish people from their land, **and others come and settle in their place. As it is stated** with regard to illicit sexual relations: **"For all these abominations have the men of the land done,** that were before you, and the land is defiled; that the land expel not you also, when you defile it, as it expelled the nation that was before you" (Leviticus 18:27–28). **And it is written: "And the land was defiled, therefore I did visit the iniquity thereof upon it,** and the land expelled her inhabitants" (Leviticus 18:25). **And it is written: "That the land expel not you also, when you defile it,** as it expelled the nation that was before you."

*Ba'avon gilluy arayot v'avodah kochavim u'shmitat shmitin v'yovlos* — for the sins of sexual immorality, idolatry, and the violation of *shmitin* and *yovlos* — *galus ba lo-olam*, exile comes to the world, and others come and settle in their place.

The *pasuk* for sexual immorality: *ki es kol ha-to'evos ha-eil asu anshei ha-aretz*, "for all these abominations were done by the people of the land" — and the *to'evos ha-eil* appear immediately after the entire *parsha* of *arayot*, connecting them by juxtaposition. *Vatitma ha-aretz va-efkod avonah aleha* — the land became impure and God visited its sin upon it. *V'lo sakia ha-aretz eschem b'tamacha'chem osah ka'asher ka'ah es ha-goy asher lifneichem* — the land will not vomit you out when you defile it, just as it vomited out the nation before you.

It is worth noting that here the framework shifts slightly — the punishment is not described merely as *ba lo-olam*, a general occurrence, but carries the sense that non-Jews too were exiled for these sins, and that the Jews' own exile follows the same pattern. The non-Jews who previously occupied the land were themselves dispossessed; and the Jews, by committing the same offenses, face the same fate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה כְּתִיב: ״וְנָתַתִּי אֶת פִּגְרֵיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַהֲשִׁמּוֹתִי אֶת מִקְדְּשֵׁיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״, ״וְאֶתְכֶם אֱזָרֶה בַגּוֹיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And with regard to idol worship it is written: "And I will cast your carcasses** upon the carcasses of your idols" (Leviticus 26:30). **And it is written: "And I will bring your sanctuaries unto desolation,** and I will not smell the savor of your sweet odors" (Leviticus 26:31). **"And you will I scatter among the nations,** and I will draw out the sword after you; and your land shall be a desolation, and your cities shall be a waste" (Leviticus 26:33).

For *avodah zarah*: *v'nasati es pigreichem al pigrei gilluleichem* — "I will place your corpses upon the corpses of your idols." For *shmitin*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁמִיטִּין וּבְיוֹבְלוֹת כְּתִיב: ״אָז תִּרְצֶה הָאָרֶץ אֶת שַׁבְּתוֹתֶיהָ כֹּל יְמֵי הֳשַׁמָּה וְאַתֶּם בְּאֶרֶץ אוֹיְבֵיכֶם וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״כׇּל יְמֵי הׇשַּׁמָּה תִּשְׁבּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to** the sin of failure to observe **the Sabbatical and Jubilee Years it is written: "Then shall the land be paid her Sabbaths, as long as it lies desolate, and you are in your enemies' land;** even then shall the land rest, and repay her Sabbaths" (Leviticus 26:34). **And it is written: "As long as it lies desolate it shall have rest;** even the rest which it had not in your Sabbaths, when you dwelt upon it" **(**Leviticus 26: 35).

*az tirtzeh ha-aretz es shabbososeha kol yemei hashamah v'atem b'eretz oyveichem* — this is stated quite explicitly in *Behar*, that the land will finally rest and recover all the *shmitin* that were not observed during the time of settlement. *Kol yemei hashamah tishbos* — throughout all the years of its desolation it will rest, compensating for the *shmitin* that were neglected.

## Foul Speech

### Foul Speech Consequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲוֹן נַבְלוּת פֶּה צָרוֹת רַבּוֹת, וּגְזֵירוֹת קָשׁוֹת מִתְחַדְּשׁוֹת, וּבַחוּרֵי שׂוֹנְאֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵתִים, יְתוֹמִים וְאַלְמָנוֹת צוֹעֲקִין וְאֵינָן נַעֲנִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל כֵּן עַל בַּחוּרָיו לֹא יִשְׂמַח ה׳ וְאֶת יְתוֹמָיו (וְאַלְמְנוֹתָיו) [וְאֶת אַלְמְנוֹתָיו] לֹא יְרַחֵם כִּי כֻלּוֹ חָנֵף וּמֵרַע וְכׇל פֶּה דּוֹבֵר נְבָלָה בְּכׇל זֹאת לֹא שָׁב אַפּוֹ וְעוֹד יָדוֹ נְטוּיָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Due to the sin of vulgar speech, troubles abound, and harsh decrees are renewed, and the youth among the enemies of Israel,** a euphemistic reference to Israel, **die,** and **orphans and widows cry out** for help **and are not answered, as it is stated: "Therefore the Lord shall have no joy in their young men, neither shall He have compassion on their fatherless and widows; for everyone is ungodly and an evildoer, and every mouth speaks wantonness. For all this His anger is not turned away, but His hand is stretched out still"** (Isaiah 9:16).

Here the *gemara* introduces something rather different from what has come before. All the prior cases involved explicit and serious *aveiros* — theft, murder, sexual immorality, idolatry, and so on. Now the *gemara* turns to *nivlus peh*, foul or degenerate speech. The *Ramban* in his comment on *kedoshim tihyu* famously holds that one must live up to the spirit of Torah even where no explicit *mitzvah* applies, and *nivlus peh* is one of his prime examples — it is not a formal prohibition, yet it falls within the scope of *kedoshim tihyu*. It is striking, then, that in the middle of this very serious discourse about catastrophic punishment the *gemara* introduces this category.

### Rav Chanan on Speech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְעוֹד יָדוֹ נְטוּיָה״ — אָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן בַּר רָבָא: הַכֹּל יוֹדְעִין כַּלָּה לָמָּה נִכְנְסָה לַחוּפָּה. אֶלָּא כׇּל הַמְנַבֵּל פִּיו אֲפִילּוּ חוֹתְמִין עָלָיו גְּזַר דִּין שֶׁל שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה לְטוֹבָה — הוֹפְכִין עָלָיו לְרָעָה. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כׇּל הַמְנַבֵּל אֶת פִּיו — מַעֲמִיקִין לוֹ גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שׁוּחָה עֲמוּקָּה פִּי זָרוֹת״. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: אַף שׁוֹמֵעַ וְשׁוֹתֵק, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״זְעוּם ה׳ יִפּוֹל שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **But His hand is stretched out still? Rabbi Ḥanan bar Rava said: Everybody knows why the bride enters the wedding canopy.** There is no secret revealed. **Nevertheless, anyone who speaks vulgarly** about it, **even if they,** on High, **sealed for him a decree of seventy years of good** fortune**, they will reverse it to bad** fortune because of this sin. And **Rabba bar Sheila said** that **Rav Ḥisda said: Anyone who speaks vulgarly, they deepen Gehenna for him, as it is stated: "The mouth that speaks perversity is a deep pit**: he that is abhorred of the Lord shall fall therein**"** (Proverbs 22:14), i.e., Gehenna is deepened for one who speaks vulgarly. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Even** one **who hears** vulgar speech **and is silent** is punished, **as it is stated: "He that is abhorred of the Lord shall fall therein,"** even if he himself does not speak at all.

*Mai v'od yado netuyah?* What does it mean that His arm is still stretched out? *Amar Rav Chanan bar Rava*: *ha-kol yod'im kallah lamah nichnesah l'chuppah* — everyone knows why a bride enters the *chuppah*. The statement is already notable for focusing on the bride rather than the groom. *Ella kol ha-menavel piv afilu chosmim alav gezar din shel shiv'im shanah l'tovah hofchim alav l'ra'ah* — but anyone who makes his mouth foul, even if they have sealed for him a decree of seventy years of good, they overturn it entirely for evil.

This is a heavy claim. One might ask what precisely connects *v'od yado netuyah* to this teaching about seventy years being reversed. The textual derivation is not immediately transparent, and *Rashi* does not seem to resolve it clearly. The precise exegetical link to *shivim shanah* or to the reversal of a favorable *gezar din* remains somewhat opaque.

What is interesting about the choice of example — the bride entering the *chuppah* — is that it reflects a context where foul speech most naturally arises around the male gaze and objectification of women. But the formulation here is actually talking about the bride as the subject, not merely the object, which is somewhat curious.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: כׇּל הַמְּמָרֵק עַצְמוֹ לַעֲבֵירָה — חַבּוּרוֹת וּפְצָעִין יוֹצְאִין בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חַבּוּרוֹת פֶּצַע תַּמְרוּק בְּרָע״. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּדּוֹן בְּהִדְרוֹקָן: שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּמַכּוֹת חַדְרֵי בָטֶן״. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: סִימָן לַעֲבֵירָה הִדְרוֹקָן.
>
> **Translation:** And in a similar vein, **Rav Oshaya said: Anyone who** prepares **himself to** commit **a sin, wounds and bruises emerge on him, as it is stated: "Sharp wounds for one devoted to evil**; so do stripes that reach the inward parts" (Proverbs 20:30). **And not only that, but** he **is sentenced to** suffer from the disease of **edema [*hidrokan*], as it is stated: So do stripes that reach the inward parts. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: A sign** indicating one who **committed** a sin is the disease ***hidrokan*,** which afflicts the inner parts.

*Amar Rav Oshaya*: one who pours himself entirely into sin — devotes himself to it, pursues it — *chaburot u'ptzaim yotzin bo*, wounds will come upon him, as the *pasuk* says: *chaburot petza tamruk b'ra'ah* — wounds of smiting will purify in evil. *V'lo od ela shenidon behidrokan* — and moreover, he is judged with *hidrokan*, an intestinal disease. *Shene'emar u'makot chadrei vaten* — plagues of the innermost chambers of the belly.

### Illness as Sign

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה מִינֵי הִדְרוֹקָן הֵן: שֶׁל עֲבֵירָה עָבֶה, וְשֶׁל רָעָב תָּפוּחַ, וְשֶׁל כְּשָׁפִים דַּק.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **There are three types of *hidrokan*:** The one that comes as punishment **for sin** is **thick; and** that which is the result **of hunger is swollen**, but not as thick; **and** the one caused **by witchcraft** is **thin,** and the flesh of the sick person becomes thin in other places.

At this point the *gemara* executes a significant logical move. Until now the discourse has been directional: if you commit sin X, you receive punishment Y. Now *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* reverses the inference: *siman l'aveira hidrokan* — *hidrokan* is a sign that one has sinned. This is a much more theologically fraught claim. It is the logical fallacy of affirming the consequent: the fact that sin leads to illness does not mean that every instance of illness signals sin. The *gemara* is aware of this problem, as we shall see.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל הַקָּטָן חָשׁ בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִי מֵפִיס? אִיתַּסִּי. אַבָּיֵי חָשׁ בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר רָבָא: יָדַעְנָא בֵּיהּ בְּנַחֲמָנִי דְּמַכְפֵּין נַפְשֵׁיהּ. רָבָא חָשׁ בֵּיהּ. וְהָא רָבָא הוּא דְּאָמַר נְפִישִׁי קְטִילֵי קְדָר מִנְּפִיחֵי כָפָן? שָׁאנֵי רָבָא, דְּאׇנְסִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן בְּעִידָּנֵיהּ בְּעַל כּוּרְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel HaKatan fell ill with** *hidrokan*. **He said: Master of the Universe, who will draw lots,** meaning, who will be able to determine that this *hidrokan* is not the consequence of sin? **He was cured. Abaye** also **fell ill with** *hidrokan*. **Rava** testified and **said** about him: **I know about Naḥmani,** Abaye, **that** he **starves himself** and that his *hidrokan* is the result of hunger. The Gemara relates that **Rava fell ill with** *hidrokan*, and they asked: **But Rava** did not starve himself, and there is no reason to suspect him of sin, and we cannot say that he contracted *hidrokan* because he did not relieve himself on time. Rava knew to relieve himself, as it is **he** who **said: More have been killed** due to the **chamber pot**, because they were not careful about relieving themselves in a timely manner, **than those swollen** due to **starvation.** The Gemara answers: **Rava is different because the Sages compel him** to remain in place **against his will while he** lectures. Since he could not relieve himself, he became sick with *hidrokan*.

The discussion makes clear that the *gemara* recognizes the difficulty of inferring sin from illness — hence the careful work done to supply an alternative, non-sinful explanation for each of these *amoraim*'s cases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַרְבָּעָה סִימָנִין הֵן: סִימָן לַעֲבֵירָה — הִדְרוֹקָן, סִימָן לְשִׂנְאַת חִנָּם — יֵרָקוֹן, סִימָן לְגַסּוּת הָרוּחַ — עֲנִיּוּת, סִימָן לְלָשׁוֹן הָרָע — אַסְכָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita* that **there are four signs: A sign of sin is *hidrokan*, a sign of gratuitous hatred is jaundice, a sign of arrogance is poverty,** and **a sign of slander is *askara*.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַסְכָּרָה בָּאָה לְעוֹלָם עַל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: עַל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע. אָמַר רָבָא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַאי קְרָאָה — ״וְהַמֶּלֶךְ יִשְׂמַח בֵּאלֹהִים יִתְהַלֵּל כׇּל הַנִּשְׁבָּע בּוֹ כִּי יִסָּכֵר פִּי דוֹבְרֵי שָׁקֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: *Askara* comes to the world** as punishment for neglecting to separate **tithes. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, says:** *Askara* comes as punishment for **slander. Rava said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** who said it: **What is the verse** that alludes to this? **"But the king shall rejoice in God; every one that swears by Him shall glory; for the mouth of them that speak lies shall be stopped"** (Psalms 63:12). The punishment for lying is that the mouth will be stopped. *Askara* affects the mouth along with other parts of the body.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: there are four signs. The sign of sin is *hadrokan*. The sign of *sinas chinam* — baseless hatred — is *yerakon*, jaundice. The sign of haughtiness is poverty. And *siman lilashon hara askara* — the sign of evil speech is *askara*, a disease of the throat and upper respiratory tract.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: *askara* comes to the world *al hama'aser* — for failure to give tithes. *Rashi* explains: eating *tevel* carries the penalty of *mita bidei shamayim*, and since it involves eating, the disease appropriately afflicts the upper body, though it presents as respiratory rather than purely digestive. *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose* says: *askara* comes for *lashon hara*. This, too, makes intuitive sense — the disease affects the mouth and throat, the instruments of speech.

*Amar Rava* — and some say *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — *mai kra?* Where is it in the *pasuk*? *Ve'hamelech yismach belohim yithalel kol hanishba bo ki yisacher pi dovrei shaker* — "the mouth of those who speak falsehood shall be stopped up," and *yisacher* is read as cognate with *askara*. It is worth noting here that *lashon hara* in this context carries a broader meaning than the familiar one of slander — it encompasses all forms of wicked speech, including lying. The *Yerushalmi* similarly understands *lashon hara* as a generic category for harmful speech of any kind.

## Kerem b'Yavne

### Askara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי עַל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע קָאָמַר, אוֹ דִילְמָא אַף עַל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע נָמֵי קָאָמַר? תָּא שְׁמַע: כְּשֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ רַבּוֹתֵינוּ לַכֶּרֶם בְּיַבְנֶה הָיָה שָׁם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נִשְׁאֲלָה שְׁאֵלָה זוֹ בִּפְנֵיהֶם: מַכָּה זוֹ, מִפְּנֵי מָה מַתְחֶלֶת בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִים וְגוֹמֶרֶת בַּפֶּה? נַעֲנָה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי אִלְעַאי ״רֹאשׁ הַמְדַבְּרִים בְּכָל מָקוֹם״ וְאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכְּלָיוֹת יוֹעֲצוֹת, וְלֵב מֵבִין, וְלָשׁוֹן מְחַתֵּךְ — פֶּה גּוֹמֵר. נַעֲנָה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְאָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאוֹכְלִין בָּהּ דְּבָרִים טְמֵאִין. דְּבָרִים טְמֵאִים סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא שֶׁאוֹכְלִין בָּהּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵינָן מְתוּקָּנִים. נַעֲנָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְאָמַר: בַּעֲוֹן בִּיטּוּל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** those who were sitting in the study hall: Did **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, say** that *askara* comes as punishment only **for slander, or perhaps he said** it was **also for slander? Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from that which was taught in a *baraita*: **When our Sages entered the vineyard in Yavne, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, and Rabbi Shimon were there, and a question was asked before them** with regard to **this plague** of *askara*: **Why does it begin in the intestines and end in the mouth? Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ila'i,** who was **the head of the speakers in every place, responded and said: Even though the kidneys advise, and the heart understands, and the tongue shapes** the voice that emerges from the mouth, still, **the mouth completes** the formation of the voice. Therefore, the disease begins in the same place that slander begins and it ends in the mouth. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, responded and said:** This disease ends in the mouth **because one eats with it non-kosher things.** They immediately wondered about this: **Does it enter your mind** to say that *askara* is caused by eating **non-kosher food?** Are those who eat non-kosher food so numerous? **Rather,** it comes as a punishment **for eating** foods **that were not** ritually **prepared,** i.e., were not tithed. **Rabbi Shimon responded and said:** This disease comes as a punishment **for the sin of dereliction in** the study of **Torah.**

The *gemara* now asks: *iba'ya lehu* — a question arose among the sages: *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose al lashon hara kama o dilma af al lashon hara kama* — when he said that *askara* comes for *lashon hara*, did he mean exclusively for *lashon hara*, or even for *lashon hara* in addition to other causes? The stakes of this question become clear when the *gemara* introduces its proof.

*Keshenichnesu raboteinu l'Kerem b'Yavne* — when our sages entered the assembly at *Kerem b'Yavne*. This, of course, is the great rabbinic convocation following the *churban*, during which the sages gathered to preserve *Torah she-be'al peh* — the oral tradition — including the landmark testimony compiled in *Masechet Eduyot*. Three sages were present at this gathering in our narrative: *Rabbi Yehuda*, *Rabbi Elazar b'Rabbi Yose*, and *Rabbi Shimon*. And *nish'alu lish'eilah zu bifneihem* — the following question was brought before them.

The question itself is arresting: *maka zu mipnei mah matchala bivne me'ayim v'gomeres bepeh* — why does this affliction, *askara*, begin in the intestines and end in the mouth? It is striking to contemplate that the sages of *Kerem b'Yavne*, charged with the preservation of the entire oral tradition, apparently also devoted time to questions of this kind — theological and, in their terms, quasi-medical speculation about why a disease follows a particular course in the body. It speaks to how much *aggadata* and theological reflection — what we might call the attempt to discern divine order in the world — was part of their project. Especially in the aftermath of the *churban*, there was comfort in the idea that *hashgacha* was still operative, that God's hand could still be detected in the smallest details of human experience.

### Rabbi Yehuda's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְרַבִּי גּוּרְיוֹן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי גּוּרְיוֹן וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרַבִּי שְׁמַעְיָה: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַצַּדִּיקִים בַּדּוֹר — צַדִּיקִים נִתְפָּסִים עַל הַדּוֹר, אֵין צַדִּיקִים בַּדּוֹר — תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן נִתְפָּסִים עַל הַדּוֹר. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר זְעֵירִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן נְזִירָא: מַאי קְרָאָה — ״אִם לֹא תֵדְעִי לָךְ הַיָּפָה בַּנָּשִׁים צְאִי לָךְ בְּעִקְבֵי הַצֹּאן וְגוֹ׳״, וְאָמְרִינַן: גְּדָיִים הַמְמוּשְׁכָּנִין עַל הָרוֹעִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אַף עַל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע נָמֵי קָאָמַר — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There**, it must be understood **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Guryon,** as **Rabbi Guryon said, and some say** that it was **Rav Yosef, son of Rabbi Shemaya,** who said it: **At a time when** there are **righteous people in the generation,** the **righteous are seized**, i.e., they die or suffer, **for** the sins of **the generation. If there are no righteous people in the generation, school children,** who are also without sin, **are seized for** the sins of **the generation**. **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Ze'iri said, and some say** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Nezira said: What is the verse** that alludes to this? **"If you know not, you fairest among women, go your way forth by the footsteps of the flock** and feed your kids, beside the shepherds' tents [*mishkenot*]**" (**Song of Songs 1:8). **And we say** in explanation of this verse: They are the **lambs that are taken as collateral [*hamemushkanin*],** which is etymologically similar to the word *mishkenot*, **in place of the shepherds.** If the shepherds and leaders of the generation corrupt the multitudes, young children die because of their sins. With regard to the dilemma, **conclude from it** that Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, **said** that the illness of *askara* **also** results from **slander,** as the *baraita* provides an additional cause of the illness. The Gemara comments: Indeed, **conclude from it.**

*Tanna Rabbi Yehuda berabbi Ilai* — who is, of course, *stam Rabbi Yehuda* throughout the *Mishna*, the foremost student of *Rabbi Akiva*, and *rosh hamedabrim bechol makom* — the one who speaks first everywhere (the significance of this title will be explained in the next section). He answered: *af al pi shekelayot yoatzot velev mevin velashon mechatech peh gomer* — even though the kidneys advise, the heart understands, the tongue enunciates, the mouth finishes. In the ancient understanding of human physiology, the kidneys serve as the seat of inner counsel, the heart of understanding. The tongue and mouth are the final instruments through which thought becomes speech. *Rashi* notes: *peh gomer lashon hara* — the mouth is where *lashon hara* is completed. The disease, therefore, follows the same anatomical trajectory as the sin itself: it begins deep within the body and ascends to conclude in the mouth.

### Three Explanations

*Tanna Rabbi Elazar berabbi Yose* offered a different explanation: *mipnei she'ochlin bidevarim tme'in* — because they eat non-kosher things. The *gemara* immediately challenges: *devarim tme'in salka da'atach?* — is that really conceivable? *Rashi* explains the difficulty: most *issurim* of non-kosher food carry the penalty of *lav*, not *mita*, and the degree of punishment described here seems disproportionate. The *gemara* therefore reads *devarim tme'in* not as forbidden foods in general, but as *devarim she'einam metukanim* — *tevel*, untithed produce, which does indeed carry the penalty of *mita bidei shamayim*, and which also involves the mouth and eating.

*Tanna Rabbi Shimon* offered a third explanation: *ba'avon bitul Torah* — for the neglect of Torah study.

### Theodicy Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: נָשִׁים יוֹכִיחוּ! — שֶׁמְּבַטְּלוֹת אֶת בַּעֲלֵיהֶן. גּוֹיִם יוֹכִיחוּ! — שֶׁמְּבַטְּלִין אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל. תִּינוֹקוֹת יוֹכִיחוּ! — שֶׁמְּבַטְּלִין אֶת אֲבִיהֶן. תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן יוֹכִיחוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him: Women will prove** that dereliction in the study of Torah is not the cause, as they are not obligated to study Torah and, nevertheless, they contract *askara*. He answered them: They are punished because **they cause their husbands to be idle** from the study of Torah. They said to him: **Gentiles will prove** that this is not the cause, as they also contract *askara* even though they are not obligated to study Torah. He answered them: They are also punished because **they cause Israel to be idle** from the study of Torah. They said to him: **Children will prove** that this is not the cause, for they are not at all obligated to study Torah and they also suffer from *askara*. He answered them: They are punished because **they cause their fathers to be idle** from the study of Torah. They said to him: **School children will prove** that this is not the cause, as they study Torah and, nevertheless, they suffer from *askara*.

At this point the *gemara* raises the objection that gives this entire passage its real bite. The sages challenge each explanation in turn on grounds of theodicy: what about those who suffer *askara* but who cannot, by any reasonable logic, be guilty of the attributed sin?

Women, they object, are not obligated in *talmud Torah* — so how do they contract *askara* if it comes for *bitul Torah*? Rabbi Shimon answers: *shemvatlos et ba'aleihen* — they cause their husbands to be neglectful of Torah study, and so they bear that consequence.

*Nochrim yochi'u* — non-Jews will prove this wrong. Non-Jews certainly are not obligated in *talmud Torah*. The answer: *shemvatlin et Yisrael* — through their decrees and impositions they prevent Israel from investing fully in Torah study.

*Tinokot yochi'u* — what about young children? They answer: *shemvatlin et avihen* — they cause their fathers to be neglectful, perhaps by waking them at night and disrupting their learning.

### Righteous Suffer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְרַבִּי גּוּרְיוֹן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי גּוּרְיוֹן וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרַבִּי שְׁמַעְיָה: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַצַּדִּיקִים בַּדּוֹר — צַדִּיקִים נִתְפָּסִים עַל הַדּוֹר, אֵין צַדִּיקִים בַּדּוֹר — תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן נִתְפָּסִים עַל הַדּוֹר. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר זְעֵירִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן נְזִירָא: מַאי קְרָאָה — ״אִם לֹא תֵדְעִי לָךְ הַיָּפָה בַּנָּשִׁים צְאִי לָךְ בְּעִקְבֵי הַצֹּאן וְגוֹ׳״, וְאָמְרִינַן: גְּדָיִים הַמְמוּשְׁכָּנִין עַל הָרוֹעִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אַף עַל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע נָמֵי קָאָמַר — שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There**, it must be understood **in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Guryon,** as **Rabbi Guryon said, and some say** that it was **Rav Yosef, son of Rabbi Shemaya,** who said it: **At a time when** there are **righteous people in the generation,** the **righteous are seized**, i.e., they die or suffer, **for** the sins of **the generation. If there are no righteous people in the generation, school children,** who are also without sin, **are seized for** the sins of **the generation**. **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Ze'iri said, and some say** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Nezira said: What is the verse** that alludes to this? **"If you know not, you fairest among women, go your way forth by the footsteps of the flock** and feed your kids, beside the shepherds' tents [*mishkenot*]**" (**Song of Songs 1:8). **And we say** in explanation of this verse: They are the **lambs that are taken as collateral [*hamemushkanin*],** which is etymologically similar to the word *mishkenot*, **in place of the shepherds.** If the shepherds and leaders of the generation corrupt the multitudes, young children die because of their sins. With regard to the dilemma, **conclude from it** that Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Yosei, **said** that the illness of *askara* **also** results from **slander,** as the *baraita* provides an additional cause of the illness. The Gemara comments: Indeed, **conclude from it.**

The *gemara* then presses further: *tinokot shel beit rabban yochi'u* — what about the children who are already sitting in school, actively learning all day? What have they done? *Rashi* distinguishes the two cases: the first *tinokot* are infants, not yet old enough to learn; but the second, the *tinokot shel beit rabban*, are precisely the ones who are learning. How can *bitul Torah* be invoked against them?

Here the *gemara* introduces a profound and theologically weighty answer: *hasam kerabbi goryon* — that follows the view of *Rabbi Goryon* (others attribute it to *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yishmael*). *Bizman she'tzaddikim b'dor tzaddikim nitpasim al hador* — when there are righteous people in the generation, the righteous are sometimes punished in place of the generation; they suffer vicariously for others' sins. *Ein tzaddikim b'dor tinokot shel beit rabban nitpasim al hador* — when there are no such righteous people, then the *tinokot shel beit rabban* — the most innocent members of the community — are seized in their stead.

The *pasuk* cited: *im lo sadi lach hayafa banashim tzei lach b'ikvei hatzon u'rei es gediyosayich al mishkenos haro'im* — "if you do not know, most beautiful among women, go out by the footsteps of the flock and shepherd your young goats by the tents of the shepherds." The *gemara* reads *mishkenos haro'im* through the lens of *mashkon* — collateral. The shepherds, the leaders of the generation, hold these children as collateral for the community. When the shepherds themselves are no longer *tzaddikim*, the innocent children become the pledges that are seized.

This is, to say the least, a striking and deeply unsettling teaching. After Christianity, the idea of vicarious punishment — the innocent suffering for the sins of others — is one that many Jews instinctively resist as a foreign theological category. Yet the notion appears here with force, and it is hardly isolated. The concept of *misas tzaddikim mechaperes* — the death of the righteous as atonement — appears in classical liturgical contexts, including the *asara harugei malchus* on *Yom Kippur*, where the martyrs function as a kind of *korban* for the generation. The idea that innocent children suffer as a form of communal punishment for their fathers is perhaps even more disturbing, and the *gemara* does not resolve the tension — it simply presents it. The *tinokot shel beit rabban* dying for the generation is difficult to absorb, and it sits alongside — without resolving — the broader problem of children suffering for parental sin that the *gemara* has been wrestling with throughout.

## Rabbi Yehuda ben Gerim

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לֵיהּ ״רֹאשׁ הַמְדַבְּרִים בְּכָל מָקוֹם״? דְּיָתְבִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְיָתֵיב יְהוּדָה בֶּן גֵּרִים גַּבַּיְיהוּ. פָּתַח רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְאָמַר: כַּמָּה נָאִים מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן שֶׁל אוּמָּה זוֹ: תִּקְּנוּ שְׁוָוקִים, תִּקְּנוּ גְּשָׁרִים, תִּקְנוּ מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי שָׁתַק. נַעֲנָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי וְאָמַר: כׇּל מַה שֶּׁתִּקְּנוּ, לֹא תִּקְּנוּ אֶלָּא לְצוֹרֶךְ עַצְמָן. תִּקְּנוּ שְׁוָוקִין — לְהוֹשִׁיב בָּהֶן זוֹנוֹת, מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת — לְעַדֵּן בָּהֶן עַצְמָן, גְּשָׁרִים — לִיטּוֹל מֵהֶן מֶכֶס. הָלַךְ יְהוּדָה בֶּן גֵּרִים וְסִיפֵּר דִּבְרֵיהֶם, וְנִשְׁמְעוּ לַמַּלְכוּת. אָמְרוּ: יְהוּדָה שֶׁעִילָּה — יִתְעַלֶּה. יוֹסֵי שֶׁשָּׁתַק — יִגְלֶה לְצִיפּוֹרִי. שִׁמְעוֹן שֶׁגִּינָּה — יֵהָרֵג.
>
> **Translation:** In this *baraita* Rabbi Yehuda is described as head of the speakers in every place. The Gemara asks: **And why did they call him head of the speakers in every place?** The Gemara relates that this resulted due to an incident that took place **when Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon were sitting, and Yehuda, son of converts, sat beside them. Rabbi Yehuda opened and said: How pleasant are the actions of this nation,** the Romans, as **they established marketplaces, established bridges,** and **established bathhouses. Rabbi Yosei was silent. Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai responded and said: Everything that they established, they established only for their own purposes. They established marketplaces, to place prostitutes in them; bathhouses, to pamper themselves;** and **bridges, to collect taxes from** all who pass over **them. Yehuda, son of converts, went and related their statements** to his household, **and** those statements continued to spread until **they were heard by the monarchy. They** ruled and **said: Yehuda, who elevated** the Roman regime, **shall be elevated** and appointed as head of the Sages, the head of the speakers in every place. **Yosei, who remained silent, shall be exiled** from his home in Judea as punishment, and sent **to** the city of **Tzippori** in the Galilee. **And Shimon, who denounced** the government, **shall be killed.**

We now understand why *Rabbi Yehuda* was called *rosh hamedabrim bechol makom* — the first speaker everywhere. The *gemara* explains: *Rabbi Yehuda, Rabbi Yose*, and *Rabbi Shimon* were sitting together, and with them sat *Yehuda ben Gerim* — the son of converts. *Rabbi Yehuda* opened and said: *kama na'im ma'aseihem shel uma zu* — how beautiful are the accomplishments of this Roman nation. *Tiknu shvakin* — they made marketplaces. *Tiknu gisharin* — they built bridges. *Tiknu merchaza'os* — they built bathhouses.

*Rabbi Yose* said nothing. *V'na'ana Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai v'amar*: *kol mah shetiknu lo tiknu ela l'tzorech atzman* — everything they built was for their own self-interest alone. *Tiknu shvakin l'hoshiv bahen zonos* — they built the marketplaces to install prostitutes in them. *Marchaza'os* — to beautify themselves. *Gsharim litol mehem meches* — the bridges were built to collect tolls.

These three positions map quite naturally onto broader attitudes toward the non-Jewish world and, by extension, to how Jews have understood their relationship to the larger civilization in which they live. Is gratitude owed? Is appreciation appropriate, even when the builders are an oppressive power? *Rabbi Yehuda* says yes — the accomplishments are genuinely good. *Rabbi Shimon* says no — the motive was purely self-serving, and we are merely incidental beneficiaries. *Rabbi Yose* remains silent — perhaps the most prudent position, and the one that will earn him the least severe consequence.

It is worth noting that this exchange is taken up in the opening *sugya* of *Masechet Avodah Zarah*, where the same argument — that Rome built everything *l'tzorech atzmam* — is placed in the mouth of God Himself, addressing Rome's self-justification at the end of history. The fact that *Rabbi Shimon's* position is there attributed to the divine voice is significant, even as one recognizes that different *gemoros* take different approaches to this question.

### Consequences

*Halach Yehuda ben Gerim v'siper divreihem* — *Yehuda ben Gerim* went and repeated what he had heard. *Rashi* notes that this was not necessarily deliberate informing — he was simply talking, perhaps at home, perhaps in the presence of people with connections to non-Jews — and *v'nishmu l'malchus*, it reached the government's ears.

The government's response was calibrated precisely to each man's position. *Yehuda she'ila* — *Yehuda* who praised them — *yisaleh* — shall be elevated. And so he was honored by the government as the first to speak at every convocation, hence *rosh hamedabrim bechol makom*. *Yose sheshatak* — *Yose* who was silent — *yigaleh l'Tzipori* — shall be exiled to *Tzipori*. *Shimon shegina* — *Shimon* who spoke disparagingly — *yehareg* — shall be put to death. Whatever the precise historicity of this account, it accurately reflects the dangerous environment in which *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* lived and which shaped his deeply adversarial attitude toward Rome.

## Cave & Learning

### Twelve Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל הוּא וּבְרֵיהּ, טְשׁוֹ בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. כׇּל יוֹמָא הֲוָה מַתְיָא לְהוּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ רִיפְתָּא וְכוּזָא דְמַיָּא וְכָרְכִי. כִּי תְּקֵיף גְּזֵירְתָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ: נָשִׁים דַּעְתָּן קַלָּה עֲלֵיהֶן, דִילְמָא מְצַעֲרִי לַהּ וּמְגַלְּיָא לַן. אֲזַלוּ טְשׁוֹ בִּמְעָרְתָּא. אִיתְרְחִישׁ נִיסָּא אִיבְּרִי לְהוּ חָרוּבָא וְעֵינָא דְמַיָּא, וַהֲווֹ מַשְׁלְחִי מָנַיְיהוּ וַהֲווֹ יָתְבִי עַד צַוְּארַיְיהוּ בְּחָלָא. כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא גָּרְסִי. בְּעִידָּן צַלּוֹיֵי לָבְשִׁי מִיכַּסּוּ וּמְצַלּוּ, וַהֲדַר מַשְׁלְחִי מָנַיְיהוּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיבְלוּ. אִיתִּיבוּ תְּרֵיסַר שְׁנֵי בִּמְעָרְתָּא. אֲתָא אֵלִיָּהוּ וְקָם אַפִּיתְחָא דִמְעָרְתָּא, אֲמַר: מַאן לוֹדְעֵיהּ לְבַר יוֹחַי דְּמִית קֵיסָר וּבְטִיל גְּזֵירְתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai **and his son,** Rabbi Elazar, **went** and **hid in the study hall. Every day** Rabbi Shimon's **wife would bring them bread and a jug of water and they would eat. When the decree intensified,** Rabbi Shimon **said to his son: Women are easily impressionable** and, therefore, there is room for concern **lest** the authorities **torture her and she reveal our** whereabouts. **They went and they hid in a cave. A miracle occurred** and **a carob** tree **was created for them as well as a spring of water. They would remove their clothes and sit** covered **in sand up to their necks**. **They would study** Torah **all day** in that manner. **At the time of prayer, they would dress, cover themselves, and pray, and they would again remove their clothes afterward so that they would not become tattered. They sat in the cave for twelve years. Elijah** the Prophet **came and stood at the entrance to the cave** and **said: Who will inform bar Yoḥai that** the **emperor died and his decree has been abrogated?**

*Azal hu u'vreh* — he and his son fled. It is interesting that the son, *Rabbi Elazar*, was apparently also subject to some threat, or at least *Rabbi Shimon* did not want to be alone. They hid first in the *beis medrash*, sustained by bread and water brought daily by *Rabbi Shimon's* wife. When the Roman edict intensified, *Rabbi Shimon* said to his son: *nashim da'atan kala aleihen* — not that women lack intelligence, but that under torture they may not be able to withstand the pressure and might reveal their location. *Dilma m'tzari lah u'mgalya lan* — perhaps the authorities will torture her and she will disclose where they are.

So they went and hid in a cave. A miracle was wrought for them: a carob tree grew and a wellspring of water appeared. They took off their clothes and sat buried in sand up to their necks in order to preserve their single set of garments, studying all day. When the time came to pray, they dressed, *davened*, and then undressed again so that the clothing would not wear out. This arrangement also preserved their modesty. *Yatvu treisar shani b'mearta* — they remained in the cave for twelve years.

Then *Eliyahu* came and stood at the entrance of the cave. There is something evocative in this detail — the echo of *Eliyahu* in his own cave on *Har Horev* is unmistakable, as is the parallel of miraculous sustenance (the ravens bringing food to *Eliyahu*, the carob tree and spring here). *Eliyahu*'s announcement was indirect: *man lodeyei l'Bar Yochai d'mit Keisar u'batla gzeirateih* — who will inform *Bar Yochai* that Caesar has died and the edict is nullified?

### Burning & Repentance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַקוּ, חֲזוֹ אִינָשֵׁי דְּקָא כָּרְבִי וְזָרְעִי, אָמְרִין: מַנִּיחִין חַיֵּי עוֹלָם וְעוֹסְקִין בְּחַיֵּי שָׁעָה. כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנּוֹתְנִין עֵינֵיהֶן מִיָּד נִשְׂרָף. יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה לָהֶם: לְהַחֲרִיב עוֹלָמִי יְצָאתֶם?! חִיזְרוּ לִמְעָרַתְכֶם! הֲדוּר אֲזוּל אִיתִּיבוּ תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא. אָמְרִי: מִשְׁפַּט רְשָׁעִים בְּגֵיהִנָּם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: צְאוּ מִמְּעָרַתְכֶם! נְפַקוּ. כָּל הֵיכָא דַּהֲוָה מָחֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, הֲוָה מַסֵּי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אָמַר לוֹ: בְּנִי, דַּי לָעוֹלָם אֲנִי וְאַתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **They emerged** from the cave, and **saw people who were plowing and sowing.** Rabbi Shimon bar Yoḥai **said:** These people **abandon eternal life** of Torah study **and engage in temporal life** for their own sustenance. The Gemara relates that **every place that** Rabbi Shimon and his son Rabbi Elazar **directed their eyes was immediately burned. A Divine Voice emerged and said to them:** Did **you emerge** from the cave in order **to destroy My world? Return to your cave. They again went** and **sat** there **for twelve months. They said: The judgment of the wicked in Gehenna lasts** for **twelve months.** Surely their sin was atoned in that time. **A Divine Voice emerged and said** to them: **Emerge from your cave. They emerged. Everywhere that Rabbi Elazar would strike, Rabbi Shimon would heal.** Rabbi Shimon **said to** Rabbi Elazar: **My son, you and I suffice for the entire world,** as the two of us are engaged in the proper study of Torah.

They emerged from the cave and saw people plowing and planting. *Menichin chayei olam v'oskin b'chayei sha'ah* — they are leaving eternal life and occupying themselves with temporal needs. Twelve years of total immersion in Torah had rendered them incapable of relating to the world of ordinary human labor. *Kol makom shenotnin eineihem miyad nisraf* — wherever they directed their gaze, people were incinerated. This connects directly to a well-known *machloket* in *Berachot* between *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* and other sages about whether one engaged in Torah study should also engage in worldly labor, or whether, if the people merit it, others will take care of their material needs while they devote themselves entirely to Torah. Here, having lived that ideal in the cave, *Rabbi Shimon* and his son could no longer abide the sight of people choosing the temporal over the eternal.

*Yatzta bat kol v'amra lahem*: *l'hachriv olami yetzatem?* — "Did you come out to destroy My world?" *Chizru l'mearatchem* — return to your cave. So they returned. This time they remained for twelve months — and here *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* reasons: *mishpat reshaim b'gehinnom yud-bet chodesh* — even the wicked are punished in *gehinnom* for no more than twelve months. Perhaps this period will serve as a *kapara* for whatever wrong they had done.

When the second *bat kol* came — *tze'u m'mearatchem* — they emerged again. *Kol heicha d'hava machi Rabbi Elazar hava masi Rabbi Shimon* — wherever *Rabbi Elazar* would smite, *Rabbi Shimon* would heal. The son had apparently not fully absorbed the lesson; the father had. *Rabbi Shimon* said to his son: *beni dai l'olam ani v'ata* — you and I together are sufficient to sustain the world in Torah; do not worry about others. This remark carries both a note of genuine reassurance — don't hold the rest of the world to a standard they cannot meet — and perhaps a trace of the ego that the cave experience had also cultivated.

### Lesson of Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּהֲדֵי פַּנְיָא דְּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא חֲזוֹ הָהוּא סָבָא דַּהֲוָה נָקֵיט תְּרֵי מַדָּאנֵי אָסָא וְרָהֵיט בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הָנֵי לְמָה לָךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: לִכְבוֹד שַׁבָּת. וְתִיסְגֵּי לָךְ בְּחַד! — חַד כְּנֶגֶד ״זָכוֹר״ וְחַד כְּנֶגֶד ״שָׁמוֹר״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לִבְרֵיהּ: חֲזִי כַּמָּה חֲבִיבִין מִצְווֹת עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִיְּתִיבָה דַּעְתַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As the sun was setting on Shabbat eve, they saw an elderly man who was holding two bundles of myrtle branches and running at twilight. They said to him: Why do you have these? He said to them: In honor of Shabbat.** They said to him: **And let one suffice.** He answered them: **One** is **corresponding to: "Remember** the Shabbat day, to keep it holy" (Exodus 20:8), **and** one is **corresponding to: "Observe** the Shabbat day, to keep it holy" (Deuteronomy 5:12). Rabbi Shimon **said to his son: See how beloved the mitzvot are to Israel. Their minds were** put **at ease** and they were no longer as upset that people were not engaged in Torah study.

*V'bahadei panya d'ma'alei shabta* — as *Erev Shabbat* was falling, they saw an old man running with two bunches of myrtle. They asked him: why do you have these? *Amar lehu lichvod shabbos* — to honor *Shabbat*. And why two? *Chad kineged zachor v'chad kineged shamor* — one corresponding to *zachor* and one to *shamor*, the two formulations of the *Shabbat* commandment in the Torah.

*Amar lei l'breih* — he said to his son: *chazi kama chavivin mitzvos alei Yisrael* — see how dear the *mitzvos* are to Israel. The old man does not merely fulfill the minimum; he doubles his *mitzvah*, running at *bein hashmashos*, deeply invested in the honor of *Shabbat*. The people do not know *Torah* at the level of *Rabbi Shimon* and his son, but the *mitzvos* that are accessible to them — these they embrace with love. *Yasiv daiteihu* — their minds settled. The rage at the world dissipated. The parallel of the story to the relationship between *Eliyahu* and his encounter with ordinary human life after his cave experience is again present in the background.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר חַתְנֵיהּ וּנְפַק לְאַפֵּיהּ. עַיְּילֵיהּ לְבֵי בָנֵי, הֲוָה קָא אָרֵיךְ לֵיהּ לְבִישְׂרֵיהּ. חֲזָא דַּהֲוָה בֵּיהּ פִּילֵי בְּגוּפֵיהּ. הֲוָה קָא בָכֵי וְקָא נָתְרָן דִּמְעָת עֵינֵיהּ וְקָמְצַוְּחָא לֵיהּ. אָמַר לוֹ: אוֹי לִי שֶׁרְאִיתִיךָ בְּכָךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: אַשְׁרֶיךָ שֶׁרְאִיתַנִי בְּכָךְ, שֶׁאִילְמָלֵא לֹא רְאִיתַנִי בְּכָךְ — לֹא מָצָאתָ בִּי כָּךְ. דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא כִּי הֲוָה מַקְשֵׁי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי קוּשְׁיָא, הֲוָה מְפָרֵק לֵיהּ רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר תְּרֵיסַר פֵּירוּקֵי. לְסוֹף, כִּי הֲוָה מַקְשֵׁי רַבִּי פִּנְחָס בֶּן יָאִיר קוּשְׁיָא — הֲוָה מְפָרֵק לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי עֶשְׂרִין וְאַרְבְּעָה פֵּירוּקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Pineḥas ben Ya'ir,** Rabbi Shimon's **son-in-law**, **heard and went out to** greet **him. He brought him into the bathhouse and** began **tending to his flesh. He saw that** Rabbi Shimon **had cracks in** the skin on **his body. He was crying, and the tears fell from his eyes and caused** Rabbi Shimon **pain.** Rabbi Pineḥas **said to** Rabbi Shimon, his father-in-law: **Woe is me, that I have seen you like this.** Rabbi Shimon **said to him: Happy are you that you have seen me like this, as had you not seen me like this, you would not have found in me this** prominence in Torah, **as** the Gemara relates: **At first, when Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai would raise a difficulty, Rabbi Pineḥas ben Ya'ir would respond** to his question with **twelve answers. Ultimately, when Rabbi Pineḥas ben Ya'ir would raise a difficulty**, **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai would respond** with **twenty-four answers.**

*Shama Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair chasnei* — his son-in-law *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* heard that *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* was coming out, went to meet him, and brought him to the bathhouse. The earlier discussion in the *sugya* of *Rabbi Shimon's* critique of Roman bathhouses built for self-indulgence now acquires an ironic undertone — he is himself brought to a bathhouse, and finds comfort in it. One wonders whether the story's redactor intended the connection.

As *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* massaged *Rabbi Shimon's* skin, he saw that it was cracked and furrowed from years of sitting in sand. He wept, and the tears fell on the fissured skin and caused *Rabbi Shimon* pain. *Rabbi Shimon* said to him: *ashrecha she're'isani b'kach* — fortunate are you to have seen me in this state, for *she'ilmalei lo re'isani b'kach lo matzasa bi kach* — had you not seen me this way, you would not have found in me what you now find. The physical mortification had been the price of the Torah he had attained.

The measure of the transformation is expressed in striking terms: before the cave, when *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* posed a difficult question, *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* — his son-in-law and himself a towering *tanna* — would offer twelve answers. After the cave, when *Rabbi Pinchas ben Yair* posed a question, *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* would give twenty-four answers. He had surpassed his interlocutor by a factor of two, and his son-in-law had previously surpassed him by the same factor.

## Purifying Cemetery

### Decision to Help

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: הוֹאִיל וְאִיתְרְחִישׁ נִיסָּא אֵיזִיל אַתְקֵין מִילְּתָא. דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּבֹא יַעֲקֹב שָׁלֵם״, וְאָמַר רַב: שָׁלֵם בְּגוּפוֹ, שָׁלֵם בְּמָמוֹנוֹ, שָׁלֵם בְּתוֹרָתוֹ. ״וַיִּחַן אֶת פְּנֵי הָעִיר״, אָמַר רַב: מַטְבֵּעַ תִּיקֵּן לָהֶם, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שְׁוָוקִים תִּיקֵּן לָהֶם, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת תִּיקֵּן לָהֶם. אֲמַר: אִיכָּא מִילְּתָא דְּבָעֵי לְתַקּוֹנֵי? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִיכָּא דּוּכְתָּא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ סְפֵק טוּמְאָה
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon **said: Since a miracle transpired** for me, **I will go** and **repair something** for the sake of others in gratitude for God's kindness, **as it is written: "And Jacob came whole** to the city of Shechem, which is in the land of Canaan, when he came from Paddan-aram; and he graced the countenance of the city" (Genesis 33:18). **Rav said,** the meaning of: And Jacob came whole, is: **Whole in his body, whole in his money, whole in his Torah.** And what did he do? **And he graced the countenance of the city;** he performed gracious acts to benefit the city. **Rav said:** Jacob **established a currency for them. And Shmuel said: He established marketplaces for them. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: He established bathhouses for them.** In any event, clearly one for whom a miracle transpires should perform an act of kindness for his neighbors as a sign of gratitude. **He said: Is there something that needs repair? They said to him: There is a place where there is uncertainty with regard to ritual impurity**

*Ho'il v'itrachish nisa* — since a miracle has been performed for me, *eizil askin milsa* — let me go and benefit the community in some way. The model he invokes is *Yaakov Avinu*. *D'ksiv vayavo Yaakov shalem* — and *Yaakov* came whole to the city of *Shechem*. *Rav* explains: *shalem b'gufo shalem b'mamono shalem b'Toraso* — whole in his body after the struggle with the angel, whole in his wealth, whole in his Torah — none of this was damaged by his years with *Lavan*.

*Vayichan es p'nei ha'ir* — "and he encamped before the city." *Rav* says: *vayichan* indicates that he gave *chen*, grace, to the city — he established coinage for them. *Shmuel* says: *shvaki tikein lahem* — he set up markets. *Rabbi Yochanan* says: *merchatzaos tikein lahem* — he built bathhouses. The echo of the Roman infrastructure debate — *shvakim*, *gisharin*, *merchatzaos* — is unmistakable and is clearly intentional on the part of the *gemara*'s redactor. *Yaakov Avinu* built for *Shechem*, a non-Jewish city; the Romans built supposedly for the general public. The significant distinction seems to be directionality: *Yaakov* built for others from a position of generosity, while the Romans built ostensibly for others but actually for themselves.

There is a *machloket* between *Rashi* and *Tosafot* on what exactly *Rabbi Shimon's* identification with *Yaakov* entails. *Tosafot* understands *avad ihu nami hachi* — he did similarly — as meaning that *Rabbi Shimon* modeled himself on *Yaakov Avinu* directly. *Rashi* appears to read it as a precedent for doing something — the parallel is that both emerged from a difficult experience and gave back. The precise scope of the analogy remains contested.

### Cemetery Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִית לְהוּ צַעֲרָא לְכֹהֲנִים לְאַקּוֹפֵי. אֲמַר: אִיכָּא אִינִישׁ דְּיָדַע דְּאִיתַּחְזַק הָכָא טָהֳרָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא סָבָא: כָּאן קִיצֵּץ בֶּן זַכַּאי תּוּרְמְסֵי תְּרוּמָה. עֲבַד אִיהוּ נָמֵי הָכִי, כָּל הֵיכָא דַּהֲוָה קְשֵׁי — טַהֲרֵיהּ, וְכָל הֵיכָא דַּהֲוָה רְפֵי — צַיְּינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **and the priests are troubled** by being forced **to circumvent it,** as it is prohibited for them to become ritually impure from contact with a corpse. There was suspicion, but no certainty, that a corpse was buried there. Therefore, they were unable to definitively determine its status. Rabbi Shimon **said: Is there a person who knows that there was a presumption of ritual purity here?** Is there anyone who remembers a time when this place was not considered ritually impure, or that at least part of it was considered to be ritually pure? **An Elder said to him: Here ben Zakkai** planted and **cut the *teruma* of lupines.** In this marketplace Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai, who himself was a priest, once planted lupines that were given to him as *teruma*. On that basis, the conclusion can be drawn that it was definitely ritually pure. Rabbi Shimon, like Jacob, **also did so** and took steps to improve the city and examined the ground (*Tosafot*). **Everywhere that** the ground **was hard, he pronounced it ritually pure** as there was certainly no corpse there, **and every place that** the ground **was soft, he marked it** indicating that perhaps a corpse was buried there. In that way, he purified the marketplace so that even priests could walk through it.

The community presented *Rabbi Shimon* with its problem: *ika duchta d'is bei safek tumah* — there is a place with doubtful *tumah*, likely a burial ground within or adjacent to a public space, *v'is lei tza'ara l'kohanim l'akufi* — causing *kohanim* considerable hardship by forcing them to circumnavigate it. Could the situation be clarified?

*Rabbi Shimon* asked whether anyone could testify that the area had previously been established as *tahor*. A certain elder replied: *kan kitzetz ben zakai turmusei terumah* — here *Ben Zakai* had handled cuttings of *terumah*, which means the space had at some point been confirmed as *tahor*, since *terumah* could not have been handled there otherwise. Based on this precedent, *Rabbi Shimon* proceeded to survey the area: wherever the ground was hard and compact — indicating virgin, undisturbed earth — he declared it *tahor*. Wherever it was soft — indicating the possibility of a burial — he marked it as requiring caution.

### Elder's Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר הָהוּא סָבָא: טִיהֵר בֶּן יוֹחַי בֵּית הַקְּבָרוֹת! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִילְמָלֵי לֹא הָיִיתָ עִמָּנוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָיִיתָ עִמָּנוּ, וְלֹא נִמְנֵיתָ עִמָּנוּ — יָפֶה אַתָּה אוֹמֵר. עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁהָיִיתָ עִמָּנוּ, וְנִמְנֵיתָ עִמָּנוּ, יֹאמְרוּ: זוֹנוֹת מְפַרְכְּסוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים לֹא כָּל שֶׁכֵּן?! יְהַב בֵּיהּ עֵינֵיהּ וְנָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ. נְפַק לְשׁוּקָא חַזְיֵיהּ לִיהוּדָה בֶּן גֵּרִים. אֲמַר: עֲדַיִן יֵשׁ לְזֶה בָּעוֹלָם? נָתַן בּוֹ עֵינָיו וְעָשָׂהוּ גַּל שֶׁל עֲצָמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain Elder said** in ridicule and surprise: **Ben Yoḥai purified the cemetery.** Rabbi Shimon got angry and **said to him: Had you not been with us, and even had you been with us and were not counted with us** in rendering this ruling, what **you say is fine.** You could have said that you were unaware of my intention or that you did not agree or participate in this decision. **Now that you were with us and were counted with us** in rendering this ruling, you will cause people to say that Sages are unwilling to cooperate with one another. **They will say:** If competing **prostitutes** still **apply makeup to each other** to help one another look beautiful, **all the more so** that **Torah scholars** should cooperate with each other. **He directed his eyes toward him and** the Elder **died.** Rabbi Shimon **went out to the marketplace** and **he saw Yehuda, son of converts,** who was the cause of this entire incident. Rabbi Shimon, **said: This** one **still has** a place **in the world? He directed his eyes toward him and turned him into a pile of bones.**

A certain elder then remarked sarcastically: *teher ben yochai beit hakevarot* — so *ben Yochai* purified a cemetery! The implication was that *Rabbi Shimon* had simply declared everything clean when the true state of affairs was more complicated.

*Rabbi Shimon* responded sharply: *ilmalei hayita imanu v'afilu hayita imanu v'lo nimneita imanu yafeh atah omer* — if you were not part of our deliberations, or if you were present but did not participate in the vote, I could understand your critique. *Achshav shehayita imanu v'nimneita imanu* — but you were part of the process and part of the decision, and you still attack me by name. He invoked the principle: *yomru zonot mefarkesot zo et zo talmidei chachamim lo kol shekein* — if prostitutes adorn one another, how much more so should scholars support rather than undermine one another, even amid competition. *Yahav beih eina v'nach nafsheih* — he gave him the evil eye, and the elder died.

*Nafak l'shuka* — *Rabbi Shimon* went out to the marketplace. Here the *shuk* reappears — one of the very symbols at the center of the debate about Roman infrastructure, now the setting for his encounter with the man whose careless speech had cost *Rabbi Shimon* twelve years. *Chazyei l'yehudah ben geirim* — he saw *Yehuda ben Gerim*, the man who had inadvertently reported the three sages' conversation to the Roman authorities.

*Amar adayin yesh lazeh ba'olam* — this person is still alive in the world? *Natan bo einav v'na'aseh gal shel atzamot* — he directed his gaze upon him, and he was reduced to a heap of bones.

The *daf* ends here. It is worth pausing over the moral texture of the ending. *Rabbi Shimon* has emerged from the cave having learned to appreciate the *mitzvot* of ordinary Jews and to check his impulse to destroy those engaged in worldly pursuits. He has performed a *tikun* for the community. And yet: an elder who challenges him dies at his hand, and *Yehuda ben Gerim* — who by *Rashi's* account was not even deliberately informing but merely speaking carelessly — is killed for it. The lesson of the myrtle branches has not fully transformed him.

The story's redactor has also carefully woven together the themes of the *daf* at its conclusion. *Yehuda ben Gerim's* offense was, at root, a failure of careful speech — not *lashon hara* in the classic sense of slander, but precisely the kind of careless talk that the *gemara* has been discussing throughout. The great irony is that *Rabbi Shimon's* own story was set in motion by a *tikun* that was itself only partial. Had the narrative ended after his encounter with the old man and the myrtle branches, or after his gift to the community of clarifying the cemetery, the arc would have been one of genuine transformation. The final verses complicate that reading considerably — and one senses that the *gemara* intends us to sit with that complication.
