# Pesachim 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ritzuy Tzitz Principle

### Ritzuy Tzitz Basics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפֶּסַח שֶׁנִּזְרַק דָּמוֹ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹדַע שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא — הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה. נִטְמָא הַגּוּף — אֵין הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ: הַנָּזִיר וְעוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח, הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל טוּמְאַת הַדָּם, וְאֵין הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל טוּמְאַת הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case of **a Paschal lamb whose blood was sprinkled and subsequently it became known that** the meat or blood **was ritually impure, the frontplate** of the High Priest **appeases** God for the ritual impurity after the fact, and the owners are exempt from observing the second *Pesaḥ*. If it became known later that **the body** of the individual who brought the Paschal lamb **had become ritually impure, the frontplate does not appease** God. The individual has not fulfilled his obligation to bring the Paschal lamb, and therefore he must observe the second *Pesaḥ*. This is **because** the Sages **said** that with regard to **the nazirite and one who performs** the ritual of **the Paschal lamb, the frontplate appeases** God **for** both **impurity of the blood** and meat of the offering, but **the frontplate does not appease** God **for impurity of the body** of the individual bringing the offering.

The *mishna* on this *daf* builds upon the concept of *ritzuy tzitz* — the mechanism by which the *kohen gadol*'s golden forehead plate renders acceptable a *korban* that would otherwise be invalid on account of *tumah*. The Torah states *venasa Aharon et avon hakodashim asher yakdishu bnei Yisrael* — that Aaron shall bear the sin of the sanctified objects consecrated by the children of Israel. The *halacha* understands this to mean that *korbanot* which contracted *tumah* are rendered acceptable through the presence of the *tzitz*, provided the *tumah* was contracted without awareness at the time of the *zerikah*, the throwing of the blood. This is the technical meaning of *ritzuy*: making the *korban* acceptable despite the *tumah*.

One background issue is whether this principle is needed in concert with the concept of *tumah hutrah b'tzibur* — that *tumah* is entirely permitted when the community is *tamei* — or only when *tumah* is merely *dechuya*, overridden but still present. Our discussion, however, shifts to the case of the individual. The *mishna* teaches that the *tzitz* works when the *korban's* blood or meat is *tamei*, not when the person offering it is *tamei*. The *tzitz* is *meratze* only for *avon hakodashim*, the sin pertaining to the sanctified objects themselves, and not *avon hamakrivim*, the sin pertaining to those who bring the offering.

### Object vs. Person Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם — הַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna introduces a *halakha* with regard to ritual impurity of the deep, a term that refers to a source of impurity that is unknown to anyone and is discovered only after it has rendered someone impure. If it became known after the offering was brought that the person had **become impure** due to **ritual impurity of the deep,** e.g., if he was informed that there was a concealed grave under the place he had sat in a house where he had previously stayed, **the frontplate appeases** God and the offering is valid.

In most circumstances, the *tumah* of the owner of the *korban* is immaterial — the owner need not be present in the *Beit Hamikdash*. But two *korbanot* are exceptional in this regard: the *korban pesach* and the *korban* of the *nazir*. If the owner of a *korban pesach* is *tamei*, he cannot participate in the *Pesach* offering and must instead bring a *Pesach Sheni*. If the *nazir* is *tamei*, his *nezirut* must begin again from the start. In these two cases, *tumah* of the person is critically determinative, and the *tzitz* does not assist even if the person was entirely unaware of the *tumah*.

### Tumat HaTehom Exception

There is, however, a remarkable exception. The *mishna* teaches that the *tzitz* does work for *tumat hatehom* — a *tumah* of the depths — even when the person himself is *tamei*. *Tumat hatehom* refers not merely to a *tumah* that was unknown to the person, but to a *tumah* that was unknowable at the time. This is treated as a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, a received tradition from Sinai, that in such a case the *tzitz* renders the *korban pesach* or the *korban* of the *nazir* acceptable. The question then follows from the preceding *gemara*: does this *halacha* apply exclusively to the owner (*be'alim*) of the *korban*, or does it extend also to the *kohen* performing the *avodah*?

## Zavah & Rabbi Yose

### Shomeret Yom Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וְזָרְקוּ עָלֶיהָ בַּשֵּׁנִי שֶׁלָּהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאֲתָה — אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת, וּפְטוּרָה מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Wasn't** the following **taught** in a *baraita*? **Rabbi Yosei says: A woman who keeps watch a day for a day** is a woman who discharges blood for one or two days at a time when she does not expect her menstrual period. The case under discussion is one where she experienced a discharge for one day and **they slaughtered** a Paschal lamb **and sprinkled** the blood **for her** **on her second** day, after she immersed in a ritual bath. At that point, it is unclear whether she will remain clean of discharges for the remainder of the day, in which case she was pure from the time she immersed and may eat the Paschal lamb at night, or whether she will experience a discharge of blood during the day, in which case her immersion is retroactively invalid and she was impure the entire time. **And after that, she saw** blood, thus retroactively clarifying that at the time the Paschal lamb was slaughtered she was unfit to participate in it. The *halakha* is that **she may not eat** from the Paschal lamb due to her ritual impurity, **but she is exempt from performing** the ritual of **the second *Pesaḥ*.

To address this question, the *gemara* introduces the position of *Rabbi Yose*, and before analyzing how his position bears on the question of the *kohen*, it is necessary to understand the underlying scenario. The case involves a woman who is a *shomeret yom kineged yom* — she observed one clean day opposite a day of *tumah*.

The relevant *halacha* is as follows. A woman who experiences uterine bleeding outside her regular menstrual period is subject to the laws of *zavah*. If she bleeds on Sunday, she is *teme'ah* that day. She then immerses in the *mikveh* Monday morning and must observe Monday without further bleeding. If she successfully remains clean through Monday, she is *tehorah*. If, however, she bleeds at some point during Monday, that becomes a second day of *tumah*, and she must observe Tuesday as well; should she bleed again on Tuesday, it becomes three consecutive days, rendering her a *zavah gedolah* who must count seven clean days.

The scenario the *gemara* highlights is this: a woman bleeds on Sunday, immerses on Monday morning, and they then bring a *korban pesach* on her behalf during Monday — since *Erev Pesach* falls on that day — while she is apparently *tehorah*. Later on Monday she bleeds again. Retroactively, it turns out she was in an ambiguous state during the entire period from Monday morning until the renewed bleeding. The *gemara* identifies this as a paradigmatic case of *tumat hatehom* — a *tumah* that was unknowable at the time of the offering. The question is whether the *tzitz* works for such a case of *zavah* or only for *tumas meis*, corpse *tumah*.

### Rabbi Yose's Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּמְרַצֶּה צִיץ? אָמְרִי: לָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הִיא מְטַמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that she is exempt from the second *Pesaḥ*? **Is it not because the frontplate appeases** God, and therefore her first Paschal lamb was valid? Consequently, it is clear that the frontplate does appease God for uncertain ritual impurity related to the discharge of a *zava*. **Say** in refutation of this proof: **No,** this is not the reason. Rather, it is **because Rabbi Yosei holds** that **she renders** objects **impure from now and onward.** When a woman who keeps watch a day for a day experiences another discharge on the second day after she immersed in a ritual bath, she is not retroactively considered to have been impure the entire time; rather, she begins a new period of impurity from the time of her second discharge. Therefore, when the Paschal lamb was slaughtered on her behalf, she was ritually pure.

A *baraita* is cited: *Rabbi Yose* states that in exactly this case — a *shomeret yom kineged yom* for whom the *korban pesach* was slaughtered and the blood thrown, and who then bled later that same day — she may not eat the *korban pesach* that night, since she is now *teme'ah*, but she is exempt from bringing a *Pesach Sheni*. The *korban* was accepted. The *gemara* initially suggests the reason must be *ritzuy tzitz* — the *tzitz* worked for her because her *tumah* was unknowable at the time of the *zerikah*.

### Rami's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: זָב בַּעַל שְׁתֵּי רְאִיּוֹת שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וְזָרְקוּ עָלָיו בַּשְּׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאָה, וְכֵן שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וְזָרְקוּ עָלֶיהָ בַּשֵּׁנִי שֶׁלָּהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאֲתָה — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מְטַמְּאִין מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב לְמַפְרֵעַ, וּפְטוּרִים מִלַּעֲשׂוֹת פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this refutation: **Wasn't** the following **taught** in a *baraita*? **Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **a *zav* who has had two sightings** of discharge **for whom they slaughtered** a Paschal lamb **and sprinkled** its blood **on his seventh** day, after he immersed in a ritual bath, **and subsequently** on that same day **he saw** an additional discharge, which makes him ritually impure for another seven days; **and similarly,** with regard to **a woman who keeps watch a day for a day for whom they slaughtered** a Paschal lamb **and sprinkled** its blood **on her second** day after she immersed in a ritual bath, **and subsequently she saw** an additional discharge on that same day; **these** *zavim* **render** objects designed for **lying and sitting impure retroactively.** Once they experience an additional discharge, any object designed for lying or sitting upon which the *zav* or *zava* leaned between his or her immersion and the new discharge is considered to be retroactively impure from the time he or she leaned on it. However, **they are exempt from performing** the ritual of **the second *Pesaḥ*.** This is proof that Rabbi Yosei holds that their ritual impurity is retroactive. Therefore, at the time the Paschal lamb is slaughtered, it is uncertain whether they are ritually pure or impure, because if they have another discharge before the end of the day, they are retroactively considered to have been impure the entire time. From the fact that they are exempt from the second *Pesaḥ* even if it turns out that they were ritually impure, it appears that the frontplate does appease God for uncertain ritual impurity due to the discharge of a *zava*.

*Rami* objects. The exemption from *Pesach Sheni* need not be attributed to *ritzuy tzitz* at all. Perhaps *Rabbi Yose* holds the position that a *zavah* who bleeds on the second day does not thereby create a retroactive continuous *tumah*. Rather, the renewed bleeding constitutes a new and independent onset of *tumah*, commencing only from the moment it occurs. Under this reading, the woman was genuinely *tehorah* from the time of her *mikveh* immersion on Monday morning until she bled again that afternoon. The *korban pesach* was therefore brought in a state of actual *taharah*, and no *tzitz* was needed. The reason she cannot eat that night is that she became *teme'ah* after the offering was completed, but the *korban* itself was entirely unimpeachable.

### Zav & Zavah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: מַאי, לְמַפְרֵעַ — מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds to this attempted proof. **Say: What** is the meaning of Rabbi Yosei's statement that the ritual impurity applies **retroactively?** It means that the ritual impurity applies by **rabbinic** decree. However, according to Torah law, the *zav* or *zava* is impure only from the time of the new sighting and onward.

The *gemara* then cites a *baraita* to push back on *Rami*'s interpretation. A *zav* — a man who experiences an abnormal penile flow (distinct from semen) — follows a similar but not identical structure of *tumah* to the *zavah*. Whereas a *zavah* is defined by three consecutive days of bleeding, the *zav* is defined by the number of flows. If he has two flows he is a *zav*, and must count seven clean days before bringing his *korbanot* and ending his *tumah*.

The *baraita* describes a case in which a *zav* who has had two flows reaches day seven, and they bring his *korban pesach* on that day. He then has another flow just before nightfall. Similarly, a *shomeret yom kineged yom* has her *korban pesach* brought on her second day, and she then bleeds again that afternoon. The *baraita* states that retroactively they are *tamei* with *tumat mishkav u'moshav* — the unique *tumah* which a *zav* or *zavah* transmit to that which they sit or lie upon, which carries an especially severe degree of *tumah* as an *av hatumah*. Retroactively, anything they had sat upon during that period is rendered *tamei*. Yet despite this retroactive *tumah*, the *baraita* states they are exempt from *Pesach Sheni*. The *korban* was good.

If *Rabbi Yose*'s position is that there is no retroactive *tumah* at all and the second day's bleeding is an entirely new onset, then why does the *baraita* explicitly invoke the language of *lemafrea* — retroactively? The *baraita* seems to say there *was* retroactive *tumah*, and yet the *korban pesach* was valid. This suggests the *korban* was saved not by the absence of any *tumah* but by the *tzitz* working for an unknowable *tumah*.

## Retroactive Tumah Debate

### Retroactivity Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא סָבַר מְטַמֵּא לְמַפְרֵעַ מִדְּרַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אוֹמֵר: אֲבָל זָב שֶׁרָאָה בַּשְּׁבִיעִי שֶׁלּוֹ — סוֹתֵר אֶת שֶׁלְּפָנָיו. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא יִסְתּוֹר אֶלָּא יוֹמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that **even Rabbi Oshaya holds** that, according to Rabbi Yosei, a *zav* or *zava* **renders** objects designed for sitting or lying **impure retroactively** only by **rabbinic** decree in these circumstances. **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Oshaya says: But** with regard to **a *zav* who saw** a discharge **on his seventh** day, **it cancels** the clean days **that preceded it. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: It should cancel only its day,** i.e., the day on which he experienced the discharge, and he should require only one additional clean day.

The *gemara* presses further. The language of the *baraita* — *metamei mishkav u'moshav lemafrea*, that one renders *mishkav* and *moshav* retroactively *tamei* — seems to directly contradict *Rami*'s interpretation that there is simply no retroactive *tumah* according to *Rabbi Yose*. If it is truly a new *tumah* from the moment of the renewed bleeding, why does the *baraita* speak in terms of retroactivity at all?

### Rav Oshaya's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי קָסָבַר לְמַפְרֵעַ הוּא מְטַמֵּא — אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ נִסְתּוֹר. אִי קָסָבַר מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא מְטַמֵּא — יוֹמוֹ נָמֵי לָא נִסְתּוֹר! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: לֹא יִסְתּוֹר וְלֹא יוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **Whichever way you** look at Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, it is difficult: **If he holds** that that the *zav* **renders** objects **impure retroactively,** the new discharge **should cancel all** the days. Conversely, **if he holds** that the *zav* **renders** objects **impure from now and onward, it should not cancel even its own day.** Since part of the seventh day was clean, the *zav* is considered to have successfully completed his seven clean days, and the new discharge renders him impure for only one day. **Rather, say instead** that Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows: **It should not cancel even its own day.**

The *gemara* notes that *Rav Oshaya* also grapples with this question in what is cited as a *baraita*, though the attribution is textually problematic: *Rav Oshaya* is the known editor of *baraitot*, but he himself is never quoted within a *baraita*, and *Rabbi Yochanan*, an *amora*, appears in this same passage responding to him. The *rishonim* accordingly emend the text from *detanya* to *amar Rav Oshaya*. *Rav Oshaya* states that a *zav* who has a flow on his seventh day thereby causes that flow to *soter shelifanav* — to retroactively undermine the preceding period of *taharah*, treating it as continuous *tumah*.

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds to him: why should it undermine everything? Should it not cost only the day on which the flow occurred? The *gemara* immediately rejects the simple reading of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s response. By the rule of *mi-mah nafshach* — whichever way you turn it — the position is incoherent: if a flow on day seven creates retroactive *tumah*, then all the preceding days are undermined, since the seven days were never truly clean; and if the flow constitutes new *tumah* from this point forward only, then even day seven itself is not lost, since the new *tumah* simply begins now.

### Rabbi Yoshia's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי קָאֵי כְּווֹתָךְ. וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מְטַמְּאִין מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב לְמַפְרֵעַ! אֶלָּא לָאו, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מְטַמֵּא לְמַפְרֵעַ מִדְּרַבָּנַן. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Oshaya **said to him:** I do not agree with you, but know that **Rabbi Yosei,** who exempts one in this circumstance from the second *Pesaḥ*, **holds in accordance with your** opinion. The Gemara analyses Rabbi Oshaya's statement: **But didn't Rabbi Yosei say** that **they render** objects designed for **lying and sitting impure retroactively,** whereas Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that they render these items impure only from the time of the new discharge and onward? **Rather,** must one **not conclude from this that** when Rabbi Yosei said **he renders** these items **impure retroactively,** he meant that this ruling is **due to rabbinic** decree and is not Torah law? The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from this.**

The *gemara* therefore reinterprets *Rabbi Yochanan*: he must have said that the flow on day seven should not cause the loss of even one day, since it is a new *tumah* with no retroactive effect. *Rabbi Yoshia* then responds to *Rabbi Yochanan* that *Rabbi Yose* agrees with him — that a flow on the final day, after the day began in a state of *taharah*, constitutes a new *tumah* and does not retroactively undermine the preceding period.

The *gemara* now objects: but we have the *baraita* that says *Rabbi Yose* rules the person is *tamei mishkav u'moshav lemafrea*! If *Rabbi Yose* holds there is no retroactive *tumah*, how can the *baraita* use that language? The *gemara* resolves this: *shma mina metamei lemafrea miderabbannan* — the retroactive *tumah* stated in the *baraita* according to *Rabbi Yose* is only rabbinic. Biblically, the person was *tahor* during the interval between the *mikveh* immersion and the renewed flow. The retroactive *tumah* of *mishkav u'moshav* is a rabbinic stringency applied after the fact, but the biblical status during that period was *tahor*, and therefore the *korban pesach* was valid without any need for the *tzitz*.

### Miktzat HaYom Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר מִכָּאן וּלְהַבָּא הוּא מְטַמֵּא [לְמֵת] בִּלְבַד, לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its discussion of whether the leniency of impurity of the deep applies to priests and Rava's attempt to prove that it does, based upon Rabbi Ḥiyya's statement that impurity of the deep is permitted only with regard to ritual impurity imparted by a corpse. The Gemara asks: **According to Rabbi Yosei, now that he said** that a *zav* who immersed and then saw an additional discharge **renders** items **impure** only **from now and onward** according to Torah law, and not retroactively, Rabbi Ḥiyya's statement that the leniency of impurity of the deep applies **only to** ritual impurity imparted by **a corpse** was stated **to exclude what** other case? Rabbi Ḥiyya could not have been excluding impurity due to the discharge of a *zav* or *zava*, as suggested above, because their offerings are valid even without the frontplate placating God.

Central to this entire discussion is the principle of *miktzat hayom k'kulo* — that part of a day counts as the whole day. This principle, familiar from the laws of *aveilut* where a mourner may rise from *shiva* at the start of the seventh day, applies here as well. When the woman immerses on Monday morning, the beginning of Monday counts as a full day of *taharah*, even if she subsequently bleeds later that day. This is the mechanism by which *Rabbi Yose* can say the day began in *taharah* — *miktzat hayom k'kulo* — and therefore the renewed bleeding is a new onset rather than a continuation.

However, this logic only applies when the day in fact began with *taharah*. The *gemara* then asks: according to *Rabbi Yose*, how can one ever arrive at a *zavah gedolah* who bleeds three consecutive days? If each day's renewed bleeding is simply a new onset, three consecutive days of *tumah* would never coalesce into the severe status of *zavah gedolah*. The *gemara* gives two answers. First, *bishofach* — she bled continuously for three days without interruption. Second, a more technically precise scenario: she bled during the *bein hashmashos* — the twilight period at the end of Sunday and again at the end of Monday, such that both days ended in *tumah* and Tuesday began in *tumah*. In this case, there was never any *miktzat hayom* of *taharah* at the start of any day to break the continuity, and therefore the three days coalesce into a single continuous *tumah* of a *zavah gedolah*.

This last scenario leads to an observation by *Rashi* and *Tosafot* about which twilight is the operative one for *miktzat hayom* — the beginning of the night or the beginning of the morning — a question that connects to broader discussions in other areas of *halacha*.

## Tzitz Timing & Aware (II)

### After Zerika Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנּוֹדַע לוֹ לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה, דְּכִי אִזְדְּרִיק דָּם — שַׁפִּיר אִיזְדְּרִיק, אֲבָל נוֹדַע לוֹ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה — לֹא מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi said: They taught** that the frontplate appeases God for impurity of the deep with regard to a nazirite and one bringing the Paschal lamb **only when** the fact that he is impure **became known to him after** the **sprinkling** of the blood of the offering, **as when the blood was sprinkled, it was sprinkled well,** because the impurity of the one bringing the offering was unknown. **However,** if his impurity **was known to him before the sprinkling** of the blood, the frontplate **does not appease** God.

The *gemara* challenges this from the *baraita* concerning *hamotzei meis mushkav l'rochbah shel derech* — a case where a dead body was found lying across the entire width of a path, making it impossible for the traveler to have avoided passing over it. The *baraita* states that for purposes of *trumah*, the person is *tamei*, but *l'nazir v'oseh pesach tahor* — for a *nazir* and one who brought the *korban pesach*, the person is *tahor*. The *baraita* uses the language of *tahor* — a language that implies one may act upon this status *lechatchila*, going forward. This seems to suggest that even before the *zerikah*, upon discovering the *meit* after walking over the path, the person could proceed to bring the *korban pesach* in the first instance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹצֵא מֵת מוּשְׁכָּב לְרׇחְבּוֹ שֶׁל דֶּרֶךְ, לִתְרוּמָה — טָמֵא. לְנָזִיר וְעוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח — טָהוֹר. וְכׇל טָמֵא וְטָהוֹר — לְהַבָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on the following *baraita*: In the case of **one who finds a corpse lying** across **the width of a road,** meaning that it had been buried there in such a way that it was impossible that the passerby could have avoided becoming impure by touching, moving, or passing over the corpse, then **with regard to *teruma*,** the passerby **is impure.** Therefore, if he is a priest, he may not eat *teruma*. However, **with regard to** both **a nazirite and one performing** the ritual of **the Paschal lamb,** the passerby **is pure** because the frontplate appeases God for impurity of the deep in these cases. **And any** time it says: **Impure, and: Pure, it is for the future.** These terms indicate a halakhic ruling that may be followed *ab initio* and not just as a leniency after the fact, if the blood of the offering was already sprinkled.

The *gemara* therefore reformulates *Mar bar Rav Ashi*'s statement. Rather than a strict limit to post-*zerikah* awareness, the corrected ruling is: do not say that the *tzitz* works only when awareness came after the *zerikah* and not before; rather, even if awareness came before the *zerikah*, the *tzitz* works.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא תֵּימָא נוֹדַע לוֹ לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה הוּא דִּמְרַצֶּה, אֲבָל נוֹדַע לוֹ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה — לֹא מְרַצֶּה, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ נוֹדַע לוֹ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה — מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if it was stated, it was stated as follows: Mar bar Rav Ashi said: Do not say** that it is only **when it became known to him after** the **sprinkling** of the blood **that** the frontplate **appeases** God, **but if it became known to him before** the **sprinkling it does not appease** God. **Rather, even if it became known to him before** the **sprinkling,** the frontplate appeases God because impurity of the deep is insignificant with regard to both the Paschal lamb and a nazirite.

*Rashi* and *Tosafot*, however, draw back from the radical implications of this formulation. Based on other evidence including a *Tosefta*, they limit the ruling to the narrow window between *shechita* and *zerikah*. If awareness comes before the *shechita*, the *korban* cannot be brought. But once the *shechita* has been performed and the *avodah* has already commenced under conditions of *tumat hatehom*, the *kohen* may proceed to the *zerikah* and complete the offering. This is because the *avodah* began properly, in ignorance of an unknowable *tumah*, and may therefore be completed.

Even this limited position is striking: the *kohen* now knows the person is *tamei*, and yet *Rashi* and *Tosafot* say he may proceed with the *zerikah*. The language of *tahor* in the *baraita* is thus the language of *lechatchila* — it describes a permission to act, though the person is not truly *tahor* in fact. The *ritzuy* of the *tzitz* does not remove the *tumah*; it renders the *korban* acceptable. The owner may not eat the *korban pesach*, and must subsequently immerse and follow the normal process of *taharah*. The *ritzuy* means only that he has fulfilled his *korban pesach* obligation.

## Tumat HaTehom & Kohen

### Rav Yosef's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: כֹּהֵן הַמְרַצֶּה בְּתָמִיד, הוּתְּרָה לוֹ טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם אוֹ לָא? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר כֹּהֵן הַמְרַצֶּה בְּקׇרְבְּנוֹתֵיהֶן (שֶׁל נָזִיר וְעוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח) הוּתְּרָה לוֹ טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם, כֹּהֵן הַמְרַצֶּה בְּתָמִיד מַאי? מִי אָמְרִינַן כִּי גְּמִירִי טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם — בְּפֶסַח, בְּתָמִיד לָא גְּמִירִי. אוֹ דִילְמָא יָלֵיף תָּמִיד מִפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised a dilemma:** With regard to **the priest who facilitates acceptance of the daily offering, is ritual impurity of the deep permitted for him or not?** The two sides of the dilemma are as follows: **If you say** that **the priest who facilitates acceptance of the offerings of the nazirite and** of **one who performs** the ritual of **the Paschal lamb, ritual impurity of the deep is permitted for him,** then in the case of **the priest who facilitates acceptance of the daily offering, what** is the *halakha*? **Do we say** that **when we learned** the *halakha* of **impurity of the deep** through oral tradition, it was **with regard to the Paschal lamb,** but **with regard to the daily offering we did not learn** it? **Or perhaps** we can **derive** that the *halakha* applies to **the daily offering from** the fact that it applies to the **Paschal lamb?**

With this background established, the *gemara* returns to the unresolved question of whether *tumat hatehom* applies only to the *be'alim* — the owners — or also to the *kohen* performing the *avodah*. *Rav Yosef* poses a further question: if the *tzitz* does work for the *kohen* bringing the *korban pesach* or the *korban* of the *nazir*, does this principle extend to a *kohen* performing the *avodah* of other *korbanot* as well — for instance, the *tamid*, the daily offering?

### Rava's Kal VaChomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא הוּתְּרָה לוֹ טוּמְאָה יְדוּעָה — הוּתְּרָה לוֹ טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם, מָקוֹם שֶׁהוּתְּרָה לוֹ טוּמְאָה יְדוּעָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהוּתְּרָה לוֹ טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** This dilemma can be resolved through an ***a fortiori* inference: Just as in a place where known ritual impurity is not permitted for him,** e.g., with regard to the Paschal lamb, for which known impurity disqualifies the offering and one would have to observe the second *Pesaḥ*, nonetheless **impurity of the deep is permitted for him,** all the more so **in a place** where **known ritual impurity is permitted for him,** with regard to communal offerings, as such offerings may be sacrificed even in a known state of impurity if there is no way to sacrifice the offering in a state of purity, **is it not right that impurity of the deep is permitted for him** completely, even if there are other priests who are ritually pure?

*Rava* proposes a *kal v'chomer*: if *tumat hatehom* is permitted in a context where ordinary known *tumah* is not — namely, the *korban pesach* and *korban nazir*, where even an aware owner who is *tamei* cannot bring the offering — then certainly in a context where known *tumah* is permitted — such as the *tamid*, which may be brought even when all available *kohanim* are *tamei* — *tumat hatehom* should likewise be permitted. The logic seems compelling: the case where *tumah* is more stringently treated already accommodates *tumat hatehom*; the case where *tumah* is more leniently treated should do so *a fortiori*.

### Halacha Lemoshe Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: וּמִי דָּיְינִינַן קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵהֲלָכָה? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: עֲקִיבָא, עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה הֲלָכָה, רְבִיעִית דָּם קַל וְחוֹמֶר, וְאֵין דָּנִין קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵהֲלָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Say** in refutation of this proof: **And do we derive an *a fortiori* inference from a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, such as the *halakha* of ritual impurity of the deep? **Wasn't** the following **taught** in a *baraita*? Rabbi Akiva employed an *a fortiori* inference to derive that a nazirite must shave if he touches or carries blood, just as he must shave if he touches or carries a bone from a corpse, and **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: Akiva, a bone the size of** a grain of **barley is** able to transmit ritual impurity due to **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, and you want to derive that the same is true of **a quarter**-*log* **of blood** on the basis of an ***a fortiori* inference, and one may not derive an *a fortiori* inference from a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai.

The *gemara* rejects this reasoning with a fundamental principle: *u'mi dayninan kal v'chomer me-halacha* — one cannot apply a *kal v'chomer* to a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. The very character of a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* is that it is an arbitrary received tradition, not derived through logic. Since *tumat hatehom* and the *ritzuy* of the *tzitz* are understood to be *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, one cannot use logical argumentation to extend their scope.

A proof for this principle is adduced from a debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Akiva*. The *halacha* that a *nazir* must shave and restart his *nezirut* upon contracting *tumah* from a bone the size of a barley grain is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. *Rabbi Akiva* attempted to extend this by a *kal v'chomer* to a *revi'it* of blood from a corpse — which causes *tumah b'ohel* while the small bone does not — arguing that if the bone triggers the obligation to shave, the blood certainly should. *Rabbi Eliezer* responded: *etzem kese'orah halacha* — the rule concerning the bone is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — *v'ein danin kal v'chomer mehalacha* — and one does not derive extensions of a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* through *kal v'chomer*.

Nonetheless, *Rava* then proposes an alternative: although *kal v'chomer* cannot be applied to an arbitrary *halacha*, a *gezeirah shavah* can be used to connect arbitrary *halachot* to one another. The word *b'moado* — "in its appointed time" — appears in the *pasuk* of the *pesach* and similarly in the *pasuk* of the *tamid*. A *gezeirah shavah* from *pesach* to *tamid* could in principle extend the *halacha* of *tumat hatehom* to the *tamid*. The logic here is that one arbitrary mechanism (*gezeirah shavah*) may legitimately be used to extend another arbitrary tradition, while a logical argument (*kal v'chomer*) may not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: יָלֵיף ״מוֹעֲדוֹ״ ״מוֹעֲדוֹ״ מִפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: It is derived** on the basis of a verbal analogy of the expressions **"its appointed time"** (Numbers 9:13) stated with regard to the Paschal lamb, and **"its appointed time"** (Numbers 28:2) stated with regard to the daily offering. Since the Torah uses this expression in both cases, the *halakha* with regard to the daily offering can be derived **from** the *halakha* in the case of **the Paschal lamb:** Just as impurity of the deep is permitted with regard to the Paschal lamb, it is permitted with regard to the daily offering.

## Source of Tumat HaTehom

### Rabbi Eliezer's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם גּוּפָא הֵיכָא כְּתִיבָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִי יָמוּת מֵת עָלָיו״ — בִּמְחֻוֶּורֶת עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **impurity of the deep itself,** which is permitted in the cases of a nazirite and one who sacrifices the Paschal lamb, due to the fact that the frontplate appeases God for the impurity, **where is it written? Rabbi Elazar said** that **the verse states** with regard to a nazirite: **"And if any man shall die** very suddenly **beside him"** (Numbers 6:9). The emphasis provided by the expression "beside him" indicates that it is **clear to him** that he has become impure. However, one is not impure if the presence of the corpse is unknown.

Having established that *tumat hatehom* operates as a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, the *gemara* now inquires whether it has any basis in scripture. *Rabbi Eliezer* cites the *pasuk* regarding the *nazir*: *v'chi yamus meis alav* — if a dead body dies upon him, implying that the *tumah* must be directly and clearly upon him, *b'michuveres elav*, in something that was connected to and known by him. If the *tumah* was unknown to him, it is as if it was not "upon him" in the relevant sense, and he need not restart his *nezirut*. This establishes the principle by the *nazir*, but leaves open the source for the *korban pesach*.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן נָזִיר. עוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״בְּדֶרֶךְ רְחוֹקָה לָכֶם״ — בִּמְחֻוֶּורֶת לָכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for impurity of the deep with regard to **a nazirite; from where do we** derive that impurity of the deep is also permitted with regard to **one who performs** the ritual of the **Paschal lamb? Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **the verse states:** "Any man **of you [*lakhem*]** who shall be ritually impure due to a corpse or **on a road far away"** (Numbers 9:10). The term *lakhem* is interpreted as indicating that the impurity must be **clear to you [*lakhem*].** However, any ritual impurity that is not clearly identified does not render one who wishes to sacrifice the Paschal lamb impure.

For the *korban pesach*, *Rabbi Yochanan* cites the word *lachem* — "to you" — that appears in the *pasuk* dealing with *pesach*: *o baderech rechokah lachem*. The *tumah* must be *michuveres lachem*, something connected and known to you. If it was not known, it falls outside the scope of the prohibition.

### Reish Lakish's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כְּדֶרֶךְ. מָה דֶּרֶךְ — בְּגָלוּי, אַף טוּמְאָה נָמֵי — בְּגָלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said:** This *halakha* may be derived in a different way, from the word "road," which is juxtaposed in the verse to the phrase "ritually impure." This indicates that the impurity is **like a road. Just as a road is in the open, so too, the impurity is in the open.**

*Reish Lakish* derives the principle differently: *k'derech* — like a road or path, which is *galuy*, open and exposed to all. Just as a public road is known to everyone, so too *tumah* must be of the known, public variety. If it is not generally visible and knowable, it is not in the relevant category.

### Scriptural Source Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵי זֶהוּ טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם? כׇּל שֶׁלֹּא הִכִּיר בָּהּ אֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם. הִכִּיר בָּהּ אֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם — אֵין זֶה טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם. לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: בִּמְחֻוֶּורֶת עָלָיו — עַד דְּיָדַע הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** to these derivations from that which was taught in the following *baraita*: **Which is the impurity of the deep** that was permitted for both a nazirite and one who sacrifices the Paschal lamb? It is impurity imparted by **any** corpse **of which no one is aware,** even **at the end of the earth;** but if even **one** person **is aware of it,** even **at the end of the earth, this is not impurity of the deep.** However, **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who said** that the expression "beside him" indicates that the impurity must be **clear to him,** it would be considered impurity of the deep **until he knew** about it; it would not be enough for some other person to be aware of the corpse.

The *gemara* objects to all three scriptural derivations. The *mishnah* and *baraita* define *tumat hatehom* with extreme precision: the *tumah* was unknown to even *one person* in the entire world. This is an extraordinarily narrow category. But the scriptural derivations would yield a much broader category. According to *Rabbi Eliezer*'s derivation, the category would be anything unknown to the person himself. According to *Rabbi Yochanan*'s derivation from *lachem* — the plural form, "to you" — it would require that the *tumah* be unknown to at least two people. According to *Reish Lakish*'s derivation from the analogy to a path, it would need to be unknown to the general public. None of these yields the precise *halacha* that even one person's knowledge anywhere in the world removes the classification of *tumat hatehom*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: ״לָכֶם״, בִּמְחֻוֶּורֶת לָכֶם — עַד דְּיָדְעִי בַּהּ תְּרֵין. לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר: כְּדֶרֶךְ — עַד דְּיָדְעִי כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **According to Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** that the *halakha* is derived from the term ***lakhem,** which teaches that it must be **clear to you,** it is considered impurity of the deep **until two** people **know about it,** as the word *lakhem* is plural. **According to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, who said** that the impurity must be **like a road,** it is impurity of the deep **until the entire world knows** about it.

### Halacha Lemoshe Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם הִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, וּקְרָא — אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one must conclude that the previous sources cited are insufficient and say that **they learned** the principle of **impurity of the deep as a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and the verse** that the *amora'im* quoted is **a mere support** for the *halakha* and not its actual source.

The conclusion is therefore clear: *tumas hatehom hilchasa gemiri lah* — *tumat hatehom* is a received tradition, a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. *U'kra asmachta b'alma* — the scriptural verses are mere supports (*asmachta*), not the true source of the law. This conclusion retroactively explains the discussion earlier in the *sugya*: when *Rava* was asked to apply a *kal v'chomer* from *pesach* to *tamid*, the response that *tumat hatehom* is *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* was precisely the conclusion arrived at here. *Rashi* notes that the discussion we have just read was chronologically prior and that the *gemara* above presupposed its outcome.

## Tumat HaTehom Scenarios

### Body Across Road

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. הַמּוֹצֵא מֵת מוּשְׁכָּב לְרׇחְבּוֹ שֶׁל דֶּרֶךְ, לִתְרוּמָה — טָמֵא, לְנָזִיר וְעוֹשֵׂה פֶסַח — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara quoted a *baraita* with regard to impurity of the deep, it returns to discuss **the matter itself.** In the case of **one who finds a corpse lying** across **the width of the road, with regard to *teruma* he is impure,** and **with regard to a nazirite and one who performs** the ritual of the **Paschal lamb he is pure.**

The *mishna* describes in detail the specific scenarios that constitute *tumat hatehom*. The first case is *hamotzei meis mushkav l'rochbah shel derech* — finding a dead body lying across the entire width of a road. As analyzed above, this renders the person *tamei* for *trumah*, but *tahor* for the *nazir* and the one who brought the *korban pesach*, provided the *tumah* was unknowable at the time.

### Broken Body vs. Grave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מָקוֹם לַעֲבוֹר. אֲבָל יֵשׁ מָקוֹם לַעֲבוֹר — אַף לִתְרוּמָה טָהוֹר. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ שָׁלֵם. אֲבָל מְשׁוּבָּר וּמְפוֹרָק טָהוֹר, שֶׁמָּא בֵּין הַפְּרָקִים עָבַר. וּבַקֶּבֶר, אֲפִילּוּ מְשׁוּבָּר וּמְפוֹרָק טָמֵא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַקֶּבֶר מְצָרְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **in what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where one finds** the body **whole. However,** if it is **broken** or **dismembered, he is pure.** One can say that **perhaps he passed between the parts** of the corpse and did not touch or pass over any of them. However, this applies only when he finds the corpse out in the open; but if he finds it **in a grave, even** if it is **broken** or **dismembered, he is impure, because the grave joins** it into one unit and renders one impure if one passed over any part of the grave, even if he did not pass over part of the corpse.

The *mishna* introduces two further refinements. If the body was *meshubar m'furak* — broken into pieces — the person is *tahor*, since it is possible to have walked through the gaps without contact or *ohel*. However, if the body was in a *kever*, even if broken into pieces, the person is *tamei*, because *kever m'tzarpo* — the grave consolidates the pieces such that the entire burial structure transmits *tumah*, and one cannot argue that the gaps between the pieces provided a safe corridor.

### Manner of Travel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּמְהַלֵּךְ בְּרַגְלָיו, אֲבָל טָעוּן אוֹ רָכוּב — טָמֵא, לְפִי שֶׁמְּהַלֵּךְ בְּרַגְלָיו אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁלֹּא יִגַּע וְלֹא יַאֲהִיל, אֲבָל טָעוּן אוֹ רָכוּב אִי אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁלֹּא יִגַּע וְלֹא יַאֲהִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement,** that if the corpse was dismembered the passerby is pure, **said?** It is said **with regard to** a passerby who **travels by foot, but** if he was **loaded** with a heavy burden **or riding** an animal, **he is impure.** The reason for this is **because one who travels by foot, it is possible that he will not touch** the corpse **and will not pass over** it; **but one who is loaded** with a heavy burden and therefore does not walk in a straight line **or someone riding** an animal, **it is impossible that he will not touch and will not pass over** the corpse.

The *mishna* further distinguishes based on how the person was traveling. One who walked on foot and traversed a path over which a broken body lay may potentially have passed through the gaps and is therefore *tahor*. One who was carrying a load (*ta'un*) or riding an animal (*rachuv*) is *tamei*, because in those modes of travel it is impossible to have avoided contact or *ohel* over the body.

### Definition of Unknown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּטוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאָה יְדוּעָה — טָמֵא: וְאֵי זֶה הִיא טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם — כׇּל שֶׁלֹּא הִכִּיר בָּהּ אֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם. אֲבָל הִכִּיר בָּהּ אֶחָד בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם — אֵין זֶה טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement,** that a nazirite and one bringing a Paschal lamb are considered pure, **said?** It is said **with regard to impurity of the deep. However,** if the source of **impurity** was **known** to some but not to the individual who became impure, **he is** nevertheless **impure.** The *baraita* continues: **And which** corpse is considered **impurity of the deep? Any** corpse **of which no one is aware,** even **at the end of the earth; but** if even **one** individual **is aware of it,** even if that individual is **at the end of the earth, this is not** considered **impurity of the deep.**

The *mishna* then provides the critical definitional statement: *eizo hi tumat hatehom* — what constitutes a *tumah* of the depths? *Kol shelo hekirah echad b'sof ha'olam* — any *tumah* that not one person in the entire world recognized or was aware of at the time. This is the operative definition: if even a single person, anywhere in the world, knew that the *tumah* was present at the time the person came into contact with it, the case does not qualify as *tumat hatehom*, and the *tzitz* does not apply.

### Exceptions to Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצָאוֹ טָמוּן בְּתֶבֶן, בְּעָפָר, וּבִצְרוֹרוֹת — הֲרֵי זֶה טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם. בְּמַיִם, בַּאֲפֵילָה, בִּנְקִיקֵי הַסְּלָעִים — אֵין זֶה טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם. וְלֹא אָמְרוּ טוּמְאַת הַתְּהוֹם אֶלָּא לְמֵת בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** In order to ascertain whether anyone ever knew about the corpse, its condition is taken into account. If **one finds it hidden in hay** or **in dirt** or **in pebbles,** and it is possible that the person died in an avalanche, in which case it is likely that the corpse had never been found, **this is impurity of the deep.** But if he finds it **in water,** or **in a dark** place, or **in the clefts of the rocks, this is not impurity of the deep.** Although these are places where people do not often go, with the passage of time the corpse is likely to be discovered, and it is quite possible that someone already passed by and saw it. **And they said** the leniency of **impurity of the deep only with regard to a corpse,** but not with regard to other sources of ritual impurity.

The *mishna* also addresses cases where a body was concealed not by being buried beneath the earth, but in other ways. If the body was *tamun b'teven, b'afar, u'v'tzrorot* — hidden in straw, loose soil, or stones — that still qualifies as *tumat hatehom*, assuming no one was aware of it. By contrast, if the body was in water, in darkness, or in a niche within a cave (*nikikei hasla'im*), that does not qualify as *tumat hatehom*. *Tosafot* explains that in those scenarios, someone presumably does know about it. Alternatively, even if no one specifically knows, those situations are *galuy* in character — with minimal effort the body could be seen or found — and therefore lack the quality of genuine inaccessibility that defines *tumat hatehom*.

### Tumat HaTehom Scope

Finally, the *mishna* states the limiting principle that opened this entire discussion: *v'lo amru ela tumat hatehom ela l'meit bilvad* — the sages said *tumat hatehom* only with respect to a corpse, and not with respect to other sources of *tumah* such as a *sheretz*. This returns us to the starting point of the *sugya*. The *gemara* had asked what case required this exclusion — if *tumat hatehom* works for the *be'alim*, then excluding *sheretz* makes sense because a person who is *tamei sheretz* on the fourteenth of *Nisan* could bring the *korban pesach* in any case (according to some opinions), or if not, the *tzitz* still would not help him even for corpse *tumah* in a way that needs demonstration. The *gemara*'s exploration of whether *tumat hatehom* applies to the *kohen* remained unresolved by direct proof, but the scope of *tumat hatehom* is in any case bounded: it is a *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* applying to corpse *tumah* alone.
