# Sanhedrin 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kareit in Avodah Zarah

### Three Kritot Intro

The daf opens on 64b with a statement by Rabbi Yossi bar Abin Chanina: *shalosh kritot b'avodah zarah* — there are three separate instances of *kareit* stated in the Torah in connection with *avodah zarah*. The immediate question is: why does the Torah find it necessary to write *kareit* three times in this context? What work does each one do?

### K'Darkah vs Lo K'Darkah

The Gemara answers that the three serve distinct purposes. One *kareit* addresses worshipping *avodah zarah* *k'darkah* — in the manner that that particular idol is normally worshipped, such as throwing stones at the idol known as *Markolis*. A second addresses worship *lo k'darkah* — in a manner that departs from the idol's usual mode of worship. The third addresses *le-Molech* — the act of passing one's child through the fire for the sake of Molech.

It should be noted that two of the three verses referenced here relate specifically to Molech. The Gemara's point is that one of those two Molech-related verses is pressed into service to derive the general rule of *lo k'darkah*, while the other addresses Molech directly.

### Molech as Avodah Zarah

The Gemara then turns to a specific dispute: what is the view of the one who holds that Molech is simply a subspecies of *avodah zarah*? For that opinion, the *kareit* assigned to Molech would seemingly be redundant — worship of *avodah zarah* already carries *kareit*, whether *k'darkah* or *lo k'darkah*. What, then, does this third *kareit* add?

The answer given is that it teaches the case of *he-avir beno le-Markolis* — one who passes his child through for Markolis rather than for Molech. This constitutes a *lo k'darkah* form of the Molech ritual applied to a different idol, and the separate *kareit* establishes this as a distinct *chiyuv*. The Mishnah elsewhere, it should be noted, does not enumerate this case among those liable to *skilah*, since the liability in question is *kareit* rather than *mitat beit din*; the four modes of execution in the Mishnah require a full *maaseh*, whereas *kareit* can obtain independently.

### Megadef & Two Kareits

The Gemara then presents a parallel question for the one who holds that *megadef* — blasphemy — is itself a form of *avodah zarah*. For that opinion, why is there a *kareit* specifically for *megadef*? The Gemara answers by citing a *braita*: *hikaret tikaret* — the doubled language implies two instances of *kareit*, one in *olam hazeh* and one in *olam haba*. That is, the verse about *megadef* teaches that one who blasphemes is cut off in both this world and the world to come.

### Rabbi Akiva Response

Rabbi Yishmael objects: we already know from another verse, *ve-nikhreta ha-nefesh ha-hi*, that *avodah zarah* carries *kareit* — including both *olam hazeh* and *olam haba*. Does the existence of a third verse imply a third world? Rabbi Akiva responds that this is simply a case of *dibra Torah kile-shon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of human beings, using emphatic repetition for rhetorical force. The doubled language of *hikaret tikaret* does not introduce new legal content; it is ordinary stylistic emphasis, and we already derive the two-world *kareit* from the earlier verse.

## Ba'al Ov & Yidoni

### Ba'al Ov Defined

The Gemara moves to the beginning of the Mishnah on 65a, which introduces two additional categories of those liable to *skilah*: the *ba'al ov* and the *yidoni*. The Mishnah defines the *ba'al ov* as *zeh ha-pitom ha-medaber mi-shichyo* — this is the *pitom*, a particular type of practitioner, who speaks from his armpit. The *yidoni* is defined as *zeh ha-medaber be-piv* — one who speaks from his mouth. Both carry the death penalty of *skilah*. The Mishnah adds that one who consults either of these practitioners — who seeks them out to learn from them — violates a *lav*, an *azharah*, and may be liable to bring a *korban*.

### Omission Query

The Gemara raises a comparison between our Mishnah and the Mishnah in Masechet Kritot. Our Mishnah lists both *ba'al ov* and *yidoni*. The Mishnah in Kritot — which enumerates all the Torah transgressions that carry *kareit* and for which one who acts inadvertently must bring a *korban chatat* — mentions *ba'al ov* but omits *yidoni* altogether. The question is: why the asymmetry? Both seem structurally comparable, and yet Kritot singles out only one.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan explains that both *ba'al ov* and *yidoni* are in fact included in the Mishnah in Kritot — they are stated together in a single *lav* in the Torah, and therefore teaching one is sufficient to encompass the other. The Mishnah in Kritot mentions *ba'al ov* alone, but *yidoni* is implicitly covered.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish disagrees. In his view, *yidoni* is omitted from the Mishnah in Kritot because there is no *maaseh* involved in *yidoni* — no qualifying act. Since *kareit* (and the attendant obligation of a *korban chatat* for inadvertent transgression) requires a *maaseh*, *yidoni* simply does not qualify for inclusion in that Mishnah. The Gemara will elaborate at length on what *maaseh* means in this context.

### First Mentioned

The Gemara then asks: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who says both are subsumed under a single *lav* and are therefore jointly included in the Mishnah in Kritot — if both are included, why does that Mishnah specify *ba'al ov* rather than *yidoni*? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *mishum de-fatach bei kra* — because the Torah consistently introduces this pair by mentioning *ba'al ov* first, as in *ba'al ov o yidoni* or *ba'al ov ve-yidoni*. The Mishnah simply follows the Torah's own word order and uses the first of the pair as representative.

### Disagreement

The Gemara then asks the reverse: why does Reish Lakish not accept Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning — namely, that both are taught under a single *lav* and therefore both are included? Rav Pappa explains on Reish Lakish's behalf: *chalukin hen be-mitah* — they are separated from one another in the verse concerning the death penalty. The Torah writes *ki yihyeh vahem ov o yidoni*, using the word *o* — "or" — which constitutes a *chalukah*, a separation, between them. Once the Torah separates them in this way, one cannot assume that teaching one implicitly includes the other.

### Chalukah Types

The Gemara now asks the converse: why does Rabbi Yochanan not accept Reish Lakish's argument from the *chalukah* in the verse about the death penalty? Rabbi Yochanan's response is that there are two distinct types of *chalukah*: *chalukah de-lav* — a separation that occurs in the context of a standard negative commandment — which is halachically significant and prevents one term from covering the other; and *chalukah de-mitah* — a separation that occurs in the context of the death penalty — which is not halachically significant in this sense. The word *o* in a verse about a *lo ta'aseh* constitutes a genuine *chalukah*, but the same word appearing in a verse about liability to *mitat beit din* does not carry the same legal weight. It is therefore the *chalukah de-lav* that matters for the Mishnah in Kritot, and since in the verse about the *lav* the two are not separated, Rabbi Yochanan holds that they remain jointly covered.

### According Akiva

The Gemara now turns to explain why Rabbi Yochanan does not accept Reish Lakish's principle that a *maaseh* is required. Rabbi Yochanan's answer is that the Mishnah in Kritot was taught according to the view of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that a *maaseh* is not required in order to incur *kareit*. Since Rabbi Akiva does not require a *maaseh*, there is no reason on that basis to exclude *yidoni* from the list — and yet the Mishnah in Kritot does exclude it, which is why Rabbi Yochanan offers his alternative explanation based on the shared *lav*.

Reish Lakish responds: even conceding that the Mishnah in Kritot follows Rabbi Akiva, one must distinguish between *maaseh rabbah* — a substantial act — and *maaseh zuta* — a minimal act. Rabbi Akiva's position is that a *maaseh rabbah* is not required; but even Rabbi Akiva agrees that *maaseh zuta* is required. And *yidoni* involves no *maaseh* even of the minimal variety, which is why it is omitted.

## What Is Maaseh?

### Megadef Maaseh

This extended discussion of *maaseh* prompts the Gemara to ask: what *maaseh* is involved in *megadef*? Blasphemy is a transgression that carries *kareit*, and yet it appears to involve nothing more than speech. The Gemara's answer is *akimat sefatav havei maaseh* — the bending or compression of the lips constitutes a *maaseh*. This is a striking claim: bodily movement of the speech organs, without any further physical effect on the world, is categorized as a qualifying act.

This has implications beyond the immediate context. The question of whether speech constitutes a *maaseh* arises in contemporary discussions of, for example, whether verbally activating a voice-controlled device on *Shabbat* involves a *maaseh*. The sugya here speaks precisely to that question.

It is also worth noting what the Gemara focuses on: the movement of the lips, rather than the production of sound. This will become significant in a later dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish.

### Ba'al Ov Maaseh

The Gemara asks the parallel question: what *maaseh* is involved in *ba'al ov*? The answer given is *hakashat zeroatav havei maaseh* — the striking or waving of the arms is a *maaseh*. Again, a bodily movement that produces no direct physical change to the environment is classified as a qualifying act. The Gemara appears to be working against a background assumption that a *maaseh* must effect something in the external world — and it is precisely that assumption it is pushing back against.

### Braita Examples

A *braita* is then cited: one is not liable unless the act contains a *maaseh* — the *braita* gives examples: slaughtering (*zevichah*), burning incense (*kitor*), pouring a libation (*nisuch*), and bowing (*hishtachavayah*). These represent paradigmatic acts of *avodah zarah*.

Reish Lakish comments that the inclusion of *hishtachavayah* — bowing — in this list indicates that this *braita* must follow the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who does not require a substantial *maaseh*. The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, would not count bowing as a sufficiently significant *maaseh* to trigger liability for *kareit*.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: even according to the *Rabbanan*, *kefifat komato* — the physical bending of one's body — constitutes a *maaseh* substantial enough to incur liability. The *braita* need not be attributed exclusively to Rabbi Akiva.

The Gemara then presses Reish Lakish: if bowing is insufficient for the *Rabbanan*, how can waving the arms (*hakashat zeroatav*) of a *ba'al ov* be sufficient? Bowing involves a more pronounced physical movement than arm-waving, and if the former is inadequate, the latter surely is as well. The Gemara resolves this by concluding that when Reish Lakish said that arm-waving is a *maaseh*, he too was speaking according to the view of Rabbi Akiva — not according to the *Rabbanan*. The *Rabbanan* would not regard arm-waving as a sufficient *maaseh* either.

The Gemara then points out a further difficulty: if the *Rabbanan* reject both bowing and arm-waving as insufficient acts, they should also exclude *ba'al ov* from the Mishnah in Kritot — and not merely exclude *megadef*. We know there is a *braita* in Kritot where the *Rabbanan* disagree with Rabbi Akiva and exclude *megadef* from *kareit* liability; but given the conclusion just reached, they should have excluded *ba'al ov* as well. Why does the *braita* omit this?

### Ulla Incense

Ulla proposes an answer: the *ba'al ov* included in the Mishnah in Kritot — the one whom even the *Rabbanan* agree is liable for *kareit* — refers not to the arm-waving practitioner, but to one who burns incense to a demon (*mekattar la-shed*). Burning incense is clearly a substantial physical act — a *maaseh gamur* — and even the *Rabbanan* would recognize it as such.

### Rava Objection

Rava objects immediately: one who burns incense to a *shed* is not a *ba'al ov* at all — he is an *oved avodah zarah*, a practitioner of full-fledged idolatry. The act of burning incense to a demon falls under the category of *avodah zarah*, not *ba'al ov*, and therefore cannot serve as the referent for *ba'al ov* in Kritot.

### Lechever

Rava therefore proposes a different identification: the *ba'al ov* in Kritot refers to one who burns incense as part of *chovev chever* — a form of necromantic witchcraft directed at a dead person rather than at an idol. This is not *avodah zarah*, and it does involve a physical act.

### Abaye Types

Abaye qualifies this further, distinguishing between different forms of *chovev chever*. There is a *chever gadol* and a *chever katan* — a major and a minor form of this practice. One who performs the more serious variety is *chayav skilah*; one who performs the lesser form is *chayav malkut* only. Rava accepts this distinction, affirming that the Torah's mention of *chover chaver* indicates *skilah*, while the lesser form carries only lashes.

### Bees & Scorpions

Abaye concludes with a practical ruling: one who conjures bees and scorpions — causing them to attack one another through incantation — falls into the category of *chovev chever* and is *chayav malkut*, even if his intent was not to cause human harm. The only exception is a life-threatening situation. Rashi notes that if it is a matter of *pikuach nefesh*, the conjuring would be permitted. A question lingers, however, since some forms of this practice border on *avodah zarah*, for which *pikuach nefesh* would not ordinarily serve as a justification — the precise categorization of the act would determine the answer.

## Speech vs Silence

### Bowing Dispute

The Gemara returns to the earlier dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan holds that bowing (*kefifat komato*) is a *maaseh* even according to the *Rabbanan*, while Reish Lakish holds it is not.

### Heart Intent

Rava introduces a different consideration. Rabbi Yochanan holds that bowing is a *maaseh* for the *Rabbanan*, but that *akimat sefatav* — the movement of the lips in speech — is *not* a *maaseh* according to the *Rabbanan*. One might ask: are they not structurally identical? Both involve physical movement of the body with no further effect on the environment. Rava explains that *megadef* is different, because the act originates in and is driven by the heart — *ho'il ve-yeshno be-leiv*. It is not merely a mechanical physical movement but an expression of inner intent. That, Rava suggests, is what disqualifies it for the *Rabbanan* — or rather, that is what distinguishes it in some way.

### Eidim Challenge

Rav Zeira challenges this from the case of *eidim zomemin*, conspiring witnesses. *Eidim zomemin* do not receive *kareit* even when their false testimony was aimed at causing another person to receive *kareit*. The principle of *kaasher zamam* — that they receive what they sought to inflict — does not extend to *kareit* for *eidim zomemin*. The stated reason is *she-ein bahem maaseh* — there is no *maaseh* involved. But if Rava's reasoning is correct — that something is considered a *maaseh* when it comes from the heart — why would *eidim zomemin* be exempt? Surely their false testimony came from the heart as well.

### Voice Not Maaseh

Rava responds: *eidim zomemin* are different because *yeshnan be-kol* — their act was performed through their voice. And a voice, in this context, does not constitute a *maaseh*. It is not the element of *leiv*, of inner intent, that drives the analysis; it is the distinction between lip-movement and voice-production that is operative. *Megadef* involves *akimat sefatav* — the physical movement of the lips — while testimony in court operates through the voice.

### Voice Maaseh

This brings the Gemara to the explicit dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish on the general question of whether voice constitutes a *maaseh*. The case used to frame the dispute is *chasamah be-kol ve-hinhigah be-kol* — if one muzzles an animal (violating the prohibition against muzzling an ox while it threshes) by shouting at it, or if one drives a *kilayim* team of mismatched animals by voice alone. Rabbi Yochanan rules liable (*chayav*); Reish Lakish rules exempt (*patur*). This is directly relevant to the contemporary question of whether speaking to a voice-activated device on *Shabbat* constitutes a *maaseh*. Notably, the *Ein Mishpat* indicates that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan.

### Lip Movement

More precisely: Rabbi Yochanan holds that *akimat piv havei maaseh* — moving the lips is a *maaseh* — while Reish Lakish holds the opposite.

### Girsa Variants

Rava, returning to *eidim zomemin*, offers a final formulation of why they are different. The standard text of the Vilna *Shas* reads *ho'il ve-yeshnan be-reiah* — their act was one of *seeing*, meaning their testimony was grounded in what they observed, and observation alone cannot constitute a *maaseh*. However, the *Rif* cites a variant reading in the name of Rav Hai Gaon: *ho'il ve-yeshnan be-ai* — their act was performed through saying "I" (or some similar declarative statement). Even on this reading, a difficulty remains: if speaking is a *maaseh* according to Rabbi Yochanan, why are *eidim zomemin* exempt? Some suggest that there is something distinctive about speech in a formal judicial proceeding — that the context of the court somehow transforms or weights the act differently — but the matter remains a subject of considerable discussion in the commentators.

## Precise Definitions

### Ba'al Ov Defined

A *braita* is cited that gives precise technical definitions of the two practitioners. The *ba'al ov* is one who speaks *bein ha-prakim* — from between the joints — *u-vein atzilei yadav*, and from the armpits. The voice or communication emanates from the cavities of the body.

### Yidoni Defined

The *yidoni* is one who takes the bone of an animal called a *yadua* and places it in his mouth, whereupon *vehu medaber me-elav* — the bone speaks of its own accord. This is something resembling ventriloquism, with the notable difference that it is the bone, not the practitioner's own voice, that produces the speech.

### Tanakh Challenge

The Gemara raises a *meitivei* against the definition of *ba'al ov*. It is unusual that a *meitivei* — a challenge from a more authoritative source — comes here from a verse in *Tanakh* rather than a *tannaic* text, but the logic is the same: a verse is inherently more authoritative than any *baraita*. The verse is *ve-hayah ke-ov me-eretz kolech* — "your voice will be like an *ov* from the earth." The implication seems to be that the voice of the *ba'al ov* is heard directly from the ground, not filtered through the joints or armpits of a human practitioner.

### Spirit Between

The Gemara resolves this: the verse does not mean the voice comes out of the ground directly. Rather, the spirit ascends from the ground and then *salika ve-yatvei bein ha-prakim* — rises and positions itself between the joints, and speaks from there. The voice is ultimately heard from the joints, not from the earth itself.

### Saul's Woman

A second *meitivei* is raised from the episode of Shaul and the woman at Ein Dor: *va-tomer ha-isha el Shaul elohim ra'iti olim min ha-aretz* — "the woman said to Shaul: I have seen divine beings rising from the earth." Again, the text seems to indicate that the communication came directly from the ground.

### Dwelling Place

The Gemara resolves this second challenge in the same way as the first: the spirits rose from the earth but then went and positioned themselves *bein ha-prakim* — between the joints — and it was from there that they made themselves heard. The ground is the origin of the ascent, not the locus of the communication. The definition in the *braita* stands: the *ba'al ov* operates through the joints and armpits, and the apparent contradiction from these biblical passages is resolved by understanding that the spirits merely pass through the earth on their way to their actual dwelling place within the human body of the practitioner.
