# Sanhedrin 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kareit in Avodah Zarah

### Three Kritot Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שָׁלֹשׁ כָּרֵיתוֹת בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָמָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says: Why** is the punishment of **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*]** mentioned **three** times **with regard to idol worship?**

The daf opens on 64b with a statement by Rabbi Yossi bar Abin Chanina: *shalosh kritot b'avodah zarah* — there are three separate instances of *kareit* stated in the Torah in connection with *avodah zarah*. The immediate question is: why does the Torah find it necessary to write *kareit* three times in this context? What work does each one do?

### K'Darkah vs Lo K'Darkah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת לִכְדַרְכָּהּ, וְאַחַת לְשֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְאַחַת לַמּוֹלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **One** mention **is for** worshipping an idol **in its** typical **manner** of worship, **and one** mention **is for** worshipping an idol **not in its** typical **manner** of worship, **and one** mention **is for** performing the ritual of **Molekh.**

The Gemara answers that the three serve distinct purposes. One *kareit* addresses worshipping *avodah zarah* *k'darkah* — in the manner that that particular idol is normally worshipped, such as throwing stones at the idol known as *Markolis*. A second addresses worship *lo k'darkah* — in a manner that departs from the idol's usual mode of worship. The third addresses *le-Molech* — the act of passing one's child through the fire for the sake of Molech.

It should be noted that two of the three verses referenced here relate specifically to Molech. The Gemara's point is that one of those two Molech-related verses is pressed into service to derive the general rule of *lo k'darkah*, while the other addresses Molech directly.

### Molech as Avodah Zarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר מוֹלֶךְ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הִיא, כָּרֵת בְּמוֹלֶךְ לְמָה לִי? לְמַעֲבִיר בְּנוֹ שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that **Molekh is** an object of **idol worship, why do I** need a special mention of ***karet* with regard to Molekh?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary **for** the case of **one who passes his child** through fire to an idol other than Molekh, where this is **not its** typical **manner** of worship.

The Gemara then turns to a specific dispute: what is the view of the one who holds that Molech is simply a subspecies of *avodah zarah*? For that opinion, the *kareit* assigned to Molech would seemingly be redundant — worship of *avodah zarah* already carries *kareit*, whether *k'darkah* or *lo k'darkah*. What, then, does this third *kareit* add?

The answer given is that it teaches the case of *he-avir beno le-Markolis* — one who passes his child through for Markolis rather than for Molech. This constitutes a *lo k'darkah* form of the Molech ritual applied to a different idol, and the separate *kareit* establishes this as a distinct *chiyuv*. The Mishnah elsewhere, it should be noted, does not enumerate this case among those liable to *skilah*, since the liability in question is *kareit* rather than *mitat beit din*; the four modes of execution in the Mishnah require a full *maaseh*, whereas *kareit* can obtain independently.

### Megadef & Two Kareits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר: מְגַדֵּף עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הִיא, כָּרֵת בִּמְגַדֵּף לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that the verse concerning **one who blasphemes is** referring to one who engages in **idol worship** and not to one who curses God, **why do I** need ***karet*** to be mentioned **with regard to one who blasphemes?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״הִכָּרֵת תִּכָּרֵת״. ״הִכָּרֵת״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״תִּכָּרֵת״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that it is necessary **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: It is stated with regard to one who blasphemes: "Because he has despised the word of the Lord, and has breached His mitzva, that soul **shall be excised [*hikkaret tikkaret*],** his iniquity shall be upon him" (Numbers 15:31). The phrase "*hikkaret tikkaret*" is interpreted as follows: **"*Hikkaret*";** the sinner is excised **in this world,** meaning that he will die prematurely. **"*Tikkaret*";** the sinner is excised **in the World-to-Come,** and he will not merit everlasting life. This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The Gemara then presents a parallel question for the one who holds that *megadef* — blasphemy — is itself a form of *avodah zarah*. For that opinion, why is there a *kareit* specifically for *megadef*? The Gemara answers by citing a *braita*: *hikaret tikaret* — the doubled language implies two instances of *kareit*, one in *olam hazeh* and one in *olam haba*. That is, the verse about *megadef* teaches that one who blasphemes is cut off in both this world and the world to come.

### Rabbi Akiva Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״וְנִכְרְתָה״! וְכִי שְׁלֹשָׁה עוֹלָמִים יֵשׁ? אֶלָּא ״וְנִכְרְתָה״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״הִכָּרֵת״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, ״תִּכָּרֵת״ – דִּבְּרָה תוֹרָה כִלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael said to him: But isn't it already stated** in the previous verse: "He blasphemes the Lord; that soul **shall be excised [*venikhreta*]"** (Numbers 15:30)? **Are there three worlds** in which the sinner is excised? **Rather,** from the term in the verse **"*venikhreta*"** it is derived that the sinner is excised **in this world,** from the term **"*hikkaret*"** it is derived that the sinner is excised **in the World-to-Come,** and nothing is derived from the doubled verb "*hikkaret **tikkaret*****,"** as **the Torah spoke in the language of people.**

Rabbi Yishmael objects: we already know from another verse, *ve-nikhreta ha-nefesh ha-hi*, that *avodah zarah* carries *kareit* — including both *olam hazeh* and *olam haba*. Does the existence of a third verse imply a third world? Rabbi Akiva responds that this is simply a case of *dibra Torah kile-shon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of human beings, using emphatic repetition for rhetorical force. The doubled language of *hikaret tikaret* does not introduce new legal content; it is ordinary stylistic emphasis, and we already derive the two-world *kareit* from the earlier verse.

## Ba'al Ov & Yidoni

### Ba'al Ov Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בַּעַל אוֹב – זֶה פִּיתוֹם הַמְדַבֵּר מִשֶּׁחְיוֹ, וְיִדְּעוֹנִי – זֶה הַמְדַבֵּר בְּפִיו. הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בִּסְקִילָה, וְהַנִּשְׁאָל בָּהֶם בְּאַזְהָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The list of those liable to be executed by stoning includes those who practice various types of sorcery. The mishna describes them: **A necromancer is a *pitom* from whose armpit** the voice of the dead appears to **speak. And a sorcerer is** one **from whose mouth** the dead appears to **speak. These,** the necromancer and the sorcerer, **are** executed **by stoning, and one who inquires** about the future **through them is in** violation of **a prohibition.**

The Gemara moves to the beginning of the Mishnah on 65a, which introduces two additional categories of those liable to *skilah*: the *ba'al ov* and the *yidoni*. The Mishnah defines the *ba'al ov* as *zeh ha-pitom ha-medaber mi-shichyo* — this is the *pitom*, a particular type of practitioner, who speaks from his armpit. The *yidoni* is defined as *zeh ha-medaber be-piv* — one who speaks from his mouth. Both carry the death penalty of *skilah*. The Mishnah adds that one who consults either of these practitioners — who seeks them out to learn from them — violates a *lav*, an *azharah*, and may be liable to bring a *korban*.

### Omission Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּקָתָנֵי בַּעַל אוֹב וְיִדְּעוֹנִי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא גַּבֵּי כָּרֵיתוֹת דְּקָתָנֵי בַּעַל אוֹב וְשַׁיְּירֵיהּ לְיִדְּעוֹנִי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What is different here, that** the mishna **teaches** the *halakhot* of both **a necromancer and a sorcerer, and what is different in** tractate ***Karetot*** (2a), **that** the mishna **teaches** the *halakha* of **a necromancer but leaves out** the *halakha* of **a sorcerer?**

The Gemara raises a comparison between our Mishnah and the Mishnah in Masechet Kritot. Our Mishnah lists both *ba'al ov* and *yidoni*. The Mishnah in Kritot — which enumerates all the Torah transgressions that carry *kareit* and for which one who acts inadvertently must bring a *korban chatat* — mentions *ba'al ov* but omits *yidoni* altogether. The question is: why the asymmetry? Both seem structurally comparable, and yet Kritot singles out only one.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וּשְׁנֵיהֶן בְּלָאו אֶחָד נֶאֶמְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The mishna in tractate *Karetot* does not count a sorcerer separately in the list of those liable to receive *karet* **since both** a necromancer and a sorcerer **are stated** in the Torah **in one prohibition.**

Rabbi Yochanan explains that both *ba'al ov* and *yidoni* are in fact included in the Mishnah in Kritot — they are stated together in a single *lav* in the Torah, and therefore teaching one is sufficient to encompass the other. The Mishnah in Kritot mentions *ba'al ov* alone, but *yidoni* is implicitly covered.

### Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: יִדְּעוֹנִי, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: A sorcerer** is not included in the list in tractate *Karetot* **because** his transgression **does not involve an action;** it involves only speech, and one does not bring a sin-offering for transgressing a prohibition that does not involve an action.

Reish Lakish disagrees. In his view, *yidoni* is omitted from the Mishnah in Kritot because there is no *maaseh* involved in *yidoni* — no qualifying act. Since *kareit* (and the attendant obligation of a *korban chatat* for inadvertent transgression) requires a *maaseh*, *yidoni* simply does not qualify for inclusion in that Mishnah. The Gemara will elaborate at length on what *maaseh* means in this context.

### First Mentioned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי שְׁנָא בַּעַל אוֹב דְּנָקֵט? מִשּׁוּם דִּפְתַח בֵּיהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** that a sorcerer is not listed because sorcery is included in the same prohibition as that of necromancy, **what is different** about **a necromancer that** the mishna **uses** specifically that example, and not the example of a sorcerer? The Gemara answers: The mishna chooses to mention the example of a necromancer **because the verse introduces** the prohibition **with it;** the case of a sorcerer is mentioned in the verse afterward (see Deuteronomy 18:11).

The Gemara then asks: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who says both are subsumed under a single *lav* and are therefore jointly included in the Mishnah in Kritot — if both are included, why does that Mishnah specify *ba'al ov* rather than *yidoni*? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *mishum de-patach bei kra* — because the Torah consistently introduces this pair by mentioning *ba'al ov* first, as in *ba'al ov o yidoni* or *ba'al ov ve-yidoni*. The Mishnah simply follows the Torah's own word order and uses the first of the pair as representative.

### Disagreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: חֲלוּקִין הֵן בְּמִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Reish Lakish did not say** an explanation **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabbi Yoḥanan? Rav Pappa says:** Reish Lakish disagrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan **because** the cases of a necromancer and a sorcerer **are divided** in the verse **with regard to** the issue of the **death** penalty, i.e., one is liable to receive the death penalty for each transgression.

The Gemara then asks the reverse: why does Reish Lakish not accept Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning — namely, that both are taught under a single *lav* and therefore both are included? Rav Pappa explains on Reish Lakish's behalf: *chalukin hen be-mitah* — they are separated from one another in the verse concerning the death penalty. The Torah writes *ki yihyeh vahem ov o yidoni*, using the word *o* — "or" — which constitutes a *chalukah*, a separation, between them. Once the Torah separates them in this way, one cannot assume that teaching one implicitly includes the other.

### Chalukah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲלוּקָּה דְּלָאו – שְׁמָהּ חֲלוּקָּה, דְמִיתָה – לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלוּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** could respond that one cannot infer the number of sin-offerings one is obligated to bring based on capital punishment. With regard to determining the number of sin-offerings, where there is **a division of a prohibition,** i.e., when two acts are listed as two separate prohibitions, it **is considered a division** that leads to a separate sin-offering, but where there is a division **of** a separate **death** penalty, i.e., when the death penalties are listed separately, it **is not considered a division** in this regard.

The Gemara now asks the converse: why does Rabbi Yochanan not accept Reish Lakish's argument from the *chalukah* in the verse about the death penalty? Rabbi Yochanan's response is that there are two distinct types of *chalukah*: *chalukah de-lav* — a separation that occurs in the context of a standard negative commandment — which is halachically significant and prevents one term from covering the other; and *chalukah de-mitah* — a separation that occurs in the context of the death penalty — which is not halachically significant in this sense. The word *o* in a verse about a *lo ta'aseh* constitutes a genuine *chalukah*, but the same word appearing in a verse about liability to *mitat beit din* does not carry the same legal weight. It is therefore the *chalukah de-lav* that matters for the Mishnah in Kritot, and since in the verse about the *lav* the two are not separated, Rabbi Yochanan holds that they remain jointly covered.

### According Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ? אָמַר לָךְ: מַתְנִיתִין דְּכָרֵיתוֹת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לָא בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan did not say** an explanation **in accordance with** the explanation of **Reish Lakish,** that a sorcerer is not listed in tractate *Karetot* because his transgression does not involve an action? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yoḥanan could have **said to you** that **the mishna of** tractate ***Karetot* is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that **we do not require an action** for one to be obligated to bring a sin-offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: נְהִי דְּלָא בָּעֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַעֲשֶׂה רַבָּה, מַעֲשֶׂה זוּטָא בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish** could have responded that **although Rabbi Akiva does not require** that one perform **a significant action** to be obligated to bring a sin-offering; nevertheless, **he does require** that one perform at least **a minor action.** Sorcery, by contrast, involves no action, and therefore even Rabbi Akiva would not deem one obligated to bring a sin-offering for it.

The Gemara then asks: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who says both are subsumed under a single *lav* and are therefore jointly included in the Mishnah in Kritot — if both are included, why does that Mishnah specify *ba'al ov* rather than *yidoni*? Rabbi Yochanan answers: *mishum de-patach bei kra* — because the Torah consistently introduces this pair by mentioning *ba'al ov* first, as in *ba'al ov o yidoni* or *ba'al ov ve-yidoni*. The Mishnah simply follows the Torah's own word order and uses the first of the pair as representative.

Reish Lakish responds: even conceding that the Mishnah in Kritot follows Rabbi Akiva, one must distinguish between *maaseh rabbah* — a substantial act — and *maaseh zuta* — a minimal act. Rabbi Akiva's position is that a *maaseh rabbah* is not required; but even Rabbi Akiva agrees that *maaseh zuta* is required. And *yidoni* involves no *maaseh* even of the minimal variety, which is why it is omitted.

## What Is Maaseh?

### Megadef Maaseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְגַדֵּף, מַאי מַעֲשֶׂה אִיכָּא? עֲקִימַת שְׂפָתָיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In the case of **a blasphemer,** i.e., one who curses God, **what action is there?** He merely speaks, and nevertheless Rabbi Akiva deems him obligated to bring a sin-offering. Clearly, Rabbi Akiva maintains that no action is necessary at all. The Gemara answers: **The twisting of his lips** while he speaks **is** considered **an action.**

This extended discussion of *maaseh* prompts the Gemara to ask: what *maaseh* is involved in *megadef*? Blasphemy is a transgression that carries *kareit*, and yet it appears to involve nothing more than speech. The Gemara's answer is *akimat sefatav havei maaseh* — the bending or compression of the lips constitutes a *maaseh*. This is a striking claim: bodily movement of the speech organs, without any further physical effect on the world, is categorized as a qualifying act.

This has implications beyond the immediate context. The question of whether speech constitutes a *maaseh* arises in contemporary discussions of, for example, whether verbally activating a voice-controlled device on *Shabbat* involves a *maaseh*. The sugya here speaks precisely to that question.

It is also worth noting what the Gemara focuses on: the movement of the lips, rather than the production of sound. This will become significant in a later dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish.

### Ba'al Ov Maaseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעַל אוֹב, מַאי מַעֲשֶׂה אִיכָּא? הַקָּשַׁת זְרוֹעוֹתָיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What action is there** in the case of **a necromancer?** The Gemara answers: **The striking of his arms** against each other in order to create the sound of a voice **is** considered **an action.**

The Gemara asks the parallel question: what *maaseh* is involved in *ba'al ov*? The answer given is *hakashat zeroatav havei maaseh* — the striking or waving of the arms is a *maaseh*. Again, a bodily movement that produces no direct physical change to the environment is classified as a qualifying act. The Gemara appears to be working against a background assumption that a *maaseh* must effect something in the external world — and it is precisely that assumption it is pushing back against.

### Braita Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן? וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, כְּגוֹן זִיבּוּחַ, קִיטּוּר, וְנִיסּוּךְ, וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is this considered an action **even according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who require a significant action for one to be obligated to bring a sin-offering? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: One **is liable** for idol worship **only for a matter that involves an action, e.g., sacrificing** an idolatrous offering, **burning** incense to an idol, **and pouring** a libation to an idol, **and bowing** to an idol.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאן תְּנָא הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה? רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לָא בָּעֵינַן מַעֲשֶׂה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, כְּפִיפַת קוֹמָתוֹ לְרַבָּנַן הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught bowing** among these examples? **It is Rabbi Akiva, who says: We do not require** a significant **action** for one to be liable; any action is sufficient. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** as **the bending of one's height, according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, is** considered a significant **action.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, כְּפִיפַת קוֹמָתוֹ לְרַבָּנַן לָא הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה, הַקָּשַׁת זְרוֹעוֹתָיו דְּבַעַל אוֹב הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה?
>
> **Translation:** **Now** consider, if **according to Reish Lakish, the bending of one's height is not** considered a significant **action according to the Rabbis,** can a less noticeable action such as **the striking of the necromancer's arms be** considered a significant **action?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ נָמֵי – לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, אֲבָל לְרַבָּנַן – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Reish Lakish concedes that according to the Rabbis, the striking of the necromancer's arms is not considered a significant action. **When Reish Lakish says** that it is considered an action, that statement is **also in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. But according to the Rabbis,** it is **not** considered a significant action.

A *braita* is then cited: one is not liable unless the act contains a *maaseh* — the *braita* gives examples: slaughtering (*zevichah*), burning incense (*kitor*), pouring a libation (*nisuch*), and bowing (*hishtachavayah*). These represent paradigmatic acts of *avodah zarah*.

Reish Lakish comments that the inclusion of *hishtachavayah* — bowing — in this list indicates that this *braita* must follow the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who does not require a substantial *maaseh*. The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, would not count bowing as a sufficiently significant *maaseh* to trigger liability for *kareit*.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: even according to the *Rabbanan*, *kefifat komato* — the physical bending of one's body — constitutes a *maaseh* substantial enough to incur liability. The *braita* need not be attributed exclusively to Rabbi Akiva.

The Gemara then presses Reish Lakish: if bowing is insufficient for the *Rabbanan*, how can waving the arms (*hakashat zeroatav*) of a *ba'al ov* be sufficient? Bowing involves a more pronounced physical movement than arm-waving, and if the former is inadequate, the latter surely is as well. The Gemara resolves this by concluding that when Reish Lakish said that arm-waving is a *maaseh*, he too was speaking according to the view of Rabbi Akiva — not according to the *Rabbanan*. The *Rabbanan* would not regard arm-waving as a sufficient *maaseh* either.

The Gemara then points out a further difficulty: if the *Rabbanan* reject both bowing and arm-waving as insufficient acts, they should also exclude *ba'al ov* from the Mishnah in Kritot — and not merely exclude *megadef*. We know there is a *braita* in Kritot where the *Rabbanan* disagree with Rabbi Akiva and exclude *megadef* from *kareit* liability; but given the conclusion just reached, they should have excluded *ba'al ov* as well. Why does the *braita* omit this?

### Ulla Incense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבִי אֶלָּא אָמַר עוּלָּא: בִּמְקַטֵּר לַשֵּׁד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Ulla says:** The reason the Rabbis do not state: Excluding a blasphemer and a necromancer, is that when the mishna there lists the case of a necromancer it is referring **to one who burns** incense **to a demon** in order to raise the dead, which is a significant action.

Ulla proposes an answer: the *ba'al ov* included in the Mishnah in Kritot — the one whom even the *Rabbanan* agree is liable for *kareit* — refers not to the arm-waving practitioner, but to one who burns incense to a demon (*mekattar la-shed*). Burning incense is clearly a substantial physical act — a *maaseh gamur* — and even the *Rabbanan* would recognize it as such.

### Rava Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מְקַטֵּר לַשֵּׁד – עוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הוּא! אֶלָּא, אָמַר רָבָא: בִּמְקַטֵּר לְחַבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Ulla: **One who burns** incense **to a demon is an idol worshipper,** which is already mentioned in the mishna in *Karetot*. **Rather, Rava says:** The mishna there is referring **to one who burns** incense to the demons not as a form of worship but as a manner of sorcery, in order **to gather** the demons, i.e., to bring them to one place.

Rava objects immediately: one who burns incense to a *shed* is not a *ba'al ov* at all — he is an *oved avodah zarah*, a practitioner of full-fledged idolatry. The act of burning incense to a demon falls under the category of *avodah zarah*, not *ba'al ov*, and therefore cannot serve as the referent for *ba'al ov* in Kritot.

### Lechever

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַמְקַטֵּר לְחַבָּר – חוֹבֵר חָבֵר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Ulla: **One who burns** incense to the demons in order **to gather** them **is** considered **a charmer,** whom the Torah relates to with a distinct prohibition (see Deuteronomy 18:11), which is not punishable by *karet*.

Rava therefore proposes a different identification: the *ba'al ov* in Kritot refers to one who burns incense as part of *chovev chever* — a form of necromantic witchcraft directed at a dead person rather than at an idol. This is not *avodah zarah*, and it does involve a physical act.

### Abaye Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: חוֹבֵר זֶה בִּסְקִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava responded: **Yes,** such a person is also considered a charmer, **but the Torah states** that **this** particular **charmer,** who gathers demons, is included in the category of a necromancer, and therefore he is executed **by stoning,** and *karet* applies as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״חֹבֵר חָבֶר״ – אֶחָד חֶבֶר גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד חֶבֶר קָטָן, וַאֲפִילּוּ נְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** with regard to **a charmer** that the prohibition applies **both** to **a gathering of large** animals to one place **and** to **a gathering of small** animals to one place, **and even** to a gathering of **snakes and scorpions.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הִלְכָּךְ, הַאי מַאן דְּצָמֵיד זִיבּוּרָא וְעַקְרַבָּא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּקָא מִיכַּוֵּין דְּלָא לַיזְּקוּ, אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Therefore,** with regard to **this** person, **who** through sorcery **gathers a hornet and a scorpion** to harm each other, he is nevertheless **prohibited** from doing so **even if he intends to prevent them from harming** him.

Abaye qualifies this further, distinguishing between different forms of *chovev chever*. There is a *chever gadol* and a *chever katan* — a major and a minor form of this practice. One who performs the more serious variety is *chayav skilah*; one who performs the lesser form is *chayav malkut* only. Rava accepts this distinction, affirming that the Torah's mention of *chover chaver* indicates *skilah*, while the lesser form carries only lashes.

### Bees & Scorpions

Abaye concludes with a practical ruling: one who conjures bees and scorpions — causing them to attack one another through incantation — falls into the category of *chovev chever* and is *chayav malkut*, even if his intent was not to cause human harm. The only exception is a life-threatening situation. Rashi notes that if it is a matter of *pikuach nefesh*, the conjuring would be permitted. A question lingers, however, since some forms of this practice border on *avodah zarah*, for which *pikuach nefesh* would not ordinarily serve as a justification — the precise categorization of the act would determine the answer.

## Speech vs Silence

### Bowing Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי שְׁנָא דִּכְפִיפַת קוֹמָתוֹ לְרַבָּנַן הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה, וַעֲקִימַת שְׂפָתָיו לָא הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who holds that according to the Rabbis one is liable for bowing to an idol but not for blaspheming, **what is different** between these cases, leading to the conclusion **that the bending of one's height** while bowing **is** considered **an action according to the Rabbis, but** a blasphemer's **twisting of his lips** to speak **is not** considered **an action?**

The Gemara returns to the earlier dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan holds that bowing (*kefifat komato*) is a *maaseh* even according to the *Rabbanan*, while Reish Lakish holds it is not.

### Heart Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי מְגַדֵּף, הוֹאִיל וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בַּלֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The case of **a blasphemer is different, since** this transgression **is in the heart** as well. The sin of blasphemy does not apply to speech alone, as the blasphemer's intention is central to the transgression; if he spoke without intention he is not considered a blasphemer. Consequently, one is not obligated to bring a sin-offering for such an action, as it is essentially a sin of the heart.

Rava introduces a different consideration. Rabbi Yochanan holds that bowing is a *maaseh* for the *Rabbanan*, but that *akimat sefatav* — the movement of the lips in speech — is *not* a *maaseh* according to the *Rabbanan*. One might ask: are they not structurally identical? Both involve physical movement of the body with no further effect on the environment. Rava explains that *megadef* is different, because the act originates in and is driven by the heart — *ho'il ve-yeshno be-leiv*. It is not merely a mechanical physical movement but an expression of inner intent. That, Rava suggests, is what disqualifies it for the *Rabbanan* — or rather, that is what distinguishes it in some way.

### Eidim Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי זֵירָא: יָצְאוּ עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין, שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן מַעֲשֶׂה. וְאַמַּאי? הָא לֵיתַנְהוּ בַּלֵּב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises an objection** to Rava's answer, as it is stated in a *baraita* that one who unwittingly commits a transgression punishable by death is obligated to bring a sin-offering, **excluding conspiring witnesses,** who are not obligated to bring a sin-offering, **as** their transgressions **do not involve an action.** Rabbi Zeira asks: **And why** is a false witness's testimony not considered a transgression that involves an action? The testimony is delivered through speech, which should be considered an action, as **this is not** a transgression that is committed **in the heart;** the witnesses are liable for what they said, and not for their intention.

Rav Zeira challenges this from the case of *eidim zomemin*, conspiring witnesses. *Eidim zomemin* do not receive *kareit* even when their false testimony was aimed at causing another person to receive *kareit*. The principle of *kaasher zamam* — that they receive what they sought to inflict — does not extend to *kareit* for *eidim zomemin*. The stated reason is *she-ein bahem maaseh* — there is no *maaseh* involved. But if Rava's reasoning is correct — that something is considered a *maaseh* when it comes from the heart — why would *eidim zomemin* be exempt? Surely their false testimony came from the heart as well.

### Voice Not Maaseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין, הוֹאִיל וְיֶשְׁנוֹ בְּקוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Conspiring witnesses are different, since** their transgression **is** committed **through** their **voice.** The essence of their transgression is not speech itself but rather making themselves heard by the court. Therefore, since the projection of one's voice does not involve action, the transgression of conspiring witnesses is considered not to be involving action.

Rava responds: *eidim zomemin* are different because *yeshnan be-kol* — their act was performed through their voice. And a voice, in this context, does not constitute a *maaseh*. It is not the element of *leiv*, of inner intent, that drives the analysis; it is the distinction between lip-movement and voice-production that is operative. *Megadef* involves *akimat sefatav* — the physical movement of the lips — while testimony in court operates through the voice.

### Voice Maaseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹל, לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, לָאו מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא? וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: חֲסָמָהּ בְּקוֹל וְהִנְהִיגָהּ בְּקוֹל, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is** projecting one's **voice not** considered **an action according to Rabbi Yoḥanan? But wasn't it stated** that *amora'im* engaged in a dispute concerning the following case: If one **muzzled** an animal **by** projecting his **voice,** by berating it whenever it tried to eat, has he transgressed the prohibition of: "You shall not muzzle an ox while it treads out the corn" (Deuteronomy 25:4)? **And** similarly, if one **led** different species to work together **by** projecting his **voice,** without performing any action, has he transgressed the prohibition of: "You shall not plow with an ox and a donkey together" (Deuteronomy 22:10)? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** he is **liable, and Reish Lakish says** he is **exempt.**

This brings the Gemara to the explicit dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish on the general question of whether voice constitutes a *maaseh*. The case used to frame the dispute is *chasamah be-kol ve-hinhigah be-kol* — if one muzzles an animal (violating the prohibition against muzzling an ox while it threshes) by shouting at it, or if one drives a *kilayim* team of mismatched animals by voice alone. Rabbi Yochanan rules liable (*chayav*); Reish Lakish rules exempt (*patur*). This is directly relevant to the contemporary question of whether speaking to a voice-activated device on *Shabbat* constitutes a *maaseh*. Notably, the *Ein Mishpat* indicates that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan.

### Lip Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, עֲקִימַת פִּיו הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פָּטוּר, עֲקִימַת פִּיו לָא הָוֵי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the reasoning behind their opinions: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** he is **liable,** as he maintains that **the twisting of** one's **mouth** to speak **is** considered **an action,** whereas **Reish Lakish says** he is **exempt,** because he holds that that **the twisting of** one's **mouth** to speak **is not** considered **an action.**

More precisely: Rabbi Yochanan holds that *akimat piv havei maaseh* — moving the lips is a *maaseh* — while Reish Lakish holds the opposite.

### Girsa Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁאנֵי עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין, הוֹאִיל וְיֶשְׁנָן בִּרְאִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava says** there is a different answer to Rabbi Zeira's objection: **Conspiring witnesses are different, since they are** rendered liable mainly **through sight,** i.e., the important part of their testimony is what they saw, which is not considered an action.

Rava, returning to *eidim zomemin*, offers a final formulation of why they are different. The standard text of the Vilna *Shas* reads *ho'il ve-yeshnan be-reiah* — their act was one of *seeing*, meaning their testimony was grounded in what they observed, and observation alone cannot constitute a *maaseh*. However, the *Rif* cites a variant reading in the name of Rav Hai Gaon: *ho'il ve-yeshnan be-ai* — their act was performed through saying "I" (or some similar declarative statement). Even on this reading, a difficulty remains: if speaking is a *maaseh* according to Rabbi Yochanan, why are *eidim zomemin* exempt? Some suggest that there is something distinctive about speech in a formal judicial proceeding — that the context of the court somehow transforms or weights the act differently — but the matter remains a subject of considerable discussion in the commentators.

## Precise Definitions

### Ba'al Ov Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בַּעַל אוֹב – זֶה הַמְדַבֵּר בֵּין הַפְּרָקִים וּמִבֵּין אַצִּילֵי יָדָיו. יִדְּעוֹנִי – זֶה הַמַּנִּיחַ עֶצֶם יָדוּעַ בְּפִיו, וְהוּא מְדַבֵּר מֵאֵלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A necromancer is one who** causes the voice of the dead to be heard **speaking** from **between** his **joints or from his armpit. A sorcerer [*yideoni*] is one who places a bone of** an animal called **a *yadua* in his mouth, and** the bone **speaks on its own.**

A *braita* is cited that gives precise technical definitions of the two practitioners. The *ba'al ov* is one who speaks *bein ha-prakim* — from between the joints — *u-vein atzilei yadav*, and from the armpits. The voice or communication emanates from the cavities of the body.

### Yidoni Defined

The *yidoni* is one who takes the bone of an animal called a *yadua* and places it in his mouth, whereupon *vehu medaber me-elav* — the bone speaks of its own accord. This is something resembling ventriloquism, with the notable difference that it is the bone, not the practitioner's own voice, that produces the speech.

### Tanakh Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״וְהָיָה כְּאוֹב מֵאֶרֶץ קוֹלֵךְ״. מַאי לָאו, דְּמִשְׁתַּעֵי כִּי אוֹרְחֵיהּ? לָא, דְּסָלֵיק וְיָתֵיב בֵּין הַפְּרָקִים וּמִשְׁתַּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from the verse: **"And your voice shall be as a ghost out of the ground"** (Isaiah 29:4). **What, does** the dead person **not speak** from the grave **on his own?** The Gemara answers: **No,** this is not so, **as** the dead person **rises** by sorcery **and sits between the joints** of the necromancer **and speaks.**

The Gemara raises a *meitivei* against the definition of *ba'al ov*. It is unusual that a *meitivei* — a challenge from a more authoritative source — comes here from a verse in *Tanakh* rather than a *tannaic* text, but the logic is the same: a verse is inherently more authoritative than any *baraita*. The verse is *ve-hayah ke-ov me-eretz kolech* — "your voice will be like an *ov* from the earth." The implication seems to be that the voice of the *ba'al ov* is heard directly from the ground, not filtered through the joints or armpits of a human practitioner.

### Spirit Between

The Gemara resolves this: the verse does not mean the voice comes out of the ground directly. Rather, the spirit ascends from the ground and then *salika ve-yatvei bein ha-prakim* — rises and positions itself between the joints, and speaks from there. The voice is ultimately heard from the joints, not from the earth itself.

### Saul's Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַתֹּאמֶר הָאִשָּׁה אֶל שָׁאוּל אֱלֹהִים רָאִיתִי עֹלִים מִן הָאָרֶץ״. מַאי לָאו, דְּמִשְׁתַּעֵי כִּי אוֹרְחֵיהּ? לָא, דְּיָתֵיב בֵּין הַפְּרָקִים וּמִשְׁתַּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the statement of the necromancer to King Saul: **"And the woman said to Saul, I see a godlike being coming up out of the earth"** (I Samuel 28:13). **What, does** the verse **not** mean to say **that** the dead person **spoke on his own?** The Gemara refutes this proof: **No,** this is not so, **as** the dead person **sits between the joints** of the necromancer **and speaks.**

A second *meitivei* is raised from the episode of Shaul and the woman at Ein Dor: *va-tomer ha-isha el Shaul elohim ra'iti olim min ha-aretz* — "the woman said to Shaul: I have seen divine beings rising from the earth." Again, the text seems to indicate that the communication came directly from the ground.

### Dwelling Place

The Gemara resolves this second challenge in the same way as the first: the spirits rose from the earth but then went and positioned themselves *bein ha-prakim* — between the joints — and it was from there that they made themselves heard. The ground is the origin of the ascent, not the locus of the communication. The definition in the *braita* stands: the *ba'al ov* operates through the joints and armpits, and the apparent contradiction from these biblical passages is resolved by understanding that the spirits merely pass through the earth on their way to their actual dwelling place within the human body of the practitioner.
