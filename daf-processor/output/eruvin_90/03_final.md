# Eruvin 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Second Story Access

### Abaye's Second Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בָּנָה עֲלִיָּיה עַל גַּבֵּי בֵּיתוֹ וְעָשָׂה לְפָנֶיהָ דַּקָּה אַרְבַּע — הוּתַּר בְּכׇל הַגַּגִּין כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** If a person **built an upper story atop his house,** by surrounding the roof with walls, **and erected before its** entrance **a small partition [*dakka*] four** cubits high that opens to other roofs, **it is permitted** for him **to** carry on **all the roofs.** His construction of the partition is indicative of his plans to utilize the other roofs, while the failure of the other owners to do so indicates that they conceded use of their roofs to him.

The *gemara* picks up at the bottom of 89b, continuing its analysis of what constitutes a sufficiently serious residential presence on a rooftop to dissolve the distinctions that the *chachamim* draw between separately owned rooftops. Recall the broader context: the *chachamim* hold that adjacent rooftops are considered distinct domains because the homes beneath them are under distinct ownership, and therefore carrying from one rooftop to another would require an *eruv*. However, the *gemara* had established that if one creates a genuinely substantial presence on one rooftop — such as constructing permanent walls or a fixed ladder — that presence dominates and the other rooftops, lacking any comparable habitation, lose their status as competing domains.

With this framework in place, Abaye introduces a new case. He states: if one built a second story atop his house — thereby transforming his rooftop into a genuine place of habitation — and then fashioned before it a *daka* of four, he is thereby permitted to access all the surrounding rooftops. The logic is continuous with what preceded: by constructing a second story, one has made the rooftop into a genuine dwelling place. The *daka*, on Rashi's reading, is a small doorway — an opening of four — that provides direct access from this second story out onto the adjoining rooftops. Without such an opening, the second story might be accessed only from inside the building, without any passage onto the roof itself; the doorway creates that connection, confirming that the rooftop is not merely a building top but an extension of a real living space. Since only this one person has done this, and the others have not made any competing claim of habitation on their own rooftops, the real dwelling presence here dominates, and there is no meaningful distinctiveness left in the surrounding rooftops.

### Daka Interpretation

The meaning of *daka* is itself a point of dispute between the *rishonim*. Rashi explains it here as a small opening or doorway — a departure from its usage elsewhere, where *daka* refers to a small barrier or partition. Tosafot, seeking consistency with the earlier usages of the term, attempts to retain the meaning of a barrier rather than an aperture. The practical implications of this disagreement are significant: on Rashi's reading, the key feature is the access created by the doorway; on Tosafot's reading, the small barrier plays a different structural role in defining the space.

### Rooftop Access

On Rashi's reading of the case, once the doorway is in place, *hutrah bechol hagagin kulan* — one is permitted access to carry across all the surrounding rooftops. The doorway opening outward onto the other rooftops establishes the second story's connection to them, effectively dissolving whatever distinctiveness those other rooftops might otherwise have had. One might ask whether this leniency extends to everyone, not merely to the person who built the second story — and indeed, this is an interesting question that the *gemara* itself does not explicitly address. The logic, however, points toward a broader permission: the real dwelling presence on the second story removes the distinctiveness of all the other rooftops as domains in their own right, regardless of who seeks to carry there. And since all these buildings already have four walls below, no *lechi* or *korah* is needed to frame the space — the walls of the buildings themselves provide all the necessary enclosure.

### Rava's Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַדַּקָּה לְאִיסּוּר, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי — דַּעֲבִידָא לַהֲדֵי תַּרְבִּיצָא דְבֵיתֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר לְנַטּוֹרֵי תַּרְבִּיצָא הוּא דַּעֲבִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Sometimes the small partition** leads **to prohibition. What are the circumstances** of this case? It is a case **where** the partition **was erected** facing **toward the garden of his house** and the sides facing the other roofs were sealed. The reason is **that** through his actions **he said** **that he built** the upper story **to protect the garden [*tarbitza*],** not to access the roofs.

Rava then introduces a counterpoint: *pe'amim shehadaka le'issur* — sometimes this very doorway can generate a prohibition rather than a leniency. Rashi explains that this applies even according to Rabbi Meir, who in the normal case holds that all rooftops constitute a single domain. The scenario Rava describes is one in which the doorway is fashioned facing a garden below, and it is understood that this configuration was created in order to oversee and guard that garden area. By orienting the rooftop toward the garden in this way, one has effectively defined this rooftop as serving the space below rather than participating in the community of rooftops. Even Rabbi Meir, who would normally group all rooftops together, would concede that this rooftop has been given a distinct identity — it is categorized together with the garden below, not with the surrounding rooftops.

## Roof vs. Platform

### Rami's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעַי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּגַג וּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּעַמּוּד מַהוּ? אָמַר רַבָּה: מַאי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, כַּרְמְלִית וּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised a dilemma:** According to Rav, who holds that one may carry only within four cubits on each roof, if he carries an object **two cubits on a roof and** another **two cubits on a pillar** ten handbreadths high and four handbreadths wide adjacent to the roof, **what is** the *halakha*? **Rabba said:** With regard to **what** matter **is he raising a dilemma?** Is it with regard to **a *karmelit* and a private domain** that **he is raising a dilemma?** The roof is a *karmelit* and the pillar is a private domain; certainly carrying from one to the other is prohibited.

The *gemara* transitions to a question posed by Rami bar Chama at the top of 91b. The question is framed within Rav's position — namely, that when the *chachamim* say each rooftop is a distinct domain, not only can one not carry from rooftop A to rooftop B, one also cannot carry more than four *amot* within rooftop A itself, because the exposure to the forbidden domain of rooftop B undermines the carrying capacity within A. Rami bar Chama accepts this as the operative framework and then poses his question: *shtei amot bagag u-shtei amot ba'amud mahu* — what is the rule if one carries two *amot* on a rooftop and two *amot* on a platform (*amud*)? One remains within four *amot* total, so the distance itself is not the concern. The question is whether the roof and the platform constitute distinct domains — and if so, even a movement of less than four *amot* that crosses from one into the other would be forbidden, just as crossing from rooftop A to rooftop B is forbidden even for short distances.

The motivation for the question is clear: rooftops are deemed distinct because different people live in the houses below. A platform, however, does not have someone living beneath it in the same way. Is it therefore to be treated differently, or is the experiential similarity of moving from one elevated surface to another enough to prohibit it?

### Rava Responds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אַגַּב חוּרְפֵּיהּ לָא עַיֵּין בַּהּ, אֶלָּא הָכִי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּגַג וּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּאַכְסַדְרָה, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא הַאי חֲזֵי לְדִירָה וְלָא הַאי חֲזֵי לְדִירָה — חֲדָא רְשׁוּתָא הִיא. אוֹ דִילְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּמִגַּג לְגַג אֲסִיר, מִגַּג לְאַכְסַדְרָה נָמֵי אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that this was not in fact the dilemma, **and Rami bar Ḥama, due to his keen** mind, **did not analyze** the dilemma carefully and was imprecise in its formulation. **Rather, this is** the **dilemma he is raising:** If one carries an object **two cubits on the roof** of a house, **and** another **two cubits on** the slanted roof of **a portico,** a roofed structure without walls, before a house belonging to someone else, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara elaborates on Rami bar Ḥama's dilemma: **Do we say** that **since neither this** roof **is fit for residence, nor is this** portico roof **fit for residence, it is** regarded as **one domain,** and therefore carrying between them is permitted? **Or perhaps since** carrying **from a roof to** another **roof is prohibited,** carrying **from a roof to a portico is likewise prohibited,** as the latter is also a domain in and of itself.

Rava dismisses the question as improperly formulated: *mai kamibaya lei — karmelit u-reshut hayachid kamibaya lei?* What kind of question is that? One of these spaces is a *karmelit* and the other is a *reshut hayachid* — of course they are distinct. Rashi explains that Rava understands the rooftop to be, in effect, a *karmelit* — not in its strict technical definition, but functionally, because one cannot carry on it in the manner of a true *reshut hayachid* given the exposure to the neighboring rooftop. The platform, by contrast, is a genuine *reshut hayachid* of the standard kind. Tosafot reverses the assignments: the platform is the *karmelit* — being less than ten *tefachim* high — and the rooftop is the *reshut hayachid*. Either way, Rava's point stands: the two spaces are obviously distinct in domain-status, so the question answers itself.

The *gemara* adds a memorable characterization: *VeRami bar Chama agav churpeih lo iyein bah* — because of his sharpness, Rami bar Chama did not slow down enough to think it through. This is a striking observation about a certain type of intellectual temperament: the question sounds incisive precisely because it was posed quickly, but a moment's reflection reveals that the categories had not been properly sorted. The *gemara* then proceeds to reconstruct what Rami bar Chama's question must have actually been — or at least, what it should have been. Whether this represents what he truly meant, or a sympathetic reformulation of an imprecise question, is itself an interesting methodological point about how the *gemara* handles such cases.

### Roof vs. Portico

Rashi offers two possible understandings of why the *achsadra* might share the quasi-*karmelit* status of the rooftop: either it is an exceptionally large space that exceeds *beit satayim* and was not enclosed for habitation, or it simply lacks proper walls altogether — being open on all sides beneath its roof, it may not qualify as a *reshut hayachid* at all and is straightforwardly a *karmelit*. The question then becomes whether carrying from a space that is *de facto* a *reshut hayachid* but functionally restricted like a *karmelit* to a space that is simply a *karmelit* is permissible. The *gemara* leaves this as an open question.

### Domain Analysis

The *gemara* frames the question in two directions. On one hand: *keivan dehai chazi ledira vehai lo chazi ledira, chada reshuta hi* — since neither space is truly fit for habitation in the primary sense, perhaps they are functionally a single domain and movement between them is permitted. The rooftop is distinct from other rooftops only because of different ownership below; the *achsadra* has no such competing ownership at all. On the other hand: *keivan demigag legag assar, migag le'achsadra nami assar* — perhaps the experiential similarity between moving from one rooftop to another and moving from a rooftop onto the roof of an *achsadra* is close enough that the same prohibition applies, even though technically no distinct ownership is in conflict.

## Roof vs. Ruin

### Bibi's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּגַג וּשְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת בְּחוּרְבָּה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye raised a** similar **dilemma:** If one carries **two cubits on the roof** of a house **and** another **two cubits** on the roof **of a ruin** belonging to someone else, one side of which was completely open to a public domain, **what is** the *halakha*?

Rav Bibi bar Abaye poses a related question: *bayis she-begago u-vayis she-bachurvah mah* — what is the rule regarding a rooftop of a standing house and a *churvah*, a ruin or vacant lot? This is a step removed from the *achsadra* case. Rashi understands the *churvah* here as a ground-level open structure — not even a roof, just an empty lot. The question is whether one may carry from the rooftop into this space.

Importantly, the *gemara* has been assuming — and will make this explicit later — that the *chachamim*'s concern is specifically about *different ownership* creating distinct domains. The *churvah*, being vacant, does not have an active competing owner. So the question is not one of competing ownership; it is a question of whether the difference in kind between a rooftop and a ruined lot is itself a sufficient barrier. Rashi adds that this particular *churvah* has three walls but one side is open to a *reshut harabim*, rendering it a *karmelit*. We thus have, once again, a question of moving between two spaces that are each effectively restricted in a similar way — yet the physical and categorical difference between a rooftop and a ground-level ruin may itself generate a prohibition.

One might note that this raises the deeper question, left somewhat unresolved in the discussion of the *chachamim*'s position, of whether carrying from a rooftop to a *chatzer* below is inherently problematic. The *chachamim* focus on distinct ownership as the operative factor; Rabbi Meir focuses on the physical category of rooftops as similar to one another. It is possible, then, that Rabbi Meir — who permits movement from rooftop to rooftop — might actually be stricter than the *chachamim* about moving from a rooftop to a structurally different space like a *chatzer*, while the *chachamim* might be more lenient about that transition since their concern is ownership rather than physical category. The two authorities may be looking at different things entirely.

### Kahana's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לָאו הַיְינוּ דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: Is that not** precisely the same dilemma raised **by Rami bar Ḥama** with regard to a portico?

Rav Kahana dismisses Rav Bibi bar Abaye's question by saying it is essentially the same as Rami bar Chama's earlier question — implying it has already been posed and left unresolved, so there is nothing new to deliberate.

### Portico vs. Ruin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: וְכִי מֵאַחֵר אֲתַאי וּנְצַאי? אַכְסַדְרָה לָא חַזְיָא לְדִירָה, וְחוּרְבָּה חַזְיָא לְדִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said: And did I come late** [***me'aḥer***] merely **to quarrel,** and meddle in other people's questions? That is not the case, as the two dilemmas are not identical. **A portico is not fit for residence, while a ruin is fit for residence.**

Rav Bibi bar Abaye pushes back sharply: *de-khi me-acher atzei u-mitnatzei* — am I pilfering someone else's questions just to make trouble? He insists his question is genuinely distinct. The difference is this: *achsadra lo chazya ledira, churva chazya ledira* — the *achsadra* is not suitable for habitation, whereas the *churvah* is, at least in theory. The *achsadra* is by nature a porch or a colonnade; the ruin is a structure that, if repaired, could serve as a dwelling. This potential habitability may be enough to give the *churvah* a latent status of distinct ownership — perhaps the absentee owner's theoretical claim to the space matters even when he is not present.

### Habitability Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּחַזְיָא לְדִירָה מַאי קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ! ״אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר״ קָאָמַר: אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר אַכְסַדְרָה לָא חַזְיָא לְדִירָה — חוּרְבָּה חַזְיָא לְדִירָה? אוֹ דִילְמָא: הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לֵית בַּהּ דָּיוֹרִין. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is surprised by this explanation: **And now that it is fit for residence, what dilemma is he raising?** The situation is comparable to the case of two standard roofs. The Gemara answers: Rav Beivai was unaware of the resolution to the dilemma raised by Rami bar Ḥama, and therefore, **he states** the dilemma employing the style: **If you say. If you say** that **a portico is not fit for residence,** and therefore carrying is permitted, it can be argued that as **a ruin is fit for residence,** the legal status of its roof should be like that of a standard roof. **Or perhaps** that is not the case, as **now in any event there are no residents in** the ruin, and therefore its roof is not comparable to a standard roof. No resolution was found for these dilemmas, and they **stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* reformulates this as a conditional question: *im timtzei lomar achsadra lo chazya ledira* — if you rule that the *achsadra* case is permissible (because neither the rooftop nor the *achsadra* constitutes a truly distinct dwelling, so they are treated as one domain), the *churvah* still presents a further question. Even if we look past the ownership issue when no one is actively present, the *churvah*'s fitness for potential habitation may mean we treat it as having a notional distinct ownership. On the other hand: *hashta miha leit bah diyurin* — right now, at this moment, no one lives there; and we have seen in the context of *eiruvin* that a person absent from home on *Shabbat* does not create a domain conflict. Perhaps the same principle should apply here.

The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — the question remains unresolved.

The broader implication, which the *gemara* makes visible but does not state explicitly, is that the *chachamim*'s framework is essentially ownership-focused, and the *gemara*'s entire line of questioning has been probing the limits of that framework: what happens when ownership is absent, theoretical, or ambiguous? What happens when the difference is not one of ownership at all but of structure or habitability? These questions will continue to reverberate throughout the subsequent discussions.

## Equal Roofs Debate

### Rav Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּגִּין הַשָּׁוִין לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְגַג יְחִידִי לְרַבָּנַן, רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses a different question. With regard to **roofs that are level,** i.e., with a height disparity of less than ten handbreadths, **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, or an isolated roof** that does not border other roofs, **according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, Rav said: It is permitted to move** an object **throughout the entire** roof; **and Shmuel said: One may move** an object **in it only within four** cubits.

The *gemara* now introduces a new debate, this time operating within the position of Rabbi Meir — who holds that all equal-height rooftops together constitute a single domain — and within the position of the *rabbanan* regarding a single standalone rooftop. In both scenarios the basic premise is that there is no competing ownership creating domain conflicts. The question is how freely one may carry in these spaces.

Rav states: *mutar letaltel bekulo* — one may carry throughout the entire combined space of connected equal-height rooftops, or on the single standalone rooftop. This is the intuitive position: if all the rooftops form one domain per Rabbi Meir, one should be able to traverse the entire expanse.

### Shmuel Restricts

Shmuel disagrees: *ein metaltelin bo ela be-arba* — one may carry no more than four *amot*. This seems counterintuitive to the point of being bizarre. Shmuel was the lenient voice in the earlier debate between Rav and Shmuel about the *chachamim*'s position, holding that even within the framework of competing ownership one may carry fully within one's own rooftop. Yet here, in a scenario where Rabbi Meir has eliminated the problem of competing ownership entirely, Shmuel says one may carry only four *amot*?

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ, קַשְׁיָא דְּרַב אַדְּרַב! הָתָם לָא מִינַּכְרָא מְחִיצְתָּא. הָכָא מִינַּכְרָא מְחִיצְתָּא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, קַשְׁיָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אַדִּשְׁמוּאֵל! הָתָם, לָא הָוֵי יוֹתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם. הָכָא, הָוֵי יוֹתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם, וְהָנֵי מְחִיצוֹת לְמַטָּה עֲבִידָן, לְמַעְלָה לָא עֲבִידָן, וְהָוֵה כְּקַרְפֵּף יָתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם שֶׁלֹּא הוּקַּף לְדִירָה, וְכׇל קַרְפֵּף יוֹתֵר מִבֵּית סָאתַיִם שֶׁלֹּא הוּקַּף לְדִירָה — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify the conflicting opinions. **Rav said** that **it is permitted to move** objects **throughout the entire** roof. This is **difficult,** as there is an apparent contradiction between one statement **of Rav and** another statement **of Rav.** With regard to level roofs, Rav said that according to the Rabbis one may carry on each roof only within four cubits. The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of a roof among roofs, **the** inner **partitions** between the houses **are not conspicuous,** and therefore, are not taken into consideration. **Here,** however, **the** outer **partitions** of a single house or group of houses **are conspicuous,** meaning that they are considered to extend upward and delineate the edge of the roof. **And Shmuel said: One may carry only within four** cubits. Once again, it is **difficult,** as there is an apparent contradiction between one statement **of Shmuel and** another statement **of Shmuel,** who said that in the case of level roofs, according to the Rabbis one may carry throughout each separate roof. The Gemara answers: **There,** the area of the roof **is no greater than two *beit se'a*;** whereas **here,** the area **is greater than two *beit se'a*. And these partitions** of the house **were erected** for use **below** as partitions for the residence itself; **they were not erected to** serve as partitions for use on the roof **above.** Consequently, even if the walls are viewed as extending upward so that they constitute surrounding partitions for the roof, the legal status of the roof is **like** that of **an enclosure greater than two *beit se'a* that was not enclosed** from the outset for the purpose of **residence; and** the principle is that with regard to **any enclosure greater than two *beit se'a* that was not enclosed** from the outset **for** the purpose of **residence, one may move** an object **in it only within four cubits.**

The *gemara* notes the apparent contradiction in Shmuel's position directly: *kashya de-Shmuel le-Shmuel*. The *gemara* first addresses the parallel tension in Rav's position, but quickly notes that Rav's position is easily explained. One might have thought that the rooftops, being exposed to the street below where a *reshut harabim* runs, should lose their *reshut hayachid* status — just as rooftop A, exposed to the forbidden domain of rooftop B, was compromised in the earlier discussion. But the *gemara* deflects this: *hatam lo minkara mechitza, hacha minkara mechitza* — in the earlier case the virtual walls between adjacent rooftops were invisible from below, creating a domain problem; here, however, the walls of the buildings are fully visible to anyone standing in the street below. The roof retains its distinct *reshut hayachid* character because the enclosure is visible and recognizable. This is a relatively easy resolution, and indeed the *gemara* treats it as such.

### Wall Visibility

The principle emerging here is significant: it is not merely the existence of walls but their *visibility* to those below that determines whether the rooftop is sufficiently distinguished from the surrounding domain. When the walls are invisible — as in the case of virtual partitions between adjacent rooftops — the domain is compromised. When they are visible — as with the actual walls of buildings seen from street level — the rooftop remains a recognizable and distinct *reshut hayachid*.

### Shmuel Explained

The harder question is Shmuel's position. The *gemara* explains: *hasam lo havya yoser mi-beis sa'atayim, hacha havya yoser mi-beis sa'atayim* — in the earlier case of the *chachamim*, where each rooftop was treated as a distinct domain, no single rooftop ever became larger than *beit satayim*; each building's roof is a bounded space. Therefore, even according to Shmuel, one could carry freely within that individual rooftop. But according to Rabbi Meir, who declares all the connected equal-height rooftops to be one unified domain, combining them all may easily produce a space that exceeds *beit satayim*. And a space greater than *beit satayim* that was not *hukaf ledira* — enclosed for the purpose of habitation — acquires the status of a *karmelit*, limiting carrying to four *amot*.

The crux is whether the walls of the buildings below render the rooftop above *hukaf ledira*. Shmuel says no: *ve-hani mechitsos lemata avidun, lemala lo avidun* — the walls down below were built for the sake of those living inside the houses; they were not built for the sake of the rooftop above. The roof therefore is not *hukaf ledira* and, when it exceeds *beit satayim*, becomes like a *karfaf* that was not enclosed for habitation — a *karmelit* in which one may carry only four *amot*.

Rav, by contrast, maintains that since the walls below and the rooftop above share the same physical structure — the same walls — the *hukaf ledira* status of the interior extends upward and renders the rooftop *hukaf ledira* as well.

The *gemara* confirms this understanding in its language: *ve-havei ke-karfap yoser mi-beis sa'atayim shelo hukaf ledira — ve-chol karfap yoser mi-beis sa'atayim shelo hukaf ledira ein metaltelin bo ela be-arba* — it is like an enclosed field exceeding *beit satayim* that was not walled in for habitation, and in any such space one may carry only four *amot*.

## Boats & Vessels (II)

### Rava on Boats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, סְפִינָה. רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלָּהּ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בָּהּ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **It was** further **stated** that these same *amora'im* disagreed with regard to **a** large **ship. Rav said: It is permitted to move** an object **throughout** the entire ship, as it is all one domain; **and Shmuel said: One may move** an object **in it only within four** cubits.

The *gemara* next applies the same conceptual framework to a boat. The question is whether one may carry freely throughout a large boat on *Shabbat*, or whether the boat's interior — if it exceeds *beit satayim* — is restricted to four *amot* as a space not *hukaf ledira*. Rava rules: *mutar le-taltel be-kula* — one may carry throughout the entire boat. The walls of the boat constitute an enclosure, and Rava holds that this enclosure qualifies as *hukaf ledira*.

### Shmuel on Boats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלָּהּ — דְּהָא אִיכָּא מְחִיצָתָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בָּהּ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — מְחִיצוֹת לְהַבְרִיחַ מַיִם עֲשׂוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates. **Rav said: It is permitted to move** an object **throughout** the boat, **as there are partitions. And Shmuel said: One may move** an object **in it only within four cubits,** as **the partitions** of the ship are not considered full-fledged partitions; they **are erected** only **to keep water out,** not to render it a residence.

Shmuel, consistent with his earlier reasoning, rules: *ein metaltelin bo ela be-arba* — one may carry only four *amot*. His explanation: *mechitsos le-avruchi maya avidun* — the walls of a boat are constructed primarily to keep out water and allow the vessel to float, not to create a habitable enclosed space. The purpose of the walls, in Shmuel's analysis, is structural and nautical rather than residential, and therefore they do not confer *hukaf ledira* status.

This is admittedly a difficult position to sustain, and the *gemara*'s discussion acknowledges as much. People do in fact live on boats, sleep in them, and use them as residential space. One might argue that the reason one wants the boat not to take on water is precisely so that one will have a vessel in which people can dwell — making habitation the ultimate end even if water-resistance is the proximate means. The difficulty of Shmuel's position is particularly acute for the enclosed hull of a boat where people sleep, rather than the open deck. If individual cabins or rooms have been constructed within the hull, those walls would clearly be *hukaf ledira*. The debate may be most coherent when applied to an open galley-style hull with no internal partitions — a large undivided space in which one might argue that the walls were built purely as a nautical structure rather than as a residential enclosure.

## Boats & Vessels (II)

### Rava's Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף לִשְׁמוּאֵל: הִילְכְתָא כְּווֹתָךְ אוֹ הִילְכְתָא כְּרַב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הִילְכְתָא כְּרַב. אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף: וּמוֹדֶה רַב שֶׁאִם כְּפָאָהּ עַל פִּיהָ, שֶׁאֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בָּהּ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. כְּפָאָהּ, לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לָדוּר תַּחְתֶּיהָ, מַאי שְׁנָא מִגַּג יְחִידִי? אֶלָּא שֶׁכְּפָאָהּ לְזוֹפְתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef said to Shmuel:** Is **the *halakha* in accordance with your** opinion **or** is **the *halakha* in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav?** Shmuel **said to him: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav,** as his rationale is more convincing. **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav Ḥiyya bar Yosef said: And Rav concedes that if one overturned** the ship **onto its mouth,** and it is more than ten handbreadths high, **that one may move** an object **on it only within four cubits.** The Gemara asks: **For what** purpose **was** the ship **overturned? If you say** it was overturned so **that** one may **reside beneath it, what is the difference** between it and **an isolated roof?** The legal status of the overturned ship should in every sense be that of a house, and therefore it should be permitted to carry throughout the entire ship. **Rather,** it must be **that he overturned it to tar it,** i.e., to add a fresh coat to its underside. In that case, the boat certainly does not serve as a residence, and its sides are not considered full-fledged partitions.

Even Rava, who permits carrying throughout the boat in its normal configuration, agrees that there are cases where the boat is restricted. Rav Gidal citing Rav Chiya bar Yosef reports: *u-modeh Rava* — Rava concedes that if one overturned the boat — *im kapa pi-ha* — and sought to use the hull space underneath as a shelter, carrying would be restricted to four *amot*. The logic is that when a boat is flipped upside down, typically the purpose is repairs — applying tar (*le-zufta*) or caulking the hull — not creating a place of habitation. Although the overturning creates a new enclosed space with walls, those walls were not fashioned *le-dirah*: they came into being for a repair function. Since the enclosure was not created for habitation, Rava agrees that it does not qualify as *hukaf ledira*, and the space is restricted.

The *gemara*'s question about whether one might flip the boat to live inside it is resolved by noting that in practice the purpose of overturning is repair, not residence. The analogy with a boat in its normal orientation is instructive: in normal use, even if water-resistance is the primary engineering motivation for the walls, people do live inside — so Rava considers it *hukaf ledira*. When overturned, no one lives in it and no one intends to; the new wall configuration is incidental to a non-residential function.

### Boat Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, לִיטַּלְטְלֵי מִגַּג לְחָצֵר?! גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי.
>
> **Translation:** The *halachic* ruling is stated explicitly: Rav Chiya bar Yosef asked Shmuel directly — *hilchosa kevassach o hilchosa ke-Rava* — do we rule like you or like Rava? Shmuel replied: *hilchosa ke-Rava* — the law follows Rava. This exchange is itself noteworthy: Shmuel, who held his own position, nonetheless readily endorsed the accepted ruling in favor of the opposing view when asked directly. He did not attempt to leverage his authority as the questioner's interlocutor to promote his own *psak*. The law therefore follows Rava: one may carry freely throughout a boat in its normal configuration.

## Portico Walls

### Rav Virtual Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַשֵׁי מַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסְּפִינָה, וְרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ אַאַכְסַדְרָא. דְּאִיתְּמַר, אַכְסַדְרָה בְּבִקְעָה. רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלָּהּ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בָּהּ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע. רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלָּהּ — אָמְרִינַן פִּי תִקְרָה יוֹרֵד וְסוֹתֵם. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בָּהּ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע — לָא אָמְרִינַן פִּי תִקְרָה יוֹרֵד וְסוֹתֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi teaches** Shmuel's acceptance of Rav's opposing view (Ritva) with regard **to a ship,** as stated above; **and Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, teaches it** with regard **to a portico, as it was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to **a portico** located **in a field.** A portico has a roof and either incomplete walls or no walls. Consequently, in the case of a portico located in a valley, which is a *karmelit*, it remains to be determined whether or not it is permitted to carry in it. **Rav said: It is permitted to move** an object **throughout the entire** portico, as it is a private domain. **And Shmuel said: One may move** an object **in it only within four** cubits. The Gemara elaborates. **Rav said: It is permitted to move** an object **throughout the entire** portico, as **we say: The edge of the roof descends** to the ground **and seals** the portico on all sides, rendering it a private domain. **And Shmuel said: One may move** an object **in it only within four** cubits, as **we do not say: The edge of the roof descends** to the ground **and seals** the portico.

The *gemara* now records a dispute — cited in two different versions by Rav Ashi and by Rav Acha son of Rava, each claiming that Shmuel's ruling in favor of Rava applied to a different debate — concerning an *achsadra b'vikah*, a portico standing in a valley. Rav rules: *mutar le-taltel bekula* — one may carry throughout the entire portico. His reasoning is that we apply the principle of *pi tikra yored v'sotem* — the edge of the roof descends and seals, creating virtual walls. Even though the portico has no actual walls — only supporting columns or posts — the edge of the roof is treated as if it extended downward, producing virtual partitions that enclose the space beneath.

### Shmuel No Walls

Shmuel rules: *ein metaltelin bah ela be-arba* — one may carry only four *amot* in the portico. He rejects the application of *pi tikra yored v'sotem* in this case: *lo amrinan pi tikra yored v'sotem* — we do not invoke this principle here. Without actual walls, and without virtual walls generated by *pi tikra yored v'sotem*, the portico is a *karmelit* and carrying is restricted.

### Wall Interpretation

This dispute has implications beyond the immediate case, and it surfaces in the *gemara* in Tractate *Sukkah* as well. The precise scope of *pi tikra yored v'sotem* — under what conditions the roof-edge is sufficient to create virtual walls — is the underlying question. Rashi and Tosafot interpret the debate differently. Rashi understands the distinctive feature of this case to be that one would need to invoke *pi tikra yored v'sotem* on all four sides simultaneously — that is, in the complete absence of any actual walls, one is relying on the roof-edge to generate the entire enclosure. It is this application on all four sides that Shmuel rejects. Tosafot, however, understands the dispute as concerning even a single virtual wall created by *pi tikra yored v'sotem*; Rav and Shmuel disagree about the principle as such, not merely about its extension to multiple sides.

It is worth noting the interesting contrast with the rule of *mechitza teluyah* — a suspended partition that does not reach the ground. There, we say that a gap of more than three *tefachim* between the bottom of the partition and the ground invalidates the partition, since small animals could pass through. The edge of a roof descending by perhaps only one *tefach* would seem far less effective than even a suspended partition. Yet *pi tikra yored v'sotem* claims that this thin roof-edge is sufficient to create a virtual wall. The reason appears to be that the roof-edge is not merely a partition but part of a roofed structure — it carries the connotation of an enclosed house or domicile — and this implicit sense of enclosure generated by a roof structure is what allows even a minimal downward projection to be treated as a wall. That conceptual rationale is precisely what Rav and Shmuel are debating.

## Roof to Courtyard

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, לִיטַּלְטְלֵי מִגַּג לְחָצֵר?! גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם דְּרַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **Rav's** statement **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Meir** that one is permitted to carry from one roof to another if they are level, it should also be permitted **to carry from a roof to a courtyard.** Why then does Rabbi Meir rule that roofs and courtyards are separate domains and that carrying between them is prohibited? The Gemara answers: It is prohibited because Rabbi Meir issued **a decree, due to** the opinion **of Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi.** As stated previously, Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi rules that one may not transfer objects between two halakhically equivalent but physically distinct domains ten or more handbreadths high. This is a decree lest one standing in a public domain adjust a burden on a mound ten handbreadths high and four handbreadths wide, which is a private domain, an act prohibited by Torah law.

The *gemara* now turns to a series of positions in which the major *amora'im* are aligned with the *tanna'im* on specific questions. The first concerns Rav according to Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Meir, as established, holds that all equal-height rooftops constitute a single domain — one may carry from any rooftop to any other. But what about carrying from a rooftop to a *chatzer* below? Even granting that the rooftops form one unified domain, a *chatzer* is a categorically different kind of space: it is at ground level, it is physically distinct from the elevated rooftop plane. Does the *hukaf ledira* quality shared by both a rooftop (as Rav reads it) and a *chatzer* make them sufficiently similar that one may carry between them?

The *gemara* states that Rav according to Rabbi Meir would prohibit carrying from a rooftop to a *chatzer*: *gezeira mishum deRabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi*. This is a *gezeira* — a rabbinic precautionary measure — based on the principle articulated by Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi: one must not confuse a *reshut hayachid* elevated above the ground with a pillar standing in the middle of a *reshut harabim*. The fear is that if one becomes accustomed to moving objects from a rooftop down to a ground-level space, one might come to carry from a pillar in a *reshut harabim* to the *reshut harabim* itself. More simply stated: the physical difference between a rooftop and a *chatzer* — one high, one low — is itself sufficient to categorize them as distinct for these purposes, even if their abstract domain qualities are similar.

### Gezeira Logic

The reasoning here is more than just technical domain-categorization. Even if one could argue that both the rooftop and the *chatzer* are *reshut hayachid*, or that both are equally *hukaf ledira* in a non-primary residential sense, the physical reality of ascending and descending creates a recognizable transition that the rabbis treated as categorically significant. The *gezeira* of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi provides the formal halachic articulation of this intuition.

## Roof to Field

### Shmuel Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּנַן, נִיטַּלְטֵל מִגַּג לְקַרְפֵּף. אָמַר רָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִפָּחֵת הַגָּג. אִי הָכִי, מִקַּרְפֵּף לְקַרְפֵּף נָמֵי לָא יְטַלְטֵל, דִּילְמָא מִיפְּחִית וְאָתֵי לְטַלְטוֹלֵי? הָתָם, אִי מִיפְּחִית מִינַּכְרָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא. הָכָא, אִי מִיפְּחִית לָא מִינַּכְרָא מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** according to **Shmuel's** statement **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis** that the legal status of an isolated roof greater than two *beit se'a* is that of a *karmelit*, it should be permitted **to move** an object **from** an isolated **roof to an enclosure** within four cubits of the roof, as the legal status of the enclosure is also that of a *karmelit*. Why then do the Rabbis rule that roofs and enclosures are separate domains and carrying from one to the other is prohibited? **Rava bar Ulla said:** It is prohibited because the Rabbis issued **a decree lest the** area of the **roof diminish** to less than two *beit se'a*, in which case it would assume the status of a private domain, as it is prohibited to carry between a private domain and an enclosure. The Gemara asks: **If so, one should also not** be permitted to **move** an object **from one enclosure to** another **enclosure,** due to the concern that **perhaps** the area of one of the enclosures will **diminish** and become a private domain, **and he** will **come** to **move** an object from one to the other as before. The Gemara answers: **There, if** the enclosure **is diminished, the matter is conspicuous,** as its walls are clearly visible. **Here,** however, **if** the roof **is diminished, the matter is not conspicuous,** as the roof does not have walls.

The *gemara* concludes with Shmuel according to the *rabbanan*. Shmuel holds, as established, that the rooftop is *lo hukaf ledira* — the walls built for the house do not render the rooftop above as *hukaf ledira*. With this in view, the *gemara* asks: why can one not carry from a rooftop to a *karfaf*, an enclosed field? A *karfaf* is technically a *reshut hayachid* — it has walls — but it is not *hukaf ledira*, because it was enclosed for agricultural or animal-keeping purposes, not for habitation. According to Shmuel, the rooftop also lacks *hukaf ledira* status. The two spaces thus share the same defining qualities: real *reshut hayachid* status in principle, but non-*hukaf ledira* in character.

Given this symmetry, one might expect that carrying between them would be permitted — or at least that the question would be no harder than any other case of two similar spaces. Yet the *chachamim* do not permit carrying from a rooftop to a *karfaf*. Why not?

Rava bar Ula provides the answer: *gezeira shema yipaches hagag* — a rabbinic precautionary measure lest the roof become smaller. Rashi clarifies the specific scenario: we are speaking of a very large roof, one that exceeds *beit satayim*. Because it exceeds *beit satayim* and lacks *hukaf ledira* status, the rooftop is in effect a *karmelit*. Similarly, the *karfaf* in question is large and not *hukaf ledira*, making it also a *karmelit*. Both are therefore *karmelit*. The question is why one cannot carry from one *karmelit* to the other.

The concern is that part of the roof might deteriorate and collapse, reducing the rooftop's area to below *beit satayim*. A rooftop smaller than *beit satayim* that is not *hukaf ledira* would then be a genuine *reshut hayachid*, not a *karmelit*. If people continued to carry as before — from the now-*reshut hayachid* rooftop to the *karmelit* field — they would be violating a prohibition that had been created by the structural change, without realizing it.

### Field vs. Roof

The *gemara* anticipates the obvious challenge: why not apply the same concern to carrying from one *karfaf* to another? If part of one enclosed field's walls collapsed, it too could become smaller than *beit satayim* and transition from *karmelit* to *reshut hayachid*, creating the same problem. Why do we not issue the same *gezeira* for field-to-field carrying?

The *gemara* answers: *hasam ei mipachas minkara milsa, hacha ei mipachas lo minkara milsa* — in a field, if the walls change, the change is visible. The walls of a *karfaf* are actual physical structures surrounding a visible space; if a wall collapses or shifts, the change in the enclosed area is readily apparent to anyone using the space. But on a rooftop, the walls are invisible — they are the virtual extensions of the building walls below, perceived by convention rather than observed directly. If the roof area changes — say a section deteriorates — the change in the effective domain boundary is not obvious. A person standing on the roof would have no easy way of knowing that the space has become smaller than *beit satayim* and has thereby changed its domain status. The *gezeira* is therefore applied specifically to rooftops, where invisible boundaries create genuine risk of unwitting violation.

This discussion serves as the conceptual bridge to the material that will follow: the *gemara* is setting up an analysis not merely of competing ownership but of the similarity and difference between various types of spaces — rooftops, courtyards, fields, ruins — and what makes two spaces close enough in kind that movement between them is treated as unproblematic, or different enough that it generates a prohibition even when ownership conflicts are absent.
