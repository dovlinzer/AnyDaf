# Shabbat 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oven Types

### Tannur Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנֵי׳ תַּנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהוּ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא לֹא יִתֵּן בֵּין מִתּוֹכוֹ בֵּין מֵעַל גַּבָּיו. כּוּפָּח שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהוּ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא — הֲרֵי זֶה כְּכִירַיִים, בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים — הֲרֵי הוּא כְּתַנּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The *halakhot* that were stated with regard to a stove were specific to a stove's unique structure and the manner in which it retains heat. However, with regard to other baking apparatuses, i.e., an oven or a *kupaḥ,* there are different rules. The mishna delineates: **An oven that they lit** even **with straw** or **rakings, one may neither place** a pot **inside it nor atop it** on Shabbat. Whereas a ***kupaḥ* that was lit with straw** or **rakings, its** legal status is **like** that of **a stove,** and one is permitted to place a pot atop it on Shabbat. If it were lit **with pomace** or **with wood, its** legal status is **like** that of **an oven** and it is prohibited to place a pot atop it on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהוּ: סָבַר רַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר ״תּוֹכוֹ״ — תּוֹכוֹ מַמָּשׁ, ״עַל גַּבָּיו״ — עַל גַּבָּיו מַמָּשׁ, אֲבָל לִסְמוֹךְ שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: כּוּפָּח שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהוּ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא הֲרֵי הוּא כְּכִירַיִים, בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים הֲרֵי הוּא כְּתַנּוּר, וְאָסוּר. הָא כְּכִירָה — שְׁרֵי. בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא עַל גַּבָּיו, וּבְמַאי? אִילֵימָא כְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ גָּרוּף וְקָטוּם, אֶלָּא כִּירָה כִּי אֵינָהּ גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה, עַל גַּבָּיו מִי שְׁרֵי? אֶלָּא לָאו לִסְמוֹךְ, וְקָתָנֵי: הֲרֵי הוּא כְּתַנּוּר, וַאֲסִיר!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to that which we learned in the mishna that **an oven that they lit** even with straw or rakings, one may neither place a pot atop it nor inside it, **Rav Yosef thought to say** that when the mishna says **inside it,** it means **actually inside it;** and when it says **atop it,** it means **actually atop it. However, to lean** a pot against an oven, it may **well** be done. **Abaye raised an objection to** Rav Yosef from that which we learned in our mishna: **A *kupaḥ* that was lit with straw** or **rakings, its** legal status is **like** that of **a stove.** If it were lit **with pomace** or **with wood, its** legal status is **like** that of **an oven, and it is prohibited.** By inference: If it were **like a stove, it** would be **permitted. With what** circumstances **are we dealing? If you say** that he placed the pot **atop** the *kupaḥ*, **and in what** case? **If you say** it is referring to a case where **it is not swept and covered with ashes; a stove** that **is not swept and covered with ashes, is it permitted** even to place a pot **atop it** on Shabbat? **Rather, isn't** it referring to a case where one seeks **to lean** a pot against a *kupaḥ*, and **it taught: It is like an oven, and prohibited?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הָכָא בְּכוּפָּח גָּרוּף וְקָטוּם, וְתַנּוּר גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה עָסְקִינַן. הֲרֵי הוּא כְּתַנּוּר — דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּגָרוּף וְקָטוּם — עַל גַּבָּיו אָסוּר, דְּאִי כְּכִירָה כִּי גְּרוּפָה וּקְטוּמָה — שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: תַּנּוּר שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהוּ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא אֵין סוֹמְכִין לוֹ, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר עַל גַּבָּיו, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר לְתוֹכוֹ, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעָצִים. כּוּפָּח שֶׁהִסִּיקוּהוּ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא סוֹמְכִין לוֹ, וְאֵין נוֹתְנִין עַל גַּבָּיו. בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים אֵין סוֹמְכִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava** said: **Here we are dealing with** the cases of **a *kupaḥ* that is swept and covered with ashes and an oven that is swept and covered with ashes,** and the mishna is to be understood as follows: **It is like an oven in** the sense **that**, **although** it is **swept and covered with ashes, it is prohibited to place** a pot **atop it; as, if** its legal status were **like** that of **a stove, when** it is **swept and covered** it may **well** be done. The Gemara comments that there is a *baraita* that **taught in accordance** with the opinion of **Abaye: An oven that one lit with straw and with rakings, one may not lean** a pot against **it, and needless to say** one may not place a pot **atop it, and needless to say** one may not place a pot **inside it, and, needless to say** if it was lit **with pomace** or **with wood** it is prohibited. While with regard to a ***kupaḥ* that was lit with straw** or **with rakings, one may lean** a pot against **it, but he may not place** a pot **atop it.** If it was lit **with pomace** or **with wood, one may not lean** a pot against **it.**

Today's *daf* is 39. We pick up in the middle of 38b, wrapping up the previous *daf*. The *mishnah* introduced two new types of ovens: the *tannur*, which is the pyramidal oven used for baking bread, and the *kupach*, which occupies a middle position. The *tannur* is treated with great stringency — even if one uses *kash* and *gvava* (straw and stubble), and even if there are no coals to stir, it remains forbidden. The *gemara*'s discussion centers on just how strict the *tannur* is: whereas with a *kira* one may place something against the outside even over an exposed flame, what is the rule for a *tannur*? *Abaye* ruled that this too is forbidden.

A *baraita* then confirms *Abaye*'s position: *tannur she-sikuhu be-kash u-ve-gvava* — a *tannur* kindled even with straw and stubble — one may not place something alongside it (*ein somchin lo*), and certainly not on top of it (*ein tzarich lomar al gabav*), and certainly not inside it (*ein tzarich lomar le-tocho*). How much more so is this true if one used actual wood and chips (*gefet u-ve-etzim*). The *baraita* thus makes explicit that one may not place something against the outside of a *tannur* even with the most lenient fuel, in accordance with *Abaye*.

At a certain point the precise rationale becomes somewhat unclear. If we are dealing with wood and chips, the concern that one might come to stir the coals is obvious. But by a *tannur* kindled with straw and stubble the same strictness applies, and there the concern is harder to pin down — it may be a matter of *mechzei ke-mevashel* (the appearance of cooking), or it may be a principle of *lo pelug* (no internal distinctions within the category). The *gemara* does not make this fully explicit.

### Kupach Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הַאי כּוּפָּח הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי כְּכִירָה דָּמֵי, אֲפִילּוּ בְּגֶפֶת וּבְעֵצִים נָמֵי! אִי כְּתַנּוּר דָּמֵי, אֲפִילּוּ בְּקַשׁ וּבִגְבָבָא נָמֵי לָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נְפִישׁ הַבְלֵיהּ מִדְּכִירָה וְזוּטַר הַבְלֵיהּ מִדְּתַנּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aha, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: This *kupaḥ*, what are its circumstances? If** it is **considered like a stove, even** if it was lit **with pomace** or **with wood,** it should **also** be permitted. **And if** it is **considered like an oven, even with straw** or **with rakings,** it should **also not** be permitted. Rav Ashi **said to him:** From a halakhic perspective, a *kupaḥ* has intermediate status. **Its heat is greater than** that of **a stove;** however, **its heat is less than** that of **an oven.**

Rav Acha the son of Rava then posed a question to Rav Ashi: *hai kupach heichi dami* — what exactly is the situation with a *kupach*? If it is like a *kira*, it should be permitted even with wood and chips as long as the coals are swept out. If it is like a *tannur*, it should be forbidden even with straw and stubble. Why does it occupy a middle category? Rav Ashi replied: *nafish hevlei mi-kira* — its retained, contained heat is greater than that of a *kira* — *ve-zuta hevlei mi-tannur* — but less than that of a *tannur*. It thus falls into a genuine middle position.

### Kupach vs Kira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי כּוּפָּח, הֵיכִי דָמֵי כִּירָה? אֲמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: כּוּפָּח — מְקוֹם שְׁפִיתַת קְדֵרָה אַחַת. כִּירָה — מְקוֹם שְׁפִיתַת שְׁתֵּי קְדֵרוֹת. אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: כִּירָה שֶׁנֶּחְלְקָה לְאוֹרְכָּהּ — טְהוֹרָה. לְרׇחְבָּהּ — טְמֵאָה. כּוּפָּח, בֵּין לְאוֹרְכּוֹ בֵּין לְרוֹחְבּוֹ — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances of a *kupaḥ*? What are the circumstances of a stove** in terms of the configuration of the vessels? **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: A *kupaḥ*** is a small vessel that is similar to a stove; however, it only has one hole with enough **space to place a single pot. A stove** is like a double *kupaḥ* with enough **space to place two pots. Abaye said, and some say** that **Rabbi Yirmeya** said: **We also learned** this matter in a mishna dealing with the laws of ritual purity and impurity: **An** impure **stove that was divided lengthwise is pure** because it can no longer be considered a vessel. It is a broken vessel, and a broken vessel cannot become ritually impure. However, if the stove was divided **widthwise,** between the spaces for the pots, then it remains **impure** because it became two small stoves. However, a ***kupaḥ*, whether** it was divided **lengthwise** or **whether** it was divided **widthwise,** is **pure** because it can no longer be used as there is no way to put even a single pot on it. That is the difference between a *kupaḥ* and a stove.

The *gemara* then asks what the physical distinction between a *kupach* and a *kira* actually is. Rabbi Yossi the son of Rabbi Chanina explains: *kupach mekom shfisas kdera achas* — a *kupach* has room for only one pot, making it a very tight, heat-retaining space. A *kira*, by contrast — *mekom shfisas shtei kderos* — has room for two pots. Because it is a larger structure, there is greater airflow and the heat is correspondingly less intense.

It should be noted that the distinction between a one-pot and a two-pot space is attested in the *mishnah*, but archaeological and literary evidence suggests the difference runs deeper than mere capacity. The *kira* appears to have had more open or exposed walls allowing greater air circulation, whereas the *kupach* was a tighter vessel with heat rising through holes at the bottom — far more contained. The one-pot/two-pot description may capture one dimension of this reality without exhausting it.

The point is confirmed from a separate *mishnah*: if a *kira* was split lengthwise, it becomes *tehorah* — unusable and thus ritually pure — because it can no longer function as intended. If split widthwise, it becomes *teme'ah*, because one now has two independent *kirot*, each holding a single pot. This demonstrates that a standard *kira* has two separate openings for two separate pots. A *kupach*, by contrast, whether split lengthwise or widthwise, is always *tahor*, since it has only one opening and either split renders it nonfunctional.

### Modern Ovens

This concludes the *mishnah*'s discussion of the distinctions among *kira*, *kupach*, and *tannur*. In terms of practical application today, the broad consensus among *poskim* is that our modern ovens — what modern Hebrew calls a *tannur* — carry the *halacha* of a *kira*. Modern ovens have significant interior space, ventilation, and airflow; they do not resemble the intensely contained, unventilated heat of the ancient *tannur*, which is better approximated by a pizza oven. Accordingly, the various leniencies that apply to a *kira* apply to our ovens as well.

### Blech Practice

We now shift away from *shehiyah ve-chazarah* (leaving food on and returning it to the flame) and turn to the broader issues of cooking on *Shabbat* that will occupy the remainder of the chapter. Before doing so, it is worth drawing together the practical conclusions about the *blech* that emerged in the preceding discussion.

We have been operating throughout with the understanding that the modern *blech* serves as the practical equivalent of *garuf ve-katum* — the swept-out or ash-covered flame of the *gemara*. This understanding is not without complication. Both Rav Moshe Feinstein and Rav Ovadia Yosef, for different reasons, note at the outset that one could argue that no such precaution is required at all for modern stoves. Rav Moshe's reason is technical: the original rabbinic concern was that one might come to stir the existing coals — that is, to rearrange the fuel already burning. Our stoves require no such stirring; the only act that would increase heat is adjusting the knobs, which is effectively adding more fuel. Since the original *gezeirah* was never directed at adding fuel but at stirring ashes, it may not technically apply. Rav Ovadia's reason is more practical: a modern gas or electric flame burns steadily and does not require intervention the way wood fires do, so the underlying concern is simply less present.

Both authorities make this argument not in order to be lenient, but to preempt a symmetrical objection: if the modern stove does not technically require any precaution, then whatever we do may also not technically satisfy the requirement of the *gemara*. The *blech* does not actually begin to extinguish the flame the way ashes do; it merely somewhat reduces the heat. One could therefore argue that it does not replicate what the *gemara* intended by *garuf ve-katum*.

Given this background, there is a further question of what exactly the *blech* must accomplish in order to be valid. The *gemara*'s arrangement served two functions: it covered the heat source, and it provided a *heker* — a visible reminder — that would alert someone who approached to adjust the fire that something was different. On our stoves, the knobs are where one would come to make an adjustment, and the *blech* covers the burners, not the knobs. Some therefore argue that one must place something on the knobs themselves — removing them, taping over them, or otherwise marking them — to create the necessary *heker* at the point of potential adjustment.

The most stringent approach combines both elements: a single piece of metal that covers the burners and extends down over the knobs simultaneously, replicating both functions at once. However, most *poskim* and most common practice hold that covering the heat source alone is sufficient, reasoning that when one approaches the knobs to make an adjustment, one will still notice the *blech* covering the burners and that will serve as adequate *heker*. Rav Aharon Kotler is quoted by Rav Eider as holding that it is sufficient to remove the knobs or mark them with a *siman*, without covering the heat source at all.

This last position raises interesting practical questions. Appliances whose heat source is already enclosed — crockpots, for instance, or the glass-top stoves that are now common — present a particular challenge. If the heat source is already covered by the appliance's design, placing a further covering over it risks creating a problem of *hatmanah* (insulating in a heat-adding medium), particularly if the covering fits snugly on all sides. One resolution is to rely on the position that covering the knobs alone is sufficient. As for the glass-top stove where the burners are already behind glass, one may similarly mark or cover the controls and rely on that as the required *heker*.

Regarding *chazarah* — returning food to the fire on *Shabbat* — the *Ashkenazi* position in principle follows *Chananya* that once food is partially cooked (to one-half or one-third doneness), it may theoretically be left on a fully exposed flame before *Shabbat*. In practice, the *lechatchila* standard follows the more stringent *Sefardi* position: unless the food is at a stage where *mitztamek ve-ra lo* (further cooking would be detrimental), one should cover the flame. For *chazarah* itself, all authorities agree that the flame must be covered. *Lechatchila* one must hold the pot in hand and maintain intent to return it; *bedieved*, the *Mishnah Berurah* holds that one condition alone suffices, and placing it briefly on a counter may in some circumstances be equivalent to holding it. Taking food directly from the refrigerator on *Shabbat* morning and placing it on the fire is not permitted *lechatchila*; at minimum the food must be fully dry or fully cooked, and there must be no appearance of cooking (*lo mechzei ke-mevashel*). Placing such food on a hot plate where no one normally cooks is more permissible; placing it directly on a *blech* remains a concern for *Ashkenazim*, while some *Sefardi poskim* permit even a *blech* in that scenario.

One further creative device deserves mention: the *kedera blech*, sometimes called the *un-blech*, a flat vessel resembling a *blech* but with a thin sealed hollow interior filled with water or oil, designed to sit on the stovetop as a "pot" so that placing food on top of it constitutes *al gabei kli* (on top of a cooking vessel) rather than direct placement on a heat source, which some authorities consider *lo derech bishul*. Whether this device genuinely changes the experiential or legal reality compared to a standard *blech* depends on how one understands the underlying rationale, and the question is live among contemporary *poskim*.

## Eggs & Fish

### Egg Cooking Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין נוֹתְנִין בֵּיצָה בְּצַד הַמֵּיחַם בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּתְגַּלְגֵּל, וְלֹא יַפְקִיעֶנָּה בְּסוּדָרִין. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר. וְלֹא יַטְמִינֶנָּה בְּחוֹל וּבַאֲבַק דְּרָכִים בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּצָּלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In addition to the *halakhot* that deal with cooking on the fire on Shabbat, several related *halakhot* are discussed. The mishna says: **One may not place a** raw **egg next to an urn** full of hot water **so that it will roast slightly. And one may not** even **wrap it in cloths,** i.e., one may not heat the egg inside cloths that were heated in the sun. **And Rabbi Yosei permits** doing so in that case. **And,** similarly, **one may not insulate it in sand** or **in road dust** that was heated in the sun **so that it will roast.** Although there is no actual cooking with fire here, it is similar to cooking and the Sages issued a decree to prohibit doing so.

The *gemara* now turns to direct cooking issues. The *mishnah* states: *ain nosnin beitsa b'tzad hameicham bishvil she-titgalgel* — one may not place an egg alongside a hot water pot in order for it to hard-boil and cook. Even though the egg is not placed on the fire itself, its proximity to the heated vessel is sufficient to cook it, and this is forbidden. Similarly, *ve-lo yafki'enah be-sudarim* — one may not crack an egg and fry it on a cloth. The *gemara* understands this second case to be referring to a cloth heated by the sun, not by fire.

This brings into view the fundamental principle distinguishing *bishul chamah* — cooking by the sun — from cooking by fire. Biblically, cooking is defined by use of fire; cooking by sunlight alone does not constitute a biblical violation. However, something that has absorbed heat from the sun presents a rabbinic problem: absorbed heat is visually indistinguishable from absorbed heat derived from fire, since in either case one sees only a warm object without knowing its origin. Therefore, while the rabbis permit frying an egg directly on a sun-heated sidewalk — where the source is obvious — they do not permit frying it on a cloth that has absorbed heat from the sun, since that cloth could just as easily have been heated by a flame.

*Rabbi Yossi* disagrees and permits use of sun-derived absorbed heat as well. His position is the more permissive one, though it does not prevail in normative *halacha*.

One might ask whether the line between fire and sun is a purely formal legal distinction — cooking is definitionally a fire-related act — or whether it reflects the more functional principle that fire is the *normal* method of cooking while sun is not. The practical difference concerns novel heat sources. Rav Moshe Feinstein held that a microwave constitutes *bishul de-oraita* on the grounds that the definition is about normative cooking methods rather than fire per se. Many other *poskim* disagree, holding either that microwave is not yet normative enough, or that fire is simply the formal definitional criterion. A similar question arises with chemical heat packs used by travelers, which generate significant heat through an exothermic reaction without any flame whatsoever.

### Rav Yosef Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: גִּלְגֵּל מַאי? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: גִּלְגֵּל — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: One who violated the *halakha* in the mishna and **slightly roasted** an egg next to an urn, **what is** the ruling? **Rav Yosef said: One** who **slightly cooked** an egg is **liable to** bring **a sin-offering,** as he performed the act of cooking on Shabbat, which is prohibited by Torah law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: כׇּל שֶׁבָּא בְּחַמִּין מִלִּפְנֵי הַשַּׁבָּת — שׁוֹרִין אוֹתוֹ בְּחַמִּין בְּשַׁבָּת, וְכׇל שֶׁלֹּא בָּא בְּחַמִּין מִלִּפְנֵי הַשַּׁבָּת — מְדִיחִין אוֹתוֹ בְּחַמִּין בַּשַּׁבָּת. חוּץ מִן הַמָּלִיחַ יָשָׁן וְקוֹלְיָיס הָאִיסְפָּנִין, שֶׁהַדָּחָתָן זוֹ הִיא גְּמַר מְלַאכְתָּן. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Ravina, said: We also learned** something similar in the mishna: **Any** salted food item **that was** already **placed in hot water,** i.e., cooked, **before Shabbat, one may soak it in hot water** even **on Shabbat. And anything that was not placed in hot water before Shabbat, one may rinse it in hot water on Shabbat,** but may not soak it, **with the exception of old salted** fish or the **colias of the Spaniards [*kolyas ha'ispanin*]** fish, **for which rinsing** with hot water **itself is completion of the prohibited labor** of cooking. Once it is rinsed in hot water, it does not require any additional cooking. The same is true with regard to an egg that was slightly cooked. Since it thereby becomes edible, one who brought it to that state has violated the prohibition of cooking. The Gemara sums up: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is its meaning.

The *gemara* asks: *gilgel mai* — if one went ahead and cooked the egg by placing it against the hot pot, what is the status of that act? Is it a rabbinic violation or a biblical one? *Rav Yosef* answers: *gilgel chayav chatas* — one has incurred a *chatas* obligation, meaning it is a biblical transgression.

This is a striking ruling. The *mishnah* does not make this explicit, and the question was genuinely open: one could easily have read the prohibition as purely rabbinic, given that the egg was not placed directly on a flame. The *Ramban*, drawing on the *Yerushalmi*, holds a reasonable position that *bishul de-oraita* requires direct contact with a flame; cooking accomplished through indirect heat — even from a vessel that is boiling hot — is not a biblical act. What *Rav Yosef* does with such *gemara* passages is not entirely clear.

But *Rav Yosef*'s ruling establishes an important principle that most *Rishonim* accept against the *Ramban*: *bishul de-oraita* is possible even when the food is not placed directly over a flame. Both of the cases in the *mishnah* — the egg against the hot pot, and the salted fish rinsed with hot water from a *kli rishon* — share the characteristic that the cooking occurs without direct contact with fire. Yet *Rav Yosef* in both cases rules *chayav chatas*.

### Ein Bishul

The *gemara* supports *Rav Yosef*'s ruling with a proof from an earlier *mishnah* (38a): *kol she-ba be-chamin milifnei ha-Shabbos* — anything that was cooked before *Shabbat* — *shorin oso be-chamin be-Shabbos* — may be re-submerged in hot water on *Shabbat*. This is the principle of *ein bishul achar bishul*: there is no act of cooking after something has already been cooked. Once food is fully cooked, placing it in boiling water on *Shabbat* causes no violation. *Ve-chol shelo ba be-chamin milifnei ha-Shabbat* — but if it was not cooked before *Shabbat* — *madichin oso be-chamin be-Shabbos* — one may only rinse it with hot water, meaning pour hot water over it, but *aval* — one may not submerge it. Submersion in hot water would constitute cooking; pouring hot water merely to rinse or warm it does not.

### Salted Fish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִן הַמָּלִיחַ יָשָׁן וְקוֹלְיָיס הָאִיסְפָּנִין, שֶׁהַדָּחָתָן זוֹ הִיא גְּמַר מְלַאכְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **with the exception of old salted** fish or the **colias of the Spaniards [*kolyas ha'ispanin*]** fish, **for which rinsing** with hot water **itself is completion of the prohibited labor** of cooking.

The *mishnah* adds an exception: *chutz min ha-maliach yashan ve-kolyas ha-ispanin* — except for old salted fish and this *kolyas ha-ispanin*, which *Rashi* identifies as a type of fish (*dag she-korei tunina*), understood by some as a mackerel-type or salt-preserved fish. In these cases, *she-hadachatan zo hi gmar melachtan* — rinsing them with hot water constitutes the completion of their preparation (*gmar melachtan*), making them fit to eat. The *mishnah* implies that doing so would be a significant violation.

*Rav Yosef* confirms, in a later *mishnah* (145), that one who pours hot water on such fish is *chayav chatas* — incurs a biblical obligation. Together with the egg case, this establishes that *bishul de-oraita* applies even without direct flame contact, even potentially in a *kli sheni* (secondary vessel), and forms the basis for the category the *gemara* later develops of *kalei ha-bishul* — things that cook easily. The entire discussion about tea and the requirement of three removes (a *kli shlishi*) derives from this principle, since easily cooked substances may achieve *bishul de-oraita* status even in a vessel far removed from the original heat source.

### Gmar Melacha

The language of the *mishnah* — *gmar melachtan* — raises a further question. The phrase echoes *makeh be-patish*, the prohibition against performing the "final blow" that completes an object, even if that final act is not itself a *melacha*. Could it be that the prohibition on rinsing the salted fish is not actually *bishul* at all, but rather *makeh be-patish* — a *gmar melacha* in the sense of completing a food's preparation, even without any cooking? The *gemara* phrase seems to point in that direction.

The *Yerushalmi* indeed adopts this reading explicitly. It states that *makeh be-patish* applies to food preparation, meaning one can incur a biblical obligation by completing the process of making food edible through any act that finalizes it — not only through cooking. This creates a significant difficulty: all the categories of *bishul chamah* and indirect heating that we thought were rabbinic exemptions would seemingly be subject to *makeh be-patish* instead. The *Yerushalmi* cites several examples that do not involve heat at all — chopping, stuffing, and similar acts of food completion.

The *Bavli*, however, effectively ignores this framework. The *poskim* follow the *Bavli* and read both the egg case and the salted fish case as instances of *bishul* rather than *makeh be-patish*, establishing that *bishul de-oraita* is possible even without direct flame contact. Had the *Yerushalmi*'s position been accepted, the restrictions would be significantly more expansive. The *pshat* of the *mishnah* undeniably supports the *Yerushalmi*'s reading — it is difficult to characterize rinsing brine off salted fish as "cooking" in any conventional sense — but the *Bavli* and the *Rishonim* who follow it limit the ruling to cases involving hot water at *yad soledes bo* temperature and frame it as a form of *bishul*.

## Sun vs Fire

### Sun Heat Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן נוֹתְנִין תַּבְשִׁיל לְתוֹךְ הַבּוֹר בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיְּהֵא שָׁמוּר, וְאֶת הַמַּיִם הַיָּפִים בָּרָעִים בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּצָּנְנוּ, וְאֶת הַצּוֹנֵן בַּחַמָּה בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיֵּחַמּוּ — לֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא וְלָא רַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna according to the first *tanna*: **And one may not wrap** an egg **in cloths** that were heated by the sun in order to heat up the egg, and Rabbi Yosei permits doing so. **And** with regard to **that which we learned** in a mishna that **one may place cooked food into a pit** on Shabbat **to protect it** from the heat; **and** one may place **good,** potable **water into bad,** non-potable water **so that it will cool; and** one may put **cold water** out **in the sun to heat it,** the Gemara asks: **Let us say** that this mishna **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** in our mishna **and not** the opinion of **the Rabbis** as represented by the first *tanna* in the mishna. The Rabbis prohibited heating food with the heat of the sun.

The *gemara* turns now to the next case in the *mishnah*: *ve-lo yafki'enu be-sudarin* — do not fry an egg on a heated cloth. A later *mishnah* is cited for contrast: *notnin tavshil le-toch ha-bor bishvil she-yishtamer* — one may place a cooked dish into a pit to preserve it, or good water into poorer water to cool it, or — and this is the key point — *ve-es ha-tzonan ba-chamah bishvil she-yeichamu* — cold water may be placed in the sun in order to warm it. Direct sunlight is thus permitted for warming or heating.

This raises the question: if the rabbis permit placing water directly in the sun, does even *Rabbi Yossi* agree, or is this only *Rabbi Yossi*'s position? The *gemara* might suggest that direct sunlight is only permissible according to *Rabbi Yossi*.

### Rav Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בְּחַמָּה — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי. בְּתוֹלְדוֹת הָאוּר — כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּתוֹלְדוֹת הַחַמָּה: מָר סָבַר גָּזְרִינַן תּוֹלְדוֹת הַחַמָּה אַטּוּ תּוֹלְדוֹת הָאוּר, וּמָר סָבַר לָא גָּזְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** With regard to heating food **in the sun** itself, **everyone agrees that one is permitted** to place food in the sun to heat it, as it is certainly neither fire nor a typical form of cooking. Likewise, **with derivatives of fire,** i.e., objects that were heated by fire, **everyone agrees that it is prohibited** to heat food with them, as heating with them is tantamount to heating with fire itself. **Where they argue** is with regard to heating **with derivatives of the sun,** i.e., objects heated with the heat of the sun. **This Sage,** who represents the opinion of the Rabbis, **holds** that **we issue a decree** prohibiting a person to heat with **derivatives of the sun due to derivatives of fire,** which are prohibited. People have no way of knowing how the cooking vessel was heated. If the Sages permit the use of objects heated in the sun, people will come to permit use of objects heated by fire as well. **And this Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that **we do not issue a decree.** Even though it is prohibited to heat with derivatives of fire, heating with derivatives of the sun is permitted.

*Rav Nachman* clarifies: *bachamah dekuli alma lo pligi deshari* — regarding direct sunlight, all authorities agree that it is permitted. *Be-toldos ha-ur* — regarding something derived from fire-generated heat — *dekuli alma lo pligi de-asir* — all agree that it is forbidden. This is the case of the egg placed against the hot water pot: heating via the derivative of fire is universally prohibited, and as *Rav Yosef* ruled, can even constitute a biblical transgression. *Ki pligi* — the dispute between the rabbis and *Rabbi Yossi* — *be-toldos chamah*: regarding something that has absorbed heat derived from the sun. Biblically this is entirely permitted; the rabbinic concern is that absorbed solar heat is indistinguishable from absorbed heat derived from fire. *Mar savar gazrinan toldos chamah atu toldos ha-ur* — the rabbis hold that we decree against using sun-derived absorbed heat lest it be confused with fire-derived absorbed heat. *Ve-Rabbi Yossi lo gazrinan* — *Rabbi Yossi* declines to make such a decree, and permits anything deriving ultimately from the sun, directly or indirectly.

### Hatmanah Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יַטְמִינֶנָּה בְּחוֹל וּבַאֲבַק דְּרָכִים בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתִּצָּלֶה: וְלִיפְלוֹג נָמֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּהָא! רַבָּה אָמַר: גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יַטְמִין בְּרֶמֶץ. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמֵּזִיז עָפָר מִמְּקוֹמוֹ. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ עָפָר תִּיחוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And one may not insulate it in sand** or **in road dust** that was heated in the sun. The Gemara asks: **And let Rabbi Yosei disagree with this** *halakha* **as well.** If he holds that one is permitted to cook on Shabbat using objects heated by the sun, the same should apply with regard to sand. The Gemara cites two answers. **Rabba said:** Rabbi Yosei agrees with the opinion of the Rabbis in this case. The Sages issued a **decree** in this case due to concern **lest** one come **to insulate** it **in hot ashes,** which is certainly prohibited, if he is permitted to insulate food in sand or road dust. Insulating in sand and insulating in hot ashes appear to be very similar. **Rav Yosef said:** Rabbi Yosei prohibits it in this case **because** when insulating it in the sand, **he displaces dirt.** It is as if he dug a hole in the sand, which is prohibited. The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the answers proposed by Rabba and Rav Yosef? Apparently, the two answers lead to the same practical conclusion. The Gemara answers: **There is** a practical difference **between them** in the case of **loose earth.** Loose earth does not require digging a hole. According to Rav Yosef's explanation, there is no reason to prohibit insulating food in loose earth, as displacing loose earth involves no prohibition. However, if the decree was issued lest one insulate an egg in hot ashes, then it applies even in the case of loose earth.

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-lo yatminenu ba-chol u-ve-avak derachim* — one may not bury an egg in hot sand or the dust of pathways in order to fry it (*bishvil she-titzaleh*). Here, strikingly, *Rabbi Yossi* does not disagree. The *gemara* asks: *ve-liflog nami Rabbi Yossi beha* — why does *Rabbi Yossi* not also permit this? After all, sand heated by the sun seems like a straightforward case of *toldos chamah*, and *Rabbi Yossi* permits *toldos chamah*.

*Rabbah* answers: *gezeirah shema yatmin be-remetz* — this is a decree lest one come to bury food in ashes. This introduces the separate category of *hatmanah* — the act of surrounding food with material that will retain or add heat in order to keep it warm. The sages prohibited *hatmanah* in heat-adding materials even before *Shabbat*, for a reason parallel to the prohibition against leaving food on an uncovered flame: just as one might come to stir the coals of an open fire, so too if one buries food in hot ashes before *Shabbat*, one might come to stir those ashes on *Shabbat* to raise the heat. Once this prohibition was established for ashes, it was extended to any material that adds heat. Hot sand adds heat, and although one cannot stir sand to raise its temperature, the category is treated uniformly — *lo pelug*.

Even *Rabbi Yossi*, who rejects the decree against *toldos chamah* when it comes to heated cloths and similar surfaces, agrees that the rabbinic category of *hatmanah* — burying in heat-adding substances — applies. Frying an egg on a cloth involves placing the egg *on* a surface; burying it in sand is categorically different. *Rabbi Yossi* accepts that the *gezeirah* of *hatmanah* applies to burial in heat-retaining or heat-adding material, even when the ultimate heat source is the sun.

*Rav Yosef* offers an alternative explanation for why the rabbis prohibited burying the egg in sand: *mipnei she-meiziz afar mi-mekomoh* — because one is moving the dirt. *Rashi* understands this as a concern of *chofer guma* — making a hole, which relates to the *melacha* of *boneh* or *choresh*. *Tosafot* understands it as a *muktzeh* concern — moving dirt that was set aside from use. The practical difference between *Rabbah* and *Rav Yosef* is the case of loose, pre-dug earth (*afar tichuach*): in that case there is no concern about making a hole and arguably no *muktzeh* issue, but there remains the *hatmanah* concern.

### Rabbi Yose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מְגַלְגְּלִין בֵּיצָה עַל גַּבֵּי גַּג רוֹתֵחַ, וְאֵין מְגַלְגְּלִין בֵּיצָה עַל גַּבֵּי סִיד רוֹתֵחַ. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יַטְמִין בְּרֶמֶץ — לֵיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמֵּזִיז עָפָר מִמְּקוֹמוֹ, לִיגְזַר? — סְתָם גַּג לֵית בֵּיהּ עָפָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from that which was taught in a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One may slightly roast an egg on a hot rooftop** heated by the sun; **however, one may not slightly roast an egg on top of boiling limestone. Granted,** this works out well **according to** the opinion of **the one who said** that insulating an egg in sand is prohibited due to a **decree lest he come insulate** it **in hot ashes. There is no** reason **to issue a decree** on a hot rooftop, as it is not at all similar to hot ashes. **However, according to the** opinion of the one **who said** that the reason is **because he is displacing dirt, let him issue a decree** and prohibit warming an egg on the rooftop as well because there is sometimes dirt on the roof. The Gemara answers: This is not difficult because, **in general, a rooftop does not have dirt,** and there is no reason to issue a decree in uncommon cases.

The *gemara* then cites a proof from a *baraita* in the name of Rabban Gamliel: *megalgelin beitzah al gabei gag roteiach* — one may fry an egg on a boiling hot roof. But *ein megalgelin beitzah al gabei sid roteiach* — one may not do so on boiling hot plaster, which has been heated by fire in the lime-burning process. The *gemara* assumes the roof's heat is from the sun (*toldos chamah*), while the plaster's heat is from fire (*toldos ha-ur*).

The *gemara* notes: this works well according to *Rabbah*, who explains *Rabbi Yossi*'s silence by the sand case as a *hatmanah* concern. Burying in sand is *hatmanah*; placing an egg on a flat hot roof is not *hatmanah* at all. So we understand why even *Rabbi Yossi* would allow the roof: there is no *hatmanah* issue there, and he has no objection to *toldos chamah* in general. But according to *Rav Yosef*, whose concern is the moving of dirt, why would the roof be unproblematic? If the roofs of that era sometimes had dirt on them, placing an egg there could involve moving dirt or creating a hole. The *gemara* dismisses this: *stam gagot leit behu afar* — ordinary roofs do not have dirt on them, so the concern does not arise.

## Tiberias Waters

### Pipe Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁעָשׂוּ אַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא וְהֵבִיאוּ סִילוֹן שֶׁל צוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ אַמָּה שֶׁל חַמִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates **a story about the people of** the city of **Tiberias, and they ran a cold-water pipe [*silon*] through a canal of hot** water from the Tiberias hot springs.

The *mishnah* relates: *ma'aseh she-asu anshei Tiveria* — there was an episode involving the people of Tiberias. *Ve-hevi'u silon shel tzonan le-toch amah shel chamim* — they brought a pipe carrying cold water into a channel of hot water. The hot water in that channel derived from *chamei Tiveria*, the famous hot springs of Tiberias.

The hot springs of Tiberias are categorized as *toldos chamah* rather than *toldos ha-ur*. This requires clarification: note that the word here is *ur* — *alef vav shuruk* — meaning fire (cognate with *Ur Kasdim*), not *or* meaning light. *Toldos ha-ur* designates heat derived from flame; *toldot ha-chamah* designates heat derived from any non-flame source, including geothermal heat. Since the Tiberian hot springs are not heated by a flame, they fall into the *chamah* category regardless of their actual geological origin. Biblically, heating water via *toldos chamah* is entirely permitted; rabbinically, the concern about visual indistinguishability from fire-derived heat makes it problematic.

The people of Tiberias ran this pipe presumably before *Shabbat* — *erev Shabbos* — threading it through the hot spring water so that the cold water inside would be warmed by the time *Shabbat* arrived.

### Water Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם חֲכָמִים: אִם בְּשַׁבָּת — כְּחַמִּים שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, וַאֲסוּרִין בִּרְחִיצָה וּבִשְׁתִיָּהּ. אִם בְּיוֹם טוֹב — כְּחַמִּים שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וַאֲסוּרִין בִּרְחִיצָה, וּמוּתָּרִין בִּשְׁתִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Rabbis said to them: If** the water passed through **on Shabbat,** its legal status is **like** that of **hot water that was heated on Shabbat, and** the water is **prohibited** both **for bathing and for drinking. And if** the water passed through **on a Festival, then it is prohibited** for **bathing but permitted for drinking.** On Festivals, one is even permitted to boil water on actual fire for the purposes of eating and drinking.

The sages responded with considerable strictness: *im be-Shabbat* — if this was done on *Shabbat* — *ke-chamim she-huchmu be-Shabbos* — the water must be treated as if it were heated directly on *Shabbat* on a fire. *Ve-asurin be-rechitzah u-vi-shtiyah* — it is forbidden for bathing and forbidden for drinking, and remains so until *Motzei Shabbos*. *Im be-Yom Tov* — if done on *Yom Tov* — *ke-chamim she-huchmu be-Yom Tov* — it is treated as water heated on *Yom Tov*. Since heating water on *Yom Tov* is permitted for drinking purposes, it is *assur be-rechitzah u-muttar be-shtiyah* — forbidden for bathing but permitted for drinking.

The prohibition on using water for bathing even when heated on *Yom Tov*, despite bathing being a legitimate bodily need, is a matter the *gemara* will address in greater detail. For now, the *mishnah* establishes it as given.

What is particularly striking about this ruling is not merely the prohibition against the act itself, but the strictness applied *bedieved* — retroactively. If they laid the pipe *erev Shabbos*, the act of heating itself occurred before *Shabbat* began. Ordinarily one might expect the penalty to be less severe. Yet the sages applied the same status to this water as to water heated on *Shabbat* itself.

### Heat Addition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מִמַּעֲשֶׂה שֶׁעָשׂוּ אַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא וְאָסְרִי לְהוּ רַבָּנַן בָּטְלָה הַטְמָנָה בְּדָבָר הַמּוֹסִיף הֶבֶל, וַאֲפִילּוּ מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **From** this **action performed by the people of Tiberias and the** fact that the **Sages prohibited them** from using the water, the conclusion is that the practice of **insulating** a pot **in something that increases the heat** over the course of Shabbat was **abolished** on Shabbat. **And** not only is it prohibited to do so on Shabbat itself, but it is also prohibited **while it is still day** before Shabbat.

*Rav Chisda* draws out the broader principle: *batlah hatmanah be-davar ha-mosif hevel va-afilu mi-boad yom* — the prohibition of *hatmanah* in a substance that adds heat applies even before *Shabbat* begins, even when done while it is still day. This is the key distinction within the laws of *hatmanah*: there are materials that merely *preserve* heat — wrapping food in towels to keep it warm — and materials that *add* heat. Preserving heat is permitted before *Shabbat* and has its own rules on *Shabbat* itself. Adding heat is forbidden in any circumstance, even *erev Shabbos*, because of the concern that one will come to stir the heat-adding material.

The Tiberian pipe, conceptually understood as a form of *hatmanah* — burying the water pipe within the hot spring water that surrounds and adds heat to it — thus violated the prohibition even though it was established before *Shabbat*. *Tosafot* notes that the prohibition of *hatmanah* in heat-adding substances *erev Shabbos* is stated explicitly in later *mishnayot*. The *chiddush* of this passage, in *Tosafot*'s reading, is therefore the *bedieved* ruling: not merely that the act was prohibited, but that one may not benefit from the water afterward, just as one who left food on an open flame in violation may not eat from it on *Shabbat*.

### Ulla & Nachman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: הֲלָכָה כְּאַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: כְּבָר תַּבְרִינְהוּ אַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא לְסִילוֹנַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the people of Tiberias. Rav Naḥman said to him: The people of Tiberias have already broken their pipes.** Even they reconsidered their position.

*Ulla* then stakes out a surprising position: *halacha ke-anshei Teverya* — the *halacha* follows the people of Tiberias. The very people whom the *mishnah* depicts as having been corrected by the sages, *Ulla* retrospectively vindicates. Whether his reasoning is that the pipe through water does not constitute *hatmanah* in the technical sense, or that the ruling follows *Rabbi Yossi* regarding *toldos chamah*, is not entirely clear. But he holds that the practice was in fact permissible.

*Rav Nachman* responds sharply: *kvar tavrunhu anshei Teveryah le-silonayehu* — the people of Tiberias have already broken their pipes. The practice does not stand, and the *halacha* is not like them. The upshot of the *gemara*'s discussion is that *toldos chamah* is rabbinically prohibited, *hatmanah* in a heat-adding substance is forbidden even *erev Shabbos*, and if either prohibition is violated, one may not benefit from the result even after the fact.

## Bathing Rules

### Pre-Heated Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁעָשׂוּ אַנְשֵׁי טְבֶרְיָא: מַאי רְחִיצָה? אִילֵּימָא רְחִיצַת כׇּל גּוּפוֹ — אֶלָּא חַמִּין שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת הוּא דַּאֲסוּרִין, הָא חַמִּין שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת מוּתָּרִין? וְהָתַנְיָא: חַמִּין שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, לְמָחָר רוֹחֵץ בָּהֶן פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו, אֲבָל לֹא כׇּל גּוּפוֹ! אֶלָּא פָּנָיו יָדָיו וְרַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna with regard to the **incident,** which related **what the people of Tiberias did,** that the legal status of water that was heated in the Tiberias hot springs is like that of water heated on Shabbat, and it is prohibited for use in bathing. The Gemara clarifies this matter: **What** type of **bathing** is this? **If you say** that it is referring to **bathing one's entire body,** that is difficult. That would indicate that only **water heated on Shabbat is prohibited** for use in bathing one's entire body; however, bathing one's entire body in **hot water heated before Shabbat is permitted.** That cannot be. **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **hot water that was heated on Shabbat eve, one may** use **it the next day** to **wash his face, his hands, and his feet** incrementally; **however, not** to wash **his entire body? Rather,** it must be that the bathing prohibited in the mishna with water heated on Shabbat is, in fact, washing **his face, his hands, and his feet.**

The *mishnah*'s ruling — that the Tiberian water, treated as water heated on *Shabbat*, is forbidden for bathing — now raises a question of definition. *Mai rechitzah* — what kind of bathing does the *mishnah* mean to prohibit? *Ilima rechitzas kol gufo* — if it means bathing one's whole body — then the implication of the *mishnah* would be that water heated before *Shabbat* is permitted for bathing the whole body. But a *baraita* contradicts this: *chamin she-huchmu me-erev Shabbos*, water heated before *Shabbat*, may only be used the next day for washing one's face, hands, and feet — *le-machar rochetz ba-hen panav yadav ve-raglav* — but not one's whole body — *aval lo kol gufo*. Pre-heated water is already insufficient for full-body bathing.

### Bathhouse Concern

The reason for this restriction is the famous *gezeirat merchatza'ot* — the rabbinic decree arising from the bathhouses. Since Roman bathhouses commonly heated water on *Shabbat* itself and the activity of bathing in hot water was pervasive, the sages feared that permitting full-body bathing even in pre-heated water would make it impossible to distinguish permissible from prohibited cases. The next time one visited the bathhouse, one might unknowingly — or carelessly — use water heated on *Shabbat*, reasoning that what one did at home was also fine. The decree therefore restricts even clearly permissible pre-heated water to partial washing — face, hands, and feet.

Returning to the Tiberian case: when the *mishnah* says that the water is "like water heated on *Shabbat*" and therefore forbidden for bathing, it cannot mean that full-body bathing is forbidden, since that is already forbidden even with pre-heated water. It must therefore mean the prohibition extends even to washing one's face, hands, and feet — the partial washing that would normally be permitted with pre-heated water is also forbidden, because the water is treated as if it were heated on *Shabbat* itself.

### Yom Tov Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בְּיוֹם טוֹב, כְּחַמִּים שֶׁהוּחַמּוּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וַאֲסוּרִים בִּרְחִיצָה וּמוּתָּרִים בִּשְׁתִיָּה. לֵימָא תְּנַן סְתָמָא כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? דִּתְנַן, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יָחֵם אָדָם חַמִּים לְרַגְלָיו אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן רְאוּיִים לִשְׁתִיָּה, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** However, if so, **say** the **latter clause** of the mishna: **On a Festival,** the legal status of the water is **like** that of **water that was heated** by fire **on a Festival, and it is prohibited for bathing and permitted for drinking.** Even on a Festival, washing one's face, hands, and feet is prohibited with this hot water. If so, **let us say** that **we learned the unattributed mishna in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai. As we learned** in a mishna, **Beit Shammai say: A person may not heat water for his feet** on a Festival **unless it is** also **fit for drinking, and Beit Hillel permit** doing so. According to Beit Hillel, it is permitted to heat water on a Festival for the purpose of washing one's feet. According to the proposed interpretation of the term bathing in the mishna, as referring to washing one's face, hands, and feet, our mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai. This is problematic, as the halakhic opinion of Beit Shammai is rejected and only rarely cited in an unattributed mishna.

This raises a further complication. The *mishnah* states that if the Tiberian water was heated on *Yom Tov*, it is treated as water heated on *Yom Tov*, and in that case: *assur be-rechitzah u-muttar be-shtiyah* — forbidden for bathing but permitted for drinking. The question is: what type of bathing is forbidden? If "forbidden for bathing" means even washing hands and feet, then the *mishnah* is saying that one cannot heat water on *Yom Tov* even for washing hands and feet.

### Beit Shammai

This leads the *gemara* to ask: *leima tnan stama ke-Veit Shammai* — is the *stam mishnah* here following the position of *Beit Shammai*? For a *mishnah* states: *Beit Shammai* holds *lo yachom adam chamim le-raglav* — one may not heat water for one's feet on *Yom Tov* — *ela im ken reuyim li-shtiyah* — unless one is heating it for the purpose of drinking. According to *Beit Shammai*, heating water on *Yom Tov* is permitted only for drinking, not for any washing purpose. *Beit Hillel* permits it. If our *mishnah* prohibits using *Yom Tov*-heated water even for washing hands and feet, it would apparently align with *Beit Shammai*'s position — which is a concern, since *halacha* does not generally follow *Beit Shammai*.

### Unresolved

This creates a genuine difficulty in reconciling the categories: water heated before *Shabbat* is permitted for hands and face but not for the whole body; water heated on *Yom Tov* is apparently not permitted even for hands and face, which would place the *stam mishnah* in the position of *Beit Shammai*. What category of water is there that is permitted for full-body bathing? And how do we account for the *Yom Tov* prohibition on even partial washing in a way that does not conflict with *Beit Hillel*?

These questions remain unresolved at the end of the *daf* and will be taken up in the next session, which will also address the broader practical question of whether and under what circumstances one may bathe in hot water on *Yom Tov* altogether.
