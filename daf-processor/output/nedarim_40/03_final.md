# Nedarim 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bikur Cholim

### No Limit to Bikur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: בִּיקּוּר חוֹלִים אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר. מַאי ״אֵין לָהּ שִׁיעוּר״? סָבַר רַב יוֹסֵף לְמֵימַר אֵין שִׁיעוּר לְמַתַּן שְׂכָרָהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְכׇל מִצְוֹת מִי יֵשׁ שִׁיעוּר לְמַתַּן שְׂכָרָן? וְהָא תְּנַן: הֱוֵי זָהִיר בְּמִצְוָה קַלָּה כְּבַחֲמוּרָה, שֶׁאֵין אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ מַתַּן שְׂכָרָן שֶׁל מִצְוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita*: The mitzva of **visiting the ill has no** fixed **measure.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of: **Has no** fixed **measure? Rav Yosef thought to say: There is no** fixed **measure for the granting of its reward. Abaye said to him: And do all** other **mitzvot have** a fixed **measure for the granting of their reward? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Avot* 2:1): **Be** as **meticulous in** the observance of **a minor mitzva as a major one, as you do not know the granting of reward for mitzvot.**

The Gemara on 39b opens with a *baraisa*: *bikur cholim ein lah shiur* — there is no limit to the obligation of *bikur cholim*. The immediate question is: what precisely does this mean?

Rav Yosef proposed that *ein lah shiur* refers to the reward — that the reward for *bikur cholim* is limitless. Abaye pushes back sharply: is *bikur cholim* unique in this respect? Is there any *mitzvah* whose reward is capped? The Mishnah itself teaches *hevei zahir bemitzvah kalah kebachamurah she'ein atah yodeia matan secharan shel mitzvot* — be as scrupulous about a light *mitzvah* as about a weighty one, because we do not know the reward for the various *mitzvot*. That very formulation implies that all *mitzvot* may have unlimited or at least unknowable reward. To single out *bikur cholim* would imply the others do not share this quality, which is untenable.

### Gadol Etzel Katan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲפִילּוּ גָּדוֹל אֵצֶל קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** There is no fixed measure for the disparity between the ill person and his visitor, as **even a prominent** person pays a visit **to a lowly** person and should not say that doing so is beneath a person of his standing.

Abaye therefore offers a different interpretation: *afilu gadol etzel katan* — the statement that there is no limit to *bikur cholim* means that there is no threshold of social status that exempts one from performing it. A person of great standing cannot say that it would be beneath his dignity to visit someone of a lower station in life. The obligation admits of no such calculus. One sees this instinct at work during *shiva* calls as well — an important person does not decline to make a condolence call simply because the mourner is less prominent. The same holds for *bikur cholim*.

### Multiple Visits & Healing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים בְּיוֹם. אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא: כׇּל הַמְבַקֵּר חוֹלֶה, נוֹטֵל אֶחָד מִשִּׁשִּׁים בְּצַעֲרוֹ. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן לִיעַלּוּן שִׁיתִּין וְלוֹקְמוּהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּעִישּׂוּרְיָיתָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי, וּבְבֶן גִּילוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There is no fixed measure for the number of times that one should visit the ill, as **even one hundred times a day** is appropriate. **Rav Aḥa bar Ḥanina said: Anyone who visits an ill person takes** from him **one-sixtieth of his suffering.** The Sages **said to him: If so, let sixty** people **enter** to visit him, **and stand him up,** and restore him to health. Rav Aḥa bar Ḥanina **said to them:** It is **like the tenths of the school of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who said that each of one's daughters inherits one-tenth of his possessions. His intent was that each daughter would receive one-tenth of the remainder after the previous daughter took her portion. Here too, each visitor takes from the ill person one-sixtieth of the suffering that remains, and consequently a degree of suffering will always remain with the ill person. **Furthermore,** visiting is effective in easing the suffering of the ill person only when the visitor is **one** born under the **same constellation** as the ill person.

Rava offers yet another reading of *ein lah shiur*: *afilu me'ah pe'amim bayom* — even one hundred times in a day. Having visited once, a person cannot simply check the obligation off his list. If the sick person needs human contact and no one else is providing it, the obligation to return remains. This parallels the formulation used elsewhere — *hakeim takem imo afilu me'ah pe'amim* — where the same act must be repeated as many times as circumstances demand.

The Gemara then cites Rav Acha bar Chanina: *kol mevaker choleh notel echad meshishim betza'aro* — whoever visits a sick person removes one-sixtieth of his suffering. (Some versions read *michol'yo*, of his illness, rather than *betza'aro*, of his anguish — a meaningful distinction, as the latter implies the visitor absorbs the suffering rather than eliminating it.) The Gemara immediately raises the obvious objection: if this is so, why not simply bring sixty visitors and cure the patient entirely? The answer is that this formula operates like the rule of *issur nechasim* from the house of Rebbi — in which each daughter's portion is calculated as a tenth of the remainder after the previous portions have been taken, rather than a flat tenth of the original estate. Similarly, each visitor removes a sixtieth of what remains of the suffering, not a sixtieth of the original whole, so that sixty visitors in sequence would not bring the patient to zero. Even if all sixty come simultaneously, the result is calculated as though they came sequentially and divided equally among them. Moreover, the effect applies only when the visitor is a *ben gilo* — someone of a cognate *mazal* — who possesses some sympathetic or harmonizing influence over the sick person.

### Rav Chelbo & R. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חֶלְבּוֹ חֲלַשׁ, נְפַק אַכְרֵיז רַב כָּהֲנָא: רַב חֶלְבּוֹ בְּאֵישׁ. לָא אִיכָּא דְּקָא אָתֵי. אָמַר לְהוּ: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתַלְמִיד אֶחָד מִתַּלְמִידֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁחָלָה. לֹא נִכְנְסוּ חֲכָמִים לְבַקְּרוֹ, וְנִכְנָס רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְבַקְּרוֹ, וּבִשְׁבִיל שֶׁכִּיבְּדוּ וְרִיבְּצוּ לְפָנָיו, חָיָה. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, הֶחֱיִיתַנִי. יָצָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְדָרַשׁ: כׇּל מִי שֶׁאֵין מְבַקֵּר חוֹלִים כְּאִילּוּ שׁוֹפֵךְ דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Ḥelbo fell ill. Rav Kahana went out and announced:** **Rav Ḥelbo fell ill. There was no one who came** to visit him. Rav Kahana **said** to the Sages: **Didn't the incident involving one of the students of Rabbi Akiva who became sick** transpire **in that manner?** In that case, **the Sages did not enter to visit him, and Rabbi Akiva entered to visit him** and instructed his students to care for him. **And since they swept and sprinkled water on the** dirt **floor before** the sick student, **he recovered.** The student **said to** Rabbi Akiva: **My teacher, you revived me. Rabbi Akiva went out and taught:** With regard to **anyone who does not visit the ill, it is as though he is spilling blood,** as it could be that the sick person has no one to care for him. If there are no visitors, no one will know his situation and therefore no one will come to his aid.

Rav Chelbo fell ill. Rav Kahana went and made a public announcement — *Rav Chelbo bish*, Rav Chelbo is sick — but no one came to visit. Rav Kahana then rebuked the people: did they not know the story of one of Rabbi Akiva's students who fell ill and the Sages did not come to visit him? Rabbi Akiva himself came, and because he honored the student — sweeping the floor and settling the dust of the room — *chaya*, the student recovered. The student said to him: *Rebbi, hechiyasani* — you have brought me back to life. Rabbi Akiva then went out and taught publicly: *kol mi she'eino mevaker* — anyone who does not visit the sick is as though he is spilling blood, since the act of visiting sustains the sick person's life.

It is worth noting what Rav Kahana's rebuke conspicuously does not record: that the people, having heard the *mussar*, then went and prayed for Rav Chelbo or visited him. The moral instruction stands on its own, whether or not it was successful in motivating action.

### Prayer & End-of-Life

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: כׇּל הַמְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה גּוֹרֵם לוֹ שֶׁיִּחְיֶה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה גּוֹרֵם לוֹ שֶׁיָּמוּת. מַאי גְּרָמָא? אִילֵּימָא כׇּל הַמְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה מְבַקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים שֶׁיִּחְיֶה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין מְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה מְבַקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים שֶׁיָּמוּת. שֶׁיָּמוּת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין מְבַקֵּר חוֹלֶה, אֵין מְבַקֵּשׁ עָלָיו רַחֲמִים, לֹא שֶׁיִּחְיֶה וְלֹא שֶׁיָּמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael** to Babylonia **he said: Anyone who visits the ill causes that he will live, and anyone who does not visit the ill causes that he will die.** The Gemara asks: In **what** way are his actions **the cause** of that result? **If we say** that **anyone who visits the ill pleads for mercy** from God **that he will live, and anyone who does not visit the ill pleads for mercy that he will die, does it enter your mind** that he would pray **that** the sick person **will die? Rather, anyone who does not visit the ill does not plead for mercy for him, neither that he will live nor that he will die.** Since he might have saved the sick person with prayers had he visited, his failure to visit is tantamount to causing his death.

When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported: *kol hamevaker chole gorem lo sheyichye* — whoever visits the sick causes him to live; *vechol mi she'eino mevaker es hachole yesh gorem lo sheyamus* — whoever does not visit causes him to die. The *Gemara* immediately scrutinizes the word *gorem*. Does it mean that the non-visitor actively prays for the patient's death? *Sheyamus salka deitach?* — can one seriously entertain that thought? Rather, the *Gemara* explains: one who does not visit simply does not pray — not for the patient's recovery, and not for his death. The passivity is the culpable act. By not being present, one never reaches the point of praying for the person, and therefore one bears some moral responsibility if the patient deteriorates.

This is a striking pivot in the Gemara's framing. The earlier passages — Rabbi Akiva sweeping the floor, Rav Acha bar Chanina's one-sixtieth principle — point to very concrete, practical dimensions of *bikur cholim*: attending to physical needs, providing human contact, emotional presence. Now the *stama deGemara* reframes the entire institution around prayer. One might ask why the Gemara makes this shift. The answer is left implicit, but the effect is the same: whether through practical care or *tefilah*, presence sustains the sick person, and absence — even if not malicious — is a form of abandonment.

At this point there is a remarkable *Ran* that carries great significance for discussions of medical ethics. The *Ran* begins: *nireh be'einai dehachi ka'amar* — it seems to me that this is what the Gemara is driving at. He writes that *pe'amim shetzarich levakesh rachamim al hachole sheyamus* — there are times when one ought to pray for a sick person to die. Specifically, when someone is *mitzta'er* greatly — in severe suffering — and *ve'i efshar lo sheyichye* — there is no possibility of recovery — then the most one can wish for this person is a swift and painless death, not a prolonged dying.

The *Ran* grounds this in the famous passage from *Perek Hanose* about Rabbi's maidservant. She observed that Rebbi was in great distress — having to go to the bathroom repeatedly and replace his *tefillin* each time — while his students continued to pray fervently for his recovery. She prayed: *yehi ratzon sheyachofu elyonim es hatachtonim* — may it be the will that the upper spheres overcome the lower spheres. The students' prayers were the lower spheres; clearly in heaven, it was Rebbi's time to die. She effectively prayed that his death not be prolonged by the students' well-intentioned but ultimately misdirected *tefilot*.

The *Ran* then applies this to the *Gemara*'s language: one who visits the sick person helps even through prayer — *afilu lehachyos*, even toward recovery if that is possible — *mipnei shehi tefilla yoser mo'eles*, because prayer offered in the presence of the patient is more effective. But one who does not visit fails on both counts: not only does he fail to pray for recovery when recovery is possible, but *afilu heicha de-ika lahna be-mituta* — even where the more modest prayer is called for, praying that the person should be able to die without prolonged suffering — *afilu ha nami lo mahanya*, even that prayer will not be effective, because it was never offered.

The *Ran* thus identifies a category of prayer for death that is not only permitted but at times obligatory — not a prayer that the person should die in a malicious sense, but a prayer that death, when it is the right outcome, should come gently and without delay. This has become foundational in discussions of end-of-life *halacha*. The central orienting principle is the distinction between prolonging life and prolonging death — one does not wish to stand in the way of a natural process of dying, but equally one does not interrupt interventions that are genuinely sustaining life. The *Ran* articulates this with precision: when recovery is not possible, the most one can do is pray that the passing be swift and merciful. The further question of whether a patient has personal autonomy in these decisions is a distinct and layered discussion. Rav Moshe Feinstein found a kind of indirect approach, arguing that when a patient's wishes are overridden, the resulting anguish can itself worsen the medical condition — so that respecting the patient's will is often the medically as well as halakhically indicated course. This argument does not translate easily to cases where the patient is unconscious, which is precisely where the hardest questions arise. The status of a *goses* — one already in the active process of dying — is a related but distinct category, involving a traditional prohibition against interfering with the natural process at that stage.

### Rava's Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא, יוֹמָא קַדְמָאָה דְּחָלֵישׁ אָמַר לְהוֹן: לָא תִּיגַלּוֹ לְאִינִישׁ, דְּלָא לִתְּרַע מַזָּלֵיהּ. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ אָמַר לְהוֹן: פּוּקוּ וְאַכְרִיזוּ בְּשׁוּקָא, דְּכׇל דְּסָנֵי לִי לִיחְדֵּי לִי, וּכְתִיב: ״בִּנְפֹל אוֹיִבְךָ אַל תִּשְׂמָח וְגוֹ׳״, וּדְרָחֵים לִי לִיבְעֵי עֲלַי רַחֲמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates with regard to **Rava:** On **the first day that he was ill, he** would **say to** his family: **Do not reveal to any person** that I am ill, so **that his luck not suffer. From this** point **forward,** when his situation deteriorated **he** would **say to them: Go and proclaim in the marketplace** that I am ill, **as** thereby **let all who hate me rejoice over my** distress, **and it is written: "Rejoice not when your enemy falls,** and let not your heart be glad when he stumbles; lest the Lord see it, and it displease Him, and He turn away His wrath from him" (Proverbs 24:17–18). **And let** all **who love me pray** that God have **mercy upon me.**

Rava's own practice regarding illness is instructive. On the first day he fell ill, he told those around him: *lo tiglu le-inshei* — do not tell people, *de-lo litra mazali* — so that his *mazal* should not be weakened. There is a sense that publicizing an illness makes it more of a concrete reality, and Rava wished to leave open the possibility that it would pass quickly and quietly. But once it became clear the illness was not passing, he reversed course entirely: *puku v'akhrizu b'shuka* — go and announce it in the marketplace. Two reasons converge here. First, *de'chol de'sani li richdu li* — those who hate him will rejoice at the news of his illness. Far from being a problem, Rava welcomes this, because the verse says *binfol oyvecha al tismach... pen yireh Hashem v'ra b'einav v'heshiv me'alav apo* — do not rejoice when your enemy falls, lest God see your rejoicing and remove His anger from your enemy. If Rava's enemies rejoice at his illness, God's attention will shift toward them and away from Rava, working to his benefit. Second, *u'derachim li libai rachamei* — those who love him will pray for him. Either way Rava gains. This passage carries a layer of biographical texture: Rava was known to have many detractors in *Mahuza*, a cosmopolitan city where he was an outspoken rabbinic authority whose rebuke was not always welcomed. His equanimity in invoking his enemies' anticipated pleasure is itself remarkable.

### Rewards for Visiting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַמְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה נִיצּוֹל מִדִּינָהּ שֶׁל גֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַשְׁרֵי מַשְׂכִּיל אֶל דָּל בְּיוֹם רָעָה יְמַלְּטֵהוּ ה׳״, אֵין דַּל אֶלָּא חוֹלֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִדַּלָּה יְבַצְּעֵנִי״. וְאִם בִּיקֵּר מָה שְׂכָרוֹ בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה — ״ה׳ יִשְׁמְרֵהוּ וִיחַיֵּהוּ וְאֻשַּׁר בָּאָרֶץ וְאַל תִּתְּנֵהוּ בְּנֶפֶשׁ אֹיְבָיו״. ״ה׳ יִשְׁמְרֵהוּ״ — מִיֵּצֶר הָרָע, ״וִיחַיֵּהוּ״ — מִן הַיִּסּוּרִין, ״וְאֻשַּׁר בָּאָרֶץ״ — שֶׁיְּהוּ הַכֹּל מִתְכַּבְּדִין בּוֹ, ״וְאַל תִּתְּנֵהוּ בְּנֶפֶשׁ אֹיְבָיו״ — שֶׁיִּזְדַּמְּנוּ לוֹ רֵיעִים כְּנַעֲמָן שֶׁרִיפּוּ אֶת צָרַעְתּוֹ, וְאַל יִזְדַּמְּנוּ לוֹ רֵיעִים כִּרְחַבְעָם שֶׁחִילְּקוּ אֶת מַלְכוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: Anyone who visits the ill is spared from the judgment of Gehenna, as it is stated: "Happy is he that considers the poor; the Lord will deliver him in the day of evil"** (Psalms 41:2). In this verse, the term **poor [*dal*]** means **nothing other** than **ill, as it is stated** in the prayer of Hezekiah when he was ill: **"He will cut me off from the illness [*middalla*]"** (Isaiah 38:12). His reward is as it is written: **"The Lord will preserve him, and keep him alive, let him be called happy in the land; and deliver not You him unto the greed of his enemies"** (Psalms 41:3). He elaborates: **"The Lord will preserve him" from the evil inclination; "and keep him alive"** and spare him **from suffering; "let him be called happy in the land"** means that **everyone will be honored** from their association **with him; "and deliver not You him unto the greed of his enemies,"** so **that companions like** those who counseled **Naaman** to seek **a cure for his leprosy** from Elisha (II Kings 5:3) **will happen to** associate with **him,** and **companions like** those who counseled **Rehoboam** with advice that resulted in **the schism in his kingdom** (I Kings 12:6–19) **will not happen to** associate with **him.**

The Gemara then enumerates the rewards for *bikur cholim*. Rav teaches: *kol hamivakeir et hacholeh nitzol midina shel Gehennam* — whoever visits the sick is saved from the punishment of *Gehennam*. The proof-text is *ashrei maskil el dal* — praised is the one who attends to the *dal*, the downtrodden or the poor, who here is understood to refer to the sick person, *ein dal ela choleh*, as supported by the verse *midala yivtza'eni*. *Biyom ra'ah yimalteihu Hashem* — on the day of evil, God will save him — and *ein ra'ah ela Gehennam*, "evil" here denotes *Gehennam*.

The Gemara then asks: is being saved from *Gehennam* not reward enough? What about reward in this world? The verse continues: *Hashem yishmereihu v'ichayeihu v'ushar ba'aretz ve'al titneihu b'nefesh oyvav* — God will preserve him and give him life and bless him in the land and not deliver him into the hand of his enemies. Each phrase corresponds to an aspect of the *bikur cholim* experience. *Hashem yishmereihu* — God will protect him from the *yeitzer hara*. *V'ichayeihu* — God will preserve him from suffering. *V'ushar ba'aretz* — that all will honor him, which the *Maharsha* connects back to the earlier principle of *gadol etzel katan*: just as even the great visit the lowly, so too will the one who visits be honored in turn by all strata of society.

The phrase *ve'al titneihu b'nefesh oyvav* — and not deliver him into the hand of his enemies — is phrased in terms of *nefesh* rather than simply "enemies," to convey something about the quality of one's friendships. The Gemara says *sheyihiyu lo rei'im keNa'aman sheripu es tzaraso* — may he have friends like those of Na'aman who helped cure him of his affliction. This is precisely what *bikur cholim* embodies: being a friend who shows up when someone has a problem. Conversely, *ve'al yizdamnu lo rei'im keRechavam shechilku es hamalchus* — may he not have friends like those of Rechavam, whose advice led to the splitting of the kingdom. The *nefesh* language alludes to the deep connection between a person and his advisors, whose counsel can either sustain or destroy.

A *baraisa* expands on this: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches that if young people tell you to build and elders tell you to tear down, listen to the elders — because *binyan yeladim stirah*, the building of the young is ultimately a destruction, and *stiras zkeinim binyan*, the tearing down of the elders is ultimately a construction. The paradigm is Rechavam ben Shlomo, who listened to the young advisors who urged him to increase the people's burdens, with catastrophic consequences for the kingdom — while the elders' advice, which might have seemed like a weakening of royal authority, would in fact have preserved it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אִם יֹאמְרוּ לָךְ יְלָדִים ״בְּנֵה״ וּזְקֵנִים ״סְתוֹר״ — שְׁמַע לַזְּקֵנִים וְאַל תִּשְׁמַע לַיְּלָדִים. שֶׁבִּנְיַן יְלָדִים סְתִירָה, וּסְתִירַת זְקֵנִים בִּנְיָן. וְסִימָן לַדָּבָר: רְחַבְעָם בֶּן שְׁלֹמֹה.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: If youths would say to you: Construct, and Elders would say to you: Demolish, heed the Elders and do not heed the youths, as the construction of youths is demolition, and the demolition of Elders is construction. And a mnemonic** device **for this matter** is **"Rehoboam, son of Solomon"** (I Kings 12:21). Had he heeded the advice of the Elders and yielded at that time, there would have been no schism.

### Timing & Divine Presence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: לָא לִיסְעוֹד אִינִישׁ קְצִירָא לָא בִּתְלָת שָׁעֵי קַדְמָיָיתָא, וְלָא בִּתְלָת שָׁעֵי בָּתְרָיָיתָא דְּיוֹמָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לַיסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ מִן רַחֲמֵי. תְּלָת שָׁעֵי קַדְמָיָיתָא — רָוְוחָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ. בָּתְרָיָיתָא — תָּקֵיף חוּלְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said: Let one not visit a sick person, neither during the first three hours** of the day, **nor in the last three hours of the day, so that he will not be diverted from** praying for **mercy.** Rav Sheisha elaborates: During **the first three hours** the sick person **is relieved,** as after a night's sleep his suffering is somewhat alleviated and the visitor will conclude that there is no need for prayer. In **the last** three hours of the day **his weakness is exacerbated,** and the visitor will despair of ameliorating his suffering and will conclude that prayer is futile.

Rav Sheshes the son of Rav Idi teaches: a person should not visit a sick person in the first three hours of the day, nor in the last three hours of the day. The reason: *ki heichi delo lisach da'ateih min rachamei* — so that he not lose the impulse to pray. In the morning (*tlas sha'ei kadmayasa*), the sick person often feels a temporary surge of energy and appears relatively well, so the visitor might conclude there is no urgent need for prayer. In the evening (*basrayasa*), when *takif chulsheih* — the illness is at its most acute — the visitor might feel that the situation is already hopeless and prayer futile. The middle of the day is therefore the appropriate time: the visitor sees the person in a state that properly motivates prayer.

It is worth observing what this implies. If *bikur cholim* were primarily about practical assistance and emotional support, the final hours of the day — when the patient is most distressed — would be precisely when the visit is most needed. The fact that the Gemara discourages visits at that time shows that prayer is being treated as the central function of the visit, and the timing is calibrated to maximize its effectiveness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִין אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִין שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא זָן אֶת הַחוֹלֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ יִסְעָדֶנּוּ עַל עֶרֶשׂ דְּוָי וְגוֹ׳״. וְאָמַר רָבִין אָמַר רַב: מִנַּיִין שֶׁהַשְּׁכִינָה שְׁרוּיָה לְמַעְלָה מִמִּטָּתוֹ שֶׁל הַחוֹלֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ יִסְעָדֶנּוּ עַל עֶרֶשׂ דְּוָי״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ravin said** that **Rav said: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He** Himself **sustains the sick person?** It is **as it is stated: "The Lord will support him upon the bed of suffering"** (Psalms 41:4). Support in this context is understood to mean that He will feed him. **And Ravin said** that **Rav said: From where** is it derived **that the Divine Presence is resting above the bed of the sick person?** It is also **as it is stated: "The Lord will support him upon the bed of suffering,"** which indicates that God is actually over his bed.

Ravin cites Rav for two further teachings. First: *minayin she'Hakadosh Baruch Hu zan es hacholeh* — how do we know that God sustains the sick person? From the verse *Hashem yisadeinu al eres devai* — God supports him on the bed of suffering — linking *sod* to the concept of *seuda*, sustenance. When a sick person cannot eat, it is God who sustains him. Second: *minayin she'hashechina shruya lemala mi'mitaso shel choleh* — how do we know that the divine presence hovers above the sick person? From the same verse: God supports him, which implies a physical presence above the bed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַנִּכְנָס לְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה, לֹא יֵשֵׁב לֹא עַל גַּבֵּי מִטָּה וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי סַפְסָל וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי כִּסֵּא, אֶלָּא מִתְעַטֵּף וְיוֹשֵׁב עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַשְּׁכִינָה שְׁרוּיָה לְמַעְלָה מִמִּטָּתוֹ שֶׁל חוֹלֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ יִסְעָדֶנּוּ עַל עֶרֶשׂ דְּוָי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **this is also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who enters to visit a sick person may neither sit on the bed nor** sit **on a bench or on a chair** that is higher than the bed upon which the sick person is lying. **Rather, he** deferentially **wraps himself** in his garment **and sits on the ground, because the Divine Presence is resting above the bed of the sick person, as it is stated: "The Lord will support him upon the bed of suffering,"** and it is inappropriate for one to sit above the place where the Divine Presence rests.

A *baraisa* makes this practically operative: when visiting the sick, one should not sit on a couch, stool, or chair — *ella mita'atef ve'yoshev al gabei karka* — but should wrap oneself and sit on the ground, because the *shechina* is present above the sick person's bed and one must not position oneself at the level of the divine presence.

## Rivers & Mikvah

### Euphrates Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבִין אָמַר רַב: מִטְרָא בְּמַעְרְבָא — סָהֲדָא רַבָּה פְּרָת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** apropos statements of Rav cited by Ravin, the Gemara cites an additional statement that **Ravin said** that **Rav said: When** there is **rain in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **a great witness to** that rainfall **is the Euphrates River,** as ultimately that rainwater increases the water flow in the Euphrates River.

The connection to what follows is that the preceding passage concluded with statements made in the name of Ravin citing Rav, and the Gemara now appends further statements attributed to the same chain of transmission. Ravin cites Rav: *mitra be'ma'arava sahada rabba Perat* — when there is rain in *Eretz Yisrael*, the great testimony to this is the Euphrates. When the Euphrates rises dramatically and its current runs fast and strong, this is evidence that there was heavy rainfall in Israel. The *mefarshim* explain that *Eretz Yisrael* sits at a higher elevation than the surrounding lands, so its rainfall runs off and eventually feeds into the Euphrates, causing it to swell. The observation has *halachic* significance, as we shall see.

### Underground Springs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: נַהְרָא מִכֵּיפֵיהּ מִתְבָּרֵיךְ. וּפְלִיגָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אַדִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין הַמַּיִם מְטַהֲרִין בְּזוֹחֲלִין אֶלָּא פְּרָת בְּיוֹמֵי תִשְׁרֵי בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this statement of Rav **disagrees** with a statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel said: A river is blessed from its banks,** i.e., the increase in its water flow is attributable to its tributaries and not to rain. The Gemara comments: **And** this statement **of Shmuel disagrees with** another statement **of Shmuel, as Shmuel said: The water purifies when flowing in the Euphrates** River **only during the days of Tishrei alone,** as after the summer, when no rain falls, it is clear that the water in the river flows from the source of the river. During the rest of the year, rain and snow are mingled with the river water, and water from rain and snow purify one who immerses in them only when the water is standing.

This statement of Ravin stands in tension with a view of Shmuel. Shmuel holds *nahara mikafih mitbarech* — a river draws its blessing from its bedrock, meaning that rivers are primarily fed by underground springs rather than by rainfall. Shmuel even states elsewhere that every drop of rain from above is met by two drops drawn up from below — so that even heavy rainfall does not fundamentally alter the character of river water, which remains predominantly spring-fed.

The *halachic* stakes of this dispute are significant. A *mikvah* constructed from collected rainwater is valid only *be'ashboren* — when the water is settled and still, not flowing. A natural body of water fed by a spring, however — being a form of *mayim chayim*, living water — is valid even when moving (*zochalin*). If a river is primarily spring-fed, it retains the status of *mayim chayim* and may be used even when its waters are in motion. But if a river's water is predominantly rainwater or melted snow, then the moving water cannot be used for *tahara*, since it would have the status of a *mikvah* with the disqualifying property of *zochalin*.

Shmuel's second statement, which contradicts his first, resolves the apparent consistency: *ein hamayim metaharim b'zochalin ela Perat miyomei Tishrei bilvad* — moving water is not valid for purification except for the Euphrates specifically during the month of Tishrei. The reasoning is that at other times of year there is cause for concern that the majority of the river's water derives from snowmelt (in spring) or from rainfall (in fall and winter), which would render the moving water invalid. Only during Tishrei — the fall, before the heavy rains arrive and after the snowmelt has subsided — can one be confident that the Euphrates's water is not predominantly rainwater, and only then is its moving water acceptable.

This second statement of Shmuel effectively concedes the point made by Ravin citing Rav: rivers cannot be assumed to be primarily spring-fed, and one must account for the possibility that their majority is rainwater or snowmelt. The two statements of Shmuel therefore conflict with each other, and it is this second, more stringent position — along with Ravin's observation about the Euphrates — that defines the operative *halacha*.

### Practical Mikvahs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל עֲבַד לְהוֹן מִקְווֹאוֹת לִבְנָתֵיהּ בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן, וּמַפָּצֵי בְּיוֹמֵי תִשְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel's father fashioned ritual baths for his daughters during the days of Nisan and** placed **mats** in the Euphrates River **during the days of Tishrei.** He fashioned ritual baths during Nisan due to the concern that the rainwater and snow water that accumulated in the river exceeded the amount of water that originated in the river's sources. Since immersion in the flowing water would be ineffective, he fashioned ritual baths of standing water elsewhere for them. He placed mats during the days of Tishrei, when they immersed in the river itself, to prevent the mud on the riverbed from acting as an interposition that would invalidate the immersion.

Shmuel's father gave practical expression to this concern. *Avuha d'Shmuel avid lehu mikvatah l'banatei b'yomei Nisan* — he constructed *mikvahs* for his daughters during the spring (*Nisan*), when snowmelt would swell the rivers and render their moving water suspect as predominantly rainwater-derived. *Umartzipi b'yomei Tishrei* — and during Tishrei, he provided them with mats. The mats served a practical purpose: using a riverbed directly is uncomfortable and potentially dangerous (sharp rocks, slippery surfaces), and walking on the riverbed could raise concerns of *chatzitzah*. By providing mats, he enabled his daughters to use the Euphrates in Tishrei — when it was permissible — without those complications.

The *Ran* elaborates considerably on the *halachic* application of these principles. He notes that even at normal water levels, if a river frequently runs dry, there may be reason to suspect that even its ordinary flow is primarily rainwater-fed, since a river sustained by underground springs would not typically dry up. He thus extends the concern beyond high-water periods. On the question of how to *pasken*, Rabbeinu Tam attempts to rule leniently in accordance with Shmuel's first position — that rivers are always primarily spring-fed — but the *Ran* and virtually all other *Rishonim* reject this, holding that wherever there is genuine concern about rainwater or snowmelt, the river may not be used as moving water. The *Ran* also discusses a second possible leniency: that within the river, one might distinguish between sections that would have been valid *mikvah*-water even without the additional rainwater (since their baseline flow was spring-fed) and sections that exist only because of the rainfall. According to this approach, the former sections might remain usable even when the overall water is a majority rainwater, since the rainwater did not create or define those sections. This is a nuanced position and the *Ran* treats it at length.

The practical consequence of all this is significant. Oceans and natural lakes, being stationary, generally do not raise these concerns — though one must verify that a given lake is not man-made. Rivers, however, present a real and ongoing issue, since they are moving and their water composition varies substantially by season, geography, and precipitation patterns.

## Exile & Poverty

### Vessels of Exile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַב: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְאַתָּה בֶן אָדָם עֲשֵׂה לְךָ כְּלֵי גוֹלָה״ — זוֹ נֵר וּקְעָרָה וְשָׁטִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said** that **Rav said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And you, son of man, make for yourself implements of exile"** (Ezekiel 12:3)? **That is** referring to **a lamp, and a bowl, and a rug,** as an exile needs those items and they are portable.

The Gemara now introduces a series of statements attributed to Ravin citing Rav, or in some textual traditions to Rabbi Ami citing Rav. The shift in topic is notable — these have nothing to do with *bikur cholim* or *Nedarim* — but the *Gemara* strings them together because they share the same chain of transmission. The verse in *Yechezkel* reads *ve'ata ben adam asei lecha k'lei gola* — and you, son of man, make for yourself vessels of exile. The *Gemara* identifies these minimal vessels as *ner*, a candle; *kearah*, a plate; and *shatiach*, a mat. These are the bare essentials for someone being driven from his home into exile, or in modern terms, the equivalent of the absolute minimum one needs to survive while displaced.

### Lacking Everything

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּחֹסֶר כֹּל״, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַב: בְּלֹא נֵר וּבְּלֹא שֻׁלְחָן. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: בְּלֹא אִשָּׁה. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: בְּלֹא שַׁמָּשׁ. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: בְּלֹא דֵּעָה. תָּנָא: בְּלֹא מֶלַח וּבְלֹא רְבָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages interpreted the following verse describing the exile experience: "Therefore shall you serve your enemy whom the Lord shall send against you, in hunger, and in thirst, and in nakedness, and **in want of all things;** and he shall put a yoke of iron upon your neck, until he has destroyed you" (Deuteronomy 28:48). **Rabbi Ami said** that **Rav said:** "In want of all things" means **without a lamp and without a table** to eat upon. **Rav Ḥisda said: Without a wife. Rav Sheshet said: Without an attendant** to aid him. **Rav Naḥman said: Without intelligence.** One of the Sages **teaches** in a *baraita*: **Without salt and without fat [*revav*]** in which to dip his bread.

This leads into an interpretation of the phrase *b'choser kol* — lacking everything — from *Devarim*. What does it mean to truly lack everything? Rabbi Ami citing Rav: *belo ner* — without even a candle — *belo shulchan*, without a table. Rav Hisda: *belo isha*, without a wife. Rav Sheshes: *belo shamash*, without an attendant — some household support. Rav Nachman: *belo de'ah*, without intellect. The *baraisa* adds: *belo melach*, without salt — the basic preservative and condiment — and *u'belo revav*, without something to dip one's bread in, some basic oil or fat. Each Amora identifies a different essential that, if missing, constitutes a state of total deprivation.

### Intellect & Sufficiency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: נָקְטִינַן — אֵין עָנִי אֶלָּא בְּדֵעָה. בְּמַעְרְבָא אָמְרִי: דְּדָא בֵּיהּ — כּוּלָּא בֵּיהּ, דְּלָא דָּא בֵּיהּ — מָה בֵּיהּ. דָּא קָנֵי — מָה חָסַר, דָּא לָא קָנֵי — מָה קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that **we have a tradition: A poor person is only** one lacking **in intelligence,** in agreement with the opinion of Rav Naḥman. **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they say:** One **who** has **this** attribute, intelligence, **in him** has **everything in him.** One **who does not** have **this** attribute **in him, what is in him?** If **he acquired this, what** else **is lacking?** If **he has not acquired this, what has he acquired?**

Abaye draws a striking conclusion from Rav Nachman's position: *naktinan ein ani ela b'de'ah* — we hold that the only true poverty is poverty of intellect. This resonates with the Mishnah's formulation *eizehu ashir hasameach b'chelko* — who is wealthy? One who is satisfied with his portion. Abaye's teaching inverts this: true poverty is not a matter of material lack but of the absence of the wisdom and understanding that allow a person to navigate life and find meaning and sufficiency within whatever circumstances he faces. If one has *de'ah*, one can manage everything else; without it, no material possession is truly adequate.

The Aramaic saying from the west (*be'ma'arava*) captures the same thought in aphoristic form: *da de-bi kula bi* — if you have this (*de'ah*, understanding), you have everything; *de-la da bi mah bi* — if you lack this, what do you truly have? *De-kane da kane mah chasar* — if you acquire this, what are you missing? *De-la kane mah kane* — and if you have not acquired this, what have you acquired? The rhetorical structure reinforces the point: the possession of intellect and understanding is the one truly constitutive good, in the absence of which everything else is hollow.
