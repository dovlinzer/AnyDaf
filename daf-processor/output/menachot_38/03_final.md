# Menachot 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzitzit & Four Corners

### Opening & Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבַּע צִיצִיּוֹת מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, שֶׁאַרְבַּעְתָּן מִצְוָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: With regard to the **four ritual fringes** on a garment, the absence of **each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, as the four of them** constitute **one mitzva.**

The daf opens with a transition from the previous *perek*'s focus on *sefarim*, *tefillin*, and *mezuzot* to the subject of *tzitzit*. This topic is introduced at the close of the third *perek* and becomes the central concern of the fourth. We pick up from the final lines of 37b, where the *mishna* is quoted.

### Four Corners Requirement

*Arba tzitziot me'akvot zu et zu she'arba'tan mitzvah achat* — the four *tzitziot* on the four corners of the garment prevent one another, meaning they are all mutually required, and all four together constitute a single *mitzvah*. The practical implication is stark: if one of the four *tzitziot* is missing, one does not fulfill three-quarters of a *mitzvah*, or even three *mitzvot* — one fulfills zero *mitzvot*.

### Tanna Kamma vs. R. Ishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אַרְבַּעְתָּן אַרְבַּע מִצְווֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael says: The four of them are four** discrete **mitzvot,** and the absence of one does not prevent fulfillment of the rest.

The *mishna* records a dissenting view. Rabbi Ishmael holds: *arba'tan arba mitzvot* — the four *tzitziot* are four separate *mitzvot*. The *gemara* seeks to understand the precise nature of this dispute and articulate its practical ramifications.

### Mai Beinihu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: סָדִין בְּצִיצִית אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** the opinions of the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yishmael? **Rav Yosef said:** The difference **between** their opinions **is** with regard to a linen **sheet with** woolen **ritual fringes** that has fewer than four ritual fringes.

The *gemara* asks: *mai beinihu* — what is the practical difference between these two positions? The most fundamental *nafka mina* is already apparent: if you have only three corners with *tzitzit*, according to the *tanna kamma*, who holds that all four combine into a single *mitzvah* and according to whom we *paskin*, you have fulfilled no *mitzvah* at all. According to Rabbi Ishmael, you have fulfilled three *mitzvot*, having simply failed to fulfill a fourth.

### Linen & Wool Case

The first *tanna* maintains that since one is not performing a *mitzvah*, he may not wrap himself in the sheet, due to the prohibition of diverse kinds, i.e., the prohibition against wearing clothing made from a mixture of wool and linen threads. Conversely, Rabbi Ishmael permits one to wrap himself in it, as each ritual fringe is a separate *mitzvah*, and the *mitzvah* of ritual fringes overrides the prohibition against wearing diverse kinds. This mixture is ordinarily forbidden but is permitted when one is fulfilling a *mitzvah*. According to the *tanna kamma*, however, if one lacks even a single *tzitzit*, no *mitzvah* is being performed at all, and one would therefore be violating the prohibition of *shatnez* without justification. According to Rabbi Ishmael, who holds that each corner is an independent *mitzvah*, the wool *techeiles* on a linen garment is permissible because a *mitzvah* is indeed being fulfilled. This represents a second-degree *nafka mina* — it follows from, rather than directly expresses, the underlying dispute.

### Five-Cornered Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא בַּר אֲהִינָא אָמַר: טַלִּית בַּעֲלַת חָמֵשׁ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Ahina said:** The difference **between** their opinions **is** with regard to **a cloak with five** corners. It is derived that a cloak of this kind requires ritual fringes (see 43b), but it is unclear whether ritual fringes must be placed on each corner.

Rava Bar Ahina identifies yet another *nafka mina*: *talis ba'alat chamesh* — a garment with five corners. A *pasuk* teaches that even a five-cornered garment is obligated in *tzitzit*. According to the *tanna kamma*, who holds that there is one *mitzvah* requiring four *tzitziot*, a five-cornered garment must have *tzitzit* on four of its five corners — the *mitzvah* is defined by four corners, not by covering every corner. But according to Rabbi Ishmael, since each corner is an independent *mitzvah*, a garment with five obligated corners would require five *tzitziot*. Again, we *paskin* like the *tanna kamma*.

### Shabbat Carrying Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: דְּרַב הוּנָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַיּוֹצֵא בְּטַלִּית שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְצוּיֶּיצֶת כְּהִלְכָתָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת – חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** The difference **between** their opinions **is** with regard to the opinion **of Rav Huna, as Rav Huna says: One who goes out** unwittingly to the public domain **on Shabbat with** a four-cornered **cloak that does not have** all of **the requisite ritual fringes** attached to its corners is **liable** to bring **a sin offering,** because the remaining fringes are not an integral part of the garment.

Ravina identifies a further *nafka mina* based on a teaching of Rav Huna: one who goes out on *Shabbat* wearing a garment with *tzitzit* that are not fulfilling the *mitzvah* as required — *she'eina metuyezet ke-hilchata* — is liable for *chatat*, because the strings hanging from the garment are not serving as a proper part of the garment. When *tzitzit* fulfill the *mitzvah*, they are considered integral to the garment and carrying them is no different from wearing a garment. But when they fail to fulfill the *mitzvah*, they are merely objects hanging from the garment, and wearing it constitutes carrying on *Shabbat*. The *nafka mina* is therefore: does a garment with *tzitzit* on only three of its four corners count as fulfilling the *mitzvah*? According to the *tanna kamma*, it does not, and the wearer is liable. According to Rabbi Ishmael, three *mitzvot* are still being fulfilled, and the wearer is not in violation. All of these *nafka minas* flow from the single foundational question of whether the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* can be fulfilled with fewer than four corners.

## Shape Modification & Loopholes

### Shortening Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: הַאי מַאן דְּבַצְּרֵיהּ לִגְלִימֵיהּ – לָא עֲבַד וְלֹא כְּלוּם, שַׁוְּיֵיהּ טַלִּית בַּעֲלַת חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said: One who cuts** the corner of **his garment has not done anything** of consequence with regard to exempting the garment from the obligation of ritual fringes, as **he has rendered it a cloak with five** corners, to which the obligation of ritual fringes applies.

Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi rules: *hai man de-vatzrei le-glimei lo avad ve-lo klum* — one who rends or cuts a garment so as to divide a corner, turning a four-cornered garment into something he hopes will be differently shaped, has accomplished nothing. As the *gemara* notes, he has merely created a five-cornered garment — *shavyei talis bat chamesh* — and as we have just established, a five-cornered garment is fully obligated in *tzitzit*. The attempt to escape the obligation fails.

### Knotting Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: הַאי מַאן דְּצַיְּירֵיהּ לִגְלִימֵיהּ – לָא עֲבַד וְלֹא כְּלוּם. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּכְמַאן דְּשַׁרְיֵיהּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya** similarly **says: One who ties his garment has not done anything** of consequence with regard to exempting the garment from the obligation of ritual fringes. **What is the reason? It is considered as though** the garment is **untied,** since the knot can be loosened at any time.

Rav Mesharshiya rules similarly: *hai man de-tzayeirei leh le-glimei lo avid ve-lo klum* — one who ties up his garment has also accomplished nothing with respect to *tzitzit*. Rashi explains the case as one where the garment was too long; the person bunched it up and knotted it to create a new hem at a higher point, then affixed *tzitzit* at what is now the new corner. The reason this fails is stated in the *gemara*'s own terms: *di-kheman de-sharya dami* — it is as though the knot is undone. Since the garment has not been permanently altered — it has only been knotted, not sewn — its *halachic* shape is assessed in its unknotted state. The *tzitzit* placed at the new knotted hem do not count.

### Knotted Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן נָמֵי: כׇּל חֲמָתוֹת הַצְּרוּרוֹת טְהוֹרוֹת, חוּץ מִשֶּׁל עַרְבִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And we learned likewise** in a mishna (*Kelim* 26:4): **All bound** leather **jugs,** i.e., those whose bottoms are not sewn but tied, **are ritually pure,** i.e., they are not susceptible to ritual impurity. This is because they are not considered receptacles, as these knots will be untied, **except for** leather **jugs of Arabs,** who would tie them with a permanent knot.

The *gemara* draws a parallel from the laws of *tumah*: *kol ha-chamatot ha-tzrurot tehorot chutz mi-shel Arviyim* — all types of leather flasks that have been knotted shut at one end are not *mekabel tumah*, because knotting does not fundamentally transform the object. The flask, knotted at one end but not sewn, is still assessed in terms of its natural, unknotted shape, and since in that shape it is open at both ends and cannot truly contain liquid, it does not qualify as a vessel susceptible to *tumah*. The exception is the flask of the Arabs, for whom this type of knotting was the standard method of closure and thus constituted the actual shape of their vessels. The *gemara* invokes this to demonstrate that merely knotting something does not change its fundamental *halachic* character.

### Sewing Garment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא: הַאי מַאן דְּחַיְּיטֵיהּ לִגְלִימֵיהּ – לָא עֲבַד וְלֹא כְּלוּם, אִם אִיתָא דְּלָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ – לִיפְסוֹק וְלִישְׁדְּיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Dimi of Neharde'a** similarly **says: One who sews his garment,** i.e., he folded over a long garment and sewed the edges together, **has not done anything** of consequence with regard to the obligation of ritual fringes, and he must place ritual fringes on the original corners. The reason is that **if it is so that he does not need** the folded part, which is why he is sewing it, **let him cut** it **and throw it** away.

Rav Dimi of Nehardea extends the principle further: *hai man de-chaytei le-glimei lo avid ve-lo klum* — even sewing up a garment does not achieve the intended result. Why not? *Ei ita de-lo miba'ei leh li-fsok u-lishadyeih* — if this person truly did not want this portion of the garment, why did he not simply cut it off and discard it? The *gemara* is saying that folding and sewing, rather than cutting, reveals that the person did not genuinely intend to alter the garment's shape, and therefore the shape is not changed *halachically*.

### Rashi's Reinterpretation

This ruling is conceptually difficult, and Rashi himself recognizes this. There are any number of legitimate reasons one might fold and sew rather than cut: the extra material could allow the garment to be lengthened later for another wearer, or a folded hem may be more durable. Furthermore, as Rashi acknowledges, a *gemara* elsewhere explicitly states that sewing a garment at a new position does change its recognized shape. These considerations lead Rashi to reinterpret the entire sugya — the knotting case and the sewing case alike — as dealing not with shortening the hem but with attempting to eliminate corners. The person is not trying to make the garment shorter; he is trying to curve or tuck in a corner so that the garment no longer has four distinct corners and he can evade the obligation of *tzitzit*. In that context, doing so by knotting is clearly insufficient because the knot can be undone and the corner remains present in the garment's fundamental shape. And even sewing it shut does not help, because the *gemara* can see that this person is transparently trying to circumvent the *mitzvah* rather than making a genuine structural alteration — for if it were genuine, he would simply cut off the corner. Were someone genuinely and sincerely altering the shape of the garment, the sewing would indeed be *halachically* effective. The special rule here applies only because the intent is transparently evasive.

## Torn Tzitzit Incident

### Following Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּוָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael.** The Gemara states: **But the *halakha*** is **not in accordance with his** opinion.

At this point the *gemara* cites the ruling: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, halacha ke-Rabbi Yishmael* — Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Ishmael, that the four *tzitziot* constitute four independent *mitzvot*. The *gemara* immediately overrides this: *ve-leis hilkheta ka-vasei* — we do not rule in accordance with Rav Yehuda's ruling in the name of Shmuel. The *halacha* follows the *chachamim*, not Rabbi Ishmael: all four *tzitziot* combine to form a single *mitzvah*, and without all four, no *mitzvah* is fulfilled.

### Witness Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא הֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל אַבָּתְרֵיהּ דְּמָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי בְּשַׁבְּתָא דְּרִיגְלָא, אִיפְּסִיק קַרְנָא דְּחוּטֵיהּ, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי. כַּד מְטָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵהָתָם אִיפְּסִיק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי אֲמַרְתְּ לִי מֵהָתָם שְׁדֵיתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Ravina was walking behind Mar bar Rav Ashi on the Shabbat of the Festival** when **the corner** of Mar bar Rav Ashi's garment **on** which **his ritual fringes** were hanging **tore, and** yet Ravina **did not say anything to him. When he arrived at** Mar bar Rav Ashi's **house,** Ravina **said to him: Back there,** along the way, the corner **tore.** Mar bar Rav Ashi **said to him: If you would have told me** then, **I would have thrown off** the garment **there,** as once one of the ritual fringes is torn no mitzva is performed with the rest, and it is prohibited to walk in the public domain on Shabbat wearing such a garment.

The *gemara* then recounts an incident. Ravina was walking behind Mar bar Rav Ashi on a *Shabbat* when the latter's community was preparing to study the *halachot* of the *regel*. While they walked, *ifsik karna de-chusei* — the corner of Mar bar Rav Ashi's garment tore, causing one of his *tzitziot* to come off. Mar bar Rav Ashi was unaware of this. Ravina, walking behind him, saw what had happened but said nothing — *ve-lo amar lei ve-lo midi*. At that point Mar bar Rav Ashi was walking on *Shabbat* wearing a garment with *tzitzit* on only three corners, which according to the *chachamim* means he was not fulfilling the *mitzvah* and was effectively carrying in the public domain. When they reached Mar bar Rav Ashi's home, Ravina informed him: *me-hasam ifsik* — your *tzitzit* tore off some time ago. Ravina had kept silent because had he informed Mar bar Rav Ashi at the moment it occurred, the latter would have been compelled to remove his *tallit* in the street and walk home without it — an undignified situation. Ravina chose to allow him to remain ignorant so he could return home without embarrassment. Mar bar Rav Ashi responded that Ravina had erred: *ei amrat lei me-hasam, shadyisei* — had you told me then, I would have removed my *tallit* immediately. You should have told me so I would not have unknowingly continued to carry.

### Two Story Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר מָר: גָּדוֹל כְּבוֹד הַבְּרִיּוֹת שֶׁדּוֹחֶה אֶת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה? תַּרְגְּומַהּ רַב בַּר שְׁבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, בְּלָאו דְּ״לֹא תָסוּר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But didn't the Master say: Great is human dignity, as it overrides a prohibition in the Torah?** This includes the prohibition against carrying on Shabbat in the public domain. That being the case, why would he remove his garment in public? The Gemara answers: **Rav bar Shabba interpreted** that statement **before Rav Kahana:** He stated this **with regard to the prohibition of: "You shall not deviate** to the left or the right of that which they tell you" (Deuteronomy 17:11). A prohibition by rabbinic law is overridden by human dignity, but not a prohibition by Torah law. Therefore, Mar bar Rav Ashi would have removed his garment had he known about the tear.

The *gemara* then challenges the first version of the story. Surely Ravina was right to say nothing? After all, we are taught — *gadol kevod ha-briyot she-docheh lo ta'aseh she-ba-Torah* — human dignity is so great that it overrides a negative prohibition in the Torah. If so, the consideration of Mar bar Rav Ashi's dignity should have permitted Ravina to remain silent, and Mar bar Rav Ashi's criticism was unwarranted. The *gemara* responds by citing the qualification offered by Mar bar Rav Shila in the name of Rav Kahana — a qualification that the *gemara* in Berakhot (top of 38a) makes as well: when we say *kevod ha-briyot* overrides a *lo ta'aseh she-ba-Torah*, we do not mean a literal Torah-level negative prohibition. We mean it overrides the *de-Rabbanan* prohibition of *lo tasur*, the obligation to follow rabbinic enactment. *Kevod ha-briyot* does not override a *de-oraita* prohibition. Therefore, if one sees another person inadvertently violating a Torah-level negative prohibition, one must inform him, even if it causes embarrassment, because human dignity cannot override a biblical transgression. This is why Mar bar Rav Ashi insisted he should have been told — carrying on *Shabbat* in a *reshut ha-rabim* would have been a *de-oraita* violation, and *kevod ha-briyot* does not override that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי מֵהָתָם אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ לְמִישְׁדְּיֵיהּ? וְהָאָמַר מָר: גָּדוֹל כְּבוֹד הַבְרִיּוֹת שֶׁדּוֹחֶה אֶת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה! וְהָא תַּרְגְּומַהּ רַב בַּר שְׁבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא בְּלָאו דְּ״לֹא תָסוּר״, הָכָא נָמֵי כַּרְמְלִית דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** there is a different version of this discussion: It was **when** they were **there,** in the place where the corner of Mar bar Rav Ashi's garment tore, that Ravina **said to him** that it had torn, **and** Mar bar Rav Ashi **said to him** in response: **What is your opinion?** Do you think **that I should throw** the garment off? **But doesn't the Master say: Great is human dignity, as it overrides a prohibition in the Torah?** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But Rav bar Shabba interpreted** that statement **before Rav Kahana:** He stated this **with regard to the prohibition of: "You shall not deviate,"** not the prohibition against carrying in the public domain, which applies by Torah law. The Gemara answers that **here too,** it is not a prohibition by Torah law, as the place where they were walking was not a full-fledged public domain but **a *karmelit*,** in which carrying **is** prohibited **by rabbinic law.**

The *gemara* then presents a second version of the story: *ve-ika de-amri* — some say that in fact Ravina did inform Mar bar Rav Ashi at the moment the *tzitzit* tore. Mar bar Rav Ashi responded: *mai dei'tach* — why are you telling me this? You expect me to remove my *tallit*? And he invoked the principle of *gadol kevod ha-briyot she-docheh lo ta'aseh she-ba-Torah* — my dignity overrides the obligation, and I will not remove my *tallit*. The *gemara* again raises the objection: but *kevod ha-briyot* only overrides a *de-Rabbanan* prohibition, not a *de-oraita* one! And answers: *hacha nami carmelit havah* — in this case they were walking through a *karmelit*, a domain that carries only a *de-Rabbanan* prohibition of carrying on *Shabbat*. In that circumstance, *kevod ha-briyot* does override, and Mar bar Rav Ashi's position is defensible.

### Kevod Habriyot Principle

In both versions of the story, the underlying *halacha* is identical and undisputed. *Kevod ha-briyot* overrides a *de-Rabbanan* prohibition but does not override a *de-oraita* one. The practical consequences differ by domain: in a *reshut ha-rabim*, carrying constitutes a *de-oraita* violation, and one must be informed immediately so as not to continue transgressing, even if it is embarrassing. In a *karmelit*, the violation is only *de-Rabbanan*, and *kevod ha-briyot* applies — but even then, as the first version of the story shows, it may be preferable to allow the person to remain unknowing rather than inform them and thereby convert an inadvertent violation into a deliberate one.

## White & Blue Strings

### Mishna Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַתְּכֵלֶת אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת הַלָּבָן, וְהַלָּבָן אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַתְּכֵלֶת. תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל יָד אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת שֶׁל רֹאשׁ, וְשֶׁל רֹאשׁ אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת שֶׁל יָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The absence of **the sky-blue [*tekhelet*]** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of ritual fringes with **the white** strings, **and the** absence of **white** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva with **the sky-blue** strings. If one has only one, he wears it without the other. Absence of the **phylacteries of** the **arm does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of the phylacteries **of** the **head, and** absence of the phylacteries **of** the **head does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of the phylacteries **of** the **arm.** If one has only one, he dons it without the other.

The *gemara* now enters the fourth *perek* with a new *mishna*: *ha-techeiles eino me'akev et ha-lavan ve-ha-lavan eino me'akev et ha-techeiles* — the blue strings of *tzitzit* do not hold back the white, and the white do not hold back the blue. This means one is *yotzei* the *mitzvah* even with only white or only blue strings.

### Four Strands Assumption

This *mishna* calls for careful analysis of what exactly is being described. As is well known, *tzitzit* consists of four strands inserted through a hole in the corner and folded over, producing eight loose strings — though the *mitzvah* is structured around four strands. The question of how many of those four strands should be *techeiles* is a matter of dispute among contemporary *poskim* who reintroduce *techeiles*, with some holding that two of the four are *techeiles* and others holding differently. For purposes of the *gemara's* discussion, we assume two of the four strands are *techeiles* and two are white — *lavan*.

What does *eino me'akev* mean in its plain sense? If taken simply, it would suggest that having only two white strands without the two blue, or only two blue without the two white, is sufficient to fulfill the *mitzvah*. This would mean *tzitzit* can be performed with only two strands rather than four. A major *Tosafot* here grapples with this reading and ultimately leans toward understanding the *mishna* differently — that four strands are always required and the question of what *eino me'akev* addresses is whether all four must be white or all four must be *techeiles*, not whether one can reduce to fewer than four. The *mitzvah* can be fulfilled with four all-white or four all-*techeiles* strands, and indeed this is the basis for the universal practice of wearing four white strings when *techeiles* is unavailable.

### Tefillin Parallel

The *mishna* offers a parallel: *tefilla shel yad eino me'akevet et shel rosh ve-shel rosh eino me'akevet et shel yad* — the *tefilla* of the hand does not hold back the *tefilla* of the head and vice versa. Each *tefilla* constitutes its own independent *mitzvah*, so that even if one has only one of the two, the *mitzvah* of that one is fulfilled. This stands in deliberate contrast to *tzitzit*, where — according to the *tanna kamma* — having only three corners with *tzitzit* fulfills no *mitzvah* at all.

### Braisa Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמְּעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מְעַכְּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse requires one to place white and sky-blue strings upon the corners of his garments and then states: **"That you may look upon it"** (Numbers 15:39), it **teaches** that the lack of either **one prevents** fulfillment of the mitzva **with the other;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **But the Rabbis say:** The lack of one **does not prevent the** fulfillment of the mitzva with the other.

The *gemara* raises a challenge: *leima masnisin de-lo ke-Rabbi* — perhaps our *mishna* does not follow the view of Rabbi (Rebbi, i.e., Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi). A *Braisa* is cited: *ur'isem oto, melamed she-me'akvin zeh et zeh, divrei Rabbi* — the verse says "and you shall see it" (in the singular), teaching that the components — white and *techeiles* — hold each other back and are required together, in the words of Rabbi. *Ve-chachamim omrim: ein me'akvin* — and the sages say they do not hold each other back. Our *mishna*, which says they do not hold each other back, would thus seem to follow the *chachamim* against Rabbi.

### Rabbi's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? דִּכְתִיב ״הַכָּנָף״ – מִין כָּנָף, וּכְתִיב ״פְּתִיל תְּכֵלֶת״, וְאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״וּרְאִיתֶם אוֹתוֹ״ – עַד דְּאִיכָּא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּחַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, i.e., how does he derive his ruling from this verse? The Gemara explains: **As it is written:** "And they shall put on the fringe of the corner a sky-blue thread" (Numbers 15:38). "The fringe of **the corner"** is a reference to strings that are of the same **type** as the **corner** of the garment. Since garments are usually white, this phrase is referring to white strings. **And it is written** in this same verse: **"A sky-blue thread." And the Merciful One states** in the following verse, referring to both types of strings: "And it shall be to you for a fringe **that you may look upon it"** (Numbers 15:39), in the singular. This teaches that one does not fulfill his obligation **until both** types **are present together.**

Before attempting to reconcile the *mishna* with Rabbi's view, the *gemara* first elaborates on Rabbi's reasoning. The Torah states: *al tzitzit ha-kanaf pesil techeiles* — on the *tzitzit* of the corner, a thread of blue. The phrase *ha-kanaf min kanaf* — from the [material of the] corner — indicates that the base *tzitzit* are of the same type as the garment. Since a standard garment is white, the base *tzitzit* are white. The Torah then adds the blue to the white. It then states *ur'isem oto* — you shall see it, in the singular — teaching that both the white and the blue combine into a single unified object. *Ad de-ika tarvaihu be-chad* — only when both are present together is the *mitzvah* fulfilled. Without both, one is not *yotzei*. On this reading, of course, none of us who wear only white *tzitzit* would be fulfilling the *mitzvah* at all.

### Chachamim's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, ״וּרְאִיתֶם אוֹתוֹ״ – כֹּל חַד לְחוֹדֵיהּ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis,** who hold that the one can fulfill one obligation without the other, understand this verse? The Gemara answers: They hold that the phrase **"that you may look upon it" indicates** that one fulfills a mitzva with **each one individually.**

The *chachamim* read *ur'isem oto* differently. *Kol chad le-chudei mashma* — "you shall see it" refers to each one individually. The word "it" can denote each component separately — you see the white, you see the blue — and the presence of one does not require the other. The *peshat* of the verse, as Rashi notes, is arguably that the singular "it" refers specifically to the *techeiles*, which is the most proximate antecedent and which, with its color evoking the heavens and the divine, serves as the memory-trigger for all the *mitzvot*. Either way, for the *chachamim*, white and blue are independent of one another, and each can stand alone.

## Reconciling Mishna

### Rav's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי, לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְקַדֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its initial suggestion: **Shall we say that** the mishna, which states that one can fulfill the mitzva with either white or sky-blue strings even in the absence of the other, is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? The Gemara responds: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, and the ruling of the mishna **is necessary only with regard to granting precedence.** The white strings should precede the blue strings, but if the order is reversed, one still fulfills the mitzva.

The *gemara* now attempts to reconcile our *mishna* with Rabbi's position, which holds that white and *techeiles* are mutually required. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav proposes: *afilu teima Rabbi* — even Rabbi can be made consistent with our *mishna*, by reinterpreting what *eino me'akev* refers to. *Lo nitzrecha ela le-akdeim* — the *mishna* is not discussing whether both colors are required; it is discussing the *order* in which they are placed. Both white and *techeiles* are indeed necessary for the *mitzvah* according to Rabbi, but the *mishna* is teaching that the *order* is not *me'akev*.

### Order of White & Blue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מִצְוָה לְהַקְדִּים לָבָן לַתְּכֵלֶת, וְאִם הִקְדִּים תְּכֵלֶת לַלָּבָן – יָצָא, אֶלָּא שֶׁחִיסֵּר מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: It is **a mitzva to** insert the **white** strings into the garment **before** inserting **the sky-blue** strings, **but if** one inserted the **sky-blue** strings **before the white** strings, he **fulfilled** his obligation **but omitted** the **mitzva.**

A *Braisa* is cited to establish what the correct order is: *mitzvah le-hakdim lavan le-techeiles* — it is a *mitzvah* to put the white first and the blue second. The basis for this is the verse *al tzitzit ha-kanaf pesil techeiles*: first the *tzitzit* of the corner (the white, matching the garment), and then the *pesil techeiles* (the blue thread) is added to it. White precedes blue.

### Blue First Result

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי חִיסֵּר מִצְוָה? אִילֵּימָא חִיסֵּר מִצְוָה דְּלָבָן וְקִיֵּים מִצְוָה דִּתְכֵלֶת – לְרַבִּי עַכּוֹבֵי מְעַכֵּב אַהֲדָדֵי. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁחִיסֵּר מִצְוָה וְעָשָׂה מִצְוָה, וּמַאי חִיסֵּר מִצְוָה? דְּלָא עֲבַד מִצְוָה מִן הַמּוּבְחָר.
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that the individual **omitted** the **mitzva of white** strings **and fulfilled** only the **mitzva of sky-blue** strings, how is this possible? **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, the absence of either one **prevents** fulfillment of the mitzva **with the other,** and therefore in this case one would not fulfill any mitzva at all. The Gemara answers that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** It means **that he omitted a mitzva but** nevertheless **performed a mitzva. And what** does it mean that he **omitted a mitzva?** It means **that he did not perform** the **mitzva in the optimal manner** because he did not insert the white strings first, but he did fulfill the mitzva of ritual fringes.

If, however, one reverses the order — *im hikdim techeiles le-lavan* — one is still *yotzei*, but *ela she-chisar mitzvah* — something is lacking. This is what the *mishna* means when it says the blue does not hold back the white: having placed the blue first does not invalidate the white that follows. The order failure does not prevent fulfillment of the *mitzvah* itself.

The *gemara* then asks: *mai chisar mitzvah* — what exactly is lacking? One reading would be that you fulfilled the *mitzvah* of blue but lost the *mitzvah* of white, since white was supposed to come first. But this cannot be correct according to Rabbi — *ikvuvei me'akevei ahadadi* — Rabbi holds that without both white and blue you cannot fulfill any *mitzvah* at all, so "losing the *mitzvah* of white" is not a coherent category within Rabbi's framework. Rather, Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav explains: *she-chisar mitzvah ve-asah mitzvah* — you are lacking a *mitzvah* but you are also doing the *mitzvah*. That is, you have fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* itself, but you have failed to perform it in the ideal manner — *de-lo avid mitzvah min ha-muvchar* — you did not fulfill the *mitzvah* of doing it in the correct order. The *mitzvah* stands; what is lost is the ideal performance.

### Two-Direction Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתִינַח לָבָן דְּאֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַתְּכֵלֶת, תְּכֵלֶת דְּאֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת הַלָּבָן – מַאי הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well** with regard to the mishna's statement that absence of the **white** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva with **the sky-blue** strings, which has been interpreted to mean that failing to insert the white strings before the sky-blue strings does not invalidate the ritual fringes. But **what is** the meaning of the mishna's statement **that** the absence of **sky-blue** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva with **the white** strings?

The *gemara* now presses: *ha teinach* — this works well for one phrase in the *mishna*, but not both. The phrase "white does not hold back *techeiles*" can be read as: having not placed the white first does not prevent the *techeiles* from counting — that is, if you put blue first, the blue is still valid. That fits the ordering interpretation. But *techeiles de-eino me'akev et ha-lavan mai hi* — what does the other phrase mean, that blue does not hold back white? If in a standard white garment white always comes first, then doing white first is simply doing it correctly. There is no scenario in which the prior presence of blue would threaten to invalidate subsequently placed white strings. The order interpretation can only work in one direction; it does not explain both phrases of the *mishna*.

### Blue Garment Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְטַלִּית שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ תְּכֵלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said:** That statement of the mishna **is necessary only** in the case of **a garment that** consists **entirely** of **sky-blue wool.** In such a case, one is supposed to insert the sky-blue strings before the white strings.

Rami Bar Chama resolves this: *lo nitzrecha ela le-talis she-kula techeiles* — the second phrase addresses the case of a garment that is entirely blue. In this case, based on the principle of *kanaf min kanaf* — that the *tzitzit* match the type of the corner — the *tzitzit* matching the blue garment would be *techeiles*, and *techeiles* would therefore properly come first. If the white strings were then placed second, that would be the correct order for a blue garment. But if someone doing a blue garment placed white first anyway, the question is whether white jumping the line prevents the blue from counting. The answer: *leis lan bah* — no it does not, the order is still not *me'akev*. And that is what the *mishna* means when it says *techeiles eino me'akev et ha-lavan* — having placed white first when blue should have gone first does not prevent the white from counting. In the standard garment, white comes first; in a blue garment, *techeiles* comes first; in both cases, violating the order does not invalidate the *mitzvah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי: אֲמַר לֵיהּ לֵוִי לִשְׁמוּאֵל: אַרְיוֹךְ, לָא תִּיתֵּיב אַכַּרְעָךְ עַד דִּמְפָרְשַׁתְּ לִי לְהָא מִילְּתָא: הַתְּכֵלֶת אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת אֶת הַלָּבָן, וְהַלָּבָן אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַתְּכֵלֶת – מַאי הִיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְסָדִין בְּצִיצִית, דְּמִצְוָה לְאַקְדּוֹמֵי לָבָן בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this **was also stated** by *amora'im*: **Levi said to Shmuel: Aryokh, do not sit on your feet until you explain to me this matter:** When the mishna states that the absence of **the sky-blue** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva of ritual fringes **with the white** strings, **and the** absence of **white** strings **does not prevent** fulfillment of the mitzva **with the sky-blue** strings, **what** does **it** mean? Shmuel **said to** Levi: That statement **is necessary only** in the case of a linen **cloak** on which one places **ritual fringes, where** there is **a mitzva to** insert the **white** strings **first.**

This interpretation is further corroborated by an exchange between Levi and Shmuel. Levi pressed Shmuel — *Aryoch*, he called him, like a lion, refusing to let him rest until he explained the *mishna* — asking how to understand the two directions of *eino me'akev* given that Rabbi requires both components. Shmuel's initial response focuses on the linen garment case, where the garment-matching color comes first; Rami Bar Chama then provides the blue garment as the solution to the second phrase, as described above.

## Rava's Objection

### Color Irrelevance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִידֵּי צִיבְעָא קָא גָרֵים?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Is it** actually the **color** of the garment that **determines** the proper order in which one should insert the strings?

Rava objects to this entire framework: *midi tzivah ka garim* — what does color have to do with it? The principle of *min kanaf* should not be understood as requiring us to assess each garment's particular color and adjust the order of *tzitzit* accordingly. As Rashi reads it, *min kanaf* establishes the standard color for a standard garment, and since the standard garment was white, white always comes first — regardless of the actual color of the garment before us.

### Tosafot Analysis

*Tosafot* sharpens this point with a textual argument. The verse says *al tzitzit ha-kanaf pesil techeiles* — add the *techeiles* to the *tzitzit* of the corner. The verse's very formulation places *techeiles* as the addition to an already-present base *tzitzit*. This means *techeiles* always comes second, and the white always comes first — not because we assess the color of each garment, but because the verse itself establishes this fixed sequence. Even if the garment happens to be blue, you do not put the *techeiles* first. White precedes blue, always.

### Unresolved Color Issue

With Rava's objection accepted, the case of the blue garment no longer serves as the explanation for the second phrase of the *mishna*. This reopens the question of how to read our *mishna* according to Rabbi. It also leaves unresolved an underlying question: is the principle of *min kanaf* understood to mean that the *lavan* strings are always white specifically, or does it mean that the strings should match the color of the garment — so that a red garment would have red *tzitzit* and blue, a blue garment blue and blue, and so on? Whether color-matching to the garment is actually required by *min kanaf* is a question the *gemara* has raised but not fully resolved at this point.

### Unresolved Material

A parallel unresolved question concerns material rather than color. The principle of *min kanaf* — that the *tzitzit* match the type of the corner — might extend beyond color to the material of which the garment is made. If the garment is linen, should the base *tzitzit* strings be linen? This is significant because *techeiles* is always wool; if a linen garment requires linen *tzitzit* as the base component matching *min kanaf*, then on such a garment the *techeiles* will always be the non-matching element and will always come second — not on account of the verse's word order alone, but because linen is what the garment dictates for the first component. These questions — whether color-matching matters and whether material-matching matters — are left open for further consideration.

## Remnant Requirement

### Rava Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְגַרְדּוּמִּין, דְּאִי אִיגַּרְדַּם תְּכֵלֶת וְקָאֵי לָבָן, וְאִי אִיגַּרְדַּם לָבָן וְקָאֵי תְּכֵלֶת – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The ruling of the mishna **is necessary only for** a case of **severed** strings. The mishna teaches **that if** the **sky-blue** strings **were severed and** the **white** ones **remain, or if** the **white** strings **were severed and** the **sky-blue** strings **remain, we have no** problem **with it,** and the ritual fringes are fit.

Having rejected the ordering interpretation as a way of reconciling the *mishna* with Rabbi's position, Rava now offers an entirely different reading: *lo nitzrecha ela le-gardumei* — the *mishna* is not discussing the initial configuration of *tzitzit* at all. It is discussing remnants — *gardumin* — what remains after some of the strings have snapped.

### Blue Snaps Scenario

*De-igdem techeiles ve-ka lavan* — if the *techeiles* strings got torn and one is left only with the white strings. After tying the knots and wraps, if the *techeiles* snaps below the last knot there is still a small remnant of the *techeiles* wrapped up within the structure. In such a case, one is still *yotzei* the *mitzvah*. The *mishna*'s statement that *techeiles* does not hold back *lavan* means: if the *techeiles* is mostly gone and only its remnant remains, that does not prevent the white from counting.

### White Snaps Scenario

*Ve-igdem lavan ve-ka techeiles* — symmetrically, if the white strings snapped and one is left with only the *techeiles*, *leis lan bah* — one is still *yotzei*. The absence of intact white strings does not invalidate the *mitzvah* when they began intact and later broke. This is what *lavan eino me'akev et ha-techeiles* means: the remnant situation does not disqualify the *mitzvah*.

### Bnei R. Chiyya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמְרִי בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: גַּרְדּוּמֵּי תְּכֵלֶת כְּשֵׁרִין, וְגַרְדּוּמֵּי אֵזוֹב כְּשֵׁרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As the sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya say: Severed** white or **sky-blue** strings **are fit, and** similarly, **severed hyssop** branches **are fit** for sprinkling the water of purification mixed with the ashes of a red heifer.

Under this reading, the *mishna* is fully compatible with Rabbi's position. Rabbi holds that both white and *techeiles* are required components — and indeed they are: you must begin with both. But if one of them subsequently snaps and only a remnant remains, the *mitzvah* is not retroactively voided. You started correctly; the breakage after the fact does not undo what was already in place.

This is supported by a teaching of the sons of Rabbi Chiyya: *gardumei techeiles kesherim* — remnants of the *techeiles* strings are still *kosher*, still valid. And similarly, *gardumei eizov kesherim* — even remnants of the hyssop used in the sprinkling of the ashes of the *parah adumah* remain valid. In each case, what started as a complete and proper component retains its *halachic* validity even when reduced to a remnant.

## Remnant Measurement

### Loop Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּר גַּרְדּוּמִּין? אָמַר בַּר הַמְדּוּרֵי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּדֵי לְעׇנְבָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What measure** do **severed** strings need to be in order to remain fit? **Bar Hamduri says** that **Shmuel says:** The strings must remain long **enough to tie them in a slipknot.**

The *gemara* asks: *ve-khamah shiur gardumin* — how large must the remnant be in order to still be considered valid? Rav Hamduri in the name of Shmuel rules: *ke-dei le-onvan* — enough that one can make a loop from it. That is, the remnant must be of sufficient length that it can be looped around itself.

### Together vs. Individually

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כְּדֵי לְעׇנְבָן – לְעׇנְבָן כּוּלְּהוּ בַּהֲדָדֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֹּל חַד וְחַד לְחוֹדֵיהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: When Shmuel says that severed strings must still be long **enough to tie them in a slipknot,** does that mean **to tie all of the** strings **together in a slipknot? Or perhaps** the strings may be even shorter, provided that they are long enough to tie **each one individually.** The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

A question arises immediately: *ke-dei le-onvan kulhu be-hadadi, o dilma ke-chad ve-chad le-chudei* — does the standard mean that the remnants collectively must be long enough to loop all of them around each other together, or does each individual remnant need to be long enough to loop around itself? The difference is significant, since looping several strings together requires more length per string than looping a single string onto itself. The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*.

### Thickness Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: אַלִּימֵי דְּלָא מִיעַנְבִי, וְאִי הֲווֹ קַטִּינֵי מִיעַנְבִי, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi asks:** If the strings are **thick** and **cannot be tied in a slipknot, but if they were** the same length but **thin** they **could be tied in a slipknot, what** is their status?

Rav Ashi poses a further question: *alimei de-lo ma'anvei va-havu ketinei ma'anvei mai* — what if the remnant strings are thick, so thick that they cannot be looped around themselves, whereas if they were thinner they would have sufficient length to loop? Is such a thick remnant still considered *ke-dei le-onvan* and thus valid?

### Thickness Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּמִינְּכַר מִצְוָתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** If the strings are long enough to be fit if they are thin, **all the more so** they are fit if they are thick, **as the mitzva** one fulfills **with them is** more **recognizable** with thicker strings.

Rav Acha son of Rava responds to Rav Ashi: *kol she-kein de-minchar mitzvata* — there is no question; thick strings are even more valid. Rav Ashi's concern was that a thick string which cannot loop might fall below the threshold, but Rav Acha points out that thickness means the string is more present, more visible, more substantial. If the string has the requisite length — that is, the length that would be sufficient to loop were it thin — then its thickness only enhances it. Greater visibility of the *mitzvah* object is an advantage, not a disqualification. The *daf* concludes here.
