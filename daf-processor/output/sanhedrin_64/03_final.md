# Sanhedrin 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Foreign God Names

### Ulla in Kalanbo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא עוּלָּא, בָּת בְּקַלְנְבוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהֵיכָא בָּת מָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּקַלְנְבוֹ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָכְתִיב ״וְשֵׁם אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים לֹא תַזְכִּירוּ״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הַכְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה – מוּתָּר לְהַזְכִּיר שְׁמָהּ. וְהָא הֵיכָא כְּתִיבָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״כָּרַע בֵּל קֹרֵס נְבוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ulla came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he lodged in** a place called **Kalnevo. Rava said to him: And where did the Master lodge?** Ulla **said to him: In Kalnevo.** Rava **said to him: But isn't it written: "And make no mention of the name of the other gods"?** Kalnevo is the name of an idol. Ulla **said to him: This** is what **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to **any** object of **idol worship that is written in the Torah, it is permitted to mention its name.** Since one may mention the idol while reading the Torah, it is permitted to mention it altogether. Rava asked: **And where is this** idol **written?** Ulla answered: **As it is written: "Bel bows down, Nevo stoops"** (Isaiah 46:1).

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion concerning swearing by and mentioning the names of foreign gods, picking up from the previous session's analysis of the *Tosfos* bearing on how *halakha* treats Christianity. The Gemara now presents the case of Ulla.

*Ki ata Ulla bat be-Kalanbo* — when Ulla came, he lodged in a place called Kalanbo, which was apparently the name of some foreign god. Rava challenged him: *ve-heicha bat mar* — where did you sleep? The issue was not the lodging itself but that Ulla had spoken the name of the town. Rava cited the verse *ve-shem elohim acherim lo tazkiru* — you shall not mention the names of other gods. How, then, could Ulla have said the name Kalanbo?

Ulla responded with a ruling in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol avodat kochavim de-ksiva be-Torah mutar lehazkir shemah* — any form of idolatrous worship whose name is already written in the Torah may be mentioned. Torah is understood here in its broader sense as *Tanakh*. The verse *kara Bel kares Nevo* — Bel bowed down, Nevo crouched — already contains the name Nevo, and the name Kalanbo (*Kal Nevo*) incorporates that name. Since it appears in *Tanakh*, it may be stated.

### Scriptural Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא כְּתִיבָא – לָא? מֵתִיב רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: רָאָה אַחַת מְרוּבָּה כְּשָׁלֹשׁ, שֶׁהִיא כְּמִגַּדְיוֹן לְשִׁילֹה, שֶׁהֵן שְׁתֵּי טְבִילוֹת וּשְׁנֵי סְפוֹגִין – הֲרֵי זֶה זָב גָּמוּר. אָמַר רָבִינָא: גַּד נָמֵי מִכְתָּב כְּתִיב, דִּכְתִיב ״הָעֹרְכִים לַגַּד שֻׁלְחָן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does this mean that **if** an idol **is not written** in the Torah, it is **not** permitted to mention its name? **Rav Mesharshiyya raises an objection** to this premise from a mishna in tractate *Zavim* (1:5): If a man **saw,** i.e., experienced, **one** gonorrhea-like discharge that lasted **as long as three** regular gonorrhea-like discharges, **which is like** the amount of time it takes to walk **from** the location of the idol **Gadyon to Shiloh, which is** the amount of time it takes to perform **two immersions and two dryings** with a towel, **he is** ritually impure with the impurity status of **a full-fledged *zav*,** even though a man generally becomes a *zav* only after three separate discharges. In any event, the mishna mentions the idol Gadyon. **Ravina says:** The idol **Gad is also written** in the Bible; **as it is written: "That prepare a table for Gad"** (Isaiah 65:11).

One might ask: what of a foreign god's name that does not appear anywhere in *Tanakh*? To address this, the Gemara brings a *Mishnah* from the laws of *zav*, in which Rav Mesharshaya teaches that one large emission is equivalent to three if it spans the time required to walk from Migiddon to Shilo — that is, the time needed for two immersions and two dryings in between. The relevance here is that Migiddon, from the root *Gad* (gimmel-dalet), is the name of an idolatrous deity, and the *Mishnah* uses it freely. The Gemara then asks where this name appears in *Tanakh*, and Ravina answers: *Gad nami k'siv* — the name Gad also appears explicitly in *Tanakh*, as the verse states *ha-orkhim le-Gad shulchan* — those who set a table for Gad, this idol.

A tangential question arises regarding names like the days of the week or the months of the year, which derive from Babylonian gods. The general assumption is that gods no longer actively worshipped pose no problem, and that if the language of daily life is suffused with such references, conversation would become nearly impossible. The names of the months, notably, were brought back from Babylonia, and some of them are the names of Babylonian deities. Whether they were still being actively worshipped at the time is a genuine question left open.

### Mocking Idolatry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כֹּל לֵיצָנוּתָא אֲסִירָא, חוּץ מִלֵּיצָנוּתָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה דְּשַׁרְיָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כָּרַע בֵּל קֹרֵס נְבוֹ ... קָרְסוּ כָרְעוּ יַחְדָּו לֹא יָכְלוּ מַלֵּט מַשָּׂא״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman says: All** types of **mockery are forbidden, except for mockery of idol worship, which is permitted, as it is written: "Bel crouches down, Nevo stoops…they stoop, they crouch down together, they could not hold back the burden"** (Isaiah 46:1–2). The verse is interpreted as meaning that they crouch in order to defecate and cannot retain their excrement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְתִיב: ״דִּבְּרוּ ... לְעֶגְלוֹת בֵּית אָוֶן יָגוּרוּ שְׁכַן שֹׁמְרוֹן כִּי אָבַל עָלָיו עַמּוֹ וּכְמָרָיו עָלָיו יָגִילוּ עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ כִּי גָּלָה מִמֶּנּוּ״. אַל תִּקְרֵי ״כְּבוֹדוֹ״, אֶלָּא ״כְּבֵידוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is** similarly **written: "They speak…the inhabitants of Samaria shall be in dread for the calves of Beth Aven; for its people shall mourn over it, and its priests shall tremble for it, for its glory [*kevodo*], because it has departed from it"** (Hosea 10:4–5). **Do not read** it as **"its glory [*kevodo*]"; rather,** read it as **its burden [*keveido*],** meaning that the idol is unable to restrain itself from defecating. These are examples of derogatory statements that are permitted only in reference to idols.

Rav Nachman then states a broader principle: *kol leitzanuta asura chutz mi-leitzanuta de-avodat kochavim* — all mockery is forbidden except mockery of *avodah zarah*, which is permitted. The proof text is the same verse from the prophet: *kara Bel kares Nevo, kirsu karu yachdav, lo yachlu malet massa* — Bel bowed, Nevo crouched, they crouched together, they could not escape the burden. The imagery implies that these gods were unable to manage their food and were forced to defecate — a deliberate literary mockery of idolatrous deities already found in the *nevi'im*. Eliyahu HaNavi famously employs a similar rhetorical strategy, and the Torah itself participates in this mockery, a point the Gemara reinforces shortly.

Another verse: *dibru la-aglot* — speak to the calves, referring to the *eglei zahav* of the northern kingdom, called *Beit Aven* (the house of sin) rather than *Beit El*. The verse continues: *yaguru shochen shamron* — those who dwell in Samaria shall fear, *ki avel alav amo* — for the nation will mourn for their calf when it is taken into exile. The priests, however, *yagilu* — will rejoice — *al kvodo ki gala mimenu* — over its honor having departed. The Gemara reads this as *al tikrei kvodo ela kveido* — do not read "its honor" but "its weight," meaning the priests will be relieved of the burden of managing all the waste of these calves.

## Motives for Idolatry

### Starving Calves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְעַתָּה יוֹסִפוּ לַחֲטֹא וַיַּעְשׂוּ לָהֶם מַסֵּכָה מִכַּסְפָּם כִּתְבוּנָם עֲצַבִּים וְגוֹ׳״ – מַאי ״כִּתְבוּנָם עֲצַבִּים״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד עֹשֶׂה דְּמוּת יִרְאָתוֹ וּמַנִּיחָהּ בְּכִיסוֹ, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁזּוֹכְרָהּ מוֹצִיאָהּ מִתּוֹךְ חֵיקוֹ וּמְחַבְּקָהּ וּמְנַשְּׁקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And now they continue to sin, and have made for themselves molten images of their silver, according to their own understanding, idols,** all of them the work of the craftsmen; of them they say: Those who sacrifice men kiss calves" (Hosea 13:2)? **What** is the meaning of the expression: **"According to their own understanding [*kitvunam*], idols"?** The word *kitvunam* is interpreted as meaning: According to their image [*ketavnitam*], and the verse **teaches that each and every** person **would make an image of his god and place it in his pocket. When he would remember it, he would remove it from his bosom and embrace it and kiss it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״זֹבְחֵי אָדָם עֲגָלִים יִשָּׁקוּן״? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק דְּבֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: שֶׁהָיוּ כּוֹמָרִים נוֹתְנִים עֵינֵיהֶם בְּבַעֲלֵי מָמוֹן, וּמַרְעִיבִים אֶת הָעֲגָלִים, וְעוֹשִׂין דְּמוּת עֲצַבִּים, וּמַעֲמִידִין בְּצַד אֲבוּסֵיהֶן, וּמוֹצִיאִין אוֹתָן לַחוּץ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאוּ אוֹתָן רָצִין אַחֲרֵיהֶן וּמְמַשְׁמְשִׁין בָּהֶן, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה חָפֵץ בְּךָ, יָבֹא וְיִזְבַּח עַצְמוֹ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the statement: **"Those who sacrifice men kiss calves"? Rabbi Yitzḥak of the school of Rabbi Ami says:** It means **that priests** of idol worship **would set their eyes on the wealthy, and** they would **starve the calves** that were objects of idol worship, **and make statues** in the **image** of those wealthy people, **and place** those statues **next to the troughs of** the calves, **and** they would **take** those calves **outside. When** the calves would **see** those wealthy people, whose image they were used to seeing near their troughs, they would **run after them and paw them.** At this point the priests would **say to** that wealthy person: The object of **idol worship,** the calf, **desires you; let** the Master **come and sacrifice himself to it.** The priests would inherit his property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי ״זֹבְחֵי אָדָם עֲגָלִים יִשָּׁקוּן״? ״עֲגָלִים יִשָּׁקוּן לִזְבֹּחַ אָדָם״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל הַזּוֹבֵחַ אֶת בְּנוֹ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אֹמֵר לוֹ: דּוֹרוֹן גָּדוֹל הִקְרִיב לוֹ, יָבֹא וְיִשַּׁק לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: That** is not the correct interpretation of the verse, as the wording **"those who sacrifice men kiss calves"** is unsuitable for that interpretation, since in that case the verse **should have** stated: **They make calves kiss to sacrifice men. Rather, Rava says** that the verse is to be understood as follows: Whenever **anyone sacrifices his son to** an object of **idol worship,** the priest **says to him:** The Master **has sacrificed a great gift [*doron*] to** the idol; therefore, he has the right to **come and kiss it.** The verse means that the reward of "those who sacrifice men" is to kiss calves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: ״וְאַנְשֵׁי בָבֶל עָשׂוּ אֶת סֻכּוֹת בְּנוֹת״, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת. ״וְאַנְשֵׁי כוּת עָשׂוּ אֶת נֵרְגַל״, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? תַּרְנְגוֹל. ״וְאַנְשֵׁי חֲמָת עָשׂוּ אֶת אֲשִׁימָא״, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? בַּרְחָא קַרְחָא. ״וְהָעַוִּים עָשׂוּ אֶת נִבְחָן וְאֶת תַּרְתָּק״, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? כֶּלֶב וַחֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says** with regard to the verse describing the peoples that were settled in Samaria by the Assyrians: **"And the men of Babylonia made Succoth-Benoth"** (II Kings 17:30); **and what is** this idol? It is the image of **a hen. "And the men of Cuth made Nergal"** (II Kings 17:30); **and what is it?** It is the image of **a rooster. "And the men of Hamath made Ashima"** (II Kings 17:30); **and what is it?** It is the image of **a bald billy goat. "And the Avvites made Nibhaz and Tartak"** (II Kings 17:31); **and what are they?** The image of **a dog and a donkey.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַסְפַרְוִים שֹׂרְפִים אֶת בְּנֵיהֶם וְאֶת בְּנוֹתֵיהֶם בָּאֵשׁ לְאַדְרַמֶּלֶךְ וַעֲנַמֶּלֶךְ אֱלֹהֵי סְפַרְוָיִם״, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? הַפֶּרֶד וְהַסּוּס. אַדְרַמֶּלֶךְ – דְּאָדַר לֵיהּ לְמָרֵיהּ בִּטְעִינָה, וַעֲנַמֶּלֶךְ – דְּעָנֵי לֵיהּ לְמָרֵיהּ בִּקְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **"And the Sepharvites burned their sons and their daughters in the fire to Adrammelech and Anammelech, the gods of Sepharvaim"** (II Kings 17:31); **and what are they? The mule and the horse.** And why are they called that? A mule is called **Adrammelech because it honors [*addar*] its master with** its **load,** i.e., because it carries the master's load, **and** a horse is called **Anammelech because it responds [*anei*] to its master,** assisting him **in battle.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף חִזְקִיָּה מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה בִּיקֵּשׁ אָבִיו לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ כֵּן, אֶלָּא שֶׁסָּכַתּוּ אִמּוֹ סָלָמַנְדְּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **The father of Hezekiah, king of Judea, also attempted to do so to him,** i.e., to burn him as an offering to an idol, **but his mother rubbed him** with the blood of **a salamander [*salamandera*],** a creature created out of fire and immune to the effects of fire, whose blood is fireproof.

Rabbi Yitzchak opens a new inquiry: *mai de-chsiv ve-ata yosifu lachato vayaasu lahem massecha mikaspam kithvunam* — now they have added sin and made for themselves a molten image from their silver, according to their image, *atzabim*. *Mai kithvunam atzabim* — what does it mean "according to their image"? The teaching is: *melamed she-kol echad ve-echad asa dmut yirato* — every individual fashioned a likeness of his own deity and kept it in his pouch. Whenever he thought of his god, he would take it out, embrace it, and kiss it — it became a personal talisman.

The Gemara then turns to the verse: *mai zovchei adam agalim yishakun* — those who slaughter people will kiss the calves. Rabbi Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Ammi explains: *she-hayu meshartei avodah zarah nosnim eineihem be-baalei mammon* — the attendants of the idols would set their eyes on wealthy individuals. They would then starve the calves — the very objects of worship — and fashion images of those wealthy people, placing the images before the calves' feeding troughs. When they brought out these starving calves and the wealthy worshippers were present, the calves, having seen only their images, would run toward them and rub against them. The priests would declare: *avodah zarah chafetz be-cha* — the god desires you, and would urge the person to offer himself as a sacrifice. That is the meaning of *zovchei adam* — the slaughter of people to the calves.

As for *agalim yishakun*, the kissing of the calves — Rava corrects the reading. The verse should be understood as: they kiss the calves *in order to* slaughter people, not as a subsequent reward. Rashi explains that it was the calves who rubbed and kissed the people, not the priests kissing the calves afterward. Rava himself offers a second reading: *kol hazoveach et bno l'avodat kochavim* — whoever slaughters his son to the gods — the priest would tell him, *doron gadol hikriv lo* — you have brought a great offering; *yavo ve-yishak lo* — come and kiss the god. That is the sense of *zovchei adam* paired with *agalim yishakun*.

The Gemara then continues with a list of idols worshipped by the various nations enumerated in Melachim. The people of Babylonia made *Succoth Benot* — which is a hen. The people of Cuth made *Nergal* — a rooster. The people of Hamath made *Ashima* — a bald goat, so called because it has no wool. The Avvim made *Nivchan* and *Tartak* — a dog and a donkey, the dog deriving from the root *nivach* (to bark). The Sepharvim burned their sons in fire for *Adrammelech* and *Anammelech*, the gods of Sepharvaim — a mule and a horse respectively. *Adrammelech* derives from *adar*, meaning it honors its master by bearing his burden, while *Anammelech* derives from *ani*, meaning it answers and follows its master even into battle.

The Gemara adds that even Chizkiyahu, king of Judah, nearly became a victim of this ritual — his father Achaz, of whom it is written *gam et bno he'evir ba'esh*, had passed his son through the fire. Since Chizkiyahu is the only son we know of, it must have been him they attempted to burn. He was saved, Rashi explains, because his mother had anointed him with the oil of a salamander — a creature said to emerge from an oven in which fire has burned for seven years — which rendered him impervious to flame.

### Sexual Freedom Motive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: יוֹדְעִין הָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ מַמָּשׁ, וְלֹא עָבְדוּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֶלָּא לְהַתִּיר לָהֶם עֲרָיוֹת בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: The Jewish people knew that idol worship is of no substance;** they did not actually believe in it. **And they worshipped idols only** in order **to permit themselves** to engage in **forbidden sexual relations in public,** since most rituals of idol worship would include public displays of forbidden sexual intercourse.

With all these forms of *avodah zarah* enumerated — and now knowing which names may be spoken freely since they appear in *Tanakh* — the Gemara returns to a deeper question: if these idols are such a mockery, why did Israel ever stray after them?

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav states: *yodin hayu Yisrael be-avodat kochavim she'ein bah mamash* — Israel always knew that idolatry was meaningless. *Ve-lo avdu avodat kochavim ela le-hatir lahem arayot be-farhesya* — they only worshipped idols in order to permit themselves illicit sexual relations in public. The motivation was not theological conviction but the desire for freedom from the Torah's demanding constraints in the realm of sexuality. It may also be that some pagan worship rituals themselves involved sexual acts, but even setting that aside, the Torah is extraordinarily demanding in matters of sexual restraint, and people wanted greater freedom.

This evokes a perennial question: when someone abandons religious observance, is it truly for theological reasons or for other motivations? The teaching suggests that in Israel's case, the questions were, as it were, the answers — the desire for freedom came first, and the theological posture followed.

### Child & Idol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: ״כִּזְכֹּר בְּנֵיהֶם מִזְבְּחוֹתָם וְגוֹ׳״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כְּאָדָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ גַּעְגּוּעִין עַל בְּנוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya raises an objection** to this statement from the following verse: **"Like the memory of their sons are their altars,** and their Asherim are by the leafy trees, upon the high hills" (Jeremiah 17:2). **And Rabbi Elazar says** that this means that the Jewish people would recall their idol worship **like a person who misses his child.** This interpretation indicates that they were truly attached to idol worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דַּאֲבִיקוּ בַּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This was the case **after** the Jewish people **became attached to** idol worship, at which point they began to actually believe in it. At first, they were drawn to idol worship only due to their lust.

Rav Mesharshya challenges Rav Yehuda: *ki zachor benehem mizbechotam* — they would remember their altars as one remembers a cherished son. Rabbi Elazar describes the emotional intensity: *ke-adam she-yesh lo ga'aguim al bno* — like a person who aches with longing for a son far away. Surely this reflects genuine passion and not mere opportunism?

The answer given is *batar d'idabku beih* — that was after they had already begun to cleave to idolatry. The initial motivation may have been the desire for sexual license, but once they spent extended time as *ovdei avodah zarah*, they began to develop a genuine, deep attachment. The point is psychologically acute: there are things that initially motivate a person, and then — once committed to an entire way of life — it is too late, because the desire has been thoroughly internalized.

A second challenge is raised from the verse *ve-natati pigrechem al pigrei giluleichem* — I will place your corpses upon the corpses of your idols. The word *giluleichem* is itself a mockery — a near-homophone for *galal*, excrement. This leads into the story of Eliyahu HaTzaddik.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְנָתַתִּי פִּגְרֵיכֶם עַל פִּגְרֵי גִּלּוּלֵיכֶם״. אָמַר: אֵלִיָּהוּ הַצַּדִּיק הָיָה מְחַזֵּר עַל תְּפוּחֵי רָעָב שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. פַּעַם אַחַת מָצָא תִּינוֹק שֶׁהָיָה תָּפוּחַ וּמוּטָל בָּאַשְׁפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof that the Jewish people engaged in idol worship for its own sake and not for the sake of engaging in forbidden sexual relations: In interpretation of the verse: **"And I shall cast your carcasses upon the carcasses of your idols"** (Leviticus 26:30), the Sages **say** that **Elijah the Righteous,** the prophet, **would search for those who were swollen with hunger in Jerusalem. He once found a child who was swollen** with hunger **and lying in the garbage.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: מֵאֵיזֶה מִשְׁפָּחָה אַתָּה? אָמַר לוֹ: מִמִּשְׁפָּחָה פְּלוֹנִית אֲנִי. אָמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר מֵאוֹתָהּ מִשְׁפָּחָה? אָמַר לוֹ: לָאו, חוּץ מִמֶּנִּי. אָמַר לוֹ: אִם אֲנִי מְלַמֶּדְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁאַתָּה חַי בּוֹ, אַתָּה לָמֵד? אָמַר לוֹ: הֵן. אָמַר לוֹ: אֱמוֹר בְּכׇל יוֹם ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד״. אָמַר לוֹ:
>
> **Translation:** Elijah **said to** the child: **From which family are you?** The child **said to him: I am from such and such family.** Elijah **said to him: Isn't** there anyone **left from that family?** The child **said to him: No** one is left **besides me.** Elijah **said to him: If I teach you something through which you will live, will you learn** it? The child **said to him: Yes.** Elijah **said to him: Say every day "Listen Israel, the Lord is our God; the Lord is One"** (Deuteronomy 6:4). The child **said to him:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַס, שֶׁלֹּא לְהַזְכִּיר בְּשֵׁם ה׳, שֶׁלֹּא לִימְּדוֹ אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Hush, so as not to mention the name of the Lord** (see Amos 6:10). The child did not want to even hear the mention of the name of the Lord, **which his father and his mother did not teach him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד הוֹצִיא יִרְאָתוֹ מֵחֵיקוֹ, וּמְחַבְּקָהּ וּמְנַשְּׁקָהּ, עַד שֶׁנִּבְקְעָה כְּרֵיסוֹ וְנָפְלָה יִרְאָתוֹ לָאָרֶץ, וְנָפַל הוּא עָלֶיהָ, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתַתִּי אֶת פִּגְרֵיכֶם עַל פִּגְרֵי גִּלּוּלֵיכֶם״. בָּתַר דַּאֲבִיקוּ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately,** the child **removed his god from his bosom and** began **hugging it and kissing it, until his stomach burst** from hunger, **and his god fell to the earth and he fell upon it, in fulfillment of that** which **is stated: "And I shall cast your carcasses upon the carcasses of your idols"** (Leviticus 26:30). This incident demonstrates that the Jewish people engaged in idol worship for its own sake. The Gemara answers: This also occurred **after** the Jewish people **became attached to** idol worship.

Eliyahu was going about Jerusalem during a famine, tending to those bloated from starvation. He found an infant, bloated and lying in a refuse heap. He asked where the child was from, and the child replied he was the sole survivor of a once-prominent family. Eliyahu asked: if I teach you something that will give you life, will you learn it? The child agreed. Eliyahu said: recite *Shma Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* — accept God as your God and abandon idolatry; that is what will give you life.

The child replied: *chas she-lo lehazkir be-shem Hashem* — God forbid that I should speak God's name — citing an actual verse, *chas le-hazkir be-shem Hashem*. He said: *she-lo limdu avi ve-imi* — my father and mother never taught me this. He immediately took his small idol from his cloak, embraced it, kissed it — until his stomach burst and he fell upon his idol, fulfilling the verse: *ve-natati pigrechem al pigrei giluleichem* — I will place your corpse upon the corpse of your idols — and in the most visceral sense, since his entire belly had exploded.

Could one say, then, that Israel engaged in idolatry merely to permit themselves sexual sins, when they were willing to die for their idols? Again: *batar d'idabku beih* — that is after they began to cleave to it. That is a profound observation: once something is truly internalized, a person will sacrifice everything for it, regardless of what originally drew them in.

### Second Temple Yeitzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וַיִּזְעֲקוּ בְּקוֹל גָּדוֹל אֶל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיהֶם״. מַאי אֲמוּר? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יוֹנָתָן: בִּיָּיא, בִּיָּיא! הַיְינוּ דְּאַחְרְבֵיהּ לְבֵיתָא, וְקַלְיֵאּ לְהֵיכְלָא, וְקַטְלִינְהוּ לְצַדִּיקֵי, וְאַגְלִינְהוּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל מֵאַרְעַיְיהוּ, וַעֲדַיִין הוּא מְרַקֵּד בֵּינַן. כְּלוּם יְהַבְתֵּיהּ לַן אֶלָּא לְקַבּוֹלֵי בֵּיהּ אַגְרָא? לָא אִיהוּ בָּעֵינַן וְלָא אַגְרֵיהּ בָּעֵינַן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another proof, from the verse: **"And they cried in a loud voice to the Lord their God"** (Nehemiah 9:4). **What did they say** in that prayer? **Rav Yehuda says, and some say** it is **Rav Yonatan** who says: **Woe, woe [*baya*, *baya*], this** evil inclination for idol worship **is what destroyed the Temple, and burned the Sanctuary, and murdered the righteous ones, and caused the Jewish people to be exiled from their land. And it still dances among us,** i.e., it still affects us. **Didn't You give it to us solely for** the purpose of our **receiving reward** for overcoming it? **We do not want it, nor do we** [want its reward].

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְתַבוּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי בְּתַעֲנִיתָא, בָּעוּ רַחֲמֵי. נְפַל לְהוּ פִּיתְקָא מֵרְקִיעָא דַּהֲוָה כְּתִיב בַּהּ: ״אֱמֶת״. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, חוֹתָמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא ׳אֱמֶת׳. נְפַק כְּגוּרְיָא דְנוּרָא מִבֵּית קָדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים. אֲמַר לְהוּ נָבִיא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: הַיְינוּ יִצְרָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָתָפְסִי לֵיהּ, אִישְׁתְּמִיט בִּינִיתָא מִינֵּיהּ, וַאֲזַל קָלֵיהּ בְּאַרְבַּע מְאָה פַּרְסֵי. אָמְרוּ: הֵיכִי נֶיעְבֵּד? דִּילְמָא מִשְּׁמַיָּא מְרַחֲמִי עֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to relate the story of the prayer in the days of Nehemiah: The people **fasted for three days** and **prayed for mercy.** In response to their prayer **a note fell for them from the heavens in which was written: Truth,** indicating that God accepted their request. The Gemara makes a parenthetical observation. **Rabbi Ḥanina says: Conclude from it** that **the seal of the Holy One, Blessed be He, is truth. The form of a fiery lion cub came forth from the chamber of the Holy of Holies.** Zechariah, **the prophet, said to the Jewish people: This is the** evil **inclination for idol worship. When they caught hold of it one of its hairs fell out, and** it let out a shriek of pain that **was heard for four hundred parasangs [*parsei*]. They said: What should we do** to kill it? **Perhaps Heaven will have mercy upon it** if we attempt to kill it, as it will certainly scream even more.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ נָבִיא: שַׁדְיוּהּ בְּדוּדָא דַאֲבָרָא, וְכַסְּיוּהּ בַּאֲבָרָא, דְּשָׁיֵיף קָלֵיהּ. דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר זֹאת הָרִשְׁעָה וַיַּשְׁלֵךְ אוֹתָהּ אֶל תּוֹךְ הָאֵיפָה וַיַּשְׁלֵךְ אֶת הָאֶבֶן הָעוֹפֶרֶת אֶל פִּיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The prophet said to them: Throw it into a container** made **of lead and cover it with lead, as** lead **absorbs sound. As it is written: "And he said: This is the evil one. And he cast it down into the midst of the measure, and he cast a stone of lead upon its opening"** (Zechariah 5:8). They followed this advice and were freed of the evil inclination for idol worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הוֹאִיל וְעֵת רָצוֹן הוּא, נִיבְעֵי רַחֲמֵי אַיִּצְרָא דַעֲבֵירָה. בְּעוֹ רַחֲמֵי, אִימְּסַר בִּידַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** When they saw that the evil inclination for idol worship was delivered into their hands as they requested, the Sages **said: Since it is an auspicious time, let us pray for mercy concerning the** evil **inclination for sin** concerning sexual matters. **They prayed for mercy, and it was** also **delivered into their hands.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַבְשׁוּהּ תְּלָתָא יוֹמֵי. בְּעוֹ בֵּיעֲתָא בַּת יוֹמָא לְחוֹלֶה, וְלָא אַשְׁכַּחוּ. אֲמַרוּ: הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? נִיבְעֵי פַּלְגָא? פַּלְגָא מֵרְקִיעָא לָא יָהֲבִי. כַּחְלִינְהוּ לְעֵינֵיהּ. אַהֲנִי בֵּיהּ דְּלָא מִיגָּרֵי אִינִישׁ בְּקָרִיבְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **imprisoned it for three days.** At that time, people **searched for a one-day-old** fresh **egg for the sick but could not find** one. Since the inclination to reproduce was quashed, the chickens stopped laying eggs. **They said: What should we do?** If **we pray for half,** i.e., that only half its power be annulled, nothing will be achieved, because **Heaven does not grant half** gifts, only whole gifts. What did they do? **They gouged** out **its eyes,** and this **was effective in** limiting **it** to the extent **that a person is no** longer **aroused to** commit incest with **his** close **relatives.**

The Gemara now transitions to a famous *agadata* about the leaders of the *Bayit Sheni* period. In the time of Nechemya, *vayiz'aku be-kol gadol el Hashem Elokeihem* — the leaders called out in a great voice to God. What did they say? Rav Yehuda, and some say Rav Yonatan, teaches: *Baya Baya* — woe, woe. They were speaking about the *yetzer hara* for *avodah zarah*. This is the force that destroyed the Temple, burned the sanctuary, caused the righteous to be murdered, sent Israel into exile — *ve-adayin hu meraked beinan* — and it is still dancing among us, here at the threshold of the Second Temple era, with people still feeling drawn to *avodah zarah*.

*Clum yahvteya lan ela le-kabuli bei agra* — the only reason God gave us this desire was so that we could receive reward by conquering it. But, they declared, *lo ihu beinan ve-lo agra beinan* — we want neither it nor its reward. We are better off without it.

The leaders fasted for three days and prayed to God to remove the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*. A little note fell from heaven bearing the word *emet* — truth — signifying divine assent. Rav Chanina notes: *chotamo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu emet* — God's seal is truth. From the Holy of Holies emerged something resembling a fiery lion cub. The *navi* told the people: *hainu yitzra de-avoda zara* — that is the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*.

This is a striking detail. The *yetzer hara* for *avodah zarah* emerges from the *Kodesh Kodashim* itself. One might understand this as a profound theological statement: the *Beit HaMikdash*, with all its physical grandeur and the *keruvim* in the inner sanctum, represents a kind of divine concession to the human need for a tangible, concrete focal point for worship. The line between that legitimate concession and the *yetzer hara* of idolatry is genuinely thin. The lion cub is also not at full strength — it is a cub — suggesting that once one begins pursuing *avodah zarah*, the *yetzer* grows stronger. Perhaps too it emphasizes how narrow the boundary is between genuine *kedushah* and its corruption.

They seized it, but as they grabbed it, a single hair fell from it, and the sound traveled four hundred *parasangs*. Concerned that heaven might reverse its decision out of compassion, they followed the *navi*'s counsel and placed the *yetzer hara* in a lead cauldron, sealing it with a leaden stone — fulfilling the verse in Zechariah: *zot ha-risha*, this is the wicked one, cast into the vessel, and the leaden stone placed over its mouth. Its voice was muffled. It was not destroyed; it was subdued.

Emboldened by their success, the leaders now prayed that they might similarly subdue the *yetzer hara* of sexual sin — since they were in a moment when heaven was receptive to their requests. God granted their request. They locked it away for three days — and immediately no fresh eggs could be found. The hens had stopped laying. All aspects of reproductive and sexual function were intertwined with this *yetzer*. They realized they could not simply eliminate it; they could not ask for half — *palga me-reki'a lo yahavi* — heaven does not give halfway. They could not ask to remove only the desire for sexual transgression while preserving healthy sexuality.

So as they released it, they managed to injure it slightly: *kachlinhu le-einei* — they blurred or blinded its eyes — *ahani bei* — and this had one measurable effect: *d'lo igrei inish bi-krovatei* — the desire for incest was diminished. The lust for a non-spouse or a *niddah* remained, but the incest drive was somewhat tamed. Not eliminated, but tamed.

Notice the intertwining of the two *yeitzarim* throughout this entire passage: Israel first worshipped *avodah zarah* to permit themselves sexual freedom; when they finally sought to remove the *yetzer hara* of *avodah zarah*, they immediately turned to the *yetzer* of sexuality. The two are deeply linked. According to this account, the deep human draw toward *avodah zarah* was something that had been internalized over centuries of pursuit — the initial motivation was sexual freedom, but what emerged was a genuine, autonomous *yetzer* for idolatry itself, one that had to be captured and sealed away. This *agadata* will be revisited in a later *perek* as well.

## Case Examples

### Pagan Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּגוֹיָה אַחַת שֶׁהָיְתָה חוֹלָה בְּיוֹתֵר, אָמְרָה: אִם תַּעֲמוֹד הָהִיא אִשָּׁה מֵחוֹלְיָהּ, תֵּלֵךְ וְתַעֲבוֹד לְכׇל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם. עָמְדָה וְעָבְדָה לְכׇל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעָה לִפְעוֹר, שָׁאֲלָה לַכּוֹמָרִים: בַּמָּה עוֹבְדִין לָזוֹ? אָמְרוּ לָהּ: אוֹכְלִין תְּרָדִין וְשׁוֹתִין שֵׁכָר וּמַתְרִיזִין בְּפָנֶיהָ. אָמְרָה: מוּטָב שֶׁתַּחֲזוֹר הָהִיא אִשָּׁה לְחוֹלְיָהּ וְלֹא תַּעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: An incident** occurred **involving a certain gentile woman who was very ill. She said: If that woman,** referring to herself, **recovers from her illness, she will go and worship every** object of **idol worship in the world. She recovered** from her illness **and** subsequently **worshipped every** object of **idol worship in the world. When she arrived at Peor she asked the priests: How does one worship this** idol? **They said to her: One eats spinach,** which causes diarrhea, **and drinks beer,** which also causes diarrhea, **and defecates before it.** The woman **said: Better for that woman,** referring to herself, **to return to her illness, and not worship an idol in such a manner.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתֶּם בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵינָן כֵּן, ״הַנִּצְמָדִים לְבַעַל פְּעוֹר״ – כְּצָמִיד פָּתִיל, ״וְאַתֶּם הַדְּבֵקִים בַּה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ – כִּשְׁתֵּי תְּמָרוֹת הַדְּבוּקוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda adds: **You, the house of Israel, are not like that** woman who could not bear the repulsiveness of Ba'al-Peor. It is stated with regard to the attitude of the Jewish people toward idol worship: **"That have attached themselves [*hanitzmadim*] to Ba'al-Peor"** (Numbers 25:5), indicating a tight attachment, **like a tightly bound cover [*ketzamid patil*]** tied firmly onto a vessel. Yet with regard to the attitude of the Jewish people toward God it is stated: **"But you who did cleave [*hadevekim*] to the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 4:4), i.e., the connection between the Jewish people and God is **like two dates that** are lightly **attached [*hadevukot*] to one another** but are not tightly pressed together.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: ״הַנִּצְמָדִים לְבַעַל פְּעוֹר״ – כְּצָמִיד עַל יְדֵי אִשָּׁה, ״וְאַתֶּם הַדְּבֵקִים בַּה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ – דְּבוּקִים מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** This comparison **was taught in a *baraita*** but with the opposite conclusion: **"That have attached themselves [*hanitzmadim*] to Ba'al-Peor"** indicates a connection that is **like a bracelet [*ketzamid*] on a woman's arm,** which is worn loosely. **"But you who did cleave to the Lord your God"** means they **actually adhered** to one another, i.e., there was a tight connection.

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav relates: *ma'aseh be-nochrit achat she-hayta chola beyoter* — a non-Jewish woman was gravely ill and on the verge of death. She declared: *im ta'amod hahi isha me-cholya* — if this woman recovers from her illness — *telech ve-ta'avod le-chol avodah zarah she-ba-olam* — I will go and worship every god in the world, covering all my bases in case any one of them was responsible for my recovery. She recovered, and set off on her tour of the world's deities.

When she came to the temple of *Peor*, she asked the priests: *be-mah ovdin le-zo* — how does one worship this god? They told her: you eat beets, drink mead, and then *matrizin be-faneha* — you defecate, in a quite unrestrained fashion, before it. She said: *mutav she-tachazor hi isha le-chola* — I would rather return to my sickness and die than worship a god in this disgusting manner. Even this non-Jewish woman, who had set out to worship every deity, drew the line at the worship of *Peor*.

This sets up a pointed contrast. The verse says of Israel: *ha-nitzmadim le-Baal Peor* — you who cleaved to Baal Peor. The word *nitzmadim* evokes *tzamid patil* — a hermetically sealed jar lid, completely vacuum-packed, utterly adhered. When Israel ran after Baal Peor, nothing could separate them from it. And compare that with how they related to God: *ve-atem ha-dvekim ba-Hashem Elokeichem* — you who cling to God — the word here is merely *dvekim*, like two dates stuck together, easily pulled apart. A severe condemnation: Israel was far more passionately attached to its *avodah zarah* than it ever was to God.

A *baraita* reads this in a softer key. *Nitzmadim* need not be read as the tight seal of a jar but as *k'tzamid al yedei isha* — like a bracelet on a woman's arm, loosely hanging, with air between it and the skin. By this reading, Israel's attachment to Baal Peor was not so extreme — and *ve-atem ha-dvekim ba-Hashem Elokeichem*, your clinging to God, is the truly profound bond. The two versions thus present opposite evaluations of Israel's relative attachment to idolatry versus God.

### Savta ben Alas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּסַבְטָא בֶּן אַלָּס, שֶׁהִשְׂכִּיר חֲמוֹרוֹ לְגוֹיָה אַחַת. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעָה לִפְעוֹר, אָמְרָה לוֹ: הַמְתֵּן עַד שֶׁאֶכָּנֵס וְאֵצֵא. לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּצְאָה, אָמַר לָהּ: אַף אַתְּ הַמְתִּינִי עַד שֶׁאֶכָּנֵס וְאֵצֵא. אָמְרָה לוֹ: וְלֹא יְהוּדִי אַתָּה? אָמַר לָהּ: וּמַאי אִיכְפַּת לִיךְ? נִכְנַס, פָּעַר בְּפָנָיו וְקִינַּח בְּחוֹטְמוֹ. וְהָיוּ כּוֹמָרִין מְקַלְּסִין לוֹ וְאוֹמְרִים: מֵעוֹלָם לֹא הָיָה אָדָם שֶׁעֲבָדוֹ לָזֶה בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident with regard to Ba'al-Peor. **The Sages taught:** There was **an incident involving** a Jew named **Sabbeta ben Alas, who rented out his donkey** and his services **to a certain gentile woman.** He was driving his donkey behind her, and **when she arrived at Peor, she said to him: Wait** here **until I go in and come out. After she came out, he said to her: You too wait** for me **until I go in and come out. She said to him: Aren't you Jewish?** Why, then, are you worshipping idols? **He said to her: And what do you care? He entered and defecated before** the idol, **and wiped** himself **with its nostril,** as he wanted to demean the idol as much as possible. **But** he was unsuccessful, as **the priests** of Peor **were praising him and saying: No person has ever worshipped it** before **with this** excellent form of worship. Although he intended to demean Ba'al-Peor, he actually worshipped it.

The Gemara brings a further story: *ma'aseh be-Savta ben Alas* — the case of Savta ben Alas (or, according to some readings, from the city of Alas), a Jew who worked as a donkey driver. He rented his donkey to a non-Jewish woman and took her on a journey. When they arrived at a temple of *Peor*, she said: wait here while I go in to perform my worship and then come out. After she emerged, he said to her: wait here by the donkey while I also go in. She said: *ve-lo Yehudi ata* — are you not a Jew? He replied: *u-mah ichpat lach* — what concern is it of yours? He entered, *paar be-fanav* — defecated before the idol — and then wiped himself on the idol's nose. The attendants of the *avodah zarah* praised him effusively, saying that no one had ever worshipped their god in such a perfect manner. A final ironic mockery: the most degrading act imaginable was understood by the worshippers as the highest form of devotion.

These narratives together close out a sustained exploration of the *yetzer hara de-avodah zarah* and its deep entanglement with sexual drive, the process of internalization, and the Gemara's persistent undercurrent of mockery directed at idolatrous practice.

## Molech Act & Intent

### Defecation Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפּוֹעֵר עַצְמוֹ לְבַעַל פְּעוֹר – הֲרֵי זֶה עֲבוֹדָתוֹ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמִיכַּוֵּין לְבִיזּוּי. הַזּוֹרֵק אֶבֶן לְמַרְקוּלִיס – זוֹ הִיא עֲבוֹדָתוֹ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמִיכַּוֵּין לְמִירְגְּמֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* is that **one who defecates before Ba'al-Peor** is obligated to bring a sin-offering to atone for idol worship, as **this is its** typical form of **worship, even if he intends to demean** the idol. Likewise, **one who throws a stone at Mercury** is obligated to bring a sin-offering to atone for idol worship, as **this is its** typical form of **worship, even if he intends to stone it.**

The Gemara now returns to strictly *halachic* discussion. *Ha-po'er atzmo le-Baal Peor harei zo avodato* — if one defecates before Baal Peor, that constitutes its act of worship and is therefore *avodah zarah*. This is so *af al gav de-michavein le-vazuya* — even if the person's intent was to degrade the idol, to perform an act of contempt or shame toward it. Intent to disgrace does not exempt one; the act itself is the act of worship.

### Throwing Stones

*Ha-zorek even le-Markulis harei zu avodato* — one who throws a stone to Markulis has performed its act of worship — *af al gav de-michavein le-mirgemei* — even if one intended the throw as an act of stoning, that is, of attacking the idol rather than venerating it. The act is determinative.

### Rav Menashya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְנַשֶּׁה הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל לְבֵי תוֹרְתָּא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הִיא דְּקָאֵי הָכָא. שְׁקַל פִּיסָּא, שְׁדָא בַּיהּ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מַרְקוּלִיס הִיא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״הַזּוֹרֵק אֶבֶן לְמַרְקוּלִיס״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Menashe was going to** a place called **Bei Torta.** The people there **said to him: There is** an object of **idol worship situated here** in this pile of stones. Rav Menashe **picked up a stone and threw** it **at** the idol to demean it. **They said to him: It is Mercury,** and it is worshipped by throwing stones at it. Rav Menashe **said to them: We learned** in the mishna that **one who throws a stone at Mercury** as a manner of worship is liable, whereas I intended to demean it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא שְׁאַל בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״הַזּוֹרֵק אֶבֶן בְּמַרְקוּלִיס״ תְּנַן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּמִיכַּוֵּין לְמִירְגְּמֵיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֵיזִיל אֶישְׁקְלַהּ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֶחָד הַנּוֹטְלָהּ וְאֶחָד הַנּוֹתְנָהּ חַיָּיב, כֹּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא רַוְוחָא לַחֲבֶירְתַּהּ שָׁבֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Menashe **went** and **asked** the Sages in **the study hall** whether his interpretation of the mishna was correct. **They said to him: We learned** in the mishna that **one who throws a stone at Mercury** is liable, which implies that he is liable **even if he intends to stone it** in order to demean it. Rav Menashe **said to** the Sages: If so, **I will go and take** back the stone I threw. **They said to him: Both one who removes it and one who places it is liable,** as **each and every one** of the stones taken away from Mercury **leaves space for another** stone. Taking a stone away from Mercury provides a place for other stones to be thrown at it.

A related episode illustrates the point. Rav Menashya was traveling to a place called Bei Tortah. People warned him: there is an idol there. He noticed the idol and, to show his contempt, *shakal patta shada bah* — took a clod of earth and threw it at the idol. Then they added: by the way, it is *Markulis*. He said: the *Mishnah* says one who throws a stone *to* Markulis — I threw it *at* it. He went to the *beit midrash* to confirm his reading, and they told him: no, even *be-Markulis* — even throwing at it — constitutes *avodah zarah*, since even a contemptuous throw is the established mode of worship.

He then asked: should he go and remove the stone, so that it should not remain there as his offering to the *avodah zarah*? They told him: *echad ha-notlah ve-echad ha-notnah chayav* — both placing a stone and removing a stone constitute acts of worship. The reason: *chada ve-chada revacha le-chavereih shevik* — each stone that is removed makes room for the next worshipper to add his own. Removal is itself a participatory act in the cult. He who inadvertently commits the act and then attempts to undo it has only compounded the transgression.

This raises a fundamental question that the *Rishonim* must address. Earlier, the Gemara discussed — particularly in the debate between Abaye and Rava — whether one who worships an idol *me-ahava u-me-yira* (from love or fear of a person, not genuine theological conviction) is liable. Rava's position seemed to require that worship be motivated by genuine belief. If so, how can throwing a stone in contempt, or defecating in degradation, constitute *avodah zarah*?

Tosafot addresses this in two ways. One approach holds that in these cases one would not be subject to the death penalty — only a *korban* would be required, since the act was performed without the proper intent, analogous to *shogeg*. Even if the intent completely changes the character of the act, the formal act of *avodah zarah* was performed, and that carries its own consequence.

Tosafot's second approach holds that even contemptuous worship implies a background belief in the deity. The person who defiles the idol is not saying it does not exist; he may believe in it but be expressing anger, or he may be performing the degrading act in a paradoxically worshipful way — the way one might take a *lulav* resentfully and still have taken the *lulav*.

A third approach, drawn from commentators on the Rambam, introduces a significant distinction: the requirements of intent — *ahava ve-yira* — apply specifically when someone is fashioning a new god from nothing, or when someone designates an object ad hoc as a deity for the first time. In such a case, the inner act of acceptance is constitutive; without it, nothing has happened. But when a god already has an established following, recognized rituals, and an interpreted cult, the act speaks for itself. If one performs the established act of worship of an established religion, it is *avodah zarah* regardless of what is occurring in one's mind, because the act carries established meaning within the community of worshippers. This reading significantly limits the scope of the earlier *ahava ve-yira* discussion: that discussion pertains only to cases involving a newly designated deity.

## Molech Prohibition

### Molech vs Idolatry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָתָנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְקָתָנֵי מוֹלֶךְ. אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין: תְּנַן כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מוֹלֶךְ לָאו עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֶחָד לַמּוֹלֶךְ וְאֶחָד לִשְׁאָר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לַמּוֹלֶךְ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁלֹּא לַמּוֹלֶךְ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The *halakhot* of one's liability for **idol worship are taught** in the mishna above (60b), **and** the *halakhot* of one's liability for the worship of **Molekh are taught** separately, in this mishna. Therefore, **Rabbi Avin says: We learn** this mishna **according to** the opinion of **the one who says** that the ritual of **Molekh is not idol worship** but is a form of witchcraft or superstition, **as** a dispute over this matter **is taught** in a *baraita*: Both **one** who transfers his child **to the** priests of **Molekh and one** who transfers his child **for** the purpose of worshipping **other idols** are **liable.** Molekh is cited merely as an example. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** One who transfers his child **to the** priests of **Molekh** is **liable,** but if he transfers him to another object of idol worship, **not to Molekh,** he is **exempt.**

The *Mishnah* at the start of a new section lists, among the transgressions punishable by *sekilah*, both *avodah zarah* and the worship of *Molech* as distinct categories. That they are listed separately suggests that *Molech* worship may not simply be a subspecies of *avodah zarah* but a distinct transgression altogether. Rabbi Avin teaches that the *Mishnah* follows the opinion that *molech lav avodah zarah hi* — the Molech ritual is not *avodah zarah*. It is a pagan ritual but not the worship of a deity. This is comprehensible: one can imagine rituals believed to engage metaphysical forces without constituting the worship of a god in the conventional sense.

A *baraita* records the dispute explicitly: the majority opinion holds that the prohibition applies equally to *Molech* and to any other form of *avodah zarah* — *echad ha-Molech ve-echad she'ar avodah zarah chayav*. Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *la-Molech chayav, she-lo la-Molech patur* — one is liable only for the Molech ritual specifically, not for performing this act in the service of any other deity.

The reasoning behind this debate is exegetical. If Molech worship is a form of *avodah zarah*, the Torah would not need to single it out — it would already be prohibited by the general prohibition against *avodah zarah*. The Torah's singling it out must therefore add something: namely, that one is liable even when performing this act not in its conventional manner, which would apply to any deity. Conversely, if Molech is not *avodah zarah*, the Torah mentions it because it is a distinct pagan ritual — and then the prohibition is inherently limited to this specific ritual, performed specifically to the Molech, whatever that entity is.

### Two Components

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּמְסְרֶנּוּ לְכוֹמָרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִזַּרְעֲךָ לֹא תִתֵּן לְהַעֲבִיר לַמֹּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yannai says:** One is **not liable** for passing his child through fire to Molekh **unless he hands him over to** the **priests** of Molekh, **as it is stated: "And you shall not give any of your offspring to pass them over to Molekh"** (Leviticus 18:21), which indicates that the prohibition is to give, i.e., to hand the child over to the priests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יָכוֹל הֶעֱבִיר וְלֹא מָסַר יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא תִתֵּן״. מָסַר לַמּוֹלֶךְ וְלֹא הֶעֱבִיר, יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לְהַעֲבִיר״. מָסַר וְהֶעֱבִיר שֶׁלֹּא לַמּוֹלֶךְ, יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לַמֹּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that one who **passed** his child through fire **but did not hand** him **over** to the priests of Molekh **should be liable.** Therefore, **the verse states: "You shall not give."** If one **handed over** his son **to the** priests of **Molekh but did not pass** him through fire, one **might** have thought that **he should be liable.** Therefore, **the verse states: "To pass,"** indicating that passing is also necessary. If one **handed over** the child **and passed** him through fire, **not to** priests of **Molekh** but rather to priests of another object of idol worship, one **might** have thought that **he should be liable.** Therefore, **the verse states: "To Molekh."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָסַר וְהֶעֱבִיר לַמּוֹלֶךְ, וְלֹא בָּאֵשׁ – יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״לְהַעֲבִיר״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְךָ מַעֲבִיר בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ בָּאֵשׁ״. מָה לְהַלָּן בָּאֵשׁ, אַף כָּאן בָּאֵשׁ. וּמָה כָּאן מוֹלֶךְ, אַף לְהַלָּן מוֹלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** If one **handed over** his child **and passed** him **over to the** priests of **Molekh, but** he did **not** pass him **through the fire,** one **might** have thought that **he should be liable. It is stated here,** in the verse: **"To pass," and it is stated there,** in another verse: **"There shall not be found among you one who passes his son or his daughter through the fire"** (Deuteronomy 18:10). **Just as there,** the verse is referring to passing one's child **through the fire, so too here,** the reference is to passing one's child **through the fire. And just as here,** the verse is referring to one who passes his child over to the priests of **Molekh, so too there,** the reference is to **Molekh** alone, excluding any other object of idol worship.

Rabbi Yannai clarifies the requirements articulated in the *Mishnah*: *eino chayav ad she-yimsarenu le-meshartei avodah zarah* — one is not liable unless one hands the child over to the attendants. The verse says *lo titten le-ha'avir la-Molech* — do not give over to pass through to the Molech. Two distinct acts are required: handing over and passing through fire. If one handed over without the fire-passing, or passed through fire without the formal hand-over to the priests, one is not liable. Both components must be fulfilled.

The *Mishnah* itself states this explicitly: *eino chayav ad she-yimsor la-Molech ve-ya'avir ba-esh* — one is not liable until one hands over to the Molech and passes through fire. *Masar la-Molech ve-lo he'evir ba-esh* — handed over but did not pass through; *he'evir ba-esh ve-lo masar la-Molech* — passed through but did not hand over: in either case, *eino chayav*.

A *braita* elaborates: *ve-natata lo* — perhaps passing through fire without handing over the child to a priest would be sufficient for liability? No — *talmud lomar lo titten* — the verse says "do not give," so the giving over is essential. Conversely: *masar la-Molech ve-lo he'evir* — perhaps handing over without the fire-passing suffices? No — *talmud lomar le-ha'avir* — the verse specifies the passing through. Each component is derived independently.

The *braita* then asks: *masar ve-he'evir she-lo la-Molech yachol yehei chayav* — perhaps one is liable even if the act was performed for some other deity, not the Molech? No — *talmud lomar la-Molech* — the verse specifies to the Molech. Each word of the verse — *lo titten, le-ha'avir, la-Molech* — is read as limiting or expanding the law in a distinct way.

### Verse Integration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָסַר וְהֶעֱבִיר לַמּוֹלֶךְ, וְלֹא בָּאֵשׁ – יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״לְהַעֲבִיר״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְךָ מַעֲבִיר בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ בָּאֵשׁ״. מָה לְהַלָּן בָּאֵשׁ, אַף כָּאן בָּאֵשׁ. וּמָה כָּאן מוֹלֶךְ, אַף לְהַלָּן מוֹלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** If one **handed over** his child **and passed** him **over to the** priests of **Molekh, but** he did **not** pass him **through the fire,** one **might** have thought that **he should be liable. It is stated here,** in the verse: **"To pass," and it is stated there,** in another verse: **"There shall not be found among you one who passes his son or his daughter through the fire"** (Deuteronomy 18:10). **Just as there,** the verse is referring to passing one's child **through the fire, so too here,** the reference is to passing one's child **through the fire. And just as here,** the verse is referring to one who passes his child over to the priests of **Molekh, so too there,** the reference is to **Molekh** alone, excluding any other object of idol worship.

The next question is whether fire is definitionally required. The verse in the Molech prohibition (*lo titten le-ha'avir la-Molech*) does not mention fire. However, a parallel verse in Devarim states: *lo yimatze becha ma'avir beno u-vito ba-esh* — let not be found among you one who passes his son or daughter through fire. The Gemara integrates the two: *mah le-hallan ba-esh, af kan ba-esh* — just as that verse involves fire, so too the Molech prohibition involves fire. And conversely, *u-mah kan Molech, af le-hallan* — just as the first verse specifies Molech, the fire-verse too refers to Molech. The two passages describe the same ritual, and reading them together supplies both elements — fire and the specific deity — to the combined prohibition.

### Definition of Zera

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הֶעֱבִיר כׇּל זַרְעוֹ – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״מִזַּרְעֲךָ״ – וְלֹא כׇּל זַרְעֲךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, says:** One who **passed all his offspring** through fire to the priests of Molekh is **exempt, as it is stated: "Of your offspring,"** indicating: **But not all your offspring.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אָשֵׁי: הֶעֱבִירוֹ סוֹמֵא, מַהוּ? יָשֵׁן, מַהוּ? בֶּן בְּנוֹ וּבֶן בִּתּוֹ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi raises a dilemma:** If one **passed** through the fire a child of his who is **blind, what is** the *halakha*? Furthermore, if one passed his child through the fire while the child was **asleep, what is** the *halakha*? Does the child need to be capable of passing through on his own, or is that not necessary? Likewise, if one passed **his son's son or his daughter's son** through the fire, **what is** the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט מִיהָא חֲדָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״כִּי מִזַּרְעוֹ נָתַן לַמֹּלֶךְ״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְךָ מַעֲבִיר בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ בָּאֵשׁ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ. בֶּן בְּנוֹ וּבֶן בִּתּוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּתִתּוֹ מִזַּרְעוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Resolve at least one** of these dilemmas, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Why** must **the verse state: "Because he has given of his offspring to Molekh"** (Leviticus 20:3)? What is added by this statement? **Because it is stated: "There shall not be found among you one who passes his son or his daughter through the fire," I have** derived **only** that it is prohibited for one to pass **his son or daughter. From where** do I derive that one who passes **his son's son or his daughter's son** is also liable? **The verse states: "When he gives of his offspring"** (Leviticus 20:4). The term "offspring" indicates that grandchildren are included. This resolves one of Rav Ashi's dilemmas.

The *Mishnah* speaks of giving over one's *zera* — seed. The verse *lo yimatze becha ma'avir beno u-vito ba-esh* specifies *bno u-vito* — his son or his daughter. A *braita* asks: from son or daughter alone, I might think the prohibition extends only to direct children. How do I know it applies to grandchildren — *ben bno u-bat bito*? The verse *ki mi-zaro natan la-Molech* — for he gave from his seed — uses the word *zera*, which is broad enough to include grandchildren. A second verse, *be-tito mi-zaro la-Molech*, supplies an additional *derashah*: *zaro* in the singular might suggest only *zera kasher* — legitimate offspring. How do I know that even *zera pasul* — a *mamzer* — is included? The extra phrase *mi-zaro* is read to include even such a case.

Rav Acha the son of Rava raises an additional ruling: *he'evir kol zaro patur* — if a person passed all of his children through the Molech ritual at one and the same time, he is exempt, since the verse says *mi-zaro* — from his seed — but not *kol zaro* — all of his seed. Tosafot clarifies: this cannot mean that performing the act for one child and then later for another retroactively exempts the first violation. Rather, the case is either one in which the person had only a single child (so that one child constitutes all his seed) or one in which all the children were passed through simultaneously.

Rav Ashi then raises questions left unresolved: *he'eviro suma mahu, yashen mahu* — what is the ruling if the child was blind, or if the child was asleep at the time? *Ben beno bat bito mahu* — what about grandchildren? The grandchildren question is resolved from the *braita* above. The questions of blind or sleeping children are left unresolved.

### Manner of Passing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיַּעֲבִירֶנּוּ דֶּרֶךְ הַעֲבָרָה. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁרָגָא דְּלִיבְנֵי בְּמִצְעֵי, נוּרָא מֵהַאי גִּיסָא וְנוּרָא מֵהַאי גִּיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says:** One is **not liable** for passing his child through fire to Molekh **unless he passes him** in the typical **manner of passing.** The Gemara asks: **What is considered** the typical manner of passing? **Abaye says:** The child is taken by foot along **a latticework [*sirega*] of bricks in the middle,** between the **fire on this side and** the **fire on that side.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כְּמַשְׁוַורְתָּא דְּפוּרַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The typical manner of passing is **like the leaps** of children **on Purim.** It was customary to light a bonfire on Purim inside a pit, and children would amuse themselves by leaping over the bonfires. Passing one's child over a fire in such a fashion is the typical manner of passing a child over to Molekh.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיַּעֲבִירֶנּוּ דֶּרֶךְ עֲבָרָהּ. הֶעֱבִירוֹ בָּרֶגֶל – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava:** One is **not liable unless he passes** his child through the fire in the typical **manner of passing. If he passed him by foot,** he is **exempt.** Evidently, the passing ritual is performed by leaping and not by walking.

The *Mishnah* requires that the child be passed *derekh ha-avara* — in the normal manner of passing. Abaye explains: *sharga de-livnei mi-beinitzia* — there would be a row of bricks running down the center, with fires burning on either side; the child would walk along the bricks between the two fires. Rava offers a different image: *ke-mashvarta de-puraya* — like the jumping over a bonfire that people do on *Purim*. The child would jump over a fire pit.

A *baraita* supports Rava's position: *eino chayav ad she-ya'avirenu derekh ha-avara* — one is liable only for the normal mode of passing. *He'eviro be-regel patur* — if one merely walked through without the jumping motion, one is exempt. Since walking disqualifies, the act must consist of leaping, not walking — consistent with Rava's description of jumping over a fire pit rather than walking between two fires.

### Recipients

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל יוֹצְאֵי יְרֵיכוֹ. הָא כֵּיצַד? בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ – חַיָּיב, אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ אָחִיו וַאֲחוֹתוֹ – פָּטוּר. הֶעֱבִיר עַצְמוֹ – פָּטוּר, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְחַיֵּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** one **is liable only for** passing **his descendants. How so?** If one passed **his son or his daughter** through the fire, he is **liable.** If he passed **his father, or his mother, his brother, or his sister,** he is **exempt.** If one **passed himself** over to Molekh, he is **exempt. And Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, deems** him **liable.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד לַמּוֹלֶךְ, וְאֶחָד לִשְׁאָר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לַמּוֹלֶךְ – חַיָּיב, שֶׁלֹּא לַמּוֹלֶךְ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Both** one who passes his child through fire **to Molekh and** one who passes his child through fire **to other** objects of **idol worship** are **liable. Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** If he passes him over **to Molekh,** he is **liable,** but if he transfers him to another object of idol worship, **not to Molekh,** he is **exempt.**

The liability extends downward through the generations — son, daughter, grandchildren — but not upward or laterally. *Aviv ve-imo, achiv ve-achoto* — passing one's father, mother, brother, or sister through the fire: *patur* — exempt. The prohibition is directed at one's descendants, not one's ancestors or siblings.

### Self-Sacrifice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְךָ״ – בְּךָ בְּעַצְמְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** who deems one who passes himself over to Molekh liable? **The verse states: "There shall not be found among you** one who passes his son or his daughter through the fire" (Deuteronomy 18:10), and the term **"among you"** is interpreted homiletically to mean: **Among yourself.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן לָא דָּרְשִׁי ״בְּךָ״? וְהָתְנַן: אֲבֵידָתוֹ וַאֲבֵידַת אָבִיו – שֶׁלּוֹ קוֹדֶמֶת. וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאי טַעְמָא? וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: אָמַר קְרָא ״אֶפֶס כִּי לֹא יִהְיֶה בְּךָ אֶבְיוֹן״ – שֶׁלּוֹ קוֹדֶמֶת לְשֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do the Rabbis not interpret** the term **"among you"** as referring to oneself? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 33a): If one finds **his lost item and his father's lost item,** taking care of **his** own lost item **takes precedence. And we said** in discussion of that mishna: **What is the reason** for this? **And Rav Yehuda said** that it is because **the verse states: "Only so that there shall be no needy among you"** (Deuteronomy 15:4), meaning that one must avoid becoming needy. Therefore, if one lost an item, tending to **his** lost item **takes precedence over** tending to the lost item **of any** other **person.** Apparently, this interpretation of the verse is based on the understanding that the term "among you" is referring to oneself, i.e., one must take care of himself so that he will not become needy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם מֵ״אֶפֶס״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **And there,** in that discussion, the *halakha* is derived **from** the word **"only,"** a limiting term, which is interpreted to mean that in preventing destitution one should begin with himself.

*He'evir atzmo patur* — if a person passed himself through the Molech fire, he is also exempt, according to the majority. *Rabbi Elazar be-Rabbi Shimon mechayiv* — Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon holds him liable.

Ulla explains Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon's reasoning: the verse *lo yimatze becha ma'avir beno u-vito ba-esh* contains the word *becha* — in you. He reads *becha be-atzmecha* — do not even do this to yourself. The self is included in the prohibition.

The Gemara challenges: the Rabbis also use *becha* in other contexts to derive similar points. The *Mishnah* teaches that one's own lost object takes precedence over one's father's — *shelo kodemet le-shel kol adam* — and this is derived from the verse *efes ki lo yihyeh becha evyon*, read as: do not let yourself become poor. If the Rabbis use *becha* to mean "you yourself," why do they not do so here?

The Gemara answers: *hatam me-Efes* — there, it is not merely the word *becha* that does the work; it is the word *efes* (eliminate, make disappear) in conjunction with *becha* that teaches one must eliminate poverty from oneself first. The word *becha* alone, in the Molech verse, does not carry the same force for the majority, and therefore they exempt one who passes himself through the fire. Rabbi Eliezer bar Rabbi Shimon, however, reads *becha* independently and derives from it that one is liable even for oneself.
