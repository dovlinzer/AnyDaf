# Bava Batra 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Years & Brothers

### Three Witness Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִים, וְאֶחָד מִצְטָרֵף עִמָּהֶם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שָׁלֹשׁ עֵדֻיוֹת, וְהֵן עֵדוּת אַחַת לַהֲזָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the testimony was given by **three brothers,** each of whom testify about one year, **and another** unrelated individual **joined with** each of the brothers as the second witness, **these are three** distinct **testimonies** and they are accepted by the court. If they were to be considered one testimony, it would not be accepted, as brothers may not testify together. **But they are one testimony for** the purpose of rendering them as **conspiring** witnesses, and the payment is divided among them.

We begin Daf 57 by finishing the *mishna* that dealt with the concept of three separate witness groups each testifying to one year of *chazakah*. The *gemara* had devoted considerable discussion to the principle of *davar ve-lo chatzi davar*: Rabbi Akiva would not permit splitting the testimony across separate witness groups, but the *chachamim* permit it because each year is treated as an independent unit. The *Rashbam* explained this independence by noting that each group of witnesses testifies to everything they observed during their respective year. *Tosafot* went further, arguing that testimony covering one year of use possesses independent legal standing before *beis din* in its own right — even if not used to establish *chazakah*, it could serve as evidence entitling the property owner to reimbursement for produce consumed. This is the conceptual basis for permitting three separate groups of witnesses to testify to three separate years.

The *mishna* then concluded with a case involving three brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — each testifying to one year of *chazakah*, with a fourth person, whom we may call Mordechai, combining with each brother individually to testify across all three years. This yields three independent testimonies in total. Although two brothers cannot testify together on the same matter, here each brother testifies to a year on which his brothers are not testifying — so the arrangement is valid. It bears noting that there are four people involved in this scheme: three brothers and one additional witness.

### Signature Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא שְׁטָרָא דַּהֲוָה חֲתִימִי עֲלֵיהּ בֵּי תְרֵי, שָׁכֵיב חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ. אֲתָא אֲחוּהּ דְּהַאי דְּקָאֵי, וְחַד אַחֲרִינָא, לְאַסְהוֹדֵי אַחֲתִימַת יְדֵיהּ דְּאִידַּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain document that had the signatures of two** individuals **on it. One of** the signatory witnesses **died,** and when the court sought to ratify the document, which requires either that the witnesses personally attest to the validity of their signatures or that two other witnesses attest to the validity of the signatures, **the brother of the** witness **who remained** alive **and one other** individual **came to testify with regard to the signature of the other,** deceased, witness, while the living witness attested to his own signature.

With this framework established, the *gemara* now presents the following case: *hahu shtara de-hava chatimi lei bei trei* — a certain document upon which two people had signed as witnesses. One of them, let us call him Mordechai, died. *Asa achua de-hai de-kai ve-chad achrina le-as-hudi a-chatimat yada de-idach* — the brother of the surviving signer, Reuven, came forward together with some additional stranger, Barak, to testify regarding the signature of the deceased Mordechai.

The structure of this arrangement is as follows: Reuven testifies to his own signature — which requires no corroboration, since a person may attest to his own signature without the need for a second witness. Mordechai cannot attest to his own signature since he has died. So Shimon, Reuven's brother, together with Barak, comes to verify Mordechai's signature. When two people attest to a signature they did not themselves make, two witnesses are required, and this pair satisfies that requirement without any familial disqualification between Shimon and Barak.

### Ravina's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְבַר רָבִינָא לְמֵימַר: הַיְינוּ מַתְנִיתִין – שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִים, וְאֶחָד מִצְטָרֵף עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina thought to say** that this case **is** the same as the *halakha* in **the mishna,** which states that if testimony was given by **three brothers,** each of whom testify about one year, **and another,** unrelated individual **joined with** each of the brothers as the second witness, these are considered to be three distinct testimonies. Similarly, in this case, one brother attests to his own signature, while the other brother attests to the signature of the deceased witness.

*Savar Ravina le-meimar hainu matnisin* — Ravina was inclined to say that this case is essentially our *mishna*: just as the case of *gimmel achin ve-echad mitztarfin immahem* — three brothers and a fourth person combining for a three-year *chazakah* — is valid because each piece of testimony stands independently, so too here. Though Shimon is Reuven's brother, they are testifying on different things: Reuven to his own signature and Shimon together with Barak to Mordechai's. It is true that in combination they validate the entire document, but one might equally object to the *mishna*'s case that Reuven and his brothers together account for the full *chazakah* testimony — and yet the *mishna* validates it precisely because each year's testimony is independent, each is free from a brother-witness on the same unit. By the same logic, the testimony of Shimon and Barak concerns only Mordechai's signature, which is a discrete unit, and Reuven contributes nothing to that unit.

### Rav Ashi Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם לָא נָפֵיק נְכֵי רִיבְעָא דְמָמוֹנָא אַפּוּמָּא דְאַחֵי, הָכָא נָפֵיק נְכֵי רִיבְעָא דְמָמוֹנָא אַפּוּמָּא דְאַחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to him: Are** these cases **comparable? There, the property less one-quarter,** i.e., three-quarters of the property in question, **is not removed** from the possession of the prior owner based **upon the mouth,** i.e., the testimony, **of brothers.** Each brother provides only half of the testimony for each year about which he testifies. **Here, the property less one-quarter is removed** from the possession of the debtor detailed in the document based **upon the mouth of brothers.** One brother attests to his own signature, which constitutes half of the testimony, while the other signature is authenticated by the testimony of the other brother and another individual. It follows that three-quarters of the testimony is given by brothers, and it is therefore invalid.

*Amar lei Rav Ashi: mi dami?* — Rav Ashi objected: you cannot draw this comparison. *Hatam lo nafik michd ei riva de-mamona apuma de-chad* — in the *mishna*, it is not the case that three-quarters of the money is extracted on the basis of a single party. *Hacha nafik tlata riv'ei demamona apuma de-achai* — but in this case, three-quarters of the money is being extracted on the basis of brothers.

The reasoning proceeds as follows. The underlying principle is that two brothers are treated as no better than one person — just as one person cannot serve as two witnesses by testifying to one thing and then stepping aside to testify to another, brothers share the same disability. So in our *mishna*, if we collapse Reuven, Shimon, and Levi into a single party and add Mordechai, the combined testimony is split equally: half rests on Reuven and half on Mordechai. That is acceptable.

Now apply this to our case. Reuven attests to his own signature — that constitutes half of the document's validation. Reuven's brother Shimon (treated as equivalent to Reuven) together with Barak attests to Mordechai's signature — that constitutes the other half. But Shimon is tantamount to Reuven, so three-quarters of the document's validation is traceable to Reuven and his brothers, with only Barak providing independent weight. This is invalid. Importantly, the same disqualification would apply even if Reuven himself — rather than his brother — attempted to testify to both his own signature and Mordechai's signature in concert with Barak; one person cannot validate three-quarters of a document. Since Reuven himself could not do this, Shimon in his stead cannot do it either.

### Tosafot's Solution

*Tosafot* notes that there is in fact a way to validate this document. Reuven need not testify to his own signature directly. Instead, he could make his signature available for others to examine — leaving a sample of his signature, for instance — and then withdraw entirely from the testimony. Two other witnesses, say Shimon and a stranger or any other pair unrelated to each other, could then come forward and testify: "We recognize this signature on the document as matching Reuven's signature, which we have seen." This would constitute valid testimony on Reuven's signature by outside witnesses with no familial connection to each other. In that configuration, the *shtar* is validated without Reuven himself testifying, eliminating the problem of brothers combining across three-quarters of the document. The difficulty arises specifically when Reuven actively testifies to his own signature while his brother simultaneously testifies to Mordechai's — that combination is what Rav Ashi invalidates.

## Possession & Chazakah

### Rashbam's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶם חֲזָקָה, וְאֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם חֲזָקָה? הָיָה מַעֲמִיד בְּהֵמָה בֶּחָצֵר; תַּנּוּר, רֵיחַיִם וְכִירַיִים; וּמְגַדֵּל תַּרְנְגוֹלִים; וְנוֹתֵן זִבְלוֹ בֶּחָצֵר – אֵינָהּ חֲזָקָה. אֲבָל עָשָׂה מְחִיצָה לִבְהֶמְתּוֹ – גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְכֵן לַתַּנּוּר וְכֵן לַכִּירַיִים וְכֵן לָרֵיחַיִם; הִכְנִיס תַּרְנְגוֹלִין לְתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת; וְעָשָׂה מָקוֹם לְזִבְלוֹ – עָמוֹק שְׁלֹשָׁה אוֹ גָבוֹהַּ שְׁלֹשָׁה; הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **These are** uses of property **that have** the means to establish the **presumption** of ownership, **and these are** uses of property **that do not have** the means to establish the **presumption** of ownership: If one **would stand an animal in a courtyard;** or if one would place **an oven, a millstone, or a stove** there; **or** if one **raises chickens** in a courtyard, **or places his fertilizer in a courtyard,** these actions **are not** sufficient to establish the **presumption** of ownership. **But** if one **constructed a partition ten handbreadths high to** contain **his animal, and similarly** if he constructed a partition **for** his **oven, and similarly** if he constructed a partition **for** his **stove, and similarly** if he constructed a partition **for** his **millstone;** or if one **brought chickens into the house, or** if he **fashioned a place** in the ground **for his fertilizer** that is **three** handbreadths **deep or three** handbreadths **high,** these actions **are** sufficient to establish the **presumption** of ownership.

We now turn to the next *mishna*: *elu devarim she-yesh lahem chazakah ve-elu devarim she-ein lahem chazakah* — the following acts constitute a valid *chazakah* and the following do not. This classic *mishna* structure — resembling *bameh madlikin u-vameh ein madlikin* and similar formulations — presents two lists of acts and evaluates each for legal effect.

An important interpretive question must first be resolved. The *Rashbam* notes vigorously that *Rabbeinu Chananel* understood this *mishna* to concern *chazakah* of *taking possession* — the mode by which one acquires ownerless property. The *Rashbam* rejects this emphatically and reads the *mishna* as returning to the main subject of this *perek*: the three-year *chazakah* that serves as evidence of ownership against a claimant. The earlier discussion of a *chazakah* to transfer property was something of a digression; we are now back to the primary subject. The *gemara*'s own analysis, as we will see, strongly supports the *Rashbam*'s reading. The *mishna* thus asks: which acts of use, sustained over three years, constitute sufficient evidence that the occupier owns the property?

### Temporary Uses

The *mishna* opens with a list of acts that do *not* create a valid *chazakah*. *Ha-ma'amid behemah be-chatzer* — stationing one's animals in a shared courtyard — does not work. Similarly, *tannur ve-kirayim* — placing an oven or stove in the courtyard — *rechayim* — a millstone — *megadel tarnigolim* — raising chickens — and *venoten zivlo be-chatzer* — depositing one's manure in the courtyard — none of these constitute a *chazakah*.

The common thread is that these are all temporary uses. The owner of the property, though he has not objected for three years, can plausibly argue: I tolerated this because it was not bothering me, and I always retained the ability to remove you whenever I wished. Since you harvested nothing that should have been mine, since no permanent change was made, there is no strong inference that you must have bought this property. He was simply a lenient neighbor. In the terminology we will encounter shortly, he was not *makpid* — he had no pressing reason to object — and therefore the failure to object proves nothing. Note that while the *mishna* specifies a *chatzer*, the question of *shutafim* — joint owners — already hovers over this analysis and will receive close attention in the *gemara*.

### Permanent Changes

The *mishna* then presents the acts that *do* create a valid *chazakah*. *Asah mechitza le-vehemto* — if one built a partition to pen one's animals — *gavo'ah asarah tefachim*, at least ten *tefachim* high — that creates a *chazakah*. The same applies to building an enclosure for an oven or stove, and *le-rechayim* — for a millstone. *Hichnis tarnigolim le-toch ha-bayit* — bringing chickens into the house itself — also works, since this transforms the interior of the structure in a way that is not a normal temporary use and would cause genuine disturbance. Likewise, *ve-asah makom le-zivlo* — if one designated and enclosed a space for one's manure with a partition of at least three *tefachim* — that too constitutes a valid *chazakah*.

The structural dividing line is clear: temporary use that leaves no permanent mark does not establish *chazakah*; acts that alter or claim the space in a lasting way do. One might wonder whether some of these acts would provoke an immediate objection — and indeed the *mishna* does not resolve the question of whether they work instantly or only after three years of sustained possession. What the *mishna* establishes is the qualitative distinction between the two categories, which the *gemara* now undertakes to explain.

## Two Types Chazakah

### Ula's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר עוּלָּא: כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר קָנָה – בְּנִכְסֵי חֲבֵירוֹ קָנָה, כֹּל שֶׁאִילּוּ בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר לֹא קָנָה – בְּנִכְסֵי חֲבֵירוֹ לֹא קָנָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is different** in **the first clause,** where having an animal stand in a courtyard is insufficient to establish the presumption of ownership, **and what is different** in **the latter clause,** where constructing a partition is sufficient to establish the presumption of ownership? **Ulla says:** With regard to **any** act **which, if** one **were** to perform it **on** ownerless property such as the **property of a convert** who dies without heirs he would **acquire** that property, that same act is sufficient for him to **acquire the property of another** if he performed it over the course of three years, provided it is accompanied by the claim that the property had been purchased. Conversely, **any** act **that if** one **were** to perform it **on** the **property of a convert** who died without heirs he would **not acquire** that property, that same act is not sufficient for him to **acquire the property of another.** Taking possession of ownerless property requires an act to be done with regard to the property itself, such as constructing a partition, but merely having an animal stand there is not sufficient. Therefore, it does not establish the presumption of ownership.

The *gemara* asks *mai shena reisha u-mai shena seifa* — what is the difference between the first list and the second? Ula answers: *kol she-ilu be-nichsei ha-ger kanah be-nichsei chaveiro kanah* — anything that would work to acquire ownerless property (*hefker*) also works to prove ownership of another's property; *kol she-ilu be-nichsei ha-ger lo kanah be-nichsei chaveiro lo kanah* — anything that would not work for *hefker* does not work for the three-year *chazakah* either.

The phrase *nichsei ha-ger* — the property of a *ger* who died without heirs — refers to acquiring truly ownerless property through a *chazakah* of taking possession. Ula's claim is therefore that the two types of *chazakah* follow the same rule. What attracted Ula to this formulation is that the classic *chazakah* of taking possession is *na'al, gader u-faratz* — locking, fencing, breaching — acts of improvement. The second half of our *mishna*, where fences and enclosures are built, tracks precisely these acts of improvement, while the first half involves mere use without improvement. So within the *mishna* itself, the two categories appear to align neatly with the two types of *chazakah*.

The *Rashbam*, as noted earlier, rejects *Rabbeinu Chananel*'s interpretation here and insists that *nichsei chaveiro* — "his fellow's property" — refers not to a willing seller but to an adversarial claim: the three-year evidentiary *chazakah* against a challenging former owner. Ula is asserting that the logic of both types of *chazakah* is essentially the same, and the *mishna* can be read as confirming this.

### Rav Sheshes Refutes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: וּכְלָלָא הוּא?! וַהֲרֵי נִיר – דִּבְנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר קָנָה, בְּנִכְסֵי חֲבֵירוֹ לֹא קָנָה! וַהֲרֵי אֲכִילַת פֵּירוֹת – דִּבְנִכְסֵי חֲבֵירוֹ קָנָה, בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר לֹא קָנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to this** explanation: **And is** this an established **principle? But there is plowing, which,** if performed **on** the **property of a convert** who died without heirs one **acquires** it, but if performed **on** the **property of another** one does **not acquire** it. **And** additionally, **there is consumption of produce** for the duration of three years, **which,** if performed **on** the **property of another** one **acquires** it by establishing the presumption of ownership, but if performed **on** the **property of a convert** who died without heirs one does **not acquire** it. These cases contradict Ulla's claim that the modes of acquisition are analogous.

*Maskif lah Rav Sheshes: ve-chlala hu?* — Rav Sheshes challenges: is this really a valid rule? He produces two counterexamples that decisively show the two types of *chazakah* are not equivalent.

First: *ve-harei nir* — what about *nir*, the act of plowing land without planting? *De-ve-nichsei ha-ger kanah* — this would work to acquire ownerless property, since you have improved the land. *Be-nichsei chaveiro lo kanah* — but this was discussed earlier in the *perek*: it does not work for the three-year evidentiary *chazakah*. The reason is clear: improving another's land does not deprive the owner of anything. He can simply let you plow as much as you like and then expel you, having received the benefit of a plowed field. This demonstrates the fundamental distinction maintained throughout this *perek*: *chazakah* of taking possession is about benefiting the property; *chazakah* of three years is about taking benefit *from* the property.

Second, the converse: *ve-harei achilas peiros* — harvesting produce. *Be-nichsei chaveiro kanah* — this is the paradigmatic three-year *chazakah*: I have been harvesting produce that should have been yours for three years, and that is my proof of ownership. *Be-nichsei ha-ger lo kanah* — but this does not work for acquiring ownerless property, because you have not improved it. These two counterexamples demonstrate that the rule Ula proposed does not hold. The two types of *chazakah* operate on fundamentally different principles, even if they sometimes overlap in particular cases. The question then returns: what *does* explain the *mishna*'s two lists?

### Chatzer Shutafim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: הָכָא בַּחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין עָסְקִינַן – דִּבְהַעֲמָדָה כְּדִי לָא קָפְדִי, אַמְּחִיצָה קָפְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: Here we are dealing with a courtyard belonging to partners, where they are not particular with regard to the mere placing** of items in the courtyard, but **are particular with regard to** the construction of **a partition.** The presumption of ownership is established only where the lack of a protest indicates that the prior owner concedes that the property is no longer his. The co-owner's silence in the face of his partner using the courtyard for a temporary purpose does not indicate a concession, but silence in the face of one who constructed a partition is a concession.

*Ella amar Rav Nachman amar Rabba bar Avuha* — Rav Nachman, in the name of Rabba bar Avuha, provides the key: *hacha be-chatzer ha-shutfim askinan* — the *mishna* is not discussing an ordinary situation where I enter a stranger's private yard. It is discussing a *chatzer* jointly owned by two people — a shared courtyard, of the type so commonly assumed in *gemara* discussions, where two dwellings open onto a common space.

This shift in context explains everything. When I am a total stranger entering Eli's private yard and placing my chickens there, that would ordinarily provoke an immediate objection. There is no question that the owner would be *makpid*. Such an act could potentially establish *chazakah* even quickly. But if Eli and I are joint owners of a courtyard, different norms apply. Certain minor or temporary usages — parking animals there, storing items — are the sort of thing a partner will tolerate without protest because the space is partly his anyway. He is simply not *makpid* on such things. But to build a permanent structure or erect a fence — *a-mechitzah kapdi* — even a partner would object to that, because it represents a genuine claim of exclusive possession over something jointly owned.

A parenthetical note: there was earlier discussion in the *perek* about whether *shutafim* can ever establish *chazakah* against one another. The short answer is that they can, when the act in question is the kind of provocation that even a partner would not let pass without objecting. And this entire discussion assumes three years, in accordance with the *Rashbam*'s reading.

### Mekpid Principle

The governing principle that emerges is thus one of *hakpadah* — objection or vested protest. In a shared courtyard, temporary uses that do not permanently alter the space are the kind of thing partners tolerate; the owner is not *makpid*, and his silence proves nothing about a sale. But structural changes — building a fence, enclosing a space, bringing animals into the interior of the house — are things a partner *would* object to, and his sustained failure to object over three years is meaningful evidence that something more than mere tolerance is at work, namely that he sold or transferred his share.

## Shutafim & Neder

### Neder Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהַעֲמָדָה כְּדִי לָא קָפְדִי?! וְהָא תְּנַן: הַשּׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁנָּדְרוּ הֲנָאָה זֶה מִזֶּה – אֲסוּרִין לִיכָּנֵס לֶחָצֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are** they **not particular with regard to the mere placing** of items? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 45b): **Partners who through vows prohibited** themselves **from deriving benefit from one another are forbidden to enter into a courtyard** jointly owned by them, since each one has a portion in it, and it would be considered a violation of one's vow if one were to benefit from any part of the other's property? This indicates that partners are particular even with regard to passage through the field; all the more so are they particular with regard to placing animals or vessels in the field.

The *gemara* now challenges this explanation. *U-be-ha'amada kidei lo kapdi?* — Are you truly saying that partners are not *makpid* on even these temporary uses in a shared courtyard? *Ve-hatnan: ha-shutfim she-nadru hana'ah zeh mi-zeh asurin likhanes le-chatzer* — but we have a *mishna* that states: partners who have vowed *hana'ah* from one another are forbidden from entering the shared courtyard altogether!

The background is vivid: two co-owners of a courtyard fall into a dispute. In the heat of the argument, each declares a *neder* forbidding the other from benefiting from his possessions. Now they must determine how to use the courtyard, which each owns in part. If using the courtyard is a form of *hana'ah* from one another — drawing benefit from the other's share — they cannot enter it. And indeed the *mishna* rules that they are forbidden. This proves that use of a shared courtyard is something a partner *is* considered to have a stake in, something he would potentially object to — for otherwise it would not constitute *hana'ah* prohibited under a *neder*. But if that is the case, why does the *gemara* say partners are not *makpid*, and why doesn't temporary use of the courtyard establish *chazakah*?

One might note the slight asymmetry in the cases — the *neder* *mishna* seems to address even the act of walking through the courtyard, while the *chazakah* discussion concerns placing animals or storage items there — but the basic tension is clear and is what drives the *gemara*'s question.

### Leniency Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בַּחֲצַר הַשּׁוּתָּפִין; וְאִיכָּא דְּקָפְדִי וְאִיכָּא דְּלָא קָפְדִי; גַּבֵּי מָמוֹנָא – לְקוּלָּא, גַּבֵּי אִיסּוּרָא – לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: This and that,** i.e., the rulings of the mishna in tractate *Nedarim* as well as the rulings of the mishna here, are stated **with regard to a courtyard belonging to partners, and** the reason for the difference in the rulings is that **there are those who are particular** with regard to the other partner placing items in or passing through the courtyard, **and there are those who are not particular.** Therefore, in the mishna here, which issues a ruling **concerning monetary matters,** the *halakha* is **to** be **lenient,** and it is assumed that the partner is not particular about placing items in the courtyard, and the presumption of ownership is established. In the mishna in tractate *Nedarim*, which issues a ruling **concerning ritual matters,** the *halakha* is **to** be **stringent,** to prevent one from violating a vow.

*Rav Pappa amar idi v'idi be-chatzer shutfin* — Rav Pappa resolves the tension by returning to the *chatzer shutafim* framing while introducing a distinction between monetary law and *issur*. *Ve-ika de-kapdi ve-ika de-lo kapdi* — some partners are *makpid* on even minor courtyard use and some are not; people vary. *Gabi mamona le-kulah, gabi issura le-chumra* — in monetary matters, we rule leniently; in matters of *issur*, such as *neder*, we rule stringently.

The *Rashbam* unpacks what this means in concrete terms. In the context of *chazakah* — a monetary matter — if someone comes to *beis din* asking whether he may use the shared courtyard to station his animals, we would allow it, reasoning that the partner is probably not *makpid*, since some people are and some are not. And since we would have permitted the use, and since the user is already a part-owner of the yard, the failure to object cannot be read as strong evidence of a sale. The basis of *chazakah* is adverse possession — the inference that the owner's silence implies he sold the property. But if his silence is equally consistent with ordinary tolerance, that inference cannot be drawn. Put differently: Eli can later say he simply was not *makpid* on this temporary use, and since that is a plausible explanation consistent with his character as an easygoing partner, his silence proves nothing.

In the context of *neder*, by contrast, we must be stringent: since there exist partners who *are* indeed *makpid* on such use, I cannot assume my partner is lenient. If he happens to be among those who are *makpid*, then my using the courtyard — receiving benefit from his share — would violate my vow. Therefore, the *neder* prohibition applies even to uses that in monetary contexts we would have permitted.

### Ravina Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא קָפְדִי, וְהָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא; דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ וִיתּוּר אָסוּר בְּמוּדַּר הֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Actually,** partners **are not particular** about placing items in the courtyard, **and** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna in tractate *Nedarim*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** negligible benefits ordinarily **waived** are **forbidden in** the case of **one prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit** from another. In other words, one prohibited by a vow may not derive any benefit from the other, even a benefit that people commonly are not particular about and allow others to enjoy without first receiving permission. Therefore, although people are ordinarily not particular about others passing through their property, according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, one who is prohibited by a vow from deriving benefit from his partner is prohibited from walking on the property.

Ravina offers a different resolution. *Le-olam lo kapdi* — partners are never *makpid* on minor temporary uses of a shared courtyard. This is not a spectrum; it is a universal rule among partners. Small usages are simply tolerated, and that is why they cannot establish *chazakah* — there is nothing adverse about them. No inference of a sale can be drawn from silence regarding something no partner would ever protest.

If so, why does the *neder* prohibition apply? *Ha mani Rabbi Eliezer* — that ruling follows the position of Rabbi Eliezer. *Rabbi Eliezer omer afilu vitor assur be-muder hana'ah* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that even a benefit which the other party habitually waives and does not care about is forbidden under a *neder* of *hana'ah*. The *Rashbam*'s example: a shopkeeper who always throws in an extra egg for every customer. If you are *muder hana'ah* from this shopkeeper and he gives you the same extra egg, Rabbi Eliezer says this is forbidden, because you have received something from him even though he waives it universally for everyone. The *chachamim* disagree: if it is something universally waived, it is not truly a benefit transferred from one person to another in a meaningful sense. Nevertheless, the ruling that partners may not enter their shared courtyard under a *neder* follows Rabbi Eliezer's stricter view: even a benefit that no partner objects to is still a received benefit, and therefore forbidden under a *neder*.

With Ravina's approach, there is thus no contradiction between the *chazakah* and *neder* rulings. For *chazakah*: partners genuinely never object to such use, so it proves nothing. For *neder*: Rabbi Eliezer rules that benefit is benefit regardless of whether the giver minds.

## Laundry Exception

### Rav Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי בְּנָאָה: בַּכֹּל שׁוּתָּפִין מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה, חוּץ מִן הַכְּבִיסָה – שֶׁאֵין דַּרְכָּן שֶׁל בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהִתְבַּזּוֹת עַל הַכְּבִיסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Bena'a: Partners may prevent each other** from using their courtyard **for any** purpose **except for** washing **laundry.** This is **because it is not the way of Jewish women to be degraded over** washing **laundry** by laundering their clothing in a public area. Therefore, they must be allowed to launder in the courtyard.

The *gemara* continues: *Rav Yochanan* states in the name of the *rabbana*: *ve-chol shutfin me-akvin zeh es zeh chutz min ha-kevisah* — co-owners of a shared courtyard can prevent one another from virtually any non-standard use of the space. This reflects the general rule that the members of a jointly-owned courtyard may regulate its use and exclude usages they did not originally contemplate. However, there is one exception: they cannot prevent one another from doing laundry in the courtyard.

### Tzniut Reason

The reason is stated directly: *she-ein darkan shel bnot yisrael le-hitbazot al ha-kevisah* — because it is not the manner of Jewish women to subject themselves to the indignity that comes with doing laundry at the river. The *Rashbam* explains that washing clothes in the river requires women to stand in the water, exposing their legs up to the calf — *legalot shok* — lifting their garments to keep them from getting wet. This violates the standard of modesty that Jewish women have set for themselves. Doing laundry in the semi-privacy of the shared courtyard, while perhaps an imposition on the other partners, is a necessity that cannot be denied. Therefore, even co-owners who wish to restrict courtyard use cannot prohibit laundry.

It is worth noting, as a participant aptly observed during the shiur, that this standard is framed as one the women have accepted for themselves — *she-ein darkan*, it is not their custom. The *gemara* is recognizing and protecting a standard of dignity that *bnot Yisrael* have established on their own terms, rather than imposing a restriction on them from without.

### Averting Eyes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְעֹצֵם עֵינָיו מֵרְאוֹת בְּרָע״ – אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: זֶה שֶׁאֵין מִסְתַּכֵּל בְּנָשִׁים בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹמְדוֹת עַל הַכְּבִיסָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאִיכָּא דַּרְכָּא אַחְרִיתָא – רָשָׁע הוּא! אִי דְּלֵיכָּא דַּרְכָּא אַחְרִיתָא – אָנוּס הוּא! לְעוֹלָם דְּלֵיכָּא דַּרְכָּא אַחְרִיתָא, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי – מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמֵינַס נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In connection with the matter of laundry being washed in public, the Gemara quotes the homiletic interpretation of a verse: "He that walks righteously, and speaks uprightly; he that despises the gain of oppressions, that shakes his hands from holding of bribes, that stops his ears from hearing of blood, **and shuts his eyes from looking upon evil"** (Isaiah 33:15). **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: This** is referring to one **who does not gaze at women while they are standing over the laundry,** as it was common for them to stand in the water and raise the hems of their garments while laundering their clothing. The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If** it is referring to a case **where there is another way** by which the one walking could reach his destination, then one who walks past the women, consequently placing himself in a situation where he will be tempted to gaze at them, **is wicked.** Alternatively, **if** it is referring to a case **where there is no other way** by which he can reach his destination, then **he** is **a victim of circumstance,** so why is he required to shut his eyes? The Gemara explains: **Actually,** it is referring to a case **where there is no other way** by which he can reach his destination, **and even so, he is required to compel himself** to avoid gazing at the women.

This leads the *gemara* to a connected discussion. The verse praises one who *otzem einav me-re'ot be-ra* — closes his eyes from seeing evil. *Amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba: zeh she-nistakel be-nashim be-sha'ah she-omdot al ha-kevisah* — this refers to a man who refrains from gazing at women while they stand doing laundry.

*Heichi dami* — the *gemara* asks: under what circumstances is this praiseworthy? *Ei de-ika darcha achrina* — if there was another route available to him, he is not praiseworthy; he is simply behaving as required. A person who could have gone a different way but chose to walk past women doing laundry and then made a virtue of not looking has done nothing admirable. *Ei de-leika darcha achrina* — if there was no other route, then he had no choice but to pass, and a person who does not avert his eyes in that situation has done nothing formally wrong — he had to get from one place to another. So what is the case in which he is praiseworthy?

*Le-olam de-leika darcha achrina* — there was no alternative route. *Ve-afilu hachi mibaye leh le-meinas nafshih* — nevertheless, even in such a case, one can make the extra effort. As the *Rashbam* explains: *le-hatot einav le-tzad acher* — to turn his eyes to the side. *De-hanu de-mishtabach beh kera de-anas nafshih chasid hu* — the one who is praised is the one who, though he had no option but to walk that way, exerted himself to avoid looking. This requires a degree of extra effort — perhaps some physical inconvenience or care — and it is precisely that voluntary exertion of restraint that makes it praiseworthy.

This *gemara* sits within a broader set of discussions in the *gemara* about men who encounter women and must manage their gaze. What is noteworthy here is that the framing is somewhat distinct from those other contexts: the emphasis is not primarily on the man's own risk of illicit thought, but on the women's privacy and dignity — the very same concern that motivated the laundry exception in the first place. The women's desire not to be observed while in a state of partial exposure is what the law is protecting. At the same time, the description of the act as *ra* — evil — indicates that the man's own conduct remains an independent concern. Both dimensions are present.

### Dress Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי בְּנָאָה: חָלוּק שֶׁל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם – כֵּיצַד? כֹּל שֶׁאֵין בְּשָׂרוֹ נִרְאֶה מִתַּחְתָּיו. טַלִּית שֶׁל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם – כֵּיצַד? כֹּל שֶׁאֵין חֲלוּקוֹ נִרְאֶה מִתַּחְתָּיו טֶפַח. שֻׁלְחָן שֶׁל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם – כֵּיצַד? שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישֵׁי גְּדִיל, וּשְׁלִישׁ גְּלַאי – וְעָלָיו קְעָרוֹת וְיָרָק. וְטַבַּעְתּוֹ מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara quotes additional matters that Rabbi Yoḥanan learned from Rabbi Bena'a. **Rabbi Yoḥanan asked Rabbi Bena'a: How** should **the garment of a Torah scholar** worn under his clothes be fashioned? He replied: He can wear **any** garment long enough **that his flesh is not visible from beneath it.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: **How** should **the cloak of a Torah scholar** be fashioned? He replied: He can wear **any** garment long enough **that a handbreadth of his garment** worn under his clothes **is not visible from beneath it.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: **How** should **the table of a Torah scholar** appear? He replied: **Two-thirds** of the table is covered with **a cloth, and one-third** is **uncovered, and upon** that third are **dishes and vegetables. And its ring,** used to hang the table, should be positioned **on the outside,** not on the side that faces the one who is eating.

The *gemara* then transitions: *ba'ei minei Rabi Yochanan me-Rabi Bana'ah* — Rabi Yochanan asked Rabi Bana'ah a question about standards of dress and comportment for Torah scholars. Since one statement was made in the name of Rabi Yochanan together with Rabi Bana'ah, the *gemara* now digresses into a series of exchanges between them — and this exchange, touching on *tzniut* as it applies to men, flows naturally from the themes just discussed.

## Garments & Furniture

### Tunic & Talit

*Chaluk shel talmid chacham keitzad?* — What should the tunic of a *talmid chacham* look like? The answer: *kol she-ein besaro nir'eh mitachtav* — it should be long enough that no skin is visible beneath it. The tunic extends fully to the feet; no part of the body is exposed. The concern for *tzniut*, we note, applies here to men — specifically to Torah scholars, whose bearing and dress should reflect their dignity.

*Talit shel talmid chacham keitzad?* — And the outer garment? *Kol she-ein chaluko nir'eh mitachtav tefach* — it should extend so that less than a *tefach* of the tunic is visible beneath it. The outer garment need not literally reach the floor; a small margin of exposed tunic at the bottom is tolerable. But the outer garment should cover almost the full length of the tunic, presenting a composed and dignified appearance.

### Scholar's Table

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֻׁלְחָן שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים – כֵּיצַד? שְׁנֵי שְׁלִישֵׁי גְּדִיל, וּשְׁלִישׁ גְּלַאי – וְעָלָיו קְעָרוֹת וְיָרָק. וְטַבַּעְתּוֹ מִבַּחוּץ. וְהָא תַּנְיָא: טַבַּעְתּוֹ מִבִּפְנִים! לָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא דְּאִיכָּא יָנוֹקָא, הָא דְּלֵיכָּא יָנוֹקָא. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דְּלֵיכָּא יָנוֹקָא; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא דְּאִיכָּא שַׁמָּעָא, הָא דְּלֵיכָּא שַׁמָּעָא. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דְּאִיכָּא שַׁמָּעָא; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא בִּימָמָא, הָא בְּלֵילְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **How** should **the table of a Torah scholar** be kept? **Two-thirds** of the table is covered with **a cloth, and one-third** is **uncovered, and upon** that third are **dishes and vegetables. And its ring,** used to hang the table, should be positioned **on the outside,** not on the side that faces the one who is eating. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **its ring** should be positioned **on the inside?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which states that its ring should be positioned on the outside, is referring to a case **where there is a child** who may play with the ring and overturn the table, while **that** *baraita*, which states that its ring should be positioned on the inside, is referring to a case **where there is no child** present. **And if you wish, say** instead that both **this and that** refer to a case **where there is no child** present, **and** this is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, which states that its ring should be positioned on the inside, is referring to a case **where there is an attendant** who may bump into the ring, while **that** *baraita*, which states that its ring should be positioned on the outside, is referring to a case **where there is no attendant.** **And if you wish, say** instead that both **this and that** refer to a case **where there is an attendant, and** this is **not difficult: This** *baraita*, which states that its ring should be positioned on the outside, is referring to when the meal is eaten **during the day,** when the attendant can see the ring and avoid it, while **that** *baraita*, which states that its ring should be positioned on the inside, is referring to when the meal is eaten **during the night.**

*Shulchan shel talmid chacham keitzad?* — What should a scholar's table look like? *Shnei shlishi gadil u-shlish gilui* — two-thirds covered with cloth, one-third left exposed.

The *Rashbam* explains this arrangement: the cloth covers two-thirds of the table's surface, and the final third — the outer edge — is exposed for the placement of dishes and cups. This way, the fine cloth is not soiled by the plates. The food is laid out on the cloth, while the diners eat with their dishes on the exposed portion of the table.

An alternative reading is that the exposed third is in the middle, with cloth at both edges. In either case, the principle is that the table presents an orderly, proportioned appearance. There is something aesthetically refined in the arrangement — the cloth and the bare surface in measured proportion.

*Ve-taba'ato mibachutz* — and the ring by which the removable table-top is hung against the wall when not in use should face outward, toward the side where the attendant moves. The reasoning is that it is less of an obstacle there than facing the seated diners inward. However, the *gemara* notes a *braita* that places the ring on the inside — *taba'ato mibifnim* — and resolves the apparent contradiction through a series of distinctions.

*Lo kashya: ha de-ika yanuka ha de-leika yanuka* — if there is a small child at the table, the ring is kept on the outside to protect the child from getting entangled in it; if there is no child, it is kept on the inside. *Ve-iba'is eima ha ve-ha de-leika yanuka* — alternatively, in a case where there is no child in either scenario: *ha de-ika shamash ha de-leika shamash* — the determining factor is whether there is an attendant. If there is an attendant moving back and forth, keep the ring on the inside so as not to impede the diners; if there is no attendant, put it on the outside. And if one prefers yet another resolution: *ha b'imama ha b'leilya* — the daytime versus nighttime, since in the dark an attendant is more likely to stumble on the ring, and so it should be kept on the inside at night.

### Ignorant Person's Table

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ – דּוֹמֶה לִמְדוּרָה, וּקְדֵרוֹת מַקִּיפוֹת אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: All of the above is referring to the table of a Torah scholar, **but** the table **of an ignoramus is similar to a bonfire,** where the fire is in the center **and pots surround it.** Similarly, the table of an ignoramus is arranged with the food in the center and is surrounded by plates.

*Ve-shel am ha-aretz* — and the table of an *am ha-aretz*? *Domeh le-medurah* — it resembles a campfire. Just as a campfire has the fire burning in the center surrounded by the cooking pots arranged around the perimeter, so too the table of an *am ha-aretz* has the cloth and the food massed in the center, *ha-mapah ve-ha-lechem be-emtza*, with the dishes scattered at the outer edges, *ha-kearot misaviv*. This is the inversion of the scholar's table — disorganized and lacking the measured, dignified proportion characteristic of *talmidei chachamim*.

### Beds & Storage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִטָּה שֶׁל תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים – כֵּיצַד? כֹּל שֶׁאֵין תַּחְתֶּיהָ אֶלָּא סַנְדָּלִין בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, וּמִנְעָלִין בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים. וְשֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ – דּוֹמָה לְאוֹצָר בָּלוּס.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: **How** should **the bed of Torah scholars** be kept? He replied: It is acceptable **as long as there** is **nothing except sandals beneath it during the summer, and shoes** beneath it **during the rainy season,** i.e., winter. **And** the bed **of an ignoramus** is **similar to a cluttered [*balus*] storehouse,** as he keeps a wide array of items beneath it.

*Mitah shel talmidei chachamim keitzad?* — What should a scholar's bed look like? *Kol she-ein tachteha ella sandalim bi-yemot ha-chamah, minalim bi-yemot ha-geshamim* — there should be nothing stored beneath it except for the shoes of the season that is not currently being worn. In the summer, the sandals — whose stiffer construction is better suited to muddy terrain — are stored underneath; in the winter, the softer shoes. The *Rashbam* explains: *derech linol minalim bi-yemot ha-chamah ve-sandalim bi-yemot ha-geshamim bishvil ha-tit* — the custom is to wear the harder sandals during the rainy, muddy season and softer shoes in the summer — so whichever pair is not in current use sits beneath the bed, clean and out of the way.

The point is restraint and order: a *talmid chacham* keeps nothing unnecessary under his bed. One pair of out-of-season shoes — that is all.

*Ve-shel am ha-aretz* — the bed of an *am ha-aretz* is described as *domeh le-otzar balus* (or per the *Rashbam*, *balus* with a *samech*, akin to *issa belusa* in Masechet Shabbat, meaning a mixed or jumbled mass): under the bed is an indiscriminate accumulation of all manner of belongings, a complete disorder. The contrast with the scholar's bed is total: discipline and proportion on one side, chaos on the other.
