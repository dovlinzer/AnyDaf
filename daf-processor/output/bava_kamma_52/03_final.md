# Bava Kamma 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Liability Transfer

### Intro & Core Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבַר עָלָיו הָרִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא כִּסָּהוּ. וְרִאשׁוֹן מֵאֵימַת מִיפְּטַר? רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה, דְּאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מָנִי – חַד אָמַר: מִשֶּׁמַּנִּיחוֹ מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ, וְחַד אָמַר: מִשֶּׁיִּמְסוֹר לוֹ דׇּלְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if **the first** partner **passed by** the pit **and did not cover it,** and then the second also passed by it and did not cover it, the second is liable. The Gemara asks: **And from what point is the first exempt** from responsibility for the pit? **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say** a ruling **in the name of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, who says** it **in the name of Rabbi Mani,** but each one presents it differently: **One says: From** the time **he leaves** the other partner **using it, and one says:** The first is absolved of responsibility only **when he conveys to him his bucket,** with which he will draw water from the pit.

Daf 52 continues the sugyot related to the *bor* — the uncovered pit — and picks up at the bottom of 51b. The *mishnah* there established that when a first owner passed over a well without covering it and a second owner then failed to cover it as well, one of them bears liability. Depending on the textual version, either the second owner is liable or the first remains so. But this creates an immediate difficulty: if both simply passed by and neither did anything, then each was negligent, and both should be liable. The *gemara* must therefore construct a scenario in which the first owner can genuinely transfer his liability to the second — a point at which his own obligation ends.

### Implicit vs Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַמְדַלֶּה מַיִם מִן הַבּוֹר, וּבָא חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ: הַנַּח לִי, וַאֲנִי אֲדַלֶּה מַיִם; כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִנִּיחוֹ מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ – פָּטוּר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁיִּמְסוֹר לוֹ דׇּלְיוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: This dispute is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to one **who draws water from a cistern, and his friend comes and says to him: Leave me and I will draw water, once** the first person **leaves** the second person while the second is **using** the cistern, the first is now **exempt** if he did not cover it. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** The first person is not absolved of responsibility until the time **when he conveys his bucket to** the second person.

The *gemara* offers two versions of how this transfer occurs. The first version holds that once the second owner came and began using the well, that act of usage was sufficient to make him implicitly responsible. The second, more demanding version holds that it is not enough for the second owner merely to use the well; rather, the first owner must actively hand over the cover of the well to the second owner — only then does responsibility formally pass. The *gemara* now turns to identify what underlying dispute drives these two positions.

### Tannaim Debate

The *gemara* introduces a parallel *tannaitic* dispute with the phrase *ke-tanai* — this mirrors a debate among the *tannaim*. The case: someone is drawing water from a well, and his fellow says to him, "Leave off and I will draw." Once the first person steps aside and the second begins using the well, the first is exempt — he has, by stepping aside, renounced his responsibility. That is the position of the first *tanna*: usage alone by the second party is sufficient to transfer liability.

### R. Eliezer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the dispute: **With regard to what** principle **do** they **disagree? Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov maintains** that **there is** a legal concept of **designation.**

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov disagrees. Mere usage is not enough; the first owner remains responsible until he explicitly hands over the cover to the second. Only that formal act of transfer — physically giving over the cover — constitutes a genuine handover of responsibility.

## Breira Concept

### Breira Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִדִּידֵיהּ קָא מְמַלֵּא, וְהַאי מִדִּידֵיהּ קָא מְמַלֵּא. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין בְּרֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** In this case, the water is considered divided between the partners even before they draw it. When they do draw it, it is treated as if **this one were filling from his** part, the location of which has now been determined retroactively, **and this one were filling from his** part. Therefore, as long as the first partner has not conveyed his bucket to the second, he, as an owner of a defined part of the water, has not absolved himself of responsibility for the cistern. **And** by contrast, **the Rabbis maintain** that **there is no designation** and they both have the right to draw water from the cistern. Therefore, as soon as the second partner is left alone to draw water, he is responsible for the cistern and all the water it contains, even without conveying the bucket.

The *gemara* asks: *b'mai kamipalgi* — what is this dispute actually about? The answer turns on the concept of *breira*, which concerns whether shared ownership in jointly held property can be retrospectively individuated. When two people jointly own a well and each uses it in turn, are they each drawing on a common, undifferentiated resource — or can we say that when one uses it, he is accessing only his own portion, and when the other uses it, he is accessing only his?

### Yesh Breira

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov holds *yesh breira* — there is distinguishability. When I use the well, I am using my half; when my partner uses it, he is using his half. The practical consequence follows directly: when the second owner drew water, he was only ever accessing his own portion of the well. He never took over my portion, and therefore he never assumed responsibility for my half. The only way my half can be transferred to him — and my responsibility along with it — is if I explicitly hand over the cover. That physical act is the unambiguous statement: "You are now responsible for this well." The *gemara* notes, in passing, that *breira* pertains to the act of using, not to the moment an animal falls in; an animal falling in does not choose which half to fall into, and liability for that event would be shared accordingly.

### Ein Breira

The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold *ein breira* — there is no distinguishability. When either partner uses the well, he is using the undivided whole, the portions of both owners together. Consequently, when the second owner begins using the well, he is accessing the entire resource, including the first owner's share, and thereby assumes full responsibility for it.

### Field Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דִּתְנַן: הַשּׁוּתָּפִין שֶׁנָּדְרוּ הֲנָאָה זֶה מִזֶּה – אֲסוּרִין לִיכָּנֵס לֶחָצֵר. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: זֶה נִכְנָס לְתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְזֶה נִכְנָס לְתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: And** in this regard, they **follow** their lines of **reasoning** in an analogous case, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Nedarim* 45b): With regard to **the** case of **partners that vowed** not to derive any **benefit from each other,** it is **prohibited for them to enter** their jointly owned **courtyard,** as this would violate their vows. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** It is permitted for them to enter the courtyard, since **this one enters into his** portion of the courtyard **and that one enters into his** portion.

Ravina notes that this same dispute appears in another *tannaitic* context. If two partners in a jointly held field each took a vow that the other may not derive benefit from him — so that my half of the field is forbidden to you and your half to me — the *rabbanan* rule that neither may enter the field at all, since each necessarily benefits from the other's portion by entering. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, however, says that when I enter, I enter my own portion, and when he enters, he enters his own portion — *yesh breira*, each use is attributed to one's own share, and therefore neither violates the other's *neder*.

### Ravina Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב סָבַר: יֵשׁ בְּרֵירָה – הַאי לִדְנַפְשֵׁיהּ עָיֵיל, וְהַאי לִדְנַפְשֵׁיהּ עָיֵיל. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין בְּרֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree? Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov maintains** that **there is** retroactive **designation,** and retroactively the section of the courtyard that each one enters becomes the portion that belongs to him. Therefore, **this one enters his** part, **and that one enters his** part. By contrast, **the Rabbis maintain** that **there is no** retroactive **designation,** and each portion of the courtyard belongs jointly to both.

Ravina draws the two disputes together: the debate about watching the well and the debate about the jointly held field are expressions of the same underlying question — when you use something owned jointly, to what degree are you accessing something that belongs to the other person, or are you only ever accessing the portion to which you have rights?

## Handing Over Keys

### Selling Well

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַמּוֹכֵר בּוֹר לַחֲבֵירוֹ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ דׇּלְיוֹ – קָנָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בְּכַסְפָּא – לִיקְנֵי בְּכַסְפָּא! אִי בַּחֲזָקָה – לִיקְנֵי בַּחֲזָקָה!
>
> **Translation:** § Having mentioned this *halakha* concerning conveying a bucket to transfer responsibility for the pit, the Gemara cites that which **Rabbi Elazar says:** With regard to **one who sells a cistern to another, once** the seller **conveys his bucket to** the buyer for drawing water from the cistern, the buyer **acquires** the cistern. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances?** How does conveying the bucket serve to effect acquisition of the cistern? **If** he is acquiring the cistern **by** means of paying **money, let him acquire** it **with money** that he paid. **If** he is acquiring the cistern **by** means of **taking possession,** then **let** the buyer **acquire** it **by taking possession,** which is accomplished by using it.

Since the *gemara* has been discussing the handing over of a cover as an act of transferring responsibility, it now pivots to examine this gesture as a potential act of *kinyan* — formal acquisition. Rabbi Elazar rules: if one sells a well to another and hands over the cover (*dalyo*), the buyer takes possession. The simple reading of *dalyo* is the bucket, but the *gemara* will treat it as the cover. At first glance, this appears to introduce an entirely new mode of *kinyan*: handing over that which symbolically controls access to the property — a kind of "handing over the keys to the kingdom."

### Authorization Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בַּחֲזָקָה, וּבָעֵי לְמֵימַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ חֲזֵק וּקְנֵי״; וְכֵיוָן שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ דׇּלְיוֹ, כְּמַאן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״לֵךְ חֲזֵק וּקְנֵי״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the case is **where** the cistern was acquired through **taking possession, and** in that case, the seller usually **must say to him: Go, take possession, and** thereby **acquire** it for yourself. **And** in this case, **when he conveys his bucket to him,** he is considered **like one who says to him: Go, take possession, and** thereby **acquire** it. Therefore, once he begins drawing water from the cistern, he has acquired it through taking possession.

The *gemara* resists this reading. Land is acquired only through *kesef*, *shtar*, or *chazakah*, and it asks: if money was paid, that should be the act of acquisition; if a *chazakah* was performed, let that suffice. What work is the handing over of the cover doing? The *gemara* resolves: the *kinyan* is always effected through *chazakah*, but for a *chazakah* to be valid the seller must authorize the buyer to perform it — he must say *lech chazek u-knei*, "go, take possession and acquire." The handing over of the cover is not itself the *kinyan*; it is the equivalent of that authorization. Once you hand over the cover, it is as though you have told the buyer: go ahead and perform the act of *chazakah*. The *gemara* thus reinterprets Rabbi Elazar's ruling: not a new form of *kinyan*, but an authorization that enables the established form.

### House Keys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַמּוֹכֵר בַּיִת לַחֲבֵירוֹ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ מַפְתֵּחַ – קָנָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בְּכַסְפָּא – לִיקְנֵי בְּכַסְפָּא! אִי בַּחֲזָקָה – לִיקְנֵי בַּחֲזָקָה! לְעוֹלָם בַּחֲזָקָה, וּבָעֵי לְמֵימַר לֵיהּ ״לֵךְ חֲזֵק וּקְנֵי״; וְכֵיוָן שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ מַפְתֵּחַ, כְּמַאן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״לֵךְ חֲזֵק וּקְנֵי״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** If one **sells a house to another,** **once he has conveyed the key to him, he has acquired** it. Here too, the Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** involving this sale? **If** it was a transaction **by** payment of **money,** then **let** he **acquire** it **by** paying **money. If** it was **by taking possession** of it, **let** he **acquire** it **by taking possession.** What is the significance of transferring the key? The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the transaction occurred by **taking possession, and** in that case, the seller usually **must say to him: Go, take possession, and** thereby **acquire** it for yourself. **And** in this case, **once he conveys the key to him,** he is considered **like one who says to him: Go, take possession, and** thereby **acquire** it.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi offers a parallel ruling at the top of 52a: one who sells a house to another — once the seller hands over the key, the buyer acquires it. The *gemara* raises the same objection: if there was already payment or a *chazakah*, why does the key matter? And it gives the same answer: *le'olam b'chazakah* — the *kinyan* is through *chazakah*, and handing over the key is the equivalent of saying *lech chazek u-knei*. It is an authorization, not an independent act of acquisition.

One might note, as *Tosafot* does, that the *Rashbam* in Bava Batra appears to take the simpler reading of these statements at face value — that handing over a key actually constitutes a form of *kinyan* — which creates tension with the *gemara*'s reinterpretation here. *Tosafot* also points to a *sugya* at the beginning of Pesachim, where the *gemara* discusses whether a landlord has handed over keys to a tenant in the context of who bears responsibility for *bedikat chametz*. *Tosafot* explains, however, that the issue there is not when the *kinyan* of rental takes effect, but the practical question of whether the renter yet has physical access to the house. A person cannot be obligated to check a house for *chametz* if he has not yet been given the means of entry. So there the key is significant, but for reasons of practical access rather than as an act of *kinyan* — a distinction that preserves the *gemara*'s general framework.

### Flock & Goat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: הַמּוֹכֵר עֵדֶר לַחֲבֵירוֹ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁמָּסַר לוֹ מַשְׁכּוּכִית – קָנָה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי בִּמְשִׁיכָה – לִיקְנֵי בִּמְשִׁיכָה! אִי בִּמְסִירָה – לִיקְנֵי בִּמְסִירָה! לְעוֹלָם בִּמְשִׁיכָה, וּבָעֵי לְמֵימַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ מְשׁוֹךְ וּקְנֵי״, וְכֵיוָן דְּמָסַר לוֹ מַשְׁכּוּכִית – כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: ״לֵךְ מְשׁוֹךְ וּקְנֵי״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Reish Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Yannai:** With regard to one **who sells a flock** of sheep **to another, once he conveys the *mashkukhit* to** the buyer, **he has acquired** the flock. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If** the transaction occurred **by pulling** the flock into his possession, then **let** the buyer **acquire** it **by pulling. If** it was **by conveying** it, **let** the buyer **acquire** it **by** the seller **conveying** it. The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it was a transaction **by pulling, and** in that case, the seller usually **must say to him: Go, pull** it **and acquire** it for yourself. **And** in this case, **once he conveys the *mashkukhit* to him,** he is **like one who says to him: Go, pull** it **and acquire** it.

Reish Lakish, citing Rabbi Yannai, extends the same pattern: one who sells a flock to another — once the seller hands over the *mashchuchis*, the buyer acquires. The word *mashchuchis* derives from the root *mashoch*, to lead or draw, suggesting something that causes the flock to follow. The *gemara* raises its now-familiar objection: if you actually moved the animals, that is *meshichah* and should be the act of *kinyan*; if you handed over something attached to the animal, that approaches *mesirah*, which works precisely because it is connected directly to the animal itself. The *mashchuchis*, however, is not attached to any individual animal in that direct sense. So again the *gemara* redefines: the *kinyan* is through *meshichah*, and handing over the *mashchuchis* is tantamount to saying *lech meshoch u-knei* — the authorization to perform the act.

### Mashchuchis ID

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁכּוּכִית? הָכָא תַּרְגִּמוּ: קַרְקַשְׁתָּא. רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: עִיזָּא דְּאָזְלָא בְּרֵישׁ עֶדְרָא – כְּדִדְרַשׁ הַהוּא גָּלִילָאָה עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: כַּד רָגֵיז רָעֲיָא עַל עָנָא, עָבֵיד לְנַגָּדָא סַמְוָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is this ***mashkukhit*? Here,** in Babylonia, **they translate** it as **a bell [*karkashta*]** that the shepherd rings and whose sound the flock follows. **Rabbi Ya'akov says:** It is referring to **the goat that goes at the front** of **the flock** that they follow. The Gemara notes: This explanation of Rabbi Ya'akov is similar to **that which a certain Galilean taught in the presence of Rav Ḥisda concerning** this goat: **When a shepherd is angry with his flock, he renders** the goat **leading [*lenaggada*]** them, i.e., the *mashkukhit*, **blind.** Similarly, when God is angry with the Jewish people, he appoints unsuitable leaders for them.

What exactly is the *mashchuchis*? The *gemara* reports two identifications. The local interpretation is that it is a bell — a ringing bell that the flock is trained to follow. Rabbi Yaakov says it is the lead goat, the animal that walks at the head of the flock and which all the others follow, functioning somewhat like a sheepdog in its role. Indeed, goats naturally fulfill this function as leaders of sheep, and the arrangement of a different species — a goat — leading sheep reflects a well-known feature of how flocks were managed.

### Locks & Punishment

A certain Galilean expounded before Rav Chisda: when a shepherd grows angry at his flock, he blinds the lead animal. The *derashah* carries a homiletic point — when the Holy One wishes to punish the Jewish people, He strikes the leaders. *Tosafot* addresses a further point arising from this entire section: while the *Rashbam* in one place seems to entertain the possibility that handing over keys constitutes a genuine *kinyan*, the *gemara* here consistently drives toward the authorization model. And separately, the *sugya* at the beginning of Pesachim, though it also invokes the handing over of keys, operates on a different plane — one of practical halachic access rather than formal acquisition.

## Second Owner Liable

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כִּסָּהוּ הָרִאשׁוֹן, וּבָא הַשֵּׁנִי וּמְצָאוֹ מְגוּלֶּה, וְלֹא כִּסָּהוּ – הַשֵּׁנִי חַיָּיב. כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. לֹא כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna lists several *halakhot* that pertain to damage classified as Pit: In the case of a pit that **the first** person who passed by **covered** after using **it, and** then **the second came** to use it **and found it uncovered** after the cover fell off or was damaged, **and** he **did not cover it, the second** one is **liable** for damage caused by the pit. If the owner **covered** the pit **appropriately and an ox or a donkey fell into it and died,** he is **exempt.** If he **did not cover** the pit **appropriately and an ox or a donkey fell into it and died,** he is **liable.**

The *gemara* now proceeds to the next *mishnah*. The *mishnah* states: if the first owner covered the well and the second owner subsequently found it uncovered and did not cover it, the second owner is liable. This *mishnah* differs from the preceding one. The previous *mishnah*, as Rashi explains, assumed that the second owner either used the well or was handed the cover — thereby assuming full responsibility. The present *mishnah* begins from the premise that if the first owner had not covered it, both would share responsibility, since both passed over it without covering. Here, the first owner has done his part by covering it; the second owner then finds it exposed and fails to act. The second owner therefore becomes fully liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לְפָנָיו מִקּוֹל הַכְּרִיָּיה – חַיָּיב, לְאַחֲרָיו מִקּוֹל הַכְּרִיָּיה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If a man was digging or widening a pit, and an ox passing by **fell forward** into it in fright **due to the sound** of **the digging,** he is **liable.** If it fell **backward** into the pit **due to the sound** of **the digging,** he is **exempt.**

The *mishnah* continues: if the first owner covered it properly (*k'ra'uy*) and an ox or donkey nonetheless fell in and died, he is exempt. If he did not cover it properly and an animal fell in and died, he is liable. It is not sufficient merely to place something over the well — the covering must be adequate to withstand animals walking over it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר וְכֵלָיו – וְנִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ, חֲמוֹר וְכֵלָיו – וְנִתְקָרְעוּ; חַיָּיב עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, וּפָטוּר עַל הַכֵּלִים. נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – חַיָּיב. בֵּן אוֹ בַּת, עֶבֶד אוֹ אָמָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **an ox and its accoutrements,** i.e., the vessels it was carrying, **fell into** the pit **and** the vessels **were broken,** or if **a donkey and its accoutrements** fell in **and** the accoutrements **were torn,** the owner of the pit is **liable for** damage to **the animal** caused by the pit, **but** he is **exempt** from liability **for** damage caused to **the vessels,** by Torah edict. If **an ox** that was impaired by being **deaf,** or an ox that was **an imbecile, or** an ox that was very **young fell into** the pit, he is **liable.** If **a boy or a girl,** a Canaanite **slave or** a Canaanite **maidservant** fell in, he is **exempt,** since there is a Torah edict that the digger of a pit is liable only for damage caused to an animal.

The *mishnah* further records: if an animal fell forward into the pit because it was startled by the sound of digging within the well, the owner of the well is liable even if the person digging is not the owner. This is striking: one might have argued that the digger should be exempt because the harm is only *grama* — indirect causation — and that the owner of the well should be exempt because it was not the well itself but the sound that caused the animal to fall. Yet the law holds the well owner liable.

Conversely, if the animal fell *backwards* into the well — that is, it was startled and fell in the opposite direction, back into the pit — the well owner is exempt. The *mishnah* then addresses the case of an ox together with its implements: if the ox fell in and its yoke broke, or a donkey fell in and its saddlebags tore, the owner is liable for the animal and exempt for the implements — consistent with the established rule of *shor v'lo adam, chamor v'lo kelim*.

The *mishnah* concludes with two further distinctions. If an ox that is deaf, mute, or a minor (*cheresh shoteh v'katan*) fell in, the owner is liable. But if a son or daughter, a slave or maidservant fell in, he is exempt. The *chiddush* in both parts is notable. One might have thought that since the well owner is generally exempt for human injuries on the grounds that people bear responsibility for themselves, a child — who cannot fully look out for himself — might generate liability. Similarly, one might have considered treating a slave as property and therefore assigning liability as for damaged property. The *mishnah* teaches that neither exception applies: both children and slaves remain within the general exemption for human beings.

### When Does Liability Return…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְרִאשׁוֹן עַד אֵימַת מִיפְּטַר? אָמַר רַב: בִּכְדֵי שֶׁיֵּדַע. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בִּכְדֵי שֶׁיּוֹדִיעוּהוּ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: בִּכְדִי שֶׁיּוֹדִיעוּהוּ, וְיִשְׂכּוֹר פּוֹעֲלִים, וְיִכְרוֹת אֲרָזִים וִיכַסֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the first case in the mishna, the Gemara asks: **And until when** is **the first** person **exempt** if the pit is later uncovered? **Rav says:** He is exempt from liability for the time necessary **for** him **to** become **aware** that it became uncovered. Once this time has passed, he bears responsibility. **And Shmuel says:** He is exempt for the time necessary **for** others **to** realize that the pit is uncovered and **inform him. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He is exempt for the time necessary **for** others **to inform him** that the cover has fallen down **and** for him to **hire workers and cut cedar trees** to make a suitable cover **and cover it.** After this period of time has passed, the first bears responsibility.

The *gemara* now raises a question about the first part of the *mishnah*: at what point does the first owner, having properly covered the well and thereby exempted himself, have his liability reignite? He is not exempt forever. If word reaches him that the cover has been removed or has failed, at some stage he becomes obligated to respond. The question is: when?

Three *amoraim* offer distinct positions. Rav says: *bichedei sheyeda* — there must have been enough time for him to have found out about it. As *Tosafot* explains, this does not require proof that the person actually heard; once sufficient time has elapsed that one would normally expect the information to have reached him through ordinary channels, he is again liable. Shmuel says: *bichedei sheyodi'uhu* — it is not enough that word could have trickled through informally; there must have been enough time, or in fact an actual occurrence, of someone going and directly informing him. Hearing a rumor does not create the same sense of imperative to act as being directly told. Rabbi Yochanan says: *bichedei sheyodi'uhu v'yiscor v'yichros arazim v'yechasenu* — there must have been enough time not only for him to be informed, but to hire workers, obtain timber, and construct a proper new cover. The *chiddush* is not that he need do nothing in the interim: even Rabbi Yochanan would agree that before the full cover can be prepared, the owner must arrange temporary supervision — placing watchers by the pit, for example — until a permanent solution can be installed. Rabbi Yochanan's ruling addresses only the question of when full liability attaches, not whether any interim obligation exists.

## Proper Covering

### Proper Cover

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. כֵּיוָן דְּכִסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, הֵיכִי נְפַל? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בַּר חָנָה: שֶׁהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If he **covered** the pit **appropriately and an ox or a donkey fell inside and died,** he is **exempt.** The Gemara asks: **Since he covered** the pit **appropriately, how** did it **fall** in? **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar bar Ḥana says:** This is a case **where** the cover **rotted from the inside,** and he could not have known that the cover was damaged. Therefore, he is not responsible for damage caused as a result.

The *gemara* proceeds to examine the clause: if the well was covered properly and an ox or donkey nonetheless fell in, the owner is exempt. This is clear in principle — if you fulfilled your obligation, you bear no liability. But the *gemara* presses: if you covered it properly, how did anything manage to fall in at all?

Rav Yitzchak bar bar Chana answers: *shehitlia mitocho* — it rotted from within, literally became infested with worms from the inside. If the owner placed on a cover that appeared sound but had been eaten away internally — by termites, say — with no external indication of its weakness, then his covering was indeed *k'ra'uy* at the time it was placed, and what subsequently caused the failure was something he had no reason to anticipate. The *gemara* will later note the possibility that a responsible owner ought to perform periodic inspections, tapping the wood to detect internal hollowness. But at this stage, *hitlia mitocho* is treated as a genuine *ones* — an unforeseeable accident — even for an owner who otherwise fulfilled his duty.

### Camel Weakening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כִּסָּהוּ כִּסּוּי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד לִפְנֵי שְׁווֹרִים, וְאֵין יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי גְמַלִּים; וַאֲתוֹ גְּמַלִּים וְאָרְעוּהּ, וְאָתוּ שְׁווֹרִים וְנָפְלִי בֵּיהּ – מַאי? אָמְרִי: הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים – פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא! וְאִי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים – אָנוּס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If he **covered it** with **a cover that** is **able to withstand** the weight of **oxen but cannot withstand** the weight of **camels,** which are heavier, **and camels came and weakened it, and** subsequently **oxen came** and broke the weakened cover **and fell into** the pit, **what** is the *halakha*? The Sages **said** in response: **What are the circumstances? If camels are** commonly **found** there, **he is negligent,** since he should have constructed the cover suitably for camels as well. And **if camels are not** commonly **found** there, then **he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control** if by chance they did come and weaken the cover.

A new question is now posed: suppose the owner placed a cover that could withstand oxen but was not strong enough to withstand camels. Camels came and weakened it. Then oxen came and fell in. Is the owner liable? The *gemara* asks: what are the circumstances?

If camels are common in that area (*shchichei gemalim*), then the owner is clearly a *poshea* — negligent. He should have known that a cover inadequate for camels in a place where camels regularly pass is not a responsible cover; he could have anticipated that the cover would be weakened and that oxen would then fall in. On the other hand, if camels are entirely uncommon (*she'einam metzuyim*), he is an *ones* — he had no reason to guard against them, and his covering was appropriate given the realistic conditions. The more challenging case, and the one the *gemara* is actually pursuing, is where camels come only occasionally (*l'prakim*) — not regularly, but not never.

### Unresolved Vigilance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲתוֹ לִפְרָקִים; מִי אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּאָתַיִין לִפְרָקִים – פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא, דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ אַסּוֹקֵי אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּהַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת לֵיכָּא – אָנוּס הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** camels **come occasionally,** in which case the question effectively becomes: **Do we say** that **since they come occasionally, he is** considered **negligent, since he should have anticipated** their coming? **Or perhaps since now, in any event, there are no** camels, **he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control.**

The *gemara* frames the question precisely: do we say that since camels come occasionally, the owner should have anticipated it — *de'ibei lei asukei ada'atei* — and therefore he is a *poshea*? Or do we say that since camels are not currently present and their appearance is not a reliable expectation, the owner is an *ones* and bears no liability? The question is genuine because it is not simply an objective actuarial matter — it is also a question of what we can realistically demand that a person think about. A common person tending a well does not sit and calculate that a camel might pass once in some period. The *gemara* is asking whether that failure of anticipation itself constitutes negligence.

This question is structurally parallel to the familiar framework of *ruach metzuyah* and *ruach she'einah metzuyah* — the common wind versus the uncommon wind — and the standard three-tiered framework of *poshea*, intermediate care, and *ones*. What the *gemara* is doing here is applying that same continuum to camels as a type of external force: a common camel is a *ruach metzuyah*; a never-present camel is a *ruach she'einah metzuyah*; an occasional camel is the difficult middle case. The *teyku* — the *gemara*'s unresolved suspension — reflects that this middle category genuinely resists easy classification.

## First Mishnah Proof

### Fit Oxen/Unfit Camels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְכָרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים – הֵיכִי נְפוּל? אֶלָּא לָאו כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים,
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a possible proof from the mishna: If he **covered** the pit **appropriately, and an ox or a donkey fell into it and died,** he is **exempt.** Now **what are the circumstances? If we say** that he covered it **appropriately for oxen and appropriately for camels,** then **how** did they **fall** in? **Rather, is it not** the case that he covered it with a covering that was **appropriate for oxen**

The *gemara* now attempts to resolve the open question from a reading of the *mishnah* itself. *Ta shema* — come and hear: if the well was covered properly (*k'ra'uy*) and an ox or donkey fell in, the owner is exempt. The *gemara* asks: what is the case? If the cover was fully adequate for both oxen and camels, how did anything fall in at all? That seems to leave only one possibility that generates a genuine *chiddush*: the cover was fit for oxen but not for camels.

### Proof from First Part

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים? וְאִי דִּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים, אַמַּאי פָּטוּר? פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא! וְאִי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים – פְּשִׁיטָא, אָנוּס הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאָתַיִין לִפְרָקִים, וַאֲתוֹ גְּמַלִּים וְאָרְעוּהּ, וַאֲתוֹ שְׁווֹרִים וּנְפַלוּ בֵּיהּ – וְקָתָנֵי פָּטוּר? אַלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּהַשְׁתָּא לֵיכָּא – אָנוּס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **but not appropriate for camels?** In this case, the following must be clarified: **If** it is a location **where camels are** commonly **found, why** is he **exempt? He is negligent. And if camels are not** commonly **found** there, it **is obvious** that he is exempt, since **he is** clearly **a victim of circumstances beyond his control. Rather, is it not** the case that this is a location **where** camels **come occasionally, and camels came and weakened** the covering, after which **oxen came and fell into it? And** with regard to this case, **it teaches** that he is **exempt. Apparently, since now,** in any event, the camels **are not** there, he is considered **a victim of circumstances beyond his control.**

From this reading, the *gemara* attempts to identify the case of the *mishnah* as one where camels occasionally come (*d'atu l'prakim*), they came and weakened the cover, and oxen subsequently fell in. If so, the *mishnah*'s ruling that the owner is exempt (*patur*) would demonstrate that occasional camel visits do not generate an obligation to reinforce the cover — the owner is an *ones*, not a *poshea*. This would resolve the open question in favor of the lenient position.

### Rebuttal: Rotting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא, לְעוֹלָם כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְכָרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים; וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ – הֵיכִי נְפוּל? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בַּר חָנָה: שֶׁהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said** in reply: **No; actually,** the scenario is where he covered it **appropriately for oxen and appropriately for camels, and as for** what was **difficult for you** to explain: **How did they fall? Rabbi Yitzḥak bar bar Ḥana says:** It is **where** the cover **rotted from the inside,** and so nothing can be derived from here with regard to the question posed above with regard to the camels.

The *gemara* rejects this reading. In fact, the cover may have been fully adequate for both oxen and camels, and the *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is supplied by the statement of Rav Yitzchak bar bar Chana: *shehitlia mitocho* — it rotted from within. The case is one of internal decay that could not have been detected or anticipated. On this reading, the question of occasional camels remains unresolved, and the *mishnah* proves nothing about it.

## Second Mishnah Part

### Improper Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – חַיָּיב. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא לֹא כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים, פְּשִׁיטָא – צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר דְּחַיָּיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a different proof from the mishna: If he **did not cover** the pit **appropriately, and an ox or a donkey fell in and died,** he is **liable.** Now, **what are the circumstances? If we say** that he did **not** cover it **appropriately for oxen and** did **not** cover it **appropriately for camels,** and they fell in, it **is obvious.** Does it **need to be said that** he is **liable** in that case?

The *gemara* now turns to the second clause of the *mishnah*: if the cover was not *k'ra'uy* and an animal died, the owner is liable. Once again the *gemara* probes: what is the case? If the cover was obviously inadequate by any measure — neither fit for oxen nor for camels — it is entirely obvious that the owner is liable. There is nothing to teach.

### Case Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים? וְאִי דִּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים – פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא! וְאִי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים – אָנוּס הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where he covered it **appropriately for oxen** but **not appropriately for camels?** And if this is the case, the following point must be clarified: **What are the circumstances? If camels are** commonly **found** there, **he is** clearly **negligent, but if camels are not** commonly **found** there, **he is a victim of circumstances beyond his control.**

For the second part of the *mishnah* to constitute a genuine *chiddush*, the *gemara* reads it as a case where the cover was fit for oxen but not fit for camels, and camels were common in that area. In that scenario, the owner was clearly negligent for not providing a cover adequate to the realistic conditions, camels came and weakened it, and oxen fell in. The *gemara* then suggests that if this is the case, and the *mishnah* declares the owner liable, perhaps the *chiddush* is different: perhaps the cover was fit for oxen but not for camels, camels were common, but in the end it was *hitlia mitocho* — internal rot — that caused the failure. The *mishnah* would then be teaching: since you were negligent with respect to camels, you are liable even for the rot — *migu d'havei poshea l'inyan gemalim, havei poshea l'inyan hatla'ah*. Because you were negligent in one direction, your negligence extends to and covers the unrelated cause of the failure.

### Migo Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאָתַיִין לִפְרָקִים, וַאֲתוֹ גְּמַלִּים וְאָרְעוּהּ, וַאֲתוֹ שְׁווֹרִים וּנְפַלוּ בֵּיהּ – וְקָתָנֵי חַיָּיב? אַלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאָתַיִין לִפְרָקִים – פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא, דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ אַסּוֹקֵי אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where** camels **come occasionally, and camels came and weakened it, and** subsequently **oxen came and fell into it? And** in this case, it **teaches** that he is **liable. Apparently, since they** do **come occasionally, he is** considered **negligent, since he should have anticipated** that they would come.

The *gemara*, however, declines this reading as well. The case of the second clause of the *mishnah* is simply one where the cover was fit for oxen but not for camels, camels were common, the camels came and weakened it, and the oxen fell in — straightforward and fully connected negligence throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים, וּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים; וַאֲתוֹ גְּמַלִּים וְאָרְעוּהּ, וַאֲתוֹ שְׁווֹרִים וּנְפַלוּ בֵּיהּ. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ – פְּשִׁיטָא, פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא! אַיְּידֵי דִּנְסֵיב רֵישָׁא ״כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי״, נְסֵיב סֵיפָא נָמֵי ״לֹא כִּסָּהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Actually,** it is referring to where he covered the pit **appropriately for oxen** but **not appropriately for camels; and camels are** commonly **found** there, **and camels came and weakened** the cover. **And,** subsequently, **oxen came and fell in. And as for** what was **difficult for you** to explain: It **is obvious** that he is liable, since **he is** clearly **negligent,** so what novelty is being introduced? The answer is that **since he** needs to **cite the first clause** of the mishna, i.e., where he **covered** the pit **appropriately, he** therefore **cites the latter clause as well,** i.e., where he **did not cover it.**

The response to the objection that this seems obvious is the same as before: since the *mishnah* stated the case of a proper cover in the first clause, it balanced it with an improper cover in the second clause, even though the liability in the second clause follows as a matter of course.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: הָא נָמֵי וַדַּאי לָא אִיבַּעְיָא לַן; דְּכֵיוָן דְּאָתַיִין לִפְרָקִים – פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא, דְּאִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ אַסּוֹקֵי אַדַּעְתֵּיהּ;
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** that the discussion is as follows: With regard to **this also we certainly did not raise the dilemma,** because **since they occasionally come, he is** considered **negligent, as he should have anticipated** this possibility.

The *gemara* then attempts the same reading of the *mishnah* in reverse — trying to derive from the first clause that *migo* does apply, and from the second clause that it does not — and reaches the same impasse. Each time, the *gemara* is able to reread the *mishnah* in a way that makes the case obvious on its own terms, without invoking *migo* in either direction. The *mishnah* therefore proves nothing about whether negligence in one domain transfers to an unrelated cause of harm. The question of *migo* remains open through this entire extended analysis.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אִיבְּעִי לַן – הָכִי הוּא דְּאִיבַּעְיָא לַן: כִּסָּהוּ כִּסּוּי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי שְׁווֹרִים וְאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵי גְמַלִּים, וּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים; וְהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ – מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: מִגּוֹ דְּהָוֵי פּוֹשֵׁעַ אֵצֶל גְּמַלִּים, הָוֵי פּוֹשֵׁעַ נָמֵי לְעִנְיַן הַתְלָעָה; אוֹ דִלְמָא לָא אָמְרִינַן מִגּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **When we raised the dilemma, this is the dilemma that we raised:** He **covered it** with **a cover that is able** to **withstand the presence of oxen but is unable to withstand the presence of camels, and camels are** commonly **found** in that location. What actually transpired was that the cover **rotted from** the **inside.** In this case, **what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say: Since he was negligent concerning camels, he is** considered **negligent also concerning** the **rotting? Or perhaps we do not say** that **since** he was negligent concerning camels he is considered negligent concerning rotting, and since in practice he is not to blame for the incident, he is not held liable?

The underlying conceptual tension can be framed as follows. One view holds that liability is always scenario-specific: you are liable only for what actually resulted from your negligence, and if the actual cause of injury was something unrelated to where your negligence lay, you are exempt for that specific outcome. The opposing view holds that once you have established a zone of negligence — you left a dangerous pit inadequately covered — you bear liability for whatever happens within that zone, even if the precise mechanism of harm differs from the one you were negligent about. The *gemara*'s sustained effort to read the *mishnah* either way, always finding an alternative explanation, indicates that neither position can be decisively established from the *mishnah* text alone.

## Blind Ox Proof

### Vulnerable Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. וְאִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בַּר חָנָה: שֶׁהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְכָרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים, וְהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ – פְּשִׁיטָא דְּפָטוּר, מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: If **he covered** the pit **appropriately and an ox or a donkey fell into it and died,** he is **exempt. And** it **was stated with regard to** this that **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar bar Ḥana says:** This *halakha* applies **where** the cover **rotted from the inside.** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that it is referring to where he covered it in a manner **appropriate for oxen and appropriate for camels and** it **rotted from the inside,** isn't it **obvious that** he is **exempt?** Being unaware of this, **what should he have done?**

The *gemara* now reaches what it presents as a stronger proof. It returns to the clause of the *mishnah* dealing with an ox that is deaf, mute, or a minor (*cheresh shoteh v'katan*), or blind (*suma*), or walking at night — in all such cases the owner of the pit is liable.

### Smart Ox Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים, וּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים, וְהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ – וְקָתָנֵי פָּטוּר? אַלְמָא לָא אָמְרִינַן מִגּוֹ דְּהָוֵי פּוֹשֵׁעַ לְעִנְיַן גְּמַלִּים הָוֵי פּוֹשֵׁעַ לְעִנְיַן הַתְלָעָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** referring to a case where he covered it **appropriately for oxen** but **not appropriately for camels, and camels are** commonly **found** in that location? **And** the strength of the cover vis-à-vis oxen and camels is not relevant to the *halakha* here because the cover **rotted from the inside. And** the mishna **teaches** that he is **exempt. Apparently, we do not say** that **since he is** considered **negligent concerning camels, he is** also considered **negligent with regard to the rotting.**

This leads to a startling implication, which the *gemara* now makes explicit. If a normal, sighted ox falls into an uncovered pit in the daytime, the owner of the pit is actually exempt. The reasoning: an ox in normal conditions looks where it is going. A sighted ox walking in daylight should have seen the pit and avoided it. The fact that it did not is attributed to the ox itself rather than to the hazard. Only when the ox cannot be expected to see or avoid the hazard — because it is blind, or because it is nighttime, or because the animal lacks ordinary awareness — does the pit owner bear liability.

### Migo Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; לְעוֹלָם כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים וּכְרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים, וְהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ – כִּי הִתְלִיעַ מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֶעְבַּד? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמֵיזַל וּמִנְקַשׁ עֲלֵיהּ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No; actually,** the case is where the cover was **appropriate for camels and appropriate for oxen, and** it **rotted from the inside. And as for** what was **difficult for you** to explain: **When** it **rotted** from the inside, **what should he have done,** and shouldn't he be exempt? There is, nevertheless, a novelty in this ruling: It is necessary **lest you say** that **he should have gone and knocked on** the covering to ensure it was not hollow on the inside. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that he is not required to check to this extent.

This creates a *migo* question directly parallel to the one that has been under discussion. The pit owner has an open, uncovered well. He is clearly negligent and liable with respect to a blind ox, a *cheresh shoteh v'katan* ox, and a nighttime scenario. Should we not then say: since he is a *poshea* in all those respects, he should be liable *across the board* — including for the normal daytime sighted ox? His negligence in leaving the pit uncovered is a general negligence, and perhaps it should generate a general, undifferentiated liability?

### Migo Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי, וְנָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר, וָמֵת – חַיָּיב. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא לֹא כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים – צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר דְּחַיָּיב?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the continuation of the mishna: If he **did not cover** the pit **appropriately and an ox or a donkey fell into it and died,** he is **liable.** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that it is referring to where he did **not** cover it **appropriately for oxen and** did **not** cover it **appropriately for camels,** does it **need to be said that** he is **liable?**

The *gemara* answers: no. *Shema mina lo amrinan migo* — we do not apply *migo* in this way. The owner is exempt for the normal sighted ox in daytime, and the fact that he is negligent in other respects does not change that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים? וְאִי דִּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים – פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא, וְאִי דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים – אָנוּס הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו דִּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים, וְהִתְלִיעַ מִתּוֹכוֹ – וְקָתָנֵי חַיָּיב? אַלְמָא אָמְרִינַן מִגּוֹ דְּהָוֵי פּוֹשֵׁעַ לְעִנְיַן גְּמַלִּים – הָוֵי פּוֹשֵׁעַ לְעִנְיַן הַתְלָעָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** a case where he covered it **appropriately for oxen** but **not appropriately for camels?** In this case, the following point needs clarification: **If camels are** commonly **found** there, **he is negligent** and should be liable. **And if camels are not** commonly **found** there, he is **a victim of circumstances beyond his control. Rather, is it not** a case **where camels are** commonly **found** there, **and it rotted from the inside,** and the mishna **teaches** that he is **liable? Apparently, we** do **say** that **since he is negligent concerning the camels, he is** considered **negligent concerning the rotting,** and he is therefore liable.

*Tosafot*, however, objects to the *gemara*'s use of this case as a proof regarding *migo*. *Tosafot* argues that the exemption for a normal sighted ox in daytime has nothing to do with the owner's negligence or its absence. The reason you are exempt is not "I was not negligent for this type of ox." The reason is that the ox did it to itself. The concept of *bor* as a tortious hazard means that the pit served as the instrument of harm — the animal tripped into it, was drawn in by it. But a normal sighted ox that walks into an open pit in broad daylight is, in the *gemara*'s framework, attributed as acting upon itself. The well was not the active cause of harm; the ox was its own cause. My well was not the *mazik* of that ox.

This is categorically different from the *migo* question that has been debated throughout the *sugya*. In the camel-weakening and rotting scenarios, the pit genuinely was the *mazik* — an animal fell in and was harmed by the pit — and the only question is whether the precise pathway of harm matches the owner's negligence. That is a *migo* question. In the case of the normal sighted ox, there is no *migo* question at all; the ox simply is not treated as a victim of the pit. *Tosafot* therefore regards the *gemara*'s proof as conceptually imprecise, even as it accepts the *gemara*'s ultimate conclusion.

### Tosafot Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא; לְעוֹלָם כָּרָאוּי לִשְׁווֹרִים וְלֹא כָּרָאוּי לִגְמַלִּים, וּשְׁכִיחִי גְּמַלִּים; וַאֲתוֹ גְּמַלִּים וְאָרְעוּהּ, וַאֲתוֹ שְׁווֹרִים וּנְפַלוּ בֵּיהּ. וּדְקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ – פְּשִׁיטָא, פּוֹשֵׁעַ הוּא! אַיְּידֵי דִּנְסֵיב רֵישָׁא ״כִּסָּהוּ כָּרָאוּי״, נְסֵיב סֵיפָא נָמֵי ״לֹא כִּסָּהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **They said** in response: **No; actually** it is referring to where he covered the pit **appropriately for oxen** but **not appropriately for camels; and camels are** commonly **found** there, **and camels came and weakened** the cover. **And,** subsequently, **oxen came and fell in. And as for** what was **difficult for you** to explain: It **is obvious** that he is liable, since **he is** clearly **negligent,** so what novelty is being introduced? The answer is that **since he** needs to **cite the first clause** of the mishna, i.e., where he **covered** the pit **appropriately, he** therefore **cites the latter clause as well,** i.e., where he **did not cover it.**

## Liability Framework

### Negligence-Specific

The *gemara*'s conclusion, toward which the entire extended discussion has been building, is that liability for a *bor* is not automatically across-the-board once any negligence is established. The traditional view emerging from the *sugya* is that you are liable only for those scenarios for which you were in fact negligent, and an unrelated cause of harm — even one occurring against a background of negligence — does not automatically draw in your liability.

### Strict Liability View

An alternative framework, however, presents itself as conceptually coherent and was raised toward the end of the discussion. On this view, the act of digging a pit creates a strict liability that persists until the danger is completely neutralized. The owner's obligation is not simply to exercise reasonable care in each individual scenario; it is to eliminate the hazard entirely. Until he does so, he remains liable for whatever harm the pit causes, regardless of whether the specific mechanism of harm corresponds to a direction in which he was individually negligent. The *mishnah*'s language of *k'ra'uy* — proper covering — then becomes the test for whether the danger has been fully neutralized, not merely a standard of reasonable care with respect to foreseeable animals.

### Causation Requirement

Even on the strict liability model, however, a causation requirement remains. The *gemara* never suggests that the pit owner is liable for harms entirely disconnected from the pit. What the strict liability model requires is not that the owner be negligent for the specific mode of injury, but that the injury itself must still be causally connected to the pit — that the pit was in fact the *mazik*. The debate, ultimately, is about whether the owner's liability is determined scenario by scenario, matching negligence to outcome, or whether it is determined globally once the underlying dangerous condition has been established and not remedied. The *gemara* leaves this framework-level question open, having demonstrated through the *mishnah* analysis that the text does not decisively resolve it in either direction.
