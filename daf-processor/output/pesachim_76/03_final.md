# Pesachim 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ila'ah Gavar / Tata'ah Gavar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָסוּר. צוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר. חַם לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן, וְצוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ חַם, רַב אָמַר: עִילָּאָה גָּבַר. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: תַּתָּאָה גָּבַר.
>
> **Translation:** that the permitted foods become **forbidden,** because they absorb some of the forbidden food. If a **cold** food item falls **into** another **cold** item, **all agree it is permitted;** the food needs only to be rinsed off. The dispute pertains to a **hot** food item that falls **into a cold** one or **a cold** food item that falls **into a hot** one. **Rav said: The upper one prevails.** The *halakha* is determined based upon the state of the upper substance. If the upper food is hot, the case is judged as though a hot food fell into another hot food because the upper food heats the lower food. If the upper food is cold, the case is similar to a situation where a cold food falls into another cold food because the upper food cools down the lower one and prevents absorption. **And Shmuel said: The lower one prevails.** In his opinion, if the upper substance is hot and the lower one is cold, the permitted food remains permitted; if the lower one is hot and upper one is cold, they are forbidden.

Daf 76 opens on the very last line of 75b, resuming from the *mishnah* studied the previous day. That *mishnah* addressed the case of a *pesach* that touches the pottery walls of the oven: if those walls are hot, the *pesach* is being roasted not directly by fire but by something that has absorbed heat, and the affected spot must be cut away. Similarly, if juices drip from the *pesach* and bounce back after being heated by the pottery walls, those juices cannot be eaten and the area they re-entered must be removed.

The *gemara* now introduces two foundational principles of *kashrus* — one on 76a and one on 76b — that are central well beyond the context of *Pesachim*. It is striking that they appear here in the middle of this tractate, but there they are.

*Itmar* — it was stated. The *gemara* begins with a tripartite framework for heat transfer between prohibited and permitted substances. *Cham l'toch cham, divrei hakol assur*: if something hot falls into something hot — say, a hot piece of meat falling into hot milk, or a hot piece of *chazir* falling into a stew — everyone agrees it is forbidden. The heat causes a transfer of taste. The operative threshold is *yad soledes bo*, commonly understood as approximately 110–115 degrees Fahrenheit, hot enough that one could not hold a finger in the liquid for more than a few seconds without pulling away. Some *rishonim* associate this threshold with cooking, but for *kashrus* the operative concern is heat sufficient to cause taste transfer, which is technically a lower standard — nonetheless, the ruling is *yad soledes bo*.

At the opposite pole: *tzonen l'toch tzonen, divrei hakol mutar* — cold into cold, everyone agrees it is permitted. If, for example, a cold apple falls into cold milk or cold *chazir*, rinsing suffices. There is at most a surface film; nothing penetrates. This assumes non-absorbent materials — an absorbent substance can complicate matters — but as a baseline, cold-into-cold poses no problem.

### Rav: Ila'ah Gavar

The difficult case is when one substance is hot and the other is cold: *cham l'toch tzonen v'tzonen l'toch cham* — something hot into something cold, or something cold into something hot. A hot piece of meat falling into cold milk; a cold piece of meat falling into hot milk. What governs?

*Rav amar ila'ah gavar* — Rav says the upper one wins out. According to Rav, the thermal outcome is determined by the substance on top. If the top substance is hot, it heats the bottom, and there is a transfer of taste — forbidden. If the top substance is cold, it cools everything down, and there is no transfer — permitted.

### Shmuel: Tata'ah Gavar

*V'Shmuel amar tata'ah gavar* — Shmuel says the lower one wins out. The bottom determines the result. If the bottom is hot, it heats everything upward — forbidden. If the bottom is cold, everything cools — permitted. This is the direct inverse of Rav's position.

### Heat Rises Physics

One might pause to note that from the standpoint of physics, Shmuel's position has a certain intuitive support: heat rises. If the hot substance is on top and the cold is on the bottom, much of the heat from above dissipates upward into the air rather than conducting downward. The ruling, of course, was reached before modern thermodynamics, but it is at least consistent with our understanding — *baruch Hashem*, as the lecturer observes, the halachic ruling and physical reality are not at odds here.

## Nataph Merutvo Al HaCheres

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נָטַף מֵרוֹטְבּוֹ עַל הַחֶרֶס וְחָזַר אֵלָיו — יִטּוֹל אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בְּחֶרֶס צוֹנֶנֶת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב דְּאָמַר ״עִילָּאָה גָּבַר״, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי יִטּוֹל אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ — דְּאָזֵל רוֹטֶב מַרְתַּח לֵיהּ לְחֶרֶס, וְהָדַר חֶרֶס מַרְתַּח לֵיהּ לְרוֹטֶב. וְכִי הָדַר רוֹטֶב אַפֶּסַח — קָא מִטְּוֵי פֶּסַח מֵחֲמַת חַמִּימוּתָא דְחֶרֶס, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״צְלִי אֵשׁ״, וְלֹא צָלִי מֵחֲמַת דָּבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If some **of the gravy of** the Paschal lamb **dripped onto the earthenware and returned to it, one** must **remove its place. It** might **enter your mind** to say that this is referring to **cold earthenware. Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav, who said the upper one prevails,** it is **due to this** reason that **one** must **remove its place.** According to Rav's view, **the gravy goes and heats the earthenware, and then the earthenware heats the gravy, and when the gravy returns to the Paschal lamb, the Paschal lamb becomes roasted from the heat of the earthenware, and the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"Roasted in fire"** (Exodus 12:8), **and not roasted due to something else.**

### Rav's Explanation

The *gemara* now attempts to derive proof from the *mishnah* itself. *Tnan* — we taught: *nataph merutvo al hacheres v'chazar elav* — if some of the juices of the *pesach* drip down onto the pottery and then bounce back, one must cut away the area of the *pesach* where the juice was reabsorbed — *yitol mekomo*. The *gemara's* initial assumption is *becheres tzonenet* — the pottery was cold to begin with.

*Bishlama le-Rav* — the *mishnah* works well according to Rav, who says the upper substance wins. The hot gravy drips from above onto the cold pottery. Since the upper is hot, *ila'ah gavar*, the pottery becomes heated. *Meratach lei lacheres* — the pottery gets hot. And then: *vehadar cheres umeratach lei lerutva* — the now-hot pottery turns around and roasts the gravy. When that gravy — which has now been heated by something other than fire — bounces back into the *pesach*, *kamatli lei lePesach machmas chamimusa decheres*: the *pesach* is being roasted by the heat of the pottery, or more directly, the gravy that was improperly heated is being reabsorbed. *Rachmana amar tzeli esh v'lo tzeli machmat davar acher* — the Torah requires roasting by fire, not by some other medium that itself absorbed fire. That explains why one must cut away the affected part.

### Challenge to Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר ״תַּתָּאָה גָּבַר״, חֶרֶס כֵּיוָן דְּצוֹנֵן הוּא — אַקּוֹרֵי מֵיקַר לֵיהּ לְרוֹטֶב, אַמַּאי יִטּוֹל אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ? כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּסוֹלֶת רוֹתַחַת, הָכָא נָמֵי בְּחֶרֶס רוֹתֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** the opinion of **Shmuel, who said the lower one prevails, since the earthenware is cold, it cools down the gravy.** In that case, **why must he remove its place?** The Gemara answers: **As Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **Shmuel said** in explanation of the mishna's next ruling in the case of gravy that dripped onto flour: The mishna is referring to **hot flour. Here, too,** it is referring to **hot earthenware.** Since the earthenware is already hot, it is a case of something hot that fell onto something hot, even according to Shmuel.

*Ela l'Shmuel d'amar tata'ah gavar* — but according to Shmuel, who rules that the bottom wins: if the pottery is cold and the hot gravy falls onto it from above, the bottom is cold. *Akorei meikar lei lerutva* — the cold pottery should be cooling off the gravy. *Amai yitol mekomo* — why then must anything be cut away? The gravy was never heated by the pottery; it was simply cooled. It should be just as if the gravy remained in contact only with the fire, and there should be no problem.

### Pottery Was Hot

The *gemara* resolves this by anticipating an answer that Rav Yirmeyah will state explicitly in Shmuel's name regarding the flour case below: *hacha nami becheres rotachat* — here too, the pottery was not cold; it was hot. If both the gravy and the pottery are hot, then regardless of whether one follows Rav or Shmuel, there is unambiguously a problem. The debate between *ila'ah gavar* and *tata'ah gavar* is relevant only when one substance is hot and one is cold. When both are hot, the *mishnah* cannot serve as proof for either position.

## Nataph Al HaSolet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נָטַף מֵרוֹטְבּוֹ עַל הַסּוֹלֶת יִקְמוֹץ אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ בְּסוֹלֶת צוֹנֶנֶת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב דְּאָמַר ״עִילָּאָה גָּבַר״, מִשּׁוּום הָכִי יִקְמוֹץ אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ — דְּמַרְתַּח לַהּ לְסוֹלֶת דְּהָדַר הוּדְרָנֵיהּ, וְהָדְרָא סוֹלֶת וּמַרְתְּחָא לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ, וְקָא מִטְּוֵי רוֹטֶב מֵחֲמַת חַמִּימוּתָא דְסוֹלֶת, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״צְלִי אֵשׁ״, וְלֹא צָלִי מֵחֲמַת דָּבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **We** also **learned** in the mishna that if some **of** the Paschal lamb's **gravy dripped onto flour, one** must **remove a handful of** flour from **its place. It** could **enter your mind** to say that this is talking about **cold flour. Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav, who said the upper one prevails,** it is **due to this** reason that **one** must **remove a handful** of flour from **its place, as** the gravy **heats the flour around it, and the flour then heats** the gravy, **and the gravy is roasted from the heat of the flour, and the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"Roasted in fire," and not roasted due to something else.**

### Gravy Into Flour

The *mishnah* continues: *nataf merotevo al hasolet* — some of the gravy drips not onto hard pottery but into flour. The flour absorbs it rather than bouncing it back, but the problem is similar: if the *pesach's* gravy is heated by something other than the fire, it may not be eaten, and the affected area of the flour must be removed.

### Rav's Flour Explanation

*Ka salka daitakh besolet tzonenet* — the initial assumption is that the flour was cold. *Bishlama le-Rav d'amar ila'ah gavar*: according to Rav, the top wins. The hot gravy falls from above, heats the cold flour — *demartach lah lasolet* — and once the flour is heated, it turns around and roasts the gravy inside it. *Veka mitvei rotev machmat chamimuta desolet* — the gravy is now being roasted by the heat of the flour. *V'Rachamana amar tzli esh v'lo tzli machmat davar acher* — the Torah says fire-roasting, not roasting by a secondary medium, even one that was itself heated. Exactly the same structure as the pottery case.

### Shmuel Challenge Flour

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר ״תַּתָּאָה גָּבַר״, סוֹלֶת כֵּיוָן דְּצוֹנֶנֶת הִיא — אַקּוֹרֵי קָא מֵיקַר לֵיהּ, לְמָה לִי יִקְמוֹץ אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ? (תִּסְגֵּי לֵיהּ בְּיִטּוֹל אֶת מְקוֹמוֹ!) אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּסוֹלֶת רוֹתַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to** the opinion of **Shmuel, who said the lower one prevails, since the flour is cold it cools down** the gravy. In that case, **why do I** need to say: **One** must **remove a handful** of flour from **its place? It should be enough for one to remove** a small amount from **its place,** and it should not be necessary to take anything more. With regard to this **Rabbi Yirmeya said** that **Shmuel said:** The mishna is referring to **hot flour.** The gravy is therefore roasted from the heat of the flour, and an entire handful of flour must be removed.

*Ela l'Shmuel d'amar tata'ah gavar*: but if the *pesach* is hot and the bottom wins, the hot *pesach* should heat up the cold oil and cause it to be absorbed throughout — *mivla bala* — not merely in the outer layer. Why then does the *mishnah* say peeling is enough? If we follow Shmuel, the entire *pesach* should be forbidden to non-*kohanim* because the oil should have penetrated throughout.

### Hot Flour Resolution

*Amar Rav Yirmeyah amar Shmuel: besolet rotachat* — Rav Yirmeyah, citing Shmuel directly, resolves it: the *mishnah* is speaking of boiling hot flour, not cold. The flour was already hot. And it is from this ruling that the *gemara* extends the answer retroactively to the pottery case — *hacha nami becheres rotachat* — the pottery was also hot. In both cases, since both substances were hot, there is no challenge to either Rav or Shmuel.

It is worth noting in passing that *Tosafot* raises the question of *ein bishul achar bishul* — the principle that once something has been fully cooked, additional cooking has no halachic effect. The *mishnah* would presumably be speaking of a *pesach* that has not yet been fully roasted, so additional "roasting" by the pottery or flour does constitute a change in its status. *Tosafot* engages with this and attempts to fit it into the *mishnah's* cases.

One might also ask: if the gravy drips into a large quantity of flour, could *bitul* apply? In theory, yes — but *bitul* would not apply to the immediate area where the gravy fell, where there is a concentrated and identifiable taste of meat juice. One would focus the removal on that localized spot.

## Sach BaShemen Shel Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: סָכוֹ בְּשֶׁמֶן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה, אִם חֲבוּרַת כֹּהֲנִים — יֹאכֵלוּ. אִם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל, אִם חַי הוּא — יְדִיחֶנּוּ, אִם צָלִי הוּא — יִקְלוֹף אֶת הַחִיצוֹן. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב דְּאָמַר ״עִילָּאָה גָּבַר״, אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי סַגִּי לֵיהּ בִּקְלִיפָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּעִילָּאָה צוֹנֵן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: In a case where **one smears** the Paschal lamb **with *teruma* oil, if** the Paschal lamb belongs to **a group of priests they may eat it,** as they are permitted to eat *teruma*. **If** it belongs to a group **of Israelites,** then **if** the Paschal lamb is still **raw, one** must **rinse it** in order to remove the *teruma* oil; **and if it is roasted, one** must **peel off the outer** layer. **Granted, according to Rav, who said the upper one prevails, for this** reason **it is sufficient** to remove only the outer **peel, because the upper one is cold** and therefore the oil is not absorbed deeply into the meat.

### Oil on Pesach Rules

The *gemara* now seeks proof from a different part of the *mishnah*: *sach ba'shemen shel terumah* — if one rubbed *terumah* oil onto the *pesach*. If the group eating the *pesach* is a *chavurat kohanim*, no problem — *kohanim* may eat *terumah* and the *pesach* together. But if the group consists of non-*kohanim*:

### Raw Pesach Rinsing

*Im chai hu, yadi'chenu* — if the *pesach* is raw, simply rinse off the oil. This is the cold-into-cold case: a cold oil on a cold raw surface, and rinsing is sufficient.

### Roasted Pesach Peeling

*Im tseli hu, yiklof et hachitzon* — if the *pesach* is roasted, one must peel off the outer layer. Even if the roasted *pesach* is now cold, roasted meat is absorbent — unlike a hard smooth surface, it is porous and soft, comparable to a sponge rather than a stone. One cannot simply rinse off the surface of an absorbent material.

### Rav: Oil on Hot Pesach

*Bishlama le-Rav d'amar ila'ah gavar*: the *mishnah* works well according to Rav. The oil is on top, and the oil is cold. Since the upper wins, even if the *pesach* is hot, everything cools down — the oil does not penetrate deeply. Only the outermost surface absorbs the oil through natural contact, hence *yiklof et hachitzon* — peeling the outer layer is sufficient. There is no concern that the oil penetrated throughout.

### Shmuel's Oil Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר ״תַּתָּאָה גָּבַר״, כֵּיוָן דְּחַם הוּא — מִבְלָע בָּלַע, אַמַּאי סַגִּי לֵיהּ בִּקְלִיפָה? נִיתְּסַר לִגְמָרֵי! שָׁאנֵי סִיכָה, דְּמַשֶּׁהוּ בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דַּעֲבִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** according **to Shmuel, who said: The lower one prevails, since** the meat, which is on the bottom, **is hot, it absorbs** the oil. In that case, **why is it enough for it** to be permitted when only the outer **peel** is removed? **It should be entirely forbidden.** The Gemara answers: **Smearing is different because it is done with only a minute amount.** Since one smears only a little bit of oil, there is not enough oil to render the entire offering forbidden.

*Ela l'Shmuel d'amar tata'ah gavar*: but if the *pesach* is hot and the bottom wins, the hot *pesach* should heat up the cold oil and cause it to be absorbed throughout — *mivla bala* — not merely in the outer layer. Why then does the *mishnah* say peeling is enough? If we follow Shmuel, the entire *pesach* should be forbidden to non-*kohanim* because the oil should have penetrated throughout.

### Oil Amount Limited

The *gemara* responds: *shani sicha de-mashu be'alma hu* — rubbing oil is different; it is only a minimal, surface-level application. Even if the *pesach* is hot and the bottom wins in principle, there simply is not enough oil to penetrate deeply. It remains limited to the outer shell. The *gemara* notes further that this is retained heat in the meat — the *pesach* is not sitting directly over a fire — which also limits how deeply the oil can penetrate.

One might wish this were a general *kashrus* principle — that a small amount of a forbidden substance need not be treated as absorbed more than superficially — but that is not how *halacha* generally operates. The *gemara* is making a specific, limited point about the mechanics of rubbing oil.

### No Proof From Mishna

The conclusion is that the *mishnah* cannot prove either Rav or Shmuel correct. In the pottery and flour cases, both substances were hot and the debate is irrelevant. In the oil-on-roasted-*pesach* case, the limited quantity of oil means the *mishnah* is consistent with both positions. The dispute between *ila'ah gavar* and *tata'ah gavar* remains unresolved from the *mishnah*.

## Baraita: Tata'ah Gavar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַם לְתוֹךְ חַם — אָסוּר. וְכֵן צוֹנֵן שֶׁנָּתַן לְתוֹךְ חַם — אָסוּר. חַם לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן וְצוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — מֵדִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** It **was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel:** If **hot** permitted food falls **into hot** forbidden food, **it is forbidden. And, so too, cold** permitted food **that one put into hot** forbidden food **is forbidden.** If **hot** food falls **into cold** food, **and** similarly, if **cold** food falls **into cold** food, **one** must **rinse** the permitted food, and it remains permitted.

### Baraita Text

*Tanya kivsei d'Shmuel* — a *beraita* was taught in accordance with Shmuel. *Cham l'toch cham assur, v'chein tzonen she-nasan l'toch cham assur* — hot into hot is forbidden, and likewise cold into hot is forbidden. In both cases, the bottom is hot, and the bottom wins — that is Shmuel's position. *Cham l'toch tzonen v'tzonen l'toch tzonen, madiach* — hot into cold, or cold into cold, since the bottom is cold, simply rinsing suffices. This *beraita* is a direct ruling like Shmuel, and it is the basis for ruling *l'halacha* like *tata'ah gavar*.

### Objection to Hot Into Cold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַם לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן מֵדִיחַ — כֵּיוָן דְּחָם הוּא, אַדְּמֵיקַר לֵיהּ — אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא בָּלַע פּוּרְתָּא, קְלִיפָה מִיהָא נִיבְעֵי! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: חַם לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — קוֹלֵף, צוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — מֵדִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is it true that if **hot** permitted food falls **into cold** forbidden food, **one** must **rinse** the permitted food and it remains permitted? **Since it is hot, until** the bottom food **cools it, it is impossible that it will not absorb a little** of the forbidden food. Therefore, **it should at least require** the removal of the outer **peel;** rinsing it should not be sufficient. **Rather, say** the following corrected version: If **hot** food falls **into cold** food, **one** must **peel** off the outer layer; if **cold** food falls **into cold** food, **one** must **rinse** it off and that is sufficient.

But the *gemara* resists accepting the *beraita* as stated. *Cham l'toch tzonen madiach?* — hot into cold and you merely rinse it off? *Keivan d'cham hu*, since the upper substance is hot, *ad mekar lei* — while the cold bottom is in the process of cooling it down, *ee efshar d'lo bala purta* — it is impossible that nothing at all is absorbed in the interim. Heat transfer takes time; during that cooling process, some minimal absorption must occur. *Klipah miha nibei* — at the very least, a *klipah*, an outer layer, should be required.

### Amended Baraita

The *gemara* accordingly revises the *beraita*: *ela eima* — say instead: *cham l'toch tzonen, kolef* — hot into cold requires peeling off an outer layer. *Tzonen l'toch tzonen, madiach* — cold into cold, just rinse. This amendment now captures the nuance: even if the cold bottom wins and cools everything down, during the process of cooling, the hot upper substance causes some minimal absorption at the point of contact.

### Meat and Milk Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בָּשָׂר רוֹתֵחַ שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ חָלָב רוֹתֵחַ, וְכֵן צוֹנֵן שֶׁנָּפַל לְתוֹךְ חַם — אָסוּר. חַם לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן וְצוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — מֵדִיחַ. חַם לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן מֵדִיחַ — כֵּיוָן דְּחַם הוּא, אַדְּמֵיקַר לֵיהּ — אִי אֶפְשָׁר דְּלָא בָּלַע פּוּרְתָּא, קְלִיפָה מִיהָא נִיבְעֵי! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: חַם לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — קוֹלֵף, צוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — מֵדִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **Hot meat that fell into hot milk, and so too, cold** meat **that fell into hot** milk, **is prohibited.** If **hot** meat falls **into cold** milk **and** similarly, if **cold** meat falls **into cold** milk, **one** must **rinse** it off and that is sufficient. The Gemara asks: Is it true that if **hot** meat falls **into cold** milk, **one** must **rinse** it off and that is sufficient? **Since it is hot, until** the bottom food **cools it, it is impossible that it will not absorb a little** of the milk. Therefore, **it should at least require** the removal of the outer **peel. Rather, say** the following corrected version: If **hot** meat falls **into cold** milk, **one** must **peel** off the outer layer; if **cold** meat falls **into cold** milk, **one** must **rinse** it off and that is sufficient.

A second *beraita* makes the identical point in the domain of *basar b'chalav*: *basar rote'ach she-nafal l'toch chalav rote'ach* — hot meat that fell into hot milk, *v'chein tzonen she-nafal l'toch cham* — or cold meat that fell into hot milk: *assur*, forbidden. The bottom is hot, the bottom wins. *Cham l'toch tzonen, tzonen l'toch tzonen, madiach* — if the bottom is cold, whether the falling substance is hot or cold, merely rinse.

### Second Objection

Again the *gemara* objects: *cham l'toch tzonen madiach?* — hot meat into cold milk and one need only rinse? *Keivan d'cham hu, ad m'karei lei, ee efshar d'lo bala purta* — while the cold milk is cooling off the hot meat, some absorption must occur. *Klipah miha ba'ei* — a *klipah* is at minimum required.

### Second Amendment

*Ela eima*: here too the *baraita* must be corrected. *Cham l'toch tzonen, kolef* — hot into cold, remove an outer layer. *Tzonen l'toch tzonen, madiach* — cold into cold, rinse.

### Kleipah Consensus

The upshot of both emended *baraitot* is this: the practical difference between Rav and Shmuel lies in which hot substance "wins." For Shmuel, the bottom wins when hot; for Rav, the top wins when hot — and whichever wins, it is as if both are hot, producing a full absorption requiring more than *klipah*. But both Rav and Shmuel would agree that when the "winning" side is cold, there is still a minimal transfer during the cooling process — and therefore at least *klipah* is required whenever one substance is hot, even if it is on the "losing" side.

*Tosafot* raises a practical question: when hot meat falls into cold milk, we require *klipah* from the meat — but how does one perform *klipah* on a liquid? *Tosafot* records two approaches. One is to estimate the volume of milk that would correspond to a *klipah*-thickness at the point of contact, and then require *shishim* against that amount in the remaining milk. *Rabbeinu Tam*, however, holds that the *klipah* requirement in this cold-bottom scenario is entirely *d'rabbanan* — a rabbinic stringency introduced at the end of the *sugya*, since fundamentally Shmuel's principle is that the cold bottom nullifies any concern. Consequently, where *klipah* is structurally impossible — as with a liquid — the rabbinic requirement does not apply, and the liquid is entirely permitted.

A related question concerns *iruy* — pouring boiling liquid in a stream onto something. One might reason: Shmuel says *tata'ah gavar*, so an uninterrupted stream of boiling milk poured onto cold meat would be cooled by the cold bottom and require only *klipah* at most. *Tosafot* addresses this and the resolution drawn from their discussion distinguishes between an uninterrupted stream — one that remains attached to the boiling source — and a broken stream. An uninterrupted pour is treated as an extension of the boiling vessel; the full force of the heat applies and *tata'ah gavar* does not apply for the duration of the stream. A broken stream, by contrast, falls under the standard analysis.

## Maliach K'Rotach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: צוֹנֵן לְתוֹךְ צוֹנֵן — מֵדִיחַ. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא מְלָחוֹ, אֲבָל מְלָחוֹ — אָסוּר. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מָלִיחַ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּרוֹתֵחַ. כָּבוּשׁ הֲרֵי הוּא כִּמְבוּשָּׁל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita* quoted above: If **cold** meat falls **into cold** milk or into a prohibited food, **one** must **rinse** it off and that is sufficient. **Rav Huna said: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where **he did not salt** either of the food items. **However, if he salted** one of them **it is forbidden, as Shmuel said: A salted** food item **is** considered **like a boiling** food item with regard to its ability to transmit flavor. Additionally, a food item **marinated** in vinegar, brine, or the like **is** considered **like a cooked** food item, as it absorbs flavor from the liquid in which it is marinated or from other foods with which it is marinated.

### Rav Huna Salted Exception

The *gemara* returns to the rule that cold-into-cold requires only rinsing. *Amar Rav Huna: lo shanu ela she-lo malcho* — this leniency applies only to unsalted meat. The rule that cold-on-cold merely requires rinsing assumes the meat has not been salted.

### Shmuel: Salted Like Hot

*De-amar Shmuel: maliach harei hu ke-rote'ach* — Shmuel teaches that something salted is halachically equivalent to something boiling. Just as heat causes absorption, so does salt. Accordingly, salted meat that falls into milk, even if everything is cold by temperature, behaves as if it were hot and cannot be resolved by mere rinsing.

### Rava: Heavy Salt Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָא דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מָלִיחַ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּרוֹתֵחַ (וְכוּ׳) — לֹא (אֲמַרַן) [שָׁנוּ] אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱכָל מֵחֲמַת מִלְחוֹ, אֲבָל נֶאֱכָל מֵחֲמַת מִלְחוֹ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** With regard to **that which Shmuel said,** that **a salted** food **is like a boiling** food, **we said** it **only** with regard to something salted to the point that **it is not** typically **eaten due to its salt. But** if the food is still **eaten due to its salt,** i.e., despite its having been salted, then it is **not** considered like something that is boiling, and it does not transmit flavor.

*Amar Rava*: not every degree of salting triggers this rule. *Lo amran ela she-eino ne'echal machmat malcho* — Shmuel's principle of *maliach k'rotach* applies only when the meat is so heavily salted that it is unfit to eat in that state. Rashi understands this as salted somewhat beyond the edible threshold, as one would salt meat for short-term preservation. *Tosafot* takes an even more stringent view, describing the level as the extreme salting used for long-term preservation, requiring substantial soaking to remove. *Avel ne'echal machmat malcho* — if the meat is still fit to eat despite the salt, it does not carry the status of *rotach* and remains in the category of cold, requiring only rinsing.

### Pigeon Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בַּר גּוֹזָלָא דִּנְפַל לְכַדָּא דְּכַמְכָּא, שַׁרְיֵיא רַב חִינָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִפַּשְׁרוּנְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a particular young bird that fell into a jug of *kamka*,** also known as *kutaḥ*, a food item that contains milk. There was a question whether the food is considered a forbidden mixture of meat and milk. **Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rava of** the city of **Pashronya, permitted** it.

The *gemara* relates a practical incident: *hahu bar gozala de-nafal le-khadda de-kamcha* — a pigeon that had been salted for the purpose of removing blood fell into *kamcha*, a dairy dip resembling sour cream or cottage cheese. *Sherayei Rav Chinana berei de-Rava mi-Pashrunya* — Rav Chinana permitted it and ruled that rinsing was sufficient.

### Rava's Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאן חַכִּים לְמִישְׁרֵא מִילְּתָא כִּי הָא, אִי לָאו רַב חִינָּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מִפַּשְׁרוּנְיָא, דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה הוּא. אָמַר לְךָ, כִּי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מָלִיחַ הֲרֵי הוּא כְּרוֹתֵחַ — שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱכָל מֵחֲמַת מִלְחוֹ, הַאי נֶאֱכָל מֵחֲמַת מִלְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** about this: **Who is wise** enough **to permit something** as complicated **as this, if not Rav Ḥinnana, son of Rava of Pashronya, as he is a great man** and can recognize the reason for leniency even in a case that appears to be prohibited? **He** could have **said to you** in explanation of his lenient ruling: **When Shmuel said** that **a salted** food item **is like a boiling** food item, that *halakha* concerned a food that was salted to the point that **it is not eaten due to its salt,** but **this** *kutaḥ* **is** still **eaten due to,** i.e., despite, **its salt.** Therefore, the case is comparable to a cold food that falls into another cold food, which is permitted after it is rinsed.

*Amar Rava: man chakim le-mishra milta ki hai, ilav Rav Chinana berei de-Rava mi-Pashrunya, de-gavra rabba hu* — Rava praised him: who but such a great scholar would have the wisdom to permit this? *Ki amar Shmuel maliach harei hu ke-rote'ach* — when Shmuel said that salted is like boiling, that was only when *she-eino ne'echal machmat malcho*, the meat is inedibly oversalted. But this pigeon, *ne'echal machmat malcho*, was still edible despite its salt, and therefore did not carry the stringent status of *rotach*. Rinsing was sufficient.

## Roasted / Porous / Spiced

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי חַי, אֲבָל צָלִי — בָּעֵי קְלִיפָה. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ פִּילֵי, אֲבָל אִית בֵּיהּ פִּילֵי — אָסוּר. וְאִי מְתוּבַּל בְּתַבְלֵי — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that **this applies** only if the bird is **raw; but** if it is **roasted, it requires** the removal of the outer **peel.** The roasting softens the meat, enabling it to absorb flavor more easily. **And we said** that the bird is permitted **only when it does not have cracks; but** if **it has cracks, it is forbidden** because the milk is absorbed into the cracks. **And if it has been flavored with spices it is forbidden** because the spices soften the meat, causing it to be absorbent.

### Roasted Cold Exception

The *gemara* now catalogues additional exceptions to the rule that cold-on-cold requires only rinsing. *V'hani milei, chai* — the leniency of rinsing applies to raw meat. *Aval tseli, ba'ei klipah* — if the meat is roasted, even if it is cold, a *klipah* must be removed. Roasted meat, even cold, is absorbent by its nature. A roasted piece of meat is soft and porous; one cannot assume that a foreign substance remains only on the surface, the way one might with a smooth non-absorbent material. It must be treated with the same removal of the outer layer that one would apply in a case of minimal heat transfer.

### Porous Meat Exception

*V'lo amrinan, ela deleit bei peila* — and even the *klipah* rule for roasted meat assumes the meat has no deep cracks or crevices. *Aval it bei peila* — if the meat does have significant cracks and fissures, even *klipah* is insufficient, because the foreign liquid can penetrate deeply into the interior through those openings. In that case the prohibition is more extensive.

### Spiced Meat Exception

*U-metubal betavlin* — similarly, if meat is heavily spiced, even cold, it is *assur*. Heavy spicing creates an absorbency analogous in effect to heavy salting: the surface becomes particularly receptive to drawing in foreign substances, even without heat.

### Absorbency Principle

The broader principle emerging from these exceptions is that absorbency — not just heat — drives halachic concern. The basic rule is that heat causes transfer and cold does not; hence cold-into-cold requires only rinsing. But that baseline assumes the cold substance is not independently absorbent. Where the substance has structural or chemical properties that cause it to absorb even in the absence of heat — roasting, spicing, cracking — the cold-rinsing rule no longer applies.

## Reicha Debate: Rav vs. Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: בְּשַׂר שְׁחוּטָה שָׁמֵן, שֶׁצְּלָאוֹ עִם בְּשַׂר נְבֵילָה כָּחוּשׁ — אָסוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא — מִפַּטְּמִי מֵהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: Fatty kosher meat that one roasted** in an oven together **with lean non-kosher meat is forbidden,** even if the two meats never came into contact with one another. **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? It is that **they are flavored from one another.** The fatty meat emits an aroma that is absorbed in the non-kosher meat. The aroma is then transferred back to the kosher meat, causing the kosher meat to absorb some aroma from the non-kosher meat.

### Rav: Reicha Transfers

The *gemara* now introduces the second major principle of the daf, shifting from the question of contact-based heat transfer to the question of *reicha* — taste transfer through aroma without physical contact. *Amar Rav: basar shechuta shemena, she-tzla'o im basar neveila kechusha* — Rav states that if fatty *shechted* kosher meat is roasted alongside lean *neveila* meat, with no contact between them, the kosher meat becomes forbidden. *Maptemi mehadadi* — they fatten one another; the fatty kosher meat, through its vapor and aroma, draws out the flavor of the lean *treif* meat and absorbs it back. Rav holds that *reicha milsa hi* — aroma has halachic status and constitutes a transfer of taste. The case is striking: even the lean *treif* meat, which emits less aroma, still contributes enough through the fatty kosher meat's drawing power that the result is forbidden.

### Levi: Reicha Irrelevant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵוִי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׂר שְׁחוּטָה כָּחוּשׁ, שֶׁצְּלָאוֹ עִם בְּשַׂר נְבֵילָה שָׁמֵן — מוּתָּר. מַאי טַעְמָא — רֵיחָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, וְרֵיחָא לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא. עָבֵיד לֵוִי עוֹבָדָא בֵּי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא בִּגְדִי וְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Levi said:** That aroma does not cause meat to be forbidden. **Even lean kosher meat that one roasted with fatty non-kosher meat is permitted. What is the reason** for this *halakha*? Although the non-kosher meat emits an aroma that is absorbed into the kosher meat, **it is merely an aroma, and an aroma is nothing** significant. The Gemara relates that **Levi took action,** meaning that he put his opinion into practice, **in the house of the Exilarch with a kid and something else,** i.e., a pig, that had been roasted together. Levi did not prohibit the meat of the kid due to the aroma of the pig.

Levi goes to the opposite extreme: *afilu basar shechuta kachush she-tzla'o im basar neveila shamen* — even if the kosher meat is lean and the *treif* meat is fatty, so that the entire atmosphere is laden with *treif* aromas, the kosher meat remains permitted. *Reicha be'alma hu, v'reicha lav milta hi* — it is mere smell, and smell has no halachic status. Levi's position may carry a further implication: even if some aroma does in fact penetrate the kosher meat and alters its taste, *reicha* as such does not constitute the halachically operative concept of *ta'am* — taste. Only contact produces genuine *ta'am*; smell alone does not.

### Levi Practical Ruling

*Amar avid Levi uvda be-Reish Galuta be-gedi v'davar acher* — Levi actually rendered a practical ruling in the house of the *Reish Galuta*, permitting a goat that had been cooked in the same oven as a pig. Whatever the pig was doing in the *Reish Galuta's* household is left unaddressed, but the ruling was clear: according to Levi, the goat was entirely permitted.

## Two Pesachim Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵין צוֹלִין שְׁנֵי פְסָחִים כְּאֶחָד מִפְּנֵי הַתַּעֲרוֹבֶת. מַאי לָאו — תַּעֲרוֹבֶת טְעָמִים, וְקַשְׁיָא לְלֵוִי! לָא, מִפְּנֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת גּוּפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection: One** may **not roast two Paschal lambs together due to the mixing. What, is it not** prohibited due to **the mixing of flavors,** i.e., due to the aromas that waft from one to the other, **and it** poses **a difficulty to** the opinion of **Levi?** The Gemara rejects this challenge: **No,** it is prohibited **due to the mixing of carcasses.** The groups who are roasting their Paschal offerings might accidentally switch offerings, in which case the offerings will be eaten by people who did not register for them.

### Baraita Text

*Meitivei* — the *gemara* challenges. A *beraita* states: *ein tzolin shnei pesachim k'echad mipnei ha-ta'arovot* — one may not roast two *korban Pesach* animals side by side, because of *ta'arovot* — mixing.

### Mixing Question

What does *ta'arovot* mean here? Either the actual bodies of the two *pesachim* will become physically confused — one group might eat from the wrong animal — or the concern is that tastes will be exchanged through aroma transfer in the air. The second reading would present a direct challenge to Levi.

Consider: if one *pesach* absorbs the aroma and flavor of another, the result is that someone is eating from the flavor of another group's *pesach*. The Torah specifies *ish kefi achlo* — each person eats from their own *pesach*; one may not eat from another *chaburah's pesach*. Accordingly, if flavor transfer through *reicha* is halachically significant, roasting two *pesachim* side by side could cause each to absorb the other's taste, thereby making the meat forbidden to its rightful owners.

### Problem for Rav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֲפִילּוּ גְּדִי וְטָלֶה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מִפְּנֵי גּוּפִין — הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי אֲפִילּוּ גְּדִי וְטָלֶה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִפְּנֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת טְעָמִים, מָה לִי גְּדִי וְטָלֶה מָה לִי גְּדִי וּגְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **So too,** one can conclude that **this** explanation **is reasonable from** the fact **that it is taught in the latter clause** that the ruling applies **even** if the two offerings are **a kid and a lamb. Granted, if you say** that the reason is **due to** the mixing of **carcasses, this is** why it **was taught** that the *halakha* applies to **even a kid and a lamb.** The *baraita* needed to teach that although they do not look alike, there is still a concern that after they have been skinned they will be mixed up. **But if you say** that the reason is **due to the mixing of flavors, what is** the difference between a case in which the two offerings are **a kid and a lamb and** one in which they are **a kid and** another **kid?** The case of the kid and the lamb mentioned at the end of the *baraita* would not teach anything new.

If *ta'arovot* in the *beraita* refers to taste-mixing through aroma, this supports the concern about *reicha* — but it presents a difficulty for Rav. The *beraita* continues: *v'afilu gedi v'taleh* — one may not even roast a goat and a sheep side by side. If the concern is taste transfer through aroma, why specify "even a goat and a sheep"? A goat-and-goat and a goat-and-sheep are equally subject to *reicha* transfer — there would be nothing novel about adding the goat-and-sheep case. The word "even" implies that the goat-and-sheep case is a *chiddush*, a surprising stringency.

### Two Pots Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי, עַל כׇּרְחָיךְ מִפְּנֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת גּוּפִין הוּא דְּאָסוּר, אֲבָל תַּעֲרוֹבֶת טְעָמִים — שְׁרֵי, לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁצְּלָאוֹ בִּשְׁתֵּי קְדֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what** do you say? **Perforce,** it is **due to the mixing of carcasses that it is prohibited, but a mixing of flavors is permitted. Let us say that this will be a refutation of** the opinion of **Rav,** who prohibited the mixing of flavors by means of an aroma. **Rabbi Yirmeya said: With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where one roasted** the offerings **in two pots.** Consequently, they do not absorb flavor from one another.

*Hachi nami mistabra* — the *gemara* accepts this: the *beraita's* concern is for *ta'arovot gufin* — mixing of the actual bodies. If two goats are roasted side by side, one might serve a piece from the wrong one; with a goat and a sheep they are easily distinguishable by appearance, yet the *beraita* still prohibits it — because even then, if they are adjacent, physical confusion remains possible, and that is the *chiddush*.

The *gemara* then asks: does this disprove Rav, since the *beraita* apparently dismisses the concern of taste-mixing through aroma? *Amar Rav Yirmiyah: hacha b'mai askinan — kegon she-tzla'o bi-shtei kedeirot* — the *beraita* speaks of a case like two covered pots, where the smells are contained and cannot transfer between them. In that configuration, there is no *reicha* concern, and the only remaining concern is physical body-mixing — hence the focus on *ta'arovot gufin*.

### Rav Reinterpreted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁתֵּי קְדֵירוֹת סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כְּעֵין שְׁתֵּי קְדֵירוֹת. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין צוֹלִין שְׁנֵי פְסָחִים כְּאֶחָד מִפְּנֵי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת. מַאי תַּעֲרוֹבֶת — תַּעֲרוֹבֶת טְעָמִים. וַאֲפִילּוּ כְּעֵין שְׁתֵּי קְדֵירוֹת, דְּלֵיכָּא תַּעֲרוֹבֶת טְעָמִים — אָסוּר מִשּׁוּם תַּעֲרוֹבֶת גּוּפִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ גְּדִי וְטָלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise: **Could it enter your mind** to say that they roasted the Paschal offerings **in two pots?** It is prohibited to the roast the Paschal offering in a pot. **Rather, say** that they were roasted in a manner **similar to two pots,** meaning that they were distanced from each other and separated by a partition. **And this is** what the *baraita* is **saying: One** may **not roast two Paschal offerings together due to mixing. What** is this **mixing?** It is **the mixing of flavors. And even** roasting them in a manner **similar to two pots, where there is no mixing of flavors, is** also **prohibited, due to** the concern with regard to the **mixing of carcasses. And** this is the *halakha* **even** if the animals are **a kid and a lamb.**

This reading of the *beraita* actually clarifies Rav's position. *V'hachi ka-amar*: according to Rav, the *beraita* should be read as follows. The primary prohibition on roasting two *pesachim* together is because of *ta'arovot ta'amim* — the smells will intermingle, and this happens in virtually every case. *V'afilu ke-ein shtei kedeiros* — and even if one sets up the scenario like two separate pots, with a dividing mound or separation that blocks scent transfer, it remains forbidden — *mishum ta'arovot gufin* — because the bodies might still get confused. *V'afilu gedi v'taleh* — and for that concern, even a goat and a sheep is prohibited. Rav's original concern for *reicha* stands, and the *beraita* adds the further point that even without *reicha* transfer, the physical mixing concern alone would forbid it. The phrase *ke-ein shtei kedeiros* — like two pots, meaning a covered or separated arrangement — emerges here as an important category that will recur.

## Bread and Wine Tannaim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי כְּתַנָּאֵי. הָרוֹדֶה פַּת חַמָּה וּנְתָנָהּ עַל פִּי חָבִית יַיִן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה — רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹסֵר, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַתִּיר. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מַתִּיר בְּשֶׁל חִיטִּין וְאוֹסֵר בְּשֶׁל שְׂעוֹרִים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַשְּׂעוֹרִים שׁוֹאֲבוֹת. מַאי לָאו תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּמָר סָבַר: רֵיחָא — לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, וּמָר סָבַר: רֵיחָא — מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said:** This is **like** the following dispute between ***tanna'im*:** In the case of **one who removes hot bread** from an oven **and places it on top of a barrel of wine** that is ***teruma*, Rabbi Meir prohibits** a non-priest from eating the bread. In his opinion, the bread absorbs the aroma of the *teruma* wine and therefore attains the status of *teruma*. **And Rabbi Yehuda permits** it. **And Rabbi Yosei permits** bread made **of wheat,** which is not very absorbent, **but prohibits** bread made **of barley, because barley draws out** and absorbs the aroma. **What, is it not** a dispute between ***tanna'im*? One Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds that an aroma is nothing** significant, **and one Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds that an aroma is something** significant.

### Bread Wine Case

*Amar Rav Mari: ke-tana'ei* — Rav Mari states that this debate between Rav and Levi parallels a *tannaic* dispute. *Ha-rodeh pas chamah u-nesanah pi chavis yayin shel terumah* — one takes a hot loaf of bread fresh from the oven and places it over the opening of a barrel of *terumah* wine. The question is whether the aroma of the wine, drawn out by the heat of the bread, penetrates the bread to the extent that the bread must now be treated as *terumah* — forbidden to non-*kohanim*.

### Three Opinions

Three *tannaitic* opinions are recorded. *Rabbi Meir osser* — Rabbi Meir forbids the bread in all cases; the heat draws the wine's aroma into the bread. *Rabbi Yehuda mattir* — Rabbi Yehuda permits in all cases; no halachically significant transfer occurs. *Rabbi Yossi mattir be-shel chittin v'osser be-shel se'orim* — Rabbi Yossi distinguishes by grain type: wheat bread is not particularly absorbent and is permitted; barley bread is absorbent — *mipnei she-ha-se'orim sho'avot*, barley draws in the wine — and is forbidden.

### Is It a Reicha Debate?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְלֵוִי, וַדַּאי תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. לְרַב, נֵימָא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: **According to** the opinion of **Levi,** i.e., that aroma is insignificant, **it certainly is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosei hold that it is significant, and Levi accepts the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda that aroma is insignificant. However, according **to** the opinion of **Rav, shall we say it is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*?**

*Mai lav tana'ei? De-mar savar reicha lav milsa hi* — is this not simply a *tannaic* debate over the halachic status of *reicha*? Rabbi Yehuda holds *reicha lav milsa* — smell has no effect; Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi (at least for barley) hold *reicha milsa hi*.

*Le-Levi vadai tana'ei* — for Levi, who rules that *reicha* is irrelevant, he must concede that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi disagree with him. He holds like Rabbi Yehuda. *Le-Rav neima tana'ei* — must Rav similarly concede that Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with him?

### Rav's Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רַב: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא רֵיחָא מִילְּתָא הִיא, לָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ דְּהַהִיא, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בְּפַת חַמָּה וְחָבִית פְּתוּחָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר. בְּפַת צוֹנֶנֶת וְחָבִית מְגוּפָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּפַת חַמָּה וְחָבִית חֲתוּמָה, פַּת צוֹנֶנֶת וְחָבִית פְּתוּחָה. וְהָא נָמֵי כְּפַת חַמָּה וְחָבִית פְּתוּחָה דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav** could have **said to you: Everyone agrees that aroma is something** significant. The dispute is about whether bread absorbs aroma in the circumstance under discussion. **Was it not stated with regard to that** mishna that **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said that Reish Lakish said: With regard to hot bread and an open barrel, everyone agrees that it is prohibited** because it certainly draws out the aroma; and **with regard to cold bread and a closed barrel, everyone agrees it is permitted? They disagreed only with regard to hot bread and a sealed barrel** because perhaps the bread nonetheless draws out aroma through the cracks. Similarly, they disputed the case of **cold bread and an open barrel. And this** case of two Paschal offerings roasted in the same oven **is also** considered **like** the case of **hot bread and an open barrel.**

*Amar lach Rav* — Rav responds that in principle, everyone agrees with him: *de-kulei alma reicha milsa hi* — the *reicha* itself, when it actually transfers, is halachically significant. The debate among the *tannaim* is not about *whether* transferred aroma matters, but about *when* the circumstances produce actual transfer. As *Rabbah bar bar Chana* cited in the name of *Reish Lakish*:

### Consensus Cases

*Be-pas chamah ve-chavis pesuchah, divrei ha-kol assur* — hot bread and an open barrel: everyone agrees this is forbidden. The heat draws out the aroma, the open barrel releases it — everyone would agree that significant transfer occurs. *Be-pas tzonenes ve-chavis megufah, divrei ha-kol muttar* — cold bread and a sealed barrel: everyone agrees this is permitted, regardless of one's principle, because there is no mechanism for transfer. *Lo nechelku ela be-pas chamah ve-chavis chasumah, i nami be-pas tzonenes ve-chavis pesuchah* — the *tannaic* debate concerns only the intermediate cases: hot bread over a sealed barrel, or cold bread over an open barrel. In those cases, whether enough transfer occurs to be halachically significant is debated among Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yehuda, and Rabbi Yossi.

Rav's synthesis is thus: *reicha milsa hi* is agreed upon by all; the disagreement concerns only the factual threshold of when *reicha* is substantial enough to trigger halachic concern.

### Pesachim Application

*De-hahi* — and accordingly, the case Rav discussed — fatty kosher meat roasted alongside lean *treif* meat — *ke-pas chamah ve-chavis petucha damya* — is like hot bread over an open barrel. Everyone, even Rabbi Yehuda, would agree this is forbidden. The *pesach* roasting scenario, by extension, is one where all would agree that *reicha* transfer is occurring at a meaningful level.

## Bread/Fish With Meat Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב כָּהֲנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִינָּנָא סָבָא: פַּת שֶׁאֲפָאָהּ עִם צָלִי בַּתַּנּוּר — אָסוּר לְאׇכְלָהּ בְּכוּתָּחָא. הָהִיא בִּינִיתָא דְּאִיטְּווֹא בַּהֲדֵי בִּישְׂרָא, אַסְרַהּ רָבָא מִפַּרְזִיקְיָא לְמֵיכְלַיהּ בְּכוּתָּחָא. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּמִילְחָא נָמֵי אֲסוּרָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּקַשְׁיָא לְרֵיחָא וּלְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana, son of Rav Ḥinnana the Elder, teaches:** In the case of **bread that one baked** together **with roasting** meat **in the oven, it is prohibited to eat** the bread **with *kutaḥ*,** which contains milk, because the bread absorbs some of the meat's aroma. The Gemara relates: There was **a certain fish that was roasted** together **with meat, Rava of Parzikiyya prohibited it from being eaten with *kutaḥ*,** due to the meat flavor absorbed in the fish. **Mar bar Rav Ashi said: Even** to merely eat it **with salt is also prohibited because** meat that is roasted or cooked with fish is **bad for odor,** meaning it causes bad breath, **and for something else,** i.e., leprosy. Therefore, one should avoid eating it due to the danger involved.

### Bread with Meat Ruling

*Tani Rav Kahana berei d'Rav Chinena Sava*: bread baked together with roasted meat in an oven — *assur l'ochla b'kutach* — may not be eaten with a dairy dip.

### Rav or Levi?

One might ask whether this ruling proves that the *halacha* follows Rav. After all, the bread is being treated as *fleishig* by virtue of having absorbed meat aroma in the oven. However, it is equally possible that even Levi would agree that *lechatchila* one should not eat such bread with dairy — not because *reicha* has created a genuine *basar b'chalav* concern, but as a precautionary rabbinic measure. And if we rule like Levi, then *b'di'eved* — after the fact — if such bread were mixed with dairy, it would not be forbidden. The ruling of Rav Kahana does not conclusively decide the question.

### Fish with Meat Ruling

A parallel case: *ha'i binisa d'itvava b'hadai bisra* — a fish that was roasted together with meat in the same oven. *Assar Rava mifarzika l'meichla b'kutcha* — Rava from Farzika forbade eating that fish with a dairy dip, again treating the fish as having absorbed meat aroma. The same ambiguity applies: whether this reflects ruling like Rav on *reicha*, or whether even Levi would agree to a *lechatchila* stringency, remains unresolved.

### Health Concern Reason

*Mar bar Rav Ashi amar: afilu b'melcha nami assur* — Mar bar Rav Ashi goes further: such fish — and here he is speaking of fish and meat combined — may not even be eaten with salt alone, meaning they may not be eaten together at all. *Mishum d'kashya l'reicha v'davar acher* — because it causes bad breath and leprosy. According to this view, the prohibition of fish and meat together is rooted not in *reicha* or *ta'am* considerations, but in a health concern.

## Rambam / Shulchan Aruch Position

### Rambam on Health

Rambam never codifies the prohibition of eating fish and meat together. His general approach was that medical and health-related guidance embedded in the *gemara* reflects the medical knowledge of its time, and does not carry the force of halachic ruling. Accordingly, the Rambam did not treat this as an *issur*.

### Shulchan Aruch Issur

Nevertheless, the *gemara's* language — *assei*, he forbade — is that of a prohibition, and the *Shulchan Aruch* does rule it as an *issur*, regardless of the underlying rationale. The health origin of the prohibition does not prevent it from being codified as a halachic rule.

### Extended Practice

The practical stringency extends considerably beyond what the *gemara* itself describes. The *gemara* addresses fish roasted together with meat in the same oven. Our practice, however, is to avoid eating cold fish and cold meat together — chewing them simultaneously even when cooked separately. This is a significant extension of the *gemara's* ruling, though it has become normative practice.

## Modern Oven Applications

### Unresolved Ruling

The *sugya* leaves unresolved which position governs as a matter of practical *psak*: *ila'ah gavar* (Rav) or *tata'ah gavar* (Shmuel), and similarly whether *reicha milsa hi* (Rav) or *reicha lav milta hi* (Levi). Both debates have support in both directions from the sources discussed.

### Shtei K'deirot Exception

Regardless of how the *ila'ah/tata'ah* debate is resolved, the *sugya* has established that the case of *ke-ein shtei kedeiros* — two separate covered arrangements — eliminates the *reicha* concern even according to Rav. If the substances are covered so that steam cannot escape and mingle, there is no transfer of aroma and no prohibition on that ground.

### Wrapping Practice

This is the basis for the practice of wrapping *kosher* airline food. The *kosher* meal can be heated in the same oven as non-*kosher* food, provided it is wrapped. There is no *halacha* that requires double-wrapping as such; the reason double-wrapping is sometimes used is purely practical — if the outer wrap is torn or compromised, the inner wrap still protects. The *halacha* requires one layer of coverage sufficient to prevent escape of steam and aroma.

### Ventilation Leniency

*Tosafot* extends the *shtei kedeiros* principle further. If the oven is a large, well-ventilated space where aromas dissipate and do not concentrate between items, that too may suffice to eliminate the *reicha* concern — even without explicit covering. The logic is that the *reicha* prohibition depends on the actual concentration and transfer of aroma; in a sufficiently airy space, the conditions for transfer do not obtain.

### Rabbeinu Tam Modern Oven

*Tosafot* cites *Rabbeinu Tam* as ruling in accordance with Rav on *reicha* — and yet permitting the baking of bread together with *fleishig* food in large ovens, *ho'il mechusos heitev havei k'shnei kedeiros*, since covered items are treated as if in separate vessels. *Tosafot* adds that even in a case without covering, *ein nira l'rabbi she'yehei assur she'yeish avir merubeh b'tanur v'hahevel mitpashet* — if the oven has abundant airflow and the vapors disperse widely, it does not appear that there would be a prohibition. This is the halachic underpinning for relying on coverage and ventilation when cooking different categories of food in close proximity — a question that extends beyond *reicha* to the broader issue of cooking *kosher* and non-*kosher* items sequentially in the same oven, a topic treated at length in *Yoreh De'ah*.
