# Pesachim 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Geula Blessing Tense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוֹתֵם בִּגְאוּלָּה. אָמַר רָבָא: קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וְהַלֵּל — ״גָּאַל יִשְׂרָאֵל״. דִּצְלוֹתָא — ״גּוֹאֵל יִשְׂרָאֵל״. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּרַחֲמֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the mishna stated that **one concludes** this section of *hallel* **with** a blessing that refers **to redemption.** With regard to the dispute over how to conclude the blessing, **Rava said:** For **the recitation of *Shema* and *hallel*** on Passover, the wording of the final blessing is: **Who redeemed Israel,** in the past tense, whereas the seventh blessing of the weekday *Amida* prayer **concludes with: Who redeems Israel,** in the present tense. **What is the reason** for this difference? Prayer **is a** supplication for mercy and therefore one mentions and requests the anticipated redemption in his prayers.

### Ga'al vs. Go'el

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: דְּקִידּוּשָׁא — ״אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתָיו וְצִוָּנוּ״. דִּצְלוֹתָא — ״קַדְּשֵׁנוּ בְּמִצְוֹתֶיךָ״. מַאי טַעְמָא — דְּרַחֲמֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rabbi Zeira said:** The formula **of *kiddush*** is: **Who sanctified us with His mitzvot and commanded us,** in the past tense. In contrast, the formula in the *Amida* **prayer** is: **Sanctify us with Your mitzvot,** in the future tense. **What is the reason** for this difference? Prayer **is a** supplication for mercy, and one submits a request for the future.

The *gemara* now turns not to the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon, but to a different question entirely: whether the concluding *chatima* should read *ga'al Yisrael* — in the past tense — or *go'el Yisrael* — in the present.

*Amar Rava*: the *beracha* concluding *Krias Shema* before *Shemoneh Esrei*, and the corresponding *beracha* at the conclusion of *Hallel* in the *Haggadah* — that is, the blessing at the end of the first two *prakim* of *Hallel* recited during *maggid* — both employ *ga'al Yisrael*, a reference to the past redemption from Egypt. By contrast, the *tzelusa* — the *Shemoneh Esrei* — uses *go'el Yisrael*. Indeed, all of the *berachos* of *Shemoneh Esrei* are framed in the present.

### Participial Form

It is worth noting that in biblical Hebrew the present-tense form is in fact a participial construction, and one could argue that Hebrew has no true present tense at all. When one says *ani shomer*, it can mean either "I am a guardian" — a noun — or "I am watching" — a verb in action. The participial form of *Shemoneh Esrei* thus functions as an ongoing descriptor: when we say *baruch atah Hashem rofei cholei amo Yisrael*, we are not merely recalling a past act of healing but affirming God as the one who continuously heals the sick of Israel — describing an active, ongoing relationship.

### Rachamei Concept

*Mai ta'ama d'rachamei ninhu* — the reason is that the *Shemoneh Esrei* is characterized as *rachamei*, a technical term the *gemara* employs in discussing *tefillah*, denoting the *bakasha* element, the quality of petition and request. Because *Shemoneh Esrei* is fundamentally an act of supplication directed toward the present and future, its formulations are cast accordingly in the present. *Amar Rabbi Zeira*: similarly, in *Kiddush* one says *asher kideshanu b'mitzvosav v'ratza vanu* — You have sanctified us in Your commandments in the past — whereas in *Shemoneh Esrei* one says *kadshenu b'mitzvossecha*, a petition: please sanctify us. The same principle applies: *mai ta'ama d'rachamei ninhu* — because of the *bakasha* dimension of *tefillah*.

## Kiddush Mention

### Exodus in Kiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: וְצָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּזְכִּיר יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם בְּקִידּוּשׁ הַיּוֹם. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״לְמַעַן תִּזְכּוֹר אֶת יוֹם״. וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״זָכוֹר אֶת יוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת לְקַדְּשׁוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: And one must mention the exodus from Egypt in the *kiddush*** of Shabbat day, despite the fact that Shabbat is not directly connected to the Exodus. The proof is that **here,** with regard to Passover, **it is written: "That you may remember the day** when you came out of the land of Egypt all the days of your life" (Deuteronomy 16:3); **and it is written there,** with regard to Shabbat: **"Remember the Shabbat day to sanctify it"** (Exodus 20:8). By means of a verbal analogy of the word "day," these verses teach that one must also recall the Exodus on Shabbat.

*Amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov*: one is required to mention *Yetzias Mitzrayim* in *Kiddush*. This ruling requires justification, since one might have thought that *Kiddush* — at least on *Shabbos* — is thematically about *Ma'aseh Bereishis*, creation, and has no inherent connection to the exodus. The question is all the more pointed because there are two accounts of the *Aseres Hadibros* in the *Torah*, each giving a different rationale for *Shabbos*.

### Two Pesukim

The *gemara* supports this ruling by juxtaposing two verses. *Ketiv hacha*: *lema'an tizkor et yom tzetcha me'eretz mitzrayim kol yemei chayecha* — so that you shall remember the day of your departure from Egypt all the days of your life. *Uchetiv hatam*: *zachor et yom hashabbat lekadesho* — remember the Sabbath day to sanctify it. The *gemara* brings these two *pesukim* together to derive that the obligation of remembrance applies to both, and they are to be woven together in *Kiddush*. This is a striking derivation, because one might more naturally have derived the connection from the explicit verse in *Devarim* that ties *Shabbos* to the experience of servitude in Egypt — *vezacharta ki eved hayita b'Eretz Mitzrayim* — which connects the freedom of *Shabbos* directly to the memory of liberation.

### Gematria Atbash

*Tosafos* notes that the *Torah* establishes *zecher l'Yetziat Mitzrayim* in *Kiddush* on the basis of *lema'an tizkor*, and quotes a teaching — attributed to the *Maharam* of Rothenburg — that works through a more elaborate path. The word *befarech*, the crushing labor imposed upon Israel in Egypt, yields through *Atbash* letter substitution the letters that form the word *v'gal*, numerically equivalent to thirty-nine, corresponding to the thirty-nine *melachot* forbidden on *Shabbos*. The inference drawn is that when Israel was redeemed from Egypt, God removed from them the burden of those thirty-nine labors and granted them *Shabbos* — the right to rest from that very work. This is a more elaborate encoding of what the *Torah* itself already says: that you were a slave and you are now free, and *Shabbos* embodies that freedom. One could verify whether it is specifically the *Maharam* of Rothenburg being cited, as it is a somewhat late attribution for *Tosafos*.

### Beyond Shabbat

The *Rashbam* extends the principle: the obligation to mention *Yetzias Mitzrayim* applies not only to *Shabbos* but *bein b'kos bein b'tefillah* — both in *Kiddush* and in *Shemoneh Esrei*. He explains that *Shabbos* derives this obligation through the *gezeirah shavah* between *lema'an tizkor* (said by *Pesach*) and *zachor* (said by *Shabbos*), and that the remaining *Yamim Tovim* derive it from *Pesach* in turn. This accounts for why we say *zecher l'Yetziat Mitzrayim* in every *Kiddush* throughout the year. The case most in need of justification is *Shavuos*, whose connection to the exodus is least explicit in the *Torah* — and the *gemara*'s derivation there, through this chain of *pesukim*, is genuinely unusual. One might suggest that the *gemara* wishes to underscore not merely the thematic association but a formal textual obligation to remember the exodus as part of *Kiddush* itself, grounded in the verse *lema'an tizkor*.

## Sanctification Forms

### Kiddush Formulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא: דִּצְלוֹתָא — ״מַצְמִיחַ קֶרֶן יְשׁוּעָה״, דְּאַפְטָרְתָּא — ״מָגֵן דָּוִד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the formulas of other prayers. **Rabba bar Sheila said:** The **prayer** that describes the future restoration of the kingship of Israel concludes with: **He Who causes the horn of salvation to flourish,** while the blessing recited **after the *haftara*,** the portion read from the Prophets, concludes with: **Shield of David.**

*Amar Rava bar Shila*: in *Shemoneh Esrei* one says *matzmiach keren yeshuah* — God causes the horn of salvation to sprout forth, expressed in the present — while in the concluding *beracha* of the *Haftarah* one says *Magen David*. Both locutions pertain to David, yet they differ.

### Redemption Language

The question naturally arises: why not say *Magen David* in *Shemoneh Esrei* as well, since *magen* appears in the present tense, just as we say *Magen Avraham*? The *Rashbam* explains, citing *Masechet Soferim* which records the *berachos* of the *Haftarah*: in *tefillah*, *matzmiach keren yeshuah* is more appropriate because the *Shemoneh Esrei* is *rachamei* — petition — and we are asking that God cause the horn of salvation to flourish for Israel. *Matzmiach keren yeshuah* looks forward to a future flowering of redemption, while *Magen David* — even grammatically present — is a more static concept, a descriptor of past protection rather than an aspiration toward future renewal. A second explanation attributed to *Rabbeinu Shlomo* — presumably Rashi — is that since we have already said *Magen Avraham*, having a second *beracha* ending with *magen* would be an undesirable redundancy; *trei chatimos k'hadadi lo shayach b'tzalla*. Both explanations can coexist.

### Haftarah Blessings

This raises the broader discussion of why we associate God's protection of David specifically with the phrase *Magen David* in the *Haftarah* blessing, which the *gemara* will address in the following section.

## Patriarchal Names

### Magen David

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְעָשִׂיתִי לְךָ שֵׁם גָּדוֹל כְּשֵׁם הַגְּדוֹלִים״, תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: זֶהוּ שֶׁאוֹמְרִים ״מָגֵן דָּוִד״.
>
> **Translation:** Incidentally, the Gemara cites the promise God issued to David through Nathan the Prophet: **"And I will make you a great name, like the names of the great ones** in the earth" (II Samuel 7:9). **Rav Yosef teaches: This is** the meaning of the phrase "like the names of the great ones," that Jews will **say: Shield of David,** just as they say: Shield of Avraham.

*Tani Rav Yosef*: the verse states *ve'asiti lecha shem gadol keshem hagedolim* — I will make for you a great name like the great ones. The *gemara* identifies this as the basis for saying *Magen David*, because God's promise to David parallels God's promise to Avraham. Who are *keshem hagedolim*? That refers to *Magen Avraham*. And indeed, God said to Avraham *ve'esecha l'goy gadol v'agadlah shemecha* — I will make you into a great nation and I will make your name great. David thus receives the same status, anchored in the same language of greatness.

### Three Patriarchs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: ״וְאֶעֶשְׂךָ לְגוֹי גָּדוֹל״, זֶהוּ שֶׁאוֹמְרִים ״אֱלֹהֵי אַבְרָהָם״. ״וַאֲבָרֶכְךָ״, זֶהוּ שֶׁאוֹמְרִים ״אֱלֹהֵי יִצְחָק״. ״וַאֲגַדְּלָה שְׁמֶךָ״, זֶהוּ שֶׁאוֹמְרִים ״אֱלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said** with regard to God's blessing of Avraham: "And I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you, and I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing" (Genesis 12:2). **"And I will make of you a great nation"; this is** fulfilled in the opening of the first blessing of the *Amida*, **as** Jews **say: God of Abraham. "And I will bless you"; this is** fulfilled when **they say: God of Isaac,** as it is a blessing for a father when the name of his son is eternalized. **"And I will make your name great"; this is** fulfilled **when they say: God of Jacob.**

*Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish*: the verse *ve'esecha l'goy gadol* corresponds to what we say *Elohei Avraham*; *u'varechecha* corresponds to *Elohei Yitzchak*; *u'gadlah shemecha* corresponds to *Elohei Yaakov*.

### Fulfilled Promises

The *Maharsha* explains how these correspondences work. *U'varechecha* is realized through Yitzchak, because immediately after Avraham's death the *Torah* says *vayivarech Elohim et Yitzchak beno* — God blessed Yitzchak his son. Thus the fulfillment of God's promise that *u'varechecha* is seen played out in Yitzchak's life. We therefore say *Elohei Yitzchak* because the truth of God's promise became visible in Yitzchak. The connection to Yaakov is made in a somewhat different manner, but the overarching principle is consistent: we associate God's name with each patriarch because God's promises to them were fulfilled, and those fulfilled promises are what we invoke.

### Avraham Singular

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהוּ חוֹתְמִין בְּכוּלָּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וֶהְיֵה בְּרָכָה״. בְּךָ חוֹתְמִין, וְאֵין חוֹתְמִין בְּכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that Jews should **conclude** the first blessing of the *Amida* prayer **with** the names of **all** the forefathers; therefore **the verse states: "And you will be a blessing"** i.e., **with you,** Avraham, **they** will **conclude** the blessing, **and they** will **not conclude with** a mention of **all** of the forefathers. This is why the first blessing of the *Amida* prayer ends: Shield of Avraham.

*Yachol yachatmu bechulam* — one might have thought that the concluding *beracha* of *Shemoneh Esrei* should enumerate all three patriarchs: *baruch atah Hashem Magen Avraham Yitzchak v'Yaakov*. The *gemara* answers from the verse *v'heyeh berachah* — and you shall be a blessing — in the singular, not *v'hayu*. *Becha chotmin v'ein chotmin bechulan*: in you, Avraham, we conclude, and we do not conclude with all of them. The emphasis at the end is upon Avraham, because his is the beginning of the relationship. That is *Magen Avraham*, with the singular focus on Avraham.

## Shabbat vs. Yom Tov

### Pumbedita Elders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינָא לְסָבֵי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: בְּשַׁבְּתָא — בֵּין בִּצְלוֹתָא בֵּין בְּקִידּוּשָׁא: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ הַשַּׁבָּת״. בְּיוֹמָא טָבָא — בֵּין בִּצְלוֹתָא וּבֵין בְּקִידּוּשָׁא: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהַזְּמַנִּים״. וְאָמֵינָא לְהוּ אֲנָא: אַדְּרַבָּה, דִּצְלוֹתָא — בֵּין בְּשַׁבְּתָא בֵּין בְּיוֹמָא טָבָא: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל״, בְּקִידּוּשָׁא דְשַׁבְּתָא: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ הַשַּׁבָּת״, בְּיוֹמָא טָבָא: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהַזְּמַנִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: I found the Elders of Pumbedita sitting and saying: On Shabbat, both in prayer and in *kiddush*,** one recites: **Who sanctifies Shabbat. On a Festival, both in prayer and in *kiddush*** one recites: **Who sanctifies Israel and the seasons. And I said to them: On the contrary,** in **prayer, both on Shabbat and on a Festival,** one should recite: **Who sanctifies Israel.** However, **in the *kiddush* of Shabbat** one should recite: **Who sanctifies Shabbat,** whereas in the *kiddush* **of a Festival** one should recite: **Who sanctifies Israel and the seasons.**

*Amar Rava ashchechihu l'savei d'Pumbedita d'yatvi v'ka'amri*: Rava recounts that he found the elders of Pumbedita sitting and saying: on *Shabbos*, whether in *Shemoneh Esrei* or in *Kiddush*, one says *Mekadesh HaShabbat*; on *Yom Tov*, whether in *Shemoneh Esrei* or in *Kiddush*, one says *Mekadesh Yisrael v'hazmanim*. That is, the distinction follows the day — *Shabbos* versus *Yom Tov* — and applies uniformly across both *tefillah* and *Kiddush*.

### Rava's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא טַעְמָא דִידִי וְטַעְמָא דִידְכוּ. טַעְמָא דִידְכוּ: שַׁבָּת, דִּקְבִיעָא וְקַיְימָא — בֵּין בִּצְלוֹתָא וּבְקִידּוּשָׁא: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ הַשַּׁבָּת״. יוֹמָא טָבָא, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא דְּקָבְעִי לֵיהּ — דְּקָמְעַבְּרִי יַרְחֵי וְקָבְעִי לְשָׁנֵי: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהַזְּמַנִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava further said to the Elders of Pumbedita: **And I** can **say my reason and your reason. Your reason** is that since **Shabbat is established and permanent,** i.e., it always occurs on the seventh day of the week, **both in prayers and in *kiddush*** one should recite: **Who sanctifies Shabbat.** It is not necessary for Israel to sanctify Shabbat, as it is permanently sanctified by God. Conversely, with regard to a **Festival, as it is Israel who establishes it, as** the Sages **add** extra days to certain **months and establish years** by intercalating them, one recites: **Who sanctifies Israel and the seasons.** This is Rava's explanation of the reason for the ruling of the Elders of Pumbedita.

Rava responded to the elders: *adarava* — on the contrary. The distinction ought to be based not on the day but on the context — *Shemoneh Esrei* versus *Kiddush*. In *Shemoneh Esrei*, whether *Shabbos* or *Yom Tov*, one should always say *Mekadesh Yisrael*. In *Kiddush*, on *Shabbos* one says *Mekadesh HaShabbat*, and on *Yom Tov* one says *Mekadesh Yisrael v'hazmanim*. So Rava maintains the distinction between *Mekadesh HaShabbat* and *Mekadesh Yisrael v'hazmanim* — but only in *Kiddush*, not in *Shemoneh Esrei*.

### Intrinsic Nature

The *gemara* then presents both rationales. *Ta'ama did'hu* — the reasoning of the Pumbedita elders: *Shabbat d'kevi'a v'kayama* — *Shabbos* is fixed and stands on its own; its *kedushah* is inherent and independent of any human role. Therefore, whether in *Shemoneh Esrei* or in *Kiddush*, one says *Mekadesh HaShabbat*, because God is the one who sanctifies the *Shabbos* without the involvement of Israel. On *Yom Tov*, by contrast, *d'Yisrael hu d'kavei leih* — it is Israel that establishes it. Israel determines whether a month is twenty-nine or thirty days, fixes the calendar, and intercalates the year. Through Israel's role the *kedushah* of the times flows. Hence: *Mekadesh Yisrael v'hazmanim* — God sanctifies Israel, and through Israel's partnership, the sacred times are established.

### Community Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דִידִי: צְלוֹתָא, דִּבְרַבִּים אִיתָא: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל״. קִידּוּשׁ, דִּבְיָחִיד אִיתָא, בְּשַׁבָּת: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ הַשַּׁבָּת״, בְּיוֹם טוֹב: ״מְקַדֵּשׁ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְהַזְּמַנִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **My reason is** that in the case of **prayer, which is in public,** one recites: **Who sanctifies Israel,** in honor of the community. Conversely, for ***kiddush*, which is** recited by an individual **alone on Shabbat,** one says: **Who sanctifies Shabbat,** as Israel does not sanctify Shabbat. **On a Festival** one recites: **Who sanctifies Israel and the seasons.** In this case, Israel is mentioned, as its Sages sanctify the Festivals.

*Ta'ama didi* — Rava's own reasoning: *tzeluta d'bei rabim* — *Shemoneh Esrei* is, in its primary form, a communal prayer, said in the synagogue. When the community gathers in the *shul* on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, one wants to acknowledge that gathering by saying *Mekadesh Yisrael*, recognizing the *kedushah* of the people Israel assembled together. *Kiddusha d'bei chavi* — *Kiddush*, by contrast, takes place in the home, in a private setting. Therefore the distinction in *Kiddush* remains between *Mekadesh HaShabbat* for *Shabbos* and *Mekadesh Yisrael v'hazmanim* for *Yom Tov*.

One might deepen Rava's reasoning: it is not merely that the congregation is assembled, but that the assembly itself provides the occasion to affirm the covenant — *ki ot hi*, the *ot* and the *bris* between God and Israel — and therefore to emphasize *Mekadesh Yisrael*, the role of Israel in relation to *Shabbos* as well. Even though *Shabbat d'kevi'a v'kayama*, Israel is the party commanded to observe it, and that *bris* is what the communal *tefillah* seeks to give voice to.

### Individual Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, צְלוֹתָא בְּיָחִיד מִי לֵיתֵיהּ? וְקִידּוּשָׁא בְּרַבִּים מִי לֵיתֵיהּ? וְרָבָא סָבַר: זִיל בָּתַר עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Rava's reason: **And that is not so. Is there not** also the **prayer** recited **by** a person who is **alone; and is there not** also kiddush **in public?** The above distinction is rendered meaningless in practice. **But Rava maintains: Follow the main** practice of each mitzva. Prayer is primarily a communal activity, whereas *kiddush* is fundamentally the obligation of each individual.

The *gemara* rejects Rava's distinction: *v'lo hi* — this argument does not hold. *Tzeluta b'yachid mi leita?* — does one not pray *Shemoneh Esrei* privately? *Kiddusha b'rabim mi leita?* — is *Kiddush* never recited communally? Since both *Shemoneh Esrei* and *Kiddush* can occur in either context, the distinction between public and private cannot be the operative difference. The *gemara* therefore reasserts that Rava's view is that one goes after the *ikar* — the primary context of each.

### Practical Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא בַּר רַב נְחֵית קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר כְּסָבֵי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי. אַלְמָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ רָבָא. רַב נָתָן אֲבוּהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן נְחֵית קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, אֲמַר כְּסָבֵי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, וְשַׁבְּחֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reports: **Ulla bar Rav descended** to lead the prayer service **before Rava. He said** the formula **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Elders of Pumbedita, and** Rava **did not say anything to him. Apparently, Rava retracted** his opinion and accepted the formula of the Elders of Pumbedita. Likewise, the Gemara relates: **Rav Natan, father of Rav Huna, son** of **Rav Natan, descended** to lead the prayer service **before Rav Pappa** and **recited** the liturgy **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Elders of Pumbedita,** and **Rav Pappa praised him** for his correct recitation.

*Ulla bar Rav nachit kamei d'Rava* — Ulla bar Rav went down to lead the *tefillah* before Rava as *shaliach tzibbur* (the term *nachit* reflects the ancient practice of praying from a lower position, calling out from the depths). He said the formula of the elders of Pumbedita — that is, in *Shemoneh Esrei* he said *Mekadesh HaShabbat* or *Mekadesh Yisrael v'hazmanim* depending on the day, rather than uniformly *Mekadesh Yisrael*. *V'lo amar lei v'lo midi* — Rava said nothing to him. *Alma hadar bei Rava* — this indicates that Rava himself reversed his position. Subsequently, Rav Natan the father of Rav Huna son of Rav Natan went down before Rav Papa and likewise said as the elders of Pumbedita. *V'shabchei Rav Papa* — Rav Papa praised him.

### Marimar Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אֲנָא אִיקְּלַעִי לְסוּרָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּמָרִימָר, וּנְחֵית קַמֵּיהּ שְׁלוּחָא דְצִיבּוּרָא וַאֲמַר כְּסָבֵי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, וַהֲווֹ מְשַׁתְּקִי לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: שִׁבְקוּהוּ הִילְכְתָא כְּסָבֵי דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא, וְלָא הֲווֹ מְשַׁתְּקוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: I happened** to come **to Sura before Mareimar, and the prayer leader descended before him and recited** the liturgy **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Elders of Pumbedita, and everyone** tried to **silence him,** as they had never heard that version of the prayer before. Mareimar **said to them: Leave him,** as **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of the Elders of Pumbedita. And** the people in attendance listened to him and **no** longer tried to **silence** the prayer leader, but allowed him to complete the prayer.

*Amar Ravina*: I came to Sura before Mareimar, and the *shaliach tzibbur* prayed according to the elders of Pumbedita. The assembled congregation tried to silence him — evidently expecting the uniform *Mekadesh Yisrael* of Rava's original position — but Mareimar said to them: *shavkuya* — leave him alone. *Hilchasa k'savei d'Pumbedita* — the *halakha* follows the elders of Pumbedita. At that, the congregation ceased its objections. An alternative *girsa* in the margin reads that it is the anonymous editorial voice of the *gemara* that declares *v'hilchasa k'savei d'Pumbedita*.

## Liturgical Variance

### Nuschaot Variance

What this entire discussion reveals — and it is important always to bear in mind when studying debates about liturgy — is how much these *beit midrash* disputes reflect actual divergent practices that existed in the communities. We see the same phenomenon elsewhere in our *gemara*, for example in the discussion around *Agosi* and how *Chazal* actually conducted themselves. Here, the people in the synagogue clearly thought the *shaliach tzibbur* was making an error — they tried to silence him — yet he was following a recognized tradition. The relationship between what was taught in the *beit midrash* and what communities actually practiced was clearly bidirectional, not simply top-down.

### Text Crystallization

This discussion also raises a more fundamental question: when exactly was the text of *Shemoneh Esrei* crystallized and finalized? The basic structure is attributed to *Anshei Knesses Hagedolah*, but the *gemara* itself — and related sources in the *Tosefta* — reflects an awareness of certain textual flux operating within defined parameters. The absolute solidification of the text, word for word, was a process that extended over time.

### Variants Parameters

There is, however, an important distinction to maintain: on one side, there are texts that represent acceptable variant *nuschaot* — formulations that circulated during a period before the text fully crystallized, each representing a legitimate tradition. On the other side, there is the question of unauthorized insertions — additions to the *tefillah* beyond its sanctioned structure. The *gemara* does speak to this: one may not make insertions into the first three *berachos*, while additions in the middle *berachos*, depending on their nature, may be permissible. These are two separate questions: the legitimacy of parallel textual traditions that predate full crystallization, and the prohibition against arbitrary personal additions.

### Oseh Hashalom

A striking case study is the practice of saying *Oseh Hashalom* at the conclusion of *Shemoneh Esrei* during the *Yamim Noraim* instead of the standard *Ha-mevarech et ammo Yisrael bashalom*. Rav Soloveitchik was strongly opposed to this practice, on the grounds that the *gemara* gives no indication that the *beracha* should be changed for the *Aseres Yemei Teshuvah*, and the *Geonim*, even when they inserted additions like *Zachreinu*, did not alter the text of the *berachah* itself. Yet fragments from the *Geniza* show that *Oseh Hashalom* was in fact a parallel text used during the year — not just during the *Yamim Noraim* — which suggests it was an authentic alternative *nusach* rather than an innovation. What apparently happened is that this alternative formulation came to be endorsed specifically for the *Yamim Noraim*. This illustrates precisely the distinction described above: a legitimate parallel text that predated full crystallization is categorically different from a new insertion. Even so, the practical reality is that even today there are differences in this matter between Artscroll and Koren siddurim, and between various communities that nominally follow the same *nusach*.

## Third Fourth Cups

### Cup Pouring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָזְגוּ לוֹ כּוֹס שְׁלִישִׁי מְבָרֵךְ עַל מְזוֹנוֹ. רְבִיעִי גּוֹמֵר עָלָיו אֶת הַלֵּל, וְאוֹמֵר עָלָיו בִּרְכַּת הַשִּׁיר. בֵּין הַכּוֹסוֹת הַלָּלוּ, אִם רוֹצֶה לִשְׁתּוֹת — יִשְׁתֶּה, בֵּין שְׁלִישִׁי לִרְבִיעִי — לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **They poured for** the leader of the seder **the third cup** of wine, **and he recites the blessing over his food,** Grace After Meals. Next, they pour him the **fourth** cup. **He completes *hallel* over it,** as he already recited the first part of *hallel* before the meal. **And he** also **recites the blessing of the song** at the end of *hallel* **over** the fourth cup. During the period **between these cups,** i.e., the first three cups established by the Sages, **if one wishes to drink** more **he** may **drink;** however, **between the third** cup **and the fourth** cup one should **not drink.**

The *mishnah* turns now to the structure of the *arba kosot*. *Mozgu lo kos shlishi* — one pours the third cup and recites *Birkat Hamazon* over it. One then pours the fourth cup, *gomer alav et ha-Hallel* — completes the *Hallel* begun after *maggid* — and says *Birkat Hashir*, the blessing of song. It is striking that the *mishnah* completely leapfrogs over the major *mitzvos* of *achilah* — *matzah*, *maror*, and *koreich* — as well as *Shulchan Orech*, and proceeds directly from the blessings of *maggid* to the third cup. The *kosos* appear to be structuring the entire rhythm of the *mishnah*'s presentation. Notably, even *afikoman* — the second eating of *matzah* after the meal — receives no mention in the *mishnah*, despite not being obvious in the way that the meal itself might be. We will return to that topic when the *gemara* addresses *ein maftirin achar hapesach afikoman*.

One might speculate that some scholars have suggested that in the world of the *mishnah*, the eating of *matzah* and *maror* actually took place much earlier in the *seder* — before *maggid* — when the *mishnah* says *hevi'u lefanav* and *hevi'u lo matzah v'chazeret v'charoset ushnei tavshilin*, and that the telling was simultaneous with the eating. This would explain why the *mishnah* does not separately mark the moment of eating.

### Inter-Cup Drinking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָנָן לְרָבָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן טְעוּנָה כּוֹס. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַרְבַּע כָּסֵי תִּיקְּנוּ רַבָּנַן דֶּרֶךְ חֵירוּת, כֹּל חַד וְחַד נַעֲבֵיד בֵּיהּ מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ran Ḥanan said to Rava:** Since the mishna states that Grace After Meals must be recited over the third cup, **learn from it** that Grace After Meals **requires a cup** of wine. Rava **said to him:** This is no proof, for although **the Sages instituted** the drinking of **four cups in the manner of freedom,** once the four cups are in place, with **each and every one** of them **we will perform a mitzva,** despite the fact that they were not originally instituted for this purpose. After the Sages instituted these four cups, they attached a special mitzva to each one. However, this does not prove that there is an obligation to recite Grace After Meals over a cup of wine during the rest of the year.

*Bein hakosos hallalu* — between the earlier cups, that is between the first and second and between the second and third, *im rotze lishtot yishte* — one may drink additionally if one wishes. *Bein shlishi l'revi'i lo yishte* — between the third and fourth cups one may not drink. The *Rashbam* quotes the *Yerushalmi*: drinking after the meal, as opposed to during it, leads to intoxication. Additionally, some suggest the concern is about washing away the taste of the *matzah* — a consideration that becomes relevant once one has eaten the *matzah* and *afikoman* and the meal has concluded. We will develop this point further in the discussion of *ein maftirin achar hapesach afikoman*. *Tosafos* notes that the restriction between the third and fourth cups applies specifically to wine; water would be permissible even according to those who give the reason as protecting the taste of *matzah*, since water does not eliminate that taste.

### Hamazon Cup

*Amar lei Rav Chana le-Rava*: since the *mishnah* specifies that the third cup is poured for *Birkat Hamazon*, one might infer that *Birkat Hamazon* generally requires a *kos* — a cup of wine. *Amar lei*: this does not necessarily follow. *Arba kasei tiknu rabanan derech cheirut* — the rabbis instituted four cups as an expression of *cheirus*, freedom. *Kol chad v'chad na'avid bei mitzvah* — they wanted each cup to be associated with a *mitzvah*, so that one was not simply drinking without ritual purpose. They therefore designated the third cup for *Birkat Hamazon*. This does not demonstrate that *Birkat Hamazon* in general requires a cup; it demonstrates rather that on *leil ha-seder*, where cups were already mandated, they were each assigned to a *mitzvah*. This *gemara* also makes clear that the four cups are specifically four — *davka* — and no more, since the entire rationale is to assign each cup to a *mitzvah* within the *seder*.

## Hallel Completion

### Hallel End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בִּרְכַּת הַשִּׁיר״? רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: ״יְהַלְלוּךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ״. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״נִשְׁמַת כָּל חַי״. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רְבִיעִי גּוֹמֵר עָלָיו אֶת הַהַלֵּל, וְאוֹמֵר הַלֵּל הַגָּדוֹל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: ״ה׳ רוֹעִי לֹא אֶחְסָר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the blessing of the song** mentioned in the mishna? **Rav Yehuda said:** It is the blessing that begins with: **They shall praise You, Lord, our God. And Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that one also recites: **The breath of all living,** a prayer that follows the verses of praise [*pesukei dezimra*]. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the **fourth** cup, **one completes *hallel* over it and recites the great *hallel*;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. And some say** that one recites: **"The Lord is my shepherd, I shall not want"** (Psalms 23:1), in appreciation of the food he ate at the meal.

*Revi'i omer alav gomer alav et ha-Hallel* — on the fourth cup one completes the *Hallel* begun during *maggid*, and *omer alav Birchat Hashir* — one recites the blessing of song at its conclusion.

### Blessing Debate

*Mai Birchat Hashir?* — what is this blessing? *Rabbi Yehuda omer Yehallelucha Hashem Elokeinu* — the standard concluding *beracha* of *Hallel*, which reads *Yehallelucha Hashem Elokeinu kol ma'asecha vachasidecha tzaddikim osei ritzonecha*, concluding with *baruch atah Hashem melech mehullal batishbachos*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *Birchat Hashir* is simply the normal concluding blessing of *Hallel*. *Rabbi Yochanan omer*: it is *Nishmas Kol Chai*, the extended passage of praise familiar from *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov Pesukei D'Zimra*, which concludes with *Yishtabach* — *El melech gadol mehullal batishbachos, El hahoda'os, adon hanifla'os, habocher b'shirei zimrah, melech El chei ha'olamim* — also concluding with *batishbachos*.

### Internal Impetus

The *rishonim* raise a pointed question: why does the *mishnah* need to specify that one ends *Hallel* with a *beracha*? Surely, if one recites *Hallel*, one ends with the standard concluding blessing, just as one always does. The answer lies in the unique nature of how *Hallel* was introduced during *maggid*. The *Haggadah* frames the beginning of *Hallel* with the language of *lefichach anachnu chayyavim* — therefore we are obligated — and *chayyav liros et atzmo* — one is obligated to see oneself as if one personally left Egypt. The *Hallel* begins not from a formal external obligation marked by a blessing of *asher kiddeshanu*, but from this internal impetus — the person is living the experience of *geulah*, and the praise flows from within. For this reason, the *mishnah* never specifies that *Hallel* begins with a *beracha*, and indeed, this is our practice: the first half of *Hallel* in the *Haggadah* begins without a formal opening blessing.

---

## Hallel Completion (II)

### Ending Formula

The explicit statement that one says a blessing at the end of *Hallel* therefore serves a purpose: to tell us that despite the unconventional opening — without a *beracha* — there is nonetheless a proper concluding *beracha*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, that is the standard *Hallel* closing; according to Rabbi Yochanan, it is *Nishmas Kol Chai* leading into *Yishtabach*.

### Why Explicit

One proof that *Hallel* does not begin with a *beracha* on *leil ha-seder* derives precisely from the fact that the *mishnah* specifies the concluding blessing: if *Hallel* had begun with a *beracha* in the ordinary way, it would go without saying that it ends with one. The very need to state the ending formula demonstrates the unusual structure of the opening.

### Begin Without

This is consistent with our received practice: the first two *prakim* of *Hallel* during *maggid* — *Betzeit Yisrael mi-Mitzrayim* and *Lo Lanu* — are recited without a preceding blessing of *asher kiddeshanu*, because they emerge from the experiential moment of reliving the *geulah*.

### Ritual Transition

By the time we reach the conclusion of *Hallel* after the meal, however, the dynamic has shifted. We have eaten, the *afikoman* has been consumed, and we are no longer in that immediate experiential moment of *geulah*. We have recited *asher ga'al Yisrael* — the *geulah* was framed in the past — and we have moved into a more retrospective and ritual mode. In this mode, it becomes possible and appropriate to conclude with a formal blessing, *melech mehullal batishbachos*.

---

## Nishmat Extension

### Nishmat Alt

According to Rabbi Yochanan, the concluding blessing is *Nishmas Kol Chai*, not the standard *Hallel* ending. This view holds that in addition to or in place of the standard *Yehallelucha* closing, one recites the extended passage of *Nishmas* and concludes with *Yishtabach*.

### Dual Endings

The *Rashbam* understands Rabbi Yochanan not as excluding Rabbi Yehuda's view but as supplementing it: one ends *Hallel* with the standard *melech mehullal batishbachos* of *Yehallelucha*, then recites *Nishmas Kol Chai*, and concludes with *Yishtabach* ending in *melech chei ha'olamim*. On this reading, two *berachos* are recited at the conclusion — one at the end of *Hallel HaMitzri* and one at the end of *Nishmas*. Other *rishonim* disagree, holding that *Birchat Hashir* is a single blessing and the debate is between the two positions: we rule like Rabbi Yochanan, reciting *Nishmas* and ending with *Yishtabach*, and do not recite *Yehallelucha* as a separate concluding *beracha* for *Hallel*.

### Nuschaot Variance

The practical division between *Ashkenaz* and *Sefard* follows from these positions. *Ashkenazim* recite *Yehallelucha* before *Hallel HaGadol* — integrating the *Hallel* conclusion there — then recite *Nishmas* and conclude with *Yishtabach* as the single *Birchat Hashir*. *Sefardim* recite *Nishmas* first and follow it with the *Yehallelucha* passage, concluding with *melech mehullal batishbachos* as the *Birchat Hashir*.

### Minhag Variance

The result is genuine confusion in practice. At any *seder* table, people may find that their *Haggadot* direct them differently at this point. This reflects multiple layered *minhagim*, traceable directly to the terseness of the *gemara* and the divergent rulings of the *rishonim*. The presentation in the *Haggadah* under study helpfully distinguishes the two *nuschaot*: it notes that *Ashkenazim* say *Yehallelucha* at the first juncture (before *Hallel HaGadol*), while *Sefardim* say it at the later juncture after *Nishmas*.

## Hallel HaGadol

### Second Hallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהֵיכָן הַלֵּל הַגָּדוֹל? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵ״הוֹדוּ״ עַד ״נַהֲרוֹת בָּבֶל״. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: מִ״שִּׁיר הַמַּעֲלוֹת״ עַד ״נַהֲרוֹת בָּבֶל״. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: מִ״כִּי יַעֲקֹב בָּחַר לוֹ יָהּ״ עַד ״נַהֲרוֹת בָּבֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where** does the **great *hallel*** begin and where does it end? **Rabbi Yehuda says: From "Give thanks"** (Psalms 136:1) **until "The rivers of Babylon"** (Psalms 137:1). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From "A song of ascents"** (Psalms 134:1) **until "The rivers of Babylon." Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said: From "For the Lord has chosen Jacob for Himself"** (Psalms 135:4) **until "The rivers of Babylon."**

The *mishnah* and *gemara* introduce a second *Hallel* said on *leil ha-seder* after completing *Hallel HaMitzri* — *Hallel HaGadol*, the great *Hallel*, which is the passage *Hodu l'Hashem ki tov ki l'olam chasdo* that we say on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* within *Pesukei D'Zimra*.

### Alt Texts

*Divrei Rabbi Tarfon*: *Hallel HaGadol* begins from *Hodu l'Hashem ki tov*. *V'yesh omrim*: there is a view that it is *Hashem ro'i lo echsar*, the opening of *Mizmor L'David*. The *Rashbam* explains this alternative as an attempt to connect the theme of *Birkat Hamazon* — God's provision, *ta'aroch l'fanai shulchan* — to the *Hallel*, creating a bridge from the meal into praise.

### Extent Views

*Meheichan Hallel HaGadol?* — from where does *Hallel HaGadol* begin and how far does it extend? *Rav Yehuda omer*: from *Hodu* through to *Naharot Bavel* — the entirety of the *Hodu* chapter concluding with the following chapter. *Rabbi Yochanan omer*: from *Shir HaMa'alot*, two *prakim* earlier, through to *Naharot Bavel*. *Rav Acha bar Yaakov omer*: from the verse *ki Yaakov bachar lo Yah*, approximately half a *perek* before the *Hodu* chapter, through to *Naharot Bavel*. This debate directly maps onto the current liturgical practice: on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* in *Pesukei D'Zimra* we lead into *Hodu l'Hashem ki tov* with the preceding *perek* — which contains *ki Yaakov bachar lo Yah* — but do not begin from *Shir HaMa'alot*, which would be two *prakim* back. Interestingly, *Sefardim* do say *Shir HaMa'alot* at this point, reflecting Rabbi Yochanan's view.

### Greatness Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ הַלֵּל הַגָּדוֹל? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא יוֹשֵׁב בְּרוּמוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם וּמְחַלֵּק מְזוֹנוֹת לְכׇל בְּרִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why is** this section **called the great *hallel*? Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Because** this passage states that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, sits in the heights of the universe and dispenses food to every creature.** The whole world praises God for His kindness through the great *hallel*, which includes the verse: "Who gives food to all flesh" (Psalms 136:25).

*Lama nikra shemo Hallel HaGadol?* — why is it called the great *Hallel*? *Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: because *mipnei shehakadosh baruch hu yoshev berumo shel olam umachalek mezonot lechol beriyotav* — God dwells at the heights of the world and yet distributes sustenance to all creatures. This is expressed in the verse *noten lechem lechol basar ki le'olam chasdo* — He gives bread to all flesh, for His lovingkindness endures forever. While *Hallel HaMitzri* focuses on dramatic supernatural salvation, *Hallel HaGadol* encompasses God as creator, sustainer, and provider — the God of nature and of daily provision, *noten lechem lechol basar* alongside *vayotzi Yisrael mitocham*.

### 26 Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הָנֵי עֶשְׂרִים וְשִׁשָּׁה ״הוֹדוּ״, כְּנֶגֶד מִי — כְּנֶגֶד עֶשְׂרִים וְשִׁשָּׁה דּוֹרוֹת שֶׁבָּרָא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּעוֹלָמוֹ וְלֹא נָתַן לָהֶם תּוֹרָה, וְזָן אוֹתָם בְּחַסְדּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: These twenty-six** mentions of the word ***hodu*,** give praise, in this *hallel* (Psalms 136), **to what** do they **correspond?** He explains: They **correspond to the twenty-six generations that the Holy One, Blessed be He, created in His world, and** to whom He **did not give the Torah.** There were ten generations from Adam to Noah, another ten from Noah to Abraham, and six generations from Abraham to Moses and the revelation at Sinai, i.e., Isaac, Jacob, Levi, Kehat, Amram, and Moses. **And** why did these generations survive, despite the fact that they did not learn Torah or perform mitzvot? They survived only because God **sustained them through His mercy,** even though they were undeserving.

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: the *Hodu* passage contains twenty-six *haduos* — twenty-six repetitions of the refrain — corresponding to the twenty-six generations from creation until *matan Torah* at *Har Sinai*. During all those generations there was no *Torah* in the world, yet *vezanam bechasdo* — God sustained them through His *chesed*. *Noten lechem lechol basar ki le'olam chasdo* — He provided for all creatures even when they did not merit it through *Torah*. This is the extraordinary *chesed* that *Hallel HaGadol* celebrates: ongoing divine provision even before the covenant of *Torah* was established.

### Provision Theme

The fundamental distinction between the two *Hallels* is this: *Hallel HaMitzri* is about miraculous salvation — the supernatural, the dramatic, the sudden reversal of fate. *Hallel HaGadol* is about ongoing provision and the God of nature who quietly sustains all creation. On *Yamim Tovim*, when we recite formal *Hallel*, we focus on the miraculous and salvific dimension. In the *Pesukei D'Zimra* of every *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*, we celebrate God as creator and sustainer. On *leil ha-seder*, in a way that is uniquely powerful, we bridge both dimensions: we begin with *Hallel HaMitzri*, the miraculous, and conclude with *Hallel HaGadol*, the providential — a transition from the drama of *yetziat Mitzrayim* into the ongoing *chesed* of daily life.

## Divine Goodness

### Debt Repayment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״הוֹדוּ לַה׳ כִּי טוֹב״? הוֹדוּ לַה׳ שֶׁגּוֹבֶה חוֹבָתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם בְּטוֹבָתוֹ: עָשִׁיר בְּשׁוֹרוֹ, וְאֶת עָנִי בְּשֵׂיוֹ, יָתוֹם בְּבֵיצָתוֹ, אַלְמָנָה בְּתַרְנְגוֹלְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Give thanks to the Lord for He is good"** (Psalms 136:1)? It means **give thanks to the Lord who exacts one's debt,** the punishment for a person's sins and wickedness, **in** accordance with the **goodness of** each individual. God punishes each person based on his means. He punishes **a wealthy** person by taking **his ox, and** He punishes **a poor** person by means of **his sheep.** He punishes the **orphan by** taking away **his egg,** and He punishes the **widow by** means of **her chicken.** God punishes each person based on his ability to endure deprivation, and He does not punish people with more than they can handle.

*Amar Rav Chisda*: what is meant by *Hodu l'Hashem ki tov*? The verse declares God good because of the manner in which God collects what is owed to Him. *Hodu lo shegoveh chovato shel adam betovato* — give thanks to God that He collects a person's debt using what God Himself has given. That is: when a person incurs a spiritual debt and God must collect it, He takes back only something He Himself previously bestowed.

### Mercy Examples

The *gemara* elaborates: if a wealthy person sins, God takes the ox — not the person himself. *De'ani besayo* — if a poor person, the sheep. *Yatom bebetzato* — an orphan, even more impoverished, loses the egg he was to eat for breakfast. *Almana betarnagoltah* — a widow loses her chicken. In every case, what God takes back is something God had given. He does not inflict suffering directly upon the person's body or life; He reclaims only from His own prior gifts.

### Chesed Reinterpret

What is remarkable about this teaching is the interpretive move it makes under conditions of hardship. *Chazal* lived through periods of persecution and deprivation, and the plain reading of *ki l'olam chasdo* — His *chesed* endures forever — could seem to mock reality. Yet this *gemara* does not acknowledge the problem. It does not say "and despite appearances, God is good." Instead, it simply reinterprets the situation: *baruch Hashem*, you know what? The fact that all that happened was God took away my wealth rather than harming me personally — that itself is the *chesed*. You find the *chesed* precisely where it is least visible, and the affirmation is all the more powerful for being arrived at through interpretation rather than through protest.

### Chazal Affirm

This is the approach of *Chazal* throughout: a constant, unwavering affirmation of God's *chesed* even within visible suffering, not by denying the suffering but by finding, through careful reading of the situation, the dimension in which God's goodness is operating. There is also the complementary framework, of course — that debts paid in *Olam Hazeh* need not be settled in *Olam Haba* — but that is not what this *gemara* is saying. What it is doing is something more striking: it reaffirms *chesed* through the act of interpretation itself.

## Parnassa Challenge

### Twice Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קָשִׁין מְזוֹנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָדָם כִּפְלַיִם כַּיּוֹלֵדָה, דְּאִילּוּ בְּיוֹלֵדָה כְּתִיב: ״בְּעֶצֶב״, וּבִמְזוֹנוֹת כְּתִיב ״בְּעִצָּבוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the praise due to God for sustaining the world, the Gemara cites a statement that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The task of providing **a person's food is twice as difficult as** the suffering endured by **a woman in childbirth. While, with regard to a woman in childbirth, it is written: "In pain [*be'etzev*]** you shall bring forth children" (Genesis 3:16), **with regard to food, it is written: "In toil [*be'itzavon*]** you shall eat of it, all the days of your life" (Genesis 3:17). *Itzavon* is a superlative form of *etzev*, which indicates that it is more difficult to support oneself than to give birth.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *kashe mezonotav shel adam kiflayim kiyoledah* — the challenges of earning a livelihood are twice as difficult as childbirth. This is a man making the comparison, and the derivation is textual: by childbirth the *Torah* says *be'etzev teldi vanim* — with *etzev*, singular. Regarding sustenance it says *be'itzavon*, which carries the force of a more intensive or plural form, hence doubly difficult.

### Harder Geula

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: קָשִׁין מְזוֹנוֹתָיו שֶׁל אָדָם יוֹתֵר מִן הַגְּאוּלָּה, דְּאִילּוּ בִּגְאוּלָּה כְּתִיב: ״הַמַּלְאָךְ הַגּוֹאֵל אוֹתִי מִכׇּל רָע״, מַלְאָךְ בְּעָלְמָא, וְאִילּוּ בִּמְזוֹנוֹת כְּתִיב: ״הָאֱלֹהִים הָרוֹעֶה אֹתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The task of providing **a person's food is more difficult than the redemption. While, with regard to the redemption, it is written: "The angel who has redeemed me from all evil"** (Genesis 48:16), indicating that **a mere angel** is sufficient to protect a person from all evil; **whereas, with regard to sustenance, it is written: "The God who has been my shepherd** all my life long to this day" (Genesis 48:15). This verse implies that only God can help one who is struggling to earn a living.

*Ve'amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *kashe mezonotav shel adam yoter min hageulah* — earning a livelihood is harder — that is, more miraculous, more requiring of divine involvement — than redemption itself. This is not a complaint. It is rather the recognition that the ongoing miracle of *parnassah* is greater than the miracle of *geulah*.

### Angel vs. God

The textual proof is elegant: regarding redemption, the *Torah* says *hamalach hagoel oti mikol ra* — the angel who redeems me from all evil. God sends an angel to accomplish redemption — *malach dealma*, just an angel. But regarding sustenance, the verse says *haElokim haro'eh oti* — God Himself shepherds me, God Himself is the one who ensures my provision. This is the *Mizmor l'David Hashem ro'i lo echsar* — God my shepherd, I shall not lack. The implications are striking: the sustained miracle of daily *parnassah* requires God's direct involvement in a way that even redemption from Egypt did not.

### Bodily Functions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: קָשִׁין נְקָבָיו שֶׁל אָדָם כְּיוֹם הַמִּיתָה וְכִקְרִיעַת יַם סוּף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִהַר צוֹעֶה לְהִפָּתֵחַ״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״רוֹגַע הַיָּם וַיֶּהֱמוּ גַּלָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said: A person's orifices,** when he cannot properly relieve himself, **are as difficult** for him **as the day of death and the splitting of the Red Sea, as it is stated: "He who is bent down shall speedily be loosed;** and he shall not go down dying into the pit, neither shall his bread fail" (Isaiah 51:14). The phrase "dying into the pit" indicates that the opening of the orifices is similar to a rescue from death. **And afterward it is written: "Who stirs up the sea, that its waves roar"** (Isaiah 51:15), which compares the previous matter to the splitting of the sea.

*Amar Rav Elazar ben Azaryah*: *kashim nekavav shel adam keyom hamitah uvekri'at Yam Suf* — the functioning of a person's bodily orifices is as weighty a matter as the day of death and as the splitting of *Yam Suf*. The verse *miher tzo'eh lehipateach* — quickly will the bound one open — is immediately followed by *rogeh hayam vayehemu galav* — God stirs the sea and its waves roar. The juxtaposition evokes *Kri'at Yam Suf*. The Chasam Sofer drew the analogy explicitly: the bodily functions that sustain human life are as miraculous as the splitting of the sea — both are daily demonstrations of divine power that we habitually take for granted.

## Human Dignity

### Labor Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁאָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לָאָדָם ״וְקוֹץ וְדַרְדַּר תַּצְמִיחַ לָךְ״ זָלְגוּ עֵינָיו דְּמָעוֹת, אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אֲנִי וַחֲמוֹרִי נֹאכַל בְּאֵבוּס אֶחָד? כֵּיוָן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״בְּזֵעַת אַפֶּךָ תֹּאכַל לֶחֶם״, נִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar statement. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: When the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Adam: "Thorns also and thistles shall it bring forth to you,** and you shall eat the herb of the field" (Genesis 3:18), **his eyes streamed with tears.** Adam **said before Him: Master of the Universe,** will **my donkey and I eat from one trough? After** God **said to him: "In the sweat of your face shall you eat bread"** (Genesis 3:19), **his mind was settled,** assured that if he toils he will be able to eat bread, unlike the donkey.

*Amar Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: when God said to Adam *kotz vedardar tatzmiach lach* — the ground will bring forth thorns and thistles — Adam's eyes filled with tears. *Amar lefanav*: Master of the Universe, shall I and my donkey eat from the same trough? Are we the same, both eating whatever grows from the ground? Once God added *beze'at apecha tochal lechem* — by the sweat of your brow you shall eat bread — *nitkarerah da'ato* — his mind settled. He understood: yes, I must labor, but the labor transforms the raw earth into *lechem* — into bread — something no animal can do. That transformation is the marker of human distinctiveness. The hard work is not a punishment so much as the price of a higher status.

### Dignity Work

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: אַשְׁרֵינוּ אִם עָמַדְנוּ בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַעֲדַיִין לָא פָּלְטִינַן מִינַּהּ, דְּקָא אָכְלִינַן עִיסְבֵי דְּדַבְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: We** would have been **fortunate had we remained under the first** decree and were still able to eat the herbs of the field. **And we still have not** entirely **escaped from** this decree, **as we** sometimes **eat the grass of the field,** in the form of vegetables and leaves.

*Amar Resh Lakish*: *ashreinu im amadnu barishonah* — it would have been better for us if we had remained in that first state, eating the grass of the field without labor. That would have been easier. The *gezeirat* of labor is hard. There is a real question here: is the dignity worth the effort?

### Animal vs. Human

*Ve'adayin lo paltinan minah* — and we have not fully escaped from the category of animals, says Resh Lakish. *Dekachlinan ishvei dedabra* — we still eat the grass of the field, meaning we still eat vegetables — which prompts the observation, familiar to anyone who has heard bread called "real food" and salad dismissed as "rabbit food," that the transformation from purely natural consumption to fully cultivated human food is never quite complete. The point is philosophical: human dignity requires effort and aspiration, not ease. The choice is between eating like an animal without effort and eating like a human being through toil — and even then, the boundary is never absolute.

### Seder Transition

This teaching functions as a powerful bridge at the close of *leil ha-seder*. The *seder* is *Leil HaSeder* — a fantasy of freedom, of *haseivah* and fine wine, of *afilu ani shebe-Yisrael lo yifchatu lo me'arba kosot* — even the poorest Jew must have four cups. But the *gemara* here acknowledges the reality: when we leave that fantasy and step back into ordinary life, it is simply hard to put food on the table. The miracle of daily *parnassah* — *kashe mikri'at Yam Suf* — is itself a continuation of the *geulah*. We carry *yetziat Mitzrayim* not only in the *seder* night but in the daily struggle, and seeing God's hand in that struggle is precisely what *Hallel HaGadol* calls us to do.

## Aggadic Excursus

### Holiday Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: כׇּל הַמְבַזֶּה אֶת הַמּוֹעֲדוֹת כְּאִילּוּ עוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהֵי מַסֵּכָה לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה לָּךְ״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״אֶת חַג הַמַּצּוֹת תִּשְׁמוֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** After citing a statement of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya that was transmitted by *amora'im*, the Gemara quotes additional expositions attributed to him. **And Rav Sheshet said, citing Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: Anyone who disparages the Festivals, it is** considered **as though he engages in idol worship.** This, too, is derived from the juxtaposition of verses, **as it is stated: "You shall make yourself no molten gods"** (Exodus 34:17), **and afterward it is written: "The Festival of *matzot* you shall keep"** (Exodus 34:18), from which it can be inferred that anyone who does not observe the Festivals properly is likened to one who fashions idols.

*Amar Rav Shishi mishmeih de-Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah*: anyone who demeans the *mo'adot* — and some understand this to refer specifically to *Chol HaMoed* — is as if he worships idols. The *gemara* derives this from the juxtaposition in the *Torah*: *elohei masechah lo ta'aseh lach* — do not make molten gods for yourself — is immediately followed by *et chag hamatzot tishmor* — you shall observe the festival of *matzos*. The adjacency implies equivalence in severity: denigrating the sanctity of the festival is akin to the gravest form of religious transgression.

### Lashon Hara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: כׇּל הַמְסַפֵּר לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, וְכׇל הַמְקַבֵּל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, וְכׇל הַמֵּעִיד עֵדוּת שֶׁקֶר בַּחֲבֵירוֹ — רָאוּי לְהַשְׁלִיכוֹ לִכְלָבִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לַכֶּלֶב תַּשְׁלִיכוּן אוֹתוֹ״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״לֹא תִשָּׂא שֵׁמַע שָׁוְא״, וּקְרִי בֵּיהּ: ״לֹא תַּשִּׂיא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet** further **said, citing Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: Anyone who speaks slander, and anyone who accepts** and believes the **slander** he hears, **and anyone who testifies falsely about another, it is fitting to throw him to the dogs, as it is stated:** "And you shall not eat any flesh that is torn of beasts in the field, **you shall cast it to the dogs"** (Exodus 22:30), **and afterward it is written: "You shall not utter [*tisa*] a false report;** put not your hand with the wicked to be an unrighteous witness" (Exodus 23:1). Uttering rumors is here equated to delivering false testimony. Furthermore, **read into** the verse as though it stated: **Do not cause** a false report **to be accepted [*tasi*],** i.e., do not lead others to accept your false reports.

In the same context, *ve'amar Rav Sheshet mishum Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah*: anyone who speaks *lashon hara*, and anyone who accepts *lashon hara*, and anyone who testifies falsely against another, deserves to be thrown to the dogs — *ra'uy lehashlicho laklavim*. The verse by the *neveilah* says *lakelev tashlichun oto* — throw it to the dog — and is immediately followed by *lo tisa shema shav* — do not carry a false report. Reading *lo tashi* — do not cause one to arise — the *gemara* derives that one must not generate false reports, not merely receive them.

## Why HaMitzri

### Both Contain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּאִיכָּא הַלֵּל הַגָּדוֹל, אֲנַן מַאי טַעְמָא אָמְרִינַן הַאי? מִשּׁוּם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ חֲמִשָּׁה דְבָרִים הַלָּלוּ: יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם, קְרִיעַת יַם סוּף, וּמַתַּן תּוֹרָה, וּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, וְחֶבְלוֹ שֶׁל מָשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And since there is** the **great *hallel*,** which contains the special praise of "Who gives food to all flesh" (Psalms 136:25), as explained above, **what is the reason** that **one** also **recites this** *hallel* of Psalms 113–118, the section recited on every joyous occasion? The Gemara answers: The reason is **because** the regular *hallel* **contains these five matters:** The remembrance of the **exodus from Egypt, the splitting of the Red Sea, the giving of the Torah, the resurrection of the dead, and the pangs of the Messiah.** Since it mentions these key concepts, this hallel is also considered important.

The *gemara* poses the question: given that *Hallel HaGadol* contains references to both *Yetzias Mitzrayim* and *Kri'at Yam Suf*, why do we say *Hallel HaMitzri* — the standard *Hallel* — on *Yamim Tovim*? Why not simply say *Hallel HaGadol*?

### Five Three

*Tanu Rabbanan*: the two *Hallels* are distinguished by their thematic content. *Hallel HaMitzri* contains five subjects that *Hallel HaGadol* does not include in the same way, while *Hallel HaGadol* contains three subjects of its own. The five of *Hallel HaMitzri* are what make it specifically appropriate for *Yamim Tovim*.

### Five Topics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּצֵאת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרַיִם״. וּקְרִיעַת יַם סוּף, דִּכְתִיב: ״הַיָּם רָאָה וַיָּנוֹס״. מַתַּן תּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״הֶהָרִים רָקְדוּ כְאֵילִים״. תְּחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶתְהַלֵּךְ לִפְנֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The exodus from Egypt, as it is written: "When Israel came forth out of Egypt,** the house of Jacob from a people of strange language" (Psalms 114:1). **And the splitting of the Red Sea, as it is written: "The sea saw it and fled;** the Jordan turned backward" (Psalms 114:3). **The giving of the Torah, as it is written: "The mountains skipped like rams"** (Psalms 114:4), which is similar to the description of the giving of the Torah found elsewhere in the books of the Prophets. **The resurrection of the dead, as it is written: "I will walk before the Lord** in the lands of the living" (Psalms 116:9), which follows the verse: "For you have delivered my soul from death." After mentioning death, the psalm describes the resurrection in the lands of the living.

The five topics of *Hallel HaMitzri* are: *yetziat Mitzrayim*, the exodus; *kri'at Yam Suf*, the splitting of the sea; *matan Torah*, the giving of the *Torah*; *techiyat hameitim*, resurrection of the dead; and *chevlo shel Moshiach*, the birth pangs of the messianic era.

### Topic Sources

*Yetziat Mitzrayim dechtiv*: *betzet Yisrael mi-Mitzrayim* — as Israel went out from Egypt. *Kri'at Yam Suf*: *hayam ra'ah vayanos* — the sea saw and fled. *Matan Torah*: *he-harim rakedu che-eilim* — the mountains danced like rams, which the *midrash* understands as the trembling of *Har Sinai*. *Techiyat hameitim*: *ethalech lifnei Hashem be'artzot hachayim* — I will walk before God in the lands of the living, that is, in the world where there is only life, referring to resurrection. *Chevlo shel Moshiach dechtiv*: *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu* — not to us, O God, not to us — which refers to a period of suffering and subjugation. *Va'amar Rav Yochanan*: *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu* — this refers to the subjugation of the nations, from which Israel will ultimately be freed at the time of redemption. *Ika d'amri*: Rabbi Yochanan says it refers to the war of Gog and Magog, the final conflict preceding the coming of *Moshiach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶבְלוֹ שֶׁל מָשִׁיחַ, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא לָנוּ ה׳ לֹא לָנוּ״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״לֹא לָנוּ ה׳ לֹא לָנוּ״ — זוֹ שִׁעְבּוּד מַלְכִּיּוֹת. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״לֹא לָנוּ ה׳ לֹא לָנוּ״ — זוֹ מִלְחֶמֶת גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** **The pangs of the Messiah, as it is written: "Not to us, God, not to us,** but to Your name give glory" (Psalms 115:1). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The verse **"Not to us, God, not to us"** and the entire psalm, including the verse "Why should the nations say, where now is their God?" (Psalms 115:2), is referring to the era of **the enslavement of the kingdoms** and the redemption of the Jewish people from their dominion. **Some say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The verse **"Not to us, God, not to us"** is referring to **the war of Gog and Magog,** the catastrophes and wars that will befall the Jewish people in the end of days from which they will be delivered.

### Context Diff

The underlying difference between the two *Hallels*, then, is not simply a list of topics but a fundamental difference in character. *Hallel HaMitzri* is the *Hallel* of the miraculous and salvific: dramatic, supernatural, spanning from Egypt through the end of days. *Hallel HaGadol* is the *Hallel* of nature and ongoing providence: God as the sustainer of all creatures, the provider of daily bread. On *Yamim Tovim* we want the *Hallel* that speaks to the miraculous dimension; in *Pesukei D'Zimra* of every *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* we want the *Hallel* that speaks to creation and provision. On *leil ha-seder*, we recite both — beginning with the miraculous and concluding with the providential.

## Lo Lanu Prayer

### Lo Lanu Intro

The *gemara* has identified *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu* as referring to *chevlo shel Moshiach* — the birth pangs of the messianic era. The verse, read in this context, expresses the suffering and subjugation of the period leading up to redemption: *lo lanu* — not by our merit, not for our sake — but for the sake of God's name and truth.

### Gog Magog

An alternative reading: *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu* refers specifically to *milchemes Gog u-Magog*, the eschatological war that precedes the final redemption. On this reading, *Hallel HaMitzri* contains within it a prayer and reference to the ultimate future deliverance, making it fitting not only for *Yamim Tovim* commemorating past salvation but also as an expression of hope for future redemption.

### Righteous Saved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מִילּוּט נְפָשׁוֹת שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים מִגֵּיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָנָּה ה׳ מַלְּטָה נַפְשִׁי״. חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ יְרִידָתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים לְכִבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, וַעֲלִיָּיתָן מִמֶּנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** Another reason why one recites *hallel* of Psalms 113–118 is **because it contains** a reference to **the deliverance of the souls of the righteous from Gehenna, as it is stated: "I beseech you, Lord, deliver my soul"** (Psalms 116:4). **Ḥizkiya said:** Another reason is **because it contains** the story of **the descent of the righteous** Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah **into the fiery furnace and their** miraculous **ascent from it.**

*Amar Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak mishum*: among the additional reasons *Hallel HaMitzri* is chosen is that it contains the *milut nafshan shel tzaddikim migehinnom* — the escape of the souls of the righteous from *Gehinnom*. The verse *ana Hashem malta nafshi* — please God, save my soul — is understood as a prayer for deliverance from the fires of *Gehinnom*. *Chizkiyah amar mishum*: it contains also the *yeridatan shel tzaddikim l'kivshan haeish v'aliyasan mimenu* — the descent of the righteous into the fiery furnace and their ascent from it, referring specifically to *Chananya Mishael v'Azarya*.

### Three Youths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְרִידָתָן, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא לָנוּ ה׳ לֹא לָנוּ״ אָמַר חֲנַנְיָה, ״כִּי לְשִׁמְךָ תֵּן כָּבוֹד״ אָמַר מִישָׁאֵל, ״עַל חַסְדְּךָ וְעַל אֲמִתֶּךָ״ אָמַר עֲזַרְיָה, ״לָמָּה יֹאמְרוּ הַגּוֹיִם״ אָמְרוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** Ḥizkiya clarifies his previous statement: **Their descent** is mentioned in this hallel, **as it is written: "Not to us, God, not to us,"** a verse that Hananiah **recited. Mishael recited: "But to Your name give glory." Azariah recited: "For Your mercy and for Your truth's sake." They all recited** together: **"Why should the nations say:** Where now is their God?"

The passage of *lo lanu Hashem lo lanu* is then read as a three-part prayer of the three young men before their ordeal: *lo lanu* — Chananya's declaration that it is not for our sake but for Yours; *ki l'shimcha ten kavod* — Mishael's appeal to God's honor; *al chasdecha v'al amittecha* — Azarya's invocation of God's *chesed* and truth. Together they are pleading: do not allow the nations to say You have abandoned us. Their prayer is one of *bakasha*. Upon their deliverance, the prayer transforms into praise: *hallu et Hashem kol goyim* — Chananya; *shabbechu'u kol ha'umim* — Mishael; *ki gavar aleinu chasdo* — Azarya; *ve'emes Hashem l'olam halleluyah* — all three together. The movement from *bakasha* to *shevach* mirrors the arc of *Yonah* in the belly of the fish: the cry from the depths, the salvation, and then the hymn of praise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲלִיָּיתָן מִכִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: ״הַלְלוּ אֶת ה׳ כׇּל גּוֹיִם״ אָמַר חֲנַנְיָה, ״שַׁבְּחוּהוּ כָּל הָאוּמִּים״ אָמַר מִישָׁאֵל, ״כִּי גָבַר עָלֵינוּ חַסְדּוֹ״ אָמַר עֲזַרְיָה, ״וֶאֱמֶת ה׳ לְעוֹלָם הַלְלוּיָהּ״ אָמְרוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** This *hallel* also alludes to **the ascent** of Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah **from the fiery furnace, as it is written:** "Praise the Lord, all you nations, laud Him all you peoples. For His mercy is great toward us, and the truth of the Lord endures forever, *halleluya*" (Psalms 117). **Hananiah recited: "Praise the Lord, all you nations,"** for the overt miracle performed for them before the nations. **Mishael recited: "Laud Him all you peoples." Azariah recited: "For His mercy is great toward us." They all recited** together: **"And the truth of the Lord endures forever, *halleluya*."**

## Furnace Stories

### Nimrod Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: ״וֶאֱמֶת ה׳ לְעוֹלָם״ גַּבְרִיאֵל אֲמָרוֹ. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהִפִּיל נִמְרוֹד הָרָשָׁע אֶת אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ לְתוֹךְ כִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ אָמַר גַּבְרִיאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אֵרֵד וַאֲצַנֵּן וְאַצִּיל אֶת הַצַּדִּיק מִכִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אֲנִי יָחִיד בְּעוֹלָמִי וְהוּא יָחִיד בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, נָאֶה לַיָּחִיד לְהַצִּיל אֶת הַיָּחִיד. וּלְפִי שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֵינוֹ מְקַפֵּחַ שְׂכַר כׇּל בְּרִיָּה, אָמַר: תִּזְכֶּה וְתַצִּיל שְׁלֹשָׁה מִבְּנֵי בָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that the angel **Gabriel recited: "And the truth of the Lord endures forever."** This Gemara elaborates: **When the evil Nimrod threw our father, Abraham, into the fiery furnace, Gabriel said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, I will descend and cool** the furnace, **and I will** thereby **save the righteous** Abraham **from the fiery furnace. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: I am unique in my world and** Abraham **is** still **unique in his world. It is fitting for the unique to save the unique.** Therefore, God Himself went down and saved him. **And as the Holy One, Blessed be He, does not withhold reward from any creature** who sought to perform a good deed, He **said** to Gabriel: **You will merit the rescue of three of his descendants** under similar circumstances.

*Amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov*: one is required to mention *Yetzias Mitzrayim* in *Kiddush*. This ruling requires justification, since one might have thought that *Kiddush* — at least on *Shabbos* — is thematically about *Ma'aseh Bereishis*, creation, and has no inherent connection to the exodus. The question is all the more pointed because there are two accounts of the *Aseres Hadibros* in the *Torah*, each giving a different rationale for *Shabbos*.

### Singular Save

God replied to Gabriel: *ani yachid be'olami v'hu yachid be'olamo* — I am singular in My world, and he is the singular one in his world who believes in Me. We have a uniquely individual relationship, the one and only God with the one and only believer. *Na'eh l'yachid lehatzil et hayachid* — it is fitting for the Singular One to save the singular one. God Himself, not an angel, would save Avraham.

### Three Saved

Yet, *velifi shehakadosh baruch hu eino mekape'ach sechar kol biryah* — because God does not withhold the reward owed to any creature — Gabriel's offer was not dismissed without consequence. God said to him: *tizkeh v'atzil sheloshah mibnei banav* — you will merit to save three of his descendants. And those three were *Chananya Mishael v'Azarya*.

### Furnace Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַשִּׁלוֹנִי: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהִפִּיל נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָרָשָׁע חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה לְתוֹךְ כִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, עָמַד יוֹרֶקְמוֹ שַׂר הַבָּרָד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אֵרֵד וַאֲצַנֵּן אֶת הַכִּבְשָׁן וְאַצִּיל לַצַּדִּיקִים הַלָּלוּ מִכִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ. אָמַר לוֹ גַּבְרִיאֵל: אֵין גְּבוּרָתוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּכָךְ שֶׁאַתָּה שַׂר בָּרָד, וְהַכֹּל יוֹדְעִין שֶׁהַמַּיִם מְכַבִּין אֶת הָאֵשׁ. אֶלָּא, אֲנִי שַׂר שֶׁל אֵשׁ אֵרֵד וַאֲקָרֵר מִבִּפְנִים, וְאַקְדִּיחַ מִבַּחוּץ, וְאֶעֱשֶׂה נֵס בְּתוֹךְ נֵס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon HaShiloni taught: When the evil** Nebuchadnezzar **threw Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah into the fiery furnace, Yurkamo, the ministering angel of hail, stood before the Holy One, Blessed be He,** and **said before Him: Master of the Universe, I will go down and cool** the fiery **furnace, and I will save these righteous ones from the fiery furnace. Gabriel said to him: The strength of the Holy One, Blessed be He, will not be** evident **in this** manner, as **you are the minister of hail, and everyone knows that water extinguishes fire.** Your action would not be regarded as a great miracle. **Rather, I, the ministering angel of fire, will descend, and I will cool** the furnace **from within, and I will burn** it **from the outside,** to consume those who threw the three righteous men into the furnace; **and I will** thereby **perform a miracle within a miracle.**

*Besha'ah shehipil Nevuchadnezzar harasha et Chananya Mishael v'Azarya l'soch kivshan haeish* — when Nevuchadnezzar cast them into the furnace — *amar Yurkamo sar habarad lifnei Hakadosh Baruch Hu*: Yurkamo, the angelic minister of frost and hail, came before God and said: Master of the Universe, let me descend and cool the furnace and save these righteous ones. Gabriel interjected: *ein gevuraso shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu bekach* — God's power is not demonstrated in that way. *Atah sar barad* — you are the minister of frost; that a frost angel can cool a fire is no great demonstration of divine might.

### Double Miracle

*Ela ani sar shel esh* — I am the minister of fire. *Eired v'akrir mibifnim v'akdiach mibachutz* — I will descend and cool from within, and heat from without, so that those inside the furnace will be saved while those surrounding it — the Babylonian soldiers who had stoked it — will be consumed. This is exactly what the story in Daniel records. *V'na'aseh nes betoch nes* — a miracle within a miracle: the same fire that burned hotter on the outside was cooling on the inside. The *gemara* adds, with a touch of humor: it is like asking of a thermos — it keeps cold things cold and hot things hot, but how does it know?

### Promise Fulfil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רֵד! בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה פָּתַח גַּבְרִיאֵל וְאָמַר: ״וֶאֱמֶת ה׳ לְעוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: Descend. At that time Gabriel began** praising God **and recited: "And the truth of the Lord endures forever"** (Psalms 117:2), as God fulfilled His promise to him from more than a thousand years earlier.

*B'otah sha'ah patach Gavriel v'amar*: at that moment Gabriel opened with the words *ve'emes Hashem l'olam* — the truth of God endures forever. God had promised Gabriel an opportunity to save the descendants of Avraham. Here was that moment. The proclamation *ve'emes Hashem l'olam* is Gabriel's affirmation that God fulfills His promises — that the *emet* of the divine word is not merely spoken but enacted across generations, from Nimrod's furnace to the furnace of Nevuchadnezzar, from Avraham to *Chananya Mishael v'Azarya*.
