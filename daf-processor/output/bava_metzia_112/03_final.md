# Bava Metzia 112 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wage Payment Framework

### Day/Night Workers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: אֶחָד שְׂכַר אָדָם וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר בְּהֵמָה וְאֶחָד שְׂכַר כֵּלִים – יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם "בְּיוֹמוֹ תִתֵּן שְׂכָרוֹ", וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין פְּעֻלַּת שָׂכִיר".
>
> **Translation:** **From here** the Sages **stated: Whether** referring to **the hire of a person or the rental of an animal or the rental of utensils,** all are **subject to** the prohibition **of: "On the same day you shall give him his wages"** (Deuteronomy 24:15), **and they are** likewise **subject to the prohibition of delaying** the payment of wages of a **hired laborer** (Leviticus 19:13).

The sugya picks up at the bottom of 111b, dealing with the obligations and prohibitions surrounding the timely payment of workers. The Torah formulates this in two distinct verses. One verse states *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itecha ad boker* — do not let the worker's wage remain with you overnight, meaning it must be paid before the following morning. The other states *bayomo titein secharo velo tavo alav hashemesh* — pay him during the day before sunset. The Gemara reconciles these two verses by distinguishing between a night worker, who must be paid by the following day, and a day worker, who must be paid by the following night.

Once the employer has transgressed this prohibition once, he cannot transgress it again — the violation is a single event. However, the rabbis understand that there remains a rabbinic obligation of *lo tisheheh*: even after the initial transgression, the employer still must pay as quickly as possible. That is the basic framework.

### Non-Jews & Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שְׂכַר אָדָם, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת בְּהֵמָה וְכֵלִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "בְּאַרְצְךָ" – כׇּל שֶׁבְּאַרְצְךָ. וְכוּלָּן עוֹבְרִים בְּכׇל הַשֵּׁמוֹת הַלָּלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived **only** that the prohibitions of delaying wages apply to **the hire of people. From where** do I know **to include** payment for the rental of **animals and utensils** in the prohibition of delaying wages? **The verse states: "In your land,"** which includes **all that is in your land. And** in **all of** the above cases of delaying payment they **transgress all of these prohibitions** which apply to delaying payment.

The Gemara then extends the prohibition in two directions that are not explicit in the *pesukim*. First, the verse uses the word *me'achecha* — "your brother" — which would seem to limit the obligation to fellow Jews. Nevertheless, *Chazal* derive from an additional word in the verse that the *mitzvah* of timely payment also applies to a *ger toshav*. This is a genuine *chidush*, given that the plain meaning of *me'achecha* points in the opposite direction. Second, the Gemara extends the obligation beyond hiring a person's labor to renting their vessels or animals.

That second extension invites scrutiny, because the verse concludes with the phrase *ve'elav hu nosei et nafsho* — "he gives his soul for this" — capturing the poignancy of a worker who has toiled all day and returns home empty-handed. That emotional force clearly does not apply with the same sharpness when one has merely rented out a vessel or an animal. And yet the verse extends to those cases as well.

It is also worth noting that the verse in question opens with *lo ta'ashok sechar ani ve'evyon me'achecha o migercha* — "do not oppress the wages of the poor and destitute among your brothers or your strangers" — followed by *bayomo titein secharo vegam ki ani hu ve'elav hu nosei nafsho*. A student might observe that the plain reading seems aimed specifically at the injustice of withholding wages from a poor person, and question whether the Gemara's division into a "day shift" and "night shift" framework compromises that moral thrust. In truth it does not: the division responds to the textual contradiction between the two verses and simply gives the employer a reasonable window within which to pay. Moreover, the prohibition applies even where the worker is not poor — a wealthy employee still has the right to be paid on time. The Gemara's framework reflects a sensitivity to the realities of commercial life without abandoning the Torah's fundamental moral demand.

### Textual Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֲפִילּוּ לֹא שְׂכָרוֹ אֶלָּא לִבְצוֹר לוֹ אֶשְׁכּוֹל אֶחָד שֶׁל עֲנָבִים – עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין".
>
> **Translation:** **as Rav Asi says: Even** if one **hired** the laborer **to harvest only one cluster of grapes for him,** one **violates the prohibition of delaying** payment of wages.

The Gemara turns to a *machloket* about what is derived from specific words in the verse. Rav Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda asks: what does one do with the word *itecha* in the phrase *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itecha ad boker* — what does one derive from that extra word? The discussion that follows leads directly into the moral dimension of the *sugyah*.

## Moral Imperative

### Single Cluster

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: מִ"וְאֵלָיו הוּא נֹשֵׂא אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ" נָפְקָא, דָּבָר הַמּוֹסֵר נַפְשׁוֹ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where does **the other** Sage, i.e., the first *tanna*, derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: He **derives** it **from** the phrase: **"For he sets his soul upon it"** (Deuteronomy 24:15). This indicates that one is liable for delaying the payment of wages due for any work; as a laborer obligates himself to perform the work, it is **something for which he gives his soul.**

The answer is what Rabbi Assi says: *afilu lo secharo ela livtzor lo eshkol echad shel anavim* — even if you only hired the worker to harvest a single cluster of grapes, you transgress *bal talin*. Tosafot links this to an earlier Gemara that established that even a worker hired to harvest one cluster of grapes may eat from that cluster as part of the right of workers to eat from the produce they harvest. If the employer fails to pay him even for that small amount, he transgresses *bayomo titein secharo* and *lo talin pe'ulat sachir itecha ad boker*. The word *itecha* — "with you" — is what teaches this: even a minimal engagement falls within the prohibition.

### Worker Risks Life

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ: הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: "וְאֵלָיו הוּא נֹשֵׂא אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ", מִפְּנֵי מָה עָלָה זֶה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ וְנִתְלָה בָּאִילָן וּמָסַר אֶת עַצְמוֹ לְמִיתָה – לֹא עַל שְׂכָרוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what does **the other** Sage, the second *tanna*, derive from this verse? The Gemara responds: **That** verse is **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The expression **"for he sets his soul upon it"** explains why one must be so precise when paying a laborer his wages: **For what** reason did **this** laborer **ascend on** a tall **ramp or suspend** himself **from a tree and risk death to himself?** Was it **not for his wages?** How, then, can his employer delay his payment?

The other opinion holds that this ruling can be derived from the phrase *ve'elav hu nosei et nafsho* — "he gives his soul for this" — meaning that anything a person invests effort and life-force into is included. According to that view, the word *itecha* is not needed for that purpose. The question then becomes what the second opinion does with the phrase *ve'elav hu nosei et nafsho*, since if it carries no independent halachic derivation, what is its function in the verse?

The Gemara answers: *miba'ei leh le-chadetanya* — it is needed for what was taught in the *baraita*. The *baraita* reads *ve'elav hu nosei et nafsho* and asks: why would a person climb a high ramp, dangle from a tree, or descend to the depths of the ocean? *Umassar atzmo le-mitah* — because he is willing to risk his life for his wages. And now you are going to withhold those wages? The alternate text that appears in the margin of the Gemara sharpens this further: the focus is not merely that he risked his life, but that he did so because he was relying on you to pay him — *lav she-titein lo secharo*. That is the real poignancy: he put himself in danger on the strength of your commitment to pay, and you are betraying it.

### Huna/Chisda Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: "וְאֵלָיו הוּא נֹשֵׂא אֶת נַפְשׁוֹ", כָּל הַכּוֹבֵשׁ שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר – כְּאִילּוּ נוֹטֵל נַפְשׁוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ. רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא, חַד אָמַר: נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל גַּזְלָן, וְחַד אָמַר: נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל נִגְזָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the words **"for he sets his soul upon it"** teach that concerning **one who withholds the wages of a hired laborer,** it is **as though he takes his soul from him. Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda** disagreed over the meaning of this statement. **One says** it is referring to **the soul of the robber,** meaning that one who steals from a hired laborer by delaying payment of his wages causes Heaven to remove his own soul, **and one says** that he takes **the soul of the robbery victim,** meaning that one who steals from a hired worker causes the death of the worker.

The Gemara then offers *davar acher* — another reading of the same phrase: *kol ha-kovesh sechar sachir ke'ilu hu notel nafsho mimenu* — whoever withholds a worker's wages is as if he has taken his very life. This could be read in a moral sense: if the man needs the money to feed his family and you deny it to him, it is as though you have murdered him. Alternatively it can be read as referring to the withholding itself — he gave his life for this work and you gave him nothing in return, which is tantamount to killing him.

Rav Huna and Rav Chisda then dispute the application of this idea. One says *nafsho shel gazlan* — it is the life of the robber himself that is forfeited: this sin is so grave that the perpetrator pays for it with his own life. Note the Gemara's use of the term *gazlan* here, even though the employer may not be refusing to pay outright but merely postponing — the Gemara is deliberately harsh in its characterization. This position is supported by the verse *al tigzol dal ki dal hu* — do not steal from the poor person, and Rashi explains: do not think that because he is poor and powerless no one will defend him. God will defend him — *ki Hashem yariv rivam* — and *kava et koveihem nafesh*, God will despoil the soul of those who despoil the poor.

The other opinion says *nafsho shel nigzal* — it is the life of the victim that is taken. The robber does not merely cost someone money; he takes their sustenance, their very capacity to live. This is supported by *kein orchot kol botzea batza et nefesh be'alav yikach* — this is the way of all who pursue ill-gotten gain: they take the soul of the owner.

### Stealing Soul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל גַּזְלָן, דִּכְתִיב: "אַל תִּגְזׇל דַּל כִּי דַל הוּא וְאַל תְּדַכֵּא עָנִי בַשָּׁעַר", וּכְתִיב: "כִּי ה׳ יָרִיב רִיבָם וְקָבַע אֶת קֹבְעֵיהֶם נָפֶשׁ". וּמַאן דְּאָמַר נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל נִגְזָל, דִּכְתִיב: "כֵּן אׇרְחוֹת כׇּל בּוֹצֵעַ בָּצַע אֶת נֶפֶשׁ בְּעָלָיו יִקָּח".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof for these two opinions. **The one who says** it is referring to **the soul of the robber** bases his opinion on a verse, **as it is written: "Do not rob from the weak because he is weak, nor crush the poor in the gate"** (Proverbs 22:22), **and** it **is written** immediately afterward: **"For the Lord will plead their cause, and spoil the soul of those who spoil them"** (Proverbs 22:23). This indicates that God will take the soul of one who steals from a poor person. **And the one who says** it is referring to **the soul of the robbery victim** bases his opinion on a verse, **as it is written: "So are the ways of everyone who is greedy for gain; it takes away the life of its owners"** (Proverbs 1:19). A robber is considered as if he removed the very soul of his victim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב "אֶת נֶפֶשׁ בְּעָלָיו יִקָּח"! בְּעָלָיו דְּהַשְׁתָּא. וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: "וְקָבַע אֶת קֹבְעֵיהֶם נָפֶשׁ"! מָה טַעַם קָאָמַר: מָה טַעַם "קָבַע אֶת קֹבְעֵיהֶם" – מִשּׁוּם דְּנָטְלוּ נָפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** Sage **too, isn't it written: "It takes away the life of its owners"?** How does he interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: This is referring to **its current owner,** i.e., the robber, who took the money and now owns it. The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** Sage **too, isn't it written: "And spoil the soul of those who spoil them"?** How does he interpret this verse? The Gemara answers: This verse employs the style known as: **What is the reason,** as follows: **What is the reason** that God will **spoil those who spoil them? Because they took** someone's **soul,** for which He will exact retribution.

Taken together, these two moral arguments address different psychological types. For a person who responds to empathy — one who is moved by the suffering of others — the message that withholding wages is like taking a life is the relevant appeal. For a person unmoved by the plight of others but highly attentive to self-interest — what the Gemara elsewhere associates with the attitude of *sheli sheli ve-shelach shelach* — the warning that this sin will cost you your own life is more compelling. These are not mutually exclusive readings, and neither, strictly speaking, is the *peshat* of the verses. The *peshat* of *ve'elav hu nosei et nafsho* is the first thing the Gemara said: the worker risked his life to earn these wages. The subsequent discussions are the Gemara using the opportunity for *mussar* around this *mitzvah*.

## Conditions & Arrangements

### Upon Demand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁתְּבָעוֹ, לֹא תְּבָעוֹ – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "לֹא תָלִין פְּעֻלַּת שָׂכִיר" יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ לֹא תְּבָעוֹ – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "אִתְּךָ", לְדַעְתְּךָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **When** does he transgress these prohibitions? He transgresses them **when** the one owed the money **claimed** the payment from **him.** If he **did not claim** his payment from **him,** the other **does not transgress** the prohibitions. **The Sages taught:** With regard to the verse: **"The wages of a hired laborer shall not remain** with you all night until the morning" (Leviticus 19:13), one **might** have thought that he should be liable **even** if the laborer **did not claim** his wages from **him. The verse states "with you,"** meaning the prohibition is not transgressed unless it is **with your knowledge** and consent that you have not paid him. But if he did not even request his wages yet, the prohibition has not been violated.

The Gemara now turns to the conditions that must be met for the transgression to occur. *Eimatai* — when does one transgress? *Bizman she-teva'o* — only when the worker has made a demand for payment. If the worker has not yet asked to be paid, the employer does not transgress. The Gemara derives this from the word *itecha* — "with you, according to your knowledge" — *le-da'atcha*: only if you are the one consciously holding it back. If the employee has not yet demanded it and has implicitly indicated he is content to wait, the obligation has not yet been triggered.

This is admittedly a striking ruling given the power dynamics involved. It is not difficult to imagine that a worker might refrain from pressing his employer out of fear of losing the job. The fact that silence is treated as acquiescence is therefore counterintuitive from a moral standpoint — and yet that is the *halakha*.

The Gemara also addresses this in the form of a *baraita*: *lo talin pe'ulat sachir* — *yakhol afilu lo teva'o* — perhaps one transgresses even without a demand? *Talmud lomar itecha le-da'atcha*: only when it is you who are choosing to withhold it.

### Available Funds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ אֵין לוֹ – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "אִתְּךָ". שֶׁיֵּשׁ אִתְּךָ. יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ הִמְחָהוּ אֵצֶל חֶנְוָנִי וְאֵצֶל שׁוּלְחָנִי, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "אִתְּךָ" וְלֹא שֶׁהִמְחָהוּ אֵצֶל חֶנְוָנִי וְאֵצֶל שׁוּלְחָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, one **might** have thought that the employer is liable **even** if he **does not have** the money to pay him. Therefore, **the verse states "with you,"** indicating **that there is** money **with you.** One **might** have thought that **even** if the employer **transferred** his payment **to a storekeeper or to a money changer,** he still violates the prohibition of delaying payment of wages. Therefore, **the verse states "with you,"** indicating that it applies only if the payment is your obligation, **but not if** he **transferred it to a storekeeper or to a money changer,** as then the payment of the laborer's wages is no longer his responsibility.

A further condition is derived from the same word *itecha*. Perhaps one transgresses even if one does not have the money available? *Talmud lomar itecha* — it has to be "with you," meaning *sheyesh itecha*, that you actually have the funds. If the employer is asset-rich but cash-poor and simply does not have liquid money to pay, the prohibition does not attach.

This raises a further question: what does "having the money" mean? Is there an obligation to liquidate investments or convert assets in order to pay on time? This is left open. And of course, if someone knowingly hired workers without the means to pay them, there is a strong moral argument — perhaps *geneivat da'at* or worse — even if the technical prohibition of *bal talin* is not triggered. The *poskim* presumably address this, though it is beyond the scope of the Gemara's discussion here.

### Third-Party Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִמְחָהוּ אֵצֶל חֶנְוָנִי וְאֵצֶל שׁוּלְחָנִי – אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חוֹזֵר, אוֹ אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר? רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר, וְרַבָּה אָמַר: חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If** the one who owes the money **transferred his** payment by leaving instructions **with a storekeeper or with a money changer** to pay him, **he does not transgress** the prohibitions. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the storekeeper or money changer neglected to pay the wages, may the laborer **return** to the employer and claim his money from him, **or** may he **not return,** as the storekeeper or money changer is now his exclusive address for complaints? **Rav Sheshet says he may not return, and Rabba says he may return.**

The Gemara continues: *yakhol afilu himchehu eitzel chanvani ve'eitzel shulchani* — perhaps one transgresses even if one directed the worker to a storekeeper or moneychanger to collect payment there, and they delayed? *Talmud lomar itecha* — it must be you who is holding it. *Lo himchehu eitzel chanvani ve'eitzel shulchani* — not when you have arranged for someone else to pay, and that third party has delayed.

Tosafot raises the question of whether the employer may impose such an arrangement unilaterally. The assumption in Tosafot is that the worker must have either requested or agreed to be paid through the third party — whether by asking for it explicitly or by accepting it as a term of employment from the outset. If the employer simply announces mid-day that the storekeeper will be paying instead, that would presumably be impermissible.

This arrangement does raise serious possibilities for circumventing the prohibition. One could in principle arrange in advance for a third party to handle all payments, making it structurally impossible to ever transgress *bal talin*. The Gemara had addressed this earlier, with Tosafot clarifying that such an arrangement is not set up *le-chatchila* in order to evade the *mitzvah* — but perhaps as a safeguard against accidental delay.

### Worker Retraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ – מִדְּקָתָנֵי: אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו, מִעְבָּר הוּא דְּלָא עָבַר, הָא מִיהְדָּר הָדַר. וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: מַאי אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר – אֵינוֹ בְּתוֹרַת לַעֲבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: From where do I state** my opinion? **From** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches: He does not transgress** the prohibition, from which it may be inferred: **He does not transgress** the prohibition, **but** the laborer **may** still **return** to him to collect his wages. **And Rav Sheshet said: What** is the meaning of the ruling that he does **not transgress** the prohibition? It means that he **is not** included **in the category of transgressing,** as his transfer of the payment exempts him from all responsibility.

The Gemara now poses the question: *chozer o eino chozer* — can the worker retract the arrangement and demand to be paid directly by the employer instead of by the storekeeper?

The very fact that this question is asked makes clear that the arrangement could not be purely the employer's imposition — the worker must have some standing in relation to it, which implies he had a role in establishing it. The practical importance of the right to retract is obvious: if the storekeeper lacks funds, the worker needs to be able to go back to the employer. Tosafot frames the question more precisely: even granting that the worker may retract, does doing so then expose the employer to transgressing *bal talin*? If the original arrangement removed the obligation from the employer, does the worker's retraction reimpose it?

Rav Sheshet says *eino chozer* — the worker cannot retract. Rabbah says *chozer* — he can. Rabbah derives this from the language of the *Mishnah* itself: the *Mishnah* says that when the employer directs the worker to a storekeeper, the employer *does not transgress*. Rabbah reads this as significant: *me'avar hu de-lo avar ha-mahadar hadar* — the point is precisely that the employer does not transgress even though the worker has reverted and now wants direct payment. Were retraction impossible, the *Mishnah* would have nothing interesting to say; the more informative reading is that even when the worker has retracted, the employer is still protected by the original arrangement.

Rav Sheshet counters: *mai eino over eino be-torat lav* — what the *Mishnah* means is simply that there is no way the employer can transgress because the worker has no right to retract in the first place. Since the arrangement cannot be undone, the employer's exemption is structurally guaranteed. The two positions thus disagree about both the procedural question (can the worker retract?) and the reading of the *Mishnah* (does the *Mishnah* presuppose retraction or preclude it?).

## Kablanut

### Bal Talin Applies?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: קַבְּלָנוּת, עוֹבֵר עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין", אוֹ אֵין עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין"?
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages **inquired of Rav Sheshet:** If the laborer worked as **a contractor,** who is paid for a completed job rather than by the hour, does the employer **violate** the prohibition **of delaying** payment of wages **or** does he **not violate** the prohibition **of delaying** payment of wages?

The Gemara proceeds to a question posed to Rav Sheshet: does *bal talin* apply to *kablanut* — contractor work — or only to *sechirut* — standard wage labor?

The moral dimension of the question is worth considering. In a typical employer-employee relationship, the power differential is clear: the worker is dependent, the employer sets the terms. A *kablan* — a contractor — occupies a fundamentally different position. He takes a job, sets his own pace, and functions more like an independent businessperson. The sense of vulnerability that underlies the Torah's concern — the worker who risked his life and goes home empty-handed — applies less sharply in this context.

The Gemara frames the halachic question in terms of *uman koneh be-shvach kli* — does the craftsman acquire ownership of the improvement he produces in the article entrusted to him? This principle has direct bearing on the characterization of the transaction.

### Craftsman Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּמָּן קוֹנֶה בִּשְׁבַח כְּלִי, וְהַלְוָאָה הִיא? אוֹ אֵין אוּמָּן קוֹנֶה בִּשְׁבַח כְּלִי, וּשְׂכִירוּת הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** The resolution to this inquiry depends on how a craftsman's wages are classified. Does **a craftsman,** who is a type of contractor, **acquire** ownership rights **through enhancement of the vessel?** This would mean that the craftsman is considered to have acquired the vessel through his work, which enhances its value, and it remains in his possession until he returns it to the owners, who are then considered to have purchased the enhanced item from him. **And** accordingly, his payment **is** akin to **a loan** in that it will not be subject to the prohibition of delaying the payment of wages. **Or** perhaps **a craftsman does not acquire** ownership rights **through enhancement of the vessel,and** the obligation of the owner to pay him is similar to the obligation to pay wages to any laborer, in which case the money **is** classified as **a wage,** and is subject to the prohibition of delaying wages.

The Gemara presents the question as follows: if I give you raw materials worth ten dollars and you fashion them into a cabinet worth one thousand dollars, the question is who owns the improvement. If *uman koneh be-shvach kli* — if the craftsman acquires the improvement — then the nine hundred ninety dollars of added value belongs to the craftsman. When you pay him for his work, you are not paying him wages; you are purchasing from him his stake in the improved object. That is more like a sale (*mecher*) than *sechirut*, and the Gemara characterizes the resulting obligation as *halva'ah* — the craftsman has in effect extended you a loan when he delivers the article and you do not immediately pay. In that case, *bal talin* would not apply, because this is not a worker-employer relationship at all.

If, on the other hand, *ein uman koneh be-shvach kli* — the craftsman does not acquire the improvement — then the entire object belongs to the owner from the moment the raw materials were handed over, and the craftsman is simply owed wages for his labor. That is *sechirut*, and *bal talin* would apply.

### Sheshet Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: עוֹבֵר! וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר! הָתָם שֶׁהִמְחָהוּ אֵצֶל חֶנְוָנִי וְאֵצֶל שׁוּלְחָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said to them:** He does **violate** the prohibition. They asked Rav Sheshet: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that a contractor **does not violate** the prohibition? Rav Sheshet replied: **There** it is referring to a case **where** he **transferred** the wages **to a storekeeper or to a money changer.**

Rav Sheshet states: *over* — the contractor does transgress, implying *ein uman koneh be-shvach kli*. This appears to be in tension with Rav Sheshet's position elsewhere, as Tosafot discusses.

The Gemara notes that a *baraita* states *eino over* — one does not transgress — in a case that might appear to be *kablanut*. The Gemara resolves this: *hatam she-himchihu eitzel chanvani ve'eitzel shulchani* — the *baraita*'s exemption is not about *kablanut* at all. It refers to the earlier ruling about directing payment through a third party. The two discussions are entirely separate, and the *baraita* provides no evidence about the *kablanut* question.

### Talit Completion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ: הַנּוֹתֵן טַלִּיתוֹ לְאוּמָּן, גְּמָרָהּ וְהוֹדִיעוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ מִכָּאן וְעַד עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים, אֵינוֹ עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין". נְתָנָהּ לוֹ בַּחֲצִי הַיּוֹם – מִשֶּׁשָּׁקְעָה עָלָיו חַמָּה עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the following *baraita* **supports** the opinion of Rav Sheshet: With regard to **one who gave his garment to a craftsman,** and the craftsman **concluded** the work **and notified** the owner that the work was complete, **even** if the owner delays paying the craftsman **from now until ten days** henceforth, **he does not violate** the **prohibition of delaying** the payment of wages. If the craftsman **gave** the garment **to him at midday,** then **once the sun has set** and the **owner** has not paid him, the owner **does violate** the **prohibition of delaying** the payment of wages.

The Gemara then attempts to bring a proof supporting Rav Sheshet's position that *bal talin* does apply to *kablanut*: *hanoten talit le-uman* — if one gives his *talit* to a craftsman for improvement — *gamrah hodio*, once the craftsman finishes and notifies the owner, *afilu mi-kan ve-ad asara yamim eino over mishum bal talin* — even if the owner waits ten days before picking it up, the employer does not transgress during that period. The clock does not begin ticking when the work is complete; it begins when the article is actually delivered and received.

However — *nasna lo be-chetzi ha-yom mishishka lo ha-chama over mishum bal talin* — once the article is handed over, even if delivered in the middle of the day, the employer transgresses once the sun sets without payment, mirroring the ruling about a *sachir sha'ot* who finishes mid-day. This demonstrates that *kablanut* is treated like ordinary *sechirut* and the same prohibition applies — presumably because *ein uman koneh be-shvach kli*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אוּמָּן קוֹנֶה בִּשְׁבַח כְּלִי – אַמַּאי עוֹבֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And if it enters your mind** to say that **a craftsman acquires** ownership rights **through** the **enhancement of the vessel, why does** the owner **violate** the prohibition of delaying the payment of wages? It is as if the craftsman acquired the garment, and the payment is considered to be a purchase of the garment by the owner, rather than a wage.

The Gemara then presses: *ve'i amarta uman koneh be-shvach kli, amai over* — if the craftsman owns the improvement, why would there be a transgression at all? The answer is that the case in the *baraita* must be one of ordinary *sechirut*, not true *kablanut*. Rav Mari brei de-Rav Kahana specifies: *be-garde de-sarvla* — the case involves a craftsman working on a thick winterweight cloth, and he was hired *le-rukei* — to beat and work the material in order to soften it, paid not for the finished product but at a rate of so many blows per unit of payment. That is *sechirut* by time or by unit of effort, not *kablanut* by the completed job. Therefore, one cannot derive anything from this case about true *kablanut*, and the question of whether *uman koneh be-shvach kli* applies — and whether *bal talin* extends to genuine contractor work — remains unresolved.

## Worker's Oath Takana

### Oath Inversion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂכִיר בִּזְמַנּוֹ נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל וְכוּ׳. שָׂכִיר אַמַּאי תַּקִּינוּ לֵיה רַבָּנַן (לְמִשְׁתְּבַע) [דְּמִשְׁתְּבַע] וְשָׁקֵיל?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **a hired laborer** requests payment **at** the proper **time** and the employer claims he already paid him, the laborer **takes an oath** that he did not receive his wages **and** then **receives** the wages from the employer. The Gemara asks: **Why** did **the Sages institute** for **a hired laborer,** who is the plaintiff, **to take an oath and receive** his wages, in opposition to the principle that in the case of a monetary dispute between two parties, the defendant takes an oath that he is not liable and thereby exempts himself from payment?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכוֹת גְּדוֹלוֹת שָׁנוּ כָּאן. הָנֵי הִלְכְתָא נִינְהוּ? הָנֵי תַּקָּנוֹת נִינְהוּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תַּקָּנוֹת גְּדוֹלוֹת שָׁנוּ כָּאן. "גְּדוֹלוֹת", מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא קְטַנּוֹת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: They taught great *halakhot* here.** The Gemara is puzzled by this choice of words: **Are these *halakhot*? They are ordinances** designed for the proper running of business transactions, not *halakhot* that apply to everyone at all times. The Gemara emends the above statement: **Rather, Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: They taught great ordinances here.** The Gemara is still unsatisfied with the terminology: Does the word **great** indicate **by inference that there are minor** ordinances? Which ordinances are considered of minor importance?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תַּקָּנוֹת קְבוּעוֹת שָׁנוּ כָּאן. שְׁבוּעָה דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת הִיא, וְעַקְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן לִשְׁבוּעָה דְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת וְשַׁדְיוּהָ אַשָּׂכִיר, מִשּׁוּם כְּדֵי חַיָּיו דְּשָׂכִיר. וּמִשּׁוּם כְּדֵי חַיָּיו דְּשָׂכִיר (מַפְסֵדְנָא) [מַפְסְדִינַן] לֵיהּ לְבַעַל הַבַּיִת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says: They taught fixed ordinances here** that are necessary for practical life. The reason is that taking the **oath is** actually the duty **of the employer, but the Sages transferred the oath of the employer and imposed it upon the hired laborer due to the livelihood of the hired laborer.** The laborer requires his wages to survive, and therefore if the employer is allowed to exempt himself by taking an oath, the laborer will be left with nothing. The Gemara asks: **And** simply **due to the livelihood of the hired laborer** should **we cause the employer** to **lose out?** If the employer is entitled to take an oath to exempt himself, why should he suffer due to the laborer's needs?

The *Mishnah* now introduces an entirely separate but related rabbinic institution. When a worker claims he was never paid and the employer disputes it, what happens in *Beis Din*? Under the normal rules governing monetary claims, this is a case of *kofer be-hakol* — a complete denial. The employer says he paid; the worker says he did not. Ordinarily, the one who makes the claim — the *motzi me-chavero* — bears the burden of proof, and since the worker cannot prove non-payment, he would walk away with nothing.

The rabbis enacted a *takana* inverting this: the worker may take a *shevuah* — an oath that he was not paid — and collect. The Gemara cites this in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel, who describes it as *halachot gedolot* — a formulation the Gemara immediately questions, since *halachot gedolot* suggests established law rather than a rabbinic innovation. The Gemara corrects itself: *takanata gedolah* — a great *takana*. But then a further objection: what does "great" mean? Does it imply there are also small *takanot*? Rav Papa finally settles on the formulation *takanata kevua* — an established and fundamental *takana*, one that forms a stable part of the economic order.

The *takana* is described as: *shevuah de-va'al habayit hi* — the oath properly belongs to the employer. He is the one being sued; he denied everything; ordinarily he would take the oath and be exempt. In some configurations, depending on whether there is partial admission (*modeh be-miktzat*), a *shevuah de-Oraita* could apply to the employer. Nevertheless, the rabbis uprooted the oath from the employer — *akruha rabanan li-shevuah me-va'al habayit ve-shadyuha a-sachir* — and transferred it to the worker.

### Economic Balance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעַל הַבַּיִת גּוּפֵיהּ נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּמִשְׁתְּבַע שָׂכִיר וְשָׁקֵיל, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיתַּגְרוּ לֵיהּ פּוֹעֲלִים. שָׂכִיר גּוּפֵיהּ נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּמִשְׁתְּבַע בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וְנִפְקַע, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיגְרוּהּ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת? עַל כֻּרְחֵיהּ אָגַר. שָׂכִיר נָמֵי בְּעַל כֻּרְחֵיהּ (אִיתְּגַר)!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **preferable for the employer himself that the hired laborer should take an oath and receive** his wages **so that laborers will hire themselves out to him** with the knowledge that their wages are secure. The Gemara asks: Why not argue the reverse, that it is **preferable for the hired laborer himself that the employer should take an oath and be exempt so that he should be hired?** If the terms of labor are too imposing, people will not hire laborers. The Gemara responds: The **employer must perforce** find a laborer to **hire.** The Gemara retorts: **A hired laborer, too, must perforce** allow himself to **be hired out.**

The Gemara asks: *mishum kedei chayav de-sachir nafasid lei le-va'al habayit* — just because the worker needs to make a living, we should make the employer lose? That seems to compromise justice in the name of social sympathy. Surely a *takana* cannot simply favor one party because they are more vulnerable.

The Gemara responds with a structural economic argument: *va'al habayit gufei nicha lei de-mishtava sachir ve-shakil ki heichi de-ligru lei po'alim* — the employer himself benefits from this *takana*. If workers cannot be protected by the oath, they will be afraid to hire themselves out, fearing they will be cheated of their wages. The employer needs workers, so from a systemic perspective the *takana* is in his interest as a class, even if in any particular case he finds it uncomfortable.

The Gemara then challenges this symmetrically: *sachir gufei nicha lei de-mishtava va'al habayit ve-ipatar ki heichi de-ligruhu va'al habayit* — one could make the exact same argument in reverse. Workers also benefit from a system in which employers are protected, because if employers feel too exposed, they will stop hiring. So why does the *takana* favor the worker rather than the employer?

The Gemara meets this with a decisive asymmetry: *lo, va'al habayit al korchei agar* — ultimately the employer has no choice but to hire. He has a field that must be plowed, a building that must go up. Protective labor laws will not stop him. So worker protection laws are ultimately not going to suppress hiring to a degree that harms workers. The symmetric argument *sachir al korchei ogar* — that the worker also has no choice but to seek employment — is equally valid, meaning neither side's economic compulsion resolves the question of which direction to tilt. The Gemara therefore sets aside the economic rationale entirely.

### Employer Distraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בַּעַל הַבַּיִת טָרוּד בְּפוֹעֲלִים הוּא. אִי הָכִי נִיתֵּב לֵיהּ בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה! כְּדֵי לְהָפִיס דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now retracts the previous explanation: **Rather, the employer is preoccupied with** many **laborers,** and it is more likely that he forgot and mistakenly believed that he already paid this laborer's wages. The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if it is reasonable that the employer forgot, we **should give** the laborer his wages **without** him taking **an oath,** as there are grounds to presume that the employer erred. The Gemara responds: The laborer takes an oath **in order to alleviate the concerns of the employer,** as, if he is not required to take an oath, the employer will feel that he has been cheated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתֵּב לֵיהּ בְּעֵדִים? טְרִיחָא לְהוּ מִילְּתָא. וְנִיתֵּב לֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא? שְׁנֵיהֶם רוֹצִים בְּהַקָּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **have** the employer instead **give him** his wages **in** the presence of **witnesses** each time, which would remove any uncertainty? The Gemara answers: **The matter** would be **an inconvenience to them** both if they needed to find witnesses before each payment. The Gemara asks: **But** why not **have** the employer **give him** the wages **at the outset,** before he starts working, when he is less preoccupied? The Gemara answers: **Both of them want** the payment to be in the form **of credit,** i.e., that the wages not be paid in advance. The employer prefers this arrangement in case he has no ready cash at his disposal, while the laborer also prefers to be paid at the end of the day so that he does not lose his money in the meantime.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ קָצַץ נָמֵי! אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא, אוּמָּן אוֹמֵר: שְׁתַּיִם קָצַצְתָּ לִי, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: לֹא קָצַצְתִּי לְךָ אֶלָּא אַחַת – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה. קְצִיצָה וַדַּאי מִידְכָּר דְּכִירִי לַהּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **even** if the dispute between them concerns **a fixed** amount of payment **as well,** the laborer should take an oath. **Why did we learn** in a *baraita*: If **the craftsman says: You fixed two** coins **for me** as my payment, **and the other,** i.e., the employer, **says: I fixed only one** coin **for you,** then **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** Why is it not assumed that the employer was preoccupied and forgot, as in the previous case? The Gemara answers: **The fixing** of wages is **certainly** an event that **people remember,** and there is no concern that the employer forgot how much he stipulated.

The Gemara then pivots to what it presents as the real justification: *va'al habayit tarud be-po'alav hu* — the employer is preoccupied with his workers. An employer managing many workers at the end of a busy day is genuinely distracted. He may have paid dozens of workers in rapid succession and simply cannot recall with certainty whether he paid any particular individual. The worker, by contrast, knows with certainty whether he was paid — it is his own pocket, his own pay.

The *takana* is therefore not simply a welfare measure for workers; it is a probabilistic judgment about where honest error is more likely to occur. We are not assuming employers are dishonest and workers are honest. We are recognizing that an employer can make an *honest* mistake about whether he paid, while the worker cannot honestly be confused about whether he received money.

Given this reasoning, the Gemara asks: *ei hachi nishklei belo shevuah* — if the presumption is that the employer simply forgot, why not let the worker collect without any oath at all? The Gemara gives two answers. First, the obvious one implicit in the discussion: opening unconstrained collection to anyone who claims non-payment is an invitation to dishonesty. The second answer the Gemara offers is more nuanced: *kedei le-hafis da'ato shel va'al habayit* — to settle the mind of the employer. When the worker swears, the employer can conclude that he must have simply misremembered, and the whole situation can be resolved without resentment and without the sense of injustice that would otherwise follow.

The Gemara raises one additional question: why not simply require that the employer always pay in the presence of witnesses, making non-payment provable? The Gemara dismisses this: *tircha de-milta* — it is an unreasonable burden on the employer to bring witnesses every time he pays any of his many workers. The alternative of pre-payment at the start of the day is similarly rejected: *shneihem rotzim be-hakafa* — both parties actually prefer the money to change hands at the end of the workday. The employer wants to delay disbursement, and the worker does not want to carry cash through a day of physical labor.

The Gemara then asks whether this *takana* should also extend to disputes about the *amount* agreed upon — *uman omer shtayim katzatzta li, va'al habayit omer lo katzatzti lecha ela achat*. The *baraita* says no: in a dispute about wages, the normal rule applies — *ha-motzi me-chavero alav ha-re'aya* — the worker bears the burden of proof and cannot use the oath to collect. The reason is that the entire rationale for the *takana* is the employer's distraction and the risk of honest error. *Katzatz vadai midkar dakar lei inshi* — people remember the salary they agreed upon. Forgetting whether you paid twenty different workers is plausible; forgetting the rate of pay you set is not. Since the rationale for the *takana* does not apply, neither does the *takana* itself.

## Oath Limitations

### Proper Time Period

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ עָבַר זְמַנּוֹ נָמֵי, אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: עָבַר זְמַנּוֹ – אֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל? חֲזָקָה: אֵין בַּעַל הַבַּיִת עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if the concern exists that the employer might have forgotten, then **even** if **his time had passed** for claiming his wages, the laborer should be entitled to take an oath and claim his wages. **Why did we learn** in the mishna: If **the time had passed** he does **not take an oath and receive** the wages? The Gemara answers: The reason in that case is that **a presumption** exists that **an employer does not** generally **violate** the prohibition **of delaying** payment of wages.

The *Mishnah* specifies: *sachir bizmano nishba ve-notel* — the worker may swear and collect only when he is still within *bizmano*, the proper time period. A day worker must make his claim during the following night; a night worker must make his claim during the following day. If he waits beyond that window, the oath mechanism is no longer available to him.

The Gemara also asks: if the employer's distraction is the reason for the *takana*, why should not the worker be able to make the oath and collect even after the time period has elapsed? *Afilu avar zmano nami* — even after the period is over, why should he lose this right?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ בַּעַל הַבַּיִת טָרוּד בְּפוֹעֲלָיו הוּא! הָנֵי מִילֵּי – מִקַּמֵּי דְּלִימְטְיֵיהּ זְמַן חִיּוּבֵיהּ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** that **the employer is preoccupied with his laborers?** The Gemara answers: **This statement** applies only **before the time of his obligation** to pay **arrives,** as it is possible that his preoccupation with other matters caused him to forget whether he had already paid him,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מְטָא זְמַן חִיּוּבֵיהּ – רָמֵי אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ וּמִידְּכַר.
>
> **Translation:** **but** when **the time of his obligation** to pay **arrives, he applies himself and remembers** all the details, so as not to violate the prohibition of delaying the payment of the laborer's wages.

The Gemara answers with a *chazakah*: *chazakah ein va'al habayit over mishum bal talin* — we presume that the employer did not transgress *bal talin*. Once the time period has passed, and the employer says he paid, we presume he is telling the truth — because a person who had a *mitzvah* obligation to pay during that window presumably would have focused and paid, rather than allow himself to transgress. During the time period itself, we understand that he may be distracted; once the obligation has crystallized and the threat of transgression is immediate, *rami anafshei u-midkar* — he focuses and remembers.

The Gemara immediately recognizes the tension: but we just said the employer is *tarud*! The Gemara resolves this: *hanei milei mikamei de-limti zman chiyuva* — the distraction is a problem *before* the moment of obligation arrives. Once the clock has run out and transgression is imminent, the employer sharpens his attention and pays. The earlier distraction was at a moment when the obligation had not yet fully pressed; at the final hour it does.

### Witness Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי שָׂכִיר עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תִּגְזֹל"? הָתָם תְּרֵי חֲזָקֵי, הָכָא חֲדָא חֲזָקָה. גַּבֵּי בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אִיכָּא תְּרֵי חֲזָקֵי: חֲדָא דְּאֵין בַּעַל הַבַּיִת עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם "בַּל תָּלִין", וַחֲדָא דְּאֵין שָׂכִיר מְשַׁהֶא שְׂכָרוֹ. וְהָכָא חֲדָא חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why would one rely upon the presumption that the employer would not transgress? **But is the hired laborer** suspected of **violating** the **prohibition of stealing?** The Gemara replies: **There,** concerning the credibility of an employer, there are **two presumptions,** whereas **here,** concerning the credibility of a laborer, there is only **one presumption.** The Gemara explains: **Concerning** the credibility of **an employer there are** the following **two presumptions: One** is **that the employer does not violate** the **prohibition of delaying** payment of wages, **and** the other **one** is **that a hired laborer does not delay** the request for **his wages. But here,** concerning the credibility of the laborer, there is only **one presumption,** i.e., that the laborer does not violate the prohibition of stealing.

This leads to a new question: if the employee does make his demand during the proper time period but cannot collect immediately and brings the case to *Beis Din* afterward, what is his standing? The Gemara asks: *vechi sachir over mishum bal tigzol* — are we now saying that after the time period has passed and we assume the employer paid, the worker who still claims non-payment is a conscious thief? If the employer is presumed honest, the worker must be lying — and the worker *knows* whether he was paid.

The Gemara resolves this with a two-against-one analysis: *terei chazakei ve-hacha chad chazakah*. Against the worker there are two presumptions: first, that the employer would not have transgressed *bal talin* and therefore paid; and second, that no worker who has truly not been paid would wait so long to press his claim — *ein sachir masheh scharo* — a worker does not sit on unpaid wages. If he had genuinely not been paid, he would have demanded payment urgently. These two presumptions work together against the worker's claim.

In favor of the worker there is only one presumption: that we do not assume he would consciously lie and consciously steal — *bal tigzol*. One presumption against two means the worker cannot use the oath mechanism after the time period has passed. If the employer makes a partial admission (*modeh be-miktzat*), a *shevuah de-Oraita* might apply, but the *takana* allowing the worker to swear and collect does not extend to this situation.

### Delay After Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁתְּבָעוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה נִשְׁבָּע וְנוֹטֵל. וְהָא (קָתָבְעוֹ לְקַמַּן) [קָא תָבַע לֵיהּ קַמַּן]? אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: שֶׁתְּבָעוֹ בִּזְמַנּוֹ. וְדִלְמָא לְבָתַר הָכִי פְּרַע? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁתְּבָעוֹ כָּל זְמַנּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **If there are witnesses** who testify **that he claimed** the money from **him, he takes an oath and receives** the money. The Gemara asks: **But** what need is there for witnesses that he lodged a claim, when **he is claiming** it from **him in front of us? Rabbi Asi said:** The *tanna* is referring to witnesses **that** testified that he **claimed** it from **him at its** proper **time.** The Gemara challenges: Even if the laborer claimed the money at the proper time, **perhaps** the employer **paid** him **afterward. Abaye said:** The witnesses testify **that he claimed** it from **him the entire time,** i.e., from the time he completed his labor until the end of that day.

The *Mishnah* concludes: *im yesh edim she-teva'o harei ze nishba ve-notel* — if there are witnesses that the demand was made during the proper time period, the worker may still swear and collect even if the court case comes later.

The Gemara asks what the witnesses actually establish. If the worker is demanding in front of *Beis Din* right now, the demand is self-evident; no witnesses are needed. Rav Ashi clarifies: the witnesses testify that the demand was made *during* the proper time period — this is not explicit in the *Mishnah* but is the necessary implication. The *Mishnah* is teaching that timely demand, proven by witnesses, preserves the right to make the oath even if collection is delayed.

The Gemara then raises a further problem: *u-dilma levatar hachi para* — perhaps the employer paid after the demand was made and before the case came to court? The Gemara had established that once the time window closes and the obligation crystallizes, the employer focuses and pays. So between the witnessed demand and the later court appearance, perhaps payment occurred. Abaye answers: *she-teva'u kol zmano* — the witnesses testify that the demand was renewed throughout the entire time period, not just at one moment. The worker kept demanding, right up until the end of the window, so that no gap exists in which unwitnessed payment could have occurred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְעוֹלָם לָא פָּרַע לֵיהּ?! אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר עוּקְבָא: כְּנֶגֶד אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁל תְּבִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** is it **always** assumed that the employer **did not pay** the laborer? Why does the fact that he claimed his money at the proper time mean that his claim against his employer is always accepted? **Rav Ḥama bar Ukva said:** The *tanna* means that he is given another day **corresponding to that day of** his **claim** during which the laborer can claim that he has not been paid.

But if the demand continued throughout the entire period, there is a final question: *u-le'olam* — can the worker then go indefinitely and make his oath two years later? Rav Chama bar Ukva answers: *kineged oto ha-yom shel tevi'ah* — the worker gets the equivalent of that day of demand. If he made his demand and can prove it with witnesses through the end of the time period, he has one additional day in which to collect via the oath mechanism. Beyond that, the *takana* expires.
