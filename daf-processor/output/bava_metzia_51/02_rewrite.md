# Bava Metzia 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Stages of Ona'ah

### Three Stages Overview

The Gemara's discussion on this daf centers on the three stages of *ona'ah* and the consequences — both practical and temporal — that attend each stage. The Mishnah, as read by the Gemara, identifies three distinct zones of pricing deviation, though it bears noting that this tripartite scheme is not entirely explicit in the Mishnah's own language.

The first zone is a deviation of less than one-sixth of the market price. This range falls under the rubric of *mechila* — it represents a tolerable variation in price, one that lies within the normal fluctuation of the market. The second zone is a deviation of exactly one-sixth. This constitutes *ona'ah* proper, and the conventional understanding is that the cheated party is given a choice: either receive back the amount of the overcharge, or opt for *bitul mekach* — cancellation of the sale entirely. The third zone is a deviation exceeding one-sixth. This is *bitul mekach*, in which either side may retract. There is, as will become apparent, some irony in this last provision: a seller who cheats more egregiously finds that his overreaching actually expands the available remedies, a point the Gemara does not pass over without notice.

### Ad She-Yeira Principle

The Mishnah introduces explicitly, in the context of *ona'ah* — that middle case — the principle of *ad she-yeira*: the cheated party retains the right to seek redress only for as long as it would reasonably take for that person to show the purchased item to merchants or relatives and receive an assessment. Once that window has passed without action, the right is forfeited.

### Time Clock on All Stages

The question now engaging the Gemara is whether this temporal limitation — the *ad she-yeira* clock — applies exclusively to the middle *ona'ah* category, or whether it also governs the outer two categories. Does *mechila* operate automatically the moment the transaction is concluded, regardless of any subsequent action by the parties? Or does *mechila* also have a clock, such that the undercharged buyer could potentially seek recovery within the *ad she-yeira* window? And symmetrically: is *bitul mekach* available *le'olam* — indefinitely — given the severity of the overcharge, or does that too expire once the *ad she-yeira* period has elapsed?

---

## Rabbi Tarfon Story

### Rabbi Tarfon Expands

To address these questions, the Gemara turns to the well-known episode involving Rabbi Tarfon and the merchants of his locale. Rabbi Tarfon's position, as recalled here, was to expand the definition of *ona'ah*: rather than a sixth, he held that overcharges up to a third of the market price would still fall within the *ona'ah* category, rather than triggering *bitul mekach*. The Gemara tracks the reactions of the merchant community to Rabbi Tarfon's rulings in order to draw inferences about the underlying structure of these categories.

### Merchants' Initial Joy

When Rabbi Tarfon initially announced this position, the merchants were pleased. The reason, as the Gemara will clarify, is that they preferred the *ona'ah* classification over *bitul mekach*. To understand why requires first understanding what the Gemara will later establish: that *bitul mekach*, according to the Rabbanan, is *le'olam chozer* — the right to cancel the sale persists indefinitely. Against that backdrop, the merchants preferred that overcharges in the range between one-sixth and one-third be reclassified as *ona'ah*, since *ona'ah* carries only a limited temporal window — the *ad she-yeira* period — rather than the indefinitely open option of *bitul mekach*. A time-limited right of redress is clearly preferable, from the seller's perspective, to one that never expires.

### Kol Ha-Yom Reversal

Rabbi Tarfon then issued a second ruling: whereas the Rabbanan's *ad she-yeira* standard amounted to something like a few hours — the time practically needed to consult a merchant or relative — he extended that window to *kol ha-yom*, the entire day. At that point, the merchants reversed course and returned to the position of the Rabbanan (*chazru l'divrei chachamim*). This reversal requires explanation, since even *kol ha-yom* is shorter than *le'olam*, and one would think the merchants would still prefer Rabbi Tarfon's bounded window over the Rabbanan's indefinite *le'olam* for *bitul mekach*.

### Mechila Automatic or Tim…

The Gemara now examines two possible readings of the merchants' behavior. One reading suggests that *mechila* is *not* automatic — that even the less-than-a-sixth category carries a clock, so that the buyer, within the *ad she-yeira* window, could potentially reclaim even a small undercharge. Under this reading, Rabbi Tarfon's expansion of *ona'ah* to cover up to a third simply shifts the clock's application to a wider range of overcharges, and the merchants' preference and subsequent reversal make intuitive sense as a response to the length of that window.

The alternative reading is that *mechila* is automatic. Under this reading, Rabbi Tarfon is not expanding the zone of *mechila*; rather, he is redefining what the Rabbanan call *ona'ah* — specifically, what for the Rabbanan triggers *ona'ah* rights at exactly one-sixth, Rabbi Tarfon treats as still within the *mechila* zone. The entire range that the Rabbanan classify as *ona'ah* (from one-sixth upward to one-third) Rabbi Tarfon instead calls *ona'ah* only starting at one-third. The zone the Rabbanan call *ona'ah* is, for Rabbi Tarfon, redefined as *mechila*, which is automatic. The argument then becomes focused only on the behavior at the *ona'ah* threshold itself — which, at whatever level it is set, carries the *ad she-yeira* clock.

### Mechila Automatic Soluti…

After considerable analysis, the Gemara proposes a resolution: it is possible that *mechila* is automatic — that is, below-threshold deviations require no action and are simply absorbed. What changes between Rabbi Tarfon and the Rabbanan is not the automatic nature of *mechila* but the placement of the threshold itself. For the Rabbanan, a deviation of exactly one-sixth is *ona'ah*; for Rabbi Tarfon, a deviation of exactly one-sixth falls within *mechila*. It is this definitional shift that motivated the merchants' initial enthusiasm and subsequent reversal.

## Unresolved Debates

### Two Key Questions

At this point in the sugya, the Gemara crystallizes the two questions that remain genuinely unresolved. First: is *mechila* automatic, or does it too require the passage of the *ad she-yeira* period before it takes effect? Second: is *bitul mekach* available *le'olam* — indefinitely — or is it also bounded by *ad she-yeira*?

### Why Prefer Rabbanan

A further analytical difficulty now presents itself. The Gemara poses the question this way: *de-bitul mekach le-rabanan le'olam chozer* — if *bitul mekach* according to the Rabbanan is indeed *le'olam*, then we understand why the merchants initially preferred Rabbi Tarfon's position. They preferred having a time-limited *ona'ah* right over an indefinite *bitul mekach* right — any bounded window beats *le'olam*. But then Rabbi Tarfon declared *kol ha-yom*, and they switched back. The question is: if *bitul mekach* is *le'olam* and *kol ha-yom* is plainly shorter than *le'olam*, why would the merchants ever prefer the Rabbanan's framework? The seller who is covered by Rabbanan's rules still faces the possibility that a buyer with a legitimate *bitul mekach* claim can return the merchandise indefinitely. Why would the merchants prefer *that* to Rabbi Tarfon's *kol ha-yom*?

The Gemara presses this even further. If *bitul mekach* is also subject to *ad she-yeira* — just like *ona'ah* — then the merchants' initial preference for Rabbi Tarfon makes no sense either. If both categories carry the same temporal limitation, there is no reason to prefer *ona'ah* over *bitul mekach*. If anything, *ona'ah* gives the cheated buyer *more* options (both money-back and cancellation), making it, if anything, worse for the seller. So the merchants' initial joy only makes sense if *bitul mekach* under the Rabbanan is *le'olam*, not *ad she-yeira*.

### Bitul Mekach Analysis

The Gemara resolves the problem of why the merchants eventually preferred the Rabbanan even after Rabbi Tarfon announced *kol ha-yom* through two combined observations. First: *bitul mekach lo shachi'ach* — instances of *bitul mekach*-level overcharging are not common. A seller does not typically overcharge by more than a sixth in the first place. So the practical exposure to an indefinite *bitul mekach* right is limited. Second, and this is the point attributed to Rashi though it goes somewhat beyond what the Gemara states explicitly: the practical difference between *kol ha-yom* and *le'olam* is not as large as it might seem. The number of buyers who would exercise their *bitul mekach* right in the period between the end of the day and the indefinite future is relatively small. By contrast, the difference between a few hours (*ad she-yeira*) and a full day (*kol ha-yom*) is quite significant — many more buyers will avail themselves of a full-day window than a few-hour window. Once Rabbi Tarfon declared *kol ha-yom*, the marginal benefit of being under the Rabbanan's framework (where *ona'ah* is limited to a few hours) outweighs the marginal cost of the Rabbanan's *le'olam* exposure on the rare *bitul mekach* case. The conclusion, then, is that both questions — whether *mechila* is automatic and whether *bitul mekach* is *le'olam* — remain genuinely open after this passage. The Gemara could argue it either way.

## Rava's Ruling

### Rava's Three Categories

Having established the theoretical framework through the Rabbi Tarfon analysis, the Gemara now turns to Rava's practical ruling. Rava states: *hilchata* — the operative law is as follows. *Pachot mi-shtus* — less than a sixth — *nikneh mekach*: the sale stands. Both Rashi and Tosafos understand this to mean that *mechila* in this range is automatic. This resolves the first of the two open questions, at least as a matter of practical *halacha*: below-threshold deviations are simply absorbed and require no action by either party. *Yeter al shtus* — more than a sixth — *bitul mekach*: the sale is subject to cancellation. *Shtus* — exactly a sixth — *kana u-machzir ona'ah*: the sale is valid but the overcharge must be returned.

One observes that Rava's formulation of the *ona'ah* case — *kana u-machzir ona'ah*, he acquired and returns the overcharge — sounds, at first reading, as though it forecloses the option of *bitul mekach* and leaves only the money-back remedy. This apparent implication is significant: it prompted Rashi to conclude that Rava is ruling here in accordance with Rabbi Natan, who holds that the cheated party's only remedy in the *ona'ah* case is return of the overcharge, with no option to invoke *bitul mekach*.

Tosafos disagrees sharply. Tosafos argues that Rava would never issue a *psak* aligned with Rabbi Natan against the view of the Rabbanan, whose position clearly allows the cheated party both options. Rava's formulation, Tosafos maintains, is simply highlighting the *distinctive* remedy available in the *ona'ah* case — the ability to recover the overpayment while retaining the sale — rather than excluding the additional option of *bitul mekach*.

Regarding *bitul mekach* and its temporal scope, Rashi reads the Gemara's subsequent language — *ve-zeh va-zeh be-khedei she-yeira'eh le-tagar o li-krovo* — as establishing that both *bitul mekach* and *ona'ah* are subject to the *ad she-yeira* clock. This is counterintuitive: one might have thought that a grossly overcharged buyer retains his right of cancellation indefinitely, given the severity of the wrong. And yet the Gemara, read this way, limits even *bitul mekach* to the *ad she-yeira* window. A policy rationale exists — allowing indefinite cancellation rights creates serious uncertainty about the permanence of past transactions — but it remains a striking result. It also confirms that *bitul mekach* is not automatic: it requires one of the parties to actively invoke the cancellation right within that window.

### Ona'ah vs. Bitul Mekach

With the framework now settled — *mechila* is automatic; both *ona'ah* and *bitul mekach* are subject to *ad she-yeira*; the principal distinction between *ona'ah* and *bitul mekach* lies elsewhere — the Gemara articulates what the remaining differences actually are. In the *ona'ah* case, the right of redress belongs exclusively to the cheated party: only the party who was overcharged or undercharged may initiate. In the *bitul mekach* case, the Gemara stated earlier — *sheneihem chozrim* — both parties may initiate the cancellation. This, too, produces a note of irony: the seller who overcharges by a greater amount thereby grants himself a right he would not have had with a smaller overcharge. As Tosafos observes, one might argue that the seller's right to initiate *bitul mekach* is not truly unilateral — it may only arise in response to the buyer's complaint, rather than as a freestanding seller's option — but that point remains a matter of discussion.

### Rabbi Natan vs. Rabbi

The Gemara now introduces a *baraita* that directly engages the dispute between Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The *baraita* states, in Rabbi Natan's name: *shtus kana u-machzir ona'ah* — at exactly one-sixth, the sale stands and the overcharge is returned — with no mention of an option to cancel. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, by contrast, formulates the rule as follows: the cheated party, whichever side that is, has the option — *ratza omer lo ten li mikchi* (give me back the object I sold) or *ten li mah she-onitani* (pay me the amount by which I was cheated) — and this choice is available within the *ad she-yeira* period.

### Rashi vs. Tosafos

The debate between Rashi and Tosafos, already noted above, crystallizes here. Rashi holds that Rava's ruling aligns with Rabbi Natan: in the *ona'ah* case, the only remedy is return of the overcharge. Tosafos maintains that Rava rules like Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, and that the cheated party retains the option of *bitul mekach* even in the exact-one-sixth case. Both Rashi and Tosafos agree, however, that *bitul mekach* — whatever its scope — is subject to the *ad she-yeira* clock rather than being available indefinitely. The difference between the two positions, under this reading, is precisely whether *ona'ah* includes the *bitul mekach* option: Rashi says no, Tosafos says yes.

## Buyer-Seller Asymmetry

### Rav Nachman's Rule

Having concluded the theoretical analysis, the Gemara introduces a ruling from Rav Nachman that upends a natural intuition. The Mishnah's discussion of *ad she-yeira* implicitly treats buyer and seller symmetrically, as if the time limit applies equally to whoever was cheated. Rav Nachman rules that this is not so: *lo shanu ela loke'ach* — the temporal limitation of *ad she-yeira* applies only to the buyer. *Aval mocher le'olam chozer* — but a seller who was cheated (that is, a seller who undersold) may seek redress indefinitely.

This is striking in both directions. First, one might have expected the seller — the party who is professionally engaged in commerce and presumably knows market prices — to have either no right of redress or a more restricted one. Yet here the seller is granted a perpetual right. Second, the Gemara attempts to prove Rav Nachman's rule from the merchants' behavior in the Rabbi Tarfon episode, reasoning as follows: if the seller has an indefinitely longer period to assert rights than the buyer, then the sellers (merchants) would naturally prefer whichever policy framework gives buyers the shortest possible window — since the sellers retain their own rights regardless. If the seller's rights were symmetric with the buyer's, the merchants would have no preference between a shorter and longer *ad she-yeira* window, since both help and hurt equally. The very fact that they preferred the shorter window implies an asymmetry in the temporal rules, which confirms Rav Nachman's position.

The Gemara, however, does not accept this proof. It responds: *tagrei Lod lo shachi'ach de-ta'u* — merchants such as those of Lod simply do not make pricing errors. A sophisticated commercial community is almost never in the position of having undersold, so the asymmetry in the rules barely affects them. Even if the seller's rights were symmetric with the buyer's, the merchants would still prefer a shorter buyer's window because they almost never occupy the seller-cheated position. The proof is therefore inconclusive.

### Rami Bar Chama Story

The practical application of Rav Nachman's ruling is illustrated immediately by a story. The innkeeper of Rami bar Chama had sold wine and, upon reflection, concluded that he had undersold it — that he had charged significantly less than market value. He was found sitting in distress. Rami bar Chama urged him to retract the sale, but the innkeeper replied that it was too late: more time had elapsed than the *ad she-yeira* window would allow. Rami bar Chama directed him to appear before Rav Nachman. Rav Nachman ruled: *be-khedei she-yeira'eh la-loke'ach* — the time limit of *ad she-yeira* applies to the buyer; *aval mocher le'olam chozer* — the seller may retract indefinitely. The innkeeper's right of redress was therefore intact.

### Reason for Asymmetry

The Gemara now explains the logic behind this asymmetry. The buyer holds the purchased object in hand. *Loke'ach mikcho be-yado* — wherever he goes, he can display it, show it to people, and receive an assessment. People will quickly tell him whether he overpaid or not. Because discovery of the overcharge comes readily to the buyer, he is given only a limited window: once he has had a reasonable opportunity to discover the problem and has not acted, his right expires. The seller, by contrast, is no longer holding the object. *Mocher de-lo nakit mikcho be-yadei* — the seller has transferred the goods and cannot easily show them to others for assessment. In the case of wine, for instance, the seller would need to find wine of comparable quality and get a reliable valuation — a process that may take considerable time and effort. *Ad de-rami lei zvinta ke-zvinta* — until he encounters a comparable item and can determine definitively whether he was cheated or not, there is no reliable way for him to know. Accordingly, no time limit is imposed on the seller.

The result is counterintuitive: precisely because the seller is the party with professional knowledge of his goods, the law extends him more generosity, not less. His professional knowledge relates to his own stock, but once he parts with it he lacks the buyer's easy ability to get a quick external assessment. The asymmetry of information after the transaction, not before, drives the rule.

## Gaming the System

### Buyer's Manipulation

The foregoing analysis opens a theoretical opening for strategic behavior. The Gemara presents a case in which a seller announces goods for six units — but would accept five and a half. A buyer, aware of this, reasons as follows: if he pays five and a half, the deviation from the true value of five is only half a unit, falling within the *mechila* zone — the sale is final and he cannot recover anything. But if he intentionally pays the full six — putting himself deliberately into the *ona'ah* zone — he will be entitled to recover the entire one-unit overcharge, effectively purchasing the goods for five. He therefore pays six and brings the seller to *din* to recover the overage.

This is a striking passage. The buyer is not being overcharged inadvertently; he is engineering his own overcharge in order to access a legal remedy that would otherwise be unavailable to him. The law of *ona'ah*, designed to protect the unwitting victim of price manipulation, is being turned into an instrument of strategic advantage by the very party it was meant to protect.

### Rav Chisda Limits Trick

Rava — and a parallel case comes before Rav Chisda with different goods (rings valued at fifty but announced at sixty, with the seller willing to accept fifty-five) — rules that this strategy is valid only under specific conditions. *Lo shanu ela be-loke'ach min ha-tagar* — the *ona'ah* rules and their strategic exploitation apply only when the buyer purchases from a merchant. *Aval be-loke'ach mi-ba'al ha-bayit ein lo alav ona'ah* — but when the purchase is from a homeowner selling personal effects, the rules of *ona'ah* do not apply at all, and the manipulation cannot be employed.

---

## Homeowner Exemption

### Personal Items No Ona'ah

The ruling introduced at the close of the previous section — that *ona'ah* does not apply when purchasing from a *ba'al ha-bayit* selling personal goods — is now examined more carefully. When a homeowner sells items from his own household for personal reasons, those items are not priced according to standard market valuations in the same way that a merchant's inventory would be. The *ona'ah* framework, which presupposes a market price against which overcharge or undercharge can be measured, does not map cleanly onto personal property sales.

### Commodity vs. Personal

A challenge is immediately raised from a Mishnah: *ke-sheim she-ona'ah le-hedyot kach ona'ah le-tagar* — just as *ona'ah* applies to a layperson, so it applies to a merchant. Doesn't *hedyot* — layperson — include a *ba'al ha-bayit*? If so, the homeowner exemption appears to contradict the Mishnah directly. Rav Chisda resolves the difficulty: the Mishnah is speaking of *tzadrayta* — canvas bags and comparable commodities. When a homeowner sells items of this type — fungible goods with a clear market price to which the homeowner has no personal attachment — the standard *ona'ah* rules apply. The homeowner is not exempt merely by virtue of being a homeowner; the exemption attaches only to genuinely personal items.

### Sentimental Premium

The rationale for the exemption is now articulated. *De-yakrei lei* — these personal items are dear to the owner. *Lo mzabin lehu ela bi-dmei yeitirei* — a person does not sell his own personal belongings except at a premium above market value. This is simply the nature of personal attachment: the seller is not implicitly representing that the price corresponds to the market rate for used goods of that category. The buyer, by engaging in the transaction, accepts the seller's subjective valuation. Rashi explicitly illustrates this with household effects — objects like the family's dishes, which carry sentimental significance wholly uncorrelated with their resale value. Both Rashi's and the broader discussion's point is the same: when personal attachment is present, the premise of *ona'ah* — that there is an objective market price from which the deviation can be measured — does not hold in the same way.

## Merchant vs. Homeown (II)

### Equal Rules for All

The Mishnah that follows states explicitly: *echad ha-loke'ach ve-echad ha-mocher yesh bahem ona'ah* — both the buyer and the seller are subject to *ona'ah*. The additional clause, *ke-sheim she-ona'ah le-hedyot kach ona'ah le-tagar*, establishes that the rules apply equally regardless of whether the party is a merchant or a layperson. As already clarified above, this formulation applies to commercial goods and commodities, not to personal items — which fall under the exemption established in the prior section. The Mishnah is affirming the symmetric application of *ona'ah* law across both sides of the transaction and across different categories of seller, subject to the personal-items qualification.

---

## Biblical Ona'ah Source

### Vayikra Source

The Gemara now traces the *ona'ah* prohibition to its biblical source, citing the verse in Vayikra 25:14: *ve-chi timkeru mimkar la-amitecha al tonu o kano mi-yad amitecha* — "When you sell a sale to your fellow, or buy from the hand of your fellow, do not cheat one another." The verse addresses both the sale and the purchase, which allows the Gemara to derive that both the seller and the buyer are prohibited from cheating the other party.

### Why Both Restricted

The Gemara develops the derivation: from the prohibition on the seller (*al tonu*) alone, one might have inferred only that sellers cannot cheat buyers, since sellers know their business (*kaim lei be-zvinthei*). But the unsophisticated buyer, who is not a professional, might be thought to fall outside the prohibition — perhaps we would say he lacks the knowledge to be held responsible for cheating the seller. The verse therefore adds the clause addressing the buyer (*o kano*) to establish that the buyer too is prohibited from cheating the seller.

Conversely, from the buyer's prohibition alone, one might have concluded that only buyers are restricted, since the buyer is in the privileged position of having acquired something of value. The seller, by contrast, has parted with a concrete object in exchange for money. The Gemara cites a folk saying: *zavnit ovdit* — you sold, you lost. Money dissipates; the object was something permanent and tangible. Without the explicit prohibition on the seller as well, one might have thought the seller is adequately punished by having parted with his goods and need not be further constrained. The Torah therefore addresses both parties to make clear that neither may exploit the other.

### Economic Philosophy

This reasoning reflects a broader economic sensibility embedded in the Gemara's discussion. The observation that cash dissipates while objects endure — *zavnes kanisa* (you bought, you acquired something), *zavnit ovdit* (you sold, the value is gone) — carries a particular resonance in an agrarian or semi-commercial context. The Gemara implicitly views holding physical goods as a more stable form of wealth than holding money. This is somewhat in tension with the Gemara's evident awareness of active commercial life, a tension that is difficult to resolve fully, but the underlying attitude appears to reflect a society in which the permanence of objects was felt more concretely than the reliability of liquid currency.

## Merchant vs. Homeown (II)

### Rabbi Yehuda's Exception

The Mishnah has stated that *ona'ah* applies equally to merchants and laypeople, but Rabbi Yehuda dissents: *ein ona'ah le-tagar* — a merchant has no *ona'ah* claim. The surface reading is that because the party is a professional merchant, they cannot avail themselves of *ona'ah* protection. The implication — that the professional should know his business — seems self-evident and intuitive.

### Safsar Definition

Rav Nachman, citing Rav, clarifies who falls within Rabbi Yehuda's category of merchant for this purpose: *be-tagar safsar shanu* — the teaching refers to a *safsar*, a broker or middleman who buys goods and resells them immediately, essentially engaging in arbitrage. Such a person knows exactly what he paid and exactly what he is charging. The margin is explicit and deliberate. This is distinct from a farmer or artisan who sells his own produce and may have genuine uncertainty about market pricing.

### Merchant Logic

The reasoning is spelled out plainly: *meida yada kama zavin ve-kama shavei* — he knows exactly how much he paid and what the goods are worth. If he nonetheless sold at a price below what he paid, *ha-de-zavin hachi mishum de-itztarichu lei zuzei achinei* — he did so because he needed liquid cash. Having gotten the cash he needed, he cannot now return and claim that the undersale constituted *ona'ah*. A sale made deliberately at a loss for business reasons is not a case of being cheated; it is a case of *mechila*, and the seller who made that choice voluntarily cannot later reverse course and claim victimhood.

---

## Rav Ashi's View

### Merchant More Rights

Rav Ashi offers a diametrically opposite reading of Rabbi Yehuda's ruling. *Mai ein le-tagar ona'ah? Eino be-torat ona'ah* — what does it mean that the merchant has no *ona'ah*? It means that the standard *ona'ah* framework does not apply to the merchant in the ordinary way. But the direction of the departure is the reverse of what Rabbi Yehuda's plain language might suggest. *She-afilu pachot mi-kdei ona'ah chozer* — even an undercharge that falls *below* the normal *ona'ah* threshold entitles the merchant to seek recovery. A deviation that would normally fall within the *mechila* zone — automatic forgiveness for the other party — does not operate as automatic *mechila* for a merchant seller. *Ein mechila* — there is no *mechila* for the professional.

### Parnassah Protection

Rashi explains the rationale: this is the merchant's *parnassah* — his livelihood. Because commerce is his means of sustenance, the law grants him heightened protection for his pricing. A deviation that a homeowner would simply absorb cannot be imposed on the professional, who is entitled to the full value of his goods. This reading transforms Rabbi Yehuda's statement from a restriction on the merchant into an expansion of his rights — a complete reversal of the intuitive reading. The Gemara notes that a *baraita* was taught in accordance with the first interpretation, that of Rav Nachman and Rav: Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning is that the merchant is expert in his pricing (*mipnei she-hu baki*), and therefore has diminished grounds to claim ignorance when he undercharges.

## Attribution Debate

### Mishna Attribution

The Gemara now addresses a structural tension between the Mishnah and the *baraita* quoted earlier. The Mishnah formulates the *ona'ah* case from the perspective of the buyer being cheated: the buyer is the party with the options. The *baraita* of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, quoted at the bottom of the previous *amud*, formulates the case from the perspective of the seller being cheated: *lo yad mocher al ha-elyona* — the seller has the options when he was the cheated party. Rabbi Elazar expressed uncertainty about how to reconcile these two sources. Rava initially suggested that the Mishnah could be aligned with Rabbi Natan — but this required modifying Rabbi Natan to allow the option of *bitul mekach* in addition to return of the overcharge, a modification of his stated position.

### Rava's Solution

Rava then advances what he presents as the straightforward resolution: *le'olam Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi hi* — the Mishnah is entirely consistent with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The Mishnah gave one example — the buyer being cheated — and the *baraita* supplied the complementary example — the seller being cheated. *U-mai de-shayer be-matnisin ka-mefaresh bi-vraisa* — what was left implicit in the Mishnah was spelled out explicitly in the *baraita*. The two are not in tension; they are complementary illustrations of the same rule.

### Rav Ashi's Proof

Rav Ashi adds a textual proof that this reading is compelled by the Mishnah itself. *De-kasani echad ha-loke'ach ve-echad ha-mocher* — the Mishnah opens by stating that *ona'ah* applies equally to the buyer and the seller. Having stated this explicitly at the outset, the Mishnah then proceeds to elaborate with the buyer's case. The opening clause establishes that the seller's case is equally governed by the same rule; the illustrative example that follows does not exhaust the rule's scope. *She-ma mina she-hu ha-din le-mocher* — one may infer that the law is the same for the seller. *Shema mina* — the inference stands.
