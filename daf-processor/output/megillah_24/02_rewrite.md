# Megillah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ezer K'negdo & Leadership

### Ezer K'negdo

There is a beautiful explanation from the Alter mi-Kelm on the words *e'eseh lo ezer k'negdo* — "I will make for him a helper corresponding to him." We know the famous Rashi that says *zachah ezer, lo zachah k'negdo*: if a man is worthy, his wife is a helper, but if he is not worthy, she is against him. The Alter explains that being *k'negdo* — being against him — is itself a form of *ezer*, a type of help. If a person is heading in the wrong direction, if he is about to fall into a pit, then the greatest help someone can provide is to stand *k'negdo*, to stand in his way and stop him from making a mistake. This is the goal of a spouse: to help the other person grow, and sometimes that help comes precisely in the form of opposition.

### Leadership Individuality

This principle carries a powerful broader lesson. We might think that a leader is someone who has a vision and expects everyone to follow it. But the true Jewish conception of leadership is of someone able to relate to each person individually, according to their unique temperament and unique soul.

### Leader & Inheritance

We see this reflected in the juxtaposition of two passages in the *Torah*. It is immediately after Moshe's request that Hashem appoint a leader for the Jewish people that the *Torah* teaches us the laws of inheritance. Why are the laws of inheritance connected to the appointment of a new leader? Because just as an inheritance is passed down from parents to children, so too leadership must be transmitted in a particular way. The leader must be someone who recognizes the unique qualities of each and every person, just as a parent recognizes the unique qualities of each of their children.

### Bnot Tzelofchad

It is specifically in this context that we encounter the story of the *Bnot Tzelofchad*, the daughters of Tzelofchad, who come before Moshe and declare that their father passed away without a son, and they wish to inherit his portion in *Eretz Yisrael*. Moshe brings the matter before Hashem, and Hashem responds: *ken Bnot Tzelofchad dovrot* — the daughters of Tzelofchad are correct; they shall receive their father's portion. This demonstrates that the leader of the Jewish people must be willing to listen even to those who may seem to have a smaller voice in the community, and to recognize the justice of their claims.

### Yehoshua Successor

Hashem then instructs Moshe to appoint Yehoshua Bin Nun as his successor. Why Yehoshua? Because he was the one who remained constantly with Moshe, who served him, and who came to understand the unique qualities of the Jewish people. Yehoshua is described as *ish asher ruach bo* — a man in whom there is spirit — and Rashi explains that this means he was able to relate to the spirit of each and every individual. This is the essence of Jewish leadership: the ability to recognize the individuality of each person while still leading the entire community forward toward a common goal.

## Eruv Tzfat

### Tzfat Streets

We turn now to the *Noda BiYehuda Tinyana*, *Orach Chaim*, siman 13, where the *Noda BiYehuda* addresses a question concerning the city of Tzfat. He was asked whether it is permissible to establish an *eruv* in Tzfat. The city is built on a mountain, and its streets are extraordinarily steep.

### Mefulash Karmelis

The question centered on whether the steepness of the streets would prevent the area from being classified as a *reshus harabbim*. For a domain to qualify as a *reshus harabbim*, it must be *mefulash* — it must constitute a straight, open thoroughfare. The *Noda BiYehuda* rules that since Tzfat is situated on a mountain and its streets are so steep, they cannot be considered *mefulash*. Absent the quality of *mefulash*, the streets do not attain the status of a *reshus harabbim d'Oraisa* — and this is true even setting aside the separate requirement of *shishim ribo*. The area is therefore classified as a *karmelis*, and an *eruv* may accordingly be established. This is a significant *teshuvah* with broad implications for the laws of *Shabbos*.

## RH/YK Services

### Hineni

The prayer beginning with the word *hineni* is a special *tefilla* recited by the *chazan* before the *musaf amida* of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. In this *tefilla*, the *chazan* expresses his humility and unworthiness as he prepares to lead the congregation in the *musaf* service. While the *chazan* is reciting *hineni*, the congregation should listen attentively and answer *amen* at its conclusion.

One who has not yet finished his silent *amida* when the *chazan* begins *hineni* should complete his *amida* first, and then listen to whatever remains of *hineni*. If he has already finished his *amida*, he may join the *chazan* in reciting *hineni* in an undertone.

### Nesi'as Kapayim

On Rosh Hashanah, the *kohanim* perform *nesi'as kapayim* during the *chazaras hashatz* of the *musaf amida*. In accordance with widespread custom, the *kohanim* do not perform *nesi'as kapayim* during *shacharis*.

Before the *musaf amida* begins, the *kohanim* must wash their hands. A *levi* should pour the water over their hands; if no *levi* is present, the *kohanim* wash their own hands. After washing, the *kohanim* should stand at the front of the *beis knesses*, near the *aron kodesh*.

When the *chazan* reaches the *beracha* of *modim*, the *kohanim* should begin preparing to perform *nesi'as kapayim*. When the *chazan* reaches the *beracha* of *retzei*, the *kohanim* recite the following prayer:

> יְהִי רָצוֹן מִלְּפָנֶיךָ ה' אֱלֹהֵינוּ וֵאלֹהֵי אֲבוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁתְּהֵא הַבְּרָכָה הַזֹּאת שֶׁצִּוִּיתָנוּ לְבָרֵךְ אֶת עַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּרָכָה שְׁלֵמָה וְלֹא יִהְיֶה בָּהּ שׁוּם מִכְשׁוֹל וְעָוֹן מֵעַתָּה וְעַד עוֹלָם.

When the *chazan* reaches the words *v'chol ha'chayim*, the *kohanim* should conclude this prayer, and the congregation answers *amen*. When the *chazan* reaches the words *ha'machazir shechinaso l'Tziyon*, the congregation responds *amen*. The *chazan* then says *Elokeinu v'Elokei avoseinu barcheinu ba'beracha ha'meshuleshes ba'Torah*, the congregation calls out *kohanim*, and the *kohanim* begin the *beracha*:

> בָּרוּךְ אַתָּה ה' אֱלֹהֵינוּ מֶלֶךְ הָעוֹלָם אֲשֶׁר קִדְּשָׁנוּ בִּקְדֻשָּׁתוֹ שֶׁל אַהֲרֹן וְצִוָּנוּ לְבָרֵךְ אֶת עַמּוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּאַהֲבָה.

The congregation answers *amen* at the end of the *beracha*.

The *kohanim* then recite the three *pesukim* of *birkas kohanim*, prompted word by word by the *chazan*. After each word recited by the *kohanim*, the congregation should not answer *amen*; they listen and respond *amen* only at the end of each of the three *pesukim*. After each *pasuk*, the congregation recites a special *tefilla* — after *yevarechecha*, they say *yehi ratzon milfanecha Hashem Elokeinu v'Elokei avoseinu she'bizchus beracha zu* — and so on after each of the three *pesukim*. These prayers are found in the *machzor*.

### Shechina Kavod

During *nesi'as kapayim*, the *kohanim* should stand facing the congregation, while the congregation stands and faces the *kohanim*. It is forbidden for anyone to look at the *kohanim* while they are performing *nesi'as kapayim*, because the *shechina* rests upon the *kohanim* at that time. Even the *kohanim* themselves should not look at their own hands. All — *kohanim* and congregation alike — should look down at the floor, close their eyes, or look into their *machzorim*.

### Aleinu Prostration

During the *chazaras hashatz* of the *musaf amida*, when the *chazan* reaches the words *va'anachnu korim u'mishtachavim u'modim* in the *tefilla* of *aleinu*, it is the custom for the entire congregation and the *chazan* to prostrate themselves fully on the floor. Before doing so, one should spread a piece of cloth or paper on the floor to separate his face from the ground, as it is forbidden to prostrate oneself directly on a stone floor.

### Shofar Sounds

According to the custom of most communities, the *shofar* is sounded during the *chazaras hashatz* of the *musaf amida*. Thirty sounds are blown in total: ten after the *beracha* of *malchuyos*, ten after the *beracha* of *zichronos*, and ten after the *beracha* of *shofros*. Some communities also have the custom of sounding the *shofar* during the silent *musaf amida*.

### Hakafos

In some communities, it is the custom to perform *hakafos* with the *sifrei Torah* on Rosh Hashanah. This is typically done after the *musaf* service. The congregation marches around the *bima* while special *piyutim* are recited.

### Shir Adon Olam

After the *musaf* service, the *shir shel yom* is recited, followed by *Adon Olam*.

## Sukkah Walls

### Tzuras Hapesach

Abaye states: *oseh adam tzuras hapesach* — a person may construct the walls of his *sukkah* using a *tzuras hapesach* even where there are no physical *dofnos*. A *tzuras hapesach*, as we know from the laws of *Shabbos* in Masechet Eruvin, consists of two side posts with a crossbeam across the top, and is considered a valid *mechitza*. Abaye is saying that this same device is effective in the context of *sukkah* as well.

### Braisa Reconciliation

This raises an immediate difficulty. We have a *braisa* that states the requirement of *shtayim kehilchatan v'shlishit afilu tefach* — two complete walls constructed according to their proper specifications, and a third wall that need only be even a *tefach* wide. The *braisa* makes no mention of a *tzuras hapesach* as a valid option. How, then, can Abaye maintain that a *tzuras hapesach* suffices? The *gemara* will need to reconcile Abaye's ruling with the *braisa*, and we will examine what the *rishonim* — Rashi and Tosafot — say in order to determine the precise scope of this *halacha*.

## Torah & Challenges

### Avos Siman Labanim

In our daily lives, we often find ourselves facing many different challenges. The *Torah* provides a roadmap for navigating through these difficult times. When we examine the *parsha*, we see that the *avos* and *imahos* each went through their own personal struggles, yet remained steadfast in their *emunah* in the *HaKadosh Baruch Hu*. The *gemara* articulates the rule of *ma'aseh avos siman labanim* — the actions of the fathers are a sign for the children. By learning from their lives, we can find the strength to overcome our own obstacles.

### Kavvanah Davening

This same orientation should infuse our *davening*. The words of the *tefilla* were composed by the *Anshei Knesset Hagedolah*, who were giants in *ruchaniut* and knew precisely what each word represents. When we recite those words, we are tapping into a higher source. Each *berakhah* carries its own significance — in the first *berakhah*, for instance, we mention the *avos*, Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov, and invoke their merit before Hashem. Recognizing this connection to our history and our roots is a powerful way to begin the *shemoneh esrei*, and attending to it with genuine *kavvanah* can elevate one's *tefilla* to an entirely different level.
