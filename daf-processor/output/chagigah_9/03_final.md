# Chagigah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tashlumin Debate

### Korbanot & Tashlumin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁלֹּא חָג בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג — חוֹגֵג אֶת כָּל הָרֶגֶל וְיוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג. עָבַר הָרֶגֶל וְלֹא חָג — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, עַל זֶה נֶאֱמַר ״מְעֻוָּות לֹא יוּכַל לִתְקוֹן וְחֶסְרוֹן לֹא יוּכַל לְהִמָּנוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who did not celebrate** by bringing **the Festival** peace-offering **on the first day of the festival** of *Sukkot*, he **may celebrate** and bring it during **the entire** remaining days of **the pilgrimage Festival, and** even **on the final day of the Festival,** i.e., on the Eighth Day of Assembly. If **the pilgrimage Festival passed and one did not celebrate** by bringing **the Festival** peace-offering, **he is not obligated** to pay **restitution** for **it.** Even if he consecrated an animal for this purpose and it was lost, once the Festival is over he has no obligation to replace it, as he has missed the opportunity for performing this mitzva. And **about this it is stated: "That which is crooked cannot be made straight; and that which is wanting cannot be numbered"** (Ecclesiastes 1:15).

The ninth daf of Chagigah takes up the *mishna*'s treatment of the *korbanot* of the festival. Earlier material — on 8b — addressed people of different means and how they ought to distribute their resources among the various *korbanot*, and the preceding *mishna* grappled with how to allocate funds between the *shalmei chagiga* and the *olat re'iyah*. The *mishna* now before us deals with the framework of *tashlumin*: the ability to make up, across the remaining days of *Yom Tov*, a *korban chagiga* that one failed to bring on the first day. Though the *mishna* does not address this principle explicitly with respect to the *olat re'iyah*, the *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that the same framework applies there as well.

Before turning to the *amoraic* debate, the *mishna* closes with the famous verse *meuvat lo yuchal litkon vechesaron lo yuchal lehimanot* — a perversion cannot be corrected and a lacking cannot be counted — applied to one who let the entire window of *tashlumin* pass without bringing the *korban chagiga*. That aggadic discussion will be taken up later; for now the *gemara* focuses on the *halachic* structure of *tashlumin* itself.

### Yochanan vs. Oshaya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמַאי תַּשְׁלוּמִין? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: תַּשְׁלוּמִין לָרִאשׁוֹן, וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: תַּשְׁלוּמִין זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the concept of **redress** mentioned in the *baraita*? The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The other days are **redress for the first** day. If one did not bring the Festival offering on the first day, he may still do so on the remaining days of the Festival. **And Rabbi Oshaya said:** The days are **redress for one another.** Each day can be considered the main day of obligation, i.e., if one did not bring the offering on the first available day he may do so on the remaining days.

The *gemara* spells out the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Oshaya. Rabbi Yochanan holds *tashlumin lerishon*: the fundamental obligation is rooted in the first day, and any subsequent day on which one brings the *korban* is understood as a makeup for that original obligation. Rabbi Oshaya holds *tashlumin zeh lazeh*: each day stands in a makeup relationship to the one before it, or more precisely — as *Tosafot* understands his position — any day of the festival can independently serve as the day on which one fulfills the obligation, with perhaps a preference for the first day but no exclusive anchoring to it.

### Chiger Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: חִיגֵּר בְּיוֹם רִאשׁוֹן וְנִתְפַּשֵּׁט בְּיוֹם שֵׁנִי אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: תַּשְׁלוּמִין לָרִאשׁוֹן, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חֲזֵי בָּרִאשׁוֹן — לָא חֲזֵי בַּשֵּׁנִי. וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: תַּשְׁלוּמִין זֶה לָזֶה, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא חֲזֵי בָּרִאשׁוֹן — חֲזֵי בִּשְׁנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** these two explanations? **Rabbi Zeira said:** The practical difference **between them** is in a case of one who was **lame on the first day** of the Festival **and was healed on the second day. Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the other days are **redress for the first** day; **since he was not fit,** i.e., was not qualified to sacrifice his offerings **on the first** day, **he is not fit** to do so even **on the second,** as the second day is redress for the first. The second day is not for those who were completely exempt on the first, but for those who were obligated to sacrifice but neglected to do so. **And Rabbi Oshaya said** that the days are **redress for one another.** Consequently, **even though he was not fit** to bring the offering **on the first** day, he is **fit** to do so **on the second.** Since a separate obligation applies on each day, even if one was unfit to bring the offering on the first day he must do so when he becomes fit.

The practical difference between these views emerges in the case of a *chiger*, a lame person. Such an individual is exempt on the first day of the festival. If he recovers during *Yom Tov*, may he still fulfill his *chagiga* obligation on the remaining days? According to Rabbi Yochanan, since the obligation is a makeup for day one and he was exempt on day one, he remains entirely exempt — he never entered the category of obligation. According to Rabbi Oshaya, since any day can independently ground the obligation, this person would be obligated once he recovers.

### Exemption Parity

One matter is worth pausing over before proceeding. The *chiger* was the individual singled out in the first *mishna* of the tractate as exempt specifically from *re'iyah* — from the obligation of *olat re'iyah* and *aliyah laregel*, based on the verse *yera'eh kol zechurcha* and related sources. The *gemara* here, in applying the same exemption to *chagiga*, quietly assumes that all exemptions from *olat re'iyah* apply equally to *shalmei chagiga*, even though the verses governing *chagiga* — *vechagosem oto chag lashem shivat yamim* and similar formulations — do not textually connect to the *aliyah laregel* requirement in any explicit way. This identification is not argued for; it is simply assumed. And it is an assumption that already appeared implicitly in the earlier *mishna*, which treated *olat re'iyah* and *shalmei chagiga* as parallel categories when discussing how to distribute one's resources between them.

One might have expected the *gemara* to use a cleaner example of first-day exemption followed by later eligibility — such as a convert or someone who becomes *bar mitzvah* in the middle of the festival period. Indeed, the same question arises in the context of *sefirat ha'omer*, where roughly one-seventh of boys who become *bar mitzvah* do so during the forty-nine-day counting period and one must ask whether they carry any obligation for days they could not have been counting as obligated individuals. The *gemara*, however, chooses the *chiger* as its illustration.

## Challenge to Yochanan

### Nazir & Night Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: נִטְמָא בַּיּוֹם — מֵבִיא, בַּלַּיְלָה — אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף בַּלַּיְלָה נָמֵי מֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say this?** Rabbi Yoḥanan was involved in a dispute with regard to a nazirite. A nazirite who becomes ritually impure as a result of contact with a corpse must undergo a seven-day process of ritual purification, after which he must bring a set of offerings and restart counting the days of his nazirite period. Usually, a nazirite may bring one set of offerings even for many occurrences of ritual impurity. However, if he came into contact with a corpse for a second time on the eighth day after he first became ritually impure he must bring two sets of offerings, as the second impurity occurred at a time when he could have begun counting the days of his nazirite vow again. The *amora'im* dispute the details of this *halakha*. **Didn't Ḥizkiya say** that if a nazirite **became ritually impure on** the eighth **day** itself, **he brings** a second set of offerings? However, if he became ritually impure **on the** previous **night he does not bring** an additional set of offerings, because he could not have brought the offering at night. Although seven complete days have passed, as he did not yet have the opportunity to bring the offering, it is as though his seven days were not yet complete. Consequently, he may still bring one set of offerings for the two instances of ritual impurity. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** if the nazirite became ritually impure **on the night** on which the eighth day begins, **he also brings** a second set of offerings. Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that this nazirite is effectively ready to begin counting the days of his vow again, as only the technicality that one may not bring offerings at night prevents him from doing so. This shows that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, even when one is incapable of sacrificing his offerings, his obligation remains intact. So too, in the case of a lame person, his obligation to bring the Festival offering applies in theory even on the first day, which means that he should be able to bring the offerings at a later date during the Festival.

The *gemara* now challenges Rabbi Yochanan's position. Would he truly maintain that someone exempt on the first day is permanently excluded from the *chagiga* obligation? The challenge comes through an analogy drawn from the laws of *nezirut*.

The *gemara* cites a ruling of Chizkiya: *nitma bayom meivi, balaila eino meivi* — if a *nazir* who has completed his seven-day *tahara* process after contracting *tumah lemet* becomes *tamei* again during the day of the eighth day, he brings a second *korban* for a second, distinct period of *tumah*; but if he became *tamei* again on the night before the eighth day — after the seven full days of *tahara* have passed but before daybreak, when alone he could actually bring a *korban* — he does not bring a second *korban*, and the second *tumah* is treated as a continuation of the first. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *af balaila nami meivi* — even if he became *tamei* again at night, it is already considered a new and distinct status, and he would bring a second *korban*.

### Nazir Tumah Case

The underlying question is this. After seven full days of *tahara*, including the requisite sprinkling of *mei chatat*, the *nazir* is entirely *tahor* — but he cannot bring his *korban* until daybreak, since *korbanot* are only offered during the day. The night that intervenes is already, in one sense, the eighth day in *halachic* terms; yet the capacity to actually bring the *korban* belongs to the morning. If this *nazir* contracts *tumah* during that night, does the night count as part of the new status — such that this is a second, independent *tumah* obligating a second *korban* — or does the night's inability to support the bringing of a *korban* mean the person has not yet fully entered the new category, making this *tumah* a continuation of the old one?

Rabbi Yochanan holds that the night is already the new status. The *gemara* presses: if that is Rabbi Yochanan's position — that even at night one is already considered to have moved into the new *korban*-bearing phase — then it seems to undercut his own position on *chagiga*, for it implies that a person unable to bring a *korban* for technical reasons (such as being lame) should not be considered fully excluded from the category of obligation. Perhaps even the *chiger* on the first day, though exempt at that moment, is not so categorically removed from the obligation as Rabbi Yochanan suggests.

### Night Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: שָׁאנֵי טוּמְאָה, דְּיֵשׁ לָהּ תַּשְׁלוּמִין בְּפֶסַח שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** The case of one who cannot bring an offering due to **ritual impurity is different.** He is not completely disqualified, **as there is redress for** ritual purity. This can be demonstrated from the *halakha* **of the second *Pesaḥ*.** Just as one who is ritually impure and may not sacrifice the Paschal offering has the opportunity to redress the situation by means of the second *Pesaḥ*, so too, anyone who cannot bring an offering due to impurity may redress this at a later date.

The *gemara* offers an initial answer through Rabbi Yirmiyah: *tumah* is different, because we know from the institution of *Pesach Sheni* that *tumah* does not fully exclude a person from a *korban* obligation — it merely defers it. The person who was *tamei* on *Pesach Rishon* can still bring on *Pesach Sheni*, demonstrating that *tumah* leaves one within the relevant category. The *gemara* acknowledges this is a somewhat peculiar line of reasoning in this context, but presses forward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פֶּסַח שֵׁנִי — תַּשְׁלוּמִין דְּרִאשׁוֹן הוּא. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שֵׁנִי רֶגֶל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ הוּא, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa strongly objects to this** reasoning: **This works out well according to the one who said** that **the second *Pesaḥ***  **is redress for the first** Paschal offering. According to this opinion, it is clear that ritual impurity does not nullify one's obligation. **However, according to the one who said** that **the second** *Pesaḥ* **is a separate pilgrimage Festival,** established for those who were unable to sacrifice the Paschal offering at the proper time, **what is there to say?** In that case, Rav Yirmeya's answer does not apply, and therefore it remains unclear that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that one who is not obligated in the Festival offerings on the first day is exempt during the remaining days.

Rav Pappa challenges even this: the argument that *tumah* does not fully exclude a person works only according to the view that *Pesach Sheni* is a *tashlumin* for *Pesach Rishon*. According to the view that *Pesach Sheni* is *regel bifnei atzmo* — an independent festival obligation in its own right — the *tumah* on *Pesach Rishon* simply creates an exemption and triggers a separate obligation later. Under that view, *tumah* would function as a full exclusion from the first obligation, and one could not derive from *Pesach Sheni* that *tumah*-exempt individuals remain in the category. This is a fascinating debate already explored in Pesachim.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לַיְלָה אֵינוֹ מְחוּסַּר זְמַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa said: Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that **night** is **not** considered part of a date **whose time has not yet** arrived. Although one may not sacrifice offerings at night, the date itself has arrived and his period of impurity is complete, which is why any further impurity requires a second set of offerings. Consequently, Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion with regard to a lame person does not contradict his ruling concerning a nazirite.

Rav Pappa therefore offers a simpler resolution: *kasavar Rabbi Yochanan laila eino mechusar zman* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that the nighttime preceding the eighth day does not constitute a *mechusar zman*, a lacking of the proper time. Since it is already the eighth day in *halachic* terms, and only the practical limitation of nighttime prevents the actual bringing of the *korban*, the person is already considered fully in the new status. The night is not an absence of eligibility but merely a waiting period within a status already achieved. The concept of *mechusar zman* — typically applied to an animal not yet of the proper age for a *korban* — here describes the person's temporal readiness, and Rabbi Yochanan's ruling is that nighttime does not produce such a lacking. This has nothing to do with whether a *chiger*, exempt for reasons entirely separate from the timing of *korban*-bringing, is or is not within the category of obligation on the first day of the festival.

## Reconciling Positions

### Zav Night Flows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רָאָה אַחַת בַּלַּיְלָה וּשְׁתַּיִם בַּיּוֹם — מֵבִיא. שְׁתַּיִם בַּלַּיְלָה וְאַחַת בַּיּוֹם — אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לַיְלָה אֵינוֹ מְחוּסַּר זְמַן, אֲפִילּוּ שְׁתַּיִם בַּלַּיְלָה וְאַחַת בַּיּוֹם — מֵבִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan** say: A *zav* at the end of his seven-day purification process who **saw** that he experienced **one** emission **at night and** then **two in the day brings** additional offerings for the second impurity? Had he experienced the emissions before he became ritually pure, he would have been required to bring only one set of offerings. However, once his purification is complete he must bring a separate set. In this case, the first emission occurred before he was able to sacrifice the offerings for his initial period of impurity. Nevertheless, as the two subsequent sightings occurred during the day, and they alone would suffice to confer upon him the status of a *zav*, they combine with the one from the previous night and he is required to bring new offerings. However, if he saw **two** emissions **at night and one in the day, he does not bring** additional offerings, because at night he could not yet sacrifice the offerings owed due to his current status, and without those two new sightings he would not become a *zav* again. **And if it enters your mind** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that **night** is **not** considered a date **whose time has not yet** arrived, then **even** in the case where he saw **two** emissions **at night and one in the day he** should have to **bring** another set of offerings. He already reached the date of sacrificing the previous offerings before these new emissions occurred. This indicates that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that night is considered part of a date whose time has not yet arrived.

The *gemara* immediately raises a further challenge against Rabbi Yochanan's position that night is not *mechusar zman*. Rabbi Yochanan himself is cited as ruling: *ra'ah achat balaila ushtayim bayom meivi; shtayim balaila ve'achat bayom eino meivi* — if a man completing his seven-day *tahara* process as a *zav* had one flow on the night of the seventh day and two flows the following morning, he brings a second *korban* for a new period of *zivah*; but if he had two flows at night and one in the morning, he does not bring a second *korban*.

The *zav* case runs parallel to the *nazir* case. A man who has a certain type of penile flow becomes a *zav* only after three flows. Crucially, the first flow does not yet categorize him as a *zav*; it renders him merely a *ba'al keri*, carrying no greater *tumah* than a seminal emission. Only the second flow begins to create the identity of *zivah*, and three flows together complete it. The *korban* obligation for *zav*-status arises after three such flows.

### Zav Flow Count

Now, after a *zav* completes his seven-day *tahara* process, he is obligated to bring a *korban* at daybreak of the eighth day. The question is whether any new flows occurring that night — before he can bring the *korban* — are part of a new, independent period of *zivah* or a continuation of the old one.

The significance of whether one flow or two flows occurred at night turns on the nature of the first flow. Since the first flow does not yet classify a person as a *zav*, a single nighttime flow is immaterial for purposes of determining whether a new status has taken root during the night. The new *zav* identity only begins to crystallize with the second flow. Therefore: if two of the three flows occurred at night, the second flow — the one that begins to establish his new *zav* identity — took root while he was still in the night, before he had yet brought his *korban*. That second flow, occurring in what Rabbi Yochanan's opponents would call the *mechusar zman* period, pulls the new status back into the old category. But if only one flow occurred at night, it is halakhically inert, and the new identity of *zivah* only begins with the two daytime flows — by which point he is already obligated in his first *korban* and the new period is clearly separate.

### Yochanan's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר לַיְלָה מְחוּסַּר זְמַן, לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר — פְּשִׁיטָא! שְׁתַּיִם בַּיּוֹם וְאַחַת בַּלַּיְלָה אִצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When Rabbi Yoḥanan said** this statement, he spoke **according to the one who says** that **night** is considered part of a date **whose time has not yet** arrived. However, he himself maintains that even if a *zav* saw all three emissions at night he must bring another offering. The Gemara asks: If he spoke only **according to the one who says** that opinion, it **is obvious** that no new offerings are required; what novel idea did Rabbi Yoḥanan intend to express? The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, **it was necessary for him** to teach the case of a *zav* who saw **two in the day and one at night.**

This teaching appears to contradict Rabbi Yochanan's position that night is not *mechusar zman*. If Rabbi Yochanan holds that night is already the new status, then even two flows at night should constitute a new, independent period of *zivah* triggering a second *korban* — yet Rabbi Yochanan himself teaches that in such a case the person does not bring a second *korban*.

The *gemara* resolves this by reframing the entire teaching: *ki ka'amar Rabbi Yochanan l'divrei ha'omer laila mechusar zman* — when Rabbi Yochanan articulated the rule about one flow at night versus two flows at night, he was not expressing his own view. He was spelling out what the consequences would be according to the position that night is *mechusar zman*, not according to his own view. Rabbi Yochanan himself holds that night is not *mechusar zman*, and under his own position even three flows entirely at night would already constitute a new, independent period of *zivah* obligating a second *korban*.

### Alternative View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא כְּאַתְקַפְתָּא דְּרַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: It was necessary for Rabbi Yoḥanan to say this, lest it **enter your mind to say** in the manner **of the strong objection of Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi** (see *Keritot* 8a), who holds that there is no reason to distinguish between a *zav* seeing one or two emissions at night. Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan **teaches us** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Yosef,** that the first sighting confers merely the status of ritual impurity of one who has a seminal emission, and he is not yet classified as a *zav*. Consequently, there is a difference between one who experienced one emission at night and one who experienced two.

Why then does Rabbi Yochanan bother to articulate the ruling as he does, if it reflects a position he does not hold? The *gemara* answers that there is a genuine *chiddush* embedded in that teaching even for the one who holds that night is *mechusar zman*. One might have thought — as Rav Sheshet the son of Rav Idi argues in another *gemara* — that even a single nighttime flow should create a continuity with the old status, since the process began during the night. Rabbi Yochanan teaches, in the name of Rav Yosef's principle, that this is not so: the first flow is halakhically inert and does not pull the subsequent flows into the old category. It takes two flows at night — enough to begin the formation of a new *zav* identity — to create that continuity. One flow at night, even under the view that night is *mechusar zman*, does not suffice.

In the end, the entire discussion about *laila mechusar zman* has taken the *gemara* considerably afield from the original question of *tashlumin* for *chagiga*. The nature of the *machlokes* between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Oshaya — whether the obligation is fundamentally anchored to the first day or is fulfillable on any day — remains, as the *gemara* itself concedes, without the sharpened resolution one might have hoped for from this excursion. The more enduring observation from this opening section is the quiet but telling assumption that the *mitzvah* of *olat re'iyah* and the *mitzvah* of *shalmei chagiga* share identical parameters of exemption — a point that is never argued but consistently taken for granted.

## Meuvat & Lasting Damage

### Chesaron Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּר הֵי הֵי לְהִלֵּל: הַאי ״לְהִימָּנוֹת״, לְהִמָּלאוֹת מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא, זֶה (שֶׁמִּנּוּהוּ) [שֶׁנִּמְנוּ] חֲבֵירָיו לִדְבַר מִצְוָה, וְהוּא לֹא נִמְנָה עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **bar Hei Hei said to Hillel** that if this is the correct interpretation of the verse, **this** term: **"Be numbered [*lehimanot*]"** is apparently inappropriate. **It should have** said: **Be filled. Rather, this** verse is referring to one **whose friends reached a consensus [*manuhu*]** with regard **to a matter of a mitzva and he was not part of their consensus,** and therefore he missed his opportunity to join them in the performance of the mitzva.

The *gemara* now turns to the *aggadic* material embedded in the *mishna*. The *mishna* closes with the verse from Kohelet: *meuvat lo yuchal litkon vechesaron lo yuchal lehimanot* — a perversion that cannot be corrected and a lacking that cannot be counted. The *mishna* applies this to one who allowed the entire period of *tashlumin* to pass without bringing the *korban chagiga*.

Bar Hei Hei — one of the more unusual names in the *gemara*, noted by *Tosafot* as a *ger* who takes the name from the *hei* added to *Avraham* and the *hei* added to *Sarah*, just as Ben Bag Bag's name reflects the *gimatriya* of five — raises a pointed question to Hillel. The verse says *lo yuchal lehimanot*, that something cannot be "counted." Why *counted*? If the idea is that something is missing, the verse should have said it cannot be *filled* or *completed*. The use of the language of counting seems out of place. Bar Hei Hei therefore interprets *chesaron lo yuchal lehimanot* as referring not to a personal deficit but to a social exclusion: *ze shenimnu chaverav l'dvar mitzvah vehu lo nimna imahem* — his colleagues were counted among those doing a *mitzvah* and he was not counted among them. It is not merely that an obligation went unfulfilled; it is that there was a communal roll call, an opportunity to stand up and be part of the group, and this person was absent.

### Braita Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״מְעֻוָּות לֹא יוּכַל לִתְקוֹן״ — זֶה שֶׁבִּיטֵּל קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שֶׁל שַׁחֲרִית, אוֹ קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע שֶׁל עַרְבִית, אוֹ שֶׁבִּיטֵּל תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל שַׁחֲרִית אוֹ תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל עַרְבִית. ״וְחֶסְרוֹן לֹא יוּכַל לְהִימָּנוֹת״ — זֶה שֶׁנִּמְנוּ חֲבֵירָיו לִדְבַר מִצְוָה וְהוּא לֹא נִמְנָה עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **This** explanation **is also taught** in a *baraita*. The meaning of the verse "That which is crooked cannot be made straight; and that which is wanting cannot be numbered" is as follows: **"That which is crooked cannot be made straight"** is referring to **one who omitted the recitation of the morning *Shema* or the recitation of the evening *Shema*, or who omitted the morning prayer or the evening prayer. "And that which is wanting cannot be numbered"** is referring to **one whose friends reached a consensus** with regard **to a matter of a mitzva and he was not part of their consensus.**

A *braita* confirms this interpretation. *Meuvat lo yuchal litkon* — that which cannot be corrected — is illustrated by one who neglected *keriat shema* of *shacharit* or *keriat shema* of *arvit*, or who missed *tefillah* of *shacharit* or *tefillah* of *arvit*. That these examples are chosen is striking. There are innumerable obligations one could cite as irreversible once the time has passed, yet the *braita* selects *keriat shema* and *tefillah*. Particularly notable is *tefillah*, which in fact does have a form of *tashlumin* — a missed *shacharit* can be partially compensated through *mincha*, and so forth. *Tosafot* argues that this is precisely the *chiddush*: even though there is a *tashlumin* for *tefillah*, that makeup does not fully repair what was lost. The *chesaron lo yuchal lehimanot* is then applied, as in Bar Hei Hei's formulation, to one whose companions gathered to perform a *mitzvah* together and who was not counted among them — missing not merely an obligation but the opportunity to be part of a collective act of *kiddush Hashem*.

### Bar Hei Hei's Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ בַּר הֵי הֵי לְהִלֵּל, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשַׁבְתֶּם וּרְאִיתֶם בֵּין צַדִּיק לְרָשָׁע בֵּין עוֹבֵד אֱלֹהִים לַאֲשֶׁר לֹא עֲבָדוֹ״, הַיְינוּ ״צַדִּיק״ — הַיְינוּ ״עוֹבֵד אֱלֹהִים״, הַיְינוּ ״רָשָׁע״ — הַיְינוּ ״אֲשֶׁר לֹא עֲבָדוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara records another discussion between bar Hei Hei and Hillel. **Bar Hei Hei said to Hillel: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Then you shall again discern between the righteous and the wicked, between he who serves God and he who does not serve Him"** (Malachi 3:18). There are two redundancies here: **"The righteous" is** the same as **"he who serves God,"** and **"the wicked" is** the same as **"he who does not serve Him."**

Bar Hei Hei then poses a second question to Hillel, this time about a verse in Malachi: *veshavtem uri'item bein tzaddik l'rasha, bein oved Elokim la'asher lo avado* — you will return and see the difference between the righteous and the wicked, between one who serves God and one who does not serve God. The redundancy is glaring: is not the *tzaddik* the same as *oved Elokim*, and the *rasha* the same as *asher lo avado*? What does the verse add by doubling the distinction?

### Hillel's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עֲבָדוֹ״ וְ״לֹא עֲבָדוֹ״ — תַּרְוַיְיהוּ צַדִּיקֵי גְּמוּרֵי נִינְהוּ, וְאֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה שׁוֹנֶה פִּרְקוֹ מֵאָה פְּעָמִים, לְשׁוֹנֶה פִּרְקוֹ מֵאָה וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Hillel **said to him:** The one **"who serves Him"** and the one **"who does not serve Him" are both** referring to **completely righteous people. But** the verse is hinting at a distinction between them, as **one who reviews his studies one hundred times is not comparable to one who reviews his studies one hundred and one** times.

Hillel responds that both the *oved Elokim* and the *asher lo avado* in the verse are in fact *tzaddikim gemurim* — complete righteous individuals. No, the verse is not drawing a contrast between the wicked and the righteous. It is drawing a contrast within the category of the righteous themselves, between one who genuinely *serves* God and one who merely satisfies obligation. The *oved Elokim* is the one who goes beyond the standard requirement. The illustration Hillel offers: *shoneh pirko me'ah pe'amim* versus *shoneh pirko me'ah va'achat* — one who reviews his chapter a hundred times versus one who reviews it a hundred and one. A hundred times: *tzaddik gamur*. A hundred and one times: *oved Elokim*. The one who does it exactly as required but no more is not, in the fullest sense, a servant of God.

This formulation implies that a hundred repetitions was understood as the standard practice for committing a passage to memory — not an extraordinary feat but the established norm of serious study in an oral culture where material had to be internalized without the aid of written texts. The one who pushed himself to go even one repetition beyond the norm transformed his act qualitatively.

The *Maharsha* notes a remarkable *gimatriya*: the *rashei teivot* of *oved Elokim la'asher* — *ayin*, *alef*, *lamed* — yield 101, while the *rashei teivot* of *lo avado* — *lamed*, *ayin* — yield 100, which may well be the textual root from which the *gemara* derives its specific numbers.

### Donkey Market

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִשּׁוּם חַד זִימְנָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״לֹא עֲבָדוֹ״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, צֵא וּלְמַד מִשּׁוּק שֶׁל חֲמָרִין: עַשְׂרָה פַּרְסֵי — בְּזוּזָא, חַד עֲשַׂר פַּרְסֵי — בִּתְרֵי זוּזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Bar Hei Hei **said to him: And due to one** extra **time** that he did not review, **the verse calls him** a person **"who does not serve Him"? He said to him: Yes. Go and learn from the market of donkey drivers.** One can hire a driver to travel up to **ten parasangs for one dinar.** However, he will travel **eleven parasangs** only **for two dinars.** This shows that any departure beyond the norm is considered a significant difference.

Bar Hei Hei presses back: can one truly say that because of a single additional iteration, the person is described as *lo avado*, as not having served God at all? Hillel answers with an analogy from the marketplace: *tze u'lemad mishuk shel chamorin* — go and look at the market of donkey drivers. If the going rate is ten *parsei* per *zuz*, and you go eleven *parsei*, the price doubles — you have crossed into the next unit entirely, and the charge restructures completely. The one additional step beyond the standard boundary transforms not just the marginal cost but the entire category. So too, the single additional review beyond the standard hundred crosses a qualitative threshold. The person who stops at exactly the normative limit has fulfilled his obligation but has not become a servant of God in the fuller sense; the one who goes a single step further has entered a different category of relationship to his learning and to his Creator.

## Eliyahu & Poverty

### Poverty Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אֵלִיָּהוּ לְבַר הֵי הֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״הִנֵּה צְרַפְתִּיךָ וְלֹא בְכָסֶף בְּחַרְתִּיךָ בְּכוּר עוֹנִי״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁחָזַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עַל כׇּל מִדּוֹת טוֹבוֹת לִיתֵּן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, וְלֹא מָצָא אֶלָּא עֲנִיּוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Elijah** the Prophet **said to bar Hei Hei, and some say** that he said this **to Rabbi Elazar: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Behold, I have refined you, but not as silver; I have tried you in the furnace of affliction [oni]"** (Isaiah 48:10)? This **teaches that the Holy One, Blessed be He, sought after all good character traits to impart** them **to the Jewish people, and He found only poverty [*aniyut*]** capable of preventing them from sin.

The *gemara* now records a separate exchange involving Bar Hei Hei, this time with the prophet Eliyahu — or according to some versions, with Rabbi Elazar. Eliyahu asks: what is the meaning of the verse *hinei tzerafticha ve-lo ve-chesef, becharticha be-chor oni* — behold I have refined you, but not as silver; I have tested you in the furnace of poverty? The response: *melamed she-chazar ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu al kol middot tovot liten le-Yisrael ve-lo matza ela aniyut* — God reviewed all the good qualities to bestow upon the Jewish people and found none so fitting as poverty.

### Red Strap Image

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יוֹסֵף, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: יָאָה עַנְיוּתָא לִיהוּדָאֵי כִּי בַּרְזָא סוּמָּקָא לְסוּסְיָא חִיוָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said, and some say** it was **Rav Yosef: This** explains the folk saying **that people say: Poverty is good for the Jewish people like a red bridle [*barza*] for a white horse.** Just as a red bridle accentuates the white color of the horse, so the challenge of poverty draws out the purity of the Jewish people.

Shmuel — and some say Rav Yosef — adds: *hainu d'amri inshi ya'ah aniyuta le-Yhuda'ei ki barza sumka le-susya chivra* — that is what people say, poverty suits the Jewish people like a red strap on a white horse. The image suggests not degradation but fitness: the red strap complements the white horse, and poverty, whatever its hardships, keeps the Jewish people aligned with the right path. This teaching resonates with the earlier material in the tractate dealing with the *churban habayit* and the effort to reframe national suffering in some larger constructive frame: the poverty that comes from lack of wealth is here imagined as a form of divine discipline that has preserved Jewish loyalty. As the *Maharsha* notes, the phrase *ve-lo ve-chesef* may be read not as "not as silver is refined" but as "I refined you through the absence of silver" — the poverty itself was the furnace.

## Meuvat Definition

### Mamzer Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: אֵי זֶה הוּא ״מְעֻוָּות לֹא יוּכַל לִתְקוֹן״ — זֶה הַבָּא עַל הָעֶרְוָה וְהוֹלִיד מִמֶּנָּה מַמְזֵר וְכוּ׳. הוֹלִיד — אִין, לֹא הוֹלִיד — לָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says: Who is** the **crooked that cannot be made straight? This** verse is referring to **one who engaged in intercourse with a woman forbidden to him and fathered a *mamzer* with her.** The Gemara infers from the mishna: If **he fathers a child, yes,** this verse applies, as he cannot remedy the situation; if **he does not father a child, no,** the verse does not apply, as he can make amends.

Returning to the *mishna*, Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya defines *meuvat lo yuchal litkon*: *eizehu me'uvat lo yuchal litkon? Zeh ha-ba al ha-ervah ve-holid mimena mamzer* — who is the one who has committed a perversion that cannot be corrected? One who engaged in illicit relations and thereby produced a *mamzer*. The lasting damage is a real-world, irreversible consequence: a human being now carries a status that affects his life and the lives of his descendants in ways that no amount of repentance or financial remedy can undo.

It is worth pausing on *Rashi*'s comment on the *mishna*. *Rashi* frames the problem not primarily in terms of the injury done to the world but in terms of the perpetrator's own capacity for *teshuvah*: since the product of the sin — the *mamzer* — continues to exist as a living reminder of the transgression, the person's *avonot* cannot be fully erased through *teshuvah*. This is a notable reorientation: the concern shifts from the impact of the sin on others to the obstacle the sin creates for the sinner's own spiritual repair.

The *gemara* asks: does the *meuvat* apply only when a child was in fact born — *holid ein, lo holid lo*? Is an irreversible perversion only created by the birth of a *mamzer*, or does illicit sexual sin create such a perversion even without a child?

### Theft Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן מְנַסְיָא אוֹמֵר: גּוֹנֵב אָדָם — אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁיַּחֲזִיר גְּנֵבוֹ וִיתַקֵּן, גּוֹזֵל אָדָם — אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁיַּחֲזִיר גְּזֵלוֹ וִיתַקֵּן, אֲבָל הַבָּא עַל אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ וַאֲסָרָהּ לְבַעְלָהּ — נִטְרַד מִן הָעוֹלָם וְהָלַךְ לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya says:** If **a person steals it is possible that he** might **return his stolen property and be made straight;** if **a person robs from another it is possible that he** might **return his robbed property and be made straight. However, one who has sexual relations with a married woman** with her consent **and** thereby **renders her forbidden to her husband is banished from the world and passes away.** There is no way for him to rectify the situation and achieve atonement, because a married woman who willingly has sexual relations with another man is permanently forbidden to her husband.

A *braita* is adduced. Rabbi Shimon ben Menasya rules: *gonev adam efshar she-yachazir gneivato ve-yitaken* — if a person steals, he can return the stolen object and repair the damage. *Gozeil adam efshar she-yachazir gezeilato ve-yitaken* — if a person robs, he can likewise return the stolen goods and make repair. But *ha-ba al eishet ish va-asarah le-ba'alah* — one who has relations with a married woman and thereby renders her forbidden to her husband — *nitrad min ha'olam ve-halach lo*, that person will be lost from the world, because he has created a lasting, irreparable injury. The *braita* thus makes clear that even without a *mamzer* there is an irreparable perversion: the marriage has been destroyed. The wife is now forbidden to her husband and cannot return to him, a damage no amount of repentance repairs. This is where *Rashi*'s emphasis becomes legible: the *nitrad min ha'olam* focuses attention on the perpetrator's fate precisely because the injury to the marriage is so comprehensive.

### Torah Sage Departure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: אֵין אוֹמֵר ״בַּקְּרוּ גָּמָל״ ״בַּקְּרוּ חֲזִיר״, אֶלָּא ״בַּקְּרוּ טָלֶה״. וְאֵי זֶה — זֶה תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁפֵּירַשׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** Someone who wants to examine an animal for blemishes to bring it as an offering **does not say: Inspect the camel, or: Inspect the pig,** as these are inherently disqualified for the altar. **Rather,** he says: **Inspect the lamb.** Similarly, the term: "Crooked," applies only to one who was previously straight. **And who is this? This is a Torah scholar who leaves** his **Torah** study.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai offers a different interpretive frame for *meuvat lo yuchal litkon*. He insists that *me'uvat* — perversion — specifically requires something that *began* good and *became* bad. To illustrate: one does not speak of *biku gamal* or *biku chazir* — inspecting a camel or a pig for a *mum* to determine fitness as a *korban*. One inspects a sheep, *bikru taleh*, something that was potentially a valid offering and is now being assessed for disqualification. Perversion is the corruption of something that had genuine worth; mere badness from the outset is not *me'uvat*.

Accordingly, Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai identifies the paradigm case of *meuvat lo yuchal litkon* as *talmid chacham she-pharash min ha-Torah* — a Torah sage who departs from the world of Torah. This is something that began as genuinely good — deep learning, a life shaped by Torah — and was then perverted. Whatever that person once had, however much he may later try to recapture it, something has been lost that cannot be fully recovered.

The difficulty is that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's principle seems to respond oddly to the preceding *braita*'s example of the adulterous act destroying a marriage. A marriage that has been violated was certainly something good that became perverted. Yet Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai appears to set that aside in favor of his own example. The *gemara* does not directly address this tension here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁפֵּירַשׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה, עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״כְּצִפּוֹר נוֹדֶדֶת מִן קִנָּהּ כֵּן אִישׁ נוֹדֵד מִמְּקוֹמוֹ״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״מַה מָּצְאוּ אֲבוֹתֵיכֶם בִּי עָוֶל כִּי רָחֲקוּ מֵעָלָי״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish said: Any Torah scholar who leaves the Torah, about him the verse says: "As a bird that wanders from her nest, so is a man who wanders from his place"** (Proverbs 27:8). **And it says: "What unrighteousness have your fathers found in Me, that they are gone far from Me?"** (Jeremiah 2:5). This indicates that the punishment is greater for one who was close to God and became distant from Him.

A teaching attributed to Rabbi Yehuda ben Lakish adds a further dimension: regarding any *talmid chacham* who departs from Torah, the verse says *katzipor nodedet min kanah, ken ish noded mimekoro* — like a bird that wanders from its nest, so is a person who leaves his place. And regarding such a departure, the verse asks: *ma matzu avoteichem bi avel ki rachaku me'alai* — what wrong did your forefathers find in Me that they distanced themselves? This framing is notably less severe than *me'uvat lo yuchal litkon*. It conveys sadness and bewilderment — why would someone leave a home that gave life meaning? — without declaring the damage irreversible. The departure from Torah is here mourned rather than condemned as an irreparable catastrophe.

## Meuvat Scope

### Incest vs. Adultery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ פְּנוּיָה, כָּאן בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ. וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּאוֹנֶס, כָּאן בְּרָצוֹן. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא הָא וְהָא בְּאוֹנֶס, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּאֵשֶׁת כֹּהֵן, כָּאן בְּאֵשֶׁת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is dealing with a case where he had forbidden sexual relations **with his unmarried sister.** Although the intercourse itself is a severe sin, if he does not sire a child it can be rectified through repentance. **There,** in the *baraita*, it is referring to a case where he sinned **with a married woman,** causing irreparable damage to her marriage. **And if you wish, say** instead: **This and that** are both referring **to a married woman. And** it is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is dealing with **a rape,** in which case it is not prohibited for the woman to return to her husband. **There,** it is referring to a woman who had relations **willfully,** and therefore she is forbidden to her husband. **And if you wish, say** that **this and that** are both dealing **with a rape, and** it is still **not difficult. Here,** where the transgression cannot be rectified, it is referring **to** one who raped **the wife of a priest,** as it is forbidden for a priest to have relations with his wife once she has intercourse with any other man, even unwillingly. **There,** it is referring **to** one who raped **the wife of an Israelite,** in which case there is no prohibition against her returning to her husband.

Having established that the *braita* requires a *mamzer* in the case of forbidden sexual relations but the *braita* about *eishet ish* creates an irreversible damage even without a child, the *gemara* resolves the apparent tension: *lo kashya, kan be'achoto penuyah, kan be'eishet ish* — there is no contradiction; one case speaks of incest with an unmarried relative, the other of adultery with a married woman. In the case of incest, the lasting *meuvat* requires the birth of a *mamzer* because the incestuous relationship itself does not destroy any pre-existing sanctioned bond. The parties were not married; there is no marriage to dissolve. The lasting damage, the irreversible perversion, is created only when a *mamzer* comes into the world. In the case of adultery, however, the act itself severs the marriage: the wife becomes forbidden to her husband, and that rupture is permanent regardless of whether a child was born.

### Rape vs. Willing

The *gemara* offers a second resolution: *ve-iba'it eima ha va-ha be-eishet ish* — both cases concern a married woman, but one involves rape and one involves willing participation. If the act was rape, the woman is not rendered forbidden to her husband and the marriage bond remains intact; therefore, the only *meuvat* that could arise from the rape of a married woman is the birth of a *mamzer*. If the act was willing, she becomes forbidden to her husband and the marriage is destroyed even without a child. This is a striking and deeply uncomfortable distinction. The *gemara* is parsing the *halachic* consequences of the act — what categories of irreversible damage are produced — without pausing to acknowledge the profound injury done to the victim of rape as such. The violence of the act, the *tza'ar* and *boshet* and the psychic devastation, are entirely absent from the analysis. There are in fact *halachic* categories — *tza'ar*, *boshet*, *pegam* — that could have framed the injury done to the rape victim as itself a form of irreversible damage. The *gemara* chose not to raise them, and that silence is worth noting.

### Kohen Status

The *gemara* offers yet a third resolution: *ve-iba'it eima ha va-ha be-ones, lo kashya, kan be-eishet kohen, kan be-eishet Yisrael* — both cases involve rape, but one involves the wife of a *kohen* and one involves the wife of an ordinary Israelite. The wife of a *kohen* becomes forbidden to her husband even through rape — that is the *halacha* regarding a *kohen*'s wife who was violated — and therefore the irreversible damage to the marriage arises even without a child. The wife of an Israelite does not become forbidden to her husband through rape alone, and therefore the only lasting *meuvat* in that case is the birth of a *mamzer*. This distinction, that rape creates a permanent severance for the wife of a *kohen* but not for the wife of an Israelite, is itself a *halacha* that carries its own profound difficulties, and belongs to a broader discussion that will be taken up properly in Yevamot.

## Leaving Torah Study

### Halakha to Mikra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלַיּוֹצֵא וְלַבָּא אֵין שָׁלוֹם״, אָמַר רַב: כֵּיוָן שֶׁיּוֹצֵא אָדָם מִדְּבַר הֲלָכָה לִדְבַר מִקְרָא — שׁוּב אֵין לוֹ שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Since the Gemara mentioned a Torah scholar who abandons the study of Torah, it cites a relevant verse: **"Neither was there any peace to him that went out or came in** due to the adversary" (Zechariah 8:10). **Rav said: Once a person leaves** the study **of *halakha*,** i.e., Mishna and Gemara, even **for** the study **of the Torah** itself, **he will no longer have peace.** The verses of the Torah are often obscure and it is difficult to learn *halakha* directly from them without the aid of the interpretations of the Talmud.

The *gemara* closes with a series of teachings about *me'uvat* in the context of leaving one domain of Torah study for another. Rav rules: *keivan she-yotzei adam mid'var halakha l'dvar mikra, shuv ein lo shalom* — once a person moves from learning *halakha* to learning *mikra*, he will no longer have *shalom*, no peace. The statement is striking: not merely leaving Torah for business or for worldly pursuits, but leaving *halakha* to learn Bible. The *gemara*'s implicit hierarchy is clear. *Rashi* explains *ein lo shalom* as meaning that one no longer has clear direction: *she'ein hora'ah mid'var mikra she-ha-mishna mefareshet stimas ha-torah* — *mikra* alone does not yield clear *hora'ah*, it is the *mishna* that unpacks the implicit law of the Torah. The person who retreats from *halakha* to *mikra* loses the capacity for clear *psak* and clear direction in observance.

### Talmud to Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: זֶה הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִתַּלְמוּד לְמִשְׁנָה. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִתַּלְמוּד לְתַלְמוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: This** is referring to **one who leaves** the study **of Talmud to** learn **Mishna.** Whereas the reasoning of the Talmud is relatively clear, the Mishna cites legal rulings without explaining their reasoning. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** The verse applies **even** to one who leaves the study **of** one **Talmud for** the other **Talmud,** i.e., who leaves off his study of the Jerusalem Talmud to begin the Babylonian Talmud, as he will encounter difficulties with the new style of learning.

Shmuel extends the ruling: *zeh ha-poreish mi-talmud le-mishna* — even leaving *gemara* to learn *mishna* results in *ein shalom*. *Mishnah* contains vast legal material, but without the analytical unpacking of *gemara*, one neither thinks the issues through nor achieves the level of mastery necessary to determine *halakha*. The statements of *mishna* remain opaque without the elaboration and comparison that *talmud* provides.

### Switching Gemarot

Rabbi Yochanan goes further still: *afilu mi-shas le-shas* — even switching from one *gemara* to another results in *ein shalom*. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* discuss which direction is problematic: whether it is leaving *Bavli* for *Yerushalmi* or the reverse. *Rashi* suggests the problematic direction is leaving *Bavli* for *Yerushalmi*, since *Bavli* is the more demanding and more analytically rigorous study and abandoning it for *Yerushalmi* represents a descent in rigor. Rabbi Yochanan himself was from *Eretz Yisrael*, which adds a layer of complexity to which *shas* he would have viewed as the higher standard.

### Tosafot's Nuance

*Tosafot* argues that the direction of the shift may not be the real issue at all. *U-meehu afilu ipcha yesh lomar* — one could say the same thing in either direction. *Tosafot* reads the underlying principle as follows: *she-peirish mi-zeh ve-adayin lo heivin be-zeh* — the problem is leaving one source before having mastered it and moving to another before achieving genuine fluency. *Shemi-tokh kach lo ya'aleh be-yado halakha berurah* — the result of constantly shifting before reaching mastery is that one never arrives at clear *halakha*. The one who flits from *Bavli* to *Yerushalmi* to *Bavli* again, always curious but never patient enough to master one fully, will never achieve the clarity and command that genuine learning demands. *Shalom* — understood here as completeness, coherence, and the settled mastery of one's learning — eludes the perpetual wanderer.
