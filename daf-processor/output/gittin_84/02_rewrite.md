# Gittin 84 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## R. Eliezer's Chutz

### R. Eliezer Intro

The daf opens in the middle of 83b, continuing with the remarkable and legally strange position of Rabbi Eliezer that a woman can be divorced *vis-à-vis* everyone except one specific person — a *get* that reads *chutz mi-ploni*, "except for so-and-so." A series of *Tannaim* responding to this position after Rabbi Eliezer's death are discussed, and we pick up with *Heishiv Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar*.

Before turning to that response, however, the *gemara* establishes an important internal concession that Rabbi Eliezer himself makes. *Modeh Rabbi Eliezer ben Horkenus*: even Rabbi Eliezer agrees that if the husband said "you are permitted to all men except Reuven," and she went ahead and married someone else — and was then widowed or divorced from that second man — she is subsequently permitted to marry Reuven. The logic is as follows. When the husband declared *chutz mi-Reuven*, this was not an *al menat* condition but a *chutz* exclusion: she remained in some sense his wife *vis-à-vis* Reuven. But once she entered into a complete new marriage with another man, that marriage entirely supplants and extinguishes whatever residual marital bond existed toward Reuven from the first husband. With the second marriage, she becomes fully the second man's wife, with no remnant of the first marriage. When the second man dies, she is therefore fully free — including *vis-à-vis* Reuven.

### R. Shimon ben Elazar

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar then offers a formal challenge to Rabbi Eliezer's entire position. He asks: *heich matzinu she-zeh oser ve-zeh matir?* — how can we find that one person prohibits and a second person permits? The first husband said she is forbidden to Reuven; how can a second husband, by marrying and then releasing her, make her permitted to Reuven?

The *gemara* immediately tests whether this principle holds universally. Is there truly no case where one party's action creates a prohibition and a different party's action dissolves it? Two apparent counterexamples are raised.

First, the case of a *yevamah*: the husband who married her caused her to become forbidden to the rest of the world upon his death, since the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* creates a *zikah* that restricts her. Then the *yavam* comes and performs *chalitzah*, releasing her to everyone. Is this not a case of one person restricting and another permitting? The *gemara* answers: no, because it is actually the *yavam*, not the husband, who creates the restriction in the first place. Had there been no brother-in-law, the wife would have been fully free upon the husband's death. It is the *yavam*'s existence that generates the *zikah*, and so it is the *yavam* himself who both creates and resolves the problem through *chalitzah*.

Second, the case of *nedarim*: a person makes a vow, restricting something upon themselves, and a *chacham* comes and annuls it. Is that not a second party lifting what the first party imposed? The *gemara* invokes Rabbi Yochanan's principle: *ein chacham matir klum ela be-charatah* — the sage does not annul anything except through *charatah*, remorse. The mechanism of *hatarat nedarim* requires that the person who made the vow articulate genuine regret for having done so, which retroactively reframes the vow as having been made in error. The sage serves as an instrument, but it is ultimately the original maker of the vow who is undoing it through remorse.

### Undoing Restrictions

A third challenge: the case of *hafarat ha-ba'al*, the husband's power to void his wife's vows. Here the wife makes a vow and the husband annuls it — with no requirement of *charatah* on her part. The husband simply declares that he does not accept it, and the vow is voided. Is this not a genuine case of one person restricting and another person lifting that restriction?

The *gemara* responds by invoking the ruling of Rav Pinchas in the name of Rava: *kol ha-noderet al da'at ba'alah hi noderet* — any time a woman makes a vow, she makes it implicitly with the understanding that her husband accepts it. The underlying presumption is that a woman does not intend her vow to take effect if her husband objects. Therefore, even in *hafarat ha-ba'al*, it is conceptually the woman herself who is undoing her vow, because she never truly intended it to bind without his acquiescence. The husband's annulment, on this reading, is simply the activation of a condition already embedded in the way she made the vow.

This is, admittedly, a striking way to frame *hafarat ha-ba'al*. The principle of *charatah* and the construction that the woman "makes her vow on her husband's *da'at*" are presented here as responses to a dialectical challenge, not necessarily as exhaustive accounts of these institutions. The *gemara* is not claiming this is the only way to understand *hatarat nedarim* or *hafarat ha-ba'al* — it is pushing back against the analogy to Rabbi Eliezer's case, where there is no plausible construction under which the first husband's restriction dissolves: he explicitly wanted her to remain bound to him *vis-à-vis* Reuven, and no subsequent act by a third party can be read as an expression of his original intent.

### Kerisus Argument

The *gemara* now introduces what it describes as the sharpest and most direct challenge to Rabbi Eliezer's position. Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah argued from the word *kerisus* — the Torah's description of a *get* as a *sefer kerisus*, a document of severance. *Kerisus* means something that cuts completely. *Almah de-shein zeh kerisus* — if the divorce excludes one person, it is not a complete severance. A *get chutz mi-Reuven* fails at the most fundamental level: it is definitionally not a *get*.

### Rabbis' Approach

If Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah's *kerisus* argument is the strongest and most decisive, why did all the other *Tannaim* bother giving their more elaborate and complex critiques of Rabbi Eliezer? Why didn't they simply cite this verse?

The answer is that the other rabbis could not freely deploy the *kerisus* argument because they needed the word *kerisus* to teach something else. The *gemara* cites a *baraita*: if a husband says *harei zeh gittech al menat shelo tishti yayin* — this is your *get* on the condition that you never drink wine — *al menat shelo telchi le-veit avich le-olam* — on the condition that you never go to your father's house for all time — *ein zeh kerisus*, that is not a *get*, because the condition perpetually hovers over her head and binds her to her first husband for the rest of her life. But if he stipulates a condition for only thirty days, *harei zeh kerisus* — that is a valid *get*. This ruling, which delimits what qualifies as a complete severance in the context of temporal conditions, is derived from *kerisus*.

Thus, for the other rabbis, the word *kerisus* is already committed to teaching that a condition hovering over her head for her entire lifetime is not a valid *get*. They cannot simultaneously use it to prove that excluding one individual is impermissible. Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah, by contrast, derives both teachings from the fuller expression *karet kerisus* — the word could have been written simply as *karet*, but the term *kerisus* teaches two things: that an eternally hovering condition is not a *get*, and that excluding a specific person is not a *get*.

The other rabbis, however, do not accept this double *derasha* from *karet/kerisus*. They read the textual elaboration as yielding only one teaching. Since they need that teaching for the hovering-condition case, they must argue against Rabbi Eliezer on other grounds.

## Temporal Conditions

### Hovering Conditions

Having established that a condition hovering over a woman's head for her entire life is not *kerisus*, the *gemara* now explores the precise parameters of this rule with greater care.

### Duration Analysis

Rava poses the following case: *harei zeh gittech al menat shelo tishti yayin kol yemei chayai* — this is your *get* on the condition that you do not drink wine for all the days of my life. This is not *kerisus*, because the condition perpetually hangs over her and maintains a residual bond to her first husband. But what if he says *kol yemei chayai ploni* — for the entire duration of Reuven's life? The *gemara* initially suggests that this *is* valid *kerisus*, because Reuven might die and the condition would then be permanently satisfied. The condition is not necessarily eternal and therefore does not perpetually subjugate her to the first husband's authority.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this distinction: *mai shna chayei ploni* — why is Reuven's lifetime different from the husband's own lifetime? Perhaps the husband will also die before her, in which case the condition would similarly resolve. If the possibility of Reuven's death makes the condition temporally limited and thus valid, the same logic should apply when the husband stipulates the duration of his own life, since the husband himself might die before the wife.

The *gemara* therefore revises the formulation. The operative distinction is: if the husband says *kol yemei chayaichi* — for all the days of *your* life — that is not *kerisus*, because then the condition will necessarily last as long as she lives and will never cease to hang over her. If the condition is tied to the husband's own lifespan or to that of a third party, it is *kerisus*, because the death of the husband or the third party — while they are both still alive — could satisfy or dissolve the condition.

This distinction, however, is not entirely satisfying. *Tosafot* points to a different *gemara* which appears to hold that both *kol yemei chayai* (the husband's life) and *kol yemei chayaichi* (the wife's life) are invalid as conditions, while only *kol yemei chayei ploni* — a third party's lifespan — is valid *kerisus*. The reasoning there is that a condition tied to the husband's lifespan provides no meaningful divorce at all: if the husband must die for her to be free of the condition, his death would have made her a widow and freed her regardless, rendering the *get* superfluous. For a *get* to have practical meaning — to free her from him while they are both still alive — the condition cannot be dissolved only by his death. That version of the rule makes considerably more intuitive sense than what the *gemara* here appears to conclude.

### One-Day Get

Rav Ami posed a question to Rav Nachman: what is the law in the case of *hayom at ishti u-le-machar at ishti* — today you are my wife, and tomorrow you are my wife again? That is, a husband gives a *get* that is valid for one day only, expiring at the end of that day, after which the marriage automatically resumes. Does this constitute a valid *get*?

The question can be framed in two directions. From Rabbi Eliezer's perspective: Rabbi Eliezer permits dividing a marriage along certain lines — she is divorced *vis-à-vis* everyone except Reuven. But in that case, at least those from whom she is divorced are divorced from her permanently. Here the division is temporal: for all people simultaneously, she is free today and not free tomorrow. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would not extend his leniency to this kind of on-and-off arrangement. Conversely, the rabbis reject Rabbi Eliezer's position because when he says *chutz mi-Reuven* she is never completely severed from her husband — the *get* never achieves full *kerisus*. But here, for one full day, she is completely and unconditionally divorced. Perhaps that momentary complete divorce is itself sufficient.

### Severance Principle

After deliberation, the *gemara* resolves the question and concludes: *keivan de-paska, paska* — once severed, severed. This applies equally according to Rabbi Eliezer and according to the rabbis. If a complete and unconditional divorce took effect — even for a single day — that divorce cannot be undone. The state of full *kerisus*, once actualized, is permanent. The husband's stipulation that the marriage would resume the following day is simply ineffective, because a divorce that has fully taken effect cannot be unilaterally reversed by the husband's subsequent declaration.

## Marriage Conditions

### Reuven Marriage Condition

The *gemara* now presents a *baraita* with a sharply different kind of condition: *harei zeh gittech al menat she-tinasi le-ploni* — this is your *get* on the condition that you marry so-and-so. The *baraita* states: *harei zo lo tinasei* — she should not marry him — and *ve-im nis'et, lo teitzei* — but if she did marry him, she does not need to leave. The precise meaning of this ruling is far from obvious, and the *gemara* will require two attempts to clarify it.

The reason to be concerned about actually fulfilling such a condition relates to appearances: *shema yomru nesheihem notnim bematana* — people will say that husbands are trading wives as gifts. If Reuven divorces his wife on the condition that she marry Shimon, and Shimon divorces his wife on the condition that she marry Reuven, the result is an exchange of wives — a practice deemed morally and socially unacceptable.

### Rav Nachman's Reading

Rav Nachman offers a first interpretation of the *baraita*. *Harei zo lo tinasei lo* — she should not marry Shimon (the specified party). The concern is the wife-swapping appearance. And *ve-im nis'et lo, lo teitzei* — if she did in fact marry Shimon, she need not leave. Although the rabbis discourage the marriage because of appearances, if she went ahead and fulfilled the condition, the *get* was valid and the new marriage stands. We do not uproot a completed marriage simply because of the societal concern.

But the *gemara* challenges this first reading. It reads *lo tinasei* as applying only to Shimon — implying that she *may* marry someone else. But how can she marry someone else? If the condition is that she marry Shimon, and the condition has not been met, she may not yet be divorced from her first husband. If she marries another man, she is potentially an *eshet ish* committing adultery. One might answer that she could marry the other man temporarily, eventually divorce him, and then go and marry Shimon to satisfy the condition — but this raises the problem of control: can she really guarantee that this chain of events will unfold? The *gemara* draws a comparison to the case of *konam einai be-sheina hayom im ishan lemachar* — my eyes are forbidden from sleep today if I sleep tomorrow — where Rav Nachman ruled that one may sleep today and trust oneself not to sleep tomorrow.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava objects to this comparison sharply. In the sleeping case, the matter is *be-didi ka'ima* — within the person's own control. *De'i ba'ei mavriz nafshei be-silu'asa* — if he really wants to, he can scratch himself with a thorn, stand under cold water, do whatever it takes to remain awake the next day. But here, *be-dida ka'ima l'garushei?* — can she get herself divorced from the other man if she wants to? Can she get Shimon to marry her? These things depend on other people's decisions entirely.

### Control Analysis

The *gemara* accepts Rava's point: the sleeping case and this case are fundamentally dissimilar because in the sleeping case the person retains direct control over the triggering condition, whereas here the woman has no meaningful control over whether the *tnai* of marrying Shimon will ever be satisfied. She cannot compel Shimon to marry her. Even if Shimon were willing, she cannot compel her interim husband to divorce her. And even if all of that were somehow achieved, Shimon might die before any of it could occur.

### Resolution

Rava therefore offers the only coherent reading of the *baraita*. *Harei zo lo tinasei — lo lo ve-lo le-acher* — she should not marry Shimon, and she should not marry anyone else either. She should not marry Shimon because of the wife-swapping appearance. She should not marry anyone else because she cannot guarantee she will ever be able to satisfy the condition, and until the condition is satisfied she may never have been divorced — meaning any children from such a union could be *mamzerim*.

*Ve-im nis'et lo, lo teitzei* — if she did marry Shimon, she does not leave, because in that case the *tnai* was in fact satisfied and the *get* was valid. *D'mishum gezeirah lo mafkinan* — we do not force her out of the marriage simply because of the appearance concern. *Aval le-acher teitzei* — but if she married someone other than Shimon, she must leave him, because *de-ba'ei kiyumei le-tnai* — the condition has not been met and may never be met, and we cannot allow the marriage to stand given the probability that she was never actually divorced. The *gemara* notes that a *baraita* confirms Rava's reading precisely: *harei zo lo tinasei lo lo ve-lo le-acher; ve-im nis'et lo, lo teitzei; le-acher, teitzei*.

This resolution raises a broader and troubling observation. The structure of this *tnai* effectively makes it impossible for the woman to marry anyone. She cannot marry Shimon because of the prohibition against the appearance of wife-swapping. She cannot marry anyone else because she cannot guarantee fulfillment of the condition. The practical result is that a husband, simply by attaching this kind of condition to a *get*, has made it impossible for his wife to remarry — while she cannot be certain she is even divorced. This is precisely the mechanism by which a *get* can simultaneously exist and create an *agunah*. Unlike the explicit *chutz mi-Reuven* of Rabbi Eliezer, which the rabbis openly reject, here the condition has the same practical effect by a more indirect route: she is, in practice, bound.

*Tosafot* pushes the analysis even further. Given that the condition is not within the woman's control to satisfy — she cannot ensure that she will marry Shimon — *Tosafot* argues that she should be considered a fully married woman (*eshet ish*) from the outset, since the *get* is conditioned on something that may never occur. If she is still considered married to her first husband, then any purported *kiddushin* to Shimon would be ineffective. Yet if she is considered *eshet ish*, then when she does go and marry Shimon to satisfy the condition, she is doing so as a married woman — and yet, by satisfying the condition, retroactively the *get* takes effect and she was divorced... but then she has left Shimon without a *get* and would need one. *Tosafot* works through these loops without fully resolving them here, but the question itself is illuminating.

## Impossible Condition (II)

### Absurd Conditions

The *gemara* now turns to a *baraita* dealing with conditions that are not merely difficult to satisfy but physically impossible. Examples given: *al menat she-ta'ali la-rakiya* — on the condition that you ascend to heaven; *al menat she-terdi li-tehom* — that you descend to the depths; *al menat she-tivle'i kaneh shel arba amot* — that you swallow a pole four *amot* long; *al menat she-tavi'i li kaneh bein me'ah amah* — that you bring a pole a hundred *amot* in length; *al menat she-ta'avri et ha-yam ha-gadol be-raglayich* — that you cross the Mediterranean Sea on foot. The anonymous view of the *baraita* holds that this is *eino get* — not a valid *get*, because the condition is perpetually unmet, and she remains in suspended uncertainty.

### R. Yehuda ben Teima

Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima disagrees: *kaze get* — this is a valid *get*. His reasoning: *kol tnai she-ei efshar lo le-kayemo be-sofo, ve-hitnah alav mi-techilato, eino ela ke-miflig bi-devarim ve-kasher* — any condition that is impossible ever to fulfill, yet was stipulated at the outset, is merely an exaggerated empty utterance and does not constitute a real *tnai*. We therefore discard it, and the *get* stands unconditionally. The *tnai* is so obviously absurd that no reasonable person could have intended it as a genuine legal condition — it must be understood as hyperbole or bluster, and the *get* is therefore valid from the moment it was given.

### Rav Nachman's Ruling

Rav Nachman rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak adds that the *mishnah* in Bava Metzia corroborates this position by implication: it states *kol she-efshar lo le-kayemo be-sofo... tnao kayam* — any condition that *can* eventually be fulfilled is binding. The inference — *ha ei efshar, tnao batel* — if it cannot be fulfilled, the condition is void. This is taken as independent support for Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima's ruling.

## Impossible Condition (II)

### Abaye vs. Rava

With the ruling in favor of Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima established, the *gemara* now probes the precise scope of what "impossible to fulfill" means. The critical question: does the category of conditions that are voided as impossible extend only to physically impossible acts, or does it also include acts that are physically possible but *halakhically* forbidden?

---

## Forbidden Acts

### Abaye's Position

Abaye holds that *hi hi* — the case of a forbidden act is the same as the case of a physically impossible act. A condition such as *al menat she-tochli basar chazir* — on the condition that you eat pig meat — is thrown out and the *get* is valid unconditionally. While the woman could physically eat the *chazir*, she is not permitted to do so, and Abaye treats this as functionally equivalent to impossibility. The general principle stated in the *baraita* — "any condition that cannot be fulfilled" — is understood by Abaye to include forbidden acts, since they cannot legitimately be performed.

### Rava's Position

Rava disagrees. *Efshar de-achla ve-lakya* — it is possible that she will eat it and receive *malkot*. The act is physically executable. The fact that it carries a penalty does not remove it from the category of possible conditions. A condition that can in principle be met — even through violation — is a binding *tnai*, and the *get* is not valid unless the condition is actually fulfilled.

The *gemara* then maps out the implications of each position for the general rule (*klal*) stated by Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima. According to Abaye, the *klal* — "anything that cannot be fulfilled" — includes even a case like *basar chazir*, which is physically possible but forbidden. The phrase *kaze* ("like this," referring to the paradigmatic absurd cases of going to heaven) is understood to establish that only the most extreme physical impossibilities are the paradigm; the *klal* extends further to include forbidden acts. According to Rava, *kaze* refers precisely to absurd physical impossibilities, and the *klal* does not extend to forbidden acts. Rather, *basar chazir* — physically possible even if forbidden — remains a binding *tnai*.

### Sexual Conditions

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that addresses sexual conditions: *harei zeh gittech al menat she-tiv'ali le-ploni* — this is your *get* on the condition that you have sexual relations with so-and-so. If the condition is met, the *get* is valid. If not, it is not a *get*. This *baraita* presents the condition as binding, consistent with Rava's position that a physically possible act — even one that may entail legal or moral problems — remains an enforceable *tnai*.

### Others' Violations

The *baraita* continues with a contrasting case. If the husband stipulates: *al menat shelo tiv'ali le-aba u-le-avich* — on the condition that you do not have sexual relations with my father or your father — *ein chosheshin shema ni'valah lahem* — we are not concerned that she might violate this. The implication is that we treat compliance with this condition as essentially guaranteed, and it is therefore a valid *get*.

The *gemara* then asks: what if the formulation were reversed — *al menat she-tiv'ali le-aba u-le-avich* — on the condition that you *do* have relations with my father or your father? Would that be a binding condition? The *gemara* implies it would not be, and this seems to support Abaye's view that forbidden acts cannot be conditions. But Rava responds by distinguishing between this case and the *basar chazir* case.

### Rava's Synthesis

Rava's answer: *bishlama basar chazir, efshar de-achla ve-lakya* — eating *chazir* is physically possible; she does it, she gets *malkot*. Similarly, *al menat she-tiv'ali le-ploni* — on the condition that she has relations with some other man — that too is physically possible; *efshar de-mishachda bei be-mamona* — perhaps she could pay him, persuade him, and the act could occur. But *aba ve-avich mi avdi issura?* — can she ensure that her father-in-law or her own father will commit this sin? Here it is not merely that the act is forbidden; it is forbidden by someone *else*, over whom she has no control. The combination of the act being prohibited *and* requiring a violation by another person places it in the category of impossibility. One who is merely being asked to violate *herself* can still choose to do so — the condition is physically executable. But a condition requiring another person to commit a serious violation is treated as tantamount to impossible, because one cannot realistically compel another person to transgress. Rava thus draws a line: a violation that the woman herself commits can be a binding *tnai*; a violation that depends on someone else committing a transgression is not a realistic condition and is voided.

## Torah Violations

### Tosefta Challenge

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *Tosefta* that directly addresses the *basar chazir* case. The *Tosefta* states: *harei zeh gittech al menat she-tochli basar chazir* — this is your *get* on the condition that you eat pig meat. Similarly: if she was a non-*kohen* — *al menat she-tochli bi-terumah* — on the condition that she eat *terumah* (which a non-*kohen* is forbidden to eat). And if she was a *nezirah* — *al menat she-tishti yayin* — on the condition that she drink wine. In each of these cases, the *Tosefta* rules: *yitkayem ha-tnai harei zeh get; ve-im lav eino get* — if the condition is fulfilled, it is a valid *get*; if not, it is not. This confirms Rava's position that a forbidden act, being physically possible, constitutes a binding *tnai*. For Rava, the ruling is clear. But for Abaye it is difficult, since Abaye holds that such conditions should be voided.

### Abaye's Response

Abaye responds: *mi savrat divrei ha-kol hi?* — who says this *Tosefta* represents everyone? The *Tosefta* reflects only the view of the *Rabbanan*, not that of Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima. And recall: even the *Rabbanan* hold that an impossible condition is still binding — meaning the woman is simply stuck, unable to fulfill an impossible *tnai* and unable to remarry. According to the *Rabbanan*, the *tnai* is always enforced, regardless of whether it is possible or forbidden. Therefore the *Tosefta*'s ruling that *basar chazir* is a binding condition tells us nothing about Rabbi Yehuda ben Teima's view — which Abaye applies to forbidden acts as well, treating them as non-binding.

### Matne al Mah Shekatuv

At this point the *gemara* poses a more fundamental objection. Rather than debating whether forbidden acts are binding *tnai'im*, perhaps the entire category should be invalidated on a different basis entirely: the principle of *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* — stipulating against what is written in the Torah. The rule is *kol ha-matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah, tnao batel* — any stipulation that runs contrary to Torah law is void. A condition requiring a violation of *halakha* would seem to fall squarely within this principle and should therefore be voided automatically, prior to any debate between Abaye and Rava.

Rav Adda bar Rav Ika addresses this challenge. The principle of *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* does not apply to every condition that touches upon a Torah law. Rather, it applies specifically when someone is attempting to alter or eliminate the fundamental legal structure of a Torah institution. The classic example is *she'erah, kesutah, ve-onatah* — a husband who says "you are my wife, but I am exempt from providing you food, clothing, and conjugal rights." There, the husband is trying to maintain the institution of marriage while stripping out its Torah-mandated obligations. He is not merely attaching a condition to his marriage; he is attempting to restructure the institution itself, removing components that are intrinsic to it. That is *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah*, and it is voided.

But here — a *get* conditioned on eating *chazir* — the husband is not trying to alter the structure of *gittin*. He is issuing a fully normal *get*; he is simply conditioning its effectiveness on a specific act. The institution of *gittin* itself is unchanged. The husband is not saying "I give you a *get* but without some Torah obligation of divorce"; he is saying "here is a complete *get* that takes effect under a certain condition." That the condition happens to require a transgression does not transform it into a *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* in the technical sense.

### Rav Adda's Clarification

The distinction Rav Adda draws is thus structural: *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* refers to attempts to *uproot* a Torah institution — to have the institution exist but without one of its Torah-defined parameters. It does not refer to conditions that require a violation in order for an institution to take effect. In the case of *she'erah, kesutah, ve-onatah*, the institution of marriage is being invoked while simultaneously eliminating obligations that are constitutive of what Torah marriage means. That is an attempt to create a Torah institution on non-Torah terms. In the case of a conditional *get*, no Torah institution is being restructured. The *get* is a complete and ordinary *get*; it simply has not yet taken effect. Once the condition is met, the *get* will function exactly as the Torah envisions.

### Ravina's Objection

Ravina challenges this framework. When the woman eats the *chazir* in order to satisfy the condition of the *get*, who is causing the violation? It is the husband who stipulated this condition. *Shehu sachar deideh* — his benefit, the divorce, is what drives her to transgress. Can one really say the husband is not implicated in the violation she commits in order to satisfy *his* condition? Is this not itself a form of *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* — using the mechanism of a Torah institution to compel a violation?

Ravina ultimately resolves this by refining the application of the principle. *Matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* applies only when the Torah institution *certainly* and *definitively* uproots a Torah obligation — as in *she'erah, kesutah, ve-onatah*, where the husband's declaration of marriage is simultaneously and unconditionally a declaration that those obligations do not apply. In the *basar chazir* case, there is no certainty that any violation will occur. *Lo teichol ve-lo tisgaresh* — she need not eat the *chazir*, and she simply will not receive the *get*. The Torah violation is not embedded in the transaction; it is something she may or may not choose to do. The condition does not force a violation; it offers a choice. Therefore, one cannot say the husband's condition is itself a *matneh al mah she-katuv ba-Torah* — the Torah is not being overridden, because nothing in this transaction definitively requires that it be violated.
