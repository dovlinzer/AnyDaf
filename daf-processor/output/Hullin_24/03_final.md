# Chullin 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Parah vs. Eglah

### Intro: Two Rituals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פָּרָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה כְּשֵׁרָה, בַּעֲרִיפָה פְּסוּלָה; עֶגְלָה בַּעֲרִיפָה כְּשֵׁרָה, בִּשְׁחִיטָה פְּסוּלָה; (נמצאת) [נִמְצָא] כָּשֵׁר בַּפָּרָה – פָּסוּל בָּעֶגְלָה, כָּשֵׁר בָּעֶגְלָה – פָּסוּל בַּפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* in explanation of the mishna: With regard to the red **heifer, with slaughter** it is **fit; with breaking the neck** it is **unfit.** With regard to the **heifer** whose neck is broken, **with breaking the neck** it is **fit; with slaughter** it is **unfit. Consequently,** that which is **fit in** a red **heifer** is **unfit in a heifer** whose neck is broken; that which is **fit in a heifer** whose neck is broken is **unfit in** a red **heifer.**

Daf 24 opens by comparing two rituals performed with bovines outside of the Mikdash: the *parah adumah* and the *eglah arufah*. One is performed through shechitah; the other through arifah, the breaking of the neck — and not vice versa. We know that the *parah adumah* requires specifically shechitah because the Torah says veshachat and also employs the word chukah — zot chukat hatorah — indicating that the method is precisely prescribed.

### Chok Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא ״וְשָׁחַט״ וְחוּקָּה: בִּשְׁחִיטָה אִין, בַּעֲרִיפָה לָא. וְכֹל הֵיכָא דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ חוּקָּה, לָא דָּרְשִׁינַן קַל וָחוֹמֶר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **the verse states** with regard to the red heifer: **"And he shall slaughter** it" (Numbers 19:3), **and** it mentions the term **statute:** "This is the statute of the Torah" (Numbers 19:2), indicating that **with slaughter, yes,** the red heifer is rendered fit; **with breaking the neck,** the red heifer is **not** rendered fit. The Gemara asks: **And** is it so that **anywhere that statute is written with regard to** a certain matter, **we do not learn an *a fortiori*** inference?

### Yom Kippur Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא גַּבֵּי יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ חוּקָּה, וְתַנְיָא: ״וְעָשָׂהוּ חַטָּאת״ – הַגּוֹרָל עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת, וְאֵין הַשֵּׁם עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **But** what **about Yom Kippur, with regard to which statute is written:** "And this shall be an everlasting statute unto you" (Leviticus 16:34), **and** nevertheless **it is taught** in a *baraita*: "And Aaron shall bring forward the goat upon which the lot came up for the Lord, **and he shall offer it for a sin offering"** (Leviticus 16:9). The verse indicates that **the lottery renders** the goat **a sin offering, but a verbal designation** of the goat with the status of a sin offering **does not render** it **a sin offering.**

The Gemara demonstrates that chok does not block a kal vachomer by citing Yom HaKippurim, where the Torah uses the language chukat olam. Anyone who has learned Yoma knows that word chok is used there to establish that many of the details of Yom Kippur are meakeiv. And yet, as a braita shows, we do attempt a kal vachomer in the context of Yom Kippur. Specifically, the Torah states vehayetah zot lachem le-chukat olam in connection with the two goats: veasahu chattat — it is the goral, the drawing of lots, that determines which goat becomes the chattat and which is sent to the cliff as the la-azazel, and not a verbal declaration.

### Lots vs. Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא קִדֵּשׁ הַגּוֹרָל, קִדֵּשׁ הַשֵּׁם, מְקוֹם שֶׁקִּדֵּשׁ הַגּוֹרָל, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁקִּדֵּשׁ הַשֵּׁם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְעָשָׂהוּ חַטָּאת״, הַגּוֹרָל עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת, וְאֵין הַשֵּׁם עוֹשֶׂה חַטָּאת. טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְעָשָׂהוּ חַטָּאת״, הָא לָאו הָכִי דָּרְשִׁינַן קַל וָחוֹמֶר!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: A verse is necessary to teach this *halakha*, **as** one **might** have thought that the opposite conclusion is correct: **Could this not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference: If in a case where the lottery does not sanctify** the animal with a specific designation, such as in the case of two birds brought by a woman after childbirth, and nevertheless **a verbal designation** of that offering **sanctifies** it, in **a case where the lottery sanctifies** the animal on Yom Kippur, **isn't it logical that a verbal designation** as a sin offering **sanctifies it?** Therefore, **the verse states: "And render it a sin offering;" the lottery renders** the goat **a sin offering, but a verbal designation** of a sin offering **does not render** the goat **a sin offering.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** that the *a fortiori* inference is not learned is **that the Merciful One writes: "And he shall offer it for a sin offering." But otherwise we** would **learn an *a fortiori*** inference, despite the fact that statute is written with regard to the Yom Kippur service.

The braita raises the possibility of a kal vachomer: umah bemakom shelo kideish hagoral kideish hashem — where lots do not sanctify, verbal declaration does work (as Rashi illustrates with the case of two birds brought as a chattat and olah, where declaration designates which is which). If in that case declaration works but lots do not, then by the goats of Yom Kippur — where lots do work — how much more so should verbal declaration also function? The braita rejects this kal vachomer through the verse veasahu chattat, which teaches that only the goral designates the chattat, and not a verbal declaration: hagoral oseh chattat, ve'ein hashem oseh chattat.

The Gemara then draws the crucial conclusion: ta'ama — the reason we do not follow the kal vachomer — is dichsiv rachmana veasahu chattat, because the verse explicitly established that only the lot creates the designation. Halo hachi, had that verse not existed, we would have used the kal vachomer even in the presence of chok. This establishes that chok does not itself prevent a kal vachomer; only an explicit contrary verse does.

Returning to the original question: how then do we know that arifah cannot be performed for the *parah adumah*? If chok alone does not block the kal vachomer, the Gemara needs another source.

### Parah Cannot Use Arifah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי עֶגְלָה הָעֲרוּפָה, ״זֹאת״ בַּעֲרִיפָה, וְאֵין אַחֶרֶת בַּעֲרִיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Actually, one may learn an *a fortiori* inference even in a case where statute is written. Nevertheless, **with regard to** the **heifer** whose neck is broken, **the Merciful One restricts** the use of breaking the neck: "And all the Elders of that city…shall wash their hands over the heifer **whose neck is broken"** (Deuteronomy 21:6). From the relative pronoun "whose" it is derived: **This** heifer **is** killed **by breaking the neck, but no other,** i.e., the red heifer, is killed **by breaking the neck.**

The Gemara answers: *mi'eit rachmana* gabi eglah ha-arufah — the Torah minimizes the applicability of arifah through the language it uses by the calf whose neck is broken.

### Definite Article Exclusi…

The Torah uses the expression *eglah ha-arufah banachal* — the calf, the one with the broken neck. The definite article heh identifies this animal uniquely: zot ba-arifah ve'ein acheret ba-arifah — this one undergoes neck-breaking, and nothing else does. This excludes even the *parah adumah* from the method of arifah.

### Eglah & Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְהֵא עֶגְלָה כְּשֵׁרָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה פָּרָה שֶׁלֹּא הוּכְשְׁרָה בַּעֲרִיפָה – כְּשֵׁרָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה, עֶגְלָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁרָה בַּעֲרִיפָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁהוּכְשְׁרָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let it be** derived that the heifer whose neck is broken is rendered **fit with slaughter by means of an *a fortiori*** inference: **If** a red **heifer, which is not rendered fit with breaking the neck,** is rendered **fit with slaughter,** then with regard to **a heifer** whose neck is broken, **which** is rendered **fit with breaking the neck, isn't it logical that it is rendered fit with slaughter?**

Having established that the *parah adumah* cannot use arifah, the Gemara attempts the kal vachomer in the reverse direction: v'tehei eglah kesheirah be-shechita mi-kal vachomer? Since the *parah adumah*, which cannot use arifah, is nevertheless valid through shechitah, then the *eglah arufah*, which can use arifah, should certainly be valid through shechitah as well. Indeed, one might ordinarily assume that shechitah — the standard ritual method of slaughter — should be at least as acceptable as neck-breaking in any ritual context.

### Limiting Arifah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְעָרְפוּ הָעֶגְלָה״ – בַּעֲרִיפָה אִין, בִּשְׁחִיטָה לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds that **the verse states: "And shall break the neck of the heifer** there in the valley" (Deuteronomy 21:4). The doubled reference to breaking the neck in the two verses indicates that **by breaking the neck, yes,** the heifer may be killed; **by slaughter,** the heifer may **not** be killed.

The Gemara rejects this: amar kra *v'arfu ha-eglah* — the Torah says "they shall break the neck of the calf." Rashi notes that the decisive point is the repetition: the Torah had already indicated that arifah is performed, and when it states ve'arfu ha-eglah a second time, this repetition is limiting — ba-arifah in, be-shechitah lo — only through neck-breaking, not through shechitah. The Gemara notes that Tosafot is quite troubled by the underlying logic of these hava amina arguments — why would one have assumed in the first place that one distinctly prescribed ritual method could substitute for another? Tosafot attempts several approaches, but the hava amina remains difficult.

## Kohanim vs. Leviyim

### Initial Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּהֲנִים, בְּמוּמִין – פְּסוּלִים, בְּשָׁנִים – כְּשֵׁרִים. לְוִיִּם, בְּמוּמִין – כְּשֵׁרִים, בְּשָׁנִים – פְּסוּלִים. נִמְצָא כָּשֵׁר בַּכֹּהֲנִים – פָּסוּל בַּלְוִיִּם, כָּשֵׁר בַּלְוִיִּם – פָּסוּל בַּכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* in explanation of the mishna: **Priests are** rendered **unfit** for Temple service **with** the **blemishes** enumerated in the Torah (see Leviticus 21:16–23), but remain **fit with** the passage of **years,** as from the moment that they reach majority they are fit for service for the rest of their lives. **Levites** remain **fit** for Temple service **with** the **blemishes** enumerated in the Torah but **are unfit with** the passage of **years,** as they are fit for service only between the ages of thirty and fifty (see Numbers 4:47). **It is found** that there is an element with which **priests** remain **fit** and **Levites are unfit,** and there is an element with which **Levites** remain **fit** and **priests are unfit.**

The Gemara transitions to another case of inverse rules. The braita states: Kohanim be-mumin psulin, be-shanim ksheirin — Kohanim are invalidated by physical blemishes, but age presents no disqualifying limit. The reverse holds for Leviyim: be-mumin ksheirin, be-shanim psulin — blemishes present no problem, but age does, since the Torah establishes that Leviyim serve between the ages of 30 and 50, and upon reaching 50, yashuv mitzva ha-avoda velo ya'avod od — he shall return from the army of his service and no longer serve.

What makes this case different from the previous ones — and genuinely surprising — is that here the potentially transferable disqualifications are not arbitrary ritual characteristics of objects but substantive personal conditions: physical deficiency on one hand, age-based incapacity on the other. Moreover, both Kohanim and Leviyim serve in the Mikdash, and a Kohen is in fact a subset of the tribe of Levi. One might well have argued that if blemishes disqualify Kohanim, they should certainly disqualify Leviyim as well — and that if age disqualifies Leviyim, it should at least disqualify Kohanim.

### Leviyim Age Derived

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״זֹאת אֲשֶׁר לַלְוִיִּם״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִבֶּן חֲמִשִּׁים שָׁנָה יָשׁוּב״, לָמַדְנוּ לַלְוִיִּם שֶׁהַשָּׁנִים פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם. יָכוֹל מוּמִין פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם? וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁאֵין הַשָּׁנִים פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶן, מוּמִין פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶן – לְוִיִּם שֶׁהַשָּׁנִים פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהוּ מוּמִין פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֹאת אֲשֶׁר לַלְוִיִּם״ – זֹאת לַלְוִיִּם, וְאֵין אַחֶרֶת לַלְוִיִּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **"This is that which pertains to the Levites"** (Numbers 8:24); **why** must **the verse state** this? **Since it is stated: "And from the age of fifty years he shall return** from the service" (Numbers 8:25), **we learned with regard to the Levites that** the passage of **years disqualifies them.** One **might** have thought that **blemishes disqualify them** too. **And** ostensibly, it could be learned through **logical inference: If priests,** with regard to **whom** the passage of **years does not disqualify them, blemishes disqualify them,** then in the case of **Levites,** with regard to **whom** the passage of **years disqualifies them, isn't it logical that blemishes disqualify them?** Therefore, **the verse states: "This is that which pertains to the Levites,"** from which it is derived: **"This,"** the passage of years, is a disqualification that pertains **to the Levites, and there is no other** disqualification that pertains **to the Levites.**

The Gemara asks, mena hani milei — where does this come from? The braita cites the verse zot asher la-Levi'im and connects it with the verse umiben hamishim shanah yashuv, establishing that years disqualify Leviyim. The relevant pesukim are in Behaalotecha, where after describing the ritual dedication of the Leviyim the Torah states: miben chamesh ve-esrim shanah va-ma'alah yavo *litzvo tzava* ba-avodat ohel mo'ed — from 25 and above they shall come to serve in the army of the Ohel Mo'ed — and then umiben hamishim shanah yashuv mitzva ha-avoda velo ya'avod od. The imagery of military service — *litzvo tzava* — applied to the Leviyim mirrors the language used when Bnei Yisrael were counted for the actual army from age 20 onward.

From here the braita establishes: lamadnu la-Levi'im shehashanim poslim bahem — we learn that years disqualify Leviyim.

### Rejecting Kal for Leviyim

Once age is established as a disqualifier for Leviyim, the braita raises the question: yachol mumin poslim bahem — perhaps blemishes also invalidate Leviyim? The kal vachomer would run: Kohanim, for whom age is not a problem, are nonetheless disqualified by blemishes — how much more so should Leviyim, for whom age is a problem, be disqualified by blemishes? The Torah preempts this inference with the phrase zot asher la-Levi'im: this — and only this — is the disqualifying criterion for Leviyim. Ve'ein acheret la-Levi'im — there is no additional criterion. Blemishes are excluded.

### Age & Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים פְּסוּלִין בְּשָׁנִים? וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה לְוִיִּם שֶׁאֵין מוּמִין פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם – שָׁנִים פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם, כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁהַמּוּמִין פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהוּ שָׁנִים פּוֹסְלִין בָּהֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲשֶׁר לַלְוִיִּם״ – וְלֹא אֲשֶׁר לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that **priests would be disqualified with** the passage of **years. And** ostensibly, **could this not** be derived through the following *a fortiori* **inference: If Levites,** with regard to **whom blemishes do not disqualify them,** the passage of **years disqualifies them,** then in the case of **priests,** with regard to **whom blemishes disqualify them, isn't it logical** that the passage of **years disqualifies them?** Therefore, **the verse states: "Which pertains to the Levites," and not which pertains to the priests.**

The braita then attempts the reverse. Yachol yehu ha-Kohanim psulin ba-shanim — perhaps Kohanim are also disqualified by age? The kal vachomer: Leviyim, for whom blemishes are not a problem, are nonetheless disqualified by age — how much more so should Kohanim, for whom blemishes are a problem, be disqualified by age? This argument is reinforced by the fact, raised in the discussion, that Kohanim are a subset of Leviyim — if anything, one would expect them to carry at least as many, if not more, disqualifications.

### Reject Reverse Kal

The Torah again blocks this inference: asher la-Levi'im — this is for Leviyim — velo asher la-Kohanim. The age-based disqualification belongs to Leviyim alone and does not extend to Kohanim.

### Ohel Moed Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אַף בְּשִׁילֹה וּבְבֵית עוֹלָמִים כֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לַעֲבֹד עֲבֹדַת עֲבוֹדָה וַעֲבֹדַת מַשָּׂא״, לֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָעֲבוֹדָה בַּכָּתֵף.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that the Levites were disqualified with the passage of years **even in Shiloh,** the permanent place of the Tabernacle, **and in the eternal Temple.** Therefore, **the verse states: "To perform the work of service, and the work of bearing burdens"** (Numbers 4:47), juxtaposing the two forms of Levite service to teach: **I stated** the disqualification of the passage of years **only at a time when** there is Levite **service** involving carrying the Tabernacle **on** their **shoulders.**

The Gemara now raises a crucial limitation that was not stated in the Mishnah itself: yachol af be-Shilo uve-Veit Olamim ken? Perhaps the age restriction for Leviyim applies even in Shilo and in the permanent *beit haMikdash* in Yerushalayim? The verse teaches otherwise: la'avod avodat avodah va-avodat masa — the service designated for the 30-to-50 age range combines the avodat avodah, meaning the singing that accompanies the korbanot, together with avodat masa, the carrying of the Mishkan. The Gemara derives from this juxtaposition: lo amarti ela mizman she-ha-avoda ba-katef — this age restriction applies only when the service includes physical carrying.

### Carrying Requirement

Once the Jewish people entered the land and established a permanent or semi-permanent sanctuary — first in Shilo, then in Yerushalayim — the Leviyim were no longer required to transport the Mishkan on their shoulders. Consequently, the age restriction no longer applies. The capacity to carry was the functional rationale behind requiring a certain age range, and once carrying ceased to be part of the service, age ceased to be the operative criterion. It is notable that once they are above 50 and exempt from service, the *torah does* say that they may still serve in mishmar, guard duty, as the Rambam explains — guarding the Mikdash is a form of showing honor and respect to a sacred place, much as important institutions post sentries as a mark of dignity.

### 25 vs. 30 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״מִבֶּן חָמֵשׁ וְעֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה וָמַעְלָה״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״מִבֶּן שְׁלֹשִׁים״, אִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר שְׁלֹשִׁים, שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר חָמֵשׁ וְעֶשְׂרִים, וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹמַר חָמֵשׁ וְעֶשְׂרִים, שֶׁכְּבָר נֶאֱמַר שְׁלֹשִׁים. הָא כֵּיצַד? חָמֵשׁ וְעֶשְׂרִים לְתַלְמוּד, וּשְׁלֹשִׁים לַעֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* notes that **one verse states: "From twenty-five years old and upward"** (Numbers 8:24), **and one verse states: "From thirty years old** and upward" (Numbers 4:47). It is **impossible to say thirty, as twenty-five is already stated, and** it is **impossible to say twenty-five, as thirty is already stated. How** can these verses be reconciled? **Twenty-five** years old is the time **for apprenticeship and thirty for service.**

A textual problem must be addressed. The Torah in Naso and Bamidbar counts the Leviyim from 30 to 50 — miben shloshim shanah ve-ad ben hamishim shanah kol haba *litzvo tzava* ba-avodat ohel mo'ed — the exact same phrase *litzvo tzava* used in Behaalotecha, where the starting age is 25. These two figures appear to be in direct contradiction.

The resolution: kaf-hei lilmod u-shloshim la-avodah — at 25 one begins studying and training; at 30 one enters active service. There is a five-year apprenticeship period. The Gemara even notes a subtle textual hint in the choice of language: when referring to age 25, the verse uses yavo litzvo, connoting coming toward a future entry, whereas the counting from 30 describes those already fully engaged in the service — though as the Gemara itself notes, the exact same phrase *litzvo tzava* appears in both contexts, so this linguistic distinction may not be definitive.

What is clear is that the resolution is: five years of training and practice, followed by twenty years of active service from 30 to 50.

### Five-Year Mastery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁלֹּא רָאָה סִימָן יָפֶה בְּמִשְׁנָתוֹ חָמֵשׁ שָׁנִים – שׁוּב אֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְגַדְּלָם שָׁנִים שָׁלֹשׁ״, ״וּלְלַמְּדָם סֵפֶר וּלְשׁוֹן כַּשְׂדִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** **From here** it is derived that **a student who did not see a positive indication in his studies** after **five years will no longer see** a productive result from those studies. **Rabbi Yosei says** that the period is **three years, as it is stated** with regard to Daniel and his cohort who instructed the king of Babylonia: **"And they should be raised three years"** (Daniel 1:5), **"and he should teach them the books and the language of the Chaldeans"** (Daniel 1:4).

The Gemara draws a broader principle from this five-year training period: letalmid shelo ra'ah siman yafeh be-mishnato chamesh shanim, shuv eino roeh — a student who has not shown positive progress in mastering a subject within five years will not succeed in it. The implication is practical: if after five years of serious study one has not achieved command of the material, it is likely time to consider a different direction.

### Three vs. Five Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ – שָׁאנֵי לְשׁוֹן כַּשְׂדִּים, דְּקַלִּיל. וְאִידַּךְ – שָׁאנֵי הִלְכוֹת עֲבוֹדָה, דְּתַקִּיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** *tanna* explain the verses in Daniel? The Gemara answers: He holds that the verses in Daniel cannot be cited as a source for this principle because **the language of the Chaldeans is different, as** it is **easy** and can be learned in a shorter period. The Gemara asks: **And** how does **the other** *tanna*, Rabbi Yosei, explain the verses with regard to the Levites? The Gemara answers: He holds that **the *halakhot* of** Temple **service are different, as they are difficult** and require a longer period of study.

Rabbi Yosei disagrees with the five-year figure: he holds that three years is the threshold — as derived from the verse in Daniel, u-legadlam shanim shalosh, referring to the three-year program in which the captive nobles of Jerusalem were taught sefer and leshon Kasdim, the literature and language of the Chaldeans. If mastery is not achieved in three years, Rabbi Yosei holds, it will not come.

The Gemara reconciles the two opinions: shani lashon Kasdim de-kalil — the language of the Chaldeans is relatively easy to acquire and therefore requires only three years; while shani hilchos avoda de-tkifin — the laws of the Temple service are particularly demanding and therefore require a full five years. The baseline for most areas lies somewhere between them, with the five-year rule applying to more complex disciplines and the three-year rule to simpler ones. One might have thought the Leviyim required little time to master their duties — after all, their service was singing and playing instruments, not the intricate halachic performance of the Kohanim — yet the Gemara uses the language hilchos avoda de-tkifin, suggesting that the full mastery of their service was considered genuinely demanding.

## Conceptual Framework

### Kohen as Kli Kodesh

Having established the halachic rules, the Gemara's discussion invites us to consider what underlying conceptual distinction drives this difference. The Rambam, in his *mishneh torah*, groups the laws pertaining to Kohanim together with the laws of klei hamikdash vehamishartim bo — the sacred vessels of the Mikdash and those who serve within it. The Kohanim are treated, in an important sense, as klei kodesh — holy vessels. This is not merely a metaphor. The Torah describes the Mikdash as a place of overwhelming aesthetic and ritual perfection: everything is gold, silver, and beautiful; the korbanot are unblemished; every object that participates in the service of the Mikdash is required to be free of blemish. The Kohen, as one of the functioning elements of the Mikdash, participates in this same demand for perfection.

Many details of halachah reflect this conception of the Kohen as a kind of sacred vessel: the Kohen does not wear shoes in the Mikdash because the *ritzpa mekadeshes* — the sanctity of the floor connects through his bare feet to him, and through him to the korban. The concept of be-atzmo shel Kohen — the body of the Kohen as a site of sacred status — appears in multiple contexts, including the bedikos. It is the Kohen's very person that must conform to the standard of unblemished holiness required of the Mikdash's sacred contents.

### Levi as Servant

The Levi, by contrast, is never cast in those terms. Although the *torah does* wave the Leviyim as a kind of korban during their dedication — treating them in some respect like a sacrificial offering — the Torah uses the word kodesh far less for them than it does for Kohanim. What dominates the description of the Leviyim is the language of service and assistance: *netunim netunim* hem lo — they are given over, designated to assist the Kohanim. The very name Levi connotes attachment and service. They are not presented as holy vessels but as capable servants.

What disqualifies a servant is not aesthetics but incapacity. The Levi's disqualifying criterion is therefore functional: can he perform the service he is assigned to? When the service involved carrying the Mishkan — physically demanding, requiring strength and endurance — age was the relevant criterion. A Levi too young had not yet reached full physical maturity, and one past 50 was presumed to have declined in physical capacity. When the service no longer involved carrying but was limited to song and musical accompaniment, the relevant criterion became the voice: ve'afilu mumin poslim bahem, a defective voice would disqualify — not because of any concern for the aesthetics of the Mikdash, but because he cannot perform the job he is there to do.

### Soloveitchik

Rav Soloveitchik in his Shiurim le-Zecher Abba Mari develops this distinction at length. His formulation is that the Levi's service is fundamentally role-based, while the Kohen's service involves being fully given over — a total sanctification of his person. He adduces a striking illustration: if a Kohen declares that he objects in principle to any one aspect of the kehunah — that he finds some element of it distasteful or refuses it — he is not a valid Kohen. The Kohen must be entirely surrendered to the whole of the service; any reservation undermines the totality of the dedication.

By contrast, the Levi's service is explicitly divided by role. Some Leviyim are assigned to the gates; others to the singing. A Levi designated for one role may not perform the other. Rav Soloveitchik sees in this precisely the contrast: the Kohen's service flows from a state of total sanctification — any crack in that totality compromises the whole. The Levi's service is about assignment to a specific function, and it is the ability to discharge that function that alone determines his fitness. This framework illuminates why mumin disqualify the Kohen — because he must be an unblemished kli kodesh — while shanim, age, disqualifies the Levi — because it determines whether he is capable of the work he has been assigned.

## Specific Requirements

### Kohanim Age Range

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּהֵן מִשֶּׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת עַד שֶׁיַּזְקִין – כָּשֵׁר לַעֲבוֹדָה, וּמוּמִין פּוֹסְלִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A priest, from** the time he reaches puberty and **grows two** pubic **hairs until he ages, is fit for** Temple **service, and blemishes disqualify him.**

The Gemara now spells out the precise parameters. Kol mi she-hevi *shtei searot* *ad she-yazkin* — a Kohen is kasher for service from the time he reaches halachic adulthood, marked by the appearance of *shtei searot*, two pubic hairs, until he becomes old. U-mumin poslin bo — and blemishes disqualify him throughout. There is no fixed upper age limit, as there was for Leviyim in the Ohel Mo'ed; rather, the criterion is a functional one determined individually.

### Leviyim Age 30–50

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן לֵוִי מִבֶּן שְׁלֹשִׁים וְעַד בֶּן חֲמִשִּׁים כָּשֵׁר לַעֲבוֹדָה, וְשָׁנִים פּוֹסְלִין בּוֹ. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד שֶׁבַּמִּדְבָּר, אֲבָל בְּשִׁילֹה וּבְבֵית עוֹלָמִים אֵין נִפְסָלִין אֶלָּא בְּקוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **A Levite from the age of thirty until the age of fifty is fit for** Temple **service, and** the passage of **years disqualifies him. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to the Tent of Meeting** of the Tabernacle **in the wilderness. But with regard to Shiloh and in the eternal Temple,** Levites **are disqualified only** due to a change **in voice** that renders them unable to recite the songs in the Temple with their brethren.

*Ben Levi* mi-ben shloshim ve-ad ben hamishim *kasher la-avodah* — a Levi is fit for service from 30 to 50, in the context of the Ohel Mo'ed in the midbar. Ve-shanim poslin bo — age disqualifies, both as too old and by implication too young, since only from 30 onward is one counted.

### Leviyim Voice Only

*Bameh devarim amurim* — when does this apply? Be-Ohel Mo'ed she-ba-midbar — only in the Ohel Mo'ed in the desert. Aval be-Shilo u-Veit Olamim — but in Shilo and in the permanent *beit haMikdash*, ein nifsalim ela be-kol — they are disqualified only on account of their voice. This is the elegant internal logic of the Levi's functional criterion: when the service was about carrying, the criterion was physical capacity as measured by age. When the service is about singing, the criterion is vocal capacity. If the voice remains strong, age is no disqualifier.

### Voice Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַאי קְרָא? ״וַיְהִי כְאֶחָד לַמְחַצְּצרִים וְלַמְשֹׁרְרִים לְהַשְׁמִיעַ קוֹל אֶחָד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said: What is the verse** from which this is derived? **"It came to pass, when the trumpeters and singers were as one, to make one sound to be heard"** (II Chronicles 5:13). This indicates that the Levites must be capable of singing in one voice, and one who is unable to do so is unfit for service.

Amar Rav Yossi: mai kra — what verse establishes that even elderly Leviyim may sing as long as their voice is good? *vayehi ke-echad la-mechatztzerim vela-meshorerim le-hashmia kol echad* — they were like one, the trumpeters and the singers together, producing a single unified voice. Rashi explains that the emphasis of vayehi ke-echad is to include even those who are older, who join in the unified choral sound. The verse encompasses them because they are still capable of contributing to the kol echad. Notably, this verse also addresses the playing of musical instruments alongside the singing — a point relevant to the broader discussion of which roles belong specifically to the Leviyim and how central each is to their designated service.

### Trembling Criterion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁיַּזְקִין – עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: עַד שֶׁיְּרַתֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that the priest is eligible for service **until he ages.** The Gemara asks: **Until when,** i.e., what is the definition of aging in this context? **Rabbi Ela says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says: Until** his hands and feet begin to **tremble.**

Returning to the Kohanim, *ad she-yazkin* — until he becomes old — raises the question: *ad kama*, how old is too old? Amar Rabbi Ila amar Rabbi Chanina: ad she-yiratev — until he trembles. There is no fixed year cutoff; the criterion is physical infirmity manifest as unsteadiness. Each person's fitness is assessed individually.

This prompts the Gemara to juxtapose a related discussion from another context. A braita teaches: baal keri she-taval velo hetil mayim — someone who had a seminal emission and immersed in the mikvah but did not urinate afterward — u-le-cheshe-yatil tamei — when he subsequently urinates, he is tamei, because a residual drop of semen is assumed to emerge with the urine. Rabbi Yossi qualifies: be-choleh u-ve-zaken tamei — this concern applies only to a sick person or an elderly person, whose full seminal discharge cannot be assumed. Be-yeled u-be-viri, tahor — for a young and healthy person, we assume complete discharge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בַּעַל קֶרִי שֶׁטָּבַל וְלֹא הֵטִיל מַיִם, לִכְשֶׁיָּטִיל – טָמֵא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּחוֹלֶה וּבְזָקֵן – טָמֵא, בְּיֶלֶד וּבְבָרִיא – טָהוֹר. יֶלֶד עַד כַּמָּה? אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כֹּל שֶׁעוֹמֵד עַל רַגְלוֹ אַחַת וְחוֹלֵץ מִנְעָלוֹ וְנוֹעֵל מִנְעָלוֹ. אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא, שֶׁהָיָה בֶּן שְׁמוֹנִים שָׁנָה וְהָיָה עוֹמֵד עַל רַגְלוֹ אַחַת וְחוֹלֵץ מִנְעָלוֹ וְנוֹעֵל מִנְעָלוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: חַמִּין וָשֶׁמֶן שֶׁסָּכַתְנִי אִמִּי בְּיַלְדוּתִי הֵן עָמְדוּ לִי בְּעֵת זִקְנוּתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Mikvaot* 8:4): With regard to **one who experienced a seminal emission who** then **immersed** in a ritual bath **and did not urinate** before immersing, **when he urinates he is ritually impure,** because residue of the semen remain in his body and was discharged with the urine, rendering him impure. **Rabbi Yosei says: In** the case of **an ill** person **and an elderly** person, he is **ritually impure; in** the case of **a young** person **and a healthy** person, he is **ritually pure,** as the semen was presumably discharged in its entirety at the outset. **Until when** is one considered **a young** person? **Rabbi Ela says** that **Rabbi Ḥanina says: Anyone who** is able to **stand on one of his legs and remove his shoe or put on his shoe** is considered young. **They said about Rabbi Ḥanina that he was eighty years old and would stand on one of his legs and remove his shoe or put on his shoe. Rabbi Ḥanina says:** The **hot water and oil that my mother smeared on me in my youth benefited me in my old age.**

Yeled *ad kama* — how young qualifies as yeled for this purpose? Amar Rabbi Ila amar Rabbi Chanina: kol she-omed al raglo achat ve-choletz minalo ve-noel minalo — anyone who can stand on one foot and remove and put on his shoe. This is a test of balance and physical stability. Amru alav al Rabbi Chanina she-haya ben shemonim shanah ve-haya omed al raglo achat ve-choletz minalo ve-noel minalo — it was said of Rabbi Chanina himself that at 80 years of age he could stand on one foot and take off and put on his shoe. He attributed this to the hot water and oil with which his mother anointed him in his youth: chamin ve-shemen she-sachtani immi be-yalduti hen amdu li be-et ziknuti — they stood by him in his old age. The test in this braita, while not directly parallel to the Kohen's service, illustrates the same principle: fitness is assessed individually based on demonstrated physical capacity, not by a fixed numerical cutoff.

### Kohen Ritual Roles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נִתְמַלֵּא זְקָנוֹ, רָאוּי לֵיעָשׂוֹת שְׁלִיחַ צִיבּוּר, וְלֵירֵד לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה, וְלִישָּׂא אֶת כַּפָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If **one's beard** is **fully** grown, **he is fit to be appointed an emissary of the community** for various matters, **and to descend before the ark** as a prayer leader, **and to lift his hands** for the Priestly Benediction.

The Gemara presents another braita on the theme of age and service. Nitmalei zikno — when a person's beard is filled out, raui *laasot shaliach tzibbur* — he is fit to serve as *shaliach tzibbur*, veleired lifnei ha-teivah — to lead davening, velisa et kapav — and if he is a Kohen, to perform Nesi'at Kapayim, the priestly blessing. Rashi explains that *shaliach tzibbur* in this context is not limited to leading prayer; it encompasses communal leadership roles more broadly: *litkoah shofar* — blowing the shofar on *rosh hashanah*, velinot — pronouncing excommunication, velimnot parnas — being appointed as a lay communal leader. In all of these functions, a mature appearance is a prerequisite — not a technical halachic threshold so much as a social requirement of communal respect. The lists found in various Gemarot discussing the qualifications of a chazan mention age, marital status, and communal acceptance — but conspicuously do not explicitly require a fine voice, though this is addressed separately.

### Rebbi's 20-Year Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאֵימָתַי כָּשֵׁר לָעֲבוֹדָה? מִשֶּׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי, עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּעֲמִידוּ [אֶת] הַלְוִיִּם מִבֶּן עֶשְׂרִים שָׁנָה וָמַעְלָה לְנַצֵּחַ עַל מְלֶאכֶת בֵּית ה׳״, וְאִידַּךְ – ״לְנַצֵּחַ״ שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **From when** is **he fit for** Temple **service?** It is **from** the time he reaches puberty and **grows two** pubic **hairs. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say** that he is not fit for Temple service **until he is twenty** years of **age. Rav Ḥisda said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? The reason is **as it is written: "And appointed the Levites, from twenty years old and upward, to oversee of the work of the House of the Lord"** (Ezra 3:8). **And** what does **the other** *tanna* hold? He holds that **to oversee is different** and requires an older priest.

On the question of when a Kohen may begin his service, the Mishnah states misheyavi *shtei searot* — from the time he achieves halachic adulthood. Rebbi, however, says: omer ani, *ad ben esrim* — I say, not until age 20. Rav Chisda explains Rebbi's source: the verse vayaamidu et ha-Levi'im miben esrim shanah va-malah le-natzeach al melechet *beit hashem* — the Leviyim were appointed from the age of 20 to oversee the work of *beit hashem*. Rashi interprets lenatzeach here as referring to physically demanding service that requires great fortitude and strength. The implication drawn is that service in the Mikdash in general requires this level of maturity.

The Gemara then asks: but this verse is written about Leviyim — how can it be applied to Kohanim? This is resolved through the principle stated by Rabbi Yehoshua *ben levi*: be-esrim ve-arba mekomot nikru Kohanim Levi'im — in twenty-four places in the Torah, Kohanim are referred to as Levi'im. This is one of them — as illustrated by the verse veha-Kohanim ha-Levi'im bnei Tzadok, where Tzadok was unmistakably a Kohen, yet described as a Levite. Rashi adds that the name Levi itself can connote service — v'ani shamati lashon Levi'im shamashim, citing kmo ve-yilavu alecha ve-ishartucha — they shall accompany and assist you — so that calling Kohanim Levi'im underscores their role as servants and assistants in the Mikdash. Against Rebbi's position, the Gemara raises the objection that this verse specifically concerns a particular kind of overseer work — likely the physical construction work in the time of Ezra — rather than the standard service of Kohanim in the Mikdash.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הַאי קְרָא בִּלְוִיִּם כְּתִיב? כִּדְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה מְקוֹמוֹת נִקְרְאוּ כֹּהֲנִים ״לְוִיִּם״, וְזֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶן: ״וְהַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם בְּנֵי צָדוֹק״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** what proof can be cited from this verse with regard to priests; **isn't that verse written with regard to Levites?** The Gemara answers: It is understood **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: In twenty-four places** in the Bible the **priests are called Levites. And this is one of those** verses: **"And the priests the Levites, the sons of Zadok"** (Ezekiel 44:15). The verse in Ezra is another one of the verses.

### Respect vs. Invalidity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִישׁ מִזַּרְעֲךָ לְדֹרֹתָם״, מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: קָטָן פָּסוּל לַעֲבוֹדָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ תָּם. מֵאֵימָתַי כָּשֵׁר לַעֲבוֹדָה? מִשֶּׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, אֲבָל אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים אֵין מַנִּיחִין אוֹתוֹ לַעֲבוֹד עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: הָא רַבִּי הִיא, וַאֲפִילּוּ פָּסוּל דְּרַבָּנַן לֵית לֵיהּ. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: רַבִּי אִית לֵיהּ פָּסוּל מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וְהָא רַבָּנַן הִיא, וּלְכַתְּחִלָּה הוּא דְּלָא, אֲבָל דִּיעֲבַד – עֲבוֹדָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"Any man of your descendants throughout their generations** that has a blemish shall not approach to offer the bread of his God" (Leviticus 21:17); **from here Rabbi Elazar says: A minor** priest is **unfit for** Temple **service, even** if he is **unblemished,** as he is not a man. **From when** is **he fit for service? From** the time he reaches puberty and **grows two** pubic **hairs. But his brethren the priests do not allow him to perform the service until he is twenty** years of **age.** **There are** those **who say: This is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and he is** of the opinion that there is **no disqualification** for one between puberty and twenty years of age **even by rabbinic law.** The other priests simply do not allow priests of that age to perform the Temple service *ab initio*. **And there are** those **who say: Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is of** the opinion that there is **disqualification by rabbinic law** in that case, **and this** statement in the *baraita* **is** the opinion of **the Rabbis, and** they hold that **it is *ab initio* that** one may **not** perform the service, **but after the fact, his service is valid.**

The Gemara presents a final ambiguity about the status of a Kohen younger than twenty. Aval echav ha-Kohanim ein manichin oto la-avodah ad she-yehei ben esrim — his fellow Kohanim will not allow him to perform the avodah until he is twenty, out of respect for the dignity of the service.

The Gemara records two views: ika de-amri ha Rebbi hi — some say this reflects Rebbi's position that twenty is a genuine requirement; ve-afilu psul de-rabbanan leit leh — and even so, Rebbi would hold that less than twenty is not even a rabbinic invalidity, only a social convention among the Kohanim. Ve-ika de-amri — others say that Rebbi does hold twenty is a genuine rabbinically-imposed invalidity; and the ruling that fellow Kohanim prevent the younger one from serving reflects the view of the other Rabbis, for whom it is merely a matter of lechatchilah — they prefer not to allow it, but bedi'eved avodato ksheirah — after the fact, the service is valid.

This discussion illuminates a distinction that runs through the entire treatment of age as a criterion: some age thresholds are hard halachic invalidities, while others are expressions of communal respect and dignity. Just as one might feel that it is inappropriate for a very young person to lead the Yamim Noraim davening for a community — even if they have a beautiful voice — the Torah and rabbinic tradition reflect an awareness that who represents and serves a community carries weight beyond the technical question of minimum capacity.

Tosafot raises a practical question arising from all of this: since children accompany their fathers to the duchan for Birkas Kohanim, and these children are clearly below *bar mitzvah* age, how does this square with the requirement of *shtei searot*? Tosafot resolves this by distinguishing between going up alone and going up alongside adults: when adults of proper age are already performing the mitzvah, a younger Kohen may join them. The concern about communal respect is satisfied by the presence of the qualified adults; the child's accompanying participation does not compromise the blessing. However, this leniency appears to apply to Birkas Kohanim specifically; in the context of actual Temple service, the age threshold of thirteen — or twenty per Rebbi — appears to be treated more strictly, with or without other Kohanim present.

## Pottery & Tumah

### Initial Rule Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָהוֹר בִּכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ – טָמֵא בְּכׇל הַכֵּלִים, טָהוֹר בְּכׇל הַכֵּלִים – טָמֵא בִּכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: That which is **ritually pure in an earthenware vessel** is **ritually impure in all the** other types of **vessels;** that which is **ritually pure in all the** other types of **vessels** is **ritually impure in an earthenware vessel.**

The Mishnah presents yet another case of inverse rules: tahor bikli cheres tamei bechol ha-keilim, tahor bechol ha-keilim tamei bikli cheres — what renders a pottery vessel tahor is what renders other vessels tamei, and what renders other vessels tahor is what renders a pottery vessel tamei.

### Space vs. Contact

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֲוִיר כְּלִי חֶרֶשׂ טָמֵא, וְגַבּוֹ טָהוֹר. אֲוִיר כׇּל הַכֵּלִים טָהוֹר, וְגַבָּן טָמֵא. נִמְצָא, טָהוֹר בִּכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ טָמֵא בְּכׇל הַכֵּלִים, טָהוֹר בְּכׇל הַכֵּלִים טָמֵא בִּכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* explaining the mishna: If a primary source of ritual impurity fell into the **airspace of an earthenware vessel** the vessel is **ritually impure, and** if it fell on **its outer side,** the vessel is **ritually pure.** If a primary source of ritual impurity fell into the **airspace of all the** other types of **vessels,** the vessels are **ritually pure, and** if it fell on **their outer side,** they are **ritually impure. It is found** that that which is **ritually pure in an earthenware vessel** is **ritually impure in all the** other **vessels,** and that which is **ritually pure in all the** other **vessels** is **ritually impure in an earthenware vessel.**

The operative distinction is this: for all ordinary vessels, tumah is transmitted through contact — a sheretz or other source of tumah that touches the vessel, whether on its exterior or interior surface, transmits tumah. For pottery vessels, the mechanism is entirely different: what matters is not contact but presence within the contained airspace — the avir — of the vessel. If a tamei object is suspended in midair within a pottery vessel without touching its walls, the vessel becomes tamei. Conversely, if that same object touches the outside of a pottery vessel, the vessel remains tahor entirely. The back of a pottery vessel — gabo — is impervious to tumah; avir kli cheres tamei, gabo tahor.

The reverse applies to ordinary vessels: avir kol ha-keilim tahor, gaban tamei — presence in the interior airspace of a non-pottery vessel does not transmit tumah, but external contact does.

### Kol Asher Betocho

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״תּוֹכוֹ״, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָגַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* based on the verse: "And every earthenware vessel into which [*tokho*] any of them falls, whatever is in it [*tokho*] shall be impure, and it you shall break" (Leviticus 11:33); if an impure item fell **"in it [*tokho*]," and even** in a case **where** the impure item **did not come into contact** with the vessel, the vessel becomes impure.

The Gemara asks, *vahani mili mena lan* — what is the scriptural basis? The braita cites the verse kol asher be-tocho — everything that is within it — said in connection with a pottery vessel. The verse makes tamei anything inside the vessel, ve-af al pi shelo naga — even if it did not touch. The question is whether the verse means merely that an object inside that is also touching becomes tamei, or whether mere presence — without physical contact — suffices.

### Two Verses Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָגַע, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נָגַע? רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן בֶּן אַבְטוּלְמוֹס אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר ״תּוֹכוֹ״ לְטַמֵּא, וְנֶאֱמַר ״תּוֹכוֹ״ לִיטַּמֵּא, מָה ״תּוֹכוֹ״ הָאָמוּר לְטַמֵּא אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָגַע, אַף ״תּוֹכוֹ״ הָאָמוּר לִיטַּמֵּא אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָגַע.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Do you say** that it is impure **even if** the impure item **did not come into contact** with the vessel, **or** perhaps **it is** impure **only if it did come into contact** with the vessel? **Rabbi Yonatan ben Avtolemos says: *Tokho* is stated** with regard **to transmitting impurity** to food in its airspace, as it is stated: "Whatever is in it [*tokho*] shall be impure," **and *tokho* is stated** with regard **to becoming impure,** as it is stated: "Into which [*tokho*] any of them falls"; **just as** in the case of ***tokho* that is stated** with regard **to transmitting impurity** to food in its airspace, the food is impure **even if** the impure item **did not come into contact** with the vessel, **so too,** in the case of ***tokho* that is stated** with regard **to** the vessel **becoming impure,** the vessel is impure **even if** the impure item **did not come into contact** with it.

Rabbi Yonasan ben Abtolmos resolves this through a textual comparison. The Torah uses the word tocho in two related contexts: one describing objects within a pottery vessel that become tamei, and another describing the vessel itself becoming tamei because of what is within it. He argues: mah tocho ha-amur leitamei — when the Torah says that the presence of a tamei object inside the vessel makes the vessel tamei — af al pi shelo naga — even if that object did not actually touch the vessel walls. Therefore, af tocho ha-amur letamei — when the Torah says objects inside the vessel become tamei, the same logic applies: af al pi shelo naga — even if those objects do not touch the vessel walls.

### Mustard Seed Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: הַתּוֹרָה הֵעִידָה עַל כְּלִי חֶרֶס וַאֲפִילּוּ מְלֹא חַרְדָּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** with regard to rendering food impure in its airspace, **from where do we** derive that the food becomes impure even if it did not come into contact with the impure vessel? **Rabbi Yonatan said: The Torah testified about an earthenware vessel** that it renders impure everything within it, **and** this is the *halakha* **even** if it is **full of mustard** seeds, in which case most of the seeds do not come in contact with the sides of the vessel, and nevertheless all the mustard seeds become impure.

The practical force of this principle is illustrated by an example. The Torah testified that a pottery vessel transmits tumah to things within it ve-afilu malei chardal — even if it is filled with mustard seeds — tiny seeds, like poppy seeds. Consider: if a pottery vessel is filled with such seeds and a tamei object enters it, only those seeds at the very surface might be said to touch anything at all. Yet the Torah indicates that all of the seeds inside — potentially millions of them — become tamei. This cannot be explained through contact, since the overwhelming majority of seeds are not in contact with the tamei object, and moreover tumah does not continue to spread from food to food indefinitely once the first degree of transmission is reached. The only coherent explanation is that the mechanism is presence within the enclosed space of the vessel, not physical contact. And since we know that the millions of seeds inside a sealed pottery vessel become tamei through presence alone, we similarly conclude that the pottery vessel itself becomes tamei through the presence of a tamei object within its airspace — regardless of whether contact occurs.
