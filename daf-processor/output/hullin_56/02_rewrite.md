# Hullin 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tereifos Ba'Of

### Intro: Birds List

The daf opens with a new *mishna* — *v'eilu tereifos ba'of* — which presents the list of *tereifos* specific to poultry. Having completed the extensive discussion of *tereifos* in large animals (*behemot*), spanning many pages and *dapim* of *gemara*, we now turn to birds. What is immediately striking is how much shorter this list is. The *mishna* covering *tereifos* in large animals is considerably more elaborate; birds, by contrast, receive a more compressed treatment. As we will see, this is not because fewer rules apply, but because a general principle — articulated by Levi — holds that whatever applies to large animals applies to birds as well, unless anatomy or explicit teaching dictates otherwise.

### Esophagus & Trachea

The first two items in the list are familiar: *nikuvat haveshet*, a hole in the esophagus, and *phuskat hagargeres*, a split trachea. By now the rules governing these organs are well established. A hole in the esophagus renders the bird a *tereifah*. A split trachea requires that the cut extend at least halfway through the circumference, as we learned from the discussion concerning large animals.

### Weasel Attack

The *mishna* then introduces *hiksa chuldah al rosha* — if a weasel struck the bird on its head, *makom she'oseh osah tereifah*, in a place where that blow could render it a *tereifah*. This formulation is notable: the mere fact of an attack is insufficient. The blow must have been in a location capable of causing a hole in the membrane surrounding the brain (*krum shel moach*). It is worth pausing on why the *mishna* frames the rule this way for birds when, in the context of large animals, the concern was stated more abstractly as a hole in the *krum*. The answer is practical: for chickens and other small birds, the realistic scenario for such a wound is precisely a weasel attack. The imagery is concrete and recognizable.

Rashi adds an important clarification. Although *hiksa* sounds like the sort of blow one might deliver with a hand or a claw — and indeed *derusah* (the *tereifah* of being clawed) involves a very different mechanism — this is explicitly not a case of *derusah*. Rather, *hiksa* here refers to a weasel attacking with its teeth. This matters halachically: the weasel bites, it does not claw. The concern is not the poison that *derusah* involves but the mechanical penetration of the skull and potentially the membrane.

### Gizzard & Organs

The *mishna* continues: *nikav hakorkovan*, a hole in the gizzard. The anatomy of the bird is relevant here. Unlike ruminants, birds have no multiple stomachs. Their digestive process involves two distinct organs: the *zefek* (crop), where food goes first, and the *korkovan* (gizzard), which comes second and grinds the food before it reaches the stomach. Artscroll's anatomical illustration is helpful here: one can see the esophagus descending into the crop, which then connects to the gizzard, followed by the small intestines. A hole in the *korkovan* is a *tereifah*; a hole in the *zefek*, as we will see in the next *mishna*, is not.

The list continues: *nikvu hadakin*, a hole in the small intestines, renders the bird a *tereifah* as well.

### Fire Damage

*Naflu la'ur venichmeru bnei mei'eha* — if the bird fell into fire and its inner organs became shriveled. The *mishna* adds a critical distinction: *im yerukim psulim*, if the organs turned green, the bird is *pasul*; *im adumim ksheirim*, if they remain red, it is *kosher*. The *gemara* will unpack this considerably, including the counterintuitive case where organs that are naturally a different color change in the opposite direction.

### Trampling Impact

Finally, the *mishna* discusses *darsa vetarfa bakosel* — if the bird was trampled or thrown against a wall, and *o she-retzatza behema u-mefarkeset* — or if an animal crushed or stomped on it, and the bird is still thrashing about (*mefarkeset*). This is a case of *risuk evarim* — the same blunt-force impact we encountered in the *mishna* about a large animal falling off a roof. There, the *gemara* had to borrow the concept of a twenty-four hour waiting period from the birds; here the *mishna* states it explicitly: *ve-shahata me-et le-et ve-shachata keshera* — if it survived for twenty-four hours and then was slaughtered, it is *kosher*. As we will revisit below, surviving the twenty-four hours does not entirely resolve the question; an internal organ check is still required.

## Weasel Testing

### Brain Push Test

Having established that the concern with a weasel attack is whether the *krum shel moach* has been pierced, the *gemara* now asks how one determines whether this occurred after the bird has been slaughtered. Rav, Shmuel, and Levi all agree on the same method: *machnis yado lifnim u-vodek* — you insert your hand from below, into the skull, and push the brain upward toward the hole that the weasel made. *im mevatzetz ha-moach treifah ve-im lav keshera* — if brain matter comes up through the hole, the membrane has been pierced and the bird is a *tereifah*; if nothing comes through, the membrane is intact and the bird is *kosher*.

### Brain Membranes

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty with this test. *ha nicha le-man de-amar ad de-mankiv kruma tata'ah* — this test works well according to the opinion that a *tereifah* requires that the inner (*tata'ah*) membrane be pierced. If that is the standard, then if nothing comes through the hole, it means neither membrane has a hole, and the test is definitive. But what about the opinion that *nikav ila'ah af al gav de-lo nikav tata'ah* — that even a hole in the outer membrane (*ila'ah*) alone suffices to render the bird a *tereifah*, even if the inner one is intact? In that case, one might worry: *dilma ila'ah nikav ve-tata'ah lo nikav* — the outer membrane has a hole but the inner one does not, so nothing comes up through the hole, yet the bird is still a *tereifah*.

The *gemara* resolves this: *tata'ah agav ruchya mifsek pasak* — when you apply pressure from below, if the outer membrane already has a hole, the pressure you generate will cause the inner membrane to tear as well, and brain matter will indeed come up through the hole. Therefore, if you push and nothing comes through, it is reliable proof that even the outer membrane is intact. The logic is that the experimental conditions are slightly altered by the act of pushing — but this actually works in the test's favor: it ensures that even a partial perforation of the outer membrane will be detected.

One might observe that this reasoning cuts in a particular direction. If you require only the outer membrane to be pierced, the test catches everything. If you require both membranes, the test still catches it — but in that case, a result showing brain matter might reflect only a hole in the outer membrane that was torn through by the pressure of the test itself, not a pre-existing hole in the inner one. So the test does have a potential for false positives under the two-membrane standard, even if it avoids false negatives. The *gemara* appears to accept this.

### Zeiri Position

Against this backdrop, *amar Zeiri: ein bdika l'chulda* — Zeiri declares that there is simply no valid method of inspection for a weasel attack. Once the skull has been struck, the bird must be treated as a *tereifah*. The reason offered is *mipnei sheshineha dakot* — because the weasel's teeth are very thin and therefore a visual inspection cannot detect the wound.

### Diagonal Holes

The *gemara* asks on Zeiri's position: *vechi shineha dakot mai havay* — so what if the teeth are thin? We have just described a test that does not rely on visual inspection at all. Rav Oshaya provides the answer: *mipnei sheshineha dakot ve'akumot* — the teeth are not only thin but also angled. A diagonal bite creates a hole in the skull that does not correspond directly to the hole in the membrane below. The hole in the membrane will be located at an offset position from the skull perforation. Therefore, even if you push from directly beneath the skull hole, you may be pushing at the wrong angle and will not drive brain matter through the membrane hole, even if one exists. The test is unreliable precisely because the path of the wound is not straight.

### Zeiri Retraction

The matter does not end there. *ki salik l'nahardea* — when Zeiri subsequently traveled to Nahardea, he sent a message back to his students: *devarim she'amarti lifneichem ta'ut hem beyadi* — what I previously taught you was a mistake; I retract it. He then transmits a ruling in the name of Reish Lakish: *bodkim l'chulda beyad aval lo bemachat* — one may indeed inspect for a weasel attack using one's hand (the pushing method), but one may not use a nail. It is worth noting the irony: Zeiri is visiting Nahardea, a Babylonian center of learning, and bringing back a teaching from Reish Lakish, who was of course an Eretz Yisrael authority — and this despite the earlier *gemara* in which Reish Lakish refused even to acknowledge Rav's authority when his teachings were cited to him.

### Hand vs. Nail

Why would a nail be considered as a testing instrument at all? The nail method works differently from the hand method: rather than pushing from below, one runs the tip of a nail along the surface of the brain membrane itself to feel whether it catches on a perforation. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar take a more permissive position, ruling *afilu masmer* — even a nail may be used — because we are not concerned that the nail itself will create a hole. The *gemara* traces this dispute back to Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemiah: *chad badik beyada vechad badik bemachata* — one tested with a hand and one with a needle.

### Testing Trade-offs

The exchange between the two positions is memorable. The one who used the hand challenged the one who used the needle: *ad matai ata mechaleh mamonam shel Yisrael* — how long will you continue to squander the money of the Jewish people? The needle test produces too many false positives — the needle itself creates holes — and so many animals are being declared *tereifah* unnecessarily, causing financial loss to their owners.

The response from the needle camp is equally pointed: *ad matai ata ma'achil leYisrael nevelot* — how long will you feed the Jewish people forbidden food? The hand test produces too many false negatives; animals with genuine perforations are being passed as *kosher*. The *gemara* notes, with characteristic precision, that the word *nevelot* is imprecise here, since the bird was in fact properly slaughtered — *veha shechutah hi* — and so the correct charge is *tereifot*, not *nevelot*. But the substance of the accusation stands.

This exchange crystallizes a tension that recurs throughout halachic literature: the concern for *mamonam shel Yisrael* — not placing undue financial burdens on people — weighing against the concern for rigorous protection of the law's integrity. Both poles have their own internal logic, and neither can be dismissed. The *gemara* concludes that it was Rabbi Yehuda who employed the hand test, as confirmed by a *baraita*: *bodkim lechulda beyad aval lo bemasmer* — test with the hand, not with a nail.

That same *baraita* adds a puzzling line: *nishbar ha'etzem af al pi shelo nikav krum shel moach* — a broken skull renders the bird a *tereifah* even if the membrane was not pierced. But if that is so, what is the entire purpose of the inspection? The *gemara* resolves this with a crucial clarification that will occupy the next section.

## Waterfowl Exception

### Thin Membranes

The *gemara* addresses the apparently contradictory final line of the *baraita* — that a cracked skull is a *tereifah* even without a pierced membrane — by explaining: *seifa atan le'of hamayim*, that ruling pertains to waterfowl. The principle is *ho'il ve'ein lo krum* — since waterfowl lack a membrane. The *gemara* immediately questions this on biological grounds: *ein lo krum salka daitach* — do you really mean to say it has no membrane at all? Of course it does. The *gemara* corrects: *ela ho'il vekrumo rach* — rather, its membrane is very thin and delicate. The consequence is that once the skull of a waterfowl is breached, one can assume that the membrane will inevitably tear as well, or alternatively, as Rashi suggests, one assumes that the membrane has already been compromised. Either way, for waterfowl, a hole in the skull alone suffices to create *tereifah* status; no inspection of the membrane is needed or possible.

### Levi's Rule

This section also introduces explicitly the teaching of Levi that serves as the foundational principle for the entire discussion of *tereifos* in birds: *treifos shemanu chachamim bebeheimah kenegdan ba'of* — whatever *tereifos* the Sages enumerated for large animals apply equally to birds. *yeser aleihen of* — birds have additional *tereifos* beyond those of large animals. Chief among these additional stringencies: *nishbar ha'etzem af al pi shelo nikav krum shel moach* — a skull fracture is a *tereifah* even without membrane penetration. This, as we have just seen, refers to waterfowl specifically. For other birds, the rules parallel those of large animals, and an intact membrane means the bird is *kosher* — with the hand-inspection available as the testing method.

### Case Study

The practical stakes of this distinction emerge in a concrete case. A chicken in the house of Rav Chana was apparently struck by a weasel. Rav Chana sent it to Rav Matna for a ruling. The facts were: *nishbar ha'etzem* — the skull was fractured — *velo nikav krum shel moach* — but the membrane was intact. Rav Matna declared it *kosher*. Rav Chana challenged this, citing Levi's principle: if birds have the additional stringency that a skull fracture alone is a *tereifah*, how can this bird be *kosher*? Rav Matna replied: *hasam be'of hashamayim* — that stringency applies to waterfowl, whose membranes are thin. For a regular chicken, the same standard as large animals applies: so long as the membrane is intact, the animal is *kosher*.

The question arises: if Rav Chana knew about this teaching and was concerned it made the chicken a *tereifah*, why did he send it to Rav Matna rather than simply rule himself? A plausible answer is that he felt genuinely conflicted — he was aware of traditions permitting inspection and traditions suggesting inspection was futile, and he could not resolve the tension. Sending the case to another authority and then pressing for an explanation of the ruling is a recognizable dynamic: one may not feel positioned to *pasken*, and once someone else has ruled, one can then engage the question analytically.

### Sun & Water Tests

The *gemara* records that different authorities employed different techniques for detecting membrane perforations. Rav Shizbi tested by holding the membrane up to sunlight, presumably to observe how light reflected off or through the tissue in a way that would reveal a hole. Rav Yeimar tested with water. Rashi offers two possible explanations of the water test. One is that the brain is scooped out of the membrane sac, which is then filled with water to observe whether any leaks through — though Rashi acknowledges this is difficult to execute given the delicacy of the tissue. The other explanation is that water is poured into the skull through the hole and then poured out: if the water comes out clear, the membrane is intact; if it comes out cloudy with brain matter, the membrane was breached. Rav Acha bar Yaakov used a thin tailor's needle run along the surface of the membrane. Notably, the language of testing with the sun and testing with water recalls the techniques used for inspecting the slaughtering knife for *pgima* — the very same terms applied to entirely different types of inspection.

### Geese Status

Rav Shizbi adds a taxonomic observation: *hani avazi didan ke'of hashamayim damyan* — our geese are classified like waterfowl. This is somewhat puzzling. If geese spend all their time in water, why the qualifying language *damyan* — "are like" — rather than simply saying they are waterfowl? Rashi suggests that geese are described as being *always in the water*, which would make the statement tautological. Perhaps the statement reflects a specific population of geese that were less aquatic in habit but were nevertheless treated halachically as waterfowl for these purposes. The word *damyan* — resembling — does retain a note of approximation rather than full identity, and this remains a point of some difficulty.

## Fire Organ Damage

### Green Amount

The *gemara* now turns to the rules governing organs that have changed color as a result of the bird falling into fire. Rabbi Yochanan teaches in the name of Rabbi Yosi ben Yehoshua: *ha-yirukatam ke-shier nikuvatan* — the amount of discoloration required to create a problem is the same as the amount of a hole that would create a *tereifah*. *mah nikuvatan b'mashehu* — just as even the smallest hole in the relevant inner organs is a *tereifah*, *af ha-yerukatam b'mashehu* — so too the slightest greening of those organs is a problem. The logic is consistent: a green spot is understood as an eventual rupture; if even a pinhole is fatal, then even the smallest discoloration matters.

### Liver Evidence

The son of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi posed a question to his father: *horika kaved keneged bnei mei'ayim mahu* — if the liver turned green in the area where it rests against the other inner organs, what is the ruling? The significance of the question is that the liver in general is a less essential organ halachically — the loss of most of the liver is not a *tereifah* — so one might think its discoloration is similarly less significant. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi ruled: *treifah*.

The reasoning is supplied by Rava: *kivan she-horika kaved keneged bnei mei'ayim, beyadua she-nafelu la'ur ve-nechamru bnei mei'ayim* — since the liver is green precisely at the point where it contacts the other inner organs, we may infer that those other organs have also been affected by the heat, even if we cannot see the discoloration on them directly. The liver is lying on top of those organs; the green on the underside of the liver, at the interface, is evidence of damage in the tissue opposite it. Even though a hole in the liver alone would be permissible, the liver's discoloration here functions as forensic evidence of damage to the organs beneath.

This raises a broader and important question: does the problem arise only when you know that the bird fell into fire, and then you look for these green spots? Or does the presence of a green spot, even without prior knowledge of a fire incident, itself create a presumption that the bird fell into the fire and the organs were damaged? The final line of Rava's ruling — *beyadua she-nafelu la'ur* — says "we know that it fell into the fire," which suggests the green spot alone is sufficient to establish that fact. If so, any green spot on the relevant organs, regardless of what you know about the bird's history, would render it a *tereifah*. This question is practically significant and the text leaves some ambiguity on this point.

### Intact Organs

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi himself had a chicken that he sent to Rabbi Elazar Hakapar b'Rabbi. Rabbi Elazar ruled *kosher*, despite the organs appearing green. The objection is immediate: the *mishna* says *yerukim pesulim* — green is the problematic color. Rabbi Elazar's ruling is explained by a limiting principle: *lo ameru yerukim pesulim ela bekorkevan uvalev uvakaved* — the rule that green discoloration is a *tereifah* applies only to three specific organs: the gizzard, the heart, and the liver. This is confirmed by a *baraita*: *be-eilu bnei mei'ayim amru* — it is specifically regarding these three organs that the rule of greenness applies.

### Color Reversals

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosef had a chicken that he sent to Rabbi Abbahu. In this case, the organs were red — *adumim havhu* — yet Rabbi Abbahu declared it a *tereifah*. The objection is now the mirror image of the previous: *adumim kesherim* — red is the kosher color according to the *mishna*! Rabbi Abbahu explains: *adumim she-horiku ve-yerukim she-edimu* — organs that are naturally red turning green is a problem, and equally, organs that are naturally greenish (or flesh-toned) turning red is also a problem. The *mishna*'s formulation is not exhaustive; it describes the paradigmatic case (naturally red organs remaining red is fine; naturally red organs turning green is not), but Rabbi Abbahu extends this symmetrically. *lo ameru adumim kesherim ela balev bekorkevan uvakaved* — only the heart, gizzard, and liver are being discussed. The intestines and other organs have a more neutral, orangeish hue, and a shift in their color in either direction is the relevant concern for those organs.

The *artscroll* anatomical illustration is actually helpful at this point: the heart and liver appear in a clear deep red; the gizzard, similarly; the intestines, by contrast, show a more orangeish-neutral tone. The color distinction that the *gemara* is drawing is visually recognizable.

### Boiling Test

Shmuel bar Chiya in the name of Rav Mani introduces a further nuance: *adumim she-horiku veshalokan vechazru ve-edimu kesherim* — if organs that should be red turned greenish, but you then cooked them in boiling water and they reverted to their normal red color, they are *kosher*. The reason: *kutra ai behu* — the discoloration was caused by smoke, not by organic damage to the tissue. The boiling process removes the external contaminant and reveals the organ's natural and healthy state. The color change was superficial rather than indicative of structural damage.

### Testing Limits

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak extends this logic in the opposite direction: if you can use the boiling test to rescue an animal that appeared problematic, should you not also apply the test to an animal that appears fine? If you boil what look like healthy, properly-colored organs and they turn the wrong color, that change reveals their true condition — *igla bahita'iyu*, their defect has been exposed.

Rav Ashi takes the logical conclusion to its extreme: *lo leichol inish ela be-shalka* — no one should eat a chicken unless the organs have been subjected to this boiling test. This is a striking statement. At face value, it seems to mandate universal testing before any bird may be consumed. But the *poskim* consistently interpret this as operating within the context already established: the bird is known or suspected to have fallen into fire. The boiling test is triggered only in that context, not as a universal requirement for every chicken slaughtered.

### No Presumed Defects

The *gemara* itself closes this discussion with the decisive limiting principle: *velo hi — achzukei rei'ata lo machzekinan* — we do not presume defects. The term *rei'ata* derives from *ra*, meaning bad; the principle is that we do not go looking for problems where none are apparent. If the organs present well visually, we do not then subject them to further testing in the hope of uncovering hidden problems. If something looks bad, one may attempt to demonstrate through testing that it is actually fine. But one does not take something that looks fine and subject it to tests designed to reveal possible defects. The *chazaka* is in favor of the animal being *kosher*, and we require a concrete *leidas ha-safek* — an actual precipitating concern — before that presumption is disturbed.

## Blunt Force Impact

### Internal Check

The *gemara* returns to the case of *darsa vetarfa bekosel* — the bird that was trampled or thrown against a wall. Rabbi Elazar ben Antignus rules in the name of Rabbi Elazar bar Yannai: *bein kach uvein kach tzericha bedika* — in either case, whether the bird survived twenty-four hours or not, an internal organ inspection is still required after slaughter. The twenty-four hours establishes a baseline — an animal that does not survive twenty-four hours is a *tereifah* even without internal organ damage — but survival does not completely resolve the question of *risuk evarim*. One must still examine the internal organs.

### 24-Hour Rule

The broader picture is worth noting. When the earlier *mishna* discussed a large animal (*behemah*) falling off a roof, there was no explicit mention of the twenty-four hour rule. The *gemara* there borrowed the concept from this very *mishna* about birds. Here, the rule is explicit: *ve-shahata me-et le'et ve-shachata keshera* — if it waited twenty-four hours and was then slaughtered, it is *kosher*, provided the organ inspection is clear. If the bird got up and walked around (or in the case of a bird, flew), even the organ inspection may be waived. These are the parameters of *risuk evarim* as applied in both contexts.

## Kosher Conditions

### Minor Damage OK

The following *mishna* presents the *kosher* counterpart: *eilu k'sheiros ba'of*. A hole in the trachea (*nikva ha-gargeres*) is acceptable, as is a crack running along its length that does not extend to the very top or bottom. These are less than the disqualifying injuries already described. Similarly, if the weasel attacked the head *makom she-eino oseh tereifah* — in a place where, even if the membrane were reached, it would not correspond to the brain — the bird remains *kosher*. Whether such a location exists anatomically is left somewhat open, but the *mishna* contemplates it.

### Missing Crop

*Nikav ha-zefek* — a hole in the crop — does not render the bird a *tereifah*. And then *rebbi* goes further: *afilu nital* — even if the crop is entirely removed, it is still *kosher*. How this is anatomically possible is not straightforward. Looking at the diagram, the esophagus flows directly into the crop, which flows into the gizzard; removing the crop without disrupting the esophagus seems difficult to execute without causing a hole in the esophageal passage. The *ran* and *beer ha-golah* address this by clarifying that "removed" does not mean fully excised, but rather that enough of the crop remains to allow food to pass to the gizzard. The functional criterion — that the food pathway remains viable — is what determines the ruling.

### Protruding Organs

*Yatzu bnei me'eha velo nikvu* — if the internal organs protruded from the body cavity but sustained no punctures or holes, the bird is *kosher*. This is a notable ruling: an open body cavity with organs hanging out is not itself a *tereifah* condition, so long as the organs themselves are intact. *nishtabru gapeha* — broken wings, *nishtabru ragleha* — broken legs: these are all *kosher*. External structural injuries, even severe ones, do not render a bird a *tereifah* absent internal organ damage.

### Feather Loss

*Nimretu knafeha* — if the bird's outer feathers have been pulled out, it is still *kosher*. But *rabbi yehuda* introduces a distinction: *im nitla ha-notza pesula* — if the *notza*, the down feathers closest to the skin, have been removed, that is a *tereifah*. The down is what insulates and protects the bird, and Rabbi Yehuda holds that without it the bird cannot survive. The outer feathers, by contrast, are not essential to the bird's viability. This parallels a discussion earlier in the tractate about skinning a large animal, where Rabbi Meir permitted and the *chachamim* prohibited. It is tempting to identify that *bar plugta* dispute as reflecting the same underlying disagreement — Rabbi Yehuda arguing for the *tereifah* status, Rabbi Meir for *kosher* — although the framing here presents Rabbi Yehuda's position as the *da'as yachid*, the minority view.

## Anatomy & Analogy

### Simai & Tzadok

A *baraita* records that Rabbi Simai and Rabbi Tzadok traveled to participate in a calendar intercalation convocation at Lod, spending Shabbos along the way in Ono. A case involving the *tarpachat* — a term for the womb — was brought before them, and they ruled *ke-Rebbi be-zefek*, like Rebbi ruled in the case of the crop. That is, the same way Rebbi had ruled that a missing crop does not render a bird a *tereifah*, they ruled that a missing womb does not render a large animal a *tereifah*. The analogy rests on the principle that some organs are not essential to the animal's survival: the crop is one such organ, and the womb, they argued, is another.

### Teiku Question

The *gemara* raises a question about how to read this *baraita*: *iba'ya lehu* — it is uncertain. Rashi's *girsa* reads: did they rule stringently about the womb (*le-issura*), with the phrase *ke-Rebbi be-zefek le-heteira* referring to a separate, second ruling they issued on the crop itself? Or does the phrase mean that they ruled leniently about the womb (*le-heteira*), using Rebbi's crop ruling as their analogy, but without necessarily indicating their own view on the crop?

The practical upshot of this ambiguity is significant: the *baraita* might serve as evidence that Rebbi's lenient ruling on the crop was accepted and followed — or it might be that the *baraita's* author is merely drawing an analogy for descriptive purposes, without implying that Rabbi Simai and Rabbi Tzadok endorsed Rebbi's position on the crop itself. The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — the question stands unresolved.

### Crop-Esophagus Line

A related anatomical question is raised by Rabbah (some say Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi): *gago shel zefek nidon ke-veshet* — the "roof" of the crop is treated as part of the esophagus. Looking at the anatomical diagram, the esophagus descends and opens into the crop; there is a point at which the tube transitions into the sac-like crop. The upper portion of that transition — the "roof" — is treated halachically as continuous with the esophagus, meaning a hole there is a *tereifah*, not a permissible crop injury.

### Stretching Test

Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye specifies the criterion: *kol she-nimtach immo* — any tissue that stretches together with the esophagus when pulled is considered part of the esophagus. Rashi explains this as referring to the point where the walls of the crop begin to be pulled taut into the esophageal tube — that is where one draws the line. Below that boundary is the crop, where a hole is permissible; above it is the esophagus, where any perforation is a *tereifah*.

## Organ Positioning

### Fallen Organs

We return to the case of *yatzu bnei me'eha* — the organs that fell out of the body cavity without being punctured.

### Wrong Order

Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak introduces a critical qualification: *lo shanu ella she-lo hiphech bahen* — this ruling (that protruding organs without holes are *kosher*) applies only if the organs have not been disturbed in their natural arrangement. *aval hiphech bahen, tereifah* — if the organs were turned over or repositioned in the wrong order, it is a *tereifah*. This could occur when someone, attempting to help the animal, pushes the organs back into the body cavity but inadvertently reverses their natural arrangement relative to one another.

### Divine Design

The *gemara* grounds this ruling in a verse: *hu ascha vayechonenecha* — from *devarim*, in *ha'azinu*, describing how God made and established Israel. *melamed she-bara hakadosh baruch hu koneni'ot ba'adam* — this teaches that God fashioned specific chambers within living creatures, such that *sheim nefalech echad mehem eino yachol lychayot* — if any one of them were to be situated in the wrong position, the creature could not survive. The *gemara* draws a parallel to the *bracha* of *asher yatzar*, with its recognition that *im yipatei'ach echad mehem o yisateim echad mehem* — if any opening were to close or any sealed space were to open, life could not be sustained. The wonder of the body's design is that every component is precisely where it must be. And from this theological reflection, the *gemara* draws a direct halachic conclusion: organs repositioned in the wrong order have been removed from the configuration God designed, and the animal cannot survive — hence, *tereifah*.

### Midrash Nation

Rabbi Meir reads the same verse — *hu ascha vayechonenecha* — differently, in a national rather than anatomical register. *ke'am karcha dekula bei* — God has made Israel like a self-sufficient city, containing within itself everything it requires: *mimeinu Kohanim mimeinu Nevi'im mimeinu sarav mimeinu melachav* — from within Israel come priests, prophets, officers, and kings. The verse speaks of completeness and self-sufficiency — *mimeinu pina mimeinu yated*, the cornerstone and the foundation both come from within. This *midrash* is applied to Israel as a nation, not to the physiology of the chicken. The *gemara* applies the first interpretation — the anatomical one — to the halachic ruling about organ positioning, while Rabbi Meir's *drasha* stands as a separate homiletical reading of the same verse.

### Life-Saving Surgery

The daf concludes with a striking narrative. A non-Jew witnessed a person fall from a roof, his abdomen burst open, with his internal organs spilling out but remaining unruptured and attached. The non-Jew devised an extraordinary intervention: he brought the injured man's son before him and, through an illusion (*beachizat einayim*), made it appear as if he was killing the son. The shock caused the injured man to faint (*ingid ve'itnach*), at which point the non-Jew replaced the organs inside the man's body, restoring them to their proper arrangement, and then sutured the abdomen closed. The man survived.

The story is told here in the context of the ruling that organs replaced in the wrong order render an animal a *tereifah* — the corollary being that organs replaced in the correct order do not. And indeed, as Rashi notes in closing, even for human beings the principle holds: organs that have fallen out and are restored in their correct anatomical order can allow the person to live. The divine engineering of the body is both the halachic foundation of the *tereifah* ruling and, as this story demonstrates, the basis for the possibility of restoration and recovery.
