# Chullin 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Attached vs. Detached

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין מְחוּבָּר מֵעִיקָּרוֹ לְתָלוּשׁ וּלְבַסּוֹף חִבְּרוֹ? דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּמוּכְנִי – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, בִּמְחוּבָּר לַקַּרְקַע – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, נָעַץ סַכִּין בַּכּוֹתֶל וְשָׁחַט בָּהּ – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, הָיָה צוֹר יוֹצֵא מִן הַכּוֹתֶל אוֹ קָנֶה עוֹלֶה מֵאֵלָיו וְשָׁחַט בּוֹ – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do you say that** there is **a difference for us between** a blade that was **attached from the outset** and a blade that was **detached and ultimately he reattached it? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who slaughters with a mechanism [*bemukhni*]** of a wheel with a knife attached to it, **his slaughter is valid; with** an item that is **attached to the ground, his slaughter is valid;** if **one embedded a knife in a wall and slaughtered with it, his slaughter is valid.** If there **was a flint emerging from a wall or a reed arising** from the ground **on its own and he slaughtered with it, his slaughter is not valid.**

We pick up six lines from the bottom of 15b, at the words *u'mina teima*. The Mishnah had established that one may slaughter with any instrument — including a rock or a reed — provided it is sufficiently smooth. This ruling leads the Gemara into a discussion of whether one may use something that is attached to the ground.

### Mishnah & Gemara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שָׁאנֵי בֵּין מְחוּבָּר מֵעִיקָּרוֹ לְתָלוּשׁ וּלְבַסּוֹף חִבְּרוֹ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ostensibly, the two clauses of the *baraita* are **difficult,** as they contradict **each other,** since the first clause states that slaughter with a blade that is attached is valid and the latter clause states that slaughter is not valid. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it** that there is **a difference between** a case where the blade was **attached from the outset and** a case where the blade was **detached and ultimately he reattached it?** The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **learn from it.**

The position of Rabbi (Rebbi) is that an instrument attached to the ground is not valid. The Mishnah implies as well that *lechatchila* it is not valid; use of such an instrument is acceptable only *bedievid*, post facto. We also established that Rabbi would permit *bedievid* a case where the instrument began as detached and was subsequently attached — *talush u'levasof chibur* — as distinct from something that was always attached, *mechubar me'ikara*. The Gemara now asks: how do we know to draw this distinction between the two categories?

### Rabbi's Position


Rabbi holds that an instrument *mechubar me'ikara* — always attached to the ground — is invalid even *bedievid*. By contrast, something that is *talush u'levasof chibur* — first detached and only later attached — is not fully considered attached. According to Rabbi, at least *bedievid*, such an instrument is valid. *lechatchila* it remains problematic, since one might confuse the two cases, but post facto it is not treated as genuinely attached.

### Proof Needed


The Gemara asks: *u'mina teima d'shani lan bein *mechubar me'ikara* l'talush u'levasof chibur* — from where do we know to distinguish between something always attached and something that was detached and then attached?

### Baraita Evidence


*D'tanya*: A baraita is cited. *ha'shochet b'muchni shechitato keshera* — if one slaughters with a knife driven by some type of wheel mechanism, the shechita is valid. *bimechubar l'karka shechitato keshera* — if the knife is attached to the ground, it is valid. *natatz sakin bakotel v'shachat ba shechitato keshera* — if one took a knife and lodged it into a wall and slaughtered with it, the shechita is valid.

Here an important point must be noted. If these instruments are attached to the ground, how does one actually perform the shechita? Rashi, in a brief but critical comment, explains: *v'holich v'heivi tzavar habeheima aleha* — one does not move the knife at all; instead, one moves the neck of the animal back and forth over the knife. This is a striking observation. We naturally assume that one must move the knife, but in fact, since all motion is relative, as long as there is a proper cutting motion between knife and animal, even if the motion is applied to the animal rather than the knife, the shechita is valid.

The baraita then continues: *hatzoer yotzei min hakotel* — if a rock protrudes from a wall, *o kaneh oleh me'eilav* — or a reed growing naturally from the ground, *v'shachat bo shechitato pesula* — and one slaughtered with it, the shechita is invalid.

### Resolution


*Kashya ahadadei* — the two halves of the baraita contradict one another. The resolution: *ela lav *shema mina* shani bein *mechubar me'ikara* l'talush u'levasof chibur* — there is a difference between something always attached and something that was once detached and later attached. *shema mina* — this is a sound proof. The case of *mechubar l'karka* in the baraita refers to something attached by human agency — *talush u'levasof chibur* — which *bedievid* is valid. The case of the reed growing naturally or the rock protruding from a cave wall — something always attached — is invalid.

One might pause at the phrase *tzoer yotzei min hakotel* — a rock coming out of a wall — since a wall is presumably man-made and constructed. The Gemara will later clarify that the reference here is to a natural wall, such as the wall of a cave, so that such a rock was always attached and never detached. This distinction — between always-attached and detached-then-attached — will occupy much of the discussion on this daf.

## Koach Gavra

### Wheel Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּמוּכְנִי שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה, וְהָתַנְיָא: שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּסַרְנָא דְּפַחְרָא, הָא בְּסַרְנָא דְּמַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said:** In the case of **one who slaughters with a mechanism** of a wheel with a knife attached to it, **his slaughter is valid.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **his slaughter is not valid?** The Gemara answers: This contradiction is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which rules that the slaughter is valid, is **in** a case where the knife was attached to **a potter's wheel,** whose movement is generated by the potter pressing on a pedal. Since the slaughter was performed by the force of the person's actions, the slaughter is valid. **That** *baraita*, which rules that the slaughter is not valid, is **in** a case where the knife was attached to **a waterwheel.** Since the slaughter was not performed by the force of the person's actions, the slaughter is not valid.

The baraita stated that one who slaughters with a knife powered by a wheel — *ha'shochet b'muchni shechitato keshera* — the shechita is valid. Yet a parallel baraita states that in this same case the shechita is invalid: *shechitato pesula*. The Gemara must resolve this contradiction.

A clarification about the mechanics: the wheel does not mean the knife moves in a circle, which would obviously be impossible for shechita. Rather, the energy of a turning wheel is transformed through a mechanism into a vertical cutting motion. The question is whether such a mechanism produces valid shechita.

### Pottery vs. Water


*Lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. *ha be'sadna de'pakhra, ha be'sadna de'maya* — one case refers to a pottery wheel and the other to a water wheel. A pottery wheel is driven by the operator's leg pushing a pedal, which spins the wheel to shape clay. A water wheel is driven by the rush of water. The distinction is consequential: in the case of the pottery wheel, the energy ultimately derives from the human operator — the mechanism is powered by the person. In the case of the water wheel, the energy comes from the rushing water itself, not from the person.

This introduces a fundamental criterion for shechita: *koach gavra* — human energy. Even if intention is not required for *ma'aseh shechita*, the energy animating the act must derive from a person. A knife blown by the wind onto an animal's throat would not constitute shechita. *ma'aseh shechita* requires human energy even where intention may not be required. A water wheel, driven by natural forces, lacks *koach gavra*; a pottery wheel, driven through a person's physical effort, possesses it.

### First vs. Second Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא בְּסַרְנָא דְּמַיָּא, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּכֹחַ רִאשׁוֹן, הָא בְּכֹחַ שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: The rulings of both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are **in** a case where the knife was attached to **a waterwheel, and** the contradiction is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which rules that the slaughter is valid, is in a case where the movement of the slaughter was generated **by primary force,** as the person releases the water that turns the wheel, and on that initial turn of the wheel the knife slaughters the animal. **That** *baraita*, which rules that the slaughter is not valid, is in a case where the slaughter was generated **by secondary force,** as the knife slaughters the animal on the second turn of the wheel.

The Gemara then proposes an alternative resolution: *v'ibayit eima, ha ve'ha be'sadna de'maya* — both contradictory baraitot may involve a water wheel, yet one permits and the other forbids. How? *lo kashya: ha be'koach rishon, ha be'koach sheni* — the difference lies in whether we are dealing with *koach rishon*, the first power, or *koach sheni*, the second power.

The question is how the water wheel came to be turning. One may imagine a river flowing nearby, with a sluice gate controlling whether the water diverts into one's channel and turns the wheel. Who opens that gate? If the person opens the gate and the water immediately rushes through and turns the wheel, that initial turning is a product of *koach rishon* — the direct consequence of the person's act. As the river continues to push the wheel on its own afterward, that is *koach sheni* — a derivative power no longer traceable directly to the person.

### Sluice Gates Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַאן דְּכַפְתֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ וְאַשְׁקֵיל עֲלֵיהּ בִּידְקָא דְּמַיָּא וּמִית – חַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? גִּירֵי דִּידֵיהּ הוּא דְּאַהֲנִי בֵּיהּ. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי – בְּכֹחַ רִאשׁוֹן, אֲבָל בְּכֹחַ שֵׁנִי – גְּרָמָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **like that which Rav Pappa says:** In the case of **a certain** person **who bound another and diverted a flow [*bidka*] of water upon him and he died,** the one who diverted the water is **liable** for his murder. **What is the reason?** It is because those were **his arrows that were effective in his** murder. **And this matter** applies in a case where he killed the other person **by primary force,** as the person was proximate to him and was directly drowned by the water. **But** if the person was further away and was killed **by secondary force** after the water flowed on its own, it is not by his direct action; rather, **it is merely an indirect** action, and he is exempt.

*Ki ha de'amar Rav Pappa* — Rav Pappa illustrates the principle with a case of murder. *hai man de'kaftei le'chavrei ve'ashkelei bidka de'maya u'mit chayav* — if a person bound his fellow and placed him in front of sluice gates over a rushing river, and then opened the gates so that the water came crashing down and killed the bound man, the perpetrator is liable. *mai ta'ama? giray didei ninhu* — they are his arrows. Although the physical energy was in the water, since the water burst through as a direct consequence of his opening the gates, it is as though he fired an arrow. The initial crash of the water is attributed to him.

However, the statement continues: *v'hanei milei be'koach rishon* — this is only true for *koach rishon*, the first, direct impact. *aval be'koach sheni, grama b'alma hu* — if it is the second power, it is merely indirect causation, *grama*.

As Rashi explains, the distinction is one of physical position. If the victim stands directly at the base of the sluice gate, the crashing water strikes him immediately — that is *koach rishon*, and the person is fully liable. But if the victim stands several feet away, the water first crashes down at the base and only then flows onward to drown the man standing further back — that derivative flow is *koach sheni*, and the perpetrator is exempt from formal liability, though the Gemara notes he remains *chayav b'dinei shamayim*, liable before Heaven.

### Application


This principle has ramifications across many areas of halacha. The distinction between direct action and *grama* — indirect causation — is not merely about physical causation in a philosophical sense, but about whether one's energy is the operative force.

One instructive application is the familiar question of closing an electrical circuit on Shabbat. At a micro level, one who switches on an incandescent light closes a circuit, causing electrons to flow and heat a filament. Although the person did not personally heat the filament, the result is so instantaneous that it is treated as *koach rishon* — as though the person performed the act directly. The immediacy collapses the intervening steps, and the action is considered a *ma'aseh*, not a *grama*. This analysis applies *l'chumra*, where what matters is not merely that one set a process in motion, but that when the result follows immediately, it is attributed to the person as their own act.

An analogous but distinct question arises in other contexts — for example, whether machine-made matzah is produced *lishma* — where the relevant criterion is not *koach gavra* but *kavana*. There, what transpires after the initial activation of the machine is arguably no longer the operator's direct action. In any event, the principle is established: shechita requires *koach gavra*, which is satisfied either by directly imparting one's physical energy or by releasing energy in such a way that the immediate result is attributed to that release — as in *koach rishon* of the water wheel.

## Defining Attachment

### Three Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַב אֲחוֹרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, וְיָתֵיב רַבִּי וְקָאָמַר: מִנַּיִן לִשְׁחִיטָה שֶׁהִיא בְּתָלוּשׁ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּקַּח אֶת הַמַּאֲכֶלֶת לִשְׁחֹט". אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא: מַאי קָאָמַר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וָי״ו דִּכְתִיב אַאוּפְתָּא קָאָמַר. וְהָא קְרָא קָאָמַר! קְרָא זְרִיזוּתֵיהּ דְּאַבְרָהָם קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav sat behind Rabbi Ḥiyya, and Rabbi Ḥiyya** sat **before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **sat and said: From where** is it derived **that slaughter is** performed specifically **with** a blade that is **detached?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated:** "And Abraham stretched forth his hand **and took the knife to slaughter** his son" (Genesis 22:10). **Rav said to Rabbi Ḥiyya: What is he saying?** Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to** Rav: **He is saying** an incorrect reason, comparable to the letter ***vav* that is written on** the rough surface of **a tree trunk [*a'ufta*].** The Gemara asks: **But didn't** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **say a verse** as proof for his statement? The Gemara answers: **The verse teaches us the diligence of Abraham,** who had a knife prepared to slaughter Isaac. It does not teach any *halakha* concerning ritual slaughter.

*Yativ Rav achorei d'Rabbi Chiyya, v'Rabbi Chiyya kammai d'Rabbi* — Rav sat behind Rabbi Chiyya, who sat before Rabbi. This scene captures three pivotal generations in the transition from Tannaim to Amoraim: Rabbi — Rebbi — the editor of the Mishnah; Rabbi Chiyya, who edited the Tosefta and the baraitot; and Rav, the first of the Amoraim. Their respective positions are likewise arranged: Rabbi holds that the knife must be detached; Rabbi Chiyya holds that attachment to the ground does not invalidate the shechita.

### Abraham & the Knife


Rabbi, sitting before them, offers a proof for his position: *minayin l'shechita shehu betalush?* — from where do we know shechita requires a detached instrument? *she'ne'emar, vayikach et hama'achelet lishchot et b'no* — as the verse says, he took the knife to slaughter his son. The word *ma'achelet* — a proper knife — implies something that functions as a vessel, as a detached implement.

It should be noted that Rabbi is not claiming shechita requires a formal *kli* — a recognized vessel. A detached rock or reed carries no status as a vessel, yet Rabbi would permit it. For *kodashim*, a different verse may demand a recognized *kli*, perhaps even a *kli sharet*, but for ordinary shechita, the inference from *ma'achelet* is only that the implement must be detached.

Rav, seated behind Rabbi Chiyya and too far to hear clearly, asks: *mai ka'amar?* — what is Rabbi saying? Rabbi Chiyya replies: *vav deketiv a'uptza ka'amar* — he is writing a *vav* on a ridge. This is an idiomatic expression for making an argument on broken ground, like trying to draw a straight line across a series of bumps — the line will be uneven and the argument does not hold together.

The Gemara then clarifies why the proof fails: *lo kra l'zrizutei d'Avraham kamma'shma lan* — the verse is not stating a legal principle; it is describing the alacrity of Avraham, who prepared himself carefully before the *akeidah* and took along a knife. As Rashi explains, Avraham may have been concerned that he would not find a suitable instrument at the site of the *akeidah*. But one cannot derive from Avraham's choice of implement a binding principle about what constitutes a valid instrument for shechita. The Torah is offering a narrative detail, not a legal prescription. Had Avraham wished, he could have sharpened a detached rock — or pulled a rock from the ground.

### Avodah Zarah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, תָּלוּשׁ וּלְבַסּוֹף חִבְּרוֹ לְעִנְיַן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הָוֵי תָּלוּשׁ, דְּאָמַר מָר: הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְבַיִת שֶׁלּוֹ אֲסָרוֹ, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ הָוֵי מְחוּבָּר – "אֱלֹהֵיהֶם עַל הֶהָרִים" וְלֹא הֶהָרִים אֱלֹהֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the issue of slaughter with a detached blade, **Rava said:** It is **obvious to me** that concerning an item that was **detached and ultimately one attached it,** with regard **to the matter of idol worship** its halakhic status **is** that of **a detached** item, **as the Master says: One who bows to his house has rendered it forbidden** as an object of idol worship. **And if it enters your mind** to say that its halakhic status **is** that of an **attached** item, it is written with regard to idolatry: **"Their gods, upon the high mountains"** (Deuteronomy 12:2), from which it is derived: **But the mountains are not their gods,** as items attached to the ground are never rendered forbidden as objects of idol worship. The halakhic status of a house built from stones that were detached is that of a detached item.

Rava states: *peshita li — talush she'basof chibro l'inyan avodat kochavim havei talush* — it is obvious to me that something once detached and subsequently attached to the ground is treated as detached for purposes of *avodah zarah*, idolatry.

### House Forbidden


The operative principle in *avodah zarah* is *ha'mishtachaveh l'vayit shelo assaro* — one who bows down to one's own house renders it forbidden in benefit. Two conditions underlie this rule. First, *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — one cannot render another person's property forbidden through one's own actions, absent a physical change to the object. Bowing down to a neighbor's house, unlike slaughtering a neighbor's animal for idolatrous purposes, involves no physical transformation, and therefore cannot alter its status. Second, and more relevant here, the object must be considered detached — *metaltelin* — in order to be rendered forbidden.

The basis for this second condition is the verse in Devarim: *eloheihem al he'harim, v'lo he'harim eloheihem* — their gods that are upon the mountains, but the mountains themselves cannot be their gods. The Gemara in *avodah zarah* states plainly that one who bows to a mountain has committed an act of *avodah zarah* and is *chayav mita*, yet the mountain itself does not become a forbidden object. This makes practical sense: if mountains and the earth itself could be rendered *avodah zarah* and forbidden in benefit, vast portions of the globe would be prohibited.

The underlying concept is that something must be capable of having its identity designated — and that capacity belongs only to things that are, in some sense, under human dominion. The earth itself, being permanently fixed, cannot have its identity designated in this way.

Now, one who bows to one's own house does render it forbidden. This demonstrates that a house — which began as individual stones and timber that were *talush*, detached — even after being constructed and attached to the ground, is treated for *avodah zarah* purposes as *talush*. It is not the ground itself, even though it is affixed to it.

*V'ei salka da'atach havei mechubar* — if you would say that something detached and later attached is now considered fully attached, the house could not become forbidden, since like a mountain it would be immune. Yet the halacha is that the house does become forbidden. This confirms that *talush u'levasof chibur* — detached-then-attached — is treated as *talush* for *avodah zarah*.

### Seeds & Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעִנְיַן הֶכְשֵׁר זְרָעִים תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַכּוֹפֶה קְעָרָה עַל הַכּוֹתֶל בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתּוּדַח – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן", בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא יִלְקֶה הַכּוֹתֶל – אֵינוֹ בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן".
>
> **Translation:** With regard **to the matter of rendering seeds susceptible** to ritual impurity, there **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as we learned** in a mishna (*Makhshirin* 4:3): In the case of **one who places a bowl on the wall** while it is raining **so that** the bowl **will be rinsed** with the rainwater, if the water from the bowl then falls onto produce, **that is under** the rubric of the verse: "But **when** water **is placed** upon the seed" (Leviticus 11:38). The water has the halakhic status of a liquid that he poured of his own volition on fruit and seeds. Consequently, it renders them susceptible to ritual impurity. But if he placed the bowl there **so that the wall will not be damaged, it is not under** the rubric of the verse "but **when** water **is placed** upon the seed." Since he had no intent to use the water, it is not considered to have entered the bowl of his own volition, and it does not render produce susceptible to impurity.

*U'l'inyan hechsher zera'im* — when it comes to making agricultural produce susceptible to *tumah*, the Gemara notes that the matter is a debate among Tannaim: *tanna'ei hi*.

By way of background: water that comes into contact with food renders that food susceptible to *tumah* — *huchshar*. But this applies only once the food has been harvested; food still attached to the ground is not susceptible. Importantly, for the water to render food susceptible, the water itself must have been desired — the principle of *ki yutan*. If the water fell upon the food unwanted, it does not render it susceptible.

The question of relevance here is this: if you desire water for the sake of something that is *talush u'levasof chibur* — detached and now attached — does your desire for that water activate the *ki yutan* principle and render food susceptible? The issue is whether such an object is treated as *talush*, in which case wanting water for it constitutes a desire for something not attached to the ground, or as *mechubar*, in which case it does not.

### Wall Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גּוּפַהּ קַשְׁיָא: אָמְרַתְּ, בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁתּוּדַח – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן", הָא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיּוּדַח הַכּוֹתֶל – אֵין זֶה בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן". וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי: בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא יִלְקֶה הַכּוֹתֶל – אֵינוֹ בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן", הָא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיּוּדַח הַכּוֹתֶל – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן". אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תִּבְרַאּ, מִי שֶׁשָּׁנָה זוֹ לֹא שָׁנָה זוֹ. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ חַד תַּנָּא הוּא, הָא בַּכּוֹתֶל מְעָרָה, הָא בְּכוֹתֶל בִּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **This** mishna **itself** is **difficult,** as the inferences from the first clause and the latter clause are contradictory. In the first clause **you said:** In the case of one who places a bowl on the wall **so that** the bowl **will be rinsed** with the rainwater, **that is under** the rubric of the verse "but **when** water **is placed** upon the seed," and the water renders produce susceptible to impurity. By inference, if he placed the bowl **so that the wall will be rinsed** by means of the bowl, **that is not under** the rubric of the verse "but **when** water **is placed** upon the seed." That water would not render produce susceptible to impurity, because the intent was for the water to rinse the wall, which is an item attached to the ground. **And then** the mishna **teaches** in the latter clause: If he placed the bowl **so that the wall will not be damaged, it is not under** the rubric of the verse: "But **when** water **is placed** upon the seed." By inference, if he placed the bowl **so that the wall will be rinsed, that is under** the rubric of the verse: "But **when** water **is placed** upon the seed," as a wall has the status of a detached item, since it was built from stones that were detached. **Rabbi Elazar said:** This mishna is **disjointed;** the *tanna* **who taught this** first clause **did not teach that** second clause. There is a tannaitic dispute whether the status of a wall that is built from detached stones is that of an attached item or a detached item. **Rav Pappa said: The entire** mishna **is** the opinion of **one *tanna*: This** first clause is **in** the case of **the wall of a cave,** which is attached from the outset; **that** latter clause is **in** the case of **the wall of a building,** which is built from stones that were detached from the ground.

*D'tenan* — a Mishnah is cited. *ha'kofeh ke'arah al ha'kotel bishvil she'tudach* — one who places a vessel against a wall because one wants the rain to clean it out: *harei zeh be'chi yutan* — this is in the category of *ki yutan*, and the water can render food susceptible. *bishvil shelo yilkeh ha'kotel* — if one placed the vessel against the wall merely to protect the wall from the rain, *eino be'chi yutan* — the water is not desired and does not render food susceptible.

The Gemara identifies an internal contradiction within this Mishnah. From the first clause, the implication is that if one wanted the water for the wall itself — not to clean the vessel, but to wash the wall — that would be *eino be'chi yutan*, since wanting water for the wall is wanting it for something attached. But from the second clause, the implication runs the other way — that wanting the water for the wall is *be'chi yutan*, sufficient to make food susceptible.

*Amar Rabbi Elazar: tavrah* — Rabbi Elazar resolves the contradiction by splitting the Mishnah. *mi she'shanah zu lo shanah zu* — whoever taught the first half did not teach the second half. The middle case — wanting water for the wall — is actually a disagreement between two anonymous Tannaim, one representing each half of the Mishnah.

Rav Pappa disagrees: *kulei chad tanna hu* — the entire Mishnah is the position of a single Tanna. *ha be'kotel me'arah, ha be'kotel binyan* — the two halves are implicitly discussing two different types of walls. One involves the wall of a cave — a natural, always-attached formation — and the other involves a constructed wall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּכוֹתֶל מְעָרָה, אֲבָל בְּכוֹתֶל בִּנְיָן – בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא יִלְקֶה הַכּוֹתֶל הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן", הָא בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיּוּדַח הַכּוֹתֶל – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּ"כִי יוּתַּן".
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **in** the case of **the wall of a cave,** which was always attached to the ground. **But in** the case of **the wall of a building,** whose stones were detached and subsequently reattached, if he places the bowl **so that the wall will not be damaged,** that **is when** it is **not under** the rubric of the verse "but **when** water **is placed** upon the seed." **But** if he places the bowl **so that the wall will be rinsed, that is under** the rubric of the verse "but **when** water **is placed** upon the seed."

The reading then runs as follows: if one places a vessel against a wall to have it cleaned, the water renders food susceptible. If, however, one only wanted the water for the wall, and it is the wall of a cave — always attached, genuinely *mechubar* — the water does not render food susceptible. But if the wall is a constructed wall — made from materials that began as *talush* — then even wanting the water for the wall suffices, since a constructed wall is treated as *talush u'levasof chibur*, not as true ground. Only if one was trying to keep the water away entirely, to protect the constructed wall, does the water fail to render food susceptible.

### Summary Across Areas


We now have three domains in which the status of *talush u'levasof chibur* has been analyzed. For shechita, although the matter remains somewhat open, at least according to Rabbi it appears that detached-then-attached is treated as *talush*. For *avodah zarah*, Rava states plainly that it is considered *talush*. For *hechsher zera'im*, Rav Pappa's resolution treats a constructed wall — a paradigmatic case of *talush u'levasof chibur* — as *talush* as well.

A possibility arose, however, that different areas of halacha might treat this category differently. The Gemara opens this question: might *talush u'levasof chibur* be treated as *talush* for *avodah zarah* but as *mechubar* for *hechsher zera'im*? And what of shechita?

At the top of 16b, Rava poses the question explicitly: *talush ul'sof chibro l'inyan shechita mai?* — what is the rule for shechita? The Gemara attempts several proofs. The baraita stating that a rock protruding from a wall or a naturally growing reed renders shechita invalid is resolved: *hacha b'mai askinan? B'kotel me'arah* — we are dealing with a cave wall, something always attached, not a constructed wall. The inference is supported by the comparison to the naturally growing reed — *deika nami d'katani dumya d'kaneh oleh me'eilav* — just as the reed was never detached, so too the rock. *shema mina.*

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: תָּלוּשׁ וּלְבַסּוֹף חִבְּרוֹ לְעִנְיַן שְׁחִיטָה מַאי? תָּא שְׁמַע: הָיָה צוֹר יוֹצֵא מִן הַכּוֹתֶל, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה קָנֶה עוֹלֶה מֵאֵלָיו וְשָׁחַט בּוֹ – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּכוֹתֶל מְעָרָה. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי דּוּמְיָא דְּ"קָנֶה עוֹלֶה מֵאֵלָיו", שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** In the case of a blade that was **detached and ultimately one attached it, with regard to slaughter, what** is the *halakha*? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita*: If there **was a flint emerging from a wall or a reed arising** from the ground **on its own and he slaughtered with it, his slaughter is not valid.** Since the wall itself was made from stones that were detached and subsequently reattached, the slaughter is not valid. The Gemara rejects that proof: **What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing **with** the case of **the wall of a cave** that was always attached. The language of the *baraita* **is also precise** in support of that explanation, **as** the *tanna* **teaches** the case of the flint emerging from a wall juxtaposed to, and therefore **similar to,** the case of **a reed arising** from the ground **on its own,** which was also always attached. The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **learn from it** that this is the case.

The baraita that permits a knife lodged in a wall — *na'atz sakin b'kotel v'shachat, shechitato kesheira* — is rejected as proof: *shani sakin d'lo mevatlei* — a knife is different because one never fully commits it to the wall; one always intends to retrieve it. It therefore never truly assumes the status of something permanently attached. One would need a case where materials genuinely become part of a structure — like constructed walls — to pose the question properly, and that remains unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נָעַץ סַכִּין בַּכּוֹתֶל וְשָׁחַט בָּהּ – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. שָׁאנֵי סַכִּין, דְּלָא מְבַטֵּל לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** proof from a *baraita*: If **one embedded a knife in a wall and slaughtered with it, his slaughter is valid.** The knife was detached and then reattached, and the slaughter is valid. The Gemara rejects the proof: The reason that the slaughter is valid is that **a knife is different, as he does not subsume it** to the wall.

### Conceptual Framework


It is worth pausing to reflect on why different areas of halacha might treat *mechubar l'karka* differently. The conceptual issue is one of categories. At the extremes, there is *karka* — the earth itself — and *metaltelin* — movable chattels. The middle category is *mechubar l'karka*: attached to the ground, but not itself the ground.

Some halachot are organized around the distinction between *karka* and everything else, such that the question is whether something qualifies as the ground itself. In *avodah zarah*, the operative verse is *eloheihem al he'harim v'lo he'harim eloheihem* — the mountains do not become gods. The principle is that actual earth, as part of the created landscape, cannot be rendered an object of worship. A constructed house, even though attached to the ground, is not *karka* — it is not the earth itself — and therefore can be rendered forbidden. The category that matters there is whether something is truly *karka* or merely attached to it.

Other halachot, however, are organized around the distinction between *metaltelin* and everything else. For shechita, according to Rabbi, the inference from *ma'achelet* points toward requiring something that functions as a movable implement. Something permanently attached to the ground fails not because it is the earth, but because it is not *metaltelin*. In those frameworks, *mechubar l'karka* may follow the laws of land not because it is land but because it is not chattel — and *talush u'levasof chibur* might or might not share that status depending on how the particular halacha conceptualizes the operative boundary.

This framework is consistent with the law of transactions: a house is bought and sold through the mechanisms of land acquisition, not *meshicha*, because it is not *metaltelin* — not true chattel. There, what matters is that it is not movable, and *mechubar l'karka* falls on the land side of that divide. The precise location of *talush u'levasof chibur* within this spectrum thus legitimately varies by context.

## Knife in Wall

### Moving the Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: נָעַץ סַכִּין בַּכּוֹתֶל וְשָׁחַט בָּהּ, שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר רַב עָנָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁהַסַּכִּין לְמַעְלָה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַטָּה, אֲבָל סַכִּין לְמַטָּה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַעְלָה – חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא יִדְרוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita*: If **one embedded a knife in a wall and slaughtered with it, his slaughter is valid. Rav Anan says** that **Shmuel says:** The *tanna* **taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where **the knife is above and** the **animal's neck is below,** and he raises the animal's head and draws it back and forth on the blade. But in a case where the **knife is below and** the **animal's neck is above,** the slaughter is not valid because **we are concerned lest he press** the knife, due to the weight of the animal, thereby cutting the *simanim* without drawing the knife back and forth, which invalidates the slaughter.

The Gemara returns to the baraita: *natan sakin bekotel v'shachat bah shechitato kesherah* — a knife inserted into a wall and used for shechita is valid. As Rashi established earlier, this works by moving the animal's neck over the knife rather than moving the knife itself.

### Position Requirements


*Amar Rav Anan amar Shmuel: lo shanu ela she'sakin l'malah v'tzavar behemah l'matah* — Shmuel rules that this is valid only when the knife is positioned above and the animal's neck is below. One must always cut toward the front of the throat — not from behind, which would break the spine and render the animal a *treifah* first. The question here is whether the animal lies with the knife pressing from above or stands with a knife pointing upward from below. In the case of an attached knife, Shmuel requires the knife to be above and the throat below.

*Aval sakin l'matah v'tzavar behemah l'malah* — if the knife points upward and the animal's throat is above it, and the animal is being moved back and forth over the upward-pointing knife, this is invalid. *chaishinan shema yidros* — we are concerned that the operator may press down on the animal while moving it, causing the cut to result from pressure rather than the knife's sharpness. This is the defect of *derisah*, which invalidates shechita.

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָתָנֵי: בֵּין שֶׁהַסַּכִּין לְמַטָּה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַעְלָה, בֵּין שֶׁהַסַּכִּין לְמַעְלָה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** explicitly in the *baraita*: With any item that cuts, one may slaughter, whether with a blade that is attached to the ground or with a blade that is detached from the ground; **whether the knife is below and** the **neck of the animal is above or the knife is above and** the **neck of the animal is below?**

The Gemara notes a baraita that appears to contradict this: *bein she'sakin l'matah v'tzavar behemah l'malah, bein she'sakin l'malah v'tzavar behemah l'matah* — whether the knife is below and the throat above, or the knife is above and the throat below, the shechita is valid either way.

### Two Cases Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: לִצְדָדִין קָתָנֵי, סַכִּין לְמַטָּה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַעְלָה – בְּתָלוּשׁ, סַכִּין לְמַעְלָה וְצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה לְמַטָּה – בִּמְחוּבָּר. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: בְּעוֹפָא דְּקַלִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said:** The *baraita* **is taught disjunctively:** In the case where the **knife is below and** the **neck of the animal is above,** the slaughter is valid **when** the blade is **detached.** In the case where the **knife is above and** the **neck of the animal is below,** the slaughter is valid even **when** the blade is **attached. Rav Pappa said:** The *baraita* that teaches that one may slaughter even when the attached knife is below is referring **to** slaughter of **a bird, which is light,** and there is no concern that the weight of the bird will cause the slaughterer to press the bird's neck onto the knife.

*Amar Rav Zevid: l'tzedadin katani* — Rav Zevid resolves the contradiction by reading the baraita's two halves as addressing separate cases. The clause permitting the knife below and the throat above applies to a *talush* — a free, detached knife. There, the shochet moves the knife with a single smooth motion and, even if the animal's throat is above, the natural weight of the animal does not create a *derisah* problem. The clause requiring the knife above applies to a *mechubar* — an attached knife. When the knife is fixed and one must move the animal instead, there is a much greater risk of pressing down on the animal and causing *derisah*. Therefore, when the knife is attached to the wall, it must be positioned above with the throat below.

### Birds Exception


*Amar Rav Pappa: be'ofa dekallil* — Rav Pappa notes an exception. If one is slaughtering a bird — which is light — the concern about *derisah* does not apply in the same way. A bird can be held in one hand and moved with a smooth, controlled motion comparable to moving a knife. Consequently, even with an attached knife, it does not matter which is above and which is below; the lightness of the bird makes *derisah* a non-concern.

## Reeds & Splinters

### Five Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בִּקְרוּמִית שֶׁל קָנֶה: אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין בָּהּ, וְאֵין מָלִין בָּהּ, וְאֵין מְחַתְּכִין בָּהּ בָּשָׂר, וְאֵין מְחַצְּצִין בָּהּ שִׁינַּיִם, וְאֵין מְקַנְּחִים בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says, and some say it was taught in a *baraita*: Five matters were said with regard to the stalk of a reed,** which is used for cutting due to its sharpness. **One may neither slaughter with it,** due to the concern that splinters will be separated and become embedded in the *simanim*, invalidating the slaughter; **nor circumcise with it** for the same reason, due to the potential danger; **nor cut meat with it,** lest splinters become embedded in the meat and endanger one who eats it; **nor pick** one's **teeth with it,** lest he wound himself; **nor wipe with it** after relieving oneself.

*Amar Rav Chisda amar Rav Yitzchak, v'amrei lah bematnita tana: chamisha devarim ne'emru bekrumei shel kaneh* — five laws were stated regarding the reed. One may not use it for shechita, for *bris milah*, to cut meat, to pick one's teeth, or to wipe one's hands. The concern underlying most of these is that reeds splinter: a reed used for shechita may produce splinters that cause tearing in the animal and invalidate the shechita; a reed used for *milah* presents obvious dangers; a reed used to cut meat may leave splinters in the flesh that cut the person eating; a reed used to pick teeth may produce splinters that lacerate the gums; a reed used to wipe the hands may leave splinters in the skin.

### Reed Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין בָּהּ, וְהָתַנְיָא: בַּכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, בֵּין בְּצוֹר, בֵּין בִּזְכוּכִית, בֵּין בִּקְרוּמִית שֶׁל קָנֶה! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּסִימוֹנָא דְּאַגְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: One may neither slaughter with it. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **With any** sharp object **one may slaughter** an animal, **whether with a flint, or with glass** shards, **or with the stalk of a reed? Rav Pappa said:** There, the reference is **to** a specific type of **reed** that grows **in a marsh,** which becomes a smooth, hard surface when it dries.

This prohibition on shechita with a reed appears to contradict an earlier teaching: *bekol shochtin bein betzur bein bizchuchit bein bekrumei shel kaneh* — one may slaughter with anything, whether a rock, glass, or even a reed. *amar rav pappa: besimona de'agma* — Rav Pappa distinguishes between types of reeds. The reed that is permitted for shechita is a specific variety that grows in swamps, hard and rigid, without the tendency to splinter. The reed prohibited is the common type prone to producing splinters. There is no contradiction; the two sources are discussing categorically different materials.

### Contextual Meat Cutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְחַתְּכִין בָּהּ בָּשָׂר. רַב פָּפָּא מְחַתֵּךְ בַּהּ קִרְבֵי דָגִים, דְּזִיגִי. רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא מְחַתֵּךְ בַּהּ עוֹפָא דְּרַכִּיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **Nor cut meat with it. Rav Pappa cuts with** the stalk of a reed **the innards of fish, which are transparent,** such that any splinters would be obvious. **Rabba bar Rav Huna cuts with it** the meat of **a bird, which is soft** and will not cause the stalk of the reed to splinter.

The prohibition on cutting meat with a reed was not universally applied in practice. *rav pappa mitchatech bah karbei dagi* — Rav Pappa himself used a reed to clean the innards of fish. *dezigi* — fish are relatively translucent, so any splinter dislodged in the flesh would be visible and could be removed before eating. *rabbah bar rav huna mitchatech bah ofa* — Rabbah bar Rav Huna used a reed to cut poultry. *derachich* — bird meat is soft, and he was therefore not concerned that the cutting would cause splinters to break off. Each authority assessed the specific context and material and determined that the concern did not apply.

### Witchcraft Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְקַנְּחִים בָּהּ, תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר מָר: הַמְקַנֵּחַ בְּדָבָר שֶׁהָאוּר שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ שִׁינָּיו נוֹשְׁרוֹת. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: קִינּוּחַ פִּי מַכָּה קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **Nor wipe with it** after relieving oneself. The Gemara objects: **Derive** that one may not wipe with it **because the Master said: One who wipes with an object that is flammable, his** lower **teeth,** i.e., the rectum that holds the intestines in place, **fall out. Rav Pappa said** in explanation: The reference in the *baraita* is not to wiping after relieving oneself. Rather, **we are speaking** with regard to **wiping** the blood or dirt from **the opening of a wound.**

The prohibition on using a reed to wipe one's hands rests on a different basis entirely. *ha'menakayach bedavar she'ha'or sholetet bo shinav noshrot* — one who cleans oneself with something flammable will suffer the falling out of teeth. *mekaneach* here refers to using material for toilet purposes, and the concern is not splinters but witchcraft: material that has come into contact with one's body in such intimate circumstances can serve as a medium through which harmful forces — or witchcraft — can take hold. The expression *shinav noshrot* — his teeth fall out — is understood to refer to serious physical deterioration in the affected area.

*Amar Pappa: l'kinuach d'maka ka'amrina* — Rav Pappa clarifies that the prohibition being extended here, based on splinters, applies to wiping in other contexts — cleaning a wound or drying one's hands. There, the witchcraft concern is inapplicable, but the concern about splinters entering the skin remains. The two prohibitions thus operate on separate rationales.

## Perpetual Permission

### Mishnah Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, וּלְעוֹלָם שׁוֹחֲטִין. הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין – הַכֹּל בִּשְׁחִיטָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ עוֹף. לְעוֹלָם שׁוֹחֲטִין, מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַבָּה: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: "כִּי יַרְחִיב ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ אֶת גְּבוּלְךָ כַּאֲשֶׁר דִּבֶּר לָךְ וְאָמַרְתָּ אֹכְלָה בָשָׂר וְגוֹ׳", רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: לֹא בָּא הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לְהַתִּיר לָהֶם בְּשַׂר תַּאֲוָה. שֶׁבַּתְּחִלָּה, נֶאֱסַר לָהֶם בְּשַׂר תַּאֲוָה, מִשֶּׁנִּכְנְסוּ לָאָרֶץ הוּתַּר לָהֶם בְּשַׂר תַּאֲוָה. וְעַכְשָׁיו שֶׁגָּלוּ, יָכוֹל יַחְזְרוּ לְאִיסּוּרָן הָרִאשׁוֹן? לְכָךְ שָׁנִינוּ: לְעוֹלָם שׁוֹחֲטִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **All slaughter [*hakkol shoḥatin*] and one may always slaughter.** The Gemara interprets the phrase: **All slaughter [*hakkol shoḥatin*],** to mean **all** animals are included in the mitzva **of slaughter, and even a bird.** With regard to the statement: **One may always slaughter, who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this *halakha*? **Rabba said:** It **is Rabbi Yishmael, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"When the Lord your God shall expand your border, as He has promised you, and you shall say: I will eat flesh…**you may eat flesh with all the desire of your soul" (Deuteronomy 12:20), **Rabbi Yishmael says: The verse comes only to permit** consumption of the non-sacrificial **meat of desire to** the Jewish people. **As, at the outset,** the **meat of desire was forbidden to them,** and anyone who wanted to eat meat would sacrifice the animal as an offering. After the priest sprinkled the blood, it was permitted for one to eat the meat. **When they entered into Eretz** Yisrael, the **meat of desire was permitted for them,** and they could slaughter and eat meat wherever they chose. Rabba added: **And now that** the Jewish people **were exiled, might** one have thought that **they return to their initial prohibition? Therefore, we learned** in the mishna: **One may always slaughter** non-sacrificial meat.

The Mishnah concludes with the phrase *hakol shochtin u'l'olam shochtin* — all may slaughter and one may always slaughter. As a likely summary of the preceding Mishnayot, *hakol shochtin* recalls the earlier discussion of *cheresh, shoteh v'katan* and the question of non-Jews; *u'l'olam shochtin* recalls the discussions of nighttime, Shabbat, and Yom Kippur — occasions when shechita is halachically impermissible but, performed in violation, is nonetheless valid *bedievid*. It is even possible that this phrase was the original kernel of the tractate — the earliest teaching — and that the preceding Mishnayot represent elaborations of its two components: who may slaughter and when may one slaughter.

### All Require Shechita


The Gemara, however, reads the Mishnah differently. *hakol shochtin* is reinterpreted as *hakol b'shechitah* — all things require shechita. The specific *chiddush* is *v'afilu of* — even birds. Fish require no shechita; birds require a less stringent form of shechita than large animals, needing only one of the two organs to be cut rather than both. There is even an opinion that birds require shechita only rabbinically, not biblically. The Mishnah comes to establish that birds, too, require shechita biblically. Correspondingly, *u'l'olam shochtin* is reread as a statement that one must always slaughter — one must always perform shechita.

### Rabbi Yishmael View


*Amar Rava: Rabbi Yishmael hi* — Rava identifies this reading as representing the position of Rabbi Yishmael. A baraita teaches: *ki yarchiv Hashem Elokecha et gevulcha ka'asher diber lach v'amarta ochla vasar ki ta'aveh nafshecha l'echol vasar b'chol avat nafshecha tochal vasar* — when God will expand your borders as He promised and you will say, I wish to eat meat, because you desire meat, with all your desire you may eat meat. This verse in Devarim addresses the transition from the desert to the Land of Israel. In the desert, all slaughter had to be performed as a *shelamim* offering brought to the *mishkan*; one who simply slaughtered an animal for meat outside that framework violated a prohibition. The Torah here permits, upon entering the Land, the slaughter and eating of meat independent of any sacrificial framework — *basar ta'avah*, meat of desire.

*Rabbi Yishmael omer: lo ba hakatuv ela l'hatir lahem basar ta'avah* — Rabbi Yishmael says the verse comes only to permit meat of desire. *she'batchila ne'esar lahem basar ta'avah* — meat of desire was originally forbidden in the desert; *mishenichnesu l'aretz hutar lahem basar ta'avah* — upon entering the Land, it became permitted.

### Rava's Inference


Rava, working from Rabbi Yishmael's framework, draws a further inference: *v'achshav shegalu, *yachol yachzor* l'issuran harishon* — now that Israel is in exile, one might think they should revert to their original state of prohibition. After all, in the desert, meat of desire was forbidden; perhaps exile — also a time of displacement — reimports that prohibition. *l'olam shochtim* — the Mishnah therefore comes to teach that one may always slaughter and eat meat, including in the present age of exile. The word *l'olam* specifically counters the possibility that the exile-era prohibition could be reinstated.

This reading carries an implicit theology: the permission to eat meat is tied, in some way, to the existence of the Temple and the sacrificial order. When korbanot are brought, they validate — or at least accompany — the general permission to eat meat. One might have thought that in exile, without the Temple, this validation is absent and the original prohibition returns.

### Rav Yosef Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי "לְעוֹלָם שׁוֹחֲטִין", "לְעוֹלָם שׁוֹחֲטִין וְאוֹכְלִין" מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! וְעוֹד, מֵעִיקָּרָא מַאי טַעְמָא אִיתְּסַר – מִשּׁוּם דְּהָווּ מְקָרְבִי לְמִשְׁכָּן, וּלְבַסּוֹף מַאי טַעְמָא אִישְׁתְּרוֹ – דַּהֲווֹ מְרַחֲקִי מִמִּשְׁכָּן, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן הַשְׁתָּא דְּאַרְחִיקוּ לְהוּ טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this.** If so, **this** phrase: **One may always slaughter,** is inappropriate; the *tanna* **should have** taught: **One may always slaughter and eat,** as the matter of permission primarily relates to eating the meat, not to slaughtering the animal. **And furthermore, initially, what is the reason** that the meat of desire **was forbidden?** It was **because** in the wilderness, **they were proximate to the Tabernacle** and could partake of sacrificial meat from the table of God. **And ultimately, what is the reason** that the meat of desire **was permitted?** The reason was **that** in Eretz Yisrael **they were distant from the Tabernacle. And,** if so, **all the more so now,** in exile, **when they are** even **more distant** from the Temple, the meat of desire should be permitted. Consequently, it is unnecessary for the mishna to teach this *halakha*.

*Matkif lah Rav Yosef* — Rav Yosef challenges this reasoning on two counts. First, if the *chiddush* of *l'olam shochtim* is that one remains permitted to eat meat in exile, the Mishnah should have said *l'olam shochtim v'ochlim mibei lei* — one may always slaughter and eat — since the point is the eating, not the shechita. The Mishnah's formulation focuses on the act of shechita, not on the permission to eat meat.

Second, and more fundamentally: *me'ikara mai ta'ama itassar?* — why was meat of desire originally forbidden in the desert in the first place? *mishum d'havu mikravi l'mishkan* — because the Israelites were encamped directly around the *mishkan*, so close to it that they were expected to bring their animals as *shelamim* rather than slaughter them for ordinary consumption. *u'l'vasof mai ta'ama ishtari?* — why then was meat permitted upon entering the Land? *d'havu mirchaki m'mishkan* — because they were distant from the Temple and it was not feasible to bring every slaughter as a korban. The permission to eat *basar ta'avah* is thus a function of distance and inaccessibility, not of any deeper theological condition.

If that is the logic, then *v'kol sheken, hashta, d'itcharku tefei* — now, in exile, they are far more distant still. There is no Temple at all. Of course meat is permitted; the rationale for permitting it applies with far greater force in exile than it did in the Land. The Mishnah cannot be telling us something so obvious. And since the Mishnah also does not say *v'ochlim*, the eating of meat cannot be the *chiddush*. What the Mishnah does mean by *l'olam shochtim* remains to be explained, and that discussion will continue in the next shiur.
