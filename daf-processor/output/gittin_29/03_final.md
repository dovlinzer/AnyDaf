# Gittin 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shaliach Appoints Shaliach

### Intro: Sick Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְחָלָה – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלְּחוֹ בְּיַד אַחֵר. וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ: ״טוֹל לִי הֵימֶנָּה חֵפֶץ פְּלוֹנִי״ – לֹא יְשַׁלְּחֶנּוּ בְּיַד אַחֵר, שֶׁאֵין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁיְּהֵא פִּקְדוֹנוֹ בְּיַד אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an agent **who brings a bill of divorce in Eretz Yisrael,** where his only responsibility is to transmit the bill of divorce to the wife, **and** the agent **became sick, this** agent may **send it in the possession of another** agent. **But if** the husband **said to** the agent: When you transmit the bill of divorce to my wife, **take for me such and such an item from her** that I left with her as a deposit, then **he may not send it in the possession of another** agent. This is because it is assumed **that it is not** the **desire** of the husband **that his deposit be in the possession of another** person whom he did not appoint as his agent.

The *daf* opens with a *mishnah* addressing a scenario central to the laws of *gittin*: a *shaliach* who has been appointed to deliver a *get* in *Eretz Yisrael* becomes ill and cannot complete his *shlichus*. The *mishnah* rules that in such a case, *harei zeh m'shalcho b'yad acher* — he may appoint another person in his stead. What is notable at the outset is that the *mishnah* appears to require no *beit din* and apparently no witnesses for this reassignment, which raises an interesting broader question about how the *shaliach*'s status is established and verified in the first place.

There is a general question of *eidei kiyum hadavar* — whether witnesses must testify to the chain of agency in order for the delivery to be valid. If someone presents himself as an agent to deliver a *get*, how does one know that this is the right party, the actual *shaliach* of the husband? One answer may be that the very act of carrying the *get* itself provides sufficient evidence of the bearer's status. The *get* is not merely a document; it is, in a sense, the embodiment of the *shaliach*'s authority. This would leave open a related question for the woman's side: if the wife appoints a *shaliach* to receive the *get*, there is no comparable physical object to attest to that person's standing. The degree to which testimony is required to establish a *shaliach*'s identity, either on the delivering or receiving side, remains a question the *mishnah* does not fully resolve.

The second clause of the *mishnah* introduces a qualification. If the husband had instructed the *shaliach*, when you deliver the *get*, to also retrieve from the wife a particular object she has been holding — *ve'im amar lo tul li heimena cheifetz ploni* — then *lo yishlachenu b'yad acher*: he may not hand the *shlichus* over to someone else. The reason given is *she'ein retzono sheyehei pikdono b'yad acher* — it is not the husband's desire that his entrusted object be in the hands of another. Just because the husband trusted this particular *shaliach* to retrieve his prized possession does not mean he would be comfortable with a stranger doing so.

This immediately raises a structural question: are these two tasks — delivering the *get* and retrieving the object — one unified *shlichus* or two separate ones? The *mishnah* appears to treat them as intertwined: it says not to send the *get* at all under these conditions, rather than simply instructing the *shaliach* to deliver the *get* while forgoing the retrieval of the object. Whether the *get* itself would be valid after the fact, if the *shaliach* gave it without having retrieved the object, is something the *mishnah* leaves unresolved.

### Chala Tenan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: ״חָלָה״ תְּנַן. פְּשִׁיטָא, ״חָלָה״ קָתָנֵי! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא הוּא הַדִּין אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא חָלָה, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״חָלָה״ אוֹרְחָא דְּמִילְּתָא קָתָנֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Kahana said: We learned** in the mishna that the agent **became sick;** otherwise, he may not appoint a second agent. The Gemara asks: This **is obvious, as** the mishna **teaches** explicitly that the agent **became sick.** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **the same is true,** i.e., that he may transfer the bill of divorce to another agent, **even though** the agent **did not become sick, and** the reason **that** the mishna **teaches** specifically that **he became sick** is that the mishna **teaches the matter in the manner in which it** typically occurs, as an agent typically fulfills his agency, barring unavoidable circumstances, therefore, Rav Kahana **teaches us** that an agent may appoint another agent only when he becomes sick.

The *Gemara* opens with an observation by Rav Kahana: *chala tenan* — our *mishnah* specifically says the *shaliach* got sick. The *Gemara* immediately asks: *peshita* — that is obvious, it says it right there in the text. Why does Rav Kahana feel the need to underscore this? Because one might have thought that *chala* is merely a common illustrative scenario and that the *halakha* would apply even if the *shaliach* did not fall ill — that he could appoint a substitute for any reason whatsoever. Rav Kahana is teaching that the *mishnah* means what it says: illness is a genuine condition, not merely an example. Absent such a circumstance, the *shaliach* is not entitled to appoint someone in his stead.

### Holech vs. At Holech

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״הוֹלֵךְ״ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא חָלָה נָמֵי! וְאִי דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״אַתְּ הוֹלֵךְ״ – אֲפִילּוּ חָלָה נָמֵי לָא! וְאִי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אֲפִילּוּ חָלָה נָמֵי לָא! דְּתַנְיָא: ״הוֹלֵךְ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי״ – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלְּחוֹ בְּיַד אַחֵר. ״אַתְּ הוֹלֵךְ גֵּט זֶה לְאִשְׁתִּי״ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יְשַׁלְּחֶנּוּ בְּיַד אַחֵר. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ – אֵין הַשָּׁלִיחַ עוֹשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances** of his appointment as an agent? **If** this is a case **where** the husband **said to** the agent: **Deliver** this bill of divorce to my wife, then **even though** the agent **did not become sick,** he may **also** appoint an agent in his place, as the husband did not state that he must be the one to deliver the bill of divorce. **And if** this is a case **where he said to** the agent: **You, deliver** this bill of divorce to my wife, then **even** if **he became sick, he also may not** appoint a second agent in his place, as the husband specified that he must be the one to deliver the bill of divorce. **And if** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** then **even** if **he became sick** he **also may not** appoint a second agent, no matter what the husband said. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* in the *Tosefta* (2:13): If the husband said to an agent: **Deliver this bill of divorce to my wife,** then **this** agent **may send it in the possession of another** agent. However, if the husband said: **You, deliver this bill of divorce to my wife,** then **this** agent **may not send it in the possession of another** agent. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Whether** the husband said it **like this or** whether the husband said it **like that, the agent may not designate another agent.**

The *Gemara* then anticipates a tension with an upcoming *braisa* and introduces the key distinction between *holech* and *at holech*. If the husband said simply *holech get zeh l'ishti* — deliver this *get* to my wife — the *tanna kamma* of the *braisa* rules that the *shaliach* may appoint a substitute even without having fallen ill. If, however, the husband said *at holech get zeh l'ishti* — you deliver this *get* to my wife — the *shaliach* may not appoint another even if he does fall ill, since the use of the word "you" implies a personal designation. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* goes further, ruling that regardless of which formulation the husband used, *ein shaliach oseh shaliach* — a *shaliach* may never appoint a substitute. Neither the *tanna kamma* nor Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel invokes illness as a relevant condition, leaving the *mishnah* apparently in tension with the *braisa*.

### Three Reconciliations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא ״הוֹלֵךְ״, וְהוּא דְּחָלָה; וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא ״אַתְּ הוֹלֵךְ״, וְחָלָה שָׁאנֵי; וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא, וְחָלָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that the mishna is referring to a case where the husband said to his agent: **Deliver** the bill of divorce, without specifying that he must be the one to do so, and Rav Kahana understood the *baraita* as follows: **And this** *halakha*, that he may send it in the possession of another agent, applies only **when** the agent **became sick. And if you wish, say** that the mishna is referring to a case where the husband said: **You deliver, but one who became sick is different,** and it is assumed that under those circumstances the husband would allow him to designate another agent. **And if you wish, say** that the mishna **is** even in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** that an agent is ordinarily not permitted to designate another agent. **But** a case where the agent **became sick is different,** and the agent may designate another agent.

The *Gemara* proposes three ways to harmonize the *braisa* with the *mishnah*.

The first and most restrictive reading holds that both conditions must be met simultaneously: the husband must have said *holech* (not *at holech*), and the *shaliach* must have fallen ill. Only under both conditions may he appoint a substitute.

The second reading is more permissive: either condition is sufficient. Even if the husband said *at holech*, if the *shaliach* fell ill, that changes matters — *chala shani*, illness is different. Conversely, if the husband said *holech* without the restrictive formulation, the *shaliach* may appoint a substitute regardless of illness.

The third reading applies this same logic to the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Even according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who rules that a *shaliach* may never appoint a substitute under ordinary circumstances, *chala shani* — illness creates an exception. His ruling stands only for the default case; when the *shaliach* is incapacitated, even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would permit reassignment.

### Husband's Intent

Underlying all three reconciliations is a single animating principle: the entire analysis is a question of the husband's intent and the scope of the authority he granted. Whether the governing factor is the specific word *at*, or the absence of illness, these are all tools for reading what the husband implicitly authorized when he empowered his *shaliach*. It is not a special dispensation triggered by illness as such — *chala* is not some independent *halacha l'Moshe m'Sinai*. Rather, when we say *chala shani*, we mean that in cases where it becomes genuinely impossible for the original *shaliach* to complete the task, we presume the husband — whose overriding interest is that the *get* be delivered — would have consented to a transfer of the agency. The same logic would presumably apply to any other *ones*, any other circumstance that genuinely prevents the *shaliach* from completing his mission. Whether this reasoning extends to a *shaliach* who simply changes his mind and refuses to complete the delivery, without any objective impediment, is a harder question whose answer is not clearly resolved.

## Shaliach Oseh Shaliach

### Two vs. Three People

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְחָלָה – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלְּחוֹ בְּיַד אַחֵר. וּרְמִינְהוּ, אָמַר לִשְׁנַיִם: ״תְּנוּ גֵּט לְאִשְׁתִּי״; אוֹ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה: ״כִּתְבוּ גֵּט וּתְנוּ לְאִשְׁתִּי״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יִכְתְּבוּ וְיִתְּנוּ. אִינְהוּ אִין, אֲבָל שָׁלִיחַ לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to an agent **who brings a bill of divorce in Eretz Yisrael, and** the agent **became sick, this** agent may **send it in the possession of another** agent. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (66a): If the husband **said to two** people: **Give a bill of divorce to my wife, or** if he said **to three** people: **Write a bill of divorce and give** it **to my wife,** then **these** people **should write** it **and give** it. The Gemara infers from the mishna: **They** themselves, **yes,** they should do so, **but** one whom they appoint as **an agent** may **not** do so.

The *Gemara* now introduces a challenge to the entire idea that a *shaliach* may appoint another *shaliach*. A *mishnah* elsewhere states: if the husband said to two people *tenu get l'ishti* — give a *get* to my wife — or to three people *kitvu get utenu l'ishti* — write a *get* and give it to my wife — those individuals themselves must carry out the task. They may not delegate it. *Inhu in, aval shaliach lo* — they yes, but a *shaliach* no.

The *Gemara* explains the internal logic of this *mishnah*. When the husband addresses three people with the word *tenu* — "give" or "see that it is given" — those three constitute a *beit din*, and the word *tenu* implies supervision rather than personal execution. They may therefore appoint someone to carry out the writing. But if he says *kitvu utenu* to those three, he is explicitly directing them to write it themselves, and personal execution is required. When he addresses only two, even if he says merely *tenu*, those two are simply agents — *shluchim* — not a *beit din*, and an agent cannot delegate his agency. The principle is stark: *ein shaliach oseh shaliach*, a *shaliach* cannot make a *shaliach*.

This apparently contradicts the *mishnah* that opened the *daf*, which permits the sick *shaliach* to appoint a replacement. The *Gemara* must explain the difference.

### Beit Din Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָתָם טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם בִּזָּיוֹן דְּבַעַל, הָכָא בַּעַל לָא קָפֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: There, what is the reason** that an agent may not be appointed? It is **due to degradation of the husband,** who does not want the matter to become known, and therefore they may not designate another agent. However, **here,** after he has already sent the bill of divorce, the **husband is not particular** that word not spread, and therefore the agent may designate another agent in his place.

The *Gemara* draws a fundamental structural distinction. When the husband addresses a *beit din* and uses the language of *tenu*, he is not appointing them as personal agents; he is conferring upon them institutional authority to oversee the process. As a public institution, a *beit din* occupies a different role than an individual *shaliach*. They are not transmitting a personal agency they received; they are exercising a public institutional function that allows them to appoint an agent to carry out the delivery. By contrast, two individuals addressed as *shluchim*, even if told simply to "see that it is given," remain personal agents and cannot pass on what they received.

This distinction also explains something that might otherwise seem arbitrary. The husband does not need to sign a document or perform a *kinyan* appointing the *beit din*; a verbal instruction is sufficient. While the ephemeral quality of purely verbal appointment will soon become a central issue, the point here is that the *beit din*'s power derives not from a transferred personal agency but from its own institutional standing, once activated by the husband's directive.

### Bizayon D'Baal

Abaye offers the first of two answers to explain why a private *shaliach* — as opposed to a *beit din* — cannot delegate the writing of a *get*. His reasoning: *mishum bizyona d'baal* — because of the embarrassment of the husband. In contrast, when it comes to the delivery of the *get* across a distance, *hacha ba'al lo kapid* — there the husband does not mind.

What specifically is embarrassing about the writing stage? Rashi explains that there was a social presumption that a husband would write his own *get*. This may seem surprising today, when the involvement of a specialist *sofer* is entirely standard, but the underlying concept is that the *get* is fundamentally an expression of the husband's own will, much as a standard deed of sale — *sadi mechura lach* — is an expression of the seller's intent. When the husband delegates the writing to someone else, there is something inherently private about that act, and the presumption is that he wants to keep it contained. Anything short of a formal *beit din*, explicitly told to oversee the matter, is assumed to reflect the husband's desire for discretion. By contrast, when the *get* is being transported across a distance, the very nature of the situation requires an intermediary; there is no presumption of discretion to protect, and no embarrassment in passing on the task.

It is important to note the character of Abaye's answer: it is contingent and contextual, not intrinsic. There is nothing about the nature of *shlichus* itself that prevents delegation; it is a presumed preference of this particular husband under these particular circumstances.

### Mili Lo Mimsran

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם דְּמִילֵּי נִינְהוּ, וּמִילֵּי לָא מִימַּסְרָן לְשָׁלִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There is a different reason that in the case of the mishna (66a) the agent may not appoint another to write the bill of divorce; it is **because** of the fact **that** in the case there, the husband's instructions **are** mere **words, and** verbal **directives cannot be delegated to an agent,** i.e., an agent cannot be deputized to give instructions on behalf of another.

Rava offers a more fundamental answer: *mishum de-mili ninhu* — because an oral appointment consists of nothing but words, and *mili lo mimsran l'shaliach* — words cannot be handed over to a *shaliach*.

This gets to something structurally deeper. When the husband says "write a *get* for my wife," there is nothing tangible that the appointed *shaliach* receives. No object changes hands, no document of agency is produced. The appointment is entirely abstract, existing only in the verbal instruction. Because there is nothing concrete to pass on, there is no mechanism by which the *shaliach* can transfer his status to another. He received a verbal charge directly from the husband, and that charge has no form that can be re-transmitted.

The case of delivering a *get* is different precisely because the *get* itself exists as a physical object. The *shaliach* carries the *get*, and the *get* embodies his authority. Whoever holds it is manifestly empowered to deliver it, and that tangible embodiment allows the agency to be transferred along with the object.

The implications of Rava's principle extend further than the *get* case. It would also explain why a woman's *shaliach* to receive a *get* cannot appoint a substitute — she gave him no object to carry forward. And it would mean that even if the husband explicitly declared he has no objection to the *shaliach* delegating his role in the writing of the *get*, this declaration would not help; the inability to delegate is not rooted in the husband's *hakpada* but in the structural impossibility of transmitting a purely verbal appointment. The *beit din* remains the sole exception, because the *beit din* is not receiving a transmitted agency at all — it is exercising its own institutional authority once the husband has activated it.

### Matanah K'Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ שְׁלִיחַ מַתָּנָה; וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל – רַב אָמַר: מַתָּנָה אֵינָהּ כְּגֵט. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מַתָּנָה הֲרֵי הִיא כְּגֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the explanations offered by Abaye and Rava? The Gemara answers: There is a practical difference **between them** in the case of **an agent** appointed to write a deed of **a gift, and** their disagreement is parallel **to the dispute of Rav and Shmuel. Rav says:** A deed of **gift is not like a bill of divorce,** as the gift giver does not care who writes the deed. Since it is not degrading to him if another agent writes the deed of gift instead of the agent that he appointed, the agent may appoint another agent. **And Shmuel says:** A deed of **gift is like a bill of divorce,** as the mere words instructing the agent to write the deed cannot be transferred to another agent.

The *Gemara* asks what the practical difference is between Abaye's and Rava's answers. The case that distinguishes them is a *shaliach* appointed to execute a gift — *l'shaliach matanah* — where the husband instructs one person to write a deed transferring a plot of land and give it to the recipient. There is no *bizayon* here; there is nothing private or embarrassing about making a gift. If Abaye's reasoning is correct, then in this case the *shaliach* could delegate the writing to someone else. But if Rava is correct, the structural impossibility of transmitting a verbal appointment applies here just as it does to the *get*, and the *shaliach* cannot delegate.

This becomes the debate of Rav and Shmuel. Rav holds that *matanah einah k'get* — a gift is unlike a *get*, and the *shaliach* for the writing of a gift deed may appoint another. Shmuel holds that *matanah harei hi k'get* — it is governed by the same principle, because the issue has nothing to do with embarrassment and everything to do with the inherent impossibility of transmitting a purely verbal appointment. In Shmuel's view, Rava's principle — *mili lo mimsran l'shaliach* — applies to any verbal agency that lacks a physical embodiment, whether the document in question is a *get* or a gift deed.

## Object Condition

### Borrower Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אָמַר לוֹ ״טוֹל לִי הֵימֶנָּה חֵפֶץ פְּלוֹנִי״. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כָּאן שָׁנָה רַבִּי: אֵין הַשּׁוֹאֵל רַשַּׁאי לְהַשְׁאִיל, וְאֵין הַשּׂוֹכֵר רַשַּׁאי לְהַשְׂכִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But if** the husband **said to** the agent: When you transmit the bill of divorce to my wife, **take for me such and such an item from her,** then the agent may not appoint a second agent, as it is not the desire of the husband that his deposit be in the possession of another. **Reish Lakish says: Here, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **taught** that **a borrower is not allowed to lend** the item that he borrowed to someone else, **and a renter is not allowed to rent out** the item that he rented to someone else. In those cases, the same reasoning applies, i.e., that it is not the desire of the owner that his item be in the possession of another.

The *Gemara* returns to the second clause of the *mishnah*: if the husband instructed the *shaliach* to retrieve a specific object from the wife, the *shaliach* may not appoint a substitute, because the husband does not want his *pikadon* in the hands of another person. Reish Lakish observes that the *mishnah* is teaching the same principle established elsewhere: *ein hasho'el rashai lehash'il ve'ein hasocher rashai lehaskir* — a borrower may not re-lend, and a renter may not sublet. Just as a person who entrusts an object to you does not thereby consent to having it in someone else's possession, so too the husband who entrusts the retrieval of his object to this *shaliach* does not consent to having another person handle it.

### Chiddush Identified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ תִּינוֹקוֹת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן יוֹדְעִים אוֹתָהּ! אֶלָּא זִימְנִין דְּגִיטָּא נָמֵי לָא הָוֵי – דְּנַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ ״אַל תְּגָרְשָׁהּ אֶלָּא בַּבַּיִת״, וְגֵירְשָׁהּ בָּעֲלִיָּיה; ״אַל תְּגָרְשָׁהּ אֶלָּא בְּיָמִין״, וְגֵירְשָׁהּ בִּשְׂמֹאל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: Even schoolchildren know this** *halakha*, and this is not the novel idea expressed in the mishna. The novel idea expressed in the mishna is as follows: Not only is it not permitted for an agent to appoint another agent in this case, as it violates the desire of the husband, **but sometimes** it happens **that the bill of divorce is also not** valid if a second agent is appointed. This is **because** the first agent **becomes as one that** it **was said to him: Divorce her only in the house, and he divorced her in the attic;** or the husband said: **Divorce her only with** your **right** hand, **and he divorced her with** his **left** hand. Here as well, since the agent violated the instructions the divorce will not be valid.

Rabbi Yochanan pushes back sharply: *zo afilu tinokos shel beis raban yode'in* — even young schoolchildren know that principle. It requires no *mishnah* to teach us that entrusting something to one person does not authorize that person to hand it to another. That is not the *chiddush* of the *mishnah* at all.

The true *chiddush*, says Rabbi Yochanan, is *zimnin de-gitta nami lo havei* — there are circumstances in which the *get* itself may be invalid as a result of this entanglement with the object. The *mishnah* is not merely saying that the *shaliach* violated a secondary duty by passing the object to someone else; in certain configurations, the very act of giving the *get* under the wrong conditions is an invalid giving. The *get* and the retrieval of the object can become so intertwined, depending on how the husband framed his instruction, that the *kashrut* of the *get* is conditional upon the object being handled correctly.

Rabbi Yochanan draws the analogy: it is as if the husband said "do not divorce her except in a house" and the *shaliach* divorced her upstairs, or "give it with your right hand" and he gave it with his left. When the husband frames an instruction as a condition on the manner of delivery, violating that condition invalidates the *get*.

### Woman Greeted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא, הֵיכָא דְּנָפְקָה לְאַפֵּיהּ וְיָהֲבָה לֵיהּ חֵפֶץ, וַהֲדַר שָׁקְלָה מִינֵּיהּ גִּיטָּא; כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּגִיטָּא – גִּיטָּא מְעַלְּיָא הָוֵי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי הֵיכָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Everyone** agrees that **when** it happens **that** the wife **went out to** greet **him and gave him the item** that the husband requested, **and then** she **took the bill of divorce from him,** in this case **everyone agrees that the bill of divorce is a proper bill of divorce. When they disagree** is in a case **when** the husband **said to** the agent:

The *Gemara* proceeds to delineate the cases. Where there is no dispute: if the woman came out to greet the *shaliach* and proactively handed him the object, and he then gave her the *get* — *kulei alma lo peligi de-gitta gitta ma'alya havei* — everyone agrees it is a valid *get*. The object was received before the *get* was given, the order was preserved, and there is no concern. This scenario is uncontroversial.

### Order Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁקוֹל מִינַּהּ חֵפֶץ וַהֲדַר הַב לַהּ גִּיטָּא; וַאֲזַל אִיהוּ וִיהַב לַהּ גִּיטָּא, וַהֲדַר שְׁקַל מִינַּהּ חֵפֶץ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן פּוֹסֵל בּוֹ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן בִּשְׁלוּחוֹ, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מַכְשִׁיר בִּשְׁלוּחוֹ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Take an item from her and then give her the bill of divorce, and he went and gave her the bill of divorce and then took the item from her. Rabbi Yoḥanan invalidates** the bill of divorce even when given **by** the first agent, as he deviated from the husband's instructions, and his agency is canceled, **and all the more so** does Rabbi Yoḥanan invalidate the bill of divorce when delivered **by** the first agent's **agent. And Reish Lakish deems** the bill of divorce **valid** when it is delivered **by** the first agent's **agent, and all the more so** when it is delivered **by** the first agent **himself.**

The dispute arises specifically when the husband framed the instruction in a particular order: *shekuli minach chefetz ve-hadar hav lah gitta* — first take the object from her, then give her the *get*. In this formulation, the husband is clearly treating the retrieval of the object as a precondition for the delivery of the *get*. Now if the *shaliach* reversed the order — *d'azil ihu ve-yahvei lah gita ve-hadar shakil minah chefetz* — he first gave her the *get*, forfeiting his leverage, and only then retrieved the object — this is where the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish comes into focus.

### Rabbi Yochanan vs. Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish holds that the *get* and the retrieval of the object are always independent of one another. Even when the husband used the language of a specific order, Reish Lakish does not read this as creating a genuine condition on the *kashrut* of the *get*. Therefore, the *get* is valid whether delivered by the original *shaliach* himself in the wrong order, or by a substitute *shaliach* — *machshir b'shlucho ve-chol shekein bo*.

Rabbi Yochanan holds that when the husband framed the instruction with a specific sequence — first the object, then the *get* — the *kashrut* of the *get* is dependent on the correct order. *Posel bo ve-chol shekein b'shlucho*: the *get* is invalid even if the original *shaliach* himself delivers it in the wrong order, and certainly if a substitute does it.

The logic of Rabbi Yochanan's position deserves attention. Even if the *shaliach* first gave the *get* and then successfully retrieved the object two seconds later, this does not remedy the defect. At the moment the *get* was given, the *shaliach* was acting contrary to the husband's expressed intent — he was surrendering his leverage before securing the object. The invalidity attaches to the moment of giving, and what happens afterward cannot retroactively cure it.

Rabbi Yochanan's reading of the *mishnah* encompasses two distinct ways in which the *get* can be invalidated when the husband expressed this conditional order. First: the original *shaliach* himself delivers the *get* before retrieving the object. Second: the original *shaliach* appoints a substitute, and even if that substitute performs the acts in the correct order, the *get* is still invalid — because the husband never consented to having a stranger handle his object at all.

As one might observe, in practical life when two parties seek leverage over each other, the standard solution is a trusted neutral intermediary to whom both simultaneously deliver their respective obligations. Such a mechanism would in principle satisfy Rabbi Yochanan's framework, provided the husband had consented to the terms. But the asymmetry here is that the husband holds unilateral power over the *get*, making the dynamic structurally different from a bilateral exchange.

## Get from Overseas

### Overseas Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט מִמְּדִינַת הַיָּם, וְחָלָה – עוֹשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ בְּבֵית דִּין וּמְשַׁלְּחוֹ, וְאוֹמֵר לִפְנֵיהֶם: ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״; וְאֵין שָׁלִיחַ אַחֲרוֹן צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״, אֶלָּא אוֹמֵר: שְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין אֲנִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an agent **who is bringing a bill of divorce from a country overseas,** who must attest to the fact that he witnessed the writing and signing of the bill of divorce, **and he became sick** and cannot complete his agency, **he appoints another agent in court and sends him. And** the first agent **says before** the court: **It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence,** and on the basis of this the court deems the bill of divorce to be valid. **And the final agent does not need to say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. Rather,** it is sufficient that **he says: I am an agent of the court.**

The *mishnah* now turns to a more complex case. A *shaliach* bringing a *get* from overseas — *mimedinat hayam* — becomes ill and cannot complete the delivery. In *Eretz Yisrael*, where the *shaliach* is not required to declare *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam*, he may simply hand off the *get* to a substitute. But outside *Eretz Yisrael*, matters are more complicated: the original *shaliach* was the one who witnessed the writing and signing of the *get* and is therefore the one qualified to make the declaration. A substitute *shaliach* was not present at those events and cannot make that declaration in his own name.

### Beit Din Testimony

The *mishnah* provides the solution: the original *shaliach* must appear before a *beit din* and testify — *omer lifneihem bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam* — he witnessed the writing and witnessed the signing. The *beit din* then appoints a new *shaliach* to carry the *get* forward. That second *shaliach* need not himself declare *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam*, because he was not a witness to those events; instead he declares *shaliach beit din ani* — I am an agent of the court. The court's appointment, grounded in the testimony of the original *shaliach*, supplies the necessary authentication.

This procedure also has an implicit bearing on the question of where and how *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam* must be recited — the *mishnah* presupposes that it is delivered before a *beit din*, a point that was subject to debate in the first *perek* of the tractate.

### Shaliach D'Shaliach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לַאֲבִימִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ,ּ שָׁלִיחַ דְּשָׁלִיחַ מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ, אוֹ לָא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָא לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לְכוּ, מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״אֵין הַשָּׁלִיחַ הָאַחֲרוֹן״, מִכְלָל דִּמְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ;
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages said to Avimei, son of Rabbi Abbahu: Raise** the following **dilemma before** your father, **Rabbi Abbahu: Can an agent of** the first **agent** also **appoint** another **agent, or not? He said to them: You should not raise this dilemma. From** the fact **that** the mishna **teaches: The final agent does not** need to say, as opposed to: The second agent does not need to say, it can be seen **by inference that** the second agent **can appoint an agent,** resulting in the existence of a final agent, not just a second agent.

The rabbis posed a question to Avimi, the son of Rabbi Abbahu, to bring to his father: can the second *shaliach* — the one appointed by the *beit din* — in turn appoint a third *shaliach*, and so on down the chain?

### Multiple Shlichim

Rabbi Abbahu dismissed the question as unnecessary. The answer is evident from the *mishnah* itself, which refers to *shaliach acharon* — the final *shaliach* — rather than simply the second *shaliach*. The use of *acharon* implies that there can be any number of *shluchim* in the chain. From the *michlal* — from the logical implication — that it says "the last *shaliach*" rather than "the second *shaliach*," it is clear that the delegation may continue indefinitely, *shaliach* appointing *shaliach* in succession.

### Court Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לְכוּ: כִּי מְשַׁוֵּי שָׁלִיחַ – בְּבֵית דִּין, אוֹ אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הָא לָא מִבַּעְיָא לַן, מִדְּקָתָנֵי: אֶלָּא אוֹמֵר ״שְׁלִיחַ בֵּית דִּין אֲנִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, when you raise the dilemma,** this is what you should ask: **When** the second agent **appoints** another **agent,** does he need to appoint him specifically **in court, or** can he do so **even while not in court? They said to him: We do not raise this dilemma,** as **from** the fact **that it teaches: Rather, he says: I am an agent of the court,** it is clear that any agent in this role must be appointed in court.

Rabbi Abbahu then identified what he considered the genuinely interesting question: when *shaliach* number two appoints *shaliach* number three, must that appointment also take place before a *beit din*, or may it be done informally? The second *shaliach* does not himself have a declaration of *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam* to offer — he can only say he was appointed by the *beit din*. The question is whether his ability to extend the chain depends on a court being involved at each successive stage, or whether having once established the chain through a *beit din*, subsequent transfers may proceed without one.

The *mishnah*'s language resolves this as well. Since every *shaliach* in the chain declares *shaliach beit din ani* — I am an agent of the court — this implies that a *beit din* must be involved at each stage of appointment. One might further suggest that the entire mechanism works precisely because at no stage is one personal *shaliach* transmitting his personal agency to another; rather, the court continuously reasserts its institutional appointment at each step, so that each *shaliach* is genuinely a direct appointee of the court.

## Eretz Yisrael Chain

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: שָׁלִיחַ בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, עוֹשֶׂה כַּמָּה שְׁלוּחִין. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִם מֵת רִאשׁוֹן – בָּטְלוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba says: An agent in Eretz Yisrael can appoint several agents,** i.e., he can appoint an agent, and the second agent can appoint another agent in his place. All of this can take place outside of court, because when a bill of divorce is sent within Eretz Yisrael there is no need to testify that it was written and signed in the agent's presence. **Rav Ashi says: If** the **first** agent **died,** then **they are all canceled,** as all the agents act on the basis of the first agent. With his death, the agency is canceled.

Rava now addresses the parallel question for *Eretz Yisrael*. *Shliach b'Eretz Yisroel oseh kama shluchim* — a *shaliach* in *Eretz Yisrael* may make any number of *shluchim*, passing the *get* down an extended chain of agents.

### No Court Needed

This is not subject to the same requirements as the overseas case. Since there is no obligation to declare *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam* in *Eretz Yisrael*, the entire procedure is private rather than court-mediated. The first *shaliach* need not say *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam*, and the final *shaliach* need not say *shaliach beit din ani*. The chain is sustained without any *beit din* involvement at any stage. As Rashi spells out: none of those formal requirements apply in *Eretz Yisrael*, and yet the chain may extend as far as needed.

### Death of First

Rav Ashi raises a striking question about this chain. What if the first *shaliach* dies? Does the death of *shaliach* number one cause all the subsequent *shluchim* to become invalid — *im meis rishon batlu kulan*? This would be the case if each successive *shaliach* were understood as deriving his authority from the one who appointed him, making the chain's validity dependent on each link remaining intact.

### Power from Husband

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָא דְּאַבָּא, דְּקַטְנוּתָא הִיא! אִילּוּ מֵת בַּעַל, מִידֵּי מְשָׁשָׁא אִית בְּהוּ?! כּוּלְּהוּ מִכֹּחַ מַאן קָאָתוּ – מִכֹּחַ דְּבַעַל קָאָתוּ; אִיתֵיהּ לְבַעַל – אִיתַנְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ, לֵיתֵיהּ לְבַעַל – לֵיתַנְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi said: This** statement **of** my **father is** from the time **of his youth,** and it is not correct, as, **if** the **husband dies,** even if all of the agents are alive, **is there is any significance to** any of **them? All of them, from whose authority do they come** to deliver the bill of divorce? **They come from the husband's authority.** Therefore, if the **husband is** alive, then **they are all** able to act as agents; if the **husband is not** alive, then **they are all not** agents. The status of the agents is dependent on the husband, not on the first agent.

Bar Rav Ashi swiftly and decisively refutes this position. *Ilu meis baal, midi mashasha is behu?* — if the husband died, would any of the *shluchim* retain validity? Of course not. The fact that a husband's death extinguishes all the *shluchim* simultaneously demonstrates that all of them derive their authority from the husband alone, not from any intermediate *shaliach*. *Kulam mi'koach d'baal ka'asu* — they all come from the power of the husband. *Isei l'baal isei l'kulehu* — as long as the husband lives, all the *shluchim* are valid. *Leisei l'baal leisei l'kulehu* — if the husband is gone, none of them have any standing regardless of how many intermediate *shluchim* remain alive.

This conclusion may seem intuitive, but it is worth noting that an analogous case — *shomer she'masar l'shomer*, where one guardian transfers an object to another guardian — is not quite so clear. There, the question is genuinely open as to whether the second *shomer* is watching the object on behalf of the first *shomer* or directly on behalf of the original owner. The *sugya* in Bava Metzia explores this in considerable depth, including the possibility that one could actually profit from the arrangement by converting a *shomer chinam* arrangement into a *shomer sachar* one at an intermediate step. But in the case of *gittin*, the logic is different: the act of divorcing one's wife is something that only the husband has standing to perform, and anyone empowered to carry out that act must necessarily be representing the husband directly. There is simply no other party they could be representing.

## Abba Bar Minyumi Case

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּשַׁדַּר לַהּ גִּיטָּא לִדְבֵיתְהוּ, אֲמַר שָׁלִיחַ: לָא יָדַעְנָא לַהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל יַהֲבֵיהּ לְאַבָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי, דְּאִיהוּ יָדַע לַהּ, וְלֵיזִיל וְלִיתְּבֵיהּ נִיהֲלַהּ. אֲתָא וְלָא אַשְׁכַּח לְאַבָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי. אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא, וְיָתֵיב רַב סָפְרָא גַּבַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was an incident involving **a certain man who sent a bill of divorce to his wife** with an agent. **The agent said: I do not know her.** The husband **said to him: Go give** the bill of divorce **to Abba bar Minyumi, as he knows her, and he will go and give it to her.** The agent **came and did not find Abba bar Minyumi,** and he did not know what to do. **He found Rabbi Abbahu and Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa, and Rav Safra was sitting among them.**

The *Gemara* now presents a practical case that tests these principles. A man sent a *get* to his wife via a *shaliach*. The *shaliach* said: I do not know who she is — *lo yadana lah* — I cannot identify the wife. The husband responded: go give it to Abba bar Minyumi, *d'ihu yada lah* — he knows who my wife is — and he will then go and give it to her. This was presumably a case involving *chutz la'aretz*, where the formal procedures of *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam* and *beit din* appointment would be implicated.

### Rabbis' Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מְסוֹר מִילָּךְ קַמֵּי דִּידַן, דְּכִי יֵיתֵי אַבָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי נִיתְּבִינֵיהּ לֵיהּ, וְלֵיזִיל וְלִיתְּבִינֵיהּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The agent asked them what he should do. The first three Sages **said to him: Transfer your words,** i.e., your agency, **before us, as when Abba bar Minyumi comes, we will give** the bill of divorce **to him, and he will go and give it to her.**

The *shaliach* arrived at the destination but could not find Abba bar Minyumi. He did, however, encounter Rabbi Abbahu, Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa, and Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha — and Rav Safra was also present. The three rabbis proposed a solution: testify before us *bifnei nichtav u-vifnei nechtam*. Once you have done so, we as a *beit din* will appoint Abba bar Minyumi when he is located — *de-chi atei Abba bar Minyumi ve-nispineih lei ve-leizil ve-lisveih nihala* — and he will go and deliver the *get* to the wife. The solution maps cleanly onto the procedure described in the *mishnah*: the first *shaliach* testifies before the court, the court appoints the next agent, and the chain continues.

### Rav Safra's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב סָפְרָא: וְהָא שְׁלִיחַ שֶׁלֹּא נִיתַּן לְגֵירוּשִׁין הוּא! אִיכְּסוּפוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra said to them: But isn't he an agent who is not granted** the ability **to** effect **divorce,** as he was given only the authority to transfer the bill of divorce to Abba bar Minyumi? Therefore, he cannot transfer the agency to another person. These Sages **were embarrassed** that they ruled improperly.

Rav Safra, who had been sitting alongside, raised a pointed objection: *ve-ha shaliach she'lo nitan l'geirushin hu* — but this *shaliach* is one who was not empowered to deliver the *get* himself. He was only empowered to hand the *get* to Abba bar Minyumi. If the *shaliach* was never fully invested with the authority to deliver the *get* directly, how can he appear before a *beit din* and transfer to them a responsibility he himself did not have?

### Full Empowerment

Rav Safra's challenge rests on a substantive principle: in order to confer upon a *beit din* the authority to oversee the delivery of a *get*, the transferring *shaliach* must himself have been fully empowered to deliver that *get*. It is a question of what he can give over — if you are only authorized to hand the *get* to a specific intermediary, you do not have the full role of a *shaliach l'gittin*, and therefore you cannot activate the *beit din*'s institutional authority in your stead. The three senior rabbis were visibly embarrassed — *ikhsifu* — by this challenge.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קַפְּחִינְהוּ רַב סָפְרָא לִתְלָתָא רַבָּנַן סְמִוכֵי. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: בְּמַאי קַפְּחִינְהוּ? מִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ ״אַבָּא בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי וְלָא אַתְּ״?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rav Safra struck [*kappeḥinhu*]** a blow **to three ordained Sages,** as although he was from Babylonia and not ordained, he was correct. **Rav Ashi said: With what did he strike them?** He did not conclusively refute their opinion, as, **did** the husband **say to the** agent: **Abba bar Minyumi** should deliver the bill of divorce **and not you?** Rather, he appointed this agent to deliver the bill of divorce, and added that if he cannot find the wife, then he can transfer the bill of divorce to Abba bar Minyumi.

Rava then commented that Rav Safra had managed to embarrass three ordained *rabbanim* with his question. Rav Ashi, however, questioned whether the embarrassment was warranted — *bemai kaphinei* — what kind of a challenge was that really? *Mi amar lei Abba bar Minyomi velo at?* The husband never said "only Abba bar Minyumi and no one else." The instruction to find Abba bar Minyumi was practical advice — Abba bar Minyumi knows who my wife is — not a restrictive condition excluding the original *shaliach* from delivering the *get* himself. If the *shaliach* had happened to encounter the wife and identified her himself, there would have been no objection to his delivering the *get* directly. He was therefore *nitan l'geirushin* — fully empowered to deliver the *get* — and Rav Safra's challenge collapses.

There is an alternative reading in the *Rishonim* in which Rav Ashi reads the situation differently, concluding that the husband did indeed mean "Abba bar Minyumi and not you." But the more logical reading of the circumstances, as presented, is that the husband's specification of Abba bar Minyumi was a means of solving the *shaliach*'s practical problem — the *shaliach* did not know the wife — not an expression of distrust in the *shaliach* himself.

What makes this case enduringly interesting, however, is not merely its resolution but the principle it exposes. If a *shaliach* were truly appointed only to hand the *get* to a designated intermediary, and had no personal authority to deliver it himself, one would have to ask: in what sense is he a *shaliach* at all? His role would be indistinguishable from a messenger service. If all he is doing is conveying the *get* to the next person in line, one might argue that the husband has really appointed Abba bar Minyumi as the true *shaliach*, and the first person is merely a courier. The question then becomes whether one can appoint a *shaliach shelo b'fanav* — in the absence of the appointee — or whether that appointment requires a direct, face-to-face charge. If the courier is not actually a *shaliach*, what is the mechanism by which Abba bar Minyumi's appointment takes effect? These questions, which the *Gemara* raises implicitly in its closing discussion, remain open.
