# Moed Katan 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Family Aveilus Unit

### Moneh Imahem

The daf opens at the bottom of 21b with a *baraisa* that articulates one of the most fundamental and powerful principles of *shiva* observance: *aveilus* is not merely an individual experience but a family one. The *tanna* teaches: a mourner who arrives from a nearby place within the first three days of *shiva* counts together with the family — *moneh imahem* — meaning his *shiva* does not begin from the moment he arrived or learned of the death, but integrates with the family's count from its original start. This is the concept of *moneh imahem*, counting together with them.

### Kariv vs. Rachok

The *baraisa* distinguishes between arriving from a nearby place (*kariv*) and arriving from a faraway place (*rachok*). One who comes from a nearby location within the first three days is considered, conceptually, to have been part of the family unit all along — as though the geographic proximity renders them together even if not physically present. The precise definition of "nearby" is given by *Tosafot* in the name of the *Ba'al Halakhot Gedolot* as ten *parsa'os*, which equals forty *mil* — roughly twenty-five miles — a distance a person can travel in a single day. If one comes from a faraway place, however, he counts on his own, *moneh le'atzmo*, even if he arrives within the first three days. After the first three days — *mikan ve'eilach* — even one arriving from a nearby place counts separately, since the window for joining the foundational period of *aveilus*, the *ikar*, has already closed.

### Gadol Habayis Role

The Gemara quotes Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, who adds an important qualification: this principle of *moneh imahem* applies only when the *gadol habayis* — the elder or dominant figure of the household — is present in the house. The *gadol habayis* is the person who serves as the central organizing figure of the family, often the eldest sibling, though the precise identification of this role in a given family is not always simple. When the *gadol habayis* is present, the entire family is considered a single observing unit, such that a relative joining from a nearby town within three days may integrate into their count. If the *gadol habayis* is absent — for example, if he is observing *shiva* in a different city — the sense of the family constituting a unified entity is disrupted, and each individual counts separately. This is not a value judgment; the Gemara is simply describing a structural reality.

### Gadol at Burial

A question is then raised: what if the *gadol habayis* accompanied the *meis* to the cemetery? The rest of the family turned back and began their *shiva* on a given day, while the *gadol habayis* only began when the burial was complete, perhaps two days later. Two different readings of the Gemara's question emerge. On one reading, the question concerns a relative coming from a nearby town to join the family: the *gadol habayis* is absent, but his absence is due to accompanying the *meis* to burial — is that absence sufficient to disrupt the family unit, or, since he is engaged in the very completion of the burial process and will presumably return, is the family still considered a unified whole? On another reading — proposed within the discussion — the question is about the *gadol habayis* himself: since he began *shiva* later than those who turned back earlier, what is the relationship between his count and theirs?

### Resolution: Three Days

The Gemara resolves the tension by citing Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: even if the *gadol habayis* went to the *beis hakevaros*, the person joining the family counts with them — *monei immahen*. On the simpler reading, this means that the *gadol habayis*'s absence for the purpose of completing the burial does not dissolve the family unit. A conflicting *baraisa*, however, states that in such a case the person counts separately — *moneh le'atzmo*. The Gemara resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between cases: *ha de'asa be-go telassa* — he arrived within the first three days — he counts with them; *ha de'lo asa be-go telassa* — he arrived after the first three days — he counts separately. This resolution returns the discussion to its foundational principle, with the *gadol habayis*'s absence resolved by the timing of the newcomer's arrival.

The Gemara then cites Rav's ruling directly: he told the people of a certain town that whoever comes within the first three days counts with them, and whoever does not come within the first three days counts separately.

## Start of Aveilus

### Rav's Ruling

Rav's instruction reinforces the core framework: arrival within the first three days permits *moneh imahem*; arrival thereafter requires *moneh le'atzmo*. This rule operates independently of the *gadol habayis* question, affirming that three days constitutes the *ikar aveilus*, the primary and foundational mourning period, within which integration into the family's count remains possible.

### Departure Point

Rava then introduces a related but distinct ruling for the people of Mechuza. He tells them: *atun de-lo azlitu basar arsa* — you who do not follow after the bier — *mikama de-hadrisu apaichu mi-baba de-avula*, from the moment you turn your faces from the gate of the city, that is when you begin to count. This is the operative *halacha*: when a *meis* is placed on a plane to Israel, for example, those who remain behind begin *shiva* from the moment they turn back from the point of departure — the city gate, the airport — and head home. Rashi explains *avula* as the city gate, *shaar ha'ir*.

### Aninus vs. Aveilus

This ruling surfaces a profound conceptual question. On the one hand, *aveilus* is beginning even though the *meis* has not yet been buried. On the other hand, the mourner's *aninus* — the pre-burial period of acute obligation to the deceased, during which a person is exempt from positive commandments and consumed entirely with burial arrangements — has ended at the moment of departure. The question is: what triggers *aveilus*? Is it the burial of the *meis*, or is it the cessation of *aninus*?

According to some *rishonim*, *aveilus* technically should begin at the moment of death; it is only the demands and psychological state of *aninus* that override and displace it. Once *aninus* is no longer present — once the mourner has taken leave of the *meis* and is no longer engaged with the burial — *aveilus* asserts itself automatically, even before the body is interred. This would explain the ruling of Rava perfectly. However, the picture is complicated by cases such as a person who is in a distant country and has no involvement whatsoever in the burial: that person would not begin *aveilus* until the *meis* is in the process of being buried. Evidently it is not purely about subjective experience — the status of the *meis* itself matters. The tension between these two frameworks — burial as the trigger versus the end of *aninus* as the trigger — is left as an open and fascinating question by the Gemara.

## Family Aveilus Unit…

### Yom Hashevi'i

Returning to the *baraisa* begun earlier: Rabbi Shimon rules that even one who arrives on the seventh day from a nearby place may count together with the family — *afilu ba beyom hashevi'i mimakom karov moneh imahem*. This is a significantly more expansive position than that of the *Tanna Kamma*, who requires arrival within the first three days. Rabbi Shimon's view reflects a stronger emphasis on the integrative character of family *aveilus*: what matters is the family unit, not the specific day of arrival. Even a few hours of shared observance on the seventh day constitutes genuine integration.

## Start of Aveilus…

### Menachamim Present

Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda quotes Rabbi Yose ben Shaul quoting *Rebbi*, who qualifies Rabbi Shimon's ruling: it applies only when the arriving family member finds *menachamim* — comforters — still present at the *shiva* house. The principle of *miktzas hayom kekulo*, that part of a day counts as the whole, effectively closes the seventh day of *shiva* relatively early. For one to integrate into the family's *shiva* on day seven, it must still be actively functioning as a *shiva* — visitors must still be arriving and sitting. If the comforters have already departed, the *shiva* has effectively concluded and there is nothing to join.

### Ninnaru Laamod

Rav Anan then poses an unresolved question to Rav Anan: *ninnaru laamod velo amadu mahu* — what if the *menachamim* have begun to stir and prepare to leave but have not yet actually risen? The family member from the distant city arrives at precisely this transitional moment. Is that still considered an active *shiva* into which one can integrate, or has the *shiva* functionally concluded the moment people began to fidget and signal their departure? The Gemara leaves this with *teiku* — unresolved.

## Scholarly Debate

### Transmission

The Gemara now engages a transmission chain that carries some ambiguity. The colleagues of Rabbi Abba bar Chiya learned from Rabbi Abba — and who were those colleagues? Rabbi Zeira. Alternatively: the colleague of Rabbi Zeira learned from Rabbi Zeira — and who was that colleague? Rabbi Abba, the son of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba. The direction of transmission is uncertain, but the teaching transmitted is clear: Rabbi Yochanan ruled that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel in matters of *treifos*, and that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon in matters of *aveilus*. The pairing is peculiar — one concerns ritual slaughter law, the other mourning practice — but both represent acceptance of a minority opinion, a *da'as yachid*, as the operative ruling. The *aveilus* ruling refers to what has already been discussed: that even arriving on day seven counts together with the family.

### Verification

One anonymous scholar declared his desire to hear this teaching directly from its author — *mipumah demarei* — to verify it firsthand. When he traveled up to *Eretz Yisrael* and found Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, he asked him pointedly: did you really say we rule like Rabban Gamliel in *treifos*? Rabbi Abba replied: I said *ein halacha* — we do **not** rule like Rabban Gamliel in *treifos*. The word of negation had been lost in transmission, and the scholar's journey confirmed the correction. It is a good thing he came to verify.

### Halacha Ki-Meikel

Regarding Rabbi Shimon's position on *aveilus*, the scholar was told: *pelugta* — it is a matter of dispute. The Gemara then reports the outcome: Rav Chisda rules that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon even regarding arrival on day seven; Rabbi Yochanan concurs. Rav Nachman, however, rules that we do not follow Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara's anonymous voice concludes: we do not rule like Rabban Gamliel in *treifos* — that would be a major leniency in *issur v'hetter* — but we do rule like Rabbi Shimon in *aveilus*, grounded in Shmuel's recurring principle throughout this *masechet*: *halacha k'divrei hameikel ba'avel*, the *halacha* follows the lenient position in matters of mourning.

### Rav Chisda vs. Nachman

The dispute between Rav Chisda and Rav Nachman on the question of day-seven arrival stands as the final articulation of the debate. Rav Chisda and Rabbi Yochanan hold that one may join the family even on the seventh day and count together with them; Rav Nachman holds that we do not rule this way. The Gemara's conclusion sides with the lenient position.

## Practices by Relation

### Speed of Burial

The Gemara now transitions to a series of distinctions between practices observed for all deceased relatives and those observed specifically for one's father and mother. The first concerns *mezachach mittato* — the speed of burial. For all other deceased, one who ensures swift burial is praised — *harei zeh meshubach*. Speed is a virtue when it comes to ensuring the *meis* does not lie unattended. For a father or mother, however, one who moves too quickly to burial is considered blameworthy — *harei zeh megunah*. The reasoning is not primarily about gathering more people, as one might initially think. Rather, it reflects the mourner's psychological readiness — or lack thereof — to transition to the next stage. The *halacha* expects a certain hesitation, a reluctance to let go, a refusal to move on prematurely when it comes to one's parents. An exception is carved out for *erev Shabbat* or *erev Yom Tov*: if delaying burial means the body will lie unattended for a day or more, then prompt burial before *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* is praiseworthy even for a parent, *she'eino oseh ela likhvod aviv ve'imo* — because in that case it is clearly being done for the honor of the father and mother.

### Business Dealings

The Gemara then addresses *isko* — business engagements. For all other deceased, an *avel* who wishes to limit his business dealings may do so, and one who does not wish to limit them need not. This raises an interesting conceptual question: is there a halachic category of commerce that is distinct from *melakha*? Several *rishonim* approach this differently. Rabbeinu Gershom reads *isko* as commerce, *sekhora*. Others argue that the passage is not carving out a separate category of commerce versus labor at all; rather, it is speaking about cases where *melakha* would otherwise be permitted — *davar ha'aved*, or *betzinatah betokh beito* after the first three days — and telling us that when such allowances exist, the mourner for other relatives has discretion whether to avail himself of them. For a father or mother, however — *al aviv ve'al imo mima'et* — even where allowances exist, one should seek to limit all such activities. The question of whether there is a fundamental halachic distinction between commerce and *melakha* in the context of *aveilus* remains an open and richly debated one among the *rishonim*.

### Chalitza

For all other deceased, uncovering the shoulder — *chalitza* — is optional: *ratza choletz ratza eino choletz*. For a father or mother, one is required to do so: *al aviv ve'al imo choletz*. This is distinct from *neilat hasandal*, the removal of shoes, which is a universal *aveilus* practice applying to all mourners and is never referred to as *chalitza*. The *chalitza* discussed here is clearly the exposure of the shoulder, not the removal of footwear. The context makes this plain.

### Gadol Habayis Story

There was a story about one great leader of the generation — a *gadol hador* — whose father died. He wished to perform *chalitza*, to expose his shoulder in mourning. A second *gadol hador* who was with him prepared to do the same, out of deference and honor to the first. The *avel* felt that while for himself — even as a *gadol hador* — the personal expression of grief through *chalitza* was appropriate, allowing this second public figure to perform *chalitza* as well, when the second figure was not himself an *avel*, would be unseemly for that person's standing and role. And so the *avel* refrained from performing *chalitza* himself so that the other would not follow suit — *venimna velo chalatz*.

Abaye identifies the figures: the *gadol hador* who was the *avel* was *Rebbi*, Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi, and the *gadol hador* who was with him was Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha — a figure whose contemporaneity with *Rebbi* one might not have assumed. An alternate tradition reverses the identification: it was Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha who was the *avel*, and *Rebbi* who was with him.

The Gemara analyzes which version is coherent. According to the second version — where *Rebbi* was the *avel* and Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha was the colleague — it is immediately understandable why *Rebbi* chose not to perform *chalitza*: he was the *gadol hador* and did not want to place Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha in the awkward position of performing *chalitza* alongside him when the latter was not an *avel*. But the first version is more difficult: if Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha was the *avel* and *Rebbi* was the colleague, why did Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha refrain? The answer is that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — *Rebbi*'s own father — had died. The *nasi* of the generation had passed away, and accordingly the entire community was obligated to perform *chalitza* out of honor for the *nasi*. Rabbi Yaakov bar Acha was not going to join *Rebbi* in *chalitza* merely as an act of personal solidarity; he was going to do so because everyone was obligated. That creates a difficulty: *kashya*. The Gemara raises the question and moves on.

This story also introduces the related principle that the *nasi* occupies a unique category — his death may generate communal obligations similar in some respects to those of *aveilus* for *aviv ve'imo*, a thread the Gemara will develop shortly.

### Haircut Timing

For all other deceased, a mourner may have a haircut after thirty days — *mistaper le'achar sheloshim yom*. The precise trigger, however, is characteristically impressionistic: not a formal calendrical marker but *ad she'yig'aru bo chaverav* — until his friends rebuke him for his disheveled appearance. When his social circle tells him he looks unsuitable to be in their company, that is the sign that sufficient time and appropriate mourning has been demonstrated. It is a striking criterion — not an authoritative ruling but a social signal.

*Tosafot* notes that this particular rule is not subject to interruption by a *regel*. The principle of *regel* interrupting *shiva* and *shloshim* applies to time-based periods, but the haircut prohibition is not primarily time-based — it is an appearance-based standard. Whether or not a *regel* has intervened, the criterion remains: does the mourner look sufficiently disheveled that his friends would object? The *Yerushalmi* confirms this: even if thirty days were counted before *Yom Tov*, the haircut prohibition persists until the social criterion is met, because *kol davar she'hu talui be'shiva ve'shloshim ha'regel mafsik bahem*, but this standard depends on appearance, not on the time period.

### Simcha Timing

For all other deceased, a mourner may enter a joyous gathering — *beit ha'simcha* — after thirty days. For a father or mother — *al aviv ve'al imo le'achar yud beis chodesh* — the restriction extends to twelve months. This is among the most prominent and well-known distinctions in mourning for parents: the prohibition on attending joyous occasions for a full year. The specific parameters of what constitutes such an occasion — whether music is involved, whether it is a *simchat mitzvah*, how to categorize a social gathering with some festive character — are extensively debated among the *rishonim* and are left relatively open by the Gemara itself.

## Simcha Categories

### Merayot Definition

Rabbah bar bar Chana introduces a distinction. When the Gemara allows a mourner for other relatives to enter a *beit ha'mishteh* after thirty days, that ruling applies specifically to *simchas mere'ut* — a gathering of friends, a social occasion — but not necessarily to a full, major *simcha* such as a wedding. The underlying principle is that *simchas mere'ut* is a lesser category: convivial, friendly, joyous in a modest sense, but not a grand public celebration. Major *simcha* — a *nisuin*, for example — would carry a stricter standard even for an *avel* whose *shiva* was for other relatives.

### Equal Duration

A *baraisa* challenges this: *ul'simcha ul'merayeos shloshim yom* — both *simcha* and *merayeos* are forbidden for thirty days equally. There is no distinction; after thirty days, both are permitted. This directly contradicts Rabbah bar bar Chana's position that even after thirty days a major *simcha* remains problematic. The Gemara flags this as difficult — *kasha*.

### Arisusa vs. Puranusa

Ameimar then transmits a more lenient version of Rabbah bar bar Chana's teaching: *simchas mere'ut* is permitted immediately — *le'altar* — presumably after *shiva*, without waiting thirty days. Only full *simcha* is restricted to thirty days. The *baraisa* that equated both at thirty days is then resolved by the Gemara: *ha b'arisusa ha b'puranusa* — the distinction concerns initiative versus reciprocation. In the practice of *simchas mere'ut*, groups of friends would take turns hosting gatherings. If one friend has hosted and the others are now obligated to reciprocate, the obligation to repay the social debt applies even within the thirty-day period. The prohibition applies only when the mourner would be initiating a new gathering — *arisusa*; returning an existing obligation — *puranusa* — is permitted sooner.

### Practical Synthesis

In practice, the distinction between *simcha* and *simchas mere'ut* tends to be conflated. We generally treat both as equally forbidden during *shloshim* for other relatives and equally forbidden for twelve months for a father or mother — except for the specific case of reciprocation. This explains why, in practice, the question people ask when navigating the twelve-month restriction for parents is not "what is larger than *simchas mere'ut*" but rather "what qualifies as *simchas mere'ut*" — meaning, what has a sufficiently festive character to be restricted at all. A purely social gathering without a joyous dimension would theoretically be permitted; it is the combination of the social and the festive that the Gemara is concerned with, and the precise threshold remains a matter of significant debate among the *poskim*.

## Keriah Practice

### Tear Extent

For all other deceased, the mourner tears a *tefach* — approximately a hand's breadth, three to four inches. For a father or mother — *al aviv ve'al imo ad sheyigaleh libo* — one must tear until the heart is exposed. The symbolic weight of this distinction is evident. Rabbi Abbahu provides the scriptural basis: *vayechezek David bivgadav vayikra'eim* — David grasped his garments and rent them. Since *achiza*, grasping, involves the hand, and the hand is a *tefach* in measure, the *tefach* dimension for other relatives is derived from this verse. For parents, the tearing must go further, to expose the heart.

### Multiple Garments

For all other deceased, even if a person is wearing ten garments, only the outermost is torn — *eino koreia ela elyon*. For a father or mother, all garments must be torn — *koreia et kulam* — again oriented toward the exposure of the heart. The exception noted is the *afikarsut* — understood by most *rishonim* as an undergarment that serves to absorb perspiration rather than functioning as a proper garment. Even for parents, such an undergarment need not be torn, though this sits in some tension with the phrase *ad sheyigaleh libo*.

The Gemara notes that there is no distinction between men and women in this regard — *echad ha'ish ve'echad ha'isha*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, however, adds a practical consideration for women: in the case of parents, where all garments must be torn, the woman should tear the lower inner garment first, then reverse it so the tear faces her back, and then tear the upper garment — *kora'at et ha'elyon*. In this way, both the outer and inner garments are torn as required, but the woman's chest remains covered.

### Mavdil Definition

For all other deceased, a mourner may optionally tear opposite the hem — *mavdil* — but is not required to do so. For a father or mother, one must tear from the hem, effectively dividing the garment into two halves — *al aviv ve'al imo mavdil*.

### Yehuda's Position

Rabbi Yehudah rejects the entire distinction between relatives. He maintains that any *keriah* that does not render the garment *mavdil* — divided from the hem into two visible halves — is nothing but frivolous tearing: *kriah shel tiflut*. Rabbi Abba explains Rabbi Yehudah's reasoning from the same verse: *vayikra'em lishnayim kera'im* — David rent them into two tears. The word "two" is superfluous — of course if you tear something there are two pieces. The verse must therefore be teaching that the *keriah* should *look* like two halves. A tear in the middle of a garment does not produce this appearance; only tearing from the hem produces the visual impression of a garment divided in two.

### Repair Timeline

For all other deceased: one may loosely pin together the torn garment — *sholel* — after seven days, and may fully sew it — *me'acheh* — after thirty days. For a father or mother: one may not even loosely pin together the tear until after thirty days — *sholel achar shloshim* — and may never fully sew it — *ve'eino me'acheh le'olam*. This is the most enduring of the physical distinctions: the garment torn for a parent is never restored to its prior state.

### Women's Keriah

An exception is made for women immediately: *veha'isha sholelet le'altar mipnei kevoda* — a woman may immediately repin the torn garment for reasons of modesty and personal dignity. This mirrors the parallel consideration for women addressed earlier in the tearing procedure itself.

### Hand Tearing

When *Ravin* came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: for all other deceased, a mourner may perform *keriah* either by hand or with an instrument — scissors, for example, to get the tear started. For a father or mother, it must be done by hand — *al aviv ve'al imo beyad*. Tearing by hand conveys immediacy, intensity, and a raw personal expression of grief; reaching for an instrument distances the act from that immediacy. In practice, where modern fabrics make hand-tearing extremely difficult, a small starting cut may be made, but the act of *keriah* itself is understood to carry this expressive dimension.

### Mipnim vs. Mibachutz

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba further quotes Rabbi Yochanan: for all other deceased, the *keriah* is performed *mipnim* — inside; for a father or mother, it is performed *mibachutz* — outside. The Ramban explains that this does not refer to a private versus public setting, noting that *mipnim* and *mibachutz* would be the wrong formulation for that. Rather, it refers to whether the hands are placed on the inside of the garment while tearing outward, or on the outside. Tearing from the outside is more visible, more demonstrative, more public in its expression of grief.

## Nasi Keriah

### Nasi Comparison

Rav Chisda rules: *ve'chen l'nasi* — the same applies to the *nasi*, the national leader. The *keriah* practices performed for a father or mother apply similarly for the *nasi*. The Gemara challenges this from a *baraisa* that states the comparison between a *nasi* and *aviv ve'imo* was only in the *halacha* of sewing — *l'ichuy bilvad* — and not in other aspects of *keriah*. The Gemara resolves this: the limitation applies to other figures who might be compared partially to *aviv ve'imo* in some respects; the *nasi* is a fuller comparison, and even the manner of tearing would mirror that of a father or mother.

### Public Keriah

When a *nasi* died, Rav Chisda instructed Rav Nachman bar Ami: overturn a mortar, stand on it, and demonstrate *keriah* to the community. The *nasi*'s death requires that the communal obligation to perform *keriah* be made visible. A prominent figure stands on a raised surface and performs *keriah* publicly, both showing the community how to do so and signaling that they are required to do so.

### Shoulder Location

For a *chacham* — a sage — one exposes the right shoulder. For the *av beit din* — the head of the court, a more senior position — one exposes the left shoulder. For the *nasi* — both shoulders, left and right. The apparent counterintuitiveness of the left shoulder for the *av beit din* may relate to the left side being over the heart, lending it a particular quality of exposure, though the Gemara leaves this without explicit explanation.

## Communal Mourning

### Study Hall Closure

The Gemara now describes graduated communal responses to the deaths of different categories of leadership. When a *chacham* dies, his own *yeshiva* ceases — *beit midrasho batel*. When the *av beit din* of a city dies, all the *batei midrashot* of that city cease — *kol batei midrashot shebeiro betelim*.

### National Closure

When the *nasi* dies, all the *batei midrashot* throughout the country cease — *batei midrashot kulam betelim*. The people then gather into the *beit haknesset*, and seven *aliyot* are read from the Torah.

### Torah Reading

The communal gathering centers on *kriat haTorah*: seven *aliyot* are read, and then the people disperse. This gathering is focused on Torah reading, not on the full communal *tefillah*.

### Private Davening

Rabbeinu Gershom explains why *tefillah* is not conducted communally: since the entire community is in a state of *aveilus*, gathering for a full communal *davening* would constitute a collective experience that is inconsistent with the individual character of mourning. Each person therefore davens privately at home. The community gathers specifically for *kriat haTorah* — a communal act of Torah engagement — while retaining the separateness that *aveilus* demands.

### Public Silence

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha adds: people should not go out and stroll through the marketplace — *lo sheyeilchu veyitaylu bashuk*. Rather, they should sit and be silent — *yoshvin vedomin*. The dynamic is a striking and carefully calibrated balance. The community is in collective *aveilus* — analogous in some ways to *Tisha B'Av* — and must demonstrate that mourning publicly. Yet *aveilus* by its nature is also about separateness from communal life. The resolution: people gather in the *beit haknesset* for Torah reading, but not for communal *tefillah*; they occupy the space together, but each sits in a different place from usual; they are present with one another, but sit in silence rather than conducting business or socializing. It is a community of mourners performing *aveilus* together while preserving, to the degree possible, the individual and inward character that genuine mourning requires.
