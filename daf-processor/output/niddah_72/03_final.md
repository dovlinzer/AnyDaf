# Niddah 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Day 11 Bleeding

### 11-Day Period Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הָרוֹאָה יוֹם אַחַד עָשָׂר, וְטָבְלָה לָעֶרֶב, וְשִׁמְּשָׁה —
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a dispute with regard to a woman **who sees** blood on the **eleventh day,** which is the final day of the period fit for the flow of a *zava*. It is permitted for her to engage in intercourse with her husband after observing one clean day corresponding to the one day that she saw blood, but in this case, she failed to observe one clean day. Rather, **she immersed** in a ritual bath that **evening,** the eve of the twelfth day, **and** then **engaged in intercourse** with her husband.

The final *mishnah* of *Maseches Niddah* — and indeed of the entire *Shas* — addresses one last aspect of the basic structure of *hilchos niddah*: a woman who sees blood on day 11 of the *zavah* period. To understand the *mishnah*'s debate, one must first recall the underlying framework. A woman's menstrual period encompasses the first seven days, during which she is a *niddah* biblically. The following eleven days — days 8 through 18 from the onset of menstruation — constitute the *zavah* period. If she bleeds for three consecutive days during this period, she becomes a *zavah gedolah* and requires seven clean days. If she bleeds for only one or two days, she is a *zavah ketanah* and requires one clean day before immersing.

### Bleeding Day 11 Scenario

The operative question concerns a woman who bleeds for the first time on day 11 of the *zavah* period — the very last day on which *zavah* bleeding is relevant. Normally, a *zavah ketanah* who bleeds on, say, day 5, must wait through day 6 as a clean day before immersing. But when the bleeding occurs on day 11, the following day — day 12 — already falls within the *niddah* period. Any bleeding she has on day 12 will not continue or compound her *zavah* status; it will simply render her a *niddah* under a new and independent status. Given this, one might reasonably ask: does she still need to wait into the morning of day 12 before immersing, when that following day cannot in any case extend or reinforce her *zavah* status?

### Beis Shammai v. Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: מְטַמְּאִין מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: פְּטוּרִים מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Shammai say:** Since she did not observe that corresponding clean day, she retains the status of a *zava*. Therefore both she, the *zava*, and her husband, who engaged in intercourse with a *zava*, **transmit impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting,** to the extent that these transmit impurity to food and drink that came in contact with them, and in her case, to people as well. **And** each of them is **liable** to bring a sin offering for participating in intercourse involving a *zava*. **And Beit Hillel say:** Although they transmit impurity to items designated for lying or sitting, **they are exempt from** bringing **the** sin **offering.** Since the twelfth day is unfit for the flow of a *zava*, and even if she were to experience bleeding on the eleventh, twelfth, and thirteenth days she would not become a greater *zava*, one who experiences bleeding on the eleventh does not need to observe a corresponding clean day.

This is precisely the question that divides *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel*. *Beis Shammai* maintains that even on day 11 one must wait for a portion of day 12 before immersing. *Beis Hillel* disagrees, holding that since day 12 cannot connect to her *zavah* status in any way, the requirement to wait into the next morning does not apply. The *mishnah* proceeds to lay out the halachic consequences of each position across two distinct scenarios.

## Mishnah Scenarios

### Night Immersion Case

The first scenario: a woman sees blood on day 11, then immerses that very night — without waiting for any part of day 12 — and subsequently has marital relations.

### Shammai Strict View

*Beis Shammai* holds that in this case she retains full biblical *zavah* status. The immersion was ineffective because it occurred before even a portion of day 12 had passed. Consequently, *metamei mishkav u-moshav* — the objects she and her husband sat or lay upon are rendered *tamei* — and both she and her husband are obligated to bring a *korban* for having had relations while she remained a *zavah* biblically.

### Hillel Lenient View

*Beis Hillel* disagrees on the biblical level. Since day 12 cannot join with day 11 to create or extend *zavah* status, there is no biblical requirement to wait into day 12 at all. Immersing on the night following day 11 is therefore biblically valid, and neither party bears an obligation to bring a *korban*. However, *Beis Hillel* does not declare them fully *tahor*. This will be spelled out explicitly in the *baraisa*: rabbinically, *Beis Hillel* requires that she wait until the morning of day 12 before immersing, so that day 11 not be confused with the middle days of the *zavah* period, where such waiting is genuinely required. On the rabbinic level, therefore, her *tumah* stands — she may not handle *terumah* and so forth — but biblically she is exempt from a *korban*.

### Day 12 Immersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָבְלָה בַּיּוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו, וְשִׁמְּשָׁה אֶת בֵּיתָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאֲתָה — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְטַמְּאִין מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וּפְטוּרִין מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** If the woman **immersed on the day following** the eleventh day **and she engaged in intercourse with** the man of **her house,** i.e., her husband, on that twelfth day **and then saw** blood, **Beit Shammai say: They transmit impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting** by rabbinic law, as the Sages issued a decree of impurity in the case when the second day is the twelfth day, due to a case when the second day is within the eleven days fit for the flow of a *zava*. **And they are exempt from** bringing **the** sin **offering,** as she observed part of the twelfth day, and the bleeding she experienced after engaging in intercourse, which occurred during her period of menstruation, is not fit to be appended to the discharge of the *zava* on the eleventh day.

The second scenario presented by the *mishnah* addresses a case where the woman does wait, immerses on the morning of day 12 in the manner that everyone should agree is correct, has relations, and then subsequently bleeds later that same day. Here, unlike a scenario during the middle days of the *zavah* period, the subsequent bleeding does not retroactively continue her *zavah* status — because day 12 is already the *niddah* period, and bleeding on that day initiates a wholly new status as a *niddah*, not an extension of the *zavah* process. The question then becomes: what is the halachic status of the period between her immersion and the later bleeding?

One might pause here over the language of the *mishnah*, which uses the word *beisah* to describe the act of marital relations. The *gemara* later uses *shimsha* and *meshamsha*; the word *bayis* applied to the woman reflects the well-known convention by which the woman's interior is compared to a house, with its various chambers — *prosdor*, *cheder*, *aliyah* — serving as anatomical metaphors throughout *hilchos niddah*. The *shamash*, by contrast, is a conventional term for the male organ. The imagery throughout positions the woman as the house being entered, which accounts for the locution.

### Post-Immersion Bleeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: הֲרֵי זֶה גַּרְגְּרָן, וּמוֹדִים בְּרוֹאָה בְּתוֹךְ אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם, וְטָבְלָה לָעֶרֶב וְשִׁמְּשָׁה — שֶׁמְּטַמְּאִין מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: That** husband **is a glutton,** as he could not wait for the conclusion of the twelfth day before engaging in intercourse. Nevertheless, the Sages did not issue a decree of impurity. **And** Beit Hillel **concede** to Beit Shammai **in** a case where the woman **sees** blood **in the midst of the eleven-day** period, **and she immersed in the evening and engaged in intercourse** with her husband without observing a corresponding clean day, **that they transmit impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting. And** each of them is **liable to** bring a sin **offering** for participating in intercourse involving a *zava*.

In this second scenario, where she immersed on the morning of day 12 and then bled later that same day, the subsequent bleeding is categorically a *niddah* status — it does not retroactively undermine the immersion, and the period between immersion and bleeding is not biblically overturned. Both *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* agree on this much.

### Shammai Stringency Case

*Beis Shammai*, in a position that at first appears surprising, concedes that in this second scenario there is no biblical *korban* obligation. Since the bleeding on day 12 creates an entirely new status and does not connect back to her *zavah* period, the act of relations that took place after her valid immersion is not retroactively rendered a biblical violation. Nevertheless, *Beis Shammai* applies a rabbinic stringency: he treats that intermediate period as *metamei mishkav u-moshav* rabbinically, out of concern that one will confuse this scenario — where she immersed on day 12 and then bled later — with the analogous scenario on days 5 and 6, where a subsequent bleeding during the day would retroactively undermine the immersion and render the entire intermediate period biblically *tamei*.

### Hillel Gagging

*Beis Hillel*'s response to this second scenario is notably different in tone. Rather than engaging in a technical halachic analysis, *Beis Hillel* declares: *harei zu gargeran* — this woman is a *gargeran*, one who is being reckless or asking for trouble. What makes her behavior problematic? She went to the *mikvah* on the morning of day 12 and then engaged in relations before the day was complete. Technically, as *Beis Hillel* himself would agree, she was *tehorah* in the moment; if she then bled later on day 12, it creates a new *niddah* status but does not retroactively undermine anything. In that sense, nothing has actually gone wrong — there is no *korban*, no retroactive *tumah*. But *Beis Hillel* finds the behavior itself problematic because it is precisely the kind of practice that, if adopted as a pattern, will lead to similar behavior in the genuinely risky analogous case. If one routinely goes to the *mikvah* on the morning of a clean day and acts immediately without waiting for nightfall, one will do the same on day 5 and 6 during the *zavah* period. On day 5 and 6, if she has relations after her morning immersion and then bleeds later that day, the immersion is retroactively undermined, the relations were retroactively forbidden, and the situation is genuinely serious. *Beis Hillel*'s language — *gargeran*, asking for trouble — is notably softer than *tarbus ra'ah*, as if to reflect that the woman herself has not actually done anything wrong in this particular instance, but the practice leads in a dangerous direction. Indeed, there is a discussion elsewhere in the *gemara* suggesting that the implication of *gargeran* amounts to a prohibition, though this is not entirely clear from the *mishnah* alone.

### Agreement Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָבְלָה בַּיּוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו, וְשִׁמְּשָׁה — הֲרֵי זֶה תַּרְבּוּת רָעָה, וּמַגָּעָן וּבְעִילָתָן תְּלוּיִין.
>
> **Translation:** If she saw blood in the midst of the eleven days and observed part of a corresponding clean day and **immersed on the day following** the day that she saw blood **and engaged in intercourse** with her husband, **that is wayward conduct,** as the possibility exists that she will experience bleeding after intercourse that will be appended to the bleeding of the previous day, rendering her a *zava* and disqualifying the immersion. **And** the status of ritually impure items with which **they came into contact and** the status of **their intercourse is contingent** upon whether she experiences bleeding on the day of her immersion, in which case the ritually pure items become impure and they are liable to bring a sin offering, or whether she does not experience bleeding that day, in which case the ritually pure items remain pure and the woman and man are exempt from bringing a sin offering.

The *mishnah* closes by noting the areas of agreement. Both *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* concur on the following: if a woman bleeds during the middle of the eleven days — the standard case of, say, day 5 — and immerses that same night without waiting at all for day 6, it is universally agreed that the immersion is ineffective. She is still *tamei* biblically, *metamei mishkav u-moshav*, and both she and her husband are obligated in a *korban* if they had relations. This case is not in dispute.

They also agree on a further case: if she waited until the beginning of day 6 and immersed on the morning of day 6, and then had relations — that immersion is technically valid in that moment, she is *tehorah* right now. But this is described as *tarbus ra'ah*, bad behavior, and anything she touched or any relations she had are *tluyim* — their status is held in abeyance, dependent on whether she bleeds later that day. The concept at work here is *miktzat ha-yom ke-kulo*: in principle one requires a full clean day, but the rabbis allow the beginning of the day to serve in place of the whole day. Because the permission to act is grounded in the principle that part of the day counts as the whole, if later in the day that principle is undermined by actual bleeding, everything retroactively loses its validity. The result is that while technically she may immerse in the morning, any subsequent action that day — touching *terumah*, marital relations — is potentially a severe violation if she bleeds later, and is therefore forbidden at least rabbinically if not more seriously. One cannot act on a status that may be retroactively undone.

## Baraisa Framework

### Baraisa Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: וְשָׁוִין בִּטְבִילַת לַיְלָה לְזָבָה — שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְבִילָה, וְשָׁוִין בְּרוֹאָה בְּתוֹךְ אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם וְטָבְלָה לָעֶרֶב וְשִׁמְּשָׁה — שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **And** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **agree with regard to** a woman who **immersed** at **night to** purify herself after having been a lesser ***zava*, that it is not** a valid **immersion. And** Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel also **agree with regard to** a woman **who sees** blood **in the midst of** her **eleven days** of *ziva*, rendering her a lesser *zava* who must wait one clean day and immerse the day after, **but** she **immersed in the** first **evening** without waiting one clean day **and engaged in intercourse** with her husband, **that she transmits impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting** that came in contact with the couple, **and** she and her husband **are liable to** bring a sin **offering.**

The *gemara* now introduces a *baraisa* that presents the same material in a more systematic and pedagogically accessible order. Where the *mishnah* characteristically begins in the middle of the conversation — with the contested case of day 11 — the *baraisa* builds up from first principles. It opens by noting: a *zavah* who immerses at night — that is, she bled on day 5 and immerses on the night leading into day 6 without waiting for any part of the day — that immersion is simply invalid (*einah tevilah*). One needs at least a portion of the clean day itself. This is the baseline on which all parties agree.

### 11-Day Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּיוֹם אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְטַמְּאִין מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן, וּבֵית הִלֵּל פּוֹטְרִין מִקׇּרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree only in** the case of a woman who sees blood on the **eleventh day** of her days of *ziva*, and immersed that evening, and engaged in intercourse with her husband. **As Beit Shammai say: They transmit impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting and are liable to** bring a sin **offering, and Beit Hillel deem** them **exempt from** bringing a sin **offering.**

The *baraisa* then restates the point of full consensus: *veshovin bero'ah betoch achad asar yom vetavlah la'erev veshamshah* — if a woman bled during the eleven days, say on day 5, went to the *mikvah* that night without waiting, and had relations, both *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* agree that she is fully *tamei* biblically, *metamei mishkav u-moshav*, and both she and her husband are obligated in a *korban*. The *baraisa* then specifies that the dispute between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* is confined to *yom achad asar* — day 11 specifically, the last of the eleven days, after which the following day is already *yom niddah*. *Beis Shammai* says she must still wait for a portion of day 12, and without it she is fully *tamei* biblically and obligated in a *korban*. *Beis Hillel* exempts her from a *korban* and considers her biblically valid, while maintaining a rabbinic *tumah* to prevent confusion with the middle days.

### Shammai Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶן בֵּית שַׁמַּאי לְבֵית הִלֵּל: מַאי שְׁנָא יוֹם אַחַד עָשָׂר מִיּוֹם תּוֹךְ אַחַד עָשָׂר? אִם שָׁיוָה לוֹ לַטּוּמְאָה, לֹא יִשְׁוֶה לוֹ לַקׇּרְבָּן?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Beit Shammai said to Beit Hillel: What the difference** between the **eleventh day** itself and another **day in the midst of** the **eleven** days of *ziva*? **If you equate** the eleventh day to the other days **with regard to ritual impurity,** will you **not equate it with regard to** liability to bring **an offering?**

Having laid out the positions, the *baraisa* presents the exchange between the schools. *Beis Shammai* challenges *Beis Hillel*: *mah shani yom achad asar mitoch achad asar yom* — what distinguishes day 11 from the middle days? You yourself agree, says *Beis Shammai*, that if she immerses on the night following day 11, she is still *tamei* — at least for purposes other than *korban*. How can you treat her as *tamei* for those purposes and yet exempt her from a *korban*? If the immersion was insufficient to render her *tehorah* for *tumah* purposes, it should equally be insufficient to exempt her from a *korban*; and if the immersion suffices to exempt her from a *korban*, it should equally suffice to render her *tehorah* entirely. You cannot, says *Beis Shammai*, have it both ways.

### Hillel Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶן בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּתוֹךְ אַחַד עָשָׂר יוֹם, שֶׁכֵּן יוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו מִצְטָרֵף עִמּוֹ לְזִיבָה, תֹּאמְרוּ בְּיוֹם אַחַד עָשָׂר, שֶׁאֵין יוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו מִצְטָרֵף עִמּוֹ לְזִיבָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: No, if you say** that she is liable to bring an offering if she experiences bleeding **in the midst of the eleven days,** that is **because the following day combines with it** as a day **of *ziva*. Will you say** the same **with regard to** the **eleventh day, when the following day does not combine with it** as a day **of *ziva*?**

*Beis Hillel* replies: the cases are not parallel. During the middle days of the *zavah* period — on days 5 and 6, for instance — the following day *does* connect to her *zavah* status. If she bled on day 6 as well, that would extend or compound her *zavah* status; if she bled for three days in a row it would make her a *zavah gedolah*. Therefore, I require a portion of the next day to be part of the clean period, because the next day genuinely belongs to the same halachic continuum. On day 11, by contrast, the following day — day 12 — cannot in any way join with day 11 to constitute *zavah* status. Biblically, there is simply no requirement to wait into a day that is halachically irrelevant to her *zavah*. I apply a rabbinic *tumah* out of concern for confusion, but I cannot apply a biblical *korban* obligation where there is no biblical violation.

### Hillel Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל: אִם הֲבֵיאנוּהוּ לִידֵי טוּמְאָה לְהַחֲמִיר, לֹא נְבִיאֵהוּ לִידֵי קׇרְבָּן לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Beit Hillel said to** Beit Shammai: Even **if we have brought** a woman who experiences bleeding on the eleventh day and the man who engages in intercourse with her **to ritual impurity,** due to a decree **to be stringent,** so that people should not confuse one who experiences bleeding on the eleventh day with one who experiences bleeding during the other days with regard to ritual impurity, **we will not bring** them **to** the liability of bringing **an offering, to be lenient.** It would be a leniency to permit them to bring an unnecessary and therefore a non-sacred animal into the Temple courtyard.

*Beis Hillel* then turns the challenge back on *Beis Shammai*: *ve-od midivreichem atem noshchim* — you are bitten by your own words. In the very next case of the *mishnah*, you yourself, *Beis Shammai*, say that when she immersed on the morning of day 12, had relations, and then bled later that day, she is *metamei mishkav u-moshav* but *patur min ha-korban* — not biblically obligated in a *korban*. That is precisely the split between *tumah* and *korban* that you accused me of making inconsistently. You acknowledge that one can apply a rabbinic *tumah* status while exempting from a *korban*, because making her bring a *korban* when she is not biblically obligated would mean introducing non-sacred animals into the *Beis HaMikdash* as purported *korbanos* — which is its own violation. *Beis Hillel* says: *hachi nami* — I say the same about the night immersion following day 11. We can be strict rabbinically without being lenient by imposing a *korban* that has no biblical basis. *Lehachmir velo lehakeil* — to be stringent where warranted, without introducing leniency disguised as stringency. This is a satisfying response, and it exposes that *Beis Shammai*'s challenge, while logically crisp on its own terms, fails to account for the rabbinic dimension that both schools in fact employ.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד מִדִּבְרֵיכֶם אַתֶּם נוֹשְׁכִין, שֶׁאַתֶּם אוֹמְרִין: טָבְלָה יוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו, וְשִׁמְּשָׁה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאֲתָה — מְטַמֵּא מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן. אַף אַתֶּם הַשְׁווּ מִדּוֹתֵיכֶם: אִם שָׁיוָה לוֹ לַטּוּמְאָה, יִשְׁוֶה לוֹ לַקׇּרְבָּן. וְאִם לֹא שָׁיוָה לוֹ לְקׇרְבָּן, לֹא יִשְׁוֶה לוֹ לַטּוּמְאָה! אֶלָּא לְהַחְמִיר וְלֹא לְהָקֵל; הָכָא נָמֵי — לְהַחְמִיר וְלֹא לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, from your** own **statement you are refuted, as you say** that if **she immersed on the following day and engaged in intercourse, and afterward she saw** blood, **she transmits impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting, but she is exempt from** bringing **the offering. You too** should **apply your method equally. If you equate** a *zava* who immersed on the twelfth day with one who immersed on the night after the eleventh day **with regard to ritual impurity, equate** these cases also **with regard to** liability to bring **an offering. And if you do not equate** them **with regard to** liability to bring **an offering, do not equate** them **with regard to ritual impurity** either. Beit Hillel continued: **Rather,** you agree with us that the two are equated **to be stringent, but not to be lenient.** We say that **here too,** the two should be equated **to be stringent but not to be lenient.**

## Rav Huna Proposal

### Rav Huna Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִשְׁכָּבָהּ וּמוֹשָׁבָהּ שֶׁבַּשֵּׁנִי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מְטַמְּאִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁטָּבְלָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא רָאֲתָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ חָזְיָא מְטַמְּאָה, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי מְטַמְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says,** in further clarification of Beit Shammai's opinion: If a woman experiences bleeding during the eleven days of *ziva* and must observe one clean day, but did not wait until the conclusion of that second day before immersing, with regard to **her** items designated for **lying or sitting, on the second** day, **Beit Shammai render** them **impure** by rabbinic law. This is the *halakha* **even though she has immersed** in a ritual bath and **even though she did not see** any blood after her immersion. **What is the reason? Since if she would see** blood **she** would **render** these surfaces **ritually impure** by Torah law, **now too she renders** them **ritually impure** by rabbinic law.

The *gemara* now introduces a claim by Rav Huna that extends *Beis Shammai*'s stringency well beyond what the *mishnah* had explicitly stated. Rav Huna asserts: *mishkavah u-moshavah she-bashini, Beis Shammai metam'im af al pi she-tavlah, af al pi she-lo ra'ata* — the objects she sat upon on the second day, even though she has gone to the *mikvah* and even though she has not in fact bled at all, *Beis Shammai* would consider *tamei*. In other words, Rav Huna is claiming that if a woman bled on day 5, immersed on the morning of day 6, and then did not bleed at all for the remainder of day 6, *Beis Shammai* would nonetheless treat everything she touched or sat upon that day as definitively *tamei* — not merely held in abeyance pending possible later bleeding, but outright *tamei* regardless of outcome. This is a striking claim, and the *gemara* will scrutinize it closely.

### Yosef Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: טָבְלָה יוֹם שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו, וְשִׁמְּשָׁה אֶת בֵּיתָהּ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ רָאֲתָה — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מְטַמְּאָה מִשְׁכָּבוֹת וּמוֹשָׁבוֹת, וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: What is** Rav Huna **teaching us? We** already **learn** in the mishna: If the woman **immersed on the day following** the eleventh day **and she engaged in intercourse with** the man of **her house** on that twelfth day **and then saw** blood, **Beit Shammai say: They transmit impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting** by rabbinic law. **And they are exempt from** bringing **the** sin **offering.** Her impurity applies by rabbinic law, lest this case be confused with one where she experiences bleeding during the eleven days when she would be impure by Torah law. By inference, if she experienced bleeding on one of her eleven days and immersed on the following day, she would likewise render items designated for lying or sitting ritually impure by rabbinic law.

Rav Yosef challenges Rav Huna immediately: *mai ka mashma lan? tenina* — what is this teaching us? We can learn the same thing directly from the *mishnah* itself. Rav Yosef reasons as follows: in the *mishnah*'s second scenario — where she immersed on the morning of day 12, had relations, and then bled later that day — *Beis Shammai* applies a rabbinic *tumah* to the intermediate period even though biblically everything is fine. If *Beis Shammai* is willing to impose rabbinic *tumah* on the 11-and-12 scenario to prevent confusion with the 5-and-6 scenario, should he not similarly impose rabbinic *tumah* on the actual 5-and-6 scenario, where a woman who has immersed on the morning of day 6 and has not yet bled might be confused with the same woman who has bled later that day? The implicit logic is that *Beis Shammai* makes *gezeiros*, and this would seem to be a natural extension of the same concern.

### Kahana Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: רָאֲתָה שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** Rav Huna is teaching that Beit Shammai render her ritually impure even if she does not experience bleeding. Based on the mishna alone, one might have thought that the case where **she saw** blood **is different,** and it is only in this situation that Beit Shammai render her impure by rabbinic law.

Rav Kahana responds with a terse but decisive distinction: *ra'ata shonah* — it is different when she actually bleeds. The 11-and-12 scenario involves actual bleeding on day 12. There, *Beis Shammai* imposes a rabbinic *tumah* because the experiential and circumstantial similarity between bleeding on day 12 and bleeding on day 6 makes confusion genuinely likely — the same physical scenario, the only difference being which day of the cycle it occurred. That is a reasonable basis for a *gezeirah*. But in the 5-and-6 case that Rav Huna is positing — where she immersed on the morning of day 6 and did *not* bleed at all for the rest of the day — asking us to treat her as *tamei* regardless would require saying that *Beis Shammai* would confuse a day of bleeding with a day of not bleeding. That is an entirely different kind of confusion, and a far less plausible basis for a *gezeirah*.

### Abaye Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: וְכִי רָאֲתָה, מַאי הָוֵי? רְאִיָּיה דְּנִדָּה הִיא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: רַב כָּהֲנָא הָכִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ — בִּשְׁלָמָא הֵיכָא דְּרָאֲתָה, גָּזְרִינַן רְאִיָּיה דְּנִדָּה אַטּוּ רְאִיָּיה דְּזָבָה, אֶלָּא הֵיכָא דְּלָא רָאֲתָה, מַאי נִגְזַר בָּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said,** in refutation of Rav Kahana's claim: **And if she saw** blood on the twelfth day, **what of it?** A discharge of blood on the twelfth day **is a sighting of a menstruating woman,** which does not render her a *zava* by Torah law. Therefore, it is comparable to a case where she experienced bleeding on one day during her eleven days of *ziva* and immersed on the following day, and she does not experience bleeding on that following day at all. **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: This is what** is **difficult for Rav Kahana: Granted,** in a case **where she saw** blood on the twelfth day, it stands to reason that **we decree** impurity with regard to **a sighting of a menstruating woman due to a sighting of a *zava*. But** in a case **where she did not see** any blood at all, for **what** reason should the Sages **decree** impurity **upon her?**

Abaye clarifies Rav Kahana's point in response to Rav Yosef's further question. Rav Yosef had asked: but if she bleeds on day 12, what halachic significance does it have? It is *reiyah de-niddah hi* — it is bleeding that creates *niddah* status, not *zavah* status, so from a *zavah* perspective it is as if she has not bled at all. Abaye explains that Rav Kahana's reasoning is not about the halachic classification of the bleeding but about its experiential reality. *Bishlama heicha de-ra'asah* — when she actually bleeds, one can say: we will decree that bleeding in the *niddah* period (*reiyah de-niddah*) should be treated with caution lest it be confused with bleeding in the *zavah* period (*reiyah de-zavah*). The circumstances are so similar that a *gezeirah* is warranted. *Heicha de-lo ra'asah* — but when she has not bled at all on day 6, what exactly are we making a decree about? We cannot ask a woman who is diligently observing all the laws of *niddah* and *zavah* to confuse a day on which she did not bleed with a day on which she did. That confusion is simply not plausible, and therefore Rav Huna's extension of *Beis Shammai*'s stringency has no proven basis.

## Zav Analogy

### Zav Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תְּנַן: הָרוֹאָה רְאִיָּיה אַחַת שֶׁל זוֹב — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כְּבַעַל קֶרִי.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **And furthermore, we learned** in a mishna (*Zavim* 1:1): If a man **sees one sighting of *ziva*** (see 35b), **Beit Shammai say:** His status on the following day is **like** that of **a woman who observes** a clean **day for a day** she experiences a discharge. In other words, he must immerse and observe that day in purity, and if he touches tithes their status is suspended, since if he sees another discharge on that day, they are retroactively impure. **And Beit Hillel say:** His status is **like** that of a man **who experiences a seminal emission,** who is purified by his immersion, and therefore any tithes this man touches on the second day remain pure even if he later experiences a second discharge.

The *gemara* now turns to the laws of *zav* — a man who experiences an unnatural emission — to draw a parallel with the *zavah* scenarios discussed above and to test the boundaries of *Beis Shammai*'s positions. The *mishnah* in *Zavim* states: *ha-ro'eh reiyah achas shel zav* — a man who sees a single flow characteristic of *zav* emission. Such a flow is non-seminal; it is defined as having a whitish color but being more fluidic and less viscous than semen, and as the *Rambam* describes, it is experienced without physical pleasure, distinguishing it entirely from seminal emission. If a man has such a flow twice — whether on consecutive days or even twice on the same day — he becomes a *zav* and is subject to a seven-day *tumah* count requiring seven clean days and *korbanos*. If he has only a single such emission, his status is that of a *ba'al keri* — the *tumah* incurred by seminal emission — which is resolved by immersion and nightfall.

The underlying difference in law between *zav* flow and *keri* is significant in several respects. With regard to *mishkav u-moshav*, the things a *zav* or *zavah* sits or lies upon become *tamei* in a way that is unique to these categories. Moreover, the Torah states explicitly — and this is directly relevant to the earlier discussion in the *mishnah* — that a man who has relations with a *niddah* acquires her *tumah* entirely, a seven-day *tumah*, as a biblical consequence of the act itself. This is a *pasuk* in the Torah. By contrast, if a *zav* has relations with his wife, he does not transfer his *zav tumah* to her through the act of intercourse. A woman would acquire *tumah* by direct contact with a *zav*'s fluid externally, rendering her a *rishon le-tumah*, but she does not acquire the full equivalence of his *tumah* through relations in the way that a husband acquires the *tumah* of a *niddah*. This asymmetry is a biblical distinction: the transfer of *tumat niddah* through relations is explicitly stated in the Torah; no corresponding transfer is written by *zav*. This also has implications for the domestic life of *kohanim*, for whom marital relations routinely rendered the husband a *ba'al keri* unable to touch *terumah* until he had immersed and waited for nightfall.

### Shammai Zav View

*Beis Shammai* holds that the man who has seen a single *zav* flow has the status of a *shomeret yom keneged yom* — like the woman who bled on day 5 and is watching day 6. This means that everything he touches after immersion is held in abeyance, contingent on whether he will have a second flow. If he has a second flow, he becomes a *zav*, and retrospectively his intermediate period will be treated as that of a *zav* in process. *Beis Hillel* holds that he has the status of a *ba'al keri* who has immersed and is waiting for nightfall — that is, there is no holding in abeyance; if he has a second flow from that point on he becomes a *zav*, but the intermediate period is not retroactively affected.

### Fluid Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: הַמֵּסִיט אֶת הָרְאִיָּיה — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: תּוֹלִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מְטַהֲרִין. מִשְׁכָּבוֹת וּמוֹשָׁבוֹת שֶׁבֵּין רְאִיָּיה רִאשׁוֹנָה לִרְאִיָּיה שְׁנִיָּיה, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי תּוֹלִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מְטַהֲרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **moves the** discharge **seen** by a *zav* on the first day, **Beit Shammai say:** Due to the uncertainty, his status of ritual purity is **contingent** upon whether he sees another emission. If the *zav* sees a discharge a second time he will retroactively have been a full-fledged *zav* and his discharge renders one who moves it impure. But if he does not see another discharge he will remain like one who experienced a seminal emission, which means that his discharge does not render one who moves it impure. **And Beit Hillel deem** him **pure.** The discharge has the status of a seminal emission and does not transmit impurity to one who moves it. The *baraita* continues: With regard to items designated for **lying or sitting** and their status **between the first sighting and the second sighting, Beit Shammai** say: Due to the uncertainty their status of ritual purity is **contingent** upon whether he sees another emission, **and Beit Hillel deem** them **pure.**

A further question arises regarding the nature of the fluid itself. When a man has a second *zav* flow and thereby becomes a *zav*, does the first fluid retroactively take on the halachic character of *zav* flow? This has practical consequences because *zav* fluid, unlike seminal emission, causes *tumah* even through *heseit* — moving or carrying it without direct contact. *Beis Shammai* says the status of the first fluid is *tolim* — held in abeyance, determined by whether a second flow follows. If a second flow comes, the first emission is treated retroactively as *zav* fluid; if not, it remains like *keri*. *Beis Hillel* says the first fluid is *tahor* — it retains the status of *keri* regardless of subsequent developments, and the second flow creates a new and distinct *zav* status going forward without altering what came before.

### Zav Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא: הָרוֹאָה רְאִיָּיה אַחַת שֶׁל זוֹב, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם. אַלְמָא שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי תּוֹלִין. לָא תֵּימָא ״שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם״, אֶלָּא אֵימָא ״כְּבוֹעֵל שׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye explains Rav Kahana's difficulty with Rav Huna's opinion: It **is taught in the first clause** of the *baraita* that if a man **sees one sighting of *ziva*, Beit Shammai say:** His status is **like** that of **a woman who observes** a clean **day for a day** she experiences a discharge. **Apparently, according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai** the ritual purity status of **a woman who observes** a clean **day for a day** she experiences a discharge is **contingent** upon whether she experiences an emission of blood. This is difficult for Rav Huna, who explains that Beit Shammai hold that a woman who observes a clean day for each day of discharge is definitely impure, by rabbinic law. The Gemara answers that Rav Huna would explain: **Do not say** that his status is like that of **a woman who observes** a clean **day for a day. Rather, say** that he is **like one who engages in intercourse with a woman who observes** a clean **day for a day** after she has immersed. Rav Huna agrees that items designated for lying or sitting that this man sits or lies on is ritually impure only if she experiences bleeding before the end of the day.

The *gemara* now draws on the *zav* case as a proof regarding the five-and-six scenario and Rav Huna's claim. Recall that Rav Huna asserted that *Beis Shammai* would treat a woman as definitively *tamei* the entire day 6, even after she had immersed on the morning of day 6 and even if she did not bleed at all. But here, in the *zav* case, *Beis Shammai* says that the man who had one *zav* flow is like a *shomeret yom keneged yom* — and what is the halachic character of a *shomeret yom*? Her status is held in abeyance; she is not *straight tamei*. *Beis Shammai* explicitly uses the *shomeret yom* as his model, and in doing so implicitly acknowledges that the *shomeret yom* herself is not definitively *tamei* after her immersion — her status is conditional, not certain. This would seem to refute Rav Huna's claim that *Beis Shammai* would make the woman on day 6 straight *tamei* regardless of whether she bleeds.

The *gemara*, however, offers a textual refinement: do not read *Beis Shammai*'s language as comparing this man to the *shomeret yom* herself; rather, read it as comparing him to *bo'el shomeret yom keneged yom* — a man who has had relations with a *shomeret yom*. In other words, according to Rav Huna's interpretation, *Beis Shammai* treats the *shomeret yom* herself as definitively *tamei* on day 6 even after immersion, but the man who had relations with her — being one degree removed — is given the more lenient treatment of having his status held in abeyance rather than being rendered definitively *tamei*. The comparison to *shomeret yom* in the *zav* case refers to this secondary, one-degree-removed status, not to the woman herself. The *zav* case thus does not contradict Rav Huna's claim; it confirms that *Beis Shammai* treats the direct case more stringently while extending a relative leniency to those one step removed.

### Sex Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא אִיהוּ דְּלָא מְטַמְּאַ מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב, וּמַאי שְׁנָא אִיהִי דִּמְטַמְּיָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** with regard to **him,** a man who engages in intercourse with a woman who is observing a clean day for a day, **that he does not transmit impurity** to items designated for **lying or sitting, and what is different** with regard to **her,** that woman herself, **that she** does **transmit impurity to** items designated for lying or sitting?

This leads to a question that the *gemara* makes explicit: *mai shana* — why the difference? Why would *Beis Shammai* treat the woman herself as definitively *tamei* on day 6 after immersion, while treating the man who had relations with her as only conditionally, not definitively, *tamei*?

### Stringency Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיהוּ דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי בֵּיהּ דָּמִים — לָא גְּזוּר בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן, אִיהִי דִּשְׁכִיחִי בַּהּ דָּמִים — גְּזוּר בַּהּ רַבָּנַן. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב — דִּמְטַמְּיָא לֵיהּ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא בּוֹעֵל — דְּלָא מְטַמְּיָא לֵיהּ? מִשְׁכָּב וּמוֹשָׁב דִּשְׁכִיחַ — מְטַמְּיָא לֵיהּ, בּוֹעֵל דְּלָא שְׁכִיחַ — לָא מְטַמְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: With regard to **him, as he does not frequently** see **blood, the Sages did not issue a decree** rendering **him** impure to such an extent, if the woman has not seen blood on the second day. Whereas with regard to **her, as she does frequently** see **blood, the Sages did issue a decree** rendering her definitely impure to this degree. The Gemara further inquires: **And** since a decree was applied to her, as she commonly sees blood, **what is different** with regard to items designated for **lying or sitting, that she transmits impurity to them, and what is different** with regard to the **one who engages in intercourse** with her, **that she does not render him impure?** The Gemara answers: With regard to items designated for **lying or sitting, she renders them ritually impure because she frequently** sits or lies on them, whereas with regard to **one who engages in intercourse** with her, **which is not** nearly as **frequent,** she does **not render him ritually impure.**

The *gemara* answers in two stages. First: *ihu de-lo shechichei bei damim lo gazru bei rabbanan; ihi de-shechichei bah damim gazru bah rabbanan* — he, the man, does not regularly experience bleeding, and therefore the rabbis did not impose a *gezeirah* upon him in the same way; she, the woman, regularly experiences bleeding, and therefore the rabbis did apply a *gezeirah* to her. The point is that the rabbinic concern motivating the stringency — preventing confusion between scenarios of bleeding and not bleeding, or between different days of the cycle — is rooted in the regular, recurring experience of uterine bleeding that is characteristic of women. A man does not share this regularity, and therefore the rabbinic decree is applied to him with correspondingly less force.

The *gemara* then addresses a further refinement: even accepting that the man is treated differently, why specifically is his *mishkav u-moshav* not treated as *tamei*, while the act of relations is left in abeyance rather than rendered definitely *tamei*? The answer is: *mishkav u-moshav de-shechiah* — sitting on objects and handling things is ubiquitous and unavoidable. A woman who has immersed in the morning will inevitably touch things, sit on things, go about her day. Since such contact is certain to occur, it is meaningful for the rabbis to declare those contacts *tamei* as a blanket stringency. *Bo'el de-lo shechiah* — but marital relations after an uncertain immersion is not the kind of activity that is likely to occur in practice, because a woman in such a situation knows she should not be engaging in relations until her status is fully resolved at nightfall. Since the scenario itself is unlikely, the rabbis did not extend the full *gezeirah* to it, and the man's status therefore remains conditional rather than definitive.
