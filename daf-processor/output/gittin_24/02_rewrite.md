# Gittin 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slave Shlichim

### Slave Status

Today's daf is 24, and we resume in the middle of the *amud*, just before the *mishnah*. The previous discussion concerned the question of whether a slave can serve as a *shaliach*. There are two basic requirements for *shelichut*: first, the agent must be a member of the Jewish community, and second, the laws governing the relevant transaction must be personally applicable to the agent.

The *gemara* had examined what can be derived from the *pasuk* of *gam atem* in the context of *terumah* — specifically, whether the phrase excludes only a non-Jew or extends to exclude a slave on the grounds that a slave is not fully considered *Yisrael*. The *gemara* concluded that a slave would in fact be included in the category of *ben brit*, obligated in *mitzvot*. Rashi points to the *pasuk* at the end of Devarim — *chotzev etzecha* — as establishing the slave's inclusion in the *brit*. A slave is therefore not categorically excluded from serving as a *shaliach*.

### Bar Hachi

Nevertheless, the second requirement remains: in order to serve as a *shaliach* for a particular legal domain, the agent must be, in the language of the *gemara*, *bar hachi* — the relevant laws must actually apply to him or her.

### Terumot/Maasrot

A slave can serve as a *shaliach* for *terumot u'maasrot* because those obligations are relevant to the slave.

### Gittin/Kiddushin Exclusi…

A slave, however, cannot serve as a *shaliach* for *gittin ve'kiddushin*, because there is no binding *kiddushin* or *gittin* that applies to a slave — even though a slave is *chayav* in *mitzvot* more broadly. That is the conceptual exclusion.

## Gitov Veyado

### Pregnant Slave Challenge

With that established, the *gemara* presents a statement of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — appearing approximately fifteen lines from the top of the *amud* — which reads: *ein eved na'aseh shaliach l'kabel get isha miyad ba'alah*, a slave cannot serve as a *shaliach* to receive a bill of divorce on behalf of a woman from her husband's hand, *lefi she'eino b'torat gittin ve'kiddushin*, because a slave is not within the legal category of *gittin* and *kiddushin*.

The statement then continues: *ve'af al pi she'shaninu*, although we have taught that a person can give his female slave a *shtar shichrur* and say, in effect: you remain a slave, but your unborn child I am freeing — and this actually works. *Im hayta ubarah zachsa lo*: if she was pregnant, she takes possession of the writ of manumission on behalf of the child and the child is freed.

The *gemara* immediately asks: *mai im hayta ubarah zachsa lo*? How is this relevant to the preceding discussion? And more pointedly — how is she functioning here? Is she serving as a *shaliach* for her *ubar*, her fetus? If so, there would be no problem on the grounds of *gittin ve'kiddushin*, since a slave is certainly within the category of *shichrur* and could in principle receive a *shtar shichrur* for herself or for another. So why should this case pose any challenge at all?

The *gemara* proceeds to develop this question. The real concern is not the *bar hachi* problem of *gittin ve'kiddushin*, but something more fundamental: the question of *netinah* — the act of giving. A slave who belongs to a master cannot be a valid separate legal domain for the purposes of receiving a document from that same master. If the master gives a *shtar shichrur* to slave A in order for slave A to receive it on behalf of slave B — when both slaves are owned by the same master — it is as though the master took it from his right hand and placed it in his left. There is no genuine act of *netinah*, because slave A, even after accepting the *shtar shichrur*, remains within the master's domain.

This contrasts with the principle of *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad*: when the master frees his own slave, in that very act of giving, the slave simultaneously becomes an independent entity. The giving itself creates a new domain, and so the act of *netinah* is valid. But this only works when the slave receiving the document is the very slave being freed by that act. When the slave receiving the document is not the one being freed in that act, no such transformation occurs, and the document cannot be said to have been genuinely transferred.

So the question becomes sharp: when the master gives a *shtar shichrur* to his pregnant slave to free her unborn child, both the slave woman and the fetus are his property. He is not freeing her — only the fetus. Why then is this not considered giving from his right hand to his left? Where is the *ma'aseh netinah*?

### Two Explanations

*Ki ata Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda amar Rabbi Yochanan tarti amar* — when Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda came and reported the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan, he clarified that Rabbi Yochanan actually said two things that were not directly connected to each other. The statement about a slave being unable to receive a *get* on behalf of a woman was one statement. The subsequent clause about the pregnant slave receiving a *shtar shichrur* for her fetus was not a challenge to it; it qualified a different earlier teaching — namely that a slave *can* serve as a *shaliach* to receive a *shtar shichrur* for a fellow slave from *another* master. The pregnant slave case is a separate ruling with its own logic, which then has to be explained.

*Emor lo shenei gedolei hador perishu et hadavar*, the *gemara* says: two of the great ones of the generation interpreted this matter. And who are they? Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak. *Chad amar* — one said one thing; *vechad amar* — the other offered a complementary explanation.

### Freeing Half

The first explanation: *ha mni Rabbi hi* — this ruling follows the position of Rabbi, who taught *hamishacher chetzi avdo kanah*, that one can free half of one's slave. The principle of *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad* applies even when the slave does not become a completely independent entity. One can renounce ownership over half the slave — not a specific limb necessarily, but a conceptual half — and because in that act of giving, half of the slave becomes free, there is enough of a transformation to constitute a valid *netinah* for that half.

### Fetus Separation

The second explanation builds on the first: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi beha* — how would Rabbi's principle apply to the specific case of the pregnant slave? The answer is *ubar yerech imo hu*, the fetus is not considered a separate entity but is legally part of the mother. Therefore, when the master frees the fetus, it is as though he is freeing a specific limb or part of the slave woman herself — *denafek minei she'it lei leachad me'eivarav*. It is as though he said: your right arm is no longer mine; only your left arm remains in my service. So freeing the fetus is a kind of *chetzi eved* — freeing an identifiable part of the slave woman.

### Gitov Principle

This then produces enough of a *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad* dynamic: because in this very act of the slave woman receiving the *shtar shichrur*, some portion of her becomes her own entity — not the fetus physically taking hold of the document, but the conceptual reality that this act simultaneously partially frees her — there is sufficient of a separate domain created to constitute a genuine *netinah*. This is admittedly a *chiddush*: the separation is entirely conceptual, not physical.

To summarize the framework: a slave can serve as *shaliach* for something relevant to the slave's own legal status — yes for *shtar shichrur*, yes for *terumot u'maasrot*, but not for *gittin ve'kiddushin*. And a master cannot give a *shtar shichrur* through one of his own slaves to free another of his own slaves, because there is no genuine *ma'aseh netinah* — it is all within his domain. The exception is when he frees that slave directly, or frees half of that slave, or frees the fetus of that slave — in all of those cases the principle of *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad* applies, and the act is valid.

## Women Witnesses

### Disqualified Witness

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*, which introduces a new dimension of the question of who may deliver a *get*. The *mishnah* states: *af hanashim she'einam ne'emanot lomar met ba'alah* — certain women who are not believed to testify that another woman's husband has died are nevertheless *ne'emanot l'havi et gittah* — believed to deliver her *get*.

This is drawn from a broader discussion in Masechet Yevamot, where we accept the testimony of a single witness, including a woman, that a husband has died, allowing the wife to remarry. However, certain women are excluded from this leniency because we suspect they harbor animosity toward the wife and might falsely report the husband's death in order to bring disaster upon her — causing her to remarry while her husband is still alive.

The *mishnah* enumerates these women: *chamotah*, the mother-in-law; *u'vat chamotah*, the mother-in-law's daughter (the sister-in-law); *v'tzaratah*, the co-wife — obviously suspect; *v'yevamtah*, the husband's brother's wife — because if the husband dies without children, the husband's brother will perform *yibum* with this woman, making the existing wife a *tzarah* to her; and *u'vat ba'alah*, the stepdaughter.

All of these women are potentially suspect of wishing harm to the wife. Yet the *mishnah* says they are believed to bring a *get*.

### Self-Proving Doc

The *mishnah* then asks: *mah bein get l'mitah*? What is the difference between delivering a *get* and testifying about death? The answer is *she'haktav mochiach* — because here there is a signed writ, and we are not relying solely on their word. The document is signed by witnesses, and so we do not suspect the women of forgery. The document speaks for itself.

The *mishnah* further states that *ha'ishah atzmah mevi'ah gittah*, a woman may bring her own *get*, *v'hi shaliach l'atzmah* — she functions as a *shaliach* to herself — though the conceptual problem of *netinah* in that arrangement will be addressed shortly by the *gemara*. In any case, she must say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*.

This is itself somewhat puzzling: the declaration of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* is only required when bringing a *get* from a distant location. If the husband gave it to her directly, why would she need to say this? And if she is bringing it from elsewhere, how does she give it to herself? These questions will be explored by the *gemara* in the next section.

A *baraita* is then cited that seems to contradict the *mishnah*: *keshem she'eina ne'emanet lomar met ba'alah kach eina ne'emanet l'havi et gittah* — the very women who are not believed to testify about death are also not believed to deliver a *get*. This appears to directly contradict the *mishnah*.

### Rav Yosef Position

Rav Yosef resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya kan b'Eretz kan b'Chutz la'Aretz* — it is not a difficulty. The two sources refer to different contexts. In *Eretz Yisrael*, where the *shaliach* does not need to declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, the woman is barely saying anything at all; the *get* speaks for itself. *B'Eretz d'lav adibura didah samchinan* — we are not relying on her testimony; the writ does the work. So the *mishnah* is correct that she is believed in *Eretz Yisrael*. In *Chutz la'Aretz*, however, *dibura didah ka samchinan* — we are relying on her declaration of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* to validate the *get*, and in that case she is not believed, as the *baraita* states.

### Abaye Counterargument

Abaye challenged this: *ad raba, ipcha mistabra* — on the contrary, the opposite logic is more compelling. In *Eretz Yisrael*, precisely because there is no formal declaration of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, the wife remains vulnerable. If the husband were ever to contest the *get*, she would have difficulty validating it, and her situation could be ruined. *D'ika l'meimar l'kula ka michavna* — we might suspect that this hostile woman engineered the entire situation precisely to set the wife up for catastrophe. Therefore, in *Eretz Yisrael*, these women should *not* be believed. In *Chutz la'Aretz*, by contrast, once *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* has been declared, the husband cannot challenge the *get*. The wife is protected. So in *Chutz la'Aretz*, these women *are* believed — the woman is secure, and there is far less to be concerned about.

### Protection Via Bifnei

A *baraita* is then cited: *tanya k'vatei d'Abaye* — the ruling follows Abaye's reasoning, establishing that the problem is specifically in *Eretz Yisrael*, where the wife is left vulnerable and susceptible to having her life ruined by a hostile *shaliach*.

Rav Ashi then adds that the *mishnah* itself supports Abaye, through close reading. The *mishnah* specifies that a woman bringing her own *get* must say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, which means the entire context of the *mishnah* — including the ruling about these potentially hostile women — is one of delivery from *Chutz la'Aretz*. The *mishnah* is therefore discussing the case where these women are *believed*, namely when they bring a *get* from outside the land and make the declaration that protects the wife. *Shma mina*.

As for Rav Yosef — who held that the *mishnah*'s leniency applies only to *Eretz Yisrael* — one might ask how he would handle the fact that the opening case in the *mishnah* (about a *suma* who cannot bring a *get*) and the closing case (a woman bringing her own *get*) both clearly concern *Chutz la'Aretz* and *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, while the middle section (these hostile women) would on his reading concern *Eretz Yisrael*. His answer: *reisha ve-seifa b'chutz la'aretz, metzi'asa ba-aretz* — yes, the beginning and end discuss *Chutz la'Aretz*, the middle discusses *Eretz Yisrael*. This is a possible reading, though the evidence overwhelmingly favors Abaye's position that the entire *mishnah* is set in a *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* context, i.e., *Chutz la'Aretz*. The phrase *mipnei she'haktav mochiach* — that the writ itself testifies — is what pushed Rav Yosef toward his reading; to him it implies that the woman is saying nothing at all and only the document speaks, pointing to *Eretz Yisrael*. But the *gemara*'s evidence tips decisively toward Abaye.

### Shimon Ben Elazar

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar then cites a teaching in the name of Rabbi Akiva: *ishah ne'emenet l'havi gittah mi'kal vachomer* — a woman may bring her own *get* by means of a *kal vachomer*. The argument runs: women who are not believed to say that this woman's husband died are nonetheless believed to deliver her *get*. Should it not follow by *kal vachomer* that a woman who *is* believed to testify that her own husband died should certainly be believed to deliver her own *get*? This establishes the wife's credibility as her own *shaliach*.

But then the *gemara* applies the limiting principle *mimakom shebata* — you cannot derive a conclusion that is stronger than its source. Since the *kal vachomer* is built on the case where those hostile women bring a *get* with the declaration *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, the same requirement must apply when the wife brings her own *get*: *tzricha shetomar bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*. And this, in turn, confirms that we are speaking throughout the *mishnah* in the context of delivery from *Chutz la'Aretz* — supporting Abaye's position.

## Woman Delivers Own Get

### Self-Agency Problem

The *gemara* now turns to the conceptual puzzle raised by the *mishnah*'s ruling that *ha'ishah atzmah mevi'ah gittah* — a woman can bring her own *get*. The problem is this: *isha, mikdei mati gitta l'yada, igrashah lah* — the moment the *get* reaches her hand, she is divorced. How, then, can she simultaneously function as a *shaliach* to deliver something to herself? The instant of delivery and the instant of receiving are the same moment, and more fundamentally, the *shaliach* and the recipient are the same person.

### Rav Huna Solution

Rav Huna offers an initial answer: *be'omer lo titgarshi bo ela bifnei beit din ploni* — the husband stipulates that the divorce should not take effect until she presents it before a specific *beit din* in another city. So she must travel to, say, Miami, and the divorce takes effect only upon her arrival there. This creates an interval during which she is a *shaliach* transporting the *get* without yet being divorced.

### Ground Placement

But the *gemara* presses: even under that formulation, she is still not a conventional *shaliach* to herself; the moment she arrives and the divorce takes effect, she is simultaneously *shaliach* and recipient. Moreover, why does she need to say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* at all if the husband gave it to her directly?

The *gemara* then considers a different approach attributed to Rav Huna bar Manoach in the name of Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika. He suggests the following formulation: the husband instructs her to travel to a designated place, and when she arrives, to place the *get* on the ground — *anachi a'ara* — and then pick it up. The idea is that she, in her capacity as *shaliach*, puts the *get* down; then, in her capacity as the wife, she picks it up. The problem is immediately apparent: this is not an act of *netinah*. She gave it to the ground, not to herself. The *gemara* invokes the teaching of Rava: *tali gittech mei'al gabei karka lo amar klum* — if a husband says to his wife, "Pick up your *get* from the ground," he has said nothing. That is not a valid act of giving. The same logic applies here.

### Status Change

The *gemara* therefore arrives at the formulation of Rav Huna bar Manoach: *hevei shaliach l'holacha ad demati hatam, ki mati hatam hevei shaliach l'kabala v'kabli at gittech* — be a *shaliach l'holacha* for transport until you arrive there, and when you arrive, your status changes: you will become a *shaliach l'kablah*, and you will receive your *get*. The Rashba notes that the husband need not literally appoint her as *shaliach l'kablah*; she simply becomes herself. But the formulation is stated in parallel terms to clarify the conceptual structure.

The key moment is this: she is holding the *get* as the husband's agent. At the designated place, at the designated moment, her identity shifts — she transitions from holding the document as *shaliach* to holding it as the wife. That transition itself, that change of legal identity while holding the document, constitutes the act of *netinah*. It is entirely conceptual and in no way physical, but that is precisely the point: she went from holding it in one capacity to holding it in another, and that is sufficient to constitute giving.

It is because of this conceptual act of *netinah* that she must say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* — she is, after all, bringing it from one jurisdiction to another, and the declaration is required.

### Agency Survival

The *gemara*, however, objects: *veha lo hazra shelichut eitzel ba'al* — the *shelichut* did not return to the husband. The principle implied here, articulated in the manner Rashi and Tosafot explain it, is that for genuine *shelichut* to exist, the agent's identity must survive beyond the act itself. Theoretically, the agent should be able to return to the principal and report: "I completed what you instructed me to do." In this case, however, the only way she can complete the act is by ceasing to be the husband's agent. Her capacity as *shaliach* is extinguished at the very moment of execution. Since the *shelichut* cannot survive the act, it cannot properly be said to have functioned as a valid agency in the first place.

### Intermediary Solutions

Given this difficulty, the *gemara* explores alternative approaches. One suggestion: when she arrives at the destination, she should appoint someone else as her *shaliach l'kablah* to receive the *get* from her. This way she transfers it as the husband's *shaliach*, and a separate person receives it on her behalf as wife — two distinct parties, two distinct roles, a genuine act of *netinah*.

This, however, immediately raises another issue: *hanicha leman de'amar isha osa shaliach l'kablah miyad shaliach ba'al* — this works only if a woman can appoint a *shaliach l'kablah* to receive a *get* from her husband's *shaliach*. And that is itself a matter of debate, which the *gemara* will now address in the following section.

Alternatively — and the *gemara* presents this as well — she is instructed: when you arrive, say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* before a *beit din*, and then the *beit din* will appoint a second *shaliach l'holacha* to carry it further and ultimately hand it to her. A *beit din* functions as an institutional intermediary, which is in fact a practical arrangement used in real cases: when a *shaliach* cannot complete delivery, the *shaliach* transfers responsibility to a *beit din*, which then arranges a replacement. In both solutions, the key move is that a second party becomes involved so that she is not simultaneously the agent delivering and the person receiving.

One may note that the *gemara* does not raise the seemingly simpler solution of placing the *get* in her property — which in many contexts constitutes a valid *kinyan* and *netinah*. This absence is itself worth noting, and the *gemara*'s preference for these conceptual solutions over that apparently straightforward one remains an open question.

## Layered Agency Debate

### Question Posed

As noted, the *gemara*'s solution of having the wife appoint her own *shaliach l'kablah* to receive the *get* from the husband's *shaliach* raises a disputed question: *hanicha leman de'amar isha osa shaliach l'kablah miyad shaliach ba'al* — this works only for the opinion that a woman *may* appoint a *shaliach* to receive a *get* from the husband's *shaliach*. *Ela leman de'amar ein isha osa shaliach l'kablah miyad shaliach ba'al* — but according to the opinion that she may *not* do so, what can be said?

A woman may certainly appoint a *shaliach l'kablah* to receive a *get* from the husband directly. The question is whether she may do so when the *get* is being delivered by the husband's own *shaliach*. This is disputed, and the *gemara* now examines the grounds for the restrictive opinion.

### Bizayon Ba'al

One explanation for why a woman cannot appoint a *shaliach l'kablah* to receive from the husband's *shaliach* is *mishum bizayon deba'al* — it is disparaging to the husband. The husband chose to send his *get* through a *shaliach*; he was not present himself. If the wife then also sends a *shaliach* rather than appearing herself, the husband might feel doubly slighted. He is not there in person, and neither is she — the *get* passes between two agents with neither principal present. This is considered an affront to the husband's dignity.

Now, if that is the concern, then *vehacha ba'al lo kapid* — in our case, the husband himself has instructed her to arrange things this way. He has no basis to object. If the *bizayon* concern is the entire rationale, then when the husband explicitly authorizes this arrangement, it is entirely resolved.

### Property Transfer Issue

A second explanation: the concern is *mishum chatzeira haba lachar mikan* — the arrangement looks too much like a case of transferring property through a series of intermediate transactions, in a way that creates structural ambiguity about whether a genuine *netinah* occurred. Consider: if a man places a *get* in a courtyard and then the courtyard is transferred to his wife, is she divorced? No — there was no valid *netinah* at the time the *get* was placed, and the subsequent transfer of the property does not retroactively create one. This case, while only loosely analogous, resembles the situation where neither the husband nor the wife is the direct party in the *get* exchange — the husband's *shaliach* gives it to the wife's *shaliach* — and it begins to look structurally similar to those cases of indirect property transfer where no genuine *netinah* takes place.

If this is the concern, it is harder to resolve simply by pointing to the husband's explicit instruction, because the structural problem — the resemblance to *chatzeira* transfer cases — persists regardless.

### Acceptable Pathways

The *gemara* therefore proposes an alternative approach for this second opinion. The husband instructs her: be a *shaliach l'holacha* until you arrive at the destination, and when you arrive, appoint a second *shaliach l'holacha* to replace you. You then receive the *get* — as yourself, the wife — from that second *shaliach l'holacha*. In this formulation, the wife is not appointing a *shaliach l'kablah* to receive from the husband's *shaliach*; rather, she transfers her role as *shaliach l'holacha* to a second party, and then separately receives the *get* as the wife from that second party. This avoids the problem regardless of which rationale underlies the restrictive opinion.

The *gemara* adds: *v'ei ba'it eima* — alternatively, she may go before a *beit din*, declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, and transfer the task to the *beit din*, which then appoints a new *shaliach l'holacha* to deliver the *get* to her. Tosafot notes that this is essentially a variation on the same structure. But the principle is the same: by bringing in a *beit din* as the institutional intermediary, the chain is broken into two clear stages, and a genuine act of *netinah* from a distinct party to the wife can take place.

The conceptual elegance of the solution — that the wife's mere change of legal status while holding the *get* could constitute an act of *netinah* — ultimately does not survive the *gemara*'s scrutiny, because of the *shelichut* survival problem. In the end, the *gemara* requires a second party to be present so that there is an actual giving from one domain to another.

## Lishmah Requirement

### Four Invalid Cases

With *Hadran alach hameivi get* — the conclusion of the second *perek* — we now enter the third *perek*, which addresses the criteria for the writing of a *get* itself, centering on the requirement of *lishmah*. This is thematically apt: according to Rabba, the very purpose of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* was to ensure *lishmah* — that the *get* was written specifically for this man and this woman. The third *perek* thus deepens that theme directly.

The *mishnah* states: *kol get shenichtav shelo l'shem ishah pasul* — any *get* written not for the sake of the specific woman is invalid. The *mishnah* then presents four cases.

### Practice Case

The first: *haya over bashuk veshamah kol sofrim makrin* — one was walking in the marketplace and heard the sound of a scribal school, where scribes were being instructed by their teacher reciting aloud: so-and-so divorces so-and-so from such-and-such a place. The student scribes were practicing by writing out this text. Now, a man hears this and recognizes that the names being called out are his name and his wife's name. May he take this *get* and use it to divorce his wife? *Pasul* — it is invalid, for it was not written for him and his wife.

Rava clarifies: *besofrim ha'asuyin lehitlamed askinan* — we are dealing with a scribal school practicing. They were not writing this *get* for anyone in particular; it was purely practice. Rav Ashi adds a textual proof: the *mishnah* says *sofrim makrin* — someone was *reciting* to them — not that the scribes themselves were writing on their own initiative. The teacher dictated; they wrote as practice. *Shma mina*.

### Discarded Get

The second case: a man wrote a *get* with intent to divorce his wife and then changed his mind and discarded it. Another man in the same city happens to share the same name, and his wife shares the same name as the first man's wife. He finds the discarded *get* and proposes using it. *Pasul legaresh es ishto* — invalid. Even though this *get* was written for the genuine purpose of *gerushin* — not mere practice — it was not written for *this* man and *this* woman.

### Wrong Wife

The third case: a man has two wives, both with the same name. He wrote a *get* to divorce his elder wife. *Lo yegaresh bo es haketanah* — he may not use it to divorce the younger. Even though the *get* was written for genuine *gerushin*, for him, the correct names appear — still, it was written for wife number one and cannot be used for wife number two.

### Unspecified Wife

The fourth case: the man instructs the *lavlar* — the scribe — to write a *get* saying: I will divorce whichever wife I choose. He leaves it open-ended, intending to decide later which wife to divorce with it. *Pasul legaresh bo* — this too is invalid.

The *gemara* presents a *tana devei Rabbi Yishmael* who articulates the escalating logic of these four cases. Not only is the one written by the scribal school invalid — for it was not written *l'shem gerushin* at all — but even one written genuinely for divorce purposes is invalid if it was written for a *different* man. And not only that, but even one written for the right man is invalid if written for the *wrong wife*. And not only that, but even one written for the right man without specifying which of his wives is also invalid — even when it is not written for a specific wrong wife but simply left open.

### Ketav Derivation

*Mai ta'ama*? The *gemara* explains. Had the Torah simply said to deliver a *sefer keritut*, the only requirement would have been that it function as an instrument of divorce. A *get* written for a different person would still qualify, since it was written as a genuine *get*. *Kasav Rachmana vekasav* — the Torah's formulation, "and he shall write" (*vekasav lah*), teaches that the writing must be *for him* — *l'shem* the specific *megaresh*. This excludes a *get* written by someone else for another person. And *lah* — "for her" — teaches that it must be written for this specific woman, excluding the case where a man wrote it for his elder wife and now wishes to use it for his younger.

### Ein Bereirah

As for the open-ended case — *le'eizo she'ertzeh agaresh bo* — the *gemara* explains this as *ein bereirah*: one cannot leave a legal act undefined and allow a later determination to retroactively fix what the act was "for." The *lah* requirement demands clarity *at the time of writing*.

Tosafot adds an important qualification: this principle applies here even according to opinions that generally accept *yesh bereirah* in other legal contexts — for instance, the retroactive designation of *terumot u'maasrot*. Even those who would normally permit later determination agree that here it is invalid, *mishum de'vekasav lah lishmah mashma sheyehe mevurar besha'as kesivah* — because the requirement of *lah*, of writing specifically for *her*, implies that it must be definitively clear at the moment of writing for whom the *get* is being written.

One may note that this entire analysis of *lishmah* follows the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who places emphasis on the *ketivah* — the act of writing. According to Rabbi Meir, *vekasav* is understood to refer to *vechasam*, the signing, and as long as the signing reflects the proper parties, the writing itself is less critical. This explains why the *stam mishnah* here, with its focus on *lishmah* requirements in writing, follows Rabbi Eliezer — while an earlier *mishnah* permitting a *cheresh shoteh ve'katan* to write a *get* reflected Rabbi Meir's position. This juxtaposition itself prompted the *gemara* to explore ways of reconciling those two *mishnayot*.

## Ambiguous Names

### Name Ambiguity

The *mishnah* states that a man may write a *get* for his elder wife and validly use it to divorce her — *hagedolah matzi megaresh bah*. This assumes that even though both wives share the same name, the *get* is nonetheless valid for the one it was written for. This immediately raises a broader question: if two people in the same city share the name of the husband, and two people share the name of the wife, is a *get* with those names sufficient? Is it valid if the document itself does not unambiguously identify which parties are intended?

This question carries particular weight according to Rabbi Meir, for whom *edei chasimah karti* — the witnesses who sign the *get* are the ones who, in effect, validate the divorce — and the *get* must also function as independent *edut*, testimony. If the testimony is ambiguous — if we cannot know from the document itself which parties are referred to — perhaps the *get* is inherently defective.

### Rava's Parallel

Rava draws a parallel to the laws of debt. If two people named Yosef ben Shimon live in the same city, and a creditor holds a *shtar chov* — a debt instrument — that says Yosef ben Shimon owes a certain sum, the creditor cannot collect from the *wrong* Yosef ben Shimon. The instrument alone does not establish which one is meant.

### Holder Identity

Yet the answer in the *get* context is that ambiguity is resolved by the identity of the party *holding* the document. If the woman is holding the *get*, then her name is established by her possession. The name in the *get* that identifies the husband is then necessarily the name of her husband — the man who was married to *her*. So even if there are two men with the same name in the city, the fact that she is holding the *get* identifies which man it refers to: by definition, it refers to her husband.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye, however, challenges this resolution with a pointed question. In the second case of the *mishnah* — where a man found a discarded *get* whose names matched his own and his wife's — we ruled that *get* invalid: *sheni hu de-lo matzi megaresh bah, ha-rishon matzi megaresh bah*. The first man could use it, the second cannot. But this seems inconsistent with Rava's principle. If the holder's identity resolves ambiguity, why can't the second man use the discarded *get*? He is holding it, and his wife's name matches — should that not settle which party is meant?

Moreover, Abaye notes: we said that when two people share the same name, one cannot use a *shtar chov* to collect from the other — *veha amrinan de-lo acher yakhol lehotzi aleihem shtar chov*. That is, a third party named Michael cannot present a debt instrument saying Yosef ben Shimon owes money and collect from either Yosef ben Shimon. But when *Yosef ben Shimon himself* holds it, the identity of the relevant Yosef ben Shimon is clear. In the *get* case, when a woman holds a *get* and the name of the husband appears in it, the question is which husband is meant. Unlike a *shtar chov*, where the holder's identity resolves the question of the debtor, in a *get* it is not the *holder* whose identity is in question — it is the *other party*, the husband. Having the wife hold the document does not by itself tell us which husband she is referring to.

Abaye's challenge is, as the *gemara* acknowledges, somewhat striking. After all, if we know who the wife is, we necessarily know who her husband is — at least in a case where she has only one husband. But Abaye is pressing on the general principle.

### Edei Mesirah

The *gemara* provides the resolution: *be'idei mesirah de-Rabbi Eliezer hi, hacha nami be'idei mesirah de-Rabbi Eliezer hi* — this entire framework operates according to the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that what validates a *get* is not *edei chasimah* — the witnesses who signed — but *edei mesirah* — witnesses to the act of delivery itself. For Rabbi Eliezer, the *get* does not need to function as self-contained testimony. Its validity rests not on the document's ability to independently attest to the divorce but on the fact that witnesses observed the act of transfer. Therefore, any ambiguity in the document itself is immaterial — the witnesses to delivery observed precisely who gave the *get* to whom, and *that* is what establishes the divorce.

For Rabbi Meir, by contrast, *edei chasimah karti* — the witnesses who signed are the essential validating mechanism — and the document must be able to testify on its own. Under Rabbi Meir's framework, a *get* whose names are shared by others in the town would be problematic, since the document cannot independently identify the parties. Rabbi Eliezer has no such requirement; external witnesses to delivery supply what the document alone might lack.

This is why the *gemara* notes that the observation could appear strange — once we know who the wife is (from her holding it), the husband's identity follows necessarily. The *gemara*'s answer, that we follow Rabbi Eliezer's framework of *edei mesirah*, clarifies that the question was never really about the document's text at all. The *get*'s validity is grounded in witnessed delivery, and once those witnesses establish that this man gave this document to this woman, no textual ambiguity can undo it.
