# Gittin 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slave Shlichim

### Slave Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּעֶבֶד כָּשֵׁר. אָמַר רַב אַסִּי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הָעֶבֶד נַעֲשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל גֵּט לְאִשָּׁה מִיַּד בַּעְלָהּ, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּתוֹרַת גִּיטִּין וְקִדּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** one can learn **by inference** that **a slave is fit. Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A slave cannot become an agent to receive a bill of divorce for a woman from the hand of her husband, because he is not** included **in the *halakhot* of divorce and betrothal,** and one can act as an agent only in a matter that applies to him.

Today's daf is 24, and we resume in the middle of the *amud*, just before the *mishnah*. The previous discussion concerned the question of whether a slave can serve as a *shaliach*. There are two basic requirements for *shelichut*: first, the agent must be a member of the Jewish community, and second, the laws governing the relevant transaction must be personally applicable to the agent.

The *gemara* had examined what can be derived from the *pasuk* of *gam atem* in the context of *terumah* — specifically, whether the phrase excludes only a non-Jew or extends to exclude a slave on the grounds that a slave is not fully considered *Yisrael*. The *gemara* concluded that a slave would in fact be included in the category of *ben brit*, obligated in *mitzvot*. Rashi points to the *pasuk* at the end of Devarim — *chotzev etzecha* — as establishing the slave's inclusion in the *brit*. A slave is therefore not categorically excluded from serving as a *shaliach*.

### Bar Hachi

Nevertheless, the second requirement remains: in order to serve as a *shaliach* for a particular legal domain, the agent must be, in the language of the *gemara*, *bar hachi* — the relevant laws must actually apply to him or her.

### Terumot/Maasrot

A slave can serve as a *shaliach* for *terumot u'maasrot* because those obligations are relevant to the slave.

### Gittin/Kiddushin Exclusion

A slave, however, cannot serve as a *shaliach* for *gittin ve'kiddushin*, because there is no binding *kiddushin* or *gittin* that applies to a slave — even though a slave is *chayav* in *mitzvot* more broadly. That is the conceptual exclusion.

## Gitov Veyado

### Pregnant Slave Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הָעֶבֶד נַעֲשֶׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל גֵּט אִשָּׁה מִיַּד בַּעְלָהּ, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּתוֹרַת גִּיטִּין וְקִידּוּשִׁין, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ שִׁפְחָה וּוְלָדֵךְ בֶּן חוֹרִין״, אִם הָיְתָה עוּבָּרָה – זָכְתָה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes a related statement: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A slave cannot become an agent to receive a bill of divorce for a woman from the hand of her husband, because he is not** included **in the *halakhot* of divorce and betrothal. And although we learned:** If a person said to his female slave: **Behold you are** still **a maidservant and your** unborn **child is a freeman, if she was pregnant** at that time, then **she acquired** freedom **for** the unborn child.

With that established, the *gemara* presents a statement of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — appearing approximately fifteen lines from the top of the *amud* — which reads: *ein eved na'aseh shaliach l'kabel get isha miyad ba'alah*, a slave cannot serve as a *shaliach* to receive a bill of divorce on behalf of a woman from her husband's hand, *lefi she'eino b'torat gittin ve'kiddushin*, because a slave is not within the legal category of *gittin* and *kiddushin*.

The statement then continues: *ve'af al pi she'shaninu*, although we have taught that a person can give his female slave a *shtar shichrur* and say, in effect: you remain a slave, but your unborn child I am freeing — and this actually works. *Im hayta ubarah zachsa lo*: if she was pregnant, she takes possession of the writ of manumission on behalf of the child and the child is freed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אִם הָיְתָה עוּבָּרָה זָכְתָה לוֹ״? כִּי אֲתָא רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה, אָמַר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן תַּרְתֵּי אָמַר: נִרְאִים דְּבָרִים שֶׁהָעֶבֶד מְקַבֵּל גֵּט לַחֲבֵירוֹ – מִיָּד רַבּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא מִיַּד רַבּוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first clarifies: **What** is the connection between the initial statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan and the clause: **If she was pregnant** at that time, **she acquired** freedom **for** the unborn child? **When Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda came, he said: Rabbi Yoḥanan said two** distinct statements: The first statement was that a slave cannot be appointed as an agent to receive a bill of divorce for a woman from her husband, and the other was: **It appears that a slave can receive a bill of manumission for his fellow** slave **from the hand of his fellow's master, but not from the hand of his own master** if both of them are enslaved by the same person.

The *gemara* immediately asks: *mai im hayta ubarah zachsa lo*? How is this relevant to the preceding discussion? And more pointedly — how is she functioning here? Is she serving as a *shaliach* for her *ubar*, her fetus? If so, there would be no problem on the grounds of *gittin ve'kiddushin*, since a slave is certainly within the category of *shichrur* and could in principle receive a *shtar shichrur* for herself or for another. So why should this case pose any challenge at all?

The *gemara* proceeds to develop this question. The real concern is not the *bar hachi* problem of *gittin ve'kiddushin*, but something more fundamental: the question of *netinah* — the act of giving. A slave who belongs to a master cannot be a valid separate legal domain for the purposes of receiving a document from that same master. If the master gives a *shtar shichrur* to slave A in order for slave A to receive it on behalf of slave B — when both slaves are owned by the same master — it is as though the master took it from his right hand and placed it in his left. There is no genuine act of *netinah*, because slave A, even after accepting the *shtar shichrur*, remains within the master's domain.

This contrasts with the principle of *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad*: when the master frees his own slave, in that very act of giving, the slave simultaneously becomes an independent entity. The giving itself creates a new domain, and so the act of *netinah* is valid. But this only works when the slave receiving the document is the very slave being freed by that act. When the slave receiving the document is not the one being freed in that act, no such transformation occurs, and the document cannot be said to have been genuinely transferred.

So the question becomes sharp: when the master gives a *shtar shichrur* to his pregnant slave to free her unborn child, both the slave woman and the fetus are his property. He is not freeing her — only the fetus. Why then is this not considered giving from his right hand to his left? Where is the *ma'aseh netinah*?

### Two Explanations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לְחָשְׁךָ אָדָם לוֹמַר: זוֹ הֲלָכָה שְׁנוּיָה – אִם הָיְתָה עוּבָּרָה, זָכְתָה לוֹ! אֱמוֹר לוֹ: שְׁנֵי גְּדוֹלֵי הַדּוֹר פֵּירְשׁוּ אֶת הַדָּבָר – רַבִּי זֵירָא, וְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** **And if a person will whisper** a question **to you, saying: This** ruling, that a slave cannot receive a bill of manumission for his fellow slave from their common master, is difficult, as **a *halakha* was taught** that states the opposite: **If** a maidservant **was pregnant** at that time, then **she acquired** freedom **for** the unborn child, and the child and mother both belong to the same master, then **say to him** that **two greats of the generation** already **explained the matter,** and they are **Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: הַמְשַׁחְרֵר חֲצִי עַבְדּוֹ – קָנָה; וְחַד אָמַר: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי בְּהָא? קָסָבַר: עוּבָּר יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא, וְנַעֲשָׂה כְּמִי שֶׁהִקְנָה לָהּ אֶחָד מֵאֵבָרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **One** of them **said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who says:** With regard to **one who emancipates half of his slave,** the slave **acquires** freedom for half of himself, **and one** of them added an explanation and **said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **for this** ruling? **He holds: A fetus is** considered as **its mother's thigh,** i.e., a part of its mother's body, **and it is as though** the master **transferred** ownership of **one of her limbs to her.** Since the maidservant is pregnant, the child is considered to be a part of her, and it is as though he emancipated a portion of her body. Therefore, the mother is not acting as an agent for the child, and this *halakha* does not present a difficulty for Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion.

*Ki ata Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda amar Rabbi Yochanan tarti amar* — when Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda came and reported the teaching of Rabbi Yochanan, he clarified that Rabbi Yochanan actually said two things that were not directly connected to each other. The statement about a slave being unable to receive a *get* on behalf of a woman was one statement. The subsequent clause about the pregnant slave receiving a *shtar shichrur* for her fetus was not a challenge to it; it qualified a different earlier teaching — namely that a slave *can* serve as a *shaliach* to receive a *shtar shichrur* for a fellow slave from *another* master. The pregnant slave case is a separate ruling with its own logic, which then has to be explained.

*Emor lo shenei gedolei hador perishu et hadavar*, the *gemara* says: two of the great ones of the generation interpreted this matter. And who are they? Rabbi Zeira and Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak. *Chad amar* — one said one thing; *vechad amar* — the other offered a complementary explanation.

### Freeing Half

The first explanation: *ha mni Rabbi hi* — this ruling follows the position of Rabbi, who taught *hamishacher chetzi avdo kanah*, that one can free half of one's slave. The principle of *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad* applies even when the slave does not become a completely independent entity. One can renounce ownership over half the slave — not a specific limb necessarily, but a conceptual half — and because in that act of giving, half of the slave becomes free, there is enough of a transformation to constitute a valid *netinah* for that half.

### Fetus Separation

The second explanation builds on the first: *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi beha* — how would Rabbi's principle apply to the specific case of the pregnant slave? The answer is *ubar yerech imo hu*, the fetus is not considered a separate entity but is legally part of the mother. Therefore, when the master frees the fetus, it is as though he is freeing a specific limb or part of the slave woman herself — *denafek minei she'it lei leachad me'eivarav*. It is as though he said: your right arm is no longer mine; only your left arm remains in my service. So freeing the fetus is a kind of *chetzi eved* — freeing an identifiable part of the slave woman.

### Gitov Principle

This then produces enough of a *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad* dynamic: because in this very act of the slave woman receiving the *shtar shichrur*, some portion of her becomes her own entity — not the fetus physically taking hold of the document, but the conceptual reality that this act simultaneously partially frees her — there is sufficient of a separate domain created to constitute a genuine *netinah*. This is admittedly a *chiddush*: the separation is entirely conceptual, not physical.

To summarize the framework: a slave can serve as *shaliach* for something relevant to the slave's own legal status — yes for *shtar shichrur*, yes for *terumot u'maasrot*, but not for *gittin ve'kiddushin*. And a master cannot give a *shtar shichrur* through one of his own slaves to free another of his own slaves, because there is no genuine *ma'aseh netinah* — it is all within his domain. The exception is when he frees that slave directly, or frees half of that slave, or frees the fetus of that slave — in all of those cases the principle of *gitov veyado ba'im ke'echad* applies, and the act is valid.

## Women Witnesses

### Disqualified Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַף הַנָּשִׁים שֶׁאֵינָן נֶאֱמָנוֹת לוֹמַר ״מֵת בַּעְלָהּ״, נֶאֱמָנוֹת לְהָבִיא אֶת גִּיטָּהּ – חֲמוֹתָהּ, וּבַת חֲמוֹתָהּ, וְצָרָתָהּ, וִיבִמְתָּהּ, וּבַת בַּעְלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There are instances in which a woman's testimony that another woman's husband has died is not deemed credible (*Yevamot* 117a). If there is a presumption that due to their familial relationship the two women hate each other, there is concern that the woman is testifying falsely in order to harm the other woman. By doing so, she can cause the other woman to remarry. If her original husband then proves to be living, she will be required to leave her second husband. This mishna teaches: **Even the women who are not deemed credible to** testify on behalf of a woman and **say: Her husband died,** and she is permitted to remarry, **are deemed credible to bring her bill of divorce.** The relatives of the woman who are not deemed credible to testify that her husband has died are: **Her mother-in-law; and her mother-in-law's daughter; and her rival wife,** i.e., another wife of her husband's; **and her *yevama*,** i.e., her husband's brother's wife; **and her husband's daughter.**

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*, which introduces a new dimension of the question of who may deliver a *get*. The *mishnah* states: *af hanashim she'einam ne'emanot lomar met ba'alah* — certain women who are not believed to testify that another woman's husband has died are nevertheless *ne'emanot l'havi et gittah* — believed to deliver her *get*.

This is drawn from a broader discussion in Masechet Yevamot, where we accept the testimony of a single witness, including a woman, that a husband has died, allowing the wife to remarry. However, certain women are excluded from this leniency because we suspect they harbor animosity toward the wife and might falsely report the husband's death in order to bring disaster upon her — causing her to remarry while her husband is still alive.

The *mishnah* enumerates these women: *chamotah*, the mother-in-law; *u'vat chamotah*, the mother-in-law's daughter (the sister-in-law); *v'tzaratah*, the co-wife — obviously suspect; *v'yevamtah*, the husband's brother's wife — because if the husband dies without children, the husband's brother will perform *yibum* with this woman, making the existing wife a *tzarah* to her; and *u'vat ba'alah*, the stepdaughter.

All of these women are potentially suspect of wishing harm to the wife. Yet the *mishnah* says they are believed to bring a *get*.

### Self-Proving Doc

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה בֵּין גֵּט לְמִיתָה? שֶׁהַכְּתָב מוֹכִיחַ. הָאִשָּׁה עַצְמָהּ מְבִיאָה אֶת גִּיטָּהּ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁהִיא צְרִיכָה לוֹמַר: ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna explains: **What is** the difference **between a bill of divorce and death,** that certain women are deemed credible to testify about one but not the other? With regard to a bill of divorce, it is so **that the writing proves** that the husband is divorcing his wife, and the testimony is needed only to supplement the bill of divorce. Similarly, **the woman herself may bring her** own **bill of divorce, provided that she is required** by the court **to state** in its presence: **It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence,** as the Gemara will explain.

The *mishnah* then asks: *mah bein get l'mitah*? What is the difference between delivering a *get* and testifying about death? The answer is *she'haktav mochiach* — because here there is a signed writ, and we are not relying solely on their word. The document is signed by witnesses, and so we do not suspect the women of forgery. The document speaks for itself.

The *mishnah* further states that *ha'ishah atzmah mevi'ah gittah*, a woman may bring her own *get*, *v'hi shaliach l'atzmah* — she functions as a *shaliach* to herself — though the conceptual problem of *netinah* in that arrangement will be addressed shortly by the *gemara*. In any case, she must say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*.

This is itself somewhat puzzling: the declaration of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* is only required when bringing a *get* from a distant location. If the husband gave it to her directly, why would she need to say this? And if she is bringing it from elsewhere, how does she give it to herself? These questions will be explored by the *gemara* in the next section.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱמָנוֹת לוֹמַר ״מֵת בַּעְלָהּ״, כָּךְ אֵין נֶאֱמָנוֹת לְהָבִיא גִּיטָּהּ! אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָא קַשְׁיָא – כָּאן בָּאָרֶץ, כָּאן בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Just as** these women **are not deemed credible to say: Her husband died, so too, they are not deemed credible to bring her bill of divorce. Rav Yosef said:** It is **not difficult. Here,** this mishna is referring to a case that took place **in Eretz** Yisrael. **There,** the *baraita* is referring **to** a case that took place **outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

A *baraita* is then cited that seems to contradict the *mishnah*: *keshem she'eina ne'emanet lomar met ba'alah kach eina ne'emanet l'havi et gittah* — the very women who are not believed to testify about death are also not believed to deliver a *get*. This appears to directly contradict the *mishnah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאָרֶץ – דְּלָאו אַדִּיבּוּרַהּ דִּידַהּ קָא סָמְכִינַן, מְהֵימְנָא; בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – דְּאַדִּיבּוּרַהּ דִּידַהּ קָא סָמְכִינַן, לָא מְהֵימְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difference: In a case that takes place **in Eretz** Yisrael, **where,** to validate the bill of divorce **we do not rely on her statement** of: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, she serves only as an agent. Consequently, she is **deemed credible** to bring the bill of divorce. However, **in** a case that takes place **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **where we rely on her statement** of: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, and no one can contest the validity of the bill of divorce after her statement has been accepted, **she is not deemed credible,** as there is a concern that this woman may be intentionally lying in order to cause harm.

### Rav Yosef Position

Rav Yosef resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya kan b'Eretz kan b'Chutz la'Aretz* — it is not a difficulty. The two sources refer to different contexts. In *Eretz Yisrael*, where the *shaliach* does not need to declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, the woman is barely saying anything at all; the *get* speaks for itself. *B'Eretz d'lav adibura didah samchinan* — we are not relying on her testimony; the writ does the work. So the *mishnah* is correct that she is believed in *Eretz Yisrael*. In *Chutz la'Aretz*, however, *dibura didah ka samchinan* — we are relying on her declaration of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* to validate the *get*, and in that case she is not believed, as the *baraita* states.

### Abaye Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַדְּרַבָּה, אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא! בָּאָרֶץ, דְּאִי אָתֵי בַּעַל מְעַרְעַר – מַשְׁגְּחִינַן בֵּיהּ, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר לְקִלְקוּלָא קָא מִיכַּוְּונָה, לָא מְהֵימְנָא; בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, דְּאִי אָתֵי בַּעַל מְעַרְעַר – לָא מַשְׁגְּחִינַן בֵּיהּ, מְהֵימְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: On the contrary, the opposite is** more **reasonable,** and the distinction should be: **In Eretz** Yisrael, **where if** the **husband** were to **come and contest** the validity of the bill of divorce, **we** would **pay attention to him** and rule that they are not divorced, **where it could be said** that the woman who hates her **intends to** do her **harm** by having her remarry based on a bill of divorce that was later contested, **she is not deemed credible.** However, **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **where if** the **husband** were to **come and contest** the validity of the bill of divorce, **we** would **not pay attention to him,** she is **deemed credible,** as she does not have the power to make trouble for the other woman and cause her to have to leave her second husband.

Abaye challenged this: *ad raba, ipcha mistabra* — on the contrary, the opposite logic is more compelling. In *Eretz Yisrael*, precisely because there is no formal declaration of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, the wife remains vulnerable. If the husband were ever to contest the *get*, she would have difficulty validating it, and her situation could be ruined. *D'ika l'meimar l'kula ka michavna* — we might suspect that this hostile woman engineered the entire situation precisely to set the wife up for catastrophe. Therefore, in *Eretz Yisrael*, these women should *not* be believed. In *Chutz la'Aretz*, by contrast, once *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* has been declared, the husband cannot challenge the *get*. The wife is protected. So in *Chutz la'Aretz*, these women *are* believed — the woman is secure, and there is far less to be concerned about.

### Protection Via Bifnei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אִשָּׁה נֶאֱמֶנֶת לְהָבִיא גִּיטָּהּ – מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה נָשִׁים שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים אֵין נֶאֱמָנוֹת לוֹמַר ״מֵת בַּעְלָהּ״ – נֶאֱמָנוֹת לְהָבִיא גִּיטָּהּ; הִיא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמֶנֶת לוֹמַר ״מֵת בַּעְלָהּ״ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנֶּאֱמֶנֶת לְהָבִיא גִּיטָּהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that it **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Abaye** (*Tosefta* 2:6): **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of Rabbi Akiva: A woman is deemed credible to bring her** own **bill of divorce through an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as women** about **whom the Sages said:** They **are not deemed credible to say: Her husband died, are** nevertheless **deemed credible to bring her bill of divorce,** with regard to the woman herself, **who is deemed credible to say** that **her husband died, is it not right that she is deemed credible to bring her** own **bill of divorce?**

A *baraita* is then cited: *tanya k'vatei d'Abaye* — the ruling follows Abaye's reasoning, establishing that the problem is specifically in *Eretz Yisrael*, where the wife is left vulnerable and susceptible to having her life ruined by a hostile *shaliach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמָּקוֹם שֶׁבָּאתָ, מָה לְהַלָּן צְרִיכוֹת שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ: ״בְּפָנֵינוּ נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנֵינוּ נֶחְתַּם״, אַף הִיא צְרִיכָה שֶׁתֹּאמַר: ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from the place that you came,** i.e., from this inference itself, one establishes: **Just as there,** those who bring the bill of divorce **must say: It was written in our presence and it was signed in our presence, so too, she** herself **must say: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence.** This *baraita* must be referring to a case in which the wife brought the bill of divorce from outside of Eretz Yisrael, as it is only then that she must state that it was written and signed in her presence. Therefore, the *baraita* supports the opinion of Abaye.

Rav Ashi then adds that the *mishnah* itself supports Abaye, through close reading. The *mishnah* specifies that a woman bringing her own *get* must say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, which means the entire context of the *mishnah* — including the ruling about these potentially hostile women — is one of delivery from *Chutz la'Aretz*. The *mishnah* is therefore discussing the case where these women are *believed*, namely when they bring a *get* from outside the land and make the declaration that protects the wife. *Shma mina*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: הָאִשָּׁה עַצְמָהּ מְבִיאָה גִּיטָּהּ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא צְרִיכָה לוֹמַר כּוּ׳; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: The mishna is also precisely** formulated in a way that supports Abaye's opinion, **as it teaches: The woman herself may bring her** own **bill of divorce, provided that she is required** by the court **to say:** It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence. **Learn from** the mishna that it is referring to a case outside of Eretz Yisrael, as Abaye explained.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף, רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, מְצִיעֲתָא בָּאָרֶץ?! אִין; רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, מְצִיעֲתָא בָּאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **Rav Yosef,** is it possible to say that **the first clause** of the mishna (23a): Anyone is fit to serve as an agent to bring a bill of divorce, **and the last clause** of the mishna (23b): The woman herself may bring her own bill of divorce, discuss a case that takes place **in** a location **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, and **the middle clause:** Even the women who are not deemed credible, discusses a case that takes place **in Eretz** Yisrael? The Gemara answers: **Yes, the first clause and the last clause** discuss a case that takes place **in** a location **outside of Eretz** Yisrael, but **the middle clause** discusses a case that takes place **in Eretz** Yisrael.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָתָנֵי: מָה בֵּין גֵּט לְמִיתָה – שֶׁהַכְּתָב מוֹכִיחַ; וְלָא קָתָנֵי: שֶׁהַכְּתָב וּפֶה מוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **From where** does Rav Yosef infer this? **From** the fact **that it teaches** in the mishna: **What is** the difference **between a bill of divorce and death,** that certain women are deemed credible to testify about one but not the other? With regard to a bill of divorce, it is so **that the writing proves** that the husband is divorcing his wife. The mishna does not teach **that the writing and statement prove** this. Consequently, the statement of: It was written in my presence, is not needed. This indicates that the mishna is discussing a case that takes place in Eretz Yisrael.

As for Rav Yosef — who held that the *mishnah*'s leniency applies only to *Eretz Yisrael* — one might ask how he would handle the fact that the opening case in the *mishnah* (about a *suma* who cannot bring a *get*) and the closing case (a woman bringing her own *get*) both clearly concern *Chutz la'Aretz* and *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, while the middle section (these hostile women) would on his reading concern *Eretz Yisrael*. His answer: *reisha ve-seifa b'chutz la'aretz, metzi'asa ba-aretz* — yes, the beginning and end discuss *Chutz la'Aretz*, the middle discusses *Eretz Yisrael*. This is a possible reading, though the evidence overwhelmingly favors Abaye's position that the entire *mishnah* is set in a *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* context, i.e., *Chutz la'Aretz*. The phrase *mipnei she'haktav mochiach* — that the writ itself testifies — is what pushed Rav Yosef toward his reading; to him it implies that the woman is saying nothing at all and only the document speaks, pointing to *Eretz Yisrael*. But the *gemara*'s evidence tips decisively toward Abaye.

### Shimon Ben Elazar

The *baraita* cites a teaching of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar in the name of Rabbi Akiva: *ishah ne'emenet l'havi gittah mi'kal vachomer* — a woman may bring her own *get* by means of a *kal vachomer*. The argument runs: women who are not believed to say that this woman's husband died are nonetheless believed to deliver her *get*. Should it not follow by *kal vachomer* that a woman who *is* believed to testify that her own husband died should certainly be believed to deliver her own *get*? This establishes the wife's credibility as her own *shaliach*.

But then the *gemara* applies the limiting principle *mimakom shebata* — you cannot derive a conclusion that is stronger than its source. Since the *kal vachomer* is built on the case where those hostile women bring a *get* with the declaration *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, the same requirement must apply when the wife brings her own *get*: *tzricha shetomar bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*. And this, in turn, confirms that we are speaking throughout the *mishnah* in the context of delivery from *Chutz la'Aretz* — supporting Abaye's position.

## Woman Delivers Own Get

### Self-Agency Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה עַצְמָהּ מְבִיאָה וְכוּ׳: אִשָּׁה, מִכִּי מָטֵי גִּיטָּהּ לְיָדָהּ אִיגָּרַשָׁה לַהּ! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּאוֹמֵר ״לֹא תִּתְגָּרְשִׁי בּוֹ אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין פְּלוֹנִי״. סוֹף סוֹף, כִּי מָטְיָא הָתָם – אִיגָּרַשָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **the woman herself may bring** her own bill of divorce and state that it was written and signed in her presence. The Gemara asks: Why does she need to bring it and testify that it was written and signed in her presence? With regard to this **woman, once her bill of divorce reaches her hand, she is divorced. Rav Huna says:** This mishna is referring **to one who says** to his wife: **You will be divorced through it only in the presence of such and such court,** and the divorce does not take effect when she receives the bill of divorce. The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, once she arrives there,** to that court, **she is** immediately **divorced through it,** as she has fulfilled the condition set forth by her husband. Why, then, is it necessary for her to bring the bill of divorce and to testify?

The *gemara* now turns to the conceptual puzzle raised by the *mishnah*'s ruling that *ha'ishah atzmah mevi'ah gittah* — a woman can bring her own *get*. The problem is this: *isha, mikdei mati gitta l'yada, igrashah lah* — the moment the *get* reaches her hand, she is divorced. How, then, can she simultaneously function as a *shaliach* to deliver something to herself? The instant of delivery and the instant of receiving are the same moment, and more fundamentally, the *shaliach* and the recipient are the same person.

### Rav Huna Solution

Rav Huna offers an initial answer: *be'omer lo titgarshi bo ela bifnei beit din ploni* — the husband stipulates that the divorce should not take effect until she presents it before a specific *beit din* in another city. So she must travel to, say, Miami, and the divorce takes effect only upon her arrival there. This creates an interval during which she is a *shaliach* transporting the *get* without yet being divorced.

### Ground Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: דְּאָמַר לַהּ: כִּי מָטֵית הָתָם, אַתְנְחֵיהּ אַאַרְעָא וְשִׁקְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ said in the name of Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika:** This mishna is referring to a case **where he said to her: When you arrive there, place** the bill of divorce **on the ground and take it.** Consequently, the divorce does not take effect immediately upon her arrival.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הֲוָה לֵיהּ: ״טְלִי גִּיטִּיךְ מֵעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע״, וְאָמַר רָבָא: ״טְלִי גִּיטִּיךְ מֵעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If that is so, isn't it** like the case where he said to his wife: **Take your bill of divorce from off the ground, and Rava says:** If a husband says to his wife: **Take your bill of divorce from off the ground,** then it is as though **he said nothing.** He is not considered to have given her a bill of divorce; rather, she has taken it on her own.

But the *gemara* presses: even under that formulation, she is still not a conventional *shaliach* to herself; the moment she arrives and the divorce takes effect, she is simultaneously *shaliach* and recipient. Moreover, why does she need to say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* at all if the husband gave it to her directly?

The *gemara* then considers a different approach attributed to Rav Huna bar Manoach in the name of Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika. He suggests the following formulation: the husband instructs her to travel to a designated place, and when she arrives, to place the *get* on the ground — *anachi a'ara* — and then pick it up. The idea is that she, in her capacity as *shaliach*, puts the *get* down; then, in her capacity as the wife, she picks it up. The problem is immediately apparent: this is not an act of *netinah*. She gave it to the ground, not to herself. The *gemara* invokes the teaching of Rava: *tali gittech mei'al gabei karka lo amar klum* — if a husband says to his wife, "Pick up your *get* from the ground," he has said nothing. That is not a valid act of giving. The same logic applies here.

### Status Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר לַהּ: הֱוַי שָׁלִיחַ לְהוֹלָכָה עַד דְּמָטְיַת הָתָם, וְכִי מָטְיַת הָתָם הֱוַי שָׁלִיחַ לְִקַבָּלָה, וְקַבִּלִי אַתְּ גִּיטִּיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the mishna should be explained as follows: This is referring to a case **where he said to** his wife: **Be** my **agent for delivery** of the bill of divorce **until you arrive there. And when you arrive there, be** your own **agent for receipt, and receive your bill of divorce** as an agent.

The *gemara* therefore arrives at the formulation of Rav Huna bar Manoach: *hevei shaliach l'holacha ad demati hatam, ki mati hatam hevei shaliach l'kabala v'kabli at gittech* — be a *shaliach l'holacha* for transport until you arrive there, and when you arrive, your status changes: you will become a *shaliach l'kablah*, and you will receive your *get*. The Rashba notes that the husband need not literally appoint her as *shaliach l'kablah*; she simply becomes herself. But the formulation is stated in parallel terms to clarify the conceptual structure.

The key moment is this: she is holding the *get* as the husband's agent. At the designated place, at the designated moment, her identity shifts — she transitions from holding the document as *shaliach* to holding it as the wife. That transition itself, that change of legal identity while holding the document, constitutes the act of *netinah*. It is entirely conceptual and in no way physical, but that is precisely the point: she went from holding it in one capacity to holding it in another, and that is sufficient to constitute giving.

It is because of this conceptual act of *netinah* that she must say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* — she is, after all, bringing it from one jurisdiction to another, and the declaration is required.

### Agency Survival

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לֹא חָזְרָה שְׁלִיחוּת אֵצֶל הַבַּעַל! דְּאָמַר לַהּ: הֱוַי שָׁלִיחַ לְהוֹלָכָה עַד דְּמָטְיַת הָתָם, וְכִי מָטְיַת הָתָם שַׁוַּי שָׁלִיחַ לְִקַבָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But the agency has not returned to the husband.** In other words, the first agency, where she acted as the agent for delivery, has not ended, because an agent must have the ability to complete his involvement in the act, return to the person who appointed him, and inform him that the agency has been carried out. In this case, once she arrives at the court, her agency ends when she assumes the role of the recipient of the bill of divorce, and her involvement does not end. Therefore, the appointment of the agent itself is deficient, and the divorce should not take effect. Rather, the mishna should be explained that he **said to her: Be an agent for delivery until you arrive there, and when you arrive there, appoint an agent for receipt** on your behalf and give him the bill of divorce.

The *gemara*, however, objects: *veha lo hazra shelichut eitzel ba'al* — the *shelichut* did not return to the husband. The principle implied here, articulated in the manner Rashi and Tosafot explain it, is that for genuine *shelichut* to exist, the agent's identity must survive beyond the act itself. Theoretically, the agent should be able to return to the principal and report: "I completed what you instructed me to do." In this case, however, the only way she can complete the act is by ceasing to be the husband's agent. Her capacity as *shaliach* is extinguished at the very moment of execution. Since the *shelichut* cannot survive the act, it cannot properly be said to have functioned as a valid agency in the first place.

### Intermediary Solutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִשָּׁה עוֹשָׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל גִּיטָּהּ מִיָּד שְׁלִיחַ בַּעְלָהּ. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה עוֹשָׂה שָׁלִיחַ לְקַבֵּל גִּיטָּהּ מִיָּד שְׁלִיחַ בַּעֲלָהּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who said: A woman can appoint an agent to receive her bill of divorce from the hand of the agent of her husband,** and it is not required that she receive the bill of divorce herself. **However, according to the one who says: A woman cannot appoint an agent to receive her bill of divorce from the hand of the agent of her husband, what can be said?**

Given this difficulty, the *gemara* explores alternative approaches. One suggestion: when she arrives at the destination, she should appoint someone else as her *shaliach l'kablah* to receive the *get* from her. This way she transfers it as the husband's *shaliach*, and a separate person receives it on her behalf as wife — two distinct parties, two distinct roles, a genuine act of *netinah*.

This, however, immediately raises another issue: *hanicha leman de'amar isha osa shaliach l'kablah miyad shaliach ba'al* — this works only if a woman can appoint a *shaliach l'kablah* to receive a *get* from her husband's *shaliach*. And that is itself a matter of debate, which the *gemara* will now address in the following section.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא מַאי – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא בִּזָּיוֹן דְּבַעַל, וְהָכָא בַּעַל לָא קָפֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What is the reason** of the one who said that the woman cannot appoint an agent to receive her bill of divorce from the agent of her husband? **Because there is degradation of the husband** in doing this, as he wishes to give the bill of divorce directly to the woman and not to an agent. **And here the husband is not particular** about the matter, as he instructed her to do so.

Alternatively — and the *gemara* presents this as well — she is instructed: when you arrive, say *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* before a *beit din*, and then the *beit din* will appoint a second *shaliach l'holacha* to carry it further and ultimately hand it to her. A *beit din* functions as an institutional intermediary, which is in fact a practical arrangement used in real cases: when a *shaliach* cannot complete delivery, the *shaliach* transfers responsibility to a *beit din*, which then arranges a replacement. In both solutions, the key move is that a second party becomes involved so that she is not simultaneously the agent delivering and the person receiving.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: מִשּׁוּם בִּזָּיוֹן דְּבַעַל; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: מִשּׁוּם חֲצֵרָהּ הַבָּאָה לְאַחַר מִיכָּן, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **This works out well according to the one who said:** The reason for this *halakha* is **due to** the concern about the **degradation of the husband. But according to the one who says** that this *halakha* is a decree **due to** the case of **her courtyard that comes afterward, what can be said?** Some say that the reason for the *halakha* that the woman cannot appoint an agent to receive the bill of divorce from the agent of her husband is that there was a concern that if she were able to do so, then the courts may eventually allow her to be divorced by purchasing a courtyard into which her husband had placed the bill of divorce. In the latter case, the divorce does not take effect. The courtyard needs to be an extension of the hand of the woman, into which the husband places the bill of divorce, but it does not act as her agent. In any event, according to this opinion, the fact that the husband is not particular does not prevent this *halakha* from applying, as it does not depend on him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר לַהּ: הֱוַי שָׁלִיחַ לְהוֹלָכָה עַד דְּמָטְיַת הָתָם, וְכִי מָטְיַת הָתָם שַׁוַּי שָׁלִיחַ לְהוֹלָכָה, וְקַבִּלִי אַתְּ גִּיטִּיךְ מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: According to this opinion, the mishna should be explained as discussing a case **when he said to her: Be an agent for delivery** of this bill of divorce **until you arrive there, and when you arrive there, appoint** another **agent for delivery, and receive your bill of divorce from him.**

The *gemara* therefore proposes an alternative approach for this second opinion. The husband instructs her: be a *shaliach l'holacha* until you arrive at the destination, and when you arrive, appoint a second *shaliach l'holacha* to replace you. You then receive the *get* — as yourself, the wife — from that second *shaliach l'holacha*. In this formulation, the wife is not appointing a *shaliach l'kablah* to receive from the husband's *shaliach*; rather, she transfers her role as *shaliach l'holacha* to a second party, and then separately receives the *get* as the wife from that second party. This avoids the problem regardless of which rationale underlies the restrictive opinion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, דְּאָמַר לַהּ: הֱוַי שָׁלִיחַ לְהוֹלָכָה עַד דְּמָטְיַת הָתָם, וְכִי מָטְיַת הָתָם אֵימַר קַמֵּי בֵּי דִינָא ״בְּפָנַי נִכְתַּב וּבְפָנַי נֶחְתַּם״, וּמְשַׁוֵּי בֵּי דִּינָא שָׁלִיחַ, וְלִיתְּבוּהּ נִיהֲלִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that **he said to her: Be an agent for delivery until you arrive there, and when you arrive there, say before the court: It was written in my presence and it was signed in my presence, and you should** then **appoint the court as an agent, and they will give** the bill of divorce **to you.**

The *gemara* adds: *v'ei ba'it eima* — alternatively, she may go before a *beit din*, declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam*, and transfer the task to the *beit din*, which then appoints a new *shaliach l'holacha* to deliver the *get* to her. Tosafot notes that this is essentially a variation on the same structure. But the principle is the same: by bringing in a *beit din* as the institutional intermediary, the chain is broken into two clear stages, and a genuine act of *netinah* from a distinct party to the wife can take place.

The conceptual elegance of the solution — that the wife's mere change of legal status while holding the *get* could constitute an act of *netinah* — ultimately does not survive the *gemara*'s scrutiny, because of the *shelichut* survival problem. In the end, the *gemara* requires a second party to be present so that there is an actual giving from one domain to another.

## Lishmah Requirement

### Four Invalid Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ הַמֵּבִיא גֵּט
>
> **Translation:** 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל גֵּט שֶׁנִּכְתַּב שֶׁלֹּא לְשׁוּם אִשָּׁה, פָּסוּל. כֵּיצַד, הָיָה עוֹבֵר בַּשּׁוּק וְשָׁמַע קוֹל סוֹפְרִים מַקְרִין: ״אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי מְגָרֵשׁ אֶת פְּלוֹנִית מִמָּקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי״, וְאָמַר: זֶה שְׁמִי וְזֶה שֵׁם אִשְׁתִּי; פָּסוּל לְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Any bill of divorce that was not written for the sake of** a specific **woman** is **invalid. How so?** In a case of a man who **was passing through the marketplace and heard the sound of scribes** who write bills of divorce **dictating** the text to their students: The **man so-and-so divorces so-and-so from the place** of **such and such; and** the man **said: This is my name and that is the name of my wife,** and he wishes to use this bill for his divorce, this bill is **unfit** for him **to divorce** his wife **with it,** as it was not written for the sake of any woman.

With *Hadran alach hameivi get* — the conclusion of the second *perek* — we now enter the third *perek*, which addresses the criteria for the writing of a *get* itself, centering on the requirement of *lishmah*. This is thematically apt: according to Rabba, the very purpose of *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* was to ensure *lishmah* — that the *get* was written specifically for this man and this woman. The third *perek* thus deepens that theme directly.

The *mishnah* states: *kol get shenichtav shelo l'shem ishah pasul* — any *get* written not for the sake of the specific woman is invalid. The *mishnah* then presents four cases.

### Practice Case

The first: *haya over bashuk veshamah kol sofrim makrin* — one was walking in the marketplace and heard the sound of a scribal school, where scribes were being instructed by their teacher reciting aloud: so-and-so divorces so-and-so from such-and-such a place. The student scribes were practicing by writing out this text. Now, a man hears this and recognizes that the names being called out are his name and his wife's name. May he take this *get* and use it to divorce his wife? *Pasul* — it is invalid, for it was not written for him and his wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר מִיכֵּן – כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְנִמְלַךְ, מְצָאוֹ בֶּן עִירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ: שְׁמִי כְּשִׁמְךָ וְשֵׁם אִשְׁתִּי כְּשֵׁם אִשְׁתְּךָ; פָּסוּל לְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover,** if one **wrote** a bill of divorce with which **to divorce his wife but** later **reconsidered,** and **a resident of his town found him and said to him: My name is** the same **as your name, and my wife's name is** the same **as your wife's name,** and we reside in the same town; give me the bill of divorce and I will use it; the bill of divorce is **unfit** for the second man **to divorce** his wife **with it.**

Rava clarifies: *besofrim ha'asuyin lehitlamed askinan* — we are dealing with a scribal school practicing. They were not writing this *get* for anyone in particular; it was purely practice. Rav Ashi adds a textual proof: the *mishnah* says *sofrim makrin* — someone was *reciting* to them — not that the scribes themselves were writing on their own initiative. The teacher dictated; they wrote as practice. *Shma mina*.

### Discarded Get

The second case: a man wrote a *get* with intent to divorce his wife and then changed his mind and discarded it. Another man in the same city happens to share the same name, and his wife shares the same name as the first man's wife. He finds the discarded *get* and proposes using it. *Pasul legaresh es ishto* — invalid. Even though this *get* was written for the genuine purpose of *gerushin* — not mere practice — it was not written for *this* man and *this* woman.

### Wrong Wife

The third case: a man has two wives, both with the same name. He wrote a *get* to divorce his elder wife. *Lo yegaresh bo es haketanah* — he may not use it to divorce the younger. Even though the *get* was written for genuine *gerushin*, for him, the correct names appear — still, it was written for wife number one and cannot be used for wife number two.

### Unspecified Wife

The fourth case: the man instructs the *lavlar* — the scribe — to write a *get* saying: I will divorce whichever wife I choose. He leaves it open-ended, intending to decide later which wife to divorce with it. *Pasul legaresh bo* — this too is invalid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר מִיכֵּן – הָיוּ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים וּשְׁמוֹתֵיהֶן שָׁווֹת, כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה – לֹא יְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover,** if **one had two wives and their names were identical,** and he **wrote** a bill of divorce **to divorce the older** one and then reconsidered, **he may not divorce the younger** one **with it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר מִיכֵּן – אָמַר לְלַבְלָר: ״כְּתוֹב, לְאֵיזוֹ שֶׁאֶרְצֶה אֲגָרֵשׁ״ – פָּסוּל לְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover,** if **he said to the scribe: Write** a bill of divorce **for whichever** one of them **that I will want** and **I will divorce** her with it, this bill of divorce is **unfit** for him **to divorce** either wife **with it.**

### Ketav Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְנִמְלַךְ וְכוּ׳: וְאֶלָּא רֵישָׁא בְּמַאי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The second clause of the mishna considers a case where one **wrote** a bill of divorce with which **to divorce his wife but** later **reconsidered,** and a resident of his town with identical personal details found him and desired to use the bill of divorce. The Gemara asks: **But** then **with what** case is the **first clause** of the mishna, where a man discovers that a scribe had written a bill of divorce with identical personal details to his own, dealing; isn't that also a case of a bill of divorce that was written for someone else? Why did the *tanna* cite two seemingly identical cases?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּסוֹפְרִין הָעֲשׂוּיִין לְהִתְלַמֵּד עָסְקִינַן. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי ״סוֹפְרִין מַקְרִין״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״סוֹפְרִים קוֹרְאִין״; שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: We are dealing with scribes who are practicing** writing; the bill of divorce in the first case was written as an exercise and not because someone requested that it be written. **Rav Ashi said:** The language of the mishna **is also precise, as it teaches: Scribes dictating,** i.e., to their students, **and it does not teach: Scribes reading** the names on their own. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from** this phrasing that this is the correct understanding of the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״יָתֵר מִיכֵּן״? תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: לֹא זֶה – שֶׁנִּכְתַּב שֶׁלֹּא לְשׁוּם גֵּירוּשִׁין; אֶלָּא אַף זֶה – שֶׁנִּכְתַּב לְשׁוּם גֵּירוּשִׁין, פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** By employing the introductory term: Moreover, the mishna indicates that each case teaches an additional novelty beyond that of the previous case. The Gemara asks: **What** novel element warrants the use of the term: **Moreover?** The Gemara answers by quoting a *baraita*: **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that **not** only **this** bill of divorce, which was written for practice and **which was not written for the sake of divorce,** is unfit, **but even that** bill of divorce, **which was written for the sake of divorce** but the husband then reconsidered and did not use it, is **unfit** to be used by someone else.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא זֶה – שֶׁנִּכְתַּב שֶׁלֹּא לְשׁוּם גֵּירוּשִׁין דִּידֵיהּ; אֶלָּא אַף זֶה – שֶׁנִּכְתַּב לְשׁוּם גֵּירוּשִׁין דִּידֵיהּ, פָּסוּל. וְלֹא זֶה – שֶׁלֹּא נִכְתַּב לְשׁוּם גֵּירוּשִׁין הָא; אֶלָּא אַף זֶה – שֶׁנִּכְתַּב לְשׁוּם גֵּירוּשִׁין הָא, פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **And not** only **this** bill of divorce, **which was not written for the sake of his own divorce,** is unfit, **but even that** bill of divorce, **which was written for the sake of his own divorce,** albeit for a particular wife, is **unfit** to be used for divorcing his other wife. **And not** only **this** bill of divorce, **which was not written for the sake of this** wife's **divorce, but even that** bill of divorce, **which was written for this** wife's **divorce,** as he instructed the scribe to write the bill of divorce for the sake of whichever wife he decides to divorce, is **unfit** to use in divorce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִי כְּתַב ״וְנָתַן סֵפֶר כְּרִיתוּת בְּיָדָהּ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְמַעוֹטֵי הַאיְךְ קַמָּא, דְּלָא עֲבִיד לְשׁוּם כְּרִיתוּת; אֲבָל כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְנִמְלַךְ, דַּעֲבִיד לְשׁוּם כְּרִיתוּת – אֵימָא כָּשֵׁר; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְכָתַב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that a bill of divorce must be written for the sake of the woman who is being divorced? It is stated in the verse that deals with divorce: "And he writes for her a scroll of severance and gives it in her hand" (Deuteronomy 24:1). This teaches the following: **If** the Merciful One had **written** only: **And he gives a scroll of severance in her hand, I would say** that the verse serves **to exclude this** case of the **first** clause of the mishna, **where it was done not for the sake of severance,** as the scribe wrote the bill of divorce as a mere exercise; **however,** if one **wrote** a bill of divorce **to divorce his wife but** then **reconsidered, where** it was **done for the sake of severance,** I would **say** that it is **valid** for another man to use for divorcing his wife. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah: **"And he writes,"** meaning that the bill of divorce must be written exclusively for the sake of his own divorce.

The *gemara* presents a *tana devei Rabbi Yishmael* who articulates the escalating logic of these four cases. Not only is the one written by the scribal school invalid — for it was not written *l'shem gerushin* at all — but even one written genuinely for divorce purposes is invalid if it was written for a *different* man. And not only that, but even one written for the right man is invalid if written for the *wrong wife*. And not only that, but even one written for the right man without specifying which of his wives is also invalid — even when it is not written for a specific wrong wife but simply left open.

*Mai ta'ama*? The *gemara* explains. Had the Torah simply said to deliver a *sefer keritut*, the only requirement would have been that it function as an instrument of divorce. A *get* written for a different person would still qualify, since it was written as a genuine *get*. *Kasav Rachmana vekasav* — the Torah's formulation, "and he shall write" (*vekasav lah*), teaches that the writing must be *for him* — *l'shem* the specific *megaresh*. This excludes a *get* written by someone else for another person. And *lah* — "for her" — teaches that it must be written for this specific woman, excluding the case where a man wrote it for his elder wife and now wishes to use it for his younger.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְכָתַב״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לְמַעוֹטֵי הַאי, דְּלָא אִיהוּ קָא כָתֵיב לַהּ; אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים, דְּאִיהוּ קָא כָתֵיב לַהּ – אֵימָא כָּשֵׁר; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא: ״לָהּ״ – לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the explanation: **If the Merciful One** had **written** only: **And he writes, I would say** that the verse serves **to exclude this** case mentioned previously, **where** the man using the bill of divorce **was not the one who wrote it; but** if a man **has two wives** with the same name, so **that** the man using the bill of divorce **is writing it, say** that it is a **valid** bill of divorce. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes** in the Torah: "And he writes **for her,"** teaching that a bill of divorce must be written **for the sake of** a specific wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסֵיפָא לְמָה לִי? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּאֵין בְּרֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need **the last clause** of the mishna, which deals with a man who writes a bill of divorce for whichever wife he later chooses? The earlier clauses made clear that one must write the bill of divorce for the sake of the woman who is being divorced. The Gemara answers: **This teaches us that there is no** retroactive **clarification,** i.e., that one does not say that since he gave the bill of divorce to this wife, it is clarified retroactively that he had written the bill of divorce for her sake; rather, he must write it for her sake from the outset.

As for the open-ended case — *le'eizo she'ertzeh agaresh bo* — the *gemara* explains this as *ein bereirah*: one cannot leave a legal act undefined and allow a later determination to retroactively fix what the act was "for." The *lah* requirement demands clarity *at the time of writing*.

Tosafot adds an important qualification: this principle applies here even according to opinions that generally accept *yesh bereirah* in other legal contexts — for instance, the retroactive designation of *terumot u'maasrot*. Even those who would normally permit later determination agree that here it is invalid, *mishum de'vekasav lah lishmah mashma sheyehe mevurar besha'as kesivah* — because the requirement of *lah*, of writing specifically for *her*, implies that it must be definitively clear at the moment of writing for whom the *get* is being written.

One may note that this entire analysis of *lishmah* follows the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who places emphasis on the *ketivah* — the act of writing. According to Rabbi Meir, *vekasav* is understood to refer to *vechasam*, the signing, and as long as the signing reflects the proper parties, the writing itself is less critical. This explains why the *stam mishnah* here, with its focus on *lishmah* requirements in writing, follows Rabbi Eliezer — while an earlier *mishnah* permitting a *cheresh shoteh ve'katan* to write a *get* reflected Rabbi Meir's position. This juxtaposition itself prompted the *gemara* to explore ways of reconciling those two *mishnayot*.

## Ambiguous Names

### Name Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתַב לְגָרֵשׁ אֶת הַגְּדוֹלָה – לֹא יְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ אֶת הַקְּטַנָּה: קְטַנָּה הוּא דְּלָא מָצֵי מְגָרֵשׁ בֵּיהּ, הָא גְּדוֹלָה – מָצֵי מְגָרֵשׁ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If he **wrote** a bill of divorce **to divorce the older** of his two wives and then reconsidered, he **may not divorce the younger** wife **with it,** even though the two wives share the same name. The Gemara deduces from this: **It is the younger** one **whom he is not able to divorce with it, but he is able to divorce the older** wife **with it,** as it was written for her from the outset. This is the case even though the younger wife would be able to collect payment of her marriage contract and remarry if she were to present this bill of divorce, as the court would think it was written for her.

The *mishnah* states that a man may write a *get* for his elder wife and validly use it to divorce her — *hagedolah matzi megaresh bah*. This assumes that even though both wives share the same name, the *get* is nonetheless valid for the one it was written for. This immediately raises a broader question: if two people in the same city share the name of the husband, and two people share the name of the wife, is a *get* with those names sufficient? Is it valid if the document itself does not unambiguously identify which parties are intended?

This question carries particular weight according to Rabbi Meir, for whom *edei chasimah karti* — the witnesses who sign the *get* are the ones who, in effect, validate the divorce — and the *get* must also function as independent *edut*, testimony. If the testimony is ambiguous — if we cannot know from the document itself which parties are referred to — perhaps the *get* is inherently defective.

### Rava's Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, שְׁנֵי יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן הַדָּרִין בְּעִיר אַחַת – מוֹצִיאִין שְׁטַר חוֹב עַל אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: That is to say** that one of **two** people with identical names, e.g., **Yosef ben Shimon, who live in one city, can present a promissory note** to claim a debt from **others,** and the borrower cannot claim that the promissory note was written for the other Yosef ben Shimon.

Rava draws a parallel to the laws of debt. If two people named Yosef ben Shimon live in the same city, and a creditor holds a *shtar chov* — a debt instrument — that says Yosef ben Shimon owes a certain sum, the creditor cannot collect from the *wrong* Yosef ben Shimon. The instrument alone does not establish which one is meant.

### Holder Identity

Yet the answer in the *get* context is that ambiguity is resolved by the identity of the party *holding* the document. If the woman is holding the *get*, then her name is established by her possession. The name in the *get* that identifies the husband is then necessarily the name of her husband — the man who was married to *her*. So even if there are two men with the same name in the city, the fact that she is holding the *get* identifies which man it refers to: by definition, it refers to her husband.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, רֵישָׁא דְּקָתָנֵי: שְׁמִי כְּשִׁמְךָ – פָּסוּל לְגָרֵשׁ בּוֹ; שֵׁנִי הוּא דְּלָא מָצֵי מְגָרֵשׁ בֵּיהּ, הָא רִאשׁוֹן מָצֵי מְגָרֵשׁ בֵּיהּ?! וְהָא אָמְרִינַן: ״וְלֹא אַחֵר יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא עֲלֵיהֶן שְׁטַר חוֹב״!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: If that is so,** then according to your reasoning, from **the earlier clause** of the mishna **that teaches** that if one man requests a bill of divorce from another, saying: **My name is** the same **as your name,** the bill of divorce is **unfit** for the second person **to divorce** his wife **with it,** one could infer that the **second** man **is** the one **who is not able to divorce with it, but** the **first** man **is able to divorce with it** even though they have the same names. **But didn't we say** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 172a): With regard to two people with identical names, neither of them can present a promissory note to the other, **and another** person **cannot present a promissory note to them,** as each one can deny that he is the one who owes the money? Here too, there is a concern lest the wife of the other use the bill of divorce to collect payment of her marriage contract even though the bill of divorce was not written for her.

Abaye, however, challenges this resolution with a pointed question. In the second case of the *mishnah* — where a man found a discarded *get* whose names matched his own and his wife's — we ruled that *get* invalid: *sheni hu de-lo matzi megaresh bah, ha-rishon matzi megaresh bah*. The first man could use it, the second cannot. But this seems inconsistent with Rava's principle. If the holder's identity resolves ambiguity, why can't the second man use the discarded *get*? He is holding it, and his wife's name matches — should that not settle which party is meant?

Moreover, Abaye notes: we said that when two people share the same name, one cannot use a *shtar chov* to collect from the other — *veha amrinan de-lo acher yakhol lehotzi aleihem shtar chov*. That is, a third party named Michael cannot present a debt instrument saying Yosef ben Shimon owes money and collect from either Yosef ben Shimon. But when *Yosef ben Shimon himself* holds it, the identity of the relevant Yosef ben Shimon is clear. In the *get* case, when a woman holds a *get* and the name of the husband appears in it, the question is which husband is meant. Unlike a *shtar chov*, where the holder's identity resolves the question of the debtor, in a *get* it is not the *holder* whose identity is in question — it is the *other party*, the husband. Having the wife hold the document does not by itself tell us which husband she is referring to.

Abaye's challenge is, as the *gemara* acknowledges, somewhat striking. After all, if we know who the wife is, we necessarily know who her husband is — at least in a case where she has only one husband. But Abaye is pressing on the general principle.

### Edei Mesirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר? בְּעֵדֵי מְסִירָה, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what have you to say** to explain how the first man can divorce his wife with this bill of divorce, and how it can be used as a reliable proof of divorce? One can say that the ruling of the mishna applies **in** a case where there are **witnesses who observe the transmission** of the bill of divorce, and they confirm in court which woman was given the bill of divorce. **And this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** who holds that presence of witnesses at the transmission of the bill of divorce is essential to its taking effect, so there is no concern that the wife of the other man will claim payment of her marriage contract.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי – בְּעֵדֵי מְסִירָה, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** in the case of two wives of the same man who have the same name, the ruling of the mishna applies **in** a case where there are **witnesses who observe the transmission** of the bill of divorce. **And this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** and there is no concern that the other wife will claim payment of her marriage contract. Therefore, one should not deduce from this, as Rava did, that one of two people with the same name can present a promissory note to claim a debt from others.

The *gemara* provides the resolution: *be'idei mesirah de-Rabbi Eliezer hi, hacha nami be'idei mesirah de-Rabbi Eliezer hi* — this entire framework operates according to the view of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that what validates a *get* is not *edei chasimah* — the witnesses who signed — but *edei mesirah* — witnesses to the act of delivery itself. For Rabbi Eliezer, the *get* does not need to function as self-contained testimony. Its validity rests not on the document's ability to independently attest to the divorce but on the fact that witnesses observed the act of transfer. Therefore, any ambiguity in the document itself is immaterial — the witnesses to delivery observed precisely who gave the *get* to whom, and *that* is what establishes the divorce.

For Rabbi Meir, by contrast, *edei chasimah karti* — the witnesses who signed are the essential validating mechanism — and the document must be able to testify on its own. Under Rabbi Meir's framework, a *get* whose names are shared by others in the town would be problematic, since the document cannot independently identify the parties. Rabbi Eliezer has no such requirement; external witnesses to delivery supply what the document alone might lack.

This is why the *gemara* notes that the observation could appear strange — once we know who the wife is (from her holding it), the husband's identity follows necessarily. The *gemara*'s answer, that we follow Rabbi Eliezer's framework of *edei mesirah*, clarifies that the question was never really about the document's text at all. The *get*'s validity is grounded in witnessed delivery, and once those witnesses establish that this man gave this document to this woman, no textual ambiguity can undo it.
