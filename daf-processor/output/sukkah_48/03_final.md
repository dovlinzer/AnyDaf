# Sukkah 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shemini Atzeret Status

### Shehecheyanu Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אוֹמְרִים זְמַן בִּשְׁמִינִי שֶׁל חַג, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין אוֹמְרִים זְמַן בִּשְׁמִינִי שֶׁל חַג. וְהִלְכְתָא: אוֹמְרִים זְמַן בִּשְׁמִינִי שֶׁל חַג. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: שְׁמִינִי
>
> **Translation:** **What** halakhic conclusion **was reached concerning** the blessing of time? **Rav Naḥman said: One recites** the blessing of **time on the eighth** day **of the festival** of *Sukkot*. **And Rav Sheshet said: One does not recite** the blessing of **time on the eighth** day **of the Festival.** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is** that **one recites** the blessing of **time on the eighth** day **of the Festival.** The Gemara notes: **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Naḥman:** The **eighth** day

The present daf opens by returning to the very last line of 47b, where the preceding discussion concluded. That discussion had explored at length whether one recites *shehecheyanu* on *Shemini Atzeret* — whether *Shemini Atzeret* stands as a sufficiently independent *chag* to warrant its own blessing of time. The Gemara there resolved the matter: *v'hilchasa omin zman b'Shemini haChag* — the *halacha* is that one does recite *shehecheyanu* on *Shemini Atzeret*. The Gemara then adds that this ruling follows the position of Rav Nachman, citing a *tanna'itic* source in its support: *tani k'vasei d'Rav Nachman*. That source is, in fact, a Tosefta, which enumerates the ways in which *Shemini Atzeret* is treated as a *regel bifnei atzmo* — a holiday unto itself.

### Pezer Keshev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֶגֶל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ לְעִנְיַן פָּזֵ״‎ר קֶשֶׁ״‎ב. פַּיִיס בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. זְמַן בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. רֶגֶל בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. קׇרְבָּן בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. שִׁירָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. בְּרָכָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** is **a Festival in and of itself** with regard **to the matter** of: ***Peh*, *zayin*, *reish*; *kuf*, *shin*, *beit*.** This is an acronym for: **A lottery [*payis*] in and of itself,** i.e., a new lottery is performed on that day to determine which priests will sacrifice the offerings that day, and the order established on *Sukkot* does not continue; the blessing of **time [*zeman*],** i.e., Who has given us life, sustained us, and brought us to this time, **in and of itself,** as it is recited just as it is recited at the start of each Festival; **a Festival [*regel* ] in and of itself,** and there is no mitzva to sit in the *sukka* (see *Tosafot*); **an offering [*korban*] in and of itself,** as the number of offerings sacrificed on the Eighth Day is not a continuation of the number sacrificed on *Sukkot* but is part of a new calculation; **a song [*shira*] in and of itself,** since the psalms recited by the Levites as the offerings are sacrificed on the Eighth Day are not a continuation of those recited on *Sukkot*; **a blessing [*berakha*] in and of itself,** as the addition to the third blessing of Grace after Meals and to the *Amida* prayer (see *Tosafot*) is phrased in a manner different from that of the addition recited on *Sukkot*.

The Tosefta formulates its list through a mnemonic: *pezer keshev* — פז״ר קש״ב — six letters standing for six dimensions of *Shemini Atzeret*'s distinct status. Each letter is the initial of a phrase: *pais bifnei atzmo*, *zman bifnei atzmo*, *regel bifnei atzmo*, *korban bifnei atzmo*, *shira bifnei atzma*, *bracha bifnei atzma*. Each represents a specific domain in which *Shemini Atzeret* operates independently of *Sukkot*, and we now turn to each in sequence.

### Pais Bifnei Atzmo

The פ of *pezer keshev* stands for *pais bifnei atzmo* — an independent lottery. In the *Beis HaMikdash*, the *kohanim* were organized into twenty-four *mishmarot*, each of which served for a designated week of the year, such that every group served approximately two weeks annually. On the *regalim*, however, all twenty-four groups served simultaneously. To distribute the *korbanot* of *Musaf* fairly among them, a lottery was held. The *mussaf* offerings of *Sukkot* involved a famously large number of animals — beginning with thirteen bulls on the first day and decreasing by one each day, producing a total of seventy bulls across the seven days of *Sukkot*. Dividing seventy by twenty-four yields slightly less than three: twenty-two groups received three bulls each, while two groups received only two.

When *Shemini Atzeret* arrived, there was only a single bull to distribute. One might suppose that simple equity dictated giving it to one of those last two groups who had received only two — completing the equalization across the holiday. But *pais bifnei atzmo* teaches otherwise: a completely fresh lottery was conducted, entirely independent of what had preceded. Whatever group won that lottery received the bull — irrespective of how many they had received during *Sukkot*. This independence is itself significant beyond the question of *korbanot*: even considerations of institutional fairness that might seem to link *Shemini Atzeret* to *Sukkot* are set aside. It is a new beginning.

### Zman Bifnei Atzmo

The ז stands for *zman bifnei atzmo*: *Shemini Atzeret* has its own *zman*, its own moment — and accordingly its own *shehecheyanu*. This is what the preceding passage on 47b established as *halacha*, and it requires no further elaboration here.

### Regel Bifnei Atzmo

The ר stands for *regel bifnei atzmo* — *Shemini Atzeret* is itself a *regel*. At first glance this appears circular: one of the six ways it is its own *regel* is that it is its own *regel*. But the substance is clear. Rashi explains that the ר signifies that on *Shemini Atzeret* one does not sit in a *sukkah* and does not take a *lulav* — the defining *mitzvot* of *Sukkot* are entirely absent. This is admittedly more of a negative characterization than a positive one, defining *Shemini Atzeret* by what it lacks rather than by what it is. Nevertheless, it establishes unambiguously that *Shemini Atzeret* is not an extension of *Sukkot*.

Tosafot offers an additional interpretation. In the laws of *aveilus*, if a family member passes away shortly before *Sukkot*, the onset of *Yom Tov* interrupts the *shiva*. The *shloshim* then continues to count, with the days of *Chol HaMoed Sukkot* contributing to its depletion. The question then arises: how is *Shemini Atzeret* reckoned in this context? The answer given by Tosafot is striking — *Shemini Atzeret* itself counts as the equivalent of a full seven-day *chag* for purposes of *shloshim* calculation. This demonstrates that *Shemini Atzeret* possesses an independent status as its own *Yom Tov*, one that bears its own weight even in halachic domains quite removed from the Temple service.

### Korban Bifnei Atzmo

The כ stands for *korban bifnei atzmo* — distinct offerings. This is perhaps the most straightforward of the six. The *musaf* of *Shemini Atzeret* is entirely its own: one bull rather than a decreasing series, seven sheep rather than fourteen, one ram rather than two. These are not a continuation of the pattern of *Sukkot* but constitute an independent *musaf* for an independent day.

Tosafot adds a possible identification for the שׁ of *shira bifnei atzma* — the distinct song: it may refer to *Lamnatzeach Al HaSheminis* (Psalm 12), the psalm whose heading makes explicit reference to the eighth. The songs that accompanied the *musaf* offerings of *Sukkot* were associated with the agricultural distribution to the poor from the harvest; *Shemini Atzeret* had its own song.

### Shira & Bracha

The ב stands for *bracha bifnei atzma* — a distinct blessing. Rashi connects this to what the Gemara had established earlier: in the liturgy, one does not refer to the day as *Chag HaSukkot* but rather as *Yom Shemini Chag HaAtzeret Hazeh* — a distinct name that reflects its distinct identity. Other interpretations exist, including a special blessing given to the king, but Rashi's primary reading ties it to the liturgical name.

A brief note before moving to the next *mishnah*: the discussion of all six items in *pezer keshev* grew out of the enumeration of multi-day practices — beginning with *lulav* and *aravah* at six and seven days respectively, together with the *mishnah* specifically addressing *Hoshana Rabba*, and then the status of the *lulav* and *esrog* on *Shemini Atzeret*. Now the text turns to a different multi-day item: *hallel* and *simcha* for eight days.

## Hallel & Simcha

### Eight Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַהַלֵּל וְהַשִּׂמְחָה — שְׁמוֹנָה. כֵּיצַד? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁחַיָּיב אָדָם בְּהַלֵּל וּבְשִׂמְחָה וּבִכְבוֹד יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג כִּשְׁאָר כׇּל יְמוֹת הַחַג.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna elaborates upon the first mishna in this chapter. **The** obligation to recite ***hallel* and the** mitzva of **rejoicing** on the Festival by sacrificing and eating the meat of peace-offerings **are** always for **eight** days. The mishna explains: **How so?** This **teaches that a person is obligated in *hallel*, and in the** mitzva of **rejoicing, and in reverence for the last day of the Festival like** he is for **all the other days of the Festival.**

The Gemara now takes up the teaching that *hallel* and *simcha* both extend for all eight days of the *chag* — including *Shemini Atzeret*.

### Mishnah Cited

The *mishnah* states: *heHallel vehaSimcha shemonah* — *hallel* and *simcha* for eight days. This is noteworthy because *Sukkot* stands in contrast to *Pesach* in precisely this regard. On *Pesach*, the rule is that full *hallel* is recited only on the first day; the prevalent practice of reciting a half-*hallel* on the remaining days of *Pesach* reflects a later custom acknowledged by the Gemara, but the full *hallel* is reserved for the first day alone. On *Sukkot*, by contrast, full *hallel* is recited every single day. The Gemara elsewhere explains this distinction: since each day of *Sukkot* has a different complement of *korbanot* in its *musaf*, each day carries a distinct identity, and therefore each warrants its own full *hallel*.

The *mishnah* then focuses more closely on *simcha*, and this is where the real *chiddush* lies — especially once we appreciate that *Shemini Atzeret* is a separate *regel* altogether. That the obligation of *shalmei simcha* continues through *Shemini Atzeret* is not self-evident, and the *mishnah* teaches: *melamed shechayav adam b'hallel uv'simcha uv'chavod Yom Tov Acharon shel chag k'sha'ar kol yemot haChag* — a person is obligated in *hallel*, *simcha*, and honoring the last day of *Yom Tov* just as on all the other days of the holiday. Honoring *Yom Tov Acharon* is not itself surprising — *Yom Tov Acharon* of *Pesach* is also honored — but the *chiddush* of *hallel* every single day and, especially, of *shalmei simcha* extending into *Shemini Atzeret* is the heart of the teaching.

### Vehayisa Ach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי — דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהָיִיתָ אַךְ שָׂמֵחַ״ — לְרַבּוֹת לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״אַךְ״ — חִלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that on the eighth day of the Festival one is obligated to rejoice, derived? It is **as the Sages taught** that the verse states with regard to *Sukkot*: **"And you shall be altogether joyful"** (Deuteronomy 16:15). The verse comes **to include the evenings of the last day of the Festival,** i.e., then too, one is obligated to rejoice by partaking of the meat of the peace-offerings sacrificed the previous day. The Gemara asks: Does the verse come to include the evening of the eighth day? **Or** perhaps **it** comes to include **only the** evening of **the first day of the Festival.** The Gemara answers: **When** the verse **says: Altogether,** it is exclusionary, and **it** has **distinguished** this night from the other nights of the Festival.

The Gemara asks: *mina hanei milei* — from where is this derived? The *baraisa* cites the verse in Devarim 16. The passage there describes *Chag HaSukkot* as a seven-day celebration: *chag haSukkot ta'aseh lecha shivat yamim* — seven days. It commands joy: *v'samachta b'chagecha atah u'vincha u'vitecha...* It then repeats the count: *shivat yamim tachog la'Hashem Elokecha*. Notably, *Shemini Atzeret* is not mentioned anywhere in this passage, nor in the surrounding verses that enumerate the *shalosh regalim*. The holiday called *Chag HaSukkot* is emphatically seven days.

So how does one derive that *simcha* applies to *Shemini Atzeret* as well? The answer lies in the closing words of that passage: *v'hayita ach sameach* — and you shall be exceedingly, or entirely, happy. The *baraisa* reads this as an extension: *lerabbos leil Yom Tov haAchron* — to include the night of the last *Yom Tov*, meaning the night of *Shemini Atzeret*. The word *ach* would serve as the limiting term, and *v'hayita sameach* the expanding one.

### Last vs First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן וּלְהוֹצִיא לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן — שֶׁיֵּשׁ שִׂמְחָה לְפָנָיו, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי לֵילֵי יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן — שֶׁאֵין שִׂמְחָה לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What did you see** that led you **to include the evenings of the last day of the Festival** in the mitzva of rejoicing **and to exclude the evenings of the first day of the Festival?** Why not require one to sacrifice peace-offerings on the afternoon preceding the Festival to be eaten on the first night? The Gemara answers: **I include the evenings of the last day of the Festival, before which there is** a day of **rejoicing,** as it is reasonable that the rejoicing should continue, **and I exclude the evenings of the first day of the Festival, before which there is not** a day of **rejoicing,** as there is no obligation to sacrifice offerings on the afternoon preceding the Festival.

But the *baraisa* does not leave this without challenge. Perhaps *v'hayita ach sameach* comes to include not the last night but the *first* night — the night that opens *Sukkot*? After all, one might think that *simcha* in the sense of *shalmei simcha* requires the prior offering of a *korban*, which cannot be done at night; perhaps the verse is extending *simcha* to cover that first night, even though no *korban* was brought on it.

The *baraisa* resolves this by invoking the limiting force of *ach*: one must choose between extending to the first night and extending to the last night, not both. And the choice is clear: *marbeh ani leil Yom Tov haAchron sheyesh simcha lefanav* — I extend to the last night, which is preceded by *simcha*, because a *korban* was in fact brought during the preceding day of *Sukkot*, and its continued consumption now extends the *simcha* into that night. *U'motzi ani leil Yom Tov haRishon she'ein simcha lefanav* — I exclude the first night, which has no *simcha* preceding it, because no *korban* was brought that day from which the eating could flow.

### Night Extension

This resolution illuminates something important about the nature of *simcha* itself. It is not merely our subjective experience of happiness, nor simply the act of eating. Rather, it is the eating as the *culmination* of having brought the *korban* before God. The Gemara states explicitly: *ein simcha ela b'vasar shelamim* — true *simcha* is realized through the meat of *shelamim*. The connection to God through the act of offering, followed by consuming the sacred meat in God's presence, is what constitutes *simchat Yom Tov* in its fullest sense. This is why the first night of *Sukkot* is excluded from this particular form of *simcha*: no *korban* was brought that day, and thus nothing precedes the eating that would give it its character of *simcha*. The last night, by contrast, emerges directly from the *korban* brought on *Sukkot*, whose eating extends lawfully into *Shemini Atzeret*.

### Shalmei Simcha

This understanding raises a further question: does the obligation of *simcha* require bringing a new *korban* on each individual day of the *chag*? A *shelamim* may be eaten on the day it is brought and the following day — so does one need to bring a fresh offering every day, or every other day at minimum? The Gemara's language — *lerabbos leil Yom Tov haAchron* — does not speak in terms of *chiyuv* per se; it speaks of inclusion within the category of *simcha*. The precise extent of the daily obligation remains genuinely ambiguous from this passage, including the question of what exactly "including the last night" entails in practical terms.

What is clear is that the meat of *shelamim* — the act of offering before God and eating in that context — is the heart of *simchat Yom Tov* in the time of the *Mikdash*. It is not that the meat tastes different from non-sacred meat; it is that the *simcha* generated by standing before God and completing the act of offering is qualitatively distinct. That is the *simcha* the Torah commands.

### Rambam Simcha

The Rambam in *Hilchos Yom Tov* (end of perek 6) makes two important points that grow naturally from this discussion. First, he underscores what the *gemara* states: *ein simcha ela b'vasar shelamim* — in the time of the *Mikdash*, true *simcha* was the *shelamim*. In our own time, he applies the principle differently, describing what each member of the household should receive to experience joy: men with meat and wine, women with fine clothing, and so forth.

But the Rambam's second point is even more striking, and it draws directly from the *psukim* themselves. The *pasuk* that commands *simcha* — *v'samachta b'chagecha atah u'vincha u'vitecha v'avdecha va'amatecha v'halevi v'hager v'hayatom v'ha'almanah asher bisharecha* — lists not only one's own household but the Levite, the stranger, the orphan, and the widow. This is no accident. *Sukkot* is the season of ingathering: the agricultural year has concluded, crops are stored, success is palpable. The natural response is great joy. The Torah's demand is that this joy be directed toward God — expressed through the *lulav* and *esrog*, through sitting in the *sukkah*, through *shelamim*. But alongside this, the *psukim* insist that those who have no land, no harvest, no share in the year's bounty must also be included in the *simcha*.

The Rambam is uncompromising: *u'kh'she-hu ochel v'shote chayav l'hachil la-ger la-yatom v'la-almanah v'sha'ar ha-aniyim ha-umlalim* — when one eats and drinks, one is obligated to provide food to the stranger, orphan, widow, and the unfortunate. One who locks the doors of his courtyard and feasts with his family alone, giving nothing to the poor and the distressed: *ein zo simchat mitzvah ela simchat kerso* — this is not the *simcha* of a *mitzvah* but the *simcha* of one's stomach. And on such a person, the Rambam invokes the verse: *v'zeriti feresh al p'neichem peresh chagichem* — I will spread the refuse of your festival offerings on your faces. *V'simcha kazu kelon hi lahem* — such joy is a disgrace.

This explains why *simcha* is so heavily underscored specifically in the context of *Sukkot*: it is the *chag* of abundance, of completed harvest, and therefore the *chag* that most powerfully demands that abundance be shared.

## Leaving Sukkah

### Removing Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סוּכָּה — שִׁבְעָה. כֵּיצַד? גָּמַר מִלֶּאֱכוֹל — לֹא יַתִּיר אֶת סוּכָּתוֹ. אֲבָל מוֹרִיד אֶת הַכֵּלִים מִן הַמִּנְחָה וּלְמַעְלָה, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד יוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mitzva of ***sukka*** is **seven** days. **How** does one fulfill this obligation for seven full days? When **one finished eating** on the seventh day, **he** should **not dismantle his *sukka*** immediately, because the obligation continues until the end of the day. **However, he takes the vessels down** from the *sukka* into the house **from *minḥa*** time **and onward in deference to the last day of the Festival,** when he will require the vessels in the house.

The Gemara now takes up the *mishnah* that addresses the practical transition out of the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret*. The *mishnah* states: *gamar mil'echol* — once one has finished eating on *Hoshana Rabba*, at the close of *Sukkot*, one does not immediately dismantle the *sukkah* — *lo yatir sukkato* — because one might need to eat again before nightfall. But from *mincha* time onward, one begins bringing one's vessels and furnishings down from the *sukkah* into the house: *aval morid et hakelim min hamincha u'lemala*, and this is done *mipnei kavod Yom Tov haAcharon shel Chag* — out of honor for the last day of *Yom Tov*.

### Kavod Yom Tov

The practical reason is straightforward: one needs one's bedding, dishes, and furnishings for the *Yom Tov* meal of *Shemini Atzeret* in the house. But the *mishnah*'s language carries a deeper subtext. It is not merely that one needs the items; rather, the very act of removing them from the *sukkah* is itself an expression of *kavod* for *Shemini Atzeret*. The implication is that one should ideally be taking the *sukkah* apart altogether — demonstrating departure — but since the *sukkah* may still be needed and cannot be dismantled, one does the next best thing: one removes everything from it, signaling that it is being abandoned.

### No Vessels?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֵין לוֹ כֵּלִים לְהוֹרִיד, מַהוּ? אֵין לוֹ כֵּלִים?! אֶלָּא כִּי אִשְׁתַּמַּשׁ בְּמַאי אִשְׁתַּמַּשׁ? אֶלָּא: אֵין לוֹ מָקוֹם לְהוֹרִיד כֵּלָיו, מַהוּ? רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר (רַב) אָמַר: פּוֹחֵת בָּהּ אַרְבָּעָה. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: מַדְלִיק בָּהּ אֶת הַנֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: If **one does not have vessels to take down** from the *sukka*, **what** should he do? The Gemara asks: **One does not have vessels? But when he utilized** his *sukka* during the Festival, **with what** vessels **did he** eat when **he utilized** the *sukka*? **Rather,** this is the question: **If he has no place** into which **he** can **take down his vessels** and he must continue eating in the *sukka*, **what** is the *halakha*? What can he do to underscore the fact that he is eating there not to fulfill a mitzva, thereby violating the prohibition against adding to the mitzvot of the Torah, but only due to the lack of an alternative? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav said: He reduces** the roofing of the *sukka* by **four** handbreadths, thereby rendering the *sukka* unfit. **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: He lights a lamp inside** the *sukka*, which is prohibited during the festival of *Sukkot*.

The Gemara presses on this: *ein lo kelim lehorid mahu* — if one has no vessels to bring down, what is one to do? The Gemara itself immediately asks: if you have no vessels, what have you been using all week? What have you been sleeping on, eating from? The question is somewhat facetious, but it exposes an important assumption built into the original ruling. The anxiety expressed — what if there is nothing to bring down? — only makes sense if the act of bringing items down is itself a required demonstration of departure from the *sukkah*, not merely a practical convenience. One must *do something* to show one is leaving.

The Gemara reformulates: *ela ein lo makom lehorid kelim mahu* — perhaps one simply has no space in the house to receive the items. Perhaps one's house is unavailable — one cannot return the furnishings there. And again, what is the problem? If there is no room, one stays in the *sukkah* and eats *Shemini Atzeret* there. The answer the Gemara gives reveals how seriously this obligation is taken.

### Invalidation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא לַן וְהָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And they do not disagree** with regard to the *halakha*. Instead, they are providing different solutions for different locations. **This is for us,** who live outside Eretz Yisrael, **and this is for them,** who live in Eretz Yisrael. Those who live in Eretz Yisrael reduce the roofing, since the obligation to sit in the *sukka* no longer applies. However, those who live outside of Eretz Yisrael, who are obligated to sit in the *sukka* on the eighth day with regard to which there is uncertainty that it might be the seventh day, must find another way to distinguish the eighth day from the days of the Festival of *Sukkot*.

Rabbi Chiya bar Rav (or bar Ashi) says: *pocheis ba'arba* — one invalidates the *sukkah* by making a breach of four *tefachim* in it. Tosafot asks why specifically four rather than three, but the principle is clear: even if one must remain in the *sukkah* physically, one must render it halachically invalid. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi offers a different solution: *madlik bah ner* — one lights a candle inside the *sukkah*. We learned earlier that in a small *sukkah*, lighting a candle causes discomfort and anxiety about fire hazard, and this is precisely the point.

The Gemara resolves that these are not competing opinions: *ha lan veha lehu* — one applies in Eretz Yisrael and the other in Babylonia. Those in Eretz Yisrael, following the practice that *Shemini Atzeret* does not require eating in the *sukkah*, can simply invalidate the *sukkah* structurally. Those outside Eretz Yisrael, who must continue to eat in the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret*, cannot invalidate it — yet they must still do something to convey that they do not genuinely belong there. The solution: light a candle that makes the *sukkah* uncomfortable, and continue eating without a *beracha* over the *sukkah*.

### EY vs Bavel

This is a striking passage. Until this point the discussion had centered on whether to recite a *beracha* in the *sukkah* on *Shemini Atzeret*. Now the Gemara goes further: not reciting a *beracha* is insufficient. One must actively undermine the *sukkah*'s character, even while sitting in it — even while eating in it in fulfillment of *Shemini Atzeret*'s requirements for those in the *diaspora*.

### Dirty Dishes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תִּינַח סוּכָּה קְטַנָּה, סוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דִּמְעַיֵּיל בַּהּ מָאנֵי מֵיכְלָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא: מָאנֵי מֵיכְלָא — בַּר מִמְּטַלַּלְתָּא. מָאנֵי מִשְׁתְּיָא — בִּמְטַלַּלְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** works out **well** with regard to **a small *sukka*,** since it is prohibited to light a lamp due to the danger of a conflagration, and lighting a lamp will underscore the distinction. However, with regard to **a large *sukka*,** in which there is no prohibition and therefore no distinction, **what can be said?** The Gemara answers: One underscores the distinction in **that he brings eating vessels,** e.g., pots in which food was cooked, **into** the *sukka*, **as Rava said: Eating vessels** are taken **out of the *sukka*; drinking vessels** remain **in the *sukka*.** By leaving the pots and pans in the *sukka*, he indicates that the *sukka* is no longer involved in fulfillment of the mitzva.

The Gemara continues: *hateniach Sukkah ketanah* — the lighting solution works for a small *sukkah*, where a candle genuinely causes anxiety. But for a large *sukkah*, where a candle poses no concern and creates no discomfort, what can one do? *Meiayeil bei mane demichla* — one brings dirty dishes into it. Rava had taught elsewhere: *mane demichla bar mimtaltesa* — one clears dirty dishes immediately out of the *sukkah*, while *mane deshtaya bimetaltesa* — drinking vessels may remain inside. The reason is obvious: dirty dishes are unpleasant and one naturally removes them without delay. Bringing dirty dishes into the *sukkah* is therefore an act that would *never* be done during *Sukkot* proper, precisely because it is so contrary to the *sukkah*'s dignity. On *Shemini Atzeret*, for one who must eat there but must simultaneously signal non-belonging, this act serves the purpose.

### Targum Onkelos

This series of *sugyot* points toward a deeper understanding of what *Shemini Atzeret* actually represents. Targum Onkelos translates the *pasuk* *bayom hashemini atzeret tihyeh lachem* in a revealing way: *bayoma timinaia atzarta min mitalalaya leveitaya* — on the eighth day shall be a gathering in from your *sukkah* into your house. Onkelos understands *atzeret* — the very name of the day — as a gathering inward, a transition from the *sukkah* back into the home.

### Transition Theme

This reading illuminates the entire structure of the *yamim tovim*. The *Yamim Noraim* — *Rosh Hashanah* and *Yom Kippur* — were about bringing God into one's inner life, culminating in entry into the *Kodesh Kodashim*. *Sukkot* then took that experience and externalized it: we moved out of the home into the *sukkah*, dwelling in a structure that evokes the *Ananei HaKavod* and the presence of God, symbolizing through the *sukkah* walls the very *Kodesh Kodashim* itself. The *gemara* elsewhere makes those connections explicit.

But the challenge has always been the follow-through. The *sukkah* makes God's presence accessible — the structure itself frames and sustains the consciousness. The real difficulty is re-entering ordinary life without losing what one has built. *Shemini Atzeret*, understood through Targum Onkelos and through the Gemara's insistence on the physical act of *leaving* the *sukkah*, is precisely the *Yom Tov* of that transition. It is the day on which one pauses at the threshold, before the ordinary rhythms of the year resume, and carries the *kedushah* of the season consciously into the house. *Kavod Yom Tov Acharon* — the honor of the final day — consists in making that departure deliberate and sacred.

This, then, is one important answer to the question of what *Shemini Atzeret* is about positively, beyond the list of what it is not. Its identity is precisely the threshold: the moment of return, the conscious crossing back from *sukkah* to house, from the sheltered presence of God to the dailiness of the year — and the aspiration that one should carry that presence with one into what comes next.

## Nisuch HaMayim

### Golden Vessel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם כֵּיצַד? צְלוֹחִית שֶׁל זָהָב מַחְזֶקֶת שְׁלֹשָׁה לוּגִּים הָיָה מְמַלֵּא מִן הַשִּׁילוֹחַ. הִגִּיעוּ לְשַׁעַר הַמַּיִם, תָּקְעוּ וְהֵרִיעוּ וְתָקְעוּ. עָלָה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לִשְׂמֹאלוֹ, שְׁנֵי סְפָלִים שֶׁל כֶּסֶף הָיוּ שָׁם. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שֶׁל סִיד הָיוּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיוּ מוּשְׁחָרִין פְּנֵיהֶם מִפְּנֵי הַיַּיִן. וּמְנוּקָּבִים
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the rite of **water libation** performed in the Temple during the Festival, **how** was it performed? **One would fill a golden jug with a capacity of three *log*** with water **from the Siloam** pool. When those who went to bring the water **reached the Gate of the Water,** so called because the water for the libation was brought through this gate leading to the Temple courtyard, **they sounded a *tekia*, sounded a *terua*, and sounded** another ***tekia*** as an expression of joy. The priest **ascended the ramp** of the altar **and turned to his left. There were two silver basins there** into which he poured the water. **Rabbi Yehuda said: They were limestone** basins, **but they would blacken due to the wine** and therefore looked like silver. The two basins were **perforated** at the bottom

The *mishnah* now turns to *nisuch hamayim* — the water-pouring ritual performed each morning during *Sukkot*. It begins: *tzelochit shel zahav mahazeket shlosha lugin* — a golden pitcher containing three *log* of water. To give a sense of measure: a *revi'it* — the minimum size of a *kiddush* cup, roughly three and a half ounces — is one quarter of a *log*. So a *log* is approximately fourteen ounces, and three *log* is roughly forty-two ounces.

### Process

This pitcher was filled from the *Shiloach* — a flowing spring outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. The water-drawer then proceeded to *Sha'ar HaMayim*, the Gate of Water on the southern side of the *Beis HaMikdash* near the ramp leading to the *mizbei'ach*. Upon reaching the gate: *teka'u veheru uteka'u* — they sounded *tekiah*, *teruah*, *tekiah*. This corresponds to the trumpet-blowing we encountered earlier in connection with the *aravah* ceremony; many of *Sukkot*'s rituals were accompanied by these blasts. The *kohen* then ascended the ramp — *alu b'kevesh ufana lismolo* — and turned left, contrary to the standard practice of turning right, which we will address shortly.

### Two Funnels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמִין שְׁנֵי חוֹטָמִין דַּקִּין, (וְאֶחָד) מְעוּבֶּה וְאֶחָד דַּק, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם כָּלִין בְּבַת אַחַת. מַעֲרָבוֹ — שֶׁל מַיִם, מִזְרָחוֹ — שֶׁל יַיִן. עֵירָה שֶׁל מַיִם לְתוֹךְ שֶׁל יַיִן, וְשֶׁל יַיִן לְתוֹךְ שֶׁל מַיִם — יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** with **two thin** perforated **nose-like** protrusions. **One** of the basins, used for the wine libation, had a perforation that was **broad, and one,** used for the water libation, had a perforation that was **thin, so that** the flow of **both** the water and the wine, which do not have the same viscosity, would **conclude simultaneously.** The basin to the **west of** the altar was **for water,** and the basin to the **east of** the altar was **for wine.** However, if **one poured** the contents of the basin **of water into** the basin **of wine, or** the contents of the basin **of wine into** the basin **of water, he fulfilled** his obligation, as failure to pour the libation from the prescribed location does not disqualify the libation after the fact.

At the southwest corner of the *mizbei'ach* were two silver funnels: *shnei sfalim shel kesef hayu sham*. Rabbi Yehudah disputes this, claiming they were actually made of plaster — *shel sid hayu* — but that they appeared black and silver-toned because of the wine poured through them repeatedly: *ella she-hayu mushcharim pneihem mipnei hayayin*.

The funnels had openings of different sizes: *menukavim k'min shnei chotamim dakim* — like two thin nostrils, but *echad mevo'e v'echad dak* — one thicker and one thinner, meaning one had a larger opening and one a smaller. The reason: *kedei she-yiyu shneihem kalim b'vas achas* — so that both would empty simultaneously.

The logic of this requires explanation. Wine was poured at the *mizbei'ach* every single day of the year as part of the libations accompanying the daily *korbanot*. Water was poured only during the seven days of *Sukkot* — *nisuch hamayim* was an annual ritual unique to this season. Each morning, the wine and water were to be poured simultaneously into their respective funnels. Since wine is more viscous than water, it flows more slowly. To ensure both completed their flow at the same moment, the wine funnel required a wider opening even when equal quantities were involved.

### Placement

*Ma'aravo shel mayim mizracho shel yayin* — the western funnel was for water, the eastern for wine. They were positioned at the southwest corner of the *mizbei'ach*. The *kohen* arriving from the south, having turned left at the top of the ramp, was therefore immediately at this corner. If one accidentally poured the water into the wine funnel or the wine into the water funnel: *yatza* — the obligation was discharged after the fact.

### R. Yehudah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּלּוֹג הָיָה מְנַסֵּךְ כׇּל שְׁמוֹנָה. וְלַמְנַסֵּךְ אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הַגְבַּהּ יָדֶךָ. שֶׁפַּעַם אֶחָד נִסֵּךְ אֶחָד עַל גַּבֵּי רַגְלָיו, וּרְגָמוּהוּ כׇּל הָעָם בְּאֶתְרוֹגֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The basin for the water libation was not that large; rather, **one would pour** the water **with** a vessel that had a capacity of **one *log*** on **all eight** days of the Festival and not only seven. **And** the appointee **says to the one pouring** the water into the silver basin: **Raise your hand,** so that his actions would be visible, **as one time** a Sadducee priest intentionally **poured** the water **on his feet,** as the Sadducees did not accept the oral tradition requiring water libation, and in their rage **all the people pelted him with their *etrogim*.**

Rabbi Yehudah disagrees in two respects: first, the quantity of water was not three *log* but only one *log*; and second, *nisuch hamayim* was performed not for seven days but for all eight — including *Shemini Atzeret*.

### Sadducee

The *mishnah* records a notorious incident: the assembly would instruct the *kohen* performing the pouring to raise his hand — *hagbeh yadcha* — so that everyone could see that he was pouring into the funnel and not elsewhere. This instruction arose because on one occasion a *kohen* who was a *Tzadoki* — a Sadducee, who rejected the *nisuch hamayim* ritual as having no Scriptural basis — poured the water on his own feet rather than into the funnel. He apparently did so covertly, hoping not to be noticed. When the crowd perceived what had happened, *regemuhu kol ha'am b'etrogeihem* — the entire assembly pelted him with their *etrogim*.

### Shabbat Prep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַעֲשֵׂהוּ בַּחוֹל כָּךְ מַעֲשֵׂהוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת, אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיָה מְמַלֵּא מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת חָבִית שֶׁל זָהָב שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת מִן הַשִּׁילוֹחַ וּמַנִּיחָהּ בַּלִּשְׁכָּה. נִשְׁפְּכָה, נִתְגַּלְּתָה, הָיָה מְמַלֵּא מִן הַכִּיּוֹר. שֶׁהַיַּיִן וְהַמַּיִם מְגוּלִּין — פְּסוּלִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda continues: **As its performance during the week, so is its performance on Shabbat, except** that on Shabbat one would not draw water. Instead, **on Shabbat eve, one would fill a golden barrel that was not consecrated** for exclusive use in the Temple **from the Siloam** pool, **and he** would **place it in the** Temple **chamber** and draw water from there on Shabbat. If the water in the barrel **spilled,** or if it **was exposed** overnight, leading to concern that a snake may have deposited poison in the water, **one would fill** the jug with water **from the basin** in the Temple courtyard, **as exposed wine or water is unfit for the altar.** Just as it is prohibited for people to drink them due to the potential danger, so too, they may not be poured on the altar.

When the seventh day of *nisuch hamayim* fell on *Shabbat*, a modification was required. Normally the water was drawn from the *Shiloach* on the morning of the ritual. But carrying on *Shabbat* was prohibited. Therefore, on *Erev Shabbat* the water was filled into a golden barrel — *chavit shel zahav she-einah mekudeshet* — that was deliberately *not* sanctified. The reason: if the vessel were sanctified, the water inside it would undergo sanctification and thereby become invalid through *linah* — passing the night in an unsanctified state outside the Temple vessels. By keeping the barrel unsanctified, the water remained unaffected by *linah*, and could be used on *Shabbat* itself. This barrel was stored in one of the *lishkos* — the chambers of the *Beis HaMikdash* — overnight, and drawn from the following morning.

### Laver Backup

Two contingencies could disrupt this arrangement: *nishpachah* — the barrel spilled — or *nitgaltah* — the water was left uncovered. The Gemara has a concern about *gilui*, uncovered water, as it was considered a potential health hazard on account of snakes drinking from it and leaving venom behind. In either case, the *kohen* would fill the pitcher *min hakiyor* — from the *kiyor*, the laver used for the *kohanim*'s hand-washing. The laver was connected to an underground well, and water drawn from it did not suffer from *pasul belinah* because when lowered back, it reconnected to the underground water source, effectively "rejoining" flowing water. The downside was that it was not drawn directly from the *Shiloach*, and thus lacked the symbolism of flowing springwater. This source was therefore a backup only.

There is an interesting dispute between Rashi and Tosafot regarding what happened after the water passed through the funnel. Rashi maintains that the water flowed from the *sefel* — the funnel — down across the top of the *mizbei'ach* and into a cavity below, passing through or near the fire. Tosafot finds no source for this and maintains the water simply flowed directly down into the cavity below without traversing the top of the *mizbei'ach* at all.

## Sources & Heretics

### Ushavtem Mayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב עֵינָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וּשְׁאַבְתֶּם מַיִם בְּשָׂשׂוֹן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the customs accompanying the drawing of the water, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Eina said** that it is **as the verse states: "With joy [*sason*] you shall draw water** out of the springs of salvation" (Isaiah 12:3), indicating that the water was to be drawn from the spring and the rite performed in extreme joy.

The Gemara asks: *mina hanei milei* — from where is the ritual of *nisuch hamayim* derived? Rashi understands the question as asking specifically about the accompanying *simcha* — the *tekiah teruah tekiah* and the rejoicing. Tosafot understands it as asking how we know the water must come specifically from a flowing spring (*ma'ayan*) rather than from a well.

Rav Eina cites the verse: *ushavtem mayim besasson mima'ainei hayeshuah* — "and you shall draw water with joy from the springs of salvation" (Yeshayahu 12:3). Rashi derives from *besasson* — with joy — the obligation to accompany the ritual with *tekiah* and celebration. Tosafot derives from *mima'ainei* — from springs — the requirement to draw from a flowing source. The verse serves both purposes, though how *besasson* specifically mandates *tekiah* is not entirely transparent.

### Sasson Simcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ תְּרֵי מִינֵי, חַד שְׁמֵיהּ שָׂשׂוֹן וְחַד שְׁמֵיהּ שִׂמְחָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שָׂשׂוֹן לְשִׂמְחָה: אֲנָא עֲדִיפְנָא מִינָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״שָׂשׂוֹן וְשִׂמְחָה יַשִּׂיגוּ וְגוֹ׳״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שִׂמְחָה לְשָׂשׂוֹן: אֲנָא עֲדִיפְנָא מִינָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״שִׂמְחָה וְשָׂשׂוֹן לַיְּהוּדִים״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שָׂשׂוֹן לְשִׂמְחָה: חַד יוֹמָא שָׁבְקוּךְ וְשַׁוְּיוּךְ פַּרְוַונְקָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי בְשִׂמְחָה תֵצֵאוּ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שִׂמְחָה לְשָׂשׂוֹן: חַד יוֹמָא שָׁבְקוּךְ וּמָלוּ בָּךְ מַיָּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּשְׁאַבְתֶּם מַיִם בְּשָׂשׂוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos this verse, the Gemara relates: There were **these two heretics, one named Sason and one named Simḥa. Sason said to Simḥa: I am superior to you, as it is written: "They shall obtain joy [*sason*] and happiness [*simḥa*],** and sorrow and sighing shall flee" (Isaiah 35:10). The verse mentions joy first. **Simḥa said to Sason,** On the contrary, **I am superior to you, as it is written: "There was happiness [*simḥa*] and joy [*sason*] for the Jews"** (Esther 8:17). **Sason said to Simḥa: One day they will dismiss you and render you a messenger [*parvanka*], as it is written: "For you shall go out with happiness [*simḥa*]"** (Isaiah 55:12). **Simḥa said to Sason: One day they will dismiss you and draw water with you, as it is written: "With joy [*sason*] you shall draw water."**

The Gemara now introduces a playful interlude involving two *minim* — heretics. In Talmudic usage, *minim* very often refers to Jewish Christians. One heretic was named *Sasson* (Joy) and the other *Simcha* (Joy in Hebrew). Sasson said to Simcha: *ana adifa minach* — I am greater than you — for the verse says *sasson v'simcha yasigu* (Yeshayahu 35:10), listing *sasson* first. Simcha replied: *ana adifa minach* — I am greater — for the verse says *simcha v'sasson laYehudim* (Esther 8:17), listing *simcha* first.

Then Sasson turned the tables: *chad yoma shavkuich v'shavyuich parvanka* — one day they will abandon you and make you a mere attendant who runs before the procession — *ki b'simcha tetzeu* (Yeshayahu 55:12), you shall go out *with* simcha, meaning *simcha* becomes the one who goes, while others simply accompany. Simcha retorted: *chad yoma shavkuich u'malo bach maya* — one day they will turn you into a flask and fill you with water — *ushavtem mayim besasson*, you shall draw water *with* sasson, meaning *sasson* is the vessel. The wordplay on the verse establishing *nisuch hamayim* — *ushavtem mayim besasson* — is of course the structural link to the surrounding discussion.

### Rabbi Abbahu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא דִּשְׁמֵיהּ שָׂשׂוֹן לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: עֲתִידִיתוּ דִּתְמַלּוּ לִי מַיִם לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּשְׁאַבְתֶּם מַיִם בְּשָׂשׂוֹן״, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הֲוָה כְּתִיב ״לְשָׂשׂוֹן״ — כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״בְּשָׂשׂוֹן״ — מַשְׁכֵּיהּ דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא מְשַׁוֵּינַן לֵיהּ גּוֹדָא וּמָלֵינַן בֵּיהּ מַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: **A certain heretic named Sason said to Rabbi Abbahu: You are** all **destined to draw water for me in the World-to-Come, as it is written: "With *sason* you shall draw water."** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: If it had been written: For *sason*,** it would have been **as you say; now that it is written: With *sason*,** it means that **the skin of that man,** you, **will be rendered a wineskin, and we will draw water with it.**

The same verse then features in a more pointed exchange. A *min* whose name was *Sasson* said to Rabbi Abbahu: *atiditu d'timlu li maya l'alma d'atei* — in the World to Come, you Jews will draw water for me, for the verse says *ushavtem mayim besasson* — you will draw water *for Sasson*, for me. Rabbi Abbahu replied: *ei hava ktiv l'Sasson*, had it been written *for Sasson* — with a *lamed* of purpose — your reading would have some basis. *Hashta d'chtiv b'sasson* — now that it says *with Sasson* — with a *beis* of accompaniment — *mashka d'hahu gavra mashvinan leih guda u'malina beih maya* — we will make a flask out of the skin of that man and fill it with water. The mockery turns on a simple grammatical distinction: *besasson* with a *beis* means *with joy*, not *for Sasson*. Rabbi Abbahu, who was based in the heavily pagan and religiously mixed city of Caesarea, is repeatedly found in these sharp exchanges with *minim*. The Gemara's broader point may be a general deflation of the heretics' enterprise of reading Scriptural support for their positions — they simply cannot read the text with precision.

## Right Turn Rule

### Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלָה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לִשְׂמֹאלוֹ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הָעוֹלִים לְמִזְבֵּחַ — עוֹלִין דֶּרֶךְ יָמִין וּמַקִּיפִין וְיוֹרְדִין דֶּרֶךְ שְׂמֹאל. חוּץ מִן הָעוֹלֶה לִשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ, שֶׁעוֹלִין דֶּרֶךְ שְׂמֹאל וְחוֹזְרִין עַל הֶעָקֵב. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: נִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם, וְנִיסּוּךְ הַיַּיִן, וְעוֹלַת הָעוֹף כְּשֶׁרָבְתָה בַּמִּזְרָח.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: The priest **ascended the ramp** of the altar **and turned to his left. The Sages taught: All who ascend the altar ascend** and turn **via** the **right, and circle** the altar, **and descend via** the **left.** This is the case **except for one ascending** to perform one of **these three tasks, as** the ones who perform these tasks **ascend via** the **left, and** then **turn on** their **heel and return** in the direction that they came. **And** these tasks **are: The water libation, and the wine libation, and the bird** sacrificed as **a burnt-offering when there were** too **many** priests engaged in the sacrifice of these burnt-offerings **in the** preferred location **east** of the altar. When that was the case, additional priests engaged in sacrificing the same offering would pinch the neck of the bird west of the altar.

The *mishnah* had stated that the *kohen* performing *nisuch hamayim* ascended the ramp and turned left. The Gemara now contextualizes this within the general rule. *Tanu rabanan*: *kol ha'olim l'mizbeach olin derech yamin* — all who ascend the *mizbei'ach* ramp do so on the right side — *u'makifin v'yordin derech smol* — they circle and descend on the left. This ensures continuous right-ward traffic flow on the ramp and altar, reflecting the general halachic preference for the right.

### Three Exceptions

Three rituals are exceptions: *nisuch hamayim*, *nisuch hayayin*, and bird *olot* when they are in excess on the eastern side of the *mizbei'ach*. Normally, bird *olot* were performed on the eastern side and bird *chataot* on a different location. But *olah* offerings were among the cheapest to bring — a bird cost very little — and therefore were extremely common. When there were too many bird *olot* to accommodate on the east, the overflow was handled elsewhere. In all three cases, rather than circling the ramp to arrive at the relevant location, the *kohen* made an immediate left turn upon reaching the top of the ramp and went directly to the appropriate corner.

### Smoke Hazard

Rashi explains the reason for these exceptions. It is not merely a matter of efficiency. The standard rule of circling rightward is a religious preference, not a mere traffic protocol. Yet it is overridden here because the southwest corner — where *nisuch hamayim* and *nisuch hayayin* were performed — was particularly smoke-laden, being near the main fire of the *mizbei'ach*. A bird brought all the way around through that smoke would die from inhalation before it could be offered. Similarly, wine or water carried that route would become smoky and unfit for use. Therefore the immediate left turn was permitted as a departure from the general rule.

## Black Funnels

### Wine Staining

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, שֶׁהָיוּ מַשְׁחִירִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּיַיִן מַשְׁחִיר, דְּמַיָּא אַמַּאי מַשְׁחִיר? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: עֵירָה שֶׁל מַיִם לְתוֹךְ שֶׁל יַיִן, וְשֶׁל יַיִן לְתוֹךְ שֶׁל מַיִם יָצָא — שֶׁל מַיִם אָתֵי לְאַשְׁחוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: Rabbi Yehuda said that they were limestone, not silver, basins, **but they would blacken** due to the wine. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the basin **for wine blackened** due to the wine; however, **why did the** basin **for water blacken?** The Gemara answers: **Since the Master said** in the mishna: However, if **one** inadvertently **poured** the contents of the basin **of water into** the basin **of wine or** the contents of the basin **of wine into** the basin **of water, he fulfilled** his obligation. Then even the basin **for water** would **come to blacken** over the course of time as well.

The *mishnah* stated, according to Rabbi Yehudah's position, that the funnels appeared black, stained by wine. The Gemara now raises a difficulty: *bishlama deyayin mashcher* — I understand why the wine funnel was black, it was used for wine all year long. *Ella demaya amai mashcher* — but why was the water funnel black? It only had water poured through it. Water does not stain.

### Post-Facto

The answer: *afilu de'amar mar irah shel mayim letoch shel yayin v'shel yayin letoch shel mayim yatza* — since the ruling is that if one accidentally poured the water into the wine funnel or the wine into the water funnel *bidi'avad* it is still valid, the wine funnel was not the only vessel through which wine ever passed. Wine was sometimes poured down the water funnel by mistake, and since the error was *bidi'avad* acceptable, it presumably happened with some regularity — enough to stain the water funnel as well.

### Psychology

What is striking about this answer is not merely the legal point but the psychological one the Gemara implicitly makes. Rather than simply noting that people occasionally made mistakes, the Gemara introduces the *bidi'avad* ruling as the *explanation* for the frequency of the mistake. Once the officiant knew that pouring wine into the wrong funnel was not catastrophic — that *bidi'avad* he was *yotze* — he became less careful. The awareness of leniency generated the error. The halachic ruling that mistakes are tolerable after the fact actually made mistakes more likely to occur. This is a remarkable observation about the relationship between leniency and human behavior, embedded quietly within an otherwise technical discussion.

## Funnel Viscosity

### R. Yehudah View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנוּקָּבִים כְּמִין שְׁנֵי חוֹטָמִין וְכוּ׳. לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא וְלָא רַבָּנַן, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּלּוֹג הָיָה מְנַסֵּךְ כׇּל שְׁמוֹנָה. דְּאִי רַבָּנַן, — כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **And** the two basins were **perforated** at the bottom with **two thin,** perforated, **nose-like** protrusions, one broad and one thin. The Gemara asks: **Let us say** that **the mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda and not** with that of **the Rabbis, as we learned** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says: One would pour** the water **with** a vessel that had a capacity of **one *log*** on **all eight** days of the Festival, unlike the wine libation, for which a three-*log* basin was used. According to his opinion, there is a difference between the capacity of the wine vessel and that of the water vessel; therefore, it is clear why the opening in the wine vessel was broader. **As, if** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, they are the same** as the capacity of the water basin, three *log*. Why, then, were there different sized openings?

The Gemara now raises a question about the different-sized funnel openings. The *mishnah* records that one funnel had a wider opening and one a narrower opening — *echad mevo'e v'echad dak*. The Gemara suggests: perhaps the *mishnah* follows the position of Rabbi Yehudah specifically. Rabbi Yehudah held that the water quantity was only one *log*, whereas the wine quantity was three *log* — the standard libation amount. Given that disparity in volume, it would make excellent sense to have different-sized openings: the wine needed a wider funnel to drain three *log* at the same rate as one *log* of water.

### Viscosity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן: חַמְרָא סְמִיךְ, מַיָּא קְלִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** the reason for the different-sized openings is that **wine is thick** and **water is thin,** and therefore wine flows more slowly than water. In order to ensure that the emptying of both basins would conclude simultaneously, the wine basin required a wider opening.

But the Gemara immediately pushes back: even according to the Rabbis — who held that both wine and water were three *log* — different openings would still be needed. *Chamra samich maya kalish* — wine is viscous, water is thin. Even with equal volumes, the wine would drain more slowly than the water through an identical opening. To achieve simultaneous completion — *kedei sheyehu shneihem kalin b'vas achas* — one must widen the wine funnel even when quantities are identical.

### Braita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — ״רָחָב״ וְ״קָצָר״ אִית לֵיהּ. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי קַשְׂווֹאוֹת הָיוּ שָׁם, אֶחָד שֶׁל מַיִם וְאֶחָד שֶׁל יַיִן. שֶׁל יַיִן — פִּיהָ רָחָב, שֶׁל מַיִם — פִּיהָ קָצָר, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם כָּלִין בְּבַת אַחַת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **So too, it is reasonable** to establish that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, **as, if** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** unlike the description of the two openings in the mishna as broad and thin, elsewhere he **is of** the opinion that the openings **as wide and narrow, as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: There were two** small **pipes there, one for water and one for wine. The mouth of** the pipe **for wine was wide and the mouth of** the pipe **for water was narrow, so that** the emptying of both basins **would conclude simultaneously.** The disparity between wide and narrow is greater than the disparity between broad and thin, thereby facilitating the simultaneous emptying of the three-*log* and one-*log* basins according to Rabbi Yehuda. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that the mishna is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

The Gemara adds that there is actually good textual evidence that the *mishnah* does not follow Rabbi Yehudah's position. Rabbi Yehudah, in a *braita*, describes the funnels using different terminology: *rachav v'katzar* — wide and narrow. The *mishnah*, by contrast, uses *mevo'e vedak* — thick and thin. These are distinct formulations, which suggests the *mishnah* reflects the *Rabbis*' position and not Rabbi Yehudah's.

### Conclusion

The conclusion is therefore that even the Rabbis — who held equal volumes of wine and water — required differently sized funnel openings, because viscosity alone is sufficient grounds to need a wider opening for wine. The purpose in both accounts is the same: *kedei sheyehu shneihem kalin b'vas achas* — wine and water must complete their flow simultaneously, and physics requires adjusting for the difference in fluidity. With this, the Gemara's treatment of *nisuch hamayim* on this daf comes to a close, with further discussion to continue on the following daf.
