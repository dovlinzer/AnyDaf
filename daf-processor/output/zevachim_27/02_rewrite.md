# Zevachim 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelo Bimkomo K'Bimkomo

### Mishnah Blood Placement

The *daf* opens by continuing a list of procedural defects that can invalidate a *korban*. An earlier *mishnah* had addressed the failure to catch the blood in a *kli shareit*; the present *mishnah* turns to invalid placements of the blood on the *mizbe'ach* itself. The cases enumerated are: placing the blood on the *kevesh* (the ramp leading up to the altar) rather than on the altar walls proper; placing it on the section of the altar wall opposite the southeast corner, which lacked a *yesod* (base) beneath it; placing blood that was designated for below the dividing line (*lemata*) above that line (*lemala*), or vice versa; and placing blood designated for the inner altar on the outer altar, or the reverse.

In all such cases the verdict is *pasul* — the sacrifice has been invalidated. The *mishnah* then adds the phrase *ve'ein bo karet*: one who eats the meat does not incur *karet*. This clause is not addressing any question about the *kohen*'s liability for mishandling the blood. Rather, it is clarifying that the meat does not acquire the status of *pigul*. *Pigul*, as will become relevant throughout the discussion, is the specific invalidity that results from a wrong-time thought during the *avodah* and that renders the meat subject to *karet* for one who eats it. The *mishnah* is telling us that although the *korban* has been invalidated, the meat is not *pigul* — whoever eats it does not incur *karet*.

### Shmuel's Position

The *gemara* opens with a striking statement from Shmuel: *pasul basar, aval be'alim nitkapru* — the meat is invalidated, but the owners have achieved *kaparah*. This is a surprising reading of the *mishnah*'s language of *pasul*. One would naturally assume that *pasul* means the entire *korban* is void. Shmuel, however, draws a distinction: when the blood reaches the *mizbe'ach* even in the wrong location, the *korban* does achieve atonement for its owners *bidi'avad*; it is only the meat that may not be eaten.

This introduces a novel conceptual model. Earlier in the *masechet* we encountered cases where the meat could be eaten but the *korban* had not atoned for the owners — a valid *korban* that was simply unconnected to the owners' obligation. Here we have almost the inverse: the owners are atoned for, the obligation is fulfilled, yet the meat cannot be eaten. It is as if the blood's work of *kaparah* is severed from the status of the meat. The *korban* functions on behalf of the owners but remains somehow incomplete in its own identity.

### Scriptural Source

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is Shmuel's reason? The answer is a *pasuk* (Vayikra 17:11): *ve'ani netativ lachem al ha-mizbe'ach lechaper* — "And I have given it to you upon the altar to atone." The verse speaks of blood placed "upon the altar" without specifying the precise location, implying that blood anywhere on the *mizbe'ach* achieves *kaparah*. Hence: *keivan she-higia dam la-mizbe'ach nitkapru be'alim* — once the blood reaches the altar, the owners have atoned.

It is worth noting that *Tosafot* flags two boundary questions. First, does this principle extend to the *kevesh* as well? Is the ramp considered part of the altar for this purpose? Second, does it apply to blood designated for the inner altar? When the *pasuk* says "upon the altar," it likely refers to the outer altar. So the scope of Shmuel's ruling — while certain at its core regarding above-and-below placements — may be subject to debate at the margins.

The *gemara* then presses: *ei hachi, basar nami* — if the *korban* worked, why not permit the meat as well? The *gemara* answers from the same *pasuk*: *lechaper netativ, velo le-davar acher* — "for atonement I gave it," and not for any other purpose. The *pesukim* that specify precise locations for the blood are what govern the *korban*'s full validity, including the permissibility of the meat. The broad verse enables *kaparah* in any location, but the specific directives are what render the meat edible. Without precise placement, the owners atone but the meat remains forbidden.

Shmuel's position is thus formulated by the *gemara* as: *shelo bimkomo k'bimkomo dami* — the wrong place on the *mizbe'ach* is treated as if it were the right place. The "as-if," however, is partial: it is sufficient for *kaparah* but insufficient to validate the meat. The wrong location is close enough to the right place for atonement to take effect, but the full identity of a valid *korban* — including the permissibility of eating the meat — requires precision.

### Challenge to Shmuel

The *gemara* challenges Shmuel from a *mishnah* quoted from a later *perek*: if blood was placed on the ramp and not opposite the *yesod*, or if it was placed above when it should have been below (or vice versa), or inside when it should have been outside, then — *im yesh dam ha-nefesh, yachzor ha-kasher ve-yekabel* — if there is remaining life-blood from the animal, a qualified *kohen* should re-catch the blood and perform the application properly.

This *mishnah* implies that the incorrect placement achieved nothing — it did not invalidate, but neither did it accomplish anything. If there is blood remaining, do it again properly. That reading sits comfortably with a view that the wrong placement is simply meaningless. But it directly challenges Shmuel: if the first application already atoned for the owners, why redo the blood? The *gemara* anticipates a possible answer — perhaps the redo is motivated by the desire to permit the meat — but immediately rejects it: *mi ika zrika d'lo mechaperes ve-sharya basar be-achilah?* Can there be an act of blood application that is not an act of *kaparah* and yet independently permits the meat? The eating of the meat is a byproduct of the *kaparah*; once the *korban* has atoned, no subsequent blood application can stand on its own to permit the meat. So according to Shmuel, the *mishnah*'s instruction to redo the blood proves that the first application achieved nothing — which contradicts Shmuel's position.

### Invalid Kohen's Role

The *gemara* resolves this by contextualizing the *mishnah*. The earlier *mishnah* from which this case is drawn was discussing an invalid *kohen* — and this is reflected in the language *yachzor ha-kasher*, "let the qualified one return," which implies the first application was done by an unqualified person. The *gemara* therefore answers: *ee d'avid kasher hachi nami* — if the first application was performed by a valid *kohen*, then according to Shmuel the deal is sealed. The *korban* has atoned, the meat cannot be eaten, and there is no further blood application that can be performed. The instruction to redo the blood applies only when the first application was done by a *pasul kohen* — in which case it was entirely meaningless, regardless of where on the altar it was placed.

This generates a further question: why is a *pasul kohen*'s action so completely without effect? The *gemara* explores whether a *pasul kohen*'s act of *zrika* should constitute a *deichuy* — a definitive rejection of the *korban* that forecloses any redo. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: if all the *pesulim* — invalid persons — caught the blood while entertaining a thought of the wrong time or wrong place, the instruction is still *im yesh dam ha-nefesh, yachzor ha-kasher ve-yekabel*. The wrong thoughts of a *pasul kohen* do not invalidate.

The *gemara* initially considers inferring from the phrase *kibla, in; zarka, lo* — that this rule (that the *pasul*'s actions are irrelevant) applies only to catching the blood, not to throwing it. Perhaps if the *pasul kohen* performs the *zrika*, that constitutes a *deichuy* and the *korban* cannot be redone. The *gemara* then considers and rejects several formulations, ultimately reaching Rava's principle.

## Invalid Kohen & Intent

### Thoughts & Kabbalah

The *mishnah* establishes that if invalid persons — those who are *pesulim* — performed the *kabbalat ha-dam* (catching of the blood) while entertaining a thought of the wrong time or wrong place, their thoughts do not invalidate the *korban*. The instruction remains: if life-blood is available, a valid *kohen* should re-catch it and proceed properly. This teaches that a *pasul kohen*'s mental intent during *kabbalah* is legally irrelevant.

### Redo Question

The *gemara* asks whether this principle — that the *pasul*'s action is irrelevant and permits a redo — applies equally to the *zrika* (the throwing of the blood onto the altar). The initial reading of the *mishnah* might suggest that only *kabbalah* is covered by the instruction to redo, and that *zrika* by a *pasul* would constitute a *deichuy*, a definitive invalidation that forecloses correction. The *gemara* entertains this reading and then challenges it.

If the *pasul kohen*'s wrong thought does not invalidate during *kabbalah*, why would it invalidate during *zrika*? The *gemara* considers answering that the two stages are different: *kabbalah* is not the act of *kaparah*, so a *pasul*'s participation there is truly null; *zrika* is the act of *kaparah*, and perhaps there the *pasul kohen*'s act carries enough weight to constitute a rejection. The *gemara* then attempts a different answer — that the combination of being *pasul* and having a wrong thought creates an invalidation — but this is rejected as internally inconsistent: since a wrong thought by a *pasul kohen* is irrelevant (as Rava will explain), the combination of the two cannot produce an invalidation that neither produces alone.

### Rava's Principle

Rava articulates the foundational rule: *ein machshava mo'eles ela be-mi she-ra'uy la-avoda* — intent is legally operative only when the person performing the act is qualified for the *avodah*. Rava adds two further conditions: the matter being performed must itself be appropriate to the *avodah*, and it must be performed in the right location. Since a *pasul kohen* is not *ra'uy la-avoda*, his thoughts — whether of *shelo bizmano*, *shelo bimkomo*, or anything else — have no legal traction whatsoever. They cannot invalidate, and they cannot create *pigul*.

### Mishnah Reading

With Rava's principle established, the *gemara* offers the correct reading of the *mishnah*. The clause referring to *kabbalah* onward is not meant to exclude *zrika* from the same leniency. Rather, the *mishnah* teaches that from *kabbalah* and beyond — through *zrika* — a *pasul kohen*'s thoughts are irrelevant. The *mishnah* is not creating a contrast between *kabbalah* and *zrika*; it is marking the point from which the *pasul*'s thoughts become entirely without effect.

The reason is grounded in Rava's principle: a *pasul kohen* does not belong in the role at all from *kabbalah* onward (since *kabbalah* and *zrika* are *avodot* requiring a valid *kohen*). His thoughts, therefore, are incapable of effecting *pasul*. What does remain meaningful even for an invalid person is *shechita*, which does not require a *kohen* at all. If the *pasul kohen* performed the *shechita* with a wrong thought of *shelo bimkomo*, that thought does carry legal weight — because in the act of *shechita*, he is in fact *ra'uy la-avoda*. The *mishnah*'s structure reflects precisely this: prior to *kabbalah*, his role is legitimate and his thoughts operative; from *kabbalah* onward, he is disqualified and his thoughts irrelevant.

## Wrong Place & Pigul

### Two Thoughts Scenario

The *mishnah* introduces a case involving two sequential thoughts during *shechita*. A *kohen* performs the *shechita* while first entertaining an irrelevant thought — such as planning to place the blood at the wrong location on the *mizbe'ach* today — and then entertaining a second thought of *chutz limkomo* (wrong place) or *chutz lizmano* (wrong time). The question is how each thought, and their combination, affects the status of the *korban*.

A thought during *shechita* of merely a different location on the same altar — intending to do it at the wrong place today — is by itself a neutral, legally inconsequential thought. Not every wrong intention invalidates; only the specific categories of *shelo bimkomo* and *shelo bizmano* in the relevant halakhic sense produce legal consequences. A vague plan to do the *zrika* at a slightly different location, intended for immediate execution, is treated as an irrelevant thought. Consequently, a subsequent second thought — of *chutz limkomo* or *chutz lizmano* — still has operative force, because the first thought did not disturb the *korban*'s validity. If that second thought is *chutz limkomo*, the *korban* is *pasul* but there is no *karet*; if it is *chutz lizmano*, the *korban* is *pigul* and *karet* applies.

The *mishnah* also presents the scenario of thinking during *shechita* that one will perform the *zrika* at the wrong place on the *mizbe'ach* tomorrow. This is *pasul* without being *pigul*, and a subsequent second thought — whether of *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* — cannot transform it into *pigul*, since the *korban* is already invalid.

### Pigul Question

This raises a sharp question that the *gemara* frames explicitly: *v'ee shelo bimkomo k'bimkomo dami* — if, according to Shmuel, the wrong place on the altar is treated as equivalent to the right place, then a thought during *shechita* of performing the *zrika* at the wrong place tomorrow should constitute a *pigul* thought. After all, you are planning to do the *zrika* in a way that, according to Shmuel, achieves *kaparah* — and you are planning to do it tomorrow. Why is this not *pigul*?

### Pigul Condition

Mar Zutra answers: *zrika de-sharya basar be-achilah mayti lidei pigul; zrika de-lo sharya basar be-achilah lo mayti lidei pigul*. A *zrika* that would render the meat permissible for eating can generate *pigul* when done at the wrong time; a *zrika* that would not render the meat permissible — even if it achieves *kaparah* — cannot generate *pigul*. The halakhic category of *pigul* is anchored in the *pasuk* (Vayikra 7:18): *im he'achol ye'achel mi-besar zevach shelamav pigul yihiyeh* — the entire framework is about eating the meat. Therefore, a thought during *shechita* about performing *zrika* in a way that will not make the meat edible is outside the *parsha* of *pigul* entirely. A *zrika* at the wrong place on the altar, even granting Shmuel's view that it achieves *kaparah*, does not permit the meat. A thought about such a *zrika* therefore cannot be a *pigul* thought.

### Scriptural Limit

Rav Ashi challenges Mar Zutra on his source: *minalach ha?* Where do you derive this? The *pasuk* states *im he'achol ye'achel mi-besar zevach shelamav pigul yihiyeh* — *mi she-pigulo garam lo*, the *pigul* thought must itself be the cause of the meat's prohibition. If it is something other than the *pigul* thought that renders the meat inedible — namely, the fact that the *zrika* was performed at the wrong location — then the *parsha* of *pigul* does not apply. Here, what makes the meat forbidden is the wrong-place *zrika*, not the wrong-time thought. Consequently, a thought about doing a wrong-place *zrika* tomorrow is not a *pigul* thought.

This being established, the *gemara* asks a further question: *ei hachi, ipsulei nami lo lipsol* — if the thought about a wrong-place *zrika* tomorrow is outside the *pigul* framework entirely, why does it invalidate the *korban* at all? The *gemara* answers, citing Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yehuda: it is comparable to a thought of *hinuach* — a plan simply not to throw the blood at all. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a thought to withhold the blood is itself a sufficiently significant *machshava* to invalidate the *korban*, even without producing *pigul*. Planning to perform the *zrika* at the wrong place tomorrow is treated as tantamount to planning not to perform the *zrika* at all, and that is enough to invalidate.

## Resh Lakish Position

### Resh Lakish Agrees

Resh Lakish now enters the discussion. His position is, at first glance, surprising: *le-olam pasul mamash* — when the *mishnah* says *pasul*, it means categorically invalid, not merely that the meat is forbidden while the *korban* still atones. The *korban* does not atone at all when the blood is placed at the wrong location. However — and this is the striking part — Resh Lakish does not disagree with Shmuel's underlying principle. He actually accepts that in general *shelo bimkomo k'bimkomo dami*: a wrong-place blood application does achieve *kaparah*. What Resh Lakish disputes is the reading of the *mishnah*. He holds that our *mishnah* is dealing with a specific scenario — one involving a wrong-time thought — and that in that scenario the *korban* is categorically invalid.

## Resh Lakish Position…

### Silent vs. Articulated

Resh Lakish resolves the apparent tension between his reading of the *mishnah* and his agreement with Shmuel's principle by distinguishing between two cases: *kan she-natan bi-sh'tika, kan she-natan bi-amira* — one case where the blood was placed silently (without a disqualifying thought), and one where it was placed with an articulated intent. When the blood is placed at the wrong location without any additional wrong thought, Shmuel's principle applies: *kaparah* is achieved, though the meat is forbidden. But our *mishnah* is describing a case where the *kohen* who placed the blood at the wrong location was simultaneously entertaining a *shelo bizmano* thought — a wrong-time intent.

### Pigul Analysis

The proof that the *mishnah* must be describing such a scenario is embedded in the *mishnah*'s own language. The *mishnah* concludes with the phrase *ve'ein bo karet*. This phrase requires explanation: the only circumstance in which eating forbidden sacrificial meat incurs *karet* is in the case of *pigul*. If the *mishnah* needs to explicitly tell us there is no *karet*, it can only be because we might have thought the meat was *pigul*. And the only way we would think that is if the case involves a *shelo bizmano* thought — a thought that would ordinarily create *pigul* — which the *mishnah* then tells us does not result in *pigul* here, precisely because the *zrika* was performed at the wrong location and therefore does not make the meat edible, placing it outside the *parsha* of *pigul* (as Mar Zutra explained). Resh Lakish thus reads the *mishnah* as: the *kohen* performed the *zrika* at the wrong place while thinking *shelo bizmano*; the *korban* is categorically *pasul*; and the *mishnah* specifies *ein bo karet* to tell us that this does not constitute *pigul*, because the wrong-place *zrika* is not the kind of act that can produce *pigul*.

Since Resh Lakish's substantive position mirrors Shmuel's, all the challenges previously mounted against Shmuel apply equally to Resh Lakish, and the *gemara* disposes of them with the same answers, compressing the repetition with the notation *ad midei de-hava a-machsheves hinuach ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda ve-chulei*.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Wrong Place Nothing

Rabbi Yochanan now presents the genuinely dissenting position: *idi va-idi bi-sh'tika* — whether done silently or with a wrong thought, *shelo bimkomo lav k'imkomo dami*. The wrong place on the *mizbe'ach* is simply not equivalent to the right place. There is no partial-*kaparah* model; the *korban* does not atone at all. The *mishnah*'s language of *pasul* means exactly what it says — total invalidity. Consequently, there is no novel conceptual model required, and the *mishnah* need not be read as addressing any special scenario of concurrent wrong-time thoughts. The *pasul* is absolute and unqualified.

However — and this is the defining feature of Rabbi Yochanan's view — the wrong-place *zrika* is so entirely without effect that it does not even invalidate the *korban* in the sense of foreclosing a redo. It is as if nothing happened. Therefore, if life-blood remains, a valid *kohen* may re-perform the *zrika* properly and the *korban* can yet be validated. The *mishnah*'s instruction to redo the blood flows naturally from this: since the wrong-place application was null, the blood can be done again.

The *gemara* notes the difficulty in Rabbi Yochanan's position regarding the phrase *ve'ein bo karet*. According to Resh Lakish, that phrase is necessary because the case involves a *shelo bizmano* thought and one might have assumed *pigul*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds the wrong-place *zrika* is simply nothing — like not having thrown the blood at all — there would never have been any reason to suspect *karet*. The phrase is superfluous. The *gemara* marks this as *kasha* without resolving it.

As for Shmuel, the *gemara* harmonizes him with Resh Lakish: the *ain bo karet* clause informs us that even if one were to perform the *zrika* in the wrong place accompanied by a *pigul*-type thought, the wrong-place context prevents the meat from being rendered edible, and since *pigul* requires that the thought be about an act that would otherwise make the meat edible, no *karet* ever applies. Thus both Shmuel and Resh Lakish read the *mishnah* as: meat forbidden, *korban* atones (in the general case), and no *pigul* can ever attach to a wrong-place *zrika*.

### Reuse Question

Having established that according to Rabbi Yochanan the wrong-place *zrika* achieves nothing, the *gemara* presses a further question: *li-havei ki-nishpach min ha-kli al ha-ritzpah ve-yasfenu* — if the wrong-place application is truly nothing, it should be no different from blood that spilled from the vessel onto the floor. And we have learned that blood spilled onto the floor — if it was originally caught in a *kli shareit* — may be gathered and reused. So why does the *mishnah* require new blood (*dam ha-nefesh*) for the redo? According to Rabbi Yochanan, one should simply scrape the blood off the wrong section of the altar and reapply it correctly.

### Rav Chisda Distinction

The *gemara* answers in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *savar lah k'man de-amar lo yasfenu* — Rabbi Yochanan holds the position that even when a *zrika* achieves nothing, once the blood is on the altar, the altar "absorbs" it (*mizbe'ach kolet*) and it may not be reused. This is Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef's report of Rabbi Yochanan's view: everyone agrees that if blood was placed in the right location — even if the *zrika* was somehow defective — it cannot be gathered and reapplied. The debate is only whether blood placed in the wrong location on the *mizbe'ach* may be reused.

Rav Chisda then sharpens the picture. He reports in the name of Avimi that in fact everyone agrees: blood placed in the wrong position on the same *mizbe'ach* — above when it should have been below, or below when it should have been above — cannot be reused. This is not like blood spilled on the floor. Once on the altar, even at the wrong location, it cannot be gathered for reuse. The sole debate between Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon concerns blood placed on the wrong *altar* entirely — blood designated for the outer altar that was placed on the inner, or vice versa. That scenario, involving a physically distinct object, may be more analogous to spillage onto the floor, and there the opinions diverge. Rabbi Yose holds it may be reused; Rabbi Shimon holds it may not. 

Rav Chisda adds a further observation about relative proximity: blood designated for above the line that was placed below is, in a sense, closer to its correct location than blood designated for below that was placed above, because blood naturally flows downward. Blood that should have been above would eventually drip downward, making below a closer approximation to the right place than above would be for blood meant to go below. This ranking, however, does not affect the operative ruling.

Our *mishnah*, which instructs the *kohen* to obtain *dam ha-nefesh* for the redo rather than gathering blood from the altar, aligns with the view that the blood on the altar cannot be reused — whether following the version of the debate attributed to Rabbi Yochanan (where the debate concerns wrong-position on the same altar) or Rav Chisda's version (where everyone agrees on the same-altar case and the debate is only about the wrong altar).

## Limbs on Altar Rule

### Al Mokdah Rule

The *gemara* now introduces Rav Nachman's observation that the very *mishnah* we have been analyzing provides independent support for certain conclusions about the reuse of blood. The *mishnah* cites the verse *Zot ha-Torah, hi ha-olah al mokdah al ha-mizbe'ach* — "This is the law of the *olah*: it shall remain on the altar's fire." From this verse the principle is derived: once the limbs of a *korban* have been placed on the altar to burn, they do not come down, even if the *korban* was in some respect invalid.

### Rabbi Yehudah Exceptions

Rabbi Yehudah, however, limits this rule. He interprets the repeated and seemingly limiting language in the verse — *zot, hi, ha-olah* — as exclusionary markers (*mi'utim*) teaching that certain severely invalid *korbanot* do not benefit from the *al mokdah* rule. Specifically, Rabbi Yehudah lists: an animal slaughtered at night (*she-nishchata ba-lailah*), one whose blood was spilled (*she-nishpach damah*), and one whose blood was carried outside the precinct of the *Mikdash* (*she-yatza damah chutz la-kela'im*). In these cases, if the limbs were placed on the altar, they must be removed, because the blood never properly performed its function and the *korban* was fundamentally broken from the outset.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Rabbi Shimon takes the more expansive position. His starting point is that only a valid *olah* is mentioned in the verse, which might imply that only a fully kosher *olah* may remain on the altar. But Rabbi Shimon reads the verse's additional language — *Torat ha-olah* — as an inclusive broadening: *Torah achat le-kol ha-olim, she-im alu lo yerdu* — one law for all that ascend, that once on the altar they do not come down.

Rabbi Shimon then proceeds to include in this leniency even cases that Rabbi Yehudah would have required removed: an animal slaughtered at night, one whose blood was spilled, one whose blood exited the precinct, *notar* and *yotzei* (meat left overnight or taken outside), *tamei* animals, animals slaughtered *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* (creating *pigul*), cases where invalid *kohanim* caught and threw the blood, and cases where the blood was applied at the wrong location on the altar.

Rabbi Shimon, however, does draw a line. He excludes: an animal used for bestiality (*ha-rovea ve-ha-nirba*), one set aside for idolatry (*muktzeh*), one worshipped as an idol (*ne'evad*), the *esnan* and *mechir* (a prostitute's hire or the price of a dog), *kilayim* (a hybrid animal), a *treifah* (an animal with a fatal defect), and an animal born by caesarean section (*yotzei dofen*). These must come down even after having been placed on the altar.

### Timing Criteria

The basis for Rabbi Shimon's distinction is: *marbeh ani et elu she-hayah psulan ba-kodesh* — I include those whose disqualification arose within the sacred precincts, i.e., during the course of the *avodah* itself. Whatever problem occurred after the animal entered the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* is an internal *avodah* failure, and the *al mokdah* principle still applies. But the excluded cases — bestiality, idolatry, *treifah*, hybrid species, and the like — are problems that predate the animal's entry into the *Mikdash* entirely. They are constitutional defects, unrelated to the performance of the *avodah*, and such animals are so fundamentally ineligible that even after being placed on the altar they must be removed.

### Wrong Place vs Spilled

The *gemara* draws a critical inference from this *mishnah*. Rabbi Shimon's list includes both the case of spilled blood (*nishpach damah*) and the case of wrong-location blood (*ha-nitnin le-ma'ala she-nasan le-mata, ve-ha-nitnin le-mata she-nasan le-ma'ala*), and in both cases he says the limbs may remain on the altar. But note that Rabbi Yehudah — who disagrees with Rabbi Shimon and says spilled blood requires the limbs to be removed — does not disagree with regard to wrong-location blood. That is, Rabbi Yehudah concedes that wrong-location blood is better than spilled blood: the limbs placed on the altar after a wrong-location *zrika* do not have to come down, even according to Rabbi Yehudah.

Now, if we follow Rabbi Yochanan's view that wrong-place *zrika* achieves nothing — is completely null — then the case of wrong-location blood should be indistinguishable from spilled blood. If Rabbi Yehudah requires the limbs to come down when the blood was spilled, he should equally require them to come down when the blood was placed at the wrong location, since both cases represent a failure of the blood to perform its *avodah*. Yet Rabbi Yehudah does not require removal in the wrong-location case.

The *gemara* concludes: *lav mishum d'kaltah mizbe'ach* — the explanation is that the altar "absorbs" the blood even when it is placed in the wrong location. Even if the wrong-place *zrika* achieves nothing in terms of *kaparah*, the blood has nonetheless reached the *mizbe'ach*, and that contact with the altar carries its own legal consequence. The altar's absorption of the blood — even at the wrong place — is sufficient to distinguish the case from spilled blood, and consequently: (a) the limbs placed on the altar need not come down, and (b) *shema mina lo yasfenu* — the blood may not be gathered from the altar and reused.

This confirms, even within Rabbi Yochanan's framework, that wrong-place blood is not simply equivalent to blood spilled on the floor. It is legally null for purposes of *kaparah*, but it is not null with respect to the altar itself. Once blood touches the *mizbe'ach*, even ineffectually, it is "absorbed" — it cannot be removed and reapplied, and the limbs placed above it are not subject to removal.
