# Zevachim 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelo Bimkomo K'Bimkomo

### Mishnah Blood Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַזֶּבַח לִזְרוֹק דָּמוֹ בַּחוּץ אוֹ מִקְצָת דָּמוֹ בַּחוּץ, לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרָיו בַּחוּץ אוֹ מִקְצָת אֵימוּרָיו בַּחוּץ, לֶאֱכוֹל בְּשָׂרוֹ בַּחוּץ אוֹ כְּזַיִת מִבְּשָׂרוֹ בַּחוּץ, אוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל כְּזַיִת מֵעוֹר הָאַלְיָה בַּחוּץ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who slaughters the offering** with intent **to sprinkle its blood outside** the Temple **or** to sprinkle **part of its blood outside** the Temple, **to burn its sacrificial portions outside** the Temple **or** to burn **part of its sacrificial portions outside** the Temple, **to partake of its meat outside** the Temple **or** to partake of **an olive-bulk of its meat outside** the Temple, **or to partake of an olive-bulk of the skin of the tail outside** the Temple, in all of these cases the offering is **disqualified, and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** one who partakes of it.

The *daf* opens by continuing a list of procedural defects that can invalidate a *korban*. An earlier *mishnah* had addressed the failure to catch the blood in a *kli shareit*; the present *mishnah* turns to invalid placements of the blood on the *mizbe'ach* itself. The cases enumerated are: placing the blood on the *kevesh* (the ramp leading up to the altar) rather than on the altar walls proper; placing it on the section of the altar wall opposite the southeast corner, which lacked a *yesod* (base) beneath it; placing blood that was designated for below the dividing line (*lemata*) above that line (*lemala*), or vice versa; and placing blood designated for the inner altar on the outer altar, or the reverse.

In all such cases the verdict is *pasul* — the sacrifice has been invalidated. The *mishnah* then adds the phrase *ve'ein bo karet*: one who eats the meat does not incur *karet*. This clause is not addressing any question about the *kohen*'s liability for mishandling the blood. Rather, it is clarifying that the meat does not acquire the status of *pigul*. *Pigul*, as will become relevant throughout the discussion, is the specific invalidity that results from a wrong-time thought during the *avodah* and that renders the meat subject to *karet* for one who eats it. The *mishnah* is telling us that although the *korban* has been invalidated, the meat is not *pigul* — whoever eats it does not incur *karet*.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: פָּסוּל בָּשָׂר, אֲבָל בְּעָלִים נִתְכַּפְּרוּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַאֲנִי נְתַתִּיו לָכֶם עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ לְכַפֵּר״ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ דָּם לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, נִתְכַּפְּרוּ בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel says:** When the mishna states that the offering is **disqualified,** it means that the **meat** is unfit for consumption. **But** the **owner** of the offering has **achieved atonement** through it. **What is the reason** for this? It is **as the verse states** with regard to the blood: **"And I have given it to you upon the altar to atone** for your souls" (Leviticus 17:11), from which it is derived that **once the blood reaches** any location on **the altar,** the **owner** of the offering has **achieved atonement.**

The *gemara* opens with a striking statement from Shmuel: *pasul basar, aval be'alim nitkapru* — the meat is invalidated, but the owners have achieved *kaparah*. This is a surprising reading of the *mishnah*'s language of *pasul*. One would naturally assume that *pasul* means the entire *korban* is void. Shmuel, however, draws a distinction: when the blood reaches the *mizbe'ach* even in the wrong location, the *korban* does achieve atonement for its owners *bidi'avad*; it is only the meat that may not be eaten.

This introduces a novel conceptual model. Earlier in the *masechet* we encountered cases where the meat could be eaten but the *korban* had not atoned for the owners — a valid *korban* that was simply unconnected to the owners' obligation. Here we have almost the inverse: the owners are atoned for, the obligation is fulfilled, yet the meat cannot be eaten. It is as if the blood's work of *kaparah* is severed from the status of the meat. The *korban* functions on behalf of the owners but remains somehow incomplete in its own identity.

### Scriptural Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בָּשָׂר נָמֵי! אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְכַפֵּר״ – לְכַפָּרָה נְתַתִּיו, וְלֹא לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** the **meat** should be fit for consumption **as well.** The Gemara responds: **The verse states "to atone,"** emphasizing that **I have given it** to you **for atonement and for nothing else,** e.g., consumption of the meat.

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is Shmuel's reason? The answer is a *pasuk* (Vayikra 17:11): *ve'ani netativ lachem al ha-mizbe'ach lechaper* — "And I have given it to you upon the altar to atone." The verse speaks of blood placed "upon the altar" without specifying the precise location, implying that blood anywhere on the *mizbe'ach* achieves *kaparah*. Hence: *keivan she-higia dam la-mizbe'ach nitkapru be'alim* — once the blood reaches the altar, the owners have atoned.

It is worth noting that *Tosafot* flags two boundary questions. First, does this principle extend to the *kevesh* as well? Is the ramp considered part of the altar for this purpose? Second, does it apply to blood designated for the inner altar? When the *pasuk* says "upon the altar," it likely refers to the outer altar. So the scope of Shmuel's ruling — while certain at its core regarding above-and-below placements — may be subject to debate at the margins.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמוֹ – כִּמְקוֹמוֹ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Apparently,** Shmuel **holds** that blood sprinkled **not in its** proper **place is considered as** though it were sprinkled in **its** proper **place.**

The *gemara* then presses: *ei hachi, basar nami* — if the *korban* worked, why not permit the meat as well? The *gemara* answers from the same *pasuk*: *lechaper netativ, velo le-davar acher* — "for atonement I gave it," and not for any other purpose. The *pesukim* that specify precise locations for the blood are what govern the *korban*'s full validity, including the permissibility of the meat. The broad verse enables *kaparah* in any location, but the specific directives are what render the meat edible. Without precise placement, the owners atone but the meat remains forbidden.

Shmuel's position is thus formulated by the *gemara* as: *shelo bimkomo k'bimkomo dami* — the wrong place on the *mizbe'ach* is treated as if it were the right place. The "as-if," however, is partial: it is sufficient for *kaparah* but insufficient to validate the meat. The wrong location is close enough to the right place for atonement to take effect, but the full identity of a valid *korban* — including the permissibility of eating the meat — requires precision.

### Challenge to Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן בְּאִידַּךְ פִּירְקִין: נְתָנוֹ עַל הַכֶּבֶשׁ שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד הַיְסוֹד; נָתַן אֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה; וְאֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה; וְאֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין בִּפְנִים בַּחוּץ; וְאֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין בַּחוּץ בִּפְנִים – אִם יֵשׁ דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ, יַחְזוֹר הַכָּשֵׁר וִיקַבֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** But **we learned** in a mishna **in another chapter** (32a): If an unfit person **placed** the blood **upon the ramp,** or on the wall of the altar **that** is **not opposite the base** of the altar; or if **he placed** the blood **that is** to be **placed below** the red line **above** the red line, **or** if he placed the blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **below** the red line; **or** if he placed the blood **that is** to be **placed inside** the Sanctuary **outside** the Sanctuary **or** the blood **that is** to be **placed outside** the Sanctuary **inside** the Sanctuary, then **if there is blood of the soul** that remains in the animal, **the** priest **fit** for Temple service **should collect** the blood **again** and sprinkle it on the altar.

This *mishnah* implies that the incorrect placement achieved nothing — it did not invalidate, but neither did it accomplish anything. If there is blood remaining, do it again properly. That reading sits comfortably with a view that the wrong placement is simply meaningless. But it directly challenges Shmuel: if the first application already atoned for the owners, why redo the blood? The *gemara* anticipates a possible answer — perhaps the redo is motivated by the desire to permit the meat — but immediately rejects it:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמוֹ כִּמְקוֹמוֹ, לְמָה לִי יַחְזוֹר הַכָּשֵׁר וִיקַבֵּל? וְכִי תֵּימָא לְהַתִּיר בָּשָׂר בַּאֲכִילָה – מִי אִיכָּא זְרִיקָה דְּלָא מְכַפְּרָא, וְשָׁרְיָא בָּשָׂר בַּאֲכִילָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And if** it would **enter your mind** to say **that** blood sprinkled **not in its** proper **place** is considered **as** though it were sprinkled in **its** proper **place, why do I** need **the fit** priest to **collect** the blood **again** and sprinkle it? **And if you would say** that although the first sprinkling already effected atonement, the second sprinkling is necessary **to permit the meat** of the offering **for consumption, is there** such a concept as **a sprinkling that does not** itself **effect atonement and** yet **permits** the **meat for consumption?** Rather, one must conclude that the first sprinkling did not effect atonement at all, since it was not sprinkled in its proper place.

Can there be an act of blood application that is not an act of *kaparah* and yet independently permits the meat? The eating of the meat is a byproduct of the *kaparah*; once the *korban* has atoned, no subsequent blood application can stand on its own to permit the meat. So according to Shmuel, the *mishnah*'s instruction to redo the blood proves that the first application achieved nothing — which contradicts Shmuel's position.

### Invalid Kohen's Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּיַהֲבֵיהּ כָּשֵׁר – הָכִי נָמֵי; הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּיַהֲבֵיהּ פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **If a fit** priest **had** initially **placed** the blood improperly, the sprinkling would **indeed** have effected atonement after the fact and there would not be another sprinkling. But **here we are dealing with** a case **where an unfit** person **placed** the blood, so that it did not effect atonement at all.

The *gemara* resolves this by contextualizing the *mishnah*. The earlier *mishnah* from which this case is drawn was discussing an invalid *kohen* — and this is reflected in the language *yachzor ha-kasher*, "let the qualified one return," which implies the first application was done by an unqualified person. The *gemara* therefore answers: *ee d'avid kasher hachi nami* — if the first application was performed by a valid *kohen*, then according to Shmuel the deal is sealed. The *korban* has atoned, the meat cannot be eaten, and there is no further blood application that can be performed. The instruction to redo the blood applies only when the first application was done by a *pasul kohen* — in which case it was entirely meaningless, regardless of where on the altar it was placed.

This generates a further question: why is a *pasul kohen*'s action so completely without effect? The *gemara* explores whether a *pasul kohen*'s act of *zrika* should constitute a *deichuy* — a definitive rejection of the *korban* that forecloses any redo. The *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: if all the *pesulim* — invalid persons — caught the blood while entertaining a thought of the wrong time or wrong place, the instruction is still *im yesh dam ha-nefesh, yachzor ha-kasher ve-yekabel*. The wrong thoughts of a *pasul kohen* do not invalidate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיהְוֵי דָּחוּי! דִּתְנַן: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – אִם יֵשׁ דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ, יַחְזוֹר הַכָּשֵׁר וִיקַבֵּל. קִיבְּלוּ אִין, זָרְקוּ לָא; מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי דָּחוּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the mishna is dealing with a case where an unfit person placed the blood initially, then the offering **should be rejected** permanently, **as we learned** in the same mishna: **And** with regard to **all** unfit people **who collected** the blood with the intent to offer it **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area, if there is blood of the soul** that remains in the animal, **the** priest **fit** for Temple service **should collect** the blood **again** and sprinkle it on the altar. Since the mishna states this *halakha* only with regard to collection of the blood, one can infer that specifically if an unfit person **collected** the blood with improper intent, a fit priest can **indeed** collect the blood again, but if they **sprinkled** the blood with improper intent he **cannot. What is the reason** for this? Is it **not because** the offering **is rejected** permanently when an unfit person sprinkles its blood?

The *gemara* initially considers inferring from the phrase *kibla, in; zarka, lo* — that this rule (that the *pasul*'s actions are irrelevant) applies only to catching the blood, not to throwing it. Perhaps if the *pasul kohen* performs the *zrika*, that constitutes a *deichuy* and the *korban* cannot be redone. The *gemara* then considers and rejects several formulations, ultimately reaching Rava's principle.

## Invalid Kohen & Intent

### Thoughts & Kabbalah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; מִשּׁוּם דִּפְסִיל בְּמַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** the offering is rejected **because it is disqualified by** the improper **intent** of the person sprinkling the blood, not because that person is unfit.

The *mishnah* establishes that if invalid persons — those who are *pesulim* — performed the *kabbalat ha-dam* (catching of the blood) while entertaining a thought of the wrong time or wrong place, their thoughts do not invalidate the *korban*. The instruction remains: if life-blood is available, a valid *kohen* should re-catch it and proceed properly. This teaches that a *pasul kohen*'s mental intent during *kabbalah* is legally irrelevant.

### Redo Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, קַבָּלָה נָמֵי! וְעוֹד, מִי פָּסְלָה מַחְשָׁבָה?! וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: אֵין מַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁרָאוּי לַעֲבוֹדָה, וּבְדָבָר הָרָאוּי לַעֲבוֹדָה, וּבְמָקוֹם הָרָאוּי לָעֲבוֹדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then improper intent with regard to **collection** of the blood should disqualify the offering **as well. And furthermore, does intent disqualify** offerings in such cases? **But doesn't Rava say: Intent is effective** to disqualify an offering **only** when it is expressed **by one who is fit for the** Temple **service, and with regard to an item that is fit for the** Temple **service, and in a place that is fit for the** Temple **service?** Here, the one collecting the blood is unfit.

The *gemara* asks whether this principle — that the *pasul*'s action is irrelevant and permits a redo — applies equally to the *zrika* (the throwing of the blood onto the altar). The initial reading of the *mishnah* might suggest that only *kabbalah* is covered by the instruction to redo, and that *zrika* by a *pasul* would constitute a *deichuy*, a definitive invalidation that forecloses correction. The *gemara* entertains this reading and then challenges it.

If the *pasul kohen*'s wrong thought does not invalidate during *kabbalah*, why would it invalidate during *zrika*? The *gemara* considers answering that the two stages are different: *kabbalah* is not the act of *kaparah*, so a *pasul*'s participation there is truly null; *zrika* is the act of *kaparah*, and perhaps there the *pasul kohen*'s act carries enough weight to constitute a rejection. The *gemara* then attempts a different answer — that the combination of being *pasul* and having a wrong thought creates an invalidation — but this is rejected as internally inconsistent: since a wrong thought by a *pasul kohen* is irrelevant (as Rava will explain), the combination of the two cannot produce an invalidation that neither produces alone.

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא תֵּימָא: זָרְקוּ לָא, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: שָׁחֲטוּ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Do not say** that one infers from the mishna that if an unfit person **sprinkled** the blood with improper intent a fit priest **cannot** collect it again. **Rather, say** that one infers that if an unfit person **slaughtered** the offering with improper intent the mistake **cannot** be rectified. Slaughter is valid if performed by one unfit for the Temple service, and therefore an unfit person's improper intent is effective to disqualify the offering. By contrast, collection and sprinkling of the blood must be performed by a fit priest. Consequently, an unfit person's intent with regard to those rites does not disqualify the offering.

Rava articulates the foundational rule: *ein machshava mo'eles ela be-mi she-ra'uy la-avoda* — intent is legally operative only when the person performing the act is qualified for the *avodah*. Rava adds two further conditions: the matter being performed must itself be appropriate to the *avodah*, and it must be performed in the right location. Since a *pasul kohen* is not *ra'uy la-avoda*, his thoughts — whether of *shelo bizmano*, *shelo bimkomo*, or anything else — have no legal traction whatsoever. They cannot invalidate, and they cannot create *pigul*.

### Mishnah Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּפָסְלָה מַחְשָׁבָה? תְּנֵינָא: לְפִיכָךְ הֵן פּוֹסְלִין בְּמַחְשָׁבָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to this interpretation, **what is** the mishna **teaching us?** Can it mean to teach only **that** improper **intent** by an unfit person during slaughter **disqualifies** the offering? **We** already **learn** this in the same mishna (31b), which states: With regard to all those who are unfit for Temple service who slaughtered an offering, their slaughter is valid, and **therefore, these** unfit people **disqualify** the offering **with** improper **intent.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּמִקַּבָּלָה וְאֵילָךְ לָא פָּסְלָה מַחְשָׁבָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּדְרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **This** is what the mishna **teaches us: That** one who is unfit can disqualify the offering only during its slaughter, but **from** the rite of **collection** of the blood **onward** the **intent** of an unfit person **does not disqualify** the offering. **What is the reason** for this? It is **like that which Rava** says: Intent is effective to disqualify an offering only when expressed by one who is fit for the service in question.

With Rava's principle established, the *gemara* offers the correct reading of the *mishnah*. The clause referring to *kabbalah* onward is not meant to exclude *zrika* from the same leniency. Rather, the *mishnah* teaches that from *kabbalah* and beyond — through *zrika* — a *pasul kohen*'s thoughts are irrelevant. The *mishnah* is not creating a contrast between *kabbalah* and *zrika*; it is marking the point from which the *pasul*'s thoughts become entirely without effect.

The reason is grounded in Rava's principle: a *pasul kohen* does not belong in the role at all from *kabbalah* onward (since *kabbalah* and *zrika* are *avodot* requiring a valid *kohen*). His thoughts, therefore, are incapable of effecting *pasul*. What does remain meaningful even for an invalid person is *shechita*, which does not require a *kohen* at all. If the *pasul kohen* performed the *shechita* with a wrong thought of *shelo bimkomo*, that thought does carry legal weight — because in the act of *shechita*, he is in fact *ra'uy la-avoda*. The *mishnah*'s structure reflects precisely this: prior to *kabbalah*, his role is legitimate and his thoughts operative; from *kabbalah* onward, he is disqualified and his thoughts irrelevant.

## Wrong Place & Pigul

### Two Thoughts Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פִּיגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** to burn or eat the offering or sprinkle its blood **outside its** designated **area,** the offering is **disqualified, and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.** But if he had intent to perform one of those actions **beyond its** designated **time,** then it is rendered ***piggul*, and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it.**

The *mishnah* introduces a case involving two sequential thoughts during *shechita*. A *kohen* performs the *shechita* while first entertaining an irrelevant thought — such as planning to place the blood at the wrong location on the *mizbe'ach* today — and then entertaining a second thought of *chutz limkomo* (wrong place) or *chutz lizmano* (wrong time). The question is how each thought, and their combination, affects the status of the *korban*.

A thought during *shechita* of merely a different location on the same altar — intending to do it at the wrong place today — is by itself a neutral, legally inconsequential thought. Not every wrong intention invalidates; only the specific categories of *shelo bimkomo* and *shelo bizmano* in the relevant halakhic sense produce legal consequences. A vague plan to do the *zrika* at a slightly different location, intended for immediate execution, is treated as an irrelevant thought. Consequently, a subsequent second thought — of *chutz limkomo* or *chutz lizmano* — still has operative force, because the first thought did not disturb the *korban*'s validity. If that second thought is *chutz limkomo*, the *korban* is *pasul* but there is no *karet*; if it is *chutz lizmano*, the *korban* is *pigul* and *karet* applies.

The *mishnah* also presents the scenario of thinking during *shechita* that one will perform the *zrika* at the wrong place on the *mizbe'ach* tomorrow. This is *pasul* without being *pigul*, and a subsequent second thought — whether of *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* — cannot transform it into *pigul*, since the *korban* is already invalid.

### Pigul Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָחָר – פָּסוּל. חָזַר וְחִישֵּׁב בֵּין חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ בֵּין חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** If he had intent to sprinkle the blood improperly **the next day,** beyond the permitted time, then the offering is **disqualified.** Nevertheless, it is not rendered *piggul*, because he also had intent to sprinkle the blood improperly. Therefore, if **he subsequently had intent** to sacrifice the offering or consume its meat, **whether beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area,** it is **disqualified and there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it,** because an offering can be rendered *piggul* only if it would have otherwise been fit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמוֹ כִּמְקוֹמוֹ דָּמֵי, הַאי פָּסוּל?! פִּיגּוּל הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if** blood sprinkled **not in its** proper **place** is considered **as** though it were sprinkled in **its** proper **place,** then in **this** case above, where he had intent to sprinkle the blood improperly the next day, is the offering merely **disqualified?** Since **it is** considered as though he had intent to sprinkle the blood properly the next day, shouldn't the offering be rendered ***piggul*?**

This raises a sharp question that the *gemara* frames explicitly: *v'ee shelo bimkomo k'bimkomo dami* — if, according to Shmuel, the wrong place on the altar is treated as equivalent to the right place, then a thought during *shechita* of performing the *zrika* at the wrong place tomorrow should constitute a *pigul* thought. After all, you are planning to do the *zrika* in a way that, according to Shmuel, achieves *kaparah* — and you are planning to do it tomorrow. Why is this not *pigul*?

### Pigul Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: זְרִיקָה דְּשָׁרְיָא בָּשָׂר בַּאֲכִילָה – מַיְיתְיָא לִידֵי פִּיגּוּל, זְרִיקָה דְּלָא שָׁרְיָא בָּשָׂר בַּאֲכִילָה – לָא מַיְיתְיָא לִידֵי פִּיגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said:** Intent with regard to **sprinkling that permits** the **meat for consumption** can **cause** the offering **to** become ***piggul*.** Intent with regard to **sprinkling that does not render** the **meat permitted for consumption does not cause** it **to** become ***piggul*.** Even Shmuel concedes that although the owner achieves atonement, if the blood is sprinkled in an improper place the meat may not be consumed. Accordingly, this offering is not rendered *piggul*.

Mar Zutra answers: *zrika de-sharya basar be-achilah mayti lidei pigul; zrika de-lo sharya basar be-achilah lo mayti lidei pigul*. A *zrika* that would render the meat permissible for eating can generate *pigul* when done at the wrong time; a *zrika* that would not render the meat permissible — even if it achieves *kaparah* — cannot generate *pigul*. The halakhic category of *pigul* is anchored in the *pasuk* (Vayikra 7:18): *im he'achol ye'achel mi-besar zevach shelamav pigul yihiyeh* — the entire framework is about eating the meat. Therefore, a thought during *shechita* about performing *zrika* in a way that will not make the meat edible is outside the *parsha* of *pigul* entirely. A *zrika* at the wrong place on the altar, even granting Shmuel's view that it achieves *kaparah*, does not permit the meat. A thought about such a *zrika* therefore cannot be a *pigul* thought.

### Scriptural Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְמָר זוּטְרָא: מְנָא לָךְ הָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאִם הֵאָכֹל יֵאָכֵל מִבְּשַׂר זֶבַח שְׁלָמָיו... פִּגּוּל יִהְיֶה״ – מִי שֶׁפִּיגּוּלוֹ גָּרַם לוֹ; יָצָא זֶה – שֶׁאֵין פִּיגּוּלוֹ גָּרַם לוֹ, אֶלָּא אִיסּוּר דָּבָר אַחֵר גָּרַם לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Mar Zutra: From where do you** derive **this?** Mar Zutra replied: I derive it from a verse, **as it is written: "And if any of the flesh of his peace offerings be at all eaten** on the third day, it shall not be accepted, neither shall it be credited to he who offers it, **it shall be *piggul*"** (Leviticus 7:18). The verse indicates that only an offering **whose** intent of ***piggul*** alone **caused it** to be disqualified is considered *piggul*. **Excluded is this** case, **whose** intent of ***piggul*** alone **did not cause it** to be disqualified; **rather, the prohibition of something else,** i.e., the intent to sprinkle the blood in an improper location, **caused it** to be disqualified.

Rav Ashi challenges Mar Zutra on his source: *minalach ha?* Where do you derive this? The *pasuk* states *im he'achol ye'achel mi-besar zevach shelamav pigul yihiyeh* — *mi she-pigulo garam lo*, the *pigul* thought must itself be the cause of the meat's prohibition. If it is something other than the *pigul* thought that renders the meat inedible — namely, the fact that the *zrika* was performed at the wrong location — then the *parsha* of *pigul* does not apply. Here, what makes the meat forbidden is the wrong-place *zrika*, not the wrong-time thought. Consequently, a thought about doing a wrong-place *zrika* tomorrow is not a *pigul* thought.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אִיפְּסוֹלֵי נָמֵי לָא לִיפְּסֵל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But **if so,** i.e., if blood applied not in its proper place is considered as though it were applied in its proper place, and the intent to sprinkle the blood the next day does not render the offering *piggul*, then **it should not even be disqualified** due to such an intention. Why, then, does the *baraita* rule that it is disqualified?

This being established, the *gemara* asks a further question: *ei hachi, ipsulei nami lo lipsol* — if the thought about a wrong-place *zrika* tomorrow is outside the *pigul* framework entirely, why does it invalidate the *korban* at all? The *gemara* answers, citing Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak in the name of Rabbi Yehuda:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמַּחְשֶׁבֶת הִינּוּחַ וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** In general, intent to perform the rites of an offering beyond its designated time disqualifies the offering, even when it does not render it *piggul*, **just as is** the case **with regard to the intent to leave** portions of the offering for the next day, as taught in a mishna in the next chapter (35b), **and according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda** there, that intent to leave the blood until the next day rather than sprinkling it on the altar disqualifies the offering even though it does not render it *piggul*.

It is comparable to a thought of *hinuach* — a plan simply not to throw the blood at all. Rabbi Yehuda holds that a thought to withhold the blood is itself a sufficiently significant *machshava* to invalidate the *korban*, even without producing *pigul*. Planning to perform the *zrika* at the wrong place tomorrow is treated as tantamount to planning not to perform the *zrika* at all, and that is enough to invalidate.

## Resh Lakish Position

### Resh Lakish Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם פָּסוּל מַמָּשׁ, וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמוֹ כִּמְקוֹמוֹ דָּמֵי. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן שֶׁנָּתַן בִּשְׁתִיקָה, כָּאן שֶׁנָּתַן בַּאֲמִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites additional opinions with regard to the statement of the mishna that blood misapplied on the altar disqualifies the offering. **Reish Lakish says: Actually,** when the mishna states that the offering is **disqualified,** this is to be taken **literally,** i.e., that the owner does not even achieve atonement through it. **And** nevertheless, blood sprinkled **not in its** proper **place is considered as** though it were sprinkled in **its** proper **place,** and it effects atonement. **And** the apparent contradiction between these two claims is **not difficult: Here, where** misapplication of the blood effects atonement, it is a case **where he placed** it **in silence,** i.e., without specific intent; **there,** in the mishna, it is a case **where he placed** it **with a statement,** i.e., intent to consume the offering beyond its appointed time.

Resh Lakish now enters the discussion. His position is, at first glance, surprising: *le-olam pasul mamash* — when the *mishnah* says *pasul*, it means categorically invalid, not merely that the meat is forbidden while the *korban* still atones. The *korban* does not atone at all when the blood is placed at the wrong location. However — and this is the striking part — Resh Lakish does not disagree with Shmuel's underlying principle. He actually accepts that in general *shelo bimkomo k'bimkomo dami*: a wrong-place blood application does achieve *kaparah*. What Resh Lakish disputes is the reading of the *mishnah*. He holds that our *mishnah* is dealing with a specific scenario — one involving a wrong-time thought — and that in that scenario the *korban* is categorically invalid.

## Resh Lakish Position…

### Silent vs. Articulated

Resh Lakish resolves the apparent tension between his reading of the *mishnah* and his agreement with Shmuel's principle by distinguishing between two cases: *kan she-natan bi-sh'tika, kan she-natan bi-amira* — one case where the blood was placed silently (without a disqualifying thought), and one where it was placed with an articulated intent. When the blood is placed at the wrong location without any additional wrong thought, Shmuel's principle applies: *kaparah* is achieved, though the meat is forbidden. But our *mishnah* is describing a case where the *kohen* who placed the blood at the wrong location was simultaneously entertaining a *shelo bizmano* thought — a wrong-time intent.

### Pigul Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: חִישֵּׁב לִיתֵּן אֶת הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַטָּה לְמַעְלָה; לְמַעְלָה לְמַטָּה עַד מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמַּחְשֶׁבֶת הִינּוּחַ וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** Since Reish Lakish agrees with the statement of Shmuel that blood applied not in its proper place is considered as though it were applied in its proper place, the Gemara poses the same difficulties to the statement of Reish Lakish as posed above to Shmuel: **We learned** in a *baraita*: If one slaughtered an offering and **had intent to place** the blood **that is** to be **placed below** the red line **above** the red line, or to place the blood that is to be placed **above** the red line **below** the red line, etc., **until** the response of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: **Just as is** the case **with regard to the intent to leave** portions of the offering for the next day, **and according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, etc.**

The proof that the *mishnah* must be describing such a scenario is embedded in the *mishnah*'s own language. The *mishnah* concludes with the phrase *ve'ein bo karet*. This phrase requires explanation: the only circumstance in which eating forbidden sacrificial meat incurs *karet* is in the case of *pigul*. If the *mishnah* needs to explicitly tell us there is no *karet*, it can only be because we might have thought the meat was *pigul*. And the only way we would think that is if the case involves a *shelo bizmano* thought — a thought that would ordinarily create *pigul* — which the *mishnah* then tells us does not result in *pigul* here, precisely because the *zrika* was performed at the wrong location and therefore does not make the meat edible, placing it outside the *parsha* of *pigul* (as Mar Zutra explained). Resh Lakish thus reads the *mishnah* as: the *kohen* performed the *zrika* at the wrong place while thinking *shelo bizmano*; the *korban* is categorically *pasul*; and the *mishnah* specifies *ein bo karet* to tell us that this does not constitute *pigul*, because the wrong-place *zrika* is not the kind of act that can produce *pigul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **We learned** in the mishna that if the blood was misapplied on the altar, the offering is **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** one who partakes of the meat. **Granted, according to Reish Lakish,** who explains that the mishna is referring to one who expresses intent to sacrifice or consume the offering beyond its designated time, **this is** the reason **that** the *tanna* **teaches: Disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it,** to stress that although one sprinkled the blood with intent of *piggul*, since the sprinkling was performed improperly, his intent does not render the offering *piggul*, and one who partakes of it is not liable to receive *karet*.

Since Resh Lakish's substantive position mirrors Shmuel's, all the challenges previously mounted against Shmuel apply equally to Resh Lakish, and the *gemara* disposes of them with the same answers, compressing the repetition with the notation *ad midei de-hava a-machsheves hinuach ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda ve-chulei*.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Wrong Place Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי שֶׁנָּתַן בִּשְׁתִיקָה, וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמוֹ לָאו כִּמְקוֹמוֹ דָּמֵי. וְהָא דְּאִיכָּא דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ, הָא דְּלֵיכָּא דַּם הַנֶּפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Both **here and there,** i.e., in the mishna here as well as in the mishna in the next chapter (32a), it is a case **where he placed** the blood **in silence. And** the mishna here rules that the offering is completely disqualified because blood applied **not in its** proper **place is not considered as** though it were applied in **its** proper **place. And that** mishna in the next chapter, which states that the blood may be collected and sprinkled again, is referring to a case **where there is blood of the soul** left in the animal to sprinkle again, while **this** mishna is referring to a case **where there is no blood of the soul** left.

Rabbi Yochanan now presents the genuinely dissenting position: *idi va-idi bi-sh'tika* — whether done silently or with a wrong thought, *shelo bimkomo lav k'imkomo dami*. The wrong place on the *mizbe'ach* is simply not equivalent to the right place. There is no partial-*kaparah* model; the *korban* does not atone at all. The *mishnah*'s language of *pasul* means exactly what it says — total invalidity. Consequently, there is no novel conceptual model required, and the *mishnah* need not be read as addressing any special scenario of concurrent wrong-time thoughts. The *pasul* is absolute and unqualified.

However — and this is the defining feature of Rabbi Yochanan's view — the wrong-place *zrika* is so entirely without effect that it does not even invalidate the *korban* in the sense of foreclosing a redo. It is as if nothing happened. Therefore, if life-blood remains, a valid *kohen* may re-perform the *zrika* properly and the *korban* can yet be validated. The *mishnah*'s instruction to redo the blood flows naturally from this: since the wrong-place application was null, the blood can be done again.

The *gemara* notes the difficulty in Rabbi Yochanan's position regarding the phrase *ve'ein bo karet*. According to Resh Lakish, that phrase is necessary because the case involves a *shelo bizmano* thought and one might have assumed *pigul*. But according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds the wrong-place *zrika* is simply nothing — like not having thrown the blood at all — there would never have been any reason to suspect *karet*. The phrase is superfluous. The *gemara* marks this as *kasha* without resolving it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי אֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who explains that the mishna is referring to a case where the blood was sprinkled with no specific intent, of **what** necessity is the clause: **There is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it?** Since the offering is disqualified because the blood was placed not in its proper place, and there was no intent of *piggul*, why would one think that there should be liability for *karet*? The Gemara responds: Indeed, this clause is **difficult** for Rabbi Yoḥanan.

As for Shmuel, the *gemara* harmonizes him with Resh Lakish: the *ain bo karet* clause informs us that even if one were to perform the *zrika* in the wrong place accompanied by a *pigul*-type thought, the wrong-place context prevents the meat from being rendered edible, and since *pigul* requires that the thought be about an act that would otherwise make the meat edible, no *karet* ever applies. Thus both Shmuel and Resh Lakish read the *mishnah* as: meat forbidden, *korban* atones (in the general case), and no *pigul* can ever attach to a wrong-place *zrika*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִשְׁמוּאֵל, מַאי אֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִם נָתַן בְּמַחְשָׁבָה – פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Shmuel,** of **what** necessity is the clause: **There is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it?** The Gemara responds: **This** is what the mishna **is saying: If one placed** the blood improperly **with intent** that would otherwise render the offering *piggul*, the offering is **disqualified, but there is no** liability for ***karet* for** burning or partaking of **it,** because such sprinkling would not have permitted the meat for consumption.

### Reuse Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – לֶיהֱוֵי כִּי נִשְׁפַּךְ מִן הַכְּלִי עַל הָרִצְפָּה – וְיַאַסְפֶנּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And** according to **Rabbi Yoḥanan, if** blood sprinkled **not in its** proper **place is not considered as** though it were sprinkled in **its** proper **place, it should be as if it spilled from the** service **vessel onto the floor, and let** the priest **gather it up** and sprinkle it again properly. Why, then, does the mishna rule that it is disqualified?

Having established that according to Rabbi Yochanan the wrong-place *zrika* achieves nothing, the *gemara* presses a further question: *li-havei ki-nishpach min ha-kli al ha-ritzpah ve-yasfenu* — if the wrong-place application is truly nothing, it should be no different from blood that spilled from the vessel onto the floor. And we have learned that blood spilled onto the floor — if it was originally caught in a *kli shareit* — may be gathered and reused. So why does the *mishnah* require new blood (*dam ha-nefesh*) for the redo? According to Rabbi Yochanan, one should simply scrape the blood off the wrong section of the altar and reapply it correctly.

### Rav Chisda Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּמַאן דַּאֲמַר לֹא יַאַסְפֶנּוּ. דְּאָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בַּנִּיתָּנִים לְמַעְלָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַעְלָה, לְמַטָּה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה; שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִצְוָתָן – לֹא יַאַסְפֶנּוּ. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בַּנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה, לְמַטָּה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַעְלָה – שֶׁרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֹא יַאַסְפֶנּוּ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יַאַסְפֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The *tanna* of the mishna **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says: He may not gather it up. As Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: All concede with regard to** the blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **that** if **one placed it above** the red line, and likewise with regard to the blood that is to be placed **below** the red line if one **placed it below** the red line, **not in accordance with** the procedure dictated by **its mitzva,** e.g., with the left hand or with improper intent, **he may not gather it up** again. **They disagree only with regard to** the blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **that one placed below** the red line, and blood that is to be placed **below** the red line **that one placed above** the red line, **as Rabbi Yosei says: He may not gather it up, and Rabbi Shimon says: He may gather it up.**

The *gemara* answers in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *savar lah k'man de-amar lo yasfenu* — Rabbi Yochanan holds the position that even when a *zrika* achieves nothing, once the blood is on the altar, the altar "absorbs" it (*mizbe'ach kolet*) and it may not be reused. This is Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef's report of Rabbi Yochanan's view: everyone agrees that if blood was placed in the right location — even if the *zrika* was somehow defective — it cannot be gathered and reapplied. The debate is only whether blood placed in the wrong location on the *mizbe'ach* may be reused.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר לֹא יַאַסְפֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And our mishna is in accordance with the statement of the one who says: He may not gather it up.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִין בַּנִּיתָּנִין לְמַטָּה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַעְלָה – שֶׁלֹּא יַאַסְפֶנּוּ; וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן בַּנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה – הוֹאִיל וְדָמִים הָעֶלְיוֹנִים לְמַטָּה הֵן בָּאִים. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בַּנִּיתָּנִין לִפְנִים שֶׁנְּתָנָן בַּחוּץ, בַּחוּץ שֶׁנְּתָנָן לִפְנִים – שֶׁרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יַאַסְפֶנּוּ, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לֹא יַאַסְפֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda** says that **Avimi says: Everyone concedes with regard to** the blood **that is** to be **placed below** the red line **that** if **one placed it above** the red line **he may not gather it up** again. **And all the more so with regard to the** blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **that one placed below** the red line, **since the blood** placed **above** the red line will eventually run down the side of the altar and **reach below** the red line. **They disagree only with regard to** the blood **that is** to be **placed inside** the Sanctuary **that one placed outside** on the external altar, or blood that is to be placed **outside that one placed inside, as Rabbi Yosei says: He may gather it up,** as though it had spilled on the floor, **and Rabbi Shimon says: He may not gather it up,** because the blood was nevertheless placed on an altar.

Rav Chisda then sharpens the picture. He reports in the name of Avimi that in fact everyone agrees: blood placed in the wrong position on the same *mizbe'ach* — above when it should have been below, or below when it should have been above — cannot be reused. This is not like blood spilled on the floor. Once on the altar, even at the wrong location, it cannot be gathered for reuse. The sole debate between Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon concerns blood placed on the wrong *altar* entirely — blood designated for the outer altar that was placed on the inner, or vice versa. That scenario, involving a physically distinct object, may be more analogous to spillage onto the floor, and there the opinions diverge. Rabbi Yose holds it may be reused; Rabbi Shimon holds it may not. 

Rav Chisda adds a further observation about relative proximity: blood designated for above the line that was placed below is, in a sense, closer to its correct location than blood designated for below that was placed above, because blood naturally flows downward. Blood that should have been above would eventually drip downward, making below a closer approximation to the right place than above would be for blood meant to go below. This ranking, however, does not affect the operative ruling.

Our *mishnah*, which instructs the *kohen* to obtain *dam ha-nefesh* for the redo rather than gathering blood from the altar, aligns with the view that the blood on the altar cannot be reused — whether following the version of the debate attributed to Rabbi Yochanan (where the debate concerns wrong-position on the same altar) or Rav Chisda's version (where everyone agrees on the same-altar case and the debate is only about the wrong altar).

## Limbs on Altar Rule

### Al Mokdah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״זֹאת הִיא הָעוֹלָה״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ מִיעוּטִין; פְּרָט לְנִשְׁחֲטָה בַּלַּיְלָה וְשֶׁנִּשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ וְשֶׁיָּצָא דָּמָהּ חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים – אִם עָלְתָה תֵּרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: We learn** in a *baraita* **as well** that if the blood is misapplied on the altar it may not be gathered, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the verse states: **"This** is the law of the burnt offering: **It is that which goes up [*ha'ola*]** on the pyre upon the altar" (Leviticus 6:2), from which it is derived that a disqualified offering that ascended upon the altar shall not descend from it. **These** terms, i.e., "this," "it," and "that," **are** three terms of **exclusion,** which serve **to exclude** three cases of disqualified offerings from this *halakha*: An offering that **was slaughtered at night,** one **whose blood was spilled, and** one **whose blood emerged outside the curtains,** i.e., the Temple courtyard. In these cases, even **if** the offering **ascended** upon the altar **it shall descend.**

The *gemara* now introduces Rav Nachman's observation that the very *mishnah* we have been analyzing provides independent support for certain conclusions about the reuse of blood. The *mishnah* cites the verse *Zot ha-Torah, hi ha-olah al mokdah al ha-mizbe'ach* — "This is the law of the *olah*: it shall remain on the altar's fire." From this verse the principle is derived: once the limbs of a *korban* have been placed on the altar to burn, they do not come down, even if the *korban* was in some respect invalid.

### Rabbi Yehudah Exceptions

Rabbi Yehudah, however, limits this rule. He interprets the repeated and seemingly limiting language in the verse — *zot, hi, ha-olah* — as exclusionary markers (*mi'utim*) teaching that certain severely invalid *korbanot* do not benefit from the *al mokdah* rule. Specifically, Rabbi Yehudah lists: an animal slaughtered at night (*she-nishchata ba-lailah*), one whose blood was spilled (*she-nishpach damah*), and one whose blood was carried outside the precinct of the *Mikdash* (*she-yatza damah chutz la-kela'im*). In these cases, if the limbs were placed on the altar, they must be removed, because the blood never properly performed its function and the *korban* was fundamentally broken from the outset.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״עוֹלָה״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא עוֹלָה כְּשֵׁרָה; מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה בַּלַּיְלָה, וְשֶׁנִּשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ, וְשֶׁיָּצָא דָּמָהּ חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים, וְהַלָּן, וְהַיּוֹצֵא, וְהַטָּמֵא, וְשֶׁנִּשְׁחַט חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וְחוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ, וְשֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִין וְזָרְקוּ אֶת דָּמָהּ, וְהַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה, וְהַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַטָּה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַעְלָה, וְהַנִּיתָּנִין בִּפְנִים שֶׁנְּתָנָן בַּחוּץ, וְהַנִּיתָּנִין בַּחוּץ שֶׁנְּתָנָן בִּפְנִים, וְהַפֶּסַח וְהַחַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** From the term **"burnt offering [*ola*]" I have** derived **only** that **a fit burnt offering** shall not descend. **From where** is it derived **that** the *halakha* **includes** an offering **that was slaughtered at night, or** one **whose blood was spilled, or** one **whose blood emerged outside the curtains, or** one **that was left overnight, or** one **that left** the courtyard, **or** one **that** became **impure, or** one **that was slaughtered** with intent to consume it **beyond its** designated **time or outside its** designated **area, or** an offering for **which an unfit** person **collected and sprinkled its blood, or** a case where **one placed** the blood **that is** to be **placed above** the red line **below** it, **or** where **one placed** the blood **that is** to be **placed below** the red line **above** it, **or** where **one placed** the blood **that is** to be **placed inside** the Sanctuary **outside** on the external altar, **or** where **one placed** the blood **that is** to be **placed outside** the Sanctuary **inside** it, **or a Paschal offering or a sin offering that one slaughtered not for their sake? From where** is it derived that if these offerings ascended they shall not descend?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תּוֹרַת הָעוֹלָה״ – רִיבָּה תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הָעוֹלִין, שֶׁאִם עָלוּ לֹא יֵרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states: "The law of the burnt offering [*ha'ola*],"** literally: That which goes up. The verse **included** under **one law all** items **that ascend** upon the altar, teaching **that if they ascended** the altar, **they shall not descend.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל שֶׁאֲנִי מְרַבֶּה אַף הָרוֹבֵעַ, וְהַנִּרְבָּע, וְהַמּוּקְצֶה, וְהַנֶּעֱבָד, וְהָאֶתְנַן, וְהַמְּחִיר, וְהַכִּלְאַיִם, וְהַטְּרֵיפָה, וְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֹאת״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought **that I** should **include even an animal that copulated with a person, or an animal that was the object of bestiality, or** an animal **that was set aside** for idol worship, **or** an animal **that was worshipped** as a deity, **or** an animal that was given as **payment** to a prostitute **or** as **the price** of a dog, **or** an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds, or** an animal that is **a *tereifa*, or** an animal **born by caesarean section. The verse** therefore **states: "This,"** to exclude these animals from the *halakha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה רָאִיתָ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת אֵלּוּ וּלְהוֹצִיא אֶת אֵלּוּ? מְרַבֶּה אֲנִי אֶת אֵלּוּ – שֶׁהָיָה פְּסוּלָן בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ, וּמוֹצִיא אֲנִי אֶת אֵלּוּ – שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה פְּסוּלָן בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what did you see** as the reason **to include** the former cases **and to exclude** the latter ones? The Gemara responds: **I include these** former cases, **whose disqualification occurred in sanctity,** i.e., in the course of the Temple service, **and I exclude these** latter cases, **whose disqualification did not occur in sanctity** and were disqualified as offerings from the outset.

Rabbi Shimon takes the more expansive position. His starting point is that only a valid *olah* is mentioned in the verse, which might imply that only a fully kosher *olah* may remain on the altar. But Rabbi Shimon reads the verse's additional language — *Torat ha-olah* — as an inclusive broadening: *Torah achat le-kol ha-olim, she-im alu lo yerdu* — one law for all that ascend, that once on the altar they do not come down.

Rabbi Shimon then proceeds to include in this leniency even cases that Rabbi Yehudah would have required removed: an animal slaughtered at night, one whose blood was spilled, one whose blood exited the precinct, *notar* and *yotzei* (meat left overnight or taken outside), *tamei* animals, animals slaughtered *chutz lizmano* or *chutz limkomo* (creating *pigul*), cases where invalid *kohanim* caught and threw the blood, and cases where the blood was applied at the wrong location on the altar.

Rabbi Shimon, however, does draw a line. He excludes: an animal used for bestiality (*ha-rovea ve-ha-nirba*), one set aside for idolatry (*muktzeh*), one worshipped as an idol (*ne'evad*), the *esnan* and *mechir* (a prostitute's hire or the price of a dog), *kilayim* (a hybrid animal), a *treifah* (an animal with a fatal defect), and an animal born by caesarean section (*yotzei dofen*). These must come down even after having been placed on the altar.

### Timing Criteria

The basis for Rabbi Shimon's distinction is: *marbeh ani et elu she-hayah psulan ba-kodesh* — I include those whose disqualification arose within the sacred precincts, i.e., during the course of the *avodah* itself. Whatever problem occurred after the animal entered the *Beit Ha-Mikdash* is an internal *avodah* failure, and the *al mokdah* principle still applies. But the excluded cases — bestiality, idolatry, *treifah*, hybrid species, and the like — are problems that predate the animal's entry into the *Mikdash* entirely. They are constitutional defects, unrelated to the performance of the *avodah*, and such animals are so fundamentally ineligible that even after being placed on the altar they must be removed.

### Wrong Place vs Spilled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא: הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַטָּה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַעְלָה, וּלְמַעְלָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה – וְלָא פְּלִיג רַבִּי יְהוּדָה; מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּקַלְטֵיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ – וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לֹא יַאַסְפֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak continues: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that if **one placed** the blood **that is** to be **placed below** the red line **above** it, **or** if **one placed** the blood that is to be placed **above** the red line **below** it, the offering does not descend from the altar. **And Rabbi Yehuda does not disagree,** even though he holds that if the blood spilled on the ground the offering descends from the altar. **What is the reason** for this? Is it **not because** even if the blood was misapplied, **the altar has absorbed** the blood and it is not considered to have been spilled on the floor? **Conclude from it** that if blood was misapplied on the altar, the priest **may not gather it up** again, in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *gemara* draws a critical inference from this *mishnah*. Rabbi Shimon's list includes both the case of spilled blood (*nishpach damah*) and the case of wrong-location blood (*ha-nitnin le-ma'ala she-nasan le-mata, ve-ha-nitnin le-mata she-nasan le-ma'ala*), and in both cases he says the limbs may remain on the altar. But note that Rabbi Yehudah — who disagrees with Rabbi Shimon and says spilled blood requires the limbs to be removed — does not disagree with regard to wrong-location blood. That is, Rabbi Yehudah concedes that wrong-location blood is better than spilled blood: the limbs placed on the altar after a wrong-location *zrika* do not have to come down, even according to Rabbi Yehudah.

Now, if we follow Rabbi Yochanan's view that wrong-place *zrika* achieves nothing — is completely null — then the case of wrong-location blood should be indistinguishable from spilled blood. If Rabbi Yehudah requires the limbs to come down when the blood was spilled, he should equally require them to come down when the blood was placed at the wrong location, since both cases represent a failure of the blood to perform its *avodah*. Yet Rabbi Yehudah does not require removal in the wrong-location case.

The *gemara* concludes: *lav mishum d'kaltah mizbe'ach* — the explanation is that the altar "absorbs" the blood even when it is placed in the wrong location. Even if the wrong-place *zrika* achieves nothing in terms of *kaparah*, the blood has nonetheless reached the *mizbe'ach*, and that contact with the altar carries its own legal consequence. The altar's absorption of the blood — even at the wrong place — is sufficient to distinguish the case from spilled blood, and consequently: (a) the limbs placed on the altar need not come down, and (b) *shema mina lo yasfenu* — the blood may not be gathered from the altar and reused.

This confirms, even within Rabbi Yochanan's framework, that wrong-place blood is not simply equivalent to blood spilled on the floor. It is legally null for purposes of *kaparah*, but it is not null with respect to the altar itself. Once blood touches the *mizbe'ach*, even ineffectually, it is "absorbed" — it cannot be removed and reapplied, and the limbs placed above it are not subject to removal.
