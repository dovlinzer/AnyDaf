# Bava Kamma 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodshim Kalim Debate

### Opening & Dedication

Today's *daf* is Bava Kamma 13. We pick up four lines from the top with the word *gufa*, and this learning should be in the *zechus* and protection of our *chayalim* and *chayalot* who are right now in the middle of very heavy fighting in Gaza. *Hakadosh Baruch Hu* should protect them and all of *Am Yisrael*.

### Gufa: Full Baraita

The Gemara now returns to a *Baraita* that was cited earlier concerning the position of Rabbi Yose Haglili. Rabbi Yose Haglili held that *kodshim kalim* — lesser holy offerings that may be eaten throughout Yerushalayim — are considered *mamon ba'alim*, still under the proprietary ownership of their owners. This position was invoked to explain a clause in the *Mishnah* stating that the laws of *nezikin* apply only to property that carries no liability for *me'ilah* — *me'ilah* being the *korban* brought for the misappropriation of *hekdesh*. At first glance, this formulation seems to imply that there exist items possessing a degree of sanctity yet falling outside the domain of *me'ilah*, to which *nezikin* would nonetheless apply. Rabbi Yose Haglili identifies those items as *kodshim kalim*: they carry *kedushah*, yet since they are essentially consumed by their owners throughout Yerushalayim and are not regarded as wholly dedicated to God, *me'ilah* does not apply to them. Consequently, if such an animal — say, a *shelamim* ox — were to gore another animal, the laws of *nezikin* would govern the case.

The Gemara now presents the full text of the *Baraita*: *"Umal-ma bashem"* — four lines from the top of 13a — *"lirbos kodshim kalim shehem mamonot, divrei Rabbi Yose Haglili."* The *pasuk* in question concerns one who, while serving as a *shomer* for his fellow's property, denies holding it and takes a false oath, obligating him to bring an *asham*. The Torah characterizes this as a trespass against God. Rabbi Yose Haglili derives from this that even if the object denied was a *shelamim* or other *kodshim kalim* held for one's fellow, the denial still constitutes *v'kichash b'amito* — a wrong against one's fellow — because the *kodshim kalim* remain *mamon ba'alim*, the owner's property.

## Ben Azzai Position

### Shelamim Specifically

Ben Azzai's position in the *Baraita* is formulated similarly but with a notable narrowing: *"lirbos es ha-shelamim"* — to include not *kodshim kalim* in general, but specifically *shelamim*, the paradigmatic form of *kodshim kalim*. This immediately raises the question of what Ben Azzai intends to exclude, and how his position differs from that of Rabbi Yose Haglili.

## Kodshim Kalim Debate (II)

### Gufa vs. Amar Mar

This passage offers a textbook illustration of two Talmudic analytical terms that are frequently encountered but are here juxtaposed in a way that clarifies their distinct functions. *Gufa* — "the body of the matter" — is the signal to step back and examine the full text of something that was previously cited only in excerpt. Earlier, only the Rabbi Yose Haglili clause regarding *mamon ba'alim* was quoted; now the Gemara says *gufa*, let us look at the entire *Baraita*, which also includes the positions of Ben Azzai and Abba Yossi Ben Dostai. *Amar Mar*, by contrast, is nearly the inverse movement: having now placed the complete text before us, the Gemara zooms in and examines each line individually. Having already addressed Rabbi Yose Haglili's position, the Gemara now turns to analyze the Ben Azzai line in detail.

## Ben Azzai Position (II)

### Shelamim Demands

The Gemara asks: *"Amar Mar: Ben Azzai omer lirbos es ha-shelamim — l'maite mai?"* If Ben Azzai specifies *shelamim*, what is he excluding? One possibility raised is that he is excluding *bechor* — the firstborn animal — which is the position that will ultimately be articulated by Abba Yossi Ben Dostai. The logic would be that *bechor* is less *mamon ba'alim* than *shelamim*. But the Gemara challenges this: *"Hashtah u-mah shelamim she-te'unim semichah ve-nesachim ve-tenufah ve-chazeh ve-shok — shelamim amarta mamon ba'alim, bechor mi-ba'ya?"*

*Shelamim* carries a substantial array of *halachos*: the owner must perform *semichah* — the laying on of hands — bring *nesachim* of wine, flour, and oil, and wave the *chazeh ve-shok*, the breast and thigh. The multiplicity of these ritual obligations would seem to reflect a diminished degree of owner control and an elevated sanctity. Yet if even *shelamim*, with all its demands, is nonetheless *mamon ba'alim*, then *bechor*, which carries far fewer such requirements, is obviously *mamon ba'alim* as well. Ben Azzai cannot therefore be excluding *bechor*.

### Ma'aser Exclusion

Rather, says Rabbi Yochanan, Ben Azzai's *shelamim* formulation comes to exclude *ma'aser* — the animal tithe. *Ma'aser* is also a form of *kodshim kalim* eaten throughout Yerushalayim, but it cannot be considered *mamon ba'alim*.

### Bechor vs. Ma'aser

The *Baraita* cited in support makes the distinction precise. Regarding *bechor* the Torah states *"lo yipadeh"* — it cannot be redeemed, meaning its sanctity cannot be transferred to a monetary substitute — yet *venimkar*, it can still be sold. An unblemished *bechor* may be sold by one *kohen* to another *kohen* who will bring it as a *korban*; a blemished *bechor* may be sold alive or after *shechita*, to be eaten outside Yerushalayim. The very fact that *bechor* can be sold is powerful evidence that it remains *mamon ba'alim* — the capacity to sell is a hallmark of ownership.

Regarding *ma'aser*, however, the Torah states *"lo yiga'el"* — a term the Gemara derives to prohibit sale entirely. *Ma'aser* cannot be sold, whether unblemished or blemished, whether alive or slaughtered. Since the owner lacks the power to sell it, it is not *mamon ba'alim*. This is why Ben Azzai, in saying *shelamim*, means to include *shelamim* and *bechor* but exclude *ma'aser*.

One might recall that at the close of the previous *daf*, an argument was introduced that *bechor* is not truly *mamon ba'alim* because the *kohen* receives it not from the owner's voluntary dedication but as a *matnat kehunah* — a priestly gift — such that his stake in it derives *mishulchan gavoah kazachu*, from the table of Heaven, not from any transfer by the owner. The Gemara acknowledges this but notes — as Rashi explains — that we are speaking either of *bechor bechutz l'aretz* or of *bechor* in a time when there is no *Beis HaMikdash*, circumstances that neutralize the *matnat kehunah* dimension of *bechor* and leave it genuinely in the owner's domain.

Now Ravina offers an alternative: he reads this entire discussion — the comparison between *bechor* and *ma'aser* — not as commentary on Ben Azzai's statement about *shelamim*, but rather on the closing statement of Abba Yossi Ben Dustai, who said that Ben Azzai spoke only of *bechor*. According to the first reading, Ben Azzai said *shelamim* (including *bechor*) but not *ma'aser*; Abba Yossi Ben Dustai then contracted that to *bechor* alone. Ravina re-anchors the *bechor*-versus-*ma'aser* analysis at Abba Yossi Ben Dustai's statement instead.

## Abba Yossi Position

### Bechor Only

Abba Yossi Ben Dustai states: *"Lo amar Ben Azzai ela b'bechor bilvad"* — Ben Azzai's ruling applies only to *bechor*. The question immediately arises: *"lemeimra demai?"* — what is this formulation coming to exclude? If the intention is to exclude *shelamim* — to say that *bechor* is the most obviously *mamon ba'alim* because *shelamim* carries heavier obligations like *nesachim* and *tenufah* — the Gemara objects to that logic.

### Alt. Reading

There is an alternative way of ordering these categories. One could argue that *shelamim* is in fact the most obviously *mamon ba'alim* of all: it begins as an ordinary *chullin* animal and only subsequently is sanctified by the owner's deliberate act of dedication. *Bechor*, by contrast, is sanctified from the very moment of birth — *"kadosh merechem"* — and never passes through a stage of being *chullin*. Moreover, from the *kohen*'s perspective, it is received as *matnat kehunah*, *mishulchan gavoah kazachu*. If so, *shelamim* is the clearest case of *mamon ba'alim*, and *bechor* is a more ambiguous case.

### Bechor Paradox

This is the crux of the tension: which is more obviously *mamon ba'alim* — *bechor*, because it carries fewer ritual demands, or *shelamim*, because it originates as the owner's own property freely dedicated? The Gemara entertains both readings.

According to the first reading: *bechor* is the easiest case because of fewer demands; then *shelamim*; then *ma'aser* is excluded entirely. According to the second reading — which is conceptually the more compelling — *shelamim* is the most obvious, *bechor* is less so (due to its innate sanctity and its character as *matnat kehunah*), and *ma'aser* is excluded.

Rabbi Yochanan endorses the second reading, citing the same *Baraita* about the sellability of *bechor* and the non-sellability of *ma'aser* as the basis for Abba Yossi Ben Dustai's exclusion: he is excluding *ma'aser* on the grounds that it cannot be sold. The Gemara observes, however, that this reading creates a textual difficulty: *"hai b'bechor bilvad ka'amar"* — the language "only by *bechor*" implies that *shelamim* is excluded, not that *shelamim* and *bechor* are both included while only *ma'aser* is excluded. The Gemara concludes: *"kashya"* — this is difficult, and the tension is left unresolved.

The conceptual progression, however, remains elegant: *shelamim* is the most *mamon ba'alim*; *bechor* is one degree less so; *ma'aser*, which cannot be sold at all, is the least *mamon ba'alim* of the three and is thus excluded by those who narrow the ruling.

## Rava's Reinterpretation

### Lay Property Reading

At this point the Gemara has completed its treatment of the question of which *kodshim* fall within the domain of *nezikin*. Rava now offers a fundamentally different reading of the *Mishnah*'s language. *"Mai nechasim she'ein bahem me'ilah?"* — what does the *Mishnah* mean by "property in which there is no *me'ilah*"? Rava says it means *"nechasim she'ein bahem din me'ilah"* — property that is entirely outside the discussion of *me'ilah* altogether, that is, non-sanctified lay property. *"U'mani ninhu? Dehedyot"* — what property is that? Ordinary property owned by a layperson. According to Rava, the *Mishnah* is simply distinguishing between lay property and *hekdesh*, and even *kodshim kalim* would be outside the realm of *nezikin*.

### Language Question

The Gemara objects: *"ve'lisni dehedyot"* — if that is the intent, why not simply say "lay property" rather than "property without *me'ilah*"? The Gemara answers *"kashya"* — it is difficult. The *Mishnah*'s terminology remains somewhat awkward on Rava's reading; it might have said "non-*hekdesh*" or "lay property" more directly. It is worth noting that *hedyot* may not have been the standard Tannaitic term for this concept, which could account for the more roundabout formulation. In any case, Rava's interpretation makes the *Mishnah* straightforwardly about the *hekdesh* / non-*hekdesh* divide, without granting any special status to *kodshim kalim*, and would presumably reflect a position not in accordance with Rabbi Yose Haglili.

## Shelamim Gores Case

### Meat vs. Innards

Having established the framework, the Gemara now turns to practical applications. Rav Abba states: *"shelamim she'iziku govin mibesaram ve'eina govin me'imureihen"* — if a *shelamim* animal causes damage, the damaged party collects from the meat of the animal but not from the *imurim*, the sacrificial innards that are burned on the altar.

The scenario runs as follows: Reuven has dedicated an ox as a *shelamim*, and it gores Shimon's cow. According to Rabbi Yose Haglili, *nezikin* applies, so there is an obligation to pay. Since the animal is a *tam* — a non-habitual gorer — Shimon does not collect out of pocket from Reuven; rather, a *tam* obligates payment from the body of the goring animal itself. Shimon therefore acquires rights in the physical body of Reuven's ox up to half the value of his damages. Reuven then brings the animal to the *Beis HaMikdash*, the blood is sprinkled, and Reuven receives credit for having brought his *shelamim*. But then Shimon claims the meat — that meat is effectively his payment, and he eats it. One should bear in mind that the value of the ox is significantly diminished by its status as a *shelamim*: what might have been worth a thousand as a *chullin* animal is worth far less, since the recipient must consume it within two days. Nevertheless, Shimon's claim is to the meat.

### Altar Restriction

Rav Abba further specifies that Shimon cannot collect from the *imurim*. The Gemara immediately asks: *"peshita"* — is that not obvious? The *imurim* ascend to the altar; how could one even imagine that Shimon might collect from them?

### Proportional Division

The answer is that the ruling is necessary for the following case: *"lo tzricha legavo mibsara kneged imurim"* — it is needed to tell us that Shimon may not collect from the meat in lieu of the *imurim*. Consider a scenario where the meat and the *imurim* are each worth a hundred, and Shimon is entitled to a total of a hundred. One might reason: Shimon's claim is to a hundred dollars from the cow; the *imurim* are inaccessible; therefore he should be able to collect his full hundred from the meat. This is precisely what the ruling rejects. The principle established here is one of proportional division: Shimon's rights are distributed proportionally across all parts of the animal. If the meat and *imurim* are each worth half, he has a fifty-dollar claim on the meat and a fifty-dollar claim on the *imurim*. Since the *imurim* go to the altar, his fifty-dollar claim on them is simply lost — he cannot redirect it and collect that additional fifty from the meat.

This is a genuine *chiddush*. The intuition that one ought to be able to collect one's full entitlement from whatever assets are available is a natural one, and the ruling runs squarely against it.

### Two Parties Distinction

The Gemara now asks according to whose view this ruling is stated. *"Ee aliba derabbanan pshita"* — if it is according to the Rabbanan, it is obvious. The Rabbanan hold the principle *"ki leka leishtalumei mehai lo mishtalam mehai"* — when you cannot collect from one party, that does not entitle you to collect the remainder from another. The Gemara illustrates this with a well-known case: an ox pushes a person into a pit and the person dies. In principle, both the owner of the ox and the owner of the pit share causal responsibility for the damage. Yet the owner of the pit is legally exempt — the pit-owner's liability attaches only when an animal falls in on its own, not when it is pushed in by another animal. The Rabbanan say: the initial allocation of liability is fifty-fifty; the fact that one party has a legal exemption does not shift his share onto the other.

The underlying conceptual framework, as Rashi and Tosafos explain on this *daf*, is that both the ox and the pit each caused one hundred percent of the damage — they are not viewed as contributing fifty percent apiece to a shared total. Given that both caused it fully, the damages are allocated fifty-fifty between them. Once that allocation is made, each party's case stands on its own. The pit-owner's exemption is a *get-out-of-jail-free* circumstance that relieves him of his fifty — but it does not transfer that fifty to the ox-owner, who already had a separate, fully adjudicated obligation. The Rabbanan's rule is thus clear: *ki leka leishtalumei mehai lo mishtalam mehai*. Applied to the *shelamim* case, it is therefore obvious that Shimon cannot redirect his claim from the *imurim* to the meat — and so according to the Rabbanan, Rav Abba's ruling is no *chiddush* at all.

### One Animal Case

If so, the ruling must be according to Rabbi Noson. Rabbi Noson holds the opposite principle: *"ki leit lei l'ishtalumi mehai mishtalem mehai"* — when you cannot collect from one, you may collect from the other. And indeed, according to Rabbi Noson, Shimon should be entitled to collect his full hundred from the meat. So why does Rav Abba rule otherwise?

The Gemara offers two answers: *"iba'it eima Rabbanan, iba'it eima Rabbi Noson"* — one can maintain the ruling according to either view.

If one wishes to say it is according to the Rabbanan: their principle of *"ki leka leishtalumei mehai lo mishtalam mehai"* applies specifically *"betrei gufei"* — when there are two distinct bodies, meaning two separate parties being sued. The initial allocation separates the liability between them, and one party's exemption cannot be transferred to the other. But here we are dealing with *"behad gufa"* — one single animal, one single *mazik*. In that case, the Rabbanan would agree that one can say *"mikol heikha d'beina mishtalemna"* — I will collect my full payment from wherever in the animal I can. There is no conceptual basis for pre-dividing a claim within the parts of a single animal.

### One Owner Paradox

This makes the case genuinely surprising. We have one owner and one animal, and yet the ruling still holds that Shimon cannot collect the full hundred from the meat. If we are according to the Rabbanan's rule as just explained — that for a single animal you collect from wherever you can — then Rav Abba's ruling seems to cut against it.

Alternatively, if one wishes to say it is according to Rabbi Noson: Rabbi Noson's allowance to collect from the second party rests on a specific argument available to the damaged party. As the Gemara explains, Rabbi Noson's position is that *"ba'al hashor l'ba'al habor ana torai bevirecha ashkachtei"* — the owner of the ox can say to the owner of the pit: I found my animal in your pit; what I cannot collect from the *tam* ox, I will collect from you, because the pit-owner bears the ultimate underlying responsibility. Rabbi Noson's rule thus derives from the special culpability of the pit-owner as the site where the animal came to rest. But *"aval hacha"* — here, in the *shelamim* case — *"mi matzi amar besra azik imurim lo azik?"* — can one say that the meat did more of the damage than the *imurim*? Of course not. No part of the animal bears greater causal responsibility than any other. The head or horns that gored bear no more guilt attributable to the meat than to the *imurim*. Without that asymmetry of culpability, Rabbi Noson's logic does not apply, and the proportional division stands.

The result is striking: whether one holds like the Rabbanan or like Rabbi Noson, the conclusion is the same — Shimon cannot collect more than fifty from the meat of this *shelamim* ox. The intuitive argument that "I should collect my full entitlement from whatever is available" is rejected on both accounts.

## Toda Gores Case

### Toda Meat/Bread

Rava now presents a parallel case involving a *toda* — a thanksgiving offering. The *toda* is a species of *shelamim* but with the distinctive feature that forty loaves of bread are brought alongside the animal offering. If a *toda* animal causes damage, *"govin mibsara ve'ein govin lachma"* — the damaged party collects from the meat but not from the bread.

### Bread Obligation

The Gemara asks: *"lechem peshita"* — is it not obvious that the damaged party has no claim to the bread? The bread was not the *mazik*; even if it is part of the same *korban*, one only collects from the animal that did the damage. The Gemara answers that the *chiddush* lies in the closing clause: *"nizak ochel basar u'mitkaper meivi lacham"* — the damaged party eats the meat as his payment, while the *mitkaper* — the one on whose behalf the *toda* was brought, the owner of the goring animal — must still bring the bread. It is his *korban* and therefore his obligation.

One might have thought otherwise: since the bread accompanies the meat as part of a single integrated *korban*, and since the bread is *"hechshera dezevach"* — a prerequisite for the validity of the offering — whoever eats the meat should bear responsibility for the bread. The *mitkaper* might argue: you ate the meat, so you bring the bread. The ruling teaches that this is not so. The bread obligation is *"chiyuva debe'alim"* — the owner's obligation, irrespective of who eats the meat. Even though Shimon eats the meat as his damages payment, the bread remains Reuven's responsibility. It is still his *korban*; the damaged party acquires only the meat, not the whole of the *korban* with its attendant obligations.

## Property Status Rules

### Designation Requirement

The Gemara returns to the *Mishnah*'s criteria for *nezikin* liability, now examining the requirement that property be *"nechasim meyuchadim"* — designated property. The question *"lema'utei mai"* — what does this exclude? — is posed, and Rav Yehuda answers: it excludes a case of *"ze omer shorcha hizik veze omer shorcha hizik"* — where two oxen from two different owners both ran after a third animal and one of them gored it, but neither party knows which ox was responsible, each pointing at the other. In such a case, both owners are exempt. The property is not *meyuchad* — not identifiable as belonging to a specific, determinable *mazik*.

### Ambiguity & Exemption

The Gemara notes that this very rule appears explicitly in a later *Mishnah*: *"hayu shnayim rodfim achar echad... ze omer shorcha hizik veze omer shorcha hizik — shneihem peturim."* Here, as earlier, the Gemara observes *"tana vehadar mefareish"* — the *Mishnah* first presents general criteria in terse form, and later *Mishnayot* elaborate on each principle individually. This is characteristic of the early *Mishnayot* at the opening of Bava Kamma, which are composed in a compressed, list-like format.

### Hekdesh/Hefker Exempt

A *Baraita* offers an alternative explanation of the *meyuchad* requirement: *"prat lenichsei hefker"* — it excludes ownerless property. The Gemara then asks: *"heichi dami?"* — what exactly is the case? If an owned ox gored an ownerless ox, of course there is no one to sue. The case must therefore be an ownerless ox that gored a person's ox. Here one might imagine the damaged party could simply seize the goring ox and take it. But if another party came first and took possession of that *hefker* ox before the damaged party could act, does the damaged party now have a lien on it? The answer is no. Just as *hekdesh* is exempt from *nezikin*, *hefker* is equally exempt. If the ox was ownerless at the moment of goring, there is no owner whose negligence grounds the *nezikin* obligation, and no valid claim attaches to the animal — even once someone else takes possession of it.

### Status Consistency

Ravina adds a further dimension: *"nagach ve'achar kach hikdish, nagach ve'achar kach hifkir"* — the *meyuchad* requirement also addresses cases where an ox gored while it was owned, but the owner subsequently declared it *hekdesh* or *hefker* before the damaged party could bring him to *beis din*. One might think that since the ox was fully owned and fully liable at the moment of goring, the damaged party retains rights against it regardless of the subsequent change of status. The ruling is that this is not so: the status must be consistent throughout.

### Anti-Avoidance Rule

An owner cannot strategically change the status of his animal after the damage event in order to escape liability. The requirement of *meyuchad* — consistent, designated ownership — forecloses this avenue.

### Shor Haniskal

The Gemara cites a *Baraita* in support: Rabbi Yehuda states that this consistency requirement extends even to the case of a *shor* that gored and killed a person, which would ordinarily be subject to stoning. If the owner sanctified or renounced ownership over the ox at any point between the act of goring and the final judgment, the ox is exempt from stoning. The derivation: the *pasuk* links *"vehu'ad biva'alav... vehemit... vehashor yissakel"* with connecting *vav*s, indicating that all elements — the warning to the owner, the death, and the judgment — must occur while the animal is in the same status. The Gemara adds that *"vegmar din lo ba'ei?"* — surely the final judgment (*gmar din*) must also be included? The answer is yes: the text *"vehashor yissakel"* implies a stoning that follows a completed judgment, so the consistent status must be maintained all the way through *gmar din*. Thus: *"ad she'tehei misah ve'ha'amadah badin vegmar din shavin ke'echad."* The same principle applies by analogy to standard *nezikin* cases: an owner cannot alter the status of his property at any point prior to the completion of judgment in order to evade liability.

## Reshut Hameyuchedes

### Domain Exemption

The final clause of the *Mishnah* under discussion establishes another exemption: *"chutz mereshut hameyuchedes lamazik"* — damage occurring in the *mazik*'s exclusive domain exempts the *mazik*. Rav Chisda citing Rav Avimi explains: *"de'amar lei torcha birshusi mai ba'ei"* — because the *mazik* can say to the damaged party: what was your ox doing in my property? If your animal entered my private domain without authorization and was gored there by my ox, I bear no liability. The damaged party had no right to be there, and therefore the *mazik* is entirely exempt when the damage occurs within his exclusive *reshut*.
