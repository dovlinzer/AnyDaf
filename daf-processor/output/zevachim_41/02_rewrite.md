# Zevachim 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Applications Export

### Inner Chatas Overview

The discussion on this daf continues from where we left off: the principle that the four blood applications of the *chatat hapnimit* — the inner sin offering — are all *me'akev*, meaning each one is indispensable, and this applies as well to the seven applications performed on the *parochet*. One detail worth noting that arose in the prior session: when comparing the *par* of Yom Kippur to the other inner *chatatot*, there are certain differences. The applications on the *kaporet* follow a pattern of seven plus one, and the *parochet* similarly follows seven plus one — though the "plus one" differs between them. There is additionally the seven on the roof of the *mizbeach*. In sum, a normal inner *chatat* has seven on the *parochet* and four on the horns; the Yom Kippur *par* has seven plus one in multiple locations. These are the various *halachot* that were being derived and exported across the different inner *chatatot*.

### Lapar Me'akev Teaching

One of the central recent conclusions was the significance of the word *lapar* — a construction meaning "for the bull" or "to the bull" — which was understood to export these *halachot* to Yom Kippur. The exported *halachot* include both the requirement that all applications are *me'akev* and the specific procedural rules, such as *etzba b'dam u'tvila* — the dipping of the finger in the blood in a particular manner. One approach, based on the plain force of this word, held that *lapar* is the vehicle for teaching all of this to the Yom Kippur context.

### Rabbi Ishmael Link

Rabbi Ishmael, however, did not need the word *lapar* for the Yom Kippur teaching; instead, he employed what was described as a rather unusual *kal v'chomer*. His argument was roughly as follows: since there is some overlap between a particular ox and a particular goat, there is certainly an overlap between one ox and another ox, and from that overlap we can derive that all the relevant *halachot* must apply. This is a striking and somewhat counterintuitive application of *kal v'chomer*. For Rabbi Ishmael, then, the word *lapar* is not consumed by the Yom Kippur teaching — so the question becomes: what does *lapar* come to teach?

### Packaging Exports

The *gemara* answers that *lapar* is needed to "package" everything together for export. The difficulty is a fundamental principle in the laws of *kodshim*: a law learned through *hekesh* — derivation by textual juxtaposition — cannot itself be turned around and exported to a third case via another *hekesh*. This is the rule of *davar halamed b'hekesh chozer u'melamed b'hekesh*. Therefore, before we can export certain laws to a new case, we need those laws to be, as it were, already "written" in the source text, not merely derived. The role of *lapar*, then, is to accomplish this — to make it as if the laws derived from elsewhere are explicitly present in this passage, so that they may be validly exported.

### Yoteret Shetei Kelayot

Rav Pappa now clarifies precisely what needs to be packaged. The *gemara* states: *mishum d'ba'aya gmurei par helam davar shel tzibbur biyoteret v'shtei haklayot l'se'irei avoda kochavim* — because we wish to complete the *par helam davar shel tzibbur*, filling it out with the requirement of the *yoteret v'shetei haklayot*, the lobe of the liver and the two kidneys that are offered on the *mizbeach* as *eimurim*, so that we can then export this to the *se'irei avoda zara*.

Here is the issue: the obligation to burn the *yoteret v'shetei haklayot* as part of the *eimurim* is written explicitly only in connection with the *par kohen mashiach*. It is not written explicitly in the passage dealing with the *par helam davar shel tzibbur*; it is only learned there through *hekesh* — through juxtaposition with the *par kohen mashiach*. Since it arrives there via *hekesh*, the rule of *davar halamed b'hekesh chozer u'melamed b'hekesh* would ordinarily prevent us from re-exporting it via another *hekesh* to the *se'irei avoda zara*.

The solution Rav Pappa offers is that the word *lapar* functions to "embed" those laws — the *yoteret v'shetei haklayot* and related requirements — as if they were explicitly written in the *par helam davar* passage itself. Once treated as explicit, they are no longer subject to the restriction on re-exporting derived teachings, and we may now legitimately carry them over to the *se'irei avoda zara*. This is the deeper function of *lapar*: not merely to link two cases, but to reify the imported content as explicit within the passage, enabling a clean chain of derivation onward.

## Avodah Zarah Link

### Baraita Parsing

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* in support of Rav Pappa — *tanya kevatei de-Rav Pappa* — and this gives us the opportunity to step back and appreciate the full structure of what has been established.

The starting point is the *pasuk* in Bamidbar that describes the communal sin offering brought when *beit din* issued an erroneous ruling regarding *avoda zara*. The *pasuk* reads: *v'hem heviu et korbanam isheh l'Hashem v'chatatam lifnei Hashem al shegagatam*. The *baraita* parses this verse: *chatatam* — this refers to the *se'irei avoda kochavim*, the goats of the communal *avoda zara* sin offering; *shegagatam* — this refers to the *par helam davar shel tzibbur*, the communal ox for an unintentional transgression.

It is worth pausing here: the *peshat* of this passage gives no indication whatsoever that the *se'ir* in question is an inner *chatat*. No mention is made of the *parochet*, the *kaporet*, or any of the specific procedures of an inner sin offering. Yet *Chazal* understood that since this is a communal *chatat*, it must function like the *par helam davar shel tzibbur* — and therefore all the laws of the inner *chatat* must apply. The common denominator that drives this is that these are all *chatatot* of the *tzibbur*. The verse's linking of *chatatam* to *shegagatam* is the textual vehicle through which the *se'ir* of *avoda zara* is tied to the *par helam davar*, enabling all the relevant laws to be imported.

The *baraita* also invokes the word *lapar* here in the phrase *va'asah ka'asher asah lapar*. The word *lapar* is what packages the entire body of law together — including the *yoteret v'shetei haklayot* that was only derivatively present in the *par helam davar* — so that the full package may be exported without running afoul of the principle against re-exporting *hekesh*-derived laws.

To appreciate the full picture: the phrase *va'asah ka'asher asah* — and he shall do as he did — along with the phrase *kein ya'aseh* — he shall do so — established that the seven and four applications are *me'akev*; *v'chipper* indicated that the *shirayim* and *semichah* are not *me'akev*; *lapar* exported the whole body of law to Yom Kippur; and *chatat* — the word for sin offering that links *chatatam* and *shegagatam* — is the vehicle by which everything is exported to the *se'irei avoda zara*. That is the structural architecture of the entire derivation.

### Link Source Debate

At this point the *gemara* raises an important question: the *baraita* says that *chatatam* — the reference to the sin offering — is what links the *se'irei avoda kochavim* to the *par helam davar shel tzibbur*. But the *gemara* challenges: *teipok lei mikra kama* — why do we need this verse? Did we not already learn from an earlier *pasuk*, in which the word *hachatat* — the definite article on *chatat* — was taken as a *ribui*, an expansionary inclusion, to bring *se'irei avoda kochavim* into the same category?

Rav Pappa answers: *itztrach* — it was necessary. One might have thought that the earlier *ribui* from *hachatat* applied only to the blood applications that are explicitly written in the *par helam davar* passage. But the *yoteret v'shetei haklayot* — the lobe of the liver and the two kidneys — are *not* explicitly written in the *par helam davar* passage; they were only brought there through derivation from the *par kohen mashiach*. Without the additional *pasuk* of *chatatam asher shagu sham*, one might have concluded that *hachatat* is sufficient to export only the explicitly written laws, but not the derivatively-held ones. The additional *pasuk* comes to include those as well, ensuring that the full package is transferred.

This is the substantive debate: does the link from *hachatat* alone export everything — both explicit and derived laws — to the *se'irei avoda zara*, or do we require an additional *pasuk* such as *chatatam asher shagu sham* to complete that export? This question leads directly into a tannaitic dispute.

### Rav Huna Challenge

Rav Huna brei de-Rav Natan poses a sharp challenge to Rav Pappa. He notes: the *tanna* said that *lapar* suffices to export *kol mah she'amur be'inyan* — everything stated in the matter — to the case of Yom Kippur. That is, when exporting to Yom Kippur, no additional *pasuk* was required to cover the *yoteret v'shetei haklayot* or the *etzba b'dam u'tvila*; the single word *lapar* was sufficient to carry the entire body of law. Why, then, when exporting to the *se'irei avoda zara*, do we require an additional *pasuk* — *chatatam asher shagu sham* — to accomplish what *lapar* alone supposedly achieved in the Yom Kippur case? If *lapar* exported everything there without need of supplementation, then *chatat* should be able to export everything here without need of a supplementary *pasuk*.

This is a genuinely difficult question, and it presses Rav Pappa's position in a meaningful way.

### Tannaitic Debate

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by stating: *tannai hi* — it is a dispute among *tannaim*. Rav Huna's objection is valid according to one *tanna*, but not according to another. *Tana de-vei Rabbi* — the school of Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi — holds that *lapar* alone exports everything, including derivatively-held laws, and no extra *pasuk* is needed to package the material together. According to this view, the same logic applies by the *avoda zara* link: *chatat* alone suffices to export everything. *Tana de-vei Rabbi Yishmael*, by contrast, requires an additional vehicle — whether the word *lapar* in the Yom Kippur context or the *pasuk* of *chatatam asher shagu sham* in the *avoda zara* context — to formally package the derived content before it can be re-exported. The two positions thus divide over whether a derivation exported by *hekesh* or *ribui* carries with it all associated derived laws automatically, or whether those derived laws must first be formally "written in" before they can travel further.

### Midrashic Omissions

Following this, the *gemara* presents a *midrashic* teaching from the school of Rabbi Yishmael that addresses a lingering textual puzzle: why does the *par helam davar shel tzibbur* passage not explicitly mention the *yoteret v'shetei haklayot*, even though these *eimurim* are required there by derivation from the *par kohen mashiach*?

The answer is offered through a *mashal*: it is like a human king who becomes angry at one he loves — *ohavo*. Because of the love he bears for this person, the king does not detail every element of the punishment or the transgression at length. He minimizes the explicit elaboration out of affection and a desire to spare the beloved from the full weight of public disgrace. Here, the *tzibbur* — or more precisely, the *beit din* that represents *Klal Yisrael* — is the beloved. Even in a passage describing their transgression and its atonement, the Torah omits certain details as an expression of endearment, not spelling out the full liturgy of what must be done.

The school of Rabbi Yishmael offers a second observation: why is it that by the *par kohen mashiach* the passage says *parochet hakodesh* — the holy *parochet* — whereas by the *par helam davar shel tzibbur* it simply says *parochet*, without the word *kodesh*? Here too a *mashal* is offered: it is like a king against whose country a rebellion has broken out. If only a minority has rebelled, his *pamalya* — his retinue of close supporters — remains intact. But if the majority has rebelled, his *pamalya* can no longer be sustained. The *kohen gadol* who sins represents an individual transgression; the *parochet* retains its designation as holy. But when the *tzibbur* as a whole sins, it is as if even the divine presence, God's inner sanctum, has been partially diminished — as if the *kedushah* of the *parochet* itself has receded because of the widespread nature of the sin.

## Ein Mefaglin Chatzi

### Mishna Four Applications

With the full derivation now established — that all four (and in the case of the inner *chatatot*, additionally seven) applications of blood are *me'akev* — the *mishna* moves to its natural corollary for the laws of *piggul*. The principle is stated with notable terseness: *ein mefaglin b'chatzi matir* — one cannot create *piggul* on only a partial component of what constitutes a single *matir*, a single permitting act.

To understand this: *piggul* is created through an improper intention — specifically, an intention to perform the service or consume the offering *chutz lizmanom*, beyond its proper time. This thought, when entertained during one of the four permitting acts of *shechita*, *holacha*, *kabala*, or *zerika*, renders the offering *piggul* and exposes the person who eats it to *karet*. But when the permitting act is not a single, discrete action — when it consists of multiple components that are each individually *me'akev* — then having a *piggul* thought during only one of those components does not suffice to create *piggul*.

For the inner *chatat*, where all four blood applications are *me'akev* (and additionally the seven applications), these collectively constitute the permitting act. A *piggul* thought during only one of these applications — without a similar intention through all of them — is insufficient to create *piggul* according to this principle.

### Piggul Implication

The implication is significant: since we have now established that all of these applications are *me'akev*, the practical consequence for *piggul* is that the *piggul* intention must accompany the entire permitting act in order to take effect. A partial intention — concentrated in only one application — fails to reach the threshold.

### Chatzi Matir Definition

The term *chatzi matir* — a half or partial *matir* — is what the *mishna* uses to define the situation where *piggul* cannot be created. A *matir* is the permitting act, the stage of the sacrificial process that renders the offering or its remainder permitted. When that permitting act consists of multiple discrete obligatory components, having a *piggul* thought at only one of them means you have acted with improper intention at only a *chatzi matir* — a partial permitting act. Since the permitting act as a whole was not performed under the improper intention, *piggul* does not take effect.

It is worth noting that some cases of apparent "halves" are not discrete in the relevant sense. By *holacha* — carrying the blood — one could speak of carrying only half the distance, but that is a quantitative division, not a division into discrete, individually obligatory components. The *chatzi matir* principle operates on discrete, separately *me'akev* stages.

### Menachos Comparison

The *gemara* then cites a related case from *Menachos*: *piggel b'kometz v'lo bi'levona* — if one had a *piggul* thought during the offering of the *kometz*, the handful, but not during the offering of the *levona*, the frankincense; or the reverse. In a *mincha*, both the *kometz* and the *levona* together constitute the *matir* — the permitting act for the remainder to be consumed. Rabbi Meir rules that in either case it is *piggul* and one is liable to *karet*. The *chachamim* disagree: *ein bo karet ad she'yefagel b'chol hamatir* — there is no *karet* unless one has expressed the *piggul* intention through the entire *matir*.

It bears noting — as *Tosafos* points out — that the *chachamim*'s position is not that the offering is *kasher*; it is *pasul*, invalid. *Tosafos* elsewhere suggests, based on a different *gemara*, that this *pesul* may in fact be only rabbinic in nature.

### Da'as Rishona Principle

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish now proposes a reframing of Rabbi Meir's position. He says: do not assume that the reason Rabbi Meir rules *piggul* when the intention occurred at only one of the two stages — say, the *kometz* but not the *levona* — is because Rabbi Meir holds *mefaglin b'chatzi matir*, that a *piggul* thought at a partial *matir* is sufficient to create *piggul*. Rather, Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish argues, Rabbi Meir actually agrees in principle that you need *piggul* at the entire *matir*. His ruling applies only to a specific scenario: where the *kometz* was offered with a *piggul* intention and the *levona* was offered in silence — *shtika* — with no articulated intention at all.

The key to Rabbi Meir's position, on this reading, is the principle *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh* — whoever acts on the basis of an initial intention, acts with that intention throughout the process, unless he actively redefines it. If the *piggul* thought was expressed at the first stage, and nothing was said thereafter, that first intention is understood to define and accompany the entire process. The silence of the second stage does not neutralize the first intention; it merely allows it to continue operating. Rabbi Meir would therefore agree that if the *piggul* thought came only at the *second* stage, without having been present at the first, it would *not* create *piggul* — because the initial intention was neutral or absent.

On the question of what *shtika* — silence — indicates: *Rashi* picks up on the *gemara*'s choice of this word and remarks explicitly that *machshava d'kodshim motzi b'pihu* — the thought that operates in the *korban* context is one that is articulated verbally. This is a point on which *Rashi* and *Tosafos* have a sustained discussion extending back to the opening *mishnah* of the tractate regarding *shelo lishmo*. Whether the invalidating intention must be verbalized or whether unspoken mental intention suffices is a significant debate, and the *gemara*'s use of *shtika* rather than *beli machshavat piggul* lends some support to the view that articulation is required.

## Piggul Thought Scope

### Gemara Question

The *gemara* now asks: how do we know that Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish is correct — that Rabbi Meir's position is based on *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona* rather than on *mefaglin b'chatzi matir*? This question drives the analysis of the remainder of the *daf*.

The *gemara* turns to our *mishna*, which states: since all four applications of the inner *chatat* are *me'akev*, if one gave all of them *ke-tiknan* — properly — and one of them *shelo ke-tiknan* — improperly — it is *pasul* but not *piggul*, and there is no *karet*.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish reads this *mishna* as implying that order matters: the formulation *ke-tiknan* and *shelo ke-tiknan* suggests that if the first three were done properly and the last one improperly, it is *pasul*; but if the reverse were true — if the first was done improperly, i.e., with a *piggul* thought, and the last three in silence — then it *would* be *piggul*. He takes this to indicate that the *mishna* is operating according to Rabbi Meir, and that Rabbi Meir's rule is specifically about the first thought defining the subsequent process — not about *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* at any stage indiscriminately.

His reasoning: if the *mishna* were according to the *rabbanan*, order would be irrelevant; the *rabbanan* would simply say that unless there was a *piggul* thought at all four applications it is never *piggul*, regardless of which one was done with the improper intention. The fact that the *mishna* appears to treat the sequence as meaningful must mean it reflects Rabbi Meir's position, and if it's Rabbi Meir, the only reason order would matter is if his rule is about the *first* intention defining the whole — *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona* — not about an indiscriminate *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* at any point.

### Lapar Sufficiency

The *gemara* challenges this derivation from the *mishna*. It points out that the phrase *ke-tiknan* more naturally implies that doing something properly makes it valid, not that doing it with a *piggul* intention is somehow "proper." Reading *ke-tiknan* to mean "with a *piggul* thought" — i.e., properly from the standpoint of creating *piggul* — is grammatically and contextually strained. The *mishna*'s language of *ke-tiknan* and *shelo ke-tiknan* reads more naturally as referring to proper versus improper execution in the conventional sense.

### Rava Reconciliation

Rava responds to the difficulty in reconciling the *mishna*'s language with the *rabbanan*'s position. He proposes: *mai ke-tiknan* — what does "properly" mean here? It means *ke-tiknan l'piggul* — in the manner that would properly produce *piggul*, i.e., with a *piggul* thought. And *mai shelo ke-tiknan* — what does "improperly" mean? It means *chutz limkomo* — with an intention of improper location, which renders the offering *pasul* rather than *piggul*.

On this reading, the *mishna* is discussing a scenario where three of the applications were done with a *piggul* intention and one was done with a *chutz limkomo* intention. The result is *pasul* and not *piggul*, because the *chutz limkomo* thought in the middle of the process disrupts the uniformity of the *piggul* intention across all four applications. This reading is consistent with the *rabbanan* — one must have a *piggul* thought at *all* of the *matir* stages — and the order plays no structural role in principle; what matters is that the full set of applications was not uniformly under a *piggul* intention.

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading of *shelo ke-tiknan*: it means *shelo lishmo* — not for the sake of the proper sacrifice. This would similarly produce *pesul* without *piggul*.

The *gemara* then questions even Rava's reading: does the *mishna*'s language not imply that if the improper thought had been *absent* — mere *shtika* — rather than actively *chutz limkomo*, the result would have been liability? That would suggest that even the *rabbanan* would call it *piggul* if three stages had the intention and the fourth was merely silence. But that cannot be right according to the *rabbanan*, who require the intention at *all* of the stages.

The *gemara* answers this with the principle *eidi de-tani reisha piggul ve-chayavin alav karet, tani nami seifa pasul ve-ein bo karet* — the *mishna* frames both halves in parallel: *piggul* with *karet* in the opening case, *pasul* without *karet* in the closing case. This parallel framing is drawn from a related context dealing with the outer *chatat*, where *chutz limkomo* before versus after the first application produces different outcomes. The *mishna* employs the same structural language here; the parallel framing does not imply that *shtika* in the fourth stage would have produced *piggul*, only that the structure follows the prior formulation.

Ultimately, even Rava's reconciliation is acknowledged to be a *dachuk* — forced — reading of the *mishna*. The more straightforward approach would have been to read the *mishna* as simply stating *ein mefaglin b'chatzi matir* without implying anything about sequence: if any one of the four applications was not done with a *piggul* thought — regardless of which one — there is no *piggul*. The *mishna* does not inherently communicate that order matters.

### Braiata Explicit

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* that appears to resolve the question definitively against Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish's reading of Rabbi Meir. The *baraita* distinguishes between blood applications on the outer *mizbeach* — where a single application suffices and *piggul* is therefore created by the *piggul* thought at that first application — and blood applications on the inner *mizbeach*.

For the inner *mizbeach*, the *baraita* calculates the numbers explicitly. On Yom Kippur there are forty-three applications of blood: the *par* and *se'ir* each receive seven plus one on the *kaporet* and seven plus one on the *parochet*, yielding thirty-two total; the mixed bloods then receive seven on top of the *mizbeach* and four on its corners, yielding eleven more — totaling forty-three. The *par kohen mashiach* and the *par helam davar shel tzibbur* each have eleven applications — seven on the *parochet* and four on the horns.

The *baraita* states: in these cases, since all applications are required, *piggel bein berishona bein beshniya bein beshlishis* — whether one had a *piggul* thought at the first application, or the second, or the third — Rabbi Meir says it is *piggul* and one is liable to *karet*. The *chachamim* say: there is no *karet* unless one expressed the *piggul* intention at all of the *matir* applications.

This *baraita* could hardly be more explicit. Rabbi Meir says *any one* of those applications — at the first, or the second, or the third — is sufficient to create *piggul*. The *chachamim* require the intention at *all* of them. This appears to state unambiguously that Rabbi Meir holds *mefaglin b'chatzi matir*: it doesn't matter at which stage the thought occurs; any single one suffices. The *rabbanan*'s explicit formulation — *yefagel b'chol hamatir* — makes clear that they stand in direct opposition, requiring the intention across the entire *matir*. How then can Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish claim that Rabbi Meir only means *piggul* at the *first* stage, not at any subsequent one?

### New Shechita Answer

Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avin offers an answer, though even the *gemara* implicitly recognizes that it is extraordinarily *dachuk* — strained. He proposes: *hacha b'mai askinan kegon shepigel bi'shchita d'chad matir hu* — we are dealing here with a specific scenario where the *piggul* thought occurred at a *shechita* that itself constitutes an entire *matir*.

The scenario he envisions is one in which, in the middle of the multi-stage blood application process — say, on Yom Kippur — the blood spilled or became disqualified in some way. A new animal had to be slaughtered, and a new *shechita* was performed. According to one opinion, this new *shechita* and its blood can continue and complete the remaining applications. Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avin argues that when the *baraita* says a *piggul* thought at the "second" or "third" stage creates *piggul* for Rabbi Meir, it is referring to a case where that *piggul* thought occurred specifically at the moment of this new *shechita* — a discrete, fresh *matir* event — not at an ordinary blood application in the middle of a sequence. Since the new *shechita* is itself a complete *matir* unto itself, having the *piggul* thought there is indeed having it at a full *matir*, not merely at a *chatzi matir*.

The *gemara* does not resolve this cleanly, and the difficulty is palpable. The *baraita*'s language — speaking of the first, second, or third application of blood — reads naturally as referring to the sequential applications within a single, continuous process. Reading it as a reference to an interruption requiring a new *shechita* is a radical reinterpretation. The *baraita* appears to state plainly that for Rabbi Meir, *piggul* at any one of the applications is sufficient — a position that coheres naturally with *mefaglin b'chatzi matir* but sits uneasily with the *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona* framework. This is where the daf concludes, and the tension will carry forward into the next session.
