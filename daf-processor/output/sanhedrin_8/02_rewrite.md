# Sanhedrin 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Divine Presence & Justice

### Truth Brings Shechinah

We pick up at the bottom of 7a, where the *Gemara* has just concluded a discussion about *bitzua* versus *din* — binding arbitration and compromise as opposed to strict justice — along with some digressions connecting popular sayings to verses. The *Gemara* now transitions to a series of statements transmitted by Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan, each bearing on the spiritual weight of judicial integrity.

The first teaching: *kol dayan shedan din emes l'amito, mashre Shechina b'Yisrael* — any judge who renders true judgment, truth to its truth, causes the *Shechina* to dwell among Israel. This is not the earlier question of choosing between *din* and compromise; we are now operating squarely within the framework of justice and asking what it means to do it correctly. The double language — *emes l'amito*, truth to its truth — is striking. It calls to mind the *Yerushalmi*'s discussion of how authentic truth encompasses not only the result but the process by which it is reached. When justice is genuinely done, the verse confirms: *Elokim nitzav ba'adas E-l, bekerev elokim yishpot* — God stands in the congregation of God; in the presence of God they judge. The divine presence rests on earth precisely when justice is being enacted.

### Perversion Removes Shech…

The converse follows immediately. Any judge who does not render *din emes l'amito* causes the *Shechina* to withdraw from Israel — *gorem l'Shechina shetistalek miYisrael*. The prooftext is: *mishod aniyim me'ankat evyonim, ata akum yomar Hashem* — from the despoiling of the poor and the outcry of the needy, now I will rise up, says God. The word "rise up" here carries a double valence: God rises to take vengeance, but also, as it were, withdraws His presence. This verse speaks to the judge who perhaps ignores injustice being done, or who recuses himself to avoid trouble with the powerful, thereby abandoning the outcry of the poor.

### Unjust Judgment Fatal

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani then transmits a further teaching in Rabbi Yonatan's name: *kol dayan shenotel mizeh venosein lizeh shelo k'din, Hakadosh Baruch Hu notel mimenu nafsho* — any judge who takes from one party and gives to the other in a manner contrary to law, God will demand his soul. If the previous verse addressed a judge who ignored injustice, this one targets the one who actively perverts it. The prooftext — *al tigzol dal ki dal hu, v'al tedakeh ani b'sha'ar* — do not steal from the poor because he is poor, and do not trample the poor person in the gate — identifies the *sha'ar*, the gate, as the locus of judicial proceedings, evoking *Lot yoshev b'sha'ar Sdom*, where justice was administered. *Ki Hashem yariv rivam, v'kava es koveihem nafesh* — God will fight their fight and take the soul of those who rob them.

### Fear of Judgment

A third teaching from the same tradent: *l'olam yireh dayan es atzmo k'ilu cherev munachas lo bein yarchosav, v'gehinnom pesucha lo mitachtav* — a judge should always see himself as if a sword is placed between his thighs and *gehinnom* is open beneath him. This is hardly an encouraging recruitment pitch, but the point is serious: the consequences of judging incorrectly are severe. The prooftext is taken from the description of Shlomo's bed: sixty mighty warriors surrounding it, each with his sword at his thigh, *mipachad balaylos* — fear of the night. The *Gemara* reads this as *mipachda shel gehinnom shedomeh lalaylah* — fear of *gehinnom*, which is likened to night. Rashi explains *melumdei milchama* as *milchamta shel Torah*, those engaged in Torah learning, evoking the classic image of the *beis medrash* — but here the teaching is specifically applied to the consequence of incorrect adjudication.

## Clarity Standard

### Morning Clarity Test

Rav Yoshaiya — or some say Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — expounds the verse: *Beit David ko amar Hashem dinu laboker mishpat v'hatzilu gazul miyad oshek* — O House of David, thus says God: judge justice in the morning and rescue the robbed one from the hand of the oppressor. The immediate question is: *v'chi baboker danin uv'chol yoma ein danin?* Do you only judge in the morning? Surely one judges throughout the day. The answer: *im barur lach hadavar kaboker amrehu* — if the matter is as clear to you as the morning light, say it. *V'im lav al tomrehu* — and if not, do not say it.

This raises an immediate challenge. If the standard for issuing a ruling is that the matter must be crystal clear — as obvious as the difference between light and darkness at dawn — what happens when a judge is not entirely certain? Does it mean justice will go unserved? *Halacha* has mechanisms for situations of doubt: *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the burden of proof rests on the claimant, so if the judge is uncertain, the case defaults to the party in possession. But that default might itself be a perversion of justice for all one knows.

### Certainty vs. Practice

This tension must be read alongside another *Gemara* that states *ein l'dayan ella mah she'einav ro'ot* — a judge has only what his own eyes can see. These two principles live in productive tension with one another. A judge must be genuinely afraid of judging wrongly; yet if he does his honest best with the information available, he is not culpable. The statement about absolute morning clarity does not demand omniscience but rather a prohibition against ruling when the judge privately knows he is operating in the dark. Certainty and conscientiousness are the demand; *ein l'dayan ella mah she'einav ro'ot* provides the relief when honest effort falls short of perfect knowledge.

### Wisdom Sister Analogy

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba transmits in the name of Rabbi Yochanan an analogous formulation from a different source: *emor lachochma achoti at* — say to wisdom, you are my sister. If the ruling is as clear to you as the prohibition against relations with one's own sister — *im barur lach hadavar k'achotcha shehi asura lach* — then give the judgment. *V'im lav al tomrehu* — and if not, do not say it. The analogy is arresting: the certainty required for judgment is compared to one of the most self-evident prohibitions in the Torah, something no person would ever need to deliberate about or second-guess.

## Shared Responsibility

### Ten Judges Yoke

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: *asara sheyoshvin bedin, kola taluy betzavra d'chulhu* — ten people who sit in judgment, the yoke hangs around all of their necks. The number ten is notable; it is not a standard *beis din* composition. The *Gemara* immediately presses: is this obvious? Of course everyone shares responsibility. The teaching adds a less intuitive extension: *tzricha ella l'talmid hayoshev lifnei rabbo* — it applies even to a student sitting before his teacher. Rashi ties this back to earlier discussion: if a student was present and could have spoken up but did not, he bears a share of responsibility for the erroneous outcome. Being part of the process — even as a subordinate — means one cannot disclaim responsibility by pointing to the many others present. The crowd does not dilute the obligation.

### Rav Huna Ten Rabbis

The *Gemara* now pivots to a complementary application: not responsibility-sharing as an excuse, but responsibility-sharing as a safeguard. *Rav Huna ki havai ati dina l'kameih, michnaf umayti asara rabbanan* — when a case would come before Rav Huna, he would gather ten rabbis from the *yeshiva*. His stated reason: *ki heichi d'limtei shiva mikashura* — so that a splinter from the beam should reach everyone, meaning the weight of responsibility should be distributed across multiple shoulders.

This is the reverse of the prior teaching's concern. There the warning was: do not use the presence of others to evade personal responsibility. Here: when you carry a difficult ruling, bring in others so that no one person bears the full burden alone. This practice is attested among *poskim* as well — some will explicitly state that they will not issue a ruling on a sensitive matter unless they can secure the agreement of several other rabbinic colleagues, so that the *shiva mikshura* is shared.

One might question how much genuine deliberation occurred when Rav Huna assembled ten unnamed rabbis from the yeshiva. In all likelihood they deferred to him. The sense that many unnamed disciples mostly follow the lead of the senior authority does somewhat undercut the claimed benefit of shared decision-making. Indeed, sociological research into group decision-making has found that groups do not necessarily produce more balanced outcomes; individuals can still exert influence over group decisions while simultaneously avoiding personal accountability for them. Nevertheless, the underlying principle is sound: *al tehi dan yechidi* — do not judge alone — and the counsel to hear other perspectives and not assume that all truth rests with oneself.

### Consulted Slaughterers

A contrasting and in some ways more compelling example follows. *Rav Ashi ki hava atei tereifta lekamei, machneif umeisei lehu lekulhu tavchei demasa* — when a question about a *tereifta* would come before Rav Ashi, he would gather all the *shochtim* of the city of Masa Machasya, and he too would invoke the principle of *ki heichi delimteiyan shiva mikshura* — so that a splinter of the beam reaches everyone.

This case is substantively different from Rav Huna's practice and, in fact, more instructive. Rav Ashi was not summoning random students; he was bringing in the very people whose livelihoods and expertise were most directly at stake. First, the *shochtim* are the ones upon whom the *psak* will have practical impact — hearing their concerns might legitimately reshape how the ruling is framed. Second, they bring technical expertise that no single *posek*, however learned, necessarily possesses in full. This is not a case of insufficient knowledge on Rav Ashi's part; rather, it reflects the principle that different practitioners bring different dimensions of knowledge and perspective to any ruling, and the *posek* who draws on those perspectives before issuing a *psak* is serving justice more fully than one who rules in isolation.

## Unfit Appointees

### King vs. Kohen

*Ki ata Rav Dimi amar, darash Nachman bar Kohen* — when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he quoted a teaching of Nachman bar Kohen — and note that the teacher himself bears the name "bar Kohen," which will prove relevant. The verse: *melech bemishpat ya'amid aretz, ve'ish terumos yeherosena* — the king with justice will establish the land, but a man of gifts will destroy it. Reading *terumos* as an allusion to the *kohen* who goes from threshing floor to threshing floor collecting his *terumah*: if the judge is like a king who needs nothing from anyone — *sheino tzarich leklum* — he will establish justice. But if he is like a *kohen* dependent on others' goodwill, going door to door for his grain, he will destroy it.

Tosafot understands this in material terms: a financially independent judge who needs no one's favor can rule freely, whereas a judge dependent on others' support will inevitably be compromised. Rashi, however, reads it differently. Looking at Rashi's second narrow column: *im domeh dayan lemelech shebaki betiv dinim* — if the judge is like a king who is expert in the nature of legal cases, *umaleh chochma* — and full of wisdom — as opposed to the *kohen* who must go around asking for his *terumah* — *shetzarich lachzor lishol ba'ish*, who must keep asking others — if the judge must constantly seek guidance because he lacks the knowledge, he will destroy it.

Rashi's reading should be balanced against what we have just learned about consulting others. The difference is clear: a genuine expert who chooses to consult is not the same as someone who lacks fundamental competence and must ask because they do not know. As in medicine, a skilled surgeon who recommends a second opinion is not confessing ignorance; rather, the seriousness of the matter warrants additional expert input. That is entirely different from a practitioner who lacks the training to know what they are doing.

### Empty Vessel Judge

The *Gemara* now arrives at a famous and darkly comic episode. *Devei nasi ukmei dayana delo hava gamir* — the house of the *Nasi* appointed a judge who did not know. This echoes directly back to the requirement for a *yachid mumcheh* to be *gamir vesavir* — knowledgeable and possessed of sound analytical capacity. Once a system of political appointments was established (*netilat reshut* from the *Nasi* or from the *Resh Galuta*), the predictable failure mode emerged: political connections substituting for competence.

Someone said to Yehuda bar Nachmani — the *meturgeman*, the announcer of Reish Lakish — *kum alei be'amora*, go serve as this judge's *amora*. Here the word *amora* does not refer to the rabbinic era but to its other meaning: the one who stood before large crowds and projected the speaker's words. The scholar would whisper his teaching and the *amora* would boom it out to the assembled. The incompetent judge was apparently meant to whisper something to Yehuda bar Nachmani, who would then pronounce it publicly.

What followed is almost farcical. *Kam gachin alei velo amar leh velo midi* — the judge leaned in and whispered to Yehuda bar Nachmani, but said nothing at all, for he had nothing to say. So what did Yehuda bar Nachmani announce? He improvised, and what he chose to recite was devastating: *hoi omer la'etz hakitza, uri la'even domem, hu yoreh, hineihu tafus zahav vakesef, vechol ruach ein bekirbo* — woe to the one who says to wood, wake up, stir yourself to the mute stone — he is the one who should judge? Behold he holds gold and silver, and there is no spirit within him. The empty idol, the silent stone that cannot speak: this is the image deployed for the unfit judge. It had the appearance of being the ruling whispered to him; in reality, it was a public condemnation of the judge himself.

*Amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu lifroa mihamaamidim* — God will ultimately exact payment not only from the incompetent appointee but from those who did the appointing. The verse: *v'Hashem b'heichal kodsho has mipanav kol ha'aretz* — God is in His holy sanctuary, silence before Him, all the earth. What follows that image of empty idols that speak nothing — the wood and the stone that hold gold and silver but have no spirit — is the silence of divine reckoning.

### Unfit Judge Idolatry

Reish Lakish draws the theological conclusion: *kol hama'amid dayan she'eino hagun, ke'ilu noteia asherah b'Yisrael* — whoever appoints an unfit judge is as if he planted an *asherah*, an idolatrous tree, among Israel. The connection to the image of the empty idol from the previous episode is direct: the unfit judge is an empty vessel, like a statue of gold and silver that has no inner life. Just as rendering true justice brings the *Shechina* to dwell among Israel, appointing someone who cannot render true justice is idolatry — it drives the divine presence away. More than that: justice is God's work on earth, and the judge is meant to be God's agent. Handing that authority to someone unworthy is to install a false god in God's place.

The *semichus* — the proximity of verses — makes this explicit: *shoftim veshotrim titein lecha* is immediately followed by *lo tita lecha asherah kol etz* — you shall set up judges and officers, and do not plant an idolatrous tree. Appointing unfit judges is the functional equivalent of spreading idolatry.

### Against the Altar

Rav Ashi adds a further gradation: *uv'makom sheyesh talmidei chachamim* — if there were qualified Torah scholars available who could have served as judges and were passed over, *ke'ilu nata eitzel mizbei'ach* — it is as if the *asherah* were planted right next to the altar itself. The verse: *lo tita asherah eitzel mizbei'ach Hashem Elokecha*. This is not merely the wrong choice; it is direct defiance, an insult planted in the very precincts of holiness.

### Gods of Silver/Gold

The *Gemara* then addresses the verse *lo taasun iti elohei chesef ve'elohei zahav* — do not make gods of silver and gods of gold alongside Me. A question is posed: surely this obviously means do not make idols of silver and gold — do we need to say this? Does it follow that idols of *wood* would be permitted? Rav Ashi resolves this: *elo'ah haba bishvil kesef* — a god that comes because of money. The verse is warning against appointing someone to a position of authority — one who functions like a god on earth, executing divine justice — simply because they paid for the position. The word *elo'ah* here encodes the judicial role: the *shofet* represents divine authority on earth. When that position is purchased rather than earned through knowledge and integrity, it is the creation of a false god. The *Gemara* clearly acknowledges the realities of the political-appointment system of its era.

## Honor & Humility

### Rav's Declaration

Having treated the weight of judicial responsibility and the corruption of unfit appointees, the *Gemara* now turns to a connected but distinct problem: the honor that attaches to the position of judge, and how a person who holds that position righteously navigates the risk of being corrupted by the honor itself.

*Rav ki hava ati l'vei dina, amar hachi* — when Rav would come to court to serve as a judge, he would make the following declaration: *bire'ut nafshiah l'katla nafik* — through my own choice I am going out to be killed, meaning if I judge incorrectly I will deserve death. *V'tzvi beiteiah leit hu avid* — I am not attending to my family's needs by being here; I will earn nothing. *V'reikan l'beiteiah ayil* — I will return home empty-handed. *Veluai shetehei biati kiyetziati* — and I only hope that when I am finished, my entering was like my leaving, meaning I have not made things worse. This declaration — addressed to himself, to others present, to anyone who would hear — was Rav's way of making explicit that he was not doing this for *kavod*, not for financial gain, not for any benefit to himself.

### Heaven & Dung

Yet *ki hava chazi ambua basreih* — when Rav would leave the court and see that crowds of people were gathering to follow him, that despite everything, honor was still accruing to him, he would say to himself: *im yaaleh lashamayim se'o, kegilalo lanetzach yovad* — even if your glory rises to heaven, in the end, like your own dung, you will be destroyed and pass away. The Hebrew is pointed and deliberately deflating. Whatever honor the crowd conferred, Rav chose to see it through the lens of ultimate insignificance: all human glory ends the same way.

### Mar Zutra Humility

The *Gemara* records a parallel practice from Mar Zutra Chasida. On *shabsa deregla* — the Shabbat before a *regel* when large masses would gather to hear Torah — people would carry him on their shoulders (*katef*) to move him quickly to the front of the assembly. Rashi explains that walking slowly through the seated crowd would require everyone to stand repeatedly, so he was carried aloft. But in that position — elevated above everyone, being physically carried by others — the risk to one's sense of proportion was acute. So Mar Zutra would say to himself: *ki lo leolam chosen, ve'im nezer ledor dor* — because wealth is not forever, and honor does not pass from generation to generation. This too will pass; whatever elevation I now experience is temporary and ultimately meaningless.

## Judicial Deliberation

### No Steps in Justice

Bar Kappara expounds: where do we derive the rabbinic principle of *hevei masun badin* — be deliberate in judgment? From the verse *lo saaleh bemaalos* — do not go up in steps — which is immediately followed by *ve'eleh hamishpatim asher tasim lifneihem*, these are the laws you shall place before them. Going up steps rather than a ramp involves skipping the intervening space — jumping over sections of the process. Deliberation in judgment means touching every point, moving slowly, not leaping to conclusions. A ramp forces one to traverse every inch; steps allow gaps. The judge must be like one ascending a ramp.

### Not Over Heads

Rav Elazar adds a further derivation from the same verse: *minayin ledayan shelo yifsa al rashei am kodesh* — how do we know that a judge should not step over people's heads? The image is literal: when a judge must move through a seated assembly to reach his place, stepping over seated people makes it appear as though he is literally stepping on them, asserting his elevation over the holy people. The verse *lo saaleh bemaalos* followed immediately by *ve'eleh hamishpatim* teaches again: the judge who represents justice must not comport himself in ways that visually assert superiority over those he serves.

### Judge's Instruments

The *Gemara* notes that the verse *asher tasim lifneihem* uses the word *tasim* — you shall place — rather than the expected *tilmedem* — you shall teach them. Rabbi Yirmiyah (or some say Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba) explains: *elu klei hadayanim* — these are the instruments of the judges. The placing refers to the tools the judge employs. Rav Huna, when going out to judge, would call for *manei chanutai* — the vessels of his trade: *makel v'retzuah, v'shofra v'sandala* — a staff and a leather strap, a *shofar*, and a sandal. The staff and strap are for the administration of lashes; the *shofar* is for *cherem*, excommunication; and the sandal, Rashi explains, is for *chalitzah*. The *shochtim* were the ones who physically administered lashes — the judge himself wielded the symbolic authority — but the point is that the responsibilities of justice are not only deliberative. They include the willingness to enforce.

### Staff Strap Balance

Returning to the verse *va'atzaveh et shoftechem ba'et hahi* from the beginning of Devarim, Rabbi Yochanan reads *va'atzaveh* as an instruction about *makel v'retzuah*: *keneged makel v'retzuah tehei zariz* — be punctilious about the staff and the strap.

Rashi and Tosafot differ on what *tehei zariz* demands. Rashi: *tehei zariz al hamakel v'al haretzuah she-hayu matilin eimah al hatzibur l'shem shamayim* — be diligent about the staff and strap, which instill proper fear in the public for the sake of Heaven. The judge must own that aspect of the role; people must sense that the authority to enforce is real and will be exercised. Tosafot reverses the emphasis: *shelo lehatil eimah al hatzibur l'rodotam yoter midai* — be careful not to impose fear upon the public excessively, not to go too far. The balance between these readings is the balance demanded of the judge: sufficient authority to be taken seriously, restraint enough not to become oppressive.

## Procedural Safeguards

### Both Parties Present

The *Gemara* continues through the *pesukim* at the opening of Devarim. *Shamo'a bein acheichem ushfatetem tzedek* — hear between your brethren and judge righteously. Rabbi Chanina derives: *azharah l'Beis Din shelo yishma divrei ba'al din kodem sheyavo ba'al din chaveiro* — a prohibition on the court from hearing one litigant's words before the other litigant arrives. The obligation cuts both ways: *v'azharah l'ba'al din shelo yateim dvarav l'dayan kodem sheyavo ba'al din chaveiro* — a litigant is also prohibited from presenting his case to the judge before the opposing party appears. *Kari bei nami shema bein acheichem* — the word can also be read as "make heard": when you make your case heard, it must be in the presence of your counterpart.

### Anchoring Effect

Rashi's comment here is particularly penetrating. Why does it matter so much that both be present simultaneously? *K'sheyavo'u shneihem yachad, shimu divreihem, v'lo tishme'u divrei zeh belo zeh* — hear them together, do not hear one without the other — *shema mesader divrei sheker k'divrei emet* — because when his opponent is absent, a person will lay out his false claims as if they were true: *lefi shehamach'shio eino sham* — since the one who would contradict him is not there.

Moreover, once the judge has heard only one side, that framing becomes the default lens through which everything subsequent is processed. When the second party's case is presented, the judge hears it against the backdrop already established by the first. This is the *anchoring* effect: the initial piece of information structures all subsequent evaluation. Requiring both parties to be present from the outset mitigates — though does not eliminate — this distortion. Someone will inevitably speak first; but if the judge frames the situation from the beginning as one with two competing versions, and if the first speaker knows the second is present and less likely to fabricate freely, the anchoring is diminished. A rabbi must be especially vigilant about this: when a congregant comes privately with a detailed account that sounds entirely persuasive, one must always hold in mind that this is one person's version, and the absent party has not yet been heard.

## Impartiality

### No Recognition

*Lo takiru fanim bamishpat* — do not recognize faces in judgment. Rabbi Yehuda interprets *lo takireihu* straightforwardly: do not show favor to someone you recognize. But the verse may carry a further dimension. Showing recognition itself — independent of any actual favoritism — creates a procedural problem. A practical illustration: when a woman came to a *beis din* for a *get* and the judges greeted her husband by name, remarking warmly on how long it had been, the message received by the woman — regardless of how the case was ultimately decided — was that the court had a pre-existing relationship with her husband that she did not share. That perception alone compromises the litigant's sense that her case will receive fair hearing.

Rabbi Eliezer reads the verse differently: *lo tinakruhu* — do not make him a stranger. If the party before you is someone you dislike, you must not treat him as a foreigner or an outsider by way of bias against him. But the practical balance is fine: you should not warmly acknowledge someone you know (*lo takiru*), yet you should not pointedly ignore them to demonstrate your impartiality (*lo tinakruhu*). The correct posture is measured evenhandedness — neither warmth nor conspicuous coldness.

## Procedural Safeguards…

### Innkeeper Disqualificati…

The *Gemara* illustrates with a story. The innkeeper where Rav was staying came before Rav to bring a legal matter. The innkeeper, seeing Rav, said: *lav ushpizichna at* — are you not my tenant? Rav responded: yes, I am. The innkeeper then said he had a case to bring. Rav replied: *psilina lach l'dina* — you have disqualified me from judging your case.

The mechanics here are worth examining. Rav was paying for his lodgings; the innkeeper was not providing charity. So the issue is not *shochad* — bribery in the conventional sense. Rashi explains: *shehizkarta li tovatecha* — because you mentioned your connection to me. Had the innkeeper said nothing, Rashi implies, it would not have been a problem. Tosafot frames it differently: *marchik hava atzmo aval midina lo mipsil* — he was distancing himself out of scrupulousness, but strictly speaking it would not have been an invalidation. The procedural problem arises precisely because the connection was surfaced; once it is named, it cannot be un-named.

### Rav Kahana Warning

Rav said to Rav Kahana: *puk dainei* — go, you take this case, since I cannot. Rav Kahana then observed that the innkeeper was behaving with excessive familiarity, projecting confidence as if his relationship with Rav guaranteed a favorable outcome. Rav Kahana warned him: *tzeita tzeita* — pay attention and show proper deference — *v'i lo, nafekna lach Rav me'unach* — if not, I will pull Rav out of your ear: Rashi explains this as a declaration that Rav's relationship with the man would not help him, and indeed Rav Kahana would put him in *cherem* — a public declaration that the court viewed him as in contempt of judicial process.

## Impartiality…

### Equal Care

Returning to the *pesukim* in Devarim: *k'katan k'gadol tishme'un* — like the small and the great you shall hear. Reish Lakish explains: *she'chaviv alecha din shel prutah k'din shel me'ah maneh* — the case of a *prutah* should be as dear to you as a case of a hundred *maneh*. The *Gemara* immediately asks: if this means that each case deserves equal deliberation and care, *pshitah* — that is obvious, at least from the perspective of *halacha*. Of course justice is justice regardless of the amount at stake. The psychological reality is another matter — a judge will naturally invest more effort in a high-stakes case — but the normative demand is clear.

The *Gemara* therefore identifies a less obvious application: even regarding which case to take up first, one may not set aside a small case to prioritize a large one. Whichever case arrives first is taken first. This is the practical content of *ke'katan kegadol*.

### No Fear

*Lo taguru mipnei ish* — do not fear any person. Rav Chanan adds: *al tachnis devarecha mipnei ish* — do not withhold your words before anyone. Even a court officer or clerk who is aware of relevant information must not suppress it out of fear of the powerful.

### Justice God's

*Ki hamishpat l'Elohim hu* — for justice belongs to God. Rabbi Chama bar Rabbi Chanina reads this as a divine complaint: it is not sufficient punishment for the wicked — and here the wicked are the judges who pervert justice, not merely ordinary thieves — that they take from one and give unjustly to another. Beyond that, *shematrichin oti lehachzir mamon l'ba'alav* — they burden Me with having to rectify their mess, returning the money to its rightful owner. When a judge rules falsely, he is in effect compelling divine intervention to clean up what human perversion has corrupted. This is the full weight of *ki hamishpat l'Elohim hu*: justice is God's domain, and to violate it is to make God responsible for the repair.

## Difficult Cases

### Difficult to God

*Ve'hadavar asher yiksheh mikem takrivun elai ushm'ativ* — and the matter that is too difficult for you, bring to me and I will hear it. Moshe presents himself here as the ultimate arbiter: if something is beyond you, bring it to me and I will resolve it. Rabbi Chanina (or some say Rabbi Yoshiyah) says: *al davar zeh ne'enash Moshe* — for this, Moshe was punished. The claim to be able to hear and resolve all cases set him up in a way that was not fully warranted.

### Moses Not Punished

This is challenged immediately: *mi ktiv v'ashmi'achem?* — does the verse say "I will tell you"? No, it says *ushm'ativ* — I will *hear* it. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak reads this as: I will hear it from God and relay the answer. *Ei gemirna gemirna* — if I already know the ruling, I will tell you; *v'i lo, azlina v'gemirna* — and if not, I will go and learn it from God. Under this reading, Moshe's statement contained no arrogance; he was simply saying that the hardest cases would ultimately be referred upward. Moshe was not punished.

### Zelophehad Merit

If Moshe was not punished — if the *parasha* of inheritance did not emerge because he overstepped — why did the case of the daughters of Zelophehad require their petition to Moshe and his subsequent inquiry to God? The *Gemara* explains: *re'uyah parshat nachalot sheticatev al yedei Moshe Rabbeinu* — the laws of inheritance were already destined to be written through Moshe regardless. Had the daughters never come forward, the ruling would have been issued anyway. *Ella shezachu Benot Zelophehad venichtava al yadan* — but they merited, because they spoke up at the right moment in the right way, that this portion of Torah was recorded through them. Their advocacy linked their names to the *parasha* in perpetuity.

This principle — *shemegalgelin chova al yedei chayav vezachut al yedei zakai* — that burdens fall upon those who bear guilt and merits upon those who are worthy — is illustrated through a parallel case. The *parasha* of the *mekoshesh*, the person who gathered wood on *Shabbat*, was likewise inevitable: the laws of *chilul Shabbat* would have been recorded regardless. But because this individual transgressed, his act became the vehicle through which the punishment was written down, and his transgression is embedded in the text forever. The merit of the daughters of Zelophehad and the guilt of the *mekoshesh* each determined the occasion and context through which God's will entered the Torah record, even though both teachings were destined to be recorded in any case.

## Leadership Balance

### Joshua's Dual Role

The *Gemara* addresses a tension in the *pesukim* concerning Yehoshua. In one place it says *ki ata tavo et ha'am hazeh* — for you will go with this people; in another, *ki ata tavi* — for you will bring them. Rabbi Yochanan explains: Moshe said to Yehoshua, *ata u'zekeinim shebador imahem* — you and the elders of the generation shall go together. That is what Moshe said. But the Holy One Blessed be He said to Yehoshua: *tul makel vehach al kadkadam* — take the staff and strike them on the head. *Ata tavi.* You lead; you do not merely accompany.

### One Leader

*Davar echad ledor ve'ein shenei devarim lador* — one leader for a generation, not two. Even though Yehoshua is not Moshe, he is the leader of his generation, and that role cannot be shared with the council of elders, even a distinguished one. He may and should consult; but he must own the leadership. The staff is his to wield. The public requires a single clear address of authority, not a committee.

## Court Summons

### Zimun Definition

The *Gemara* concludes with a *baraita*: *zimmun bishlosha* — a *zimmun* requires three. The immediate question: *mai zimmun?* — what is this *zimmun*? If it refers to *birkat zimmun* — the invitation to bentch together — that is already well known and not in need of restatement here. And if the *baraita* is simply elaborating by distinguishing *zimmun* from *birkat zimmun*, that distinction is immediately undermined by another *baraita* that lists them as one: *zimmun uvirkat zimmun bishlosha*. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *zimmun* under discussion here refers to something else entirely: *azmunei ledin* — summoning someone to a rabbinic court. This form of *zimmun* must be issued by, or in the name of, the full *beis din* of three.

### One Judge Summons

Rava's ruling establishes the operative rule: if the court is sitting and the court's messenger goes out and summons a party to appear *mishmeia dechad* — in the name of one judge only — *lo amar klum* — this counts for nothing. The summons is invalid and the recipient need not respond.

### Full Court Zimun

A valid *zimmun* to court requires the messenger to invoke the authority of *mishmeia dekulhu* — all the judges. A summons issued in the name of only one member of the *beis din* does not constitute a legitimate court summons and carries no binding obligation on the recipient.

### Court Days Presumption

There is, however, an important qualification. *Hanei mili belo yoma dedina* — the above applies when it is not a court day. On a regular day, a summons in the name of only one judge is insufficient because there is no presumptive understanding that the full court stands behind him. But *aval beyoma dedina* — on a day when the court is formally sitting (Rashi specifies Mondays and Thursdays, the regular court days) — even a summons issued *mishmeia dechad* is valid, because on that day it is understood that any message from a judge speaks on behalf of the sitting *beis din* as a whole. The context of the sitting court confers collective authority on the individual voice.
