# Pesachim 39 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Five Vegetables

### Intro to Maror

Having spent several pages on *matzah* — its special status regarding *ma'aser sheni*, the question of what constitutes *lechem*, the specifics of *lechem oni*, comparisons to *challah*, and what substances are capable of becoming *chametz* — the Gemara now turns to a more focused discussion: *maror*. We open at the top of 39a.

### Mishnah: Five Plants

The Mishnah opens: *ve'eilu yerakot she'adam yotzei bahen yedei chovato bapesach* — these are the vegetables with which one fulfills the obligation of *maror* on Pesach. The five are: *chazeret*, *tamcha*, *charchavina*, *ulshin*, and *maror*. It is worth noting at the outset that the Torah uses the term *merorim* (Numbers 9:11) — bitter herbs in the plural — which is not a reference to any particular species but rather to a category of things that are bitter. The Mishnah's identification of precisely five varieties is therefore itself a subject for the Gemara to explore.

The Mishnah continues: *yotzin bahen bein lachin bein yeveshim* — one fulfills the obligation whether the vegetables are moist or dry. However, if they are pickled (*kevusha*), that is not acceptable — pickling overwhelms the plant's own taste and presumably eliminates whatever bitterness it had. Nor does one fulfill the obligation with *shelukin* (double-cooked, almost dissolved) or *mevushalim* (cooked), all of which significantly alter the taste. The Mishnah also states that one can combine smaller amounts of different species to make up a *kezayit*, and that the obligation can be fulfilled with the stalk (*klach*) of the plant, not only with its leaves.

### Ma'aser Status

The Mishnah closes by noting that *maror* of *demai* status (produce from which one is uncertain whether *terumot u'ma'asrot* have been separated), or *ma'aser rishon* from which *terumah* has been taken, or *ma'aser sheni* and *hekdesh* that have been redeemed, all satisfy the obligation — paralleling what we saw by *matzah*. One point worth flagging: the obligation of *terumot u'ma'asrot* for vegetables is entirely rabbinic in nature, meaning that whatever *ma'aser* status attaches to these plants is rabbinic at most. That distinction will become significant later.

## Plant Identifications

### Chazeret & Ulshin

The Gemara begins identifying the five plants. *Chazeret hi chasa* — *chazeret* is *chasa*, ordinarily understood to be a type of lettuce. Rashi gives the Old French *letuga*, which clearly corresponds to the Latin *Lactuca sativa*, the standard botanical term for lettuce. The Gemara then identifies *ulshin* as *hindivi*. Rashi describes it as a type of weed or herb, while the resemblance between *hindivi* and the word "endive" is itself suggestive. Indeed, Cichorium endivia — endive, chicory, and escarole are all of the same species — is the identification offered by modern commentators on the basis of both Rashi's language and the Gemara's term.

### Tamcha & Charchavina

For *tamcha*, Rabba bar Bar Chana simply says *tamchata shemah* — that is its name — which is not especially illuminating. Rashi's identification (*maruvya*) has led some to understand *tamcha* as horehound, while others identify it as horseradish. The distinction matters: horseradish is a root vegetable, while horehound is an herbaceous plant with a bitter, pungent flavor.

*Charchavina* is identified by Rabbi Shimon Lakish as *atzvata d'dikla* — a type of vine that grows around a palm tree. Modern botanical commentators have suggested Eryngium creticum, a perennial plant found in Israel, though this identification does not obviously match the description of something climbing a palm.

As for the fifth plant, *maror* (or *meririta*), Rashi offers *ampurya* or *vipupres*, and various modern translations suggest common sow thistle (Sonchus oleraceus) or a species of wild lettuce. The honest assessment is that none of these identifications is certain.

This uncertainty has real historical and practical implications. For many generations, the Ashkenazic practice was to use horseradish for *maror* — likely reflecting both the unavailability of the more ancient plants in northern Europe and a preference for something with an unmistakable sharpness. In more recent decades, rabbinic authorities have increasingly stressed that Romaine lettuce — as a form of *chazeret* — is actually the preferred vegetable according to the Gemara itself. Today, many fulfill the obligation with both.

### Tannaim on Plants

The Gemara introduces several tannaitic traditions that expand the list. Bar Kappara teaches a list of vegetables that includes *ulshin*, *tamcha*, *charchalin* (not *charchavina* as in our Mishnah), and *chazeret* — omitting *maror* by name but apparently substituting *charchalin* as a variant. Rabbi Yehuda adds that one may fulfill the obligation with both wild endives (*ulshei sadei*) and cultivated endives (*ulshei gina*) — the innovation being that even the wild variety is acceptable, equivalent to the cultivated form and to *chazeret*.

Rabbi Meir adds three further plants: *atzvata*, *tura*, and *mar yeruar*. Rabbi Yose responds that *atzvata* and *tura* are actually the same plant, and that *mar* is itself *yeruar* — so Rabbi Meir's three reduce to two. The Gemara cites a textual variant on the side suggesting the words mean something sharp or singular, but in any reading, Rabbi Yose is reducing the apparent additions.

Rabbi Ila reports in the name of Rabbi Eliezer that even *arkavalim* — which Rashi describes as some kind of vine — is acceptable. Significantly, Rabbi Ila adds that he searched through all of Rabbi Eliezer's students to find corroboration for having heard this ruling, and found none. He then approached Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, who conceded the point — though it is not entirely clear whether he was conceding that Rabbi Eliezer had said so, or merely that he agreed the ruling was correct.

### Shmuel's List

The school of Shmuel (*tana d'vei Shmuel*) offers yet another list: *chazeret*, *ulshin*, *tamcha*, *charchavina*, *charginnim* (identified by some as oregano — a plant whose Aramaic name bears resemblance to the word), and *hardofnin* (which Rashi describes as a kind of wormwood; the *Arukh* identifies it as oleander). Rabbi Yehuda adds two subspecies of *chazeret*: *chazeret yulin* and *chazeret galin*, noting that these too count as valid — the novelty being the inclusion of these particular variants, which are on par with the standard *chazeret* already listed.

## Criteria: Bitter Herbs

### Sap Criterion

The proliferation of tannaitic opinions raises a fundamental question: what principle underlies all of these identifications? Without a clear criterion, one cannot determine whether a given plant is valid for *maror*. The Gemara now turns to this.

Rabbi Yehuda states: *kol sheyesh lo saraf* — any plant that, when cut, exudes a type of sap. If you cut open a lettuce leaf, for instance, you can see a milky liquid along the spine. The difficulty is understanding why this criterion is significant. Does the presence of sap necessarily indicate a bitter taste? The Gemara will address this, but even before that, the question stands: what exactly are we trying to identify here? If the criterion is ultimately bitterness, why not simply say so?

Part of the answer may lie in the imprecision of taste as a subjective experience. The categories of bitter, sour, sharp, and pungent overlap considerably. Horseradish, for instance, would be described by most people as sharp or hot, not bitter in the way that black coffee or grapefruit peel is bitter. Given these difficulties of classification, it is understandable that the Gemara seeks more objective, measurable criteria.

### Pale Face

Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka adds a different criterion: *kol she-panav makhsifin* — any plant whose face (surface) is pale. Rashi explains that this means not a dark green but a lighter, more washed-out shade. A leek, for instance, would exemplify this. Again, the connection to bitterness is not immediately obvious, but the criterion is at least more visually verifiable than taste alone.

### Both Criteria

*Acherim* then say: *kol yerek mar yesh lo saraf u-fanav makhsifin* — any bitter herb has both features: it exudes sap and has a pale face. This formulation synthesizes the two individual opinions and claims that bitter herbs are characterized by the conjunction of both properties.

### Halakhic Ruling

Rabbi Yochanan draws the conclusion: from all of these views, we learn that bitter herbs (*yerek mar*) have sap and a pale face. He presents this as something that follows from the totality of the opinions, reading them as complementary rather than contradictory. The logic, however, requires care: showing that all valid *maror* plants share these two features does not by itself prove that every plant with these features is valid *maror*. The inference runs in one direction. Nevertheless, the practical thrust is that these criteria help identify eligible plants more reliably than relying on subjective taste assessments alone.

Rav Yehuda rules: *halakha ke-Acherim* — we follow the view of *Acherim*, meaning both criteria together define the eligible category.

## Chazeret Preferred

### Ravina & Rav Acha

With the criteria established, the Gemara turns to a brief but instructive episode. Ravina found Rav Acha the son of Rava searching for *marisa* — the fifth item on the list, which may correspond to something more pungent or sharp in character. Ravina challenged him: why specifically that one? Because it is more bitter (*meririn tfei*)? But the Mishnah lists *chazeret* first, and primacy in a tannaitic list typically signals preference. Moreover, both the teaching of the school of Shmuel and the explicit ruling of Rabbi Oshaya place *chazeret* at the top and identify it as the preferred species for the *mitzvah*.

### Symbolism: Chasa

Rava then provides an explanation for why *chazeret* is preferred that goes beyond technical eligibility. *Mai chazeret? — mai chasa?* — what is the significance of *chasa*? *De-chas Rachmana alan* — that God (*Rachmana*) had compassion (*chas*) on us. The name *chasa* evokes *chesed* or compassion, and so using it at the Seder carries a symbolic resonance beyond its botanical properties. Since all valid *maror* species are in principle equivalent in fulfilling the obligation, choosing one that also conveys meaningful symbolism adds an additional dimension. This kind of symbolic layering is, of course, fundamental to the *Seder* — indeed, it is what the *Seder* is built upon.

### Maror Symbolism

The Gemara deepens the symbolism further. Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani says in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: why were the Egyptians compared to *maror*? The verse does not explicitly compare the Egyptians to *maror*; the language is *va-yimararu et chayeihem* — they embittered their lives — and it is the actions of the Egyptians that are described as embittering. The teaching, then, is this: *ma maror zeh she-tchilato rach ve-sofo kasheh* — just as *maror* begins soft and ends hard, so too the Egyptians: *af Mitzrim tchilatan rakah ve-sofan kashah* — at first they were softer toward the Israelites, and in the end they were harder.

Rashi understands this to refer to the physical plant itself: the stalk begins soft and gradually hardens as the plant matures. Others read it as referring to taste — that lettuce begins mild and becomes more bitter as the leaves mature. Either way, the symbolism is apt: the Egyptian enslavement did not begin overnight. The process was gradual, moving from welcome to exploitation to brutal oppression, in the manner of a slow tightening — not unlike the way the Nuremberg laws and similar mechanisms historically have worked, drawing their targets in incrementally before the full weight of subjugation becomes apparent.

This observation reinforces the preference for *chazeret* over something like the fifth, sharper plant: the symbolism of *chazeret* — a soft beginning that becomes harder — captures the nature of Egyptian oppression more faithfully than something that simply overwhelms with immediate sharpness. Following this exchange, Rav Acha conceded: *hadri bi* — I retract; I will use the lettuce.

## Maror Definition

### Is Maror Plant?

Rav Chumi raises a foundational question to Abaye: *mimai de-hai maror min yerak hu?* — what makes us assume that *maror* must be a vegetable at all? The Torah says *merorim*, which simply means something bitter. Perhaps, he suggests, one could even use *merirta de-chufya* — the bitter part of a fish called *chufya*. Who says it must come from a plant?

### Kal Vachomer

The Gemara answers through an analogy to *matzah*: *dumya de-matzah* — just as *matzah* must come from something that grows from the ground (*gidulei karka*), so too *maror* must come from the ground. The Gemara then asks: even granting that it must come from the ground, perhaps it could be from a tree — specifically from something like *hirdof*, a plant (Rashi notes it may be toxic to animals) that grows as bark or timber? Again the answer is *dumya de-matzah*: just as *matzah* comes from *min zera'im* — from something planted and harvested as a crop rather than a multi-year tree — so too *maror*. And finally: even granting all of that, perhaps the bitter thing could come from something like *hirzifu*, a plant that may be mildly toxic and not normally considered edible? Again: *dumya de-matzah* — just as *matzah* is something one would purchase with *ma'aser* money, i.e., a food considered ordinarily edible, so too *maror* must be something that qualifies as a normal food, purchasable with *ma'aser* money.

Through this chain of *kal va-chomer*-style reasoning, the Gemara delimits the category: *maror* must be a plant, not a tree, and must be ordinarily edible.

Rava bar Bar Chana then asks Abaye: even granting that *maror* is a plant, perhaps only one species is meant? The Torah says *merorim* in the plural — does that give us two, or many? *Dumya de-matzah*: just as *matzah* may be made from many types of grain, so *maror* encompasses many types of plants, not a single species.

### Planted in One Bed

Rav Huna in the name of Rav adds: all the vegetables eligible for *maror* can be planted together in a single bed (*aruga achat*) without violating *kilayim*. The implication is that since they all share the designation *maror*, they are treated as a related category. The Gemara, however, pushes back immediately.

### Kilayim Objection

The Gemara cites a Mishnah in tractate Kilayim: *chazeret* and *chazeret galim* may be planted together; *ulshin* and *ulshei sadeh* may be planted together; *kerishin* and *kerishei sadeh* similarly; and so on with *kusbar*, *chardal*, and certain gourds. Each pair may be planted together because the two members of each pair are the same species. But the Mishnah is clearly structured in pairs — and the inference is unmistakable: *chazeret* and *ulshin* may *not* be planted together. Rav himself confirmed that the Mishnah is to be read as pairs (*zugot zugot*), not as a blanket permission to combine everything.

### Vegetables Allowed

So what did Rav mean when he said all the *maror* vegetables can be planted in one bed? The Gemara resolves this: *nizra'im ke-hilchatan* — they may be planted in accordance with the general rule for diverse species, namely the well-known arrangement of five species in a six-by-six *tefach* bed. In such an arrangement, four species occupy the four sides or corners and a fifth occupies the center (or similar configurations), with sufficient visual separation that *kilayim* is not violated.

But this rule is already stated explicitly in the Mishnah in Kilayim — so what does Rav add? The *chiddush* is that one might have thought this rule applies only to grain (*zera'im* in the sense of crops like wheat), while vegetables, being larger and drawing more nutrients from the soil, might require additional separation. Rav teaches that vegetables are no different — they can be arranged in the same five-in-a-bed configuration. In fact, a Mishnah in Kilayim explicitly states the opposite concern: that *all types of seeds* (*kol minei zra'im*) cannot be in one bed, while *all types of vegetables* (*kol minei yerakot*) can be, following the proper arrangement. So vegetables are actually more lenient than grains in this regard, and Rav's statement confirms that the *maror* vegetables fall into this lenient category.

### Chazeret Space

The Gemara raises one remaining complication specific to *chazeret*. One might have thought that *chazeret* requires more space than other vegetables, because of the property already noted in connection with the symbolism of *maror*: *sofo lehakshot* — it eventually hardens and thickens as it matures. As it grows larger, it takes up more room, potentially creating a *kilayim* problem that other vegetables do not. Rabbi Yossi b'Rabbi Chanina had stated that a cabbage stalk, once it thickens and hardens, requires the space of a *beit rova* — considerably more than a standard planting. Perhaps *chazeret*, which similarly hardens over time, would require comparable extra space. The Gemara concludes that it does not — though the precise reason for distinguishing *chazeret* from cabbage in this regard is not fully spelled out and remains somewhat difficult.

## Moist vs. Dry

### Rav Chisda: Dry

The Mishnah had stated that one fulfills the obligation with *maror* whether it is moist or dry, and also with the stalk (*klach*). Rav Chisda draws a distinction: *lo shanu ela be-klach* — the rule permitting dry *maror* applies only to the stalk that has dried out. But if it is the leaves that are dried, then only moist leaves fulfill the obligation, not dry ones — because a dried-out leaf has essentially lost its taste.

### Meir vs. Chachamim

The Gemara cites a *braita* that stages the dispute more explicitly. Rabbi Meir says: one fulfills the obligation with them and with their stalks, whether moist or dry — applying the permission for dryness to both leaves and stalks. The *Chachamim* say: moist, yes; dried, no. The plain reading of both the Mishnah and the *braita* seems to address leaves, since stalks are introduced separately — making Rav Chisda's reading, which confines the "dry is permitted" rule to the stalk, somewhat forced. The Gemara defends it nonetheless, reading the Mishnah as structured so that "moist or dry" is a description of the stalk that follows, not of the leaves mentioned earlier.

One observation worth noting: dried herbs — oregano being the classic example — actually concentrate flavor. Whether drying intensifies the bitterness of something like lettuce specifically is another matter, and an open empirical question.

### Wilted Permitted

Both Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* agree on one middle category: *kemushin* — wilted. If the *maror* has wilted (begun to lose moisture but not fully dried out), it remains acceptable. This is clearly a category that applies to leaves, which further complicates the attempt to confine the moist/dry discussion entirely to stalks. The agreement also extends in the negative direction: pickled (*kevusha*) and double-cooked (*shelukin*) and boiled (*mevushalim*) are all invalid according to both.

### Must Retain Taste

The *braita* concludes with a general principle: *kol sheyesh bo ta'am maror yotzin bo* — anything that retains the bitter taste fulfills the obligation; *kol she'ein bo ta'am maror ein yotzin bo* — if the bitter taste has been lost, whether through processing or drying, one does not fulfill the obligation. This formulation places taste at the center — the various processing modes (boiling, pickling, drying) are problematic precisely because they eliminate or overwhelm the characteristic bitterness.

## Ma'aser Sheni

### Question Posed

Rami bar Chama raises a question: *mahu sheyeitzei adam yedei chovato be-maror shel ma'aser sheni bi-Yerushalayim?* — can one fulfill the obligation of *maror* with produce that has the status of *ma'aser sheni* while in Jerusalem? The issue parallels the earlier discussion about *matzah*: *ma'aser sheni* may be eaten in Jerusalem, but its consumption is restricted to that location. By contrast, the verse *bechol moshvoteichem* (Exodus 12:20) requires *matzah* to be something that can in principle be eaten anywhere.

### Rabbi Akiva View

The question is posed against the background of two tannaitic positions we have already encountered. According to Rabbi Akiva, who permitted *ma'aser sheni matzah* in Jerusalem, there is no question about *maror*: *aliba de-Rabbi Akiva lo tiba'i lach* — for Rabbi Akiva, if *ma'aser sheni* is acceptable for *matzah*, which is a biblical obligation (*d'oraisa*), then certainly it is acceptable for *maror*, which — as the Gemara will explain — carries a rabbinic status (*derabbanan*).

### Yosi HaGlili View

The genuine question arises according to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, who held that *ma'aser sheni matzah* in Jerusalem does *not* fulfill the obligation. The question is whether the same stringency applies to *maror*. Two possibilities present themselves: either the disqualification of *ma'aser sheni* applies specifically to *matzah* because *matzah* is a biblical obligation, and the weaker status of *maror* means it is not subject to the same restriction; or, conversely, whatever standards the rabbis enacted for rabbinic obligations were modeled after the biblical ones — *kol de-tikkun rabbanan ke-ein d'oraisa tikkun* — and so *maror* would be treated the same as *matzah*.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava rules: *mistavra matzah maror* — the logical connection between *matzah* and *maror* holds. Just as we derived numerous properties of *maror* from *matzah* (it must be a plant, must be edible, etc.), so here: if *matzah* of *ma'aser sheni* is invalid in Jerusalem according to Rabbi Yosi HaGlili, then *maror* of *ma'aser sheni* is likewise invalid.

### Rashi Explains

The phrase "matzah is *d'oraisa*; maror is *derabbanan*" invites interpretation. One reading — which Rashi rejects — is that the contrast refers to the current state of the obligations: nowadays, the *mitzvah* of *matzah* is biblical while *maror* is only rabbinic (since *maror* is tied to the *korban pesach* and therefore no longer obligatory on a biblical level). Rashi rejects this reading because if that were the meaning, the Gemara should have said so explicitly. Moreover, the case of *ma'aser sheni* in Jerusalem is inherently a Temple-era scenario, so the discussion is set at a time when both *matzah* and *maror* were biblically operative.

Rashi therefore reads *matzah d'oraisa* and *maror derabbanan* as referring not to the level of the eating-obligation but to the level of the *ma'aser* obligation itself. *Matzah* is made from grain, and the obligation to tithe grain (*terumot u'ma'asrot*) is biblical. *Maror* consists of vegetables, and the obligation to tithe vegetables is entirely rabbinic. Thus the *ma'aser sheni* status of the grain used for *matzah* is a more serious biblical-level restriction, whereas the *ma'aser sheni* status of the *maror* vegetables is only rabbinic — and perhaps, on that basis, Rabbi Yosi HaGlili might be more lenient about *maror* even while being stringent about *matzah*.

### Conceptual Issue

This raises a striking conceptual point. According to Rashi, we are discussing a Temple-era scenario: one is in Jerusalem eating the *korban pesach* together with *matzah* and *maror*. The *matzah* of *ma'aser sheni* cannot be taken out of Jerusalem; neither can the *maror*. The *korban pesach* itself must be eaten in Jerusalem. Yet the Gemara — following the argument that the *matzah* must be something eligible to be eaten *bechol moshvoteichem*, anywhere — holds that even in the Temple-era context, *ma'aser sheni matzah* may not satisfy the obligation. And now, following Rava, the same applies to *maror*.

This is a striking formulation: one is eating one's *korban pesach* in Jerusalem, which by definition can only be eaten in Jerusalem, and yet the *matzah* and *maror* eaten alongside it must in principle be edible outside Jerusalem. The requirement of *bechol moshvoteichem* governs the *matzah* even when the *matzah* is being eaten together with something that is itself Jerusalem-restricted.

A further conceptual question emerges from the Mishnah itself. Our Mishnah discusses a *kezayit* of *maror*, implying it is treated as an independent *mitzvah* requiring a standard measure of eating. But biblically, *maror* is not an independent obligation — it is an accompaniment to the *korban pesach*: *al matzot u-merorim yochluhu* (Numbers 9:11) describes the *korban pesach* as the primary act, with *matzah* and *maror* as its accompaniments. Several *Rishonim* hold that biblically one does not need a *kezayit* of *maror* to fulfill that verse — the presence of any *maror* alongside the *korban pesach* suffices. The independent *mitzvah* of *maror* with its *kezayit* requirement is a rabbinic enactment for post-Temple life. The fact that our Mishnah discusses *maror* and its *kezayit* measure as a standalone topic — without a corresponding Mishnah about the *korban pesach* eating — is itself consistent with the understanding that the Mishnah is addressing the rabbinic, post-Temple obligation rather than the biblical accompaniment to the *korban*.

## Soaking Bran

### Mishnah: Soaking

The Gemara now pivots back to *chametz* concerns — interrupting what had been a lengthy discussion of *matzah* and *maror* to return to the broader tractate topic. The Mishnah states: *ein shorin et ha-mursan la-tarnagolim* — one may not soak bran in water to use as feed for chickens, since contact with water will cause it to become *chametz*. The *chametz* concern is not limited to intentional bread-making; any grain left in contact with water is subject to fermentation.

### Chalut Permitted

However, *chalut* is permitted: if one scalds the bran with already-boiling water, the heat arrests the fermentation process. This was discussed earlier in the context of the general rules about *chametz*: *chalut* — grain or flour poured into or covered with actively boiling water — is cooked before fermentation can begin.

The Mishnah similarly prohibits a woman from soaking bran to take to the bathhouse for cosmetic use as a skin treatment. However, if her skin is already dry, she may rub dry bran directly against the skin — the prohibition is specifically against soaking it in water first.

### Chewing Wheat

A person may not chew wheat and apply the resulting paste as a poultice on a wound, since the chewing process introduces moisture and warmth that would promote fermentation. All of these cases illustrate the same underlying principle: any combination of grain and water, left without immediately applying heat sufficient to stop fermentation, creates *chametz*.

## No Chametz: Baked

### Three Methods

A *braita* now lists three processes that prevent *chametz* formation: if the grain has been baked (*afuy*), cooked (*mevushal*), or scalded (*chalut*) — specifically with boiling water (*shechalato be-rotchin*) — it cannot become *chametz*.

### Baking vs. Cooking

The Gemara challenges the inclusion of *mevushal*: if you place grain in water before it reaches a boil and cook it slowly, fermentation has time to begin before the cooking is complete — *ad de-mivashil lei machma* — by the time it is cooked, it has already fermented. This seemingly undermines the claim that cooking prevents *chametz*.

### Gebrochts

Rav Pappa resolves this: the *braita* is not describing slow-cooking of raw grain. Rather, it is describing the case of *ha-afuy she-bishlo* — something already baked that is subsequently cooked in water. Once grain has been baked, it cannot ferment; cooking it afterward poses no *chametz* risk. Rav Pappa identifies this as the subject of the entire *braita*: baked goods immersed in liquid — the phenomenon known as *gebrochts*. The halakhic conclusion is that this is permitted.

### Dripping Water

A further *braita* cites Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda: flour upon which water drips continuously — *kol ha-yom kulo* — does not become *chametz* as a result. The constant agitation or contact prevents the fermentation process from taking hold, analogous to the principle that continuously kneaded dough does not ferment. Rav Pappa adds: this applies only when the dripping is truly continuous, *tip la-chad tip* — drop immediately following drop, with no pause.

### Vatika

Regarding *vatika* — a food item made from flour — a dispute emerges. One source permits it; a *braita* prohibits it. The Gemara resolves the contradiction: the permissive ruling applies when *vatika* is prepared with oil and salt, with no water involved (*de-avdi be-mishcha u-milcha*), since oil does not cause fermentation (*mei peirot ein machmitzin*). The prohibition applies when it is prepared with water (*de-avdi be-maya u-milcha*).

### Gebrochts Concern

Mar Zutra adds a practical caution that reveals the underlying concern behind *gebrochts* restrictions. One should not thicken a pot of soup with roasted grain (*kimcha d'avishna*), even though we have established that already-baked grain cannot become *chametz*. The reason: *dilma lo bishal shapir* — perhaps the grain was not roasted sufficiently. If some flour was undercooked, placing it in warm liquid could cause those insufficiently roasted particles to ferment. This is precisely the logic behind the *gebrochts* concern: the *halakha* is that baked goods in liquid do not become *chametz*, but the practical worry is that not every piece was thoroughly baked, and residual raw flour in contact with liquid could ferment. The actual *halakha* does not prohibit *gebrochts*, but Mar Zutra's caution — that one should not rely on roasting being complete — shows exactly where the concern originates.
