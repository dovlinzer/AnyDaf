# Bava Metzia 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slave Seizure & Ribbit

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ לֹא יָדוּר בַּחֲצֵרוֹ חִנָּם, וְלֹא יִשְׂכּוֹר מִמֶּנּוּ בְּפָחוֹת – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who lends another** money **may not reside in** the borrower's **courtyard free** of charge, **nor may he rent** living quarters **from him at less** than the going rate, **because this is interest.** The benefit he receives from living on the borrower's property constitutes the equivalent of an additional payment as interest on the loan.

The *mishnah* on this *daf* turns to the question of benefits that a lender receives which were not explicitly stipulated at the time of the loan, and yet — because the lender is receiving some advantage on account of the loan — the arrangement is classified as a form of *ribbit*. The *mishnah* addresses cases such as a lender renting something to the borrower at a discount, or a lender dwelling in the borrower's house without charge. The *Gemara* raises the discussion from Bava Kamma regarding *zeh nehene ve'zeh lo chaser* — one party benefits while the other incurs no loss — and notes that even a benefit with no assignable monetary value, something that would normally cost nothing and could not be collected upon in court, is nonetheless impermissible in the context of a loan relationship.

This prompts *Tosafot* to ask a pointed question: does this mean that a borrower may not do any favor for his lender that he would ordinarily do for others? Surely, if the favor is entirely routine and would have been extended regardless of the loan, it should not suddenly become *ribbit* merely because a loan exists. *Tosafot* answers that such ordinary, habitual favors would indeed remain permissible. The *mishnah*'s prohibition applies in one of two distinct situations: either the favor is highly public, so that even if it is technically unproblematic, observers will perceive it as *ribbit* — or the lender is not receiving an invitation but is rather unilaterally seizing the benefit. If the lender simply arrives and occupies the borrower's house without being asked, he is exercising the coercive power dynamic inherent in a lending relationship, and that assertion of power is precisely what makes the act prohibited, even when no enforceable monetary claim would otherwise exist.

### Slave Work Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא תָּקֵיף עַבְדֵי דְאִינָשֵׁי דְּמַסֵּיק בְּהוּ זוּזֵי, וְעָבֵיד בְּהוּ עֲבִידְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef bar Ḥama,** Rava's father, **would seize the slaves of people who owed him money, and he would work them** against the will of their owners.

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* introduces the case of Rav Yosef bar Chama, who seized the slaves of his debtors and put them to work on his behalf. On its face, this seems plainly problematic: the lender is extracting productive labor from the borrower's assets without any corresponding reduction of the debt.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא עָבֵיד מָר הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava, son of** Rav Yosef bar Ḥama, **said to him: What is the reason that** the **Master does this,** i.e., seizes and uses these slaves?

Rava, Rav Yosef bar Chama's own son, confronted his father directly — addressing him, notably, as "master" — and asked: why does the master act this way?

### Rav Nachman Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא כְּרַב נַחְמָן סְבִירָא לִי, דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: עַבְדָּא, נְהוֹם כְּרֵיסֵיהּ לָא שָׁוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef bar Ḥama **said to him: I maintain** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman, as Rav Naḥman said: A slave is not worth** even **the bread in his stomach.** When the slaves work for me and eat in my home, I am not causing the owners any monetary loss.

Rav Yosef bar Chama replied that he was relying on a principle of Rav Nachman: *avda de-umkarsai lo shavei* — a slave is not even worth the cost of feeding him. In other words, the labor extracted from a slave is so minimal that it does not even offset the expense of his upkeep. By this reasoning, Rav Yosef was gaining nothing of real value from working the slave, and there would be no meaningful benefit to qualify as *ribbit*.

### Servants Differ

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן כְּגוֹן דָּארוּ עַבְדֵּיהּ דִּמְרַקֵּיד בֵּי כוּבֵי, עַבְדֵי אַחֲרִינֵי מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: I will say that Rav Naḥman said** this with regard to specific slaves, **such as his slave Dari, who** only **dances among** the wine **barrels [*khuvei*]** and does not perform any labor. **Did he say** this concerning **other slaves?** All other slaves perform labor, and their labor is worth more than their board.

Rava pushed back: when did Rav Nachman make that statement? He was speaking about his own particular servant, Dari, who was notorious for dancing in taverns and doing no useful work. That kind of slave — utterly indolent — might fairly be said to produce less than his own food costs. But one cannot apply that principle to every slave as a blanket rule. By other slaves, Rav Nachman never said this. Accordingly, Rav Yosef bar Chama's debtors' slaves may well produce meaningful labor, and working them would constitute a real benefit — and therefore *ribbit*.

### Rav Daniel Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא, כִּי הָא דְּרַב דָּנִיאֵל בַּר רַב קַטִּינָא אָמַר רַב סְבִירָא לִי. דְּאָמַר: הַתּוֹקֵף עַבְדּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ וְעָשָׂה בּוֹ מְלָאכָה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** His father **said to him: I maintain** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with this** statement said by **Rav Daniel bar Rav Ketina** that **Rav says, as he says: One who seizes another's slave and has him perform labor is exempt** from paying the master for the labor of the slave,

Rav Yosef bar Chama then shifted to a second defense, citing a ruling of Rav Daniel bar Rav Katina in the name of Rav: one who seizes another person's slave and puts him to work is exempt from payment. This ruling seems to establish that using another's slave is not a compensable act.

### Master Benefits Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּלָא נִסְתְּרֵי עַבְדֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **as** it is **satisfactory for** the master **that** the work habits of **his slave not be undone.**

The *Gemara* explains the reasoning: *d'nicha leh d'lo nistaryei avdei* — the slave's master actually prefers that his slave remain occupied rather than idle. A slave left without work risks becoming indolent, and the master would not want that. Thus, paradoxically, working the slave is doing the master a favor, and there is consequently no actionable harm.

### Context Changes Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא מַסֵּיק בֵּיהּ זוּזֵי. מָר כֵּיוָן דְּמַסֵּיק בְּהוּ זוּזֵי מִחֲזֵי כְּרִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: This statement** of Rav Daniel applies **when** the one who seizes the slave **is not owed money** by the owner of the slave. But **since the Master is owed money by** the owner of the slave, this **has** the **appearance of interest,**

Rava's response, however, is decisive: *hani milei heicha d'lo masik bei zuzei* — that reasoning applies only when the person working the slave is not the slave owner's creditor. In that context, one might invoke the logic of *zeh nehene ve'zeh lo chaser* or simply the master's preference for a working slave. But once money is owed — *keivan d'masik bei zuzei michezi k'ribbit* — the entire situation is recast. Using the debtor's slave now has the unmistakable appearance of *ribbit*, regardless of whatever technical arguments might otherwise apply.

### Living in House Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ הַדָּר בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתּוֹ אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר. הִלְוָהוּ וְדָר בַּחֲצֵירוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהַעֲלוֹת לוֹ שָׂכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **as Rav Yosef bar Minyumi says** that **Rav Naḥman says: Even though** the Sages **said** that **one who resides in another's courtyard without his knowledge does not have to pay him rent,** nevertheless, if **he lent** a courtyard owner money **and** then **resides in his courtyard,** the lender **must pay him rent.**

In support of this principle, Rava cites a ruling of Rav Yosef bar Minyumi in the name of Rav Nachman, which bears directly on our *mishnah*: even though the general rule is that one who dwells in another's courtyard without permission is not obligated to pay rent — a classic *zeh nehene ve'zeh lo chaser* scenario — if the dwelling party had previously lent the homeowner money, he is obligated to pay. *Halvahu v'dar b'chatzeiro tzarich le'haalos lo sachar*: after lending him money and then residing in his house, he must pay rent, because the arrangement is indistinguishable from *ribbit*.

### Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲדַרִי בִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **bar Ḥama said to him: I retract** my opinion, and I will no longer seize the slaves of my debtors.

Upon hearing this, Rav Yosef bar Chama conceded: *hadri bi* — I retract. His son had successfully demonstrated that his conduct was improper.

This exchange is instructive on multiple levels. The prohibition here does not rest on a technically actionable monetary benefit — as noted, one could not normally sue for reimbursement in such cases. What makes it forbidden is the combination of context and initiative: the lender is acting on his own initiative, seizing what is not his in a relationship defined by his power over the borrower. *Eved loveh l'ish malveh* — the borrower is servant to the lender — and it is the exercise of that power dynamic, forcefully and publicly, that transforms an otherwise neutral act into *ribbit*.

## Ribbit Ketzuza Cases

### Interest Cases Intro

The *Gemara* now turns to a series of cases involving situations where *ribbit* is actually being charged — where parties knowingly enter into interest-bearing loans. It is striking, as it is in other areas of *halacha* such as *shemittah*, that the *Gemara* confronts the phenomenon of widespread violation directly and works through its practical consequences. The central legal backdrop is the question of *ribbit ketzuza yotze b'dayanim*: when interest is made explicit in a loan agreement, creating a Torah-level prohibition, can the borrower subsequently go to court to recover what he paid? The answer, as will be confirmed in the *halachic* rulings at the end of this section, is yes — and it is against that background that the following cases unfold.

### Grain Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַאן דְּמַסֵּיק זוּזֵי דְרִיבִּיתָא בְּחַבְרֵיהּ וְקָא אָזְלִי חִטֵּי אַרְבְּעָה גְּרִיוֵי בְּזוּזָא בְּשׁוּקָא, וְיָהֵיב לֵיהּ אִיהוּ חַמְשָׁה, כִּי מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ – אַרְבְּעָה מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** In the case of **this one, who was owed a dinar of interest by another, and wheat was going** for the price of **four *se'a* [*gerivei*] for a dinar in the market, and** the borrower **gave** the lender **five** *se'a* of wheat as the interest payment, **when we,** the court, **remove** the interest the lender took **from** the borrower, **we remove** only **four** *se'a*, worth one dinar, **from** the lender.

Abayei presents the following scenario: a lender is owed one *zuz* of interest by a borrower. The market price for grain is four units per *zuz*. Instead of paying one *zuz* in cash, the borrower pays five units of grain — slightly more than the market equivalent of one *zuz*. The borrower now goes to court to reclaim his interest payment. The question: does he recover four units of grain (the exact market equivalent of the interest owed) or five (everything he actually paid)?

### Abayei's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִידַּךְ – אוֹזוֹלֵי הוּא דְּקָא מוֹזֵיל גַּבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** As **the other,** additional *se'a* **is a discount he granted him,** it is merely a favor, not counted as part of the interest.

Abayei rules that only four units are returned. His reasoning is that the contractual *ribbit* obligation was for one *zuz* — equivalent to four units of grain at market price. The fifth unit was not part of any interest stipulation; rather, the borrower simply priced his grain favorably to the lender, valuing the five units as if they were worth only one *zuz*. That extra unit was either a voluntary discount on the grain or something resembling a gift — but it was not, in any technical sense, *ribbit ketzuza*. Since the court-ordered recovery (*mafkinan*) applies only to the explicitly stipulated interest, only what was formally required under the loan terms can be reclaimed.

### Rava's Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: חַמְשָׁה מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא בְּתוֹרַת רִיבִּיתָא אֲתַאי לִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We take** all **five** *se'a* **from him, as** all of the wheat **initially came into his possession in the form of interest,** and therefore it is all classified as interest.

Rava disagrees: all five units must be returned. His reasoning is that in the totality of the transaction, the borrower was delivering the grain as an interest payment. Whatever the internal pricing might have been, the grain was handed over *ad'ata d'ribisa* — with the intention and understanding that it constituted the interest payment. Since the entire payment was made in the name of *ribbit*, the entire amount must be returned.

### Cloak Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּמַסֵּיק תְּרֵיסַר זוּזֵי דְרִיבִּיתָא בְּחַבְרֵיהּ וְיָהֵיב לֵיהּ גְּלִימָא בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ, כִּי מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ – אַרְבְּעָה מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ, גְּלִימָא לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **this one, who was owed four dinars of interest by another, and** the borrower **gave him a cloak** as payment **for it, when we take** the interest **from** the lender **we take four** dinars **from him, but we do not take the** actual **cloak from him,** as the giving of the cloak is considered a sale.

Abayei then advances a more extreme case: the borrower owes four *zuz* of interest and, instead of paying four *zuz* in cash, gives the lender a cloak — which is worth considerably more than four *zuz*. Now when the borrower seeks to recover his *ribbit* payment, what does the court order returned?

### Explicit Interest Only

Abayei maintains his position: only four *zuz* are returned in cash. The contractual *ribbit* was four *zuz*, and only that amount was formally stipulated. The excess value of the cloak was beyond any interest obligation; the borrower may have given it willingly as a gift or an overpayment, but the court cannot order its return because it was never part of the explicit *ribbit* term.

### Return All Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: גְּלִימָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ. מַאי טַעְמָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לֵימְרוּ: גְּלִימָא דִּמְכַסֵּי וְקָאֵי – גְּלִימָא דְרִיבִּיתָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: We take the cloak from him. What is the reason** for this? **So that** people **should not say** that **the cloak** so-and-so **is wearing is a cloak** procured as **interest.**

Rava again disagrees, ruling that the entire cloak must be returned.

### Perception Problem

His reasoning here is subtly different from his position in the grain case. Rava acknowledges that the cloak clearly exceeds the value of the contractual *ribbit* — unlike the grain case, where the overpayment was marginal and could be analyzed as a valuation question. Here, there is no pretending that the cloak *is* the four *zuz*. Nonetheless, Rava insists on full return based on a perception concern: *ki heichi d'lo leimru glima d'michasi v'kai glima d'ribisa hu* — so that people not say, "that coat he is wearing is an interest coat." If the lender is seen wearing the cloak while it is known that he lends money with interest, the public perception of *ribbit* is unavoidable. The *Rambam*, it should be noted, appears to rule broadly that whenever a physical object is given as part of an interest payment — even if its value exceeds the amount owed — the entire object must be returned, reflecting Rava's general approach in both cases.

### House Rental Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַאי מַאן דְּמַסֵּיק תְּרֵיסַר זוּזֵי דְּרִיבִּיתָא בְּחַבְרֵיהּ וְאֹגַר לֵיהּ חָצֵר דְּמִתַּגְרָא בְּעַשְׂרָה וְאוֹגְרַיהּ נִהֲלֵיהּ בִּתְרֵיסַר, כִּי מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ – תְּרֵיסַר מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** further **said:** Consider the case of **this one, who was owed twelve dinars of interest by another, and** the lender **rented a courtyard from** the borrower that was **generally rented for ten** dinars, **but he rented** it **to him** with the price inflated **to twelve** dinars. The borrower agreed to forgo the entire rental payment, thereby effectively repaying the twelve dinars of interest. **When we take** the interest **from** the lender **we take twelve** dinars **from him** to pay for the rental, as this was the amount of interest he is considered to have collected from him.

Rava himself then presents a third, more complex case. A lender is owed twelve *zuz* of interest by a borrower. The borrower has a rental house that normally commands ten *zuz* a month on the open market. The borrower and lender agree that the lender will rent the house and pay twelve *zuz* per month — the extra two *zuz* above market rate effectively serving as the monthly interest payment. At the end of the arrangement, the borrower goes to court to reclaim his *ribbit*. The question: does the lender return twelve *zuz* — the amount they agreed to attribute to the rent — or ten *zuz*, the actual market value of what he received?

### Lender Agreed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: וְלֵימָא לֵיהּ, כִּי (אַגְרָא) [אֲגַרִי] הָכִי – דַּהֲוָה קָא מִשְׁתָּרְשִׁי לִי, הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא מִשְׁתָּרְשִׁי לִי – כִּדְאָגְרִי כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הוּא דְּאָגַרְנָא, מִשּׁוּם דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: סְבַרְתְּ וְקַבֵּלְתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: But let** the lender **say** to the borrower: If I were to rent it now, I would pay not twelve dinars, but only ten. As **when I rented it from you** at the higher price it was because **I profited from it** by not having to pay for it, but **now that I am not profiting, I** will **rent it as everyone else rents,** for ten. The Gemara responds: This claim is rejected, **as we say to him: You considered and** willingly **accepted it** for the price of twelve dinars.

Rav Acha miDifti challenges the ruling, arguing in favor of the lender: when the lender agreed to the twelve-*zuz* valuation, he did so freely, knowing that the excess above market rate was being treated as profit — as his *ribbit* payment — and so he was willing to accept the inflated rate. Now that he is being compelled to surrender his *ribbit*, why should he be held to the inflated rental valuation? From the perspective of a neutral observer, what he actually received was a ten-*zuz* rental. He should only have to return ten.

### Accepted Terms

The response to Rav Acha miDifti is unequivocal: *savart vekibalt* — you understood the terms and you accepted them. The lender entered the arrangement on the understanding that the rental was worth twelve *zuz*. Having accepted that valuation — having treated it as a twelve-*zuz* benefit — he cannot now revalue it at ten when it suits him. He received twelve *zuz* of value by his own reckoning, and he must return twelve *zuz*.

### Borrower Protection

A broader observation emerges from this series of rulings: the analysis consistently tilts in favor of the borrower. While it is true that the borrower also transgresses in entering an interest-bearing arrangement, and there are *poskim* who address that dimension, the *Gemara*'s practical rulings reflect a certain sympathy for the borrower's position. The borrower is often, as the principle *eved loveh l'ish malveh* suggests, operating from a position of economic vulnerability — putting food on the table by whatever means available — while the lender is actively exploiting that need. The rules of recovery are calibrated accordingly, ensuring that the lender cannot retain any portion of what he extracted under the terms of the prohibited arrangement.

## Rental vs. Sale

### Mishna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַרְבִּין עַל הַשָּׂכָר וְאֵין מַרְבִּין עַל הַמֶּכֶר. כֵּיצַד? הִשְׂכִּיר לוֹ אֶת חֲצֵרוֹ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״אִם מֵעַכְשָׁיו אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִי – הֲרֵי הוּא לְךָ בְּעֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים לַשָּׁנָה, וְאִם שֶׁל חוֹדֶשׁ בַּחוֹדֶשׁ – סֶלַע לַחוֹדֶשׁ״, מוּתָּר. מָכַר לוֹ אֶת שָׂדֵהוּ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״אִם מֵעַכְשָׁיו אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִי – הֲרֵי הִיא שֶׁלְּךָ בְּאֶלֶף זוּז, אִם לַגּוֹרֶן – בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מָנֶה״, אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may increase** the price of **rent** to be received at a later date instead of at an earlier one, **but one may not** similarly **increase** the price of **a sale. How so?** If a courtyard owner **rented his courtyard to** a renter, **and** the owner **said to** the renter: **If you give me** the payment **now,** the rental **is yours for ten *sela* a year, but if** you pay **on a monthly** basis it will cost **a *sela* for** each **month,** equaling twelve *sela* a year. Such a practice is **permitted,** despite the fact that he charges more for a monthly payment. If a field owner **sold his field to** a buyer **and said to him: If you give me** the payment **now, it is yours for one thousand dinars,** but **if** you wait and pay me **at** the time of **the harvest,** it is yours **for twelve hundred** dinars, this transaction is **prohibited** as interest.

The *mishnah* states: *marbin al hasachar ve'ein marbin al hamekach* — one may increase the rental price for deferred payment, but one may not increase the sale price for deferred payment. Both involve delayed payment, yet the *halacha* treats them differently. The *mishnah* illustrates each case before the *Gemara* explains the underlying distinction.

### Rental Example

In the rental case: if you rent your courtyard to someone and say, "If you pay me now for the full year, it will be ten *sela'im*; if you pay month by month, it will be one *sela* per month" — that is permitted. Even though you are effectively charging twenty percent more for monthly payment, the arrangement is not considered *ribbit*.

### Sale Example

In the sale case: if you sell a field and say, "If you pay me now it is one thousand *zuz*; if you want to wait until the threshing floor when you will have cash from selling the harvest, it will be twelve hundred *zuz*" — that is forbidden. Both situations involve a higher price for later payment, yet only the rental is permissible.

This distinction has obvious resonance with modern financing arrangements. Car payments and mortgages operate on precisely this model: the buyer receives the asset immediately but lacks the full purchase price, so the lender effectively extends the balance as a loan with interest attached. What the *mishnah* is addressing is precisely that structure.

### Baseline Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: שְׂכִירוּת אֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּלֶּמֶת אֶלָּא בַּסּוֹף, וְהַאי כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מְטָא זִמְנֵיהּ לְמִיגְבֵּא – לָאו אֲגַר נְטַר לֵיהּ, מִשְׁווֹא הוּא דְּהָכִי שָׁוְיָא, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ ״אִם מֵעַכְשָׁיו אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִי הֲרֵי הוּא לְךָ בְּעֶשֶׂר סְלָעִים לְשָׁנָה״ – אוֹזוֹלֵי הוּא דְּקָא מוֹזֵיל גַּבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara poses a question: **What is different** in **the first clause** of the mishna, **and what is different** in **the latter clause?** Why is the additional charge for later payment permitted in the case of rent but prohibited in the case of a sale? **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say:** The *halakha* is that the obligation **to pay a rental** fee is incurred **only at the end** of the rental period, **and** therefore, in **this** case, **since its time to be claimed had not** yet **arrived** at the beginning of the rental, the early payment is **not** considered **payment for waiting.** Rather, the opposite is the case: **This** higher price **is the value it is worth, and that which he says to him: If you give me** payment **now it is yours for ten *sela* a year, is a discount that he offers him** for the early payment, and therefore it is not interest.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai shna reisha u'mai shna seifa* — what distinguishes the first case from the second?

### Discount vs. Interest

Rava and Rav Yosef answer jointly: *sechirus eina mishtalemes ela b'sof* — rental payment is not due until the end of the rental period. The tenant acquires the benefit of the rental — the use of the property — in monthly or annual increments, and the payment for each unit falls due only at the conclusion of that unit. Since the natural payment date for rent is the *end* of the period, the baseline rate is what you would pay at that time. When a landlord offers a discounted price for upfront payment, he is not charging extra for late payment — he is giving a *discount* for *early* payment. That is entirely permissible. The critical question is always: relative to the default payment date, are you adding a surcharge for lateness or offering a reduction for earliness? For rent, the default is end-of-period, so early payment merits a discount.

### Sale Baseline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא: כֵּיוָן דִּזְבִינֵי נִינְהוּ וּבָעֵי לְמִשְׁקַל דְּמֵי מֵעַכְשָׁיו, הִלְכָּךְ אֲגַר נְטַר לֵיהּ הוּא, וְאָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, in **the latter clause, since it is a sale, and** the owner of the field **wants** to **take** the **money from now,** if he agrees to a later payment at a higher price, this is **therefore** considered **payment for waiting, and** it is **forbidden.**

For a sale, the analysis reverses. When you sell a field, payment is due immediately at the moment of purchase. If the seller agrees to accept payment later, he is postponing what is rightfully due now. Any premium attached to that later payment is therefore *agar natar* — compensation for delayed payment — which is *ribbit*, and is forbidden.

### Scriptural Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: דָּקוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן בְּהָא מִילְּתָא וְאוֹקְמוּהָ אַקְּרָא: ״כִּשְׂכִיר שָׁנָה בְּשָׁנָה״, שְׂכִירוּת שֶׁל שָׁנָה זוֹ אֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּלֶּמֶת אֶלָּא בְּשָׁנָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The Sages examined this matter closely and established** that this *halakha* is derived **from the verse** that states: **"Like a hired laborer, year by year"** (Leviticus 25:53). It is derived from this verse that the obligation **to pay the rental** fee **of this year is** incurred **only in the following year.**

Rava adds that the Rabbis found the principle that rental is payable at the end of the period grounded in a *pasuk*: *kisechir shana b'shana* — like the rental of a year, by year. This was read to mean that the rental for a given year is paid at the beginning of the following year — that is, at the end of the rental period rather than at its start.

### Debt vs. Payable

This raises a question — familiar from the debate of *yeshna l'sechirus mitechila v'ad sof* versus *eina l'sechirus ela b'sof* — about when the rental obligation accrues. Does the tenant incur a debt moment by moment throughout the rental period, or only at its conclusion? *Tosafot* addresses this directly and draws an important distinction: even if one holds that the debt accrues incrementally throughout the period, the obligation to *pay* does not arise until the end of the period. The debt may be running continuously, but it is not yet *due*. Accordingly, a payment made at the beginning of the year is a payment made before the due date — an early payment that warrants a discount, not a late payment that incurs a surcharge.

## Tarsha & Pricing

### Rav Nachman Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם לַגּוֹרֶן בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מָנֶה – אָסוּר. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: טַרְשָׁא שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If the field owner said: **But if** you wait and pay me **at** the time of **the harvest,** the field is yours **for twelve hundred** dinars, this is **prohibited. Rav Naḥman said: Tacit interest [*tarsha*]** is **permitted.** In other words, one may make a contract for a sale with a later payment date at an increased price, as long as he does not specify that the higher price is due to the delay but merely states a price and a date.

Rav Nachman states: *tarsha shari* — *tarsha* is permitted. This comes immediately after the *mishnah*'s ruling that charging more for deferred payment in a sale is forbidden, which makes the ruling striking on its face.

### Tarsha Definition

*Tarsha* refers to a situation where a seller names a higher price for deferred payment without explicitly contrasting it to a lower price for immediate payment. In the *mishnah*'s forbidden case, the seller states both prices openly: "one thousand now, twelve hundred later." The two prices are placed side by side, making the surcharge for deferred payment explicit — the technical term for this is *katzlei*, meaning the seller has "cut" or articulated the two prices in contrast. *Tarsha* is different: the seller simply names a single price — twelve hundred — for whoever will be paying six months from now. No alternative price is stated. No contrast is drawn. The seller has not framed the higher price as a premium for lateness; it is simply the price.

The practical implication is consequential: if a seller quotes twelve hundred for deferred payment without ever mentioning that today's market price is one thousand, that is *tarsha* — and it is permissible, according to Rav Nachman, because the *ribbit* prohibition requires an explicit statement of the differential.

### Mishna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרַב נַחְמָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: וְאִם לַגּוֹרֶן בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מָנֶה – אָסוּר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם – קַץ לֵיהּ, הָכָא – לָא קַץ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised an objection to Rav Naḥman, and some say** it was **Rav Ukva bar Ḥama** who raised the objection **to Rav Naḥman:** The mishna states: **But if** you wait and pay me **at** the time of **the harvest,** the field is yours **for twelve hundred** dinars, this is **prohibited.** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** There is a difference between the two cases. **There,** in the case of the mishna, **he fixed** the price **for him** as interest, as he explicitly stated that there was an additional cost for a later payment, whereas **here,** in the case concerning which I stated my ruling, **he did not fix** it **for him** as interest, but merely quoted a higher price.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges Rav Nachman: but the *mishnah* forbids increasing the price for deferred payment. How can simply quoting a higher price for later payment be permissible?

### Explicit vs. Implicit

Rav Nachman's response is that in the *mishnah*, both prices were made explicit — the seller articulated the lower price for immediate payment and the higher price for deferred payment. In his case of *tarsha*, only one price is ever named. Without the explicit contrast, the transaction does not carry the prohibited structure of *ribbit*.

### Rav Pappa Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: טַרְשָׁא דִּידִי שְׁרֵי, מַאי טַעְמָא – שִׁכְרַאי לָא פָּסֵיד, זוּזֵי לָא צְרִיכְנָא, אֲנָא הוּא דְּקָא עָבֵידְנָא מִילְּתָא גַּבֵּי לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: My tacit interest** arrangement that I offer customers, where I sell my liquor at the times when the market price is low and agree to have the buyer pay me for it later on when the market price is higher, is **permitted. What is the reason** for this? **My liquor would not be lost** were I to store it for a while, **and I do not need money** at the present time. I could therefore simply hold it to sell at a higher price later. I sell it early because **it is I who am performing** a beneficial **matter for the customer,** by selling it to him earlier without demanding immediate payment, and this is not interest.

Rav Pappa then advances his own claim: *tarsha didi shari* — my *tarsha* is permissible. He was in the beer business, and his case involves selling beer at the future higher price while allowing the buyer to pay later.

### No Lender Benefit

Rav Pappa's justification runs as follows: the market for beer in *Tishrei* is low — say, one *zuz* a barrel. By *Nisan*, the price will be higher. Rav Pappa sells beer now for the future price of one and a half *zuz* per barrel, payable in six months. Why is this permissible? *Shecharai lo passit* — his beer will not spoil. *Zozei lo tzarichna* — he does not need the money. He could just as easily store his beer, wait six months, and sell it then at the exact same price. The buyer is therefore not doing him any favor by purchasing now; Rav Pappa could achieve the identical financial outcome without the sale. The premium reflects the future market value, not compensation for any sacrifice or delay on the seller's part.

### Borrower Perspective

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי לְרַב פָּפָּא: מַאי חָזֵי מָר דְּקָא אָזְלַתְּ בָּתַר דִּידָךְ? זִיל בָּתַר דִּידְהוּ, דְּאִילּוּ הֲווֹ לְהוּ זוּזֵי – הֲווֹ שָׁקְלִי כִּי הַשְׁתָּא, הַשְׁתָּא דְּלֵית לְהוּ זוּזֵי – שָׁקְלִי כְּיוּקְרָא דִּלְקַמֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet, son of Rav Idi, said to Rav Pappa: What does the Master see to** indicate that you should **follow yourself,** i.e., consider this matter from your perspective, and therefore conclude that it is permitted because you are not earning interest? You could equally **follow their** side, and consider the matter from the perspective of your customers, **as, if they had money** available **they would buy** liquor **at** the **current** price, but **now that they have no money** available **they buy** it **in accordance with the future,** more **expensive** rate. Consequently, they are actually paying interest in exchange for the delay in payment.

One might accept that from Rav Pappa's perspective, he loses nothing by waiting — but that is not the whole picture.

### Buyer's Need

Rav Sheshet's son challenges Rav Pappa directly: *mai chazei mar deka azlat batar didach* — why do you look at this only through your own eyes? *Zil batar dideihu* — look at the buyer's situation. If the buyer had cash available, he would be paying today's price — one *zuz* a barrel. Because he lacks ready cash — *deleis lehu zuzei* — he must pay tomorrow's price — one and a half. The buyer's lack of liquidity is what drives the higher price. That is precisely what *ribbit* is: the party in the weaker financial position ends up paying more than he otherwise would, because he needs something — in this case beer — but cannot pay for it today. The definition of *ribbit* cannot be determined solely from the seller's vantage point. Whatever Rav Pappa's financial comfort, the buyer is paying extra because of his own financial constraint — and that is the *neshech* dynamic the Torah prohibits.

## Distant Market Case

### Market Arbitrage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָמָא: טַרְשָׁא דִּידִי וַדַּאי שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥama said: My tacit interest** arrangement is **certainly permitted.**

Rav Chama introduces a distinct case of *tarsha* that he considers permissible. A buyer takes the seller's merchandise to a distant market where prices are higher, pays for the goods at the higher distant-market price, and remits payment only after completing the sale there.

### Price Differential

The specific structure: goods worth one *zuz* here are worth two *zuz* at the distant market. The buyer takes the goods there, sells them for two *zuz*, and pays the original seller two *zuz* — double what the goods were worth at the point of transfer.

### Permission Granted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא – נִיחָא לְהוּ דְּלֵיקוּ בִּרְשׁוּתִי, דְּכֹל הֵיכָא דְּקָא אָזְלִי – שָׁבְקִי לְהוּ מִכְסָא (וּנְקוּט) [וְנָקְטִי] לְהוּ שׁוּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this? Rav Ḥama said: It is **convenient for them** that the merchandise remains **established in my domain, as anywhere that they go,** the authorities **leave them alone** with regard to **taxes and** other people **maintain the market for** these sellers.

Rav Chama rules this *tarsha* permissible, and the *Gemara* explains why in terms of the concrete benefits that flow to all parties. The analysis involves several interlocking considerations.

### Product Insurance

First, the seller insures the product until it is sold at the destination. If something happens to the merchandise en route or before it is sold, the seller absorbs the loss even though the buyer has technically taken possession. This means the transaction is not, in the fullest sense, a completed sale in which the buyer now simply owes money. The seller retains a degree of ongoing interest in the merchandise — it is, to some extent, still effectively his product being carried to market on his behalf. This reframing significantly changes the character of the arrangement.

### Buyer Benefits

Second, the buyer derives concrete advantages from the seller's reputation and standing. Because the merchandise is associated with the seller, the buyer is exempted from import taxes that would otherwise apply — customs officials, knowing the goods belong to the prominent seller, waive the duty. Additionally, the buyer is given the first right to sell upon arriving at the market, ahead of other merchants. These are real, tangible benefits accruing to the buyer on account of the goods being associated with the seller.

### Favor Exchange

*Tosafot* develops the analysis further. Even granting that the seller's insurance of the product until sale means the transaction is effectively structured as the seller sending his own goods to market through an agent, there remains a question: the buyer is doing the seller a favor by transporting the merchandise to a market where it commands a higher price — two *zuz* instead of one. What is the buyer receiving in exchange for providing that service? The answer is that the seller effectively lends the buyer the sale proceeds after the sale is completed, giving the buyer the opportunity to use that capital to purchase cheap goods at the distant market and bring them back for resale at a profit. The buyer's service of carrying the goods to the higher-priced market is counterbalanced by the seller's lending of capital, and that exchange of favors — rather than a simple *ribbit* dynamic — is what makes the arrangement permissible.

## Halachic Rulings

### Rav Chama Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַב חָמָא, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי, דְּאָמַר: מָה לִי הֵן וּמָה לִי דְּמֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara summarizes the rulings in a number of the cases discussed above: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Ḥama,** that his type of tacit interest agreement is permitted. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** that fixed interest can be reclaimed in court. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yannai, who says: What** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce, **and what** difference is it **to me** if he referred to the produce's **value?**

The *Gemara* rules: *vehilchasa keRav Chama* — the *halacha* follows Rav Chama's position with respect to the distant market transaction. That case, notwithstanding its surface resemblance to *tarsha* with an explicit price differential, is permissible for the reasons explained.

### Ribbit Ketzuza

*Ribbit ketzuza yotze b'dayanim*: explicit interest stipulated in a loan contract constitutes a Torah-level prohibition and can be recovered through the courts. This was the implicit premise of the entire preceding series of cases involving *ribbit ketzuza*, and it is here confirmed as *halacha*.

### Rav Yannai Rule

*Rav Yannai* rules that just as produce (grain) can be delivered to satisfy a debt, money representing its value can be delivered in its place — and this does not constitute *ribbit*.

## Tarsha & Pricing…

### Reconciliation

*Tosafot* resolves the apparent tension between the rulings of Rav Nachman and Rav Pappa regarding *tarsha*. Rav Nachman permits *tarsha* in the general case — where there is no established, publicly known market price against which the quoted price can be measured as a premium. Rav Pappa's case, by contrast, involved beer with a well-known current market price. Even though Rav Pappa never said the words "one *zuz* now, one and a half *zuz* later," the fact that the current price was universally known made the higher price for deferred payment functionally equivalent to *katzlei* — the explicit articulation of a two-price differential. It is therefore a case of contextually explicit *ribbit*, not genuine *tarsha*. We rule like Rav Nachman — *tarsha* without an established market price serving as implicit contrast is permissible — but not like Rav Pappa, whose situation involved a price comparison that was effectively explicit by virtue of public knowledge.

## Land Sale & Fruit

### Partial Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָכַר לוֹ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה, וְנָתַן לוֹ מִקְצָת דָּמִים, וְאָמַר לוֹ: אֵימָתַי שֶׁתִּרְצֶה הָבֵא מָעוֹת וְטוֹל אֶת שֶׁלְּךָ – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **sold** another **a field and** the buyer **gave him some of** the **money, and** the seller **said to him: Whenever you wish, bring** the outstanding **money and take your** field at that point, this is **prohibited.**

The *mishnah* presents a case involving the partial payment for a field with a deferred sale condition. Reuven sells Shimon his field; Shimon pays a portion of the purchase price and Reuven says: whenever you bring me the remaining balance, the sale will be effective — retroactively — as of today.

### Fruit Ambiguity

This creates a serious *ribbit* problem with respect to the produce (*peiros*) of the field during the interim period. If Reuven eats the fruit while waiting for Shimon to pay the balance, and Shimon ultimately pays and the sale goes through retroactively, then Reuven has consumed the fruit of what was retroactively Shimon's field while holding Shimon's partial payment — which looks precisely like *ribbit* on the partial payment Shimon advanced. On the other hand, if Shimon eats the fruit, anticipating that the field will retroactively have been his all along, but then fails to pay the balance — causing the sale to be voided and the partial payment returned — then Shimon has consumed the fruit of what turned out to be Reuven's field while Reuven held his money, again creating a *ribbit* problem. Since it is unknown at the outset which way the transaction will ultimately resolve, it is impossible to say in advance who is the borrower and who is the lender, and therefore neither party can permissibly eat the fruit.

### Third Party Hold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְוָהוּ עַל שָׂדֵהוּ, וְאָמַר לוֹ: אִם אִי אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִי מִכָּאן וְעַד שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים – הֲרֵי הִיא שֶׁלִּי, הֲרֵי הִיא שֶׁלּוֹ. וְכָךְ הָיָה בַּיְתוֹס בֶּן זוֹנִין עוֹשֶׂה עַל פִּי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** If one **lent** money **to** another **on** the basis of the borrower's **field** serving as a guarantee, **and said to him: If you do not give me** the money now and instead delay your payment **from now until three years** have passed, the field **is mine,** then after three years, the field **is his.** This is permitted even if the field is worth more than the amount of the loan. **And this is what Baitos ben Zunin would do, with the consent of the Sages,** when he lent money.

The solution offered is practical: neither party eats the fruit. Instead, the fruit is held by a third party. At the conclusion of the arrangement — once it is determined whether the sale was completed or voided — the fruit is distributed to whoever is retroactively identified as the owner of the field during the interim period. With that mechanism in place, the arrangement is permissible.

### Conditional Pledge

The *mishnah* then addresses a distinct but related case: a lender gives a loan and the borrower pledges his land as collateral, with the explicit agreement that if the loan is not repaid within a set period, the land becomes the lender's.

### Bisos Example

This was the practice of Bisos ben Zonin, conducted with the approval of the Sages: he would lend money, designate the borrower's property as the asset to be forfeited if repayment was not made within a specified grace period, and proceed accordingly. The property pledged might be worth more than the loan amount — which raises the question of why this is not itself a form of *ribbit*. One possible line of reasoning is that since repayment remains an option, there is a *tzad echad* — a possible outcome — under which no *ribbit* occurs at all, which connects to the dispute involving Rabbi Yehuda discussed below.

## Fruit Ownership

### Seller Eats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִי אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מוֹכֵר אוֹכֵל פֵּירוֹת. רַב עָנָן אָמַר: מְשַׁלְּשִׁין אֶת הַפֵּירוֹת. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי. הָא דְּאָמַר: לְכִי מַיְיתֵית קְנִי. הָא דְּאָמַר: לְכִי מַיְיתֵית קְנִי מֵעַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The ruling of the mishna is unclear, as it requires clarification: In a sale such as this, when the buyer has made only a partial payment, **who enjoys the profits** of this field in the interim period, until the entire transaction is complete? **Rav Huna says:** The **seller enjoys the profits** until he receives the full payment, while **Rav Anan says:** In the meantime **they deposit the profits** in escrow **with a third party** until the transaction is finalized. The Gemara comments: **And** Rav Huna and Rav Anan **do not disagree. This one** is referring to a case **where** the seller **says: When you bring** all the money you will **acquire** the field, whereas **that one** is referring to a case **where he says** at the time of the transaction: **When you bring** all the money you will **acquire** it **from now.**

Rav Huna rules that in this case — where a partial payment was received — the seller eats the fruit during the interim period.

### Third Party

The Sages disagree and rule that the fruit is held by a third party until the ultimate status of the transaction is resolved. Rav Huna and the Sages are not in fundamental dispute; the difference lies in the precise terms of the transaction as expressed between the parties. When the seller says "you will own the field *when* you deliver the balance" — that is, ownership transfers only at the moment of final payment, with no retroactive element — then the field belongs unambiguously to the seller throughout the interim, and the seller may eat the fruit without any *ribbit* concern. But when the seller says "you will own it *as of now* when you pay the balance" — implying retroactive ownership — then the outcome is uncertain and the fruit must go to a third party.

## Land Sale & Fruit…

### Sequential Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב סָפְרָא בְּרִבִּית דְּבֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מוּתָּרִין, פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִין, וּפְעָמִים שֶׁהַמּוֹכֵר מוּתָּר וְלוֹקֵחַ אָסוּר, וּפְעָמִים שֶׁהַלּוֹקֵחַ מוּתָּר וּמוֹכֵר אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Safra taught** the following *halakhot* of **interest** as a *baraita* **of the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya: Sometimes they are both permitted** to enjoy the profits of the field, as this does not constitute interest at all; **sometimes they are both prohibited** from doing so. **And sometimes** it is **permitted for the seller** to enjoy the profits of the field **but** it is **prohibited for the buyer** to do so; **and sometimes** it is **permitted for the buyer** to enjoy the profits of the field **and** it is **prohibited for the seller** to do so.

The *mishnah* endorses a permissible alternative formulation: the sale is structured so that ownership transfers only at the moment of full payment, without retroactivity. Under this arrangement, the seller remains unambiguously the owner throughout the interim and eats the fruit as a matter of right, not as a consequence of any loan dynamic. Since the partial payment is simply a deposit and the sale is not yet complete, there is no loan relationship to speak of, and no *ribbit* issue arises.

## Fruit Ownership…

### Raba's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָנֵי רָבָא בָּתְרֵיהּ: פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם מוּתָּרִין, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: קְנִי כְּשִׁיעוּר זוּזָךְ. פְּעָמִים שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִין, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: לְכִי מַיְיתֵית קְנִי מֵעַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** When Rav Safra taught this *baraita*, **Rava answered after him,** in explanation: **Sometimes they are both permitted** to benefit from the field. For example, **if** the seller **said to him: Acquire** the field for yourself **in accordance with the measure of your money,** he effectively acquires the field incrementally in small portions, which is certainly permitted. **Sometimes they are both prohibited** from benefiting from the field. For example, **if** the seller **said to him: When you bring** the money you will **acquire** it all **from now,** if the seller enjoys the profits until the remainder of the money is paid, he has clearly taken interest. If the buyer does not make the final payment, the field will remain the property of the seller and his partial payment will be returned, and the field will be considered a loan given to the seller. If the buyer enjoys the profits of the field in the interim, he will have taken interest.

Raba undertakes a systematic analysis of the *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Chiyya, which enumerates four possible configurations: both parties permitted to eat, both forbidden, seller permitted and buyer forbidden, and buyer permitted and seller forbidden. Raba supplies the conditions that generate each scenario.

### Proportional Share

Both parties may eat when the seller says to the buyer: "you own a proportional share corresponding to the amount you have paid." Shimon pays one hundred of a one thousand *zuz* purchase price, and Reuven says: you own ten percent of the field right now. Shimon may then eat ten percent of the fruit; Reuven eats the remaining ninety percent. The ownership is clear and proportional from the outset, and no ambiguity exists.

### Retroactive Ambiguity

Both parties are forbidden — the case of the *mishnah* — when the seller conditions full ownership on final payment with retroactive effect: "when you pay, you will own it as of now." Here, neither the outcome nor the ownership status during the interim is determinable in advance, and neither party may eat.

### Sequential Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּעָמִים מוֹכֵר מוּתָּר וְלוֹקֵחַ אָסוּר, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: לְכִי מַיְיתֵית קְנִי. פְּעָמִים שֶׁהַלּוֹקֵחַ מוּתָּר וּמוֹכֵר אָסוּר, דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: קְנִי מֵעַכְשָׁיו וְזוּזַאי לִהְווֹ הַלְוָאָה גַּבָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **Sometimes the seller is permitted** to benefit from the field **and the buyer** is **prohibited** from benefiting from it, i.e., the transaction is considered interest from the perspective of the buyer but not the seller. For example, **if** the seller **said to him: When you bring** the money you will **acquire** the field. **And sometimes the buyer is permitted** to benefit from the field **and the seller** is **prohibited** from benefiting from it. For example, **if** the seller **said to him: Acquire** it **from now,** and the money you still owe me **will be** considered **a loan** in **your** possession.

The seller is permitted and the buyer forbidden when the arrangement is purely sequential: "when you pay, you will own it" — meaning ownership transfers at the moment of payment, with no retroactivity. Until that moment arrives, the field is entirely the seller's, and he eats all the fruit without restriction.

### Immediate Ownership

The buyer is permitted and the seller is forbidden when the seller transfers full ownership immediately: "you own it right now." The remaining nine hundred *zuz* is then simply a loan the buyer owes the seller — a separate financial obligation unconnected to the ownership of the field. Since the buyer unambiguously owns the field from the outset, he eats all the fruit; since the seller now holds the remaining balance as a creditor, he must not eat any fruit that could be construed as interest on the debt.

### Rabbi Yehuda Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תָּנָא שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִין? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּאִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָאָמַר: צַד אֶחָד בְּרִבִּית – מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that **they are both prohibited** from benefiting from the field if he says: When you bring the money you will acquire it from now? **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says** that the opinion of this *tanna* is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. As, if** it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, doesn't** Rabbi Yehuda **say** that **uncertain interest is permitted?** In this case as well, it is uncertain whether the arrangement will result in the payment of interest, as it depends on whether or not the rest of the payment is given.

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua adds that the rule prohibiting both parties from eating — the case of retroactive ownership — does not follow Rabbi Yehuda.

### Tzad Echad Rule

Rabbi Yehuda holds *tzad echad b'ribbit mutar* — a situation that has *one possible outcome* in which no *ribbit* occurs is permissible, even if another possible outcome would involve *ribbit*. Applied to the retroactive sale case: if, at the end of the day, the sale goes through and Shimon eats fruit of what was retroactively his field, there is no *ribbit*. That the arrangement might have resolved otherwise — had Shimon not paid — does not, for Rabbi Yehuda, taint the transaction from the outset. The *mishnah*, which forbids both parties from eating, does not follow Rabbi Yehuda; it follows the Sages, who prohibit even the possibility of a *ribbit* outcome, regardless of whether it ultimately materialized.
