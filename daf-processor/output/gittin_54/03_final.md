# Gittin 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Invisible Damage

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁשָּׁקַל בָּהֶן מִשְׁקָלוֹת, כְּשֵׁרָה! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּגוּפָן, הָא בִּכְנֶגְדָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **with which he weighed weights** is **fit?** The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult: This** *baraita* is referring to a case where he weighs an object **with** the water **itself,** and therefore the water is disqualified. And **this** statement of Rava's, that the water is fit, is referring to a case where he weighs an object **against** the water.

The *mishna* establishes the basic framework: when a person causes damage that is not perceptible to the eye — damage that exists in a halachic sense, because the object is worth less due to its legal status, yet cannot be seen — liability turns on intentionality. A *meizid*, one who acts willfully, must pay; a *shogeg*, one who acts accidentally, is exempt.

### Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּגוּפָן – מַעֲשֶׂה קָא עָבֵיד בְּהוּ; וְאִי הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק, בְּדִינֵי אָדָם נָמֵי לְחַיֵּיב! אֶלָּא אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בִּכְנֶגְדָּן; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא דְּאַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ. הָא דְּלָא אַסַּח דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If he weighs an object **with** the water **itself,** then **he performs** a real **task with it, and if damage that is not evident is** nevertheless **categorized** as **damage,** then **he should also be liable according to human laws** to pay for performing a task with the water. **Rather,** it is necessary to say that both **this and that** refer to a case where he weighed an object **against** the water, **and** still it is **not difficult: This** *baraita* is referring to a case **where** in the course of the weighing the object **his attention was diverted** from guarding the water, and owing to this lapse in attention the water became disqualified. And **that** statement of Rava's is referring to a case **where his attention was not diverted,** and therefore the water did not become disqualified.

The *gemara*'s opening posture leans strongly toward treating *hezek she'eino nikar* as not constituting genuine damage at all. The reasoning is that *halacha* looks to an act of damage, not merely the causing of a loss; if the harm is invisible, it would not be classified as a true act of *mazik*, and one would be fundamentally exempt. The rabbinic intervention, then, is directed specifically at the *meizid*: in order to close the loophole that would allow a person to willfully damage another's property without consequence, the Sages decreed that intentional actors must pay. This is the *tikun olam* rationale — the rabbinic corrective is the imposition of liability in the *meizid* case. The competing position, that of Chizkiyah, runs in the opposite direction: fundamentally the actor is liable, and the Sages provided relief in the *shogeg* case so that the damaging party would have an incentive to inform the owner. The weight of the evidence accumulated thus far has been pointing toward the first view — *lo shemei hezek*, it does not constitute damage.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב פָּפָּא: גָּזַל מַטְבֵּעַ, וְנִפְסַל; תְּרוּמָה, וְנִטְמֵאת; חָמֵץ, וְעָבַר עָלָיו הַפֶּסַח; אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises an objection** against Ḥizkiyya's opinion from that which is taught in a *baraita*: If **one robbed** another **of a coin and** afterward the coin **was rendered invalid** by the government, or if he robbed another of ***teruma* and it became** ritually **impure,** or if he robbed another of **leavened bread and Passover** then **elapsed over it,** rendering it forbidden, in each of these cases the robber **can** return the item and **say to** the robbery victim: **That which is yours is before you.** Since the robber returned the stolen item, he is not required to compensate the victim of the robbery for his monetary loss, although the stolen items are currently of minimal or no value.

At the two-dot marker near the top of 53b, Rav Pappa raises a challenge. The *gemara* cites three cases: one who steals a coin that subsequently becomes invalid, *teruma* that becomes *tamei*, and *chametz* over which Passover has passed and which is therefore worthless. In each of these cases, the thief may say to the victim *harei shelcha lefanecha* — here is your object before you, I am returning it — and this constitutes valid restitution despite the object now being essentially without value. The underlying principle is that the primary obligation of a thief is to return the stolen object itself, *v'heshiv et hagezeilah*. Since the object is being returned in the same physical state in which it was stolen, the obligation of return is discharged.

It is worth pausing to note that the three cases are not perfectly parallel. The invalid coin retains its precious metal value. *Teruma* that is *tamei* can still be burned, so it retains some use. And *chametz* that has passed over Passover is forbidden only by rabbinic decree. So none of them is truly and completely worthless — but the *gemara* treats them as functionally equivalent for purposes of this discussion.

### Harei Shelcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק, הַאי גַּזְלָן הוּא, מָמוֹנָא מְעַלְּיָא בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **damage that is not evident is categorized** as **damage,** then **this** man **is a robber,** and **he should be required to pay full compensation** for the damage he caused. The Gemara concludes: This is **a conclusive refutation,** and the opinion of Ḥizkiyya is rejected.

The connection to *hezek she'eino nikar* is as follows. The *gemara* argues: if you maintain that *hezek she'eino nikar shemei hezek* — that invisible damage does constitute genuine damage — then this thief has caused real damage to the object, and *harei shelcha lefanecha* should not work. He would be obligated to pay full compensation, since he cannot be said to be returning the object in its original state if he has genuinely damaged it. The *gemara* therefore concludes *tiyuvta* — this is a decisive challenge — implying that the law of *harei shelcha lefanecha* proves that we do not treat such a changed object as truly different, and therefore the *mazik* would not have to pay.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot presses on something genuinely puzzling here. Until this point, every proof brought in the debate about *hezek she'eino nikar* has come from the laws of *mazik*. This case, however, comes from the laws of the *gazlan* — the thief — and whether he can return a now-valueless object. These are distinct areas of *halacha*. Even if one were to say that a *mazik* who causes invisible damage must pay in the laws of damages, one might still argue that in the laws of theft — where the fundamental obligation is to return the object — the thief can say *harei shelcha lefanecha* as long as the physical object is unchanged, regardless of its changed halachic value. Why, then, does the *gemara* treat these two areas as mutually illuminating?

Tosafot answers that the comparison itself reveals something important: the question of *hezek she'eino nikar* is not merely a local question within the laws of *mazik*. It is a more fundamental and abstract question: when an object loses value without any physical change, do we consider the object itself to be different? Has it become, in some legally meaningful sense, a different object? If the answer is yes — if we say the object is substantively changed — then a *mazik* would have to pay and a *gazlan* could not say *harei shelcha lefanecha*. If the answer is no — if we say the object is not considered different — then the *mazik* is exempt and the *gazlan* may return it with that declaration. The fact that the *gemara* draws the proof from the *gazlan* case demonstrates that this is the underlying unified question, cutting across both bodies of law. The conclusion the *gemara* draws is that we do not consider the object to be changed, and thus the presumption is that a *mazik* is not liable.

## Tannaim Debate

### Mishna Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַמְטַמֵּא וְהַמְדַמֵּעַ וְהַמְנַסֵּךְ, אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד – חַיָּיב, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – פָּטוּר, בְּמֵזִיד – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this amoraic dispute is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it was taught in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who renders** another's food ritually **impure, or one who mixes** *teruma* with another's non-sacred produce, **or one who pours** another's wine as a libation before an idol, **whether** he did so **unintentionally or intentionally,** he is **liable** to pay for the damage he caused; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he acted **unintentionally,** he is **exempt;** if he acted **intentionally,** he is **liable** to pay.

Having established that the weight of evidence favors *lo shemei hezek*, the *gemara* now asks whether this might nonetheless be a *tannaitic* debate. The *gemara* introduces a *baraita*: *ha-metamei*, *ha-medamea*, and *ha-menasech* — one who causes *tumah* to *teruma*, one who mixes *teruma* with *chullin*, and one who pours libation wine — whether done accidentally (*shogeg*) or intentionally (*meizid*), is liable according to Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: by *shogeg* one is exempt, by *meizid* one is liable. That is the position reflected in the *mishna* itself.

### Rabbi Meir Position

The natural reading would be that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda are debating the fundamental question of *hezek she'eino nikar* — Rabbi Meir holding *shemei hezek* (genuine damage, hence you pay even *beshogeg*) and Rabbi Yehuda holding *lo shemei hezek* (not genuine damage, hence you are fundamentally exempt but are fined in the *meizid* case).

### Rav Nachman Resolve

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר לָא שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק; וְהָכָא בְּקָנְסוּ שׁוֹגֵג אַטּוּ מֵזִיד קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמָר סָבַר: קָנְסוּ שׁוֹגֵג אַטּוּ מֵזִיד; וּמָר סָבַר: לֹא קָנְסוּ שׁוֹגֵג אַטּוּ מֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that it is possible to say that **everyone,** including Rabbi Meir, **agrees that damage that is not evident is not categorized** as **damage. And here, they disagree with regard to** this question: Did the Sages **penalize an unintentional** offender **due to an intentional** offender? **As** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, who states that the one who caused the damage is liable even if he acted unintentionally, **holds** that the Sages **penalized an unintentional** offender **due to an intentional** offender. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, who states that one is liable only if he acted intentionally, **holds** that **they did not penalize an unintentional** offender **due to an intentional** offender.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, however, rejects this framing. He maintains that both opinions agree: *hezek she'eino nikar lo shemei hezek* — it does not constitute genuine damage. All the evidence has been pointing that way, and Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak insists the conclusion is universal. The debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is therefore not about the fundamental nature of the damage. Rather, it concerns the scope of the rabbinic fine: given that we have already decided to impose a penalty on the *meizid*, do we extend that fine to the *shogeg* as well?

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Meir says yes: *knasu shogeg atu meizid* — the Sages fined the *shogeg* on account of the *meizid*. The reasons for extending the fine are practical: it is often impossible to distinguish between a *meizid* and a *shogeg*, and a willful actor could always claim accident; furthermore, even the inadvertent actor has caused real loss to another and may deserve some deterrent consequence. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, says *lo knasu shogeg atu meizid* — the Sages did not extend the fine that broadly; they limited it to the *meizid* alone. Both positions treat the underlying liability as a *knas* — a rabbinic penalty — not a Torah-based obligation. The debate is only about how narrow or how broad that penalty should be.

## Reversals & Cases

### Shabbat Cooking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרָמֵי דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דְּתַנְיָא: הַמְבַשֵּׁל בְּשַׁבָּת; בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֵאָכֵל, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֵאָכֵל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֵאָכֵל לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֵאָכֵל עוֹלָמִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But** then it is possible to **raise a contradiction between** this statement **of Rabbi Meir and** another statement **of Rabbi Meir; and** it is also possible to **raise a contradiction between** this statement **of Rabbi Yehuda and** another statement **of Rabbi Yehuda.** The other statements are **as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Shabbat* 2:5): With regard to **one who cooks on Shabbat,** if he did so **unintentionally, he may eat** what he cooked. If he acted **intentionally, he may not eat** what he cooked; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he cooked the food **unintentionally, he may eat** what he cooked, but only **at the conclusion of Shabbat.** If he cooked it **intentionally, he may never eat** what he cooked.

With this framework established, the *gemara* proceeds to demonstrate that when one examines other cases attributed to these same *tannaim*, their positions appear to reverse — sometimes dramatically. The *gemara* introduces a *baraita* about one who cooks on Shabbat. Rabbi Meir says: *beshogeg yokhal, bemeizid lo yokhal* — if done accidentally, one may eat the food; if done willfully, one may not. So here, Rabbi Meir does *not* impose a fine on the *shogeg*, only on the *meizid*. That appears to be the opposite of his position in the *hezek she'eino nikar* cases.

Rabbi Yehuda says: *beshogeg yokhal lemotza'ei Shabbat* — if done accidentally, one may eat after Shabbat ends, but not on Shabbat itself; *bemeizid lo yokhal le'olam* — if done willfully, one may never eat the food. So Rabbi Yehuda here does impose a consequence on the *shogeg* — he may not benefit from it during Shabbat itself — though not as severe a consequence as in the *meizid* case. A third opinion, that of Rabbi Yochanan HaSandlar, holds that *bemeizid* neither the cook nor anyone else may ever eat the food, because from a Torah perspective the product of a willful *chilul Shabbat* is intrinsically forbidden, not merely subject to rabbinic fine. That is a separate *de'oraita* framework and is noted only in passing.

The chart that emerges is striking: in the *hezek she'eino nikar* case, Rabbi Meir fines *shogeg* and Rabbi Yehuda does not. In the Shabbat cooking case, Rabbi Meir does *not* fine *shogeg* and Rabbi Yehuda does. They have switched sides.

### Meir's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הַסַּנְדְּלָר אוֹמֵר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג, יֵאָכֵל לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת – לַאֲחֵרִים, וְלֹא לוֹ; בְּמֵזִיד, לֹא יֵאָכֵל עוֹלָמִית – לֹא לוֹ, וְלֹא לַאֲחֵרִים. קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yoḥanan HaSandlar says:** If he acted **unintentionally,** what he cooked **may be eaten at the conclusion of Shabbat by others, but not by him,** as the food is forbidden to him forever. If he cooked the food **intentionally,** what he cooked **may never be eaten, neither by him nor by others.** Consequently, there is **a contradiction between** one statement **of Rabbi Meir and** the other statement **of Rabbi Meir,** and there is also **a contradiction between** one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda and** the other statement **of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* resolves Rabbi Meir's apparent self-contradiction: *ki kanis bidrabbanan, bede'oraita lo kanis* — Rabbi Meir imposes the fine on a *shogeg* only in the context of a rabbinic violation, not a biblical one. The logic runs as follows: when the underlying prohibition is *de'oraita*, people are already vigilant; the weight of the Torah obligation creates its own deterrent, and there is no particular laxity to correct. By contrast, where the prohibition is only rabbinic, people are more likely to be casual, and there is a genuine slippery slope that requires the rabbis to step in and fine even the *shogeg* to tighten enforcement.

In the *hezek she'eino nikar* cases (*metamei*, *medamea*, *menasech*), the underlying acts — causing others financial loss — are in the domain of a rabbinic corrective, and so Rabbi Meir fines the *shogeg*. In the Shabbat cooking case, one has violated a *de'oraita* prohibition, and Rabbi Meir does not extend the fine.

### Avodah Zara Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר לָא קַשְׁיָא – כִּי קָנֵיס, בִּדְרַבָּנַן; בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא קָנֵיס. וְהָא מְנַסֵּךְ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא, וְקָא קָנֵיס! מִשּׁוּם חוּמְרָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, קָנֵס לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There is no contradiction **between** one statement **of Rabbi Meir and** the other statement **of Rabbi Meir,** because one can draw a distinction between them. **When** Rabbi Meir **penalizes** an offender for even an unintentional offense, it is where the offender violated **a rabbinic law,** e.g., he rendered food impure. But where he violated **a Torah law,** e.g., he cooked on Shabbat, **he does not penalize** him. The reason for this distinction is that people treat Torah prohibitions more seriously, and consequently there is no need to impose a fine for unintentional transgression in order to distance people from the transgression.
>
> The Gemara asks: **But isn't pouring** wine as a libation before an idol prohibited **by Torah law, and** even so Rabbi Meir **penalizes** the offender, even if his transgression is unintentional? The Gemara answers: This is an exception to the principle. **Due to the severity** of the prohibition against **idol worship,** Rabbi Meir **penalizes** the offender even if he acts unintentionally.

The *gemara* immediately presses a difficulty: one of the cases in the *mishna* is *menasech* — pouring libations to *avodah zarah* — which is itself a *de'oraita* violation. Why does Rabbi Meir fine there? The *gemara* answers: *mishum chumra de'avodah zarah kanis lei* — because of the unique severity of *avodah zarah*, Rabbi Meir treats it as a special case and does fine. The very extremity of *avodah zarah* as a transgression overrides the general rule, and the fine is imposed regardless. This is structurally ironic — the gravity of the offense that would normally counsel against fining is here the very reason for the fine — but the *gemara* accepts this as the explanation.

### Yehuda's Reverse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא קַשְׁיָא – כִּי לָא קָנֵיס, בִּדְרַבָּנַן; בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – קָנֵיס. וְהָא מְנַסֵּךְ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְלָא קָנֵיס! מִשּׁוּם חוּמְרָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. מִיבְדָּל בְּדִילִי מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: And there is also **no contradiction** between one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda and** the other statement **of Rabbi Yehuda. When** Rabbi Yehuda **does not penalize** an offender for an unintentional offense, it is where he violated **a rabbinic law.** But where he violated **a Torah law,** he **penalizes** him even if he transgressed unintentionally, owing to the severity of the transgression. The Gemara asks: **But isn't pouring** wine as a libation before an idol prohibited **by Torah law, and** even so Rabbi Yehuda **does not penalize** the offender if he acted unintentionally? The Gemara answers: The argument raised previously can be reversed: **Due to the** exceptional **severity** of the prohibition against **idol worship** people **avoid it** on their own, and so there is no need to impose a fine for unintentional transgression in order to distance people from it.

Rabbi Yehuda's self-contradiction is resolved by applying the mirror-image principle: *ki lo kanis bidrabbanan, de'oraita kanis* — Rabbi Yehuda does *not* fine the *shogeg* for a rabbinic violation, but does fine him for a biblical one. Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning operates in the opposite direction from Rabbi Meir's: where the violation is biblical and weighty, Rabbi Yehuda feels there is both justification and need for imposing the fine even on the inadvertent actor. Where the violation is merely rabbinic, he limits the fine to the *meizid*.

Again the *gemara* presses: the *mishna* case of *menasech* is also biblical — why does Rabbi Yehuda not fine there? The answer is the same logic reversed: *mishum chumra de'avodat kokhavim mivdal badle minei* — the severity of idolatry is so extreme that people are simply not going to commit it; there is no need for a rabbinic deterrent, because *avodah zarah* is self-deterring by virtue of its theological weight.

### Planting Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר אַדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – דְּתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹטֵעַ בַּשַּׁבָּת, בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יְקַיֵּים; בְּמֵזִיד – יַעֲקֹר; וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית – בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד יַעֲקֹר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּשְּׁבִיעִית – בְּשׁוֹגֵג יְקַיֵּים, בְּמֵזִיד יַעֲקֹר; וּבַשַּׁבָּת – בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד, יַעֲקֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **But** then it is possible to **raise a contradiction between** one statement of **Rabbi Meir and** another statement of **Rabbi Meir** even with respect to matters that are prohibited **by Torah law, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Shabbat* 2:11): With regard to **one who plants** a tree **on Shabbat,** if he does so **unintentionally, he may keep** the tree. If he acted **intentionally, it must be uprooted. And** if he planted the tree **during the Sabbatical** Year, then **whether** he did so **unintentionally or intentionally, it must be uprooted;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.**
>
> **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to planting a tree **in the Sabbatical** Year, if one does so **unintentionally, he may keep** the tree. If he acted **intentionally, it must be uprooted. And** if he planted the tree **on Shabbat,** then **whether** he did so **unintentionally or intentionally, it must be uprooted.** Although it is prohibited by Torah law to plant a tree in the Sabbatical Year, Rabbi Meir penalizes the offender and requires that the tree be uprooted, even if he acted unintentionally. This seems to contradict Rabbi Meir's ruling in the previously mentioned *baraita*, that one who unintentionally cooked on Shabbat is not penalized.

The *gemara* is not yet satisfied. It introduces a *baraita* about planting on Shabbat and planting during *shemita* to demonstrate that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda appear to reverse themselves even within the category of *de'oraita* violations. The *baraita* states: one who plants on Shabbat — *beshogeg yekayem*, if done accidentally the planting may stand; *bemeizid ye'aker*, if done willfully it must be uprooted — according to Rabbi Meir. On *shemita*, however, whether *shogeg* or *meizid*, the planting must be uprooted — Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Yehuda takes the opposite positions: on *shemita*, *beshogeg yekayem*; on Shabbat, whether *shogeg* or *meizid*, it must be uprooted.

This is more difficult to resolve through the *de'oraita*/*derabbanan* distinction because both Shabbat and *shemita* are *de'oraita*. The *gemara* points out that the problem actually emerges from within the *baraita* itself: why do both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda treat Shabbat and *shemita* differently from each other when both are biblical?

### Shemita Laxity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, תִּקָּשֶׁה לָךְ הִיא גּוּפַהּ – מִכְּדֵי הָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וְהָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מַאי שְׁנָא שַׁבָּת וּמַאי שְׁנָא שְׁבִיעִית? אֶלָּא הָתָם, כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא – אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֲנִי אוֹמֵר בַּשַּׁבָּת – בְּשׁוֹגֵג יְקַיֵּים, בְּמֵזִיד יַעֲקֹר; וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית – בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד יַעֲקֹר? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל מוֹנִין לַשְּׁבִיעִית, וְאֵין מוֹנִין לְשַׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to your reasoning,** that you raise such a contradiction, **raise a contradiction** in **this** *baraita* **itself. Since this** prohibition of planting on Shabbat **is by Torah law and this** prohibition of planting in the Sabbatical Year **is by Torah law, what is different about Shabbat and what is different about the Sabbatical** Year, that both Sages distinguish between the two *halakhot*. **Rather, there the reason** is **as is taught** explicitly: **Rabbi Meir said: For what** reason **do I say:** If one planted a tree **on Shabbat unintentionally, he may keep** the tree, but if he did so **intentionally, it must be uprooted; but in the Sabbatical** Year, **whether** the tree was planted **unintentionally or intentionally, it must be uprooted?** It is **because Jews count** the years of the tree, with regard to the prohibition against eating the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting [*orla*] and with regard to the *halakha* of fourth-year produce, **from the Sabbatical** Year. Therefore, if the tree was planted in the Sabbatical Year, people will remember, and they might come to think planting a tree in the Sabbatical Year is permitted. For this reason, Rabbi Meir imposed a fine and required that the tree be uprooted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מוֹנִין לְשַׁבָּתוֹת. דָּבָר אַחֵר: נֶחְשְׁדוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית, וְלֹא נֶחְשְׁדוּ עַל הַשַּׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **But they do not count from Shabbat,** as there is no significance to the day of the week on which the tree was planted, and consequently no one remembers that the tree was planted on Shabbat.
>
> **Alternatively,** the difference between Shabbat and the Sabbatical Year can be explained as follows: **Jews are suspected of** desecrating **the Sabbatical** Year, **but they are not suspected of** desecrating **Shabbat.** Therefore, Rabbi Meir imposes a penalty for the unintentional desecration of the Sabbatical Year, but he imposes no such penalty for the unintentional desecration of Shabbat.

The *gemara* turns to a statement in the *baraita* where Rabbi Meir himself explains his reasoning. He says: why do I rule *beshogeg yekayem* for Shabbat but impose uprooting even for *shogeg* on *shemita*? One reason: *mipnei she-yisrael monin lishmita ve-ein monin leShabbasos* — because the Jewish people count years according to the *shemita* cycle but do not count by Shabbat weeks in the same way. The practical consequence is that if someone planted during *shemita* and one does the arithmetic from when the fruit becomes edible, it will be evident to everyone that the planting occurred during the *shemita* year. That public visibility is a violation of *shemita*'s sanctity, and therefore we force uprooting to prevent the impression that such planting was condoned. By contrast, there is no corresponding calculation that reveals whether a planting occurred on a weekday or on Shabbat.

An additional explanation is given: *necheshdu Yisrael al hashevi'is ve-lo necheshdu al haShabbatos* — Israel was suspected of laxity regarding *shemita* observance, but not regarding Shabbat. This is the more direct parallel to Rabbi Meir's general principle. Even though *shemita* is *de'oraita*, the actual observance level in the community was loose — there was practical laxity around *shemita* — and so it resembles a *derabbanan* situation in terms of behavioral patterns. Since people treat it with insufficient seriousness, Rabbi Meir applies the same logic that would normally attach to a *derabbanan* violation: fine the *shogeg* to create enforcement. The *gemara* offers a brief intermediate discussion about whether someone planting near the end of a year could be detected as having planted on Shabbat, before ultimately settling on the *necheshdu* principle as the operative reason.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״דָּבָר אַחֵר״? הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכִי תֵּימָא שַׁבָּת נָמֵי, זִימְנִין דְּמִיקְּלַע יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים – בְּשַׁבָּת, דְּאִי נָטַע הַהוּא יוֹמָא הוּא דְּסָלְקָא לֵיהּ שַׁתָּא, וְאִי לָא – לָא סָלְקָא לֵיהּ שַׁתָּא, תָּא שְׁמַע דָּבָר אַחֵר: נֶחְשְׁדוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית, וְלֹא נֶחְשְׁדוּ עַל הַשַּׁבָּתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the point of Rabbi Meir adding the second reason, introduced with the term **alternatively?** The Gemara answers: **This is what** Rabbi Meir **is saying: And if you would say** to contest the first reason: Jews **also** count from **Shabbat,** and unless the tree is uprooted, people will remember that it was planted on Shabbat, as at **times the thirtieth day** before Rosh HaShana **falls on Shabbat. As, if he planted** the tree **on that day,** the time from when he planted it **counts for him as** a full **year** with regard to the prohibition of fruit that grows during the first three years after the tree was planted. **And if** he did **not** plant it on that Shabbat, but rather on the next day, the twenty-ninth day before Rosh HaShana, **it does not count for him as** a full **year.** In such a case people will remember that the tree was planted on Shabbat, and they might come to think that planting a tree on Shabbat is permitted.
>
> Consequently, Rabbi Meir adds: **Come** and **hear** that **alternatively,** the difference between Shabbat and the Sabbatical Year can be explained as follows: **Jews are suspected of** desecrating **the Sabbatical** Year, **but they are not suspected of** desecrating **Shabbat.** Therefore, Rabbi Meir imposes a penalty for the unintentional desecration of the Sabbatical Year, but not for the unintentional desecration of Shabbat.

Rabbi Yehuda's reverse position on *shemita* is explained by the local tradition in Rabbi Yehuda's locale: *be'atrei de-Rabbi Yehuda chamru lehu shevi'is* — in Rabbi Yehuda's region, *shemita* was observed with great stringency, and therefore there was no need for an additional rabbinic fine in the *shogeg* case. The *gemara* cites an anecdote about someone in that region who boasted to a neighbor that he did not eat *shemita* produce as others did, suggesting a culture of heightened *shemita* observance. Admittedly the connection is not entirely self-evident — the anecdote might equally demonstrate that violations were common enough to be noteworthy — but the *gemara* cites it as evidence of local stringency.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא קַשְׁיָא – בְּאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה חֲמִירָא לְהוּ שְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: And there is also **no contradiction** between one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** with regard to the Sabbatical Year **and** the other statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** with regard to Shabbat. This is because **in Rabbi Yehuda's place** the prohibition against desecrating **the Sabbatical** Year **was** regarded **by them** as a most **serious** one, and therefore there was no need to impose a penalty for its unintentional desecration, although it is a Torah law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לַחֲבֵירוֹ: דַּיָּיר בַּר דַּיָּירְתָּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא לָא אָכְלִי פֵּירֵי דִשְׁבִיעִית כְּווֹתָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adduces proof that this is true: It once happened **that** there was **a certain** person **who said to another** to insult him: **Convert [*dayyar*], son of a convert.** In anger the second person **said to** the first in response: At least **I don't eat produce of the Sabbatical** Year **as you** do. This indicates that the Sabbatical Year was treated in that place as a very serious prohibition; therefore, Rabbi Yehuda deemed it unnecessary to impose a penalty for its unintentional desecration.

## Teruma Case

### Tashlumin Halakha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה – מְשַׁלֵּם חוּלִּין טְהוֹרִין. שִׁילֵּם חוּלִּין טְמֵאִים, מַהוּ? אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – תַּשְׁלוּמָיו תַּשְׁלוּמִין, בְּמֵזִיד – אֵין תַּשְׁלוּמָיו תַּשְׁלוּמִין. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה תַּשְׁלוּמָיו תַּשְׁלוּמִין, וְחוֹזֵר וּמְשַׁלֵּם חוּלִּין טְהוֹרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to discuss another apparent contradiction between rulings of Rabbi Meir with regard to penalties imposed for the unintentional transgression of a rabbinic law: **Come** and **hear** what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 7:7): If **one partook of *teruma*,**which is the property of a priest, and it was ritually **impure, he pays** restitution with ritually **pure, non-sacred** produce. **What is** the *halakha* if **he paid** restitution with ritually **impure, non-sacred** produce? **Sumakhos said in the name of Rabbi Meir:** If he paid restitution with ritually impure, non-sacred produce **unintentionally, his payment is valid.** But if he did so **intentionally, his payment is not valid. And the Rabbis say:** Although **both** in **this** case **and** in **that** case **his payment is valid,** the Sages imposed a penalty and said that **he returns and pays** restitution a second time with ritually **pure, non-sacred** produce.

The *gemara* now introduces the laws of someone who eats a *kohen*'s *teruma*. The baseline *halacha* is that eating *teruma* intentionally (*meizid*) makes one liable to pay its monetary value. Eating it accidentally (*shogeg*), however, triggers a different mechanism: one must pay back in food, and that food, upon transfer to the *kohen*, itself becomes *teruma*. There is a debate about whether the quantity or the value must be matched, but the basic structure is that the repayment is made in food which takes on *teruma* status in the act of being given.

The present case involves someone who ate *teruma temeiah* — ritually impure *teruma*, which has substantially diminished value, since a *kohen* in a state of *tumah* can consume it but its general utility is limited. The question is what happens when this person repays with *chullin temeiim* — impure ordinary food — rather than *chullin tehorim*, which is what he should have given, since the latter would upon transfer become *teruma tehorah*, the more valuable form of *teruma*.

### Sumchus Position

**Sumchus, in the name of Rabbi Meir, rules: *beshogeg, tashlumin tashlumin* — if done accidentally, the repayment counts and the obligation is discharged; *bemeizid, ein tashlumin tashlumin* — if done willfully, the repayment does not count. The *hakhamim* say: in both cases the payment counts as *teruma*, but in both cases the person must pay a second time with *chullin tehorim*. So Rabbi Meir fines only the *meizid*, whereas the *hakhamim* fine even the *shogeg*.**

### Challenge to Fine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: בְּמֵזִיד – אַמַּאי אֵין תַּשְׁלוּמָיו תַּשְׁלוּמִין? תָּבֹא עָלָיו בְּרָכָה – דְּאָכֵיל מִינֵּיהּ מִידֵּי דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתוֹ, וְקָא מְשַׁלֵּם לֵיהּ מִידֵּי דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ בִּימֵי טוּמְאָתוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed** the following question concerning the opinion of Rabbi Meir: In the case where he paid restitution with ritually impure, non-sacred produce **intentionally, why is his payment not valid?** On the contrary, **blessing should come upon him, as he partook of something that is not fit for** the priest to partake of even **during the days of his impurity,** since it is prohibited for a priest to partake of impure *teruma*, whether he himself is pure or impure, **and he pays** restitution **to him with something,** i.e., impure, non-sacred produce, **which is** at least **fit for him** to partake of **during the days of his impurity.**

Before applying this to the larger debate, the *gemara* raises a striking logical question: why should willfully repaying *chullin temeiim* not count at all? The *kohen* ate *teruma temeiah*, which he could consume only in a state of *tumah* and which was of diminished value. The person repaying gave back *chullin temeiim*, which the *kohen* could likewise consume while *tamei*. Yes, the food became *teruma temeiah* upon transfer, but what exactly is the harm? The *kohen* has received back something functionally equivalent to what was taken.

### Rava Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ כְּדִי: חַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: אָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה – מְשַׁלֵּם כָּל דְּהוּ. אָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה – מְשַׁלֵּם חוּלִּין טְהוֹרִים. שִׁילֵּם חוּלִּין טְמֵאִים, מַהוּ? סוֹמְכוֹס אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי מֵאִיר: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – תַּשְׁלוּמָיו תַּשְׁלוּמִין, בְּמֵזִיד – אֵין תַּשְׁלוּמָיו תַּשְׁלוּמִין. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה – תַּשְׁלוּמָיו תַּשְׁלוּמִין, וְחוֹזֵר וּמְשַׁלֵּם חוּלִּין טְהוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said, and some say it** is **unattributed:** The *baraita* **is incomplete and this is what it is teaching:** If **one partook of** ritually **impure *teruma*, he can pay** restitution with **anything,** even impure, non-sacred produce. If **he partook of** ritually **pure *teruma*, he pays** restitution with ritually **pure, non-sacred** produce. The *baraita* continues: **What is** the *halakha* if **he paid** restitution for ritually pure *teruma* with ritually **impure, non-sacred** produce? There is a tannaitic dispute about this. **Sumakhos said in the name of Rabbi Meir:** If he paid restitution with ritually impure, non-sacred produce **unintentionally, his payment is valid.** But if he did so **intentionally, his payment is not valid. And the Rabbis say: Both** in **this** case **and** in **that** case, **his payment is valid,** but the Sages imposed a penalty and said that **he returns and pays** restitution a second time with ritually **pure, non-sacred** produce.

Rava, and some transmit it anonymously, responds with a radical solution: *chasuri michasra vehachi katani* — the *baraita* is incomplete as stated and must be reread. The entire scenario must be reframed. The *baraita* is not speaking of a case where one ate *teruma temeiah* and repaid with *chullin temeiim*. Rather, the case must be that one ate *teruma tehorah* — pure *teruma*, of full value — and then repaid with *chullin temeiim*. Now it is obvious why repaying with impure food is inadequate: the *kohen* had pure *teruma* taken from him, and he is now receiving back something far less valuable (whether measured by value according to Rashi, or by utility and convenience according to Tosafot, who holds the repayment is matched by value but notes that people prefer less *teruma tehorah* over more *teruma temeiah*). The *baraita* thus becomes: *achal teruma tehorah, meshalem chullin tehorim* — you ate pure *teruma*, you must repay with pure *chullin*. If instead you repaid with *chullin temeiim* (*shilem chullin temeim*), Sumchus in the name of Rabbi Meir says: *beshogeg, tashlumin tashlumin*; *bemeizid, ein tashlumin tashlumin*. And the *hakhamim* fine in both cases.

### Restitution Policy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – גַּבְרָא לְשַׁלּוֹמֵי קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, אֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְלִיקְנְסֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **How can** these cases **be compared? There, the man intends to pay; should we arise and penalize him?** It is specifically with regard to this case that Rabbi Meir holds that no penalty is imposed, as his mistake was made in the course of performing a laudatory action. By contrast, in cases where one rendered another's produce impure and the like, there is a penalty, as the mistake was made while performing an action he should not have been performing.

Applying this to the Rabbi Meir / Rabbi Yehuda structure: the inadequate repayment is a *derabbanan* violation, and Rabbi Meir would normally fine the *shogeg* in *derabbanan* cases. Why then does Rabbi Meir not fine the *shogeg* here? The *gemara* says: *hatam gavra lishlumei ka michavein, anushei uveli knisa?* — there, the person is trying to make restitution; are we going to impose a fine on him? The critical distinction Rabbi Meir draws is between an act that is fundamentally bad — causing damage, acting destructively — and an act that is fundamentally good or remedial. Rabbi Meir's policy of fining *shogegin* in *derabbanan* cases is directed at curbing negligent harmful conduct. When someone is in the process of trying to make things right, even if he does it imperfectly, applying that fine is counterproductive. We do not penalize people who are genuinely trying to remedy a situation.

This principle is then applied to two additional cases the *gemara* had already addressed but which fit the same analysis. One who takes *maaser* on Shabbat (*hame'aser beShabbat*) — a rabbinic violation of Shabbat — is told *beshogeg yokhal, bemeizid lo yokhal*. Why is Rabbi Meir not fining the *shogeg* here, given that this is a *derabbanan* violation? *Hatam gavra letikuni kamikhaven* — the person was trying to do a *mitzvah*, separating *terumot u'maasrot*. Similarly, one who immerses vessels in a *mikvah* on Shabbat (*hamatbil kelim beShabbat*), again a rabbinic violation: *beshogeg yeshtamesh bahem, bemeizid lo yeshtamesh bahem*. Why no fine for *shogeg*? *Hatam gavra letahorei kamikhaven* — he was trying to purify his vessels.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמְעַשֵּׂר בַּשַּׁבָּת; בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יֹאכַל, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יֹאכַל. הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – גַּבְרָא לְתַקּוֹנֵי קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, אֲנַן לֵיקוּם וְלִיקְנְסֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to adduce further proof: **Come** and **hear** what was taught in a mishna (*Terumot* 2:3): With regard to **one who tithes** produce **on Shabbat,** which is prohibited by rabbinic law because it appears as though he were repairing an article that requires repair, if he did this **unintentionally, he may consume** the produce, as it has been tithed and rendered fit for consumption. But if he did this **intentionally, he may not consume** it. Apparently, the Sages did not penalize the unintentional offender due to an intentional offender, although tithing produce on Shabbat is prohibited by rabbinic law. The Gemara answers: **How can** these cases **be compared? There, the man intends to** do a worthy deed and **repair** the produce by tithing it; **should we arise and penalize him?** As above, Rabbi Meir would agree that in such a case the unintentional offender is not penalized.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמַּטְבִּיל כֵּלִים בַּשַּׁבָּת; בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן! הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – גַּבְרָא לְטַהוֹרֵי מָאנֵי קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, אֲנַן לֵיקוּם וְלִיקְנְסֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara brings yet another proof: **Come** and **hear** what was taught in that same mishna (*Terumot* 2:3): With regard to **one who immerses utensils** in a ritual bath **on Shabbat,** which is also prohibited by the Sages because it appears as if he were repairing an article that requires repair, if he immerses them **unintentionally, he may use them.** But if he immerses them **intentionally, he may not use them.** Here too the Sages did not penalize the unintentional offender due to an intentional offender. The Gemara answers as before: **How can** these cases **be compared? There, the man intends to** do a praiseworthy act and **purify the utensils; should we arise and penalize him?** The same distinction stated previously applies in this mishna as well.

There is one additional case that was returned to after it had initially been overlooked: *dam she-nitma ve-zarko* — blood of a *korban* that became *tamei* and was thrown on the *mizbeach*. The *halacha* is that even if such blood is thrown *bemeizid*, the *korban* is still valid because the *tzitz* worn by the *kohen gadol* has the power to render such service acceptable. Rashi clarifies that throwing *tamei* blood is itself only a rabbinic violation, since the presence of the *tzitz* makes it *de'oraita* permissible. The *baraita* rules: *beshogeg hurtzah, bemeizid lo hurtzah* — if accidental, the service is accepted; if willful, it is not, meaning the *kohanim* may not eat the meat. Why does Rabbi Meir not fine the *shogeg* here, given the *derabbanan* character of the violation? *Hatam gavra lechapporei kamikhaven* — the *kohen* is engaged in the *avodah*, trying to achieve atonement for the owners. One does not impose fines on people in the midst of performing *mitzvot* and the Temple service.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דָּם שֶׁנִּטְמָא, וּזְרָקוֹ; בְּשׁוֹגֵג – הוּרְצָה, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא הוּרְצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara tries to bring proof concerning the previously stated opinion of Rabbi Meir that a penalty is imposed in a case of an unintentional transgression of a rabbinical law: **Come** and **hear** what was taught in a *baraita*: In the case of **blood** of an offering **that became impure and** a priest **sprinkled it** on the altar, if he did so **unintentionally,** the offering is **accepted** and achieves atonement for the owner of the offering. If he sprinkled the blood **intentionally,** the offering **is not accepted.** In any event, the *tanna* of this *baraita* did not penalize the unintentional offender due to an intentional offender, and this sprinkling of blood is prohibited by rabbinic law.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי מֵאִיר: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם – גַּבְרָא לְכַפּוֹרֵי קָא מְכַוֵּין, אֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִקְנְסֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Meir** could have **said to you: How can** these cases **be compared? There, the person intends to** do a mitzva and **atone; should we arise and penalize him?** Here too, the mistake was made while performing a laudatory act. In such a case, even Rabbi Meir agrees that an unintentional offender is not penalized due to an intentional offender.

The corresponding case for Rabbi Yehuda's apparent reversal also involves the *derabbanan* category. In the case of *nafelu nitpatzu* — precious nuts, some of which are *orlah*, that all get cracked and thereby lose their *chashivus* (halachic significance) and become *batel* — Rabbi Meir says whether *shogeg* or *meizid*, the mixture remains forbidden. This is consistent with Rabbi Meir. But Rabbi Yehuda joins Rabbi Meir — which is inconsistent with Rabbi Yehuda's usual position of not fining in *derabbanan* cases. Rabbis Yose and Shimon, by contrast, are lenient and only disallow the mixture when it was *meizid*. The *gemara* explains Rabbi Yehuda's strictness: *mishum de-ati le'arumei* — because in this scenario there is a powerful incentive to scheme. If a person's entire stock of valuable nuts is ruined because of a single *orlah* nut, the temptation to deliberately crush them all and then claim it was an accident is enormous. Rabbi Yehuda therefore closes off this avenue by not distinguishing between *shogeg* and *meizid* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – בִּדְרַבָּנַן, דְּתַנְיָא: נָפְלוּ וְנִתְפַּצְּעוּ; אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג, וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד – לֹא יַעֲלוּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: בְּשׁוֹגֵג – יַעֲלוּ, בְּמֵזִיד – לֹא יַעֲלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda: **And they raised a contradiction between** one statement **of Rabbi Yehuda and** another statement **of Rabbi Yehuda** with regard to matters that are prohibited only **by rabbinic law. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If there are nuts from Perekh that are *orla*, and **they fell** into other nuts and became intermingled with them, the entire mixture is forbidden, even if the nuts that are *orla* are few in number. This is because they are deemed significant when they are whole and they are not nullified in a mixture. If the nuts **were broken** afterward, **whether** they were broken **unintentionally or** they were broken **intentionally, they are not nullified** in the mixture, despite the fact that they are no longer regarded as significant and should therefore be subject to nullification; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon** disagree and **say:** If they were broken **unintentionally, they are nullified,** but if they were broken **intentionally, they are not nullified.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא חַד בִּתְרֵי בָּטֵל, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דִּגְזוּר, וְקָא קָנֵיס רַבִּי יְהוּדָה! הָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתֵי לְאִיעָרוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But here, by Torah law** the forbidden substance **is nullified** if its ratio in the mixture is not more than **one in two,** i.e., when the majority of the mixture is permitted, **and** it was **the Sages who decreed** that significant items are not subject to nullification. **And** nevertheless, **Rabbi Yehuda penalizes** an unintentional offender due to an intentional offender. This seems to contradict what was stated previously, that Rabbi Yehuda does not impose a penalty for an unintentional offense if the transgression involves the violation of a rabbinic law. The Gemara answers: **There, this is the reasoning of Rabbi Yehuda,** that a penalty was imposed in the case where he unintentionally broke the nuts **due to** the concern **that** without a penalty **he will come to employ artifice** and intentionally break the nuts in order to effect nullification of the Perekh nuts.

Finally, regarding Rabbi Yose's reversal in the case of a shoot of *orlah* mixed with other plantings (*netia shel orlah she-nit'arba benetiot acherot*): Rabbi Yose there rules that even intentional picking (which creates *bitul*) is acceptable — *afilu mitkavein lilkot, ya'alu be'echad umatayim*. This seems far more lenient than his position on the nuts. The *gemara* resolves this by citing the principle of Rabbah and Rabbi Yochanan: *chazakah ein adam oser karmo benetiyah achat* — it is simply inconceivable that a person would allow an *orlah* shoot to be planted among his other vines without having taken precautions. The scenario is so implausible, so unlikely ever to arise in practice, that the rabbis did not impose their stricter rules upon it. The nuts case, by contrast, involves a genuinely common scenario, and there fining is warranted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָמֵי דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אַדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – דִּתְנַן: נְטִיעָה שֶׁל עׇרְלָה וְשֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ בִּנְטִיעוֹת אֲחֵרוֹת, הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יְלַקֵּט. וְאִם לִיקֵּט – יַעֲלוּ בְּאֶחָד וּמָאתַיִם, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לְלַקֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the previous *baraita*: **And they raised a contradiction between** one statement **of Rabbi Yosei and** another statement **of Rabbi Yosei, as we learned** in a mishna (*Orla* 1:6): If **a sapling** that has the status **of *orla* or** a grapevine sapling has the status **of diverse kinds in a vineyard,** e.g., one vine in a vineyard had grain planted near it and become prohibited, and the grain was then uprooted, **became intermingled with other** saplings, and one does not know which is the forbidden sapling, **he may not gather** the produce of any of the saplings. **And if he gathered** the produce, the forbidden produce **is nullified** if its ratio in the mixture is not more than **one** part forbidden produce **in two hundred** parts permitted produce, **provided that he did not intend to gather** the produce in order that the forbidden produce will become nullified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמִּתְכַּוֵּין לְלַקֵּט – יַעֲלוּ בְּאֶחָד וּמָאתַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even if he intentionally gathered** the produce in order that the forbidden produce would become nullified, the forbidden produce **is nullified** if its ratio in the mixture is not more than **one** part forbidden produce **in two hundred** parts permitted produce. This seems to contradict what Rabbi Yosei said in the *baraita* cited previously with regard to nuts, that if the nuts were broken intentionally, they are not nullified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רָבָא: חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר אֶת כַּרְמוֹ בִּנְטִיעָה אַחַת. וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר אֶת כַּרְמוֹ בִּנְטִיעָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Wasn't it** already **stated with regard to** that mishna in explanation of Rabbi Yosei's opinion that **Rava says:** There is **a presumption** that **a person does not render his entire vineyard forbidden for the sake of one sapling.** Therefore, it can be assumed that one does not intentionally plant a sapling that has the status of *orla* or of diverse kinds in a vineyard among other saplings without properly marking it. If he did so, it is uncommon, and the Sages did not impose a penalty in an uncommon case. **And similarly, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** There is **a presumption** that **a person does not render his vineyard forbidden for the sake of one sapling,** and therefore the Sages did not impose a penalty.

## Pigul & Testimony

### Pigul Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכֹּהֲנִים שֶׁפִּגְּלוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, מְזִידִין – חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **priests disqualified an offering with improper intention in the Temple,** by expressing, while sacrificing the offering, the intention of sprinkling the blood of the offering, burning its fats on the altar, or consuming it, after its appointed time, and they did so **intentionally, they are liable** to pay the value of the offering to its owner, who must now bring another offering.

The *gemara* now pivots from the question of liability for *hezek she'eino nikar* to the question of testimonial credibility regarding invisible acts. The setting is *pigul*: a *korban* is rendered *pigul* — halachically disqualified — when the *kohen* performing the service harbors the intention at the wrong moment to eat it or offer it beyond its permitted time. This is an invisible act; no one can see what the *kohen* is thinking. The *mishna* says that *kohanim* who intentionally cause *pigul* are liable to the owners. The underlying *hezek she'eino nikar* question has already been resolved — we fine the *meizid*. But now the *gemara* turns to a different question: when will we believe someone who claims such an invisible act occurred?

### Abaye: Immediate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ בִּטְהָרוֹת, וְאָמַר לוֹ: טְהָרוֹת שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמְּךָ, נִטְמְאוּ; הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה עִמּוֹ בִּזְבָחִים, וְאָמַר לוֹ: זְבָחִים שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמְּךָ, נִתְפַּגְּלוּ – נֶאֱמָן. אֲבָל אָמַר לוֹ: טְהָרוֹת שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמְּךָ בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי נִטְמְאוּ, וּזְבָחִים שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמְּךָ בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי נִתְפַּגְּלוּ – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 2:2): If one **was preparing ritually pure** food **with** another, **and he said to him: The ritually pure** food **that I prepared with you became ritually impure,** or if **he was preparing sacrifices with** another person, **and he said to him: The sacrifices that I prepared with you became disqualified due to improper intention,** he is **deemed credible** with regard to these claims. **But** if **he said to** the other: **The ritually pure** food **that I prepared with you on such and such a day became ritually impure,** or **the sacrifices that I prepared with you on such and such a day became disqualified due to improper intention, he is not deemed credible.**

A *baraita* is introduced: if one was working together with another person on *taharot* (maintaining ritual purity) and declared *taharot she'asiti imcha nitme'u* — the pure items we were working on together have become *tamei* — he is believed (*ne'eman*). Similarly, a *kohen* who declares during or immediately upon the service that *zvachim she'asiti imcha nitpiglu* — the sacrifices I performed for you have been rendered *pigul* — is believed. But if the same person comes days later and says *taharot she'asiti imcha beyom ploni nitme'u* or *zvachim she'asiti imcha beyom ploni nitpiglu* — referring to events of a specific past date — he is not believed (*eino ne'eman*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כֹּל שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ – נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in **the first clause** of the *baraita* **and what is different** in **the latter clause,** that in the first clause he is deemed credible, whereas in the latter clause he is not? **Abaye says** that the principle is as follows: **As long as it is** still **in his power** to do what he said he had done, he is **deemed credible.** Therefore, while he is involved in the preparation of the ritually pure food or the sacrifices, and consequently he is still able to disqualify them, he is deemed credible when he says that they already became disqualified. But once he makes a statement about actions he performed in the past and he is no longer able to disqualify the objects of those actions, he is not deemed credible.

Abaye explains the distinction through the principle of *kol shebeyado ne'eman* — whoever has the matter in his control is believed. At the moment of the first declaration, the person is still actively working with the *taharot* or the *kohen* has shechted the animal but not yet thrown the blood. Because he retains control over the outcome — he could render them *tamei* or *pigul* at this very moment if he chose — this gives his claim a special credibility, somewhat analogous to a *migo*. According to Abaye, this principle applies strictly: the person must literally still have the matter in hand.

### Rava: Suspicion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּאַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי, וּלְבָתַר הָכִי אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Both the first clause and the latter clause deal with testimony about the past. The difference is that the latter clause is referring to a case **where he found him** a first time **and told him nothing** about disqualification, **and** then **afterward he found him** a second time **and told him** what had purportedly happened. In such a case he is suspected of lying, as, if it were true that the pure food had become impure or the offering had become disqualified, he would have imparted that information earlier. Since he had said nothing at the time, and he spoke up only later, it is assumed that he was lying and that his intention was merely to annoy the other.

Rava disagrees with Abaye's framework. It is not necessary that the act still be *mamash beyado* at the moment of the claim. Rather, the reason for disbelieving the later claim is not absence of control but suspicious delay: *keivan de'ashkechei velo amar lei velo midi* — since he encountered the person and said nothing at the time, and only later makes the claim, the delay itself is suspicious. Why would someone who witnessed or caused a problem fail to mention it immediately? The belated disclosure raises the inference of ulterior motive. But if someone reports the issue promptly after completing the service — even if it is technically no longer *beyado* — he would be believed under Rava's view.

### Delayed Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ: טְהָרוֹת שֶׁעָשִׂיתִי עִמְּךָ בְּיוֹם פְּלוֹנִי – נִטְמְאוּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שׁוּרַת הַדִּין אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן. אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי אַסִּי: רַבִּי, אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן? הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה הֶאֱמִינַתּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It is related that there was **a certain** person **who said to another: The ritually pure** food **that I prepared with you on such and such a day became ritually impure.** The owner of the food **came before Rabbi Ami,** asking him what to do. Rabbi Ami **said to him:** You may continue to treat the food as ritually pure, as **in principle,** the other person **is not deemed credible. Rabbi Asi said before him: My teacher, do you say this? So said Rabbi Yoḥanan in the name of Rabbi Yosei: What can I do** when I see **that the Torah deemed him credible** in such a case?

The *gemara* now addresses a case brought before Rabbi Ami involving precisely this scenario, and Rabbi Ami rules *eino ne'eman* — he is not believed, because the claim concerns something that happened in the past with no immediate disclosure.

### Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָן הֶאֱמִינַתּוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בִּיסְנָא: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים יוֹכִיחַ, דְּכִי אָמַר פִּגּוּל – מְהֵימַן; וּמְנָא יָדְעִינַן? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְכׇל אָדָם לֹא יִהְיֶה בְּאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״! אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּמְהֵימַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Where** does it **deem him credible? Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Bisna says: The High Priest on Yom Kippur will prove** this point, **as when** he **says** that he had **improper intent,** he is **deemed credible. And from where do we know** that he had improper intent? **But isn't it written: "And no man shall be in the Tent of Meeting** when he goes in to make atonement for the holy place" (Leviticus 16:17)? **Rather, is it not because** he is **deemed credible** in his testimony even after he performed the service, and it is no longer in his power to disqualify the offering?

Rabbi Assi raises a counterposition in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Yossi: *ma e'eseh she-haTorah he'eminaso* — what can I do, the Torah has believed him. The *gemara* asks: where does the Torah demonstrate this believability? Rabbi Yitzchak bar Bisna answers: the *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur serves as proof. If the *kohen gadol* were to emerge from the *Kodesh Kodashim* and declare that something had gone wrong — that the service had been rendered *pigul*, for example — presumably we would believe him, even though the act occurred in a space where no other person was permitted. This shows that even after the fact, the Torah grants believability to such declarations.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא דִּשְׁמַעְנֵיהּ דְּפַגֵּיל! אִי לָאו דִּמְהֵימַן, אַף עַל גַּב דִּשְׁמַעְנֵיהּ נָמֵי לָא מְהֵימַן, דְּדִלְמָא לְבָתַר הָכִי קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **But perhaps we heard that he disqualified** the offering **with improper intention,** i.e., maybe he stated his intention out loud while performing the service and it was heard outside. The Gemara answers: **Were** he **not deemed credible** about the matter, then **even if we heard him** voice his intention, he **would also not be deemed credible.** Why? The reason is **that perhaps** he actually sprinkled the blood with the proper intention, and it was only **afterward** that **he said** what he said, and at that time he could no longer disqualify the offering. Rather, he is certainly deemed credible when he says that he disqualified the offering with improper intention.

The *gemara* challenges this: perhaps we would only believe the *kohen gadol* because we heard his voice from within, indicating something was said at the time of the service. The *gemara* responds: that would not suffice either, because the *kohen gadol* could have uttered those words after performing the service, intending to deceive. Therefore it must be that the Torah grants inherent credibility to such testimony. The *gemara* then raises a further challenge — perhaps there were peepholes through which observers could have seen the *kohen gadol* — and ultimately concedes *kashya*: it is not an airtight proof. The case of the *kohen gadol* may be consistent with Rava's position — that if the declaration came immediately upon emerging, there was no suspicious delay — so it does not definitively establish the broader point.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא דַּחֲזֵינֵיהּ בְּפִישְׁפָּשׁ? קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection: **But perhaps we saw** what the High Priest was doing **through a small door,** through which it was possible to monitor his actions, and we saw that he sprinkled the blood with improper intention. The Gemara says: This is **difficult** for Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Bisna, as his proof can be refuted.

## Sefer Torah Cases

### Rabbi Ami Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁכָּתַבְתִּי לִפְלוֹנִי, אַזְכָּרוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ לֹא כְּתַבְתִּים לִשְׁמָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בְּיַד מִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּיַד לוֹקֵחַ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: נֶאֱמָן אַתָּה לְהַפְסִיד שְׂכָרְךָ, וְאִי אַתָּה נֶאֱמָן לְהַפְסִיד סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It is similarly related that there was **a certain** person **who came before Rabbi Ami** and **said to him:** In **the Torah scroll that I wrote for so-and-so, I did not write the mentions** of God's name **with the proper intention** that is required when writing a holy name, and therefore the scroll is not valid. Rabbi Ami **said to him:** This **Torah scroll** is currently **in whose possession?** The scribe **said to him:** It is now **in the buyer's possession.** Rabbi Ami **said to him: You are deemed credible to cause the loss of your wage,** as you admit that you wrote the Torah scroll in a faulty manner, and therefore the buyer can refuse to pay you. **But you are not deemed credible to cause a loss** to, i.e., to invalidate, **the Torah scroll.**

A case is brought before Rabbi Ami: a scribe who had written a *Sefer Torah* for a client comes forward and admits that when writing the divine names (*azkarot*), he did not write them *lishmah* — with the proper intention. The *Sefer Torah* has already been transferred to the purchaser; it is no longer *beyado*. Rabbi Ami rules: *ne'eman atah lehafsid sekharekha ve-i atah ne'eman lehafsid Sefer Torah* — you are believed to the extent that you forfeit your fee, but you are not believed to render the *Sefer Torah pasul*. The *Sefer Torah* retains its *kasher* status in the hands of the purchaser, while the scribe loses his payment for having admitted to performing the work improperly.

This creates an apparent internal tension: if the *Sefer Torah* is going to be treated as valid for the purchaser's use, why should the scribe not be paid for a valid *Sefer Torah*? The *gemara* does not fully resolve this in this context; Rabbi Ami's ruling simply bifurcates the two consequences.

### Yirmiyah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: נְהִי דְּהִפְסִיד שְׂכַר אַזְכָּרוֹת, שְׂכַר דְּסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה כּוּלֵּיהּ מִי הִפְסִיד?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, שֶׁכׇּל סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁאֵין אַזְכָּרוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ כְּתוּבוֹת לִשְׁמָן – אֵינוֹ שָׁוֶה כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said to him: Although he lost** his **wage** with regard **to the mentions** of God's name that he wrote in a faulty manner, **did he lose his wage** with regard **to the entire Torah scroll,** which he wrote correctly? Rabbi Ami **said to him: Yes,** he lost his wage for the entire Torah scroll, **as any Torah scroll in which the mentions** of God's name **have not been written with the proper intention is not worth anything.**

Rabbi Yirmiyah presses the ruling further: granted that the scribe has forfeited some fee — but must he lose the entire fee for the *Sefer Torah*? He wrote the entire text correctly; only the *azkarot* were written without proper intention. Surely he should at minimum be paid for all the writing other than the *Shem Hashem* portions?

### Worthlessness

Rabbi Ami responds: *she-kol Sefer Torah she-ein azkaroteha ketuvot lishmah einah shaveh klum* — a *Sefer Torah* whose divine names were not written *lishmah* is worth nothing at all. The reason: one cannot simply rewrite the names. Erasing *Shem Hashem* is forbidden. There is no remediation available.

### Spotted Look

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיעַבַּר עֲלַיְיהוּ קוּלְמוֹס, וְלִיקַדְּשֵׁיהּ! כְּמַאן – נֵימָא דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? דִּתְנַן: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה צָרִיךְ לִכְתּוֹב אֶת הַשֵּׁם; וְנִתְכַּוֵּון לִכְתּוֹב ״יְהוּדָה״, וְטָעָה וְלֹא הֵטִיל בּוֹ דָּלֶת; מַעֲבִיר עָלָיו קוּלְמוֹס, וּמְקַדְּשׁוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הַשֵּׁם מִן הַמּוּבְחָר. אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֶלָּא בַּחֲדָא אַזְכָּרָה, אֲבָל דְּכוּלֵּי סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה – לָא, מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כִּמְנוּמָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the scribe **pass a reed** pen **[*kulemos*]** with additional ink over instances of God's name **and sanctify them,** going over the names with the proper intention. And since there is no option to correct the Torah scroll in this manner, **in accordance with whose** opinion was this ruling issued? **Shall we say** that it is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda?**
>
> This is **as we learned** in a *baraita*: If a scribe writing a Torah scroll was at a point in the text **that he needed to write the name** of God, spelled *yod*, *heh*, *vav*, *heh*; and he erred **and intended to write Yehuda,** spelled *yod*, *heh*, *vav*, *dalet*, *heh*, **but he made a mistake** when writing Yehuda **and did not place a *dalet* in** the word, thereby unintentionally writing the name of God in the correct place, then **he should pass over it with a reed** pen. He writes over what was written **and sanctifies it** with the intention that he is writing the name of God. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say:** Even if he adds a second layer of ink, **the name** has **not** been written **in the optimal manner.** The fact that Rabbi Ami did not offer the option to rewrite the mentions of God's name to correct the scroll indicates that the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, and not in accordance with that of Rabbi Yehuda.
>
> You **can** even say that in general the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** but there is a distinction between the cases. **Rabbi Yehuda states** his opinion **only with regard to a single mention** of God's name that was initially written without the proper intention. In such a case, it is possible to pass over the name with additional ink and thereby sanctify it. **But** passing over all of the holy names found in an **entire Torah scroll** is **not** possible. Why not? **Because** if the scribe would pass his pen over all the names of God found in a Torah scroll, **it would look speckled,** as the instances of the Divine Name would be written with a thicker layer of ink and stand out.

The *gemara* presses: why not simply re-ink over the names? Could Rabbi Yehuda's ruling be invoked here — that *ma'avir alav kulmus u-mekadesho*, one may re-ink and thereby sanctify the writing? The *gemara* answers that Rabbi Yehuda only permits re-inking a single name. To re-ink every instance of *Shem Hashem* throughout an entire *Sefer Torah* — *mishum de-mikhzei ke-menumar* — would make the scroll appear spotted, with every divine name visibly different from the surrounding text. A *Sefer Torah* that appears this way is not fit for use, whether this is a *de'oraita* or *derabbanan* disqualification. Either way, the scribe has produced something worthless, and accordingly forfeits his entire fee.

### Rabbi Abbahu Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁכָּתַבְתִּי לִפְלוֹנִי, גְּוִילִין שֶׁלּוֹ לֹא עִיבַּדְתִּים לִשְׁמָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בְּיַד מִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּיַד לוֹקֵחַ. אָמַר לוֹ: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאַתָּה נֶאֱמָן לְהַפְסִיד שְׂכָרְךָ, אַתָּה נֶאֱמָן לְהַפְסִיד סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** It is further related that there was **a certain** person **who came before Rabbi Abbahu** and **said to him:** With regard to **the Torah scroll that I wrote for so-and-so, I did not process its parchment with the proper intention.** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: The Torah scroll is** currently **in whose possession?** The scribe **said to him:** It is **in the possession of the buyer.** Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: Since you are deemed credible to cause the loss of your wage,** as you have admitted that the parchment upon which the Torah scroll was written is invalid, **you are deemed credible to cause a loss** to, i.e., disqualify, **the Torah scroll.**

A parallel case is brought before Rabbi Abbahu: a scribe comes forward and says that the *gevilin* — the parchment — of a *Sefer Torah* he wrote for a client were not processed *lishmah*, with the requisite intention during their preparation. The *Sefer Torah* is already in the purchaser's possession. Rabbi Abbahu rules: *mitoch she'ata ne'eman lehafsid sekharekha, ata ne'eman lehafsid Sefer Torah* — since you are believed to lose your fee, you are also believed to render the *Sefer Torah pasul*. This is the opposite of Rabbi Ami's ruling: Rabbi Abbahu believes the scribe not only about his financial loss but about the invalidity of the scroll.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִדְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי? הָתָם, אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר טָעֵי בִּדְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה. הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּקָא מַפְסֵיד כּוּלֵּיהּ אַגְרֵיהּ – וַאֲתָא וַאֲמַר, אֵימוֹר קוּשְׁטָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** the case in which **Rabbi Ami** said that the scribe is not deemed credible to disqualify the Torah scroll? The Gemara answers: **There it can be said** that the scribe was lying and merely wished to distress the purchaser of the Torah scroll. He claimed that he had written God's names without the proper intention because **he made the mistake of Rabbi Yirmeya.** He thought, as Rabbi Yirmeya did, that as a result of his purported admission he would lose only his wage for writing the holy names, but he would still receive payment for the rest of the scroll. **Here,** by contrast, **since** the scribe knows **that** by claiming that he did not process the parchment with the proper intention, **he causes the loss of his entire wage, and** he nevertheless **comes and says** this, you should **say** that **he speaks** the **truth** and should be deemed credible. Since he is deemed credible and there is no concern that he merely wished to distress the purchaser, the Torah scroll is disqualified.

The *gemara* confronts the apparent contradiction between Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Abbahu. It resolves the two cases by focusing on the scribe's financial exposure. In Rabbi Ami's case, where only the *azkarot* were written without *lishmah*, Rabbi Yirmiyah had raised a reasonable argument that the scribe should perhaps only lose the fee for writing the divine names, not the entire fee. One might say the scribe calculated that he was in a relatively favorable position — he could claim to lose only a small portion of his fee while the *Sefer Torah* would be disqualified. There was therefore an incentive to make the false claim: he would lose very little while harming the purchaser's *Sefer Torah*. Since the claim carried only limited financial self-damage, the *migo*-like circumstantial argument for truthfulness is not compelling, and we therefore do not extend his believability to *poseling* the scroll.

In Rabbi Abbahu's case, the *gevilin* themselves — every single piece of parchment — were not processed *lishmah*. There is no question of partial loss: the entire *Sefer Torah* is unambiguously worthless, and the scribe will forfeit his entire fee. *Kivan de-ka mafsid kula agrei* — since he knows he will lose everything and comes forward anyway, *eimar kushta ka'amar* — we must presume he is telling the truth. The magnitude of his financial self-sacrifice eliminates any plausible ulterior motive, and this circumstantial evidence of truthfulness is sufficient to believe him even though the matter is no longer technically *beyado*.
