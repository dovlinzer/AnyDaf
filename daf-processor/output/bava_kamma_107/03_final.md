# Bava Kamma 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Toein Ta'anat Ganav

### Basic Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. טָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another ruling concerning the topic of a bailee who claims that a deposit in his possession was stolen. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One who** falsely **states the claim, with regard to a deposit,** that **a thief** stole it, and it becomes clear that he stole it himself, **pays double payment. If he slaughtered or sold** it, **he** must **pay fourfold or fivefold payment.**

The *daf* opens with a teaching of R. Chiya bar Abba in the name of R. Yochanan concerning a *shomer* who falsely claims that a *pikadon* entrusted to him was stolen. The case is one of *toein ta'anat ganav b'pikadon*: the watchman denies having the object, swears falsely that it was stolen, and is subsequently exposed. R. Yochanan teaches that such a person pays *tashlumei kefel* — double payment. This follows from the established reading of the Torah's section on *pikadon*, where the verse *asher yarshiun Elohim yeshalem shnayim l'rei'eihu* is understood to encompass not only the genuine *ganav* but also the watchman who falsely invokes *gneivah* as his excuse.

### Ganav vs. Gazlan

One might pause here and ask: why should a *ganav* pay double at all? Why is the *gazlan* — who openly seizes property in broad daylight — exempt from *kefel*, while the *ganav* who steals covertly must pay double? The Gemara offers two distinct rationales. One reason, not stated explicitly in the Gemara but developed by the *mefarshim*, is that the *ganav* who breaks into a home commits a more intimate violation of the victim's space. The second, and the Gemara's own reason, is theological: the *ganav* fears human detection but not divine judgment — a hypocrisy that the Torah singles out for sanction. The *gazlan*, by contrast, acts brazenly in full public view, showing at least a kind of consistency: he demonstrates no greater fear of people than of God. It is this theological distinction that makes the *ganav* more culpable in the Torah's eyes, even though, or perhaps precisely because, the *gazlan* seems more brazen.

Neither of these rationales applies straightforwardly to the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*. He did not break into anyone's home, and he displays no particular hypocrisy between his fear of man and fear of God. Nevertheless, the Torah appears to assign him the status of *kefel* — and R. Yochanan is now taking the next step.

### Double to 4 & 5

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְגַנָּב מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל – מָה גַּנָּב, שֶׁהוּא מְשַׁלֵּם כֶּפֶל, טָבַח וּמָכַר מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה; אַף טוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן, כְּשֶׁהוּא מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, טָבַח וּמָכַר מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** He explains his ruling by means of a comparison: **Since a thief pays double payment, and** one who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit also **pays double payment,** it follows that **just as a thief, who** ordinarily **pays double payment,** and if he **slaughtered or sold** an animal he stole **pays fourfold or fivefold payment, so too,** one who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole **a deposit, who** ordinarily **pays double payment,** if he **slaughtered or sold** the animal he received as a deposit **pays fourfold or fivefold payment.**

R. Yochanan reasons as follows: since the genuine *ganav* pays *kefel*, and the *to'ein ta'anat ganav* also pays *kefel*, we may draw an analogy. Just as the *ganav* who subsequently slaughters or sells the animal — *tavach u'machar* — pays *arba'ah v'chamishah* (four times the value for a sheep, five times for an ox), so too the *to'ein ta'anat ganav* who then slaughters or sells the animal would be liable for four and five. The logic is that *kefel* is the marker of full *ganav* status, and once that status is established, all its consequences follow — including the penalty of *arba'ah v'chamishah* when the animal is subsequently disposed of.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְגַנָּב – שֶׁכֵּן מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה, תֹּאמַר בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב – שֶׁאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is notable **about** the *halakha* of **a thief?** It is notable **in that** he **pays double payment** even **without** having taken a false **oath; will you say** that this stringency should apply **with regard to** one who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, **who pays double payment only upon** taking a false **oath?** Therefore, one cannot derive from the obligation of a thief to pay fourfold or fivefold payment to the case of one who falsely states the claim that the deposit was stolen.

The Gemara challenges this reasoning. The *ganav* pays *kefel* independently, purely by virtue of the act of theft, without any oath. The *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, by contrast, incurs *kefel* only by virtue of taking a false *shvuah*. This is not a minor distinction: one could argue that the *shvuah* is not merely a procedural trigger but is constitutively part of what generates the liability. If the *shvuah* is what creates the *kefel* — rather than the act of *gneivah* itself — then perhaps the *to'ein ta'anat ganav*'s *kefel* is a consequence of *shvuat sheker*, not of *ganav* status at all. In that case, the analogy to the full *ganav* — who pays four and five for *tavach u'machar* — would not hold. The two situations, while producing the same monetary outcome, may rest on entirely different foundations.

### Hekesh Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הֶיקֵּישָׁא הִיא, וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין עַל הֶיקֵּישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: Rabbi Yoḥanan did not base his ruling on a logical inference; rather, **it is** a derivation based upon **a juxtaposition,** as the verses discussing a thief and one who falsely states the claim that a deposit was stolen are juxtaposed, **and one cannot refute** through analysis a derivation based upon **juxtaposition.**

The Gemara responds: *lo, hekesha hi*, this is not a mere analogy but a *hekesh* — an explicit textual linkage within the Torah itself — and one does not challenge a *hekesh* on the grounds that the cases are not fully analogous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חַד בְּגַנָּב וְחַד בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַאי ״אִם יִמָּצֵא הַגַּנָּב״, וְ״אִם לֹא יִמָּצֵא״ – תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב; מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **one** verse is stated **with regard to a thief and one** verse is stated **with regard to** one who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit. According to him, there is a juxtaposition, and one can **well** derive one from the other. **But according to the one who says** that **both this** verse: **"If the thief shall be found,** he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:6), **and: "If the thief shall not be found"** (Exodus 22:7), are referring to one who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit, and neither verse is referring to a thief, **what is there to say?**

The nature of this linkage depends on a dispute about how to read the relevant *pesukim*. One opinion holds that the two verses — *im yimatzei haganav yeshalem shnayim* and *im lo yimatzei haganav venikrav ba'al habayit el ha'Elohim... asher yarshiun Elohim yeshalem shnayim l'rei'eihu* — describe two distinct scenarios within the same *parshah*: the first, an actual *ganav*; the second, one who claims the item was stolen and swears falsely. According to this reading, the *to'ein ta'anat ganav* and the genuine *ganav* are literally juxtaposed in the same Torah passage, constituting a *hekesh* that links their legal statuses fully.

However, according to the opinion that *both* of those verses describe cases of *to'ein ta'anat ganav*, while the actual *ganav*'s obligation of *kefel* derives from an entirely separate *pasuk* — *chayim shnayim yeshalem* — the two categories are not in the same *parshah* at all, and the *hekesh* argument would seem unavailable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: ״גַּנָּב–הַגַּנָּב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling is derived from an amplification indicated by the verse not employing the term **"thief [*gannav*],"** and instead employing the expanded term **"the thief [*hagannav*]."** This teaches that the *halakha* of the fourfold and fivefold payment applies to one who falsely states the claim that the deposit was stolen, as well.

The Gemara resolves this by invoking the word *haganav* — the definite article attached to the word *ganav* in the phrase *im lo yimatzei haganav*. Rather than functioning as a limiting term (excluding all but the classic case), the Gemara reads it as an identification: *you*, the one who claimed the item was stolen, *are* the *ganav*, you are *haganav*. This is less a *hekesh* in the technical sense and more a *ribui* — a derivation from an emphatic or extra-textual element — which equates the *to'ein ta'anat ganav* with the full *ganav* and assigns him complete *ganav* status, including liability for *arba'ah v'chamishah* upon *tavach u'machar*.

## Baraita Objection

### Michael Eating Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״הֵיכָן שׁוֹרִי?״ ״נִגְנַב״. ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״, וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. וְהָא הָכָא, דְּאִי אֶפְשָׁר לִכְזַיִת בָּשָׂר בְּלֹא שְׁחִיטָה, וְקָתָנֵי: מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל; תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל – אִין, תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba raised an objection to** the statement of **Rabbi Yoḥanan** from a *baraita*: In a scenario where one **said to an unpaid bailee: Where is my ox?** Then the bailee responded: It **was stolen.** And the owner said: **I administer an oath to you, and** the bailee **said: Amen. And the witnesses testify about** the bailee **that he ate it,** he **pays double payment. But here,** the *halakha* is **that it is impossible to** eat even **an olive-bulk of meat without slaughtering** the animal first, indicating that it must have been slaughtered, **and** nevertheless **it teaches** that he **pays double payment.** It can be inferred that **double payment, yes,** he pays; but **fourfold or fivefold payment, no,** he does not pay.

Having established that the *to'ein ta'anat ganav* bears the full legal status of a *ganav* — including the penalty of *arba'ah v'chamishah* when he subsequently slaughters or sells the animal — the Gemara now tests this against a *baraita* that appears to contradict it.

R. Chiya bar Abba, who transmitted R. Yochanan's teaching, himself raises this challenge. It is striking that the very *amora* who conveyed the *halakha* was not fully satisfied with it. The *baraita* describes the following case: a person was watching an ox, claimed it had been stolen (*nignav*), was asked to take a *shvuah* and confirmed it — thus becoming a *to'ein ta'anat ganav*. Witnesses then come forward and testify that he had actually been eating the ox. Since he must have *shechted* it in order to eat it, R. Yochanan's principle would seem to demand that he pay four and five. Yet the *baraita* rules that he pays only *kefel*. *Tashlumei arba'ah v'chamishah* — no.

The question is sharp: if *to'ein ta'anat ganav* achieves full *ganav* status, and this person clearly slaughtered and consumed the animal, why does he not pay four and five? The analogy to *tavach u'machar* appears to be precisely realized. One might suggest that the witnesses saw him consuming the meat but not the act of *shechitah* itself — and since *kefel* for *tavach u'machar* is a *knas*, it likely requires direct testimony of the *shechitah* act rather than circumstantial inference. The Gemara, however, does not pursue this line of reasoning.

### Neveilah Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁאֲכָלוֹ נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where he ate it** as an unslaughtered **animal carcass.** Since it was not killed according to the *halakhot* of ritual slaughter, the thief does not pay the fourfold or fivefold payment.

Instead, the Gemara's first proposed answer is: *kegon she'achalo neveilah* — the case is one in which he ate it without performing *shechitah* at all. He simply killed the animal in a non-*kosher* manner — struck it, shot it, or otherwise dispatched it — and ate it as *neveilah*. In that case, no act of *shechitah* ever occurred, and therefore no liability for four and five arises, since *tavach* requires an actual *shechitah*.

### Treifah & R. Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁאֲכָלוֹ טְרֵיפָה! כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה – שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **answer** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba by saying that it is a case **such as where he ate it** as **an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** in accordance with Rabbi Shimon's opinion that the legal status of an act of slaughter that is not fit for accomplishing its full ritual purpose is not considered an act of slaughter for which the thief must pay the fourfold or fivefold payment. The Gemara responds that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that the legal status of an act of **slaughter that is not fit** for accomplishing its full ritual purpose **is considered** an act of **slaughter,** and the thief would pay the fourfold or fivefold payment.

The Gemara asks: why go to the extreme of *neveilah*? Why not instead say that he did perform *shechitah*, but the animal turned out to be *treifah* — afflicted with a disqualifying condition — and the meat was therefore not *kosher*? That too would be a case where he ate the animal but one might question whether full *arba'ah v'chamishah* applies.

The Gemara responds: that answer would only work according to those who hold that *shechitah she'einah re'uyah* — a slaughter whose result is not *permitted* meat — does not constitute a valid act of *shechitah* for these purposes. But R. Meir rules that *shechitah she'einah re'uyah shemah shechitah* — even a *shechitah* that does not render the animal *muttar* is still legally considered an act of *shechitah* and would therefore trigger the penalty of four and five. Since the Gemara wants its answer to be consistent even with R. Meir's position, the *treifah* scenario cannot serve as a universal solution. One must posit *neveilah* — a case with no *shechitah* at all — for the answer to hold across all opinions.

### Ben Pakuah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ בְּבֶן פְּקוּעָה! כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: בֶּן פְּקוּעָה טָעוּן שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **answer** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba by saying that the ruling of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to an animal removed from its mother's womb after the mother was ritually slaughtered [*ben pekua*],** which is permitted for consumption without slaughter, so that there is a possibility that the thief ate it without first slaughtering it. The Gemara responds that Rabbi Yoḥanan holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says** that **a *ben pekua* requires slaughter.**

The Gemara raises yet another possible resolution: *v'lishnei leh b'ven paku'ah*. Perhaps the animal in question was a *ben paku'ah* — an animal born after its mother had been properly *shechted* while pregnant. Such an animal is halakhically considered to have been pre-slaughtered through its mother's *shechitah*, and requires no further *shechitah* of its own. Even if the animal grew to full size and was walking about, one could, in principle, slaughter it in a non-*kosher* manner or eat a limb from it, and the meat would be permitted — since *halakhically* it is already considered as though it were *shechted* through the mother.

If the stolen ox were a *ben paku'ah*, the watchman could have consumed it without performing *shechitah*, and no liability for four and five would arise — yet he would not be eating *neveilah*, since the meat is in fact *kosher*.

The Gemara rejects this answer as well on the basis of R. Meir's opinion: R. Meir holds that even a *ben paku'ah* requires *shechitah* — possibly as a rabbinic requirement due to *marit ayin* or some related concern. Therefore, in order for the answer to hold according to all positions, including R. Meir's, one cannot use the *ben paku'ah* case. The answer must revert to *neveilah*.

### Court Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִישַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָמַד בַּדִּין, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״צֵא תֵּן לוֹ״! דְּהָא אָמַר רָבָא: ״צֵא תֵּן לוֹ״, טָבַח וּמָכַר – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּפַסְקֵיהּ לְמִילְּתֵיהּ, וְטָבַח וּמָכַר – הָוֵי גַּזְלָן, וְגַזְלָן לָא מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **answer** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba by saying that it is a case **where** the bailee **stood in judgment** in court, **and** the judges **said to him: Go out** and **give him** the stolen animal, and he did not do so, and subsequently slaughtered or sold it? In this case he would not pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, **as doesn't Rava say** that if the judges instruct a thief: **Go out** and **give him** the stolen animal, **and he** instead **slaughtered or sold** it, he is **exempt** from paying the fourfold or fivefold payment. **What is the reason** for this? **Once** the court **ruled on his matter,** requiring him to give the animal to the owner, and he did not heed the court's ruling **but slaughtered or sold** the animal, **he is** considered **a robber,** and not a thief; **and a robber does not pay fourfold or fivefold payment.**

The Gemara then raises a third line of resolution — considerably more complex in its reconstruction of the *baraita*'s case: *v'lichzei lei de'amad b'din v'amru lo tzei ten lo*. What if the sequence of events included an intervening court proceeding? Specifically: the watchman swore falsely, was exposed in court, the court ruled against him, and then — after the court said *tzei ten lo*, "go pay him," issuing a formal mandate to hand over the animal — he went ahead and *shechted* it.

The Gemara cites Rabbah's principle: once a *beit din* has issued the instruction *tzei ten lo* to a *ganav*, if he subsequently slaughters or sells the animal, he is *patur* from four and five. At that point, the animal's legal identity has shifted. The court's mandate has re-established the owner's rights to the object; it is no longer the case that the *ganav* is secretly holding onto something that is legally still navigating through the ambiguity of theft. If he now takes or destroys the animal, he is effectively committing a new act of *gezeila* — openly seizing another person's established property — and a *gazlan* does not pay four and five.

### Two Court Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״חַיָּיב אַתָּה לִיתֵּן לוֹ״, וְטָבַח וּמָכַר – חַיָּיב. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל כַּמָּה דְּלָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא, אַכַּתִּי גַּנָּב הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: But if the judges say only: **You are obligated to give** the animal **to him,** and he did not heed the court's statement **but slaughtered or sold** the animal, then he is **obligated** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment. **What is the reason** for this? **Since** the court **has not issued a definitive ruling in** this **matter, he is still** considered **a thief** rather than a robber.

This ruling, however, only applies once *tzei ten lo* has been issued. The Gemara makes an important distinction between two stages of a court's disposition:

The first stage is *chayav ata liten lo* — "you are liable to give to him." At this point, the court has declared the defendant guilty and announced his obligation, but has not yet issued the operational mandate to pay. The second and subsequent stage is *tzei ten lo* — "go give him" — which is the court's active directive to act on the judgment, analogous to the issuance of a writ of execution.

Conceptually, this distinction might seem subtle to the point of obscurity: in both cases the court has spoken, in both cases the defendant knows he has been found guilty. Yet the Gemara treats them as halakhically distinct. After *chayav ata liten lo*, the defendant has been declared guilty, but the legal relationship between the *ganav* and the stolen object has not yet been formally severed. He remains, in a sense, still defined as a *ganav* with respect to that object — the chain of *gneivah* has not been conclusively broken. Therefore, if he *shechts* the animal at this intermediate stage, he remains *chayav* for four and five.

Only once the court issues *tzei ten lo* — the order actively commanding return of the property and thereby formally reconstituting the owner's claim — is the *ganav*'s identity with respect to that act of *gneivah* interrupted. Any subsequent disposition of the animal is then a new act of *gezeila*, not a continuation of the original *gneivah*, and *arba'ah v'chamishah* no longer applies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, לִישַׁנֵּי לֵיהּ בְּשׁוּתָּף שֶׁטָּבַח שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת חֲבֵירוֹ! אֶלָּא חֲדָא מִתְּרֵי וּתְלָתָא נָקֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **And according to your reasoning, let** Rabbi Yoḥanan **answer** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba by saying that the ruling of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to a partner who slaughtered** an animal stolen by him and his partner **without the other** partner's **knowledge,** as the *halakha* is that in such a case, the thief, i.e., the partner who slaughtered the animal, does not pay fourfold or fivefold payment. **Rather,** Rabbi Yoḥanan **selected one of two or three** possible answers but did not mean to say that it was the only possible answer.

The Gemara also acknowledges at this point that the *baraita*'s case could in principle have been explained differently — for instance, by positing that the *ganav* was a *shutaf*, a partner in the theft, and *shechted* the animal *shelo l'daat chaveiro*, without the knowledge of his fellow partner. The rule is that four and five applies only to the full animal, not to fractional shares; if one partner acts unilaterally, he cannot be liable for four times or five times the full value, nor is he liable for a partial share of four and five. However, the Gemara's response is simply *chada m'tartei nakat* — the *baraita* chose one of several available answers, and *neveilah* is equally valid.

## Aveidah Case

### Aveidah Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בַּאֲבֵידָה, מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל כׇּל אֲבֵדָה אֲשֶׁר יֹאמַר״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a related ruling: **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **one who** falsely **states the claim, with regard to a lost item,** that **a thief** stole it, i.e., he found a lost item, and when the owner requested its return, he claimed that it had been stolen from him, and it became known that he lied and had taken the item himself, **he pays double payment. What is the reason** for this? **As it is written** in the verse concerning double payment for a thief: "For any matter of trespass, from an ox, for a donkey, for a sheep, for a garment, or **for any lost item about which one shall say:** This is it, the claims of both of them shall come before the judges; the one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:8). It can be seen from here that double payment applies to a false claim of theft even with regard to a lost item.

Having established the *halakhot* of *to'ein ta'anat ganav b'pikadon*, the Gemara introduces a parallel teaching: *to'ein ta'anat ganav b'aveidah*. Here too R. Chiya bar Abba cites R. Yochanan: one who is watching a lost object (*shomer aveidah*), falsely claims it was stolen, and takes a *shvuah* to that effect — pays *kefel*.

The case is structurally distinct from *pikadon*: the object was not entrusted to the watchman by its owner. Rather, he found it on the street — perhaps something bearing the owner's name — and rather than returning it, he decided to keep it and later denied having it, claiming it was stolen. The Torah's *parshah* about *to'ein ta'anat ganav* begins with *ki yitein ish el re'eihu* — "when a man gives to his fellow" — which describes a *pikadon*. But it then continues with the phrase *al kol aveidah asher yomar ki hu zeh* — "concerning any lost object about which he says, this is it." This expansion in the text is taken as a direct indication that the *halakha* of *kefel* applies not only to the watchman of a *pikadon* but also to one watching a lost object.

### Minor Owner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: ״כִּי יִתֵּן אִישׁ״ – אֵין נְתִינַת קָטָן כְּלוּם. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא קָטָן, וּתְבָעוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא קָטָן; נְתָנוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא קָטָן וּתְבָעוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא גָּדוֹל – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַד הָאֱלֹהִים יָבֹא דְּבַר שְׁנֵיהֶם״ – עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא נְתִינָה וּתְבִיעָה שָׁוִין כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel raised an objection to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** from a *baraita*: The verse states: **"If a man gives** his neighbor money or vessels to safeguard, and it was stolen from the house of the man, if the thief shall be found, he shall pay double" (Exodus 22:6). The use of the word "man" indicates that the **giving** of a deposit **by a minor is nothing. And I have** derived **only** a case **where he gives** the deposit **when he is a minor and lodges the claim** concerning it **against** the bailee **when he is** still a **minor. From where** is it derived that the *halakha* would be the same in a case where **he gives** the deposit **when he is a minor and lodges the claim** concerning it **against** the bailee **when he is an adult? The verse states: "The claims of both of them shall come before the judges"** (Exodus 22:8), i.e., the claim is valid **only when** the **giving** of the deposit **and** the **claim are equal,** both done when he is an adult.

The Gemara immediately raises a challenge from a *baraita*. The *baraita* derives from the phrase *ki yitein ish* — "when a man gives" — that the *halakha* applies only when it is an *ish*, an adult, who entrusted the object. *Ein netinas katan kelum* — the giving of a minor carries no legal weight. Therefore, if a minor gave an object to a watchman who subsequently swore falsely, there is no *chiyuv kefel*.

The *baraita* then extends this: not only is there no *kefel* when the owner was a minor at the time of the *nesinah* (the original entrustment) and at the time of the *tevi'ah* (the demand for return), but even if the owner was a minor at the time of the *nesinah* and became an adult by the time of the *tevi'ah*, the watchman is still exempt. The *baraita* derives this from the phrase *ad ha'Elohim yavo devar sheneichem* — "the matter of both of them shall come before God" — which requires, through the word *sheneichem* ("both of them"), that both parties be in equivalent adult status. The *nesinah* and *tevi'ah* must both occur when the owner is a *ben daat* — a legally capable adult.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, תֶּיהְוֵי נָמֵי כַּאֲבֵידָה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁאֲכָלוֹ כְּשֶׁהוּא קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the ruling of this *baraita*, Rabbi Abba bar Memel raises his objection: **And if it is so** that even one who finds a lost item pays double payment for claiming falsely that it was stolen, indicating that the giving of the item as a deposit by the owner is not an essential condition to render the thief liable for double payment, then the claim of a minor **should be like a lost item as well,** and one who received a deposit from a minor should be liable for double payment. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the bailee **consumed** the deposit **when** the owner **was** still **a minor,** so that at the time it was consumed, the owner had no legal standing. Therefore, the bailee is not obligated to pay.

The challenge to R. Yochanan's *aveidah* ruling is then posed: if *to'ein ta'anat ganav b'aveidah* is governed by the same *kefel* liability as *pikadon*, the cases should be parallel. But in the case of a *katan* who grew up, you might think the situation is analogous to *aveidah*: in both cases, there was no legally binding *nesinah* by a *ben daat* at the outset, yet the watchman came to hold the object of an adult and had obligations with respect to it. Why, then, should *to'ein ta'anat ganav b'aveidah* be different from the case of the *katan* who later became an adult?

### Rav Ashi Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל כְּשֶׁהוּא גָּדוֹל מַאי, הָכִי נָמֵי דִּמְשַׁלֵּם? אִי הָכִי, אַדִּתְנָא: עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא נְתִינָה וּתְבִיעָה שָׁוִין כְּאֶחָד, לִיתְנֵי: עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא אֲכִילָה וּתְבִיעָה שָׁוִין כְּאֶחָד! אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנִי: עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא אֲכִילָה וּתְבִיעָה שָׁוִין כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abba bar Memel questions this answer: **But** had the bailee consumed it **when** the owner was already **an adult, what** is the *halakha*; **indeed,** would the *halakha* be **that** the bailee **pays? If so, rather than teaching:** The claim is valid **only when** the **giving** of the deposit **and** the **claim are equal, let** the *baraita* **teach:** The claim is valid **only when** the **consumption** of the deposit **and** the **claim are equal,** which would be a more precise ruling. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him:** Emend the *baraita* and **teach:** The claim is valid **only when** the **consumption** of the deposit **and** the **claim are equal.**

The Gemara's first answer — that the *baraita* should be amended to read that exemption applies only when the *watchman consumed or took the object while the owner was still a minor* — is acknowledged to be a weak resolution, and is quickly revised.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לָא דָּמֵי; אֲבֵידָה – קָא אָתְיָא מִכֹּחַ בֶּן דַּעַת, וְהָא – לָא אָתְיָא מִכֹּחַ בֶּן דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** a different answer to Rabbi Abba bar Memel's objection: The two cases **are not comparable,** so an objection to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba's statement concerning a lost item cannot be derived from the case of a minor giving a deposit, because the **lost item came** into the possession of the finder **by** the **power of one** who is **mentally competent,** as the one who lost it is presumed to be an adult, though he did not personally give it to the finder; **but this** deposit given by a minor **did not come** into his possession **by** the **power of one** who is **mentally competent,** as from a halakhic perspective, a minor is not considered mentally competent. Therefore, he has no grounds for a claim.

Rav Ashi provides the definitive answer: *lo dami* — the two cases are not comparable. *Aveidah ka'ati mikoach ben daat* — when I found a lost object, my initial relationship with that object — and the obligations of *shmirah* that came with it — arose by virtue of my encounter with the property of an adult. Even though there was no act of entrusting, the legal obligations of *shmirah* began in the context of an adult owner's property. That original relationship is therefore one with full legal weight.

The case of the *katan* is different: *hahu lo ati mikoach ben daat* — the initial relationship between the watchman and the object did not originate through a legally binding transaction with a *ben daat*. When the minor handed it over, no *chiyuv shmirah* arose. Even though the child later matured, and even though the watchman now holds the property of an adult, the foundational relationship was never properly constituted. The obligations of *shmirah* were never validly initiated, and therefore the false *shvuah* taken in that context does not give rise to *chiyuv kefel*.

The question is briefly noted: if one finds the *aveidah* of a *katan*, does one have the same *chiyuvei shmirah* as when one finds the *aveidah* of an adult? The Torah's formulation of *shomer aveidah* may not recognize legal obligations toward those who are not *bnei daat*. While one is surely obligated to return the object — it is not one's own — whether the full transactional framework of *shmirah* with its attendant liabilities applies to property of a *katan* is left as an open question.

In any case, Rav Ashi's answer establishes that R. Yochanan's ruling — *to'ein ta'anat ganav b'aveidah meshalaem tashlumei kefel* — stands, provided the *aveidah* originated as the property of a *ben daat*. The case of the *katan* does not undermine it, because the analogy fails at the foundational level.

## Modeh B'Miktzat

### R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן, אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּכְפּוֹר בְּמִקְצָת וְיוֹדֶה בְּמִקְצָת. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי הוּא זֶה״. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר יוֹסֵף:
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara quotes a related ruling: **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **one who** falsely **states the claim, with regard to a deposit,** that **a thief** stole it, **he is not obligated** to take an oath, nor to pay double payment, **until he denies part of** the claim **and admits to part of** the claim. **What is the reason** for this? **As the verse states:** "About which one shall say: **This is it"** (Exodus 22:8), indicating an admittance of only this part, but no more. The Gemara notes: **And** this **disagrees** with the ruling **of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef, as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Yosef says:**

The Gemara now turns to a further teaching of R. Chiya bar Abba in the name of R. Yochanan, one that is both surprising and far-reaching in its implications for the entire institution of *shvuat hashomrim*. R. Yochanan teaches: *hatoein ta'anat ganav b'pikadon eino chayav ad sheyichpor b'miktzat v'yodeh b'miktzat* — the *to'ein ta'anat ganav b'pikadon* is not liable unless he has denied part and admitted part.

This is a striking claim. We assume as a basic premise that any *shomer* who is challenged about an object in his care and offers an account of what happened — whether he says it was stolen, lost to circumstances beyond his control, or died — is required to take a *shvuat hashomrim*. How do we know this? How can I compel Michael to swear? By presenting the challenge and demanding the oath. That is the standard framework.

R. Yochanan now says: this is insufficient. The *shvuat hashomrim* is only triggered when there is simultaneously a *modeh b'miktzat* structure underlying the case. That is: Michael was watching three objects for me. He concedes that one he still has — that is his *hodaah*, his admission. He denies that a second one was ever given to him — that is his *kefirah*, his denial. And as for the third, he says it was stolen — that is his claim about what happened. Only in this triangulated structure — partial admission, partial denial, and the intermediate claim — does R. Yochanan hold that a *shvuat hashomrim* is imposed.

The textual basis for this is the phrase *ki hu zeh* in the relevant *parshah*. This expression, which is the classic scriptural foundation for the requirement of *modeh b'miktzat* in *shvuat milveh*, appears directly within the *parshah* of *pikadon* and *toein ta'anat ganav*. R. Yochanan reads this as a signal that the same *modeh b'miktzat* structure that conditions *shvuat milveh* is also a prerequisite for the *shvuat hashomrim* of the *pikadon* context.

### Parshiyot Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵירוּב פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת כָּתוּב כָּאן, וְכִי כְּתִיב: ״כִּי הוּא זֶה״ – אַמִּלְוָה הוּא דִּכְתִיב. וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִלְוָה?
>
> **Translation:** **A merging of** Torah **portions is written here,** and the *halakha* written in this passage is in fact meant to be applied to a different passage. **And when it is written: "This is it,"** from which the *halakha* of a partial admission is derived, **it is written concerning a loan,** not a deposit. The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about **a loan** that this *halakha* would apply only there?

R. Chiya bar Yosef disputes this reading. He holds that *eiruv parshiyot kasuv kan* — what appears to be a single unified passage is in fact a conflation of two separate *parshiyot*. The *ki hu zeh* belongs to the *parshah* of *milveh*, where *modeh b'miktzat* is genuinely operative. The *parshah* of *pikadon* and *shvuat hashomrim* is an entirely separate unit, and the two should not be read as conditioning each other. On his view, the *shvuat hashomrim* is an independent institution that does not require any *modeh b'miktzat* framing.

### Two Pikadon Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַבָּה – דְּאָמַר רַבָּה, מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: מוֹדֶה בְּמִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה יִשָּׁבַע? חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם מֵעִיז פָּנָיו בִּפְנֵי בַּעַל חוֹבוֹ; וְהַאי – בְּכוּלֵּי[הּ] בָּעֵי דְּנִכְפְּרֵיהּ, וְהַאי דְּלָא כַּפְרֵיהּ – מִשּׁוּם דְּאֵין אָדָם מֵעִיז פָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabba, as Rabba says: For what** reason **did the Torah say** that one who **admits to a part of the claim must take an oath?** It is because there is **a presumption** that **a person does not exhibit insolence** by lying **in the presence of his creditor,** who had done him a favor by lending money to him. **And this** person who denies part of the claim actually **wants to deny all of** the debt, so as to be exempt, **and this** fact **that he does not deny** all of **it** is **because a person does not exhibit insolence.**

The Gemara draws a consequential distinction between two types of *pikadon* cases, one of which maps onto *milveh* and one of which does not.

The first case: I gave Michael an iPhone to watch, and I ask him what happened to it. He says: "What iPhone? I have no idea what you're talking about." This is a total denial of the *pikadon* itself — he is not acknowledging that he ever received the object. This case is structurally identical to *kofer hakol* in *milveh*: I say I lent you money, you say you never received anything. In both cases, the defendant denies the foundational transaction. According to R. Yochanan's approach, and as the Gemara here develops, total denial in this case does not trigger a *shvuah* unless there is a simultaneous *modeh b'miktzat* — i.e., unless he admits to having received some items and denies others.

The second case: I gave Michael an iPhone to watch, and he says: "Yes, I was watching it, but unfortunately it was stolen." Here the structure is fundamentally different. Michael is acknowledging that he received the object and that he bore obligations as a *shomer*. The content of the dispute is not whether he received it, but what happened to it. In this case, the *shvuat hashomrim* is triggered without any additional *modeh b'miktzat* structure, because the basis for the *shvuah* is not the denial of a transaction but the claim about events.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכוּלֵּי[הּ] בָּעֵי דְּלוֹדֵי לֵיהּ, וְהַאי דִּכְפַר לֵיהּ בְּמִקְצָת – סָבַר: אִי מוֹדֵינָא לֵיהּ בְּכוּלֵּיהּ – תָּבַע לִי בְּכוּלֵּיהּ; אֶישְׁתְּמֵיט לֵי[הּ] מִיהָא הַשְׁתָּא אַדְּהָווּ לִי זוּזֵי, וּפָרַעְנָא. הִלְכָּךְ רְמָא רַחֲמָנָא שְׁבוּעָה עִילָּוֵיהּ – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלוֹדֵי לֵיהּ בְּכוּלֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba continues: **And** in order not to exhibit insolence, he **wants to admit to** the creditor **with regard to all of** the debt, **and this** fact **that he denies** owing **him in part** is because he **reasons: If I admit to him with regard to all of** the debt, **he will lodge a claim against me with regard to all of it,** and right now I do not have the money to pay. **I will evade him at least** for **now until I have money, and** then **I will pay** him all of it. This rationalization enables one to falsely deny part of a claim. **Therefore, the Merciful One imposes an oath on him, in order** to ensure **that he will admit to him with regard to all of** the debt.

The psychological and moral foundation of *shvuat modeh b'miktzat* is explored here. The one who borrowed money from you, and who knows perfectly well that you lent it to him, cannot simply look you in the eye and say: "I have no idea what you're talking about." The psychological and social cost of such brazen denial is too high. There is a *chazakah* — a presumption rooted in observed human behavior — that a person will not ordinarily be that shameless.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגַבֵּי מִלְוָה הוּא דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר הָכִי, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי פִּקָּדוֹן – מֵעִיז וּמֵעִיז.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara completes its answer: **And it is with regard to a loan that this can be said,** as the basis for this explanation is that one will not exhibit insolence before his creditor, who did him a favor by lending him money; **but with regard to a deposit, one will certainly exhibit insolence,** as the claimant did him no favor. Therefore, there is no reason to say that one who completely denies a claim concerning a deposit is deemed credible any more than one who admitted to part of it, and he is obligated to take an oath in either case.

*Rashi* disagrees with this analysis. He holds that by a *pikadon*, even a total denial — Michael says "What iPhone?" — triggers a *shvuah* immediately, without any need for *modeh b'miktzat*. The rationale Rashi offers is that in a *milveh* the borrower received a benefit, and therefore the dynamic of *ein adam meiz panav* (discussed below) applies. In a *pikadon*, the watchman did the owner a favor by accepting the object, so the psychological calculus is reversed — the watchman may be more willing to deny brazenly. Therefore, by a *pikadon*, one can always compel a *shvuah*.

*Tosafot* rejects this and offers the more analytically compelling position: the distinction is not between *milveh* and *pikadon* as legal categories, but between total denial of a transaction (in both *milveh* and *pikadon*) and the watchman's claim about what happened. A total denial — whether of a loan or of a *pikadon* — does not trigger a *shvuah* in the absence of a *modeh b'miktzat* structure. But the *pikadon* case where the watchman acknowledges his role as *shomer* and disputes only the fate of the object is unique: there, a *shvuah* is required even without *modeh b'miktzat*, because the watchman concedes the fundamental relationship and the other party cannot know whether the claim of theft is true.

## Psychology of Oath

### Ein Adam Meiz

This *chazakah*, however, raises an immediate question: if we presume the debtor cannot deny completely, why does he in fact deny half? And conversely, if he is admitting half, why not simply invoke *migo* — since he could have denied everything and been believed, he should certainly be believed when he admits to something?

The Gemara resolves both questions through a single psychological insight. The debtor genuinely would like to deny everything — *b'chulei bei d'nikfreih* — but he cannot bring himself to do it, because *ein adam meiz panav*. At the same time, some part of him truly would like to admit to the whole thing — *b'chulei bei d'lodei leih*. He is caught between these two impulses. His partial admission is not a free choice that could ground a *migo*; it is a compromise forced upon him by competing moral pressures.

Why then does he deny the other half? *Savar: im modina leih b'chulei, tava li b'chulei* — he calculates that if he admits to the whole amount, the lender will demand immediate full payment. He does not have the money at this moment — *ishtamit li mineih hashta* — and so he denies half in order to buy time: *ad de-havu li zuzei u-farana*, until he can gather the funds and repay. He is not fundamentally dishonest; he is stalling. This is why the *shvuah* can be effective: *remi rachmana shvuah ileiveih ki heichi de-lodei leih b'chulei*, the Torah imposes the *shvuah* upon him precisely to break this rationalization and bring him to admit the whole obligation.

### Watchman Claim

The Gemara now applies this framework to *pikadon*. The reasoning holds straightforwardly in the *milveh* context — the dynamic of *ein adam meiz panav*, the partial admission, the stalling calculation, all apply. But does it apply in the *pikadon* context when Michael simply says "What iPhone?"

According to Rashi, the answer is no: the *pikadon* case of total denial is different from *milveh* because the watchman may actually have greater ease in denying brazenly. He did the owner a favor by agreeing to watch the object; the moral asymmetry runs in the opposite direction. Therefore, Rashi holds that *meiz u'meiz* — in the *pikadon* context, a person is capable of denying completely — and therefore *kofer hakol* by *pikadon* can trigger a *shvuah* even without *modeh b'miktzat*.

*Tosafot*, as noted, disagrees. On *Tosafot*'s view, the *ein adam meiz panav* principle applies equally to *milveh* and *pikadon* when the dispute is about a transaction: if Michael received something from me, he cannot look me in the eye and deny it completely any more than a borrower can. The *milveh*-style analysis therefore applies to both. It is only the second type of *pikadon* case — where Michael admits he was a *shomer* and claims the object was stolen — that stands apart, and there the *shvuah* is triggered not by *modeh b'miktzat* reasoning at all, but by the independent institution of *shvuat hashomrim*, grounded in the watchman's acknowledged role and the owner's inability to know whether the claim is true.

## Four Watchmen

### Rami Bar Chama

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: אַרְבָּעָה שׁוֹמְרִין צְרִיכִין כְּפִירָה בְּמִקְצָת וְהוֹדָאָה בְּמִקְצָת, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר וְהַשּׂוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rami bar Ḥama teaches** this *baraita*: All **four** types of **bailees require denial of a part of** the claim **and admittance of a part of** the claim in order to be obligated to take an oath when someone claims to have given them an item as a deposit, **and these are they: An unpaid bailee, and a borrower, a paid bailee, and a renter.**

The Gemara closes the *daf* with a *baraita* attributed to Rami bar Chama that extends the requirement — following the opinion that *modeh b'miktzat* is a prerequisite for *shvuat hashomrim* — to all four categories of *shomrim*. The teaching is: *arba shomrim tzrichin kfirah b'miktzat v'hoda'ah b'miktzat* — all four watchmen require the structure of partial denial and partial admission in order for the *shvuat hashomrim* to be imposed. These four are: the *shomer chinam* (unpaid watchman), the *shoel* (borrower), the *shomer sachar* (paid watchman), and the *socher* (renter).

### Ki Yiten Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא? שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם – בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: ״כִּי הוּא זֶה״. שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר – יָלֵיף ״נְתִינָה״–״נְתִינָה״ מִשּׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes the source for this statement. **Rava said: What is the reasoning** for the statement **of Rami bar Ḥama?** Concerning **an unpaid bailee, it is explicitly written with regard to him: "This is it,"** as explained earlier. Concerning **a paid bailee,** Rami bar Ḥama **learns** by means of a verbal analogy to the term **giving** used with regard to a paid bailee **from** the term **giving** used with regard to **an unpaid bailee,** since the verses about both a paid and an unpaid bailee begin: "If a man gives his neighbor" (Exodus 22:6, 9).

The Gemara asks for the textual basis of each category. The *shomer chinam* is the straightforward case: the phrase *ki hu zeh* is written explicitly *b'hedya* — openly and directly — within the *parshah* of the *shomer chinam*, and it is from there that the *modeh b'miktzat* requirement is derived. The *shomer sachar* is linked to the *shomer chinam* through a *gezeirah shavah* (or *hekesh*) of *netinah-netinah*: the *parshah* of the *shomer chinam* uses the phrase *ki yitein ish el re'eihu kesef o keilim lishmor*, and the *parshah* of the *shomer sachar* uses the phrase *ki yitein ish el re'eihu shor chamor seh lishmor*. Both use *ki yitein*, linking them and extending the *modeh b'miktzat* requirement from one to the other.

### Shoel & Socher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹאֵל – ״וְכִי יִשְׁאַל״, וָיו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן. שׂוֹכֵר – אִי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּשׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר, הַיְינוּ שׁוֹמֵר שָׂכָר; אִי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּשׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם, הַיְינוּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: Rami bar Ḥama learns the *halakha* concerning **a borrower** from the verse: **"And if [*vekhi*] a man borrow"** (Exodus 22:13). There is a principle that the conjunction "and," represented by the letter ***vav*, adds to the previous topic.** Based on this principle, the *halakhot* of a borrower are connected to those of the subject of the previous verse, the bailee. Concerning **a renter, if** Rami bar Ḥama is stating his ruling **according to the one who says** that a renter is **like a paid bailee,** this **is** the same as **a paid bailee; if** he is stating his ruling **according to the one who says** that a renter is **like an unpaid bailee,** this **is** the same as **an unpaid bailee.**

The *shoel* is derived from the *shomrim* through the *vav* that introduces the passage of borrowing — *v'chi yishal* — which is read as *vav mosif al inyan rishon*, a *vav* of addition that attaches the *shoel* to the preceding subject matter of *shmirah*. The same *halakhic* framework thus extends to the borrower.

The *socher* — the renter — does not require independent derivation, because its liability is subsumed under one of the existing categories. Those who hold that the *socher*'s liability is equivalent to that of a *shomer sachar* have already derived his law from the *shomer sachar*; those who hold his liability is equivalent to a *shomer chinam* have already derived it from there. In either case, the *modeh b'miktzat* requirement follows along.

Thus the *daf* concludes with the principle that the entire framework of *shvuat hashomrim* — across all four categories of watchmen — is conditioned on the existence of a *modeh b'miktzat* structure: some admission, some denial, and a claim about what occurred. This reading, as surprising as it may seem against the backdrop of the standard assumption that any watchman must swear in support of his account, emerges from the Torah's own placement of *ki hu zeh* within the *parshah* of *pikadon* — a textual signal that the Gemara takes with full seriousness.
