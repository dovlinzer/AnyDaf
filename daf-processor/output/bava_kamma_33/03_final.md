# Bava Kamma 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Reshut & Negligence

### Permission ≠ Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְבַקֵּעַ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – וְהִזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד; בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – וְהִזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים; בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד – וְהִזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד אַחֵר, חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who was chopping** wood **in the public domain and** a chip flew off and **caused damage in** the **private property** of another person, or one who was chopping wood **in** his **private property and caused damage in the public domain,** or one who was chopping wood **in** his **private property** and **caused damage in** the **private property** of **another,** in all these cases he is **liable.**

The earlier *mishnayot* in this chapter discussed cases of people colliding in the *reshut harabbim* and ruled that the one who caused the damage was *patur*, because both parties were operating with *reshut* — with permission to be doing what they were doing. This created an impression that an *adam hamazik* who acts with *reshut* enjoys a broad exemption from liability. But the *mishna* we have just studied refutes that impression decisively: a person chopping wood in his own private domain, where a splinter flies off and injures someone on adjacent private property, is *chaiyav*. He was entirely permitted to chop wood in his own yard; the splinter flew not even into the *reshut harabbim* but into someone else's private domain — and yet he is liable.

The *reshut harabbim* exemption, then, is not a blanket rule that permission equals exemption. One might have supposed that the exemption there rested on something like contributory negligence — a principle that when everyone is using the public thoroughfare, everyone bears responsibility for watching out for themselves, so that being injured by another's act in the *reshut harabbim* is partly *pi ha-nizak*. The *Gemara*, however, emphasizes a different logic: in those earlier cases, both parties were in motion simultaneously, and when that is so, one cannot isolate a single *mazik* and a single *nizak*. If both people are moving, the question of whose beam struck whose pitcher — or whose fist met whose face — becomes indeterminate. That is the specific logic of the *reshut harabbim* exemption, and it does not extend to the case of the carpenter whose splinter injures a bystander, nor to cases analogous to *bor*, where even disposing of garbage or water in the *reshut harabbim* with permission leaves one liable.

### Carpenter's Splinter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לַחֲנוּתוֹ שֶׁל נַגָּר שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וְנִתְּזָה בְּקַעַת וְטָפְחָה עַל פָּנָיו, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. וְאִם נִכְנַס בִּרְשׁוּת – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who entered the workshop of a carpenter without** the latter's **permission, and a chip of wood flew off and hit him in the face and he died,** the carpenter is **exempt. But if he entered** the shop **with permission,** the carpenter is **liable.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חַיָּיב? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים. וּפָטוּר מִגָּלוּת,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does the *baraita* mean when it rules that the carpenter is **liable? Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** If the other person was injured, he is **liable to** pay **four types of indemnity** that one who injures another must pay. These are: Cost of the damage, pain, medical costs, and loss of livelihood. **But** if the one who entered was killed, he is **exempt from exile.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנִי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא: הַנִּכְנָס לַחֲנוּתוֹ שֶׁל נַגָּר שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְשׁוּת, וְנִתְּזָה לוֹ בְּקַעַת וְטָפְחָה לוֹ עַל פָּנָיו, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים, וּפָטוּר מִגָּלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa taught in the name of Rava** that **this** aforementioned statement of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina is in reference not to the latter clause of the *baraita* but is **in reference to the first clause:** With regard to **one who enters the workshop of a carpenter without permission, and a chip** of wood **flies off and strikes him in the face and he dies,** the carpenter is **exempt.** In reference to this clause **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** If the one who entered was merely injured, the carpenter is **liable to** pay **four types of indemnity. But** if the one who entered was killed, he is **exempt from exile.**

This combination of rulings generates an immediate tension. *Adam muad l'olam* — a person is always considered forewarned with respect to *nezek*. But the additional payments of *arba'ah devarim* — the payments for lost work, pain, and medical care — require *peshiah*, actual negligence. Payment for *boshet* requires intent to damage. So if the ruling states *chaiyav be-arba'ah devarim*, that concedes negligence was present. And if there was negligence — if the carpenter was aware of the person in the workshop — why is he *patur* from *galus*? The earlier discussion had suggested that exemption from *galus* could result from the carpenter being *shogeg karov l'meizid*, negligent to the point of near-intentionality. But that reading is now being challenged.

### Rashi's Formal Exclusion

Rashi resolves the difficulty not through the lens of degrees of negligence — neither too much nor too little — but through a formal distinction rooted in the structure of the *Torah*'s cases. The paradigm case for *galus* in the *Torah* is two people chopping wood together in a forest (*dumya d'yaar*): both have permission to be there, and the killing is genuinely accidental. Rashi argues that being obligated in *arba'ah devarim* does not preclude exemption from *galus* — the exemption from *galus* in this case is not because negligence is absent, but because the situation is formally dissimilar from the *yaar* case. If the victim entered without permission, that formal disanalogy is sufficient to remove the carpenter from the domain of *galus* liability, regardless of whether he was in fact aware of the victim's presence.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁאֵין דּוֹמֶה לְיַעַר. יַעַר – זֶה לִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִכְנָס, וְזֶה לִרְשׁוּתוֹ נִכְנָס; זֶה – לִרְשׁוּת חֲבֵירוֹ נִכְנַס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that he is exempt **because** this case **is not similar to** the case of **a forest,** which is the archetypal case stated in the Torah requiring one who kills unintentionally to be exiled, as it is written: "As when a man goes into the forest with his neighbor to chop wood, and his hand fetches a stroke with the axe to cut down the tree, and the head slips off the helve, and finds his neighbor, and he dies" (Deuteronomy 19:5). This is because in the case of the **forest,** both **this one entered his domain and that one entered his domain,** as anyone may use the public domain, whereas in **this** case, the victim **entered another's property.** Therefore, the carpenter is not exiled.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַסֵּיפָא – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַרֵישָׁא; וּמַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַרֵישָׁא – אֲבָל אַסֵּיפָא, כֵּיוָן דְּבִרְשׁוּת – חַיָּיב גָּלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: With regard to **the one who teaches this** statement **in reference to the last clause** of the *baraita*, where one enters with permission, **all the more so** would he teach it **in reference to the first clause,** where one enters without permission. **But the one who teaches this** statement **in reference to the first clause** teaches it only in reference to that clause. **But in the** case of the **last clause, since** he entered **with permission** the carpenter is **liable** to go into **exile.**

The *Gemara* then presents the structural contrast: according to the one who teaches the ruling about the *reisha* (the case where the victim entered without permission), exemption from *galus* follows naturally in that case, and when one turns to the *seifa* — the case where the victim entered *bireshut*, with permission — one would say the carpenter is *chaiyav galus*, since the formal condition of the *yaar* parallel is now met. According to the one who teaches the *seifa* case, the exemption from *galus* even with permission follows, and the *reisha* case of no permission is all the more clearly *patur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי חַיָּיב גָּלוּת? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַנִּכְנָס לַחֲנוּתוֹ שֶׁל נַפָּח, וְנִתְּזוּ נִיצוֹצוֹת וְטָפְחוּ לוֹ עַל פָּנָיו וָמֵת – פָּטוּר, וַאֲפִילּוּ נִכְנַס בִּרְשׁוּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is he liable** to go into **exile** when the victim had permission to enter? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that with regard to **one who enters the workshop of a welder, and sparks [*nitzotzot*] fly off and strike him in his face and he dies,** the welder is **exempt, and** this is the *halakha* **even if** the victim **entered with permission?**

The *Gemara* then introduces a *braita* about a metalworker's shop: someone enters the ironworker's premises and is killed by a flying spark — and the *braita* rules *patur* even though he entered *bireshut*, with permission. This seems to flatly contradict the reading that permission triggers *galus* liability.

### The Apprentice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּשְׁוַלְיָא דְנַפָּחֵי. שְׁוַלְיָא דְנַפָּחֵי לְמִקְטְלֵאּ קָאֵי? כְּשֶׁרַבּוֹ מְסַרְהֵב בּוֹ לָצֵאת, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here,** in this *baraita*? We are dealing **with the welder's apprentice** who enters his workshop. The Gemara asks: **Does the welder's apprentice stand to be killed,** i.e., is it permitted to kill him? The Gemara answers: It is a case **where his mentor is urging him to leave, and he does not leave.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִשּׁוּם דְּרַבּוֹ מְסַרְהֵב בּוֹ לָצֵאת – לְמִיקְטְלֵיהּ קָאֵי?! כְּסָבוּר יָצָא. אִי הָכִי, אַחֵר נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And because his mentor is urging him to leave, does he stand to be killed?** The welder should be careful until his apprentice leaves. The Gemara answers: The welder **thought** that **he** had already **left** when the accident happened. The Gemara asks: **If so,** why establish that the *baraita* refers specifically to an apprentice? The welder would be exempt if it were any **other** person **also.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחֵר – לֵית לֵיהּ אֵימְתָא דְרַבֵּיהּ, הַאי – אִית לֵיהּ אֵימְתָא דְרַבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Another** person **does not have awe of his mentor.** Therefore, even if the welder urges another person to leave, he must ascertain that that person actually did so, and otherwise he is liable to be exiled. By contrast, **this** apprentice **has awe of his mentor,** and so the welder may assume that if he instructed him to leave, he certainly did. Therefore, if in reality the apprentice did not leave and is killed by the sparks, the welder is not liable to be exiled, as he is not held accountable.

The *Gemara* resolves this by recharacterizing the case. We are dealing, it says, with a *shulya d'napcha* — an apprentice of the metalworker. The *Gemara* itself immediately objects: is the fact that he is an apprentice sufficient grounds to render his death the owner's *reshut*-exempt? Does mere employment create a license to be killed?

The *Gemara* refines: the case is one where the master has been repeatedly telling the apprentice to leave — *mesarhev bo latzet* — and the apprentice refuses to go. But again the *Gemara* objects: just because the master is urging him to leave, does that expose him to be killed without liability?

The final resolution is: *kasavar yatza* — the master concluded that the apprentice had in fact listened and left. This reframes the case as one of no negligence: the master, having told his apprentice to go and reasonably assuming the apprentice obeyed, was genuinely unaware of his continued presence. The *Gemara* then asks why this same logic cannot apply to any stranger who entered. The answer: *acher leit lei eimata d'rabbeih* — a stranger has no particular deference to the carpenter's authority, so one cannot assume he left simply because he was told to. But an apprentice, who has *eimata d'rabbeih*, fear of his master, may be assumed to have complied. This is a very narrow *ukimta*, limiting the exemption to the specific circumstance of an apprentice who was ordered out and reasonably presumed to have obeyed.

The net result of this section is that the *Gemara* has offered two competing frameworks for understanding exemption from *galus*: one in which excessive negligence (*shogeg karov l'meizid*) disqualifies the perpetrator from the formal category of unintentional killing, and another in which *Rashi*'s reading prevails — no exemption based on degrees of negligence, but rather formal disqualification when the case is not *dumya d'yaar*, such as when the victim entered without permission. The case of the metalworker's apprentice ultimately rests on factual absence of negligence: the master did not know the apprentice was still there.

## Rock & Trajectory

### Flying Axe Head

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי לַהּ אַהָא: ״וּמָצָא״ – פְּרָט לְמַמְצִיא אֶת עַצְמוֹ. מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: מִי שֶׁיָּצְתָה אֶבֶן מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ, וְהוֹצִיא הַלָּה אֶת רֹאשׁוֹ וְקִיבְּלָהּ – פָּטוּר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: פָּטוּר מִגָּלוּת, וְחַיָּיב בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid taught in the name of Rava** that **this** aforementioned statement of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina is in reference not to the above *baraita* but is **in reference to this** *baraita*: It is stated in the verse concerning one who kills unintentionally: "And the head slips off the helve, **and finds** his neighbor, and he dies" (Deuteronomy 19:5); this serves to **exclude** one **who introduces himself** into an area of danger, in which case the one who kills unintentionally is exempt from exile. **From here Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says:** With regard to **one whom a stone departed from his hand, and another** person **stuck out his head and received** a blow from **it** and died, the one who threw the stone is **exempt** from exile. It is in reference to this statement that **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says: He is exempt from exile** for killing him. **But** if the victim was merely injured, he is **liable to** pay **four types of indemnity.**

Razid in the name of Rava taught that the ruling about exemption from *galus* applies to yet another category of case — not the question of whether the victim had permission to be present, but rather the question of causation and trajectory. The *Torah* uses the language that the axe-head "found" (*u-matza*) the victim, which the *Gemara* takes to exclude the case of *mamtzi et atzmo* — the victim who "causes himself to be found," who moves himself into the path of the flying object.

### Moving Into Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַהָא – כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אַקַּמַּיְיתָא; וּמַאן דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַקַּמַּיְיתָא – אֲבָל אַהָא פָּטוּר לִגְמָרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The one who teaches this** statement **in reference to this** *baraita*, **all the more so** he would teach it **in reference to the first** *baraita*, where one entered the workshop of the carpenter. **But the one who teaches it with regard to the first** *baraita* teaches it only in reference to that *baraita*. **But in this** *baraita* he is **entirely exempt** from liability for injury, as one could claim that he is completely blameless.

The *Gemara* illustrates this with a *braita* in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov: one throws a rock into the *reshut harabbim* — or perhaps it slips from his hand — and while the rock is in mid-flight, another person emerges and extends his head into the rock's trajectory. The rock strikes him and he dies. The thrower is *patur* from *galus*.

This is, notably, not an exemption based on lack of negligence. Throwing an object into the *reshut harabbim* is unambiguously negligent — people are present and moving in the public thoroughfare, and one must anticipate that someone may be struck. But the victim, while not himself negligent, has actively participated in the causal sequence that brought the rock to his head. The *Gemara* draws an analogy to the cases of two people moving simultaneously in the *reshut harabbim*: when the victim is an active participant in the event, even if not blameworthy, there is a formal ground for exemption from *galus*, since this too is not *dumya d'yaar*.

Rabbi Yose bar Chanina then states: in this case as well, the thrower is *patur mi-galus* but *chaiyav be-arba'ah devarim*. The thrower was negligent — he threw something into the public thoroughfare — and that negligence is sufficient to generate monetary liability for injury. The exemption from *galus*, again, is formal rather than substantive: the case does not fit the paradigm of the *yaar*, because the victim moved into the path.

The *Gemara* then notes the structural interplay between the two teachings. According to the one who teaches the exemption specifically in this *mamtzi et atzmo* case, one can argue *kol sheken* that the monetary obligation of *arba'ah devarim* certainly applies in the earlier cases, where the carpenter's negligence was more straightforwardly present. But according to the one who teaches the *arba'ah devarim* obligation in the earlier cases, one might say that in the *mamtzi et atzmo* case the thrower is *patur legamre* — fully exempt — because perhaps here, given that the victim walked into the rock's path, one cannot attribute sufficient negligence to the thrower to ground even the *arba'ah devarim*. The *Gemara* is candid that this reasoning is not entirely transparent: it is not obvious why the negligence in the *reshut harabbim* case should be treated as categorically less than the carpenter's negligence. The *Gemara* seems to be working with assumed degrees of negligence that depend on the particular circumstances of each case, and it acknowledges that a general framework based purely on the facts would simply ask what the individual circumstances warrant.

The takeaway of these sections is the distinction between the requirements for *arba'ah devarim* — which require negligence (*peshiah*) — and the requirements for *galus* — which are subject to formal exclusions based on the structure of the *Torah*'s paradigm cases, independent of the degree of negligence actually present.

## Workers & Wages

### Implicit Right to Enter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פּוֹעֲלִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לִתְבּוֹעַ שְׂכָרָן מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וּנְגָחָן שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת וּנְשָׁכָן כַּלְבּוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וָמֵת – פָּטוּר. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: רַשָּׁאִין פּוֹעֲלִין לִתְבּוֹעַ שְׂכָרָן מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** With regard to salaried **laborers who came** into their employer's courtyard **to claim their wages from the homeowner, and the homeowner's ox gored them, or the homeowner's dog bit them, and** a laborer **died,** the homeowner is **exempt. Others say** that he is liable, as salaried **laborers are allowed** to enter their employer's property **to claim their wages from the homeowner.**

The *Gemara* now presents another *braita* along these lines. Workers come to demand their wages (*po'alim she-bau litvo'a scharam mi-ba'al ha-bayis*) from their employer. They enter his property and are gored by his ox or bitten by his dog; the *braita* rules *patur* — the owner is exempt. The employer's exemption in cases of injury in his *reshut* normally requires that the victim entered without permission. The implicit question is therefore whether workers who come to collect wages have an inherent right of entry that would constitute *reshut*, or whether they need explicit permission.

The *Gemara* then cites *Acherim*, who argue that workers indeed have an implicit right to enter to demand payment, and the employer would not be exempt.

### Ambiguity of 'En'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דִּשְׁכִיחַ בְּמָתָא – מַאי טַעְמָא דַּאֲחֵרִים? אִי דִּשְׁכִיחַ בַּבַּיִת – מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** the employer **can be found in the city, what is the reason of the others,** who hold him liable? The laborers could have met him in the city to claim their wages and did not need to enter his courtyard. **If he can be found** only **at home, what is the reason of the first *tanna*,** who exempts him? Clearly they are entitled to claim their wages.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, בְּגַבְרָא דִּשְׁכִיחַ וְלָא שְׁכִיחַ, וְקָרֵי אַבָּבָא וַאֲמַר לְהוּ ״אִין״; מַר סָבַר: ״אִין״ – ״עוּל תָּא״ מַשְׁמַע. וּמַר סָבַר: ״אִין״ – ״קוּם אַדּוּכְתָּךְ״ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** these are not the circumstances under discussion. This *halakha* is **necessary** only **with regard to a man who can** sometimes **be found** in town **and** sometimes **cannot be found** in town, **and** the laborers **called** to him **at the gate** of his courtyard, **and he said to them: Yes.** One **Sage,** referred to as the others, **holds** that the term **yes** in this context **indicates: Come in.** Therefore, he is liable for their death. **And** one **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that the term **yes** in this context **indicates: Stand in your place** and I will come out to you. Since he did not give them permission to enter, he is exempt.

The *Gemara* asks: what is the actual case being debated? If the employer is regularly found in the town, the workers have no right to enter his property unannounced — they should wait to find him in the public domain. If he is virtually never found anywhere but his home, then obviously the workers must enter his property to get paid — there is no practical alternative — and the first opinion's exemption would be difficult to sustain. The debate must therefore concern an intermediate case: a man who is sometimes found in town and sometimes not.

And there is a further wrinkle. The workers knock on the gate and call out, and the employer responds: *in* — yes. The dispute is over what that word means. One opinion holds that *in ul* means "come in" — permission has been explicitly granted. The other holds that *in kum be-duchtach mashma* — "yes, stay where you are, I'll come to you." *En* is intrinsically ambiguous, and which interpretation one adopts determines whether the workers had *reshut* to enter.

### Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״אִין״ – ״קוּם אַדּוּכְתָּךְ״ מַשְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: פּוֹעֵל שֶׁנִּכְנַס לִתְבּוֹעַ שְׂכָרוֹ מִבַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וּנְגָחוֹ שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אוֹ נְשָׁכוֹ כַּלְבּוֹ, פָּטוּר – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּכְנַס בִּרְשׁוּת. אַמַּאי פָּטוּר? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּקָרֵי אַבָּבָא, וְאָמַר לֵיהּ ״אִין״, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ ״אִין״ – ״קוּם אַדּוּכְתָּךְ״ מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that **yes** in this context **indicates: Stand in your place. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a salaried **laborer who entered** his employer's courtyard **to claim his wages from the homeowner, and the homeowner's ox gored him, or his dog bit him,** the homeowner **is exempt, although** the laborer **entered with permission.** The Gemara asks: **Why** is he **exempt** if the laborer entered with permission? **Rather, is it not** because it is a case **where** the laborer **called** him **at the gate, and** he **said to him: Yes? Conclude from it** that **yes** in this context **indicates: Stand in your place.**

The *Gemara* then brings a *braita* that appears to rule the owner *patur* even where the worker entered *bireshut* — with permission — and treats this as confirming the reading that *in* means "stay where you are." The *braita*'s description of the worker as having entered *birshut* is accordingly reinterpreted: it is not that he had actual permission, but rather that he *thought* he had permission — he entered believing he was invited in, when in fact the employer's *in* was merely an acknowledgment of his presence, not a grant of entry. The *braita* thus calls it *nichnas birshut* loosely, meaning he entered with an expectation of permission that was not, in fact, conferred.

The broader lesson the *Gemara* draws from this section is consistent with the preceding analysis: the *braitot* had appeared to make sweeping statements — that being on someone's *reshut* is sufficient for the owner's exemption regardless of circumstances — but the *Gemara* systematically presses each case back to its specific factual foundation: either the victim was genuinely without permission, or there was a genuine absence of negligence, or the employer's acknowledgment did not actually constitute permission. No broad general rule emerges beyond the case-by-case inquiry into whether *reshut* was actually granted.

## To Goring Ox

The *Gemara* now transitions — and it is worth pausing to appreciate the structure of these chapters. The first chapter established the *klalim*, the general principles. The second chapter addressed *shen ve-regel*, and concluded with the *mishna* introducing *adam muad l'olam*. The intervening *sugya* has been devoted entirely to *adam hamazik* — liability of persons for harm they cause. Now the *Gemara* pivots decisively to *shor hamazik* — liability arising from a goring ox — which will be the subject of the remainder of the tractate's discussion.

Yet before leaving *adam hamazik* entirely, the *Gemara* introduces a *mishna* about two oxen goring one another that requires careful analysis in terms of both ox liability and, subsequently, the parallel case of two people who injure one another.

## Two Oxen Goring

### Two Tame Oxen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁנֵי שְׁווֹרִין תַּמִּין שֶׁחָבְלוּ זֶה אֶת זֶה – מְשַׁלְּמִין בַּמּוֹתָר חֲצִי נֶזֶק. שְׁנֵיהֶן מוּעָדִין – מְשַׁלְּמִין בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **two innocuous oxen that injured each other,** the respective damages are evaluated, and if one amount is more than the other, the owner **pays half the damages with regard to the difference.** In other words, the owner of the ox that caused the greater damage pays the other owner half the difference. If **both oxen were forewarned,** the owner of the ox that caused the greater damage **pays the full** cost of the **damage with regard to the difference.**

The *mishna* presents several configurations of two oxen that gore one another. When two *tam* oxen — oxen that have not yet established a pattern of goring — injure each other simultaneously, each pays half of the *difference* in damages (*bemotar chatzi nezek*). If one inflicted a hundred dollars of damage and the other inflicted eighty, the one that did more pays half of the twenty-dollar difference, that is, ten dollars. When both are *muad* — established habitual gorers — each pays the full difference: twenty dollars.

### Tame & Muad Mixed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד תָּם וְאֶחָד מוּעָד – מוּעָד בַּתָּם מְשַׁלֵּם בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, תָּם בַּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם בַּמּוֹתָר חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **one** of the oxen was **innocuous and** the other **one** was **forewarned,** if the **forewarned** ox caused greater damage **to** the **innocuous** ox than the reverse, the owner of the forewarned ox **pays the full** cost of the **damage with regard to the difference.** If the **innocuous** ox caused greater damage **to** the **forewarned** ox, its owner **pays half the damage with regard to the difference.**

The *mishna* then addresses mixed cases. If the *muad* inflicted more damage than the *tam*, the *muad* pays the full difference. If the *tam* inflicted more damage than the *muad*, the *tam* pays half of the difference. *Tosafot* notes that at first glance this *mishna* seems entirely obvious — whatever the difference is, you pay it at the appropriate rate — and one wonders what the *chiddush* could possibly be.

The *Rosh* explains that the *chiddush* is significant, and it is specifically the mixed case of *tam* and *muad* that reveals it. Consider: the *tam* inflicted a hundred dollars of damage, and the *muad* inflicted eighty. If these were two independent acts, one would first calculate the *tam*'s liability — half of a hundred, fifty dollars — and separately calculate the *muad*'s liability — full eighty dollars. Netting those two obligations against each other, the *muad* would actually owe the *tam*'s owner thirty dollars, not ten. Yet the *mishna* says the *tam* pays half the difference of twenty, that is, ten. That is a striking and counterintuitive ruling.

The *Rosh* furthermore establishes a critical precondition for the entire *mishna*: this scenario only applies where both oxen started simultaneously. If one ox attacked first and the other reacted, the entire framework changes — the reacting ox's owner is *patur* entirely, under the principle of *kol hamishaneh b'acher v'shinah bo* (one who provokes another and the other responds is not held liable for the response). An ox that is gored cannot be blamed for goring back.

Given that both started simultaneously, the *chiddush* of the *mishna* is that we do not treat this as two separate acts of damage. We look at the *net* outcome. Everything that cancels out between the two sides is treated as though it never happened. Only the difference in damages — the residual harm — is the actual damage recognized by *halacha*. This has a further consequence for *Tosafot*'s analysis, which anticipates a debate we will encounter shortly. According to the view that when a *tam* gores an ox, the injured party does not merely have a monetary claim against the owner but actually acquires a percentage ownership interest in the goring ox, the *mishna*'s approach again follows: we do not say each party now owns a stake in the other's ox. We say only the net difference creates any ownership transfer or obligation. This is a *chiddush* of enormous scope, and the *Gemara* does not explicitly explain why this netting principle applies, but the *Rosh* establishes it as the foundation of the *mishna*'s ruling.

## Two People Fighting

### Payment by Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים שֶׁחָבְלוּ זֶה בָּזֶה – מְשַׁלְּמִין בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** with regard to **two people who injured each other,** the one who did greater damage **pays the full** cost of the **damage with regard to the difference,** since one is always considered forewarned with regard to damage he causes.

The *mishna* continues: *v'chen shenei anashim shechavlu zeh bazeh* — and similarly, two people who intentionally injured one another pay the difference in full (*bemotar nezek shalem*). The *mishna* also addresses cases of a person fighting a *muad* ox, and of a person fighting a *tam* ox. When a person inflicts more damage on a *tam* ox than the *tam* inflicted on the person, the person pays the full difference. When the *tam* inflicted more on the person, the owner of the *tam* pays half the difference — not half the *tam*'s damage calculated independently and then netted against the full human liability, but half of the net difference.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָדָם בַּמוּעָד וּמוּעָד בָּאָדָם – מְשַׁלֵּם בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם. אָדָם בַּתָּם וְתָם בָּאָדָם – אָדָם בַּתָּם מְשַׁלֵּם בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, תָּם בָּאָדָם מְשַׁלֵּם בַּמּוֹתָר חֲצִי נֶזֶק. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אַף תָּם שֶׁחָבַל בָּאָדָם – מְשַׁלֵּם בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** If **a person** caused damage **to a forewarned** ox **and** the **forewarned** ox caused damage **to** the **person,** whichever side caused the greater damage **pays the full** cost of the **damage with regard to the difference.** In a case where **a person** caused damage **to an innocuous** ox **and** the **innocuous** ox caused damage **to** the **person,** if **the person** caused greater financial damage **to** the **innocuous** ox **he pays the full** cost of the **damage with regard to the difference.** If the **innocuous** ox caused greater damage **to the person,** its owner **pays** only **half the damage with regard to the difference. Rabbi Akiva says:** The owner of the **innocuous** ox **that injured a person also pays the full** cost of the **damage with regard to the difference.** Rabbi Akiva does not distinguish between an innocuous and a forewarned ox in a case where an ox injures a person.

Rabbi Akiva then introduces his well-known position: even a *tam* that gores a person pays full damages. This anticipates a *braita* that the *Gemara* will now analyze through the relevant *pesukim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כַּמִּשְׁפָּט הַזֶּה יֵעָשֶׂה לּוֹ״ – כְּמִשְׁפַּט שׁוֹר בְּשׁוֹר, כָּךְ מִשְׁפַּט שׁוֹר בְּאָדָם. מָה שׁוֹר בְּשׁוֹר – תָּם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק, וּמוּעָד נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, אַף שׁוֹר בְּאָדָם – תָּם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק, וּמוּעָד נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis about a case where an ox injures a person, **the Sages taught:** It is derived from the verse: "Whether it has gored a son, or has gored a daughter, **according to this judgment shall it be done to him"** (Exodus 21:31), that **as is the judgment** concerning **an ox** that causes damage **to an ox, so is the judgment** with regard to **an ox** that causes damage **to a person. Just as** with regard to **an ox** that causes damage **to an ox,** if it is **innocuous** its owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage and** if it is **forewarned** he pays the **full** cost of the **damage, so too,** with regard to **an ox** that causes damage **to a person,** if it is an **innocuous** ox its owner **pays half** the cost of **the damage and** if it is a **forewarned** ox the owner pays the **full** cost of the **damage.**

The *Gemara* brings the relevant *braita* interpreting the verse *kamishpat hazeh ye'aseh lo* — "as this law shall be done to him" — which the Torah uses when discussing an ox that gores a child. The rabbis understand this to be referring not to a killing but to an injury, specifically when the ox injures a person, and they debate what law applies. The *Tanna Kamma* argues: *kamishpat shor beshor kach mishpat shor be'adam* — just as an ox goring another ox is subject to the standard rules of *tam* (half payment) and *muad* (full payment), so too an ox injuring a person. And the phrase *kamishpat hazeh* also serves to exclude the additional payments that apply when a *person* injures another person: the *arba'ah devarim* of *nezek*, *shevet*, *tza'ar*, and *ripuy* do not apply when an ox injures a person, since those derive from the verse *ish ki yiten mum be'amito* — a person who inflicts a blemish on his fellow — and an ox is not a person.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״כַּמִּשְׁפָּט הַזֶּה״ – כַּתַּחְתּוֹן, וְלֹא כָּעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** It is derived from the phrase **"according to this judgment"** that the *halakha* with regard to an ox that gores a person is judged **like** the case that appears in **the lower** verse, i.e., the case of a forewarned ox, which appears in Exodus 21:29, **and not like** the case that appears in **the upper** verse, i.e., the case of an innocuous ox, which appears in Exodus 21:28.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל מְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יֵעָשֶׂה לוֹ״ – מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that since the case of an ox that gored a person is compared to the case of a forewarned ox, the owner also **pays from** his **superior-quality** property. Therefore, **the verse states: "Shall it be done to him [*lo*],"** indicating **he pays** restitution exclusively **from** the proceeds of the sale of the **body** of his belligerent ox **and does not pay from** his **superior-quality** property, as the word *lo* can also be understood as referring to the ox. In this manner the case of an innocuous ox that gores a person is compared to the *halakha* of an innocuous ox that gores another ox, whereas with regard to the amount of restitution, it is compared to the case of a forewarned ox.

Rabbi Akiva reads *kamishpat hazeh* differently. The *Torah*'s structure in these *parshiyot* is as follows: first, an ox kills a person in a *tam* scenario; then, an ox kills a person in a *muad* scenario (where *kofer* is paid); then, an ox gores another ox in *tam* and *muad* configurations. When the *Torah* says *kamishpat hazeh ye'aseh lo* — do like *this* law — it refers to the most proximate precedent, which is the *muad* case. Therefore, *ketachton v'lo ke'elyon* — like the lower case and not the upper — when an ox injures a person, one applies the law of *muad* and pays full damages. However, *yigach yaaseh lo* — the phrase "it shall be done *to it*," the ox — indicates that one pays from the body of the ox (*migufo*), not from the owner's general assets, even though the amount is full damages. It is an unusual hybrid: the full rate of a *muad* but the collection mechanism of a *tam*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן – ״זֶה״ לְמָה לִי? לְפוֹטְרוֹ מֵאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who do not differentiate between an ox that gores a person and one that gores an animal, inasmuch as the distinction between an innocuous and a forewarned ox applies in both cases, **why do I** need the seemingly superfluous word **"this"?** The Gemara answers: The word is stated **to exempt him from the four types of indemnity** that one who injures another person is liable to pay, thereby emphasizing the comparison to the case of an ox that gores an ox.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – לְפוֹטְרוֹ מֵאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵ״אִישׁ כִּי יִתֵּן מוּם בַּעֲמִיתוֹ״; ״אִישׁ בַּעֲמִיתוֹ״ – וְלֹא שׁוֹר בַּעֲמִיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Akiva** derive the *halakha* **exempting him from** paying these **four types of indemnity?** The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: **"And if a man maims his neighbor,** as he has done, so shall be done to him" (Leviticus 24:19). Rabbi Akiva derives from here that only when **a man** injures **his neighbor** is he liable to pay these four types of indemnity, **but not** when **an ox** injures **his neighbor.**

The *Gemara* then asks how Rabbi Akiva knows that an ox injuring a person is exempt from *arba'ah devarim*, since the verse he is using for his *muad* reading cannot also serve to exclude the additional payments. The answer: Rabbi Akiva derives the exclusion of *arba'ah devarim* from the verse *ish ki yiten mum be'amito* — *ish be'amito* and not *shor be'amito*, these payments apply only when a *person* injures another person.

The rabbis, for their part, could not have derived the exclusion of *arba'ah devarim* from that verse alone, because they would have said: perhaps we only learn the exclusion of *tza'ar* from there, but *ripuy* and *shevet*, which are derived from different verses, might still apply when an ox injures a person. It is *kamishpat hazeh* that tells us the law of an ox injuring a person follows the standard goring-ox framework entirely, excluding all such additional payments.

### Self-Defense Rights

Now, before fully departing the topic of *adam hamazik*, there is a critical ruling of the *Rosh* in *Siman* 13 of the third *perek* that bears directly on the *mishna* about two people who injure one another. The *Rosh* begins by applying the principle of *kol hamishaneh b'acher v'shinah bo* to the human case as well: if Reuven and Shimon started fighting simultaneously, or if Reuven beat up Shimon and later Shimon came back and beat up Reuven, the net difference rule applies. But if Reuven started hitting Shimon and Shimon fought back, Shimon is *patur* — he was defending himself. This is true even though the principle of *kol hamishaneh* does not technically apply to people as it does to oxen, because the right to self-defense provides an independent basis for exemption. And this right extends, the *Rosh* notes, even to using physical force to protect property — drawing on the principle of *avid inish dina l'nafshei* discussed earlier in the tractate, where even violence was permitted to prevent an immediate, irrecoverable loss.

### Force Limitations

The *Rosh* then introduces the critical limiting principle. The right of self-defense is not unlimited. He cites the case of the woman in the *Torah* who grabs a man's private parts while defending her husband from an attacker: she is liable for *boshet* if she had an alternative, but exempt if she had no other means of stopping the assault. The *Rosh* extrapolates from this: if one could have protected oneself with lesser force but instead inflicted greater injury, one is *chaiyav* for the excess. The *Gemara*'s standard from *rodef* applies — only the force necessary to stop the threat is authorized. Once the attacker is incapacitated, or once a lesser measure would have sufficed, continued or disproportionate force becomes its own tortious act.

### Extended Protection

The *Rosh* then extends the analysis outward. If a person sees someone beating his father, his children, or his brother, and intervenes physically to protect the family member — and there was no other means of stopping the assault — he is *patur*, analogous to the woman who had no alternative to physical intervention to save her husband. The same right that exists for self-protection extends to protecting one's immediate family members.

The *Rosh* then turns to an even broader case: if a person witnesses one Jew physically attacking another, and cannot stop it except by striking the attacker, is it permitted to intervene even though this is not self-defense and not protection of family? The *Rosh* rules that it is permitted — but notably, the *halachic* mechanism he invokes is not an extension of *avid inish dina l'nafshei*, but rather the principle of *l'hafrisho me-issura*: one may use force to stop a person from committing a transgression. This framing is meaningful. *Avid inish dina l'nafshei* is explicitly *l'nafshei* — for oneself — and the *Rosh* does not extend that principle to third-party protection. Instead, he grounds third-party intervention in the obligation to prevent another person from perpetrating a sin.

This distinction has practical implications that the *Rosh* himself acknowledges. Framing the right of intervention around the *aveirah* of the aggressor rather than the protection of the victim raises difficult questions. What if the aggressor is a *katan*, who is not halachically culpable for his actions? Can one use force to stop a non-culpable aggressor on the basis of preventing a transgression? The *Rosh*'s framework does not straightforwardly accommodate that case.

One might have wished to say instead that any case of physical injury to a person is, by its nature, a *pesida* — an irreversible harm — and therefore the logic that permits immediate action without waiting for the courts (*avid inish dina l'nafshei* where *ika pesseida*) should extend to protecting third parties from physical harm, just as one may intervene to prevent a *rodef* from killing. The *Rosh* is effectively reaching the same practical destination, but through a different halachic mechanism, and it is worth flagging that the grounding in *l'hafrisho me-issura* rather than in protection of the victim creates a different structural framework with different outer limits. This is a very significant *Rosh*, and it serves as an important complement to the earlier discussion of *avid inish dina l'nafshei*.

## Ox Goring Ox

### Takes Entire Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָנֶה שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָאתַיִם, וְאֵין הַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה כְּלוּם – נוֹטֵל אֶת הַשּׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to an innocuous **ox worth one hundred dinars that gored an ox worth two hundred** dinars, **and the carcass** of the dead ox **is not worth anything,** its owner **takes the** entire **ox** that gored it, since it is worth half the value of the damage.

The *mishna* at the bottom of 33b now introduces the case of an ox worth a *maneh* (one hundred *zuz*) that gores and kills an ox worth two hundred *zuz*, where the carcass is worthless. Since the *tam* pays half of the damage — here, one hundred *zuz* — and that equals the full value of the goring ox, the *mishna* rules that the injured party simply takes the entire goring ox. The *chiddush* is not merely that payment is capped at the value of the goring ox (*migufo*). The deeper *chiddush* is that the injured party takes the ox itself, not merely its equivalent value in cash. The question — how literally do we mean *migufo*, from its body — will drive the debate between Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva.

### Mistaken Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי – רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: יוּשַׁם הַשּׁוֹר בְּבֵית דִּין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: הוּחְלַט הַשּׁוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Whose** opinion is expressed in the **mishna,** which rules that the injured party takes the ox immediately? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: After it gores another ox, **the** belligerent **ox shall be appraised in court** before it is taken by the injured party, this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: The ox was** already **assigned** to the owner of the dead ox as payment, and if the amount of damages is not contested by the owner of the goring ox, no further legal steps are required.

The *Gemara* establishes that this *mishna* reflects the position of Rabbi Akiva, not that of the rabbis generally. This is demonstrated from a *braita*.

### Creditor vs. Partner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: בַּעַל חוֹב הוּא, וְזוּזֵי הוּא דְּמַסֵּיק לֵיהּ. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: שׁוּתָּפֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With regard to what** principle **do** they **disagree? Rabbi Yishmael holds** that the owner of the dead ox **is** considered **a creditor** of the owner of the belligerent ox, **and it is money that he is claiming from him,** but he has no ownership of the body of the belligerent ox. **And Rabbi Akiva holds** that **they are partners,** i.e., from the time the innocuous ox killed the other ox, the owner of the dead ox has a share of ownership in the belligerent ox.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בְּהַאי קְרָא: ״וּמָכְרוּ אֶת הַשּׁוֹר הַחַי וְחָצוּ אֶת כַּסְפּוֹ״ – רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: לְבֵי דִינָא קָמַזְהַר רַחֲמָנָא; וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: לְנִיזָּק וּמַזִּיק מַזְהַר לְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And they disagree with regard to** the meaning of **this verse: "Then they shall sell the live ox, and divide its monetary value"** (Exodus 21:35). **Rabbi Yishmael holds** that **the Merciful One is commanding the court** to evaluate the damages in this manner, **and Rabbi Akiva holds** that **the Merciful One is commanding the injured** party **and the one liable for damage** to split ownership of the live ox, without the involvement of the court.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? הִקְדִּישׁוֹ נִיזָּק אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the two opinions as to whether or not they are considered partners? The Gemara answers: There **is** a practical difference **between them** in a case where **the injured** party **consecrated** the ox to the Temple. According to the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael, until the court transfers the ox to the injured party, it still belongs to its owner, and therefore the injured party cannot consecrate it. According to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, the injured party owns the ox from the time the damage was inflicted, and he can therefore consecrate it.

The *braita* records that Rabbi Ishmael says: *ve-yusham ha-shor be-veit din* — the ox should be assessed by *beit din*. Rabbi Akiva says: *huchlat ha-shor* — the ox is directly transferred. The *Gemara* identifies the root of their dispute. Rabbi Ishmael holds that the injured party (*nizak*) is merely a *ba'al chov* — a creditor. The injuring party owes him money, and the ox is the mechanism through which that debt is satisfied, but Shimon does not own any part of Reuven's ox. Rabbi Akiva holds *shutfin ninhu* — they become partners. The moment Reuven's ox gores Shimon's ox, Shimon acquires a partial ownership interest in Reuven's ox.

The dispute is anchored in the reading of the verse *u-machru et ha-shor ha-chai v'chatzu et kaspo* — they shall sell the living ox and divide its value. The *Torah*'s paradigm case has two oxen of equal value, one of which kills the other and renders it worthless. The injured party is therefore owed half the value of the goring ox. Rabbi Ishmael reads *u-machru* as addressed to *beit din*: the court sells the ox and distributes the proceeds. The injured party is a creditor; *beit din* is the agent that liquidates the ox to satisfy the debt. Rabbi Akiva reads *u-machru* as addressed to the two parties themselves: *you* — the *nizak* and the *mazik* together — sell the ox and divide the proceeds. If both parties are selling the ox, it follows that both are already owners of it. From the moment of the goring, Shimon became a co-owner.

## Sell Ox or Pay Cash

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: מְכָרוֹ מַזִּיק, לְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּעַל חוֹב הוּא, וְזוּזֵי הוּא דְּמַסֵּיק לֵיהּ – מָכוּר; אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דִּמְשַׁעְבַּד לֵיהּ לְנִיזָּק – לָאו כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ מָכוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava asked Rav Naḥman:** If **the one liable for damage sold** the ox, **what is** the *halakha* **according to Rabbi Yishmael?** Is it that **since Rabbi Yishmael says** that the injured party **is** considered **a creditor, and it is** merely **money that he is claiming from him, it is sold? Or perhaps** does he say that **since** the ox **is liened** to the debt **to the injured** party, who will collect it should the ox's owner not have sufficient funds, **it is not in his** power to sell it? Rav Naḥman **said to him: It is not sold.**

Rava poses a question to Rav Nachman: according to Rabbi Ishmael, if Reuven — the owner of the goring ox — sells the ox before Shimon collects, what is the result? According to Rabbi Akiva, this question is easy: Reuven cannot sell what is no longer fully his; Shimon already owns a portion of the ox. But according to Rabbi Ishmael, Reuven is simply a debtor who owes money. In ordinary cases, a debtor can sell his assets and pay his creditor cash. Is that true here, or does Shimon's relationship to *this specific ox* constrain Reuven's ability to alienate it?

### Sale & Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: מְכָרוֹ – מָכוּר! חוֹזֵר וְגוֹבֵהוּ. וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁחוֹזֵר וְגוֹבֵהוּ, לָמָּה מָכוּר? לְרִידְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava asked him: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if **he sold it, it is sold?** Rav Naḥman replied: Nevertheless, the injured party **then collects it** from the purchaser. The Gemara asks: **Since** the injured party **then collects it** from the purchaser, **with regard to what** matter **is it sold?** His right to collect it negates the effectiveness of the sale. The Gemara answers: It is sold **for** the purpose of **plowing [*ridya*].** The purchaser may use the ox for plowing until the injured party collects it from him, and the purchaser is not required to reimburse the injured party for the use of his ox.

Rav Nachman's answer: *eino machur* — it is not fully sold. Even though Shimon does not own the ox under Rabbi Ishmael's view, Shimon's right to *this ox specifically* is strong enough that Reuven cannot unilaterally sell it away from him. The *Gemara* then cites a *braita* that appears to say *machur*, the sale stands. The *Gemara* reconciles: the sale does stand insofar as Levi (the buyer) acquires the ox — it was Reuven's to sell. But *chozer v'goveh* — Shimon can go and collect it from Levi. Reuven sold encumbered property, and Levi bought it with Shimon's lien attached. The sale is valid as between Reuven and Levi; it does not extinguish Shimon's right.

The *Gemara* then asks: if Shimon can collect the ox even after the sale to Levi, what exactly did Levi acquire? The answer: *l'radya* — Levi may use the ox to plow his field. He owns the ox in the interim; he can work with it and derive benefit from it. Until Shimon chooses to exercise his lien and come collect it, Levi's ownership is fully operative. This is analogous to any purchase of property that carries a prior lien: the buyer acquires real ownership and can use the asset, but must surrender it if the lienholder comes to collect.

### Lien Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לֹוֶה וּמוֹכֵר מִטַּלְטְלִין – בֵּית דִּין גּוֹבִין לוֹ מֵהֶם? שָׁנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְמַאן דַּעֲשָׂאוֹ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Should one **conclude from** this ruling that with regard to one who **borrows** money **and** then **sells** his **movable property, the court can collect** the debt **from** this property **on behalf of** the creditor, as according to Rabbi Yishmael the belligerent ox is only a lien for the debt owed to the injured party? The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of the belligerent ox, **it is different, as** the owner of the ox **is** considered **like one who rendered it designated payment** of the debt, since the Torah specifies that the injured party collects damages from the ox. In general, however, movable property that is sold by a debtor cannot be collected by the creditor.

This raises a difficult technical problem. The mechanism by which Shimon can collect the ox even after Reuven has sold it to Levi is that of a lien — *meshubad* — on the ox. But standard *halacha* holds that liens operate on land, not on *metaltelin*, movable property including cattle. If Reuven owes money and sells his cattle, a creditor normally cannot pursue those cattle in the hands of a third-party buyer. How, then, can Shimon collect the ox from Levi?

The *Gemara* initially suggests we might derive a general principle that a creditor can collect *metaltelin* from a third-party purchaser. Rava pushes back: this is not the general rule. His proof: if one makes an *apotiki* — an explicit lien — on one's slave and sells the slave, *beit din* will enforce that lien and allow collection from the buyer. But if one makes an explicit *apotiki* on one's ox and sells it, *beit din* will not enforce the lien against the buyer. The difference is that a slave has the legal status closer to land (*karka*) for certain purposes, whereas ordinary cattle do not. An explicit *apotiki* on ordinary cattle cannot be collected from third-party purchasers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: עָשָׂה עַבְדּוֹ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי, וּמְכָרוֹ – בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה הֵימֶנּוּ. שׁוֹרוֹ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי וּמְכָרוֹ – אֵין בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה הֵימֶנּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rava say** that if one **rendered his slave** as **designated repayment** for a debt **and** subsequently **sold him,** the **creditor collects** payment **from** the purchaser, whereas if one rendered **his ox** as **designated repayment and** then **sold it, the creditor cannot collect** it **from** the purchaser? This contradicts the previous statement that the belligerent ox is considered designated repayment, and therefore even if it is sold the injured party can collect it from the purchaser.

If ordinary cattle with an explicit *apotiki* cannot be collected from buyers, why can Shimon collect the goring ox from Levi, given that Shimon's right under Rabbi Ishmael is merely that of a creditor with an implicit *Torah*-granted lien?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶבֶד מַאי טַעְמָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּאִית לֵיהּ קָלָא; הַאי נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דִּנְגַח – קָלָא אִית לֵיהּ, דְּ״תוֹרָא נַגְחָנָא״ קָרוּ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The distinction made in Rava's statement answers this question. **What is the reason** that **a slave** who was rendered as designated repayment can be collected from the purchaser? It is **because** rendering a slave as designated repayment is not common and generates **publicity.** The purchaser was therefore aware of this when he bought the slave. Similarly, with regard to **this** ox **as well, since it gored** an animal, it generates **publicity, as** it is publicly **called a goring ox,** and so the purchaser was aware of the lien attached to it. Therefore, the injured party can collect it from the purchaser.

The *Gemara*'s answer is elegant and grounded in practical reality. The reason explicit liens on slaves are enforceable against buyers (*apotiki* on *eved*) is *mishum d'is lei kala* — because information about enslaved persons becomes widely known. Slaves are significant enough pieces of property that encumbrances on them circulate as public knowledge, so buyers are on notice and the legal maxim of *lav kol keminei* — the seller cannot unilaterally strip the creditor's rights — applies. Ordinary cattle, by contrast, are commonly bought and sold without any public notice of encumbrances, so liens on them cannot be enforced against purchasers.

But a goring ox is different: *keivan d'nagach kala is lei* — since it has gored, the word gets out. *Tora nagchana karu lei* — people call it "Reuven's goring ox." A goring ox is notorious; its status becomes public knowledge. Therefore, even though it is cattle and not land, the *kala* — the public awareness — that attaches to a goring ox is sufficient to treat it like property on which an effective lien can be maintained and collected even from a third-party purchaser.

The *Gemara* is thus careful not to generate a *gezeiras hakasuv* where a principled explanation is available. Rather than simply saying the *Torah* granted this right and that is that, the *Gemara* fits the ruling back into the existing framework of lien law: the goring ox's notoriety makes it function like property on which an effective *apotiki* can operate, and Shimon's *Torah*-granted right to that specific ox is therefore enforceable even against Levi. The debate between Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva — creditor versus partner — will continue to generate further practical differences that the *Gemara* will explore in the coming *daf*.
