# Menachot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava's Approach

### Shelo Lishmah Intro

The third daf of Menachot continues to grapple with the position of Rabbi Shimon on the question of *shelo lishmah* — offering a sacrifice with intent for a different type of sacrifice. The parallel tractate, Zevachim, deals with this issue by *zevachim*, animal sacrifices, while Menachot addresses it in the context of flour offerings. Rabbi Shimon's distinctive position is that *shelo lishmah* does not invalidate a *mincha* when the false intent is self-evidently contradicted by physical reality. If a *kohen* performs the *kemitza* of a dry *mincha* while thinking of it as a wet one — or a fried one as a baked one — Rabbi Shimon holds that this *machshava* is meaningless, because the physical reality so plainly contradicts it that the intent cannot be taken seriously.

This logic is compelling on its face. Yet it must be reconciled with another statement of Rabbi Shimon, where he explicitly compares *mincha* to *chatas* and *asham* and affirms that a *shelo lishmah* intent does invalidate. How are these two positions to be reconciled?

### Rava's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – בְּקוֹמֵץ מִנְחָה לְשׁוּם מִנְחָה, כָּאן – בְּקוֹמֵץ מִנְחָה לְשׁוּם זֶבַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **not difficult. Here,** where Rabbi Shimon says that a meal offering that was sacrificed not for its own sake fulfills the owner's obligation, he is referring to a case **where one removes a handful** from **a meal offering for the sake of a meal offering. There,** where he says that it does not fulfill the owner's obligation, he is referring to a case **where one removes a handful** from **a meal offering for the sake of a slaughtered offering.**

Rava's answer takes Rabbi Shimon at his word. He affirms that ordinarily *shelo lishmah* is indeed meaningless when contradicted by reality, and that when Rabbi Shimon holds it *is* meaningful, that refers to intent involving a different owner — a case where the false premise is not self-evidently absurd. This is a straightforward reading of Rabbi Shimon's reasoning: intent matters only when it reflects a coherent, even if incorrect, possibility.

### Gemara's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחָה לְשׁוּם מִנְחָה – ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה״, תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הַמְּנָחוֹת; מִנְחָה לְשׁוּם זֶבַח – ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה וְזֶבַח״ לָא כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** If one removes a handful from **a meal offering for the sake of** a different **meal offering** he fulfills his obligation, as the verse states: **"And this is the law of the meal offering"** (Leviticus 6:7). This indicates that there is **one law for all the meal offerings,** i.e., they are all considered variations of the same offering, and are different only with regard to how they should be sacrificed *ab initio*. Conversely, if one sacrificed **a meal offering for the sake of a slaughtered offering,** since the phrase: **And this is the law of the meal offering and a slaughtered offering, is not written** anywhere, there is no reason to conclude that one fulfills his obligation under such circumstances.

Having established this framework, the *gemara* presses further. If we accept that any intent plainly contradicted by reality is meaningless, there ought to be many such cases across the laws of *korbanos* — not just by *mincha*. Are there not similar cases involving birds? Are there not other scenarios where *shelo lishmah* intent is so obviously false that Rabbi Shimon would ignore it? The *gemara* proceeds through a series of examples, testing whether the principle truly extends as broadly as Rava's reading of Rabbi Shimon implies.

## Kodshei/Kalim Cases

### Kodshei Kodashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן בַּצָּפוֹן לְשֵׁם קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, לִירַצּוֹ, מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן מוֹכִיחִין עֲלֵיהֶן דְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים נִינְהוּ, דְּאִי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים – בַּדָּרוֹם הֲוָה עָבֵיד לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Offerings of the most sacred order that one slaughtered in the northern** part of the Temple courtyard, which is a requirement that applies only to offerings of the most sacred order, **for the sake of offerings of lesser sanctity, should effect acceptance** for their owners, as **the actions** performed **on them prove that they are offerings of the most sacred order. Because if** they are in fact **offerings of lesser sanctity, he would have performed their** slaughter **in the southern** part of the Temple courtyard.

The *gemara* opens with what might seem like a simple case. If *kodshei kodashim* — the most sacred offerings such as a *chatas* — are slaughtered in the north of the *azara* (as required) with intent that they are *kodashim kalim*, such as *shelamim*, should that intent be disregarded? The *maaseh mochiach aleihem* — the act itself proves their identity — because *kodashim kalim* are slaughtered in the south. The fact of the northern *shechita* makes the false intent self-evident.

### Kodashim Kalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא אַף בַּדָּרוֹם, בַּדָּרוֹם וְלֹא בַּצָּפוֹן מִי אָמַר? דִּתְנַן: שְׁחִיטָתָן בְּכׇל מָקוֹם בָּעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The slaughter of an offering in the northern part of the Temple courtyard is not indicative of the type of offering one intends it to be, as one can **say that the Merciful One states** that offerings of lesser sanctity may be slaughtered **even in the southern** part of the courtyard. **Did** He **say** that these offerings must be slaughtered specifically **in the southern** part **and not in the northern** part? This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 55a), that **the slaughter of** offerings of lesser sanctity may be performed **in any place within the** Temple **courtyard.**

However, the *gemara* immediately deflates this as a softball question. The *gemara* notes that *shelamim* are in fact permitted to be slaughtered anywhere in the *azara* — there is no restriction to the south. The Mishnah elsewhere states *shechitasan b'chol makom ba'azarah* — they may be slaughtered anywhere in the Temple courtyard. Slaughtering a *kodshei kodashim* in the north with intent for *shelamim* is therefore not patently absurd, since *shelamim* may legitimately be slaughtered in the north as well. So this case does not lend itself easily to the argument that the intent is self-evidently false.

### Kodashim Kalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן בַּדָּרוֹם לְשֵׁם קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים לִירַצּוֹ, מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן מוֹכִיחִין עֲלֵיהֶן, דְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים נִינְהוּ, דְּאִי קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים – בַּצָּפוֹן הֲוָה עָבֵיד לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Offerings of lesser sanctity that one slaughtered in the southern** part of the Temple courtyard **for the sake of offerings of the most sacred order should effect acceptance** for their owners, as **the actions** performed **on them prove that they are offerings of lesser sanctity. Because if** they are in fact **offerings of the most sacred order, he would have performed their** slaughter **in the northern** part of the Temple courtyard.

The *gemara* turns to the reverse: *kodashim kalim* slaughtered in the south with intent for *kodshei kodashim*, such as *chatas*. Here, one might argue, the *maaseh mochiach aleihem* — the southern *shechita* — shows it cannot be a *chatas*, which requires the north. The intent should therefore be disregarded as obviously wrong.

### Mistake Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים נִינְהוּ, וּמִיעְבָּר הוּא דַּעֲבַר, וּשְׁחַט לְהוּ בַּדָּרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The fact that an offering was slaughtered in the southern part of the Temple courtyard is not a clear indication that it was intended as an offering of lesser sanctity, as people might say: They are** in fact **offerings of the most sacred order, but** the priest **transgressed** the mitzva to slaughter them in the northern part of the courtyard **and slaughtered them in the southern** part.

The *gemara* objects: although *chatas* must be slaughtered in the north, there is no rule that prevents an animal from being slaughtered in the south and thereby being treated *as if* it were a *chatas* done in the wrong location. That is, one cannot say with certainty that the scenario of a *chatas* slaughtered in the south is impossible — it can happen through error. An animal could have been consecrated as a *chatas* and accidentally slaughtered in the south. The intent that it is a *chatas* is therefore not categorically groundless, because the scenario, however mistaken, is conceivable.

This raises a deeper problem. If we allow that mistakes occur — that a *chatas* can wind up being slaughtered in the south by accident, or a *marcheshes* brought incorrectly as a *machavat* — then virtually every *shelo lishmah* intent becomes potentially meaningful. After all, a person could always have vowed one thing and accidentally brought another. If the mere possibility of such a mistake renders an intent non-absurd, then the *gemara*'s entire theoretical framework for dismissing intent collapses.

### Machavas/Marcheshes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַחֲבַת לְשֵׁם מַרְחֶשֶׁת נָמֵי, הַאי דְּקָא קָמֵיץ לֵהּ לְמַרְחֶשֶׁת, אֲמַר: הַאי בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת נְדַר, וְהָא דְּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ בְּמַחֲבַת – דְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת הִיא, וּמִעְבָּר הוּא דַּעֲבַר וְאַתְיַיהּ בְּמַחֲבַת!
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** i.e., if people might suspect an individual of performing the rite of an offering in an improper manner, then if a priest removes a handful from **a pan** meal offering **for the sake of a deep-pan** meal offering **as well,** one who sees **that this** priest **removes a handful for** the sake of **a deep-pan** meal offering might **say: This** owner **took a vow** to bring a meal offering **in a deep pan, and** as for the fact **that he brings it in a pan, it is** actually the meal offering **of a deep pan and** the owner **transgressed** the mitzva to bring it in a deep pan, **and brought it in a pan.** Why, then, does Rabbi Shimon maintain that such a meal offering satisfies the obligation of its owner?

The *gemara* illustrates this with the case of *machavas l'shem marcheshes*: if someone performs *kemitza* from a *machavat* — a flat pan — with intent that it is a *marcheshes* — a deep, heavily oiled pan — why not say this intent too is meaningful? Maybe this *mincha* was vowed as a *marcheshes* and accidentally brought as a *machavat*. If such mistakes can happen, the intent that it is a *marcheshes* is not absurd, and should therefore invalidate.

The *gemara* provides a decisive resolution for this case, one that hinges on a principle specific to *minchas*: the physical vessel ultimately defines the identity of the offering, regardless of what was vowed. The *gemara* cites a *baraisa*:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, כִּי נָמֵי בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת נָדַר, כִּי מַיְיתֵי לַהּ בְּמַחֲבַת – מַחֲבַת הָוְיָא. כְּדִתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי בְּמַחֲבַת״ וְהֵבִיא בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, ״בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת״ וְהֵבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת – מַה שֶּׁהֵבִיא הֵבִיא, וִידֵי נִדְרוֹ לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** where the priest removed the handful from a pan meal offering for the sake of a deep-pan meal offering, **even if** the owner **took a vow** to bring a meal offering **in a deep pan, when he brings it in a pan it is** considered a valid **pan** meal offering, and therefore the priest's intention is recognizably false. This is **as we learned** in a mishna (102b): **One who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring a meal offering prepared **in a pan, and he brought** it **in a deep pan,** or if he says: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering prepared **in a deep pan, and he brought** it **in a pan,** the meal offering **that he brought, he brought,** i.e., it is valid and the priest performs the rites of the meal offering that he actually brought, **but he did not fulfill** the obligation resulting from **his vow,** and he must bring another meal offering to fulfill that obligation.

if one says *harei alai b'machavat* — I hereby undertake to bring a *machavat mincha* — and brings it in a *marcheshes*, or vice versa, whatever was actually brought is what was brought (*mah shehevi hevi*), though one has not fulfilled one's vow. The identity of the *korban* follows the physical reality of how it was prepared and offered, not the initial declaration.

This has a striking implication: once a *mincha* is brought in a *machavat*, it is, by definition, a *machavat* — whatever was said or vowed beforehand. This principle is stated explicitly in the Mishnah and is affirmed, crucially, by Rabbi Shimon himself. Rabbi Shimon holds that even if one directly sanctifies an object with the words *harei zo l'marcheshes* — I hereby designate this flour and oil as a *marcheshes* — and then brings it as a *machavat*, one has fulfilled one's vow, because the verbal sanctification is understood as a generic *harei zo l'mincha*, and the specific type is always determined by what is brought at the end. For Rabbi Shimon, the kind of *mincha* it is depends entirely on how it arrives, not on how it was sanctified verbally.

The consequence is definitive: there is no possible scenario in which an item brought in a *machavat* is actually a *marcheshes*. Even if one had originally sanctified the specific flour and oil as a *marcheshes*, once it is brought as a *machavat*, it is a *machavat*. This is not a matter of an error producing a mislabeled offering — the physical reality constitutes the identity absolutely. Therefore, intent that the *machavat* is a *marcheshes* is not merely unlikely or unusual; it is metaphysically impossible. There is no conceivable world in which this item being brought before us is a *marcheshes*. The intent is categorically groundless, and Rabbi Shimon therefore ignores it.

This contrasts sharply with the case of *shelamim* slaughtered in the south for the sake of *chatas*. There, one can conceive of a scenario — a *chatas* accidentally slaughtered in the south. The scenario is wrong, invalid, but it can occur. The intent, therefore, reflects a real if erroneous possibility, and is meaningful enough to invalidate. But for *machavas* and *marcheshes*, no such scenario exists. Whatever preceded the bringing, the brought reality is what it is, always and entirely.

The *gemara* briefly considers a complication: what if one said *harei zo l'marcheshes* — directly sanctifying the specific flour and oil — and then brought it in a *machavat*? Would not this be a case where the item was truly a *marcheshes* done incorrectly?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא אָמַר ״זוֹ לְהָבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת״ וְהֵבִיא בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, כְּדִתְנַן: זוֹ לְהָבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת וְהֵבִיא בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת וְהֵבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת – הֲרֵי זוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** even so, the priest's improper intent remains indiscernible, as people might think: **Perhaps** the owner **said: This** tenth of an ephah of flour is a meal offering that I must **bring in a pan, and** in spite of this **he brought** it **in a deep pan.** Such a meal offering is disqualified and is not considered a valid pan meal offering, **as we learned** in that same mishna: One who took a vow, saying: **This** tenth of an ephah of flour is a meal offering that I must **bring in a pan, and he brought** it **in a deep pan,** or if he vowed: This tenth of an ephah of flour is a meal offering that I must bring **in a deep pan, and he brought** it **in a pan, it is disqualified,** as he did not fulfill his vow.

The *gemara* answers: even then, one who has intent that this *machavat* is a *marcheshes* entertains an impossible premise. The *baraisa* teaches that even in the case of *zo l'marcheshes v'hevi b'machavat*, it is *pesulah* — invalid — meaning it does not validly serve as either, but its identity has nonetheless been resolved by the physical form in which it arrived.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּנַן הָכִי נָמֵי, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: ״אַף יְדֵי נִדְרוֹ יָצָא״, אַלְמָא קְבִיעוּתָא דְמָנָא וְלָא כְּלוּם הוּא, וְלָא שְׁנָא אָמַר ״זוֹ״, וְלָא שְׁנָא אָמַר ״עָלַי״.
>
> **Translation:** **According to the Rabbis,** i.e., according to the opinion of the mishna just cited, there is **indeed** a difficulty. But **according to Rabbi Shimon** there is no difficulty, **since** with regard to a case where one took a vow, saying: It is incumbent upon me to bring a pan meal offering, and then he brought it in a deep pan, **Rabbi Shimon says: He has fulfilled even** the obligation resulting from **his vow. Evidently, the designation of the vessel** for a meal offering **is nothing** according to Rabbi Shimon, **and** there **is no difference** in this regard whether **he said: This** is for a particular type of meal offering, **and there is no difference** whether **he said:** It is incumbent **upon me** to bring a particular meal offering.

Rabbi Shimon's position that even *zo l'marcheshes* is fulfilled by a *machavat* removes this residual scenario entirely. Therefore for Rabbi Shimon, whenever a *machavat* stands before us, no intent treating it as a *marcheshes* can reflect any conceivable reality. The *maaseh mochiach* — the act as its own testimony — operates absolutely in this domain.

## Gender/Species Cases

### Olah/Chatas Gender

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, עוֹלָה שֶׁשָּׁחַט לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת תְּרַצֶּה, דְּהַאי זָכָר וְהַאי נְקֵבָה, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא שְׂעִיר נָשִׂיא דְּזָכָר הוּא – לָא יְדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **If that is so,** that in a case where the manner of its preparation proves the identity of an offering the owner fulfills his obligation, then **a burnt offering that one slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering should effect acceptance.** His actions prove that it is a burnt offering, **as this,** a burnt offering, is always **male, and that,** a sin offering, is **female.** The Gemara answers: **Since there is** one sin offering, **the goat of** the ***Nasi*, which is male, it is unknown** whether this animal was a burnt offering or the sin offering of the *Nasi*, and its gender is not conclusive proof.

Having established that the relevant distinction is between impossible intent and merely-mistaken intent, the *gemara* now asks whether the same principle would apply in cases involving gender distinctions between sacrifices. An *olah* must be male, while a *chatas* is female. If one slaughters a female *chatas* with intent for an *olah*, should that intent be dismissed as self-evidently false — this is clearly not a male *olah* — and therefore not invalidating?

### Fat Tail Visibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר: ״לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת יָחִיד״, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? וְתוּ, חַטָּאת יָחִיד שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה תְּרַצֶּה, דְּחַטָּאת נְקֵבָה וְעוֹלָה זָכָר? מִיכַּסְּיָא בְּאַלְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: But in a case where one **said:** I am hereby slaughtering this animal **for the sake of a sin offering of an individual,** which is always female, **what can be said?** Since a male animal cannot be mistaken for such a sin offering, why doesn't this burnt offering satisfy the obligation of the owner? **And furthermore, a sin offering of an individual that one slaughtered for the sake of a burnt offering should effect acceptance, as** such **a sin offering** is always **female, and a burnt offering** is always **male.** The Gemara responds: It is difficult to discern whether a lamb is male or female, as its genitals are **covered by** its **tail.** Accordingly, its gender is not considered proof of the type of offering being sacrificed.

The *gemara*'s answer shifts the frame of analysis in a remarkable direction. Rather than asking whether the false intent reflects an objectively impossible scenario, it asks whether observers would perceive the intent as obviously false. The *gemara* says: *mechasyah b'alyah* — the tail covers it. If the animal is a sheep with a fat tail, the male or female identity of the animal is not visually apparent to bystanders. Therefore, the intent that a female *chatas* is being slaughtered as an *olah* is not, through the eyes of those present, obviously and manifestly wrong.

This is a striking move. The criterion has shifted from objective metaphysical possibility to social perception — would someone observing the act perceive it as clearly false? If observers would not immediately notice that the animal is female, the false intent retains a kind of apparent plausibility, and therefore remains meaningful enough to invalidate.

### Age Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֶּסַח שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ לְשֵׁם אָשָׁם לִירַצֵּי, דְּהַאי בֶּן שָׁנָה, וְהַאי בֶּן שְׁתֵּים, כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע, לָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A Paschal offering that one slaughtered for the sake of a guilt offering should effect acceptance, as this,** the Paschal offering, is **in its** first **year, and that,** a guilt offering, is **in its second** year. The Gemara answers: **Since there are** two guilt offerings that are brought from lambs in their first year, i.e., **the guilt offering of a nazirite** (see Numbers 6:12) **and the guilt offering of a leper** (see Leviticus 14:10), **it is not definitively** clear **to** an onlooker what type of offering this lamb is.

The *gemara* presses further: what if the animal is a goat, which does not have a fat tail? Then the gender would be apparent, and one might argue the intent is clearly false. The *gemara* responds with the more sweeping principle: *bein zachar l'nekeivah lav adateih de'inshi* — people simply do not pay close attention to whether animals are male or female. The standard, as it emerges, is not strict visual availability of information but rather ordinary human attentiveness. If people generally do not attend to a distinction, the intent exploiting that distinction is not dismissed as obviously wrong.

This represents a notable re-description of what it means for intent to be "self-evidently contradicted by reality." It is not a question of logical impossibility, as in the *machavat*/*marcheshes* case, nor even of what a perfectly attentive observer would see. It is a question of what people ordinarily notice.

### Species Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, בֵּין בֶּן שָׁנָה לְבֵין בֶּן שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים לָאו אַדַּעְתַּיְיהוּ דְאִינָשֵׁי, דְּאִיכָּא בֶּן שָׁנָה דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּבֶן שְׁתַּיִם, וְאִיכָּא בֶּן שְׁתַּיִם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כְּבֶן שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the difference in appearance **between** an animal that is **in its** first **year and** one that is **in its second year is not on people's minds,** i.e., this is not a clearly recognizable difference, **as there** can **be** an animal **in its** first **year that appears as** though it is **in its second** year, **and there** can **be** an animal **in its second** year **that appears as** though it is **in its** first **year.**

When the *gemara* considers a goat slaughtered as an *asham* — an offering that requires a sheep — it initially seems that this would be obviously wrong, since one has wool (*tzemer*) and the other hair (*se'ar*). The *gemara* dismisses even this:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂעִיר שֶׁשְּׁחָטוֹ לְשׁוּם אָשָׁם לִירַצֵּי, דְּהַאי צֶמֶר וְהַאי שֵׂיעָר, אָמְרִי: דִּיכְרָא אוּכָּמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **A goat** sin offering **that one slaughtered for the sake of a guilt offering should effect acceptance.** It is clearly not a guilt offering, **as this,** a guilt offering, is a ram, with white **wool, and that,** a goat, has black **hair.** The Gemara responds: People will **say** that this goat **is** actually **a black ram,** and they may indeed mistake it for a guilt offering.

people will simply say the black goat looks like a dark-colored ram. Finally, the *gemara* reaches a case where it concedes the difference is manifest:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵגֶל וּפַר שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן לְשׁוּם פֶּסַח וְאָשָׁם, לִירַצּוֹ, דְּעֵגֶל וּפַר בְּפֶסַח וְאָשָׁם לֵיכָּא! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי,
>
> **Translation:** **A calf or a bull that one slaughtered for the sake of** either **a Paschal offering or a guilt offering should effect acceptance.** It is clear that such animals cannot be either offering, **as a calf or bull** offering **is not** sacrificed either **as a Paschal offering or** as **a guilt offering.** The Gemara responds: **Yes,** it **is indeed so,** as according to Rabbi Shimon such offerings effect acceptance for their owners.

a calf or ox slaughtered for the sake of a *pesach* or *asham*. A cow is so plainly different from the sheep or goat used for those offerings that no reasonable observer could mistake them. In this case, *ein hachi nami* — indeed, the intent is meaningless, and Rabbi Shimon would hold that even by animal sacrifices, when the false intent is so patently obvious, we discard it.

This conclusion allows a partial vindication of the overall approach. Rabbi Shimon's statement that *shelo lishmah* by animal sacrifices is a problem refers to most animal sacrifices, where the distinctions are not readily visible. But even by animal sacrifices, if the difference is as manifest as that between a cow and a lamb, Rabbi Shimon would agree that the false intent is disregarded.

## Mincha vs. Zevach

### The Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי זְבָחִים? רוֹב זְבָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** does Rabbi Shimon mean when he says that meal offerings are not similar to **slaughtered offerings?** He means that they are not similar to **most slaughtered offerings.** There are, however, certain slaughtered offerings whose preparation, which is unique to that particular offering, is proof that it is that offering. In those instances, Rabbi Shimon maintains that the offerings fulfill the obligations of their respective owners despite the improper intent.

The *gemara* now turns to Rava's alternative resolution of the apparent contradiction in Rabbi Shimon's position. Rava frames the issue differently: *kan b'komatz mincha l'shum mincha, kan b'komatz mincha l'shum zevach* — the case where Rabbi Shimon says it does not invalidate is *mincha* with intent for another *mincha*; the case where he says it does invalidate is *mincha* with intent for a *zevach*, an animal sacrifice. The contradiction is not really between the two statements — they address different scenarios.

This approach is strikingly at odds with the previous one. According to Rava here, the more patently false an intent is, the more problematic it is — not less. A *mincha* with intent for a sheep *olah* is flagrantly wrong, and it invalidates precisely because it is so clearly a misidentification. If that is true, then why does *mincha* for *mincha* not invalidate?

### One Category Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנָּא ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין עָלֶיהָ״ קָאָמַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּמַחְשָׁבָה דְּלָא מִינַּכְרָא הִיא, וְתִיפְּסֵל, ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה״ – תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל הַמְּנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions how Rava can say that the verse "And this is the law of the meal offering" serves as the basis for the opinion of Rabbi Shimon: **But doesn't the *tanna*,** i.e., Rabbi Shimon, **say** that his reasoning is **that** for any particular meal offering, **its mode of preparation proves** what **it** is? The Gemara explains that **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying: Even though** sacrificing a meal offering for the sake of a different meal offering is a case **where it is not recognizable** that the **intention** fits the offering, i.e., the intent does not match the rites being performed, **and** the offering **should** therefore **be disqualified,** the verse states: **"And this is the law of the meal offering,"** which teaches that there is **one law for all the meal offerings.**

Rava's answer: *vezos toras hamincha, torah achas lechol haminchos* — because the Torah uses a unifying formulation for all *minchas*, treating them as one fundamental category. The verse "This is the law of the *mincha*" establishes that all *minchas* are governed by a single legal identity. Therefore, *mincha* A with intent for *mincha* B is not considered *shelo lishma* at all — they are the same category of offering, just as slaughtering a sheep *olah* while thinking of it as a goat *olah* would not invalidate, since both are *olah*. The type of *mincha* — whether *machavat* or *marcheshes*, fried or baked — is an internal subdivision, not a separate identity.

By contrast, a *mincha* with intent for a *zevach* clearly crosses the categorical boundary. *Vezos toras hamincha* covers only *minchas*; *zevach* is a fundamentally different category and receives no such unifying verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי אֲבָל בִּזְבָחִים אֵינוֹ כֵן? אַף עַל גַּב דִּשְׁחִיטָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן, ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה וְזֶבַח״ לָא כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** does Rabbi Shimon mean when he says: **But with regard to slaughtered offerings it is not so?** This clause indicates that the similarity between the rites of various slaughtered offerings is a reason why they should not be valid, whereas according to the above reasoning the opposite is so.

The *gemara* notes that this reading requires a creative re-understanding of the *baraisa*, which phrases its explanation in terms of *maaseh mochiach aleha* — the act proves what it is. On a straightforward reading, that phrase gives the reason why *mincha* for *mincha* is acceptable: it is obviously wrong, and therefore meaningless. Rava's reading inverts this: the phrase should be understood as an *af al gav* — although the intent is obviously wrong and might therefore seem more problematic, it is still valid because all *minchas* are one category. Similarly, although *olah* for *shelamim* is not obviously wrong — the act does not prove it is not a *shelamim*, since both involve the same basic *shechita* — it is nonetheless invalid because *olah* and *shelamim* belong to different categories, and no unifying verse equates them.

This is admittedly a creative reread of the *baraisa*, flipping what appears to be the *reason* for each ruling into an *even-though* that is overridden by the deeper logic.

### Chatas Implications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, חַטַּאת חֵלֶב שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת דָּם, לְשׁוּם חַטַּאת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, לְשׁוּם חַטַּאת נָזִיר, לְשׁוּם חַטַּאת מְצוֹרָע – תְּהֵא כְּשֵׁירָה וּתְרַצֶּה, דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַחַטָּאת״, תּוֹרָה אַחַת לְכׇל חַטָּאוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **If that is so,** that the phrase "And this is the law of the meal offering" teaches that a meal offering is not disqualified despite the improper intent for a different meal offering, then analogously, **a sin offering** brought to atone for eating forbidden **fat that one slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering** to atone for drinking **blood,** or **for the sake of a sin offering** to atone for **idol worship,** or **for the sake of a sin offering of a nazirite,** or **for the sake of a sin offering of a leper should be valid and effect acceptance. As the Merciful One states: "And this is the law of the sin offering"** (Leviticus 6:18), from which it should be derived that there is **one law for all sin offerings.**

This logic raises a natural question. If all *minchas* are one category by virtue of *vezos toras hamincha*, what of *chatas*? The Torah uses parallel language: *vezos toras hachatas, torah achas l'chol hachataos* — this is the law of the *chatas*, one law for all *chataos*. By the same token, should not *chatas* A with intent for *chatas* B be permitted? Should not a *chatas chelev* slaughtered with intent for a *chatas stam* or a *chatas avodas kochavim* be valid?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הָכִי נָמֵי, לְרַבָּנַן הָא אָמַר: חַטַּאת חֵלֶב שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת דָּם, לְשׁוּם חַטַּאת עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – כְּשֵׁירָה, לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת נָזִיר מְצוֹרָע – פְּסוּלָה, דְּהָנֵי עוֹלוֹת בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** such a sin offering is **indeed** valid and effects acceptance for its owner. The Gemara notes: **According to the Rabbis,** Rava **says: A sin offering** brought to atone for eating forbidden **fat that one slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering** to atone for drinking **blood** or **for the sake of a sin offering** to atone for **idol worship** is **valid.** But if he slaughtered it **for the sake of a sin offering of a nazirite** or for the sake of a sin offering of **a leper** it is **disqualified, as** with regard to **these** offerings, **there are burnt offerings** brought together **with them.**

The *gemara* affirms: *le'Rabbi Shimon*, yes, this would indeed be valid. Rava himself rules accordingly — a *chatas chelev* slaughtered as a *chatas stam* is *kesheira*. The unifying principle applies equally.

However, the *gemara* introduces a limit. If one slaughters a *chatas* with intent for a *chatas nazir* or a *chatas metzora*, that would invalidate. The reason given is *d'hanei olos bahadeihu ninhu* — those *chataos* are accompanied by *olos*, making them a distinct category of offering. Alternatively, those *chataos* address matters of status rather than sin, rendering them a different type of *korban* entirely. Either way, the unifying verse *toras hachatas* does not extend to bring them under the same umbrella.

This analysis is challenged by Rav Acha brei d'Rava, who argues that the verse *v'shachat osah l'chatas* — and he shall slaughter it as a *chatas* — requires specificity, identifying the *chatas* with its particular sin. This verse counterbalances *vezos toras hachatas* and may lead us to say that even for Rabbi Shimon, a *chatas chelev* slaughtered for a *chatas stam* is invalidated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא מַתְנֵי לֵיהּ לְכוּלְּהוּ לִפְסוּלָא, ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתָהּ לְחַטָּאת״ – לְשֵׁם אוֹתוֹ חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, teaches** that Rava holds that the *halakha* in **all of these** cases is **disqualification** of the sin offering. The reason is that the verse states: **"And slaughter it for a sin offering"** (Leviticus 4:33). The word "it" indicates that it must be sacrificed **for the sake of that** particular **sin offering,** and it may not be slaughtered for the sake of a different one.

Whether Rava's position or Rav Acha's reflects the correct reading of Rabbi Shimon remains a point of dispute — and indeed, the *gemara* acknowledges that the application of these principles to *chatas* is less settled than their application to *mincha*, where the categories are more generic and the *psukim* less specific in their demands.

## Rav Ashi's View

### Vessel vs. Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – בְּקוֹמֵץ מִנְחַת מַחֲבַת לְשׁוּם מַרְחֶשֶׁת, כָּאן – בְּקוֹמֵץ מִנְחַת מַחֲבַת לְשׁוּם מִנְחַת מַרְחֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** that it is **not difficult. Here,** where Rabbi Shimon says that the meal offering is fit and fulfills the obligation of the owner, he is referring to a case **where** one states that he is **removing a handful** from **a pan meal offering for the sake of a deep pan,** i.e., he mentions only the vessel and not the offering. **There, where** it does not fulfill the owner's obligation, **he** states that he is **removing a handful** from **a pan meal offering for the sake of a deep-pan meal offering.**

Rav Ashi offers a third approach to reconciling Rabbi Shimon's statements. *Lo kashya*, he says — there is no difficulty. The two cases that Rabbi Shimon addresses differ not in the obviousness of the false intent, nor in whether one category or two are involved, but in what the intent is *about*. When one performs *kemitza* from a *minchas machavat* while mentally treating it as a *marcheshes*, the intent concerns the *vessel* — the pan — not the identity of the *korban*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחַת מַחֲבַת לְשׁוּם מַרְחֶשֶׁת – בְּמָנָא קָא מְחַשֵּׁב, וּמַחְשָׁבָה בְּמָנָא לָא פָּסְלָה; מִנְחַת מַחֲבַת לְשׁוּם מִנְחַת מַרְחֶשֶׁת – בְּמִנְחָה דְּפָסְלָה בָּהּ מַחְשָׁבָה קָא מְחַשֵּׁב.
>
> **Translation:** When one bringing a pan meal offering states that he is removing a handful from **a pan meal offering for the sake of a deep pan, he has intent** only **with regard to** the type of **vessel, and intention with regard to** the type of **vessel does not disqualify** offerings, as he is not sacrificing the vessel, and therefore the owner's obligation is fulfilled. By contrast, when he states that he is removing a handful from **a pan meal offering for the sake of a deep-pan meal offering, he has intent with regard to** the type of **meal offering, which** improper **intention does disqualify.** Consequently, the owner's obligation is not fulfilled.

*Machshava bimana lo pasla* — an intent about the vessel does not invalidate. By contrast, when one explicitly identifies the *mincha* as a *mincha* of a different type, one is reconstituting the identity of the *korban* itself, and that does invalidate.

### Intent Classification

According to this reading, the question of whether the intent is obviously false or not is irrelevant to Rabbi Shimon's principle. The relevant question is structural: is this a *machshava* about the *kelim* or about the identity of the *zevach*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנָּא ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּעֲשֶׂיהָ מוֹכִיחִין עָלֶיהָ״ קָאָמַר? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּמַחְשָׁבָה מִינְּכַר הִיא, תִּיפְּסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions how Rav Ashi can maintain that this is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon: **But doesn't the *tanna*,** i.e., Rabbi Shimon, **say** that his reasoning is **that** for any particular meal offering, **its mode of preparation proves** what **it** is? The Gemara explains that **this** is what Rabbi Shimon **is saying: Even though** when he removes a handful from a pan meal offering explicitly for the sake of a deep pan, it is a case where the falseness of the **intention is recognizable** and the offering **should** therefore **be disqualified,** it is nevertheless valid and fulfills the owner's obligation, as intentions with regard to the vessel are irrelevant.

The *gemara* again notes that the *baraisa* phrases its explanation in terms of *maaseh mochiach aleha*, and applies this reading as another *af al gav*: although the intent is plainly visible as wrong — which, if anything, should make it more disqualifying — it does not invalidate because the *machshava* is only about the vessel. And for animal sacrifices, although the act of *shechita* is the same for all and it is therefore not obviously wrong, the intent addresses the identity of the *korban*, which does invalidate.

### Challenges to Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: חֲרֵבָה מִשּׁוּם בְּלוּלָה, אַמַּאי אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְשׁוּם בִּילָּה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** If this is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon, then with regard to a case where one removes a handful from **a dry** meal offering **for the sake of** a meal offering **mixed** with oil, **why did Rabbi Shimon say** that it fulfills the owner's obligation? Since his intent concerns the meal offering itself, it should disqualify the offering. Rav Ashi **said to him:** When Rabbi Shimon says that such a meal offering fulfills the owner's obligation he is referring to a case where one removes a handful **merely for the sake of mixing,** not for the sake of a meal offering mixed with oil.

Rav Acha brei d'Rava challenges this framework directly. The case of *chareiva mishum belulah* — a dry *mincha* treated with intent as a wet (oil-mixed) one — does not seem to be about a vessel at all. A *minchas belulah* and a *minchas chareiva* differ not in the pan used but in whether oil is mixed in. So the intent that a dry *mincha* is a wet one is about the identity of the *mincha*, not the vessel. By Rav Ashi's logic, this should invalidate — yet Rabbi Shimon holds it does not.

Rav Ashi replies: *mishum bilah b'alma* — when one thinks of the dry *mincha* as something wet, one is not calling it a *minchas belulah*. One is merely having a vague mental association with wetness, not explicitly renaming the offering. Since *belulah* in this context is a descriptive adjective (*belulah bashemen* — mixed with oil) and not the proper name of a separate category of *korban*, saying *l'shem belulah* is not truly assigning it the identity of a different *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לְשׁוּם שְׁלָמִים נָמֵי, לְשׁוּם שְׁלָמִים בְּעָלְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then when one slaughters a burnt offering **for the sake of a peace offering as well,** one can say that his intent is **merely for the sake of peace [*shelamim*]** between himself and God. Why then does Rabbi Shimon say that this burnt offering does not fulfill the obligation of the owner?

Rav Acha pushes back with a reductio: if that reasoning holds, then slaughtering a *chatas* with intent *l'shem shelamim* should also be disregarded — one could say the intent is merely for *shalmi b'alma*, a generic sense of peace, not specifically for the *korban shelamim*. Rav Ashi responds that *shelamim* is the actual proper name of a *korban* — the Torah uses it explicitly — whereas *belulah* is only an adjective.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם זֶבַח גּוּפֵהּ אִיקְּרִי שְׁלָמִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״הַמַּקְרִיב אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים״, ״הַזֹּרֵק אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים״. הָכָא מִנְחָה גּוּפַהּ מִי אִיקְּרַי בְּלוּלָה? ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה בְלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן״ כְּתִיב, ״בְּלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן״ אִיקְּרַי, ״בְּלוּלָה״ סְתָמָא לָא אִיקְּרַי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this challenge: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** the **offering itself is called *shelamim*, as it is written: "Who sacrifices the blood of the peace offering [*shelamim*]"** (Leviticus 7:33), and it is also written: **"Who sprinkles the blood of the peace offering"** (Leviticus 7:14). **Here,** by contrast, **is a meal offering itself called mixed?** It **is written: "And every meal offering, mixed with oil"** (Leviticus 7:10), indicating that there is a meal offering that **is called: Mixed with oil,** but **it is not simply called: Mixed.** One's intent to sacrifice a dry meal offering for the sake of mixing is therefore not a reference to the offering itself.

*Minchas belulah* is a description; *shelamim* is a name. Only intending the *korban's* name as a different *korban's* name constitutes the kind of *machshava* that invalidates. A descriptive term like *belulah* does not rise to that level unless the full name *minchas belulah* is invoked.

## Unresolved Debate

### Rabba Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלְּהוּ כְּרַבָּה לָא אָמְרִי, דְּאַדְּרַבָּה, מַחְשָׁבָה דְּמִנַּכְרָא פְּסַל רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **All** the other Sages, i.e., Rava and Rav Ashi, **do not say as Rabba** did in resolving the contradiction, as they do not accept his reasoning, claiming **that on the contrary, the Merciful One disqualifies recognizably** false **intent.**

The *gemara* now reviews why each of the three resolutions ultimately remains incomplete, with no one approach fully adopted. Rabba's reading — the simplest and most natural reading of Rabbi Shimon's language — holds that when intent is manifestly false it is not a problem, and when it is plausibly conceivable it is. This fits the plain meaning of the *baraisa*. Yet Abaye raised a challenge: Rabbi Shimon himself links *mincha* to *chatas* and *asham* through the formulation *kodesh kodashim hi k'chatas uk'asham*, implying that all *machshavot* — even those that would be obviously false — should operate the same way across *korbanos*. This challenge prevented unqualified acceptance of Rabba's position.

### Rava Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרָבָא לָא אָמְרִי, ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת״ לָא מַשְׁמַע לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: Rava resolved the contradiction by saying that a meal offering from which a handful was removed for the sake of another meal offering effects acceptance for the owner, as the verse states: "And this is the law of the meal offering" (Leviticus 6:7), indicating that there is one law for all meal offerings, whereas a meal offering from which a handful was removed for the sake of an animal offering does not effect acceptance. Rabba and Rav Ashi **did not say as Rava** did in resolving the contradiction, as the verse that states: **"And this is the law** of the meal offering," **does not indicate to them** that there should be one law for all meal offerings.

Rava's approach — that all *minchas* are one category by virtue of *vezos toras hamincha* — is elegant and explains much, but it carries an implication the *gemara* finds difficult to absorb: if *vezos toras hamincha* collapses all *minchas* into one, then *vezos toras hachatas* should equally collapse all *chataos* into one. Yet not all *chataos* are treated identically, and the verse *oshachat osah l'chatas* introduces specificity that seems to resist the unifying move. The objection from *vezos toras hachatas* is what prevents full adoption of Rava's approach.

### Ashi Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַב אָשֵׁי לָא אָמְרִי, מִשּׁוּם קוּשְׁיָא דְּרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, Rav Asi **did not resolve** the dilemma **in accordance with** the resolution stated by **Rav Ashi,** that there is a distinction between one who removes the handful of a meal offering prepared in one vessel for the sake of a different vessel, and one who removes it for the sake of a meal offering prepared in a different vessel, **because of the difficulty** posed by **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava.** This difficulty concerns a case where one removes the handful of a dry meal offering for the sake of one mixed with oil; Rabbi Shimon holds that such a meal offering is valid despite the fact that the person's intent referred to the meal offering itself, not the vessel.

Rav Ashi's distinction between intent about vessels and intent about identity is similarly left incomplete, defeated by the question of Rav Acha brei d'Rava: the *chareiva mishum belulah* case cannot comfortably be classified as mere vessel-intent, and Rav Ashi's technical answer — that *belulah* is only an adjective, not a name — is found insufficient to ground the whole framework.

### Uncertain Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילְּתָא דִּפְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרַבָּה לְהַאי גִּיסָא, וּלְרָבָא לְהַאי גִּיסָא, מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא. דְּבָעֵי רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא מֵרַבִּי אַסִּי: מִנְחָה לְשׁוּם זֶבַח
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **The matter that is obvious to Rabba in one way,** i.e., that with regard to meal offerings recognizably false intention is disregarded, but when the false intention is not recognizable the offering does not fulfill the owner's obligation, **and** that is obvious **to Rava in the other way,** i.e., that in cases of recognizably false intention the offering should not fulfill the owner's obligation, **is a dilemma for Rav Hoshaya. As Rav Hoshaya raises a dilemma, and some say** that **Rav Hoshaya raises** this **dilemma before Rav Asi:** In the case of one who sacrifices **a meal offering for the sake of a slaughtered offering,**

The *gemara* closes with the statement of Rav Ashi himself, who, when pressed on the question of what Rabbi Shimon's true reasoning is, responds: *kelumi ganu sof da'ato d'Rabbi Shimon?* — have we truly reached the bottom of Rabbi Shimon's reasoning? The honest answer is that we have not. Three coherent readings have been offered. Rabba reads Rabbi Shimon as making an epistemological claim: manifestly false intent is not really intent. Rava reads him as making a categorical claim: *minchas* are one category, and intra-category variations are not *shelo lishmah*. Rav Ashi reads him as making a structural claim: only intent that reassigns the identity of the *korban* by name is halakhically operative. Each reading has textual support, each has logical force, and each faces challenges that the *gemara* declines to fully answer. The daf concludes with Rabbi Shimon's reasoning genuinely undetermined.
