# Makkot 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overview: Lashes

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״פְּאַת רֹאשׁוֹ״ – סוֹף רֹאשׁוֹ, וְאֵיזֶהוּ סוֹף רֹאשׁוֹ – זֶה הַמַּשְׁוֶה צְדָעָיו לַאֲחוֹרֵי אׇזְנוֹ וּלְפַדַּחְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: **The edge of his head** is **the extremity of his head. And what is the extremity of his head? This is** a reference to **one who levels** the hairline of **his temples to** the hairline **behind his ear and to** the hairline of **his forehead.** There is no hair behind the ears or on the forehead. One who removes the hair from the temples so that they are like those areas violates the prohibition against rounding the edges of his head.

The daf before us continues the tractate's sustained engagement with a cluster of bodily prohibitions that incur *malkot*: making a bald spot on the head in mourning, cutting the *peot* (sideburns), destroying the corners of the beard, incisions in the flesh, and tattooing. The Mishnah enumerates all of these, and the Gemara on this daf works through them with considerable technical precision, examining the anatomical definitions, the identity of the liable parties, the required implements, and the relationship between a single physical act and multiple distinct transgressions.

### Makif & Peot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: אֶחָד הַמַּקִּיף וְאֶחָד הַנִּיקָּף – לוֹקֶה. אָמַר לֵיהּ: מַאן דְּאָכֵיל תַּמְרֵי בְּאַרְבֵּילָא לָקֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **The *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Ḥisda: Both one who rounds** the edges of his head **and one** for **whom** the edges of his head **are rounded** are **flogged.** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: Is one who eats dates** that are **in a sieve [*arbeila*] flogged?** By analogy, if a person rounded the edges of another's head, why should the person whose head was rounded receive lashes? He performed no action.

The Torah's prohibition against cutting the sideburns uses the term *makif* — literally "one who circles" or "cuts around." This is in contrast to the prohibition concerning the beard, which uses the word *mashchit* ("destroy"), a distinction that turns out to carry significant halachic weight. Because the beard prohibition employs the language of *hashchata*, the manner of cutting becomes central: only a method that constitutes destruction — reaching the root — triggers liability. The sideburn prohibition, by contrast, uses only *makif*, raising the question of what specific act is targeted.

According to Rashi and the *Rivan* (the commentary that serves as our primary *rishon* for this portion of the tractate), *makif* refers specifically to making a straight line — essentially leveling off the entire section of hair from one anatomical boundary to another, cutting it completely away. It is not merely any trimming of the area; it is the wholesale removal of that defined region. Several *rishonim*, including Rambam, understand the prohibition differently — Rambam speaks of forty hairs — but the basic contour of Rashi's reading is that the violation requires the complete excision of the *pe'ah*.

### Sof Rosho Definition

The Gemara opens with a *Baraita*: *at rosho sof rosho* — "the end of the head." The Gemara asks: what is *sof rosho*? The answer: *ze hamoshve tzadav le-achorei ozno* — this is the one who makes the temples (*tzadaav*) level with the back of the ear. The Gemara then adds: *ule-padachto le-achorei ozno* — and also to his forehead and the back of the ear. The picture that emerges is a straight line drawn from the forehead, along the temple, to the back of the ear — an entire section swept away in one motion.

This definition aligns well with Rashi's reading. The practical implication is that the prohibition on *peot ha-rosh* is triggered by that complete removal, not by trimming incrementally. It is worth noting that the prevailing practice in many communities is considerably stricter than even this reading of the Gemara would require, a reflection of layers of custom and stringency that have accumulated over the centuries.

## Actor vs Recipient

### Echad Hamakif

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר לָךְ: מַנִּי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה לוֹקִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** In response to one **who says to you: Who is** the *tanna* of the *baraita*? Say that **it is Rabbi Yehuda, who says:** In the case of **a prohibition that does not involve an action, one is flogged for its** violation.

A *Tanna* taught before Rav Chisda: *echad ha-makif ve-echad ha-nikaf lokeh* — both the one who performs the haircut and the one who receives it are liable for *malkot*. This is a striking statement. The Torah says *lo takifu* — "do not make circuits" — which appears to be directed at the one performing the act. The one sitting passively while another cuts around his sideburns seems, on a plain reading, to be entirely outside the scope of that prohibition.

Rav Chisda's reaction captures this intuition vividly. He responds with an Aramaic idiom: *man de-achil tamrei be-arvilei loki?* — "Is someone who eats dates through a sieve going to get lashes?" The image is of someone who accomplishes nothing at all. The idiom cuts to two related but distinct issues: first, whether the *nikaf* transgresses any prohibition at all; and second, even if he does transgress, whether his passivity disqualifies him from *malkot*, given the principle that a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — a prohibition violated without a physical act — does not ordinarily incur lashes.

### Lav She'ein Bo

The Gemara identifies the position that the *nikaf* receives lashes as that of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh lokin alav* — even a passive violation incurs *malkot*. What is notable here is that nobody in the Gemara appears to contest the underlying transgression of the *nikaf*; the debate is only over whether he is liable for lashes. The existence of the prohibition as applied to the recipient seems to be taken as established, the question being solely whether the passive nature of his participation shields him from corporal punishment.

### Lo Takifu & Mesayeia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אוֹמֵר: בְּמַקִּיף לְעַצְמוֹ, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל. רַב אָשֵׁי אוֹמֵר: בִּמְסַיֵּיעַ. וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** This *baraita* can be explained as referring **to one who rounds** the edges of his head **for himself,** and he is liable both for rounding the edges of his head and for having the edges of his head rounded; **and everyone agrees** that he is flogged because he performed an action. **Rav Ashi says:** This *baraita* can be explained as referring to a case **where** the one for whom the edges of his head are being rounded **assists** the person rounding his head, by repositioning his head to facilitate that rounding, **and everyone agrees** that he is flogged.

Three resolutions are offered. First, the *Rivan* suggests that the plural form *lo takifu* — "you [plural] shall not circuit" — encompasses both parties: the one doing the cutting and the one submitting to it. Second, Rava proposes: *b'makif l'atzmo* — when a person cuts his own sideburns, he is simultaneously both the *makif* and the *nikaf*, and in that case, by universal agreement (*divrei ha-kol*), he is liable under both aspects of the prohibition and would receive two sets of lashes.

Third — and this is Rav Ashi's solution, which also aligns with what emerges naturally from the situation — the *nikaf* is liable because he is a *mesayei'a*: he actively assists in the act. When the barber says to tilt the head, hold steady, or position in a certain way, and the recipient complies, that participation constitutes sufficient action to make him an active transgressor and render him liable for *malkot*.

This generates an important conceptual distinction. In our case, everyone already agrees that the *nikaf* is a transgressor; the *mesayei'a* principle is invoked to establish that his participation is sufficiently active to warrant lashes. That is categorically different from a case where a person was not a transgressor to begin with and one wishes to argue that their *mesayei'a* makes them one. The *poskim* debate the latter scenario separately. One contemporary application involves a woman who arrives at the *mikvah* on Friday night having forgotten to cut her nails — in a community where the practice required complete cutting — and the question of whether she may assist a non-Jew in doing so on *Shabbos*. Her *mesayei'a* in holding her hand in position raises the question of how much participation is needed to transform her from a bystander into a transgressor. The *sugya* here is directly relevant, though the categories must be handled with care.

## Beard Cutting

### Five Corners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּיב עַל הָרֹאשׁ. מַחְוֵי רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בֵּין פִּירְקֵי רֵישָׁא. וְעַל הַזָּקָן, שְׁתַּיִם מִכָּאן וּשְׁתַּיִם מִכָּאן וְאַחַת מִלְּמַטָּה. מַחְוֵי רַב שֵׁשֶׁת בֵּין פִּירְקֵי דִּיקְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And for** rounding the edges of his **head,** one is **liable** to receive two sets of lashes, one from here, the hair adjacent to one ear, and one from there, the hair adjacent to the other ear. The Gemara relates that when he taught this mishna, **Rav Sheshet** would **gesture** toward his temple, which is the point **between the** two **parts of the head,** the front and back. Similarly, with regard to the *halakha* in the mishna: **For** marring the edges of his **beard** there are **two** edges **from here, and two from there, and one from below, Rav Sheshet** would **gesture** toward the point **between the parts of the beard.**

The Mishnah refers to *pe'at ha-zakan* — the corners of the beard — and the Gemara specifies that there are five such corners: two on each side of the face and one below. The *Rivan* explains the anatomical picture as follows: the *pe'ah* of the head runs from the bone near the ear to the temple, while the *pe'at ha-zakan* runs from that same bone downward along the jaw to the chin, and then again symmetrically on the other side, together with the strip below. The boundary point is thus the bone in the middle of the ear — from that bone upward defines the *peot ha-rosh*, and from that bone downward defines the *pe'at ha-zakan*.

A point worth noting is that the beard prohibition does not encompass the mustache. It appears, at least tentatively, that a straight razor may be used on the mustache without transgressing the prohibition — though this is not a common practice. The Amish custom of growing the beard while trimming the mustache is presumably rooted in exactly this distinction.

## Flesh Incisions

### Sherit for Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְשֶׂרֶט״ – יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ שָׂרַט עַל בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁנָּפַל וְעַל סְפִינָתוֹ שֶׁטָּבְעָה בַּיָּם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְנֶפֶשׁ״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הַמֵּת בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a halakhic midrash on the verse: "And an incision for the soul you shall not place in your flesh" (Leviticus 19:28). Had the verse stated only: **"And an incision,"** one **might** have thought that this prohibition applies to any incision cut in sorrow, **even** if he **cut** an incision in sorrow **over his house that collapsed, or over his ship that sunk at sea.** Therefore, **the verse states: "For the soul,"** from which it is derived that **he is liable** for an incision in mourning **only for the dead alone.**

The Gemara cites a *Baraita* on *ve-saret la-nefesh* — the prohibition against cutting the flesh. One might have thought that any act of self-cutting made in grief — even over a collapsed house or a sunken ship — would be prohibited. The *Baraita* teaches: *talmud lomar la-nefesh* — the verse says "for a soul," indicating that liability applies only when the incision is made as a mourning rite for one who has died. An incision expressing anguish over financial loss or property is not included.

### One Act, Multiple Dead

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן לַמְשָׂרֵט חָמֵשׁ שְׂרִיטוֹת עַל מֵת אֶחָד שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְשֶׂרֶט״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל שְׂרִיטָה וּשְׂרִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **that one who cuts five incisions** in mourning **over one dead** person **is liable for each and every** incision? **The verse states: "And an incision" to render** him **liable for each and every incision.**

The *Baraita* continues: if one makes five separate incisions for a single dead person, he is liable for each one — *talmud lomar ve-saret*, the use of the noun form teaching that each individual incision is counted separately. The added implication, as with the parallel case of the five bald spots mentioned earlier in the tractate, is that if the five incisions are made simultaneously — one can imagine a multi-pronged implement doing all five at once — that too counts as five separate violations in a single act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לַמְשָׂרֵט שְׂרִיטָה אַחַת עַל חֲמִשָּׁה מֵתִים, שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לְנֶפֶשׁ״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל נֶפֶשׁ וָנֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: From where** is it derived **concerning one who cuts one incision** in mourning **over five dead** people, **that he is liable for each and every** person for whom he cut that incision? **The verse states: "For the soul," to render** him **liable for each and every dead** person.

Rabbi Yose adds a further dimension: if one makes a single incision on behalf of five different dead persons, he is liable five times — *l'chayev al kol nefesh va-nefesh*, one violation for each soul. This is the ruling in the Mishnah, though the Gemara notes that it represents Rabbi Yose's position rather than the anonymous, uncontested view.

### Sherita vs Gedida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: שְׂרִיטָה וּגְדִידָה אַחַת הִיא, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״לְמֵת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yosei derives that *halakha* from a different source, as **Rabbi Yosei holds** that **an incision** over the dead **and a laceration** over the dead **are one** transgression, **and it is written there,** with regard to laceration: "You shall neither lacerate yourselves, nor place a bald spot between your eyes **for the dead"** (Deuteronomy 14:1), indicating that one is liable for cutting oneself in mourning only over a dead person. Therefore he can derive from the term "for the soul" that one is liable for each and every soul over whom he cut the incision.

The Gemara now addresses the relationship between *sherita* (incision by hand) and *gedida* (incision with a tool, such as a knife). The general assumption is that they are separate acts described by separate verses: *ve-saret la-nefesh* covers hand-incision, and *lo tisgodedu* (from Deuteronomy) covers tool-incision. Rabbi Yose, however, holds that *sherita* and *gedida* are one and the same act — the two words simply describe the same prohibition without distinguishing by method. The practical consequence of his view is that the verse *la-nefesh* and the word *la-met* (found in the context of *lo tisgodedu*) are then redundant with each other, and that redundancy is what Rabbi Yose uses to derive that a single incision over multiple dead persons is itself multiple violations.

### Samuel's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמְשָׂרֵט בִּכְלִי – חַיָּיב. מֵיתִיבִי: שְׂרִיטָה וּגְדִידָה אַחַת הִיא, אֶלָּא שֶׁשְּׂרִיטָה בַּיָּד וּגְדִידָה בִּכְלִי! הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, the Gemara cites that which **Shmuel says: One who cuts [*mesaret*]** his flesh **with a utensil** in mourning over the dead is **liable.** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **An incision [*serita*] and a laceration are one** transgression, as they are both performed over the dead; **but** the difference is that **an incision is** cut **by hand,** i.e., with one's fingernails, while **a laceration is** cut **with a utensil.** Apparently, cutting an incision is not accomplished with a utensil. The Gemara answers: Shmuel **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who holds that an incision and a laceration are synonymous in every sense.

Shmuel states: *ha-masaret bi-khli chayav* — one who makes an incision *using a tool* is liable, and he uses the word *masaret* (*sherita*) rather than *gedida*. This implies that Shmuel holds like Rabbi Yose, that *sherita* encompasses both hand and implement, and the two terms are not distinguishing by method. The Gemara raises an objection from a *Baraita* that explicitly states *srita beyad u-gedida bi-khli* — that *sherita* is by hand and *gedida* is by instrument — but resolves it: Shmuel follows the position of Rabbi Yose.

## Avoda Zara Context

### Pagan Ritual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַל מֵת, בֵּין בַּיָּד בֵּין בִּכְלִי – חַיָּיב, עַל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, בַּיָּד – חַיָּיב, בִּכְלִי – פָּטוּר. וְהָא אִיפְּכָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּתְגֹּדְדוּ כְּמִשְׁפָּטָם בַּחֲרָבוֹת וּבָרְמָחִים״! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: בַּיָּד – פָּטוּר, בִּכְלִי – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **A *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** One who cuts an incision **over a dead** person, **whether** he did so **by hand or with a utensil,** is **liable.** Concerning one who cuts an incision **for idolatry,** if he does so **by hand,** he is **liable,** but if he does so **with a utensil,** he is **exempt.** Rabbi Yoḥanan was puzzled by this *baraita*: **But isn't the reverse written** with regard to the priests of Baal: **"And they lacerated themselves in accordance with their custom, with swords and lances"** (I Kings 18:28), indicating that customarily laceration is with a utensil? Rabbi Yoḥanan instructed the *tanna*: **Rather,** emend the *baraita* and **say:** Concerning one who cuts an incision for idolatry, if he does so **by hand,** he is **exempt;** but if he does so **with a utensil,** he is **liable.**

A *Tanna* taught before Rabbi Yochanan: for a dead person, whether the incision is by hand or by tool, one is liable; for *avodah zarah*, liability differs by method. Making incisions in the flesh was a recognized pagan ritual — the paradigmatic Talmudic example being the prophets of Baal on Har Carmel who *va-yisgodedu ke-mishpatam, ba-charavot u-va-ramachim* — they made incisions as was their practice, with swords and spears. That is an instrument, a *kli*.

The Gemara therefore asks: but the verse shows pagan incisions being done with tools — so how can the rule say that for *avodah zarah* the tool is exempt? The Gemara reverses the formulation: by hand is exempt; by tool, one is liable.

### Rambam on Paganism

The Rambam in *Moreh Nevukhim* argues that many of the prohibitions discussed throughout this passage — bald spots, flesh incisions, cutting the beard, and, as we will see, tattooing — are rooted in their association with pagan ritual practice. The Torah is not necessarily prohibiting excessive mourning per se; rather, these were the actual forms that pagan mourning and pagan worship took, and the Torah targets them for that reason. This framing has implications for how one analyzes the boundaries of each prohibition.

### Hand vs Tool

The resulting legal map is as follows: for incisions made over the dead, liability applies whether the incision is made by hand or with a tool. For incisions made in the context of *avodah zarah*, liability applies when made with a tool, not by hand. This is the inverse of what one might initially expect. The Tosafot explain the *avodah zarah* case in terms of the category of *avodah she-lo ke-darkhah* — worship performed in a manner atypical for a given idol, which incurs *malkot* without the death penalty. Since making incisions was so widespread a feature of pagan worship generally, it constitutes a recognizable act of adulation toward any idol, even one not normally worshipped this way. By contrast, an act that no one would recognize as reverence — even if the worshiper intends it as such — would not qualify. The line is whether an outside observer would identify it as an act of *kavod* to a deity.

## Beard Cutting…

### Rabbi Eliezer Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם נְטָלָן וְכוּ׳. קָסָבַר: חַד לָאו הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Eliezer says: If he removed** the hair on all the edges of his beard in one action, he is liable to receive only one set of lashes. The Gemara clarifies: **He maintains** that the prohibition of marring the edges of the beard **is a single prohibition,** and therefore, one is not liable for marring each edge.

Returning to the beard: Rabbi Eliezer holds that if one removes all five corners of the beard simultaneously, he is liable only once — *kasavar chad lav hu*, it is one prohibition. The Sages apparently count each corner as a separate prohibition. The precise nature of this debate is somewhat puzzling, since one would think that separate warnings between acts would result in separate liability in any case, and a single warning would not generate multiple sets of *malkot* regardless. The debate may be most meaningful in the case of truly simultaneous removal of all five corners at once — analogous to the earlier case of the bald spots, where one act could generate multiple violations if the five spots were made in a single motion.

### Razor vs Scissors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּטְּלֶנּוּ בְּתַעַר. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּפְאַת זְקָנָם לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ גַּלָּחוֹ בְּמִסְפָּרַיִם יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא תַשְׁחִית״. אִי ״לֹא תַשְׁחִית״, יָכוֹל אִם לִקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט וּרְהִיטָנִי יְהֵא חַיָּיב? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יְגַלֵּחוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And one is liable** for marring the edges of his beard **only if he removes** the hair **with a razor. The Sages taught** a halakhic midrash: The verse states: **"And the edge of their beard they may not shave"** (Leviticus 21:5). One **might** have thought that for any manner of shaving, **even if he shaved** the beard **with scissors, he would be liable;** therefore, **the verse states: "You may not mar** the edge of your beard" (Leviticus 19:27), indicating that one is liable only for shaving that destroys the hair from the root, which is not the case with scissors. **If** the verse had stated only: **"You may not mar,"** one **might** have thought that even **if he removed** the hair **with tweezers or with a plane** he **would be liable** to receive lashes, as they destroy the hair from its roots; therefore, **the verse states: "They may not shave,"** indicating that only hair removal by means of shaving is prohibited, and that is not accomplished with a tweezers and a plane.

The Mishnah rules: *ve-eino chayav ad she-yitlenu be-ta'ar* — he is not liable unless he removes it with a razor. Rabbi Eliezer adds that even tweezers (*malket* and *rehitani*) incur liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד – גִּילּוּחַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ הַשְׁחָתָה, הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה תַּעַר.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** Based on these two verses, for what form of hair removal is one liable? One is liable only for **shaving that involves marring. You must say** that **this** is shaving with **a razor.**

The Gemara derives the razor requirement from two separate verses: *pe'at zekanam lo yegaleichu* (applied to *kohanim*, but read together with the parallel prohibition applying to all of Israel) and *lo tashchit et pe'at zekanecha*. These two verses are not read as establishing two separate *lavin*, but rather as jointly defining the act that is prohibited: it must be both *giluach* (shaving) and *tashchit* (destruction — i.e., reaching the root). The Gemara then maps the three implements:

A razor satisfies both conditions — it reaches the root (*tashchit*) and is a normal act of shaving (*giluach*). Scissors do not reach the root — they cut above the surface of the skin — and therefore lack *tashchit*, even though they constitute *giluach*. Tweezers, by contrast, do reach the root by pulling the hair from it, satisfying *tashchit*, but the *Rivan* explains they are not *giluach* because they do not involve a normal manner of shaving — they are not *derekh giluach*. An alternative reading is that *giluach* implies a cutting or slicing action, and tweezers perform neither; they pluck. Either way, only a razor satisfies both conditions and therefore only a razor incurs liability. Hence Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that tweezers are liable, must explain tweezers as constituting *giluach* — the *Rivan* explains that for Rabbi Eliezer, *giluach* is defined more broadly, perhaps as anything that produces the end result of removal, which he prioritizes over the question of method.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ לִקְּטוֹ בְּמַלְקֵט וּרְהִיטָנִי יְהֵא חַיָּיב. מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי גְּמִיר גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה – לִיבְעֵי תַּעַר, אִי לָא גְּמִיר גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה – מִסְפָּרַיִם נָמֵי [לָא]!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even** if **he removed** the hair **with tweezers or with a plane, he would be liable** to receive lashes. The Gemara objects: **Whichever way you** look at it, it is difficult. **If he derives** the *halakha* by means of **a verbal analogy** that he received from his teachers between the *halakhot* of shaving written with regard to priests (Leviticus 21:5) and the *halakhot* of marring written with regard to non-priests (Leviticus 19:27), **let** Rabbi Eliezer **require** shaving with **a razor** in order to render him liable, as did the Rabbis. **If he does not derive** the *halakha* by means of **a verbal analogy,** he should **also not** exempt one who removes the hair with **scissors,** as that should be included in the category of shaving.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם גְּמִיר גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה, וְקָסָבַר: הָנֵי נָמֵי גִּילּוּחַ עָבְדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually, he derives** the *halakha* by means of **a verbal analogy, and he holds** that **these too,** the tweezers and the plane, **accomplish shaving.** His dispute with the Rabbis does not relate to the derivation of the *halakha*; rather it is a dispute with regard to the definition of shaving.

### Electric Shavers

The question of electric shavers is not addressed explicitly in the Gemara, but the analysis of the razor-versus-scissors distinction generates the analytical framework within which *poskim* have debated it. The issue is whether an electric shaver satisfies *tashchit* (getting to the root) and *giluach* (the appropriate method of shaving).

On the question of *tashchit*: a standard electric shaver works through a foil mechanism — the foil rests against the skin, hair enters the foil, and the blade cuts within. Because the foil intervenes between blade and skin, the blade never achieves the proximity to the skin that a straight razor does. Some *poskim* therefore argue that electric shavers do not constitute *tashchit*, since they do not cut at the root in the way a razor does. This explains why the conversation about electric shavers often focuses on how close a particular model shaves — the claim that it "shaves as close as a blade" is precisely what would be halachically concerning under this analysis.

On the question of *giluach*: if one follows the view that the distinction between razors and scissors lies in the razor's slicing action versus the scissors' two-blade cutting action, then an electric shaver presents a further question. The foil-and-blade mechanism of a shaver resembles the scissors model — the foil holds the hair while the blade cuts it. This would suggest it is more like scissors than a razor, and therefore even if it were to reach the root, it might still be permitted because it is not *derekh giluach* in the razor sense. Others argue the opposite, contending that the blade inside is doing a slicing motion regardless of the foil.

The Tosafot in another tractate develop the concept of *misparayim ke-ein ta'ar* — scissors operating very close to the skin, functioning almost like a razor — and rule it permissible even so. This *chiddush* does not appear explicitly here but underlies much of the discussion. If scissors are permitted even when approaching razor-level closeness, the question becomes: what exactly is it that makes a razor different? The three answers — closer to the root; slicing versus cutting; more *derekh giluach* — each point in a different direction when applied to an electric shaver.

It should also be noted that the *misparayim ke-ein ta'ar* leniency does not apply to *peot ha-rosh*. The verb *mashchit* is absent from the *pe'ah* prohibition, and the methodology of the beard prohibition therefore does not carry over. So while scissors may be permitted for the beard even when cutting very close, they are not permitted for the sideburns under the same logic — though, as the Gemara indicated earlier, the *pe'ah* prohibition may require a more sweeping act of removal than any question of implement would address.

## Tattooing

### Ketiva & Ke'aka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכּוֹתֵב כְּתוֹבֶת קַעֲקַע. כָּתַב וְלֹא קִעֲקַע, קִעֲקַע וְלֹא כָּתַב – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב, עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב וִיקַעְקַע (בְּיָדוֹ) [בִּדְיוֹ] וּבִכְחוֹל וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא רוֹשֵׁם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who imprints a tattoo,** by inserting a dye into recesses carved in the skin, is also liable to receive lashes. If **one imprinted** on the skin with a dye **but did not carve** the skin, or if **one carved** the skin **but did not imprint** the tattoo by adding a dye, **he is not liable;** he is not liable **until he imprints and carves** the skin, **with ink, or with kohl [*keḥol*], or with any substance that marks.**

The Mishnah on *ketovot ka'aka* — tattooing — states that liability requires both *ketiva* (writing or marking) and *ke'aka* (incision into the skin). Neither element alone is sufficient. The mechanism described by Rashi is that ink is applied to the skin and then an incision is made into it, causing the ink to be absorbed beneath the surface where it remains permanently.

### Ink Without Cut

If one wrote the ink marking without making the incision, or made the incision without applying ink — such as the temporary tattoo-like markings sometimes made today — there is no liability. One is not liable until both the writing and the incision have occurred together: *eino chayav ad she-yikhtov ve-yaka'keia*. The Mishnah also specifies the substances — ink (*dyo*), *kochal* (another pigment), and any substance that leaves a lasting mark — thus indicating that the critical feature is permanent pigmentation beneath the skin, not any particular material.

### Rabbi Shimon's Name

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב שָׁם אֶת הַשֵּׁם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּכְתֹבֶת קַעֲקַע לֹא תִתְּנוּ בָּכֶם אֲנִי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon: He is liable only if he writes the name there, as it is stated: "And a tattoo inscription you shall not place upon you, I am the Lord"** (Leviticus 19:28).

Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Shimon holds that liability applies only if one writes *ha-shem* — the Name — into the skin. His derivation: *ke-sovet ka-aka lo titenu bachem Ani Hashem* — "you shall not place a tattooing inscription upon you; I am the Lord." The plain reading would suggest one must literally write the words *Ani Hashem* to be liable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: עַד דְּיִכְתּוֹב ״אֲנִי ה׳״ מַמָּשׁ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא. כִּדְתָנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא: אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב שֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּכְתוֹבֶת קַעֲקַע לֹא תִתְּנוּ בָּכֶם אֲנִי ה׳״ – אֲנִי ה׳, וְלֹא אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** Is Rabbi Shimon saying that one is liable only **if he actually inscribes** the words **"I am the Lord"** in his skin? Rav Ashi **said to him: No,** he is saying **as bar Kappara teaches: One is liable only if he inscribes a name of** an object of **idol worship, as it is stated: "And a tattoo inscription you shall not place upon you, I am the Lord,"** which means: Do not place an idolatrous name on your skin, as **I am the Lord, and no** one **else.**

The Gemara, however, pushes back immediately. Rav Acha son of Rava asks Rav Ashi: does one really have to write the words *Ani Hashem* literally? That cannot be the intent. Rav Ashi explains with reference to a teaching of Bar Kappara: the liability arises not from writing the name of God, but from writing the name of *avodah zarah*. The verse is read as follows: "Do not inscribe a tattoo upon yourselves — [because I am] *Ani Hashem*," meaning: *do not bear the brand of another*. The prohibition is against tattooing the name of a foreign deity onto one's body, marking oneself as belonging to that god. The Gemara notes that this reading aligns naturally with the Rambam's framework, which roots these prohibitions in their pagan context.

### Ashes in Wounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַלְכִּיָּא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: אָסוּר לוֹ לְאָדָם שֶׁיִּתֵּן אֵפֶר מִקְלֶה עַל גַּבֵּי מַכָּתוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֵית כִּכְתוֹבֶת קַעֲקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Malkiyya says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: It is prohibited for a person to place burnt ashes on his wound** to promote healing, **because it looks like a tattoo.**

Rav Malkiya said in the name of Rav Adda bar Ahava: it is forbidden to rub *efer mikleh* — ashes — into a wound, because when the wound heals, the ash remains beneath the skin and creates the appearance of a tattoo. This is prohibited *mipnei she-nireh ki-ketovot ka'aka* — because it looks like a tattooed inscription.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם מַכָּה – מַכָּתוֹ מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: Any place where there is a wound, his wound proves about itself**  that the person's intent when he covers it with ashes is to promote healing, and it is not a tattoo.

This raises an immediate question: if ash rubbed into a wound for a medical purpose leaves a permanent mark, is that prohibited? The answer from the Gemara appears to be yes, at least in the general case. Rav Ashi, however, qualifies this: *kol makom she-yesh sham makah, makato mochiach alav* — wherever there is a wound, the wound itself demonstrates the purpose. That is, if it is evident from the wound that the incision or the marking was made for healing and not for ornamentation or religious branding, there is no violation.

The *Rivan* adds a further qualification to the prohibition on ashes: the term *mikleh* — referring to ashes charred from a fuel stove, which leave a particularly lasting and dark impression — distinguishes this case from ordinary dust or dirt, which would not create the same appearance. The Ritva understands the distinction as separating this type of ash from regular ash; the *Rivan* seems to be distinguishing *efer* (ash) from *afar* (ordinary dirt), clarifying that it is specifically the type of ash that leaves a permanent subdermal pigment. Whatever the precise scope, the principle is that the prohibition applies when the substance leaves a lasting mark beneath the skin.

Rav Imi bar Abba was stringent even about the residue left by a lancet — a cutting instrument used for piercing boils — lest it incidentally leave a mark beneath the skin.

## Tattooing…

### Breast Reconstruction

A directly contemporary question flows from Rav Ashi's ruling. Women who undergo mastectomy and subsequent reconstructive surgery often receive tattooing to recreate the areola around the reconstructed nipple, restoring natural appearance. Does this constitute a biblical violation of *ketovot ka'aka*?

Several considerations bear on the analysis. According to the Rambam's *Moreh Nevukhim* framework, these prohibitions are rooted in their pagan context — but of course the Rambam's explanation of *ta'amei ha-mitzvot* does not translate into a halachic exemption simply because the original social context is absent; that is not how *ta'amei ha-mitzvot* operate in practice.

A more textually grounded argument advanced by one *acharon* is that *ketovot ka'aka* as a *de-Oraita* prohibition requires actual *ketiva* — writing, i.e., letters or at least a recognizable depiction — and that a purely cosmetic mark not constituting writing might be only *de-rabbanan* at most. However, as the lecture notes, the Gemara itself prohibited the mere rubbing of ashes into a wound because it *appears* as a tattoo, without any suggestion that the absence of letter-formation reduces the prohibition. The *Gemara* did not draw that distinction, and its absence is significant.

The *makah mochiach* principle of Rav Ashi — that a wound demonstrates the medical purpose — is the more solid ground. If the tattooing is clearly done as part of documented medical reconstruction, and the medical purpose is self-evident, one can argue that the *makah mochiach alav* principle applies: the context makes clear it is not *avodah zarah* branding, not cosmetic marking for its own sake, and not a violation of the prohibition's intent. Many *poskim* lean on the combination of these considerations, treating such cases as at most *de-rabbanan* and permissible given significant medical and psychological need.

## Multiple Acts Principle

### Nazir Wine Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה שׁוֹתֶה יַיִן כׇּל הַיּוֹם – אֵין חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. אָמְרוּ לוֹ ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״, ״אַל תִּשְׁתֶּה״, וְהוּא שׁוֹתֶה – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A nazirite who was drinking wine all day is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If onlookers **said to him: Do not drink, do not drink,** forewarning him several times, **and he drinks** after each forewarning, he is **liable** to receive lashes **for each and every** drink.

The Mishnah teaches: if a *nazir* was drinking wine the entire day, he is liable only once — *eino chayav ela achat* — because there was a single warning and the drinking constitutes one continuous process. If, however, he received warnings between drinks — *amru lo al tish-teh, al tish-teh* — and continued drinking each time, he is liable for each separate act of drinking.

### Warnings Divide Acts

The underlying principle is that a single continuous action, performed without intervening warnings, constitutes one transgression regardless of how many times the prohibited behavior is repeated during that stretch. An intervening warning (*hatara*) serves to separate what would otherwise be a continuous act into distinct, independently countable violations. The warning functions as a break — it signals that the person has been notified and is now making a fresh, conscious choice to transgress again.

### Ongoing Actions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה מִטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים כׇּל הַיּוֹם – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, אָמְרוּ לוֹ ״אַל תִּטַּמֵּא״, ״אַל תִּטַּמֵּא״, וְהוּא מִטַּמֵּא – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. הָיָה מְגַלֵּחַ כׇּל הַיּוֹם – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, אָמְרוּ לוֹ ״אַל תְּגַלֵּחַ״, ״אַל תְּגַלֵּחַ״, וְהוּא מְגַלֵּחַ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** If the nazirite **was rendering himself impure** through exposure **to corpses all day, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If **they said to him: Do not render yourself impure, do not render yourself impure, and he renders himself impure** after each forewarning, he is **liable for each and every** incident. If the nazirite **was shaving** his hair **all day, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If **they said to him: Do not shave, do not shave, and** after each forewarning **he shaves,** he is **liable for each and every** time he shaves.

The Mishnah applies this principle to a series of cases. A *nazir* who spent the entire day in a cemetery — even moving from one to the next — incurs only one set of lashes, either because each subsequent entry lacked its own warning, or because the entire sequence of contaminations is treated as one continuous state. If he received a warning before each entry and continued, he is liable for each one.

Similarly, a person who was shaving continuously all day — cutting very slowly — incurs only one set of lashes. If warned repeatedly between strokes, each warned stroke is a separate transgression. This case also returns to the question of the five corners: since the five corners of the beard are distinct prohibitions, the question of how warnings and continuous action interact with multiple anatomical sites is directly relevant, though the Gemara does not resolve it in detail here.

### State vs Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה לָבוּשׁ בְּכִלְאַיִם כׇּל הַיּוֹם – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״אַל תִּלְבַּשׁ״, ״אַל תִּלְבַּשׁ״, וְהוּא פּוֹשֵׁט וְלוֹבֵשׁ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** If a person **was wearing** a garment consisting of **diverse kinds** of wool and linen **all day, he is liable** to receive **only one** set of lashes. If **they said to him: Do not wear** it, **do not wear** it, **and he removes** it **and dons** it after each forewarning, he is **liable for each and every** time that he dons the garment.

The case of *kilayim* — wearing *sha'atnez* — introduces a critical distinction. If a person wore *sha'atnez* all day without interruption, he is liable only once regardless of any number of warnings, because wearing *sha'atnez* is a *state*, not a repeated act. Remaining in a state of violation, even a prolonged one, does not multiply into separate transgressions by virtue of warnings alone.

However, if he took off the forbidden garment and put it back on — *poshet ve-lovesh* — and received a warning between each such act, he is liable for each. The taking off and putting back on transforms what would be a continuous state into a series of discrete physical acts, each of which can be independently warned and independently punished. The distinction is thus between an ongoing state (in which the person is continuously transgressing but performing no new act) versus a repeated action (in which the person is actively performing the prohibited act anew each time).

## Eight Violations

### Choresh Telem Echad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ חוֹרֵשׁ תֶּלֶם אֶחָד וְחַיָּיב עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם שְׁמוֹנָה לָאוִין: הַחוֹרֵשׁ בְּשׁוֹר וַחֲמוֹר, וְהֵן מוּקְדָּשִׁין, וְכִלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם, וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית, וְיוֹם טוֹב, וְכֹהֵן וְנָזִיר בְּבֵית הַטּוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the case where one receives several sets of lashes for performing a single action, the mishna continues: **There is** one who **plows a single furrow and is liable** to receive lashes **for** violating **eight prohibitions.** How so? For **plowing with an ox and a donkey,** in violation of the prohibition: "You shall not plow with an ox and a donkey together" (Deuteronomy 22:10); **and they are consecrated,** and therefore he is guilty of misuse of consecrated property; **and** he is plowing **diverse kinds in a vineyard; and** it is **during the Sabbatical** Year, when it is prohibited to work the land; **and** it is on **a Festival,** when plowing is a prohibited labor; **and** he is both **a priest and a nazirite** and is performing the plowing **in a place of impurity** imparted by a corpse, which is prohibited for both a priest (see Leviticus 21:1) and a nazirite (see Numbers 6:6).

The Mishnah concludes with the celebrated *choresh telem echad* — a single furrow that can generate liability for eight separate prohibitions simultaneously. The Mishnah enumerates: *ha-choresh be-shor va-chamor* — plowing with an ox and a donkey, which is the *kilayim be-vehema* prohibition; *ve-hem mukdashim* — and they are sanctified, so that the ox is a *bechor* (a firstborn animal that may not be worked: *lo ta'avod bi-vchor shorecha*) and the donkey is *hekdesh bedek habayit* (consecrated to Temple maintenance, so that working it involves *me'ila*); *ve-kilayim b'kerem* — the plowing simultaneously covers over seeds of mixed kinds in a vineyard, constituting *kilayim* in the category of *kilay ha-kerem* (as the Gemara explains, the plow covering seeds over the earth constitutes a form of planting); *u-vi-shevi'it* — it is the *shmitta* year, during which plowing is forbidden; *ve-Yom Tov* — it is also a festival day, during which agricultural labor is prohibited; *ve-Kohen ve-Nazir be-veis ha-tumah* — and the person plowing is both a *kohen* and a *nazir*, both of whom are forbidden from entering or remaining in a space containing the dead.

That is the list as constructed, yielding eight.

### Chananya's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן חֲכִינַאי אוֹמֵר: אַף הַלּוֹבֵשׁ כִּלְאַיִם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵינוֹ הַשֵּׁם. אָמַר לָהֶם: אַף הַנָּזִיר לֹא הוּא הַשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥananya ben Ḥakhinai says:** If he **was wearing** a garment consisting of **diverse kinds** of wool and linen while plowing he is **also** flogged for violating that prohibition. The Sages **said to him:** That is **not** a prohibition in **the** same **category** as the others, as it is not connected to the act of plowing. Ḥananya ben Ḥakhinai **said to them:** According to that criterion, the fact that he is **a nazirite is also not** in **the** same **category,** as a nazirite and a priest are not flogged for plowing; rather, they are flogged for contracting impurity imparted by a corpse.

Chananya ben Chakinai raises an objection in the Mishnah itself: *af ha-lovesh kilayim* — one might add yet another violation if the person is wearing *sha'atnez* while plowing. The Sages respond: *eino min ha-shem* — wearing *sha'atnez* is not connected to the act of plowing; it is an independent state unrelated to the furrow. Chananya responds pointedly: *af nazir lo min ha-shem* — then neither is the *nazir* being in a cemetery connected to the act of plowing! Being a *nazir* or a *kohen* in a cemetery is equally extraneous to the furrow itself. This is a strong argument. The Sages appear to have no fully compelling rejoinder within the framework of the list as they have constructed it.

### Methodological Note

The Sages' response, and indeed the entire exercise, functions not as a definitive halachic ruling but as a demonstration of the principle of multiple simultaneous violations in a single act. There is no authoritative conclusion about exactly which eight — or how many — violations are generated; the list is designed to illustrate how densely a single physical act can be laden with distinct prohibitions when the right combination of circumstances obtains.

### Tosafot's Count

Tosafot note a difficulty with the count and resolve it by redefining one of the entries. Rather than treating the donkey as *hekdesh bedek habayit* — which generates one violation — Tosafot hold that only the ox is *hekdesh*, as a *bechor*, and the donkey is not separately consecrated. To restore the count to eight, Tosafot identify two distinct violations of *kilayim* in the plowing: *kilay ha-kerem* (mixed planting in the context of a vineyard) and *kilay zera'im* (mixing two different species of grain). If the person is covering over a grape seed along with two different types of grain, both the *kilay ha-kerem* and the *kilay zera'im* prohibitions are triggered simultaneously, yielding two separate violations from the single act of covering the seeds — and thereby restoring the count to eight without requiring the donkey to be *hekdesh*.
