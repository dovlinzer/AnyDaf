# Nedarim 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hefker & Nedarim Intro

### Madir & Hefker Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִם הָיָה הֶפְקֵירוֹ קוֹדֵם לְנִדְרוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּתָּר. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ עַד דְּאָתֵי לִרְשׁוּת זוֹכֶה, מָה לִי נִדְרוֹ קוֹדֵם לְהֶפְקֵירוֹ, מָה לִי הֶפְקֵירוֹ קוֹדֵם לְנִדְרוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **However, if his declaration** that the food is **ownerless predates his vow,** it is **permitted** for the other person to eat the food. **And if you say** that the reason for the opinion of Rabbi Yosei is because the food remains in the possession of the owner **until it comes into the possession of the one** who **acquires** it, **what is** different **for me** if **his vow predates his declaration** that the food is **ownerless,** and **what is** different **for me** if **his declaration** that the food is **ownerless predates his vow?** In any event, the item remains in the possession of its owner and the one for whom benefit is forbidden benefits from it.

We begin on daf 44a, though in order to organize the relevant background, we must first briefly revisit the mishnah that precedes it. The mishnah addresses a case of a *madir* — someone who has rendered another person (*mudar*) forbidden from deriving *hana'ah* from him. If the *madir* has made all of his possessions *assur* to the *mudar* and the two are traveling together, with only enough food for one, the question arises how the *mudar* might be permitted to eat. One solution suggested is that the *madir* declare his food *hefker* — he places it on a rock and announces that it is ownerless, available to whoever wishes to take it — thereby permitting the *mudar* to partake.

### Rabbi Yose's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מוֹתֵיב לַהּ וְהוּא מְשַׁנֵּי לַהּ: כׇּל הַנּוֹדֵר אֵין דַּעְתּוֹ עַל מַה שֶּׁהִפְקִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Abba **raised the objection and he answered** that objection. The difference between the cases does not relate to the *halakhot* of ownerless property; rather, it relates to the nature of vows. **The intent of anyone who vows is** that the vow **not** apply **to** an item **that he rendered ownerless.** Therefore, when he declares the food ownerless and then vows, he does not intend to include the ownerless food in his vow, and the prohibition does not take effect upon it.

Rabbi Yose, however, rejects this solution. The *gemara* explains, through Rabbi Yochanan's analysis, why Rabbi Yose considers this approach ineffective.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Matana…

Rabbi Yochanan explains that Rabbi Yose holds *hefker* to be analogous to a *matana*: a gift does not take legal effect until the recipient takes possession of it. Accordingly, until a third party actually comes and claims the *hefker* item, that item is still considered to be within the *reshut* of the original owner. Since it remains in the *reshut* of the *madir*, the *mudar* is still forbidden to benefit from it. Only after an intermediate party has claimed it and removed it from the *madir*'s *reshut* could it then pass to the *mudar*.

### Baalut & Ownership

This position carries an interesting implication: it suggests that there is no such thing in the world of *metaltelin* — movable property — that belongs to absolutely no one. Everything, at any given moment, resides in the *reshut* of some person and carries some degree of *baalut*. Parallels can be found in the laws of *nezikin*, where one who declares his *kad* ownerless in the *reshut harabbim* may still bear responsibility if someone trips over it — indicating that even an act of *hefker* does not necessarily sever all legal connection between an owner and his property. The word *hefker* itself means "consciously abandoned" — it requires verbalization, either to oneself or to others — and is distinct from *yeush*, the mere mental resignation to loss, which is itself a *machloket rishonim* as to whether it operates *mishum hefker*.

Although an earlier portion of the *sugya* appears to have raised a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan's explanation, the *gemara* continues to operate on the assumption that his position remains viable. That baseline — *hefker* is like a *matana* and requires *zechiyas* by another person before it exits the original owner's *reshut* — underlies everything that follows.

## Field Hefker & 3-Day Rule

### Why Make Fields Hefker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: הַמַּפְקִיר אֶת שָׂדֵהוּ, כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים — יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — אֵין יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who declares his field ownerless,** for the **entire three days** after that declaration **he is able to retract** it. **From this** point **forward, he is unable to retract** the declaration.

We now turn to the mishnah at the bottom of 43b, which serves as our primary text for the rest of the *sugya*. The mishnah states: one who declares his field *hefker* may retract within three days; thereafter he may not retract. If he declared it *hefker* for a limited period — one day, one week, one month, one year, or one *shavua* (a seven-year cycle; note that in the Talmud *shavua* means a *shemita* cycle, while *shabbat* denotes a seven-day period) — then as long as no one has taken possession, whether he himself or another, he may retract; once someone has taken possession, he may not.

Why would a person declare his field *hefker* in the first place? The reason is that *chiyuv* in *trumot u'maasrot* depends on ownership. A field that has been declared *hefker* and then repossessed from *hefker* is *patur* from *trumot u'maasrot*. So the scheme is: declare the field *hefker*, gamble that no one will immediately pounce on it, and then recollect it — now ostensibly exempt from *trumot u'maasrot* for that season. This is, to put it plainly, a sleazy practice, and the Talmud will have much to say about it.

### 3-Day Retraction Period

The mishnah's first clause tells us that for the first three days one may retract the *hefker* declaration. The Ran explains that even if someone has come along and claimed the field during those three days, the original owner may still retract. The logic here is that both parties are, in a sense, engaged in a kind of gamble: the owner hopes no one will take it, while an opportunistic third party — himself not entirely above suspicion — waits to claim it the moment it is declared *hefker*. During those first three days, the owner may say, "I withdraw; it was never really *hefker* after all."

Importantly, during those three days the field is also not *patur* from *trumot u'maasrot*. The owner does not get the benefit of *hefker* during the window when he retains the right to retract. After three days, however, he can no longer retract at all. If he wishes to recollect the field, he must do so as a fresh act of *zechiya* — claiming it from *hefker* just as anyone else would — not by retroactively voiding the *hefker* declaration.

The question naturally arises: why three days specifically? The answer offered is, essentially, that three days is the pragmatic middle ground — two days is too short, and four days is too long. The Rabbinic literature frequently uses three as a meaningful number, and common sense aligns with it as a period long enough to be meaningful but short enough to be definitive.

### Limited-Time Hefker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר ״תְּהֵא שָׂדֶה זוֹ מוּפְקֶרֶת לְיוֹם אֶחָד״, ״לְשַׁבָּת אַחַת״, ״לְחֹדֶשׁ אֶחָד״, ״לְשָׁנָה אַחַת״, ״לְשָׁבוּעַ אֶחַת״ — עַד שֶׁלֹּא זָכָה בָּהּ, בֵּין הוּא בֵּין אַחֵר יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ. מִשֶּׁזָּכָה בָּהּ — בֵּין הוּא בֵּין אַחֵר, אֵין יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one said: This field will be ownerless for one day, for one week, for one month, for one year,** or **for one** seven-year Sabbatical cycle, **as long as no one took possession of** the field, **neither** the one who declared it ownerless **nor another** person, **he is able to retract** his declaration. **Once one took possession** of the field, **whether** it is **he** or **whether** it is **another** person, **he is unable to retract** his declaration.

The second clause of the mishnah introduces a distinction. When a person declares his field *hefker* only for a limited period — a day, a week, a month, a year, or a seven-year cycle — the three-day retraction mechanism does not apply in the same way. Instead, the rule is: as long as no one has taken possession, whether the owner himself or another, he may retract; once someone has taken possession, he may not.

### Possession & No Retracti…

To clarify the practical distinction between retracting a *hefker* declaration and actively claiming the field from *hefker*: retracting means saying, "I withdraw the declaration; the field was never *hefker*." Claiming means going out and physically taking possession — building a fence, walking the boundaries — which is the standard method of acquiring a field. One who claims it thereby obtains it as *hefker* property, and if the *hefker* was valid, that acquisition carries a *petur* from *trumot u'maasrot*. Retracting, by contrast, is asserting that the field never left one's possession at all.

The question of whether the owner may then claim the field for himself as an act of *zechiya* — distinct from retraction — is meaningful. Taking possession as *zocheh* from *hefker* is an act consistent with *hefker* being real; retraction treats it as though *hefker* never occurred. If the owner takes possession as *zocheh*, the field is *patur* from *trumot u'maasrot*. If he retracts, it was never *hefker* and remains *chayav*. This distinction will become central in the analysis that follows.

## Reisha vs. Seifa Attribu…

### Rabbinic Three-Day Solut…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא רַבָּנַן, סֵיפָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** This *baraita* is difficult, as **the first clause** is apparently in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that rendering property ownerless is unilateral and not contingent upon whether one took possession of it, and **the latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who holds that rendering property ownerless is complete only when one takes possession of that property.

Having laid out the two clauses of the mishnah, the *gemara* now asks whether the *reisha* (the first clause, with the three-day rule) reflects the position of the *rabbanan*, while the *seifa* (the second clause, which ties retractability to possession rather than time) reflects the position of Rabbi Yose. The reasoning is as follows: the *seifa*, which allows retraction so long as no one has taken possession, fits naturally with Rabbi Yose's underlying view that *hefker* never truly exits the owner's *reshut* until someone claims it. The *reisha*, by contrast, which cuts off retraction after three days regardless of whether anyone has claimed it, seems to reflect the *rabbanan*'s view that *hefker* is a real act of dispossession.

### The Rammaim Problem

What motivates the *rabbanan*'s position in the *reisha*? They are troubled by the practice of *rammaim* — those who declare fields *hefker* with the full intention of reclaiming them in order to avoid *trumot u'maasrot*. The Rabbis were unwilling to allow this scheme to proceed unchecked.

### Rav's Patience Theory

Rav explains the logic: the *rabbanan* imposed a *de'rabbanan* *chiyuv* in *trumot u'maasrot* during the first three days — the period during which the *madir* retains the right to retract. Even though *de'oraita* the *hefker* may be valid and the field should be *patur*, the Rabbis said: as long as you can still retract, you remain *chayav de'rabbanan*. And, critically, Rav observes that the *rammaim* do not have the patience to wait longer than three days. They want their field back quickly. By making the three-day period a window of continued *chiyuv* — with freedom only arriving after three days — the Rabbis effectively neutralized the scheme: those who couldn't wait three days would be *chayav*, and those who did wait would find their *hefker* to be real and irrevocable.

One might ask: how can the Rabbis empower the original owner to retract even after a third party has taken possession *de'oraita*? The answer invoked is *hefker beit din hefker*: the *beit din* possesses the authority to dispossess and transfer property. The Rabbis, acting through this principle, can give the owner the right to retract his *hefker* even in circumstances where *de'oraita* he could not.

### Ulla: Both Rabbanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: סֵיפָא נָמֵי רַבָּנַן הִיא. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי עַד שֶׁלֹּא זָכָה בָּהּ בֵּין הוּא בֵּין אַחֵר, יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר בּוֹ? שָׁאנֵי שָׁנָה וְשָׁבוּעַ דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: The latter clause is also** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The Gemara asks: **If so, why** is the ruling in the *baraita*: **As long as one did not take possession of** the field, **neither** the one who declared it ownerless **nor another** person, **he is able to retract** his declaration; according to the Rabbis, once he declared the property ownerless, although he can take possession of it, he is unable to retract the declaration. The Gemara answers: It **is different** when one declares an item ownerless for **a year or a seven-year** period, **as** doing so is **uncommon.** Since from the outset he limited the duration of ownerless status, clearly he reserved for himself certain rights; therefore, he can retract his declaration.

Ulla takes a different approach to the *reisha*-*seifa* division. He maintains that both clauses of the mishnah reflect the position of the *rabbanan*, not a split between *rabbanan* and Rabbi Yose. The *gemara* challenges Ulla: if the *seifa* is also *rabbanan*, why does it condition retractability on whether someone has taken possession, rather than simply applying the three-day rule? Ulla's answer is: *shani shanah v'shavua de'la shechicha* — the cases of a limited-time *hefker* (for a day, a week, a month, etc.) are unusual enough that the *rabbanan* did not bother applying the three-day mechanism to them. The anti-*rammaim* legislation targets the standard *hefker* scenario, which is declaring the field fully ownerless without a time limit. The limited-time *hefker* is not the typical exploit the Rabbis were concerned about, and so they left it subject only to the basic rule: once someone has claimed it, there is no retraction.

One might press: if the scheme is to declare *hefker* for just one day, complete the harvest, and reclaim the field, isn't that even more efficient than waiting three days? The answer seems to be that the critical moment of *chiyuv* in *trumot u'maasrot* is connected to the land itself, not the harvested crop. Once produce has been brought into a *kri* — the storage pile — it becomes *chayav*, but the mechanism of *hefker* to avoid *trumot u'maasrot* operates at the level of the field's ownership at the time of harvest. Making the field *hefker* for a single day is simply too unusual a practice for the Rabbis to have specifically legislated against, and thus the limited-time *hefker* falls outside the three-day framework entirely.

## Reish Lakish: Both Yose

### Reish Lakish's Attributi…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. וְרֵישָׁא, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא — דְּלָא לִישְׁתַּכַּח תּוֹרַת הֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said: From** the fact **that the latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, the first clause is also** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. And** in **the first clause, this is the reason** that one cannot retract his declaration once three days have passed: It is an ordinance instituted by the Sages to ensure **that the** halakhic **status of ownerlessness** would **not be forgotten.** If one could retract his declaration of ownerless status after three days, people would not distinguish between a case where another took possession, where, according to Rabbi Yosei, one is unable to retract the declaration, and a case where another did not take possession, where, according to Rabbi Yosei, one is able to retract the declaration. The result would be that people would mistakenly conclude that ownerless status can always be reversed, even after it was claimed by another. Therefore, the Sages instituted a limit of three days, after which one may not retract his declaration.

Reish Lakish offers an entirely different reading of the mishnah. Rather than splitting the clauses between *rabbanan* and Rabbi Yose, or attributing both to *rabbanan*, Reish Lakish holds that both the *reisha* and the *seifa* reflect the position of Rabbi Yose. The *seifa* is identifiable as Rabbi Yose for the reasons already discussed — it conditions retractability on possession rather than time, consistent with Rabbi Yose's view that *hefker* remains in the owner's *reshut* until claimed. And since the *seifa* is Rabbi Yose, says Reish Lakish, the *reisha* must be as well.

But then why does the *reisha* impose a three-day limit at all? If Rabbi Yose's fundamental position is that you may retract at any time until someone takes possession, why did he institute a cutoff at three days? This requires explanation.

### Ran's Confusion Theory

The Ran offers what he considers the most coherent account of Reish Lakish's reading. Rabbi Yose, says the Ran, is concerned about a particular confusion that would arise if *hefker* were always revocable up to the moment of possession. If people knew that the owner could retract at literally any moment before someone claims the field — that the field is, in Rabbi Yose's view, still in the owner's *reshut* the entire time — they might begin to think that *hefker* is not really *hefker* at all, but simply a kind of deferred *matana*.

### Hefker vs. Matana Confus…

The Ran develops this concern. If the owner can retract up until the very moment someone takes possession, then from the perspective of the person who ultimately claims the field, what he received was not truly ownerless property — it was property that the original owner could have reclaimed at any instant. This makes the transaction look far more like a *matana* than a claim from *hefker*. And that confusion has practical consequences.

The person who claimed the field might reason as follows: since the owner retained *reshut* over this property until the very moment I claimed it, perhaps this field is still *chayav* in *trumot u'maasrot*, just as property received as a gift is *chayav*. If he thinks that, he will then try to separate *trumot u'maasrot* from this field for other fields, or from other fields for this one — generating a problem of *min ha-chayav al ha-patur* or *min ha-patur al ha-chayav*. That is a serious *de'oraita* concern: separating tithes from produce that is actually *patur* on behalf of produce that is *chayav*, or vice versa, invalidates the separation entirely.

To make this vivid: the field was declared *hefker*, but the owner could have retracted at any moment. The person who ultimately claimed it is in a state of genuine uncertainty. Was this a real *hefker*? Or was this more like receiving a gift from someone who retained ownership throughout? That uncertainty is precisely what Rabbi Yose sought to prevent.

### Terumot U'Maasrot Confus…

The Ran elaborates further on why this confusion is specifically dangerous in the context of *trumot u'maasrot*. He writes (at the top of 44b) that if we allow the owner to retract at any point before someone has claimed it, people will say: since the owner could have retracted even after a long period, that means the property must have been in his *reshut* the whole time, which in turn means that anyone who claims it is really receiving a *matana* — and therefore the field remains *chayav* in *trumot u'maasrot*.

The Ran's language is precise: *atu le-meimar de-chi lo chazar bo nami lizchei bah acher lo me-hefker ka-zachei* — someone who claims the field will not believe they are claiming it from genuine *hefker*, but rather from the owner's continuing possession. And: *venimtza she-ein ze zocheh min ha-hefker ella mekabel matanah* — it turns out that this person is not taking from *hefker* at all, but receiving a *matana*. And therefore: *ve-chasvri de-michayav be-maaser* — they will think the field is *chayav* in *maaser*, and they will then proceed to treat it as such, mixing *chayav* and *patur* produce in their *tithe* separations.

To make this concrete with an analogy: if someone announces a field is *hefker* but it is common knowledge that the owner can retract at any moment, then anyone rushing to claim it understands they are racing against the clock of the owner's own will. The field is "available" only because the owner permits it to be, and could close that window at any instant. This feels structurally identical to receiving a gift, not to claiming genuinely ownerless property.

It is therefore to prevent this confusion — the collapsing of *hefker* into *matana* in the public's legal understanding — that Rabbi Yose institutes the three-day rule. For three days, the owner may retract. That is consistent with Rabbi Yose's *de'oraita* position, since the property is still in his *reshut*. But after three days, Rabbi Yose institutes a *de'rabbanan* rule: you may no longer retract. This ensures that people understand there is a real *torat hefker* at work — a genuine legal category of ownerless property — and not merely an elaborate mechanism for making gifts.

## Gemara Challenge & Rava

### Three-Day Puzzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ מִיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן נָמֵי לֶיהֱוֵי הֶפְקֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **let** the Sages institute that the item **is** considered **ownerless even from the first day as well** and that he cannot retract his declaration at all.

The *gemara* now raises a sharp question against Reish Lakish's reading. If the point of the three-day limit is to ensure that people understand *hefker* is real — i.e., that *torat hefker* should not be forgotten — then why impose a three-day limit at all? If the concern is that people will confuse *hefker* with *matana*, the solution should be to declare *hefker* fully effective from day one, not to give the owner three days of retraction and only then declare it irrevocable. Why not simply make it *hefker* immediately, so that there is never any confusion?

### Rava: Rammaim Balance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מִפְּנֵי הָרַמָּאִין, דְּמַפְקִירִין וְהָדְרִין בְּהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** The reason that the Sages did not render the item ownerless from the first day **is due to the swindlers, who declare** the field **ownerless** in order to render the produce of the field exempt from the requirement of tithing, **and** then **retract** that declaration and immediately reclaim the field. Therefore, the Sages instituted that ownerless status takes effect only after three days.

Rava provides the answer: there are two competing considerations. On one side is the concern that *torat hefker* will be forgotten — that people will not understand *hefker* as a genuine legal category distinct from *matana*. On the other side is the concern about the *rammaim*: those who exploit *hefker* declarations to escape *trumot u'maasrot*. These two values must be balanced.

### After 3 Days Fully Hefker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא הָוֵי הֶפְקֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages instituted that one cannot retract his declaration of ownerless status once three days have passed. **However, by Torah law, isn't** the item **ownerless** according to Rabbi Yosei, regardless of when he retracted his declaration as long as no other person acquired it?

The three-day period represents this balance. During the first three days, the owner retains the right to retract — consistent with Rabbi Yose's *de'oraita* position — and during this period the field remains *chayav de'rabbanan* in *trumot u'maasrot*, frustrating the *rammaim*. After three days, the *de'rabbanan* rule kicks in: the *hefker* becomes fully irrevocable, and the field is now genuinely *hefker de'rabbanan*, so that people understand it to be truly ownerless.

### Long-Term Reclaim Confus…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא אָתֵי לְעַשּׂוֹרֵי מִן הַחִיּוּב עַל הַפְּטוּר וּמִן הַפְּטוּר עַל הַחִיּוּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** that ordinance could lead to a problem, as **perhaps he** will **come to tithe from** produce that **requires** tithing by Torah law **for** produce **exempt** from tithing by Torah law **and from** produce **exempt** from tithing by Torah law **for** produce that **requires** tithing by Torah law. By Torah law, the produce is not ownerless and requires tithing. However, due to the ordinance, people might relate to it as ownerless produce and assume that one is exempt from tithing it. Alternatively, they will erroneously conclude that the obligation to tithe from that produce is by rabbinic law and will tithe produce from it that one is exempt from tithing by Torah law, or they will tithe that produce from produce that one is exempt from tithing by Torah law. The result will be produce that is not properly tithed, as one may tithe produce that one is obligated to tithe by Torah law only from other produce that one is obligated to tithe by Torah law.

Without this three-day limit, a further problem would emerge. Suppose the owner declares *hefker* and simply waits — for weeks or months — until he is ready to reclaim the field. He then walks in and takes possession. According to Rabbi Yose's *de'oraita* position, that is entirely permissible: the field was in his *reshut* the whole time, and no one else has claimed it. But if the public observes this pattern — someone declares *hefker*, and then months later simply takes the field back — they will be deeply confused about the *chiyuv* in *trumot u'maasrot*. Did the person who eventually harvests this field acquire it from genuine *hefker*? Or is it still the original owner's property? That long-term ambiguity is even more dangerous than the short-term confusion the three-day rule is designed to address. By cutting off retractability at three days *de'rabbanan*, the system ensures that any claim made after three days is understood to be a fresh *zechiya* from *hefker*, carrying a *petur* from *trumot u'maasrot*.

## Baraita: Hefker Exemption

### Vineyard Hefker Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיבִי: הַמַּפְקִיר אֶת כַּרְמוֹ, וְלַשַּׁחַר עָמַד וּבְצָרוֹ — חַיָּיב בְּפֶרֶט וּבְעוֹלֵלוֹת וּבְשִׁכְחָה וּבְפֵיאָה. וּפָטוּר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **One who declares his vineyard ownerless, and at dawn he arose and picked grapes** from the vineyard, **is obligated in** the mitzva of leaving **individual fallen** grapes left for the poor **[*peret*], and in** the mitzva of leaving **incompletely formed clusters** of grapes for the poor **[*olelot*], and in** the mitzva of leaving **forgotten** sheaves, **and in** the mitzva of ***pe'a*,** produce from the corner of the field or vineyard, as the obligation to separate those gifts from the poor is incumbent upon the one who harvests the field (see Leviticus 19:9–10). **And he is exempt from** the obligation to separate **the tithe** from the grapes. Because the vineyard is ownerless, there is no obligation to tithe the produce.

A *baraita* is now introduced that challenges the positions developed above. The *baraita* states: one who declares his vineyard *hefker* and then, in the morning, rises and harvests it himself is *chayav* in *peret*, *olelot*, *shichecha*, and *pe'ah* — all the agricultural obligations associated with those categories — but is *patur* from *maaser*. The reason for the *petur* from *maaser* is derived from a *midrash* on the relevant *pasuk*: the double language of *azov ta'azov* that appears in connection with certain agricultural gifts does not appear with *maaser*, and therefore *hefker* exempts from *maaser* but not from those other obligations.

### Ulla Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְעוּלָּא, דְּרַבָּנַן קָתָנֵי לַהּ וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** this is so **according to Ulla,** who explained **that the Rabbis taught** the previous *baraita* and explains that although the Sages instituted that the ownerless status does not take effect completely until three days have passed, by Torah law it takes effect immediately, **and that this** *baraita* is **taught in accordance with Torah law.** That is the reason that one is exempt from tithing the grapes.

Ulla can accommodate this *baraita* within his framework without difficulty. According to Ulla, both the *baraita* and the mishnah follow the *rabbanan*. The *baraita* is describing the situation *de'oraita*: when the owner harvests his field on the morning after declaring it *hefker*, this falls within the three-day window during which, *de'rabbanan*, the *hefker* has not yet taken full effect. But *de'oraita* the *hefker* is real, and therefore the field is *patur* from *maaser de'oraita*. *De'rabbanan* there may be additional obligations, but the *baraita* is speaking of the *de'oraita* exemption.

### Reish Lakish Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אַמַּאי פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר?
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to Reish Lakish, why is he exempt from** separating **the tithe?** Until three days after the declaration, neither by Torah law nor by rabbinic law does ownerless status take effect.

Reish Lakish, however, has a problem. If the *baraita* follows the *rabbanan*, then during the three-day period the field should be treated as though it is still the owner's — *chayav* in *trumot u'maasrot* — as that is precisely the Rabbinic mechanism designed to foil the *rammaim*. Why then is the *baraita* telling us that he is *patur* from *maaser*? If the three-day rule is meant to maintain *chiyuv de'rabbanan* during that period, the *baraita* should be saying he is *chayav*, not *patur*. The *baraita* seems to undercut the very logic of the three-day Rabbinic legislation.

### Ran: Implicit Retraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ: כִּי אֲמַרִי אֲנָא — לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הָא — רַבָּנַן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Reish Lakish could have **said to you:** Although **when** I explained the first clause and the latter clause of that *baraita* **I said** that both are **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who said that an ownerless item leaves the possession of the owner only when it enters the possession of another, **this** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that it leaves the possession of the owner immediately upon the declaration of ownerless status.

The Ran offers a resolution. The key is in what the owner did on the morning after declaring *hefker*. He simply went down to the vineyard and started harvesting — he never made any explicit declaration that he was reclaiming the field as his own through *zechiya* from *hefker*. Since he did not explicitly state that he was being *zocheh* from the *hefker*, the Ran says: *kivan de-lo parish she-hu zocheh bo... neima de-mehadar* — we interpret his action not as a fresh act of *zechiya* from *hefker*, but as an implicit retraction of the *hefker* itself. He changed his mind and reverted to treating the field as his own.

If this is a retraction, then according to Rabbi Yose — whose *de'oraita* position allows retraction at any point before someone has taken possession — the retraction is valid *de'oraita*, and the field was never *hefker*. If the field was never *hefker*, then it is *chayav* in *trumot u'maasrot* and not *patur*. But the *baraita* says *patur from maaser* — a result which, under this reading, fits precisely because the retraction is understood as a reversal within Rabbi Yose's framework, and the *baraita* is speaking to the resulting status.

### Reish Lakish: Attribute…

Reish Lakish sidesteps the difficulty entirely with a clean solution: he attributes the entire *baraita* to *rabbanan*, not to Rabbi Yose. In other words, Reish Lakish says: when I associated the mishnah with Rabbi Yose, that did not require me to associate this *baraita* with Rabbi Yose as well. The *baraita* can simply be understood as following the *rabbanan*, and the various technical details work themselves out under that attribution without generating the contradictions that plague the alternative reading.

## Two vs. Three Witnesses

### Two vs. Three Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא דְּאַפְקְרֵיהּ בְּאַנְפֵּי תְרֵין, וְהָא דְּאַפְקְרֵיהּ בְּאַפֵּי תְלָתָא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: כׇּל הַמַּפְקִיר בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה — הָוֵי הֶפְקֵר, בִּפְנֵי שְׁנַיִם — לָא הָוֵי הֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead: **That** *baraita*, in which it is taught that the item does not leave the possession of the owner until it enters the possession of another, is referring to a case **where** one declared it ownerless **before two** people; **and this** *baraita*, in which it is taught that the item is ownerless when it is declared ownerless, is referring to a case **where** one declared it ownerless **before three** people. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak:** With regard to **anyone who declares** an item **ownerless before three** people, that item **is ownerless;** if he does so **before two** people, **it is not ownerless.**

An alternative explanation (*i ba'it eima*) is offered to resolve the tension between the *baraita* and the mishnah without attributing them to different *tanna'im*. The distinction is not between Rabbi Yose and *rabbanan* at all, but between two different circumstances of *hefker*: whether the declaration was made in the presence of two people (*bifnei terei*) or three people (*bifnei tlata*).

When *hefker* is declared before only two people, the complexities discussed throughout — the right to retract, the three-day period, the ambiguity about *chiyuv* in *trumot u'maasrot* — all apply. The declaration is not maximally effective, and the various Rabbinic and *de'oraita* rules come into play. The mishnah, with its three-day retraction rule and all its attendant complications, speaks to this case.

### Three Witnesses Effective

But Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak, and Elazar ben Anas both hold that if *hefker* is declared before three people, it is fully and immediately effective. The declaration before three confers a qualitatively different legal status on the *hefker*: it is irrevocable from the outset, and its effect as genuine ownerless property — *patur* from *trumot u'maasrot* — is immediate. The *baraita*, which speaks of a *petur* from *maaser*, is therefore the case of *hefker* before three: the *hefker* took full effect immediately, and the owner who harvests the next morning is indeed *patur*.

### Three Witnesses Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶחָד — הָוֵי הֶפְקֵר, וּמַה טַּעַם אָמְרוּ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא אֶחָד זוֹכֶה וּשְׁנַיִם מְעִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: By Torah law, even with one** person, the item **is ownerless, and what** is the **reason** that the Sages **said** that ownerless status must be declared **with three** people? It is **so that one will take possession** of the item **and two** will **testify** that the item was declared ownerless and that it was acquired by that person. It is not a requirement fundamental to the declaration of ownerless status.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds a further nuance. He holds that *devar Torah* — as a matter of pure Torah law — even declaring *hefker* before a single person is sufficient to render the property ownerless. The Rabbinic requirement of three is not because three are needed for the *hefker* itself to be effective, but for a purely practical purpose: one person to take possession of the *hefker* property, and two to serve as witnesses to that act of possession. The three-person rule is thus a procedural safeguard for proof, not a substantive requirement for the *de'oraita* validity of the *hefker* declaration itself.
