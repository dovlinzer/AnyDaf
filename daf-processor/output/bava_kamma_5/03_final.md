# Bava Kamma 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Categories

### Opening

We pick up today on daf 4b, approximately ten lines from the bottom, at the words *tani Rabbi Chiya*.

### Mishna Review

By way of orientation, we have by this point worked through the different explanations of the *mishna*'s four categories: *shor*, *bor*, *ma'aveh*, and *hev'er*. Along the way, we discussed that when an animal walking kicks up stones, it pays half damages, since that is a form of *kocho* — the animal's indirect force — and is incidental rather than the animal's primary damaging act. We also discussed the *mishna*'s inclusion of *adam*, a person who causes damage, and the two competing frameworks for understanding that liability: strict liability by virtue of the act itself, or negligence as the operative basis. A parallel question arises regarding animal liability — whether the owner pays because of what the animal did as such, or because of a failure to watch it adequately.

### Rabbi Oshaya List

Following the *mishna*'s four categories, the *gemara* now presents several *braisot* that expand the list of *avot nezikin*. Rabbi Oshaya's expanded *braisa* brought the total to thirteen categories, moving beyond the *mishna*'s four by incorporating personal injury cases — where the tortfeasor pays not only for the damage itself but for pain, lost labor, medical expenses, and related items — and by incorporating the laws of *shomrim*, the various categories of bailee, who do not directly cause damage but who bear liability through negligence or, in the case of a *sho'el*, even without negligence. This gives us thirteen distinct categories. The *gemara* now introduces a still broader enumeration.

## Rabbi Chiya 24 Categories

### 24 Categories Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה אֲבוֹת נְזִיקִין: תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, וְגַנָּב, וְגַזְלָן, וְעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין, וְהָאוֹנֶס, וְהַמְפַתֶּה, וּמוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע, וְהַמְטַמֵּא, וְהַמְדַמֵּעַ, וְהַמְנַסֵּךְ; וְהָנֵי תְּלֵיסַר – הָא עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** that there are **twenty-four primary categories of damage: Payment of double** the principal, paid by a thief who is apprehended and convicted based on the testimony of witnesses and who does not admit his crime; **and payment of four or five** times the principal, paid by a thief who steals an ox or sheep, respectively, and then slaughters or sells it; **and** payment of the principal, by **a thief** who admits his crime; **and a robber, who** steals openly and by force or threat of violence; **and conspiring witnesses** who pay the individual against whom they falsely testified with regard to a sum that they conspired to cause him to lose. **And the rapist, and the seducer,** who seduces an unmarried young woman, who pay a fine of fifty *sela*; **and the defamer,** i.e., one who defames his wife by claiming falsely in court that he discovered that she was not a virgin when he consummated the marriage and alleges that she engaged in intercourse with another man while betrothed, who pays a fine of one hundred *sela*; **and one who causes** another's *teruma* **to become ritually impure,** rendering it prohibited to partake of that *teruma*; **and one who mixes** *teruma* with another's non-sacred food, rendering it prohibited for any non-priest to partake of it; **and one who pours** another's wine **as a libation** for idolatry. When one combines the eleven categories enumerated by **Rabbi Ḥiyya and these thirteen** categories enumerated by Rabbi Oshaya, **this** totals **twenty-four** principal categories of damage.

*Tani Rabbi Chiya esrim v'arba avot nezikin* — Rabbi Chiya's *braisa* enumerated twenty-four categories of *nezikin*. That is eleven more than Rabbi Oshaya's thirteen, and the *gemara* proceeds to identify them.

The first additions are *tashlumei kefel* — the double payment in cases of theft — and *tashlumei arba v'chamisha* — the fourfold and fivefold payments when the thief additionally slaughters or sells the stolen animal, applicable specifically to an ox or a sheep. Then *haganav v'hagazlan* — the principal payment by a thief (without the double) or a robber, who also pays only the principal. That brings us to four new categories. Fifth is *eidim zomemim* — conspiratorial false witnesses who, when they are exposed, pay what they had sought to extract from the defendant. Sixth through eighth are *v'ha'ones v'hamifateh u'motzi shem ra* — the rapist, the seducer, and the husband who falsely libels his bride as having committed adultery. Ninth through eleventh are *v'hametameh v'hamedameh v'hamenasech* — one who renders another's *terumah* ritually impure, one who mixes *terumah* with *chullin*, and one who pours another's wine as a libation to *avoda zara*, rendering it worthless. These last three derive not from *psukim* but from rabbinic teaching and are instances of *hezeik she'eino nikar* — damage that is not visibly apparent. Adding these eleven to Rabbi Oshaya's thirteen yields the total of twenty-four.

### Kanasim & Mamona

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא תָּנֵי הָנֵי? בְּמָמוֹנָא קָמַיְירֵי, בִּקְנָסָא לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Oshaya, what is the reason** that **he did not teach these** eleven additional categories? The Gemara answers: It is **with regard to** cases where one is liable to pay **monetary restitution** that Rabbi Oshaya **speaks. With regard to** cases where one is liable to pay **a fine,** Rabbi Oshaya **does not speak.**

The *gemara* now asks: why did Rabbi Oshaya not include these additional categories in his own list? The answer is *bemamona ka'mar uveknasa lo ka'mar* — Rabbi Oshaya was dealing exclusively with compensatory monetary obligations, *mamona*, and not with *knasot*, fines. Many of the items on Rabbi Chiya's expanded list are not true compensation but rather punitive or arbitrary assessments, and therefore fell outside Rabbi Oshaya's conceptual scope.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּנָּב וְגַזְלָן, דְּמָמוֹנָא הוּא, לִיתְנֵי! הָא קָתָנֵי לֵיהּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל. וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא נָמֵי, הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם וְהַשּׁוֹאֵל! תָּנֵי מָמוֹנָא דַּאֲתָא לִידֵיהּ בְּהֶיתֵּירָא, וְקָתָנֵי מָמוֹנָא דַּאֲתָא לִידֵיהּ בְּאִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What of the cases of **a thief and a robber, which** are cases where one is liable to pay **monetary restitution? Let** Rabbi Oshaya also **teach** those cases and include them in his list. The Gemara answers: **Doesn't he teach** those cases, as he enumerates in his list the **unpaid bailee and the borrower?** An unpaid bailee who takes a false oath that the deposit was stolen, when in fact it remained in his possession, is liable to pay restitution like a thief. The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Ḥiyya too, doesn't he teach** those cases; as he enumerates in his list the **unpaid bailee and the borrower?** Why does Rabbi Ḥiyya list them separately? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ḥiyya makes a distinction between different types of theft: He **teaches** cases of theft with regard to **property that came into** one's **possession in a permitted** manner, e.g., an unpaid bailee who was entrusted with a deposit and later misappropriated it, **and** he **teaches** cases of theft with regard to **property that came into** one's **possession in a prohibited** manner, e.g., the actions of a thief and a robber.

The *gemara* proceeds to examine which of Rabbi Chiya's additions are in fact *mamona* rather than *knas*. It notes that *ganav v'gazlan* — when paying only the principal amount — would seem to be straightforward compensation. Why then did Rabbi Oshaya not include them? The answer is that Rabbi Oshaya's list already included the *shomrim*. The connection is this: if a *shomer* falsely swears that an item was stolen or robbed, he is treated legally as a *ganav* or *gazlan* respectively. The *gemara* then turns the question around and asks why Rabbi Chiya lists both *shomrim* and *ganav v'gazlan* separately, if the former subsumes the latter. The answer is *tana mamona d'atei liyadei b'heter ukatani mamona d'atei liyadei b'issur* — Rabbi Chiya deliberately distinguishes between money that came into one's possession legitimately at the outset, as in the case of a *shomer* who then betrayed his charge, and money that was taken unlawfully from the very beginning, as in the case of an outright thief or robber. These are conceptually distinct categories, and Rabbi Chiya wished to enumerate both.

## Mamona vs. Knas

### False Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין, דְּמָמוֹנָא הוּא, לִיתְנֵי! סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין מְשַׁלְּמִין עַל פִּי עַצְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the payment made by **conspiring witnesses, which is** categorized as **monetary restitution,** as the witnesses pay the precise sum that they conspired to cause the defendant to lose, **let** Rabbi Oshaya also **teach it** as one of the categories on his list. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Oshaya **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says** that conspiring witnesses **do not pay on the basis of their own** admission. Evidently, theirs is not a payment of monetary restitution; rather, it is a fine, as only one who was found liable based on the testimony of witnesses pays a fine, not one who was found liable based on his own admission.

The *gemara* then turns to *eidim zomemim*, asserting that this is *mamona* — genuine compensation rather than a fine. This claim warrants close attention. On its surface it is surprising: the Torah's rule is *ka'asher zamam v'lo ka'asher asa l'achiv* — the conspiratorial witnesses are punished for what they plotted, not for what they actually accomplished. By definition, then, the false witnesses are punished only when they did not succeed. If there was no actual loss to the intended victim, in what sense can the payment be called compensation?

### Rashi Criteria

Rashi explains the criteria for distinguishing *mamona* from *knas*. Any payment that exceeds the actual damage caused is by definition a *knas* — the excess beyond the harm is clearly a punitive addition and not compensatory. He cites *kefel* and *tashlumei dalet v'hei* as examples. Alternatively, even where the payment does not exceed the harm, if it is a fixed statutory amount rather than a direct function of the damage caused, that too constitutes a *knas*, since it is arbitrary and symbolic rather than truly compensatory. As examples, Rashi mentions the fifty *shekel* payment by the rapist or seducer, and the payment by the husband who libels his bride — and he notes that *motzi shem ra* shares this character. A cleaner example, one might add, is the thirty *shekel* payment when an ox gores a slave: there is a real loss — indeed a loss of life — but instead of assessing the actual value of the slave, the Torah fixes a statutory sum of thirty *shekel*. That is the mark of a *knas*.

On these criteria, *eidim zomemim* qualifies as *mamona*: they pay an amount equivalent to the damage they attempted to cause, not more, and it is not a fixed arbitrary sum but proportionate to the specific harm they schemed to bring about. The payment is equivalent to the intended loss — *ka'asher zamam* is precisely calibrated. Rashi is explaining why, despite the fact that the plot did not succeed and there was no actual loss, the payment is still classified as *mamona* rather than *knas*: it is neither in excess of the damage nor arbitrary in amount.

### Tosafot Analysis

Yet one might still object: if *mamona* requires that there be an actual loss, how can *eidim zomemim* be classified as *mamona* when, by the rule of *ka'asher zamam v'lo ka'asher asa*, they pay only when the intended victim suffered no actual loss?

Tosafot on daf 4b addresses this directly, citing the explanation of the Riva. Tosafot argues that in monetary cases, *eidim zomemim* are liable even when they did succeed — that is, even when the intended victim was actually made to pay based on their false testimony. The reason is *delo shayach b'mamona ka'asher zamam v'lo ka'asher asa d'efshar b'hazara* — the principle of *ka'asher zamam v'lo ka'asher asa* does not fully apply in monetary matters, because monetary outcomes are always reversible. If a court issued a ruling based on false testimony, that ruling can be undone and the money returned. It is therefore never truly a case of *ka'asher asa* in monetary matters — the damage is never final the way, say, corporal punishment is final. The witnesses are therefore always in the position of having plotted, not having irreversibly accomplished, and accordingly the Torah does impose the payment — but because it applies even when the victim did pay, there is a real loss being compensated, which is why it is *mamona*.

The Riva then adds a second, independent approach: one need not rely on this argument at all. *Degabei mamon mechayavinan lei bekal vachomer* — in monetary matters, we can impose liability by *kal vachomer* even in the absence of an explicit verse. The Torah says *ka'asher zamam*, and we derive by *kal vachomer* that if they pay when they only plotted, they should certainly pay when they actually succeeded. In capital and corporal cases one cannot punish on the basis of a *kal vachomer* — *onshin min hadin* is forbidden — but in monetary matters this restriction does not apply, and *onshin min hadin* is permitted. Thus, even according to this approach, the false witnesses pay in the case where there was an actual loss, and the payment is therefore genuine compensation — *mamona*.

This analysis reveals the essential requirement of Tosafot's position: for a payment to constitute *mamona*, it is not sufficient that the amount be equivalent and non-arbitrary; there must also be an actual loss being compensated. If *eidim zomemim* paid only when the intended victim suffered no loss, Tosafot would apparently classify that as *knas*. The *gemara*'s classification of *eidim zomemim* as *mamona* therefore implies, for Tosafot, that they pay in the case of actual loss as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי סָבַר לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, לִיתְנֵי תְּרֵי גַוְונֵי שׁוֹר – לִיתְנֵי שׁוֹר דְּאַזֵּיק שׁוֹר, וְלִיתְנֵי שׁוֹר דְּאַזֵּיק אָדָם; דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אַף תָּם שֶׁחָבַל בְּאָדָם – מְשַׁלֵּם בַּמּוֹתָר נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם! הָא תַּבְרֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לִגְזִיזֵיהּ; דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל אַף תָּם שֶׁחָבַל בְּאָדָם, יְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יֵעָשֶׂה לוֹ״ – מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** Rabbi Oshaya **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, let him teach two** distinct **types of** categories of damage under the rubric of **Ox: Let him teach** one category for **an ox that damages an ox** or other property belonging to another person, **and let him** also **teach** the case of **an ox that injures a person. As we learned** in a mishna (33a): In a case where the two parties damaged each other concurrently, **Rabbi Akiva says:** The owner of the **innocuous** ox **that injured a person also pays the full** cost of the **damage with regard to the difference** between the damage caused by his ox and the damage that the person caused the ox. Rabbi Akiva holds that if an innocuous ox injures a person, the owner pays the full cost of the damage, whereas if an innocuous ox damages another's property then the owner is liable only for half the cost of the damage. Since the owner's liability in the two cases is different, each case should be classified as a distinct category, and Rabbi Oshaya should list them separately in his *baraita*. **Didn't Rabbi Akiva break** the force of **his fist [*gezizeih*],** i.e., he significantly tempered the force of his statement, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says:** One **might** have thought that **even** in the case of **an innocuous ox that injured a person,** the owner of the ox **would pay from** his **superior-quality** property and not merely from the body of his animal, as in the case of an innocuous ox that damages property; therefore, **the verse states** with regard to an ox that gores a person: "According to this judgment **shall it be done to him"** (Exodus 21:31), from which it is derived that the owner of the ox **pays** restitution exclusively **from** proceeds of the sale of the **body** of his ox, **and he does not pay from** his **superior-quality** property. The mishna states that one pays restitution from his superior-quality property in instances of all primary categories of damage. Since in the case of an innocuous ox that injured a person even Rabbi Akiva concedes that the owner does not pay from his superior-quality property, it should not be included in a list of the primary categories.

The *gemara* continues: *savar lah kerabi akiva de'amar ein meshalmin al pi atzman* — Rabbi Oshaya's list may reflect the position of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *eidim zomemim* do not pay if they confess to their own false testimony. According to Rabbi Akiva, even in monetary cases, the self-incriminating admission does not obligate payment, which is the hallmark of a *knas*. That is why *eidim zomemim* appears on Rabbi Chiya's expanded list of *knasot* and not on Rabbi Oshaya's list of pure *mamona*.

The *gemara* then raises a further question: if we are operating within Rabbi Akiva's framework and treating *eidim zomemim* as *knas*, then according to Rabbi Akiva there is another distinct category of ox — namely, an ox that gores a person — which Rabbi Akiva holds pays full damages rather than the standard half, even for a *tam*. *Af tam shechaval b'adam meshalem bemotar nezek shalem* — even a *tam* ox that injures a person pays full damages for the excess of harm over the countervailing harm. Should this not be added as a separate category, making twenty-five?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תַּבְרֵיהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לִגְזִיזֵיהּ; דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל אַף תָּם שֶׁחָבַל בְּאָדָם, יְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יֵעָשֶׂה לוֹ״ – מִגּוּפוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם, וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Didn't Rabbi Akiva break** the force of **his fist [*gezizeih*],** i.e., he significantly tempered the force of his statement, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says:** One **might** have thought that **even** in the case of **an innocuous ox that injured a person,** the owner of the ox **would pay from** his **superior-quality** property and not merely from the body of his animal, as in the case of an innocuous ox that damages property; therefore, **the verse states** with regard to an ox that gores a person: "According to this judgment **shall it be done to him"** (Exodus 21:31), from which it is derived that the owner of the ox **pays** restitution exclusively **from** proceeds of the sale of the **body** of his ox, **and he does not pay from** his **superior-quality** property.

The *gemara* responds: *hatam Rabbi Akiva legaziziah* — Rabbi Akiva blunted the force of his own ruling. Even though he holds that a *tam* ox pays full damages when it injures a person, he also holds that the payment is limited to the body of the animal itself: *miguf meshalem v'eino meshalem min ha'aliyah* — you pay from the animal's body, not from general assets, even though the payment is nominally full rather than half. As a result, this is not a standard unrestricted payment that would belong on a list whose purpose is to declare that all enumerated categories require payment from the best property. Tosafot makes this precise: since the distinctive purpose of these lists is to establish the rule of *meitav* — that payment from land must come from the best land — Rabbi Akiva's special case of *tam* goring a person is excluded because it is categorically limited to the body of the animal and cannot invoke the *meitav* rule at all.

## Personal Injury & Hidden…

### Ones & Mifateh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹנֶס וְהַמְפַתֶּה וְהַמּוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע, דְּמָמוֹנָא הוּא, לִיתְנֵי! מָה נַפְשָׁךְ; אִי נֵזֶק – תְּנָא לֵיהּ, אִי צַעַר – תְּנָא לֵיהּ, אִי בּשֶׁת – תְּנָא לֵיהּ, אִי פְּגָם – הַיְינוּ נֵזֶק. מָה אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – קְנָסָא? בִּקְנָסָא לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: As for **the rapist, and the seducer, and the defamer,** all of **which are** cases in which the offender pays **monetary restitution, let** Rabbi Oshaya **teach** them as categories in his list. The Gemara explains why Rabbi Oshaya did not teach them as separate categories: **Whichever way you** look at it, they should not be listed. **If** the payments listed by Rabbi Ḥiyya are for **damage** caused to the woman's body, which reduces her value, he already **taught it; if** the payments are for **pain,** he already **taught it; if** the payments are for **humiliation,** he already **taught it; if** the payment is for **degradation, that** too **is** a payment for **damage,** which he already taught. **What,** then, **have you to say?** What is the nature of the payments paid by the rapist and the seducer mentioned by Rabbi Ḥiyya? In these cases, each payment paid is considered **a fine [*kenasa*],** and as explained, **with regard to** cases in which one is liable to pay **a fine,** Rabbi Oshaya **does not speak.**

The *gemara* next addresses why *ones*, *mifateh*, and *motzi shem ra* — rape, seduction, and libel — appear on Rabbi Chiya's list but not on Rabbi Oshaya's. The answer is straightforward: *mamma nafshach* — whatever payment in these cases is *mamona*, it is already subsumed within the categories Rabbi Oshaya did enumerate. If we are speaking of *nezek* — the basic loss of value — Rabbi Oshaya's list included *nezek* under personal injury. If we are speaking of *tza'ar* — pain — that too was on his list. If *boshet* — humiliation — again it was included. And *pagam* — the stigma — *hainu nezek*, that is essentially subsumed in *nezek* as well, as will be analyzed more fully when these cases are treated directly.

The only element of *ones* and *mifateh* that is not a personal injury payment is the *knas* — the statutory fine. Rabbi Oshaya does not deal with *knasot*; Rabbi Chiya does. *Motzi shem ra* is similarly classified primarily as *knas*, since one might question whether a payment for reputational harm constitutes genuine monetary compensation in the way that tangible injury does. Rabbi Chiya includes all three because he is cataloguing *knasot* as well.

### Hidden Damages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְטַמֵּא וְהַמְדַמֵּעַ וְהַמְנַסֵּךְ, דְּמָמוֹנָא הוּא, לִיתְנֵי! מָה נַפְשָׁךְ; אִי הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק – הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ נֵזֶק, אִי הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר לָא שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק – הָוֵה לֵיהּ קְנָסָא, וּבִקְנָסָא לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **And one who causes** another's *teruma* **to become ritually impure, and one who mixes** *teruma* with another's non-sacred food, **and one who pours** another's wine **as a libation** for idolatry, each of **which is** a case in which the offender must pay **monetary restitution** to compensate the owner for his loss due to the lowered value of his food or wine, **let** Rabbi Oshaya **teach** them as categories in his list. The Gemara answers: **Whichever way you** look at it, these cases should not be listed. **If** Rabbi Oshaya holds that **damage that is not evident,** i.e., where the object is physically unchanged but its halakhic status is changed, **is characterized as damage** for which one is liable to pay restitution, Rabbi Oshaya already **taught,** i.e., included in his list, restitution for **damage,** and **if** he holds that **damage that is not evident is not characterized as damage** for which one is liable to pay restitution, then **it is a fine, and,** as explained, **with regard to** cases for which one is liable to pay **a fine,** Rabbi Oshaya **does not speak.**

The final three categories — *hametameh*, *hamedameh*, and *hamenasech* — are compensatory: making someone's *terumah* impure, mixing *terumah* with *chullin*, or pouring wine as a libation to *avoda zara* all cause concrete loss, rendering the affected produce either worthless or worth significantly less. The *gemara* asks why these were not on Rabbi Oshaya's list if they are genuine *mamona*.

### Visibility Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא הֶיזֵּק שֶׁאֵינוֹ נִיכָּר לָא שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק? דְּאִי שְׁמֵיהּ הֶיזֵּק, הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ נֵזֶק! תְּנָא הֶיזֵּקָא דְּמִינַּכְרָא, וּתְנָא הֶיזֵּקָא דְּלָא מִינַּכְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Since Rabbi Ḥiyya lists these cases as distinct categories, **let us say** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya holds** that **damage that is not evident is not characterized as damage** for which one is liable to pay restitution, **as, if it were characterized as damage** for which one is liable to pay restitution, **didn't** he already **teach,** i.e., include in his list, restitution for **damage?** The Gemara rejects that suggestion: Even if he holds that damage that is evident is characterized as damage for which one is liable to pay restitution, he distinguishes between different types of damage. He **teaches** cases of **damage that is evident and** he **teaches** cases of **damage that is not evident.**

The *gemara* raises a dilemma: *ei hezeik she'eino nikar shemeih hezeik* — if non-visible damage is considered real damage in the legal sense, then these cases would have been covered under the *nezek* category already on Rabbi Oshaya's list, and they need not have been enumerated separately. *Ei hezeik she'eino nikar lo shemeih hezeik* — if non-visible damage is not legally cognizable damage, then it is only a rabbinic imposition, functioning more like a *knas*, and Rabbi Oshaya would have excluded it from his *mamona*-only list.

The *gemara* resolves that this is not a decisive inference either way. Rabbi Chiya may well hold that *hezeik she'eino nikar shemeih hezeik* — that non-visible damage is real damage — and yet still list these categories separately in order to distinguish between two varieties of *nezek*: visible damage, which is the paradigm, and non-visible damage, which, though equally real, is a distinct type and deserves explicit mention. Just as the *gemara* earlier explained that Rabbi Chiya separately enumerates *shomrim* and *ganav v'gazlan* not because they are legally unrelated, but to underscore that they are conceptually distinct varieties of the same broad category, so too here: *tana hezeka de-minchar v'tana hezeka de-lo minchar* — he teaches visible damage and he teaches non-visible damage, not to resolve the question of whether the latter is legally cognizable, but to ensure that both types are explicitly covered.

A related question arises in the course of this discussion: could reputational harm itself not be characterized as a form of *hezeik she'eino nikar*, particularly when it directly affects one's livelihood? The absence of such a framework in *halacha* is notable, and the question of why defamation is not abstracted into a broader category of non-visible damage — especially when the harm is economically concrete — is an interesting one that the *gemara* here does not directly address.

## List Purpose & Exclusions

### Moser & Mefagel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְתַנָּא דִּידַן, תְּנָא מִנְיָינָא – לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא תְּנָא מִנְיָינָא – לְמַעוֹטֵי דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא. אֶלָּא מִנְיָינָא דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי מוֹסֵר וּמְפַגֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers the fact that each of the lists of primary categories is introduced with the number that they total: **Granted, according to the *tanna* of our** mishna, he **taught the number** of four primary categories in introducing his list **to exclude** those additional categories **of Rabbi Oshaya. And** similarly, **Rabbi Oshaya taught the number** of thirteen primary categories **to exclude** those additional categories **of Rabbi Ḥiyya. But what** does **the number** of twenty-four primary categories **of Rabbi Ḥiyya** serve **to exclude? To exclude** the cases of **an informer,** who provides information to the authorities leading them to confiscate another person's property; **and** to exclude the case of a priest who **renders** an offering ***piggul*** by performing the sacrificial rites for one offering with the intent to sacrifice it for the sake of another offering, in which case the person who brought the offering must replace it with another.

The *gemara* now turns to what Rabbi Chiya's number of twenty-four is designed to exclude — the logic being that if a count is stated, it implies that something is being left out. Two candidates are identified: *moser* and *mefagel*.

*Mefagel* refers to a *kohen* who, at the moment of *shechita*, entertains the intention to eat the offering at the wrong time or in the wrong place, thereby rendering the sacrifice *pigul* and worthless. This case is straightforwardly excluded from Rabbi Chiya's list because *bekodashim lo ka meiri* — these lists are not dealing with the domain of sacred offerings. The *kohen* does pay in such a case — that is not in question — but it falls conceptually outside the scope of the *masechet*.

*Moser* refers to an informer who discloses another person's property or affairs to the authorities, causing the government to seize that property. The informer does pay. The question is why this case is also excluded from Rabbi Chiya's list.

### Words & Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי! בִּשְׁלָמָא מְפַגֵּל – בְּקָדָשִׁים לָא קָמַיְירֵי. אֶלָּא מוֹסֵר, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא תָּנֵי? שָׁאנֵי מוֹסֵר – דְּדִיבּוּרָא, וּבְדִיבּוּרָא לָא קָמַיְירֵי. וְהָא מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע, דְּדִיבּוּרָא הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי! דִּיבּוּרָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא. וְהָא עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין – דִּיבּוּרָא דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא, וְקָתָנֵי! הָתָם, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה, רַחֲמָנָא קַרְיֵיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** Rabbi Ḥiyya **teach** these cases. **Granted,** he did not include the case of a priest **who renders** an offering ***piggul*.** Perhaps, **with regard to** cases involving **consecrated** items, Rabbi Ḥiyya **does not speak. But** with regard to the case of **an informer, what is the reason** Rabbi Ḥiyya **did not teach it** and include it in his list of categories? The Gemara answers: The case of **an informer is different, as** the damage is caused through **speech, and with regard to** damage caused through **speech,** Rabbi Ḥiyya **does not speak. But isn't** there the case of **a defamer, where** the damage **is** caused through **speech, and** yet Rabbi Ḥiyya **teaches** it and includes it in his list? The Gemara answers: He includes that case because **it is** a case of **speech that involves an action.** One who defames his wife is liable only if he made his allegation after engaging in intercourse with her. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** the case of **conspiring witnesses** a case of **speech that involves no action,** as they are liable for their testimony, **and** Rabbi Ḥiyya **teaches** it and includes it in his list? The Gemara answers: **There, although it involves no action, the Merciful One characterizes it** as **an action, as it is written: "And you shall do to him as he conspired to do unto his brother"** (Deuteronomy 19:19).

The *gemara*'s answer is *shani moser d'dibura, u'vidibura lo ka meiri* — the case of the informer involves damage caused purely through speech, and the lists here are not dealing with damage caused by words alone.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: what about *motzi shem ra*, which is on the list and also involves speech? The response is that *motzi shem ra* as it appears in the *parsha* is not pure speech. The *parsha* in the Torah addresses the specific case of a husband who first has sexual relations with his bride and then spreads the false claim that she was not a virgin. There is thus an act — the marital relations — behind the verbal libel. The words do not stand alone. If the husband had spread the same libel without the prior act, he would not fall under the *parsha* at all and would not be liable.

The *gemara* then challenges further: *eidim zomemim* is on the list, and that involves no act at all — it is pure testimony, pure speech. The answer is *rachmana karyei ma'aseh* — the Torah itself characterizes it as an act, as it is written, *v'asitem lo ka'asher zamam la'asot le'achiv* — you shall do to him as he schemed to do to his brother. The Torah describes the conspiratorial testimony using the language of *asiyah*, of doing. Even though the false witnesses did nothing but speak, the Torah treats their speech as equivalent to an act, and that is what allows it to appear on the list. Something so purely a matter of words as informing, without such a textual re-characterization, remains outside the list's scope — even though, as noted, the informer's liability is a genuine topic addressed later in the *masechet*.

## Avot & Best Property

### Abbahu's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְתַנָּא דִּידַן, תַּנָּא ״אָבוֹת״ – מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא תּוֹלְדוֹת; אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא, ״אָבוֹת״ – מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא תּוֹלְדוֹת?! תּוֹלְדוֹתֵיהֶן מַאי נִיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כּוּלָּן כְּאָבוֹת לְשַׁלֵּם מִמֵּיטַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers the subcategories of the various primary categories listed: **Granted, according to the *tanna* of our** mishna, his terminology is understandable, as **he taught primary categories,** which, **by inference,** means **that there are** also additional forms of damage that are **subcategories** of those primary categories. **But** according **to Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Oshaya,** why did they employ the terminology they employed? They employed the term **primary categories,** which, **by inference,** means **that there are subcategories. What are the subcategories** of the primary categories they listed? It appears that they enumerated all the various categories of damage. **Rabbi Abbahu says:** When Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Oshaya characterize, as primary categories, the various categories of damage that they listed, it is not to indicate that there are subcategories; rather, it is to indicate that **all of them are characterized as primary categories,** in the sense that one is liable **to pay from** one's **best**-quality property.

The *gemara* now turns to a more fundamental question: what is the purpose of calling these categories *avot* at all? For the *tanna* of our *mishna*, the answer is clear — *avot* implies *toldot*, that is, the four concrete cases in the Torah are abstract categories with conceptual offspring, other scenarios that share their essential features and are governed by the same rules. But for Rabbi Oshaya and Rabbi Chiya, whose expanded lists include *eidim zomemim*, *motzi shem ra*, and the like, what could *avot* possibly mean? There is no abstracted category of *eidim zomemim* that includes related sub-cases; there is just the one scenario. Why call these *avot*?

Rabbi Abbahu answers: *kulan k'avot l'shaleim m'meitav* — all of these function like the four classic *avot nezikin* in one crucial respect: they require payment from the best property. The rule of *meitav sadeihu u'meitav karmo yeshallem* — that payment from land must come from the best land — is established in the Torah specifically in connection with *shen v'regel*. The four *avot nezikin* of the *mishna* are understood to be linked to one another, and therefore the *meitav* rule derived from *shen v'regel* applies to all four — to *keren*, *bor*, and *eish* as well. Now, by placing all of Rabbi Oshaya's and Rabbi Chiya's additional categories on the same list and calling them *avot*, the point is that they too share this rule: any of these payments, if made from land, must come from the best land.

### Key Terminology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אַתְיָא ״תַּחַת״, ״נְתִינָה״, ״יְשַׁלֵּם״, ״כֶּסֶף״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this, i.e., from where is it derived? The Gemara explains: It **is derived** using a verbal analogy from the terms **"in place of," "giving," "he shall pay,"** and **"money."** The term "in place of" is written with regard to a forewarned ox that gores (Exodus 21:36); "he shall give" is written with regard to an ox that kills a slave (Exodus 21:32); "he shall pay" is written with regard to the categories of damage of Eating and Trampling (Exodus 22:4); and "money" is written with regard to Pit (Exodus 21:34). The *halakha* that one must pay from his best-quality property is stated explicitly in each of those four verses. In each of the twenty-four categories listed by Rabbi Ḥiyya one of those four terms is written, so by means of a verbal analogy, the requirement to pay from one's best-quality property applies to each of them.

The *gemara* then explains how this connection is established: *asya tachas tachas nesina yishalem kesef*. Each of the four classic *avot nezikin* contains one of four key terms — *tachas*, *nesina*, *yishalem*, or *kesef* — and these words serve as the lexical hooks by which the derivation from *shen v'regel*'s *meitav* rule is extended to all of them. *Tachas* appears, for instance, in *shaleim yeshalem shor tachas hashor*; *yishalem* appears in *meitav sadeihu u'meitav karmo yeshallem*, the direct source of the *meitav* rule; *nesina* appears in *kesef shloshim shekalim yitein la'adonav* in connection with the ox that gores a slave; and *kesef* appears in *kesef yashiv l'va'alav*. Having established that all four classic *avot* are linked by this vocabulary to the *meitav* rule, the broader lists declare that everything they enumerate likewise contains one of these key terms and is therefore likewise subject to *meitav*. The reader may verify which term appears in each of the enumerated categories by consulting Rashi's explanation directly.

The conceptual upshot is twofold: the four concrete cases in the Torah are abstracted into categories with *toldot*, and then the four categories themselves are grouped into a single network in which the *meitav* rule circulates across all of them and, by extension, across all the additional categories that carry the relevant terminology.

## Four Categories Necessity

### Mishna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הֲרֵי הַשּׁוֹר כַּהֲרֵי הַמַּבְעֶה. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר – לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא חֲדָא, וְתֵיתֵי אִידַּךְ מִינֵּיהּ; הֲדַר אֲמַר: חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתְיָא. וְלֹא זֶה וָזֶה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן רוּחַ חַיִּים. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָכִי קָאָמַר – לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא תַּרְתֵּי, וְתֵיתֵי אִידַּךְ מִינַּיְיהוּ; הֲדַר אָמַר: חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי לָא אָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: The defining characteristic of **the** primary category of **Ox is not similar to** the defining characteristic of **the** primary category of ***Maveh*.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the mishna **saying? Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava** that **this is** what the mishna **is saying: Let the Merciful One write one** of the primary categories **and derive the other** categories **from it. Then** the *tanna* **says:** It is **not** possible to **derive one** category **from one** other category, as each category has its unique characteristics. The mishna continues: **And** the defining characteristics of **this** category of Ox **and that** category of *Maveh*, **in which there is a living spirit,** are not similar to the defining characteristic of the category of Fire, in which there is no living spirit. The Gemara asks: **What is** the *tanna* **saying? Rav Mesharshiyya said in the name of Rava** that **this is** what the *tanna* **is saying: Let the Merciful One write** only **two** of the primary categories **and derive** the **other** categories **from them. Then** the *tanna* **says:** It is **not** possible to **derive one** category **from two** other categories, as each category has its own unique characteristics, which prevent the derivation of a new category even from a combination of two categories.

The *mishna* states that one cannot derive all four categories from fewer — *lo ha-rei ha-shor k'harei ha-mav'eh* and so on. The *gemara* asks what this assertion means operationally. Why does the *mishna* require all four? Could the Torah not have written fewer and allowed us to derive the rest?

The *gemara* explains this through the structure of *Zevid* and *Mesharshaya*'s respective readings of the *mishna*. The *mishna* is saying: the Torah could not have written just one category and expected us to derive the rest, because each has some feature the others lack. Could it have written two? No — any two share a feature that makes them inapplicable to everything else. Could it have written three? Consider *shen*, *regel*, and *eish*: all three are things that go out and cause damage, whereas *bor* stays in one place and the damage comes to it. One could not have learned *bor* from the other three. So why not just write three plus *bor*, and derive everything from the four minus one?

### Rava's Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְכוּלְּהוּ כִּי שָׁדֵית בּוֹר בֵּינַיְיהוּ – אָתְיָא כּוּלְּהוּ בַּמֶּה הַצַּד, לְבַר מִקֶּרֶן; מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ, מָה לְכוּלְּהוּ שֶׁכֵּן מוּעָדִין מִתְּחִילָּתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And** with regard to **all of** the primary categories in the mishna, **when you cast** the primary category of **Pit among them, all** of the rest **of them can** then **be derived through** an analogy based on **the common factor** of two categories. This is the case with all of the categories **except for Goring, due to** the fact **that there is** room **to refute** the analogy to teach the *halakha* of Goring with the following contention: **What** is notable **about all of** the other categories? They are notable **in that they are forewarned from their outset.** Since it is the typical manner of the animal to trample and eat, and the typical manner of fire to burn, and the typical manner of a pit to serve as an obstacle, the owner is always forewarned with regard to safeguarding against damage and is liable to pay full damages for his failure to prevent the damage even the first time. With regard to Goring, the animal is innocuous for the first three instances of damage and only thereafter is the animal forewarned, rendering its owner liable to pay full damages.

Rava intervenes with a sharpened analysis: in truth, *d'shadita bor beineihu asu chulhu b'ma hatzad* — if you insert *bor* alongside any one of the other categories, you can derive everything from that pair by *ma hatzad*, the common denominator. Here is the reasoning. *Bor* is the weakest of the categories: it does not go out to damage, it does not have *ruach chayim*, and its whole hazardous character consists in the fact that its creation is itself the beginning of the hazard — *techilat asiyato lenezek*. If you are liable for *bor*, which has none of the features that make the other categories obviously dangerous, surely you should be liable for those others which do possess those features. Conversely, *bor* lacks the feature of going out to damage, which *shen*, *regel*, and *eish* all share — but if you add any one of those alongside *bor*, the common denominator eliminates both distinctive features and yields a pure principle: anything that is my property and that I have an obligation to guard, if it causes damage, I am liable. That principle captures *shen*, *regel*, *eish*, and *bor* together.

The one thing that *bor* plus one other category cannot derive is *keren*.

### Keren Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אַדְּרַבָּה, קֶרֶן עֲדִיפָא – שֶׁכַּוּוֹנָתוֹ לְהַזִּיק; אֲפִילּוּ קֶרֶן נָמֵי אָתְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to the one who says: On the contrary, Goring is superior** in the sense that one's liability in a case of Goring is more apparent, **as the objective of** the ox's action **is to cause damage, even Goring** can **be derived** from the common factor of two sources.

Why can *keren* not be derived? Because *keren* is weaker than all the others in one critical respect: *mu'adin mitichlatan* — all the other categories have an inherent propensity to cause the damage they cause from the very outset. *Shen* naturally eats; *regel* naturally tramples; *eish* naturally spreads. Even *bor* is, by its nature, a hazard. *Keren* — goring — is not the natural behavior of an ox. A normal ox does not gore. Therefore, if the Torah had only given me the other categories and had not separately addressed goring, I might have concluded that the first three instances of goring were so unanticipated as to create no obligation at all — complete exemption. I would never have inferred from the other categories that you pay even half damages for completely unexpected behavior. *Keren* had to be stated independently.

Rava's conclusion is therefore: you needed *bor* plus one other category, and you needed *keren* independently. You did not need all four distinct categories. What the *mishna* says is strictly true — you cannot learn one from the other — but the *gemara* here is sharpening that claim: the impossibility is not between every pair, but between the *keren* case and everything else.

## Keren Characterization

### Below vs. Above Line

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא כַּתְבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא? לְהִלְכוֹתֵיהֶן. קֶרֶן – לְחַלֵּק בֵּין תַּמָּה לְמוּעֶדֶת. שֵׁן וָרֶגֶל – לְפוֹטְרָן בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים. בּוֹר – לִפְטוֹר בּוֹ אֶת הַכֵּלִים. וּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל נִזְקֵי כֵלִים בְּבוֹר – לִפְטוֹר בּוֹ אֶת הָאָדָם. אָדָם – לְחַיְּיבוֹ בְּאַרְבָּעָה דְּבָרִים. אֵשׁ – לִפְטוֹר בּוֹ אֶת הַטָּמוּן. וּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל נִזְקֵי טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ, לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if all the categories can be derived from another category and Pit, **for** the purpose of deriving **what *halakha* did the Merciful One write** all the primary categories explicitly? The Gemara answers: It is in order **to** derive **their** unique ***halakhot*** that apply exclusively to each category. **Goring** is written explicitly in order **to distinguish between** the *halakhot* of damage caused by **an innocuous** animal **and** damage caused by **a forewarned** animal. **Eating and Trampling** are written explicitly in order **to exempt** from liability those whose animals perform the actions in these categories **in a public domain. Pit** is written explicitly in order **to exempt** one from liability **for** damage to **vessels** caused when they fall into a pit. The Gemara adds: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who deems one liable** even **for damage to vessels** caused when they fall **into a pit,** Pit was written in order **to exempt** one from liability **for** the death of **a person** who was killed by falling into a pit. Rabbi Yehuda concedes that the possessor of the pit is exempt because the person who ultimately fell into the pit was capable of avoiding it. **Man** is written explicitly in order **to render him liable for four** additional **types** of indemnity, beyond the payment of damages for the diminution in value caused when one injures a person. **Fire** is written explicitly in order **to exempt** one from liability for damage caused to **a concealed** object, e.g., one hidden by grain, that was consumed by fire. **And according to Rabbi Yehuda, who deems one liable** even **for damage** done to **a concealed** object damaged by **fire, to add what** *halakha* does the Torah mention the category of Fire explicitly?

The question can be framed in two ways. One approach holds that ordinary animals do not have a propensity to gore — *stam shor bechezkat shimur*, a standard ox is presumed to be watched and not dangerous. If an ox gores, it is essentially an *ones* for the owner; strictly speaking, the owner should be fully exempt. The Torah, however, imposes a half-payment as a kind of incentive to be more vigilant. On this reading, the half-payment of *keren* is a *knas* — the owner is technically not negligent, and the payment is not compensation for recognized harm but rather a punitive imposition.

The opposing approach holds that there is just enough inherent risk in an ox — animals are occasionally prone to this behavior — that an owner does have some baseline obligation of care. The ox is technically just above the line of required vigilance. Therefore, strictly speaking, the owner should pay full damages. But because it is so close to the line, the Torah goes easy on the owner the first three times and requires only half. On this reading, the half-payment is *mamona* — the owner is genuinely negligent, the damage is real, and the reduced payment reflects leniency applied to a borderline case.

This debate — which will be treated fully at the end of the *perek* — governs the present discussion. If *keren* is below the line, it cannot be derived from the other categories, because those categories all involve clear liability; *keren* would be a special imposition of its own. If *keren* is above the line — if it really is the case that an owner ought to anticipate goring — then the *tzad hashaveh* argument would have reached it, and the Torah's explicit mention of *keren* comes only to narrow its scope.

## Category-Specific Laws

### Keren Laws

Once the *gemara* establishes that the four categories nonetheless stand as distinct *avot*, it enumerates the unique *halachot* that attach to each one and that justify the separate enumeration. For *keren*: the half-damage rule and the distinction between a *tam* — an ox that has not yet established a pattern of goring, for which only half damages are paid — and a *mu'ad* — an ox that has gored three times and has been formally warned, for which full damages are paid.

### Shen & Regel Laws

For *shen* and *regel*: the exemption in the public domain — *patur b'reshut harabim*. When an animal eats or tramples in the public domain, the owner is exempt. This may reflect a public policy consideration that people must be permitted to move their animals through public spaces, or it may reflect the principle of *pechitat hanizek* — contributory exposure, the idea that one who leaves produce in a public thoroughfare where animals are expected to pass has assumed the risk.

### Bor Laws

For *bor*: the exemption for vessels. If a camel falls into a pit and vessels carried on its back are broken, the one who dug the pit is exempt for the vessels. And according to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that one is liable even for vessels damaged in a pit, *bor* carries a different distinctive rule: exemption when a person is killed by falling into the pit. The distinctive contours of liability in each case justify *bor* as a separate category.

### Adam Laws

For *adam* — a human being who directly injures another person: the obligation to pay the four additional elements beyond *nezek* that apply when the victim is a person rather than property — *tza'ar*, *ripui*, *shevet*, and *boshet* — pain, medical expenses, lost labor, and humiliation.

### Eish Laws

For *eish*: the exemption for concealed objects. If a fire spreads and burns a haystack, and within the haystack there was something buried — a hidden object not exposed to view — the one who sent the fire is exempt from liability for that concealed item. According to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds one liable even for buried objects, the special *chiddush* of the *eish* category is the rule that one is liable even when the fire charred rather than fully consumed — *lichacha niro v'sichacha avanav*. If a fire singed the surface of another's plowing or charred stones, though it did not fully destroy the underlying material, liability still attaches. Since this is not the paradigmatic manner in which fire destroys, one might have thought it falls outside the category — the *chiddush* is that it does not.

## Concrete & Abstract

### Summary & Open Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **The common denominator of** the components in all these categories is that it is their typical manner to cause damage. The Gemara asks: In order **to add what** *halakha* is this statement cited in the mishna?

Each of the four *avot nezikin* thus carries its own distinctive *halachot* that apply specifically when a case is categorized within it. The *mishna* required all four to be stated independently because these distinct laws — the half-damage rule for *keren*, the public domain exemption for *shen* and *regel*, the vessel and person exemptions for *bor*, the personal injury payments for *adam*, and the concealment rule for *eish* — could not be derived from one another or collapsed into a smaller number of categories.

But this generates a question that will carry the discussion forward: having established four concrete categories with their own specific laws, the *gemara* has also identified a broad abstract principle that encompasses all of them and more.

### Preview Tomorrow

That principle is *kol she-darko lehazik u'shemirasan alecha chav hamazik leshallem* — anything that has a propensity to cause damage and whose guarding falls upon you obligates payment if it damages. This *tzad hashaveh*, the common denominator across all four categories, is itself a *halachic* proposition. The question that opens tomorrow's discussion is: when a case fits neatly under this abstract rule but cannot be placed precisely within any one of the four concrete categories, what *halachot* govern it? Does it inherit the specific rules of whichever category it most resembles — the half-damage rule, the public domain exemption, the concealment exemption — or does the abstract category operate with its own distinct set of rules, independent of the four concrete ones? The challenge of holding both a set of specific concrete categories and a single broad abstract principle simultaneously, and of applying them coherently to novel cases, is the agenda for the discussion ahead.
