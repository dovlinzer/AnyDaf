# Bava Kamma 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Answers Intro

### Core Problem Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּהֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – שְׁחִיטָה מַתֶּרֶת?! וַהֲלֹא זְרִיקָה מַתֶּרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar wondered about this** discussion: **According to** the explanations attributed to **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** which are based on the assumption that this is referring to slaughtering a sacrificial animal inside the Temple, is it the **slaughtering** of the animal that **renders** it **permitted** for consumption? **But isn't it** the **sprinkling** of the blood that **renders** it **permitted?** It is prohibited to eat sacrificial meat immediately after the slaughter; only after the sprinkling of the blood is it permitted to consume the meat. As the slaughter itself does not render the animal's meat permitted, there should be no liability to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment.

The sugya before us opens with a fundamental difficulty rooted in Rabbi Shimon's own position. The Mishnah addresses a case where a thief steals a cow or sheep that is *kodshim* — a sanctified animal. Because the thief bears *achrayus*, financial responsibility to replace the animal should it be lost, the animal is treated as if it were his own for purposes of the theft law, making him liable for *kefel* (double payment). All of this is straightforward. The difficulty arises when the thief proceeds to slaughter or sell the animal, triggering the four-and-five penalty. How can one be liable for *tvicha* — slaughter — of a *kodshim* animal?

Rabbi Shimon himself is the very authority who rules that a *shechita* which does not render the meat permissible for eating does not qualify as a valid act of *shechita* (*shechita she'eina re'uya*). And here, if the animal is *kodshim* slaughtered outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, it is *assur behana'ah* — forbidden to derive any benefit from — making the slaughter apparently invalid. So how does one ever arrive at a case of four-and-five involving a stolen *kodshim* animal?

### Yochanan Answer 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – שְׁחִיטָה מַתֶּרֶת?! וַהֲלֹא זְרִיקָה מַתֶּרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** **According to** the explanations attributed to **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** which are based on the assumption that this is referring to slaughtering a sacrificial animal inside the Temple, is it the **slaughtering** of the animal that **renders** it **permitted** for consumption? **But isn't it** the **sprinkling** of the blood that **renders** it **permitted?**

Rabbi Yochanan's first answer is that the thief slaughtered the animal inside the *Beis Hamikdash* as a *korban* on behalf of the animal's owner. At first glance, this seems to undermine the premise of the case entirely — bringing the animal as a *korban* for the owner would be a form of returning it, such that the theft obligation would dissolve. The Gemara therefore qualifies the scenario: the thief slaughtered it with that intent, but then the blood was spilled before *zerika* — the blood-sprinkling on the *mizbe'ach* — could be performed. As a result, the owner never received the benefit of the *korban*, and the thief's act remained one of ongoing *geneiva*. Yet since the slaughter was performed in a context where the blood could have been sprinkled — which would have made the meat permissible — it is considered a *shechita re'uya*, a valid act of slaughter. That this is so even though the blood was ultimately never thrown is precisely what the Gemara will now have to establish.

### Yochanan Answer 2

Rabbi Yochanan's second answer is more direct. The thief slaughtered the animal inside the *Beis Hamikdash* not for the sake of the owner — *shelo lishmo*, not for the owner's obligation. Since the *korban* was brought *shelo lishmo*, it does not discharge the owner's obligation (*lo oleh labaalim lishum chova*), so there is no concern that this constitutes a return of the stolen animal. And yet the slaughter is undeniably valid, the meat is permissible — it will be eaten by the *kohanim* or by those who brought a *shelamim* — making this unambiguously a *shechita re'uya*.

### Reish Lakish Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: בְּשׁוֹחֵט בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says** yet another explanation as to why the principal has not been returned to the owner: This is referring to a case **where** the thief **slaughters blemished** animals **outside** the Temple. A blemished animal may be slaughtered outside the Temple and its meat may be eaten, provided that it is redeemed with money.

Reish Lakish offers a third scenario entirely. Rather than situating the slaughter inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, Reish Lakish says the thief slaughtered the animal outside, but it was a *ba'al mum* — a blemished animal. Because the animal had a blemish, there is no violation of *shechutei chutz* (slaughtering sanctified animals outside the Temple), since such an animal is not *ra'uy lepetach ohel moed* — it could not have been brought to the altar. More importantly for our purposes, a blemished *kodshim* animal can be redeemed. Because it is not fit for the *mizbe'ach*, it is eligible for *pidyon* — redemption — through which its sanctity transfers to the redemption money, rendering the animal itself ordinary and permissible for consumption. It is precisely this potential for redemption, Reish Lakish argues, that qualifies the slaughter as a *shechita re'uya*.

## Kol Ha'omed Doctrine

### Elazar's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – שְׁחִיטָה מַתֶּרֶת?! וַהֲלֹא פְּדִיָּיה מַתֶּרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Elazar continues: And **according to** the explanation of **Reish Lakish,** who states that the case is referring to the slaughter of a consecrated blemished animal outside the Temple, is it the **slaughtering** of the animal that **renders** it **permitted** for consumption? **But isn't it** the **redemption** of the animal that **renders** it **permitted?** Since the thief slaughtered a sacrificial animal, it is prohibited to eat its meat until its sanctity is removed by redeeming it with money. Once again, the slaughter itself does not render the meat permitted, and here too, there should be no liability to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment.

Rabbi Elazar was unsatisfied — the Aramaic term used is *toyba*, which Rashi reads not as wonder but as something that didn't pass the "sniff test," in the spirit of *tohey bat tiha*. His challenge cuts across both Rabbi Yochanan's first answer and Reish Lakish's answer: in each case, is it the *shechita* alone that renders the meat permissible? For Rabbi Yochanan's first scenario — where the blood was ultimately spilled — you need *zerika*, the throwing of the blood, to permit the meat. For Reish Lakish's scenario, you need *pidyon* — redemption — to permit the meat. In neither case does the *shechita* itself complete the process. So how can either be called a *shechita re'uya*?

Tosafot add an important clarification here. Regarding Rabbi Yochanan's second answer — the animal slaughtered in the *Beis Hamikdash shelo lishmo* — Tosafot do not consider this to be problematic at all. If the blood is eventually thrown, we look back retroactively and treat the entire sequence — *shechita* followed by *zerika* — as the act that rendered the meat permissible. The *shechita* was part of a complete process that culminated in the meat becoming *muttar*. The genuine difficulty, Tosafot explain, is only in the two scenarios where the second step was not completed: either because the blood was spilled, or because the animal was a *ba'al mum* slaughtered outside the *Beis Hamikdash*. In those cases, the meat never actually became permissible, and it may be too late to do anything about it — Tosafot raise the question of whether one can even redeem an animal post-slaughter. For it is one thing to say the *shechita* initiated a process that was completed; it is another to say the *shechita* initiated a process that was never completed, and call the *shechita* valid nonetheless.

### Kol Ha'omed Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כׇּל הָעוֹמֵד לִזְרוֹק – כְּזָרוּק דָּמֵי, וְכׇל הָעוֹמֵד לִפְדּוֹת – כְּפָדוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Apparently, **this** following statement **of Rabbi Shimon escaped** Rabbi Elazar, who asked these questions. Rabbi Shimon holds that **any** blood **that is ready to be sprinkled is considered** as though it had already been **sprinkled, and** likewise, **any** animal **that is ready to be redeemed is considered** as if it had already been **redeemed.**

The Gemara's response is that Rabbi Elazar had forgotten a principle of Rabbi Shimon: *kol ha'omed lizrok kezarok dami* — anything that was in a position to have its blood sprinkled is treated as if the blood was sprinkled. And the parallel: *kol ha'omed lifdos k'padui dami* — anything that was in a position to be redeemed is treated as if it was redeemed.

A clarification is essential here: these principles do not mean that one achieves *kappara* without actual *zerika*, or that one may benefit from *hekdesh* without actual *pidyon*. The scope of the principle is narrower and precisely defined: it is asking whether the meat may be considered *ra'ui la'achila* — fit for eating, even in a notional sense. *Ra'ui*, fitness, is by its nature a hypothetical category — was this meat in a position to become edible? If there existed a path by which it could have been rendered edible, then it is considered food, and that status is what underlies both the law of *tumas ochlin* (food-related impurity) and the definition of *shechita re'uya*.

### Notar Food Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הָעוֹמֵד לִזְרוֹק כְּזָרוּק דָּמֵי – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁהוּא מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין, וְיֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proves that Rabbi Shimon maintains these two opinions. Rabbi Shimon holds that **any** blood **that is ready to be sprinkled is considered** as though it had already been **sprinkled, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: There are** cases involving **leftover** sacrificial meat **after the time allotted for its consumption [*notar*],** in **which** the meat is susceptible to **contracting ritual impurity of food, and there are** cases of **leftover** sacrificial meat in which the meat **is not** susceptible to **contracting ritual impurity of food.**

Rabbi Elazar is told that he had overlooked a teaching of Rabbi Shimon — a different Rabbi Shimon, though as the Gemara proceeds, all the Rabbi Shimons converge beautifully. The principle: *kol ha'omed lizrok kezarok dami* — if the blood was in a position to be thrown on the *mizbe'ach*, it is treated as though it was thrown. This does not mean one obtains atonement without the actual *zerika*; it means specifically that for the purpose of evaluating whether the meat can be considered food — whether it is *ra'ui la'achila* — we apply this principle. Since the word *ra'ui* itself is inherently hypothetical (was the meat *fit* to be eaten?), it is sufficient that there existed a realistic possibility of the meat becoming edible.

### Notar Before/After Zerika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? לָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין. לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If sacrificial meat was disqualified due to the fact that it **remained overnight,** i.e., after the allotted time for that offering, **before** the **sprinkling** of its blood, it **is not** susceptible to **contracting ritual impurity of food,** as it was never fit for consumption. According to Rabbi Shimon, for an item to be susceptible to contracting ritual impurity of food it must have been permitted for consumption or benefit, either at present or at some point in the past. Sacrificial meat may not be consumed before the sprinkling of its blood, nor may it be consumed if it had been left over after a specific period of time, in most cases overnight. If it was disqualified by being left overnight before its blood had been sprinkled, there was never a time when the meat was permitted. By contrast, if the meat was disqualified by being left over **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, it **is** susceptible to **contracting ritual impurity of food,** as it was permitted for consumption at some point after the sprinkling of the blood.

The *Baraisa* draws the distinction as follows. If the meat *lan lifnei zerika* — spent the night before *zerika* was performed — it does not contract *tumas ochlin*. If it became *notar* after *zerika* — *le'achar zerika* — it does.

The Gemara then clarifies what *lifnei zerika* and *le'achar zerika* mean in practice. They do not refer simply to whether the blood was literally thrown before or after. Rather: *kodem shenir'eh lezerika* — the meat spent the night before it ever had an opportunity for the blood to be thrown, versus *le'achar shenir'eh lezerika* — after there had been at least one moment during which the blood could have been thrown.

### Paradigm Case of No Zerika Opportunity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַיְימָא לַן: מַאי ״לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה״ – קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּרְאָה לִזְרִיקָה. ״לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה״ – לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּרְאָה לִזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And we maintain,** as a result of a discussion recorded in tractate *Menaḥot* (101b): **What** is the meaning of: **Before** the **sprinkling** of the blood? It means **before** the blood **became fit for sprinkling.** And what is meant by: **After** the **sprinkling** of the blood? This means **after** the blood **became fit for sprinkling,** i.e., immediately subsequent to the slaughter.

The paradigm case of meat that never had a moment for *zerika*: the animal was slaughtered just before sunset, right at the edge of nightfall, at a point when there was simply no time left in the day for the *avoda* to be performed in the *Mikdash*. The blood was caught, but the moment the day ended there was no longer an opportunity for *zerika*. Such meat never had *sha'as hakosher* — a window of fitness — and therefore is not considered food. It cannot become *tamei* with *tumas ochlin*.

Contrast this with meat where the animal was slaughtered with sufficient daylight remaining that the blood could have been thrown. In that case, even if the *zerika* was never performed, the meat had its moment of potential fitness. And that moment suffices to categorize it as food.

### Elicit Kol Ha'omed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּרְאָה לִזְרִיקָה לָן – מַאי הִיא? דְּלָא הָוְיָא שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם לְמִזְרְקֵיהּ, דְּשַׁחְטֵיהּ סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה – וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What is** the case in which the meat **remained overnight before** the blood **became fit for sprinkling?** Since the blood is fit for sprinkling almost immediately after the animal's slaughter, how is it possible for the meat to become *notar* before that stage? The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case **where there was not enough time in the day to sprinkle** the blood, **as he slaughtered** the offering **immediately prior to sunset.** The blood was never fit for sprinkling, as the act of sprinkling must be performed before sunset. **And** in this case the meat is **not** susceptible to **contracting ritual impurity of food,** as there was never a time when it was permitted for consumption.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּרְאָה לִזְרִיקָה לָן, דַּהֲוַיא שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם לְמִזְרְקֵיהּ – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין. אַלְמָא כָּל הָעוֹמֵד לִזְרוֹק – כְּזָרוּק דָּמֵי,
>
> **Translation:** What is the case where the meat **remained overnight after** the blood **became fit for sprinkling?** It was **when there was** enough **time** remaining **in the day** after the slaughter **to sprinkle** the blood, and therefore the meat **is** susceptible to **contracting ritual impurity of food.** The Gemara states its conclusion: **Evidently,** Rabbi Shimon maintains that **any** blood **that is ready to be sprinkled is considered** as though it had already been **sprinkled.**

The Gemara draws its conclusion: *alma kol ha'omed lizrok kezarok dami* — you see from this that anything which was in a position to have its blood sprinkled is treated as though the blood was sprinkled — at least for the purpose of determining whether the meat is considered food. This is precisely the operative principle for *shechita re'uya* as well. The goal of *shechita re'uya* is to establish that the act of slaughter produced edible meat. Since the blood could have been thrown at the moment of *shechita*, we treat the meat as though it were edible. Accordingly, even in Rabbi Yochanan's first scenario where the blood was ultimately spilled, because at the time of slaughter the blood could have been thrown, this qualifies as a *shechita re'uya*, and the thief is liable for the four-and-five payment.

## Kol Ha'omed Redemption

### Para Aduma Impurity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הָעוֹמֵד לִפְדּוֹת – כְּפָדוּי דָּמֵי. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר:
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Shimon holds that **any** animal **that is ready to be redeemed is considered** as though it is already **redeemed, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרָה – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין, הוֹאִיל וְהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The meat of the red **heifer is** susceptible to **contracting ritual impurity of food,** despite the fact that it is prohibited to derive benefit from such meat, **since it had a time when it was fit** for consumption.

The Gemara now extends the parallel principle — *kol ha'omed lifdos k'padui dami* — through an analogous *Baraisa*. The case involves a *para aduma*, the red heifer. It should be noted that the *para aduma* occupies a fascinating hybrid status: it is *kodshei bedek habayis* rather than *kodshei mizbe'ach*, not technically a *korban* in the conventional sense, though sharing many similarities with one. The question posed is whether the slaughtered *para aduma* can contract *tumas ochlin* — food-related impurity.

This raises an immediate puzzle: the meat of a *para aduma* is never intended to be eaten. It is *kodshim*, entirely *assur*, and destined to be burned. How could it ever be considered food? Tosafot engage at length with a further complication — the *para aduma* is itself a source of *tuma*, so why discuss it acquiring *tuma* from outside? — but the straightforward resolution is that the *para aduma* generates *tuma* specifically in the context of the purification process, not through ordinary contact.

### Para Redemption Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: פָּרָה נִפְדֵּית עַל גַּבֵּי מַעֲרַכְתָּהּ. אַלְמָא כׇּל הָעוֹמֵד לִפְדּוֹת – כְּפָדוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says,** in explanation of Rabbi Shimon's statement: **Rabbi Shimon would say** that the red **heifer can be redeemed** with money even when it has already been slaughtered **upon its pyre,** i.e., for the sake of purification. For this reason Rabbi Shimon claimed that the meat had a time when it was fit for consumption, as it can be rendered permitted for consumption through redemption. The Gemara states its conclusion: **Evidently,** Rabbi Shimon holds that **any** animal **that is ready to be redeemed is considered** as though it has already been **redeemed.**

Reish Lakish transmits a teaching in the name of Rabbi Shimon — and once again, all the same figures converge: *amar Reish Lakish amar Rabbi Shimon*, Rabbi Shimon used to say: *para nifdeis al gabei maarachta* — a *para aduma* can be redeemed even while its carcass sits atop the pyre, right up until the moment the blood is sprinkled. Even at that ultimate last moment before burning commences, one could declare: we will redeem this *para aduma* and begin the process again, perhaps with a better animal or with improved intentions. Tosafot note that presumably this window closes once *zrikas hadam* has been performed, but until that point, redemption remains available.

### Fitness for Redemption

This teaching establishes that the *para aduma*, between the moment of slaughter and the moment of *zerika*, had a window during which it could have been redeemed and its sanctity transferred to the redemption money, rendering the meat theoretically permissible. That window of fitness — *sha'as hakosher* — is sufficient to classify the meat as food, even though no *mitzva* requires one to redeem it, and even though no one ever would. Indeed, this case is an even more striking *chiddush* than the *zerika* case: by a *korban*, there is an affirmative *mitzva* to perform *zerika*. Here, there is no *mitzva* to redeem the *para aduma* — redemption is merely a theoretical option. And yet the mere theoretical possibility of redemption, the fact that it *could have* been eaten after redemption, confers upon the meat the status of food.

The Gemara therefore states: *alma kol ha'omed lifdos k'padui dami* — anything that was in a position to be redeemed is treated as though it was redeemed, for the purpose of determining whether it is considered food.

### Reish Lakish Vindicated

The application to Reish Lakish's original answer follows directly. When a thief slaughters a blemished *kodshim* animal outside the *Beis Hamikdash*, the meat was never actually eaten and the animal was never actually redeemed. But because it *could have* been redeemed — because, as Reish Lakish himself taught, redemption remains available right up until the last moment — the slaughter is treated as a *shechita re'uya*. The thief is therefore liable for the four-and-five penalty.

## Yochanan vs Reish Lakish

### Yochanan's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ – דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְאוֹקֹמַהּ לְמַתְנִיתִין אֲפִילּוּ בִּתְמִימִין. אֶלָּא רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ – מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the relative merits of the answers provided by Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish: **Granted, Rabbi Yoḥanan did not state** an answer **in accordance with** that of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** i.e., Reish Lakish, that the mishna is referring to a blemished animal, **because he wants to interpret the mishna** as dealing with all kinds of sacrificial animals, **even with unblemished** ones. **But what is the reason** that **Reish Lakish did not state** an answer **in accordance with** that of **Rabbi Yoḥanan?**

Having established that both Rabbi Yochanan's and Reish Lakish's scenarios are internally coherent, the Gemara turns to an equally important question: why did each *amora* not simply adopt the other's scenario?

The Gemara begins with the easier case. *Bishlama* Rabbi Yochanan did not employ Reish Lakish's *ba'al mum* scenario — *d'kabai l'okme masnisin afilu b'tamimim* — because he did not want to limit the Mishnah's ruling to blemished animals. Rabbi Yochanan did not dispute Reish Lakish; he acknowledged that a blemished animal slaughtered outside the *Beis Hamikdash* would indeed be a valid case. But the Mishnah's law should apply even to unblemished animals brought inside the *Beis Hamikdash*, and Rabbi Yochanan therefore framed his answers to account for that broader scope.

### Tvicha u-Mechira Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ: ״וּטְבָחוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ״ – כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ בִּמְכִירָה אִיתֵיהּ בִּטְבִיחָה, וְכֹל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בִּמְכִירָה לֵיתַנְהוּ בִּטְבִיחָה. וְהָנֵי קֳדָשִׁים – הוֹאִיל דְּכִי מְזַבֵּין קֳדָשִׁים לָא הָוְיָא מְכִירָה, לֵיתַנְהוּ בִּטְבִיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish could have **said to you** that the verse states: "If a man steals an ox or a sheep, **and slaughters it or sells it"** (Exodus 21:37). This verse compares slaughtering to selling, thereby indicating that **anywhere that** the fourfold or fivefold payment **is** applicable **for** the **sale** of a particular animal **it is** likewise applicable **for** its **slaughter, and anywhere that** the fourfold or fivefold payment **is not** applicable **for** the **sale** of an animal **it is not** applicable **for** its **slaughter** either. **And** with regard to **this** case of **sacrificial** animals, **since when one sells sacrificial** animals **it is not** a valid **sale,** and no fourfold or fivefold payment is incurred, so too, **there is no** fourfold or fivefold payment for their **slaughter.**

The more substantive question is why Reish Lakish did not adopt Rabbi Yochanan's scenarios. Here the Gemara introduces a new principle — *tvicha u-mechira*. The verse states *u'tvacho o mechoro* — "and slaughtered it or sold it" — and Reish Lakish derives from this juxtaposition a rule of structural symmetry: *kol heicha de-isa bi-mechira isa bi-tvicha* — wherever there is liability for *mechira* (sale), there is liability for *tvicha* (slaughter); *ve-chol heicha de-leisa bi-mechira leisa bi-tvicha* — wherever there is no liability for *mechira*, there is no liability for *tvicha*. The two forms of liability are interdependent.

### Unblemished Sale Impossible

This principle explains why Reish Lakish cannot accept Rabbi Yochanan's scenarios. An unblemished *kodshim* animal cannot be sold. If the thief attempted to transfer the animal to a third party, no valid sale could occur — one cannot acquire a *kodshim* animal through a standard commercial transaction, because the animal's sanctity cannot be transferred by sale. Consequently, since the thief could not have been liable for *mechira*, he cannot be liable for *tvicha* either. This is not a disagreement with Rabbi Yochanan about whether the *shechita* was technically valid — Reish Lakish may well agree that slaughtering an unblemished *kodshim* inside the *Beis Hamikdash* constitutes a *shechita re'uya*. Rather, his objection is structural: the Torah's four-and-five penalty operates through a linked framework where liability for sale and liability for slaughter stand or fall together.

### Both Sages Consistent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ – דְּאִתְּמַר: הַמּוֹכֵר טְרֵיפָה לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חַיָּיב, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** these two Sages **follow their** own lines of **reasoning. As it was stated: According to the statement of Rabbi Shimon** that an act of slaughter that is not fit for accomplishing its full ritual purpose is not considered an act of slaughter at all and does not entail liability to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, in the case of a thief who **sells** a stolen **animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The thief is **liable** to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment if he sells the animal rather than slaughters it. **And Reish Lakish says:** He is **exempt.**

The Gemara confirms that this dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish is not ad hoc; they are each applying their own consistently held positions. *Ve-azdu le-ta'amaihu* — and they have gone each according to his own reasoning — meaning this is a known, independent debate between these two *amoraim* about the relationship between *shechita* and *mechira* within the four-and-five framework.

### Reish Lakish Interdependence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר חַיָּיב – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בִּטְבִיחָה, אִיתֵיהּ בִּמְכִירָה. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר פָּטוּר – כֵּיוָן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בִּטְבִיחָה, לֵיתֵיהּ בִּמְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that he is **liable,** as **even though** the fourfold or fivefold payment **is not** applicable **for the slaughter** of a *tereifa* according to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, as its meat may not be eaten, nevertheless, **it is** applicable **for** its **sale. And Reish Lakish says** that the thief is **exempt,** as, **since** the fourfold or fivefold payment **is not** applicable **for** the **slaughter** of a *tereifa* according to Rabbi Shimon, **it is not** applicable **for** its **sale** either.

Reish Lakish's position is stated with full symmetry: *kol heicha de-isa bi-mechira isa bi-tvicha ve-chol heicha de-leisa bi-mechira leisa bi-tvicha*. The liability for slaughter and the liability for sale are perfectly interdependent. Being unable to incur liability for one necessarily precludes liability for the other. It is this principle that drives Reish Lakish to require the *ba'al mum* scenario: a blemished *kodshim* animal can in principle be sold — through redemption, the sanctity transfers and the animal can change hands — and therefore the *tvicha* liability can also attach.

## Treifa Case Test

### Treifa Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: גָּנַב כִּלְאַיִם וּטְבָחָהּ, טְרֵיפָה וּמְכָרָהּ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. מַאי, לָאו רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא? אַלְמָא אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בִּטְבִיחָה – אִיתֵיהּ בִּמְכִירָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** from the following *baraita*: If one **stole** an animal of **diverse kinds,** e.g., a sheep-goat hybrid, **and slaughtered it,** or if he stole **a *tereifa* and sold it, he pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment. What, is it not** correct to say that this *baraita*, which mentions the case of selling a stolen *tereifa* but not slaughtering it, **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon? Apparently,** according to Rabbi Shimon, **even though** the fourfold or fivefold payment **is not** applicable **for** the **slaughter** of a *tereifa*, **it is** nevertheless applicable **for** its **sale.**

The Gemara now introduces a *Baraisa* that puts this debate to the test. *Ganav kilayim u-tvacho, tereifa u-mechoro, meshalem tashlumei arba ve-chamisha* — if one stole a *kilayim* (crossbred animal) and slaughtered it, or a *tereifa* and sold it, he pays four-and-five. The Gemara immediately flags this as significant: why does the *Baraisa* specifically cite *selling* a *tereifa*, rather than *slaughtering* it? This is surprising if, according to Rabbi Shimon, *shechita* of a *tereifa* is not valid — since the meat of a *tereifa* is never permissible, the slaughter does not render it edible, and therefore it is not a *shechita re'uya*.

### Yochanan's Challenge

Rabbi Yochanan presses the point. The very fact that the *Baraisa* chooses to discuss *selling* a *tereifa* and not *slaughtering* it suggests that the *Baraisa* is operating according to Rabbi Shimon's position. Under Rabbi Shimon, *shechita* of a *tereifa* generates no liability because it is not a *shechita re'uya* — so the *chiddush* of the *Baraisa* is that even though you cannot be liable for slaughtering a *tereifa*, you can be liable for selling it. This directly supports Rabbi Yochanan's view that the two forms of liability — *tvicha* and *mechira* — are independent, and that one can be liable for sale even without potential liability for slaughter.

### Reish Lakish Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא; רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan in response: **No;** this *baraita* is not taught in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon. Rather, it follows the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Shimon and maintain that a thief is liable for the fourfold or fivefold payment for slaughtering an animal even if the act of slaughter does not render its meat fit for consumption, as in the case of a *tereifa*.

Reish Lakish responds: the *Baraisa* follows the *Rabanan*, not Rabbi Shimon. According to the *Rabanan*, *shechita* of a *tereifa* is perfectly valid and does generate liability. So the *Baraisa* is simply presenting one illustrative scenario per case: it mentions *slaughtering* a *kilayim* and *selling* a *tereifa*, but this is not meant to be exhaustive — the same liability would apply in either scenario for either animal. The *Baraisa* chose one case for each; it does not mean to exclude the other.

### Gemara Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבָּנַן, טְרֵיפָה בִּמְכִירָה אִיתַהּ – בִּזְבִיחָה לֵיתַהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the *baraita* reflects the opinion of **the Rabbis,** why does it mention the sale of a *tereifa* but not its slaughter? According to the opinion of the Rabbis, **is** the fourfold or fivefold payment applicable **for** the **sale** of **a *tereifa*** while **it is not** applicable **for** its **slaughter?** This is certainly not the case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן?! כִּלְאַיִם בִּטְבִיחָה אִיתַהּ, בִּמְכִירָה לֵיתַהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds with a counter-question: **Rather, what** would you say, that the *baraita* reflects the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon?** The same difficulty could be raised in the case of **diverse kinds,** as the *baraita* mentions the animal's slaughter but not its sale: **Is** the fourfold or fivefold payment applicable **for** the **slaughter** of an animal of diverse kinds, while **it is not** applicable **for** its **sale?** There is certainly no difference between slaughtering and selling in this case.

The Gemara is unpersuaded. *Hai mai* — what kind of answer is this? If the *Baraisa* follows the *Rabanan*, then there is no reason to structure it the way it does. If we are going to mention a *tereifa* under the *Rabanan*, and *shechita* of a *tereifa* is fully valid and generates liability just like any other case, then the *Baraisa* should have combined both animals into a single statement: *ganav kilayim v'tereifa, tavchan u-mecharan, meshallem tashlumei arba'ah ve-chamisha* — if you stole a *kilayim* or a *tereifa*, whether you slaughtered them or sold them, you pay four-and-five. The fact that the *Baraisa* deliberately separates the two animals and assigns different scenarios to each — *shechita* for *kilayim* and *mechira* for *tereifa* — is inexplicable under the *Rabanan*'s view, where the distinction carries no significance.

Moreover — though the Gemara does not press this point explicitly — if the *Baraisa* truly follows the *Rabanan* and is simply picking one scenario per animal for stylistic reasons, why pick *selling* a *tereifa* at all? Under the *Rabanan*, the *chiddush* would be *slaughtering* a *tereifa*, since that is where they disagree with Rabbi Shimon. Selecting the *mechira* scenario for the *tereifa* case only makes sense if the *Baraisa* is telling us that *shechita* of a *tereifa* is *exempt* — i.e., it is operating according to Rabbi Shimon — and the *chiddush* is precisely that liability attaches through *mechira* even when it would not attach through *tvicha*.

### Unresolved Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא תְּנָא טְבִיחָה – וְהוּא הַדִּין לִמְכִירָה; אֵימָא לְרַבָּנַן נָמֵי, תְּנָא מְכִירָה – וְהוּא הַדִּין לִטְבִיחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one cannot make such inferences from the *baraita*, as it **teaches** liability with regard to the **slaughter** of diverse kinds, **and the same is true** with regard **to** its **sale.** If so, one can **say** the same **for** the opinion of **the Rabbis as well:** The *baraita* **teaches** liability with regard to the **sale** of a *tereifa*, **and the same is true** with regard **to** its **slaughter.** Once the *baraita* is interpreted in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis and not that of Rabbi Shimon, it has no bearing on the dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר לָךְ: הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא טְרֵיפָה בַּחֲדָא – תְּנָא כִּלְאַיִם בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** could **say to you: What** is **this** comparison? **Granted, if you say** that the *baraita* reflects the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** and that is why it referred specifically to the sale of a *tereifa*, the *baraita* can be explained: **Since it** necessarily **taught** the case of ***tereifa* in** only **one** manner, that of selling, it maintained the same style of presentation and **taught** the case of **diverse kinds in** only **one** manner, that of slaughtering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבָּנַן, נְעָרְבִינְהוּ וְנִיתְנִינְהוּ: גָּנַב כִּלְאַיִם וּטְרֵיפָה, טְבָחָן וּמְכָרָן – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** there is no reason for it to present just one scenario in either case. Rather, **let** the *baraita* **combine them and teach them** in a single sentence: **If one stole** an animal of **diverse kinds or a *tereifa*** and then **slaughtered or sold** either one of **them, he pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.** The Gemara concludes: This is **difficult** according to the opinion of Reish Lakish.

The Gemara concedes: *kashya*. The difficulty stands. The *Baraisa*'s structure remains awkward and unexplained on Reish Lakish's reading. The Gemara does not resolve the tension, and the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding the interdependence of *tvicha* and *mechira* remains unresolved.

## Kilayim Interpretation

### Kilayim Included

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּלְאַיִם – ״שֶׂה״ כְּתִיב; וְאָמַר רָבָא: זֶה בָּנָה אָב – כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״שֶׂה״, אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַכִּלְאַיִם!
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* teaches that a thief is liable to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment in the case of an animal of **diverse kinds.** The Gemara asks: It is **written** in the Torah: "If a man steals an ox or **a sheep [*seh*]"** (Exodus 21:37), **and Rava says,** concerning the verse: "These are the animals that you may eat: The ox, the *seh* of a sheep, and the *seh* of a goat" (Deuteronomy 14:4), that **this** verse **establishes a paradigm** for other cases: **Wherever** the word ***seh* is stated** in the Torah, **it** serves **to exclude only** an animal of **diverse kinds.** The Hebrew word *seh* denotes either a sheep or a goat. A hybrid, which is neither a sheep nor a goat, does not qualify as a *seh*. Why, then, is the fourfold or fivefold payment applicable to one who stole this kind of animal?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַכִּלְאַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is different here,** in the case of the fourfold or fivefold payment, **as the verse states "or"** (Exodus 21:37), a term that could have been avoided, as explained earlier (67b). This extra word serves **to include** an animal of **diverse kinds,** i.e., the sheep-goat hybrid.

Having introduced the case of *kilayim* in the *Baraisa*, the Gemara pauses to explain the legal basis for the four-and-five liability in that case. The Torah's formulation — *ki yignov ish shor o seh* — "if a man steals an ox or a *seh*" — is the source of the law. But is a crossbred animal a *seh*? The Gemara establishes that it is, and explains why through an analysis of how the word *o* ("or") functions in different biblical contexts.

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל ״אוֹ״ לְרַבּוֹת הוּא? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ כֶשֶׂב״ – פְּרָט לְכִלְאַיִם, ״אוֹ עֵז״ – פְּרָט לְנִדְמֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **every** instance of the word **"or"** serve **to include** diverse kinds? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: It is written: "When a bull or a sheep or a goat is born, it shall be seven days under its mother and from the eighth day onward it may be accepted as a fire-offering to the Lord" (Leviticus 22:27). The phrase **"a bull or a sheep"** serves to **exclude diverse kinds,** i.e., an animal of diverse kinds may not be brought as an offering. The phrase **"or a goat"** serves to **exclude** an animal that **resembles** another, i.e., a sheep that is the offspring of two sheep but that looks like a goat, or vice versa.

Rava introduces the analytical framework. As a baseline: *kol makom shene'emar seh eino ela l'hotzi et hakilayim* — wherever the Torah uses the word *seh* on its own, it signifies to the exclusion of crossbreeds. This is Rava's stated default position. However — and this is the crux of the analysis — whether the word *o* that follows confirms or overrides this default cannot be determined by the word alone. It depends entirely on context.

### Theft Law Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא מֵעִנְיָינֵיהּ דִּקְרָא, וְהָכָא מֵעִנְיָינֵיהּ דִּקְרָא. הָכָא גַּבֵּי גְּנֵיבָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה״, שֶׁאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא כִּלְאַיִם מִבֵּינֵיהֶם – ״אוֹ״ לְרַבּוֹת כִּלְאַיִם. גַּבֵּי קֳדָשִׁים, דִּכְתִיב: ״כֶּשֶׂב וָעֵז״, שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא כִּלְאַיִם מִבֵּינֵיהֶם – ״אוֹ״ לְמַעֵט הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response: The derivation from the word "or" depends on the verse in question. **Here,** it is interpreted **based on the context of the verse, and there,** it is also interpreted **based on the context of the verse. Here, with regard to theft,** the reason the word "or" serves as an inclusion is **that it is written: "An ox or a sheep,"** which are two animals **from which you cannot produce diverse kinds,** as they cannot procreate together, and therefore the word **"or"** serves **to include** an animal of **diverse kinds.** By contrast, **with regard to sacrificial** animals, the reason the word "or" serves as an exclusion is **that it is written "sheep" and "goat,"** which are two animals **from which you can produce diverse kinds.** Consequently, the word **"or"** serves **to exclude** diverse kinds.

In the law of theft, the verse reads *shor o seh* — an ox or a *seh*. Now consider the default assumption absent the word *o*: an ox and a sheep cannot interbreed. The very pairing of these two animals in the same verse would, on its own, suggest that crossbreeds are excluded — because no crossbreed of an ox and a sheep is possible, the category defined by their conjunction implicitly excludes hybrid animals. If the default without the *o* is already that crossbreeds are excluded, then the *o* must be serving the opposite function: it comes to *include* crossbreeds (*o l'rabos kilayim*). Thus in theft law, *kilayim* — a crossbreed of, say, a sheep and a goat — is included in the four-and-five penalty.

### Kodshim Law Analysis

The situation is reversed when we turn to *kodshim*. The verse concerning sacrificial animals reads *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled* — "an ox or a sheep or a goat when it is born." Within this verse, the relevant pairing for our purposes is *chesev* and *ez* — sheep and goat. Unlike an ox and a sheep, a sheep and a goat *can* interbreed (as was established in Masechet *Chullin*). Therefore, the juxtaposition of sheep and goat in the same verse, without the word *o*, would naturally suggest that crossbreeds are *included* — because a crossbreed of these two is a real biological possibility, and mentioning them together implies coverage of the full range. Since the default assumption — absent the *o* — would be that crossbreeds are included, the *o* must be serving the opposite function here: it comes to *exclude* (*o l'ma'utei kilayim*).

But there are two *o*'s in this verse. The first (*o chesev*) excludes *kilayim* — genuine crossbreeds of sheep and goat. The second (*o ez*) excludes something even further: *nidmeh* — an animal that is biologically a purebred offspring of sheep but happens to look like a goat, or vice versa. Such an animal is also disqualified from being brought as a *korban*, despite being genetically pure.

### Why Two Or's

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גַּבֵּי קֳדָשִׁים נָמֵי נֶאֱמַר: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ כֶשֶׂב״ – שֶׁאֵין אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא כִּלְאַיִם מִבֵּינֵיהֶם, וּנְרַבֵּי! מִדְּסֵיפָא לְמַעֵט, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי לְמַעֵט. אַדְּרַבָּה; מִדְּרֵישָׁא לְרַבּוֹת, סֵיפָא נָמֵי לְרַבּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But with regard to sacrificial** animals **it is also stated** in the first part of the verse: **"A bull or a sheep"** (Leviticus 22:27), which are two animals **from which you cannot produce diverse kinds. And** accordingly, **let us include** an animal of diverse kinds as being fit for sacrifice on the altar on that account. The Gemara answers: **From** the fact **that the latter clause** of the verse: "Or a goat," serves **to exclude** a type of animal from being fit for sacrifice on the altar, it may be inferred that the **first clause:** "A bull or a sheep," also serves **to exclude** a type of animal from being fit for sacrifice on the altar. The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** one could equally argue that **from** the fact **that the first clause** of the verse serves **to include** a type of animal as being fit for sacrifice on the altar, the **latter clause also** serves **to include** a type of animal as being fit for sacrifice on the altar.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַעֵט – הוּא דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ תְּרֵי מִיעוּטֵי; דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִימַּעַט כִּלְאַיִם, אִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמַעוֹטֵי נִדְמֶה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְרַבּוֹת – תְּרֵי רִיבּוּיֵי לְמָה? הַשְׁתָּה כִּלְאַיִם אִירַבִּי לֵיהּ, נִדְמֶה מִבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **What** is **this** comparison? **Granted, if you say** that both expressions serve **to exclude** types of animals from being fit for sacrifice on the altar, **this is** why **two exclusions were necessary:** One exclusion is referring to diverse kinds and the other is referring to an animal that resembles another species, **as even though** an animal of **diverse kinds is excluded, it was** still **necessary to exclude** an animal that **resembles** another. **But if you say** that both phrases serve **to include** types of animals as being fit for sacrifice on the altar, **why are two inclusions** required? **Now that** an animal of **diverse kinds has been included,** in the first inclusion, is it **necessary** to say that an animal that **resembles** another is also included? There is more justification for including an animal that resembles another, whose parentage is entirely of one species, than for including an animal of diverse kinds.

This explains why two *o*'s are required. If the *o*'s were serving an *inclusive* function, one *o* would suffice — once you have included *kilayim*, there is no remaining category left to include, since *nidmeh* (which is biologically pure) would *a fortiori* be included. But if the *o*'s are serving an *exclusive* function, then two *o*'s make perfect sense: the first excludes *kilayim* — genuine hybrids — while the second is needed to extend the exclusion to *nidmeh* — animals that are genetically pure but look different from their parents. These are two distinct categories, each requiring its own exclusion. The logical structure thus forces us to read both *o*'s as exclusionary, confirming that the verse in *kodshim* limits eligible animals to those that are genuinely typical specimens of their species.

### Default Principle Question

This raises a pointed question that the Gemara leaves open. Rava articulated a principle — the word *seh* by itself defaults to excluding *kilayim*. But as the preceding analysis has shown, the entire interpretive exercise was driven not by this default but by the surrounding context: in each case, the *o* was read against the background assumption created by the other animals mentioned in the verse, not against any free-standing default meaning of *seh*. One might therefore ask: does Rava's stated default ever actually do independent work? In every case we have examined, the direction of the *o* — whether inclusive or exclusive — was determined entirely by contextual considerations. The Gemara does not resolve this question.
