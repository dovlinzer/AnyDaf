# Hullin 120 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yad & Shomer

### Yad/Shomer Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִיכָּא שׁוֹמֵר עַל גַּבֵּי שׁוֹמֵר? חַלְחוּלֵי מְחַלְחֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Is** the *halakha* of protection applicable with regard to **protection** that is **on top of** another **protection?** Rabbi Yochanan answered: The hair **penetrates through** the hide and touches the flesh, thereby providing protection directly for the flesh.

The daf opens by completing a discussion that began at the very bottom of 119b — literally the final three words of that amud — concerning the concepts of *yad* and *shomer* as they relate to the communication of *tumah*. The fundamental question is whether certain things attached to food objects — handles, protective coverings, and the like — can serve as conduits for *tumah*, and whether they can combine with the food itself to constitute the minimum size required for the food to become *tamei*.

The background principle is straightforward: in order for food to receive and transmit *tumah*, it must meet a minimum size, typically the volume of an egg (*ke-beitzah*). The question is whether items connected to the food can contribute to that minimum. The *halacha* establishes two distinct categories. A *shomer* — a protective covering, such as the skin around meat or a shell around a nut — is treated as fully integrated with the food itself and therefore does add to the minimum size. Moreover, if something *tamei* touches the *shomer*, it communicates *tumah* both to the *shomer* and through it to the food. A *yad* — a handle or manipulator, something through which you can grasp or move the food — does not add to the minimum size, since it is not truly integrated with the food, but it does serve as a conduit: touching a *yad* communicates *tumah* in both directions.

### Hair as Shomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא עֶצֶם, דְּהָוֵי שׁוֹמֵר, אֲבָל נִימָא לָא הָוְיָא שׁוֹמֵר. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ נִימָא נָמֵי הָוְיָא שׁוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said:** The Sages **taught** that **only a bone** and the other items mentioned in the mishna join together with the meat to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity **because** they **constitute protection** for the meat. **But a hair** does not join together with the meat to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity because it **is not protection** for the meat. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even a hair is protection** for the meat and therefore joins together with the meat to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity.

With this backdrop, the Gemara records a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish that is taught, according to one tradition, specifically in connection with the Mishnah of our chapter. The Mishnah lists various things that combine with meat to constitute the minimum food size for receiving *tumah*, including the skin (*ha-or*). Reish Lakish holds that while a bone or skin — items that genuinely function as protectors — qualify as a *shomer*, a hair (*nima*) protruding from a piece of meat does not: *lo havei shomer*. Since the hair does no meaningful work of protection, if something *tamei* contacts the hair, the contact is halachically irrelevant. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu nima nami havei shomer* — even a hair qualifies as a *shomer*.

Reish Lakish immediately challenges Rabbi Yochanan: *u-mi ika shomer agav shomer?* Can one *shomer* rest on top of another *shomer*? The skin already protects the meat; the hair sits on top of the skin. The Gemara had established in an earlier session that a *shomer* atop a *shomer* does not qualify — analogous to the principle of *gezeirah li-gezeirah*, that a rabbinic safeguard built upon another rabbinic safeguard has no traction. A *shomer* is halachically meaningful only when it directly relates to the food itself; something two steps removed loses that connection.

### Tefillin Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, תְּפִילִּין הֵיכִי כָּתְבִינַן? הָא בָּעֵינַן כְּתִיבָה תַּמָּה, וְלֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa objects to this** answer: **If that is so,** that there are perforations in the hide through which the hairs penetrate, **how can we write phylacteries? Don't we require** phylacteries to be written with **a perfect writing** with no perforations in the letters? **And** that **is not** possible if there are perforations in the hide.

Rabbi Yochanan's response to Reish Lakish is that the hair does not merely rest on top of the skin — *de-kulei mechalchel* — it actually pierces through the skin and reaches the flesh below. Therefore the hair is directly connected to the flesh, not simply sitting atop a *shomer* of the skin, and qualifies as a *shomer* in its own right.

Rav Achai immediately challenges this: *ela me-ata tefillin heichi katvinan?* If the hair follicle truly penetrates all the way through the leather to the flesh beneath, then the leather used to write *tefillin* is riddled with tiny invisible holes. Given the requirement of *ketivah tamah* — that the writing of *tefillin* must be complete and uninterrupted — how can one write valid *tefillin* on such leather?

### Ink Passes Over

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּאָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: כׇּל נֶקֶב שֶׁהַדְּיוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו – אֵינוֹ נֶקֶב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** *halakha* **which they say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **escaped** Rav Aḥa: **Any perforation over which the ink passes** and which it covers **is not** considered **a perforation** that invalidates the writing.

The Gemara resolves this difficulty by citing a ruling known in the West, in *Eretz Yisrael*: *kol nekav she-ha-dyo over alav eino nekav* — any hole over which ink can pass without interruption does not count as a hole for the purposes of *tefillin*. The hair follicles, being minuscule, do not cause any perceptible break in the flow of ink across the parchment, and therefore they do not invalidate the writing. The *tefillin* challenge dissolves.

### Hair Pierces Skin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עוֹר שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר, הַנּוֹגֵעַ בְּצִיב הַיּוֹצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ, וּבְשַׂעֲרָה שֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ – טָמֵא. מַאי לָאו מִשּׁוּם שׁוֹמֵר? לָא, מִשּׁוּם יָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** opinion from the mishna taught later (124a): In the case of the **hide** of an animal carcass **upon which there is an olive-bulk of flesh, one who touches a strand** of flesh **emerging from** the flesh **or** touches **a hair that is** on the side of the hide **opposite** the flesh **is ritually impure,** even though he did not touch an olive-bulk of the flesh. **What** is the reason that one who touches the strand of flesh or the hair becomes impure? **Is it not because** they constitute **protection** for the flesh? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **because** the hair constitutes **a handle** for the flesh.

Having defended his own position against Rav Achai's challenge, Rabbi Yochanan now turns the tables on Reish Lakish. He adduces a source: if a piece of skin (*or*) bearing at least an olive's worth (*ke-zayit*) of flesh from a *neveilah* is lying before you, and someone touches a strand (*tziv*) emerging from the skin — *hanogei'a ba-tziv ha-yotzei mimenu* — or, more pointedly, hair that is directly opposite the flesh (*u-vasar she-kenegdo*) — that person becomes *tamei*. This, Rabbi Yochanan argues, demonstrates that the hair communicates *tumah*, vindicating his position.

### Multiple Hairs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִימָא אַחַת לְמַאי חַזְיָא? כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: בִּמְלַאי שֶׁבֵּין הַמְּלָאִין, הָכִי נָמֵי בְּנִימָא שֶׁבֵּין הַנִּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what** function **is one hair fit** such that it constitutes a handle? The Gemara answers: One can explain that mishna **as Rabbi Ela said** in explanation of a different mishna: It is stated **with regard to** the case of **an awn among** many **awns. Here too,** the mishna is stated **with regard to** the case of **a hair among** many **hairs** and not the case of a single hair. The hair serves as a handle for the flesh because one can hold the hair and lift the flesh without the hair becoming detached.

Reish Lakish responds by reframing the source: the reason touching the hair makes one *tamei* is not that the hair is a *shomer* — it is that the hair is a *yad*, a handle. But the Gemara immediately objects: *nima achas le-mai chazya?* A single hair is useless as a handle; if you try to lift the meat by one hair it will simply snap off. How can it be called a *yad*?

The Gemara resolves this by invoking a ruling of Rabbi Ila: the same logic applies to the fine strands (*melo'i*) at the top of a wheat stalk. No individual strand can be used to manipulate the wheat, yet Rabbi Ila teaches — *bimloi she-bein ha-mloi'im* — that it is precisely because they function collectively that each individual strand counts as a *yad*. When you have a full complement of strands, you can grasp the bundle and use it to move the wheat. Similarly, *hacha nami binima she-bein ha-nimim* — here too, among the hairs, since the plurality of hairs together can be used to manipulate the meat, each individual hair, in context, serves the function of a *yad*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא אִיתְּמַר דְּרַבִּי אִילְעָא? אַהָא דִּתְנַן: הַמְּלַאי שֶׁבַּשִּׁבֳּלִין מְטַמְּאִין וּמִיטַּמְּאִין וְאֵין מִצְטָרְפִין. מְלַאי לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: בִּמְלַאי שֶׁבֵּין הַמְּלָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And where was** the opinion **of Rabbi Ela stated?** It **was stated with regard to that which we learned** in a mishna: **An awn that is on** top of **a stalk can become impure and impart impurity, but it does not join together** with the grains to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity. It was asked: **For what** function **is an awn fit** such that it is considered to be a handle for the stalk? **Rabbi Ela said:** The mishna is stated **with regard to** the case of **an awn among** many **awns.**

The Gemara then specifies where Rabbi Ila's ruling was originally stated. It is the *halacha* of the strands of a wheat stalk that they communicate *tumah* and receive *tumah* — *ha-metamin u-mitamin* — but they do not combine with the wheat to constitute the minimum size — *ein mitztarfin*. This is precisely the distinction between *yad* and *shomer*: a *yad* transmits *tumah* but does not add to the minimum size; a *shomer* does both.

### Yad/Shomer Summary

With this, the Gemara concludes the discussion of *yad* and *shomer*, a topic treated extensively in its own right in *Masechet Uktzin*, one of the more obscure tractates of the order of *Taharos*.

A brief review of the conceptual structure is in order. The *yad* and *shomer* framework exists because *tumah* requires a minimum quantity of food: typically a *ke-beitzah*. Items attached to the food may or may not contribute to that minimum. A *shomer* — a genuine protective covering — is fully integrated into the food and contributes to the minimum size; touching a *shomer* communicates *tumah* in both directions, but only when the *shomer* is directly opposite the food it protects. A *shomer* upon a *shomer* does not qualify, for the same reason a *gezeirah li-gezeirah* does not: it is too far removed from the original item. A *yad* — a manipulator — does not add to the minimum size, since it is not one with the food, but it does transmit *tumah*, even indirectly. Unlike a *shomer*, a *yad* can communicate *tumah* even when it is not positioned directly opposite the food. Several further questions were touched upon in the broader discussion: whether the concepts of *yad* and *shomer* apply not only to *tumah* but also to *hechsher* (the liquid-contact that renders food susceptible to *tumah*), and what minimum quantity of food is required before the *yad* or *shomer* framework can be invoked at all.

## Rotev Gravy

### What is Rotev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שׁוּמְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The term *rotev* is referring to **the fat** that floats on top of a soup of cooked meat.

The Mishnah of our chapter lists a series of things that combine with meat to constitute the minimum food quantity required for *tumah*: the skin (*ha-or*), the gravy (*ha-rotev*), and the *kipah* — to be defined momentarily. The Gemara now turns to *rotev*. What exactly is *rotev*? Rava answers: *shumna* — the fat and the fatty liquids that run off of meat during cooking.

### Gravy Combining

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא חֵלֶב דִּקְרִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the term *rotev* is referring to **fat that** oozed out of the meat and **congealed.** That fat is not eaten, but it does join together with the meat to constitute the requisite egg-bulk to impart the impurity of food.

Abaye immediately challenges this definition: if *rotev* is something substantive enough that a person could eat it directly, why does the Mishnah present it only as something that *combines* with the meat to reach the minimum size? It should constitute food on its own and become *tamei* independently.

The proper reading of the text, as confirmed by the textual tradition, replaces the potentially misleading word with *chala de-karish* — a clear liquid runoff from the meat that, crucially, has congealed. Two issues are now being raised simultaneously. The first is that *rotev* refers to something that no one would eat on their own — it is eaten only in conjunction with meat, the way one might pour gravy over a piece of chicken or dip bread into it, but would not consume it independently. This is precisely why the Mishnah says it *combines* with the meat: it is not an independent food source, but together with the meat it constitutes additional foodstuff. The second issue, which will occupy the Gemara at greater length, is the question of physical state: must the *rotev* be congealed (a solid) in order to combine with the meat (also a solid), or can a liquid combine with a solid to reach the minimum food quantity?

### Gelled vs. Liquid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: צִיר שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי יָרָק מִצְטָרֵף לִכְכוֹתֶבֶת בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As Reish Lakish said: Brine on a vegetable,** even though it is a liquid, **combines** with the vegetable **to** constitute **a large date-bulk [*kakotevet*]** with regard to rendering one liable for violating the prohibition against eating **on Yom Kippur.** Similarly, liquid fat should combine with the meat to constitute the requisite volume to impart the impurity of food.

Abaye asserts that the *rotev* must be *karish* — congealed — in order to combine with the meat. The Gemara challenges this requirement. Reish Lakish teaches that the juices running off a vegetable (*tzir she-al gabei yerek*) combine with the vegetable itself to constitute the minimum quantity on *Yom Kippur*: *mitztaref le-kikoseves be-Yom ha-Kippurim*. On *Yom Kippur*, transgression requires consuming the volume of a *kikoseves* (a dried date), and if one ate a vegetable slightly below that threshold but with its liquid runoff, the liquid and solid together may constitute the requisite amount. Here, then, liquid combines with solid — without any requirement of congealing. Why, then, does Abaye insist that *rotev* must be congealed in order to combine with meat?

### Yishuv Hadaat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם מִשּׁוּם יַתּוֹבֵי דַּעְתָּא הוּא, בְּכֹל דְּהוּ מְיַתְּבָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Liquids and solids do not join together to constitute the requisite egg-bulk to impart the impurity of food. The reason for the *halakha* **there,** with regard to Yom Kippur, **is because** one is liable even for the consumption of a liquid that is not characterized as food if the **mind** of the one who consumes it is **settled.** This is because with regard to the fast of Yom Kippur the Torah is concerned with a person's affliction, as the verse states: "You shall afflict your souls" (Leviticus 23:27). Therefore, one is liable on Yom Kippur for **any** eating that **settles his mind.**

The Gemara answers that the two cases operate under entirely different legal frameworks. The *Yom Kippur* case is governed by the principle of *yishuv ha-daat* — the negation of affliction. The *Torah* does not, by *Yom Kippur*, explicitly forbid eating or drinking as such; it commands *inuy*, affliction, and the rabbis define the transgression as neutralizing that affliction. The measure of the *kikoseves*, larger than the standard *ke-zayit* required for most eating prohibitions, reflects the fact that we are not defining an act of eating but rather asking what quantity of consumption is sufficient to counteract the subjective sense of *inuy*. Because the legal question is about the effect on the person — *yishuv ha-daat*, the settling of the mind and body — liquid and solid can work together toward that effect even without congealing. The Gemara puts it directly: *hasam mishum yishuv ha-daat hu; hacha mishum itztarufei hu* — there it is about settling the affliction, here it is about combining as food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא מִשּׁוּם אִיצְטְרוֹפֵי הוּא, אִי קְרִישׁ – מִצְטָרֵף, אִי לָא קְרִישׁ – לָא מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** But that is not the case **here with regard to** impurity. For two substances to join together to impart impurity, **a combination** between substances that have a common requisite measure for imparting impurity **is** necessary. Liquid and solid foods do not have the same requisite measure. Therefore, **if** the fat **is congealed, it joins together** with the meat. **If it is not congealed, it does not join together** with the meat.

When we turn to the question of *tumah*, by contrast, the legal category is the object itself: we need a minimum quantity of *food*, of solid foodstuff, and liquids belong to a different category altogether. Liquids have their own minimum quantities for *tumah*, and they do not combine with solids to reach the minimum applicable to solids. The *rotev*, as a liquid, therefore does not combine with the meat unless it has congealed into a solid. That is Abaye's position, and the Gemara accepts it: *karish mitztaref; i lo karish lo mitztaref* — if it is congealed it combines; if not, it does not.

## Kipah & Tavlin

### What is Kipah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: פִּירְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** The term *kifa* is referring to a congealed **hash** of cooked meat that settled to the bottom of the pot.

The Mishnah also mentions *kipah* as something that combines with meat to constitute the minimum food quantity. What is *kipah*? Rabba (or Rava) answers: *pirma* — the small remnants and thin pieces of meat that settle at the bottom of a pot. Rashi explains this as the residue left after the main meat has been removed, the sort of thing that a hungry person might scrape from the bottom of the pot.

### Tavlin Spices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: תַּבְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa said:** The term *kifa* is referring to **spices,** which are not eaten themselves but do join together with the meat to constitute the requisite egg-bulk to impart the impurity of food.

Abaye again challenges: if *kipah* is simply small pieces of meat, they are food in their own right and should become *tamei* independently, not merely in combination with the main piece. Why does the Mishnah present *kipah* only as something that combines?

Rav Pappa answers: *tavlin* — the word *kipah* in the Mishnah refers not to meat remnants but to spices mixed into the pot. Spices are not eaten independently; one does not sit down to a bowl of spices. Therefore they are not food in their own right and would not become *tamei* on their own. But when they are mixed together with the meat in the pot, they are part of the ensemble of foodstuff that one eats, and they therefore combine with the meat to constitute the minimum size for *tumah*.

### Non-Food Items

What exactly counts as "spices" (*tavlin*) here is a question of context. Ground spices are clearly never eaten straight. Onions and garlic present a more ambiguous case — some people eat them independently, others only in combination with bread or meat. The operative principle is that something which is never consumed on its own, but which is always eaten as part of a larger food preparation, may combine with that preparation to contribute to the minimum food quantity. If it can be eaten independently, it would be *tamei* on its own. If it cannot, it combines. The spices of the *kipah* fall into the former category: they are not independent food, but in combination with meat they are part of the food.

## Liquefying Foods

### Blood Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: הִקְפָּה אֶת הַדָּם וַאֲכָלוֹ, אוֹ שֶׁהִמְחָה אֶת הַחֵלֶב וּגְמָעוֹ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a *baraita* **there:** One who **caused blood to coagulate and ate it or melted** forbidden **fat and swallowed it is liable.**

A *baraita* introduces a new set of cases that substantially expand the discussion: *hikpah et ha-dam ve-achalo o she-himcha et ha-chelev u-gema'o — chayav*. If one took blood and congealed it, converting it from its natural liquid state into a solid, and then ate it — one is liable. Or if one took forbidden fat (*chelev*) and liquefied it, converting it from its natural solid state into a liquid, and then drank it — one is liable.

The first case, blood, is relatively straightforward. The Torah prohibition on blood (*kol dam lo tochelu*) uses the language of eating. By congealing the blood and consuming it as a solid, one has performed an act of eating in the most classical sense — indeed, in an even more emphatic sense than merely drinking liquid blood. Furthermore, by congealing it, *achshuvei achshavei* — one has elevated its status, made it more significant. There is no difficulty here.

### Fat Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אָמַר קְרָא ״נֶפֶשׁ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשּׁוֹתֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said:** One is liable even for drinking the melted forbidden fat of an animal. **The verse states:** "For all who eat the fat of the animal that one could offer from it a fire offering to the Lord, the soul that eats it shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:25). The term **"soul"** is interpreted homiletically **to include** in the prohibition **one who drinks** the fat.

The second case is the challenging one. The Torah says don't *eat* forbidden fat — *achilah ketiv beih* — yet here one is not eating it, one is drinking it. The fat has been liquefied and consumed as a liquid. Does one transgress?

This raises a broader question that *Tosafot* engages with directly. There is a general principle that *shtiyah bi-khlal achilah* — drinking is subsumed under the prohibition of eating. When the Torah says "do not eat," this ordinarily extends to drinking as well. But that principle typically applies when the item being consumed is naturally a liquid — as when the Torah prohibits blood, which is in its natural state a liquid. The present question is different: here we are talking about an item that is naturally a solid and has been artificially converted into a liquid form. Taking a piece of *chelev*, liquefying it, and drinking the result is a thoroughly bizarre mode of consumption. One might well argue that the Torah's prohibition of eating *chelev* does not extend to drinking liquefied *chelev* — that the *Torah* only prohibited conventional acts of consumption: eating meat, drinking wine, not drinking meat.

### Nefesh Includes

Reish Lakish resolves the question by pointing to a word in the relevant verse. When the Torah discusses *neveilah* — and, by analogy, similar cases — it uses the word *nefesh*: the *nefesh* that eats *chelev* shall be cut off. Rashi explains that *nefesh* points toward the inner experience, the holistic impact of consumption on the person, rather than to the specific physical act. In other words, the prohibition is framed in terms of what happens to the *nefesh* — and therefore it includes even drinking. *Nefesh le-rabot et ha-shoteh* — the word *nefesh* comes to include even one who drinks.

It is worth noting that scholars of Semitic languages observe that *nefesh* has a cognate meaning of "throat" in other ancient Near Eastern languages, a reading that has some resonance here: whatever passes through the throat — whether eaten or drunk — is included in the prohibition.

### Chametz Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי גַּבֵּי חָמֵץ כְּהַאי גַּוְנָא: הִמְחָהוּ וּגְמָעוֹ – אִם חָמֵץ הוּא, עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת; אִם מַצָּה הִיא, אֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהּ יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: A novelty **of this kind is also taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to** the prohibition against eating **leavened bread** on Passover: If one took bread, **dissolved it** in water, **and swallowed** this mixture on Passover, the *halakha* is as follows: **If it is leavened bread, he is punished with *karet*; if it is *matza*,** then **a person does not fulfill his obligation** to eat *matza* **on Passover with** this food.

A *baraita* then teaches a parallel by *chametz*: *himchehu u-gema'o* — if one liquefied *chametz* and drank it, one is still punishable by *karet*. Again, the verse says *achilah* — eating — and drinking liquefied *chametz* is not eating in the conventional sense. Again, Reish Lakish invokes the word *nefesh* from the relevant verse as the basis for including drinking.

### Matzah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא אִם מַצָּה הִיא אֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא בָּהּ יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בַּפֶּסַח, ״לֶחֶם עֹנִי״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי לָאו לֶחֶם עֹנִי הוּא. אֶלָּא אִם חָמֵץ הוּא עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת, אֲכִילָה כְּתִיבָא בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Granted, if it is *matza*, a person does not fulfill his obligation** to eat *matza* **on Passover with** this food, as **the Merciful One states** with regard to the prohibition against eating leavened bread on Passover: "You shall eat no leavened bread with it; seven days you shall eat unleavened bread with it, even **the bread of affliction"** (Deuteronomy 16:3), indicating that one must eat the bread of affliction, **but this** bread dissolved in water **is not** considered **the bread of affliction. But if** the bread **is leavened bread,** why **is he punished with *karet*? Eating is written with regard to** the prohibition against leavened bread, and in this case he did not eat it but rather drank it.

The same *baraita* teaches the parallel for *matzah*: if one liquefied *matzah* and drank it on *Pesach*, one does not fulfill the obligation. The rationale is different: the Torah says *lechem oni* — bread of affliction — and liquefied *matzah* is no longer bread. This is a striking asymmetry. Liquefying undermines the positive character of *matzah* as bread and therefore disqualifies it from fulfilling the *Pesach* obligation. But it does not undermine the negative character of *chametz* as a prohibited substance. The prohibition of *chametz* survives the liquefying; the fulfillment of *matzah* does not. The same physical transformation — converting a solid grain product into a liquid — has opposite effects depending on whether the legal question concerns a prohibition (where the *nefesh* verse extends coverage) or a positive act of fulfillment (where the requirement of *lechem* demands a bread-form).

### Dead Bird Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא נָמֵי גַּבֵּי נִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר כְּהַאי גַּוְנָא: הִמְחָהוּ בָּאוּר – טָמֵא, בַּחַמָּה – טָהוֹר. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: אֲכִילָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: A novelty **of this kind is also taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Zavim* 5:9) **with regard to** one who eats **the** unslaughtered **carcass of a kosher bird: One who liquefied** the carcass of an unslaughtered kosher bird **in fire** and drank the substance **is impure.** But one who liquefied it **in the sun** and drank it **is pure. And we discussed it: Eating** and not drinking **is stated** with regard to the impurity of a carcass, as it is written: "And every soul that eats an unslaughtered carcass or that which is mauled by an animal, whether he is native or stranger, he shall wash his garments, bathe in water, and shall be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 17:15). Why, then, does one who drinks a kosher bird carcass become impure?

The *baraita* also teaches the parallel case in *tumah*: the carcass of a permitted bird (*neveilat of tahor*) renders one *tamei* if eaten. If one liquefied the carcass over fire (*himchehu ba-ur*), one becomes *tamei* upon consuming it — *tamei*. But if one liquefied it in the sun (*be-chama*), one remains *tahor*.

Why the difference? *Be-chama isruchei yasreich* — liquefying by extended exposure to the sun takes a long time, and during that process the flesh decomposes, becomes rancid, and loses its essential character as the flesh of a bird. When something becomes thoroughly rancid, it is no longer recognizable as the original substance; it has lost its identity and therefore its status. Liquefying over fire, by contrast, is rapid and preserves the substance's identity. In that case, even in liquid form, consuming it renders one *tamei*, again derived from the word *nefesh* in the relevant verse: *nefesh le-rabot et ha-shoteh*.

### Three Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא חֵלֶב – חָמֵץ לָא אָתֵי מִינֵּיהּ, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר; נְבֵלָה לָא אָתֵי מִינֵּיהּ, שֶׁכֵּן עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cited three instances in which Reish Lakish interpreted the term "soul" as including one who drinks. The Gemara explains: All three mentions of the term "soul" **are necessary. As, if the Merciful One had written** the term "soul" only with regard to forbidden **fat,** liability for drinking liquefied **leavened bread could not have been derived from it, since** the prohibition against the consumption of forbidden fat has an element of stringency that does not apply to leavened bread in that the forbidden fat **had no period of fitness** for consumption. Leavened bread, on the other hand, may be consumed before Passover. Likewise, impurity contracted by drinking a liquefied **carcass could not be derived from** the *halakha* of forbidden fat **because** the consumption of forbidden fat is **punishable by *karet*,** which is not the case with regard to the consumption of a carcass.

The Gemara now asks the expected question: given that all three cases — *chelev*, *chametz*, and *neveilat of tahor* — carry the same teaching (that liquefied consumption counts), why does the Torah need to state the principle three times? Could we not derive the rule from one or two of them?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא חָמֵץ – חֵלֶב לָא אָתֵי מִינֵּיהּ, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ, וּנְבֵלָה לָא אָתְיָא מִינֵּיהּ, שֶׁכֵּן עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One had written** the term "soul" only with regard to **leavened bread,** liability for drinking melted forbidden **fat could not be derived from it, as** there are **no** circumstances in which **the general prohibition** against eating leavened bread **was permitted.** The consumption of forbidden fat, on the other hand, is permitted with regard to the fat of an undomesticated animal. **And** similarly, impurity contracted by drinking a liquefied **carcass could not be derived from** the *halakha* of leavened bread, **because** the *halakha* of leavened bread has an element of stringency that does not apply to a carcass in that its consumption is **punishable by *karet*.**

The Gemara works through every possible reduction. Had the Torah written it only for *chelev*, we could not derive *chametz*: *she-kein lo hayta lo sha'at ha-kosher* — *chelev* was never permissible, whereas *chametz* was once permitted. Had it written it only for *chametz*, we could not derive *chelev*: *she-kein huhtra mi-kelalo* — *chelev* of a *chaya* (a deer) is permissible to a layperson, giving *chelev* a dimension of leniency from which we might have hesitated to derive the stricter case of *chametz*. Had it written it only for *neveilah*, we could not derive the others: *she-kein metam'ah* — *neveilah* has the additional stringency of causing *tumah*, which the others do not, so we could not use it as a model. And even combining two of the three in any pairing fails, because each of the remaining two can be identified as sharing a stringency absent in the third. Therefore the Torah had no choice but to write the principle explicitly in all three places.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בִּנְבֵלָה – הָנָךְ לָא אָתְיָא מִינַּהּ, שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמְּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One had written** the term "soul" only **with regard to a carcass, those** prohibitions against drinking melted fat and liquefied leavened bread **could not be derived from it, because** a carcass has an element of stringency that does not apply to those prohibitions in that **it transmits impurity** to one who eats it. Therefore, all three mentions of the term "soul" are necessary.

The Gemara then raises the case of *shratzim* — forbidden rodents and insects — and their juices and the *kipah* at the bottom of the vessel. Could one not have learned the rule of liquid equivalence from the three cases just discussed, rather than needing a separate verse for the *shratzim*? The Gemara answers that one could not: the principle derived from the three cases operates with the minimum quantity of those prohibitions — a *ke-zayit* — but *shratzim* cause *tumah* and transgression at the size of a lentil bean (*ke-adasha*), smaller than an olive. Had the rule for *shratzim* been derived from the other cases, one might have applied a *ke-zayit* minimum even to the liquid form of *shratzim*, whereas the proper minimum is a *ke-adasha*. The separate verse for *shratzim* is therefore necessary to establish that the liquid of *shratzim* follows the rules specific to *shratzim*, including the smaller minimum quantity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתְיָא, תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי! הֵי תֵּיתֵי? לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בִּנְבֵלָה וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן עָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: It is true that **one** *halakha* **cannot be derived from** either **one** of the others, as detailed above. Nevertheless, one can **derive** the *halakha* of **one** of them **from** the other **two.** The Gemara responds: This is not possible, as **which** *halakha* can one **derive** from the others? **Let the Merciful One not write** this *halakha* **with regard to** a liquefied **carcass and derive** it **from these** other prohibitions against eating forbidden fat and leavened bread. One can refute this derivation: **What** is notable **about these** other prohibitions? They are notable **in that** one who transgresses them **is punished with *karet*,** contrary to one who eats a carcass.

Conversely, could one not have written the liquids rule only by *shratzim* and derived the rest from it? No: *mah li-shratzim she-kein tumatam be-mashehu* — the very stringency of *shratzim*, that they cause *tumah* in a small amount, is a reason one might not have applied the logic to other prohibited items that require a larger minimum. Thus both directions of derivation fail, and the full set of verses is necessary to establish the principle across all categories.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּחָמֵץ, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הָיְתָה לָהֶן שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let the Merciful One not write** this *halakha* **with regard to** dissolved **leavened bread and derive** it **from these** *halakhot* of forbidden fat and a carcass. The Gemara refutes the derivation: **What** is notable **about these** *halakhot*? They are notable **in that they had no period of fitness** for consumption, as opposed to leavened bread, which was fit for consumption before Passover.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא בְּחֵלֶב, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ – מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן לֹא הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלָן, תֹּאמַר בְּחֵלֶב שֶׁהוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let the Merciful One not write** this *halakha* **with regard to** forbidden **fat and derive** it **from these** *halakhot* of consuming leavened bread and a carcass. The Gemara refutes the derivation: **What** is notable **about these** *halakhot*? They are notable **in that** there are **no** circumstances in which **their general prohibition was permitted. Shall you say** the same **with regard to** forbidden **fat, whose general prohibition was permitted** in certain circumstances? Therefore, all three mentions of the term "soul" are necessary.

It is worth noting, as *Tosafot* observes, that throughout this sugya the Gemara treats two conceptually distinct phenomena as roughly equivalent: (a) liquefying a solid substance and consuming it in liquid form, and (b) liquids that naturally run off from a substance. These are quite different. Liquefied meat is still the meat itself in altered form; the juices running off meat are a separate substance that emerged from the meat. One could in principle imagine different rulings for the two — and indeed, there is an argument that liquefied meat, as the very substance of the original item, should be *more* clearly prohibited, while runoff juices, which are not the item itself, might be treated differently. The Gemara, however, treats them as equivalent for purposes of this discussion.

## Ag Product Liquids

### Forbidden Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתַנְיָא: הַטֶּבֶל, וְהֶחָדָשׁ, וְהַהֶקְדֵּשׁ, וְהַשְּׁבִיעִית, וְהַכִּלְאַיִם – כּוּלָּן מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצְאִין מֵהֶן כְּמוֹתָן. מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara continues to examine the source for the status of liquids with regard to various *halakhot* based on **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **untithed produce; and the new crop** of grain of the year, which is forbidden until after the *omer* offering is brought on the sixteenth of Nisan (see Leviticus 23:14); **and consecrated** produce; **and** the produce of **the Sabbatical** Year after the designated time when it must be removed from one's house; **and diverse kinds,** i.e., the produce of a vineyard in which grains were sown; with regard to **all of them,** it is prohibited to consume **liquid that emerges from them just as** it is prohibited to consume them **themselves.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*?

The Gemara now applies the principle of liquid equivalence to a new set of cases. A *baraita* teaches: *ha-tevel ve-ha-chadash ve-ha-hekdesh ve-ha-shvi'it ve-ha-kilayim* — untithed grain (*tevel*), new grain before *Pesach* (*chadash*), sanctified items (*hekdesh*), Sabbatical year produce (*shvi'it*), and forbidden agricultural mixtures (*kilayim*) — *kulan mashkin ha-yotzin me-hen ke-motan*: the liquids that emerge from any of these items have the same status as the items themselves.

### Liquid Status

The rule appears straightforward in light of what has just been established: liquids from forbidden items share the status of the items. But the Gemara now asks — where does this rule come from? Can it be derived from the cases already discussed?

### Natural vs. Imposed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לִיגְמַר מֵהָנָךְ? מָה לְהָנָךְ – שֶׁכֵּן אִיסּוּר הַבָּא מֵאֵלָיו הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: Let** those prohibitions **be learned from these** prohibitions of consuming the liquid that seeps from creeping animals, liquefied fat, dissolved leavened bread, and liquefied bird carcasses, one can respond that this derivation can be refuted. **What** is notable **about these** prohibitions? They are notable **in that** each of them is **a prohibition that develops on its own** without any human intervention, as opposed to the prohibitions in the *baraita*, which do not necessarily share this element of stringency.

The problem is that the three cases of *chelev*, *chametz*, and *neveilah* all involve items whose forbidden status is entirely natural — their prohibition arises from what they inherently are. The cases of *hekdesh* and *kilayim*, however, involve an imposed status: *hekdesh* becomes sanctified through a human act of designation; *kilayim* acquires its forbidden character through the human act of cross-planting. One might argue that an imposed status, being less intrinsic to the object, would operate differently — perhaps it attaches only to the object in its natural form and does not extend to the liquids that emerge from it. This is the distinction: *issur ha-ba me-elav* (a prohibition that arises of itself, naturally) versus an imposed prohibition. Rashi understands the point in terms of *hekdesh*; *Tosafot* identifies *kilayim* as the primary example.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּינַח הֵיכָא דְּאִיסּוּר בָּא מֵאֵלָיו, הֵיכָא דְּלָאו אִיסּוּר הַבָּא מֵאֵלָיו – מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **it works out well** to derive the prohibition against consuming liquids that emerged from a solid **where the prohibition** is one that **develops on its own,** such as untithed produce, the new crop, Sabbatical Year produce and diverse kinds. But with regard to consecrated produce, **where the prohibition does not develop on its own** but rather only after a person consecrates the produce, **from where do we** derive that it is prohibited to consume the liquid that emerges from the produce just as it is prohibited to consume the produce itself?

So the Gemara can derive the rule for naturally prohibited items from the earlier cases, but cannot derive it for items with imposed status. The cases where the prohibition arises naturally can draw on the precedent of *chelev*, *chametz*, and *neveilah*. The question is: what is the source for the imposed-status cases?

### Bikurim Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּמְרִינַן מִבִּכּוּרִים, וּבִכּוּרִים גּוּפַיְיהוּ מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We learn** this *halakha* **from** the case of **first fruits.** Consumption of first fruits is a prohibition that does not develop on its own, because the owner must set aside the first fruits. And consumption of the liquid that emerges from first fruits is prohibited like consumption of the fruit itself. The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive this *halakha* with regard to **first fruits themselves?**

The Gemara answers: we learn it from *bikurim*. *Bikurim* is not a natural state — one must actively designate produce as first-fruits — and yet the principle of liquid equivalence applies there. Therefore *bikurim* serves as the model for all cases of imposed status.

### Bikurim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: ״פְּרִי״ – פְּרִי אַתָּה מֵבִיא, וְאִי אַתָּה מֵבִיא מַשְׁקֶה. הֵבִיא עֲנָבִים וּדְרָכָן – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תָּבִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that first fruits can be brought as wine, **as Rabbi Yosei teaches:** The verse states with regard to first fruits: "You shall take of the first of all the **fruit** of the ground, which you shall bring in from your land" (Deuteronomy 26:2). Since the verse mentions fruit, this indicates that **you must bring** actual **fruit, and you may not bring** the first fruits in the form of **beverages.** But if one **brought grapes and** he had already **pressed them** into wine, **from where** is it derived that he has fulfilled his obligation? **The verse states: "You shall bring in** from your land." This superfluous term serves to teach that if one brings wine for the mitzva of first fruits, he has fulfilled his obligation.

Where do we know that liquid equivalence applies by *bikurim*? Rabbi Yosi teaches from the verse: the Torah says to bring *pri* — fruit — from the first of the land's produce. *Pri ata meivi ve-ee ata meivi mashke* — you bring fruit, not liquid; one does not bring mere juice to the *Beit ha-Mikdash* in lieu of the fruit. However, if one brought the fruit and subsequently pressed it into wine, a different verse — *tavi*, "you shall bring," interpreted inclusively — indicates that it retains its *bikurim* status even in transformed liquid form. So: first bring the fruit intact; but once it has been brought and designated as *bikurim*, transforming it into a liquid does not strip it of that status.

### Bikurim Weight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְבִכּוּרִים, שֶׁכֵּן טְעוּנִין קְרִיָּיה וְהַנָּחָה!
>
> **Translation:** But this derivation from the case of first fruits **can be refuted: What** is notable **about first fruits?** They are notable **in that they require reciting** the passage which begins: "My father was a wandering Aramean" (Deuteronomy 26:5), **and placing** the fruits in the Temple, whereas no such requirements exists with regard to consecrated produce.

One might challenge the extension from *bikurim* to other cases: *mah le-bikurim she-kein te'unim kri'ah ve-hanacha* — *bikurim* is uniquely weighty, requiring a formal declaration (*kri'ah*) and placement before the altar (*hanacha*). Perhaps the principle of liquid equivalence reflects this heightened significance of *bikurim* and does not apply to lesser categories.

### Terumah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גָּמַר מִתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one can **derive** the prohibition against consuming the liquid that emerges from consecrated produce **from** the prohibition against consuming liquid that emerges from ***teruma*,** i.e., the portion of produce designated for the priest.

The Gemara therefore shifts to *terumah*. Like *bikurim*, *terumah* is an imposed status (the priest's portion is designated by human action). We learn liquid equivalence by *terumah* from its link to *bikurim*: the verse speaks of *terumat yadecha* — the *terumah* of your hand — and the rabbis identify this as a reference to *bikurim*. Thus *bikurim* are called *terumah*, and what is true of *bikurim* regarding liquids is therefore true of *terumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְרוּמָה גּוּפַהּ מְנָלַן? דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ לְבִכּוּרִים, דְּאָמַר מָר: ״וּתְרוּמַת יָדֶךָ״ – אֵלּוּ בִּכּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive this *halakha* with regard to ***teruma* itself?** the Gemara answers: It is derived from the fact **that** *teruma* **is compared to first fruits, as** it is written: "You may not eat within your gates the tithe of your grain, or of your wine, or of your oil, or the firstborn of your cattle and of your flocks, or any of your vows that you will vow, or your pledges, nor the offering of your hand" (Deuteronomy 12:17), and **the Master said** with regard to the phrase **"nor the offering [*teruma*] of your hand"** that **these are** the **first fruits.** Since the verse uses the term "*teruma*" with regard to first fruits, the *halakha* of *teruma* is compared to the *halakha* of first fruits: Just as liquid that emerges from first fruits is forbidden like the fruit itself, so too liquid that emerges from *teruma* is forbidden like the produce itself. Consequently, the prohibition against consuming the liquid that emerges from consecrated produce can be derived from *teruma*.

### Terumah Weight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לִתְרוּמָה, שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this derivation from *teruma*: **What** is notable **about *teruma*?** It is notable **in that** a non-priest **is liable** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven **for** consuming *teruma*, **and** he must restore the value of the produce he ate, adding **one-fifth** of its value as a fine. This element of stringency does not exist with regard to one who consumes consecrated produce.

But *terumah* too is weightier than ordinary cases: one who inadvertently eats *terumah* is liable to pay the principal and add a fifth (*chomesh*), and eating *terumah* intentionally carries a penalty of death at heaven's hand (*mita bi-ydei shamayim*). Perhaps the principle of liquid equivalence by *terumah* reflects this stringency and cannot be exported to less weighty prohibitions like *kilayim*.

### Combined Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, גָּמַר מִתַּרְוַיְיהוּ, מִתְּרוּמָה וּבִכּוּרִים – מָה לִתְרוּמָה וּבִכּוּרִים, שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶם מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one can **learn** the prohibition against consuming the liquid that emerges from consecrated produce **from both *teruma* and first fruits.** The Gemara refutes this derivation: **What** is notable **about *teruma* and first fruits?** They are notable **in that** a non-priest **is liable** to receive the punishment of **death** at the hand of Heaven **for** consuming *teruma* or first fruits, **and** he must restore the value of the produce he ate, adding **one-fifth** of its value as a fine. This is not true with regard to one who consumes consecrated produce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אָתְיָא מִתְּרוּמָה וְחַד מֵהָנָךְ, אוֹ מִבִּכּוּרִים וְחַד מֵהָנָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the prohibition against consuming the liquid that emerges from consecrated produce **is derived from *teruma* and one of these** prohibitions, i.e., a liquefied carcass of a bird or dissolved leavened bread; **or** it can be derived **from first fruits and one of these** prohibitions.

The Gemara's resolution is to combine cases. By taking *terumah* and *bikurim* together with one of the naturally-prohibited cases (such as *shratzim*, or *neveilah*, or *chametz*), we can establish the principle across all types of prohibitions: naturally arising and imposed, lightly penalized and heavily penalized. Each pairing addresses the challenge raised by the other member's unique stringency, and together they establish that liquid equivalence is a universal rule applying to all forbidden items, whether their status arises naturally or by human imposition.

## Terumah Liquids

### Terumah Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: דְּבַשׁ תְּמָרִים, וְיֵין תַּפּוּחִים, וְחוֹמֶץ סִיתְוָונִיּוֹת, וּשְׁאָר מִינֵי פֵירוֹת שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה – רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara elaborates on the comparison between *teruma* and first fruits with regard to the *halakha* that the status of liquid that emerges from produce is like that of the produce itself, **and** it explains **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 11:2): If a non-priest ate **date honey, or apple wine, or vinegar** made **from grapes of autumn** that grow stunted at the end of the season and are unfit for wine production, **or** any of the **other types of** juice made from **fruits of *teruma*, Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **obligated** to repay **the principal and** an additional **fifth,** like the penalty for one who ate the *teruma* produce itself. **And Rabbi Yehoshua deems** him **exempt** from payment.

Having established that liquids emerging from *terumah* share the status of *terumah* itself, the Gemara cites a *mishnah* (actually a *baraita*) that applies this principle in practice, while revealing that there is a significant dispute about its scope. The *baraita* lists: honey made from dates (*devash temarim*), apple cider (*yayin tapuchim*), vinegar made from the bitter late-season grapes (*chometz sitvaniyot*), and other liquid products made from *terumah* fruits.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

Rabbi Eliezer rules that all such liquid products of *terumah* have full *terumah* status: one who eats them inadvertently must pay the principal (*keren*) plus the added fifth (*chomesh*) — *mechayev keren ve-chomesh*. In Rabbi Eliezer's view, the principle we have established — that liquids from *terumah* share the status of *terumah* — applies universally to all fruits designated as *terumah* and their liquid derivatives.

### Rabbi Yehoshua View

Rabbi Yehoshua exempts — *poter* — holding that these liquids do not carry *terumah* status. The dispute is not about the general principle that liquids can share the status of their source; both authorities accept that. The debate is about how to apply the derivation.

### Damino Mimo Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? בְּדוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ, וּבְּדוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With regard to what** principle do Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua **disagree? They disagree with regard to** the principles governing the process of deriving one *halakha* from another *halakha*. One maintains the exegetical principle: **Infer from it, and** again **from it,** i.e., when one case is derived from another all the details of the source case are applied to the other case; **and** one maintains the principle: **Infer from it but interpret** the *halakha* **according to its** own **place,** i.e., one derives only the basic principle of the source case, whereas all other aspects of the source case are not applied to the case at hand.

The Gemara explains: *be-damino mino u-be-damino ve-okei ba-asra paligi* — they dispute whether, when learning a rule from an analogy, one preserves the original context of the source (*damino mino*) or applies the context of the target case (*okei ba-asra*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וּמִינַּהּ, מָה בִּכּוּרִים – מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצֵא מֵהֶן כְּמוֹתָן, אַף תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי – מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצֵא מֵהֶן כְּמוֹתָן, וּמִינַּהּ – מָה בִּכּוּרִים אֲפִילּוּ שְׁאָר מִינִין, אַף תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי אֲפִילּוּ שְׁאָר מִינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Eliezer holds: Infer from it, and** again **from it. Just as** with regard to **first fruits,** the status of the **liquid that emerges from them is like** that of the produce itself, **so too** with regard to ***teruma*,** the status of the **liquid that emerges from it is like** that of the produce itself. **And** again infer **from it: Just as** the *halakha* of **first fruits** includes not only olives and grapes but **even other types** of produce from the seven species for which Eretz Yisrael is praised, and the status of liquid that emerges from that produce is like that of the produce itself, **so too** with regard to ***teruma*,** although oil and wine are the only liquids from which one is obligated to separate *teruma*, **even** liquid that emerges from **other types** of produce designated as *teruma* besides olives and grapes has the same status as the produce itself.

Rabbi Eliezer holds *damino mino*: we derived the liquid equivalence rule for *terumah* from *bikurim*. Just as by *bikurim* the Torah says *pri ha-adamah* — the fruit of the ground — and therefore the liquids of all types of fruit count as *bikurim* in liquid form, so too by *terumah*, the liquids of all types of fruit designated as *terumah* carry *terumah* status. We preserve the broad context of *bikurim* — all fruits — and carry it over to *terumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר, דּוּן מִינַּהּ: מָה בִּכּוּרִים – מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצְאִין מֵהֶן כְּמוֹתָן, אַף תְּרוּמָה – מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצְאִין מֵהֶן כְּמוֹתָן. וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ: מָה מַשְׁקִין דְּקָדָשִׁים בִּתְרוּמָה, תִּירוֹשׁ וְיִצְהָר – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא, אַף מַשְׁקִין הַיּוֹצְאִין מֵהֶן כְּמוֹתָן, תִּירוֹשׁ וְיִצְהָר – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua holds: Infer from it** that **just as** with regard to **first fruits** the status of the **liquid that emerges from them is like** that of the produce itself, **so too** with regard to ***teruma*,** the status of the **liquid that emerges from it is like** that of the produce itself. **But interpret** the *halakha* **according to its** own **place: Just as** in the case of **liquids that are consecrated as *teruma*,** with regard to **wine and oil, yes,** they are included, **but other** types are **not** included, **so too** in the case of **liquid that emerges from** *teruma*, as with regard to **wine and oil, yes,** they **are like** the produce itself, **but any other** type of liquid that emerges from *teruma* is **not** like the produce itself. Therefore, Rabbi Yehoshua deems exempt a non-priest who consumed any of the beverages mentioned in the mishna.

Rabbi Yehoshua holds *okei ba-asra*: yes, we learn from *bikurim* that liquid equivalence applies. But now apply it within the specific context of *terumah*. What liquids does *terumah* biblically recognize? Only *tirosh* and *yitzhar* — wine and oil. The Torah's language for *terumah* is *dagan, tirosh, ve-yitzhar*: grain, wine, and oil. Other liquids are not mentioned in the biblical law of *terumah*. Therefore, when we learn the principle of liquid equivalence from *bikurim* and apply it to *terumah*, we apply it only to those liquids that *terumah* itself explicitly recognizes: wine and oil.

### Wine & Oil Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: אֵין מְבִיאִין בִּכּוּרִים מַשְׁקֶה אֶלָּא הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַזֵּיתִים וּמִן הָעֲנָבִים – מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Terumot* 11:3): **One may bring beverages** made from **first fruits** to the Temple **only** in the case of **that which emerges from the olives,** i.e., oil, **or from the grapes,** i.e., wine. The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion is that mishna?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דְּאָמַר: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְאַתְרַהּ, וְגָמַר לְהוּ לְבִכּוּרִים מִתְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, who says: Infer from it but interpret** the *halakha* **according to its** own **place.** He derives the *halakha* of liquid that emerges from *teruma* from first fruits only with regard to wine and oil, **and** in the opposite direction, **he derives** the *halakha* **of** liquid that emerges from **first fruits from *teruma*.** Therefore, even with regard to first fruits the status of liquid that emerges from the produce is like that of the produce itself only with regard to wine and oil. But according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the *halakha* of liquid that emerges from *teruma* is derived from first fruits even with regard to other types of produce besides grapes and olives, contrary to the statement of that mishna, one may bring beverages extracted from other first fruits besides wine and oil to the Temple.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דִּתְנַן: אֵין סוֹפְגִין אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים מִשּׁוּם עׇרְלָה, אֶלָּא עַל הַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַזֵּיתִים וּמִן הָעֲנָבִים – מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **that which we learned** in the same mishna (*Terumot* 11:3): **One incurs the forty** lashes **due to** drinking the juice squeezed from ***orla*,** i.e., the fruit of a tree during the first three years after its planting, **only for that which emerges from the olives or from the grapes.** The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion is that mishna?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, דְּאָמַר: דּוּן מִינַּהּ וְאוֹקֵי בְּאַתְרַהּ, וְגָמַר לְהוּ לְבִכּוּרִים מִתְּרוּמָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua, who says: Infer from it but interpret** the *halakha* **according to its** own **place, and he derives** the *halakha* **of** liquid that emerges from **first fruits from *teruma*.** Therefore, even with regard to first fruits, the status of liquid that emerges from the produce is like that of the produce itself only with regard to wine and oil.

The practical consequence of Rabbi Yehoshua's position, as *Tosafot* explains it, is that the only liquid products of *terumah* fruits that carry the full *terumah* status — requiring payment of principal and fifth if inadvertently consumed — are wine and oil. Honey of dates, apple cider, and other fruit juices, even from fruits that are unquestionably *terumah*, do not carry *terumah* status as liquids, because the Torah did not recognize such liquids within the framework of *terumah* law. Notably, this may extend even to liquids that run off of grain or barley, since the Torah by *terumah* highlights specifically the liquids of wine and oil, implicitly excluding others.

The daf thus closes at a turning point. The long arc of the discussion has established that liquids are generally treated as equivalent to the solids from which they derive — whether through liquefaction or natural runoff, whether the prohibition is natural or imposed. Yet that principle is now shown to have limits. For *terumah*, at least according to Rabbi Yehoshua, the recognized liquids are specifically wine and oil; other liquid derivatives of *terumah* produce do not carry full *terumah* status. The discussion of precisely where liquid equivalence applies and where it breaks down will continue into the next amud.
