# Hullin 120 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yad & Shomer

### Yad/Shomer Intro

The daf opens by completing a discussion that began at the very bottom of 119b — literally the final three words of that amud — concerning the concepts of *yad* and *shomer* as they relate to the communication of *tumah*. The fundamental question is whether certain things attached to food objects — handles, protective coverings, and the like — can serve as conduits for *tumah*, and whether they can combine with the food itself to constitute the minimum size required for the food to become *tamei*.

The background principle is straightforward: in order for food to receive and transmit *tumah*, it must meet a minimum size, typically the volume of an egg (*ke-beitzah*). The question is whether items connected to the food can contribute to that minimum. The *halacha* establishes two distinct categories. A *shomer* — a protective covering, such as the skin around meat or a shell around a nut — is treated as fully integrated with the food itself and therefore does add to the minimum size. Moreover, if something *tamei* touches the *shomer*, it communicates *tumah* both to the *shomer* and through it to the food. A *yad* — a handle or manipulator, something through which you can grasp or move the food — does not add to the minimum size, since it is not truly integrated with the food, but it does serve as a conduit: touching a *yad* communicates *tumah* in both directions.

### Hair as Shomer

With this backdrop, the Gemara records a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish that is taught, according to one tradition, specifically in connection with the Mishnah of our chapter. The Mishnah lists various things that combine with meat to constitute the minimum food size for receiving *tumah*, including the skin (*ha-or*). Reish Lakish holds that while a bone or skin — items that genuinely function as protectors — qualify as a *shomer*, a hair (*nima*) protruding from a piece of meat does not: *lo havei shomer*. Since the hair does no meaningful work of protection, if something *tamei* contacts the hair, the contact is halachically irrelevant. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu nima nami havei shomer* — even a hair qualifies as a *shomer*.

Reish Lakish immediately challenges Rabbi Yochanan: *u-mi ika shomer agav shomer?* Can one *shomer* rest on top of another *shomer*? The skin already protects the meat; the hair sits on top of the skin. The Gemara had established in an earlier session that a *shomer* atop a *shomer* does not qualify — analogous to the principle of *gezeirah li-gezeirah*, that a rabbinic safeguard built upon another rabbinic safeguard has no traction. A *shomer* is halachically meaningful only when it directly relates to the food itself; something two steps removed loses that connection.

### Tefillin Challenge

Rabbi Yochanan's response to Reish Lakish is that the hair does not merely rest on top of the skin — *de-kulei mechalchel* — it actually pierces through the skin and reaches the flesh below. Therefore the hair is directly connected to the flesh, not simply sitting atop a *shomer* of the skin, and qualifies as a *shomer* in its own right.

Rav Achai immediately challenges this: *ela me-ata tefillin heichi katvinan?* If the hair follicle truly penetrates all the way through the leather to the flesh beneath, then the leather used to write *tefillin* is riddled with tiny invisible holes. Given the requirement of *ketivah tamah* — that the writing of *tefillin* must be complete and uninterrupted — how can one write valid *tefillin* on such leather?

### Ink Passes Over

The Gemara resolves this difficulty by citing a ruling known in the West, in *Eretz Yisrael*: *kol nekav she-ha-dyo over alav eino nekav* — any hole over which ink can pass without interruption does not count as a hole for the purposes of *tefillin*. The hair follicles, being minuscule, do not cause any perceptible break in the flow of ink across the parchment, and therefore they do not invalidate the writing. The *tefillin* challenge dissolves.

### Hair Pierces Skin

Having defended his own position against Rav Achai's challenge, Rabbi Yochanan now turns the tables on Reish Lakish. He adduces a source: if a piece of skin (*or*) bearing at least an olive's worth (*ke-zayit*) of flesh from a *neveilah* is lying before you, and someone touches a strand (*tziv*) emerging from the skin — *hanogei'a ba-tziv ha-yotzei mimenu* — or, more pointedly, hair that is directly opposite the flesh (*u-vasar she-kenegdo*) — that person becomes *tamei*. This, Rabbi Yochanan argues, demonstrates that the hair communicates *tumah*, vindicating his position.

### Multiple Hairs

Reish Lakish responds by reframing the source: the reason touching the hair makes one *tamei* is not that the hair is a *shomer* — it is that the hair is a *yad*, a handle. But the Gemara immediately objects: *nima achas le-mai chazya?* A single hair is useless as a handle; if you try to lift the meat by one hair it will simply snap off. How can it be called a *yad*?

The Gemara resolves this by invoking a ruling of Rabbi Ila: the same logic applies to the fine strands (*melo'i*) at the top of a wheat stalk. No individual strand can be used to manipulate the wheat, yet Rabbi Ila teaches — *bimloi she-bein ha-mloi'im* — that it is precisely because they function collectively that each individual strand counts as a *yad*. When you have a full complement of strands, you can grasp the bundle and use it to move the wheat. Similarly, *hacha nami binima she-bein ha-nimim* — here too, among the hairs, since the plurality of hairs together can be used to manipulate the meat, each individual hair, in context, serves the function of a *yad*.

The Gemara then specifies where Rabbi Ila's ruling was originally stated. It is the *halacha* of the strands of a wheat stalk that they communicate *tumah* and receive *tumah* — *ha-metamin u-mitamin* — but they do not combine with the wheat to constitute the minimum size — *ein mitztarfin*. This is precisely the distinction between *yad* and *shomer*: a *yad* transmits *tumah* but does not add to the minimum size; a *shomer* does both.

### Yad/Shomer Summary

With this, the Gemara concludes the discussion of *yad* and *shomer*, a topic treated extensively in its own right in *Masechet Uktzin*, one of the more obscure tractates of the order of *Taharos*.

A brief review of the conceptual structure is in order. The *yad* and *shomer* framework exists because *tumah* requires a minimum quantity of food: typically a *ke-beitzah*. Items attached to the food may or may not contribute to that minimum. A *shomer* — a genuine protective covering — is fully integrated into the food and contributes to the minimum size; touching a *shomer* communicates *tumah* in both directions, but only when the *shomer* is directly opposite the food it protects. A *shomer* upon a *shomer* does not qualify, for the same reason a *gezeirah li-gezeirah* does not: it is too far removed from the original item. A *yad* — a manipulator — does not add to the minimum size, since it is not one with the food, but it does transmit *tumah*, even indirectly. Unlike a *shomer*, a *yad* can communicate *tumah* even when it is not positioned directly opposite the food. Several further questions were touched upon in the broader discussion: whether the concepts of *yad* and *shomer* apply not only to *tumah* but also to *hechsher* (the liquid-contact that renders food susceptible to *tumah*), and what minimum quantity of food is required before the *yad* or *shomer* framework can be invoked at all.

## Rotev Gravy

### What is Rotev

The Mishnah of our chapter lists a series of things that combine with meat to constitute the minimum food quantity required for *tumah*: the skin (*ha-or*), the gravy (*ha-rotev*), and the *kipah* — to be defined momentarily. The Gemara now turns to *rotev*. What exactly is *rotev*? Rava answers: *shumna* — the fat and the fatty liquids that run off of meat during cooking.

### Gravy Combining

Abaye immediately challenges this definition: if *rotev* is something substantive enough that a person could eat it directly, why does the Mishnah present it only as something that *combines* with the meat to reach the minimum size? It should constitute food on its own and become *tamei* independently.

The proper reading of the text, as confirmed by the textual tradition, replaces the potentially misleading word with *chala de-karish* — a clear liquid runoff from the meat that, crucially, has congealed. Two issues are now being raised simultaneously. The first is that *rotev* refers to something that no one would eat on its own — it is eaten only in conjunction with meat, the way one might pour gravy over a piece of chicken or dip bread into it, but would not consume it independently. This is precisely why the Mishnah says it *combines* with the meat: it is not an independent food source, but together with the meat it constitutes additional foodstuff. The second issue, which will occupy the Gemara at greater length, is the question of physical state: must the *rotev* be congealed (a solid) in order to combine with the meat (also a solid), or can a liquid combine with a solid to reach the minimum food quantity?

### Gelled vs. Liquid

Abaye asserts that the *rotev* must be *karish* — congealed — in order to combine with the meat. The Gemara challenges this requirement. Reish Lakish teaches that the juices running off a vegetable (*tzir she-al gabei yerek*) combine with the vegetable itself to constitute the minimum quantity on *Yom Kippur*: *mitztaref le-kikoseves be-Yom ha-Kippurim*. On *Yom Kippur*, transgression requires consuming the volume of a *kikoseves* (a dried date), and if one ate a vegetable slightly below that threshold but with its liquid runoff, the liquid and solid together may constitute the requisite amount. Here, then, liquid combines with solid — without any requirement of congealing. Why, then, does Abaye insist that *rotev* must be congealed in order to combine with meat?

### Yishuv Hadaat

The Gemara answers that the two cases operate under entirely different legal frameworks. The *Yom Kippur* case is governed by the principle of *yishuv ha-daat* — the negation of affliction. The *Torah* does not, by *Yom Kippur*, explicitly forbid eating or drinking as such; it commands *inuy*, affliction, and the rabbis define the transgression as neutralizing that affliction. The measure of the *kikoseves*, larger than the standard *ke-zayit* required for most eating prohibitions, reflects the fact that we are not defining an act of eating but rather asking what quantity of consumption is sufficient to counteract the subjective sense of *inuy*. Because the legal question is about the effect on the person — *yishuv ha-daat*, the settling of the mind and body — liquid and solid can work together toward that effect even without congealing. The Gemara puts it directly: *hasam mishum yishuv ha-daat hu; hacha mishum itztarufei hu* — there it is about settling the affliction, here it is about combining as food.

When we turn to the question of *tumah*, by contrast, the legal category is the object itself: we need a minimum quantity of *food*, of solid foodstuff, and liquids belong to a different category altogether. Liquids have their own minimum quantities for *tumah*, and they do not combine with solids to reach the minimum applicable to solids. The *rotev*, as a liquid, therefore does not combine with the meat unless it has congealed into a solid. That is Abaye's position, and the Gemara accepts it: *karish mitztaref; i lo karish lo mitztaref* — if it is congealed it combines; if not, it does not.

## Kipah & Tavlin

### What is Kipah

The Mishnah also mentions *kipah* as something that combines with meat to constitute the minimum food quantity. What is *kipah*? Rabba (or Rava) answers: *pirma* — the small remnants and thin pieces of meat that settle at the bottom of a pot. Rashi explains this as the residue left after the main meat has been removed, the sort of thing that a hungry person might scrape from the bottom of the pot.

### Tavlin Spices

Abaye again challenges: if *kipah* is simply small pieces of meat, they are food in their own right and should become *tamei* independently, not merely in combination with the main piece. Why does the Mishnah present *kipah* only as something that combines?

Rav Pappa answers: *tavlin* — the word *kipah* in the Mishnah refers not to meat remnants but to spices mixed into the pot. Spices are not eaten independently; one does not sit down to a bowl of spices. Therefore they are not food in their own right and would not become *tamei* on their own. But when they are mixed together with the meat in the pot, they are part of the ensemble of foodstuff that one eats, and they therefore combine with the meat to constitute the minimum size for *tumah*.

### Non-Food Items

What exactly counts as "spices" (*tavlin*) here is a question of context. Ground spices are clearly never eaten straight. Onions and garlic present a more ambiguous case — some people eat them independently, others only in combination with bread or meat. The operative principle is that something which is never consumed on its own, but which is always eaten as part of a larger food preparation, may combine with that preparation to contribute to the minimum food quantity. If it can be eaten independently, it would be *tamei* on its own. If it cannot, it combines. The spices of the *kipah* fall into the former category: they are not independent food, but in combination with meat they are part of the food.

## Liquefying Foods

### Blood Case

A *baraita* introduces a new set of cases that substantially expand the discussion: *hikpah et ha-dam ve-achalo o she-himcha et ha-chelev u-gema'o — chayav*. If one took blood and congealed it, converting it from its natural liquid state into a solid, and then ate it — one is liable. Or if one took forbidden fat (*chelev*) and liquefied it, converting it from its natural solid state into a liquid, and then drank it — one is liable.

The first case, blood, is relatively straightforward. The Torah prohibition on blood (*kol dam lo tochelu*) uses the language of eating. By congealing the blood and consuming it as a solid, one has performed an act of eating in the most classical sense — indeed, in an even more emphatic sense than merely drinking liquid blood. Furthermore, by congealing it, *achshuvei achshavei* — one has elevated its status, made it more significant. There is no difficulty here.

### Fat Case

The second case is the challenging one. The Torah says don't *eat* forbidden fat — *achilah ketiv beih* — yet here one is not eating it, one is drinking it. The fat has been liquefied and consumed as a liquid. Does one transgress?

This raises a broader question that *Tosafot* engages with directly. There is a general principle that *shtiyah bi-khlal achilah* — drinking is subsumed under the prohibition of eating. When the Torah says "do not eat," this ordinarily extends to drinking as well. But that principle typically applies when the item being consumed is naturally a liquid — as when the Torah prohibits blood, which is in its natural state a liquid. The present question is different: here we are talking about an item that is naturally a solid and has been artificially converted into a liquid form. Taking a piece of *chelev*, liquefying it, and drinking the result is a thoroughly bizarre mode of consumption. One might well argue that the Torah's prohibition of eating *chelev* does not extend to drinking liquefied *chelev* — that the *Torah* only prohibited conventional acts of consumption: eating meat, drinking wine, not drinking meat.

### Nefesh Includes

Reish Lakish resolves the question by pointing to a word in the relevant verse. When the Torah discusses *neveilah* — and, by analogy, similar cases — it uses the word *nefesh*: the *nefesh* that eats *chelev* shall be cut off. Rashi explains that *nefesh* points toward the inner experience, the holistic impact of consumption on the person, rather than to the specific physical act. In other words, the prohibition is framed in terms of what happens to the *nefesh* — and therefore it includes even drinking. *Nefesh le-rabot et ha-shoteh* — the word *nefesh* comes to include even one who drinks.

It is worth noting that scholars of Semitic languages observe that *nefesh* has a cognate meaning of "throat" in other ancient Near Eastern languages, a reading that has some resonance here: whatever passes through the throat — whether eaten or drunk — is included in the prohibition.

### Chametz Case

A *baraita* then teaches a parallel by *chametz*: *himchehu u-gema'o* — if one liquefied *chametz* and drank it, one is still punishable by *karet*. Again, the verse says *achilah* — eating — and drinking liquefied *chametz* is not eating in the conventional sense. Again, Reish Lakish invokes the word *nefesh* from the relevant verse as the basis for including drinking.

### Matzah Case

The same *baraita* teaches the parallel for *matzah*: if one liquefied *matzah* and drank it on *Pesach*, one does not fulfill the obligation. The rationale is different: the Torah says *lechem oni* — bread of affliction — and liquefied *matzah* is no longer bread. This is a striking asymmetry. Liquefying undermines the positive character of *matzah* as bread and therefore disqualifies it from fulfilling the *Pesach* obligation. But it does not undermine the negative character of *chametz* as a prohibited substance. The prohibition of *chametz* survives the liquefying; the fulfillment of *matzah* does not. The same physical transformation — converting a solid grain product into a liquid — has opposite effects depending on whether the legal question concerns a prohibition (where the *nefesh* verse extends coverage) or a positive act of fulfillment (where the requirement of *lechem* demands a bread-form).

### Dead Bird Case

The *baraita* also teaches the parallel case in *tumah*: the carcass of a permitted bird (*neveilat of tahor*) renders one *tamei* if eaten. If one liquefied the carcass over fire (*himchehu ba-ur*), one becomes *tamei* upon consuming it — *tamei*. But if one liquefied it in the sun (*be-chama*), one remains *tahor*.

Why the difference? *Be-chama isruchei yasreich* — liquefying by extended exposure to the sun takes a long time, and during that process the flesh decomposes, becomes rancid, and loses its essential character as the flesh of a bird. When something becomes thoroughly rancid, it is no longer recognizable as the original substance; it has lost its identity and therefore its status. Liquefying over fire, by contrast, is rapid and preserves the substance's identity. In that case, even in liquid form, consuming it renders one *tamei*, again derived from the word *nefesh* in the relevant verse: *nefesh le-rabot et ha-shoteh*.

### Three Cases

The Gemara now asks the expected question: given that all three cases — *chelev*, *chametz*, and *neveilat of tahor* — carry the same teaching (that liquefied consumption counts), why does the Torah need to state the principle three times? Could we not derive the rule from one or two of them?

The Gemara works through every possible reduction. Had the Torah written it only for *chelev*, we could not derive *chametz*: *she-kein lo hayta lo sha'at ha-kosher* — *chelev* was never permissible, whereas *chametz* was once permitted. Had it written it only for *chametz*, we could not derive *chelev*: *she-kein huhtra mi-kelalo* — *chelev* of a *chaya* (a deer) is permissible to a layperson, giving *chelev* a dimension of leniency from which we might have hesitated to derive the stricter case of *chametz*. Had it written it only for *neveilah*, we could not derive the others: *she-kein metam'ah* — *neveilah* has the additional stringency of causing *tumah*, which the others do not, so we could not use it as a model. And even combining two of the three in any pairing fails, because each of the remaining two can be identified as sharing a stringency absent in the third. Therefore the Torah had no choice but to write the principle explicitly in all three places.

The Gemara then raises the case of *shratzim* — forbidden rodents and insects — and their juices and the *kipah* at the bottom of the vessel. Could one not have learned the rule of liquid equivalence from the three cases just discussed, rather than needing a separate verse for the *shratzim*? The Gemara answers that one could not: the principle derived from the three cases operates with the minimum quantity of those prohibitions — a *ke-zayit* — but *shratzim* cause *tumah* and transgression at the size of a lentil bean (*ke-adasha*), smaller than an olive. Had the rule for *shratzim* been derived from the other cases, one might have applied a *ke-zayit* minimum even to the liquid form of *shratzim*, whereas the proper minimum is a *ke-adasha*. The separate verse for *shratzim* is therefore necessary to establish that the liquid of *shratzim* follows the rules specific to *shratzim*, including the smaller minimum quantity.

Conversely, could one not have written the liquids rule only by *shratzim* and derived the rest from it? No: *mah li-shratzim she-kein tumatam be-mashehu* — the very stringency of *shratzim*, that they cause *tumah* in a small amount, is a reason one might not have applied the logic to other prohibited items that require a larger minimum. Thus both directions of derivation fail, and the full set of verses is necessary to establish the principle across all categories.

It is worth noting, as *Tosafot* observes, that throughout this sugya the Gemara treats two conceptually distinct phenomena as roughly equivalent: (a) liquefying a solid substance and consuming it in liquid form, and (b) liquids that naturally run off from a substance. These are quite different. Liquefied meat is still the meat itself in altered form; the juices running off meat are a separate substance that emerged from the meat. One could in principle imagine different rulings for the two — and indeed, there is an argument that liquefied meat, as the very substance of the original item, should be *more* clearly prohibited, while runoff juices, which are not the item itself, might be treated differently. The Gemara, however, treats them as equivalent for purposes of this discussion.

## Ag Product Liquids

### Forbidden Items

The Gemara now applies the principle of liquid equivalence to a new set of cases. A *baraita* teaches: *ha-tevel ve-ha-chadash ve-ha-hekdesh ve-ha-shvi'it ve-ha-kilayim* — untithed grain (*tevel*), new grain before *Pesach* (*chadash*), sanctified items (*hekdesh*), Sabbatical year produce (*shvi'it*), and forbidden agricultural mixtures (*kilayim*) — *kulan mashkin ha-yotzin me-hen ke-motan*: the liquids that emerge from any of these items have the same status as the items themselves.

### Liquid Status

The rule appears straightforward in light of what has just been established: liquids from forbidden items share the status of the items. But the Gemara now asks — where does this rule come from? Can it be derived from the cases already discussed?

### Natural vs. Imposed

The problem is that the three cases of *chelev*, *chametz*, and *neveilah* all involve items whose forbidden status is entirely natural — their prohibition arises from what they inherently are. The cases of *hekdesh* and *kilayim*, however, involve an imposed status: *hekdesh* becomes sanctified through a human act of designation; *kilayim* acquires its forbidden character through the human act of cross-planting. One might argue that an imposed status, being less intrinsic to the object, would operate differently — perhaps it attaches only to the object in its natural form and does not extend to the liquids that emerge from it. This is the distinction: *issur ha-ba me-elav* (a prohibition that arises of itself, naturally) versus an imposed prohibition. Rashi understands the point in terms of *hekdesh*; *Tosafot* identifies *kilayim* as the primary example.

So the Gemara can derive the rule for naturally prohibited items from the earlier cases, but cannot derive it for items with imposed status. The cases where the prohibition arises naturally can draw on the precedent of *chelev*, *chametz*, and *neveilah*. The question is: what is the source for the imposed-status cases?

### Bikurim Model

The Gemara answers: we learn it from *bikurim*. *Bikurim* is not a natural state — one must actively designate produce as first-fruits — and yet the principle of liquid equivalence applies there. Therefore *bikurim* serves as the model for all cases of imposed status.

### Bikurim Source

Where do we know that liquid equivalence applies by *bikurim*? Rabbi Yosi teaches from the verse: the Torah says to bring *pri* — fruit — from the first of the land's produce. *Pri ata meivi ve-ee ata meivi mashke* — you bring fruit, not liquid; one does not bring mere juice to the *Beit ha-Mikdash* in lieu of the fruit. However, if one brought the fruit and subsequently pressed it into wine, a different verse — *tavi*, "you shall bring," interpreted inclusively — indicates that it retains its *bikurim* status even in transformed liquid form. So: first bring the fruit intact; but once it has been brought and designated as *bikurim*, transforming it into a liquid does not strip it of that status.

### Bikurim Weight

One might challenge the extension from *bikurim* to other cases: *mah le-bikurim she-kein te'unim kri'ah ve-hanacha* — *bikurim* is uniquely weighty, requiring a formal declaration (*kri'ah*) and placement before the altar (*hanacha*). Perhaps the principle of liquid equivalence reflects this heightened significance of *bikurim* and does not apply to lesser categories.

### Terumah Link

The Gemara therefore shifts to *terumah*. Like *bikurim*, *terumah* is an imposed status (the priest's portion is designated by human action). We learn liquid equivalence by *terumah* from its link to *bikurim*: the verse speaks of *terumat yadecha* — the *terumah* of your hand — and the rabbis identify this as a reference to *bikurim*. Thus *bikurim* are called *terumah*, and what is true of *bikurim* regarding liquids is therefore true of *terumah*.

### Terumah Weight

But *terumah* too is weightier than ordinary cases: one who inadvertently eats *terumah* is liable to pay the principal and add a fifth (*chomesh*), and eating *terumah* intentionally carries a penalty of death at heaven's hand (*mita bi-ydei shamayim*). Perhaps the principle of liquid equivalence by *terumah* reflects this stringency and cannot be exported to less weighty prohibitions like *kilayim*.

### Combined Cases

The Gemara's resolution is to combine cases. By taking *terumah* and *bikurim* together with one of the naturally-prohibited cases (such as *shratzim*, or *neveilah*, or *chametz*), we can establish the principle across all types of prohibitions: naturally arising and imposed, lightly penalized and heavily penalized. Each pairing addresses the challenge raised by the other member's unique stringency, and together they establish that liquid equivalence is a universal rule applying to all forbidden items, whether their status arises naturally or by human imposition.

## Terumah Liquids

### Terumah Examples

Having established that liquids emerging from *terumah* share the status of *terumah* itself, the Gemara cites a *mishnah* (actually a *baraita*) that applies this principle in practice, while revealing that there is a significant dispute about its scope. The *baraita* lists: honey made from dates (*devash temarim*), apple cider (*yayin tapuchim*), vinegar made from the bitter late-season grapes (*chometz sitvaniyot*), and other liquid products made from *terumah* fruits.

### Rabbi Eliezer View

Rabbi Eliezer rules that all such liquid products of *terumah* have full *terumah* status: one who eats them inadvertently must pay the principal (*keren*) plus the added fifth (*chomesh*) — *mechayev keren ve-chomesh*. In Rabbi Eliezer's view, the principle we have established — that liquids from *terumah* share the status of *terumah* — applies universally to all fruits designated as *terumah* and their liquid derivatives.

### Rabbi Yehoshua View

Rabbi Yehoshua exempts — *poter* — holding that these liquids do not carry *terumah* status. The dispute is not about the general principle that liquids can share the status of their source; both authorities accept that. The debate is about how to apply the derivation.

### Damino Mimo Debate

The Gemara explains: *be-damino mino u-be-damino ve-okei ba-asra paligi* — they dispute whether, when learning a rule from an analogy, one preserves the original context of the source (*damino mino*) or applies the context of the target case (*okei ba-asra*).

Rabbi Eliezer holds *damino mino*: we derived the liquid equivalence rule for *terumah* from *bikurim*. Just as by *bikurim* the Torah says *pri ha-adamah* — the fruit of the ground — and therefore the liquids of all types of fruit count as *bikurim* in liquid form, so too by *terumah*, the liquids of all types of fruit designated as *terumah* carry *terumah* status. We preserve the broad context of *bikurim* — all fruits — and carry it over to *terumah*.

Rabbi Yehoshua holds *okei ba-asra*: yes, we learn from *bikurim* that liquid equivalence applies. But now apply it within the specific context of *terumah*. What liquids does *terumah* biblically recognize? Only *tirosh* and *yitzhar* — wine and oil. The Torah's language for *terumah* is *dagan, tirosh, ve-yitzhar*: grain, wine, and oil. Other liquids are not mentioned in the biblical law of *terumah*. Therefore, when we learn the principle of liquid equivalence from *bikurim* and apply it to *terumah*, we apply it only to those liquids that *terumah* itself explicitly recognizes: wine and oil.

### Wine & Oil Only

The practical consequence of Rabbi Yehoshua's position, as *Tosafot* explains it, is that the only liquid products of *terumah* fruits that carry the full *terumah* status — requiring payment of principal and fifth if inadvertently consumed — are wine and oil. Honey of dates, apple cider, and other fruit juices, even from fruits that are unquestionably *terumah*, do not carry *terumah* status as liquids, because the Torah did not recognize such liquids within the framework of *terumah* law. Notably, this may extend even to liquids that run off of grain or barley, since the Torah by *terumah* highlights specifically the liquids of wine and oil, implicitly excluding others.

The daf thus closes at a turning point. The long arc of the discussion has established that liquids are generally treated as equivalent to the solids from which they derive — whether through liquefaction or natural runoff, whether the prohibition is natural or imposed. Yet that principle is now shown to have limits. For *terumah*, at least according to Rabbi Yehoshua, the recognized liquids are specifically wine and oil; other liquid derivatives of *terumah* produce do not carry full *terumah* status. The discussion of precisely where liquid equivalence applies and where it breaks down will continue into the next amud.
