# Hullin 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Locusts: Species vs. Tra…

### Four Locusts Listed

The *gemara* on 66a opens with the words *b'mai kamiflagi* — "what is the point of debate?" — picking up a discussion that had been underway concerning which grasshoppers and locusts are permitted for consumption. The background is straightforward: the Torah lists four creatures — *arbeh*, *salam*, *chargol*, and *chagav* — and two distinct approaches had emerged for understanding the scope of these designations.

The first approach treats the enumeration as exhaustive, analogous to the Torah's list of non-kosher birds: only those four specific species, and their close relatives by virtue of *leminehu* ("and their kind"), are permitted. This is a reasonable position, paralleling how *peshat* normally works when the Torah enumerates rather than characterizes. The second approach, by contrast, looks for the common denominators among the four, extracting a general category defined by certain *simanim*: jumper legs, four legs, wings covering the majority of the body, and the creature being called a *chagav*. This approach is at once broader — not limited to those four species — and narrower, because it ultimately requires a *masorah*, a living tradition of identification, to determine what is and is not called a *chagav*. This is precisely why most Jews today, even if inclined toward curiosity, do not eat grasshoppers: the necessary *masorah* has been interrupted for most communities, though Yemenite Jews and certain others do maintain such a tradition.

### Tana D'bei Rav Approach

The *gemara* identifies the first approach as that of *tana d'bei rav* — the *tanna* of the house of Rav — and Rashi's commentary clarifies precisely what this means. Three lines down in his wide-line commentary, Rashi explains: *kari l'braita kammaita she-shnuyah b'Torat Kohanim* — this refers to the first *braita*, which appears in the *torat kohanim*, the halakhic *midrash* on Vayikra, known as the *sifra*. Rashi adds that it is called *sifra d'bei rav* — the book of the house of the master — because it was the standard text, memorized in the house of study: *lefi she-hayah shagur b'veit hamidrash b'fi kulam*. The parallel works on Bamidbar and Devarim are called *sifrei*, a plural of the same root, designating the books of the school of the master. Critically, *d'bei rav* does not refer to Rav the *amora*; this is a *tannaitic* work, and the title simply means "the master's text."

The *tana d'bei rav*'s position operates through *klal u'prat*: the phrase *asher lo chra'ayim* — "which has jumper legs" — is a general statement, the *klal*. Then the Torah enumerates the four specific species: *arbeh*, *salam*, *chargol*, *chagav* — that is the *prat*, the specific. Under the rule *klal u'prat ein baklal ela mah shebaprat*, the general yields to the specific, and we learn only from that which the specific includes. *leminehu* broadens the category modestly — related members of those four species are included — but the essential limitation is that only creatures belonging to those four species, and their relatives, are permitted. *d'mineih in, d'la d'mineih lo*: of those four species, yes; not of those four species, no. On this view, a creature with an elongated head — which none of the four Torah-listed locusts possess — simply does not belong to those species and is therefore excluded, full stop.

There is actually a genuine tension here that deserves attention. By fish, the Torah provides only characteristics: fins and scales. By birds, it provides only a list, no characteristics at all. By mammals, it does both — enumerating some kosher species like *ayal*, *tzvi*, and *yachmur*, while also providing the identifying characteristics of split hooves and cud-chewing. In practice we treat the characteristics as primary for mammals, somewhat discounting the list. The locusts present the same ambiguity: the Torah both specifies characteristics (*asher lo chra'ayim mima'al leraglav*) and enumerates four species. Which takes precedence? That is precisely the debate. The *tana d'bei rav* says the list takes precedence; the second approach, as we shall see, says the characteristics take precedence, with the list serving to sharpen them.

### Rabbi Yishmael Approach

The second approach is attributed to *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael* — the school of Rabbi Yishmael. Its hermeneutical structure is different: *asher lo chra'ayim* is the first general principle, the first *klal*; *arbeh*, *salam*, *chargol*, *chagav* constitute the *prat*, the specific; and then *leminehu* — rather than being absorbed into the *prat* as a modest extension of those species — functions as a second, separate general statement, a second *klal*. The result is a *klal u'prat u'klal* structure: general, specific, general. Under this hermeneutic, *ei ata dan ela k'ein haprat* — one may include anything similar to the specific — and furthermore, *u'marbeh kol d'dami leih b'chad tzad*: we include everything comparable to the *pratim* in at least one significant respect.

This does not mean, however, that similarity in any single arbitrary feature suffices. In practice the approach demands multiple *simanim*: the jumper legs that the Torah explicitly mentions, four legs in total, wings covering the majority of the body, and the requirement that the creature be called a *chagav*. What the *klal u'prat u'klal* structure does allow is flexibility at the margins — the creature need not be identical to the *pratim* in every last detail. The specific way this plays out is that all four Torah species happen to have rounded heads, yet *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael* does not demand a rounded head as a *siman*, because the *klal u'prat u'klal* structure allows inclusion of things that are not similar in all respects, and therefore even a creature with an elongated head may be permitted.

### Elongated Head Question

The *gemara* now makes explicit that the *b'rosho aroch kamiflagi* — the elongated head — is precisely the practical difference between the two approaches. The *tana d'bei rav*, operating through *klal u'prat*, restricts permitted locusts to those of the four listed species and their relatives, none of which have elongated heads; thus elongated-headed creatures are categorically excluded. *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael*, using *klal u'prat u'klal*, can be more inclusive, not demanding that the creature replicate all characteristics of the *pratim*. Tosafot notes that it might be that *chazal* believed there was no grasshopper possessing all the other kosher *simanim* but lacking an elongated head — and therefore the *tana d'bei rav* could have stated the exclusion even more broadly — yet the salient point remains that the elongated head functions as the practical test case distinguishing the two approaches.

## Klal Prat Klal Rules

### Two Klals Not Equal

The *gemara* now raises a technical challenge to the *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael*'s entire structure: *v'ha lo dami chlala kama lechlala basra* — the first *klal* is not equivalent to the last *klal*. For a valid *klal u'prat u'klal* derivation, the standard requirement is that the two flanking general statements be comparable in scope — a structure of A-B-A, where the two A's are not necessarily identical in wording but must have a similar range.

Here, the first *klal* — *asher lo chra'ayim* — is quite broad: any creature with jumper legs may be eaten; any without may not. The final *klal* — *leminehu* — is considerably narrower, since it abstracts from the specific four species listed in the *prat* and therefore carries the implicit limitations of those species. *kullala basra* demands similarity in four *simanim*, whereas *kullala kama* demanded only the presence of jumper legs. These two general statements do not have equivalent scope.

### Yishmael's Flexibility

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty with a pointed statement: *tanna devei Rabbi Yishmael be-chlali u-prati ki hai gavna don* — Rabbi Yishmael's school applies *klal u'prat* to configurations of precisely this type. And the *gemara* adds a reflective observation: *v'damrina nami b'alma dadon tanna devei Rabbi Yishmael be-chlali u-prati ki hai gavna — me-hacha*. Wherever one encounters the principle in *shas* that Rabbi Yishmael applies *klal u'prat u'klal* even when the two *klal*s are not equivalent in scope — that ruling is derived from precisely this *sugya*. This is not a peripheral concession; it is the source text for understanding the scope of Rabbi Yishmael's hermeneutical flexibility throughout the *talmud*.

One may reasonably wonder whether this flexibility is connected to the broader characterization of Rabbi Yishmael as the proponent of *dibrah Torah kilshon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the language of human beings — in contrast to Rabbi Akiva, who derived *tilei tilim shel halachot* from every nuance and jot. Rabbi Akiva's approach treats every variation in expression as juristically significant, demanding precise symmetry in constructions like *klal u'prat u'klal*. Rabbi Yishmael is less moved by slight formal variations, treating them as ordinary features of human expression rather than precise technical signals. His more flexible approach to *klal u'prat u'klal* — tolerating unequal *klal*s — coheres naturally with a disposition toward *dibrah Torah kilshon bnei adam*. Whether the two are formally connected is left open, but the parallel is striking.

### Ribui Miyut Approach

Rashi adds a further dimension, which addresses directly the question Charlie raised earlier: even when performing the *klal u'prat u'klal*, there are two distinct ways of executing the derivation. One method requires that the included item share all characteristics of the *pratim*, merely abstracting from them. The other, sometimes called *ribui miyut v'ribui*, proceeds in the opposite direction: the starting inclination is toward maximal inclusion, and the *pratim* serve only to establish that the included item cannot be radically different from the specific examples. Under this second approach, the analysis is much more permissive. It is precisely this inclusive methodology that Rashi says is operative here, and it is what allows the school of Rabbi Yishmael not to worry about the shape of the head. The *pratim* inform the characteristics that matter — jumper legs, four legs, wings covering the majority, being called *chagav* — but they do not require replication of every incidental detail that the four species happen to share, such as a rounded head.

## Salam & Head Shape

### Chagav Alone Enough?

The *gemara* then turns to a potential question from within the structure of *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael*'s *braita* itself. That *braita* establishes, by virtue of the fourth category *chagav*, that any permitted locust must be called a *chagav*. This raises a natural question: once we introduce the requirement that it be called a *chagav*, could one argue that this alone suffices — that the name *chagav* without the other *simanim* would be enough to render the creature kosher? The *braita* addresses this: *yachol ein bo kol hasimanim halalu* — might one think it does not need all these characteristics? And it answers: *talmud lomar leminehu* — the Torah says "according to its kind," meaning *ad she-yeish bo kol hasimanim halalu* — it must still possess all the characteristics. Being called a *chagav* is necessary but not sufficient.

The *gemara* then pushes back with *m'heicha teisei* — "from where does this idea even arise?" If not a creature called *chagav*, why would one ever think that the name alone suffices without the other requirements? After all, the prior discussion had established the characteristics based on the convergence of *arbeh* and *chargol*; no one simply introduced the name *chagav* as a stand-alone criterion. The *gemara* answers: *i lav ksiv salam, kide'ka amrat* — had *salam* not been written, you would be right, and such a *hava amina* would never arise. But *hashta d'ksiv salam l'ribui rosho aroch* — now that *salam* was introduced for the purpose of broadening the category to include even the elongated head, one might momentarily think that *salam*'s introduction licenses dismissing all distinctive characteristics, leaving only the name *chagav* as the operative criterion.

### Salam Dismisses Head

The mechanism by which *salam* accomplishes this requires brief recapitulation of the prior day's discussion. The question had been: if none of the four Torah-listed locusts has an elongated head, how does the school of Rabbi Yishmael justify extending permission to creatures with elongated heads at all? The answer, based on Rav Achai's question, was that *salam* was entirely superfluous as a listing. Everything that could be learned from *salam* — the *simanim* it establishes and the dismissal of other distinctive characteristics — could already have been derived from *arbeh* and *chargol* alone. Because *salam* was wholly extra, its introduction was available to do additional work: specifically, *l'ribui rosho aroch*, to include even the elongated head by signaling that head shape is not a decisive characteristic.

Once *salam* performs this function — dismissing one distinctive feature shared by all the *pratim* — the *gemara* entertains the possibility that *salam*'s introduction could be understood even more expansively: *eima l'rabuyei nami kol dehu*, perhaps *salam* is telling us to dismiss all distinctive characteristics, leaving nothing required beyond the name *chagav*. This would produce an extremely broad category of permitted creatures.

### Leminehu Final Check

The final *leminehu* closes this opening. *atya kamashma lan* — therefore it teaches us. The concluding *leminehu* reinstates the requirement that the creature belong to the same kind as the *pratim*. One does not dismiss all characteristics; rather, *salam* licenses dismissing the concern about head shape specifically, but the creature must still possess jumper legs, four legs, wings covering the majority, and be called a *chagav*. The final *leminehu* functions as a ceiling on the broadening permitted by *salam*'s introduction, ensuring that *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael*'s approach, while more inclusive than the *tana d'bei rav*'s, does not collapse into a purely name-based determination with no physical *simanim* at all.

## Locust Names by Place

### Opposite Names Used

A final question closes the locust discussion. When the *sifra* (*tana d'bei rav*) identified the four locusts by their contemporary equivalents, it said *salam ze rishon* and *chargol ze nipol* — using those terms to identify *salam* and *chargol* respectively. But when *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael* identified them, the correspondences were reversed: *salam ze nipol* and *chargol ze rishon*. They used opposite identifications for the same two creatures. One might suppose that this represents a genuine disagreement about which creature is which — but accepting that conclusion would undermine the entire preceding analysis. The *gemara* throughout has been assuming that we know which creatures have elongated heads, which have tails, which have certain shaped foreheads, and so on; the entire discussion of distinctive characteristics presupposed confident identification. If the two schools actually disagree about which creature is called *salam* and which *chargol*, much of the foregoing discussion would be called into question.

### Mar Ki Atrei Ruling

The *gemara*'s resolution is *mar ki atrei u-mar ki atrei* — each one follows the usage of his own locale. This is not a substantive disagreement about the nature of the creatures; it is a difference in regional terminology. Place a particular animal in front of both schools and they would agree on what it is; they simply use different local names to refer to it. The *gemara* could theoretically have said it is a genuine dispute about identity, but given the coherence of everything said previously, it prefers the resolution that each school employs its own geographical nomenclature.

This, as a general observation, makes all such discussions frustrating when read at a temporal and geographic remove of fifteen centuries. The *gemara* translates biblical terms into terms familiar to its audience — but those terms were meaningful only for the speakers of that time and place. A translation into Aramaic or local vernacular that conveyed precise identification to a third-century Babylonian sage conveys nothing to us today.

## Shechita Not Required

### No Shechita Needed

The discussion of grasshoppers concludes with the question of *shechita*. For those who maintain a *masorah* permitting consumption of certain locusts, or simply as a matter of law, do grasshoppers require ritual slaughter? We know from the tractate that *shechita* is required for mammals. For birds, while there is technically a debate, the dominant and consensus position is that *shechita* is required, though only one *siman* — cutting either the trachea or esophagus — suffices. The implicit assumption has been that creatures of a lesser order than birds — fish and the like — do not require *shechita*, but the *gemara* has never stated this explicitly regarding grasshoppers.

### Halachot Gedolot Source

Rashi here quotes the *halachot gedolot*, a highly authoritative early Geonic work often attributed to Rav Yehudai Gaon (seventh century). The relevant passage in Rashi begins with the words *ve-chagavim hallalu* in the line starting *yesh lo zanav*, nine lines before the wider lines in the right-hand column of Rashi's commentary. The *halachot gedolot* states: *ein te'unim shechita* — grasshoppers do not require *shechita* — *she-harei achar dagim hizkiram hakatuv* — because the Torah mentions them after fish, and fish certainly do not require *shechita*.

The source text is the *pasuk* that concludes the kosher laws: *zot torat habehemah* — this is the law of the *behemah*; *ve-ha-of* — and the bird. Then: *ve-chol nefesh ha-chayah ha-romeset ba-mayim* — every living creature that moves in the waters — *elu dagim*, those are fish. And *ul'chol nefesh ha-sheretzet al ha-aretz* — everything that creeps on the ground — *elu chagavim*, those are grasshoppers. The *pasuk* thus enumerates a sequence: mammals, birds, fish, grasshoppers. Since fish do not require *shechita*, grasshoppers, which appear after fish in this progression, certainly do not. Rashi emphasizes that the *bahag* is reading this *pasuk* as referring specifically to kosher animals — not to the full range of crustaceans, rodents, and other non-kosher creatures that might also be described by these phrases — in order to establish the continuum.

Tosafot notes that this conclusion could have been proven from other *gemarot* that explicitly discuss eating grasshoppers alive, without *shechita*, without any legal impediment. Rashi chose to cite the *bahag* specifically because the *bahag* grounds the ruling in a *pasuk* rather than leaving it as a *kal va-chomer* from scattered *gemarot*.

### Aiver Min HaChai

This raises a further question: if grasshoppers require no *shechita*, is there a prohibition of *eiver min ha-chai* — eating a limb torn from a living creature — when consuming them? The answer is that *eiver min ha-chai* does not apply to grasshoppers, and this point is conceptually important. There are two possible explanations for why *eiver min ha-chai* is inapplicable. One line of reasoning holds that *eiver min ha-chai* applies only to creatures for which *shechita* is required — because *shechita* is the Torah's signal that the animal is forbidden until a specific act makes it permissible, and the prohibition of *eiver min ha-chai* is conceptually tethered to that framework. Since grasshoppers and fish require no *shechita*, they fall outside this framework entirely.

An alternative route runs through the prohibition of *dam* — blood. The *pasuk* from which *eiver min ha-chai* is learned is connected to a prohibition that also encompasses *dam min ha-chai*, and that prohibition extends only to creatures for which there is an *issur dam* — a prohibition on consuming their blood. Fish and grasshoppers carry no *issur dam*, and therefore *eiver min ha-chai* does not apply to them on that basis either. The matter warrants further thought as to which of these conceptual foundations is more precisely correct, but either way the conclusion is the same: for the fish-and-grasshopper category, the *issur* of *eiver min ha-chai* does not apply.

In connection with fish: the *gemara* elsewhere says *asifatan zu hi shechitatan* — gathering them from the water is equivalent to their *shechita*. Whether this means fish technically require *shechita* but that gathering suffices, or whether it means they have no *shechita* requirement at all and the phrase is purely descriptive, remains a question. With grasshoppers, there is no such ambiguity, as the *gemara* explicitly discusses consuming them alive without legal difficulty.

## Fish Fins & Scales

### Scales Imply Fins

The discussion now turns to fish. The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *ein lo achshav ve-atid legadel leachar zman* — if a fish does not currently have fins or scales but will grow them later — *kegon ha-sultanit ve-ha-afyan*, like the *sultanit* and *afyan* (apparently small fish, perhaps a variety of sardine or young tuna that have not yet developed their scales) — *harei zeh mutar*, it is nevertheless permitted. Permissibility is determined by species, not by the particular state of the individual specimen at the moment of examination. As long as the species characteristically possesses fins and scales, an individual that has not yet grown them, or that will shed them, is still permitted.

The same principle applies in the reverse direction: *yesh lo achshav ve-atid le-hashir besha'ah she-oleh min ha-mayim* — if the fish currently has fins and scales but will shed them, particularly the scales, when removed from the water. The *gemara* lists several examples: *achonis*, *aphunis*, *kaspatiyam*, *achspatiyas*, and *atunis* — the last of which is almost certainly some variety of tuna. Rashi identifies these as Persian terms; Rabbeinu Chananel, cited in Artscroll's notes to *avodah zarah* 39a, says they are Greek names, which is more consonant with their phonology. In any case, Tosafot in another context raises the practice of eating certain fish that lacked observable scales at the moment of capture, relying on examination of the fish's eggs to determine species identity and thus establish permissibility on a species basis.

*Harei zeh mutar* — such fish are permitted. The operative principle is that species membership, not the current state of the individual specimen, determines permissibility.

### Immature Scales OK

This ruling coheres with the *mishna* in *niddah* (cited by the *gemara* as *tnan hatam* — "we taught over there"), which appears in a section of that tractate that, in the manner of several tractates, collects disparate topics following a common logical pattern. The *mishna* states: *kol she-yesh lo kaskeset yesh lo snapir* — anything that has scales has fins. Conversely, *ve-yesh she-yesh lo snapir ve-ein lo kaskeset* — there are fish that have fins but no scales. The practical import is clear: one need only verify scales; fins will follow automatically. The permissible species — those that have both — are identified by scales alone, since scales guarantee fins but not vice versa. Any fish with scales is therefore kosher *de-oraita*, and verifying scales alone is sufficient.

### Gemara's Question: Why Both Fins Scales

The *mishna* in *niddah* then states the obvious conclusion: if a fish has *kaskeset* — scales — *ve-yesh lo snapir* — and fins — it is kosher. If it has fins but not scales, it is *tamei*, non-kosher. This is perfectly logical given the relationship just described. But it immediately generates a sharp question: *mikdi, a-kaskeset samchinan* — we rely on the scales; we don't actually need to examine the fin, since having scales guarantees fins. If so, *lichtov Rachmana kaskeset v'lo lichtov snapir* — let the Torah write scales and not mention fins at all. Why did the Torah need to mention both?

## Both Terms Purpose

### Disambiguation

The *gemara*'s initial response is that in principle the objection is well-taken, but: *i katav Rachmana kaskeset v'lo katav snapir* — had the Torah written only *kaskeset* and not *snapir*, *hava amina* — I might not have known what the word *kaskeset* meant. One might have thought *mai kaskeset* — what is *kaskeset*? — and concluded it referred to fins, *snapir*. The word itself, taken in isolation, could conceivably be interpreted as referring to some type of flat, movable, plate-like protrusion — which fins resemble in a loose sense. By writing both *snapir* and *kaskeset*, the Torah forces the reader to distinguish them: one of them must be fins, the other must be something else, i.e., scales. The two words together disambiguate each other.

### Armor of Scales

The *gemara* does not find this response fully satisfying. *ve-hashta de-katav rachmana snapir ve-kaskeset* — now that it wrote both words — *l'mai de-kaskeset levusha hu* — we now understand *kaskeset* to mean scales, a type of covering. But how do we know this? Even granted that there are now two words and one must mean fins, how do we know which is which?

The answer is that we know the meaning of *kaskeset* independently from another *pasuk*: the description of Goliath in Shmuel says *ve-shiryon kaskasim hu lavush* — and he wore armor of *kaskasim*. There, *kaskasim* refers to overlapping plate-like coverings worn as protective armor — the type of segmented plate armor, perhaps analogous to what later traditions would call scale mail or lamellar armor, consisting of interlocking tiles or plates. Since *kaskasim* demonstrably means a layered plate-like covering in that context, *kaskeset* in the fish laws must mean the same — the scales that cover the fish's body. This being so, we can derive the meaning of *kaskeset* from outside the fish laws, and the *gemara* is therefore forced to restate the question: *lichtov Rachmana kaskeset ve-lo lichtov snapir*. Now that we know what *kaskeset* means, write just *kaskeset* — scales — and not *snapir* — fins — since scales already guarantee fins. Why mention both?

### Yagdil Torah

The *gemara*'s final answer is attributed to Rabbi Abbahu, who cites *tana devei Rabbi Yishmael*: *yagdil Torah ve-yadir* — the Torah glorifies and magnifies itself. This is a quotation from Yeshayahu (42:21). The *gemara*'s invocation of this principle signals that the Torah did not need to write both terms, but it does so in order to be *marbeh divrei Torah* — to multiply the words of Torah. Rather than a minimalist presentation that merely enables correct *halakhic* conclusions, the Torah presents its material with fullness and redundancy, and that very redundancy is itself an expression of the Torah's dignity and grandeur.

Tosafot, in *bava batra*, offers an additional gloss. The line *hacha amar lan tana le-meida mahu kaskeset* suggests that the dual mention here teaches us in multiple ways what the word *kaskeset* means. Even if one could derive it from the *pasuk* about Goliath's armor, writing both *snapir* and *kaskeset* in the fish laws makes it maximally clear, without requiring the reader to draw on a verse from Nevi'im written centuries later. The Torah tells us the same thing through multiple pathways — and that multiplication of clarity is itself a form of *yagdil Torah ve-yadir*.

It is worth noting that this principle is again associated specifically with *tana d'bei rabbi yishmael* — the same school that we earlier saw willing to treat certain formally extra words as not carrying additional technical legal weight (*dibrah Torah kilshon bnei adam*). There is an interesting internal consistency: the school that is less committed to the doctrine that every extra word must carry unique halakhic freight is also the school prepared to say that some words are written simply for the sake of glorifying the Torah through fullness of expression.

## Tosafot Knowledge Questi…

### 700 Species Known

Tosafot raises a penetrating question about the methodological foundations of what *chazal* claim to know about fish. The *gemara* states as a matter of settled law that *kol she-yesh lo kaskeset yesh lo snapir* — every fish that has scales has fins. This is not just an empirical generalization about observed fish; it is presented as a universal rule upon which *halakhic* decisions may be reliably made. And separately, a *braita* states that there are precisely seven hundred non-kosher species of fish — or at least that the number of non-kosher species is bounded in this way, such that if one excludes those species, all others are permitted.

The question Tosafot poses is simple and searching: how did the Rabbis know these things? To establish that every fish with scales has fins, they would need to have observed every species of fish in existence. Even granting that they were supremely diligent naturalists, observation of hundreds of species gives you confidence in what you have seen — it does not give you confidence in what you have not. And the same question applies to the enumeration of seven hundred non-kosher species: *minayin haya lahem she-hittiru b'kach et ha-shar* — how did they know that by excluding those seven hundred they could declare all others permitted? How did they know there were no additional non-kosher species not on their list?

### Adam Named All

Tosafot proposes that the answer lies in a *masorah* — a tradition — going back to *adam haRishon*. When *adam haRishon* named all the creatures of creation, he did not merely assign arbitrary labels; he understood the nature and characteristics of each species with a comprehensiveness unavailable to later naturalists. If that identification was transmitted through the generations, *chazal* would have had access to a body of knowledge about the entirety of animal species that transcends what ordinary observation could establish.

However, Tosafot immediately notes the difficulty with this answer. The Torah says: *va-yikra ha-adam shemot le-chol ha-behemah u-le-of ha-shamayim* — and Adam called names to all the land animals and to the birds of the sky. The *pasuk* explicitly mentions only the *behemot* and the *of* — land animals and birds. There is no mention of Adam naming the fish or aquatic creatures, and therefore the *masorah* from Adam seemingly covers only land animals and birds, not fish. And without a tradition from Adam about fish, one cannot explain how *chazal* could make universal claims about them.

### Kol Asher Yikra

Tosafot resolves this difficulty by citing a phrase in the same passage: *mikol asher yikra lo ha-adam* — "from all that Adam called a name." This formulation, *kol asher yikra*, is expansive: it includes everything that Adam named, without restriction. *yesh l'rabot afilu dagim* — this serves to include even fish. Even though the verse explicitly mentions only *behemot* and *of*, the inclusive phrase *kol asher yikra* extends Adam's naming — and therefore the transmitted *masorah* — to aquatic creatures as well.

On this reading, the universal claim that every fish with scales has fins, and the precise enumeration of non-kosher species, both rest not on exhaustive scientific observation by the Rabbis themselves, but on a received tradition from Adam HaRishon whose comprehensive naming of all creatures constituted a complete and authoritative taxonomy. The *masorah* that flowed from that original act of naming provided *chazal* with knowledge they could not otherwise have acquired through observation alone.

What is notable — and the lecturer flags it explicitly — is what Tosafot does *not* say. The answer is not *ruach ha-kodesh* — divine inspiration granted to the Rabbis themselves. Tosafot does not invoke that possibility even in passing. Instead the answer is empirical-historical: a tradition of knowledge, traceable to Adam's original act of naming, that was transmitted through the generations. This is a striking methodological choice, and it raises a question that Tosafot apparently considers legitimate rather than presumptuous: how did they know? The answer is not "they knew because they were great enough that God told them," but rather "they had a tradition, and here is its source."

## Positive Negative Both

### Eat & Don't Eat

A *braita* (*tanu rabbanan*) now examines why the Torah states both the positive commandment regarding kosher fish and the negative commandment regarding non-kosher fish, when each should be inferable from the other. The Torah says *et zeh tochlu mikol asher ba-mayim* — this is what you may eat from all that is in the water — listing creatures with *snapir* and *kaskeset*. It then separately says *ve-zeh lo tochlu mikol asher ba-mayim kol asher ein lo* — and this you shall not eat from all that is in the water, anything that lacks them.

The *braita* notes the apparent redundancy. From the positive — *et sheyeish lo echol* — one would naturally infer the negative: do not eat what lacks these *simanim*. And from the negative — *et she-ein lo al tochal* — one would equally infer the positive: eat what does have them. Each formulation implies the other. So why did the Torah state both?

### Aseh Docheh Lo

The answer is: *la'avor alav belo ta'aseh va-aseh* — to make one who eats non-kosher fish transgress not only a negative commandment but also a positive one. The positive commandment to eat fish with the proper *simanim* frames its obverse — not eating fish without those *simanim* — as the violation of a positive commandment in addition to the negative. This is not merely rhetorical emphasis; it carries technical *halakhic* consequence.

The operative principle is *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* — a positive commandment can, under certain conditions, override a negative commandment when they directly conflict and cannot both be fulfilled. This is a well-established Talmudic principle with significant practical applications. However, *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* applies only when the conflicting commandment is a negative alone; it does not apply when one is confronting both a negative and a positive commandment simultaneously. By framing the fish prohibition as both an *aseh* and a *lo ta'aseh*, the Torah forecloses any possible argument that some other positive commandment might override the fish prohibition. Even if one were to construct a scenario — say, a hypothetical where the only available *matzah* for the Pesach obligation contained non-kosher fish oil, and the question arose whether the *aseh* of eating *matzah* could override the *lo ta'aseh* of eating non-kosher fish — the *aseh* framing of the fish laws eliminates that discussion entirely. Where both positive and negative apply, no external *aseh* can override.

## Water Source Rules

### Two Eating Mentions

The *gemara* now turns to the question of what water sources trigger the requirement for kosher *simanim* in the first place. The relevant observation is that the *pasuk* governing fish uses the language of permission twice: once in the formulation *et zeh tochlu mikol asher ba-mayim* — this is what you eat from all that is in the water — and once at the conclusion, *otam tochelu* — you shall eat them. *mah talmud lomar* — what do we learn from this repetition? Each of these *eating* mentions serves a distinct purpose, as the *gemara* proceeds to demonstrate.

### Running Water Rule

The primary reference — *bayamim u-va-nechalim* — in seas and rivers — establishes the basic rule: creatures in natural running bodies of water require kosher *simanim*. *d'chi it lei echol, d'leit lei lo tichol* — if it has the required *simanim*, eat; if not, do not eat. The categories of *yamim* and *nechalim* — seas and rivers — share the characteristic of being naturally running, flowing bodies of water.

### Vessels Wells OK

The explicit framing around *yamim* and *nechalim* immediately raises a question about other water contexts. What about creatures that form not in rivers or seas but in standing water — in a vessel, a cistern, a well? *ha be-keilim, af al gav d'leit lei* — creatures in vessels, even though they lack the kosher *simanim*, are permitted. The *pasuk*'s deliberate focus on seas and rivers excludes standing water from the requirement. Creatures that spontaneously develop in collected, non-flowing water — the *gemara* envisions creatures that form in rainwater or standing water, not ones taken from a river and placed there — are permissible without any examination of *simanim*.

This has real practical significance. In periods and places where water was not routinely filtered, one might find various water insects in a collected water source. The *halakha* as derived here says: if those creatures formed in standing water and were not drawn from a river or sea, one need not concern oneself with whether they possess kosher *simanim*. This parallels another principle found elsewhere in *chullin*: a worm that grew inside a fruit and has not yet crawled on the ground is permitted, because the prohibition against *sheratzim* applies to creatures that move across the earth, not to those that formed within a contained medium.

### Wells Cisterns

But one might think this leniency applies only to truly artificial containers — a pail or barrel. What about *borot*, *shichin*, and *me'arot* — wells, ditches, and caves? These are not man-made vessels in any ordinary sense; they are naturally occurring or dug-out repositories of ground water. The *gemara* extends the leniency to these as well: *minayin l'rabot borot shichin u-me'arot* — from where do we include wells, ditches, and caves? And the answer expands the scope beyond mere vessels to any water that does not share the character of *yamim* and *nechalim* — naturally flowing sources. Since wells, ditches, and cave springs are not running water, creatures within them are likewise exempt from the *simanim* requirement.

The practical importance of this ruling is underscored by the description of such water sources as places *she-shocheh ve-shoteh me-hen ve-eino nimna* — where one can bow down and drink without restraint. In the ancient world, a traveler finding a well in a remote location would need to drink from it immediately. Having to investigate the kosher status of the microscopic or visible aquatic life within it would be a serious practical burden. The *halakha* relieves that burden entirely.

### Wells Cisterns

*Talmud lomar tochlu mikol asher ba-mayim* — this is why the verse says "you shall eat from all that is in the water" a second time. The first mention established the positive-and-negative structure discussed previously. This second mention extends the permission beyond vessels to wells and cisterns — any standing or ground-sourced water that is not the flowing variety of *yamim* and *nechalim*.

### Mayim Chayim

A striking corollary emerges from this analysis. *mayim chayim* — "living water" — is the Torah's designation for water that is particularly valued for ritual purity purposes: it is the type of water required for a *mikveh* fed by a natural spring, and for other purification contexts. One tends to imagine *mayim chayim* as gushing, rushing spring water — water that is vigorously alive and moving. But in the technical *halakhic* framework, *mayim chayim* can actually refer to well water — water that rises from an underground aquifer directly from the ground, not from collected rainfall. Such water is "living" in the sense that it has a natural source below ground, yet it is not running water in the *yamim u-nechalim* sense.

The consequence is that *mayim chayim* — live water in the technical sense — is precisely the kind of water whose creatures do not require kosher *simanim*. The very water most prized for ritual purposes belongs to the category of standing or ground water that is exempt from the fish-*simanim* requirement. The discussion will continue in the next session.
