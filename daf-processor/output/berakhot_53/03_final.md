# Berakhot 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Crumbs vs. Hand-Washing

### Basic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים מְכַבְּדִין אֶת הַבַּיִת וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹטְלִין לַיָּדַיִם, שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר נוֹטְלִין לַיָּדַיִם תְּחִלָּה, נִמְצָא אַתָּה מַפְסִיד אֶת הָאוֹכָלִין. אֲבָל נְטִילַת יָדַיִם לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי תְּחִלָּה לָא סְבִירָא לְהוּ. מַאי טַעְמָא? — מִשּׁוּם פֵּירוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta* where this issue is discussed in greater detail: **Beit Shammai say: One sweeps the** area of the **house** where the meal took place **and washes his hands thereafter, as if you say that one washes his hands first,** the water is liable to splash on the remaining crumbs and **you will have ruined the food. But Beit Shammai do not hold that the washing of the hands is first. What is the reason? Due to** concern, lest the **crumbs** will be made disgusting.

The Gemara resumes with the *baraita* on *mayim acharonim*: does one first clear the crumbs from the table, or first wash one's hands? The *baraita* presents the positions of both houses: *tanu rabbanan, Beit Shammai omrim mechabdin et habayit ve'achar kach notlin layadayim* — Beit Shammai holds that one first sweeps away the crumbs and then performs *mayim acharonim*, while Beit Hillel reverses the order.

### Beit Shammai View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אִם שַׁמָּשׁ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הוּא, נוֹטֵל פֵּירוּרִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶם כְּזַיִת, וּמַנִּיחַ פֵּירוּרִין שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: If the attendant is a Torah scholar,** he **removes the crumbs that are an olive-bulk** from the table at the end of the meal **and leaves** only **crumbs that are not an olive-bulk,** as food that is less than an olive-bulk is not considered food and there is no prohibition to ruin it.

The reason Beit Hillel prefers *mayim acharonim* first is that if one washes first, the water will spill onto the crumbs and ruin food one might wish to eat later. Beit Shammai does not insist that hand-washing come first; rather, their primary concern is that the crumbs be cleared before any water is introduced. There may also be an argument that *mayim acharonim* should ideally follow immediately upon finishing the meal — particularly given its function of removing filth, odor, and the *melach Sedomit* — but Beit Shammai subordinates that concern to the protection of the food.

### Beit Hillel View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פֵּירוּרִין שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶם כְּזַיִת — מוּתָּר לְאַבְּדָן בַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** This **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Crumbs that are less than an olive-bulk** in size, **one may destroy them with his hand** without violating the prohibition against ruining food.

Beit Hillel responds that if the *shamash* is a *talmid chacham*, he will know on his own to remove any pieces of bread larger than a *kezayit* before the washing begins. *U'maniach et hapeirurin she'ein bahen kezayit* — he leaves only the small crumbs that contain less than a *kezayit*. And such crumbs, as Rabbi Yochanan ruled, may even be destroyed directly by hand, since no one intends to eat them. The text uses this to support Rabbi Yochanan's position: *peirurin she'ein bahen kezayit mutar le'abdan beyad*, that fragments below a *kezayit* do not carry the concern of *bizui ochlin*.

### Root: Am Ha'aretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? בֵּית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי אָסוּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּשַׁמָּשׁ עַם הָאָרֶץ. וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּשַׁמָּשׁ עַם הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Here too the Gemara poses the question: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: The basis of their argument is that **Beit Hillel hold: One is forbidden to use** the services of **a waiter** who is an ***am ha'aretz*.** Therefore, there is no room for concern that food will be ruined as only crumbs remain on the table. **And Beit Shammai hold: One is permitted to use** the services of **an attendant** who is an ***am ha'aretz*.** Food will remain on the table and, therefore, there is room for concern that food will be ruined. The solution is to clean the food off the table and only then wash one's hands.

The Gemara asks: *bemai kamifligi* — what is the root of their debate? The surprising answer is that the dispute turns on whether it is permitted to use an attendant who is an *am ha'aretz*. Beit Hillel holds it is forbidden to employ an *am ha'aretz* as a *shamash*, and therefore one cannot rely on him to sort the crumbs properly; hence, *mayim acharonim* must wait until the crumbs are cleared. Beit Shammai holds it is permitted to use such an attendant — but then one must worry that large pieces will remain on the table when the washing begins, making it necessary to clear the crumbs first.

This outcome is, as the Gemara acknowledges, somewhat ironic. It is Beit Shammai — not Beit Hillel — who displays the more inclusive posture toward the *am ha'aretz*.

### Ruling Beit Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּכוּלֵּיהּ פִּרְקִין הֲלָכָה כְּבֵית הִלֵּל, בַּר מֵהָא דַּהֲלָכָה כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, וְרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא מַתְנִי אִיפְּכָא, וּבְהָא נָמֵי הֲלָכָה כְּבֵית הִלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said** that **Rav Huna said: In our entire chapter, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel, except for this** case, **where the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai. And Rabbi Oshaya would teach the opposite** and reverse the opinions of Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai as they appear in our mishna, **and in this** case **as well, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Hillel.**

Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Chanina citing Rav Huna establishes: *bechol pirka halacha ke'Beit Hillel* — throughout this entire chapter, the ruling follows Beit Hillel. However, *bar meiha* — except for this case. Here, *hahalacha ke'Beit Shammai*. In the one instance where Beit Shammai's position carries the traditionally Hillel-like sensibility, Beit Shammai prevails.

### Rabbi Oshaya View

Rabbi Oshaya resolves the tension differently: he reverses the attributed positions entirely. According to his version, *Beit Hillel* is the one who permits using an *am ha'aretz* as a *shamash* and therefore requires that the crumbs be cleared first — with the result that the *halacha* again follows Beit Hillel, and the anomaly disappears.

## Havdalah Order

### Initial Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בַּר יְהוּדָה אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רָבָא. חַזְיֵיהּ לְרָבָא דְּבָרֵיךְ אַבְּשָׂמִים בְּרֵישָׁא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִכְּדִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל אַמָּאוֹר לָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּתַנְיָא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: נֵר וּמָזוֹן, בְּשָׂמִים וְהַבְדָּלָה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: נֵר וּבְשָׂמִים, מָזוֹן וְהַבְדָּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Huna bar Yehuda happened** to come **to Rava's house. He saw that Rava recited a blessing over the spices first.** Rav Huna bar Yehuda **said to him: Now since Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai do not disagree with regard to** the blessing over **light, as we learned** in our mishna that **Beit Shammai say:** One recites the blessing over the **candle,** then the Grace after **Meals** blessing, then the blessing over the **spices,** and finally the blessing of ***havdala*. And Beit Hillel say:** The order is **candle, and spices,** Grace after **Meals, and *havdala*,** why did you recite the blessing over the spices first?

The Mishnah places the question of *birkat hamazon* and *havdalah* together in a striking way — embedding the *havdalah* components within the structure of *bensching* over a *kos*, as one might do when concluding a *seuda shlishit* that runs into *motzaei Shabbos*. The Gemara's discussion of exactly how these blessings are ordered begins with an episode: Rav Huna bar Yehuda came to the house of Rava and observed that Rava recited *besamim* first — before *ner*. This puzzled him, since the Mishnah seems to indicate that both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that the blessing over light comes before *besamim*.

### Ner Comes First

Rava's response makes clear that on the question of *ner* coming before everything else, there is no dispute: *Beit Shammai ve'Beit Hillel be'maor lo peligi*. A *baraita* confirms this: Beit Shammai's order is *ner, mazon, besamim, havdalah*, and Beit Hillel's is *ner, besamim, mazon, havdalah*. Both houses begin with *ner*.

### Besamim vs Mazon

The actual question is where *birkat hamazon* and *besamim* fall relative to one another. The Gemara is thus asking Rava: why were you performing *besamim* before *ner*, when all agree *ner* comes first?

### Meir vs Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָנֵי רָבָא בָּתְרֵיהּ: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, אֲבָל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל הַמָּזוֹן שֶׁהוּא בַּתְּחִילָּה וְעַל הַבְדָּלָה שֶׁהִיא בַּסּוֹף, עַל מָה נֶחְלְקוּ — עַל הַמָּאוֹר וְעַל הַבְּשָׂמִים, שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים עַל הַמָּאוֹר וְאַחַר כָּךְ בְּשָׂמִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים בְּשָׂמִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ מָאוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava answered after him:** Indeed, **that is the statement of Rabbi Meir. However, Rabbi Yehuda says in** a *baraita* that **Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai neither disagree with regard to** Grace after **Meals that it is** recited **first, nor with regard to *havdala*, which is** recited **last. With regard to what do they disagree?** They disagree **with regard to the light and the spices. Beit Shammai say:** One recites a blessing **over light and over spices thereafter, and Beit Hillel say:** One recites a blessing **over spices and over light thereafter.**

Rava explains: the *baraita* just quoted, in which there is a three-way debate among the elements, represents the view of Rabbi Meir. But Rabbi Yehuda's position — which the Gemara had already cited on the first *amud* — is that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel never debated the placement of *birkat hamazon* at all. According to Rabbi Yehuda, everyone agrees that *birkat hamazon* comes first, and *havdalah* comes last. What they debated is solely the relative order of *ma'or* and *besamim*. It is in keeping with Rabbi Yehuda's approach that Rava was practicing — doing *besamim* first, in accordance with Beit Hillel — and Rabbi Yochanan rules: *halacha ke'Beit Hillel aliba de'Rabbi Yehuda*. Practically speaking, when combining *birkat hamazon* with *havdalah*, one begins with *birkat hamazon*, concludes with *havdalah*, and within the *havdalah* proper, *besamim* precedes *ma'or*.

The Gemara does not elaborate on the underlying reason for this specific debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel — unlike earlier disputes in the chapter, where the *gemara* probed deeply to find the root of disagreement. One might understand that *ner* carries the more overtly religious message — both as a marker that fire could not be kindled throughout *Shabbos*, and as a memorial to the first creative act of humanity, the making of fire by *Adam HaRishon* after the first *Shabbos*. Beit Shammai may therefore place *ma'or* first because of the primacy of that message. The *besamim*, by contrast, serves to restore the spirit after the departure of the *neshama yeseira*. Beit Hillel may wish to revive the person first, and only then turn to the more formal religious act.

### Shammai/Hillel Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים עַל הַמָּאוֹר וְאַחַר כָּךְ בְּשָׂמִים. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים בְּשָׂמִים וְאַחַר כָּךְ מָאוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** From the baraita: **Beit Shammai say:** One recites a blessing **over light and over spices thereafter. Beit Hillel say: Over spices and over light thereafter.**

To summarize the two positions in the *baraita*: *Beit Shammai omrim al hama'or ve'achar kach al habesamim* — first *ma'or* and then *besamim*. *Beit Hillel omrim besamim ve'achar kach hama'or* — *besamim* first, then light.

### Tadir Discussion

One student raised an interesting observation: the distinction between *ma'or* and *besamim* parallels the *tadir* principle. *Ma'or* is performed only once a week, on *motzaei Shabbos*, whereas *besamim* could potentially be encountered more frequently. Beit Hillel, who elsewhere rules that *yayin* comes before *yom* because it is *tadir*, may here be applying the same logic — *besamim*, being the more regularly performed, comes first. This is a compelling parallel for understanding Beit Hillel's position, even if it leaves Beit Shammai's reasoning to be accounted for separately.

## Bara vs Borei

### Text Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים שֶׁבָּרָא מְאוֹר הָאֵשׁ. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים בּוֹרֵא מְאוֹרֵי הָאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** From the mishna: **Beit Shammai say: Who created [*bara*] the light of fire. And Beit Hillel say: Who creates [*boreh*] the lights of fire.**

The Mishnah records that Beit Shammai formulates the blessing on fire as *she'bara ma'or ha'eish* — using the past-tense verb *bara* — while Beit Hillel says *borei me'orei ha'eish*, using the present-participial form *borei*.

### Rava Clarifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּ״בָרָא״ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבָרָא מַשְׁמַע. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּ״בוֹרֵא״, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי ״בּוֹרֵא״ דַּעֲתִיד לְמִבְרָא, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי ״בּוֹרֵא״ נָמֵי דִּבְרָא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** Regarding this, Rava says: **With regard to** the word ***bara*, everyone agrees that it means created** in the past. **Where they disagree is with regard to** the word ***boreh*. Beit Shammai hold: *Boreh*** means that God **will create in the future, and Beit Hillel hold: *Boreh* also means that He has created** in the past.

Rava observes that on the question of *bara* versus *borei*, there is actually no dispute: *be'bara kuli alma lo peligi de'bara mashma* — everyone agrees that *bara* signifies the past tense. The apparent debate over *bara* and *borei* dissolves once one recognizes that *borei*, too, can carry the sense of past creation. Rav Yosef challenges this by citing multiple biblical phrases — *yotzer or u'vorei choshech*, *yotzer harim u'vorei ruach*, *borei shamayim ve'noteihem* — all of which use *borei* in contexts that clearly encompass God's past and ongoing creative activity. One might add the grammatical observation that some scholars debate whether Biblical Hebrew truly has a present tense, and whether the participial form functions as a verb at all or rather as an attributive adjective — either way, *borei* plainly encompasses past creation. Rav Yosef's challenge therefore lands: there is nothing to dispute between the two houses regarding *bara* and *borei*.

### Singular vs Plural

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב יוֹסֵף: ״יוֹצֵר אוֹר וּבוֹרֵא חֹשֶׁךְ״, ״יוֹצֵר הָרִים וּבוֹרֵא רוּחַ״, ״בּוֹרֵא הַשָּׁמַיִם וְנוֹטֵיהֶם״. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בְּ״בָרָא״ וּ״בוֹרֵא״ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבָרָא מַשְׁמַע, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּ״מְאוֹר״ וּ״מְאוֹרֵי״. דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי חֲדָא נְהוֹרָא אִיכָּא בְּנוּרָא, וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי טוּבָא נְהוֹרֵי אִיכָּא בְּנוּרָא. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, אָמְרוּ לָהֶם בֵּית הִלֵּל לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי: הַרְבֵּה מְאוֹרוֹת יֵשׁ בָּאוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection:** How can there be a dispute over the meaning of the word *boreh*? In the following verses it is clear that it refers to acts of creation in the past: **"Who forms light and creates [*boreh*] darkness"** (Isaiah 45:7), **"Who forms mountains and creates [*boreh*] wind"** (Amos 4:13), or **"Who creates [*boreh*] the heavens and stretches them out"** (Isaiah 42:5). **Rather, said Rav Yosef: With regard to both *bara* and *boreh*, everyone agrees that** they **mean created. Where they disagree is with regard to the light** of the fire or **the lights** of the fire. **As Beit Shammai hold** that **there is one light in a fire, and Beit Hillel hold** that **there are many lights in a fire,** as a flame consists of red, green, and white light. **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai: There are many lights in the fire.**

*Ki peligi be'ma'or u'me'orei* — the real debate concerns the form of the noun. Beit Shammai says *ma'or* (singular), Beit Hillel says *me'orei* (plural). That is where the houses genuinely differ.

### Shammai/Hillel Flames

*De'Beit Shammai savri chada nehora ika be'nura* — Beit Shammai holds that there is fundamentally a single light within a flame. *U'Beit Hillel savri tuva nehora ika be'nura* — Beit Hillel holds that a flame contains many distinct lights, of different colors and intensities. A *baraita* confirms this: Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai, *harbe me'orot yesh ba'or* — there are many lights within fire, and therefore the plural *me'orei* is appropriate.

### Vilna Gaon

The Vilna Gaon offers a reading in which the two disputes — *bara* versus *borei*, and *ma'or* versus *me'orei* — are not separate but interrelated. He argues that what Beit Shammai describes when it says *bara* and *ma'or* is the *yesod ha'eish*, the ideal or essential form of fire — the Platonic archetype, as it were, which is singular and was created in the past. Beit Hillel, by contrast, describes fire as it actually exists in this world: ongoing (*borei*) and multiple (*me'orei*), because fire in reality consists of many differentiated flames. This interpretation maps neatly onto a broader pattern: Beit Shammai tends toward the idealized, heavenly conception of things, while Beit Hillel is rooted in how things actually appear in the world. It is a notable reading, though not mentioned in the Gemara itself.

## Non-Jewish Sources

### Light Lo Shavat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבִי: הָיְתָה לוֹ נֵר טְמוּנָה בְּחֵיקוֹ, אוֹ בְּפַנָּס, אוֹ שֶׁרָאָה שַׁלְהֶבֶת וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ, אוֹ נִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ וְלֹא רָאָה שַׁלְהֶבֶת — אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ עַד שֶׁיִּרְאֶה שַׁלְהֶבֶת וְיִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ לְאוֹרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *Tosefta*: **One who had a candle hidden in his lap** or placed **inside an** opaque **lamp, or if he saw a flame and did not utilize its light, or if he utilized its light and did not see a flame, may not recite a blessing until he** both **sees the flame and utilizes its light.**

The Gemara now addresses the rules governing what light and *besamim* may be used for the *havdalah* blessing. The starting point is *ein mevarchin* — one may not recite the blessing over light or *besamim* that come from a non-Jew. The case of the *ner* is somewhat understandable: *mishum de'lo shavat* — because the fire did not rest. If the light was kindled on *Shabbos*, it is in some sense the product of a violation of *Shabbos*, and the very meaning of the blessing is that one could not kindle light until now. A fire that was lit in apparent violation undermines that.

### Avodah Zarah Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָכָא בִּמְסִבַּת גּוֹיִם עָסְקִינַן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּתָם מְסִבַּת גּוֹיִם לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Here we are dealing with a party** arranged by **gentiles** and the spices used at that party were prohibited because **the parties of gentiles are generally** devoted **to idolatry.**

The case of *besamim* is less obvious. Rav Yehuda citing Rav explains: the *baraita* is speaking of fragrances that were used at a feast of non-Jews, and we presume that such a feast was conducted in honor of their gods — *de'mistama mesibat ovdei kochavim l'avodah zarah hi*. Therefore, those *besamim* are associated with *avodah zarah*.

The Gemara then raises a challenge: if the end of the Mishnah specifies *besamim shel avodah zarah* — fragrances connected to idolatrous worship — then the beginning of the Mishnah, which speaks only of *besamim* belonging to a non-Jew in general, must be addressing a different concern, one unrelated to *avodah zarah*. Otherwise, why would the Mishnah need to make the distinction at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא: ״מַה טַּעַם״ קָאָמַר: מַה טַּעַם אֵין מְבָרְכִין עַל הַנֵּר וְלֹא עַל הַבְּשָׂמִים שֶׁל גּוֹיִם — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁסְּתָם מְסִבַּת גּוֹיִם לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina of Sura said:** These two *halakhot* are complementary, and the mishna **states** the *halakha* employing the style of: **What is the reason.** The mishna should be understood as follows: **What is the reason that one may neither recite a blessing over the candle nor over the spices of gentiles? Because the parties of gentiles are generally** devoted **to idolatry** and one may neither recite a blessing over the candle nor over the spices of idolatry.

Rav Chanina of Sura resolves this: the end of the Mishnah is not adding a new case but is explaining the beginning. *Mah ta'am ein mevarchin lo al haner velo al habesamim shel akum* — why may one not use the *ner* or *besamim* of a non-Jew? The answer is *mipnei she'stam mesibat ovdei kochavim l'avodah zarah* — because we assume that these items came from a feast that was dedicated to idol worship. The concern for *avodah zarah* is thus the explanation for the general restriction on non-Jewish *besamim*, while the distinct *lo shavat* concern governs the *ner*.

### Definition Shavat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוּר שֶׁשָּׁבַת — מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו, וְשֶׁלֹּא שָׁבַת — אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו. מַאי ״שָׁבַת״ וּמַאי ״לֹא שָׁבַת״?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Over fire that rested, one may recite a blessing** in *havdala*, **and over** fire **that did not rest, one may not recite a blessing.** The Gemara asks: **What is** meant by **rested, and what is** meant by **did not rest?**

A *baraita* clarifies the concept of *shavat* and *lo shavat*: *or she'shavat mevarchin alav* — if the light rested, one may bless upon it; *ve'shelo shavat ein mevarchin alav* — if it did not rest, one may not. But what do these terms mean?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נֵימָא לֹא שָׁבַת מֵחֲמַת מְלָאכָה אֲפִילּוּ מִמְּלָאכָה דְּהֶתֵּירָא, וְהָתַנְיָא: אוּר שֶׁל חַיָּה וְשֶׁל חוֹלֶה — מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** that did not rest means that **it did not rest from labor, even from labor that is permitted? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **over light** that was kindled on Shabbat **for a woman giving birth or a** dangerously **ill person,** for whom one is permitted to perform prohibited labor on Shabbat, **one may recite a blessing** during *havdala* at the conclusion of Shabbat?

If one were to say that *lo shavat* means the light was produced through a *melacha* — a forbidden act — then a light kindled for a midwife or a dangerously ill patient on *Shabbos* should be prohibited, since it was kindled on *Shabbos*. Yet a *baraita* rules explicitly that *or shel chaya ve'shel choleh mevarchin alav* — one may bless on a light that was kindled for such purposes on *Shabbos*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak therefore explains: *shavat machamat aveira* — the relevant concept is not whether the light was kindled on *Shabbos* per se, but whether it was kindled in a manner that constitutes a violation. A light kindled for *pikuach nefesh* was kindled in a fully sanctioned manner and is therefore considered to have "rested." A light kindled without such license, even by a non-Jew who is not commanded, carries the conceptual taint of Shabbos violation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי ״שָׁבַת״ — שֶׁשָׁבַת מֵחֲמַת מְלֶאכֶת עֲבֵירָה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: עֲשָׁשִׁית שֶׁהָיְתָה דּוֹלֶקֶת וְהוֹלֶכֶת כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת מְבָרְכִין עָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: What is** meant by **rested?** Light **that rested from labor of transgression** on Shabbat. However, if the light burned for the entire Shabbat or was kindled on Shabbat in a permissible manner, one may recite a blessing over it. **That** *halakha* **was also taught** in a *baraita*: **A lantern that was continuously burning throughout the entire day** of Shabbat, **one may recite a blessing over it at the conclusion of Shabbat.**

A further *baraita* reinforces this: a lantern burning continuously throughout the entire day of *Shabbos* may be blessed over at *motzaei Shabbos*. Even though it was burning on *Shabbos*, it was kindled before *Shabbos* and never violated it.

This leads to a parenthetical but practically significant observation. On *Yom Kippur*, the *halacha* requires that the blessing *borei me'orei ha'eish* be recited only over a *ner she'shavat* — a flame that was burning before *Yom Kippur* and remained lit throughout. One might think the opposite: if the purpose of the blessing is to acknowledge that fire cannot be kindled on *Yom Kippur*, the most dramatic demonstration would be to light a new flame the moment *Yom Kippur* ends. Yet the *halacha* requires instead a flame that persisted throughout — a point that has puzzled many, and which does not receive a clear resolution here.

### Nochri Mi'Nochri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גּוֹי שֶׁהִדְלִיק מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהִדְלִיק מִגּוֹי מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו. גּוֹי מִגּוֹי — אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A gentile who lit** a candle **from** a candle that was in the possession of **a Jew or if a Jew lit** a candle **from a gentile, one may recite a blessing over it** at the conclusion of Shabbat. However, **if a gentile** lit a candle **from a gentile, one may not recite a blessing over it.**

The Gemara continues with a *baraita*: *nochri she'hidlik ner le'Yisrael ve'Yisrael she'hidlik mi'nochri mevarchin alav* — whether a non-Jew lit a candle for a Jew, or a Jew lit from a non-Jew, one may bless upon it. *Nochri mi'nochri ein mevarchin alav* — but if a non-Jew lit from another non-Jew, one may not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא גּוֹי מִגּוֹי דְּלָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּלֹא שָׁבַת? אִי הָכִי, יִשְׂרָאֵל מִגּוֹי נָמֵי הָא לֹא שָׁבַת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about a candle that **a gentile** lit **from a gentile, that** one may **not** recite a blessing over it? **Because** the light **did not rest** on Shabbat. **If so,** the light of **a Jew** who lit a candle **from a gentile also did not rest** on Shabbat.

The Gemara probes this carefully. Why may one not use a non-Jew's flame taken from another non-Jew? Because *mishum de'lo shavat* — we presume it traces back to a fire that did not rest. But if that is the logic, then a Jew who takes fire from a non-Jew should face the same problem, since the original flame also did not rest. The Gemara offers the reasoning that when a Jew kindles from the non-Jew's flame, what was forbidden continues in the original flame while something new — *atosfes de'heteira* — is added in the Jewish kindling; it is upon that new portion that the blessing is recited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הַךְ אִיסּוּרָא אֲזַל לֵיהּ, וְהָא אַחֲרִינָא הוּא, וּבִידָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל קָא מִתְיַלְדָא — אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: הַמּוֹצִיא שַׁלְהֶבֶת לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — חַיָּיב. אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? מַה שֶּׁעָקַר לֹא הִנִּיחַ, וּמַה שֶּׁהִנִּיחַ לֹא עָקַר!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **this prohibited** flame **has gone and this** flame **is a** new and **different** one which **came into being in the possession of a Jew,** as a flame is not a concrete, static object, but rather it constantly recreates itself; **however, this** *halakha* **that was taught** in a *Tosefta* in tractate *Shabbat* states: **One who carries out a flame** from the private **to the public domain** on Shabbat **is liable** for carrying out from one domain to another. If the flame is constantly recreating itself, **why is he liable? That** flame **which he lifted** from the private domain **he did not place** in the public domain **and that which he placed he did not lift.** One is only liable for carrying out on Shabbat if he lifted an object from one domain and placed that same object in another domain. Since one who carries out a flame on Shabbat is considered liable, evidently, despite any change that it may undergo, the flame is essentially considered a single entity.

But then the challenge returns: if fire is continuously self-renewing — if *mah she'akar lo hiniach u'mah she'hiniach lo akar*, what one picks up is not what one puts down — why should *nochri mi'nochri* be prohibited at all? Each moment the flame is new. The Gemara resolves this by invoking the *halacha* that one who carries a flame to *reshut harabbim* on *Shabbos* is *chayav* for the flame itself, even when there is not enough of the underlying material to constitute a prohibited transfer — proof that legally the flame maintains its identity across time. The flame is not a perpetually new entity; it is the same object.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיסּוּרָא נָמֵי אִיתֵיהּ, וְכִי קָא מְבָרֵךְ — אַתּוֹסֶפְתָּא דְּהֶתֵּירָא קָא מְבָרֵךְ, אִי הָכִי, גּוֹי מִגּוֹי נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, actually that prohibited** flame **is also extant, and when one recites the blessing, he recites the blessing over the permitted addition** to that flame. The Gemara asks: **If so,** even if **a gentile** lit a candle **from a gentile as well,** the flame should be considered essentially new; one should be able to recite a blessing over the addition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין הָכִי נָמֵי, גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם גּוֹי רִאשׁוֹן וְעַמּוּד רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes, it is indeed so.** Fundamentally, there is no reason to prohibit doing so. However, the Sages issued **a decree because of the first gentile,** who did not light the flame from another gentile, **and the first pillar** of flame that was kindled on Shabbat. Consequently, they prohibited all somewhat similar cases, including when a gentile lights a flame from another gentile.

The *maskana* of the Gemara — as Rashi and Tosafos understand it — is that technically, even a flame kindled on *Shabbos* becomes permissible after some time has passed, since new fire has been added to the old. The entire prohibition in the case of *nochri mi'nochri* is a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic safeguard — enacted because of the one specific problematic case: when the non-Jew lit it shortly before *Shabbos* ended, and the very original flame is still burning. To avoid requiring people to make such fine distinctions, the Rabbis prohibited any flame in which the chain of transmission runs entirely through non-Jews, and permitted only when there is Jewish involvement at some point in the chain.

## Fire Use Rules

### Fire Outside City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ חוּץ לַכְּרַךְ וְרָאָה אוּר, אִם רוֹב גּוֹיִם — אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ, אִם רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל — מְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **If one was walking outside the city, saw fire** there, and wanted to recite the blessing over it as part of *havdala*, **if** the city has a **majority of gentiles he may not recite the blessing** over the fire, but **if** the city has a **majority of Jews, he may recite the blessing.**

A *baraita* addresses one walking outside a city who sees a flame: *im rov nochrim eino mevarech* — if the majority of the city's residents are non-Jews, one does not recite the blessing; *im rov Yisrael mevarech* — if the majority are Jews, one may. The Gemara immediately notes an internal contradiction: if a majority of non-Jews means one may not bless, then a fifty-fifty split should be sufficient to bless. Yet the second clause implies a majority of Jews is required. The Gemara resolves this by clarifying that even *mechtza al mechtza* — an equal split — is sufficient. The clauses of the *baraita* were phrased as parallel contrasts (*rov nochrim* / *rov Yisrael*) for stylistic symmetry, but the actual ruling is that fifty-fifty suffices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא, אָמְרַתְּ אִם רוֹב גּוֹיִם — אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ, הָא מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה — מְבָרֵךְ, וַהֲדַר תָּנֵי אִם רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל — מְבָרֵךְ, הָא מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה — אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **The matter itself is difficult** in this *baraita*. **You said** in the *baraita* that if the town has a **majority of gentiles he may not recite the blessing.** By inference, if the town population was **half** gentiles **and half** Jews, **one may recite a blessing. And then you teach** that **if** the town has a **majority of Jews, he may recite the blessing.** By inference, if the town population was **half** gentiles **and half** Jews, **one may not recite a blessing.** The inferences from two sections of the *baraita* are contradictory.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּדִין הוּא דַּאֲפִילּוּ מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה נָמֵי מְבָרֵךְ, וְאַיְּידִי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא ״רוֹב גּוֹיִם״, תְּנָא סֵיפָא ״רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **By right,** the *baraita* should have taught that **even** if the town population was **half** gentiles **and half** Jews, **one may recite a blessing,** but **since in the first clause it taught: The majority of gentiles, in the latter clause it** used the same expression and **taught: The majority of Jews.**

### Infant Inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ חוּץ לַכְּרַךְ וְרָאָה תִּינוֹק וַאֲבוּקָה בְּיָדוֹ, בּוֹדֵק אַחֲרָיו; אִם יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא — מְבָרֵךְ, אִם נָכְרִי הוּא — אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** And **the Sages taught: One who was walking outside the city** at the conclusion of Shabbat **and saw a child with a torch in his hand, he** must **check after his** background. **If** the child **is a Jew, he may recite a blessing** over this flame, **but if** the child **is a gentile, he may not recite a blessing** over it.

A *baraita* presents a further case: one who was walking outside the city and saw a child holding a torch. *Bodek achrav* — one inquires about the child. If found to be Jewish, one recites the blessing; if not Jewish, one does not. The Gemara asks: why does the *baraita* specify a child? Even an adult presents the same question. Rav Yehuda citing Rav explains that the scenario is *besamuch li'shki'at hachama* — immediately after nightfall. In that context, an adult found carrying a newly lit torch would almost certainly be a non-Jew, since a Jew would not have had occasion to kindle a new flame so quickly after *Shabbos*. A child, however, might have simply grabbed a flame that was already burning — *im Yisrael hu ikri ve'nakit* — and thus the inquiry is necessary. The reasoning here is somewhat difficult to follow, and the Gemara does not resolve the apparent tension fully.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא תִּינוֹק? אֲפִילּוּ גָּדוֹל נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why was it taught** specifically with regard to **a child? Even if he were an adult,** one would **also** need to investigate whether he was a Jew or a gentile in order to determine whether or not he may recite a blessing over the torch.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָכָא בְּסָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה עָסְקִינַן. גָּדוֹל, מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא דְּוַדַּאי נָכְרִי הוּא. תִּינוֹק, אֵימַר יִשְׂרָאֵל הוּא, אִקְּרִי וּנְקֵיט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Here we are dealing with** a case where, although it was the conclusion of Shabbat, it was still **soon after sunset.** Therefore, in the case of **an adult, it is self-evident that he is a gentile,** as a Jew would not be so quick to take fire in his hand immediately after Shabbat. In the case of **a child,** however, **say that** perhaps **he is a Jew** and **it happened that he took** the torch.

### Flame Thickness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ חוּץ לַכְּרַךְ וְרָאָה אוּר, אִם עָבֶה כְּפִי הַכִּבְשָׁן — מְבָרֵךְ עָלָיו. וְאִם לָאו אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** And **the Sages taught: One who was walking outside the city** at the conclusion of Shabbat **and saw a fire, if** the fire **is** at least **as thick as the opening of a furnace, one may recite a blessing over it,** as a fire of that kind is kindled for the light it produces as well. **And if** it is **not** at least that thick, **one may not recite a blessing over it.**

A *baraita* rules that if one was outside the city and saw a flame, one may bless upon it only *im ava kefi kivshan* — if it is as thick as the opening of a furnace. If the flame is thinner, one may not bless. This introduces the principle that the blessing *borei me'orei ha'eish* is restricted to a flame serving the purpose of providing light.

### Non-Light Purposes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אוּר שֶׁל כִּבְשָׁן מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in **one** *baraita*: During *havdala*, **one may recite a blessing over the fire of a furnace; and it was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **One may not recite a blessing over** the fire of a furnace. There is an apparent contradiction between the *baraitot*.

The Gemara explores what it means for a fire to be lit for the purpose of light through a series of apparent contradictions. One *baraita* says one may bless upon the fire of a kiln; another says one may not. The resolution: at the beginning of the kiln's operation, the fire is entirely for the purpose of smelting, not for illumination; but at the end, when material may be added to make the fire bright enough to work by, it serves the additional purpose of providing light, and the blessing may be recited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בַּתְּחִלָּה, הָא לְבַסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult,** as **this** *baraita* which prohibits reciting the blessing is speaking **at the beginning** when the furnace was just kindled and the fire is designated solely to heat the objects in the furnace; **that** *baraita,* which permits reciting the blessing, is speaking **at the end,** when the fire is no longer needed to heat the objects in the furnace, and its light is used for other purposes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אוּר שֶׁל תַּנּוּר וְשֶׁל כִּירַיִם מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar contradiction between *baraitot*: **It was taught** in **one** *baraita*: During *havdala*, **one may recite a blessing over the fire of an oven or a stove; and it was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **One may not recite a blessing over it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בַּתְּחִלָּה, הָא לְבַסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult,** as a similar distinction between the *baraitot* may be suggested. **This** *baraita,* which prohibits reciting the blessing, is speaking **at the beginning,** when the oven or stove was just kindled and the fire is designated solely to heat the objects on the stove or in the oven; **that** *baraita,* which permits reciting the blessing, is speaking **at the end,** when the fire is no longer needed to heat the objects on the stove or in the oven and its light is used for other purposes.

The same structure applies to the fire of an oven or stove: at the beginning it is for cooking; at the end, if tended for illumination as well, it qualifies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: אוּר שֶׁל בֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְשֶׁל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another contradiction: **It was taught** in **one** *baraita*: During *havdala*, **one may recite a blessing over the light of a synagogue or a study hall; and it was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **One may not recite a blessing over it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִיכָּא אָדָם חָשׁוּב, הָא דְּלֵיכָּא אָדָם חָשׁוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult,** as **this** *baraita,* which prohibits reciting the blessing, is speaking in a case **where there is an important person** in the synagogue and the fire is kindled in his honor and not to provide light; **that** *baraita,* which permits reciting the blessing, is speaking in a case **where there is no important person** present and the fire is kindled to provide light.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא הָא וְהָא דְּאִיכָּא אָדָם חָשׁוּב, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִיכָּא חַזָּנָא, הָא דְּלֵיכָּא חַזָּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **this** *baraita* and **that** *baraita* are speaking in a case **where there is an important person** present in the synagogue, **and** this is **not difficult** because the contradiction can be resolved as follows: **This** *baraita,* which permits reciting the blessing, is speaking in a case **where there is a caretaker** in the synagogue who uses the light; **that** *baraita,* which prohibits reciting the blessing, is speaking in a case **where there is no caretaker** and the light is kindled for purposes of honor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא הָא וְהָא דְּאִיכָּא חַזָּנָא, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִיכָּא סֵהֲרָא, וְהָא דְּלֵיכָּא סֵהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are both referring to a case **where there is a caretaker** present in the synagogue, **and** this is **not difficult** because the contradiction can be resolved as follows: **This** *baraita,* which prohibits reciting the blessing, is speaking in a case **where there is moonlight,** so the caretaker did not light the fire to provide light as the moonlight is sufficient; **that** *baraita,* which permits reciting the blessing, is speaking in a case **where there is no moonlight,** and the caretaker lights the fire to provide light.

The case of the *beit knesset* and *beit midrash* generates a different kind of ambiguity. One *baraita* permits the blessing on such a fire; another prohibits it. The first resolution: if there is an *adam chashuv* present, Rashi holds that the lights are kindled for his honor — *lichvod* — not for illumination, and one may not bless. Tosafos reads the case inversely: ordinarily the lights in a synagogue or study hall are for honor, but the presence of an important person might prompt the addition of extra light for him to see by. Either way, when the lights serve primarily the function of honor, the blessing may not be recited.

An alternative resolution introduces two further variables: whether there is a *chazan* — here meaning a sexton who resides in the building and needs the light — and whether there is moonlight outside. If a *chazan* is present, the light may be understood as serving a practical function of illumination; if moonlight is available, the indoor lights serve only honor. The *poskim* of later generations applied these principles to candles in synagogues in their own time, and there was genuine debate about whether such candles were fundamentally for *kavod* or for light.

## Individual vs Communal

### Shammai Individual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְהֵבִיאוּ אוּר לִפְנֵיהֶם, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **People were seated in the study hall and they brought fire before them** at the conclusion of Shabbat. **Beit Shammai say: Each and every individual recites a blessing for himself.**

The Gemara turns to the case of people seated together in the *beit midrash* on *motzaei Shabbos* when a flame is brought before them. Beit Shammai rules: *kol echad ve'echad mevarech le'atzmo* — each person recites the blessing individually.

### Hillel Communal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד מְבָרֵךְ לְכוּלָּן — מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּרׇב עָם הַדְרַת מֶלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say: One recites a blessing on behalf of everyone** and the others answer amen. Beit Hillel's reasoning is **as it is stated: "The splendor of the King is in the multitude of the people"** (Proverbs 14:28). When everyone joins together to hear the blessing, the name of God is glorified.

Beit Hillel rules: *echad mevarech le'kulam* — one person recites the blessing on behalf of all, *mishum she'ne'emar be'rov am hadrat melekh* — as it is written, "in the multitude of the people is the glory of the King." It is better to perform the blessing collectively.

### Bittal Beit Midrash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּית הִלֵּל מְפָרְשִׁי טַעְמָא, אֶלָּא בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מַאי טַעְמָא? קָסָבְרִי מִפְּנֵי בִּיטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted, Beit Hillel,** they **explain** their **reasoning, but what is the reason** for the opinion **of Beit Shammai** to prohibit reciting the blessing communally? The Gemara answers: **They hold** that it is prohibited **due to** the fact that it will lead to **suspension of** study in **the study hall.** Waiting for someone to recite the blessing will interrupt Torah study for several minutes.

The Gemara finds Beit Hillel's reasoning self-evident. Beit Shammai's position requires explanation. The answer: *mishum bittul beit ha'midrash* — if everyone must stop, gather attention, and listen together, more time is wasted from Torah study than if each individual recites the brief blessing on his own schedule without interruption. A supporting *baraita* notes that in the household of Rabban Gamliel they did not say the equivalent of "gesundheit" when someone sneezed in the *beit midrash* — *mipnei bittul beit ha'midrash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לֹא הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים ״מַרְפֵּא״ בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, מִפְּנֵי בִּיטּוּל בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **This** concern for disrupting Torah study **was also taught** in a *baraita*: The members of **the house of Rabban Gamliel would not say good health** when someone sneezed **in the study hall, due to** the fact that it would lead to **suspension of** study in **the study hall.**

This passage also touches on the broader question of *rov am* versus individual recitation that arises in other contexts — including *kiddush* and *havdalah* — and the related issue of whether it is *gadol ha'oneh* or *gadol ha'mevarech* that is preferred. Those discussions are not resolved here; the only consideration the Gemara raises in this context is *bittul beit ha'midrash*.

## Light/Spices Dead/Bad

### Dead Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְבָרְכִין לֹא עַל הַנֵּר וְלֹא עַל הַבְּשָׂמִים שֶׁל מֵתִים. מַאי טַעְמָא? נֵר — לְכָבוֹד הוּא דַּעֲבִידָא, בְּשָׂמִים לְעַבּוֹרֵי רֵיחָא הוּא דַּעֲבִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One may neither recite a blessing over the candle nor over the spices** designated to honor **the dead.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason?** Because **a candle** of the dead **is kindled for** the purpose of **honoring** the dead, not for light; **the spices** are **to neutralize the** bad **odor,** not for their pleasant fragrance.

The Mishnah rules: *ein mevarchin lo al ha'ner velo al ha'besamim shel meitim* — one may not recite a blessing over a light or *besamim* that were used for the dead.

### Honor vs Light

The Gemara explains: *ner lichvod hu de'avida* — when a candle is placed in a funeral procession or beside a corpse, it is not there to provide light but to provide honor to the deceased. Since the blessing on fire is contingent on the fire serving the function of illumination, such a flame does not qualify.

For *besamim*, the principle established earlier applies: the blessing on fragrances requires that they be present *lereicha* — for the purpose of providing a pleasant aroma to those present. The *besamim* placed near a corpse are there to mask the odor of death, not to provide a positive fragrance for smelling. That purpose does not satisfy the requirement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כֹּל שֶׁמּוֹצִיאִין לְפָנָיו בַּיּוֹם וּבַלַּיְלָה — אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו. וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין מוֹצִיאִין לְפָנָיו אֶלָּא בַּלַּיְלָה — מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** And **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Any** deceased **before whom** a candle **is taken out** both **by day and by night,** it is evident that the candle is for the purpose of honoring the deceased; therefore, **one may not recite a blessing over it. And any** deceased **before whom** a candle **is taken out only by night,** it is evident that the purpose of the candle is for its light alone, and **one may recite a blessing over it.**

Rav Yehuda citing Rav adds a further distinction: for a *meis* of sufficient importance that candles are carried before the bier both by day and by night, the candles are clearly for *kavod*, and one may not bless upon them. For a *meis* before whom candles are carried only at night — that is, primarily for light — one may bless. The Gemara notes that in any case, the concern of *hana'ah mi'meis* — deriving benefit from the dead — does not extend to such a candle; the connection is too attenuated to trigger that prohibition.

### Bad Smell Masking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּשָׂמִים שֶׁל בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא, וְשֶׁמֶן הֶעָשׂוּי לְהַעֲבִיר אֶת הַזּוּהֲמָא — אֵין מְבָרְכִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Huna said: Over spices** used to deodorize **the bathroom and** fragrant **oil** intended **to remove filth, one may not recite a blessing** as they are not used for their pleasant fragrance.

Rav Huna extends the principle: *besamim shel beit ha'kisei* — fragrances placed in a bathroom to eliminate unpleasant odors — and *shemen ha'asui le'haavir et ha'zuhama* — oil applied to one's hands to remove filth after a meal — do not qualify for a blessing. In both cases, the fragrance or oil serves not to provide pleasant smell as an end in itself, but to eliminate something unpleasant. The ruling applies regardless of location: if the fundamental purpose of the fragrance is odor elimination rather than the positive experience of *reiach*, no blessing is recited. This principle applies even outside the bathroom — for example, household air fresheners whose function is to mask bad smells.

## Spice Blessings

### Perfume Store

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּכֹל הֵיכָא דְּלָאו לְרֵיחָא עֲבִידָא לָא מְבָרְכִין עִלָּוֵיהּ? מֵתִיבִי: הַנִּכְנָס לַחֲנוּתוֹ שֶׁל בַּשָּׂם וְהֵרִיחַ רֵיחַ, אֲפִילּוּ יָשַׁב שָׁם כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ אֶלָּא פַּעַם אַחַת. נִכְנַס וְיָצָא, נִכְנַס וְיָצָא — מְבָרֵךְ עַל כׇּל פַּעַם וּפַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on the *Tosefta*: **One who enters the store of a perfumer, and smelled a fragrance, even if he sat there throughout the entire day, he only recites a blessing once.** However, **if one entered and exited, entered and exited, he recites a blessing on each and every occasion.**

The Gemara introduces the case of one who enters a perfume dealer's shop and smells the fragrances. *Afilu yashav sham kol hayom kulo eino mevarech ela paam achas* — even if one sits there the entire day, one recites the blessing only once. But *nichnas ve'yatza nichnas ve'yatza, mevarech al kol paam va'faam* — if one enters and exits repeatedly, one blesses each time. The question of what constitutes a distinct act of smelling for purposes of the blessing is relevant here, and the *poskim* also debate whether an inadvertent smell — one not consciously intended — requires a blessing at all. If *birkat hareiach* is a *birkat hanehenin*, then even unintended benefit would seem to require a blessing; if it is more in the nature of a *birkat hashevach*, the analysis may differ. The prevailing practice treats it more as a *birkat hanehenin*, given that one recites the blessing before smelling.

### Store Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא דְּלָאו לְרֵיחָא הוּא דַּעֲבִידָא, וְקָמְבָרֵךְ!
>
> **Translation:** Isn't** it a case **here, where** the spices **are not intended for fragrance,** as they are not used to improve the scent in the store, **and,** nevertheless, **one recites a blessing?**

The Gemara raises a challenge: in the perfume shop, the fragrances are arguably not there *lereicha* — not for the purpose of being smelled. If that is so, how can one recite a blessing upon them? This is puzzling, since a perfume store is self-evidently a place of fragrance. The Gemara answers that the fragrances in the store are indeed made for smelling — *heicha de'nirchi inshi ve'nisvei ve'nizbenu minei* — the whole purpose is that people should smell and be attracted to come in and purchase. The good smell in the store is intentional advertising. The *hava amina* is not fully transparent, but the Gemara's conclusion is that the fragrances in a perfume shop do qualify as *lereicha avida*, and the blessing is in order.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, לְרֵיחָא נָמֵי הוּא דַּעֲבִידָא, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנֵירְחוּ אִינָשֵׁי וְנֵיתוּ וְנִזְבּוֹן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Yes,** in this case the spices **are also intended for fragrance;** they are used to generate a scent in the store **so that people will smell** them **and come and purchase from him.**

A practical question arises from this: when entering a spice shop — such as a commercial spice store — does one recite a blessing? The fragrances there may be incidental to the purpose of purchasing packaged spices rather than intentionally provided for smelling. If one intentionally pauses to smell a pleasing spice, however, one intends to smell and the blessing would seem appropriate. The practical advice: once one begins to enter and the smell begins to reach one, recite the blessing at that moment and then smell deliberately.

### Outdoor Smell

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ חוּץ לַכְּרַךְ וְהֵרִיחַ רֵיחַ, אִם רוֹב גּוֹיִם — אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ, אִם רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל — מְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who was walking outside a city and smelled a scent; if the majority** of the town's residents **are gentiles he may not recite a blessing** over the scent, but **if the majority are Jews, he may recite a blessing.**

A *baraita* addresses one walking outside a city who smells a pleasant fragrance from an unknown source. *Im rov ovdei kochavim eino mevarech* — if most of the population is non-Jewish, one does not bless. *Im rov Yisrael mevarech* — if most are Jewish, one may bless.

### Rabbi Yosi Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ רוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל — נָמֵי אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל מְקַטְּרוֹת לִכְשָׁפִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even if the majority are Jews, one may not recite a blessing, as the daughters of Israel burn incense to witchcraft** and the spices were certainly made for witchcraft, not for their fragrance.

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *afilu rov Yisrael nami eino mevarech* — even a majority of Jews is insufficient. The reason is *mipnei she'benot Yisrael mekatrot lichshafim* — Jewish women sometimes burn incense for purposes of witchcraft. The Gemara pushes back: surely the majority do not practice witchcraft. Rabbi Yosi's concern is more nuanced. There are in fact two minority uses of fragrant burning that disqualify: some burn incense for witchcraft, and some burn incense *legamurei et ha'kelim* — to infuse clothing with scent. This latter purpose is explicitly not *lereicha* in the relevant sense, since the fragrance is not intended to be smelled by people but to be absorbed into fabric. Together, these two minority uses are sufficient, in Rabbi Yosi's view, to make the majority of outdoor fragrances in a Jewish town unreliable for purposes of the blessing — *ve'chol ruba de'lav lereicha avida lo mevorchinan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ כּוּלְּהוּ לִכְשָׁפִים מְקַטְּרָן?! — הָוֵה לַהּ מִיעוּטָא לִכְשָׁפִים, וּמִיעוּטָא נָמֵי לְגַמֵּר אֶת הַכֵּלִים. אִשְׁתְּכַח רוּבָּא דְּלָאו לְרֵיחָא עֲבִיד, וְכׇל רוּבָּא דְּלָאו לְרֵיחָא עֲבִיד לָא מְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that they all burn incense to witchcraft?** Rather, **there is a minority** of people who burn incense **to witchcraft, and a** different **minority** who burn spices in order **to perfume** their **garments with incense. A majority,** therefore, exists that **does not use it for fragrance, and in a case where the majority does not use it for fragrance, one does not recite a blessing.**

### Regional Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַמְהַלֵּךְ בְּעַרְבֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת בִּטְבֶרְיָא וּבְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּתוֹת בְּצִפּוֹרִי וְהֵרִיחַ רֵיחַ, אֵינוֹ מְבָרֵךְ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֶזְקָתוֹ אֵינוֹ עָשׂוּי אֶלָּא לְגַמֵּר בּוֹ אֶת הַכֵּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who walks on Shabbat eve in Tiberias** or **at the conclusion of Shabbat in Tzippori, and smelled the scent** of incense **may not recite a blessing, as the presumption is** that **it was intended to perfume garments.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan gives a concrete regional illustration: one who walks in Teveria on *erev Shabbos* or in Tzippori on *motzaei Shabbos* and smells fragrance should not bless, *mipnei she'chazkatah eino asui ela legamurei et ha'kelim* — because in those places it was standard practice to use incense or fragrances for scenting clothing. In Teveria the custom was to do this *erev Shabbos*, and in Tzippori on *motzaei Shabbos*. The fragrance one smells in those places at those times is presumed to be for fabric-scenting, not for the nose.

### Akum Marketplace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּשּׁוּק שֶׁל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה נִתְרַצָּה לְהָרִיחַ — הֲרֵי זֶה חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** On a related note, the Gemara cites the following: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who was walking in the marketplace of idolators and willingly smelled** the incense wafting there, **he is a sinner,** as he should not have the intention to smell it.

A final *baraita* states: if one is walking through a non-Jewish marketplace and deliberately desires to smell the fragrances there — *nitretzeh lehareich, harei zeh chotei* — this person has sinned. Given the *avodah zarah* concerns associated with such a marketplace, the intentional act of smelling — *nitretzeh* — is the critical factor. A related discussion in Masechet *Pesachim* explores the broader question of how intentionality defines a halachic act of smelling, and how to understand situations where one smells inadvertently. Here the Gemara makes clear that *nitretzeh* is what activates the *issur*.

## Benefit Threshold

### Benefit Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְבָרְכִין עַל הַנֵּר עַד שֶׁיֵּאוֹתוּ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And one does not recite the blessing over the candle until he derives benefit** from its light.

The Gemara closes with a principle that applies to the blessing on light: *ve'ein mevarchin al ha'ner ad she'yei'otu* — one does not recite the blessing on *ner* until one actually derives benefit from it. The discussion of this rule, and its practical and conceptual implications, is where the shiur concludes.
