# Berakhot 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Crumbs vs. Hand-Washing

### Basic Dispute

The Gemara resumes with the *baraita* on *mayim acharonim*: does one first clear the crumbs from the table, or first wash one's hands? The *baraita* presents the positions of both houses: *tanu rabbanan, Beit Shammai omrim mechabdin et habayit ve'achar kach notlin layadayim* — Beit Shammai holds that one first sweeps away the crumbs and then performs *mayim acharonim*, while Beit Hillel reverses the order.

### Beit Shammai View

The reason Beit Hillel prefers *mayim acharonim* first is that if one washes first, the water will spill onto the crumbs and ruin food one might wish to eat later. Beit Shammai does not insist that hand-washing come first; rather, their primary concern is that the crumbs be cleared before any water is introduced. There may also be an argument that *mayim acharonim* should ideally follow immediately upon finishing the meal — particularly given its function of removing filth, odor, and the *melach Sedomit* — but Beit Shammai subordinates that concern to the protection of the food.

### Beit Hillel View

Beit Hillel responds that if the *shamash* is a *talmid chacham*, he will know on his own to remove any pieces of bread larger than a *kezayit* before the washing begins. *U'maniach et hapeirurin she'ein bahen kezayit* — he leaves only the small crumbs that contain less than a *kezayit*. And such crumbs, as Rabbi Yochanan ruled, may even be destroyed directly by hand, since no one intends to eat them. The text uses this to support Rabbi Yochanan's position: *peirurin she'ein bahen kezayit mutar le'abdan beyad*, that fragments below a *kezayit* do not carry the concern of *bizui ochlin*.

### Root: Am Ha'aretz

The Gemara asks: *bemai kamifligi* — what is the root of their debate? The surprising answer is that the dispute turns on whether it is permitted to use an attendant who is an *am ha'aretz*. Beit Hillel holds it is forbidden to employ an *am ha'aretz* as a *shamash*, and therefore one cannot rely on him to sort the crumbs properly; hence, *mayim acharonim* must wait until the crumbs are cleared. Beit Shammai holds it is permitted to use such an attendant — but then one must worry that large pieces will remain on the table when the washing begins, making it necessary to clear the crumbs first.

This outcome is, as the Gemara acknowledges, somewhat ironic. It is Beit Shammai — not Beit Hillel — who displays the more inclusive posture toward the *am ha'aretz*.

### Ruling Beit Shammai

Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Chanina citing Rav Huna establishes: *bechol pirka halacha ke'Beit Hillel* — throughout this entire chapter, the ruling follows Beit Hillel. However, *bar meiha* — except for this case. Here, *hahalacha ke'Beit Shammai*. In the one instance where Beit Shammai's position carries the traditionally Hillel-like sensibility, Beit Shammai prevails.

### Rabbi Oshaya View

Rabbi Oshaya resolves the tension differently: he reverses the attributed positions entirely. According to his version, *Beit Hillel* is the one who permits using an *am ha'aretz* as a *shamash* and therefore requires that the crumbs be cleared first — with the result that the *halacha* again follows Beit Hillel, and the anomaly disappears.

## Havdalah Order

### Initial Problem

The Mishnah places the question of *birkat hamazon* and *havdalah* together in a striking way — embedding the *havdalah* components within the structure of *bensching* over a *kos*, as one might do when concluding a *seuda shlishit* that runs into *motzaei Shabbos*. The Gemara's discussion of exactly how these blessings are ordered begins with an episode: Rav Huna bar Yehuda came to the house of Rava and observed that Rava recited *besamim* first — before *ner*. This puzzled him, since the Mishnah seems to indicate that both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that the blessing over light comes before *besamim*.

### Ner Comes First

Rava's response makes clear that on the question of *ner* coming before everything else, there is no dispute: *Beit Shammai ve'Beit Hillel be'maor lo peligi*. A *baraita* confirms this: Beit Shammai's order is *ner, mazon, besamim, havdalah*, and Beit Hillel's is *ner, besamim, mazon, havdalah*. Both houses begin with *ner*.

### Besamim vs Mazon

The actual question is where *birkat hamazon* and *besamim* fall relative to one another. The Gemara is thus asking Rava: why were you performing *besamim* before *ner*, when all agree *ner* comes first?

### Meir vs Yehuda

Rava explains: the *baraita* just quoted, in which there is a three-way debate among the elements, represents the view of Rabbi Meir. But Rabbi Yehuda's position — which the Gemara had already cited on the first *amud* — is that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel never debated the placement of *birkat hamazon* at all. According to Rabbi Yehuda, everyone agrees that *birkat hamazon* comes first, and *havdalah* comes last. What they debated is solely the relative order of *ma'or* and *besamim*. It is in keeping with Rabbi Yehuda's approach that Rava was practicing — doing *besamim* first, in accordance with Beit Hillel — and Rabbi Yochanan rules: *halacha ke'Beit Hillel aliba de'Rabbi Yehuda*. Practically speaking, when combining *birkat hamazon* with *havdalah*, one begins with *birkat hamazon*, concludes with *havdalah*, and within the *havdalah* proper, *besamim* precedes *ma'or*.

The Gemara does not elaborate on the underlying reason for this specific debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel — unlike earlier disputes in the chapter, where the *gemara* probed deeply to find the root of disagreement. One might understand that *ner* carries the more overtly religious message — both as a marker that fire could not be kindled throughout *Shabbos*, and as a memorial to the first creative act of humanity, the making of fire by *Adam HaRishon* after the first *Shabbos*. Beit Shammai may therefore place *ma'or* first because of the primacy of that message. The *besamim*, by contrast, serves to restore the spirit after the departure of the *neshama yeseira*. Beit Hillel may wish to revive the person first, and only then turn to the more formal religious act.

### Shammai/Hillel Order

To summarize the two positions in the *baraita*: *Beit Shammai omrim al hama'or ve'achar kach al habesamim* — first *ma'or* and then *besamim*. *Beit Hillel omrim besamim ve'achar kach hama'or* — *besamim* first, then light.

### Tadir Discussion

One student raised an interesting observation: the distinction between *ma'or* and *besamim* parallels the *tadir* principle. *Ma'or* is performed only once a week, on *motzaei Shabbos*, whereas *besamim* could potentially be encountered more frequently. Beit Hillel, who elsewhere rules that *yayin* comes before *yom* because it is *tadir*, may here be applying the same logic — *besamim*, being the more regularly performed, comes first. This is a compelling parallel for understanding Beit Hillel's position, even if it leaves Beit Shammai's reasoning to be accounted for separately.

## Bara vs Borei

### Text Difference

The Mishnah records that Beit Shammai formulates the blessing on fire as *she'bara ma'or ha'eish* — using the past-tense verb *bara* — while Beit Hillel says *borei me'orei ha'eish*, using the present-participial form *borei*.

### Rava Clarifies

Rava observes that on the question of *bara* versus *borei*, there is actually no dispute: *be'bara kuli alma lo peligi de'bara mashma* — everyone agrees that *bara* signifies the past tense. The apparent debate over *bara* and *borei* dissolves once one recognizes that *borei*, too, can carry the sense of past creation. Rav Yosef challenges this by citing multiple biblical phrases — *yotzer or u'vorei choshech*, *yotzer harim u'vorei ruach*, *borei shamayim ve'noteihem* — all of which use *borei* in contexts that clearly encompass God's past and ongoing creative activity. One might add the grammatical observation that some scholars debate whether Biblical Hebrew truly has a present tense, and whether the participial form functions as a verb at all or rather as an attributive adjective — either way, *borei* plainly encompasses past creation. Rav Yosef's challenge therefore lands: there is nothing to dispute between the two houses regarding *bara* and *borei*.

### Singular vs Plural

*Ki peligi be'ma'or u'me'orei* — the real debate concerns the form of the noun. Beit Shammai says *ma'or* (singular), Beit Hillel says *me'orei* (plural). That is where the houses genuinely differ.

### Shammai/Hillel Flames

*De'Beit Shammai savri chada nehora ika be'nura* — Beit Shammai holds that there is fundamentally a single light within a flame. *U'Beit Hillel savri tuva nehora ika be'nura* — Beit Hillel holds that a flame contains many distinct lights, of different colors and intensities. A *baraita* confirms this: Beit Hillel said to Beit Shammai, *harbe me'orot yesh ba'or* — there are many lights within fire, and therefore the plural *me'orei* is appropriate.

### Vilna Gaon

The Vilna Gaon offers a reading in which the two disputes — *bara* versus *borei*, and *ma'or* versus *me'orei* — are not separate but interrelated. He argues that what Beit Shammai describes when it says *bara* and *ma'or* is the *yesod ha'eish*, the ideal or essential form of fire — the Platonic archetype, as it were, which is singular and was created in the past. Beit Hillel, by contrast, describes fire as it actually exists in this world: ongoing (*borei*) and multiple (*me'orei*), because fire in reality consists of many differentiated flames. This interpretation maps neatly onto a broader pattern: Beit Shammai tends toward the idealized, heavenly conception of things, while Beit Hillel is rooted in how things actually appear in the world. It is a notable reading, though not mentioned in the Gemara itself.

## Non-Jewish Sources

### Light Lo Shavat

The Gemara now addresses the rules governing what light and *besamim* may be used for the *havdalah* blessing. The starting point is *ein mevarchin* — one may not recite the blessing over light or *besamim* that come from a non-Jew. The case of the *ner* is somewhat understandable: *mishum de'lo shavat* — because the fire did not rest. If the light was kindled on *Shabbos*, it is in some sense the product of a violation of *Shabbos*, and the very meaning of the blessing is that one could not kindle light until now. A fire that was lit in apparent violation undermines that.

### Avodah Zarah Concern

The case of *besamim* is less obvious. Rav Yehuda citing Rav explains: the *baraita* is speaking of fragrances that were used at a feast of non-Jews, and we presume that such a feast was conducted in honor of their gods — *de'mistama mesibat ovdei kochavim l'avodah zarah hi*. Therefore, those *besamim* are associated with *avodah zarah*.

The Gemara then raises a challenge: if the end of the Mishnah specifies *besamim shel avodah zarah* — fragrances connected to idolatrous worship — then the beginning of the Mishnah, which speaks only of *besamim* belonging to a non-Jew in general, must be addressing a different concern, one unrelated to *avodah zarah*. Otherwise, why would the Mishnah need to make the distinction at all?

Rav Chanina of Sura resolves this: the end of the Mishnah is not adding a new case but is explaining the beginning. *Mah ta'am ein mevarchin lo al haner velo al habesamim shel akum* — why may one not use the *ner* or *besamim* of a non-Jew? The answer is *mipnei she'stam mesibat ovdei kochavim l'avodah zarah* — because we assume that these items came from a feast that was dedicated to idol worship. The concern for *avodah zarah* is thus the explanation for the general restriction on non-Jewish *besamim*, while the distinct *lo shavat* concern governs the *ner*.

### Definition Shavat

A *baraita* clarifies the concept of *shavat* and *lo shavat*: *or she'shavat mevarchin alav* — if the light rested, one may bless upon it; *ve'shelo shavat ein mevarchin alav* — if it did not rest, one may not. But what do these terms mean?

If one were to say that *lo shavat* means the light was produced through a *melacha* — a forbidden act — then a light kindled for a midwife or a dangerously ill patient on *Shabbos* should be prohibited, since it was kindled on *Shabbos*. Yet a *baraita* rules explicitly that *or shel chaya ve'shel choleh mevarchin alav* — one may bless on a light that was kindled for such purposes on *Shabbos*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak therefore explains: *shavat machamat aveira* — the relevant concept is not whether the light was kindled on *Shabbos* per se, but whether it was kindled in a manner that constitutes a violation. A light kindled for *pikuach nefesh* was kindled in a fully sanctioned manner and is therefore considered to have "rested." A light kindled without such license, even by a non-Jew who is not commanded, carries the conceptual taint of Shabbos violation.

A further *baraita* reinforces this: a lantern burning continuously throughout the entire day of *Shabbos* may be blessed over at *motzaei Shabbos*. Even though it was burning on *Shabbos*, it was kindled before *Shabbos* and never violated it.

This leads to a parenthetical but practically significant observation. On *Yom Kippur*, the *halacha* requires that the blessing *borei me'orei ha'eish* be recited only over a *ner she'shavat* — a flame that was burning before *Yom Kippur* and remained lit throughout. One might think the opposite: if the purpose of the blessing is to acknowledge that fire cannot be kindled on *Yom Kippur*, the most dramatic demonstration would be to light a new flame the moment *Yom Kippur* ends. Yet the *halacha* requires instead a flame that persisted throughout — a point that has puzzled many, and which does not receive a clear resolution here.

### Nochri Mi'Nochri

The Gemara continues with a *baraita*: *nochri she'hidlik ner le'Yisrael ve'Yisrael she'hidlik mi'nochri mevarchin alav* — whether a non-Jew lit a candle for a Jew, or a Jew lit from a non-Jew, one may bless upon it. *Nochri mi'nochri ein mevarchin alav* — but if a non-Jew lit from another non-Jew, one may not.

The Gemara probes this carefully. Why may one not use a non-Jew's flame taken from another non-Jew? Because *mishum de'lo shavat* — we presume it traces back to a fire that did not rest. But if that is the logic, then a Jew who takes fire from a non-Jew should face the same problem, since the original flame also did not rest. The Gemara offers the reasoning that when a Jew kindles from the non-Jew's flame, what was forbidden continues in the original flame while something new — *atosfes de'heteira* — is added in the Jewish kindling; it is upon that new portion that the blessing is recited.

But then the challenge returns: if fire is continuously self-renewing — if *mah she'akar lo hiniach u'mah she'hiniach lo akar*, what one picks up is not what one puts down — why should *nochri mi'nochri* be prohibited at all? Each moment the flame is new. The Gemara resolves this by invoking the *halacha* that one who carries a flame to *reshut harabbim* on *Shabbos* is *chayav* for the flame itself, even when there is not enough of the underlying material to constitute a prohibited transfer — proof that legally the flame maintains its identity across time. The flame is not a perpetually new entity; it is the same object.

The *maskana* of the Gemara — as Rashi and Tosafos understand it — is that technically, even a flame kindled on *Shabbos* becomes permissible after some time has passed, since new fire has been added to the old. The entire prohibition in the case of *nochri mi'nochri* is a *gezeirah* — a rabbinic safeguard — enacted because of the one specific problematic case: when the non-Jew lit it shortly before *Shabbos* ended, and the very original flame is still burning. To avoid requiring people to make such fine distinctions, the Rabbis prohibited any flame in which the chain of transmission runs entirely through non-Jews, and permitted only when there is Jewish involvement at some point in the chain.

## Fire Use Rules

### Fire Outside City

A *baraita* addresses one walking outside a city who sees a flame: *im rov nochrim eino mevarech* — if the majority of the city's residents are non-Jews, one does not recite the blessing; *im rov Yisrael mevarech* — if the majority are Jews, one may. The Gemara immediately notes an internal contradiction: if a majority of non-Jews means one may not bless, then a fifty-fifty split should be sufficient to bless. Yet the second clause implies a majority of Jews is required. The Gemara resolves this by clarifying that even *mechtza al mechtza* — an equal split — is sufficient. The clauses of the *baraita* were phrased as parallel contrasts (*rov nochrim* / *rov Yisrael*) for stylistic symmetry, but the actual ruling is that fifty-fifty suffices.

### Infant Inquiry

A *baraita* presents a further case: one who was walking outside the city and saw a child holding a torch. *Bodek achrav* — one inquires about the child. If found to be Jewish, one recites the blessing; if not Jewish, one does not. The Gemara asks: why does the *baraita* specify a child? Even an adult presents the same question. Rav Yehuda citing Rav explains that the scenario is *besamuch li'shki'at hachama* — immediately after nightfall. In that context, an adult found carrying a newly lit torch would almost certainly be a non-Jew, since a Jew would not have had occasion to kindle a new flame so quickly after *Shabbos*. A child, however, might have simply grabbed a flame that was already burning — *im Yisrael hu ikri ve'nakit* — and thus the inquiry is necessary. The reasoning here is somewhat difficult to follow, and the Gemara does not resolve the apparent tension fully.

### Flame Thickness

A *baraita* rules that if one was outside the city and saw a flame, one may bless upon it only *im ava kefi kivshan* — if it is as thick as the opening of a furnace. If the flame is thinner, one may not bless. This introduces the principle that the blessing *borei me'orei ha'eish* is restricted to a flame serving the purpose of providing light.

### Non-Light Purposes

The Gemara explores what it means for a fire to be lit for the purpose of light through a series of apparent contradictions. One *baraita* says one may bless upon the fire of a kiln; another says one may not. The resolution: at the beginning of the kiln's operation, the fire is entirely for the purpose of smelting, not for illumination; but at the end, when material may be added to make the fire bright enough to work by, it serves the additional purpose of providing light, and the blessing may be recited.

The same structure applies to the fire of an oven or stove: at the beginning it is for cooking; at the end, if tended for illumination as well, it qualifies.

The case of the *beit knesset* and *beit midrash* generates a different kind of ambiguity. One *baraita* permits the blessing on such a fire; another prohibits it. The first resolution: if there is an *adam chashuv* present, Rashi holds that the lights are kindled for his honor — *lichvod* — not for illumination, and one may not bless. Tosafos reads the case inversely: ordinarily the lights in a synagogue or study hall are for honor, but the presence of an important person might prompt the addition of extra light for him to see by. Either way, when the lights serve primarily the function of honor, the blessing may not be recited.

An alternative resolution introduces two further variables: whether there is a *chazan* — here meaning a sexton who resides in the building and needs the light — and whether there is moonlight outside. If a *chazan* is present, the light may be understood as serving a practical function of illumination; if moonlight is available, the indoor lights serve only honor. The *poskim* of later generations applied these principles to candles in synagogues in their own time, and there was genuine debate about whether such candles were fundamentally for *kavod* or for light.

## Individual vs Communal

### Shammai Individual

The Gemara turns to the case of people seated together in the *beit midrash* on *motzaei Shabbos* when a flame is brought before them. Beit Shammai rules: *kol echad ve'echad mevarech le'atzmo* — each person recites the blessing individually.

### Hillel Communal

Beit Hillel rules: *echad mevarech le'kulam* — one person recites the blessing on behalf of all, *mishum she'ne'emar be'rov am hadrat melekh* — as it is written, "in the multitude of the people is the glory of the King." It is better to perform the blessing collectively.

### Bittal Beit Midrash

The Gemara finds Beit Hillel's reasoning self-evident. Beit Shammai's position requires explanation. The answer: *mishum bittul beit ha'midrash* — if everyone must stop, gather attention, and listen together, more time is wasted from Torah study than if each individual recites the brief blessing on his own schedule without interruption. A supporting *baraita* notes that in the household of Rabban Gamliel they did not say the equivalent of "gesundheit" when someone sneezed in the *beit midrash* — *mipnei bittul beit ha'midrash*.

This passage also touches on the broader question of *rov am* versus individual recitation that arises in other contexts — including *kiddush* and *havdalah* — and the related issue of whether it is *gadol ha'oneh* or *gadol ha'mevarech* that is preferred. Those discussions are not resolved here; the only consideration the Gemara raises in this context is *bittul beit ha'midrash*.

## Light/Spices Dead/Bad

### Dead Prohibition

The Mishnah rules: *ein mevarchin lo al ha'ner velo al ha'besamim shel meitim* — one may not recite a blessing over a light or *besamim* that were used for the dead.

### Honor vs Light

The Gemara explains: *ner lichvod hu de'avida* — when a candle is placed in a funeral procession or beside a corpse, it is not there to provide light but to provide honor to the deceased. Since the blessing on fire is contingent on the fire serving the function of illumination, such a flame does not qualify.

For *besamim*, the principle established earlier applies: the blessing on fragrances requires that they be present *lereicha* — for the purpose of providing a pleasant aroma to those present. The *besamim* placed near a corpse are there to mask the odor of death, not to provide a positive fragrance for smelling. That purpose does not satisfy the requirement.

Rav Yehuda citing Rav adds a further distinction: for a *meis* of sufficient importance that candles are carried before the bier both by day and by night, the candles are clearly for *kavod*, and one may not bless upon them. For a *meis* before whom candles are carried only at night — that is, primarily for light — one may bless. The Gemara notes that in any case, the concern of *hana'ah mi'meis* — deriving benefit from the dead — does not extend to such a candle; the connection is too attenuated to trigger that prohibition.

### Bad Smell Masking

Rav Huna extends the principle: *besamim shel beit ha'kisei* — fragrances placed in a bathroom to eliminate unpleasant odors — and *shemen ha'asui le'haavir et ha'zuhama* — oil applied to one's hands to remove filth after a meal — do not qualify for a blessing. In both cases, the fragrance or oil serves not to provide pleasant smell as an end in itself, but to eliminate something unpleasant. The ruling applies regardless of location: if the fundamental purpose of the fragrance is odor elimination rather than the positive experience of *reiach*, no blessing is recited. This principle applies even outside the bathroom — for example, household air fresheners whose function is to mask bad smells.

## Spice Blessings

### Perfume Store

The Gemara introduces the case of one who enters a perfume dealer's shop and smells the fragrances. *Afilu yashav sham kol hayom kulo eino mevarech ela paam achas* — even if one sits there the entire day, one recites the blessing only once. But *nichnas ve'yatza nichnas ve'yatza, mevarech al kol paam va'faam* — if one enters and exits repeatedly, one blesses each time. The question of what constitutes a distinct act of smelling for purposes of the blessing is relevant here, and the *poskim* also debate whether an inadvertent smell — one not consciously intended — requires a blessing at all. If *birkat hareiach* is a *birkat hanehenin*, then even unintended benefit would seem to require a blessing; if it is more in the nature of a *birkat hashevach*, the analysis may differ. The prevailing practice treats it more as a *birkat hanehenin*, given that one recites the blessing before smelling.

### Store Purpose

The Gemara raises a challenge: in the perfume shop, the fragrances are arguably not there *lereicha* — not for the purpose of being smelled. If that is so, how can one recite a blessing upon them? This is puzzling, since a perfume store is self-evidently a place of fragrance. The Gemara answers that the fragrances in the store are indeed made for smelling — *heicha de'nirchi inshi ve'nisvei ve'nizbenu minei* — the whole purpose is that people should smell and be attracted to come in and purchase. The good smell in the store is intentional advertising. The *hava amina* is not fully transparent, but the Gemara's conclusion is that the fragrances in a perfume shop do qualify as *lereicha avida*, and the blessing is in order.

A practical question arises from this: when entering a spice shop — such as a commercial spice store — does one recite a blessing? The fragrances there may be incidental to the purpose of purchasing packaged spices rather than intentionally provided for smelling. If one intentionally pauses to smell a pleasing spice, however, one intends to smell and the blessing would seem appropriate. The practical advice: once one begins to enter and the smell begins to reach one, recite the blessing at that moment and then smell deliberately.

### Outdoor Smell

A *baraita* addresses one walking outside a city who smells a pleasant fragrance from an unknown source. *Im rov ovdei kochavim eino mevarech* — if most of the population is non-Jewish, one does not bless. *Im rov Yisrael mevarech* — if most are Jewish, one may bless.

### Rabbi Yosi Concern

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *afilu rov Yisrael nami eino mevarech* — even a majority of Jews is insufficient. The reason is *mipnei she'benot Yisrael mekatrot lichshafim* — Jewish women sometimes burn incense for purposes of witchcraft. The Gemara pushes back: surely the majority do not practice witchcraft. Rabbi Yosi's concern is more nuanced. There are in fact two minority uses of fragrant burning that disqualify: some burn incense for witchcraft, and some burn incense *legamurei et ha'kelim* — to infuse clothing with scent. This latter purpose is explicitly not *lereicha* in the relevant sense, since the fragrance is not intended to be smelled by people but to be absorbed into fabric. Together, these two minority uses are sufficient, in Rabbi Yosi's view, to make the majority of outdoor fragrances in a Jewish town unreliable for purposes of the blessing — *ve'chol ruba de'lav lereicha avida lo mevorchinan*.

### Regional Practice

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba citing Rabbi Yochanan gives a concrete regional illustration: one who walks in Teveria on *erev Shabbos* or in Tzippori on *motzaei Shabbos* and smells fragrance should not bless, *mipnei she'chazkatah eino asui ela legamurei et ha'kelim* — because in those places it was standard practice to use incense or fragrances for scenting clothing. In Teveria the custom was to do this *erev Shabbos*, and in Tzippori on *motzaei Shabbos*. The fragrance one smells in those places at those times is presumed to be for fabric-scenting, not for the nose.

### Akum Marketplace

A final *baraita* states: if one is walking through a non-Jewish marketplace and deliberately desires to smell the fragrances there — *nitretzeh lehareich, harei zeh chotei* — this person has sinned. Given the *avodah zarah* concerns associated with such a marketplace, the intentional act of smelling — *nitretzeh* — is the critical factor. A related discussion in Masechet *Pesachim* explores the broader question of how intentionality defines a halachic act of smelling, and how to understand situations where one smells inadvertently. Here the Gemara makes clear that *nitretzeh* is what activates the *issur*.

## Benefit Threshold

### Benefit Required

The Gemara closes with a principle that applies to the blessing on light: *ve'ein mevarchin al ha'ner ad she'yei'otu* — one does not recite the blessing on *ner* until one actually derives benefit from it. The discussion of this rule, and its practical and conceptual implications, is where the shiur concludes.
