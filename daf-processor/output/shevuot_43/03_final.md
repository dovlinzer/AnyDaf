# Shevuot 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oath to Minor/Heir

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עֲלֵיהֶן: הָעֲבָדִים וְהַשְּׁטָרוֹת וְהַקַּרְקָעוֹת וְהַהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are items concerning which one does not take an oath** by Torah law: Canaanite **slaves, and** financial **documents, and land, and consecrated** property.

The daf begins in the middle of 42b, with the *mishna* that will carry us into 43. The *mishna* states that one does not take a *shvua* on the basis of a claim made by a *cheresh shoteh ve'katan* — a deaf-mute, a mentally incompetent person, or a minor — against another party. Yet the very same *mishna* then states that one is *nishba'in lekatan*, that one does take an oath *to* a *katan*. The apparent contradiction demands resolution: which is it?

### R. Elazar ben Yaakov

The first answer offered is that the *mishna*'s phrase *nishba'in lekatan* should not be read literally as referring to a minor at all. Rather, it refers to taking an oath to an heir — specifically, when the son of a deceased creditor comes to collect a debt owed to his father. The question then becomes: if that heir comes with a *bari* — a definite claim that you certainly owe his father money — and you are *modeh bemiktzas*, admitting to part of the debt, do you take an oath? One might argue that you should not, on the grounds of *migo*: since you feel no particular moral relationship to the son (as opposed to the father who lent you the money), you could just as easily have denied the debt entirely. Since you could have been a *kofer hakol*, the partial admission should function like *meshiv aveida* — a voluntary gift — and you should be exempt.

This is precisely the position of *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov*, and the *Gemara* considers interpreting our *mishna* in accordance with his view.

The *Gemara* challenges this reading: the earlier part of the *mishna* already addresses the heir case explicitly, teaching *maneh l'aba beyadcha, ein lecha beyadi ella chamishin dinar — patur mipney meshiv aveida*. If someone says "you owe my father a hundred dollars" and you reply "I only owe fifty," you are exempt, because the partial admission is treated as returning a lost object. Why, then, does the later clause need to introduce a different rule?

The answer lies in a distinction. In the earlier clause, the heir comes with a *safeik* — a tentative claim, perhaps "I think you owe my father money." In that case, since the claim is both from an heir and uncertain, everyone agrees that any admission functions as *meshiv aveida* and there is no *shvua*. But in the later clause, the heir comes with a *bari* — "I know for certain you owe my father" — and it is specifically in that case that *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* holds you are not exempt by *migo*, and therefore must take a *shvuat modeh bemiktzas*. This reconciles the two parts of the *mishna*.

### Shmuel's Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: ״לְקָטָן״ – לִיפָּרַע מִנִּכְסֵי קָטָן, ״לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ״ – לִיפָּרַע מִנִּכְסֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to the Gemara's question with regard to the last clause of the mishna, which states that one takes an oath to a minor, or to a representative of the Temple treasury, **Shmuel said** a different answer: When the mishna spoke about taking an oath **to a minor,** it was referring to a case where the debtor died; the creditor must take an oath to the minor heir attesting that he was not repaid in order **to collect from** the **minor's property.** Similarly, if one's debtor consecrated his property, he takes an oath **to** a representative of **the Temple** treasury in order **to collect from the consecrated property.**

*Shmuel* offers an entirely different and remarkably elegant reading of the *mishna*. He argues that *nishba'in lekatan* does not mean responding to a claim made *by* a *katan*, but rather taking an oath when you are coming to *collect from* a *katan* — *lifra minichsei katan*. Similarly, *lehekdesh* means *lifra minichsei hekdesh* — collecting from sanctified property.

Under Shmuel's reading, the case is not one of *modeh bemiktzas* at all. Rather: you hold a *shtar* proving that the deceased father owed you money, and you now seek to collect from the minor orphan heir. The minor cannot mount a proper defense — he lacks the capacity to examine witnesses, present counterclaims, or argue effectively. Therefore, even though all the evidence is in your favor, you must first take a *shvua* that the debt is genuinely still owed before collecting.

The case of *hekdesh* is analogous. The scenario the *Gemara* envisions is not that *hekdesh* itself borrowed money, but rather that David owed you money, you held a prior lien on his property, and David then consecrated that property to *hekdesh*. Since your lien predates the consecration, you may seize the property — and at the moment of seizure, retroactively, it is as though it never became *hekdesh*. Nevertheless, before collecting in this way, you must take a *shvua* that David has not already repaid the debt.

### Heirs & Orphans

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְקָטָן לִיפָּרַע מִנִּכְסֵי קָטָן – תְּנֵינָא: מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים לֹא יִפָּרַע אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה! תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: The *halakha* that one takes an oath **to a minor** in order **to collect from a minor's property** is one that **we learn** in the mishna (45a): A woman who comes to collect the payment for her marriage contract **from the property of orphans collects only by means of an oath. Why do I** need **two** *mishnayot* to teach this *halakha*?

The *Gemara* notes that the obligation to take a *shvua* when collecting from *yetomim* is already taught explicitly in a forthcoming *mishna* — *lo yifra ela bishvua*. Why does the present *mishna* need to say it again?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָמַשְׁמַע לַן – כִּדְאַבָּיֵי קַשִּׁישָׁא; דְּתָנֵי אַבָּיֵי קַשִּׁישָׁא: יְתוֹמִין שֶׁאָמְרוּ – גְּדוֹלִים, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר קְטַנִּים; בֵּין לִשְׁבוּעָה בֵּין לְזִיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: By mentioning this *halakha* twice, the Mishna **teaches us this:** The *halakha* applies with regard to both minor and adult orphans, **in accordance with** the statement **of Abaye the Elder; as Abaye the Elder taught:** The **orphans of which** the Sages **spoke** are **adult** orphans, **and needless to say,** the same *halakha* also applies to **minor** orphans. This principle applies **with regard to both** the *halakha* that a debt can be collected from the property of an orphan only by means of **an oath, and to** the *halakha* that a debt can be collected from the property of an orphan only from **inferior-quality** land.

The *Gemara* answers that the repetition conveys the *chiddush* of *Abaye Kashisha*, the elder Abaye, who taught: *yetomim she'amru gedolim* — when the *mishna* says you must swear before collecting from heirs, this applies even if the heirs are adults, and needless to say when they are minors.

One might have thought that if the heirs are adults, they can mount a full defense and no special protection is needed. The *chiddush* is that since the adult heirs were not themselves party to the original loan — it was their father who borrowed — they may lack crucial information about what transpired. Therefore the obligation to take a *shvua* before collecting applies regardless of the heirs' age. This applies both to the *shvua* itself and to the rule that one collects only from *zivoris*, third-grade land, even from adult heirs.

### Hekdesh Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהֶקְדֵּשׁ לִיפָּרַע מִנִּכְסֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ – תְּנֵינָא: מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים לֹא יִפָּרְעוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה; וּמָה לִי מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים לְהֶדְיוֹט, וּמָה לִי מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים לְגָבוֹהַּ?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Shmuel's explanation of the mishna that one takes an oath **to** a representative of **the Temple** treasury in order **to collect** a debt **from consecrated property,** the Gemara asks: **We learn** this *halakha* in the mishna (45a): **From liened property** that has been sold one **collects** a debt **only by means of an oath. And what** difference is it **to me** whether the property was **liened to an ordinary** person, **and what** difference is it **to me** whether the property was **liened to the Most High,** i.e., it was consecrated?

Returning to the case of collecting from *hekdesh*: the *Gemara* observes that this too is already taught in the forthcoming *mishna* regarding *nechasim meshubadim* — encumbered property — so why does the present *mishna* need to state it separately?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הֶדְיוֹט הוּא דְּאָדָם עוֹשֶׂה קְנוּנְיָא עַל הֶדְיוֹט; אֲבָל הֶקְדֵּשׁ, דְּאֵין אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה קְנוּנְיָא עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ – קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** for this *halakha* to be stated separately with regard to collecting a debt from the Temple treasury. Otherwise it might **enter your mind to say** that **it is** specifically in order to collect a debt from **an ordinary** person that one is required to take an oath, **as a person** is liable to **collude** with another **against an ordinary** person who purchased property, by producing a promissory note for a debt that was already repaid in order to collect property from the purchasers of land that had been liened to that debt. **But** one might have thought that in order to collect a debt from the **Temple** treasury, a person is not required to take an oath, **as a person does not collude** with another **against** the **Temple** treasury. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that one is required to take an oath even in order to collect a debt from the Temple treasury, as one is suspected of collusion in this case as well.

The answer is that the two cases are distinct and each needed to be said independently. One might have thought that the requirement to take a *shvua* applies only when a lay person (*hedyot*) holds the lien and might be defrauded, but that no one would scheme (*knunya*) to cheat out *hekdesh*. Therefore the *mishna* teaches that we do require a *shvua* even when *hekdesh* is the party with the lien, since a person who is alive and healthy may indeed conspire to defraud *hekdesh* and receive a kickback.

However, the *Gemara* cites *Rav Huna*, who rules that a *shechiv mera* — a person on his deathbed — who consecrates all his property and then adds "by the way, I owe Reuven a hundred dollars which is already liened and not included," is believed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁ כׇּל נְכָסָיו, וְאָמַר: ״מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי בְּיָדִי״ – נֶאֱמָן, חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה קְנוּנְיָא עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ! אָמְרִי: הָנֵי מִילֵּי שְׁכִיב מְרַע, דְּאֵין אָדָם חוֹטֵא וְלֹא לוֹ; אֲבָל גַּבֵּי בָּרִיא, וַדַּאי חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rav Huna say** that in the case of **a person on his deathbed who consecrated all of his property, and said: So-and-so has one hundred dinars in my possession,** his statement is **deemed credible,** as the **presumption** is that **a person does not collude** with another **against** the **Temple** treasury? The Sages **said** in response: **That statement** applies only in the case of **a person on his deathbed, as a person sins only for his own** benefit. One is not suspected of deceiving the Temple treasury for the benefit of his heirs. **But with regard to a healthy person, we are certainly concerned** about collusion, even against the Temple treasury.

The reasoning is *chazaka ein adam osef knunya al hekdesh* — we may rely on the presumption that a person will not scheme to defraud *hekdesh*. Does this not contradict the requirement of a *shvua*?

The resolution distinguishes between the two cases. By a *shechiv mera*, two factors combine: first, one is reluctant to engage in such schemes on one's deathbed; second, and perhaps more importantly, the dying person himself will not live to receive any kickback — it is his children who benefit, a step sufficiently removed that we do not attribute such scheming to him. By contrast, a healthy person (*bari*) who has many years ahead of him and stands to personally benefit from the scheme — there we are certainly concerned, and the *shvua* is required.

### Encumbered Property

The case of *nechasim meshubadim* — encumbered property — involves a scenario where David owes money to two creditors: you and Charlie. You hold a prior lien, and you seek to collect from David's property. But since Charlie also has a claim on that same property, your collecting from it affects Charlie as well. The concern is that David and you might conspire: David will not contest your claim, you will seize the property as though under a prior lien — even if that lien is fictitious — and later return part of the proceeds to David under the table, leaving Charlie unable to collect. This conspiracy is called a *knunya*.

Because a third party (Charlie) stands to lose when the lienholding creditor seizes the property, David's failure to contest your claim is insufficient protection for Charlie. You must therefore take a *shvua* to forestall any such collusion. The same applies when it is *hekdesh* rather than a private individual that holds the parallel lien, as the *Gemara* establishes.

## Excluded Objects (II)

### Five Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עֲלֵיהֶן: הָעֲבָדִים וְהַשְּׁטָרוֹת וְהַקַּרְקָעוֹת וְהַהֶקְדֵּשׁוֹת. אֵין בָּהֶן תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְלֹא תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה, שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם אֵינוֹ נִשְׁבָּע, נוֹשֵׂא שָׂכָר אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these are items concerning which one does not take an oath** by Torah law: Canaanite **slaves, and** financial **documents, and land, and consecrated** property. **In** a case where **these** items are stolen, **there is no payment of double** the principal, **nor is there payment of four or five** times the principal in a case where one stole a consecrated animal and slaughtered or sold it. **An unpaid bailee** who lost one of these items **does not take an oath** that he was not negligent in safeguarding it, and **a paid bailee does not pay** for the loss or theft of one of these items.

The *mishna* now introduces the following categories: *v'eilu devarim she'ein nishba'in aleihen* — these are the objects regarding which no oath is taken. The list comprises slaves (*avadim*), writs of debt (*shtaros*), land (*karka*), and sanctified objects (*hekdesh*). These four categories are excluded from the oath of *modeh bemiktzas*, from the oath of a single witness (*ed echad*), and — as the *mishna* goes on to specify — from several other *halachic* obligations as well.

The first additional exclusion concerns *tashlumei kefel* — double payment for theft. Normally, a burglar pays *kefel*, double the value of the stolen object. If he then slaughters or sells the stolen animal, he pays four or five times its value. All four categories are exempt from *kefel*, and consequently from the four-and-five payment as well, since once *kefel* is eliminated, the incremental payment above *kefel* falls away automatically.

The *mishna* then addresses the laws of *shomrim* — guardians. *Shomer chinam eino nishba, nosei sachar eino meshalem* — an unpaid guardian (*shomer chinam*) need not take an oath regarding these objects, and a paid guardian (*shomer sachar*) need not pay if something happens to them.

It is worth pausing on the classic *shvuat shomrim*. This oath is not a response to an adversarial claim of lying; rather, it functions as corroboration when the guardian reports what befell the object. The owner asks what happened, the guardian explains there was a theft or loss, and the *Torah* requires an oath to substantiate that truthful account. One might therefore have thought that this category of oath — which is not about disputed amounts but about disputed facts — might apply even to the excluded objects. The *mishna* teaches otherwise: these objects are exempt from the *shomrim* oath as well. An unpaid guardian simply asserts what happened and is exempt; a paid guardian who would normally be liable for theft or loss (and who admits as much) is nonetheless exempt from payment when the object falls into one of these categories.

An important question raised by the *Rishonim* but left unresolved explicitly in the *mishna* is whether a *shomer* of these objects bears any *chiyuv shemira* — any legal obligation of care — at all. The exemptions from oath and payment do not necessarily speak to whether the guardian incurred any duty in the first place. Furthermore, several *Rishonim* hold that *poshea hi k'mazik* — negligence is legally equivalent to direct damage. If so, a negligent guardian might be liable even for these excluded objects, not under the laws of *shomrim* but as a tortfeasor. Whether the exclusion of the *shomrim* framework also eliminates the underlying duty of care remains an open question not explicitly addressed in the *mishna*.

### R. Shimon's Kodashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: קֳדָשִׁים שֶׁחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן – נִשְׁבָּעִין עֲלֵיהֶן, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָן – אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says** there is a distinction between different types of consecrated property: With regard to **consecrated** property **for which** one **bears** the financial **responsibility** to compensate the Temple treasury in the event **of their** loss, such as in a case where he vowed to bring an offering and then set aside an animal to be sacrificed in fulfillment of the vow, **one takes an oath concerning them,** as they are considered his own property. **But** with regard to consecrated property **for which** he **does not bear** the financial **responsibility for their** loss, **one does not take an oath concerning them.**

*Rabbi Shimon* draws a distinction within the category of *kodashim*: *kodashim she-chayav be-achrayutan nishba'in aleihem, ve-she-eino chayav be-achrayutan ein nishba'in aleihem* — sanctified objects for which one bears a replacement obligation are subject to the oath, while those for which one bears no such obligation are not.

The case is this: Charlie takes a *neder* — a personal vow — to bring two *olot*, two burnt offerings. He then designates two sheep for this purpose and gives them to Dov to watch. Dov claims he only received one. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, Dov must take a *shvuat modeh bemiktzas*. Why? Because Charlie's *neder* obligated him personally to bring two *olot*; if those sheep are lost, Charlie must replace them out of his own pocket. The sheep are therefore not merely *hekdesh* from Charlie's perspective — they represent a real financial exposure. Their loss *goremes le-mamon*, it causes a monetary loss. Such sanctified objects are called *davar ha-gorem le-mamon*, and *Rabbi Shimon* treats them as property rather than as *hekdesh* for purposes of the oath requirement.

This stands in contrast to a case where someone merely sanctified an animal without a personal vow: if that animal is lost, there is no obligation to replace it, and it is treated as pure *hekdesh*, exempt from the oath.

### R. Meir Laden Vines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ דְּבָרִים שֶׁהֵן בְּקַרְקַע וְאֵינָן כְּקַרְקַע; וְאֵין חֲכָמִים מוֹדִים לוֹ. כֵּיצַד? ״עֶשֶׂר גְּפָנִים טְעוּנוֹת מָסַרְתִּי לָךְ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵינָן אֶלָּא חָמֵשׁ״. רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב שְׁבוּעָה, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַמְחוּבָּר לְקַרְקַע – הֲרֵי הוּא כְּקַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says: There are** certain **items that are** physically **on the land but are not** treated **like land** from a halakhic perspective, **and the Rabbis do not concede to him** concerning this point. **How so?** If one makes the claim: **I assigned you ten grapevines laden** with fruit to safeguard, **and the other one says: They are only five** vines, **Rabbi Meir deems** the defendant **liable** to take **an oath,** as he admitted to a part of the claim, and although the claim concerned grapevines, the primary aspect of the claim was the grapes. **And the Rabbis say:** The halakhic status of **anything that is attached to the land is like the land** itself, and therefore he is exempt from taking an oath.

*Rabbi Meir* introduces a further nuance regarding *karka*. The *mishna* states: *yesh devarim she-hen ke-karka ve-einam ke-karka* — there are things that are like land yet are not treated as land *halachically*, and the *chachamim* do not agree with him. His example: if Charlie says "I gave you ten vines laden with grapes to watch" (*esser gefanim te'unot*), and Dov responds "you only gave me five," *Rabbi Meir* holds that Dov must take an oath. The *chachamim* respond: anything attached to the ground (*kol ha-mechubar le-karka*) has the status of *karka* and is excluded.

A surface reading of *Rabbi Meir*'s position suggests that he holds attachment to the ground confers no *karka* status on growing things at all. But the *Gemara* limits his view considerably.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אֶלָּא) אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הָכָא בַּעֲנָבִים עוֹמְדוֹת לְהִבָּצֵר קָמִיפַּלְגִי; דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: כִּבְצוּרוֹת דָּמְיָין, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא כִּבְצוּרוֹת דָּמְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said** that **they disagree here** not with regard to any item that is attached to the land, as Rabbi Meir concedes that such items usually have the halakhic status of the land itself. The dispute is specifically **with regard to grapes that are ready to be harvested, as Rabbi Meir holds** that their halakhic status is **similar to** that of grapes that are already **harvested, and the Rabbis hold** that their halakhic status is **not similar to** that of grapes that are already **harvested,** and that they still have the status of land.

*Rav Yosef* citing *Rabbi Chanina* explains: normally *Rabbi Meir* agrees that things attached to the ground are treated as *karka*. The dispute here is specifically because the grapes are *omdot libatzir* — ready for harvest. *Rabbi Meir* holds that grapes at the point of harvest are *k'vatzur dami* — regarded as if already detached and no longer part of the land. The *chachamim* hold they remain *ke-karka* until actually harvested.

## Quantifiability (II)

### Mishna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין אֶלָּא עַל דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה וְשֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן. כֵּיצַד? ״בַּיִת מָלֵא מָסַרְתִּי לָךְ״ וְ״כִיס מָלֵא מָסַרְתִּי לָךְ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ, אֶלָּא מָה שֶׁהִנַּחְתָּ אַתָּה נוֹטֵל״ – פָּטוּר. זֶה אוֹמֵר ״עַד הַזִּיז״ וְזֶה אוֹמֵר ״עַד הַחַלּוֹן״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **One takes an oath only concerning an item that is** defined **by size, by weight, or by number. How so?** If the claimant says: **I transferred to you a house full** of produce, **or: I transferred to you a pouch full** of money, **and the other** person **says: I do not know** how much you gave me, **but what you left** in my possession **you may take,** and the amount in the house or pouch at that time is less than that claimed by the claimant, the defendant is **exempt** from taking an oath, as the amounts in the claim and the admission are undefined. But if **this** party **says** that the house was full **up to the ledge, and that** party **says** that it was full **up to the window,** the defendant is **liable** to take an oath, as the dispute relates to a defined amount.

The *mishna* states: *ein nishba'in ela al davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan* — one takes a *shvuat modeh bemiktzas* only when the disputed object is quantifiable in measure, weight, or number.

### House Full Case

The *mishna*'s example of a non-quantifiable claim: *bayit malei masarti lach* — "I gave you a house full of potatoes." The respondent replies: *ela mah she-inhata atah notel* — "I don't know how much you gave me; take back whatever is here." Since neither the claim nor the response is expressed in any unit of measure, there is no *shvuat modeh bemiktzas*.

By contrast: *zeh omer ad ha-ziz ve-zeh omer ad ha-chalon* — one says the house was filled up to the protruding beam, the other says only up to the windowsill — this is *chayav*, because while no explicit unit is used, the positions of the beam and the window can be measured, and the disagreement is quantifiable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״בַּיִת״ סְתָם, אֲבָל אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״בַּיִת זֶה מָלֵא״ – יְדִיעָא טַעַנְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye said: They taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where** the claimant **said to him:** I gave you **a house** full of produce, **without specification. But** if **he said to him:** I gave you **this** specific **house full** of produce, **his claim is known** and defined, and the defendant is therefore required to take an oath concerning it.

*Abaye* proposes that the *mishna*'s distinction turns on whether the claim references *this* house specifically. If Charlie says *bayit zeh malei* — "this house, full" — its dimensions can be measured and the claim becomes quantifiable. The generic *bayit malei* is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: זֶה אוֹמֵר ״עַד הַזִּיז״ וְזֶה אוֹמֵר ״עַד הַחַלּוֹן״ – חַיָּיב; לִיפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּ״בַּיִת מָלֵא״, אֲבָל ״בַּיִת זֶה מָלֵא״ – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so, rather than teaching** in **the last clause** of the mishna: If **this** party **says** that the house was full **up to the ledge, and that** party **says** that it was full **up to the window,** the defendant is **liable** to take an oath, **let** the *tanna* **distinguish and teach** the distinction **within** the case **itself,** where the claim was for a house full of produce, and say: **In what** case **is this statement,** that the defendant is exempt, **said?** It is said **in** a case where the claim was for an unspecified **house full** of produce; **but** if the claim was for **this** particular **house full** of produce, the defendant is **liable** to take an oath. Since the mishna did not make this distinction, evidently the defendant is exempt even if the claim is referring to a specific house.

*Rava* objects: if *bayit zeh malei* were considered quantifiable and therefore *chayav*, the *mishna* should have made that its *chiddush* — teaching that even *bayit malei* can suffice when a specific house is named. The fact that the *mishna* instead contrasts *bayit malei* with the *ad ha-ziz / ad ha-chalon* case indicates that even *bayit zeh malei* is insufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּטְעָנֶנּוּ בְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה שֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן, וְיוֹדֶה לוֹ בְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה וְשֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The defendant **is never liable** to take an oath **unless** the claimant **claims from him an item that is** defined **by size, by weight, or by number, and** the defendant **admits to him with regard to** a part of the claim that is **an item that is** defined **by size, by weight, or by number.**

The reason is that *Rava* requires not merely that the *difference* between claim and response be quantifiable, but that each be articulated in affirmatively quantifiable terms. Saying "this house full" remains too imprecise, lacking any explicit reference to a unit or boundary point. Saying "up to the beam" or "up to the window" crosses that threshold, even without invoking units, because it specifies an objective boundary from which a measurement can be taken.

### Baraita Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: ״כּוֹר תְּבוּאָה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ – פָּטוּר. ״מְנוֹרָה גְּדוֹלָה יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא מְנוֹרָה קְטַנָּה״ – פָּטוּר. ״אֲזוֹרָה גְּדוֹלָה יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא אֲזוֹרָה קְטַנָּה״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava:** If the claimant says: **I have a *kor* of produce in your possession, and the other one says: Nothing of yours is in my possession,** the defendant is **exempt** from taking an oath, as he denies the entire debt. If he says: **I have a large candelabrum in your possession,** and the defendant responds: **You have only a small candelabrum in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath, as he admits not to part of the claim, but to possessing a different item. Similarly, if the claimant says: **I have a large belt in your possession,** and the defendant responds: **You have only a small belt in my possession,** he is **exempt** from taking an oath.

A *baraita* supports *Rava*'s position with a series of cases. First: "I gave you a gallon (*kor*) of grain to watch" — the other responds "you never gave me any grain" — *patur*, since the denier is a *kofer hakol*. Second: "I gave you a large *menorah* to watch" — the other responds "you only gave me a small *menorah*" — *patur*. Third: "I gave you a large belt (*azur*) to watch" — the other responds "you only gave me a small belt" — *patur*.

Why is the *menorah* case exempt from a *shvua*? Not only is "large" and "small" insufficiently quantified, but more fundamentally, a large *menorah* and a small *menorah* are different objects.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אָמַר לוֹ: ״כּוֹר תְּבוּאָה יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא לֶתֶךְ״ – חַיָּיב. ״מְנוֹרָה בַּת עֶשֶׂר לִיטְרִין יֵשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא בַּת חָמֵשׁ לִיטְרִין״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **But** if the claimant **said to him: I have a *kor* of produce in your possession, and the other one says: You have only a half-*kor* in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath. Similarly, if the claimant says: **I have a candelabrum** weighing **ten *litra* in your possession,** and the defendant responds: **You have only a five-*litra*** candelabrum **in my possession,** he is **liable** to take an oath.

Contrast the exempt cases with those that generate a *shvua*: "I gave you a gallon (*kor*) of grain" — "I only have a half-gallon (*letach*)" — *chayav*. "I gave you a ten-pound *menorah*" — "I only have a five-pound *menorah*" — *chayav*.

*Ma she-ta'ano lo hoda lo u-ma she-hoda lo lo ta'ano* — what was claimed was not conceded, and what was conceded was not claimed. *Modeh bemiktzas* requires that the admission be to a partial quantity of the very thing claimed. If someone says "I gave you this large table" and you respond "I have only a small table," you are not conceding to part of the claim — you are describing an entirely different object. The *kefel* payment by a *ganav* turns on the same structure: the stolen object must be a subset of what was claimed.

### Dual Quantifiability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּטְעָנֶנּוּ בְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה וְשֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן, וְיוֹדֶה לוֹ בְּדָבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה וְשֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* concludes: The **principle of the matter** is that the defendant **is never liable** to take an oath **unless** the claimant **claims from him an item that is** defined **by size, or by weight, or by number, and** the defendant **admits to him with regard to** a part of the claim that is **an item that is** defined **by size, by weight, or by number.**

The *baraita* closes with an explicit formulation of the principle: *kelalo shel davar: l'olam eino chayav ad she-yit-anennu be-davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan, ve-yodeh lo be-davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan* — the general rule is that one is not liable unless both the claim and the response are quantifiable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לָאו לְאֵתוֹיֵי ״בַּיִת זֶה מָלֵא״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does the *baraita* **add** that was not already taught, by mentioning the **principle of the matter? Doesn't** the *baraita* mention this principle **to add** that even if the claim is for **this** specific **house full** of produce, the defendant is exempt, as this is not considered an item defined by size? Accordingly, the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Rava.

The *Gemara* notes that this explicit statement is included specifically to rule out *bayit zeh malei* — to emphasize that even when a specific house is named, making the dimensions in principle measurable, the claim must be articulated in explicitly quantifiable terms. *Bayit zeh malei* lacks the necessary specificity.

### Menorah Subset

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מְנוֹרָה גְּדוֹלָה וּמְנוֹרָה קְטַנָּה? מַה שֶּׁטְּעָנוֹ לֹא הוֹדָה לוֹ, וּמַה שֶּׁהוֹדָה לוֹ לֹא טְעָנוֹ! אִי הָכִי, בַּת עֶשֶׂר בַּת חָמֵשׁ נָמֵי – מַה שֶּׁטְּעָנוֹ לֹא הוֹדָה לוֹ, וּמַה שֶּׁהוֹדָה לוֹ לֹא טְעָנוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** Having cited the *baraita*, the Gemara now analyzes it: **What is different** about a case where the claimant claimed **a large candelabrum and** the defendant admitted to owing **a small candelabrum** that renders the defendant exempt from taking an oath? It is because **that which he claimed from him, he did not admit to** at all, **and that which he admitted to, he had not claimed from him. If so,** in a case where the claimant claimed **a ten**-*litra* candelabrum **and** the defendant admitted to owing **a five**-*litra* candelabrum, the defendant should **also** be exempt, **as that which he claimed from him,** a heavier candelabrum, **he did not admit to** at all, **and that which he admitted to,** a lighter candelabrum, **he had not claimed from him.**

The *menorah* case — where a ten-pound *menorah* is claimed and a five-pound *menorah* is admitted — naturally raises the question: is this not also a case of two different objects? *Ma she-ta'ano lo hoda lo* — just as a large *menorah* and a small *menorah* are different, so too a ten-pound and a five-pound *menorah* would seem to be different objects.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: הָכָא בִּמְנוֹרָה שֶׁל חֻלְיוֹת עָסְקִינַן, דְּקָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak said: Here** in the latter case **we are dealing with a candelabrum** composed **of segments** that detach; the defendant is liable to take an oath **because he admits to** owing **him** part **of** the candelabrum claimed by the claimant.

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak* initially proposes that the case involves a *menorah shel chuliyot* — a modular *menorah* assembled from components that can be added or removed. A five-pound *menorah* assembled from fewer components is therefore literally a subset of a ten-pound *menorah* assembled from more components, and the admission is to part of the very thing claimed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲזוֹרָה נָמֵי נִיתְנֵי – וְלוֹקְמֵי בִּדְלַיְיפִי! אֶלָּא דְּלַיְיפִי לָא קָתָנֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי – בְּשֶׁל חֻלְיוֹת לָא קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, let** the *baraita* **teach** the case in which the defendant is liable to take an oath involving **a belt as well, and interpret** the case as referring **to** a belt made **of** pieces that are **connected** to each other; the claimant claims a belt with a larger number of pieces, and the defendant claims that he owes him a belt with fewer pieces. **Rather,** clearly the *baraita* **is not teaching** cases involving items made **of** pieces **connected** to each other. **Here too,** in the case of the candelabrum, the *baraita* **is not teaching** a case of a candelabrum composed **of segments** that detach.

The *Gemara* challenges this: if the principle is that modular, addable objects create a subset relationship, why does the *baraita* not teach a similar case for belts, which can be expanded by adding links? The *Gemara* replies that the *baraita* simply did not enumerate every applicable case (*d'laifi lo kasani*), and similarly, one could say the *menorah shel chuliyot* is not the only possible explanation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: שָׁאנֵי מְנוֹרָה, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְגוֹרְרָהּ וּלְהַעֲמִידָהּ עַל חָמֵשׁ לִיטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Abba bar Memel said** that the case of **a candelabrum is different because** one **can scrape** a *ten-litra* candelabrum **and reduce it to a five-litra** one. Therefore, he admitted to a part of the claim.

*Rav Papa bar Manmiyo* therefore offers a different account. The key is that a ten-pound *menorah* can physically be converted into a five-pound *menorah* by scraping down and thinning out the metal. A shorter *menorah* cannot be produced from a taller one without destroying its integrity — cutting off the base or the top renders it non-functional. But scraping the exterior to reduce weight preserves the object's form and function. Therefore, a five-pound *menorah* is indeed a potential subset of a ten-pound one, since the latter can be transformed into the former. This conversion relationship is what justifies treating the admission as *modeh bemiktzas* rather than as a claim about a different object.

## Mashkon Cases

### Mashkon Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן וְאָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, אָמַר לוֹ: ״סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו וְשֶׁקֶל הָיָה שָׁוֶה״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו וְסֶלַע הָיָה שָׁוֶה״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There is a case of **one who lends** money **to another on** the basis of **collateral, and the collateral was lost** while in the possession of the creditor, **and** the creditor **says to** the debtor: **I lent you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** that collateral **was worth a shekel,** i.e., a half-sela. Therefore, you owe me a shekel. **And the other** individual, the debtor, **says** in response to that claim: **That is not** the case. **Rather, you lent me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral, **and** the collateral **was worth a *sela*;** I owe you nothing. In this case, the debtor is **exempt** from payment.

The *mishna* now presents a series of cases involving *mashkon* — collateral. The scenario is: I lend Charlie a hundred dollars (*sela*), and Charlie gives me his watch as collateral. Subsequently the watch is lost — meaning I, as the *shomer* of the collateral, must bear its loss and reimburse Charlie. A dispute arises between us about the watch's value and what, if anything, Charlie still owes me. The *mishna* works through four permutations.

### Full Denial

The first case is straightforward. I claim Charlie owes me fifty dollars; Charlie says he owes me nothing because the watch was worth the full hundred. Charlie is denying my entire claim against him, making him a *kofer hakol*. The *halacha*: *patur* — no oath is required.

### Partial Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו, וְשֶׁקֶל הָיָה שָׁוֶה״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה דִּינָרִים הָיָה שָׁוֶה״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** There is a case of a creditor who claims: **I lent you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and it was worth a shekel. And the other** individual, the debtor, **says: That is not** the case; **rather, you lent me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral, **and** the collateral **was worth three dinars,** i.e., three-quarters of a *sela*. In this case, the debtor is **liable** to take an oath, due to the fact that he responded to the claim of the creditor with a partial admission.

The second case: I claim the watch was worth only fifty dollars, so Charlie owes me fifty. Charlie responds that the watch was worth seventy-five dollars, so he owes me only twenty-five. Charlie is conceding that he owes something but disputes the amount — this is *modeh bemiktzas*, and he is *chayav* to take an oath.

### Reversed Denial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו וּשְׁתַּיִם הָיָה שָׁוֶה״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו וְסֶלַע הָיָה שָׁוֶה״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If in that case the debtor said: **You lent me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** the collateral **was worth two** *sela*, so now you owe me a *sela*. **And the other** party, i.e., the creditor, **said: That is not** the case; **rather, I lent you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** the collateral **was worth a *sela*.** Here, the creditor is **exempt.**

The third case reverses the roles. Charlie claims the watch was worth two hundred dollars, so I owe him a hundred. I respond that the watch was worth exactly a hundred dollars, equal to the loan; we're even, and Charlie owes me nothing. I am the *kofer hakol* in this configuration. *Patur* — no oath required.

### Reversed Partial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו וּשְׁתַּיִם הָיָה שָׁוֶה״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו וַחֲמִשָּׁה דִּינָרִים הָיָה שָׁוֶה״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If in that case the debtor said: **You lent me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** the collateral **was worth two** *sela*. **And the other** party, i.e., the creditor, **said: That is not** the case; **rather, I lent you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** the collateral **was worth five dinars.** Here, the creditor is **liable** to take an oath due to the fact that he responded to the claim of the debtor with a partial admission.

The fourth case: Charlie claims the watch was worth two hundred dollars, so I owe him a hundred. I respond that the watch was worth only a hundred and twenty-five dollars, so I owe him only twenty-five. I am now the *modeh bemiktzas* — admitting I owe something but disputing the amount. *Chayav* — I take an oath.

### Unusual Oath Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי נִשְׁבָּע? מִי שֶׁהַפִּקָּדוֹן אֶצְלוֹ. שֶׁמָּא יִשָּׁבַע זֶה, וְיוֹצִיא הַלָּה אֶת הַפִּקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And who takes** the **oath? The one** in **whose** possession **the deposit had been** located, i.e., the creditor, who took collateral from the debtor. The Sages instituted this provision **lest this** party, i.e., the debtor, **take an oath and** then **the other** party, the creditor, **produce the deposit** and prove the oath false.

These first four cases, while instructive, follow the standard rules of *modeh bemiktzas* in a straightforward way. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* lies in the second case, the one where Charlie should logically be taking the oath. I claim he owes me fifty; he says he owes only twenty-five — he is the *modeh bemiktzas*. Yet the *mishna* rules: *u-mi nishba? mi she-ha-pikadon etzlo* — who takes the oath? The one who is holding the collateral — meaning, *I* take the oath, not Charlie. The *Gemara* will explain why.

## Mashkon Oath Reasoning

### False Oath Risk

Why does the *mishna* transfer the oath from Charlie to me? *Shema yishava zeh ve-yotzi hallah et ha-pikadon* — lest Charlie take the oath that the watch was worth seventy-five dollars and only twenty-five is owed, and then I produce the watch and demonstrate it was actually worth only fifty.

The concern is subtle and important. We are not primarily worried that Charlie is lying. Valuation of an object is inherently subjective, and each party has a natural (and perhaps unconscious) bias: the owner of lost collateral tends to recall it as more valuable; the party who owes money has reason to believe it was worth less. Charlie might take his oath in complete good faith, and yet when the watch reappears it could turn out to be worth only fifty dollars, rendering him technically a false swearer despite his sincerity. The *halacha* is concerned about protecting a person from being shown to have taken a false oath, even innocently.

### Lender Takes Oath

To protect Charlie from this predicament, the rabbis transfer the oath to me. I swear — to what? The implication is that I swear the watch was indeed worth only fifty, and on the basis of that oath I collect the full fifty dollars that I claimed. This outcome seems harsh to Charlie: not only is he denied the benefit of his own oath, but he must now pay the full amount I claimed rather than the twenty-five he was prepared to acknowledge. The protection of Charlie's integrity comes at the cost of his money.

### Rav Ashi Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַסֵּיפָא – וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דִּשְׁבוּעָה גַּבֵּי מַלְוֶה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **To which** case is the final statement in the mishna, which says the creditor is the one who takes the oath, referring? **If we say** it is referring **to** the case in **the latter clause** of the mishna, where the debtor claims that the collateral was worth more than the loan, **derive** this *halakha* from the fact **that** the **oath is** anyway taken **by the creditor,** as he is the defendant in this case. The additional statement is superfluous.

Before accepting this outcome as final, the *Gemara* introduces a principle stated by *Rav Ashi* in a different context in *Bava Metzia*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַרֵישָׁא. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב: אַרֵישָׁא. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** In response, **Shmuel says:** This statement relates **to** the case in **the first clause** of the mishna, where the debtor is the defendant. **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav similarly says** that it relates **to the first clause. And Rabbi Yoḥanan similarly says** that it relates **to the first clause.**

The case is: I asked David to watch my watch. David comes to me and says, "Something happened to it — it was stolen — but I accept liability and will reimburse you." One might think David need say nothing further. He is admitting liability; what more could be required?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּשְׁבוּעָה גַּבֵּי לֹוֶה הִיא, וְשַׁקְלוּהָ רַבָּנַן מִלֹּוֶה וְשַׁדְיוּהָ אַמַּלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **As** in this case, **the oath** should in principle be taken **by the debtor,** since he is the one who admitted to a part of the creditor's claim, **but the Sages removed** the obligation to take an oath **from the debtor and imposed it on the creditor,** deeming him liable to take an oath that the collateral was not worth more than a shekel.

*Rav Ashi* holds that David must nonetheless swear *she'eina birshuto* — that the object is no longer in his possession. Why? Because David may have coveted the watch all along, and is now fabricating a theft story so he can pay its value and keep it. To foreclose that possibility, even a *shomer* who voluntarily admits liability must swear that the object is genuinely no longer with him.

### Rav Ashi Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי דְּקַיְימָא לַן זֶה נִשְׁבָּע שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ וְזֶה נִשְׁבָּע כַּמָּה שָׁוֶה – הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִי נִשְׁבָּע תְּחִלָּה? מִי שֶׁהַפִּקָּדוֹן אֶצְלוֹ. שֶׁמָּא יִשָּׁבַע זֶה, וְיוֹצִיא הַלָּה אֶת הַפִּקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And now that Rav Ashi says that we maintain** that two oaths are taken in this case, as **this** party, the creditor, **takes an oath that** the collateral **is not in his possession, and that** party, the debtor, **takes an oath** concerning **how much** the collateral **was worth, this** is what the mishna **is saying: Who takes an oath first? The one in whose** possession **the deposit** had been located, i.e., the creditor, first takes an oath that the collateral is not in his possession, **lest this** party, the debtor, **take an oath and** then **the other** party, the creditor, **produce the deposit** and prove the oath false.

With *Rav Ashi*'s principle established, the *Gemara* revisits the *mashkon* case. In that scenario, I am the *shomer* of Charlie's watch. I am saying the watch was lost and that I must deduct its value from the loan — meaning I am in the position of a *shomer* who accepts liability for the object. Applying *Rav Ashi*: before any further oath or collection occurs, I must first swear that I no longer have the watch. This addresses the fear that I might be holding onto it.

Once I have sworn I no longer possess the watch, the concern evaporates: I cannot later "produce" the watch to demonstrate that Charlie's oath was false, because I have already sworn under oath that it is not in my possession. Charlie's oath is therefore protected.

### Sequential Oaths

This is how *Rav Ashi* would read the *mishna*: *mi nishba techila? mi she-ha-pikadon etzlo* — who takes the oath *first*? I do — I swear the collateral is no longer in my possession. Only after that does Charlie take his oath about the watch's value. The ordering is crucial: if Charlie were to go first and swear the watch was worth seventy-five, the concern would remain that I could produce it and expose him. But once I have first sworn it is gone, that concern is eliminated, and Charlie may safely take his own oath.

The result under *Rav Ashi* is importantly different from the simple reading of the *mishna*. In the simple reading, I take the oath and collect the full fifty dollars, leaving Charlie worse off. Under *Rav Ashi*, both parties take oaths in sequence — I first, then Charlie — and Charlie is then able to stand by his claim that the watch was worth seventy-five and pay only twenty-five. The justice of the outcome is restored: Charlie is protected not by stripping him of his money, but by ensuring that the sequence of oaths renders his own *shvua* immune to later contradiction.
