# Shevuot 43 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oath to Minor/Heir

### Mishna Overview

The daf begins in the middle of 42b, with the *mishna* that will carry us into 43. The *mishna* states that one does not take a *shvua* on the basis of a claim made by a *cheresh shoteh ve'katan* — a deaf-mute, a mentally incompetent person, or a minor — against another party. Yet the very same *mishna* then states that one is *nishba'in lekatan*, that one does take an oath *to* a *katan*. The apparent contradiction demands resolution: which is it?

### R. Elazar ben Yaakov

The first answer offered is that the *mishna*'s phrase *nishba'in lekatan* should not be read literally as referring to a minor at all. Rather, it refers to taking an oath to an heir — specifically, when the son of a deceased creditor comes to collect a debt owed to his father. The question then becomes: if that heir comes with a *bari* — a definite claim that you certainly owe his father money — and you are *modeh bemiktzas*, admitting to part of the debt, do you take an oath? One might argue that you should not, on the grounds of *migo*: since you feel no particular moral relationship to the son (as opposed to the father who lent you the money), you could just as easily have denied the debt entirely. Since you could have been a *kofer hakol*, the partial admission should function like *meshiv aveida* — a voluntary gift — and you should be exempt.

This is precisely the position of *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov*, and the *Gemara* considers interpreting our *mishna* in accordance with his view.

The *Gemara* challenges this reading: the earlier part of the *mishna* already addresses the heir case explicitly, teaching *maneh l'aba beyadcha, ein lecha beyadi ella chamishin dinar — patur mipney meshiv aveida*. If someone says "you owe my father a hundred dollars" and you reply "I only owe fifty," you are exempt, because the partial admission is treated as returning a lost object. Why, then, does the later clause need to introduce a different rule?

The answer lies in a distinction. In the earlier clause, the heir comes with a *safeik* — a tentative claim, perhaps "I think you owe my father money." In that case, since the claim is both from an heir and uncertain, everyone agrees that any admission functions as *meshiv aveida* and there is no *shvua*. But in the later clause, the heir comes with a *bari* — "I know for certain you owe my father" — and it is specifically in that case that *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov* holds you are not exempt by *migo*, and therefore must take a *shvuat modeh bemiktzas*. This reconciles the two parts of the *mishna*.

### Shmuel's Approach

*Shmuel* offers an entirely different and remarkably elegant reading of the *mishna*. He argues that *nishba'in lekatan* does not mean responding to a claim made *by* a *katan*, but rather taking an oath when you are coming to *collect from* a *katan* — *lifra minichsei katan*. Similarly, *lehekdesh* means *lifra minichsei hekdesh* — collecting from sanctified property.

Under Shmuel's reading, the case is not one of *modeh bemiktzas* at all. Rather: you hold a *shtar* proving that the deceased father owed you money, and you now seek to collect from the minor orphan heir. The minor cannot mount a proper defense — he lacks the capacity to examine witnesses, present counterclaims, or argue effectively. Therefore, even though all the evidence is in your favor, you must first take a *shvua* that the debt is genuinely still owed before collecting.

The case of *hekdesh* is analogous. The scenario the *Gemara* envisions is not that *hekdesh* itself borrowed money, but rather that David owed you money, you held a prior lien on his property, and David then consecrated that property to *hekdesh*. Since your lien predates the consecration, you may seize the property — and at the moment of seizure, retroactively, it is as though it never became *hekdesh*. Nevertheless, before collecting in this way, you must take a *shvua* that David has not already repaid the debt.

### Heirs & Orphans

The *Gemara* notes that the obligation to take a *shvua* when collecting from *yetomim* is already taught explicitly in a forthcoming *mishna* — *lo yifra ela bishvua*. Why does the present *mishna* need to say it again? The *Gemara* answers that the repetition conveys the *chiddush* of *Abaye Kashisha*, the elder Abaye, who taught: *yetomim she'amru gedolim* — when the *mishna* says you must swear before collecting from heirs, this applies even if the heirs are adults, and needless to say when they are minors.

One might have thought that if the heirs are adults, they can mount a full defense and no special protection is needed. The *chiddush* is that since the adult heirs were not themselves party to the original loan — it was their father who borrowed — they may lack crucial information about what transpired. Therefore the obligation to take a *shvua* before collecting applies regardless of the heirs' age. This applies both to the *shvua* itself and to the rule that one collects only from *zivoris*, third-grade land, even from adult heirs.

### Hekdesh Collection

Returning to the case of collecting from *hekdesh*: the *Gemara* observes that this too is already taught in the forthcoming *mishna* regarding *nechasim meshubadim* — encumbered property — so why does the present *mishna* need to state it separately? The answer is that the two cases are distinct and each needed to be said independently. One might have thought that the requirement to take a *shvua* applies only when a lay person (*hedyot*) holds the lien and might be defrauded, but that no one would scheme (*knunya*) to cheat out *hekdesh*. Therefore the *mishna* teaches that we do require a *shvua* even when *hekdesh* is the party with the lien, since a person who is alive and healthy may indeed conspire to defraud *hekdesh* and receive a kickback.

However, the *Gemara* cites *Rav Huna*, who rules that a *shechiv mera* — a person on his deathbed — who consecrates all his property and then adds "by the way, I owe Reuven a hundred dollars which is already liened and not included," is believed. The reasoning is *chazaka ein adam oseh knunya al hekdesh* — we may rely on the presumption that a person will not scheme to defraud *hekdesh*. Does this not contradict the requirement of a *shvua*?

The resolution distinguishes between the two cases. By a *shechiv mera*, two factors combine: first, one is reluctant to engage in such schemes on one's deathbed; second, and perhaps more importantly, the dying person himself will not live to receive any kickback — it is his children who benefit, a step sufficiently removed that we do not attribute such scheming to him. By contrast, a healthy person (*bari*) who has many years ahead of him and stands to personally benefit from the scheme — there we are certainly concerned, and the *shvua* is required.

### Encumbered Property

The case of *nechasim meshubadim* — encumbered property — involves a scenario where David owes money to two creditors: you and Charlie. You hold a prior lien, and you seek to collect from David's property. But since Charlie also has a claim on that same property, your collecting from it affects Charlie as well. The concern is that David and you might conspire: David will not contest your claim, you will seize the property as though under a prior lien — even if that lien is fictitious — and later return part of the proceeds to David under the table, leaving Charlie unable to collect. This conspiracy is called a *knunya*.

Because a third party (Charlie) stands to lose when the lienholding creditor seizes the property, David's failure to contest your claim is insufficient protection for Charlie. You must therefore take a *shvua* to forestall any such collusion. The same applies when it is *hekdesh* rather than a private individual that holds the parallel lien, as the *Gemara* establishes.

## Excluded Objects

### Five Exclusions

The *mishna* now introduces the following categories: *v'eilu devarim she'ein nishba'in aleihen* — these are the objects regarding which no oath is taken. The list comprises slaves (*avadim*), writs of debt (*shtaros*), land (*karka*), and sanctified objects (*hekdesh*). These four categories are excluded from the oath of *modeh bemiktzas*, from the oath of a single witness (*ed echad*), and — as the *mishna* goes on to specify — from several other *halachic* obligations as well.

The first additional exclusion concerns *tashlumei kefel* — double payment for theft. Normally, a burglar pays *kefel*, double the value of the stolen object. If he then slaughters or sells the stolen animal, he pays four or five times its value. All four categories are exempt from *kefel*, and consequently from the four-and-five payment as well, since once *kefel* is eliminated, the incremental payment above *kefel* falls away automatically.

The *mishna* then addresses the laws of *shomrim* — guardians. *Shomer chinam eino nishba, nosei sachar eino meshalem* — an unpaid guardian (*shomer chinam*) need not take an oath regarding these objects, and a paid guardian (*shomer sachar*) need not pay if something happens to them.

It is worth pausing on the classic *shvuat shomrim*. This oath is not a response to an adversarial claim of lying; rather, it functions as corroboration when the guardian reports what befell the object. The owner asks what happened, the guardian explains there was a theft or loss, and the *Torah* requires an oath to substantiate that truthful account. One might therefore have thought that this category of oath — which is not about disputed amounts but about disputed facts — might apply even to the excluded objects. The *mishna* teaches otherwise: these objects are exempt from the *shomrim* oath as well. An unpaid guardian simply asserts what happened and is exempt; a paid guardian who would normally be liable for theft or loss (and who admits as much) is nonetheless exempt from payment when the object falls into one of these categories.

An important question raised by the *Rishonim* but left unresolved explicitly in the *mishna* is whether a *shomer* of these objects bears any *chiyuv shemira* — any legal obligation of care — at all. The exemptions from oath and payment do not necessarily speak to whether the guardian incurred any duty in the first place. Furthermore, several *Rishonim* hold that *poshea hi k'mazik* — negligence is legally equivalent to direct damage. If so, a negligent guardian might be liable even for these excluded objects, not under the laws of *shomrim* but as a tortfeasor. Whether the exclusion of the *shomrim* framework also eliminates the underlying duty of care remains an open question not explicitly addressed in the *mishna*.

### R. Shimon's Kodashim

*Rabbi Shimon* draws a distinction within the category of *kodashim*: *kodashim she-chayav be-achrayutan nishba'in aleihem, ve-she-eino chayav be-achrayutan ein nishba'in aleihem* — sanctified objects for which one bears a replacement obligation are subject to the oath, while those for which one bears no such obligation are not.

The case is this: Charlie takes a *neder* — a personal vow — to bring two *olot*, two burnt offerings. He then designates two sheep for this purpose and gives them to Dov to watch. Dov claims he only received one. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, Dov must take a *shvuat modeh bemiktzas*. Why? Because Charlie's *neder* obligated him personally to bring two *olot*; if those sheep are lost, Charlie must replace them out of his own pocket. The sheep are therefore not merely *hekdesh* from Charlie's perspective — they represent a real financial exposure. Their loss *goremes le-mamon*, it causes a monetary loss. Such sanctified objects are called *davar ha-gorem le-mamon*, and *Rabbi Shimon* treats them as property rather than as *hekdesh* for purposes of the oath requirement.

This stands in contrast to a case where someone merely sanctified an animal without a personal vow: if that animal is lost, there is no obligation to replace it, and it is treated as pure *hekdesh*, exempt from the oath.

### R. Meir Laden Vines

*Rabbi Meir* introduces a further nuance regarding *karka*. The *mishna* states: *yesh devarim she-hen ke-karka ve-einam ke-karka* — there are things that are like land yet are not treated as land *halachically*, and the *chachamim* do not agree with him. His example: if Charlie says "I gave you ten vines laden with grapes to watch" (*esser gefanim te'unot*), and Dov responds "you only gave me five," *Rabbi Meir* holds that Dov must take an oath. The *chachamim* respond: anything attached to the ground (*kol ha-mechubar le-karka*) has the status of *karka* and is excluded.

A surface reading of *Rabbi Meir*'s position suggests that he holds attachment to the ground confers no *karka* status on growing things at all. But the *Gemara* limits his view considerably. *Rav Yosef* citing *Rabbi Chanina* explains: normally *Rabbi Meir* agrees that things attached to the ground are treated as *karka*. The dispute here is specifically because the grapes are *omdot libatzir* — ready for harvest. *Rabbi Meir* holds that grapes at the point of harvest are *k'vatzur dami* — regarded as if already detached and no longer part of the land. The *chachamim* hold they remain *ke-karka* until actually harvested.

## Quantifiability

### Mishna Rule

The *mishna* states: *ein nishba'in ela al davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan* — one takes a *shvuat modeh bemiktzas* only when the disputed object is quantifiable in measure, weight, or number.

### House Full Case

The *mishna*'s example of a non-quantifiable claim: *bayit malei masarti lach* — "I gave you a house full of potatoes." The respondent replies: *ela mah she-inhata atah notel* — "I don't know how much you gave me; take back whatever is here." Since neither the claim nor the response is expressed in any unit of measure, there is no *shvuat modeh bemiktzas*.

By contrast: *zeh omer ad ha-ziz ve-zeh omer ad ha-chalon* — one says the house was filled up to the protruding beam, the other says only up to the windowsill — this is *chayav*, because while no explicit unit is used, the positions of the beam and the window can be measured, and the disagreement is quantifiable.

*Abaye* proposes that the *mishna*'s distinction turns on whether the claim references *this* house specifically. If Charlie says *bayit zeh malei* — "this house, full" — its dimensions can be measured and the claim becomes quantifiable. The generic *bayit malei* is not.

*Rava* objects: if *bayit zeh malei* were considered quantifiable and therefore *chayav*, the *mishna* should have made that its *chiddush* — teaching that even *bayit malei* can suffice when a specific house is named. The fact that the *mishna* instead contrasts *bayit malei* with the *ad ha-ziz / ad ha-chalon* case indicates that even *bayit zeh malei* is insufficient. The reason is that *Rava* requires not merely that the *difference* between claim and response be quantifiable, but that each be articulated in affirmatively quantifiable terms. Saying "this house full" remains too imprecise, lacking any explicit reference to a unit or boundary point. Saying "up to the beam" or "up to the window" crosses that threshold, even without invoking units, because it specifies an objective boundary from which a measurement can be taken.

## Excluded Objects…

### Kelal-Prat-Kelal

The *Gemara* now derives the exclusion of these categories from the Biblical text using the hermeneutic of *kelal-prat-kelal* — general-particular-general. The verse *al kol devar pesha* ("on any matter of negligence") is a *kelal*; *al shor al chamor al seh al salmah* ("on ox, donkey, sheep, and garment") is a *prat*; *al kol aveidah* ("on any lost object") is a return to the *kelal*. When a *kelal-prat-kelal* structure appears, one derives only cases similar to the particulars. The particular cases — ox, donkey, sheep, garment — are all movable objects (*meitaltel*) with intrinsic value (*gufo mamon*). Therefore the rule applies only to *davar ha-metaltel ve-gufo mamon*: movable objects of intrinsic value. This same *parsha* of the *Torah* governs the oath of *modeh bemiktzas*, the obligations of *shomrim*, and the double payment of *kefel*.

### Kefel Exclusion

From this *kelal-prat-kelal*, the *Gemara* derives the exclusions from *kefel*. Land (*karka*) is excluded because it is not movable. Slaves (*avadim*) are excluded because they are legally equated to land (*hukshu le-karka'os*). *Shtarot* — writs — are excluded because, although they are physically movable, they have no intrinsic monetary value (*ein gufan mamon*); they merely represent value. *Hekdesh* is excluded because the verse says *ki yitein ish el re'ehu* — one person gives to his *neighbor*, specifically a lay person, not consecrated property.

Regarding the four-and-five payment: *tashlumei arba va-chamisha amar Rachmana, ve-lo tashlumei shlosha ve-arba'a* — the *Torah* specified four and five in the context where *kefel* already applies. Once *kefel* is eliminated for these categories, the incremental payments of four and five are automatically eliminated as well, since those amounts presuppose the *kefel* baseline.

### Shomer Obligations

The same *kelal-prat-kelal* structure appears in the *parsha* of *shomer chinam*: *ki yitein ish el re'ehu* is the *kelal*; *kesef o keilim* ("silver or vessels") is the *prat*; *lishmor* ("to watch") functions as the return to the *kelal*. The derivation excludes the identical categories: land (not movable), slaves (equated to land), *shtarot* (movable but no intrinsic value), and *hekdesh* (*re'ehu ketiv* — it says "neighbor," not consecrated property).

The *shomer sachar* exclusion is derived from a parallel *kelal-prat-kelal* in the *parsha* of the paid guardian: *chamor o shor o seh* is the *prat*; *ve-chol behemah lishmor* is the closing *kelal*. The *Gemara* does not belabor the derivation, noting that the structure and conclusions are identical. *Hekdesh* is again excluded by *re'ehu ketiv*.

As noted, the question of whether a *shomer* retains any *chiyuv shemira* — an underlying duty of care — for these objects even after the formal exemptions apply is not explicitly resolved. If *poshea hi k'mazik* — if negligence functions as direct damage — then a *shomer* who is genuinely negligent with such objects might still face liability as a *mazik* rather than as a *shomer*, though this depends on whether the acceptance of the *shomer* role generates any duty of care in the first place.

## Quantifiability…

### Rava's Standard

*Rava*'s position, introduced above in response to *Abaye*, is now stated as the governing principle: *l'olam eino chayav ad she-yit-anennu be-davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan, ve-yodeh lo be-davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan* — one is never liable for a *shvuat modeh bemiktzas* unless both the claim is articulated in quantifiable terms and the admission is likewise articulated in quantifiable terms. It is not enough that the *difference* between claim and response is quantifiable; the claim itself and the response itself must each be expressible in units of measure, weight, or number.

The implication for the *bayit malei* case is that even if one could in principle calculate the volume of a house, the claimant who says merely "a house full" has not articulated a quantifiable claim. One who says "up to the beam" or "up to the window" has done so — not in conventional units, but by specifying a measurable boundary. *Rava* holds that this distinction is meaningful: the former is insufficiently precise to anchor a *shvua*, the latter is not.

### Baraita Examples

A *baraita* supports *Rava*'s position with a series of cases. First: "I gave you a gallon (*kor*) of grain to watch" — the other responds "you never gave me any grain" — *patur*, since the denier is a *kofer hakol*. Second: "I gave you a large *menorah* to watch" — the other responds "you only gave me a small *menorah*" — *patur*. Third: "I gave you a large belt (*azur*) to watch" — the other responds "you only gave me a small belt" — *patur*.

Why is the *menorah* case exempt from a *shvua*? Not only is "large" and "small" insufficiently quantified, but more fundamentally, a large *menorah* and a small *menorah* are different objects. *Ma she-ta'ano lo hoda lo u-ma she-hoda lo lo ta'ano* — what was claimed was not conceded, and what was conceded was not claimed. *Modeh bemiktzas* requires that the admission be to a partial quantity of the very thing claimed. If someone says "I gave you this large table" and you respond "I have only a small table," you are not conceding to part of the claim — you are describing an entirely different object. The *kefel* payment by a *ganav* turns on the same structure: the stolen object must be a subset of what was claimed.

Contrast the exempt cases with those that generate a *shvua*: "I gave you a gallon (*kor*) of grain" — "I only have a half-gallon (*letach*)" — *chayav*. "I gave you a ten-pound *menorah*" — "I only have a five-pound *menorah*" — *chayav*.

### Dual Quantifiability

The *baraita* closes with an explicit formulation of the principle: *kelalo shel davar: l'olam eino chayav ad she-yit-anennu be-davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan, ve-yodeh lo be-davar she-be-mida u-ve-mishkal u-ve-minyan* — the general rule is that one is not liable unless both the claim and the response are quantifiable. The *Gemara* notes that this explicit statement is included specifically to rule out *bayit zeh malei* — to emphasize that even when a specific house is named, making the dimensions in principle measurable, the claim must be articulated in explicitly quantifiable terms. *Bayit zeh malei* lacks the necessary specificity.

### Menorah Subset

The *menorah* case — where a ten-pound *menorah* is claimed and a five-pound *menorah* is admitted — naturally raises the question: is this not also a case of two different objects? *Ma she-ta'ano lo hoda lo* — just as a large *menorah* and a small *menorah* are different, so too a ten-pound and a five-pound *menorah* would seem to be different objects.

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak* initially proposes that the case involves a *menorah shel chuliyot* — a modular *menorah* assembled from components that can be added or removed. A five-pound *menorah* assembled from fewer components is therefore literally a subset of a ten-pound *menorah* assembled from more components, and the admission is to part of the very thing claimed.

The *Gemara* challenges this: if the principle is that modular, addable objects create a subset relationship, why does the *baraita* not teach a similar case for belts, which can be expanded by adding links? The *Gemara* replies that the *baraita* simply did not enumerate every applicable case (*d'laifi lo kasani*), and similarly, one could say the *menorah shel chuliyot* is not the only possible explanation.

*Rav Papa bar Manmiyo* therefore offers a different account. The key is that a ten-pound *menorah* can physically be converted into a five-pound *menorah* by scraping down and thinning out the metal. A shorter *menorah* cannot be produced from a taller one without destroying its integrity — cutting off the base or the top renders it non-functional. But scraping the exterior to reduce weight preserves the object's form and function. Therefore, a five-pound *menorah* is indeed a potential subset of a ten-pound one, since the latter can be transformed into the former. This conversion relationship is what justifies treating the admission as *modeh bemiktzas* rather than as a claim about a different object.

## Mashkon Cases

### Mashkon Setup

The *mishna* now presents a series of cases involving *mashkon* — collateral. The scenario is: I lend Charlie a hundred dollars (*sela*), and Charlie gives me his watch as collateral. Subsequently the watch is lost — meaning I, as the *shomer* of the collateral, must bear its loss and reimburse Charlie. A dispute arises between us about the watch's value and what, if anything, Charlie still owes me. The *mishna* works through four permutations.

### Full Denial

First case: I say to Charlie, "I lent you a hundred dollars and the watch was worth only fifty (*shekel* = half a *sela*); you still owe me fifty." Charlie responds: "You lent me a hundred dollars and the watch was worth a hundred — I owe you nothing." Charlie is denying my entire claim against him, making him a *kofer hakol*. The *halacha*: *patur* — no oath is required.

### Partial Admission

Second case: I say to Charlie, "I lent you a hundred dollars and the watch was worth fifty; you still owe me fifty." Charlie responds: "You lent me a hundred dollars, but the watch was worth seventy-five (*shlosha dinarim* = three quarters of a *sela*); I owe you only twenty-five, not fifty." Charlie is conceding that he owes something but disputes the amount — this is *modeh bemiktzas*, and he is *chayav* to take an oath.

### Reversed Denial

Third case: Charlie reverses the roles. He says: "That watch was worth two hundred dollars (*shtayim*). You owe me a hundred dollars more than the loan." I respond: "No — the watch was worth exactly a hundred dollars, equal to the loan; we're even, you owe me nothing." I am the *kofer hakol* in this configuration. *Patur* — no oath required.

### Reversed Partial

Fourth case: Charlie says the watch was worth two hundred dollars, so I owe him a hundred. I respond: "The watch was worth only a hundred and twenty-five (*chamishah dinarim* = five quarters of a *sela*); I owe you twenty-five, not a hundred." I am now the *modeh bemiktzas* — admitting I owe something but disputing the amount. *Chayav* — I take an oath.

### Unusual Oath Rule

These first four cases, while instructive, follow the standard rules of *modeh bemiktzas* in a straightforward way. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* lies in the second case, the one where Charlie should logically be taking the oath. I claim he owes me fifty; he says he owes only twenty-five — he is the *modeh bemiktzas*. Yet the *mishna* rules: *u-mi nishba? mi she-ha-pikadon etzlo* — who takes the oath? The one who is holding the collateral — meaning, *I* take the oath, not Charlie. The *Gemara* will explain why.

## Mashkon Oath Reasoning

### False Oath Risk

Why does the *mishna* transfer the oath from Charlie to me? *Shema yishava zeh ve-yotzi hallah et ha-pikadon* — lest Charlie take the oath that the watch was worth seventy-five dollars and only twenty-five is owed, and then I produce the watch and demonstrate it was actually worth only fifty.

The concern is subtle and important. We are not primarily worried that Charlie is lying. Valuation of an object is inherently subjective, and each party has a natural (and perhaps unconscious) bias: the owner of lost collateral tends to recall it as more valuable; the party who owes money has reason to believe it was worth less. Charlie might take his oath in complete good faith, and yet when the watch reappears it could turn out to be worth only fifty dollars, rendering him technically a false swearer despite his sincerity. The *halacha* is concerned about protecting a person from being shown to have taken a false oath, even innocently.

### Lender Takes Oath

To protect Charlie from this predicament, the rabbis transfer the oath to me. I swear — to what? The implication is that I swear the watch was indeed worth only fifty, and on the basis of that oath I collect the full fifty dollars that I claimed. This outcome seems harsh to Charlie: not only is he denied the benefit of his own oath, but he must now pay the full amount I claimed rather than the twenty-five he was prepared to acknowledge. The protection of Charlie's integrity comes at the cost of his money.

### Rav Ashi Principle

Before accepting this outcome as final, the *Gemara* introduces a principle stated by *Rav Ashi* in a different context in *Bava Metzia*. The case is: I asked David to watch my watch. David comes to me and says, "Something happened to it — it was stolen — but I accept liability and will reimburse you." One might think David need say nothing further. He is admitting liability; what more could be required?

*Rav Ashi* holds that David must nonetheless swear *she'eina birshuto* — that the object is no longer in his possession. Why? Because David may have coveted the watch all along, and is now fabricating a theft story so he can pay its value and keep it. To foreclose that possibility, even a *shomer* who voluntarily admits liability must swear that the object is genuinely no longer with him.

### Rav Ashi Solution

With *Rav Ashi*'s principle established, the *Gemara* revisits the *mashkon* case. In that scenario, I am the *shomer* of Charlie's watch. I am saying the watch was lost and that I must deduct its value from the loan — meaning I am in the position of a *shomer* who accepts liability for the object. Applying *Rav Ashi*: before any further oath or collection occurs, I must first swear that I no longer have the watch. This addresses the fear that I might be holding onto it.

Once I have sworn I no longer possess the watch, the concern evaporates: I cannot later "produce" the watch to demonstrate that Charlie's oath was false, because I have already sworn under oath that it is not in my possession. Charlie's oath is therefore protected.

### Sequential Oaths

This is how *Rav Ashi* would read the *mishna*: *mi nishba techila? mi she-ha-pikadon etzlo* — who takes the oath *first*? I do — I swear the collateral is no longer in my possession. Only after that does Charlie take his oath about the watch's value. The ordering is crucial: if Charlie were to go first and swear the watch was worth seventy-five, the concern would remain that I could produce it and expose him. But once I have first sworn it is gone, that concern is eliminated, and Charlie may safely take his own oath.

The result under *Rav Ashi* is importantly different from the simple reading of the *mishna*. In the simple reading, I take the oath and collect the full fifty dollars, leaving Charlie worse off. Under *Rav Ashi*, both parties take oaths in sequence — I first, then Charlie — and Charlie is then able to stand by his claim that the watch was worth seventy-five and pay only twenty-five. The justice of the outcome is restored: Charlie is protected not by stripping him of his money, but by ensuring that the sequence of oaths renders his own *shvua* immune to later contradiction.
