# Bava Kamma 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shinuy & Acquisition

### Mari Shemeh Lav

The Gemara opens by rejecting a proposed *shinuy hashem* — a change of name — in the case of a stolen beam incorporated into a roof. The objection is *mari shemeh lav*: simply calling something by a new name does not constitute a genuine change if the object itself remains substantively the same. The Gemara supports this from a *baraita* in Yechezkel regarding the construction of the third *Beit HaMikdash*, where the term *uvim* (groups or structures) is explained as *marshin* — beams. Even when a beam serves as part of a roof, it is still identified as a beam. The name has not truly changed.

This stands in contrast to a piece of leather fashioned into shoes: there, looking at the object, one would say "those are shoes," not "that is a piece of leather." The *shinuy hashem* is genuine. But looking up at a roof, one might readily say, "there's a 2x4" — the beam hasn't shed its identity. So there is no real *shinuy hashem* in the beam case, and the objection from that case falls away.

### Shinuy Hachozer

Rabbi Zeira offers a different approach. His principle is: *shinnuy hachozer livriyato beshinnuy hashem lo havei shinnuy* — a reversible *shinuy hashem* is not a genuine *shinuy* at all. If the transformation can be undone, it lacks the halakhic weight needed to effect acquisition.

Applied to the beam case: even granting that there is some *shinuy hashem*, since the beam could in principle be removed from the roof and restored to its original state — it was never cut, trimmed, dyed, or sculpted, merely placed among other beams — the change is reversible, and therefore insufficient to be *koneh*. This is the position that supports Rav Yosef's broader claim that without a genuine, irreversible *shinuy*, acquisition through the thief cannot occur. The only reason the thief is not required to physically dismantle the roof and return the beam is *takanat hashavim* — the rabbinic ordinance enacted to encourage penitent thieves to make restitution without facing impossibly burdensome demands. Without that ordinance, the reversibility of the change would require the beam's actual return.

### Shoes & Trays

Rabbi Zeira's principle can be sharpened by contrast with cases of irreversible *shinuy ma'aseh*. If stolen leather has been fashioned into shoes, the former owner cannot demand "give me back my leather" — the object has undergone a genuine *shinuy ma'aseh* and a genuine *shinuy hashem*. The leather is now shoes; the transformation is complete and irreversible. Similarly with a tray that has been warped, stitched, or otherwise worked: if the change is a *shinuy ma'aseh*, the thief acquires through it. The principle thus operates with precision: a mere *shinuy hashem* that is reversible does not confer acquisition; an irreversible *shinuy ma'aseh* does.

### Pipe Case

The Gemara now challenges the premise that a reversible *shinuy hashem* does not count as a *shinuy*, while an irreversible one does. The challenge comes from the law of *mayim she'uvim* — drawn water — which invalidates a *mikvah*. The case involves a wooden pipe: a block of wood is hollowed out to form a pipe and then affixed to the ground, channeling rainwater into a pit that serves as a *mikvah*. The ruling in a *baraita* is as follows: if one first hollowed out the wood (creating the pipe as a *kli*, a vessel) and then affixed it to the ground, the *mikvah* is invalid because the water passed through a vessel. But if one first affixed the block of wood to the ground and then hollowed it out, the *mikvah* is valid.

The Gemara's challenge: if we say that an irreversible *shinuy hashem* (the wood becoming a pipe, which is not reversible) constitutes a genuine *shinuy*, then even in the second case — where the wood was affixed and then hollowed — there has been an irreversible *shinuy hashem*, and it should still invalidate the *mikvah*. Why does the second case produce a valid *mikvah*?

The Gemara attempts to deflect: the laws of *mayim she'uvim* are themselves rabbinic, so perhaps the *shinuy* here only matters in a rabbinic context. But this response immediately invites a follow-up challenge: if it is all *de-rabbanan*, why are we making any distinction at all? Either the rabbinic rule applies in both cases, or it applies in neither. Why is the first case problematic while the second is not?

### Kli Presence

The Gemara resolves the problem by distinguishing based on the presence of a *kli*. The relevant question is not merely whether a *shinuy hashem* occurred, but whether the object had the status of a *kli* at the moment it was in its detached (*talush*) state, before being affixed to the ground. In the first case — hollow it out, then affix it — the wood was a *kli* (a pipe) while it was still detached. That *kli*-status in its *talush* state is what triggers the rabbinic stringency. In the second case — affix it, then hollow it out — the wood was still a plain block when it was detached and affixed; it only became a pipe-shaped form after being fixed in the ground. Since it was not a *kli* in its *talush* state, the rabbinic strictness does not apply.

This resolution preserves the underlying framework: the Gemara is not abandoning Rabbi Zeira's position, nor is it abandoning the distinction between reversible and irreversible *shinuy*. The *mikvah* case is governed by an independent rabbinic criterion — *kli*-status at the time of detachment — that does not disturb the core *shinuy* analysis. Rav Yosef's position that straight *yeush* without any *shinuy* does not confer acquisition therefore remains intact.

## Rav Yosef Defense

### Yeush Alone

With the *shinuy* discussion concluded, the Gemara returns to the core position requiring defense: Rav Yosef holds that *yeush* alone — the owner's despair of recovering his stolen property, without any accompanying *shinuy* — does not render the thief *koneh*. Every challenge to this position has, to this point, involved a case where some form of *shinuy* was also present, whether a *shinuy hashem*, a *shinuy ma'aseh*, or a reversible variant thereof. Rav Yosef can consistently respond: you are not challenging me on a pure *yeush* case; there was always a *shinuy* involved. The core claim — that *yeush* by itself has no effect — has not yet been directly refuted.

### Ganav Challenge

A challenge is now raised from a *baraita*: a *ganav*, a *gazlan*, or one who takes by force — if they consecrate an animal (*hikdisho hekdesh*), the *hekdesh* takes effect; if they separate *terumah*, the *terumah* is valid; if they separate *ma'aser*, the *ma'aser* is valid. This appears to demonstrate that even without an obvious *shinuy*, the thief has acquired the object sufficiently to effect these religious-legal changes. If *yeush* alone does not confer acquisition, how can the thief's *hekdesh* or *terumah* be valid?

### Shinuy Hashem Answer

Rav Yosef's response: there is in fact a *shinuy hashem* present in every one of these cases. When stolen wheat was *tevel* (untithed produce with no separated portions) and the thief separates *terumah*, the object is now called *terumah* — a fundamentally different legal category with its own body of laws. When stolen *chulin* is consecrated as *hekdesh*, it is no longer *chulin* — it carries an entirely different name and legal status. In each case, the object before and after bears a different name. This is precisely a *shinuy hashem*, and Rav Yosef's position is that *yeush* combined with *shinuy hashem* — even one that is arguably reversible — may suffice to confer acquisition, depending on the details. What he insists upon is that *yeush* alone, without any *shinuy* whatsoever, accomplishes nothing.

One might note, as indeed the question arose in the shiur, that using a stolen object for a *mitzvah* raises its own difficulty — for a stolen species, for instance, is invalid for use in a *mitzvah*. The Gemara returns to this matter subsequently.

## Two Yonatan Teachings

### Shinuy Koneh

Rav Chisda transmits a teaching in the name of Yonatan: how do we know that *shinuy* is *koneh* — that a change of form effects acquisition? From the verse (*Vayikra* 5:23): *vehishiv et hagezeilah asher gazal* — "he shall return the stolen object which he stole." The phrase *asher gazal* seems superfluous; of course the returned object is the one he stole, not one he didn't steal. The *drasha* is: *im ke'ein shegazal yachazir ve'im lav damim be'alma ba'ei shilumei* — if it is in the same state as when he stole it, he returns the object itself; if not — because it has undergone a *shinuy* — he pays its monetary value. The requirement to return the *gezeilah* applies only when it remains *asher gazal*, in its original form. If it has changed, he owes *damim*, implying that the *shinuy* has effected acquisition and he can no longer return the object itself.

One might then object: if *asher gazal* is doing this work, there is another law that also needs this phrase — the rule that one who confesses a theft and makes voluntary restitution must pay the *keren* plus a *chomesh* (an additional fifth), but if the *thief's heir* makes restitution, no *chomesh* is required. This too is derived from *asher gazal*: the obligation to add a *chomesh* falls on the original thief — *al gezeilo shelo* — not on his heirs. Since one phrase cannot serve both purposes, we might think we must choose. The Gemara resolves this by demonstrating that both *drashos* can coexist: the Torah could have written simply *vehishiv et hagezeilah* (without *asher gazal*) if only the *chomesh* exemption were needed, or it could have written *vehishiv et hagezeilah asher gazal* without separately specifying *gezeilah*. The redundancy of having both allows us to learn both laws — *shma mina tarti*.

### Shinuy Lo Koneh

Immediately thereafter, the Gemara presents a second tradition, also attributed to Rav Chisda in the name of Yonatan — but reaching the opposite conclusion. *Menayin leshinuy she'eino koneh* — from where do we know that *shinuy* does *not* confer acquisition? From the same verse: *vehishiv et hagezeilah* — he must return the *gezeilah* in all circumstances, regardless of what state it is in. *Shinuy* does not help; he still owes the object.

And what then is *asher gazal* doing? It is entirely devoted to the *chomesh* exemption for heirs — *al gezeilo shelo hu shemosif chomesh* — and carries no implication about the state of the object. The same verse, the same phrase, yields diametrically opposed conclusions in the two versions of this teaching. The fundamental *machloket* between Rav Yosef and Rava — does *yeush*, does *shinuy*, effect acquisition? — remains unresolved even at the level of these *Amoraic* teachings, and pointedly, both versions derive from the selfsame *pasuk*.

## Ulla & Rava

### Yeush No Effect

Ulla now offers a proof that *yeush* does not work — supporting Rav Yosef's position. The verse in Malachi (1:13) states: *vahasaitem gazul ve'et hapise'ach ve'et hacholeh* — you bring to God what is stolen, lame, or sick. God rejects such offerings. Ulla derives: *gazul dumya de-pise'ach* — the case of a stolen animal is compared to a lame one. Just as a *pise'ach* — a lame animal — has no remedy as long as it remains lame (one cannot reattach a missing limb), and its disqualification is absolute, so too a stolen animal offered as a *korban* has no remedy. And this is true *velo shna lifnei yeush velo shna le'achar yeush* — whether before or after the owner's *yeush*, the stolen *korban* remains unacceptable. *Yeush* effects nothing.

### Rava Korban

Rava offers a parallel teaching: the verse (*Vayikra* 1:2) says *korbano* — his offering, implying the *korban* must be his own and not stolen: *korbano velo gazul*. The Gemara asks: when does this apply? If before *yeush* — obviously the stolen animal cannot be offered; no verse is needed to tell us that. Rather, the verse is addressing the case *after yeush*, and it is teaching that even then the stolen animal is invalid. *Shma mina yeiush lo kani* — we learn from here that *yeush* does not confer acquisition.

### Case Distinction

At this point the Gemara raises an internal tension. Earlier in the discussion, when Rava's position was being examined, the question of a stolen *korban* was addressed with a different answer — that Rava's statement *korbano velo gazul* was specifically about a case where one stole the animal *in its already-consecrated state* as a *korban*, not a case where *chulin* was stolen and then consecrated. That earlier resolution distinguished those two scenarios. Now Rava appears to be using the verse to prove that *yeush* has no effect in a general sense, which would seem to contradict his earlier position that *yeush* does effect acquisition in the case of stolen *chulin*.

The Gemara notes the tension: *shinuyei smi mikan* — has Rava retracted? Or, as Rav Papa clarifies: these are in fact different rulings from different authorities, and the two positions attributed to Rava may not both be his. At a minimum, we now have two clear teachings — Ulla's and the teaching associated with Rava — both establishing that *yeush* does not confer acquisition, in alignment with Rav Yosef's position. The earlier case involving stolen *chulin* made into a *korban* remains a matter the Gemara acknowledges it has not fully resolved, possibly distinguishing along the lines of whether the theft occurred before or after the animal's consecration, or whether different *Tannaitic* sources are in play.

## Arba V'Chamisha

### Exodus Pesach

The Gemara now turns to a different topic: the law of fourfold and fivefold restitution, *arba v'chamisha*. The *Mishnah* has already established that this obligation applies exclusively to a *shor* (ox) and a *seh* (sheep or goat) — not to other animals, such as a *chamor* (donkey). The question is: what is the biblical basis for this restriction?

The relevant verse is Shemot 21:37: *ki yignov ish shor o seh* — if a man steals an ox or a sheep — *u'tvacho o mecharo* — and slaughters or sells it — *chamishah vakar yishalem tachat hashor v'arba tzon tachat haseh* — he shall pay five cattle in place of the ox, and four sheep in place of the sheep. Two things are immediately notable: first, the verse specifies *shor o seh* explicitly in its opening clause; second, the closing clause specifies the payment again as *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh*, naming the animals a second time.

### Shabbat Parallel

A challenge presents itself: why not derive from the laws of *Shabbat* that *arba v'chamisha* should apply to all animals? The *pasuk* in Shemot 23:12 requires that one's *shor* and *chamor* rest on *Shabbat*; and the verse in Devarim (5:14) expands this — *l'ma'an yanuach shorecha vachamorecha* — so that all one's animals and dependents rest. Just as *Shabbat* rest is not restricted to an ox and a sheep but extends to all animals in one's possession, so too the fourfold and fivefold restitution should extend beyond *shor v'seh* to all animals. By analogy, stealing any animal and slaughtering or selling it should incur the enhanced penalty.

### Rava Double Mention

Rava responds: *kra shor v'seh shor v'seh shtei pe'amim — shor v'seh in, midi achrina lo* — the verse mentions *shor v'seh* twice, and this double mention constitutes a deliberate exclusion of all other animals. Mentioning *shor v'seh* at the beginning and *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh* at the end signals that the law is specifically and exclusively limited to these two species. The repetition is meaningful and restrictive.

The force of Rava's answer depends, however, on the claim that this repetition is truly surplus — that the verse could have been formulated differently and still conveyed the same information. If the verse had no choice but to name the animals twice for structural reasons, then the double mention teaches nothing and the *Shabbat* comparison would stand unchallenged. The Gemara therefore proceeds to examine whether either mention of *shor v'seh* could have been omitted.

### Pasuk Formulation

The Gemara works through the possibilities systematically. Could the Torah have omitted the second mention — *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh* — and written instead *chamishah vakar yishalem tachtav v'arba tzon tachtav*? The term *tachtav* ("in its place") would refer back to the *shor o seh* mentioned at the opening, and we would understand that five *bakar* are paid for the *shor* and four *tzon* for the *seh*. If so, the closing *tachat hashor v'tachat haseh* is surplus and could serve as the restrictive teaching.

The Gemara rejects this: had the verse written *tachtav* without specifying the animal, one might have thought the obligation was *nine*-fold — that for stealing either a *shor* or a *seh*, one owes *both* five *bakar* and four *tzon*, since *tachtav* in the singular refers ambiguously to whatever was stolen. The payment formula would then apply collectively: steal any one of these animals, pay nine. Therefore the verse must specify *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh* separately to make clear that the five *bakar* go for the *shor*-case and the four *tzon* go for the *seh*-case.

But then, the Gemara asks: is *tachtav* even available to be used this way? The term *tachtav* itself is needed for an independent *drasha* — that the repayment must be *in kind*, of comparable quality. One who stole a healthy ox worth a *maneh* cannot pay back weakling oxen at death's door. *Tachtav* teaches that the quality must be equivalent. Since *tachtav* is already claimed for this teaching, it cannot simultaneously serve to distinguish between the *shor* and *seh* payments — making the repetition of *tachat hashor v'tachat haseh* doubly necessary.

Could the Torah have omitted the opening *shor o seh* instead? Could the verse have read simply *ki yignov ish u'tvacho o mecharo chamishah vakar yishalem tachat hashor v'arba tzon tachat haseh*, with the closing clause alone identifying the animals? The Gemara considers this and concludes it would fail: without the opening *shor o seh*, one might think the law applies only when both animals are stolen together — that the thief stole a *shor and* a *seh*, slaughtered one and sold the other, and only then does the fivefold and fourfold obligation arise. Every argument that it applies to one animal individually (*u'tvacho* in the singular, *o mecharo* in the singular) is deflected: the Gemara pushes through each iteration — slaughter both, sell both, slaughter one and sell one — showing that some ambiguity remains unless the opening clause explicitly states *shor o seh* as alternative cases.

### Both Needed

The conclusion is that both mentions of *shor v'seh* — at the opening and in the closing payment clause — are independently necessary. The opening is needed to make clear that the law applies to either animal stolen individually, and the closing is needed to prevent the misreading that nine animals are owed or that the quality of repayment is unconstrained.

Since both mentions are required for their own purposes, neither is truly surplus. How, then, does Rava's argument work? The Gemara arrives at a subtler formulation: *ela shor d'seifa v'shor d'reisha miat* — it is the combination of *shor* at the end and *shor* at the beginning together that effects the limitation. The Torah could theoretically have written the verse in an asymmetric form — mentioning *shor* in the opening and then using *tachtav* in the closing for one of the animals, or some analogous construction — which would have accomplished the necessary clarifications without doubling the explicit mention of both species. The fact that the Torah chose to name *shor v'seh* prominently in both positions, when the verse could have been formulated otherwise with one implicit reference, is what signals the deliberate exclusivity. *Shma mina shor v'seh in, midi achrina lo* — only an ox and a sheep, nothing else.

Whether this *drasha* fully satisfies one's sense of the argument or not, the underlying *halakha* — that *arba v'chamisha* applies only to a *shor* and a *seh* — is in any case the received rule, and the Gemara's analysis here serves to anchor that intuition in a textual basis. With this the discussion on this *daf* concludes.
