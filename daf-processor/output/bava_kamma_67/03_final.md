# Bava Kamma 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shinuy & Acquisition

### Mari Shemeh Lav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מָרִישׁ – שְׁמוֹ עָלָיו, דְּתַנְיָא: ״צַלְעוֹת הַבַּיִת״ – אֵלּוּ הַמַּלְטְטִין, ״וְהָעֻבִּים״ – אֵלּוּ הַמְּרִישׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This is not a genuine change in name, as **a beam retains its name** even after it is inserted into a building. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to two obscure terms: The verse states: "And there were narrow windows and palm trees on the one side and on the other side, on the sides of the porch; there were also the *tzalot* of the house, and the *ubbim*" (Ezekiel 41:26). **"*Tzalot* of the house"; these are the casings [*hamaltetin*]. "And the *ubbim*"; these are the beams [*hamerishot*].** This shows that a beam can retain its name even after it has been built into a house.

The Gemara opens by rejecting a proposed *shinuy hashem* — a change of name — in the case of a stolen beam incorporated into a roof. The objection is *mari shemeh lav*: simply calling something by a new name does not constitute a genuine change if the object itself remains substantively the same. The Gemara supports this from a *baraita* in Yechezkel regarding the construction of the third *Beit HaMikdash*, where the term *uvim* (groups or structures) is explained as *marshin* — beams. Even when a beam serves as part of a roof, it is still identified as a beam. The name has not truly changed.

This stands in contrast to a piece of leather fashioned into shoes: there, looking at the object, one would say "those are shoes," not "that is a piece of leather." The *shinuy hashem* is genuine. But looking up at a roof, one might readily say, "there's a 2x4" — the beam hasn't shed its identity. So there is no real *shinuy hashem* in the beam case, and the objection from that case falls away.

### Shinuy Hachozer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר: שִׁינּוּי הַחוֹזֵר לִבְרִיָּיתוֹ – בְּשִׁינּוּי הַשֵּׁם – לָא הָוֵי שִׁינּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** a different answer: With regard to **a change in name, a change** in which the item **can revert to its original state is not** considered enough of **a change** for the thief to acquire the stolen item. Since the joist can be removed and revert to being called a beam, it is not considered a true change of name.

Rabbi Zeira offers a different approach. His principle is: *shinnuy hachozer livriyato beshinnuy hashem lo havei shinnuy* — a reversible *shinuy hashem* is not a genuine *shinuy* at all. If the transformation can be undone, it lacks the halakhic weight needed to effect acquisition.

Applied to the beam case: even granting that there is some *shinuy hashem*, since the beam could in principle be removed from the roof and restored to its original state — it was never cut, trimmed, dyed, or sculpted, merely placed among other beams — the change is reversible, and therefore insufficient to be *koneh*. This is the position that supports Rav Yosef's broader claim that without a genuine, irreversible *shinuy*, acquisition through the thief cannot occur. The only reason the thief is not required to physically dismantle the roof and return the beam is *takanat hashavim* — the rabbinic ordinance enacted to encourage penitent thieves to make restitution without facing impossibly burdensome demands. Without that ordinance, the reversibility of the change would require the beam's actual return.

### Shoes & Trays

Rabbi Zeira's principle can be sharpened by contrast with cases of irreversible *shinuy ma'aseh*. If stolen leather has been fashioned into shoes, the former owner cannot demand "give me back my leather" — the object has undergone a genuine *shinuy ma'aseh* and a genuine *shinuy hashem*. The leather is now shoes; the transformation is complete and irreversible. Similarly with a tray that has been warped, stitched, or otherwise worked: if the change is a *shinuy ma'aseh*, the thief acquires through it. The principle thus operates with precision: a mere *shinuy hashem* that is reversible does not confer acquisition; an irreversible *shinuy ma'aseh* does.

### Pipe Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשִׁינּוּי הַשֵּׁם שֶׁאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר לִבְרִיָּיתוֹ, מִי הָוֵי שִׁינּוּי? וַהֲרֵי צִינּוֹר – דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא ״קְצִיצְתָּא״ וְהַשְׁתָּא ״צִינּוֹרָא״, וְתַנְיָא: צִינּוֹר שֶׁחֲקָקוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף קְבָעוֹ – פּוֹסֵל אֶת הַמִּקְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is a change in name** in **which** the item **does not revert to its original state** really considered **a change? But there is** the case of a water **duct, where** it was **initially** called **a log [*ketzitzta*] and now** it is called **a duct, and it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A duct that one hollowed out and afterward attached** to the ground or to a building **invalidates a ritual bath** through the water it channels to the bath. The water in a ritual bath must be gathered directly from rain or a stream, not drawn with vessels. If one hollowed out a log and used it to channel water into the bath, this is considered drawn water, as he used a vessel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְבָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף חֲקָקוֹ – אֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל אֶת הַמִּקְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if **one attached it** first **and afterward hollowed it out, it does not invalidate the ritual bath.** Before the log was hollowed out, it was already attached to and considered part of the ground, and therefore the act of hollowing it out does not turn it into a vessel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ שִׁינּוּי הַשֵּׁם מִילְּתָא הִיא, אֲפִילּוּ קְבָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף חֲקָקוֹ נָמֵי לִיפְסֹל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states its question: **And if you say that a change in name is** considered **a matter** of significance, i.e., it is considered a real change in the item, then **even if one attached** the log first **and afterward hollowed it out,** the ritual bath **should also be invalid.** Since hollowing out the log leads to a change in name, it should be considered a new vessel at that point.

The Gemara now challenges the premise that a reversible *shinuy hashem* does not count as a *shinuy*, while an irreversible one does. The challenge comes from the law of *mayim she'uvim* — drawn water — which invalidates a *mikvah*. The case involves a wooden pipe: a block of wood is hollowed out to form a pipe and then affixed to the ground, channeling rainwater into a pit that serves as a *mikvah*. The ruling in a *baraita* is as follows: if one first hollowed out the wood (creating the pipe as a *kli*, a vessel) and then affixed it to the ground, the *mikvah* is invalid because the water passed through a vessel. But if one first affixed the block of wood to the ground and then hollowed it out, the *mikvah* is valid.

The Gemara's challenge: if we say that an irreversible *shinuy hashem* (the wood becoming a pipe, which is not reversible) constitutes a genuine *shinuy*, then even in the second case — where the wood was affixed and then hollowed — there has been an irreversible *shinuy hashem*, and it should still invalidate the *mikvah*. Why does the second case produce a valid *mikvah*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי שְׁאִיבָה, דְּמִדְּרַבָּנַן הִיא. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ דְּרֵישָׁא נָמֵי! הָתָם אִיכָּא תּוֹרַת כְּלִי עָלָיו בְּתָלוּשׁ, הָכָא אֵין תּוֹרַת כְּלִי עָלָיו בְּתָלוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* that **drawn** water may not be used for a ritual bath **is different, as it** applies **by rabbinic law,** and consequently the Sages were lenient in this case. The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **even in the first clause,** where the log was hollowed out before being attached, it should be permitted **as well.** The Gemara answers: There is a difference between the two cases. **There,** in the first clause, the log **had the status of a vessel while** it was still **detached. Here,** in the second case, it did **not have the status of a vessel while** it was **detached,** but only after it became attached to the ground, and therefore the Sages were lenient.

The Gemara attempts to deflect: the laws of *mayim she'uvim* are themselves rabbinic, so perhaps the *shinuy* here only matters in a rabbinic context. But this response immediately invites a follow-up challenge: if it is all *de-rabbanan*, why are we making any distinction at all? Either the rabbinic rule applies in both cases, or it applies in neither. Why is the first case problematic while the second is not?

### Kli Presence

The Gemara resolves the problem by distinguishing based on the presence of a *kli*. The relevant question is not merely whether a *shinuy hashem* occurred, but whether the object had the status of a *kli* at the moment it was in its detached (*talush*) state, before being affixed to the ground. In the first case — hollow it out, then affix it — the wood was a *kli* (a pipe) while it was still detached. That *kli*-status in its *talush* state is what triggers the rabbinic stringency. In the second case — affix it, then hollow it out — the wood was still a plain block when it was detached and affixed; it only became a pipe-shaped form after being fixed in the ground. Since it was not a *kli* in its *talush* state, the rabbinic strictness does not apply.

This resolution preserves the underlying framework: the Gemara is not abandoning Rabbi Zeira's position, nor is it abandoning the distinction between reversible and irreversible *shinuy*. The *mikvah* case is governed by an independent rabbinic criterion — *kli*-status at the time of detachment — that does not disturb the core *shinuy* analysis. Rav Yosef's position that straight *yeush* without any *shinuy* does not confer acquisition therefore remains intact.

## Rav Yosef Defense

### Yeush Alone

With the *shinuy* discussion concluded, the Gemara returns to the core position requiring defense: Rav Yosef holds that *yeush* alone — the owner's despair of recovering his stolen property, without any accompanying *shinuy* — does not render the thief *koneh*. Every challenge to this position has, to this point, involved a case where some form of *shinuy* was also present, whether a *shinuy hashem*, a *shinuy ma'aseh*, or a reversible variant thereof. Rav Yosef can consistently respond: you are not challenging me on a pure *yeush* case; there was always a *shinuy* involved. The core claim — that *yeush* by itself has no effect — has not yet been directly refuted.

### Ganav Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַגַּנָּב וְהַגַּזְלָן וְהָאַנָּס – הֶקְדֵּישָׁן הֶקְדֵּשׁ, וּתְרוּמָתָן תְּרוּמָה, וּמַעְשְׂרוֹתָן מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against Rav Yosef's opinion that the owner's despair that he will recover his stolen items does not effect acquisition for the thief, from a *baraita*: With regard to **a thief, a robber, and one who forces** people to sell him items against their will, **their consecration** of the items they obtained in these manners is a valid **consecration; and the *teruma* that they separate** from the produce they obtained in these manners is valid ***teruma*; and the tithes that they separate** from those foods are valid **tithes.** Consecrations, *terumot*, and tithes must be performed by the owner of the item in question. Since there was no physical change in these items, this shows that the thief is considered the new owner merely by virtue of the owner's despair of recovery.

A challenge is now raised from a *baraita*: a *ganav*, a *gazlan*, or one who takes by force — if they consecrate an animal (*hikdisho hekdesh*), the *hekdesh* takes effect; if they separate *terumah*, the *terumah* is valid; if they separate *ma'aser*, the *ma'aser* is valid. This appears to demonstrate that even without an obvious *shinuy*, the thief has acquired the object sufficiently to effect these religious-legal changes. If *yeush* alone does not confer acquisition, how can the thief's *hekdesh* or *terumah* be valid?

### Shinuy Hashem Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָתָם אִיכָּא שִׁינּוּי הַשֵּׁם – דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא ״טִיבְלָא״ וְהַשְׁתָּה ״תְּרוּמָה״, הֶקְדֵּשׁ מֵעִיקָּרָא ״חוּלִּין״ וְהַשְׁתָּה ״הֶקְדֵּשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say,** in answer to this question: **There,** in those cases involving consecrations, *terumot*, and tithes, **there is a change in name** of the stolen item, **as initially** the stolen food was called **untithed produce,** i.e., produce from which *teruma* and tithes have not been separated, **and now** it is called ***teruma*** or tithe. With regard to **consecration,** it was **initially** called **non-sacred** property, **and now** it is called **consecrated** property.

Rav Yosef's response: there is in fact a *shinuy hashem* present in every one of these cases. When stolen wheat was *tevel* (untithed produce with no separated portions) and the thief separates *terumah*, the object is now called *terumah* — a fundamentally different legal category with its own body of laws. When stolen *chulin* is consecrated as *hekdesh*, it is no longer *chulin* — it carries an entirely different name and legal status. In each case, the object before and after bears a different name. This is precisely a *shinuy hashem*, and Rav Yosef's position is that *yeush* combined with *shinuy hashem* — even one that is arguably reversible — may suffice to confer acquisition, depending on the details. What he insists upon is that *yeush* alone, without any *shinuy* whatsoever, accomplishes nothing.

One might note, as indeed the question arose in the shiur, that using a stolen object for a *mitzvah* raises its own difficulty — for a stolen species, for instance, is invalid for use in a *mitzvah*. The Gemara returns to this matter subsequently.

## Two Yonatan Teachings

### Shinuy Koneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִין לְשִׁנּוּי שֶׁהוּא קוֹנֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הַגְּזֵלָה״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״? אִם כְּעֵין שֶׁגָּזַל – יַחֲזִיר, וְאִם לָאו – דָּמִים בְּעָלְמָא בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: From where** is it derived **that a change** in a stolen item causes the thief to **acquire** it? **As it is stated: "And he shall return the stolen item that he took by robbery"** (Leviticus 5:23). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states** the seemingly superfluous phrase **"that he took by robbery"?** It teaches that only **if** the item is in the same state **as when he took it by robbery** it **he must return** it. **But if not, he is required to pay only money;** the stolen item remains his to keep.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״ מִיבְּעֵי לְמַעוֹטֵי גֶּזֶל אָבִיו, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ עַל גֶּזֶל אָבִיו! אִם כֵּן, נִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת גְּזֵילוֹ״, ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״ לְמָה לִי לְמִכְתַּב? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to this derivation: **But this** phrase: **"That he took by robbery," is required to exclude** the case of an item **stolen by one's father,** i.e., **that** when a son returns an item or money that his father had stolen, **he does not add one-fifth to** the principal amount of that which **his father stole.** The passage in question addresses one who stole and took a false oath. This individual is obligated to add one-fifth to the amount he stole (see Leviticus 5:21–24). The apparently redundant phrase "that he took by robbery," teaches that the requirement to add one-fifth applies only to the sinner himself, not to his heirs (see 104b). **If so,** if the verse teaches only that *halakha*, **let the Merciful One write** merely: **And he shall return his stolen item,** as from that phrase alone it could be derived that an heir does not have to add one-fifth. **Why do I** need the Torah **to write** the expression **"that he took by robbery"?** One may therefore **conclude two** conclusions **from** this verse: A son need not add one-fifth, and a change in a stolen item effects acquisition for the thief.

Rav Chisda transmits a teaching in the name of Yonatan: how do we know that *shinuy* is *koneh* — that a change of form effects acquisition? From the verse (*Vayikra* 5:23): *vehishiv et hagezeilah asher gazal* — "he shall return the stolen object which he stole." The phrase *asher gazal* seems superfluous; of course the returned object is the one he stole, not one he didn't steal. The *drasha* is: *im ke'ein shegazal yachazir ve'im lav damim be'alma ba'ei shilumei* — if it is in the same state as when he stole it, he returns the object itself; if not — because it has undergone a *shinuy* — he pays its monetary value. The requirement to return the *gezeilah* applies only when it remains *asher gazal*, in its original form. If it has changed, he owes *damim*, implying that the *shinuy* has effected acquisition and he can no longer return the object itself.

One might then object: if *asher gazal* is doing this work, there is another law that also needs this phrase — the rule that one who confesses a theft and makes voluntary restitution must pay the *keren* plus a *chomesh* (an additional fifth), but if the *thief's heir* makes restitution, no *chomesh* is required. This too is derived from *asher gazal*: the obligation to add a *chomesh* falls on the original thief — *al gezeilo shelo* — not on his heirs. Since one phrase cannot serve both purposes, we might think we must choose. The Gemara resolves this by demonstrating that both *drashos* can coexist: the Torah could have written simply *vehishiv et hagezeilah* (without *asher gazal*) if only the *chomesh* exemption were needed, or it could have written *vehishiv et hagezeilah asher gazal* without separately specifying *gezeilah*. The redundancy of having both allows us to learn both laws — *shma mina tarti*.

### Shinuy Lo Koneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִין לְשִׁנּוּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת הַגְּזֵילָה״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר גָּזָל״! הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ – עַל גְּזֵילוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ, וְאֵין מוֹסִיף חוֹמֶשׁ עַל גֶּזֶל אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** a different version of the above statement: **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: From where** is it derived that **a change** in a stolen item **does not** cause the thief to **acquire** it? **As it is stated: "And he shall return the stolen item that he took by robbery"** (Leviticus 5:23), from which it may be inferred that he must return it **in any case,** even if it has undergone a change. The Gemara asks: **But it is written "that he took by robbery,"** which indicates that, on the contrary, the item need be returned only if it is in a similar state to when it was stolen. The Gemara answers: **That** phrase **is required** to teach another *halakha*: **One adds one-fifth upon** returning **his own stolen** item concerning which he had taken a false oath, **but he does not add one-fifth upon** returning something **stolen by his father.**

Immediately thereafter, the Gemara presents a second tradition, also attributed to Rav Chisda in the name of Yonatan — but reaching the opposite conclusion. *Menayin leshinuy she'eino koneh* — from where do we know that *shinuy* does *not* confer acquisition? From the same verse: *vehishiv et hagezeilah* — he must return the *gezeilah* in all circumstances, regardless of what state it is in. *Shinuy* does not help; he still owes the object.

And what then is *asher gazal* doing? It is entirely devoted to the *chomesh* exemption for heirs — *al gezeilo shelo hu shemosif chomesh* — and carries no implication about the state of the object. The same verse, the same phrase, yields diametrically opposed conclusions in the two versions of this teaching. The fundamental *machloket* between Rav Yosef and Rava — does *yeush*, does *shinuy*, effect acquisition? — remains unresolved even at the level of these *Amoraic* teachings, and pointedly, both versions derive from the selfsame *pasuk*.

## Ulla & Rava

### Yeush No Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: מִנַּיִין לְיֵאוּשׁ שֶׁאֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַהֲבֵאתֶם גָּזוּל אֶת הַפִּסֵּחַ וְאֶת הַחוֹלֶה״ – גָּזוּל דּוּמְיָא דְּפִסֵּחַ, מָה פִּסֵּחַ דְּלֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא כְּלָל, אַף גָּזוּל דְּלֵית לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא; לָא שְׁנָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ, וְלָא שְׁנָא אַחַר יֵאוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla says: From where** is it derived **that** the owner's **despair** of recovering his stolen item **does not** cause the thief to **acquire** it? **As it is stated** in the criticism of the Jewish people for bringing inferior animals as offerings: **"And you have brought that which is stolen, and the lame, and the sick"** (Malachi 1:13). From the juxtaposition of these cases it may be derived that **a stolen** animal **is similar to a lame** animal: **Just as a lame** animal **has no rectification at all** with regard to its disqualification as an offering, **so too a stolen** animal **has no rectification** for its disqualification. There is **no difference whether** one is dealing with a stolen animal **before** the owner's **despair** of recovering it, **and** there is **no difference** if it is **after** the owner's **despair.** In either case, it is disqualified. This shows that the owner's despair does not effect acquisition for the thief.

Ulla now offers a proof that *yeush* does not work — supporting Rav Yosef's position. The verse in Malachi (1:13) states: *vahasaitem gazul ve'et hapise'ach ve'et hacholeh* — you bring to God what is stolen, lame, or sick. God rejects such offerings. Ulla derives: *gazul dumya de-pise'ach* — the case of a stolen animal is compared to a lame one. Just as a *pise'ach* — a lame animal — has no remedy as long as it remains lame (one cannot reattach a missing limb), and its disqualification is absolute, so too a stolen animal offered as a *korban* has no remedy. And this is true *velo shna lifnei yeush velo shna le'achar yeush* — whether before or after the owner's *yeush*, the stolen *korban* remains unacceptable. *Yeush* effects nothing.

### Rava Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״קׇרְבָּנוֹ״ – וְלֹא הַגָּזוּל. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא לִפְנֵי יֵאוּשׁ – פְּשִׁיטָא, לְמָה לִי קְרָא? אֶלָּא לָאו לְאַחַר יֵאוּשׁ – וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ יֵאוּשׁ לָא קָנֵי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This *halakha* may be derived **from here,** a *baraita*: The verse: "If his offering is a burnt-offering of the herd" (Leviticus 1:3), indicates that one's offering must be **"his offering," but not** an animal **stolen** from another. **When,** i.e., in which circumstances, is it necessary to teach this *halakha*? **If we say** it is dealing with a stolen animal that the robber consecrated and sacrificed **before** the owner's **despair** of recovery, **why do I** need **a verse** to teach this? It **is obvious** that it is disqualified, as one cannot even consecrate an animal that does not belong to him. **Rather, is it not** referring to one who seeks to consecrate and sacrifice a stolen animal **after** the owner's **despair?** And yet the *baraita* teaches that the animal cannot be consecrated by the thief. **Conclude from** the *baraita* that the owner's **despair** of recovering a stolen item **does not** cause the thief to **acquire** it, as if it belonged to him he would be able to consecrate and sacrifice it. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from** the *baraita* that it is so.

Rava offers a parallel teaching: the verse (*Vayikra* 1:2) says *korbano* — his offering, implying the *korban* must be his own and not stolen: *korbano velo gazul*. The Gemara asks: when does this apply? If before *yeush* — obviously the stolen animal cannot be offered; no verse is needed to tell us that. Rather, the verse is addressing the case *after yeush*, and it is teaching that even then the stolen animal is invalid. *Shma mina yeiush lo kani* — we learn from here that *yeush* does not confer acquisition.

### Case Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רָבָא הוּא דְּאָמַר דִּגְזַל קׇרְבָּן דְּחַבְרֵיהּ! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הֲדַר בֵּיהּ, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ רַב פָּפָּא אַמְרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But Rava was the one who said** that this proof can be refuted, as the *baraita* can be interpreted as dealing with one **who robbed another of an offering** that was already consecrated. Rava apparently contradicts himself. The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that Rava **retracted** one of these two statements. **And if you wish, say** instead that **Rav Pappa,** not Rava, **said one of these** two statements.

At this point the Gemara raises an internal tension. Earlier in the discussion, when Rava's position was being examined, the question of a stolen *korban* was addressed with a different answer — that Rava's statement *korbano velo gazul* was specifically about a case where one stole the animal *in its already-consecrated state* as a *korban*, not a case where *chulin* was stolen and then consecrated. That earlier resolution distinguished those two scenarios. Now Rava appears to be using the verse to prove that *yeush* has no effect in a general sense, which would seem to contradict his earlier position that *yeush* does effect acquisition in the case of stolen *chulin*.

The Gemara notes the tension: *shinuyei smi mikan* — has Rava retracted? Or, as Rav Papa clarifies: these are in fact different rulings from different authorities, and the two positions attributed to Rava may not both be his. At a minimum, we now have two clear teachings — Ulla's and the teaching associated with Rava — both establishing that *yeush* does not confer acquisition, in alignment with Rav Yosef's position. The earlier case involving stolen *chulin* made into a *korban* remains a matter the Gemara acknowledges it has not fully resolved, possibly distinguishing along the lines of whether the theft occurred before or after the animal's consecration, or whether different *Tannaitic* sources are in play.

## Arba V'Chamisha

### Exodus Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the mishna, which teaches: **But the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment** applies only to the theft of an ox or a sheep, as it is stated: "If a man steal an ox or a sheep, and slaughter it or sell it, he shall pay five oxen for an ox and four sheep for a sheep" (Exodus 21:37).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? נֵילַף ״שׁוֹר״–״שׁוֹר״ מִשַּׁבָּת – מָה לְהַלָּן חַיָּה וְעוֹף כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן, אַף כָּאן חַיָּה וָעוֹף כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why** is the fourfold and fivefold payment limited to oxen and sheep? **Let us derive** otherwise by a verbal analogy of the term **"ox"** in this verse and **"ox" from** a passage dealing with **Shabbat,** where it is stated: "And the seventh day is Sabbath to the Lord your God, you shall not perform any labor, you, your son, and your daughter, your slave, and your maidservant, and your ox, and your donkey, and all your animals, and the gentile that is within your gates" (Deuteronomy 5:13). **Just as there,** with regard to Shabbat, the *halakha* stated with regard to an ox is not limited to oxen, as **undomesticated animals and fowl are similar to** oxen in that they too are included in this prohibition, as the verse states: "Nor any of your animals," **so too here,** in the case of theft, one can say that **undomesticated animals and fowl are similar to** oxen in that the fourfold or fivefold payment is incurred for their theft.

The Gemara now turns to a different topic: the law of fourfold and fivefold restitution, *arba v'chamisha*. The *Mishnah* has already established that this obligation applies exclusively to a *shor* (ox) and a *seh* (sheep or goat) — not to other animals, such as a *chamor* (donkey). The question is: what is the biblical basis for this restriction?

The relevant verse is Shemot 21:37: *ki yignov ish shor o seh* — if a man steals an ox or a sheep — *u'tvacho o mecharo* — and slaughters or sells it — *chamishah vacar yishalem tachat hashor v'arba tzon tachat haseh* — he shall pay five cattle in place of the ox, and four sheep in place of the sheep. Two things are immediately notable: first, the verse specifies *shor o seh* explicitly in its opening clause; second, the closing clause specifies the payment again as *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh*, naming the animals a second time.

### Shabbat Parallel

A challenge presents itself: why not derive from the laws of *Shabbat* that *arba v'chamisha* should apply to all animals? The *pasuk* in Shemot 23:12 requires that one's *shor* and *chamor* rest on *Shabbat*; and the verse in Devarim (5:14) expands this — *l'ma'an yanuach shorecha vachamorecha* — so that all one's animals and dependents rest. Just as *Shabbat* rest is not restricted to an ox and a sheep but extends to all animals in one's possession, so too the fourfold and fivefold restitution should extend beyond *shor v'seh* to all animals. By analogy, stealing any animal and slaughtering or selling it should incur the enhanced penalty.

### Rava Double Mention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה״ ״שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים – שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The verse** dealing with theft **states "ox" and "sheep," "ox" and "sheep" twice.** This repetition indicates that for **an ox and a sheep, yes,** there is a fourfold or fivefold payment, and **for other items, no,** there is no fourfold or fivefold payment.

Rava responds: *kra shor v'seh shor v'seh shtei pe'amim — shor v'seh in, midi achrina lo* — the verse mentions *shor v'seh* twice, and this double mention constitutes a deliberate exclusion of all other animals. Mentioning *shor v'seh* at the beginning and *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh* at the end signals that the law is specifically and exclusively limited to these two species. The repetition is meaningful and restrictive.

The force of Rava's answer depends, however, on the claim that this repetition is truly surplus — that the verse could have been formulated differently and still conveyed the same information. If the verse had no choice but to name the animals twice for structural reasons, then the double mention teaches nothing and the *Shabbat* comparison would stand unchallenged. The Gemara therefore proceeds to examine whether either mention of *shor v'seh* could have been omitted.

### Pasuk Formulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הֵי מְיַיתַּר? אִילֵימָא ״שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה״ דְּסֵיפָא מְיַיתַּר, דְּנִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״כִּי יִגְנֹב שׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה וּטְבָחוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ, חֲמִשָּׁה בָקָר יְשַׁלֵּם תַּחְתָּיו וְאַרְבַּע צֹאן תַּחְתָּיו״; אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא בָּעֵי שַׁלּוֹמֵי תִּשְׁעָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **says,** with regard to Rava's assertion that one of the instances of "ox" and "sheep" is superfluous: **Which** instance of the word "ox" or "sheep" **is** the **superfluous** one? **If we say** that the instance of **"ox" and "sheep" at the end** of the verse **is superfluous, due to** the following consideration: **Let the Merciful One write: If one steal an ox or a sheep, and slaughter it or sell it, he shall pay five oxen for it and four sheep for it,** without repeating the words "ox" and "sheep," this is not possible. **If the Merciful One had written** the verse **like this, I would say** that the thief is **required to pay nine** animals, five oxen and four sheep, **for each and every** animal stolen.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָא כְּתִיב ״תַּחְתָּיו״ ״תַּחְתָּיו״ – חַד ״תַּחְתָּיו״ מְיַיתַּר;
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that this interpretation is not possible, as the suggestion is that the verse would have **written: For it, for it,** twice, whereas if a payment of nine animals were required for each stolen animal the verse would have written: For it, only once; then **one** mention of: **For it, is superfluous,** to teach us that it is five for an ox and four for a sheep.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לִדְרָשָׁה אַחֲרִינָא – דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל גָּנַב שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָנֶה, יְשַׁלֵּם תַּחְתָּיו [חֲמִשָּׁה] נְגִידִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תַּחְתָּיו״ ״תַּחְתָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **That** repetition of: For it, **is required for another interpretation,** and cannot teach that it is five for an ox and four for a sheep. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that if one **stole** an expensive **ox worth one hundred dinars he may pay for it** with **lean,** inferior **animals [*negidin*].** To counter this, **the verse states: "For** the ox" and **"for** the sheep," which indicates that the oxen and sheep used for payment must be similar to the stolen animal in quality. Since one might have erred and understood that the thief is required to pay nine animals, five oxen and four sheep, for each and every animal stolen, that suggested version of the verse is not a possibility. The words "ox" and "sheep" at the end of the verse could not have been omitted, so they are not superfluous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה״ דְּרֵישָׁא מְיַיתַּר – דְּנִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״כִּי יִגְנֹב אִישׁ וּטְבָחוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ, חֲמִשָּׁה בָקָר יְשַׁלֵּם תַּחַת הַשּׁוֹר, וְאַרְבַּע צֹאן תַּחַת הַשֶּׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather,** the words **"ox" and "sheep" in the first** part of the verse **are superfluous, due to** the following consideration: **Let the Merciful One write: If a man steal, and slaughter it or sell it, he shall pay five oxen for the ox, and four sheep for the sheep,** without mentioning "ox" or "sheep" at the beginning of the verse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא הָכִי, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: עַד דְּגָנֵיב תְּרֵי וְטָבַח לְהוּ! ״וּטְבָחוֹ״ כְּתִיב – לְחַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: It is not possible for the verse to have been written this way, as **if the Merciful One had written** it in **this** manner **I would say:** A thief is not required to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment **unless he steals two** animals, an ox and a sheep, **and slaughters them** both. The Gemara responds: One could not interpret the verse in this manner, as it **is written: "And slaughter it."** The singular pronoun "it" is referring **to** the slaughter of only **one** animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: עַד דְּגָנֵיב תַּרְוַיְיהוּ וּמְזַבֵּין לְהוּ! ״וּמְכָרוֹ״ כְּתִיב – לְחַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** one could **say** that a thief is not required to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment **unless he steals both** of these animals, an ox and a sheep, **and sells them** both. The Gemara rejects this suggestion in a similar manner: This interpretation is also not possible, as it is **written: "Or sell it,"** which is referring **to** the sale of only **one** animal.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: הָוֵה אָמֵינָא עַד דְּגָנֵיב תְּרֵי, וְטָבַח חַד וּמְזַבֵּין חַד! ״אוֹ מְכָרוֹ״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But** one could **say** a different interpretation: **I would say** that a thief is not required to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment **unless he steals two** animals, an ox and a sheep, **and slaughters one** of them **and sells** the other **one.** The Gemara rejects this suggestion as well: This interpretation is impossible, as it is **written:** "And slaughter it **or sell it."** The term "or" indicates that only one of these two actions results in paying the penalty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי הֲוָה אָמֵינָא עַד דְּגָנֵיב תַּרְוַיְיהוּ וְטָבַח חַד וּמְשַׁיַּיר חַד, אוֹ מְזַבֵּין חַד וּמְשַׁיַּיר חַד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still,** had the verse been worded without mentioning an ox or sheep at the start, **I would say:** A thief does not pay the fourfold or fivefold payment **unless he steals two** animals, an ox and a sheep, **and slaughters one** of them **and leaves** the other **one, or sells one and leaves** the other **one.** It is therefore impossible to omit the words "ox" and "sheep" in the beginning of the verse, which means that these words are not superfluous.

The Gemara works through the possibilities systematically. Could the Torah have omitted the second mention — *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh* — and written instead *chamishah vacar yishalem tachtav v'arba tzon tachtav*? The term *tachtav* ("in its place") would refer back to the *shor o seh* mentioned at the opening, and we would understand that five *bakar* are paid for the *shor* and four *tzon* for the *seh*. If so, the closing *tachat hashor v'tachat haseh* is surplus and could serve as the restrictive teaching.

The Gemara rejects this: had the verse written *tachtav* without specifying the animal, one might have thought the obligation was *nine*-fold — that for stealing either a *shor* or a *seh*, one owes *both* five *bakar* and four *tzon*, since *tachtav* in the singular refers ambiguously to whatever was stolen. The payment formula would then apply collectively: steal any one of these animals, pay nine. Therefore the verse must specify *tachat hashor* and *tachat haseh* separately to make clear that the five *bakar* go for the *shor*-case and the four *tzon* go for the *seh*-case.

But then, the Gemara asks: is *tachtav* even available to be used this way? The term *tachtav* itself is needed for an independent *drasha* — that the repayment must be *in kind*, of comparable quality. One who stole a healthy ox worth a *maneh* cannot pay back weakling oxen at death's door. *Tachtav* teaches that the quality must be equivalent. Since *tachtav* is already claimed for this teaching, it cannot simultaneously serve to distinguish between the *shor* and *seh* payments — making the repetition of *tachat hashor v'tachat haseh* doubly necessary.

Could the Torah have omitted the opening *shor o seh* instead? Could the verse have read simply *ki yignov ish u'tvacho o mecharo chamishah vacar yishalem tachat hashor v'arba tzon tachat haseh*, with the closing clause alone identifying the animals? The Gemara considers this and concludes it would fail: without the opening *shor o seh*, one might think the law applies only when both animals are stolen together — that the thief stole a *shor and* a *seh*, slaughtered one and sold the other, and only then does the fivefold and fourfold obligation arise. Every argument that it applies to one animal individually (*u'tvacho* in the singular, *o mecharo* in the singular) is deflected: the Gemara pushes through each iteration — slaughter both, sell both, slaughter one and sell one — showing that some ambiguity remains unless the opening clause explicitly states *shor o seh* as alternative cases.

### Both Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: ״שׁוֹר״ דְּסֵיפָא וְ״שֶׂה״ דְּרֵישָׁא מְיַיתַּר, דְּנִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״כִּי יִגְנֹב אִישׁ שׁוֹר וּטְבָחוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ, חֲמִשָּׁה בָקָר יְשַׁלֵּם תַּחְתָּיו, וְאַרְבַּע צֹאן תַּחַת הַשֶּׂה״; ״שׁוֹר״ דְּסֵיפָא וְ״שֶׂה״ דְּרֵישָׁא לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara gives its final explanation of Rava's statement: **Rather, "ox" at the end** of the verse **and "sheep" in the first** part of the verse **are superfluous, due to** the following consideration: **Let the Merciful One write: If a man steal an ox and slaughter it or sell it, he shall pay five oxen for it, and four sheep for a sheep. Why do I** need the word **"ox" at the end** of the verse **and** the word **"sheep" in the first** part of the verse? **Conclude from** these apparently superfluous words that for **an ox and a sheep, yes,** a thief is obligated to pay the fourfold or fivefold payment, but for stealing **other items, no,** he is not.

The conclusion is that both mentions of *shor v'seh* — at the opening and in the closing payment clause — are independently necessary. The opening is needed to make clear that the law applies to either animal stolen individually, and the closing is needed to prevent the misreading that nine animals are owed or that the quality of repayment is unconstrained.

Since both mentions are required for their own purposes, neither is truly surplus. How, then, does Rava's argument work? The Gemara arrives at a subtler formulation: *ela shor d'seifa v'shor d'reisha miat* — it is the combination of *shor* at the end and *shor* at the beginning together that effects the limitation. The Torah could theoretically have written the verse in an asymmetric form — mentioning *shor* in the opening and then using *tachtav* in the closing for one of the animals, or some analogous construction — which would have accomplished the necessary clarifications without doubling the explicit mention of both species. The fact that the Torah chose to name *shor v'seh* prominently in both positions, when the verse could have been formulated otherwise with one implicit reference, is what signals the deliberate exclusivity. *Shma mina shor v'seh in, midi achrina lo* — only an ox and a sheep, nothing else.

Whether this *drasha* fully satisfies one's sense of the argument or not, the underlying *halakha* — that *arba v'chamisha* applies only to a *shor* and a *seh* — is in any case the received rule, and the Gemara's analysis here serves to anchor that intuition in a textual basis. With this the discussion on this *daf* concludes.
