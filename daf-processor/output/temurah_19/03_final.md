# Temurah 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Toda: Children & Substit…

### Mishna 22 Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְלַד תּוֹדָה וּתְמוּרָתָהּ, וְלָדָן וְלַד וְלָדָן עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ כְּתוֹדָה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁאֵין טְעוּנִין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The offspring of a thanks offering and the substitute** of a thanks offering, and **the offspring** of the offspring and its substitute, **and the offspring of their offspring until the end of all time, they are** all **like thanks offerings,** with the **only** difference being **that they do not require** the accompanying **loaves,** unlike the thanks offering itself.

Mishna 22 opens by examining the status of the child and *temurah* of animals sanctified as *korbanot* — specifically, whether they may themselves be brought as offerings. The preceding material established that by *shelamim*, the child and *temurah* carry the same status as the parent and may be brought, with certain limited exceptions for children. We now turn to the *toda*, the thanksgiving offering, which is a subcategory of *shelamim*.

The *mishna* teaches: *vlad toda* — the child of a *toda* — and *tmurasah*, its *temurah*, and *vlad vlada*, the child of its child, and so on *ad sof kol hadoros*, until the end of all generations — *harei elu k'toda bilvad she'ein te'unim lachem*: these are treated in every respect like a *toda*, with the single exception that they do not require bread. Unlike the fuller *shelamim* procedure — which includes *smicha*, *nesachim*, and *chazeh v'shok* — the *toda* ordinarily also requires forty loaves of bread. Its derivatives, however, are brought without that bread.

One notable feature of this *mishna* is the absence of any debate. The position of Rabbi Eliezer — encountered earlier — which holds that the children of certain *korbanot* are not brought, does not appear here. As will become clear, the *gemara* uses precisely this absence to press the inquiry into what Rabbi Eliezer's position really is and why it does not apply in the case of *toda*.

### Toda Law Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַהוּ אוֹמֵר ״יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ״?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived, that the offspring and substitute of a thanks offering do not require the accompanying loaves? The Gemara answers: **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to the verse: "If he offers it for a thanksgiving, then he shall offer with the sacrifice of thanksgiving unleavened cakes mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil" (Leviticus 7:12). **What** does the term **"offer it [*yakrivenu*]"** serve to **say?**

The *gemara* asks *m'nan hani mili* — where do we know this? The *tanna'im* derive the law from a close reading of the relevant *pesukim*. The *pasuk* concerning *shelamim* uses the verb *yakrivenu*, and the *pasuk* concerning *toda* reads *im al toda yakrivenu* — "if it is for a *toda*, bring it." The *gemara* proceeds through a series of derivations:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַפְרִישׁ תּוֹדָה, וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְנִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת. מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵיזוֹ שֶׁיִּרְצֶה יַקְרִיב וְלַחְמָהּ עִמָּהּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תּוֹדָה יַקְרִיב״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: The term is referring to one who **designates a thanks offering and it was lost, and he separated another in its place, and** then **the first** animal **was found, and** now **both of them stand before him** available for sacrifice. **From where** is it derived **that he may sacrifice whichever** one **he wants and** bring **its** accompanying **loaves with it? The verse states: "He offers a thanks offering,"** indicating that he may offer either one.

If one designated a *toda* and it was lost, and he designated a replacement, and then the original was found, we know from the phrase *im al toda* — understood broadly — that either one may be brought. The question then is: which of them requires the bread? From the word *yakrivenu* — the singular form — we learn *achas v'lo shtayim*: one, not two. Only one of the two is brought with bread; the other is brought without.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל תְּהֵא שְׁנִיָּה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יַקְרִיבֶנּוּ״ — אַחַת וְלֹא שְׁתַּיִם. מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת וְלָדוֹת תְּמוּרוֹת וַחֲלִיפוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְאִם עַל תּוֹדָה״. יָכוֹל יְהוּ כּוּלָּן טְעוּנוֹת לֶחֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַל זֶבַח הַתּוֹדָה״ — תּוֹדָה טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם, וְלֹא וְלָדָהּ וּתְמוּרָתָהּ וַחֲלִיפָתָהּ טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that even **the second should require** the accompanying **loaves.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Offer it,"** to teach that only **one** of them is brought with the loaves, **and not two. From where** is it derived that **the offspring, substitutes, and replacement** offerings, in the case where the animal was lost, another was separated in its place, and then the original animal was found, are **included** and may be sacrificed as well? **The verse states: "And if…as a thanksgiving,"** teaching that they may all be sacrificed as thanks offerings. One **might** have thought that **all of them require loaves.** Therefore, **the verse states: "With the sacrifice of thanksgiving,"** which indicates that only **the thanks offering** itself **requires loaves, but its offspring and its substitute and its replacement do not require loaves.**

Having established the law for *chalifin* — the exchange case — the *gemara* then asks how we extend the rule to include children and *tmuros* as well. The answer is that the additional phrase *v'im al toda* introduces further cases. Once again, the question is raised: perhaps these should also require bread? The *gemara* answers from the phrase *al zevach hatoda* — bread accompanies the *toda* sacrifice itself, *toda te'una lachem*, not the child, not the *temurah*, and not the *chalifa*. The one that was not ultimately brought has the status of a *chalifa* and does not require bread.

### Bread Exclusion Explained

It is notable that the *gemara* derived the law of *toda* from a separate *pasuk* rather than simply extending the general rule established for *shelamim*. The derivation is necessary in part because it establishes not just that these derivatives are brought, but also that they are brought *without* bread. *Tosafot* raises the broader question of whether the general rule is that derivatives may be brought unless there is a specific reason to exclude them, or vice versa. The impression is that the default is to bring them unless excluded — yet *toda* still required its own derivation, particularly to resolve the bread question.

Why would the bread requirement fall away for the derivative? A useful conceptual framework places the *toda* somewhere between the generic *korbanot* — *shelamim* and *olah*, which are non-specific — and the more singular *korbanot* such as *chatas* and *asham*, which are bound to a particular transgression. A *toda* is by its nature a response to a specific experience of divine deliverance. It has an individual identity, a specific narrative behind it. Only when the *toda* itself is brought does the full expression of that particularity — embodied in the bread — apply. The derivative shares the *toda*'s *kedusha* but not its singular, personal occasion, and therefore its more distinctive characteristic, the bread, is not carried over. That said, it does not become a generic *shelamim* in every sense: it retains the *toda*'s stricter eating schedule of one day and one night, rather than the two days allowed for *shelamim*.

The question of why Rabbi Eliezer's position does not generate a debate here is what drove the *gemara* in the preceding discussion to reframe his position. Had Rabbi Eliezer's view been a principled objection to ever bringing derivatives, it would apply to *toda* as well. The *gemara* therefore concluded that his position rests on a *gezeira* that does not apply to *toda* because *toda* is uncommon — *lo shechicha*, and therefore *lo gazru*. This reading renders Rabbi Eliezer's position considerably more limited in scope.

## Olah: Gender & Derivativ…

### Olah Chain Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תְּמוּרַת עוֹלָה, וְלַד תְּמוּרָה, וְלַד וְלַד וְלָדָה עַד סוֹף כׇּל הָעוֹלָם — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ כְּעוֹלָה, וּטְעוּנִין הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִיתּוּחַ וְכָלִיל לָאִשִּׁים. הַמַּפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָה וְיָלְדָה זָכָר — יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב וְיִמָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמָיו עוֹלָה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הוּא עַצְמוֹ יִקְרַב עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the substitute of a burnt offering, the offspring of the substitute,** e.g., if one substituted a female animal for a burnt offering, and it gave birth to a male, and **the offspring of the offspring of its offspring until the end of all time, they are** all **like burnt offerings and** therefore **they require flaying and cutting** into pieces **and** must be burned **completely in the fire.** In the case of **one who designates a female** animal **as a burnt offering,** which may be brought only from males, **and** that female **gave birth to a male,** although it is a male, **it** is left to **graze until it becomes unfit [*sheyista'ev*] and** then **it is sold, and he brings a burnt offering with** the **money** received for **its** sale. **Rabbi Elazar says:** The male offspring **itself is sacrificed as a burnt offering.**

The *mishna* turns next to the *olah*, the elevation offering. Unlike the *shelamim* or *toda*, the *olah* must be male. This constraint makes the discussion of children and derivatives considerably more complex, since one cannot simply speak of the child of an *olah* — female offspring cannot be brought as an *olah* at all, and even male offspring of a female *olah* pose difficulties. The discussion therefore focuses on what happens when sanctity travels through the chain via *temurah* and then through offspring.

The *mishna* states: the *temurah* of an *olah*, or the *vlad* of a *temurah* — note that it is the child of the *temurah*, not the child of the *olah* itself, since the *temurah* can be female — along with the child's child, and all subsequent descendants *ad sof kol haolam*, all carry the status of an *olah*. Wherever along the chain a male appears, that animal may be brought as an *olah* — *harei elu k'olah* — and is subject to the full procedure: *hafshat v'nituach v'kalil l'ishim*, flaying, dissection, and complete burning on the *mizbeach*.

What then of the females along the chain? The assumption of the *mishna* is that any female derivative — which cannot itself be brought as an *olah* — must wait until it develops a *mum*, a physical blemish, at which point it is redeemed and the proceeds go toward an *olah*. Crucially, the fact of being female is not itself considered a *mum* — this is not a statement about gender per se, but rather a halakhic point that a *mum* must be a concrete physical defect. The wrong gender, like the wrong gender of a male *chatas*, does not qualify for redemption on its own.

### Female Olah Debate

The *mishna* then addresses a new case: *hamafrish nekeivah l'olah* — one who directly sanctified a female as an *olah*. If it then gave birth to a male, what is the law of that male child? The *mishna* rules that the child is not brought directly as an *olah*; instead, one waits for the child to develop a *mum*, sells it, and uses the proceeds for an *olah* — *yeireh ad sheyista'ev v'yimacher v'yavi bidamav olah*. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: *hu atzmo yakriv olah* — the male child itself may be brought as an *olah*.

This generates an apparent inconsistency within the *mishna*. In the first half, wherever a male appears in the descendant chain — even after passing through female intermediaries — it may be brought as an *olah*, and there seems to be no debate about this. Yet in the second half, when the top of the chain is a female directly sanctified as an *olah*, the rabbis say that even the male child may not be brought. What distinguishes these two situations?

### Beginning vs. End Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא דְּלָא פְּלִיגִי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא דִּפְלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The first clause of the mishna states that the offspring of a female substitute for a burnt offering is brought as a burnt offering, whereas in the latter clause the Rabbis and Rabbi Elazar disagree about the status of a female animal that was consecrated as a burnt offering. The Gemara therefore asks: **What is different** in **the first clause that they do not disagree, and what is different** in **the latter clause that they disagree?**

The *gemara* puts this question directly: *mai shna reisha d'lo palgi u'mai shna seifa d'palgi*? Why is there no debate in the first clause, but there is in the second?

### Rabbah bar Chana's Posit…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה: בְּמַחְלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא פְלִיגִי עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֶלָּא גַּבֵּי מַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָה, דְּאִימֵּיהּ לָא קְרֵיבָה, אֲבָל תְּמוּרָה דְּאִימֵּיהּ נָמֵי קְרֵיבָה — אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן מוֹדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** Even the first clause is **taught as** a matter in **dispute** between the Rabbis and Rabbi Elazar, **and** the ruling there **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar. And Rava said: You** may **even say** that the first clause is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** but they **disagree with Rabbi Elazar only with regard to** one who **designates a female** animal **for a burnt offering,** as they maintain that the offspring may not be sacrificed **because its mother may not be sacrificed. But** with regard to the offspring of **a substitute, since its mother,** i.e., the original burnt offering that is the source of the mother's sanctity as a substitute, **may also be sacrificed, even the Rabbis concede** that the offspring may be sacrificed.

Rabbah bar bar Chana answers: *b'machlokes shnuya* — actually, the first clause is also disputed. The opening of the *mishna*, which seems to permit bringing any male that appears in the descendant chain, reflects only the position of Rabbi Eliezer. The rabbis would actually hold that once a derivative male goes through a female, even in the first scenario, one may never bring the descendant directly — only the proceeds. According to this reading, there is no inconsistency between the two halves; the apparent consensus in the first half is simply Rabbi Eliezer's uncontested position, while the rabbis would contest it there as well.

### Rava's Kedusha Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל גַּבֵּי תְּמוּרַת וְלַד אָשָׁם, דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ, מוֹדֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דִּבְדָמָיו אִין הוּא עַצְמוֹ לָא קָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **But with regard to the offspring of the substitute of a guilt offering, where there is no burnt offering status for its mother,** as the animal for which it was substituted was a guilt offering, **Rabbi Elazar concedes that** an animal purchased **with its money,** received from selling the offspring, **yes,** it is sacrificed as a burnt offering, but the offspring **itself is not sacrificed** as a burnt offering.

Rava disagrees: *afilu teima Rabbanan* — even the rabbis concede the first half of the *mishna*, which represents their view as well. The distinction lies elsewhere.

The rabbis dispute Rabbi Eliezer only in the case of *hamafrish nekeivah l'olah* — where a female was directly sanctified as an *olah*. Why? Because *d'imei lo karva* — the mother could never have been brought as an *olah*. The beginning of the chain was itself unbringable. This is directly analogous to the earlier case of sanctifying an animal with a *mum* and then having an unblemished child — if the original animal could not be brought, its derivatives cannot be brought either, even when a viable animal appears further down the chain.

*Aval temurah d'imei nami karva* — but if you begin with a male *olah* that is duly sanctified, and the *temurah* happens to be female, the top of the chain *could* be brought. *Afilu Rabbanan modu* — even the rabbis agree that when you subsequently hit a male further down that chain, you may bring it. The *kedusha* at the top of the chain was a full, bringable *kedusha*, and the chain retains that character.

Rava's model is thus precise: the rabbis require that the top of the chain be an animal that itself could have been brought. Rabbi Eliezer relaxes that requirement — he does not demand that the mother be bringable, only that there be a consistency of *kedusha* throughout the chain. If you start with a female *olah*, it cannot be brought, but it carries *kedushas olah*, and when the descendant is male and also carries *kedushas olah*, Rabbi Eliezer says you may bring it.

### Asham Case Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר יִקְרַב עוֹלָה הוּא עַצְמוֹ? וּרְמִינְהוּ: תְּמוּרַת אָשָׁם, וְלַד תְּמוּרָה, וְלָדָן, וְלַד וְלָדָן עַד סוֹף כׇּל הָעוֹלָם — יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיִפְּלוּ דְּמֵיהֶן לִנְדָבָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָמוּתוּ. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: יָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶן עוֹלָה. בַּדָּמִים — אִין, הוּא עַצְמוֹ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Elazar say** that the **offspring** of a female designated as a burnt offering **itself is sacrificed as a burnt offering? But** one may **raise a contradiction** from the mishna (20b): **The substitute of a guilt offering, the offspring of that substitute,** and **their offspring** and **the offspring of their offspring, until the end of all time,** all **graze until they become unfit, and then** each animal **is sold, and** the **money** received for the sale **is allocated for** communal **gift offerings. Rabbi Eliezer says:** These animals are not left to graze until they become unfit and then each animal is sold; rather **they** are left to **die. Rabbi Elazar says:** Communal gift offerings are not purchased with the money from the sale; rather, the owner should **bring** an individual **burnt offering with** the **money** received for **its** sale. The Gemara infers from Rabbi Elazar's statement that **with** the **money** received for its sale, **yes,** one brings an individual burnt offering, but the offspring of the substitute **itself, no,** one may not bring it as a burnt offering.

The *gemara* now tests Rava's model with a new *beraisa* concerning the *asham*. The *asham*, like the *olah*, is male-only, but it differs in an important respect: the *temurah* or descendant of an *asham* is not brought directly — it must be left to graze, develop a *mum*, and then the proceeds are used. In the case of a standard *asham* that gets "derailed" — whether through death of the owner, prior atonement, or other circumstances — the proceeds go to a communal *olah*. This is not the same as bringing the animal as an *olah*; it is a secondary resolution.

The *beraisa* reads: *tmuras asham u'vlad tmura u'vlad vlad*, all the way to the end of time — *yiru ad sheyista'avu v'yimachru v'yipelu demeihem linedava* — they graze until they develop a *mum*, are sold, and the proceeds go toward *nedava*, i.e., a communal *olah*. Rabbi Eliezer says they must die — consistent with his general position that *ha'asham ke'chatas*, the *asham* follows the laws of a *chatas*, and since the offspring of a *chatas* must die, so must the offspring of an *asham*. Rabbi Elazar (a distinct figure from Rabbi Eliezer) says *yavi bidmeihen olah* — use the money for an *olah* — referring to a private, rather than communal, *olah*.

Crucially, neither the *tanna kamma* nor Rabbi Elazar says to bring the animal itself, even if a male is reached down the chain. This is precisely where the *gemara* asks its pointed question: *bidamim in, gufo lo* — the money, yes; the animal itself, no. But this scenario begins with a male *asham* at the top of the chain. According to Rava, the rabbis should agree that when you hit a male descendant, you can bring it — because the top of the chain *was* bringable. Why then does everyone agree here that you cannot?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְדִבְרֵיהֶם דְּרַבָּנַן קָאָמַר לְהוּ, לְדִידִי סְבִירָא לִי דַּאֲפִילּוּ וָלָד נָמֵי קָרֵב עוֹלָה, לְדִידְכוּ דְּאָמְרִיתוּ רוֹעֶה — אוֹדוֹ לִי מִיהַת דְּמוֹתָרוֹת לְנִדְבַת יָחִיד אָזְלִי, וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מוֹתָרוֹת לְנִדְבַת צִיבּוּר אָזְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: Rabbi Elazar was speaking to them in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis,** as follows: **According to me, I hold that even the offspring** itself **is also sacrificed** as **a burnt offering.** But **according to you, who say** that the animal is left to **graze** until it becomes blemished and then it is sold and the money used for the purchase of burnt offerings, **concede to me at least that the remainder** of the money **goes for** the purchase of **individual gift** offerings, rather than communal offerings. **And** the Rabbis **said to him** in response that **the remainder** of the money **goes for communal gift** offerings.

Rav Chisda resolves this according to the approach of Rabbah bar bar Chana: he argues that Rabbi Elazar is speaking *lidvreihem d'Rabbanan* — he is engaging the rabbis on their own terms. The rabbis, in his view, would always say you cannot bring a descendant, regardless of whether the chain began with a bringable animal. Rabbi Elazar's own position, as he himself says, is that even the descendant itself could be brought as an *olah* if it is male — *lididi sevira li de'afilu vlad nami karev olah*. He is only challenging the rabbis on whether the proceeds go to a communal or private *olah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: עַד כַּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הוּא עַצְמוֹ יִקְרַב עוֹלָה, אֶלָּא בְּמַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָה, דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** another explanation: **Rabbi Elazar says** that the offspring **itself is offered** as **a burnt offering only** in a case **where he designated a female** animal **as a burnt offering** and it gave birth, **since there is burnt offering status** for a bird that is the same sex as **its mother.**

Rava must answer differently, since his model holds that the rabbis would agree to bring the descendant when the chain began with a bringable animal. The key is this: *ad kan lo ka'amar Rabbi Eliezer hu atzmo yakriv olah ela b'mafrish nekeivah l'olah d'ika shem olah al imo*, Rabbi Eliezer only permits bringing the descendant in the case of a female *olah* — *d'ika shem olah al imo*, because the mother retains the *shem olah*, the name of *olah*. *Aval gabei tmuras vlad asham*, in the *asham* case, *deleika shem olah al imo* — the mother does not have the name of an *olah* at all.

This is the heart of Rava's refined model. When an *asham* becomes derailed — through *temurah*, through offspring passing through female intermediaries — the proceeds, when they must eventually be realized, go toward an *olah*, not an *asham*. This means the *kedusha* at the point of eventual bringing is *kedushas olah*, while the original sanctification was *kedushas asham*. They do not match. Even Rabbi Eliezer, who permits bringing a descendant that began from an unbringable mother, insists on one condition: that the *kedusha* of the descendant must be the same as the *kedusha* of the original. When the chain begins as *asham* and the descendant would be brought as an *olah*, that condition is not met — *modei Rabbi Eliezer debidmei in hu atzmo lo*.

The resulting picture is nuanced but coherent. According to the rabbis, the top of the chain must have been itself bringable. According to Rabbi Eliezer, the top need not have been bringable, but the *kedusha* must remain consistent throughout the chain. When the *asham* chain is brought as an *olah*, consistency is broken, and even Rabbi Eliezer agrees the animal itself may not be brought.

One might ask whether this creates a practical absurdity: if someone committed a transgression requiring an *asham*, could none of these derived animals serve? The answer is yes — one would need to set aside a third animal as the actual *asham*. The derived animal, once it has a *mum*, generates proceeds that go toward an *olah*, not toward the *asham* obligation.

### Abaye's Motar Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְכִי בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: הַמַּפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְפִסְחוֹ — תִּרְעֶה עַד (שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב) [שֶׁתִּסְתָּאֵב], וְתִמָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמֶיהָ פֶּסַח. יָלְדָה — יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב, וְיִמָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמָיו פֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection** to Rava: **And does Rabbi Elazar require that there be burnt offering status for its mother,** in order for the offspring to be sacrificed as a burnt offering? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who designates a female** animal **for his Paschal offering,** which must be a male, the animal is left to **graze until it becomes unfit, and it is** then **sold and he brings a Paschal offering with** the **money** received for **its** sale. If the female animal **gave birth** to a male,the offspring may not be sacrificed as a Paschal offering despite the fact that it is a male. Rather, **it** is left to **graze until it becomes unfit, and** then **it is sold and he brings a Paschal offering with** the **money** received from **its** sale.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁתַּיְּירָה אַחַר הַפֶּסַח, תִּרְעֶה עַד (שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב) [שֶׁתִּסְתָּאֵב], וְיָבִיא בְּדָמֶיהָ שְׁלָמִים. יָלְדָה, יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב, וְיִמָּכֵר וְיָבִיא בְּדָמָיו שְׁלָמִים. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הוּא עַצְמוֹ יִקְרַב שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **remained** without a blemish until **after Passover, it** is left to **graze until it becomes unfit, and he brings a peace offering with** the **money** received for **its** sale. If **it gave birth** to a male after Passover, the offspring too is left to **graze until it becomes unfit, and** then **it is sold, and he brings a peace offering with** the **money** received for **its** sale. **Rabbi Elazar** disagrees in the latter case and **says:** The offspring **itself is sacrificed as a peace offering.**

Abaye challenges Rava's model. *Ve-chi ba'ei Rabbi Eliezer shem olah al imo* — does Rabbi Eliezer truly require that the original animal carry the same *kedusha* as the descendant one seeks to bring? Abaye proposes an entirely different framework. He cites the principle: *lemakom shehaMosar holech haVlad holech* — wherever the *motar* goes, the child goes. The *motar*, the leftover, refers to cases where you have an excess or substitute sanctified animal — where, for instance, you lost your *korban*, designated another, and then found the original. The *halacha* for what is done with that leftover animal is taken as the template for what is done with any descendant.

### Pesach Before/After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁם שְׁלָמִים עַל אִמּוֹ, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר יִקְרַב שְׁלָמִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ (רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר) [רָבָא]: ״אַחַר הַפֶּסַח״ קָא אָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי אַחַר הַפֶּסַח, דְּמוֹתַר פֶּסַח גּוּפֵיהּ קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye explains his objection: **But here** it is a case **where there is no peace offering status for its mother,** as the mother was consecrated as a Paschal offering, **and** yet **Rabbi Elazar said** that the offspring **is sacrificed** as **a peace offering.** Rava **said to** Abaye in response: **Do you say** that this statement of **Rabbi Elazar** with regard to a Paschal offering **after Passover** contradicts my explanation? Not so; the status of a Paschal offering **after Passover is different, as a leftover Paschal offering itself is sacrificed as a peace offering.** Therefore, a female animal designated as a Paschal offering has the status of a peace offering after Passover.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נִיפְלוֹג נָמֵי בְּרֵישָׁא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, וּפְלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye asked Rava: **If so,** that the reason Rabbi Elazar permits the offspring to be sacrificed is that the mother also has the status of a peace offering, **let** Rabbi Elazar **also disagree** with the Rabbis **in the first clause** of the *baraita*, where the female animal designated as a Paschal offering gave birth before Passover. Rabbi Elazar should state that this offspring itself may be brought as a peace offering, as here too the mother has the status of a peace offering, since a Paschal offering slaughtered before Passover as a peace offering is valid. Rava **said to** Abaye: **Yes,** it **is indeed so, and they disagree** in this case as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: לָא פְּלִיג מִידֵּי, דִּגְמִירִי לִמְקוֹם שֶׁהַמּוֹתָר הוֹלֵךְ — הַוָּלָד הוֹלֵךְ; לְאַחַר הַפֶּסַח, דְּמוֹתָר קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים — וָלָד נָמֵי קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye** suggested another explanation of the opinion of Rabbi Elazar and **said:** In a case where the female animal designated as a Paschal offering gave birth before Passover, there is **nobody** who **disagrees;** rather, they all agree that the offspring may not be sacrificed. **As** it **is learned** as a tradition that **to the place that the leftover** offering **goes, the offspring goes** as well. Therefore, **after Passover, when the leftover** Paschal offering **is sacrificed** as **a peace offering, the offspring is also sacrificed as a peace offering.**

To illustrate the *motar* model, Abaye works through the cases. Begin with the *olah*: if you lose an *olah* and designate another, and then find the original, both are brought. Therefore, any descendant of an *olah* is also brought. Now consider the *asham*: if you lose an *asham* and designate another, and then find the original, you have *niskaper baal be'acher ba'asham* — you bring one, and the other must graze and cannot be brought itself. Therefore, the descendant of an *asham* is likewise not brought itself, only its proceeds are used. The *motar* model maps the treatment of leftover substitutes onto the treatment of descendants.

The *pesach* case is particularly instructive. The *mishna* under discussion begins with *hamafrish nekeivah l'pesach* — a female designated as a *korban pesach*, which must be male. She and her children must graze until they develop a *mum*, after which the proceeds go toward a *pesach*. This is the *before-Pesach* scenario. But then: *nish'ar achar Pesach* — if the animal remains after Pesach has passed, it grazes and the proceeds go toward a *shelamim*. Why? Because a *pesach* that passes Pesach without being offered becomes a *shelamim*: *mosar pesach gufa karev shelamim*. And if the child is born after Pesach, Rabbi Eliezer says *hu atzmo yakriv shelamim* — the child itself may be brought as a *shelamim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לִפְנֵי הַפֶּסַח, אִימֵּיהּ לְמַאי אַקְדְּשַׁהּ? לִדְמֵי פֶסַח, וָלָד נָמֵי לִדְמֵי פֶסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **But before Passover,** when the Paschal offering is not yet considered leftover, the offspring is endowed with the same sanctity as the mother. In **what** way **is the mother consecrated?** It is consecrated **for the value of a Paschal offering,** that is, so that it should be sold and a Paschal offering should be purchased with the proceeds, as the female animal itself may not be sacrificed as a Paschal offering. If so, **the offspring as well** is consecrated only **for the value of a Paschal offering.**

This case is used to challenge Rava's model in a striking way. Here the *kedusha* at the top of the chain is *kedushas pesach*, not *kedushas shelamim*. Yet Rabbi Eliezer permits bringing the child as a *shelamim*. Does he not require, as Rava claims, that the descendant's *kedusha* match the original? The *gemara* responds on Rava's behalf: *shani achar Pesach, demosar Pesach gufia karev shelamim* — the case after Pesach is different precisely because a leftover *pesach* itself becomes a *shelamim*. The *kedusha* of the chain has effectively transformed into *kedushas shelamim*, and therefore the descendant can be brought as a *shelamim*. Rava's requirement of matching *kedusha* is thus satisfied, since by the time the child is to be brought, the *kedusha* throughout the chain is *shelamim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: וּמִי אָמְרִינַן מִדְּאִימֵּיהּ לִדְמֵי, וָלָד נָמֵי לִדְמֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַמַּפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לַפֶּסַח — הִיא וּוַלְדוֹתֶיהָ יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶם פֶּסַח. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הוּא עַצְמוֹ יִקְרַב פֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ukva bar Ḥama raises an objection** to this explanation of Abaye: **And do we say** that Rabbi Elazar maintains **that as its mother** is consecrated only **for the value of** a Paschal offering, **the offspring as well** is consecrated only **for the value of** a Paschal offering? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who designates a female** animal **as a Paschal offering, it and its offspring** are left to **graze until they become unfit, and** then **they are sold, and he brings a Paschal offering with** the **money** received for **their** sale. **Rabbi Elazar says:** The offspring **itself is sacrificed** as **a Paschal offering.**

But if so, the *gemara* asks, why does Rabbi Eliezer not argue in the *before-Pesach* case — why not allow the male child to be brought as a *pesach*? After all, the mother has *kedushas pesach*, and the male child has the same *kedusha*, which is exactly what Rava requires. Rava responds: *ein hachi nami palig* — actually, Rabbi Eliezer would indeed argue there too. The *braisa* only chose to illustrate his position in the after-Pesach case, but he would equally permit the male child before Pesach to be brought as a *pesach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הָכָא, דְּאִימֵּיהּ לִדְמֵי, וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הוּא עַצְמוֹ קָרֵב פֶּסַח, וְלָא מוֹקְמִינַן לֵיהּ בְּאִימֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye explains his objection: **But here** it is a case **where its mother** was consecrated **for the value of** a Paschal offering, **and** nevertheless **Rabbi Elazar said** that the offspring **itself is sacrificed as a Paschal offering, and we do not establish** the status of the offspring based upon the sanctity **of the mother.**

Abaye, however, says *lo palig* — Rabbi Eliezer would not argue in the before-Pesach case. His reasoning invokes the *motar* principle. Abaye says: *midi degamiri*, this is a received tradition — *lemakom shehaMosar holech haVlad holech*. Before Pesach, if you have a leftover or substitute *pesach* that is not brought, what do you do? You use it for *dmei pesach* — the proceeds are used toward a future *pesach*, not the animal itself. Therefore, the same applies to the child: *vlad nami lidmei pesach* — the child is also only for the value of a *pesach*, not the *pesach* itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: בְּמַפְרִישׁ בְּהֵמָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת עָסְקִינַן, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר סָבַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: אִם שִׁיְּירוֹ מְשׁוּיָּיר, דְּעוּבָּר לָאו יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא, וְאִמּוֹ הִיא דְּלָא קָדְשָׁה קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, אֲבָל הִיא קָדְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says:** One can answer that here **we are dealing with** a case of one **who designates a pregnant animal,** and **Rabbi Elazar holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** with regard to one who consecrates a pregnant animal for a specific purpose, that **if he left it out,** i.e., designated the fetus as having a different sanctity, it is **left out** from the sanctity of the mother and consecrated in accordance with the designated sanctity. The reason is **that a fetus is not** considered **the thigh of its mother,** but rather the mother and its offspring are considered two separate animals. Here too, it is only **its mother that is not sanctified with the inherent sanctity** of a Paschal offering, as it is female, but only for the value of a Paschal offering, as it is female. **But** the offspring **is consecrated** as a Paschal offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּבִבְהֵמָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת עָסְקִינַן, מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״הִיא וּוַלְדוֹתֶיהָ״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina: This, too, stands to reason, that we are dealing with** a case where he designated **a pregnant animal, from** the fact **that** the *baraita* **teaches: It and its offspring.** This indicates that both the mother and its offspring were in existence at the time of the consecration. The Gemara comments: **Conclude from here** that this explanation is correct.

Tosafot, however, while noting that this *motar* language does not fit the words of the *gemara* entirely cleanly, proposes a deeper explanation that he considers to be the heart of Abaye's position. The real reason Rabbi Eliezer would not permit the male child to be brought as a *pesach* before Pesach — even though the *kedusha* matches — is that bringing the child effectively *derails* the mother. When a *korban pesach* has already been brought by someone else, and the original becomes a leftover, the original is genuinely derailed. Here, if the male child is brought as the *pesach*, it is as if to say: the child is the *pesach*, not the mother. But each person may only bring one *korban pesach*, tied to a specific *chabura*. By selecting the child over the mother, you are rejecting the mother's claim entirely.

This stands in contrast to the *olah* case. If a female *olah* cannot be brought, and the male child is brought as an *olah*, no competition or rejection is involved — if the mother had been male, both would have been brought. The mother's inability to be brought is simply a function of gender, not a displacement. But with *pesach*, there is only one per person, and choosing the child necessarily excludes the mother. Tosafot understands Abaye's position as: you cannot bring the child when doing so constitutes a real rejection — an effective stripping of status — from the mother.

The same logic, Tosafot notes, applies to *asham*. Just as there is only one *korban pesach* per person per year, so too there is only one *asham* per specific transgression. To bring the male child as an *asham* when the mother is also an *asham* is to say that the child's *asham* is the real one, and the mother's *asham* is not. That constitutes a derailment of the mother's status, and therefore even Rabbi Eliezer agrees in that case.

The *gemara* concludes by introducing the challenge of Rav Ukva bar Rachma: *umi amrinan mid'imi lidmei vlad nami lidmei* — would we truly say that whenever the mother is only for the proceeds, so too the child? A *braisa* seems to contradict this: it says that one who sets aside a female for *pesach* — she and her children graze until they develop a *mum*, and the proceeds go for a *pesach*. Yet Rabbi Eliezer says *hu atzmo yikrav pesach* — the child itself may be brought as a *pesach*. This directly contradicts Abaye's view that the child could not be brought as a *pesach* in the before-Pesach case.

### Pregnant Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: בְּמַפְרִישׁ בְּהֵמָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת עָסְקִינַן, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר סָבַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: אִם שִׁיְּירוֹ מְשׁוּיָּיר, דְּעוּבָּר לָאו יֶרֶךְ אִמּוֹ הוּא, וְאִמּוֹ הִיא דְּלָא קָדְשָׁה קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף, אֲבָל הִיא קָדְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says:** One can answer that here **we are dealing with** a case of one **who designates a pregnant animal,** and **Rabbi Elazar holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who said** with regard to one who consecrates a pregnant animal for a specific purpose, that **if he left it out,** i.e., designated the fetus as having a different sanctity, it is **left out** from the sanctity of the mother and consecrated in accordance with the designated sanctity. The reason is **that a fetus is not** considered **the thigh of its mother,** but rather the mother and its offspring are considered two separate animals. Here too, it is only **its mother that is not sanctified with the inherent sanctity** of a Paschal offering, but only for the value of a Paschal offering, as it is female. **But** the offspring **is consecrated** as a Paschal offering.

Ravina resolves the contradiction: *bemafrish beheima me'uberes askinan* — the *braisa* is dealing with a case where the animal was already pregnant at the time of sanctification. Ravina invokes the position of Rabbi Yochanan: *im shiyaro meshuyar*, that the fetus may be sanctified independently of the mother, since *ubar lav yerech imo hu* — the fetus is not considered a limb of the mother and may receive its own independent *kedusha*.

In the *braisa*'s case, the mother was sanctified for the *value* of a *pesach* but the fetus was directly sanctified as a *pesach* itself. There is no question of derivative *kedusha*, no question of rejecting the mother, because the child's *kedusha* does not derive from the mother's at all. It received its own direct sanctification as a *pesach* and may therefore be brought as one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא דְּבִבְהֵמָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת עָסְקִינַן, מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״הִיא וּוַלְדוֹתֶיהָ״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina: This, too, stands to reason, that we are dealing with** a case where he designated **a pregnant animal, from** the fact **that** the *baraita* **teaches: It and its offspring.** This indicates that both the mother and its offspring were in existence at the time of the consecration. The Gemara comments: **Conclude from here** that this explanation is correct.

Mar Zutra son of Rav Mari confirms that this reading is indeed compelling, pointing to the *braisa*'s language: *hi u'veladoseha* — "she and her children" — which implies the children were already present or imminent at the time of sanctification, unlike the other *braisa* which describes a subsequent birth.

The overall framework emerging from Rava and Abaye's positions, as clarified throughout this extended discussion, is as follows. According to the rabbis, a descendant may be brought in its own right only if the top of the chain was itself bringable. According to Rabbi Eliezer, even an unbringable animal at the top of the chain does not disqualify the descendants — provided two conditions are met: first, the *kedusha* must be consistent throughout (*asham* cannot beget an *olah*); and second, bringing the descendant must not constitute a real rejection or derailment of the mother's own claim (*pesach* and *asham* both fail this test). Where neither of these limitations applies — as in the case of a female *olah* giving birth to a male — Rabbi Eliezer permits the child to be brought directly.

## Female Asham Case

### Female Asham Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּמַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְאָשָׁם שֶׁאֵין בְּנָהּ קָרֵב אָשָׁם. פְּשִׁיטָא! עַד כַּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אֶלָּא בְּמַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְעוֹלָה, דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ, אֲבָל גַּבֵּי מַפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְאָשָׁם דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁם אָשָׁם עַל אִמּוֹ — אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר מוֹדֶה דְּלֹא קָרֵב אָשָׁם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: And Rabbi Elazar concedes** to the opinion of the Rabbis with regard to a case **where** one **designates a female** animal **for a guilt offering,** which is only brought from a male animal, **that its offspring is not sacrificed** as **a guilt offering.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** As **Rabbi Elazar states** that the offspring may be sacrificed **only if** one **designates a female** animal **for a burnt offering** and it gives birth, due to the fact **that there is burnt offering status** for a bird that is the same sex as **its mother. But with regard to** one who **designates a female** animal **as a guilt offering, where there is no guilt offering status for its mother, even Rabbi Elazar concedes that** its offspring **is not sacrificed** as **a guilt offering.**

With that framework in place, the *gemara* turns to a directly related statement. Rav Yosef bar Chanina says: *umodeh Rabbi Eliezer b'mafrish nekeivah l'asham she'ein benah karev asham* — even Rabbi Eliezer agrees that if one sets aside a female as an *asham*, the male child may not be brought as an *asham*. This is not an instance where Rabbi Eliezer takes his expansive view; he concedes that the child cannot itself be brought directly.

The case here is subtler than the earlier *asham* discussion. Previously, the reason the descendant could not be brought was that the chain had passed through a *temurah*, at which point the *kedusha* shifted from *kedushas asham* to *kedushas olah*. Once you perform *temurah* on an *asham*, the resulting animal — if it cannot be brought — is treated as derailed, and the proceeds go toward an *olah*, not an *asham*. So the *kedusha* at the bottom of the chain is *olah* while the top is *asham*, and Rava's matching requirement is violated.

But here, no *temurah* has occurred. One simply sanctified a female directly as an *asham* and it gave birth to a male. No lateral move into *olah* territory has taken place. The mother's *kedusha* is *kedushas asham*, the child's potential *kedusha* is *kedushas asham* — the *kedusha* appears to match. Why then does even Rabbi Eliezer agree the child cannot be brought?

### Mother Status in Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָאו דְּאַשְׁמְעִינַן, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּשֵׁם עוֹלָה עַל אִמּוֹ, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲזֵי וָלָד לְהַקְרָבָה, וְהַאי נָמֵי הָא חֲזֵי לְהַקְרָבָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this statement is nevertheless necessary, for **if** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, had **not informed us** of this *halakha*, **I would say** that **the reason** for the ruling **of Rabbi Elazar,** that one who designates a female animal for a burnt offering may sacrifice the offspring as a burnt offering, **is not due to** the fact **that** there is **burnt offering status** for a bird with the same sex **as its mother; rather** it is **because the offspring is fit as an offering,** as it is a male, **and this too,** the offspring of the female that was designated as a guilt offering, **is** likewise **fit as an offering,** as it is a male. Therefore, he **teaches us** that this offspring is not sacrificed even according to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar.

The *gemara* responds: *peshita* — this is obvious! *Asham lo ka'amar Rabbi Eliezer ela b'mafrish nekeivah l'olah d'ika shem olah al imo* — Rabbi Eliezer permitted bringing the male child only in the case of a female *olah*, because in that case the mother still carries the *shem olah*, the name of *olah* — she simply cannot be brought due to gender. *Aval gabei mafrish nekeivah l'asham deleika shem asham al imo* — by contrast, a female sanctified as an *asham* does not truly carry the *shem asham*, the identity of an *asham*. And therefore *afilu Rabbi Eliezer modeh delo karev asham* — even Rabbi Eliezer agrees the child may not be brought as an *asham*.

### Derailing the Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַדְּמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאֵין בְּנָהּ קָרֵב אָשָׁם, נַישְׁמְעִינַן דְּאֵין בְּנָהּ קָרֵב עוֹלָה, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְאָשָׁם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina's ruling is necessary to exclude the possibility that Rabbi Elazar's reason is that the offspring is suitable as an offering, then say the following: **Rather than teach us that the offspring** of a female designated as a guilt offering **is not sacrificed** as **a guilt offering, let him teach us** a more expansive ruling, **that its offspring is not sacrificed** as **a burnt offering,** despite the fact that the mother is left to graze until it becomes unfit, at which point it is sold, and the proceeds are used for the purchase of a burnt offering. **And** from that ruling one would know that **the same is true** that the offspring is not sacrificed **as a guilt offering.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן עוֹלָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: עוֹלָה הוּא דְּלָא קָרְבָה, דְּלָא אַקְדְּשַׁהּ לְאִמַּהּ (קְדוּשָּׁה עוּבָּרָהּ) [קְדוּשַּׁת עוֹלָה], אֲבָל אָשָׁם — [כֵּיוָן דְּאַקְדְּשַׁהּ לְאִימֵּיהּ לְאָשָׁם] — אֵימָא: וָלָד קָרֵב אָשָׁם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If he would teach us** that according to Rabbi Elazar the offspring is not sacrificed as **a burnt offering, I would say** that it is only as **a burnt offering that** the offspring **is not sacrificed, because he did not consecrate the mother** with the same type of **sanctity** for the sake of which **the fetus** would be sacrificed. This is because the mother was consecrated as a guilt offering whereas the offspring would have been sacrificed as a burnt offering. **But** with regard to the option of sacrificing the offspring as **a guilt offering,** when the offspring has the same type of sanctity as that with which the mother was consecrated, I might **say** that **the offspring is sacrificed as a guilt offering.** Therefore, he **teaches us** that it is not sacrificed as a guilt offering, even according to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar.

The *gemara*'s language — that the mother "does not have the name of *asham*" — requires explanation, since it is not immediately clear how this differs from the *olah* case. Tosafot addresses this directly, drawing on the principle established through the *pesach* discussion: the reason is that bringing the child as an *asham* would effectively remove the *shem asham* from the mother. An *asham* is brought for a specific transgression; one may only bring one *asham* for a given sin. If the child is presented as the *asham* for that transgression, the mother is thereby cast aside — her status as an *asham* is stripped away by the act of bringing the child. This is precisely the same dynamic as the *pesach* case: choosing the child to be the *korban* necessarily rejects the mother's own claim to that status.

### Kedusha Transfer Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְרִישׁ נְקֵבָה לְאָשָׁם, תִּרְעֶה עַד שֶׁתִּסְתָּאֵב, וְתִימָּכֵר, וְיָבִיא בְּדָמֶיהָ אָשָׁם. וְאִם קָרַב אֲשָׁמוֹ, יִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who designates a female** animal **for a guilt offering,** which may be brought only from males, **it** is left to **graze until it becomes blemished and** then **it is sold, and he brings a guilt offering with** the **money** received for **its** sale. **And if** in the interim, he designated a male animal and **his guilt offering was** already **sacrificed,** so that a guilt offering is no longer needed, the **money** received for the sale of the blemished female **is allocated for** communal **gift offerings.**

To summarize the accumulated rules: according to the rabbis, a descendant may be brought directly only if the top of the chain was itself a bringable animal — that is, if one began with a male for a male-only *korban*, and a male reappears further down the chain. According to Rabbi Eliezer, one need not begin with a bringable animal, but two conditions govern his leniency: first, the *kedusha* must remain consistent from top to bottom of the chain (the *asham*-to-*olah* shift disqualifies the descendant); and second, bringing the child must not constitute a genuine displacement or rejection of the mother's claim (the single-*pesach*, single-*asham* principle). Where both conditions are satisfied — as in the case of a female *olah* with a male child — Rabbi Eliezer permits the child to be brought. Where either condition fails, he concurs with the rabbis that the child may not be brought and the proceeds alone are used.

## Female Asham Mishna

### Female Asham Blemish Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: תִּימָּכֵר שֶׁלֹּא בְּמוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Since a female is unfit to be sacrificed as a guilt offering, its halakhic status is like that of a blemished animal in the sense that it does not become inherently sacred; rather, its value alone becomes sacred. Therefore, **it may be sold without a blemish,** and a guilt offering is purchased with the money received for its sale.

The final *mishna* of this *daf* returns to the practical resolution of the female *asham* case. If one sets aside a female for an *asham*, she grazes — *tireh ad shetista'ev* — until she develops a *mum*. The gender mismatch is, as noted, not itself sufficient for redemption; a genuine physical *mum* is required. Once she has a *mum* she is sold, and the proceeds are used to bring an *asham* — *vetimacher v'yavi bidmeiha asham*.

This conclusion may initially seem surprising: one might have assumed that since the proceeds of a derailed *asham* typically go toward an *olah*, the same would apply here. The distinction is important: the proceeds go to an *olah* only when the *asham* has been genuinely derailed — through a *temurah*, through the children becoming derivatives, or through the owner having already been atoned by another means (*nitkapru be'aleha be'acher*). When the animal simply has a *mum* — or, in this case, is the wrong gender but otherwise intact in its sanctity — it has not been derailed. It is merely unable to be brought in its current form. In that case, the proceeds are used for a proper *asham*, maintaining the continuity of the original *kedusha*.

### Derailment Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּלְמָה לִי תִּסְתָּאֵב? תִּימָּכֵר! כֵּיוָן דְּלָא חַזְיָא לְמִילְּתָא — הַיְינוּ מוּמָא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And why do I** need to wait until the female animal that was designated as a guilt offering **becomes blemished** before it can be sold? **Let it be sold** immediately even without a blemish: **Since it is** a female and therefore **unfit for the matter** for which it was designated, **this is** the same thing as **a blemish.**

If, however, the owner has already brought another *asham* — *ve'im kariv ashamo* — then this animal is now genuinely *nitkapru be'aleha be'acher*, the owner has been atoned by a different animal. At that point the female *asham* is fully derailed, and *yiplu dameha l'nedavah* — the proceeds go to *nedavah*, the communal *olah* fund. The distinction between these two outcomes turns entirely on whether genuine derailment has occurred or whether the animal simply requires replacement due to a formal disqualification.

### Rabbi Shimon Opinion

Rabbi Shimon takes a dissenting position: *timacher shelo b'mum* — the wrong gender is itself equivalent to a *mum* for purposes of redemption. In his view, the practical inability of the female to ever be brought as an *asham* functions halakhically like a blemish, and therefore the animal may be redeemed without waiting for a physical defect to develop. The majority position, as reflected in the first clause of the *mishna*, is that gender mismatch is not equivalent to a *mum* and the animal must wait for an actual blemish before redemption is available.
