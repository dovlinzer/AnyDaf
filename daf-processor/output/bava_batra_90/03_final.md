# Bava Batra 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inaccurate Measures

### Assur L'Adam Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָסוּר לָאָדָם שֶׁיַּשְׁהֶה מִדָּה חֲסֵרָה אוֹ יְתֵרָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הִיא עָבִיט שֶׁל מֵימֵי רַגְלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: It is prohibited for a person to keep in his house a measure** that is **too small or too large** relative to its supposed volume or weight, **and** this is the case **even** if he does not measure with it but simply uses **it** as **a chamber pot for urine.**

The *daf* opens at the bottom of 89b, where Rav Yehuda cites Rav: *assur l'adam she'yish'heh midah chasera o yesera betoch beito* — it is forbidden for a person to keep in his home a measure that is either too small or too large. As Tosafot notes, the biblical prohibition of *lo yihiyeh lecha be'veitcha even va'aven* applies even before one actively uses the inaccurate measure to defraud; the mere act of keeping it with the intention to use it for that purpose already constitutes a biblical violation. However, if one has no intention of using it as a measure — for instance, one discovered it was inaccurate and intends to repurpose it as a storage bin — there is no biblical prohibition. Nevertheless, Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav rules that even then it is *assur lish'ha betoch beito* — forbidden to keep it around at all.

This prohibition extends even to the most mundane repurposing: *v'afilu la'asot b'hein mei raglayim* — even if one intends to use it as a bedpan. One might reason that a vessel pressed into service as a receptacle for urine would never be returned to commercial use. The Gemara rejects this: we are always concerned that in a moment of forgetfulness or urgency, one might repurpose it back for measuring, and thereby come to use an inaccurate measure inadvertently.

### Raba: Gov't Stamps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּאַתְרָא דְּלָא חֲתִימִי, אֲבָל בְּאַתְרָא דַּחֲתִימִי – אִי לָא חָזֵי חֲתִימָה לָא שָׁקֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: We said** this prohibition **only with regard to a place where** measures **are not stamped** with the government's seal, which confirms that the measure is accurate. **But in a place where** the measures **are stamped, if** the buyer **does not see the seal he would not take** the merchandise.

Raba qualifies this ruling: *lo aman ela b'asrah d'lo chasimi* — this prohibition applies only in a locale where the government does not require official stamps on weights and measures. Where no stamps are required, any container might be pressed into service as a measure, and the concern is genuine. However, *aval b'asrah d'chasimi* — in a place where governmental authorities require that all merchant measures bear an official approval stamp — *ei lo chazi chasima lo shakil*: if a buyer sees no stamp, he will simply refuse to use it. Under those circumstances, there is no realistic concern that an unstamped vessel will be used fraudulently, and the prohibition does not apply with the same force.

This reflects a historically fascinating reality: governmental stamp systems for weights and measures already existed in the Talmudic period, paralleling the development of minted coinage, where the act of minting was itself the governmental guarantee of accurate weight in the precious metal.

### Bein Hashmashot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא; זִימְנִין דְּמִיקְּרֵי בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת, וּמִיקְּרֵי וְשָׁקֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** that **is not so;** one is never permitted to keep incorrect measures in his house, as **sometimes it happens** that one measures **at twilight,** when people are hurried, **and** consequently **it happens** that the buyer **takes** the merchandise despite the fact that it was measured with an incorrect measure.

The Gemara adds a further qualification: *b'asrah d'lo chasimi nami lo amin ela d'lo mehandesi* — even in a place that lacks stamping, the concern is further limited to locales where government inspectors (*mehandesi*) do not circulate and conduct spot inspections. If inspectors do make rounds, one presumably would not risk using an unauthorized measure. This raises an internal question: how would the presence of circulating inspectors prevent *accidental* misuse, as opposed to merely deterring intentional fraud? The Gemara's answer seems to address primarily intentional misuse.

In any case, the Gemara concludes — *v'lo hi* — that relying on circulating inspectors is ultimately insufficient. The reason: *zimnin d'mikri bein hashmashot d'mishkei v'shakil* — there are times when *bein hashmashot* of *Erev Shabbat* arrives, and in the rush of those final moments before Shabbat, the merchant, the buyer, and any Jewish government inspector will all be preoccupied. Under such conditions, an inaccurate measure may be grabbed and used without the usual care. Therefore, even the presence of governmental oversight does not justify keeping an inaccurate measure in one's home.

### Sefer Torah Analogy

A parallel principle applies in other domains: one may not keep a *sefer Torah* in one's home that contains uncorrected errors, for *lo yishkon be-oholecha avla* — one may not allow something that is in a state of perversion to reside in one's tent. The underlying concern is the same: whether the object is a defective measure or a defective *sefer Torah*, keeping it in one's environment creates the conditions for its misuse, whether intentional or inadvertent.

### Modern Scales Q&A

This discussion raises a natural contemporary question: what is the *halacha* in practice today regarding scales in Jewish-owned stores? Digital scales in modern markets are far more precise than the manual balance-and-weight systems of antiquity, but the question of whether a store owner may systematically offset a digital scale slightly in the customer's favor — in order to give a bit more than the exact reading — touches on these very issues. Indeed, one notable kosher establishment apparently calibrates its scale to begin slightly below zero, effectively ensuring the customer always receives a bit extra. As for which type of scale is inherently more accurate, it is worth noting that a traditional balance scale using physical weights has several advantages over a modern spring-loaded scale: springs lose tension over time and are affected by temperature fluctuations, whereas physical weights remain constant. Moreover, a balance scale is unaffected by gravitational variation with elevation. These are not merely theoretical points — they bear on the practical reliability of commercial weighing instruments.

## Permitted Measures

### Baraita Se'ah List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: לֹא יַשְׁהֶה אָדָם מִדָּה חֲסֵרָה אוֹ יְתֵרָה בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ הִיא עָבִיט שֶׁל מֵימֵי רַגְלַיִם. אֲבָל עוֹשֶׂה הוּא סְאָה, תַּרְקַב, וַחֲצִי תַּרְקַב, וְקַב, וַחֲצִי קַב, וְרוֹבַע, וְתוֹמֶן, וַחֲצִי תּוֹמֶן,
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may not keep in his house a measure** that is **too small or too large, even** if **it** is used as **a chamber pot for urine. But he** may **prepare** measures in accordance with the established format: Measurements of **a *se'a*; a *tarkav*,** which is three *kav* or one-half of a *se'a*; **and a half-*tarkav*,** which is one and one-half *kav*; **and a *kav*; and a half-*kav*; and a quarter-*kav***; **and a *tomen*,** which is one-eighth of a *kav*; **and a half-*tomen*;**

A *baraita* reinforces and extends Rav Yehuda's ruling: *lo yisha adam mida chaseira yoter betoch beiso v'afilu hu yavi otam mei raglayim* — one may not keep an inaccurate measure in one's home even as a bedpan. However, the *baraita* then enumerates which measures one *may* produce and keep: *aval asu se'ah, tarkav, chatzi tarkav...* and so on through a list of standard units. The underlying principle governing this list is not merely that these units are legitimate measures — obviously one may keep legitimate measures — but that we are not concerned any of these measures will be confused with one another and thereby used deceptively. The specific question that will emerge is whether any two measures on the list are so close in size that an unscrupulous merchant might substitute one for the other without a buyer's detection.

### Dry Measures

Beginning with dry measures: a *se'ah* is a large unit equal to six *kavs*. A *tarkav* — the name itself being a contraction of *trei va-kav*, meaning "two plus a *kav*" — equals three *kavs*, i.e., half a *se'ah*. Below that comes *chatzi tarkav*, half of a *tarkav*, which is one and a half *kavs*. Then a single *kav*. Then *chatzi kav*. Then *rova*, a quarter *kav*. The pattern is one of successive halving throughout most of the list.

### Log & Ounces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוּכְלָא. וְכַמָּה הִיא עוּכְלָא? אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה בִּרְבִיעַ. וּבְמִדַּת הַלַּח הוּא עוֹשֶׂה הִין, וַחֲצִי הִין, וּשְׁלִישִׁית הַהִין, וּרְבִיעִית הַהִין, וְלוֹג, וַחֲצִי לוֹג, וּרְבִיעִית, וּשְׁמִינִית, וְאֶחָד מִשְּׁמוֹנָה בִּשְׁמִינִית – וְזֶהוּ קוּרְטוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **and an *ukla*. And how much is an *ukla*?** It is **one-fifth of a quarter** of a *kav*. **And** in the case **of liquid measures, one** may **prepare a *hin*,** which is twelve *log*; **and a half-*hin*,** or six *log*; **and a third**-***hin*,** or four *log*; **and a quarter-*hin*,** three *log*; **and a *log*; and a half**-***log*; and a quarter**-*log*; **and an eighth-***log*; **and an eighth of an eighth-***log*, **and this,** the last mentioned, **is a *kortov*.**

A *rova ha-kav* — the quarter-*kav* dry measure — corresponds in volume to a *log* in liquid measure. A *log* is approximately 14 to 16 ounces. Working upward from a *log*: two *log* equal a *tuman*, four *log* equal roughly a half-gallon equivalent, and six *kavs* (a *se'ah*) works out to something in the range of three gallons in liquid terms, with all the usual caveats about cross-applying dry and liquid volume equivalences. A *revi'it* — one quarter of a *log* — is approximately 3.5 ounces, the well-known minimum for a *kiddush* cup.

### Hin & Liquid

For liquid measures, the *baraita* introduces the *hin*, a biblical unit equal to 12 *log* (equivalent in volume to a *tarkav* in dry measure). The Rashbam's equation of these two terms — *hin* and *tarkav* — reflects not a shared name but a shared volume, and explains why the two lists begin to re-synchronize at this point.

The *baraita* permits: a *hin*, a half *hin*, a third of a *hin*, a quarter of a *hin*, and then a *log*, a half *log*, a *revi'it* (quarter *log*), and an eighth of a *log*. Because liquid measures serve more varied purposes than dry — wine is measured out in small quantities for liturgical use, recipes, and daily consumption — the liquid list descends to much smaller increments than the dry list does. Below the *revi'it*, the *baraita* permits an eighth of a *log* and even an eighth of an eighth, which works out to roughly a tenth of an ounce, known as a *kortav*. The *kortav* appears famously in the context of *mikvah*: if a *mikvah* of forty *se'ah* is missing even a *kortav* — a mere smidgen — it is *pasul*.

### Kav Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֶעְבֵּיד נָמֵי קַבַּיִים! אָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בְּתַרְקַב. אַלְמָא טָעוּ אִינָשֵׁי תִּילְתָּא – אִי הָכִי, קַב נָמֵי לָא לֶיעְבֵּיד; דְּאָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בַּחֲצִי תַּרְקַב! אֶלָּא קַבַּיִים הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּלָא עָבֵיד – דְּאָתֵי לְאִיחַלּוֹפֵי בַּחֲצִי תַּרְקַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let one also prepare** a measure equal to **two *kav*.** The Gemara answers that this measure is not used, lest people **come to mistake it for a *tarkav*,** which is three *kav*. The Gemara observes: **Apparently, people err** by **one-third** of a measure. **If that is so, one should also not prepare** a measure equal to **a *kav*, as** people may **come to mistake it for a half-*tarkav*,** which is equal to one and one-half *kav*. **Rather, this is the reason that one may not prepare a two-*kav*** measure: **That** people might **come to mistake it for a half-*tarkav*,** which is equal to one and one-half *kav*.

The Gemara raises a pointed question about one apparent gap in the list: why is a measure of two *kavs* not included? After all, the list permits both one *kav* and one-and-a-half *kavs*, and separately permits three *kavs* (*tarkav*). If the gap between one and one-and-a-half is acceptable, shouldn't the gap between one-and-a-half and two also be acceptable, since both pairs share the same ratio?

The Gemara initially suggests that two *kavs* might be confused with three *kavs* (the *tarkav*), but immediately objects to that answer: *alma tau inshi tilsa?* — are we saying people make errors of a full third? If that were the threshold, then a *kav* should also be prohibited, since the difference between one and one-and-a-half is also a third of the larger figure. The Gemara therefore reframes the answer: two *kavs* is excluded not because it would be confused with three, but because it falls too close to *chatzi tarkav*, which is one-and-a-half *kavs*. The difference between two and one-and-a-half is a quarter of the larger number — and we are apparently concerned that people will not reliably discern a difference of a quarter.

### Quarter & Fifth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא טָעֵי אִינִישׁ רִיבְעָא – אִי הָכִי, חֲצִי תּוֹמֶן וְעוּכְלָא לָא לֶיעְבֵּיד! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מִדּוֹת קְטַנּוֹת בְּקִיאִי בְּהוּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara again suggests: **Apparently, people err** by **one-quarter** of a measure. **If that is so, one should also not prepare** measures of **a half-*tomen*,** which is one-sixteenth of a *kav*, **and an *ukla*,** which is one-twentieth of a *kav*. Since they differ by only one-fifth, there is a concern that people might mistake one measure for the other. **Rav Pappa said: People are well-versed in small measures** and can distinguish between them.

This conclusion — that a discernment threshold of a quarter is too close — sets up the next question: if so, why does the *baraita* permit both a *chatzi tuman* (a quarter *log*) and an *uchla* (a fifth of a *log*)? The difference between these two measures is smaller than a quarter. Specifically, if one considers the relative gap between four and five equal parts, the difference amounts to a fifth of the larger — even less than the quarter that was just ruled insufficient. How can these two measures both appear on the permitted list?

Rav Pappa answers: *midot katanot beki'ei behu inshi* — with small measures, people are highly discerning. When the measures themselves are tiny objects, users who work with them regularly develop a practiced eye and can readily detect the difference between, say, a quarter *log* and a fifth of a *log*. This is at least the Gemara's claim, even if it runs somewhat counter to intuition: one might think larger measures are easier to distinguish because the absolute difference is greater, while smaller measures might seem harder to differentiate. The Gemara's position appears to be that familiarity and daily handling of small measures sharpens one's ability to detect differences, even when the absolute quantities involved are minute.

### Mikdash Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלִישִׁית הַהִין, רְבִיעִית הַהִין – לָא לֶיעְבֵּיד! כֵּיוָן דַּהֲווֹ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן. בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ נָמֵי לִיגְזוֹר! כֹּהֲנִים זְרִיזִין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If people err by one-quarter of a measure, then since one may prepare a measure equal to four *log*, **one-third of a *hin*, let one not prepare** a measure equal to three *log*, **one-quarter** of **a *hin*.** The Gemara answers: **Since** these measures **were** used **in the Temple, the Sages did not decree** that they not be used. The Gemara asks: **In the Temple as well, let** the Sages **decree** that they should not be used, in case the two measures are mistaken for each other. The Gemara answers: The **priests** who serve in the Temple **are vigilant** and would not commit this error.

A final question remains: the *baraita* permits both a third of a *hin* and a quarter of a *hin*. A third of a *hin* is four *log*, and a quarter of a *hin* is three *log*. This is the same ratio as four to three — precisely the ratio of two *kavs* to one-and-a-half *kavs* — which we just established is close enough to cause confusion. How, then, can a third and a quarter of a *hin* both be permitted?

The Gemara answers: *keivan de-havu be-Mikdash lo gazru behu Rabbanan* — since these measures are found in the Torah in the context of the *Mikdash*, the Rabbis did not wish to prohibit their use. To prohibit a third and a quarter of a *hin* would effectively outlaw biblical measures. The concern would otherwise be that an unscrupulous merchant could substitute one for the other without a buyer's awareness; but we do not legislate away biblically mandated measures.

Tosafot asks a pointed follow-up: even granting that one cannot simply abolish these measures, why not require that they always be measured out in *log* units rather than as fractional *hin* — e.g., three *log* rather than a quarter *hin*? The Gemara's answer is directed specifically at the *Mikdash* context: *Kohanim zerizim heim* — the *kohanim* are careful and meticulous in their service, and there is no realistic concern that a *kohen* performing the *avodah* will confuse a third of a *hin* with a quarter of a *hin*. Outside the *Mikdash*, for ordinary commerce, the Rabbis were rightly concerned about substitution. Within the *Mikdash*, that concern does not apply with the same force.

## Shmuel's Sixth Rule

### Ein Mosifin Shtut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הַמִּדּוֹת יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת, וְלֹא עַל הַמַּטְבֵּעַ יָתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת, וְהַמִּשְׂתַּכֵּר אַל יִשְׁתַּכֵּר יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel says:** If the residents of a certain place want to change the standard of their measures and augment them by a certain fraction, **they may not increase the measures** by **more than one-sixth, and** they may **not** increase the value of **a coin** by **more than one-sixth** of its previous value. **And one who profits** from his sales **may not profit** by **more than one-sixth.**

Shmuel states a broader and more conceptually significant rule: *ein mosifin al ha-middot yoter mi-shtut, v'lo al ha-matbeia yoter mi-shtut* — one may not increase measures by more than a sixth, and one may not alter coinage by more than a sixth. This statement addresses a specific but consequential practice: the governmental or communal redefinition of weights, measures, or currency so that the old name now refers to a larger (or different) quantity. The example is vivid: if what was once called a gallon contained 100 units, and authorities now mandate that a "gallon" contain 150, that is a 50% increase — far beyond what Shmuel permits. Even if the intent is to standardize or modernize the system, the increase cannot exceed a sixth of the new, larger measure.

The coinage parallel is equally direct. Rather than a fixed-weight precious metal simply being recoined at a different weight, the concern is that the name of the unit — the *shekel*, the *maneh* — might be reassigned to refer to a different quantity of silver or gold. Shmuel rules that any such redefinition is capped at a sixth.

The *shtut* — the sixth — immediately recalls the threshold in *ona'ah*, and the Gemara will shortly engage with whether this rule is indeed grounded in concerns similar to *ona'ah* or something distinct.

### Price Spike Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הַמִּדּוֹת יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת – מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם אַפְקוֹעֵי תַּרְעָא, שְׁתוּת נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes these statements. When Shmuel said: **They may not increase the measures** by **more than one-sixth, what is the reason** for this? **If we say** it is **because** doing so causes market **prices to rise,** the same concern should apply to raising the prices by **one-sixth,** and therefore this should **also not** be allowed.

The Gemara raises the first candidate rationale: *ilima mishum afkuei tara* — perhaps the concern is that a large increase in measure size will cause a spike in prices. The reasoning: if what was sold as a "gallon" now contains fifty percent more, the nominal price of a gallon must rise proportionally. Once a merchant has occasion to raise prices significantly, he may pad the increase beyond what is justified. A small change can be managed cleanly; a large change invites abuse.

But the Gemara immediately challenges this reasoning: *shtut nami hava* — even a sixth can give rise to a price spike. If a merchant is inclined to use the legitimate occasion of measure-adjustment to pad his prices beyond what is warranted, a sixth increase is just as much an opportunity as a larger increase. There is no principled reason why the price-spike concern would draw the line precisely at a sixth rather than at some other figure. This explanation for the rule therefore fails.

### Rava: Bitul Mekach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם אוֹנָאָה – דְּלָא לֶיהֱוֵי בִּיטּוּל מִקָּח? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁבְּמִדָּה וְשֶׁבְּמִשְׁקָל וְשֶׁבְּמִנְיָן – אֲפִילּוּ פָּחוֹת מִכְּדֵי אוֹנָאָה חוֹזֵר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** you will say that the prohibition is **due to** concern for **exploitation;** and they may increase the measures only by up to one-sixth, so **that there will not be nullification of the transaction,** as the transaction is nullified only when the disparity is more than one-sixth of the value of the item. The Gemara raises an objection: **But doesn't Rava say:** With regard to **any item that** is otherwise subject to the *halakhot* of exploitation, and it is sold **by measure, or by weight, or by number, even** if the disparity was **less than the measure of exploitation** in the transaction, the transaction is **reversed.** A disparity of one-sixth between the value of an item and its price constitutes exploitation only in cases where there is room for error in assessing the value of an item. In a case where the details of the item are easily quantifiable, any deviation from the designated quantity results in a nullification of the transaction.

The Gemara pivots to a second candidate rationale: *ella mishum ona'ah — de-lo le-havei bitul mekach* — the concern is *ona'ah*, specifically that a large change in measures will lead to widespread cancellation of sales. The scenario: a merchant travels between towns selling oil. He is unaware that the destination town has redefined its standard measure to be substantially larger. He sells what he believes is a standard gallon but is actually receiving, from the buyer's perspective, significantly less than a gallon's worth. When the discrepancy is discovered, the sale must be unwound. If this happens on a large scale, commercial life is severely disrupted.

The Gemara then invokes Rava's significant principle — *kol davar she-be-midah u-she-be-mishkal u-she-be-minyan afilu pachot mi-kedi ona'ah chozer* — any transaction involving a mistake in measure, weight, or count may be unwound even if the discrepancy is smaller than the threshold of *ona'ah*. This is a critical distinction: *ona'ah* concerns a mismatch between the agreed price and the market price for a known object. A mistake in *midah* or *mishkal* is categorically different — it is not that the price was wrong, but that the *object* delivered was not the object purchased. You ordered a pound and received ten ounces; the mismatch is in the *res*, not merely in the pricing. Such a transaction is a *bitul mekach* — a nullification of the sale — and even a tiny discrepancy triggers this result.

This principle, established by Rava and endorsed by the Gemara's citation of it, undermines the second candidate rationale: if *any* mistake in measure — however small — leads to *bitul mekach*, then capping the measure change at a sixth accomplishes nothing with respect to this concern. A sixth is still a mistake in measure, and the sale would be voidable regardless. The *bitul mekach* rationale thus cannot explain why a sixth was chosen as the threshold.

### Merchant Loss

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּלָא לֶיהֱוֵי פְּסֵידָא לְתַגָּרָא. פְּסֵידָא הוּא דְּלָא לֶיהֱוֵי לֵיהּ – רַוְוחָא לָא בָּעֵי?! זְבַן וְזַבֵּין – תַּגָּרָא אִיקְּרִי?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the prohibition is **so that there will not be a loss** suffered **by the merchant,** who might not realize that a new standard was issued, and sell in accordance with the old standard. Since a merchant usually enjoys a profit of one-sixth of the value of an item, if the standard is not increased by more than this amount he will not suffer a loss, as at worst he will forfeit his profit margin. This Gemara notes: This explanation is also difficult, since even if the aim is to ensure **that there will not be a loss** for the merchant, **does he not need** to earn **a profit?** There is a well-known adage in this regard: If you **buy and sell** without making any profit, **will** you **be called a merchant?** A merchant must profit from his sales; therefore, if this decree was instituted for the protection of merchants, the Sages should have ensured that they earn a profit.

The Gemara therefore settles on a third rationale: *ella de-lo le-hevei psida le-tagra* — so that the merchant not suffer a loss. This explanation operates in conjunction with Shmuel's other ruling — stated later in the same passage — that a merchant may not profit by more than a sixth. Suppose a merchant buys produce in one town and travels to another to sell it. Following Shmuel's dictate, he adds one-sixth to his cost and sets his price accordingly. Now he arrives in a town that has, unbeknownst to him, enlarged its standard measure by more than a sixth. When he fills the buyer's measure, he is dispensing more produce than his price accounts for. If the increase in the local measure is exactly a sixth, the merchant breaks even — his one-sixth markup is precisely absorbed by the one-sixth excess he dispenses. If the increase were greater than a sixth, he would actually lose money on the transaction.

The Gemara presses the point: is it sufficient that the merchant merely not suffer a loss? *Revacha lo ba'ei* — does he not also deserve to make a profit? He invested capital, bore risk, and transported goods. *Zavin ve-zavin tagra ikri* — someone who buys and sells at cost is not a merchant in any meaningful sense. The Gemara's answer to this objection is that the principle is grounded in a scriptural source, not purely in economic logic, and the source determines its limits. The goal of protecting merchants from outright loss is what the rule achieves; the guarantee of profit is not what the rule promises.

### Yechezkel Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שְׁמוּאֵל קְרָא אַשְׁכַּח וּדְרַשׁ – ״וְהַשֶּׁקֶל – עֶשְׂרִים גֵּרָה; עֶשְׂרִים שְׁקָלִים, חֲמִשָּׁה וְעֶשְׂרִים שְׁקָלִים, עֲשָׂרָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁקֶל – הַמָּנֶה יִהְיֶה לָכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ḥisda said:** The prohibition is not based on logical reasoning. Instead, **Shmuel found a verse and interpreted it homiletically: "And the shekel shall be twenty *gera*; twenty shekels, five and twenty shekels, ten, and five shekels, shall be your *maneh*"** (Ezekiel 45:12). According to this verse, the combination of all of these numbers, sixty shekels, is equivalent to a *maneh*.

Rav Chisda states that Shmuel *kra ashkach ve-darash* — found and expounded a biblical verse as the source for this rule. The verse is from Yechezkel: *ve-ha-shekel esrim gerah; esrim shekalim, chamesh ve-esrim shekalim, asarah va-chamishah shekel — ha-maneh yihiyeh lachem*. That is, twenty *gerah* to the *shekel*, and the *maneh* consists of twenty, twenty-five, and fifteen *shekalim*, totaling sixty *shekalim* to the *maneh*.

### Maneh 50 to 60

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָנֶה?! מָאתָן וְאַרְבְּעִים הָווּ! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מָנֶה שֶׁל קֹדֶשׁ – כָּפוּל הָיָה; וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מוֹסִיפִין עַל הַמִּדּוֹת – וְאֵין מוֹסִיפִין יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת; וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שְׁתוּתָא – מִלְּבַר.
>
> **Translation:** This is problematic: How can **a *maneh*** consist of sixty shekels? Since each biblical shekel is equivalent to four dinars, if a *maneh* is equal to sixty shekels, a *maneh* **is two hundred and forty** dinars. But a *maneh* is actually equal to twenty-five shekels, which is one hundred dinars. **Rather,** one can **learn from** the verse **three** matters: **Learn from it** that the **sacred *maneh* was doubled,** so that it equaled fifty dinars, not twenty-five. **And** furthermore, as Ezekiel stated that the *maneh* will be sixty dinars, not fifty, **learn from it** that a community may **increase measures, but they may not increase** them by **more than one-sixth. And learn from it** that **the one-sixth** is calculated **from the outside,** i.e., it is one-sixth of the final sum, which is one-fifth of the previous sum.

The difficulty is immediate: a *maneh* is standardly 50 *shekalim*, not 60. How can Yechezkel say it is 60?

The Gemara resolves this by drawing three simultaneous inferences — *shema mina tlat*:

First, *shema mina maneh shel kodesh kaful hayah* — the *maneh* of the *Mikdash* was double the standard *maneh*. A standard *maneh* is 25 *shekalim* (which, at four *dinar* per *shekel*, yields 100 *dinar* — the familiar *maneh* of 100 *zuz*). In the context of the *Mikdash*, the *maneh* was double: 50 *shekalim*.

Second, *shema mina mosifin al ha-middot* — it is permissible to increase the value of measures. We see that what was once 50 is now being described as 60. The old *maneh* has been redefined to contain more.

Third, *shema mina shtuta milbar* — the sixth is measured from the outside, i.e., from the larger number, after the addition has been made. From 50 to 60 is an increase of 10. Measured against the new, larger figure of 60, that increase is one-sixth. Measured against the old figure of 50, it would be one-fifth. The rule requires that the sixth be computed from the new, larger total — *milbar*, from the outside. Therefore: the maximum increase permissible is one-sixth of the final, enlarged measure, which corresponds to one-fifth of the original.

### Tosafot Gerah

Tosafot raises an alternative reading of how the *maneh*'s redefinition actually worked. The opening of the verse in Yechezkel states *ve-ha-shekel esrim gerah* — the *shekel* is twenty *gerah*. But Tosafot observes from other sources that a *shekel* is actually twenty-four *gerah*, not twenty. Tosafot therefore proposes that what changed was not the number of *shekalim* in a *maneh*, but rather the number of *gerah* in a *shekel*. If a *shekel* was originally twenty *gerah* and was enlarged to twenty-four *gerah*, that is an increase of four out of twenty-four — exactly one-sixth, measured from the larger final figure. And a *maneh* containing fifty of these enlarged *shekalim* would contain the same quantity of metal as sixty of the old *shekalim*. The mathematical identity is preserved; only the mechanism differs.

### Birchat Kohanim

Tosafot cites the view of Rabbeinu Meshullam of Antioch, who asks: if Yechezkel intended simply to state that a *maneh* equals sixty *shekalim*, why did he not say sixty directly? Why specify twenty, twenty-five, and fifteen as separate components?

Rabbeinu Meshullam answers that these three numbers — fifteen, twenty, and twenty-five — correspond to the three verses of *Birchat Kohanim*. *Yevarechecha Hashem ve-yishmerecha* contains fifteen letters; *ya'er Hashem panav eilecha vi-chuneka* contains twenty letters; *yissa Hashem panav eilecha ve-yasem lecha shalom* contains twenty-five letters. Yechezkel expressed the *maneh* in terms of these three numbers because Israel is blessed through the giving of *shekalim* to the *Mikdash*, and so the measure of the *maneh* was encoded in the very letters of the priestly blessing.

## Applications

### Rav Pappa Cheila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל תַּקֵּין כְּיָילָא בַּר תְּלָתָא קְפִיזֵי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ, וְהָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל הַמִּדּוֹת יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתוּת! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא כְּיָילָא חַדְתָּא תַּקֵּינִי. שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא – וְלָא קַבְּלוּהּ, שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְפַאפּוּנְיָא – וְקַבְּלוּהּ; וְקָרוּ לֵיהּ: ״רוּז פָּפָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa bar Shmuel instituted** a new **measure of three *kefiza*,** which is equal to three *log*. The Sages **said to him: But doesn't Shmuel say** that **one may not increase the measures** by **more than one-sixth?** You have added one-third, as there already exists a measure of a half-kav, which is the equivalent of two *log*. Rav Pappa bar Shmuel **said to them: I instituted a new measure.** He **sent** the measure **to Pumbedita, and they did not accept it; he sent it to** the city of **Paphunya, and they accepted it and called it the measure** of **Pappa.**

Rav Pappa bar Shmuel created a new container — a *cheila* — of a certain size (the Rashbam understands it as nine *log*, built from three *kafizei*). He was immediately challenged: *ve-ha-amar Shmuel ein mosifin al ha-middot yoter mi-shtut* — has not Shmuel ruled that one may not increase measures by more than a sixth? If this *cheila* was being positioned as a replacement for an established six-*log* measure, moving from six to nine is an increase of fifty percent — far exceeding the permitted sixth.

Rav Pappa replied: *ana cheila chadta takkini* — I did not enlarge an old measure; I created an entirely new one. Shmuel's prohibition applies to the renaming of existing measures: if a unit that previously contained X is now being declared to contain more than one-sixth above X under the same name, that is forbidden. But introducing a brand-new measure with a new name falls outside the prohibition entirely. The concern that motivated Shmuel's rule — that merchants traveling between towns might be caught unaware by a changed measure — simply does not arise when the new measure is genuinely novel and is not displacing an existing standard.

### Pumbedita & Papunia

Rav Pappa's new measure was then disseminated to two communities with opposite results. It was sent to Pumbedita — *v'lo kibluhu* — and they refused to adopt it. It was sent to Papunia — *ve-kibluhu* — and they accepted it, calling it by the name *Roz Papa*, apparently a vernacular designation for Rav Pappa's measure. The episode illustrates that even when the halakhic legitimacy of a new measure is established, its practical adoption depends on community reception — and different communities may respond differently to the same innovation.

## Hoarding Fruit

### Otzrei Peirot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוֹצְרֵי פֵירוֹת, וּמַלְוֵי בְּרִבִּית, וּמַקְטִינֵי אֵיפָה, וּמַפְקִיעֵי שְׁעָרִים – עֲלֵיהֶן הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״לֵאמֹר מָתַי יַעֲבֹר הַחֹדֶשׁ וְנַשְׁבִּירָה שֶּׁבֶר, וְהַשַּׁבָּת וְנִפְתְּחָה בָּר, לְהַקְטִין אֵיפָה וּלְהַגְדִּיל שֶׁקֶל, וּלְעַוֵּת מֹאזְנֵי מִרְמָה״. וּכְתִיב: ״נִשְׁבַּע ה׳ בִּגְאוֹן יַעֲקֹב, אִם אֶשְׁכַּח לָנֶצַח כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Hoarders of produce,** who drive up prices by causing a shortage of available goods, **and usurers, and those** sellers **who** falsely **reduce** their **measures, and those who raise** market **prices** by selling for more than the accepted price, **about them the verse states:** "You that would swallow the needy and destroy the poor of the land, **saying: When will the new moon be gone, that we may sell produce? And the Shabbat, that we may set forth grain? Making the measure small, and the shekel great, and falsifying the balances of deceit"** (Amos 8:4–5). **And it is written: "The Lord has sworn by the pride of Jacob: Surely I will never forget any of their works"** (Amos 8:7).

A *baraita* catalogues several categories of economic wrongdoers: *otzrei peirot* — those who hoard produce to drive up prices; *malvei be-ribbit* — those who lend at interest; *maktinei eifah* — those who use undersized measures; and *mafkiei she'arim* — those who manipulate market prices. These are not presented as independent transgressions but as a coherent portrait of those who exploit markets and the vulnerable people who depend on them.

### Amos Example

The *baraita* draws its conceptual vocabulary directly from the prophet Amos, citing the verse *leimor matai ya'avor ha-chodesh ve-nashbirah shever, ve-ha-Shabbat ve-niftachah bar* — "saying: when will the month be over so that we may sell grain, and the Shabbat so that we may open the grain store?" These are people who hold their produce off the market while awaiting optimal prices — waiting for the end of the month or the end of *shemita* — and then flood the market at elevated prices. The verse continues with references to *lehaktin eifah* — making the measure small — and *lehagdil shekel* — demanding that payment be measured in heavier weights — as well as *le'avvet moznei mirmah* — perverting the scales of deceit. The *baraita* quotes the divine oath: *nishba Hashem bi-gaon Yaakov im eshkach la-netzach kol ma'aseihem* — God swears by the pride of Yaakov that He will never forget their deeds.

When the Gemara asks for a concrete example of a *otzar peirot*, Rav Yochanan answers: someone like Shabtai the fruit hoarder — a figure apparently known by reputation for cornering the market.

### Abua Dishmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל מְזַבֵּין לְהוּ לְפֵירֵי בְּתַרְעָא חָרְפָא, כְּתַרְעָא חָרְפָא. שְׁמוּאֵל בְּרֵיהּ, מַשְׁהֵי לְפֵירֵי, וּמְזַבֵּין לְהוּ בְּתַרְעָא אַפְלָא – כְּתַרְעָא חָרְפָא. שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: טָבָא דְּאַבָּא מִדִּבְרָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? תַּרְעָא דְּרָוַוח – רָוַוח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel's father would sell produce during** the period of the **early** market **price,** when produce is cheap, **for the early** market **price. His son Shmuel** acted differently, and **would keep the produce and sell it during** the period of the **late** market **price,** when produce is expensive, **for the early** market **price. They sent** a message **from there,** Eretz Yisrael: The practice **of the father is better than** that **of the son. What is the reason** for this? A market **price that** has been **eased** and starts out low will remain **eased,** with little increase over the course of the year. Therefore, one who makes produce available at the beginning of the season, like Shmuel's father, aids people during the entire year. By contrast, a market price that starts out high, because people are not making their produce available at the market, is not easily lowered.

Shmuel's father employed a specific strategy to counteract market manipulation: *mazbin lehu la-peirei be-tara charfa ke-tara charfa* — he would sell his produce at the early-season price, even as the season progressed. When produce was first harvested, prices were at their lowest. Rather than allowing prices to drift upward as supply was absorbed and demand continued, he maintained the early-season low price throughout, preventing the gradual escalation that would otherwise occur.

Shmuel's own son employed a different approach: *mashhei la-peirei* — he would withhold some of his produce from early-season sale. Rather than selling everything at the outset when prices were already low, he held reserves. Later in the season, when everyone else's prices had risen, he would bring his stored fruit to market — *u-mazbin lehu be-tara afla ke-tara charfa* — selling at what was now the going high-season rate, but at the old early-season low price. The effect was to drive prices back down at precisely the moment when the market had elevated them, using supply to counteract price inflation.

### Father Better

Both strategies were aimed at keeping prices affordable. The question of which was superior was resolved by a ruling sent from Eretz Yisrael: *tava de-abba mi-dereih* — the father's approach is better than the son's. The reason: *tara de-ravach ravach* — once prices have been eased from the start, they remain low. If the early-season price is kept at a low baseline, the market tends to hold there. By contrast, if prices are allowed to rise and then one attempts to bring them back down by releasing stored supply at lower prices, one is fighting an established higher price point. Once a higher price becomes the going rate in people's minds and market practice, it is far harder to dislodge. It is better never to allow prices to escalate than to intervene after they have already risen.
