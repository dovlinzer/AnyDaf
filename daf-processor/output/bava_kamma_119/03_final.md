# Bava Kamma 119 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Purchasing from Shepherds

### Home vs. Wilderness Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִן הָרוֹעִים – צֶמֶר וְחָלָב וּגְדָיִים, וְלֹא מִשּׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת – עֵצִים וּפֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not purchase wool, milk, and kids from the shepherds** who tend the flocks of others, due to the concern that they have stolen these items from the owners of the flocks. **And** similarly, one may **not** purchase **wood and produce from produce watchmen.**

The final *daf* of *Bava Kamma* opens with a *mishnah* concerning the permissibility of purchasing goods from shepherds, given the ever-present concern that such goods may be stolen from the flock's owner. The question is whether one may buy sheep, milk, or wool from a shepherd, and *Rabbi Yehuda* weighs in with a distinction based on where the animals are kept.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּיָּיתוֹת לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן, מִדְבָּרִיּוֹת אֵין לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: כֹּל שֶׁהָרוֹעֶה מוֹכֵר וּבַעַל הַבַּיִת מַרְגִּישׁ בּוֹ – לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן, אֵין מַרְגִּישׁ בּוֹ – אֵין לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says: One may purchase domesticated** animals **from them,** as it is unlikely that the shepherds would steal them from their owner, who would notice if they did not return home. Conversely, **one may not purchase desert,** i.e., non-domesticated, animals **from them,** as it is more likely that a shepherd would steal these animals. **The principle of the matter** is that with regard to **anything that the shepherd sells and the owner** would **perceive its** absence if it were stolen, **one may purchase** it **from them.** But if the owner **would not perceive its** absence, one **may not purchase** it **from them.**

*Rabbi Yehuda* rules that if the animals are *beisos* — home animals, kept close to town — one may purchase from the shepherd, because the owner will readily notice if animals go missing. By contrast, if the animals are *midbarios* — raised out in the wilderness — one may not purchase from the shepherd, since the owner will have no way of knowing the animals are gone.

### Either Criterion Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ צֹאן, אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ גִּיזִּין
>
> **Translation:** And one may always purchase four or five sheep** from them.**

This ruling prompts a question in the *Gemara*: is *Rabbi Yehuda* being strict on the first clause — meaning that to permit a purchase, both conditions must be met (a large number *and* home animals) — or is he being lenient on the final clause, meaning that either condition alone suffices to permit the purchase?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּאָמַר ״בְּכׇל מָקוֹם״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַסֵּיפָא קָאֵי, וּלְקוּלָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **From** the fact **that he stated** that four or five animals may **always** be purchased, **conclude from it** that Rabbi Yehuda's statement **is referring to the latter clause, and** his statement is a **leniency.**

The resolution comes from a *braita* that makes the matter explicit: *Rabbi Yehuda* says one may purchase home animals from a shepherd, one may not purchase wilderness animals, and in any location one may purchase four or five animals. The *braita*'s use of the phrase "in any location" (*be-khol makom*) demonstrates that he is commenting on the lenient end of the ruling — and the conclusion is that either criterion alone suffices. Either a large quantity or the status of home animals is enough to permit the purchase.

### Fruit Watchers Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא מִשּׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת כּוּ׳. רָבָא זָבֵין שְׁבִישָׁתָא מֵאֲרִיסָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְהָא תְּנַן: וְלֹא מִשּׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת – עֵצִים וּפֵירוֹת! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּשׁוֹמֵר, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ בְּגוּפָא דְאַרְעָא מִידֵּי; אֲבָל אָרִיס, דְּאִית לֵיהּ בְּגַוֵּויהּ – אֵימָא מִדְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ קָא מְזַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** similarly, one may **not** purchase wood and produce **from produce watchmen.** The Gemara relates that **Rava purchased grapevine branches from a sharecropper. Abaye said to him: But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And** similarly, one may **not** purchase **wood and produce from produce watchmen?** The same *halakha* should apply with regard to a sharecropper, who, like a watchman, is not the owner of the produce. Rav **said to him: This statement applies** only **with regard to a watchman, as he has no** share **at all in the land itself. But** with regard to **a sharecropper, who does have** a share **in it, say: He is selling** merchandise **from his own** share of the land. Since it is plausible that the sharecropper is selling his own property, it is permitted to buy it from him.

The *Gemara* then addresses a related prohibition: one may not purchase wood or fruit from those who watch fruit, since one must be concerned that they took these items from the very garden they were hired to guard.

Yet a question arises from a case involving Rav himself, who purchased a bundle of wood from an *arisa* — a sharecropper. Abaye challenged him, citing the rule that one cannot purchase wood or fruit from those who watch fruit. Rav's reply draws a sharp distinction: that rule applies only to a hired watchman (*shomer*) who receives a flat fee and has no share in the produce itself. An *arisa*, by contrast, receives a portion of the actual produce as his compensation. Therefore, when an *arisa* sells produce, one may presume he is selling his own share rather than the owner's property.

### Public Sales Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת – לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן כְּשֶׁהֵן יוֹשְׁבִין וּמוֹכְרִין, וְהַסַּלִּין לִפְנֵיהֶם, וְטוּרְטָנֵי לִפְנֵיהֶם. וְכוּלָּן שֶׁאָמְרוּ: ״הַטְמֵן״ – אָסוּר. לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן מִפֶּתַח הַגִּינָּה, אֲבָל לֹא מֵאֲחוֹרֵי הַגִּינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a *baraita* which discusses purchasing items from watchmen: **The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 11:8): With regard to **produce watchmen, one may purchase** produce **from them when they are sitting and selling** the produce, **and the baskets are before them and the scales [*veturtanei*] are before them,** as in these circumstances it is reasonable to assume that they are not selling stolen merchandise. **But** in **all** cases **where they said** to the buyer: **Conceal** your purchase, it is **prohibited** to purchase from them, as there is good reason to suspect that the merchandise is stolen. The *baraita* adds: **One may purchase from** a watchman **from the entrance of the garden, but not from the back of the garden,** because if the produce is being sold inconspicuously, there is a concern that it might have been stolen.

A *braita* adds a further avenue of permissibility: one may purchase from fruit watchers when they are openly and obviously conducting a sale — seated at a stand, with baskets of produce arranged before them and large scales displayed. The assumption is that no one would be so brazen as to conduct an outright public operation without the owner's awareness; word would inevitably reach the owner. However, in any of these circumstances, if the seller says "keep this to yourself," the sale is forbidden. Similarly, one may purchase from those who are positioned at the front entrance of a garden, but not from those operating out of the back entrance.

## Known Thieves Majority Rule

### Rav: Majority of Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: גַּזְלָן, מֵאֵימַת מוּתָּר לִקְנוֹת הֵימֶנּוּ? רַב אָמַר: עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא רוֹב מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מִיעוּט שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § Having discussed the *halakha* pertaining to suspected theft, the Gemara proceeds to examine the *halakha* pertaining to purchasing items from a known robber. **It was stated:** With regard to **a robber, from when is it permitted to purchase** items **from him? Rav says:** It is prohibited **until the majority** of his possessions are **from his** own property, i.e., property that he obtained legally. **And Shmuel says:** It is permitted to purchase items from a known robber **even** if only **a minority** of his possessions are from **his** own property.

The *Gemara* now turns to a related but distinct scenario: a person who is known to be a *gazlan* — an established thief. Unlike the shepherd, where the question is whether to presume the worst about an otherwise ordinary person, here one knows with certainty that the individual engages in theft. The question is: from what point may one purchase items from such a person?

Rav rules that one may purchase from a *gazlan* as long as the majority of his property (*rov mamono*) is legitimately his own — not stolen. *Rashi* explains this as referring to the majority of his possessions overall. The practical logic, as developed in the *shiur*, is that one must find it more probable than not that the item being purchased is actually the seller's own property.

### Shmuel: Minority Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹרִי לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְאַדָּא דַּיָּילָא כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר אֲפִילּוּ מִיעוּט שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **Rav Yehuda instructed Adda,** his **attendant, in accordance with the statement of the one who says:** It is permitted to purchase items from a known robber **even** if only **a minority** of his possessions are from **his** own property, i.e., in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel.

Shmuel takes a strikingly more permissive position: even if only a *miyut* — a minority — of the *gazlan*'s property is legitimately his, one may still purchase from him. This position is transmitted as a teaching that Rav Yehuda conveyed to his attendant Ada: the law follows the lenient ruling that even a minority of legitimate property suffices.

### Why Majority Rule Works

This raises a genuinely troubling question. In general, one is not permitted to take possession of stolen goods. If one knows that the majority of a person's possessions are stolen, why should it be permissible to purchase anything from him — and why does the *chazaka* that "what is in a person's possession belongs to him" (*chazaka ma she-tachat yad adam harei hu shelo*) override what is effectively a known-majority reality of theft?

One might compare this to the question of *lifnei iver* — the prohibition against enabling another's transgression. In those discussions, the *halacha* tends toward leniency when there remains even a minority possibility that the object will be used for a permitted purpose. But that analysis concerns the future use of an item; here, the question is about the present ownership of an object — a different *cheshbon* entirely. The tension is not resolved cleanly: it is simply striking that the *chazaka* of present possession apparently overrides the majority inference that the goods are stolen, and even more striking that Shmuel permits purchase even when the majority of the *gazlan*'s property is stolen.

This difficulty is compounded by the concern — notably absent from this entire passage — about providing a market for stolen goods. The *Gemara* elsewhere recognizes that thieves are sustained by those who purchase from them ("it is not the rat that steals, it is the hole"), yet here that concern is set aside. The answer implicit in the *Gemara* seems to be that once an object has undergone a *shinui* — a significant alteration — it technically no longer constitutes the stolen object itself, and the market-enabling concern recedes. But the question remains pointed, and the *Gemara* does not address it directly.

## Severity of Theft

### Rav Chisda Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָמוֹן מָסוֹר – רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה; חַד אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְאַבְּדוֹ בַּיָּד, וְחַד אָמַר: אָסוּר לְאַבְּדוֹ בַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the **property** of **an informer,** i.e., one who informs gentiles of the whereabouts of another's property, facilitating its theft or misuse, there is a dispute between **Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda. One says:** It is **permitted to physically destroy it, and one says:** It is **prohibited to physically destroy it.**

A passage about the *mamon* of an informer (*masur*) provides a brief digression. One who informs against another Jew — even merely handing over that person's property to the government — is considered a *rodef*, since governmental involvement can ultimately endanger the informer's victim. The dispute between Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda concerns whether one may actively destroy the informer's property as a form of societal enforcement. One view permits it — if the person can be stopped by greater means, his property certainly can be harmed. The opposing view holds that one may not destroy his property, for perhaps he will have righteous children who should not be punished for their father's sins, as the verse states: *yachin rasha ve-tzaddik yilbash* — the wicked man prepares and the righteous one wears it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהוּא אֲרִיסָא, דַּהֲוָה תָּקֵיל וְיָהֵיב תָּקֵיל וְשָׁקֵיל. סַלְּקֵיהּ, קְרָא אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ: ״וְצָפוּן לַצַּדִּיק חֵיל חוֹטֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Ḥisda had a certain sharecropper who would weigh** the field's produce **and give** Rav Ḥisda his portion, and **weigh** the produce **and take** his own portion. Rav Ḥisda **dismissed him,** and **read** the following verse **about him:** "A good man leaves an inheritance to his children's children; **and the wealth of the sinner is laid up for the righteous"** (Proverbs 13:22).

Following this digression, the *Gemara* presents the story of Rav Chisda and his sharecropper. Rav Chisda had an *arisa* who, according to the *Maharsha*'s explanation, was using two different sets of weights — one to measure what he gave and another to measure what he took — effectively cheating Rav Chisda through false measures, an instance of *even va-even*. Rav Chisda dismissed him. Upon doing so, Rav Chisda applied to himself the verse: "treasured away for the righteous person is the wealth of the sinner" — implying that the portion the sharecropper forfeited reverted to Rav Chisda, who deserved it.

### Theft as Taking Life

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי מַה תִּקְוַת חָנֵף כִּי יִבְצָע, כִּי יֵשֶׁל אֱלוֹהַּ נַפְשׁוֹ״ – רַב הוּנָא וְרַב חִסְדָּא; חַד אָמַר: נַפְשׁוֹ דְּנִגְזָל, וְחַד אָמַר: נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל גַּזְלָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara examines various verses pertaining to robbers. **"For what is the hope of the godless, though he profits, when God takes away his soul?"** (Job 27:8). This verse is the subject of a dispute between **Rav Huna and Rav Ḥisda. One says** that the phrase "God takes away his soul" is referring to **the soul of the robbed, and one says** that it is referring to the **soul of the robber.**

It is at this point — nearly at the very end of *Bava Kamma*, after 118 *dafim* devoted almost entirely to the financial consequences of theft and damage — that the *Gemara* finally turns to address the moral and spiritual enormity of the act itself. The *Gemara* cites the verse: *ki mah tikvat chanef ki yivatza ki yeshal Eloka nafsho* — "What is the hope of the flatterer who takes ill-gotten gain? God will remove his *nefesh*."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל נִגְזָל – דִּכְתִיב: ״כֵּן אׇרְחוֹת כׇּל בֹּצֵעַ בָּצַע, אֶת נֶפֶשׁ בְּעָלָיו יִקָּח״. מַאן דְּאָמַר נַפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל גַּזְלָן – דִּכְתִיב: ״אַל תִּגְזׇל דַּל כִּי דַּל הוּא, וְאַל תְּדַכֵּא עָנִי בַשָּׁעַר. כִּי ה׳ יָרִיב רִיבָם, וְקָבַע אֶת קֹבְעֵיהֶם נָפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: The rationale of **the one who says** that the verse is referring to **the soul of the robbed** is **as it is written: "So are the ways of every one that is greedy for profit; it takes away the life of the owner thereof"** (Proverbs 1:19), which teaches that one who robs another of his property is considered as one who robbed him of his soul. And the rationale of **the one who says** that it is referring to **the soul of the robber** is **as it is written: "Rob not the weak, because he is weak, neither crush the poor in the gate; for the Lord will plead their cause, and despoil of life those that despoil them"** (Proverbs 22:22–23).

Rav Huna and Rav Chisda dispute whose *nefesh* is meant. One holds it refers to the *nefesh* of the victim — the person who was stolen from — meaning that the thief, though he imagines he is only taking property, is in truth taking something of the victim's very life. The other holds it refers to the *nefesh* of the *gazlan* himself — God will punish the thief with death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״נֶפֶשׁ בְּעָלָיו יִקָּח״! מַאי ״בְּעָלָיו״ – בְּעָלָיו דְּהַשְׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions each opinion: **And** according to **the other** opinion, i.e., the latter one, **isn't it also written: "It takes away the life of the owner thereof,"** which indicates that the soul of the robbed is taken? The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"the owner thereof"?** It is referring to **its current owner,** i.e., the robber, who has acquired the stolen item.

Each opinion marshals a supporting verse. For the view that it is the victim's soul: *ken orchot kol botzea batza et nefesh ba'alav yikach* — "so are the ways of all who take ill-gotten gains; they take the soul of their owner." For the view that it is the thief's soul: *al tignol dal ki dal hu... ki Hashem yariv rivam ve-kava et koveihem nafesh* — "Do not steal from the poor... for God will fight their cause and take the soul of those who despoil them."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״וְקָבַע אֶת קֹבְעֵיהֶם נָפֶשׁ״! ״מָה טַעַם״ קָאָמַר – מָה טַעַם ״וְקָבַע אֶת קֹבְעֵיהֶם״? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָבְעִי נֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to **the other** opinion, who said that the life of the robbed is taken, **isn't it also written: "And despoil of life those that despoil them,"** which indicates that the soul of the robber is taken? The Gemara answers that the verse **is saying: What is the reason.** The verse teaches: **What is the reason** that God **will despoil** the life of **those who despoil them?** It is **because they despoiled the soul** of their victims.

The Gemara then asks: how does each opinion handle the verse cited by the other? The opinion focused on the victim's *nefesh* reads *ba'alav* as referring to the current owner — namely the *gazlan* who now holds the stolen property — so God is taking the soul of the thief, not the original owner. The opinion focused on the *gazlan*'s punishment reads the verse about taking the victim's soul not as a separate claim, but as the *reason* God punishes the thief: God executes the *gazlan* precisely because the *gazlan* effectively took the victim's soul. Both truths are thus present simultaneously — the theft destroys the victim and brings death upon the thief.

### Even a Penny Equals Life

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – כְּאִילּוּ נוֹטֵל נִשְׁמָתוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כֵּן אׇרְחוֹת כׇּל בּוֹצֵעַ בָּצַע, אֶת נֶפֶשׁ בְּעָלָיו יִקָּח״; וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְאָכַל קְצִירְךָ וְלַחְמֶךָ, בָּנֶיךָ וּבְנוֹתֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Anyone who robs another** of an item **worth one *peruta*** is considered **as though he takes his soul from him, as it is stated: "So are the ways of every one that is greedy for profit; it takes away the life of the owner thereof"** (Proverbs 1:19). **And it states: "And they shall consume your harvest, and your bread,** they shall consume **your sons and your daughters"** (Jeremiah 5:17). Since they will consume the harvest and bread, it is as though they consume one's children as well because there will be no food to feed them.

*Rabbi Yochanan* states the principle directly and without exegetical mediation: whoever steals from his fellow even the value of a *pruta* — the smallest coin — it is as though he took his very soul. The verse adduced is the same: *ken orchot kol botzea batza et nefesh ba'alav yikach*.

### Theft Destroys Livelihood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״מֵחֲמַס בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה, אֲשֶׁר שָׁפְכוּ דָם נָקִי בְּאַרְצָם״; וְאוֹמֵר: ״אֶל שָׁאוּל וְאֶל בֵּית הַדָּמִים, עַל אֲשֶׁר הֵמִית אֶת הַגִּבְעֹנִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it states:** "Egypt shall be a desolation, and Edom shall be a desolate wilderness, **for the extortion of the children of Judah, because they have shed innocent blood in their land"** (Joel 4:19). The verse here considers extortion like the shedding of innocent blood. **And it states** with regard to a famine: "And the Lord said: **It is for Saul, and for his bloody house, because he put to death the Gibeonites"** (II Samuel 21:1).

The *Gemara* then chains together several verses to establish a cascading argument, anticipating and closing each potential objection. One might say: granted, it is as though one took the victim's soul — but not as though one destroyed the lives of the victim's children. The verse responds: *ve-achal katzircha ve-lachmecha yochlu banecha u-vnotecha* — "they will eat your harvest and your bread; it will be as though they consumed the flesh of your sons and daughters."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא יָהֵיב דְּמֵי, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּיָהֵיב דְּמֵי לָא – תָּא שְׁמַע: ״מֵחֲמַס בְּנֵי יְהוּדָה, אֲשֶׁר שָׁפְכוּ דָם נָקִי (בְּאַרְצְכֶם) [בְּאַרְצָם]״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: This matter applies** only **where** the robber **does not give** his victim **compensation** for the stolen item, **but where he gave compensation** for the stolen item, it is **not** comparable to murder, **come** and **hear** the verse: **"For the extortion of the children of Judah, because they have shed innocent blood in their land."** Extortion is referring to coercing someone to sell an item that he does not want to sell. It is a form of robbery, and the verse equates it with murder.

One might then say: that is only when the theft involves no reimbursement whatsoever. But if the thief takes the property and at least pays for it, has he not left the victim with money to feed his family? The verse responds with the case of *chamas* — coercive taking, understood in rabbinic tradition to mean forcing an owner to sell against his will, even at fair market value. Of this the verse says *asher shafchu dam naki be-artzam* — "they have spilled innocent blood in their land." Even compelled sale, even with payment, is likened to bloodshed. The act of stripping a person of property they did not wish to part with is itself a violation tantamount to violence.

### Shaul and Givonim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּקָעָבֵיד בְּיָדַיִם, אֲבָל גְּרָמָא לָא – תָּא שְׁמַע: ״אֶל שָׁאוּל וְאֶל בֵּית הַדָּמִים, [עַל] אֲשֶׁר הֵמִית אֶת הַגִּבְעֹנִים״; וְכִי הֵיכָן מָצִינוּ שֶׁהָרַג שָׁאוּל אֶת הַגִּבְעוֹנִים? אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁהָרַג נוֹב עִיר הַכֹּהֲנִים שֶׁהָיוּ מַסְפִּיקִין לָהֶן מַיִם וּמָזוֹן, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ הֲרָגָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: This matter applies** only **where he committed** the robbery **by** direct **action, but** if he committed it through **indirect** action, the transgression is **not** as severe, **come** and **hear** the verse: **"It is for Saul, and for his bloody house, because he put to death the Gibeonites." And where did we** ever **find that Saul killed the Gibeonites?** He did not do so. **Rather, due to** the fact that **he killed** the residents of **Nob, the city of the priests, who would provide** the Gibeonites with **water** (see I Samuel, chapter 22) **and food, the verse ascribes him** blame for their death **as though he** had **killed** the Gibeonites himself.

One might further argue: all of this applies only to direct taking. But indirect harm — *grama* — surely cannot rise to the level of murder. The *Gemara* responds with the verse about Shaul and the Givonim: *El Shaul ve-el beit ha-damim asher hemit et ha-Givonim* — "to Shaul and to the house of blood, because he killed the Givonim." Yet where do we find that Shaul directly killed the Givonim? The *Gemara* explains: he destroyed Nov, the city of *Kohanim*, who had been providing the Givonim with water and food and employing them. By destroying the priestly city, Shaul eliminated the Givonim's entire livelihood. Scripture nonetheless holds him accountable as if he had killed them directly. Preventing a person from earning a living is thus equivalent to murder — even when accomplished indirectly.

The moral weight of all of this at the close of the *masechet* is striking. The entire tractate has been concerned with the technical legal consequences of theft and damage — damages, payments, the *takanat ha-shavim* that allows a thief to simply return the object without bearing the full cost of its use, and the various rules that soften the legal burden on the *gazlan*. Only now, on the final *daf*, does the *Gemara* turn to say: and yet the act itself is among the most grievous a person can commit. The balance between these two realities — the severity of the sin and the pragmatic leniency of the law — reflects a difference between what something is morally and what the best societal response to it is in practice.

## Women and Slaves Sales

### Women's Sales Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מִן הַנָּשִׁים. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לוֹקְחִין מִן הַנָּשִׁים כְּלֵי צֶמֶר בִּיהוּדָה וּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן בַּגָּלִיל, אֲבָל לֹא יֵינוֹת וּשְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת. וְלֹא מִן הָעֲבָדִים, וְלֹא מִן הַתִּינוֹקוֹת. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: מוֹכֶרֶת אִשָּׁה בְּאַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה דִּינָרִ[ין], כְּדֵי לַעֲשׂוֹת כִּפָּה לְרֹאשָׁהּ. וְכוּלָּן שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְהַטְמִין – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But one may purchase** specific goods **from women** in certain places. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:5): **One may purchase from women woolen goods in Judea, and linen goods in the Galilee, but not wines, oils, and flours,** as these are not usually sold by women and there is a concern that perhaps the women stole them from their husbands. **And no** items may be purchased **from slaves, or from children. Abba Shaul says: A woman may sell** items **for** up to **four and five dinars in order to make a cap [*kippa*] for her head,** as it is assumed that her husband allows her to sell these items in order to purchase additional articles of clothing. **And** with regard to **all** of **those** cases, **where they told** the buyer **to conceal** his purchase, it is **prohibited** to enter into the transaction.

The *Gemara* now shifts to a set of rules about purchasing from members of a household who are not the primary owner of the property: women, slaves, and children. In the legal world of the *Gemara*, a wife's property belonged to her husband, a slave's property to his master, and a child's property to his father. The question is therefore not about outright theft but about whether such persons have the implicit authority of the household to transfer goods.

The *Tanna* rules that one may purchase wool garments from women in Judea and linen garments in the Galilee, since these are standard local trades that women conduct, and the owner (the husband) would be aware of and implicitly sanction such sales. By contrast, one may not purchase wine, oil, or flour from women, nor may one purchase from slaves or children. The concern is that these goods fall outside the understood scope of what such household members have permission to dispose of.

### Pin Money Exception

*Abba Shaul* adds an important qualification: a woman may sell up to four and five *dinarim* worth of goods — this is the *Gemara*'s explicit language, not a vague number of items. The concept is essentially one of *pin money*: there is a presumed general consent from the husband for a wife to make small, ordinary transactions for her personal needs, such as purchasing a small head covering. Whether the threshold applies per transaction or per period is left unresolved in the *shiur*, but in either case, it represents a recognized zone of autonomous economic activity. Any transaction within that threshold is presumed to fall within permitted bounds. As before, all of these permissions are voided if the seller asks the buyer to keep the transaction quiet.

### Charity Collections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גַּבָּאֵי צְדָקָה לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן דָּבָר מוּעָט, אֲבָל לֹא דָּבָר מְרוּבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Charity collectors may take something** worth **a small** amount **from** women, **but not something** worth **a large** amount, as it is possible that they do not have permission to give away expensive items.

The *Gemara* then addresses *gabbai tzedakah* — those who collect charitable donations. The same logic that permits small sales extends to small donations: a *gabbai tzedakah* going door to door may accept a modest donation from a woman, a slave, or a child, without requiring the husband, master, or father to appear and explicitly consent. Just as there is a presumption that the head of household implicitly permits small transactions, there is a parallel presumption of implicit consent for small charitable contributions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַבַּדָּדִין – לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן זֵתִים בְּמִידָּה, וְשֶׁמֶן בְּמִידָּה. אֲבָל לֹא זֵיתִים בְּמוּעָט וְשֶׁמֶן בְּמוּעָט. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לוֹקְחִין מִנָּשִׁים זֵיתִים בְּמוּעָט בַּגָּלִיל הָעֶלְיוֹן, שֶׁפְּעָמִים אָדָם בּוֹשׁ לִמְכּוֹר עַל פֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ, וְנוֹתֵן לְאִשְׁתּוֹ וּמוֹכֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **olive pressers,** one **may purchase from them olives in** a substantial **measure, and oil in** a substantial **measure,** as there is no concern that they would steal such a large amount, **but not olives in a small** amount, **and** not **oil in a small** amount. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One may purchase olives in small** amounts **from women in the Upper Galilee,** where olives are exceptionally expensive, **as at times, a person is embarrassed to sell** olives **at the entrance of his house, and** so **he gives** some olives **to his wife, and she sells** them. Since there is a reason to presume that the women have been given the right to sell the olives by their husbands, and it is unlikely that the women would sell them without permission, as even a small amount missing would be noticed due to their great value, it may be assumed that the olives are being sold with permission.

The *Gemara* adds a related ruling: those who press oil (*badadin*) may purchase large quantities of olives and oil from oil-pressers, since large volumes suggest a legitimate commercial arrangement. Small quantities of olives or oil from oil-pressers, however, are suspect, since the fear is that these small amounts were simply pilfered. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* adds a nuance regarding the Upper Galilee: one may purchase even small amounts of olives from women there, because in that region it is socially common for a man who wishes to sell a small amount — but finds it embarrassing to do so himself, since it would advertise his straitened circumstances — to give the goods to his wife to sell on his behalf. Women's sales of small amounts are thus more ordinary there than elsewhere.

### Large as Small Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי מָחוֹזָא, אֲתוֹ נְשֵׁי דְּבֵי מָחוֹזָא רְמוֹ קַמֵּיהּ כַּבְלֵי וְשֵׁירֵי, קַבֵּיל מִינַּיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה תּוֹסְפָאָה לְרָבִינָא, וְהָתַנְיָא: גַּבָּאֵי צְדָקָה מְקַבְּלִין מֵהֶן דָּבָר מוּעָט, אֲבָל לֹא דָּבָר מְרוּבֶּה! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי, לִבְנֵי מָחוֹזָא דָּבָר מוּעָט נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: When **Ravina arrived at Bei Meḥoza, the women of Bei Meḥoza came and tossed chains and bracelets before him** so that he could distribute the jewelry as charity, and **he accepted** it **from them. Rabba Tosfa'a said to Ravina: But isn't it taught** in the *baraita*: **Charity collectors may accept something** worth **a small** amount **from** women, **but not something** worth **a large** amount? How can you accept jewelry, which is worth a significant sum? Ravina **said to him: For the residents of Meḥoza, these** chains and bracelets **are** considered **something small,** and it is therefore permitted for me to accept them.

Ravina once came to Mechuza, where the wealthy women of the city threw gold necklaces and bracelets before him as charitable donations. He accepted them. When *Rabbah Tosfa'ah* challenged him — citing the rule that *gabbai tzedakah* may accept only small donations, not large ones, from women — Ravina replied that the rule about "small donations" is relative to the community's standard of wealth. In Mechuza, where women are extremely wealthy, such gold ornaments count as a modest donation. The category of "small" is not absolute but contextual.

## Craftsmen's Remnants

### Launderer's Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מוֹכִין שֶׁהַכּוֹבֵס מוֹצִיא – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְהַסּוֹרֵק מוֹצִיא – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. כּוֹבֵס נוֹטֵל שְׁלֹשָׁה חוּטִין – וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ, יָתֵר מִכֵּן – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. אִם הָיָה שָׁחוֹר עַל גַּבֵּי הַלָּבָן – נוֹטֵל אֶת הַכֹּל וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Strands** of wool **that the launderer removes** from the garment **belong to him,** as it can be assumed that the customer is uninterested in them, **but** strands that **the carder,** i.e., one who prepares wool for use as a textile, **removes belong to the customer,** as it is assumed that the customer would want them, since the carder often removes a significant number of strands. **A launderer takes three threads** that were inserted at the edge of a garment, **and they are his,** but with regard to **more** threads **than this, these** additional threads **belong to the customer. If** these **were black** threads **on a white** garment, **he takes all of them and they are his.** As the removal of the threads improves the appearance of the garment, the customer does not want them.

With the final *mishnah* of *Bava Kamma*, the *Gemara* turns to a detailed and remarkably precise set of rules about what craftsmen may retain from the materials entrusted to them by clients. These rules carry significant legal weight: retaining something to which one is not entitled constitutes *gezel*, regardless of how trivial the item appears.

The *mishnah* opens with the launderer (*koves*). If a launderer removes stuffing (*muchin*) from a pillow in the course of his work, those pieces are assumed to belong to him — the owner is presumed not to care about such incidental loss. However, if a comber (*sorek*) removes material in the course of combing flax, that material belongs to the owner and must be returned.

A launderer may take up to three threads from the hem of a garment — these are threads he removes in the normal course of his work, such as threads that keep socks together in laundering — and these are his to keep. More than three belongs to the owner. If there are black threads on a white garment, he may take all of them, since they clearly do not belong on the garment. Regarding tailors: if a tailor leaves over a thread long enough to make a stitch, or a patch of cloth of at least three by three fingerbreadths, these belong to the owner. Regarding carpenters: wood chips that come off from planing (*ma'atzad*) belong to the carpenter, while larger pieces from hewing (*kashil*) belong to the owner. If the craftsman is working on the owner's premises, however, even the sawdust belongs to the owner.

### Launderer Instructions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לוֹקְחִין מוֹכִין מִן הַכּוֹבֵס, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ. הַכּוֹבֵס נוֹטֵל שְׁנֵי חוּטִין הָעֶלְיוֹנִים – וְהֵן שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:13): **One may purchase strands** of wool **from a launderer, because they are** assumed to be **his. A launderer may take the two upper threads** of a garment, **and they are his.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יָטִיל בּוֹ יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה חוּבִּין, וְלֹא יִסְרוֹק הַבֶּגֶד לְשִׁתְיוֹ אֶלָּא לְעֶרְבּוֹ. וּמַשְׁוֵיהוּ לְאׇרְכּוֹ, אֲבָל לֹא לְרׇחְבּוֹ. וְאִם בָּא לְהַשְׁווֹתוֹ עַד טֶפַח – רַשַּׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** a carder **should not place in** a piece of cloth **more than three stitches** for each loop that he attaches to the cloth in order to stretch it out, as this causes the cloth to overstretch and require trimming. **And he should not card the cloth** along **its warp,** i.e., vertically, **but** along **its weft,** i.e., horizontally. **And he may even out** the cloth by cutting it along **its length, but not** along **its width, and if he comes to even out** the cloth by removing **up to a handbreadth** of material, it is **permitted** for him to do so.

The *braita* elaborates on the launderer's entitlements and adds a remarkable set of instructions about how the launderer must conduct his work in order to protect the client's property — instructions that are notable for shifting from the question of what the craftsman may keep to the question of how he must do his job responsibly.

A launderer may take two of the upper threads from a garment. He may not insert more than three of the special stretching stitches (*chuvin*) into the garment, as adding more may damage it. A contradiction between the *mishnah* (which permits three threads) and the *braita* (which permits two) is resolved by noting that thick threads require a limit of two while thin threads allow three.

When combing the garment, the launderer must comb it in the direction of the warp (*sheti*) and not the woof (*arav*), since combing against the grain damages the fabric. A contradicting *braita* is resolved by distinguishing between types of garments: a cloak (*glima*), which is worn for appearance, is combed in the direction that makes it look nicer; a coat (*sarvla*), worn primarily for warmth, is combed in the direction that makes it last longer.

If the launderer must trim a garment to even it out, he must trim lengthwise, not widthwise, since trimming the width compromises the garment's integrity more than trimming the length. He may trim up to a *tefach* if necessary to achieve an even edge.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: שְׁנֵי חוּטִין. וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: שָׁלֹשׁ! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא בְּאַלִּימֵי, וְהָא בְּקַטִּינֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **The Master said** in the *baraita* that the launderer may take **two threads. But didn't we learn** in the mishna that the launderer may take **three** threads? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult:** The ruling of **this** *baraita*, which allows only two threads, is stated **with regard to thick** threads, **and** the ruling of **that** mishna, which allows three threads, is stated **with regard to thin** ones.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִסְרוֹק הַבֶּגֶד לְשִׁתְיוֹ, אֶלָּא לְעֶרְבּוֹ. וְהָתַנְיָא אִיפְּכָא! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא בִּגְלִימָא, הָא בְּסַרְבָּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** It was also stated in the *baraita*: **And he should not card the cloth** along **its warp but** along **its weft.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in another *baraita* that **the opposite** is the *halakha*? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult:** The ruling of **this** *baraita*, which prohibits carding along the warp, is stated **with regard to** an ordinary **garment,** which is designed for durability, and one should therefore avoid wearing out the material by carding along the warp. The ruling of **that** *baraita*, which allows one to card along the warp, is stated **with regard to** an elegant **cape [*besarbela*],** which is made for aesthetic appearance and is therefore improved by carding in this manner.

*Rav Yehuda* adds that the launderer is called *kitsra* in Aramaic, and the trimmed edge pieces (*kitsra*) are his to keep. He further rules that if the three removable threads at the garment's hem have not been removed by the launderer, they count toward the measurement required for *tzitzit* placement — specifically, they are included in calculating the *melo kesher gudal* (a thumb-width's distance from the edge within which *tzitzit* must be placed). If the threads are still attached, the edge of the garment is measured from their outermost point.

### Comber Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִן הַסּוֹרֵק מוֹכִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ. וּבִמְקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִהְיוֹת שֶׁלּוֹ – לוֹקְחִין. וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן כַּר מָלֵא מוֹכִין וְכֶסֶת מְלֵאָה מוֹכִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? קְנָנְהוּ בְּשִׁינּוּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a *baraita* that discusses which items may be purchased from a carder. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:12): **One may not purchase strands** of thread **from a carder, because** it is assumed that **they are not his. And in a place where** the residents **were accustomed** to allow carders to retain strands, the strands may be presumed **to be his** and **one may purchase** them. **And in every place, one may purchase from them a cushion full of** stuffing made from **strands, or a mattress full of** stuffing made from **strands. What is the reason** that it is permitted? The reason is that even if the carder had stolen the strands, once he uses them to make a cushion or mattress, **he has acquired** them **through a change** of form.

In contrast to the launderer, one may not purchase stuffing material from a comber (*sorek*), since any such material belongs to the owner. The exception is a locale where local custom (*minhag*) has established that the comber retains such material — in that case, one may purchase it from him. In all places, however, one may purchase pillows that are already filled with stuffing, because even if the stuffing was originally stolen, the act of filling the pillow constitutes a *shinui* — a significant alteration — by which the craftsman has acquired it.

### Weaver's Materials

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִגַּרְדִּי לֹא אִירִין, וְלֹא נִירִין, וְלֹא פּוּנְקָלִין, וְלֹא שִׁיּוּרֵי פַּקְיעִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the *halakhot* pertaining to weaving, the Gemara cites a *baraita* that discusses which items may be purchased from various craftsmen. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:11): **One may purchase from a weaver neither** woolen **wads [*irin*],** which are used to hold the bobbin in place on a shuttle, **nor heddles [*nirin*], nor threads of the bobbin [*punkalin*], nor remnants of** coils of thread that were left on the **spool,** as there is a concern that these items were taken from the customer without his consent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן בֶּגֶד מְנוּמָּר, עֵרֶב, וּשְׁתִי, טְוִוי, וְאָרִיג.
>
> **Translation:** **But one may purchase a spotted garment from them,** even though the design indicates that it was made from strands of different colors, which the weaver may have stolen from other garments that he was weaving. It is also permitted to purchase **warp** threads **and weft** threads from them, as well as wool that was **spun** into thread **or woven.** All of these items may be purchased from the weaver because they have undergone a physical change, and have therefore been acquired by the weaver even if he did steal them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הַשְׁתָּא טְוִוי שָׁקְלִי, אָרוּג מִבַּעְיָא? מַאי ״אָרִיג״ – תִּיכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Say: Now** that the *baraita* taught that one **may purchase spun** wool despite the fact that it has undergone only a minor physical change, **is** it **necessary** to teach that one may purchase **woven** wool, which has undergone a greater change of form? The Gemara answers: **What** does the *baraita* mean when it mentions **woven** wool? It is not referring to wool woven into a garment, but to wool that was twisted into **chains,** which is also a minor physical change.

From a weaver (*gardi*), one may not purchase thread or implements associated with the loom: neither the *irin* nor the *nirin* — the threads associated with the warp and woof around the shuttle — nor the tapestry bobbins (*punkalin*), nor the remnants of thread spools. All of these, even if seemingly trivial, belong to the owner of the material.

However, one may purchase from a weaver a patchwork garment (*beged menumar*), because even if the patches were improperly obtained, the act of creating the garment constitutes a *shinui* by which the weaver has acquired them. Similarly, one may purchase thread that has been spun (*tavui*) or woven. If someone asks whether one may purchase something that has been woven as well as spun — since spinning alone already creates a *shinui* — the *Gemara* clarifies that "woven" in this context does not mean woven after spinning, but rather twisted (*tichi*), a different form of processing that is also a *shinui*.

### Dyer's Samples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִן הַצַּבָּע לֹא אוֹתוֹת וְלֹא דּוּגְמוֹת וְלֹא תְּלוּשִׁים שֶׁל צֶמֶר, אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן בֶּגֶד צָבוּעַ, טְוִוי, בְּגָדִים. הַשְׁתָּא טְוִוי שָׁקֵיל, בְּגָדִים מִיבַּעְיָא? מַאי ״בְּגָדִים״ – נַמְטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines which items may be bought from a dyer. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may purchase from a dyer neither** pieces of wool used for **tests, nor** pieces used as a color **sample [*dugmut*], nor detached** pieces **of wool,** as these might have been stolen. **But one may purchase from him a colored garment, spun** threads, and **clothes** fashioned from the aforementioned pieces of material. The Gemara asks: **Now** that the *baraita* taught that one **may purchase spun** threads from the dyer, **is** it **necessary** to teach that one may purchase **clothes?** It is obvious that one may purchase clothes, as the clothes themselves are made of spun threads. The Gemara explains: **What** does the *baraita* mean when it mentions **clothes?** It is referring to **felt** garments, which are not made of spun thread.

One may not purchase from a dyer (*tzaya*) either *otot* or *dugmaot* — samples and test pieces. *Rashi* explains: an *ot* is a small piece of cloth given to the dyer so he may test the dye on it before applying it to the full garment; a *dugma* is an example piece showing the color the owner desires as the final result. These tiny items are presumed to be wanted back by the owner. Similarly, one may not purchase plucked wool (*tluishim*) that comes off garments in the dyeing process.

One may, however, purchase an already-dyed garment (*beged tzavua*) from a dyer, since either the owner has abandoned it or — if it was improperly obtained — the act of dyeing constitutes a *shinui* by which the dyer has acquired it. The question of whether dyeing alone constitutes a *shinui* is worth noting: is this a *shinui ha-shem*, a change of name and identity? The *Gemara* proceeds without resolving this directly.

One may also purchase spun wool (*tavui*) and garments from a dyer. A *Gemara* question notes that if spun wool alone suffices to create a *shinui*, surely a completed garment does as well — and the *Gemara* clarifies that "garments" here refers to felt (*namti*), which is pressed rather than spun, and this too constitutes a *shinui*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנּוֹתֵן עוֹרוֹת לְעַבְּדָן; הַקִּיצּוּעִין וְהַתְּלוּשִׁין – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, וְהָעוֹלֶה וּמִשַּׁטֵּף בְּמַיִם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:16): In the case of **one who gives hides to a tanner, the trimmings** of hide **and the detached** hairs **belong to the customer, but** the substance that **comes up** while being **washed in water belongs to him,** the tanner.

The *braita* then addresses a tanner: pieces trimmed from a hide and hair that comes off in the processing belong to the owner. But material that floats to the surface when the hide is submerged in water belongs to the tanner.

### Tailor's Thresholds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחַיָּיט שֶׁשִּׁיֵּיר אֶת הַחוּט כְּדֵי לִתְפּוֹר בּוֹ, וּמַטְלֵית שֶׁהִיא שָׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a tailor who left enough** **thread** attached to the cloth in order to sew with it, or if there was **a patch** of cloth **that is three** fingerbreadths **by three** fingerbreadths left from the cloth given to the tailor by the customer, **these** items **belong to the customer.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַחַיָּיט שֶׁשִּׁיֵּיר אֶת הַחוּט פָּחוֹת מִכְּדֵי לִתְפּוֹר בּוֹ, וּמַטְלֵית שֶׁהִיא פְּחוּתָה מִשָּׁלֹשׁ עַל שָׁלֹשׁ – בִּזְמַן שֶׁבַּעַל הַבַּיִת מַקְפִּיד עֲלֵיהֶן, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת; אֵין בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מַקְפִּיד עֲלֵיהֶן, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a tailor who left the thread** attached to the cloth, but it was **less than** the length necessary **in order to sew with it, or** if there was **a patch** of cloth **that is less than three** fingerbreadths **by three** fingerbreadths left from the cloth given to the tailor by the customer, the *halakha* is dependent upon the customer's inclination: **When the customer is particular about** such items, **these** items **belong to the customer,** but if **the customer is not particular about them, these** items **belong to** the tailor.

Returning to the tailor (*chayat*): if the tailor leaves over a thread sufficient to make a stitch, it belongs to the owner. How much thread is "sufficient to make a stitch"? *Rav Assi* says: the length of the needle, plus the length that extends outside the needle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מְלֹא מַחַט, וְחוּץ לַמַּחַט כִּמְלֹא מַחַט; אוֹ דִלְמָא מְלֹא מַחַט, וְחוּץ לַמַּחַט מַשֶּׁהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a dilemma: Did Rav Asi mean that the thread must be **the size of** the **needle and** that **beyond the needle** there must be an additional amount of thread **equivalent to the size of** the **needle? Or perhaps** he meant that it must be **the size of** the **needle, and** that **beyond the needle** there must be **any** minimal **amount** of additional string. In other words, was Rav Asi saying that the thread must be two needle lengths, or slightly more than one needle length?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא מְלֹא מַחַט, וְחוּץ לַמַּחַט כִּמְלֹא מַחַט – פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן חֲזֵי לְסִיכְּתָא; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מְלֹא מַחַט, וְחוּץ לַמַּחַט מַשֶּׁהוּ – פָּחוֹת מִכָּאן לְמַאי חֲזֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita* to deduce the answer: **Granted, if you say** that Rav Asi meant to say that the thread must be **the size of** the **needle and** that **beyond the needle** there must be an additional amount of thread **equivalent to the size of** the **needle,** then the ruling in the *baraita* that a slightly shorter thread belongs to the customer if he wishes to keep it is reasonable, since a thread which is **less than that** length **is** still **fit for** use as the stitching of **a loop. But if you say** that Rav Asi meant that the thread must be **the size of** the **needle and** that **beyond the needle** there must be **any amount** of additional string, then with regard to a thread that is even **less than that,** for what use **is** it **fit** that the customer might wish to keep it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מְלֹא מַחַט, וְחוּץ לַמַּחַט כִּמְלֹא מַחַט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, learn from** the *baraita* that Rav Asi meant that the thread must be **the size of** the **needle and** that **beyond the needle** there must be an additional amount of thread **equivalent to the size of** the **needle,** so that the thread must be a total of two needle lengths.

A *she'ila* arises from this formulation: does "the length of the needle plus outside the needle" mean one needle's length *plus* another full needle's length (two needle-lengths total), or does it mean the needle's length *plus* a minimal additional amount sufficient to exit the needle's eye? The resolution comes from a *braita*: if the tailor leaves over a thread *less* than the sufficient stitch-length, or a patch *less* than three by three fingerbreadths, and the owner is *makpid* — particular about these items — then they belong to the owner. If the owner is not *makpid*, they belong to the tailor, which is the default presumption in these cases.

The *Gemara* reasons: if "the length of the needle plus outside" means two full needle-lengths, then the statement that anything less belongs to the tailor makes sense, because a thread just under two needle-lengths can still be used for a specific stitch (*sichsa*). But if "outside" means only a tiny bit, then anything less than that would be too small to serve any purpose at all, and it would be meaningless to say it belongs to the tailor. Therefore, the formulation must mean two full needle-lengths.

### Carpenter's Shavings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה שֶּׁהֶחָרָשׁ מוֹצִיא בְּמַעֲצָד – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, וּבְכַשִּׁיל – שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. וְאִם הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה אֵצֶל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת – אַף הַנְּסָרִים שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a tailor who left** enough **thread** attached to the cloth that it could be used **in order to sew with it, or** if there was **a patch** of cloth **that is three** fingerbreadths **by three** fingerbreadths left from the cloth given to the tailor by the customer, **these** items **belong to the customer. That which a carpenter removes with an adze belongs to him,** because an adze removes only small shavings of wood, which the customer is uninterested in; **but** what he removes **with an ax [*uvakashil*] belongs to the customer. And if he was doing** his work **in** the domain of **the customer,** then **even the sawdust belongs to the customer.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה שֶּׁהֶחָרָשׁ כּוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהִי: מַה שֶּׁהֶחָרָשׁ מוֹצִיא בְּמַעֲצָד, וְהַנִּפְסָק בִּמְגֵירָה – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת; וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת מַקְדֵּחַ וּמִתַּחַת רָהִיטְנֵי, וְהַנִּגְרָר בִּמְגֵירָה – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **That which the carpenter** removes with an adze belongs to him, but what he removes with an ax belongs to the customer. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to this ruling based upon a *baraita*: **That which the carpenter removes with an adze and that which is severed with a saw belong to the customer. But** with regard to **that which comes out from under a drill or under a plane [*rehitni*], and that which is scraped by the saw,** i.e., sawdust, **these belong to** the carpenter. Whereas the mishna rules that the carpenter may keep what is removed with an adze, the *baraita* rules that it belongs to the customer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּאַתְרָא דְּתַנָּא דִּידַן אִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי חֲצִינֵי, לְרַבָּתִי קָרֵי לַהּ ״כַּשִּׁיל״, וּלְזוּטַרְתִּי קָרֵי לַהּ ״מַעֲצָד״. בְּאַתְרָא דְּתַנָּא בָּרָא חַד הוּא דְּאִיכָּא, וְקָרוּ לַהּ ״מַעֲצָד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents an answer: **Rava said: In the place of the *tanna* of our** mishna, **there are two** kinds of **blades** used by carpenters: **The larger** blade **is called an ax, and the smaller** one **is called an adze.** By contrast, **in the place of the *tanna* of the *baraita*, there is** only **one** carpenter's **blade, and they called it an adze.** Consequently, the adze referred to in the *baraita* is actually an ax and the rulings are therefore congruent.

As noted in the *mishnah*, wood chips from planing (*ma'atzad*) go to the carpenter, while larger pieces from hewing (*kashil*) go to the owner. A *braita*, however, seems to say that chips from planing go to the owner. Rava resolves the contradiction by noting that in the locale of the *Tanna* of the *mishnah*, there are two types of cutting implements: the large one is called *kashil* and the small one *ma'atzad*. The *mishnah* accordingly assigns the fine shavings of the *ma'atzad* to the carpenter and the larger pieces of the *kashil* to the owner. In the locale of the *braita*'s *Tanna*, there is only one such implement and it happens to be the large version, yet it is called *ma'atzad* — which is why that *braita* assigns its output to the owner.

## Custom and Context

### Produce Removers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְסַתְּתֵי אֲבָנִים – אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל. מְפַסְּגֵי אִילָנוֹת, מְפַסְּגֵי גְפָנִים, מְנַקְּפֵי הִיגֵי, מְנַכְּשִׁי זְרָעִים וְעוֹדְרֵי יְרָקוֹת – בִּזְמַן שֶׁבַּעַל הַבַּיִת מַקְפִּיד עֲלֵיהֶם, יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל; אֵין בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מַקְפִּיד עֲלֵיהֶן, הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And if he was doing** his work **in** the domain of the customer, then even the sawdust belongs to the customer. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:18): **Stone chiselers are not in** violation of a transgression **due to** the prohibition against **robbery** if they take the leftover chips of rock. Furthermore, with regard to **those who prune trees, those who prune vines, those who trim shrubs, those who weed plants, and those who hoe vegetables,** the *halakha* is dependent upon the owner's inclination: **When the owner is particular about** the plant trimmings, the workers **are in** violation of a transgression **due to** the prohibition against **robbery** if they take the trimmings, but if **the owner is not particular about them,** then **these** items **belong to** the workers.

The *Gemara* then addresses a general category of workers who remove material from an owner's property in the course of their labor: stone carvers, those who prune trees and vines, those who cut thornbushes, those who weed gardens, and those who thin out crowded vegetables. In all these cases, the rule is: if the owner is *makpid* — particular about retaining the removed material — then keeping it constitutes *gezel*. If the owner is not *makpid*, the worker may keep it. What is notably absent here is any declared default: the passage does not specify which presumption governs in the absence of explicit protest, suggesting that in these cases the parties must simply clarify.

### Hops and Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כְּשׁוּת וַחֲזִיז – אֵין בָּהֶם מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל. בְּאַתְרָא דְּקָפְדִי – יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: Dodder [*keshut*] and green grain [*veḥaziz*], are not subject to** the prohibition against **robbery,** as they grow on their own and no one tends to them. But **in a place where** people **are particular** about the ownership of dodder and green grain, **they are subject to** the prohibition against **robbery.**

*Rav Yehuda* offers a ruling about specific plants: *kashus* (hops) and *chaziz* (green grain, which regrows after being cut) — the worker may keep them without any concern of *gezel*. However, in a locale where the custom is that owners are *makpid* about such things, then keeping them does constitute *gezel*.

### Mata Mechasya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: וּמָתָא מַחְסֵיָא – אַתְרָא דְּקָפְדִי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: And the city** of **Meḥasya is a city where** the residents **are particular** about dodder and green wheat.

*Ravina* adds a local application: Mata Mechasya — his own city — is a place where owners are habitually *makpid* about such remnants, and therefore what would elsewhere be freely retained by a worker constitutes *gezel* there.

This is the note on which *Bava Kamma* concludes. It is a fittingly precise ending: after exhortations about how even a *pruta*'s worth of theft is like taking a life, the tractate closes by asking whether a worker may keep the weeds he pulls from a garden or the hops he cuts from a vine. The answer depends on where you are and what people expect. Every small thing matters. The *masechet* ends — *hukdran alach ha-gozel batra ve-seika lah Masechet Bava Kamma*.
