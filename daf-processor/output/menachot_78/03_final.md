# Menachot 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Teruma & Michubar

### Michubar Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִקְרִיב מִמֶּנּוּ״ – מִן הַמְחוּבָּר, ״אֶחָד״ – שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל פָּרוּס, ״מִכׇּל קׇרְבָּן״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ כׇּל הַקָּרְבָּנוֹת שָׁוִים, שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן עַל חֲבֵירוֹ, ״תְּרוּמָה לַה׳״ – אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מִכַּמָּה הִיא,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches some of the *halakhot* of *teruma* to be taken from the loaves of the thanks offering that are derived from the verse: "And he shall present from it one of each offering as a *teruma* unto the Lord." The Gemara cites a *baraita* that interprets the same verse: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The phrase **"And he shall present from it"** indicates that the loaves must all be as one, i.e., *teruma* may be taken **from** the loaves only when they are joined **together** in one place. **"One"** indicates **that one should not take a sliced** loaf. **"Of each offering"** indicates **that all the offerings should be equal,** i.e., **that one should not take** a loaf **from one** type of **offering for another** type. When the verse states: **"As a *teruma* unto the Lord," I do not know from how many** loaves the *teruma* **is** taken.

We pick up at the bottom of ayin-zayin amud bet, having just quoted an extremely long braita, and now turn to unpacking it. The braita begins with amar mar: *ve-hikriv mimenu* — you shall bring from it. This concerns the breads of the toda and the principle that the separation must be made *min hamichubar*, from what is connected — meaning, as Rashi explains, that all the loaves remain together in the same location when the separation is performed, so that the gift represents a lifting-up from a unified whole.

### One-in-Ten Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר אֲבִימִי: שֶׁלֹּא יְנַתֵּחַ בָּשָׂר קוֹדֶם שֶׁיִּטּוֹל אֵימוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The sacrificial portions of the sin offering must be sacrificed from that which is joined together, **in accordance with** that **which Rav Ḥisda** says that **Avimi says, as Rav Ḥisda says** that **Avimi says:** The priest **may not cut up the meat** of the offering **before taking** from it **the sacrificial portions** that are to be burned upon the altar.

The Gemara immediately raises a challenge: the word *mimenu* also appears elsewhere in the Torah — *et kol chelbo yarim mimenu*, you should lift up all the fat from the sacrifice. There too it says *mimenu*, yet what is the novelty? Of course the fat is *mechubar* — it is literally inside the animal. Tosafot answers that this is precisely the point: the fact that it is so obviously connected is why the Torah need not innovate there. What we learn from that verse, as Rav Chisda said in the name of Avimi, is a procedural instruction: *she-lo yenateach basar kodem she-yitol emurav* — do not first chop up the meat and then remove the fat. Rather, the fat should be removed while still forming one whole with the korban.

### Excluding Midyan/Challah


This is not merely a technicality. The concept of *mimenu* and *mechubar* captures the very essence of terumah: a gift that represents the whole from which it is separated — and specifically the finest of that whole. The *chelev* represents the choicest part of the korban; presenting it while still unified with the animal conveys that this is the gift of the entire korban to God. The same logic applies to terumah from grain and to the loaves of the toda: one does not simply hand over some bread to the kohen — one lifts a gift from the entirety of the forty loaves.

### Mimenu Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״תְּרוּמָה״, וְנֶאֱמַר בִּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר ״תְּרוּמָה״, מָה לְהַלָּן אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה, אַף כָּאן אֶחָד מֵעֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **I** therefore **derive** this from a verbal analogy: It **is stated here,** in the passage of the loaves describing the thanks offering: **"*Teruma*," and** it **is stated with regard to *teruma* of the tithe:** "Then you shall set apart from it **a *teruma*** for the Lord, even a tithe of the tithe" (Numbers 18:26). **Just as below,** with regard to *teruma* of the tithe, **one** out of every **ten** is separated as *teruma*, **so too here,** with regard to the loaves of the thanks offering, **one** out **of** every **ten** loaves is separated as *teruma*.

The Gemara continues: *nemar kan terumah ve-nemar be-terumat ma'aser terumah* — the word *terumah* appears here by the toda-loaves, and the same word appears by terumat ma'aser. It is through this verbal analogy that we derive the proportion: one loaf out of ten. One might then ask: why not derive the proportion from *trumat Midyan*? There the Torah also uses the word *terumah*, and the proportion there is one in five hundred — a very different ratio.

### Mita v'Chomesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִים תְּרוּמָה הַנּוֹהֶגֶת לְדוֹרוֹת מִתְּרוּמָה הַנּוֹהֶגֶת לְדוֹרוֹת, וְאַל תּוֹכִיחַ תְּרוּמַת מִדְיָן שֶׁאֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת לְדוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **One derives** the *halakha* of *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering, **which is practiced for** all **generations, from *teruma*** of the tithe, **which is** also **practiced for** all **generations. And *teruma* of Midian shall not** serve as **proof, as it is not practiced for** all **generations.**

The Gemara answers: *danim terumah hanohaget ladorot mi-terumah hanohaget ladorot* — we derive a terumah that applies for all generations from another terumah that applies for all generations, namely terumat ma'aser. The gift from the booty of Midyan, *trumat Midyan*, was a one-time event, *she-eina nohaget ladorot*, and therefore cannot serve as a halachic model.

### Dimua Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ ״מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמַת ה׳״ מִדָּבָר שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ ״תְּרוּמָה לַה׳״, לְאַפּוֹקֵי תְּרוּמַת חַלָּה דְּלֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ ״מִמֶּנּוּ תְּרוּמָה לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that **one derives a matter,** i.e., *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering, **of which it is stated: "From it a *teruma* unto the Lord"** (Leviticus 7:14), **from a matter,** i.e., *teruma* of the tithe, **of which it is stated:** "From it **a *teruma* for the Lord"** (Numbers 18:26). This serves **to exclude *teruma* of *ḥalla*, of which it is not stated: From it a *teruma* for the Lord.**

The Gemara then asks: let us instead derive the proportion from *trumat challah*, which also uses the word *terumah*. There the Sages indicate the amount is one in twenty-four — why not apply that proportion here? The school of Rabbi Yishmael responds by focusing on a more precise verbal parallel. The verse by the toda uses the phrase *mimenu terumah l'Hashem*, and the verse by terumat ma'aser uses the analogous phrase *mimenu trumat Hashem*. Both share not only the word *terumah* but also the word *mimenu*. The case of *trumat challah*, by contrast, does not use the word *mimenu* — and this additional word therefore serves *l'afukei trumat challah*, to exclude the challah proportion from the comparison. We thus confirm that the proportion is one in ten, as derived from terumat ma'aser.

## Rava's Questions

### Double Yud Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: תְּרוּמַת לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה, חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ, אוֹ אֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִיתָה וָחוֹמֶשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava raises a dilemma:** With regard to ***teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering,** is a non-priest who intentionally partakes **of it liable** to receive **death** at the hand of Heaven, as is the *halakha* concerning a non-priest who intentionally consumes *teruma* of the tithe? **And** similarly, is a non-priest who unwittingly partakes of *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering liable to pay the value of what he consumed as well as an additional **one-fifth** of that value, as is the *halakha* concerning a non-priest who partakes of *teruma* of the tithe unwittingly (see Leviticus 22:9, 14)? **Or is one not** liable to receive **death** at the hand of Heaven **or** to pay an additional **one-fifth for their** consumption?

Having established that the toda-loaves are called *terumah*, Rava raises a fascinating question: *trumat lachmei todah — chayavin aleha mita v'chomesh o ein chayavin aleha mita v'chomesh?* If a non-kohen eats this bread, does he incur *mita b'yadei shamayim* for intentional consumption? And does he add a fifth when paying back for inadvertent consumption — the classic laws that apply to terumah?

### Assara Esronot


The question is whether the word *terumah* here is technical or merely generic, meaning simply a gift or separation. The classic agricultural terumah — grain, challah, bikurim, terumat ma'aser — all share the character of being standard, obligatory gifts from the produce of the land, given to the kohen as God's representative. The toda-loaves operate in an entirely different conceptual universe: the toda offering is optional, and the loaves accompany it not as a gift from agricultural bounty but as an appurtenance of the korban itself. The *terumah* here is lifted not from the grain of one's field but from the forty loaves one has prepared. This raises the genuine question of whether the technical halachic consequences of terumah-status follow.

Rava formulates the question precisely: since the toda-bread is *itkash*, linked, to terumat ma'aser through the gezeira shava, perhaps it is *k'terumat ma'aser dami* — similar to terumat ma'aser in all its laws. Or perhaps the verse *bo v'chamishito* and *u-metu vo* — the death penalty and the added fifth — specifically exclude this case, as the "it" (*bo*) limits these consequences to classic terumah.

### Matza Piggyback

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְדַמַּעַת, אוֹ אֵינָהּ מְדַמַּעַת? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava raises another dilemma based on the comparison of *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering and *teruma* of the tithe: Does *teruma* of the loaves of the thanks offering that fell into non-sacred produce **render the mixture** forbidden, as is the *halakha* concerning *teruma* of the tithe? **Or** does the comparison of *teruma* of the loaves to *teruma* of the tithe apply only with regard to the proper measure, and it **does not render the mixture** forbidden? The Gemara comments: These dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

The question of mita and chomesh is left unresolved. Similarly, the Gemara asks whether the toda-bread creates *dimua* — the status that results when terumah becomes mixed with non-sanctified produce, rendering the mixture forbidden to non-kohanim. This too is left as *teiku*. It is worth noting that the laws of dimua are in large part rabbinic in origin, which makes the question somewhat unusual, yet the Gemara raises it nonetheless.

### Gezeira Shava Chain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: ״תִּהְיֶינָה״ כְּתִיב. אֵימָא עֲשָׂרָה קְפִיזֵי? אָמַר רָבָא: בְּעֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת דִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi said: "They shall be" [*tihyena*] is written** with two instances of the letter *yod*. The superfluous *yod*, whose numerical value is ten, is interpreted to indicate that the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering must be prepared from ten tenths of flour. The Gemara raises an objection: **Say** that the superfluous *yod* indicates that the loaves must be prepared with **ten half-kav,** a smaller measure than ten tenths of an ephah? **Rava says:** One cannot say this, since **the verse** habitually **spoke of tenths** of an ephah and not other measures.

The Gemara now returns to the braita. The next point concerns the measure of flour per loaf. The question is whether we should understand the model of *shtei halechem* to mean one *issaron* per loaf — which would give ten *esronot* for the ten *chametz* loaves — or merely two *esronot* total divided across all the loaves. The braita says: *talmud lomar tihyena* — the verse says *tihyena*.

### Tavyu Inclusion


The Gemara asks: *mai talmuda* — what exactly do we derive from this? Rav Yosef bar Avdimi explains: *tihyena ksiv* — it is written *tihyena*, with a double *yud*. Both Rashi and Tosafot explain that the double *yud* signals that the measure applies to ten loaves. Since *shtei halechem* is two loaves each of one *issaron*, the verse signals by means of *tihyena* that this unit of one *issaron* per loaf applies equally to the ten *chametz* loaves of the toda — yielding *asara esronot* for those ten loaves.

One might then ask: perhaps the *yud* teaches ten, but perhaps ten of a different measure — ten *kafizi* rather than ten *esronot*? Rava answers: *be-esronot diber ha-kasuv* — the Torah speaks in units of *esronot*. The verse explicitly calls the *shtei halechem* by the measure of *shtei esronot*, and therefore when the word teaches ten, it must mean *asara esronot*.

### Miluim Three Types


We have thus established that the ten *chametz* loaves of the toda require *asara esronot* — one *issaron* per loaf.

### Revucha Identification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָמַדְנוּ עֲשָׂרָה לְחָמֵץ. עֲשָׂרָה לְמַצָּה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַל חַלּוֹת לֶחֶם חָמֵץ״, כְּנֶגֶד חָמֵץ הָבֵא מַצָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its analysis of the *baraita*: **We have learned** that there are **ten** tenths of an ephah of flour **for the** loaves of **leavened bread** accompanying the thanks offering. **From where** is it derived that there are **ten** tenths of an ephah **for** the thirty loaves of ***matza*? The verse states: "With cakes of leavened bread,"** after mentioning the three types of *matza* accompanying the thanks offering (Leviticus 7:12–13), indicating that one must **bring** the ***matza*** in a measure **corresponding to** the measure of the loaves of **leavened bread.**

The Gemara now asks: *asara l'matzah minayin* — how do we know that the thirty *matzah* loaves are also prepared from *asara esronot*? The verse says *al chalos lechem chametz* — alongside the *chametz* loaves bring these *matzah* loaves. This teaches *keneged chametz havei matzah* — the *matzah* is equivalent in total flour to the *chametz*. While the *chametz* consists of ten loaves and the *matzah* of thirty, the total quantity of flour is the same: *asara esronot*. Each *matzah* loaf is therefore one-third the size of each *chametz* loaf.

### Kohen Gadol Initiation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ? הֵימֶנּוּ וְדָבָר אַחֵר הוּא, וְכֹל הֵימֶנּוּ וְדָבָר אַחֵר – לָא הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But can a matter that was derived by comparison** then **come back and teach** the matter **by comparison** with regard to consecrated matters? Since the *halakha* that the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering are prepared from ten tenths of an ephah is itself derived by comparison to the two loaves, can one then derive from it that the *matza* loaves of the thanks offering consist of ten tenths of an ephah? The Gemara responds: The *halakha* that the loaves of leavened bread consist of ten loaves made from ten tenths of an ephah is not derived solely by comparison to the two loaves; rather, **it is** derived **from itself,** as the *halakha* that there are ten loaves is stated in the verse concerning the thanks offering, **and** from **another matter, and any** *halakha* derived **from itself and** from **another matter is not** considered **a comparison.** Consequently, the *halakha* with regard to the *matza* loaves may be derived from it.

Tosafot raises a significant methodological objection. We learned the *issaron*-per-loaf principle from *shtei halechem* (source A), and applied it to the *chametz* loaves of the toda (B). Now we are deriving the *matzah* measure (C) from the *chametz* (B). This is a daisy-chained derivation: A to B, then B to C. The Gemara's principle is *v'chi davar ha-lamad be-hekkesh chozer u-melamed be-hekkesh?* — can something learned through a *hekkesh* turn around and teach through another *hekkesh*?

### Nazir Proportions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? ״תָּבִיאוּ״ רִבּוּיָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that a *halakha* derived from itself and from another matter **does not constitute a comparison, but according to the one who says** that a *halakha* derived from itself and from another matter **does constitute a comparison, what is there to say?** The Gemara responds: According to that opinion, the *halakha* that each loaf is prepared from a tenth of an ephah is derived from that which the verse states with regard to the two loaves: **"You shall bring"** (Leviticus 23:17), which **is** a term of **amplification** that serves to teach that the loaves of leavened bread of the thanks offering consist of ten tenths of an ephah of flour. Since this *halakha* is derived not by comparison but by amplification from the two loaves, one can derive the *halakha* with regard to the *matza* loaves by comparison to the loaves of leavened bread.

The Gemara answers: *himenu ve-davar akher hu* — it is "from it and another thing." The principle is that when you are already learning something locally from B to C — in this case, the equivalence of *chametz* and *matzah* flour directly from the verse — then you may *schlepp* along what you additionally learned from A. This is the distinction between a pure daisy-chain, which is prohibited, and a piggyback, which is permitted: since the connection from the *chametz* to the *matzah* is established on its own terms, one may bring along the measure of *asara esronot* as well.

The Gemara then asks: what of the opinion that even *himenu ve-davar akher* is still considered a *hekkesh* and is therefore forbidden? *mai ika le-meimar?*

### Matzos Klal Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: אֲנִי דִּמִּיתִיהוּ לְחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, וְאַתָּה דִּמִּיתוֹ לְבַעַל מוּם, נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה – אִם דּוֹמֶה לְחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, נְדוּנֶנּוּ מֵחוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, אִם דּוֹמֶה לְבַעַל מוּם, נְדוּנֶנּוּ מִבַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** [Note: This passage is from Menachot 79a and relates to a different sugya regarding slaughter conditions.]

The answer is *tavi'u ribuya hi*. The derivation of the *chametz* measure from *shtei halechem* was based on the verse *mimoshvoteichem tavi'u lechem tenufah shtayim esronim*. The word *tavi'u* — "you shall bring" — is understood as an inclusive term, encompassing many different cases of bringing. Just as it was used to extend the *issaron* measure to the *chametz* loaves, the same word *tavi'u* can be read to include a teaching about the *matzah* loaves as well — and this operates not as a *hekkesh* but as a *ribbui*, an amplification. This sidesteps the problem of chaining *hekkesh* derivations entirely.

To summarize what we have established: we know there are ten loaves per type because of the *terumah* gezeira shava with terumat ma'aser, and the Gemara has explained why we do not learn from *trumat Midyan* or *trumat challah*. One loaf of each type goes to the kohen, yielding forty loaves total with one loaf from each set of ten going to the kohen. Rava raised the open question of the halachic status of that gift. And we have established that the ten *chametz* loaves require *asara esronot* — one *issaron* per loaf — which means each chametz loaf is enormous, approximately the size of a loaf baked from a full five-pound bag of flour. The thirty *matzah* loaves share the same total quantity of flour, so each *matzah* loaf is one-third the size. In total, a person bringing a toda presents forty loaves made from the equivalent of twenty such five-pound bags — a staggering quantity of bread.

## Ten Loaves & Flour

### Inside-Outside Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמִּילּוּאִים הָיוּ בָּאִים כַּמַּצָּה שֶׁבַּתּוֹדָה – חַלּוֹת, וּרְקִיקִין, (וּרְבִיכָה) [וּרְבוּכָה].
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The loaves that accompanied the ram of **the inauguration** of the Tabernacle **would come parallel to the** three types of ***matza* that** accompany **the thanks offering: Loaves, wafers, and** loaves **poached** in water and made with oil (see Leviticus 8:26). The loaves of leavened bread that accompany the thanks offering were not brought with the ram of inauguration.

The Mishnah now transitions to a historical note: *ha-miluim haya ba'im ke-matzah she-ba-toda* — when they inaugurated the kohanim into the avodah, they brought loaves similar to the *matzah* of the toda. There was no *chametz* component in the miluim, but there were three types of *matzah* loaves. The Mishnah identifies these as *challos, rekikin, u-revichah*.

### Location Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְזִירוּת הָיְתָה בָּאָה שְׁתֵּי יָדוֹת כְּמַצָּה שֶׁל תּוֹדָה, חַלּוֹת וּרְקִיקִין, וְאֵין בָּהּ רְבוּכָה, נִמְצָא עֲשָׂרָה קַבִּין יְרוּשַׁלְמִית, שֶׁהֵן שִׁשָּׁה עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת וְעוֹדְיִין.
>
> **Translation:** The loaves that accompany the offering that the nazirite brings upon completion of his period of **naziriteship would come** with only **two parts** of the three types of *matza* that accompany the thanks offering, namely, **loaves and wafers, but there is no** *matza* **poached** in water (see Numbers 6:15). **Consequently,** the loaves of the offering of a nazirite are from **ten *kav*** of fine flour according to the **Jerusalem** measure, as taught in the previous mishna that each type of the loaves of *matza* comes from five *kav* of flour, **which** equal **six-and-two-thirds tenths** of an ephah according to the wilderness measure, as each type of the loaves of *matza* comes from three-and-one-third tenths of an ephah.

By a nazir, who brings certain loaves with his *shlamim* upon completing his nazirite vow, the Mishnah states: *hayta ba'ah shtei yadot ke-matzah* — he brings two-thirds of the *matzah* types brought by the toda. Specifically, *challos* and *rekikin* — but not *revuchah*, the heavily oil-soaked type.

The calculation follows directly. The toda's thirty *matzah* loaves use *asara esronot*, which equals fifteen *kavim* in the wilderness measure. Since the nazir brings only two of the three types, proportionally he uses two-thirds of that flour. Two-thirds of ten *esronot* is six and two-thirds *esronot*, which in the Jerusalem measure equals *asara kabin Yerushalmiyot* — ten Jerusalem *kavim*, as the Mishnah states.

### Pesach Chametz Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וּמִסַּל הַמַּצּוֹת אֲשֶׁר לִפְנֵי ה׳ לָקַח חַלַּת מַצָּה אַחַת וְחַלַּת לֶחֶם שֶׁמֶן אַחַת וְרָקִיק אֶחָד״. בִּשְׁלָמָא ״חַלּוֹת״ – חַלּוֹת, ״רָקִיק״ – רָקִיק, ״שֶׁמֶן״ מַאי נִינְהוּ? לָאו רְבוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the loaves that accompanied the ram of the inauguration of the Tabernacle consisted of the three types of *matza* brought with a thanks offering: Loaves, wafers, and loaves poached in water and made with oil. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said:** It is derived from **that** which **the verse states: "And out of the basket of unleavened bread that was before the Lord, he took one unleavened cake, and one cake of oiled bread, and one wafer"** (Leviticus 8:26). **Granted, "cakes"** are **cakes,** and **"wafer"** is **a wafer.** But **what is "oiled** bread" referring to? Is this **not** referring to *matza* **poached** in water and made with oil?

The Gemara asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for saying the miluim included these three types of *matzah*? Rav Chisda said in the name of Rav Chama bar Guria: the verse describing the miluim states *u-misal ha-matzot asher lifnei Hashem lakach challat matzah achat ve-challat lechem shemen achat ve-rakik echad* — from the basket of matzot before God he took one *challat matzah*, one *challat lechem shemen*, and one *rakik*.

### Yerushalayim Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַוְיָא: אֵימָא אַנְתָּא דְּמִשְׁחָא! אֶלָּא, כִּדְדָרֵשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי טַבְלָא: ״זֶה קׇרְבַּן אַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳ בְּיוֹם הִמָּשַׁח אֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Avya objects to this:** One can **say** that "oiled bread" is referring to **a cake of oil. Rather,** derive it **as Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda taught in the name of Rabbi Tavla,** that the verse states: **"This is the offering of Aaron and of his sons, which they shall bring to the Lord on the day when he is anointed:** The tenth part of an ephah of fine flour for a meal offering perpetually, half of it in the morning and half of it in the evening. On a pan it shall be made with oil; when it is poached, you shall bring it in; in broken pieces shall you bring the meal offering for a pleasing aroma to the Lord. And the anointed priest that shall be in his stead from among his sons shall bring it" (Leviticus 6:13–15).

Two of the three are readily identified by the Gemara: *bishlama challos challos* — when the verse says *challat matzah* by the miluim, that parallels the *chalos matzos* of the toda; and *rakik* is simply *rakik*, matching the *rekikin* of the toda. Two out of three are straightforward.

### Crust Requirement


The third type mentioned by the miluim is *challat lechem shemen* — a loaf of oily bread. The Gemara naturally assumes this corresponds to the *revuchah* of the toda. But Rav Chavya bar Rav Avia challenges this: *eima anta de-mishcha* — perhaps *challat lechem shemen* means simply some kind of oil-drenched cake, *uga shel shemen*, something entirely different from the *revuchah* mentioned by the toda. The identification of *lechem shemen* with *revuchah* is attractive but not logically compelled. The pseudo-Rashi goes even further, entertaining the possibility that *challah* might not refer to bread at all here, though he acknowledges the difficulty given that the verse says *challat lechem*.

### All Loaves Must Be Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מָה לָמַדְנוּ לְבָנָיו בְּיוֹם הִמָּשְׁחוֹ? אֶלָּא מַקִּישׁ חִינּוּכוֹ לְהִמָּשְׁחוֹ: מָה הִמָּשְׁחוֹ רְבוּכָה, אַף חִינּוּכוֹ רְבוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** One can ask: **And what have we learned with regard to** Aaron's **sons,** i.e., the ordinary priests, from the instructions for the High Priest **on the day that he is anointed? Rather,** the verse **juxtaposes** the meal offering brought by an ordinary priest during **his inauguration with** the meal offering brought by the High Priest when **he is anointed.** This juxtaposition teaches that **just as** the meal offering brought by the High Priest when **he is anointed is poached in water, so too** the meal offering brought by an ordinary priest during **his inauguration is poached in water.** Similarly, the loaves brought with the ram of the inauguration of the Tabernacle included loaves poached in water.

The Gemara resolves the question through a teaching of Nachman bar Rav Chisda in the name of Rav Tavla. The verse states: *zeh korban Aharon u-vanav asher yakrivu la-Hashem beyom himashach oto* — this is the korban of Aharon and his sons that they shall bring on the day of their anointing. This verse serves multiple functions: it records what was brought at the historical miluim, and it also establishes a principle for all future kohanim, *makkish chinucho le-himashcho* — it equates the *chinuch*, the initiation, of a kohen into the avodah with the anointing of the Kohen Gadol. The daily *minchah* that the Kohen Gadol brings — his *chavitin* — is the expression of *himashcho*, his anointed status. The verse in Vayikra describing the Kohen Gadol's daily offering uses the word *murbeches*, which is precisely the *revuchah* form.

### Lishmah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל הַמִּתְקָרֵב לַעֲבוֹדָה צָרִיךְ שְׁתֵּי עֶשְׂרוֹנוֹת הָאֵיפָה, אַחַת לְהִמָּשְׁחוֹ, וְאַחַת לְחִינּוּכוֹ. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: שָׁלֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § Concerning the matter of the High Priest's meal offering on the day of his inaugural service in the Temple, **Rav Ḥisda says: A High Priest who approaches the** Temple **service** on the day that he is anointed is **required** to bring two meal offerings consisting of **two tenths of an ephah, one for** the fact that **he is anointed** as the High Priest and must bring the daily meal offering of the High Priest, **and one for his inauguration. Mar bar Rav Ashi says:** He requires **three** tenths of an ephah.

From this equation, the Gemara derives: *mah himashcho bi-revuchah af chinucho bi-revuchah* — just as the Kohen Gadol's daily *chavitin* is *revuchah*, so too the initiation offering of any kohen entering the avodah for the first time is *revuchah*. This identifies the third type of the miluim loaves. Rav Chisda applies this to a practical halacha: a Kohen Gadol entering service for the first time must bring two *minchot* — one for his daily *chavitin* obligation (*l'himashcho*) and one for his initiation into the avodah (*l'chinucho*). Mar bar Rav Ashi adds that if this Kohen Gadol has never previously served even as a regular kohen, he requires three *minchot*: one to initiate him into the priesthood generally, one to initiate him as Kohen Gadol specifically, and one for his daily *chavitin*. The two opinions are not in dispute — *v'lo pligi* — they address different cases: where the individual had previously served as a regular kohen before assuming the high priesthood, and where he had never served at all.

### Non-Stringy Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא פְּלִיגִי, הָא – דַּעֲבַד עֲבוֹדָה כְּשֶׁהוּא כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, הָא – דְּלָא עֲבַד עֲבוֹדָה כְּשֶׁהוּא כֹּהֵן הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** **And they do not disagree. This** one, Rav Ḥisda, is referring to a High Priest **who** already **performed** the Temple **service as an ordinary priest.** Accordingly, he has already brought a meal offering for his inauguration as an ordinary priest. He therefore brings only two tenths of flour, one for his inauguration as High Priest and one for the daily meal offering of the High Priest. And **that** one, Mar bar Rav Ashi, is referring to a High Priest **who has not** yet **performed** the Temple **service as an ordinary priest.** He must therefore bring three tenths, one for his inauguration as an ordinary priest, one for his inauguration as High Priest, and one for the daily meal offering of the High Priest.

Having established the three types of miluim loaves and their connection to the toda, the Gemara turns to why the nazir's offering excludes the *revuchah*.

### Forty Sanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״שְׁלָמָיו״ – לְרַבּוֹת שַׁלְמֵי נָזִיר, לַעֲשֶׂרֶת קַבִּין יְרוּשַׁלְמִיּוֹת וְלִרְבִיעִית שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the loaves brought with a thanks offering: "If he sacrifices it for a thanks offering, then he shall sacrifice with the thanks offering unleavened cakes mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil, and cakes mingled with oil, of fine flour poached. With cakes of leavened bread he shall present his offering with the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanks" (Leviticus 7:12–13). **"His peace offerings"** serves **to include the nazirite's peace offering in** the *halakhot* that its accompanying meal offerings must consist of **ten Jerusalem *kav* and one-quarter** of a *log* **of oil,** just like the measurements required for the cakes and wafers of the thanks offering.

A braita is cited connecting the toda and the nazir through the shared term *shlamav* — the toda is called *zevach todas shlamav*, a sacrifice of thanks among his *shlamim*, which serves to include the *shlamei nazir* within the framework of the toda. This connection establishes the amount of flour (ten *kavim*) and the proportional amount of oil for the nazir's offering.

### All-or-Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לְכׇל מָה שֶׁאָמוּר בָּעִנְיָן, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מַצּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that the verse indicates that the meal offering of the nazirite's peace offering is equivalent to the loaves of the thanks offering **concerning all that is stated with regard to the matter** of the thanks offering, and that one brings three types of *matza* consisting of fifteen Jerusalem *kav* and half a *log* of oil. Therefore, **the verse states** with regard to the nazirite's peace offering: "And a basket of **unleavened bread,** loaves of fine flour mingled with oil, and unleavened wafers spread with oil" (Numbers 6:15), to indicate that the nazirite's meal offering consists only of *matza* loaves and *matza* wafers, and not *matza* poached in water.

One might think the nazir should bring all three *matzah* types including the *revuchah*. Rav Pappa answers through a verbal analogy: the verse by the nazir says *sal matzos*, a basket of *matzos*, and then specifies *challos* and *rekikin*. The word *matzos* appears explicitly when describing both *challos* and *rekikin* in the toda verse — but the *revuchah* is called *solet murbechet* without the word *matzah*. Since the nazir verse specifies *matzos*, it connects the nazir only to those types described as *matzah*, thereby excluding the *revuchah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ ״מַצּוֹת״, לְאַפּוֹקֵי רְבוּכָה דְּלֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ ״מַצּוֹת״. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״מַצּוֹת״ – כָּלַל, ״חַלּוֹת וּרְקִיקִין״ – פָּרַט, כְּלָל וּפְרָט אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מָה שֶׁבַּפְּרָט, חַלּוֹת וּרְקִיקִין – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is the** biblical **derivation** by which "*matza*" indicates that loaves poached in water were not brought? **Rav Pappa says:** The verse indicates that the nazirite brings *matza* specifically from **an item of which it was stated: "*Matzot*."** This serves **to exclude** loaves **poached in water, of which it is not stated: *Matzot*.** In addition, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: "Unleavened bread"** is **a generalization; "loaves** of fine flour mingled with oil, **and** unleavened **wafers** spread with oil" **is a detail.** When the Torah states **a generalization and a detail, the generalization includes only what is in the detail.** Therefore, **loaves and wafers are** included in the nazirite's meal offering, while **other items are not.**

The school of Rabbi Yishmael offers a more elegant resolution: the verse by the nazir employs *klal u-prat* — a general statement (*matzos*) followed by a specification (*challos ve-rekikin*). By the rule of *klal u-prat ein ba-klal ela mah she-ba-prat*, one applies only what is explicitly enumerated and nothing more. There is thus no need to derive the exclusion of *revuchah* from the toda at all — the Pasuk explicitly limits the nazir's loaves to just these two types. Tosafot notes that the purpose of stating a general term before specifying is precisely to signal exclusivity: had the Torah enumerated only the two types without a preceding general term, one might have thought other similar types are also included. The *klal* followed by *prat* signals: only these, and nothing else.

What then is derived from the connection to the toda? The amount of flour and the proportional amount of oil — information not stated explicitly in the nazir's passage.

## Miluim Bread Types

### Explicit Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתּוֹדָה לִפְנִים וְלַחְמָהּ חוּץ לַחוֹמָה – לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם. שְׁחָטָהּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא קָרְמוּ פָּנֶיהָ בַּתַּנּוּר, וַאֲפִילּוּ קָרְמוּ כּוּלָּן חוּץ מֵאֶחָד מֵהֶן – לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters the thanks offering** in its proper place **inside** the Temple courtyard, **and** at that time **its** forty **loaves were outside the wall, the loaves were not consecrated.** Likewise, **if he slaughtered** the thanks offering **before the surface** of the loaves **formed a crust in the oven, and even** if the surface of all the loaves **formed a crust except for one of them, the loaves were not consecrated.**

The Mishnah on ayin-chet amud bet presents a foundational principle about how the toda loaves acquire sanctity: *ha-shochet et ha-toda lifnim ve-lachmah chutz la-chomah — lo kideish ha-lechem*. If one slaughtered the toda offering inside while the bread was outside the wall, the bread is not sanctified. The slaughtering of the toda, rather than any vessel or oven, is what creates the *kedushah* of its accompanying loaves. This is structurally different from a regular *minchah*, whose sanctity is established through its placement in a *kli sharet* without any associated korban. By the toda-bread, full kedushah is achieved only at the moment of shechitah.

### Knife Power Debate


The Mishnah further rules that if the loaves had not yet developed a crust in the oven — *ad shelo karmu paneha ba-tanur* — they are not yet considered *lechem* and thus cannot be sanctified by the shechitah. Furthermore, even if all the loaves but one had formed a crust, *lo kideish ha-lechem* — none of them are sanctified. The Mishnah thus establishes three requirements: the slaughtering must be done as a toda; the bread must be within the appropriate boundary; and all loaves must qualify as *lechem* by having formed a crust.

### Rabbi Yochanan vs Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״חוּץ לַחוֹמָה״? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוּץ לְחוֹמַת בֵּית פָּאגֵי, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: חוּץ לְחוֹמַת הָעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara clarifies: **What** is meant by **outside the wall? Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it means **outside the wall of Beit Pagei** in Jerusalem. **And Reish Lakish says** that it means **outside the wall of the** Temple **courtyard.**

The Gemara asks: *mai chutz la-chomah* — which wall are we speaking of? *reish lakish* says: *chutz le-chomat ha-azarah* — outside the wall of the Azarah itself. The word *al* in *al chalos lechem chametz*, he argues, means the bread must be literally alongside the korban — *be'inan al be-samoch*. If the korban is slaughtered inside the Azarah, the bread must be there with it. Rabbi Yochanan says: *chutz le-chomat Beit Pagi* — only if the bread is outside the outer wall of Jerusalem is it a problem. Within Jerusalem anywhere is sufficient; *lo be'inan al be-samoch*, the word *al* does not require physical proximity. Rabbi Yochanan's position is conceptually grounded in the status of the toda as *kodashim kalim*: since these breads may be eaten throughout Jerusalem, their sanctification can occur throughout Jerusalem, and need not be confined to the Azarah proper.

### Pesach chametz parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: חוּץ לְחוֹמַת הָעֲזָרָה, בָּעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Reish Lakish says** that it means **outside the wall of the** Temple **courtyard,** because the verse states with regard to the loaves of the thanks offering: "Then he shall sacrifice with [*al*] the thanks offering unleavened cakes mingled with oil" (Leviticus 7:12). And **we require** that the thanks offering be slaughtered strictly **"with [*al*]"** the accompanying loaves, i.e., with the loaves **nearby.** If the loaves are outside the wall of the Temple courtyard, they are not considered nearby.

The Gemara notes that this dispute parallels an earlier argument between the same Amoraim regarding *la tishchat *al chametz* dam zivchi* — do not slaughter my Pesach sacrifice while *chametz* exists. The question there is likewise what *al* means: does the *chametz* have to be physically present in the Azarah with the one performing the slaughter, or does it suffice that the person owns *chametz* somewhere, even at home? *reish lakish* holds the former; Rabbi Yochanan holds the latter.

### Need both cases explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חוּץ לְחוֹמַת בֵּית פָּאגֵי, אֲבָל חוּץ לָעֲזָרָה – קָדוֹשׁ, וְלָא בָּעֵינַן ״עַל״ בְּסָמוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If the loaves were **outside the wall of Beit Pagei,** they are not consecrated, because it is prohibited to eat the loaves outside the wall of Beit Pagei. Since if they were taken outside the wall of Beit Pagei, they would be disqualified because they were removed from their permitted area, they cannot be consecrated if they are there. **But** if they were merely **outside the** Temple **courtyard,** where they may be eaten, they are **consecrated. And we do not require** that the thanks offering be slaughtered strictly **"with"** the accompanying loaves, i.e., **nearby.**

The Gemara explains that both cases are necessary: *tzricha*. Had Rabbi Yochanan stated his lenient position only by *chametz* and Pesach, one might have conceded that in a context of prohibition, the Torah cares wherever the chametz is — but by sanctification of bread, perhaps Rabbi Yochanan would agree with *reish lakish* that physical proximity is required. Conversely, had *reish lakish* stated his strict position only regarding the toda-bread, one might have thought that in the context of a positive act of sanctification, closeness is logically required, but regarding *chametz* he might agree with Rabbi Yochanan that ownership anywhere constitutes a problem. Since both scholars maintain their positions in both cases, both rulings are necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיפְּלִיגוּ בַּהּ חֲדָא זִימְנָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: Why is it necessary for Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish to disagree with regard to a case where the loaves were situated outside the wall when the thanks offering was slaughtered? **But didn't they** already **disagree with regard to this** matter **one** other **time?**

### Braita like Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתּוֹדָה לִפְנִים, וְלַחְמָהּ חוּץ לְחוֹמַת בֵּית פָּאגֵי – לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** In the case of **one who slaughters the thanks offering inside** the Temple courtyard, **and** at that time **its loaves were outside the walls of Beit Pagei, the loaves were not consecrated.**

A braita is cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan: *ha-shochet et ha-toda bifnim ve-lachmah chutz le-chomat Beit Pagi — lo kideish ha-lechem*. If the bread is outside of Beit Pagi — outside Jerusalem — it is not sanctified. This implies that bread anywhere within Jerusalem, even outside the Azarah, is sanctified by the shechitah, consistent with Rabbi Yochanan's position. The braita is noted as surprising given that the logic of the Gemara would seem to favor *reish lakish*, yet the braita sides with Rabbi Yochanan.

## Nazir Bread Types

### Crust requirement definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עַל חַלּוֹת לֶחֶם חָמֵץ יַקְרִיב קׇרְבָּנוֹ עַל זֶבַח״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵין הַלֶּחֶם קָדוֹשׁ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קָרְמוּ פָּנֶיהָ בַּתַּנּוּר. ״יַקְרִיב קׇרְבָּנוֹ עַל זֶבַח״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵין הַלֶּחֶם קָדוֹשׁ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁחִיטַת הַזֶּבַח. ״זֶבַח תּוֹדַת״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאִם שָׁחַט שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן לֹא קָדַשׁ הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks to clarify: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught:** The verse states with regard to the loaves accompanying the thanks offering: **"With cakes of leavened bread he shall present his offering with the sacrifice** of his peace offerings for thanks" (Leviticus 7:13). This **teaches that the loaves are not consecrated unless the surface** of the loaves **formed a crust in the oven,** as only then can they be considered "cakes of leavened bread." The phrase **"he shall present his offering with the sacrifice" teaches that the loaves are consecrated only upon the slaughtering of the offering.** The phrase **"the sacrifice** of his peace offerings **for thanks" teaches that if one slaughtered** the offering **not for its sake, the loaves were not consecrated.**

Having established the wall-boundary requirement, the Gemara turns to the crust requirement. The source is a braita: *al chalas lechem chametz yakriv korbano al zevach* — he shall bring on the loaves of chametz bread alongside the slaughtering. The word *lechem* teaches that the bread must qualify as *lechem*: *she-lo kideish ha-lechem ela im kein karmu paneha ba-tanur* — it does not become sanctified unless its surface has crusted in the oven. The verse also teaches two further principles: the slaughtering of the korban is what sanctifies the bread (*she-ein ha-lechem kadosh ela bi-shechitat ha-zevach*); and if the korban was slaughtered *shelo lishmah* — not with the intent of offering it as a toda — the bread is not sanctified. A shechitah lacking proper *kavanah* cannot impart full toda-status to the animal, and therefore cannot activate the accompanying bread.

### Pesach matzah cooking standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יוֹצְאִין בְּמַצָּה נָא, וּבְמַצָּה הָעֲשׂוּיָה בְּאִילְפָּס. מַאי מַצָּה נָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל שֶׁפּוֹרְסָהּ וְאֵין חוּטִין נִמְשָׁכִין הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § On a similar note, **the Sages taught:** One **fulfills** the obligation to eat *matza* on Passover **with half-done,** i.e., not fully baked, ***matza*, and with *matza* made in a stewpot [*ilpas*]** rather than baked in an oven. The Gemara asks: **What** constitutes **half-done *matza*? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** It is **any** *matza* that is sufficiently baked such **that** if **one breaks it, threads** of dough **are not drawn from it.**

The Gemara connects the *lechem* requirement to the analogous question of what constitutes adequate baking for *matzah* on Pesach. A braita taught: *yotzin be-matzah na* — one can fulfill the mitzvah of matzah even with under-baked matzah, and even with matzah baked in a pot rather than an oven. The Gemara asks: *mai matzah na* — what precisely is under-baked matzah, and what is the minimum threshold?

### Strands test for bread


Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *kol she-porsa ve-ein chutin nimshachin heimena* — any loaf that when broken does not produce doughy strands pulling from the interior has sufficiently baked. If breaking it still reveals stretching strands of raw dough, it has not achieved the status of bread.

### Lechem definition parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן לַחְמֵי תוֹדָה, פְּשִׁיטָא! הָכָא ״לֶחֶם״ כְּתִיב, וְהָכָא ״לֶחֶם״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And so** this is the *halakha* **with regard to the matter of the loaves of the thanks offering,** i.e., if the loaves were already half-done at the time of the slaughter of the thanks offering, they are consecrated. The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary for Rava to state this? Isn't it **obvious? Here,** with regard to *matza*, the term **"bread" is written** (Deuteronomy 16:3), **and here,** with regard to the loaves of the thanks offering, the term **"bread" is written.**

Rava then applies this standard explicitly to toda-bread: *ve-chen le-inyan lachmei toda* — the same measure applies to the loaves of the toda. The Gemara asks: *peshita* — is this not obvious? Both cases use the word *lechem*, and therefore the same definition must apply: *hacha lechem ketiv ve-hacha lechem ketiv*. Rava's contribution, the Gemara clarifies, is to forestall a possible objection: since one loaf of ten must be given intact to the kohen, *echad amar Rachmana she-lo yitpares*, one might worry that a barely-baked loaf, while meeting the definition of bread, might crumble too easily and thus effectively be a *paris* — a broken piece, which would not be valid. Rava therefore teaches *ka mashma lan* that once the bread meets the threshold of *lechem*, it is fully valid regardless of its structural fragility.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״אֶחָד״ אֲמַר רַחֲמָנָא – שֶׁלֹּא יִטּוֹל פָּרוּס, וְהָא כְּמַאן דִּפְרִיסָא דָּמְיָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Lest you say** that since **the Merciful One states** with regard to the loaves of the thanks offering: "And of it he shall present **one** out of each offering for a gift unto the Lord" (Leviticus 7:14), this indicates **that one may not take sliced** bread for the thanks offering, **and this** half-done bread **is considered like one that is sliced,** as it will break apart upon being lifted, and it should consequently be disqualified for the bread of the thanks offering, therefore Rava **teaches us** that once it is baked to that point, it is considered as one loaf and it is valid for the thanks offering.

## Bread Sanctification

### Eighty loaves scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר, תּוֹדָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל שְׁמוֹנִים חַלּוֹת: חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: קָדְשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לֹא קָדְשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that there is a dispute among the *amora'im* with regard to **a thanks offering that one slaughtered** accompanied **by eighty loaves** rather than the required forty. **Ḥizkiyya says: Forty of the eighty** loaves **are consecrated, and Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Not** even **forty of** the **eighty** loaves **are consecrated.**

The Gemara now addresses a vivid practical case: *toda she-shachata al shemonim chalot* — someone brought eighty loaves with the toda instead of the required forty and then slaughtered the korban. What is the result?

### Chizkiya forty sanctified


Chizkiya holds: *kidshu arbaim mitoch shemonim* — forty of the eighty loaves become sanctified. The shechitah operates on those forty for which it is halachically effective. One does not know which forty, and therefore all eighty must be treated stringently — eaten within Jerusalem, within the allotted time — but the person has fulfilled his toda obligation.

### Rabbi Yochanan all or nothing


Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *lo kidshu arbaim mitoch shemonim* — none of the loaves are sanctified. Since the person brought eighty and the toda only accommodates forty, the shechitah cannot partially operate. All or nothing: since it cannot sanctify all eighty, it sanctifies none, and the toda is therefore brought without its accompanying bread.

### Rabbi Zeira intent resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים, הֵיכָא דְּאָמַר ״לִיקְדְּשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים״ – קָדְשׁוּ, ״אַל יִקְדְּשׁוּ אַרְבָּעִים אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יִקְדְּשׁוּ שְׁמוֹנִים״ – לֹא קָדְשׁוּ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בִּסְתָמָא, מָר סָבַר: לְאַחְרָיוּת קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, וּמָר סָבַר: לְקׇרְבָּן גָּדוֹל קָא מִיכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this dispute, **Rabbi Zeira says: Everyone,** even Rabbi Yoḥanan, **concedes** that in a case **where** the individual bringing the offering **said: Let forty of** the **eighty** loaves **be consecrated, that** forty **are consecrated.** Likewise, everyone concedes that where he said: **Forty should not be consecrated unless eighty will be consecrated,** that **they were not consecrated. They disagree when** one brings eighty loaves **without specification.** One **Sage,** Ḥizkiyya, **holds: He intends** to consecrate only forty of them, but he brought eighty in order **to guarantee** that there will be sufficient loaves. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds: He intends to** bring **a large offering** of eighty loaves; consequently, none of the loaves are consecrated.

Rabbi Zeira clarifies the limits of the dispute. All agree that if the person explicitly stated his intent — *likdeshu arbaim mitoch shemonim*, "let forty of the eighty be sanctified" — then forty are sanctified. All also agree that if the person said explicitly that forty should be sanctified only if all eighty are sanctified — making it conditional — then nothing is sanctified, because the Torah does not recognize eighty. The dispute between Chizkiya and Rabbi Yochanan concerns only *stama* — when no explicit intent was stated. Chizkiya holds we presume the extra loaves were *le-achrayut*, a backup in case some were lost. Rabbi Yochanan holds we presume the person intended a *korban gadol*, a grander offering with all eighty, and since that cannot work, nothing works.

### Abaye kli sharet debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְאַחְרָיוּת קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, וּבִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת מְקַדְּשִׁין שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת קָא מִיפַּלְגִי. מָר סָבַר: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת מְקַדְּשִׁין שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת, וּמָר סָבַר: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye** offered another explanation of the dispute and **said: Everyone** agrees that **he intends to guarantee** that there will be sufficient loaves, **and they disagree with regard to** whether **service vessels consecrate** the appropriate measure **without** the **intention** of the one bringing the offering. One **Sage,** Ḥizkiyya, **holds** that **service vessels consecrate** items **without intention.** Accordingly, the knife that slaughters the thanks offering and consecrates its accompanying loaves consecrates forty of the eighty. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **service vessels do not consecrate** items **without intention.**

Abaye reframes the dispute. He holds that everyone agrees the person intended all eighty — *le-korban gadol kamichaven*. The disagreement is about a different question: *be-kli sharet mekadeshin shelo mida-at kamifligei* — does a *kli sharet* sanctify independently of the owner's intention? Here, the *kli sharet* at issue is not a bowl or vessel but the *sakin*, the slaughtering knife, since it is the act of shechitah that conveys sanctity to the bread. Chizkiya holds that a *kli sharet* sanctifies regardless of intent, and therefore the knife's action sanctifies forty even though the person wanted eighty. Rabbi Yochanan holds that a *kli sharet* only sanctifies in accordance with intent, and since the intent was for eighty — which cannot be sanctified — nothing is sanctified.

### Rav Papa knife power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת מְקַדְּשִׁין שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת, וְהָכָא בְּסַכִּין קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – מָר סָבַר: סַכִּין מְקַדֶּשֶׁת כִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת, וּמָר סָבַר: סַכִּין, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ תּוֹךְ, אֵינָהּ מְקַדֶּשֶׁת כִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa** offered another explanation of the dispute and **said: Everyone** agrees that **service vessels consecrate** items **without intention, and here they disagree with regard to** whether this is also the *halakha* with regard to the slaughtering **knife.** One **Sage,** Ḥizkiyya, **holds** that **the knife consecrates** items **as a service vessel** does. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **since the knife has no inside, it does not consecrate** items **as a service vessel** does.

Rav Papa offers yet another framing. He holds that everyone agrees a *kli sharet* requires *da'at* — intent — to operate. The debate, rather, concerns whether the *sakin* is more powerful than a standard *kli sharet*: *ve-hacha be-sakin kamifligei*. Chizkiya holds *sakin alimah mi-kli sharet* — the knife is more potent than an ordinary sacred vessel. Precisely because a knife has no *toch*, no containing space, and yet the Torah empowers it to impart sanctity to the bread, this shows that it operates in a uniquely powerful manner, overriding even the owner's contrary intention and sanctifying forty. Rabbi Yochanan holds *sakin lo alimah mi-kol sha'ar kli sharet* — the knife is not more potent. Since it lacks a container, it functions no differently from a standard *kli sharet*, which requires *da'at*. The owner's intent for eighty controls, and since eighty cannot be sanctified, nothing is sanctified.

This final sugya weaves together several deep questions simultaneously: the all-or-nothing logic of partial fulfillment, the independent sanctifying power of sacred vessels, and the unique status of the shechitah-knife as a mechanism of *kiddush lechem* — a rich conclusion to a daf devoted to every dimension of the toda's bread.
