# Gittin 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tna'im Overview

### Cloak & Conditions

The *daf* opens by continuing a rich *gemara* about what constitutes satisfaction of *tna'im* — conditions — in *gittin*. The *gemara* has been reading the *Mishna* as presenting the following case: a husband says "give me my cloak" as the condition of a *get*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that if the cloak is lost, giving its monetary value suffices. The *rabbanan* disagree, holding that the cloak itself must be returned.

### Waiving vs. Hana'ah

The *gemara* then compared this to a separate question: when a condition stipulates that the wife give 200 *zuz*, may the husband simply waive the *tnai* outright — *achulei machilna lach* — or alternatively, may he not waive it formally but instead declare that his *hana'ah* has been satisfied, treating the condition as though fulfilled? A parallel case from *nedarim* was introduced in which the latter approach does work. In the context of *gittin*, however, the *gemara* suggests a distinction may be warranted.

### Nedarim Parallel

The critical variable is what the husband had in mind when he formulated the *tnai*. If the 200 *zuz* were meant as an expression of his *hana'ah* — his personal benefit — then declaring that benefit received might well suffice, parallel to the *nedarim* case. But if the point of the *tnai* was that she must suffer, that she must endure the burden of satisfying his demand, then no declaration of *hana'ah* can substitute for that. The other person still has not suffered. This raises a deeper question about how we interpret any given *tnai*: what was the underlying purpose the party had in mind when formulating it?

## Sharecropper Case

### Rain & Irrigation

The *gemara* now presents the case of *hahu gavra de-amar lei le-ariseh* — a man who said to his sharecropper: "All other sharecroppers irrigate the field three times and receive a quarter of the produce. You irrigate it four times, and I will give you a third." The sharecropper agreed. At the end of the season, however, rain fell and rendered the extra irrigation unnecessary — indeed, counter-productive. Rav Yosef rules: *ha lo dala* — he did not irrigate as agreed, so he is not entitled to the extra share. Rava disagrees: *ha lo itztrecha* — it was not required. The purpose of the extra irrigation was achieved by the rain; the *tnai* is accordingly satisfied.

### Money vs. Cloak

The *gemara* proposes that this dispute maps onto the earlier one: perhaps Rav Yosef follows the *rabbanan* — a *tnai* must be fulfilled precisely as stated — while Rava follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who permits equivalents. Just as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says money may substitute for the cloak, so Rava holds that rain substitutes for irrigation. The *gemara* immediately rejects this alignment, noting that we generally rule like Rava, and yet we do *not* rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding the cloak. The two cases must therefore rest on different logic.

### Gittin Specificity

The *gemara* resolves the tension as follows: even according to the *rabbanan*, who insist that the cloak itself must be returned, Rava's ruling regarding the irrigation remains valid — because the two situations are fundamentally different. In the *get* context, the *gemara* says, *delitzira kamichavein* — the husband's intent in imposing the *tnai* is to make demands of her, to impose hardship. Whether the condition involves money or a garment or anything else, we presuppose he wants no latitude given, no substitutes accepted. His *da'at* is to demand precisely what he stated.

By contrast, the sharecropper arrangement is a business transaction — *mishum arrucha*, it is all about profit — and the *tnai* is naturally read in terms of its economic purpose. If the rain accomplished exactly what the extra irrigation was meant to accomplish, then *ha lo itztrecha* — it simply was not required, and the *tnai* is satisfied. The irrigation was never the point; it was a means to an end. In a *get*, however, every *tnai* is presumed to be *davka*, precisely as stated, because we assume the husband is not in the business of granting the wife any latitude whatsoever.

## Hillel Takkana

### Hiding Practice

The *gemara* now introduces a *Mishna* from *Arachin* about a related phenomenon — the buyback right in a walled city during the period when *yovel* was operative. A seller retained the right to repurchase his house within a year of the sale. If a year passed and the right was not exercised, the sale became permanent. The practice that developed was that the buyer would hide on the last day of the year — *hayah nitman yom shenem asar chodesh kedei sheyehe chalut lo* — so that the seller could not find him, deposit the money, and thereby exercise the buyback.

### Deposit Institution

Hillel instituted a *takkana* addressing this abuse: *sheyehe cholesh ma'otav lalishkah* — the seller could deposit the money in a chamber (apparently at the *Beis HaMikdash* or a designated *lishkah*), then break down the buyer's door and reclaim the house. The buyer, wherever he might be hiding, was informed that the money had been deposited and was available for collection whenever he wished.

### Rava's Inference

Rava draws a *halachic* inference from Hillel's *takkana*: if a husband writes *harei zeh gittech al menas shetitni li masayim zuz* — "here is your *get* on the condition you give me 200 *zuz*" — and she gives him the money with his willing acceptance, she is divorced. But what if he refuses to accept it — *al korcho* — and she throws the money at him, or places it in his home? Rava says she is not divorced. The parallel to Hillel: the fact that Hillel needed to institute a *takkana* for the buyer-in-hiding implies that without it, depositing money against someone's will would not have constituted valid payment. *M'klal d'be'alma nethina ba'al korcho lo havya nethina* — ordinarily, giving something against a person's will does not count as giving. Rashi adds that Hillel's *takkana*, whatever its scope, cannot apply to *gittin*, because the personal status of a woman as married or divorced cannot be altered by rabbinic enactment the same way monetary matters can.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

Rav Pappa (or, in an alternative formulation, Rav Shimi bar Ashi) challenges Rava's inference. The premise is that Hillel's *takkana* was necessitated by *nesinat ba'al korcho* — the unwillingness of the buyer to accept payment. But perhaps, Rav Pappa suggests, Hillel's *takkana* was required solely because the buyer was not physically present — *shelo befanav*. Perhaps, had the buyer been standing there, payment against his will would have been fully effective even without any *takkana*. The buyer ran away precisely to avoid the embarrassment of refusing face-to-face, not because refusal would have been legally effective. If so, Hillel addressed only the problem of absence, not of unwillingness.

Tosafot explains that the buyer's motive for hiding was indeed to avoid the social humiliation of standing there and overtly refusing to accept money when the seller had a *Torah*-granted right to tender it. The buyer was not hiding because the law empowered him to refuse; he was hiding because the law did not clearly protect him if he refused in person.

### Ambiguity Remains

The *gemara* then presents an alternative version of the entire exchange — *v'ika d'amri* — in which Rava himself is the one who began by saying that from Hillel's *takkana* one proves that *nesinat ba'al korcho havya nethina*: giving against someone's will does work, and Hillel's *takkana* addressed only the problem of physical absence. Rav Pappa then challenges: perhaps even had the buyer been present and refused, it would not have worked, and Hillel's *takkana* covered both scenarios — presence-and-refusal as well as absence. The *gemara* leaves the question unresolved. We do not know definitively whether *nesinat ba'al korcho* — tendering against someone's will — constitutes valid *nesinat* in the context of a *get* condition. One might argue on principle that in a *Torah*-granted right, such as the seller's right to redeem his house, the right exists regardless of the buyer's preferences, whereas when a husband creates a *tnai* of his own accord, his implicit intent may be that he must willingly accept the money. But the *gemara* itself does not resolve the question on those grounds.

## Paper & Get

### Rabban Shimon Rule

Rabbah bar bar Chana cites Rabbi Yochanan: *kol makom sheshana Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel bemishnasenu halacha kemoso* — wherever Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears in *our Mishna* (i.e., the *Mishna* of the tractate under study), we rule in accordance with his view — *chutz* from three exceptions: *arev* (a *Mishna* about guarantors), *Tzidan* (our very case, the cloak *Mishna*), and *raya achrona* (a second debate in *Sanhedrin* concerning the deadline for producing evidence). So we do *not* rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the cloak case of our *Mishna*.

### Paper Condition

A *baraisa* is then cited: *harei ze gittech vehaniyar sheli aina megureshes* — "here is your *get*, but the paper is mine," she is not divorced, because she has not received any physical object. She has not taken possession of a *get*. However, *al menas shetachziri li es haniyar megureshes* — "on the condition you return the paper to me," she is divorced. The *gemara* asks the obvious question: *mai shna reisha u'mai shna seifa* — what distinguishes the first clause from the second? In the first clause, he keeps the paper outright; in the second, he gives it to her but conditions the divorce on her returning it. If *al menas* does not mean *me'achshav* — from now — but only takes effect when the condition is fulfilled, then in the second clause, at the moment she returns the paper she no longer holds the *get*, and yet it is valid. How?

### Rav Chisda Explains

Rav Chisda offers an explanation: this *baraisa* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, *de'amar titee lo et dameha* — who holds that the monetary value of the cloak can substitute for the cloak itself. By analogy, even without physically returning the *get* parchment, the wife could appease the husband with the monetary value of the paper, and the *tnai* is thereby satisfied. Rav Chisda uses the word *mifayes* — appease — which is notable, as it reintroduces the earlier question of whether payment against one's will suffices, or whether agreement is required.

### Abaye Challenge

Abaye challenges this explanation sharply: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel only allowed a substitute when the original object was unavailable — *heicha de'leisa be'einei*, where the cloak no longer exists. In such a case, money is a reasonable stand-in. But here, *heicha de'isa be'einei* — the paper is still extant. Would Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel really say money substitutes for an object that is right there in hand? The challenge stands, and the *gemara* turns to a series of explanations grounded not in the equivalence of substitutes but in the formal rules of *tna'im* — why the *tnai* itself is invalid and therefore does not bind her.

## Formal Rules

### Doubling Requirement

Abaye himself offers a new resolution: *Rabbi Meir hi de'amar beinan tnai kaful* — the *baraisa* follows Rabbi Meir, who requires that any *tnai* be stated in doubled form — *v'hacha lo kafla l'tna'ei* — and here the husband did not double the condition. Rabbi Meir's position derives from the model of *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven*: when Moshe laid out their condition for receiving a portion in *Ever HaYarden*, the *Torah* stated both: if they cross the Jordan, they receive their portion in Canaan, and if they do not cross, they receive their portion there in *Ever HaYarden*. Both the positive and negative outcomes were spelled out explicitly. As Dov notes, in propositional logic the statement "if P then Q" does not entail "if not-P then not-Q," and Rabbi Meir requires that both possibilities be articulated. Since that doubling was not performed here, the *tnai* is invalid — and an invalid *tnai* means the *get* takes effect unconditionally.

This is a genuinely puzzling moment in the *gemara*. The *baraisa* is structured to contrast two cases — the husband who keeps the paper outright versus the husband who conditions return of the paper — and the implicit contrast suggests they have different *halachic* outcomes. Yet Abaye's resolution essentially collapses that contrast: the reason the second case works is not because the *tnai* was satisfied but because the *tnai* was never binding to begin with. The *chiddush* of the *baraisa* turns out to be a separate and entirely independent *halacha* about the invalidity of an improperly formulated *tnai* — which is a rather striking way to structure a *baraisa*.

### Rava Objection

Rava challenges Abaye directly: *ta'ama d'lo kaflia l'tna'ei* — you say the problem is that he did not double the *tnai*. But *ha kaflia l'tna'ei lo havi gitta* — had he doubled it, the *tnai* would have been binding, and the *get* would only take effect upon return of the paper. When she returns the paper she no longer holds it, and yet you want the *get* to work? Properly doubled, the *tnai* creates an insurmountable problem. So the doubling issue alone does not explain the *baraisa*'s ruling. Rava therefore turns to a different formal deficiency.

### Tnai Precedes Act

*Kol tnai me-heicha gamrinan lehu* — from where do we derive all the rules governing *tna'im*? From the *tnai* of *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven*. *Mah hatam tnai kodem l'maaseh af kol tnai kodem l'maaseh* — there, the *tnai* preceded the *maaseh*: the verse first states "if they cross and you shall give them their portion," and only then the act. So too in every *tnai*, the condition must be formulated before the act. Here, however, the husband said *harei zeh gittech* — "here is your *get*" — and only afterward added *al menas shetachziri li es haniyar* — "on the condition you return the paper." The *maaseh* preceded the *tnai*. This renders the *tnai* invalid on formal grounds independent of the doubling requirement.

Rashi understands *tnai kodem l'maaseh* as a purely formalistic requirement about the order of words — the condition must be stated first. Some *Rishonim* understand it more substantively, but Rashi's reading is supported by the *gemara*'s treatment of the *shachiv meira* case below, where the requirement clearly concerns verbal formulation.

### Different Domains

Rav Adda bar Ahava presses further: even if we accept that *tnai kodem l'maaseh* explains the invalidity, there is yet another formal deficiency. *Mah hatam tnai bidavar echad u'maaseh bidavar acher* — in the *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven* case, the *tnai* pertained to one domain (crossing the Jordan to fight) and the *maaseh* pertained to a different domain (receiving a portion in *Ever HaYarden*). This separateness is required: *af kol tnai bidavar echad u'maaseh bidavar acher*. In our case, however, *hatnai ve'hamaaseh bidavar echad* — the *tnai* and the *maaseh* concern the same thing, namely the *get* and its paper.

Rashi understands this as a prohibition on making a *tnai* about the same object as the *maaseh* — one simply cannot condition the *get* on something related to the *get* itself. Tosafot refines this: the problem is not mere topical overlap but logical self-contradiction. A *tnai* whose fulfillment would undermine the very *maaseh* it is meant to condition cannot be a valid *tnai*, since the entire purpose of a *tnai* is to enable the *maaseh*. A condition that, if satisfied, destroys what it was meant to activate defeats its own purpose and is therefore not recognized as a *tnai* at all.

The *gemara* thus accumulates multiple independent grounds — lack of doubling, *maaseh* preceding *tnai*, and *tnai* and *maaseh* in the same domain — each of which independently invalidates the *tnai* in the *baraisa*. This is also an opportunity for the *gemara* to rehearse the full body of *mishpatei ha-tna'im*, the formal rules governing conditions, which derive from the paradigmatic case of *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven*.

## Tosafot Categories

### Tnai Kaful

Tosafot on *daf* 75a takes up the question of how we rule on these formal requirements. Tosafot concludes that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Meir and requires *tnai kaful* — the doubled condition — since Shmuel and Rava in the immediately following *sugya* both operate according to Rabbi Meir's position. As for all the *mishnas* in the tractate that deploy *al menas* — the condition that she give 200 *zuz*, that she nurse the child, and so on — Tosafot holds that those cases all involve a *tnai kaful*, even though the *Mishna* does not spell it out explicitly: *af al gav d'lo peireish*. The *gemara* later explains that the *tnai* is understood as doubled even when not stated in full. And this applies throughout *Shas*: wherever *al menas* appears and is assumed to be effective — for example, the *esrog* given *al menas l'hachazir* — we must assume the *tnai* was in fact doubled.

### Three Categories

Yet Tosafot acknowledges that this framework requires careful differentiation. At the end of that *Tosafot*, Rabbeinu Yitzchak articulates three distinct categories: *gimel inyanim yesh*. First, there are situations that require a fully formal *tnai* — *tnai gamur* — with all the formal rules including *tnai kaful* as per Rabbi Meir. Second, there are situations requiring only *giluy da'at* — disclosure of intent — without all the formal machinery of a *tnai*. Third, there are situations where not even *giluy da'at* is required, because the intent is assumed from context.

### Intent & Demands

This typology resolves what would otherwise be a bewildering proliferation of difficulty: if every *al menas* requires *tnai kaful* and *tnai kodem l'maaseh* and *tnai bidavar acher*, how have so many *mishnas* and *beraisos* deployed *al menas* without apparent concern for these rules? Tosafot's framework answers: the formal rules apply only when the *tnai* is genuinely external to the core *da'at* of the transaction. When a person truly desires to do something — marry, divorce, buy, sell — but is in a position to attach external demands, those demands are peripheral to his underlying intent. Because they are peripheral and could theoretically be dispensed with, the *Torah* requires that they be formulated with rigorous precision, following all the rules of *mishpatei ha-tna'im*, in order to override what would otherwise be an unconditional *maaseh*.

By contrast, when something is so central to a person's *da'at* that he would not have agreed to the transaction without it, this is not properly a *tnai* at all — it is a fundamental premise of his intent. Such a premise does not require the formal machinery of *tna'im*. It must, however, be articulated; otherwise it falls under *d'varim shebilev einam d'varim* — unexpressed inner thoughts have no legal standing.

The third category is *d'varim shebilev kol adam* — matters so universally understood that no articulation is needed. The classic *gemara* example is someone who sold all his possessions *adaita lemeisak l'ara d'Yisrael* — with the intention of making *aliyah* — and was subsequently prevented from going. The transactions he undertook in preparation are treated as void, even though he never explicitly conditioned each sale on his *aliyah* plans going through. No such articulation was necessary because every bystander understood perfectly well the assumption on which the sales were made.

Tosafot's framework thus creates an organic relationship between legal methodology and the reading of the *gemara*'s *pshat*. Beginning from the conclusion that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Meir on *tnai kaful*, Tosafot is impelled to read all the *mishnas* as implicitly involving doubled conditions, and then to construct a typology of which cases require those formal conditions at all. The legal methodology and the *pshat* reading of the *gemara* are not separable enterprises.

## Shachiv Meira

### Avoiding Evil Eye

Shmuel instituted a *takkana* for the *get* of a *shachiv meira* — a person on his deathbed who wishes to give a *get* contingent on his death. The text Shmuel prescribed is: *im lo meisi lo yehei get v'im meisi yehei get* — "if I do not die it shall not be a *get*, and if I do die it shall be a *get*." The reason for beginning with the negative — "if I do not die" — is *lo makdmi inish b'puranusa*: a person does not open by invoking a bad outcome for himself. Beginning with "if I die" has the character of *ayin hara*.

### Yes Before No

The *gemara* challenges: why not simply say *lo yehei get im lo meisi* — "it shall not be a *get* if I do not die" — and avoid the need to open with death at all? The answer is *be'ina tnai kodem l'maaseh* — the *tnai* must precede the *maaseh* in formulation. One cannot begin with the *maaseh* ("it shall not be a *get*") and then attach the *tnai* afterward.

Rava then challenges from a different angle: Shmuel's formulation opens with *im lo meisi* — "if I do not die" — which is the negative case. But the formal requirement derived from *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven* is *hen kodem l'lav* — the affirmative must precede the negative. The "yes" — the case where the *tnai* is fulfilled — must come first. Shmuel's formulation violates this by leading with the negative.

The resolution: Shmuel's *takkana* addresses both requirements simultaneously in a way that avoids *ayin hara*. One opens with a statement that is technically the negative (*im lo meisi lo yehei get*), which is not the troubling part because it posits the hopeful outcome. Then one proceeds *im meisi yehei get v'im lo meisi lo yehei get* — stating the affirmative before the negative in the doubled portion, thereby satisfying *hen kodem l'lav*. The opening preliminary statement avoids the need to lead with "if I die" while the doubled formulation that follows preserves the formal requirement of affirmative before negative. This *sugya* makes clear that *tnai kodem l'maaseh* is indeed a matter of verbal formulation and word order.

## Nursing Condition

### Implicit Duration

The *Mishna* now turns to conditions regarding service and nursing: *harei zeh gittech al menas she-teshamshi es abba* — "here is your *get* on the condition you attend to my father," or *al menas she-teniki es beni* — "on the condition you nurse my son." These conditions are binding. The *Mishna* asks: *kamah hi menikaso* — for how long must she nurse? The answer is *shtei shanim* — two years, the conventional nursing duration. Rabbi Yehuda says eighteen months.

### Death Exemption

The *Mishna* then states that if the child or the father dies — *meis haben o she-meis ha-av* — the *get* remains valid. Rashi understands this to mean that she performed at least one act of nursing or one act of attending to the father, and once the subject of the condition is gone, the *tnai* is considered satisfied for its duration. Tosafot rejects this reading as too obvious to require a *Mishna*. Tosafot holds that the *chiddush* is precisely that she did nothing — the father or child died before she had an opportunity to attend to them at all — and yet the *get* is valid. This parallels the rain-and-irrigation case discussed earlier: when the purpose of the *tnai* has been rendered moot by circumstances, the *tnai* is treated as satisfied. That the husband said "nurse my child" and she never nursed him, yet the *get* holds because there was no longer a child to be nursed — this is a substantial *chiddush*.

### Explicit Duration

The *Mishna* then distinguishes: *harei zeh gittech al menas she-teshamshi es abba shtei shanim* — if he specified a duration of two years — then even if the father dies or the child dies before the time has elapsed, *eino get* — it is not a valid *get*. When a specific duration is stated, that duration is required to be served. The condition is no longer implicitly "as needed"; it is a fixed term. Similarly, if the father says *ee efshar she-teshamisheni* — "I do not wish her to attend to me" — without any fault on her part (*she-lo b'kapdah*), the *get* is still invalid, because the explicit duration was not satisfied.

### Rabban Shimon Rule

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents: *kol akavah she-einah heimena harei zeh get* — any failure to fulfill the condition that is not her fault renders the *get* valid. The principle is framed around *ones* — external compulsion. If the father refuses her attendance without cause, or if circumstances beyond her control prevent performance, it is as though the condition has been met. This may be understood not merely as a liberal reading of an implicit "as needed" provision, but as an application of *yesh ones b'gittin* — the doctrine that circumstances of compulsion count as fulfillment. Just as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was more liberal in the cloak case in accepting substitutes, he is more liberal here in accepting non-performance due to circumstances beyond her control.

## Liberal vs. Strict

### One Day Question

A *baraisa* is cited that appears to contradict the *Mishna*: *henikato yom echad shimshato yom echad harei zeh get* — if she nursed the child for one day or attended to the father for one day, the *get* is valid. If no duration was specified, a single day of fulfillment satisfies the *tnai*. But the *Mishna* said that nursing, even without a stated duration, runs until the child is two years old. The contradiction is direct.

### Lenient vs. Strict

Rav Chisda resolves it: *lo kashya ha rabbanan v'ha Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the *Mishna* and the *baraisa* reflect different opinions. The *Mishna*, whose opening section does not belong to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel (since he is only introduced at the *Mishna*'s end), follows the *rabbanan*. The *baraisa* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who interprets the satisfaction of *tna'im* more liberally. Since Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was prepared to accept money in place of a cloak, he is prepared here to accept one day of nursing as satisfying a condition that mentioned no specific duration. The *rabbanan*, who demanded the cloak itself, demand the full implied duration.

### Interpretation Method

The *gemara* refines this further. The issue is not precisely one of leniency versus stringency, but of interpretive approach: do we read a *tnai* in a hyper-literal, maximally precise manner, or do we read it in terms of the evident purpose? The *rabbanan*, who read *tnai* language precisely — cloak means cloak, no substitute — also read "nurse" to mean "nurse for the full conventional duration" even when that duration is unstated. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who accepts functional equivalents, also reads the unstated duration liberally — one day of nursing fulfills the underlying intent of the condition. The *gemara* closes by noting that this framing in terms of hyper-literal versus liberal interpretation is more precise than simply mapping the positions onto leniency and stringency, since those categories shift depending on the case.
