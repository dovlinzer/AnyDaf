# Gittin 75 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tna'im Overview

### Cloak & Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּצַיְדָּן כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said:** There was **an incident in** the city of **Tzaidan** involving one who said to his wife: This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me my coat, and she lost his coat. And the Rabbis said that she should give him the value of that coat, and by doing so she fulfills the condition and is divorced.

The *daf* opens by continuing a rich *gemara* about what constitutes satisfaction of *tna'im* — conditions — in *gittin*. The *gemara* has been reading the *Mishna* as presenting the following case: a husband says "give me my cloak" as the condition of a *get*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that if the cloak is lost, giving its monetary value suffices. The *rabbanan* disagree, holding that the cloak itself must be returned.

### Waiving vs. Hana'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אַסִּי מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר לָהּ ״מְחוּלִים לָךְ״, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If a man says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars, and** afterward **he returned and said to her: It is waived for you,** then **what is** the *halakha*?

The *gemara* then compared this to a separate question: when a condition stipulates that the wife give 200 *zuz*, may the husband simply waive the *tnai* outright — *achulei machilna lach* — or alternatively, may he not waive it formally but instead declare that his *hana'ah* has been satisfied, treating the condition as though fulfilled? A parallel case from *nedarim* was introduced in which the latter approach does work. In the context of *gittin*, however, the *gemara* suggests a distinction may be warranted.

### Nedarim Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם לְצַעוֹרַהּ קָא מְכַוֵּין – וְלָא צַיעֲרַהּ. הָכָא מִשּׁוּם הַרְוָוחָה הוּא, וְהָא לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of a bill of divorce, **he intends to vex her, and** until she pays him **he did not vex her.** If he forgives this condition, his initial intention has not been fulfilled. **Here,** in the case of the vow, the reason the father took the vow is **for the sake of profit;** he wants his son to receive a valuable gift, **but** subsequently the one who took the vow decides that **it was not necessary.** Therefore, he can waive his claim.

The critical variable is what the husband had in mind when he formulated the *tnai*. If the 200 *zuz* were meant as an expression of his *hana'ah* — his personal benefit — then declaring that benefit received might well suffice, parallel to the *nedarim* case. But if the point of the *tnai* was that she must suffer, that she must endure the burden of satisfying his demand, then no declaration of *hana'ah* can substitute for that. The other person still has not suffered. This raises a deeper question about how we interpret any given *tnai*: what was the underlying purpose the party had in mind when formulating it?

## Sharecropper Case

### Rain & Irrigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲרִיסֵיהּ: כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא דְּלוּ תְּלָת דָּלָווֹתָא, וְאָכְלִי רִיבְעָא; אַתְּ דְּלִי אַרְבְּעָה, וֶאֱכוֹל תִּילְתָּא. לְסוֹף אֲתָא מִיטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates an incident of **a certain man who said to his sharecropper: Everyone waters** the field **three** times during the season, **and they consume,** i.e., receive as payment, **one quarter** of the crops from the field. Will **you water four** times **and consume** as your payment **one-third** of the produce? **Ultimately, rain came** when the sharecropper would have needed to water a fourth time, and so he did not need to water the field a fourth time.

The *gemara* now presents the case of *hahu gavra de-amar lei le-ariseh* — a man who said to his sharecropper: "All other sharecroppers irrigate the field three times and receive a quarter of the produce. You irrigate it four times, and I will give you a third." The sharecropper agreed. At the end of the season, however, rain fell and rendered the extra irrigation unnecessary — indeed, counter-productive. Rav Yosef rules: *ha lo dala* — he did not irrigate as agreed, so he is not entitled to the extra share. Rava disagrees: *ha lo itztrecha* — it was not required. The purpose of the extra irrigation was achieved by the rain; the *tnai* is accordingly satisfied.

### Money vs. Cloak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָא לָא דְּלָה. רַבָּה אָמַר: הָא לָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: But he did not water** the field a fourth time, so the condition was not fulfilled. Consequently, he deserves the same as the other sharecroppers, and receives only one quarter of the produce. **Rabba said: But it was not necessary** to water the field the fourth time because the rain fell to the benefit of the sharecropper, and therefore he should receive one-third of the produce.

The *gemara* proposes that this dispute maps onto the earlier one: perhaps Rav Yosef follows the *rabbanan* — a *tnai* must be fulfilled precisely as stated — while Rava follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who permits equivalents. Just as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says money may substitute for the cloak, so Rava holds that rain substitutes for irrigation. The *gemara* immediately rejects this alignment, noting that we generally rule like Rava, and yet we do *not* rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding the cloak. The two cases must therefore rest on different logic.

### Gittin Specificity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם כְּרַבָּנַן. רַב יוֹסֵף – כְּרַבָּנַן; וְרַבָּה אָמַר לָךְ: אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי אֲפִילּוּ לְרַבָּנַן, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם – אֶלָּא דִּלְצַעוֹרַהּ קָא מִיכַּוֵּין; אֲבָל הָכָא, מִשּׁוּם הַרְוָוחָה הוּא, וְהָא לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** according to both opinions, this dispute is **actually in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis. Rav Yosef** clearly rules **in accordance with the Rabbis. And Rabba** could have **said to you: I was saying** my statement **even according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** as **the Rabbis are saying there** that she is not divorced if she does not return the coat itself **only when** the husband **intends to vex her,** and if she does not need to give the coat itself she will not be vexed, as it is not difficult for her to give him its value. But **here,** in the case of watering the field, the reason the owner is making the condition **is** only **for the sake of** increasing his **profit, and it was not necessary** to actually water the field.

The *gemara* resolves the tension as follows: even according to the *rabbanan*, who insist that the cloak itself must be returned, Rava's ruling regarding the irrigation remains valid — because the two situations are fundamentally different. In the *get* context, the *gemara* says, *delitzira kamichavein* — the husband's intent in imposing the *tnai* is to make demands of her, to impose hardship. Whether the condition involves money or a garment or anything else, we presuppose he wants no latitude given, no substitutes accepted. His *da'at* is to demand precisely what he stated.

By contrast, the sharecropper arrangement is a business transaction — *mishum arrucha*, it is all about profit — and the *tnai* is naturally read in terms of its economic purpose. If the rain accomplished exactly what the extra irrigation was meant to accomplish, then *ha lo itztrecha* — it simply was not required, and the *tnai* is satisfied. The irrigation was never the point; it was a means to an end. In a *get*, however, every *tnai* is presumed to be *davka*, precisely as stated, because we assume the husband is not in the business of granting the wife any latitude whatsoever.

## Hillel Takkana

### Hiding Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיָה נִטְמָן יוֹם שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא חָלוּט לוֹ. הִתְקִין הִלֵּל הַזָּקֵן שֶׁיְּהֵא חוֹלֵשׁ אֶת מְעוֹתָיו לַלִּשְׁכָּה, וִיהֵא שׁוֹבֵר אֶת הַדֶּלֶת וְנִכְנָס. וְאֵימָתַי שֶׁיִּרְצֶה הַלָּה, יָבוֹא וְיִטּוֹל אֶת מְעוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the redemption of houses in walled cities, **we learned elsewhere** in a mishna (*Arakhin* 31b) that if a house was sold and not redeemed by its owners within twelve months it remains permanently in the possession of the purchaser. The Gemara describes this: **At first** the purchaser **would hide** for all of the final **day of the twelfth month so that** the house would be **confirmed** as **his** (see Leviticus 25:29–30). Because the purchaser was in hiding, the seller would be unable to redeem the house from him. **Hillel the Elder instituted** an ordinance **whereby** the seller **may deposit [*ḥolesh*] his** redemption **money in** the Temple treasury **chamber, and** then **he may break the door** of his house **and enter** and take possession. **And when that** purchaser **wishes, he should come and take his money** from the Temple treasury.

The *gemara* now introduces a *Mishna* from *Arachin* about a related phenomenon — the buyback right in a walled city during the period when *yovel* was operative. A seller retained the right to repurchase his house within a year of the sale. If a year passed and the right was not exercised, the sale became permanent. The practice that developed was that the buyer would hide on the last day of the year — *hayah nitman yom shenem asar chodesh kedei sheyehe chalut lo* — so that the seller could not find him, deposit the money, and thereby exercise the buyback.

### Rava's Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: מִתַּקָּנָתוֹ שֶׁל הִלֵּל נִשְׁמַע – ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״, וְנָתְנָה לוֹ מִדַּעְתּוֹ, מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת; עַל כּוֹרְחוֹ, אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת – מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ לְהִלֵּל לְתַקּוֹנֵי נְתִינָה בְּעַל כּוּרְחֵיהּ – דְּהָוְיָא לַהּ נְתִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: From the ordinance of Hillel we learn** that if one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars, and she gave him** the money **with his consent,** then she is **divorced.** If she gave it to him **against his will** then **she is not divorced. How does Rava prove this? From** the fact **that** it **was necessary for Hillel to institute** a unique ordinance in the case of houses in walled cities whereby **giving against the will** of the receiver **is** considered **giving.**

Rava draws a *halachic* inference from Hillel's *takkana*: if a husband writes *harei zeh gittech al menas shetitni li masayim zuz* — "here is your *get* on the condition you give me 200 *zuz*" — and she gives him the money with his willing acceptance, she is divorced. But what if he refuses to accept it — *al korcho* — and she throws the money at him, or places it in his home? Rava says she is not divorced. The parallel to Hillel: the fact that Hillel needed to institute a *takkana* for the buyer-in-hiding implies that without it, depositing money against someone's will would not have constituted valid payment. *M'klal d'be'alma nethina ba'al korcho lo havya nethina* — ordinarily, giving something against a person's will does not count as giving. Rashi adds that Hillel's *takkana*, whatever its scope, cannot apply to *gittin*, because the personal status of a woman as married or divorced cannot be altered by rabbinic enactment the same way monetary matters can.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דִּבְעָלְמָא, נְתִינָה בְּעַל כֻּרְחֵיהּ לָא הָוְיָא נְתִינָה. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: וְדִלְמָא כִּי אִצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ לְתַקּוֹנֵי – שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, אֲבָל בְּפָנָיו – בֵּין מִדַּעְתּוֹ בֵּין בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ הָוְיָא נְתִינָה!
>
> **Translation:** This proves **by inference that generally, giving against** the recipient's **will is not** considered valid **giving,** as if it were, then Hillel would not have needed to institute this ordinance. **Rav Pappa objects to this, and some say** it was **Rav Shimi bar Ashi** who raises this objection: **And perhaps when it was necessary** for Hillel **to institute** an ordinance allowing the seller to repay the money against the will of the purchaser it was specifically in a case **where** he gives the money **not in** the **presence** of the purchaser. **But** when he repays him **in his presence, whether** the recipient was **willing** or **whether** it was **against his will, it is** considered valid **giving.** Accordingly, one cannot apply Hillel's ordinance to the case of conditional bills of divorce.

Rav Pappa (or, in an alternative formulation, Rav Shimi bar Ashi) challenges Rava's inference. The premise is that Hillel's *takkana* was necessitated by *nesinat ba'al korcho* — the unwillingness of the buyer to accept payment. But perhaps, Rav Pappa suggests, Hillel's *takkana* was required solely because the buyer was not physically present — *shelo befanav*. Perhaps, had the buyer been standing there, payment against his will would have been fully effective even without any *takkana*. The buyer ran away precisely to avoid the embarrassment of refusing face-to-face, not because refusal would have been legally effective. If so, Hillel addressed only the problem of absence, not of unwillingness.

Tosafot explains that the buyer's motive for hiding was indeed to avoid the social humiliation of standing there and overtly refusing to accept money when the seller had a *Torah*-granted right to tender it. The buyer was not hiding because the law empowered him to refuse; he was hiding because the law did not clearly protect him if he refused in person.

### Ambiguity Remains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רָבָא: מִתַּקָּנָתוֹ שֶׁל הִלֵּל – ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטֵּךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״, וּנְתָנָהּ לוֹ – בֵּין מִדַּעְתּוֹ וּבֵּין בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ הָוְיָא נְתִינָה; וְכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ לְהִלֵּל לְתַקּוֹנֵי – שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, אֲבָל בְּפָנָיו – בֵּין מִדַּעְתּוֹ בֵּין בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ הָוְיָא נְתִינָה. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: וְדִלְמָא אֲפִילּוּ בְּפָנָיו נָמֵי – מִדַּעְתּוֹ אִין, עַל כׇּרְחוֹ לָא; וְהִלֵּל – מַאי דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ תַּקֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** a different version of this discussion. **Rava says: From the ordinance of Hillel** it can be inferred that if one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars, and she gave** it **to him, whether** it was **with his consent or whether** it was **against his will, it is** valid **giving. And** the case **where it was necessary for Hillel to institute** his ordinance was **when** the giving of the money **was not in his presence. But** if the repayment was **in his presence, whether** it was **with his consent** or **whether** it was **against his will, it is** considered valid **giving. Rav Pappa objects to this, and some say** it was **Rav Shimi bar Ashi** who raises this objection: **And perhaps even** in a case where she gives him the money **in his presence,** if she gives it **with his consent, yes,** it is valid. If she gives it **against his will, no,** it is not considered to be valid giving. **And Hillel** specifically **instituted what was necessary,** because in the case of a house in a walled city the purchaser would hide, and therefore that ordinance was necessary there.

The *gemara* then presents an alternative version of the entire exchange — *v'ika d'amri* — in which Rava himself is the one who began by saying that from Hillel's *takkana* one proves that *nesinat ba'al korcho havya nethina*: giving against someone's will does work, and Hillel's *takkana* addressed only the problem of physical absence. Rav Pappa then challenges: perhaps even had the buyer been present and refused, it would not have worked, and Hillel's *takkana* covered both scenarios — presence-and-refusal as well as absence. The *gemara* leaves the question unresolved. We do not know definitively whether *nesinat ba'al korcho* — tendering against someone's will — constitutes valid *nesinat* in the context of a *get* condition. One might argue on principle that in a *Torah*-granted right, such as the seller's right to redeem his house, the right exists regardless of the buyer's preferences, whereas when a husband creates a *tnai* of his own accord, his implicit intent may be that he must willingly accept the money. But the *gemara* itself does not resolve the question on those grounds.

## Paper & Get

### Rabban Shimon Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ – הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ; חוּץ מֵעָרֵב, וְצַיְדָן, וּרְאָיָה אַחֲרוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbah bar bar Ḥana says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In **every place where Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel taught** a *halakha* **in our** body of **Mishna, the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** his opinion, even though it is cited as an individual opinion, **except** for three cases. With regard to the *halakha* of **a guarantor** (*Bava Batra* 173b), if the creditor stipulated that he can collect his debt from either the debtor or the guarantor, according to the Rabbis he can collect from the guarantor's property even if the debtor has available property. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that the creditor can collect the debt only from the debtor. **And** likewise the *halakha* is not like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel with regard to the case mentioned here concerning the incident that occurred in **Tzaidan. And** similarly, the *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel concerning the dispute with regard to **evidence** in the **final** disagreement (*Sanhedrin* 31a), where the Rabbis hold that if one claims that he has no evidence or witnesses, but subsequently brings evidence to court, the judges do not accept it. According to the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel they can accept it.

Rabbah bar bar Chana cites Rabbi Yochanan: *kol makom sheshana Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel bemishnasenu halacha kemoso* — wherever Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears in *our Mishna* (i.e., the *Mishna* of the tractate under study), we rule in accordance with his view — *chutz* from three exceptions: *arev* (a *Mishna* about guarantors), *Tzidan* (our very case, the cloak *Mishna*), and *raya achrona* (a second debate in *Sanhedrin* concerning the deadline for producing evidence). So we do *not* rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in the cloak case of our *Mishna*.

### Paper Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ, וְהַנְּיָיר שֶׁלִּי״ – אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירִי לִי אֶת הַנְּיָיר״ – מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the husband said to his wife: **Behold this is your bill of divorce,** but **the paper** on which it is written **is** still **mine,** then **she is not divorced,** as he must give her the actual bill of divorce in order for the divorce to take effect. Since the paper still belongs to him, it is as if he had given her only the writing. But if he said to her: Behold this is your bill of divorce **on the condition that you return the paper to me,** then **she is divorced.** The bill of divorce belongs entirely to her, and the returning of the paper is only a stipulation that must be fulfilled later.

A *baraisa* is then cited: *harei ze gittech vehaniyar sheli aina megureshes* — "here is your *get*, but the paper is mine," she is not divorced, because she has not received any physical object. She has not taken possession of a *get*. However, *al menas shetachziri li es haniyar megureshes* — "on the condition you return the paper to me," she is divorced. The *gemara* asks the obvious question: *mai shna reisha u'mai shna seifa* — what distinguishes the first clause from the second? In the first clause, he keeps the paper outright; in the second, he gives it to her but conditions the divorce on her returning it. If *al menas* does not mean *me'achshav* — from now — but only takes effect when the condition is fulfilled, then in the second clause, at the moment she returns the paper she no longer holds the *get*, and yet it is valid. How?

### Rav Chisda Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָא מַנִּי – רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא, דְּאָמַר: תִּתֵּן לוֹ אֶת דָּמֶיהָ; הָכָא נָמֵי – אֶפְשָׁר דִּמְפַיְּיסָה לֵיהּ בִּדְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in **the first clause** of the *baraita* **and what is different** in **the latter clause?** In neither case does she have ownership of the bill of divorce. **Rav Ḥisda said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who says** with regard to his coat that **she** can **give him its value** and does not need to give the item itself in order to fulfill the condition. **Here also,** since **it is possible for her to appease him with money** and she may give him the value of the paper, therefore it is considered as though she received the paper.

Rav Chisda offers an explanation: this *baraisa* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, *de'amar titee lo et dameha* — who holds that the monetary value of the cloak can substitute for the cloak itself. By analogy, even without physically returning the *get* parchment, the wife could appease the husband with the monetary value of the paper, and the *tnai* is thereby satisfied. Rav Chisda uses the word *mifayes* — appease — which is notable, as it reintroduces the earlier question of whether payment against one's will suffices, or whether agreement is required.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֵימוֹר דְּאָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – הֵיכָא דְּלֵיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ, הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ בְּעֵינֵיהּ מִי אָמַר?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to this** answer: **Say that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says** that it is acceptable to give the value instead of the item itself **when** the item **is not extant,** because it is lost. But in a case **where it is extant,** such as the paper in this case, **did** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel actually **say** that giving the value of the item is sufficient?

Abaye challenges this explanation sharply: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel only allowed a substitute when the original object was unavailable — *heicha de'leisa be'einei*, where the cloak no longer exists. In such a case, money is a reasonable stand-in. But here, *heicha de'isa be'einei* — the paper is still extant. Would Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel really say money substitutes for an object that is right there in hand? The challenge stands, and the *gemara* turns to a series of explanations grounded not in the equivalence of substitutes but in the formal rules of *tna'im* — why the *tnai* itself is invalid and therefore does not bind her.

## Formal Rules

### Doubling Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בָּעֵינַן תְּנַאי כָּפוּל; וְהָכָא – הָא לָא כַּפְלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: We require** any stipulated condition to be structured as **a compound condition** describing both outcomes, meaning that the condition must mention what will happen both if the condition is fulfilled and if it is not fulfilled. **And here he did not compound his condition.** The husband said only: This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you return the paper to me. He did not specify that it will not be a valid bill of divorce if she does not return it.

Abaye himself offers a new resolution: *Rabbi Meir hi de'amar beinan tnai kaful* — the *baraisa* follows Rabbi Meir, who requires that any *tnai* be stated in doubled form — *v'hacha lo kafla l'tna'ei* — and here the husband did not double the condition. Rabbi Meir's position derives from the model of *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven*: when Moshe laid out their condition for receiving a portion in *Ever HaYarden*, the *Torah* stated both: if they cross the Jordan, they receive their portion in Canaan, and if they do not cross, they receive their portion there in *Ever HaYarden*. Both the positive and negative outcomes were spelled out explicitly. As Dov notes, in propositional logic the statement "if P then Q" does not entail "if not-P then not-Q," and Rabbi Meir requires that both possibilities be articulated. Since that doubling was not performed here, the *tnai* is invalid — and an invalid *tnai* means the *get* takes effect unconditionally.

This is a genuinely puzzling moment in the *gemara*. The *baraisa* is structured to contrast two cases — the husband who keeps the paper outright versus the husband who conditions return of the paper — and the implicit contrast suggests they have different *halachic* outcomes. Yet Abaye's resolution essentially collapses that contrast: the reason the second case works is not because the *tnai* was satisfied but because the *tnai* was never binding to begin with. The *chiddush* of the *baraisa* turns out to be a separate and entirely independent *halacha* about the invalidity of an improperly formulated *tnai* — which is a rather striking way to structure a *baraisa*.

### Rava Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: טַעְמָא דְּלָא כַּפְלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ, הָא כַּפְלֵיהּ לִתְנָאֵיהּ – לָא הָוֵי גִּיטָּא; מִכְּדֵי כֹּל תְּנָאֵי מֵהֵיכָא גָּמְרִינַן לְהוּ? מִתְּנַאי בְּנֵי גָּד וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן; מָה הָתָם – תְּנַאי קוֹדֵם לְמַעֲשֶׂה, אַף כֹּל תְּנַאי קוֹדֵם לְמַעֲשֶׂה; לְאַפּוֹקֵי הָכָא – דְּמַעֲשֶׂה קוֹדֵם לִתְנַאי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this** explanation: According to Abaye **the reason** the bill of divorce is valid is **that** the husband **did not compound his condition, but if** the husband did **compound his condition** then it **would not be** a valid **bill of divorce. Now, from where do we learn** the *halakhot* of **all conditions?** They are derived **from the condition of the children of Gad and the children of Reuben.** Moses stipulated with them that if they fight the battles with the Jewish people in Eretz Yisrael, they will inherit the land of Gilead in the Transjordan, as they requested; but if they do not fight the battles with the Jewish people in Eretz Yisrael, they will not inherit that land (see Numbers, chapter 32). **Just as there,** in the conditions that Moses made, the language of the **condition precedes the** consequent **action,** for he first stated the condition and afterward he described the result if they fulfill the condition: "And you shall give them the land of Gilead as an inheritance" (Numbers 32:29), **so too, any condition** is valid only when it is stated **before the** resultant **action.** And this serves **to exclude** the case mentioned **here, where the** resultant **action** of the giving of the bill of divorce **precedes the condition.** Consequently, according to Rabbi Meir this bill of divorce would not be valid, even if the husband had compounded the condition.

Rava challenges Abaye directly: *ta'ama d'lo kaflia l'tna'ei* — you say the problem is that he did not double the *tnai*. But *ha kaflia l'tna'ei lo havi gitta* — had he doubled it, the *tnai* would have been binding, and the *get* would only take effect upon return of the paper. When she returns the paper she no longer holds it, and yet you want the *get* to work? Properly doubled, the *tnai* creates an insurmountable problem. So the doubling issue alone does not explain the *baraisa*'s ruling. Rava therefore turns to a different formal deficiency.

### Tnai Precedes Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּמַעֲשֶׂה קוֹדֵם לִתְנַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The condition does not apply and the woman is divorced **because** the **action** of giving the bill of divorce **precedes** the **condition.**

*Kol tnai me-heicha gamrinan lehu* — from where do we derive all the rules governing *tna'im*? From the *tnai* of *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven*. *Mah hatam tnai kodem l'maaseh af kol tnai kodem l'maaseh* — there, the *tnai* preceded the *maaseh*: the verse first states "if they cross and you shall give them their portion," and only then the act. So too in every *tnai*, the condition must be formulated before the act. Here, however, the husband said *harei zeh gittech* — "here is your *get*" — and only afterward added *al menas shetachziri li es haniyar* — "on the condition you return the paper." The *maaseh* preceded the *tnai*. This renders the *tnai* invalid on formal grounds independent of the doubling requirement.

Rashi understands *tnai kodem l'maaseh* as a purely formalistic requirement about the order of words — the condition must be stated first. Some *Rishonim* understand it more substantively, but Rashi's reading is supported by the *gemara*'s treatment of the *shachiv meira* case below, where the requirement clearly concerns verbal formulation.

### Different Domains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: טַעְמָא דְּמַעֲשֶׂה קוֹדֵם לִתְנַאי, הָא תְּנַאי קוֹדֵם לְמַעֲשֶׂה – לָא הָוֵי גִּיטָּא; מִכְּדֵי כֹּל תְּנָאֵי מֵהֵיכָא גָּמְרִינַן לְהוּ – מִתְּנַאי בְּנֵי גָּד וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן, מָה הָתָם – תְּנַאי בְּדָבָר אֶחָד וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּדָבָר אַחֵר, אַף כֹּל; לְאַפּוֹקֵי הָכָא – דִּתְנַאי וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּדָבָר אֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava objects to this** explanation: **The reason** that the bill of divorce is valid is **that the action precedes the condition,** and the condition does not take effect. **But** if **the condition** were to **precede the action** then it would **not be** a valid **bill of divorce. Now, from where do we learn all** the *halakhot* of **conditions?** They are derived **from the condition of the children of Gad and the children of Reuben. Just as there** it is **a condition with regard to one matter,** i.e., that they should fight along with the rest of the Jewish people, **and** a resultant **action with regard to another matter,** i.e., that they would receive the land of Gilead, **so too, every** other condition must follow this pattern. This serves **to exclude** the case mentioned **here,** **where the condition and the action are about the same matter,** the giving of the bill of divorce. Accordingly, the condition would not be valid even if one were to disregard the concern of the action preceding the condition.

Rav Adda bar Ahava presses further: even if we accept that *tnai kodem l'maaseh* explains the invalidity, there is yet another formal deficiency. *Mah hatam tnai bidavar echad u'maaseh bidavar acher* — in the *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven* case, the *tnai* pertained to one domain (crossing the Jordan to fight) and the *maaseh* pertained to a different domain (receiving a portion in *Ever HaYarden*). This separateness is required: *af kol tnai bidavar echad u'maaseh bidavar acher*. In our case, however, *hatnai ve'hamaaseh bidavar echad* — the *tnai* and the *maaseh* concern the same thing, namely the *get* and its paper.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: מִשּׁוּם דִּתְנַאי וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּדָבָר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that this condition is void **because the condition and the action are about the same matter,** and therefore the bill of divorce is valid even without fulfillment of the condition.

Rashi understands this as a prohibition on making a *tnai* about the same object as the *maaseh* — one simply cannot condition the *get* on something related to the *get* itself. Tosafot refines this: the problem is not mere topical overlap but logical self-contradiction. A *tnai* whose fulfillment would undermine the very *maaseh* it is meant to condition cannot be a valid *tnai*, since the entire purpose of a *tnai* is to enable the *maaseh*. A condition that, if satisfied, destroys what it was meant to activate defeats its own purpose and is therefore not recognized as a *tnai* at all.

The *gemara* thus accumulates multiple independent grounds — lack of doubling, *maaseh* preceding *tnai*, and *tnai* and *maaseh* in the same domain — each of which independently invalidates the *tnai* in the *baraisa*. This is also an opportunity for the *gemara* to rehearse the full body of *mishpatei ha-tna'im*, the formal rules governing conditions, which derive from the paradigmatic case of *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven*.

## Tosafot Categories

### Tnai Kaful

The discussion of how the *halacha* follows these formal requirements in practice is preserved in the *poskim*'s analysis, particularly in Tosafot on *daf* 75a, which takes up the question of these formal requirements. Tosafot concludes that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Meir and requires *tnai kaful* — the doubled condition — since Shmuel and Rava in the immediately following *sugya* both operate according to Rabbi Meir's position. As for all the *mishnas* in the tractate that deploy *al menas* — the condition that she give 200 *zuz*, that she nurse the child, and so on — Tosafot holds that those cases all involve a *tnai kaful*, even though the *Mishna* does not spell it out explicitly: *af al gav d'lo peireish*. The *gemara* later explains that the *tnai* is understood as doubled even when not stated in full. And this applies throughout *Shas*: wherever *al menas* appears and is assumed to be effective — for example, the *esrog* given *al menas l'hachazir* — we must assume the *tnai* was in fact doubled.

### Three Categories

Yet Tosafot acknowledges that this framework requires careful differentiation. At the end of that *Tosafot*, Rabbeinu Yitzchak articulates three distinct categories: *gimel inyanim yesh*. First, there are situations that require a fully formal *tnai* — *tnai gamur* — with all the formal rules including *tnai kaful* as per Rabbi Meir. Second, there are situations requiring only *giluy da'at* — disclosure of intent — without all the formal machinery of a *tnai*. Third, there are situations where not even *giluy da'at* is required, because the intent is assumed from context.

### Intent & Demands

This typology resolves what would otherwise be a bewildering proliferation of difficulty: if every *al menas* requires *tnai kaful* and *tnai kodem l'maaseh* and *tnai bidavar acher*, how have so many *mishnas* and *beraisos* deployed *al menas* without apparent concern for these rules? Tosafot's framework answers: the formal rules apply only when the *tnai* is genuinely external to the core *da'at* of the transaction. When a person truly desires to do something — marry, divorce, buy, sell — but is in a position to attach external demands, those demands are peripheral to his underlying intent. Because they are peripheral and could theoretically be dispensed with, the *Torah* requires that they be formulated with rigorous precision, following all the rules of *mishpatei ha-tna'im*, in order to override what would otherwise be an unconditional *maaseh*.

By contrast, when something is so central to a person's *da'at* that he would not have agreed to the transaction without it, this is not properly a *tnai* at all — it is a fundamental premise of his intent. Such a premise does not require the formal machinery of *tna'im*. It must, however, be articulated; otherwise it falls under *d'varim shebilev einam d'varim* — unexpressed inner thoughts have no legal standing.

The third category is *d'varim shebilev kol adam* — matters so universally understood that no articulation is needed. The classic *gemara* example is someone who sold all his possessions *adaita lemeisak l'ara d'Yisrael* — with the intention of making *aliyah* — and was subsequently prevented from going. The transactions he undertook in preparation are treated as void, even though he never explicitly conditioned each sale on his *aliyah* plans going through. No such articulation was necessary because every bystander understood perfectly well the assumption on which the sales were made.

Tosafot's framework thus creates an organic relationship between legal methodology and the reading of the *gemara*'s *pshat*. Beginning from the conclusion that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Meir on *tnai kaful*, Tosafot is impelled to read all the *mishnas* as implicitly involving doubled conditions, and then to construct a typology of which cases require those formal conditions at all. The legal methodology and the *pshat* reading of the *gemara* are not separable enterprises.

## Shachiv Meira

### Avoiding Evil Eye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְקֵין שְׁמוּאֵל בְּגִיטָּא דִּשְׁכִיב מְרַע: ״אִם לֹא מַתִּי לֹא יְהֵא גֵּט, וְאִם מַתִּי יְהֵא גֵּט״.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to conditions in a bill of divorce, **Shmuel instituted** that **in a bill of divorce of a person on his deathbed** the following expression should be written: **If I do not die this will not be** a valid **bill of divorce, and if I die it will be** a valid **bill of divorce.**

Shmuel instituted a *takkana* for the *get* of a *shachiv meira* — a person on his deathbed who wishes to give a *get* contingent on his death. The text Shmuel prescribed is: *im lo meisi lo yehei get v'im meisi yehei get* — "if I do not die it shall not be a *get*, and if I do die it shall be a *get*." The reason for beginning with the negative — "if I do not die" — is *lo makdmi inish b'puranusa*: a person does not open by invoking a bad outcome for himself. Beginning with "if I die" has the character of *ayin hara*.

### Yes Before No

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא: ״אִם מַתִּי יְהֵא גֵּט, וְאִם לֹא מַתִּי לֹא יְהֵא גֵּט״! לָא מַקְדֵּים אִינִישׁ פּוּרְעָנוּתָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ. וְלֵימָא: ״לֹא יְהֵא גֵּט אִם לֹא מַתִּי״! בָּעֵינַן תְּנַאי קוֹדֵם לְמַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about the wording used here: **But let us say** this statement in a more intuitive order: **If I die it will be** a valid **bill of divorce, and if I do not die this will not be** a valid **bill of divorce.** The Gemara explains: **A person does not hasten a calamity upon himself.** Consequently, he does not wish to mention his death first. The Gemara asks: Why did Shmuel use this phrasing? **But let us** say the condition using the following formulation: **It will not be** a valid **bill of divorce if I do not die,** and it will be a valid bill of divorce if I do die. The Gemara asks: When stipulating a condition, **we require the condition** to come **before the action,** but in this formulation the resultant action, that the bill of divorce is not valid, precedes the condition, if I do not die.

The *gemara* challenges: why not simply say *lo yehei get im lo meisi* — "it shall not be a *get* if I do not die" — and avoid the need to open with death at all? The answer is *be'ina tnai kodem l'maaseh* — the *tnai* must precede the *maaseh* in formulation. One cannot begin with the *maaseh* ("it shall not be a *get*") and then attach the *tnai* afterward.

Rava then challenges from a different angle: Shmuel's formulation opens with *im lo meisi* — "if I do not die" — which is the negative case. But the formal requirement derived from *bnei Gad u'vnei Reuven* is *hen kodem l'lav* — the affirmative must precede the negative. The "yes" — the case where the *tnai* is fulfilled — must come first. Shmuel's formulation violates this by leading with the negative.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: מִכְּדֵי כֹּל תְּנָאֵי מֵהֵיכָא גָּמְרִינַן – מִתְּנַאי בְּנֵי גָּד וּבְנֵי רְאוּבֵן; מָה הָתָם – הֵן קוֹדֵם לְלָאו, אַף כֹּל [תְּנָאֵי, הֵן קוֹדֵם לְלָאו]. אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: ״אִם לֹא מַתִּי לֹא יְהֵא גֵּט, אִם מַתִּי יְהֵא גֵּט, אִם לֹא מַתִּי לֹא יְהֵא גֵּט״ – ״אִם לֹא מַתִּי לֹא יְהֵא גֵּט״ – לָא מַקְדֵּים אִינִישׁ פּוּרְעָנוּתָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ; ״אִם מַתִּי יְהֵא גֵּט, אִם לֹא מַתִּי לֹא יְהֵא גֵּט״ – בָּעֵינַן הֵן קוֹדֵם לְלָאו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this** explanation: **Now, from where do we learn** the principles of **all conditions?** They are derived **from the condition of the children of Gad and the children of Reuben. Just as there, the affirmative precedes the negative,** meaning that the positive portion that speaks about what will occur if the condition is fulfilled appears before the negative portions that describe what will exist if the condition is not fulfilled, **so too, all** conditions must be formulated in this manner. This serves **to exclude** the case **here,** instituted by Shmuel, **where the negative precedes the affirmative. Rather, Rava said** that the condition in the bill of divorce of a dying man should be worded in the following manner: **If I do not die this will not be a bill of divorce. If I die this will be a bill of divorce,** and **if I do not die this will not be a bill of divorce.** The Gemara explains the necessity for such a formulation: The husband first says: **If I do not die this will not be a bill of divorce,** because **a person does not hasten a calamity upon himself.** Therefore, he first mentions the possibility that he will not die. Then he states the compound condition in the following order: **If I die this will be a bill of divorce,** and **if I do not die this will not be a bill of divorce.** This is because **we require** that **the affirmative precedes the negative.**

The resolution: Shmuel's *takkana* addresses both requirements simultaneously in a way that avoids *ayin hara*. One opens with a statement that is technically the negative (*im lo meisi lo yehei get*), which is not the troubling part because it posits the hopeful outcome. Then one proceeds *im meisi yehei get v'im lo meisi lo yehei get* — stating the affirmative before the negative in the doubled portion, thereby satisfying *hen kodem l'lav*. The opening preliminary statement avoids the need to lead with "if I die" while the doubled formulation that follows preserves the formal requirement of affirmative before negative. This *sugya* makes clear that *tnai kodem l'maaseh* is indeed a matter of verbal formulation and word order.

## Nursing Condition

### Implicit Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתְּשַׁמְּשִׁי אֶת אַבָּא״; ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתָּנִיקִי אֶת בְּנִי״ – כַּמָּה הִיא מְנִיקָתוֹ? שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁמֹנָה עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ. מֵת הַבֵּן אוֹ שֶׁמֵּת הָאָב – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a husband says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will serve** my **father,** or: **On the condition that you will nurse,** i.e., breastfeed, **my son,** without specifying a time period, **how long** is **she** required **to nurse him** in order to fulfill the condition? She is required to nurse the baby for **two years** from his birth, which is the length of time generally designated for nursing. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The time for nursing is only **eighteen months.** If **the** baby **son died or the** husband's **father died, this is** a valid **bill of divorce,** even though the condition was not fulfilled.

The *Mishna* now turns to conditions regarding service and nursing: *harei zeh gittech al menas she-teshamshi es abba* — "here is your *get* on the condition you attend to my father," or *al menas she-teniki es beni* — "on the condition you nurse my son." These conditions are binding. The *Mishna* asks: *kamah hi menikaso* — for how long must she nurse? The answer is *shtei shanim* — two years, the conventional nursing duration. Rabbi Yehuda says eighteen months.

### Death Exemption

The *Mishna* then states that if the child or the father dies — *meis haben o she-meis ha-av* — the *get* remains valid. Rashi understands this to mean that she performed at least one act of nursing or one act of attending to the father, and once the subject of the condition is gone, the *tnai* is considered satisfied for its duration. Tosafot rejects this reading as too obvious to require a *Mishna*. Tosafot holds that the *chiddush* is precisely that she did nothing — the father or child died before she had an opportunity to attend to them at all — and yet the *get* is valid. This parallels the rain-and-irrigation case discussed earlier: when the purpose of the *tnai* has been rendered moot by circumstances, the *tnai* is treated as satisfied. That the husband said "nurse my child" and she never nursed him, yet the *get* holds because there was no longer a child to be nursed — this is a substantial *chiddush*.

### Explicit Duration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתְּשַׁמְּשִׁי אֶת אַבָּא שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים״; ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתָּנִיקִי אֶת בְּנִי שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים״ – מֵת הַבֵּן, אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר הָאָב: ״אִי אֶפְשִׁי שֶׁתְּשַׁמְּשֵׁנִי״ שֶׁלֹּא בְּהַקְפָּדָה – אֵינוֹ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** But if the husband said to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will serve** my **father** for **two years,** or: **On the condition that you will nurse my son** for **two years,** and **the son died** before she nursed him for two years, **or the father said: I do not want you to serve me,** then even if the father **did not** say this **in anger** and she did everything she was expected to do, it **is not** a valid **bill of divorce** because the condition was not fulfilled.

The *Mishna* then distinguishes: *harei zeh gittech al menas she-teshamshi es abba shtei shanim* — if he specified a duration of two years — then even if the father dies or the child dies before the time has elapsed, *eino get* — it is not a valid *get*. When a specific duration is stated, that duration is required to be served. The condition is no longer implicitly "as needed"; it is a fixed term. Similarly, if the father says *ee efshar she-teshamisheni* — "I do not wish her to attend to me" — without any fault on her part (*she-lo b'kapdah*), the *get* is still invalid, because the explicit duration was not satisfied.

### Rabban Shimon Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל עַכָּבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הֵימֶנָּה – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט. כְּלָל אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: כׇּל עַכָּבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הֵימֶנָּה – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** In a case **like this** it is a valid **bill of divorce. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel stated a principle:** If there is **any hindrance** to the fulfillment of the condition **that does not** result **from her,** then **it is** a valid **bill of divorce.**

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel dissents: *kol akavah she-einah heimena harei zeh get* — any failure to fulfill the condition that is not her fault renders the *get* valid. The principle is framed around *ones* — external compulsion. If the father refuses her attendance without cause, or if circumstances beyond her control prevent performance, it is as though the condition has been met. This may be understood not merely as a liberal reading of an implicit "as needed" provision, but as an application of *yesh ones b'gittin* — the doctrine that circumstances of compulsion count as fulfillment. Just as Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was more liberal in the cloak case in accepting substitutes, he is more liberal here in accepting non-performance due to circumstances beyond her control.

## Liberal vs. Strict

### One Day Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמִי בָּעֵינַן כּוּלֵּי הַאי?! וּרְמִינְהִי: שִׁמְּשַׁתּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד; הֱנִיקַתּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד – הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **But do we require** that she serve the father or nurse the son for **all of this** time that was stipulated? **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** based on what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Nidda* 2:2): If **she served** the father for **one day** or **she nursed** the son for **one day it is** a valid **bill of divorce.**

A *baraisa* is cited that appears to contradict the *Mishna*: *henikato yom echad shimshato yom echad harei zeh get* — if she nursed the child for one day or attended to the father for one day, the *get* is valid. If no duration was specified, a single day of fulfillment satisfies the *tnai*. But the *Mishna* said that nursing, even without a stated duration, runs until the child is two years old. The contradiction is direct.

### Lenient vs. Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבָּנַן, וְהָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – מַתְנִיתִין רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וּבָרַיְיתָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** that this is **not difficult: This** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and this** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** who disagreed in the previous mishna (74a) with regard to a coat that was lost. According to the opinion of the Rabbis there, if there is no possibility that the wife can fulfill the condition by returning the coat, as it was lost, the bill of divorce is not valid. According to the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel there, she can give the husband the coat's value and the bill of divorce is valid. The Gemara explains: **The mishna** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** who claims that the wife can give the husband the equivalent of the stipulated item to render the bill of divorce valid, as the primary factor is his receiving the benefit he intended when stating his condition. Therefore, she must nurse the child for the standard length of time, as that is the benefit he intended when stating his condition, or else the bill of divorce is not valid. **And the *baraita*** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that the wife must fulfill the literal terms of the condition. If she nurses the baby for even one day, or serves the father for a single day, the literal terms of the condition have been fulfilled.

Rav Chisda resolves it: *lo kashya ha rabbanan v'ha Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the *Mishna* and the *baraisa* reflect different opinions. The *Mishna*, whose opening section does not belong to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel (since he is only introduced at the *Mishna*'s end), follows the *rabbanan*. The *baraisa* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who interprets the satisfaction of *tna'im* more liberally. Since Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was prepared to accept money in place of a cloak, he is prepared here to accept one day of nursing as satisfying a condition that mentioned no specific duration. The *rabbanan*, who demanded the cloak itself, demand the full implied duration.

### Interpretation Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָוֵי, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לָאו רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל! אֶלָּא בָּרַיְיתָא – רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִיא, דְּמֵיקֵל בִּתְנָאֵי; מַתְנִיתִין – רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: Is this explanation possible? **But from** the fact that **the latter clause** of the mishna **is** attributed to **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** it can be derived **by inference that the first clause** is **not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rather,** the ***baraita* is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who is lenient with regard to a condition,** and interprets it so that it can be fulfilled. **The mishna** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who interpret the condition in an expanded manner.

The *gemara* refines this further. The issue is not precisely one of leniency versus stringency, but of interpretive approach: do we read a *tnai* in a hyper-literal, maximally precise manner, or do we read it in terms of the evident purpose? The *rabbanan*, who read *tnai* language precisely — cloak means cloak, no substitute — also read "nurse" to mean "nurse for the full conventional duration" even when that duration is unstated. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who accepts functional equivalents, also reads the unstated duration liberally — one day of nursing fulfills the underlying intent of the condition. The *gemara* closes by noting that this framing in terms of hyper-literal versus liberal interpretation is more precise than simply mapping the positions onto leniency and stringency, since those categories shift depending on the case.
