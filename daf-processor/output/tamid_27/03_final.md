# Tamid 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohanim & Entry Rules

### One Kohen or Group?

We open on daf 27a of Tamid, picking up in the middle of the first *perek*. A point worth noting from earlier discussion: the *Gemara* had given the impression that there was only one *Kohen* per watching station. The *Rambam*, however, when he discusses the *mishmarot*, describes an *edah shel Kohanim* at each station — a community or collective. A number of *Acharonim* suggest he means ten, deriving the concept of *edah* from the same source we derive the requirement for a *minyan*. In any event, the *Rambam*'s position is clear: it was not a single *Kohen* at each station but a group of *Kohanim*.

### Non-Jewish Access

The question of non-Jewish access to the *Mikdash* was also raised. Non-Jews are not permitted in the *Ezrat Nashim*; they are kept outside of the *cheil*, which is the outer wall surrounding the Temple complex. They do not enter beyond that perimeter.

### Tumah Restrictions

Similarly, while a *tamei meis* is, biblically speaking, permitted on *Har Habayit* — and technically the *Ezrat Nashim* carries the status of *Har Habayit* — nevertheless, the Rabbis accorded the *Ezrat Nashim* an additional level of *kedushah* and decreed that a *tamei meis* was not permitted there. That is the extra rabbinic restriction that applies to a *tamei meis*. A *ba'al keri*, as we will see in today's *Gemara*, is *assur* on *Har Habayit* outright. A *tevul yom* is likewise *assur*. As for non-Jews who brought *korbanos*, according to the understanding of the *Mishnah* and *Gemara*, they would send their offerings without being personally present.

## Leviim 24 Watches

### Middot Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת הַכֹּהֲנִים שׁוֹמְרִים בְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ: בְּבֵית אַבְטִינָס, וּבְבֵית הַנִּיצוֹץ, וּבְבֵית הַמּוֹקֵד. וְהַלְוִיִּם בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד מְקוֹמוֹת: חֲמִשָּׁה – עַל חֲמִשָּׁה שַׁעֲרֵי הַר הַבַּיִת. אַרְבָּעָה – עַל אַרְבַּע פִּנּוֹתָיו מִבִּפְנִים. חֲמִשָּׁה – עַל חֲמִשָּׁה שַׁעֲרֵי עֲזָרָה. וְאַרְבָּעָה – עַל אַרְבַּע פִּנּוֹתָיו מִבַּחוּץ. אֶחָד – בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַקׇּרְבָּן, וְאֶחָד – בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַפָּרוֹכֶת, וְאֶחָד – אֲחוֹרֵי בֵּית הַכַּפּוֹרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Middot* 1:1): **The priests** would **keep watch in three places in the Temple** courtyard: **In the Chamber of Avtinas, and in the Chamber of the Spark, and in the Chamber of the Hearth. And the Levites** would keep watch **in twenty-one places,** as follows: **Five upon** the **five gates of the Temple Mount; four upon** the **four corners of** the Temple Mount, **within** the wall surrounding the Temple Mount; **five upon** the **five gates of** the Temple **courtyard, and four upon** the **four corners of** the Temple courtyard **outside** the courtyard wall, surrounding the Temple courtyard. **One** watch is observed **in the Chamber of the Offering,** where animals that had been checked for blemishes were held in readiness for sacrifice; **one** watch is kept **in the Chamber of the Curtain,** where the Curtain separating the Sanctuary and the Holy of Holies was woven; **and** finally, **one** watch is kept **behind the Chamber of the Ark Cover,** in the area between the Holy of Holies and the western wall of the Temple Mount.

The *Gemara* now cites *t'nan hatam* — a parallel *Mishnah* found at the beginning of *Middot*. When we first encountered our *Mishnah* in *Tamid*, it was noted that the *Mishnah* in *Middot* addresses not only the *Kohanim* but also the *Levi'im*. The text of that *Mishnah* runs as follows:

*Bishlosha mekomos haKohanim shomrim b'Veis HaMikdash* — in three places the *Kohanim* guard in the *Beis HaMikdash*: in *Beis Avtinas*, in *Beis HaNitzotz*, and in *Beis HaMoked* — the three chambers embedded within the walls of the *Azarah*. *VehaLevi'im b'esrim v'echad makom* — and the *Levi'im* in twenty-one places.

The *Mishnah* then details those twenty-one locations. *Chamisha al chamisha sha'arei Har Habayit* — five at the five gates of *Har Habayit*. These are the gates entering the outer Temple Mount, such as those visible today in the archaeological excavations along the southern wall. *Arba al arba pinosav* — and four at the four corners. One notes that the *Meforesh* here translates *pinos* as "angles," remarkably close to the English word. Importantly, *mitocham* — all of this watching on *Har Habayit* was performed from the inside of the walls.

This requires some reflection. One would naturally expect guards — particularly at corners and gates — to be stationed on the outside, where they would be more visible and better positioned to monitor approach. Yet the *Mishnah* specifies the inside. Together that gives us nine stations for *Har Habayit*: five gates plus four corners.

Next: *chamisha al chamisha sha'arei azarah* — five at the five gates of the *Azarah*. We will shortly discuss whether there were really five or seven (or even eight) gates; for now the *Mishnah* states five. *Arba al arba pinoteha* — and four at the four corners of the *Azarah*. This time, however, the *Mishnah* specifies *mibachutz* — on the outside. So for the *Azarah* proper we have five plus four on the outside, for another nine, bringing the running total to eighteen.

Finally, the *Mishnah* adds three more locations: *echad beLishkat HaKorban* — one in the *Lishkat HaKorban*, which was a chamber within the large space of the *Beis HaMoked*; *ve'echad beLishkat HaParochet* — one in the *Lishkat HaParochet*, the location of which is not entirely clear; and *ve'echad achorei Beis HaKapporet* — one behind the *Kodesh Kodashim*. These three bring the total to twenty-one.

### Deriving 21 Watches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה מִסּוּרָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״לַמִּזְרָח הַלְוִיִּם שִׁשָּׁה לַצָּפוֹנָה לְוִיִּם אַרְבָּעָה לַנֶּגְבָּה לְוִיִּם אַרְבָּעָה וְלָאֲסֻפִּים שְׁנַיִם שְׁנָיִם לַפַּרְבָּר לַמַּעֲרָב אַרְבָּעָה לַמְסִלָּה שְׁנַיִם לַפַּרְבָּר״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to these twenty-one places where the Levites keep watch, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Yehuda of Sura said, and some say** that **it was taught in a *baraita*:** This is derived **as it is written** with regard to the Levites assigned by David to serve as gatekeepers upon the future construction of the Temple: **Eastward were six Levites, northward four Levites, southward four Levites, and for the *Asuppim* two and two. For the *Parbar* westward, four at the causeway, and two at the *Parbar*** (see I Chronicles 26:17–18).

The *Gemara* now asks: *Mina hani mili*? Where do we derive these twenty-one watching locations? Yesterday we learned that the obligation for *Kohanim* to watch was derived from the *pasuk* of *sha'aru vanu veno*, and that *Levi'im* shared in that watch as well, drawn from the *pasuk* of *veyilavu alecha* — the *Kohanim* above, the *Levi'im* below. But the question of where twenty-one specifically is derived from remains open. The *Gemara*'s working assumption is that this number traces back not merely to convention but to *Torah* — or at least to a substantive source. One might fairly ask: why does it feel the need to derive this from a *pasuk* at all? Even if the derivation will come from *Divrei HaYamim* rather than the *Torah* itself, it does not establish the number as biblically mandated. The best answer seems to be that the *Gemara* is not asking "how do we know this is true" — it accepts the *Mishnah* as factually accurate — but rather "what is it based on?" The goal is to establish continuity, to show that this arrangement traces back to the time of King David and was not invented arbitrarily.

### Divrei Hayamim Source

The *pasuk* reads: *Lemizrach HaLeviyim shisha* — to the east, six *Levi'im*. *Latzafona laLevi'im arba'ah* — to the north, four. *Lanegba laLevi'im arba'ah* — to the south, four. *La'asufim shnayim shnayim* — to the *asufim* (some translate this as antechambers or a similar space), two and two. *LaParbar lama'arav arba'ah lamesilah ushnayim laParbar* — for the *Parbar* to the west: four to the path leading up to it and two to the *Parbar* itself. The *Gemara* will shortly explain what *Parbar* means.

### Math to 21

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָנֵי עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה הָווּ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: לָאֲסֻפִּים – שְׁנַיִם (שְׁנַיִם). אַכַּתִּי, עֶשְׂרִין וּתְרֵי הָווּ! הַיְאךְ דְּפַרְבָּר – חַד הֲוָה, וְאַחֲרִינָא, בְּצַוְותָּא הוּא דְּאָזֵיל וְיָתֵיב גַּבֵּיהּ – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָאֵי אַבָּרַאי. מַאי ״לַפַּרְבָּר״? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״כְּלַפֵּי בַּר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** that if these verses are the source for the twenty-one places in which the Levites keep watch, that is difficult, as **these** watches enumerated in the verse **are twenty-four** in total. **Abaye said: This** is what the verse **is saying: "For the *Asuppim*** two," and they are always only **two.** It is not uncommon for a verse to repeat a word for emphasis in this manner, especially at the end of a verse. The Gemara objects: Even if two locations are removed from the list, according to the verse **there are still twenty-two** watches, rather than twenty-one. The Gemara explains: **That** watch, **which** was situated at the ***Parbar*, was** composed of only **one** watchman, **and** as for **the other** Levite mentioned in the verse, **it was** merely to serve **as company that he went and sat with** the watchman, **due to** the fact **that** the *Parbar* **was situated on the outer** side and was isolated from the other watches. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the term **"at the *Parbar* [*laParbar*]"** (I Chronicles 26:18)? **Rabba bar Rav Sheila said:** This term is a contraction of two Aramaic words, and it is **like one who says: Toward the outside [*kelapei bar*].**

If one simply totals these figures — six, four, four, four, four, and two — the sum is twenty-four, not twenty-one. The *Gemara* immediately confronts this: *ami hani chaf daled havu* — but that adds up to twenty-four.

*Amar Abaye*: *hachi ka'amar* — here is how to read the *pasuk*. When it says *la'asufim shnayim shnayim*, the doubled expression "two and two" does not mean four. It means simply two. As the *Meforesh* explains, *shnayim shnayim* is a way of concluding the *pasuk*, after which the next *pasuk* begins with *laParbar*. If the intent were four, it would have said four. The doubling is merely emphatic — it really means only two. This reduces the total from twenty-four to twenty-two.

But the *Gemara* presses: *achti esrin utrei havei* — it is still twenty-two, not twenty-one. The *Gemara* answers: *hacha deparbar, chad havei* — regarding the *Parbar*, it said two but only one station was actually required. Why then does the text mention two? *Vachrina betzavta hu de'azil veyatv gabeih* — the second person simply went along to keep the first one company. It was only one *Levi* genuinely on watch; the second was there so he would not be alone. This implies, incidentally, that the *Gemara* reads these numbers quite literally — one means one — though as noted earlier, the *Rambam* uses the term *edah* for each station. Perhaps the *Parbar* was a particularly isolated outpost, the furthest point from the flow of traffic, and therefore a companion was sent along.

Rabba bar Rav Sheila offers an Aramaic gloss on the word *Parbar* itself: *keman de'amar klapei bar* — it means "toward the outside," the most outward and distant station. That accounts for the companion. And the point about *derech kavod* — that no part of the Temple precinct should be left unwatched, unattended — explains why a watch was maintained there at all.

### Kohanim as Leviim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה, כְּדִכְתִיב. תְּלָתָא מִינַּיְיהוּ – דְּכֹהֲנִים, וְעֶשְׂרִין וְחַד – דִּלְוִיִּם. וְהָא הָכָא, לְוִיִּם הוּא דִּכְתִיב! כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה מְקוֹמוֹת נִקְרְאוּ כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם, וְזֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶן – ״וְהַכֹּהֲנִים הַלְוִיִּם בְּנֵי צָדוֹק״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents an alternative answer to the question with regard to the verse: **And if you wish, say** instead that **actually** there are **twenty-four** watches, and the verse may be interpreted literally **as it is written. Three of them** are the watches kept **by** the **priests, and** the remaining **twenty-one** are the watches kept **by** the **Levites. But isn't it written here** in the verse that all twenty-four watches are kept by the **Levites?** The Gemara answers: This interpretation is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: In twenty-four places** in the Bible the **priests are called Levites, and this is one of them: "But the priests the Levites, the sons of Zadok,** that kept the charge of My Sanctuary when the children of Israel went astray from Me, they shall come near to Me to serve Me" (Ezekiel 44:15).

The *Gemara* then offers an alternative resolution: *ve'iba'it eima le'olam chaf daled* — perhaps the total really is twenty-four. *Tlata mineihu deKohanim* — three of those twenty-four stations belonged to the *Kohanim*. If three were for *Kohanim* and twenty-one for *Levi'im*, then the *pasuk* in *Divrei HaYamim*, which totals twenty-four, fits precisely: three *Kohanim* plus twenty-one *Levi'im*.

But this raises an obvious objection: the *pasuk* speaks throughout of *Levi'im*, not *Kohanim*. The *Gemara* invokes Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who taught that in twenty-four places in *Tanach* the *Kohanim* are called *Levi'im*, because *Kohanim* are a subset of *Levi'im*. One particularly clear example: *vehaKohanim haLevi'im b'nei Tzadok* — "the *Kohanim*, the *Levi'im*, the sons of Tzadok." These are unmistakably *Kohanim*, yet they are referred to as *Levi'im*. Thus, even where the *pasuk* in *Divrei HaYamim* says *Levi'im*, it may also encompass the three *Kohen* stations. The *pasuk* derives from the era of King David — before the *Mikdash* was built, when the arrangement was still that of the *Mishkan*. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* treats it as the model for what would later obtain in the *Beis HaMikdash* of Shlomo, and thereafter as well, emphasizing continuity across the generations.

## Inside/Outside Watch

### Difference Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמִשָּׁה – עַל חֲמִשָּׁה שַׁעֲרֵי הַר הַבַּיִת, וְאַרְבָּעָה – עַל אַרְבַּע פִּנּוֹתָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ. חֲמִשָּׁה – עַל חֲמִשָּׁה שַׁעֲרֵי עֲזָרָה, וְאַרְבָּעָה – עַל אַרְבַּע פִּנּוֹתָיו מִבַּחוּץ. מַאי שְׁנָא הַר הַבַּיִת דְּעָבְדִינַן מִתּוֹכוֹ, וּמַאי שְׁנָא עֲזָרָה דְּעָבְדִינַן מִבַּחוּץ?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna (*Middot* 1:1) teaches that **five** watches are kept **upon** the **five gates of the Temple Mount, and four** watches are kept **upon** the **four corners of** the Temple Mount **within** the Temple Mount wall. **Five** watches are kept **upon** the **five gates of** the Temple **courtyard, and four** are kept **upon** the **four corners of** the Temple courtyard **outside** the courtyard wall, on the Temple Mount. The Gemara asks: **What is different** with regard to **the Temple Mount that we perform** the watch **within** the walls, **and what is different** with regard to the Temple **courtyard that we perform** the watch **outside** its walls?

Having established the twenty-one Levitical stations and their locations, the *Gemara* turns to a pointed question: *Mai shna Har Habayit de'avidina mitcha* — why, when it comes to *Har Habayit*, did the *Levi'im* watch from the inside? *U'mai shna Azarah de'avidina mibachutz* — and why, when it came to the *Azarah* proper, did they watch from the outside?

This asymmetry is not self-evident. One might expect the default to be watching from the outside, where an honor guard would be more visible and where those approaching could be monitored. That the inside should be the default — and that the *Azarah* represents a deviation from it — requires explanation.

### Sitting Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הַר הַבַּיִת, דְּאִי תָּמַהּ וּבָעֵי מֵיתַב – יָתֵיב, אָמְרִינַן מִתּוֹכוֹ. עֲזָרָה, דְּאִי תָּמַהּ וּבָעֵי לְמֵיתַב – לָא מָצֵי יָתֵיב, דְּאָמַר מָר: אֵין יְשִׁיבָה בָּעֲזָרָה אֶלָּא לְמַלְכֵי בֵית דָּוִד בִּלְבַד, אָמְרִינַן מִבַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say:** With regard to the watches on **the Temple Mount, if** the watchman **tires and wants** to **sit** down, **he may sit** down, as it is permitted to sit on the Temple Mount. Therefore, **we say** that the watch is kept **within** the Temple Mount. By contrast, if a watch is observed in the Temple **courtyard,** even **if** the watchman **tires and wants to sit** down, **he may not sit** down, **as the Master said: Sitting in the** Temple **courtyard** is **permitted only for kings of the house of David.** Therefore, **we say** that the watch is kept **outside** the walls of the Temple courtyard, so that the watchman may sit down if he wishes.

The *Gemara* answers: *Har Habayit de'itama, ubai l'meysev, yasiv* — on *Har Habayit*, if a *Levi* grew weary and wished to sit and rest, he could do so. Because sitting is permitted on *Har Habayit*, we station them on the inside; no accommodation is needed. But in the *Azarah* — *de'itama, ubai l'meysev, lo matzi yasiv* — if a *Levi* grew weary and wished to sit, he could not. Why? *Ein yeshiva b'Azarah ela l'malchei Beis David bilvad* — sitting in the *Azarah* proper is reserved exclusively for kings of the Davidic line. Therefore, since a *Levi* stationed inside the *Azarah* would have no opportunity to rest, the *Gemara* says we move him to the outside, where he can sit down when he needs to.

This answer, however, leaves one question somewhat open. If the default is the inside, one might have expected the *Azarah* corners — particularly those adjacent to the *Ezrat Nashim*, where sitting would not be prohibited — to also be on the inside. Yet the *Mishnah* says *mibachutz* for all the *Azarah* corners and gates, which suggests that the outer positions of those corners were at the walls of the *Ezrat Nashim*, and thus the "outside" of those corners places the *Levi'im* at points that are not architecturally visible corners at all, but midpoints of the outer wall. The question of precisely where those four corners were located — whether the inner corners of the *Azarah* proper or the outer corners including the *Ezrat Nashim* — remains somewhat open in the discussion.

## Gate Count Debate

### Five vs Seven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: חֲמִשָּׁה עַל חֲמִשָּׁה שַׁעֲרֵי עֲזָרָה. וַחֲמִשָּׁה שְׁעָרִים הוּא דְּהָוֵי בַּעֲזָרָה?! וּרְמִינְהִי שִׁבְעָה שְׁעָרִים הָיוּ בָּעֲזָרָה, שְׁלֹשָׁה בַּצָּפוֹן, וּשְׁלֹשָׁה בַּדָּרוֹם, וְאֶחָד בַּמִּזְרָח!
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** above that **five** watches are kept **upon** the **five gates of** the Temple **courtyard.** The Gemara asks: **But was it** only **five gates that were** constructed **in the** walls of **the** Temple **courtyard? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Middot* 1:4): **There were seven gates in the** Temple **courtyard: Three in the north, and three in the south, and one in the east.**

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Mishnah*'s statement that the *Levi'im* watched at the five gates of the *Azarah*. The *Gemara* objects: *shiva'ah sha'arei hayu ba'Azarah* — were there not seven gates in the *Azarah*? Three in the north, three in the south, and one in the east. Most diagrams of the *Azarah* that have come down to us indeed show seven chambers and gates, and some depictions show even more. How then does the *Mishnah* say five?

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תְּרֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ לָא צְרִיכִי שִׁימּוּר. רָבָא אָמַר: תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר גִּזְבָּרִין וּמִשִּׁבְעָה אֲמַרְכָּלִין. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר גִּזְבָּרִין, כְּנֶגֶד שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שְׁעָרִים. דַּל חַמְשָׁה דְּהַר הַבַּיִת – פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תְּמָנְיָא דַּעֲזָרָה. אַלְמָא, אִיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר תְּמָנְיָא הָווּ, וְאִיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר שִׁבְעָה, וְאִיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּאָמַר חַמְשָׁה הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** Although there were seven gates, **two of them,** the gate to the Chamber of the Spark and the gate to the Chamber of the Hearth, **did not require a watch** of the Levites, as the priests kept watch there. **Rava said:** The number of gates **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **There must be no fewer than thirteen treasurers and seven trustees** appointed over the Temple administration. **Rabbi Natan says: There must be no fewer than thirteen treasurers, corresponding to** the **thirteen gates. Remove** from the total of thirteen gates the **five** gates **of the Temple Mount,** and **there remain eight** gates **to the** Temple **courtyard. Evidently, there is a *tanna* who said** that **there were eight** gates, **and there is a *tanna* who said** that there were **seven** gates, **and there is** also **a *tanna* who said** that **there were five** gates.

*Amar Abaye*: *trei mineihu lo tzrichi shimur* — two of the seven gates did not require separate watching. The explanation given by Rabbeinu Gershom and the *Rosh* is that if the two outer gates in a row were guarded, the middle gate was effectively covered as well, since a watchman positioned atop an outer gate could observe in both directions and effectively monitor the gate between them. Thus, while there were seven gates, only five required dedicated watchmen. This is Abaye's resolution.

### Officers Debate

*Rava amar*, however: this is a dispute among *Tanna'im*. A *Baraisa* teaches: *ein pochtin mi-yud gimmel gizbarin u'mi-shiva'ah amarkalin* — there were no fewer than thirteen *gizbarin* (officers, at a lower supervisory level) and seven *amarkalin* (senior supervisors) in the *Mikdash* at any given time. Rabbi Natan asks, where does the number thirteen *gizbarin* come from? *Keneged yud gimmel she'arim* — corresponding to thirteen gates in the entire Temple complex. Five belong to *Har Habayit*, leaving eight for the *Azarah*. Thus Rabbi Natan's formulation implicitly presupposes eight gates in the *Azarah*, not five or seven.

### Factual Dispute

The *Gemara* concludes: *alma ika tanna de'amar tamnya havu* — we now see there are those who say eight; *ika tanna de'amar shiv'ah* — those who say seven; *ve'ika tanna de'amar chamishah havu* — and those who say five. This is, as the *Gemara* acknowledges, a dispute. What is notable is that this is not a *halachic* dispute — it is a factual-historical one. These things are generally stated without attribution, as accepted fact, and it is somewhat unusual to frame them as a *machloket*. One possibility: the disagreement may be partly definitional — what exactly counts as a "gate" warranting its own designation may have varied. In any event, the *Gemara* accepts that there were divergent traditions regarding the precise number of gates in the *Azarah*.

## Sleep Garments

### Under the Head

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הָיוּ יְשֵׁנִים בְּבִגְדֵי קֹדֶשׁ כּוּ׳. שֵׁינָה הוּא דְּלָא, אֲבָל הִילּוּךְ – מְהַלְּכִים. שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ: בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה נִיתְּנוּ לֵיהָנוֹת בָּהֶן!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna (25b) teaches that the priests **would not sleep** dressed **in the sacred vestments;** rather, they would remove them and place them beneath their heads. The Gemara infers from here that **it is** only **sleep that is not** permitted while a priest is dressed in the sacred vestments, lest he pass wind during his sleep. **But** with regard to wearing such vestments while the priests are awake and engaged in various activities, e.g., **walking, they may walk** about dressed in the vestments, even when they do not need to wear them for the Temple service. **You may** therefore **conclude from** the mishna that **it is permitted to derive benefit from priestly vestments.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the *Mishnah*'s description of the *Kohanim* sleeping in the *Beis HaMoked*. They slept in their own garments, not in *Bigdei Kodesh*, and would take off their *Bigdei Kehunah*, fold them, and place them *tachat rosheihem* — under their heads. It is worth noting, following the *Rambam*, that it was specifically the *Kohanim* who were on watch — or at least those watching in that chamber — who slept there, presumably on some rotation system.

### Benefit Question

The *Gemara* infers from the *Mishnah*'s formulation: since it says they did not *sleep* in the *Bigdei Kehunah* — implying that walking around in them would have been permitted — can we derive from this that *Bigdei Kehunah nitnu lhenot bahen*? That is, that one may derive personal benefit from the priestly garments? This is a significant question, given that we have just finished *Meilah*, with its prohibitions on deriving benefit from sanctified objects. During the *Avodah*, a *Kohen*'s wearing of the *Bigdei Kehunah* is not considered personal benefit; it is required. But what about moments outside the *Avodah*?

The *Gemara* deflects this inference: one could say that even walking around in them was not permitted; the reason the *Mishnah* says "they did not sleep in them" is simply to set up what follows — *ela poshtin umikaplin umanichin otan tachat rosheihen* — that they took them off, folded them, and placed them beneath their heads. The *Mishnah* is being descriptive about sleep-time behavior, not drawing a contrast that would imply other uses were permitted.

### Opposite Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הוּא הַדִּין דַּאֲפִילּוּ הִילּוּךְ נָמֵי לָא, וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי לֹא הָיוּ יְשֵׁנִים – מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵי לְמִיתְנֵא סֵיפָא: אֶלָּא פּוֹשְׁטִין וּמְקַפְּלִין וּמַנִּיחִין אוֹתָן תַּחַת רָאשֵׁיהֶן, קָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא נָמֵי: לֹא הָיוּ יְשֵׁנִים. וְהָא גּוּפַהּ קָא קַשְׁיָא: וּמַנִּיחִין אוֹתָן תַּחַת רָאשֵׁיהֶן – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה נִיתְּנוּ לֵיהָנוֹת בָּהֶם! אֵימָא: נֶגֶד רָאשֵׁיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** that this inference is incorrect. **The same is true** of walking, **as even walking** while wearing the vestments **is not** permitted, **and** the reason **that** the *tanna* **teaches** specifically that the priests **would not sleep** dressed in the vestments is **due to** the fact **that** the *tanna* **wanted to teach the latter clause: Rather, they** would **remove** them **and fold** them, **and** then **they** would **place them beneath their heads.** Since the latter clause is referring specifically to sleeping, the *tanna* **teaches** in **the former clause as well** that the priests **would not sleep** dressed in the vestments. The Gemara objects: **But** according to this interpretation, the mishna **itself is difficult,** as the mishna states: **And they** would **place** the priestly vestments **beneath their heads,** as a cushion. One may **conclude from** this statement that **it is permitted to derive benefit from priestly vestments.** The Gemara rejects this interpretation: **Say** that the mishna means that they would place the vestments **next to their heads,** not literally beneath them.

But now the *Gemara* turns to that description itself. *Umanichin otan tachat rosheihen* — they placed them under their heads. This sounds as though they used the garments as a pillow. Would that not constitute deriving benefit from *Bigdei Kehunah*? One might answer: *eima keneged rosheihem* — perhaps *tachat* here does not mean literally beneath but merely near, adjacent to. The garments were kept close at hand, not used as actual pillows. The classic use of *tachat* to mean something like "at the foot of" or "in proximity to" — as in *tachat hahar* — supports this reading, even if it is a stretch.

### Kilayim Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא דִּכְנֶגֶד רָאשֵׁיהֶן, דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ תַּחַת רָאשֵׁיהֶן, נְהִי דְּנִיתְּנוּ לֵיהָנוֹת בָּהֶן, תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם אִיסּוּרָא דְכִלְאַיִם! וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּמַפְסֵיק מִידֵּי, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל מִשּׁוּם קְהָלָא קַדִּישָׁא שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם: אֲפִילּוּ עֶשֶׂר מַצָּעוֹת זוֹ עַל גַּב זוֹ, וְכִלְאַיִם תַּחְתֵּיהֶן – אָסוּר לִישַׁן עֲלֵיהֶן. אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, נֶגֶד רָאשֵׁיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** to say **that** the mishna permits the vestments to be placed only **next to their heads. As, if you say** that the mishna permits the vestments to be placed literally **beneath their heads,** this is difficult. **Granted that it is permitted to derive benefit from them,** but one **could derive** that it is prohibited to sleep upon them **due to the prohibition of diverse kinds** of wool and linen. The priestly vestments contain both wool and linen, which is a prohibited mixture in every other context. The Torah specifically permits the priests to wear them while they are performing the Temple service, but this does not extend to using the vestments as a cushion while sleeping. **And if you would say** that the priests could place the vestments beneath their heads in such a manner **that something separates** between their flesh and the vestments, as the fibers could not wrap themselves upon their flesh, such conduct would still be prohibited. **Doesn't Rabbi Shimon say** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says** that **Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul says in the name of the holy community in Jerusalem: Even** if there are **ten mattresses** piled **one atop the other and** a garment of **diverse kinds** is placed **beneath** all of **them, it is prohibited to sleep upon them?** This is because the rabbinic decree applies equally to all cases, irrespective of whether the concern that motivated the decree exists. **Rather,** one may **conclude from** here that the mishna permits the vestments to be placed only **next to their heads.**

Rav Papa draws a practical ruling from this passage: *shma mina tefillin min hatzad sharyon* — from the fact that even if the garments were only placed near the head, and we are not concerned that a person might roll over onto them in the night, we may infer that *tefillin* placed to the side of one who is napping need not be worried about. *Velo chayshinan dilma mignadar vnafal aleihu* — we are not concerned that one might roll over and come to rest upon them. This inference draws on the well-established parallel between *tefillin* and *Bigdei Kehunah* — including the *tzitz* — as objects that confer a sense of *kedushah* and direct one's thoughts toward God.

The *Gemara* now offers a stronger proof that *tachat rosheihem* cannot be taken literally. *Hachi nami mistabra* — this interpretation also makes better sense for the following reason: even setting aside the question of benefit from *Bigdei Kehunah*, there is the problem of *kilayim*. The belt (*avnet*) of the *Kohen* was, by the account of many, made from wool and linen together — *shatnez*. During the *Avodah*, wearing *shatnez* is permitted because the *Torah* explicitly requires the *Bigdei Kehunah* in that context. But as soon as the *Kohen* is no longer engaged in the *Avodah*, the prohibition of *kilayim* reasserts itself. To use the belt as a pillow — placing it beneath one's head — would constitute a form of using *shatnez*, even without wearing it.

One might object: the *pasuk* forbids *shatnez lo ya'aleh alecha* — it shall not be placed *upon* you. If it is beneath you, perhaps it is not prohibited? The *Gemara* addresses this: *d'Oraita*, that might be correct. But *amar amru chachamim assur la'asot ken* — the Rabbis nonetheless prohibited it, *shema tichrach nima achat al besaro* — lest even a single thread curl upward onto the skin, providing a trace of warmth. This is a rabbinic *gezeirah*, and the *Gemara* adds that even ten blankets laid atop *kilayim* do not resolve the problem, as taught by the holy congregation in Jerusalem in a tradition transmitted through Rabbi Yosi ben Shaul, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and Rav Shimon: *afilu eser matza'ot zu al gav zu, v'kilayim tachteichem, assur lishon aleihem* — even ten mattresses stacked one upon the other, with *kilayim* beneath them all, are forbidden to sleep upon. This is also structurally reminiscent of the *halacha* of *mishkav u'moshav* of a *zav* or *zavah*, where the object beneath is affected even through many layers.

Accordingly, if even rabbinic restriction prevents using *shatnez* as a pillow — and *ein shvut b'Mikdash* does not apply here since this does not impede the *Avodah* — it is clear that *tachat rosheihem* cannot mean they literally used the *Bigdei Kehunah* as pillows. It must mean near their heads.

### No Belt Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּאוֹתָן שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן כִּלְאַיִם. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה קָשִׁים הֵן, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הָא נַמְטָא גַּמְדָּא דְּנַרֶשׁ – שַׁרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests alternative solutions: **And if you wish, say** instead that the mishna does permit the priests to place the vestments beneath their heads, as it is referring **to those** vestments **that do not contain diverse kinds. Rav Ashi says:** The mishna permits the priests to place even the belt that contains diverse kinds beneath their heads. This is because the **priestly vestments,** and specifically the belt, **are stiff,** and therefore it is not prohibited to lie on them. **As Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Yehoshua, said: This stiff felt [*namta*],** which is manufactured **in** the city of **Neresh** and is made of diverse kinds, **is permitted.** The prohibition of diverse kinds applies only to items that are similar to garments, which one derives pleasure from wearing. A stiff garment does not provide warmth, and is therefore not included in this prohibition.

The *Gemara* offers a partial counter: *haniha leman de'amar avneto shel Kohen Gadol lo zehu avneto shel Kohen Hedyot* — this reasoning works well if one holds that the belt of the *Kohen Gadol* was not the same as the belt of the ordinary *Kohen*. If the ordinary *Kohen*'s belt did not contain *shatnez*, then the *kilayim* argument collapses and perhaps the *Kohanim* did literally use the garments as pillows. But *ella leman de'amar avneto shel Kohen Hedyot zehu avneto shel Kohen Gadol* — if one holds the opposing view, that the ordinary *Kohen*'s belt was made of the same material as the *Kohen Gadol*'s — wool and linen — then the *kilayim* proof stands.

The *Gemara* also entertains another escape: *de'chi teima kilayim be'ha'alah u'velivishah hu de'assur* — perhaps *kilayim* is only prohibited when worn or placed upon one. If it is beneath one, maybe it is permitted even rabbinically? The *Gemara* rejects this, as noted above, based on the *Baraisa* that even placing it beneath many coverings is rabbinically forbidden. And the suggestion that perhaps the garments were folded beneath something else — so the *shatnez* fibers would never be in contact with the skin — is also rejected by the ruling of the holy congregation in Jerusalem.

However, the *Gemara* notes: *ve'iba'it eima, be'avnet she'ein bo kilayim* — alternatively, perhaps they used the garments as pillows and simply excluded the belt. The belt was the only garment containing *kilayim*; the outer garment, the *mitznefet*, and other items did not. So perhaps they placed everything except the belt beneath their heads, and that was perfectly fine. This leaves the question genuinely unresolved: we cannot definitively prove from the *kilayim* argument alone that *tachat rosheihem* is not literal.

### Hard Fabric Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה, הַיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן לִמְדִינָה – אָסוּר. וּבַמִּקְדָּשׁ, בֵּין בִּשְׁעַת עֲבוֹדָה וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת עֲבוֹדָה – מוּתָּר. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה נִיתְּנוּ לֵיהָנוֹת בָּהֶן. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss the earlier dilemma, of whether it is permitted to derive benefit from priestly vestments. **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: With regard to the **priestly vestments,** the act of **one who leaves** the Temple dressed **in them** and goes out **to the country,** i.e., outside the Temple, is **prohibited. But in the Temple, both at the time of** the Temple **service and not at the time of** the **service,** wearing the vestments is **permitted, as it is permitted to derive benefit from the priestly vestments.** The Gemara concludes: One may **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is permitted to derive benefit from the priestly vestments.

*Rav Ashi amar Bigdei Kehunah kashim hein* — Rav Ashi offers yet another approach: the *Bigdei Kehunah* were hard and stiff. This is developed from a teaching of Rav Huna beray de-Rav Yehoshua: *ha-Namta Gamda de-Narsh sharya* — the hard felt of Narsh (a place name) is permitted, even if it contains *kilayim*. The principle is that if a fabric is extremely stiff and rigid — more like leather than cloth — it is physically incapable of draping over the skin and providing warmth in the manner of a garment. The entire *kilayim* prohibition in the context of *mishkav* is predicated on the possibility that a thread might curl up and touch the skin. If the fabric is so rigid that this is impossible, the rabbinic decree does not apply.

Ironically, this is the inverse of the usual softness concern: a soft fabric is forbidden even beneath ten mattresses because a single fiber might reach the skin; a hard, stiff fabric is permitted even directly beneath one because it is structurally incapable of doing so. If the *Bigdei Kehunah* were of this stiff, hard consistency, then even lying upon the belt directly would pose no *kilayim* concern, and *tachat rosheihem* may well be literal after all.

The *Gemara* thus concludes with multiple positions: the *kilayim* argument cannot definitively prove that the *Kohanim* did not sleep on the garments, because (a) the ordinary *Kohen*'s belt may not have been *shatnez*; (b) even if it was, they may have excluded it from what they placed beneath their heads; (c) even if they included it, the stiffness of the fabric may have resolved the rabbinic concern. Whether *Bigdei Kehunah nitnu lhenot bahen* when in the *Mikdash* — and whether the *Kohanim* literally used their garments as pillows — remains a matter of genuine discussion.

## Ba'al Keri Protocol

### Tunnel Route

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵירַע קֶרִי בְּאֶחָד מֵהֶן. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר: מְחִילּוֹת לֹא נִתְקַדְּשׁוּ. בַּעַל קֶרִי מִשְׁתַּלֵּחַ חוּץ לִשְׁנֵי מַחֲנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches (25b): If **a seminal emission befell one of** the priests and rendered him ritually impure, he would leave the Chamber of the Hearth and he would walk through the circuitous passage that extended beneath the Temple, as he could not pass through the Temple courtyard, due to his impurity. The Gemara notes that this mishna **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who says:** The **tunnels** beneath the Temple Mount **were not sanctified,** neither with the sanctity of the Temple courtyard nor with the sanctity of the Temple Mount. The Gemara cites a related statement of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **A man who experienced a seminal emission is sent outside of two camps,** the camp of the Divine Presence and the camp of the Levites. Accordingly, he may not remain in the Temple courtyard, which has the status of the camp of the Divine Presence, nor on the Temple Mount, which has the status of the camp of the Levites.

The *Gemara* now turns to what happened if one of the *Kohanim* sleeping in the *Beis HaMoked* became a *ba'al keri* — experienced a nocturnal emission. He would exit through a tunnel, immerse in a *mikvah*, and wait until morning.

### Unconsecrated Tunnels

*Mesayei lei l'Rebbi Yochanan d'amar mechilos lo niskadshu* — this supports Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that the underground tunnels were not consecrated with *kedushat Mikdash*. The very fact that the *ba'al keri* would route himself through the tunnel, rather than walking out openly, demonstrates that the tunnel offered a solution — and it could only offer a solution if the tunnels lacked the sanctified status of the areas above. Had the tunnels shared the *kedushah* of *Har Habayit*, passing through them would not have helped.

### Two Camps Rule

The procedure also establishes a second principle: *u'ba'al keri mishtale'ach chutz l'shnei machanos* — a *ba'al keri* is required to leave two camps. In the *Mishkan* in the wilderness, there were three concentric zones: the *Machaneh Kehunah* (the *Mishkan* itself), the *Machaneh Leviyah* (the *Levi'im* encamped around it), and the *Machaneh Yisrael* (the broader Israelite camp). These translate in *Yerushalayim* as follows: the *Azarah* corresponds to *Machaneh Shechina*; *Har Habayit* (including the *Ezrat Nashim*) corresponds to *Machaneh Leviyah*; and *Yerushalayim* itself corresponds to *Machaneh Yisrael*. The *metzora* — the most severe form of *tumah* — must leave all three camps, as the *Torah* says *badad yeisheiv*, he dwells completely alone outside the camp. The *zav*, *zavah*, *niddah*, and — as *Chazal* understand — the *ba'al keri* as well (*tumah yozei migufo*) are sent out of two camps: they may not be on *Har Habayit* (*Machaneh Leviyah*), though they remain within the city of *Yerushalayim*. The entire tunnel procedure thus demonstrates that even the *ba'al keri*, sleeping in the outer half of the *Beis HaMoked* which carried only *kedushat Har Habayit*, was nonetheless required to leave *Machaneh Leviyah*.

### Tevul Yom Status

After immersing in the *mikvah*, the *ba'al keri* becomes a *tevul yom* — one who has immersed but for whom nightfall has not yet arrived. A *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri* is also prohibited on *Har Habayit*, just as the *tevul yom* of a *zav*, *zavah*, or *niddah* is prohibited there. The *Gemara* does not enter into the question of whether this prohibition is *d'Oraita* or rabbinic. The simplest explanation for why he was permitted to remain until morning — once the immersion was complete — is that the *tevul yom*'s prohibition on *Har Habayit* is only rabbinic in nature, and therefore it was not enforced in a way that required him to remain outside for the entire night.

### Beit Kisei Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַנֵּרוֹת דּוֹלְקִין מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And the lamps were burning on this** side **and on that** side of the passage…and there was a bathroom of honor in the Chamber of Immersion.

The tunnel was lit with candles on both sides as the *Kohen* made his way through it. At the far end was the *beis kisei shel kavod* — the bathroom of dignity. Its *kavod* consisted precisely in its having a door. A locked door signaled that someone was present inside, and no one would enter an occupied bathroom. This was the standard of privacy maintained in the *Mikdash* itself.

## Rav Safra Story

### Snort Signal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב סָפְרָא הֲוָה יָתֵיב בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, אֲתָא רַבִּי אַבָּא נְחַר לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵיעוּל מָר! בָּתַר דְּנָפֵיק, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: עַד כָּאן לָא סְלֵיקְתְּ לְשֵׂעִיר, גְּמַרְתְּ מִילֵּי דְּשֵׂעִיר?! לָאו הָכִי תְּנַן: מְצָאוֹ נָעוּל – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם אָדָם! לְמֵימְרָא דְּלָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמֵיעַל! וְרַב סָפְרָא סָבַר: דִּלְמָא מְסוּכָּן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Safra was sitting in the bathroom** when **Rabbi Abba came** along. Since there was no door, Rabbi Abba **coughed** outside **to** alert anyone within of his presence and thereby inquire whether he could enter. Rav Safra **said to** Rabbi Abba: **Enter, Master,** and Rabbi Abba therefore entered the bathroom. **When he came out, Rabbi Abba said to** Rav Safra: **Until now,** although you have traveled widely, **you have never entered Seir,** the land of the Edomites, who behave immodestly. Nevertheless, **you have learned the ways of Seir. Didn't we learn this** in the mishna: If one **found** the door **closed, it was known that there was a person there,** and one would wait for him to exit before entering. This serves **to say that** a person **should not enter** the bathroom while another person is inside. Therefore, Rav Safra should not have told Rabbi Abba to enter. The Gemara explains that **Rav Safra** told Rabbi Abba to enter because he **thought: Perhaps** Rabbi Abba **is in danger.**

The *Gemara* now applies this last detail to a contemporary episode. *Rav Safra hava yativ b'veit hakeisei* — Rav Safra was sitting in a bathroom. Rav Abba came and snorted — *nachar lei*. This was apparently the accepted signal: since one does not speak in a bathroom, one would snort, and an occupant would snort back to indicate the facility was in use. Rav Safra, however, did not snort back — he spoke. Not only that, but he said *leieil mar* — please come in.

### Rebuke

After Rav Safra emerged, Rav Abba rebuked him: *ad kan lo salikat l'Seir v'gamart milei d'Seir* — you have never been to Seir and yet you act like a Seirite. The reference to Seir — whether to Rome or to some other people known for boorish behavior — was a pointed insult: you have adopted the ways of the immodest without even going there. *Hachi tnan* — did we not learn in our *Mishnah* in *Tamid*: *matzo naul beyadua sheyeish sham adam* — if the door was found locked, it was known that someone was inside. Our very *Mishnah* reflects the principle that one does not intrude upon another in a bathroom. That is the behavior which *Tamid* calls *beis kisei shel kavod* — privacy as a form of dignity.

### Safra's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב סָפְרָא סָבַר: דִּלְמָא מְסוּכָּן הוּא. כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: עַמּוּד הַחוֹזֵר – מֵבִיא אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי הִדְרוֹקָן, סִילוֹן הַחוֹזֵר – מֵבִיא אֶת הָאָדָם לִידֵי יֵרָקוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Safra **thought: Perhaps** Rabbi Abba **is in danger. Rav Safra was concerned that if Rabbi Abba waited for him to exit, Rabbi Abba might jeopardize his health, **as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A column** of feces **that is held back,** because one refrains from relieving himself, **causes a person to** suffer from **edema [*hidrokan*]. A stream** of urine **that is held back causes a person to** suffer from **jaundice [*yerakon*].**

Rav Safra defended himself: *dilma mesukan hu* — he was afraid the other might be in danger. If someone urgently needed to relieve himself and was forced to wait, Rav Safra was prepared to waive his own *kavod* and dignity rather than allow the other person to suffer potential harm. As the following *Baraisa* makes clear, this concern is medically serious.

## Bathroom Etiquette

### Health Consequences

The *Gemara* continues with the medical observations: *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer*: *amud hachozer meivi et ha'adam lidei hadrokan* — the pillar that returns (meaning, the feces that is withheld and drawn back) leads a person to a condition of *hadrokan*, a painful abdominal illness, understood to include hemorrhoids. *Silon hachozer meivi et ha'adam lidei yarkon* — withholding urination leads to *yarkon*, jaundice. These medical observations explain why Rav Safra considered the situation potentially dangerous and why he was willing to compromise his modesty.

### Optimal Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְחִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ, וְכֵן אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ: חַשֵּׁיךְ תַּקֵּין נַפְשָׁךְ, וּקְדֵים תַּקֵּין נַפְשָׁךְ – כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּרְחַק. תּוּב וְגַלִּי. כַּסִּי וְקוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to his son Ḥiyya, and likewise Rav Huna said to his son Rabba: Relieve yourself** when it **gets dark, and relieve yourself before** daybreak, even if you have no particular need to do so. The reason is that the streets are mostly empty at these times, and one can relieve himself near his home without concern that he might be seen. This is important, **so that** you **will not** have to relieve yourself during the day, when the streets are full, and you will be compelled to retain your feces while you **distance yourself,** which is liable to jeopardize your health. Furthermore, when relieving yourself, you should behave modestly. **Sit** down first **and** only then **uncover** yourself; afterward, **cover** yourself first **and** only then **stand up.**

*Amar lei Rav l'Chiya breih*, and similarly *amar lei Rav Huna l'Rabbah breih*: *chashich takin nafshach, v'kadim takin nafshach* — when it gets dark, take care of yourself; and when you rise early in the morning, take care of yourself. The reasoning: at nightfall, when people have returned home, and in the early morning, before they have gone out to work, one can relieve oneself close to home without embarrassment and without having to venture far into a forest or field. *Ki heichi d'lo tirchak* — so that you do not have to go far. Traveling deep into a forest or isolated area creates its own dangers, both in terms of physical safety and in terms of the discomfort that leads to illness when one avoids going to the bathroom because the journey is too inconvenient. By timing these activities well, *shalom al Yisrael* — one avoids endangering oneself altogether.

When one actually uses the bathroom: *pra v'sheiv* — first sit, then uncover yourself. Do not expose yourself and then sit. *Kasi v'kum* — cover yourself before standing up. One remains covered whenever one is on one's feet. The principle is that exposure occurs only while seated.

### Cup Hygiene

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁטוֹף וּשְׁתִי. [שְׁטוֹף] וְאַחֵית. וּכְשֶׁאַתָּה שׁוֹתֶה מַיִם – שְׁפוֹךְ מֵהֶן, וְאַחַר כָּךְ תֵּן לְתַלְמִידֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to drinking, these *amora'im* instructed their sons: When you drink wine, **rinse** the cup first **and** only then **drink** from it; after you drink, **rinse** the cup **and** only then **set** it back in its place. **But when you drink water,** it is not necessary to rinse the cup afterward; rather, **pour** out some **of the** water to rinse the rim of the cup, **and afterward you may give** the cup **to your student,** if he wants to drink.

*Shtof u'shetei* — rinse a cup before drinking. *Shtof ve'achit* — and rinse it after putting it down, for the benefit of whoever uses it next. One does not know whether the previous user was as careful about cleanliness; one should likewise not assume the next user will be. General hygienic consideration for others governs these practices.

*Shfoch mehen ve'achar kach ten letalmeidecha* — when sharing a cup of water with students, first pour some out, and then give it to them. The *Rosh* explains: a teacher would naturally share a cup with students as a form of *shirayim*, an honor conferred by passing one's cup. But the students would then be drinking from the part of the rim touched by the teacher's lips. By spilling some water from the edge, one effectively rinses the lip of the cup and spares the students from ingesting what had accumulated there.

### Death Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם מַיִם וְיִתֵּן לְתַלְמִידוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן שָׁפַךְ מֵהֶן. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׁתָה מַיִם וְלֹא שָׁפַךְ מֵהֶן, וְנָתַן לְתַלְמִידוֹ, וְאוֹתוֹ תַּלְמִיד אִיסְטְנִיס הָיָה, וְלֹא רָצָה לִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמֵת בַּצָּמָא. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם מַיִם וְיִתֵּן לְתַלְמִידוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן שָׁפַךְ מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A person should not drink water and give** the remaining water **to his student, unless he** first **poured** some **of it** out. **And** there was **an incident involving a certain** individual **who drank water and did not pour** some **of it** out, **and he gave** the cup **to his student. And that student was a delicate person [*istenis*], and** due to his sensitivity **he did not want to drink** from the cup, **and he died of thirst. At that time,** the Sages **said: A person should not drink water and give** the remaining water **to his student unless** he first **poured** some **of it** out.

*Uma'aseh be'echad sheshata mayim velo shafach mehen venatan letalmido* — there was once a case in which someone drank water and did not pour any out before passing the cup to his student. *Ve'oto talmid istanis haya* — that particular student was extremely fastidious. *Velo ratza lishtot* — he refused to drink. *Umet batzama* — and he died of thirst. This is a striking *Gemara*. The story implies there was no other water available and that the student would not pour out the water himself — presumably because doing so might have appeared disrespectful toward his teacher. The lesson drawn is severe: *be'ota sha'ah amru, lo yishte adam mayim veyiten letalmido ela im ken shafach mehen* — from that moment it was declared that one must pour out first before sharing.

### Respect Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הִילְכָּךְ, הַאי תַּלְמִידָא דְּשָׁפֵיךְ קַמֵּי רַבֵּיהּ – לֵית בֵּיהּ מִשּׁוּם אַפְקִירוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Therefore,** in the case of **this student who pours** water from the cup that his teacher drank from first, even if he does so **in the presence of his teacher,** his actions **are not** prohibited **due to disrespect [*afkiruta*].**

*Rav Ashi amar*: *hilkach hai talmida deshaffich kamey rabbeih, leit beih mishum apikureita* — therefore a student who pours out water before his master should not be considered to have committed *apikureita*. The term *apikureita*, as *Sanhedrin* defines it, refers to disrespect toward Torah scholars. One might worry that spilling water in the presence of one's *Rebbe* looks like an act of contempt. Rav Ashi clarifies: it is nothing of the sort. The student does it for cleanliness, not out of disrespect, and it is an act that saves lives. There is no insult in it, and therefore no concern.
