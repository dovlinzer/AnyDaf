# Ketubot 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Women, No Ketubah

### Mema'enes Definition

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 100b opens with the case of the *mema'enes* — the minor girl who refuses her husband. When a girl's father was absent, her mother and brothers were permitted by rabbinic enactment to marry her off while she was still underage. This institution, known as *kiddushei ketanah*, was established not as a financial arrangement but as a form of protection: the Rabbis were concerned that without a father present, a young girl would be vulnerable to exploitation. Crucially, the *kiddushin* in such a case is only rabbinic in force, not biblical, and the *nisuin* — the full consummation of the marriage — would ordinarily not take place until after she became a *bat mitzvah*. Since the marriage was not biblically binding, she retained the right to refuse at any point before she came of age. When she exercises that right of *mi'un* — refusal — the marriage is retroactively annulled, as though it had never taken place.

### Shniya Category

The second woman listed is the *shniya*, meaning a woman who is forbidden as a "secondary" relation — not a biblically defined relative, but one prohibited by rabbinic extension of the incest laws, as elaborated in Masechet Yevamos. She is not biblically forbidden; the prohibition is purely rabbinic, covering degrees of relationship removed enough that the Torah itself does not address them. Nevertheless, the Rabbis treated this category with seriousness, and the consequences for entering such a marriage are severe, as the *mishnah* goes on to describe.

### Ailonis Definition

The third case is the *ailonis* — a woman who not only is infertile but exhibits what the Rabbis understood to be physiologically male characteristics, indicating that she never developed normally as a female. The term itself derives from *ayil*, the ram, reflecting this association with maleness. The key halachic significance of this category is that it is treated as a *mekach ta'ut* — a mistaken transaction — on the theory that had the husband known she was an *ailonis*, he never would have agreed to marry her. There is therefore a presumption that the marriage was entered under a fundamental error.

### No Ketubah Rationale

In all three of these cases — the *mema'enes*, the *shniya*, and the *ailonis* — the *mishnah* rules that these women receive no *ketubah*. The reasoning differs by case. The *mema'enes* receives no *ketubah* because her *mi'un* retroactively erases the marriage entirely; there was never, in the end, a marriage at all, and therefore no *ketubah* obligation was ever created. The *ailonis*, similarly, exits through the logic of *mekach ta'ut* — the marriage was a transaction entered in error, and so likewise never legally materialized. The *shniya* is the most interesting case: the marriage was in fact binding, but the Rabbis, wishing to discourage such unions, strip her of her *ketubah* as a punitive measure. If the woman knows that there is no *ketubah* awaiting her when she leaves, the hope is that she will be less inclined to enter the forbidden marriage in the first place.

### Rashi: Peiros/Mezonos

The *mishnah* adds that not only do these women forfeit the *ketubah*, they also lose *peiros*, *mezonos*, and *bla'os*. Rashi explains that in this context *peiros* does not refer to the husband's right to enjoy the income from his wife's property — which is his benefit — but rather to what the wife is normally entitled to receive in exchange, namely the right to be redeemed if she is taken captive. *Mezonos* refers to her ongoing food support; she is not entitled to it, and indeed if she borrowed money to cover her food expenses during the marriage she cannot seek reimbursement. *Bla'os* — worn-out or consumed property — means either, as Tosafot reads it, that she cannot be reimbursed for property she brought into the marriage that was consumed during it, or, as Rashi reads it, that she cannot even take with her property that is still present but has become worn. The general thrust is that she walks away with nothing — an outcome even more severe than the departure of a freed slave.

### Biblical Prohibition Par…

The end of the *mishnah*, however, is striking. The *mishnah* lists cases of biblically forbidden marriages — a *kohen gadol* marrying a widow (*almanah*), an ordinary *kohen* marrying a divorcée or a woman who performed *chalitzah*, and a *mamzeret* or *nesinah* marrying a *yisrael* — and rules that in all these cases, even though the marriage is prohibited by the Torah itself, *yesh lahen ketubah*: these women do receive a *ketubah*. This is genuinely puzzling. One would think that the Rabbis' institution of the *ketubah*, designed to protect the wife and structure the marriage, would be the last thing one would apply to a biblically prohibited union. Extending the *ketubah* to such a marriage seems almost to confer on it a stamp of rabbinic recognition, even legitimacy.

### Rashi: Rabbinic vs. Bibl…

Rashi's explanation addresses this paradox directly, and it rests on a specific sociological assumption about who is the driving force behind each type of forbidden marriage. In the case of the *shniya*, a rabbinically forbidden marriage, Rashi reasons that the woman is unlikely to be deterred by the prohibition on her own, because the consequences fall lightly on her: her personal status is unaffected, she is not rendered forbidden to a *kohen*, and her children's status is not impaired. She therefore has an incentive to pursue the marriage and, in the Rabbis' framing, to seduce or persuade the man into it. The penalty of losing the *ketubah* is thus directed at her as a disincentive.

But in a biblically forbidden marriage — say, a *kohen gadol* marrying a widow — the woman faces real consequences. Rashi explains: *mipnei she-hi psulah v'valadah pasul al yedei nisuin* — the marriage renders her a *chalalah* and her children *chalalim*, which is a serious degradation of her and her offspring's status. She therefore has no reason to pursue such a marriage; the one with an incentive is the man, since his own status is not compromised. Thus the financial burden of the *ketubah* is placed on him — not as an endorsement of the marriage, but as a disincentive targeted at the party who might otherwise be inclined to enter it.

This explanation is elegant but not entirely satisfying. As the discussion in the shiur makes clear, not all the cases listed involve *kohanim*, so the status-change argument does not universally apply. More broadly, there is something genuinely strange about the Rabbis institutionalizing the *ketubah* within a biblically prohibited marriage. The *ketubah* is an ongoing legal instrument that sustains the structure of the marriage relationship. Attaching it to a biblically forbidden union does seem to implicitly recognize and reinforce that relationship in a way that sits uncomfortably alongside the prohibition. The contrast is almost ironic: the rabbinically forbidden marriage loses the *ketubah*, while the biblically forbidden one retains it. One might say the Rabbis were using the *ketubah* purely as a practical device to affect behavior, not as an expression of approval — but once one says the marriage is binding, the force of that logic becomes harder to maintain cleanly.

There is also a separate question, not resolved in this *mishnah*, of whether the husband in a biblically forbidden binding marriage has an ongoing obligation to pay *mezonot* during the marriage itself. It is possible that other *gemarot* would say he does not — since requiring him to sustain the marriage financially would be a much more direct form of support for it. The *mishnah*'s ruling about the *ketubah* on divorce is one thing; the question of ongoing obligations during the marriage is another.

## Rav vs. Shmuel Debate

### Rav: No Ketubah

The *gemara* now turns to a dispute between Rav and Shmuel regarding the *ketanah* — the minor girl — who finds herself exiting a rabbinic marriage. Rav's version of the *mishnah* was more expansive than Shmuel's: Rav held that not only does the *mema'enes* — the girl who refuses — receive no *ketubah*, but even a *ketanah* who was divorced by her husband while still a minor receives no *ketubah* either. The reasoning is that Rav views the entire institution as so fundamentally not-a-marriage that even when the husband goes through the formal process of giving a *get*, the transaction is treated as having been essentially play-acting. If the marriage never rose to the level of a real marriage, the *get* cannot transform it into one retroactively. And since there was never a real marriage, there is no *ketubah*.

### Shmuel: Miaun vs. Get

Shmuel, by contrast, draws a sharp distinction. He teaches: *mema'enes ein lah ketubah* — the girl who refuses gets no *ketubah*, because her *mi'un* retroactively uproots the marriage. But *yotzei beget yesh lah ketubah* — if the husband gave her a *get* while she was still a *ketanah*, she does receive a *ketubah*. The reason is that in Shmuel's view, the *get* is not a nullification of the marriage — it is a termination of one. A *mi'un* says: there never was a marriage. A *get* says: there was a marriage, and now it is over. Even if the marriage was only rabbinic in origin, it was real while it lasted, and the *get* that ends it entitles the woman to the protections that normally accompany divorce.

Shmuel draws out the full implications of this distinction: after *mi'un*, the man is permitted to marry her relatives and she is permitted to marry his, since retroactively they were never husband and wife. She is also not *pesulah* — not disqualified from marrying a *kohen* — because *mi'un* leaves no trace of a prior marriage. But if he gave her a *get*: *paslah min ha-achim* — she may not marry his brothers — and *paslah min ha-kehunah* — she is halachically a divorcée and may not marry a *kohen*. Even though it was only a rabbinic marriage and a rabbinic divorce, Shmuel insists that these consequences follow as they would from any recognized marital dissolution.

### Shmuel: Three-Month Wait

Shmuel adds a further ruling: a *mema'enes* does not need to wait three months before remarrying, while a *ketanah* who received a *get* does. The three-month waiting period exists to prevent uncertainty about the paternity of a potential child. One might think the concern is irrelevant in the case of a *ketanah*, since we do not expect a minor girl to conceive. Rashi addresses this with a *lo plug* rationale — the Rabbis did not subdivide their enactment: wherever there was a genuine marriage and a genuine divorce, even a rabbinic one, the three-month waiting period applies, regardless of whether pregnancy is practically possible. The analogy is to a post-menopausal woman who converts together with her husband — the Rabbis still require the three-month separation even when conception is demonstrably impossible, because the rule was applied broadly across all cases of recognized marital dissolution. The *mema'enes*, by contrast, never had a recognized marriage at all, so no waiting period applies. This ruling — the three-month requirement for a *ketanah* who received a *get* — was the specific *chiddush* that Shmuel was adding beyond what was already known from earlier sources.

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

The *gemara* asks whether this dispute between Rav and Shmuel can be traced back to a *tanaitic* disagreement, and it finds one. A *baraita* records an explicit debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua on exactly this question.

### Eliezer: No Effect

Rabbi Eliezer holds: *ein maaseh ketanah klum* — the legal acts of a *ketanah* are without effect. This sweeping statement encompasses the entire rabbinic institution of *kiddushei ketanah* when performed by the mother and brothers rather than the father. Because the father is absent and the marriage was not biblically authorized, Rabbi Eliezer treats it as non-marriage. The husband is not entitled to her found objects, her earnings, or the annulment of her vows; he does not inherit her; if he is a *kohen* he does not become *tamei* upon her death. *Klalo shel davar* — the summary principle — is that she is not his wife in any legal sense. The sole *halacha* that does apply is that she requires *mi'un* to exit: either she must affirmatively refuse before becoming a *bat mitzvah*, or else, if she remains with him past that point, she becomes his wife fully. But while she is a *ketanah*, nothing about the arrangement constitutes marriage.

### Yehoshua: Binding

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees fundamentally: *maaseh ketanah klum* — the acts of a *ketanah* do mean something. He holds that this rabbinic marriage is a genuine marriage in every respect. The husband is entitled to her found objects, her earnings, and the annulment of her vows; he inherits her; he becomes *tamei* to her. *Harei hi k'ishto l'chol davar* — she is his wife in every legal dimension. The one difference between her status and that of a fully adult wife is that she can exit by *mi'un* alone, without requiring a *get*. For an adult woman, only a *get* terminates the marriage; for this *ketanah*, *mi'un* suffices. But absent *mi'un*, the marriage is fully in force.

### Amoraim Align with Tanna…

The *gemara* then maps the *amoraic* dispute onto the *tanaitic* one. Rav, who says that even a *get* does not give the *ketanah* any *ketubah*, follows Rabbi Eliezer: since the marriage was never real, giving a *get* changes nothing about its essential non-status, and no *ketubah* was ever owed. Shmuel, who distinguishes between *mi'un* and *get*, follows Rabbi Yehoshua: since the marriage was real — rabbinically binding and carrying all the normal marital consequences — a *get* is a genuine termination of a genuine marriage, and the *ketubah* is owed.

The *gemara* refines this, however. It notes that according to Rabbi Eliezer, there is no debate to be had: if the marriage was never real, obviously neither *mi'un* nor *get* changes that, and obviously there is no *ketubah*. The real dispute occurs *aliba deRabbi Yehoshua* — within the framework of Rabbi Yehoshua, who agrees the marriage was real. Shmuel simply applies Rabbi Yehoshua's view consistently: real marriage, real divorce, real *ketubah*. But Rav, even granting Rabbi Yehoshua's framework, introduces an asymmetry: Rabbi Yehoshua only said the marriage is real *mididah l'didei* — in the direction from her to him, meaning the husband enjoys all the marital benefits that flow from her. He did not say the marriage is real *mididei l'didah* — in the direction from him to her, meaning she is entitled to extract financial obligations from him. The *ketubah*, which flows from husband to wife, falls into that second category, and Rav denies it.

## Asymmetrical Marriage Ri…

### Rashi: Husband Incentive

Rashi explains the logic behind Rav's asymmetrical position. The institution of *kiddushei ketanah* by the mother and brothers was created entirely for the girl's benefit — to protect her in the absence of a father. But for the institution to function, a man had to be willing to enter it. Why would a man agree to marry a minor girl under these circumstances, especially given that, as Rashi notes, *ki heichi delo limnu mil'misbah* — we need to ensure that men not refrain from marrying her? An arrangement that gives him nothing while requiring financial obligations from him would provide no incentive whatsoever.

### Mother's Protection Moti…

The mother's motive for arranging such a marriage is clear: she either cannot afford to support another child, or she fears the girl will be taken advantage of without male protection. Rashi cites the formulation from the relevant chapter in Yevamos: *shema yinhagu bah minhag hefker* — lest people treat her as ownerless, with no one to protect her — *she'ein ketanah yoda'at lishmor atzmah me'lehitpatot* — since a minor girl does not know how to protect herself from being seduced. The marriage is thus a protective institution, not primarily a financial one. The girl moves into the man's household, where he provides for and protects her, until she comes of age and the marriage becomes fully binding.

But the critical asymmetry in the arrangement is this: even once he takes her in and fulfills this protective role, she retains the right to walk away at any moment before she becomes a *bat mitzvah*. Unlike a marriage arranged by her father, which is biblically binding and locks in the arrangement, this rabbinic marriage carries no guarantee. The man can perform his obligations for years and then have her exercise *mi'un* and leave. Given this uncertainty, the only way to make the institution viable — the only way to give men any incentive to enter into it at all — is to grant the husband the full range of marital benefits from her side of the equation.

### Rav's Asymmetry Logic

This is Rav's logic: we give the man everything that flows from her to him — her earnings, her found objects, her *peiros*, inheritance — because without that incentive no one would enter this arrangement, and the institution would fail in its purpose of protecting vulnerable minor girls. But we do not require him to pay the *ketubah* upon divorce, which is a financial cost that flows from him to her, because that cost is borne precisely in consideration of a real, locked-in marriage — and this arrangement is neither locked in nor guaranteed to become permanent. Rav may even concede, in other respects, that rabbinic consequences of the marriage apply — that she is *pesulah la-kehunah* after a *get*, that the relatives cannot intermarry — while still maintaining that the *ketubah*, as a particular institution requiring specific justification, was never attached to this arrangement.

## Blaos Doctrine

### Shmuel on Milog

The *mishnah* also states that these women do not receive *bla'os* — worn or consumed property. Rav Huna bar Chiya reports a ruling in the name of Shmuel that clarifies the scope of this provision: *lo shanu ela nichsei milog* — the rule that she does not recover property applies only to *nichsei milog*, the property over which she retained title throughout the marriage. *Nichsei milog* is property she brought into the marriage that was never formally transferred to the husband — the Ferrari in the marriage, so to speak, still legally hers, though he had the right to enjoy its use (*ochel peirot*). Normally, even if such property was consumed or destroyed during the marriage, she would be entitled to recover its value upon divorce, since it was always her principal. But in these cases, she is denied even that recovery.

By contrast, says Shmuel, *nichsei tzon barzel* — the dowry that she formally assigned to him and that was recorded in the *ketubah* — she does recover. *Nichsei tzon barzel* is property over which the husband received legal title: she brought in the Jaguar as a dowry, it became his, but the obligation was that upon divorce he must return it or its equivalent value. In these cases, that obligation remains in force.

This ruling is counterintuitive. One would expect the reverse: that she recover the property over which she always held title (*nichsei milog*) and lose the property whose title had passed to him (*nichsei tzon barzel*). That she loses what was hers and keeps what was his seems backwards.

### Pappa's Question

Rav Pappa asks: on which case in the *mishnah* does Shmuel's ruling apply? The *mishnah* addresses the *mema'enes*, the *ailonis*, and the *shniya* — and each case presents different problems for Shmuel's formulation.

### Ailonit Logic

Consider the *ailonis* case. The marriage was a *mekach ta'ut* — a mistaken transaction. If the property is still present, she should simply walk away with all of it: there was no marriage, it was all hers. If it has been consumed or destroyed, the question is whether he was ever legitimately entitled to use it. The logic would seem to say: as for the *nichsei milog* — the property that was always legally hers — he was never entitled to sell or consume it without her consent, so if he did, he owes her reimbursement. As for the *nichsei tzon barzel* — the property over which he held title during what they both thought was a marriage — he was entitled at the time to treat it as his, and she has no legal instrument, namely no *ketubah*, to compel him to repay it. So the *ailonis* case suggests she should recover *nichsei milog* and lose *nichsei tzon barzel* — exactly the reverse of Shmuel's ruling.

### Shniya Reciprocal Penalty

The *gemara* therefore concludes that Shmuel's ruling applies to the *shniya* case. Here the reasoning is explicitly punitive: *deknasu Rabbanan ledida bedidei uledidei bedida* — the Rabbis penalized each party through the other's entitlements. Normally she would recover her *nichsei milog*, since it was always her property. The Rabbis take that away from her as a penalty. Normally he would retain the *nichsei tzon barzel*, since title had passed to him. The Rabbis take that away from him and give it to her. Everyone loses something they were entitled to. The psychology is that the very deprivation of something one has a right to serves as a genuine disincentive — it is not merely a neutral reallocation but an experienced loss. Whether this fully explains the ruling or not, the *gemara* accepts it as the operative logic for the *shniya*.

### Cloak as Principal

From Rav Kahana's discussion of this ruling, the *gemara* draws a broader principle about consumable property in marriage. The case of a cloak brought into the marriage illustrates the point. One might think that when a wife brings a cloak into a marriage, we should conceptualize it as simply providing ten years of *peiros* — ten years of use — with no separate *keren*, no principal, that she is entitled to recover. On this view, the husband uses the cloak, it wears out, and there is nothing left for her to claim, because there was no conceptual principal separate from the use.

But this is not the *halacha*. The *gemara* infers from Rav Kahana's formulation that *karna havei* — a cloak brought into a marriage is treated as a principal, not merely as a stream of fruit. The husband cannot simply use it until it is worn out. The proper procedure is to sell it, convert the proceeds into land, and derive the *peiros* from the land's income — leaving an intact principal for her to recover. The existence of a *ketubah* obligation in a normal marriage reflects precisely this understanding: even consumables brought into a marriage are conceptualized as principal that she is entitled to take back out.

### Nachman Disagrees

Rav Nachman, however, disagrees. He holds that *peira havei* — a consumable brought into the marriage is treated as *peiros*, not as principal. In his view, when she brings a cloak, it is simply ten years of use, and the husband is entitled to wear it until it wears out, with nothing owed when it is gone. The *gemara* notes that this position would contradict the implication of the earlier ruling, since the whole discussion of *bla'os* and recovery of consumed property presupposes that there is a principal to be recovered in the first place. Rav Nachman's position disagrees with that framework.

## Supplementary Ketubah

### Shmuel: Base vs. Tosefes

A further ruling of Shmuel addresses the *tosefet ketubah* — the supplementary amount that a husband adds to the *ketubah* beyond what the Rabbis required. The base *ketubah* is *maneh* for a non-virgin or *masayim* for a virgin; this is the rabbinically mandated amount. But many husbands added more as a personal gift to their wives. Shmuel rules: *lo shanu ela maneh masayim* — when we say these women forfeit their *ketubah*, we mean only the base amount mandated by the Rabbis. *Aval tosefes yesh lahen* — they do retain the supplementary gift. The reason is that the *tosefes* was not a rabbinic institution; it was a private gift from husband to wife. Even if the rabbinic marriage is retroactively undone, even if she is penalized for having entered a forbidden union, the gift he gave her personally — in recognition of the fact that they would be living as husband and wife, whatever the legal status of that arrangement — remains hers. A *baraita* confirms this reading: *ein lahem maneh masayim aval tosefes yesh lahem* — they do not have the hundred or two hundred, but they do have the additional amount.

### Anti-Halachic Women

A different category of cases, however, receives harsher treatment. The *mishnah* elsewhere describes women who are divorced for actively violating the norms of Jewish law and marriage — women who behave in ways that constitute *overet al das*, going against the standards of conduct expected of a Jewish wife: flirting with other men, going out with her hair uncovered, feeding her husband non-*kosher* food. In those cases, not only does she forfeit the base *ketubah*, she forfeits the *tosefes* as well. The formulation there is not merely that she lacks a *ketubah* but that she must leave without one — a stronger formulation expressing active rabbinic censure of her conduct. *Ein lahem tosefes, v'chol sheken maneh masayim* — she loses the supplementary gift and certainly loses the mandated base amount.

### Adultery Case: Possessio…

A related but distinct case involves a woman who is divorced because of a *shem ra* — a suspicion of adultery. Even in this extreme case, the ruling is: *notelet mah she-lefaneha v'yotzet* — she takes whatever property is physically present before her and leaves with it. The focus here shifts from the *ketubah* — which she does not collect — to the physical property she owns that is still present. She brought certain items into the marriage; they are still in the house; she is entitled to take them with her even in disgrace.

### Huna: Adultery/Clothing

This ruling supports a position of Rav Huna, who states: *zinsah lo hifsidah bla'oseha* — even if a woman commits adultery, she does not forfeit her *bla'os*, her worn clothing and physical belongings that are still present. The act of adultery, however grave, does not strip her of ownership of her physical possessions.

### Clothing Forfeiture Deba…

A *tanna* taught before Rav Nachman an opposing view: *zinsah hifsidah bla'oseha kayamin* — if she commits adultery, she loses even her clothes that are still present. Rav Nachman's response is terse and pointed: *im hi zinsah, kalei mi zanai?* — if she committed adultery, did her clothing commit adultery? The penalty falls on her moral conduct; her physical property is an entirely separate matter. It would be a kind of absurdity to penalize inanimate objects for their owner's moral failing.

### Consensus: Property Pres…

The *gemara* resolves the dispute by attributing the stricter position — that she loses even her clothing — to a *stam baraita* in the name of Rabbi Menachem, a minority opinion. Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbah bar bar Chana confirms: *aval chachamim omrim, zinsah lo hifsidah bla'oseha kayamin* — the sages hold that even an adulteress does not forfeit the physical property that is still present and in her possession. The consensus of the *chachamim* is that whatever she may have done, any property over which she held title and which is still physically present remains hers.

## Knowledge Requirement

### Huna: Ailonit & Knowledge

Returning to the *ailonis*, the *mishnah* states: *im techilah nisa'ah l'shem ilonis* — if from the outset he married her knowing she was an *ailonis*, then *yesh lah ketubah* — she receives a *ketubah*. If he knew, it was not a *mekach ta'ut*; he entered with full knowledge and consented to this woman as his wife. Rav Huna draws a general principle from this: *ailonis isha v'einah isha* — an *ailonis* is in some respects his wife and in some respects not. The determining factor is his knowledge. *Heikir bah* — if he recognized her condition — *yesh lah ketubah*. *Lo heikir bah* — if he did not — *ein lah ketubah*.

### Almanah: No Knowledge Ne…

By contrast, Rav Huna holds that an *almanah* — a widow who married a *kohen gadol*, or analogously a divorcée who married an ordinary *kohen* — is *ishah gemurah*, fully his wife in every respect, and she always receives a *ketubah* regardless of whether the *kohen* knew of her status or not. In Rav Huna's reading, the biblical prohibition does not transform the marriage into a *mekach ta'ut*. She was not hiding anything about her personal nature — she is simply a widow, a status that does not affect her fitness as a woman but happens to disqualify her for a *kohen*. Whether he knew or not, the marriage was real, and she is entitled to the *ketubah*.

### Yehudah: Knowledge for B…

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. He holds: *achat zu v'achat zu isha v'einah isha* — the same rule applies to both the *ailonis* and the *almanah*. In both cases, knowledge is the determining factor. *Heikir bah, yesh lah ketubah; lo heikir bah, ein lah ketubah*. If the *kohen* did not know he was marrying a woman forbidden to him, then however we classify the marriage legally — whether or not it is technically binding — she is not entitled to a *ketubah*, because there was a fundamental failure of informed consent on his part.

### Metivta: Declaration vs.…

A *metivta* — a *yeshiva* tradition — challenges the position of Rav Huna on the *almanah*. The *baraita* states: *kinsah b'chezkat shehi ken v'nimtzat shehi ken, yesh lah ketubah* — if he married her in the presumption that she was an *almanah* and she turned out to be an *almanah*, she receives a *ketubah*. The implication appears to be that this is precisely because he knew; had he not known, and had she turned out to be an *almanah*, she would not receive a *ketubah*. A second *baraita* makes this fully explicit: *kinsah biyeduah v'nimtzas biyeduah, yesh lah ketubah* — he married her knowingly and she turned out as expected, she gets the *ketubah*. *Kinsah stam v'nimtzat ken* — he married her without any explicit discussion and she turned out to be an *almanah* or *gerushah* — *ein lah ketubah*. This is a direct *tiyuvta* — a refutation — of Rav Huna's position that an *almanah* always receives a *ketubah* regardless of the *kohen*'s knowledge.

The logic makes intuitive sense: why would we presume that a *kohen* would be content to marry a woman biblically forbidden to him if he did not know of the prohibition? Since we cannot presume such consent, the absence of knowledge makes it a *ta'ut*, and she is not entitled to a *ketubah*.

### Huna's Misreading

The *gemara* accounts for Rav Huna's error: *veRav Huna matnisin at'yei* — Rav Huna was misled by the *mishnah*. He observed that the *mishnah* explicitly introduced the knowledge distinction in the case of the *ailonis* — stating that if he knew she was an *ailonis* she gets a *ketubah*, implying that without knowledge she does not — but made no such explicit distinction in the case of the *almanah*, simply stating she gets a *ketubah*. He reasonably concluded that the *almanah*'s *ketubah* was unconditional. This was a defensible reading of the *mishnah* on its face.

### Resolution: Distinction…

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: when the *mishnah* moved from the *ailonis* to the *almanah*, it did so within the context of the distinction it had already established for the *ailonis*. The knowledge requirement was already on the table. When the *mishnah* then stated that the *almanah* receives a *ketubah*, it meant within that same framework — namely, that she receives it when he knew of her status, just as the *ailonis* does. The *mishnah* did not need to restate the distinction because it had already been set as the operative framework. Rav Huna read the silence as a contrast; the *gemara* reads it as continuation. In the end, both the *ailonis* and the *almanah* share the same rule: *yesh lah ketubah* only when he married her with knowledge of her status.
