# Nazir 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Unspecified Funds

### Intro: Pile of Funds

We pick up in the middle of 27b, at the point where *masiv Rava* — Rava raises a challenge. We are finally wrapping up the *sugya* concerning the pile of unspecified funds that goes toward an *olah* in the case of a *nazir*. Recall the background: the three *korbanot* of the *nazir* — the *shelamim*, the *chatas*, and the *olah* — could in principle come from a single pile of unspecified funds, and the *halacha* directs such funds toward the *olah*. We had been working through a long discussion about whether that *halacha* is limited to money or whether it applies to other objects as well — things like beams of wood, bars of precious metal, or animals with a blemish. Even within the position that it applies only to money, we did not take "money" entirely literally. The argument was that anything capable of being quickly and easily liquidated should be treated as equivalent to money — including, perhaps, blemished animals, which on account of their *kedusha* would presumably find a ready market and would not require an extended valuation process.

### Money vs. Objects

That is the backdrop for the current discussion. The question is whether blemished animals properly belong in the "money" category — that is, whether the *halacha* that unspecified funds go toward the *olah* would apply to them as well. If a person sanctified three blemished animals as non-specified funds for his *nezirut* and the *nezirut* was subsequently nullified, would those animals fall under this rule?

### Blemished Animals

Consider further: if one sanctified three cows — animals that cannot themselves be used as *korbanot* of a *nazir* — and then the person died or the *nezirut* was annulled, does the *halacha* that the unspecified *kedusha* devolves upon an *olah* apply? The answer would depend on whether those cows, blemished or otherwise inappropriate for sacrifice, are treated as equivalent to money or to specified sacrificial animals. This is the question one more attempt will try to resolve, before we proceed to the *mishna* on the following *daf*.

## Rava's Challenge

### Korban - Personal Sin

Rava raises a challenge from a *braita* that bears directly on this entire discussion. The *braita* draws on the *pasuk* describing the *chatas*: *nefesh ki techeta* — if a soul sins — *v'heivi korbano seirat izim al chatato asher chata* — he shall bring his *korban*, a she-goat, for the sin that he has sinned. The language of the verse is highly specific: it is his *korban*, for his sin. The *braita* proceeds to unpack that specificity at length.

*Bekorbano hu yotzei v'eino yotzei b'korban aviv* — a person fulfills his obligation with his own *korban*, not with his father's. This is the governing principle: a *chatas* is bound absolutely to the person who committed the transgression and to the specific sin for which it was designated. This principle, as we have been consistently noting, is why an animal sanctified as a *chatas* that cannot be used must be left to die — it cannot be redirected elsewhere.

There is, of course, a striking counterpoint in the law of *nezirut*: if a father sets aside an unspecified pile of money for his *nezirut* and then dies, a son who subsequently accepts *nezirut* with the express intention of using that money may in fact do so. The money may thus be redirected from father to son. But that is a special application of the rule of unspecified funds — and, crucially, it is only possible when the funds are *stumim*, unspecified, not when they have been designated for a particular *korban*. That distinction is central to everything that follows.

### Father's Chatas

The *braita* begins by positing the most apparently extreme case and then walking toward what might seem more lenient. Perhaps one cannot use his father's *korban* when the animal was set aside for a lighter category of sin and he wishes to apply it to a weightier category — *min hakal al hachamur* — or from a weightier to a lighter — *min hachamur al hakal*. In those cases the identity of the *korban* has been substantially altered, and clearly it cannot serve. But perhaps, the *braita* suggests, if one keeps it within the same category of sin — light for light, weighty for weighty — maybe using the father's *korban* for the son's equivalent obligation would be permissible?

*Talmud lomar: korbano* — the *pasuk* says "his," explicitly and deliberately. It is his *korban*, not his father's — even if the category of sin is identical.

### Chatas Specificity

The *braita* then introduces the next layer: *yachol lo yetze b'korban aviv uvehemah she-hifrish afilu min hakal al hakal min hachamur al hachamur* — perhaps it is clear that one cannot use his father's sacrifice even if it is within the same category, when the father actually sanctified a specific animal. That we understand. But what about a case where the father only set aside money, not a designated animal?

The justification for this concern is drawn from the *halacha* of *nezirut* itself. *She-harei adam megalayach al maot aviv b'nezirot bizman she-hen stumim v'lo bizman she-hen meforashim* — a person may use his father's money for his own *nezirut*, but only when those funds are *stumim*, unspecified. Once the father has designated a particular pile for a particular *korban* — this pile for the *chatas*, this pile for the *olah* — the son may not reassign it. The capacity to reassign exists only when the funds remain amorphous and undirected.

Nevertheless, the *braita* presses the question: since we do observe at least some flexibility with money in the context of *nezirut* — a son can use a father's unspecified funds — perhaps that flexibility extends to *chatas* money as well. If the father set aside money specifically designated for his *chatas* and died, perhaps the son can use it for the son's own *chatas*, on the theory that money is inherently more reassignable than a designated animal.

*Talmud lomar: korbano* — again the verse says "his." Even money that the father sanctified for a *chatas* cannot be used by the son. The *halacha* of unspecified funds permitting a son to use his father's money for *nezirut* does not extend to *chatas*. A *chatas* must be specifically his — from the outset, not inherited or redirected from someone else — whether the sanctified object is an animal or a pile of money.

### Own Money Limits

The *braita* now advances to a further question: what about reassigning one's own *chatas* funds? We have established that a person cannot use his father's *chatas* *korban* — not an animal, not money. But what about using his own money, sanctified for one *chatas*, for a different *chatas*?

*Yachol lo yetze b'behemah she-hifrish l'atzmo min hakal al hakal u-min hachamur al hachamur* — perhaps even for oneself, one cannot reassign an animal from one sin to another, even keeping it within the same category. And indeed the *braita* offers a demonstration of why not: if one set aside an animal for *chelev* and then slaughtered it with the intention of bringing it for *dam*, or vice versa — *sheharei lo ma'al v'lo kiper* — he has neither committed *me'ila* nor achieved atonement. He has not achieved atonement, because the animal cannot be reassigned from one sin to another; and because he has not succeeded in reassigning it, he has not technically misused *hekdesh* in the full sense. The attempted reassignment simply failed.

One might note that this argument is somewhat circular — it more assumes than proves the conclusion — but the *braita* takes it as established that an animal designated as a *chatas* for a specific sin cannot be reassigned even to a different sin of one's own.

This raises an interesting question: if I sanctified an animal for *chelev* and slaughtered it intending to bring it for *dam*, am I still *yotzei* for *chelev*? I have certainly not fulfilled my obligation for *dam*. But the animal was a *chatas* — just brought *shelo lishma*, for the wrong sin. The question of whether it might still satisfy the original designation is genuinely interesting, though we set it aside here.

The *braita* continues: *aval yotzei b'maot* — perhaps money, though, is more freely reassignable. If the father set aside money for *chelev* and the son wishes to use it for *dam*, or if a person set aside his own money for one *chatas* and wishes to redirect it to another — perhaps that works. The logic would be: *sheken im hifrish l'atzmo ma'ot min hachelev v'heivi al hadam... sheharei ma'al v'kiper* — because with money, if one were to redirect his *chatas* funds from one sin to another, he would actually have committed *me'ila* and achieved atonement. Unlike the animal case, where the reassignment simply fails, with money something seems to happen: an act of *me'ila* has occurred, and its effect may be to discharge the *kedusha* and allow the funds to be reapplied. The *talmud lomar: al chatato* — the verse says "for his sin," meaning *ad she-yehei korbano l'shum chatato* — his *korban* must be for the specific sin. Even money designated for one *chatas* cannot be redirected to another. The verse leaves no opening.

### Money & Me'ila

At this point, *Tosafot* provides an illuminating explanation of the logic underlying the *braita*'s argument about money and *me'ila*. *Tosafot* notes the correct textual reading: *she'ein mo'al v'kiper she'hame'ot ein bahem kedushat haguf* — the money does not possess *kedushat haguf*, intrinsic sanctity. This distinction is essential.

There are two levels of *kedusha* for objects dedicated to the *Beit HaMikdash*. *Kedushat haguf* refers to an object that is itself intrinsically sanctified — an animal designated as an *olah*, for instance, whose very body is consecrated for use. *Kedushat damim* refers to an object whose sanctity consists only in its monetary value — it is destined to be sold, with the proceeds used to acquire whatever is actually needed. Money set aside for a *korban* is *kedushat damim*: the money itself is not going to be burned on the altar; it will be used to purchase an animal.

This distinction bears directly on the laws of *me'ila*. When one misuses *hekdesh* in error — *be'shogeg* — the effect of that *me'ila* is to transfer the object out of *hekdesh*. But this desanctification operates differently for *kedushat haguf* versus *kedushat damim*. An object with *kedushat haguf*, even if misused, retains its intrinsic sanctity — one violation does not strip an *olah* of its identity as an *olah*. But an object with only *kedushat damim* is more loosely bound to the domain of *hekdesh*; it is essentially property of the *Beit HaMikdash* waiting to be liquidated. If such an object is misused *be'shogeg*, the act of *me'ila* effectively removes it from *hekdesh*, much as an act of theft removes something from its owner's domain.

Two conditions, then, govern whether *me'ila* results in desanctification: the misuse must be *be'shogeg* — not intentional — and the object must have *kedushat damim*, not *kedushat haguf*. Money designated for a *chatas* satisfies both: it has only *kedushat damim*, and if misused accidentally it would constitute *me'ila* that strips the *kedusha*. *Tosafot* articulates the inference: *dehazi lemifak l'chulin al yedei me'ila* — the money is susceptible to losing its *kedusha* through *me'ila*, which demonstrates that its *kedusha* is less permanently affixed to its identity. One might therefore argue that the *kedusha* is more fungible, and that a conscious reassignment to a different *chatas* should similarly be effective — since *be'shogeg* the act of reassignment would have worked, perhaps *be'mezid* it can work as well: *ho'il v'shayeich bo shinui b'shogeg*. This is the *braita*'s argument for why money *should* be reassignable to a different *chatas*.

The *talmud lomar: al chatato* closes the door even on this reasoning. Whatever the theoretical flexibility of money's *kedusha*, the verse insists that the *korban* must be designated for this specific sin. There is no room to reassign — not from father to son, not from one sin to another, whether the sanctified object is an animal or money.

It is worth noting the parallel this discussion bears to the concept of *shinui* in the laws of *geneiva*. When a thief effects a qualitative change — *shinui ma'aseh* or *shinui haguf* — in a stolen object, this can vest ownership in the thief under certain conditions. Here, the very act of *me'ila be'shogeg* functions as a kind of *shinui reshut*, a change of domain, that removes the object from *hekdesh*. There are *gemarot* that draw precisely this parallel between *ye'ush* and *shinui reshut* in the laws of theft and certain cases of *me'ila*.

One should also note that this *braita* is stylistically unmistakably *midrash halacha* — it moves verse by verse through the *pasuk*, deriving each successive rule from a subtle textual reading. While the *Gemara* does not signal whether a given *braita* derives from a *tosefta* or a *midrash halacha*, the sustained focus on the *pesukim* makes the origin here transparent.

### Braita Relevance

One might wonder what the relevance of this lengthy *braita* is to our original question about blemished animals and unspecified funds. The connection lies in one of the *braita*'s early moves: when considering whether money might be reassignable, the *braita* notes that a person can use his father's money for his own *nezirut* — but only when those funds are *stumim*, unspecified, *v'lo bizman she-hen meforashim*. The *braita* drew a contrast between money and animals: even in *nezirut*, a son cannot use his father's sanctified animal. It is only an unspecified pile of money that carries this reassignability.

That contrast — between money and animals in the context of what a son may use for his own *nezirut* — is what our *sugya* wants to analyze. The question is: when the *braita* says a son cannot use his father's animal, does *behemah* mean only the specifically designated unblemished sacrificial animals? Or does it also include blemished animals that cannot themselves be offered? The answer to that question determines whether blemished animals fall in the "money" category or the "animal" category for purposes of the *halacha* of unspecified funds.

## Objects = Money

### Blemished Animals?

The *Gemara* now draws on that line in the *braita* directly. *Katani miha* — at least the *braita* teaches: a son cannot reuse his father's animal for *nezirut*, but he can reuse his father's money. When the *braita* says "animal," does it mean *afilu ba'alet mum* — even a blemished animal? If so, then even blemished animals are not in the money category and cannot be reassigned — which would mean they do not fall under the *halacha* of unspecified funds going toward the *olah*.

The *Gemara* rejects this reading: *lo temimah* — no. When the *braita* said "animal" in the context of things a son cannot reassign for his own *nezirut*, it was referring to unblemished animals, the properly designated sacrificial animals. Those are the ones whose identity is so concretely fixed that they cannot be reassigned even in *nezirut*.

### All = Money

The *Gemara* then presses further: *aval ba'alet mum mai?* — but what do you want to say about blemished animals? Would you say *kistuma damya* — that they are treated like an unspecified pile of money and may be reassigned? If so, then why does the *braita* mention only money — *maot she-hifrish aviv* — as the category of things a son may use? Why not also mention blemished animals, or beams of wood, or any other non-sacrificial object?

The answer is straightforward: *hacha nami* — those other objects are equally included. *D'lamai khazya? Lidmai* — for what are they fit? For their monetary value, their proceeds. Anything that is not itself going to be used as the sacrifice — whether it is cash, a blemished animal, a beam of wood, or any other object — is fit only to be sold, with the proceeds going toward the ultimate purpose. In that sense, everything in this category is equivalent to money. *Demei hani maos* — it is all in the same category as the pile of money.

The *Gemara*'s method here is instructive. The difficulty throughout this long *sugya* arose because the *mishnayot* and *braitot* dealt with two poles — an amorphous pile of money on one side, and a concretely specified sanctified animal on the other — leaving ambiguous everything that falls between. The *Gemara* now resolves that ambiguity definitively: the critical distinction is not between money and animals in the abstract, but between objects that will themselves be used as *korbanot* and everything else. Everything else — regardless of its form — is treated as money, because its *kedusha* is *kedushat damim*, value-based rather than identity-based. The mention of money in the *braita* was representative, not exhaustive.

With that, the *Gemara* brings this extended *sugya* to a close, and we turn to a new *mishna*.

## Annulment at Sacrifice

### Blood Sprinkled

The final *mishna* of this section addresses the annulment of a woman's *nezirut* by her husband at an extremely inopportune moment — not merely after she has set aside funds or animals, but while the *korbanot* are actually being brought. The husband has been away; he returns, finds his wife concluding her *nezirut* at the *Beit HaMikdash*, and seeks to annul her vow on the spot. The day of his return is the *yom shmoa* — the day he first learned of the vow — and so according to the laws of annulment, this is in principle the day he may act.

*Nizrak aleha echad min hadamim eino yachol lehafer* — if the blood of one of the sacrifices has already been sprinkled on her behalf, the husband can no longer annul. The reason is not procedural inconvenience. After even one *korban* has been completed, the restrictions of *nezirut* fall away from her. She may immediately drink wine. Since the husband's power to annul is grounded in the *nazir* vow's character as *inuy nefesh* — an affliction of the soul, a category encompassing vows that restrict the woman's conduct or impact their relationship — once the restrictions are lifted, that footing is gone. He no longer has any basis on which to annul.

### R. Akiva: Slaughter

*Rabbi Akiva omer afilu nishchata aleha achas mikol habehemah eino yachol lehafer* — Rabbi Akiva holds that he cannot annul even earlier, immediately after one of the animals has been slaughtered but before the blood has been sprinkled. At this moment, the woman is technically still bound by the restrictions of *nezirut* — the *korban* is not yet complete — and therefore the *Tanna Kamma* would permit the husband to annul. Rabbi Akiva disagrees.

The reason is *hefsed kodashim*. If the husband annuls after the animal has been slaughtered but before the blood is sprinkled, the *korban* — specifically, as the *Gemara* will clarify, the *chatas* — cannot be completed, since the woman is no longer a *nezirah*. The blood cannot be sprinkled for a *chatas* that no longer belongs to an obligated *nazir*, and the entire animal must be destroyed. This represents a devastating waste of a sacred object. Rabbi Akiva holds that the trivial benefit of escaping one final minute of the wine restriction does not justify this destruction. What would she even do — drink wine in the *Beit HaMikdash*? The disproportion between gain and loss is so stark that Rabbi Akiva forbids the annulment at this stage.

*Tosafot* raises a fascinating question: if the husband ignores this ruling and annuls anyway, does the annulment take effect? The question is whether *yesh koach beyad chachamim la'akor davar min haTorah* — whether the Sages can void what would otherwise be a biblically valid annulment on policy grounds. *Tosafot* leaves this unresolved.

### When Annul Ends

The positions of the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Akiva thus represent different thresholds. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that once the blood of even one *korban* has been sprinkled, the husband can no longer annul — since the restrictions have lapsed. Rabbi Akiva extends the prohibition one step earlier: even after slaughter, before sprinkling, if annulling would destroy the *chatas*, he cannot annul. Both positions turn on the interplay between the husband's residual standing to annul and the cost — in wasted *kodashim* — of exercising that standing at this late stage.

## Tamei & Shaving

### Tamei Case

The *mishna* draws a distinction between *tiglachat tahara* — the normal shaving that concludes *nezirut* after a complete, uninterrupted term — and *tiglachat tumah*, the shaving that follows after the *nezirah* has become ritually impure. In the *tumah* case, the woman must begin her *nezirut* anew — an additional full month stretches ahead of her. In such a case, *yafeh* — even if the *chatas* from the *tumah* process has already been slaughtered and the blood has not yet been sprinkled, or even after the blood has been sprinkled, the *Tanna Kamma* holds that the husband can still annul. Why? *Shehu yachol lomar: ee efshi b'isha menuvelat* — because he can say, I do not want a wife whose appearance is disorderly. But more substantively, since there is a full month of *nezirut* still ahead of her, the *nezirut* is far from over. The reason annulment was barred in the *tahara* case was that after one *korban* the restrictions were effectively finished. Here, with a full month remaining, there is ample basis for the husband to annul, regardless of what stage the *korbanot* have reached.

### R. Meir: Final Stage

*Rabbi Meir omer: af betiglachat tahara yafer mipnei she-yachol lomar ee efshi b'isha megulachat* — Rabbi Meir holds that even in a normal *tahara* process, even after all three animals have been brought and only the final shaving remains, the husband may annul. His justification: he can declare that he does not want his wife to shave her head. Even at the final moment, that declared preference constitutes sufficient grounds for annulment, and the *halacha* entitles him to prevent that last stage.

## Chatas & Shelo Lishma

### Olah/Shelamim OK

Rabbi Zeira challenges the ruling of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva had held that after the animal is slaughtered but before the blood is sprinkled, the husband cannot annul because the *chatas* would be destroyed. But Rabbi Zeira asks: *v'amai? V'lizrok daman shelo lishman v'yishtarei b'chamra* — why is there any problem at all? Even after the annulment, simply sprinkle the blood — it will be brought *shelo lishma*, for an unintended purpose, since the woman is no longer a *nezirah*, but an *olah* or *shelamim* brought *shelo lishma* is still *kasher*. The meat will be permissible; the *korban* is not wasted.

### Shelo Lishma Valid

The *Gemara* supports this from a *braita* concerning *kivsei atzeret* — the lambs brought on *Shavuot* in conjunction with the *shtei halechem*. If those lambs were slaughtered *shelo lishman*, or before or after *Shavuot*, the ruling is: *hadam yizarek v'basar ye'achel* — sprinkle the blood and eat the meat. Even brought at the wrong time or for the wrong purpose, they remain valid. If it fell on *Shabbat*, the sprinkling should not be done *ab initio* — since the wrong purpose removes the basis for overriding *Shabbat* — but if done, it is valid, and the *imurin* are burned that evening.

The *braita*'s relevance is that it demonstrates the general principle: *korbanot* other than a *chatas*, brought *shelo lishma*, remain valid. The case of *kivsei atzeret* is particularly instructive because it also encompasses situations where the intent may be correct but the context is wrong — bringing at the wrong time, for instance, is itself a form of *shelo lishma*. The annulment case is analogous: the person slaughtering may intend correctly, but by virtue of the woman's no longer being a *nezirah*, the *korban* is by definition not being brought for its intended purpose. Nevertheless, it remains valid.

Thus, if we are dealing with an *olah* or *shelamim*, Rabbi Akiva would have no objection to annulling — simply complete the *korban* as a voluntary offering.

### Chatas Unique

*Aval hacha b'mai askinan? K'gon she-shachat chatas b'reisha* — the case Rabbi Akiva is addressing is specifically where the *chatas* was slaughtered first. The *halacha* is *gilach al achat mishaloshan yatza* — shaving over any one of the three *korbanot* concludes the *nezirut*, so the *chatas* need not be first in order — but Rabbi Akiva's ruling applies to the scenario where the *chatas* is the first animal slaughtered. A *chatas* brought *shelo lishma* is invalid — unlike an *olah* or *shelamim*, a *chatas* for the wrong person or wrong purpose does not merely fail to satisfy its obligation; it is an entirely invalid *korban* and must be destroyed. Therefore, once the *chatas* has been slaughtered, annulling the *nezirut* before the blood is sprinkled would leave an unusable carcass. There is no option of simply completing it as a valid offering. This is why Rabbi Akiva says: we will not allow the annulment, even though technically the woman is still bound by the restrictions for one more moment — because the cost, the total destruction of the *chatas*, is wildly disproportionate to the benefit.

If the first animal slaughtered had been the *olah* or the *shelamim*, Rabbi Akiva would agree with the *Tanna Kamma*: the husband may annul, since those animals can still be validly offered even after the annulment, *shelo lishma*.

## Tamei & Shaving (II)

### Wig Debate

We return to complete the *mishna*. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that in a normal *tahara* conclusion, once the blood of one *korban* has been sprinkled, the husband can no longer annul. Rabbi Meir holds that he may annul even at the final stage — even after all three *korbanot* have been brought — to prevent her from shaving, declaring *ee efshi b'isha megulachat*: I do not want a bald wife.

The *Tanna Kamma* responds: *efshar b'pe'ah nachris* — she can wear a wig. The husband's complaint that he does not want a shaved-headed wife is not a compelling objection if the condition is remediable through a wig. Since she can cover the shaved head, the claimed hardship is not real, and the husband has no standing to annul merely to prevent the shaving.

Rabbi Meir, however, is not persuaded: *depei nachris idi d'zuama lo nicha lei* — a wig, being foreign hair, has an unfamiliar smell. It does not smell like her own hair. The husband may legitimately object to that difference, and therefore his stated preference not to have her undergo the shaving remains a genuine and valid basis for annulment, even at this final stage.
