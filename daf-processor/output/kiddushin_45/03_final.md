# Kiddushin 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketanah Marriage Setup

### Na'arah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קְטַנָּה שֶׁנִּתְקַדְּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: צְרִיכָה גֵּט, וּצְרִיכָה מֵיאוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed in the case of **a minor who became betrothed without her father's consent. Shmuel says:** If her husband seeks to divorce her **she requires a bill of divorce** to sever the marriage, **and she** also **requires refusal**, which annuls a betrothal that is valid by rabbinic law but not by Torah law.

The daf opens in the context of a preceding dispute concerning the *na'arah* — the girl who has entered adolescence but has not yet reached majority. The *Chachamim* hold that she may receive a *get*, while Rabbi Yehuda maintains she cannot even do that. Rabbi Yochanan's position is that receiving a *get* is precisely all she may do: since it is passive — *b'al karchah*, against her will in a sense — it lies within her capacity. Any transaction requiring her affirmative decision, such as entering into marriage, remains entirely within the father's authority. Even though the *na'arah* is legally an adult in certain respects, all real decision-making power over consequential transactions rests with the father.

### Ketanah & History

With that context established, the discussion shifts to the *ketanah* — the underage girl, clearly not an adult, for whom any independent legal act would seem entirely without effect. Yet the subject of today's daf, extending into tomorrow's as well, concerns a scenario in which this *ketanah* goes ahead and marries herself off. One might assume that if such an act carries no weight even for a *na'arah*, it would certainly be meaningless for a *ketanah*. Nevertheless, we will see that the *Gemara* entertains the possibility that her act carries some legal weight, and we will need to understand why.

### Core Question

This is not merely a speculative exercise. The *Rishonim* engage in a vigorous debate about how to rule (*pasken*) because cases of this kind apparently did arise in practice. Likely the more common scenario was not a ten-year-old acting entirely on her own initiative, but rather a situation where the father was overseas — *medinat hayam* — for an extended period, and the mother proceeded to arrange the girl's marriage. As Tosafot notes at the beginning of the *perek*, despite the *Gemara*'s stated preference that a man not marry off his daughter until she is old enough to express her own wishes, economic and historical realities created a practical necessity to arrange marriages for girls even before they reached *bat mitzvah*. This would have been especially acute when the father, though alive, was simply not present.

## Shmuel's Teaching

### Shmuel Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קַרְנָא: דְּבָרִים בְּגוֹ: אִם גֵּט, לָמָּה מֵיאוּן? אִם מֵיאוּן, לָמָּה גֵּט?
>
> **Translation:** **Karna says:** There are puzzling **matters** included **within** this statement, since it is self-contradictory: **If** she needs **a bill of divorce,** and the betrothal is evidently treated as a proper betrothal, **why** does she require **refusal?** Conversely, **if** she requires **refusal,** indicating that her betrothal was of no consequence by Torah law, **why** does she require **a bill of divorce?**

The *Gemara* opens with the word *itmar* — indicating a dispute among *Amoraim*: *ketanah sheniskadshah shelo l'da'at aviha* — a *ketanah* who betrothed herself without her father's knowledge or consent. Shmuel rules: *tzrichah get v'tzrichah mi'un* — she requires both a *get* and a *mi'un*.

This is immediately striking. *Mi'un* — the formal repudiation by a minor — ordinarily applies only when the mother and brothers have arranged the marriage in the father's absence. What is *mi'un* doing here at all? And why would both be required? Normally, *mi'un* serves in place of a *get*, functioning as the appropriate dissolution of what is recognized as *kiddushin d'rabbanan*. If the marriage is treated as *kiddushin d'rabbanan*, a *mi'un* alone should suffice. If it is treated as *kiddushin d'oraita*, a *get* is required — but then why *mi'un* as well?

### Karna Challenge

This internal contradiction is precisely what *Karna*, a named *Chacham*, raises. He states: *devarim b'go* — there are problems inside here, things do not fit together. His challenge is direct: *im get lama mi'un, im mi'un lama get* — if a *get* is required, why *mi'un*? And if *mi'un* suffices, why a *get*? A *get* belongs to the world of *d'oraita* marriage; *mi'un* belongs to the world of *d'rabbanan* marriage. Combining them seems internally contradictory.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הָא מָר עוּקְבָא וּבֵי דִינֵיהּ בְּכַפְרִי. אַפְכוּהָ, שַׁדְּרוּהָ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָאֱלֹהִים! צְרִיכָה גֵּט, וּצְרִיכָה מֵיאוּן. וְחַס לֵיהּ לְזַרְעֵיהּ דְּאַבָּא בַּר אַבָּא, דְּנֵימָא הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said to him: Now** that **Mar Ukva and his court are in** the town of **Kafrei,** let us pose the question to them. Furthermore, **they reversed** the names of the respective opinions of Shmuel and Karna and **sent** the question **before Rav.** He **said to** the messengers in the form of an oath: **By God! She requires a bill of divorce and she requires refusal. And** God **forbid that the seed of Abba bar Abba,** i.e., Shmuel, should **say such** a thing, that she does not require both.

The *batei midrash* respond that if Karna has a problem with Shmuel's ruling, he should take it to Rav Ukva and his *beit din* in the nearby town of Kafri. Apparently they then did so, and the *beit din* referred the matter further.

### Blind Review

What happens next is a striking procedural maneuver: *afchuha shadroha lekamei d'Rav* — they reversed the positions and sent the question to Rav. That is, they presented Shmuel's position as Karna's — that both *get* and *mi'un* are required — and Karna's objection as Shmuel's. This was a form of blind review, designed to prevent the result from being influenced by collegial loyalty. Had Rav known the ruling came from Shmuel, Karna might have dismissed Rav's endorsement as mere solidarity between colleagues. By reversing the attribution, they ensured Rav would respond on the merits alone — and moreover, if Rav sided with the position that both are required even when he thought Shmuel had opposed it, that would be a powerful and unbiased confirmation.

### Rav Confirms

The result is emphatic. Rav declares: *Hai Elohim, tzricha get v'tzrichah mi'un* — in God's name, she requires both a *get* and a *mi'un*. He adds that it would be unthinkable — *vechas lei lezarei d'Abba bar Abba* — that the son of Abba bar Abba, meaning Shmuel himself, could have held otherwise. So Rav not only affirmed the ruling but expressed something approaching indignation at the suggestion Shmuel would doubt it, all while in fact believing, based on the reversed presentation, that Shmuel was the one raising the objection. Rav held firm. Both Rav and Shmuel thus stand in strong agreement: this *ketanah* requires both a *get* and a *mi'un*.

## Get & Mi'un Logic

### Get Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי? אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: צְרִיכָה גֵּט – שֶׁמָּא נִתְרַצָּה הָאָב בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, צְרִיכָה מֵיאוּן – שֶׁמָּא לֹא נִתְרַצָּה הָאָב בְּקִידּוּשִׁין, וְיֹאמְרוּ אֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that there is a need for both a bill of divorce and refusal? **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said: She requires a bill of divorce** because **perhaps the father desired the betrothal,** which would mean it would take effect retroactively. **She** also **requires refusal** because **perhaps the father did not desire the betrothal,** and it did not take effect. If she receives only a bill of divorce, **and** her former husband proceeds to betroth her sister, people **will say** that the **betrothal does not take effect with her sister.** The *halakha* is that betrothal can take effect with the sister of one's wife or former wife only after the first wife has died. She is therefore required to perform refusal, to indicate that the validity of her betrothal was uncertain. If he later betroths her sister, he would be required to divorce her due to the uncertainty if this second betrothal took effect.

The *Gemara* now asks: *v'ta'ama mai?* — what is the actual reason? The answer is provided by Rav Acha the son of Rav Ika. He explains each requirement independently. She needs a *get* — *shema nitratza ha'av* — because perhaps the father, upon learning of what happened, will ultimately accept the marriage. If the father does come to accept it, then the marriage could be treated as *kiddushin d'oraita*, and a proper *get* would be required to dissolve it.

### Mi'un Rationale

She needs a *mi'un* — *shema lo nitratza ha'av bekiddushin* — because perhaps the father did not accept the marriage. In that case, there was never any genuine *kiddushin*, and the *get* would have been given for nothing. But if all that people observed was a *get*, they would conclude that there had been a real *kiddushin d'oraita*, making her a *grusha d'oraita*, a definitively divorced woman. The *mi'un* is therefore given as a signal: its presence alongside the *get* communicates that even the *get* may have been only a stringency, a *chumra*, and not truly required — because if the marriage were certainly *d'oraita*, no *mi'un* would be added. The combination signals ambiguity and uncertainty.

### Sister Concern

The concrete harm the *Gemara* seeks to prevent is the following: if people believe she is a *grusha d'oraita*, they will conclude that the man who gave the *get* was once truly married to her. And since a man cannot marry his ex-wife's sister while the ex-wife is still alive, people will think that if this man were to betroth her sister, the *kiddushin* would not take effect — *ein kiddushin tofsin b'achota*. But in truth, since the original *kiddushin* may never have been valid, the sister might well be fully available — *kiddushin* to her could be *chal*. The *mi'un* prevents this false inference by signaling that the *get* was of uncertain necessity.

At the same time, this explanation raises deep difficulties that the *Rishonim* grapple with at length. Even granting that the father might retrospectively accept the marriage, the mechanics are difficult to reconstruct. A *ketanah* cannot serve as a *shaliach*, so how could money given directly to her function as *kesef kiddushin*? The Ritva raises this question and resolves it by analogy to the rule at the beginning of the tractate, where a woman may instruct a man to place the betrothal money on a rock or throw it into the sea, and it nonetheless counts as *kesef kiddushin* — because she derives *hana'ah* from his compliance with her wishes, or through related mechanisms.

More fundamentally, most *Rishonim* reconstruct the scenario as follows: the girl comes home and informs the father of what occurred. The father, at least initially, says nothing. His silence is ambiguous — it might indicate acquiescence or merely a delayed reaction. If he later objects, the question becomes whether that objection reflects his original position or represents a change from an initial acceptance. The *Ra'avad* suggests the father may have been predisposed to accept even from the outset, but most *Rishonim* find this difficult to sustain across the typical cases. And even if the father's acquiescence can be established, the question of how that retroactive agreement transforms money already in her pocket into operative *kesef kiddushin* remains deeply problematic. The entire edifice appears highly rabbinic in character — a *chashash*, a concern — and this makes it all the more striking that Rav reacted with such vehemence at the thought that Shmuel could dismiss it.

## Rav Nachman Limit

### Shidduchin Gate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וְהוּא שֶׁשִּׁדְּכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: And this** concern that the father of a minor might later state that he desired her betrothal is relevant only **when they arranged** the match before she betrothed herself, as in that case it is likely that the father desired the betrothal.

Rav Nachman attempts to limit the applicability of this entire framework: *v'hu sheshidduchu* — all of the above applies only if *shidduchin* preceded the event. That is, there had to have been prior conversation and negotiation between the families — the prospective groom and his family had discussed the possibility of marrying the daughter with the father present and involved. Only then, when *kiddushin* money was given outside the father's direct presence, is there a basis for the concern that the father implicitly agreed. This limitation substantially clarifies the *nitratza ha'av* concern, even if it does not resolve the mechanical difficulties: it at least supplies a plausible basis for presuming that the father had the marriage in mind.

## Ulla Rejects All

### Ulla's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מֵיאוּן אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes another opinion. **Ulla said:** If a minor accepted betrothal without her father's consent, her betrothal does not take effect, and **she does not even require refusal.**

Ulla takes the opposite extreme: *afilu mi'un einah tzrichah* — she does not even require a *mi'un*. Nothing here means anything. She is a *ketanah*; her act of betrothing herself is entirely without legal effect, whether *d'oraita* or *d'rabbanan*. There is no *get*, no *mi'un*, nothing.

### Shidduchin Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל גַּב דְּשִׁידְּכוּ? מַאן דְּמַתְנֵי הָא, לָא מַתְנֵי הָא. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי אָמַר עוּלָּא: קְטַנָּה שֶׁנִּתְקַדְּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ – אֲפִילּוּ מֵיאוּן אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Does Ulla's statement apply **even though they arranged** the match? The Gemara answers: **He who teaches this does not teach that.** The one who cited Ulla's statement does not hold that Shmuel's ruling applies only in the case of an arranged match, rather it applies in all cases. According to this opinion, Ulla stated his *halakha* only in a case where the match was not arranged. **There are** those **who say** that **Ulla says:** If **a minor became betrothed without her father's consent, she does not even require refusal,** including a case where the match was arranged.

The *Gemara* then raises the question: does Ulla maintain this position even when *shidduchin* preceded the event? The *Gemara* answers by distinguishing between two formulations of Ulla's statement. According to one version (*man demasni ha lo masni ha*), whoever transmits the teaching that this law applies specifically in a case of *shidduchin* does not transmit Ulla's counter-ruling — implying that even Ulla would concede some concern when *shidduchin* occurred. But the second formulation (*ika d'amri*) presents Ulla's ruling as an independent absolute statement: even in a case of *shidduchin*, she requires nothing at all.

This distinction matters greatly for practical ruling. As we will see, most *Rishonim* rule against Rav and Shmuel, following the line of Ulla and Ravina. It is therefore essential to establish whether Ulla's rejection extends even to cases of *shidduchin*, so that the final ruling can be applied without reservation across all circumstances.

## Yibum Scenario

### Emancipated Minor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב כָּהֲנָא: וְכוּלָּן, אִם מֵתוּ, אוֹ מֵיאֲנוּ, אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשׁוּ, אוֹ שֶׁנִּמְצְאוּ אַיְילוֹנִיּוֹת – צָרוֹתֵיהֶן מוּתָּרוֹת. דְּקַדְּשַׁהּ מַאן? אִילֵימָא דְּקַדְּשַׁהּ אָבִיהָ, בְּמֵיאוּן סַגִּי לַהּ?! גֵּט מְעַלְּיָא בָּעֲיָא! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּקַדִּשָׁה אִיהִי נַפְשַׁהּ, וְקָתָנֵי דְּבָעֲיָא מֵיאוּן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana raises an objection:** The mishna in *Yevamot* (2a) teaches that if a potential *yevama* is in one of the fifteen categories of women forbidden to the *yavam* as a relative, not only is she forbidden to him, but he also may not perform levirate marriage with any of her rival wives. The Sages then clarified this *halakha*: **And if,** before the husband had died, **any of** those forbidden women **had died, or had performed refusal; or had been divorced; or had been found** to be **a sexually underdeveloped woman [*ailonit*],** who is incapable of bearing children, then **her rival wives are permitted** to the *yavam*. Rav Kahana focuses on the case of one who performed refusal: With regard to this refusal that she performed, what type of betrothal did it follow? **When she was betrothed by whom? If we say** that **her father betrothed her** to the brother who died, is **refusal** alone **enough for her** to dissolve the marriage? Doesn't she **require a proper bill of divorce? Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where she betrothed herself** when she was a minor, **and** the mishna nevertheless **teaches that she requires refusal.** This presents a difficulty for Ulla, who holds that she does not need even refusal.

The *Gemara* now pivots to a different context that illuminates the scope of *mi'un* for a *ketanah*. The *Mishnah* in Yevamot teaches (*v'chulan im metu o mianu o nitgarshu o shenimtzu ailonit tzaroteihen mutarot*): if any of the women who are *ervah* to the surviving brother — and who would therefore exempt all co-wives from *yibum* — were to die, perform *mi'un*, be divorced, or be found to be an *ailonit*, then the co-wives become permitted for *yibum* or *chalitza*.

The case of *mi'un* here is the one that requires explanation. Consider: Shimon is married to Rachel, his daughter. Reuven, Rachel's uncle, dies. Because Rachel is an *ervah* to Shimon, no *yibum* occurs with any of Reuven's other wives. But if Rachel performed *mi'un* — indicating her marriage to Reuven had been only *kiddushin d'rabbanan* — then the *zika* bond to Shimon is dissolved, and the co-wives can enter *yibum*.

The question is: how did Rachel, a *ketanah*, come to be married to Reuven with a marriage of *kiddushin d'rabbanan* while her father Shimon is still alive? If Shimon had married her off himself, the marriage would be *d'oraita* and *mi'un* would be inapplicable — she would need a full *get*. The *Gemara* therefore asks: who married her? If it was the father, *mi'un* is not the appropriate mechanism; she would need a real *get*. The answer must be: *ella lav d'kadshah inafshah* — she married herself off. And the *Gemara* notes this as proof that such self-marriage by a *ketanah* is treated as *kiddushin d'rabbanan*, requiring *mi'un*.

This functions as a challenge to Ulla's position as well, since the *Mishnah* implies that *mi'un* is relevant in such a scenario. The *Gemara* itself both raises and resolves this question, answering that the relevant case is *k'gon she-na'asah lah ma'aseh yetomah b'chayei aviha* — she was treated legally as an orphan during her father's lifetime. This occurs when she had been previously married by her father, that marriage included *nisuin*, the husband died or divorced her, and through the completion of that full marital process she left her father's domain entirely. Having been fully emancipated in this manner while still a minor, she then married herself off a second time. This second self-marriage, occurring outside any paternal authority, is treated like the case of a girl married off by her mother and brothers — *kiddushin d'rabbanan* requiring *mi'un*. This scenario is structurally analogous to, but distinct from, the case where a father is present and the *ketanah* marries herself off without his involvement.

### Rav Hamnuna Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין שֶׁמּוֹכְרָהּ אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, גְּרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט. הַאי אַלְמָנָה הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּקַדְּשַׁהּ אָבִיהָ, מִי מָצֵי מְזַבֵּין לַהּ? הָא אֵין אָדָם מוֹכֵר אֶת בִּתּוֹ לְשִׁפְחוּת אַחַר אִישׁוּת! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּקַדִּישׁ אִיהִי נַפְשַׁהּ וְקָא קָרֵי לַהּ אַלְמָנָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And they agree that he can sell her to a High Priest** even if she is **a widow,** or **to a common priest** even if she is **a divorcée or** is **a *yevama* who performed *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*].** Although such marriages are forbidden, they do take effect. The Gemara analyzes this: **What are the circumstances of this widow** who can be sold as a maidservant by her father? **If we say** that **her father betrothed her** and her husband subsequently died while she was still a minor, **is he able to sell her** after her betrothal? **A person cannot sell his daughter into servitude after** he has **betrothed** her. **Rather, isn't** the *baraita* referring to a case **when she betrothed herself** as a minor, **and** yet it **calls her a widow,** indicating that such a betrothal is effective, contrary to the opinion of Ulla.

Rav Hamnuna raises a further challenge from a *baraita* concerning the sale of a daughter as a Hebrew maidservant. The rule is stated: *ein mocherah l'krovim* — one may not sell her to relatives with whom *kiddushin* is impossible, because the entire purpose of the sale is to lead to *yi'ud* (the transformation of servitude into marriage), and a sale to such relatives forecloses that possibility. Rabbi Eliezer dissents, permitting such a sale without any prospect of *yi'ud*. But both opinions agree (*v'shavin she-mocher almana l'kohen gadol*) that a man may sell his daughter who is an *almana* (widow) to a *kohen gadol*, or one who is a *grusha* or *chalutzah* to an ordinary *kohen* — because even though such a marriage would be forbidden, the *kiddushin* would nonetheless take effect (*chal*), and therefore *yi'ud* is not categorically off the table.

The question the *Gemara* poses is: *hai almana heichi dami?* — how did this daughter come to be a *widow* while her father is still alive and still selling her? If the father had previously betrothed her (*eirusin*), and the *chatan* died, she would be an *almana* — but could the father then sell her again? The *Gemara* invokes the rule that once a father has married off his daughter (*ishus*), he may not then sell her as a maidservant. So if the *almana* status arose through the father's own *kiddushin*, the subsequent sale would be prohibited.

### Self-Marriage Proof

It follows that the *almana* in the *baraita* must have acquired that status through a marriage the father himself did not initiate. The only way this is possible — given that the father is alive and present — is if she married herself off: *ella lav d'kadshah hi nafshah*. The *Gemara* observes that she is being called an *almana* despite having married herself, which implies that even a self-initiated marriage by a *ketanah* can produce a recognized status of *almana* or *grusha*. This again suggests some degree of legal weight attaches to such a marriage.

### Yiud Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: הָכָא בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי יִעוּד. וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: מָעוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת לָאו לְקִידּוּשִׁין נִיתְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Here** it deals with a minor girl widowed **from a betrothal of designation,** i.e., her father sold her as a Hebrew maidservant, and the master designated her as his wife but died before he married her. **And** this is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who says: The original money** of the sale of the maidservant **was not given for** the purpose of **betrothal.** Rather, her betrothal goes into effect when her master relinquishes his rights to have her serve as a Hebrew maidservant. Since this betrothal was not accepted by the father, he is later permitted to sell her as a maidservant again.

But the *Gemara* does not accept this as the plain reading of the *baraita*. Rav Amram in the name of Rav Yitzchak explains: *hacha bekiddushei yi'ud va'aliba deRabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, d'amar ma'ot harishonot lav lekiddushin nitnu* — the case here involves *kiddushei yi'ud* according to Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the original purchase money was not retroactively converted into *kesef kiddushin* when *yi'ud* occurred.

The mechanics are as follows. The father sold his daughter to husband number one as a maidservant — *shifcha*. Husband number one then performed *yi'ud*, transforming the remaining service into a form of *kiddushin*. According to Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda, this *yi'ud* does not retroactively treat the original sale money as *kesef kiddushin* — rather, what becomes the operative act of *kiddushin* is the service she still owes him: he accepts that future labor as *kiddushin*, so to speak. This is significant because it means the father's original transaction was purely a *sale* — not a marriage — and the marriage that emerged from *yi'ud* was the doing of the buyer, not the father.

After husband number one dies or divorces her, she returns to the father's domain. Since her previous entry into marriage came through *shifchus* (servitude), not through direct *kiddushin* by the father, the subsequent transaction is classified as *shifcha achar shifcha* — a sale following a prior sale — not *shifcha achar ishus* — a sale following a marriage. The prohibition against re-selling applies only to the latter. The father may therefore sell her again to a second man — for instance, to a *kohen gadol* — while she carries the status of *almana* or *grusha* from her first marriage. The case in the *baraita* thus does not require positing that she married herself; it involves the distinct mechanism of *kiddushei yi'ud* under the specific opinion of Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda.

What this discussion establishes for the broader *sugya* is the question of how far the concept of *mi'un* — and *kiddushin d'rabbanan* for a *ketanah* — can extend beyond its classic context of a mother and brothers marrying off a fatherless minor. We have now seen two candidate scenarios: the emancipated minor who marries herself (accepted), and the *ketanah* who marries herself while her father is present (contested). The *Gemara* will now proceed to apply these principles in the context of *yibum*.

## Yibum Details

### Rav Huna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מֵת וְנָפְלָה לִפְנֵי אָחִיו לְיִבּוּם, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: מְמָאֶנֶת לְמַאֲמָרוֹ, וְאֵינָהּ מְמָאֶנֶת לְזִיקָּתוֹ. כֵּיצַד? עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר – צְרִיכָה גֵּט, וּצְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה, וּצְרִיכָה מֵיאוּן.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** If a man who betrothed a minor without her father's consent **died, and she happened before his brothers for levirate marriage, Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** She **performs refusal for his levirate betrothal,** i.e., if the *yavam* performed levirate betrothal with her, divorce is effected only by means of refusal in addition to a bill of divorce, **but she** does not **perform refusal for his** levirate **bond** to her. If he did not perform levirate betrothal, she does not require refusal as well as *ḥalitza*. **How so?** If **he performed levirate betrothal with her, she requires a bill of divorce, and she requires *ḥalitza*, and she requires refusal.**

The *Gemara* now presents the case directly. The *ketanah* married herself off to Reuven — without her father's involvement. Reuven then dies, and the case falls before Reuven's brother Shimon for *yibum*. According to Rav and Shmuel, this self-marriage was at least a matter of *chashash*, and both a *get* and a *mi'un* would have been required to exit it. Now what is Shimon required to do?

Rav Huna in the name of Rav rules: *mema'enes l'ma'amaro v'einah mema'enes l'zikatoh* — she performs *mi'un* in response to Shimon's *ma'amar*, but not merely in response to the *zika* bond alone. *Ma'amar* is the act by which a *yavam* — the surviving brother — performs a form of *kiddushin* with the *yevamah* while she awaits *yibum* or *chalitza*. If Shimon does *ma'amar*, that introduces an additional dimension; if he does not, the situation is different.

### Triple Remedy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָה גֵּט – שֶׁמָּא נִתְרַצָּה הָאָב בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי שֵׁנִי. צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה – שֶׁמָּא נִתְרַצָּה הָאָב בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי רִאשׁוֹן. צְרִיכָה מֵיאוּן – שֶׁמָּא לֹא נִתְרַצָּה הָאָב לֹא בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי רִאשׁוֹן וְלֹא בְּקִידּוּשֵׁי שֵׁנִי, וְיֹאמְרוּ אֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **She requires a bill of divorce,** as **perhaps the father desired the betrothal of** only **the second** man. Levirate betrothal is performed in the same manner as standard betrothal, i.e., by giving money. If the father did not desire the first betrothal she is not a *yevama*, and the second betrothal goes into effect, requiring a bill of divorce to end the betrothal. She **requires *ḥalitza*,** as **perhaps the father desired the betrothal of the first** man, in which case she is a regular *yevama*, who requires *ḥalitza* to be released from the *yavam*. **She requires refusal,** as **perhaps the father did not desire either the betrothal of the first** man **or the betrothal of the second** man. If she receives a bill of divorce and performs *ḥalitza*, **and** the second man proceeds to betroth her sister, people **will say** that the **betrothal does not take effect with her sister,** as they will think that the first betrothal was fully valid.

If Shimon performed *ma'amar*, the *Gemara* specifies: *tzricha get v'tzricha chalitza v'tzricha mi'un* — she requires all three from Shimon. Each is explained separately.

She needs a *get* — *shema nitratza ha'av bekiddushei sheini* — because perhaps the father did not accept the first marriage to Reuven, but he did accept the *ma'amar* of Shimon. If the father accepted Shimon's *ma'amar* as valid *kiddushin d'oraita*, then Shimon has truly married her and must give her a *get* to release her.

She needs *chalitza* — *shema nitratza bekiddushei rishon* — because perhaps the father accepted the original marriage to Reuven. In that case, there is a genuine *zika* between her and Shimon as the surviving brother, and Shimon must perform *chalitza* to release her from that bond.

She needs *mi'un* — *shema lo nitratza lo bekiddushei rishon velo bekiddushei sheini* — because perhaps the father accepted neither marriage, in which case she was never truly married to anyone. The *get* and the *chalitza* would then both be legally meaningless — performed only as *chumrot*. The *mi'un* signals this uncertainty and prevents people from drawing the erroneous inference that the *chalitza* confirms a real *d'oraita* marriage and that if Shimon were to betroth her sister, the *kiddushin* would not take effect — *ein kiddushin tofsin b'achota*.

### Chalitza Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר – אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה אֶלָּא חֲלִיצָה בִּלְבַד. מַאי אָמְרַתְּ: תִּיבְּעֵי נָמֵי מֵיאוּן, שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ אֵין קִידּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ? הַכֹּל יוֹדְעִים: אֲחוֹת חֲלוּצָה דְּרַבָּנַן. דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כָּאן שָׁנָה רַבִּי אֲחוֹת גְּרוּשָׁה מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֲחוֹת חֲלוּצָה מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **he did not perform levirate betrothal with her, she requires only *ḥalitza*.** The Gemara explains: **If you say** that **she should require refusal as well, lest** people **say** that **betrothal does not take effect with her sister,** that is unnecessary. **Everyone knows** that **a sister of** one's ***ḥalutza*** is forbidden **by rabbinic law** only; therefore, they also know that betrothal with the sister would be effective, and they would not permit the sister to marry others without receiving a bill of divorce. This is **as Reish Lakish said** with regard to the wording of a mishna (*Yevamot* 41a): **Here Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi incidentally **taught** that **a sister of** one's **divorcée** is forbidden to him **by Torah law,** whereas **a sister of** one's ***ḥalutza*** is forbidden to him **by rabbinic law.**

If Shimon did *not* perform *ma'amar*, the ruling is simpler: *einah tzricha ela chalitza bilvad* — she needs only *chalitza*. The logic is that there are only two relevant scenarios: either the father accepted the marriage to Reuven, in which case there is a real *zika* and Shimon needs *chalitza*; or the father did not accept it, in which case Shimon has no connection to her at all. Since Shimon did no *ma'amar*, there is no third scenario involving Shimon's own *kiddushin* — and therefore no need for a *get*.

One might ask: should she not also need a *mi'un* even in this case, to signal that the *chalitza* was of uncertain necessity and prevent people from concluding that Shimon marrying her sister would not be *chal*? The *Gemara* answers: *hakol yodim achot chaluza d'rabbanan* — everyone knows that the sister of a *chalutzah* is forbidden only *d'rabbanan*, not *d'oraita*. Reish Lakish establishes this in Yevamot: the sister of a *grusha* (divorced woman) is forbidden *d'oraita*, but the sister of a *chalutzah* is forbidden only *midivrei sofrim*. Accordingly, even if observers see a *chalitza* here and conclude that there was a genuine *zika*, they will also know that any subsequent *kiddushin* to her sister would take effect *d'oraita* — there is no risk of a false inference that would permit a prohibited union or negate a valid one. The *mi'un* is therefore unnecessary when there was no *ma'amar*.

## Ravina Dispute

### Father & Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ בֵּי תְרֵי דַּהֲווֹ קָא שָׁתוּ חַמְרָא תּוּתֵי צִיפֵּי בְּבָבֶל. שְׁקַל חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ כָּסָא דְחַמְרָא, יְהַב לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ. אֲמַר: מִיקַּדְּשָׁא לִי בְּרַתָּיךְ לִבְרִי. אֲמַר רָבִינָא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא נִתְרַצָּה הָאָב – שֶׁמָּא נִתְרַצָּה הַבֵּן לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There were **these two** people **that were** sitting and **drinking wine under poplar trees [*tzifei*] in Babylonia. One of them took a cup of wine** and **gave it to his friend. He said: Betroth for me your daughter to my son** by receiving this cup of wine. **Ravina says: Even according to the one who says** that in the case of a minor girl who became betrothed without her father's consent, **we are concerned** that **perhaps the father desired** the betrothal, and **we do not say** that **perhaps the son desired** the betrothal.

The *Gemara* now introduces a case that frames Ravina's broader rejection of the *nitratza ha'av* concern. Two men were drinking wine together under an awning in Bavel. One took a cup of wine, gave it to the other, and said: *mekadsha li bartech livri* — let your daughter be betrothed to my son. Here the man who accepted the wine had the authority to marry off his daughter; there is no question about that side of the transaction. The issue is that the man giving the wine was acting on behalf of his son without having been appointed as the son's *shaliach*. Could we say — by parallel to the *nitratza ha'av* reasoning — that perhaps when the son finds out he will be pleased with the marriage, and that retroactive acceptance will make the father's action valid?

Ravina says no: *shema nitratzei haben lo amrinan* — we do not invoke the concern that the son may come to accept it. The *Gemara* presents the reason on behalf of the rabbis who explained Ravina's position: *de'av bekiddushei bito bechol dehu nicha lei* — a father has a low threshold for accepting whatever arrangement his daughter has entered into, because, as the *Gemara* characterizes it, *detav lemeitav tan du* — it is preferable for a woman to be partnered than to be alone, and fathers tend to embrace opportunities to see their daughters settled. But *aval gavra dayek venasiv* — a man is exacting and discerning about who he marries.

### Gender Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרָבִינָא: וְדִילְמָא שָׁלִיחַ שַׁוְּיֵהּ? לָא חֲצִיף אִינִישׁ לְשַׁוּוֹיֵי לַאֲבוּהּ שָׁלִיחַ. וְדִילְמָא אַרְצוֹיֵי אַרְצְיַיהּ קַמֵּיהּ? אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבָּה בַּר שִׁימִי: בְּפֵירוּשׁ אָמַר מָר דְּלָא סָבַר לְהָא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Ravina: But perhaps** the son **appointed** his father as **an agent** to betroth her, and the betrothal should take effect. He replied: **A person is not** so **brazen** as **to appoint his father** as his **agent** and thereby treat him as an assistant of sorts. They further inquired: **But perhaps** the son **made his desire** known **to** his father by speaking of his desire to marry the woman, and the father acted of his own accord upon his son's wishes and betrothed her to him. **Rabba bar Shimi said to** Ravina: This is not a concern, **since the Master,** i.e., Ravina, **explicitly said that he does not agree with this** opinion **of Rav and Shmuel** that when a minor girl accepts betrothal there is a concern that perhaps the father desired it. So too, there is no concern that a father can act for the son without his awareness.

There is therefore an asymmetry between the two cases. For a daughter: the father's presumed willingness to accept after the fact provides at least some basis — however thin — for the *nitratza ha'av* concern. For a son: no comparable presumption exists. The son is expected to care deeply about his choice of spouse and cannot be presumed to accept whatever was arranged without his input.

Furthermore — and this is a second, independent reason — even if the son were inclined to accept what the father did, that acceptance alone would be insufficient. To marry on someone's behalf, one must be a *shaliach*; retroactive willingness does not substitute for the proper appointment of agency. This applies regardless of one's views on the *nitratza ha'av* concern: *ubein eino birshut aviv lekadesh lo isha shelo bishlucho* — the father does not have the standing to marry for his son without having been formally designated as a *shaliach*.

### Ravina Rejects

The *Gemara* makes explicit what this case implies: *befirush amar mar d'lo savar lah l'ha d'Rav u'Shmuel* — Ravina explicitly stated that he does not accept the ruling of Rav and Shmuel. And since the discussion in the *Gemara* has been framing the son's case as parallel — in some ways — to a *shidduchin* scenario (the son had been expressing interest in the girl, which created a context of presumed desirability, analogous to prior *shidduchin* with the daughter), Ravina's rejection extends even to cases where *shidduchin* preceded the event. We do not invoke *nitratza ha'av* even then.

## Bizayon Exception

### Vegetables Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקַדֵּישׁ בְּכִישָׁא דְיַרְקָא בְּשׁוּקָא. אֲמַר רָבִינָא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חוֹשְׁשִׁין שֶׁמָּא נִתְרַצָּה הָאָב – הָנֵי מִילֵּי דֶּרֶךְ כָּבוֹד, אֲבָל דֶּרֶךְ בִּזָּיוֹן – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who betrothed** a minor girl without her father's consent **with a bundle of vegetables in the marketplace. Ravina says: Even according to the one who says** that when a minor becomes betrothed without her father's consent **we are concerned** that **perhaps the father desired** the betrothal, **this matter applies** only if the man betrothed her **in a dignified manner. But** as the betrothal in this case was done **in a degrading manner,** there is **no** concern.

Even from the perspective of Rav and Shmuel — who do invoke the *nitratza ha'av* concern — not every case of self-*kiddushin* by a *ketanah* triggers it. Ravina articulates an important limitation. A man betrothed a woman with a bundle of vegetables in the marketplace. Ravina rules: *afilu man d'amar chaishinan shema nitratzei ha'av, hani mili derech kavod* — even according to those who hold that we are concerned the father may have been *meratze* after the fact, that concern applies only when the original *kiddushin* was performed in a respectful manner. *Aval hacha derech bizayon* — when the betrothal was done in a degrading manner, we have no concern at all that the father would ever come to accept it.

### Bizayon Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: בִּזָּיוֹן דְּמַאי? אִי בִּזָּיוֹן דְּיַרְקָא, אִי בִּזָּיוֹן דְּשׁוּקָא? נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ דְּקַדֵּישׁ בְּכַסְפָּא בְּשׁוּקָא, אוֹ בְּכִישָׁא דְיַרְקָא בְּבֵיתָא, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דֶּרֶךְ בִּזָּיוֹן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: What** was the **degradation** in this manner of betrothal? **Was** the **degradation** from the fact **that** he used **vegetables,** or **was** the **degradation** due to the betrothal having been performed **in the marketplace?** The **practical difference** concerns cases **where one betrothed** a minor girl **with money in the marketplace, or** where one betrothed a minor girl **with a bundle of vegetables in a house. What** is the *halakha*? Ravina **said to him:** Both **this and that,** i.e., each of them **is** considered **a degrading manner.**

Rav Acha of Difti asks Ravina: *bizayon d'mai* — what constituted the degradation here? Was it the object — a mere bundle of vegetables — or was it the setting — the public marketplace? The distinction matters practically: *nafka mina d'kadesh b'chaspa beshuka* — if he betrothed her with money but in the marketplace, or with a bundle of vegetables but in a private home, which of these constitutes *derech bizayon*? Ravina answers: *idei v'idei derech bizayon hu* — both constitute degradation. Either the object or the setting, taken alone, is sufficient to render the betrothal a *derech bizayon*, removing it from the scope of the *nitratza ha'av* concern.

## Public Commitment

### Wife Persuasion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא דְּאָמַר לְקָרִיבַאי, וְהִיא אָמְרָה לְקָרִיבַהּ. כְּפַתֵּיהּ עַד דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: ״תֶּיהְוֵי לְקָרִיבַהּ״. אַדְּאָכְלִי וְשָׁתוּ אֲתָא קָרִיבֵיהּ בְּאִיגָּרָא וְקַדְּשַׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates: A couple wanted to marry off their minor daughter. **That** one, the father, **said:** I want to marry her off **to my relative, while she,** the mother, **said** she wanted to marry the daughter off **to her relative.** His wife pressured him and **forced him until he said to** her: **Let** the girl **be** married **to her,** i.e., the mother's, **relative. While they were eating and drinking** the festive meal before the betrothal, **his relative came to the roof and betrothed her** to himself.

The *Gemara* presents a case reminiscent of one discussed in tractate Nedarim. A father wanted to marry his daughter to his own relative. His wife disagreed and insisted that the daughter be married to her brother — the father's brother-in-law. The wife prevailed: *kafstei* — she pressed him until he conceded and agreed. They then celebrated with a party, announcing the engagement to the wife's brother. And then, while everyone was eating and drinking, the husband's own relative came up to the second floor and gave the daughter *kesef kiddushin* — betrothing her himself, exactly as the father had originally wanted. The question is whether we invoke *nitratza ha'av*: the father clearly had preferred this match all along and only reluctantly agreed to the other arrangement.

### Abaye Finality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּתִיב ״שְׁאֵרִית יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא יַעֲשׂוּ עַוְלָה וְלֹא יְדַבְּרוּ כָזָב״. רָבָא אָמַר: חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם טוֹרֵחַ בִּסְעוּדָה וּמַפְסִידָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara assumes that the father did not desire this betrothal. Why? **Abaye said: It is written: "The remnant of Israel shall not do iniquity, nor speak lies"** (Zephaniah 3:13). The father had agreed that she would marry his wife's relative, and he would stand by his word. Since the betrothal of his minor daughter is dependent on his desire, there is no concern that the father desired his relative's betrothal. **Rava said** a different reason: There is **a presumption that a person does not take the trouble of** preparing **a meal and** then **cause it to be lost.** Since he prepared a festive meal in honor of his daughter's betrothal to his wife's relative, he would not desire a betrothal that would render his efforts for naught.

Abaye rules that we do not invoke *nitratza ha'av* here. He invokes the verse *she'erit Yisrael lo ya'asu avlah velo yedabru kazav* — the remnant of Israel will not act perversely or speak falsehood. Once the father gave his word and committed to the other match — and especially once he made that commitment at a public gathering, announcing the engagement — we do not fear that he will reverse himself. The public commitment constitutes a moral and social bond that forecloses the *nitratza* concern.

### Rava Expense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּלָא טְרַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** these two explanations? The Gemara answers: **The** difference **between them** concerns a case **where he did not take the trouble** of preparing a meal. According to Rava, since he had not gone to any effort, there should be concern that the betrothal is valid. Abaye would hold that since he would not go back on his word, they are not betrothed.

Rava adds a further condition: *chazakah ein adam torach beseuda umafsidah* — there is a presumption that once a man has gone to the trouble and expense of making a party, he will not allow that effort to be wasted. It is not merely the announcement that removes the concern; it is the concrete investment in a celebration. The practical difference between Abaye and Rava is a case where the announcement was made publicly but no party with actual expense took place — *delo tarach*. Abaye would say that even the announcement alone suffices to preclude the reversal; Rava requires that actual expense have been incurred.

## Final Pesak

### Tosafot Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, וְהָלַךְ אָבִיהָ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְעָמְדָה וְנִישֵּׂאת, אָמַר רַב: אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה. רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת, שֶׁמָּא יָבוֹא אָבִיהָ וִימַחֶה, וְנִמְצֵאת זָרָה אוֹכֶלֶת בִּתְרוּמָה לְמַפְרֵעַ. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וְחַשׁ לַהּ רַב לְהָא דְּרַב אַסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** § If a minor **became betrothed with her father's consent, and her father left for overseas, and she went ahead and got married** of her own accord in her father's absence, **Rav says:** If she is an Israelite who married a priest, **she may partake of *teruma* until her father comes and protests,** explicitly stating that he does not agree to the marriage. **Rav Asi said: She may not partake** of *teruma*. **Perhaps her father will come and protest, and it will be found retroactively that a non-priest has partaken of *teruma*.** The Gemara relates: **There was an incident** of this kind, **and Rav was concerned** for this opinion **of Rav Asi** and did not allow a girl in this situation to partake of *teruma*.

The *sugya* concludes with the question of how to rule. Do we follow Rav and Shmuel — who require both *get* and *mi'un*, limited perhaps by Rav Nachman to cases of *shidduchin* and further qualified by the exceptions for *bizayon* and public commitment — or do we follow the line of Ulla and Ravina, who reject the *nitratza ha'av* concern entirely? Tosafot rules explicitly — *befirush amar mar lo sevira lei deShmuel* — and cites Rav Alfas as ruling in the *She'iltot* of Rav Achai that the *halachah* follows Ravina. The rationale is that Ravina was a later authority — *batra* — representing the *sof hora'ah* at the close of the Talmudic period. Rav Ashi, too, concurred. Accordingly: *ein tzarich lo get velo mi'un* — she requires neither a *get* nor a *mi'un* — *va'afilu shidduchu* — even if *shidduchin* had preceded the event. The *nitratza ha'av* concern is entirely set aside.

### Father Absent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַדְּשָׁה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת אָבִיהָ, וְנִיסֵּת שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת, וְאָבִיהָ כָּאן, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אוֹכֶלֶת, רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אֵינָהּ אוֹכֶלֶת. אָמַר עוּלָּא: הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא ״כַּחֹמֶץ לַשִּׁנַּיִם וְכֶעָשָׁן לָעֵינַיִם״. הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה הָתָם דְּקִידּוּשֵׁי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא אָמְרַתְּ לָא אָכְלָה, הָכָא לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another case: If a minor **became betrothed without the consent of her father, and married without** his **consent, and her father is here, Rav Huna said: She may partake** of *teruma*. **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba said: She may not partake** of *teruma*. **Ulla said: This** opinion **of Rav Huna,** that in this case she may partake of *teruma*, is irritating **"as vinegar to the teeth, and as smoke to the eyes"** (Proverbs 10:26), as it contradicts his earlier ruling. **Now** consider: **And what** about **there,** i.e., in a case where her betrothal was with her father's consent, **when** there is at least **betrothal by Torah law, you said she may not partake** of *teruma* in case he did not consent to the marriage. Is it **not all the more so** the case that **here,** i.e., where even the betrothal was performed without her father's consent, she should not be permitted to partake of *teruma*?

A separate question remains: what if the father is not present at all — not because he is dead, but because he has traveled abroad for an extended period? In that case, the *She'iltot* holds that the *ketanah* can enter into a *kiddushin d'rabbanan* — not *d'oraita*, which is never available without the father, but a rabbinic marriage with the status of *mi'un* as the mode of exit. The reasoning parallels the case of a true orphan: *kedin yetomah de'ein lah av detaknu rabbanan kiddushin mishum hefker* — the rabbis instituted *kiddushin* in such cases to prevent the girl from being treated without dignity or taken advantage of. When the father is overseas and effectively unavailable, she is in a comparable position: *v'zu she-halchah l'medinat hayam, harei hu k'yetomah mishum hefker* — this girl, with her father overseas, is like an orphan in terms of the concern that she will be treated as *hefker*. A *kiddushin d'rabbanan* is therefore permitted under such circumstances.

### Remote Marriage

Rav Menachem Me'yuni dissents. He does not accept that a *kiddushin d'rabbanan* can be effected in the father's absence when the father is alive. He cites a specific case: a man traveled to a distant land and left behind a young daughter; she was married off; and Rav Menachem ruled that the marriage could not be maintained. His reason goes beyond a mere disagreement about whether *kiddushin d'rabbanan* applies — he raises a new concern: *chaishinan shema y'kadshana aviha l'ish acher bimkom shehu* — we must fear that the father, wherever he is, has in the meantime married her off to some other man. How then can we permit this marriage to stand?

Rabbeinu Tam responds sharply: if one takes that concern seriously, then even after the father dies the girl could never marry, because perhaps the father had betrothed her to someone while abroad, unknown to anyone here. The *Gemara* does not treat such unknowable possibilities as legally operative concerns. We are not afraid of what an absent father may have done without our knowledge.

The upshot is a layered picture: when the father is present and the *ketanah* marries herself off, the final ruling — following Ravina, Rav Ashi, Rav Alfas, and Tosafot — is that nothing is required, not a *get* and not a *mi'un*, even with prior *shidduchin*. When the father is absent and alive, there is a serious dispute about whether a *kiddushin d'rabbanan* can exist at all, with an additional concern, raised by Rav Menachem, about what the father may have arranged independently. The *sugya* thus ties together the speculative legal question with which it began and a practical reality that the *Rishonim* clearly recognized as pressing in their own communities.
