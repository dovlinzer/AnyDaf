# Nazir 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Heter Mitztaref & K'zayis

### Heter Mitztaref Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל אִיסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה אֵין הֶיתֵּר מִצְטָרֵף לְאִיסּוּר, חוּץ מֵאִיסּוּרֵי נָזִיר, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״מִשְׁרַת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **all prohibitions that are** written **in the Torah, a permitted** substance **does not combine with a forbidden** substance. If one eats a permitted food with a forbidden food and together they constitute the minimum prohibited measure, he is exempt from punishment for this act of consumption. This principle applies to all *halakhot* **except for the prohibitions of a nazirite,** who is liable for eating a mixture of that kind, **as the Torah said** with regard to a nazirite: "Neither shall he drink anything **soaked** in grapes" (Numbers 6:3). This verse indicates that a nazirite is prohibited from consuming not only wine and vinegar, but also any food that was soaked in these liquids.

The sugya we are engaged with on this daf is one of the foundational texts for the study of *kashrut*, and it introduces in sequence three major principles governing the law of mixtures. The first two were treated in the previous session; today we turn to the third, which is the most fundamental of all. But it is worth briefly recalling what came before.

The first principle is *heter mitztaref le-issur* — the permissible food combines with the forbidden food to reach the minimum quantity required for a transgression. The specific application is to the *nazir*, where the verse in the Torah speaks of *mishrat anavim* — something soaked in grapes — which we understand as referring to bread soaked in wine. According to this reading, if the bread and wine together reach a *k'zayis*, the *nazir* incurs lashes even if the wine alone does not constitute a full *k'zayis*. Whether this is because the *heter* genuinely combines with the *issur* toward the minimum, or because the soaking creates an independent prohibition — a prohibition of the *mixture* of wine — is a question that can be read either way from the language of *mishrat*. What the Gemara makes clear, at least according to Rabbi Akiva, is that *heter mitztaref le-issur* is not a general principle; it applies to *nazir* and, as we will see, perhaps to one or two other cases as well.

### Mixture Principles

A related but distinct principle involves cases where forbidden food is recognizably present — *nikar*, physically distinct — within a mixture of permitted food. The example is *kutach habavli*, the Babylonian dipping sauce made with sour milk and bread crumbs, which contains *chametz* on Pesach. There the *issur* does not transform the *heter*; rather, even within the mixture, one remains obligated with respect to the forbidden component. The *heter* does not lend its neutral status to the *issur*. Forbidden things that are *nikar* in a mixture retain their identity — they have not been *batel*.

### K'zayis B'chdei Pras

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲכִילַת פְּרָס דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּכוּתָּח הַבַּבְלִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַנַּח לְכוּתָּח הַבַּבְלִי, דְּלֵיכָּא כְּזַיִת בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye asked him: **But is** eating an olive-bulk in the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread prohibited **by Torah law,** and is one flogged for it? Rav Dimi **said to him: Yes.** Abaye asked in response: **If so, why do the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Eliezer with regard to** eating **Babylonian *kutaḥ*,** a dip that contains bread, on Passover? The Rabbis maintain that one is not punished by Torah law for eating a mixture that contains leaven. Although the Rabbis do not derive from the term "nothing [*kol*]," that leaven in a mixture is forbidden, they should nevertheless hold one liable for eating an olive-bulk of a forbidden substance in the time it takes to eat a half-loaf of bread. Rav Dimi **said to** Abaye: **Leave** aside the case of **Babylonian *kutaḥ*, as there is no** possibility that one will consume an **olive-bulk** of the leaven in the time it takes **to eat a half-loaf** of bread.

The second major principle established yesterday is *k'zayis bichdei achilas pras*: one transgresses if one has consumed a *k'zayis* of forbidden food within the time it takes to eat half a loaf of bread — roughly eight or nine *beitzim* — perhaps ten minutes. This applies even when the forbidden food is embedded within permitted food in a mixture, and even if one is not eating the *issur* directly. If one eats enough of the mixture to have ingested a *k'zayis* of the *issur* within that time span, one transgresses.

### Bittul & Taste Prevention

What was striking about how the Gemara concluded that discussion was that it extended the principle even to a case of *min b'mino* — the same species mixed with the same species — where the forbidden food is not visibly distinct and where *bittul* would ordinarily be expected to apply. The implication seems to be that this proportion — enough to yield a *k'zayis bichdei achilas pras* — is itself sufficient to prevent *bittul*. However, as a matter of *halacha* we do not in fact apply that conclusion to *min b'mino*. The operative rule is that *bittul* is prevented only where the forbidden food is *min b'eino mino* — unlike species mixed together — and where the forbidden food gives off its taste into the mixture. It is precisely that transfer of taste which prevents *bittul*. The practical upshot is that *k'zayis bichdei achilas pras* governs one's liability for consuming the *issur* even when dispersed in a mixture, but it is the impartation of taste that prevents the *bittul* in the first place.

## Taam K'Ikar Intro

### Two Taste Scenarios

We now arrive at the third and central principle of this *sugya*: *taam k'ikar* — the taste of a forbidden thing counts like the thing itself. This is arguably the most consequential concept in practical *kashrut*, and it requires careful unpacking.

There are two distinct scenarios in which *taam k'ikar* is invoked. The first is what is called *taamo ve-lo mamasho* — the taste without the physical substance. Consider a piece of meat that falls into a pot of stew and is then removed. From a human experiential standpoint, we do not perceive that meat as having dissolved into the stew; we experience it as having imparted flavor. Or consider the Gemara's own example: grapes soaked in water. After sitting long enough, the water acquires a wine-like taste. The grapes have imparted taste without leaving any discernible physical residue in the water. This is *taamo ve-lo mamasho* — pure taste, the physical substance absent.

The second scenario is *taamo u-mamasho* — taste accompanied by the actual substance, though the substance is no longer distinguishable. If one pours milk into chicken soup, the milk does not disappear; it is physically present throughout the pot, yet it has become indistinguishable. One would still invoke the *chiddush* of *taam k'ikar* here, because one might otherwise argue that since the milk cannot be identified and pointed to, it loses its halakhic identity and is *batel*.

### Taam K'Ikar Ideas

These two scenarios point to two distinct underlying ideas driving the principle of *taam k'ikar*. The first idea addresses *taamo ve-lo mamasho*: one might have supposed that what remains after the meat is removed from the stew is simply "flavor" — a sensory impression, not a halakhically cognizable substance. The analogy to *reiach* is instructive: if a loaf of bread sits near frying forbidden meat and absorbs its smell, no one suggests the bread is forbidden. So too one might say that the residual taste in the soup is nothing more than a sensory impression, halakhically inert. The first *chiddush* of *taam k'ikar* counters this: no, that taste represents actual minute particles of the forbidden food that have dispersed into the soup. Even if one saw the meat go in whole and come out whole, tiny fragments of it remain. There is real *chazir* in that soup, halakhically speaking.

The second idea addresses *taamo u-mamasho*: even where the physical substance of the *issur* is undeniably present — as when liquid *chazir* dissolves into a soup — one might still argue for *bittul* on the grounds that the *chazir* has lost its distinct identity, that it is no longer a recognizable entity. Here *taam k'ikar* teaches that the very fact one can taste the *chazir* in every spoonful demonstrates that the *chazir* has not ceased to exist. Its persistent perceptibility prevents its disappearance. *Bittul* does not occur.

### Quantity vs Taste Issur

Both of these ideas converge on a shared conclusion: the taste represents the thing itself, and one transgresses for consuming it. And this is what distinguishes *taam k'ikar* sharply from *heter mitztaref le-issur*. Under *taam k'ikar*, no one is suggesting that the soup becomes *chazir*. The claim is only that the dispersed particles of *chazir* remain present and have not been annulled. One transgresses for the quantity of *chazir* actually present, not for the quantity of soup consumed. The soup does not become forbidden in its entirety; only the *chazir* embedded within it remains forbidden, and one's liability corresponds to the actual amount of *chazir* ingested.

This is the position of virtually all the *rishonim* — with the notable exception of the Rosh, which we will not pursue here. One transgresses for the actual quantity of the *issur* present.

There is, however, an alternative approach found in some *rishonim*, which holds that *taam k'ikar* gives rise to an independent prohibition — an *issur ta'am*. On this view, the underlying *issur* substance may in fact be *batel*, or in the case of *taamo ve-lo mamasho* may not be physically present at all. Nevertheless, there is a separate prohibition: just as it is forbidden to eat *chazir*, so too is it forbidden to eat something that carries the taste of *chazir* that originated from actual *chazir*. Critically, this is not a prohibition of *chazir* itself; it is a new category, an *issur ta'am*. The taste must genuinely have originated from the forbidden food — imitation flavoring carries no such prohibition even on this view.

## Abaye's Challenge (II)

### Mishrat Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִמַּאי דְּהַאי ״מִשְׁרַת״ לְהֶיתֵּר מִצְטָרֵף לְאִיסּוּר הוּא דַּאֲתָא? דִּילְמָא לִיתֵּן טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר הוּא דַּאֲתָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to his** opinion: **From where** do you derive **that this** verse: "Nor shall he drink anything **soaked** in grapes" (Numbers 6:3), **comes** to teach the principle that **permitted** food **combines with forbidden** food, as stated by Rabbi Yoḥanan (35b)? **Perhaps** instead **it comes to establish** the principle that the legal status of the **flavor** of a forbidden food is **like** that of its **substance.** This principle states that any food that absorbs the taste of a forbidden item assumes the status of this forbidden item itself.

With this conceptual backdrop established, the Gemara turns to a sharp question posed by Abaye to Rav Dimi. The question is straightforward: *mimai d'hai mishrat l'aitu'ei heter mitztaref le-issur* — how do you know that the verse *mishrat anavim* comes to teach the principle of *heter mitztaref le-issur*? *Dilma l'taam k'ikar hu de'ata* — perhaps it comes to teach the broader and more fundamental principle of *taam k'ikar*?

The Gemara explains the history of Abaye's challenge. Initially, Abaye had been troubled by Rav Dimi's limitation of *heter mitztaref le-issur* exclusively to *nazir*, and had pressed him with a series of questions aiming to apply the principle more broadly. After Rav Dimi provided answers that successfully confined *heter mitztaref le-issur* to *nazir*, Abaye shifted direction entirely: rather than arguing for the broader application of *heter mitztaref le-issur*, he now questions whether *heter mitztaref le-issur* is the correct reading of *mishrat* at all. Perhaps the verse teaches not this narrow and powerful combination-principle, but rather the universal principle that taste counts as the thing itself.

## Mishrat Anavim Derash

### Mishrat Text & Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״מִשְׁרַת״ לִיתֵּן טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר, שֶׁאִם שָׁרָה עֲנָבִים בְּמַיִם וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶן טַעַם יַיִן — חַיָּיב. וּמִכָּאן אַתָּה דָּן כׇּל אִיסּוּרִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is relevant **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The term **"soaked"** serves **to establish** the principle that the legal status of the **flavor** of a forbidden food is **like** that of its **substance. As, if** a nazirite soaked **grapes in water and** the water **has the taste of wine,** he **is liable** to receive punishment for drinking this liquid, as it assumes the status of wine. **And from here you derive** the *halakha* with regard **to all prohibitions of the Torah;** in all cases, the legal status of the taste of a forbidden food is like that of its substance.

The Gemara now brings the textual basis for deriving *taam k'ikar* from *mishrat anavim*. The *braita* reads: *mishrat anavim bamayim* — if grapes are immersed in water and the water takes on the taste of wine, one is liable. *U-mikan atah dan l'chol issurin she-ba-Torah* — from here you extend this principle to all prohibitions in the Torah. The word *mishrat* points to a situation in which there is pure taste — the water has acquired the flavor of wine — and the *nazir* is nonetheless liable. This establishes that taste is treated as the thing itself.

### Kal V'Chomer Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה נָזִיר שֶׁאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם, וְאֵין אִיסּוּרוֹ אִיסּוּר הֲנָאָה, וְיֵשׁ הֶיתֵּר לְאִיסּוּרוֹ — עָשָׂה בּוֹ טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר. כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם, שֶׁאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר עוֹלָם, וְאִיסּוּרָן אִיסּוּר הֲנָיָיה, וְאֵין הֶיתֵּר לְאִיסּוּרָן — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה בּוֹ טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר?!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains the derivation: **And just as** with regard to **a nazirite, whose prohibition** against eating grapes **is not a permanent prohibition,** as he will be permitted to eat grapes once his term of naziriteship is over, **and** furthermore **his prohibition is not a prohibition** against deriving **benefit** from wine, **and there is** a way **to permit his prohibition** against eating grape products by requesting from a halakhic authority to dissolve his vow, and nevertheless, in **his** case the Torah **rendered** the legal status of the **flavor** of food **like** that of its **substance.** With regard to a forbidden mixture of **diverse kinds in a vineyard,** i.e., grain seeds sown with grape seeds, **whose prohibition is a permanent prohibition and whose prohibition is a prohibition** against deriving **benefit, and there is no** way **to permit their prohibition, is it not right that** the Torah **should render** the legal status of the **flavor** of its forbidden food **like** that of its **substance?**

The extension to all other prohibitions in the Torah is accomplished through a *kal v'chomer*. The *nazir* prohibition is a lenient one in multiple respects: *ein issuro issur olam* — it is not permanent, *nezirut* expires after thirty days; *ein issuro issur hana'ah* — a *nazir* is not prohibited from deriving benefit from wine; and *yeish heter le-issuro* — there is a legitimate path to becoming a *nazir* (one is permitted to place oneself in that situation, and furthermore one can have the vow dissolved by a *chacham*). Despite all these leniencies, the Torah treats the taste of wine like wine itself for the *nazir*. How much more so, then, should *taam k'ikar* apply to *kilayim* of the vine, which is an *issur olam*, an *issur hana'ah*, and — on the attractive reading — an *issur* one may not create: one is prohibited from planting *kilayim* in the first place. *Eino din she-ye'aseh bo taam k'ikar* — is it not a matter of logical inference that *taam k'ikar* would apply there as well? And the same argument extends to virtually every other prohibition in the Torah, which is at minimum an *issur olam*, unlike *nezirut*.

The Gemara notes that *orlah* shares two of the three features — it is an *issur olam* and an *issur hana'ah* — though on the reading that *yeish heter le-issuro* means one is permitted to create the prohibition, *orlah* also shares that leniency, since planting a tree is permitted.

### Taam Not Combination

It is important to appreciate what this *derasha* is and is not teaching. The verse is not being read as combining the bread and the wine together to reach a minimum *k'zayis* of the combined mixture. That would be *heter mitztaref le-issur*. Rather, the verse is teaching that when the taste of grapes is present in the water, the water-with-grape-taste is treated as though it contains the grapes themselves. The transgression is for having consumed something carrying that taste — not for having combined two quantities.

This distinction also clarifies what the liability attaches to. One does not transgress for every *k'zayis* of bread or every *revi'is* of water one consumes; one transgresses when one has ingested enough of the absorbed taste — enough of what represents the actual *issur* — to constitute the minimum quantity for liability. In that respect, *taam k'ikar* remains anchored to the actual quantity of the *issur* present, rather than transforming the *heter* into *issur*.

A related question arises: when grapes soak in water and the water changes color, does not that visible change make the *issur* *nikar*, such that we need no special principle to establish its presence? The answer requires precision about what *nikar* means in this context. *Nikar* in the halakhic sense does not merely mean that one can detect that something was mixed in. It means the forbidden item is physically distinct — identifiable, separable, possessed of its own independent existence. If I can take it out, point to it, and remove it, it has not been *batel* because it never lost its identity. But when taste or color disperses through a medium, the forbidden substance is no longer distinct in that sense. It has lost its separate identity; what remains is flavored or colored water. That is precisely why *bittul* becomes a conceptual possibility in the first place, and precisely why *taam k'ikar* is needed as a *chiddush* to counter it. As for whether a change in color, rather than taste, would serve the same function as taste in preventing *bittul* for food prohibitions — the general rule is that color governs in non-food contexts (as in the case of blood mixed with water to be sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach*, where the criterion is whether the water retains the color of the blood), while for food, the operative criterion is taste, since it is taste that constitutes the primary experience of eating.

## Abaye's Challenge (II)

### Rabbi Akiva's Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן: רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ כִּי קָאָמַר לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּהָכָא, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ שָׁרָה פִּיתּוֹ בְּיַיִן וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְצָרֵף כְּזַיִת — חַיָּיב, וּמִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא הוּא דְּאִיכָּא כְּזַיִת בָּעֵינָא!
>
> **Translation:** **One of the Sages said to** Abaye: **When Rabbi Abbahu said** that Rabbi Yoḥanan said that the principle of permitted food combining with forbidden food is derived from the term "soaked," he spoke in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva.** The Gemara asks: To **which** statement of **Rabbi Akiva** is the Gemara referring? **If we say** it is referring to the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva of this** mishna, **as we learned** (*Shevuot* 21b) that **Rabbi Akiva says: Even** if **a nazirite soaked his bread in wine, and** the bread and the wine **contain** enough **to combine to** constitute **an olive-bulk, he is liable; but from where** do we know that Rabbi Akiva means an olive-bulk taken from the bread and the wine together? **Perhaps that** ruling applies only **when there is an olive-bulk** of wine **as is,** without the bread?

After Rav Dimi's position has been explained and the *braita* establishing *taam k'ikar* from *mishrat anavim* has been quoted, the Gemara addresses the coherence of Abaye's challenge. His question was: why read *mishrat* as *heter mitztaref le-issur* when it appears to be the source for the universal principle of *taam k'ikar*? The answer is that when Rav Dimi attributed *heter mitztaref le-issur* to *mishrat anavim*, he was presenting that reading only *according to Rabbi Akiva*. Rabbi Akiva reads *mishrat* as the narrow but powerful principle of *heter mitztaref le-issur*, applicable to *nazir* alone. The *braita* that reads *mishrat* as *taam k'ikar* and extends it to *kol issurin she-ba-Torah* reflects the position of the Rabbis (*rabbanan*), who understand the verse differently. There is in fact textual evidence that both *derashot* appear in the *midreshei halacha* on the verse of *mishrat anavim* — the *derasha* for *taam k'ikar* and the *derasha* for *heter mitztaref le-issur* — and the division between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis determines which reading is operative.

This resolution prompts the natural follow-up: if Rabbi Akiva uses *mishrat* for *heter mitztaref le-issur* rather than *taam k'ikar*, where does Rabbi Akiva derive the principle of *taam k'ikar*? This is the next question the Gemara takes up.

## Akiva's Taam Source

### Basar B'Chalav Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן: רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ כִּי קָאָמַר לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּהָכָא, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ שָׁרָה פִּיתּוֹ בְּיַיִן וְיֵשׁ בּוֹ כְּדֵי לְצָרֵף כְּזַיִת — חַיָּיב, וּמִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא הוּא דְּאִיכָּא כְּזַיִת בָּעֵינָא! וְכִי תֵּימָא: מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִתַּנָּא קַמָּא, דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁתֶּה רְבִיעִית יַיִן! אֶלָּא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּבָרַיְיתָא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: נָזִיר שֶׁשָּׁרָה פִּתּוֹ בְּיַיִן, וְאָכַל כְּזַיִת מִפַּת וּמִיַּיִן — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **One of the Sages said to** Abaye: **When Rabbi Abbahu said** that Rabbi Yoḥanan said that the principle of permitted food combining with forbidden food is derived from the term "soaked," he spoke in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva.** The Gemara asks: To **which** statement of **Rabbi Akiva** is the Gemara referring? **If we say** it is referring to the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva of this** mishna, **as we learned** (*Shevuot* 21b) that **Rabbi Akiva says: Even** if **a nazirite soaked his bread in wine, and** the bread and the wine **contain** enough **to combine to** constitute **an olive-bulk, he is liable; but from where** do we know that Rabbi Akiva means an olive-bulk taken from the bread and the wine together? **Perhaps that** ruling applies only **when there is an olive-bulk** of wine **as is,** without the bread? **And if you would say:** In that case, **what** is the purpose **of stating** this ruling? What is the novelty of Rabbi Akiva's statement if the mixture contains an olive-bulk of wine? One can say that it serves **to exclude** the opinion of **the first *tanna*, who said** that he is liable **only if he drinks a quarter**-*log* **of wine.** Rabbi Akiva emphasizes that one is liable even if he drinks the amount of an olive-bulk. **Rather,** the reference is to the following statement of **Rabbi Akiva in a *baraita*. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says: A nazirite who soaked his bread in wine and ate an olive-bulk of** the mixture of **bread and wine is liable.** This *baraita* indicates that according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva a permitted substance combines with a forbidden substance.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Avya asks Rav Ashi: according to Rabbi Akiva, who uses *v'chol mishrat* for the narrow principle of *heter mitztaref le-issur*, where does he know *taam k'ikar* from? The Gemara's first answer is that Rabbi Akiva derives it from *basar b'chalav* — meat cooked with milk.

The logic is straightforward. When meat is cooked in milk, all that the milk acquires from the meat — or that the meat acquires from the milk — is flavor. If you cook meat in milk and then remove the meat, and you drink the remaining milk, you are consuming nothing but the taste of meat. There is no meat physically present. And yet it is forbidden. Does this not demonstrate that taste counts as the thing itself? Similarly, if you remove the milk from consideration and focus on the meat, the meat has absorbed the taste of milk without there being milk *per se* remaining in it — yet it is forbidden. The classic case of *taamo ve-lo mamasho* appears to be vividly illustrated by *basar b'chalav*, and one would think the prohibition proves that *taam* counts as the *ikar*.

The Rabbis push back: *basar b'chalav lo gamrinan d'chiddush hu* — we cannot derive general halakhic principles from *basar b'chalav* because it is an anomaly. Its foundational structure is paradoxical: meat alone is permitted, milk alone is permitted, and combining them creates a prohibition. That is deeply unusual. The Gemara tests whether this claimed anomalousness is truly distinctive by raising *kilayim*: there too, grapes are permitted, wheat is permitted, and planting them together creates a prohibition. The Gemara responds that *kilayim* is indeed unusual in that sense, but *basar b'chalav* carries a second, more pointed anomaly: *detara lei kulei yama be-chalva, sharei* — if you soak meat in milk all day, so that by the end the milk has fully permeated the meat and you can taste milk throughout the flesh, it is nonetheless biblically permitted. Only when you cook them together does the prohibition apply. This is the decisive peculiarity: two things can be combined with full interpenetration of taste and remain *muttar*, and become *assur* only through the specific act of cooking. That cooking-requirement detaches *basar b'chalav* from any general principle about taste.

There is a sharper way to articulate the Gemara's point. The prohibition of *basar b'chalav* is not defined as "eating meat commingled with milk." It is defined as eating meat and milk that have been cooked together. The verse says *lo tevashel gedi ba-chalev imo* — do not cook a kid in its mother's milk. The forbidden act generates a forbidden product, but the product is defined by the cooking, not by the presence of mixed taste. If one could somehow cook meat and milk together without any transfer of taste occurring — which the Gemara addresses by noting that cooking without taste-transfer would not constitute *derech bishul* — one might still have *basar b'chalav de-oraita*. In any case, the fact that soaking without cooking produces no biblical prohibition demonstrates that the *issur* is not tracking the mixture of tastes. The milk does not become forbidden because it absorbed the flavor of meat; it becomes forbidden because it was cooked with meat. One cannot therefore derive from *basar b'chalav* that taste-as-such triggers the prohibition of the underlying substance.

This also has consequences for how one understands the prohibitions of benefit and consumption derivable from *basar b'chalav* — a topic that properly belongs to the beginning of *Chullin* — but the core point here is that *basar b'chalav* cannot serve as the source for a general principle of *taam k'ikar*.

### Kelei Midian Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא יָלִיף מִגִּי'וּלֵי עוֹבְדֵי כּוֹכָבִים. גִּי'וּלֵי עוֹבְדֵי כּוֹכָבִים לָאו טַעַם בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, וְאָסוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore says: *ela yalif mi-gi'ulei ovdei kochavim* — Rabbi Akiva derives *taam k'ikar* from the vessels of Midian. When the Jewish people took vessels from the Midianites, the Torah required those vessels to undergo a process — understood halakhically as a kashering process — before they could be used. The underlying reason is that those vessels had been used to cook non-kosher food, and the absorbed taste of that food would transfer into anything subsequently cooked in them. The verse states *kol davar asher yavo ba-esh* — anything that had been brought over fire must be kashered through fire. *Gi'ulei ovdei kochavim lav ta'ama be-alma hu, ve-assur* — what those vessels impart is nothing but taste, absorbed flavor from non-kosher food, and yet that taste renders the subsequently cooked food forbidden. This is precisely *taam k'ikar*: the taste of the forbidden food counts as the forbidden food itself.

### Kelei Midian Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, מִבָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב לָא גָּמְרִינַן, דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא. מַאי חִידּוּשֵׁיהּ? אִילֵּימָא דְּהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ שְׁרֵי, וּבַהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי אָסוּר, כִּלְאַיִם נָמֵי: הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ שְׁרֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ שְׁרֵי, וּבַהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי אָסוּר! אֶלָּא: דְּאִי תָּרוּ לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא בַּחֲלָבָא — שְׁרֵי, וּמְבַשֵּׁיל לֵיהּ בַּשּׁוֹלֵי — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis respond that *gi'ulei ovdei kochavim* cannot serve as the basis for the general principle of *taam k'ikar*, because it too is a *chiddush* — a special halakhic category that does not follow the normal rules of taste. The reason: *be-chol ha-Torah kulah, noten ta'am li-fgam mutar* — throughout the entire Torah, if the taste imparted by a forbidden substance worsens the food into which it is imparted, the food remains permitted. This is the principle of *ta'am le-fgam*. But when taste is absorbed into the walls of a vessel and then re-released into subsequently cooked food, the reasonable assumption is that the re-released taste is by definition somewhat degraded — it has been absorbed, sat in walls, and been released again — and thus constitutes *ta'am le-fgam*. Yet the *kelim* remain forbidden despite this. The Torah apparently forbids the use of these vessels even when the taste they impart should logically be *ta'am le-fgam*. Since *kelei Midian* deviates from the standard rules of taste, it cannot be the basis for deriving those standard rules. **Rather,** the novelty is **that if one soaks** meat **in milk all day, it is permitted** by Torah law, despite the fact that the meat certainly absorbed some taste of the milk, whereas if **one cooked** meat in milk even for a short time, the mixture **is forbidden** by Torah law. The novelty is that it is not the fact that they are mixed together that renders meat and milk forbidden, but the act of cooking.

The reason: *be-chol ha-Torah kulah, noten ta'am li-fgam mutar* — throughout the entire Torah, if the taste imparted by a forbidden substance worsens the food into which it is imparted, the food remains permitted. Yet the *kelim* remain forbidden despite this. The Torah apparently forbids the use of these vessels even when the taste they impart should logically be *ta'am le-fgam*. Since *kelei Midian* deviates from the standard rules of taste, it cannot be the basis for deriving those standard rules.

### Ben Yomo & Identity

Rabbi Akiva's response to this is the concept of *ben yomo*. Rav Huna bar Chiyya states: *lo asra Torah ela kdeira bat yoma de-lav noten ta'am li-fgam hu* — the Torah forbade use of the vessel only on the same day it was used, because only on that day is the absorbed taste still fresh and therefore *not* a bad taste. This is why we rule that *bedi'avad*, if one cooked in a vessel that had not been used for non-kosher within the last twenty-four hours — *eino ben yomo* — the food is permitted: because the taste that transfers is *ta'am le-fgam*, a degraded taste. The *rishonim* ask the obvious question: if waiting twenty-four hours renders it permitted, why did the soldiers at Midian go through the elaborate kashering process rather than simply waiting? The answer is practical: they wanted to use the vessels immediately. The prohibition of *lecha'tchila* — which is *miderabbanan* — prevents one from cooking in an *eino ben yomo* vessel on purpose, but if one waited, the *de'oraita* prohibition would not apply.

According to Rabbi Akiva, then, the vessels of Midian were forbidden because they were *ben yomo* — their absorbed taste was still good — and therefore the taste they would impart would be a genuine, flavorful taste, not *ta'am le-fgam*. This fits squarely within the normal rules of *ta'am*, and the principle derived from them — that taste counts as the thing itself — is a genuine general principle, not a special law applicable only to vessels.

The Rabbis disagree: *kdeira bat yoma nami ee efshar de-la pagma purta* — even on the very same day, a vessel cannot absorb and re-release taste without that taste being at least slightly degraded. The transfer through walls inevitably introduces some diminution of quality. Therefore, even the *ben yomo* case falls under *ta'am le-fgam*, and *kelei Midian* cannot ground a general principle of *taam k'ikar*.

This debate also opens a deeper conceptual question about the nature of the laws of *kashering kelim* altogether. Are they fundamentally about absorbed taste that will transfer into food — a concern with what the food becomes — or are they about the identity of the vessel itself? A vessel that was used to cook *treif* acquires an identity as a *treif* vessel, and the prohibition is not to use *treif* vessels. This second framework would explain a *nafka mina* that arises in *kashrut* practice: when kashering a vessel, the method of kashering is determined by the vessel's predominant use, not by the most intensive use it ever experienced. If kashering were purely about extracting absorbed taste, every modality of use would need to be addressed at the highest level. But if the concern is the vessel's identity, then the identity is defined by its predominant function, and kashering restores that identity accordingly. The concept of *ben yomo* fits neatly into an identity framework as well: the vessel retains its clear and fresh identity on the day it was used; once past, that identity fades.

## Rabbanan vs Akiva

### Kelei Midian Special

Having established that each proposed source has been challenged as a *chiddush* — *basar b'chalav* because its prohibition is generated by cooking rather than by commingled taste, and *kelei Midian* because the taste transfer from vessel walls is arguably always somewhat degraded — the Gemara now draws out the positions of Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis in full. The Rabbis hold that *kelei Midian* indeed cannot serve as the basis for the general principle of *taam k'ikar*, precisely because even *ben yomo* involves some degree of degraded taste. It is a special halakhic category, isolated from the normal operation of taste rules, and one cannot derive the general principle of *taam k'ikar* from it.

### Kol HaTorah Kulah

The Rabbis therefore derive *taam k'ikar* from *mishrat anavim*, reading the verse as teaching that the taste of wine in water is treated as wine itself, and extending this principle — via the *kal v'chomer* from *nazir* as a lenient prohibition to all more stringent prohibitions — to *kol ha-Torah kulah*, the entirety of Torah law. This is the phrase that appears in the *braita*: *mikan atah dan le-chol issurin she-ba-Torah* — from here you reason to all the prohibitions in the Torah. The phrase *kol ha-Torah kulah* does not indicate that the principle applies only at a *de'oraita* level; it means that the principle is not to be confined to the specific case from which it is derived, whether *nazir* or *basar b'chalav* or *kelim*. It becomes a cross-categorical rule.

## Nazir vs Chatas

### Shnei Ketuvim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּבָרַיְיתָא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: נָזִיר שֶׁשָׁרָה פִּתּוֹ בְּיַיִן, וְאָכַל כְּזַיִת מִפַּת וּמִיַּיִן — חַיָּיב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַוְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּקָא מוֹקֵים לֵיהּ לְהַאי ״וְכׇל מִשְׁרַת״ לְהֶיתֵּר מִצְטָרֵף לְאִיסּוּר, לִיתֵּן טַעַם כְּעִיקָּר מְנָא לֵיהּ? יָלֵיף מִבָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reference is to the following statement of **Rabbi Akiva in a *baraita*. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says: A nazirite who soaked his bread in wine and ate an olive-bulk of** the mixture of **bread and wine is liable.** This *baraita* indicates that according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva a permitted substance combines with a forbidden substance. **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Avya, said to Rav Ashi: According to Rabbi Akiva, who establishes** the verse **"neither** shall he drink **anything soaked"** (Numbers 6:3) as referring **to** the principle that the **permitted combines with the forbidden, from where does he** derive the principle that the legal status of the **flavor** is **like** that of the **substance** itself? The Gemara answers: **He derives** this principle **from** the prohibition of **meat** cooked **in milk.**

We now reach the question of why Rabbi Akiva cannot extend *heter mitztaref le-issur* — which he derives from *mishrat anavim* in *nazir* — to the rest of the Torah, the same way the Rabbis extend *taam k'ikar* from *mishrat anavim* everywhere. The Gemara's answer invokes the rule of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-echad ein melamdin* — when an identical rule appears in two separate places in the Torah, that redundancy indicates that the rule is exceptional rather than general. Had the Torah intended the rule to apply everywhere, it would have stated it once and we would derive the general principle. The fact that it is stated twice suggests instead that the two cases are precisely the exceptions, not illustrations of a rule. The rule as such does not exist; only those two cases carry that *din*.

### Chatas Source

The second locus of *heter mitztaref le-issur*, alongside *nazir*, is the *korban chatas* — the sin-offering. The verse states *kol asher yiga bivsara yikdash* — anything that touches the flesh of the *chatas* becomes sanctified. The Gemara understands this through the word *bivsara*: the contact must result in actual absorption, *ad she-yivla bivsarato* — until it is absorbed into its flesh. Once absorbed, the absorbing object takes on the status of the *chatas*: if the *chatas* was invalid, the other object becomes invalid; if it was valid, the other object must be eaten under the stringencies of the *chatas* — by *kohanim* only, within one day.

This is not yet transparently *heter mitztaref le-issur* — it might appear to be no more than *taam k'ikar* applied within *kodashim*. Tosafot clarifies the connection. Since we already know *taam k'ikar* as a general principle from elsewhere, the Torah's reiteration of the principle in the context of *chatas* cannot be teaching *taam k'ikar* as such — we already know that. It must therefore be teaching something beyond *taam k'ikar*: namely, that if half a *zayit* of *chatas* fat is absorbed into half a *zayit* of *shlamim* flesh, one who eats a *k'zayis* of that combined mixture is liable as though having eaten a *k'zayis* of *chatas*. The *heter* — the *shlamim* portion — combines with the *issur* — the *chatas* portion — to constitute the minimum quantity. That is *heter mitztaref le-issur*.

### Akiva vs Rabbanan

Rabbi Akiva's reading, then, is that both *mishrat* in *nazir* and the *chatas* verse teach *heter mitztaref le-issur*. Since the rule appears in two places, the principle of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-echad* applies, and we cannot generalize: *heter mitztaref le-issur* applies only in *nazir* and in *chatas*, nowhere else. The Rabbis, by contrast, read *mishrat* in *nazir* as teaching *taam k'ikar*, which is a general principle applicable everywhere.

### Kodashim Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרַבָּנַן, מִבָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב לָא גָּמְרִינַן, דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis claim that **we do not derive** other prohibitions **from meat** cooked **in milk, as** that prohibition **is a novelty,** and one does not learn general *halakhot* from unusual cases.

The Rabbis' counter-argument is that the two cases are not truly redundant, because *tzrichi* — both were necessary. Had the Torah written the *chatas* rule alone, one could never have derived *nazir* from it: *mi-kodashim lo yafinan* — we do not apply stringencies of *kodashim* to ordinary prohibitions outside the Temple domain. So the *chatas* case, standing alone, would be understood as a unique law of *kodashim* inapplicable beyond that domain. Conversely, had the Torah written it only in *nazir*, one could not have derived *chatas* from it — because *nazir* carries its own distinctive stringency, in that even the grape skin (*chartzan*) and pit are forbidden to the *nazir*, which might lead us to say that this combination-principle is unique to *nazir*'s unusually comprehensive prohibitions and does not apply even to *chatas*. Since both cases were necessary, there is no redundancy, and the rule of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-echad* does not apply; one can in principle derive a general rule.

### Final Resolution

The Gemara concludes with the full positions of both sides articulated precisely. The Rabbis hold that what is written in *nazir* — specifically *mishrat anavim* — teaches *taam k'ikar*, a general principle extending to all prohibitions in the Torah. The *chatas* verse teaches *heter mitztaref le-issur*, but that is a *chumra* unique to *kodashim*, not derivable elsewhere: *chumra mi-kodashim lo gamrinan*. And *mishrat* in *nazir* extends everywhere — *mikan atah dan le-chol issurin she-ba-Torah* — because what is written in the domain of *nazir*, a lenient prohibition, certainly applies to all more stringent prohibitions.

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, takes *mishrat* in *nazir* as teaching *heter mitztaref le-issur*. He already knows *taam k'ikar* from *kelei Midian* — specifically from the *ben yomo* case, in which the taste transferred from the vessel was still fresh and full, fitting normal taste rules. Since he derives *taam k'ikar* independently from *kelei Midian*, he is free to use *mishrat* for the different and more powerful principle of *heter mitztaref le-issur*. And since *heter mitztaref le-issur* then appears in two places — *nazir* and *chatas* — the rule of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-echad* applies, and Rabbi Akiva limits *heter mitztaref le-issur* to precisely those two cases.

The resolution is thus elegant and symmetrical. One way or another, there exists a Torah-level principle of *taam k'ikar*: for the Rabbis, it derives from *mishrat anavim* and applies universally; for Rabbi Akiva, it derives from *kelei Midian* and applies equally universally. The difference is only in what one does with *mishrat anavim* in *nazir* — whether it teaches the general principle of taste, or the narrower and more intense principle of combination.
