# Bava Batra 152 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Matnat Shchiv Meira Basi…

### Kol vs. Miktzat

We pick up toward the bottom of 151b, in the middle of drawing together the general rules of *matnat shchiv meira*. The *mishna* itself distinguished between two fundamental categories: *kol nekhasav* — a gift of all one's property — and *miktzat nekhasav* — a gift of only part of one's property. These two categories serve as the baseline for the *halacha* of *amad chozer* and *amad eino chozer*, that is, whether recovering from one's illness voids the gift.

The *gemara*, however, introduces several additional distinctions that do not appear anywhere in the *mishna*, distinctions that many *bnei yeshiva* assume to be the ironclad rule of *shchiv meira* and yet which represent genuine *chiddushim*. Chief among these is the principle that a *shchiv meira* does not require a *kinyan*: *divrei shchiv meira ke-khtuvim u-ke-msurim damu*, the words of a dying person are treated as if they have already been written and transferred. This is not an assumption of the *mishna* and not everyone initially agreed to it.

### Kinyan Requirements

The *gemara* ultimately concludes that this special dispensation — the exemption from requiring a *kinyan* — is limited to the case of *kol nekhasav*. A gift of *kol nekhasav* does not require a *kinyan*. A gift of *miktzat nekhasav*, by contrast, does require a *kinyan*, because outside of the special *halachic* framework of *matnat shchiv meira*, such a gift operates like an ordinary *matnat bari* and must follow the standard rules of acquisition.

### Amad Chozer Rule

The *gemara* further introduces the rule of *chozer b'matnaso* and the parallel rule of *diatiki mevateles diatiki* — a later will overrides an earlier one. These two rules are related but distinct. The principle of *amad chozer* concerns the case where the donor recovers: because the entire gift was predicated on the assumption of impending death, if the person gets better, the gift is automatically voided. The rule of *diatiki mevateles diatiki* addresses a different scenario: the donor in fact dies, so one might think the original gift should stand; yet the *gemara*'s *chiddush* is that even so, a later will and testament can override an earlier one. Both of these rules — *amad chozer* and *diatiki mevateles diatiki* — are limited to the classic category of *matnat shchiv meira* of *kol nekhasav*.

### Metzaveh Makhmas Misa

The *gemara* also introduces the concept of *metzaveh makhmas misa* as a way of bringing a *miktzat nekhasav* case within the special *halacha* of *shchiv meira*. The mere fact that a person is bedridden does not automatically mean that every transaction they conduct is a *matnat shchiv meira*. A person may be ill for months and still be conducting ordinary business. It is therefore possible for someone lying on their deathbed to give away part of their property simply as a normal gift, operating fully within the *matnat bari* framework.

However, if the donor is explicit — if they say, in effect, "I am giving this because I fear I am about to die" — then even a gift of *miktzat nekhasav* is elevated into the *shchiv meira* category. The *gemara*'s example is the woman who, in her distress, gave away her necklace. When a person is thus explicit about giving *makhmas misa*, all the special *halachot* of *shchiv meira* apply even to a partial gift. The key point is that *metzaveh makhmas misa* requires an explicit verbal declaration; it cannot be inferred merely from context.

## Rava vs. Rav Nachman

### Shayar Karka Case

The *gemara* records that Rava challenged Rav Nachman on the question of whether *miktzat nekhasav* requires a *kinyan*. Rava had affirmed, and indeed defended forcefully, the position of Rav Nachman that *miktzat nekhasav* does require a *kinyan* — dismissing those who attributed the opposite view to Rav Nachman as saying something foolish. Having established that position, Rava then proceeds to challenge it from the *mishna*.

The *mishna* states: *shayer karka kol she-hu, matnaso kayyemet* — if the *shchiv meira* left over any amount of land for himself, his gift remains valid even if he recovers. This is the classic *miktzat nekhasav* case. Rava's challenge is: does this *mishna* not demonstrate that *miktzat nekhasav* is valid even without a *kinyan*? Just as *kol nekhasav* is valid without a *kinyan*, perhaps this case of *miktzat* is also valid without one?

### Shimuel's Answer

The *gemara* responds: *lo, de-kanu minei* — no, the *mishna* is speaking of a case where a *kinyan* was in fact performed. The *mishna* thus speaks of both categories with a *kinyan*: in the *miktzat* case the gift remains valid upon recovery, and in the *kol nekhasav* case the gift is voided upon recovery.

But this answer immediately raises a further difficulty. If the *mishna*'s case of *kol nekhasav* — where recovery voids the gift — also involved a *kinyan*, why does the *kinyan* not convert the transaction into a *matnat bari*, which would be *amad eino chozer*? If performing a *kinyan* defines the transaction as operating in the *matnat bari* mode, then recovery should not void it.

The *gemara* answers by quoting Shimuel's ruling: *shchiv meira she-kasav kol nekhasav la-acherim, af al pi she-kanu mi-yado, amad chozer* — a *shchiv meira* who gives all his property to others, even if a *kinyan* was performed, the gift is voided if he recovers. The reason, the *gemara* explains, is *bi-yadu'a she-lo haya kinyan ella makhmas ha-misa* — once one is in the *kol nekhasav* category, it is evident that the entire act, including the *kinyan*, was done in anticipation of death. The *kinyan* therefore does not shift the transaction into the *matnat bari* framework. Whether or not a *kinyan* accompanies a *kol nekhasav* gift, it remains a *matnat shchiv meira* in every respect: no *kinyan* is needed to make it effective, and even a *kinyan* does not prevent it from being voided upon recovery.

### Rochel Story

Rav Mesharsheya then challenged Rava on the requirement of a *kinyan* for *miktzat nekhasav* from a *ma'aseh*: the story of the mother of the sons of Rochel. This woman was ill and said, "Let my necklace be given to my daughter" — a necklace worth twelve *maneh*, which was thus a *miktzat nekhasav* gift. She then died. The *chachamim* upheld her words. Apparently, this was an entirely verbal gift with no *kinyan*, yet it was honored. This seems to demonstrate that even *miktzat nekhasav* does not require a *kinyan*.

### Metzaveh Response

The *gemara* answers: *hasam be-metzaveh makhmas misa* — that case involved an explicit declaration of *metzaveh makhmas misa*. The woman's cry of distress placed her squarely in the *shchiv meira* category even for a partial gift. It was precisely this explicit invocation of imminent death that elevated the *miktzat* gift into the special *halacha*, such that it required no *kinyan* and would likewise be voided upon recovery.

This answer, it must be said, begins to feel like a remarkably flexible tool: any time a *miktzat* case appears to function without a *kinyan*, one can always resolve the difficulty by invoking *metzaveh makhmas misa*. Yet the *gemara*'s consistent deployment of this answer reflects a principled distinction: every case where *miktzat nekhasav* appears to operate without a *kinyan* turns out, on examination, to involve either *kol nekhasav* or an explicit *metzaveh makhmas misa*. The *gemara* is insisting on a sharp line between the two categories.

It is worth pausing here to distinguish *matnat shchiv meira* from the related but distinct principle of *mitzvah le-kayyem divrei ha-meit* — the moral obligation to fulfill the wishes of the deceased. The latter is not a legal transfer; it does not cause property to change hands automatically. It is rather a moral charge upon the heirs, as in the case of David's final instructions to Solomon. *Matnat shchiv meira* operates as an actual legal acquisition. The question of which cases fall into which category — and whether the sons of Rochel may have been acting on *mitzvah le-kayyem divrei ha-meit* rather than on the legal force of *matnat shchiv meira* — is a separate discussion.

## Get vs. Money Case

### Get Comparison

Ravina challenged Rava from a *baraita* involving two parallel scenarios. In the first, a dying man says: "Give this *get* to my wife and this writ of manumission to my slave," and then dies. The *baraita* rules that the *get* and writ of manumission may not be given after death, because the man's legal capacity to divorce his wife or free his slave does not survive him; his wife has become a widow and his slave passes to his heirs. In the second scenario, the man says: "Give one hundred *maneh* to Reuven," and then dies. Here the *baraita* rules that the money is given after death.

### No Kinyan Assumption

How do we know there was no *kinyan* in the money case? The *gemara* reasons *dumya de-get*: it is analogous to the *get*. By a *get*, a *kinyan* is not the operative mechanism and would be irrelevant to its effectiveness. Since the *baraita* presents the monetary case in exact parallel to the *get* case, it would be incongruous to introduce a *kinyan* into the money scenario. The entire context of the *baraita* is one of verbal instructions alone, with no further act of acquisition. Yet the money transfer is upheld. This seems to demonstrate that a *miktzat* verbal gift without a *kinyan* is valid — directly contrary to what Rav Nachman and Rava maintain.

### Money Case Resolution

The *gemara* again answers: *hacha nami de-metzaveh makhmas misa* — here too the case involves an explicit *metzaveh makhmas misa*, which places the gift within the *shchiv meira* category even without a *kinyan*. Alternatively, Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua adds a second resolution: *de-tani hani matnita be-machalek kol nekhasav* — the *baraita* is dealing with a case where the man was distributing all of his property. Either the hundred *maneh* constituted his entire estate, or he distributed this sum in the context of disposing of all his assets together. In either reading, the case falls under *kol nekhasav*, which requires no *kinyan* and is squarely within the *matnat shchiv meira* framework.

## Metzaveh Kinyan Need

### Rav Huna's Ruling

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua makes an important clarification at this point: *metzaveh makhmas misa b'alma ba'ei kinyan* — even a case of *metzaveh makhmas misa* requires a *kinyan* as a general rule. This is a significant qualification. One might have thought that invoking *metzaveh makhmas misa* entirely dissolves the requirement of a *kinyan* for any partial gift. Rav Huna corrects this impression. The *metzaveh makhmas misa* category indeed places the donor within the *shchiv meira* framework, with all that entails regarding *amad chozer*, but it does not per se eliminate the *kinyan* requirement.

### Distribution Defense

This then requires an explanation of the *baraita*'s money case, where apparently no *kinyan* was performed and yet the transfer is upheld. Rav Huna's answer is that the *baraita* speaks of a case of *machalek kol nekhasav* — distributing all one's property. It is this feature, the *kol nekhasav* character of the distribution, that exempts the gift from requiring a *kinyan*, not the *metzaveh makhmas misa* element alone. By being situated within a comprehensive distribution of all assets, even a particular bequest within that distribution acquires the status of a *matnat shchiv meira* of *kol nekhasav*.

## Halakhic Summary

### Kol Nekhasav Rules

The *gemara* now draws together its conclusions. In the category of *matnat shchiv meira* of *kol nekhasav*: no *kinyan* is required to make the gift effective. If the donor recovers, *amad chozer* — the gift is voided. This remains true even in the presence of a *metzaveh makhmas misa* declaration, which operates, for this purpose, on the same footing as *kol nekhasav*.

### Miktzat Kinyan Rule

In the category of *matnat shchiv meira* of *miktzat nekhasav*, by contrast, a *kinyan* is always required. Without a *kinyan*, the gift simply does not take effect — regardless of whether the donor subsequently dies. A *miktzat nekhasav* gift without a *kinyan* operates entirely within the *matnat bari* framework, and if no *kinyan* was performed, there is no acquisition. If there was a *kinyan* and the donor dies, the gift stands; if there was a *kinyan* and the donor recovers, *amad eino chozer* — the gift remains valid, because it was a *matnat bari* transaction from the outset.

### Kinyan Effect

This brings into focus the precise role a *kinyan* plays within the *kol nekhasav* category. Once a donor is in the *kol nekhasav* category — either because the gift encompasses all their property, or because of an explicit *metzaveh makhmas misa* — a *kinyan* does not alter the fundamental nature of the transaction. It does not transform the gift into a *matnat bari*. The gift remains a *matnat shchiv meira* whether a *kinyan* was performed or not. A *kinyan* in this category does not prevent *amad chozer*; if the person recovers, the gift is voided even where a *kinyan* took place, because the *kinyan* itself is understood to have been performed *makhmas ha-misa* — on account of the anticipated death — and does not operate as an independent act of acquisition of the *matnat bari* type.

One might ask: if the *kinyan* changes nothing, why would anyone perform one in the *kol nekhasav* category? The *gemara* will address this shortly, considering the possibility that in certain circumstances a *kinyan* — particularly when combined with a *shtar* — is intended to effect *amad eino chozer*. But that is the complication the *gemara* now proceeds to explore. The baseline established here is: in the *kol nekhasav* category, the presence or absence of a *kinyan* does not change the essential *halacha*; in the *miktzat nekhasav* category, a *kinyan* is constitutive of the gift's validity.

## Shtar + Kinyan

### Itmar Scenario

The *gemara* now introduces a more complex case, recorded with the word *itmar*: *matnat shchiv meira she-kasav bah kinyan* — a *matnat shchiv meira* in which a *kinyan* was performed and that fact was written into the *shtar*. The *gemara* thus contemplates a case where three elements converge: the donor is a *shchiv meira* operating in the *kol nekhasav* framework; a *shtar* was written recording the gift; and a *kinyan* was explicitly performed and memorialized within that *shtar*, in the manner that *shtarot* record, for instance, *v'kanina minei b'umana de-kasher le-miknaya beih*.

The question is whether this combination — the full formality of a *shtar* with an explicit *kinyan* written into it — changes the *halachic* character of the gift. We have already established that a *kinyan* alone does not transform a *kol nekhasav* gift into a *matnat bari*. We have also established that a *shtar* alone — a *diatiki* — does not prevent *amad chozer* or *diatiki mevateles diatiki*. But together, do they produce a different result?

The approach of the *Rashbam* is to treat this as re-opening the earlier discussion about a *kinyan* alone. The *Tosafot*, however, insist that the earlier question has been resolved: a *kinyan* alone does nothing to alter the *matnat shchiv meira* framework. The new question can only be about the combination of a *shtar* with an explicitly written *kinyan*, not merely a *kinyan* by itself. The following analysis follows the reading of *Tosafot*.

### Rav: Two Chariots

*Bei Rav* — the academy of Rav — said in the name of Rava: *arkvei atei richshei* — it is riding on two chariots. This vivid image captures the dual nature of such a gift: it partakes simultaneously of the character of a *matnat bari* and the character of a *matnat shchiv meira*. The formalities — the *shtar*, the explicitly recorded *kinyan* — give it the weight of a *matnat bari*. The circumstances — the donor lying ill in bed, the gift described explicitly as a *matnat shchiv meira* — keep it within the *shchiv meira* framework.

The practical consequence is that it operates like a *matnat bari* in one critical respect: *amad eino chozer* — if the donor recovers, the gift is not voided. This is the effect of the added formality. At the same time, it retains the power of a *matnat shchiv meira* in the following sense: a *shchiv meira* can transfer a debt directly to a third party without the need for *ma'amad sheloshtan*. Ordinarily, if one person owes money to another and the creditor wishes to transfer that debt to a third party, the only mechanism available — short of *ma'amad sheloshtan*, convening all three parties together — is to transfer the written *shtar chov*, which still leaves open the possibility of *mechila* by the original creditor. A *shchiv meira*, however, can transfer the debt directly to the recipient, bypassing *ma'amad sheloshtan* entirely and removing the creditor's ability to be *mochel*. This power is unique to *matnat shchiv meira* and cannot be replicated by ordinary *kinyanim*. So the gift with a *shtar* and *kinyan* retains this *shchiv meira* power while gaining the additional strength of *amad eino chozer*.

### Debt Transfer Power

The significance of this *shchiv meira* power over debts should not be understated. If a dying person has extensive outstanding debts owed to them, the ability to transfer those obligations directly to beneficiaries without *ma'amad sheloshtan* — and in a manner that the original creditor can no longer be *mochel* — represents a substantial practical advantage. The *matnat shchiv meira* thus continues to have distinctive legal force even in the beefed-up form under discussion.

### Shimuel: Unknown

Shimuel, however, says: *lo yadana mai adun bah* — I do not know how to treat it. The combination of *shtar* and *kinyan* creates such an ambiguous hybrid that Shimuel declines to assign it a clear *halachic* status.

### Shtar Delivery Limit

The reasoning behind Shimuel's uncertainty is this: *shema lo gamar le-haknoso ela be-shtar, ve-ein shtar le-achar mita* — perhaps by doing all of this the donor indicated that he only wished the gift to take effect through the *shtar*, and a *shtar* cannot operate after death. This principle of *ein shtar le-achar mita* operates on the model of *ein get le-achar mita*: just as a *get* cannot take effect after the husband's death, a *shtar* that is intended as the operative instrument of transfer cannot take effect if the donor has already died before it was delivered.

But the difficulty runs deeper than the question of whether the *shtar* was or was not delivered before death. Shimuel's concern, as explained by the *Rashbam* and accepted by *Tosafot*, is one of intent. The donor who surrounds his *shchiv meira* gift with all the apparatus of a *matnat bari* — a formal *shtar*, an explicitly recorded *kinyan* — may be signaling that he wants the gift to operate on the *matnat bari* track, taking effect after death. But a *matnat bari* cannot take effect after death. If I declare today, while in full health, that I wish someone to own my property after my death, that declaration has no legal effect; a transfer cannot be accomplished by a living person to take effect at the moment of death unless the person is operating within the special *shchiv meira* framework. If the donor is trying to use the *matnat bari* mechanism to achieve what is effectively a posthumous transfer, he gets neither: not a valid *matnat bari*, because it cannot take effect after death, and not a valid *matnat shchiv meira*, because he has chosen to operate on a different track. The result is total invalidity, and the property reverts to the estate.

So for Rav, the combined *shtar* and *kinyan* produces a beefed-up *matnat shchiv meira* — more powerful than the ordinary kind, but still recognizably a *matnat shchiv meira*, with its distinctive powers. For Shimuel, the same combination produces a failed *matnat bari* — an attempt to use *bari* mechanisms for a posthumous transfer, which collapses entirely.

## Self-Contradiction

### Rav Contradiction

Having established the positions of Rav and Shimuel, the *gemara* notes that each appears to contradict himself. The contradiction is introduced with the formula *v'rami de-Rav a-de-Rav v'de-Shmuel a-de-Shmuel*.

Rav's apparent self-contradiction: Rav said earlier that a *matnat shchiv meira* with a *shtar* and *kinyan* is a strengthened *matnat shchiv meira* — *amad eino chozer*. Yet a tradition transmitted from Rav through Rabbi Elazar to Rav Ravin and then to the exile says: *shchiv meira she-amar kisvu u-tnu maneh le-ploni u-meit, ein kosvin ve-nosnin* — if a *shchiv meira* said "write and give a hundred *maneh* to so-and-so" and then died before it was done, you do not write the *shtar* and give it. The reason given is *shema lo gamar le-haknoso ela be-shtar ve-ein shtar le-achar mita* — perhaps he only intended the gift to take effect through the *shtar*, and the *shtar* cannot operate after death. So here Rav seems to be saying that a *shtar* context undermines the *matnat shchiv meira* rather than strengthening it.

### Shimuel Contradiction

Shimuel's apparent self-contradiction: Shimuel said earlier that a *matnat shchiv meira* with a *shtar* and *kinyan* is ineffective — a failed *matnat bari*. Yet Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Shimuel that the *halacha* is *kosvin ve-nosnin* — in the "write and give" case, you do write the *shtar* and give it. This seems to say that even a *shtar*-mediated *shchiv meira* gift is valid, the opposite of what Shimuel seemed to hold.

### Rav Resolution

The *gemara* resolves Rav's contradiction: *ha di-kanu minei, ha de-lo kanu minei* — the two cases differ in whether a *kinyan* was performed. When the *shchiv meira* said only "write and give" without a *kinyan*, Rav says the *shtar* has not yet been delivered, and without delivery — since the donor chose the *shtar* track — the gift cannot take effect after death. When, however, a *kinyan* was also explicitly performed and written into the *shtar*, Rav says this combination strengthens the *matnat shchiv meira* and effects *amad eino chozer*. The two cases are thus not contradictory but represent different factual scenarios.

This resolution, as *Tosafot* notes, raises its own question, because it seems to suggest that the distinction rests on whether the *kinyan* was merely performed alongside the *shtar* or was explicitly written into it. *Tosafot* reads *kanu minei* in the decisive case as referring specifically to the *kinyan* being recorded within the *shtar* itself — it is that integration of *kinyan* and *shtar* into a single instrument that gives the gift its enhanced standing. When the *kinyan* is absent altogether, the "write and give" instruction places the gift entirely on the *shtar* track, and *ein shtar le-achar mita* applies.

There is a relevant parallel to an earlier *mishna* where a will was found tied to a person's side — the *diatiki keshura al matno* — and yet was ruled ineffective because it had not been handed over. The principle that emerges is that when someone chooses the *shtar* path of *matnat shchiv meira*, delivery of the *shtar* becomes a necessary condition of the gift's taking effect. The verbal *matnat shchiv meira* path and the *shtar* path are distinct; choosing the latter means accepting its conditions.

### Shimuel Resolution

The *gemara* resolves Shimuel's contradiction by introducing the principle of *bi-myafes kocho* — when one makes clear through explicit language that the intent of the *kinyan* and *shtar* is to strengthen and supplement the *matnat shchiv meira* rather than to replace it or operate on a different track. When that explicit clarification is present, even Shimuel agrees that the gift is effective. When it is absent, Shimuel reads the combination of *shtar* and *kinyan* as an attempt to craft a *matnat bari* operative after death, and rules the gift void.

The two positions of the earlier Shimuel and the later Shimuel thus represent two different factual scenarios: without *bi-myafes kocho*, the gift is interpreted as a failed *matnat bari*; with *bi-myafes kocho*, even Shimuel recognizes it as a valid, strengthened *matnat shchiv meira*.

## Myafes Kocho Formula

### Kanina Mineih

The *gemara* now asks: *heichi dami myafes kocho* — what precisely does the explicit language of *myafes kocho* look like? Rav Chisda provides the formula: *kanina mineih* — we performed a *kinyan* with him — *mosif a-matnata da* — in addition to this gift. This is how the *shtar* should record the act.

### Mosif Keyword

The critical word, as Rav Chisda makes clear, is *mosif* — "in addition." This single word does all the work. It signals unambiguously that the *kinyan* is not the operative mechanism of the gift, not an attempt to shift the transaction onto the *matnat bari* track, but simply an addition to an already-valid *matnat shchiv meira*. The gift stands on its own as a *matnat shchiv meira*; the *kinyan* is being layered on top of it, supplementing it, strengthening it. Without that word, the *kinyan* and *shtar* together might be read as constituting an entirely different kind of transaction — a *matnat bari* — with all the problems that Shimuel identifies.

### Explicit Intent Effect

When the formula *kanina mineih mosif a-matnata da* is used, Rav Chisda says, he would agree with Rava's position. The explicit language removes Shimuel's concern entirely. There is no longer any ambiguity about which track the donor is riding. He is on the *matnat shchiv meira* track, and everything that follows — the *shtar*, the *kinyan* — is in service of that *matnat shchiv meira*, not in substitution for it. The gift is valid, *amad eino chozer*, and retains the distinctive powers of *matnat shchiv meira*, including the ability to transfer debts without *ma'amad sheloshtan*.

## Will Override Cases

### Simple Override

The *gemara* now turns to a related but distinct question: what happens when a *shchiv meira* writes one will and then writes a second will? The *gemara* presents this as *pshita* — apparently obvious — based on what has already been established. We know the rule of *diatiki mevateles diatiki*: a later will overrides an earlier one, operating on the same logic as *amad chozer* — since the entire gift was predicated on the anticipation of death, the donor retains the power to revise his instructions as long as he lives.

This is captured in the tradition that Rav Dimi brought from *Eretz Yisrael*: *de-asi kanina u-mevatel de-asi kanina* — a later *diatiki* cancels the earlier *diatiki*. This applies whenever the *matnat shchiv meira* is in the standard framework: either *kol nekhasav* or *metzaveh makhmas misa*.

### Rav: First Binds

The *gemara* then asks: what if the donor did not merely write a will twice, but in each case wrote it with full formality — *katav ve-zikah lo, ve-katav ve-zikah lo*? Here the *gemara* understands *zikah* as referring not merely to handing over the *shtar* — which would have been the case in the simple *diatiki* scenario as well, since an undelivered *shtar* would be ineffective — but to performing the gift together with a *kinyan*. The question is whether this added formality prevents the second will from overriding the first.

Rav rules: *rishon kana* — the first recipient has acquired the property, and the second will does not override it. Rav describes this as *harei hi ke-matnat bari* — it functions like a *matnat bari* — meaning that the effect of the added formality is that the gift cannot be retracted. This parallels precisely what Rav said in the earlier case of *matnat shchiv meira she-kasav bah kinyan*: the combination of *shtar* and *kinyan* creates an irrevocable gift, *amad eino chozer*, and similarly *eino chozer be-matnaso* — the donor cannot use a later will to override the earlier one.

### Shimuel: Second Overrides

Shimuel rules: *sheini kanah* — the second recipient acquires. Shimuel describes this as *harei hi ke-matnat shchiv meira* — it still operates like a *matnat shchiv meira*, which means the *kinyan* does not render it irrevocable, and the rule of *diatiki mevateles diatiki* continues to apply.

### Parallel to Shtar Case

The *gemara* immediately notes: *i-plegu vah chada zimna* — haven't they already disputed this exact question in the earlier case of *matnat shchiv meira she-kasav bah kinyan*? The parallel is precise: the question in both cases is whether a *matnat shchiv meira* accompanied by the formalities of *shtar* and *kinyan* is irrevocable, or whether it retains the full retractability of a standard *matnat shchiv meira*. Rav says it becomes irrevocable; Shimuel says it remains retractable.

### Why Both Cases Needed

The *gemara* answers: *tzricha* — you need both cases, because each teaches something the other does not. In the first case — *matnat shchiv meira she-kasav bah kinyan* — one might have thought Rav's ruling of *amad eino chozer* applies only because the *kinyan* was explicitly written into the *shtar*, making the connection between the two maximally clear. Without that written integration, one might have thought Rav would agree to Shimuel. By stating the ruling again in the *diatiki* case, the *gemara* teaches that even when the *kinyan* and *shtar* are not written together in one integrated document — even when they are conducted in parallel — Rav says *rishon kanah* and the gift is irrevocable.

Conversely, one might have thought that Shimuel's ruling of *sheini kanah* applies only in the *diatiki* case, where the *kinyan* was not written into the *shtar*; perhaps when the *kinyan* is written into the *shtar* as a single integrated act, Shimuel would agree with Rav. By stating Shimuel's ruling in both cases, the *gemara* teaches that even with full integration Shimuel maintains that *sheini kanah* — unless there is explicit *myafes kocho* language.

## Sura Query

### Query Details

The *gemara* concludes the *daf* with a report from the Academy of Sura. *Rav Yirmeya bar Abba* relates that they sent an inquiry from the house of Rav to Shimuel: *shchiv meira she-kasav kol nekhasav la-acherim ve-kanu mi-yado, mahu?* — a *shchiv meira* who wrote all his property to others and a *kinyan* was performed — what is the *halacha*? Note that here, no *shtar* is mentioned; the question concerns a *kinyan* alone accompanying a *kol nekhasav* gift.

### No Retraction

Shimuel's response was: *ein achar kinyan klum* — after a *kinyan*, nothing; there is no retraction. This seems to say that once a *kinyan* has been performed, the gift cannot be undone.

### Initial Misread

The initial understanding of this response was that it applied only to the case of someone writing another will to override the gift — *le-achar*, to another. But, reasoned those who first heard the ruling, surely if he recovered — *le-atzmo* — the gift would be voided, since *amad chozer* is a fundamental principle of *matnat shchiv meira* and a *kinyan* alone in the *kol nekhasav* category does not change this, as Shimuel himself ruled earlier.

### Chisda Correction

Rav Chisda corrected this misunderstanding, citing the clarification that Rav Huna brought from Kufi: *bein le-atzmo bein le-acherin* — whether regarding the donor himself recovering or regarding a subsequent will overriding the first, *amad eino chozer* and *eino chozer be-matnaso*. Once a *kinyan* was performed together with a *kol nekhasav* gift, the donation is irrevocable in both senses.

This, then, represents a point of agreement between Rav and Shimuel, at least according to this version. When a *shchiv meira* performs a *kinyan* alongside a *kol nekhasav* gift — even without a *shtar*, or at least without the full integration of *kinyan* into *shtar* — both authorities agree that the gift is strengthened to the point of *amad eino chozer*. Where they continue to disagree, according to the version that emerges from *Tosafot*'s reading, is in the case where *kinyan* and *shtar* are fully integrated into a single instrument without explicit *myafes kocho* language: Rav reads this as a beefed-up *matnat shchiv meira*, while Shimuel reads it as a failed *matnat bari*. The formula of *kanina mineih mosif a-matnata da* is the key that, even for Shimuel, resolves the ambiguity in favor of validity.
