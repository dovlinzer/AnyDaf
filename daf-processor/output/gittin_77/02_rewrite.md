# Gittin 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Im vs. Al Menat

### Opening Cases

The daf opens by completing the previous perek's discussion of conditional language in the get, picking up from the mishna on 76b. The mishna presents the case of a husband who says *harei zeh gittech im lo bati mikan ad yod bet chodesh* — "Here is your get if I do not return from here within twelve months" — and then dies within those twelve months. The ruling is *eino get*: it is not a valid divorce. The reasoning turns on the word *im*. Without the qualifier *mehayom* ("as of now"), *im lo bati* means "when I do not come" — that is, the get is intended to take effect only at the end of twelve months, at which point the husband is already dead. A get that takes effect after death is a *get l'achar mita*, which is invalid.

By contrast, if the husband said *harei zeh gittech mehayom im lo bati mikan ad yod bet chodesh* — "as of now, if I do not return" — and then died within the twelve months, it is a valid get. Since the condition will certainly be fulfilled and the get was designated as effective from the present moment, the divorce is good.

A third case: the husband instructs others, *kitvu utnu get l'ishti im lo bati mikan ad yod bet chodesh* — "Write and give a get to my wife if I do not return within twelve months." Here the condition applies not only to the giving of the get but to its very writing. If they wrote it within the twelve months and gave it afterward, the get is invalid — the instruction was: write it, and give it, when I have not returned, meaning the writing itself was conditioned on the non-return.

The final case in this section is where the husband reversed the order of his statement: *kitvu utnu get l'ishti* first, then *im lo bati mikan ad yod bet chodesh*. The first ruling — that writing it early and giving it late renders it invalid — is the same. But Rabbi Yossi disagrees in this formulation. He reads the *kitvu* as grammatically severed from the conditional clause: "Write it" — then a pause — "and give it *if* I do not return." Because the instruction to write precedes the condition, Rabbi Yossi frees the writing from conditionality. Only the giving is conditional.

The mishna then addresses the case where the husband instructed *kitvu v'chatmu l'achar yod bet chodesh v'natnu l'achar yod bet chodesh* — write it and give it after twelve months — and he subsequently died. If the get was given before his death, it is valid; if death preceded the giving, it is not. If the sequence is unknown, she is in a state of *safek megureshes* — doubtfully divorced — which is, as the mishna acknowledges, the worst of all outcomes.

### Rabbi Yossi & Dating

The Gemara introduces a *baraisa*: *raboseinu* — "our teachers" — permitted the woman to remarry even in the case where the husband said simply *im lo bati*, without explicitly saying *mehayom*. The Gemara asks: who are these *raboseinu*? Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel identifies them as the *beis din* that permitted the oil of non-Jews — the court of Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah, known to have been willing to issue lenient rulings and occasionally criticized for it.

How did this *beis din* permit her to remarry when the language *im lo bati* would seem to mean "when I do not return," yielding a *get l'achar mita*? The Gemara answers: *sabri lah k'Rabbi Yossi* — they held in accordance with a different Rabbi Yossi, one cited in Bava Basra, who rules *zmano shel shtar mochiach alav* — the date of a document establishes its validity. Rabbi Yossi's principle is that when a *shechiv mera* gives a document that says *l'achar mita*, we read it as *mehayom v'l'achar mita* because the date written on the document anchors it to the present moment. Applied here: even though the *tnai* of *im lo bati* was stated verbally and thus might seem to stand on its own as meaning "when," the date inscribed in the get causes us to read it in conjunction with the condition, effectively transforming *im* from a temporal "when" into a conditional "if" effective as of the date.

Rabbi Abba the son of Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba then quotes Rabbi Yochanan: Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah — specifically identified as the son of Rabban Gamliel and grandson of Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi — could not get his colleagues to agree with him on this ruling. Throughout his life, no one from his circle concurred.

### Scope of Dating

Abbaye frames the positions of Rabbi Yossi more precisely. Everyone agrees on the two clear cases: if the husband says *lich'she'tetzei chama min hartika* — "when the sun rises" — and dies at night, it is unambiguously a *get l'achar mita*, since *lich'she* means "when." If he says *al m'nat she'tetzei chama min hartika* — "on the condition that the sun rises" — it is unambiguously a present-moment get with a future condition, because the principle articulated by Rav Huna in the name of Rav is *kol ha'omer al m'nat k'omer me'achshav dami* — whoever says "on condition" speaks as though saying "as of now." The debate of Rabbi Yossi applies exclusively to the word *im*. Without *zmano shel shtar*, *im* means "when." With *zmano shel shtar*, Rabbi Yossi holds that *im* is read as a conditional "if," and the date supplies the *me'achshav*; his colleagues disagree.

A student raised a sharp point: since *zmano shel shtar* — the date on the get — records only the day, not the hour, if the get was delivered at night and the husband dies that same night, even according to Rabbi Yossi the date cannot mean "as of this moment," since the date written on the document reflects only the day, which might refer to the daylight hours that follow. This would explain Rashi's position that when one says *lich'she'tetzei*, it is categorically a *get l'achar mita* even according to Rabbi Yossi — because the date, which marks a day, cannot reach back to anchor a nighttime death.

The discussion then turns to a technical ruling: in the formulation *kitvu utnu get l'ishti im lo bati*, Rav Yemar asks Rav Ashi whether Rabbi Yossi might hold that the *writing* of a get cannot be made conditional at all — that *ktav get al tnai* is fundamentally invalid. The Gemara rejects this: Rabbi Yossi agrees one can condition the writing. His point is purely grammatical and interpretive. When the husband says *kitvu utnu get l'ishti im lo bati*, Rabbi Yossi reads this as: "Write it now" — and then separately — "give it if I do not return." The condition attaches only to the proximate verb, the giving. Had he wanted to condition the writing as well, he should have said *im lo bati kitvu utnu* — placing the condition first. The Rabbis disagree and rule that regardless of word order, both the writing and the giving are conditioned on the non-return.

### Tosafot: Safek Get

The Gemara raises a question that is left without resolution: once it is known that the husband has died — and thus that the condition will certainly be fulfilled — can the wife remarry immediately, or must she wait until the twelve months have elapsed? The question is asked both about the case in the mishna (where *mehayom* was stated explicitly) and about the case of Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah's court (where the date of the document supplies the *me'achshav*). The Gemara notes that the question on the *beis din* case was posed to an elder who had actually participated in such a ruling, since a practical ruling would presumably have included thinking through its parameters — which makes the question more pointed than merely a theoretical one about the mishna. Nevertheless, the Gemara offers no answer.

Tosafot addresses this question directly. Tosafot asks: *le'alter mah yesh lachosh* — if he has died, what is there to worry about? He will not return. Tosafot answers that there is a concern about appearances. People are aware that the condition was "if he does not return in twelve months," and if they observe the woman marrying immediately, they will not know he has died and will conclude that the twelve-month condition need not be waited out in general — creating a false impression about how conditional divorces work. This is therefore a rabbinic-level concern about *mar'it ayin* or improper inference, not a question about the mechanism of conditional fulfillment.

Tosafot also addresses a further point: even if one holds like Rabbi Yossi that *zmano shel shtar mochiach alav*, the result in the case where *im* is combined with the date of the document is at best a *safek get* — a doubtful divorce — not a definite one. This is because there remains a question whether the date alone is sufficient to convert *im* from a temporal "when" to a conditional "if." Moreover, Tosafot notes that the question of whether we rule like Rabbi Yossi in these cases remains genuinely open. Even though it is reported that his colleagues did not agree with him, one cannot prove from that historical fact that the *halacha* rejects his position. Rabbi Yochanan, who reports that Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah could not convince his colleagues, may himself have agreed with Rabbi Yossi. And even if there is a basis to reject Rabbi Yossi where the *tnai* is external and verbal while the date is in the document, one might still apply his principle where both the date and the condition appear within the document itself.

## Timing Language

### After = Distance

With the transition to a new perek, the Gemara turns from questions about the content of conditional language to questions about temporal language — specifically, what it means to say that a document takes effect "after" (*l'achar*) a given unit of time.

A *baraisa* establishes the principle through a series of cases, each demonstrating that "after" does not mean immediately after but rather fully separated from. If one says a document takes effect *l'achar hashavua* — after the seven-year shmitta cycle — it takes effect a full *shana*, year, afterward. If one says *l'achar hashana* — after this year — it takes effect a *chodesh*, a month, into the following year. If one says *l'achar hachodesh* — after the month — it takes effect a full *Shabbos*, a week, later. And if one says *l'achar Shabbos* — after this Shabbat — the question arises: one day later?

Rav Zeira, and some say Rav Assi before Rabbi Yochanan, rules that Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday are still *batar Shabbos* — in the wake of the preceding Shabbat, still connected to it. One can still recite *havdala* in those days; one is still in the gravitational field of that Shabbat. Wednesday, Thursday, and Friday are already *kamei Shabbos* — oriented toward the coming Shabbat. The idea throughout is that "after" means when one is genuinely distant from the unit in question — when one is no longer in its shadow or afterglow. Just as someone writing a check in early January might still write the previous year out of habit, the period immediately following a major unit of time is still considered to be "of" that unit, not fully beyond it.

### Rabbi: 30 Days

A *baraisa* teaches in the name of Rabbi: *l'achar haregel shloshim yom* — "after the festival" means thirty days after the conclusion of Yom Tov. The festival's influence is understood to extend across an entire month.

### Attribution Issues

Rabbi Hiyya went out and taught this ruling in the name of Rabbi, and he was praised for doing so. When he subsequently taught the same ruling as though it represented a majority opinion rather than Rabbi's individual position, he was not praised — one does not present a *da'at yachid* as a consensus. The ruling, it seems, is not followed: the festival's shadow does not actually extend thirty days.

This prompts a brief observation: two instances appear in close proximity in this *sugya* where a rabbinic position fails to gain acceptance. Earlier, Rabbi Yehuda Nesiah could not convince his colleagues of Rabbi Yossi's principle regarding *zmano shel shtar*. Here, Rabbi's ruling on thirty days after the festival is likewise not accepted. In each case, the question of the correct *halacha* remains open — the non-acceptance of a position in a historical sense does not automatically determine the ruling, as Tosafot noted above.

## Giving the Get

### Mishna Cases

The new chapter opens with a mishna addressing how the get must physically be transferred to the wife. The central case is *hazorek get l'ishto vehi betoch beisah o betoch chatzerah* — if a man throws a get to his wife while she is in her house or in her yard, she is divorced. The get has landed in her domain, and that suffices. But if he threw it while they are in his house or his yard — even if she is lying beside him in bed — she is not divorced. The domain in which the get lands determines validity; his domain does not count as her receiving it.

However, if the get landed *betoch cheikah* — on her body, in her lap or bosom — or into her *kaltah*, her basket or purse, she is divorced even if they are standing in his domain. The get reached her person, not merely her property.

### Kinyan vs. Transfer

This mishna raises a fundamental conceptual question: is the act of giving a get an act of *kinyan* — of transferring legal ownership in a halakhically defined sense — or is it fundamentally a *ma'aseh netinah*, an act of physical giving? The question has practical implications. If it is a *kinyan*, then the conditions for valid transfer must mirror those of ordinary acquisition: the object must be capable of being owned, the domain must belong to the recipient, and so on. If it is an act of giving, perhaps more physical or interpersonal factors control validity rather than the formal mechanics of property transfer.

One test case mentioned earlier in the tractate is a get written on *issurei hana'ah* — objects from which benefit is forbidden. Some hold that such objects cannot be legally possessed at all, existing as it were outside the framework of ownership. If a get on such an object is valid, that would suggest the act is one of giving rather than of kinyan. But since other opinions hold that *issurei hana'ah* can in fact be possessed, the test case is not decisive. The question remains genuinely open, and the *sugya* here is best read as probing precisely this tension.

### Derasha

The Gemara asks: from where do we derive that the wife's roof, yard, and *karfif* — a smaller enclosure — can serve as a valid location for receiving the get? The verse says *ve-natan beyadah* — "and he shall give it into her hand." One might have thought only the hand is valid. The Gemara answers with a *derasha*: *talmud lomar ve-natan mikol makom* — the formulation of *ve-natan* by itself, separable from *beyadah*, extends the act of giving to any of her domains. Rashi explains that the verse could have written *beyadah yitnenah*, making the hand the exclusive object; by writing *ve-natan beyadah* the Torah allows *ve-natan* to be expounded independently.

A parallel *derasha* appears in the laws of theft. The verse *im himatzei timatzei beyado ha-geneivah* — "if the stolen object is found in his hand" — might suggest liability only when the thief holds the object directly. But the same *midrash* style extends liability to the thief's roof, yard, and *karfif* as well: *talmud lomar himatzei timatzei mikol makom*.

### Get vs. Theft

The Gemara then establishes that both *derashot* are necessary — one cannot be inferred from the other. If we had only the case of the get, one might think the wife's domain works because the husband divorces her against her will (*be'al korcha megarshah*) — the husband has greater power in the divorce context, so perhaps a broader range of situations counts as "giving to her." But for theft, where there is no such coercive power dynamic, one might have thought property acquisition would require more direct action. Conversely, if we had only the case of the thief, one might think his domain is implicated because the Torah penalized him — the rabbinic expansion of liability could reflect the punitive context — while for the get, perhaps the domain rule would not apply.

Rashi sharpens the novelty in the theft case. The obvious case — where the thief actively picked up the object and brought it to his yard — requires no *derasha* at all: the thief performed a direct act of *meshichah*, of drawing the object toward him, and is clearly liable. What the *derasha* comes to teach is the case where, say, a cow wandered into the thief's yard on its own, and the thief decided not to return it and locked the gate. He performed no direct act on the object. The *chiddush* is that closing the gate — a passive act relative to the object itself — nonetheless constitutes an act of *geneivah*. Just as if a baseball were thrown and accidentally landed in someone's hands and he refused to return it — that constitutes theft under this *derasha*. The willingness of the Torah to define such a passive situation as possession for purposes of theft liability is itself a striking *chiddush*.

## Husband Rights

### Rights Problem

The Gemara now asks the pointed question: the mishna says that throwing the get into the wife's yard effects a divorce. But *mah she'kaneta isha kanah ba'alah* — whatever the wife acquires, her husband acquires. So if the get lands in her yard, the yard's acquisitions belong to the husband — which means the get immediately becomes his property, not hers. How then does it constitute giving it to her?

As noted above, there are two ways to frame this problem. One reading focuses on the mechanics of ownership: when the get lands in her yard, it transfers to the husband. But this framing invites the response that surely he could waive those rights in advance. The other reading focuses on the nature of the yard itself: since the husband has full rights to the *peiros* — the produce and fruits — of his wife's property, the yard is not genuinely "hers" in the sense required for the verse *ve-natan beyadah*. On this reading, the question is not about who takes possession of the get, but about whether the yard can count as an extension of her person when he has such deep claims upon it.

The latter framing is more conceptually powerful. If we understand *ve-natan beyadah* as requiring that the get be given to *her* — to her as a person, to her domain — then a yard over which the husband has concurrent rights is not fully her domain. The analogy to the hand makes this vivid: giving something into her hand gives it to her because the hand is unambiguously her person. But a yard in which the husband has *kinyan peiros* — or perhaps even *kinyan haguf* on some views — is not unambiguously hers.

There is a broader halakhic backdrop here. The question of *kinyan peiros k'kinyan haguf dami* — whether the right to produce is equivalent to ownership of the body of the property — bears directly on this. If it is, then in a meaningful sense the husband is the owner of the yard during the marriage, and putting the get in that yard is like putting it in his own yard. If it is not — if *kinyan peiros* is merely a limited usufruct right — then the wife retains underlying ownership and the yard may still count as hers. Tosafot engages at length with these issues.

### Waiving Rights

Rav Elazar proposes that the case works when the husband has written her a document stating *din u'dvarim ein li benikhsayikh* — "I have no claim or dispute against your property." By waiving his rights in writing, he removes himself from the yard, leaving it fully hers.

The Gemara objects: a *baraisa* teaches that if a business partner tells his partner *din u'dvarim ein li al sadeh zu, v'ein li esek bo, yadi mesuleket heimena* — "I have no claim on this field, I have nothing to do with it, my hand is removed from it" — *lo amar klum*, it is legally meaningless. Rights and ownership are distinct categories. One can disclaim an adversarial claim (*din u'dvarim*) without transferring or relinquishing one's underlying ownership interest. To give up ownership, one must actually transfer it — a mere declaration of non-claim is insufficient. The same would apply to the husband's rights in the wife's yard.

### Timing of Waiver

The answer proposed by *d'vei Rabbi Yannai* is that the case works when the husband wrote the waiver *v'odah arusah* — while she was still betrothed, before the *nissuin*. The husband's rights to *kinyan peiros* in the wife's property do not attach until the *nissuin*. Therefore, if before the *nissuin* he declared in writing that he is waiving any rights he would otherwise receive over the fruits of her property, he never acquires those rights in the first place.

The mechanism is explained through two principles. First, *nachlah haba'ah lo l'adam mimakom acher* — an inheritance or benefit coming from an external source, not a biblical default of succession, can be declined in advance. A child cannot preemptively disclaim an inheritance from a parent because the Torah automatically transfers it; but a right that flows from rabbinic institution, arriving from an external source in a contingent way, can be refused before it takes effect. Second, Rava's principle: *ha'omer ee efshi b'takkanat chakhamim k'gon zu shom'in lo* — if someone declares that he does not want a rabbinic *takkanah* that was enacted for his benefit, we listen to him. Since the right of *kinyan peiros* was enacted as a rabbinic benefit for the husband, he may decline it before the marriage creates the entitlement.

Rav Huna in the name of Rav provides the paradigm: a wife may say to her husband *eini nizonet v'eini osah* — I will not be supported by you and I will not labor for you — because the arrangement of support in exchange for labor was enacted fundamentally for her benefit. She may therefore decline it. This refusal can even be toggled on and off during the marriage, since the arrangement is ongoing and personal.

The case of *kinyan peiros*, however, differs in an important respect. Once the *nissuin* occurs and the husband receives his *kinyan* in the wife's property, he cannot undo it merely by saying *din u'dvarim ein li* — that is the Gemara's earlier objection. To restore the property rights to her he would need to perform an actual *makneh*, a proper act of transferring ownership back. But before the *nissuin*, he can simply refuse the *takkanat chakhamim*, in which case he never acquires the right at all, and the yard remains unambiguously hers, allowing the get placed therein to count as given to her.

### Ba'im K'Echad

Rava, however, proposes a fundamentally different solution — one that does not require any prior waiver. *Atu yadah mi lo kanya leh l'baal?* — Is the hand of the wife not also, in a sense, "owned" by the husband? His rights to her labor might be understood as a kind of claim on her hand itself. Yet when the get is placed in her hand, it works. Why? Because *gito v'yadah ba'im k'echad* — the get and her hand come into effect simultaneously.

The insight is this: it is true that while the marriage subsists, the husband has rights to her hand through his entitlement to her labor, and rights to her yard through *kinyan peiros*. But the get and the divorce take effect at the same moment. At the instant the get is received, the divorce occurs — and at that same instant, her hand and her yard become entirely hers, free of any claim. The moment of reception and the moment of divorce are not sequential; they are simultaneous. Therefore, at the very moment that matters — the moment of reception — her hand and her yard belong to her fully.

Ravina then sharpens a question against Rava's formulation: granted, there is a meaningful analogy between the yard and the hand regarding simultaneity, but the cases are not truly parallel. The husband does not "own" her hand in any proprietary sense — his right to her labor (*ma'aseh yadeha*) is not the same as ownership of the hand as a physical object. Yet with the yard, the husband has something closer to a property interest. Is Rava's proof from the hand of the wife really compelling enough to extend to the yard?

Rava responds that his actual proof was never from the hand of the wife. It was from the hand of an *eved Kena'ani* — a Canaanite slave. The slave is legally property: *yad eved k'yad rabbo damya*, the slave's hand is like the master's hand. Yet the Gemara accepts that one can free a slave by handing him his writ of manumission directly — *le-man de-amar bi-shtar al yadei atzmo*. Why should that work? The slave's hand is entirely the master's property. The only possible answer is *gito v'yado ba'im k'echad* — the writ of manumission and the slave's hand become free simultaneously. At the moment of reception, the slave is freed, and at that same moment the hand is his own. The simultaneity principle is thus established on solid ground, independent of any ambiguity about the wife's hand.

From this principle, the application to the wife's yard follows: *hacha nami gito v'chatzerah ba'im k'echad* — here too, the get and her yard come into effect as one.

## Eved Case

### Freeing Eved

The case of the *eved Kena'ani* thus serves as the clearest proof of the *ba'im k'echad* principle. The slave is unambiguously property — his hand belongs to his master in a much more complete sense than the wife's hand or yard belong to her husband. There is no rabbinic *takkanah* to waive, no prior condition to arrange. The master simply hands the *shtar shichrur* to the slave, and it works. The only coherent explanation is that the act of freeing and the conferral of legal personhood upon the slave occur simultaneously — the hand that receives the document is, at that very instant, the hand of a free person.

### Divorce Timing

The same logic then resolves the problem of the wife's yard. Just as the slave's hand becomes legally his at the moment of reception, the wife's yard becomes legally hers at the moment the get is received. The husband's *kinyan peiros*, whatever its scope, is extinguished at the instant of the divorce — and since the divorce and the reception are simultaneous, the yard is hers at the relevant moment. *Gito v'chatzerah ba'im k'echad*. This is Rava's answer, and it operates independently of the solution proposed by *d'vei Rabbi Yannai*, which required a specific prior waiver.

## Shabbat Deathbed

### Case Setup

The final *sugya* on this daf presents a vivid practical scenario: a *shechiv mera* — a person on his deathbed — wrote a get for his wife on the eve of Shabbat but did not manage to give it to her before Shabbat came in. On Shabbat, his condition deteriorated severely. He needed to transfer the get urgently, both to spare her the ordeal of *yibbum* and because it was unclear how long he would survive.

The immediate problem is identified by the *rishonim* in accordance with Rashi's framing: the get is *muktzeh*. Giving a get or performing *kiddushin* on Shabbat is normally prohibited as a rabbinic restriction, but the *poskim* note that a *shechiv mera* is given latitude to perform acts on Shabbat that would ordinarily be impermissible — since we do everything possible to ease his distress. Therefore the concern is not the act of divorce per se on Shabbat, but the *muktzeh* status of the get document itself, which cannot simply be handed over.

### Kinyan Solution

They brought the question before Rava. Rava suggested: tell the *shechiv mera* to transfer ownership of the small piece of property on which the get is resting to the wife — *le-kanyei nihalah le-hahu duchsah de-yasiv beih gitta*. Then let her go and perform *kinyan chazakah* on that spot: *tisgor v'tiftach* — let her lock and open the enclosure — since the mishna and *baraisa* teach that locking a gate, closing a fence, or breaching one as needed constitutes *chazakah* on land — *na'al, gader, u-faratz, kol she-hu l'tikkuna, harei zu chazakah*. By acquiring the *makom* — the physical spot — through *chazakah*, she thereby acquires the get that sits upon it, since an object resting in one's *chatzer* is acquired along with the *chatzer* itself.

### Kinyan Problem

An immediate objection is raised: this solution seems to trade one rabbinic prohibition for another. If the problem is *muktzeh* — a rabbinic restriction — how does performing a *kinyan* on Shabbat, which is itself rabbinically prohibited, constitute a solution? The Ran addresses this by suggesting that the *kinyan chazakah* performed through opening and closing a door is experientially innocuous — it feels like an ordinary domestic act, not like a formal *kinyan* — whereas *muktzeh* involves an overt act of handling the forbidden object. The She'iltos offers a different explanation: the case is actually one where the get is located across a *reshut ha-rabbim* — a public domain — in another house, making it physically impossible to bring the get to the dying man on Shabbat. The problem is therefore not *muktzeh* per se but inaccessibility, and Rava's solution of having the wife acquire the spot through *kinyan chazakah* in the remote location is the only available mechanism.

### Arusa Not Nesua

Rava's solution was met with embarrassment when Rav Ilish pointed out the objection discussed above: *mah she-kaneta isha kanah ba'alah* — the husband would immediately acquire whatever she acquires, so her *kinyan* of the property would revert to him and the get sitting on it would become his. Rava was taken aback — *ikhsif*, he was embarrassed.

But then it emerged that the circumstances of the case were different from what had been assumed: the woman was an *arusa*, merely betrothed, not yet a *nesua*. The rule of *mah she-kaneta isha kanah ba'alah* and the husband's *kinyan peiros* apply only after *nissuin*; they do not apply to an *arusa*. Therefore, Rava had not actually made an error — the specific facts of the case meant that his solution was valid after all, since the *arusat*'s property was entirely her own.

### Final Position

Nevertheless, the Gemara records that Rava then retracted even this concession and reaffirmed his position without qualification: *lo shena arusa v'lo shena nesua — gita v'chatzerah ba'im k'echad*. Whether she is betrothed or married, the principle of simultaneity applies. The get and her domain — including a domain she acquires in the very act — come into effect as one.

The Gemara then notes that it had cited Rava's ruling of *gito v'chatzerah ba'im k'echad* earlier in the *sugya*, seemingly before this story was told. It explains: *mei-ikara ki amar Rava hai ma'isa amrah* — when Rava first formulated the principle of *ba'im k'echad*, it was this very story that gave rise to it. The deathbed Shabbat case was the original occasion for Rava's insight. What was presented earlier in the *sugya* as Rava's ruling was, chronologically, a conclusion that emerged from this episode — the story is the origin, and the earlier citation anticipated the result.
