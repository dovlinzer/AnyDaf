# Pesachim 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ein Oseh Tumah

### Mashkin Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין עוֹשָׂה טוּמְאָה כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ
>
> **Translation:** does not create like

By way of review: the premise established in prior discussion is that *mashkin* cannot be *metame* *mashkin*, and *ochlin* cannot be *metame* *ochlin*. The cross-contamination is permitted — *mashkin* can be *metame* *ochlin* and vice versa — but the rule of *ein oseh tumah kayotze bah*, "does not create like," prevents like from contaminating like. This does not extend to *keilim*, as was established through a series of *kal va'chomers*, the application of the principle of *dyo*, and the double usage of *yitamei* in a relevant *pasuk*. That double formulation was understood to refer to *mashkin*, given their inherent predisposition to receive *tumah*, and through them the contamination works its way into *ochlin* — not directly because *ochlin* contaminate *ochlin*, but because *mashkin* serve as the enabling agent, the *makhshir*, for everything else.

### Seeds & Sheretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וְכִי יֻתַּן מַיִם עַל זֶרַע וְנָפַל מִנִּבְלָתָם עָלָיו טָמֵא הוּא״ — הוּא טָמֵא, וְאֵין עוֹשֶׂה טוּמְאָה כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** *"But if water is put upon the seed, and any of their carcass falls upon it, it is impure for you"* — **It is impure; however, it does not transmit impurity to a similar item.**

The *Gemara* now raises a challenge: is the principle of *ein oseh tumah kayotze bah* really derived from the double *yitamei*, as we have been assuming? Surely, it is derived from elsewhere entirely — from the *pasuk* dealing with seeds and a *sheretz*: *v'chi yutan mayim al zera* — if water had been placed on seed — *v'nafal mivlatam alav*, and then the carcass of the *sheretz* falls upon it — *tamei hu*. The Torah says *tamei hu*, and the *Gemara* darshens the word *hu* as a limiting particle: it is *tamei*, and it does not create *tumah* in the manner of itself. The seed becomes *tamei*, but it cannot make another object of the same type *tamei*.

To clarify what becomes *tamei* in this case: the water falls on produce — seeds, kernels of wheat — and renders them susceptible to *tumah*; then the dead *sheretz* falls upon them and they become *tamei*. But those *tamei* seeds cannot in turn render other food *tamei*. They can render *mashkin* *tamei*, since *ochlin* can contaminate *mashkin* and *mashkin* can contaminate *ochlin*, but *ochlin* cannot contaminate *ochlin de-oraita*. Given that we already derive *ein oseh tumah kayotze bah* from *tamei hu*, why do we need the double *yitamei* at all?

### Direct vs. Indirect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַד בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִין מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ, וְחַד בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִין מֵחֲמַת כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **one refers to liquids that come to a state of impurity due to contact with a creeping animal, while one verse refers to liquids that come to a state of impurity due to contact with an impure vessel.**

The answer is that the two *pesukim* are addressing two distinct cases. One — *chad* — speaks of *mashkin ha-ba'im mechamat sheretz*: liquid that became *tamei* through direct contact with a *sheretz*. The other speaks of *mashkin ha-ba'im mechamat kli*: liquid that became *tamei* not because the *sheretz* touched the liquid directly, but because the *sheretz* touched the *kli* containing the liquid, and through that vessel the liquid became *tamei*. In the second case, the *sheretz* touched the *kli*, the *kli* contained the *mashkeh*, and the *mashkeh* became *tamei* via the vessel — a more indirect path.

### Two Verses Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי? דְּאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִין מֵחֲמַת כְּלִי — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא חֲמִירִי. אֲבָל בְּמַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ — דַּחֲמִירִי, אֵימָא עוֹשֶׂה טוּמְאָה כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And both verses are necessary, as neither of the *halakhot* could have been derived from the other. As, had the verse taught us only the *halakha* with regard to liquids that come to a state of impurity due to contact with a vessel, one might have thought that liquids do not transfer impurity to similar items only due to the fact that their impurity is not severe, as it did not result from contact with a primary source of impurity; however, with regard to liquids that come to a state of impurity due to contact with a creeping animal, which are impure with a severe form of impurity that resulted from contact with a primary source of impurity, say that they transmit impurity to a similar item.**

Even though both cases involve a *sheretz* and both arrive at the same *halachic* outcome — *ein oseh tumah kayotze bah* — both *pesukim* are necessary, and neither can be inferred from the other alone.

If we had only the *pasuk* about *mashkin* coming through a *kli* — the indirect case — one might have thought the rule applies only there, *mishum delo chamiri*: precisely because that route is less direct, the *tumah* is weaker. By the time the contamination travels from *sheretz* to *kli* to *mashkeh*, the *mashkeh* is already a *sheni*, and being a *sheni*, it is less powerful. One might therefore say that such a weakened *tumah* cannot create like — but that a more direct *tumah*, where the *sheretz* touches the *mashkeh* itself, would be powerful enough to do so. Hence the necessity of the other *pasuk*, which addresses the direct case and teaches that even there — *ein oseh tumah kayotze bah*.

Conversely, had we only the direct case — *mashkin ha-ba'im mechamat sheretz* — and from it derived that even strong, direct *tumah* cannot create like, one might conclude *kal va'chomer* that certainly the weaker, indirect case cannot create like either. So in principle, one *pasuk* could have sufficed and the other inferred logically.

### Svara vs. Mikra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר טָרַח וְכָתֵב לַהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is not difficult, as at times with regard to **a matter that could be derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference, the verse nevertheless unnecessarily wrote it explicitly.**

This brings us to a striking tension. The *Gemara* acknowledges the principle of *milta d'atya b'kal va'chomer tarach v'chtav lah kra*: sometimes the Torah goes to the trouble of writing out explicitly what could have been derived through logical reasoning. This is essentially another way of saying *ein hachi nami* — yes, you could have inferred it, but the Torah chose to state it explicitly anyway.

This highlights a genuine tension in *halachic* methodology between learning a *din al pi svara* — through human reasoning — and learning it *al pi hamikra* — from an explicit scriptural source. The *Gemara* here takes the position that even where a logical derivation would suffice, an explicit *pasuk* is preferred. This is perhaps especially true in the domain of *tumah v'taharah*, which is sufficiently abstruse that explicit textual grounding is more desirable than relying on human creativity alone — even very sophisticated creativity.

## Rabbi Akiva/Yosei

### Ravina's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָא אָמַר רָבָא: לָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְלָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: But didn't Rava say that Rabbi Yosei does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva** with regard to the third-degree ritual impurity status of non-sacred items? Contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Yosei maintains that an item with second-degree ritual impurity does not confer third-degree impurity status upon non-sacred items by Torah law. **And similarly, Rabbi Akiva does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei** with regard to the fourth-degree impurity of consecrated property.

Ravina now challenges Rav Ashi by invoking a statement of Rava: *lo Rabbi Yosei savar ke-Rabbi Akiva v'lo Rabbi Akiva savar ke-Rabbi Yosei* — Rabbi Yosei does not hold like Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Akiva does not hold like Rabbi Yosei. This requires unpacking.

Recall that among the *Tannaim*, there were multiple positions regarding how far *tumos* extend. Rabbi Meir rejected any rabbinic extension of *tumah* altogether. Rabbi Yehudah accepted a more expansive position, though the question arose as to how far he ultimately retracted. Rabbi Yosei occupied a middle position — *ochlin* yes, *keilim* no — citing Rabbi Akiva's methodology while not endorsing his conclusions. And Rabbi Akiva himself pushed the categories further, maintaining that one can reach a *shlishi be-chullin*.

The question now is: how exactly do Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Akiva diverge? Rav Ashi formulates this for Rav Kahana.

### Yosei's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב כָּהֲנָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לָא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִנַּיִין לָרְבִיעִי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל? וְדִין הוּא: וּמָה מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים שֶׁמּוּתָּר בִּתְרוּמָה, פָּסוּל בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ. שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁפָּסוּל בִּתְרוּמָה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיַּעֲשֶׂה רְבִיעִי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ? וְלָמַדְנוּ שְׁלִישִׁי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ מִן הַתּוֹרָה, וּרְבִיעִי מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר. שְׁלִישִׁי מִן הַתּוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל״, מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּנָגַע בְּשֵׁנִי. רְבִיעִי מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, כִּדְאָמְרִינַן. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ סָבַר כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, נִיתְנֵי נָמֵי: רְבִיעִי בַּתְּרוּמָה וַחֲמִישִׁי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rav Kahana with regard to Rava's statement: Granted, Rabbi Yosei does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, as it was taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yosei said: From where is it derived with regard to a consecrated item with fourth-degree ritual impurity that it is only disqualified and does not transfer impurity to other objects? And this *halakha* is a logical *a fortiori* inference: Just as one who lacks atonement, e.g., a *zav* or leper who immersed at the conclusion of his period of impurity but has not yet brought an offering for his atonement, who is permitted to eat *teruma*, disqualifies a consecrated item if he comes into contact with it, with regard to third-degree ritual impurity, which disqualifies *teruma*, and in that way is more severe than one who lacks atonement, is it not right that it should confer fourth-degree ritual impurity status upon a consecrated item? And we derived third-degree impurity with regard to consecrated items from the Torah, and fourth-degree impurity of consecrated items by means of the above *a fortiori* inference. The fact that consecrated objects can assume third-degree impurity status is derived from the Torah, as it is written: "And the flesh that touches any impure thing shall not be eaten"** (Leviticus 7:19). **Are we not dealing even with a case where meat touched an object that was ritually impure with second-degree ritual impurity? And nevertheless, the verse states explicitly that it is impure and assumes third-degree ritual impurity status. No other source is needed to teach that consecrated objects can assume third-degree ritual impurity status. Therefore, fourth-degree impurity status can be derived by means of the *a fortiori* inference, as we stated above. And if it enters your mind that Rabbi Yosei holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva that non-sacred objects can assume third-degree impurity status, let him also teach the *halakha* of the fourth degree of impurity with regard to *teruma*, and the fifth degree with regard to consecrated items, on the basis of that same *a fortiori* inference.**

*Bishlama* — it is understandable why Rabbi Yosei does not hold like Rabbi Akiva. The proof comes from a *baraita* in which Rabbi Yosei himself teaches: *minayin li-revii ba-kodesh she-hu pasul* — from where do we know that a *revii* in *kodesh* is *pasul*? He then reasons as follows.

Everyone agrees that a *revii be-kodesh* is *pasul*. In the standard framework, *chullin* extends only to a *sheni*; *terumah* extends to a *shlishi*; *kodesh* extends to a *revii*. Rabbi Yosei establishes the *shlishi be-kodesh* as *min ha-Torah*, derived explicitly from the verse *ve-ha-basar asher yiga be-chol tamei lo ye-akhel* — the meat that touches anything *tamei* shall not be eaten. The *Gemara* reasons: *mi lo askinan di-nega be-sheni* — are we not dealing with a case where this flesh of *kodesh* touches a *sheni*? The flesh then becomes a *shlishi*, and the Torah says it may not be eaten. Since a *sheni* is called *tamei* — as established by the double *yitamei* discussed earlier — the verse teaches explicitly that a *shlishi be-kodesh* is *pasul min ha-Torah*.

From there, Rabbi Yosei derives the *revii be-kodesh* through a *kal va'chomer*: a *mechusar kippurim* — one who has immersed in a *mikvah* but not yet brought the required *korban*, and who may eat *terumah* but not *kodesh* — is *pasul* from *kodesh* though permitted in *terumah*. A *shlishi* is *pasul* from *terumah*. *Kal va'chomer*: if *mechusar kippurim*, who is less restricted in *terumah*, is excluded from *kodesh*, then a *shlishi* that is *pasul* in *terumah* should certainly produce a *revii* that is *pasul* in *kodesh*. The *kal va'chomer* derives the *revii* as *min ha-Torah* — not that a *kal va'chomer* automatically makes something *de-rabbanan*, but rather that the logical deduction draws its force from the implications of the underlying *pesukim* and thus carries *de-oraita* weight.

Now, if Rabbi Yosei were actually operating on Rabbi Akiva's framework — which extends *chullin* one further level to a *shlishi be-chullin* — he should logically have also taught that there is a *revii be-terumah* and a *chamishi be-kodesh*. But he does not. He stops at *revii be-kodesh* and *shlishi be-terumah*. This proves that Rabbi Yosei does not hold like Rabbi Akiva.

### Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לָא סָבַר כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי מְנָלַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּלָא לִישְׁתְּמִיט תַּנָּא וְלִיתְנֵי רְבִיעִי בַּתְּרוּמָה וַחֲמִישִׁי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ, וְנֵימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא. וַאֲנַן אַהָכִי נֵיקוּם וְנִסְמוֹךְ?!
>
> **Translation:** **However, with regard to the fact that Rabbi Akiva does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, from where do we derive this? Perhaps he accepts Rabbi Yosei's *a fortiori* inference and holds that *teruma* assumes fourth-degree impurity status and consecrated items assume fifth-degree impurity status. Rav Kahana said to Rav Ashi that there is indirect proof that this is the case. As it is not possible to avoid finding at least one *tanna* who teaches fourth-degree impurity with regard to *teruma* and fifth-degree impurity with regard to consecrated items, and says that this is the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who derived it from the *a fortiori* inference of Rabbi Yosei. In response to this claim, the Gemara asks: And will we stand and rely on that proof? Can proof for Rabbi Akiva's opinion be cited from the fact that no such *tanna* was found? Perhaps there is some source for that *halakha*.**

The reverse — that Rabbi Akiva does not hold like Rabbi Yosei — is established differently. Rav Ashi argues: if Rabbi Akiva's position implied the further extension that Rabbi Yosei's *kal va'chomerim* would logically entail, then surely some *tanna* would at some point have come forward and taught explicitly in Rabbi Akiva's name that there is a *revii be-terumah* and a *chamishi be-kodesh*. The fact that no such teaching exists anywhere suggests that Rabbi Akiva's position does not generate that conclusion.

This is, however, immediately questioned: *v'anan hachi nekum v'nismach* — can we really rely on this? Is absence of evidence evidence of absence? Is the fact that no *tanna* ever formulated such a teaching sufficient proof that Rabbi Akiva would have rejected it? This is a striking methodological moment: the later Amoraim are openly debating whether silence in the tradition can constitute positive proof of a rejected position.

### Kli Metzaref

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַק רַב אָשֵׁי, וְאִי תֵּימָא רַב כָּהֲנָא, דַּק וְאַשְׁכַּח הָא דִּתְנַן: הַכְּלִי מְצָרֵף אֶת מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ לַקּוֹדֶשׁ, אֲבָל לֹא לַתְּרוּמָה. וְהָרְבִיעִי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ פָּסוּל, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי בַּתְּרוּמָה. וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵעֵדוּתוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נִשְׁנֵית מִשְׁנָה זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi, and some say it was Rav Kahana, left the study hall to examine this matter. He analyzed the issue and found proof positive that Rabbi Akiva does not hold that there is fifth-degree impurity with regard to consecrated items. He proved this from that which we learned in a mishna: A vessel joins that which is in it into a single unit. For example, if there are fruits in a vessel between which there is no contact and one of them became ritually impure, all of the fruits are impure, as they are joined by the vessel. This principle applies with regard to consecrated property, but not with regard to *teruma*. And the fourth degree of impurity disqualifies consecrated items but does not transmit impurity, while third-degree impurity disqualifies *teruma*. And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is from the testimony of Rabbi Akiva that this mishna is taught.**

To resolve the question, Rav Ashi — or, as the *Gemara* says, *v'isayma* Rav Kahana — investigated and found an explicit teaching. *Dak v'ashkach*: he searched and discovered. *Hachi tnan*: the following is a *Mishnah*: *ha-kli m'tzaref es mah she-b'tocho l'kodesh aval lo l'terumah* — the vessel combines everything contained within it for purposes of *kodesh*, but not for *terumah*. And accordingly, a *revii be-kodesh* is *pasul* and a *shlishi be-terumah* is *pasul*.

This teaching requires explanation, particularly to distinguish it from the case of apples lined up in a row. If one apple touches the next, and the next touches the next, the *tumah* would theoretically extend without limit — or rather, it would reach whatever level of depletion *halakha* recognizes, but the point is that the contamination would flow through sequential contact, not through the vessel itself. The principle of *kli metzaref mah she-b'tocho* is different: it is not the sequential touching that matters, but the fact that all the contents share a common vessel that unites them as one. The *kli* acts as an encompassing entity, and through its containing power, everything inside it is treated as a unit — and the *tumah* of that unit extends as far as the *kli metzaref* concept carries it, reaching to *revii be-kodesh* but no further.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba then says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *me'iduto shel Rabbi Akiva nishnit mishna zu* — this *Mishnah* was taught based on the testimony of Rabbi Akiva.

### Revii Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (דְּתַנְיָא:) הוֹסִיף רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הַסּוֹלֶת וְהַקְּטוֹרֶת וְהַלְּבוֹנָה וְהַגֶּחָלִים, שֶׁאִם נָגַע טְבוּל יוֹם בְּמִקְצָתָן פָּסַל אֶת כּוּלָּן. רְבִיעִי אִין, חֲמִישִׁי לָא. שְׁלִישִׁי אִין, רְבִיעִי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **as it was taught in a *baraita*: Rabbi Akiva added in his testimony with regard to the fine flour, the incense, the frankincense, and the coals on the altar, which are not foods and do not ordinarily become impure, that if a person who immersed during that day, who disqualifies consecrated items, touches some of them, he disqualifies all of them, as the vessel joins them into one unit. This *baraita*, which is the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, states that with regard to fourth-degree impurity, yes, consecrated objects assume that status; however, with regard to fifth-degree impurity, no, consecrated objects do not assume that status. With regard to third-degree impurity, yes, *teruma* assumes that status; however, with regard to fourth-degree impurity, no, *teruma* does not assume that status.**

The attribution to Rabbi Akiva is confirmed from a *Mishnah* in *Eduyot*. The case involves a *tvul yom* — one who has immersed but for whom the sun has not yet set, and who therefore has not yet achieved full *taharah*. Rabbi Akiva added to the standard list of items susceptible to contamination: not only food items like *soles*, but also *ketores*, *levonah*, and *gechalim* — the coals used to burn the *ketores*, which are certainly not food. All of these, Rabbi Akiva taught, are subject to the rule that if a *tvul yom* touches part of them — *im naga tvul yom be-miktzatam* — the entire contents are rendered *pasul*. This is precisely because they are in a *kli* together: the vessel unites them, and the *tvul yom* touching one of the coals on the *mizbei'ach* renders all the coals in that vessel as one unit.

The crucial point is that Rabbi Akiva's teaching goes only as far as a *revii*. *Ve-amar Rabbah bar bar Chanah amar Rabbi Yochanan me'eiduto shel Rabbi Akiva nishnit mishna zu* — and the *Mishnah* reaches only to *revii be-kodesh*. If Rabbi Akiva had accepted the *kal va'chomer* logic that Rabbi Yosei employed, he should have extended the chain further — to a *revii be-terumah* and a *chamishi be-kodesh*. He did not. The explicit teaching attributed to him reaches only as far as *revii be-kodesh* and *shlishi be-terumah*, thereby confirming that Rabbi Akiva does not hold the *kal va'chomer* that would extend things further. The absence-of-evidence argument is thus vindicated, but now backed by explicit textual support: we have a positive teaching from Rabbi Akiva that stops precisely where it should stop if he rejects the further extension.

### Tziruf Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: צֵירוּף דְּרַבָּנַן. וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַבִּי חָנִין, דְּאָמַר: צֵירוּף דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כַּף אַחַת עֲשָׂרָה זָהָב מְלֵאָה קְטֹרֶת״, הַכָּתוּב עָשָׂה כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבַּכַּף אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara comments: Apparently, Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that joining in a single vessel, of frankincense, incense, or coals, is a *halakha* by rabbinic law, not by Torah law, as the ritual impurity of frankincense and coals is by rabbinic law. And Rabbi Yoḥanan disputes that statement of Rabbi Ḥanin, who said: Joining in a vessel is a *halakha* by Torah law, as it is stated: "One golden spoon of ten shekels, filled with incense"** (Numbers 7:20). **The verse rendered everything in the spoon, i.e., all the incense, as one entity.**

The *Gemara* then notes that this entire principle of *tziruf* — of the vessel combining its contents — is, in Rabbi Akiva's view, *de-rabbanan*, and he therefore disagrees with Rabbi Chanin.

Rabbi Chanin holds that *tziruf de-oraita* — the combining power of a vessel is a Torah-level concept — and he derives this from the *korbanot* of the *nesi'im*: *kaf achas asara zahav meleia ketores* — one ladle of ten [*shekalim*] weight, full of *ketores*. The verse says *kaf achas* — one cup — and the *Gemara* reads this *derashah*: the *kaf* makes everything within it *achas*, one unified entity. From this, Rabbi Chanin derives that the combining power of a vessel is *min ha-Torah*.

Rabbi Akiva, however, holds that *tziruf d'rabbanan* — the concept is rabbinic in nature, since the Torah-level discussions of *tumah* concerned *ochlin* and *mashkin*, and extending it to *keilim* and their combining power requires rabbinic enactment.

## Needle Case

### Needle Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: עַל מַחַט שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת בַּבָּשָׂר, שֶׁהַסַּכִּין וְהַיָּדַיִם — טְהוֹרוֹת, וְהַבָּשָׂר טָמֵא. נִמְצֵאת בַּפֶּרֶשׁ — הַכֹּל טָהוֹר. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: זָכִינוּ שֶׁאֵין טוּמְאַת יָדַיִם בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos ritual purity and impurity in the Temple, the Gemara cites that **we learned in a mishna there: The Sages testified about the case of a needle that was found in the meat of an animal that was led through water, that the knife and the hands that touched the needle are ritually pure but the meat is impure, as the needle might have been impure. If the needle was found in the secretions of the animal's stomach, everything is pure. Rabbi Akiva said: We were privileged to learn a novel *halakha* from here, which is that there is no impurity of hands in the Temple** as in this case the hands did not become impure upon contact with the needle.

The *Gemara* transitions to a new topic with the teaching *tenan hasam*: a *Mishnah* cited from elsewhere. *Ha-machat she-nimtzet ba-basar* — a needle that was found in meat. The case is that an animal of *kodshim* was slaughtered and a needle was discovered inside the flesh. The ruling: *ha-sakin ve-ha-yadayim tehorot* — the knife and the hands remain *tahor*. We do not assume that the knife, used in cutting the meat, necessarily struck the needle; nor do we assume the hands came into contact with it. However, *ve-ha-basar tamei* — the meat itself is *tamei*. And if the needle was found in the excrement of the animal rather than in the flesh, *ha-kol tahor* — everything, including the meat, is *tahor*.

### No Hand Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא: שֶׁאֵין טוּמְאַת יָדַיִם וְכֵלִים בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: יָדַיִם קוֹדֶם גְּזֵירַת כֵּלִים נִשְׁנוּ. אָמַר רָבָא: וְהָא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם גָּזְרוּ, דִּתְנַן: הַסֵּפֶר וְהַיָּדַיִם וְהַטְּבוּל יוֹם וְהָאוֹכָלִין וְהַכֵּלִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ בְּמַשְׁקִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us say that Rabbi Akiva says that we learn from here that there is no ritual impurity of hands and vessels in the Temple, as the mishna says that the knife which touched the needle is also pure. Rav Yehuda said that Rav said, and some say that it was Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina:** The testimony that there is no ritual impurity for **hands was taught prior to the decree of impurity for vessels** that came in contact with impure liquids outside the Temple. Therefore, there was no novelty in the fact that there is no ritual impurity of vessels in the Temple. **Rava said: But weren't both decrees issued on the same day? As we learned in a mishna: The impurity of a Torah scroll and other sacred scrolls, and the impurity of hands that were not washed or immersed, and the impurity of one who immersed himself during that day, and the impurity of foods and vessels that became impure by contact with impure liquids, all these are included in the eighteen matters with regard to which decrees were issued on the same day.**

Upon this, Rabbi Akiva comments: *zachinu she-ein tumas yadayim ba-Mikdash* — we have gained, we are fortunate, that there is no *tumat yadayim* in the *Mikdash*. The point requires context. *Tumat yadayim* is fundamentally a rabbinic enactment — hands are presumed *shniyot le-tumah* by rabbinic decree, and there was no corresponding *gezeirah* imposed within the *Mikdash* itself. Rabbi Akiva's comment reflects that outside the *Mikdash* we are more *machmir* in this regard, while the *Mikdash* was deliberately left free of this particular rabbinic imposition.

### Kli Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הַנַּח לְטוּמְאַת סַכִּין, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּחוּלִּין נָמֵי לָא מְטַמֵּא. הַאי סַכִּין דִּנְגַע בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא דִּנְגַע בְּבָשָׂר — הָא אֵין אוֹכֶל מְטַמֵּא כְּלִי, וְאֶלָּא דִּנְגַע בְּמַחַט — וְהָא אֵין כְּלִי מְטַמֵּא כְּלִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: Leave the impurity of the knife, as even outside the Temple in non-sacred circumstances it does not become impure. As in the case of this knife, what did it touch that could transmit impurity? If you say that it touched the meat, food does not transmit impurity to a vessel. If you say, rather, that it touched the needle, a vessel does not transmit impurity to another vessel.**

One might ask: the *Mishnah*'s case mentions that the *sakin* was *tahor* — yet Rabbi Akiva speaks only of *yadayim*. Why does he not also say that we have gained that there is no *tumat keilim* in the *Mikdash*?

A first answer suggested is that of Rav Yehudah citing Rav: the laws of *tumat yadayim* and *tumat keilim* were enacted at different times, and Rabbi Akiva's comment was made after the *gezeirah* on *yadayim* but before the *gezeirah* on *keilim* had been enacted — hence no need to mention what did not yet exist.

Rava rejects this: both *gezeirot* were enacted on the same day. The *Gemara* cites the *Mishnah* listing the famous eighteen decrees issued in the *aliyah* of Chananya ben Chizkiya ben Garon, in a session in which *Beis Shammai* prevailed. That list mentions *ha-sefer*, *ha-yadayim*, *ha-tvul yom*, and *ha-ochlin ve-ha-keilim she-nitme'u be-mashkin* — all together, in one sitting. *Yadayim* and *keilim* are part of the same decree. One cannot say Rabbi Akiva was commenting before one but after the other.

The true answer, Rava explains, is simpler: *hanach l'tumas sakin de-afilu be-chullin nami lo metamei*. Leave aside the *tumah* of the *sakin* entirely — even in *chullin*, the knife would not have been *tamei*. Why not? *Hai sakin d'naga b'mai* — what did the knife touch that would make it *tamei*? If it touched the meat — *ein ochel metamei kli*: food cannot render a vessel *tamei*. If it touched the needle — *ein kli metamei kli*: a vessel cannot render another vessel *tamei*. The *machat* is a *kli* and the *sakin* is a *kli*, and we have established that like does not contaminate like in this sense either. Therefore there was nothing to say: even in *chullin*, the knife would never have been *tamei* from this situation, and so there was no gain specific to the *Mikdash* worth announcing.

### Safek in Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַחַט מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? אִי נֵימָא סְפֵק מַחַט, וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, חַד אָמַר: לֹא גָּזְרוּ עַל סְפֵק הָרוּקִּין שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְחַד אָמַר: לֹא גָּזְרוּ עַל סְפֵק הַכֵּלִים שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **this needle, the Gemara asks: What is its impurity status? If we say that there is uncertainty with regard to the impurity of the needle, wasn't it stated that there is a dispute between Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina? One said:** The Sages **did not issue a decree in the case of uncertainty with regard to the impurity of spittle that is found in Jerusalem.** Any spittle found outside of Jerusalem might have come from a *zav* or from a gentile, whose legal status in this regard is like that of a *zav*. The Sages decreed that any contact with this spittle should be treated as uncertain contact with a primary source of ritual impurity. That decree was not issued with regard to spittle found in Jerusalem. **And one said:** The Sages **did not issue a decree in the case of uncertainty with regard to the impurity of vessels in Jerusalem.** As opposed to the situation outside of Jerusalem, there is no presumption of impurity with regard to vessels found in Jerusalem, including a needle.

This brings the *Gemara* to a new question: what exactly makes the needle *tamei* in the first place? The ruling is that the meat is *tamei* because of the needle, but why should we assume the needle is *tamei*? One might say it is a *safek* — an uncertainty — and surely there is a principle at work: the *Gemara* cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi Elazar, Rabbi Yosei, and Rabbi Chanina all hold that no *gezeirah* of *tumah* was enacted upon *safek*-cases in Jerusalem. One version of the teaching is *lo gazru al safek ha-rukin she-bi-Yerushalayim* — we do not rule *tamei* regarding doubtfully *tamei* spit found in Jerusalem. Since Jerusalem is populated by people striving toward *taharah*, and since those with *zav*-status would presumably avoid spitting publicly, the *rov* of spit found there may be presumed *tahor*.

Another version of the teaching states: *lo gazru al safek ha-keilim she-bi-Yerushalayim* — we do not rule *tamei* regarding vessels of uncertain status found in Jerusalem, since people are generally careful to maintain *taharah* of their vessels in the holy city. If the needle is at most a *safek* as to whether it was rendered *tamei*, and if *safek keilim bi-Yerushalayim* are presumed *tahor*, then the needle should be *tahor* — and the meat should be *tahor* as well. Why, then, does the *Mishnah* rule the meat *tamei*?

### Vadai Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: הָכָא לָא בְּסָפֵק, אָלָא בְּוַדַּאי, כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָבְדָה לוֹ מַחַט טְמֵא מֵת, וְהִכִּירָהּ בַּבָּשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda citing Rav resolves this: This is not a *safek* case at all. The case of the *Mishnah* is a *vadai*: specifically, a person who was *tamei met* had lost a needle, and now he recognizes it in the meat.**

Rav Yehudah citing Rav resolves this: *hacha lav be-safek* — this is not a *safek* case at all. The case of the *Mishnah* is a *vadai*: specifically, *kegon she-ivad lo machat temei met ve-hikkirah ba-basar* — a person who was *tamei met* had lost a needle, and now he recognizes it in the meat. He is a *tamei met*, he touched this needle, and here it is — found in the animal that was slaughtered. The needle is definitively *tamei*, not doubtfully so, and therefore the meat it was embedded in is *tamei*. The tailorer who was *tamei met* lost his needle; it was swallowed by the animal; the needle is now recovered from the flesh. The principles regarding *safek keilim bi-Yerushalayim* simply do not apply, since there is no *safek* here.

### Muzzled Cow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה פָּרָה חֲסוּמָה, וּבָאָה מִחוּץ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Avin, said:** This is referring to a case **where the cow was muzzled as it came from outside of Jerusalem.** The needle is clearly from outside of Jerusalem, and in all cases of uncertainty with regard to vessels outside of Jerusalem the ruling is that they are impure.

Rabbi Yosei bar Avin offers an alternative construction of the case, one that preserves the *safek* framework while still explaining why the meat is *tamei*. The case is *kegon she-haytah parah chasumah u-va'ah michutz li-Yerushalayim* — a cow that was muzzled and came from outside Jerusalem. Since the cow was muzzled, it could not have ingested anything while in Jerusalem; therefore any needle found inside it must have been swallowed before it arrived — that is, from outside Jerusalem. And outside Jerusalem, the principle of *lo gazru al safek* does not apply: *safek* *tumah* in the public domain is resolved as *tamei* in the normal way. The needle came from outside, its *safek* status is therefore *tamei*, and accordingly the meat is *tamei*. The muzzled cow thus establishes that the needle could not have come from within Jerusalem, removing any benefit of the special leniency that applies to *safek keilim* within the city.
