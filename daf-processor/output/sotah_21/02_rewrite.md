# Sotah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Merit Forestall Punishme…

### Merit Three Years

The *mishnah* established that *zechus* can forestall the punishment of the *sotah* for as long as three years. The *gemara* now asks: *zechus d'mai*? What type of merit possesses sufficient power to delay punishment for that length of time?

The *gemara* raises two possibilities. The first is *zechus d'Torah* — perhaps it is the merit of learning Torah. Yet this is immediately challenged: *ha einah metzuva v'osa* — a woman is not commanded to learn Torah, and therefore, as an *eina metzuva v'osa*, the merit she accumulates through Torah study does not carry the full weight that *talmud Torah* ordinarily confers. The second possibility is *zechus d'mitzvah* — the merit of other *mitzvos* she has performed. But this too is called into question. *Zechus d'mitzvah mi magna kulei hai?* Since when does a *mitzvah* wield that kind of protective power?

### Ner Mitzvah Torah Or

To press this challenge, the *gemara* cites a *braita* in the name of Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi, a teaching entirely general in character, concerned with the relative protective power of *mitzvah* as against Torah. *Ki ner mitzvah v'Torah or* — for a *mitzvah* is a candle and the Torah is light. The verse, says the *braita*, deliberately compares the *mitzvah* to a *ner* and the Torah to *or*, and the lesson drawn from each comparison is distinct.

*Ma ner einah megina ela lefi sha'ah* — just as a candle provides light or protection only for a limited time, so too *af mitzvah einah megina ela lefi sha'ah*, a *mitzvah* only protects for a brief period. *Torah*, by contrast, is compared to *or* in the broader sense of light itself — *ma or magin l'olam*, light illuminates perpetually — and so *af Torah megina l'olam*, Torah protects forever.

The *gemara* then cites the verse from Mishlei: *b'hishalchecha tancha osach, b'shochbecha tishmor alecha, v'hakitzosa hi tisichecha*. The *braita* reads this verse as a sequence. *B'hishalchecha tancha osach* — when you walk, it will guide you — *zeh l'olam hazeh*, this refers to Torah's guidance in this world. *B'shochbecha tishmor alecha* — when you sleep, it will guard you — *zu misah*, Torah protects even in death. *V'hakitzosa hi tisichecha* — and when you awaken it will speak for you — *l'asid lavo*, in the future world, where the Torah one has learned will serve as an advocate.

The difficulty this creates for the *gemara*'s question is now clear. If a *mitzvah* protects only briefly, it would seem insufficient to forestall punishment for as long as three years. And if the relevant *zechus* is Torah, the power of that protection would seem unavailable to a woman at full strength, since she is an *eina metzuva v'osa*.

One might argue that the protective power of Torah does not derive from the *kiyum hamitzvah* of *talmud Torah*, but from the intrinsic quality of Torah as *dvar Hashem* — from connecting with the word of God — and that this power operates regardless of whether one is commanded. That argument is available, and it is not without force. Yet the *stam* of the *gemara*, at least at this stage of the discussion, appears to proceed on the assumption that the protective quality of Torah flows through the value of fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *talmud Torah*, not from some independent sacramental power of Torah itself. This raises a further question: even if a woman's Torah study is understood merely as *hechsher mitzvah* — a means of learning the *halachos* of what she must do — how could that instrumental learning confer greater protection than the *mitzvos* it enables her to perform? If a *mitzvah* protects only briefly, the learning that enables it cannot protect longer. The *gemara* thus leaves both possibilities in difficulty.

Before moving to the *gemara*'s resolution, it is worth noting an apparent tension in the tradition's broader framework. The *Shulchan Aruch* rules that women do not recite a *bracha* over *mitzvas asei shehazman grama* if they choose to perform them, yet women do recite *birchas haTorah* in the morning. The Soloveitchik analysis holds that *birchas haTorah* is not a *birchas hamitzvah* in the ordinary sense but rather a *bracha* on the *cheftza* of Torah itself — as evidenced by the blessing's own language: *asher kidshanu b'mitzvosav v'tzivanu la'asok b'divrei Torah* points toward the *mitzvah* dimension, but *v'ha'arev na*, *melamed Torah l'amo Yisrael*, and *bocher asher bachar banu* address Torah's intrinsic character as *Nosen HaTorah*. On this view, the protective power of Torah could indeed be understood as independent of *kiyum hamitzvah*. Yet, as noted, this is not the path the *gemara* chooses to take.

The *braita* then adds a second formulation: *aveira mechaba mitzvah, v'ein aveira mechaba Torah* — a sin extinguishes a *mitzvah*, but a sin cannot extinguish Torah. Rashi explains that "extinguish" here refers not to the protective power of the *mitzvah* but to its *s'char* — its reward. One who has performed *mitzvos* and also committed *aveiros* may find that *le'atid lavo* they cancel each other out. But *aveiros* will not cancel the reward of Torah. This is supported by the verse *mayim rabim lo yuchlu lechabot es ha'ahavah* — great waters cannot extinguish the love. The *Maharsha* observes that the imagery is striking: Torah is generally compared to water, yet here the *aveiros* are the water and Torah is the flame — the *ner*, the *or* — that cannot be quenched. So *mitzvos* afford brief, momentary protection that may ultimately be neutralized by *aveiros*, while Torah confers a lasting, encompassing protection that endures. Neither option, then, cleanly solves the *gemara*'s problem.

### Parable Traveler

To illustrate the difference between the protection of *mitzvah* and the protection of Torah, the *braita* of Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi offers a *mashal*. A person is traveling in the darkness of night, *b'ishon layla va'afeila*, and he fears the thorns, the pits, the thistles, the wild animals, and the bandits. He also does not know which path to take.

He then chances upon a torch — *avuka shel or*. The torch immediately delivers him from the thorns, the pits, and the thistles — he can now see what lies immediately before him. Yet he is still afraid of the wild animals and the bandits, and he still does not know which path to take. When dawn breaks — *keivan she'ala amud hashachar* — the daylight saves him from the animals and the bandits, for these are dangers of the night that the light of day dispels. Yet even now he still does not know which way to go. When he reaches a crossroads — *parshas drachim* — he is saved from everything and knows the right path.

The *mashal* is clear in its first two stages. The torch is the *ner mitzvah*: it provides immediate protection from immediate dangers, but its reach is limited. The light of day is the *or Torah*: it is broader, more comprehensive, and protects from graver threats. But what is this *parshas drachim*, this crossroads where one finally knows the right direction? The *gemara* will address this — and the three opinions about its meaning constitute a striking discussion in their own right.

## Rav Yosef Answer

### Rav Yosef Position

Rav Yosef proposes a resolution that cuts through the apparent impasse. He distinguishes between two dimensions of protection — protection from punishment and protection from sin — and argues that *mitzvah* and Torah provide these in different combinations.

*Mitzvah b'idna d'asik bah megana u'matzla* — a *mitzvah* while you are in the course of performing it both protects you from punishment and, as Rashi explains based on the continuation of the *gemara*, saves you from sin. *B'idna d'lo asik bah* — once you have finished, *aguni magnei* — it still forestalls punishment, but *atzulei lo matzla* — it no longer protects you from sinning. Torah, by contrast, *bein b'idna d'asik bah u'vein b'idna d'lo asik bah*, whether you are actively learning or not, *megana u'matzla* — it both forestalls punishment and protects from sin. The educative power of Torah shapes character in a lasting way, setting a person on a more elevated path even when he is not sitting in the *beit midrash*. On this account, a *mitzvah* does forestall punishment even after it is completed, and that is sufficient to explain how this *sotah*'s merit delayed her punishment for up to three years. Torah's additional power is the ongoing protection from future sin.

### Rava Doeg Challenge

Rava challenges Rav Yosef's position. *Ela me'ata* — if Torah protects one from sinning even when not actively learning, how do we account for Doeg and Achitophel? These two figures, prominent in rabbinic literature as great *talmidei chachamim*, nonetheless sinned grievously, pursuing and attacking David. *Mi lo askei beTorah* — they certainly engaged in Torah study. *Amai lo higina aleihu* — why did their Torah learning not prevent them from sinning? The objection is fundamental: there are those who learn Torah and nonetheless go badly astray. Rav Yosef's formulation that Torah protects from sin while not learning cannot be sustained if it is falsified by such prominent counterexamples.

### Rava Revision

Rava therefore revises the framework. Torah, *b'idna de'asik bah*, while one is actively learning, *magna u'matzla* — protects from punishment and saves from sin. *B'idna delo asik bah*, when not learning, *aguni magna* — it still forestalls punishment, but *atzulei lo matzla* — it does not necessarily prevent sin. A person can learn Torah and then leave the *beit midrash* and sin. *Mitzvah*, meanwhile — *bein b'idna de'asik bah bein b'idna delo asik bah* — whether performing or not, *aguni magna* — protects from punishment, but *atzulei lo matzla* — cannot protect from sin, even during the act of performance. (It is at least theoretically possible, as the *Mishnah Berurah* hints regarding *lashon hara* in a *sukkah*, to be engaged in a *mitzvah* while simultaneously sinning in another way.)

This revised formulation serves the *gemara*'s purpose. The advantage of Torah over *mitzvah* is now narrowly defined: Torah, uniquely, prevents sin *while one is actively learning*. Both Torah and *mitzvah* forestall punishment after their performance is complete. This explains how a *sotah*'s merit of *mitzvah* could protect her for up to three years. It also accounts for Doeg and Achitophel — men who learned Torah but sinned when they left their learning. The difficulty with Rava's formulation is that it substantially attenuates the lovely imagery of the *braita*, which suggested Torah's protection was far more sweeping and enduring. But this is where the *gemara* arrives.

## Ravina Women Merit

### Ravina Merit Enable

Ravina offers a different resolution entirely, one that returns to the original question about the *sotah* rather than reconstructing the topology of *mitzvah* and Torah protection. *Le'olam zechus Torah* — it is in fact the merit of Torah that protects this woman. And as for the objection that she is an *eina metzuva v'osa* and therefore her Torah learning lacks full merit — *nihi depikudei lo mifkada* — it is true she is not commanded, *be'agra demakrin u'matnyan bnaihu* — but she shares in the reward that accrues from causing her sons to read *pesukim* and study *mishnah*, and *u'natrin lehu le'gavreihu ad de'atu mi-bei midrasha* — from waiting patiently for her husband to return from the study hall, maintaining the home, and enabling him to learn. *Mi lo palga bahadayhu?* — does she not receive a portion of that reward? By enabling the Torah learning of the men in her life — her sons and her husband — she participates in their *mitzva ve'oseh*, and it is that participation which confers upon her the *zechus Torah* that can forestall punishment.

### Women Enablers

This answer is candid about its assumptions: a woman's own Torah learning, as *eina metzuva v'osa*, does not carry the full protective weight of a man's Torah learning. The merit she acquires comes not from her own direct engagement with Torah as a commanded act, but from her role as enabler. She sends her children to *cheder*; she waits for her husband to return from the *beit midrash*.

This is not a *gemara* that necessarily sits easily with contemporary sensibilities, and it is worth noting that the *gemara* did not have to construct the question this way. The *mishnah* earlier cited Nevuchadnezzar's capacity for *zechus* to forestall punishment for three years — and his merit was simply giving charity to the poor. Nothing required the *gemara* to introduce the *braita* of *ner mitzvah v'Torah or*, to reject the possibility that an ordinary *mitzvah* could carry this power, or to resist the idea that a woman's Torah study as *eina metzuva v'osa* might nonetheless provide protection. The *gemara* chose this path. And the path it chose has a pointed contextual relationship with what follows: the later *mishnah* contains both Ben Azzai's view that one is obligated to teach one's daughter Torah and Rabbi Eliezer's opposition. Ravina's answer, appearing immediately before that discussion, effectively frames women's relationship to Torah learning in instrumental rather than intrinsic terms — their real merit lies in enabling men's Torah, not in their own engagement with it as an independent value.

The contrast with the *sotah* herself is also worth observing. The *sotah* was waiting — apparently — for her paramour. The righteous woman whom Ravina describes is waiting for her husband to return from learning Torah. The *gemara* appears to be drawing a deliberate contrast between two types of waiting.

## Merit Forestall Punish…

### Crossroads Interpretatio…

The *gemara* now returns to the unresolved element of the parable — the *parshas drachim*, the crossroads where the traveler is finally saved from everything and knows which path to take. *Mai parshas drachim?* Three *amoraim* offer interpretations, and taken together they form a suggestive continuum.

Rav Chisda says: *zu talmid chacham v'yom ha-misa* — this is a *talmid chacham* and the day of death. The Torah has guided one through life, protected from dangers both immediate and profound, and the *parshas drachim* is that final threshold — the moment of departing this world for the next. At that crossroads, one does not yet know whether the path leads to *olam ha-ba* or not — *al ta'amin b'atzmecha ad yom mosecha* — yet having arrived there through the guidance and protection of Torah, one now knows the Torah has led one through to the end, set one on the right path. The image is almost entirely sacramental: Torah, sustained throughout a lifetime of learning, carries a person through to the final reckoning.

Rav Yitzchak says: *zeh talmid chacham b'yirat chet* — this is a *talmid chacham* with fear of sin. For Rav Yitzchak, Torah illuminates the world and shows what is right, but Torah alone cannot guarantee that one will choose correctly. *Ra'eh nasati lefanecha hayom et ha-chayim v'et ha-tov v'et ha-mavet v'et ha-ra* — one now knows the two paths, but will one choose the right one? That depends on bringing to the *parshas drachim* a quality that Torah cannot by itself supply: *yirat chet*, the internalized fear of sin. Torah is necessary but not sufficient.

Mar Zutra says: *zeh talmid chacham d'sakla shma'ta aliba d'hilchasa* — this is a *talmid chacham* who successfully brings legal reasoning to its correct *halachic* conclusion. For Mar Zutra, even choosing to act rightly is not enough if one's Torah learning leads to incorrect *halachic* conclusions. Torah shows what is right in principle, but one must be able to translate that learning into sound *halacha l'maaseh*.

These three positions form a continuum from the mystical to the rational. For Rav Chisda, Torah has an almost intrinsic, sacramental protective power — sustain it till you die, and it will carry you through. For Rav Yitzchak, Torah is necessary but must be complemented by character, by *yirat chet*, the quality that ensures one actually chooses rightly when confronted with the fork in the road. For Mar Zutra, Torah must be disciplined into correct *halachic* reasoning — even character and good intent are insufficient if one's learning has led to wrong conclusions. Each of the latter two answers also implicitly pulls back from the claim that Torah does everything: one can be a *talmid chacham* without having the right character; one can learn Torah without arriving at the correct *halacha*.

## Aveira Sin Extinguish

### Aveira Mechabah

The *gemara* now returns to the formulation encountered earlier: *aveira mechaba mitzvah, v'ein aveira mechaba Torah* — a sin extinguishes a *mitzvah*, but a sin cannot extinguish Torah. The verse from Shir HaShirim is adduced: *mayim rabim lo yuchlu lechabot es ha'ahavah* — great waters cannot extinguish the love, where "love" signifies Torah. The *Maharsha* notes the poignancy of the imagery: elsewhere Torah is compared to water, but here the *aveiros* are the water and Torah is the flame — the *ner*, the *or* — that cannot be drowned.

### Doeg Achitophel

Rav Yosef introduces a *drasha* by Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi on a verse that Doeg and Achitophel might have encountered — and which, had they properly understood it as Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yosi did, *lo radfu batar Dovid*, they would not have pursued David.

Their reasoning, it appears, ran as follows. The verse *v'lo yeira'eh becha ervat davar v'shav me'acharecha* teaches that a sin of forbidden sexual relations causes God to withdraw. David had sinned in the matter of Bathsheba. Therefore, they concluded, God had abandoned David, and he was now susceptible to attack. *Leimor Elohim azavo* — God has forsaken him.

What they failed to understand — *v'hein einam yodim* — is precisely the principle of *aveira mechaba mitzvah, v'ein aveira mechaba Torah*. It is true that a *sin* can extinguish the protection of a *mitzvah*. But it cannot extinguish the protection of Torah. David was not merely a performer of *mitzvos*; he was a *talmid chacham*, a man of Torah. And because Torah's protection cannot be extinguished by an *aveira*, God did not forsake David. Doeg and Achitophel, brilliant scholars though they were in their own right, misread the verse and drew the wrong conclusion — they did not reckon with the distinct and indelible protective power of Torah.

The *gemara* then turns to the verse *im yitein ish es kol hon beiso b'ahavah boz yavozu lo* — if a man were to give all the wealth of his house for love, people would scorn him. The discussion of Hillel and his brother Shevna illuminates an ideal: Hillel devoted himself entirely to Torah without relying on material support from others, unwilling to trade any portion of his Torah's reward for financial ease. When Shevna proposed a *Yissachar-Zevulun* arrangement, a *bat kol* emerged and cited this very verse. Hillel would not commodify his Torah, would not give up so much as a fraction of his *talmud Torah* for worldly benefit. Ulla notes that this surpasses the model of those who learned Torah under the support of a brother or a *nasi* — worthy arrangements, but not the highest ideal.

## Teach Daughters Torah

### Ben Azzai Teach

With the new *amud* the *gemara* turns to the next *mishnah*, which contains a debate about teaching one's daughter Torah. Ben Azzai states: *chayav adam l'lamed es bito Torah* — a person is required to teach his daughter Torah. The reason is connected to the *sotah* procedure: the *mishnah* establishes that *zechus* can suspend the effects of the water, and a woman who has learned Torah will understand this — she will know why the water may have failed to act immediately and not suppose the *sotah* ritual has been definitively disproven. There is at least a practical argument for teaching women the relevant Torah.

Yet the discussion that follows is inflected by the *gemara* we have just completed. If teaching one's daughter Torah confers upon her the *zechus* of Torah — or at least, as Ravina suggested, enables her to become an instrument of Torah's merit — one might think there is every reason to teach her. But the opposing view raises a different concern entirely.

### Rabbi Eliezer Tiflus

Rabbi Eliezer states: *kol ha-melamed bito Torah k'ilu melamdah tiflus* — whoever teaches his daughter Torah is as though he has taught her *tiflus*. The term *tiflus*, which in context is clearly juxtaposed with *perishus* and tied to sexual transgression, most naturally denotes sexual licentiousness or conduct of a sexually improper character. Several sources confirm this reading: the *gemara* elsewhere connects *tiflus* to the disqualification of women and slaves from *zimmun* together, and the word appears in descriptions of the banquet of Achashverosh where the officers competed in boasting about women.

Why would teaching one's daughter Torah be equivalent to teaching her *tiflus*? Several possibilities present themselves. The most direct, and most relevant to the *sotah* context, is that Torah knowledge shows her the loopholes — she learns the *halachos* and learns how the system works, and that knowledge enables her to exploit it. A second possibility: Torah study develops nuance and analytical subtlety, and that capacity for fine distinctions can be turned to finding ways around prohibited conduct. A third, more structural concern is that Torah's *zechus* will protect her from punishment, thereby removing the deterrent effect of the *sotah* ritual.

### Torah Effect Tiflus

The *gemara* clarifies that Rabbi Eliezer's statement cannot be taken literally — Torah is obviously not itself *tiflus*. *Ela eima k'ilu limda tiflus* — it is as if he taught her *tiflus*, because the effect of her having learned Torah is equivalent in its practical consequences to having been taught licentiousness. It is not that Torah is shameful; it is that in this context, for this person, the knowledge functions as an opening toward transgression.

### Wisdom Craftiness

Rabbi Abbahu identifies the underlying principle of Rabbi Eliezer's position: *d'ksiv ani chochma shakanti arma* — I am wisdom, I have dwelt with craftiness. *Keivan she-nichnas chochma b'adam nichnesa imo armumis* — once wisdom enters a person, craftiness enters with it. Wisdom and cunning travel together; learning sharpens the mind, and a sharper mind finds more ways to achieve what it desires. The concern, then, is not that Torah is corrupting in any direct sense, but that it arms one with intellectual tools that can be turned in any direction.

The obvious question arises: what of men? Men learn Torah and thereby also acquire *arma* — craftiness. Rabbi Abbahu's principle seems to apply universally. The *gemara* does not resolve this question explicitly here. One might say that men are obligated to learn regardless and cannot be exempt on account of this danger, or that the tradition places greater trust in men's use of those tools — though neither answer is articulated in so many words.

### Rabbanan Three Positions

The Rabbanan who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer ask: *hai ani chochma mai avdi lei?* What do they do with the verse *ani chochma shakanti arma*? They apply it differently. Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina reads *arma* not as craftiness but as nakedness — *ein divrei Torah miskaymin ela b'mi she-ma'amid atzmo arom aleihen* — Torah endures only in one who makes himself naked for it, strips away all other pursuits and devotes himself entirely. Rashi, however, reads it as a variant of the craftiness interpretation: one who schemes and maneuvers to find every possible opportunity to learn Torah, seizing moments wherever they appear. Rabbi Yochanan adds: *ein divrei Torah miskaymin ela b'mi she-mesim atzmo k'mi she-eino* — Torah endures only in one who makes himself as nothing, practicing a radical self-negation and self-sacrifice for the sake of learning. *V'hachochma me'ayin timatzei* — wisdom comes from nothingness; make yourself nothing, and Torah will find its home in you.

These three readings of *arma* — the sacramental (nakedness, total devotion), the strategic (seizing every opportunity to learn), and the spiritual (self-negation) — stand in contrast to Rabbi Eliezer's use of the same word. Rabbi Eliezer sees *arma* as the danger that Torah's wisdom produces; the Rabbanan see *arma* as precisely the quality one must bring *to* Torah in order to truly acquire it.

### Yerushalmi Matron

Tosafot cites a crucial passage from the Yerushalmi that illuminates the full scope of Rabbi Eliezer's position. A *matrona* — a Roman noblewoman — posed a genuine question to Rabbi Eliezer: why did the sin of the golden calf, which was one sin, result in three different forms of death? Rabbi Eliezer refused to answer her. *Ein isha chachma ela bapalach* — a woman's wisdom is only in her weaving. The school in Israel called *Pelech* derives its name from this phrase, chosen deliberately and ironically.

When his son Horkenos reproached him — *bishvil shelo le'ashivah davar echad min haTorah*, you refused to answer a single question of Torah to this woman, and by insulting her you have cost me three hundred *kor* of *ma'aser* annually — Rabbi Eliezer replied: *yisrefu divrei Torah v'al yimsaru divrei Torah l'nashim* — better that the words of Torah be burnt than that they be transmitted to women.

This was not merely the concern about *melamdah tiflus*. Rabbi Eliezer held a principled, sweeping opposition to women receiving Torah. The image is striking in context: in the *sotah* ritual, Torah is dissolved into water to be tested against the woman; here, Rabbi Eliezer would rather Torah burn than be given over to women. In both cases, Torah must be protected from a certain kind of contact with women — though obviously for entirely different reasons and with very different valences.

The Yerushalmi also provides important context for Ben Azzai's position. Ben Azzai is said to follow the view of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah, who expounded the verse of *hakhel*: *ha'anashim, v'hanashim, v'hatef* — the men came to learn, *nashim ba'os lishmoa*, the women came to hear. As Tosafot explains, this means women's participation in *hakhel* is not for the sake of *zechus Torah* per se, but *kedei sheyeidu lekayeim mitzvah* — so that they know how to fulfill *mitzvos*. This establishes a middle position between the poles of the debate: it is not an intrinsic *Torah lishma* for women, but neither is it *melamdah tiflus*. There are thus three positions in the tradition: Torah study for women has intrinsic value; Torah study for women is valuable as instrumental knowledge enabling *kiyum mitzvos*; and Torah study for women is actively dangerous.

## Rabbi Yehoshua Woman

### Kav Tiflus Nine

Rabbi Yehoshua's statement returns the discussion to the sexual dimension that runs throughout the tractate. *Rotze isha bekav ve'tiflut mitisha kabim u'frishus* — a woman prefers one measure of sustenance with *tiflus* over nine measures with *perishus*, with asceticism and separation.

### Income Presence

Two readings of this statement emerge from the *rishonim*. Rashi understands *tiflus* in the general sense of greater sexual freedom, and the contrast is between a modest life that affords greater sexual opportunity and an affluent one with more separation. Rabbeinu Chananel, cited by Tosafot, makes the *pshat* more specific and more domestic: a woman would rather her husband be a donkey driver who comes home regularly — once a week on *Shabbat*, providing *onah* — earning a modest income of one *kav*, than have him be a camel driver who travels on long routes, comes home only once every thirty days, and earns the much larger income of nine *kabim*. *Chavivah aleiha onah bechol Shabbat vekav echad mitisha kabim u'frishus sheloshim yom*.

One might observe that this preference — for the husband's presence over material wealth — seems entirely natural and even admirable. Yet in context Rabbi Yehoshua presents it as a concern, because the same drive, misdirected, becomes the source of the *sotah*'s downfall. And the teaching connects back to the question of Torah instruction: the danger of teaching a woman Torah, in Rabbi Eliezer's view, is that Torah arms her with the tools to pursue what she desires. Rabbi Yehoshua simply asserts that what she desires, at bottom, is this.

## Four Destroyers

### Chasid Shoteh

In the same context, the *mishnah* records a teaching attributed to Rabbi Yehoshua: there are those who destroy the world, and among them is the *chasid shoteh*, the foolish pious person. *Heichy dami chasid shoteh?* — what does a *chasid shoteh* look like? *Kegon deka taveia itta benahra, ve'amar lav orach ara listakulei bah, ve'atzulah* — a woman is drowning in the river, and he says it is improper to gaze at a woman, and he fails to save her.

Tosafot cites a parallel from the Yerushalmi: an infant is drowning, and the *chasid shoteh* says, let me first remove my *tefillin* and then I will save him — *ad shecholeitz tefillin yatzah nishmato*, and by the time he finishes removing his *tefillin*, the child has died. There is also the neat wordplay embedded in the Yerushalmi version: *echlotz* — I will remove — echoes the language of *tefillin* removal, *chalitzah*, while the word itself sounds like a cry of urgency. The point in both cases is the same: piety that is divorced from practical moral reasoning, that privileges the form of righteousness over its substance, destroys lives.

### Rasha Arum

The *rasha arum*, the crafty wicked person, is the counterpart of the *chasid shoteh*. Where the *chasid shoteh* is conspicuously righteous while causing harm through his failure to engage with reality, the *rasha arum* conceals his wickedness behind a facade of ordinariness or even apparent virtue. What ties this figure to the others in the list is the same element — he is not what he appears. His *rishus* is executed *b'armimus*, with craftiness, such that it is not apparent to observers that anything wrongful has occurred.

### Early Case Judge

Rabbi Yochanan identifies the *rasha arum* as *zeh hamati'im devarav le'dayan kodem sheba ba'al din chaveiro* — one who presents his version of the case to the judge before the opposing litigant arrives. Even if the judge intends to be impartial, once a case has been framed in a particular way it is very difficult to genuinely reorganize one's understanding around the opposing account. First impressions in litigation create a bias that is hard to dislodge. There is in fact a prohibition against hearing one *ba'al din* in the absence of the other, and Rabbi Yochanan's identification of this as *rasha arum* behavior underlines why: it is subtle, it does not appear to be a violation of anything, and yet it has a real and corrupting effect on the judicial process.

### Poor Disqualify

Rabbi Abbahu identifies the *rasha arum* as one who gives a poor person just enough money to push him over the threshold of eligibility — *zeh ha-nosein dinar le'ani le'hashlim lo masayim zuz*. The rule, as the *mishnah* establishes, is that one who possesses two hundred *zuz* may not take *leket*, *shichecha*, *peah*, or *maaser ani* — he is not considered an *ani*. One who has one *dinar* less than two hundred *zuz* is an *ani* and may receive these gifts in full. By giving the poor person precisely the *dinar* that brings him to two hundred *zuz*, the donor appears to be performing an act of generosity — he gives the man something! — while in fact cutting him off from all future entitlement to *matnos aniyim*. The one-time gift costs the donor a *dinar*; the loss to the poor person is potentially enormous.

### Estate Sale

Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, identifies a further instance of the *rasha arum*: *zeh ha-masi eitza limkor b'nechasim mu'atim* — one who counsels the sale of a small estate in circumstances where this advice will harm the orphaned daughters.

The legal background, as Rabbi Yosi cites in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is the rule that if male heirs from a small estate sell it prematurely, *mah she-machru machru* — what they have sold is sold. In a small estate, all the assets are in principle earmarked to support the daughters — *mezonos* for the unmarried sisters — until they marry. The sons technically have no share. Yet if the sons liquidate the estate, the daughters' claim, which lies against real property, cannot follow the proceeds; the sale stands and the daughters' support disappears.

The *rasha arum* here is not the son who sells — Tosafot notes that even the son's action may not be strictly forbidden. What is wrongful is the unsolicited outsider who, having no stake in the outcome, volunteers advice that serves one party at the direct expense of another and subverts the intent of the original estate arrangement.

Abaye adds a variant: *zeh ha-masi eitza limkor b'nechasim k'Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel*. The relevant dispute between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel concerns a gift made with a subsequent gift-over: I give my property to Reuven, and after Reuven, to Shimon. If Reuven sells the property during his lifetime, Rabbi holds that Shimon can reclaim it from the purchasers; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that Shimon receives only what Reuven leaves over — if Reuven sold it, it is gone. The *rasha arum* advises Reuven to sell under the authority of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position, thereby defeating Shimon's expectation under the original gift. Again, it is not the advice itself that is necessarily wrong if solicited by one's client, but the uninvited intervention to harm one party for the benefit of another that constitutes *rasha arum* conduct.
