# Avodah Zarah 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Talmud Torah Framework

### Stadiums & Entertainment

The daf begins at the second-to-last line of 18b, with the words *amar Rav Shmuel*, and we find ourselves in the midst of an extended *agadta* devoted to the importance of *Talmud Torah*. The broader context for this *agadta* is provided by the Mishnah's discussion of stadiums and other forms of public entertainment. A *braita* already cited in the preceding discussion had catalogued the various diversions of the Roman world — stadiums, circuses, theaters, and the *moshav leitzim* — and established that rather than being drawn after these time-wasters, one should dedicate oneself to Torah *yomam valailah*. Torah study also figures as the counterpoint to the entire pagan world of *avodah zarah* more broadly, as seen in the earlier discussion of Rabbi Hanina ben Teradyon, who sacrificed his life rather than cease *mak'hil kehilot berabbim* — gathering communities publicly to teach Torah. It is against this backdrop that the *Gemara* now embarks on a sustained homiletical treatment of the value and proper methodology of *Talmud Torah*.

### Ashrei Ha'ish (Part 1)

The *Gemara* opens with Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani citing Rabbi Yonatan on the verse *ashrei ha'ish asher lo halach ba'atzat resha'im* (Tehillim 1:1) — "praised is the man who did not walk in the counsel of the wicked." The verse had already been interpreted earlier in the context of theaters and stadiums. Here the *Gemara* offers a new *drasha*, locating the verse in the biography of Avraham Avinu.

### Avraham Avinu Example

At the top of 19a, the *Gemara* states: *zeh Avraham Avinu shelo halach ba'atzat anshei Dor Haflagah* — this refers to Avraham, who did not go along with the counsel of the people of the Generation of the Dispersion. Rashi demonstrates from the chronology that Avraham lived at the end of the days of Peleg, when the *Migdal Bavel* is assumed to have been built. Those people were wicked, and their wickedness took the form of a plan — *hava nivneh lanu ir*, "come, let us build ourselves a city" — a counsel, an *eitzah*, in which Avraham took no part.

*Uv'derech chata'im lo amad* — "and in the path of sinners he did not stand" — this corresponds to *shelo amad be'amidat Sedom*, that Avraham did not stand in the presence of Sodom. The people of Sodom are described in the verse as *ra'im ve'chata'im l'Hashem me'od*, and the word *chata'im* appears explicitly there. Avraham, for his part, went in the opposite direction and did not remain near Sodom. Finally, *uv'moshav leitzim lo yashav* — "and in the seat of mockers he did not dwell" — corresponds to *shelo yashav bemoshav anshei Plishtim*, for although Avraham sojourned in the land of the Philistines (*vayagar Avraham b'eretz Plishtim yamim rabbim*), he did not truly dwell among them or mix with the people. The Philistines are identified as *leitzanim*, mockers, as demonstrated from the episode of Shimshon: *vay'hi k'tov libam vayomeru kir'u l'Shimshon v'yisachek lanu* — when their hearts were merry, they called upon Shimshon to make sport for them, a paradigmatic act of frivolity and mockery.

### Gender in Ashrei

Having arrived at the phrase *ashrei ha'ish*, the *Gemara* pauses to ask: *ashrei ha'ish v'lo ashrei ha'isha* — is the praise of "the man" to the exclusion of women? Tosafot notes that this question was not raised by the earlier *drasha* on *ashrei ha'ish asher lo halach*, because the word *ha'ish* there, with the definite article, can be understood generically as "the person." Here, however, the verse reads *ashrei ha'ish yarei et Hashem* — "praised is the man who fears God" — without the same linguistic ambiguity, and the question is therefore pressing.

### Fear of God & Teshuvah

Rav Amram in the name of Rav answers that the verse does not intend to exclude women. Rather, *ashrei mi she'oseh teshuvah k'shehu ish* — praised is the one who does *teshuvah* while he or she is still at the strength of their vigor. The word *ish*, "man," evokes strength and power: do *teshuvah* when you still have many years ahead of you, when the *yetzer hara* is still active and the challenge is genuine — not when desire has already faded and the path of virtue has become easy by default. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi adds a parallel formulation: *ashrei mi shemitgaber al yitzro k'ish* — praised is the one who overpowers their *yetzer hara* like a man, with real force, conquering the desires that pull one away from the right path. The theme of ongoing struggle with the *yetzer* remains central.

## Torah from the Heart

### Mitzvot for Their Own Sa…

The *Gemara* turns to the end of the verse: *b'mitzvotav chafetz me'od* — in His *mitzvot* he desires greatly. Rabbi Elazar interprets: *b'mitzvotav v'lo b'sechar mitzvotav* — the desire should be for the *mitzvot* themselves, not for their reward. One should not perform *mitzvot* with an eye toward compensation. The *Gemara* connects this to the teaching in Pirkei Avot: *al tihyu k'avadim hameshamshim et harav al m'nat l'kabel pras* — do not be like servants who serve the master for the sake of receiving a reward; rather, serve even without anticipation of reward, because it is the right thing to do, and in the end the good will follow.

### Learning Where Heart Des…

The *Gemara* returns to the opening verse and its continuation: *ki im b'torat Hashem cheftzo* — "for in the Torah of God is his desire." In contrast to the frivolous pursuits catalogued by the Mishnah, the devoted person turns his deepest longing toward Torah. From this phrase, Rabbi — Rebbi — derives the principle: *ein adam lomed Torah ela mimakom shelibo chafetz* — a person will truly learn Torah only from the place toward which his heart is drawn.

This is a profound pedagogical insight. One can compel someone to sit in front of a text that does not speak to them, but the learning will not be deep. The analogy to a scholar-in-residence invited to speak on any subject illustrates the point well: the most effective presentation is the one the speaker is most passionate about at that moment. *Ein adam lomed Torah ela mimakom shelibo chafetz* — the desire and the Torah must coincide, and when they do, real immersion becomes possible.

### Levi & R. Shimon Story

A story illustrates this principle directly. Levi and Rabbi Shimon, the son of Rebbi, were sitting before Rebbi and learning, going through a book of Tanach together. When they completed a *sefer*, the question arose of what to study next. Levi proposed *Mishlei*; Rabbi Shimon proposed *Tehillim*. Rabbi Shimon's preference prevailed — perhaps because of his relationship to Rebbi, or perhaps simply because Rebbi sided with him — and *Tehillim* was taken up. When they arrived at the second verse of *Tehillim* — *ki im b'torat Hashem cheftzo* — Rebbi expounded: *ein adam lomed Torah ela mimakom shelibo chafetz*. Upon hearing this, Levi said: *Rabbi natata lanu reshut la'amod* — Rebbi, you have given me permission to leave. If a person can only truly learn in the place that draws him, and *Tehillim* was not his preference, then he is released — not from the obligation of Torah, but from this particular forced context — and he should go learn what truly speaks to him.

### Avdimi: God's Fulfillment

Rav Avdimi bar Chama teaches: *kol ha'osek baTorah haKadosh Baruch Hu oseh lo chafatzav* — whoever occupies himself with Torah, the Holy One, blessed be He, fulfills his desires. If one devotes oneself to *Torat Hashem*, then *cheftzo* — one's desires — will be satisfied. The verse is thus read bidirectionally: desire in Torah leads to God's granting of one's other desires as well. This stands alongside the earlier *drasha*, which reads *cheftzo* as the location — learn in the place you desire — creating two complementary teachings drawn from the same phrase.

### Rava's Advice & Ownership

Rava frames Rebbi's principle as practical advice rather than strict limitation: *le'olam yilmad adam Torah bimkom shelibo chafetz* — one should always learn Torah in the place where his heart desires. The slight shift in language — *bimkom* rather than *mimakom* — may or may not carry independent significance, but the spirit is the same: direct yourself toward what draws you.

Rava then adds a teaching about the nature of Torah ownership. *Batchila nikra al shem haKadosh Baruch Hu* — at the beginning, the Torah one studies is called by God's name; it is external, a divine inheritance one is working to receive. *Velibasof nikra al shemo* — but in the end, it is called by one's own name. The proof-text: the verse begins *b'torat Hashem cheftzo* — God's Torah is his desire — and ends *uvetorato yehgeh yomam valayla* — and *in his Torah* he meditates day and night. The pronoun shifts from God to the person: once one devotes oneself fully, internalizes, and brings one's own thought and creative energy to the learning, the Torah becomes genuinely one's own. This principle carries *halachic* implications as well — it is the basis upon which a *rav* may be *mohel al kevodo* in certain contexts, since the Torah he represents is, to a real degree, his own.

## Internalizing Torah

### Learning Before Analyzing

Rava teaches: *le'olam ligros inish ve'achar kach yehege* — a person should first learn the text and only afterward contemplate and analyze it. This might seem to simply describe what Torah study always is, but it reflects the realities of an oral culture. In a world before printed books, the process of learning required first memorizing the material — the content of the Mishnah, the statements of the *Amoraim*, the *pesukim* of the Torah — and only subsequently subjecting that material to analysis. One must acquire the raw information before the mind can engage it critically. Moreover, the capacity for memorization is strongest in youth, and attempting analysis before the textual foundation is in place yields impoverished results. The proof-text makes this explicit: the verse first says *b'torat Hashem* — it is God's Torah, something external being received — and only then *uvetorato yehege* — he meditates in his own Torah. There are two stages: acquisition first, then creative engagement.

### Girsa & Breaking Wheat

Rava adds a further teaching: *le'olam ligros inish ve'af agav demishkach*, and *ve'af agav d'lo yada mai ka'amar* — one should always be *gores*, even if one fears forgetting, and even if one does not fully understand what one is learning. The word *gores* is related to *geres carmel*, the breaking of a grain kernel — not grinding it fine, but beginning to crack it open. The imagery of wheat is a recurring metaphor for Torah study: the *Gemara* elsewhere observes *hacol tzrichim lemarei chitya*, everyone needs the master of wheat — you must have the grain before you can grind it. So too, *girsa* — the correct text, the raw material of tradition — must be absorbed before analysis can begin. Even if the material is not yet fully understood, or if one's teacher does not explain it fully at that stage, it enters the mind and shapes understanding at a level that is not always consciously accessible. The verse cited: *garsa nafshi leta'ava* — my soul has *gores*-ed with desire — uses the language of *gersa ktiv*, it is written as "broken," not *tachna*, not "ground." The initial stage is the cracking open, and the grinding — the deep analysis — comes later.

### Wings to Chair & Heights

Rava raises a contradiction between two verses. One verse speaks of *al gappei meromei karet* — upon the wings of the heights of the city; another, later in Mishlei 9, speaks of *al kisei meromei karet* — upon the chair of the heights. The resolution: *batchila al gappei* — at the beginning of one's learning, one is as if on wings, unsteady, uncertain, lacking solid footing. *Ulesof al kisei* — but in the end, the learning becomes firm and settled, like one seated in a chair.

A second contradiction: one verse places wisdom *berosh meromim* — at the top of the high places, an isolated position — while another says *alei derech*, by the way, accessible and public. The resolution: *batchila berosh meromim* — when one begins, the learning is necessarily solitary; one must absorb the material, perhaps sitting in a large *yeshiva*, yet fundamentally alone in the work of memorization and internalization. *Ulesof alei derech* — but ultimately one emerges onto the path, equipped to teach the many.

### Cistern to Well

Ulla raises a parallel contradiction. One verse says *shetei mayim miborcha* — drink water from your *bor*, your cistern, which collects only rainwater from outside. Another verse, the continuation of the same passage, says *venozlim mitoch be'erecha* — and flowing water from your *be'er*, your well, which draws fresh water from underground. Water, of course, is the classic metaphor for Torah. The resolution: *batchila shetei miborcha* — at the beginning one drinks from the cistern; one is gathering and storing the teachings of others, not yet producing original Torah. *Ulesof venozlim mitoch be'erecha* — but in the end, the water flows fresh from within, as one's own Torah emerges.

### Bundles vs. Handfuls

Rava in the name of Rav Sechora in the name of Rav Huna asks what is meant by the verse *hon mehevel yimat vekovetz al yad yarbe* — wealth acquired through emptiness will diminish, but one who gathers little by little will increase. The *drasha* reads *mehevel* as *chavilot chavilot* — bundles upon bundles. If one makes one's Torah like bundles — piling on more and more, always moving to the next *daf*, the next tractate, accumulating material at speed without pausing to consolidate — *mitma'et*, it will diminish; one cannot hold on to it. But *vekovetz al yad yarbe* — if one gathers handful by handful, learning a little, reviewing it, ensuring comprehension, then moving to the next portion, reviewing again — each piece builds on the last, and in the end one truly possesses a great deal of Torah.

Rava himself acknowledges the irony: *yadei rabanan leha milta ve'avri ala* — Torah scholars know this principle and regularly violate it. It is always more exciting to move forward than to review what has already been learned. A practical habit that exemplifies the principle: upon completing a *daf* or *amud*, pausing to identify the two or three main points one wishes to retain makes an enormous difference in internalization.

### Nachman's Testimony

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers personal testimony: *ana avidta* — I followed Rava's method, learning gradually and reviewing, *ve'kayem biyadi* — and in the end it remained established in my hand. These teachings about the process of learning Torah — how to absorb it, how it becomes yours, and how to retain it — form a remarkable sequence of practical wisdom embedded in this *agadta*.

## Hunter Analogy

### Skillful vs. Deceitful

Rav Shizvi in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah cites the verse *lo yachroch rmiyah tzeido* — the deceitful one will not roast his game. The word *yachroch*, to char or roast, is read here as a play on *yichyeh velo ya'arich yamim* — the *tziyad haramai*, the deceitful hunter, will not live long or have length of days. Rashi identifies the deceitful hunter as one who goes around superficially quoting things, performing the appearance of Torah without genuine dedication. *Vachachamim omrim tziyad haramai yachroch* — but the sages say: shall the skillful hunter roast his game? Yes — the one who hunts his Torah with craft and care, takes hold of what he has caught, and then processes it — reviews it, owns it — will indeed succeed. This reading continues the imagery developed throughout this section: the game that one catches but does not secure is lost.

### Dimi's Bird Parable

When Rav Dimi came from the Land of Israel, he offered a parable: it is like a person hunting birds — *im meshaber knafeha harishonah kulam mitkayemet biyado* — if you break the wing of the first one, all the birds you subsequently catch will remain in your hand as well. The logic may be that once the first bird is secured and cannot escape, one has established the habit of retention, and everything that follows is likewise retained. *Ve'im lav ein mitkayemet biyado* — but if not, if one allows the first catch to fly away — if the learning goes in one ear and out the other — nothing will stay. The image reinforces the central message of this entire section: initial mastery and consolidation are the precondition for accumulating Torah.

## Tree by Waters

### Replanted Tree

The *Gemara* returns to the opening verse of Tehillim 1: *vehayah ke'etz shatul al palgei mayim* — he will be like a tree planted beside streams of water. The school of Rabbi Yannai notes the precise word: *ke'etz shatul* — like a tree that has been *replanted*, transplanted from one location to another — *velo ke'etz natua*, not like a tree that was planted once and remains forever in a single place.

### Multiple Teachers

The implication: *kol halomed Torah merav echad eino ro'eh siman bracha le'olam* — whoever learns all his Torah from a single rabbi will never see a *siman bracha*. By remaining rooted in one place, one is not stimulated by different waters, different perspectives, different ways of thinking. One may accumulate a great deal of content, but without the *bracha* of growth and expansion that comes from exposure to multiple streams of Torah.

### Chisda's Dilemma

Rav Chisda illustrates the personal cost of this truth. He gathered his students and said: *ba'ina de'eima lachon milta mistafina dishvakiton li ve'azliton* — I want to tell you something, but I am afraid that as soon as I do, you will abandon me and leave. He told them nonetheless — and they did exactly what he feared, departing immediately to study before Rava.

### Svara vs. Gmara

Rava — and the *Gemara* suggests it may be Rava who addresses the departed students — clarifies the principle: *hanei milei svara, aval gmara merav echad adif* — this applies to *svara*, to analytical reasoning and conceptual thinking, which benefits from exposure to multiple teachers and multiple frameworks. But when it comes to *gmara* — the transmission of the received text — it is better to have one rabbi. The implication may be that Rava was directing the students back to Rav Chisda for the foundational textual learning, with the freedom to seek multiple teachers for analysis.

### Terminology Consistency

At the top of 19b, the reason for maintaining a single teacher for textual learning is explained: *ki heichi d'lo liflog lishnei* — in order not to create confusion among different terminologies. Different teachers use different technical vocabularies for the same concepts — what one calls *metaltelin* and *karka*, another may call *nechasim bnei chorin* and *nechasim meshubadim*. If one absorbs the same material through multiple terminological frameworks simultaneously, the conceptual categories will blur, and one will mistake terminology for substance. The textual foundation — the *girsa* discussed earlier — must be laid with consistent framing, and only then can analysis be pursued from multiple vantage points.

## Three-Part Study

### Tanchum's Division

The phrase *palgei mayim* — streams of water in the plural — is taken to suggest that one's Torah should be divided into multiple channels. Rav Tanchum bar Chanina teaches: *le'olam yeshalesh adam shnotav* — a person should always divide his years into three portions: *shlish beMikra, shlish beMishna, shlish beTalmud* — a third in Scripture, a third in Mishnah, and a third in Talmud, meaning analysis. The foundational texts and the analytical tradition receive equal weight.

### Lifespan Uncertainty

The *Gemara* immediately raises an objection: *mi yada inish kama chayei* — how can one divide one's years when one does not know one's lifespan? If the division is chronological — Scripture from birth to age twenty, Mishnah from twenty to forty — one cannot know in advance how many years remain for each phase.

### Weekly Division

*Dechi ka'amrinan beyomei* — the teaching must refer to one's days, not one's years. Rashi interprets this as a weekly division: two days for Scripture, two days for Mishnah, two days for Talmud, with Shabbat devoted to review of all three.

### Bavli's Inclusion

Tosafot asks: how does one even know one will survive the week? The pointed humor aside, Tosafot's more substantive answer is that the structure of the daily liturgy already implements this division. The *psukei hatamid*, the verses recited in the morning service, fulfill the portion of Scripture; the Mishnah of *Eizehu Mekoman* fulfills the Mishnah portion; and the *Braita deRabbi Yishmael* — the thirteen hermeneutical principles — represents the beginning of *Talmud*, of analysis. Even those who do not formally divide their learning already encounter all three each day. For those who devote themselves to learning *gemara* exclusively, Rabbeinu Tam argues further: *anu she'oskim beTalmud Bavli* — we who occupy ourselves with the Babylonian Talmud need not make a strict third-by-third division, *ki hu balul beMikra uv'Mishna uv'Talmud* — because the Bavli itself is interwoven with Scripture and Mishnah. Studying the Bavli keeps one in conversation with all three dimensions, even if not in equal proportions.

### Rambam's Stages

Rambam spells out a practical scenario. He describes a person with a profession who works three hours a day, leaving nine hours for Torah: three hours of Scripture, three hours of Mishnah, three hours of Talmud. Rambam's scenario presupposes, notably, that the professional devotes three hours to work and nine to Torah — a ratio worth appreciating in itself. But Rambam also emphasizes that this three-part division applies only to the *beginning* of one's learning, when the goal is to acquire the core texts and establish foundations. Once one reaches a more mature stage, the entire effort should shift toward the higher level of intellectual analysis. Tosafot's answer and Rambam's answer are thus complementary rather than contradictory: Rambam addresses the initial stage of formation, while Tosafot addresses the ongoing reality of mature learning. Both the continued engagement with foundational texts and the movement toward higher analysis have their place within a complete Torah life.

## Fruit in Season

### Fruit & Leaves

The *Gemara* continues through the verse: *asher piryo yitein be'ito* — who gives his fruit in its season — and the next phrase, *ve'alehu lo yibol* — and his leaves will not wither. Rava notes that the verse conditions the promise: *im piryo yitein be'ito, ve'alehu lo yibol* — if the fruit is given in its season, then the leaves will not wither. But if not — *ve'im lav* — the subsequent verse applies: *lo chen ha'reshaim ki im ka'motz asher tidfenu ruach* — not so the wicked, for they are like chaff that the wind blows away.

### Unripe vs. Refusing

Rava interprets this through the lens of *hora'ah* — the giving of *psak halacha*. The reference to *elu melamed u'melamed* — both the one who is being taught and the one who teaches — suggests that the "fruit" is the practical ruling, the application of Torah to life. Two categories of failure are identified, and they receive opposite condemnations drawn from the same cluster of verses.

Rav Abba in the name of Rav Huna in the name of Rav cites the verse: *ki rabbim chalalim hipila ve'atzumim kol harugeha* — for many slain has she cast down, and great are all those she has killed. The first clause — *rabbim chalalim hipila* — refers to a student who has not yet reached the level of *hora'ah* yet rules anyway. The word *hipila* is connected to *nefel*, an unformed fetus; such a student is spiritually unripe, not yet fit to issue rulings, and by doing so causes many to sin — ruling too leniently, or too stringently, or simply incorrectly. *Rabbim chalalim hipila* — many casualties from the premature *posek*.

The second clause — *ve'atzumim kol harugeha* — is read differently. *Atzumim*, enormous, is connected to *le'otzeim*, to hold back or shut. This refers to the student who has reached the level of *hora'ah* and yet refuses to rule. By withholding his *psak* from those who need it, he too causes great damage — enormous are all those "slain" by his reticence.

### Age Forty

The *Gemara* asks: *ve'ad kama* — until what age should one refrain from issuing rulings? The answer: until forty years of age.

### Rava's Exception

But a challenge immediately follows: did not Rava himself give *psak halacha*, and did Rava not die at the age of forty? *Hatam bishavin* — that was a case of equivalence, or indeed of superiority: when there is no one at a greater level than you, one need not wait for the age of forty. But where there are those who surpass you in learning and authority, one should defer until that threshold is reached.

## Leaves Not Wither

### Mundane Wisdom

Rav Acha bar Adda — with variant attributions running through Rav Acha bar Abba and Rav Hamnuna — cites Rav on the phrase *ve'alehu lo yibol*, "and his leaves will not wither": *she'afilu sichat chullin shel talmidei chachamim tzricha talmud* — even the mundane conversation of Torah scholars requires study and attention. There is wisdom embedded in how a *talmid chacham* speaks, carries himself, and interacts with others that goes beyond the explicit content of his teaching. The *Gemara* elsewhere formulates this as *gadol shimusha yoter milimmuda* — attending upon a Torah scholar teaches more than formal study alone. One observes the tone, the word choice, the manner — and these are themselves a form of Torah. Even the leaves — not the fruit — are not to be dismissed; they too carry content and significance.

## All Success

### Tripled Promise

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states: *davar zeh katuv baTorah veshunuy baNevi'im umeshulash baKetuvim* — this matter is written in the Torah, repeated in the Prophets, and said a third time in the Writings. What matter? *Kol ha'osek baTorah* — whoever occupies himself with Torah — *drachav matzlichin* (following the *Gra*'s emendation, which fits the verses better than the manuscript reading of *nechasav matzlichin*) — his paths succeed.

### Written: Covenant

The verse in the Torah: *ushmartem et divrei habrit hazot va'asitem otam lema'an taskilu et kol asher ta'asun* — you shall observe the words of this covenant and do them, in order that you may act wisely in all that you do. Success in all endeavors flows from faithfulness to the covenant and its teachings.

### Nevi'im: Meditation

The verse in the Prophets — in Yehoshua — *lo yamush sefer haTorah hazeh mipicha vehagita bo yomam valailah lema'an tishmor la'asot kechol hakatuv bo ki az tatzliach et drachecha ve'az taskil* — the book of Torah shall not depart from your lips, and you shall meditate in it day and night, in order to observe all that is written in it, for then your paths will be successful and you will act wisely. The word *tatzliach*, succeed, appears explicitly, and the *Gra*'s emendation of the *girsa* to *drachav matzlichin* aligns precisely with *tatzliach et drachecha*.

### Ketuvim: Tree

The verse in the Writings is the very passage of Tehillim 1 that the *Gemara* has been expounding throughout: *ki im b'torat Hashem cheftzo uvtorato yehgeh yomam valailah, vehayah ke'etz shatul al palgei mayim asher piryo yiten be'ito ve'alehu lo yibol vechol asher ya'aseh yatzliach* — for in God's Torah is his desire, and in his Torah he meditates day and night; he shall be like a tree replanted beside streams of water, giving its fruit in its season, whose leaves do not wither, and all that he does will succeed. The entire *agadta* on *Talmud Torah* closes with this verse, the promise of success threaded through Torah, Prophets, and Writings alike.

## Alexandri's Proclamation

### Who Wants Life?

Rabbi Alexandri would go to a town and make a public proclamation: *man ba'ei chayei? man ba'ei chayei?* — who wants life? Who wants life? The crowd gathered: *kanaf ve'atu kulei alma laggabeih* — everyone came to him. *Amru leih hav lan chayei* — give us life! He replied by citing the verse: *mi ha'ish hachafetz chayim ohev yamim lir'ot tov* — who is the person who desires life, who loves days, to see good? This is why the Chafetz Chayim took his name from this verse.

### Guard Your Speech

*Netzor leshoncha mera usfatecha middaber mirma* — guard your tongue from evil and your lips from speaking deceit. Do not speak *lashon hara*. This is the secret of life.

### Evil to Good

But Rabbi Alexandri continues with the next verse. One might hear *netzor leshoncha mera* and conclude: I have watched my tongue, I speak no evil — *eilech ve'etgareh beshinah*, let me now go and sleep. The abstention from evil, the avoidance of harm, might seem sufficient. It is not. *Talmud lomar sur mera va'asei tov bakesh shalom verodfehu* — the very next verse adds: turn from evil *and do good*, seek peace and pursue it. Negative commandments, the refraining from transgression, are not enough in isolation. Positive action is required.

### Torah as Good

And what is that good? *Ve'ein tov ela Torah* — there is no good but Torah. The proof-text: *ki lekach tov natati lachem Torati al ta'azovu* — for I have given you a good teaching, My Torah; do not forsake it. This brings the entire extended *agadta* on *Talmud Torah* to its culmination. All the teachings about desire and heart, about *girsa* and analysis, about multiple teachers and the right season for *hora'ah*, about the speech of Torah scholars and the universal promise of success — all of it flows from and returns to the identification of Torah as the supreme good, the elixir of life itself.

## Idol Niche Mishna

### Stop at Niche

The *Gemara* returns to the Mishnah. The Mishnah had stated that one may assist in the construction of a bathhouse or other building for idolaters, but *higia lekippa* — when one reaches the niche, the *kippa*, the place where they set up the idol — one must stop. One cannot participate in constructing the actual site of idolatrous worship.

### Payment Permissible

Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Yochanan rules: *im bana, scharo mutar* — if a person did build it in violation of this prohibition and thereby transgressed — creating at minimum a problem of *mesaye'a lifnei iver* at the rabbinic level — the payment one received is nonetheless *mutar*, permitted. One may keep the fee.

---

## Niche as Tashmish

### Idol vs. Tashmish

The *Gemara* asks: this ruling is obvious — why would it even occur to us that the payment should be forbidden? The niche is a *tashmish*, an appurtenance of the idol, not the idol itself.

### Both Amoraim Agree

*Meshamshei avodat kochavim hem* — such appurtenances are indeed subject to specific rules. *Umeshamshei avodat kochavim bein le-Rabbi Yishmael bein le-Rabbi Akiva einam asurin ad she-ye'avedu* — the appurtenances of an idol are forbidden, according to both Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva, only once they have actually been used in the context of idol worship. Mere designation or construction of an appurtenance does not render it *asur behana'ah*. Since the niche has not yet had an idol placed upon it, it has not yet been used in worship and is therefore not yet forbidden in benefit. Consequently, there is no reason for the payment to be forbidden, and the ruling that it is *mutar* seems self-evident.

It is worth pausing here on a conceptual distinction embedded in the *Gemara*'s reasoning. The direct, classical sense of *issur hana'ah* — prohibition of benefit — covers physical enjoyment (eating, smelling, incorporating into one's body) and monetary benefit (selling and taking the proceeds). The benefit derived from having performed labor associated with a forbidden object is somewhat distinct in category. Yet the *Gemara* treats the laborer's fee as governed by the same principle: if the object is not *asur behana'ah*, the payment for work involving it is likewise permitted. Similarly, one might have expected a *rabbinic* prohibition on the payment even where the classical *issur hana'ah* does not apply — analogous to the rabbinic rule that proceeds from business conducted on an idolater's market day (*yom hashuk*) must be discarded. The *Gemara*'s assumption appears to be that absent an explicit rabbinic extension of the prohibition, the default rule follows the standard categories of *issur hana'ah*.

### Jew vs. Non-Jew

Rabbi Yirmiyah proposes that the novelty of Rabbi Elazar's ruling lies elsewhere entirely: not in the case of a *tashmish*, but in the case of the idol itself — *avodat kochavim atzmah*. Even if one actually made the idol, Rabbi Elazar rules the payment is permitted. This is striking. The *Gemara* engages with the debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva regarding when an idol becomes forbidden. The key distinction is between an idol made for or by a Jew and one made for or by a non-Jew. An idol *shel Yisrael* — made on behalf of a Jew, or in Rashi's reading, owned temporarily by a Jew (perhaps a merchant intending to sell it) — is *asura mi-yad*, forbidden immediately upon its creation. An idol *shel oved kochavim* — made for and by a non-Jew — according to the opposing view is *ad she-te'aved*, forbidden only once actually worshipped.

According to the view that a non-Jew's idol is forbidden only upon worship, the ruling that the payment is permitted is indeed obvious: even the idol itself is not yet *asur behana'ah* at the moment of its construction, and therefore the laborer's payment is unproblematically permitted. But according to the view that even a non-Jew's idol is forbidden immediately upon completion — *asura mi-yad* — the ruling requires explanation. At the moment the idol is complete, it becomes *asur behana'ah*, and at that very moment the laborer's work is finished and his fee is earned. Why should the payment be permitted?

## Last Hammer Blow

### Makkesh Acharon

Rava bar Ula resolves the difficulty: *lo nitzrecha ela bemakkesh acharon* — the ruling is necessary specifically with regard to the last hammer blow. The idol does not become an idol — does not become *asur behana'ah* — until *gemar melachah*, until the work is complete. And *gemar melachah* is the very last hammer blow or tap that completes the object.

### Gemar Melachah

An idol in the process of being fashioned is not yet an idol. Whatever prohibitions may attach to the completed object do not apply until that final act of completion.

### Less Than Pruta

*Makkesh acharon leit beih shaveh prutah* — the last hammer blow is not worth a *prutah*. A craftsman working on an idol throughout the week is accumulating wages continuously. By the time he delivers the final tap, he has already earned essentially the entire fee. That final moment — the only moment at which the idol is actually *asur behana'ah* — corresponds to a fraction of a *prutah* of his total compensation. Since that amount is legally negligible, it is as though nothing of the forbidden object's value contaminated his payment. The bulk of his wages were earned before the prohibition came into effect.

### Wages Throughout

The *Gemara* adds: *ve'alma kasavar yeshnah lisechirut mitechilah ve'ad sof* — this analysis reveals an underlying principle about the nature of wages. Conceptually, a hired worker earns his fee continuously throughout the performance of his work, not only at the moment of its completion. Even if payment is made only upon delivery, the entitlement to that payment accrues progressively from the beginning of the labor to its end. It is therefore not the case that the laborer earned nothing for ninety-nine percent of his work and then earned everything at the moment of completion. He earned throughout — and throughout, the object was not yet *asur behana'ah*. The only moment it was forbidden was the final tap, and that moment was worth less than a *prutah*. Hence the fee is permitted in its entirety.

It is worth noting, in closing, how striking the *Gemara*'s silence is regarding any rabbinic prohibition on building idols for non-Jews. The contrast with the *Gemara*'s earlier discussion of business on idolaters' market days — where rabbinic enactments rendered proceeds forbidden — is marked. Perhaps the explanation lies in prevalence: market-day commerce with idolaters was a common enough phenomenon to demand serious rabbinic deterrence, whereas Jews constructing idols for non-Jews was a sufficiently rare scenario that the Sages left it to be governed by the underlying law of *issur hana'ah* alone.
