# Sanhedrin 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gonev Nefesh Intro

### Kidnapping Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹנֵב נֶפֶשׁ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיַּכְנִיסֶנּוּ לִרְשׁוּתוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיַּכְנִיסֶנּוּ לִרְשׁוּתוֹ וְיִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִתְעַמֵּר בּוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who abducts a Jewish person is not liable** to be executed **unless he brings** the abductee **into his domain. Rabbi Yehuda says:** He is not liable **unless he brings him into his domain and exploits him, as it is stated:** "If a man shall be found abducting a person of his brethren from the children of Israel, **and he exploited him and sold him,** then that abductor shall die" (Deuteronomy 24:7). The phrase "exploited him" indicates using him for labor.

The Mishnah on 85b concludes the laws of one who strikes a parent and transitions to the next capital offense under the category of *chenek*: *hagonev nefesh miYisrael*, the kidnapping of a fellow Jew. When we hear the word "kidnapping" today, our minds go immediately to ransom — holding someone captive in exchange for payment. But in the context of the *pesukim*, kidnapping was fundamentally about stealing a person to sell them as a slave.

### Yosef/Slave Trade

The paradigmatic case in the Torah is the story of Yosef. The brothers seized him and sold him off as a slave — and it is precisely this act that is commemorated with such anguish in the *Elleh Ezkerah* prayer. The historical slave trade operated in much the same way: captives were taken — sometimes as a byproduct of war, sometimes through deliberate raids — and then sold. The *gonev nefesh* prohibition addresses precisely this phenomenon.

### Tikkun Olam Limit

A related issue appears in Gittin, where one of the enactments made *mipnei tikkun olam* is that one may not pay an exorbitant sum to redeem a captive — the principle being that doing so only encourages further kidnapping, much as negotiating with terrorists emboldens further hostage-taking. The limit set is *yoter mikedei demaihen* — one may not pay more than the captive's standard market value. What is that standard? In the ancient world, it was the slave market price. If someone demands a ransom far beyond that, the community is forbidden to pay it, lest the practice be incentivized. Tosafot in Gittin raises various complications — what if it is a close family member, and so on — but the baseline principle is clear: the standard redemption price serves as the ceiling.

The *pasuk* that governs the capital offense reads: *ki yimatze ish gonev nefesh me'achiv mibnei Yisrael vehit'amer bo umacharo umet haganav hahu* — if a man is found having stolen a fellow Israelite, having made use of him, and having sold him, that kidnapper shall be put to death. Unstated execution (*stam mita*) means *chenek*, strangulation. The offense thus has three components: stealing, using, and selling — with the use serving as a condition rather than as the primary act. The core transgression is stealing and selling, with the *vehit'amer bo* functioning as contextual framing for what follows.

## Mishnah Requirements

### Reshut Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹנֵב נֶפֶשׁ מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיַּכְנִיסֶנּוּ לִרְשׁוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who abducts a Jewish person is not liable** to be executed **unless he brings** the abductee **into his domain.**

The Mishnah states: *hagonev nefesh miYisrael eino chayav ad sheyachnisenu lirshuto* — one is not liable for kidnapping until one has brought the victim into one's own domain. This parallels the general law of *gneivah*, which requires some act of *kinyan* — an act demonstrating legal control — before liability attaches. However, there is a significant conceptual tension here, since one cannot technically perform a *kinyan* on a free person. Accordingly, some *Rishonim* suggest that the standard act of *hagbahah* — lifting the object — may be insufficient here. The *pasuk* says *gonev nefesh mibnei Yisrael*, which may imply a more substantive taking: literally bringing the person under one's control by moving them into one's domain. The Mishnah's formulation — *ad sheyachnisenu lirshuto* — supports this reading.

### Rabbi Yehuda Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיַּכְנִיסֶנּוּ לִרְשׁוּתוֹ וְיִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִתְעַמֵּר בּוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** He is not liable **unless he brings him into his domain and exploits him, as it is stated:** "If a man shall be found abducting a person of his brethren from the children of Israel, **and he exploited him and sold him,** then that abductor shall die" (Deuteronomy 24:7).

*Rabbi Yehuda omer: ad sheyachnisenu lirshuto veyishtamesh bo* — Rabbi Yehuda adds a further requirement: not only must the kidnapper bring the person into his domain, he must also make use of the victim. Rabbi Yehuda derives this from the *pasuk* itself: *vehit'amer bo umacharo* — he made use of him and then sold him. According to Rabbi Yehuda, this use must constitute at least a *shaveh prutah*'s worth. The *tanna kamma*, by contrast, requires some use but not necessarily to that minimum monetary threshold — even a use worth less than a *prutah* suffices. The *Rambam* rules in accordance with the *tanna kamma*.

### Own Son Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגּוֹנֵב אֶת בְּנוֹ, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who abducts his** own **son** and sells him, **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, deems** him **liable, and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.**

The Mishnah addresses a further case: *hagonev et beno* — one who kidnaps his own son. Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka holds one liable (*mechayev*), while the *chachamim* exempt (*potrin*). The reasoning will be developed in the Gemara.

### Half-Slave Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּנַב מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **one abducted one who is a half-slave half-freeman,** i.e., a Canaanite slave who belonged to two owners and was emancipated by one of them, **Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **liable, and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.**

The Mishnah then turns to the case of *ganav mi shechetzyo eved vechetzyo ben chorin* — kidnapping a person who is half-slave and half-free, such as someone owned by two partners where one partner has freed his share. Rabbi Yehuda holds one liable; the *chachamim* exempt. This case is notable because the victim was not fully free to begin with, yet Rabbi Yehuda maintains that stealing and selling such a person is sufficient to incur capital liability.

One might ask whether the *purchaser* of the kidnapped person bears liability as well. The purchaser does not incur the death penalty, since that requires both stealing *and* selling, and the buyer has done neither. If, however, the buyer knows the person is free and takes possession knowingly, he would be transgressing the prohibition of stealing — participating in the enslavement of a free person — though he would not be liable for having sold him. There are thus two distinct prohibitions at play, which will be elaborated in the Gemara.

## Eimur Requirement

### Eimur Value Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְתַנָּא קַמָּא לָא בָּעֵי עִימּוּר? אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: עִימּוּר פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And does the first *tanna* not require exploitation** as a condition for liability? The Torah states it explicitly in the verse in Deuteronomy. **Rabbi Aḥa, son of Rava, says:** The difference **between them** is in a case of **exploitation** worth **less than the value of one *peruta*.** The first *tanna* holds that one is liable for any exploitation and there is no minimum value in order to establish liability. Rabbi Yehuda holds that one is liable only if one derives benefit equal to at least one *peruta* from his exploitation.

The Gemara opens by asking: *vtanna kamma lo baey imur?* — how can the *tanna kamma* not require the use (*eimur*) of the victim, when it is explicit in the *pasuk*? The Gemara answers in the name of Rabbi Acha the son of Rava: the debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda concerns the *minimum* value of the use. The *tanna kamma* holds that even use worth less than a *shaveh prutah* suffices — any degree of use will do. Rabbi Yehuda requires use worth at least a *prutah*. Both agree the use requirement exists; they disagree on its threshold.

### Eimur Logic

This raises a deeper question: what is the conceptual purpose of the *eimur* requirement? Why should making use of the victim matter, given that the central prohibitions are stealing and selling?

The answer, it seems, is that the act of use creates the context of enslavement. Simply picking someone up and immediately transacting a sale might technically involve stealing and selling, but it does not fully transform a free person into a slave in the eyes of the perpetrator. When the kidnapper makes use of the victim — treats them as one treats a slave — he thereby defines the victim as a slave and himself as the one who has effected that transformation. The *eimur* is not about extracting monetary value from the victim; it is about creating the enslaving context. This explains why even a use worth less than a *prutah* satisfies the *tanna kamma*: what matters is not the monetary significance of the use but its qualitative character — that the kidnapper has treated this person as a slave. The *eimur*, on this reading, is what ensures that the kidnapper is not merely an incidental participant in a transaction but the one who has concretely enslaved this person.

### Pregnant Woman Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: גְּנָבוֹ וּמְכָרוֹ יָשֵׁן, מַהוּ? מָכַר אִשָּׁה לְעוּבָּרָהּ, מַהוּ? יֵשׁ דֶּרֶךְ עִימּוּר בְּכָךְ, אוֹ אֵין דֶּרֶךְ עִימּוּר בְּכָךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If **one abducted** another **and sold him** while he was **asleep, what is** the *halakha*? If one abducted and **sold** a pregnant **woman** solely **for** benefit from **her fetus, what is** the *halakha*? **Is that a manner of exploitation** for which one is liable to be executed **or is that not a manner of exploitation** for which one is liable to be executed?

Rabbi Yirmiyah poses a challenging question: *ganavo umacharo yashan* — what if he stole and sold someone who was asleep? And: *machar isha le'ubarrah mahu* — what if a man steals a pregnant woman and sells her specifically for the sake of her fetus, retaining ownership of the woman herself but conveying the fetus to the buyer?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּלֵיכָּא עִימּוּר כְּלָל? לָא צְרִיכָא: יָשֵׁן – דִּזְגָא עֲלֵיהּ, אִשָּׁה – דְּאוֹקְמַהּ בְּאַפֵּי זִיקָא. דֶּרֶךְ עִימּוּר בְּכָךְ אוֹ אֵין דֶּרֶךְ עִימּוּר בְּכָךְ? מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the dilemmas raised by Rabbi Yirmeya: **But let him derive** that the abductor is not liable, **as there is no exploitation at all** of a sleeping individual or a fetus. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to raise the dilemma only with regard to the **sleeping** individual in a case **where one reclines on him,** and with regard to the pregnant **woman where one stands her in the face of the wind** to protect himself from the wind. In those cases, **is that a manner of exploitation** for which one is liable to be executed **or is that not a manner of exploitation** for which one is liable to be executed? **What** is the *halakha*? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand [*teiku*]** unresolved.

These cases probe the nature of the *eimur* requirement. The fetus does not yet have an independent legal identity; the *pasuk*'s requirement that a born person be stolen and sold (*gonev nefesh me'achav mibnei Yisrael*) applies to those already born. So the woman herself is the one being stolen, but she was sold only insofar as her fetus was conveyed. Is that a valid *mechira* of the woman? And is the use of a sleeping person — who is entirely unaware of what is happening to them — a valid *eimur*?

The Gemara clarifies that the question is not whether the person can be used in some technical sense. For a sleeping person: *yashen dizaga aleih* — one lies on the sleeping person, using him as a cushion or rest. For the pregnant woman: *de'ukma be'apei zika* — one stands behind her as a windbreak, and because she is pregnant she provides greater protection from the wind, with the fetus effectively doing the work. So there *is* a technical use in both cases.

But the Gemara ends with *teiku* — the question remains unresolved. What is the deeper issue? It appears that the *eimur* is not merely a checkbox to verify. Rather, it functions to establish that the person has been enslaved — that the status of enslavement has been imposed upon them. If the victim is asleep throughout and never becomes aware that they are being treated as a slave, one might question whether the *eimur* has achieved its purpose of creating the enslaving context. Similarly, if what was sold is the fetus rather than the woman as such, even if she was technically "used," the nature of the sale does not reflect her enslavement. The question *yesh derech imur bekach o ein derech imur bekach* — is this a normal mode of *eimur* — really asks whether the use and the sale together constitute an act of enslavement, or merely a collection of technical acts that individually satisfy criteria but together fail to define the person as a slave. The *eimur*'s purpose is to ensure that the kidnapper is the one who has transformed a free person into a slave, and where the victim is wholly oblivious or the sale applies only to the fetus, that transformation may not have occurred in any meaningful sense. The question is left unresolved.

## Text Derivations

### Women/Gentile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא אִישׁ גֹּנֵב נֶפֶשׁ מֵאֶחָיו״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ שֶׁגָּנַב, אִשָּׁה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְגֹנֵב אִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: **"If a man shall be found abducting a person of his brethren** from the children of Israel, and he exploited him and sold him, then that abductor shall die" (Deuteronomy 24:7). **I have** derived **only** that **a man who abducted** another is liable. **From where** is it derived that **a woman** who abducts another is liable as well? It is derived from **the verse** that **states: And one who abducts a man,** and sells him, if he be found in his hand, he shall be put to death" (Exodus 21:16), where the sex of the abductor is not specified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ שֶׁגָּנַב, בֵּין אִשָּׁה וּבֵין אִישׁ, וְאִשָּׁה שֶׁגָּנְבָה אִישׁ. אִשָּׁה שֶׁגָּנְבָה אִשָּׁה מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמֵת הַגַּנָּב הַהוּא״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived **only** that **a man who abducted** another, **whether a man or a woman,** is liable, as it is written: "If a man shall be found abducting a person"; the abductor is male but the sex of the abductee is not specified. **And** I derived that **a woman who abducted a man** is liable, as it is written: "And one who abducts a man"; the sex of the abductor is not specified but the abductee is male. **From where** is it derived that **a woman who abducted a woman** is liable as well? It is derived from **the verse** that **states: "Then that abductor shall die"** (Deuteronomy 24:7); the abductor shall die **in any case,** regardless of the sex of the one who was abducted.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא אִישׁ גֹּנֵב נֶפֶשׁ מֵאֶחָיו״ – אֶחָד הַגּוֹנֵב אֶת הָאִישׁ, וְאֶחָד הַגּוֹנֵב אֶת הָאִשָּׁה, וְאֶחָד גֵּר, וְאֶחָד עֶבֶד מְשׁוּחְרָר וְקָטָן – חַיָּיב. גְּנָבוֹ וְלֹא מְכָרוֹ, מְכָרוֹ וַעֲדַיִין יֶשְׁנוֹ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ – פָּטוּר. מְכָרוֹ לְאָבִיו אוֹ לְאָחִיו אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִן הַקְּרוֹבִים – חַיָּיב. הַגּוֹנֵב אֶת הָעֲבָדִים – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita* that it is written: **"If a man is found abducting a person of his brethren,"** from which it is derived that **whether he abducts a man, or** whether **he abducts a woman, or** whether he abducts **a convert, or** whether he abducts **an emancipated slave or a minor,** he is **liable.** If he **abducted** another **but did not sell him,** or if he **sold him but he remains in his domain,** the abductor is **exempt.** If he **sold him to** the **father of** the abductee, **or to his brother, or to one of** his other **relatives,** the abductor is **liable. One who abducts slaves is exempt.**

The Gemara presents a *baraita*: the *pasuk* reads *ki yimatze ish gonev nefesh me'achiv* — if a *man* steals. One might think this applies only when a man is the kidnapper. How do we know a woman who kidnaps is also liable? A second *pasuk* reads *vegonev ish* — and one who steals a man — which drops the specification of the kidnapper's gender, thereby including a female kidnapper. But now it specifies the victim as *ish* — a man — which would seem to exclude a female victim.

This produces an impasse: one *pasuk* gives us any kidnapper but only a male victim; the other gives us any victim but only a male kidnapper. How do we know a woman is liable for kidnapping another woman, or for kidnapping a minor? The Gemara resolves this from a third formulation: *umet haganav hahu* — that kidnapper shall die — employing an inclusive formulation that encompasses all combinations. Whether kidnapper or victim is male or female, the prohibition applies.

It is worth noting that Tosafot raises a general question in this context: since the general rule is *kol ha-Torah kulah bilshon zachar ne'emar* — the entire Torah is written in the masculine — and we apply *huksha ishah le'ish* to extend all prohibitions equally to women unless context demands otherwise, why do we sometimes need a special derivation to include women? Tosafot engages this question in various places and offers different answers; the question is simply worth flagging here.

A second *baraita* adds: *echad hagonev et ha'ish ve'echad hagonev et ha'isha* — whether the stolen person is a man or a woman — since the *pasuk* says *nefesh*, which encompasses either. Additionally, *ve'echad ger ve'echad eved meshuchrar vekatan chayav* — a convert, a freed slave, and a minor are all included in the category of victims, since *me'achav* (his brothers) is not read so narrowly as to exclude these. All are "brethren" in the relevant sense.

### Slave Excluded

By contrast, *hagonev et ha'avadim patur* — one who steals a currently enslaved person is exempt. The question is whether this exemption derives from the word *me'achav* — brothers, from which slaves are excluded — or from the substantive nature of the crime, which requires the enslavement of a *free* person. Taking someone from one master and delivering them to another does not constitute the act of enslaving a free person; that person was already a slave.

### Sell to Family

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר ״מֵאֶחָיו״ – עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיאֶנּוּ מֵרְשׁוּת אֶחָיו, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ חַיָּיב?! תְּנִי: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **A *tanna*** who recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the study hall **recited** that *baraita*, where the *tanna* holds that one is liable for abducting another and selling him to the abductee's father, **before Rav Sheshet.** Rav Sheshet **said to him: I teach** that **Rabbi Shimon says:** From the term **"of his brethren"** it is derived that there is no liability **unless he removes** the abductee **from the domain of his brethren, and you say** that one who sells the abductee to his father is **liable?** Emend the *baraita* and **teach** instead: He is **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קוּשְׁיָא? דִּילְמָא הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, הָא רַבָּנַן. לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סְתָם מַתְנִיתִין – רַבִּי מֵאִיר, סְתַם תּוֹסֶפְתָּא – רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, סְתַם סִפְרָא – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, סְתַם סִפְרֵי – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, וְכוּלְּהוּ אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is **the difficulty** raised by Rav Sheshet? **Perhaps that** statement that he cited is the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** while **this** *baraita* is the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with him. The Gemara responds: That should **not enter your mind, as** the unattributed *baraita* that was cited is a passage from the halakhic midrash on the books of Numbers and Deuteronomy entitled *Sifrei*, and **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: An unattributed mishna** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir; an unattributed** *baraita* in the ***Tosefta*** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya; an unattributed** *baraita* in the ***Sifra*,** the halakhic midrash on the book of Leviticus, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda;** and **an unattributed** *baraita* in the ***Sifrei*** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And all of these are in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva,** as all the Sages mentioned were his disciples. Therefore, it is unlikely that an unattributed *baraita* from the *Sifrei* would run counter to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon.

A *baraita* teaches that one who sells the kidnapped person back to the victim's own father, brothers, or other close relatives is still *chayav*. This is challenged by a *tanna* who taught before Rav Sheshes: Rabbi Shimon's position, derived from the *pasuk* in Devarim (*gonev nefesh me'achav* — stealing from his brethren), is that one must actually remove the victim from the family's domain. If one sells him back to the family, one has not taken him *me'achav*, and one is therefore *patur*.

Rav Sheshes responds: the *baraita* you cited must be corrected to accord with Rabbi Shimon's view — the one who sells back to the family is *patur*. The Gemara then asks why this reading is necessary — perhaps the *baraita* and Rabbi Shimon reflect different opinions. The answer: *lo salka datach*. Rabbi Yochanan establishes the rule that an anonymous *Mishnah* follows Rabbi Meir, an anonymous *Tosefta* follows Rabbi Nechemyah, an anonymous *Sifra* follows Rabbi Yehuda, and an anonymous *Sifrei* follows Rabbi Shimon — all students of Rabbi Akiva, as opposed to the *midreshei halacha* of the school of Rabbi Yishmael. Since the *baraita* expounding the *pasuk* in Devarim is drawn from a *Sifrei* context, it must represent Rabbi Shimon's view, and therefore must be corrected to say *patur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגּוֹנֵב בְּנוֹ – מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that there is a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis with regard to the liability of **one who abducts his son.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the Rabbis,** who deem him exempt?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּאָמַר קְרָא, ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא״ – פְּרָט לְמָצוּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It is derived **from the verse** that **states: "If** a man **shall be found** abducting a person of his brethren" (Deuteronomy 24:7), to **exclude** one who is already **found** in the custody of the abductor before the abduction. Since the son is already in the custody of his father, the father is not liable for abducting him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא אִישׁ שֹׁכֵב עִם אִשָּׁה בְעֻלַת בַּעַל״, הָכִי נָמֵי ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא״ – פְּרָט לְמָצוּי? כְּגוֹן שֶׁל בֵּית פְּלוֹנִי דִּשְׁכִיחָן גַּבַּיְיהוּ, הָכִי נָמֵי דִּפְטִירִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: If that is so,** then the verse: **"If a man shall be found lying with a woman married to a husband,** then they shall both of them die" (Deuteronomy 22:22), may **also** be interpreted: **"If** a man **shall be found,"** to **exclude** one who was already **found. So too,** would one say **that** adulterers **are exempt** from liability if they commit adultery in, **for example, the house of so-and-so, where** married women **are commonly** found and they have a preexisting reputation for licentiousness?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אֲנָא מִ״וְנִמְצָא בְּיָדוֹ״ קָאָמֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa: The Rabbis' opinion is derived from the phrase: **"Or if he is found in his possession,** he shall be put to death" (Exodus 21:16), from which **I am saying** my inference, that it is derived: If he is found, to the exclusion of one who was already found.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכָּךְ, הָנֵי מַקְרֵי דַרְדְּקֵי וּמַתְנוּ רַבָּנַן כִּמְצוּיִין בְּיָדָן דָּמוּ, וּפְטִירִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Therefore,** with regard to **those teachers of children [*dardekei*] and those who recite** *mishnayot* **to Torah scholars,** the status of their students **is as** though they are **found in their possession, and** the teachers **are exempt** from liability for abducting them.

Returning to the case of *hagonev et beno* — kidnapping one's own son — the Gemara asks what the *chachamim*'s reason for exemption is. Abaye answers: *kara ki yimatzei, prat l'matzuy* — the *pasuk* says *ki yimatzei*, implying that the stolen person is one who is "found" (i.e., discovered in a place they do not normally belong). A son who lives in his father's domain is already "found" there — he is not a person who is unexpectedly present in the kidnapper's possession. The closest parallel is the *mitzvah* of *shilu'ach haken*: the *pasuk* there says *ki yikarei* — if you chance upon a nest — and the same derivation of *prat l'matzuy* exempts one who is raising birds at home, since the nest is not "chanced upon" but is constantly available.

Rav Pappa challenges Abaye: the same logic should apply to one who commits adultery with a neighbor's wife if they live under the same roof and the woman is therefore constantly "available" to him. Abaye responds that he is not deriving from *ki yimatzei* in that *pasuk* at all, but from the phrase *venimtza beyado* in the *pasuk* in Shemot — *vegonev ish umacharo venimtza beyado mot yumat*. That phrase implies that the victim was "found" in the kidnapper's possession in a sense suggesting they did not belong there — i.e., the victim must be someone who would not normally be under the kidnapper's control.

Rava applies this to a practical ruling: *mikrei dardeki* — schoolteachers of young children — and *masnei rabbanan* — teachers of older students — are *kimtzuim biyadan dami*, treated as if the students are already in their possession, since the children are regularly entrusted to them. If such a teacher were to kidnap one of their own charges, they would be *patur* — which is admittedly striking, since these are precisely the positions of trust where such concerns are most acute.

## Half-Slave Debate

### Boshet/Brotherhood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין לַעֲבָדִים בּוֹשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Bava Kamma* 87a) that **Rabbi Yehuda says: There is no** indemnity for the **humiliation of a slave,** since he is not a full-fledged Jew.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי יִנָּצוּ אֲנָשִׁים יַחְדָּו אִישׁ וְאָחִיו״. מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ אַחְוָה – יָצָא עֶבֶד שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אַחְוָה. וְרַבָּנַן, אָחִיו הוּא בְּמִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda?** It is derived from **the verse** that **states: "When men struggle together, a man and his brother,** and the wife of the one drew near to deliver her husband from the hand of the one who smites him, and extended her hand, and grabbed his genitals" (Deuteronomy 25:11). This is the source for liability to pay restitution for humiliating another. From the term "his brother" it is derived that **one who has brotherhood,** i.e., who is halakhically related to his biological family, receives payment for humiliation. **A slave is excluded, as he has no brotherhood,** i.e., he is not halakhically related to his family. **And** what is the reason for the opinion of **the Rabbis?** They hold that although the slave has no family ties, he **is the brother of** the assailant **with regard to the** fulfillment of **mitzvot,** as a Canaanite slave is obligated to fulfill the same mitzvot that a woman is obligated to fulfill.

The Mishnah's case of kidnapping a half-slave is illuminated by a parallel debate about *boshet* — embarrassment damages — which appears to produce a curious reversal of positions between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Rabbanan*.

The *baraita* cites the verse *ki yinatzu anashim yachdav ish ve'achiv* — when two men fight, a man and his brother — as the source for the obligation to pay *boshet*. From the word *achiv* (his brother), the *baraita* derives: *mi she'yesh lo achva* — only one who stands in a relationship of brotherhood can claim *boshet* payments. *Yatza eved she'ein lo achva* — a slave is excluded because slaves do not have legal brotherhood. What does this mean? Tosafot clarifies: it does not mean that biological ties are simply irrelevant. A convert, after all, is also severed from prior biological family, yet a convert's children are considered relatives to one another once they have converted. The point by slaves is different: the law never recognized the family structure of enslaved persons. Even a slave's children bore no legal familial connection to him. This reflected — and entrenched — the reality that slave families were routinely broken apart. Because a slave has no *achva* — no legally recognized brotherhood — Rabbi Yehuda holds there is no *boshet* payment for embarrassing one.

### Half-Slave Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא, הֵיכִי דָּרֵישׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה? סָבַר: ״מֵאֶחָיו״ – לְאַפּוֹקֵי עֲבָדִים, ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, ״מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – לְמַעוֹטֵי מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין. הָוֵי מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט, וְאֵין מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט אֶלָּא לְרַבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And here,** with regard to abduction, **how does Rabbi Yehuda interpret** the verses and arrive at the conclusion that one is liable for abducting one who is half-slave half-freeman? Shouldn't the term "from his brethren" render exempt from liability one who abducts a slave? The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yehuda **holds** that the term in the verse: **"From his brethren"** (Deuteronomy (24:7), serves **to exclude** from liability one who abducts **slaves.** Had the verse continued: **The children of Israel,** that phrase would have been interpreted **to exclude** from liability one who abducts **one who is half-slave half-freeman.** Since the verse states: **"From the children of Israel,"** the prefix letter *mem*, meaning from, indicates that there are some from the children of Israel for whose abduction one is liable and there are some for whose abduction one is exempt. That prefix also serves **to exclude** from liability one who abducts **one who is half-slave half-freeman.** Therefore, **this is** an example of **a restriction following a restriction, and** there is a hermeneutical principle that **a restriction following a restriction** serves **only to amplify** the *halakha* and to include in the category of those who are liable one who abducts one who is half-slave half-freeman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, ״מֵאֶחָיו״ לְאַפּוֹקֵי עֲבָדִים לָא מַשְׁמַע לְהוּ, דְּהָא אָחִיו הוּא בְּמִצְוֹת. ״בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ ״מִבְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – חַד לְמַעוֹטֵי עֶבֶד, וְחַד לְמַעוֹטֵי מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who deem one who abducts one who is half-slave half-freeman exempt, how do they interpret the verse? **They do not exclude slaves** based on the term **"from his brethren," as** the slave **is the brother of** the abductor **with regard to the** fulfillment of **mitzvot.** Concerning the expression **"children of Israel"** and the more expanded expression **"from the children of Israel," one** serves **to exclude** from liability one who abducts **slaves,** as the slave is not a full-fledged Jew, **and one** serves **to exclude** from liability one who abducts **one who is half-slave half-freeman.**

The *Rabbanan* dispute Rabbi Yehuda on the *boshet* question: *achiv hu b'mitzvot* — a slave is still one's "brother" in the world of *mitzvot*, because a slave is obligated in *mitzvot* and thereby remains part of the Jewish community in a meaningful sense. Accordingly, the *Rabbanan* hold that slaves *can* claim *boshet* payments.

This produces the curious flip in the kidnapping context. The Gemara asks: *vehacha heichi darish Rabbi Yehuda* — how does Rabbi Yehuda read the kidnapping *pesukim* to arrive at his position that kidnapping a half-slave incurs liability?

Rabbi Yehuda reasons as follows. The *pasuk* says *gonev nefesh me'achav mibnei Yisrael* — stealing from his brothers among the children of Israel. *Me'achav* would, on his view, already exclude slaves (since slaves have no *achva*). *Bnei Yisrael* provides an additional exclusion. *Mi-Bnei Yisrael* — the prefixed *mem* — provides yet a third. Having already excluded slaves through *me'achav*, the additional exclusions of *Bnei Yisrael* and *Mi-Bnei Yisrael* now constitute *mi'ut achar mi'ut* — a double exclusion — and the principle is *ein mi'ut achar mi'ut ela l'rabos*: when the Torah is so insistent about excluding something that it excludes it twice, the effect is actually to *include* it. Thus Rabbi Yehuda winds up including the half-slave within the prohibition.

The *Rabbanan*, by contrast, do not read *me'achav* as excluding slaves at all — since *achiv hu b'mitzvot*, the slave retains brotherhood. For them, only *Bnei Yisrael* and *Mi-Bnei Yisrael* serve as exclusions — one for a full slave, and one for a half-slave. They therefore have two straightforward exclusions, and the half-slave remains exempt.

The irony is apparent: Rabbi Yehuda, who is more aggressive in excluding slaves from *boshet*, winds up via the *mi'ut achar mi'ut* principle including the half-slave in the kidnapping prohibition. The *Rabbanan*, who are more inclusive on *boshet*, wind up excluding the half-slave from the kidnapping prohibition. The positions appear reversed because the hermeneutical logic moves in opposite directions.

## Two Prohibitions

### Two Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַזְהָרָה לְגוֹנֵב נֶפֶשׁ מִנַּיִן? רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה אָמַר: מִ״לֹּא תִגְנֹב״. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מִ״לֹּא יִמָּכְרוּ מִמְכֶּרֶת עֶבֶד״. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי, מָר קָא חָשֵׁיב לָאו דִּגְנֵיבָה, וּמָר קָא חָשֵׁיב לָאו דִּמְכִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **From where** is **a prohibition against abducting a person** derived? **Rabbi Yoshiya says** that it is derived **from** the verse: **"You shall not steal"** (Exodus 20:13). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is derived **from** the verse: **"They shall not be sold as slaves"** (Leviticus 25:42). The Gemara comments: **And they do not disagree,** as each requires both verses to derive the prohibition. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoshiya, **enumerates** the **prohibition against abduction, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **enumerates** the **prohibition against selling** the abductee into slavery.

Having established the elements of the crime, the Gemara asks: *azhara l'gonev nefesh minayin* — where is the prohibitory warning (*azharah*) against kidnapping? Rabbi Yoshiya says it derives from *lo tignov* in the Ten Commandments. Rabbi Yochanan says it derives from *lo yimachru mimcheras aved* — they shall not be sold as a slave is sold — the verse in Vayikra addressing the treatment of a Jew who sells himself into servitude. The Torah declares: *ki avadai heim asher hotzesi osam me'eretz Mitzrayim* — they are My servants whom I brought out of Egypt — and therefore *lo yimachru mimcheras aved* — they shall not be sold in the manner of slaves.

The Gemara resolves that these authorities do not actually disagree: *mar kachashiv lav d'geneivah u'mar kachashiv lav d'mechira* — one is identifying the prohibition against stealing a person, and the other is identifying the separate prohibition against selling a person. There are thus two distinct prohibitions. Capital liability requires the violation of both — stealing *and* selling — but each act is independently prohibited. This raises an interesting theoretical question: could one violate the prohibition of *lo yimachru mimcheras aved* without having first stolen anyone? For instance, if someone fraudulently represents a free person as their slave and "sells" them to an unknowing buyer who then takes possession — has the "seller" transgressed the prohibition against selling even absent the act of theft? This question is left open.

### Lo Tignov Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא תִגְנֹב״ – בְּגוֹנֵב נְפָשׁוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגוֹנֵב נְפָשׁוֹת, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּגוֹנֵב מָמוֹן? אָמַרְתָּ: צֵא וּלְמַד מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה נִדְרֶשֶׁת בָּהֶן, דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָינוֹ. בְּמָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? בִּנְפָשׁוֹת. אַף כָּאן בִּנְפָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **"You shall not steal"** (Exodus 20:13), and it is **with regard to** one who **abducts people** that **the verse is speaking. Do you say** that the verse is speaking **with regard to** one who **abducts people, or** perhaps the verse is speaking **only with regard to** one who **steals property? You say: Go out and learn from** one of **the thirteen hermeneutical principles: A matter derived from its context. With regard to what** context are the adjacent prohibitions "You shall not kill; you shall not commit adultery" in **the verse speaking?** They are speaking **with regard to capital** cases. **So too here,** the prohibition is speaking **with regard to a capital** case of abduction.

The Gemara then presents a *baraita* establishing why *lo tignov* in the Ten Commandments refers to kidnapping rather than property theft. The *baraita* says: *atah omer b'gonev nefashos o eino ela b'gonev mamon* — you might think this verse speaks of property theft. The answer: *tze ulemad mishlosh esrei middos shehatorah nidreshet bahem* — go and learn from the thirteen hermeneutical principles, one of which is *davar halamed me'inyano* — a matter is understood from its context. What is the surrounding context? *Lo tirtzach, lo tin'af, lo tignov* — the preceding commandments are all capital offenses. Since the context is capital crime, *lo tignov* here must refer to a form of stealing that constitutes a capital crime — namely, kidnapping.

### Lo Tignovu Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״לֹא תִגְנֹבוּ״ – בְּגוֹנֵב מָמוֹן הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּגוֹנֵב מָמוֹן, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּגוֹנֵב נְפָשׁוֹת? אָמַרְתָּ: צֵא וּלְמַד מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מִדּוֹת שֶׁהַתּוֹרָה נִדְרֶשֶׁת בָּהֶן, דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד מֵעִנְיָנוֹ. בְּמָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? בְּמָמוֹן. אַף כָּאן בְּמָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **"You shall not steal"** (Leviticus 19:11), and it is **with regard to** one who **steals property** that **the verse is speaking. Do you say** that the verse is speaking **with regard to** one who **steals property, or** perhaps the verse is speaking **only with regard to** one who **abducts people? You say: Go out and learn from** one of **the thirteen hermeneutical principles: A matter derived from its context. With regard to what** context **is the** subsequent **verse:** "You shall neither exploit your neighbor nor rob him" (Leviticus 19:13), **speaking?** It is speaking **with regard to property. So too here,** the verse is speaking **with regard to property.**

A second *baraita* establishes the complementary point for the verse in Vayikra: *lo tignovu* — in the plural — speaks of property theft. Again: *atah omer b'gonev mamon o eino ela b'gonev nefashos?* The same hermeneutical principle applies. The surrounding context in Vayikra is: *lo tishakru velo techachashu velo tignovu* — do not lie, do not deny, do not steal — a passage concerned with monetary matters and interpersonal deception rather than capital crimes. Therefore *lo tignovu* there refers to property theft, not kidnapping.

## Davar Debate

### Chizkiyah/Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: עֵידֵי גְנֵיבָה וְעֵידֵי מְכִירָה בְּנֶפֶשׁ שֶׁהוּזַּמּוּ, חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: נֶהֱרָגִין.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** If **the witnesses to the abduction and the witnesses to the sale of a person were rendered conspiring witnesses, Ḥizkiyya says:** The typical sentence of conspiring witnesses is not implemented and **they are not executed. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They are executed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִזְקִיָּה, דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: ״דָּבָר״ וְלֹא חֲצִי דָּבָר. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: ״דָּבָר״, וַאֲפִילּוּ חֲצִי דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: It is **Ḥizkiyya who said** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said** one derives from the verse: "On the basis of two witnesses…shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 19:15), that the testimony of witnesses is valid only when they attest to an entire **matter, but not** to **half a matter.** Since each pair of witnesses provides testimony concerning only half the transgression for which the perpetrator would be liable, i.e., they each testify to only the abduction or the sale, the testimony of each pair is not valid. Therefore, when they are deemed conspiring witnesses, they are not executed. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who said** that one derives from the verse that the testimony is valid when they testify with regard to an entire **matter, and even** when they testify with regard to **half a matter.** Since the testimony of the two pairs of witnesses together constitutes a complete testimony, if they are rendered conspiring witnesses, they are executed.

Since there are two distinct elements of the crime — stealing and selling — the Gemara turns to the question of how testimony combining both elements functions. The specific framing involves *eidim zomemim* (false witnesses), but as the Gemara makes clear, the underlying question is when testimonies given by different sets of witnesses can be combined to establish liability.

*Eidei gneivah ve'eidei mechira* — two witnesses testify that the accused stole the victim, and two separate witnesses testify that he sold the victim. Both sets are subsequently proven to be *eidim zomemim* — contradicted by *hazamah*. The question: are they subject to the death penalty that they sought to impose? Chizkiyah says no; Rabbi Yochanan says yes. Chizkiyah holds *ke'Rabbi Akiva de'amar davar velo chatzi davar* — following Rabbi Akiva, who requires that testimony constitute a *davar*, a complete and legally operative matter, not merely a *chatzi davar*, a partial one. Neither set of witnesses alone testified to the complete crime; each testified only to one element. Rabbi Yochanan follows the *Rabbanan*, who accept *chatzi davar* — partial testimony can combine with other partial testimony to establish the complete crime.

The underlying issue before the *hazamah* question is: can two separate sets of witnesses, each testifying to only one element of the crime, combine to convict? For Chizkiyah, the answer is no, because each testimony standing alone has no legal effect — it proves nothing actionable. For Rabbi Yochanan, the testimonies combine and the full crime is established.

### Ben Sorer Moreh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה חִזְקִיָּה בְּעֵדִים הָאַחֲרוֹנִים שֶׁל בֶּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה שֶׁהוּזַּמּוּ, שֶׁנֶּהֱרָגִין, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיְּכוֹלִים לוֹמַר הָרִאשׁוֹנִים: לְהַלְקוֹתוֹ בָּאנוּ, וְהָנֵי אַחֲרִינֵי כּוּלֵּיהּ דָּבָר קָא עָבְדִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Ḥizkiyya concedes with regard to the final witnesses of a stubborn and rebellious son who were rendered conspiring witnesses that they are executed.** A stubborn and rebellious son is executed only if witnesses testified that he engaged in gluttonous and drunken conduct and he was flogged, and then a second pair of witnesses testifies that he again engaged in gluttonous and drunken conduct. His death sentence is based solely on the testimony of the second pair, **as the first** witnesses **could say:** It is in order **to flog him,** not to execute him, that **we came** to court. **And these other** witnesses, through their testimony, are the ones who **did** this **to him,** i.e., they are responsible for the **entire matter** of his execution, and are therefore liable to be executed for giving conspiring testimony.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אִי הָכִי, עֵידֵי מְכִירָה נָמֵי לִיקְטְלֵיהּ, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיְּכוֹלִין עֵידֵי גְנֵיבָה לוֹמַר ״לְהַלְקוֹתוֹ בָּאנוּ״! וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּקָסָבַר חִזְקִיָּה דְּלָא לָקֵי, וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: עֵידֵי גְּנֵיבָה בְּנֶפֶשׁ שֶׁהוּזַּמּוּ, חִזְקִיָּה וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – חַד אָמַר: לוֹקִין, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין לוֹקִין. וְאָמְרִינַן: תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּחִזְקִיָּה דְּאָמַר לוֹקִין, מִדְּאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this: If so,** and Ḥizkiyya concedes to Rabbi Yoḥanan in the case of the final testimony of the stubborn and rebellious son, **let them also execute the witnesses to the sale** of one who was abducted, **as the witnesses to the abduction could say:** It is in order **to flog** the abductor, not to execute him, that **we came** to court. **And if you would say that Ḥizkiyya holds that** one who abducts another and does not sell him **is not flogged,** that is difficult. **But wasn't it stated:** With regard to **witnesses to the abduction who were rendered conspiring witnesses** prior to the testimony of the witnesses to the sale, **Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Yoḥanan** disagree. **One says: They are flogged, and one says: They are not flogged. And we say: It may be concluded that** it is **Ḥizkiyya who said** that **they are flogged, from** the fact **that Ḥizkiyya said: They are not executed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר נֶהֱרָגִין, הָוֵה לֵיהּ לָאו שֶׁנִּיתַּן לְאַזְהָרַת מִיתַת בֵּית דִּין, וְכׇל לָאו שֶׁנִּיתַּן לְאַזְהָרַת מִיתַת בֵּית דִּין אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו. אִיהוּ לָא לָקֵי, אִינְהוּ הֵיכִי לָקוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Since if** one were to suggest that it is **Rabbi Yoḥanan** who said that they are flogged, that cannot be. **Since he says** that conspiring witnesses **are executed** in this case, **it is a prohibition that is given as a warning** of liability for **a court**-imposed **death** penalty, **and** the principle is: With regard to **any prohibition that is given as a warning** of liability for **a court**-imposed **death** penalty, **one is not flogged for its** violation, even in a case where the transgressor is not executed. The abductor **is not flogged. How** then **could** the conspiring witnesses **be flogged** for testifying against them, as the punishment for conspiring witnesses is identical to the punishment of the one against whom they testified? Rather, it is certain that Ḥizkiyya holds that the conspiring witnesses to the abduction are flogged, and therefore everyone agrees that the conspiring witnesses to the sale are executed.

The Gemara notes: *u'modeh Chizkiyah b'eidim ha'achronim shel ben sorer u'moreh she'huzmu she'neheragim* — Chizkiyah concedes that in the case of the *ben sorer u'moreh* (the wayward son), the *second* set of witnesses, if proven *zomemim*, are killed. Why? Because the *first* set of witnesses testified to an act — the initial theft and eating — that by itself was sufficient to administer lashes. Once the court accepted that testimony and administered lashes, the second testimony builds on something already legally operative. The second witnesses are not combining a *chatzi davar* with another *chatzi davar*; they are adding to a *davar* that was already complete in itself, advancing the matter to its capital conclusion. *Hanei kulla milta ka'avdi* — the second witnesses are doing something complete.

The Gemara then asks whether the same logic should apply to the witnesses who testified to the *gneivah* (theft) in the kidnapping case. If those witnesses already established liability for lashes — since *lo tignov* is an independent prohibition — then the second witnesses who testify to the *mechira* (sale) should be able to build on that accepted testimony, just as in the *ben sorer u'moreh* case.

### Lashes Acceptance

The question becomes: does the *gneivah* testimony, standing alone, give rise to lashes? A *baraita* records a dispute between Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan on exactly this point — *chad amar lokin, chad amar ein lokin* — one says lashes are administered, one says they are not. The Gemara reasons that Chizkiyah is the one who says lashes *are* administered. The logic: since Chizkiyah holds that the two testimonies cannot combine to impose the death penalty, there is no basis to invoke the principle of *nitan l'azharas mitat beit din* — that a prohibition which can lead to a capital sentence does not give rise to lashes in lieu thereof. For Chizkiyah, since the theft testimony cannot lead to a capital conviction (because it cannot combine with the sale testimony), the theft transgression remains an ordinary *lav* for which lashes are appropriate. For Rabbi Yochanan, who holds that the two testimonies *do* combine and can produce a capital conviction, the theft prohibition is *nitan l'azharas mitat beit din*, and therefore no lashes are administered for it.

This creates the following internally coherent structure for each opinion. According to Chizkiyah: the theft testimony is accepted by itself and produces lashes; but the two testimonies do not combine for capital purposes; accordingly, the *eidei gneivah*, if proven *zomemim*, cannot be killed. According to Rabbi Yochanan: the theft and sale constitute a single unified capital offense; no lashes are administered for the theft alone; but the two testimonies fully combine, and if the *eidei gneivah* are proven *zomemim*, they are killed along with the *eidei mechira* as co-conspirators in the same capital case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּעֵידֵי מְכִירָה דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּנֶהֱרָגִין, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּעֵידֵי גְנֵיבָה. חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין, גְּנֵיבָה לְחוֹדַהּ קָיְימָא וּמְכִירָה לְחוּדַהּ קָיְימָא. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: נֶהֱרָגִין, גְּנֵיבָה אַתְחַלְתָּא דִמְכִירָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa says:** The previous explanation is rejected, and instead the dispute must be explained as follows: **With regard to the witnesses to the sale** of the abductee, it is clear **that everyone agrees that they are executed,** as theirs is testimony concerning an entire matter and would have led to his execution. **When they disagree,** it is **with regard to the witnesses to the abduction. Ḥizkiyya says: They are not executed,** as he holds that **abduction stands discrete** as an independent prohibition punishable by lashes, **and** the **sale stands discrete** as an independent prohibition punishable by strangulation. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They are executed,** as the **abduction is the beginning of** the process that culminates with **the sale.** The witnesses testifying to the abduction are testifying to a transgression that will culminate with the sale of the abductee.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּעֵדִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים שֶׁל בֵּן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה שֶׁהוּזַּמּוּ, שֶׁאֵין נֶהֱרָגִין, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיְּכוֹלִים לוֹמַר: ״לְהַלְקוֹתוֹ בָּאנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan concedes with regard to the initial witnesses concerning a stubborn and rebellious son** who testified that he engaged in gluttonous and drunken conduct and **who were rendered conspiring witnesses that they are not executed, as they could say:** It is in order **to flog him** for past conduct, not to execute him for actions that he might perform in the future, that **we came** to court. Therefore, there is no connection between their testimony and punishment for future actions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה, וּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה. הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה: בְּעֵדִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, שֶׁאֵין נֶהֱרָגִין, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיְּכוֹלִים לוֹמַר: ״לְהַלְקוֹתוֹ בָּאנוּ״. וְהַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה: בְּעֵדִים אַחֲרוֹנִים שֶׁנֶּהֱרָגִים, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁעֵדִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים יְכוֹלִים לוֹמַר: ״לְהַלְקוֹתוֹ בָּאנוּ״, וְהָנֵי כּוּלֵּיהּ דָּבָר קָא עָבְדִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in summary: **All concede in** the case of **a stubborn and rebellious son, and all concede in** the case of **a stubborn and rebellious son, and** there is **a dispute with regard to a stubborn and rebellious son.** The Gemara elaborates: **With regard to the initial witnesses, all,** even Rabbi Yoḥanan, **concede in** the case of **a stubborn and rebellious son that they are not executed** if they are rendered conspiring witnesses, **as they could say:** It is in order **to flog him** for past conduct, not to execute him, that **we came** to court. **And with regard to the final witnesses, all,** even Ḥizkiyya, **concede in** the case of **a stubborn and rebellious son that they are executed** if they are rendered conspiring witnesses, **due to** the fact **that the initial witnesses could say:** It is in order **to flog him** for past conduct that **we came** to court, **and** these final witnesses are the ones who **did** this **to him,** i.e., they are responsible for the **entire matter** of his execution and are therefore liable to be executed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַחְלוֹקֶת בְּבֵן סוֹרֵר וּמוֹרֶה: שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״בְּפָנֵינוּ גָּנַב״, וּשְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים: ״בְּפָנֵינוּ אָכַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And there is a dispute with regard to a stubborn and rebellious son** in a case where **two** of the final witnesses, who testify after the son was already flogged for engaging in gluttonous and drunken conduct, **say: He stole in our presence, and two** other witnesses **say: He ate in our presence.** The dispute is whether the testimony of these two pairs of witnesses is testimony concerning an entire matter or testimony concerning half a matter.

## Sequential Witnesses

### Rav Assi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: עֵידֵי מְכִירָה בְּנֶפֶשׁ שֶׁהוּזַּמּוּ אֵין נֶהֱרָגִין, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁיָּכוֹל לוֹמַר ״עַבְדִּי מָכַרְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says: The witnesses to the sale of a person who were rendered conspiring witnesses are not executed, due to** the fact **that** the one against whom they testified **could say:** Although they testified that I sold an individual, it was **my slave** that **I sold.** In that case, the witnesses are not testifying that he violated a capital transgression, as they cannot attest to the fact that the individual he sold was first abducted.

Rav Assi rules: *eidei mechira b'nefesh she'huzmu ein neheragim mishum she'yecholim lomar avdi macharti* — witnesses who testified only to the sale, and were proven *zomemim*, are not subject to the death penalty, because they can plausibly claim: "We were testifying that he sold his own slave." The testimony of sale, standing entirely alone without accompanying testimony of theft, does not establish any crime at all — for all the court knows, the person being sold was a legitimate slave. Not only does their false testimony not combine to impose capital liability, but the testimony is meaningless even to establish a lesser violation.

### After-Fact Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא שְׁמַעְתָּא דְּרַב אַסִּי? כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: ״דָּבָר״, וְלֹא חֲצִי דָּבָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: דְּאִי כְּרַבָּנַן, נֶהֱרָגִין? הָא ״מִתּוֹךְ״ קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: In accordance with whose opinion is that *halakha* of Rav Asi?** It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says:** The testimony of witnesses is valid only when they attest to an entire **matter and not** to **half a matter. Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: According to your explanation, it is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, **as, if** it were **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold: The testimony is valid when they testify to an entire matter and even when they testify to half a matter, are the conspiring witnesses **executed? Doesn't** Rav Asi **say** that they are not executed **due to** the fact that the one against whom they testified could say: Although they testified that I sold an individual, it was my slave that I sold? According to that reasoning, even the Rabbis would concede that they are not executed.

Rav Yosef notes that Rav Assi's ruling follows Rabbi Akiva's principle of *davar velo chatzi davar*. Abaye corrects him: this conclusion holds even according to the *Rabbanan*, who accept *chatzi davar* testimony. The *Rabbanan* only permit combination of two partial testimonies when *both* have been given and together they establish the complete crime. Here, the sale witnesses testified alone, without any corroborating theft witnesses. *Delo atu eidei gneivah* — the theft witnesses never came. Of course their testimony is meaningless — this is not a question of *davar* vs. *chatzi davar* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, וּבִדְלָא אֲתוֹ עֵידֵי גְּנֵיבָה. אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲתוֹ לְבַסּוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, you** may **even say** that Rav Asi's statement is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and** it is concerning a case **where** only witnesses to the sale came to testify and **witnesses to the abduction did not come** to testify. In that case the accused can avoid punishment; therefore, the conspiring witnesses are not executed. The Gemara asks: **If so, what** is the purpose **of stating** that *halakha*? Obviously, in that case they are not executed, as there is no way to determine that the one he sold is not a slave. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach **that** they are not executed **even if** witnesses to the abduction **ultimately came** after the witnesses to the sale had testified and testified that he sold a freeman, not his slave.

The Gemara responds: *tzricha d'af al gav d'atu l'vasaf* — the ruling is necessary for the case where the theft witnesses *subsequently* arrive. One might think: now that the theft has been established, can the court retrospectively combine the earlier sale testimony with the newly arrived theft testimony, and treat the sale witnesses as *zomemim* in a capital proceeding? The answer is no — and this is the operative ruling.

### Remiza/Signals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָא מְרַמְּזִי רַמּוֹזֵי. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רְמִיזָא מִילְּתָא הִיא? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: רְמִיזָא לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But still, what** is the purpose **of stating** that *halakha*? When the witnesses to the sale testified, their testimony was not sufficient to execute the accused. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach the *halakha* in a case where they are not executed even **where** the first and second pairs of witnesses **gesture** to one another, ostensibly indicating that the conspiring witnesses to the sale were aware that the witnesses to the abduction would follow and that therefore the initial witnesses are part of the conspiracy to testify and execute the accused. And consequently, it is necessary to teach this *halakha* **lest you say: Gesturing is** a significant **matter,** and the legal status of the two testimonies is that of a single testimony. Therefore, Rav Asi **teaches us** that **gesturing is nothing** of significance.

Why not? The Gemara acknowledges the argument: the sale witnesses could claim complete innocence, saying they had no idea theft was involved. The Gemara then addresses a case where *remiza* — signals — were observed. *De'ka meramze remuze* — the first witnesses and the second witnesses were seen signaling to one another, suggesting coordinated conspiracy. One might think that *remiza milta hi* — that such signaling is legally significant and establishes their complicity, making them liable as *eidim zomemim* once the testimony is combined in retrospect. The Gemara rules: *remiza lav klum hi* — signals are legally inconsequential. Unless the witnesses were part of a single coordinated testimony from the outset, their subsequent signals cannot transform what was initially innocent partial testimony into a capital conspiracy.

The practical implication is worth noting: a sophisticated conspirator who wished to avoid liability could arrange for the witnesses to testify sequentially rather than together. If the plot succeeds, the combined testimony convicts. If the witnesses are proven *zomemim*, only the sale witnesses will have testified to something that, by itself, threatened a capital outcome — and even that is mitigated by the *avdi macharti* defense. The *remiza* ruling forecloses the ability of the court to treat coordination as sufficient to unify what were legally separate acts of testimony.
