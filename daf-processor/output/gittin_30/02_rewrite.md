# Gittin 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shliach shelo nitan

### Shliach empowerment

The daf is 30. We pick up at the bottom of 29b, continuing the discussion of a *shliach* appointing another *shliach*. When the delivery of a *get* takes place within Eretz Yisrael but originates from *chutz l'aretz*, the *shliach* must make the transfer before a *beis din* by declaring *befanai nechtav*. The preceding sugya had introduced the concept of a *shliach shelo nitan l'geirushin* — a messenger who has not yet been activated to deliver the *get* himself, and who therefore lacks the full empowerment necessary to transfer that responsibility to another.

### Thirty-day delay

The Gemara now turns to a new case: *hahu gavra dishadar leh gitta l'vei ittei* — a man sent a *get* to his wife through a *shliach*, instructing him, *lo titein lah ad t'latasin yomin* — do not give it to her until thirty days have passed. Within those thirty days, however, circumstances intervened and the *shliach* was compelled to leave before the thirty days elapsed. The *get* was not yet valid for delivery — not because it was literally postdated, but because the husband had placed a temporal hold on its delivery. Unable to wait, the *shliach* came before Rava.

### Rava's ruling

Rava reasoned as follows: the Mishnah had already established that a *shliach* may appoint a substitute when he falls ill — *chala ta'ama mai? mishum d'anus* — because illness is beyond his control, and we assume the husband would not object to the transfer of the *shliach*'s responsibility under such compelled circumstances. So too in this case — *hai nami anus hu* — this is equally a situation of *ones*. Rava therefore instructed the *shliach*: *mesoru milach kamei didan* — declare your appointment before us. The *beis din* would then, after thirty days, appoint a new *shliach* to deliver the *get*. The case maps neatly onto the Mishnah: a transfer of agency, declared before a *beis din*, under circumstances of *ones* — entirely in keeping with established procedure.

### Challenge to Rava

The Rabbis immediately challenged Rava: *ve'ha shliach shelo nitan l'geirushin hu* — is this not a *shliach* who has not yet been empowered to deliver the *get*? His *shlichus* is, as it were, on hold until the thirtieth day. He has not yet been activated. And if he is not yet empowered to deliver the *get* himself, how can he transfer that authority to the *beis din*?

### Empowerment debate

Rava responded by drawing a distinction between fundamental empowerment and temporal activation. *Kevan d'levatar t'latasin yomin matzi m'garesh* — since at the conclusion of thirty days he will have full authority to deliver the *get* — *k'shliach she'nitan l'geirushin hu*: he is fundamentally a *shliach* who has been empowered to deliver the *get*, merely waiting for the designated moment. The fact that the moment has not yet arrived does not redefine him as someone who lacks empowerment altogether. His agency is real; it is only the timing that is constrained. He may therefore transfer that responsibility to the *beis din*.

### Piyes concern

The Gemara now raises a different concern: *ve'neichosh shema piyes* — should we not worry that in the interim thirty days, the husband may have reconciled with his wife? Why would a husband put a *get* on hold for thirty days in the first place? Possibly because he was considering whether to go through with the divorce. If they reconciled, and then the husband and wife were in *yichud* together — or worse, actually cohabited — the *get* becomes disqualified *m'divrei sofrim*, because the *get* bears a date prior to that encounter. People seeing the *get*'s date will calculate that the child was born out of wedlock. This is the problem of a *get yashan*: the *get* is dated January first, delivered February first, and if they slept together on January fifteenth, observers will conclude from the *get* that she was divorced at the time of that encounter, creating a false impression about the child's status.

Concretely: the concern is not merely the general delay any *shliach* traveling from *chutz l'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael* might experience — where the husband is distant and the delay is incidental — but rather a deliberate thirty-day hold explicitly imposed by the husband. The husband is not necessarily far away; the *shliach* was nearby, waiting. The deliberate withholding strongly suggests that the husband was allowing himself time to attempt reconciliation.

### 12-month condition

The Gemara draws a parallel to a case taught elsewhere: *me'achshav im lo basi mikan v'ad yud beis chodesh* — if a husband says "this is your *get* effective now if I do not return within twelve months," and he then died within that period, *harei zeh get* — it is a valid *get*, since he did not return. Yet the Gemara there already raised the same challenge: *ve'neichosh shema piyes* — perhaps before he died he secretly returned and reconciled? The Gemara answered that the resolution depends on a particular formulation: *be'omer ne'emenes alai lomar shelo basi* — the husband had declared that his wife is believed over a hundred witnesses to testify that he had not returned.

This principle of *ne'emenes alai* is a significant one. It has contemporary relevance in the context of halachic pre-nuptial agreements, where one of the provisions that can be included is a statement by the husband that his wife is believed even over his own counterclaims about what transpired or what conditions were set. It directly addresses the problem of disputed facts and counter-claims.

### Ne'emenes alai

The upshot of the parallel is clear: absent a specific declaration of *ne'emenes alai*, we would indeed be concerned that the husband returned and reconciled. In the case of the thirty-day hold, no such declaration was made. How, then, could Rava have proceeded to allow the *get* to be delivered?

### Arusa distinction

The Gemara resolves the difficulty by noting that *ichsof* — Rava was embarrassed by the question. And then it emerged: *u'lesof iglaei milsa de'arusa havaya* — it turned out that the woman in question was only an *arusa*, not a *nesuah*. Since they had never lived together as a married couple, there is considerably less reason to fear either that reconciliation is likely or that *yichud* and cohabitation would have occurred in the interim. Rava then stated: *im amru be'nesuah, yomru be'arusa?* — if they applied this concern about reconciliation in the case of a *nesuah*, would they apply it to an *arusa* as well? The concern does not extend to an *arusa* in the same way.

This passage raises a significant broader point: whenever a husband has had the opportunity to return and had explicitly put a *get* on hold, we face a potential concern — absent particular circumstances — that he may have returned and reconciled. This is not a trivial issue and applies across various scenarios.

### Shliach presence

The Gemara now raises a further procedural question: *ki mashvei bei dina shliach befanav v'shelo befanav* — when the *beis din* appoints a new *shliach* to replace the original one, must the first *shliach* be physically present at the time of that appointment? The Gemara then answers: *bein befanav bein shelo befanav* — whether in his presence or not. This ruling was also transmitted from Eretz Yisrael, confirming the same conclusion. The reasoning is clear: if the first *shliach* were himself a *shliach* of a *shliach*, one might think his presence necessary to formally demonstrate the transfer. But as established earlier, the *shliach* appointed by *beis din* is fundamentally the *shliach* of the husband — or at minimum the *shliach* of the *beis din* — and so there is no requirement for the first *shliach* to be present. The agency has been transferred to the *beis din*, and the *beis din* acts independently.

## Ones be-gitin

### Return condition

Having mentioned the case of "if I do not return within twelve months," the Gemara transitions into a self-contained sugya that also appears in Ketubot and elsewhere. The case: *hahu de'amar — ilu lo ateina taltin yomei, leihavei gita* — a man declared that his *get* should take effect if he does not return within thirty days. He then came back before the thirty days were up — *ata* — but *upaska mabara* — he found that the river crossing was blocked. The banks had risen and he could find no way to cross. As the deadline ran out, he called out from the other side: *chazu de'atai chazu de'atai* — "see, I'm here, I'm coming, I'm trying to return."

### Shmuel's ruling

Shmuel ruled: *lo shmei matya* — his calling out and trying to return does not count as having returned. The condition was formulated as "if I don't return," and he did not return. The *get* is therefore valid. His intention to return, his effort, his crying out — none of it satisfies the literal language of the condition.

### Ones doctrine

This ruling opens the sugya of *yesh ones be-gitin o ein ones be-gitin* — whether the doctrine of *ones* applies in the context of conditional divorces. The argument in favor of *ones* applying would run as follows: when a person frames a condition, he presumably means it to cover the range of situations he could foresee. If his intent was "if I willingly choose not to return, the *get* takes effect," then a case where he tried desperately to return but was physically prevented was never within the scope of what he meant. We ought to interpret his words in light of his evident intent.

Against this stands Shmuel's position: halacha must go by what a person says, not by the more nuanced intention one might retroactively attribute to him. If he wanted to exclude cases of *ones*, he should have said so explicitly. The condition as stated is unambiguous — he did not return.

### Reconciliation debate

A second case sharpens the debate. *Hahu gavra d'amar lehu — ee lo paisna lah tlasi yomin, leihavei gita* — a man told witnesses that if he does not reconcile with his wife within thirty days, the *get* should take effect. He then went to try to reconcile — *aza pisa velo ipisa* — but she refused to be reconciled.

Rav Yosef challenges the claim that this is a genuine *ones*: *mi yahiv lah tikra tarkava de-dinari velo paisa?* — did he send her a basket full of gold coins and she still refused? Perhaps he simply did not try hard enough. However, some report Rav Yosef's position in the opposite direction: *midi tarkava de-dinari baia u-maihava lah?* — does he have to give her a basket of gold coins? That would be excessive. He tried, and that should be sufficient.

Tosafot notes that even in the version where Rav Yosef questions whether he tried hard enough, the underlying point should really be: even if he had given her a basket of gold coins and she still refused, the condition still was not met. The issue is not the quality of the effort, but whether trying is sufficient at all.

### Ones conclusion

The Gemara concludes by mapping the two cases onto the two positions: *kad keman de'amar yesh ones be-gitin, ha keman de'amar ein ones be-gitin* — one case follows the view that *ones* is a valid consideration in *gitin*, the other follows the view that it is not. The question at stake is whether, when a person formulates a condition, we interpret that condition as implicitly excluding cases where he tried and was prevented — or whether we hold strictly to the letter of what was said. The sugya in Ketubot develops this at greater length; here we note the two positions and the cases that illustrate them.

## Teruma/Ma'aser loan

### Loan setup

The Gemara now turns to a Mishnah that, at first glance, has nothing to do with *gitin*. The connection lies in the theme of acting with a *chazakah* — a presumption — that someone is still alive, paralleling the earlier discussion of a *shliach* who delivers a *get* presuming that the husband remains living. This Mishnah introduces a range of similar scenarios and also serves as a bridge toward the next *perek*, which opens with a *get* case and then introduces the concept of *tikkun olam*, leading into two full chapters of material largely unrelated to *gitin* but connected through that broader idea.

The Mishnah: *hamalveh ma'os et ha-kohen ve'et ha-levi ve'et he-ani* — one who lends money to a *kohen*, a *levi*, or a poor person, with the following arrangement: the lender is a farmer who, after his crops come in, will separate *terumah* or *ma'aser* and designate it toward repayment of the loan, rather than physically delivering it to the *kohen* or *levi* and having it returned. Instead, he will simply separate it and retain it himself — since the debt is thereby offset, and there is no need to go through the formality of delivery and return.

The *halacha*: *mafrish aleihem b'chezkat she-hen kayamim* — he may go ahead and separate and retain it, acting on the presumption that the *kohen*, *levi*, or *ani* is still alive. He is not required to worry — *ve'eino choshesh shema meis ha-kohen o ha-levi o ha-ani o she'he'eshir he'ani* — that perhaps the *kohen* died, or the *levi* died, or the poor man died, or that the poor man became wealthy. If the *ani* became wealthy, he would no longer be eligible to receive *ma'aser ani*, and the arrangement would be undermined. Nevertheless, the lender may proceed on the presumption that the original circumstances still obtain.

### Separation mechanism

The mechanism works as follows. Rather than physically handing the *terumah* or *ma'aser* to the *kohen* or *levi* and having it handed back as repayment, the lender simply declares the separation mentally and retains the produce. In the case of *ma'aser ani*, the lender can himself eat *ma'aser* — it is not restricted to *kohanim* — and so the same logic applies. The arrangement was made in advance: when I separate, I keep it, and it counts as repayment of the debt.

### Presumption aliyah

The Mishnah permits this to proceed even when the *kohen*, *levi*, or *ani* is not present — operating on the *chazakah* that he is still alive, precisely as a *shliach* delivering a *get* presumes the husband still lives.

### Death of creditor

If, however, it becomes known that the *kohen*, *levi*, or *ani* has actually died, the situation becomes more complex. The question is whether the heirs assume the obligation. In general, heirs do not inherit the personal debts of their father as a personal obligation; rather, there is a *mitzvah al ha-yorshim liproa chov avihem* — a rabbinic obligation for heirs to repay their father's debts — but not a legally enforceable personal liability. If, however, there is a lien on inherited property — established through a *shtar chov* — then the lender retains rights against that property.

In the present case, since the arrangement was likely made orally rather than through a formal *shtar*, there probably would be no lien on the property. Nevertheless, the Mishnah provides a solution: if the heirs — who are themselves *kohanim* or *leviyim* — agree to honor their father's arrangement, the lender may retain the *terumah* or *ma'aser* as repayment. The mechanism is conceptually as though the lender gives the *terumah* to the heirs, and they return it as repayment. This requires the heirs' agreement — they are not legally obligated — but if they consent, the arrangement transfers smoothly. In the case of an *ani* who died, the same could apply in principle, though complications arise if the heirs are not themselves poor.

If the original loan was made *bifnei beis din* — in the presence of a *beis din* — the Mishnah rules *eino tzorich litol reshus min ha-yorshim*: the lender does not need the heirs' permission. The *beis din*, having official standing in matters of *dinei mammonos*, can in effect sanction the arrangement and speak on behalf of the heirs — analogous, in a certain way, to the institution of *prozbul*, which we will encounter later in Gittin.

### Possession problem

The Gemara raises a sharp challenge: *ve'af al gav de'lo asu le'yadei* — even though the *terumah* never came into the *kohen*'s possession — how does this arrangement work at all? There are two distinct problems. First, a straightforward *dinei mammonos* question: this *terumah* right now could be given to any *kohen*. How does this particular *kohen* acquire ownership over it such that the lender can then retain it as repayment of the debt? Second, there is a separate *mitzvah* of *nesina* — delivery — which requires actually handing the *terumah* to the *kohen*. When and how was that *mitzvah* ever fulfilled?

Rashi articulates both points explicitly: *maan zachye lei le-hai kohen* — who conferred ownership on this particular *kohen*? And *heicha yatza yedei nesina* — where did the lender ever fulfill the obligation of giving?

### Established families

Rav answers: *be-makirei kehuna u-leviya* — this applies to established families, where there is a longstanding and well-known practice of giving *terumah* and *ma'aser* to a specific household. In such a case, the very act of separating, given the firmly established pattern, is sufficient to confer ownership on that family automatically, even without a formal act of delivery. This was a known institution — families who had, across generations, given consistently to a particular *kohen* family or *levi* family. In that context, separation alone suffices.

### Third party

Shmuel offers a different explanation: *be-mezakeh lahem al yedei acheirim* — the lender calls over a third party, asks him to take possession of the *terumah* on behalf of the *kohen* or *levi*, and then the third party transfers it back to the lender as repayment. This fulfills both the ownership transfer and the act of giving, through the mechanism of *zechiyah* — acquiring on behalf of another. Shmuel's arrangement is simply more practical in cases where the *kohen* or *levi* is not nearby.

### Asu institution

Ulla offers a third explanation: *ha mani Rabbi Yossi hi — de'amar asu et she'eino zacheh ke'zacheh* — this Mishnah follows the view of Rabbi Yossi, who held that the Rabbis enacted that a person who has not formally acquired something is treated as if he has, in order to allow this institution to function. Ulla's explanation is frankly rabbinic: the Rabbis recognized that technically the *kohen* or *levi* does not own the *terumah* or *ma'aser*, but they enacted that it should be treated as though they did, in order to make this system viable.

The institution is in everyone's interest. The *kohen*, *levi*, or *ani* benefits enormously: they can borrow money and have it repaid with something they would likely have received anyway — or perhaps even something they would not have received had the lender not agreed to this arrangement. It is, in effect, free money. The lender benefits as well: he has confidence that his loan will be repaid, and the terms — as we will see — are quite favorable to him. The Rabbis therefore greased the wheels, as it were, and declared that the mechanics of *kinyan* are satisfied even without a formal act of delivery.

The Gemara notes that none of the three explanations fully satisfies the commentators' preference. *Ke-Rav lo amri* — Rav's explanation of *makirei kehuna* is not mentioned in the Mishnah. *Ke-Shmuel lo amri* — the third-party mechanism is also not mentioned. *Ke-Ulla nami lo amri* — why not like Ulla? Because *bi'yechida lo mokminan* — we prefer not to establish a Mishnah as following a single minority opinion. Nevertheless, one way or another, the institution of ownership transfer must be operative; the debate concerns only the precise mechanism.

A *baraita* then repeats essentially the same case, adding the detail of *posekin imahen ke-sha'ar ha-zol* — the exchange rate to be used when calculating repayment.

### Exchange rate

The *baraita* states: *ufosek imahem ke-sha'ar ha-zol* — one stipulates with them according to the lowest market rate. That is, when calculating how much produce the lender retains as repayment, the parties agree that the produce is valued at the lowest prevailing market price. If wheat, for instance, fluctuates between one and two dollars per bushel over the course of the season, the agreement stipulates the one-dollar rate. This maximizes the quantity the lender can retain — a hundred bushels repays a hundred dollars at one dollar per bushel, which at the higher market price might actually be worth two hundred dollars.

The Gemara initially observes *peshita* — this is obvious, since there is already a Mishnah in Bava Metzia permitting such arrangements. The underlying logic treats this less as a loan and more as a pre-purchase or forward transaction: the lender is in some sense pre-acquiring the *terumah* or *ma'aser* that will later be separated. The Gemara clarifies that the *chidush* is the implicit presumption: even if the parties did not explicitly fix the exchange rate, we assume the lowest market rate applies, since that is what benefits the lender and incentivizes the arrangement.

### Ribbis exemption

The *baraita* states: *ve-ein bo mishum ribbit* — there is no concern of *ribbit* in this arrangement. Even if the lender ends up retaining produce worth considerably more than the original loan — because the market price rose while the exchange rate was fixed at the low end — this does not constitute forbidden interest.

Tosafot extends this further: not only does the *sha'ar ha-zol* rule not raise a *ribbit* issue, but even if the parties agree to an exchange rate lower than anything the market actually reached — a rate more favorable to the lender than any prevailing price — there is still no *ribbit* violation. The Rabbis permitted this generously in order to make the institution as attractive as possible to both parties.

The Gemara explains the exemption: *keivan de-ki leis lei lo yahiv lei* — since if the crop fails entirely and there is nothing to separate, the borrower has no obligation to repay the loan at all. This fundamentally distinguishes the arrangement from a standard loan. In an ordinary loan, if the borrower cannot pay, the debt still exists. Here, if there is no crop, there is no debt. That feature makes the arrangement structurally closer to a forward sale than to a loan, and it is precisely that feature which removes the *ribbit* concern. *Ki it lei nami, ein bo mishum ribbit* — when the crop does come in and the lender retains more than he lent, we analyze it as the profit of a sale, not as interest on a loan.

### Shemita exception

The *baraita* further states: *ve-ein shevi'it meshamtattu* — the *shemita* year does not annul this debt. Normally, *shemita* annuls loans by prohibiting the creditor from pressing the borrower for repayment, which ultimately renders the debt uncollectable. Here, however, the borrower is essentially out of the picture — there is no creditor pressing anyone. The lender will simply separate *terumah* and retain it himself. Since the mechanism of *shemita*'s annulment — *lo yigos* — requires active pressure on a debtor, and no such pressure exists in this arrangement, *shemita* does not apply.

### Retraction rules

The *baraita* then states: *ve-im ba lachazor eino chozer* — if one wants to retract, he cannot. The Gemara, following Rav Pappa, clarifies which party is being addressed. The *ba'al ha-bayis* — the lender — cannot retract from the arrangement. But *aval kohen be-ba'al ha-bayis, im ba lachazor chozer* — the *kohen* can retract.

The reasoning draws on the principle that in a normal transaction, if the buyer has paid but has not yet taken possession of the object, he may retract — the transaction is not complete. The Gemara applies a parallel logic here. From the *ba'al ha-bayis*'s perspective, his side of the transaction is complete: he lent the money, and there is nothing further for him to do. He cannot claim that his act is unfinished and seek to reverse it. From the *kohen*'s perspective, however, his side of the transaction — the act of receiving the *terumah* and having it returned as repayment — is not yet complete. His portion of the transaction remains open, and that incompleteness gives him a degree of flexibility to withdraw.

Rashi formulates it this way: the *ba'al ha-bayis* has discharged his obligation on his end; the *kohen* has not yet fulfilled his end, and therefore retains the ability to reverse course. A student raised a helpful way of putting it: the lender has lent the money; the *kohen* effectively holds an option of repaying through this mechanism, but until the separation and retention actually occur, that side of the transaction is not complete, and the option remains his to exercise or not.

If the *kohen* does retract, the lender is entitled to a *mi she-para* — a rabbinic censure — but no more.

### Ye'ush concept

The *baraita* also states: *nityashu ha-be'alim, ein mafrishin aleihem* — if the lender has despaired of collecting, he may no longer use the *terumah* or *ma'aser* toward repayment of the debt. *Mipnei she-hu mafrish al ha-avud* — because he would be separating in relation to something already lost.

This is a surprising ruling. Normally, *ye'ush* — despair — does not in itself extinguish a debt. If a borrower absconds, the lender's despair does not cancel what is owed. What makes this case different is the concrete, specific nature of the anticipated payment. The repayment here was not a general monetary obligation but was tied to a particular object — the *terumah* and *ma'aser* from this lender's crop. When the lender despairs and considers that produce lost, he has effectively relinquished his claim on that specific object as a vehicle of repayment. It is a *ye'ush* directed at the thing itself, not merely at the hope of collection. Once that despair is registered, even if the crop recovers, the lender can no longer use it as repayment.

The Gemara further clarifies that this ruling is needed even in a case where the grain was already standing in the straw when it was damaged — one might have thought that such grain, which often recovers, was never truly lost, and that the lender's despair was therefore premature and ineffective. The Gemara teaches that even so, if the lender genuinely despaired, that *ye'ush* takes effect and forfeits his right to use that produce as repayment.

### Beis din takana

A *baraita* then cites Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: *hamalveh ma'os le-kohen u'le-levi bifnei beis din u'meisu, mafrish aleihem b'chezkas oso ha-shevet* — if the loan was arranged before a *beis din* and the *kohen* or *levi* subsequently died, the lender may still separate and retain the *terumah* or *ma'aser* by virtue of the status of that entire tribe. He does not need the heirs' permission.

The logic here is institutional. When the original arrangement was made formally and publicly before a *beis din*, the *beis din* implicitly speaks on behalf of the *kohanim* and *leviyim* as a class. The broader community of *kohanim* and *leviyim* has an interest in this institution functioning — after all, the ability to borrow money and have it repaid through *terumah* and *ma'aser* is a tremendous benefit for them. The *beis din*, acting in their collective interest, can therefore authorize the lender to retain the *terumah* even when the original *kohen* has died, without needing the specific heirs to consent.

A version in the Tosefta reads not *b'chezkat oso ha-shevet* but *bi-reshus oso ha-shevet* — by the permission of that tribe — capturing more explicitly the idea that the *beis din* speaks with the implicit authorization of the *kohanim* or *leviyim* as a group.

For an *ani*: *mafrish aleihem b'chezkat aniyei Yisrael* — one may presume on behalf of the poor of Israel generally. Rabbi Akiva extends this further: *b'chezkat aniyei olam* — on behalf of the poor of the world. The practical difference, as the Gemara notes, concerns *aniyei Kuti* — Samaritan poor in the town. If one holds like *aniyei Yisrael*, one speaks on behalf of Jewish poor specifically; if there are no Jewish poor in the town and only Samaritan poor, it may not work in certain configurations. Rabbi Akiva's formulation, reaching to *aniyei olam*, ensures that Jewish poor elsewhere can also serve as the relevant class.

### Death vs. wealth

The *baraita* adds: *he'eshir he'ani, ein mafrish alav* — if the *ani* became wealthy, the arrangement no longer applies, even when the loan was made before a *beis din*. The *beis din* speaks on behalf of the poor as a class in order to sustain this institution — but only in the case of death. If the *ani* merely got rich, the lender cannot retain the *ma'aser ani* and consider the debt repaid.

Why does the rabbinic enactment cover death but not wealth? The Gemara answers: *misa shchicha, ashirus lo shchicha* — death is common; getting rich is not. The Rabbis constructed the institution to address predictable and frequent occurrences. Death is universal and inevitable; the Rabbis therefore made provision for it, allowing the institution to continue functioning when the *kohen*, *levi*, or *ani* dies. But enrichment of a poor man is unusual enough that the Rabbis did not extend the *takkanah* to cover it. Rav Pappa encapsulates this with a folk saying: if you hear that your friend died, you can take it as true — as the *chazakah* holds, people do die. But if you hear that your friend got rich, do not be so quick to believe it.

### Heirs and land

The Mishnah had stated that if the *kohen* or *levi* dies, the lender must obtain permission from the heirs — *tzorich litol reshus min ha-yorshim*. A *baraita* citing Rebbi then stipulates: this applies to heirs who inherited — *yorshim she-yarshu*. The Gemara asks: are there heirs who did not inherit? Rabbi Yochanan clarifies: *yorshim she-yarshu karka v'lo she-yarshu kesafim* — heirs who inherited land, not merely cash. Only when there is actual landed property is there a lien upon which a debt can be collected; and only then is the permission of the heirs relevant in this framework.

Rabbi Yochanan then rules: *hiniah m'lo machat gove m'lo machat, m'lo kardom gove m'lo kardom* — if the deceased left an estate worth only a pin, the lender may collect only a pin's worth; if worth a hammer, he collects a hammer's worth. One can collect only proportionally to the actual land.

However, a further ruling — associated with the story of the small fields of Abaye in Ketubot — holds that even if the estate is tiny, one can collect the full debt through repeated cycles: take the land, accept the heirs' cash payment to redeem the land, apply that cash as partial payment of the debt, take the land again, and so on. Since this entire framework is a *takkanah d'rabbanan* aimed at making the institution work, Tosafot reads this position as applying even where the heirs have not formally consented — where there is a lien, the *beis din*'s institution ensures the debt can ultimately be collected even from a small estate.
