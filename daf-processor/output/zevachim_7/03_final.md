# Zevachim 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Se'irim/Shavuot

### Why Two Se'irim?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֵין נִפְדִּין תְּמִימִים, לְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים נִפְדִּין תְּמִימִין!
>
> **Translation:** **according to the statement of Rabbi Shimon, they may not be redeemed** as long as they are **unblemished,** since they retain the status of offerings. But **according to the statement of the Rabbis, they may be redeemed** even when they are **unblemished,** as the court initially stipulates that if they are not needed they will not assume the status of an offering. Apparently Rabbi Shimon does not accept the notion that the court can stipulate that there be a delay in the consecration of communal offerings, and the *baraita* can be adduced as evidence that offerings atone for transgressions committed after their designation as offerings.

The discussion opens at the end of Daf 6, in the context of the two different *se'irei chatat* brought on *Shavuot*. One *se'ir chatat* is brought as part of the regular *korban musaf*, and another is brought in connection with the *shtei halechem*. This raises a natural question: why are two distinct *korbanot chatat* necessary? One proposed rationale is that the second *se'ir* is needed to provide *kapparah* for *aveirot* that may have occurred during the interval between the *hafrashah* — the initial designation of the animal — and the actual *shechitah*.

### Designation at Shechitah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאַפְרְשִׁינְהוּ בְּזֶה אַחַר זֶה
>
> **Translation:** and an incident involving impurity may have occurred between their respective designations.

This explanation, however, is complicated by a teaching cited from the end of Daf 6: *de'amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel korbanot tzibbur tzrichin moshachtan lema shehen* — for communal sacrifices, it is the act of *shechitah* itself that finally designates them to their sacred status. If that is the case, there would be no meaningful gap period between the designation and the *shechitah*, and thus no window during which additional *aveirot* could occur that would require a second *chatat*.

### Beit Din Matneh/Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב פָּפָּא: וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לֵב בֵּית דִּין מַתְנֶה עֲלֵיהֶן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef, son of Rav Shmuel, said to Rav Pappa: But does Rabbi Shimon accept** the opinion that **the court can make a non-verbal stipulation about** communal offerings?

This leads Rav Yosef the son of Rav Shmuel to challenge Rav Pappa: *u'mi it leh leRabbi Shimon lev Beit Din matneh aleihem?* — Does Rabbi Shimon actually subscribe to the principle that the *Beit Din* makes a conditional designation that only takes effect at the moment of *shechitah*? The answer turns on a teaching transmitted through a long chain of authorities.

### Temidin/Leftover Sheep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תְּמִידִין שֶׁלֹּא הוּצְרְכוּ לַצִּבּוּר
>
> **Translation:** **Doesn't Rav Idi bar Avin say** that **Rav Amram says** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to animals designated as **daily offerings** but **which** in the end **were not necessary for** use by **the public,**

Rabbi Yochanan's teaching is introduced via Rav Iddi bar Avin, Rav Amram, and Rav Yitzchak: the case of *temidin shelo hutzrechu latzibbur* — the sheep purchased and designated for the *korban tamid* of a given year that turn out to be surplus by the end of the year. Once *Rosh Chodesh Nisan* arrives, the *tamid* must be funded from the newly collected *shekalim*, and so the leftover animals become superfluous. The debate between Rabbi Shimon and the *Chachamim* concerns what happens to these animals: according to Rabbi Shimon, *ein nifdin temimim* — they cannot be redeemed while still unblemished; according to the *Chachamim*, *nifdin temimim* — they may be redeemed even in an unblemished state.

### Gap Period/Averot

The crucial inference is that Rabbi Shimon does *not* hold of *lev Beit Din matneh aleihem* — he does not regard the *hafrashah* as retroactively delayed until *shechitah*. Instead, Rabbi Shimon recognizes a genuine gap period between the time the *korban* is designated and the time it is actually slaughtered. It is precisely this gap that creates the theoretical need for a second *se'ir chatat* on *Shavuot* — to provide *kapparah* for *aveirot* that might occur during that window.

That said, the obvious answer remains that the *Torah* simply commands two *se'irim*, each accompanying a different element of the *musaf* structure of *Shavuot*, and there is no intrinsic need to find a further rationale. Indeed, it is worth noting that every *korban musaf* — with the exception of *Shabbat* — comes with a *chatat*. One might speculate that the *chatat* is not necessarily tied to some specific *cheit* but perhaps reflects a kind of trepidation about the act of bringing *korbanot* on the holidays themselves, or relates to the purifying function that *chatat* can also serve — as extensively discussed at the beginning of *Masechet Shevuot* — to cleanse the *mikdash* and the *mizbe'ach*.

## Se'irei Atzeret

### Question About 2nd Se'ir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מֵרַבִּי זֵירָא: שְׂעִירֵי עֲצֶרֶת שֶׁקִּבֵּל דָּמָן בִּשְׁנֵי כּוֹסוֹת, וְנִזְרַק דָּמוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן; שֵׁנִי לָמָה הוּא בָּא? עַל טוּמְאָה שֶׁאוֹרְעָה בֵּין [זְרִיקָה שֶׁל] זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** continued Rav Yosef, son of Rav Shmuel, one can otherwise infer from the goats sacrificed on *Shavuot* that an offering can atone for transgressions committed after designation; as **Rabbi Yirmeya asked Rabbi Zeira:** If the **goats** sacrificed on ***Shavuot*** were slaughtered simultaneously and **their blood was collected in two cups, and the blood of** the **first** goat **was sprinkled, for what** sin **is** the **second** goat **brought?** There was no time in the interim for other transgressions to occur. Rabbi Zeira answered: It atones **for** any incident involving **impurity that occurred between the sprinkling** of the blood **of that** first goat **and** the sprinkling of the blood of **this** second goat.

The *sugya* continues with a question posed by Rabbi Yirmeyah to Rabbi Zeira: *she'irei Atzeret shekibel danam bishnei kosot, venizrak dam shel rishon — sheni lama hu ba?* If the blood of the two *se'irim* on *Shavuot* was collected in two separate vessels and the blood of the first was already sprinkled, why is the second *se'ir* still needed?

### Simultaneous Shechita

Rashi explains the premise: the two goats were slaughtered simultaneously — *shnei kosot nishchatin ke'echad* — so there was no opportunity for a *tumah* or *aveirah* to occur between the *shechitah* of one and the *shechitah* of the other. Given that, once the *zerikah* of the first is complete, what purpose does the second serve?

### Gap in Zerikat Hadam

The answer given is: *al tumah she'ire'ah bein zerikah shel zeh lazeh* — the second *se'ir* provides *kapparah* for *tumah* that might arise between the *zerikat hadam* of the first *chatat* and the *zerikat hadam* of the second. The window of concern has now shifted: it is not between *shechitah* and *zerikah* that we worry, but between the two acts of *zerikah* themselves.

This is a striking observation. It shows that *zerikat hadam* is not merely a procedural step but is itself a primary locus of *kapparah* — it is the blood, as the life-force of the animal, that effects the atonement. The fact that a gap between two *zerikos* can generate the need for an additional *korban* underscores the independent significance of *zerikah* as a time-point for *kapparah*.

### Unresolved Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא ״אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר״ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Perhaps** Rabbi Yirmeya's dilemma was also with regard to the violations committed after designation. He phrased his question to apply to violations committed after slaughter because **he was saying:** Even **if you say** that an offering atones for violations committed after designation, does it atone for those violations committed after slaughter? It may be that neither issue was clear to him.

However, the *sugya* is left unresolved. As the *Gemara* itself notes with the phrase *dilma im timtzi lomar ka'amar*, the answer about the gap between the two *zerikos* does not fully address the question of what happens in the case of a *tumah* that occurred after *hafrashah* alone — that is, before *shechitah* had even taken place. The *sugya* is left open.

### Time Points/Kapparah

What emerges from this entire discussion is a clarification of the key time-points relevant to a *korban*: *hafrashah*, *shechitah*, and *zerikat hadam* each play a distinct and significant role. At the level of practical halacha, the real reason for the two *se'irim* on *Shavuot* is almost certainly simply the *Torah*'s requirement. But in probing *why* the *Torah* might have structured things this way, the *Gemara* opens a window into the internal logic of *kapparah* and the spiritual significance of each stage of the sacrificial process.

## Korban Todah

### Todah Intro/Short Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: תּוֹדָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם תּוֹדַת חֲבֵירוֹ (כְּשֵׁרָה) – רַבָּה אָמַר: כְּשֵׁרָה, רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** with regard to **a thanks offering that** one **slaughtered for the sake of another's thanks offering: Rabba says** that it is **fit** and satisfies its owner's obligation, and **Rav Ḥisda says** it is **unfit** in this regard.

The *sugya* now turns to the *korban todah*, which occupies the bulk of this *amud*. The *korban todah* is a subcategory of *korban shelamim*, but it differs from a regular *shelamim* in one critical respect: its eating window is dramatically shorter. A standard *shelamim* may be eaten for close to two full days; a *korban todah* must be consumed only until midnight of the night following the day it is brought. Moreover, the *korban todah* is a large animal — typically a cow — and is brought together with forty loaves of bread: thirty loaves of *matzah* of three varieties and ten loaves of *chametz*, making it one of only two *korbanot* brought in conjunction with *chametz*.

The shortened deadline to midnight is, in fact, a rabbinic stringency. The Gemara elsewhere records the position of Rabban Gamliel — cited in the very first *mishnah* in *Shas* — that the midnight limit was instituted as a *chumra*, and that *b'dieved* one has until *alot hashachar*.

### Todah/Todah Interchange

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר כְּשֵׁרָה – תּוֹדָה לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה נִשְׁחֲטָה. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר פְּסוּלָה – לְשׁוּם שְׁלָמִים דִּידֵיהּ נִשְׁחֲטָה בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says** it is **fit** because the **thanks offering was slaughtered for the sake of a thanks offering. Rav Ḥisda says** it is **unfit** because **it must be slaughtered for the sake of his own peace offering.**

The central question that now arises from the *mishnah* is about the interplay between *korban todah* and *korban shelamim* when there is a *shelo lishmah* designation. The specific case introduced is: *todah sheshachatah leshem todat chaveiro* — what if a *kohen* performing the *shechitah* of one person's *todah* has in mind the *todah* of a different person?

### Shinui Kodesh

The analysis of this case turns on a careful reading of Rashi's interpretation. He writes: *lo sheshachatah al menat sheyitkaper bah mi she'eino ba'aleha* — this is not a case of *shinui ba'alim* at all. Rather, he classifies it as *shinui kodesh* — a change in the sacred designation of the offering. As he elaborates: the scenario is that Reuven has his own *todah* and Shimon has his own *todah* for different reasons, and the *kohen* slaughtered one with the designation of the other in mind — not intending that the wrong person be atoned for, but that the sacred character of one animal be transferred to the other. Rashi adds that the same analysis applies to a *kohen* who slaughters a *chatat chelev* with a *chatat dam* in mind.

Rashi's reading is admittedly difficult and somewhat counterintuitive — the plain sense of the case seems to involve *shinui ba'alim*. But Rashi deliberately avoids that categorization, and as will become clear from the subsequent *amoraic* debate, the underlying question turns on *shinui kodesh*.

### Rabba/Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וּבְשַׂר זֶבַח תּוֹדַת שְׁלָמָיו בְּיוֹם הַקְרִיבוֹ אֶת זִבְחוֹ וְגוֹ׳״ – אַבָּא חָנִין אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, בָּא לְלַמֵּד: תּוֹדָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם שְׁלָמִים – כְּשֵׁרָה, שְׁלָמִים שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה – פְּסוּלִים. וּמָה הֶפְרֵשׁ בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה? תּוֹדָה קְרוּיָה שְׁלָמִים, וְאֵין שְׁלָמִים קְרוּיִין תּוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: From where do I say** that it is fit? It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And the meat of the sacrifice of his thanksgiving peace offering**…**on the day that he presents his offering** it shall be eaten" (Leviticus 7:15–16). **Abba Ḥanin said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer:** The verse **comes to teach** that **a thanks offering that one slaughtered for the sake of a peace offering** is **fit,** whereas **a peace offering that was slaughtered for the sake of a thanks offering** is **unfit. And what** is the **difference between this** offering **and that** offering? **A thanks offering is called a peace offering** in the verse, **but a peace offering is not called a thanks offering.**

The *amoraic* dispute is as follows: Rabba says the *korban* is *kesheirah* — and by *kesheirah* is meant not merely that the animal retains its sanctity but that it counts *alu l'ba'alim l'shem chovah*, it fulfills the owner's obligation. Rav Chisda says it is *psula* — the *korban* is technically valid but does not discharge the owner's obligation. The reasoning of each position is clear: Rabba holds that *todah leshem todah nishchatah* — it was designated as a *todah*, and that is sufficient. Rav Chisda counters — *leshem shelamim didei nishchatah ba'inan* — the *shechitah* must be oriented toward the specific *shelamim* that belongs to this particular owner.

### Todah/Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלָמִים לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה – פְּסוּלָה, הָא תּוֹדָה לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה – כְּשֵׁרָה; מַאי, לָאו דְּחַבְרֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba explains: The *baraita* indicates that **a peace offering** that was slaughtered **for the sake of a thanks offering** is **unfit, but a thanks offering** that was slaughtered **for the sake of** some other **thanks offering** is **fit. What, is it not** referring to the thanks offering **of another,** teaching that it satisfies its owner's obligation?

Rabba supports his position from a *Tannaitic* teaching. The pasuk *uvesar zevach todat shelamov beyom korbano yacheil* is cited, and Abba Chanin in the name of Rabbi Eliezer teaches: *todah sheshachatah leshem shelamim, kesheirah* — if a *todah* was slaughtered with a *shelamim* in mind, it is valid. Conversely, *shelamim shenishchatu leshem todah psulim* — a regular *shelamim* slaughtered with a *todah* in mind is disqualified. The asymmetry is explained: since *todah* is a subcategory of *shelamim*, having *shelamim* in mind when slaughtering a *todah* is simply thinking of a broader category that includes the *todah* itself — and that is permissible. But designating a *shelamim* as a *todah* means assigning it a more specific status it does not possess, which disqualifies it.

### Rava's Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is referring to another thanks offering **of his own,** which he designated to give thanks for another incident of deliverance.

From this *Tannaitic* discussion, Rava draws an inference: if *todah leshem todah kesherah* — meaning even if the *kohen* had in mind a *todah* that was not specifically the one being slaughtered — then this holds even where two different owners are involved. For if we have already learned that a *todah* being mentally exchanged with a *shelamim* is valid in one direction, and the case of *todah leshem todah* was left as an implicit conclusion of the *midrash*, then evidently *todah leshem todah* — even another person's — is *kesheirah*.

### Rav Chisda Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל דְּחַבְרֵיהּ מַאי – פְּסוּלָה? אַדְּתָנֵי ״שְׁלָמִים לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה פְּסוּלָה״, לִיתְנֵי ״תּוֹדָה לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה״ – וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שְׁלָמִים לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if one slaughtered his thanks offering for the sake of **another's** thanks offering, **what** is the *halakha*? Is it **unfit,** i.e., does it not satisfy the owner's obligation? If so, **rather than teaching** that **a peace offering** slaughtered **for the sake of a thanks offering** is **unfit, let** the *baraita* **teach** that **a thanks offering** that was slaughtered **for the sake of** another's **thanks offering** is unfit, **and** one could infer that **all the more so a peace offering** slaughtered **for the sake of a thanks offering** is unfit.

Rav Chisda, however, rejects this inference. He argues that the reference to *todah leshem todah* in that *Tannaitic* teaching concerns only one person who designated multiple *todot* — not two different owners. The *limud* is thus necessary to teach something else entirely. One might have thought, argues Rav Chisda, that since the *Torah* itself calls a *todah* a *shelamim*, the terms are fully interchangeable — that a *todah* and a *shelamim* are legally identical in all respects, differing only incidentally in the length of the eating window and the accompanying loaves. The teaching therefore comes to negate that assumption: a *todah* and a *shelamim* are genuinely distinct categories. But it does not follow that two different owners' *todot* are interchangeable with one another.

Rav Chisda's final defense is compelling: had the *midrash* intended to teach about two different *todot* belonging to different owners, it should have stated *todah leshem todah* explicitly alongside *shelamim leshem todah*. Since it did not, the exclusion of that case is deliberate — and *Rabba*'s inference fails. The *halacha* regarding the interchange of two different people's *todot* thus remains the subject of unresolved *amoraic* dispute.

## Rava/Chatat Teachings

### Chatat/Chatat vs. Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת – כְּשֵׁירָה, לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה – פְּסוּלָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתָהּ לְחַטָּאת״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וַהֲרֵי חַטָּאת לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת נִשְׁחֲטָה. לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: A sin offering that one slaughtered for the sake of** another **sin offering** he was obligated to bring is **fit;** but if one slaughtered it **for the sake of a burnt offering** it is **unfit. What is the reason** for this distinction? **The Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And slaughter it for a sin offering"** (Leviticus 4:33), **and** therefore, as long as the **sin offering was slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering,** even another sin offering, it is fit. But if it was slaughtered **for the sake of a burnt offering** it is **unfit.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a series of brief, sequential teachings attributed to Rava. The first: *chatat sheshachatah leshem chatat, kesheirah; leshem olah, psula* — a *chatat* slaughtered with a *chatat* in mind is valid; slaughtered with an *olah* in mind, it is disqualified. This appears redundant given what the *mishnah* already stated, but the *Gemara* anchors it in a *pasuk*: the Torah says *veshachat otah l'chatat* — the *shechitah* of the *chatat* must be performed as a *chatat*. And so it follows directly that having an *olah* in mind invalidates it.

### Shinui Ba'alim Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת – פְּסוּלָה. עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב עוֹלָה – כְּשֵׁרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו״ – עָלָיו וְלֹא עַל חֲבֵירוֹ; חֲבֵירוֹ דּוּמְיָא דִידֵיהּ, בִּמְחוּיָּיב כַּפָּרָה כְּמוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: A sin offering that one slaughtered for** a person other than its owner, but **who** nevertheless was **obligated** to bring **a sin offering,** is **unfit.** But if one slaughtered it **for** one **who** was **obligated** to bring **a burnt offering,** it is **fit. What is the reason** for this distinction? The Torah states: **"And he shall be forgiven"** (Leviticus 4:26), indicating that it must be specifically **he,** the owner, **who** shall be forgiven, **and not another** person. **The other** person to whom this is referring, with regard to whom the offering is disqualified, is presumably **similar to** the owner **in** that **he is** also **obligated** to achieve **atonement similar to** the owner's by bringing a sin offering. Whereas if the other person is obligated to bring a burnt offering, and not a sin offering, this disqualification does not apply.

The second teaching: *chatat sheshachatah al mi shemechuyav chatat, psula; al mi shemechuyav olah, kesheirah*. If I slaughter a *chatat* on behalf of another person who is likewise obligated to bring a *chatat*, it is disqualified. But if that other person is obligated only in an *olah* — meaning he has violated a *mitzvat aseh* rather than an *issur kareit* — then the *chatat* remains valid. The *Gemara* explains through the principle of *vekipper alav* — the *Torah* says the *chatat* must atone *for him*, which excludes others — but only *chavero hadomeh lo*, someone comparable to him in his obligation to bring a *chatat*. One who is not *mechuyav chatat* at all is outside that category and creates no disqualifying interference.

### Aino Mechuyav Klum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּיָּיב כְּלוּם – פְּסוּלָה, שֶׁאֵין לְךָ אָדָם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּיָּיב עֲשֵׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: A sin offering that one slaughtered for** a person **who is not obligated** to bring **anything** is **unfit, as there is no person among the Jewish people who is not obligated** to bring an offering for violating **a positive mitzva.** Therefore, the one for whom he slaughtered the offering is considered one who was obligated to bring a sin offering.

The third teaching: *chatat sheshachatah al mi she'aino mechuyav klum, psula* — a *chatat* slaughtered on behalf of someone who has no obligation whatsoever is disqualified. However, Rava immediately qualifies this: *she'ein lecha adam b'Yisrael she'eino mechuyav aseh* — there is no one in Israel who has not at some point violated a *mitzvat aseh*. Therefore, no one is truly *aino mechuyav klum*, and the case of disqualification is purely theoretical.

### Chatat/Aseh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: חַטָּאת מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל חַיָּיבֵי עֲשֵׂה מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר – עַל חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת מְכַפֶּרֶת, עַל חַיָּיבֵי עֲשֵׂה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says** that **a sin offering atones for those obligated** to bring a burnt offering for violating **a positive mitzva, due to an *a fortiori*** inference: If a sin offering **atones for those who are liable** for transgressions for which one receives **excision from the World-to-Come** if he performs them intentionally, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that it atones **for those liable** for failing to fulfill **a positive mitzva?**

The fourth teaching of Rava: *chatat mechaperet al chayavei aseh* — a *chatat* can provide *kapparah* even for violations of positive commandments, derived through a *kal vachomer*. Ordinarily, one would bring an *olah* in connection with a *mitzvat aseh* violation. But the *chatat*, which possesses greater atoning power, can also *mechapper*, at least partially — *mikupya mechapper* — for one who is *mechuyav aseh*. This follows directly from the preceding teaching that a *chatat* brought for someone *mechuyav olah* is *kesheirah*: since such a person's obligation is predicated on having violated a *mitzvat aseh*, the *chatat* retroactively provides at least partial *kapparah* for that *aseh* violation.

## Olah Shelo Lishmah

### Shelo Lishmah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: עוֹלָה שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ – אָסוּר לִזְרוֹק דָּמָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** As for **a burnt offering that one slaughtered not for its sake, it is** still **prohibited to sprinkle its blood not for its sake.**

The *Gemara* now addresses the *olah* brought *shelo lishmah*: *olah sheshachatah shelo lishmah — asur lizrok dama shelo lishmah*. That is, if the *shechitah* of an *olah* was performed without the proper *lishma* intention, it is prohibited to then perform the subsequent *zerikah* also *shelo lishmah*. One must do the best one can to restore proper intention at each remaining stage of the *avodah*.

### Two Justifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא: ״מוֹצָא שְׂפָתֶיךָ תִּשְׁמֹר וְגוֹ׳״. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּשַׁנִּי בַּהּ כּוּ׳, כִּדְרֵישׁ פִּירְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** Why is this so? **If you wish, cite a verse;** and **if you wish, propose a logical argument.** The Gemara elaborates: **If you wish, cite a verse: "That which has gone out of your lips you shall observe** and do; according to what you have vowed freely to the Lord your God, even that which you have promised with your mouth" (Deuteronomy 23:24). The derivation from this verse was explained in 2a. **If you wish, propose a logical argument:** Just **because** one **deviated** from protocol **in its** slaughter, shall he continue to deviate from protocol in the rest of the sacrificial process? This is **as** it was stated at **the beginning of the chapter** (2a).

Two independent justifications are offered for this rule. The first is based on the *pasuk*: *motza sefatecha tishmor ve'asita* — one must take care to fulfill what has emerged from one's lips, and since you designated this as an *olah*, each action must be done with that *olah* designation in mind. The second is based on *svara* — sheer logic — *mishum de'shanya bah*: you should perform each step of the *avodah* as correctly as possible. It is notable that both the *kra* and the *svara* converge here, as the *Gemara* itself cross-references this rule to an earlier discussion at the beginning of the *masechet*.

### Olah/After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: עוֹלָה הַבָּאָה לְאַחַר מִיתָה; שְׁחָטָהּ בְּשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ – פְּסוּלָה, בְּשִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים – כְּשֵׁרָה, דְּאֵין בְּעָלִים לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** With regard to **a burnt offering that is brought** by its owner's heirs **after** his **death,** if one **slaughtered it with a deviation** from the type of **offering,** i.e., for the sake of another type of offering, it is **unfit,** i.e., it does not satisfy the owner's obligation, and the heirs must therefore bring another offering. But if it was slaughtered **with a deviation** with regard to the **owner,** i.e., for the sake of another person, it is **fit, as** the offering **has no** legal **owner after** the owner's **death.**

Another teaching of Rava: *olah haba'ah le'achar mitah sheshachatah beshinui kodesh, pesulah* — an *olah* brought after the owner's death, if slaughtered with a *shinui kodesh* (the wrong sacred designation in mind), is disqualified, and the heirs must bring another. But *beshinui ba'alim, kesheirah* — if brought with a different *ba'al* in mind, it is valid — *de'ein ba'alim le'achar mitah*. Once the owner has died, the very concept of *ba'alim* no longer applies in the same way. The heirs have an obligation to bring the designated *korban*, but the requirement that the *shechitah* be oriented toward the specific owner is dissolved by the owner's death.

This ruling opens a broader conceptual question about the nature of *hekdesh*. Two fundamentally different types exist: *kedushat damim*, where the animal's monetary value is consecrated to the *Beit HaMikdash* as corporate property, and *kedushat haguf*, where the animal itself is designated to be brought as a *korban* but continues in a meaningful sense to "belong" to the owner, who bears the obligation to bring it. The animal designated as a *korban* has *kedushat haguf* — it belongs to the owner even as he is obligated to bring it to the *mizbe'ach*.

### Owner/After Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב פִּנְחָס בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי אָמַר: יֵשׁ בְּעָלִים לְאַחַר מִיתָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב פִּנְחָס בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי: דַּוְקָא קָאָמַר מָר יֵשׁ בְּעָלִים לְאַחַר מִיתָה – וּבָעֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי עוֹלָה אַחֲרִיתִי; אוֹ דִּלְמָא, דְּאִי אִיכָּא כַּמָּה עֲשֵׂה גַּבֵּיהּ מְכַפְּרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Pineḥas, son of Rav Ami, says** that an offering **has an owner after** its owner's **death,** specifically, the heir. **Rav Ashi said to Rav Pineḥas, son of Rav Ami: Is the Master saying** that an offering **actually has an owner after** its owner's **death, and** that if it was slaughtered for the sake of the wrong owner, the heir **must bring another burnt offering,** contrary to the opinion of Rava? **Or perhaps** the Master is merely saying **that if** the heir **has several** violations of **positive mitzvot,** the offering **atones** for them, but still if it was slaughtered for the sake of the wrong owner, the heir is not required to bring another offering.

Rav Pinchas the son of Rav Ami dissents from Rava and holds *yesh ba'alim le'achar mitah* — the owner's designation remains legally operative even after death. Rav Ashi then presses Rav Pinchas for clarification: when you say *yesh ba'alim le'achar mitah*, do you mean *davka* that the heirs must bring a second *olah* if the first was slaughtered with a *shinui ba'alim*? Or perhaps you mean only that if the deceased had multiple *chiyuvei asei*, the heirs may bring additional *olot* to address them? Rav Pinchas responds *davka kamina* — he means it precisely: if the *olah* was invalidated through *shinui ba'alim* after the owner's death, the heirs are indeed obligated to bring another.

### Olah as Doron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דַּוְקָא קָאָמֵינָא. וְאָמַר רָבָא: עוֹלָה – דּוֹרוֹן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pineḥas **said to him: I am saying** that the heir is **actually** the owner, and if the offering was slaughtered for someone else, he must bring another offering. **And Rava says: A burnt offering is a gift [*doron*]** to God; its essential purpose is not atonement.

Rava then offers what is perhaps his most conceptually striking teaching in this sequence: *olah doron hi* — the *olah* is, at its core, a gift to *Hashem*. Tosafot note an apparent tension: the *olah* does possess the capacity to provide *kapparah* for *mitzvat aseh* violations. Yet Rava's point is that this atoning function is secondary, not primary. The *olah* is fundamentally a *doron*, an act of giving.

### Kapparah/Teshuva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלֵיכָּא תְּשׁוּבָה – ״זֶבַח רְשָׁעִים תּוֹעֵבָה״! וְאִי דְּאִיכָּא תְּשׁוּבָה – הָתַנְיָא: עָבַר עַל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה וְשָׁב, לֹא זָז מִשָּׁם עַד שֶׁמּוֹחֲלִים לוֹ! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דּוֹרוֹן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava supports his assertion: **What are the circumstances** under which a burnt offering atones for violating a positive mitzva? **If** one brings a burnt offering **without repentance** for his transgression, he may not sacrifice it at all, as **"the sacrifice of the wicked is an abomination"** (Proverbs 21:27). **And if there is repentance, isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one **violates a positive mitzva and repents,** God **forgives him before he** even **moves from** his place? If so, why is he required to bring an offering? **Rather, conclude from it** that a burnt offering **is a gift** that one brings in order to appease God even after he is forgiven.

The *Gemara* then explores what this means in practice. If there is no *teshuva*, the offering of a wicked person is *to'evah* — abominable. But if *teshuva* has been done, a *Tannaitic* teaching already states: *avar al mitzvat aseh veshav, lo zaz misham ad shemochalin lo* — one who violates a *mitzvat aseh* and repents is forgiven immediately, before taking a single step. Therefore, by the time one brings an *olah* for a *mitzvat aseh* violation, the *kapparah* has already been achieved through *teshuva*. The *olah*, then, is not needed for *kapparah* in any strict sense — it is a gift, a *doron*, brought after forgiveness has already been granted.

Rashi captures this precisely: *olah doron hi eina ba'ah lechapper al aseh kappara mamash ela achar shekapparah teshuva al aseh* — after *teshuva* has already achieved *kapparah* for the *aseh* violation, the *olah* is brought not as atonement but as an expression of gratitude and closeness.

### Chatat Before Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטָּאת עַל מִי מְכַפֵּר, עוֹלָה לְאַחַר דּוֹרוֹן – סִימָן. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: חַטָּאת לָמָּה בָּאָה? לָמָּה בָּאָה?! לְכַפֵּר! אֶלָּא לָמָּה בָּאָה לִפְנֵי עוֹלָה? לִפְרַקְלִיט שֶׁנִּכְנָס, [רִיצָּה פְּרַקְלִיט – נִכְנָס] דּוֹרוֹן אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states **a mnemonic** for this series of statements by Rava: **A sin offering atones for who, a burnt offering after a gift. This** last statement of Rava **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon said: For what** purpose **is a sin offering brought?** The Gemara interrupts: **For what** purpose **is it brought?** Is it not brought **to atone** for a transgression? **Rather,** Rabbi Shimon clearly meant: **For what** reason **is it brought before a burnt offering** in all circumstances where both are brought? Rabbi Shimon answered: It is comparable **to an advocate [*lifraklit*] who enters** before the king to convince him to pardon the defendant. Once the **advocate has appeased** the king, the **gift is brought in after him.**

A *Tannaitic* teaching of Rabbi Shimon concludes this discussion and beautifully synthesizes the relationship between *chatat* and *olah*. Rabbi Shimon asks: why does the *chatat* always precede the *olah* — as seen throughout *Sefer Vayikra*, where those who must bring both, such as a *metzora* or a *yoledet*, always bring the *chatat* first? His answer: *leparaklit shenichnas, ritza paraklit nichnas doron acharav* — it is like a royal courtier who first enters to appease the king for an offense that was committed, and only once forgiveness has been granted does the gift follow. You first address what is wrong — *chatat* removes the sin — and only then may you offer a gift from a place of love and gratitude. The *olah* is the gift; the *chatat* is the appeasement.

This explains the profound pairing of *chatat* and *olah* throughout the *korbanot*: they are two sides of the same spiritual act. The *chatat* takes away something negative; the *olah* adds something positive. And the *chatat* always comes first.

## Pesach/Lishmah

### Pesach Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִן הַפֶּסַח וְהַחַטָּאת כּוּ׳. פֶּסַח מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that all offerings are fit even if slaughtered not for their sake, **except for the Paschal offering and the sin offering.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that **a Paschal offering** is required to be slaughtered for its sake?

The *mishnah* stated: *kol hazvachim shenizbechu shelo lishmam kesherim, ela shelo alu l'ba'alim l'shem chovah — chutz min haPesach vehaChatat. HaPesach bizmano, vehaChatat bechol zman* — every sacrifice brought *shelo lishmah* remains valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation, except for the *pesach* in its proper time and the *chatat* at any time. The *pesach* is unique in that if brought with a different *korban* in mind, it is not merely unfulfilling of the obligation — it is actually disqualified entirely.

### Pesach Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״שָׁמוֹר אֶת חֹדֶשׁ הָאָבִיב וְעָשִׂיתָ פֶּסַח״ – שֶׁיְּהוּ כׇּל עֲשִׂיּוֹתָיו לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "Observe the month of the spring and offer [*ve'asita*] the Paschal offering"** (Deuteronomy 16:1), teaching **that all of the actions [*asiyyotav*] of** the Paschal offering, all of its sacrificial rites, must be performed **for the sake of** the **Paschal offering.**

The *Gemara* asks: *pesach minalan* — what is the source for this stringency? The *pasuk* cited is *shamor es chodesh ha'Aviv ve'asisa Pesach* — *sheyihyu kol asiyosav l'shem pesach*: every act performed for this *korban* must be done with the *korban pesach* in mind. This establishes a positive requirement of *lishmah* for all the *avodot* of the *pesach*.

### Shinuy Kodesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ, שִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲמַרְתֶּם זֶבַח פֶּסַח הוּא״ – שֶׁתְּהֵא זְבִיחָה לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח; אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְשִׁנּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ, תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לְשִׁנּוּי בְעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the *halakha* that **deviation** from the type of **offering** is prohibited. **From where do we** derive that **deviation** with regard to the **owner** is also prohibited? The Gemara answers that this is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "And you shall say:** It is **the sacrifice [*zevaḥ*] of** the Lord's **Passover"** (Exodus 12:27), which indicates **that** the **slaughter [*zeviḥa*] must be** performed **for the sake of** the **Paschal offering. If** the verse **is not** necessary in order **to** derive **the matter** of **deviation** from the type of **offering,** as that *halakha* is derived from Deuteronomy 16:1, **apply it** to the **matter of deviation** with regard to the **owner.**

The *pasuk shamor es chodesh ha'Aviv ve'asisa Pesach* addresses *shinui kodesh* — the requirement that the *shechitah* be designated for the *pesach* and not for some other *korban* such as a *shelamim* or *chatat*.

### Shinuy Ba'alim

For *shinui ba'alim*, a separate *pasuk* is invoked: *va'amartem zevach pesach hu* — you must declare "this is the *zevach pesach*," which the *Gemara* interprets as requiring that the *zevichah* be designated for the *pesach* and for its proper *chaburah*. This is distinctive to *korban pesach*: not only must the owner have the proper intent, but all those who will be eating the *korban* — the entire *chaburah* — must have been designated in advance as part of the group on whose behalf this *korban* is brought.

### Rav Safra Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב סָפְרָא: הַאי ״וְזָבַחְתָּ״ לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַב נַחְמָן – דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: מִנַּיִן לְמוֹתַר פֶּסַח שֶׁקָּרֵב שְׁלָמִים? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְזָבַחְתָּ פֶּסַח לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ צֹאן וּבָקָר״ – וַהֲלֹא אֵין פֶּסַח בָּא אֶלָּא מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים וּמִן הָעִזִּים! מִכָּאן לְמוֹתַר הַפֶּסַח שֶׁיְּהֵא לְדָבָר הַבָּא מִן הַצֹּאן וּמִן הַבָּקָר, וּמַאי נִיהוּ – שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Safra objects to this: Does this** verse: **"And you shall sacrifice," come to** teach **this** *halakha*? **It is necessary for** the statement **of Rav Naḥman, as Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: From where** is it derived **that a leftover Paschal offering,** an animal consecrated but not ultimately sacrificed on Passover eve, **is sacrificed as a peace offering** afterward? It is derived from **that** which **is stated: "And you shall sacrifice the Passover offering unto the Lord, your God, of the flock and the herd."** The verse is difficult: **But isn't a Paschal offering brought only from the lambs and from the goats? From here** it is derived **that a leftover Paschal offering should be** sacrificed **as** an offering **brought** both **from the flock and from the herd. And what is that?** It is **a peace offering.**

Rav Safra challenges the derivation of *le'akeiv* from the *pasuk* *vezavachta Pesach l'Hashem Elokecha*. He argues: *hai miba'ei lei l'chidrav Nachman* — that *pasuk* is already needed to teach what Rav Nachman derived. Rav Nachman, in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha, learned from *vezavachta Pesach l'Hashem Elokecha tzon uvakar* that a leftover *korban pesach* animal — one that was designated as a *pesach* but was not brought in time — can be brought subsequently as a *shelamim*. The apparent difficulty is that the *pesach* can only be a sheep or goat, not cattle; yet the *pasuk* mentions *tzon uvakar*. Rav Nachman resolves this by saying the mention of cattle teaches that the leftover animal may become a *shelamim*, which can be brought from either sheep or cattle. Since the *pasuk* is already needed for this purpose, it cannot simultaneously serve as the source for *shinui ba'alim le'akeiv*.

### Sources/Responses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: ״וְזָבַחְתָּ פֶּסַח״ – לְכִדְרַב נַחְמָן; ״שָׁמוֹר אֶת חֹדֶשׁ הָאָבִיב״ – לְמִצְוָה בְּשִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ; ״וַאֲמַרְתֶּם זֶבַח פֶּסַח״ – בְּשִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים לְמִצְוָה; ״הוּא״ – לְעַכֵּב בֵּין הָכָא וְהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Safra says** that the verses should be expounded as follows: The verse **"And you shall sacrifice the Paschal offering"** teaches the statement **of Rav Naḥman,** that a leftover Paschal offering is sacrificed as a peace offering. The verse: **"Observe the month of the spring** and offer the Paschal offering" (Deuteronomy 16:1), teaches that there is **a mitzva** to sacrifice the Paschal offering without **deviation** from the type of **offering.** The verse: **"And you shall say:** It is **the sacrifice of** the Lord's **Passover"** (Exodus 12:27), teaches that there is **a mitzva** to slaughter it without **deviation** with regard to the **owner.** The term **"it is"** in the latter verse teaches that **both here and there,** i.e., with regard to both the offering and the owner, proper intent is **indispensable.**

Rav Safra therefore restructures the derivations. *Shamor es chodesh ha'Aviv ve'asisa Pesach* teaches the *mitzvah* requirement of *lishma* as it concerns *shinui kodesh*. *Va'amartem zevach pesach hu* teaches the *mitzvah* requirement as it concerns *shinui ba'alim*. And *vezavachta Pesach* is reserved for Rav Nachman's teaching about *mosar pesach* becoming *shelamim*.

As for the requirement of *le'akeiv* — that violation actually disqualifies the *korban* — the *Gemara* derives this from the convergence of multiple *peshakim* that each mention *zevichah*: *bein hacha vehacha ashkechan zvicha*. All the relevant *pesukim* focus on the act of *shechitah*, establishing it as *me'akeiv*. For the other three *avodot* — *kabalah*, *holacha*, and *zerikah* — the principle of *ho'il vegalei galei* is invoked: once the *Torah* has revealed the requirement for *shechitah*, the same rule applies to all the *avodot*.

### Pesach/Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: ״בְּיוֹם צַוֹּתוֹ אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהַקְרִיב אֶת קׇרְבְּנֵיהֶם״ – זֶה בְּכוֹר וּמַעֲשֵׂר וָפֶסַח; הִקִּישׁוֹ הַכָּתוּב לִשְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the verse: **"In the day that he commanded the children of Israel to present their offerings"** (Leviticus 7:38), **this** word, i.e., "offerings," is referring to the **firstborn** animal, the animal **tithe, and** the **Paschal offering,** which are not mentioned in the previous verse. **The text juxtaposes** all types of offerings mentioned in both verses **with a peace offering,** indicating that **just as** one who sacrifices **a peace offering is required** to do so with the proper intent, **both** concerning **deviation** from the **offering and** concerning **deviation** with regard to the **owner,** and in addition, the *halakha* of proper intent is only **as a mitzva,** not as an indispensable requirement, **so too,** in the case of **any** type of offering that must have proper intent, **both** concerning **deviation** from the **offering and** concerning **deviation** with regard to the **owner,** the *halakha* of proper intent is only **as a mitzva.** Therefore, the mitzva *ab initio* is derived from the *halakha* of a peace offering; and those verses mentioned earlier repeat the *halakha* to teach that it is indispensable with regard to slaughter.

A further *Tannaitic* source establishes the broader framework. The *pasuk* *beyom tzavoto b'nei Yisrael l'hakriv et korbeneihem* refers specifically to *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *pesach* — the three *kodashim kalim* most intimately associated with their owners — and compares them through a *hekesh* to *shelamim*. From this comparison it follows: just as by *shelamim* there is no distinction between *zevichah* and the other *avodot* with respect to the requirement of *lishmah*, so too by *pesach*. And just as by *shelamim*, *shinui kodesh* and *shinui ba'alim* apply equally to all the *avodot*, so too by *pesach*.

### Word Hu/Me'akeiv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִשְׁלָמִים; מָה שְׁלָמִים – בֵּין זְבִיחָה בֵּין שְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת, לֹא חָלַקְתָּ בָּהֶן, לְמִצְוָה; אַף פֶּסַח – לֹא תַּחְלוֹק בּוֹ בֵּין זְבִיחָה לִשְׁאָר עֲבוֹדוֹת, לְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the juxtaposition of the peace offering with the Paschal offering indicates that a Paschal offering is **similar to a peace offering** in another respect: **Just as** with regard to **a peace offering, you did not distinguish between slaughter and** the **other rites with regard to** the **mitzva** *ab initio* of proper intent, **so too, with regard to** the **Paschal offering, do not distinguish between slaughter and** the **other** sacrificial **rites** with regard **to** the *halakha* that proper intent is **indispensable.**

The word *hu* in the context of the *korban pesach* — *zevach pesach hu* — functions as a marker that the requirement is *me'akeiv*: if the *shechitah* is not performed *lishmah*, the *korban* is entirely disqualified. Notably, by contrast, the *korban asham* does not use the word *hu* in the relevant context. The *Gemara* derives from this absence that for the *asham*, if the *imurim* — the sacrificial fats — were not burned on the *mizbe'ach*, the *korban* remains valid *be'dieved*. The presence of *hu* signals indispensability; its absence signals that the step, while required, is not strictly *me'akeiv* in every dimension.
