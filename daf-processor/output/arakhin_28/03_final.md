# Arakhin 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Field Valuation & Auction

### Market Value Today

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין מוֹסִיפִין חוֹמֶשׁ עַל עִלּוּיוֹ שֶׁל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **as** the owner adds one-fifth only to the amount that he bid, and **does not add one-fifth to the addition of that** other person.

Today's daf is 28, and we begin from the bottom of 27b. We are in the middle of discussing what happens nowadays — when there is no fixed value assigned to a field — in determining how to assess it. This question is interesting, because one could in principle also raise it regarding a *sedei mikneh*, a purchased field, to which the Torah also assigns no fixed value. Yet there the situation is less clear: the value of a *sedei mikneh* might in fact be fixed, albeit proportional to the years remaining — it is simply that what happens at *yovel* is different. The *pesukim* are not entirely clear on this point.

The question before us is: nowadays, when *yovel* no longer applies, when there is no fixed value and *yovel* has no operative effect, and a sale is a sale and a *hekdesh* is a *hekdesh* — how does one determine the market value of a field that has been consecrated and must be redeemed? The scenario, as Tosafot points out, presupposes that there is a *beit hamikdash*, but no *yovel* — which was precisely the situation throughout the entire Second Temple period. Tosafot further notes that nowadays, since there is no *beit hamikdash* and therefore no financial interest in redemption, the entire purpose of the transaction is simply to remove the *kedushah*, and one would redeem it for a *perutah*.

One might add that today people are so far removed from the act of being *makdish* that it is an open question whether such a declaration even carries full halakhic force. On the one hand, the wrong word renders an act of *hekdesh* ineffective — it is a matter of formal speech. On the other hand, as was noted at the very beginning of this *masechet*, in matters of *hafla'ah*, one's inner intention is highly significant. Whether someone who has learned the relevant *halachot* — through *yeshiva* study or *Daf Yomi* — is in a different category is an interesting question left unresolved.

### Assessment vs. Auction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אֶחָד: הֲרֵי הִיא שֶׁלִּי בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְשֵׁשׁ כּוּ׳. רָצוּ — אִין, לֹא רָצוּ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** If the owner offers to pay twenty *sela* and **one** other person **said:** The field is **hereby mine for twenty-six** *sela*, if the owner wished to pay thirty-one *sela* and a dinar, the owner takes precedence. The Gemara elaborates: If the owner **wished** to pay the additional sum, then **yes,** he redeems the field, but if the owner **did not wish** to do so, **no,** he is not forced to redeem it.

The *mishnah* presents two methods for determining value. One is a standard assessment: appraisers determine the market value. The other is an auction — apparently something like a silent auction — in which a competitive bidding process is used in the hope of achieving a price higher than the baseline market rate. The owner is not merely invited to participate; he is compelled to do so. There are multiple reasons for this: there is a *mitzvah* for the owner to redeem his own field; if no one else wants the field, at least someone will buy it; and once the owner enters the auction process, he becomes liable to add a *chomesh*, a fifth. So the owner is drawn into the auction on all of these grounds.

### Owner's Fifth Advantage

What follows from this are some remarkable rules about who "wins" the auction. It is not simply the highest bidder in the ordinary sense. Because the owner adds a *chomesh* on top of his base bid, a higher base bid by a competitor may nonetheless be defeated by the owner — since the owner's base bid, once the fifth is added, produces a higher total that the *hekdesh* will ultimately receive. This means the owner must enter the auction knowing the rules thoroughly. Whatever base bid he makes may be escalated — against his initial expectations — and he should be prepared for that eventuality before going in.

### Case Law Philosophy

One might ask why the *mishnah* does not simply state the general principle: treat the owner's bid as though it were twenty percent higher, and award accordingly. The answer is that the *mishnah* has an even more striking rule — if someone bids twenty-one and the owner bids twenty, we award it to the owner and force him to accept the higher bid as his base. The *mishnah*, in other words, does not abstract to a general principle; it articulates concrete cases.

This reflects something important about the development of Talmudic discourse. The Torah is almost entirely case law. The *mishnah* is also largely case law, though occasionally one finds the construction *zeh kelal* — "this is the rule" — which represents a move toward abstraction. The Gemara then always asks: *zeh kelal l'atuyei mai*, what does this inclusive rule come to add? But perhaps a *zeh kelal* is not always coming to include a new case; sometimes it is the *mishnah* itself stepping back from the concrete and attempting to articulate the abstract. As one moves further into the Amoraic period, abstraction becomes progressively more prominent.

### Owner Coercion

A natural question arises about the owner's liability once he is drawn into the auction process. Can he withdraw without penalty if he finds the final price beyond his means? The answer, based on the language throughout the *mishnah*, is that he apparently cannot — *mamashknin oto*, we seize from him. He is stuck. What happens in the extreme case where the owner has no assets whatsoever — whether the *hekdesh* would then turn to the next bidder or treat the obligation as an outstanding debt — is not resolved, though presumably *hekdesh* would act in its best financial interest in such circumstances.

## Equal Bids Problem

### Ishtakach Gavra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אֶחָד: הֲרֵי הוּא שֶׁלִּי כּוּ׳. בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ נוֹתְנִין שְׁלֹשִׁים. וְלֵימְרוּ הַבְּעָלִים: אֲתָא גַּבְרָא בַּחֲרִיקִין! דַּאֲמוּר בְּעָלִים דִּינָר.
>
> **Translation:** If the owner says he will pay twenty *sela* and **one** other person **said:** The field **is hereby mine for** a payment of **twenty-five** *sela*, the owner **gives thirty** *sela*. The Gemara suggests: **And let the owner say: A man has come in my place [*baḥarikin*]** to redeem the field, as even when accounting for the one-fifth that I must add, my offer stands at twenty-five *sela*, identical to this man's offer. Why, then, am I forced to redeem the field for a greater sum? The Gemara responds: The mishna is referring to a case **where the owner** initially **said** he would pay twenty *sela* and an additional **dinar,** one-quarter of one *sela*.

We are now approximately seven lines down in the wide section of the daf. The Gemara presents the following case: someone other than the owner bids twenty-five, and the owner bids twenty. We know that the owner's twenty becomes twenty-five once the *chomesh* is added. So we award it to the owner, and now use the outsider's twenty-five as the base, making the owner's total obligation thirty. But the Gemara is dissatisfied with this reasoning when the bids are exactly equal at the base level. The Gemara asks: why can the owner not say *ishtakach gavra b'chariki* — I have a man standing in my place? If this outsider bid twenty-five and my twenty, once my fifth is added, also becomes twenty-five, they are effectively identical. There is no basis for preferring me over him, and I should be able to say: take the other fellow.

The Gemara finds this argument compelling. Contrary to what one might have thought was the *chiddush* of the *mishnah* — that we always push the owner to produce the maximum amount — the Gemara draws a line here. If the owner's bid plus his fifth equals exactly the outsider's bid, we would indeed go to the outsider. The *chiddush* of the *mishnah* must therefore be a case where the owner bid slightly more than twenty.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי דִּינָר! לָא דָּק. וְלָא? וְהָא קָתָנֵי: אִם רָצוּ הַבְּעָלִים לִיתֵּן שְׁלֹשִׁים וְאֶחָד וְדִינָר — בְּעָלִים קוֹדְמִין! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: דַּאֲמוּר בְּעָלִים פְּרוּטָה, וְלָא דָּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **let** the mishna **teach** explicitly that he offered an additional **dinar.** The Gemara responds: The *tanna* was **not meticulous** with regard to the mention of small sums of money such as one dinar. The Gemara asks: **And** was the *tanna* **not** meticulous? **But isn't it taught** in the mishna that in a case where one said: The field is hereby mine for a payment of twenty-six *sela*, **if the owner wished to pay thirty-one** *sela* **and a dinar the owner takes precedence? Rather, Rava said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where the owner said** he would redeem it for twenty *sela* and **one *peruta*, and** the *tanna* was **not meticulous** with regard to mentioning such a tiny sum.

The Gemara therefore reads the *mishnah* as describing a case where the owner bid twenty and a *dinar* — a *dinar* being a quarter of a *shekel*. The owner bid twenty and a bit; once his *chomesh* is added, the total exceeds twenty-five, and the owner genuinely wins. The outsider's twenty-five then becomes the base, yielding thirty as the owner's total payment.

### Fraction Precision

The Gemara then raises an objection: if that is the case, why does the *mishnah* not say so explicitly — *v'litnei dinar*, let it state "and a dinar"? The answer given is *v'lo dak*, the *mishnah* is not being precise. But this is a surprising answer, since the entire *halakha* hinges on that extra amount. The Gemara then points out that the *mishnah* does in fact mention a *dinar* at its conclusion — in the rule that the owners may bid thirty-one and a *dinar* — demonstrating that the *mishnah* is not averse to mentioning fractions. Rava therefore reformulates: the owner bid twenty and a *perutah* — a penny — and the *mishnah* simply rounds and omits it.

This remains somewhat unsatisfying, since the legal outcome depends entirely on that marginal addition. But the Gemara's conclusion is clear: if the bids are truly equal — twenty producing twenty-five with the *chomesh*, meeting the outsider's twenty-five exactly — we would give it to the outsider, and the *mishnah*'s case is one where the owner's bid, however slightly, exceeded that threshold.

## Assessment Reinforcement

### Rabbi Yehuda's Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין מוֹסִיפִין חוֹמֶשׁ עַל עִלּוּיוֹ כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא נִישּׁוֹם הֶקְדֵּשׁ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, אֲבָל נִישּׁוֹם הֶקְדֵּשׁ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה — מוֹסִיפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that even when the owner must give the amount offered by the second person for the field, he adds one-fifth only to the amount that he himself bid, **as he does not add one-fifth to the addition** of the other person. **Rav Ḥisda says:** The mishna **taught** that the owner does not add one-fifth to the addition of the other person **only when** the **consecrated** property **was not appraised by three** people. **But** if the **consecrated** property **was appraised by three** people, and the field was found to be worth the amount offered by the other person, the owner **does add** one-fifth to the addition of the other person.

The Gemara continues. The *mishnah* has stated that the *chomesh* is based on the owner's own bid, not the higher bid of the outsider. However, Rabbi Yehuda qualifies this: *lo shanu ela shelo nishom hekdesh b'shlosha* — this is only true when *hekdesh* did not have the field independently assessed by a panel of three. Rashi explains that the *chiddush* of specifying three is that the general rule requires *hekdesh* to assess with ten — a principle derived in Sanhedrin. But if there was at minimum an assessment by three, that assessment has binding force.

### Ten vs. Three

What does this mean practically? If someone else bids twenty-two and there is an independent assessment also arriving at twenty-two — thereby reinforcing the higher bid — and the owner bid only twenty, then while the owner still wins the field (since his twenty plus the *chomesh* exceeds twenty-two), his *chomesh* is now based not on his own bid of twenty but on the reinforced base of twenty-two. This is the principle that Rav Chisda had in mind: when an independent assessment supports the outsider's higher bid, the owner's fifth must be calculated on that higher figure.

Questions naturally arise. What if the independent assessment was higher than the outsider's bid — would the owner be bound by that even higher figure? What if the assessment is lower? Rashi's framing suggests the assessment must reinforce the outsider's bid specifically, but the Gemara does not address these edge cases explicitly. Also left unaddressed is the scenario the *mishnah* implies but never states: what happens when all bids come in below the market price? Presumably *hekdesh* is not bound by such bids — like a minimum bid in a standard auction — but this is not spelled out.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מוֹסִיפִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹסִיפִין. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי לָא נִישּׁוֹם, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? אֶלָּא דְּנִישּׁוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **this is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Beit Shammai say** the owner **adds** one-fifth to the addition of the other person **and Beit Hillel say** the owner **does not add** that one-fifth. The Gemara explains: **What are the circumstances** of this *baraita*? **If** it is discussing a case where the field was **not appraised** by three people, then **what is the reasoning of Beit Shammai** for their ruling that the owner must add one-fifth to the addition of the other person? **Rather,** the *baraita* must be referring to a case **where** the field was **appraised** by three people and the appraisal matched the offer of the other person. The owner must therefore add one-fifth to the addition of that other person.

The Gemara brings a *baraisa* in support: *Beit Shammai omerim mosifin u-Beit Hillel omerim ein mosifin* — Beit Shammai says that the owner must add his fifth based on the higher bid, while Beit Hillel says he does not. The Gemara immediately asks: what is the case? If *hekdesh* made no independent assessment, what is Beit Shammai's reasoning for applying the *chomesh* to the higher bid? The Gemara finds this difficult. It concludes that the *baraisa* must be speaking about a case where an independent assessment was made — only then does Beit Shammai's position become intelligible.

### Gemara Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא רַב חִסְדָּא דְּאָמַר כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי? לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא נִישּׁוֹם, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי מַחְמִירִין. אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם דְּנִישּׁוֹם, וְאֵיפוֹךְ — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹסִיף. וְלִיתְנְיַהּ גַּבֵּי קוּלֵּי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וְחוּמְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל! אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם דְּלֹא נִישּׁוֹם, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי מַחְמִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Shall we say that Rav Ḥisda says** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai?** This is difficult, as there is a principle that the *halakha* is ruled in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel. The Gemara responds: **Actually,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the field was **not appraised** by three people, **and Beit Shammai are stringent** and rule that the owner must nevertheless add one-fifth to the addition of the other person. Rav Ḥisda rules in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel that the owner adds one-fifth to the addition of the other person only when the field was appraised by three people.

This, however, raises a new problem: if Rav Chisda's ruling — that the *chomesh* is based on the independently assessed figure — represents Beit Shammai's position, since when do we rule like Beit Shammai? The Gemara offers two resolutions. The first is that the *baraisa* is speaking of a case *without* assessment, and Beit Shammai is simply being stringent in requiring the owner's fifth to be based on the higher bid even without an independent assessment. The second is to reverse the positions entirely — *v'eiphach* — with Beit Shammai saying *ein mosifin* and Beit Hillel saying *mosifin*, so that Rav Chisda would align with Beit Hillel. But this second reading is rejected, because if Beit Shammai were the lenient party, the rule would have been listed among the well-known *kulei Beit Shammai* and *chumrei Beit Hillel*.

The Gemara therefore settles on the first explanation: the *baraisa* concerns a case without assessment, and Beit Shammai is stringent. In the end, this *baraisa* neither supports nor contradicts Rav Chisda, since Rav Chisda's principle specifically concerns the case *with* independent assessment — a case the *baraisa* is not addressing.

## Owner's Final Option

### Last Chance Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? דַּאֲמוּר בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד — הַבְּעָלִים קוֹדְמִין, וְאִם לָאו — אוֹמֵר לוֹ: הִגַּעְתִּיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **What are the circumstances** of such an offer? It is in a case **where** the owner initially **said** he would purchase the field **for twenty-one** *sela*. In such an instance, the additional one-fifth amounts to five *sela* and one dinar, which means that the total payment of the owner is twenty-six *sela* and one dinar, greater than the offer of the other person. Accordingly, **the owner takes precedence,** and when the one-fifth is added to the twenty-six *sela* offer of the other person, the total price paid by the owner is equal to thirty-one *sela* and one dinar. **And if** the payment of the owner does **not** exceed the offer of the other person even when accounting for the additional one-fifth, then the treasurer **says to** the other person: The field **has come into your** possession.

The *mishnah* presents a final case: someone bid twenty-six, while the owner bid twenty. The owner's twenty plus the *chomesh* of five yields only twenty-five, which does not match the outsider's twenty-six — so the outsider wins. But the *mishnah* adds: *im ratzu haba'alim b'shloshim v'echad v'dinar*, if the owners wish, they may bid thirty-one and a *dinar*. How is thirty-one and a *dinar* derived? A fifth of twenty-six is five and a *dinar* (since there are four *dinars* in a *shekel*), and twenty-six plus five and a *dinar* is thirty-one and a *dinar*. Exactly what the *mishnah* says.

The plain reading of the *mishnah*, then, is that even after the outsider has won the bidding, the owner is given one last chance. We turn to him and say: if you wish, you may now bid twenty-one — which, with your *chomesh*, will exceed the outsider's twenty-six and yield a total of thirty-one and a *dinar*, in which case you would win. This seems to reflect the principle of *mitzva d'ba'alim*, the preferential standing of the owner, extended all the way to a final option after the auction has nominally concluded.

### Rav Sheshet Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִינָר מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכִי קָאָמַר, אִם רָצוּ בְּעָלִים מֵעִיקָּרָא לִיתֵּן חֶשְׁבּוֹן הַמַּגִּיעַ לְאֶחָד וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְדִינָר;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** the additional **dinar** that the owner gives, **what is its purpose? Rav Sheshet said this** is what the mishna **is saying: If the owner wished from the outset to give** based on **a calculation that reaches thirty-one** *sela* **and** one **dinar,** the owner takes precedence.

The Gemara, however, does not read the *mishnah* this way. Rav Sheshet introduces the word *me'ikara* — from the outset. The Gemara reads the *mishnah* as saying: if the owners had *originally* made a higher bid — specifically, a bid of twenty-one — then the resulting calculation of thirty-one and a *dinar* follows automatically from the rules already established. The case of twenty-one as the owner's initial bid plus the outsider's twenty-six yields the owner's victory, with the base set at twenty-six, yielding thirty-one and a *dinar*.

The Gemara also raises the question of the *dinar* directly: *v'dinar mai avidtei* — why the extra *dinar*? If the owner's bid stays at twenty and he is simply given a last option to match the outsider's twenty-six, then the total should be thirty-one even — not thirty-one and a *dinar*. The *dinar* only appears because the *chomesh* is calculated on twenty-one (the owner's original bid), and a fifth of twenty-one is five and a *dinar*. This arithmetic works only if the owner's original bid was twenty-one, not twenty.

### Bid Timing Dispute

The straightforward reading of the *mishnah* is that the owner is given a post-hoc opportunity: you didn't bid high enough, but we are now giving you one last chance to say twenty-one, and if you do, the math will yield thirty-one and a *dinar* and you will win. The Gemara's introduction of *me'ikara* takes that away: no such last-chance option exists. You must have made that higher bid initially. If you bid twenty, and the outsider bid twenty-six, the twenty-six wins and you have no recourse.

As the *Shita Mekubetzet* notes, quoting Tosafot at 126, 16, what is the *chiddush* of this last rule if all it does is reiterate the earlier principle? The entire force of the *mishnah*'s conclusion seems to be the grant of a final option to the owner. But the Gemara, with the insertion of *me'ikara*, reads that away entirely. The *chiddush* evaporates, and we are left with a mere restatement. This is indeed a striking observation, and the tension between the plain sense of the *mishnah* and the Gemara's reading remains.

## Cherem Intro

### Cherem vs. Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַחֲרִים אָדָם מִצֹּאנוֹ וּמִבְּקָרוֹ, וּמֵעֲבָדָיו וּמִשִּׁפְחוֹתָיו הַכְּנַעֲנִים, וּמִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּתוֹ, וְאִם הֶחְרִים אֶת כּוּלָּם אֵינָם מוּחְרָמִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person** may **dedicate,** for sacred or priestly use, some **of his flock and** some **of his cattle, and** some **of his Canaanite slaves and maidservants, and some of his ancestral field. But if he dedicated all** that he has **of** any type of property, **they are not dedicated,** i.e., the dedication does not take effect; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

We turn now to the top of 28a, and to the next *mishnah*. As we move through the final section of *Parshat Bechukotai*, we progress from *erchin* to *sdei achuzah* to *sdei mikneh* and now arrive at *cherem*. The term *cherem* differs meaningfully from *hekdesh*. *Hekdesh* is a positive act: one is granting property to a recipient — the *hekdesh* institution. *Cherem*, by contrast, is focused on removal from oneself: one is declaring something off-limits, banned. This difference in emphasis is not merely semantic; it will shape the legal analysis that follows.

### Kisedei Hacherem

The textual basis for *cherem* in this context comes from two places in the Torah. First, from the verse near the end of *Bechukotai* describing a field that was consecrated but not redeemed: *vehaysa hasadeh betzeiso bayovel kodesh l'Hashem kisedei hacherem lakohein tihyeh achuzaso* — the field, upon the *yovel*, shall be holy to God, like a field of *cherem*, and shall become the possession of the *kohen*. Within this context, the Torah introduces the phrase *kisedei hacherem*, "like a field of *cherem*." Second, in the portion of Korach, where the gifts of the *kohanim* are enumerated, it says: *kol cherem b'Yisrael lecha yihiyeh* — all *cherem* in Israel shall be yours. So *cherem* goes to the *kohanim*, and we are now going to analyze what that entails.

One should note that the term *cherem* appears in multiple contexts with somewhat different resonances. In the narratives of Yehoshua and the city of Ai, *cherem* meant a total ban — nothing could benefit from it, everything was forbidden. Elsewhere, the verse says *kol cherem kodesh kodashim hu l'Hashem*, it is holy of holies to God. Whether *cherem* goes to *bedek habayit* or to the *kohen* will itself be a matter of discussion in the Gemara, though several *pesukim* clearly indicate *kol cherem lecha yihiyeh* — it goes to the *kohen*.

### Cherem Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: מָה אִם לַגָּבוֹהַּ אֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאי לְהַחְרִים כׇּל נְכָסָיו, עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה שֶׁיְּהֵא אָדָם חָס עַל נְכָסָיו!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said: If for the Most High a person may not dedicate all his property,** it is **all the more so** the case **that a person should spare his property** and not give all of it to others.

The *mishnah* introduces the laws of *cherem*: a person may declare *cherem* over some of his flock, some of his cattle, some of his servants, some of his female servants — all Canaanite slaves — and some of his inherited fields. The prefix *mem* in each case — *mitzono*, *umibakaro*, *umiavadav* — is critically important, as the Gemara will emphasize. If, however, he attempts to make all of an entire category *cherem*, the *mishnah* records the ruling of Rabbi Eliezer that it does not take effect.

## Cherem Limitations

### Rabbi Eliezer Partial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מִכׇּל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ — וְלֹא כׇּל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ, ״מֵאָדָם״ — וְלֹא כׇּל אָדָם, ״מִבְּהֵמָה״ — וְלֹא כׇּל בְּהֵמָה, ״מִשְּׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה״ — וְלֹא כׇּל שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** stated in the mishna, derived? The Gemara explains that this is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*, with regard to the verse: "Notwithstanding, no dedicated thing that a man may dedicate to the Lord of all that he has, whether of man or animal, or of his ancestral field" (Leviticus 27:28). The verse indicates that one may dedicate **"of all that he has" but not all that he has;** likewise **"of man" but not every man,** i.e., not every slave of his; "of man **or animal" but not every animal** of his; and finally one may dedicate **"of** his **ancestral field" but not all of** his **ancestral field.**

Rabbi Eliezer's position is that *cherem* can only operate on a portion of one's property within a given category. If one attempts to declare all of his fields, or all of his slaves, or any complete category *cherem*, it simply does not take effect. At first glance, this is surprising. We might have expected that since the declaration of *cherem* operates through formal speech to transfer a metaphysical status, it should make no difference whether the quantity is partial or complete. Yet Rabbi Eliezer says otherwise.

There is an analogy to *terumah*, where one may give as little as one kernel or as much as everything minus one kernel — but it must be separated from something, meaning there must be *shirayim*, a remainder. However, *terumah* is conceptually different: the Torah frames it as taking an offering from one's produce, presupposing a larger whole from which a portion is set aside. *Cherem*, by contrast, is more akin to writing a check directly to the *beit hamikdash* — not a taking-from but a direct designation. Despite this conceptual difference, Rabbi Eliezer applies the same limitation.

### Moral Argument

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah draws out the broader moral and *derech eretz* lesson: if even toward the Most High (*gavoha*) a person is not permitted to declare all of his property *cherem*, how much more so should a person be protective of his own property in general — *al achas kamah v'chamah she'adam chas al nechasav*. The lesson is not merely about *cherem* as a formal institution but about proper comportment with one's resources. Do not give away everything even to a worthy cause; one has obligations to oneself.

This teaching is worth contextualizing. It is no coincidence that around the time of the *mishnah*, there were well-known examples of individuals donating all their property to institutions and becoming destitute. The Gemara is clear that this is not the desired model. One continues to maintain oneself, thereby remaining a productive member of society — and *only from that position* can one also help the *beit hamikdash* and the poor. Becoming one of the *aniyim* through excessive generosity defeats the purpose.

The question Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's formulation raises is one of hierarchy: is giving to the *beit hamikdash* of greater value than giving to *tzedakah* for the poor? The *kal v'chomer* — if you cannot give everything to the *beit hamikdash*, how much more so you cannot give everything to the poor — implies that the *beit hamikdash* stands higher in the hierarchy. Rashi indeed reads it this way, and that is also how the Gemara ultimately understands it, though one might have thought the reverse: the *beit hamikdash* has ample resources, whereas the poor urgently need support. Regardless, if that is the hierarchy, the *kal v'chomer* is meaningful.

One must be careful about the scope of Rabbi Eliezer's rule. It is not a general prohibition on giving away all of one's property. If a person declares all of his property *hekdesh* to *bedek habayit* — using the term *hekdesh* rather than *cherem* — that does take effect. The limitation on effectiveness applies specifically and technically when one employs the term *cherem*. As for whether the act was advisable — that is a separate question; the rabbis may counsel against it, but the act stands.

### Mikol Asher Lo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יַחְרִים, וְאִם הֶחְרִים יְהוּ מוּחְרָמִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַךְ״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** It **might** have been thought that **one may not dedicate** all his properties *ab initio*, **but if he did dedicate** all of them, **they should be dedicated.** Therefore **the verse states: "Notwithstanding,"** to teach that they are not dedicated. This is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer.**

The Gemara asks: where is this derived from? The *baraisa* teaches: *mikol asher lo* — from all that is his — *velo kol asher lo* — not everything, but from all. The verse in *Bechukotai* reads: *ach kol cherem asher yacharim ish l'Hashem mikol asher lo, me'adam u-vehema u-misdei achuzato* — every *cherem* that a man dedicates to God from anything of his, whether person, animal, or his inherited field, is not sold or redeemed; it is holy of holies to God. The *baraisa* reads *mikol asher lo* as "from all" — implying some but not all — *me'adam* as "from [some of] his people [i.e., slaves]" but not all of them, *mibehema* as "from [some of] his animals" but not all, and *misdei achuzato* as "from [some of] his inherited field" but not all.

One might object: perhaps this is merely a behavioral instruction — do not declare all *cherem* — but if you did so, it would nonetheless take effect. The answer is the word *ach*, which the *baraisa* reads as a limiting particle: it does not always take effect, and if one declares an entire category *cherem*, it does not take effect. This is the ruling of Rabbi Eliezer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מִכׇּל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: כֹּל דְּאִית לֵיהּ לָא לַחְרֵים, אֲבָל מִין אֶחָד לַיחְרְמֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מֵאָדָם״ — וְלֹא כׇּל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it is **necessary** to derive this *halakha* with regard to all the categories in the verse. **As, if the Merciful One had written** only: **"Of all that he has," I would say** that **one may not dedicate all that he has, but let him dedicate all of one type** of property. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes "of man,"** to teach that he may **not** dedicate **every man.**

The Gemara then adds that all three formulations — the limitation on chattel, on slaves, and on fields — are necessary: *tzrichi*. The reason is that each category might have yielded a different inference without the others. One might have thought that the prohibition is on giving away all one's property across categories, but giving away all of one single category would be acceptable. The verse therefore specifies each category separately to make clear that even all within a single category is impermissible. Conversely, one might have thought that since slaves (*adam*) and fields (*sdei achuzah*) are each essential for livelihood in an agricultural society, the Torah naturally limits *cherem* on them — you simply cannot manage without field labor and without land — but chattel (*metaltelin*), the loss of which does not threaten basic survival, could be given away entirely. Therefore the verse must also address chattel explicitly.

## Analysis of Limitation

### Necessity Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״מֵאָדָם״, דְּלָא סַגִּיא לֵיהּ בְּלָא עֲבוֹדָה, אֲבָל שָׂדֶה — סַגִּיא לֵיהּ בְּדִיסְתּוּרָן. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָכָא חִיּוּתָא וְהָכָא חִיּוּתָא, אֲבָל מְטַלְטְלִין — לַיחְרְמִינְהוּ כּוּלְּהוּ, צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One had written** only: **"of man,"** one might say that he may not dedicate all of his slaves, **as it is not possible for him** to be **without** a slave to perform his **work; but** with regard to **a field, it is possible for him** to maintain a livelihood **by sharecropping [*distoran*]. And if** the Torah **had taught** this *halakha* with regard to only **these two** types, i.e., ancestral fields and slaves, one might say these may not be dedicated in their entirety **because here** there is **livelihood and here** there is also **livelihood. But** in the case of **movable property,** on which maintaining a livelihood does not depend, **let him dedicate all of them.** Therefore, all of these derivations **are necessary.**

The Gemara now probes why it is that one cannot make *cherem* of an entire category. It begins by examining the distinction between types of property. Regarding slaves (*adam*) and fields (*sdei achuzah*), the argument is relatively intuitive: in a pre-industrial agricultural society, both are absolutely essential for livelihood. One cannot manage without labor, and one cannot manage without land. But these two grounds are distinct — you cannot work someone else's land if you have no slaves of your own, and you cannot deploy slaves productively if you have no land. Hence, *ha chayuso v'ha chayuso* — both are essential for survival, each in its own right.

Regarding chattel (*metaltelin*), the Gemara acknowledges that chattel does not carry the same necessity — if one retains slaves and land, one could theoretically survive without one's movable property. Nevertheless, the verse limits even that. All three must be stated.

### Ownership Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּהֵמָה״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יַחְרִים אָדָם בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ, עַבְדּוֹ וְשִׁפְחָתוֹ הָעִבְרִיִּים, וּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָתוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּהֵמָה״, מָה בְּהֵמָה יֵשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְמוֹכְרָהּ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְמוֹכְרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **Why do I** need for the verse to state "or **animal"?** The Gemara explains it is necessary **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought **a person can dedicate his son or his daughter, his Hebrew slave or maidservant, or his purchased field.** Therefore, **the verse states "animal"** to teach that **just as an animal** is an item **that he has permission to sell, so too,** one can dedicate **any** item **that he has permission to sell.** He cannot dedicate an item that he does not have the ability to sell.

The deeper structural principle underlying much of this discussion is that *cherem* operates only on things one fully owns. *She'ein adam machrim davar she'eino shelo* — a person cannot declare *cherem* on something that is not truly his. This principle does not come from a specific *derasha* alone but reflects the foundational logic of *cherem* as a complete transfer. If one does not have full and unconditional title, the act of *cherem* has nothing to fully operate on.

### Exclusions Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא בִּתּוֹ קְטַנָּה יֵשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְמוֹכְרָהּ, יָכוֹל יַחְרִימֶנָּה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּהֵמָה״, מָה בְּהֵמָה יֵשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְמוֹכְרָהּ לְעוֹלָם, אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ רְשׁוּת לְמוֹכְרָהּ לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **But** in the case of **his minor daughter, he has permission to sell her** as a Hebrew maidservant. If so, one **might** have thought **he may dedicate her.** Therefore, **the verse states "animal,"** indicating that **just as an animal** is an item **that he always has permission to sell, so too,** one can dedicate **any** item **that he always has permission to sell,** whereas one may not sell his daughter once she reaches majority. A field as well can be sold only until the Jubilee Year.

The *mishnah* — which effectively parallels the *baraisa* cited earlier — enumerates explicitly what cannot be made *cherem*: one's children, one's Hebrew slaves (*avdo veshifchato haivriyim*), and a purchased field (*sedei miknato*). Hebrew slaves, whether male or female, are not fully owned — they function more as indentured servants, with a legally bounded term and personal status that precludes complete ownership. Similarly, a *sedei miknah* during the period of *yovel* is in effect a lease, not an outright purchase. The remaining years of use can be transferred, but true title to the land reverts at *yovel*, meaning the "owner" does not hold it unconditionally. Therefore *cherem* cannot apply.

It is worth contrasting this with *hekdesh*: one *can* declare a *sedei miknah* as *hekdesh*, because in doing so one is simply consecrating the years of use until *yovel*, and that functional right is genuinely one's own. *Cherem*, however, demands the kind of full, absolute ownership — like one has over a *Canaanite* slave — that a *sedei miknah* does not represent.

The Gemara also raises the case of a minor daughter (*bito ketana*): can one declare her *cherem*? After all, a father can sell his daughter. The answer is no, for the same reason that applies to Hebrew slaves. When a father sells his daughter, she becomes an *amah ivriyah* — a Hebrew bondswoman — which is a form of indentured servitude, not outright ownership as property. The underlying reason is not merely the duration but the nature of the transaction itself: selling one's daughter into *amah ivriyah* status is not a transfer of property in the full sense; it is not real ownership.

### Permanent Sale Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: אִם לַגָּבוֹהַּ אֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאי כּוּ׳. הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said: If for the Most High a person** may **not** dedicate all his property, it is all the more so the case that one should spare his property and not give it all to others. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **This is** identical to the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** i.e., Rabbi Eliezer, who prohibits the dedication of all one's property.

The Gemara derives the *mechkar* principle — the test of permanent salability — from the word *behemah* in the verse. Just as an animal can be sold outright and permanently, so too, only things that can be permanently sold can be made *cherem*: *mah behemah yesh lo reshut limkor leolam, af kol sheyesh lo reshut limkor leolam*. What is the quality of "permanent"? It must be something over which one has complete and irrevocable title to sell. This excludes Hebrew slaves, children, and purchased fields — none of which can be sold with permanent, unconditional effect.

The Gemara notes that this framing through *leolam* — permanence — is a natural way of articulating the rule, though arguably the more fundamental point is that Hebrew servitude is not ownership as property at all, not merely a question of duration.

## Kohen/Levi Cherem

### Kohanim Cannot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם אֵין מַחְרִימִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: הַכֹּהֲנִים אֵין מַחְרִימִין — שֶׁהַחֲרָמִין שֶׁלָּהֶן, וְהַלְוִיִּם מַחְרִימִין — שֶׁאֵין הַחֲרָמִין שֶׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Priests and Levites may not dedicate** their property; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says: Priests may not dedicate** their property, **as** all **dedicated** property **is theirs;** it is one of the priestly gifts, as the verse states: "Everything dedicated in Israel shall be yours" (Numbers 18:14). **But Levites may dedicate** their property, **as dedicated** property **is not theirs.**

The *mishnah* continues: *hakohanim vehaLeviyim ein machrimim* — *kohanim* and *Levi'im* cannot make *cherem* — according to Rabbi Yehuda. The Gemara begins its analysis: the case of *kohanim* is understandable — *shecharmeihem shelahem*, the *cherem* goes to the *kohanim* anyway. Since a *kohen* is entitled to receive *cherem* property, his own act of declaring something *cherem* is meaningless from a practical standpoint: he is effectively giving to himself. One could argue that he might wish to designate the property to a different *kohen*, or that a *kohen* recipient who is reluctant to accept direct charity might accept *cherem* more readily, but the operative logic is that since *cherem* reverts to him anyway, the act lacks the defining character of a genuine transfer.

### Shimon's Logic

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *kohanim hein machrimim* — *kohanim* can in fact declare *cherem*. His reasoning is that since the *cherem* goes to the *kohanim* in any event, the act is still meaningful — the *kohen* can designate which *kohen* receives it, and the sanctity of the *cherem* designation itself has independent significance. However, Rabbi Shimon concedes that *Levi'im* cannot make *cherem* of land — as we will see below — and can only make *cherem* of chattel.

### Leviyim Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, בִּשְׁלָמָא כֹּהֲנִים אֵין מַחְרִימִין — שֶׁחֲרָמִין שֶׁלָּהֶן, אֶלָּא לְוִיִּם, בִּשְׁלָמָא מְקַרְקְעֵי לָא מַחְרְמִי — דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי אֲחוּזַּת עוֹלָם הִיא לָהֶם״, אֶלָּא מִטַּלְטְלֵי לַיחְרְמוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi Yehuda,** what is his reasoning? **Granted, priests may not dedicate** property, **as dedicated** property **is theirs;** it is one of the gifts of the priesthood. **But** why are **Levites** unable to dedicate their property? **Granted,** they may **not dedicate land, as it is written: "As that is their perpetual possession"** (Leviticus 25:34). **But let them dedicate movable property.**

The reason *Levi'im* cannot make *cherem* of their inherited land is derived from the verse: *ki achuzat olam hi lahem* — it is an eternal inheritance to them. When the Torah establishes the cities of the *Levi'im*, it characterizes their holdings as an *achuzat olam*, a permanent inheritance that cannot be divested. If the *Levi'im* attempt to declare their *sdei achuzah* as *cherem*, this permanent designation by the Torah overrides and nullifies the act.

### Rabbi's Compromise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר לוֹ... וּמִשְּׂדֵה אֲחֻזָּתוֹ״ — מַקִּישׁ מִטַּלְטְלִין לְקַרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **the verse states:** "Notwithstanding, no dedicated thing, that a man may dedicate to the Lord **of all that he has,** whether of man or animal, **or of his ancestral field"** (Leviticus 27:28). The verse **juxtaposes** the dedication of **movable property to** that of **land.** Accordingly, as Levites cannot dedicate land, they cannot dedicate movable property either.

Rabbi (Rebbi) offers what appears to be a compromise: *nir'in divrei Rabbi Yehuda bekarka'ot* — it seems to me that Rabbi Yehuda is right regarding land, and *divrei Rabbi Shimon bemataltelin* — Rabbi Shimon is right regarding chattel. At first reading, Rebbi appears to be splitting the difference between two positions. By land, Rabbi Yehuda prevails — *Levi'im* cannot make *cherem* of their inherited land due to *achuzat olam*. By chattel, Rabbi Shimon prevails — *Levi'im* can make *cherem* of movable property since they do not retain it.

### Gemara Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, בִּשְׁלָמָא כֹּהֲנִים — כְּדַאֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא לְוִיִּם, בִּשְׁלָמָא מְטַלְטְלִים לַחְרֵים — דְּלָא מַקֵּישׁ, אֶלָּא מְקַרְקְעֵי אַמַּאי? הָכְתִיב ״אֲחֻזַּת עוֹלָם הִיא לָהֶם״! מַאי ״מַחְרִימִין״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר? מִטַּלְטְלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **Rabbi Shimon,** what is his reasoning? **Granted, priests** may not dedicate their property, **as we stated** above, because dedicated property is theirs. **But** why may **Levites** dedicate their property? **Granted, let them dedicate movable property, as** Rabbi Shimon **does not juxtapose** movable property to land. **But why** may Levites dedicate **land? Isn't it written: That is their perpetual possession?** The Gemara answers: **What** does Rabbi Shimon mean **when he says** Levites may dedicate property? He means they may dedicate only **movable property.**

The Gemara, however, will not allow this reading to stand. From the fact that the *mishnah* records Rebbi saying that Rabbi Yehuda seems right by land and Rabbi Shimon seems right by chattel, the Gemara infers: *michlal*, the implication is that Rabbi Shimon himself maintained that *Levi'im* can declare *cherem* even on *karka* — land. Otherwise, Rebbi's statement would not constitute a compromise but merely a redundant agreement.

But how can that be, given the verse *ki achuzat olam hi lahem*? The Gemara therefore reinterprets Rebbi's statement: *hachi ka'amar* — this is what Rebbi is actually saying. He is not offering a compromise; he is offering an *interpretation* of Rabbi Shimon. When Rabbi Shimon said *Levi'im* can make *cherem*, he surely meant only chattel — he could not have meant land, given the clear force of *achuzat olam*. Therefore Rebbi is saying: it seems to me that even Rabbi Shimon himself would agree with Rabbi Yehuda regarding land; his position that they can make *cherem* was never meant to extend there. The apparent dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon on land was never a real one — *af Rabbi Shimon lo nechelak ela bemataltelin*, even Rabbi Shimon disputed only regarding chattel.

This is a striking reinterpretation. Rather than a compromise between two tannaitic authorities, Rebbi is read as collapsing a dispute entirely — arguing that once one examines the underlying sources, Rabbi Shimon's position was never as broad as it appeared, and *bishlama karka* they would agree, *aval bemataltelin modeh lo*. The question left open is what the *halacha* would be for a *Levi* seeking to make *hekdesh* rather than *cherem* of his *achuzah*, or seeking to make *cherem* of fields other than his ancestral *arei haLevi'im* — fields that are not subject to the *achuzat olam* designation. The analysis here addresses primarily the *sdei achuzah* of the *Levi'im*, and generalizing beyond that may require additional reasoning.

## Cherem Distribution

### Designating Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: הֶחְרִים מְטַלְטְלִין — נוֹתְנָן לְכׇל כֹּהֵן שֶׁיִּרְצֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל חֵרֶם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל לְךָ יִהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin says:** One who **dedicated movable property may give it to any priest that he wishes, as it is stated: "Everything dedicated in Israel shall be yours"** (Numbers 18:14), i.e., for Aaron and any of his descendants.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abin establishes the rule for chattel *cherem*: *hachrim metaltelin, nosen le-chol kohen she-yirtzeh* — one who makes chattel *cherem* may give it to any *kohen* he wishes. This was already implicit in the earlier discussion, where the argument against *kohanim* making *cherem* rested precisely on the fact that they are entitled to receive it — which presupposes that the dedicator gets to choose the recipient. The textual basis is the verse in the portion of Korach: *kol cherem b'Yisrael lecha yihiyeh* — all *cherem* in Israel shall be *to you*, with *lecha* in the singular suggesting that an individual *kohen* can be designated. This parallels *terumah* and *maaser*, where the owner may choose which *kohen* or *Levi* to give to.

### Land Goes to Mishmar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֶחְרִים שְׂדוֹתָיו — נוֹתְנָן לַכֹּהֵן שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מִשְׁמָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּשְׂדֵה הַחֵרֶם לַכֹּהֵן תִּהְיֶה אֲחֻזָּתוֹ״, וְגָמַר ״לַכֹּהֵן״ ״לַכֹּהֵן״ מִגֶּזֶל הַגֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **But if one dedicated his fields, he gives them to a priest of that** priestly **watch** which is currently serving in the Temple, **as it is stated: "As a field dedicated; its possession shall be to the priest"** (Leviticus 27:21). **And one derives** a verbal analogy between **"to the priest"** here and **"to the priest" from** a verse (Numbers 5:8) discussing property **robbed** from **a convert.** The *halakha* in that case is that if one took an oath that he did not rob a convert, and then after the convert died and left no descendants he admitted to taking a false oath, he must give the stolen property to the priests of the priestly watch currently serving in the Temple.

However, a different rule governs *cherem* of fields: *sadosav nosen le-kohen sheba-oso mishmar* — his fields go to the *kohen* who belongs to that *mishmar*, the priestly rotation serving that week. He does not get to designate a specific *kohen* of his choosing. The textual basis is the phrase *kisedei hacherem* in *Bechukotai*: when describing how a field that was consecrated and not redeemed passes to the *kohanim* at *yovel*, the Torah says it goes like a *sdei hacherem* — like a field of *cherem*. This comparison suggests that *sdei hacherem* and the fields returning at *yovel* share the same distribution rule.

### Yovel Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״לַה׳ לַכֹּהֵן״ — קְנָאוֹ הַשֵּׁם וּנְתָנוֹ לַכֹּהֵן שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ מִשְׁמָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And there,** in the case of property robbed from a convert, **from where do we** derive that it is given to the priests of the current priestly watch? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the verse: "But if the man has no kinsman to whom restitution may be made for the guilt, the restitution for guilt that is made shall be the Lord's, to the priest" (Numbers 5:8), the phrase: **"Shall be the Lord's, to the priest,"** indicates that **God** has **acquired** the restitution for the guilt **and** has **given it to the priest of that** priestly **watch.**

That rule is: the fields that go to the *kohanim* at *yovel* are distributed to the *mishmar* serving during the *yovel* — assumed to mean the *mishmar* working on *Yom Kippur* of the *yovel* year, since *yovel* is proclaimed with the *shofar* on *Yom Kippur*. By the *gezerah shavah* of *la-kohen / la-kohen*, derived from the law of *gezel ha-ger* — theft from a convert who dies without heirs — the Gemara establishes that *cherem* property likewise goes to the *mishmar* of the *kohen* on duty at the relevant time. In *gezel ha-ger*, the money and the atoning sacrifice (*asham gezeilos*) are brought together, and the *kohen* officiating that week receives both — establishing the principle that it is the *mishmar* on duty at the time that receives the designated property.

### Yovel on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂדֶה הַיּוֹצְאָה לַכֹּהֲנִים בַּיּוֹבֵל, נוֹתְנָהּ לַמִּשְׁמָר שֶׁפָּגַע בּוֹ יוֹבֵל. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פָּגַע בּוֹ בְּשַׁבָּת, מַאי? אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אַמֵּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּחוּלְפָּנָא: נוֹתְנָהּ לַמִּשְׁמָר הַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** § Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin states another *halakha* involving property that is given to the priests. With regard to **a field** that was consecrated by its owner and was not redeemed by him, **which goes out** of his possession and passes **to** the possession of **the priests at the Jubilee** Year, **one gives it to the** members of the priestly **watch** serving **when the Jubilee** Year **occurred,** i.e., the watch serving at the beginning of the Jubilee Year. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the beginning of the Jubilee Year **occurred on Shabbat,** which is the day on which the priestly watches rotate, **what** is the *halakha*? Which priestly watch receives the property? **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ami says in the name of Ḥulfana: One gives it to the watch that leaves** the service of the Temple on that Shabbat.

A question is then posed: what if *Yom Kippur* of the *yovel* year falls on *Shabbat*? Since *Shabbat* is the changeover day for the priestly *mishmarot*, two *mishmarot* are both in a sense "on duty" — one completing its week and one beginning. Which one receives the fields? Rabbi Chiya bar Ami in the name of Chofna rules: *nitnat lamishmar hayotzei* — it goes to the outgoing *mishmar*, the one that had been serving that week. The logic, as the Gemara will develop, is that the outgoing *mishmar* was already present and serving at the onset of the *yovel* year, giving it greater claim.

### Yovel Timing Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: תַּנְיָא, נִמְצֵאתָ אַתָּה אוֹמֵר, אֶחָד יוֹבֵל וְאֶחָד שְׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמְּטִין כְּאֶחָד, אֶלָּא שֶׁיּוֹבֵל בִּתְחִילָּתוֹ וְהַשְּׁמִטָּה בְּסוֹפָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: It is taught** in a *baraita* in accordance with these statements: **You are found** to **say** that **both the Jubilee** Year, when fields that were sold are returned to their owners, **and the Sabbatical** Year, when debts are canceled, **abrogate at once,** i.e., at the same time, **except that the Jubilee** Year abrogates **at its beginning and the Sabbatical** Year abrogates **at its end.** In other words, the evening of Rosh HaShana at the conclusion of the Sabbatical Year, which is the forty-ninth year of the Jubilee cycle, is also the beginning of the Jubilee Year, which is the fiftieth year. At this time, all debts and land purchases are abrogated, and consecrated fields that were not redeemed become the property of the priests. Accordingly, if Rosh HaShana occurs on a Shabbat, the priestly watch that ends their service receives the fields, as the priestly watches rotate only in the morning.

The Gemara supports this ruling by connecting it to a broader *tanna'itic* debate about when *yovel* takes effect. The *baraisa* states: *echad Yovel ve-echad Shevi'is*, both *yovel* and *shemitah* annul simultaneously. This requires explanation: *shemitah* annuls debts at the *end* of the seventh year — *miketz sheva shanim ta'aseh shemitah* — while *yovel* might seem to begin at *Yom Kippur*. Yet the *baraisa* says they happen together. The Gemara is struck: the verse explicitly says *beyom haKippurim ta'aviru shofar bechol artzechem* — the *shofar* of *yovel* is sounded on *Yom Kippur*. How then can *yovel* and *shemitah* annul at the same moment if *yovel* begins on *Yom Kippur* and *shemitah* ends at the close of the forty-ninth year?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי הֲוַאי! אֵימָא: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַיּוֹבֵל כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the terminology of the *baraita*: Why does the *baraita* state: Except that? This statement indicates that the Sabbatical Year and the Jubilee Year abrogate simultaneously despite the fact that the Jubilee Year abrogates at its beginning. **On the contrary,** this *halakha* **is due to that** very reason, i.e., they abrogate simultaneously because the Jubilee Year abrogates at its beginning and the Sabbatical Year at its end. The Gemara explains that in fact one should **say** the *baraita* as follows: They abrogate at the same time, **due to** the fact **that the Jubilee** Year abrogates at its beginning and the Sabbatical Year at its end.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁבִיעִית בְּסוֹפָהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״מִקֵּץ שֶׁבַע שָׁנִים תַּעֲשֶׂה שְׁמִטָּה״, אֶלָּא יוֹבֵל בִּתְחִילָּתוֹ? בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּיּוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים תַּעֲבִירוּ שׁוֹפָר בְּכׇל אַרְצְכֶם״! הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מֵרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה הוּא דְּחָיֵיל יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** it is clear that the **Sabbatical** Year abrogates **at its end, as it is written: "At the end of every seven years you shall make a release"** (Deuteronomy 15:1). **But** does the **Jubilee** Year abrogate **at its** very **beginning? It** abrogates only ten days later, **on Yom Kippur, as it is written: "On Yom Kippur shall you make proclamation with the shofar throughout all your land**…and you shall return every man to his possession" (Leviticus 25:9–10). The Gemara explains: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, who says: It is from Rosh HaShana that the Jubilee** Year **takes effect.**

The answer is: *ha meni Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka hi* — this *baraita* follows the view of Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, who holds that *yovel* takes effect from *Rosh Hashanah*, not *Yom Kippur*. The Gemara in *Masechet Rosh Hashanah* discusses this at length. According to this view, beginning on *Rosh Hashanah* of the fiftieth year, one may no longer enslave servants — their personal freedom is initiated — but they do not physically depart until *Yom Kippur*, when the *shofar* is sounded. Since the effective beginning of *yovel* is *Rosh Hashanah*, it coincides with the very moment that *shemitah* ends. Both take effect in the transition from the forty-ninth year to the fiftieth.

The relevance to our question about the *mishmar*: because *yovel* already begins in earnest on *Rosh Hashanah* — ten days before *Yom Kippur* — the *mishmar* that was serving on and around *Rosh Hashanah* has a temporal claim to the *yovel* period that predates the *Yom Kippur* changeover. It was already present at the onset of the *yovel* year. This lends weight to awarding the fields to the outgoing *mishmar* when *Yom Kippur* happens to fall on *Shabbat*.

### Abbahu's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁמְעַהּ חִזְקִיָּה בַּר בִּילְוָטִי, וַאֲזַל אֲמַר קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: וְלַיקֵּישׁ מִטַּלְטְלֵי לְקַרְקָעוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the statement of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin that one who dedicates his movable property may give it to a priest of his choosing, whereas one who dedicates his land gives it to members of the current priestly watch. The Gemara relates that **Ḥizkiyya bar Biluto heard this** statement **and went** and **said** the following difficulty **before Rabbi Abbahu: But let one juxtapose movable property to land,** as the verse states: "That a man may dedicate to the Lord of all that he has, whether of man or animal, or of his ancestral field" (Leviticus 27:28). If so, movable property must also be given to members of the current priestly watch.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? דְּאִיכָּא דְּמַקֵּישׁ, וְאִיכָּא דְּלָא מַקֵּישׁ, כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: ״לָא מַקְּשִׁינַן״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu responded: **Is it not** a dispute between ***tanna'im*? As there are** those **who juxtapose and there are** those **who do not juxtapose,** in accordance with the earlier explanation of the Gemara that this is the basis for the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon with regard to whether or not Levites may dedicate movable property. Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says we do not juxtapose** movable property and land.

The sugya closes with a challenge raised by Rabbi Abbahu, as reported by Chizkiyah bar Biloti. If chattel *cherem* goes to a *kohen* of the dedicator's choosing, while *cherem* of fields goes to the *mishmar*, how can one maintain this distinction when the verse explicitly links them together — mentioning land and chattel in the same passage? The same *pasuk* encompasses *adam, behemah*, and *sdei achuzah* together. How can we treat chattel differently from land?

Rabbi Abbahu's answer is: *la tannai de-makkish u-de-la makkish* — it depends on which *tanna* one follows: whether the *tanna* who links (*makkish*) chattel to land, or the one who does not. We have already encountered this dispute in the context of the *Levi'im* — whether the verse's mention of *metaltelin* and *karka* together creates a genuine halakhic equation between them. The *tanna* who does not create the *hekkesh* is free to distinguish: chattel *cherem* goes to a designated *kohen*, while *cherem* of fields follows the *mishmar*. This is a satisfying resolution that ties together the earlier dispute about *Levi'im* with the present question about *cherem* distribution.

What has also been made explicit in this closing passage is something that was implicit throughout: the *kohanim* of the *mishmar* serving at *yovel* are the ones who receive the fields that return at *yovel*. This point, which has been in the background of several discussions, is now formally articulated — and with it, the Gemara's treatment of *cherem* and its connections to *yovel*, the priestly rotation, and the laws of sanctified property is substantially advanced.
