# Sanhedrin 8b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Motzi Shem Ra Intro

### Case Classification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מוֹצִיא שֵׁם רַע בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** An accused **defamer** is judged **by** a court of **twenty-three** judges.

The daf begins near the bottom of 8a, approximately five lines from the end of the amud, dealing with the case of *motzi shem ra* — the husband who defames his wife by claiming she was not a virgin at the time of their marriage. The central question is how to classify this case for purposes of judicial composition: how many judges are required?

### Yerushalmi Two Views

The *Yerushalmi* offers two distinct explanations of the underlying dispute. The first frames the debate as concerning the case against the woman: the proceedings begin as *dinei mamonot*, a monetary case, but carry within them the potential to escalate into *dinei nefashot*, a capital case. The second frames it as concerning the case against the man — where from the outset there are two simultaneous dimensions, *dinei nefashot* against the witnesses (who, if proven *zomemim*, face execution) and *dinei mamonot* against the husband himself. These two framings provide a clean and transparent structure for the debate. As we shall see, the *Bavli*'s treatment of the same dispute is considerably more opaque.

## Ulla & La'az Debate

### Bavli Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי יֵשׁ בּוֹ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, מַאי הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But although** the *halakha* of a defamer **contains** the potential to qualify as a case of **capital law, what of it?** As long as this particular case has no such potential, why do the Rabbis require twenty-three judges?

The *Bavli* records the dispute tersely: *ve-chachamim omrim motzi shem ra be-esrim u-shlosha* — the Sages hold that the case of *motzi shem ra* requires a court of twenty-three, while Rabbi Meir holds that three suffice. The *Gemara* immediately challenges the Sages' position: granted that there is an element of *dinei nefashot* embedded in this case, but so what? We are dealing with the *dinei mamonot* aspect of the proceedings, not the capital dimension. Why should that require twenty-three?

### Ulla's Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: בְּחוֹשְׁשִׁין לְלַעַז קָמִיפַּלְגִי. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְלַעַז, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְלַעַז.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to** the question of whether one needs to be **concerned with** the possibility of **rumors,** meaning that if the husband raises the claim that his wife committed adultery, although he has no witnesses and there is no potential for a capital conviction, should the court be concerned with the possibility that the publicity generated by the proceedings may lead to the emergence of witnesses, which would make it a capital case? **Rabbi Meir holds** that one need **not** be **concerned with** the possibility of **rumors. And the Rabbis hold** that one does need to be **concerned with** the possibility of **rumors.**

Ulla offers the following resolution: *chosheshin la-la'az kamipalgi* — the dispute turns on whether we are concerned about *la'az*, a kind of escalating rumor or public disclosure. Rabbi Meir holds *ein chosheshin la-la'az*, that there is no such concern, while the Sages hold *chosheshin la-la'az*, that there is. The *Bavli*, compared to the *Yerushalmi*, is far more cryptic here. The meaning of Ulla's formulation requires unpacking, and Rashi and Tosafot read it in fundamentally different ways.

### Rashi's Reading

Rashi understands the case as being against the woman. The proceedings begin as a monetary case adjudicated before three judges — that is Rabbi Meir's position. The Sages, however, are concerned that once word gets out — once people begin hearing that there is a case against this woman alleging she was not a virgin on her wedding night — witnesses may come forward and testify that she actually committed adultery. If that occurs, the case transforms from a monetary proceeding into a full capital case, requiring twenty-three. Rabbi Meir is not troubled by this possibility: if it happens, one can simply add judges at that point. The Sages, by contrast, say we must start with twenty-three now, because we are concerned the case will evolve into *dinei nefashot*. On this reading, *chosheshin la-la'az* means something like: we are worried that the word will spread and draw out witnesses, turning the case into a capital one.

### Tosafot Disputes

Tosafot is deeply dissatisfied with Rashi's reading on several grounds. First, Tosafot objects to Rashi's translation of the term *la'az* itself. Second, and more fundamentally, Tosafot insists that the phrase *motzi shem ra* throughout the *Gemara* refers specifically to the case against the man — not the case against the woman. This objection will be recognized immediately by anyone who has just studied the *Yerushalmi*: the identical pushback appears there, where the second opinion in the *Yerushalmi* protests that *motzi shem ra* means the case against the man, not against the woman. Rashi, in effect, reads like the first opinion in the *Yerushalmi*; Tosafot reads like the second.

### Tosafot Zomemin Theory

Tosafot therefore reframes the entire case as being against the man. But this creates a significant difficulty, because in the standard case against the man, his witnesses are proven *zomemim* — and it is in that moment that the man faces both lashes and monetary liability. If the witnesses are already definitively *zomemim*, there is nothing merely monetary about the initial proceedings; the capital dimension of the witnesses' fate is already established. To resolve this, Tosafot advances a bold and acknowledged *chidush*: he posits a case in which the husband's witnesses were not made *zomemim* but were instead neutralized — that is, contradicting witnesses came forward and testified on the woman's behalf, and the result was a standoff, two against two, with neither set of witnesses definitively discredited. In this scenario, Tosafot argues, the husband would still be required to pay the fine and receive lashes — despite the fact that it is entirely unclear he was lying, since it was simply a case of contradictory testimony. This is, Tosafot concedes, a remarkable position. But it allows Tosafot to construct a case in which the proceedings against the man begin as *dinei mamonot* while retaining the potential to become *dinei nefashot* if the husband's witnesses are subsequently proven *zomemim*.

### La'az Meanings

On Tosafot's reading, what does *chosheshin la-la'az* mean? Here, a crucial distinction emerges between two possible meanings of the phrase. The first meaning — Rashi's reading — is that *la'az* refers to the fear that news of the case will spread and attract witnesses, causing the case to escalate into a capital proceeding. This, one might note, is not obviously a bad thing. The second meaning, which Tosafot prefers and which was raised in discussion, is that *la'az* refers to concern for the honor of the original judges: if a court of three initially convenes and then it later becomes necessary to convene a court of twenty-three, people may say that the original judges were somehow inadequate — an embarrassment to the *beit din*. Under both meanings, the underlying question is the same: whether a *dinei mamonot* case might evolve into a *dinei nefashot* case. But they diverge on what specifically is feared about that evolution. Rabbi Meir, under the second reading, says: if we have to add judges, we will add them then — *ein chosheshin la-la'az*, we are not troubled by how that looks. The Sages say: we are troubled, and therefore we begin with twenty-three.

### Bavli-Yerushalmi Parallel

Tosafot makes an observation of striking methodological importance. Near the end of the extended Tosafot on 8b, in the passage beginning *ve-heivi ha-av edim ve-hizimum*, Tosafot cites a finding by the *Rashba* — here referring to Rabbi Shimshon, not the later Sefardic authority — that in the *Yerushalmi*, the very dispute between Rashi and Tosafot about how to read the *Bavli* is explicitly present as a dispute between *Amoraim*. One opinion in the *Yerushalmi* explains the case as being about the *kesubah*, paralleling Rashi's reading of the case against the woman; another explains it as being about the hundred-*sela* fine, paralleling Tosafot's reading of the case against the man. What this reveals is that the *Bavli*'s cryptic formulation is not merely ambiguous prose — it reflects an inherent ambiguity in the *sugya* itself. The *Yerushalmi* preserves that ambiguity transparently, as an explicit dispute; the debates of the *Rishonim* on how to read the *Bavli* are effectively the same debate, mapped onto a different layer of tradition. It is a pattern one encounters repeatedly: implicit divergences in reading a *Bavli* text turn out to parallel explicit disagreements recorded in the *Yerushalmi*.

## Rava's Honor Theory

### Rava Reframes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לְלַעַז לָא חָיְשִׁינַן, וְהָכָא בְּחוֹשְׁשִׁין לִכְבוֹדָן שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹנִים קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Everyone** agrees that one need **not** be **concerned with** the possibility of **rumors. And here** they **disagree about** whether one need be **concerned for the honor of the first** judges.

Rava offers a fundamentally different reading of the dispute. He opens with a broad claim: *kuli alma la-la'az lo chashinan* — everyone agrees that we are not concerned about a court of three devolving into or evolving into a court of twenty-three by virtue of public rumor. That question is off the table. The real dispute, Rava says, lies elsewhere.

### Honor of Judges

According to Rava, the debate concerns *chosheshin li-khodan shel rishonim* — concern for the honor of the original judges. This sounds superficially like one of Ulla's readings of *la'az*, but Rava inverts the direction of the problem entirely. It is not about adding judges; it is about dismissing them.

### Dispersal Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכְּנִיף בֵּי עֶשְׂרִין וּתְלָתָא לְמֵידַן דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, וְאִיבַּדּוּר, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: דַּיְּינוּ לִי מִיהָא דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing, **for example,** with a case **where a court of twenty-three** judges **was convened to judge** a case of **capital law,** because the husband accused his wife of committing adultery. As there were no witnesses to the alleged adultery, the court of twenty-three judges **was disbanded, and** then the husband **said to them: At least adjudicate for me the** aspect of **monetary law,** to reduce the sum in her marriage contract to the amount for a non-virgin.

The case Rava constructs is the following: a full court of twenty-three has already assembled to adjudicate what began as a potential capital case — the husband claimed his wife committed adultery and sought to bring witnesses to prove it. The court gathered *le-midon dinei nefashot*, to try a capital matter. But the case collapsed — the witnesses could not substantiate their claim — and the court began to disperse. At that point the husband calls out: do not all leave! Even if I cannot prove adultery, at least adjudicate the monetary question, whether my wife is entitled to her *kesubah*.

### Dismissal Dispute

Now the question is inverted: do we need to keep all twenty-three judges to hear a monetary question, or may the plaintiff make do with three, dismissing the other twenty? Rabbi Meir says twenty may be released; three remain to hear the *dinei mamonot*. The Sages say: since we started with twenty-three, we keep twenty-three — dispersing the majority of the court would be a slight to their honor. Ulla's reading strikes this analysis as more intuitive than Rava's, but Rava's reframing provides a coherent and internally consistent account of the dispute.

## Baraita & Cases

### Baraita Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, תְּבָעוֹ מָמוֹן – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, תְּבָעוֹ נְפָשׁוֹת – בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 1:2): **And the Rabbis say:** If he took her to court and **claimed money,** the case is to be adjudicated **by three** judges. But if he took her to court and **claimed** that she was liable to receive the **death** penalty, the case must be judged **by twenty-three** judges.

The *Gemara* challenges the foregoing discussion by citing a *beraita*: the Sages say, if a monetary claim is brought, a court of three suffices; if a capital matter is brought, twenty-three are required. This *beraita* attributes to the Sages a position that sounds remarkably like Rabbi Meir's position in the *Mishnah* — namely, that a monetary case requires only three.

### Ulla's Problem

The difficulty is most acute for Ulla. Ulla's account of the debate is that the Sages and Rabbi Meir are arguing specifically about what happens when the case *begins* as a monetary proceeding — Rabbi Meir says three, the Sages say twenty-three because it may escalate. But now the *beraita* presents the Sages themselves as agreeing that a monetary case requires only three. How can Ulla's reading be sustained?

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֲנִי וַאֲרִי שֶׁבַּחֲבוּרָה תַּרְגֵּימְנַהּ. וּמַנּוּ? רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? שֶׁהֵבִיא הַבַּעַל עֵדִים שֶׁזִּינְּתָה, וְהֵבִיא הָאָב עֵדִים וֶהֱזִימּוּם לְעֵדֵי הַבַּעַל. בָּא לִגְבּוֹת מָמוֹן מִבַּעַל – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, וּבִמְקוֹם נְפָשׁוֹת – בְּעֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: I and the lion of the group** both **explained** this. The Gemara asks: **And who is** the lion of the group? It is **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin.** He and Rava explained: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where the husband brought witnesses** to testify **that** the wife **committed adultery, and the** wife's **father brought witnesses, and** through their testimony that they were with the first witnesses in some other place at the time of the alleged transgression, they **classified the husband's witnesses** as **conspiring witnesses.** In that case, if the father **comes to collect monetary restitution from the husband** for defamation, the case may be adjudicated **by three** judges. **And in** a case of **capital punishment,** such as the trial of the husband's witnesses, the case must be judged **by twenty-three** judges.

Rava responds — identifying himself as one who explained this alongside *ari she-ba-chavura*, the lion of the group, Rabbi Chiya bar Avin — that the *beraita* must be read in a very specific context. The case is one in which the husband brought witnesses that his wife committed adultery, and then the father brought witnesses to prove those witnesses were *zomemim*. The proceedings have now shifted: we are no longer dealing with a case against the woman but with a case against the man. The question before the court is whether to extract money from the husband.

### Man vs Woman Cases

In the case against the man, Rava explains, a court of three is always appropriate. The husband is never going to face a capital proceeding — whatever happens, his liability is monetary. It is therefore accurate for the Sages to say: when the case is a *dinei mamonot* case against the man, three suffice. The phrase *u-vimkom nefashot be-esrim u-shlosha* — and where there is a capital dimension, twenty-three — most naturally refers to the case involving the witnesses or, alternatively, to the case against the woman, who genuinely risks a capital outcome if the proceedings against her take a certain turn. The *beraita*'s formulation thus does not contradict Ulla at all: it is simply addressing a different sub-case, the case against the man, where the Sages willingly grant that three suffice.

### Yerushalmi Parallel

This explanation closely mirrors the structure of the *Yerushalmi*, which we had already studied. The *Yerushalmi* oscillates between two framings: the case against the woman, where a capital outcome is always in the background and the Sages therefore insist on twenty-three, and the case against the man, where the monetary dimension is primary and a court of three is appropriate. Reading the *Bavli*'s *beraita* in light of the *Yerushalmi* now renders the passage considerably more transparent. The Sages who insist on twenty-three when the case is against the woman — because she herself could be subjected to capital punishment if adultery is proven — readily concede that when the case is against the man, who will never face a capital proceeding, three are sufficient. The *Yerushalmi*'s two framings and the *Bavli*'s resolution of the *beraita* are telling the same story.

## Alternative Readings

### Abaye & Hatra'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא חָיְשִׁינַן לְלַעַז, וּמִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹנִים. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן דְּאַתְרוֹ בַּהּ סְתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis can be explained in another manner. According to **everyone,** one need be **concerned for rumors, and** also **for the honor of the first** judges. And with regard to the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis, **here we are dealing with** a case **where** the witnesses to the adultery **warned her** that adultery is a capital transgression **without specification** as to the exact manner of death penalty she would receive.

At this point the *sugya* takes a dramatic turn. The *Yerushalmi* would stop here; the *Bavli* does not. Having established the core positions, the *Gemara* deploys a characteristic move: it uses the ambiguity of the dispute in the *Mishnah* as an invitation to import a series of entirely distinct legal debates and read them into the disagreement between Rabbi Meir and the Sages. Each of the following readings is offered by a later *Amora* and is, as will become apparent, quite distant from what the *Mishnah* most plainly seems to be discussing.

Abaye offers the first alternative reading. He sets aside both the *la'az* question and the concern for the honor of the judges, asserting that everyone agrees on those points. The real dispute, Abaye says, concerns the specificity required in a *hatra'ah* — the formal warning that must be delivered to a defendant before a capital sentence can be imposed. The case, Abaye maintains, is one where the witnesses warned the woman that adultery is forbidden and punishable by death, but did not specify *which* form of death she would incur.

### Baraita Death Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: וּשְׁאָר כׇּל חַיָּיבֵי מִיתוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, אֵין מְמִיתִין אוֹתָם אֶלָּא בְּעֵדָה וְעֵדִים וְהַתְרָאָה, וְעַד שֶׁיּוֹדִיעוּהוּ שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב מִיתָה בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the matter **is** in accordance with **this *tanna*,** whose statement follows: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **all the others,** those who are **liable for** the various **death penalties** stated **in the Torah** other than the inciter to idol worship, the court **executes them only when** the following elements are present: **The congregation,** represented by the court; **and witnesses; and forewarning** just before the defendant commits the transgression. **And** the court does not execute him **unless** the witnesses had **informed** the defendant **that he is liable** to receive **the death penalty from** the **court.**

The *Gemara* cites a *beraita* that establishes the general rule for capital cases: all those liable for death penalties in the Torah require a full court (*edah*) of twenty-three, open witnesses (not concealed, as in the case of the *meisit u-madiach*), and explicit *hatra'ah* — a warning that makes clear the person faces capital liability before the *beit din*. The ordinary standard is that the defendant must be informed he faces a death penalty.

### Meir No Capital

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיּוֹדִיעוּהוּ בְּאֵיזֹה מִיתָה הוּא נֶהֱרָג.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The defendant is not executed **unless** the witnesses had **informed** the defendant **by which** form of the **death** penalty **he is** to be **killed.**

But Rabbi Yehuda — cited in that same *beraita* — goes further: the warning must specify *which* form of execution the person faces, not merely that death is at stake. Abaye maps this debate onto the *Mishnah*. Rabbi Meir holds that the warning here was delivered in the general form — death, without specification — and therefore, adopting the stringent position of Rabbi Yehuda, no valid capital case can proceed. Without a capital case, the proceedings are purely monetary, and three judges suffice. The Sages, by contrast, hold that the general-form warning is sufficient to support a capital proceeding, and therefore twenty-three are required. Whatever one thinks of this reading's fidelity to the *Mishnah*'s plain meaning, it is internally coherent.

### Pappa Chaverah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הָכָא בְּאִשָּׁה חֲבֵירָה עָסְקִינַן, וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה וְרַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: חָבֵר אֵין צָרִיךְ הַתְרָאָה, לְפִי שֶׁלֹּא נִיתְּנָה הַתְרָאָה אֶלָּא לְהַבְחִין בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג לְמֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** a similar explanation: **Here we are dealing with a woman** who is **a *ḥaveira*,** meaning that she is knowledgeable with regard to Torah matters. **And they disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda and the Rabbis. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda says: A *ḥaver*,** as he is knowledgeable in Torah, **does not need** to be issued **a forewarning** by the witnesses, **because forewarning is given only to distinguish between unintentional** sin **and intentional** sin, and in the case of a *ḥaver* it is clear that he is aware of the *halakha*.

Rav Pappa offers yet another reading. He proposes that the case concerns a woman who is a *chaverah* — a learned woman, familiar with Jewish law. The dispute, he says, parallels the debate between Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages on the question of whether a *talmid chakham* requires *hatra'ah* at all before being subjected to capital punishment.

The reasoning behind the exemption from *hatra'ah* for a scholar is that the entire purpose of *hatra'ah* is to establish that the person acted with full intentionality — to distinguish *shogeg* from *mezid*. If someone is demonstrably learned, they obviously know what they are doing, and the warning adds nothing, much as one would not need to warn a criminal lawyer that what they are doing is a crime. The counterargument — which the other Sages maintain — is that *hatra'ah* serves purposes beyond establishing intent. One reason is that people can forget in a moment of passion, even if they know the law. Another, deeper rationale is that the warning converts the act into one of defiance: being told explicitly and still proceeding is an act of willful rejection of the law. The *Gemara* later in Sanhedrin will develop this further, articulating the principle of *ad she-yatir atzmo le-oso mita* — the requirement that the person effectively accept upon themselves the capital liability as a condition of the court's authority to execute. On Rav Pappa's reading, Rabbi Meir holds that a *chaverah* does not require *hatra'ah* and therefore is never rendered liable for death — the case remains monetary and three suffice. The Sages hold that even a *chaverah* requires *hatra'ah*, and since she received it, the case is a capital one requiring twenty-three.

### Ashi Wrong Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דְּאַתְרוֹ בַּיהּ מַלְקוֹת, וְלָא אַתְרוֹ בַּיהּ קַטְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says** a different explanation: The dispute in the mishna concerns a case **where** the witnesses **warned her** that she would be liable to receive **lashes, but** they **did not warn her** that she would be liable to receive the **death** penalty.

Rav Ashi proposes a different variant: the warning was delivered, but the content was wrong. The witnesses warned the woman that she faced *malkus* — lashes — not death. Since the warning was incorrectly formulated, she can never be subject to the death penalty. But the question then becomes: does a case of *malkus* require three judges or twenty-three? The *Gemara* notes that this parallels the dispute recorded later in our own *Mishnah*: the Mishnah there states that *malkus* requires three judges, but in the name of Rabbi Yishmael it was taught that it requires twenty-three. On Rav Ashi's reading, Rabbi Meir adopts the position that *malkus* requires three, and the Sages adopt the position that it requires twenty-three. This reading, one must acknowledge, fits the language of the *Mishnah* very poorly: the *Mishnah*'s Sages explicitly invoke *dinei nefashot* as the reason for requiring twenty-three, yet on Rav Ashi's reading the case has nothing to do with *dinei nefashot* at all. One could perhaps have read this more naturally as a case against the husband — who pays a hundred *shekel* and receives *malkus* — and then framed the debate simply as whether *malkus* requires three or twenty-three. But as it stands, the reading is notably strained.

### Ravina Relative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּמְצָא אֶחָד מִן הָעֵדִים קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל, וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** a different explanation: The case in the mishna is discussing a situation **where one of the witnesses is found** to be a close **relative or a disqualified** witness, but two valid witnesses still remain. **And** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

Ravina offers a final alternative in this series. The case, he says, involves three witnesses, one of whom turns out to be a relative — *karov* — or otherwise disqualified. The question is whether the presence of the invalid witness nullifies the testimony of the remaining two for purposes of a capital case, or whether the two valid witnesses can still function. Rabbi Meir says they cannot: following the position of Rabbi Yosi, the two valid witnesses may still testify in a monetary proceeding, but the invalid witness disqualifies the entire group from serving in *dinei nefashot* — leaving the case as a purely monetary matter, with three judges. The Sages say the case remains a capital one, paralleling the position of Rabbi (Rebbi), who, as we shall see in the following section, holds that an invalid witness disqualifies the group only under certain conditions of active participation.

## Akiva & Witnesses

### Akiva Third Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר, לֹא בָּא שְׁלִישִׁי אֶלָּא לְהַחְמִיר עָלָיו, לַעֲשׂוֹת דִּינוֹ כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּאֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Makkot* 5b) that **Rabbi Akiva says:** When the verse states: "At the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall a matter be established" (Deuteronomy 17:6), **the third** witness is mentioned **only to be stringent with him, to make his status like these** other two witnesses.

The foregoing discussion of *karov* and *pasul* draws the *Gemara* into a broader analysis of Rabbi Akiva's position on the relationship between multiple witnesses. Rabbi Akiva addresses the verse *al pi shnayim eidim o shlosha eidim yumat ha-met* — "by the mouth of two witnesses or three witnesses shall the dead be put to death." The redundancy is striking: if two witnesses suffice to impose capital liability, why mention three? Rabbi Akiva explains that the verse comes to be *machmir* — strict — with respect to the third witness, teaching that he receives the same punishment as the first two if they are all found to be *zomemim*. The argument that the third witness might raise — that his testimony was superfluous, that even without him the first two had already established the defendant's liability, and therefore he should not be punished — is rejected. The Torah links two and three witnesses together, treating the third as fully culpable alongside the first two.

The further implication Rabbi Akiva draws is a moral one: if the Torah treats one who peripherally attaches himself (*nitepal*) to sinners as bearing the full liability of the principal sinners, then all the more so should one who attaches himself to those performing a *mitzvah* receive the full reward of the principal actors. It is a finely structured *derash*.

### All Void Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה שְׁנַיִם, נִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵלָה, אַף שְׁלֹשָׁה, נִמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵלָה. וּמִנַּיִין שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עֵדִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, this teaches that **just as** in the case of **two** witnesses, if **one of them is found** to be a close **relative or a disqualified** witness **their testimony is nullified,** as the single remaining witness is not able to testify alone, **so too,** in the case of **three** witnesses, if **one of them is found** to be a close **relative or a disqualified** witness **their testimony is nullified. And from where** is it derived **that** this applies **even** to a group of **one hundred** witnesses? **The verse states: "Witnesses."**

Rabbi Akiva proceeds to derive from this linkage of two and three a broader principle of witness invalidation. Just as with two witnesses — if one of them is found to be *karov* or *pasul*, the entire testimony is void — so too with three: *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul, eidusam batelah*, if one of three witnesses is disqualified, the entire testimony is nullified. And this extends further: even if there are a hundred witnesses, and one proves to be invalid, all are disqualified. The verse's use of the word *eidim* — witnesses — treats the entire group as a single unit.

### Yosi Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּדִינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, אֲבָל בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת תִּתְקַיֵּים עֵדוּת בַּשְּׁאָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: In what** situation **is this statement,** that if one of them is found to be a close relative or a disqualified witness their testimony is nullified, **said? In** cases of **capital law. But in** cases of **monetary law, the testimony** may be **upheld with the other** witnesses.

Rabbi Yosi limits this principle. He holds that *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* applies exclusively to *dinei nefashot* — capital cases. In *dinei mamonot* — monetary cases — if one of multiple witnesses is invalid, the remaining valid witnesses may continue to testify and their testimony stands. The invalid witness does not drag down the entire group in a monetary context.

### Rabbi Active Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת וְאֶחָד דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, וְאֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהִתְרוּ בָּהֶן, אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁלֹּא הִתְרוּ בָּהֶן,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** Rabbi Akiva's opinion applies to **both** cases of **monetary law and** cases of **capital law. And when** is this so? **When** the relatives or disqualified witnesses also **warned** the transgressors and therefore actively included themselves in the group of witnesses; **but when** they **did not warn** the transgressors, they are not counted as witnesses at all.

Rabbi (Rebbi Yehuda ha-Nasi) takes a different approach. He holds that the principle applies to both *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot* — the invalid witness can theoretically nullify the group in monetary cases as well. However, Rebbi qualifies his position: *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* applies only *be-zman she-histru bahen* — when the other witnesses actively demonstrated their participation, such as by delivering the *hatra'ah* warning. If they merely happened to be present and observed the event passively, without actively presenting themselves as witnesses, they are not treated as part of a unified group, and the invalidity of one does not contaminate the others.

Rebbi illustrates this with a case: two brothers are walking down the street with a third person and they witness a murder. If the brothers — who are *krovim* to each other and hence mutually disqualified — observed the event alongside the third witness, does that invalidate the third witness's testimony? Rebbi's answer is no, because passive observation does not constitute active membership in a witness group. The logic is that *nimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* presupposes a unified group of witnesses; passive co-presence does not create such a group.

### Tosafot Yichud

Tosafot on 9a raises the practical implications of Rebbi's principle for everyday life, asking why weddings and other public events at which relatives are present do not render the valid witnesses who also attend permanently disqualified, if those relatives intended to serve as witnesses. Tosafot resolves this by pointing to the principle of *yichud eidim* — the formal identification of witnesses as a distinct unit — and by citing the ruling in the first chapter of Makkot: a witness is only considered to have formally joined the witness group if he actively appears before the *beit din* to testify, not merely by intending to testify at the time of the witnessed event. Even the intention to serve as a witness at the time of the act, without subsequently appearing before a court, is insufficient to constitute one a formal member of the witness group. As Tosafot clarifies: *she-holchin u-me'idin kulam be-veit din* — they must actually go and testify before the *beit din* — and mere intent at the time of witnessing falls short of this threshold.

## Invalid Witness Reading

### Meir One Invalid

Returning to the application of these principles to the *Mishnah*, the *Gemara* now works through Ravina's reading in greater detail. The case has three witnesses, one of whom is *pasul*. Rabbi Meir, on this account, holds that the presence of the invalid witness means that the remaining two valid witnesses cannot serve in a capital case — the group is treated as invalidated for *dinei nefashot*. However, following Rabbi Yosi's distinction, the two valid witnesses can still function in a monetary proceeding. The case is therefore only a *dinei mamonot* case, and three judges suffice.

### Chachamim Two Valid

The Sages, by contrast, follow Rebbi's position. They argue that the invalid witness did not give the *hatra'ah* warning and did not actively participate in the witness group in the manner required by Rebbi's standard — he was not, in the relevant sense, an active member of the witness group at all. Accordingly, the remaining two valid witnesses are unaffected by his disqualification and can serve fully in a *dinei nefashot* proceeding. The case therefore remains a capital one, requiring twenty-three.

### Non-Witness Warning

Another variant is offered: the warning was given not by the witnesses themselves but by bystanders — others who were present but who were not testifying in the case. This too bears on whether the case can proceed as a capital matter. The debate is framed as paralleling Rabbi Yosi's position that *l'olam eino neherag ad she-yiyu pi shnayim eidim matrin bo* — the person can only be executed if the witnesses themselves delivered the warning, based on the verse *al pi shnayim eidim*, "by the mouth of two witnesses," implying that the warning must come from the mouths of those who are also testifying. Rabbi Meir holds that the warning delivered by non-witnesses does not suffice for a capital proceeding, and therefore the case remains monetary, with three judges. The Sages hold that the warning is valid regardless of who delivered it, and the case may proceed as a capital matter.

### Bedikos Contradiction

A final variant: the witnesses contradicted one another not in the *chakiyrot* — the core examination of when and where the event took place — but in the *bedikot*, the supplementary questioning on ancillary details. The *chakiyrot* are the examinations that can render witnesses *zomemim*: if witnesses cannot agree on the time and place of the event, their testimony is invalidated as a capital matter. But a contradiction in the *bedikot* — say, a discrepancy about the precise description of the fig tree under which the event occurred — is a different matter. The *Gemara* cites the famous case of Ben Zakkai, who would press witnesses on minute details such as the direction of the fig stems, seeking any inconsistency to disqualify them from capital testimony. The debate between Ben Zakkai and the Sages is whether such contradictions in the *bedikot* suffice to invalidate testimony for *dinei nefashot*. Rabbi Meir follows the view that they do — the testimony is void as a capital matter, leaving only a monetary case with three judges — while the Sages hold that such minor inconsistencies do not disqualify the witnesses, and the case proceeds as a capital matter with twenty-three.

It is worth pausing to take stock. The *Bavli* has now generated approximately ten distinct readings of the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Sages in the *Mishnah*. Of these, perhaps two — the ones paralleling the *Yerushalmi*'s explicit framings — are genuinely grounded in what the *Mishnah* most plainly seems to be debating. The remaining readings are, transparently, imports from other areas of the law, used to populate an ambiguous dispute with content drawn from elsewhere. This is the *Bavli* at its most characteristic: treating the uncertain space within a cryptic *Mishnaic* dispute as an opportunity to explore a range of other legal questions and to map the contours of broader principles across an array of connected domains.

## Double Aveira

### Classic Setup

The *sugya* concludes with a passage from Rav Yosef that returns to the classical scenario of *motzi shem ra*: the husband brings witnesses that his wife committed adultery; the father then brings witnesses who prove the husband's witnesses were *zomemim* — that is, that they were lying about the time or place of the alleged adultery and therefore could not have seen what they claimed to have seen.

### Witnesses Executed

The immediate consequence: the husband's witnesses are executed, since they sought to have the woman put to death and the law of *eidim zomemim* requires that they receive the punishment they sought to impose. However — and this is where *kim lei* enters — even though their false testimony would also have cost the woman her *kesubah*, the witnesses do not additionally pay the monetary amount. As Rashi reads it, this is an application of *kim lei*: the capital penalty they face is the greater punishment, and it subsumes the monetary liability.

### Reversal Case

Now the scenario reverses. The husband himself goes on the offensive: he brings a new set of witnesses who prove that the father's witnesses — the ones who had just gotten the husband's first witnesses condemned as *zomemim* — were themselves lying. The father's witnesses, it turns out, were the false ones all along.

### Both Penalties

The father's witnesses are now executed: they attempted to have the husband's legitimate witnesses condemned and killed as *zomemim*, and the law of *eidim zomemim* applies to them in kind. But crucially, they are also required to pay money to the husband — specifically, the hundred *shekel* that is the penalty of *motzi shem ra*. The husband's first witnesses, when they testified, faced the potential consequence of the hundred-*shekel* fine being imposed on the husband. When the father's witnesses falsely negated that testimony and caused the husband's witnesses to be condemned, they created a monetary liability for the husband. They now owe that money to him.

### Separate Crimes

Why does *kim lei* not apply here, allowing the father's witnesses to escape the monetary penalty by virtue of being executed? The answer is *mammon le-zeh ve-nefashot le-zeh* — the money is owed to one party and the capital liability is to another. The crime against the witnesses is adjudicated as a capital matter: the father's witnesses tried to have the husband's witnesses killed, and for that they are executed. The crime against the husband is adjudicated as a separate monetary matter: the father's witnesses caused the husband to incur a financial loss by falsely discrediting the testimony that would have established his right to the hundred-*shekel* payment. Because these are separate offenses against separate parties — each adjudicated independently — there is no single proceeding in which *kim lei* can operate to override the lesser penalty. Both penalties stand: the father's witnesses (or their estates) owe the husband one hundred *shekel*, and the father's witnesses are also put to death. The elegance of *mammon le-zeh ve-nefashot le-zeh* lies precisely in this separation: by disaggregating the offenses, the law ensures that neither the capital victim nor the monetary victim is left without remedy.
