# Kiddushin 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Korcha & Women

### Issi's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסִי תָּנֵי: אַף ״בַּל יִקְרְחוּ״ – נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּאִיסִי דְּדָרֵישׁ הָכִי? ״בָּנִים אַתֶּם לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם לֹא תִתְגֹּדְדוּ וְלֹא תָשִׂימוּ קׇרְחָה בֵּין עֵינֵיכֶם לָמֵת. כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״, בָּנִים וְלֹא בָּנוֹת – לְקׇרְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Isi taught** in a *baraita*: **Women are also exempt** from the prohibition: **Do not make baldness** upon your heads, a prohibition against tearing out one's hair in grief over someone's death. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Isi?** The Gemara explains **that he teaches as follows:** The verse states: **"You are the sons of the Lord your God; you shall not cut yourselves, nor make any baldness between your eyes for the dead. For you are a holy people to the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 14:1–2). This verse, which applies to **sons and not daughters,** is referring **to** causing **baldness,** and therefore this prohibition includes only men.

The *gemara* picks up at the very bottom of 35b, continuing the discussion of the scope of *mitzvot* as they apply to women and men. The established principle is that all negative prohibitions apply equally to both, with the exception of the shaving and cutting of the forelocks, the shaving of the beard, and the *mitzvah* of *tuma* for *Kohanim*. We are now also dealing with the position of *Issi*, who holds that the prohibition of making a bald spot on one's head — *korcha* — likewise does not apply to women.

### Banim v'lo Banot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְקׇרְחָה, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לִגְדִידָה? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״ – הֲרֵי גְּדִידָה אָמוּר, הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים בָּנִים וְלֹא בָּנוֹת – לְקׇרְחָה.
>
> **Translation:** Do **you say** that this is referring **to** causing **baldness, or is it** perhaps referring **only to** the prohibition against **cutting,** which appears first? The Gemara answers that **when it states: "For you are a holy people to the Lord your God,"** it **is stated** with regard to the prohibition against **cutting,** and this verse applies to both men and women, as they are all members of God's people. **How** then **do I realize** and explain the emphasis on **sons and not daughters?** This is referring **to** the prohibition against causing **baldness.**

The *gemara* raises an initial challenge to *Issi*'s position. The verse introducing the prohibition of *korcha* opens with the phrase *banim atem la-Shem Elokeichem* — "you are children of the Lord your God" — immediately before prohibiting the making of a bald spot or a laceration. *Issi* reads *banim* and not *banot* as excluding women from *korcha*. But the *gemara* asks: *ve'eima banim ve-lo banot bein le'korcha bein le'gedida* — why not say that the exclusion of women applies equally to both the bald spot and the laceration? After all, both appear in close proximity to the same phrase.

The *gemara* answers: *ve'hachi ksiv Am Kadosh atah la-Shem Elokecha bis'rita hu d'chsiv* — the end of the verse, from which *Issi* derives the inclusion of women in the prohibition of laceration, speaks specifically of *s'rita*. *Rashi* explains that *s'rita* refers to a laceration made with the hand rather than with an implement such as a knife, as distinct from *gedida*. One might then ask: since *s'rita* and *gedida* appear in different verses, perhaps women should be excluded from both based on *banim* and not *banot*? The *gemara* replies: *ksavar Issi s'rita u-gedida achas hi* — *Issi* holds that *s'rita* and *gedida* are in substance the same prohibition. Therefore the verse employing the phrase *Am Kadosh* comes to include women in that single prohibition of laceration, and women are correspondingly excluded only from *korcha*.

### Abaye's Gezeira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּאִיסִי, דְּגָמַר ״קׇרְחָה״ ״קׇרְחָה״ מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, מָה לְהַלָּן נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת – אַף כָּאן נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that **this is the reason of Isi** for exempting women from the prohibition against baldness: **As he learns** the prohibition against causing **baldness** in grief over someone who is dead through a verbal analogy **from** the prohibition against causing **baldness** stated with regard to **the sons of Aaron.** The verse states with regard to priests: "They shall not make baldness upon their head" (Leviticus 21:5). **Just as there,** with regard to priests, **women are exempt,** as the expression "the sons of Aaron" serves to exclude the daughters of Aaron, **so too here, women are exempt.**

At the top of 36a, the *gemara* presents *Abaye*'s derivation of *Issi*'s position: *Abaye amar hainu ta'ama d'Issi, de-gamar korcha korcha mi-Bnei Aharon* — he learns it through a *gezeira shava* of *korcha-korcha* from the passage dealing with *Bnei Aharon*. The Torah states separately, regarding the *Kohanim*: *lo yikrechu korcha be-rosham u-fe'at zekanam lo yegalechu* — they shall not make a bald spot on their head nor shall they shave the corners of their beards. This parallel prohibition is linked by the shared term *korcha* to the prohibition incumbent upon all of Israel.

Because the verse concerning *Bnei Aharon* says *banim* — sons — this naturally implies the exclusion of daughters. *Abaye* argues: *mi-Bnei Aharon mah le-halan nashim peturot af kan nashim peturot* — just as the prohibition for *Kohanim* is limited to the males and not the females, so too the parallel prohibition for all Israel is limited to men and not women.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּכִי כְּתִיב קְרָא, בְּכוּלֵּי עִנְיָינָא הוּא דִּכְתִיב, נִשְׁתּוֹק קְרָא מִינֵּיהּ וְתֵיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: וּמָה כֹּהֲנִים שֶׁרִיבָּה בָּהֶם הַכָּתוּב מִצְוֹת יְתֵירוֹת – ״בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״ – וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אַהֲרֹן, יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if we maintain that when** the Merciful One **writes:** "The sons of Aaron" (Leviticus 21:1), it **is written with regard to the entire matter** of that chapter, including the prohibition against causing baldness, **let the verse be silent about** this prohibition concerning all Jews. **And** this *halakha* **could be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, as **I** could **say** the following: **If** in the case of **priests, for whom the verse includes additional mitzvot,** the prohibition against causing baldness applies only to **the sons of Aaron and not the daughters of Aaron,** is it **not all the more so** the case with regard to **Israelites,** who have fewer mitzvot, that only the men should be obligated and not the women?

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: if *Issi* is already relying on the formulation *Bnei Aharon ve-lo bnot Aharon*, he has no need for a *gezeira shava* at all. Once we know that female *Kohanim* are exempt from *korcha*, we could simply apply a *kal va-chomer*: if women are excluded even among *Kohanim* — who bear additional *mitzvot* — then certainly women among ordinary Israelites should be excluded. *Mah Kohanim she-ribba bahem ha-katuv mitzvot yetirot Bnei Aharon ve-lo bnot Aharon, Yisrael lo kol she-ken* — all the more so for non-*Kohanim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי לָאו גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Nevertheless, the verbal analogy is necessary. As, **were it not for** the **verbal analogy I would say** that the *halakhot* of ritual impurity **concluded** discussion of **that matter,** and the prohibition against causing baldness applies to all the descendants of Aaron, including women.

The *gemara* responds: *ilmalei gezeira shava hava amina hifsik ha-inyan* — had there been no *gezeira shava*, one might have thought that the language *Bnei Aharon* does not apply to *korcha* in that passage because there is a textual interruption between the introduction of *Bnei Aharon* and the eventual verse prohibiting the bald spot. The *gezeira shava*, by linking the two passages, tells us there is something to be learned from the connection, and what is learned is the limitation: *banim* and not *banot*. Without the *gezeira shava*, the interruption in the text would have prevented that inference.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי נֵימָא הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן, וְאִי מִשּׁוּם גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה – מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״לֹא יִקְרְחוּ״, יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ קָרַח אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ קְרָחוֹת לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״קׇרְחָה״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **now too, let us say** that the *halakhot* of ritual impurity **concluded** discussion of **that matter,** and it is prohibited for the daughters of Aaron to cause baldness. **And if** you maintain that the reason the prohibition stated with regard to priests does not apply to women is **due to** the **verbal analogy** employing the term "make baldness," which serves to connect the *halakha* stated with regard to priests with the *halakha* stated with regard to all Jews, this verbal analogy **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"They shall not make baldness [*yikreḥu*** *korḥa*]" (Leviticus 21:5). If the verse had stated only "*yikreḥu*," one **might** have thought that **even** if **one made four or five** patches of **baldness he would be obligated** for **only one** violation, as there is only one prohibition against making a bald spot. Therefore **the verse also states "*korḥa*," to render him liable for each and every one** of the bald spots.

The *gemara* then pushes further: but now that there is a *gezeira shava*, let us say the interruption nonetheless applies, and do not derive the exclusion of women from it. The *gezeira shava* must simply be telling us something else — namely, what we learn in the *baraita*. The question is therefore: what is the *gezeira shava* of *korcha-korcha* actually accomplishing?

### Gezeira Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּרֹאשָׁם״ מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תִתְגֹּדְדוּ וְלֹא תָשִׂימוּ קׇרְחָה בֵּין עֵינֵיכֶם לָמֵת״, יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל בֵּין הָעֵינַיִם, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת כָּל הָרֹאשׁ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּרֹאשָׁם״ – לְחַיֵּיב עַל הָרֹאשׁ כְּבֵין הָעֵינַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **What** is the meaning when **the verse states: "Upon their head"** (Leviticus 21:5), with regard to the prohibition against a priest causing baldness? **Because it is stated: "You shall not cut yourselves, nor make any baldness between your eyes for the dead"** (Deuteronomy 14:1), one **might** have thought that **he is obligated only for** removing the hair **between the eyes. From where** is it derived **to include the entire head** in this prohibition? When **the verse states "upon their head,"** it serves **to render** a priest **liable** for removing hair **on** his entire **head like** the spot **between the eyes.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא כֹּהֲנִים, שֶׁרִיבָּה בָּהֶם הַכָּתוּב מִצְוֹת יְתֵירוֹת, יִשְׂרָאֵל מְנָלַן? – נֶאֱמַר כָּאן: ״קׇרְחָה״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״קׇרְחָה״, מָה כָּאן חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל קׇרְחָה וְקׇרְחָה, וְחַיָּיב עַל הָרֹאשׁ כְּבֵין הָעֵינַיִם – אַף לְהַלָּן חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל קׇרְחָה וְקׇרְחָה, וְחַיָּיב עַל הָרֹאשׁ כְּבֵין הָעֵינַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And I have** derived **only** that this *halakha* applies to **priests, for whom the verse includes additional mitzvot; from where do we** derive that it applies to every **Israelite,** i.e., all Jews are liable for each and every bald spot and for the entire head? **It is stated here,** with regard to priests, **"baldness," and it is stated there,** with regard to non-priests, **"baldness." Just as here,** with regard to priests, **one is obligated for each and every bald spot, and one is obligated for the entire head like** the spot **between the eyes, so too there,** with regard to all Jews, **one is obligated for each and every bald spot, and one is obligated for the entire head like** the spot **between the eyes.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה לְהַלָּן עַל מֵת, אַף כָּאן עַל מֵת. אִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״קֹרַח״, מַאי ״קׇרְחָה״ – שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** conversely, **just as there,** in the case of Israelites, it is referring specifically to causing baldness **over** someone who is **dead, so too here,** with regard to priests, it is prohibited to cause baldness only **over** someone who is **dead,** not in other circumstances. This *baraita* shows that the verbal analogy is required for the *halakhot* of bald spots. How, then, can it teach the exemption of women? The Gemara answers: **If** it is **so** that it may be used only for one purpose, **let the verse be written** merely **bald [*keraḥ*]. What** is meant by the term **"baldness [*korḥa*]"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** both the verbal analogy that exempts women and the *halakha* that each bald spot constitutes a separate violation.

The *gemara* explains the full content of the *gezeira shava* as presented in the *baraita*. The verse by *Kohanim* reads *lo yikrechu korcha be-rosham* — they shall not make a bald spot on their head. One might have thought that making multiple bald spots constitutes only a single prohibition. *Talmud lomar korcha* — the singular form *korcha* teaches that one is liable for each individual bald spot, *le-chayev al kol achat ve-achat*.

Why then does the verse add the word *be-rosham*, "on their head"? Because the verse for Israel reads *lo titgodedu ve-lo tasimu korcha bein eineichem la-met* — do not make a laceration and do not place a bald spot between your eyes for the dead. One might have thought that the prohibition of *korcha* is limited specifically to the area between the eyes. *Talmud lomar be-rosham* — "on their head" teaches that the prohibition extends across the entire head, *le-chayev al kol ha-rosh ke-vein einav*.

These two details — liability for each separate bald spot, and application to the whole head — might have been thought to apply only to *Kohanim*, who bear additional *mitzvot*. So how do we know they apply to Israel as well? *Ne'emar kan korcha ve-ne'emar le-halan korcha* — the same term appears in both contexts, and through the *gezeira shava* we derive that just as by *Kohanim* one is liable for each bald spot and across the entire head, so too by Israel. And in the reverse direction, just as by Israel the prohibition is specifically in the context of mourning a dead person, *al ha-met*, so too by *Kohanim* the prohibition is limited to the context of mourning a dead person.

This *gezeira shava* of *korcha-korcha* is thus carrying a great deal of substantive content. The *gemara* therefore argues that it is already fully occupied with these teachings, and one cannot assume it is additionally teaching the exclusion of women. The question becomes: how does *Abaye* then derive the exclusion of women from the *gezeira shava*?

### Double Karcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא קֹרַח, מַאי ״קׇרְחָה״ – שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves this in favor of *Abaye*: **If** it is **so** that the Torah merely wanted to convey those teachings, it could have written *kerach* rather than *korcha*. **What** is meant by the term **"baldness [*korḥa*]"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** both the verbal analogy that exempts women and the *halakha* that each bald spot constitutes a separate violation.

The *gemara* resolves this in favor of *Abaye*: *im ken nichtov kra kerach* — if the Torah merely wanted to convey those teachings, it could have written *kerach* rather than *korcha*. The fact that both the verse for *Kohanim* and the verse for Israel use the form *korcha* — with the additional *heh* — signals that there is yet another *gezeira shava* embedded in the text, a secondary derivation. And that secondary *gezeira shava* is what teaches us the limitation of *banim* and not *banot*: the exclusion of women from the prohibition of *korcha* as derived from *Bnei Aharon ve-lo bnot Aharon*. This is *Issi*'s reason, as reconstructed by *Abaye*.

### Rava's Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּאִיסִי, דְּיָלֵיף ״בֵּין עֵינֵיכֶם״ מִתְּפִילִּין, מָה לְהַלָּן נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת, אַף כָּאן נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that **this is Isi's reason, as he derives** the verbal analogy of **"between your eyes"** with regard to baldness **from phylacteries,** concerning which it says: "And they shall be for frontlets between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 11:18): **Just as there,** with regard to phylacteries, **women are exempt, so too here,** in the case of baldness, **women are exempt.**

*Rava* offers an entirely different derivation of *Issi*'s position: *de-yalif bein eineichem mi-tefillin* — he derives the exemption of women from *korcha* through an analogy to *tefillin*. The verse for Israel says *lo tasimu korcha bein eineichem la-met* — do not place a bald spot between your eyes. The same phrase *bein eineichem* also appears in the context of *tefillin*. Since women are exempt from *tefillin*, we derive by analogy — *mah le-halan nashim peturot af kan nashim peturot* — that women are also exempt from the prohibition of *korcha*.

### Abaye v. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּאַבַּיֵּי? ״קֹרַח״ ״קׇרְחָה״ לָא מַשְׁמַע לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **Rava did not state** the same reason **as Abaye?** The Gemara answers: Rava **does not learn** anything from the distinction between ***keraḥ*** and ***korḥa*,** as he maintains that no *halakha* can be derived from this slight difference in language.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּרָבָא? אָמַר לָךְ: תְּפִילִּין גּוּפַיְיהוּ מֵהָכָא גָּמַר לַהּ: מָה לְהַלָּן מְקוֹם שֶׁעוֹשִׂים קׇרְחָה, בְּגוֹבַהּה שֶׁל רֹאשׁ, אַף כָּאן מְקוֹם הַנָּחָה בְּגוֹבַהּ הָרֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And what is the reason** that **Abaye did not state** the same reason **as Rava?** The Gemara answers that Abaye could have **said to you: Phylacteries themselves are derived from here,** i.e., the meaning of the phrase "between your eyes" stated with regard to phylacteries is understood from the case of baldness: **Just as there,** with regard to a bald spot, "between your eyes" is referring to **a place where baldness is formed,** a spot where there is hair, which is **on the upper part of the head** but not actually between the eyes, **so too, the place** where phylacteries are **donned is on the upper part of the head.**

The *gemara* then inquires into each *amora*'s reasons for rejecting the other's approach. *Rava mai ta'ama lo amar ke-Abaye* — why did *Rava* not accept *Abaye*'s derivation? *Kerach karcha lo mashma lei* — he does not accept that the difference in spelling between *kerach* and *korcha* generates a second *gezeira shava*. He remains troubled by the distance in the text between the introduction of *Bnei Aharon* and the eventual discussion of *korcha*, and therefore finds *Abaye*'s approach unconvincing.

Conversely, *v'Abaye mai ta'ama lo amar ke-Rava* — why did *Abaye* not accept *Rava*'s derivation from *tefillin*? *Tefillin gufayhu mei-hacha gamir lah* — because the very rule that *tefillin* are placed on the upper part of the head, corresponding to where hair grows above the forehead and opposite the space between the eyes, is itself learned from the passage about *korcha*. The *gezeira shava* of *bein eineichem* was originally employed to teach something about *tefillin*: *mah le-halan makom she-osim korcha be-govah shel rosh, af kan makom hanacha be-govah ha-rosh* — just as the bald spot between the eyes is understood to refer to the top of the head where hair grows, so too the placement of *tefillin* is on the top of the head. The *rishonim* raise the question of whether one *gezeira shava* can simultaneously teach two separate laws, and the conclusion is that once a *gezeira shava* is focused on clarifying the meaning of a word — in this case, what *bein einav* means — it cannot simultaneously pivot to teach an entirely separate *halacha* such as the exemption of women.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵין לְאַבָּיֵי וּבֵין לְרָבָא, הַאי ״בָּנִים אַתֶּם״, מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And both Abaye and Rava, what do they derive from this** verse: **"You are the sons** to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 14:1)? According to the first explanation of Isi's opinion, the exclusion of women is derived from this phrase, whereas they derive that *halakha* from a different source.

The *gemara* then asks: since both *Abaye* and *Rava* derive the exclusion of women from *korcha* through means other than the opening phrase *banim atem la-Shem Elokeichem*, what do they do with that phrase? The answer is that *Abaye* and *Rava* both require that phrase for an altogether different teaching, as presented in the *baraita* that follows.

## Banim Atem

### Children Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״בָּנִים אַתֶּם לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאַתֶּם נוֹהֲגִים מִנְהַג בָּנִים – אַתֶּם קְרוּיִם בָּנִים, אֵין אַתֶּם נוֹהֲגִים מִנְהַג בָּנִים – אֵין אַתֶּם קְרוּיִם בָּנִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** This verse **is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse: **"You are the sons to the Lord your God,"** indicates that **when you act like sons** and cleave to the Holy One, Blessed be He, **you are called sons,** but when **you do not act like sons you are not called sons.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* transitions to a broader discussion of the phrase *banim atem la-Shem Elokeichem*. According to *Abaye* and *Rava*, this phrase cannot be used to derive the exclusion of women from *korcha* since they each have alternative derivations for that. The phrase is instead required for the teaching of the *baraita*: *banim atem la-Shem Elokeichem, bi-zman she-atem nohagim minhag banim atem keruim banim* — when you conduct yourselves as children, you are called children of God.

### R. Yehuda

This is the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*: *ein atem nohagim minhag banim ein atem keruim banim, divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — when you do not conduct yourselves as children, you are not called children. The implication is that the title is conditional on appropriate behavior, and that engaging in idolatrous practices such as the very rituals of laceration and bald spots — which the *Torah* is here prohibiting — would forfeit the relationship.

### R. Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ אַתֶּם קְרוּיִם בָּנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּנִים סְכָלִים הֵמָּה״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״בָּנִים לֹא אֵמֻן בָּם״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״זֶרַע מְרֵעִים בָּנִים מַשְׁחִיתִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יֵאָמֵר לָהֶם לֹא עַמִּי אַתֶּם יֵאָמֵר לָהֶם בְּנֵי אֵל חָי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir says: Either way you are** still **called sons, as it is stated: "They are foolish sons"** (Jeremiah 4:22). **And it** also **states: "Sons in whom there is no faithfulness"** (Deuteronomy 32:20). **And it states: "A seed of evildoers, sons who deal corruptly"** (Isaiah 1:4). **And it states: "And it shall come to pass that, instead of what was said to them: You are not My people, it shall be said to them: Sons of the living God"** (Hosea 2:1).

*Rabbi Meir* takes a strikingly more compassionate view: *bein kach u-vein kach atem keruim banim* — either way, you are called children of God. This reading seems especially fitting for a period of reflection and repentance, as *Rabbi Meir* insists that the parent-child relationship between God and Israel is never truly severed.

### Scriptural Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא: סְכָלֵי הוּא דְּמִקְּרוּ בְּנֵי, כִּי לֵית בְּהוּ הֵימָנוּתַיְיהוּ לָא מִיקְּרוּ בְּנֵי – תָּא שְׁמַע, וְאוֹמֵר: ״בָּנִים לֹא אֵמֻן בָּם״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** is it necessary to cite these additional proofs introduced by the phrase: **And it states?** All these verses apparently make the same point. The Gemara explains why all the quotes are necessary. **And if you would say:** Granted, when they are **foolish they are** still **called sons,** as the verse states: "Foolish sons," but **when they do not have faithfulness they are not called sons;** therefore, **come** and **hear** another verse. **And** that verse **states: "Sons in whom there is no faithfulness."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: כִּי לֵית בְּהוּ הֵימָנוּתָא הוּא דְּמִיקְּרוּ בָּנִים, כִּי פָּלְחוּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָא מִיקְּרוּ בָּנִים – תָּא שְׁמַע, וְאוֹמֵר: ״זֶרַע מְרֵעִים בָּנִים מַשְׁחִיתִים״. וְכִי תֵּימָא: ״בָּנִים מַשְׁחִיתִים״ הוּא דְּמִיקְּרוּ, בְּנֵי מְעַלְּיָיא לָא מִיקְּרוּ – תָּא שְׁמַע, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יֵאָמֵר לָהֶם לֹא עַמִּי אַתֶּם יֵאָמֵר לָהֶם בְּנֵי אֵל חָי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say: It is when they do not have faithfulness that they are called sons,** as stated, but **when** they **worship idols they are not called sons** anymore; therefore, **come** and **hear: And** the verse **states: "A seed of evildoers, sons who deal corruptly,"** which alludes to the corruption of idol worship. **And if you would say** that although **they are called "sons who deal corruptly," they are no** longer **called full-fledged sons** of God once they have sinned, **come** and **hear: And** the **verse states: "And it shall come to pass that, instead of what was said to them: You are not My people, it shall be said to them: Sons of the living God."** This verse indicates that when the Jews repent they are again called full-fledged sons of God.

The *gemara* presents multiple scriptural proofs in support of *Rabbi Meir*'s position. The verse *banim sechalim hem* — "they are foolish children" — demonstrates that even foolish children retain the designation of children. The verse *banim lo emun bam* — "children without trustworthiness" — shows that even when Israel acts without faithfulness they are still called children. The verse *zera me-re'im banim mash'chitim* — "an evil seed, children who deal corruptly" — shows that even when Israel engages in corruption, which the *gemara* reads as *avoda zara* in line with the usage of *shichet amcha*, they are still called children. And finally, *v'haya bi-mkom asher ye'amer lahem lo ami atem ye'amer lahem bnei Kel chai* — "in the place where it is said to them 'you are not my nation,' it will be said to them 'you are the children of the living God'" — showing that ultimately, even when the most severe divine rejection seems to apply, Israel will still be recognized as God's children, and indeed as children without any negative qualifier, fully restored to their ideal relationship.

## Korban Service

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳, הַסְּמִיכוֹת, וְהַתְּנוּפוֹת, וְהַהַגָּשׁוֹת, וְהַקְּמִיצוֹת, וְהַ[הַ]קְטָרוֹת, וְהַמְּלִיקוֹת, וְהַקַּבָּלוֹת, וְהַזָּאוֹת – נוֹהֲגִים בַּאֲנָשִׁים וְלֹא בְּנָשִׁים, חוּץ מִמִּנְחַת סוֹטָה וּנְזִירָה שֶׁהֵן מְנִיפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the placing** of **hands** on the head of an offering, **and the waving** of certain offerings, **and the bringing near** of meal-offerings to the corner of the altar, **and the removal of a handful** from meal-offerings, **and the burning** of sacrificial parts on the altar, **and the pinching** of bird-offerings, **and the collecting** of blood of offerings in a vessel, **and the sprinkling** of blood, these **apply to men and not to women.** All these mitzvot apply specifically to men and not to women, **except for the meal-offering of a *sota*, and** the meal-offering of **a nazirite woman, which** these women **wave.**

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishnah* dealing with acts associated with *korbanot* from which women are excluded. The *mishnah* enumerates: *ha-smichot ve-ha-tnufot ve-ha-hagashot* — placing the hand on the offering, waving it, and bringing it to the altar — along with *kmitzot*, *haktarot*, *melikot*, *kabalot*, and *hazayot* — taking the handful, burning it, slaughtering the bird with the fingernail, receiving the blood, and sprinkling the blood. All of these, the *mishnah* states, *nohagim ba-anashim ve-lo ba-nashim* — apply to men and not to women. The first two items — *smicha* and *tnufa* — pertain to the owner of the *korban*, while the remaining acts are those performed by the *Kohanim*, from whom female descendants of *Kohanim* are similarly excluded.

### Smicha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ סְמִיכוֹת. דִּכְתִיב: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל... וְסָמַךְ״ – בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל סוֹמְכִים, וְאֵין בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל סוֹמְכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: From where is it derived that **placing** of **hands** on the head of an offering applies only to men? **As it is written: "Speak to the sons of Israel…and he shall place** his hand" (Leviticus 1:2–4), which indicates that **the sons of Israel place hands** on offerings, **but the daughters of Israel do not place hands.**

The *gemara* derives the exclusion of women from *smicha* from the verse *Daber el Bnei Yisrael ve-samach* — speak to the children of Israel, and he shall place his hand. *Bnei Yisrael somchim ve-ein bnot Yisrael somchot* — only the sons of Israel, not the daughters. The *gemara* notes that *Tosafot* raises the broader question of whether women may nonetheless perform *smicha* on a voluntary basis — a significant debate with implications extending beyond this particular act to the general question of women's participation in *mitzvot* from which they are technically exempt. *Ramban* similarly raises the question of whether every instance of the phrase *Bnei Yisrael* in the Torah is meant to exclude women, noting that the phrase appears in many contexts — but for its purposes, *Chazal* understand it as limiting this act to men.

### Tnufa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנוּפוֹת. ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל... וְהֵנִיף״ – בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מְנִיפִין, וְאֵין בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל מְנִיפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that the *halakha* of **waving** is derived from the following verse: **"Speak to the sons of Israel…and he shall wave"** (see Leviticus 7:29–30), which likewise teaches that **the sons of Israel wave, but the daughters of Israel do not wave.**

For *tnufa*, the waving of the *korban*, the verse reads *Daber el Bnei Yisrael ve-henif* — and they shall wave. The derivation is parallel: *Bnei Yisrael menifim ve-ein bnot Yisrael menifot* — only the sons of Israel wave, not the daughters.

### Hagasha & Kmitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגָּשׁוֹת. דִּכְתִיב: ״וְזֹאת תּוֹרַת הַמִּנְחָה... הַקְרֵב אֹתָהּ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״ – בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **bringing near,** this is **as it is written: "And this is the law of the meal-offering: The sons of Aaron shall bring it near"** (Leviticus 6:7). This teaches that **the sons of Aaron** bring the meal-offering near, **but not the daughters of Aaron.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְמִיצוֹת. דִּכְתִיב: ״וֶהֱבִיאָהּ אֶל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן וְקָמַץ״ – בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **the removal of a handful,** this is **as it is written: "And he shall bring it to the sons of Aaron,** the priests, **and he shall remove a handful"** (Leviticus 2:2). This teaches that **the sons of Aaron** take a handful, **but not the daughters of Aaron.**

For *hagasha* — the bringing of the *mincha* to the corner of the altar — the verse reads *Ve-zot torat ha-mincha, hakrev otah bnei Aharon* — this is the law of the *mincha*, the sons of Aaron shall bring it. *Bnei Aharon ve-lo bnot Aharon* — the sons and not the daughters. For *kmitza* — the taking of the handful — the verse reads *ve-heviah el bnei Aharon ve-kamatz* — and he shall bring it to the sons of Aaron and they shall take the *kmitza*. Again, *bnei Aharon ve-lo bnot Aharon*.

### Haktara & Melika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקְטָרוֹת. דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִקְטִירוּ אֹתוֹ בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״ – בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן, וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the **burning** of sacrificial parts, this is **as it is written: "And the sons of Aaron shall burn it"** (Leviticus 3:5). This teaches that it is **the sons of Aaron** who burn the parts, **but not the daughters of Aaron.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְּלִיקוֹת. דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמָלַק... וְהִקְטִיר״, אִיתַּקַּשׁ מְלִיקָה לְהַקְטָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **pinching,** this is **as it is written: "And he shall pinch…and he shall burn"** (Leviticus 1:15). The verse **juxtaposes killing to burning,** and just as the burning of an offering must be performed by men, so too pinching may be performed only by men.

For *haktara* — burning the offering on the altar — the verse reads *ve-hiktiru oto bnei Aharon* — the sons of Aaron shall burn it. For *melika* — the slaughtering of the bird with the fingernail — the verse reads *u-malak ve-hiktir* — he shall cut it and burn it on the altar. The *gemara* notes that *melika* is juxtaposed to *haktara* — *ve-hukash melika le-haktara* — and since *haktara* requires *bnei Aharon* and not *bnot Aharon*, so too *melika* is limited to male *Kohanim*.

### Hazayot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַזָּאוֹת. הַזָּאָה דְּהֵיכָא? אִי דְּפָרָה, ״אֶלְעָזָר״ כְּתִיב בַּהּ. אִי דְּפָנִים, ״הַכֹּהֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ״ כְּתִיב בַּהּ. אֶלָּא הַזָּאָה דְּבֶן עוֹף,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the mishna's ruling that women do not perform **sprinkling: To which sprinkling** is this referring? **If** this is the sprinkling of the ashes **of** the red **heifer, "Elazar" is written with regard to that** action (Numbers 19:4), i.e., this sprinkling is performed by the deputy High Priest. If even other priests cannot perform it, certainly women cannot. **If** this is referring **to** the sprinkling performed **inside** the Holy of Holies, the phrase: **"The anointed priest"** (Leviticus 4:16), **is written with regard to that** rite, and consequently there is no question that women are ineligible. **Rather,** it is referring to **sprinkling** the blood **of a bird**-offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִבֶּן צֹאן. וּמָה בֶּן צֹאן שֶׁלֹּא קָבַע לוֹ כֹּהֵן לִשְׁחִיטָתוֹ – קָבַע לוֹ כֹּהֵן לְהַזָּאָתוֹ, בֶּן עוֹף שֶׁקָּבַע לוֹ כֹּהֵן לִמְלִיקָתוֹ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּקְבַּע לוֹ לְהַזָּאָתוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains **that this is derived through an *a fortiori*** inference **from a sheep** offering: **If** with regard to **a sheep** offering, concerning **which** the Torah **did not establish a priest for its slaughter,** since it may be slaughtered by anybody, the Torah nevertheless **established a priest for its sprinkling,** as the verse states: "And the sons of Aaron, the priests, shall sprinkle its blood" (Leviticus 1:11), **is it not logical** that with regard to **a bird**-offering, concerning **which** the Torah **established a priest for its pinching,** an act parallel to slaughtering a sheep, the Torah likewise **established a priest for its sprinkling?** This proves that the sprinkling of the blood of a bird-offering can be performed only by priests, not by women.

For *hazayot* — sprinkling — the *gemara* is careful to identify which act of sprinkling is meant. If it refers to the sprinkling of the ashes of the *Para Aduma*, the verse explicitly specifies *Elazar*, a named individual, and even though we rule that a *Kohen Gadol* is not strictly required for this act, the explicit naming of a *Kohen Gadol* makes the exclusion of women obvious. Similarly, if it refers to the inner sprinklings on the *Mizbe'ach ha-zahav* — the golden altar — the verse speaks of *ha-Kohen ha-Mashiach*, the anointed *Kohen*, i.e., the *Kohen Gadol*, which again makes the exclusion self-evident. The *mishnah* must therefore be referring to something less obvious: the sprinkling of the blood of a bird offering, where the *Torah* uses the language of *hazayah* rather than *zerikah* because the quantity of blood is too small for the standard act of throwing.

This case is derived through a *kal va-chomer* from an ordinary animal offering. *U-mah ben tzon she-lo kava lo Kohen li-shechitato* — for a sheep or cattle, the slaughter itself does not require a *Kohen* and can be performed even by a woman, yet *kava lo Kohen le-hazayato* — the throwing of the blood still requires a *Kohen*, explicitly derived from *bnei Aharon ve-lo bnot Aharon*. *Ben of she-kava lo Kohen le-melichato* — a bird offering, where even the slaughter requires a *Kohen* to perform it personally, *eino din she-kava lo Kohen le-hazzatah* — it is certainly a logical inference that the sprinkling of its blood likewise requires a *Kohen*, and therefore also excludes women.

## Sota & Nezirah

### Rav Elazar Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִמִּנְחַת סוֹטָה וּנְזִירָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה דְּדָרֵיהּ: לָא תֵּיתֵיב אַכַּרְעָךְ עַד דְּאָמְרַתְּ לִי לְהָא שְׁמַעְתְּתָא: מִנַּיִן לְמִנְחַת סוֹטָה שֶׁטְּעוּנָה תְּנוּפָה? מְנָלַן?! בְּגוּפַהּ כְּתִיב: ״וְהֵנִיף אֶת הַמִּנְחָה״! אֶלָּא: תְּנוּפָה בִּבְעָלִים מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that these mitzvot apply specifically to men but not to women, **except for the meal-offering of a *sota* and a nazirite woman,** which these women wave. **Rabbi Elazar said to Rabbi Yoshiya of his generation,** i.e., the *amora* who was his contemporary, not the *tanna* with the same name who lived earlier: **Do not sit down until you explain this statement to me; from where** is it derived **that the meal-offering of a *sota* requires waving?** The Gemara expresses surprise: What is the meaning of the question: **From where do we** derive it? **It is written in** the chapter dealing with a *sota* **itself: "And he shall wave the meal-offering"** (Numbers 5:25). **Rather,** the question is as follows: **From where do we** derive that the **waving** is performed **by the owners?** Perhaps only the priest waves it?

The *mishnah* concludes with the exceptions: *chutz mi-minchat Sotah ve-nezirah she-hen menifot* — excluding the *mincha* of the *Sotah* and the *nezirah*, which they wave themselves. *Rav Elazar* said to *Rabbi Yoshiya* of his generation: do not sit down until you explain to me this *shemata*. *Minayin li-minchat Sotah she-te'unah tenufah* — from where do we derive that the *mincha* of the *Sotah* requires waving, and moreover that the *Sotah* herself waves it?

### Sota Yad-Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָתְיָא ״יָד״ ״יָד״ מִשְּׁלָמִים. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וְלָקַח הַכֹּהֵן מִיַּד הָאִשָּׁה״ וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״יָדָיו תְּבִיאֶנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoshiya responded: This *halakha* **is derived** through the verbal analogy of the term **"hand"** stated with regard to a *sota* **from** the term **"hand"** stated with regard to **a peace-offering. It is written here,** with regard to the meal-offering of a *sota*: **"And the priest shall take** the meal-offering of jealousy **from the woman's hand** and he shall wave the meal-offering" (Numbers 5:25), **and it is written there: "His own hands shall bring** the offerings of the Lord made by fire; the fat with the breast shall he bring, that the breast may be waved before the Lord" (Leviticus 7:30).

*Rabbi Yoshiya* responds: *be-gufeih ketiv ve-henif et ha-mincha* — it is written explicitly in the passage of the *Sotah* that the *mincha* is waved. But *Rav Elazar* clarifies his question: he already knows the *Kohen* waves it. What he wants to know is *tenufah bi-ve'alim me-nayin* — from where do we know that the owner herself, the woman, waves it? The answer: *atya yad yad mi-shelamim* — this is derived through a *gezeira shava* of *yad-yad* from *shelamim*. In the passage of the *Sotah*, the verse reads *ve-lakach ha-Kohen mi-yad ha-ishah* — the *Kohen* shall take from the hand of the woman, and then he waves it. By *shelamim*, the verse reads *yadav tevinah* — his hands shall bring it, which means the owner waves it.

### Sota Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה כָּאן כֹּהֵן – אַף לְהַלָּן כֹּהֵן, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּעָלִים – אַף כָּאן בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as here,** with regard to a *sota*, **a priest** performs the waving, **so too there,** with regard to a peace-offering, **a priest** performs the waving. And **just as there,** with regard to a peace-offering, the **owner** performs the waving, as indicated by the verse, **so too here,** with regard to a *sota*, the **owner** performs the waving.

From this *gezeira shava*, both passages illuminate each other in a mutual derivation. By the *Sotah*, it is explicit that the *Kohen* waves it — he takes it from her hand and waves it. This teaches us that by *shelamim* as well, not only does the owner wave it, but the *Kohen* also waves it alongside the owner. In the reverse direction, by *shelamim* it is explicit that the owner waves it — *yadav tevinah*. This teaches us that by the *Sotah* as well, the owner, that is, the woman herself, waves it — *mah le-halan be'alim af kan be'alim*.

### Holding Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא כֵּיצַד? כֹּהֵן מַכְנִיס יָדוֹ תַּחַת יָד בְּעָלִים וּמֵנִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** How can both the priest and the owner perform the waving? The owner places his hands beneath the peace-offering, and the **priest places his hand under the hand of the owner and waves** it together with him.

The *gemara* then asks *ha keitzad* — how exactly is this accomplished in practice, given that there is one physical item being waved? *Kohen meniach yado tachat yad be'alim u-menif* — the *Kohen* places his hand beneath the hands of the owner and they wave together. *Tosafot* raises the question of how precisely this is understood: does "beneath" mean the hands are in direct contact with one another, in which case there is no concern about *negiah* but there might be a concern about whether the *Kohen*'s hand constitutes a *hefsek* — an interposition — between the owner's hand and the vessel? Or does "beneath" mean that the owner holds the vessel near its rim while the *Kohen* holds it near its base? *Tosafot* presents both possibilities.

### Nezirah Kaf-Kaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן סוֹטָה, נְזִירָה מְנָלַן? אָתְיָא: ״כַּף״ ״כַּף״ מִסּוֹטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the meal-offering of **a *sota*; from where do we** derive that **a nazirite woman** also waves her meal-offering? The Gemara answers: **It is derived** through a verbal analogy of the term **"palm"** stated with regard to naziriteship **from** the term **"palm"** stated with regard to **a *sota*.** The verse concerning naziriteship states: "And he shall place them upon the palms of the nazirite" (Numbers 6:19), and the verse concerning a *sota* states: "And he shall place it on her palms" (Numbers 5:18). Just as a *sota* waves her meal-offering, so too, a nazirite woman waves hers.

Having established the *Sotah*'s obligation and the manner in which it is fulfilled, the *gemara* notes: *ash'kechan Sotah — nezirah me-nayin* — we have found a source for the *Sotah*, but from where do we derive the same for a *nezirah*? The answer: *atya kaf kaf mi-Sotah* — through a *gezeira shava* of *kaf-kaf*, the hand, linking the passage of the *nezirah* to that of the *Sotah*. Just as by the *Sotah* both the *Kohen* and the owner wave it together, so too by the *nezirah* both the *Kohen* and the owner wave it together, and the *nezirah* herself participates in the waving of her own offering.
