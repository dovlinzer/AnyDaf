# Bekhorot 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ma'aser Behema Intro

### Why in Bechorot

Masechet Bechorot concludes with its final *perek*, dedicated to *ma'aser behema*. Having completed the discussions of *pidyon haben* and the status of the *bechor* with respect to inheritance — a topic developed at greater length in Bava Batra — the tractate turns to this final subject. The immediate question is one of placement: what does *ma'aser behema* have to do with *bechor*?

### Tosafot's Triad

Tosafot addresses this question by noting that the *Gemara* and *Mishnah* frequently speak of a triad: *ma'aser*, *bechor*, and *korban Pesach*. These three are treated similarly in their processing within the *Beit HaMikdash* — particularly in the handling of the *dam* and the manner in which they are brought. Since *korban Pesach* has its own dedicated *masechet*, the connection here is primarily between *bechor* and *ma'aser*.

### Eating w/ Blemish

Beyond the technical parallel Tosafot identifies, there is a more substantive reason why *ma'aser behema* belongs alongside *bechor* in this tractate. These two *korbanot* are unique in that when they acquire a *mum*, they are not redeemed in the conventional sense. With most sanctified animals, a blemish triggers redemption: the animal's value is transferred to money, and that money is used to purchase a future *korban*. Not so with *bechor* and *ma'aser behema*. Their *kedusha* remains intact even after the blemish appears, but that *kedusha* no longer demands that they be brought to the *Beit HaMikdash* as a *korban*. Instead, they are eaten outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — anywhere at all, as the verse states *bish'arecha tochelenu* — in a state of *kedusha* that nonetheless restricts what may be done with them. While the animal is alive, one may neither shear it nor put it to work; the only permitted act is to *shecht* it and eat it. This combination — *kedusha* without the requirement to bring it as a *korban*, together with the ability to eat it with a blemish outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — is what truly distinguishes *bechor* and *ma'aser behema* from all other sanctified animals, and it is this shared characteristic that justifies their treatment together in Masechet Bechorot.

## Mishnah Parameters

### Applies Everywhere

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה נוֹהֵג בָּאָרֶץ וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת, בַּחוּלִּין אֲבָל לֹא בַּמּוּקְדָּשִׁין. וְנוֹהֵג בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן, וְאֵינָן מִתְעַשְּׂרִים מִזֶּה עַל זֶה. בַּכְּבָשִׂים וּבָעִזִּים וּמִתְעַשְּׂרִין מִזֶּה עַל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mitzva of **animal tithe is in effect** both **in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael, **in the presence of,** i.e., in the time of, **the Temple and not in the presence of the Temple.** It is in effect **with regard to non-sacred** animals **but not with regard to sacrificial** animals. **And it is in effect with regard to the herd and the flock, but they are not tithed from one for the other;** and it is in effect **with regard to sheep and goats, and they are tithed from one for the other.**

The *Mishnah* at the opening of the *perek* on 53a establishes the basic parameters of *ma'aser behema*. It begins with a structure familiar from several chapters in Chullin: *ma'aser behema noheg ba'aretz u'v'chutz la'aretz* — it applies both in Eretz Yisrael and outside it. Since the *Torah* obligates the giving of *ma'aser*, the obligation is not inherently territorial. Without a *Beit HaMikdash* one cannot bring it as a *korban*, but the obligation to sanctify the animal and deal with the consequences remains — both *bifnei habayit veshelo bifnei habayit*, whether or not the *Beit HaMikdash* stands.

### Counting Requirement

The *Mishnah* further specifies that the obligation applies to *chullin* — non-sanctified animals — but not to animals already possessing *kedusha*, which are obviously excluded since they belong neither to the ordinary flock nor to their owner in the relevant sense. The *Gemara* will address the details of these exclusions in due course.

### Rosh Chodesh Elul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּחָדָשׁ וּבַיָּשָׁן, וְאֵינָן מִתְעַשְּׂרִים מִזֶּה עַל זֶה. שֶׁהָיָה בַּדִּין, וּמָה אִם הֶחָדָשׁ וְהַיָּשָׁן שֶׁאֵינָן כִּלְאַיִם זֶה בָּזֶה אֵינָם מִתְעַשְּׂרִים מִזֶּה עַל זֶה, כְּבָשִׂים וְעִזִּים שֶׁהֵן כִּלְאַיִם זֶה בָּזֶה — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מִתְעַשְּׂרִים מִזֶּה עַל זֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְצֹאן״, מַשְׁמַע כׇּל צֹאן אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** And it is in effect **with regard to** animals from **the new** flock **and with regard to** animals from **the old** flock, **but they are not tithed from one for the other. As by right, it should be** inferred: **If** in the case of animals from the **new** flock **and the old** flock, **which do not** carry the prohibition of mating **diverse kinds** when mated **with each other** because they are one species, **are** nevertheless **not tithed from one for the other,** then with regard to **sheep and goats, which do** carry the prohibition of mating **diverse kinds** when mated **with each other, is it not right that they will not be tithed from one for the other?** Therefore, **the verse states:** "And all the tithe of the herd **or the flock,** whatever passes under the rod, the tenth shall be sacred to the Lord" (Leviticus 27:32), indicating that with regard to animal tithe, **all** animals that are included in the term **flock** are **one** species.

The *Mishnah* states that *ma'aser behema* applies to cattle and to flock. The operative structure is as follows: all animals born in a given year — with the *Rosh Hashanah* for *ma'aser behema* being Rosh Chodesh Elul, one of the four *rashei shanim* enumerated in the opening *Mishnah* of Rosh Hashanah — are gathered into a pen and counted out. The tenth animal to emerge receives the *kedusha* of *ma'aser behema*. Rosh Chodesh Elul marks the relevant annual boundary because it corresponds to the season in which animals typically become impregnated and begin a new cycle of births, analogously to the way Tishrei marks the new year for grain.

### Mixing Animals

The *Mishnah* specifies that cattle and flock cannot be combined for the purpose of *ma'aser* counting: *ve'ein mit'asrin mizeh al zeh*. If one owns ten cattle and ten sheep, one must count each species separately and may not give a sheep on behalf of cattle or vice versa. Within the category of flock, however, the *Mishnah* distinguishes: sheep and goats — *kevashim u've'izim* — may be counted together and *ma'aser* may be taken from one species on behalf of the other. Thus, a flock of nine goats and one sheep, if the sheep emerges tenth, yields a valid *ma'aser* of sheep on goats.

### Year Separation

The *Mishnah* further states that old animals and newly born animals of different years cannot be combined: *bachadash u'v'yashan umisasrin mizeh al zeh*. If one has procrastinated and failed to count from a previous year's flock, one must count the animals of each year separately. The animals born in one year may not be counted together with those of another year, even though this means that remainders — animals left over after the last group of ten — do not combine across years. This is ultimately a leniency, as it reduces the total number of animals subject to *ma'aser*, but it also means one cannot give a younger animal on behalf of an older year's flock.

## Midrash Halacha

### Kilayim Argument

The *Mishnah* itself engages in a brief *midrash halacha* to justify the rule permitting sheep and goats to be counted together. One might have argued precisely the opposite: since animals of different years — which are not *kilayim* with one another, as they are the same species and may be crossbred freely — nevertheless cannot be counted together for *ma'aser*, how much more so should sheep and goats, which *are kilayim* with one another and represent fundamentally distinct species, be forbidden to count together. In many other areas of *halacha* sheep and goats are rigorously distinguished; they are different enough that crossbreeding them is prohibited. To treat them as a single unit for *ma'aser* would seem to violate the logic applied even to animals of the same species separated by a year.

### Torah's Generic Use

The *Torah*, however, does not say *kivsei u've'izzei* here but rather uses the generic term *tzon* — flock — without distinction: *mi-kol tzoncha*. By employing this undifferentiated term in the context of *ma'aser behema*, the *Torah* signals that for purposes of this particular *mitzvah*, all flock constitutes a single category. The *Mishnah* is not making a broader statement about the general equivalence of sheep and goats; to the contrary, it is noting that precisely because they are ordinarily so distinct, one would have expected them to be separated here as well — and therefore the *Torah*'s generic usage comes specifically to override that expectation and unite them for *ma'aser*. This applies only to *ma'aser behema* and carries no implications for other *halachot* such as the classification of wool.

## Rabbi Akiva Position

### R. Akiva's Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַעֲלֶה אָדָם מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ וְיַקְרִיבֶנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַהֲבֵאתֶם שָׁמָּה עֹלֹתֵיכֶם וְזִבְחֵיכֶם וְאֵת מַעְשְׂרֹתֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the mitzva of animal tithe is in effect both in Eretz Yisrael and outside of Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says:** One **might** have thought that **a person** must **bring up** his **animal tithe from outside of Eretz** Yisrael **and sacrifice it** in the Temple. Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall bring there your burnt offerings, and your offerings, and your tithes,** and the offering of your hand, and your vows, and your gift offerings, and the firstborn of your herd and of your flock" (Deuteronomy 12:6).

Having established the basic framework of the *Mishnah*, the *Gemara* raises a potential conflict: *leima masnisin de-lo ke-Rabbi Akiva* — perhaps our *Mishnah* does not accord with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. The *Gemara* cites a *braita* in which Rabbi Akiva asks whether one might bring *ma'aser behema* from outside the land of Israel and offer it as a *korban* in the *Beit HaMikdash*. He derives his answer from the verse *vahavatem shamma oloteichem vezivcheichem ve'et masroteichem* — you shall bring there your *olot* and sacrifices and your *ma'asrot*. The verse's mention of *ma'asrot* in the plural leads Rabbi Akiva to identify two types of *ma'aser* that are brought to Jerusalem: *ma'aser behema* and *ma'aser dagan* — specifically *ma'aser sheni*, the grain tithe brought to Jerusalem for consumption there. From this parallel, Rabbi Akiva draws a *gezeirah shava*-style inference: just as *ma'aser dagan* is brought only from Eretz Yisrael, so too *ma'aser behema* is brought only from Eretz Yisrael; and just as *ma'aser dagan* is not brought from outside Eretz Yisrael, neither is *ma'aser behema*.

### Agricultural Economy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁתֵּי מַעַשְׂרוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אֶחָד מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה וְאֶחָד מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן, מִמָּקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מַעֲלֶה מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה, וּמִמְּקוֹם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה מַעְשַׂר דָּגָן אִי אַתָּה מַעֲלֶה מַעְשַׂר בְּהֵמָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva explains: The plural form "tithes" indicates that **the verse is speaking of two tithes; one** is the **animal tithe, and** the other **one** is the second **tithe of grain,** i.e., produce. Since these two tithes are included in the same word, it is derived from here that **from the place where you bring up** the **tithe of grain,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, as the obligation to separate tithe of grain applies only there, **you** also **bring up** the **animal tithe; but from a place where you do not bring up** the **tithe of grain,** i.e., outside of Eretz Yisrael, **you do not bring up** the **animal tithe** either. This *baraita*, which states the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, apparently contradicts the ruling of the mishna.

This position rewards careful conceptual analysis. Why should *ma'aser behema* be linked to the land of Israel? Animals, unlike grain, do not literally grow from the soil. One approach frames the connection in metaphysical terms: the *kedusha* of the land is somehow absorbed into what is produced there. But this account strains when applied to animals, who at most graze on the land's grass rather than growing from it in any direct sense. A more compelling framework understands *ma'aser* as an expression of the Jewish people functioning as a national entity in the land of Israel. The obligations of *terumot u'ma'asrot* reflect not merely a private religious gesture but the agricultural output of an entire people governing itself in its homeland. In an agricultural society, the two pillars of the national economy are grain and livestock. The annual act of *ma'aser* — whether of grain or of animals — is the annual reckoning of what "we as a country" have produced. An individual Jew raising animals in France or Ohio is engaged in a personal economic activity, not in the collective agricultural output of the Jewish people as a *polity*. This, it seems, is what underlies Rabbi Akiva's position: the reason *ma'aser* is given is tied to Israel's economy as a nation, and animals are part of that same agricultural reality. This also illuminates the later development of *ma'aser kesafim* among the *Rishonim* — the practice of giving a tenth of one's income — which takes the underlying religious value of acknowledging God's hand in one's livelihood and translates it for Jews living outside the land, in non-agricultural settings, as an expression of individual gratitude rather than national covenantal obligation.

### Diaspora Evidence

One might ask whether there is any historical evidence bearing on Rabbi Akiva's position. Rabbi Akiva lived through the late Second Temple period and the Hadrianic persecutions of approximately 130 CE. If *ma'aser behema* were obligatory from outside Israel, one would expect to find some trace of Babylonian Jewish communities transporting flocks to Jerusalem for offering. No such evidence appears to exist. This silence may itself have informed Rabbi Akiva's position, or at least accorded with it. The caveat, of course, is that most diaspora communities of the late first and early second century were not observant in the rabbinic mold — particularly in *Bavel*, where the community was large but likely heterogeneous in its religious practice — making the argument from silence less than decisive. It raises a broader question about the extent of rabbinic observance outside Israel during the Temple period, and about how one would even have identified such a *korban* arriving from abroad, though a flow of animals in large numbers would presumably have been visible.

### Sanctify Not Offer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, כָּאן לִיקְרַב, כָּאן לִיקְדַּשׁ. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָא נָסֵיב לַהּ תַּלְמוּדָא ״וַהֲבֵאתֶם שָׁמָּה״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **You** may **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** as the *baraita* and the mishna are referring to different cases. The *baraita* **there** is referring to the requirement to bring the animal tithe to Jerusalem for it **to be sacrificed** on the altar. By contrast, the mishna **here** is speaking of the status of every tenth animal; these animals are **to be** separated and **consecrated.** The Gemara adds that the language of the *baraita* **is also precise,** as it **adduces a derivation** from the phrase **"and you shall bring there." Conclude from** this citation that the *baraita* is referring specifically to the requirement to bring the animal tithe and sacrifice it in Jerusalem.

The *Gemara* attempts to reconcile the *Mishnah* — which rules that *ma'aser behema* applies in *chutz la'aretz* — with Rabbi Akiva's position. The proposed resolution is that Rabbi Akiva distinguishes between *lehakriv*, offering the animal as a *korban*, and *lekadesh*, sanctifying it. When the verse says *vahavatem shamma*, it addresses the act of *bringing* — that is, offering — and Rabbi Akiva rules that one does not bring *ma'aser behema* as a *korban* from outside Israel. But perhaps the obligation to *sanctify* the tenth animal still applies even in *chutz la'aretz*, and that is precisely what our *Mishnah* rules. The *Gemara* notes that this reading is supported by the language of the *braita* itself, which frames Rabbi Akiva's question as *yakhol ya'aleh adam veyakrivenu* — perhaps one could bring it up and *offer* it — suggesting that his ruling addresses the question of offering specifically, not the question of sanctification.

### Resolution of Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מֵאַחַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָרֵב, אַמַּאי קָדוֹשׁ? לְנֶאֱכָל בְּמוּמוֹ לַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, since** animal tithe outside of Eretz Yisrael **is not sacrificed** on the altar, **why is it consecrated?** What is the significance of its sanctity? The Gemara answers that it is consecrated **to** the extent that it may **be eaten by its owner** only **when** it develops **a blemish.**

This reconciliation creates a challenging intermediate position: the owner outside Israel must count his flock, sanctify the tenth animal, and be restricted by its *kedusha* — yet can never actually bring it as a *korban* because it originates outside Israel. The *Gemara* asks directly: *mai ta'ama lekadesh* — what is the point of sanctifying it if it can never be offered? The answer given is *lema'achal bemumo livealim*: the *kedusha* has practical consequence even without *hekravah*. The animal retains its sanctified status, and once it acquires a *mum*, its owner may slaughter and eat it — restricted all the while by the *kedusha* that prohibits shearing and working the animal before that point.

One way to understand this "worst of both worlds" position is that *ma'aser behema* contains two distinct elements. One element is the personal religious obligation to acknowledge God's gifts through an annual sanctification — an obligation not tied to the land, analogous to the impulse behind *ma'aser kesafim*. A second element is the national-communal obligation of bringing the produce of the land to Jerusalem, which Rabbi Akiva links exclusively to Eretz Yisrael. Under this reading, the owner in *chutz la'aretz* is bound by the first element but not the second: he must sanctify, but need not — indeed, cannot — bring the offering. This also illuminates why *ma'aser sheni* offers the closest analogy: like *ma'aser sheni*, *ma'aser behema* is not given to a *kohen* or a *levi* but is consumed by the owner in a context of divine acknowledgment, giving expression to the religious value of gratitude that lies at the heart of *ma'aser* in all its forms.

## Prohibition Today

### Orphan Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ הָאִידָּנָא נָמֵי! כִּדְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם יָתוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the animal tithe is in effect both **in the presence of the Temple and not in the presence of the Temple.** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then **even nowadays** the *halakha* of the animal tithe should be in effect. Why then do people not remove every tenth animal from their flock or herd? The Gemara answers: The reason is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Huna, as Rav Huna says:** This is a rabbinic **decree due to an orphan,** i.e., a baby animal whose mother died before it was born, lest it be included with the other animals to be tithed; the obligation of animal tithe does not apply to it.

Returning to the *Mishnah*'s ruling that *ma'aser behema* applies even when there is no *Beit HaMikdash*, the *Gemara* asks the obvious question: *afilu idna nami* — so even today we should be counting our animals and giving *ma'aser*? The *Gemara* answers by citing Rav Huna, who raises the problem of the *yasom* — the orphan animal. The *yasom* is an animal born at the precise moment its mother dies, such that there was never an instant in which it lived *tachat imo*, under its mother who was alive after its birth. The *pasuk* of *shivat yamim yihiyeh tachat imo* disqualifies such an animal from serving as a *korban*, and it likewise cannot acquire *kedushat ma'aser*. Similarly, a *kilayim* animal, a *treifah*, or a *yotzei dofen* — an animal delivered by caesarean section — would fail to acquire *kedushat ma'aser* even if it emerged as the tenth in the count.

The procedure requires that these disqualified animals be removed from the pen before counting begins. Tosafot notes that the *yasom* is singled out as representative of this category because it is the most obscure and least visually apparent of the disqualifying conditions. If an owner, unaware of these *halachot*, fails to remove such animals before counting, and one of them emerges tenth, two serious errors follow: the owner erroneously believes the animal has *kedushat ma'aser*, and all subsequent animals in the count are displaced, compounding the error throughout the entire flock.

### Stumbling Block

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מֵעִיקָּרָא נָמֵי לָא! אֶלָּא, אֶפְשָׁר בְּהַכְרָזָה. הָכָא נָמֵי, אֶפְשָׁר בְּהַכְרָזָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** then even **originally,** when the Temple stood, the practice should have **also** been **not** to separate animal tithe in case an orphaned animal was included in the counting. **Rather,** the Gemara explains that there was no concern for an orphaned animal when the Temple stood, as it was **possible** to remove this concern **by** issuing **an announcement** to everyone that an orphaned animal is exempt from animal tithe. The Gemara suggests: **Here too,** it is **possible** to remove the concern **by** means of **an announcement** that an orphaned animal is exempt from animal tithe.

The immediate follow-up question is: why should this concern prevent the *mitzvah* altogether today? When there was a *Beit HaMikdash*, these same problems existed. The *Gemara* responds that the answer lies not in ignorance per se but in the problem of *takala* — a stumbling block — as formulated by Rava. In the time of the *Beit HaMikdash*, once an animal was sanctified with *kedushat ma'aser*, it could be handled immediately: either brought as a *korban* to the *Beit HaMikdash* or, if it had a *mum*, slaughtered and eaten promptly. The *kedusha* was resolved quickly. Today, with no *Beit HaMikdash* and no *mum* on the animal, a sanctified *ma'aser behema* cannot be offered and, if unblemished, cannot be eaten. The animal sits in a state of *kedusha* indefinitely, and the owner who interacts with it over months and years will almost inevitably misuse it — violating the restrictions that *kedusha* imposes. It is this near-certain stumbling block, not the theoretical possibility of ignorance, that is the real concern.

### Takala Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: מִשּׁוּם תַּקָּלָה, וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּחָיְישִׁינַן לְתַקָּלָה? דִּתְנַן: אֵין מַקְדִּישִׁין, וְאֵין מַעֲרִיכִין, וְאֵין מַחְרִימִין בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabba says:** The reason animal tithe is no longer separated is **due to** concern that **a mishap** might occur. **And from where do you say that we are concerned for a mishap** after the destruction of the Temple? **As we learned** in a *baraita*: **One may neither consecrate** items, **nor valuate** an item's worth based on its appraisal, **nor dedicate** items for sacred use (see Leviticus, chapter 27) and dedicate its monetary worth to the Temple treasury, **in the present** time, when the Temple is no longer standing.

One might object that education is always the proper response to the risk of error: *hacha nami efshar d'machraza* — here too, let us announce the *halachot* and teach people. The *Gemara* dismisses this: the problem Rava identifies is not one that more instruction can solve. It is not that people do not know the *halachot*; it is that the structural situation of *kedusha* without any available mechanism for resolution creates an inevitable *takala* regardless of knowledge. Therefore, the Rabbis instituted *shev v'al ta'aseh* — a passive non-fulfillment of the *mitzvah* — instructing that one should simply not count the tenth animal at all. Since *ma'aser behema*, unlike *ma'aser dagan*, does not create *tevel* — the animals are not forbidden to eat without the count — the owner may simply slaughter and eat his animals without ever having performed the count. He transgresses the *mitzvah* of *ma'aser behema* by omission, but he does not violate a prohibition, and the Rabbis determined that this is the preferable outcome.

## Destroy Sanctified

### No Sanctifying Today

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הִקְדִּישׁ וְהֶעֱרִיךְ וְהֶחְרִים, בְּהֵמָה — תֵּיעָקֵר, פֵּירוֹת כְּסוּת וְכֵלִים — יֵרָקְבוּ, מָעוֹת וּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת — יוֹלִיךְ לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. וְאֵיזֶהוּ עִיקּוּר? נוֹעֵל דֶּלֶת בְּפָנֶיהָ וְהִיא מֵתָה מֵאֵילֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And if one did consecrate, or valuate, or dedicate** items for sacred use, the presence of these items might lead to the mishap of the violation of the prohibition against using consecrated property. Therefore, if one dedicated **an animal it should be destroyed.** If he dedicated **produce, garments, or vessels** made from materials that decompose, he should store them until **they decompose.** And if he dedicated **money or metal vessels, he should take them** and cast them **into the Dead Sea.** The Gemara adds: **And** in the case of an animal, **what** constitutes **uprooting? He locks the door before it, and it dies on its own** from hunger. This *baraita* indicates that animals that cannot be sacrificed nowadays must be left to die so that they are not used improperly.

The *Gemara* grounds this concern about *takala* in a broader principle established by a *baraita*: *ein makdishin v'ein ma'arichin v'ein machrimim b'zman hazeh* — one should not nowadays sanctify objects to the *Beit HaMikdash*, whether through *hekdesh*, *arachin*, or *cherem*.

### Me'ila Concern

If one nevertheless goes ahead and does so, the object acquires *kedusha* with no available outlet. One cannot deliver it to any functioning *Beit HaMikdash*, cannot use it for personal benefit, and if one accidentally derives benefit from it, one has transgressed the prohibition of *me'ila* — misuse of *hekdesh*. The object becomes a sitting liability.

### Destruction Methods

The *braita* therefore prescribes destruction as the solution for objects inadvertently sanctified in the present era. For animals: *beheimah t'aker* — the animal must be "made sterile," which the *Gemara* clarifies means that one locks the door against it and allows it to die on its own. For produce, garments, and other objects subject to decomposition: *peiros kesus v'keilim yerakevu* — lock them away and let them rot. For money and metal objects that resist decomposition: *yolich l'yam hamelach* — cast them into the Dead Sea, or as Tosafot discusses, grind them down or cast them into other bodies of water.

The destruction of *hekdesh* — as opposed to its misuse — does not violate the prohibition of *me'ila*, since *me'ila* targets benefit derived from *hekdesh*, not its destruction. Nor is destruction considered improper deprivation of *hekdesh*, since in the present era *hekdesh* cannot practically benefit from the object anyway.

### Ethical Questions

The prescription to lock the animal in a room and allow it to die raises serious questions. These *Gemarot* are among the sources that *Rishonim* cite in arguing that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* — animal suffering — may be permitted when it serves a recognized religious or human purpose. The logic here appears to be that the Rabbis, by mandating this severe measure rather than the more lenient option of transferring the *kedusha* to a coin of lesser value, sought to impress upon the community the severity of having transgressed by sanctifying the animal in the first place.

Yet this is not the only question the ruling raises. Even setting aside the animal suffering problem: if one takes a sanctified object and destroys it, does that not undermine the very concept of *kedusha* — the recognition of God's ownership and holiness — that the act of sanctification was meant to express? An alternative approach would be to transfer the *kedusha* to a coin and then destroy the coin, thereby eliminating the stumbling block without the dramatic act of destroying the sacred object itself. Indeed, the *Gemara* does countenance the possibility of transferring *kedusha* to an object of lesser value. This principle, as stated by Shmuel — *hekdesh shaveh maneh shechilelo al shaveh perutah mechulal* — holds that even if an object worth a hundred *maneh* has its *kedusha* transferred to something worth a *perutah*, the transfer is effective. In ordinary circumstances this would constitute stealing from *hekdesh*, but in the present era, since *hekdesh* cannot derive any practical benefit, it is not considered a loss and the transfer is permitted.

### Transfer Kedusha

This principle has a striking practical application in contemporary Israel, where the *mitzvah* of *terumos u'ma'asrot* is observed. When transferring *kedushat ma'aser sheni* to a coin, one transfers only a *perutah*'s worth of *kedusha* each time, even if the produce involved is worth far more. Over many transfers, the coin gradually fills with accumulated *kedusha* until it reaches its full monetary value. At that point the coin is "full" and a second coin must be used. The solution employed is to transfer the *kedusha* from the first coin to a single *perutah*'s worth of the second coin — then from the second coin back to a *perutah*'s worth of the first, and so on indefinitely. This creates a kind of perpetual motion machine for *kedusha* transfer, cycling back and forth between two coins without either one ever needing to be destroyed. It is the application of the same underlying principle: in the present era, since *hekdesh* incurs no real loss from the transaction, even a transfer from high value to trivial value is valid.

Returning to the case of inadvertently sanctified objects, the question remains: why did the Rabbis insist on destruction rather than permitting this more elegant transfer-and-retain approach? The best available answer is that the concern of *takala* was considered so overriding — so important as a communal lesson about the responsibility of sanctifying objects carelessly — that they demanded a visibly severe response, even at the cost of the tensions just noted.

## Bechor vs Ma'aser

### Bechor Auto Sanctify

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: לִיקְנִינְהוּ לְאוּדְנַיְיהוּ לְגוֹיִם, דְּלָא לִיקְדֹּשׁ מֵעִיקָּרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this** is what the questioner **is saying: Let us sell the ears** of all firstborn animals **to gentiles** before their births, so **that they are not consecrated from the outset.** A firstborn animal that is jointly owned by a Jew and a gentile does not become consecrated as a firstborn.

Having established that the concern of *takala* leads the Rabbis to instruct that *ma'aser behema* simply not be counted today, the *Gemara* asks: the same logic should apply to *bechor* — why not instruct that *bechor* also not receive *kedusha*? The answer is immediate: *bechor beyadan talya milsa? Merachem kadesh* — is the sanctification of a *bechor* in our hands? It becomes sanctified automatically through the womb. We have no power to prevent it. The parallel with *ma'aser behema* therefore breaks down at the most basic level: *ma'aser behema* requires an affirmative act of counting, which we can simply refrain from performing; *bechor* acquires *kedusha* whether we act or not.

The *Gemara* then asks a reformulated question: given that *bechor* is automatically sanctified and given the genuine concern about *takala*, why not at least advise that one sell the ear of the pregnant mother to a non-Jew before birth, thereby ensuring the firstborn is jointly owned and never acquires *kedushat bechor* in the first place?

### Create Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶפְשָׁר כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מוּתָּר לוֹ לָאָדָם לְהַטִּיל מוּם בִּבְכוֹר קוֹדֶם שֶׁיָּצָא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם. הָכָא נָמֵי, אֶפְשָׁר דְּשָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא מֵעִיקָּרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that it is **possible** to act in a different manner, so that the priest does not lose out on his rights to the firstborn animal. One can act **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, as Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is **permitted for a person to inflict a blemish upon a firstborn** animal fetus **before it emerges into the air of the world.** The Gemara challenges: If so, **here too, it is possible** for one **to inflict a blemish from the outset,** before the animal becomes consecrated as the tithed animal.

The *Gemara* responds that a better solution exists: *efshar k'de-Rav Yehuda* — follow the ruling of Rav Yehuda. Rav Yehuda rules that it is permitted to make a *mum* in the *bechor* before it enters the world — either by inserting one's hand into the womb and creating a wound, or by cutting the lip or ear of the animal as it emerges, before the majority of its head has appeared. A pre-birth blemish is permissible because the animal does not yet have *kedusha* at that moment, and once born blemished, it acquires *kedushat bechor* but may be given immediately to a *kohen* and slaughtered for consumption. The *takala* concern is resolved at once.

### Blemish Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי יָדַע הֵי נָפֵיק? וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ בְּרֵישׁ עַשְׂרָה — ״לֹא יְבַקֵּר בֵּין טוֹב לָרַע״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **How** can one **know which** animal will **emerge** as the tenth? Since it is impossible to know this, one cannot inflict a blemish on the animal before it becomes consecrated. **And if you would say that** one should **bring out** the animal upon which he has inflicted the blemish **at the head of** the **ten** animals to ensure that it will be the tithed animal, this is not a valid option, as **the Merciful One states: "He shall not inquire whether it be good or bad"** (Leviticus 27:33). This means one may not plan in advance which animal will be the tenth.

Why is this preferable to selling the ear? Three considerations favor the Rav Yehuda approach. First, the *kedusha* of the *bechor* is preserved — the animal becomes *kadosh*, it is given to a *kohen*, and the structure of the *mitzvah* is honored rather than circumvented. Second, the *kohen* receives the benefit of the animal: a *bechor* with a blemish yields a *kohen* a week's worth of meat. Selling the ear to a non-Jew prevents the *kohen* from ever receiving anything. Third, the concerns of *takala* are neutralized because the blemished *bechor* can be handled immediately.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot disagrees with this prioritization. Making a *mum* before birth is a complicated and often impractical procedure: one must be present at precisely the right moment of delivery and either successfully manipulate the animal in utero or make the wound before the majority of the head emerges. In practice, this is frequently not achievable. Because the *Gemara* has clearly articulated the concern of *takala*, and because the method Rav Yehuda describes is not reliably accessible, Tosafot holds that the practical solution is indeed to sell the ear of the mother to a non-Jew. This is the position the *Shulchan Aruch* codifies, citing Tosafot's reasoning and ruling it a *mitzvah* nowadays to sell the mother's ear to a non-Jew so as to avoid the *takala* associated with *bechor* in the present era. The *Gemara*'s preference for Rav Yehuda's approach represents the ideal; the *Shulchan Aruch*'s ruling reflects the practical reality.

## Ma'aser Blemish Problem

### Which Is Tenth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: אֶפְשָׁר דְּשָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא בְּכוּלֵּיהּ עֶדְרֵיהּ — מְהֵרָה יִבָּנֶה בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וּבָעֵינַן בְּהֵמָה לְהַקְרָבָה וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say it is possible to inflict a blemish on the entire herd** of animals and thereby ensure the tenth one will be blemished, that is also undesirable. The reason is that **soon the Temple will be rebuilt and we** will **require animals for offerings, and** there will be **none** remaining without a blemish.

Having established that for *bechor* the Rav Yehuda approach is theoretically available, the *Gemara* considers whether the same strategy — creating a *mum* in advance of sanctification — could resolve the *takala* problem for *ma'aser behema* as well, allowing the *mitzvah* to be observed without the dangers of long-term unresolved *kedusha*. The first obstacle is immediate: *mi yadei hai napak* — who knows which animal will be the tenth? You cannot selectively blemish the would-be *ma'aser* animal in advance, because you do not know in advance which animal will emerge tenth from the pen.

### Blanket Blemish

The *Gemara* anticipates the obvious rejoinder: make a *mum* in every animal in the flock. If every animal is blemished before the counting begins, then whatever emerges tenth is already blemished, the *kedusha* of *ma'aser* descends upon a blemished animal, and the animal can be eaten immediately. The *mitzvah* is performed, the *kedusha* is preserved, and the *takala* is avoided.

### Future Temple Need

This solution, however, runs into a different and weighty objection: *bimherah yibaneh beis hamikdash u'veinan behemah lehakravah veleika* — God willing, the *Beit HaMikdash* will be rebuilt speedily, and at that time we will need unblemished animals to bring as *korbanot*. If every animal born each year is routinely blemished as part of the *ma'aser* procedure, there will be no stock of unblemished animals available when the *Beit HaMikdash* is rebuilt. This concern is not trivial: it reflects the principle that our current conduct must not undermine our readiness for the future restoration of the *avodah*. Deliberately blemishing the entire national flock generation after generation would leave us unable to bring *korbanot* the moment we are called upon to do so.

### Purchased Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי בִּבְכוֹר בְּהֵמָה, מְהֵרָה יִבָּנֶה בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וּבָעֵינַן בְּהֵמָה לְהַקְרָבָה וְלֵיכָּא! אֶפְשָׁר בִּפְשׁוּטִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But **here too, with regard to a firstborn animal,** why not also say that **soon the Temple will be rebuilt and we** will **require animals for offerings and** there will be **none** remaining without a blemish? The Gemara answers: It is **possible** to bring the offerings **from ordinary** animals that are not firstborns.

One might respond that we can always purchase animals from non-Jews. Indeed, purchased animals — *likuchim* — are in a separate category: the obligation of *ma'aser behema* applies only to animals born into one's flock, not to purchased animals. If one were to sell one's animals before the end of the year, one would be exempt, and the buyer, having acquired rather than bred them, would also be exempt. However, the *Gemara* points out that the blanket blemishing strategy still creates a serious practical problem: *keivan de'ihu shadi bemuma bechulei edrei* — since the owner blemishes his entire flock, and natural blemishes are in any case common (*dukin sheba'ayin* — cataracts alone suffice to disqualify an animal from *kodshim*), the cumulative effect would be that virtually no animal in circulation would be unblemished. Even accounting for purchased animals, the sheer prevalence of blemishes — both artificial and natural — would reduce the available pool of fit *korban* animals to a negligible number. That is an unacceptable state of affairs.

### Do Nothing Best

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, אֶפְשָׁר בִּלְקוּחִין! כֵּיוָן דְּאִיהוּ שָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא בְּכוּלֵּיהּ עֶדְרֵיהּ, וּשְׁכִיחִי מוּמֵי דְּפָסְילִי בְּקָדָשִׁים, דַּאֲפִילּוּ דּוּקִּין שֶׁבָּעַיִן נָמֵי פָּסְלִי — לָא שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Here too,** with regard to the animal tithe, **it is possible** to bring the offerings **from** animals **purchased** from another person, which are exempt from animal tithe, as taught in the mishna on 55b. The Gemara answers: **Since** anyone who has animals **inflicts a blemish on all of** the animals in **his herd** to avoid consecrating the tithe, there will be very few unblemished animals available for purchase. **And** furthermore, **blemishes that disqualify sacrificial** animals **are common, as even** if the blemish is small, e.g., **on the** animal's **eyelid, it disqualifies** the animal from being used for sacrifice. Therefore, it will **not** be **common** to find animals fit to be used as offerings. For this reason the Sages decreed that one should not tithe his animals in the present time.

The *Gemara*'s conclusion is therefore that the best available course for *ma'aser behema* in the present era is to do nothing — *shev v'al ta'aseh*. The owner simply refrains from counting and never triggers the *kedusha*. The *mitzvah* is suspended, the animals remain *chullin*, and the stumbling block is avoided entirely. This is preferable to counting and dealing with long-term unresolved *kedusha*, and preferable to blanket blemishing that would compromise the nation's readiness for the rebuilt *Beit HaMikdash*.

## Future Readiness

### Resume When Ready

The *Gemara*'s prescription is explicitly calibrated for the present era and is not intended as a permanent abandonment of the *mitzvah*. The suspension of *ma'aser behema* is held in careful balance against the anticipation of the *Beit HaMikdash*'s rebuilding. When the *Beit HaMikdash* is rebuilt, the entire calculus changes: the *takala* concern dissolves, because sanctified animals can once again be brought immediately as *korbanot* or, if blemished, slaughtered and eaten straightforwardly. At that point, the practice of counting the tenth animal and giving *ma'aser behema* will resume. The Rabbis' instruction to refrain today is thus not a negation of the *mitzvah* but a holding pattern — a deliberate preservation of both the integrity of *kedusha* and the nation's capacity to resume full *avodah* the moment circumstances permit.
