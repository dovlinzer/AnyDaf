# Keritot 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mit'asek vs. Shogeg

### Mit'asek Definition

We begin at the top of 20a with the concept of *mit'asek*. The word itself reflects a kind of self-involvement or reflexive absorption — one is occupied with a particular act when, incidentally, something else occurs. This distinguishes *mit'asek* from *shogeg*: in the case of *shogeg*, one performs the exact act intended but is unaware of some relevant fact — the nature of the object, or that the act is forbidden. *Mit'asek*, by contrast, involves doing a different physical act than the one intended. A useful illustration is gesticulating and accidentally hitting a light switch; the classic Shabbat case is reaching for one thing and inadvertently touching another.

### Chalavim v'Arayos

The halachic consequences follow from this distinction. A *shogeg* is liable to bring a *korban* if the act is subject to *chayav kares*. A *mit'asek*, because the act is not genuinely attributable to him, is exempt from a *korban* entirely — it is as though the act is not his at all. However, this exemption carries an important qualification: in cases of *chalavim v'arayos* — forbidden fats and illicit sexual relations — because the person derives pleasure from the act, the act cannot be fully divorced from him, and he is treated like a *shogeg* and must bring a *korban*. With that exception, the exemption for *mit'asek* applies *b'chol haTorah kulah* when one is attempting a *heter* and accidentally performs an *issur*.

### Shabbat Mit'asek

On Shabbat, however, a distinct and broader principle applies. Rav Nachman citing Shmuel teaches that *mit'asek* on Shabbat carries its own exemption — not merely the general principle that an unintended act is not attributed to a person, but a specific *pasuk*: *meleches machsheves*, which requires that a Shabbat violation be an intentional, purposeful act. Because of this textual basis, the Shabbat exemption for *mit'asek* applies even when the person was attempting to perform an *issur*. One who is reaching for a cluster of black grapes and instead picks a cluster of green grapes — even though the person was trying to violate Shabbat through the act of picking — is exempt, because the precise act intended was not performed. This is a distinctive *halacha* of Shabbat grounded in *meleches machsheves*.

### Fundamental vs. Technical

This distinction invites a philosophical observation. The first category of *mit'asek* — attempting a *heter* and accidentally performing an *issur* — represents a more fundamental exemption: the act is entirely divorced from the person, as though it never happened. The Shabbat variety is more of a technicality. After all, the person intended to pick grapes and picked grapes; he intended to violate Shabbat and, in a meaningful sense, violated Shabbat. The exemption from a *korban* follows from the technical requirement of *meleches machsheves*, but it does not mean the act is fundamentally unattributable. The practical ramification of this distinction is, for example, the question of *teshuvah*: if one was reaching for a permissible object and accidentally flipped a light switch, there is arguably no need for *teshuvah* whatsoever — the act is not his. But if one was reaching for red grapes and inadvertently picked green grapes on Shabbat, *teshuvah* would still seem to be in order, even if there is no *korban* liability.

## Mishna Debate

### Rabbi Shimon's View

The *Mishnah* at the top of 20a presents positions from Rabbi Shimon Shezuri and Rabbi Shimon regarding the parameters of the *mit'asek* debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri states: *lo nechelku al davar shehu mishum shem echad shehu chayav* — there is no debate when the act falls under the same essential category (*shem echad*); in that case, everyone agrees one is liable. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua — where Rabbi Eliezer obligates a *chatas* and Rabbi Yehoshua exempts — applies only when the act involves *shnei shemos*, two different categories. What precisely constitutes *shnei shemos* is not immediately clear: does it mean red grapes and green grapes, grapes and figs, or two fundamentally different prohibitions?

Rabbi Yehuda then intervenes and says the debate applies even to a case as similar as black grapes and white grapes. This implies that what Rabbi Shimon Shezuri means by *shnei shemos* involves something more radically different than black and white grapes.

### True Mit'asek

The *Gemara* now turns to its central analytical task: how to reconcile this *Mishnah* — which seems to debate the liability of *mit'asek* in various cases — with the position that by Shabbat, even black grapes and black grapes constitutes *mit'asek* and everyone would agree one is exempt. The answer offered previously was that the *Mishnah* is discussing a special case called *avad melaket milibo*: the picker forgot what he was doing. The scenario is this — the person wanted green grapes, but at the moment of picking he forgot his goal and thought he wanted black grapes, then inadvertently picked the green grapes. At the end, he got what he originally wanted, but not what he was intending at the moment of the act. This is a distinction between *desire* and *intent*: he desired the green grapes but was intending for the black ones. That special case is where the debate applies. But in a true *mit'asek* — reaching for black grapes and getting different black grapes — there is no debate; everyone agrees one is exempt on Shabbat.

### Rabbi Yehuda

The *Gemara* now challenges this reading. Rabbi Yehuda's case of black grapes and white grapes, when set alongside Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's language of *shnei shemos*, seems to be saying the same thing — unless we understand what distinguishes their positions. The *Gemara* proposes: *ela lav mit'asek ika beinaihu* — perhaps the real debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon Shezuri is whether *mit'asek* itself carries liability. Rabbi Yehuda holds *mit'asek chayav*; Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Shimon Shezuri hold *mit'asek patur*. Under this reading, Rabbi Yehuda's case of black and white grapes is a genuine *mit'asek*, and he is telling us that even real *mit'asek* is sometimes debated. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's cases of *shnei shemos*, on the other hand, reflect the special *avad melaket milibo* scenario — not true *mit'asek* — and that is where he locates the debate.

### Shem Echad Resolution

The *Gemara* ultimately rejects this approach and reasserts the earlier framework: *mit'asek dekulei alma patur* — in a genuine *mit'asek*, everyone agrees one is exempt on Shabbat. The entire *Mishnah* discussion, including Rabbi Yehuda's case of black and white grapes, pertains exclusively to *avad melaket milibo*. The resolution proceeds as follows. Rabbi Shimon Shezuri holds that in *avad melaket milibo*, when the two items belong to *shem echad* — even black and white grapes, which the *Gemara* now treats as *shem echad* — everyone agrees one is liable, because the similarity is too close. The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua applies only when it is *shnei shemos* — something like grapes and figs. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and says the debate applies even in *shem echad*: even something as similar as black and white grapes is subject to dispute, with some holding you may be exempt even there.

What the *Gemara* is effectively doing is reclaiming the principle that true *mit'asek* on Shabbat is universally exempt — even black grapes for black grapes, even candle A for candle B — and consigning the entire *Mishnah* debate to the narrow and unusual case of *avad melaket milibo*, where the person ultimately obtained what he originally wanted but not what he was intending at the moment of action.

## Rava on Order

### Candle Baraisa

Rava offers a different reading of the debate between Rabbi Shimon Shezuri and Rabbi Yehuda. He proposes that the *Mishnah* is not about *avad melaket milibo* at all, but rather about a distinct type of case: one in which the person did exactly what he intended to do, but in a different order than planned. The proof is from a *Baraisa*: if one had two candles before him — one lit, one extinguished — and intended to extinguish this one but instead extinguished that one, or intended to light this one and instead lit that one, he is exempt. This illustrates the *mit'asek* principle: even performing the exact type of act intended, on an almost identical object, is sufficient grounds for exemption on Shabbat, because it was not the precise object intended.

### One vs. Two Breaths

The *Baraisa* continues with a more complex scenario: one intended to light one candle and extinguish another, but reversed the order — he extinguished first and then lit. If this happened *binshima achat*, in a single breath — one candle was blown out and the flame simultaneously caught on the neighboring candle — he is liable. If it happened *bishtei nishimot*, in two separate breaths, such that the actions were truly sequential, he is exempt, because he genuinely reversed the intended order.

The *Gemara* notes that this ruling regarding a single breath is not self-evident. One might have said: even in the case of a single breath, the person should be exempt, because *his intention was not fulfilled* — he wanted to light first and then extinguish, and instead he extinguished and then lit. The *Baraisa* therefore teaches: *nehi deakdumumi lo makdim, achurumi nami lo maacher* — granted, he did not do what he wanted to do first; but on the other hand, it was not fully the reverse of what he wanted, because he did not want the extinguishing to come last, and indeed it did not come last — since both actions occurred simultaneously. It was not exactly what he wanted, but neither was it exactly the reverse. That is close enough to his intent to render him liable.

### Machshavto

The *Gemara* clarifies the significance of this *Baraisa* within Rava's framework. The case of two separate breaths — where one truly reverses the intended order — is sufficient to exempt, even when the acts themselves (*shnei shemos*: lighting and extinguishing) are exactly what one intended. This shows how exacting the criterion of intent is on Shabbat: even a reversal of order is enough of a departure from *machshavto*, his intention, to be treated as *mit'asek*. The area of potential debate, in Rava's reading of the *Mishnah*, is when the order issue arises in the context of *shem echad* — for example, two acts of lighting in reversed sequence. There, the difference is so subtle that some authorities may still hold one liable. But when there is a genuine reversal of order in *shnei shemos*, or a genuine *mit'asek* even in *shem echad*, everyone would agree one is exempt on Shabbat.

## Shoveling Coals

### Coal Case Setup

The *Gemara* now introduces a *Baraisa* describing a different type of case. One who shovels burning coals on Shabbat — *ha-choteh gechalim b'Shabbat* — is liable to bring a *chatas*. The act of shoveling produces two simultaneous effects: the top coals, which are being moved, are thereby extinguished; and the bottom coals, now exposed, are caused to burn more intensely. The *Tanna Kamma* rules one is liable once; Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar citing Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Tzadok rules one is liable twice — once for extinguishing the top coals (*mechabeh*) and once for causing the bottom coals to ignite (*mavir*).

### Havara Dispute

The *Gemara* asks: what exactly is the scenario? If the person intended to do both — to extinguish the top coals and ignite the bottom ones — then why would anyone say he is liable only once? He did precisely what he intended. Conversely, if he did not intend to cause the bottom coals to ignite, then the igniting of the lower coals is a case of *eino mitkavein* — he performed act A (shoveling) and incidentally, as a byproduct, act B (igniting) occurred. On Shabbat, we know that *eino mitkavein* which is a *psik reisha*, a necessary consequence, renders one liable. But the *Gemara* now operates under the assumption that this is *not* a *psik reisha* — it was not necessarily going to happen. In that case, why would anyone say one is liable for the incidental igniting?

The answer, proposed by Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Chanina speaking together, is: the case involves a smelter who explicitly does not want the bottom coals to ignite. He is trying to extinguish the top coals precisely because he wants glowing embers rather than open flames — the flame actually damages the coals he needs for his craft. Rashi reads the word *k'dei* in the text not as "in order to cause the bottom ones to burn," but rather as "in spite of the fact that the bottom ones will burn." The person's focus is entirely on extinguishing; the igniting of the lower coals is not only unintended but genuinely unwanted — *lo nicha lei*.

This transforms the question: what we have is *eino mitkavein*, *lo nicha lei* — an unintended, unwanted consequence. The *Tanna Kamma* exempts because of a further principle: *mekalkel b'hav'arah patur*. For this smelter, the igniting of the coals is destructive to his purpose — it burns away coals he wants to keep as embers. A destructive act (*mekalkel*) is generally exempt on Shabbat, and so even when it comes to fire, the *Tanna Kamma* maintains the exemption. Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Tzadok holds *mechayev* because he maintains that *mekalkel b'hav'arah chayav* — when the *melachah* is fire, even a destructive act carries liability.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan confirms that we are speaking of a smelter (*benofeach*) who genuinely has no interest in the flame. Yet even after establishing this, he adds a striking remark: *ad kan lo niskalsah ta'amah shel halachah zu* — the reasoning of this *halachah* has not yet been fully explicated. The explanation given so far is not fully satisfying, and the *Gemara* continues to seek a more adequate account.

### Ami and Chanina

Ami bar Abbin and Chanina bar Abbin both offer a different reading of the case. They suggest that the scenario involves someone who intended to do *both* — to extinguish and to ignite — *kegon sheniskaven lechabos u'lehavir*. If so, why would anyone say he is liable only once rather than twice? The answer lies in a fundamental dispute about the status of *hav'arah*: the *Tanna Kamma* follows Rabbi Yossi's view that the explicit Torah prohibition against kindling fire on Shabbat — *lo tiv'aru esh b'chol moshvoteichem b'yom HaShabbos* — is intended merely as a *lav*, a negative commandment, but not a *melachah* that incurs *karet* or *chatas*. Therefore, one who kindles fire on Shabbat in a case of *shogeg* does not bring a *korban*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Tzadok follows Rabbi Natan's view that *hav'arah le-chalek yatza'as* — the verse was stated to teach that one is liable separately for each distinct *melachah*, and fire is indeed a full *melachah* carrying *chatas* liability. Under this reading, the debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Tzadok is not about the special circumstances of the smelter or questions of *eino mitkavein* at all; it reduces simply to the dispute whether one incurs a *korban chatas* for *hav'arah*.

Rava then reintegrates this *Baraisa* into his earlier framework about order. He notes that the language of the *Baraisa* — extinguishing and igniting — parallels the candle case discussed earlier. According to Rava, the dispute here is about reversed order: the person intended to first perform one act and then the other, but the two acts happened in the opposite sequence or simultaneously. The second opinion holds one liable twice because order does not matter — both intended acts were accomplished. The *Tanna Kamma* holds one liable only once because there is some departure from the precise intention; the *Shita Mekubetzet* attempts to explain this by arguing that when acts occur simultaneously rather than sequentially, something of the intended structure is preserved — the person cannot be said to have truly reversed his intention — and yet the full doubling of liability is also not warranted. The result is liability once. The precise mechanism remains somewhat difficult to articulate, and the *Gemara* itself does not fully resolve it.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi offers yet a further reading. He frames the case as follows: *kegon deniskaven lechabos vehuvru me'aleihem* — the person intended only to extinguish, and the other coals ignited on their own, without it being a necessary consequence. This returns the case squarely to the domain of *eino mitkavein* without *psik reisha*. The emphasis of *me'aleihem* — "on their own" — signals that the igniting was unexpected and not inevitable.

The *Tanna Kamma* follows Rabbi Shimon's principle that *davar she'eino mitkavein patur* — an unintended consequence does not carry liability. Rabbi Shimon ben Rabbi Tzadok follows Rabbi Yehuda's position that an unintended consequence is nonetheless *chayav* — or at minimum *assur*. This is distinct from the *psik reisha* analysis discussed earlier: there, one would need to invoke the special factors of *lo nicha lei* and *mekalkel b'hav'arah*. Here, the scenario is not a *psik reisha* at all — the outcome was not inevitable. The dispute is therefore simply whether Rabbi Shimon or Rabbi Yehuda governs: Rabbi Shimon exempts *eino mitkavein* that is not *psik reisha* entirely; Rabbi Yehuda holds that even when the outcome was not inevitable, once it actually occurred, one is retroactively liable for a *chatas*. One of the *Ba'alei HaTosafot*, the *Ri*, suggests elsewhere that Rabbi Yehuda's position may mean only that such an act is *assur d'rabanan* before the fact, and that even Rabbi Yehuda would agree to exemption from a biblical *chatas*. This *Gemara*, however, seems to read Rabbi Yehuda as imposing genuine biblical liability once the outcome materialized. This same dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon runs throughout Masechet Shabbat and is central to the entire *inyan* of *mitkavein* on Shabbat — making this *sugya*, buried here in the middle of Keritot, a crucial locus for the topic.

## Warming with Coals

### Warming Case

A new *Baraisa* is introduced: one who shovels burning coals on Shabbat *lehitchamem bahem* — in order to warm himself with them. This is no longer the case of the smelter who wants embers and dreads flame; here the person genuinely wants the warmth, and a flame happens to arise. One *Baraisa* rules he is liable; another rules he is exempt. The *Gemara* undertakes to resolve the contradiction.

### Obligation Debate

The *Baraisa* that rules liable and the one that rules exempt are addressing different conceptual frameworks. Understanding their disagreement requires introducing a principle that is, in the *Gemara*'s words, one of the most conceptually demanding in all of Hilchot Shabbat.

### Melacha Framework

The concept at stake is *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* — performing a *melachah* but not for the purpose for which that *melachah* is defined. The classic illustration: a person wants dirt. He digs a hole to get the dirt. On Shabbat, the *melachah* is digging a hole — a form of *binyan*. The person is performing the act of digging, and he knows he is digging a hole; yet he does not want the hole for its own sake. He wants the dirt. Does performing the prescribed *melachah* for a purpose other than its defined purpose exempt him? This is the debate of *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*, which is itself the subject of dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon.

In our case, one is moving coals. Moving the coals will inevitably produce a flame — Rashi reads it as a *psik reisha*, a necessary consequence. The person knows this will happen and may even welcome the warmth from the flame. Yet what he is doing, properly described, is moving coals, not making a flame. Just as the digger is getting dirt and not making a hole, the coal-shoveler is moving coals and not kindling fire. The *melachah* of fire (*hav'arah*) is technically being performed, but not for the sake of fire as such. One opinion holds *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa chayav aleha*; the other holds *patur aleha*.

### vs. Eino Mitkavein

One might wonder whether *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* is simply a form of *eino mitkavein*. The *Gemara*'s discussion suggests they are distinct, though the precise boundary is not always explicit. The difference seems to lie in the following: *eino mitkavein* applies when one is not cognizant of the secondary consequence at all — one is dragging an object and may or may not be making a furrow, and one has not even registered the secondary effect as part of what one is doing. *Melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*, by contrast, applies when the person is fully aware that he is performing the prohibited act — he knows he is digging a hole, he knows a flame will arise — but he redefines the purpose of his action. He says: what I am doing is not making a hole, it is getting dirt; what I am doing is not kindling a flame, it is moving coals. That purposive redefinition is what renders the act *she'eina tzricha legufa*.

There is something somewhat counterintuitive here: one might expect that being aware of the prohibited consequence would make a person *more* liable, not less. But the argument runs in the other direction: precisely because he has consciously reidentified what he is doing — I am a digger of dirt, not a maker of holes — he has placed the act outside the defined purpose of the *melachah*. Whether that redefinition is sufficient for exemption is the debate. In the case of *eino mitkavein* that is not *psik reisha*, by contrast, there has been no purposive redefinition at all — the act simply happened as an unregistered byproduct — and the debate there is whether that unregistered, non-necessary consequence still generates liability retroactively.

## Blood Laws

### Blood Categories

The *Gemara* now turns to an entirely different subject, opening a new *perek* dedicated to the specific details of prohibitions carrying *karet* and *korban chatas*. The first topic is *dam* — blood. The *Mishnah* identifies which blood carries liability for *karet* and which does not, anchored in the principle *ki hadam hu hanefesh*: blood is identified with the life-force of the creature. Accordingly, liability attaches specifically to *dam shehaneshama yotze bo* — the blood with which the soul of the animal departs, that is, life-blood. Other blood from an animal may carry a *lav* but not *karet* or a *korban chatas*, and the *perek*'s focus is precisely the latter.

The *Mishnah* enumerates cases that carry full *chayav karet* liability: blood from *shechita* — blood that flows at the act of slaughter — whether from a *behema*, *chaya*, or bird, whether kosher or non-kosher species. The inclusion of non-kosher animals is itself noteworthy from the perspective of *issur chal al issur* questions. *Dam nechira* and *dam ikur* — blood resulting from other methods of killing, even without valid *shechita* — are also included. *Dam hakaza* — blood that emerges through bloodletting — is included but with a qualification: only *sheneshama yotze bo*, blood that the soul departs with, renders one liable. Not every drop of blood drawn through bloodletting qualifies as life-blood.

### Blood Types

The *Mishnah* then identifies several categories of blood that do *not* carry liability: *dam hatichel* (blood of the spleen), *dam halev* (blood of the heart), *dam beitzim* (blood in eggs, specifically a blood spot forming within a developing egg, as Rashi's second explanation makes clear from the later *Gemara*), *dam chagavim* (blood of grasshoppers), and *dam tamtzit* — residual blood that is not life-blood. For none of these is one liable for *karet* or obligated in a *chatas*.

The conceptual categories underlying these exclusions are instructive. The blood of the spleen and the heart represents *dam evarim* — blood absorbed within the walls of organs, as distinct from blood coursing through the veins. Experientially, before the microscopic understanding of capillaries, this blood was understood as categorically different from circulatory blood, and it is treated accordingly. Regarding the heart specifically, the *Rishonim* generally hold that the blood in the walls of the heart is what is excluded; blood in the cavity of the heart, which is full arterial blood, may well be life-blood and carry *karet* liability. *Dam tamtzit* is blood of the animal that is nonetheless not life-blood — it may carry a *lav* but not *karet*. The eggs and grasshoppers raise different considerations, addressed in the *Gemara* below.

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda takes a stricter position: *Rabbi Yehuda mechayev b'dam hatamtzit* — he holds that even non-life blood of an animal carries full liability. The point of contention, as throughout this *perek*, is whether the liability in question is *karet* and *korban chatas*, not whether there is any prohibition at all; the *lav* may not be disputed.

### Tuma Exclusions

A *Baraisa* is cited that derives the exclusions from a different angle — through the framework of *tuma*. The verse states: *kol dam lo tochalu — l'of ul'vehema* — do not eat any blood, specifying birds and animals. From the juxtaposition of a universal prohibition ("any blood") and a particular specification ("of birds and of animals"), the *Baraisa* derives that the prohibition is calibrated to the specific characteristics that birds and animals share.

### No Prohibition

Those distinguishing characteristics are enumerated: birds and animals have *tuma kala* — a light form of impurity, meaning they can function as food and receive *tuma* from an external source — and *tuma chamura* — a heavy form, meaning they can themselves be a primary source of *tuma* (as a *neveila*). They have both a state of *issur* (when not slaughtered properly) and a state of *heter* (when slaughtered correctly). And they are *min basar* — a type of flesh.

From these characteristics, several exclusions follow. *Dam ha-holech al shtayim* — human blood — is excluded because human beings have *tuma chamura* but not *tuma kala*: a human corpse is among the heaviest sources of *tuma*, but human flesh is never classified as food that receives *tuma* from elsewhere (*tumat ochlin*). Accordingly, human blood falls outside the prohibition entirely. This is why, for instance, one who sucks blood from a cut finger has not violated any biblical prohibition.

The blood of *sheratzim* — certain small creatures — is excluded because even those eight *sheratzim* that are an *av ha-tuma* do not possess the full weight of *tuma chamura* that characterizes *neveila*: they produce *tuma* only through contact, not through carrying, and they render the person *tamei* but not his garments. *Dam beitzim* is excluded because an egg is *ein min basar* — not a type of flesh. Fish and grasshoppers are excluded because *she-kulo heter* — they are entirely in a state of permissibility; they require no *shechita* and may even be consumed alive. For these categories, there is no prohibition against consuming their blood whatsoever, which is why fish blood requires no removal, and the only concern with human blood would be at the rabbinic level. These are categorically different from *dam tamtzit*, which is blood of a *behema* that may carry a *lav* but simply not *karet*.
