# Zevachim 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eizehu Mekoman

### Mishna Overview

The *mishnah* of *Eizehu Mekoman* — the chapter that many recite each morning as part of the *korbanot* section of *davening* — reads as a straightforward catalogue of where each sacrifice is performed in the *Beis HaMikdash*. There is no debate, no dispute, simply a list: this *korban* here, that *korban* there. In a sense, this would have made it a natural opening chapter for *Zevachim* — a clean, organized introduction to the laws of *kodshim*. The reason it does not open the *masechet*, however, is that there is very little to say analytically about a simple list. Once you have stated that a *chatat* is slaughtered here and an *olah* there, the discussion seems to be over.

What the *Gemara* therefore does throughout this *perek* is ask: from where are all of these locations derived? And this is where the challenge lies — not in the halachic stakes of each ruling, but in the *drashot*, the hermeneutic work of extracting these laws from the *pesukim*. Moreover, as will become clear as the *sugya* progresses, the discussion moves beyond mere derivations into a meta-level inquiry: which modes of interpretation are permitted in *kodshim* and which are not? We are, in other words, studying the ground rules of *kodshim* hermeneutics.

### Repetition & Méakev

One of the principles the *Gemara* has established is that in the world of *kodshim*, the mere statement of a procedural requirement does not, by itself, render that requirement *me'akev* — indispensable to the validity of the service. For a requirement to be *me'akev*, meaning that failure to observe it invalidates the *korban*, the Torah must repeat it. A single mention establishes the proper procedure *lechatchila*; only repetition signals that the procedure is essential.

### Chatat from Ola

With this principle in hand, the *Gemara* turns to a specific question. We have seen that the Torah states the *chatat* must be slaughtered where the *olah* is slaughtered — that is, in the north of the *azarah*. And because the requirement of north is repeated in connection with the *chatat*, we know it is *me'akev* for the *chatat*. But how do we know that north is *me'akev* for the *olah* itself?

The initial argument runs as follows: the *chatat* derives its law of north entirely from the *olah*. If north is *me'akev* for the *chatat*, which is merely the derivative, then certainly it must be *me'akev* for the *olah*, which is the source. The child cannot be weightier than the parent.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning: is it really true that a derived law can never be stronger than its source? And it raises the case of *temura*.

## Temura Kedusha

### Mum Kavua

The case involves an animal with a *mum kavua* — a permanent, fixed blemish. If a person consecrates such an animal as an *olah*, the animal acquires only *kedushat damim*, sanctity of monetary value, not *kedushat haguf*, bodily sanctity. The practical consequence is significant: once redeemed, the animal may be used for any purpose — it may even be worked or shorn — because it never possessed genuine bodily sanctity. The *kedushah* was, from the outset, merely nominal.

### Temura with Mum

Now consider the case of *temura*. If a person consecrated a healthy animal as an *olah* — which does possess *kedushat haguf* — and then attempted to exchange it by declaring a second animal, one with a permanent blemish, as its substitute, the *temura* takes effect. The animal with the missing limb acquires *kedushat haguf*, not mere *kedushat damim*. Consequently, even after redemption, it may not be worked or shorn. Here, then, the derivative *kedushah* — the *temura* — achieves what the original *kedushah* could not. An animal with a *mum kavua* that could not be directly sanctified with *kedushat haguf* does acquire *kedushat haguf* through *temura*. The child appears stronger than the parent.

### Source of Kedusha

The *Gemara* resolves this by reframing the question entirely. The comparison should not be between the original act of sanctification and the *temura*, but between their respective *sources* of *kedushah*. When a person directly consecrates an animal with a *mum kavua*, the *kedushah* is being created, as it were, *yesh me'ayin* — out of nothing. The starting point is purely *chulin*, and the words of sanctification are attempting to generate *kedushah* from a zero baseline. It is precisely because of this weaker foundation — creating holiness from nothing — that the *kedushah* cannot take hold on an animal with a permanent defect.

By contrast, when a person makes a *temura*, the *kedushah* does not originate from nothing. It originates from the genuine *kedushat haguf* already present in the consecrated animal being exchanged. The *kedushah* of *temura* begins with a stronger foundation — actual existing *kedushah* — and it is therefore capable of extending even to an animal with a *mum kavua*. The apparent paradox dissolves: the *temura* is not stronger than its parent; rather, the true parent in the *temura* case is the original *kedushat haguf*, not the intermediate act of exchange.

### Pesach Identity

The *Gemara* then raises the case of a *korban pesach*. A *pesach* is subject to fewer requirements than a *shelamim*: it does not require *semicha* (the laying on of hands), *nesachim* (the wine and flour and oil libations), or the waving of the *chazeh ve-shok* (the breast and thigh). Yet if a *pesach* is not offered on *Pesach* — whether because the animal was left over or the money designated for it was left over — it is subsequently brought as a *shelamim*, which does require all of those things. Here again the derived offering appears more demanding than the original.

The *Gemara* dismisses this comparison directly: a *pesach* that is brought during the rest of the year as a *shelamim* is not functioning as a derivative *pesach* at all. The *halacha* is that it undergoes a complete change of identity — it is now simply a *shelamim*. Its additional requirements flow from its new identity, not from any derivation from *pesach*. This point is reinforced by a question raised in the discussion: how long may such an animal be eaten? The answer — two days and the intervening night, as a *shelamim* — confirms that it is not a weakened version of a *pesach*, which may only be eaten on the first night, but a full-fledged *shelamim* in every respect. The change is of identity, not of degree.

## North Méakev

### HaOla Extra Hei

Having addressed the *temura* challenge, the *Gemara* now provides an independent derivation to establish that north is *me'akev* for the *olah*. The verse states: *bimkom asher tishachat ha-chatat tishachat ha-olah* — "in the place where the *chatat* is slaughtered, the *olah* shall be slaughtered." The word *ha-olah* — with the definite article, the extra *hei* — is taken as a signal of emphasis: do it precisely in its designated place. This teaches that north is not merely the proper procedure for an *olah* but is *me'akev*.

### Asham Derivation

Having established north as *me'akev* for both the *olah* and the *chatat*, the *Gemara* turns to the *asham*. The verse states: *bimkom asher yishchatu et ha-olah yishchatu et ha-asham* — "in the place where the *olah* is slaughtered, the *asham* shall be slaughtered." This links the *asham* to the *olah* in the north.

### Asham North

The verse, however, does more than address the *shechita*. It continues: *ve-et damo yizrok al ha-mizbe'ach* — "and its blood shall be dashed upon the altar." Reading the verse continuously, the *Gemara* derives that the blood-catching (*kabbalat ha-dam*) must also be performed in the north, since the verse groups the catching of the blood together with the *shechita* in the place designated for the *olah*. Furthermore, the extra word *et* before *damo* teaches that not only the blood but also the one receiving it — the *mekabel* — must stand in the north. Thus the *shechita*, the catching of the blood, and the position of the *kohen* performing the *kabbalah* are all established as taking place in the north.

### Asham Metzora

This establishes that north is the proper procedure — *lemitzvah* — for the *asham*. But how do we know it is *me'akev*? The *Gemara* points to a second verse, this one from the laws of the *metzora* (Vayikra 14:13): *veshachat et ha-kesev bimkom asher yishchat et ha-chatat ve-et ha-olah bimkom ha-kodesh ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* — "and he shall slaughter the sheep in the place where he slaughters the *chatat* and the *olah*, in the sacred place, for the *asham* is like the *chatat* to the *kohen*."

This verse, dealing specifically with the *asham metzora*, explicitly restates that the slaughter must take place in the north — and does so in a context where north has already been established. The repetition would seem to signal that north is *me'akev* for the *asham*. But the *Gemara* raises a complication.

### Davar Biklal

The complication is rooted in one of the thirteen hermeneutic principles: *davar shehayah biklal veyatza lidon bedavar chadash, i attah rashai lehachziro liklalo ad sheyachzirenu hakatuv liklalo beferush* — a matter that was part of a general category and was singled out to teach a new law cannot be returned to the general category unless the Torah explicitly restores it.

The *asham metzora* has unique laws: its blood is applied to the *metzora*'s right ear, right thumb, and right big toe. This singularity removes the *asham metzora* from the general category of *ashamot*. Once it has been singled out for special treatment, one cannot simply assume that all the standard laws of an *asham* — including the requirement of north — still apply to it automatically.

### Asham Special Uses

This principle is illustrated directly in the *beraita* cited by the *Gemara*. One might have thought: since the *asham metzora* has its unique blood applications, perhaps it no longer requires the standard *matan damim* on the *mizbe'ach* or the burning of *eimurim*. It has been separated from the general category; why assume the ordinary procedures persist? This is precisely the concern raised by the principle of *davar shehayah biklal veyatza*.

### Kachatas Lemachzir

It is to address this concern that the end of the verse — *ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* — is required. This phrase functions as a *machzir*, explicitly returning the *asham metzora* to the general category. It teaches that just as the *chatat* requires *matan damim* on the *mizbe'ach* and the burning of *eimurim*, so too does the *asham metzora*.

Given that the verse is already needed to teach this *halachah*, perhaps the earlier mention of north in the same verse is serving only to confirm that the *asham metzora* follows the standard locational rules — not to tell us that north is *me'akev* for *ashamot* in general. On this reading, the repetition is explained by the logic of *davar shehayah biklal veyatza*, leaving us without a proof that north is *me'akev*.

### Gamir Miklalo

The *Gemara* considers whether the concern can be resolved through a principle of inference: even if one cannot apply general rules to an exception (*kilo gamir miklalo* — the exception does not learn from its category), perhaps one can do the reverse — infer general rules *from* the exception. If north is stated by the *asham metzora*, can we not infer that it is equally true — and perhaps *me'akev* — for all *ashamot*?

The *Gemara* examines both sides. If we hold that the exception cannot learn from the general rule but the general rule *can* learn from the exception, then the verse's mention of north by the *asham metzora* would have sufficed to establish north as operative for all *ashamot*, and its earlier statement by the general *asham* would be the repetition needed to prove *me'akev*. That reading is coherent.

But if we hold that neither direction of inference is available — the exception cannot learn from its general category, *and* the general category cannot learn from the exception — then each statement serves its own local purpose, and we still lack a repetition that establishes north as *me'akev*.

### Repetition Teaches

The *Gemara* ultimately resolves the matter. The verse says *ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* — this umbrella phrase, returning the *asham metzora* to the general rules, effectively treats the *asham metzora* as if all its standard laws had been restated explicitly. Once this phrase is in place, the *asham metzora* is fully restored to the general category of *ashamot*.

An objection is raised: perhaps *kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* refers only to those laws that require *kehunah* — specifically the *matan damim* and the *eimurim* — but not to *shechita*, which does not require a *kohen* and might therefore not be covered by the restoration. The response is decisive: if that were so, why does the verse say *kachatat ha-asham* rather than simply *kachatat hu la-kohen*? The explicit inclusion of *ha-asham* — the *asham* — signals that this exception is to be treated like all other *ashamot* in every respect. Once the *asham metzora* has been fully restored to the general category of *ashamot*, north is implicit in that restoration. Therefore, the separate explicit statement of north in the same passage must be serving a different purpose entirely — it is teaching that north is *me'akev*.

## Davar Halamed

### Both Links

With the *me'akev* status of north established for the *olah*, the *chatat*, and the *asham*, the *Gemara* turns to a new question. The verse in the *asham metzora* passage connects the *kesev* — the lamb of the *asham metzora* — to both the *chatat* and the *olah*: *bimkom asher yishchat et ha-chatat ve-et ha-olah*. Why is this double linkage necessary? Why mention both?

### Chain Problem

Had the verse linked the *asham metzora* only to the *chatat* — "slaughter it where the *chatat* is slaughtered" — we would still have known it belongs in the north, because the *chatat* is slaughtered where the *olah* is slaughtered. The chain runs: *olah* establishes north; *chatat* derives north from *olah*; *asham* derives north from *chatat*. The logic appears sound. So why go back to the *olah*?

### Ravina's Answer

Ravina explains: the double linkage is necessary precisely because the chain is insufficient. The *chatat* knows that north is required only because the Torah linked the *chatat* to the *olah*. If we then attempt to use the *chatat*'s derived knowledge of north to teach it to the *asham*, we have committed a hermeneutic error. This is the principle of *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* — a law that is itself learned through *hekesh* (the linking of two subjects in a verse) may not be used to teach another law through yet another *hekesh*.

### Hekesh Principle

The principle works as follows. Call the *olah* A, the *chatat* B, and the *asham* C. The Torah explicitly links B to A; from this we derive that B — the *chatat* — must be slaughtered in the north. If we then attempt to derive C's obligation of north from B by means of another *hekesh*, we are doing precisely what the principle forbids: we are taking a law that B learned by *hekesh* (from A) and using it to teach C by *hekesh*. This is *davar halamed behekesh chozer umelamed behekesh* — applying a transitively learned law through another inferential link — and it is forbidden.

### Both Teaches Principle

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Mari raises a pointed question to Ravina: if the purpose was to establish that the *asham* requires north, why mention the *chatat* at all? Why not simply link the *asham* directly to the *olah* and be done with it?

Ravina's response is illuminating. Had the verse linked the *asham* only to the *olah* without mentioning the *chatat*, we would not have learned the hermeneutic principle. We would have said: the verse linked the *asham* to the *olah* rather than to the *chatat* — perhaps as a stylistic choice, or because the *olah* is the primary source. We would have had no grounds to conclude that linking to the *chatat* would have been prohibited. It is specifically by linking to both — first to the *chatat*, and then additionally to the *olah* — that the Torah signals: linking to the *chatat* alone would have been hermeneutically insufficient. The fact that the verse had to go directly to the *olah*, and could not rely on the chain through the *chatat*, is what teaches us that *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh*.

## Eimurim Proof

### Eimurim Sources

Rava cites an independent source for the same principle of *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh*, drawn from the laws of *eimurim* — the sacrificial portions burned on the *mizbe'ach*. The relevant verse is *ka-asher yuram mi-shor zevach ha-shelamim* — "just as it is raised from the ox of the *shelamim* sacrifice" — and it appears in the context of the *par kohen mashiach*, the bull of the anointed High Priest. The *Gemara* proceeds to show why this verse is indispensable, and how its presence proves the principle.

### Par Explicit

The *par kohen mashiach* explicitly states which *eimurim* are burned: the *yoteret*, the *kaved*, and the two *kilyot*. This is stated directly in the body of that passage. Two other categories of *korban* are relevant here. The *par he'elem davar* — the communal bull brought when the Sanhedrin rules erroneously — does *not* have its *eimurim* stated explicitly in its own passage. And the *se'irei avodah zarah* — the goats brought for communal inadvertent idolatry — similarly lack an explicit statement of *eimurim*.

### Shirei Chain

The result is that in order to apply *eimurim* to the *se'irei avodah zarah*, one would need a two-step inferential chain: first, derive *eimurim* for the *par he'elem davar* from the *par kohen mashiach* (since *he'elem davar* does not state them explicitly); second, derive *eimurim* for the *se'irei avodah zarah* from the *par he'elem davar*. This is precisely the structure that *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* prohibits.

### Keasher Yuram

This is where the verse *ka-asher yuram mi-shor zevach ha-shelamim* becomes essential. This phrase is redundant in its immediate context — the *eimurim* of the *par kohen mashiach* have already been specified. The *Gemara* explains that the verse functions to make the *eimurim* of the *par he'elem davar* treated *as if* they had been written explicitly in its own passage, rather than merely derived by *hekesh* from the *par kohen mashiach*. Once the *eimurim* of the *par he'elem davar* are treated as explicitly stated, the second link — to *se'irei avodah zarah* — is not an instance of *davar halamed behekesh chozer umelamed behekesh*, because the intermediate step is now considered direct rather than derivative.

### Extra Verse Proof

The implication is stark: without this extra verse, the two-step chain would have been unavailable, and there would have been no valid basis for applying *eimurim* to the *se'irei avodah zarah*. The very necessity of this redundant verse demonstrates that *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* — transitive *hekesh* is insufficient, and an independent basis is required at each step.

### Rav Papa

Rav Papa presses Rava: if the purpose was to make the *eimurim* of the *par he'elem davar* explicit, why not simply write them explicitly in the *par he'elem davar* passage itself? Why do it through this indirect device of a redundant verse by the *par kohen mashiach*?

### Redundancy Teaches

The answer parallels exactly what was said earlier about the *chatat* and the *olah*. Had the *eimurim* been stated explicitly in the *par he'elem davar* passage from the outset, we would never have known that the two-step chain was prohibited. We would have concluded that the Torah simply chose to write it directly for stylistic reasons, and that in principle a *hekesh* chain could also have worked. By instead writing the *eimurim* explicitly by the *par kohen mashiach* and allowing the chain to proceed — while simultaneously inserting a verse that makes the intermediate step *as if* explicit — the Torah reveals what would have been missing without it. The necessity of that extra verse teaches us that without it, the chain would have failed: *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh*. The principle is thus demonstrated in precisely the same way as by the *chatat* and the *olah*: the Torah could have taken the more direct route but did not, and the reason it did not is what teaches the hermeneutic rule.

## Gezeira Shava

### Tzaraat Proof

The *Gemara* now asks a more refined question: we have established that in *kodshim*, one may not apply a law that was itself learned through *hekesh* to yet another subject through another *hekesh*. But what if the second step is not another *hekesh* but a *gezeira shava* — the inference drawn from identical or parallel language in two verses? Perhaps a *gezeira shava* is more powerful than a *hekesh*, and the restriction does not extend to this combination.

To explore whether *davar halamed behekesh* applies beyond *kodshim* altogether — and whether *hekesh* followed by *gezeira shava* might work even in *kodshim* — the *Gemara* brings a proof from *tzara'at*. Rabbi Natan ben Avtulas asks: from where do we know that if a *tzara'at* lesion spreads across an entire garment, it renders the garment *tahor*? The answer invokes the words *karachto* and *gavachto* — terms that appear in the context of skin *tzara'at* and, strikingly, also in the context of garment *tzara'at* (Vayikra 13:55). The shared language creates a *gezeira shava*, linking garment *tzara'at* to skin *tzara'at*.

### Two Methods

But the chain does not begin with the *gezeira shava*. The principle that spreading over the whole body renders a person *tahor* is established first by the skin — derived from the verse *mi-rosho ve-ad raglo*, "from his head to his foot," which links the *nega* of the skin to the *nega* of the hair of the head through a *hekesh*. The logic is: just as spreading of the skin *nega* across the whole body is *tahor*, so too spreading of the hair *nega* across the whole head is *tahor*. From hair to garments, however, the link is a *gezeira shava* — the parallel language *karachto ve-gavachto*. Thus the chain runs: skin to hair via *hekesh*, and hair to garments via *gezeira shava*. Here is an instance of *hekesh* followed by *gezeira shava* producing a valid ruling.

### Kodshim Only

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this immediately: throughout the entire Torah, one may apply a law that was learned by *hekesh* further, whether by another *hekesh* or by *gezeira shava*. The restriction — *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* — applies exclusively to *kodshim*. The *tzara'at* proof thus raises no difficulty at all for the principle as it operates in the realm of sacrificial law. This is a striking point. The intensive legislative detail of *kodshim* generates two related but distinct strictures: on the one hand, no requirement is treated as *me'akev* unless the Torah repeats it; on the other hand, the inferential chains by which laws are derived are more tightly constrained. Both reflect the same underlying orientation toward *kodshim* as a domain governed by extraordinary precision.

### Hekesh Plus Gezeira

Within *kodshim*, then, the question remains open: is the combination of *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* permitted, or is it also forbidden? The *Gemara* now provides an answer.

### Forbidden Combo

Rabbi Yochanan's proof that even *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* is forbidden in *kodshim* is derived from the same *pasuk* we have already studied: *ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen kodesh kodashim hu* — "for the *asham* is like the *chatat* to the *kohen*; it is *kodesh kodashim*."

### If Allowed

If *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* were permitted in *kodshim*, there would have been a simpler path to establish that the *asham* requires north. The *chatat* is known to be slaughtered in the north — derived through *hekesh* from the *olah*. The *asham* is called *kodesh kodashim*, and so is the *chatat*. A *gezeira shava* on the term *kodesh kodashim* would then link the *asham* to the *chatat*. Through that *gezeira shava*, one could transfer the *chatat*'s requirement of north to the *asham*.

### Direct Link

But the Torah did not do this. Instead, the verse links the *asham* directly to the *olah*. Why bypass the *chatat* and go straight to the *olah*? Precisely because a *hekesh*-derived law — the *chatat*'s requirement of north, which the *chatat* itself learned by *hekesh* from the *olah* — may not be extended further, not even through a *gezeira shava*. The direct link to the *olah* was necessary because the indirect route through the *chatat* was closed, even via *gezeira shava*.

### Chatat Weakness

One might object: perhaps the *gezeira shava* between the *chatat* and the *asham* was insufficient not because of the principle of *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava*, but simply because the *chatat* is a weightier *korban* — it atones for those liable to *karet* — whereas the *asham* does not. A *gezeira shava* that links an unequal pair may be deficient for that reason alone, independent of the transitivity question.

### Kodesh Extra

The *Gemara* dismisses this concern. The term *kodesh kodashim* is stated multiple times in connection with both the *chatat* and the *asham*. In the language of *gezeira shava*, when the shared words are themselves fully superfluous — *mufneh* — the inference is valid even when the two cases are not perfectly analogous. The repeated, extra appearances of *kodesh kodashim* in both contexts would make the *gezeira shava* robust enough to carry the law regardless of the substantive differences between *chatat* and *asham*. Therefore, the only reason the Torah had to link the *asham* directly to the *olah* — bypassing the *chatat* entirely — is that *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* is also forbidden in *kodshim*, just as *hekesh* plus *hekesh* is forbidden. The two restrictions stand in parallel: in the domain of *kodshim*, neither a second *hekesh* nor a *gezeira shava* may be used to extend a law that was itself derived by *hekesh*.
