# Zevachim 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eizehu Mekoman

### Mishna Overview

The *mishnah* of *Eizehu Mekoman* — the chapter that many recite each morning as part of the *korbanot* section of *davening* — reads as a straightforward catalogue of where each sacrifice is performed in the *Beis HaMikdash*. There is no debate, no dispute, simply a list: this *korban* here, that *korban* there. In a sense, this would have made it a natural opening chapter for *Zevachim* — a clean, organized introduction to the laws of *kodshim*. The reason it does not open the *masechet*, however, is that there is very little to say analytically about a simple list. Once you have stated that a *chatat* is slaughtered here and an *olah* there, the discussion seems to be over.

What the *Gemara* therefore does throughout this *perek* is ask: from where are all of these locations derived? And this is where the challenge lies — not in the halachic stakes of each ruling, but in the *drashot*, the hermeneutic work of extracting these laws from the *pesukim*. Moreover, as will become clear as the *sugya* progresses, the discussion moves beyond mere derivations into a meta-level inquiry: which modes of interpretation are permitted in *kodshim* and which are not? We are, in other words, studying the ground rules of *kodshim* hermeneutics.

### Repetition & Méakev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁחִיטָה וְקַבָּלָה לְעַכֵּב מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **We have found** a source that the **slaughter** must be in the north. **From where do we** derive that the **collection** of the blood must also be in the north?

One of the principles the *Gemara* has established is that in the world of *kodshim*, the mere statement of a procedural requirement does not, by itself, render that requirement *me'akev* — indispensable to the validity of the service. For a requirement to be *me'akev*, meaning that failure to observe it invalidates the *korban*, the Torah must repeat it. A single mention establishes the proper procedure *lechatchila*; only repetition signals that the procedure is essential.

### Chatat from Ola

With this principle in hand, the *Gemara* turns to a specific question. We have seen that the Torah states the *chatat* must be slaughtered where the *olah* is slaughtered — that is, in the north of the *azarah*. And because the requirement of north is repeated in connection with the *chatat*, we know it is *me'akev* for the *chatat*. But how do we know that north is *me'akev* for the *olah* itself?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״הָעוֹלָה״ – בִּמְקוֹמָהּ תְּהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** there is a different proof that even after the fact a burnt offering that was not slaughtered in the north is disqualified. **The verse states:** "And slaughter the sin offering in the place of **the burnt offering"** (Leviticus 4:29). Since the earlier verse (Leviticus 4:24) already stated: "In the place of the burnt offering," the repetition in this verse stresses that **it shall be in its place,** meaning that the offering is disqualified if it is slaughtered anywhere else.

The initial argument runs as follows: the *chatat* derives its law of north entirely from the *olah*. If north is *me'akev* for the *chatat*, which is merely the derivative, then certainly it must be *me'akev* for the *olah*, which is the source. The child cannot be weightier than the parent.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning: is it really true that a derived law can never be stronger than its source? And it raises the case of *temura*.

## Temura Kedusha

### Mum Kavua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי תְּמוּרָה – דְּאִילּוּ קֳדָשִׁים לָא חָיְילִי עַל בַּעַל מוּם קָבוּעַ, וְאִילּוּ אִיהִי חָיְילָא!
>
> **Translation:** Mar Zutra asks: **But there is** the case of **a substitution, as** the status of **an offering does not take effect with regard to a permanently blemished** animal, **while** consecration performed via substitution does **take effect** with regard to an animal with a permanent blemish. Although the animal cannot be sacrificed as an offering and must be redeemed, even after its redemption it may not be used for labor and its wool may not be used.

The case involves an animal with a *mum kavua* — a permanent, fixed blemish. If a person consecrates such an animal as an *olah*, the animal acquires only *kedushat damim*, sanctity of monetary value, not *kedushat haguf*, bodily sanctity. The practical consequence is significant: once redeemed, the animal may be used for any purpose — it may even be worked or shorn — because it never possessed genuine bodily sanctity. The *kedushah* was, from the outset, merely nominal.

### Temura with Mum

Now consider the case of *temura*. If a person consecrated a healthy animal as an *olah* — which does possess *kedushat haguf* — and then attempted to exchange it by declaring a second animal, one with a permanent blemish, as its substitute, the *temura* takes effect. The animal with the missing limb acquires *kedushat haguf*, not mere *kedushat damim*. Consequently, even after redemption, it may not be worked or shorn. Here, then, the derivative *kedushah* — the *temura* — achieves what the original *kedushah* could not. An animal with a *mum kavua* that could not be directly sanctified with *kedushat haguf* does acquire *kedushat haguf* through *temura*. The child appears stronger than the parent.

### Source of Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּמוּרָה מִכֹּחַ קָדָשִׁים קָא אָתְיָא, וְקָדָשִׁים מִכֹּחַ חוּלִּין (קָאָתֵי) [קָאָתוּ].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The sanctity of **a substitution comes by virtue of a consecrated** animal; therefore it has a stronger sanctity. **But** the sanctity of **a consecrated** animal itself **comes by virtue of a non-sacred** animal, as there was no consecrated animal by which the owner extended the sanctity to this animal.

The *Gemara* resolves this by reframing the question entirely. The comparison should not be between the original act of sanctification and the *temura*, but between their respective *sources* of *kedushah*. When a person directly consecrates an animal with a *mum kavua*, the *kedushah* is being created, as it were, *yesh me'ayin* — out of nothing. The starting point is purely *chulin*, and the words of sanctification are attempting to generate *kedushah* from a zero baseline. It is precisely because of this weaker foundation — creating holiness from nothing — that the *kedushah* cannot take hold on an animal with a permanent defect.

By contrast, when a person makes a *temura*, the *kedushah* does not originate from nothing. It originates from the genuine *kedushat haguf* already present in the consecrated animal being exchanged. The *kedushah* of *temura* begins with a stronger foundation — actual existing *kedushah* — and it is therefore capable of extending even to an animal with a *mum kavua*. The apparent paradox dissolves: the *temura* is not stronger than its parent; rather, the true parent in the *temura* case is the original *kedushat haguf*, not the intermediate act of exchange.

### Pesach Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי פֶּסַח – דְּהוּא אֵינוֹ טָעוּן סְמִיכָה וּנְסָכִים וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק, וְאִילּוּ מוֹתָר דִּידֵיהּ טָעוּן סְמִיכָה וּנְסָכִים וּתְנוּפַת חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק!
>
> **Translation:** Mar Zutra asks: But **there is** the case of **a Paschal offering, as it does not require** the **placing** of **hands** on its head, **or** wine **libations** to accompany the offering, **or waving of** the **breast and thigh. While its remainder,** an animal that was designated as a Paschal offering but was not sacrificed at the right time, which is then sacrificed as a peace offering, has more stringent *halakhot*, because it **requires** the **placing** of **hands** on its head, **and** wine **libations** to accompany the offering, **and waving of** the **breast and thigh.**

The *Gemara* then raises the case of a *korban pesach*. A *pesach* is subject to fewer requirements than a *shelamim*: it does not require *semicha* (the laying on of hands), *nesachim* (the wine and flour and oil libations), or the waving of the *chazeh ve-shok* (the breast and thigh). Yet if a *pesach* is not offered on *Pesach* — whether because the animal was left over or the money designated for it was left over — it is subsequently brought as a *shelamim*, which does require all of those things. Here again the derived offering appears more demanding than the original.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֶּסַח בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה – שְׁלָמִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **A Paschal offering on the rest of the days of the year is** the same as **a peace offering,** and the animal is no longer considered to be a Paschal offering. Since it is a peace offering, all the *halakhot* of a peace offering apply to it, and the fact that it was once designated as a Paschal offering is irrelevant.

The *Gemara* dismisses this comparison directly: a *pesach* that is brought during the rest of the year as a *shelamim* is not functioning as a derivative *pesach* at all. The *halacha* is that it undergoes a complete change of identity — it is now simply a *shelamim*. Its additional requirements flow from its new identity, not from any derivation from *pesach*. This point is reinforced by a question raised in the discussion: how long may such an animal be eaten? The answer — two days and the intervening night, as a *shelamim* — confirms that it is not a weakened version of a *pesach*, which may only be eaten on the first night, but a full-fledged *shelamim* in every respect. The change is of identity, not of degree.

## North Méakev

### HaOla Extra Hei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָשָׁם מְנָלַן דְּבָעֵי צָפוֹן? דִּכְתִיב: ״בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחֲטוּ אֶת הָעֹלָה יִשְׁחֲטוּ אֶת הָאָשָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that a guilt offering,** which is an offering of the most sacred order, **requires** slaughter in the **north?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "In the place where they slaughter the burnt offering they shall slaughter the guilt offering,** and its blood shall be sprinkled around the altar" (Leviticus 7:2). Since the burnt offering must be slaughtered in the north, the guilt offering must also be slaughtered in the north.

Having addressed the *temura* challenge, the *Gemara* now provides an independent derivation to establish that north is *me'akev* for the *olah*. The verse states: *bimkom asher tishachat ha-chatat tishachat ha-olah* — "in the place where the *chatat* is slaughtered, the *olah* shall be slaughtered." The word *ha-olah* — with the definite article, the extra *hei* — is taken as a signal of emphasis: do it precisely in its designated place. This teaches that north is not merely the proper procedure for an *olah* but is *me'akev*.

### Asham Derivation

Having established north as *me'akev* for both the *olah* and the *chatat*, the *Gemara* turns to the *asham*. The verse states: *bimkom asher yishchatu et ha-olah yishchatu et ha-asham* — "in the place where the *olah* is slaughtered, the *asham* shall be slaughtered." This links the *asham* to the *olah* in the north.

### Asham North

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁחִיטָה, קַבָּלָה מְנָא לַן? ״וְאֶת דָּמוֹ יִזְרוֹק״ – קִבּוּל דָּמוֹ נָמֵי בַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We have found** a source that the **slaughter** must be in the north. **From where do we** derive that the **collection** of the blood must also be in the north? The Gemara answers that the second half of the verse states: **"And its blood shall be sprinkled** around the altar." Since the blood must be collected immediately after the slaughter and before the sprinkling, just as the slaughter must be in the north, so the **collection of its blood** must **also** be **in the north.**

The verse, however, does more than address the *shechita*. It continues: *ve-et damo yizrok al ha-mizbe'ach* — "and its blood shall be dashed upon the altar." Reading the verse continuously, the *Gemara* derives that the blood-catching (*kabbalat ha-dam*) must also be performed in the north, since the verse groups the catching of the blood together with the *shechita* in the place designated for the *olah*. Furthermore, the extra word *et* before *damo* teaches that not only the blood but also the one receiving it — the *mekabel* — must stand in the north. Thus the *shechita*, the catching of the blood, and the position of the *kohen* performing the *kabbalah* are all established as taking place in the north.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקַבֵּל עַצְמוֹ מְנָא לַן? ״דָּמוֹ״–״וְאֶת דָּמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that the one who **collects** the blood must **himself** stand in the north? The Gemara answers: It is derived from the fact that the verse does not only state: **"Its blood** shall be sprinkled," but states: **"And its blood** shall be sprinkled" (Leviticus 7:2). The conjunction "and [*ve'et*]" serves to include the one who collects the blood.

### Asham Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן לְמִצְוָה, לְעַכֵּב מְנָא לַן? קְרָא אַחֲרִינָא כְּתִיב: ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We have found** a verse teaching that in order **to** perform the **mitzva** in the optimal manner, the slaughtering of the guilt offering is in the north. **From where do we** derive **to disqualify** after the fact an offering slaughtered elsewhere, not in the north? The Gemara answers: **Another verse is written,** referring to a guilt offering, which states: **"And he shall slaughter the sheep** in the place where they slaughter the sin offering and the burnt offering, in the place of the Sanctuary; for as the sin offering, so is the guilt offering; to the priest, it is most holy" (Leviticus 14:13). This teaches that all guilt offerings are disqualified if they are not slaughtered in the north.

This establishes that north is the proper procedure — *lemitzvah* — for the *asham*. But how do we know it is *me'akev*? The *Gemara* points to a second verse, this one from the laws of the *metzora* (Vayikra 14:13): *veshachat et ha-kesev bimkom asher yishchat et ha-chatat ve-et ha-olah bimkom ha-kodesh ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* — "and he shall slaughter the sheep in the place where he slaughters the *chatat* and the *olah*, in the sacred place, for the *asham* is like the *chatat* to the *kohen*."

This verse, dealing specifically with the *asham metzora*, explicitly restates that the slaughter must take place in the north — and does so in a context where north has already been established. The repetition would seem to signal that north is *me'akev* for the *asham*. But the *Gemara* raises a complication.

### Davar Biklal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: דָּבָר שֶׁהָיָה בַּכְּלָל וְיָצָא לִידּוֹן בְּדָבָר הֶחָדָשׁ, אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לְהַחְזִירוֹ לִכְלָלוֹ עַד שֶׁיַּחְזִירֶנּוּ הַכָּתוּב לִכְלָלוֹ בְּפֵירוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But does this** verse **come to** teach **this** *halakha*? **This** verse is **necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a matter that was included in a general** category **but left to discuss a new matter,** i.e., if a novel aspect or special ruling is taught with regard to a specific case within a broader general category, **you may not return it to its general** category even for other matters, and this case is understood to have been entirely removed from the general category, **until the verse explicitly returns it to its general** category.

The complication is rooted in one of the thirteen hermeneutic principles: *davar shehayah biklal veyatza lidon bedavar chadash, i attah rashai lehachziro liklalo ad sheyachzirenu hakatuv liklalo beferush* — a matter that was part of a general category and was singled out to teach a new law cannot be returned to the general category unless the Torah explicitly restores it.

The *asham metzora* has unique laws: its blood is applied to the *metzora*'s right ear, right thumb, and right big toe. This singularity removes the *asham metzora* from the general category of *ashamot*. Once it has been singled out for special treatment, one cannot simply assume that all the standard laws of an *asham* — including the requirement of north — still apply to it automatically.

### Asham Special Uses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? ״וְשָׁחַט אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ בִּמְקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁחַט אֶת הַחַטָּאת וְאֶת הָעוֹלָה בִּמְקוֹם הַקֹּדֶשׁ, כִּי כַּחַטָּאת הָאָשָׁם הוּא וְגוֹ׳״ – שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כַּחַטָּאת הָאָשָׁם״; מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כַּחַטָּאת הָאָשָׁם״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **How so?** The verse states: **"And he shall slaughter the sheep in the place where they slaughter the sin offering and the burnt offering, in the place of the Sanctuary; for as the sin offering, so is the guilt offering;** to the priest, it is most holy" (Leviticus 14:13). **As** there is **no** need for **the verse to state: "As the sin offering, so is the guilt offering,"** since the verse had already equated the guilt offering with the sin offering, **why** does the **verse state: "As the sin offering, so is the guilt offering"?** What does it serve to teach?

This principle is illustrated directly in the *beraita* cited by the *Gemara*. One might have thought: since the *asham metzora* has its unique blood applications, perhaps it no longer requires the standard *matan damim* on the *mizbe'ach* or the burning of *eimurim*. It has been separated from the general category; why assume the ordinary procedures persist? This is precisely the concern raised by the principle of *davar shehayah biklal veyatza*.

### Kachatas Lemachzir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁיָּצָא אֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע לִידּוֹן בְּדָבָר הֶחָדָשׁ – בְּבוֹהֶן יָד וּבוֹהֶן רֶגֶל וְאֹזֶן יְמָנִית; יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא טָעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ?
>
> **Translation:** **Since the guilt offering of a leper left** the general category of guilt offerings **to teach a new matter,** as there is a *halakha* unique to the guilt offering of a leper in that blood of the offering is placed **on** the right **thumb of the hand and** the right **big toe of the foot and** the **right ear** of the leper, one **might** have thought that this guilt offering **does not require placement of blood or** the burning of **sacrificial portions on the altar,** as the *halakhot* of this guilt offering are unique.

It is to address this concern that the end of the verse — *ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* — is required. This phrase functions as a *machzir*, explicitly returning the *asham metzora* to the general category. It teaches that just as the *chatat* requires *matan damim* on the *mizbe'ach* and the burning of *eimurim*, so too does the *asham metzora*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כַּחַטָּאת הָאָשָׁם הוּא״ – מָה חַטָּאת טְעוּנָה מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, אַף אֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע טָעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים וְאֵימוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** To counter this reasoning, **the verse states: "As the sin offering, so is the guilt offering." Just as the sin offering requires the placement of the blood and** the burning of **sacrificial portions on the altar, so too, the guilt offering of a leper requires placement of the blood and** the burning of **sacrificial portions on** the **altar.** Similarly, just as the verse had to teach these two *halakhot*, so too, the verse had to state: "And he shall slaughter the sheep in the place where they slaughter the sin offering and the burnt offering," to teach that it requires slaughter in the north. It cannot serve as a source for the general *halakha* that a guilt offering slaughtered in a place other than in the north is disqualified.

Given that the verse is already needed to teach this *halachah*, perhaps the earlier mention of north in the same verse is serving only to confirm that the *asham metzora* follows the standard locational rules — not to tell us that north is *me'akev* for *ashamot* in general. On this reading, the repetition is explained by the logic of *davar shehayah biklal veyatza*, leaving us without a proof that north is *me'akev*.

### Gamir Miklalo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב בְּהַאי וְלָא נִכְתּוֹב בְּהַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If so,** that the optimal manner to perform the mitzva is to slaughter a guilt offering in the north but failure to do so does not disqualify the offering, **let** the Torah **write** the requirement to slaughter in the north **in this** context, i.e., in the context of the guilt offering of a leper, **and not write it in that** context, i.e., that of the standard guilt offering. The requirement for slaughter in the north for all other guilt offerings could be derived from the guilt offering of the leper. The repetition of the requirement to slaughter in the north serves to disqualify a guilt offering whose slaughter is not in the north.

The *Gemara* considers whether the concern can be resolved through a principle of inference: even if one cannot apply general rules to an exception (*kilo garim miklalo* — the exception does not learn from its category), perhaps one can do the reverse — infer general rules *from* the exception. If north is stated by the *asham metzora*, can we not infer that it is equally true — and perhaps *me'akev* — for all *ashamot*?

The *Gemara* examines both sides. If we hold that the exception cannot learn from the general rule but the general rule *can* learn from the exception, then the verse's mention of north by the *asham metzora* would have sufficed to establish north as operative for all *ashamot*, and its earlier statement by the general *asham* would be the repetition needed to prove *me'akev*. That reading is coherent.

But if we hold that neither direction of inference is available — the exception cannot learn from its general category, *and* the general category cannot learn from the exception — then each statement serves its own local purpose, and we still lack a repetition that establishes north as *me'akev*.

### Repetition Teaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא אִי סְבִירָא לַן יָצָא לִידּוֹן בְּדָבָר הֶחָדָשׁ – אִיהוּ הוּא דְּלָא גָּמַר מִכְּלָלוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well if we hold** that with regard to a matter that **left** its category **to teach a new matter,** the following principle applies: That matter itself **does not learn** *halakhot* **from its general** category until the Torah explicitly returns it to that category,

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל כְּלָלוֹ גָּמַר מִינֵּיהּ (לְצָפוֹן) שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי סְבִירָא לַן דְּלָא הוּא גָּמַר מִכְּלָלוֹ וְלָא כְּלָלוֹ גָּמַר מִינֵּיהּ – הַאי לְגוּפֵיהּ אִיצְטְרִיךְ!
>
> **Translation:** **but** other matters in **its general** category **learn** *halakhot* **from it;** in this case **with regard to** the *halakha* of slaughtering a guilt offering in the **north,** it is **well.** Although some of the *halakhot* of the guilt offering of a leper do not apply to the category of guilt offerings in general, it can still serve as the source for the *halakha* that any guilt offering is disqualified if slaughtered not in the north. **But if we hold that** in the case of a matter that left its category to teach a new matter, **it,** the original matter, **does not learn** *halakhot* **from its general** category **and its general** category **does not learn** *halakhot* **from it,** then **this** verse **is necessary to** teach **its own** *halakha*.

The *Gemara* ultimately resolves the matter. The verse says *ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* — this umbrella phrase, returning the *asham metzora* to the general rules, effectively treats the *asham metzora* as if all its standard laws had been restated explicitly. Once this phrase is in place, the *asham metzora* is fully restored to the general category of *ashamot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאַהְדְּרֵיהּ, אַהְדְּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Once** the verse **returned** the *halakha* of the guilt offering of a leper to the general category by stating "as the sin offering, so is the guilt offering," **it returned it** completely. Therefore, when the verse states that it requires slaughter in the north of the Temple courtyard, the phrase is not needed to teach the *halakha* about the guilt offering of a leper, and it can be used to teach the *halakha* about guilt offerings in general.

An objection is raised: perhaps *kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen* refers only to those laws that require *kehunah* — specifically the *matan damim* and the *eimurim* — but not to *shechita*, which does not require a *kohen* and might therefore not be covered by the restoration. The response is decisive: if that were so, why does the verse say *kachatat ha-asham hu* rather than simply *kachatat hu la-kohen*? The explicit inclusion of *ha-asham* — the *asham* — signals that this exception is to be treated like all other *ashamot* in every respect. Once the *asham metzora* has been fully restored to the general category of *ashamot*, north is implicit in that restoration. Therefore, the separate explicit statement of north in the same passage must be serving a different purpose entirely — it is teaching that north is *me'akev*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב בְּגוּפֵיהּ וְלָא נִכְתּוֹב בְּהַאי.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina answered him: **If so,** why should the Torah write the requirement to slaughter a guilt offering in the north with regard to the guilt offering of a leper? **Let the verse state: For as the sin offering, so is it. What** is added by the expanded phrase: "For **as the sin offering, so is the guilt offering"?** It teaches that the guilt offering of a leper **will be like the rest of** the **guilt offerings.**

## Davar Halamed

### Both Links

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְאַקְשׁוֹיֵי לְחַטָּאת, לְמָה לִי לְאַקְשׁוֹיֵי לְעוֹלָה?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **to juxtapose** the guilt offering of a leper **to a sin offering** to teach the *halakha* that it must be slaughtered in the north, and **why do I** need **to juxtapose** it **to a burnt offering** to teach the same *halakha*? The verse states: "And he shall slaughter the sheep in the place where they slaughter the sin offering and the burnt offering, in the place of the Sanctuary" (Leviticus 14:13).

With the *me'akev* status of north established for the *olah*, the *chatat*, and the *asham*, the *Gemara* turns to a new question. The verse in the *asham metzora* passage connects the *kesev* — the lamb of the *asham metzora* — to both the *chatat* and the *olah*: *bimkom asher yishchat et ha-chatat ve-et ha-olah*. Why is this double linkage necessary? Why mention both?

### Chain Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אִיצְטְרִיךְ; אִי אַקְּשֵׁיהּ לְחַטָּאת וְלָא אַקְּשֵׁיהּ לְעוֹלָה – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: חַטָּאת מֵהֵיכָן לָמְדָה – מֵעוֹלָה; דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** It **was necessary** to juxtapose the guilt offering to both of them, as, **if** the verse **had juxtaposed it** only **to a sin offering but had not juxtaposed it to a burnt offering, I would say: From where** is the requirement to slaughter **a sin offering** in the north **derived?** It is **from** the *halakha* of **a burnt offering,** as explained on 48a. I would then assume that **a matter derived via a juxtaposition,** i.e., the *halakha* of a sin offering, which was derived via a juxtaposition to the *halakha* of a burnt offering, **then teaches** that *halakha* to another case **via a juxtaposition.** But there is a principle that with regard to matters of consecration the *halakha* may not be derived via a juxtaposition from another *halakha* that was itself derived via a juxtaposition. To prevent this incorrect assumption, the verse had to specifically teach the juxtaposition to a burnt offering.

Had the verse linked the *asham metzora* only to the *chatat* — "slaughter it where the *chatat* is slaughtered" — we would still have known it belongs in the north, because the *chatat* is slaughtered where the *olah* is slaughtered. The chain runs: *olah* establishes north; *chatat* derives north from *olah*; *asham* derives north from *chatat*. The logic appears sound. So why go back to the *olah*?

### Ravina's Answer

Ravina explains: the double linkage is necessary precisely because the chain is insufficient. The *chatat* knows that north is required only because the Torah linked the *chatat* to the *olah*. If we then attempt to use the *chatat*'s derived knowledge of north to teach it to the *asham*, we have committed a hermeneutic error. This is the principle of *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* — a law that is itself learned through *hekesh* (the linking of two subjects in a verse) may not be used to teach another law through yet another *hekesh*.

### Hekesh Principle

The principle works as follows. Call the *olah* A, the *chatat* B, and the *asham* C. The Torah explicitly links B to A; from this we derive that B — the *chatat* — must be slaughtered in the north. If we then attempt to derive C's obligation of north from B by means of another *hekesh*, we are doing precisely what the principle forbids: we are taking a law that B learned by *hekesh* (from A) and using it to teach C by *hekesh*. This is *davar halamed behekesh chozer umelamed behekesh* — applying a transitively learned law through another inferential link — and it is forbidden.

### Both Teaches Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי לְרָבִינָא: וְנַיקְּשֵׁיה לְעוֹלָה, וְלָא נַיקְּשֵׁיה לְחַטָּאת!
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, said to Ravina: But let** the verse **juxtapose** the guilt offering of a leper only **to a burnt offering and not juxtapose it to a sin offering.** The juxtaposition to a sin offering appears superfluous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ – חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ; וְכִי תֵּימָא: (ניקשי) [נַיקְּשֵׁיהּ] אַקּוֹשֵׁי לְחַטָּאת, נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּמַקֵּישׁ לֵיהּ לְעִיקָּר וְלָא נַקֵּישׁ לֵיהּ לְטָפֵל; לְהָכִי אַקְּשֵׁיהּ לְחַטָּאת וְאַקְּשֵׁיהּ לְעוֹלָה – לְמֵימַר דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ שֶׁאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina answered: If that had been so, **I would** still **say** that **a matter derived via a juxtaposition then teaches** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition. And if you would say** that if that were to be so, **let** the verse **juxtapose** the guilt offering of a leper only **to a sin offering,** one could answer that **it is preferable for** the Torah **that it juxtaposes** the guilt offering **to the primary** offering about which it states that it must be slaughtered in the north, i.e., the burnt offering, **and not juxtapose it to the secondary** offering, the sin offering. **For this** reason, i.e., to prevent the incorrect assumption that a matter derived via a juxtaposition then teaches its *halakha* via a juxtaposition, the verse **juxtaposed it to a sin offering and** also **juxtaposed it to a burnt offering, to say** that **a matter derived via a juxtaposition does not then teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition.**

Mar Zutra the son of Rav Mari raises a pointed question to Ravina: if the purpose was to establish that the *asham* requires north, why mention the *chatat* at all? Why not simply link the *asham* directly to the *olah* and be done with it?

Ravina's response is illuminating. Had the verse linked the *asham* only to the *olah* without mentioning the *chatat*, we would not have learned the hermeneutic principle. We would have said: the verse linked the *asham* to the *olah* rather than to the *chatat* — perhaps as a stylistic choice, or because the *olah* is the primary source. We would have had no grounds to conclude that linking to the *chatat* would have been prohibited. It is specifically by linking to both — first to the *chatat*, and then additionally to the *olah* — that the Torah signals: linking to the *chatat* alone would have been hermeneutically insufficient. The fact that the verse had to go directly to the *olah*, and could not rely on the chain through the *chatat*, is what teaches us that *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh*.

## Eimurim Proof

### Eimurim Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יוּרַם מִשּׁוֹר זֶבַח הַשְּׁלָמִים״ – לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אִי לְיוֹתֶרֶת הַכָּבֵד וּשְׁתֵּי הַכְּלָיוֹת – בְּגוּפֵיהּ כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The principle that a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach its *halakha* via a juxtaposition is derived **from here, as it is written** with regard to the sin offering brought by the High Priest: "And the two kidneys, and the fat that is upon them, which is by the loins, and the diaphragm with the liver, which he shall take away by the kidneys. **As it is taken off from the ox of the sacrifice of peace offerings;** and the priest shall make them smoke upon the altar of burnt offering" (Leviticus 4:9–10). One can ask: **For what *halakha*** is this offering juxtaposed to that of a peace offering? **If** it is **to** teach that the priest must sacrifice **the diaphragm with the liver and the two kidneys** from the offering, that is **written with regard to** the offering **itself,** in the previous verse. This does not need to be derived via a juxtaposition.

Rava cites an independent source for the same principle of *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh*, drawn from the laws of *eimurim* — the sacrificial portions burned on the *mizbe'ach*. The relevant verse is *ka-asher yuram mi-shor zevach ha-shelamim* — "just as it is raised from the ox of the *shelamim* sacrifice" — and it appears in the context of the *par kohen mashiach*, the bull of the anointed High Priest. The *Gemara* proceeds to show why this verse is indispensable, and how its presence proves the principle.

### Par Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵי אַגְמוֹרֵי יוֹתֶרֶת הַכָּבֵד וּשְׁתֵּי הַכְּלָיוֹת מִפַּר הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר שֶׁל צִיבּוּר לִשְׂעִירֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה; בְּגוּפֵיהּ לָא כְּתִיב, וּמִפַּר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ הוּא דְּגָמַר;
>
> **Translation:** It is written **due to** the fact **that** the verse **wants to teach** the *halakha* of **the diaphragm with the liver and the two kidneys,** deriving it **from** the *halakha* of the offering of **the bull for an unwitting communal sin,** then teaching it **to** apply it to the *halakha* of the **goats** brought as sin offerings for communal **idol worship. It is not written** explicitly **in** the passage discussing the bull for an unwitting communal sin **itself, and it is derived from** the *halakha* of **the bull for** an unwitting sin of **an anointed priest,** i.e., the High Priest. The offering for an unwitting communal sin is juxtaposed to the sin offering brought by the High Priest in the verse: "So shall he do with the bull; as he did with the bull of the sin offering, so shall he do with this" (Leviticus 4:20).

The *par kohen mashiach* explicitly states which *eimurim* are burned: the *yoteret*, the *kaved*, and the two *kilyot*. This is stated directly in the body of that passage. Two other categories of *korban* are relevant here. The *par he'elem davar* — the communal bull brought when the Sanhedrin rules erroneously — does *not* have its *eimurim* stated explicitly in its own passage. And the *se'irei avodah zarah* — the goats brought for communal inadvertent idolatry — similarly lack an explicit statement of *eimurim*.

### Shirei Chain

The result is that in order to apply *eimurim* to the *se'irei avodah zarah*, one would need a two-step inferential chain: first, derive *eimurim* for the *par he'elem davar* from the *par kohen mashiach* (since *he'elem davar* does not state them explicitly); second, derive *eimurim* for the *se'irei avodah zarah* from the *par he'elem davar*. This is precisely the structure that *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* prohibits.

### Keasher Yuram

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהָכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ ״כַּאֲשֶׁר יוּרַם״ – דְּנֶיהְוֵי כְּמַאן דִּכְתִב בְּגוּפֵיהּ, וְלָא נִיהְוֵי דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **For this** reason it **was necessary** for the verse to state: **"As it is taken off** from the ox" (Leviticus 4:10), so **that it is as though it wrote** explicitly **in** the passage discussing the bull for an unwitting communal sin **itself.** One of the hermeneutical principles applied to understanding verses in the Torah is: If the *halakha* stated is not required for the matter in which it is written, apply it to another matter. When this principle is employed, the *halakha* written in one context is viewed as if it were written elsewhere. In this case, as it was not necessary for the verse to write the juxtaposition to a peace offering in the context of the sin offering brought by the High Priest, it is applied to the case of the bull for an unwitting communal sin. **And** therefore it **will not be** a case of **a matter derived via a juxtaposition** that **then teaches** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition.**

This is where the verse *ka-asher yuram mi-shor zevach ha-shelamim* becomes essential. This phrase is redundant in its immediate context — the *eimurim* of the *par kohen mashiach* have already been specified. The *Gemara* explains that the verse functions to make the *eimurim* of the *par he'elem davar* treated *as if* they had been written explicitly in its own passage, rather than merely derived by *hekesh* from the *par kohen mashiach*. Once the *eimurim* of the *par he'elem davar* are treated as explicitly stated, the second link — to *se'irei avodah zarah* — is not an instance of *davar halamed behekesh chozer umelamed behekesh*, because the intermediate step is now considered direct rather than derivative.

### Extra Verse Proof

The implication is stark: without this extra verse, the two-step chain would have been unavailable, and there would have been no valid basis for applying *eimurim* to the *se'irei avodah zarah*. The very necessity of this redundant verse demonstrates that *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* — transitive *hekesh* is insufficient, and an independent basis is required at each step.

### Rav Papa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: וְלִיכְתְּבֵיהּ בְּגוּפֵיהּ, וְלָא נַקֵּישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rava: But** why not **let** the Torah **write** explicitly **in** the passage discussing the bull for an unwitting communal sin **itself** that the diaphragm with the liver and two kidneys must be removed from the ox, **and not juxtapose** it to a peace offering in this convoluted manner?

Rav Papa presses Rava: if the purpose was to make the *eimurim* of the *par he'elem davar* explicit, why not simply write them explicitly in the *par he'elem davar* passage itself? Why do it through this indirect device of a redundant verse by the *par kohen mashiach*?

### Redundancy Teaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי כְּתַב בְּגוּפֵיהּ וְלָא אַקֵּישׁ – הֲוָה אָמֵינָא דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ; וְכִי תֵּימָא (נקשי) [נַקְּשֵׁיהּ] אַקּוֹשֵׁי – נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּכַתְבֵיהּ בְּגוּפֵיהּ מִדְּאַקֵּישׁ לֵיהּ אַקּוֹשֵׁי; לְהָכִי כַּתְבֵיהּ וְאַקְּשֵׁיהּ, לְמֵימְרָא דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ אֵין חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava answered: **If** the Torah **had written** it **in** that passage **itself, and not juxtaposed** it, **I would say** that in general **a matter derived via a juxtaposition then teaches via a juxtaposition,** as one would not have this instance to serve as a counterexample to that principle. **And if you would say:** If so, why not simply **juxtapose** the case of the bull for an unwitting communal sin to the case of the bull of the anointed priest, I would answer: **It is preferable for** the verse **that it writes it in** the passage **itself** rather **than to juxtapose it** alone. It is **for this** reason that **it wrote it and juxtaposed it, to say** that **a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition.**

The answer parallels exactly what was said earlier about the *chatat* and the *olah*. Had the *eimurim* been stated explicitly in the *par he'elem davar* passage from the outset, we would never have known that the two-step chain was prohibited. We would have concluded that the Torah simply chose to write it directly for stylistic reasons, and that in principle a *hekesh* chain could also have worked. By instead writing the *eimurim* explicitly by the *par kohen mashiach* and allowing the chain to proceed — while simultaneously inserting a verse that makes the intermediate step *as if* explicit — the Torah reveals what would have been missing without it. The necessity of that extra verse teaches us that without it, the chain would have failed: *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh*. The principle is thus demonstrated in precisely the same way as by the *chatat* and the *olah*: the Torah could have taken the more direct route but did not, and the reason it did not is what teaches the hermeneutic rule.

## Gezeira Shava

### Tzaraat Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *halakha* as to whether **a matter derived via a juxtaposition** can **teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a verbal analogy?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי נָתָן בֶּן אַבְטוּלְמוֹס אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לִפְרִיחָה בִּבְגָדִים שֶׁהִיא טְהוֹרָה? נֶאֱמַר קָרַחַת וְגַבַּחַת בַּבְּגָדִים, וְנֶאֱמַר קָרַחַת וְגַבַּחַת בָּאָדָם;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan ben Avtolemos says: From where** is it derived **that** if leprosy **of garments spreads** throughout an entire garment **that it is pure?** It is derived via a verbal analogy: **A bareness within [*karaḥat*] and a bareness without [*gabbaḥat*]** are **stated with regard to** leprosy of **garments:** "And the priest shall look, after that the mark is washed; and behold, if the mark has not changed its color, and the mark has not spread, it is impure; you shall burn it in the fire; it is a fret, whether the bareness be within or without" (Leviticus 13:55); **and a bald head [*karaḥat*] and a bald forehead [*gabbaḥat*]** are **stated with regard to** leprosy of **a person:** "But if there be in the bald head, or the bald forehead, a reddish-white plague, it is leprosy breaking out in his bald head, or his bald forehead" (Leviticus 13:42).

The *Gemara* now asks a more refined question: we have established that in *kodshim*, one may not apply a law that was itself learned through *hekesh* to yet another subject through another *hekesh*. But what if the second step is not another *hekesh* but a *gezeira shava* — the inference drawn from identical or parallel language in two verses? Perhaps a *gezeira shava* is more powerful than a *hekesh*, and the restriction does not extend to this combination.

To explore whether *davar halamed behekesh* applies beyond *kodshim* altogether — and whether *hekesh* followed by *gezeira shava* might work even in *kodshim* — the *Gemara* brings a proof from *tzara'at*. Rabbi Natan ben Avtulas asks: from where do we know that if a *tzara'at* lesion spreads across an entire garment, it renders the garment *tahor*? The answer invokes the words *karachto* and *gavachto* — terms that appear in the context of skin *tzara'at* and, strikingly, also in the context of garment *tzara'at* (Vayikra 13:55). The shared language creates a *gezeira shava*, linking garment *tzara'at* to skin *tzara'at*.

### Two Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַלָּן – פָּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ טָהוֹר, אַף כָּאן – פָּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Just as there,** with regard to a person, if the leprosy **spread throughout him** entirely he is **pure,** as the verse states: "Then the priest shall look; and behold, if the leprosy has covered all of his flesh, he shall pronounce the one who has the mark pure; it is all turned white: He is pure" (Leviticus 13:13), **so too here** with regard to garments, if the leprosy **spread throughout the entire** garment it is **pure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָם מְנָא לַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵרֹאשׁוֹ וְעַד רַגְלָיו״ – וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ רֹאשׁוֹ (לרגל) [לְרַגְלָיו]; מָה לְהַלָּן, כּוּלּוֹ הָפַךְ לָבָן פֶּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ – טָהוֹר; אַף כָּאן, כּוּלּוֹ הָפַךְ לָבָן פָּרַח בְּכוּלּוֹ – טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its proof: **But there,** with regard to the head, which serves as the source for this verbal analogy, **from where do we** derive that if the leprosy spreads throughout the head he is pure? **As it is written:** "And if the leprosy breaks out on the skin, and the leprosy covers all the skin of the one who has the mark, **from his head to his feet,** as far as it appears to the priest" (Leviticus 13:12). **And** the verse thereby **juxtaposes** leprosy on **his head to** leprosy on his **foot,** teaching the following *halakha*: **Just as there,** with regard to leprosy of the body and foot, if **its entirety turned white,** and it **spread all over him,** he is **pure, so too here,** in the case of leprosy of the head, if **its entirety turned white and it spread over all his** head, he is **pure.** Evidently, a matter derived via a juxtaposition can then teach its *halakha* to another matter via a verbal analogy.

But the chain does not begin with the *gezeira shava*. The principle that spreading over the whole body renders a person *tahor* is established first by the skin — derived from the verse *mi-rosho ve-ad raglo*, "from his head to his foot," which links the *nega* of the skin to the *nega* of the hair of the head through a *hekesh*. The logic is: just as spreading of the skin *nega* across the whole body is *tahor*, so too spreading of the hair *nega* across the whole head is *tahor*. From hair to garments, however, the link is a *gezeira shava* — the parallel language *karachto ve-gavachto*. Thus the chain runs: skin to hair via *hekesh*, and hair to garments via *gezeira shava*. Here is an instance of *hekesh* followed by *gezeira shava* producing a valid ruling.

### Kodshim Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ לְמֵידִין לָמֵד מִלָּמֵד, חוּץ מִן הַקֳּדָשִׁים – שֶׁאֵין דָּנִין לָמֵד מִלָּמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This cannot serve as a proof with regard to the *halakhot* of consecrated matters. **With regard to the entire Torah,** one **derives** a *halakha* **derived** via a verbal analogy **from** a *halakha* **derived** via a juxtaposition, **apart from** with regard to **consecrated** matters, **where** one **does not derive** a *halakha* **derived** via a verbal analogy **from** a *halakha* **derived** via a juxtaposition.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves this immediately: throughout the entire Torah, one may apply a law that was learned by *hekesh* further, whether by another *hekesh* or by *gezeira shava*. The restriction — *davar halamed behekesh eino chozer umelamed behekesh* — applies exclusively to *kodshim*. The *tzara'at* proof thus raises no difficulty at all for the principle as it operates in the realm of sacrificial law. This is a striking point. The intensive legislative detail of *kodshim* generates two related but distinct strictures: on the one hand, no requirement is treated as *me'akev* unless the Torah repeats it; on the other hand, the inferential chains by which laws are derived are more tightly constrained. Both reflect the same underlying orientation toward *kodshim* as a domain governed by extraordinary precision.

### Hekesh Plus Gezeira

Within *kodshim*, then, the question remains open: is the combination of *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* permitted, or is it also forbidden? The *Gemara* now provides an answer.

### Forbidden Combo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִם כֵּן – לֹא יֵאָמֵר צָפוֹנָה בְּאָשָׁם, וְתֵיתֵי בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה דְּ״קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים״ מֵחַטָּאוֹת;
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: **As if** it were to be **so** that one could derive a *halakha* in this manner even concerning consecrated matters, the verse **should not state** the requirement for slaughter in the **north with regard to a guilt offering,** as stated from the explicit juxtaposition of a burnt offering and a sin offering, **and** instead **derive it through a verbal analogy.** The verse describes a guilt offering **as an offering of the most sacred order** (see Leviticus 7:1), and its *halakha* can be derived via a verbal analogy **from** that of **a sin offering,** which is described in the same manner (see Leviticus 6:18).

Rabbi Yochanan's proof that even *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* is forbidden in *kodshim* is derived from the same *pasuk* we have already studied: *ki kachatat ha-asham hu la-kohen kodesh kodashim hu* — "for the *asham* is like the *chatat* to the *kohen*; it is *kodesh kodashim*."

### If Allowed

If *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* were permitted in *kodshim*, there would have been a simpler path to establish that the *asham* requires north. The *chatat* is known to be slaughtered in the north — derived through *hekesh* from the *olah*. The *asham* is called *kodesh kodashim*, and so is the *chatat*. A *gezeira shava* on the term *kodesh kodashim* would then link the *asham* to the *chatat*. Through that *gezeira shava*, one could transfer the *chatat*'s requirement of north to the *asham*.

### Direct Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאו לְמֵימְרָא דְּדָבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ אֵין חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בִּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan continues: Is this **not to say,** at least with regard to consecrated matters, **that a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach** its *halakha* to another matter **via a verbal analogy?**

But the Torah did not do this. Instead, the verse links the *asham* directly to the *olah*. Why bypass the *chatat* and go straight to the *olah*? Precisely because a *hekesh*-derived law — the *chatat*'s requirement of north, which the *chatat* itself learned by *hekesh* from the *olah* — may not be extended further, not even through a *gezeira shava*. The direct link to the *olah* was necessary because the indirect route through the *chatat* was closed, even via *gezeira shava*.

### Chatat Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְחַטָּאת, שֶׁכֵּן מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects Rabbi Yoḥanan's proof: **But perhaps** the requirement to slaughter a guilt offering in the north must be written explicitly **because** the verbal analogy **can be refuted** as follows: **What** is notable **about a sin offering?** It is notable **in that it atones for** those sins **liable for** punishment by ***karet*,** which is not so with regard to a guilt offering.

One might object: perhaps the *gezeira shava* between the *chatat* and the *asham* was insufficient not because of the principle of *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava*, but simply because the *chatat* is a weightier *korban* — it atones for those liable to *karet* — whereas the *asham* does not. A *gezeira shava* that links an unequal pair may be deficient for that reason alone, independent of the transitivity question.

### Kodesh Extra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים״ יַתִּירֵי כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this claim: There are **additional** descriptions of a guilt offering and a sin offering as **offerings of the most sacred order written** in the verses: "This shall be yours of the most sacred items, reserved from the fire: Every offering of theirs, every meal offering of theirs, and every sin offering of theirs, and every guilt offering of theirs, which they may offer to Me, shall be most holy for you and for your sons" (Numbers 18:9). A verbal analogy derived from extra phrases in the verse cannot be refuted with a logical claim. Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan's claim stands, and, at least with regard to consecrated matters, a matter derived via one of the hermeneutical principles cannot teach via another of those principles.

The *Gemara* dismisses this concern. The term *kodesh kodashim* is stated multiple times in connection with both the *chatat* and the *asham*. In the language of *gezeira shava*, when the shared words are themselves fully superfluous — *mufneh* — the inference is valid even when the two cases are not perfectly analogous. The repeated, extra appearances of *kodesh kodashim* in both contexts would make the *gezeira shava* robust enough to carry the law regardless of the substantive differences between *chatat* and *asham*. Therefore, the only reason the Torah had to link the *asham* directly to the *olah* — bypassing the *chatat* entirely — is that *hekesh* plus *gezeira shava* is also forbidden in *kodshim*, just as *hekesh* plus *hekesh* is forbidden. The two restrictions stand in parallel: in the domain of *kodshim*, neither a second *hekesh* nor a *gezeira shava* may be used to extend a law that was itself derived by *hekesh*.
