# Avodah Zarah 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Barrel, Removed Spout

### Setup: Spout Falls Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא חָבִיתָא דְּחַמְרָא דְּאִישְׁתְּקִיל לְבַרְזָא, אֲתָא גּוֹי, אִידְּרִי אַנַּח יְדֵיהּ עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain barrel of wine** from **which the stopper had been removed** and the wine was spilling out. **A gentile came, jumped up,** and **placed his hand on** the hole to prevent the wine from leaking.

The *gemara* opens with a case involving a barrel of wine whose spout has been dislodged. With the spout removed, there is simply a hole in the barrel, and the wine begins to flow out. A non-Jewish person comes over and places his hand over the hole to prevent the wine from spilling — *ata oved kochavim idrei anach yadei iloy*. The question before Rav Papa is what status to assign to the wine remaining in the barrel.

### Rav Papa v. 1: Proximity…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כֹּל דְּלַהֲדֵי בַּרְזָא — חַמְרָא אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Any wine that is adjacent** to **the stopper** is **prohibited,** as it was touched by the gentile,

In the first version of Rav Papa's ruling, he says: *kol de-lehavi barza assur* — whatever wine was in the vicinity of the spout opening is prohibited, because the non-Jewish person made contact with it. *Ve-idach shari* — the rest of the wine in the barrel, however, remains permissible. This is the more lenient position: only the wine in direct proximity to the opening is affected.

### Rav Papa v. 2: Drainage…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידָּךְ שְׁרֵי, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עַד (הברזא) [בַּרְזָא] — חַמְרָא אֲסִיר, וְאִידָּךְ שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **but the other** wine in the barrel **is permitted. There are** those **who say** that **Rav Pappa said: The wine until the stopper,** i.e., in the upper portion of the barrel, is **prohibited, but the other** wine in the barrel, below the stopper, is **permitted.**

The second version of Rav Papa's ruling is considerably stricter. He says: *ad ha-barza chamra assur* — all of the wine in the upper portion of the barrel, meaning everything that would have drained out through the hole had the non-Jew not been holding his hand there, is prohibited. Since it is only the non-Jew's physical presence that keeps that wine from flowing out, it is considered as though he controls it. *Ve-idach shari* — only the wine in the lower portion, which would not have drained out regardless, remains permissible.

### Rav Yeimar: Tannaic Para…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יֵימַר, כְּתַנָּאֵי: חָבִית שֶׁנִּקְּבָה, בֵּין מִפִּיהָ, בֵּין מִשּׁוּלֶיהָ, וּבֵין מִצִּידֶּיהָ, וְנָגַע בּוֹ טְבוּל יוֹם — טְמֵאָה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִפִּיהָ וּמִשּׁוּלֶיהָ — טְמֵאָה, מִצִּידֶּיהָ — טְהוֹרָה מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar says:** Rav Pappa's ruling is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as the mishna teaches (*Tevul Yom* 2:7): In the case of **a barrel that was pierced, whether on its top, on its bottom, or on** one of **its sides,** if **one who immersed that day touched it,** it is **ritually impure. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If it was pierced **on its top or on its bottom,** it is **impure.** If it was pierced **on** one of **its sides,** the wine is **pure,** whether it was touched **from here or from there,** i.e., on either side. Only the wine that he touched is rendered impure. According to the first version of Rav Pappa's statement he holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

Rav Yeimar then raises a challenge: *ke-tannai* — perhaps Rav Papa's ruling corresponds to a *machloket* among *tanna'im*, and specifically that Rav Papa's position aligns with the minority opinion. As Rashi explains on 60a, Rav Yeimar is pointing out that Rav Papa stands with Rabbi Yehuda, and since the rule is *yachid ve-rabbim halacha ke-rabbim*, aligning with Rabbi Yehuda places Rav Papa in the minority.

The *tannaic* parallel Rav Yeimar invokes comes from Masechet Tevul Yom. The *mishnah* there discusses a barrel that was pierced — *chavita she-nikba* — and a *tevul yom* (someone who has immersed in a *mikvah* but must still wait until nightfall, and is therefore *sheni le-tumah*) makes contact with the wine. The *mishnah* specifies: if the barrel was pierced at its mouth (*mi-piha*) or at its base (*mi-shuleha*), the wine is rendered *tamei*. But if the hole is on the side, it is *tehorah mikan u-mikan* — pure on both sides of the hole. Rabbi Yehuda's position distinguishes between the mouth, the base, and the side, and it is this position that maps onto the dispute underlying Rav Papa's ruling.

### Rashi: Basis Concept

Rashi's explanation of Rabbi Yehuda's position illuminates the underlying logic. If the barrel is pierced at its mouth — the top — then whatever wine the *tevul yom* physically touches is only up near the opening, but the wine below forms a *basis*, a supporting base, for the wine above. Because the lower wine is what makes it possible for the upper wine to remain at the level of the opening, it is considered *chibbur*, a connection, such that all of it becomes *tamei* together.

If the hole is at the base, the rationale is different but the result is the same: all the wine would drain out through the hole, so the entire contents are considered connected — *ho-il ve-chol ha-yayin nimshach achar ha-nekev havei chibbur*.

If the hole is on the side, however, neither of those two rationales applies cleanly. Only what is above the hole would drain, and the *basis* logic does not extend as fully. The result is *tehorah mikan u-mikan* — the wine on each side of the hole retains its status independently.

It is worth noting that the association being drawn here is a striking one: the *amora* Rav Yeimar is connecting the laws of *yayin nesech* to the laws of *tumah*, using the standard of contact required to disqualify something under *tumah* law as a model for determining what constitutes actionable contact in the context of *yayin nesech*.

## Wine Pouring Scenarios

### Non-Jew Tilts Barrel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: גּוֹי אַדַּנָּא וְיִשְׂרָאֵל אַכּוּבָּא — חַמְרָא אֲסִיר, מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּי קָאָתֵי — מִכֹּחַ גּוֹי קָאָתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** In a case where **a gentile** is pouring the wine from **the barrel and a Jew** is holding **the beaker [*kuva*]** into which it is poured, **the wine** is **prohibited. What is the reason? When** the wine **comes** out of the barrel, **it comes** out **by force of the gentile's** action.

Rav Papa addresses a new scenario: *ovdei kochavim adana ve-Yisrael akuba* — the non-Jewish person is tilting the large barrel (*adana*) to pour wine out, while the Jewish person holds the smaller flask (*kuba*) to receive it. Rav Papa rules: *chamra asir* — the wine is prohibited. The reason is *ki ka-ati mi-koach ovdei kochavim ka-ati*: the wine flows by virtue of the non-Jew's strength and control. He is the one directing the pour; the flow of wine is a product of his force.

### Jew Tilts Barrel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִשְׂרָאֵל אַדַּנָּא וְגוֹי אַכּוּבָּא — חַמְרָא שְׁרֵי, וְאִי מְצַדֵּד צַדּוֹדֵי — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **a Jew** is pouring the wine from **the barrel and a gentile** is holding **the beaker** into which it is poured, **the wine** is **permitted. But if** the gentile **tilts** the beaker **to the side,** the wine is **prohibited.**

The reverse case: the Jewish person tilts the large barrel and the non-Jew holds the flask stationary. Here, *chamra shari* — the wine is permissible. The non-Jew is merely receiving the wine passively; he is not the one causing it to move.

### Non-Jew Tilts Flask

However, if the non-Jew, while holding the flask, tilts it sideways — *ve-im mi-tzadadi tzedudi* — then it is prohibited (*asir*). Tilting the flask is an active intervention in the movement of the wine, and that changes the analysis.

### Contact & Control

The governing principle across these cases is the distinction between active control and passive receipt. A non-Jew who merely holds a vessel in a fixed position is not considered to be "handling" the wine in a halachically significant sense. But a non-Jew who controls the flow — whether by tilting the barrel or by manipulating the receiving vessel — has exercised sufficient agency over the wine to render it prohibited. It should be noted that the concern in these cases is not necessarily that the non-Jew had any idolatrous intent; the Rabbinic prohibition of *stam yeinam* applies regardless of intent, and what matters is the degree of handling and control.

## Transport: Skins vs. Jars

### Wine Skin Transport

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי גּוֹי דְּדָרֵי זִיקָּא וְקָאָזֵיל יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲחוֹרֵיהּ, מַלְיָא — שְׁרֵי, דְּלָא מְקַרְקֵשׁ. חַסִּירָא — אֲסִיר, דִּלְמָא מְקַרְקֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** In the case of **this gentile who carries** a sealed **wineskin and a Jew is walking behind him** and ensuring that the gentile does not touch the wine itself, the *halakha* depends on the circumstances. If the wineskin is **full,** the wine is **permitted, as** the wine in the wineskin **is not shaken.** If the wineskin is **incompletely** filled, the wine is **prohibited,** as **perhaps** the wine in the wineskin might have been **shaken** by the gentile, which would have the same *halakha* as wine poured as a libation.

The *gemara* turns to a case of wine being transported: *hai oved kochavim de-dari zika ve-kazil Yisrael achorei* — a non-Jewish person is carrying wine in a skin (*zika*), a bag-type container, while a Jewish person follows behind to supervise and ensure the wine is not tampered with.

### Rav Papa: Full vs. Parti…

Rav Papa draws a distinction based on how full the skin is. If it is full — *malya* — it is permissible, *de-lo mekarkesh*: there is no air space, so the wine cannot be shaken around. If it is only partially full — *chasira* — it is prohibited, *dilma mekarkesh*: the non-Jew could shake the bag, agitating the wine in a way that could constitute prohibited handling.

### Open Jar: Opposite Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּבָּא: מַלְיָא — אֲסִיר, דִּלְמָא נָגַע. חַסִּירָא — שְׁרֵי, דְּלָא נָגַע.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a beaker,** which is open on top, if it is **full** the wine is **prohibited,** as **perhaps** the gentile might have **touched** the wine. If the beaker is **incompletely** filled, the wine is **permitted, as** the gentile **did not touch** the wine.

For an open jar (*kuba*), the rule is precisely the opposite. If the jar is full, it is prohibited — *dilma naga* — because the wine reaches near the top and the non-Jew might touch it. If it is only partially full, it is permissible — *de-lo naga* — because the wine level is too low for him to have made contact. So for the skin, full is better; for the open jar, less full is better. The different physical properties of each container drive the opposite conclusions.

### Rav Ashi: Skins Always OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: זִיקָא, בֵּין מַלְיָא וּבֵין חַסִּירָא — שְׁרֵי, מַאי טַעְמָא? אֵין דֶּרֶךְ נִיסּוּךְ בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** In the case of **a wineskin, whether** it is **full or incompletely** filled it is **permitted. What is the reason** that the wine is permitted even if it is shaken within the wineskin? It is because **this is not the** typical **manner of offering a libation.**

Rav Ashi disagrees with Rav Papa's distinction regarding wine skins. He holds that *zika*, a wine skin, is permissible whether full or partially full — *bein malya u-vein chasira shari*.

### Not Customary Manner

Rav Ashi's rationale is: *ein derech nisuch b'kach* — this is not the customary manner in which idolatrous libations are performed. The wine skin is a lesser, more disposable container, and it is not the kind of vessel through which one would conduct a proper act of *nisuch*. The *derech nisuch* — the standard mode of libation — requires a level of formality and presentation that a wine skin does not afford. Using a wine carried in a skin for idolatrous purposes would be, as it were, beneath the conventions of the practice.

## Wine Press Debate

### Mechanical Press Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעְצְרָא זָיְירָא — רַב פַּפִּי שָׁרֵי, רַב אָשֵׁי, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי, אָסַר.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **a winepress** where the grapes are pressed with beams, rather than trod by foot, **Rav Pappi deemed permitted** wine that is produced by a gentile, as the gentile does not touch the wine. **Rav Ashi, and some say** it was **Rav Shimi bar Ashi, deemed** the wine **prohibited.**

The *gemara* now turns to the wine press. The setting is *me-atzra zeira* — a wine press of a specific kind. Rashi explains: *gat she-ein dorechin ela kofshin ba-kora* — a press where the grapes are not trodden by foot but instead crushed by a mechanical beam. The mechanism involves placing grapes in the press, using a lever or piston to apply downward force, and allowing the juice to flow out. The non-Jewish person operates this press without any direct physical contact with the grapes or the wine itself. The question is whether this kind of indirect involvement renders the wine prohibited.

### Direct vs. Secondary For…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכֹחוֹ, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר. כִּי פְּלִיגִי — בְּכֹחַ כֹּחוֹ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: בְּכֹחַ כֹּחוֹ, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי — בְּכֹחוֹ. הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא בְּכֹחַ כֹּחוֹ, וְאָסַר רַב יַעֲקֹב מִנְּהַר פְּקוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **In** a case where the wine is pressed by means of the gentile's direct **force everyone agrees that** the wine is **prohibited. They disagree when** the wine is pressed by means of **a force** generated **by his force.** Conversely, **there are** those **who say** that **in** a case where the wine is pressed **by** means of **a force** generated **by** the gentile's **force everyone agrees that** the wine is **permitted. They disagree when** the wine is pressed by means of the gentile's direct **force.** The Gemara relates: **There was an incident in** which wine was pressed by means of **a force** generated **by** the gentile's **force, and Rav Yaakov from Nehar Pekod deemed** the wine **prohibited.**

Rav Pappi rules it permissible; Rav Ashi — or according to another version, Rav Shimi bar Ashi — rules it prohibited. The *gemara* then clarifies the scope of their debate. *Bekocho* — if the non-Jew applied his own physical strength directly to the press mechanism, pressing the lever down himself — *kulei alma lo peligi de-assur*: everyone agrees it is prohibited. That is equivalent to tilting the barrel: the wine is produced by his direct force.

*Ki peligi* — where they disagree — is *bekoach kocho*: a secondary degree of force. For instance, if the non-Jew loaded rocks into a basket that weighs down the press beam, so that it is the rocks, not the non-Jew's arm, that crushes the grapes — is that level of mediated control sufficient to prohibit the wine?

### Rashi: Two Degrees

Rashi spells out the distinction clearly. *Bekocho* is when the non-Jew presses the lever directly with his own strength — his body is the instrument of force. *Koach kocho* is when he does something that sets a secondary mechanism in motion — for example, *she-megalgel ve-hagalgal korei vaitz* — he turns a wheel, and the wheel operates the beam, and the beam crushes the grapes. His action is one step removed from the wine itself.

### All Agree on Direct

A second version of the dispute reframes its scope entirely. According to this version, *bekoach kocho kulei alma lo peligi de-shari* — everyone agrees that a secondary degree of force is permissible. The debate between Rav Pappi and Rav Ashi concerns *bekocho* — direct force — and even there, perhaps, there is room to permit it since the non-Jew never actually touched the grapes or wine. *Koach kocho*, two degrees removed, would be universally permitted.

### Rav Yaakov Case

A practical case was brought: *hava uvda bekoach kocho* — a real incident occurred involving secondary force, *ve-asar Rav Yaakov mi-nahar pekod* — and Rav Yaakov from Nahar Pekod ruled it prohibited. This ruling serves as a *ma'aseh rav*, a practical precedent supporting Rav Ashi's more stringent position as understood in the first version of the dispute.

### Modern Automation

This debate carries direct relevance to contemporary wine production. If a mechanical press is powered by hydraulics or an electric motor that operates entirely without human contact with the grapes, it is difficult to characterize the process even as *bekocho* in the classical sense — and certainly not as direct contact. If there is a step where a person presses a button or engages a lever, the question of *koach kocho* becomes live. The discussion cuts in more than one direction, however. The very same logic about automation and human intent that would tend toward leniency in the context of *yayin nesech* raises a stringency when applied to the requirement of *lishmah* — human intention — for items like machine-made *matzos* or *tzitzis*. Whether activating a machine that produces thousands of units constitutes sufficient intentional human agency for *mitzvah* purposes is the flip side of the same conceptual coin.

## Barrel Split Lengthwise

### Non-Jew Hugs Barrel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא חָבִיתָא דְּאִיפְּקַעָה לְאוּרְכַּהּ, אִידְּרִי הָהוּא גּוֹי חַבְּקַהּ, שַׁרְיַיהּ רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא, וְאִי תֵּימָא רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, לְזַבּוֹנֵי לְגוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain barrel** **that split lengthwise** from top to bottom, and **a certain gentile jumped up** and **encircled it** with his arms in order to prevent the wine from spilling. **Rafram bar Pappa, and some say** it was **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, deemed it permitted to sell** the wine **to gentiles,** as the wine was rendered prohibited only for drinking but not with regard to deriving benefit.

The *gemara* presents a case at the bottom of 60a: *hahu chavita de-itka'a le-orka* — a wine barrel that split lengthwise, meaning a crack running from top to bottom. The wine is about to spill. A non-Jewish passerby, wishing to prevent the loss, hugs the barrel — *chavkah* — pressing the two sides together to hold the crack closed.

### Two Versions

Two versions of the ruling are recorded. According to one, Rafram bar Papa permitted the wine even for drinking. According to the other, it was Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua who gave the ruling, and he permitted only *l-zavuney* — selling the wine to non-Jews — but not drinking it. This intermediate category, permissible to sell but not to drink, will shortly become the subject of broader discussion in the context of Rabbi Shimon's position.

### Lengthwise vs. Horizontal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דִּפְקַעָה לְאוּרְכַּהּ, אֲבָל לְפוּתְיַיהּ — אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁתִיָּה שְׁרֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? מַעֲשֵׂה לְבֵינָה קָעָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This statement** applies only in a case **where it split lengthwise. But** where the barrel split **widthwise** and the gentile held the top and bottom halves together, it is **permitted even for drinking. What is the reason** that the wine is permitted? The gentile **is** merely **performing the action of a brick** by weighing the barrel down, and he is not doing anything to the wine.

The leniency in the case of a horizontally split barrel — cracked around its equator — is greater than in the longitudinal case. *Ve-hanei milei de-faka le-orcha* — this discussion applies specifically to a lengthwise split. *Aval le-futyeh* — but if the barrel was cracked horizontally — *afilu bi-shtiyah sharei* — even Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua would permit drinking the wine.

### Brick Analogy

The reason is: *ma'aseh leveinah ka avid* — the non-Jew is doing the work of a brick. To hold a longitudinal crack closed, the non-Jew must apply lateral force from both sides simultaneously, hugging the barrel and actively maintaining pressure. That constitutes a meaningful human intervention. But if the crack is horizontal and the barrel simply needs downward pressure to stay together, pressing down from above is no different from placing a brick on top of it — it is purely passive, mechanical pressure that any inanimate object could supply equally well. Such pressure does not rise to the level of halachically significant contact with the wine.

### Non-Jew in Press

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גּוֹי דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח דַּהֲוָה קָאֵי בְּמַעְצַרְתָּא, אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִי אִיכָּא טוֹפֵחַ לְהַטְפִּיחַ — בָּעֵי הַדָּחָה וּבָעֵי נִיגּוּב, וְאִי לָא — בְּהַדָּחָה בְּעָלְמָא סַגִּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain gentile who was found standing in a winepress. Rav Ashi said: If there is** enough wine in the winepress that it is **moist** enough **to moisten** other items, the winepress **requires rinsing and requires** a more thorough **cleansing,** as the Gemara will explain (74b). **But if** there is **not** enough wine to moisten other items, **merely rinsing is sufficient for it.**

Separately, Rav Ashi addresses a case where a non-Jew was found standing inside the wine press: *hahu oved kochavim de-ishtakach de-hava ka-ei be-ma'atzarta*. Rav Ashi rules based on the degree of moisture present. If the floor of the press was wet to the level of *tfo'ach le-hatpi'ach* — wet enough that contact with it would wet something else, a standard measure of moisture in *halacha* — then *ba-ei hadachah u-va-ei niggub*: the press must both be washed out and then dried thoroughly. The concern is that *yayin nesech* residue remains even in the wash water and must be eliminated entirely. If the moisture level was below that threshold, *be-hadachah be-alma sagi leh* — washing alone is sufficient, without requiring subsequent drying.

## Storage Pit Mishnah

### Debt as Motive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נׇכְרִי שֶׁנִּמְצָא עוֹמֵד בְּצַד הַבּוֹר שֶׁל יַיִן, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ מִלְוָה עָלָיו — אָסוּר, אֵין לוֹ מִלְוָה עָלָיו — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a gentile who was found standing next to the wine** collection **vat, if there is a loan** owed **by** the owner of the wine vat **to** the gentile, the wine is **prohibited.** Since the gentile maintains that he has a right to the owner's property he has no compunctions about touching the wine. But if **there is no loan** owed **by** the owner of the wine vat **to** the gentile, the wine is **permitted,** as it is assumed that the gentile did not touch the wine that was not his.

The *gemara* moves to the *mishnah* on 60b. *Oved kochavim she-nimtza omed be-tzad ha-bor shel yayin* — a non-Jewish person was found standing next to a storage pit of wine. The *mishnah* rules: *im yesh lo milveh alav assur* — if the non-Jew has a debt that the Jewish owner owes him, the wine is prohibited. The reasoning is that the non-Jew, believing the wine to be his practical collateral, may feel entitled to handle or take it. *Ein lo milveh alav mutar* — if there is no such debt, the wine remains permissible, as there is no motivation for him to have interfered with it.

### Touching Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לַבּוֹר וְעָלָה, מְדָדוֹ בְּקָנֶה, הִתִּיז אֶת הַצִּרְעָה בְּקָנֶה, אוֹ שֶׁהָיָה מְטַפֵּיחַ עַל פִּי חָבִית מְרוּתַּחַת — בְּכׇל אֵלּוּ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה וְאָמְרוּ: יִמָּכֵר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר. נָטַל אֶת הֶחָבִית וּזְרָקָהּ בַּחֲמָתוֹ לַבּוֹר, זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה וְהִכְשִׁירוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If a gentile **fell into the** wine collection **vat and emerged** from it, or if **he measured** the wine in the winepress **with a pole** without touching it with his hands, or if **he cast a hornet** out of the wine **by** means of **a pole** and the pole touched the wine, **or where** the gentile **was removing the foam** that was **on** the **top of a fermenting barrel** of wine; **with regard to all these** cases **there was** such **an incident. And** the Sages **said** that the wine **may be sold** to gentiles, as it is permitted to derive benefit from the wine, but not to drink it. **And Rabbi Shimon deems** the wine **permitted** even for drinking. In a case where a gentile **took the barrel** of wine **and threw it, in his anger, into the** wine collection **vat, this was an incident** that occurred **and** the Sages **deemed** the wine **fit** for drinking.

The *mishnah* then lists several additional cases: *nafal la-bor ve-alah* — a non-Jew fell into the wine pit and came back out; *medado be-kaneh* — he measured the wine with a rod; *hetiz et ha-tzirah be-kaneh* — he removed a wasp from the wine with a rod; or he wiped the foam off the top of an open barrel. In all of these cases — *ve-chol elu hayah ma'aseh* — each was an actual incident that came before the authorities — *ve-amru yimacher* — and they ruled the wine should be sold (but not consumed by a Jew).

### Angry Throwing

The *mishnah* also records: *natal et ha-chavit ve-zarkah be-chamato la-bor* — a non-Jewish person, in a fit of anger, picked up a barrel of wine and threw it into the storage pit. *Zeh hayah ma'aseh ve-hikhshiru* — that too was a real case, and the wine was ruled permissible.

### Shmuel: Specific Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְהוּא שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מִלְוָה עַל אוֹתוֹ יַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that in the case of a gentile who was found standing next to a wine collection vat, if the owner of the vat owes money to the gentile the wine is prohibited. **Shmuel says: And this** *halakha* applies only **when** the **loan** includes the qualification that the gentile **has** a lien **on that wine,** as only then does the gentile feel that he is entitled to touch the wine.

Shmuel qualifies the opening ruling of the *mishnah*: *ve-hu she-yesh lo milveh al oto yayin* — it is not sufficient that the non-Jew has any debt owed to him by the Jewish owner. The wine is only at risk if the debt is specifically secured by or against *that* wine. Only then does the non-Jew have reason to think of the wine as something he can lay claim to and therefore handle freely. A general debt without any connection to this particular wine does not create sufficient motivation for him to mishandle it.

### Rav Ashi: Contract Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דִּתְנַן: הַמְטַהֵר יֵינוֹ שֶׁל נׇכְרִי וְנוֹתְנוֹ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ, וְהַלָּה כּוֹתֵב לוֹ: ״הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי מִמְּךָ מָעוֹת״ — מוּתָּר, אֲבָל אִם יִרְצֶה יִשְׂרָאֵל לְהוֹצִיאוֹ וְאֵין מַנִּיחוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן לוֹ מְעוֹתָיו, זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבֵית שְׁאָן וְאָסְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The wording of **the mishna is also precise** according to Shmuel's interpretation, **as we learned** in the following mishna (61a): In the case of a Jew **who renders the wine of a gentile permitted** by treading the gentile's grapes so that the wine can be sold to Jews, **and he** then **places** the wine **in** the gentile's **domain** until he sells it, the *halakha* depends on the circumstances. If **that one,** the gentile, **writes for** the Jew: **I received money from you** in payment for the wine, even though he did not yet receive the actual payment, the wine is **permitted.** This is because the wine is considered the Jew's property and there is no reason to suppose that the gentile might touch it. **But** in a case **where the Jew desires to remove** the wine **and** the gentile **does not allow him** to do so **until** the Jew **gives him the money** due to **him, this was an incident** that occurred **in Beit She'an and** the Sages **deemed** the wine **prohibited.**

Rav Ashi brings a proof for Shmuel's reading from the following *mishnah* in the same *perek*. That *mishnah* addresses a case of *ha-metaher yeino shel ovdei kochavim* — a Jew who undertakes to produce kosher wine using a non-Jew's grapes and facilities. The arrangement is that the non-Jew writes a *shtar* — a document — saying *hitkabalti mimcha ma'ot*, that he has already received payment, even before he actually has. Once such a document is written, the wine is permissible, because the non-Jew no longer thinks of the wine as having any claim on it.

However, if after writing the *hitkabalti*, the non-Jew refuses to let the Jew retrieve the wine until actual payment is made — *ve-ein menicho ad she-yiten lo ma'ot* — the *mishnah* rules that such a case occurred in Beit She'an and the wine was prohibited. The *ta'am* is: *ta'ama de-ein menicho* — the reason the wine was prohibited in that case is precisely because he would not release the wine. This shows he still regarded the wine as his collateral. Conversely, *ha menicho — shari*: had he permitted the Jew to take the wine freely, it would have been permissible. Rav Ashi concludes: *shema mina milveh al oto yayin ba'inan* — this proves that Shmuel's specification is correct: the concern applies only when the debt is specifically tied to that wine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּאֵין מַנִּיחוֹ, הָא מַנִּיחוֹ שְׁרֵי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מִלְוָה עַל אוֹתוֹ יַיִן בָּעֵינַן. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi explains: **The reason** that the wine is prohibited is **that** the gentile **does not allow** the Jew to remove the wine, and therefore the gentile is considered to have some degree of ownership of the wine. **Therefore,** one can infer that if the gentile **allows him** to remove the wine, the wine is **permitted,** even though the Jew still owes him money. One may **conclude from** the mishna that in order for the wine to be prohibited **we require** the **loan** to include the qualification that the gentile has a lien **on that wine.** The Gemara affirms: One **may conclude** Shmuel's principle **from** the mishna.

## Four Cases & Rabi Shimon

### Fell & Drowned Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לַבּוֹר וְעָלָה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁעָלָה מֵת, אֲבָל עָלָה חַי — אָסוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּדָמֵי עֲלֵיהּ כְּיוֹם אֵידָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a gentile **fell into the** wine collection **vat and emerged,** it is not prohibited to derive benefit from the wine. **Rav Pappa says:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where** the gentile **emerged** from the vat **dead. But** if he **emerged alive,** the wine is **prohibited.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the wine is prohibited? **Rav Pappa said: Since** the gentile was rescued from death, **he considers** that day **like their festival day,** and he offers the wine as an idolatrous libation in thanksgiving.

Returning to the case of the non-Jew who fell into the wine pit, Rav Papa rules: *lo shanu ella she-ala met* — the *mishnah*'s leniency applies only if the non-Jew drowned in the wine and was removed dead. *Aval ala chai* — if he emerged alive, the wine is prohibited.

### Yom Eidam Analogy

Rav Papa explains: *de-dami alei ke-yom eidam* — when a person's life was in danger and he survived, the experience is to him like a *yom eidam*, a celebratory occasion. Just as we are concerned about increased idolatrous fervor on the days that are holidays for non-Jews, so too a person who narrowly escaped death will feel intense gratitude and will likely want to offer a libation to his idol. That spontaneous religious impulse is sufficient reason to prohibit the wine.

### Rabbanan: Prohibit, Sell

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְדָדוֹ בְּקָנֶה וְכוּ׳. כׇּל אֵלּוּ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה וְאָמְרוּ: יִמָּכֵר, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a gentile **measured** the wine in the winepress **with a pole,** or if he cast a hornet out of the wine by means of a pole, or if he removed the foam on top of a fermenting barrel of wine, with regard to **all these** cases **there was** such **an incident, and** the Sages **said** that the wine **may be sold** to gentiles but one may not drink it. **And Rabbi Shimon deems** the wine **permitted** even for drinking.

The remaining cases in the *mishnah* — measuring with a rod, removing a wasp, wiping foam from the barrel — all represent situations where the non-Jew interacted with the wine in some way but did not directly handle it. The anonymous *tanna'im*, the *rabbanan*, rule that in such cases the wine is prohibited for Jewish consumption but may be sold.

### Rabbi Shimon Permits All

Rabbi Shimon takes a different position altogether: *matir* — he permits the wine in all of these cases, apparently even to drink. His view is that these incidental and indirect contacts do not rise to the level of prohibited handling.

### Rav Adda Praises

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: יָנוּחוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרָכוֹת עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, כְּשֶׁהוּא מַתִּיר — מַתִּיר אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁתִיָּה, וּכְשֶׁהוּא אוֹסֵר — אוֹסֵר אֲפִילּוּ בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: May blessings rest upon Rabbi Shimon's head,** as his reasoning is clear. **When he deems** the wine **permitted, he deems** it **permitted even with regard to drinking, and when he deems** the wine **prohibited, he deems** it **prohibited even with regard to** deriving **benefit** from it.

Rav Adda bar Ahava praises Rabbi Shimon's consistency: *yanuchu lo le-Rabbi Shimon brachot al rosho* — may blessings rest upon Rabbi Shimon's head. The reason for the praise: *ke-shehu matir matir afilu bi-shtiyah* — when he permits, he permits entirely, even for drinking; *u-che-shehu osser osser afilu be-hana'ah* — and when he prohibits, he prohibits entirely, even for any benefit. Rabbi Shimon has only two categories, with no ambiguous intermediate zone. Either the wine is fully permitted or it is fully prohibited, and he does not create the uncertain middle ground of permissible for sale but not for drinking.

There is a note by the *Rashash* that suggests the formula of blessing would only be appropriate if Rabbi Shimon were still alive, and therefore proposes that there were two scholars named Rav Adda bar Ahava. This is an interesting suggestion, though it may be questioned — not least because belief in *Olam Haba* means that *brachot al rosho* need not be limited to the living. The *Rashash*, it should be said, is frequently cited for his original and incisive observations, and the story is told that Rav Shlomo Zalman Auerbach, upon hearing someone suggest that the *Rashash*'s commentary was printed in the Vilna *Shas* only because his father-in-law was wealthy enough to pay for it, responded that he too would have happily paid to have the *Rashash* included.

### Halakha Not Like

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּאַבָּא בַּר נַחְמָנִי, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר זְעֵירִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲמַר לִי אַבָּא בַּר חָנָן, הָכִי אָמַר זְעֵירִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְאֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya, son of Abba bar Naḥmani, says** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav says, and some say** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Ze'eiri says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. There are** those **who say** that **Rav Ḥisda says: Abba bar Ḥanan said to me: This** is what **Ze'eiri says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** Despite this, the Gemara concludes: **But** the ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.**

Despite the enthusiasm of Rav Adda bar Ahava and the traditions transmitted in the names of Rav Chisda, Zeiri, and others that *halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon* — the *gemara* concludes: *ve-ein halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon*. The final ruling does not follow Rabbi Shimon. The *rabbanan*'s intermediate position, prohibiting the wine for drinking while permitting its sale, stands. In general, Rabbi Shimon's rulings are followed only in specific cases; when he disagrees with Rabbi Yehuda, for instance, the *halacha* typically follows Rabbi Yehuda rather than Rabbi Shimon.

## Angry Throwing Case

### Zav Contact Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כׇּל שֶׁבַּזָּב טָמֵא בְּגוֹי עוֹשֶׂה יֵין נֶסֶךְ, כָּל שֶׁבַּזָּב טָהוֹר בְּגוֹי אֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה יֵין נֶסֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ashi says:** With regard to **any** form of contact **through which a *zav*** renders an object **ritually impure, in** a case where **a gentile** has that same type of contact with wine, he **renders** it **wine** used for **a libation.** In the case of **any** form of contact **through which a *zav*** does not transmit ritual impurity, leaving an object **ritually pure, a gentile does not render** the wine with which he has contact **wine** used for **a libation.**

Rav Ashi articulates a general principle: *kol she-be-zav tamei be-oved kochavim oseh yayin nesech* — whatever form of contact would render something *tamei* when performed by a *zav* will, when performed by a non-Jew, render wine *yayin nesech*. Conversely, *kol she-be-zav tahor be-oved kochavim eino oseh yayin nesech* — whatever form of contact would not create *tumah* when done by a *zav* does not create *yayin nesech* when done by a non-Jew.

This comparison is not arbitrary. The Rabbis decreed that non-Jewish people carry the status of *tumah* equivalent to a *zav* or *zavah*, even though they do not carry *tumah* under Torah law. Given that the Rabbinic level of impurity assigned to a non-Jew parallels that of a *zav*, it is a coherent move for Rav Ashi to use the *zav*'s contact standards as the benchmark for determining what level of non-Jewish interaction with wine creates *yayin nesech*. The association between idolatry and *tumah* is itself ancient: the *mishnah* in Shabbat already teaches that idols themselves are *metamei ke-sheretz*, and *kitvei kodesh* are *metamei et ha-yadayim*, though for different reasons and at different levels.

### Rav Huna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: נָטַל אֶת הֶחָבִית וּזְרָקָהּ בַּחֲמָתוֹ לַבּוֹר, זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבֵית שְׁאָן וְהִכְשִׁירוּ. בַּחֲמָתוֹ — אִין, שֶׁלֹּא בַּחֲמָתוֹ — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna raised an objection to Rav Ashi** from the mishna: With regard to the case where a gentile **took the barrel** of wine **and threw it, in his anger, into the** wine collection **vat, this was an incident** that occurred **in Beit She'an and** the Sages **deemed** the wine **fit** for drinking. One may infer that if the gentile threw the wine **in his anger, yes,** it is permitted. But if it was **not in his anger** the wine is **not** permitted, even though in the case of a *zav*, if he threw an object at a vessel, it does not render the vessel impure.

Rav Huna challenges Rav Ashi's principle by pointing to the very case in the *mishnah*: *natal ha-chavit ve-zarkah be-chamato la-bor ze hayah ma'aseh be-Veit She'an ve-hikhshiru* — the non-Jew picked up the barrel and threw it in anger, and the wine was ruled permissible. Rav Huna reads the word *be-chamato* — in his anger — as legally significant, not merely descriptive. The *mishnah* must be teaching: *be-chamato* — yes, when he threw it in anger, the wine is permissible; *she-lo be-chamato* — but had he thrown it calmly and deliberately, the wine would have been prohibited. If so, it is the absence of *kavanah* that makes the case permissible — and Rav Ashi's blanket principle, which ties the analysis purely to the *zav* contact standard without any consideration of intent, cannot stand.

### Rav Ashi Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, דְּקָאָזֵיל מִינֵּיהּ וּמִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi replied: **There,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the barrel is not actually thrown. Rather, **it is propelled by** the gentile, **and** again propelled **by him,** until it reaches the vat. Therefore, if the gentile did so not in anger, there is reason to be concerned that he might have touched the wine.

Rav Ashi responds by reinterpreting the *mishnah*. The word *zaraka* — threw — does not, he argues, describe a throw in the ordinary sense. Rather, Rashi explains: *she-holikh u-mitgalgel ha-kli kol sha'ah al yedei she-hu megalgelo u-morido la-bor* — the non-Jew was rolling the barrel to the edge and letting it tumble in. The wine only risked contact at the very moment when he lowered it into the pit, at the end of the roll, and the concern is that at that final point he may have touched the wine in the pit. *Hilkhakh she-lo be-chamato lo — de-haishinan dilma naga*: if he was not angry, we would be concerned that as he rolled it in he made actual contact with the wine. When he was angry, the act was more impulsive and less deliberate, reducing the likelihood of that final moment of contact.

### Amoraic Method

This exchange is itself a striking illustration of a broader Amoraic method. Rav Huna engages in what might be called *omni-significance*: the premise that every word in a *mishnah* carries independent legal weight. The word *be-chamato* is not merely coloring a story — it must be bearing a *halachic* distinction. From that premise, Rav Huna derives a rule that seemingly contradicts Rav Ashi's general principle, forcing Rav Ashi to stretch the language of *zaraka* to mean something other than what it plainly says — rolling rather than throwing.

What is notable is that the *mishnah* is recording an actual case that occurred: *zeh hayah ma'aseh ve-hikhshiru*. Yet the *amora'im*, working generations later, invest every detail of that narrative — including the emotional state of the actor — with binding legal significance, and then, having derived a rule on that basis, require the counter-position to reinterpret the very words of the *mishnah* in order to preserve a more general principle. It is a vivid example of how the *amora'im* engaged with *mishnayot* as texts of unambiguous legal density, where nothing is merely descriptive and every word bears legal consequence.
