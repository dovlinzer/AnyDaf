# Horayot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Hora'ah

### New Perek Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הוֹרָה כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ לְעַצְמוֹ שׁוֹגֵג וְעָשָׂה שׁוֹגֵג – מֵבִיא פַּר. שׁוֹגֵג וְעָשָׂה מֵזִיד, מֵזִיד וְעָשָׂה שׁוֹגֵג – פָּטוּר, שֶׁהוֹרָאַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ לְעַצְמוֹ כְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין לַצִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In a case where **an anointed priest,** i.e., the High Priest, **issued** an erroneous **ruling for himself** permitting performance of an action prohibited by Torah law, if he issued the ruling **unwittingly and** then **unwittingly performed** the transgression in accordance with his ruling, he is liable to **bring a bull** as a sin-offering for an unwitting transgression by the anointed priest. If he issued the ruling **unwittingly, and performed** the transgression **intentionally,** or if he issued the ruling **intentionally and performed** the transgression **unwittingly,** he is **exempt** from liability to bring any offering, **as** there is a principle: The legal status of **the ruling of an anointed priest for himself is like** that of **the ruling of the court for the** general **public.** Therefore, the High Priest is liable to bring the bull as a sin-offering for his unwitting transgression in a case when the court would be liable to bring the bull as a communal sin-offering for an unwitting transgression performed by the general public.

With this daf we enter a new *perek* whose subject is the *korban* obligation of the *kohen gadol* — an obligation that parallels, in its formal structure, that of the *beit din*: it requires both a *hora'ah* and a *ma'aseh*. The *kohen gadol* must *pasken* and then act on his own ruling. This is admittedly a peculiar kind of *psak*, since the *kohen gadol* is not, in the ordinary sense, a body constituted for issuing legal decisions, and his ruling operates only with respect to himself — yet that is precisely what the *pesukim* teach.

### Mezid & Shogeg

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹגֵג וְעָשָׂה שׁוֹגֵג – מֵבִיא פַּר. פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: If he issued the ruling **unwittingly and** then **unwittingly performed** the transgression in accordance with his ruling, he is liable to **bring a bull.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** This is certainly a case of an unwitting transgression, for which the Torah deems him liable to bring an offering.

The Gemara picks up at the very bottom of 6b with the phrase *mezid ve'asa shogeg* — if his *psak* was *bemezid* and his action was *beshogeg* (that is, if he rendered a deliberate ruling but sinned inadvertently without a *psak*), he does not bring any *korban* at all. The *kohen gadol* has no separate *korban* for the *psak* alone and another for the ordinary inadvertent act. The only *korban* he brings is where the *psak* and the action are united. The Gemara therefore asks *minalan mili* — from where do we derive all of this?

## Le'ashmat Ha'am

### Source Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵזִיד וְעָשָׂה שׁוֹגֵג כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לְאַשְׁמַת הָעָם״, הֲרֵי מָשִׁיחַ כְּצִבּוּר. שֶׁיָּכוֹל, וַהֲלֹא דִין הוּא:
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If he issued the ruling unwittingly, and performed the transgression intentionally, or if he issued the ruling **intentionally and performed** the transgression **unwittingly,** he is exempt. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** the *halakhot* unique to the sin-offering for an unwitting transgression by the anointed priest, derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is written: "If the anointed priest shall sin **so as to bring guilt upon the people"** (Leviticus 4:3), from which it is derived that the liability of the **anointed** priest **is** incurred **like** that of the general **public.** The Gemara discusses this derivation. **As** one **might** have thought that the verse is superfluous: **Could this not** be derived through **logical inference?**

The Gemara cites a *braita*: the derivation begins with the phrase *le'ashmat ha'am* — "for the guilt of the congregation" — appearing at the end of *Parshat Vayikra* in connection with the *kohen gadol*'s sin: *im hakohen hamashiach yecheta le'ashmat ha'am*. From this the rabbis derive: *harei mashiach ketzibur* — the *kohen mashiach* is analogous to the *tzibur*, specifically the case of *par he'elam davar* of the *tzibur* that the preceding *perek* has been discussing.

### Tzibur Par Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צִבּוּר מוּצָא מִכְּלַל יָחִיד, וּמָשִׁיחַ מוּצָא מִכְּלַל יָחִיד. מָה צִבּוּר אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה, אַף מָשִׁיחַ לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The general **public is removed from the category of an individual,** as an individual brings a ewe or female goat as a sin-offering, whereas when the general public sins the sin-offering is a bull. **And** likewise, **an anointed** priest **is removed from the category of an individual,** as his sin-offering is also a bull. Therefore, **just as** the general **public is liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter** by the court together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** by the people, **so too, an anointed** priest **will be liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter** together **with unwitting** performance **of an action.**

But before accepting this equation, the *braita* sets out why one might have arrived at it independently, and why one equally might not have. On one hand: *tzibur mutza miklal yachid, umashiach mutza miklal yachid* — both the *tzibur* and the *kohen mashiach* bring a *par*, an ox, which is different from the standard *chatat* of an ordinary individual (who brings a female goat or female lamb). Since both bring an ox, it seems natural to place them in the same halachic category — and since the *par he'elam davar* of the *tzibur* requires a *psak* followed by an action, one might have concluded the same for the *kohen gadol* without needing a special *pasuk*.

### Yachid Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נָשִׂיא מוּצָא מִכְּלַל יָחִיד, וּמָשִׁיחַ מוּצָא מִכְּלַל יָחִיד. מָה נָשִׂיא מֵבִיא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה בְּלֹא הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר, אַף מָשִׁיחַ מֵבִיא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה בְּלֹא הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר!
>
> **Translation:** **Or** perhaps **go this way** and draw a different analogy: **A king [*Nasi*] is removed from the category of an individual,** as his sin-offering is a goat. **And an anointed** priest **is removed from the category of an individual.** Therefore, **just as a king brings** an offering **for unwitting** performance **of an action,** even **without absence** of awareness **of** the **matter** leading to an erroneous ruling, like any individual liable to bring a sin-offering, **so too, an anointed** priest **brings** an offering **for unwitting** performance **of an action,** even **without absence** of awareness **of** the **matter** leading to an erroneous ruling. It is possible to liken the anointed priest to either the general public or to the king.

Yet the *braita* immediately raises the counter-argument: *o khalach lederech zu* — go the other direction. *Nasi mutza miklal yachid, umashiach mutza miklal yachid*: both the *nasi* and the *kohen gadol* are similarly distinct from the ordinary individual, and both are distinct in the same direction. The *nasi* brings a *se'ir* — a male goat — rather than the standard female animal of a *yachid*; the *kohen gadol* brings an ox. So one might equally group the *kohen gadol* with the *nasi*. And since we know that the *nasi* brings his *korban* for a straightforward *shgagah* — there is no basis in the *pesukim* to require anything beyond an inadvertent action — perhaps *mah nasi mevi beshiggat ma'aseh, af mashiach mevi beshiggat ma'aseh*.

The Gemara also introduces a further consideration on the side of grouping the *kohen gadol* with the *tzibur*: both bring a *par* and neither brings an *asham talui*. An *asham talui* is the contingent guilt offering brought when one is uncertain whether a sin was committed. Before establishing why the *kohen gadol* does not bring one (to which we will return shortly), the *braita* notes this as a point of similarity with the *tzibur*. On the other side, the *kohen gadol* and the *nasi* are similarly grouped: both bring a *seirah* — a female goat — for a sin of *avoda zara*, as the individual-category in *Parshat Shelach Lecha* governs both of them, and both bring an *asham vadai* (a definite guilt offering, such as in cases of *me'ilah* or theft with an oath) — which the *tzibur* does not bring, since those cases, as Rashi points out, involve *mezid* and thus cannot be predicated on following a mistaken ruling.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה? צִבּוּר – בְּפַר וְאֵין מְבִיאִין אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, וּמָשִׁיחַ – בְּפַר וְאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי. מָה צִבּוּר אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה, אַף מָשִׁיחַ לֹא יְהֵא חַיָּיב אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה. אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נָשִׂיא מֵבִיא שְׂעִירָה בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וּמֵבִיא אָשָׁם וַדַּאי, וּמָשִׁיחַ מֵבִיא שְׂעִירָה בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וּמֵבִיא אָשָׁם וַדַּאי. מָה נָשִׂיא מֵבִיא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה, אַף מָשִׁיחַ מֵבִיא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְאַשְׁמַת הָעָם״, הֲרֵי הוּא מָשִׁיחַ כְּצִבּוּר. מָה צִבּוּר אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה, אַף מָשִׁיחַ אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers these two comparisons: **Let us see to which** of them, the general public or the king, an anointed priest **is similar.** The general **public** achieves atonement **with a bull and does not bring a provisional guilt-offering, and an anointed** priest achieves atonement **with a bull and does not bring a provisional guilt-offering.** Therefore one might say: **Just as** the general **public is liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter** by the court together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** by the people, **so too, an anointed** priest **will be liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter** together **with unwitting** performance **of an action.** **Or** perhaps **go this way** and draw a different analogy: **The king brings a female goat for** unwitting **idol worship and brings a definite guilt-offering** for certain other transgressions where there is liability to bring a guilt-offering, e.g., misuse of consecrated property and robbery, **and an anointed** priest **brings a female goat for** unwitting **idol worship and brings a definite guilt-offering** for the same transgressions as the king. This is in contrast to the general public, which brings a bull as a sin-offering for unwitting idol worship and does not bring a definite guilt-offering at all. Therefore conclude: **Just as a king brings** an offering **for unwitting** performance **of an action** without absence of awareness of the matter, like any individual liable to bring a sin-offering, **so too, an anointed** priest **brings** an offering **for unwitting** performance **of an action** without absence of awareness of the matter. Since either conclusion can be derived logically, another source is necessary. Therefore, **the verse states:** "If the anointed priest shall sin **so as to bring guilt upon the people"** (Leviticus 4:3), indicating that the status of the **anointed** priest **is like** that of the general **public.** Therefore, **just as** the general **public brings** an offering **only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter** by the court **with unwitting** performance **of an action** by the people, **so too, an anointed** priest **brings** an offering **only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter with unwitting** performance **of an action.**

One could therefore have placed the *kohen gadol* in either category with equal logical force. *Nireh lemi domeh* — to which is he more similar? Without a definitive *pasuk*, there is no way to resolve it. Therefore, *talmud lomar le'ashmat ha'am*: the Torah specifically uses the phrase "for the guilt of the people," connecting the *kohen mashiach* to the sin of the *tzibur* — *hareihu mashiach ketzibur*. Just as the *tzibur* is only liable through *he'elam davar* combined with *shiggas ma'aseh*, so too the *kohen gadol* is only liable through a *psak* combined with an action.

### Own Sin Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: מָה צִבּוּר הוֹרָה וְעָשׂוּ אַחֲרָיו בְּהוֹרָאָתוֹ – חַיָּיבִין, אַף מָשִׁיחַ כְּשֶׁהוֹרָה וְעָשׂוּ אַחֲרָיו בְּהוֹרָאָתוֹ – יְהֵא חַיָּיב! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהִקְרִיב עַל חַטָּאתוֹ אֲשֶׁר חָטָא״, עַל מָה שֶׁחָטָא הוּא מֵבִיא, וְאֵין מֵבִיא עַל מָה שֶׁחָטְאוּ אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the comparison: Based on the comparison between the anointed priest and the general public, why not **say: Just as** with regard to the general **public,** if the court **issued a ruling and** the general public **performed** the transgression **after its** ruling and **in accordance with its ruling,** the court is **liable, so too,** with regard to **an anointed** priest, **when he issued a ruling and** the general public **performed** the transgression **after his** ruling and **in accordance with his ruling, he should be liable.** Therefore, **the verse states** with regard to the anointed priest: **"Then he shall offer for his sin that he has sinned"** (Leviticus 4:3), from which it is derived: **He brings** an offering **for that** sin **that he sinned** on the basis of his ruling, **but he does not bring** an offering **for that** sin **that others sinned** on the basis of his ruling.

The *braita* continues with a further question. If the *kohen gadol* is truly equated with the *tzibur*, perhaps the analogy should extend to the mechanics of the *tzibur*'s *korban* as well: by the *tzibur*, the *beit din* rules and *other people* act on that ruling. Perhaps then the *kohen gadol* rules and others follow his ruling and act — and that would be the basis for his obligation. The *braita* rejects this: *talmud lomar al chattato asher chata* — "for his sin which he has sinned" — he brings for the sin he himself committed, not for what others did. He is therefore both the one who ruled and the one who acted.

## Circular Reasoning

### Asham Talui Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: מָשִׁיחַ בְּפַר וְאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי. מְנָא לֵיהּ דְּאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו הַכֹּהֵן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר שָׁגָג״, מִי שֶׁחַטָּאתוֹ וְשִׁגְגָתוֹ שָׁוָה, יָצָא מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁאֵין שִׁגְגָתוֹ וְחַטָּאתוֹ שָׁוָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְאַשְׁמַת הָעָם״, הֲרֵי הוּא מָשִׁיחַ כְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on that which **the Master said: An anointed** priest achieves atonement **with a bull and does not bring a provisional guilt-offering.** The Gemara asks: **From where does** the *tanna* derive **that** an anointed priest **does not bring a provisional guilt-offering?** The Gemara answers: It is derived from a verse, **as it is written** with regard to the guilt-offering: **"And the priest shall atone for him for his unwitting act that he performed unwittingly"** (Leviticus 5:18), from which it is derived that this *halakha* applies only to **one whose transgression and his unwitting** action **are equal,** i.e., an ordinary person, whose unwitting act is the very transgression that he performed unwittingly. This serves to **exclude an anointed** priest, **whose unwitting** action **and his transgression are not equal,** as his unwitting act is the erroneous ruling and he is liable to bring an offering only if he performed the transgression on the basis of that ruling. **As it is written** about the anointed priest: **"So as to bring guilt upon the people"** (Leviticus 4:3), indicating that the status of the **anointed** priest **is like** that of the general **public.**

The Gemara now turns to a difficulty within the *braita* itself. The *braita* had argued that one reason to group the *kohen gadol* with the *tzibur* (rather than the *nasi*) is that both the *kohen gadol* and the *tzibur* do not bring an *asham talui*. The Gemara asks: *minalan de-ein meivi asham talui*? How do we know the *kohen gadol* does not bring an *asham talui*?

The answer offered is: the *pasuk* by *asham talui* reads *ve-chiper alav ha-kohen al shigegato asher shagag* — "and the *kohen* shall atone for him for his error that he erred." The doubling of the language — *shigegato asher shagag* — teaches that an *asham talui* applies only to *mi she-chattato ve-shigegato shaveh*: someone for whom the sin and the inadvertence are entirely one and the same — meaning someone who simply acted inadvertently, without any separate component such as a *hora'ah*. This excludes the *kohen gadol*, whose *chiyuv* is not based solely on the *shgaga* of the act but also on the *psak* that preceded it. (The same logic applies to the *beit din* as well.)

### Circularity Identified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְאַשְׁמַת הָעָם״, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר לֵיהּ. אֶלָּא אָשָׁם כְּדִי נַסְבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this proof: How can the Gemara base the *halakha* on an interpretation of the verse: **"So as to bring guilt upon the people"? To this point,** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **did not state** this verse. The *tanna* first states that the anointed priest does not bring a provisional guilt-offering and only then cites the verse from which he proves the *halakha* that an anointed priest brings an offering only for absence of awareness of the matter together with unwitting performance of an action. **Rather,** he stated the *halakha* of the provisional guilt-offering **for no reason.** Although the *halakha* is correct, there was no reason to cite it in the *baraita*.

The Gemara, however, identifies a circularity: the reason for excluding the *kohen gadol* from *asham talui* is that he requires a *hora'ah* in addition to action — but we know he requires a *hora'ah* only because of the verse *le'ashmat ha'am*, which equates him to the *tzibur*. And the reason offered in the *braita* for making that equation in the first place (rather than equating him with the *nasi*) included the fact that neither the *kohen gadol* nor the *tzibur* brings an *asham talui*. But that point presupposes the very conclusion we are trying to establish. Until we have derived from *le'ashmat ha'am* that the *kohen gadol* requires a *hora'ah*, we have no basis to claim he does not bring an *asham talui*. The argument is therefore circular.

### Asham Side Point

The Gemara concedes the point: *ela asham kedei nesavah* — the mention of *asham talui* as a point of comparison was simply thrown in. It is not part of the logical argument that determined the categorization. The only legitimate basis for placing the *kohen gadol* in the *tzibur* category, absent the verse, would have been the shared *par*. And once *le'ashmat ha'am* teaches us the equation with the *tzibur*, it follows as a corollary that the *kohen gadol* similarly does not bring an *asham talui* — for the same reason the *tzibur* does not.

## Alone vs Tzibur

### Separate Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הוֹרָה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וְעָשָׂה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ. הוֹרָה עִם הַצִּבּוּר וְעָשָׂה עִם הַצִּבּוּר – מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר, שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין חַיָּיבִין עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ לְבַטֵּל מִקְצָת וּלְקַיֵּים מִקְצָת, וְכֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ, וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ לְבַטֵּל מִקְצָת וּלְקַיֵּים מִקְצָת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the anointed priest **issued a ruling by himself and performed** a transgression **by himself, he achieves atonement by himself** by bringing a bull as his sin-offering. If **he issued a ruling with the** general **public,** i.e., the Sanhedrin, **and performed** a transgression **with the** general **public,** i.e., the Jewish people, **he achieves atonement with the** general **public. As, the court is not liable unless** the judges **issue a ruling to nullify part** of a commandment **and to sustain part** of that commandment, **and likewise** with regard to the ruling of **the anointed** priest. **And** the court and the priest are **not** liable for a ruling **with regard to idol worship unless they issue a ruling to nullify part** of that commandment **and to sustain part** of it.

The next *mishnah* states: *horah bifnei atzmo ve'asah bifnei atzmo, mitkaper lo bifnei atzmo* — if the *kohen gadol* ruled for himself and acted for himself, he brings his own *korban*. This is the obvious case: he independently reached a mistaken conclusion and acted on it, and is therefore liable for his own individual *korban*.

### With Tzibur

*Horah im hatzibur ve'asah im hatzibur, mitkaper lo im hatzibur* — if he ruled together with the *tzibur* and acted together with the *tzibur*, he is included in their *korban*. The simple reading of this would be that when the *beit din hagadol* is issuing a ruling and the *kohen gadol* is part of that process, or is independently converging on the same ruling, and the action follows — he is functioning as a member of the *am* and is covered by the *korban* of the *beit din*. No separate *korban* is required for him. As we will see, however, the Gemara will push this to considerably more radical territory in understanding what *im hatzibur* means.

## Im Hatzibur

### Source Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: הוֹרָה עִם הַצִּבּוּר וְעָשָׂה עִם הַצִּבּוּר, יָכוֹל יָבִיא פַּר לְעַצְמוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Concerning the *halakha* that there is a difference between an unwitting transgression that the anointed priest performs by himself and one that he performs with the general public, the Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived? The Gemara explains: It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the anointed priest **issued a ruling with the** general **public and performed** a transgression **with the** general **public,** one **might** have thought that he is liable to **bring a bull** as a sin-offering **for himself.**

The Gemara asks *mana hanei milei* — what is the source for the rule that when the *kohen gadol* acts together with the *tzibur*, he is included in their *korban*? A *braita* is cited: *horah im hatzibur ve'asah im hatzibur — yachol yavi par le'atzmo*: one might have thought that since the *kohen gadol* has his own special *korban*, he should still bring his own *par* even when he sinned together with the *tzibur*.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא: נָשִׂיא מוּצָא מִכְּלַל יָחִיד וּמָשִׁיחַ מוּצָא מִכְּלַל יָחִיד, מָה נָשִׂיא, חָטָא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – מֵבִיא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, חָטָא עִם הַצִּבּוּר – מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר. אַף מָשִׁיחַ, חָטָא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – מֵבִיא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, חָטָא עִם הַצִּבּוּר – מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** there **is a logical inference** to support this: **A king is removed from the category of an individual and an anointed** priest **is removed from the category of an individual,** as each brings a different sin-offering than an individual. **Just as** with regard to **a king,** if **he sinned by himself he brings** his sin-offering of a goat **by himself,** and if **he sinned with the** general **public, he achieves atonement with the** general **public, so too,** with regard to **an anointed** priest, if **he sinned by himself he brings** his sin-offering of a bull **by himself,** and if **he sinned with the** general **public, he achieves atonement with the** general **public.**

The *braita* sets out a *kal vachomer* via the *nasi*: both the *nasi* and the *kohen gadol* are distinct from the ordinary *yachid* (*nasi mutza miklal yachid, umashiach mutza miklal yachid*) — neither brings the standard individual *chatat*. Now, by the *nasi*, there is no concept of *hora'ah* — he simply sins, and if he sins along with the *tzibur* (following the ruling of the *beit din*), he is plainly covered by the *tzibur*'s *korban*. One would therefore argue: just as the *nasi* who sins *bifnei atzmo* brings *bifnei atzmo*, and if he sins *im hatzibur* is atoned for *im hatzibur* — so too the *kohen gadol*.

### Yom Kippur Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּנָשִׂיא – שֶׁכֵּן מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים. תֹּאמַר בְּמָשִׁיחַ – שֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* rejects this: **No, if you said with regard to a king** that he achieves atonement with the general public, that is logical, **as he achieves atonement with the** general **public on Yom Kippur. Shall you** also **say** the same **with regard to an anointed** priest, **who does not achieve atonement with the** general **public on Yom Kippur** but rather brings his own atonement offering?

The *braita* immediately pushes back: one cannot simply transfer the logic of *nasi* to *kohen gadol* in this direction, because the *nasi* has independent evidence that he belongs within the communal framework — on *Yom Kippur*, the blood of the *se'ir* sprinkled inside atones for the entire *tzibur*, including the *nasi*. This shows that the *nasi* is inherently part of the congregational atonement structure. By contrast, the *kohen gadol* is never included in such a communal *kapparah* — on *Yom Kippur*, the *par* is specifically for him and the *kohanim*, separate from the *tzibur*. Therefore it is not logically obvious that when he sins together with the *tzibur* he should be covered by their *korban*.

### Al Chatasoy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹאִיל וְאֵין מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, יָכוֹל יָבִיא פַּר לְעַצְמוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַל חַטָּאתוֹ אֲשֶׁר חָטָא״, הָא כֵּיצַד? חָטָא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ – מֵבִיא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, חָטָא עִם הַצִּבּוּר – מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ עִם הַצִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Since he does not achieve atonement with the** general **public on Yom Kippur,** one **might** have thought that **he will bring a bull for himself** even if he unwittingly performed a transgression with the general public. Therefore, **the verse states:** "Then he shall offer **for his sin that he has sinned"** (Leviticus 4:3), indicating that he sinned alone, not with the general public. **How so?** If **he sinned by himself he brings** his sin-offering of a bull **by himself;** if **he sinned with the** general **public, he achieves atonement with the** general **public.**

Since the question cannot be resolved by analogy alone, the *braita* derives it from a *pasuk*: *talmud lomar al chataso asher chata* — "for his sin which he has sinned." When the sin is uniquely and exclusively his, he brings his special *korban*; when it is not exclusively his — when he is part of the *tzibur*'s sin — he is not in his own box but in theirs, and is atoned for by the *tzibur*'s *korban*. *Ha keitzad*: *chata bifnei atzmo, mevi bifnei atzmo; chata im hatzibur, mitkaper lo im hatzibur*.

This raises the question: what exactly defines *im hatzibur*? The Gemara embarks on a careful analysis. The first scenario to consider would be one in which he is a *mufla* — an expert fit for *hora'ah* — while they are not. But this is immediately dismissed: *peshita demistakbel bifnei atzmo, hora'ah dilehon lav klum hi* — obviously in that case there is no valid *psak* on the part of the *beit din* at all, and no one brings the communal *korban*; all individuals, including the *kohen gadol*, bring as *yechidim*. The inverse — that the *beit din* is fully qualified but the *kohen gadol* is not *ra'uy le'hora'ah* — is equally problematic: *hora'ah dideih lav klum hi*, his *psak* is not a valid *psak*, and he will never have a case of bringing *bifnei atzmo* at all.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּהוּא מוּפְלָא וְהֵם אֵינָן מוּפְלָאִין, פְּשִׁיטָא דְּמִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, הוֹרָאָה דִּלְהוֹן וְלֹא כְּלוּם. וּבָעֵי אֵתוֹיֵי כִּשְׂבָּה אוֹ שְׂעִירָה כֹּל חַד וְחַד! וְאִי דְּאִינּוּן מוּפְלָאִין וְהוּא לָאו מוּפְלָא, אַמַּאי מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ? הָא הוֹרָאָה דִּידֵיהּ וְלֹא כְּלוּם הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What are the circumstances** of an anointed priest issuing a ruling for himself? **If we say** that it is a case **where** the High Priest **is a distinguished** scholar **and** the judges of the court **are not distinguished** scholars, it is **obvious that he achieves atonement by himself,** as **their ruling is nothing** at all, since they did not consult the generation's most prominent scholar. **And** accordingly, **each and every one** who performed a transgression **needs to bring a ewe or female goat** as an individual sin-offering. **And** if it is a case **where** the judges **are distinguished** scholars **and he is not a distinguished** scholar, **why does he achieve atonement by himself? Isn't his ruling nothing at all,** and his transgression is an unwitting performance of an action alone, rather than a transgression performed on the basis of a ruling?

At this point the Gemara notes an interesting digression from the *Rosh*, who remarks that the word *mufla* as used here requires elucidation. Normally *mufla* should mean one who is *ra'uy le'hora'ah* — *gamir vesavir*, thoroughly learned and analytically capable, as is required for membership on the *beit din hagadol*. But the *Rosh* observes that the Talmud implies it is possible to seat members of the *sanhedrin* who are not fully *ra'uy le'hora'ah* if there are not enough such individuals available — and those who truly are *ra'uy le'hora'ah* would be specifically designated *mufla'im*. This is a striking observation: that the *sanhedrin* of seventy-one could theoretically include members who had not yet attained the full qualifications for independent ruling.

### Both Mufla'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ מוּפְלִין שְׁנֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says:** The reference is to a case **where both** the anointed priest and the court, i.e., the judges, **were distinguished** Torah scholars with the authority to issue rulings.

The conclusion is stated by Rav Pappa: *kegon shehayu mufla'im sheneihem* — the operative scenario is that they are both expert, both the *beit din* and the *kohen gadol*. Both are issuing valid *psak*, and both are following their respective rulings. The question then becomes: when does the *kohen gadol*'s *psak* count as *bifnei atzmo*, and when is it *im hatzibur*?

### Two Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר אַבָּיֵי לְמֵימַר: חָטָא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ וְעָשָׂה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? דְּיָתְבִי בִּשְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת וְקָא מוֹרוּ בִּתְרֵי אִיסּוּרֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אַטּוּ שְׁנֵי מְקוֹמוֹת גּוֹרְמִין? אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ יָתְבִי בְּחַד מָקוֹם, וְכֵיוָן דְּקָא מוֹרוּ בִּתְרֵי אִיסּוּרֵי – חָטָא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye thought to say** that with regard to the cases in the mishna: If the anointed priest **sinned by himself and performed** a transgression **by himself, what are the circumstances?** It is a case **where** the priest and the court **were convened in two** different **places and issuing rulings with regard to two** different **prohibitions. Rava said to** Abaye: **Is that to say** that the fact that there are **two places determines** that he sinned by himself? **Rather,** it is **even** in a case where the High Priest and the court **are convened in one place. But since they are issuing rulings with regard to two** different **prohibitions, it is** a case where the anointed priest **sinned by himself.**

One might have supposed that the critical factor is geography: if they are sitting in two different locations and ruling on two different *issurim*, that constitutes *bifnei atzmo*.

### Rava's View

Rava rejects this: *atu shenei mekomot garmei* — geography is irrelevant. What matters is whether the rulings are connected, not where the parties happen to be sitting. Even if they occupy the same physical space, if they are ruling on different matters they are operating independently: *keivan daka morei bitrei issurei, chad bifnei atzmo hu* — since they are ruling on two different prohibitions, each stands alone.

### Obvious Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא: הוּא בְּחֵלֶב וְהֵן בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – חָטָא בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ הוּא, דְּהָא חֲלוּקִין בְּטַעְמַיְיהוּ, וַחֲלוּקִין בְּקׇרְבָּנוֹת. דְּהוּא בְּפַר, וְהֵן בְּפַר וְשָׂעִיר – דְּהָא קָא מַיְיתוּ הָנֵי שָׂעִיר, וְהוּא לָא מַיְיתֵי. וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן הוּא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְהֵן בְּחֵלֶב, דַּחֲלוּקִין בְּקׇרְבְּנוֹתֵיהֶן [לִגְמָרֵי] – דְּהוּא שְׂעִירָה, וְאִינְהוּ פַּר,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: It is **obvious** that in a case where the anointed priest issued a ruling **with regard to** forbidden **fat and** the judges issued a ruling **with regard to idol worship, it is** a case where the anointed priest **sinned by himself. As** in this case the rulings **are distinct in** terms of **their reasons,** as each ruling is based on a different Torah source, **and they are** also **distinct in** terms of **their offerings, since** the priest achieves atonement **with a bull** as a sin-offering, **and** the judges achieve atonement **with a bull and a goat** as a sin-offering for unwitting idol worship, **as these** judges **bring a goat and** the priest **does not bring** a goat. **And all the more so** in a case where the anointed priest issued a ruling **with regard to idol worship and** the judges issued a ruling **with regard to** forbidden **fat, it is** a case where the anointed priest sinned by himself, **as** these rulings **are totally distinct in** terms of **their offerings, since** the priest brings **a female goat** as a sin-offering **and** the judges bring **a bull.**

The Gemara then works through the obvious cases. If he is *paskening* about *chelev* and they are *paskening* about *avoda zara*: clearly he is *bifnei atzmo* — the *issurim* are different, the reasons are different, and the *korbanot* diverge entirely. He would bring a *par*, whereas they bring a *par* for an *olah* and a *se'ir* for a *chatat* in the *avoda zara* framework — the structures are incommensurable. And certainly *ve'chol shekein* if the positions are reversed — he *paskening* about *avoda zara* (bringing a *seirah*) and they about *chelev* (bringing a *par*) — there the *korbanot* are completely separate and there is no basis for combination whatsoever.

### Nuanced Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הוּא בְּחֵלֶב הַמְכַסֶּה אֶת הַקֶּרֶב, וְהֵן בְּחֵלֶב שֶׁעַל הַדַּקִּין, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: אַף עַל גַּב דְּקׇרְבָּנָן שָׁוֶה, כֵּיוָן דְּמִתְּרֵי קְרָאֵי קָאָתוּ, הָא פְּלִיגִין בְּטַעְמַיְיהוּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא שֵׁם חֵלֶב אֶחָד הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **But** in a case where the anointed priest issued a ruling **with regard to** forbidden **fat that covers the innards,** for example, **and** the judges issued a ruling **with regard to** forbidden **fat that is on the small intestines, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say: Although their offerings are equal,** nevertheless, **since it is from two** different **verses** that the prohibitions **come,** the rulings **are distinct in** terms of **their reasons? Or perhaps, the category of** forbidden **fat is one** designation, and all types of forbidden fat are considered one transgression.

The more interesting case: *hu be-chelev hamichaseh es hakerev ve-hein be-chelev she'al hadakin* — he is ruling about the *chelev* that covers the inner organs, and they are ruling about the *chelev* on the intestines. Both are ruling about *chelev*, and both would bring a *par*. What is the rule here? One might argue that since the two categories of *chelev* are derived from different *pesukim* — the *vav* of *ve-es hachelev* coming to include the *chelev al hadakin* — the halachic arguments are perforce distinct. The logical underpinnings of the two *pesakim* have nothing to do with each other, and therefore he cannot be said to have been following their *psak*.

### Chalev Unity Q

Or perhaps, the Gemara entertains, since it is all subsumed under *shem chelev echad* — the single name and category of *chelev* — he would be considered as having acted together with them. This is admittedly difficult to understand intuitively: he ruled about one form of *chelev*, they ruled about another, and he ate his form of *chelev* based entirely on his own independent *psak* that had nothing to do with theirs. Yet the Gemara entertains that the shared rubric of *chelev* might be sufficient to bring him under their *korban*. The question is left open.

### Radical Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר שֵׁם חֵלֶב אֶחָד הוּא, הוּא בְּחֵלֶב וְהֵן בְּדָם, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: בְּטַעְמַיְיהוּ הָא פְּלִיגִין, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁוִין בְּקׇרְבָּן בָּתַר קׇרְבָּן אָזְלִינַן? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **If you say** that **the category of** forbidden **fat is one** designation, then in a case where the priest issues a ruling **with regard to** forbidden **fat and** the judges issue a ruling **with regard to blood, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say they are distinct in** terms of **their reasons,** as each prohibition has a different Torah source? **Or perhaps, since** forbidden **fat and blood are equal in** terms of the atonement **offering** that one is liable to bring for unwitting consumption, **we follow the offering,** so that the rulings of the priest and the judges are considered one ruling? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara then posits an even more extreme case: *hu be-chelev ve-hein be-dam* — he rules about *chelev* and they rule about *dam*. Now the *issurim* are entirely different. But both he and the *beit din* would bring a *par* — and the Gemara asks: *o dilma, keivan de-shavin be-korbanam*, perhaps the mere coincidence that the *korban* in each case is the same is enough for him to be included? *Teiku* — the Gemara leaves this unresolved. This is a striking conclusion: the Gemara is genuinely uncertain whether a *kohen gadol* who transgressed *chelev* could be atoned for by a *korban* brought by a *tzibur* that transgressed *dam*, on the grounds that in both cases a *par* would be the appropriate *korban*.

## Negate/Affirm Part

### Previous Perek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין חַיָּיבִין עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ לְבַטֵּל מִקְצָת וּלְקַיֵּים מִקְצָת וְכוּ׳. מְנָלַן דְּעַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ לְבַטֵּל מִקְצָת וּלְקַיֵּים מִקְצָת? כִּדְאָמְרִינַן בְּאִידַּךְ פִּירְקִין: ״וְנֶעֱלַם דָּבָר״ – ״דָּבָר״ – וְלֹא כָּל הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the *halakhot* stated in the mishna: **As the court is not liable unless** the judges **issue a ruling to nullify part** of a mitzva **and to sustain part** of that mitzva. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that** there is no liability **unless** the judges **issue a ruling to nullify part** of a mitzva **and to sustain part** of it? The Gemara answers: It is **as we say in the other chapter** of this tractate (3b): From the verse: **"And the matter is hidden"** (Leviticus 4:13), it is derived that there is liability if only **a matter,** a single detail, is hidden, **but not** if **the entire essence** of a mitzva is hidden.

The *mishnah* states that a *beit din* is only liable if they *pasken* in a manner that negates part of the *mitzvah* while affirming part of it. This was already established in the previous *perek* through the exegesis of *venelam davar* — "a matter was hidden," not the entirety of the body of law, implying that their ruling must address a discrete component while leaving the broader framework intact.

### Kohen Gadol Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״לְאַשְׁמַת הָעָם״, הֲרֵי מָשִׁיחַ כְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And likewise** with regard to the ruling of **the anointed** priest. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? It is derived from a verse, **as it is written:** "If the anointed priest shall sin **so as to bring guilt upon the people"** (Leviticus 4:3), indicating that the status of **an anointed** priest **is like** that of the general **public.**

*Vechen hamashiach* — the same applies to the *kohen gadol*. Since we have established through *le'ashmat ha'am* that the *kohen gadol* is equated to the *tzibur* in all the formal requirements of *hora'ah*, it follows that the same structure of partial negation and partial affirmation is required for his *psak* to generate a *chiyuv*.

### Avoda Zara Partial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה כּוּ׳. מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְפִי שֶׁיָּצְאָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָדוּן בְּעַצְמָהּ, יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין עַל עֲקִירַת מִצְוָה כּוּלָּהּ? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מֵעֵינֵי״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״מֵעֵינֵי״. מָה לְהַלָּן בְּבֵית דִּין, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי בְּבֵית דִּין. וּמָה לְהַלָּן ״דָּבָר״ וְלֹא כׇּל הַגּוּף, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי ״דָּבָר״ וְלֹא כׇּל הַגּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And** the court and the priest are liable for a ruling **with regard to idol worship only for absence** of awareness **of the matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of the action** on the basis of that ruling. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? It is derived **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Due to** the fact **that idol worship left** the category of unwitting transgressions **to be discussed by itself** (see Numbers, chapter 15), one **might** have thought **that** the court and the anointed priest **would be liable** even **for nullifying the mitzva in its entirety.** Therefore, the term **"from the eyes of" is stated here,** with regard to idol worship (Numbers 15:24), **and** the term **"from the eyes of" is stated there,** with regard to an unwitting communal sin-offering for all other mitzvot (Leviticus 4:13). **Just as there** the reference is to nullifying the mitzva **in court, so too here,** the reference is to nullifying the mitzva **in court. And just as there,** with regard to an unwitting communal sin-offering, the reference is to nullifying **a matter, but not the entire essence, so too here,** the reference is to nullifying **a matter, but not the entire essence.**

The *mishnah* then extends this to *avoda zara* as well: *velo ba'avoda zarah ad sheyoru levatel miktzat ulekayem miktzat* — even for the special *korban* of *avoda zara* with its *par* for an *olah* and *se'ir* for a *chatat*, the requirement of partial negation with partial affirmation still applies. The Gemara provides the source: the *braita* notes that since *avoda zara* was singled out to be judged on its own terms with its own distinct *korban*, one might have thought that the criteria for liability also differ — perhaps a full uprooting of a *mitzvah* would suffice. A *gezeirah shavah* of *me'einei* — *me'einei* appears both in the *par he'elam davar* context and in *Parshat Shelach Lecha* — equates the two: just as *he'elam davar* requires *davar velo kol haguf* (a matter, not the entirety of the law), so too *avoda zara* requires the same partial-negation structure.

### Omission Note

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת הַמַּעֲשֶׂה, וְכֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ, וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת הַמַּעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The court is liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of the action** by the general public on the basis of that ruling. **And likewise, the anointed** priest is liable only for an erroneous ruling and his unwitting performance of an action on the basis of that ruling. **And** the court and the priest are liable for a ruling **with regard to idol worship only for absence** of awareness **of the matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of the action** on the basis of that ruling.

What is notable, as will be explored further, is that the *mishnah* does not conclude this second clause with *vechen hamashiach*. It says *vechen hamashiach* by the first part — the general *he'elam davar* requirement — but not by the *avoda zara* clause. This omission is pointed, because as we have seen, the *kohen gadol* is in the category of the *yachid* for *avoda zara*, bringing a *seirah* like any other individual. Whether the full *hora'ah* apparatus still applies to him for *avoda zara* is therefore a genuine question.

## Hora'ah+Ma'aseh

### Tzibur Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״יִשְׁגּוּ״ – יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין עַל שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יִשְׁגּוּ וְנֶעֱלַם דָּבָר״, אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the *halakha* that the court is liable only for absence of awareness of the matter together with unwitting performance of an action, the Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? It is derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* that it is stated: "And if the entire congregation of Israel **shall act unwittingly"** (Leviticus 4:13). One **might** have thought **that they will be liable** to bring a bull **for** every case of **unwitting** performance **of an action.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Shall act unwittingly, and the matter was hidden"** (Leviticus 4:13), from which it is derived that the court is **liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** on the basis of that ruling.

The Gemara now systematically establishes the scriptural sources for the requirement that both *hora'ah* and *ma'aseh* must be present in each of the three categories — *tzibur*, *kohen gadol*, and *avoda zara*. For the *tzibur*: the verse says *yishgu venelam davar* — they sinned inadvertently and a matter was hidden. The initial reading might have been that *shiggas ma'aseh* alone suffices; the *talmud lomar yishgu venelam davar* teaches that both conditions must be present. *Davar, ein chayavin ela al helem davar im shiggas hama'aseh.*

### Kohen Gadol Eq

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הַמָּשִׁיחַ. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״לְאַשְׁמַת הַעָם״, הֲרֵי מָשִׁיחַ כְּצִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And likewise the anointed** priest. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? It is derived from a verse, **as it is written** with regard to the anointed priest: "If the anointed priest shall sin **so as to bring guilt upon the people"** (Leviticus 4:3), indicating that the status of **an anointed** priest **is like** that of the general **public.**

For the *kohen gadol*: *minalan dichsiv le'ashmat ha'am harei mashiach ketzibur* — the equation established from *le'ashmat ha'am* carries the full force of the *tzibur*'s requirement, including *he'elam davar* combined with *shiggas ma'aseh*, with the further specification that it must be his own action.

### Avoda Zara Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה. מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְפִי שֶׁיָּצְאָה עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָדוּן בְּעַצְמָהּ, יָכוֹל יְהוּ חַיָּיבִין עַל שִׁגְגַת הַמַּעֲשֶׂה? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״מֵעֵינֵי״ וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן ״מֵעֵינֵי״, מָה לְהַלָּן אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה, אַף כָּאן אֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And** the court and the priest are liable for a ruling **with regard to idol worship only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** on the basis of that ruling. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? It is derived **as the Sages taught: Due to** the fact **that idol worship left** the category of unwitting transgressions **to be discussed by itself** (see Numbers, chapter 15), one **might** have thought **that** the court and the priest **would be liable for** a mere **unwitting** performance of **the action.** Therefore, the term **"from the eyes of" is stated here,** with regard to idol worship (Numbers 15:24), **and "from the eyes of" is stated there,** with regard to an unwitting communal sin-offering for all other mitzvot (Leviticus 4:13). **Just as there they are liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** on the basis of that ruling, **so too here, they are liable only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** on the basis of that ruling.

For *avoda zara*: since *avoda zara* was given its own treatment and its own *korban*, one might have concluded that *shiggas ma'aseh* alone suffices. The *me'einei me'einei gezeirah shavah* links *avoda zara* back to the *he'elam davar* framework, establishing that even here *hora'ah* followed by action is required.

## Tannaim Dispute

### Rabbi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ מָשִׁיחַ בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לָא קָתָנֵי, מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: מָשִׁיחַ בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בְּהֶעְלֵם דָּבָר. וְשָׁוִין שֶׁבִּשְׂעִירָה, וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Whereas,** the *halakha* that the status of **an anointed** priest who issues a ruling **with regard to idol worship** is like that of the court **is not taught** in the mishna. **Whose** opinion is expressed in **the mishna? It is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara explains: This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **an anointed** priest unwittingly engages **in idol worship, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** He brings an offering **for unwitting** performance **of an action,** like any other Jew. **And the Rabbis say:** He brings an offering **for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling. **And they agree that** the atonement of an anointed priest is **with a female goat** as a sin-offering, **and they agree that he does not bring a provisional guilt-offering.**

With the systematic derivation complete, the Gemara turns to the anomaly identified earlier: the *mishnah* does not say *vechen hamashiach* when discussing *avoda zara*. The Gemara concludes: *masnisin mani Rabbi hi* — our *mishnah* follows the view of Rabbi. A *braita* is cited which presents the Tannaitic dispute explicitly. Rabbi holds: *mashiach ba'avoda zarah — beshiggas ma'aseh* — for a *kohen gadol* transgressing *avoda zara*, the *shgagah* of the action alone is sufficient to generate liability, without requiring a *hora'ah*.

### Chachamim View

The *chachamim* disagree: *b'he'elam davar* — even for *avoda zara*, the *kohen gadol* requires the full *hora'ah* framework, the same as for any other sin. The silence of the *mishnah* on *vechen hamashiach* in the *avoda zara* clause reflects Rabbi's position that the *kohen gadol* in that context is simply like any other individual, for whom only action is required.

### Agreement Point

Both Rabbi and the *chachamim* agree on a critical point: the *kohen gadol* brings a *seirah* — not a *par* — for *avoda zara*. He is firmly within the category of *ve-hanefesh ha-achas* in *Parshat Shelach Lecha*, which encompasses all individuals regardless of their special status. This they do not dispute.

### No Asham

Both also agree that a *kohen gadol* never brings an *asham talui*. The basis for this agreement (and the difference in reasoning between the two positions) will be analyzed in the sections that follow.

## Rabbi Reasoning

### Vekhiper Pesuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִפֶּר הַכֹּהֵן עַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ הַשֹּׁגֶגֶת בְּחֶטְאָה בִשְׁגָגָה״. ״הַנֶּפֶשׁ״ – זֶה מָשִׁיחַ, ״הַשֹּׁגֶגֶת״ – זֶה נָשִׂיא, ״בְּחֶטְאָה בִּשְׁגָגָה״ – רַבִּי סָבַר: חֵטְא זֶה בִּשְׁגָגָה יְהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? It is as **the verse states** with regard to idol worship: **"And the priest shall atone for the soul that acted unwittingly, when he sins unwittingly"** (Numbers 15:28). With regard to the term **"the soul," that is** referring to **an anointed** priest; with regard to the term **"that acted unwittingly," that is** referring to **a king;** and due to the phrase **"when he sins unwittingly," Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds this sin shall be** one performed **unwittingly,** not the result of absence of awareness of the matter, leading to an erroneous ruling.

Rabbi's reasoning begins with the *pasuk* in *Bamidbar* 15 dealing with the *avoda zara* context: *ve-chiper ha-kohen al ha-nefesh ha-shogeges* — "the *kohen* shall atone for the inadvertently sinning soul." The *nefesh* here encompasses the *kohen gadol* (as well as the *nasi*), as both are subsumed within the *yachid* framework of *avoda zara*.

### Chet Bishgaga

Rabbi derives from the phrase *be-cheto bishgaga* — "when he sins through inadvertence" — that the *chet* for which this atonement applies is defined entirely by the *shgagah* of the action. For those within this category — including the *kohen gadol* when it comes to *avoda zara* — the *hora'ah* is simply not the operative criterion. The sin and the inadvertence are identical, and only the inadvertent act is *mechayev*.

## Chachamim Reasoning

### Pesuk Emphasis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מִי שֶׁחַטָּאתוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה, יָצָא מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁאֵין חַטָּאתוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה אֶלָּא בְּהֶעְלֵם דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis hold:** This phrase serves to teach that the *halakha* that this offering is brought for an unwitting sin applies to **one whose sin-offering** for all other transgressions is **for an unwitting** act. This serves to **exclude an anointed** priest, **whose** liability to bring **a sin-offering for an unwitting** act is **only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, **together** with unwitting performance of an action on the basis of that ruling.

The *chachamim* read the same phrase differently. *Ve-chiper ha-kohen al ha-nefesh ha-shogeges* — "for the inadvertently sinning soul": this describes the ordinary condition of individuals for whom the sin is defined entirely by the inadvertence of the action. That is the paradigm case.

### Exclusion

But the *kohen gadol* does not fit this paradigm. *Yatza mashiach she-ein cheto bishgaga ella b'he'elam davar* — the *kohen gadol* is excluded, because his sin is not defined by the *shgagah* of the action alone but by *he'elam davar* — the faulty ruling. Therefore, even for *avoda zara*, where he brings a *seirah* like any *yachid*, the *chachamim* maintain that the requirement of a *hora'ah* combined with a *ma'aseh* still governs his liability.

## Asham Talui

### Asham Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין שֶׁאֵין מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו הַכֹּהֵן עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ אֲשֶׁר שָׁגָג״, מִי שֶׁכׇּל חֶטְאוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין [כׇּל] חֶטְאוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה, אֶלָּא בְּהֶעְלֵם דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **And they agree that he does not bring a provisional guilt-offering.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? It is derived from a verse, **as it is written** with regard to the provisional guilt-offering: **"And the priest shall atone for him for his unwitting act that he performed unwittingly"** (Leviticus 5:18). Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis disagree with regard to the interpretation of this verse, in accordance with their opinions. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds:** This is referring to **one whose sin-offering** for **all** transgressions is **for an unwitting** act. This serves to **exclude that** anointed priest, **as all of his sin-offerings are not for an unwitting** act alone; **rather,** he brings a sin-offering only if that unwitting act is performed on the basis **of absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling.

The *mishnah* on 8b states, and both Rabbi and the *chachamim* affirm, that a *kohen gadol* does not bring an *asham talui*. The source *pasuk* is *ve-chiper alav ha-kohen al shigagato asher shagag* — "the *kohen* shall atone for him for his inadvertent act that he inadvertently did," which is the verse governing *asham talui*.

### Rabbi Exclusion

Rabbi derives from the doubling — *shigagato asher shagag* — that *asham talui* applies only to *mi she-kol cheto bishgaga*: someone all of whose sins are determined purely by the inadvertence of the action. The *kohen gadol*, though he is treated like an ordinary individual for *avoda zara* (requiring only *shgagah* of action), is nevertheless excluded from *asham talui* because the vast majority of his sins — everything other than *avoda zara* — requires a *hora'ah* in addition to the action. Since not all of his sins are defined by *shgagah* alone — *she-ein kol cheto bishgaga* — he is not in the class of persons for whom *asham talui* was instituted.

### Asher Shagag Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי ״כׇּל״ כְּתִיב? אִין דְּאִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב ״עַל שִׁגְגָתוֹ״. לְמָה לִי ״אֲשֶׁר שָׁגָג״ הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּעַד דְּאִיכָּא כׇּל חֶטְאוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה. יָצָא מָשִׁיחַ שֶׁאֵין כׇּל חֶטְאוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה אֶלָּא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְלֹא בִּשְׁאָר מִצְווֹת אֶלָּא בְּהֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is it written** in the verse: For **all** his unwitting acts that he performed unwittingly? Only the phrase "for his unwitting act that he performed unwittingly" is written. The Gemara answers: **Yes,** it is as though the word: All, is written. **As if** it were **so** that the verse is not referring to all unwitting acts, **let** the Torah **write** only: **"For his unwitting act." Why do I** need the additional phrase: **"That he performed unwittingly"? This teaches us that** there is no liability to bring a provisional guilt-offering **unless** liability to bring **all his sin-offerings is for unwitting** acts. This serves to **exclude an anointed** priest, **all of whose** liability to bring **a sin-offering for an unwitting** act alone **is only in** cases of **idol worship, but in** cases involving **the rest of** the **mitzvot** there is liability **only for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** on the basis of that ruling.

The Gemara presses a textual question on this derivation: it does not actually say *kol* — "all" — in the verse. It says *asher shagag* — "that he inadvertently did." How does Rabbi derive from this the requirement that *all* of one's sins must be *bishgaga*?

### Entire Sin

The Gemara responds: *d'im kein yichtov al shiggaso* — if the verse merely intended to identify this particular act as inadvertent, it would have sufficed to say *al shiggaso*, "for his inadvertence." The addition of *asher shagag* is superfluous unless it is conveying something further: namely, that only a person for whom the entire structure of liability is always through *shgagah* — and nothing else — comes within the ambit of *asham talui*.

### Chachamim: Never

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן: מִי שֶׁחֶטְאוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה, יָצָא מָשִׁיחַ, שֶׁאֵין חֶטְאוֹ בִּשְׁגָגָה לֹא בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וְלֹא בִּשְׁאָר מִצְווֹת, אֶלָּא בְּהֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** interpret the verse in accordance with their opinion that an anointed priest, even in cases of idol worship, brings a sin-offering only for absence of awareness of the matter, leading to an erroneous ruling, together with unwitting performance of an action on the basis of that ruling. They hold: This teaches us that there is liability to bring a provisional guilt-offering only for **one whose** liability to bring his **sin-offerings is for an unwitting** act. This serves to **exclude an anointed** priest, **whose** liability to bring **a sin-offering is not for an unwitting** act alone, **neither in** cases of **idol worship, nor in** cases involving **the rest of** the **mitzvot,** but **rather for absence** of awareness **of** the **matter,** leading to an erroneous ruling, together **with unwitting** performance **of an action** on the basis of that ruling.

For the *chachamim*, the point is straightforward and unambiguous: *mi she-chataso bishgaga* — *asham talui* applies to someone whose sin is always defined by the inadvertence of action alone. The *kohen gadol* is excluded categorically — *lo ba'avoda zara velo bishe'ar mitzvot* — neither for *avoda zara* nor for any other prohibition. In every case, whether *avoda zara* or ordinary sins, the *kohen gadol*'s *chiyuv* requires *he'elam davar* combined with *shiggas ma'aseh*. He therefore never falls into the class of one whose liability is constituted purely by action, and consequently can never bring an *asham talui*.

Thus the daf concludes with the framework fully articulated: the *kohen gadol* brings a *seirah* for *avoda zara*; the Tannaitic dispute between Rabbi and the *chachamim* concerns whether *avoda zara* requires *hora'ah* or *shgagah* alone; and both agree that *asham talui* is entirely inapplicable to the *kohen gadol*, though they differ in the precise reasoning.
