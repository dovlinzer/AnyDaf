# Shabbat 130 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bloodletting & Timing

### Frequency & Age Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: פּוּרְסָא דִדְמָא — כׇּל תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין. וּבֵין הַפְּרָקִים — יְמַעֵט, וּבֵין הַפְּרָקִים יַחְזוֹר וִימַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said: The interval for bloodletting** is **every thirty days. And during the** middle **periods** of one's life, **he should decrease** the frequency of bloodletting; **and during the** later **periods, he should decrease** its frequency **again.**

The daf begins on 129b, picking up from where the preceding *perek* left off. Because the end of the previous chapter addressed the status of a woman in childbirth as a case of *sakanas nefashos* — someone for whom *Shabbos* may be violated — the *Gemara* naturally transitions into related health matters, particularly the practice of bloodletting, which was a standard medical intervention of the time. The rabbis recognized, however, that bloodletting also left the patient in a vulnerable state, and so they legislated carefully around it.

*Shmuel* opens with the rule: *porsa de-dama kol tlasin yomin* — the interval between bleedings is thirty days. One does not let blood twice within a single month. *Shmuel* adds further: *u-veina perakim yema'et* — between the chapters of life, one should do even less. *Rashi* explains this as a reference to age: after forty, assuming a lifespan of eighty years, one is in the intermediate chapter and should reduce the frequency. After sixty, the final chapter, one should reduce further still — *Rashi* suggests once every two or three months.

### Prohibited Days & Mars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: פּוּרְסָא דִדְמָא — חַד בְּשַׁבְּתָא, אַרְבָּעָה, וּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא, אֲבָל שֵׁנִי וַחֲמִישִׁי — לָא. דְּאָמַר מָר: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ זְכוּת אָבוֹת יַקִּיז דָּם בְּשֵׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי, שֶׁבֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה וְשֶׁל מַטָּה שָׁוִין כְּאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: The times for bloodletting** are **the first** day **of the week, the fourth** day of the week **and Shabbat eve. However,** on the **second and the fifth** days of the week, **no,** one should not let blood, **as the Master said:** Only **one who has the merit of** his **ancestors** and relies on it **should let blood on** the **second and on the fifth** days of the week, **as the court on High,** in the heavens, **and the court below are equal.** The courts in the cities convene on Mondays and Thursdays, as does the heavenly court. Letting blood on a day of judgment is dangerous. If one is judged unfavorably all his blood could flow out.

*Shmuel* also specifies the appropriate days for bloodletting: Sunday, Wednesday, and Friday. *Shabbos* is excluded in any case because of *melacha*. But what of the remaining days? Monday and Thursday are explicitly prohibited: *mi she-yesh lo zechus avos yeza'at dam be-sheni va-chamishi* — only one who has exceptional ancestral merit and wishes to take a risk would let blood on those days. The reason is that both the heavenly court and the earthly court convene on Mondays and Thursdays. The earthly courts would hold sessions in the cities on those days, making them days of judgment. Since bloodletting weakens the body and creates vulnerability, one should not add to that risk on a day of divine scrutiny.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּתְלָתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּקָיְימָא לֵיהּ מַאְדִּים בְּזָוֵוי. מַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא נָמֵי קָיְימָא בְּזָוֵוי! כֵּיוָן דְּדָשׁוּ בֵּיהּ רַבִּים — ״שׁוֹמֵר פְּתָאִים ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **On the third day of the week, what is the reason** that one does **not** let blood? It is **because** the planet **Mars is dominant during the even** hours. Since it is a planet of blood, and the even hours are a bad omen, that combination gives cause for concern. The Gemara asks: On **Shabbat eve,** Mars **also dominates during the even** hours. The Gemara answers: **Since** the **multitudes have** already become **accustomed to** letting blood on Shabbat eve, the verse: **"The Lord protects the simple-hearted"** (Psalms 116:6) applies in this case.

Tuesday presents a separate problem: *mishum de-kaimei lei Ma'adim bi-zuvei* — because *Ma'adim*, Mars, appears in an even hour on that day. The *Gemara* operates within a framework in which seven celestial bodies cycle through the hours of the day in a fixed sequence. *Rashi* spells out the order with the mnemonic *ShTzaM ChaNKaL*: *Shabtai* (Saturn), *Tzedek* (Jupiter), *Ma'adim* (Mars), *Chamah* (the Sun), *Kochav* (Mercury), *Nogah* (Venus), and *Levanah* (the Moon). Beginning from the moment of creation and proceeding through a seven-hour rotation, the mathematics work out such that on Tuesday, Mars always falls on an even-numbered hour. The *Gemara* emphasizes this specifically because Mars governs blood, and an even hour was considered particularly dangerous. It is worth noting that in several European languages — French and Spanish among them — Tuesday's name is etymologically derived from Mars, *Mardi* and *Martes* respectively, which is a remarkable convergence.

Friday poses a further complication: Mars also falls on an even hour on Friday, in the fourth hour. Yet bloodletting on Friday was widespread practice. The logic was pragmatic: people would let blood on Friday, then be hungry enough to eat a substantial meal entering *Shabbos*, which they needed in any case, and those without much money could combine the occasions conveniently. The *Gemara* invokes the principle *shomer pesayim Hashem* — God protects the simple — acknowledging that because the practice was already so entrenched, prohibiting it would be futile. This principle appears again in a responsum of Rav Moshe Feinberg regarding smoking: it is prohibited to begin, but for those already accustomed, *shomer pesayim Hashem*.

The question of how seriously to take astrological considerations is worth pausing over. The belief in astrology was widespread, extending well beyond the *Gemara* into the *Rishonim* — the *Ibn Ezra*, for example, was deeply invested in astrological thinking. The *Rambam*, characteristically, either dismisses these beliefs as simply false or argues that the science has advanced and corrected earlier errors. There is, however, an interesting asymmetry in the tradition: matters that touch on the mechanics of the heavenly spheres and their impact on human events tended to receive more weight than straightforwardly medical or astronomical advice. These texts about bloodletting and dangerous hours occupy a middle register, and even in contemporary *yeshivos*, one does not observe these *Gemaros* being treated with the same normative weight as halachic rulings.

### Dangerous Dates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַרְבַּע דְּהוּא אַרְבַּע, אַרְבַּע דְּהוּא אַרְבֵּיסַר, אַרְבַּע דְּהוּא עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבְּעָה, אַרְבַּע דְּלֵיכָּא אַרְבַּע בָּתְרֵיהּ — סַכַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Shmuel says:** On **the fourth** day of the week that **is the fourth day** of the month; on **the fourth** day of the week that **is the fourteenth** of the month; on **the fourth** day of the week that **is the twenty-fourth** of the month; and on **the fourth** day of the week after which **there are not four** days remaining in the month **it is dangerous** to let blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֹאשׁ חוֹדֶשׁ וְשֵׁנִי לוֹ — חוּלְשָׁא. שְׁלִישִׁי לוֹ — סַכָּנָה. מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא טָבָא — חוּלְשָׁא. מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דַעֲצַרְתָּא — סַכַּנְתָּא. וּגְזַרוּ רַבָּנַן אַכּוּלְּהוּ מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא טָבָא מִשּׁוּם יוֹמָא (טָבָא) דַעֲצַרְתָּא, דְּנָפֵיק בֵּיהּ זִיקָא וּשְׁמֵיהּ ״טָבוֹחַ״, דְּאִי לָא קַבִּלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל תּוֹרָה — הֲוָה טָבַח לְהוּ לְבִשְׂרַיְיהוּ וְלִדְמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Bloodletting on **the New Moon and** on the **second** day of the month causes **weakness;** bloodletting on the **third** day of the month leads to **danger.** Bloodletting on **the eve of a Festival** causes **weakness;** bloodletting on **the eve of the festival of *Shavuot*** leads to **danger. And the Sages issued a decree** prohibiting bloodletting **on the eve of every Festival due to the festival** of ***Shavuot*. On** *Shavuot*, **an evil spirit named *Tibbuaḥ*,** from the Hebrew word meaning slaughter, **emerges, as had the Jewish people not accepted the Torah** on the festival of *Shavuot*, *Tibbuaḥ* **would have slaughtered their flesh and their blood.** Consequently, it remains a dangerous day.

*Shmuel* goes on to enumerate specific dates that are dangerous for bloodletting. A Wednesday that falls on the 4th of the month (*arba d'hu arba*), a Wednesday on the 14th (*arba d'arba'is*), a Wednesday on the 24th (*arba d'esrim v'arba*), or a Wednesday near month's end when fewer than four days remain — all of these are *sakanta*, dangerous. *Rosh Chodesh* itself and the day after cause weakness (*chulusha*). The third of the month is an outright danger. *Erev Yom Tov* weakens; *Erev Shavuos* actually endangers. The rabbis extended the *Erev Shavuos* prohibition to all *Erev Yom Tov* occasions.

The reason given for the special danger of *Erev Shavuos* is striking: *d'nafik beizika u-shmei tavu'ach* — on that day, a wind goes forth whose name is "slaughter," *because had the Jewish people not accepted the Torah, it would have slaughtered their flesh and blood*. The day carries residual mortal danger, and one should not court it through bloodletting.

### Eating & Bloodletting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אָכַל חִטָּה וְהִקִּיז דָּם — לֹא הִקִּיז אֶלָּא לְאוֹתָהּ חִטָּה. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לִרְפוּאָה, אֲבָל אַקּוֹלֵי — מֵיקֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said:** If **one ate wheat and** afterward **let blood, he only let that wheat.** The bloodletting was ineffective as the wheat replaced any blood that was let. **And this** ineffectiveness **applies** only if he let blood **to cure** an illness; **however, to relieve** discomfort, bloodletting after eating wheat **relieves** one's discomfort.

*Shmuel* rules that if one eats wheat — or more broadly, eats anything — immediately before bloodletting, the bloodletting only serves to drain what was just consumed. It does not provide the more general health benefit one would otherwise expect. This applies when the purpose is therapeutic (*l'refuah*). However, if the goal is merely to lighten the blood — to relieve a sense of heaviness — then it is effective even after eating.

### Post-Bloodletting Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּקִּיז דָּם, שְׁתִיָּיה — לְאַלְתַּר. אֲכִילָה — עַד חֲצִי מִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **One who lets blood** should engage in **drinking immediately;** he should not engage in **eating until** the time that it takes to walk **half a *mil*** has elapsed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שְׁתִיָּיה לְאַלְתַּר מְעַלֵּי, אֲבָל בָּתַר הָכִי קָשֵׁי, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא קָשֵׁי וְלָא מְעַלֵּי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: When they said that one should engage in **drinking immediately,** does that mean that drinking immediately is **beneficial; however, thereafter, it is injurious? Or perhaps,** thereafter **it is neither injurious nor beneficial.** No resolution is found for this dilemma, therefore **it stands** unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אֲכִילָה עַד חֲצִי מִיל הוּא דְּקָמְעַלֵּי, הָא בָּתַר הָכִי וּמִקַּמֵּי הָכִי — קָשֵׁי, אוֹ דִילְמָא לָא קָשֵׁי וְלָא מְעַלֵּי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: When they said one should not engage in **eating until** the time that it takes to walk **half a *mil*** has elapsed, does that mean **that** only at that time **it is beneficial,** however, **after that** time **or before that** time **it is injurious? Or perhaps,** before and after that time **it is neither injurious nor beneficial.** No resolution is found for this dilemma, therefore **it** too **stands** unresolved.

After bloodletting, one may drink immediately. One should eat only after the time it takes to walk a *chatzi mil* — approximately ten minutes — has elapsed. Two questions are then raised and left unresolved in *teiku*. First: is drinking immediately after bloodletting actually beneficial, and does delay make it harmful, or is delay merely neutral? Second: is eating within a half-*mil* beneficial, and is eating before or after that window harmful, or merely neutral? Both questions remain open.

### Barbers & Pricing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְרִיז רַב: מֵאָה קַרֵי בְּזוּזָא, מֵאָה רֵישֵׁי בְּזוּזָא, מֵאָה שְׂפָמֵי וְלָא כְלוּם. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַב הוּנָא, יוֹמָא דִּמְפַגְּרִי בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן, אָמְרִי: הָאִידָּנָא יוֹמָא דִשְׂפָמֵי הוּא, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי קָאָמְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav** would **announce: One hundred bloodlettings for a *zuz*;** cutting the hair on **one hundred heads for a *zuz*;** grooming **one hundred moustaches** does **not** cost **anything** (*ge'onim*). Blood letters typically served as barbers as well and did not charge for grooming a moustache. If one hundred men happened to arrive one day to have their moustaches groomed, the barber would work all day without receiving any pay. **Rav Yosef said: When we were** students **in Rav Huna's school,** on **a day when the Sages were sluggish** in their studies, **they would say: Today is the day of the moustaches, and I did not know what they were saying.** Now that I heard Rav's statement, I understand that they meant that it was a day without purpose.

*Rav* would announce publicly: *me'a karnei bezuza, me'a reishi bezuza* — a hundred horn-applications (bloodlettings by horn) for a *zuz*, a hundred head-treatments (haircuts) for a *zuz*. *Tosafos* explains that bloodletters and barbers were the same tradesmen, sharing the same sharp instruments. The proclamation was essentially advertising a standard price: a *zuz* per service. As for mustaches — *me'a sepami velo klum* — a hundred mustaches for nothing, meaning the mustache trim was thrown in for free alongside the paid service.

*Rav Yosef* adds a charming postscript: when he was a student in the house of *Rav Huna*, on days when the rabbis were unproductive in their learning, they would say: *haidna yoma de-shapmi hu* — today is a mustache day. He reports that at the time he did not understand the expression, but now he does. Since the barber received nothing for the mustache, a day when one exerts effort and receives nothing — or simply produces nothing of value — became known as a mustache day.

## Childbirth & Shabbat

### Umbilical Cord Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹשְׁרִין הַטַּבּוּר. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קוֹשְׁרִין הַטַּבּוּר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף חוֹתְכִין. וְטוֹמְנִין הַשִּׁלְיָא כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּחַם הַוָּלָד.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And one may tie the umbilical cord** of a child born on Shabbat. **The Sages taught** similarly in the *Tosefta* and even added to it: **One may tie the umbilical cord** of a child born on Shabbat. **Rabbi Yosei said: One may even cut** the umbilical cord. **And one may insulate the placenta** as a healing remedy **so as to warm the newborn.**

The *Gemara* transitions to matters surrounding childbirth, which connects organically to the preceding chapter's discussion of a woman in labor as a case of *sakanas nefashos*. The *Gemara* states: *vekorshin hatabur* — one may tie the umbilical cord on *Shabbos*. A *baraisa* is then cited: *vekorshin hatabur* — one may tie the umbilical cord.

### Placenta Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בְּנוֹת מְלָכִים — טוֹמְנוֹת בִּסְפָלִים שֶׁל שֶׁמֶן, בְּנוֹת עֲשִׁירִים — בִּסְפוֹגִים שֶׁל צֶמֶר, בְּנוֹת עֲנִיִּים בְּמוֹכִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: Princesses insulate** the placenta **in mugs of oil; the daughters of the wealthy** do so **in combed wool; the daughters of the poor in** soft **rags.**

*Rabbi Yossi* adds: *af chotchin* — one may even cut the umbilical cord. A further practice is mentioned: *vetomnin hashilya kedei sheyeicham havlad* — one buries the placenta so that the infant will be warm, reflecting a medical belief of the time that the placenta provided warmth to the newborn.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* describes the method of burial as varying by socioeconomic status. Daughters of kings — meaning the very wealthy — would bury the placenta in basins of oil (*b'sefalim shel shemen*). Daughters of the wealthy would bury it in wool sponges or piles of wool. Daughters of the poor would bury it in pieces of cloth (*b'mochin*).

### Cord Cutting Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **Rav said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** that it is even permitted to cut the umbilical cord on Shabbat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: מוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּטַבּוּר שֶׁל שְׁנֵי תִינוֹקוֹת, שֶׁחוֹתְכִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּמְנַתְּחִי אַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **Rav said: The Rabbis concede to Rabbi Yosei with regard to the umbilical cord** attached to **twin babies that one may cut** it on Shabbat. **What is the reason** for this? In that case, leaving the cord attached is dangerous. **Since** the attached twins will try to **disengage from each other,** they could potentially rip each other's cords.

*Rav Nachman* transmitted in the name of *Rabbah bar Avuha* in the name of *Rav*: the *halacha* follows *Rabbi Yossi* — one may indeed cut the umbilical cord on *Shabbos*, not only tie it.

*Rav Nachman* further transmits: even the *Chachamim* who generally dispute *Rabbi Yossi* would agree that when twins share an umbilical cord, one may cut it to separate them. The reason is *d'manchi ahadadei* — they pull against one another, and if left connected they can endanger each other. Even the more restrictive opinion grants this exception.

### Childbirth Acts Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הָאָמוּר בְּפָרָשַׁת תּוֹכָחָה עוֹשִׂין לְחַיָּה בְּשַׁבָּת. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמוֹלְדוֹתַיִךְ בְּיוֹם הוּלֶּדֶת אוֹתָךְ לֹא כׇרַּת שׇׁרֵּךְ וּבְמַיִם לֹא רֻחַצְתְּ לְמִשְׁעִי וְהׇמְלֵחַ לֹא הֻמְלַחַתְּ וְהׇחְתֵּל לֹא חֻתָּלְתְּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **Rav said: Everything stated in the passage of rebuke** (Ezekiel 16) **one may perform for a woman in childbirth on Shabbat.** Since that chapter speaks of the dangerous birth of an abandoned child, for all other children, these issues should be addressed. **As it is stated** there: **"And as for your birth, on the day you were born, your navel was not cut, and you were not washed in water for cleansing, and you were not salted, nor were you swaddled"** (Ezekiel 16:4).

*Rav Nachman* transmits a third ruling in the same chain: *kol ha-amur b'parshas tochacha — osim l'chaya b'Shabbos*. Anything described in the passage of remonstration in *Yechezkel*, where God reproaches Israel by describing what proper care at birth looks like — all of those acts may be performed for a woman who has just given birth on *Shabbos*.

### Yechezkel Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּמוֹלְדוֹתַיִךְ בְּיוֹם הוּלֶּדֶת״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁמְיַילְּדִים אֶת הַוָּלָד בְּשַׁבָּת. ״לֹא כׇרַּת שׇׁרֵּךְ״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁחוֹתְכִין הַטַּבּוּר בְּשַׁבָּת. ״וּבְמַיִם לֹא רֻחַצְתְּ לְמִשְׁעִי״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁרוֹחֲצִים הַוָּלָד בְּשַׁבָּת. ״וְהׇמְלֵחַ לֹא הֻמְלַחַתְּ״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁמּוֹלְחִים הַוָּלָד בְּשַׁבָּת. ״וְהׇחְתֵּל לֹא חֻתָּלְתְּ״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁמְּלַפְּפִין הַוָּלָד בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **"And as for your birth, on the day you were born"; from here** it is derived **that one delivers the newborn on Shabbat. "Your navel was not cut"; from here** it is derived **that one cuts the umbilical cord on Shabbat. "And you were not washed in water for cleansing"; from here** it is derived **that one washes the newborn on Shabbat. "And you were not salted"; from here** it is derived **that one salts the newborn on Shabbat. "Nor were you swaddled"; from here** it is derived **that one swaddles the newborn on Shabbat.**

The *Gemara* proceeds to derive each practice from the specific language of the *pasuk* in *Yechezkel*: *u-moladosayich b'yom huledet osach* — from the phrase "your birth on the day you were born," the *Gemara* derives *mikan she-meyaldin et havlad b'Shabbos* — that one may assist a woman in childbirth on *Shabbos* (the *hif'il* form indicating another person actively assisting). *Lo kores sarech* — "your navel was not cut" — yields *mikan she-chotchin et hatabur b'Shabbos*, that one may cut the umbilical cord. *U-v'mayim lo ruchatzat l'mish'i* — "you were not bathed in water to smooth you" — yields that one may bathe the newborn. *V'hamlei'ach lo humlachas* — "you were not salted" — yields that one may rub salt on the newborn, which was believed to promote health. *V'hachtel lo huchtalt* — "you were not swaddled" — yields that one may swaddle the newborn on *Shabbos*.

A brief but important remark from *Tosafos* clarifies the nature of this derivation: *lav mikra mafik* — these are not actually being derived as *halachos* from the *pasuk*. Rather, *ella mistama*, since *Yechezkel* mentions these acts in a context of reproach — pointing out that they were neglected — *im ken tzar hu* — if they are not performed, it causes suffering to the newborn. And since *u-v'chulan ein bahem issur d'Oraisa* — none of these acts involves a biblical prohibition (cutting an umbilical cord, for instance, is not a *melacha* in the biblical sense; the most that could arise would be incidental bleeding from certain cutting actions) — the point is simply that where there is no biblical violation and there is real suffering involved, these acts are entirely permitted.

## Circumcision Shabbat

### Tools on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם לֹא הֵבִיא כְּלִי מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — מְבִיאוֹ בְּשַׁבָּת מְגוּלֶּה, וּבַסַּכָּנָה מְכַסֵּהוּ עַל פִּי עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: As a continuation to the discussion at the end of the previous chapter, which mentioned circumcision in the context of a discussion of the *halakhot* of childbirth on Shabbat, the mishna continues to address the *halakhot* of circumcision. **Rabbi Eliezer says: If he did not bring an implement** for circumcising the child **on Shabbat eve, he brings it on Shabbat** itself **uncovered** so that it will be clear to all that he is bringing a circumcision scalpel. **And in** times of **danger,** when decrees of persecution prohibit Jews from circumcising their children, **one covers it in the presence of witnesses** who can testify that he transported the scalpel to perform a mitzva.

The *Gemara* now opens the new *perek*, called *Rabbi Eliezer d'Milah*, which is devoted to the *halachos* of *mila* on *Shabbos* as well as *mila* in general. The name disambiguates this chapter from another *perek* whose opening line also features *Rabbi Eliezer* — specifically *Rabbi Eliezer d'ha-oreg*, the chapter dealing with weaving. Since the present chapter lacks a convenient verbal hook in its opening line, it is identified by its subject matter.

The *perek* opens: *Rabbi Eliezer omer: im lo hevi kli me-erev Shabbat, mevi'o be-Shabbat meguleh* — if one did not bring the circumcision implements before *Shabbos*, one may bring them on *Shabbos* itself, and one does so *meguleh*, openly, so that everyone can see that a *mila* knife is being carried. This is permitted because *brit mila* overrides *Shabbos*. Carrying the knife openly removes any suspicion that one is carrying something forbidden.

In a case of danger — where non-Jews have prohibited *brit mila* — one may cover the knife, but *al pi edim*, in the presence of witnesses. Jews, at least, should know what is being carried beneath one's garments, to dispel any suspicion among the Jewish community.

### Multi-Stage Violations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: כּוֹרְתִים עֵצִים לַעֲשׂוֹת פֶּחָמִין לַעֲשׂוֹת (כְּלִי) בַּרְזֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rabbi Eliezer said** with regard to this issue: **One** may even **cut down trees to prepare charcoal** in order **to fashion iron tools** for the purpose of circumcision.

*Rabbi Eliezer* goes further still: *kortim etzim la-asos pechamim la-asos barzel* — one may even cut down trees on *Shabbos* to make charcoal, and use the charcoal to forge the iron for the *mila* knife. In other words, *Rabbi Eliezer* permits violations many stages removed from the *mila* itself, so long as they are necessary for performing the *brit*. If there is no other way, any act required — no matter how indirect — is permitted on *Shabbos* for the sake of *brit mila*.

## Akiva Restriction

### Akiva's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לַעֲשׂוֹתָהּ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — אֵינָהּ דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת, וּמִילָה שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לַעֲשׂוֹתָהּ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת דּוֹחָה אֶת הַשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer's approach was not universally accepted, and **a principle was stated by Rabbi Akiva: Any prohibited labor that can be performed on Shabbat eve does not override Shabbat,** including transporting the circumcision scalpel. However, any prohibited labor involved in the mitzva of **circumcision** itself **that cannot be performed on Shabbat eve overrides Shabbat.**

*Rabbi Akiva* articulates a sharply contrasting principle: *kol melacha she-efshar la-asotah me-erev Shabbat einah docha et ha-Shabbat* — any *melacha* that could have been done before *Shabbos* does not override *Shabbos*. Only that which could not have been done beforehand — the *mila* proper, which by definition must be performed on the eighth day and therefore cannot be done in advance — is what overrides *Shabbos*. Everything else, in theory, could have been done before *Shabbos* began, and therefore it does not override it.

This principle is not about practical impossibility — it does not matter whether, say, the person carrying the knife was unavoidably delayed. The question is purely theoretical: could it have been done beforehand? If yes, it does not override *Shabbos*. Practically, this means that if the knife was forgotten, one waits until *Motzaei Shabbos* and performs the *brit* on Sunday. There is a strong *mitzvah* to perform *mila* on the eighth day itself — derived from the phrase *u-vayom ha-shemini yimol*, which *Chazal* understand to indicate a special imperative even to the point of overriding *Shabbos* — but once the conditions for that override are not met, one simply performs it as soon as *Shabbos* ends.

## Circumcision Shabbat (II)

### Exposure Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מִשּׁוּם חַבּוֹבֵי מִצְוָה, אוֹ דִילְמָא מִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְאֵתוֹיֵי מְכוּסֶּה עַל פִּי עֵדִים. אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם חַבּוֹבֵי מִצְוָה, מְגוּלֶּה — אִין, מְכוּסֶּה — לָא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא — אֲפִילּוּ מְכוּסֶּה, שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is **the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion that the scalpel must be uncovered **due to affection for the mitzva** and the desire to publicize it, **or perhaps** it is **due to** avoiding **suspicion?** The Gemara asks: **What** practical **difference is there** between the two reasons suggested for Rabbi Eliezer's opinion? The Gemara answers: The difference is with regard to the question of whether or not it is permitted **to bring** the scalpel **covered in the presence of witnesses** who are aware that one is bringing the scalpel for the purpose of circumcision. **If you say** the reason is **due to affection for the mitzva,** then if it is **uncovered, yes,** there is a display of affection for the mitzva. If it is **covered, no,** there is no display of affection. **However, if you say** the reason for this ruling is **due to** avoiding **suspicion, even** if it is **covered** he may **well** do so, because the witnesses are aware that a circumcision will be performed. **What** is the resolution of this dilemma?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: לֹא אֲמָרָהּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אֶלָּא לְחַבּוֹבֵי מִצְוָה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מְבִיאוֹ מְגוּלֶּה וְאֵין מְבִיאוֹ מְכוּסֶּה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: וּבִשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה מְכַסֵּהוּ עַל פִּי עֵדִים, בְּסַכָּנָה — אִין, שֶׁלֹּא בְּסַכָּנָה — לָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִשּׁוּם חַבּוֹבֵי מִצְוָה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that **Rabbi Levi said: Rabbi Eliezer only stated** this ruling **to** express **affection for the mitzva. That** opinion **was also taught** in a *baraita*: If a child is to be circumcised on Shabbat and they failed to bring the scalpel on Shabbat eve, **one brings it** on Shabbat **uncovered,** but **he does not bring it covered;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rav Ashi said:** The language of **the mishna is also precise** in support of this opinion, **as it teaches: And in a time of danger he covers it in the presence of witnesses.** By inference, **in** a time of **danger, yes,** he covers it; when it is **not** a time of **danger, no,** he does not cover it. **Conclude from this** that the scalpel is uncovered **due to affection for the mitzva.** The Gemara states: Indeed, **conclude from this.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: מְבִיאוֹ מְגוּלֶּה וְאֵין מְבִיאוֹ מְכוּסֶּה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: נוֹהֲגִין הָיוּ בִּשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה שֶׁהָיוּ מְבִיאִין מְכוּסֶּה עַל פִּי עֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in **another** *baraita*: **He brings** the scalpel **uncovered, and he does not bring it covered;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: In a time of danger they would customarily bring** the scalpel **covered in the presence of witnesses.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ, עֵדִים דְּקָאָמַר: אִיהוּ וְחַד, אוֹ דִילְמָא: הוּא וּתְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to these witnesses, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **The** necessary **witnesses that he is saying,** do they include **he** who is bringing the scalpel **and one** other witness? **Or perhaps** they include **he** who brought the scalpel **and two** other witnesses to testify on his behalf.

The *Gemara* returns to *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling about carrying the knife openly and poses the question: what is the reason he requires this? Is it *mishum chavuv mitzvah* — to endear the *mitzvah*, to publicize how important *brit mila* is such that one carries the knife openly on *Shabbos* as a demonstration? Or is it *mishum chashada* — simply to avoid the suspicion that one is carrying something forbidden, with no particular positive statement being made?

The practical consequence is this: if the reason is *chavuv mitzvah*, then carrying it openly is specifically required — *meguleh in, makhuseh lo*. If the reason is only to avoid suspicion, then covering it with witnesses would serve equally well.

*Rabbi Levi* rules: *lo amar Rabbi Eliezer ela l'chavuv mitzvah* — *Rabbi Eliezer* said it specifically to endear the *mitzvah*, not merely to avoid suspicion. A *baraisa* underscores this by formulating the rule as *mevi'o meguleh ve-ein mevi'o makhuseh* — one brings it openly and specifically does not bring it covered. The double formulation — both the affirmative and the negative — implies it must be done this way.

*Rav Ashi* adds that the *Mishnah* itself implies the same conclusion: it states that in a time of danger one covers it. This implies that when there is *no* danger, covering is not permitted. If the sole concern were avoiding suspicion, hiding it with witnesses would presumably always be adequate. The fact that covering is restricted to times of actual danger demonstrates that the exposure serves the affirmative purpose of *chavuv mitzvah*. *Shema mina* — this is a valid proof.

A further *baraisa* records that *Rabbi Yehuda* reported in the name of *Rabbi Eliezer*: in times of danger, the practice was to bring the knife covered with witnesses present. This is essentially a restatement of what the *Mishnah* records. The *Gemara* then asks: when we say "witnesses," does this mean the carrier plus one other person, or two witnesses beyond the carrier? The *Gemara* resolves that it must mean two beyond the carrier — since the carrier cannot formally testify on his own behalf — or alternatively, he and another together would be understood as capable of serving as witnesses in other contexts, satisfying the concern even if not constituting formal *edut*.

## Localized Authorities

### Community Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָיוּ כּוֹרְתִים עֵצִים לַעֲשׂוֹת פֶּחָמִין לַעֲשׂוֹת בַּרְזֶל בְּשַׁבָּת, בִּמְקוֹמוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הָיוּ אוֹכְלִין בְּשַׂר עוֹף בְּחָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **In the locale of Rabbi Eliezer,** where they would follow his ruling, **they would** even **cut down trees** on Shabbat **to prepare charcoal** from it in order **to fashion iron** tools with which to circumcise a child **on Shabbat.** On a related note, the *baraita* relates: **In the locale of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili they would eat poultry meat in milk,** as Rabbi Yosei HaGelili held that the prohibition of meat in milk does not include poultry.

The *Gemara* now broadens its lens. It is a striking feature of the *halachic* landscape of the *Chazal* period that different communities followed different local authorities, and what was practiced in one place was not necessarily the standard elsewhere. Three communities are held up as examples.

In the place of *Rabbi Eliezer*, people would actually chop wood on *Shabbos* to make charcoal, to make iron, to forge the *mila* knife — performing the very multi-stage violations *Rabbi Eliezer* permitted. The community genuinely followed his ruling in practice.

### Fowl & Milk Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי יוֹסֵף רִישְׁבָּא. קָרִיבוּ לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא דְטַוּוֹסָא בַּחֲלָבָא, לָא אֲכַל. כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי לָא תְּשַׁמְּתִינְהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה הֲוָה, וְאָמֵינָא: דִּילְמָא דְּרַשׁ לְהוּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Levi happened** to come **to the house of Yosef the hunter. They served him the head of a peacock [*tavsa*] in milk** and **he did not eat. When** Levi **came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, the latter **said to him: Why did you not excommunicate** these people who eat poultry in milk, contrary to the decree of the Sages? Levi **said to him: It was** in **the locale** of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, and I said: Perhaps he taught them** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** who permits the eating of poultry meat in milk. Given the possibility that their rabbi rules that it is permitted, I cannot come and prohibit it, and I certainly cannot excommunicate them for it.

In the place of *Rabbi Yose HaGlili*, they would eat *basar of b'chalav* — fowl cooked with milk. *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* held that there was no biblical prohibition on fowl with milk, and moreover that there was not even a rabbinic prohibition — derived from the juxtaposition with *neveila* and the limiting phrase *b'chalev imo*, "in its mother's milk," which explicitly excludes fowl since fowl has no mother's milk. His community acted accordingly.

*Levi* once came to the house of *Yose Rishba* — *Yose* the hunter — and was served the head of a peacock cooked in milk. He did not eat it. When he later came before *Rabbi*, *Rabbi* asked him sharply: *amai lo tshamtemehu* — why did you not put him in *cherem*? He is violating a rabbinic prohibition. *Levi* responded: I recognized that this was the town of *Rabbi Yehuda ben Batera*, the local authority, and I concluded that perhaps *Rabbi Yehuda ben Batera* ruled like *Rabbi Yose HaGlili*. That is indeed the *baraisa*'s teaching: *Rabbi Yose HaGlili omer* — the verse says do not eat *neveila*, and elsewhere it says do not cook a kid in its mother's milk. *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* derives from the juxtaposition that the milk prohibition extends only to animals also subject to *neveila*, while fowl, though subject to *neveila*, is explicitly excluded by *imo* — "its mother's milk" — since fowl has none. He understands this exclusion as total, dispensing with even the rabbinic enactment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תֹאכְלוּ כׇל נְבֵלָה״, וְנֶאֱמַר ״לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״. אֶת שֶׁאָסוּר מִשּׁוּם נְבֵלָה — אָסוּר לְבַשֵּׁל בְּחָלָב. עוֹף שֶׁאָסוּר מִשּׁוּם נְבֵלָה, יָכוֹל יְהֵא אָסוּר לְבַשֵּׁל בְּחָלָב — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״, יָצָא עוֹף שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חֲלֵב אֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna, **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: It is stated** in the verse: **"You shall not eat anything that dies of itself;** to the stranger at your gates you may give it, that he may eat it; or you may sell it to a foreigner; for you are a holy people to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 14:21), **and it is stated** later in the same verse: **"You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk."** From the juxtaposition of the two issues it is derived: **That which is prohibited due to** the prohibition against eating an **unslaughtered animal, it is prohibited to cook** it **in milk.** The prohibition against cooking a creature in milk is not limited to only a kid. If so, with regard to **poultry, which is prohibited due to** the prohibition against eating an **unslaughtered animal,** I **might** have thought **it should be prohibited to cook** it **in milk; therefore, the verse states: "In its mother's milk."** This **excludes poultry, which does not have mother's milk** and is therefore not included in the prohibition.

### Reward for Devotion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: עִיר אַחַת הָיְתָה בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹשִׂין כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְהָיוּ מֵתִים בִּזְמַנָּן. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁפַּעַם אַחַת גָּזְרָה מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה גְּזֵרָה עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל הַמִּילָה, וְעַל אוֹתָהּ הָעִיר לָא גָּזְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: There was one city in Eretz Yisrael where they would act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer** with regard to circumcision, **and they would die at their appointed time** and not earlier, as a reward for their affection for this mitzva. **And not only that, but on one occasion the wicked empire,** Rome, **issued a decree against the Jewish people** prohibiting **circumcision; but against that city it did not** issue the **decree.**

*Rabbi Yitzchak* then reports that the community that followed *Rabbi Eliezer*'s ruling on *brit mila*, and showed such tremendous devotion to this *mitzvah*, was rewarded: all its members lived to a ripe old age, and moreover, when Rome issued its decree prohibiting *brit mila*, that community was somehow spared from the *gezeirah*. Rather than treating *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position as a dangerous aberration to be censured, *Chazal* pointedly praise the devotion it represented and note its reward — a remarkable statement that might seem counterintuitive given how firmly the majority rules against *Rabbi Eliezer* on this matter.

## Joy vs. Displeasure

### Embraced Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם בְּשִׂמְחָה, כְּגוֹן מִילָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״שָׂשׂ אָנֹכִי עַל אִמְרָתֶךָ כְּמוֹצֵא שָׁלָל רָב״ — עֲדַיִין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָהּ בְּשִׂמְחָה. וְכׇל מִצְוָה שֶׁקִּבְּלוּ עֲלֵיהֶם בִּקְטָטָה, כְּגוֹן עֲרָיוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁמַע מֹשֶׁה אֶת הָעָם בּוֹכֶה לְמִשְׁפְּחוֹתָיו״, עַל עִסְקֵי מִשְׁפְּחוֹתָיו — עֲדַיִין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָהּ בִּקְטָטָה, דְּלֵיכָּא כְּתוּבָּה דְּלָא רָמוּ בַּהּ תִּיגְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos affection for the mitzva of circumcision, the Gemara cites a *baraita* in which **it was taught** that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Every mitzva that** the Jews initially **accepted upon themselves with joy, such as circumcision, as it is written: "I rejoice at Your word as one who finds great spoil"** (Psalms 119:162), and as the Sages explained, this "word" refers to the mitzva of circumcision, over which they rejoiced, **they still perform** it **with joy. And every mitzva that** the Jews initially **accepted upon themselves with contentiousness** and regret, **such as** the prohibition against **incestuous relations, as it is written: "And Moses heard the people weeping, family by family"** (Numbers 11:10), and as the Sages interpreted homiletically: They wept **over matters** pertaining to **their families,** as they were prohibited at that time from marrying family members, **they still perform with contentiousness.** The fact is **that there is no marriage contract** and wedding **in which contentiousness does not arise,** as there is inevitably some conflict between the parties. The *baraita* asserts that this is because, initially, the Jews did not accept the laws governing marriage and family relationships willingly.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* now formulates a broader principle: *kol mitzvah she-kiblu aleihem be-simcha, kegon mila* — any *mitzvah* that the Jewish people originally accepted with joy, such as *mila*, is still performed with joy today. He derives this from the *pasuk* cited at every *brit mila*: *sas anochi al imratecha ke-motze shalal rav* — I rejoice over Your word like one who finds great treasure. *Rashi* identifies "Your word" (singular) as *brit mila*, the singular command Avraham received. *Tosafos* adds that the *perek* in *Tehillim* to which this belongs is *lamnatzeach al ha-sheminit* — the psalm oriented toward the eighth day, directly evoking the eighth-day *mila*. *Tosafos* further cites the *derasha* that *Avraham* made a great feast on the day *Yitzchak* was *higamel* — the word *higamel* being read as *hey-gimel-mem-lamed*, where *hey* and *gimel* equal eight and *mal* means circumcised, encoding the eighth-day *brit mila* in the very word. The point is that joy has accompanied *brit mila* from the outset, and it continues to be marked with a great *seudah*.

### Disputed Mitzvot

By contrast: *kol mitzvah she-kiblu aleihem be-katata* — any *mitzvah* originally received with dispute and displeasure, *kegon arayos* — like the prohibitions of forbidden sexual relations. The *pasuk* records: *Vayishma Moshe et ha-am boche le-mishpechotav* — Moshe heard the people weeping according to their families. The *derasha* is *al iskei mishpechotav* — they were weeping over the newly imposed restrictions within the family, the prohibitions of incest and the related *arayos*. And so those *mitzvos* — *adayin osin osah be-katata* — remain fraught: there is hardly a *kesuvah* written without some attendant dispute about its terms, some measure of contention clinging to the whole domain.

### Martyrdom Mitzvot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מִצְוָה שֶׁמָּסְרוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַצְמָן עֲלֵיהֶם לְמִיתָה בִּשְׁעַת גְּזֵרַת הַמַּלְכוּת, כְּגוֹן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וּמִילָה — עֲדַיִין הִיא מוּחְזֶקֶת בְּיָדָם, וְכׇל מִצְוָה שֶׁלֹּא מָסְרוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל עַצְמָן עָלֶיהָ לְמִיתָה בִּשְׁעַת גְּזֵרַת הַמַּלְכוּת, כְּגוֹן תְּפִילִּין — עֲדַיִין הִיא מְרוּפָּה בְּיָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in** a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says** in praise of the observance of the mitzva of circumcision: **Any mitzva for which the Jews sacrificed their lives at the time of the decrees of the** wicked **empire, such as** the prohibition of **idolatry and** the mitzva of **circumcision, is still steadfastly observed. And any mitzva for which the Jews did not sacrifice their lives at the time of the decrees of the** wicked **empire, such as phylacteries, is still casually observed,** meaning that they are not as careful in its fulfillment as they should be.

*Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* articulates a further principle: any *mitzvah* for which the Jewish people were willing to give their lives during times of governmental persecution — *kegon avodat kochavim u-mila* — like the rejection of idolatry and the practice of *brit mila* (this almost certainly reflects the Chanukah period and similar decrees) — *adayin hi mukhzeket be-yadam*, it remains strong and firm in their hands. By contrast, any *mitzvah* for which Jews did not demonstrate that willingness to die, *kegon tefillin*, is *adayin hi merufeh be-yadam* — weak in their hands. It is somewhat surprising to see *tefillin* named here, given how central it appears among male observant practice. Yet the *Gemara* proceeds to explain in a way that illuminates the weakness.

### Tefillin Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: תְּפִילִּין צְרִיכִין גּוּף נָקִי כֶּאֱלִישָׁע בַּעַל כְּנָפַיִם. מַאי הִיא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שֶׁלֹּא יָפִיחַ בָּהֶם, רָבָא אָמַר: שֶׁלֹּא יִישַׁן בָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites proof that the mitzva of phylacteries was not fulfilled properly at the time of the decrees, based upon an incident related to the following *halakha*. **As Rabbi Yannai said:** Donning **phylacteries requires a clean body like** that of **Elisha, Man of Wings. What is** included in the requirement to have a clean body? **Abaye said: That one may not pass wind in them. Rava said: That one may not sleep in them.**

*Rabbi Yannai* states: *tefillin tzrichin guf naki k'Elisha Ba'al Knafayim* — *tefillin* require a clean body like *Elisha Ba'al Knafayim*. *Abaye* explains *guf naki* as *she-lo yafiach bahem* — one may not pass gas while wearing *tefillin*. *Rava* adds: *she-lo yishan bahem* — one may not even sleep in them, out of concern that gas may pass during sleep. The implication is that maintaining this standard throughout the hours of wearing *tefillin* is genuinely demanding, and that historically many did not meet the standard consistently. *Tosafos* attests that even in their own era, *tefillin* was not being worn as diligently as it should have been. Whether because of the *guf naki* requirement or simply through laxity in practice, the observation that *tefillin* is *merufeh* — weakened in Israel's hands — carries historical weight.

### Elisha Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי קָרוּ לֵיהּ ״אֱלִישָׁע בַּעַל כְּנָפַיִם״? שֶׁפַּעַם אַחַת גָּזְרָה מַלְכוּת הָרְשָׁעָה גְּזֵרָה עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁכׇּל הַמַּנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין עַל רֹאשׁוֹ — יִקְּרוּ אֶת מוֹחוֹ, וְהָיָה אֱלִישָׁע מַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין וְיָצָא לַשּׁוּק, וְרָאָהוּ קַסְדּוֹר אֶחָד. רָץ מִלְּפָנָיו וְרָץ אַחֲרָיו. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ אֶצְלוֹ, נְטָלָן מֵרֹאשׁוֹ וַאֲחָזָן בְּיָדוֹ. אָמַר לוֹ: מַה בְּיָדְךָ? אָמַר לוֹ: כַּנְפֵי יוֹנָה. פָּשַׁט אֶת יָדוֹ וְנִמְצְאוּ בָּהּ כַּנְפֵי יוֹנָה. לְפִיכָךְ הָיוּ קוֹרְאִין אוֹתוֹ ״בַּעַל כְּנָפַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why did they call him Elisha, Man of Wings? Because on one occasion the wicked empire** of Rome **issued a decree against the Jewish people that,** as punishment, **they would pierce the brain of anyone who dons phylacteries on his head.** Nevertheless, **Elisha would don them and** defiantly **go out to the marketplace.** One day, **an official** who was appointed to enforce the decree **saw him.** Elisha **ran** away **from him, and** the official **ran after him. When** the official **reached him,** Elisha **removed** the phylacteries **from his head and held them in his hand.** The officer **asked him: What is in your hand?** Elisha **said to him:** It is merely **a dove's wings.** A miracle took place: **He opened his hand, and,** indeed, it **was found** to be **a dove's wings. Therefore,** in commemoration of this miracle, **they** would **call him** Elisha, **Man of Wings.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא כַּנְפֵי יוֹנָה דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁאָר עוֹפוֹת? מִשּׁוּם דְּדָמְיָא כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְיוֹנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כַּנְפֵי יוֹנָה נֶחְפָּה בַכֶּסֶף וְאֶבְרוֹתֶיהָ בִּירַקְרַק חָרוּץ״ — מַה יּוֹנָה זוֹ כְּנָפֶיהָ מְגִינּוֹת עָלֶיהָ, אַף יִשְׂרָאֵל מִצְוֹת מְגִינּוֹת עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about **a dove's wings,** that Elisha specifically **told him** that he was holding the wings of a dove **and he did not tell him** he was holding the wings of **other birds?** The Gemara answers: **Because the congregation of Israel is likened to a dove, as it is stated:** "You shall shine as **the wings of a dove covered with silver and her pinions with yellow gold"** (Psalms 68:14). **Just as a dove** has only **its wings to protect it, so too, the Jewish people have** only **mitzvot to protect them.** Apparently, Elisha Man of Wings was vigilant in fulfilling the mitzva of phylacteries in the face of the decree, whereas the rest of the people were not.

*Ve-amai karu lei Elisha Ba'al Knafayim* — why is he called the master of the wings? The story: *shepa'am achat gazra malchut ha-risha'ah* — once, the evil government of Rome issued a decree that anyone who places *tefillin* on his head would have his skull pierced. *Elisha* would nonetheless put on *tefillin* and go out into the marketplace, publicly, presumably to bolster the resolve of the Jewish community.

One Roman officer spotted him and gave chase. When the officer caught up, *Elisha* tore the *tefillin* from his own head and held them in his hand. "What is in your hand?" the officer demanded. "The wings of a dove," *Elisha* answered. He opened his hand — and dove's wings were found there.

*Mai shna kanfei yonah* — why a dove specifically, and not any other bird? *Mishum de-damya Knesses Yisrael le-yona* — because the Jewish people are compared to a dove: *kanfei yonah nechpah ba-kesef ve-evroteiha bi-yrakrak charutz* — the wings of a dove covered in silver and its pinions like fine gold. Just as a dove's wings protect it — *Rashi* notes that a dove relies on its wings more than its beak for defense — so too the *mitzvos* protect the Jewish people: *af Yisrael mitzvos meginot aleihem*.

*Tosafos* points out something arresting: even *Elisha* was not technically *moser nefesh* here, since rather than declaring "these are *tefillin*," he denied it and saved himself. One need not go out of one's way to be killed. Still, the fact that *Elisha* wore *tefillin* publicly in defiance of the decree demonstrates a degree of devotion that stands in contrast to the general laxity — and his public witness was precisely the point.

## Forgotten Knife Case

### Knife Transport

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר רַב אַדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: פַּעַם אַחַת שָׁכְחוּ וְלֹא הֵבִיאוּ אִיזְמֵל מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, וֶהֱבִיאוּהוּ בְּשַׁבָּת [דֶּרֶךְ גַּגּוֹת וְדֶרֶךְ חֲצֵירוֹת]
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Rav Adda said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said** as follows: **On one occasion** they were supposed to circumcise a baby on Shabbat, and **they forgot and did not bring a scalpel** with which to circumcise him **on Shabbat eve, and they brought it on Shabbat via roofs and via courtyards,**

*Rav Abba bar Rav Adda* reported in the name of *Rabbi Yitzchak*: there was once an incident in which they forgot to bring the *mila* knife from *erev Shabbos*, and brought it on *Shabbos* by way of the rooftops and courtyards — *derech gagot ve-derech chatzerot* — following *Rabbi Eliezer*'s general permission for carrying implements needed for *brit mila*.

### Eliezer Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **contrary to the wishes of Rabbi Eliezer.** Even though the roofs and courtyards were not halakhically joined in a manner where it would be permitted to carry from one to the other, the Rabbis permitted carrying the scalpel in this manner.

This was, however, done *shelo birtzon Rabbi Eliezer* — not in a manner that pleased *Rabbi Eliezer*. *Rabbi Eliezer* himself would have had them bring it directly through *reshut ha-rabbim*. His position, it emerges, permits the most direct route even if it involves a biblical violation, because the *mila*'s need effectively licenses whatever is necessary. The roundabout path through rooftops and courtyards — technically only rabbinic violations — was more restrictive than *Rabbi Eliezer* would have demanded.

### Quandary Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקִיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר! אַדְּרַבָּה, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הוּא דְּשָׁרֵי! וְכִי תֵּימָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּשָׁרֵי אֲפִילּוּ בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אֶלָּא בִּרְצוֹן רַבָּנַן דְּאָסְרִי דֶּרֶךְ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְשָׁרוּ דֶּרֶךְ גַּגּוֹת דֶּרֶךְ חֲצֵירוֹת וְקַרְפֵּיפוֹת — וּמִי שָׁרוּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין מְבִיאִין אוֹתוֹ דֶּרֶךְ רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, כָּךְ אֵין מְבִיאִין אוֹתוֹ לֹא דֶּרֶךְ גַּגּוֹת וְלֹא דֶּרֶךְ קַרְפֵּיפוֹת וְלֹא דֶּרֶךְ חֲצֵירוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef strongly objects to this: Contrary to the wishes of Rabbi Eliezer? On the contrary, it is Rabbi Eliezer who permits** this, as he permits carrying the scalpel even through the public domain. **And if you say** that it means **contrary to the wishes of Rabbi Eliezer, who permits** carrying **even in the public domain, but in accordance with the wishes of the Rabbis,** who **prohibited** carrying **via the public domain and** only **permitted** carrying **via roofs, via courtyards and enclosures,** that is also difficult. **And is it permitted** according to the opinion of the Rabbis? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Just as one may not bring** the circumcision knife **via the public domain, so too, one may not bring it via roofs, via enclosures, or via courtyards?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: שֶׁלֹּא בִּרְצוֹן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וּמַחְלוּקְתּוֹ, אֶלָּא בִּרְצוֹן רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד גַּגּוֹת וְאֶחָד קַרְפֵּיפוֹת וְאֶחָד חֲצֵירוֹת — כּוּלָּן רְשׁוּת אֶחָד הֵן לְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בְּתוֹכָן, וְלֹא לְכֵלִים שֶׁשָּׁבְתוּ בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** It means **contrary to the wishes of Rabbi Eliezer and his disputants, but in accordance with the wishes of Rabbi Shimon. As we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Shimon says: Roofs, enclosures, and courtyards are all** considered as **one domain with regard to vessels that rested inside them** at the beginning of Shabbat. Therefore, it is permitted to carry vessels that rested inside one to another. However, they are **not** considered the same domain **with regard to vessels that rested inside the house** at the beginning of Shabbat. If the homeowners did not join the courtyard by means of an *eiruv*, it is prohibited to carry vessels from their houses to the courtyard. Even if the houses in a courtyard were joined, it is prohibited to carry from the courtyard to an enclosure unless they were joined by means of an *eiruv*. In any case, there are circumstances in which Rabbi Shimon allows carrying via roofs, courtyards, and enclosures.

*Rav Yosef* challenges the formulation of the incident: if it was *shelo birtzon Rabbi Eliezer*, surely it was even more against the will of the *Chachamim*, who would not permit even rabbinic violations for something that could have been done before *Shabbos*. A *baraisa* makes this explicit: the *Chachamim* say that just as you cannot bring the knife through *reshut ha-rabbim*, so too you cannot bring it through rooftops or courtyards or fields. Even these rabbinic violations are not sanctioned when the task was omissible before *Shabbos*. So according to whom was the incident performed?

*Rav Ashi* resolves: *shelo birtzon Rabbi Eliezer u-machlokto* — it was not according to the will of *Rabbi Eliezer* or his disputants. Neither side was satisfied. Rather, *ela birtzon Rabbi Shimon* — it was consistent with the position of *Rabbi Shimon*.

### Shimon's Domain

For *Rabbi Shimon*, rooftops, enclosed fields, and courtyards — assuming they all have sufficient walls — are all a single domain: *kulan reshut achas hen l'keilim she-shavsu b'sochan* — they are all one domain for objects that began *Shabbos* there. If a vessel was in the courtyard or on the rooftop when *Shabbos* began, it may be carried freely among all such spaces without any *eiruv* between them, because *Rabbi Shimon* considers them a single *reshut ha-yachid*. The only need for an *eiruv* is to carry from inside a house into those spaces, or from those spaces back into a house. Something that began in those outdoor spaces can move among all of them freely.

The knife began *Shabbos* in one of those outdoor spaces, and so carrying it from one end of the courtyard complex or rooftops to the other was, according to *Rabbi Shimon*, not even a rabbinic violation. He was the one authority satisfied with what had occurred.

## Mavoi Carrying Rules

### Mavoi Without Shituf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא מֵרַבִּי אַסִּי: מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ, מַהוּ לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ? מִי אָמְרִינַן כֶּחָצֵר דָּמֵי: מָה חָצֵר, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא עֵרְבוּ — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ, הַאי נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ, אוֹ דִילְמָא: לָא דָּמֵי לֶחָצֵר, דְּחָצֵר אִית לֵיהּ אַרְבַּע מְחִיצּוֹת, הַאי לֵית לֵיהּ אַרְבַּע מְחִיצּוֹת. אִי נָמֵי: חָצֵר אִית בֵּיהּ דָּיוֹרִין, הַאי לֵית בֵּיהּ דָּיוֹרִין? שְׁתִיק וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raised a dilemma before Rabbi Asi: An alleyway** whose residents **did not merge** together, **what is** its legal status with regard **to carrying** items **in all of it** according to Rabbi Shimon? Rabbi Zeira explains the dilemma: **Do we say** that **it is like a courtyard,** and **just as** with regard to **a courtyard, even though they did not join** the houses in it together and despite the fact that it is prohibited to carry out items from the houses to the courtyard, **it is** nonetheless **permitted to carry in all of it?** Therefore, in **this** alleyway **too, even though they did not merge** together, **it is permitted to carry in all of it** despite the fact that it is prohibited to carry items into the alleyway. **Or, perhaps** an alleyway is **not similar** in this regard **to a courtyard,** as a **courtyard has four partitions,** whereas **this,** the alleyway, **does not have four partitions,** but only three. **Alternatively,** there may be a different reason for the inferior status of an alleyway in this regard: **A courtyard has residents** and can therefore be considered like a house, which would allow carrying within it, whereas **this** alleyway **does not have residents.** Rabbi Asi **was silent and did not say anything to him,** as he was unable to provide a satisfactory response.

This resolution prompts *Rav Zeira* to pose a question to *Rav Assi*: *mavoi shelo nishtatfu bo, mahu le-taltel be-chulo* — if an alleyway did not have a *shituf mavoi* established, and a vessel began *Shabbos* within it, may one carry that vessel throughout the alleyway?

To understand the question: the typical spatial configuration is houses opening into a courtyard, and multiple courtyards opening into a *mavoi*, an alleyway. The *mavoi* has three walls but lacks the fourth. To carry within the *mavoi*, all the courtyards must jointly establish a *shituf mavoi* — a partnership, typically through shared food — which binds them together as a unified domain. The question is: what if this *shituf* was not made? A *chatzer* without an *eiruv* among its houses still permits carrying within the *chatzer* for objects that began there — since it has four walls and serves as a *reshut ha-yachid* — but does the *mavoi* enjoy this same leniency?

Two factors might distinguish the *mavoi* unfavorably: it has only three walls (not four), and it lacks the intimacy of *diyurin* — residents directly opening onto it. People access the *mavoi* only through their courtyards, not directly from their homes, making it a less integrated space.

### Zeira-Assi Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִימְנִין אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּיָתֵיב וְקָא אָמַר, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא: פַּעַם אַחַת שָׁכְחוּ וְלֹא הֵבִיאוּ אִיזְמֵל מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, וֶהֱבִיאוּהוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְהָיָה הַדָּבָר קָשֶׁה לַחֲכָמִים: הֵיאַךְ מַנִּיחִין דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים וְעוֹשִׂין כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. חֲדָא: דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שַׁמּוּתִי הוּא! וְעוֹד, יָחִיד וְרַבִּים — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּים!
>
> **Translation:** **On another occasion** Rabbi Zeira **found** Rabbi Asi **sitting and saying: Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: One time they forgot and did not bring a scalpel on Shabbat eve** for the purpose of circumcising a child on Shabbat, **and they brought it on Shabbat, and the matter was difficult** in the eyes of **the Rabbis: How can they abandon the words of the Rabbis,** who prohibit doing so, **and act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer?** Why did they find this difficult? **One** reason was **that Rabbi Eliezer was a *Shammuti*,** i.e., a follower of the views of Beit Shammai (Jerusalem Talmud), and the *halakha* is generally in accordance with the opinion of Beit Hillel in their disputes with Beit Shammai. **And furthermore,** there is a general rule that in a dispute between **an individual and** the **many,** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **the many.** Here too, the *halakha* should certainly be in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, who disagree with Rabbi Eliezer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: שְׁאֵילִית אֶת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַגּוֹזֵר וְאָמַר לִי: מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ הֲוָה, וְאַיְיתוּהוּ מֵהַאי רֵישָׁא לְהַאי רֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Oshaya said: I asked Rabbi Yehuda the Cutter,** i.e., the circumcisor, **and he told me** that this incident occurred in **an alleyway** whose residents **did not merge** together, **and they brought** the scalpel **from this end** of the alleyway **to that end,** where the baby was. That concludes Rabbi Asi's account of the event. Rabbi Zeira **said to** Rabbi Asi: Since you related that story without expressing any reservations, it appears that **the Master** must **hold** that with regard to **an alleyway** whose residents **did not merge** together, **it is permitted to carry in all of it. And** Rabbi Asi **said to him: Yes,** that is the *halakha*. Rabbi Zeira **said to him: But on a** different **occasion I raised a dilemma** on this matter **before you and you did not say so to me. Perhaps in the course** of your studies **your knowledge was restored to you? He said to him: Yes, in the course** of my studies **my knowledge was restored to me,** and I remembered this *halakha*.

*Rav Zeira* then turned to *Rav Assi*: clearly, from your citation of this incident, you hold that one may carry throughout a *mavoi* that lacked a *shituf* if the object began there. *Rav Assi* confirmed: yes, that is his position. *Rav Zeira* was puzzled — he had asked this question many times and received no answer, and now here was the ruling embedded in a story about *brit mila*. *Rav Assi* replied: *dilma agav shitpach rahit lach gamrach* — perhaps in the rush of moving through your learning you happened across this teaching of *Reish Lakish* about *Rabbi Yehuda Nesia* and the *brit mila* incident, and through that you uncovered the *halacha*. *Rav Assi* acknowledged: yes, that is precisely what happened.

### Paradoxical Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the very same *halakha*, **it was stated** as a principle that **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav said: An alleyway that was not merged** by the residents of the courtyards that open into it, **it is only permitted to carry in it** within **four cubits.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָא מִילְּתָא אַמְרַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא וְלָא פֵּירְשַׁהּ עַד דַּאֲתָא רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ וּפֵירְשַׁהּ. דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ אָמַר רַב: מָבוֹי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בּוֹ, עֵירְבוּ חֲצֵירוֹת עִם בָּתִּים — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בּוֹ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. לֹא עֵירְבוּ חֲצֵירוֹת עִם בָּתִּים — מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This** halakhic **matter was stated by Rabbi Zeira, and he did not explain it.** It remained enigmatic **until Rabba bar Avuh came and explained it. As Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **an alleyway that was not merged** by the residents of the courtyards that open into it, if they **joined the courtyards with the houses,** i.e., the homeowners within each courtyard joined together and are therefore permitted to carry within the courtyards themselves, **it is only permitted to carry in it within four cubits,** as in an intermediate domain [*karmelit*]. However, if they **did not join the courtyards with the houses** and it is prohibited to carry within the courtyards, **it is permitted to carry in the entire** alleyway.

The *Gemara* closes with a ruling transmitted through *Rav Nachman* in the name of *Rabbah bar Avuha* in the name of *Rav*, which clarifies an apparent paradox:

If the courtyards all made their own internal *eiruvin* — joining each courtyard's houses together — then one *cannot* carry throughout the *mavoi* without a *shituf*. If the courtyards did *not* make *eiruvin* — meaning everything is "open" and unresolved — one *may* carry throughout the *mavoi*.

This is left as a striking paradox to contemplate, with the explanation deferred to the following day's *shiur*. The counterintuitive conclusion — that doing the right thing (making *eiruvin* in the courtyards) results in greater restriction in the *mavoi*, while neglecting to do so results in greater permissibility — inverts ordinary expectations and will require a fuller treatment of the underlying principles of *reshuyos* and *eiruvin* to resolve.
