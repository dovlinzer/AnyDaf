# Menachot 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dam Neveilah Tumah

### Dead Mule, Revi'it Blood

The daf opens by picking up from the very bottom of 103b, where we had just encountered an important statement of Reb Yehuda, who was ראש המדברים בכל מקום — Rosh Hamedabrim Bechol Makom — who held the right to speak first in any gathering, followed by Reb Shimon, concerning the sixty עשרונות and the principle that the rabbis' שיעורים must have firm cut-off points. A new story now follows, and we will see how it does or does not connect to what came before.

אמר רב ביבי — Rav Bibi said, citing Reb Yehoshua ben Levi: there was a case involving a mule from Rebbi's household that died — מעשה בפרדה אחת שמתה — and the Sages measured its blood and found that it contained a רביעית of blood. A רביעית is roughly three to three and a half ounces, the same minimum measure used for a כוס of קידוש. Presumably this was a very young mule.

The reason the Sages measured it was as follows. A dead animal's carcass communicates טומאת נבילה — impurity — and the assumption is that this would apply not only to the meat but to the blood as well. The operative principle is that the relevant quantity of blood is the amount which, were it to congeal, would equal the size of a כזית — an olive's bulk — because a כזית of flesh is the minimum quantity of an animal's body that communicates impurity. A רביעית of liquid blood, when congealed, becomes approximately a כזית of solid matter. That is why the Sages measured the blood: to determine whether a רביעית was present and whether it would therefore communicate impurity.

### Eduyot: Blood is Pure

מתיבי רב יצחק בר ביסנא — Reb Yitzchak bar Bisna raised an objection from Mishnah Eduyot. The Mishnah states: העיד רבי יהושע ורבי יהושע בן בתירא על דם נבילה שהוא טהור — Reb Yehoshua and Reb Yehoshua ben Beteira testified that the blood of a נבילה is pure. According to their testimony, no quantity of blood from a carcass communicates impurity; only the flesh does so. This stands in direct tension with the story of Rebbi's mule, where the blood was measured precisely to assess its impurity.

### Lions Stadium Story

Reb Yehoshua ben Beteira's testimony in Mishnah Eduyot was not merely a theoretical ruling — he supported it with an account of actual practice. מעשה והיו נוחרים ארדיות לאריות באסטרייא — there was a case where animals were slaughtered, through נחירה rather than through שחיטה, for the lions in the stadium — אסטרייא likely referring to the Roman amphitheater in the land of Israel. והיו עולי רגלים שוקעים עד ארכובותיהם בדם ולא אמרו להם דבר — and when pilgrims would come up for one of the three major festivals, they would be wading up to their knees in the blood of these carcasses, and no one ever said anything to them about impurity. Had dam neveilah communicated טומאה, someone would certainly have objected — these pilgrims were coming to the Temple. The fact that no objection was raised is compelling evidence that the blood was considered pure.

One may also wonder what precisely these pilgrims were doing in the vicinity of the stadium, but apparently visiting such attractions was part of the culture of the city during the festival period.

### Rav Bibi's Poverty

אישתיק — Rav Bibi fell silent. He had no response. Rav Zerika then asked him: מאי טעמא לא קמהדר מר — why did you not give an answer? Rav Bibi replied: היכי אהדר ליה — how could I be expected to respond?

His explanation is striking. He cited a teaching of Reb Chanina, who interprets the verse in the curses of Devarim: והיו חייך תלוים לך מנגד — your life will hang suspended before you. Reb Chanina explains that this refers to someone who must purchase grain from year to year rather than growing his own. As Rashi notes, the verse says לוקח — one who purchases — specifically indicating someone without land, for a landowner has a sense of security in his ability to provide for himself, while a purchaser does not, even if he has enough money this year. The verse continues: ופחדת לילה ויומם — you will fear day and night. This, explains Reb Chanina, refers to someone who can only purchase grain from week to week, anxious each day whether he will have the money to buy next week's supply. Finally, the verse concludes: ולא תאמין בחייך — you will have no faith in your own life — meaning someone who relies entirely on the baker's daily charity for his bread, not knowing each day where his next meal will come from.

Rav Bibi then said: and that man — meaning himself — על פלטר סמך, I rely on the baker every day. He had absolutely no livelihood, no security, and lacked the ישוב הדעת — the presence of mind — to formulate an adequate response to the challenge.

### Beit Hillel: Blood Tamei

מאי הוי עלה — so what is the resolution? Two traditions stood in conflict: one in which the blood of a carcass communicates טומאה, and one in which it does not.

אמר רב יוסף — Rav Yosef resolved the matter. רבי יהודה מוריינא דבי נשיא הוה — Reb Yehuda served as the halachic authority in the house of the Nasi. Rashi explains that מוריינא means he served as the posek for Rebbi's household. ואורי ליה כשמעתיה — and he ruled for Rebbi's household according to his own teaching. It was therefore not Rebbi himself who determined that the blood was impure; it was Reb Yehuda ruling for Rebbi's household in accordance with his own position.

What is Reb Yehuda's position? The Gemara cites a Mishnah: רבי יהודה אומר ששה דברים מקולי בית שמאי ומחומרי בית הלל — Reb Yehuda says that there are six matters in which Beit Shammai is lenient and Beit Hillel is strict, the reverse of the usual pattern. One of those six is דם נבילה. Beit Shammai holds that dam neveilah does not communicate טומאה — only the flesh does. Beit Hillel holds that it does communicate טומאה. Since we rule like Beit Hillel even when they are strict, dam neveilah is tamei. Reb Yehuda ruled accordingly for Rebbi's household when measuring the blood of the mule.

The story in Mishnah Eduyot, with the pilgrims wading through the blood at the stadium, reflects the opposing position — that dam neveilah is pure — and thus represents a different opinion.

### Revi'it Congeals Kezayit

The Gemara adds a further nuance. אמר רבי יוסי ברבי יהודה — Reb Yosi the son of Reb Yehuda said: אף כשטימאו בית הלל לא טימאו אלא ברביעית — even when Beit Hillel ruled that dam neveilah is tamei, they only ruled so for a quantity of a רביעית, הואיל ויכול להקריש ולעמוד על כזית — because that is the amount of blood that can congeal to the size of a כזית. The logic is that blood does not communicate טומאה as blood in its own right; rather, it communicates טומאה as a form of meat — as an extension of the category of flesh. The threshold for flesh is a כזית, a solid measure. The liquid measure of a רביעית is therefore the operative standard for blood, because that is precisely the volume of blood whose congealed form equals a כזית.

### Shiurim Connection

Rashi explains that this passage appears here because the end of yesterday's daf involved a broad discussion of the rabbis' שיעורים — the precise measurements they established for various halachic categories — and this story presents a closely related issue about how blood is measured against the standard of meat. Tosafot adds a further connection: yesterday we noted that Reb Yehuda was ראש המדברים בכל מקום, and today we learn that he also served as the halachic authority in Rebbi's household — perhaps indicating a relationship between his precedence in public discourse and the role he played in that domestic context.

## Nesachim Quantities

### Mishnah: Log Limits

The Mishnah now before us teaches: אין מתנדבים לוג שנים וחמשה אבל מתנדבים שלשה וארבעה וששה ומששה ולמעלה — one may not donate two or five לוג of wine, but one may donate three, four, or six לוג, or any quantity above six.

### Wine Denominations

The framework of this Mishnah presupposes that the wine may be brought as a separate, independent sacrifice — a נדבה of wine — even when not accompanying an animal sacrifice. This category is called מנחת נסכים, the libation offering. The Mishnah's permitted quantities are derived from the wine that accompanied animal sacrifices, since that is the only model available. The quantities of wine brought with the various animals are stated in the מוסף prayer: חצי ההין לפר — half a הין for a bull — שלישית ההין לאיל — a third of a הין for a ram — and רביעית ההין לכבש — a quarter of a הין for a lamb.

A הין consists of twelve לוג. Half of twelve is six לוג for a bull, a third of twelve is four לוג for a ram, and a quarter of twelve is three לוג for a lamb. Accordingly, the valid denominations for an independent wine donation are three, four, and six. One לוג is excluded because it falls below the minimum of three. Two לוג is excluded for the same reason. Five is excluded because it is not itself one of the three denominations. As for quantities above six: seven can be composed of three and four; eight of four and four; nine of six and three; ten of six and four — and so on. Any whole number above six can be composed of some combination of three, four, and six. That is the basis for the Mishnah's ruling that from six and above all quantities are permitted.

### Keva L'Nesachim?

איבעיא להו — the Gemara raises a question: יש קבע לנסכים או אין קבע לנסכים — is there a fixed unit for nesachim donations, or is there no fixed unit?

The practical stakes of this question are illustrated through a concrete case. Suppose one has sanctified five לוג of wine. The Mishnah rules that five is an impermissible quantity. What is to be done? If there is no fixed unit — אין קבע לנסכים — then the five לוג need not be offered as a single unit. One could extract four לוג from the five, offer those four לוג as the denomination appropriate for a ram, and the remaining one לוג would be treated as a separate donation — given to another individual who needs an extra לוג to complete his own sacrifice. If, however, there is a fixed unit — יש קבע לנסכים — then the five cannot be subdivided. One would be obligated to add a sixth לוג and offer all six together as the denomination for a bull.

### Six Shofarot Boxes

אמר אביי — Abaye proposed a proof from the institution of the Temple collection boxes. In the Temple there were thirteen essentially tzedakah-style boxes into which people would deposit leftover funds from various types of sacrifices — money that had been sanctified for an obligation, partially used, and left over with no further application to the original purpose. These funds would be drawn upon on slow days at the altar to purchase communal burnt offerings, maintaining the appearance of an active Temple service.

Of these thirteen boxes, six were designated לנדבה — for free-will donations. The Mishnah asks: וכנגד מי — what do these six represent? Each of the six corresponds to a distinct category of leftover obligatory funds: מותר חטאת — money left over from a sin offering; ומותר אשם — from a guilt offering; ומותר אשם נזיר — from the guilt offering a Nazirite brings at the conclusion of his period of nezirut, which is not an atonement offering but a rite of termination; ומותר אשם מצורע — from the guilt offering of a person with tzaraat who underwent a process of purification; ומותר קינים — from bird sacrifices; and ומותר מנחת חוטא — from a sin-mincha offering.

The common denominator among all six categories is that the funds had been designated for an obligatory sacrifice of a specific type. Once that obligation was discharged, the leftover money could not be redirected toward fulfilling the same obligation, but it also had no other designated use, and so it was transferred to these Temple funds and used to sustain the altar's activity.

Abaye's argument runs as follows: if it were true that leftover wine from nesachim donations created a distinct and commonly occurring scenario — specifically, if one who donated five לוג could not subdivide them and instead had an unavoidable remainder — then we would have expected a seventh שופר box dedicated to מותר נסכים. The absence of such a box implies that the leftover-wine scenario does not commonly arise as a distinct category, which would be explained if the wine could always be subdivided and redistributed without remainder.

### Leftover Wine Pooled

The Gemara rejects Abaye's proof. הנך לנדבת ציבור אזלי — those boxes and their funds are used for communal burnt offerings. הני שכיחי — those other categories are common in the sense that the leftover money genuinely has nowhere else to go. But leftover wine is different: אפשר דמצטרפי דמר ודמר בהדי הדדי — it is easily possible for the leftover wine of one person to be combined with the need of another. Since almost every animal sacrifice requires wine as part of its nesachim, a person who has slightly less wine than he needs can readily receive the leftover לוג from someone else. The leftover wine need never become a stranded surplus at all; it is instantly reabsorbed into the system. There was therefore no need for a dedicated שופר box for this category, and Abaye's proof from the absence of such a box is inconclusive. וקרבי — and it would simply be offered as part of someone else's sacrifice.

### Ezrach: Independent Wine

אמר רבא — Rava offered a different approach, now seeking a proof from the scriptural basis for the very institution of independent wine donations. The verses in the Torah regarding nesachim deploy the word אזרח — citizen — which is an unusual term in this context and is therefore read as expanding the scope of the law. What does it expand to teach? מלמד שמתנדבין נסכים — it teaches that one may donate wine as an independent sacrifice. וכמה — and how much? שלושה לוגין — the minimum is three לוג, which is the smallest denomination that accompanies any animal sacrifice.

The verse also contains the word יהיה — shall be — which broadens the law further. ומניין שאם רוצה להוסיף יוסיף — how do I know that one may add more than three? From that word. And the word כך — thus or in this manner — excludes the possibility of bringing less than three: יכול יפחות — one might have thought one could bring less than three לוג — תלמוד לומר ככה — the Torah says no, it must be this way. From these words we derive all the principles of the Mishnah: wine may be given as an independent sacrifice, but only in the prescribed denominations and not below the minimum of three לוג.

### Yosef = Five Log

The Gemara now presses the analysis of the word יוסיף in the verse — "one may add." מאי יוסיף — what does "may add" mean? If it means that one may donate the other standard denominations alongside three — namely four and six לוג — that would be unnecessary, since those denominations are already established by the same principle that permits three לוג. Just as three is permitted because it is the denomination for a sheep, four and six are permitted on the same basis for a ram and a bull respectively. The word יוסיף therefore cannot be teaching those standard units.

אלא לאו — rather, it must mean something beyond the standard denominations. Since the Mishnah specifies that five is forbidden, the word יוסיף must be teaching that one can bring five. But if five is permitted, how does one offer five? Only by extracting four from the five and using the remaining one לוג in some other way. ושמע מינה — this implies אין קבע לנסכים — there is no fixed unit, and therefore a five-לוג donation can be subdivided and partially offered.

### Rav Ashi: Five = Two

אמר רב אשי — Rav Ashi objected. ואנן לא תנן הכי — this does not accord with our Mishnah. The Mishnah states: אין מתנדבין לוג שנים וחמשה — one may not donate two or five לוג. קתני חמשה דומיא דשנים — by listing five alongside two, the Mishnah implies that the problem with five is analogous to the problem with two. מה שנים דלא חזו כלל — just as two לוג cannot be brought to the altar at all because the minimum denomination is three, אף חמשה נמי דלא חזו כלל — so too five לוג cannot be brought at all. One must wait until a sixth לוג is added to reach the denomination of six. This would suggest that five is entirely unusable as offered, implying יש קבע — there is a fixed unit.

### Different Grounds

The Gemara dismisses Rav Ashi's objection. מידי איריא — the parallel does not hold. הכי איתא והכי איתא — each case operates on its own terms. Two and five are both listed as forbidden, but they are forbidden for entirely different reasons. Two לוג simply do not reach the minimum; there is no way to offer any part of a two-לוג donation. Five לוג, however, may well be subtractable: perhaps four can be extracted and offered, with the one remainder handled separately. Listing them together in the same sentence does not mean they share the same legal mechanism. The proof from the Mishnah's grouping therefore fails.

### Eleven Log Problem

אמר אביי — Abaye now turns the question in a different direction. אם תמצא לומר אין קבע לנסכים — if one concludes there is no fixed unit, then the question is entirely resolved and no further difficulties arise. But אם תמצא לומר יש קבע לנסכים — if one concludes that there is a fixed unit and a donor must offer his entire vowed quantity without subdivision, a new question emerges.

עד עשרה פשיטא לי — for any vowed quantity up to ten לוג, the answer is obvious. Seven can be composed of three and four; eight of four and four; nine of six and three; ten of six and four. All of these are clean combinations of the standard denominations. But חד עשר מיבעיא לי — eleven לוג presents a difficulty. Can eleven be composed of the standard denominations? Four, four, and three — that is indeed eleven — but this is not a clean two-unit combination; it requires two units of one denomination and one of another. Is such a combination valid?

One possibility is that the person who vowed eleven was mentally aiming for twelve — two sixes, the denomination for two bulls — and simply fell short. In that case, גברא לשני פרים קמיכוין — he had two bulls in mind — ועד דמלא להו לא קרבי — and until the full twelve לוג are assembled, nothing may be offered. The other possibility is that he intended two rams and one sheep — two fours and a three — which total eleven exactly. Is it valid to interpret a vow as combining two of one denomination with one of another? תרי מחד מינא וחד מחד מינא אמרינן או לא — do we say such a combination is acceptable, or is the asymmetry of the combination problematic?

### Teiku: Eleven Log

תיקו — the question is left unresolved. The underlying question of whether יש קבע לנסכים or אין קבע לנסכים itself remains open, and this specific application — the eleven-לוג vow — is likewise left without an answer.

## Oil Donation Debate

### Akiva vs. Tarfon: Oil

The Mishnah now addresses the question of oil. We have established that wine may be donated independently as a free-will sacrifice. The question arises whether the same is true of oil alone. Both oil and wine accompany animal sacrifices — with the animal come both flour mixed with oil and a separate measure of wine. May one donate just oil, as one may donate just wine?

מתנדבין יין ואין מתנדבין שמן — one may donate wine as a free-will sacrifice but not oil, דברי רבי עקיבא — this is the position of Rabbi Akiva. רבי טרפון אומר מתנדבין שמן — Rabbi Tarfon says one may donate oil independently.

### Tarfon: Oil Like Wine

Rabbi Tarfon's argument by analogy: מה מצינו ביין שהוא בא בחובה ובא בנדבה — just as we find that wine comes as an obligation when accompanying an animal sacrifice and also comes as a voluntary free-will offering, אף שמן — so too oil: just as wine can come independently, oil should be able to come independently as well.

### Akiva: Oil Not Alone

Rabbi Akiva rejects the analogy. אם אמרת ביין שכן קרב עם חובתו בפני עצמו — wine, when it accompanies the obligatory animal sacrifice, is brought as a discrete unit in its own right. It is a separate measure of wine, standing alone. That is why wine can also be given as an independent free-will offering — there is a model of wine standing alone. תאמר בשמן שאינו קרב עם חובתו בפני עצמו — but can you say the same of oil? Oil never comes by itself even when it accompanies an obligatory sacrifice. It always comes as a flour-and-oil mixture, a מנחה. There is no model of offering pure oil independently. Since oil never comes alone even in its obligatory form, there is no basis for saying it may come alone as a voluntary donation.

### Minchat Nesachim Daily

אמר רבא — Rava draws an inference from the very structure of the debate. מדברי שניהם — from the positions of both Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon — נלמוד — we can infer — מתנדב אדם מנחת נסכים בכל יום — that a person may donate minchat nesachim, the flour-and-oil mixture that accompanies animal sacrifices, as an independent free-will sacrifice at any time.

The reasoning is as follows. Rabbi Akiva's very argument against oil turns on the fact that oil does not come alone — it always comes as a flour-and-oil mixture. This implicitly concedes that the flour-and-oil mixture itself may be donated independently. And Rabbi Tarfon, who permits oil alone, would certainly permit the mixture. So both opinions, from their respective angles, imply that minchat nesachim can be brought independently.

One might object: of course minchat nesachim can be donated, since a מנחה can generally be given as a free-will offering. What is new here? The answer is that there is a difference. The מנחות listed in ויקרא have a specific ratio of flour to oil. The minchat nesachim uses a different ratio — the ratio that accompanies the animal sacrifices. One might have thought that only the מנחות explicitly catalogued in the Torah may be brought independently, and that the nesachim ratio is not among them.

### Explicit Nesachim Mincha

פשיטא — the Gemara initially reacts: is this not obvious? The response is that it is not. מהו דתימא — one might have argued — מנחת נדבה גלי בה רחמנא — the Torah made explicit which מנחות may be donated voluntarily — הני חמשה מנחות — those five types of מנחה prepared by different methods listed in ויקרא — טפי לא — and no others. Wine, the Torah never discussed as an independent sacrifice, and so we derive that permission through the drasha on אזרח. But מנחה — the Torah did discuss explicitly — and the listed five might be exhaustive. One might then have said that the flour-and-oil mixture in the nesachim ratio falls outside this list and therefore cannot be donated independently.

קא משמע לן — therefore this teaches us: הני מילי בסתמא — this exclusion applies only when a person donates a מנחה without specifying what type. In that case, we assume he means one of the five established types. אבל היכא דפריש פריש — but if he explicitly specifies that he wishes to donate a מנחה according to the nesachim ratio — the ratio that accompanies animal sacrifices — he may do so.

## Nefesh: No Mincha Partner

### Mishnah: No Joint Mincha

The פרק concludes with a ruling that closes the Mishnah: אין שנים מתנדבין עישרון אחד — two people may not jointly donate a single עישרון, the minimum measure of a מנחה, as a partnership offering. אבל מתנדבין עולה ושלמים — they may, however, jointly donate a free-will עולה or שלמים. ועוף — they may even jointly bring a bird as an עולה, אפילו פרידה אחת — even a single bird, though normally birds come in pairs in the Torah. Any animal sacrifice, however small, may be jointly donated. A מנחה may not.

### Nefesh = Individual

The Gemara asks: מאי טעמא — what is the reason? The initial proposal is that the Torah uses the singular form תקריב — you shall bring — when speaking of the מנחה, implying that only an individual may bring it. But this proposal fails because עולה also uses the singular יקריב. The response is that for עולה, another verse uses the plural לעולותיכם — for your עולות — indicating that multiple people may be jointly involved. If so, the same argument should apply to מנחה, since a similar verse uses the plural למנחותיכם — for your מנחות. There is no difference in grammatical structure between the two.

אלא משום דכתיב ביה נפש — rather, the reason is the specific word used in the מנחה passage. When the Torah introduces animal sacrifices it says אדם כי יקריב מכם קרבן — a person who offers a sacrifice. But when it introduces the מנחה it says ונפש כי תקריב — and if a נפש — a soul — brings a מנחה. This shift from person to soul carries halachic weight: the נפש implies a single individual, reflecting the utter individuality and uniqueness of a soul.

### Baraita: Mincha Alone

תניא נמי הכי — a Baraita confirms this ruling. The verse says that one will bring his sacrifice for all of their vows and free-will offerings that they bring to God, moving from the singular to the plural. הכל באים בשותפות — this teaches that all sacrifices may come in partnership. לא סילקה הכתוב אלא מנחה שנאמר נפש — the Torah excludes only the מנחה, because it uses the word נפש — a soul — requiring that the מנחה come from a single individual.

### Poor Person's Nefesh

אמר רבי יצחק — Rabbi Yitzchak asks: מפני מה נשתנית מנחה שנאמר בה נפש — why is it specifically the מנחה for which the Torah uses the word נפש? The answer lies in asking: מי דרכו להביא מנחה — who is it that typically brings a מנחה rather than an animal sacrifice? The answer is evident: עני — a poor person who cannot afford an animal. For a poor person, the flour-and-oil offering is not a diminished substitute but the fullest expression of what he can give. Therefore, says Rabbi Yitzchak, God declares: מעלה אני עליו כאילו הקריב נפשו לפני — I regard it as though he has offered his very soul before Me.

This is in fact one of the places in the Talmudic literature where the principle of אחד הממעיט ואחד המרבה ובלבד שיכוון לבו לשמים is invoked — it does not matter whether one does much or little, what matters is that one's heart is directed toward heaven.

### Five Preparations Parable

אמר רבי יצחק — Rabbi Yitzchak continues: מה נשתנית מנחה שנאמר בה חמשה מיני טיגון הללו — why does the Torah describe the מנחה in five different forms of preparation? One may be baked, one pan-fried, one deep-fried — all from the same flour and oil. Why this elaborate variety for what is, in objective terms, a simple inexpensive offering?

משל למלך בשר ודם שעשו לו אוהבו סעודה — it is like a king whose close friend prepared a meal for him. ויודע בו שהוא עני — but the king knew his friend was poor and could not provide extravagant delicacies. So the king said to him: אמר לו עשה לי מן חמשה מיני טיגון כדי שאהנה ממך — please prepare for me five different types of pastries and confections from what you have. Not because the king needed the food, but because this gave the poor friend the fullest possible opportunity to express love and honor for the king within the constraints of his means — to do everything he was capable of doing, and to do it with care and variety. The five methods of preparation are the Torah's gift to the poor person: they allow him to bring something elaborate and meaningful, something that genuinely expresses devotion, rather than something that feels like the minimum.

### Dignity of Poor's Gift

This theological understanding of the מנחה — that it is the gift of the poor person's very soul, and that the Torah goes out of its way to give it dignity and meaning — illuminates, perhaps, why partnership is forbidden. If one may form a partnership of any size — two people, ten people, twenty people — then the offering loses its character as the full, personal gift of a single individual. The whole point of the מנחה, in Rabbi Yitzchak's reading, is that when a poor person brings it he has truly given something. He has brought everything he was able to bring; this is his soul.

If we say — well, find a partner, bring half of one together — we are in effect saying that even this small gift can be subdivided further, made lighter, reduced to a fraction of what it already was. It becomes performative rather than meaningful. As the principle goes: it is not the quantity that matters but the direction of one's heart toward heaven. But that principle depends on the giving being genuine and whole. A gift halved indefinitely ceases to carry that weight. The מנחה must come from a single soul — because its entire significance lies in the fact that it is the full expression of that one soul's devotion.
