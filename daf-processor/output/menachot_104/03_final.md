# Menachot 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dam Neveilah Tumah

### Dead Mule, Revi'it Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּפִרְדָּה אַחַת שֶׁל בֵּית רַבִּי שֶׁמֵּתָה, וְשִׁיעֲרוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת דָּמָהּ בִּרְבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Beivai says** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** There was **an incident involving a mule** belonging **to the house of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **that died, and the Sages estimated** the amount of **its blood** that emerged **as a quarter-***log*, which is the minimum measurement for it to impart ritual impurity.

The daf opens by picking up from the very bottom of 103b, where we had just encountered an important statement of Reb Yehuda, who was ראש המדברים בכל מקום — *rosh hamedabrim bechol makom* — who held the right to speak first in any gathering, followed by Reb Shimon, concerning the sixty *esronot* and the principle that the rabbis' *shiurim like* must have firm cut-off points. A new story now follows, and we will see how it does or does not connect to what came before.

*amar* רב ביבי — Rav Bibi said, citing Reb Yehoshua ben Levi: there was a case involving a mule from Rebbi's household that died — מעשה בפרדה אחת *she-metah* — and the Sages measured its blood and found that it contained a *revi’it* of blood. A *revi’it* is roughly three to three and a half ounces, the same minimum measure used for a כוס of *kiddush cup*. Presumably this was a very young mule.

The reason the Sages measured it was as follows. A dead animal's carcass communicates טומאת *neveilah* — impurity — and the assumption is that this would apply not only to the meat but to the blood as well. The operative principle is that the relevant quantity of blood is the amount which, were it to congeal, would equal the size of a *kezayit* — an olive's bulk — because a *kezayit* of flesh is the minimum quantity of an animal's body that communicates impurity. A *revi’it* of liquid blood, when congealed, becomes approximately a *kezayit* of solid matter. That is why the Sages measured the blood: to determine whether a *revi’it* was present and whether it would therefore communicate impurity.

### Eduyot: Blood is Pure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר בִּיסְנָא: הֵעִיד רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן בְּתֵירָא עַל דַּם נְבֵילוֹת שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן בְּתֵירָא: מַעֲשֶׂה וְהָיוּ נוֹחֲרִין עֲרוֹדְיָאוֹת לַאֲרָיוֹת בְּאִיסְטַרְיָא שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ, וְהָיוּ עוֹלֵי רְגָלִים שׁוֹקְעִין עַד רְכוּבּוֹתֵיהֶן בַּדָּם, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ לָהֶם דָּבָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak bar Bisna raises an objection: Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Beteira testified** that they had a tradition **concerning the blood of** unslaughtered animal **carcasses that it is ritually pure.** Similarly, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Beteira said: An incident** occurred where people **were killing wild donkeys** in order to feed the meat **to the lions** that were **in the king's stadium [*be'istarya*], and** those **ascending to Jerusalem on the pilgrimage festival were wading up to their knees [*rekhuboteihen*] in** the donkeys' **blood, and** the Sages **did not say anything to them** about them becoming impure. Apparently, the blood of an animal carcass does not transmit ritual impurity, even though the carcass itself does.

מתיבי רב יצחק בר ביסנא — Reb Yitzchak bar Bisna raised an objection from Mishnah Eduyot. The Mishnah states: העיד *rebi* יהושע ורבי יהושע בן בתירא על דם *neveilah* שהוא טהור — Reb Yehoshua and Reb Yehoshua ben Beteira testified that the blood of a *neveilah* is pure. According to their testimony, no quantity of blood from a carcass communicates impurity; only the flesh does so. This stands in direct tension with the story of Rebbi's mule, where the blood was measured precisely to assess its impurity.

### Lions Stadium Story

Reb Yehoshua ben Beteira's testimony in Mishnah Eduyot was not merely a theoretical ruling — he supported it with an account of actual practice. מעשה והיו *nochrim means* *arudyot because I* לאריות באסטרייא — there was a case where animals were slaughtered, through נחירה rather than through *shechitah*, for the lions in the stadium — *istraya probably means* likely referring to the Roman amphitheater in the land of Israel. והיו עולי רגלים שוקעים עד ארכובותיהם בדם ולא אמרו להם דבר — and when pilgrims would come up for one of the three major festivals, they would be wading up to their knees in the blood of these carcasses, and no one ever said anything to them about impurity. Had *dam neveilah* communicated *tumah*, someone would certainly have objected — these pilgrims were coming to the Temple. The fact that no objection was raised is compelling evidence that the blood was considered pure.

One may also wonder what precisely these pilgrims were doing in the vicinity of the stadium, but apparently visiting such attractions was part of the culture of the city during the festival period.

### Rav Bibi's Poverty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישְׁתִּיק, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זְרִיקָא: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא קָא מַהְדַּר מָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חָנִין: ״וְהָיוּ חַיֶּיךָ תְּלֻאִים לְךָ מִנֶּגֶד״ – זֶה הַלּוֹקֵחַ תְּבוּאָה מִשָּׁנָה לְשָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Beivai **was silent** and did not answer. **Rabbi Zerika said to him: What is the reason** that **the Master does not respond** to the question? Rabbi Beivai **said to him: How can I respond to him?** My circumstances can be described **as Rabbi Ḥanin said** in interpreting the verse: "And your life shall hang in doubt before you; and you shall fear night and day, and you shall have no assurance of your life" (Deuteronomy 28:66). **"And your life shall hang in doubt before you"; this** is referring to one **who purchases grain from one year for the next,** because he is not certain that he will find grain to eat in the next year.

*Ishtik* — Rav Bibi fell silent. He had no response. Rav Zerika then asked him: *mai* טעמא **Lo** קמהדר מר — why did you not give an answer? Rav Bibi replied: היכי אהדר ליה — how could I be expected to respond?

His explanation is striking. He cited a teaching of Reb Chanina, who interprets the verse in the curses of Devarim: והיו חייך תלוים לך *mineged* — your life will hang suspended before you. Reb Chanina explains that this refers to someone who must purchase grain from year to year rather than growing his own. As Rashi notes, the verse says *lokeach somebody* — one who purchases — specifically indicating someone without land, for a landowner has a sense of security in his ability to provide for himself, while a purchaser does not, even if he has enough money this year. The verse continues: ופחדת לילה ויומם — you will fear day and night. This, explains Reb Chanina, refers to someone who can only purchase grain from week to week, anxious each day whether he will have the money to buy next week's supply. Finally, the verse concludes: ולא תאמין בחייך — you will have no faith in your own life — meaning someone who relies entirely on the baker's daily charity for his bread, not knowing each day where his next meal will come from.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּפָחַדְתָּ לַיְלָה וְיוֹמָם״ – זֶה הַלּוֹקֵחַ תְּבוּאָה מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, ״וְאַל תַּאֲמֵן בְּחַיֶּיךָ״ – זֶה הַסּוֹמֵךְ עַל הַפַּלְטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **"And you shall fear night and day"; this** is referring to one **who purchases grain** from one **Shabbat eve to another** because he does not have the resources to provide for himself further. **"And you shall have no assurance of your life"; this** is referring to one **who relies on the baker [*hapalter*]** to give him bread because he has no grain of his own.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא עַל פַּלְטֵר סָמֵיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Beivai concludes: **And that man,** i.e., I, **relies on a baker.** Therefore, my mind is not sufficiently settled to answer the question properly.

Rav Bibi then said: and that man — meaning himself — על פלטר סמך, I rely on the baker every day. He had absolutely no livelihood, no security, and lacked the ישוב הדעת — the presence of mind — to formulate an adequate response to the challenge.

### Beit Hillel: Blood Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מוֹרְיָינָא דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה הֲוָה, וְאוֹרִי לֵיהּ כִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** conclusion **was** reached **about** this question? **Rav Yosef said: Rabbi Yehuda was the halakhic decisor of the house of the *Nasi*, and he instructed** them **according to his tradition** that the blood of an animal carcass is impure.

*mai* הוי עלה — so what is the resolution? Two traditions stood in conflict: one in which the blood of a carcass communicates *tumah*, and one in which it does not.

*amar* רב יוסף — Rav Yosef resolved the matter. *rebi* יהודה *morayna* דבי *nasi* הוה — Reb Yehuda served as the halachic authority in the house of the Nasi. Rashi explains that *morayna* means he served as the posek for Rebbi's household. ואורי ליה כשמעתיה — and he ruled for Rebbi's household according to his own teaching. It was therefore not Rebbi himself who determined that the blood was impure; it was Reb Yehuda ruling for Rebbi's household in accordance with his own position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים מִקּוּלֵּי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּמֵחוּמְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. דַּם נְבֵילוֹת – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מְטַהֲרִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מְטַמְּאִין. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אַף כְּשֶׁטִּמְּאוּ בֵּית הִלֵּל, לֹא טִמְּאוּ אֶלָּא בִּרְבִיעִית, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לִקְרוֹשׁ וְלַעֲמוֹד עַל כְּזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says** that **six matters are among the leniencies of Beit Shammai and among the stringencies of Beit Hillel.** They include **the blood of a carcass,** which **Beit Shammai deem ritually pure,** as in their opinion only the flesh of a carcass imparts impurity; **and Beit Hillel deem** it **ritually impure. Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, said: Even when Beit Hillel deemed** it **impure, they deemed** it **impure only** when it is **in** the quantity of at least **a quarter-***log*, **since** that amount, were it **to congeal, could constitute an olive-bulk,** which is the minimum amount of the flesh of a carcass that is deemed impure.

What is Reb Yehuda's position? The Gemara cites a Mishnah: *rebi* יהודה אומר ששה *devarim* מקולי בית שמאי ומחומרי בית הלל — Reb Yehuda says that there are six matters in which *beit shammai* is lenient and Beit Hillel is strict, the reverse of the usual pattern. One of those six is דם *neveilah*. *beit shammai* holds that *dam neveilah* does not communicate *tumah* — only the flesh does. Beit Hillel holds that it does communicate *tumah*. Since we rule like Beit Hillel even when they are strict, *dam neveilah* is tamei. Reb Yehuda ruled accordingly for Rebbi's household when measuring the blood of the mule.

The story in Mishnah Eduyot, with the pilgrims wading through the blood at the stadium, reflects the opposing position — that *dam neveilah* is pure — and thus represents a different opinion.

### Revi'it Congeals Kezayit


The Gemara adds a further nuance. *amar* *rebi* יוסי ברבי יהודה — Reb Yosi the son of Reb Yehuda said: אף כשטימאו בית הלל **Lo** טימאו *ela* ברביעית — even when Beit Hillel ruled that *dam neveilah* is tamei, they only ruled so for a quantity of a *revi’it*, הואיל ויכול להקריש ולעמוד על *kezayit* — because that is the amount of blood that can congeal to the size of a *kezayit*. The logic is that blood does not communicate *tumah* as blood in its own right; rather, it communicates *tumah* as a form of meat — as an extension of the category of flesh. The threshold for flesh is a *kezayit*, a solid measure. The liquid measure of a *revi’it* is therefore the operative standard for blood, because that is precisely the volume of blood whose congealed form equals a *kezayit*.

### Shiurim Connection


Rashi explains that this passage appears here because the end of yesterday's daf involved a broad discussion of the rabbis' *shiurim like* — the precise measurements they established for various halachic categories — and this story presents a closely related issue about how blood is measured against the standard of meat. Tosafot adds a further connection: yesterday we noted that Reb Yehuda was ראש המדברים בכל מקום, and today we learn that he also served as the halachic authority in Rebbi's household — perhaps indicating a relationship between his precedence in public discourse and the role he played in that domestic context.

## Nesachim Quantities

### Mishnah: Log Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מִתְנַדְּבִין לוֹג, שְׁנַיִם, וַחֲמִשָּׁה, אֲבָל מִתְנַדְּבִין שְׁלֹשָׁה, וְאַרְבָּעָה, וְשִׁשָּׁה, וּמִשִּׁשָּׁה וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One does not pledge** a libation of one ***log*, two** *log*, **or five** *log* of wine, because there are no existing libations with those measures of wine. **But one pledges** a libation of **three** *log*, which is the measure of wine brought with a lamb, **or four** *log*, which is the measure of wine brought with a ram, **or six** *log*, which is the measure of wine brought with a bull. **And** one may pledge a libation of **six** *log* **and beyond,** as any greater amount can be composed of combinations of these three.

The Mishnah now before us teaches: *ein* מתנדבים *because that's* **Ein mitnadvim log shnayim*—one speaker move* **vachamisha** אבל מתנדבים שלשה וארבעה וששה ומששה ולמעלה — one may not donate two *because that's* of wine, but one may donate three, four, or six *because that's*, or any quantity above six.

### Wine Denominations


The framework of this Mishnah presupposes that the wine may be brought as a separate, independent sacrifice — a נדבה of wine — even when not accompanying an animal sacrifice. This category is called מנחת *nesachim*, the libation offering. The Mishnah's permitted quantities are derived from the wine that accompanied animal sacrifices, since that is the only model available. The quantities of wine brought with the various animals are stated in the **Musaf** prayer: חצי ההין לפר — half a **hin** for a bull — שלישית ההין לאיל — a third of a **hin** for a ram — and *revi’it* ההין לכבש — a quarter of a **hin** for a lamb.

A **hin** consists of twelve *because that's*. Half of twelve is six *because that's* for a bull, a third of twelve is four *because that's* for a ram, and a quarter of twelve is three *because that's* for a lamb. Accordingly, the valid denominations for an independent wine donation are three, four, and six. One *because that's* is excluded because it falls below the minimum of three. Two *because that's* is excluded for the same reason. Five is excluded because it is not itself one of the three denominations. As for quantities above six: seven can be composed of three and four; eight of four and four; nine of six and three; ten of six and four — and so on. Any whole number above six can be composed of some combination of three, four, and six. That is the basis for the Mishnah's ruling that from six and above all quantities are permitted.

### Keva L'Nesachim?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ – יֵשׁ קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים, אוֹ אֵין קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Is there a fixed** amount **for libations,** in that when one vows to bring a certain number of *log* of wine they are not offered separately, **or is there no fixed** amount **for libations,** and it is permitted to divide them and offer them in smaller amounts?

איבעיא להו — the Gemara raises a question: יש קבע *lenesachim* או *ein* קבע *lenesachim* — is there a fixed unit for nesachim donations, or is there no fixed unit?

The practical stakes of this question are illustrated through a concrete case. Suppose one has sanctified five *because that's* of wine. The Mishnah rules that five is an impermissible quantity. What is to be done? If there is no fixed unit — *ein* קבע *lenesachim* — then the five *because that's* need not be offered as a single unit. One could extract four *because that's* from the five, offer those four *because that's* as the denomination appropriate for a ram, and the remaining one *because that's* would be treated as a separate donation — given to another individual who needs an extra *because that's* to complete his own sacrifice. If, however, there is a fixed unit — יש קבע *lenesachim* — then the five cannot be subdivided. One would be obligated to add a sixth *because that's* and offer all six together as the denomination for a bull.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? כְּגוֹן דְּאַיְיתִי חַמְשָׁה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵין קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים – מָשֵׁיךְ וּמַקְרֵיב אַרְבְּעָה מִינַּיְיהוּ, דַּחֲזוּ לְאַיִל, וְאִידַּךְ הָוֵי נְדָבָה. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ יֵשׁ קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים – עַד דִּמְמַלֵּי לְהוּ לָא קׇרְבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances?** It is a case **where he brought five** *log* of wine. **If you say** that **there is no fixed** amount **for libations,** then **he removes and offers four of them, as** those **are suitable for** the libations of **a ram, and the other** *log*, which is not of sufficient volume, **is** allocated for communal **gift** offerings. Either it itself is offered, or it is redeemed and the money is used to buy offerings for the repletion of the altar, i.e., for times when the altar is idle. But **if you say** that **there is a fixed** amount **for libations** and the wine may not be divided and offered separately, then it **is not offered** itself or redeemed with money and offered **until he adds to** the existing measurement such that the total number of *log* may be offered as an independent libation.

### Six Shofarot Boxes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תָּא שְׁמַע, שִׁשָּׁה לִנְדָבָה, וְאָמְרִינַן: כְּנֶגֶד מִי? כְּנֶגֶד מוֹתַר חַטָּאת, וּמוֹתַר אֲשָׁמוֹת, וּמוֹתַר אֲשַׁם נָזִיר, וּמוֹתַר אֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע, וּמוֹתַר קִינִּין, וּמוֹתַר מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the *halakha*? **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** the answer from a mishna (*Shekalim* 6:5): There were **six** collection horns **for** communal **gift** offerings, **and we say: For what** were they **designated?** They were designated **for** funds **left over** from the purchase of **sin offerings,** for funds **left over** from **the** purchase of **guilt offerings,** for funds **left over** from the purchase of a **guilt offering of a nazirite,** for funds **left over** from the purchase of the **guilt offering of a leper,** for funds **left over** from the purchase of **pairs** of birds, and for funds **left over** from the purchase of the **meal offering of a sinner.**

*amar* *abaye* — Abaye proposed a proof from the institution of the Temple collection boxes. In the Temple there were thirteen essentially tzedakah-style boxes into which people would deposit leftover funds from various types of sacrifices — money that had been sanctified for an obligation, partially used, and left over with no further application to the original purpose. These funds would be drawn upon on slow days at the altar to purchase communal burnt offerings, maintaining the appearance of an active Temple service.

Of these thirteen boxes, six were designated לנדבה — for free-will donations. The Mishnah asks: וכנגד מי — what do these six represent? Each of the six corresponds to a distinct category of leftover obligatory funds: *motar* חטאת — money left over from a sin offering; ומותר *asham* — from a guilt offering; ומותר *asham* *nazir brings* — from the guilt offering a Nazirite brings at the conclusion of his period of nezirut, which is not an atonement offering but a rite of termination; ומותר *asham* *metzora somebody* — from the guilt offering of a person with tzaraat who underwent a process of purification; ומותר קינים — from bird sacrifices; and ומותר מנחת חוטא — from a sin-mincha offering.

The common denominator among all six categories is that the funds had been designated for an obligatory sacrifice of a specific type. Once that obligation was discharged, the leftover money could not be redirected toward fulfilling the same obligation, but it also had no other designated use, and so it was transferred to these Temple funds and used to sustain the altar's activity.

Abaye's argument runs as follows: if it were true that leftover wine from nesachim donations created a distinct and commonly occurring scenario — specifically, if one who donated five *because that's* could not subdivide them and instead had an unavoidable remainder — then we would have expected a seventh *shofar like* box dedicated to *motar* *nesachim*. The absence of such a box implies that the leftover-wine scenario does not commonly arise as a distinct category, which would be explained if the wine could always be subdivided and redistributed without remainder.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, לִיתַקֵּין שׁוֹפָר אַחֲרִינָא כְּנֶגֶד מוֹתַר נְסָכִים!
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is so** that there is no fixed amount for libations, and if one vowed to bring five *log* then four are offered as a ram's libation and the fifth can be redeemed and its money used for a communal gift offering, then **let them institute an additional** collection **horn** in the Temple for funds **left over** from the purchase **of libations.**

### Leftover Wine Pooled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנָךְ לְנִדְבַת צִבּוּר אָזְלִי, הָנֵי שְׁכִיחִי, אֶפְשָׁר דְּמִצְטָרְפִי דְּמָר וּדְמָר בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וְקָרְבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **These** six horns are dedicated to funds that **go to communal gift offerings,** and **these** *log* of wine left over from libations **are common,** so there is no need to store them, since it is **possible** that the libation **of this person** will **be combined with** the libation of another **person together** in order to reach the desired amount, **and** then it **is offered** immediately. Therefore, no additional horn was necessary for the money from the redemption of libations.

The Gemara rejects Abaye's proof. *hanach* לנדבת ציבור *azli* — those boxes and their funds are used for communal burnt offerings. *hani* *shechichi* — those other categories are common in the sense that the leftover money genuinely has nowhere else to go. But leftover wine is different: *efshar* *demitztrarpi* *demar* *udemar* בהדי הדדי — it is easily possible for the leftover wine of one person to be combined with the need of another. Since almost every animal sacrifice requires wine as part of its nesachim, a person who has slightly less wine than he needs can readily receive the leftover *because that's* from someone else. The leftover wine need never become a stranded surplus at all; it is instantly reabsorbed into the system. There was therefore no need for a dedicated *shofar like* box for this category, and Abaye's proof from the absence of such a box is inconclusive. *vekarvi* — and it would simply be offered as part of someone else's sacrifice.

### Ezrach: Independent Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, ״אֶזְרָח״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמִּתְנַדְּבִין נְסָכִים, וְכַמָּה? שְׁלֹשֶׁת לוּגִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from a *baraita*: The Torah states with regard to libations: "All that are **home born** shall do these things in this manner, in presenting an offering made by fire, of a pleasing aroma to the Lord" (Numbers 15:13). As this verse is superfluous, the various terms in it are used to derive *halakhot*. The term **"home born" teaches that one may pledge libations** even when they are not sacrificed together with an offering. **And how much** is the minimum volume that is offered? **Three *log*,** which is the smallest measurement of a libation in the Torah, i.e., the libation that is offered with a lamb.

*amar* רבא — Rava offered a different approach, now seeking a proof from the scriptural basis for the very institution of independent wine donations. The verses in the Torah regarding nesachim deploy the word *ezrach I don't know exactly* — citizen — which is an unusual term in this context and is therefore read as expanding the scope of the law. What does it expand to teach? *melamed* *shemisnadvin* *nesachim* — it teaches that one may donate wine as an independent sacrifice. *vekama* — and how much? *shlosha* *login* — the minimum is three *because that's*, which is the smallest denomination that accompanies any animal sacrifice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם רָצָה לְהוֹסִיף יוֹסִיף? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יִהְיֶה״. יָכוֹל יִפְחוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כָּכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **that if one desires to add** to this amount **he may add? The verse states** with regard to libations associated with the additional offerings for the New Moon: "And their libations: Half a *hin* of wine **shall be** for the bull, and one-third of a *hin* for the ram, and one-quarter of a *hin* for the lamb" (Numbers 28:14). From the superfluous "shall be" one may understand that there are others, and derive that independent libations may also be brought. **Can** one **decrease** the amount of wine in a libation to less than three *log*? **The verse states:** "All that are home born shall do these things, **in this manner"** (Numbers 15:13), i.e., one may not bring less than three *log* of wine.

The verse also contains the word *yihye another word* — shall be — which broadens the law further. *uminiyan* *she'im* *rotze* *lehosef* *yosef* — how do I know that one may add more than three? From that word. And the word כך — thus or in this manner — excludes the possibility of bringing less than three: *yachol* *yifchos* — one might have thought one could bring less than three *because that's* — תלמוד לומר *kacha* — the Torah says no, it must be this way. From these words we derive all the principles of the Mishnah: wine may be given as an independent sacrifice, but only in the prescribed denominations and not below the minimum of three *because that's*.

### Yosef = Five Log

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״יוֹסִיף״? אִילֵּימָא אַרְבָּעָה וְשִׁשָּׁה – מַאי שְׁנָא שְׁלֹשָׁה? דַּחֲזוּ לְכֶבֶשׂ – אַרְבָּעָה וְשִׁשָּׁה נָמֵי חֲזוּ לְאַיִל וּפַר! אֶלָּא לָאו, חֲמִשָּׁה, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What** does the *baraita* mean when it says that **one may add** to the minimum of three *log* for an independent libation? **If we say** it means that it is permitted to offer **four or six** *log*, then **what is different** about **three** *log* that it was specified? It is **because it is suitable for** the libations of **a lamb.** If so, then **four or six** *log* are **also suitable,** as four *log* is offered as the libation **for a ram and** six *log* is offered as the libation for **a bull. Rather,** in saying that one may add, **isn't** the *baraita* referring to **five** *log* of wine, despite the fact that it is not a measurement used with any of the offerings? **And learn from** this *baraita* that **there is no fixed** amount **for libations.** The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **learn from** the *baraita* that this is so.

The Gemara now presses the analysis of the word *yosef* in the verse — "one may add." *mai* *yosef* — what does "may add" mean? If it means that one may donate the other standard denominations alongside three — namely four and six *because that's* — that would be unnecessary, since those denominations are already established by the same principle that permits three *because that's*. Just as three is permitted because it is the denomination for a sheep, four and six are permitted on the same basis for a ram and a bull respectively. The word *yosef* therefore cannot be teaching those standard units.

*ela* *lav* — rather, it must mean something beyond the standard denominations. Since the Mishnah specifies that five is forbidden, the word *yosef* must be teaching that one can bring five. But if five is permitted, how does one offer five? Only by extracting four from the five and using the remaining one *because that's* in some other way. ושמע מינה — this implies *ein* קבע *lenesachim* — there is no fixed unit, and therefore a five-*because that's* donation can be subdivided and partially offered.

### Rav Ashi: Five = Two

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָא אֲנַן לָא תְּנַן הָכִי? אֵין מִתְנַדְּבִין לוֹג שְׁנַיִם וַחֲמִשָּׁה. קָתָנֵי חֲמִשָּׁה דּוּמְיָא דִּשְׁנַיִם, מָה שְׁנַיִם דְּלָא חֲזוּ כְּלָל, אַף חֲמִשָּׁה נָמֵי דְּלָא חֲזוּ כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: But didn't we learn this** in the mishna: **One does not pledge** a libation of one ***log*, two** *log*, **or five** *log* of wine? Rav Ashi continues: In phrasing the mishna in this manner, the *tanna* **teaches** that the status of **five** *log* is **similar** to the status **of two** *log*: **Just as two** *log* **are not suitable at all** to be offered independently, **so too, five** *log* are **also not suitable at all** to offer, as there are no libations of this size. This demonstrates that there is a fixed amount to libations, and one may not divide them into two.

*amar* רב אשי — Rav Ashi objected. *va'anan* **Lo** תנן *hachi* — this does not accord with our Mishnah. The Mishnah states: *ein* מתנדבים *because that's* **Ein mitnadvim log shnayim*—one speaker move* **vachamisha** — one may not donate two or five *because that's*. קתני חמשה דומיא דשנים — by listing five alongside two, the Mishnah implies that the problem with five is analogous to the problem with two. מה **Ein mitnadvim log shnayim*—one speaker move* דלא *chazu* כלל — just as two *because that's* cannot be brought to the altar at all because the minimum denomination is three, אף חמשה *nami* דלא *chazu* כלל — so too five *because that's* cannot be brought at all. One must wait until a sixth *because that's* is added to reach the denomination of six. This would suggest that five is entirely unusable as offered, implying יש קבע — there is a fixed unit.

### Different Grounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי אִירְיָא? הָא כִּדְאִיתַהּ, וְהָא כִּדְאִיתַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Are the cases comparable? This,** two *log*, is **as it is, and that,** five *log*, is **as it is,** and there is no reason to compare them. Four of the five *log* are offered, with the fifth allocated for a communal gift offering.

The Gemara dismisses Rav Ashi's objection. מידי איריא — the parallel does not hold. *hachi* איתא והכי איתא — each case operates on its own terms. Two and five are both listed as forbidden, but they are forbidden for entirely different reasons. Two *because that's* simply do not reach the minimum; there is no way to offer any part of a two-*because that's* donation. Five *because that's*, however, may well be subtractable: perhaps four can be extracted and offered, with the one remainder handled separately. Listing them together in the same sentence does not mean they share the same legal mechanism. The proof from the Mishnah's grouping therefore fails.

### Eleven Log Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר אֵין קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים – הָא אֵין קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים, אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר יֵשׁ קֶבַע לִנְסָכִים – עַד עֲשָׂרָה פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, אַחַד עָשָׂר
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: If you say** that **there is no fixed** amount **for libations, then there is no fixed** amount **for libations,** and no further discussion is necessary. **If you say** that **there is a fixed** amount **for libations,** then the *halakha* of **up to ten** *log* of wine is **obvious to me.** One *log*, two *log*, or five *log* may not be brought, since these amounts are not offered as standard libations, but three, four, or six *log* are brought, as their amount is equivalent to those of standard libations. Between six and ten *log* may also be brought, since these amounts can be composed of a combination of the various wine libations. With regard to **eleven** *log*,

*amar* *abaye* — Abaye now turns the question in a different direction. אם תמצא לומר *ein* קבע *lenesachim* — if one concludes there is no fixed unit, then the question is entirely resolved and no further difficulties arise. But אם תמצא לומר יש קבע *lenesachim* — if one concludes that there is a fixed unit and a donor must offer his entire vowed quantity without subdivision, a new question emerges.

עד עשרה **Pshita** לי — for any vowed quantity up to ten *because that's*, the answer is obvious. Seven can be composed of three and four; eight of four and four; nine of six and three; ten of six and four. All of these are clean combinations of the standard denominations. But חד עשר מיבעיא לי — eleven *because that's* presents a difficulty. Can eleven be composed of the standard denominations? Four, four, and three — that is indeed eleven — but this is not a clean two-unit combination; it requires two units of one denomination and one of another. Is such a combination valid?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיבַּעְיָא לִי, מַאי: גַּבְרָא לִשְׁנֵי פָּרִים קָא מְכַוֵּין, וְעַד דִּמְמַלֵּי לְהוּ לָא קׇרְבִי, אוֹ דִלְמָא לִשְׁנֵי אֵילִים וְכֶבֶשׂ אֶחָד קָמְכַוֵּוין? תְּרֵי מֵחַד מִינָא וְחַד מֵחַד מִינָא אָמְרִינַן, אוֹ לָא? מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **I ask, what** is the *halakha*? Does **the man** who vowed to bring eleven *log* **intend** to bring an amount of wine corresponding to that which is brought in association with an offering **of two bulls,** i.e., twelve *log*, **and until he accumulates that** amount, the libation **is not brought? Or perhaps he intends** to bring an amount equal to that associated **with two rams and one lamb,** i.e., four for each ram and three for the lamb, totaling eleven. **Do we say** that he intended to bring **two** libations associated **with one type,** i.e., a ram, **and one** libation associated **with another type,** i.e., a lamb, **or not? What** is the *halakha*? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

One possibility is that the person who vowed eleven was mentally aiming for twelve — two sixes, the denomination for two bulls — and simply fell short. In that case, גברא לשני פרים קמיכוין — he had two bulls in mind — ועד דמלא להו **Lo** **ad demamlei lehu lo karvei** — and until the full twelve *because that's* are assembled, nothing may be offered. The other possibility is that he intended two rams and one sheep — two fours and a three — which total eleven exactly. Is it valid to interpret a vow as combining two of one denomination with one of another? תרי מחד מינא וחד מחד מינא אמרינן או **Lo** — do we say such a combination is acceptable, or is the asymmetry of the combination problematic?

### Teiku: Eleven Log


*teiku* — the question is left unresolved. The underlying question of whether יש קבע *lenesachim* or *ein* קבע *lenesachim* itself remains open, and this specific application — the eleven-*because that's* vow — is likewise left without an answer.

## Oil Donation Debate

### Akiva vs. Tarfon: Oil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִתְנַדְּבִין יַיִן, וְאֵין מִתְנַדְּבִין שֶׁמֶן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִתְנַדְּבִין שֶׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may pledge** to bring independent libations of **wine, but one may not pledge oil** alone; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Tarfon says: One may pledge oil** as well.

The Mishnah now addresses the question of oil. We have established that wine may be donated independently as a free-will sacrifice. The question arises whether the same is true of oil alone. Both oil and wine accompany animal sacrifices — with the animal come both flour mixed with oil and a separate measure of wine. May one donate just oil, as one may donate just wine?

מתנדבים **reviis hahin lakeves yayin** ואין מתנדבים *shemen* — one may donate wine as a free-will sacrifice but not oil, דברי *rebi* עקיבא — this is the position of Rabbi Akiva. *rebi* טרפון אומר מתנדבים *shemen* — Rabbi Tarfon says one may donate oil independently.

### Tarfon: Oil Like Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מָה מָצִינוּ בְּיַיִן, שֶׁהוּא בָּא חוֹבָה וּבָא נְדָבָה, אַף שֶׁמֶן שֶׁהוּא בָּא חוֹבָה בָּא נְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said: Just as we found that wine comes** as **an obligation and comes** as **a gift offering** independent of any offering, **so too,** we find **that oil comes** as **an obligation and comes** as **a gift** offering.

Rabbi Tarfon's argument by analogy: מה מצינו ביין שהוא בא בחובה ובא בנדבה — just as we find that wine comes as an obligation when accompanying an animal sacrifice and also comes as a voluntary free-will offering, אף *shemen* — so too oil: just as wine can come independently, oil should be able to come independently as well.

### Akiva: Oil Not Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּיַיִן שֶׁכֵּן קָרֵב עִם חוֹבָתוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, תֹּאמַר בְּשֶׁמֶן שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָרֵב עִם חוֹבָתוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: No, if you said** that this is true **with regard to wine, as** it is **sacrificed with its obligatory** offering **by itself, shall you** also **say** that this is also the case **with regard to oil, which is never sacrificed with its obligatory** offering **by itself but only** mixed with the flour?

Rabbi Akiva rejects the analogy. אם אמרת ביין שכן קרב עם חובתו בפני עצמו — wine, when it accompanies the obligatory animal sacrifice, is brought as a discrete unit in its own right. It is a separate measure of wine, standing alone. That is why wine can also be given as an independent free-will offering — there is a model of wine standing alone. תאמר בשמן שאינו קרב עם חובתו בפני עצמו — but can you say the same of oil? Oil never comes by itself even when it accompanies an obligatory sacrifice. It always comes as a flour-and-oil mixture, a *it's like*. There is no model of offering pure oil independently. Since oil never comes alone even in its obligatory form, there is no basis for saying it may come alone as a voluntary donation.

### Minchat Nesachim Daily

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִדִּבְרֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם נִלְמוֹד, מִתְנַדֵּב אָדָם מִנְחַת נְסָכִים בְּכׇל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva about whether one can pledge oil, **Rava said: From the statements of both of them, we learn** that **a person may pledge a meal offering** like those brought **with the libations** that accompany animal offerings every day.

*amar* רבא — Rava draws an inference from the very structure of the debate. מדברי שניהם — from the positions of both Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Tarfon — **nilmod** — we can infer — מתנדב אדם מנחת *nesachim* בכל יום — that a person may donate minchat nesachim, the flour-and-oil mixture that accompanies animal sacrifices, as an independent free-will sacrifice at any time.

The reasoning is as follows. Rabbi Akiva's very argument against oil turns on the fact that oil does not come alone — it always comes as a flour-and-oil mixture. This implicitly concedes that the flour-and-oil mixture itself may be donated independently. And Rabbi Tarfon, who permits oil alone, would certainly permit the mixture. So both opinions, from their respective angles, imply that minchat nesachim can be brought independently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מִנְחַת נְדָבָה גַּלִּי בַּהּ רַחֲמָנָא – הָנֵי חֲמִשָּׁה מְנָחוֹת אִין, טְפֵי לָא! קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּסְתָמָא, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּפָרֵישׁ – פָּרֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara explains: It needed to be said, **lest you say** that **the Merciful One revealed** the nature of **a voluntary meal offering** (see Leviticus, chapter 2). Perhaps only **those five meal offerings** detailed there are **indeed** brought as voluntary offerings, but any **additional** types, such as a meal offering brought with libations, are **not.** Rava therefore **teaches us** that **these matters** in the Torah apply only **where** his pledge of a meal offering was **unspecified, but where he specified** that the oil and flour about which he vows should be brought in the manner of a meal offering with libations, then **he has specified,** and the vow takes effect.

One might object: of course minchat nesachim can be donated, since a *it's like* can generally be given as a free-will offering. What is new here? The answer is that there is a difference. The **menachos** listed in **Vayikra** have a specific ratio of flour to oil. The minchat nesachim uses a different ratio — the ratio that accompanies the animal sacrifices. One might have thought that only the **menachos** explicitly catalogued in the Torah may be brought independently, and that the nesachim ratio is not among them.

### Explicit Nesachim Mincha


**Pshita** — the Gemara initially reacts: is this not obvious? The response is that it is not. מהו דתימא — one might have argued — מנחת נדבה גלי בה רחמנא — the Torah made explicit which **menachos** may be donated voluntarily — *hani* חמשה **menachos** — those five types of *it's like* prepared by different methods listed in **Vayikra** — טפי **Lo** — and no others. Wine, the Torah never discussed as an independent sacrifice, and so we derive that permission through the drasha on *ezrach I don't know exactly*. But *it's like* — the Torah did discuss explicitly — and the listed five might be exhaustive. One might then have said that the flour-and-oil mixture in the nesachim ratio falls outside this list and therefore cannot be donated independently.

קא משמע לן — therefore this teaches us: *hani* מילי בסתמא — this exclusion applies only when a person donates a *it's like* without specifying what type. In that case, we assume he means one of the five established types. אבל היכא דפריש פריש — but if he explicitly specifies that he wishes to donate a *it's like* according to the nesachim ratio — the ratio that accompanies animal sacrifices — he may do so.

## Nefesh: No Mincha Partner

### Mishnah: No Joint Mincha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שְׁנַיִם מִתְנַדְּבִין עִשָּׂרוֹן אֶחָד, אֲבָל מִתְנַדְּבִין עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים, וְעוֹף – אֲפִילּוּ פְּרֵידָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna concludes with a ruling concerning a voluntary meal offering: **Two** people **do not pledge** a meal offering of **a tenth** of an ephah as partners, **but they may pledge a burnt offering and a peace offering. And** they may pledge to bring even **an individual bird,** not only a pair.

The **perek** concludes with a ruling that closes the Mishnah: *ein* **Ein mitnadvim log shnayim*—one speaker move* מתנדבים **isaron** אחד — two people may not jointly donate a single **isaron**, the minimum measure of a *it's like*, as a partnership offering. אבל מתנדבים **olah** ושלמים — they may, however, jointly donate a free-will **olah** or **shlamim* peace offering*. **V'of** — they may even jointly bring a bird as an **olah**, אפילו פרידה אחת — even a single bird, though normally birds come in pairs in the Torah. Any animal sacrifice, however small, may be jointly donated. A *it's like* may not.

### Nefesh = Individual

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבָא: מִדִּבְרֵי שְׁנֵיהֶם נִלְמוֹד, מִתְנַדֵּב אָדָם מִנְחַת נְסָכִים בְּכׇל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva about whether one can pledge oil, **Rava said: From the statements of both of them, we learn** that **a person may pledge a meal offering** like those brought **with the libations** that accompany animal offerings every day.

The Gemara asks: *mai* טעמא — what is the reason? The initial proposal is that the Torah uses the singular form **takriv** — you shall bring — when speaking of the *it's like*, implying that only an individual may bring it. But this proposal fails because **olah** also uses the singular **yakriv**. The response is that for **olah**, another verse uses the plural **l'oloseichem** — for your **olos** — indicating that multiple people may be jointly involved. If so, the same argument should apply to *it's like*, since a similar verse uses the plural **l'minchoseichem* your plural* — for your **menachos**. There is no difference in grammatical structure between the two.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב בָּהּ ״נֶפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the reason that one may not bring a meal offering as a partnership is **because it is written:** "And when **an individual** brings a meal offering" (Leviticus 2:1), indicating that only one individual may offer it, but not two together.

*ela* משום **D'ksiv* because another verse says* ביה נפש — rather, the reason is the specific word used in the *it's like* passage. When the Torah introduces animal sacrifices it says אדם כי **yakriv** מכם **korban** — a person who offers a sacrifice. But when it introduces the *it's like* it says ונפש כי **takriv** — and if a נפש — a soul — brings a *it's like*. This shift from person to soul carries halachic weight: the נפש implies a single individual, reflecting the utter individuality and uniqueness of a soul.

### Baraita: Mincha Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיב קׇרְבָּנוֹ לְכׇל נִדְרֵיהֶם וּלְכׇל נִדְבוֹתָם אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיבוּ לַה׳״ – הַכֹּל בָּאִין בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת, לֹא סִילֵּק הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא מִנְחָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נֶפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **states** an explanation of the following verse: "Anyone **that brings his offering, whether it be any of their vows, or any of their free-will offerings that they sacrifice to the Lord"** (Leviticus 22:18). It is evident from the use of the plural in the second part of the verse that **any** offering can **be brought in a partnership, and the verse excludes only a meal offering** from this principle, **as it is stated:** "And when **an individual** brings a meal offering."

תניא *nami* *hachi* — a Baraita confirms this ruling. The verse says that one will bring his sacrifice for all of their vows and free-will offerings that they bring to God, moving from the singular to the plural. הכל באים בשותפות — this teaches that all sacrifices may come in partnership. **Lo** סילקה הכתוב *ela* *it's like* שנאמר נפש — the Torah excludes only the *it's like*, because it uses the word נפש — a soul — requiring that the *it's like* come from a single individual.

### Poor Person's Nefesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִפְּנֵי מָה נִשְׁתַּנֵּית מִנְחָה שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בָּהּ ״נֶפֶשׁ״? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: מִי דַּרְכּוֹ לְהָבִיא מִנְחָה? עָנִי, מַעֲלֶה אֲנִי עָלָיו כְּאִילּוּ הִקְרִיב נַפְשׁוֹ לְפָנַי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another comment about the fact that the Torah introduces the voluntary meal offering by emphasizing that it is brought by an individual. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: For what** reason **is the meal offering different** from other offerings in **that** the term **"an individual [*nefesh*]" is stated with regard to it? The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Whose practice** is it **to bring a meal offering?** It is that of a **poor** individual; and **I will ascribe him** credit **as if he offered up his soul [*nafsho*] in front of Me.**

*amar* *rebi* יצחק — Rabbi Yitzchak asks: מפני מה נשתנית *it's like* שנאמר בה נפש — why is it specifically the *it's like* for which the Torah uses the word נפש? The answer lies in asking: מי דרכו להביא *it's like* — who is it that typically brings a *it's like* rather than an animal sacrifice? The answer is evident: *ani* — a poor person who cannot afford an animal. For a poor person, the flour-and-oil offering is not a diminished substitute but the fullest expression of what he can give. Therefore, says Rabbi Yitzchak, God declares: מעלה אני עליו כאילו הקריב נפשו לפני — I regard it as though he has offered his very soul before Me.

This is in fact one of the places in the Talmudic literature where the principle of אחד הממעיט ואחד המרבה ובלבד שיכוון לבו לשמים is invoked — it does not matter whether one does much or little, what matters is that one's heart is directed toward heaven.

### Five Preparations Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מָה נִשְׁתַּנֵּית מִנְחָה, (שנאמר) [שֶׁנֶּאֶמְרוּ] בָּהּ חֲמִשָּׁה מִינֵי טִיגּוּן הַלָּלוּ? מָשָׁל לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וְדָם שֶׁעָשָׂה לוֹ אוֹהֲבוֹ סְעוּדָה, וְיוֹדֵעַ בּוֹ שֶׁהוּא עָנִי. אָמַר לוֹ: עֲשֵׂה לִי מִן חֲמִשָּׁה מִינֵי טִיגּוּן, כְּדֵי שֶׁאֵהָנֶה מִמְּךָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another comment made by Rabbi Yitzḥak about meal offerings. **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What is** the reason that the **meal offering is differentiated** from other offerings in **that** the Torah **stated these five types of preparations with oil** with regard to it? The five types of voluntary meal offerings are: A meal offering of fine flour, a meal offering in a shallow pan, a meal offering in a deep pan, a meal offering of loaves baked in an oven, and a meal offering of wafers baked in an oven. All are brought with oil. This can be explained by **a parable of a flesh-and-blood king whose friend made a festive meal for him, but** the king **knows that** the friend **is poor.** The king **said to him: Make for me** foods **from five types of fried** dishes, **so that I may benefit from you.**

*amar* *rebi* יצחק — Rabbi Yitzchak continues: מה נשתנית *it's like* שנאמר בה חמשה מיני טיגון הללו — why does the Torah describe the *it's like* in five different forms of preparation? One may be baked, one pan-fried, one deep-fried — all from the same flour and oil. Why this elaborate variety for what is, in objective terms, a simple inexpensive offering?

משל למלך בשר ודם שעשו לו אוהבו סעודה — it is like a king whose close friend prepared a meal for him. ויודע *bo* שהוא *ani* — but the king knew his friend was poor and could not provide extravagant delicacies. So the king said to him: *amar* לו עשה לי מן חמשה מיני טיגון כדי שאהנה ממך — please prepare for me five different types of pastries and confections from what you have. Not because the king needed the food, but because this gave the poor friend the fullest possible opportunity to express love and honor for the king within the constraints of his means — to do everything he was capable of doing, and to do it with care and variety. The five methods of preparation are the Torah's gift to the poor person: they allow him to bring something elaborate and meaningful, something that genuinely expresses devotion, rather than something that feels like the minimum.

### Dignity of Poor's Gift


This theological understanding of the *it's like* — that it is the gift of the poor person's very soul, and that the Torah goes out of its way to give it dignity and meaning — illuminates, perhaps, why partnership is forbidden. If one may form a partnership of any size — two people, ten people, twenty people — then the offering loses its character as the full, personal gift of a single individual. The whole point of the *it's like*, in Rabbi Yitzchak's reading, is that when a poor person brings it he has truly given something. He has brought everything he was able to bring; this is his soul.

If we say — well, find a partner, bring half of one together — we are in effect saying that even this small gift can be subdivided further, made lighter, reduced to a fraction of what it already was. It becomes performative rather than meaningful. As the principle goes: it is not the quantity that matters but the direction of one's heart toward heaven. But that principle depends on the giving being genuine and whole. A gift halved indefinitely ceases to carry that weight. The *it's like* must come from a single soul — because its entire significance lies in the fact that it is the full expression of that one soul's devotion.
