# Niddah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gender & Procreation

### Male & Peace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: כֵּיוָן שֶׁבָּא זָכָר בָּעוֹלָם — בָּא שָׁלוֹם בָּעוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״שִׁלְחוּ כַר מוֹשֵׁל אֶרֶץ״. ״זָכָר״ — זֶה כַּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says** that **Rabbi Ami says: When a male comes into the world,** i.e., when a male baby is born, **peace comes to the world, as it is stated: "Send the lambs [*khar*] for the ruler of the land"** (Isaiah 16:1). This *khar*, or *kar*, a gift one sends the ruler, contributes to the stability of the government and peace, and the word **male [*zakhar*]** can be interpreted homiletically as an abbreviation of: **This is a *kar* [*zeh kar*].**

The *aggadata* of 31b concludes with a teaching of Rabbi Yitzchak in the school of Rabbi Yannai: *kivan sheba zachar l'olam ba shalom l'olam* — when a male comes into the world, peace comes into the world. The proof-text offered is *shilchu kar moshel aretz* ("send out the *kar*, master of the land"), where Rashi reads *zachar* as a contraction of *ze kar* — "this is the gift," the peace-offering, the thing of value that the male brings into the world.

### Male Production, Female…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק דְּבֵי רַבִּי אַמֵּי: בָּא זָכָר בָּעוֹלָם — בָּא כִּכָּרוֹ בְּיָדוֹ, ״זָכָר״ — זֶה כַּר, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּכְרֶה לָהֶם כֵּרָה גְדוֹלָה״. נְקֵבָה — אֵין עִמָּהּ כְּלוּם, נְקֵבָה — נְקִיָּיה בָּאָה, עַד דְּאָמְרָה: ״מְזוֹנֵי״ — לָא יָהֲבִי לַהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״נׇקְבָה שְׂכָרְךָ עָלַי וְאֶתֵּנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak from the school of Rabbi Ami says: When a male comes into the world, his loaf** of bread, i.e., his sustenance, **comes into his possession.** In other words, a male can provide for himself. This is based on the aforementioned interpretation of the word **male [*zakhar*]** as an abbreviation of: **This is a *kar* [*zeh kar*],** and the term *kar* refers to sustenance, **as it is written: "And he prepared great provision [*kera*] for them"** (II Kings 6:23). By contrast, **when a female comes into the world, nothing,** i.e., no sustenance, comes **with her.** This is derived from the homiletic interpretation of the word **female [*nekeva*]** as an abbreviation of the phrase: **She comes clean [*nekiya ba'a*],** i.e., empty. Furthermore, **until she says:** Give me **sustenance,** people **do not give her, as it is written** in Laban's request of Jacob: **"Appoint me [*nokva*] your wages, and I will give it"** (Genesis 30:28). Laban used the word *nokva*, similar to *nekeva*, when he said that he would pay Jacob only if he explicitly demanded his wages.

A second teaching from the same school continues the wordplay: *ba zachar l'olam ba k'reih beyado* — when a male comes into the world, his loaf of bread is in his hands. The word *zachar* is again parsed as *ze kar*, this time in the sense of productive capacity, supported by the verse *vayikra lahem k'ra gedolah* ("he dug for them a great pit"), which is taken to evoke the ability to produce and be productive. By contrast, *nekeivah ein imah klum* — the female comes with nothing. The wordplay here is *nekiyah ba'ah*, she comes empty-handed. The proof-text is *nakvah s'charach alai v'etenah* ("articulate your fee and I will pay it"), Lavan's words to Yaakov, which is read to mean that she must ask — *ad d'amrah mezuni lo yahavu lah* — "until she says, give me my sustenance, they do not give her."

Rashi renders this as a matter of prayer — *sheyeda l'hitpaleil* — turning the formula into a petition to God. But the more natural reading is that it reflects the social realities of the time: women lacked the opportunity and education for independent means of earning a living. Indeed, the *gemara* throughout assumes that the husband bears the obligation of *mezonos* for his wife precisely because the working assumption is that she cannot earn independently, even though the *gemara* certainly knew of counterexamples.

### Essentialist vs. Societal

What makes this *aggadata* more than a sociological observation is that it is framed as an essentialist claim — not merely that women in this society lacked economic opportunity, but that the nature of the male is to produce and the nature of the female is to be without. This raises the question of whether the Rabbis are describing a societal reality or encoding it as a metaphysical truth. The framing in the *gemara* slides toward the latter, which is notable.

There is also, one might add, an implicit anatomical dimension to this framing: ancient and medieval thinkers — the Rabbis perhaps among them — sometimes conceived of the female as a male who lacks something, since the female anatomy is internal and concealed. The imagery of the male arriving with something in hand while the female arrives empty fits naturally into that broader perspective, however uncomfortable it may be from a modern vantage point.

### Shalom Debate

The *shalom* dimension of the opening teaching deserves its own examination. The straightforward reading might be that the birth of a boy brings domestic joy and harmony. But one might equally ask: men are historically the ones who go to war, so in what sense does the arrival of a male bring *shalom*? A more compelling reading is that *shalom* here means protection and security — the male as the protector of the household. The word *shalom* thus carries a sense not of peace in the abstract but of the physical safety and security that a male presence was understood to provide in that cultural context.

## Korban & Labor Oath

### Students' Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה יוֹלֶדֶת מְבִיאָה קׇרְבָּן?
>
> **Translation:** **The students of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai asked him: For what** reason **does the Torah say** that **a woman after childbirth brings an offering?**

The *gemara* turns to a famous piece of *aggadata* in which the students of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai pose the question: *mipnei mah amrah Torah yoledet meviah korban?* — why does a woman who gives birth have to bring a *korban*? This question rests on an implicit assumption that a *chatas* is about sin. As we have studied at length in *Kerisos* and *Shavuos*, a *chatas* is not primarily about sin; rather, it belongs to the category of *mechusrei kapparah*, those whose *tumah* is concluded through the bringing of a *korban* — a *zav*, a *metzora*, a *yoledet*, and similar cases. The *gemara* in *Shavuos* says this explicitly. Yet here the students treat the *chatas* as a response to transgression and want to know what sin was committed.

### Labor Oath Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכּוֹרַעַת לֵילֵד, קוֹפֶצֶת וְנִשְׁבַּעַת שֶׁלֹּא תִּזָּקֵק לְבַעְלָהּ, לְפִיכָךְ אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה תָּבִיא קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: At the time that** a woman **crouches to give birth,** her pain is so great that **she impulsively takes an oath that she will not engage in intercourse with her husband** ever again, so that she will never again experience this pain. **Therefore, the Torah says** that **she must bring an offering** for violating her oath and continuing to engage in intercourse with her husband.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai answers: *besha'ah shekora'at laleid, kofetzet venishba'at shelo tizakek leva'alah* — at the moment of crouching to give birth, she swears that she will never again have relations with her husband, because of the pain of childbirth. *Lefikach amrah Torah tavi korban* — therefore the Torah said she must bring a *korban*, because she will inevitably go back on that oath when she wishes to resume her marital life. Rashi explains it as the transgression of that *shevuah* that requires atonement.

### Rav Yosef Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וְהָא מְזִידָה הִיא, וּבַחֲרָטָה תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא! וְעוֹד — קׇרְבַּן שְׁבוּעָה (בעי) [בָּעֲיָא] אֵיתוֹיֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this** answer: **But isn't** the woman **an intentional violator** of her oath? **And** if she wishes that her oath be dissolved, so that she may engage in intercourse with her husband, **the matter depends on** her **regret** of her oath. One is obligated to bring an offering for violating an oath of an utterance only if his transgression is unwitting. **And furthermore,** if the purpose of the offering that a woman brings after childbirth is to atone for violating an oath, then **she** should be **required to bring** a female lamb or goat as **an offering,** which is the requirement of one who violated **an oath,** rather than the bird offering brought by a woman after childbirth.

*Matkif lah Rav Yosef*: Rav Yosef challenges this sharply. *Veha mezidah hi* — but she is a *mezid*, someone who transgresses willingly. She knew she had taken the oath and she knowingly violated it. *Vechi chatas al mezid teiti?* — can a *chatas*, which is for inadvertent sin, apply to an intentional transgression? Furthermore, even if she felt genuine *charatah* and sought release from her oath, the mechanism for that is *hatarat nedarim*, not a *korban chatas*. And if there were a *korban* to bring for an oath, it would be a *korban shevuah*, a specific category of its own. The *gemara* offers no resolution, which effectively signals that the *gemara* was not fully persuaded by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's answer. The more fundamental answer — that the *korban* is not about sin at all but about concluding the state of *tumah* — remains, as stated elsewhere in the *gemara*, the more solid foundation.

## Tumah Periods

### One vs. Two Weeks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה זָכָר לְשִׁבְעָה וּנְקֵבָה לְאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר?
>
> **Translation:** **And** the students of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai further inquired of him: **For what** reason **does the Torah say** that a woman who gives birth to **a male** is ritually impure **for seven** days, **but** a woman who gives birth to **a female** is impure **for fourteen** days?

The *gemara* proceeds to another question in the same vein: *mipnei mah amrah Torah zachar leshiv'ah unekevah le'arba'ah asar?* — why does the Torah prescribe one week of *tumah* after the birth of a boy and two weeks after the birth of a girl? This is a pointed question, especially given that the physical process of childbirth does not differ so dramatically between the cases, at least not according to all views.

### Joy vs. Sadness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָכָר, שֶׁהַכֹּל שְׂמֵחִים בּוֹ — מִתְחָרֶטֶת לְשִׁבְעָה. נְקֵבָה, שֶׁהַכֹּל עֲצֵבִים בָּהּ — מִתְחָרֶטֶת לְאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** When a woman gives birth to **a male, over which everyone is happy, she regrets** her oath, that she will never again engage in intercourse with her husband, already **seven** days after giving birth. By contrast, after giving birth to **a female, over which everyone is unhappy, she regrets** her oath only **fourteen** days after giving birth.

The answer given is rooted in the emotional context of the birth. By a *zachar*, *shehakol smeichim bo mitcharata leshiv'ah* — because everyone is joyous at the birth of a boy, the mother regrets her oath after one week; she is happy enough to return to her husband. By a *nekevah*, *shehakol atzeivim bah mitcharata le'arba'ah asar* — because everyone is saddened, disappointed that it was not a boy, the mother remains in her distress for two weeks before she is willing to go back.

This is a striking passage, reflecting with uncomfortable directness the cultural assumption that the birth of a daughter was received with less joy than the birth of a son. There is, to some degree, an empirical truth embedded here that persists even today: in communities where a son receives a *bris* on the eighth day, the birth of a boy carries a built-in communal celebration that the birth of a girl does not automatically receive. If someone in a *haredi* community announces "so-and-so had a baby," the assumption is often that it was a girl — because if it were a boy they would say so directly.

### Modern Brachot Rulings

This has *halachic* ramifications as well. Classically, the birth of a boy would occasion a *bracha* of *Shehecheyanu* or *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, while the birth of a girl would not. Contemporary *poskim*, however — not merely the most modern ones but a meaningful range of later authorities — have ruled that since societal attitudes have genuinely shifted and people do celebrate the birth of daughters with real *simcha*, one may and should recite *Shehecheyanu* and *HaTov VeHaMeitiv* for a daughter as well. The *HaTov VeHaMeitiv* is recited over wine, not directly over the child. This evolution in *p'sak* is itself instructive: it acknowledges that the *gemara*'s distinction was rooted in societal norms rather than timeless metaphysics.

The *gemara* also notes that the *bris milah* is performed on the eighth day — which corresponds to the end of the mother's week of *tumah* after a boy — so that the husband and wife can be together at the time of the communal *simcha*. This connection no longer applies in the same way given later developments in *hilchos niddah*.

## Niddah Seven Days

### Why Seven Days?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי מָה אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה נִדָּה לְשִׁבְעָה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרָגִיל בָּהּ וְקָץ בָּהּ. אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״תְּהֵא טְמֵאָה שִׁבְעָה יָמִים״, כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא חֲבִיבָה עַל בַּעְלָהּ כִּשְׁעַת כְּנִיסָתָהּ לַחוּפָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: For what** reason **does the Torah say** that **a menstruating woman** is prohibited from engaging in intercourse with her husband **for seven** days? It is **because** if a woman were permitted to engage in intercourse with her husband all the time, her husband would be too **accustomed to her, and** would eventually be **repulsed by her.** Therefore, **the Torah says** that a menstruating woman **shall be ritually impure** for **seven days,** during which she is prohibited from engaging in intercourse with her husband, **so that** when she becomes pure again **she will be dear to her husband as** at **the time when she entered the wedding canopy** with him.

A *Baraisa* is cited in the name of Rabbi Meir: *mipnei mah amrah Torah niddah leshiv'ah?* — why did the Torah prescribe seven days for a *niddah*? The question has force because most bodily *tumos* that are natural — relating to the living body rather than death or animal carcasses — last only a single day. A *ba'al keri*, for instance, is *tamei* for only one day. Why should *niddah* be seven days?

### Familiarity Remedy

The answer is: *mipnei sheragil bah vekatzah bah* — because a husband becomes too accustomed to his wife, and familiarity leads to a kind of boredom or dulling of desire. *Amrah Torah tehei temei'ah shivah yamim* — therefore the Torah said she should be *temei'ah* for seven days, *kedei shetehei chavivah al baalah kesha'at kenisatah lachuppah* — so that she should be as beloved to her husband as at the moment she entered the *chuppah*. The seven-day separation is a mechanism for renewal, for resetting the emotional and physical intimacy of the marriage to something closer to its original freshness.

### Modern Marriage Context

There is genuine psychological truth to this. Statistics on long-term marriages show a consistent pattern of declining frequency of intimate relations, driven precisely by the dynamic the *gemara* describes — everything becoming routine, familiar, undifferentiated. The *gemara*'s answer identifies this tendency and offers the *niddah* cycle as a structural corrective. It is worth noting that this should not be taught with false idealism, as though the *mikvah* night will always be experienced as a wedding night and expectations will always be met. Setting realistic expectations matters. But the core insight — that periodic separation followed by reunion can restore a sense of novelty and desire — is well-founded.

### Rambam Analogy

The Rambam in the *Guide to the Perplexed* offers a related observation about *Beis HaMikdash*: one of the purposes of *tumah* and *taharah* rules is to create periods during which one cannot enter the *Beis HaMikdash*, so that when access is restored, the encounter carries weight and appreciation. The analogy is instructive even if imperfect: the *Beis HaMikdash* is something one wants to experience rarely and at special moments of elevation, whereas marriage is something that should be regular and sustaining. The mechanism is similar — restricted access creates renewed appreciation — but the goal is different in each case.

## Gender Dynamics

### Man Pursues Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי בְּרַבִּי יַנַּאי: מִפְּנֵי מָה אִישׁ מְחַזֵּר עַל אִשָּׁה, וְאֵין אִשָּׁה מְחַזֶּרֶת עַל אִישׁ? מָשָׁל לְאָדָם (שאבד) [שֶׁאָבְדָה] לוֹ אֲבֵידָה — מִי מְחַזֵּר עַל מִי? בַּעַל אֲבֵידָה מְחַזֵּר עַל אֲבֵידָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The students of Rabbi Dostai, son of Rabbi Yannai, asked him: For what** reason is it the norm that **a man pursues a woman** for marriage, **but a woman does not pursue a man?** Rabbi Dostai answered them by citing **a parable of a person who lost an item. Who searches for what?** Certainly **the owner of the lost item searches for his item;** the item does not search for its owner. Since the first woman was created from the body of the first man, the man seeks that which he has lost.

A *Baraisa* from the school of Rabbi Dostai bar Yannai asks: *mipnei mah ish mechazeir al ha-ishah ve'ein ishah mechazeret al ha-ish?* — why is it that the man pursues the woman in courtship and not the reverse? Both need to marry; why is the man generally the more active party?

The answer: *mashal le-adam she-avad lo aveidah* — it is like a person who has lost an object. *Mi mechazeir al mi? Ba'al ha-aveidah mechazeir al aveidato* — who looks for whom? The one who lost the object searches for what he lost; the lost object does not search for its owner. This echoes back to the narrative of *Bereishit*, where *Chavah* is formed from *Adam* — something that was part of him and was taken from him. When he encounters her he says *etzem mi-atzamai u-basar mi-besari* — he has found the part of himself that was missing. He is the one who lacks, and so he is the one who searches.

The metaphor is in one sense beautiful: the man is not pursuing something alien but recovering something that completes him, makes him whole. At the same time, the use of the word *aveidah* — a lost object — is undeniably possessive. The woman here is framed as an object that belongs to someone rather than as a subject who seeks completion herself. A *Rosh Yeshiva* once cited this *gemara* at the opening of a *zman* of *Masechet Kiddushin* and quipped to students who were slow to marry: if you are looking for an *aveidah*, you will find it; if you are looking for a *metziah*, it will take forever.

### Downward/Upward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אִישׁ פָּנָיו לְמַטָּה, וְאִשָּׁה פָּנֶיהָ לְמַעְלָה כְּלַפֵּי הָאִישׁ? זֶה מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרָא, וְזוֹ מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרֵאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the students of Rabbi Dostai further asked him: **For what** reason does **a man** engage in intercourse **facing down, and a woman** engage in intercourse **facing up toward the man?** Rabbi Dostai answered them: **This** man faces **the place from which he was created,** i.e., the earth, **and that** woman faces **the place from which she was created,** namely man.

The *gemara* continues: *mipnei mah ish panav le-mata ve-ishah paneha le-ma'ala?* — why does the man face downward and the woman face upward during marital relations, assuming this to be the standard position? *Zeh mi-makom she-nivra ve-zeh mi-makom she-nivra* — each faces toward the place from which they were created. The man looks down toward the earth, from which he was formed. The woman looks up toward the man, from whom she was formed. One might read this as the man expressing humility before his earthly origins, but the framing also reinforces a sense of the woman orienting herself toward the man as her source, which carries obvious symbolic weight in terms of hierarchy and dependence.

### Appeasement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִפְּנֵי מָה הָאִישׁ מְקַבֵּל פִּיּוּס, וְאֵין אִשָּׁה מְקַבֶּלֶת פִּיּוּס? זֶה מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרָא, וְזוֹ מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרֵאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the students also inquired: **For what** reason is **a man** who is angry likely to **accept appeasement, but a woman** is **not** as likely to **accept appeasement?** Rabbi Dostai answered them: It is **because this** man behaves like **the place from which he was created,** i.e., the earth, which yields to pressure, **and that** woman behaves like **the place from which she was created,** i.e., from bone, which cannot be molded easily.

*Umipnei mah ha'ish mekabel piyus ve'ein ha'ishah mekabeles piyus?* — why is a man easily appeased when angered, while a woman is not? Again, *zeh mi-makom she-nivra ve-zo mi-makom she-nivret*. The man, created from earth, is like earth — pliable, moldable, amenable to tilling and replanting. The woman, created from bone, is harder and less yielding. Rashi glosses this as *no'ach leratzos* — easy to pacify or to win over — and some read *piyus* here specifically in the marital context, noting that a man can more readily be persuaded toward intimacy than a woman, which also rings true.

### Voice Beauty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי מָה אִשָּׁה קוֹלָהּ עָרֵב, וְאֵין אִישׁ קוֹלוֹ עָרֵב? זֶה מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרָא, וְזוֹ מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁנִּבְרֵאת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי קוֹלֵךְ עָרֵב וּמַרְאֵךְ נָאוֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** The students continued to ask Rabbi Dostai: **For what** reason **is a woman's voice pleasant, but a man's voice is not pleasant?** He answered: **This** man is similar to **the place from which he was created,** the earth, which does not issue a sound when it is struck, **and that** woman is similar to **the place from which she was created,** a bone, which makes a sound when it is struck. The proof that a woman's voice is pleasant is **that it is stated** in Song of Songs that the man says to his beloved: **"For sweet is your voice, and your countenance is beautiful"** (Song of Songs 2:14).

Finally: *umipnei mah ha'ishah kolah arev ve'ein ha'ish kolo arev?* — why does a woman have a beautiful, pleasing voice while a man does not? Again the same formula: *zeh mi-makom she-nivra ve-zo mi-makom she-nivret*. The man was formed from earth, which produces no melody. The woman was formed from bone, and Rashi notes that bone — when fashioned into an instrument — produces resonant, pleasing sound. The verse *ki kolech arev umarech naveh* ("for your voice is pleasing and your appearance is lovely") is cited as confirmation that women possess beautiful voices, though it serves as proof of the fact rather than explanation of the cause.

## Benot Kutim

### Status Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' בְּנוֹת כּוּתִים נִדּוֹת מֵעֲרִיסָתָן, וְהַכּוּתִים מְטַמְּאִים מִשְׁכָּב תַּחְתּוֹן כָּעֶלְיוֹן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן בּוֹעֲלֵי נִדּוֹת, וְהֵן יוֹשְׁבוֹת עַל כׇּל דָּם וָדָם. וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן עַל בִּיאַת מִקְדָּשׁ, וְאֵין שׂוֹרְפִין עֲלֵיהֶם אֶת הַתְּרוּמָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁטּוּמְאָתָן סָפֵק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Samaritan **girls** are considered **menstruating women from** the time they lie in **their cradle. And the Samaritan** men **impart ritual impurity** to the **lower bedding like** the **upper** bedding, i.e., all layers of bedding beneath them are impure, and their status is like the bedding above a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*]: The status of both levels of bedding is that of first-degree ritual impurity, which can impart impurity to food and drink. This is **due to** the fact **that** Samaritan men are considered men who **engage in intercourse with menstruating women.** **And** they are considered men who engage in intercourse with menstruating women because Samaritan women **observe** the seven-day menstrual period of ritual impurity **for each and every** emission of **blood,** even for blood that does not render them impure. Accordingly, if a Samaritan woman has an emission of impure blood during the seven-day period, she will nevertheless continue counting seven days from the first emission. It is therefore possible that the Samaritan men will engage in intercourse with their wives while they are still halakhically considered menstruating women, as the seven-day period of impurity should have been counted from the emission of the impure blood. **But** one who enters the Temple while wearing **those** garments upon which a Samaritan had lain **is not liable** to bring an offering **for entering the Temple** in a status of impurity, **nor does one burn *teruma*** that came into contact with **those** garments, **because their impurity** is **uncertain.**

With the conclusion of the *aggadata* we turn to the opening of a new *Mishnah* — *benot Kutim niddot me'arisatan*. The daughters of the *Kutim* — the Samaritans — are considered *niddot* from their very cradles, from the moment of birth. *Kutim* were Samaritans, a population that maintained certain Jewish practices but not all, and whose halachic status oscillated between Jewish and non-Jewish. The *gemara* works within the assumption that they are Jewish, but that they do not observe the details of *hilchos niddah* in the same manner.

The *Mishnah* adds that the *Kutim* men themselves — *metamme'im mishkav tachton ke'elyon* — render the lower couch or seat *tamei* like the upper one, the logic being that since their wives are always considered *niddot*, the men are always considered *bo'alei niddot*, and this status renders whatever they sit or lie upon *tamei* in the manner of *mishkav u'moshav*. *Mipnei she'hen bo'alei niddot* — because they are treated as having had relations with *niddot*, they carry the corresponding *tumah*.

There is a significant *Tosafos* here. Elsewhere in the *gemara* (in the tractate of *Shabbat*), these *gezeiros* against *Kutim* are listed among the eighteen things enacted by *Beis Shammai*, many of which involve *tumah* and *taharah* and others of which concern limiting interaction with non-Jews. *Tosafos* reconciles the two sources by noting that even if the motivation behind some of these *gezeiros* was to erect social barriers, a *gezeirah* cannot be entirely a fiction — it must be rooted in some halachic reality. Hence our *gemara* focuses on the actual halachic ground for treating *benot Kutim* as *niddot*.

### Blood Color Indistinction

The reason given is: *ve'hen yoshvot al kol dam va'dam* — they sit upon every type of blood. That is, they do not distinguish between the different colors of blood, some of which render a woman *tamei* and some of which do not. Whatever blood they see, they treat as *niddah* blood. One might think this is a *chumra* — they're being stricter than the law requires. But in fact this is a textbook case of *chumra de'ati lidei kula* — a stringency that produces a leniency.

### Stringency/Leniency Para…

The mechanism works as follows. If a woman begins to bleed with a color that is not actually a *niddah* color — say, a brownish discharge two or three days before the true red flow — and she begins counting her seven days from that non-qualifying blood rather than from the onset of the actual qualifying *niddah* flow, her seven-day count ends too early. She will go to the *mikvah* while she is still technically a *niddah*, and thus never actually becomes *tehorah*. Her *chumra* of counting from the earliest drop, regardless of color, causes her to go to the *mikvah* at the wrong time, and her *tumah* is never resolved. This is the fundamental problem: the stringency of not distinguishing blood colors ends up producing the leniency of never actually achieving *taharah*.

### Safek Status

Despite all this, the *Mishnah* qualifies the consequence: *ve'ein chayavin aleihen bi'at mikdash* — if someone who became *tamei* through contact with a Samaritan woman entered the *Beis HaMikdash*, they are not liable for *karet*, because the *tumah* is a matter of *safek* — we do not know with certainty that this particular woman was indeed a *niddah* at the time. Similarly, *ve'ein sorfin aleihen et ha'terumah* — if a Samaritan woman touches *terumah*, it is not burned, because there remains a possibility that she was in fact *tehorah*. The ruling is: treat them as *tamei* for practical purposes, but since it is never a certainty, the severe consequences reserved for definite *tumah* do not apply.

## Infant Emissions

### Actual Bleeding?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּקָא חָזְיָין — אֲפִילּוּ דִּידַן נָמֵי, וְאִי דְּלָא קָחָזְיָין — דִּידְהוּ נָמֵי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that Samaritan girls are considered menstruating women from the time they lie in their cradle. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this statement? **If** the mishna is referring to girls **who** already **see** menstrual blood, then **even our own,** i.e., Jewish girls, are **also** considered menstruating women under such circumstances. **And if** it is referring to girls **who do not** yet **see** menstrual blood, then **their** girls, i.e., those of the Samaritans, should **also not** have the status of menstruating women.

The *gemara* opens with the classic *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If Samaritan infants actually bleed (*ei de'chazya*), then our infants would also be subject to the same concern — *afilu didan nami*. And if they do not bleed (*ei de'la kachazya*), then *didehu nami lo* — their infants are also not a concern, and the ruling seems to be baseless either way.

### Minority Possibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בִּסְתָמָא, דְּכֵיוָן דְּאִיכָּא מִיעוּטָא דְּחָזְיָין — חָיְשִׁינַן. וּמַאן תַּנָּא דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava, son of Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna, says** that **Rav Sheshet says: Here we are dealing with an unspecified** case, i.e., it is unknown whether these girls have experienced their first menstrual period. **Since there is a minority** of girls **who see** menstrual blood, **we are concerned** with regard to each Samaritan girl that she might be from this minority. The Gemara asks: **And who** is the *tanna* who **taught that one must be concerned for the minority?**

Rava, son of Rav Acha bar Rav Huna, citing Rav Sheshet, resolves this: *hacha be'mai askinan be'stama* — we are dealing with a case of uncertainty, where we simply do not know whether a given infant has bled. And *de'keivan de'ika mi'uta de'chazya* — since there is a small minority of infants who do in fact experience some bleeding, we are *chaishinan* — concerned — that it may have occurred. The *gemara* then asks: whose view is this in the *Mishnah*, that we are concerned for a minority? The answer is Rabbi Meir.

### Rabbi Meir Minority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: קָטָן וּקְטַנָּה לֹא חוֹלְצִין וְלֹא מְיַבְּמִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: יָפֶה אָמַרְתָּ שֶׁאֵין חוֹלְצִין, ״אִישׁ״ כָּתוּב בַּפָּרָשָׁה, וּמַקְּשִׁינַן אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ. וּמָה טַעַם אֵין מְיַבְּמִין? אָמַר לָהֶן: קָטָן — שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא סָרִיס, קְטַנָּה — שֶׁמָּא תִּמָּצֵא אַיְלוֹנִית, וְנִמְצְאוּ פּוֹגְעִין בְּעֶרְוָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם מִצְוָה. וְרַבָּנַן? זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא דִּקְטַנִּים, וְרוֹב קְטַנִּים לָאו סָרִיסִים נִינְהוּ; זִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא דִּקְטַנּוֹת, וְרוֹב קְטַנּוֹת לָאו אַיְלוֹנִיּוֹת נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **It is Rabbi Meir, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A minor boy and a minor girl may not perform the ritual through which a *yavam* frees a *yevama* of her levirate bonds [*ḥalitza*], nor may they enter into levirate marriage.** In other words, a minor boy whose brother died childless may not perform *ḥalitza* with his brother's widow, nor may he enter into levirate marriage with her, even if she is an adult. Likewise, a minor girl whose husband died childless may not perform *ḥalitza* with her husband's brother, nor may she enter into levirate marriage with him, even if he is an adult. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Meir: You have aptly stated that they may not perform *ḥalitza*,** since **"man" is written in the passage** of the Torah discussing *ḥalitza* (Deuteronomy 25:7), **and we compare a woman to a man,** as the aforementioned verse states: "And if the man does not want to take his brother's wife." Consequently, neither a minor boy nor a minor girl may perform *ḥalitza*. **But what is the reason** that **they may not enter into levirate marriage?** Rabbi Meir **said to them: A minor boy** may not enter into levirate marriage **lest,** once he is older, **he be found** to be **a sexually underdeveloped man,** who is incapable of fathering children. Likewise, **a minor girl** may not enter into levirate marriage **lest,** once she is older, **she be found** to be **a sexually underdeveloped woman. And** if a sexually underdeveloped boy or girl enters into levirate marriage **they will be found** to be **infringing upon** prohibitions against **forbidden sexual intercourse where no mitzva applies,** as the entire purpose of levirate marriage is to bear children in the name of the deceased. The Gemara notes: **And the Rabbis** maintain that one **follows the majority of minor boys, and most minor boys are not** going to be **sexually underdeveloped men;** likewise, one **follows the majority of** minor girls, **and most** minor girls **are not** going to be **sexually underdeveloped women.** In any event, the *baraita* indicates that Rabbi Meir is concerned for the minority.

The *gemara* cites a *Braisa*. *Katan ve'ketanah lo choltzin ve'lo meyabemin* — a minor boy or girl cannot perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum*. Rabbi Meir forbids *yibbum* for minors not because there is a formal age requirement, but because *katan shema yimatze saris, ketanah shema timatze aylonit* — there is a minority possibility that the boy will prove to be a *saris* (infertile), or that the girl will prove to be an *aylonit* (infertile). If either is the case, the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* was never present, and the intercourse constituted a forbidden relationship with an *ervah* without the justifying *mitzvah* — *venimtze'u pog'in be'ervah shelo bimkom mitzvah*. The Rabbis respond: *zil batar rubba* — go by the majority. Most minor boys are not *sarisim* and most minor girls are not *ayloniyot*. The debate is thus whether we act on a minority possibility.

The *gemara* then introduces a limiting principle: Rabbi Meir is concerned for a minority when it is *miyuta deshchiach* — a minority that is not exceedingly rare. A minority that is *lo shchiach* — almost never encountered — would not, even by Rabbi Meir, generate a *gezeirah*. The question then is whether infant bleeding falls into the category of *shchiach*. The *gemara* maintains that it does, based on actual reported cases.

### Historical Stories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי מִיעוּטָא דִּשְׁכִיחַ הוּא. דְּתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּעֵין בּוּל וְהִטְבִּילוּהָ קוֹדֶם לְאִמָּהּ, וְאָמַר רַבִּי: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבֵית שְׁעָרִים וְהִטְבִּילוּהָ קוֹדֶם לְאִמָּהּ. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא וְהִטְבִּילוּהָ קוֹדֶם לְאִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This** minority of young girls who menstruate **is also a common minority. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said:** There was **an incident in** the town of **Ein Bul** where **they immersed** a baby girl in a ritual bath **before her mother.** In other words, the baby girl experienced bleeding so soon after birth that her immersion in a ritual bath occurred before her mother immersed fourteen days after giving birth. **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi likewise **said:** There was **an incident in Beit She'arim** where **they immersed** a baby girl **before her mother. And Rav Yosef said:** There was **an incident in Pumbedita** where **they immersed** a baby girl **before her mother.**

Three stories are adduced. Rabbi Yosi reports a case in *Ein Bul* where an infant girl was taken to the *mikvah* before her mother — the infant bled shortly after birth, began her seven-day *niddah* count, and reached the end of those seven days before her mother, whose post-partum *tumah* for a girl extended fourteen days. Rabbi (Rebbi) reports a similar case in *Beit She'arim*, and Rav Yosef reports a case in *Pumbedita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וּדְרַבִּי — מִשּׁוּם תְּרוּמַת אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֶלָּא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף לְמָה לִי? וְהָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין תְּרוּמַת חוּץ לָאָרֶץ אֲסוּרָה אֶלָּא בְּמִי שֶׁטּוּמְאָה יוֹצְאָה מִגּוּפוֹ, וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי בַּאֲכִילָה, אֲבָל בִּנְגִיעָה לָא! אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְסוּכָהּ שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ אֶת קׇדְשֵׁי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר יָרִימוּ לַה׳״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסָּךְ וְאֶת הַשּׁוֹתֶה. שׁוֹתָה לְמָה לִי קְרָא? שְׁתִיָּה בִּכְלַל אֲכִילָה! אֶלָּא, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסָּךְ כַּשּׁוֹתֶה. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״וַתָּבֹא כַמַּיִם בְּקִרְבּוֹ וְכַשֶּׁמֶן בְּעַצְמוֹתָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the immersions reported **by Rabbi Yosei and by Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi are understandable, **due to the *teruma* of Eretz Yisrael,** i.e., these incidents occurred in Eretz Yisrael, where the touch of a menstruating girl disqualifies *teruma*. **But** in the incident reported **by Rav Yosef,** which occurred in Babylonia, **why do I** need to immerse the baby girl? **But doesn't Shmuel say:** The ***teruma* of outside of Eretz Yisrael is prohibited only to one whose impurity** is due to **an emission from his body,** e.g., a menstruating woman, or one who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*]. **And this statement** applies only **with regard to eating** *teruma*, **but with regard to touching** *teruma*, there is **no** prohibition. Since the touch of a menstruating woman does not disqualify *teruma* outside Eretz Yisrael, why was it necessary to immerse the baby girl in the incident reported by Rav Yosef? **Mar Zutra says:** That immersion **was necessary only for smearing oil of *teruma*** of outside of Eretz Yisrael. Since smearing is equivalent to eating, it would have been prohibited to smear such oil on the baby girl, were it not for her immersion in a ritual bath. And from where is it derived that smearing is like eating with regard to *teruma*? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse discussing the prohibition against consuming *teruma* in a state of ritual impurity states: **"And they shall not desecrate the sacred items of the children of Israel, which they set apart for the Lord"** (Leviticus 22:15). The verse serves **to include** in this prohibition **one who smears and one who drinks.** Mar Zutra continues: **Why do I** need **a verse** to teach that one who **drinks** *teruma* in a state of impurity is liable? Isn't **drinking included** in the category of **eating? Rather,** the *baraita* means that the verse serves **to include one who smears,** teaching that he is **like one who drinks. And if you wish, say** that one may derive that smearing is like drinking **from here: "And it came into his innards like water, and like oil into his bones"** (Psalms 109:18).

The *gemara* then notes a discrepancy. One can understand why Rabbi Yosi and Rebbi in *Eretz Yisrael* would bother taking the infant to the *mikvah*: there was *terumah* in *Eretz Yisrael* and one would not want the infant to touch it while *tamei*. But why would Rav Yosef in *Bavel* bother? Shmuel rules that the *terumah* of *chutz la'aretz* is only forbidden to someone whose *tumah* comes from their own body (*mi shetumah yotze'ah miguffo*) — a *ba'al keri*, a *zav*, a *zavah* — which would include a *niddah*. So the girl as a *niddah* is indeed covered by this prohibition. But the concern shifts: an infant cannot eat *terumah* in any case — she is months from eating solid food. And the *gemara* rules that the prohibition of *terumah chutz la'aretz* in this context applies to *achilah* — eating — but not to *negi'ah* — mere touching. So what was the concern?

Mar Zutra answers: *lo nitzrecha ella lesochah* — it was necessary because of *shemen shel terumah*, olive oil from *terumah*, which one wished to rub onto the infant as a lotion or ointment. The principle of *sichah k'shtiyah* — rubbing oil into the skin is halachically equivalent to drinking — renders this equivalent to ingesting the *terumah*, which a *niddah* may not do, even *terumah* of *chutz la'aretz* according to Shmuel's ruling. A *Baraisa* confirms: the verse *velo yichalelu et kodshei benei Yisrael et asher yarimu l'Adonai* teaches that *hasach* — anointing — is included in the prohibition alongside *achilah*, and the verse *vattava chamayim b'kirbo u'chashemen b'atzmosav* ("it entered like water within him and like oil into his bones") similarly equates oil absorption with drinking. This is why even in *Bavel*, where they would not worry about the infant touching *terumah*, they took her to the *mikvah* — so she could have *terumah* oil applied to her skin.

## Our vs. Their Infants

### Drasha from Tanakh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי דִּידַן נָמֵי? אֲנַן, דְּדָרְשִׁינַן ״אִשָּׁה״ ״וְאִשָּׁה״, וְכִי חָזְיָין (מפרשי) [מַפְרְשִׁינַן] לְהוּ — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן. אִינְהוּ דְּלָא דָּרְשִׁי ״אִשָּׁה״ ״וְאִשָּׁה״, וְכִי חָזְיָין לָא מַפְרְשִׁי לְהוּ — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** As it stands, the *halakha* that Samaritan girls are considered menstruating women from the time they lie in their cradle is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who is concerned for the minority of young girls who menstruate. The Gemara objects: **If so,** let us be concerned for the same minority with regard to **our** girls **as well.** The Gemara explains: There is no need to be concerned with regard to **our** young girls, **as we interpret** the verse: "And if a woman has an issue" (Leviticus 15:19), and derive from the fact that the verse does not merely state: **"A woman,"** but: **"And** if **a woman,"** that even minor girls are included in the *halakhot* of a menstruating woman. **And** consequently, **when** our girls **see** menstrual blood, **we separate them** in the manner of all menstruating women. Therefore, **the Sages did not decree with regard to them** that all young Jewish girls assume the status of menstruating women. By contrast, with regard to **them,** Samaritans, **who do not interpret** the difference between **"a woman"** and **"and** if **a woman," when** their girls **see** menstrual blood **they do not separate them,** and therefore **the Sages decreed with regard to them** that all Samaritan girls assume the status of menstruating women.

The *gemara* returns to the central question: if infant bleeding is a real if rare phenomenon, why does the ruling apply specifically to *benot Kutim* and not to our own infant daughters? The answer is: *anan dedarshinan ishah v'ishah* — we derive from the Torah (as will be spelled out momentarily) that even underage girls can become *niddot*, all the way down to one day old.

### Our Knowledge vs. Theirs

Because we have this *drasha* and apply it, *d'chi chazi mafreshinan lehu* — when we see that an infant girl has bled, we immediately treat her as *tamei*, separate her from *taharos*, and handle the situation appropriately. We know the *halacha* and we act on it.

### Gezeirah Logic

The *Kutim*, by contrast, *d'lo darshi ishah v'ishah* — do not have this *drasha* and do not accept that underage girls can become *niddot*. Therefore *v'chi chazi lo mafreshi lehu* — even when they see that an infant has bled, they do not separate her or treat her as *tamei*. Because they are systematically unaware of and inattentive to this *halacha*, the Rabbis were compelled to issue a *gezeirah* treating all of their infants as *tamei*. *Gazru behu rabbanan* — the Rabbis decreed. This is the operative logic: it is not that their infants are more likely to bleed than ours; it is that when ours do bleed we respond correctly, and when theirs do, nothing is done about it.

*Tosafos* raises a further question: once you have already declared all *benot Kutim tamei* as infants, why is a separate reason needed for declaring adult *Kutim* women *tamei* — namely, their failure to distinguish blood colors? If the infant *gezeirah* already applies to them, does it not follow through to adulthood? *Tosafos* answers that the infant *gezeirah* was issued specifically because *Kutim* do not recognize that infants can become *niddot*. But once they reach adulthood, one might have thought we could trust them. A separate and independent reason is needed to explain why we cannot trust adult *Kutim* women either — and that reason is their failure to distinguish between qualifying and non-qualifying blood colors.

## Extra Vav Niddah

### Infant Girl Niddah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אִשָּׁה״ ״וְאִשָּׁה״? דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִשָּׁה״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִשָּׁה, תִּינוֹקֶת בַּת יוֹם אֶחָד לְנִדָּה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What** is this interpretation of the difference between **"a woman"** and **"and** if **a woman"? As it is taught** in a *baraita* that from **"a woman" I have** derived **only** that the *halakhot* of menstruation apply to an adult **woman. From where** do I derive **that** the *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman** also apply to **a one-day-old girl? The verse states: "And** if **a woman."**

The *gemara* now spells out the *drasha* itself. *Mai ishah v'ishah?* — what is the derivation from the "extra" word? A *Baraisa* teaches: *isha, ein li ela isha* — from the word *isha* ("woman") I know only an adult woman can become *tamei* as a *niddah*. *Tinokes bat yom echad leniddah minayin?* — how do I derive that even an infant girl one day old can become a *niddah*? *Talmud lomar v'isha* — the extra *vav* (i.e., *ve'isha* rather than *isha*) teaches an additional case, including even a one-day-old infant.

### Vav Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, כִּי מְרַבֵּי קְרָא בַּת יוֹם אֶחָד מְרַבֵּי, וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״אִשָּׁה״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִשָּׁה, תִּינוֹקֶת בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד לְבִיאָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִשָּׁה״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Apparently, when the verse includes** young girls through the word "and" it **includes** even **a one-day-old. But** you can **raise a contradiction** from another *baraita*, which discusses the verse: "And the woman with whom a man shall lie carnally, they shall both bathe themselves in water, and be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 15:18). From the word **"woman" I have** derived **only** that the sexual intercourse of an adult **woman** is considered intercourse that renders her impure. **From where** do I derive that the **sexual intercourse of a girl aged three years and one day** is also classified as intercourse? **The verse states: And the woman.** Evidently, the word "and" includes only a girl aged three years and one day.

The *gemara* then raises a challenge: if the extra *vav* includes someone as young as one day old when we go below the normal age, why does a different *baraisa* seem to stop at three years old? For the *tumah* of *shichvat zera* — the *tumah* acquired through an act of *bi'ah* — the *Baraisa* states *isha ein li ela isha, tinokes bas shalosh shanim v'yom echad l'bi'ah minayin, talmud lomar v'isha*. Here the extra *vav* brings us down to three years and a day, not to one day old.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא נִינְהוּ, וְאַסְמְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן אַקְּרָאֵי. הֵי קְרָא וְהֵי הִלְכְתָא? אִילֵּימָא בַּת יוֹם אֶחָד — הִלְכְתָא, בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — קְרָא, קְרָא סְתָמָא כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא בַּת שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — הִלְכְתָא, בַּת יוֹם אֶחָד — קְרָא, וּמֵאַחַר דְּהִלְכְתָא, קְרָא לָמָּה לִי? לְמַעוֹטֵי אִישׁ מֵאוֹדֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: These are *halakhot*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and the Sages** merely **supported them with verses.** There is therefore no contradiction. The Gemara asks: **Which** *halakha* is derived from **a verse and which** is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai? **If we say** that the *halakha* that the status of a menstruating woman may apply to **a one-day-old** girl is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, and the *halakha* that the intercourse of a girl **aged three years and one day** is considered intercourse is derived from **a verse,** then one may object: But **the verse is written** in **an unspecified** manner; consequently, a one-day-old girl should be included by the verse in the same manner as a three-year-old girl. **Rather,** the *halakha* with regard to the intercourse of a girl **aged three years and one day** is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, whereas the *halakha* with regard to the menstruation of **a one-day-old** girl is derived from **a verse.** The Gemara asks: **And now that** it has been established that the *halakha* with regard to the intercourse of a three-year-old girl is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **why do I** need **a verse?**

Rava resolves this: *hilchata ninhu ve'asmi'chinhu rabbanan akra'ei* — both *shiurim* rest on tradition (*halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai*), and the Rabbis found scriptural support for each. The question is which *shiur* is the tradition and which is the *pasuk*. Rava concludes: the *shiur* of three years and one day for the definition of an act of *bi'ah* is the transmitted tradition, since no *pasuk* could independently yield such a specific number. The *pasuk* of *v'isha*, once it extends the category below the adult age, naturally extends all the way to the minimum — one day. Conversely, *bas yom echad* for *niddah* is rooted in the *pasuk*, while *bas shalosh shanim v'yom echad* is the tradition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַעוֹטֵי אִישׁ מֵאוֹדֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The verse serves **to exclude a man from** contracting ritual impurity due to **red** semen.

This leads to a further question: if the *pasuk* of *v'isha* by the *tumah* of *shichvat zera* is not needed for the *shiur* of three years (since that is *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai*), and if the one-day-old *niddah* can alternatively be inferred once we know that an infant can become a *zavah* (since there can be no *zivah* without *niddah* preceding it), what function does the *pasuk* of *v'isha* actually serve in each context? The *gemara* answers: *l'mautei ish mi'odem* — to exclude the male from the *tumah* of red blood. By emphasizing *v'isha* — the woman — the Torah underscores that *tumah* through blood or through *shichvat zera* applies to women in these forms and not to men. Specifically, two exclusions are operative: one by *niddah* and one by *shichvat zera*, excluding respectively (a) semen that appears red, and (b) actual blood that emerges from the male organ — neither renders the man *tamei* in the manner of a *niddah*.

### Zavah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּתַנְיָא: אִשָּׁה — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִשָּׁה, בַּת עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים לְזִיבָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְאִשָּׁה״. לְמָה לִי? לִיגְמַר מִנִּדָּה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** what about **that which is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to a woman who experiences a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period [*zava*]: The verse states: "And if a woman has an issue of her blood many days" (Leviticus 15:25). From the word **"woman" I have** derived **only** that *ziva* applies to an adult **woman. From where** do I derive **that a ten-day-old** girl is included in the *halakhot* of ***ziva*? The verse states: "And** if **a woman." Why do I** need the verse? **Let one derive** that a ten-day-old girl is included in the *halakhot* of *ziva* **from** the fact that a one-day-old girl is included in the *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman,** as a woman may become a *zava* only after seven days of menstruation and three subsequent days of experiencing uterine discharge.

The *gemara* then examines the parallel by *zavah*: *isha ein li ela isha, bas yud yamim v'zivah minayin?* — I know only an adult woman can become a *zavah*; how do I know that a girl as young as ten days old can become a *zavah*? *Talmud lomar v'isha* — the extra *vav* by *zavah*. The *gemara* asks why this separate derivation is needed; once we know an infant girl can become a *niddah*, why can't we infer that she can become a *zavah* as well?

### Female Exclusivity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּנִדָּה — הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: נִדָּה, מִשּׁוּם דְּכִי חֲזַאי חַד יוֹמָא בָּעֲיָא לְמֵיתַב שִׁבְעָה; אֲבָל זָבָה, דְּאִי חֲזַאי חַד יוֹמָא — בְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יוֹם כְּנֶגֶד יוֹם סַגִּי לַהּ, אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It was **necessary** for the verse to teach that the *halakhot* of *ziva* apply to a ten-day-old girl. **As, if the Merciful One had written** only that a one-day-old girl is included **in** the *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman, I would say:** The *halakhot* of **a menstruating woman** apply to a one-day-old girl **because** of their stringency, **as when** a woman **sees blood** on only **one day she is required to sit** for the **seven** days of menstruation. **But** with regard to **a *zava*,** since the *halakha* is **that if** a woman **sees** blood on only **one day** she has the status of a lesser *zava*, and it is **enough for her** to **observe** a clean **day for a day** she experiences a discharge, one might **say** that the *halakhot* of *ziva* do **not** apply to a ten-day-old girl. It was therefore **necessary** for the verse to indicate otherwise.

The *gemara* answers that neither direction of inference works cleanly on its own. Had the Torah taught only *niddah* for infants, one might have said that *niddah* applies to infants because it is strict — a single day of bleeding generates a seven-day *tumah* — and therefore extends even to infants. But *zivah* is less strict — a single day of bleeding requires only a one-day watch — and one might have thought it would not apply to infants. Conversely, had the Torah taught only *zavah* for infants, one might have argued: *zavah* applies because even *zivah* be'ones — bleeding due to external trauma — creates *tumah* for a woman, making *zivah* strict in its own way, and therefore even infants are included. But one could not then infer *niddah*, since we cannot infer one category from the other in either direction. Both are necessary. The *pasuk*s of *v'isha* by *niddah* and *v'isha* by *zavah* each do independent work and cannot be reduced to each other.

## Ish Ish Zav

### Ish Ish Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן זְכָרִים. דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״ — מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״? לְרַבּוֹת תִּינוֹק בִּן יוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁהוּא מְטַמֵּא בְּזִיבָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the *halakha* of *ziva* with regard to a male: **And so with regard to males,** the *halakhot* of a *zav* apply even to minor boys. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states concerning a *zav*: "When **any man** has an issue out of his flesh, his issue is impure" (Leviticus 15:2). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states "any man"?** The verse serves **to include a one-day-old baby,** teaching **that** even **he is susceptible to impurity of *ziva*.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* now turns to the parallel derivation for males. *V'chen b'zcharim* — the same logic of including infants applies to boys. The *Baraisa*: *ish ish* — the double expression of *ish* (*ish ish ki yihyeh zav*) — *mah talmud lomar ish ish? l'rabos tinok ben yom echad she'metamei b'ziva* — the double *ish* serves to include a one-day-old infant boy who is able to become a *zav*. This is in the name of Rabbi Yehudah. The ability of an infant to become a *zav* is already accessible from the first day of life because the requirements for male *zivah* do not include a preceding *niddah* period — three separate sightings of the discharge suffice, potentially even on the same day.

### Yishmael Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אֵין צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לַזָּכָר וְלַנְּקֵבָה״ — ״לַזָּכָר״ כֹּל שֶׁהוּא זָכָר, בֵּין שֶׁהוּא גָּדוֹל בֵּין שֶׁהוּא קָטָן, ״וְלַנְּקֵבָה״ כֹּל שֶׁהִיא נְקֵבָה, בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה בֵּין קְטַנָּה. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ אִישׁ״? דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says:** This derivation **is not necessary, as** the verse **states:** "And of them that have an issue, **whether it be a male or a female"** (Leviticus 15:33). **"Whether it be a male"** includes **anyone who is a male, whether he is an adult** or **whether he is a minor; "or a female"** includes **anyone who is a female, whether** she is **an adult** or **whether** she is **a minor. If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states "any man"? The Torah spoke in the language of people,** and one is not meant to derive anything from this verse.

Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka disagrees: *ein tzarich* — no derivation is necessary. *Harei hu omer l'zachar u'l'nekeivah* — the Torah states explicitly "male or female" in the context of *zivah*. *L'zachar kol she'hu zachar bein gadol bein katan* — "male" means any male, adult or minor. *Ul'nekeivah kol she'hi nekeivah bein gedolah bein ketanah* — and "female" means any female, adult or minor. If so, why does the Torah say *ish ish* at all?

### Divine Language

Rabbi Yishmael's answer: *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of human language. The doubling of *ish* is simply a stylistic feature of biblical Hebrew, not a hermeneutical signal. Therefore one cannot derive the inclusion of minors from *ish ish*, because that formulation carries no additional legal force — it is simply how people speak.

## Both Genders Needed

### Male Zav Strictness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא, כִּי מְרַבֵּי קְרָא — בֶּן יוֹם אֶחָד מְרַבֵּי. וּרְמִינְהוּ: ״אִישׁ״ — אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ, בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Apparently, when the verse includes** a minor boy it **includes** even **a one-day-old. But raise a contradiction** from another *baraita*, which addresses the verse: "And if the flow of seed go out from a man, then he shall bathe all his flesh in water and be impure until the evening" (Leviticus 15:16). From the word **"man" I have** derived **only** that **a man** is rendered ritually impure through a seminal emission. **From where** do I derive that the seminal emission of a boy **aged nine years and one day** renders him impure as well? **The verse states: "And a man."** Evidently, the verse does not include a one-day-old boy.

The *gemara* asks why we need both the derivation for underage girls (*v'isha*) and the derivation for underage boys (*ish ish* or *l'zachar u'l'nekeivah*) — why not derive one gender from the other? The answer is that the *tumah* categories differ between male and female in ways that preclude cross-inference. For males, *zivah* is in some respects strict: *mishum demitmu bire'iyot kiveyamim* — a male becomes a *zav* through three sightings of the discharge, which can all occur on the same day, without any requirement that they fall on separate days. Because male *zivah* is such an expansive category in this sense, one might readily assume it extends even to infants.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא נִינְהוּ, וְאַסְמְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן אַקְּרָאֵי. הֵי הִלְכְתָא וְהֵי קְרָא? אִילֵּימָא בֶּן יוֹם אֶחָד — הִלְכְתָא, וּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — קְרָא, קְרָא סְתָמָא כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — הִלְכְתָא, וּבֶן יוֹם אֶחָד — קְרָא. וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּהִלְכְתָא הִיא, קְרָא לְמָה לִי? לְמַעוֹטֵי אִשָּׁה מִלּוֹבֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: These are *halakhot*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and the Sages** merely **supported them with verses.** Therefore, there is no contradiction. The Gemara asks: **Which is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and which** *halakha* **is** derived from **a verse? If we say** that the *halakha* that **a one-day-old** boy is included in the *halakhot* of a *zav* is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and** the *halakha* that the seminal emission of a boy **aged nine years and one day** renders him impure is derived from **a verse,** then one may object: But **the verse is written** in **an unspecified** manner; consequently, even a one-day-old boy should be included in the verse. **Rather,** the *halakha* with regard to the seminal emission of a boy **aged nine years and one day** is **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and** the *halakha* that **a one-day-old** boy is included in the *halakhot* of a *zav* is derived from **a verse.** The Gemara asks: **And now that** the *halakha* with regard to the seminal emission of a boy aged nine years and one day renders **is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **why do I** need **a verse?** The Gemara answers: The verse serves **to exclude a woman from** contracting the impurity of a *zava* due to **a white** discharge.

### Female Zavah Strictness

For females, the *tumah* of *zivah* requires three days of bleeding on three separate days — a more specific and in that sense stricter requirement. One might have thought that because this category is more circumscribed, it would not extend to infants. On the other hand, female *zivah* is strict in a different dimension: *ba'ah be'ones* — bleeding caused by trauma or an external force still creates *tumah* for a woman, whereas the analogous situation for a man — an emission caused by illness or something he ate — does not make him a *zav*. Because each category has its own dimensions of stringency and leniency, no inference from one to the other is valid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמִכְתַּב בִּזְכָרִים, וּלְמָה לִי לְמִכְתַּב בִּנְקֵבוֹת? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בִּזְכָרִים — מִשּׁוּם דִּמְטַמּוּ בִּרְאִיּוֹת כִּבְיָמִים, אֲבָל נְקֵבוֹת דְּלָא מְטַמּוּ בִּרְאִיּוֹת כִּבְיָמִים — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא בִּנְקֵבוֹת — מִשּׁוּם דְּקָמְטַמּוּ בְּאוֹנֶס, אֲבָל זְכָרִים דְּלָא מְטַמּוּ בְּאוֹנֶס — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need for the Merciful One **to write** that a one-day-old is included in the *halakhot* of *ziva* **with regard to males, and why do I** need for the Merciful One **to write** that a one-day-old is included in the *halakhot* of a menstruating woman and a ten-day-old is included in the *halakhot* of *ziva* **with regard to females?** The Gemara answers that both verses **are necessary. As, if the Merciful One had written** only **concerning males,** one might say: A one-day-old boy is included in the *halakhot* of *ziva* **because** the *halakha* is more stringent with regard to males, **as they are rendered impure through** three **sightings** of *ziva* on one day just **like through** sightings on three consecutive **days. But** with regard to **females, who are not rendered impure through** three **sightings** on one day **as** they are **through** sightings on three consecutive **days,** one might **say** that the *halakhot* of *ziva* do **not** apply to ten-day-old girls. **And** by contrast, **if the Merciful One had written** only **with regard to females,** one might say: The *halakhot* of *ziva* apply to ten-day-old girls, **because** of the fact that **they are rendered impure** even on account of sightings that occur **due to circumstances beyond** their **control. But** with regard to **males, who are not rendered impure** on account of sightings that occur **due to circumstances beyond** their **control,** one might **say** that one-day-old boys are **not** included in the *halakhot* of *ziva*. Therefore, both verses are **necessary.**

### No Cross-Gender Inference

The conclusion: *tzricha* — both derivations are needed. One cannot derive the inclusion of underage girls from the law governing underage boys, nor vice versa. The differences in the structure of *tumah* between male *zivah* and female *zivah* are too significant to permit the inference to travel in either direction. Both the *pasuk* of *v'isha* by *zavah* and the derivation for males are independently necessary.

## Mishnah Tumah Ages

### Girl Tumah Ages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' טִינוֹקֶת בַּת יוֹם אֶחָד מְטַמְּאָה בִּנְדִיבָה, בַּת עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים מְטַמְּאָה בִּזְיָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A girl** aged **one day** is considered **capable of becoming impure** as **a menstruating woman, and a girl** aged **ten days** is considered **capable of becoming impure** as **a *zava*.**

A new *Mishnah* opens with a systematic statement of the ages at which *tumah* takes effect. *Tinoket bat yom echad metama beniddah* — a girl one day old can become *tamei* as a *niddah*. *Bat asara yamim metama bezivah* — a girl ten days old can become a *zavah*. The logic of ten days follows from the structure of the *niddah/zivah* cycle: the first day she can become a *niddah*, her seven-day *niddah* period encompasses days one through seven, and the eleven days of *zivah* begin on day eight. If she bleeds on days eight, nine, and ten, she is a *gedolah zavah* on day ten. Ten days old is thus the earliest she can qualify.

### Boy Tumah Ages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טִינוֹק בֶּן יוֹם אֶחָד מְטַמֵּא בִּזְיָבָה וּמְטַמֵּא בִּנְגָעִים וּמְטַמֵּא בְּטוּמְאַת מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **A boy aged one day** is considered **capable of becoming impure** as **a *zav*,** is considered **capable of becoming impure through leprosy-like** afflictions [*nega'im*], **and is** considered **capable of becoming impure through contact with a corpse.**

*Tinok ben yom echad metamei bezivah* — a one-day-old boy can already become a *zav*, and *umetamei benega'im* — the infant is also subject to the *tumah* of *nega'im*, the leprosy-like skin afflictions — and *umetamei betumat meit* — and is affected by the *tumah* of contact with a corpse. All of these *tumos* apply from the first day of life.

### Yibum/Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזָקוּק לְיִבּוּם וּפוֹטֵר מִן הַיִּבּוּם וּמַאֲכִיל בַּתְּרוּמָה וּפוֹסֵל מִן הַתְּרוּמָה וְנוֹחֵל וּמַנְחִיל וְהוֹרֵג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ. הֲרֵי הוּא כְּגָדוֹל שָׁלֵם לְכׇל דְּבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And is subject to the laws of levirate marriage, and releases from the laws of levirate marriage, enables his mother to eat *teruma*, disqualifies his mother from eating *teruma*, can inherit property, can transfer property through inheritance, and the killing of an infant is considered murder. He is regarded as an adult in all respects.**

The *Mishnah* then lists civil and personal status consequences of a one-day-old child. *V'zakuk leyibum* — the child is significant for the purpose of *yibbum*: a one-day-old son exempts his mother from *yibbum* should his father die, because the father did not die childless. And under certain circumstances, the child's death and his brother's death could make *yibbum* necessary for a surviving widow. *Ufoteir min hayibum* — and releases from *yibbum*. *Uma'achil biterumah* — if the father was a *kohen* and the mother was not a *kohenet*, the birth of a one-day-old child grants the mother the right to eat *terumah*, since she is now the mother of a *kohen*. *Ufoseil min haterumah* — conversely, if the mother was the daughter of a *kohen* but married a non-*kohen*, the birth of this child disqualifies her from eating *terumah* in her father's house.

*Venocheil umanchil* — a one-day-old child can inherit from his father and can pass on that inheritance. If the child was born and his father died moments later, the child inherits the estate; and if the child then dies, his maternal relatives inherit through him, rather than the estate reverting directly to the father's line. *Vehoreig et hanefesh* — if a one-day-old child kills someone, he is technically a murderer in the eyes of *halacha*, though obviously he cannot be executed. *Harei hu kegadol shaleim lechol devarav* — he is like a complete adult for all these matters. The *Mishnah* presents this as a sweeping and striking statement: on its very first day of life, a newborn child is already a full legal subject, bearing *tumah*, carrying halachic significance for *yibbum* and *terumah*, capable of inheriting, and recognized as a person whose death at another's hand constitutes murder.
