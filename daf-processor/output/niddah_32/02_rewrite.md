# Niddah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gender & Procreation

### Male & Peace

The *aggadata* of 31b concludes with a teaching of Rabbi Yitzchak in the school of Rabbi Yannai: *kivan sheba zachar l'olam ba shalom l'olam* — when a male comes into the world, peace comes into the world. The proof-text offered is *shilchu kar moshel aretz* ("send out the *kar*, master of the land"), where Rashi reads *zachar* as a contraction of *ze kar* — "this is the gift," the peace-offering, the thing of value that the male brings into the world.

### Male Production, Female…

A second teaching from the same school continues the wordplay: *ba zachar l'olam ba k'reih beyado* — when a male comes into the world, his loaf of bread is in his hands. The word *zachar* is again parsed as *ze kar*, this time in the sense of productive capacity, supported by the verse *vayikra lahem k'ra gedolah* ("he dug for them a great pit"), which is taken to evoke the ability to produce and be productive. By contrast, *nekeivah ein imah klum* — the female comes with nothing. The wordplay here is *nekiyah ba'ah*, she comes empty-handed. The proof-text is *nakvah s'charach alai v'etenah* ("articulate your fee and I will pay it"), Lavan's words to Yaakov, which is read to mean that she must ask — *ad d'amrah mezuni lo yahavu lah* — "until she says, give me my sustenance, they do not give her."

Rashi renders this as a matter of prayer — *sheyeda l'hitpaleil* — turning the formula into a petition to God. But the more natural reading is that it reflects the social realities of the time: women lacked the opportunity and education for independent means of earning a living. Indeed, the *gemara* throughout assumes that the husband bears the obligation of *mezonos* for his wife precisely because the working assumption is that she cannot earn independently, even though the *gemara* certainly knew of counterexamples.

### Essentialist vs. Societal

What makes this *aggadata* more than a sociological observation is that it is framed as an essentialist claim — not merely that women in this society lacked economic opportunity, but that the nature of the male is to produce and the nature of the female is to be without. This raises the question of whether the Rabbis are describing a societal reality or encoding it as a metaphysical truth. The framing in the *gemara* slides toward the latter, which is notable.

There is also, one might add, an implicit anatomical dimension to this framing: ancient and medieval thinkers — the Rabbis perhaps among them — sometimes conceived of the female as a male who lacks something, since the female anatomy is internal and concealed. The imagery of the male arriving with something in hand while the female arrives empty fits naturally into that broader perspective, however uncomfortable it may be from a modern vantage point.

### Shalom Debate

The *shalom* dimension of the opening teaching deserves its own examination. The straightforward reading might be that the birth of a boy brings domestic joy and harmony. But one might equally ask: men are historically the ones who go to war, so in what sense does the arrival of a male bring *shalom*? A more compelling reading is that *shalom* here means protection and security — the male as the protector of the household. The word *shalom* thus carries a sense not of peace in the abstract but of the physical safety and security that a male presence was understood to provide in that cultural context.

## Korban & Labor Oath

### Students' Question

The *gemara* turns to a famous piece of *aggadata* in which the students of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai pose the question: *mipnei mah amrah Torah yoledet meviah korban?* — why does a woman who gives birth have to bring a *korban*? This question rests on an implicit assumption that a *chatas* is about sin. As we have studied at length in *Kerisos* and *Shavuos*, a *chatas* is not primarily about sin; rather, it belongs to the category of *mechusrei kapparah*, those whose *tumah* is concluded through the bringing of a *korban* — a *zav*, a *metzora*, a *yoledet*, and similar cases. The *gemara* in *Shavuos* says this explicitly. Yet here the students treat the *chatas* as a response to transgression and want to know what sin was committed.

### Labor Oath Answer

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai answers: *besha'ah shekora'at laleid, kofetzet venishba'at shelo tizakek leva'alah* — at the moment of crouching to give birth, she swears that she will never again have relations with her husband, because of the pain of childbirth. *Lefikach amrah Torah tavi korban* — therefore the Torah said she must bring a *korban*, because she will inevitably go back on that oath when she wishes to resume her marital life. Rashi explains it as the transgression of that *shevuah* that requires atonement.

### Rav Yosef Objects

*Matkif lah Rav Yosef*: Rav Yosef challenges this sharply. *Veha mezidah hi* — but she is a *mezid*, someone who transgresses willingly. She knew she had taken the oath and she knowingly violated it. *Vechi chatas al mezid teiti?* — can a *chatas*, which is for inadvertent sin, apply to an intentional transgression? Furthermore, even if she felt genuine *charatah* and sought release from her oath, the mechanism for that is *hatarat nedarim*, not a *korban chatas*. And if there were a *korban* to bring for an oath, it would be a *korban shevuah*, a specific category of its own. The *gemara* offers no resolution, which effectively signals that the *gemara* was not fully persuaded by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's answer. The more fundamental answer — that the *korban* is not about sin at all but about concluding the state of *tumah* — remains, as stated elsewhere in the *gemara*, the more solid foundation.

## Tumah Periods

### One vs. Two Weeks

The *gemara* proceeds to another question in the same vein: *mipnei mah amrah Torah zachar leshiv'ah unekevah le'arba'ah asar?* — why does the Torah prescribe one week of *tumah* after the birth of a boy and two weeks after the birth of a girl? This is a pointed question, especially given that the physical process of childbirth does not differ so dramatically between the cases, at least not according to all views.

### Joy vs. Sadness

The answer given is rooted in the emotional context of the birth. By a *zachar*, *shehakol smeichim bo mitcharata leshiv'ah* — because everyone is joyous at the birth of a boy, the mother regrets her oath after one week; she is happy enough to return to her husband. By a *nekevah*, *shehakol atzeivim bah mitcharata le'arba'ah asar* — because everyone is saddened, disappointed that it was not a boy, the mother remains in her distress for two weeks before she is willing to go back.

This is a striking passage, reflecting with uncomfortable directness the cultural assumption that the birth of a daughter was received with less joy than the birth of a son. There is, to some degree, an empirical truth embedded here that persists even today: in communities where a son receives a *bris* on the eighth day, the birth of a boy carries a built-in communal celebration that the birth of a girl does not automatically receive. If someone in a *haredi* community announces "so-and-so had a baby," the assumption is often that it was a girl — because if it were a boy they would say so directly.

### Modern Brachot Rulings

This has *halachic* ramifications as well. Classically, the birth of a boy would occasion a *bracha* of *Shehecheyanu* or *HaTov VeHaMeitiv*, while the birth of a girl would not. Contemporary *poskim*, however — not merely the most modern ones but a meaningful range of later authorities — have ruled that since societal attitudes have genuinely shifted and people do celebrate the birth of daughters with real *simcha*, one may and should recite *Shehecheyanu* and *HaTov VeHaMeitiv* for a daughter as well. The *HaTov VeHaMeitiv* is recited over wine, not directly over the child. This evolution in *p'sak* is itself instructive: it acknowledges that the *gemara*'s distinction was rooted in societal norms rather than timeless metaphysics.

The *gemara* also notes that the *bris milah* is performed on the eighth day — which corresponds to the end of the mother's week of *tumah* after a boy — so that the husband and wife can be together at the time of the communal *simcha*. This connection no longer applies in the same way given later developments in *hilchos niddah*.

## Niddah Seven Days

### Why Seven Days?

A *Baraisa* is cited in the name of Rabbi Meir: *mipnei mah amrah Torah niddah leshiv'ah?* — why did the Torah prescribe seven days for a *niddah*? The question has force because most bodily *tumos* that are natural — relating to the living body rather than death or animal carcasses — last only a single day. A *ba'al keri*, for instance, is *tamei* for only one day. Why should *niddah* be seven days?

### Familiarity Remedy

The answer is: *mipnei sheragil bah vekatzah bah* — because a husband becomes too accustomed to his wife, and familiarity leads to a kind of boredom or dulling of desire. *Amrah Torah tehei temei'ah shivah yamim* — therefore the Torah said she should be *temei'ah* for seven days, *kedei shetehei chavivah al baalah kesha'at kenisatah lachuppah* — so that she should be as beloved to her husband as at the moment she entered the *chuppah*. The seven-day separation is a mechanism for renewal, for resetting the emotional and physical intimacy of the marriage to something closer to its original freshness.

### Modern Marriage Context

There is genuine psychological truth to this. Statistics on long-term marriages show a consistent pattern of declining frequency of intimate relations, driven precisely by the dynamic the *gemara* describes — everything becoming routine, familiar, undifferentiated. The *gemara*'s answer identifies this tendency and offers the *niddah* cycle as a structural corrective. It is worth noting that this should not be taught with false idealism, as though the *mikvah* night will always be experienced as a wedding night and expectations will always be met. Setting realistic expectations matters. But the core insight — that periodic separation followed by reunion can restore a sense of novelty and desire — is well-founded.

### Rambam Analogy

The Rambam in the *Guide to the Perplexed* offers a related observation about *Beis HaMikdash*: one of the purposes of *tumah* and *taharah* rules is to create periods during which one cannot enter the *Beis HaMikdash*, so that when access is restored, the encounter carries weight and appreciation. The analogy is instructive even if imperfect: the *Beis HaMikdash* is something one wants to experience rarely and at special moments of elevation, whereas marriage is something that should be regular and sustaining. The mechanism is similar — restricted access creates renewed appreciation — but the goal is different in each case.

## Gender Dynamics

### Man Pursues Woman

A *Baraisa* from the school of Rabbi Dostai bar Yannai asks: *mipnei mah ish mechazeir al ha-ishah ve-ein ishah mechazeret al ha-ish?* — why is it that the man pursues the woman in courtship and not the reverse? Both need to marry; why is the man generally the more active party?

The answer: *mashal le-adam she-avad lo aveidah* — it is like a person who has lost an object. *Mi mechazeir al mi? Ba'al ha-aveidah mechazeir al aveidato* — who looks for whom? The one who lost the object searches for what he lost; the lost object does not search for its owner. This echoes back to the narrative of *Bereishit*, where *Chavah* is formed from *Adam* — something that was part of him and was taken from him. When he encounters her he says *etzem mi-atzamai u-basar mi-besari* — he has found the part of himself that was missing. He is the one who lacks, and so he is the one who searches.

The metaphor is in one sense beautiful: the man is not pursuing something alien but recovering something that completes him, makes him whole. At the same time, the use of the word *aveidah* — a lost object — is undeniably possessive. The woman here is framed as an object that belongs to someone rather than as a subject who seeks completion herself. A *Rosh Yeshiva* once cited this *gemara* at the opening of a *zman* of *Masechet Kiddushin* and quipped to students who were slow to marry: if you are looking for an *aveidah*, you will find it; if you are looking for a *metziah*, it will take forever.

### Downward/Upward

The *gemara* continues: *mipnei mah ish panav le-mata ve-ishah paneha le-ma'ala?* — why does the man face downward and the woman face upward during marital relations, assuming this to be the standard position? *Zeh mi-makom she-nivra ve-zeh mi-makom she-nivra* — each faces toward the place from which they were created. The man looks down toward the earth, from which he was formed. The woman looks up toward the man, from whom she was formed. One might read this as the man expressing humility before his earthly origins, but the framing also reinforces a sense of the woman orienting herself toward the man as her source, which carries obvious symbolic weight in terms of hierarchy and dependence.

### Appeasement

*Umipnei mah ha'ish mekabel piyus ve'ein ha'ishah mekabeles piyus?* — why is a man easily appeased when angered, while a woman is not? Again, *zeh mi-makom she-nivra ve-zo mi-makom she-nivret*. The man, created from earth, is like earth — pliable, moldable, amenable to tilling and replanting. The woman, created from bone, is harder and less yielding. Rashi glosses this as *no'ach leratzos* — easy to pacify or to win over — and some read *piyus* here specifically in the marital context, noting that a man can more readily be persuaded toward intimacy than a woman, which also rings true.

### Voice Beauty

Finally: *umipnei mah ha'ishah kolah arev ve'ein ha'ish kolo arev?* — why does a woman have a beautiful, pleasing voice while a man does not? Again the same formula: *zeh mi-makom she-nivra ve-zo mi-makom she-nivret*. The man was formed from earth, which produces no melody. The woman was formed from bone, and Rashi notes that bone — when fashioned into an instrument — produces resonant, pleasing sound. The verse *ki kolech arev umarech naveh* ("for your voice is pleasing and your appearance is lovely") is cited as confirmation that women possess beautiful voices, though it serves as proof of the fact rather than explanation of the cause.

## Benot Kutim

### Status Overview

With the conclusion of the *aggadata* we turn to the opening of a new *Mishnah* — *benot Kutim niddot me'arisatan*. The daughters of the *Kutim* — the Samaritans — are considered *niddot* from their very cradles, from the moment of birth. *Kutim* were Samaritans, a population that maintained certain Jewish practices but not all, and whose halachic status oscillated between Jewish and non-Jewish. The *gemara* works within the assumption that they are Jewish, but that they do not observe the details of *hilchos niddah* in the same manner.

The *Mishnah* adds that the *Kutim* men themselves — *metamme'im mishkav tachton ke'elyon* — render the lower couch or seat *tamei* like the upper one, the logic being that since their wives are always considered *niddot*, the men are always considered *bo'alei niddot*, and this status renders whatever they sit or lie upon *tamei* in the manner of *mishkav u'moshav*. *Mipnei she'hen bo'alei niddot* — because they are treated as having had relations with *niddot*, they carry the corresponding *tumah*.

There is a significant *Tosafos* here. Elsewhere in the *gemara* (in the tractate of *Shabbat*), these *gezeiros* against *Kutim* are listed among the eighteen things enacted by *Beis Shammai*, many of which involve *tumah* and *taharah* and others of which concern limiting interaction with non-Jews. *Tosafos* reconciles the two sources by noting that even if the motivation behind some of these *gezeiros* was to erect social barriers, a *gezeirah* cannot be entirely a fiction — it must be rooted in some halachic reality. Hence our *gemara* focuses on the actual halachic ground for treating *benot Kutim* as *niddot*.

### Blood Color Indistinction

The reason given is: *ve'hen yoshvot al kol dam va'dam* — they sit upon every type of blood. That is, they do not distinguish between the different colors of blood, some of which render a woman *tamei* and some of which do not. Whatever blood they see, they treat as *niddah* blood. One might think this is a *chumra* — they're being stricter than the law requires. But in fact this is a textbook case of *chumra de'ati lidei kula* — a stringency that produces a leniency.

### Stringency/Leniency Para…

The mechanism works as follows. If a woman begins to bleed with a color that is not actually a *niddah* color — say, a brownish discharge two or three days before the true red flow — and she begins counting her seven days from that non-qualifying blood rather than from the onset of the actual qualifying *niddah* flow, her seven-day count ends too early. She will go to the *mikvah* while she is still technically a *niddah*, and thus never actually becomes *tehorah*. Her *chumra* of counting from the earliest drop, regardless of color, causes her to go to the *mikvah* at the wrong time, and her *tumah* is never resolved. This is the fundamental problem: the stringency of not distinguishing blood colors ends up producing the leniency of never actually achieving *taharah*.

### Safek Status

Despite all this, the *Mishnah* qualifies the consequence: *ve'ein chayavin aleihen bi'at mikdash* — if someone who became *tamei* through contact with a Samaritan woman entered the *Beis HaMikdash*, they are not liable for *karet*, because the *tumah* is a matter of *safek* — we do not know with certainty that this particular woman was indeed a *niddah* at the time. Similarly, *ve'ein sorfin aleihen et ha'terumah* — if a Samaritan woman touches *terumah*, it is not burned, because there remains a possibility that she was in fact *tehorah*. The ruling is: treat them as *tamei* for practical purposes, but since it is never a certainty, the severe consequences reserved for definite *tumah* do not apply.

## Infant Emissions

### Actual Bleeding?

The *gemara* opens with the classic *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If Samaritan infants actually bleed (*ei de'chazya*), then our infants would also be subject to the same concern — *afilu didan nami*. And if they do not bleed (*ei de'la kachazya*), then *didehu nami lo* — their infants are also not a concern, and the ruling seems to be baseless either way.

### Minority Possibility

Rava, son of Rav Acha bar Rav Huna, citing Rav Sheshet, resolves this: *hacha be'mai askinan be'stama* — we are dealing with a case of uncertainty, where we simply do not know whether a given infant has bled. And *de'keivan de'ika mi'uta de'chazya* — since there is a small minority of infants who do in fact experience some bleeding, we are *chaishinan* — concerned — that it may have occurred. The *gemara* then asks: whose view is this in the *Mishnah*, that we are concerned for a minority? The answer is Rabbi Meir.

### Rabbi Meir Minority

The *gemara* cites a *Braisa*. *Katan ve'ketanah lo choltzin ve'lo meyabemin* — a minor boy or girl cannot perform *chalitzah* or *yibbum*. Rabbi Meir forbids *yibbum* for minors not because there is a formal age requirement, but because *katan shema yimatze saris, ketanah shema timatze aylonit* — there is a minority possibility that the boy will prove to be a *saris* (infertile), or that the girl will prove to be an *aylonit* (infertile). If either is the case, the *mitzvah* of *yibbum* was never present, and the intercourse constituted a forbidden relationship with an *ervah* without the justifying *mitzvah* — *venimtze'u pog'in be'ervah shelo bimkom mitzvah*. The Rabbis respond: *zil batar rubba* — go by the majority. Most minor boys are not *sarisim* and most minor girls are not *ayloniyot*. The debate is thus whether we act on a minority possibility.

The *gemara* then introduces a limiting principle: Rabbi Meir is concerned for a minority when it is *miyuta deshchiach* — a minority that is not exceedingly rare. A minority that is *lo shchiach* — almost never encountered — would not, even by Rabbi Meir, generate a *gezeirah*. The question then is whether infant bleeding falls into the category of *shchiach*. The *gemara* maintains that it does, based on actual reported cases.

### Historical Stories

Three stories are adduced. Rabbi Yosi reports a case in *Ein Bul* where an infant girl was taken to the *mikvah* before her mother — the infant bled shortly after birth, began her seven-day *niddah* count, and reached the end of those seven days before her mother, whose post-partum *tumah* for a girl extended fourteen days. Rabbi (Rebbi) reports a similar case in *Beit She'arim*, and Rav Yosef reports a case in *Pumbedita*.

The *gemara* then notes a discrepancy. One can understand why Rabbi Yosi and Rebbi in *Eretz Yisrael* would bother taking the infant to the *mikvah*: there was *terumah* in *Eretz Yisrael* and one would not want the infant to touch it while *tamei*. But why would Rav Yosef in *Bavel* bother? Shmuel rules that the *terumah* of *chutz la'aretz* is only forbidden to someone whose *tumah* comes from their own body (*mi shetumah yotze'ah miguffo*) — a *ba'al keri*, a *zav*, a *zavah* — which would include a *niddah*. So the girl as a *niddah* is indeed covered by this prohibition. But the concern shifts: an infant cannot eat *terumah* in any case — she is months from eating solid food. And the *gemara* rules that the prohibition of *terumah chutz la'aretz* in this context applies to *achilah* — eating — but not to *negi'ah* — mere touching. So what was the concern?

Mar Zutra answers: *lo nitzrecha ella lesochah* — it was necessary because of *shemen shel terumah*, olive oil from *terumah*, which one wished to rub onto the infant as a lotion or ointment. The principle of *sichah k'shtiyah* — rubbing oil into the skin is halachically equivalent to drinking — renders this equivalent to ingesting the *terumah*, which a *niddah* may not do, even *terumah* of *chutz la'aretz* according to Shmuel's ruling. A *Baraisa* confirms: the verse *velo yichalelu et kodshei benei Yisrael et asher yarimu l'Adonai* teaches that *hasach* — anointing — is included in the prohibition alongside *achilah*, and the verse *vattavo chamayim b'kirbo u'chashemen b'atzmosav* ("it entered like water within him and like oil into his bones") similarly equates oil absorption with drinking. This is why even in *Bavel*, where they would not worry about the infant touching *terumah*, they took her to the *mikvah* — so she could have *terumah* oil applied to her skin.

## Our vs. Their Infants

### Drasha from Tanakh

The *gemara* returns to the central question: if infant bleeding is a real if rare phenomenon, why does the ruling apply specifically to *benot Kutim* and not to our own infant daughters? The answer is: *anan dedarshinan ishah v'ishah* — we derive from the Torah (as will be spelled out momentarily) that even underage girls can become *niddot*, all the way down to one day old.

### Our Knowledge vs. Theirs

Because we have this *drasha* and apply it, *d'chi chazi mafreshinan lehu* — when we see that an infant girl has bled, we immediately treat her as *tamei*, separate her from *taharos*, and handle the situation appropriately. We know the *halacha* and we act on it.

### Gezeirah Logic

The *Kutim*, by contrast, *d'lo darshi ishah v'ishah* — do not have this *drasha* and do not accept that underage girls can become *niddot*. Therefore *v'chi chazi lo mafreshi lehu* — even when they see that an infant has bled, they do not separate her or treat her as *tamei*. Because they are systematically unaware of and inattentive to this *halacha*, the Rabbis were compelled to issue a *gezeirah* treating all of their infants as *tamei*. *Gazru behu rabbanan* — the Rabbis decreed. This is the operative logic: it is not that their infants are more likely to bleed than ours; it is that when ours do bleed we respond correctly, and when theirs do, nothing is done about it.

*Tosafos* raises a further question: once you have already declared all *benot Kutim tamei* as infants, why is a separate reason needed for declaring adult *Kutim* women *tamei* — namely, their failure to distinguish blood colors? If the infant *gezeirah* already applies to them, does it not follow through to adulthood? *Tosafos* answers that the infant *gezeirah* was issued specifically because *Kutim* do not recognize that infants can become *niddot*. But once they reach adulthood, one might have thought we could trust them. A separate and independent reason is needed to explain why we cannot trust adult *Kutim* women either — and that reason is their failure to distinguish between qualifying and non-qualifying blood colors.

## Extra Vav Niddah

### Infant Girl Niddah

The *gemara* now spells out the *drasha* itself. *Mai ishah v'ishah?* — what is the derivation from the "extra" word? A *Baraisa* teaches: *isha, ein li ela isha* — from the word *isha* ("woman") I know only an adult woman can become *tamei* as a *niddah*. *Tinokes bat yom echad leniddah minayin?* — how do I derive that even an infant girl one day old can become a *niddah*? *Talmud lomar v'isha* — the extra *vav* (i.e., *ve'isha* rather than *isha*) teaches an additional case, including even a one-day-old infant.

### Vav Inclusion

The *gemara* then raises a challenge: if the extra *vav* includes someone as young as one day old when we go below the normal age, why does a different *baraisa* seem to stop at three years old? For the *tumah* of *shichvat zera* — the *tumah* acquired through an act of *bi'ah* — the *Baraisa* states *isha ein li ela isha, tinokes bas shalosh shanim v'yom echad l'bi'ah minayin, talmud lomar v'isha*. Here the extra *vav* brings us down to three years and a day, not to one day old.

Rava resolves this: *hilchata ninhu ve'asmi'chinhu rabbanan akra'ei* — both *shiurim* rest on tradition (*halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai*), and the Rabbis found scriptural support for each. The question is which *shiur* is the tradition and which is the *pasuk*. Rava concludes: the *shiur* of three years and one day for the definition of an act of *bi'ah* is the transmitted tradition, since no *pasuk* could independently yield such a specific number. The *pasuk* of *v'isha*, once it extends the category below the adult age, naturally extends all the way to the minimum — one day. Conversely, *bas yom echad* for *niddah* is rooted in the *pasuk*, while *bas shalosh shanim v'yom echad* is the tradition.

This leads to a further question: if the *pasuk* of *v'isha* by the *tumah* of *shichvat zera* is not needed for the *shiur* of three years (since that is *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai*), and if the one-day-old *niddah* can alternatively be inferred once we know that an infant can become a *zavah* (since there can be no *zivah* without *niddah* preceding it), what function does the *pasuk* of *v'isha* actually serve in each context? The *gemara* answers: *l'mautei ish mi'odem* — to exclude the male from the *tumah* of red blood. By emphasizing *v'isha* — the woman — the Torah underscores that *tumah* through blood or through *shichvat zera* applies to women in these forms and not to men. Specifically, two exclusions are operative: one by *niddah* and one by *shichvat zera*, excluding respectively (a) semen that appears red, and (b) actual blood that emerges from the male organ — neither renders the man *tamei* in the manner of a *niddah*.

### Zavah Question

The *gemara* then examines the parallel by *zavah*: *isha ein li ela isha, bas yud yamim v'zivah minayin?* — I know only an adult woman can become a *zavah*; how do I know that a girl as young as ten days old can become a *zavah*? *Talmud lomar v'isha* — the extra *vav* by *zavah*. The *gemara* asks why this separate derivation is needed; once we know an infant girl can become a *niddah*, why can't we infer that she can become a *zavah* as well?

### Female Exclusivity

The *gemara* answers that neither direction of inference works cleanly on its own. Had the Torah taught only *niddah* for infants, one might have said that *niddah* applies to infants because it is strict — a single day of bleeding generates a seven-day *tumah* — and therefore extends even to infants. But *zivah* is less strict — a single day of bleeding requires only a one-day watch — and one might have thought it would not apply to infants. Conversely, had the Torah taught only *zavah* for infants, one might have argued: *zavah* applies because even *zivah* be'ones — bleeding due to external trauma — creates *tumah* for a woman, making *zivah* strict in its own way, and therefore even infants are included. But one could not then infer *niddah*, since we cannot infer one category from the other in either direction. Both are necessary. The *pasuk*s of *v'isha* by *niddah* and *v'isha* by *zavah* each do independent work and cannot be reduced to each other.

---

## Ish Ish Zav

### Ish Ish Meaning

The *gemara* now turns to the parallel derivation for males. *V'chen b'zcharim* — the same logic of including infants applies to boys. The *Baraisa*: *ish ish* — the double expression of *ish* (*ish ish ki yihyeh zav*) — *mah talmud lomar ish ish? l'rabos tinok ben yom echad she'metamei b'ziva* — the double *ish* serves to include a one-day-old infant boy who is able to become a *zav*. This is in the name of Rabbi Yehudah. The ability of an infant to become a *zav* is already accessible from the first day of life because the requirements for male *zivah* do not include a preceding *niddah* period — three separate sightings of the discharge suffice, potentially even on the same day.

### Yishmael Objection

Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka disagrees: *ein tzarich* — no derivation is necessary. *Harei hu omer l'zachar u'l'nekeivah* — the Torah states explicitly "male or female" in the context of *zivah*. *L'zachar kol she'hu zachar bein gadol bein katan* — "male" means any male, adult or minor. *Ul'nekeivah kol she'hi nekeivah bein gedolah bein ketanah* — and "female" means any female, adult or minor. If so, why does the Torah say *ish ish* at all?

### Divine Language

Rabbi Yishmael's answer: *dibra Torah k'lishon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in the manner of human language. The doubling of *ish* is simply a stylistic feature of biblical Hebrew, not a hermeneutical signal. Therefore one cannot derive the inclusion of minors from *ish ish*, because that formulation carries no additional legal force — it is simply how people speak.

---

## Both Genders Needed

### Male Zav Strictness

The *gemara* asks why we need both the derivation for underage girls (*v'isha*) and the derivation for underage boys (*ish ish* or *l'zachar u'l'nekeivah*) — why not derive one gender from the other? The answer is that the *tumah* categories differ between male and female in ways that preclude cross-inference. For males, *zivah* is in some respects strict: *mishum demitmu bire'iyot kiveyamim* — a male becomes a *zav* through three sightings of the discharge, which can all occur on the same day, without any requirement that they fall on separate days. Because male *zivah* is such an expansive category in this sense, one might readily assume it extends even to infants.

### Female Zavah Strictness

For females, the *tumah* of *zivah* requires three days of bleeding on three separate days — a more specific and in that sense stricter requirement. One might have thought that because this category is more circumscribed, it would not extend to infants. On the other hand, female *zivah* is strict in a different dimension: *ba'ah be'ones* — bleeding caused by trauma or an external force still creates *tumah* for a woman, whereas the analogous situation for a man — an emission caused by illness or something he ate — does not make him a *zav*. Because each category has its own dimensions of stringency and leniency, no inference from one to the other is valid.

### No Cross-Gender Inference

The conclusion: *tzricha* — both derivations are needed. One cannot derive the inclusion of underage girls from the law governing underage boys, nor vice versa. The differences in the structure of *tumah* between male *zivah* and female *zivah* are too significant to permit the inference to travel in either direction. Both the *pasuk* of *v'isha* by *zavah* and the derivation for males are independently necessary.

## Mishnah Tumah Ages

### Girl Tumah Ages

A new *Mishnah* opens with a systematic statement of the ages at which *tumah* takes effect. *Tinoket bat yom echad metama beniddah* — a girl one day old can become *tamei* as a *niddah*. *Bat asara yamim metama bezivah* — a girl ten days old can become a *zavah*. The logic of ten days follows from the structure of the *niddah/zivah* cycle: the first day she can become a *niddah*, her seven-day *niddah* period encompasses days one through seven, and the eleven days of *zivah* begin on day eight. If she bleeds on days eight, nine, and ten, she is a *gedolah zavah* on day ten. Ten days old is thus the earliest she can qualify.

### Boy Tumah Ages

*Tinok ben yom echad metamei bezivah* — a one-day-old boy can already become a *zav*, and *umetamei benega'im* — the infant is also subject to the *tumah* of *nega'im*, the leprosy-like skin afflictions — and *umetamei betumat meit* — and is affected by the *tumah* of contact with a corpse. All of these *tumos* apply from the first day of life.

### Yibum/Terumah

The *Mishnah* then lists civil and personal status consequences of a one-day-old child. *V'zakuk leyibum* — the child is significant for the purpose of *yibbum*: a one-day-old son exempts his mother from *yibbum* should his father die, because the father did not die childless. And under certain circumstances, the child's death and his brother's death could make *yibbum* necessary for a surviving widow. *Ufoteir min hayibum* — and releases from *yibbum*. *Uma'achil biterumah* — if the father was a *kohen* and the mother was not a *kohenet*, the birth of a one-day-old child grants the mother the right to eat *terumah*, since she is now the mother of a *kohen*. *Ufoseil min haterumah* — conversely, if the mother was the daughter of a *kohen* but married a non-*kohen*, the birth of this child disqualifies her from eating *terumah* in her father's house.

### Legal Capacity

*Venocheil umanchil* — a one-day-old child can inherit from his father and can pass on that inheritance. If the child was born and his father died moments later, the child inherits the estate; and if the child then dies, his maternal relatives inherit through him, rather than the estate reverting directly to the father's line. *Vehoreig et hanefesh* — if a one-day-old child kills someone, he is technically a murderer in the eyes of *halacha*, though obviously he cannot be executed. *Harei hu kegadol shaleim lechol devarav* — he is like a complete adult for all these matters. The *Mishnah* presents this as a sweeping and striking statement: on its very first day of life, a newborn child is already a full legal subject, bearing *tumah*, carrying halachic significance for *yibbum* and *terumah*, capable of inheriting, and recognized as a person whose death at another's hand constitutes murder.
