# Sanhedrin 76 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Daughter-in-law debate

### Step-daughter-in-law

The *gemara* on 76a opens by continuing to work through the web of forbidden relationships involving a man's daughters, granddaughters, mother-in-law, grandmother-in-law, and their attendant punishments. We have established that an entire series of relatives — her mother, her daughter, her granddaughter, and so on — are forbidden and carry the penalty of *sreifa*. The *gemara* now raises a challenge: since his *kalato* — his daughter-in-law, his son's wife — is forbidden to him even after the marriage ends (even if the son divorces her), why is his step-daughter-in-law not similarly forbidden? If everything else has been rendered parallel through analogy, why not extend the same logic to forbid, say, his step-son's wife?

### Abaye: textual limit

Abaye answers by appealing directly to the text. The verse prohibiting the daughter-in-law states explicitly *eshet bincha hi* — she is your son's wife. The verse says *mishum eshet bincha ata mechayevo v'ee ata mechayevo mishum eshet bino*: you are liable on account of your own son's wife, not on account of your step-son's wife. The text itself forecloses the extension.

### Rava: analogy fails

Rava takes a different approach, characteristic of his tendency toward logical argumentation rather than textual derivation alone. He contends that the analogy fails on its own terms, regardless of which hermeneutical rule one follows. The *gemara* frames this around the well-known dispute between two methodological principles: *don minah u'minah* — draw the analogy and retain the original context — and *don minah v'okimah b'atrah* — draw the analogy but then apply it within the local context. Rava argues that neither approach can sustain the proposed analogy.

### Penalty weight mismatch

Consider first the view of *don minah u'minah*. The analogy would run: just as his *kalato* is forbidden to him, so too her *kalato* — his step-daughter-in-law — should be forbidden. And *u'minah*, retaining the original context, her punishment would be *skila*, stoning, since his daughter-in-law's violation carries *skila*. But this immediately generates an objection. If *skila* is considered the weightier form of execution, then one can challenge the entire analogy: his cluster of forbidden relatives is governed by the weightier *skila*, whereas hers are governed by *sreifa*. The fact that his side carries a heavier penalty means his prohibitions cannot simply be imported to her side. One might counter: well, for him too there is an asymmetry — his own daughter is *sreifa* while his daughter-in-law is *skila* — so inconsistency within one person's prohibited relatives is not itself disqualifying. The *gemara* accepts this counter: *hu atzmo yochiach dibito bisreifa v'chalato biskila*, he himself demonstrates the pattern whereby daughter is *sreifa* and daughter-in-law is *skila*.

### Inconsistency objection

But a second objection remains, and this one is decisive. For him, his mother and his daughter-in-law are both punished by *skila* — there is uniformity across that set of non-daughter-line relatives. If we now apply the analogy and give her step-daughter-in-law a punishment of *skila*, we create an inconsistency on her side: her mother is *sreifa*, yet her daughter-in-law would be *skila*. The principle is *af hi lo tachlok bah bein imo l'chalato*: just as the analogy requires us to preserve the relationship between mother and daughter-in-law as it exists in the original case — both *skila* — we cannot disrupt that symmetry on the other side. This asymmetry makes the analogy fail.

### Context application prob…

What about *don minah v'okimah b'atrah*? Here the argument runs: his daughter-in-law is forbidden, so her daughter-in-law should be forbidden, but now apply the local context — since her relatives are all *sreifa*, her step-daughter-in-law would also be *sreifa*. This would eliminate the inconsistency between mother and daughter-in-law on her side, since all become *sreifa*. But now the problem shifts: if *skila* is the weightier penalty, then the entire analogy is suspect from the start because his side is weighted more heavily than hers — *ika l'mifrach mah l'hu sheken imo biskila*, we can challenge the analogy by noting that his mother is *skila* whereas her mother is *sreifa*, demonstrating that his side is intrinsically weightier. Beyond that, a second difficulty emerges: the very act of applying local context and giving her step-daughter-in-law *sreifa* erases the distinction that existed in the original case between daughter and daughter-in-law. For him, the daughter is *sreifa* and the daughter-in-law is *skila* — there is a meaningful difference. The analogy, to be valid, would need to preserve that difference, requiring *tachlok bein bitah l'chalatah* — a distinction between her daughter and her daughter-in-law. But putting it all in the local context of *sreifa* collapses that distinction. Even if one holds that *sreifa* is weightier and thus sidesteps the first challenge, *me'hai kushya lo nidon* — this second problem remains and blocks the analogy entirely.

So whether by Abaye's textual argument or Rava's structural reasoning, the conclusion is the same: one cannot extend the prohibition to the step-daughter-in-law.

## Daughter from rape

### Abaye kal vachomer

With that settled, the *gemara* turns to an issue carried over from the previous *daf*: what is the source for the prohibition and punishment regarding *bito me'anusato* — his daughter from a woman he raped (or, as Rashi notes, from any illicit union, whether by rape or seduction or consensual relations outside of marriage, though the *gemara* frames the case as rape)? The Torah explicitly addresses his granddaughter but does not explicitly address his daughter in this context. Abaye proposes a *kal vachomer*: if the Torah imposes a punishment for his granddaughter, then certainly his own daughter should be subject to the same — *a bat bito onesh, a bito lo kol sheken*.

### No punishment via kal

The *gemara* immediately objects: *v'chi onshin min hadin*? One cannot derive a criminal penalty solely from a *kal vachomer*, no matter how compelling the logic. This is a well-established principle. So how can Abaye's argument stand?

### Giluy milta concept

The *gemara* resolves this by recharacterizing the argument: *giluy milta b'alma hu*. It is not a formal *kal vachomer* establishing a new penalty; it is rather an implicit revelation of something already embedded in the text. Rashi explains: the reason the granddaughter is forbidden is precisely because she descends through the daughter. The prohibition of the granddaughter already presupposes the prohibition of the daughter — the daughter's forbidden status is the premise upon which the granddaughter's status rests. The Torah's statement about the granddaughter therefore does not derive the daughter's prohibition through logical inference; it reveals it implicitly. The daughter's forbidden status is the latent foundation of the granddaughter ruling.

### Rava gezeirah shavah

Rava — here acting through Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi — takes a different route, characteristic of his preference for formal hermeneutical tools. He derives the daughter's status through a *gezeirah shavah* using the word *hennah-hennah*, the parallel appearance of the same term in two related verses. The *gezeirah shavah* connecting the prohibitions of *her* daughters to *his* daughters, which we employed earlier to extend the punishments, also serves here: just as her daughters are explicitly forbidden and carry *sreifa*, so too his daughters. The link established by the *hennah-hennah* brings his daughter within the same prohibition and penalty as her daughters.

### Bat ish kohen source

A *baraita* cited in the name of the father of Rabbi Avin offers yet another derivation. The *baraita* notes that since the prohibition of his daughter from an illicit union has no explicit verse, *hutzrach hakatuv lomar* — the Torah found it necessary to state — *uvat ish kohen ki teichel liznot*: the daughter of a *kohen* who commits adultery. The extra word *ish* — it could have simply said *uvat kohen* — is expounded as *ish ki teichel uvat ish ki teichel liznot*: the daughter of a man who commits forbidden sexual relations with her father. The conclusion is *et aviha hi mechalelet ba'eish tisaref*: she is burned. This verse thus provides both the prohibition and the penalty of *sreifa* explicitly.

## Bat kohen analogy

### Split punishment Q

The derivation from *uvat ish kohen* raises an immediate question. We have a principle regarding the *bat kohen* who commits adultery: *hi ba'eish tisaref* — she is burned — but *ein bo'ala bisreifa*, the man who had relations with her does not receive *sreifa*; he receives the penalty he would have received had she not been a *bat kohen*, namely *chenek* (strangulation). Why not apply the same split here: the daughter receives *sreifa*, but the father who violated her does not?

### Abaye: shame context

Abaye answers by drawing a principled distinction. The rule *hi ba'eish tisaref v'ein bo'ala bisreifa* applies specifically when it is she who brings shame and desecration upon her father — *mi shemechalelet et aviha*. That is the scenario the verse describes: a *bat kohen* who acts licentiousy thereby desecrates her father's honor. The penalty split reflects the asymmetry in culpability.

### No split if father

But in this case, the father is the one who violates and desecrates his daughter — *aviha mchalalah*. The dynamic is entirely reversed. The exempting logic of the *bat kohen* rule simply does not apply when the father is the perpetrator rather than the one who is shamed.

### Rava: status removal

Rava offers a structural argument. In the standard *bat kohen* case, one can coherently say: remove her *bat kohen* status for the purpose of determining his penalty, and then apply whatever death penalty would follow from a married woman who is not a *bat kohen* — he still receives a form of capital punishment, just a different one (*chenek*). The framework holds. But here, *hacha adina d'man mokmis lei*? If we were to remove the daughter relationship and not treat her as his daughter for the purpose of determining his penalty — what category would she fall into?

---

## Bat kohen analogy…

### Impossible outcome

She would have to be treated as a *pnuyah* — a single, unattached woman. And if that is the case, there is no prohibition at all for him, and he would receive no punishment whatsoever. That is an impossible outcome: she would be punished and he would go free entirely.

### Both get sreifa

Since no alternative legal category can be applied to him without generating an absurd result — the perpetrating father going completely unpunished — the relationship cannot be severed. Both must be treated under the same prohibition, and both receive *sreifa*. There is no room for a split.

## Prohibition source

### Implicit derivation

The *gemara* now asks a separate question. We have been discussing the *onesh* — the punishment — for a man who has relations with his daughter from an illicit union. But where does the explicit *azharah* — the prohibition — come from? For Abaye, who derives the punishment implicitly through *giluy milta* from the granddaughter, the same implicit textual basis also yields the prohibition: since the granddaughter's prohibition already presupposes the daughter's, the prohibition is embedded there. For Rava, who uses the *gezeirah shavah* of *hennah-hennah* to link his daughters to her daughters, the same link that imports the *sreifa* penalty also imports the prohibition — just as her daughters are forbidden, so are his.

### Al techalel source

But what of the *baraita* of the father of Rabbi Avin, who derives punishment from *uvat ish kohen*? That verse speaks only to the penalty of *sreifa* — it does not state an explicit prohibition. The *gemara* therefore brings the answer of Ulla: *amar kra al techalel et bitcha l'haznotah* — the verse states, "Do not desecrate your daughter to make her a harlot." This is the source of the prohibition: a man must not desecrate his daughter through an act of forbidden sexual intercourse.

### Ulla interpretation

Ulla understands the verse as prohibiting a father from engaging in an act of *haznotah* — forbidden sexual intercourse — with his own daughter. The word *l'haznotah* is the operative term: it is not merely about desecration in a general social sense, but specifically desecration through an act of the category of *znut*.

---

## Al techalel meanings

### Rabbi Yaakov challenge

Rabbi Yaakov, brother of Rabbi Acha bar Yaakov, raises an objection. He asks: *hai al techalel et bitcha l'haznotah amrat lah* — is this really the verse you are applying to the case of a father and daughter? That verse is needed for what is taught in a *baraita*: one might have thought *al techalel et bitcha l'haznotah* refers to a *kohen* who marries his daughter off to a *Levi* or an *Yisrael* — thereby making her *chol*, removing her from the sphere of holiness, preventing her from eating *terumah*. That would be one form of *chillul*.

### L'haznotah limits

The *baraita* itself resolves this: *talmud lomar l'haznotah* — the word *l'haznotah* limits the verse. It is not speaking of any generic desecration such as a status change in priestly class; it must involve *chillul shebiznut*, a form of desecration through illicit sexual conduct. The specific application articulated in the *baraita* is *b'moser bito shelo l'shem ishut*: one who gives over his daughter for sexual relations outside the framework of proper marriage. That is the plain reading of the verse. So Rabbi Yaakov's challenge is: the verse is already needed for this teaching — how can Ulla simultaneously apply it to prohibit a father from having intercourse with his daughter?

---

## Al techalel meanings…

### Double lamed analysis

The *gemara* resolves the tension by noting a textual nuance: if the verse intended only one prohibition, it would have said *al tachal* — a simpler form. Instead it uses the doubled form *al techalel*, with a doubled *lamed*. *Shma mina tarti*: the doubled language implies two distinct prohibitions. One is the prohibition addressed in the *baraita* — giving over a daughter for sexual relations outside of marriage. The other is Ulla's ruling — a father who has illicit sexual intercourse with his own daughter. The doubled form accommodates both, and neither reading is displaced by the other.

## Techalel cases

### Elderly husband

Rav Mani explains that *al techalel et bitcha l'haznotah* also applies to a case of *hamasi bito l'zaken* — one who marries his daughter off to an elderly man who cannot satisfy her sexually. The concern is not merely the absence of procreation but the active creation of conditions that will lead her to stray. A marriage that cannot fulfill her needs is one that invites *znut* — and a father who arranges such a marriage is implicated in that desecration.

### Delaying marriage

Rabbi Akiva identifies a different case: *hamasheh bito bogeret* — a father who delays marrying off his daughter after she has become a *bogeret*, sexually mature (generally understood as twelve and a half years of age and beyond). Ordinarily a daughter would be married off as a *ketanah* or *na'arah*, before this stage. A father who allows his daughter to remain in his home past maturity exposes her to sexual temptation, and if she succumbs, the father bears some responsibility for creating the conditions.

### Rasha arum

Abaye harmonizes the two cases and integrates the description of the *rasha arum* — the sly wicked person — by noting they are all facets of one phenomenon. The paradigmatic case is: *eizehu ani rasha arum — zeh hamishaheh bito bogeret*. He is an *ani*, poor, and so keeps her at home because he cannot afford a servant and needs her domestic labor. He is a *rasha* because he thereby exposes her to sexual temptation. And he is *arum* — cunning — because he presents his delay as beneficent, claiming perhaps that he is waiting for a better match or a higher dowry, when in reality he is serving his own interests. The Maharsha notes this self-serving posture as the defining feature of the *arum* dimension.

### Self-serving advice

Rav Kahana, in the name of Rabbi Akiva, appends a related maxim: *hevei zahir min hayo'atzcha lefi darko* — be wary of someone who gives you advice according to his own interests. The Maharsha connects this directly to the case at hand: the father who tells his daughter to wait for a better match is advising her according to his own benefit, not hers. The warning is a broader principle, but it is illustrated perfectly by the *rasha arum* of this context.

## Sated and thirsty

### Three case list

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav lists three categories of conduct about which the verse *lema'an sfot haravah et hatzmei'ah* — "to add the sated to the thirsty" — is applied, and about which it is said *lo yoveh Hashem slo'ach lo*, God will not forgive: one who marries his daughter to an elderly man, one who marries a young minor son to a mature woman, and one who returns a lost object to a non-Jew.

### Appetite mismatch

The phrase *lema'an sfot haravah et hatzmei'ah* describes the joining of one who is *raveh* — sated — with one who is *tzamei* — thirsty. In the marital cases, this describes a fundamental mismatch of sexual appetite or capacity. One party has no capacity or desire; the other does. The union does not satisfy; it only creates conditions for transgression.

### Rashi interpretation

Rashi reads the metaphor straightforwardly: the elderly man is *raveh* — sated — having lived a long life, no longer interested in or capable of sexual relations. The young woman married to him is *tzamei* — she has desire that cannot be met. The same logic applies in reverse: a young minor boy is *raveh* in the sense that he has not yet developed sexual desire or capacity, while a mature woman is *tzamei* and capable of desire. In each case, the father has matched an unmet thirst with someone who cannot address it.

### Maharsha variant

The Maharsha offers a slightly different reading of the metaphor, drawing on the Ramban's interpretation of the verse. *Lema'an sfot haravah et hatzmei'ah* need not mean joining two pre-existing conditions; it can mean transforming someone from *raveh* to *tzamei* — awakening an appetite that was dormant. In the case of marrying off a young woman or girl to an elderly man, she may have been *raveh* beforehand — not yet awakened sexually, or simply not tempted. The marriage, by placing her in a sexual context without satisfying her, actually creates and then leaves unmet a sexual appetite. The father has taken someone who was *raveh* and made her *tzamei*.

### Tosafot question

Tosafot raises an interesting question about the second case — marrying a minor son to a mature woman. Since when is a minor son's *kiddushin* recognized even rabbinically? If the marriage of a minor son carries no halachic recognition, what exactly is the father doing, and in what sense is it a marriage at all? Tosafot is uncertain on this point. The question remains open: if the arrangement is not halachically recognized even at the rabbinic level, the nature of the transgression — and the application of the *raveh v'tzamei* metaphor — becomes complicated. The practical scenario is that they are treated as a couple in some social sense, even if the formal halachic status is ambiguous.

## Proper marriage timing

### Baraita ideals

A *baraita* sets out a cluster of domestic ideals: one who loves his wife as himself — *ha'ohev et ishto k'gufo* — and who honors her even more than himself; one who guides his sons and daughters in the straight path, *b'derech yeshara*; and one who marries them off near to their age of maturity. The *baraita* includes the ideals of loving the neighbor, bringing relatives close, and so on, which we will return to shortly.

### Samuch l'firkan

The phrase *v'hamisiyan samuch l'firkan* — marrying them off close to their age of maturity — carries practical import. The Rabbis' approach to the challenge of prohibited sexual relations outside of marriage was, in large part, to ensure that children were married before they became sexually active. Marrying them off just before *bar* or *bat mitzvah* — *samuch l'firkan* — meant that when sexual maturity arrived, they were already within the framework of marriage.

### Peace in tent

Regarding such a father, the verse says *v'yadata ki shalom ohalecha* — "you will know that your tent is at peace" — *v'lo techeta*, and you will not sin. The tent, meaning wife and children, will all be ordered and secure. The children, having been given appropriate guidance and timely marriage, will not be exposed to temptation or caused to sin.

### Kiddushin vs nisuin

The *gemara* notes a potential tension: we said earlier not to marry off a minor son, yet here we praise marrying off children near the age of maturity — which might suggest doing so while still somewhat young. The *gemara* resolves this with *samuch l'firkan shani* — being close to maturity is a different matter entirely from marrying off a significantly young child. Additionally, there is a meaningful distinction between *kiddushin* and *nisuin*. *Kiddushin* — betrothal — was commonly performed earlier, even for younger children. It is *nisuin*, the actual marriage and cohabitation, that the *baraita* prescribes should take place *samuch l'firkan*, near the threshold of maturity, so that the couple enters into a full marital relationship just as they are about to become sexually active.

## Kindness deeds

### Five acts listed

Another *baraita* lists five acts of kindness or closeness that merit the divine response expressed in the verse *az tikra vaHashem ya'aneh* — "then you will call and the Lord will answer." These are: loving one's neighbor, bringing one's relatives close, marrying one's niece, lending to a poor person in his moment of distress, and honoring guests.

### Marrying niece

One who marries his sister's daughter — *hanosei et bat achoto* — exemplifies the value of *kiruv krovim*, bringing relatives close. This is likely why the case is cited in proximity to the themes of closeness and family: the earlier discussions of forbidden relatives are set alongside the positive ideal of strengthening familial bonds through permitted and indeed praiseworthy marriages.

### Emergency lending

*V'hamilveh sela l'ani b'shas dochako* — lending a *sela* to a poor person specifically when he is in distress and has nowhere else to turn — is a supreme act of *chesed*. The *gemara* emphasizes the specificity: not a routine loan but one made when the person is under pressure and at the limit of his resources. Such a person may have no one else to go to, and the lender steps in precisely at that moment.

### Then you will call

Rashi explains the connection to *az tikra vaHashem ya'aneh* through the preceding phrase in the verse: *mibesarcha lo titalem*, do not hide yourself from your own flesh. Acts of closeness — to neighbor, to relative, to the destitute — are precisely the refusal to hide oneself from those who are near. They are an expression of intimacy and presence. And it is that closeness — the willingness to be present, to bring near, to lend at the moment of need — that merits the intimacy of divine response: *az tikra vaHashem ya'aneh*.

## Burning relatives

### Them identification

The *gemara* now returns to the laws of *sreifa* in the case of a man who has relations with his wife's mother or grandmother. The verse states *ba'eish yisrefu oto ve'ethen* — he and *ethen* shall be burned. The plain sense is that "them" refers to multiple parties. But who are they? The context of the verse involves a man, his wife, and his wife's mother. The wife herself, however, cannot be included in the burning, since she has done nothing wrong — she is entirely permitted to him. The *chazal* therefore understand the wife as outside the scope of the penalty. What then does *ethen*, the plural, refer to?

### Yishmael one

Rabbi Yishmael holds: *oto ve'et achat mehen* — him and one of them. The plural *ethen* is interpreted as referring to only one forbidden relative. This appears to read against the grammatical plain sense.

### Greek etymology

The *gemara* explains Rabbi Yishmael's reading: *sheken bilshon Yavani korin le'achat Hina* — in Greek, the word for "one" is *hina*. The *ethen* of the verse thus echoes the Greek *hina* and should be understood as "one." This is a striking use of Greek lexicography in a Talmudic hermeneutical argument, illustrating that the *amora'im* were conversant with the broader linguistic world in which they lived.

### Yishmael learns grandmot…

If Rabbi Yishmael reads *ethen* as referring to only one relative — the mother-in-law — how does he account for the prohibition of the grandmother-in-law and her penalty? He derives it separately through a *gezeirah shavah* or another *drasha*, as we established earlier in the *sugya*.

### Akiva both

Rabbi Akiva disagrees and reads *ethen* straightforwardly as plural — both of them. *Oto ve'et shteihen*: him and both of them.

### Akiva explicit

But who are the two? Not the wife and the mother-in-law, since the wife is excluded. Rabbi Akiva, as we have seen, reads the verse *isha ve'et imah* — a woman and her mother — as encompassing both the mother-in-law and the grandmother-in-law. Both are present in the verse itself. Thus *ethen* refers to two forbidden relatives: the mother-in-law and the grandmother-in-law, both of whom receive *sreifa* along with him. This is consistent with Rabbi Akiva's earlier reading in which the grandmother-in-law is explicitly included in the verse's prohibition.

## After wife dies

### Real dispute

Rava challenges Abaye's characterization that the dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is merely *mashma'ut dorshin* — a disagreement only about how to read the verse, without a practical halachic difference. Rava insists there is a real and significant halachic dispute: *chamoto le'achar mita ika beinaihu* — the status of the mother-in-law after the wife has died.

### Yishmael continues

Rabbi Yishmael holds that the mother-in-law remains fully forbidden even after the wife dies, and that the punishment remains *sreifa*. His reading of *ethen* as "one of them" carries this implication: even when only one of the women is present — even if the wife has died and only the mother-in-law remains — the prohibition and its penalty continue in full force.

### Akiva limited

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, holds that the prohibition of the mother-in-law is contingent on the wife being alive — *oto ve'et shteihen*, him and both of them together. If the wife dies (or, presumably, is divorced), the mother-in-law is no longer subject to the biblical prohibition carrying *sreifa*. She remains forbidden, but only rabbinically — Rashi notes she falls within the category of the *arurim* and similar rabbinic prohibitions — and the violation would not be punishable by death. This is a substantial halachic difference: according to Rabbi Akiva, the death of the wife effectively removes the *areivut* of the marriage that grounded the biblical severity of the mother-in-law prohibition.

This concludes the *gemara*'s extended treatment of *sreifa*, working through the variations of mother-in-law, grandmother-in-law, and related forbidden relatives.
