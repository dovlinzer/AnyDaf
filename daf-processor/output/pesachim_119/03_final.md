# Pesachim 119 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hallel & Red Sea

### Hallel Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַקְדִּיחַ מִבַּחוּץ, וְאֶעֱשֶׂה נֵס בְּתוֹךְ נֵס. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רֵד! בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה פָּתַח גַּבְרִיאֵל וְאָמַר: ״וֶאֱמֶת ה׳ לְעוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **and I will burn** it **from the outside,** to consume those who threw the three righteous men into the furnace; **and I will** thereby **perform a miracle within a miracle. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to him: Descend. At that time Gabriel began** praising God **and recited: "And the truth of the Lord endures forever"** (Psalms 117:2), as God fulfilled His promise to him from more than a thousand years earlier.

The daf under discussion is 119b. We pick up several lines into the page where the Gemara is expounding various *pesukim* from the *hallel*. The verse in question is *ve'emes Hashem le'olam haleluyah* — "God's truth endures forever, Halleluyah." We had already established in the preceding discussion that this verse was said by Gavriel when he was granted the right to save Chananyah, Mishael, and Azaryah. Now the Gemara introduces a new voice: *dagim shebayam amruha* — the fish of the sea said it — and connects the verse to the story of *yetziat Mitzrayim*.

### Fish & Exodus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: ״וֶאֱמֶת ה׳ לְעוֹלָם״ — דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם אֲמָרוּהוּ, כִּדְרַב הוּנָא. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁבְּאוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר מִקְּטַנֵּי אֲמָנָה הָיוּ, וְכִדְדָרֵשׁ רַבָּה בַּר מָרִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וַיַּמְרוּ עַל יָם בְּיַם סוּף״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִמְרוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה וְאָמְרוּ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאָנוּ עוֹלִין מִצַּד אֶחָד, כָּךְ מִצְרִיִּים עוֹלִים מִצַּד אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan says: "And the truth of the Lord endures forever"** was actually **recited by the fish in the sea, in accordance with** a statement of **Rav Huna.** As **Rav Huna said: The Jewish people of that generation,** during the Exodus, **were of little faith. And** this statement **is as Rabba bar Mari taught: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "But they were rebellious at the sea, even at the Red Sea"** (Psalms 106:7)? This **teaches that the Jews rebelled** against Moses **at that time, and said:** Perhaps, **just as we** are **ascending from one side, so too the Egyptians** are **ascending from** the **other side,** and we will not be saved.

The Gemara cites a teaching of Rav Huna: *Yisrael she-be'oto hador mikatney amanah hayu* — the Jewish people of that generation were of weak and small faith. This is elaborated by Rabba bar Mari, who expounds the verse in Tehillim, *vayamru al yam beyam Suf* — they rebelled against God at the *Yam Suf*. The teaching is that even at the very moment the sea was splitting, the *Bnei Yisrael* expressed doubt. They said to Moshe: just as we are coming up on one side, perhaps the Egyptians have found another path through the sea and are coming up on the other side.

### Angel & Kishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְשַׂר שֶׁל יָם: פְּלוֹט אוֹתָן לַיַּבָּשָׁה! אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, כְּלוּם יֵשׁ עֶבֶד שֶׁנּוֹתֵן לוֹ רַבּוֹ מַתָּנָה וְחוֹזֵר וְנוֹטֵל מִמֶּנּוּ? אָמַר לוֹ: אֶתֵּן לָךְ אֶחָד וּמֶחֱצָה שֶׁבָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to the ministering angel of the sea: Spew out** the dead Egyptians **onto dry land.** The sea **said before Him: Master of the Universe, is there a servant whose master gives him a gift and then takes it from him?** Since the dead Egyptians were given to me for my fish to eat, how can God retract His gift? **He said to him: I will give you one and a half** times **their** number. Although I am taking them back now, later I will give you one and a half times as many people.

In response to this crisis of faith, God said to the *sar shel yam* — the angel appointed over the sea — *pelot otam leyabashah*: spit out the Egyptians onto dry land, so that Israel could see that they had truly drowned. The angel of the sea replied: *Ribbono Shel Olam, klum yesh eved shenoten lerabo matanah vechozer venotel heimenah?* Is there ever a slave to whom a master gives a gift and then demands it back? You gave me all these Egyptians as food for the fish of the sea — you want me to return them?

God responded: *efra lecha echad umchetza shebahen* — I will pay you back one and a half times this in the future. The angel countered: *Ribbono Shel Olam, yesh eved shetoveh et rabo?* How could a slave ever make a claim against his master? I cannot request payment from You. God then offered a solution: *Nachal Kishon yihiyeh li arev* — the *Nachal Kishon* will serve as my guarantor. You will not need to demand it from Me; you can demand it from the *Nachal Kishon*, who will be held responsible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, יֵשׁ עֶבֶד שֶׁתּוֹבֵעַ אֶת רַבּוֹ? אָמַר לוֹ: נַחַל קִישׁוֹן יְהֵא לִי עָרֵב. מִיָּד פָּלַט אוֹתָן לַיַּבָּשָׁה וּבָאוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְרָאוּ אוֹתָן. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְא יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת מִצְרַיִם מֵת עַל שְׂפַת הַיָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: Master of the Universe, can a servant issue a claim against his master** for a gift promised to him? Who will be my guarantor? **He said to him: The Kishon River will be a guarantor for Me. Immediately,** the sea **spewed them out onto the land, and the Jewish people came and saw** that **they** were dead. **As it is stated: "And Israel saw the Egyptians dead upon the seashore"** (Exodus 14:30).

Immediately, the angel of the sea complied and cast the Egyptians onto the dry land. *Uba'u Yisrael vera'u otam* — the *Bnei Yisrael* came and saw them, as it says: *vayar Yisrael et Mitzrayim met al sfat hayam* — Israel saw Egypt dead on the shore of the sea.

### Sisra Defeat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אֶחָד וּמֶחֱצָה שֶׁבָּהֶן? דְּאִילּוּ בְּפַרְעֹה כְּתִיב: ״שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת רֶכֶב בָּחוּר״, וְאִילּוּ בְּסִיסְרָא כְּתִיב: ״תְּשַׁע מֵאוֹת רֶכֶב בַּרְזֶל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this **one and a half** times **their** number? How was God's promise fulfilled? The Gemara answers: **While, with regard to Pharaoh, it is written: "Six hundred chosen chariots"** (Exodus 14:7), **whereas, with regard to Sisera, it is written: "Nine hundred iron chariots"** (Judges 4:13).

The Gemara then asks: *mai echad umchetza shebahen?* — what in fact was this promised payment of one and a half times? The answer involves a striking comparison. By Pharaoh it is written *shesh me'ot rechev bachur* — six hundred chosen chariots. By Sisra, it is written *tesha me'ot rechev barzel* — nine hundred iron chariots. Nine hundred is precisely one and a half times six hundred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא סִיסְרָא [אֲתָא עֲלַיְיהוּ בְּדִקְרֵי דְפַרְזְלָא, הוֹצִיא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עֲלֵיהֶם כּוֹכָבִים מִמְּסִילּוֹתָם, דִּכְתִיב:] ״מִן שָׁמַיִם נִלְחָמוּ הַכּוֹכָבִים״. כֵּיוָן דִּנְחִיתוּ כּוֹכְבֵי שָׁמַיִם עֲלַיְיהוּ, אַקְדִּירוּ הָנֵי דִּקְרֵי דְפַרְזְלָא. נְחִיתוּ לְאַקְרוֹרֵי לְמִיסְחֵי נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ בְּנַחַל קִישׁוֹן,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Sisera came** to fight Israel, **he came upon them with iron spears,** whereupon **the Holy One, Blessed be He, removed the stars from their orbits** to fight **against** Sisera's army, **as it is written: "They fought from heaven, the stars** in their courses fought against Sisera" (Judges 5:20). **Since the stars fell on them, these iron spears heated up** and **they went to cool them and wash themselves in the Kishon River.**

The fulfillment of the guarantee occurred as follows. When Sisra came to war against Israel, he attacked with his iron spears and weapons. God caused the stars to deviate from their orbits and come close to the earth to burn up Sisra's forces, as it says in *Shirat Devorah*: *min hashamayim nilchamu hakochavim* — from the heavens the stars fought against them. The heat made the iron weapons of Sisra's armies burning hot, so they ran to cool themselves in *Nachal Kishon*. At that moment, God said to *Nachal Kishon*: *lech vehashev ervonecha* — go and pay back your surety. *Miyad garafam Nachal Kishon vehishpicham layam* — immediately, *Nachal Kishon* swept them up and cast them into the sea, as it says: *Nachal Kishon garafam Nachal Kedumim* — the *Nachal Kishon* swept them away, the *nachal* of ancient times. *Mai Nachal Kedumim?* — *Nachal shenasa arev mi-kedem* — it had long ago been made the guarantor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְנַחַל קִישׁוֹן: לֵךְ וְהַשְׁלֵם עֵרְבוֹנְךָ. מִיָּד גְּרָפָם נַחַל קִישׁוֹן וְהִשְׁלִיכָן לַיָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נַחַל קִישׁוֹן גְּרָפָם נַחַל קְדוּמִים״, מַאי ״נַחַל קְדוּמִים״? נַחַל שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה עָרֵב מִקֶּדֶם. בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה פָּתְחוּ דָּגִים שֶׁל יָם וְאָמְרוּ: ״וֶאֱמֶת ה׳ לְעוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** At this point, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to the Kishon River: Go and pay your guarantee** that you issued to the ministering angel of the sea. **Immediately, the Kishon River swept them away and cast them into the sea, as it is stated: "The Kishon River swept them away, that ancient river"** (Judges 5:21). **What is** the meaning of the expression: **"ancient river"?** The Gemara explains: This is referring to **the river that was appointed a guarantor from ancient** times. **At that time, the fish of the sea began** praising God **and recited: "And the truth of the Lord endures forever,"** in reference to God's fulfillment of the promise He issued centuries earlier.

At that moment, *patchu dagim shel yam v'amru ve'emes Hashem le'olam* — the fish of the sea opened their mouths and declared: God's truth endures forever. God keeps His promises — not only to angels and to Israel, but even to the fish. The original promise was really made to the *sar shel yam*, but the fish received the fulfillment. The verse was thus said by the fish.

## Divine Response

### Akeres Habayis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״מוֹשִׁיבִי עֲקֶרֶת הַבַּיִת״ — אָמְרָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, שָׂמוּנִי בָּנֶיךָ כְּחוּלְדָּה זוֹ הַדָּרָה בְּעִיקָּרֵי בָתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites more *midrashim* on the verses of *hallel*. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Who makes the barren woman dwell in her house** as a joyful mother of children, *halleluya*" (Psalms 113:9)? **The Congregation of Israel,** i.e., the leaders of the Jewish people, **said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe,** due to their sins, **Your children treat me like this rat that dwells in the vaults of houses,** barely able to find a place in the house.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish turns to another verse from the *hallel*: *moshivi akeres habayis em habanim smeicha haleluyah* — He who causes the barren woman of the house to dwell as a joyful mother of children, Halleluyah. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish explains that *Kneses Yisrael* said to God: *Ribbono Shel Olam, smuni vanayich kachulda zu hadara be-ikrei habatim* — your children have made me like a weasel that dwells in the crevices of walls. On this reading, *akeres habayis* refers not to a barren woman but to the crevices of a house, and the *Rashbam* explains that *Kneses Yisrael* is saying: through their sins, they have caused me to become lowly, like a weasel hiding in the cracks. The second half of the verse — *em habanim smeicha* — reflects the reversal that comes with redemption. It may seem a curious choice of metaphor over the more obvious image of a barren woman; nonetheless, this is how the *Rashbam* reads the verse.

A further observation is worth making here. The *Rashbam* explains *smuni vanayich* — your children have made me thus — as referring to *hazekeinim vehachashuvim shebahem*, the elders and the distinguished ones among them, understanding *Kneses Yisrael* as the honorable leadership. In Kabbalistic thought, however, *Kneses Yisrael* refers to something metaphysical — almost an emanation of the divine that represents the Jewish people — rather than simply the physical nation. This accounts for the striking feature of the verse, where *Kneses Yisrael* appears as a distinct entity in conversation with God.

### Angels & Direct Action

A student raised a pointed question in connection with the previous aggadah about the *sar shel yam*: do we not say on *Shevi'i shel Pesach* — the seventh day of Pesach — *ani velo malach, ani velo acher*, that God acted alone at the *Keri'at Yam Suf* and not through any angel? The response here is nuanced. Even granting the principle of *ani velo malach*, it applies most directly to the *makkas bechoros* and the *yetziah* itself. The passage also says in that context *velo yiten hamashchis lavo el bateichem lingof* — and He will not allow the destroyer to enter your homes — which implies at least some engagement with the idea of an intermediary force, however God directed it. In the current story, the involvement of the *sar shel yam* concerns a secondary matter — having the Egyptians' bodies cast to the shore — not the splitting of the sea itself. Still, the question is well-taken, and the *Midrash* generally emphasizes *ani hu velo acher*, meaning there are indeed counter-voices within the *midrashim* and even within certain *pesukim*.

More broadly, the graphic and almost theatrical character of these *midrashim* — where angels bargain with God, where God makes promises and pays them back, where the *Nachal Kishon* becomes a legal guarantor — is difficult to assimilate into a comfortable theology. These stories have great power to seize the imagination, and in rabbinic literature this entire world of angelic personalities and divine negotiations is elaborated at length. In *Tanakh* itself, angels appear as part of a divine retinue that keeps its distance from the human drama — as in Yechezkel's visions or in the stories of the angels visiting Avraham. The only place in *Tanakh* where something like a personal discussion occurs in the divine presence is in Iyov, where the *Satan* comes before God to discuss Iyov's worthiness. In rabbinic literature, however, this dimension expands dramatically.

### Ahavti Ki Yishma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אָהַבְתִּי כִּי יִשְׁמַע ה׳״ — אָמְרָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם אֵימַת אֲנִי אֲהוּבָה לְפָנֶיךָ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁתִּשְׁמַע קוֹל תַּחֲנוּנַי. ״דַּלּוֹתִי וְלִי יְהוֹשִׁיעַ״ — אָמְרָה כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁדַּלָּה אֲנִי בְּמִצְוֹת, לְךָ אֲנִי וְלִי נָאֶה לְהוֹשִׁיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "I love that the Lord should hear** my voice and my supplications" (Psalms 116:1)? **The Congregation of Israel said: Master of the Universe, when am I beloved by You,** and I know that I am loved? **When You hear the voice of my supplications. "I was brought low, and He saved me"** (Psalms 116:6). **The Congregation of Israel said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, although I am lowly in mitzvot,** as I do not always fulfill them properly, **I am Yours,** and therefore **it is fitting** that **I** should **be saved.**

Rava offers an exposition of another *hallel* verse: *ahavti ki yishma Hashem et koli tachanunai* — I have loved that God has heard the voice of my supplication. Rava reads this as *Kneses Yisrael* speaking before God: *Ribbono Shel Olam, ematai ani ahuva lefanecha? Bishaa shetishma kol tachanunai* — when am I beloved to You? When You hear my supplication. Rather than reading *ahavti* simply as "I have loved," the *derasha* reads it in a passive or relational sense: I am beloved when You hear me. The moment of being heard is both the demonstration and the creation of the beloved relationship.

The following verse, *dalosi veli yehoshia* — I was poor and God saved me — is similarly read as *Kneses Yisrael* speaking to God: *Ribbono Shel Olam, af al pi shedala ani bemitzvot, lecha ani* — even though I may be poor in *mitzvot*, I belong to You. *Veli na'eh lehoshia* — it is fitting for You to save me. The appeal here is not based on merit but on relationship. The people say: not because of my *mitzvot*, not because I deserve it, but because of Your love and our unique bond — because I am true to You — save me.

## Rabbi Yishmael Teachings

### Nations Praise God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כְּשֶׁחָלָה רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, שָׁלַח לוֹ רַבִּי: אֱמוֹר לָנוּ שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים שֶׁאָמַרְתָּ לָנוּ מִשּׁוּם אָבִיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said: When Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, fell ill, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **sent** a message **to him: Tell us two or three matters that you said to us, citing your father.**

Rav Kahana relates that when Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yosi took ill, Rebbi sent to him a request: *emor lanu shenayim o shelosha devarim she'amarta lanu mishum avicha* — tell us two or three things that you have reported in your father's name, perhaps fearing these teachings would be lost. The *Rashbam* notes with interest that in multiple places in *Shas* we find this scenario of Rebbi asking Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yosi during illness to share his father's teachings, and that the answers given are not always the same. He suggests two possibilities: either Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yosi fell ill many times and each time Rebbi came to him received different teachings; or Rabbi Yishmael gave all of these teachings at one time, and the *Gemara* distributed them to different tractates, each in its appropriate thematic context.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁלַח לוֹ: כָּךְ אָמַר אַבָּא, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״הַלְלוּ אֶת ה׳ כׇּל גּוֹיִם״, אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? הָכִי קָאָמַר: ״הַלָּלוּ אֶת ה׳ כׇּל גּוֹיִם״ אַגְּבוּרוֹת וְנִפְלָאוֹת דְּעָבֵיד בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן אָנוּ דְּ״גָבַר עָלֵינוּ חַסְדּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **He sent** back **to him: My father said as follows: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Praise the Lord, all you nations,** laud Him all you peoples. For His mercy is great toward us, and the truth of the Lord endures forever, *halleluya*" (Psalms 117)? **What are the nations of the world doing** by praising God for His great mercies toward Israel? Rabbi Yosei explained: Rather, **this is** what the verse **is saying: "Praise the Lord, all you nations" for the mighty acts and the wonders that** God **performed before** their eyes. **All the more so we,** the recipients of these acts, should praise and thank Him, **as His mercy is great toward us.**

Rabbi Yishmael relays his father Rabbi Yosi's interpretation of the verse *halelu et Hashem kol goyim shabechuhu kol ha'ummim* — praise God, all nations, laud Him, all peoples. The question is sharp: *umot ha'olam mai avidtaihu* — why would the nations be the ones praising God? Particularly in light of the verse's ending: *ki gavar aleinu chasdo* — for His kindness has been mighty *upon us*. The interpretation offered is: the nations should praise God for the wonders and mighty acts He has done *for them*, all the wonders of creation and the natural world. And if the nations who receive general providence should praise God, *kol sheken anu* — how much more so we, for whom God's special relationship and *chesed* has been particularly mighty.

### Future Gifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד: עֲתִידָה מִצְרַיִם שֶׁתָּבִיא דּוֹרוֹן לַמָּשִׁיחַ. כְּסָבוּר, אֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל מֵהֶם. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַמָּשִׁיחַ: קַבֵּל מֵהֶם! אַכְסַנְיָא עָשׂוּ לְבָנַיי בְּמִצְרַיִם. מִיָּד: ״יֶאֱתָיוּ חַשְׁמַנִּים מִנִּי מִצְרָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** Rabbi Yishmael sent another of his father's teachings to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **In the future, Egypt will bring a gift to the Messiah** when all of the nations gather before him, as the verse says: "Out of Your temple at Jerusalem, where kings shall bring presents to you" (Psalms 68:30). The Messiah **will think** it appropriate **not to accept** gifts **from them,** as the Egyptians abused Israel. **The Holy One, Blessed be He,** will **say to the Messiah: Accept** their gifts **from them,** as **they granted lodging to My children in Egypt.** They lived there for many years before being enslaved. **Immediately, "Nobles shall come out of Egypt"** (Psalms 68:32).

Rabbi Yishmael then shares his father's teaching about the future: *atida Mitzrayim shetavi doron leMashiach* — in the future, at the end of days, Egypt will bring gifts to Mashiach. Mashiach will initially reason that he should not accept from them. But God will say: *kabel mehem* — accept it from them. *Achsanya asu levanai beMitzrayim* — they were a host to My children in Egypt. Even with all the enslavement, they did house them in the land. Immediately, *vaye'etayu chashmanim mini Mitzrayim* — the nobles of Egypt came forward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשְׂאָה כּוּשׁ קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמָהּ: וּמָה הַלָּלוּ שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּעְבְּדוּ בָּהֶן כָּךְ, אֲנִי שֶׁלֹּא נִשְׁתַּעְבַּדְתִּי בָּהֶן לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: קַבֵּל מֵהֶם. מִיָּד ״כּוּשׁ תָּרִיץ יָדָיו לֵאלֹהִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Upon seeing that Egypt's gift has been accepted, Kush will want to bring a gift too. **Kush** will **draw an *a fortiori*** inference **with regard to itself: Just as** the gifts of **these** Egyptians were accepted despite the fact that **they enslaved** the Jewish people, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that a gift from **me, who did not enslave them,** should be accepted? **The Holy One, Blessed be He,** will **say to** the Messiah: **Accept** it **from them. Immediately, "Kush shall hasten to stretch out her hands to God"** (Psalms 68:32).

Upon seeing Egypt's gifts accepted, Kush made a *kal vachomer* to itself: if Egypt, who enslaved Israel, will have their gifts accepted, how much more so I, who never enslaved them, will surely have my gifts accepted as well. God says: accept it. *Miyad Kush taritz yadav l'Elohim* — immediately Kush stretched its hands toward God.

Then Rome made its own *kal vachomer*: if these nations whose gifts are accepted, *anu she'anu acheihem* — we who are their brethren — the *Gemara* associating Rome with Eisav — how much more so should our gifts be accepted? Here, however, the response changes entirely.

### Rome Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָשְׂאָה מַלְכוּת (רוֹמִי) הָרְשָׁעָה קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמָהּ: וּמָה הַלָּלוּ שֶׁאֵין אֲחֵיהֶן כָּךְ, אָנוּ שֶׁאָנוּ אֲחֵיהֶן — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְגַבְרִיאֵל: ״גְּעַר חַיַּת קָנֶה״ — גְּעוֹר חַיָּה וּקְנֵה לְךָ עֵדָה.
>
> **Translation:** After that, **the wicked kingdom of Rome** will **draw an *a fortiori*** inference **with regard to itself: Just as** the gifts of **these, who are not their brothers,** were accepted in this manner, **we,** the descendants of Edom, **who are their brothers, all the more so** is it **not** clear that our gifts will be accepted? **The Holy One, Blessed be He,** will **say to Gabriel: "Rebuke the wild beast of the reeds [*kaneh*],** the multitude of [*adat*] the bulls" (Psalms 68:31). **Rebuke the beast and acquire [*keneh*]** the **congregation [*eda*]** of Israel. The nation of Rome, which enslaved Israel in its current exile, is less worthy than the other two nations.

God says to Gavriel — the same Gavriel who appeared earlier in the daf — *ga'ar chayat kaneh* — rebuke, roar at the beast of the reed. Gavriel is to reject Rome's gift and *keneh lecha edah* — acquire for yourself the congregation of Israel. Rome is too evil to deserve acceptance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״גְּעוֹר חַיַּת קָנֶה״ שֶׁדָּרָה בֵּין הַקָּנִים, דִּכְתִיב: ״יְכַרְסְמֶנָּה חֲזִיר מִיָּעַר וְזִיז שָׂדַי יִרְעֶנָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** the verse **"Rebuke the wild beast of the reeds,"** means rebuke the beast **that dwells between the reeds, as it is written: "The pig out of the wood ravages it, and that which moves in the field feeds on it"** (Psalms 80:14). This verse is referring to Edom, whose behavior is compared elsewhere to that of a pig.

An alternative reading of *ga'ar chayat kaneh* — *she-dara bein hakanim* — identifies this beast as the one that dwells among the reeds, evoking the verse *yechasmenah chazir miya'ar* — the wild boar of the forest will ravage her. This is the familiar association of Rome and Edom with the pig. The pig, of course, is the one non-kosher animal that possesses split hooves — it displays an outward sign of *kashrut* while being inwardly *treif*. Archaeologically, this association runs deep: in excavations of settlements from the First Temple period, one of the ways scholars identify Jewish versus non-Jewish villages is the complete absence of pig bones in certain sites, even where other signs of religious compromise are found.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גְּעוֹר בְּחַיָּה שֶׁכׇּל מַעֲשֶׂיהָ נִכְתָּבִין בְּקוּלְמוֹס אֶחָד. ״עֲדַת אַבִּירִים בְּעֶגְלֵי עַמִּים״ — שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ אַבִּירִים כַּעֲגָלִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the verse means: **Rebuke the beast, all of whose actions are written with a single quill.** *Kaneh* can mean a reed, or a quill. In other words, rebuke a nation that speaks of others in only one manner, for the worse. The verse continues: "The multitude of the bulls, with the calves of the peoples, everyone submitting himself with pieces of silver. He has scattered the peoples who delight in war" (Psalms 68:31). **"The multitude of the bulls [*abirim*], with the calves of the peoples";** this is referring to Rome, **who slaughtered mighty people [*abirim*] like ownerless calves.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ga'ar be-chaya she-kol ma'aseha nichtavin be-kulmos echad* — rebuke the beast all of whose deeds are written with a single reed, meaning Rome always acts destructively against Israel and never otherwise. Every entry in its ledger is one of harm.

This is a striking contrast within the *aggadah*. Egypt, despite all the enslavement and the drowning of children in the Nile, is ultimately given credit for hosting Israel and its gifts are accepted in messianic times. Kush, which never persecuted Israel at all, is certainly included. But Rome is rejected absolutely. One might ask whether this reflects the sheer severity of Roman persecution — the Herodian persecutions, the destructions, the martyrdoms — or whether it reflects the lack of historical distance, since the *Amoraim* were still living under Roman authority. The Egyptians could be viewed with more equanimity precisely because their domination was centuries past. Whatever the reason, it is particularly striking in the context of this tractate, where we have seen throughout that aspects of the *seder* itself are consciously modeled on the banqueting customs of wealthy Romans — the reclining, the wine — even as this deep resentment and unwillingness to extend any forgiveness to Rome simultaneously runs through the discussion.

## Wealth & Exile

### Harugei Malchus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עֲדַת אַבִּירִים בְּעֶגְלֵי עַמִּים״ — שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ אַבִּירִים כַּעֲגָלִים שֶׁאֵין לָהֶם בְּעָלִים. ״מִתְרַפֵּס בְּרַצֵּי כָסֶף״ — שֶׁפּוֹשְׁטִין יָד לְקַבֵּל מָמוֹן, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹן בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **"The multitude of the bulls [*abirim*], with the calves of the peoples";** this is referring to Rome, **who slaughtered mighty people [*abirim*] like ownerless calves.** **"Everyone submitting himself with pieces of silver,"** means **that** the officials of Rome **open their hands to receive money** as bribes or taxes, **but** actually they **do not perform the will of their masters,** as they cannot be trusted even in this regard.

The Gemara continues its exposition of *pesukim* from the same *perek* of Tehillim. The verse *adat abbirim be-eglei amim* — the congregation of mighty ones among the calves of the nations — is read as a reference to the *harugei malchus*, the great martyrs killed by the ruling power. *She-shachtu abbirim ke-agalim she-ein lahem ba'alim* — they slaughtered the mighty ones like calves that had no owners, like ownerless animals, like they were nothing.

*Mitrapes be-ratzei chesev* — the *Rashbam* understands this as a reference to the practice of extending one's hand to receive money, suggesting that the oppressors would take bribes with promises of waiving their edicts, and then *ve'ein osim ratzon ba'alim* — they would not follow through and would proceed with their persecutions anyway.

### Pizar Amim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״פִּיזַּר עַמִּים קְרָבוֹת יֶחְפָּצוּן״ — מִי גָּרַם לָהֶם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיִּתְפַּזְּרוּ לְבֵין אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם? קְרִיבוֹת שֶׁהָיוּ חֲפֵצִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **"He has scattered the peoples who delight in war"; what caused the Jewish people to be scattered among the nations of the world? The intimacies that they desired with them.** Their exile among the nations was caused by their attraction to the customs and behavior of gentiles.

The verse *pizar amim keravot yechpatzu* — He scattered the peoples who desire battles — prompts the question: *mi garam lahem l'Yisrael she-yispazru l'vein umot ha'olam* — who caused Israel to be scattered throughout the nations of the world? *Keravot she-hayu chafetzim bahem* — their desire for *keravot*. One reading understands *keravot* as closeness: their desire to assimilate, to be close to the nations and to be like them, ultimately led to the loss of their distinct identity and to exile. A second reading, offered by the *Maharsha*, takes *karva* to mean war: Israel was too eager for military confrontation. In the time of the First Temple they refused to submit to Babylonian authority, and in the time of the Second Temple they refused to submit to Rome, and their insistence on going to war brought about their dispersion. The *Maharsha* acknowledges this is not the typical formulation of *Chazal*, which usually focuses on specific sins as causes of exile. But it is a notably provocative reading — and a historically grounded one — suggesting that had Israel been more willing to accept the authority of the ruling powers, they might have maintained their presence in the Land.

### Rome's Wealth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד שָׁלַח לוֹ: שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה שְׁוָוקִים בִּכְרַךְ גָּדוֹל שֶׁל רוֹמִי, וְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד (הָיוּ) [יֵשׁ] בּוֹ שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה בִּירָנִיּוֹת, וְכׇל בִּירָנִית וּבִּירָנִית (הָיוּ בּוֹ) [יֵשׁ בָּהּ] שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת וְשִׁשִּׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה מַעֲלוֹת, וְכׇל מַעֲלָה וּמַעֲלָה (הָיוּ בּוֹ) [יֵשׁ בָּהּ] כְּדֵי לָזוּן אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rabbi Yishmael **further sent to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: There were **365 markets in the great city of Rome, and each and every one** of them **contained 365 towers, and each and every tower contained 365 floors, and each and every floor contained enough** food **to sustain the entire world.**

Continuing with his father Rabbi Yosi's teachings, Rabbi Yishmael reports: *tlas mei'ah v'shishim v'chamisha shvakim be-krach gadol shel Romi* — there were 365 marketplaces in the great metropolis of Rome. Each marketplace contained 365 castles, each castle had 365 stories, and each story contained enough grain to sustain the entire world. Rome was effectively hoarding all the world's sustenance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי (יִשְׁמָעֵאל) [שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי] לְרַבִּי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ (לְ)רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בַּר יוֹסֵי [לְרַבִּי]: הָנֵי לְמַן? לָךְ וּלְחַבְרָךְ וּלְחַבְרוּתָךְ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״סַחְרָהּ וְאֶתְנַנָּהּ קוֹדֶשׁ לַה׳ לֹא יֵאָצֵר וְלֹא יֵחָסֵן כִּי לַיּוֹשְׁבִים לִפְנֵי ה׳ יִהְיֶה וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael** also **said to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and some say** that he said **it to Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei: For whom** did they store all **this** food? He replied: **For you and your colleagues and the colleagues of your colleagues,** i.e., for the Jewish people and their Sages, **as it is stated: "Her grain and her hire shall be consecrated to the Lord; it shall not be treasured nor laid up; for her gain shall be for those who dwell before the Lord,** to eat their fill, and for stately clothing" (Isaiah 23:18).

Rabbi Shimon Berabi said to Rebbi — and some versions attribute it to Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yosi — *halalu l'man* — all this wealth, for whom is it ultimately stored? The answer: *lecha ul'chavercha ul'chavrosayich* — for you and your friends, meaning the people who occupy themselves with *Torah*. As it says: *sachrah ve-esnanah kodesh l'Hashem lo ye'atzer ve-lo yeichasen ki l'yoshvim lifnei Hashem yihyeh* — its hire and its wealth will be sanctified to God, it will not be stored up nor kept in treasure, for it will belong to those who dwell before God. All the grain and gold and silver that Rome accumulated will ultimately go to *Torah* scholars.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא יֵאָצֵר״? תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: ״לֹא יֵאָצֵר״ — זֶה בֵּית אוֹצָר, ״וְלֹא יֵחָסֵן״ — זֶה בֵּית גְּנִיזָה. מַאי ״כִּי לַיּוֹשְׁבִים לִפְנֵי ה׳״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **"It shall not be treasured"? Rav Yosef teaches: "It shall not be treasured," this** is referring to items brought into **a storehouse; "nor laid up," this** means **a treasury,** where valuable items were stored behind guarded walls. **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **"Who dwell before the Lord?" Rabbi Elazar said:**

Rav Yosef clarifies: *lo ye'atzer* refers to *beit otzar*, a storehouse of grain; *lo yeichasen* refers to *beit genizah*, a treasury of gold and silver. Both will be transferred.

## Yoshvim Lifnei

### Knowing Seats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַמַּכִּיר מְקוֹם חֲבֵירוֹ בַּיְּשִׁיבָה. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: זֶה הַמְקַבֵּל פְּנֵי חֲבֵירוֹ בַּיְּשִׁיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **This is one who recognizes his colleague's place in the yeshiva,** as he is there often enough to know where everyone sits. **Some say** that **Rabbi Elazar said** a different explanation: **This is** one **who greets his colleague in the yeshiva,** as he is always there to meet him.

The Gemara asks: *mai ki la-yoshvim lifnei Hashem* — what exactly does it mean that the wealth will go to those who sit before God? Rabbi Elazar answers: *zeh hamakir mekom chaveiro be-yeshiva* — this refers to one who is so regular a presence in the *beit midrash* that he knows where every other person sits. He knows this person's place and that person's place. Beyond the simple fact of regularity, the formulation *makir mekom chaveiro* carries a deeper resonance: you know everyone else's *makom*, you respect it, you do not take it, you do not displace anyone.

An alternate version: *zeh hamikabel pnei chaveiro be-yeshiva* — one who greets and welcomes every person who enters the *beit midrash*, because you are always there, always the fixture of the place. Both formulations — knowing where everyone sits and welcoming those who arrive — emphasize not only personal diligence in learning but a sensitivity to the community and to the other people present. It is a notable balance: being *yoshvim lifnei Hashem* is described not in purely solitary terms but in terms of awareness of and relationship to others who share the *beit midrash*.

### Sitrei vs Taamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לִמְכַסֶּה עָתִיק״ — זֶה הַמְכַסֶּה דְּבָרִים שֶׁכִּיסָּה עַתִּיק יוֹמִין, וּמַאי נִינְהוּ? סִתְרֵי תוֹרָה. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: זֶה הַמְגַלֶּה דְּבָרִים שֶׁכִּיסָּה עַתִּיק יוֹמִין, מַאי נִינְהוּ? טַעֲמֵי תוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the continuation of this verse: **"For stately clothing [*limekhaseh atik*]" This is** one **who conceals [*mekhaseh*]** matters **that the Ancient of Days [*atik yomin*],** i.e., God, **concealed. And what are these?** These are **the secrets of the Torah,** the esoteric Act of Creation and the Act of the Divine Chariot, which should remain hidden. **And some say: This** verse is referring to one **who reveals matters that the Ancient of Days concealed. And what are these?** These are the **reasons** for different mitzvot in the **Torah,** which should be kept secret.

The verse continues: *ul-michaseh atik* — and for the one who covers the Ancient of Days. *Zeh hamichaseh devarim shekissah atik yomin* — this refers to one who covers up the things that God has covered up. *Umai nihu?* — and what are these things? *Sitrei Torah* — the hidden mysteries of the Torah, the esoteric secrets of *Ma'aseh Merkavah* and the like. One fulfills this by refraining from revealing what ought not to be revealed.

An alternate version: *zeh hamigaleh devarim shekissah atik yomin* — one who reveals things that have been concealed. *Umai nihu? Ta'amei Torah* — not *sitrei Torah* but *ta'amei Torah*, the reasons and meanings of the Torah. The distinction between the two is important. *Sitrei Torah* are the deep esoteric knowledge — the powerful mysteries that are not to be widely shared. *Ta'amei Torah* are the efforts to understand the meaning of *halachot*, of narratives, of *mitzvot* — to ask what they signify, what they teach, what lies beneath the surface. This is not hidden knowledge being inappropriately uncovered; it is precisely the kind of exploration and reflection that *mefarshim*, *midrashim*, and *parsha shiurim* are all engaged in.

### Maharsha Reasons

The *Maharsha* raises a pointed question: the Gemara says elsewhere that the Torah deliberately did not reveal *ta'amei ha-mitzvot* — the reasons for the commandments — precisely because once you know the reason, you might conclude it does not apply to you. The classic example is *lo yarbeh lo nashim pen yasur levavo* — the king shall not take many wives lest his heart turn away. Shlomo reasoned that he could take many wives without his heart turning, and he stumbled. So if the Torah withholds its reasons precisely to prevent such reasoning, how can the verse here praise one who *reveals* the reasons?

The *Maharsha* offers two responses. First, perhaps *ta'amei Torah* is distinct from *ta'amei mitzvot* specifically — they are not the same category. But the second answer is particularly compelling: it is only dangerous when the *Torah* reveals the reason, because then the person treats the reason as the entire substance of the *mitzvah* and discounts the commandment when they believe the reason does not apply. But when *you* work to discover the reason, it comes with the recognition that your conclusion is ultimately speculation — you have not supplanted the divine word, you have only approximated it. You understand that at the end of the day your reasoning remains tentative. This seems a very beautiful distinction: the pursuit of reasons is not only permitted but encouraged, so long as it is accompanied by the humility to recognize that the *Torah*'s authority does not depend on your having understood the reason correctly.

## Rav Kahana

### Lamnatzeach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״לַמְנַצֵּחַ מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד״ — זַמְּרוּ לְמִי שֶׁנּוֹצְחִין אוֹתוֹ וְשָׂמֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another statement attributed to Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei. **Rav Kahana said, citing Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "*Lamenatzeaḥ* a psalm of David"** (e.g., Psalms 13:1)? It means: **Sing to the One who rejoices when conquered [*shenotzḥin oto*].**

Rav Kahana in the name of Rabbi Yishmael beRabbi Yosi expounds the verse *lamnatzeach mizmor l'David zamru* — to the *menatzeach* a psalm of David, sing. Rather than reading *lamnatzeach* in its musical sense — some sort of musical instruction — the *derasha* reads it from the root *nitzachon*, victory: *l'mi shenotzchin oto ve-sameach* — to the One whom others are victorious over, and who is happy about it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֹּא וּרְאֵה שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִדַּת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם. בָּשָׂר וָדָם — מְנַצְּחִין אוֹתוֹ וְעָצֵב, אֲבָל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא — נוֹצְחִין אוֹתוֹ וְשָׂמֵחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לְהַשְׁמִידָם לוּלֵי מֹשֶׁה בְחִירוֹ עָמַד בַּפֶּרֶץ לְפָנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Come and see how the characteristics of the Holy One, Blessed be He, are unlike the characteristics of flesh and blood: When a flesh and blood** person **is conquered, he is sad; however, when the Holy One, Blessed be He, is conquered, He rejoices, as it is stated: "Therefore He said that He would destroy them, had not Moses His chosen stood before Him in the breach,** to turn back His wrath lest He should destroy them" (Psalms 106:23). In this verse Moses is called "His chosen," although he defeated God, as it were, by preventing Him from destroying the Jewish people.

*Bo u-re'eh shelo ke-middat haKadosh Baruch Hu middat basar v'dam* — come and see that God's way is not the way of flesh and blood. When a person of flesh and blood is bested by someone, he is distraught. But *haKadosh Baruch Hu notzchin oto ve-sameach* — when God is, so to speak, bested, He is glad. As it says: *vayomer le-hashmidam lulei Moshe bechiro amad baperetz lefanav* — God wanted to destroy them, were it not that Moshe His chosen one stood in the breach before Him. Moshe fought against God's decree and was victorious, and the Torah records it approvingly — *Moshe bechiro*, His chosen one.

This connects the present passage to the earlier *aggadah* in a meaningful way. We saw the angels bargaining with God, the *sar shel yam* arguing that God owes him payment. Gavriel negotiating. Now the Gemara teaches that this is not merely a feature of the angelic realm — it is built into our relationship with God as well. *Lamnatzeach* — God wants to be challenged, God wants to be argued with, God rejoices when we stand in the breach. It translates the somewhat unfamiliar image of angels arguing in the heavenly court into a human reality: this is the kind of relationship we are meant to have.

### God's Hand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וִידֵי אָדָם מִתַּחַת כַּנְפֵיהֶם״, ״יָדוֹ״ כְּתִיב — זֶה יָדוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁפְּרוּסָה תַּחַת כַּנְפֵי הַחַיּוֹת כְּדֵי לְקַבֵּל בַּעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה מִיַּד מִדַּת הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **Rav Kahana said, citing Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei,** who said that **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said, citing Rabbi Yehuda Nesia: What is** the meaning of that **which is written** in the description of the sacred *ḥayyot*, the angels that carried the Divine chariot: **"And they had the hands of a man under their wings"** (Ezekiel 1:8)? Although the word is read hands in the plural, actually **"his hand" is written** in the singular. **This is the hand of the Holy One, Blessed be He, that is spread under the wings of the *ḥayyot* to accept penitents from** the claims of **the attribute of justice.** God accepts sincere penitents, despite the fact that in accordance with the strict attribute of justice they should not be given the opportunity to repent.

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish in the name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah expounds: *ve-yedei adam mitachat kanfeihem* — and the hands of a man are under their wings, from the opening vision of Yechezkel. The word *yedei* — hands, plural — is actually written as *yado*, with a *vav*, suggesting a singular reading. Whose hand? *Yado shel haKadosh Baruch Hu* — this is God's hand, *she-perusha tachat kanfei ha-chayot* — stretched out beneath the wings of the celestial beings that surround and, in a sense, shield the divine presence from direct access. And the purpose of this outstretched hand: *k'dei l'kabel ba'alei teshuvah* — to receive those who repent, *mipnei midat ha-din* — in spite of, or against, the attribute of strict justice.

This image is striking. The angelic wings serve as a barrier — they keep the human world at a distance from the divine. Yet God stretches His hand *beneath* those wings, reaching through the barrier specifically to draw in *ba'alei teshuvah*. The theme is consistent with what has come just before: the special intimacy that God maintains with Israel, the relationship that breaks through barriers. First we saw that God wants us to argue with Him. Now we see that even the structures of the heavenly court do not prevent God from reaching out to receive those who return to Him.

## Wealth History

### Yosef's Wealth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם יוֹסֵף לִקְּטוֹ וֶהֱבִיאוֹ לְמִצְרַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְלַקֵּט יוֹסֵף אֶת כׇּל הַכֶּסֶף הַנִּמְצָא״. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם וְשֶׁבְּאֶרֶץ כְּנַעַן, בִּשְׁאָר אֲרָצוֹת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל הָאָרֶץ בָּאוּ מִצְרַיְמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Joseph collected all the silver and gold in the world and brought it to Egypt, as it is stated: "And Joseph collected all the money found** in the land of Egypt and in the land of Canaan" (Genesis 47:14). **I have** derived **only** that he collected the money **that** was **in the land of Egypt and that** was **in the Land of Canaan. From where** do I derive that he also collected all the money **that** was **in other lands? The verse states "And all the land came to Egypt** to buy food from Joseph, because the famine was sore in all the earth" (Genesis 41:57).

Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *kol kessef ve-zahav sheba-olam Yosef likto ve-hevi'o le-Mitzrayim* — all the gold and silver in the world, Yosef gathered it and brought it to Egypt. As it says: *vayelaket Yosef et kol ha-kessef ha-nimtza be-eretz Mitzrayim u-ve-eretz Kena'an*. One might think this refers only to Egypt and Canaan, but the verse also says *ve-chol ha-aretz ba'u Mitzraymah* — all the lands came to Egypt — indicating that all the world's wealth was concentrated there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁעָלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרַיִם הֶעֱלוּהוּ עִמָּהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְנַצְּלוּ אֶת מִצְרָיִם״, רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: עֲשָׂאוּהָ כִּמְצוּדָה זוֹ שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דָּגָן, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמַר: כִּמְצוּלָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דָּגִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And when the Jewish people ascended from Egypt they took** this treasure **with them, as it is stated: "They despoiled [*vayenatzlu*] Egypt"** (Exodus 12:36). The Sages explain this term. **Rav Asi said: They made** Egypt **like this trap [*metzuda*]** for birds, where grain is usually placed as bait, **in which there is no grain. Rabbi Shimon said:** They made Egypt **like the depths [*kimetzula*]** of the sea **in which there are no fish.**

When Israel left Egypt, they took it all with them: *vayenatzlu et Mitzrayim* — they stripped Egypt. *Rav Assi* reads *vayenatzlu* as related to *metzudah* — a bird trap completely emptied of its grain; Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish reads it as *metzula she-ein bah dagim* — a pond emptied of all its fish. In either case, Israel departed with the entire world's accumulated wealth.

### Empire Succession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָיָה מוּנָּח עַד רְחַבְעָם. בָּא שִׁישַׁק מֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם וּנְטָלוֹ מֵרְחַבְעָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי בַּשָּׁנָה הַחֲמִישִׁית לַמֶּלֶךְ רְחַבְעָם עָלָה שִׁישַׁק מֶלֶךְ מִצְרַיִם [עַל יְרוּשָׁלִַים] וַיִּקַּח אֶת אוֹצְרוֹת בֵּית ה׳ וְאֶת אוֹצְרוֹת בֵּית הַמֶּלֶךְ״. בָּא זֶרַח מֶלֶךְ כּוּשׁ וּנְטָלוֹ מִשִּׁישַׁק.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this treasure **remained** in Eretz Yisrael **until** the time of **Rehoboam,** at which point **Shishak, king of Egypt, came and took it from Rehoboam, as it is stated: "And it came to pass in the fifth year of king Rehoboam, that Shishak king of Egypt came up against Jerusalem. And he took the treasures of the house of the Lord, and the treasures of the king's house;** and he took away all" (I Kings 14:25–26). **Zerah, king of Kush,** who ruled over Egypt, later **came and took it from Shishak.**

That wealth remained in Israel's possession until the reign of Rechavam, son of Shlomo, at which point Shishak king of Egypt came and took it from Rechavam — as recorded explicitly in *Tanakh*: *vayikach et otzrot beit Hashem ve-et otzrot beit ha-melech*. The Egyptians had reclaimed what Israel had taken from them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא אָסָא וּנְטָלוֹ מִזֶּרַח מֶלֶךְ כּוּשׁ וְשִׁיגְּרוֹ לְהַדְרִימּוֹן בֶּן טַבְרִימּוֹן. בָּאוּ בְּנֵי עַמּוֹן וּנְטָלוּם מֵהַדְרִימּוֹן בֶּן טַבְרִימּוֹן. בָּא יְהוֹשָׁפָט וּנְטָלוֹ מִבְּנֵי עַמּוֹן וְהָיָה מוּנָּח עַד אָחָז.
>
> **Translation:** **Asa came and took it from Zerah, king of Kush,** when he defeated him in battle (II Chronicles 14) **and sent it to Hadrimmon ben Tabrimmon,** king of Aram (see I Kings 15). **The children of Ammon came and took it from Hadrimmon ben Tabrimmon,** as learned by tradition. **Jehosaphat came and took it from the children of Ammon** (see II Chronicles 20), **and it remained** in Eretz Yisrael **until** the reign of **Ahaz.**

Then came Zerach king of Kush and took it from Shishak — this is not in *pesukim* explicitly, Rashi notes it is tradition. Then Asa waged war against Zerach king of Kush and recovered it — this is recorded in *pesukim* — and sent it to Hadardimon ben Tavrimon, king of Aram, in exchange for protection against Baasha king of Israel. The Ammonites then took it from Hadardimon ben Tavrimon. Yehoshaphat came and took it from *Bnei Amon* — we have *pesukim* about Yehoshaphat going to war against *Bnei Amon*. It remained in the hands of the Judean kings until Achaz.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא סַנְחֵרִיב וּנְטָלוֹ מֵאָחָז. בָּא חִזְקִיָּה וּנְטָלוֹ מִסַּנְחֵרִיב וְהָיָה מוּנָּח עַד צִדְקִיָּה. בָּאוּ כַּשְׂדִּיִּים וּנְטָלוּהוּ מִצִּדְקִיָּה. בָּאוּ פָּרְסִיִּים וּנְטָלוּהוּ מִכַּשְׂדִּיִּים. בָּאוּ יְוָונִים וּנְטָלוּהוּ מִפָּרְסִיִּים. בָּאוּ רוֹמִיִּים וּנְטָלוּהוּ מִיַּד יְוָונִים, וַעֲדַיִין מוּנָּח בְּרוֹמִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Sennacherib came and took it from Ahaz. Hezekiah came and took it from Sennacherib, and it remained** in Jerusalem **until** the reign of **Zedekiah. The Chaldeans came and took it from Zedekiah. The Persians came and took it from the Chaldeans. The Greeks came and took it from the Persians. The Romans came and took it from the Greeks, and** this treasure of silver and gold **still remains in Rome.**

Sancheriv came and took it from Achaz. Chizkiyahu then recovered it after the angel wiped out Sancheriv's armies — presumably Israel looted the devastated camp and recovered the wealth. It then remained with the Judean kings until Tzidkiyahu. The Kasdim came and took it from Tzidkiyahu. The Persians took it from the Kasdim. The Greeks — Alexander and the Hellenistic empires — took it from the Persians. Rome took it from the Greeks. *Ve-adayin munach be-Romi* — and it still sits in Rome.

This historical survey provides a satisfying closure to the teaching we encountered earlier: all of Rome's accumulated wealth will ultimately go to those who sit in *yeshiva*. Since the chain of this wealth traces back through empire after empire to the original taking from Egypt — itself a taking that was prophesied — there is a sense in which the wealth was always destined to return to Israel in the end.

### Three Treasures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָמָא (בַּר) חֲנִינָא: שָׁלֹשׁ מַטְמוֹנִיּוֹת הִטְמִין יוֹסֵף בְּמִצְרַיִם, אַחַת נִתְגַּלָּה לְקֹרַח, וְאַחַת נִתְגַּלָּה לְאַנְטוֹנִינוּס בֶּן אַסְוִירוּס, וְאַחַת גְּנוּזָה לְצַדִּיקִים לֶעָתִיד לָבֹא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this matter, **Rabbi Ḥama bar Ḥanina said: Joseph hid three treasures in Egypt. One** of them **was revealed to Korah, one was revealed to Antoninos ben Asveiros,** king of Rome, **and one is hidden for the righteous in the future.**

Rav Chama beRabbi Chanina adds: *shalosh matmoniyot hitmin Yosef be-Mitzrayim* — three treasures Yosef buried in Egypt. One was revealed to Korach — that is the source of Korach's legendary wealth. One was revealed to Antoninus ben Asveirus — a figure identified tentatively with someone in the line of Roman emperors who bore the name Antoninus, possibly associated with Marcus Aurelius Antoninus Augustus or Antoninus Pius. And one is still hidden and reserved *l'tzadikim l'asid lavo* — for the righteous in the time to come. It is notable that the distribution is not "everything goes to Israel" but rather one third each: one portion was revealed in antiquity, one in the Roman period, and one awaits the future.

## Korach Wealth

### Wealth Ruin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עוֹשֶׁר שָׁמוּר לִבְעָלָיו לְרָעָתוֹ״, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: זוֹ עׇשְׁרוֹ שֶׁל קֹרַח. (שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר:) ״וְאֶת כׇּל הַיְקוּם אֲשֶׁר בְּרַגְלֵיהֶם״, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: זֶה מָמוֹנוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם שֶׁמַּעֲמִידוֹ עַל רַגְלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Korah's wealth, the Gemara cites the verse: **"Riches kept by his owner to his hurt"** (Ecclesiastes 5:12). **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: This is the wealth of Korah,** which caused him to grow arrogant and lead to his destruction. **As it is stated:** "And what He did to Dathan and Abiram, the sons of Eliab, son of Reuben; how the earth opened her mouth and swallowed them up, and their households, and their tents, **and all the sustenance that was at their feet"** (Deuteronomy 11:6). **Rabbi Elazar said: This** is referring to **a person's money that stands him upon his** own two **feet.**

The Gemara cites the verse *osher shamur le-va'alav le-ra'ato* — wealth held in store for its owner, to his ruin. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: *zeh oshro shel Korach* — this is the wealth of Korach. His wealth made him haughty, led him to believe he could challenge Moshe, and ultimately brought about his destruction. And the verse *ve-et kol ha-yakum asher be-ragleihem* — all the substance that was at their feet — prompts Rabbi Elazar to observe: *zeh mamono shel adam she-ma'amido al raglov* — this is a person's money that makes him stand on his feet, that gives him his sense of dignity and standing. It was that very dignity and pride, conferred by his enormous wealth, that drove Korach's rebellion and that was ultimately swallowed up along with him.

### 300 Mules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: מַשּׂאוֹי שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת פְּרָדוֹת לְבָנוֹת הָיוּ מַפְתְּחוֹת בֵּית גְּנָזָיו שֶׁל קֹרַח, וְכוּלְּהוּ אַקְלִידֵי וּקְלִיפֵּי דְגִלְדָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi said: The keys to Korah's treasuries were** a **load of three hundred** strong **white mules, and they were all keys [*aklidei*] and locks** made **of leather.**

Rabbi Levi teaches: *masa shalosh me'ot pardot levanot hayu mafteichot beit genaziv shel Korach* — the load of three hundred white mules was the keys to Korach's treasure houses. And these were not the actual treasures — just the keys. Moreover, *vechulu aklidi ve-klifi de-galda ninhu* — they were all keys and locks made of leather, which are light. Even so, three hundred white mules were needed to carry them, which gives some sense of the staggering scale of the actual treasure. We had just seen that Rome possessed 365 marketplaces each with 365 castles each with 365 stories of provisions — and yet even Korach's wealth as described here strains the imagination.

## Hallel Enactment

### David Stone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (דיא״ש אדי״ש כשד״ך מאוד״ך סִימָן). אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: ״אוֹדְךָ כִּי עֲנִיתָנִי״ — אָמַר דָּוִד. ״אֶבֶן מָאֲסוּ הַבּוֹנִים הָיְתָה לְרֹאשׁ פִּנָּה״ — אָמַר יִשַׁי. ״מֵאֵת ה׳ הָיְתָה זֹּאת״ — אָמְרוּ אֶחָיו. ״זֶה הַיּוֹם עָשָׂה ה׳״ — אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** ***Dalet, yod, alef, shin, alef, dalet, yod, shin, khaf, shin, dalet, khaf, mem, alef, vav, dalet, khaf*** is **a mnemonic** device for the following passage. Returning to the issue of *hallel*, the Gemara states that these psalms include choruses in which each section is sung by a different person. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said** that **David recited: "I will give thanks to You, for You have answered me"** (Psalms 118:21), with regard to the success of his reign. **Yishai recited: "The stone which the builders rejected has become the chief keystone"** (Psalms 118:22). **The brothers of** David **recited: "This is the Lord's doing;** it is marvelous in our eyes" (Psalms 118:23). **Samuel** the Prophet **recited: "This is the day which the Lord has made;** we will rejoice and be glad in it" (Psalms 118:24).

The Gemara returns to *pesukim* in the *hallel*. The verse *odecha Hashem ki anisani* — I will thank You because You have answered me — was said by David, presumably in some moment of divine rescue from his many troubles. The following verse, *even ma'asu ha-bonim hayesa le-rosh pinah* — the stone rejected by the builders became the cornerstone — was said by Yishai, David's father. The context is the challenge to David's very legitimacy: he was the youngest and least considered of Yishai's sons, his lineage through Rus HaMoaviya was challenged by *talmidei chachamim*, and his right to kingship was questioned. Playing on the word *bonim* — reading it as *bonayim*, builders, i.e., *talmidei chachamim* — the verse says: those who build and who rejected him became the cornerstone. The *Maharsha* elaborates the full *midrash* about David's lineage being challenged and ultimately vindicated.

### Verse Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָנָּא ה׳ הוֹשִׁיעָה נָּא״ — אָמְרוּ אֶחָיו. ״אָנָּא ה׳ הַצְלִיחָה נָּא״ — אָמַר דָּוִד. ״בָּרוּךְ הַבָּא בְּשֵׁם ה׳״ — אָמַר יִשַׁי. ״בֵּרַכְנוּכֶם מִבֵּית ה׳״ — אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. ״אֵל ה׳ וַיָּאֶר לָנוּ״ — אָמְרוּ כּוּלָּן. ״אִסְרוּ חַג בַּעֲבוֹתִים״ — אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל. ״אֵלִי אַתָּה וְאוֹדֶךָּ״ — אָמַר דָּוִד. ״אֱלֹהַי אֲרוֹמְמֶךָּ״ — אָמְרוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **The brothers of** David **recited: "We beseech You, Lord, save now"** (Psalms 118:25). **David recited: "We beseech You, Lord, make us prosper now"** (Psalms 118:25). **Yishai recited: "Blessed be he who comes in the name of the Lord"** (Psalms 118:26). **Samuel recited: "We bless you out of the house of the Lord"** (Psalms 118:26). **They all recited: "The Lord is God, and has given us light"** (Psalms 118:27). **Samuel recited: "Order the Festival procession with boughs,** even to the horns of the altar" (Psalms 118:27). **David recited: "You are my God, and I will give thanks to You"** (Psalms 118:28). **They all recited: "You are my God, I will exalt You"** (Psalms 118:28).

The Gemara proceeds to assign various *hallel* verses to specific speakers at specific historical moments:

*Me'eis Hashem haysa zos* — this was from God, this thing — was said by Yishai, reflecting the astonishment that the rejected youngest son would become king. *Hi niflas be-eineinu* — it is wondrous in our eyes — is in the plural and so was said by *echav*, his brothers. *Zeh hayom asa Hashem* — this is the day God has made — was said by Shmuel, presumably at the moment of David's anointing. *Anna Hashem hoshia na* — was said by *echav*, David's brothers. *Anna Hashem hatzlicha na* — was said by David himself. *Baruch haba be-shem Hashem* — was said by Yishai. *Berachnuchem mi-beis Hashem* — was said by Shmuel. *Keil Hashem va-ya'er lanu* — was said by all of them together. *Isru chag ba-avotim* — was said by Shmuel. *Eli ata ve-odeka* — was said by David. *Elokai aromemeka* — was said by all of them together.

### Hallel Drama

The Gemara does not provide the full context for each of these attributions, but what it is clearly doing is bringing a generic sequence of praise verses to life by grounding each line in a specific voice and dramatic moment — the anointing of David, his brothers' reaction, Shmuel's proclamation, the communal response. This raises the intriguing possibility that the *hallel* was not merely recited but in some sense enacted when it was said, with different people taking different parts. We saw something similar earlier in the daf with the verse *lo lanu lo lanu*, where the same kind of dramatic assignment was implied. One can imagine that at certain points the saying of *hallel* had a quasi-theatrical character, with assigned roles bringing the text alive as something more than rote recitation.

## Doubling Verses

### Custom Doubling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לִכְפּוֹל — יִכְפּוֹל, לִפְשׁוֹט — יִפְשׁוֹט, לְבָרֵךְ אַחֲרָיו — יְבָרֵךְ, הַכֹּל כְּמִנְהַג הַמְּדִינָה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְאַחֲרָיו, אֲבָל לְפָנָיו — [מִצְוָה] לְבָרֵךְ. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַמִּצְוֹת מְבָרֵךְ עֲלֵיהֶן עוֹבֵר לַעֲשִׂיָּיתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there,** in *Sukka*: In **a place where they were accustomed to double** certain verses in *hallel*, **one doubles them** and reads them twice. In a place where the custom is to recite them **simply,** i.e., only once, one recites them **simply.** In a place where it is customary **to recite a blessing after** *hallel*, one should **recite a blessing. Everything is in accordance with the** regional **custom. Abaye said: They taught** that it depends on the local custom **only** with regard to the blessing **after** *hallel*; **however,** in all places it is **a mitzva to recite a blessing before** *hallel*. **As Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **all the mitzvot, one recites a blessing over them prior to their performance.**

The Gemara now takes up a brief *halachic* discussion drawn from a *baraita* in Sukkah. *Makom shenahagu lichpol yichpol* — in a place where the custom is to double certain verses in the *hallel*, one doubles them. *Liphshot* — where the custom is to recite them straight without doubling, one does so. *Levarech acharav yevarech* — if the custom is to make a concluding *bracha* at the end of *hallel*, one makes it. *Hakol ke-minhag ha-medinah* — everything follows the local custom. The doubling referred to here is the repetition of verses such as *odecha Hashem ki anisani* and others near the end of *hallel* that are not repeated as written in the original *pesukim* but are recited twice as a matter of *minhag*.

## Bracha Status

### Bracha Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״עוֹבֵר״ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאַקְדּוֹמֵי הוּא? אָמַר רַבִּי נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיָּרׇץ אֲחִימַעַץ דֶּרֶךְ הַכִּכָּר וַיַּעֲבוֹר אֶת הַכּוּשִׁי״. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וְהוּא עָבַר לִפְנֵיהֶם״. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי מֵהָכָא: ״וַיַּעֲבוֹר מַלְכָּם לִפְנֵיהֶם וַה׳ בְּרֹאשָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where may it be inferred that** the word ***over* is a formulation** that means **before** an action is performed? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said that** this is **as it is written: "And Ahimaaz ran by way of the square and he passed [*vaya'avor*] the Kushite"** (II Samuel 18:23), i.e., Ahimaaz overtook the Kushite. **Abaye said** that it is derived **from here: "And he passed [*avar*] before them"** (Genesis 33:3). **Some say** that the proof is **from here: "And their king shall pass on [*vaya'avor*] before them, and God at their head"** (Micah 2:13).

Abaye clarifies: the debate and variation in custom described above applies only to the concluding *bracha* — whether one says a *bracha* at the end of *hallel* or not varies by locale. But *aval lefanav mitzvah levarech* — before *hallel*, one must make a *bracha*, as one makes a *bracha* before every *mitzvah*. This is based on Rav Yehuda's ruling in the name of Shmuel: *kol mitzvot mevarach aleihen over la'asiyasan* — for all *mitzvot*, one recites the *bracha* before performing them.

The Gemara asks: *mai mashma d'over lishna de-akdumi hu* — how do we know that *over* means "before" rather than something else? Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak cites the verse *vayaratz Achima'atz derech ha-kikar vaya'avor et ha-Kushi* — Achima'atz ran through the valley and passed in front of the Ethiopian, meaning *avor* indicates passing in front, preceding. Abaye cites *ve-hu avar lifneihem* — he passed before them. A third version cites *vaya'avor malkam lifneihem ve-Hashem be-rosham* — their king passed before them with God at their head. The image of *over la'asiyasan* is thus of the *bracha* running ahead of the act, announcing and proclaiming and giving identity to the *mitzvah* — the *bracha* precedes and, in a sense, inaugurates what follows.

## Doubling Verses…

### Rabbi Doubling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי כּוֹפֵל בָּהּ דְּבָרִים, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פַּרְטָא מוֹסִיף בָּהּ דְּבָרִים. מַאי מוֹסִיף? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מוֹסִיף לִכְפּוֹל מֵ״אוֹדְךָ״ לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **doubles** certain **matters in** *hallel*. **Rabbi Elazar ben Perata added matters to** *hallel*. The Gemara asks: **What** did he **add?** Certainly this cannot mean that Rabbi Elazar ben Perata added statements of his own to *hallel*. **Abaye said:** He **added repetitions,** i.e., he repeated other verses, **from "I will give thanks to You" and onward.** From that point on, he repeated each verse.

A *baraita* records: *Rebbi kofel bidvarim* — Rebbi would double certain verses. *Rebbi Elazar ben Parta mosif bidvarim* — Rabbi Elazar ben Parta would add. Abaye explains that *mosif* means he would do more doubling than Rebbi: *mosif lichpol me-odecha le-mata* — he would extend the doubling beginning from the verse *odecha Hashem ki anisani*, which precedes *Anna Hashem hoshia na*. The *Rashbam* indicates that Rebbi's doubling began only from *baruch haba be-shem Hashem*, whereas Rabbi Elazar ben Parta doubled from an earlier point in the *hallel*.

## Future Seudah

### Yigdal Hayeled

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַב עַוִּירָא, זִימְנִין אָמַר לֵיהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַמֵּי וְזִימְנִין אָמַר לֵיהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַסִּי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּגְדַּל הַיֶּלֶד וַיִּגָּמַל״ — עָתִיד הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַעֲשׂוֹת סְעוּדָה לַצַּדִּיקִים בְּיוֹם שֶׁיִּגְמֹל חַסְדּוֹ לְזַרְעוֹ שֶׁל יִצְחָק. לְאַחַר שֶׁאוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין נוֹתְנִין לוֹ לְאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ כּוֹס שֶׁל בְּרָכָה לְבָרֵךְ,
>
> **Translation:** In connection to its discussion of *hallel*, the Gemara cites a statement that **Rav Avira taught. Sometimes he said** this exposition **citing Rav Ami, and sometimes he said** it **citing Rav Asi: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And the child grew and was weaned [*vayiggamal*],** and Abraham made a great feast on the day that Isaac was weaned" (Genesis 21:8)? **In the future, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will prepare a feast for the righteous on the day that He extends [*sheyigmol*] His mercy to the descendants of Isaac. After they eat and drink,** the celebrants will **give Abraham our father a cup of blessing to recite the blessing,** as he is the first of our forefathers.

Rav Avira expounded — sometimes attributed in the name of Rav, sometimes in the name of Rav Assi — on the verse *vayigdal ha-yeled vayigamel* — the child grew up and was weaned. This verse, which this week's *parasha* brought back to mind, is read not only literally in the context of Yitzchak's weaning but also as an allusion to the future. *Asid haKadosh Baruch Hu la'asos seuda la-tzadikim be-yom sheyigamel chasdo le-zaro shel Yitzchak* — God will in the future make a great banquet for the righteous on the day He fulfills His *chesed* to the descendants of Yitzchak, in the era of Mashiach. Just as Avraham made a great *mishteh* upon Yitzchak's weaning, *vaya'as Avraham mishteh gadol*, God will make the ultimate *mishteh gadol* in the future.

### Disqualifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר לָהֶן: אֵינִי מְבָרֵךְ, שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. אוֹמֵר לוֹ לְיִצְחָק: טוֹל וּבָרֵךְ. אוֹמֵר לָהֶן: אֵינִי מְבָרֵךְ, שֶׁיָּצָא מִמֶּנִּי עֵשָׂו. אוֹמֵר לוֹ לְיַעֲקֹב: טוֹל וּבָרֵךְ. אוֹמֵר לָהֶם: אֵינִי מְבָרֵךְ, שֶׁנָּשָׂאתִי שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת בְּחַיֵּיהֶן, שֶׁעֲתִידָה תּוֹרָה לְאוֹסְרָן עָלַי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Abraham will **say to them: I will not recite the blessing, as** I am blemished, for the wicked **Ishmael came from me.** Abraham will **say to Isaac: Take** the cup **and recite the blessing.** Isaac will **say to them: I will not recite the blessing, as** the wicked **Esau came from me.** Isaac will **say to Jacob: Take** the cup **and recite the blessing.** Jacob will **say to them: I will not recite the blessing, as I married two sisters,** Rachel and Leah, **in their lifetimes,** and **in the future the Torah forbade them to me.** Although at the time it was not prohibited to wed two sisters, this practice would eventually be considered a serious transgression.

After the righteous eat and drink at this banquet, the question arises of who should lead the *birkat ha-mazon*. The *kos shel bracha* is offered to Avraham Avinu. He declines: *eini mevarach, sheyatza mimeni Yishmael* — I will not lead the blessing, for Yishmael came from me. I do not fully represent the *kedushah* of *Klal Yisrael*.

They offer it to Yitzchak. He declines: *eini mevarach, sheyatza mimeni Eisav* — for Eisav came from me. Not that Yitzchak sinned, but in this context of messianic completeness, he does not see himself as the right representative of the whole of Israel.

They offer it to Yaakov. He declines: *eini mevarach, she-nasasi shtei achayot be-chayeihen, she-asida Torah le-osran alai* — for I married two sisters while they were both alive, which the Torah would subsequently forbid. Again, he acted without wrongdoing — the Torah had not yet been given — but in the ultimate moment, he does not see himself as the complete representative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹמֵר לוֹ לְמֹשֶׁה: טוֹל וּבָרֵךְ. אוֹמֵר לָהֶם: אֵינִי מְבָרֵךְ, שֶׁלֹּא זָכִיתִי לִיכָּנֵס לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, לֹא בְּחַיַּי וְלֹא בְּמוֹתִי. אוֹמֵר לוֹ לִיהוֹשֻׁעַ: טוֹל וּבָרֵךְ, אוֹמֵר לָהֶן: אֵינִי מְבָרֵךְ, שֶׁלֹּא זָכִיתִי לְבֵן, דִּכְתִיב: ״יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן״, ״נוֹן בְּנוֹ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּנוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** Jacob will **say to Moses: Take** the cup **and recite the blessing.** Moses will **say to them: I will not recite the blessing, as I did not merit to enter Eretz Yisrael, neither in my life nor in my death.** Moses will **say to Joshua: Take** the cup **and recite the blessing.** Joshua will **say to them: I will not recite the blessing, as I did not merit to** have **a son.** The proof for this is **that it is written: "Joshua the son of Nun"** (Numbers 14:6), and in the genealogical list of Ephraim it states: **"Nun his son, Joshua his son"** (I Chronicles 7:27). Since the verse does not mention any children of Joshua, evidently he had no sons.

They offer it to Moshe. He declines: *eini mevarach, shelo zachisi le-hikanes le-Eretz Yisrael, lo be-chayai ve-lo be-mosi* — for I did not merit to enter the Land of Israel, neither in life nor in death. The ideal of the Jewish people is living in *Eretz Yisrael*, and Moshe represents more of a transitional stage, the period of *galus* and wilderness.

They offer it to Yehoshua. He declines: *eini mevarach, shelo zachisi le-ven* — for I had no son. When genealogies are listed it says Yehoshua bin Nun and Nun and Yehoshua, and the line does not extend further. He cannot represent the full continuity of the Jewish people.

### David Worthy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹמֵר לוֹ לְדָוִד: טוֹל וּבָרֵךְ. אוֹמֵר לָהֶן: אֲנִי אֲבָרֵךְ, וְלִי נָאֶה לְבָרֵךְ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כּוֹס יְשׁוּעוֹת אֶשָּׂא וּבְשֵׁם ה׳ אֶקְרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** Joshua will **say to David: Take** the cup **and recite the blessing.** David will **say to them: I will recite the blessing, and it is fitting for me to recite the blessing, as it is stated: "I will lift up the cup of salvation, and I will call upon the name of the Lord"** (Psalms 116:13).

They offer it to David. He accepts without hesitation: *ani avarech ve-li na'eh le-varech* — I will lead the blessing, and it is fitting for me to do so. *Shene'emar kos yeshuot esa u-ve-shem Hashem ekra* — as the verse from *hallel* says: I will raise the cup of salvations and call upon the name of God.

David, of course, was also a flawed individual in various ways, and he did not merit to build the Temple. Yet he alone accepts the role and declares himself fitting. The reason becomes clear: if the theme of *asid lavo* is the resurgence of *malchus beit David* and the coming of the Messiah — if what we are celebrating is precisely the continuity and survival and ultimate sovereignty of the Jewish people — then David is the figure in whom all of that is concentrated. He represents the *malchus* that looks forward to messianic times, the kingship that is never wholly extinguished. Moshe didn't enter the Land, Yehoshua had no descendants, but David is the figure around whom the messianic hope is organized. In this sense, *li na'eh le-varech* — it is genuinely fitting for him.

## Doubling Verses……

### Repetition Concern

A question was raised about whether the practice of repeating words or verses in liturgical contexts — a concern that has grown increasingly prominent in certain communities with regard to chazzanim who repeat words in *tefillah* — connects to this discussion of doubling in *hallel*. This is indeed an undercurrent of the present passage. Whether the concern rises to a strictly *halachic* level is debated, and context matters significantly: repeating in the context of *Shemoneh Esrei*, where one is in the formal structure of a *bracha*, is a different matter from repetition in other settings. The fact that the Gemara here must give explicit license for the doubling of verses suggests that in general, such repetition requires justification. There is also a distinction between repeating an entire verse — which is what the *hallel* custom involves — and repeating individual words within a verse as a melodic device, though both have generated literature. The broader point stands: the Gemara's need to grant permission for doubling implies that doubling is not the default and should be approached with care.

## Bracha Status…

### Hallel Status

The discussion of the *bracha* before *hallel* actually conceals a non-trivial conceptual question. Abaye states as a matter of course that of course one makes a *bracha* before *hallel*, since one makes a *bracha* before every *mitzvah*. But this is not as obvious as it sounds.

Consider *keri'at Shema*: we say *Shema* every day in *davening*, and it is certainly a *mitzvah*, yet we do not say *asher kideshanu be-mitzvosav ve-tzivanu al keri'at Shema*. Instead we have *birkot keri'at Shema* — *Yotzer ha-Me'orot* and *Ahavat Olam* — which are not *birkat ha-mitzvah* in the classic sense but rather *brachos* that expand upon and draw out the themes of *Shema* as part of *tefillah*. They envelop *Shema* as part of the flow of prayer, rather than introducing it as a distinct commandment.

*Hallel* occupies an analogous position. It is said in the context of *tefillah* and has the character of prayer — it is praise addressed to God, not an action performed upon an object. The *chidush* — the genuine innovation — of saying *asher kideshanu be-mitzvosav ve-tzivanu al keri'at ha-hallel* is that it reconstitutes *hallel* as a *ma'aseh mitzvah*, a commandment-act, rather than treating it as an expansion of prayer. One might have thought, on the contrary, that since *hallel* is inherently *tefillah* in character, it would not receive a *birkat ha-mitzvah* at all, just as *Shema* does not receive one in the standard morning liturgy.

There is a further wrinkle: why do we not say a *birkat ha-mitzvah* before *birkat ha-mazon*? After all, benching is a *mitzvah* — yet we simply bench, without first saying *asher kideshanu be-mitzvosav ve-tzivanu le-varech al ha-mazon*. Because *birkat ha-mazon* is itself a *bracha*, a prayer, and once something is itself a prayer one does not generally introduce it with a separate *birkat ha-mitzvah*. This makes the practice with *hallel* all the more distinctive: here we do introduce a prayer with a *birkat ha-mitzvah*, making it simultaneously an act of praise and a formal commandment. The *Tosafos* understand the concluding *bracha* of *hallel* — recited according to our custom — as resolving this tension: the concluding *bracha* transforms the whole of *hallel* into an extended *bracha* structure, with the *bracha* of *asher kideshanu* at the opening and the concluding *bracha* at the close forming a unified framework. That is our custom, but it is worth appreciating that the conceptual question it answers is a genuine one.

## Afikoman Mishnah

### Mishnah Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מַפְטִירִין אַחַר הַפֶּסַח אֲפִיקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One does not conclude after the Paschal lamb with an *afikoman*.**

The Mishnah states: *ein maftirin achar ha-Pesach afikoman* — one does not conclude after the *Pesach* with an *afikoman*. This is identified as the answer to the *ben chacham* — the wise son — who asks about the statutes and practices of this night.

Before turning to the definition of *afikoman*, it is worth pausing on the framing of the Mishnah. The phrase *achar ha-Pesach* — after the *Pesach* — presupposes that one is eating a *korban Pesach*. This raises the broader question of whether this *perek* is speaking about *zman ha-mikdash* — the time of the Temple — or about post-Temple practice. The first Mishnah in the *perek* had said *u-va-mikdash hayu mevi'in lefanav gufo shel pesach*, which indicated that that particular Mishnah distinguished between Temple times and post-Temple practice. Several of the four questions referred to *halailah hazeh kulo tzali* — tonight everything is roasted — which could refer either to Temple times or to a post-Temple *minhag* to eat only roasted meat in commemoration. The present Mishnah, however, comes with no such distinction and appears to take for granted the context of *zman ha-mikdash*.

This impression is strengthened by looking at the final several Mishnayot of the tractate: *lo yochlu ha-Pesach achar chatzot*, *yishnamu lo yochlu ha-Pesach*, *birkhat ha-Pesach* — they all discuss eating the *Pesach*. And significantly, throughout the *perek*'s discussion of the *seder*, the eating of *matzah* and *marror* was not independently addressed. This is striking if the Mishnah were speaking about post-Temple practice — *matzah* and *marror* would be the central items. But if the context is *zman ha-mikdash*, where the *korban Pesach* is the primary obligation and the *matzah* and *marror* are its accompaniments, it makes sense that the Mishnah focuses on the *Pesach* and largely passes over the *matzah* and *marror*. The Gemara will bring them in as it develops the discussion, but the Mishnah's organization itself reflects a Temple-era framework.

### Afikoman Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי אֲפִיקוֹמָן? אָמַר רַב: שֶׁלֹּא יֵעָקְרוּ מֵחֲבוּרָה לַחֲבוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of ***afikoman*? Rav said:** It means **that** a member of a group that ate the Paschal lamb together should **not leave** that **group to** join another **group.** One who joined one group for the Paschal lamb may not leave and take food with him. According to this interpretation, *afikoman* is derived from the phrase *afiku mani*, take out the vessels. The reason for this prohibition is that people might remove the Paschal lamb to another location after they had begun to eat it elsewhere. This is prohibited, as the Paschal lamb must be eaten in a single location by one group.

*Mai afikoman?* — what is this *afikoman*? Rav explains: *shelo ye'akor me-chabura le-chabura* — one may not leave one group eating the *Pesach* and go to eat with another group. There is a formal prohibition against eating the *korban Pesach* in two different *chaburot*, and the *Rashbam* adds that even eating other foods in a second *chabura* is prohibited, as it may lead to eating the *Pesach* itself in two places.

How does the word *afikoman* convey this meaning? The *Rashbam* attempts a reading of the word as a Hebrew compound, but more straightforwardly, *afikoman* is a Greek word — it refers to a kind of carousing that would follow a meal in Greek and Roman culture: *epikomion*, an after-party of visiting other people's homes, drinking wine, socializing from one gathering to the next in a licentious and festive manner. The Mishnah is saying: after eating the *Pesach*, don't go out for the *afikoman*, the Greek-style carousing. The Gemara reads this halakhically rather than morally — the concern is not the carousing per se but the violation of the rule about *chaburot*.

### Afikoman Dessert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: כְּגוֹן אוֹרְדִּילָאֵי לִי וְגוֹזָלַיָּיא לְאַבָּא. וְרַב חֲנִינָא בַּר שֵׁילָא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן (אָמַר) [אָמְרוּ]: כְּגוֹן תְּמָרִים קְלָיוֹת וֶאֱגוֹזִים. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין מַפְטִירִין אַחַר הַפֶּסַח כְּגוֹן תְּמָרִים קְלָיוֹת וֶאֱגוֹזִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** It means that one may not eat dessert after the meal, **like mushrooms** [*urdila'ei*] **for me, and chicks for Abba,** Rav. It was customary for them to eat delicacies after the meal. **And Rav Ḥanina bar Sheila and Rabbi Yoḥanan** say: *Afikoman* refers to foods **such as dates, roasted grains, and nuts,** which are eaten during the meal. It **was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan: One does not conclude** by eating **after the Paschal lamb** foods **such as dates, roasted grains, and nuts.**

Shmuel offers a different reading: the *afikoman* refers not to going elsewhere but to eating a dessert course here, in the same place. *Ke-gon urdilai li ve-gozlaya le-abba* — for example, fungi such as mushrooms for me, and pigeons for Abba, i.e., for Rav. These would be the kinds of delicacies one might eat as a dessert following the main meal. Similarly, Rav Chanina bar Shila and Rabbi Yochanan: *kegon temarim klayot ve-egozim* — dates, roasted grain, and nuts. *Tanya kevatch d'Rabbi Yochanan*: one may not eat dessert after the *Pesach*.

On this reading, the concern is entirely different: it is not about moving between groups but about obliterating the taste and flavor of the *korban Pesach* in one's mouth. The *Pesach* is meant to be the last thing one eats, so that its taste remains. The word *afikoman* would then have to be read in its Greek sense of a dessert course — an *epicomion* in the sense of an after-course — rather than the carousing sense. This latter explanation — preserving the taste of the *Pesach* — is how the contemporary practice of the *afikoman* piece of *matzah* is typically understood: the *matzah* is eaten last and nothing follows it, so that the taste of the *matzah* remains. The *Gemara*'s first explanation, however — rooted in the prohibition of *chaburot* — is in some ways closer to the literal Greek meaning of the word and to the cultural context in which the *seder* was formulated. The discussion will continue on the following daf.
