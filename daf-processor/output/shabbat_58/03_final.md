# Shabbat 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Context

### Opening Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יוֹצֵא הָעֶבֶד בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ אֲבָל לֹא בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: יוֹצֵא הָעֶבֶד בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ אֲבָל לֹא בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses **the matter itself: Shmuel said** that **a slave may go out with a seal that is around his neck but not with a seal that is on his clothes.** That opinion **was also taught** in a *baraita*: **A slave may go out with a seal that is around his neck but not with a seal that is on his clothes.**

The *daf* under discussion is 58a, and we pick up approximately ten lines down with the word *gufa* — the Gemara's own signal that it is now addressing the substance of a previously cited ruling. By way of orientation: the broader *perek* has been cataloguing items that may or may not be worn on *Shabbat*, particularly those that are carried or worn in the public domain. The animating concern in most of these cases is not a biblical prohibition — many of these items are, strictly speaking, legitimate forms of clothing or jewelry — but rather a rabbinic decree rooted in the fear that the wearer might remove the item to show it to someone, or that it might fall off, prompting the wearer to carry it.

### Kvul & Slave Pendants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: לֹא יֵצֵא הָעֶבֶד בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ וְלֹא בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ, זֶה וָזֶה אֵין מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה. וְלֹא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא הוּא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ, זֶה וָזֶה מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita*: **The slave may neither go out with a seal that is around his neck nor with a seal that is on his clothes** on Shabbat, and both **this and that cannot become ritually impure. And** he may **not** go out **with a bell that is** hung **around his neck; however, he may go out with a bell that is on his clothes,** and both **this and that can become ritually impure.**

The most recent discussion had addressed something called a *kvul*, understood by some as a scarf or secondary cap worn beneath a hairnet. The debate there turned on whether a woman would ever remove this underlying garment while outdoors: since she would be reluctant to expose her hair by removing the hairnet, one might argue there is no realistic concern she would take off the *kvul* either. Others, however, understood the *kvul* as a pendant worn by a slave — a form of identification marking him as belonging to a particular master, attached either to his person or to his garment. In that reading, the concern was that if the pendant fell from his garment, he would not want to be seen wearing his clothing without the identifying mark, and would therefore remove his garment and carry it. It is this slave-pendant case — the *chotam* — that now occupies the Gemara's attention.

## Slave Law Excursus

### Ravad on Slave Status

Before entering the Gemara's analysis directly, a brief excursus on the underlying legal landscape is warranted. The slave under discussion is a non-Jewish slave who has undergone *milah* and *tevilah*, thereby becoming obligated in *mitzvot* at the same level as Jewish women. Such a slave is thus himself bound by the laws of *Shabbat* and, in principle, by the rabbinic enactments built upon them.

There is, however, a striking position advanced by the *Ravad* (Rabbi Abraham ben David). When the Torah commands — *lo ta'aseh kol melachah atah uvincha uvitecha avdecha va'amascha* — "do not do any labor, you and your son and your daughter and your slave and your slave-woman," the *Ravad* argues that this *pasuk* is not referring to the halachically integrated slave who has undergone *milah* and *tevilah*. That slave is already independently obligated. Rather, the verse addresses those who are functionally under the master's authority — workers or bondsmen who have not formally entered the halachic category of Jewish-obligated slaves — and the master's obligation is to ensure that they do not perform labor *for him*, even if they may perform labor for themselves.

### Master's Obligation

The verse *lema'an yanuach shorcha va'chamorecha... avdecha va'amascha kamocha* — "so that your ox and donkey shall rest... your slave and slave-woman like yourself" — makes plain that the master bears a distinct obligation with respect to those under his control. Alongside this: *lo ta'aseh kol melachah atah uvincha uvitecha avdecha va'amascha* imposes on the master a prohibition against allowing his slaves to perform *melachah*. Consequently, when a slave who has gone through the halachic process violates *Shabbat*, not only does the slave himself transgress, but the master may be in transgression as well. The master therefore has an independent stake in the *Shabbat* conduct of his slaves.

This framework — that the master's concern for the pendant is legally salient — will prove important momentarily when the Gemara evaluates the conditions under which a slave may or may not go out wearing the *chotam*.

### Categorical Structure Q

One might ask why the *Mishnah* begins with a single declaration of *bameh ha'adam yotze* — "with what does a person go out" — only to subdivide the discussion into the separate categories of men, women, and slaves, each with their own distinct *halachot*. Clothing is highly gendered, and the social norms that determine what counts as an ornament, a burden, or an ordinary garment differ substantially across these categories. Each category will therefore generate different legal consequences; that they were grouped together at the outset reflects the underlying unity of the question, even as the details necessarily diverge. The same social-norm sensitivity that distinguishes men's clothing from women's clothing is precisely what will drive the analysis of the slave's pendant.

## Slave Pendants Analysis

### Samuel's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ רַבֵּיהּ, הָא דַּעֲבַד אִיהוּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This,** where Shmuel said that one may go out with a slave's seal on Shabbat, **is** referring to a case **where his master made it for him.** The slave will not remove it because he fears his master. Therefore, there is no concern lest he carry it. However, **that,** where the mishna said that it is prohibited to go out with a *kavul*, which according to Shmuel is the seal of a slave, is referring to a case **where he made it for himself** to indicate to all who his master is so that he may enjoy his master's protection. In that case, since it is dependent solely upon his discretion, there is concern lest he remove the seal and carry it. Therefore, the Sages prohibited going out with it into the public domain.

The Gemara now addresses the *gufa* — the substance of the ruling. *Amar Shmuel: yotze ha'eved bechotam shebetzavaro aval lo bechotam shebichsuto* — Shmuel rules that a slave may go out on *Shabbat* with the identifying seal worn around his neck, but not with a seal attached to his garment. The rationale is straightforward: if the seal worn around his neck breaks or falls off, the slave has little incentive to do anything about it — he cannot reattach it on *Shabbat*, and leaving it where it fell creates no particular problem for him. But if the seal affixed to his garment falls off, he faces a different dilemma. He does not want to be seen by his master wearing the garment without the identifying mark, and so he might well remove the garment and carry it — an act of *hotza'ah* on *Shabbat*. It is that risk which the rabbinic decree addresses.

A *baraita* is then cited in support: *yotze ha'eved bechotam shebetzavaro aval lo bechotam shebichsuto* — the same formulation as Shmuel's ruling, and therefore *tanya nami hachi*, the *baraita* accords with Shmuel.

### Baraita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקִימְתָּא לְהָא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל — דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ רַבֵּיהּ? בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ אַמַּאי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what** case **did you establish this** statement **of Shmuel?** It is in the case of a seal **that his master made for him.** If so, **why** may he **not** go out **with a seal on his clothes?** There too, since his master made it for him he will not remove it.

The Gemara immediately raises a contradiction from a second *baraita*: *lo yetze ha'eved bechotam shebetzavaro velo bechotam shebichsuto* — the slave should not go out with the seal either around his neck or on his garment. This directly contradicts Shmuel, who permitted the seal around the neck.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא מִיפְּסַק, וּמִירְתַת וּמְיקַפֵּל לֵיהּ וּמַחֵית לֵיהּ אַכַּתְפֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: There the concern is that **perhaps** the seal **will be severed, and** the slave **will fear** his master **and fold** his cloak **and place it on his shoulders** so that his master will not see that he has no seal on his clothing. That concern is consistent with the principle established elsewhere.

One resolution offered is that the difference lies in *who made* the pendant. If the master had it made and insists the slave wear it — *de'avid lei rabeih* — then there is no concern the slave will voluntarily remove it, and if it falls off around his neck, he will simply leave it. In that case the neck-worn seal is permissible, as Shmuel rules. But if the slave had it made for himself — *de'avid lei le'nafsheih* — then we are concerned he might voluntarily remove it, and the *baraita* prohibiting it around the neck applies.

### Pendants & Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוּקִימְתָּא — בִּשְׁלָמָא שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת — הָנֵי הוּא דְּלָא מְקַבְּלִי טוּמְאָה, הָא כֵּלִים דִּידְהוּ מְקַבְּלִי טוּמְאָה. אֲבָל אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּשֶׁל טִיט תְּנַן, הָנֵי הוּא דְּלָא מְקַבְּלִי טוּמְאָה, הָא כֵּלִים דִּידְהוּ מְקַבְּלִי טוּמְאָה?! וְהָא תַּנְיָא: כְּלֵי אֲבָנִים כְּלֵי גְלָלִים וּכְלֵי אֲדָמָה — אֵין מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה לֹא מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה וְלֹא מִדִּבְרֵי סוֹפְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what case did you establish** the *baraita*? **Granted, if you say** it is referring to a **metal** seal, it is possible to understand the novel aspect of the *baraita* as follows: **These are the** objects **that cannot become ritually impure; however, their vessels** made of the same material **can become ritually impure.** **However, if you say** that **we learned** with regard to seals **of clay,** can it be similarly inferred that **these** seals **are the** objects that **cannot become ritually impure; however, their vessels** made of the same material **can become ritually impure?** **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Vessels of stone, vessels of dung, and vessels of earth** that are not made into earthenware **can neither become ritually impure by Torah law nor by rabbinic law?**

The *baraita* appends a further ruling: *ve-ze ve-ze ein mekabelin tumah* — neither the neck-worn seal nor the garment-affixed seal becomes *tamei*. This observation prompts analysis. Rashi explains that the pendant is not jewelry, because the slave does not wear it as an ornament or out of any desire to look attractive — he wears it because his master compels him to. It is therefore not susceptible to *tumah* as jewelry would be. Nor, Rashi adds, is it a *kli* — a functional vessel being put to some use; it simply rests on the person without being actively employed.

This is a striking conclusion, however, because it creates an apparent tension. If the pendant is neither jewelry nor vessel, on what basis is the slave *biblically* permitted to wear it at all? If it were merely a burden, he could not. The implicit assumption of the Gemara's entire discussion is that there is no biblical prohibition — the rabbinic decree is the only obstacle in certain configurations. Yet the category that would permit it — clothing or ornament — is also the category that would make it *mekabel tumah*. To say it is neither jewelry nor vessel, and yet is biblically permissible to wear, points toward the conclusion that it functions as a form of clothing. But if so, why is it not *tamei*? The answer may lie in the material: clothing made of metal, as opposed to fabric, does not enter the halachic category of clothing susceptible to *tumah*.

### Status Puzzle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ שֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, learn from it** that this *baraita* is referring to utensils made **of metal.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from it.**

This puzzle — that the pendant appears to occupy no clean halachic category — is genuinely difficult. It is not jewelry, not a functional vessel, not fabric clothing. If it is some form of metal clothing, then the rule that only fabric garments contract *tumah* as clothing would explain its *taharah*, while still allowing it to be worn biblically. But this remains an unresolved tension: if it is clothing, the question is what kind and under what theory; if it is none of the above, the biblical permission to wear it demands explanation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אִידִי וְאִידִי דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ רַבֵּיהּ — וְכָאן בְּשֶׁל מַתֶּכֶת, וְכָאן בְּשֶׁל טִיט, וְכִדְרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: דָּבָר הַמַּקְפִּיד עָלָיו רַבּוֹ — אֵין יוֹצְאִין בּוֹ. דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין מַקְפִּיד עָלָיו — יוֹצְאִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this resolution: **No,** both **this and that are** referring to a case **where his master made** it **for him.** The difference can be explained differently. **And here,** where it was prohibited, it is referring **to** a seal **of metal, and here,** where it was permitted, it is a seal **of clay. And as Rav Naḥman** said that **Rabba bar Avuh said: With an object about which his master is particular, one may not go out** on Shabbat, lest it become detached from the garment, and fear of his master lead the slave to carry it in his hand. **With an object about which his master is not particular, one may go out with it.**

What the Gemara has established so far, then, is: the slave's identifying pendant is not *mekabel tumah* — it does not become *tamei*. Yet it is biblically permissible to wear. Rabbinically, however, wearing it on the garment is prohibited (lest it fall off and prompt carrying the garment), and wearing it around the neck is subject to further conditions.

The Gemara then reasons: *midekatani zeh vezeh ein mekabelin tumah* — since both forms of the pendant are said not to become *tamei*, and since the pendant is made of a material that *could* in principle be *tamei* (were it fashioned into a proper *kli*), the Gemara infers that we must be speaking of metal pendants. *Shma mina de'matachet* — conclude from this that we are dealing with metal. The inference is clinched by a *Mishnah* establishing that vessels made of stone, dried dung, or unfired earth — *klei avanim, klei glalim, ve'klei adama* — are never *mekabelin tumah*, neither biblically nor rabbinically. If the pendant were made of plaster or clay, it would be trivially obvious that it is not *tamei*, and the *baraita* would be making a pointless observation. The fact that the *baraita* finds it necessary to state that the pendant does not become *tamei* confirms that it is made of metal — a material that, when fashioned into a proper vessel, certainly would be susceptible. Here, however, because it is neither jewelry nor vessel, the metal pendant remains *tahor*.

### Metal vs Plaster

With that established, the Gemara returns to the contradiction between Shmuel and the second *baraita*. Rather than resolving it through the distinction of who made the pendant, the Gemara proposes an alternative: *ve-kan be-shel matechet, kan be-shel tina* — one case involves a metal pendant and the other a plaster pendant. Even when the master had the pendant made and insists the slave wear it, there remains a meaningful difference based on material.

If the pendant is made of plaster, it has essentially no monetary value — it may even shatter upon falling. If it falls off around the slave's neck, he will simply leave it where it lies; there is no incentive whatsoever to pick it up. In this case the seal around the neck is permitted, consistent with Shmuel's ruling. But if the pendant is made of metal, a different concern arises: even though the slave will not voluntarily remove it (the master insists he wear it), if it falls off, the slave faces a dilemma. Leaving a piece of metal — something of monetary value — lying in the public domain is its own problem; the slave may fear his master's displeasure at losing the valuable object, and so he may pick it up and carry it home. That carrying is the forbidden act. Hence the second *baraita*, which prohibits even the neck-worn seal, is speaking of a metal pendant.

### Makpid Criterion

Rav Nachman bar Ami, citing Rabbah bar Avuha, formulates this as a general criterion: *davar ha-makpid alav rabo — ein yotzin bo*. If the master is *makpid* — particular, insistent — about the object's value, and the slave knows he will face consequences for losing it, then the slave cannot go out with it on *Shabbat*, because we anticipate that if it falls, he will carry it home rather than risk his master's anger. Conversely, *davar she'ein makpid alav rabo — yotzin bo*: if the master cares that the slave wear it but is indifferent to its material value, and therefore the slave has no reason to pick it up if it falls, he may go out wearing it. The master's concern — his *makpid*-ness — functions as the operative halachic criterion distinguishing permissible from impermissible configurations.

## Social Norms & Halacha

### Beged Ish/Isha

The broader question underlying much of this discussion is how social norms interact with halachic categorization. This is not a marginal issue: the very categories at stake — *beged ish* and *beged isha*, men's clothing and women's clothing — are defined not by any a priori Platonic essence but by the social realities of a given time and place. The biblical prohibition on cross-dressing (*lo yilbash gever simlat ishah*) does not itself specify which garments belong to which category; that is entirely a function of convention.

A clear example is trousers. When women first began wearing trousers in modern Western society, a period existed during which pants were understood, by virtually everyone, as distinctly men's garments. At that stage, one could plausibly argue that a woman wearing pants was violating the prohibition of *beged ish*. As social norms shifted and trousers were cut specifically for women and worn universally, that argument became increasingly difficult to sustain on the grounds of *beged ish* alone. Today, objections to women wearing trousers are primarily grounded in *tzniut* — a separate and independently operating concern — rather than in the cross-dressing prohibition. The two concerns must not be conflated: *tzniut* and the prohibition of *beged ish* rest on different foundations and are triggered by different conditions.

### Locked Categories?

This raises a significant question that runs through every area of *halacha*: once the *Sages* specifically identified concrete items as falling under a given category — this garment is *beged ish*, this practice violates that rule — do those identifications become permanently fixed even when the underlying social reality has changed? The categories themselves (men's clothing, women's clothing, burden, ornament) are not static; they are defined relationally to social practice. But it is possible to argue that once a rabbinic enactment or identification is articulated in terms of a specific item, it acquires a life of its own and is no longer hostage to shifting conventions.

This question — how much are specific applications "written in stone" as opposed to being revisable when circumstances change — is perennial in *halachic* literature and arises in every domain. In the context of *Shabbat*, what counts as clothing versus a burden, what constitutes a reasonable concern that an item will be taken off and shown to someone, what qualifies as jewelry as opposed to a mere carrying mechanism — all of these determinations are sensitive, to varying degrees, to the facts on the ground. The categories are social constructs, and therefore they track, at least partially, social change.

Alongside this, the question of whether rabbinic concerns are applied categorically or case-by-case also surfaces here. Tosafot's general approach is that once a concern attaches to some members of a category, the decree extends to the whole category, even when the particular circumstances might not generate the specific danger. The Gemara itself is not fully explicit on this point, but the working assumption in many of these cases is that we prohibit the entire category once a systemic concern has been identified.

## Bells & Jewelry

### Slave Bell Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְלֹא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא הוּא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ. וְזֶה וָזֶה מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** In that same *baraita* **the Master said** that the slave may **not** go out **with a bell that is around his neck, but he may go out with a bell that is on his clothes.** Both this and that can become **ritually impure.**

The Gemara now turns from the identifying seal to the *zug* — the bell. *Ve-lo bezug shebetzavaro* — a slave may not go out on *Shabbat* with a bell worn around his neck. *Aval yotze hu bezug she-biksuto* — he may, however, go out with a bell woven into his garment. Importantly, *ve-ze ve-ze mekabelin tumah* — both configurations of the bell, whether around the neck or woven into the garment, are *mekabelin tumah*, susceptible to ritual impurity. The bell is jewelry, and jewelry, unlike the identifying pendant, does become *tamei*.

### Loose vs. Woven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ אַמַּאי לָא — דִּילְמָא מִיפְּסִיק וְאָתֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי? זוֹג שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ נָמֵי לִיחוּשׁ דִּילְמָא מִיפְּסִיק וְאָתֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי! הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן — דִּימְחָא בֵּיהּ מוּמְחָא. וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא אָרוּג לֹא גָּזְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With **a bell that is around his neck, why** may he **not** go out? It is due to concern **lest it be severed and he come to carry it.** If so, with **a bell on his clothes too, let us be concerned lest it be severed and he come to carry it.** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where** the bell **is woven into** the garment, **and** it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, as Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said:** A**nything that is woven** into a garment, **the Sages did not issue a decree** prohibiting going out with it on Shabbat.

The question is why the distinction between around the neck and woven into the garment should matter, given that both are equally jewelry. The Gemara asks: *zug shebetzavaro amai lo?* — why may he not go out with the bell around his neck? The answer: *dilma mipsik va'ate l'atuyei* — we are concerned that the strap may break and he will pick it up and carry it home, since it has value as jewelry.

But if that is the concern, then *zug she-biksuto nami leichosh dilma mipsik va'ate l'atuyei* — the same concern should apply to the bell woven into the garment; it too could come loose and be carried. The Gemara resolves this: *de'micha bei mumcha* — when the bell is on the garment, we are speaking of a case where it is woven in. A bell properly woven into a garment will not simply fall off; the fear of breakage and subsequent carrying does not apply. The bell around the neck, by contrast, hangs on a strap that is vulnerable to snapping.

### Kol She-Arag

This ruling is grounded in the principle articulated by Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua: *kol she'arag lo gazru* — anything that has been woven in is not subject to the rabbinic decree. It is worth noting, as both Rashi and Tosafot observe, that this principle appears in a strikingly different application elsewhere in the tractate. In an earlier context, it was invoked in connection with strings woven tightly into a woman's hair — the question being whether tightly interwoven ornaments constitute a *chatzitzah* for immersion, and whether loosely woven ornamental chains in the hair might be permissible to wear on *Shabbat* because they would not be removed for *mikvah*. There, *kol she'arag* addressed whether something is securely attached to the hair. Here, it addresses whether something affixed to a garment presents a concern of falling off and being carried.

This is a point well worth reflection. The same *amora'im*'s statement — *kol she'arag lo gazru* — is being deployed with two distinct meanings in two distinct contexts. Whether both interpretations reflect the original intent of the statement, or whether the Gemara in each case is reading a general principle through the lens of its own context, is a question that David Weiss Halivni has raised regarding the way the Gemara transmits and reapplies earlier *amoraic* formulations.

## Animal Restrictions

### Animal Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְלֹא תֵּצֵא בְּהֵמָה לֹא בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבְּצַוָּארָהּ, וְלֹא בְּחוֹתָם שֶׁבִּכְסוּתָהּ, וְלֹא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארָהּ, וְלֹא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבִּכְסוּתָהּ, זֶה וָזֶה אֵין מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** In the *baraita* cited earlier, it was taught that **the Master said: An animal may neither go out with a seal that is around its neck, nor with a seal that is on its clothes, nor with a bell that is on its clothes, nor with a bell that is around its neck.** Both **this and that cannot become ritually impure.**

The *baraita* continues with the case of the *behemah* — the animal. *Ve-lo tetze behemah lo be-chotam shebetzvarah ve-lo be-chotam shebichsutah* — an animal may not go out wearing identifying tags, either around its neck or affixed to any covering it wears. What is remarkable here — and what was flagged in an earlier session as genuinely puzzling — is that by an animal, the identifying tag is treated as a biblical burden (*massa*), not merely a rabbinic problem. Yet by a slave, the identifying pendant is biblically permissible; the restrictions are rabbinic in nature. The inversion is difficult to explain, and no ready discussion of the distinction was found in the *rishonim*.

The animal also may not go out with bells: *ve-lo bezug shebetzvarah ve-lo bezug shebichsutah* — neither around the neck nor woven into a garment placed on the animal.

### Animal Ornaments

The *baraita* states: *ve-ze ve-ze ein mekabelin tumah* — neither the identifying tags nor the bells worn by an animal become *tamei*. The logic in the case of the identifying tags follows from what has already been established: they are not jewelry (the animal has no aesthetic interest in looking adorned), they are not functional vessels in the ordinary sense, and they do not enter the halachic category of *kelim* susceptible to *tumah*. The case of the bells, however, is more complex. The Gemara will need to account for why bells on an animal do not become *tamei*, given that bells on a person are explicitly *mekabelin tumah* as jewelry.

The reference to the animal's *kesut* — its covering — is explained by Rashi as referring to a blanket or headdress placed on the animal for warmth or appearance, a practice attested with camels and horses.

### Bell Tamei Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹג דִּבְהֵמָה אֵין מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה? וּרְמִינְהוּ: זוֹג שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה — טְמֵאָה, וְשֶׁל דֶּלֶת — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does a bell of an animal not become ritually impure?** The Gemara proceeds to **raise a contradiction** from that which was taught in another *baraita*: **The bell of an animal** can become **ritually impure,** **and** the bell **of a door is ritually pure.**

The Gemara immediately challenges the claim that a bell worn by an animal does not become *tamei*. *Ureminhu: zug she-beheimah tme'iah, ve-shel delet tehorah* — a *baraita* teaches that a bell on an animal is *tamei*, while a bell on a door is *tahor*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל דֶּלֶת וַעֲשָׂאוֹ לִבְהֵמָה — טָמֵא. שֶׁל בְּהֵמָה וַעֲשָׂאוֹ לְדֶלֶת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחִבְּרוֹ לַדֶּלֶת וּקְבָעוֹ בְּמַסְמְרִים — טָמֵא, שֶׁכׇּל הַכֵּלִים יוֹרְדִין לִידֵי טוּמְאָתָן בְּמַחְשָׁבָה, וְאֵין עוֹלִין מִידֵּי טוּמְאָתָן אֶלָּא בְּשִׁנּוּי מַעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** If one took the bell **of a door and converted it** into a bell **for an animal, it** can become **ritually impure;** however, if one took the bell **of an animal and converted it** into a bell **for a door, even though he attached it to the door and** even **fastened it with nails, it** can still become **ritually impure because all utensils descend into their** state of **ritual impurity by means of thought** alone, i.e., as a result of a decision to designate them for a specific purpose through which they will become susceptible to ritual impurity, they receive that status immediately. **However, they only ascend from their** state of **ritual impurity by means of an action** that effects physical **change** to the vessel itself. A change in designation alone is ineffective.

The logic of the door's *taharah* is clear: a bell affixed to a door, if nailed down firmly, is considered *mechubar la'karka* — connected to the ground through the structure of the house — and objects attached to the ground cannot contract *tumah*. A bell merely hung on a nail and easily removable would be different; that remains a *kli* and would be *tamei*. But one properly fixed to the door participates in the door's status and is therefore *tahor*.

The *baraita* then adds a critical further ruling: *shel delet va'asao la-behemah — tme'iah*. If the bell had been nailed into the door and was then removed and assigned to an animal, it becomes *tamei*. The reason: it has now been assigned to the category of an animal's bell, and that assignment — *machshavah*, intention — suffices to enter it into the category of things susceptible to *tumah*. Conversely, *shel behemah va'asao la-delet* — if it had been an animal's bell and was subsequently nailed into a door — *af al pi she-chibro la-delet, tamei*: even though it is now affixed to the door, it remains *tamei*. The Gemara provides the governing principle: *she-kol ha-kelim yordim lidei tum'ah be-machshavah ve-ein olim midei tum'atan ela be-shinui ma'aseh* — vessels enter the category susceptible to *tumah* through mere intention or assignment, but they exit that category only through a *shinui ma'aseh*, a genuine physical change to the object itself.

## Sticky Principle

### Door to Animal

The asymmetry embedded in this principle — what might be called the sticky principle — merits emphasis. When the bell was on the door, affixed by nails, it appeared to have undergone a physical change: it was physically integrated into a larger structure. Yet the Gemara does not count this as a *shinui ma'aseh* sufficient to change the bell's status. The reason is that the bell itself was not physically altered; only its location changed, and it was attached to something. The actual object — the bell — remains physically identical to what it was when it was an animal's bell. Nailing it to a door does not change the bell; it merely repositions it. Hence the bell retains its susceptibility to *tumah*.

### Entry & Exit

The underlying principle is sharp: entry into *tumah* status is easy — it requires only *machshavah*, the mental act of assigning an object to a particular use or category. The moment one decides a bell will serve as an animal's bell, it enters that category and becomes susceptible to *tumah*. Exit from that status, by contrast, is difficult — it demands a genuine *shinui ma'aseh*, a physical change to the object itself, not merely a change of location, owner, or intended use. This asymmetry reflects a broader halachic disposition: the status of *kelim* adheres tenaciously once acquired, and superficial changes do not dislodge it.

## Knocker Principle

### With/Without Knocker

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ עִינְבָּל, הָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ עִינְבָּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, where it was taught that the bell can become ritually impure, is referring to a case **where it has a clapper [*inbal*]. That** *baraita*, where it was taught that the bell cannot become ritually impure, is referring to a case **where it does not have a clapper.**

Given that a *baraita* says an animal's bell is *tamei*, how is this reconciled with the *baraita* that said an animal's bells do not become *tamei*? The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *ha d'it lei inbal ve-ha d'leit lei inbal* — the one source speaks of a bell that has a knocker (an *inbal*, a clapper inside), and the other speaks of one without a knocker. A bell with a knocker — one that actually sounds as the animal walks — becomes *tamei*. A bell without a knocker does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי מָנָא הוא, אַף עַל פִּי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ עִינְבָּל. אִי לָאו מָנָא הוא — עִינְבָּל מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ מָנָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Whichever** way **you** look at it, this is difficult. **If** the bell **is a vessel,** then **even though it has no clapper** it should be susceptible to ritual impurity. **If it is not a vessel, does a clapper render it a vessel?**

But the Gemara immediately presses: *mama nafshach* — either way, the reasoning seems difficult. If the bell is considered a vessel, then even without the knocker it should be *tamei*, since a vessel is a vessel. If it is not considered a vessel, then why does adding a knocker suddenly transform it into one susceptible to *tumah*?

### Sound Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, כִּדְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִן לְמַשְׁמִיעַ קוֹל בִּכְלֵי מַתָּכוֹת שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל דָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יָבֹא בָאֵשׁ תַּעֲבִירוּ בָאֵשׁ״ — אֲפִילּוּ דִּבּוּר, יָבֹא בָּאֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Yes,** the clapper can determine the bell's status with regard to ritual impurity, **in accordance with** that which **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said. As Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: From where** is it derived **that a metal vessel that produces sound is** considered a vessel and can become **ritually impure? As it is stated: "Every thing that passes through the fire, you shall make it pass through the fire,** and it shall be clean; nevertheless, it shall be purified with the water of sprinkling; and all that does not pass through the fire you shall make to go through water" (Numbers 31:23). And the Sages interpret the verse homiletically: Every thing [*davar*], **even speech [*dibbur*];** in other words, even an object that makes a sound you **shall pass through fire** to purify it because it is a vessel.

The answer lies in a *derashah* of Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan. The verse in the account of the Midianite spoils — *kol davar asher yavo va'esh ta'aviru va'esh ve-taher* — "anything that comes through fire, you shall pass through fire and purify it" — is parsed not for its primary *peshat* meaning (which the *Sages* elsewhere interpret as the source for *kashering* vessels that absorbed *treif* foods), but rather as a basis for the rule that metal objects that produce sound are *tamei*. The *derashah* focuses on the word *davar* — thing or word — and reads it as encompassing even *dibur*, sound. Thus: things that produce sound from metal are to be included in the category of *kelim* susceptible to *tumah*.

The context here is illuminating. The spoils of Midian included musical instruments and bells among the *kelim* brought back from war. Elazar the Kohen instructed the people on how to render these vessels *tahor*. The Gemara reads this as a scriptural basis establishing that sound-producing metal vessels are a recognized category of *kli* with its own rules concerning *tumah* and *taharah*.

### Interpretations

Two distinct understandings of this principle emerge. The simpler reading — and apparently the straightforward reading of the Gemara — is that there is a substantive independent category: metal sound-producers (*mashmi'ei kol*) are *kelim* susceptible to *tumah* by virtue of what they are and what they do, regardless of for whom they are made. On this reading, even a bell hung on an animal enters the category of *kelim* through the knocker, because making sound is itself the defining function that renders it a vessel.

Tosafot resists positing an entirely new category of sound-makers. Instead, Tosafot argues that the real point is functional utility: a person puts a bell on an animal in order to track the animal's location. The bell's sounding serves the owner directly — just as a rod used to guide an animal is considered the owner's *kli* because the person uses it actively, so too a bell whose sound the owner relies upon to locate the animal is the owner's *kli*. Without the knocker, the bell makes no sound, serves no locating function, and is therefore not the owner's vessel. With the knocker, it does serve that function, and it therefore becomes a *kli* susceptible to *tumah*. On this reading, there is no new categorical principle about sound-makers; rather, the knocker is the mechanism by which the person-owner actually uses the object.

### Context Determines

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקִימְתָּא — בִּדְלֵית לֵיהּ עִינְבָּל? אֵימָא מְצִיעֲתָא: וְלֹא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבְּצַוָּארוֹ, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא הוּא בְּזוֹג שֶׁבִּכְסוּתוֹ, וְזֶה וָזֶה מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה. אִי דְּלֵית לֵיהּ עִינְבָּל מִי מְקַבְּלִי טוּמְאָה?
>
> **Translation:** However, the matter can be clarified further. **In what** case **did you establish** the *baraita*; in the case of a bell **that does not have a clapper?** If so, **say the middle** clause of that *baraita*: **And** he may **not** go out **with a bell that is** hung **around his neck; however, he may go out with a bell that is on his clothes,** and both **this and that can become ritually impure. If** it is referring to a bell that **does not have a clapper, can it become ritually impure?**

The Gemara now returns to the middle case — the bell worn by a person, not an animal — and notes a complication. The *baraita* stated that bells on a person (*ve-ze ve-ze mekabelin tumah*) are *tamei* in both configurations — around the neck and woven into a garment. But if we have been explaining the animal's case by saying that only a bell with a knocker becomes *tamei*, the same logic should apply to the person's case, and the *baraita* about the person's bells should likewise distinguish between those with and without knockers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: הָעוֹשֶׂה זֹגִים לְמַכְתֶּשֶׁת וְלַעֲרִיסָה וּלְמִטְפְּחוֹת סְפָרִים וּלְמִטְפְּחוֹת תִּינוֹקוֹת, יֵשׁ לָהֶם עִינְבָּל — טְמֵאִין, אֵין לָהֶם עִינְבָּל — טְהוֹרִין, נִיטְּלוּ עִינְבְּלֵיהֶן — עֲדַיִן טוּמְאָתָן עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from the *Tosefta*: **One who makes bells for the mortar** used to crush spices, **and for the cradle, and for mantles of** Torah **scrolls, and for coverings of small children,** if **they have a clapper they** can become **ritually impure,** and if **they do not have a clapper they are ritually pure** and cannot become impure. If after they became ritually impure **their clappers were removed, their ritual impurity still** remains **upon them.**

The Gemara then draws a distinction: *hani mili be-tinok de-la-kala avdi lei* — when we require a knocker for the bell to be *tamei*, that is specifically in the case of an infant or a crib, where the *sole purpose* of the bell is to produce sound. Without the knocker, it produces no sound, serves no function, and is therefore not a *kli*. *Aval gadol — tachshit hu lo* — but for an adult, a bell is an ornament even without a knocker, because adults wear bells as jewelry simply for their aesthetic quality. Therefore, for an adult, a bell without a knocker is still *tamei* because it is worn as jewelry; for an infant's crib or the child's scarf, only a functioning bell with a knocker enters the category of *kelim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּתִינוֹק, דִּלְקָלָא עָבְדִי לֵיהּ. אֲבָל גָּדוֹל — תַּכְשִׁיט הוּא לֵיהּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ עִינְבָּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This applies only to** the bells of **a small child, since they are placed** on **him to** produce **sound.** If the bell does not make a sound, it serves no purpose and, consequently, cannot become ritually impure. **However,** with regard to **an adult,** the bell **is an ornament for him even though it does not have a clapper.**

### Watch Analogy

This last distinction — that the same object can function differently depending on context, and that its halachic status as a *kli* tracks its actual function — is illuminating in a broader sense. It calls to mind a *teshuvah* of Rav Moshe Feinstein addressing the question of wearing a watch without an *eiruv* on *Shabbat*. A watch is not obviously jewelry, nor is it obviously clothing; one might argue it is simply a convenient method of carrying a time-measuring device on one's wrist — a burden rather than an ornament. Rav Moshe's answer was that a watch may be worn without an *eiruv* only if one would wear it even if it were not working — because only then is it being worn as an ornament or jewelry rather than as a functional time-piece that happens to be worn on the body. The parallel to the bell-without-a-knocker analysis is clear: when the functional element is absent and the object is still worn, it is worn as jewelry; when the function is the entire point, its removal empties the object of its *kli* status.

## Sticky Principle…

### Knocker Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: נִיטְּלוּ עִינְבְּלֵיהֶן עֲדַיִן טוּמְאָתָן עֲלֵיהֶם. לְמַאי חֲזוּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הוֹאִיל שֶׁהַהֶדְיוֹט יָכוֹל לְהַחֲזִירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *Tosefta* that **the Master said:** If **their clappers were removed** after they became ritually impure, **their ritual impurity still** remains **upon them.** The Gemara wonders: **For what** use **are they suited** after their clappers are removed? They are essentially broken and should therefore become ritually pure. **Abaye said:** The reason that their impurity remains is **because a common person is able to replace** the clapper into the bell. According to Abaye, with regard to any vessel that comes apart, if a common person is capable of reassembling it and it does not require the expertise of a craftsman, it is not considered broken and its ritual impurity is not nullified.

Returning to the *baraita*'s statement that removing the knocker does not change the bell's *tumah* status — *nitlu inbeleihen adayin tumatan aleihem* — the Gemara asks: *le-mai chazi?* — to what use can the bell now be put, without its knocker, if it was made for a child or a crib where sound was the whole point? If it serves no function, it should lose its *kli* status. One might also argue that removing the knocker constitutes a *shinui ma'aseh* — a physical change — which, as established, is precisely what is required to exit the category of *tamei* vessels.

Abaye answers: *ho'il ve-hedyot yakhol le-hakhziro* — since any ordinary person can replace the knocker without special skill, the removal is not really a change of the object. It is a trivially reversible act. The knocker is likely removed periodically — when one does not want the bell to sound — and reinserted when desired. Because the object remains what it was, fully restorable by a layman, it retains its status as a *kli* and therefore its susceptibility to *tumah*.

This principle — *hedyot yakhol le-hakhziro* — has broader resonance in the laws of *Shabbat*. It surfaces in discussions about whether reassembling objects on *Shabbat* constitutes forbidden *boneh* or *makeh ba-patish*: if a layman can trivially restore something to its former state, the disassembly may not have genuinely altered the object's status.

## Scissors & Chibbur

### Scissors Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רָבָא: הַזּוּג וְהָעִינְבָּל חִבּוּר. וְכִי תֵּימָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מְחַבַּר — כְּמַאן דִּמְחַבַּר דָּמֵי. וְהָתַנְיָא: מַסְפּוֹרֶת שֶׁל פְּרָקִים וְאִיזְמֵל שֶׁל רָהִיטְנֵי — חִבּוּר לַטּוּמְאָה וְאֵין חִבּוּר לַהַזָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection** to this explanation from that which was taught: The connection between **the bell and the clapper,** this **is a connection.** Therefore, if they are detached from each other, the bell should be considered broken. And he adds: **And if you say that** when employing the term connection, **it is saying as follows: Even though it is not connected, it** has the legal status **as if it were connected. Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The connection between the different parts of **scissors** made **of** different **parts** that are made to come apart **and** the connection between **the blade of a** carpenter's **plane,** which can be removed from its handle, and its handle **are** considered **a connection with regard to** contracting **ritual impurity?** If one part becomes ritually impure, the other parts become ritually impure as well. The *baraita* continues: **However,** they are **not** considered **a connection with regard to** the **sprinkling** of the waters of a purification offering.

Rava challenges Abaye's reasoning by citing the case of the *masporet shel perakim* — scissors assembled from separable components — and the *izel shel rehatni*, the carpenter's plane, which consists of a wooden frame and a metal blade. The rule stated: *chibbur le-tumah, ve-ein chibbur le-haza'ah* — these multi-part tools are considered a single connected unit for the purposes of contracting *tumah* (if one part becomes *tamei*, the other does too), but they are *not* considered a single unit for the purposes of *haza'ah*, the sprinkling of *parah adumah* water (if the water touches one part, the other is not rendered *tahor*).

This seems contradictory. If the two blades of the scissors are one unit, they should be one unit for all purposes — both for contracting *tumah* and for becoming *tahor* through *haza'ah*. If they are separate units, they should be separate for all purposes.

### Raba's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמְרִינַן: מָה נַפְשָׁךְ, אִי חִבּוּר הוּא — אֲפִלּוּ לְהַזָּאָה, וְאִי לָא חִבּוּר הוּא — אֲפִילּוּ לְטוּמְאָה נָמֵי לָא! וְאָמַר רַבָּה: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה — חִבּוּר בֵּין לְטוּמְאָה בֵּין לְהַזָּאָה. שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה — אֵינוֹ חִבּוּר לֹא לְטוּמְאָה וְלֹא לְהַזָּאָה. וְגָזְרוּ עַל טוּמְאָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה מִשּׁוּם טוּמְאָה שֶׁהִיא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה, וְעַל הַזָּאָה שֶׁהִיא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה מִשּׁוּם הַזָּאָה שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Whichever** way **you** look at it, there is a difficulty: **If** it is considered **a connection,** they should be considered connected **even** with regard **to sprinkling;** and **if** they are **not** considered **a connection,** they should **not** be so considered **even** with regard **to ritual impurity.** **And Rabba said: By Torah law, when in use,** they are considered **a connection, both** with regard **to ritual impurity** and with regard **to sprinkling. And when not in use,** even if the parts are connected, since they are made to come apart and they are commonly dismantled, they are **neither** considered **a connection** with regard **to ritual impurity nor** with regard **to sprinkling. And** the Sages **issued a decree** that they should be considered a connection **with regard to ritual impurity** even **when not in use, due to ritual impurity when in use.** If one component becomes ritually impure, the other component becomes ritually impure as well. **And,** as a further stringency, they issued a decree that they should not be considered a connection **with regard to sprinkling** even **when in use, due to sprinkling when not in use.** The waters of purification must be sprinkled on each part individually. Nevertheless, this type of connection with regard to ritual impurity is only relevant when the two parts are actually connected. When the parts are separate, even if they can be reattached easily, the vessel is considered broken.

Rava resolves this. Biblically, the rule is as follows: *bish'at melachah* — when the scissors are actively in use (or in the midst of a sewing session even if not at the precise moment of cutting) — they are considered a single unit for all purposes, both for *tumah* and for *haza'ah*. *Shelo bish'at melachah* — when the work session is over, and indeed standard practice was to disassemble the scissors when putting them away — they are considered two separate units for all purposes, both for *tumah* and for *haza'ah*.

The apparent contradiction arises from rabbinic stringency applied asymmetrically. The rabbis decreed that for *tumah*, the scissors should be treated as a single unit even *shelo bish'at melachah* — even when not in use — because people will not make fine distinctions, and inconsistency in *tumah* rules breeds confusion. Conversely, for *haza'ah*, which renders the vessel *tahor*, the rabbis decreed that the scissors be treated as two separate units even *bish'at melachah* — even when in use — in order to be stringent about ensuring full *taharah*. The result: rabbinically, they are always one unit for *tumah* (easy to contract impurity), and always two units for *haza'ah* (harder to achieve purity). This dual stringency recapitulates, in a different register, the sticky principle: entering *tumah* status is easy, exiting it is hard.

## Rava vs. Abaye

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְהַקִּישׁוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי חֶרֶס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** It should be explained differently: It is considered a vessel **since** a bell without a clapper **is suited to strike on earthenware** and produce a sound of similar quality to that produced by a clapper.

Rava's challenge to Abaye now comes into focus. If, according to the *halacha* of the scissors, even the position of *shelo bish'at melachah* — when the tool is laid aside and the intention is to take it apart — already renders the components separate vessels, then certainly once the components are physically separated, they are unambiguously two distinct objects. Applying this to the bell: once the knocker is removed from the bell, they are certainly two separate things. The bell without the knocker is no longer the same vessel it was. If that is so, removing the knocker is indeed a genuine *shinui ma'aseh*, and Abaye's answer — that *hedyot yakhol le-hakhziro* prevents the status from changing — appears insufficient. The scissors principle seems to say the opposite: once separated, the status does change.

Rava responds with his own answer, displacing Abaye's: *ho'il ve-ra'uy le-hakkisho al gabbei cheres pizi* — since one can still use the bell by banging it against a piece of pottery. A child's favorite instrument, after all, is a pot and a wooden spoon. The bell without its knocker can still be struck against pottery to produce sound. Therefore, Rava argues, the removal of the knocker does not render the bell nonfunctional; it simply changes the mode of use. The bell remains a sound-producing vessel, capable of being used in this alternative fashion. It therefore retains its *kli* status and does not lose its susceptibility to *tumah*. The *hedyot yakhol le-hakhziro* reasoning of Abaye is unnecessary; Rava's reasoning is that the object remains a functional *kli* on its own terms.

### Alternative Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְהַקִּישׁוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי חֶרֶס. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּי לְגַמֵּעַ בּוֹ מַיִם לְתִינוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** **It was also stated** that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** It is considered a vessel, **since** a bell without a clapper **is suited to strike on earthenware. Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is considered a vessel, **since it is suited** for use **to give water to a child** to drink.

This alternative-use reasoning is then corroborated by a parallel *memra*. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Chanina both cite the *ho'il ve-ra'uy le-hakkisho al gabbei cheres* reasoning. But Rabbi Yochanan adds a distinct alternative: *ho'il ve-ra'uy lig-mei bo mayim le-tinok* — one can use the bell as a small scoop or ladle to give water to an infant. The bell without its knocker retains its hollow form and can serve as a vessel for liquid.

### Function Change Q

This sets up a question that is explicitly left unresolved at this juncture. Rabbi Yochanan's alternative-use reasoning raises the possibility that a vessel which has become non-functional in its original capacity but can be repurposed for a different function retains its *kli* status. But the question is not trivial: does a change of function preserve the vessel's status, or does the fact that it is no longer being used for its original purpose constitute a *shinui ma'aseh* sufficient to alter that status? Rava's answer — that the bell can still be banged on pottery — preserves the same *type* of use (sound production). Rabbi Yochanan's answer — that it can serve as a water scoop — involves a genuinely different use. Whether these two are halachically equivalent, and whether change of function preserves or disrupts vessel status, remains to be developed in the continuation of the *sugya*.
