# Hullin 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tumah Beluah

### Swallowed Rings

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion of *tumah beluah* — the principle of absorbed impurity. When something *tamei* is inside a body, whether a person's or an animal's, its *tumah* is effectively suspended. It does not radiate outward. Even a dead fetus inside its mother's womb, which would ordinarily cause *tumah* in an enclosed space, does not do so while it remains within the mother. Conversely, if something *tahor* is inside a body, it remains unaffected even if the person carrying it becomes *tamei*. This principle was established by Rabba, who demonstrated it from various *pesukim* and from an explicit *mishnah* in *mikvaot* involving a ring — *tamei* or *tahor* — that was swallowed. If the principle was already stated in that *mishnah*, one might ask: what was Rabba's *chiddush*?

### Two Rings Case

The *gemara* answers by specifying Rabba's novel case: *kegon shebala shtei tabaot* — someone swallowed two rings sequentially, one *tamei* and one *tahor*. Rabba's innovation is the ruling that *dilo metamaya la mitmea letehora* — the *tamei* ring does not render the *tahor* ring *tamei*, even though they may well be touching inside the stomach. This is a striking *chiddush*: *tumah beluah* does not merely prevent impurity from radiating outward to people on the outside; it means that once something is absorbed within a body, it is as though it does not exist even from the perspective of other objects sharing that same interior space. Even contact occurring entirely inside the body is halakhically inert.

Tosafot raises the natural follow-up: where does Rabba derive this principle if it is not explicit in prior *mishnayot*? Tosafot answers that the source is a case mentioned earlier in which someone who had swallowed a *tamei* object was permitted to eat *terumah*. The implicit concern would be that the *terumah*, upon being swallowed, would enter the stomach and contact the *tamei* object there. That this is not treated as a problem demonstrates that even within the body, a *tamei* object does not render a *tahor* one *tamei*. Once absorbed in a body, the *tumah* does not exist even from the inside.

### Fetus-Midwife Problem

This principle, however, appears to conflict directly with an established *mishnah*. That *mishnah* discusses a dead fetus inside a woman's womb: the mother remains *tehorah* because the fetus is *beluah*, but the midwife who inserts her hand and touches the fetus becomes *temei'ah*. The midwife's hand inside the womb is in precisely the same situation as the second ring inside the stomach — both are in the same interior space as the *tamei* object. Yet in the midwife's case, *tumah* is transferred. The *gemara* at the top of 72a formulates the contradiction directly: *keshtei tabaot dami* — the fetus and midwife case is analogous to two rings in a stomach — *vekamitamela ubar lachaya*, and yet the fetus does render the midwife *temei'ah*.

The *gemara* initially suggests that the fetus is different because *sofo latzeis* — it is destined to emerge. But this cannot be the distinction, since a swallowed ring is equally destined to exit the body by a different route. The *gemara* acknowledges this and abandons that line of reasoning.

### Rav Yosef Resolution

The *gemara* then says that the explanation is known to the Pumpeditans — *umanhu Rav Yosef* — and it is Rav Yosef. In the chain of transmission Rav Yosef amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, the ruling is stated: *tumah zu einah mi-divrei Torah ella mi-divrei Sofrim* — the *tumah* of the midwife is only rabbinic. This resolves the contradiction entirely. Rabba is correct that *tumah beluah* suspends *tumah* even for things sharing the same interior space, including the midwife's hand inside the womb. The reason the midwife is *temei'ah* in that *mishnah* is not because of a biblical principle, but because the rabbis enacted a special *gezeirah*.

The *gemara* notes that the formulation "it is not from *divrei Torah* but from *divrei Sofrim*" is doing more than simply declaring the ruling rabbinic. It is preemptively addressing the position of Rabbi Akiva — whom we will encounter shortly — who holds that a dead fetus inside its mother *does* cause *tumah* biblically. One might have thought that our *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Akiva's view. Rav Yosef's statement is therefore saying: even according to Rabbi Yishmael, who holds that the fetus is *tahor* while still in the womb, the rabbis nonetheless imposed *tumah* on the midwife.

### Midwife Stricture Logic

The *gemara* then asks: *mai ta'ama* — what is the reason for this rabbinic *gezeirah*? Rav Hoshaya explains: *gezeirah shema yotzi pevah rosho chutz l'prozdor* — it is a precautionary decree lest the fetus's head emerge beyond the *prozdor*, the birth canal, and the midwife fail to notice. Once the head emerges, the fetus is no longer *beluah* and causes *tumah* biblically. Since the midwife is in the process of delivering the baby and may not notice the precise moment of emergence, the rabbis ruled that from the moment she is handling it inside the womb, she is already considered *temei'ah*.

The *gemara* then asks: if so, why not also impose this *gezeirah* on the mother herself? The answer is *margeshes b'atzmah* — the mother will sense when the baby has emerged. The *gemara* then asks: but could the mother not simply inform the midwife? The answer is *trida* — she is preoccupied and cannot reliably be expected to communicate the precise moment of emergence. Hence the rabbinic stringency applies specifically to the midwife.

The upshot of all this is striking: the principle of *tumah beluah* applies even when two objects are in the same interior space, such as two rings in a stomach or a midwife's hand touching a fetus in the womb. The midwife's *tumah* is an exceptional rabbinic enactment born of a specific practical concern about the moment of emergence, not a reflection of any inherent limitation of *tumah beluah* itself.

## Rabbi Akiva vs. Yishmael

### Biblical Sources

The *gemara* now spells out the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael regarding whether a dead fetus inside its mother causes *tumah* biblically. A *baraita* is cited. The verse in *parashat chukat* states: *vechol asher yiga al p'nei hasadeh* — anyone who touches something on the open field, whether someone slain by the sword or a dead body, is *tamei* for seven days.

### Rabbi Akiva Position

Rabbi Akiva reads the phrase *hanogei bemeit benefesh* — one who touches a dead body *be-nefesh* — as referring to a dead body *within* another person, i.e., a fetus inside its mother. The word *benefesh* is read not as a qualifier of the corpse but as indicating the location: the dead body is inside another soul. *eizo meit shebenefesh shel adam* — what is a dead body inside a person? *hevei omer zeh ubar she-be-mei isha* — this is the fetus in the mother's womb. According to Rabbi Akiva, touching such a fetus causes *tumah* biblically.

### Rabbi Yishmael Position

Rabbi Yishmael reads the same phrase differently. The verse's phrase *al p'nei hasadeh* — on the open field — indicates that *tumah* applies only to what is out in the world and exposed. This *lehotzi* — comes to exclude — a fetus inside the mother's womb. Since it is not in the open field, it does not cause *tumah*. As for *hanogei bemeit benefesh*, Rabbi Yishmael does not use this phrase to teach the fetus case; he uses it instead to derive that even a *revi'it* of blood from a corpse causes *tumah* — *dam hu hanefesh*, blood is the soul, and so a quantity of blood representing a *nefesh* carries the same *tumah* as the body itself. The amount of a *revi'it* was understood by the rabbis to represent the quantity of blood initially present in a newborn, and hence the threshold of blood upon which life depends.

Rabbi Akiva, for his part, derives the rule about a *revi'it* of blood from a different verse — *ve'al kol nafshos meis lo yavo* — which uses the plural *nafshos*, teaching that even a *revi'it* of blood combined from two separate corpses causes *tumah*. Rabbi Yishmael disagrees with that position as well, holding that *benefesh adam* in the singular means the blood must derive from a single corpse.

### Tosafot on Stages

Does Rabbi Akiva then reject *tumah beluah* entirely? Tosafot offers the most cogent resolution. There are distinct stages to consider. When the fetus is in the uterus, it is genuinely *beluah* — absorbed deeply enough within the body that it does not exist for *tumah* purposes even vis-à-vis the mother and even for the midwife touching it (absent the rabbinic *gezeirah*). But once the fetus begins descending through the birth canal, it is no longer *beluah* — it is no longer hidden in the deep interior of the body — while it is also not yet *born*, since birth occurs only when the head emerges. This intermediate stage — in the birth canal but not yet born — is the point of contention.

*Beit hastarim* refers to a surface or space that is hidden but not necessarily *beluah* in the strict sense. Something inside the mouth is *beit hastarim* but not *beluah*; one could open the mouth and expose it. *beluah* requires a deeper level of absorption, inaccessible without intervention.

### Debate Summary

According to Tosafot's resolution, Rabbi Akiva holds that once the fetus is in the birth canal, it is no longer *beluah*, and since it is dead, it causes *tumah*. The verse does not need to exclude it because it is no longer absorbed in the sense that triggers *tumah beluah*. Rabbi Yishmael, however, holds that there is a special rule regarding a fetus: as long as it has not yet been born — head not yet emerged — it does not cause *tumah*, even when it is in the birth canal and thus no longer strictly *beluah*. The *chiddush* is thus Rabbi Yishmael's, not Rabbi Akiva's. Rabbi Akiva follows the ordinary logic that once something is no longer *beluah* it can cause *tumah*; Rabbi Yishmael introduces a special exclusion applying to the fetus specifically.

Our *mishnah*'s ruling that the mother is *tehorah* until birth follows Rabbi Yishmael. And the three stages are: in the uterus (*beluah* — *tahor* according to everyone); in the birth canal (debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael); emerged (*tamei* according to everyone).

## Protruding Limb Case

### Limb Cut Before

The *gemara* now turns to a case discussed in the *mishnah*: *behema hamaksha leyeled* — an animal experiencing a difficult labor — and the fetus extends a limb out of the womb. Someone cuts off this limb in order to assist the delivery. What is the status of the limb and the mother's meat?

If the limb was cut off and afterwards the mother was slaughtered — *vehotzi ubar yado, vechatacha, ve'achar kach shachat et imo* — the meat of the mother is *tahor*. The limb itself is *ever min hachai*, a limb severed from a living animal, which is forbidden. However, since it was severed while the mother was alive, it did not confer *tumah* on the mother (living animals do not become *tamei*), and the subsequent *shechita* of the mother renders all her meat *tahor*. The limb had no effect on the mother's status.

### Limb Cut After

The more complex case is when the mother was slaughtered first and the limb was cut off afterwards — *shachat et imo ve'achar kach chatacha*. The *shechita* of the mother was effective for the fetus inside but not for the protruding limb. The limb was outside, beyond the scope of the *shechita*. What is its status?

Two categories present themselves. A *neveila* is an animal that never had a *shechita* performed, or died without *shechita*; it causes *tumah*. A *tereifa* is an animal whose *shechita* was performed but was not halakhically effective to render it fit for eating; it does not cause *tumah*. The question is which of these two categories applies to this limb. Was there *shechita* for the limb — in which case it is like a *tereifa*, no *tumah* — or was there no *shechita* for it at all, making it a *neveila* that does cause *tumah*?

### Rabbi Meir Debate

Rabbi Meir rules: *maga neveila* — the meat is treated as having touched a *neveila*, meaning the limb is a *neveila* and causes *tumah*. The *chachamim* rule: *maga tereifa shechuta* — it is treated as having touched a slaughtered *tereifa*, which does not cause *tumah*.

The *chachamim* reason by analogy: *ma matzinu betereifa sheshechitatah metaheratah* — just as we find that a *tereifa* animal's *shechita* prevents it from becoming a *neveila*, so too the *shechita* of the mother, even though it did not make the limb permissible, is sufficient to prevent the limb from carrying the status of *neveila*. An act of *shechita* was performed; it simply was not effective for the limb.

### Meir's Argument

Rabbi Meir responds: the comparison does not hold. When a *tereifa* animal is slaughtered and is thereby prevented from becoming a *neveila*, that is because *davar she'be'gufah* — the *shechita* was performed on its very body. One may say an act of *shechita* was done to this animal. But to say that the mother's *shechita* should purify the limb — *davar she'eina gufah* — something that is not part of the mother's body? That is a different matter entirely.

Rabbi Meir's argument is grounded in the principle of *yesh leida l'eivarim* — the limb that has protruded is treated as if it has already been born, as if it has already left the mother. Once it is regarded as no longer part of the mother, the mother's *shechita* has no relevance to it, and since no *shechita* ever applied to the limb, it is a *neveila*. The *chachamim* presumably disagree with one of two premises: either they hold that the limb is still considered *gufah* — part of the mother's body — or they concede it is not *gufah* but maintain that its physical attachment and dependence on the mother is sufficient for the mother's *shechita* to relate to it. The *gemara* does not fully articulate which of these two positions the *chachamim* are taking.

## Tereifa Shechita

### Mishnah Logic

The *mishnah* now uses the preceding debate as an opportunity to establish on what basis a *tereifa*'s *shechita* prevents it from acquiring *neveila* status — a principle everyone in the preceding argument accepted. The *mishnah* poses the question in the form of a logical challenge: *behema tmei'a asura ba'achila* — a non-kosher animal may not be eaten; *af treifa asura ba'achila* — a *tereifa* may not be eaten. If we say about a non-kosher animal that its *shechita* does not make it *tahor* — it still causes *tumah* as a *neveila* — perhaps we should say the same about a *tereifa*.

### Non-Kosher vs. Tereifa

The *mishnah* responds: *im amarta be-behema tmeah she-lo hayta lah sha'at ha-kosher* — a non-kosher animal was never in the category of something that could be eaten. The concept of *shechita* is simply irrelevant to it; the entire institution of *shechita* exists to render kosher animals permissible, and it has no meaning for a species that cannot be eaten under any circumstances. A *tereifa*, by contrast, began life as an animal that could in principle have been eaten. *she-hayta lah sha'at ha-kosher* — it had a time when it was fit. Since *shechita* is a meaningful and operative act for its species, and the *shechita* was performed, that is sufficient to remove it from the category of *neveila*.

### Born Tereifa

The *mishnah* then presses further: what if an animal was born with a defect that rendered it *tereifa* from the very moment of birth? It never had a *sha'at ha-kosher*. Should it not be treated like a non-kosher animal? The *mishnah* answers: *she-ken ein be-minah shechita* — for a non-kosher species, *shechita* has no relevance whatsoever to any member of that species. For a kosher animal, even one born as a *tereifa*, *shechita* remains a conceptually meaningful act for its *min*. Because *shechita* is meaningful for the species, the performance of *shechita* is sufficient to prevent even a *tereifa* born as such from acquiring *neveila* status. The *mishnah* acknowledges this is a basis for distinction rather than a proof, but on that basis concludes that *shechita* of a *tereifa* prevents *tumah* regardless of whether it was born *tereifa* or became one later.

### Eighth-Month Animal

The *mishnah* concludes with an exceptional case: *ben shmonah chai* — an animal born in the eighth month of gestation. The rabbis understood such an animal to be nonviable, effectively stillborn even if it appears alive. Because it is considered as though already dead, *shechita* has no effect on it — *ein shechitatu metaharatu, lefi she-ein be-mino shechita*. The *mishnah*'s formulation — that there is no *shechita* in its *min* — is somewhat imprecise, since it is still a cow and the species is certainly subject to *shechita*. The more accurate understanding, as the *gemara* implies, is that *shechita* has no meaning when performed on something already effectively dead. Just as *shechita* on an animal that has already died accomplishes nothing, so too with an eighth-month animal whose viability has not been established: the act of *shechita* is halakhically inert, and such an animal carries the status of *neveila*.

## Hidden Surface Impurity

### Hidden Contact Problem

The *gemara* now raises a sharp difficulty within Rabbi Meir's position. If the protruding limb is a *neveila* and the fetus is *tamei* by virtue of touching it, the question is: where exactly are they touching? The limb is attached to the fetus. There is no exposed surface at the point of attachment — it is all one continuous body. The surfaces that are in contact are hidden, interior surfaces — *beit hastarim*. And the principle is that *negiyat beit hastarim* — contact at a hidden surface — does not transfer *tumah*. The act of touching must occur at an exposed surface. How then does the *neveila* limb render the fetus *tamei*?

### Self-Touching Theory

The *gemara* suggests that perhaps Rabbi Meir's ruling is consistent with a position he holds elsewhere — that an object can be considered to be touching itself.

### Splitting Seat Case

The *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: a cloth that is *shlosha tefachim al shlosha tefachim* in size — large enough to serve as a seat — is *tamei* as *midras* if a *zav* sat upon it, since *mishkav u'moshav* makes the seat an *av hatumah*. If this cloth is then torn in half, each half is no longer large enough to be a seat and loses its *midras* status. The question is whether the remnant cloth retains some *tumah* through having "touched" the *midras* as it was being torn.

Rabbi Meir rules: *tahor min hamidras, aval tamei maga midras* — it is no longer *tamei* as a *midras*, but it has the *tumah* of something that touched a *midras*, one degree removed. Rabbi Yose challenges this: *vechi be'eizeh midras naga zeh* — what *midras* did this piece of cloth touch? It did not touch a *midras*; it *was* a *midras*. The only way to say it touched a *midras* is to say it touched itself — that one half of the cloth is considered to have touched the other half. Rabbi Yose rejects this and says: if a *zav* had both touched the cloth and sat upon it, then upon tearing it loses its *midras* status but retains the *tumah* of having touched a *zav* — that *tumah* survives the splitting. But if the cloth only ever had *midras* status and was never *tamei* through contact, tearing it in half simply ends its *tumah* entirely.

So Rabbi Meir holds that something can be considered to be touching itself — two parts of a single object are treated as touching one another — while Rabbi Yose does not. If Rabbi Meir's view governs our case of the limb and fetus, then even the hidden, interior contact between the attached limb and fetus can transfer *tumah*, resolving the *beit hastarim* problem.

### Ulla's Distinction

The *gemara* does not settle for this resolution, because Ulla draws a distinction within Rabbi Yose's own view. Ulla says: *lo shanu ela shlosha al shlosha shenechlak* — the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yose applies only to the case where a cloth that was exactly the minimum size for a seat was torn in half, so that the seat ceased to exist in the process. In that scenario, Rabbi Yose says: the original *midras* went out of existence as you tore it, so there is no ongoing *midras* to be the source of transferred *tumah*.

But, says Ulla, *aval shlosha al shlosha haba mibeged gadol* — if you tear a piece of three-by-three *etzbaot* (large enough to be *tamei* but not large enough to be a seat) off of a large cloth that itself remains a full *midras*, then *besha'at perishatan me'avihen mekablot tumah me'avihen* — as the piece separates from its source, it receives *tumah* from its source. In this case, even Rabbi Yose would agree that the torn-off piece is *tamei*, because the original *midras* still exists and is still a source of *tumah* at the very moment of separation.

And this, says Ulla, is precisely the case with the fetus and the protruding limb. When the limb is cut from the fetus, the fetus itself continues to exist and continues to be subject to *tumah*. It does not go out of existence in the cutting. Therefore, as the limb is being separated, the fetus receives *tumah* from the limb — *besha'at perisha me'eiver mekabel tumah me'eiver* — even according to Rabbi Yose. The *mishnah*'s ruling is thus not dependent on Rabbi Meir's unique principle of self-touching at all.

Rashi explains this mechanism as not requiring that contact occurred while the limb was wholly attached, which would be *beit hastarim*. Rather, as the limb is physically cut away, there will necessarily be a moment where the cut surface of the limb comes into contact with the exposed surface of the fetus — a fleeting but genuine surface contact at an exposed place. It is that moment of contact, during the process of separation, that transfers the *tumah*.

### Ravina's Approach

Ravina offers an independent resolution. He distinguishes between a cloth and a protruding limb with respect to the very concept of self-touching. *beged lav lechaticha kai* — a cloth is not in a state of readiness to be cut. Even if you have a large cloth, there is nothing about the cloth itself that tells you to cut it; you see it as one integral unit. The notion that one part of the cloth is "touching" another part is therefore conceptually strained.

*Ubar lechaticha kai* — the protruding limb of a fetus, by contrast, stands to be cut. The limb cannot be eaten while the fetus can; it is already recognized as something that must be removed. Because it is already conceived of as something that will necessarily be severed, we treat it *halakhically* as though it has already been cut: *kol ha-omed lachtokh ke-khatukh dami* — anything that is destined to be cut off is considered as already cut off. Once it is conceptualized as already cut off, its surface is considered exposed, and the contact between the limb and the fetus is no longer *negiyat beit hastarim* but ordinary surface contact between two adjacent objects. The *tumah* is therefore transferred directly, without need for the moment of physical separation. The *gemara* will continue with this principle on the following daf.
