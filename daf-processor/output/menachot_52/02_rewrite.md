# Menachot 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Par He'elam Davar

### Funding Debate

Today's daf is Menachot 52, and with it we complete the *perek*. We pick up five lines from the top, beginning with a *tanu rabbanan*. The preceding discussion has centered on the debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda regarding the *minchat chavitin* of the *kohen gadol* when no *kohen gadol* exists — specifically, whether the *mincha* is funded by the heirs or by communal funds. The Gemara now extends this general question to a related set of communal sacrifices.

*Tanu rabbanan*: the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* and the *seirei avodah zarah*. The *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* is the communal *chatas* brought when the *Beis Din HaGadol* ruled incorrectly and the majority of *Am Yisrael* followed that ruling — the subject of Masechet Horayos. The *seirei avodah zarah* refers to the case where the community collectively sinned in the realm of *avodah zarah*. The question is: from where does the funding for these communal sacrifices come?

Rabbi Yehuda says *b'techilah magbin lahen* — one conducts a special collection for them. The reasoning is intuitive: the entire *tzibur* sinned, and there is a certain appropriateness in having each member consciously contribute, so that the community is not entirely oblivious to the *korban* being brought on their behalf. Rabbi Shimon says *mitrumas halishkah hein ba'in* — they come from the regular communal funds drawn upon each year.

### Conflicting Braitot

The Gemara immediately raises a complication: *ha'tanya ipcha* — there is a *braisa* that records Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon holding the reverse positions. Rather than simply declaring one *braisa* erroneous, the Gemara attempts to determine which version reflects the later, authoritative ruling. *Hai mineihu achrita?* — which one is the reliable final position?

### Rav Ashi's Resolution

The rabbis before Rav Ashi suggested that the first version we cited — where Rabbi Shimon says the sacrifices come from communal funds — should be regarded as the authoritative latter position. Their reasoning: *d'shameinan lei l'Rabbi Shimon d'chayish l'pshia* — we know that Rabbi Shimon is concerned about negligence. As discussed on the preceding daf, Rabbi Shimon observed that the heirs were neglecting the *kohen gadol*'s *mincha* and therefore moved it back to the *de'oraisa* arrangement of communal funding. That demonstrated concern for negligence aligns well with his position here, where he insists on drawing from existing communal funds rather than relying on a new ad hoc collection that people might fail to contribute to adequately.

Rav Ashi, however, responds that one can even say the second *braisa* is the authoritative one — meaning that in the context of the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur*, Rabbi Shimon would actually be comfortable with a special collection. Why the distinction? *Ki kachayish Rabbi Shimon l'pshia b'milsa d'leisa beh kapara b'gavah* — Rabbi Shimon is concerned about negligence only in a case where there is no personal atonement at stake for the parties responsible for funding. The heirs of a deceased *kohen gadol* bear an obligation but derive no personal atonement from fulfilling it; left to their own devices, they may shirk it. But *b'milsa d'is beh kapara b'gavah* — in a case like the *par he'elam davar* or *seirei avodah zarah*, where the entire community that sinned must contribute toward their own atonement, the personal stake involved means Rabbi Shimon is not concerned they will be negligent. This distinction also has the character of a one-time event rather than a recurring obligation, which further reduces the concern.

### Textual Proof

The Gemara then asks *mai havah alah* — what is the conclusion? Rav Zuta said to Rav Ashi: come and hear. The verse concerning the *tamid* reads: *es korbani lachmi l'ishai rei'ach nichochi tishmeru l'hakriv li b'mo'ado* — "My offering, My bread for My fire-offering, a pleasing aroma, you shall be careful to bring to Me in its appointed time." The extra language *es lachmi l'ishai* comes to include the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* and the *seirei avodah zarah*, indicating that just as the *tamid* comes from *trumas halishkah*, so too do these sacrifices — and this is the position of Rabbi Shimon.

This establishes consistency: Rabbi Shimon in our *Mishnah* rules that the *minchat chavitin* of the *kohen gadol* comes from communal funds when no *kohen gadol* has been appointed, and here too, Rabbi Shimon derives from a *pasuk* that the *par he'elam davar* and *seirei avodah zarah* come from the same communal funds. It may be that this ruling has nothing to do with the concern for negligence at all and is simply *pasuk*-based — which would explain why the *braisa* presenting this as Rabbi Shimon's authoritative position should be preferred. The conclusion is thus that it is Rabbi Yehuda who requires a special collection for these sacrifices, while Rabbi Shimon holds they are funded from the regular *trumas halishkah*.

## Mincha When No Kohen Gad…

### Two Halves or Whole

Returning to our *Mishnah*, which established that when there is no *kohen gadol* the *mincha* is brought as a complete, undivided unit (*ushlema tikrav*), Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that this raises an obvious question: *shlema shacharis ushlema bein ha'arbaim o dilma shlema shacharis u-vetela bein ha'arbaim* — are you bringing a full *issaron* in the morning and a full *issaron* in the afternoon, or are you bringing one full *issaron* only in the morning and nothing in the afternoon? Under normal circumstances, one *issaron* is divided into two halves, one offered each time. The question is whether, when the circumstance changes and the offering must be complete and undivided, you bring two complete offerings — one at each of the two daily intervals — or consolidate into a single complete offering in the morning alone.

### Rava's Proof

Rava proposed a proof. The *Mishnah* describing the division of labor among the *kohanim* in the daily *avodah* states that the eighth *kohen* brings the afternoon *chavitin* of the *kohen gadol*. This *chavitin* was brought between the components of the afternoon *tamid*. Now, Rava reasons: *v'i issa de-vatela bein ha'arbaim* — if it were ever the case that there is no afternoon *chavitin* to bring, such as when there is no *kohen gadol* and the morning offering is the only one, then *hazimnin d'lo mashkachas la shmini b'chavitin* — sometimes the *Mishnah*'s designation of "the eighth brings the *chavitin*" would be inapplicable. The *Mishnah* assumes there will always be afternoon *chavitin*, which implies that even without a *kohen gadol*, a full *issaron* is brought in the morning and a full *issaron* in the afternoon.

### Standard vs. Aberration

The rabbis reported Rava's argument to Rabbi Yirmiyah in Eretz Yisrael. His reaction was sharp: *Bavla'ei tipshai* — "Those foolish Babylonians, because they dwell in a dark land they say dark ideas." The proof, he insisted, proves nothing. The *Mishnah* lists that the seventh *kohen* brings the *mincha* of the afternoon *tamid*, the eighth brings the *chavitin* of the *kohen gadol*, and the ninth brings the wine of the afternoon *tamid*. Does that mean those offerings can never fail to appear? We know explicitly that *minchasam v'niskaihem balaila minchasam v'niskaihem afilu l'machar* — the *menachos* and *nesachim* of a *korban* remain valid if brought that night or even the following day, meaning that on some days the afternoon *tamid* might not have its *mincha* and wine accompanying it in real time. The fact that the *Mishnah* assigns these tasks to specific *kohanim* does not mean it is impossible for them not to occur. *Ela de'i lo katani* — the *Mishnah* is teaching standard operating procedure. It does not trouble itself with exceptional, aberrant scenarios. Therefore, the fact that the *Mishnah* assumes afternoon *chavitin* tells us nothing about what happens in the anomalous case where there is no *kohen gadol*.

The rabbis returned to Rava and relayed Rabbi Yirmiyah's objection, including his caustic remark. Rava responded with characteristic sharpness: *mibishutan amri kamaihu mitivutan lo amri kamaihu* — "They report my weak ideas before him but do not report my good ones." The bad news travels; the brilliant teachings of Bavel go unreported, leaving Rabbi Yirmiyah with a distorted impression. But Rava then said *hanei nami tivutan havu* — "You know what? Even this position is one of my good teachings." The conclusion he drew, he maintains, is correct. He simply acknowledges that the proof from the *Mishnah* was not valid — and Rabbi Yirmiyah was right to dismiss it.

### Tamid Proof

Rava now offered a different proof entirely. The verse concerning the *chavitin* reads *solet mincha tamid* — "flour, a *mincha*, *tamid*." This tells us the *chavitin* is to be treated like the *minchat tmidim*, the *mincha* brought with the daily *tamid*. Just as the *tamid* is always brought once in the morning and once in the afternoon, so too the *chavitin* must always have a morning component and an afternoon component. Consequently, when there is no *kohen gadol* and the offering must be brought whole, you bring a full *issaron* in the morning and a full *issaron* in the afternoon, mirroring the structure of the *tamid* — half-and-half when a *kohen gadol* is present, full-and-full when none exists.

### Baraita Resolution

The Gemara asks *mai havya alah* — what is the final ruling? Rav Natan bar Yitzchak says: come and hear — there is a *braisa*: *shlema shacharis u'shlema bein ha'arbayim*, a full *issaron* in the morning and a full *issaron* in the afternoon. This resolves the question in accordance with Rava's position.

## Levonah Debate

### Two Full Handfuls

Having established that when there is no *kohen gadol* a full *issaron* is brought morning and afternoon, the Gemara steps back to examine certain details of the *minchat chavitin* as it is normally brought when a *kohen gadol* is present. Rabbi Yochanan states that a dispute between Abba Yossi ben Dostai and the rabbis addresses the following question. The standard *minchat chavitin* consists of one *issaron* divided into two halves, offered morning and afternoon. Each portion of a *mincha* normally requires *levonah* — frankincense — in the amount of a *kometz* (a handful), with the exception of the *minchat chote*. How much *levonah* is brought for the *chavitin*?

Abba Yossi ben Dostai says *mafrish lah shnei kmatzim* — two full handfuls of *levonah*, one in the morning and one in the afternoon: *kometz shacharis v'kometz bein ha'arbayim*. Even though the morning and afternoon portions each represent only half an *issaron*, each receives a complete *kometz* of *levonah*.

### One Handful Divided

The rabbis disagree: *mafrish lah kometz echad* — only one *kometz* of *levonah* in total, divided in half: *chatzi kometz shacharis v'chatzi kometz bein ha'arbayim*. Just as the single *issaron* is split into two offerings, so too the single *kometz* of *levonah* is split between morning and afternoon.

### Never a Half-Handful

What is the root of this disagreement? Abba Yossi ben Dostai reasons: *lo ashkechan chatzi kometz d'kariv* — we have never found a precedent for offering a half-handful of *levonah* on the *mizbeach*. Yes, it is already a *chiddush* to offer half an *issaron* of flour as a distinct *mincha*. But we should not extend one *chiddush* further than necessary. If *levonah* is to be offered at all, it must be brought in the normal minimum unit — a full *kometz*. Accordingly, each half-offering receives a full *kometz*.

The rabbis counter: *lo ashkechan issaron d'va'ei shnei kmatzin* — we have never found that a single *issaron* of a *mincha* requires two *kmatzim* of *levonah*. The natural proportion is one *kometz* per *issaron*. Since the offering is one *issaron* divided into two, the *levonah* follows the same division. That reasoning strikes most as more internally consistent — but the debate stands.

## Doubling Proportions

### Doubling Levonah?

Rabbi Yochanan now asks a derivative question that flows from the positions just articulated. In the scenario where the *kohen gadol* has died and no substitute has been appointed, we have established that a full *issaron* is brought in the morning and a full *issaron* in the afternoon. The question is posed specifically for the rabbis, whose position in the normal case is a *chatzi kometz* of *levonah* morning and afternoon. Now that the flour has doubled, does the *levonah* double as well?

The question can be framed two ways. On one hand: *mitoch shehuchpela solto huchpela levonato* — since the flour has been doubled, the *levonah* should be doubled proportionally, which would bring us to a full *kometz* per offering. This is appealing both because it maintains consistent proportions and because a full *kometz* is the natural, standard quantity. On the other hand: *mai degali gali mai delo gali lo gali* — the Torah introduced a *chiddush* specifically regarding the flour, doubling it in this circumstance. That *chiddush* is confined to what was explicitly stated. The *levonah* was not mentioned; it therefore remains as it was, a *chatzi kometz* each time, even though the flour has now doubled.

The question is not asked regarding Abba Yossi ben Dostai, for whom the *levonah* is already a full *kometz* in the normal case. If the flour doubles, one might think to also double the *levonah* and produce two *kmatzim* per session, but that would yield the highly unusual quantity of two full handfuls of *levonah* per offering — a sufficiently strange outcome that we might simply say for Abba Yossi ben Dostai the *levonah* remains stable at one *kometz* per half. But the question for the rabbis is genuine.

### Doubling Oil?

A second question follows. Regardless of which position one holds regarding *levonah*, there is a separate question about the oil. When the *kohen gadol* was present, the offering consisted of one *issaron* of flour with three *lugin* of oil, divided in half: one and a half *lugin* with the morning half and one and a half *lugin* with the afternoon half. This preserves a ratio of one *issaron* to three *lugin*. Now that there is no *kohen gadol* and a full *issaron* is offered each session, does the oil also double to three *lugin* per session — maintaining the ratio — or does it remain at one and a half *lugin* per session even though the flour has doubled?

This question applies to both Abba Yossi ben Dostai and the rabbis alike. Their debate concerned the *levonah*; neither of them addressed the oil in their dispute. Yet the same structural tension applies: do we maintain proportionality and double the oil, or do we treat the doubling of flour as a discrete *chiddush* that does not extend to the accompanying amounts?

### Five Handfuls Proof

Rava attempts a proof. A *Mishnah* later in the tractate teaches that there are five instances of a *kometz* in the *Beis Hamikdash* — the *kometz* of *levonah* from a *mincha*, the *kometz* of a *mincha* itself, and so on. Notably absent from that enumeration is the *kometz* of *levonah* accompanying the *chavitin* of the *kohen gadol*. In a standard situation, the *levonah* of the *chavitin* comes in halves — *chatzi kometz* each time — and the *Mishnah* speaks only of whole *kmatzim*, so the normal *chavitin* is naturally excluded. But, Rava argues, *v'i isa* — if it were true that when there is no *kohen gadol* you bring a full *kometz* of *levonah* in the morning and a full *kometz* in the afternoon according to the rabbis, then *zimnin d'mashkachat lei shiva* — sometimes you would have seven *kmatzim* (the standard five, plus two for the *chavitin* morning and afternoon). The fact that the *Mishnah* never mentions seven *kmatzim* indicates that even when there is no *kohen gadol* and the flour is doubled, the *levonah* is not doubled — it remains at *chatzi kometz* morning and afternoon in accordance with the rabbis' position.

### Aberration Counter

Rav Pappa sat and contemplated this argument. Rav Yosef bar Shmaya raised an objection: *veha ma'aleh kometz bachutz* — the *Mishnah* listing five *kmatzim* also includes the case of one who takes a *kometz* of a *mincha* and offers it *outside* the *Beis Hamikdash*, making him liable for *ha'alas chutz*. That is manifestly not standard operating procedure — it is an aberrant, prohibited act. Yet the *Mishnah* includes it in its enumeration. This would suggest that the *Mishnah* is not limited to normal cases; it catalogues all instances of a *kometz*, including anomalous ones. If so, the fact that seven *kmatzim* are never mentioned cannot be used to prove that the doubled *chavitin* does not yield two additional full *kmatzim* — the *Mishnah* would have included even that aberrant case if it were applicable.

This objection is compelling, and the Gemara does not reject it outright. The matter therefore remains unresolved through that proof alone.

## Oil/Levonah Stability

### Baraita Teaching

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves the entire cluster of questions by citing a *braisa*. The *braisa* states regarding a *kohen gadol* who died without a successor: *sheleima shacharis v'sheleima bein ha'arbayim* — a full measure of flour in the morning and a full measure in the afternoon, as already established. *U-mafrisha bei kmatzim* — two *kmatzim* of *levonah*, a *kometz* in the morning and a *kometz* in the afternoon. *U-mafrisha shlosha lugin* — and three *lugin* of oil in total, *log vachetzi shacharis v'log vachetzi bein ha'arbayim* — one and a half in the morning and one and a half in the afternoon.

The picture is now clear: the flour doubles (a full *issaron* each session), two *kmatzim* of *levonah* are brought (one per session), and the oil remains at one and a half *lugin* per session rather than doubling to three.

### Abba Yossi Ben Dostai

The Gemara asks: *mani* — whose position does this *braisa* follow? If we say it reflects the opinion of the rabbis, a problem immediately arises. The rabbis hold that in a normal case the *levonah* is *chatzi kometz* each time. But this *braisa* says two full *kmatzim* are brought when there is no *kohen gadol*. That would mean the *levonah* doubled while the oil did not — *mai shna levonah d'huchpela mai shna shemen d'lo huchpela*. Why would we double the *levonah* to restore it to a full *kometz*, when we would not double the oil to restore it to three *lugin*? A log and a half is not a standard meaningful measurement in the same way a full *kometz* is. There is no coherent basis for treating *levonah* and oil differently in this respect. Therefore the *braisa* cannot reflect the rabbis' position.

Rather, the *braisa* must follow Abba Yossi ben Dostai, who holds that even in a normal situation the *chavitin* requires a full *kometz* of *levonah* morning and afternoon. For Abba Yossi ben Dostai, the two *kmatzim* of *levonah* in this *braisa* are not a doubling — they are exactly what would have been brought even when the *kohen gadol* was alive. The *levonah* is stable, not doubled. And similarly, the oil is stable at one and a half *lugin* per session.

### Parallel Inference

This analysis yields an important inference for the rabbis as well. *Hash'ta d'amar Abba Yossi ben Dostai v'levonah lo huchpala v'shemen lo huchpal* — since we see that for Abba Yossi ben Dostai, when the flour doubles, both the *levonah* and the oil remain stable, we can conclude that the same principle applies for the rabbis: *levonah v'shmanah l'rabbanan nami lo huchpalu* — both the *levonah* and the oil do not increase. The only question between Abba Yossi ben Dostai and the rabbis is the baseline amount of *levonah* brought in a normal situation with a living *kohen gadol*. But the structural rule — that doubling the flour does not lead to doubling the *levonah* or the oil — is common ground. For the rabbis, therefore, when there is no *kohen gadol*, the *levonah* remains *chatzi kometz* each session and the oil remains one and a half *lugin* each session.

### Final Ruling

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi ben Dustai* — the *halacha* follows Abba Yossi ben Dostai that a full *kometz* of *levonah* is brought each session. Tosafot notes the obvious question: *teima hilchata lemashiach?* — are we rendering a practical *halachic* ruling for the era of *Mashiach*, when the Temple will be restored? The question of the *chavitin* is an entirely theoretical exercise in our current reality. Yet the Gemara states the ruling, and Tosafot flags the peculiarity of doing so.

### Objection/Resolution

A further difficulty is raised: *u'mi amar Rabbi Yochanan hachi?* — did Rabbi Yochanan really rule this way? Rabbi Yochanan himself is known for the principle that *halacha k'stam Mishna* — we rule in accordance with an unattributed *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* listing five *kmatzim* — which, as discussed, must reflect the position of the rabbis rather than Abba Yossi ben Dostai, since for him the *chavitin* would generate more than five — is an unattributed *Mishnah*. How then can Rabbi Yochanan rule against it?

The Gemara resolves this with the observation that *Amoraim ninhu aliba d'Rabbi Yochanan* — there is in fact a dispute among *Amoraim* as to what Rabbi Yochanan himself held. One *Amora* attributes to Rabbi Yochanan the principle that we always follow the *stam Mishna*, which would point to the rabbis' position. The other *Amora*, who attributes to Rabbi Yochanan the ruling like Abba Yossi ben Dostai, must hold that Rabbi Yochanan did not apply the *stam Mishna* principle universally or without exception. Both attributions exist, and the Gemara records them without definitively adjudicating between them.

With this, we conclude the *perek*. *Hadran alach hatchelet*.
