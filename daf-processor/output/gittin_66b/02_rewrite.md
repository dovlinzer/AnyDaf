# Gittin 66b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Messengers

### Two People: Write & Deli…

The present daf picks up with the final line of 66a, continuing the *mishna*'s discussion of the language a husband uses when appointing a *shaliach* and the scope of the word *kitvu*. The next *mishna* turns to a related but distinct issue: whether the individuals whom the husband has appointed must carry out the task personally, or whether they may delegate that responsibility to others.

The *mishna* opens with two cases. If the husband says to two people, *tnu get l'ishti* — give a *get* to my wife — then since *tnu* implies giving but necessarily also writing, the two must themselves carry out both actions. Similarly, if he says to three people, *kitvu get u'tnu l'ishti* — write a *get* and give it to my wife — then *harei elu yichtevu v'yitnu*: it must be precisely those individuals who do it.

### Three People: Delivery O…

There is, however, an important exception. If the husband says to three people, *tnu get l'ishti* — give a *get* to my wife — without specifying *kitvu*, then those three may in turn instruct others to write it: *harei elu yomru l'acherim v'yichtevu*. The *mishna* explains: *mipnei she'asah beis din* — because he has constituted them as a *beis din* — and this is *divrei Rebbi Meir*.

The distinction between two and three people rests on a conceptual difference in what a group of that size represents. Two people are not a constituted body; they are simply the individuals appointed, and the expectation is that they themselves perform the task. Three people, by contrast, constitute a *beis din*, and a *beis din* is understood as a supervisory body — one that takes responsibility for seeing that something is done, not necessarily one that performs every action directly. Accordingly, when the husband says *kitvu u'tnu* explicitly to a group of three, he is being concrete about the actions and means that they themselves must perform them. But when he says only *tnu* to three, the word is understood as conveying oversight and administrative authority, which they may then delegate.

### Beit Din Authority

The *mishna* adds that this ruling was transmitted by Rebbi Chanina ish Ono from prison — and Rashi explains that this means in the name of Rebbi Akiva, who was imprisoned at the time due to the Roman decrees. The report states: *mekubal ani b'omer lishlosha tnu get l'ishti sheyomru l'acherim v'yichtevu mipnei she'asah beis din* — I have a tradition that when one says to three people "give a *get* to my wife," they may transmit that to others, because he has made them a *beis din*.

### Rabbi Yosi's Tradition

Rebbi Yosi responds with a counter-tradition. *Af anu mekubalim* — we too have a received tradition — and his tradition is far more demanding. Even if the husband says *tnu get l'ishti* to the *beis din hagadol sheb'Yerushalayim* — the Great Court of Jerusalem itself — and says only *tnu* without *kitvu*, those distinguished jurists cannot delegate the task. If they wish to give the *get*, *sheyilmedu v'yichtevu v'yitnu* — they must learn how to write a *get* and write it themselves. The appointment, according to Rebbi Yosi, is non-transferable; only the individuals who heard the instruction directly from the husband may execute it.

### Ten People: Role Distrib…

The *mishna* then turns to a different scenario: the husband says to ten people, *kitvu get u'tnu l'ishti*. Since he has said *kitvu* explicitly, he cannot have meant mere oversight; he means they themselves should act. But how is that interpreted when there are more than the minimum number of required participants? The *mishna* rules: *echad kotev u'shnayim chotmim* — one writes it and two sign it. The word *kitvu* addressed to the group does not mean all ten must write and all ten must sign; it means that from among you, the necessary actions must be performed.

### Kulchem: All Present

If, however, the husband says *kulchem kitvu* — all of you write — then *echad kotev v'chulam chotmim*: one writes and they all sign. The word *kulchem* changes the character of the instruction.

### Kitvu: Write or Sign

At this point the *mishna* raises — or at least invites — the question that the *Gemara* will pursue explicitly: what is the primary meaning of the word *kitvu* in this context? Does it refer to the scribing of the *get*, or to signing it? It would seem from the *mishna* that signing is the more fundamental referent, since in the case of *kulchem kitvu* addressed to ten people, only one person actually scribes the document while all of them sign. The *mishna* is evidently not troubled by the fact that not all ten physically write the text. Rather, it seems to be the signing that constitutes the operative meaning of *kitvu* — or at least that signing is its core dimension. One might instead argue that even if *kitvu* also encompasses the scribing, the *mishna* interprets it practically: for the scribing, it suffices that one person writes on behalf of all, whereas for the signing, each must affix his own signature. The *Gemara* will analyze this more carefully.

### Death Invalidates Get

Finally, the *mishna* states: *im mes echad mehem harei zeh get batel* — if one of the group dies after the husband said *kulchem*, the *get* is invalid. This is striking, and worth pausing over. One might have thought that a *get* has a fixed form and fixed requirements — a particular text, two witnesses — and it would not be obvious how the husband's specifications could render an otherwise valid *get* invalid. But what we see here is how central the husband's *da'at* and *ratzon* are to the very nature of the *get*. The husband has shaped the conditions of his own *get*, and if those conditions are not fulfilled — if he required everyone's participation and one of them dies — then the *get* has not been executed according to his will, and it is therefore *batel*. The *get*'s validity is inseparable from the husband's intent as to how it is to be produced.

## Kitvu Meaning Debate

### Rav's Question to Shmuel

The *Gemara* opens with a remarkable report: *shalechu mibei Rav l'Shmuel* — they sent from the *yeshiva* of Rav to Shmuel. This is itself a striking phenomenon, since Rav and Shmuel were contemporaries of roughly equal stature, and yet the scholars of Rav's academy directed this question to Shmuel. The question they sent was: *amar l'shnayim kitvu u'tnu get l'ishti v'amru l'sofer v'chatav v'chatmu hem mahu* — if the husband said to two people "write and give a *get* to my wife," and they did not scribe it themselves but instead had a *sofer* write it while they themselves signed, what is the *halacha*?

The question is fundamentally about the meaning of *kitvu*. The two individuals were appointed, they signed the document, but the actual scribing was done by someone else. Does the husband's instruction *kitvu* require that they personally scribe the *get*, or does it require only that they sign it? If *kitvu* means scribing, then having the *sofer* do the scribing violates the husband's instruction. If *kitvu* means signing, then the two have fully complied.

### Shmuel: Needs Investigat…

Shmuel's response was: *tetzei v'hadavar tzarich talmud* — if she remarried on the basis of this *get*, she cannot remain with her second husband, because the *get* is of uncertain validity; but the matter requires further investigation. Shmuel was genuinely uncertain, and the *Gemara* now sets out to determine what the core of the investigation must be.

### Words & Delegation

The *Gemara* asks: what exactly is the issue Shmuel identified as requiring investigation? One possibility is that the question is whether *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — whether words, meaning a purely verbal appointment, can be passed on to another agent. This principle, which will be central to much of the remainder of the *daf*, holds that when an agency involves no physical object, only a verbal appointment, the agent may not delegate that agency to a further agent. When the husband gives the *shaliach* the physical *get* to deliver to the wife, the physical object in some sense represents and carries the agency. But the appointment of the *sofer* to write a *get* — before the *get* even exists — is purely verbal, and the question is whether that verbal appointment can be passed on.

### Verbal vs. Physical

To understand this, it is useful to recall the case of a *shaliach lekabbalah* — an agent appointed to receive the *get* on behalf of the wife — who becomes ill. The question of whether such an agent may appoint a substitute agent is precisely this problem: the agent has only a verbal appointment; there is no physical object that he received which he could now transfer. Without the physical object, the authority may not be transferable.

The *Gemara* raises a different formulation of the same issue. The concern of *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* is that the originally appointed agents cannot pass on their appointment to the *sofer*; they were appointed, and they cannot on their own authority re-appoint someone to take their place. This is the position underlying the entire *mishna*: the whole structure of "you must do it yourselves" presupposes that the verbal agency cannot be transferred.

But if this is already the logic of the *mishna*, and if Shmuel himself rules that *halacha k'Rebbi Yosi d'amar mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — the *halacha* follows Rebbi Yosi who holds that a verbal agency cannot be passed on — then the question seems already answered: the *sofer* cannot have been used, because the two appointed agents could not delegate to him. Why then does Shmuel say *tzarich talmud*?

The *Gemara* therefore reframes the question. Shmuel is not uncertain about *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — that rule he knows and accepts. The question is rather: *hai kitvu, iksav yadan iksav haget?* — when the husband says *kitvu*, does he mean "sign it" or does he mean "scribe it"? These two possibilities lead to fundamentally different analyses.

If *kitvu* means "scribe it," then the two appointed agents were supposed to do the scribing themselves, and having the *sofer* scribe it is a violation of the appointment — a case of *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*.

If *kitvu* means "sign it," then the husband never appointed the two to scribe anything. He told them to sign the *get* — and implicitly, to find someone to write it. In that case, they are not delegating their agency at all. They remain the signatories; they are merely communicating to the *sofer* that the husband wants him to write the *get*. That is a different concept entirely: *omer imru* — informing a third party of the husband's wish — rather than transferring one's own agency. And *omer imru*, as we will see, may be permitted even according to positions that prohibit *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*.

### Mishna Presumption

The *Gemara* asks: can we not simply resolve this from the *mishna* itself? The *mishna* states that if the husband says to two people *kitvu u'tnu get l'ishti*, then *harei elu yichtevu v'yitnu* — they must write and give it. This suggests that *kitvu* means something they must do themselves, which would favor the interpretation that *kitvu* refers to scribing. The *Gemara* responds that the *mishna* itself is the subject of the question — the question of what *yichtevu* in the *mishna* means is precisely what we are investigating. The *mishna* does not independently resolve the ambiguity; it merely reuses the same term.

### Beit Din Learning

The *Gemara* then argues more forcefully that *kitvu* must mean scribing, drawing on Rebbi Yosi's ruling in the *mishna*. Rebbi Yosi says that even the *beis din hagadol shebiYerushalayim*, if told *tnu get l'ishti*, must *she'ilmedu v'yichtevu v'yachtemu* — learn how to write a *get* and do so themselves. If *kitvu* meant only signing, this passage would be absurd: *mi ika bei dina d'lo yadei michtav chasimas yedaihu* — is there conceivably a *beis din* whose members do not know how to sign their own names? The very premise of Rebbi Yosi's ruling — that they would need to *learn* — only makes sense if what they must learn is the scribing of a *get*, not the writing of their signatures.

### Signing Possibility

The *Gemara* deflects this argument. One could theoretically posit a newly constituted *beis din* whose members have not yet established their customary signatures. While the *Gemara* acknowledges this is a weak answer, it means the proof from Rebbi Yosi's ruling is not airtight. The possibility remains, at least formally, that *kitvu* refers to signing. The *Gemara* then approaches the issue from yet another angle: if *kitvu* means signing and therefore implies that the two signatories may find their own *sofer* to scribe the *get*, is that not itself a form of *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*? The *Gemara* answers: no. If the husband said *kitvu* meaning "sign," then the husband himself implicitly authorized the signatories to procure a *sofer*. Whoever they find to write the *get* is, in effect, being appointed by the husband through the mode of *omer imru* — the husband is essentially saying, through his agents, "go tell someone to write it." The signatories are not re-delegating their own agency; they are transmitting the husband's appointment to the *sofer*. And *omer imru* — indirect appointment by informing a third party — may well be permitted.

## Chasam Sofer V'Ed

### Witnesses Inform Sofer

The *Gemara* now raises a new case to probe these distinctions. A husband says to two people: *imru l'sofer v'yichtov u'l'ploni u'ploni v'yachtemu* — tell the *sofer* to write it, and tell so-and-so and so-and-so to sign it. Here the husband himself is using *omer imru*: he is appointing the two people not to sign or scribe, but merely to convey his wishes to the *sofer* and to the designated witnesses. Rav Huna citing Rav rules: *keshera v'lo ta'aseh zo b'Yisrael* — it is technically valid, but this should never be done.

### Scenario Requirement

This scenario is worth noting. It only makes sense to entertain this case if *omer imru* is at least conceivably permitted. Were *omer imru* entirely invalid — were the rule that every participant must hear the appointment directly from the husband — then this entire arrangement would be obviously invalid and the *Gemara* would not need to say it is *keshera* while adding a caution against doing it. The very structure of the ruling — *keshera b'lo ta'aseh* — implies that *omer imru* is halachically operative.

### Embarrassment Problem

The *Gemara* asks: if it is *keshera*, why should one not do it? The answer is: *mishum kisufa d'sofer* — because of the embarrassment of the *sofer*. Consider: the two messengers approach the *sofer* and tell him to write the *get*, and then instruct him that Reuven and Shimon will sign it. The *sofer* may well feel affronted: you trust me to write this document but not to sign it? Am I not a fit witness? Out of concern for the *sofer*'s dignity, the messenger may quietly adjust the arrangement — he might tell the *sofer* to sign as well, and drop one of the originally designated witnesses. The resulting *get* would then have been executed in a manner the husband never sanctioned. The document would appear valid but would in fact be *pasul*, since the witnesses who signed it were not those the husband designated.

### Permitted, Not Done

The *Gemara* resolves this concern through the ruling itself: *kivan d'amar mar kasher v'lo ta'aseh kein b'Yisrael* — since we rule that it is *kasher* but should not be done, this means the *sofer* and the designated witnesses should refuse to participate in any arrangement where the embarrassment dynamic might lead them to deviate from the husband's instructions. Since they are expected to refuse such a scenario, the feared outcome is unlikely to materialize — *lo shechiach* — and the theoretical validity of *omer imru* does not, in practice, give rise to the feared abuse.

## Witnesses Find Sofer

### Direct Witness Appointme…

A related but distinct case now presents itself. Suppose the husband says directly to two people: *imru l'sofer v'yichtov v'atem chatomu* — tell the *sofer* to write it, and you yourselves sign it. Here the witnesses are directly appointed — they will personally sign the *get* — and they are only asking the *sofer* to write the text. The witnesses have a direct appointment, and the *sofer*'s involvement is not a re-delegation but a practical arrangement for the scribing.

### Permitted Debate

Whether this arrangement may be followed *lechatchila*, or whether it too falls under the prohibition of *lo ta'aseh*, is debated. The *Gemara* will return to this at the end of the *daf*. The general concern, as will become clear, remains that a woman might hire individuals to falsely report the husband's instructions, and any arrangement in which the *sofer* is not directly appointed by the husband is potentially vulnerable to that abuse.

## Mili vs. Omer Imru

### Two Concepts

Before proceeding, it is important to clearly distinguish between two concepts that run through this entire discussion. The first is *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*: if I have appointed you as my agent, you may not pass that appointment on to another. Your agency is personal; it came to you directly from me, and the authority it carries cannot be transferred further. The second concept is the question of *shlichus shelo befanav* — can the husband appoint an agent without speaking to that agent directly? Can he tell Michael to inform Jenna that she is to write the *get*, so that Jenna becomes the husband's agent even though the husband never spoke to her personally?

These are conceptually distinct. *Mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* addresses what happens after an appointment has been validly made — the appointee cannot re-delegate. The question of *shlichus shelo befanav* addresses whether a valid appointment can be made indirectly in the first place. *Omer imru* is precisely the question of *shlichus shelo befanav*: does the husband's instruction, conveyed through a third party, constitute a valid appointment?

---

## Mili vs. Omer Imru…

### Rabbi Yosi: Both

Rebbi Yosi, as the *Gemara* now establishes, holds both restrictions. He prohibits *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — once you are appointed, you cannot pass the agency on — and he also prohibits *omer imru* — the husband cannot appoint someone through an intermediary; the appointee must hear the instruction directly. This is precisely why, in Rebbi Yosi's ruling in the *mishna*, even the *beis din hagadol shebiYerushalayim* cannot delegate the writing of the *get*: the members of the *beis din* are the only ones who heard it from the husband, and only they may execute it. Any indirect appointment — even one where the husband clearly intended to confer authority on others — is invalid.

### Shmuel's Position

Shmuel, by contrast, accepts *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — he rules like Rebbi Yosi that you cannot re-delegate a verbal appointment — but he permits *omer imru*. The husband may communicate his appointment through a third party, and the person thus informed becomes a valid agent. Shmuel therefore occupies a middle position: tighter than the *tanna kamma* but not as restrictive as Rebbi Yosi across the board.

---

## Kitvu Meaning Debate…

### Shmuel's Split

This split in Shmuel's position is the key to understanding his original question. When the husband says *kitvu* to two people, if it means "scribe it," then they cannot have the *sofer* do it, because they cannot re-delegate their verbal appointment — *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*. But if *kitvu* means "sign it," then implicitly the husband authorized the two to find a *sofer* through *omer imru*, which Shmuel permits. In that case, the *sofer* writes entirely validly, and the *get* is *kasher*. Shmuel's uncertainty — *tzarich talmud* — is thus the unresolved question of which meaning *kitvu* carries.

## Following Rabbi Yosi

### Rabbi Shimon's Question

The *Gemara* returns to the ruling cited earlier: *amar Shmuel amar Rebbi halacha k'Rebbi Yosi d'amar mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — Shmuel said in the name of Rebbi that the *halacha* follows Rebbi Yosi. Rebbi Shimon the son of Rebbi is surprised. Given that both Rebbi Meir and Chanina ish Ono — reporting in the name of Rebbi Akiva — disagree with Rebbi Yosi and hold that a group of three constituted as a *beis din* may delegate the task, what motivated Rebbi to rule like Rebbi Yosi? *Mah ra'ah Rebbi lomar halacha k'Rebbi Yosi?* — what did Rebbi see that led him to do so?

### Nimuko Imo

The response is: *shtok beni shtok. V'ei illu lo ra'isa es Rebbi Yosi* — be silent, my son. Had you ever merited to see Rebbi Yosi in person, you would understand. *Nimuko imo* — his reasoning is with him. This is the famous principle by which the *halacha* generally follows Rebbi Yosi: *halacha k'Rebbi Yosi shenimuko imo*. Whatever Rebbi Yosi ruled was backed by particularly thorough and well-grounded reasoning. Even when his position is outnumbered, even when the logic on the surface seems to favor his opponents, the presumption is that Rebbi Yosi's position has a depth of reasoning that justifies the ruling in his favor.

### Praises of Sages

The *Gemara* supports this by citing a *braisa* from *Avos d'Rebbi Natan*, in which Issi ben Yehuda would enumerate the distinctive qualities of the Sages. *Rebbi Meir chacham v'sofer* — Rebbi Meir was a sage and a scribe. Notably, he was literally a scribe, which lends an interesting resonance given our *sugya*'s concern with the scribing of a *get*. *Rebbi Yehuda chacham lecheshe'yirtzeh* — Rebbi Yehuda was wise when he chose to be; Rashi explains that when he took the time to think something through, he could be very wise. *Rebbi Tarfon gal shel egozim* — Rebbi Tarfon was like a pile of nuts: ask him something and everything comes cascading out, one thing flowing from the next in abundance. *Rebbi Yishmael chanut meyuzenet* — Rebbi Yishmael was like a well-stocked store; whatever you need, he has it, and he knows where to find it. *Rebbi Akiva otzar balum* — Rebbi Akiva was a warehouse organized into compartments. Rashi and Tosafos debate the precise meaning of *balum*, but *Avos d'Rebbi Natan* itself clarifies: Rebbi Akiva would receive teachings in a mixed, unsorted form — *midrash*, *halacha*, analysis — and then come home and sort everything into its proper category. This is indeed what his students did in editing the *midrashei halacha* — the *Sifra*, the *Sifrei* — systematizing the entire corpus, a feat not unlike what the *Rambam* later achieved.

*Rebbi Yochanan ben Nuri kupa shel rochlim* — Rebbi Yochanan ben Nuri was like the basket of traveling merchants, who carried unusual and varied wares from town to town. Unlike the stocked store with its staples, the merchant's basket contains the rare and the unexpected. *Rebbi Elazar ben Azarya kupa shel besamim* — he was like the spice merchant's basket; Rashi says this is similar in concept, though perhaps smaller in scope. *Mishnas Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov kav v'naki* — the teaching of Rebbi Eliezer ben Yaakov is only a *kav* — a small measure — but it is a very clean measure: few rulings, but each one carefully sifted and pure, so that what he does say carries correspondingly great weight.

And then: *Rebbi Yosi nimuko imo* — Rebbi Yosi's reasoning is with him. This is striking, because the reasoning is typically not spelled out explicitly in the *mishna*; on the surface, one might even think the more logical position belongs to Rebbi Meir and Chanina ish Ono, who held that a group constituted as a *beis din* may naturally delegate. Yet the received principle is that Rebbi Yosi's underlying reasoning, even when unstated, is so sound that we rule with him.

The *Gemara* also cites the description of Rebbi Shimon: *tochen harbeh u'motzi kim'a* — he grinds a great deal and produces only a little. This sounds like a criticism, but the *Gemara* clarifies: *mishake'ach kim'a* — he forgets only a little. The grinding is thorough; what he "produces" outward, what he actually forgets, is minimal — and even what he does forget is *suvin*, the chaff, the material that should have been discarded anyway. Rebbi Shimon himself told his students: *shanu midotai* — learn my methodology or my teachings — *sheymidotai trumot mitrumot midotav shel Rebbi Akiva* — for they are the choicest of the choicest of Rebbi Akiva's methods.

## Lo Ta'aseh Practice

### Rav Huna's Ruling

The *Gemara* now examines the practical ruling. The case is: *amar lishnayim imru l'sofer v'yichtov u'l'ploni ploni v'yachtemu* — the husband says to two people: go tell the *sofer* to write the *get*, and go tell so-and-so and so-and-so to sign it. Rav Huna citing Rav rules: *keshera v'lo ta'aseh zo b'Yisrael* — it is valid but should not be done this way.

---

## Lo Ta'aseh Practice…

### Woman Hiring Concern

The question is pressed: if it is *keshera*, why the prohibition? The answer is: *chaishinan shema tischor eidim* — we are concerned that the woman may have hired witnesses. If the husband did not personally appoint anyone, and two people come to the *sofer* and the witnesses claiming that the husband instructed them to arrange the *get*, there is a real concern that the woman herself hired those two people to make this claim. Since the intermediaries bear no direct personal responsibility — they are not signing the *get* themselves, they are merely conveying a message — they might be persuadable to lie. The *sofer* and the witnesses, by relying on these intermediaries, risk participating in the production of a fraudulent *get*.

### Dibbura vs. Ma'aseh

The *Gemara* challenges this concern: *eidim hachatumim al hasadeh mekach v'al get ishah lo chashu chachamim l'davar zeh* — witnesses who are signed on a bill of sale or on a *get* are not suspected of forgery; the Sages had no general concern that witnesses might falsify documents. The *Gemara* distinguishes: *ma'aseh lo avdi* — people will not commit an actual act of forgery, will not sign a false document. But *dibbura ka'amri* — they may be willing merely to convey a false oral message, to tell the *sofer* and the witnesses that the husband gave these instructions when in fact he did not. The act of signing a forged document is a categorical offense that people generally refuse. But repeating a message — even a false one — without personally being a signatory to the resulting *get*, carries a lower psychological threshold, and the concern that the woman's hired agents might do so is realistic. The prohibition of *lo ta'aseh ken b'Yisrael* addresses precisely this vulnerability.

### Amoraic Debate

A subsequent case introduces a further variation: *amar lishnayim imru l'sofer v'yichtov v'atem chatomu* — suppose the husband tells two people: go tell the *sofer* to write it, but you yourselves sign it. Here, the witnesses are directly appointed — they will personally sign the *get*, with full personal accountability — and they are only instructing the *sofer* to write. Rav Chisda rules: *keshera b'lo ta'aseh* — even this arrangement is valid but should not be done, though here the prohibition is only a *gezeira*, a protective extension, lest permitting this lead to permitting the prior case. Rabba bar bar Chana permits it outright: *kasher v'ta'aseh*. Rav Nachman rules *kasher v'lo ta'aseh*. Rav Sheshet says *kasher v'ta'aseh*. Rabba says *kasher v'lo ta'aseh*. Rav Yosef says *kasher v'ta'aseh*. The *Gemara* notes that some authorities reverse the positions attributed to Rabba and Rav Yosef.

The practical bottom line is nuanced. The *halacha* of *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* is accepted, ruling like Rebbi Yosi. *Omer imru*, however — the indirect appointment through an intermediary — is technically valid according to Shmuel's position, which the *Gemara* does not fully abandon. Yet *omer imru* in practice should not be relied upon, not because it is halachically invalid in principle, but because of the risk that those conveying the message may not genuinely be representing the husband.

---

## Witnesses Find Sofer…

### Hired Witnesses Fear

This connects back to the case where witnesses are appointed directly but find their own *sofer*. There too, the real concern underlying the prohibition of *lo ta'aseh* is not the technical question of *shlichus shelo befanav* but the fear that the woman has hired witnesses to fabricate the husband's instructions. *Ma'aseh lo avdi* — people will not forge a signature on the document — but *dibbura ka'amri* — they may be willing to falsely represent a verbal instruction. The responsibility falls on the last link in the chain: the *sofer* and the witnesses who will ultimately produce the *get* must not rely on intermediaries whose report cannot be independently verified, lest the entire get rest on a fabricated mandate.

## Counting Ten People

### Enumeration Question

The *Gemara* raises a final question concerning the *mishna*'s case of ten people. If the husband did not say *kulchem* — all of you — but instead counted them one by one: one, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight, nine, ten, give a *get* to my wife — what is the legal effect of enumeration? Does counting each person mean the same as saying *kulchem*, requiring all of them to participate? Or does it mean merely that he selected this group as the pool from which the requisite participants are to be drawn?

### Rav Huna on Counting

Rav Huna rules: *mana eino kechulam* — counting is not equivalent to *kulchem*. Enumerating the individuals simply designates the group; it does not require all of them to participate. Any of them may act on behalf of the group.

### Rav Yochanan on Counting

Rav Yochanan, citing Rabbi Elazar from Rome, rules: *mana harei hu kechulam* — counting is equivalent to *kulchem*. When the husband counts each person individually, he is singling out each one specifically, and the effect is the same as explicitly requiring all of them.

### Rav Papa's Resolution

Rav Papa offers a resolution: *velo pligi* — they do not actually dispute each other. The disagreement depends on a specific factual distinction: *ha demana kulhu, ha demana mikztayehu* — one ruling applies where the husband counted all the people present, and the other applies where he counted only some of them. The *Gemara* notes that there is a further dispute as to which direction the rule runs. Some say: if he counted all of them, it is equivalent to *kulchem*, since he specifically accounted for each one; if he counted only some, it merely designates a subgroup from which any may act. Others reverse this: if he counted all of them, it is not equivalent to *kulchem*, since that is simply how one refers to a group; but if he singled out a subset, the enumeration of that specific subset implies that each of the counted individuals is required.

### Rav Yehuda's Takanah

Rav Yehuda instituted a *takana* to address the ambiguity surrounding *kulchem*. He established a recommended text for cases where a husband wishes to involve multiple people, specifying: *ktovu o kulchem o kol echad v'echad michem* — write, either all of you or any one of you; *chatmu tarrei o kulchem o kol tarrei michem* — sign, either all of you or any two of you; *avilu o kulchem o kol echad v'echad michem* — deliver, either all of you or any one of you. By explicitly including both possibilities in the husband's instructions, the ambiguity is dissolved: whether all participate or only some, the *get* is valid.

### Rava's Practical Advice

Rava, however, objects to this formulation. *Zimnin d'gazlei le-diburei v'amar kulchem v'lo amar kol echad v'echad michem* — it sometimes happens that one seizes on the first part of the instruction and says *kulchem* without completing the formulation with *kol echad v'echad michem*, thereby inadvertently invoking the more restrictive requirement. The problem is that beginning with *kulchem* plants a word that, if the remainder of the formula is omitted or forgotten, will invalidate the *get*. Rava therefore advises: eliminate *kulchem* from the formula altogether. Simply instruct: *ktovu kol echad michem* — write, any one of you; *chatmu kol tarrei michem* — sign, any two of you; *avilu kol echad michem* — deliver, any one of you. By removing *kulchem* entirely from the husband's instructions, the risk of inadvertently imposing that requirement disappears.

With this practical guidance, the *perek* concludes: *hadran alach ha'omer*.
