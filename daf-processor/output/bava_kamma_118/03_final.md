# Bava Kamma 118 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Damaging Through Words

### Property Pointed to Gov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דְּאַחְוִי אַמְּטַכְסָא דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא. יְתֵיב רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ וְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פַּפִּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא, וְיָתֵיב רַבִּי אִילְעָא גַּבַּיְיהוּ. סְבוּר לְחַיּוֹבֵיהּ מֵהָא דִּתְנַן: דָּן אֶת הַדִּין – זִיכָּה אֶת הַחַיָּיב וְחִיֵּיב אֶת הַזַּכַּאי, טִימֵּא אֶת הַטָּהוֹר וְטִיהֵר אֶת הַטָּמֵא – מַה שֶּׁעָשָׂה עָשׂוּי, וִישַׁלֵּם מִבֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates another incident pertaining to one who informed gentiles of the whereabouts of another Jew's property. There was **a certain** individual **who showed Rabbi Abba's silk [*ametakesa*]** to gentiles, who later seized it. **Rabbi Abbahu and Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappi and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa sat** together to determine whether Rabbi Abba was entitled to compensation from the informer, **and Rabbi Ile'a sat next to them.** The judges **thought to deem** the informer **liable** to reimburse Rabbi Abba **based upon that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 28b): If a judge **issued a judgment** and erred, and he **acquitted** one who was in fact **liable,** or **deemed liable** one who should have in fact been **acquitted,** or if he **ruled** that **a pure** item is **impure,** or **ruled** that **an impure** item is **pure,** and by doing so he caused a litigant a monetary loss, **what he did is done,** i.e., the judgment stands, **and** the judge **must pay** damages **from his home,** i.e., from his personal funds. This indicates that one is liable to pay for a financial loss that he causes even if his involvement was only through speech.

The rabbis sitting together — Rav Abba, Rav Chanina bar Pappa, Rav Yitzchak Naphcha, and Rav Yehuda among them — initially thought the informer should be liable for damages, drawing support from the *mishna* in Sanhedrin: a judge who renders an erroneous verdict, exempting the liable party or condemning the innocent, or who renders a ruling of *tameh* or *tahor* incorrectly, cannot reverse what he has done (*ma she'asa asuy*), yet must compensate from his own pocket (*yeshalem mibeito*). Since the damage was accomplished through words alone and is nonetheless actionable, the same logic would seem to apply here.

### Rav: Nasav Ve-Natan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי אִילְעָא, הָכִי אָמַר רַב: וְהוּא שֶׁנָּשָׂא וְנָתַן בַּיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ile'a said to them: This is** what **Rav says: And that** mishna is discussing a case **where** the judge not only issued a ruling, but actively **took** the money from the one whom he found liable, **and gave** it to the other party **by** his own **hand.**

Rav, however, rejects this parallel. *Vehu shenatal venatan beyad* — the judge's liability obtains only when he physically transferred property from one party to another following his ruling. Mere verbal causation, without a physical act of transfer, is insufficient to impose liability under the judge's malpractice ruling.

### Stolen Field, Gov Seizure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: זִיל לְגַבֵּי דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְיָקִים וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן פְּדָת, דְּדָיְינִי דִּינָא דִגְרָמֵי. אֲזַל לְגַבַּיְיהוּ, חַיְּיבֵיהּ מִמַּתְנִיתִין – אִם מֵחֲמַת הַגַּזְלָן, חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ שָׂדֶה אַחֵר; וְאוֹקִימְנָא דְּאַחְוִי אַחְווֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages serving as judges **said to** Rabbi Abba: **Go to Rabbi Shimon ben Elyakim and Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat, who rule** that there is **liability** for damage caused by **indirect** action. Rabbi Abba **went to them,** and **they deemed** the informer **liable** to reimburse Rabbi Abba, as it is taught **in the mishna: If** the thugs seized the field **due to the robber, he is liable to provide** the owner with **a different field. And it was established that** the mishna is referring to a case **where** an individual **showed** the field to thugs who later seized it.

The rabbis therefore direct the aggrieved party to Rav Shimon ben Elyakim and Rav Elazar ben Pedat, who adjudicate cases of *dina d'garmi* — indirect but sufficiently direct causation that imposes liability. These authorities find the informer liable based on the *mishna* stating that if a field was seized because of the *gazlan*, the *gazlan* must replace it (*im machmat hagazlan chayav le'amidi sadeh acheret*). The *gemara* had previously established that this applies even where the *gazlan* did not physically steal the field himself but pointed it out to the authorities (*achvei achvaye*). This conclusion thus confirms that the liability rests squarely on the doctrine of *dina d'garmi*.

## Saving Self w/ Other's

### Silver Cup Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה מִפְּקִיד לֵיהּ כָּסָא דְכַסְפָּא. סְלִיקוּ גַּנָּבֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ, שַׁקְלַהּ יַהֲבַהּ לְהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה, פַּטְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain man** with **whom a silver cup was deposited. Thieves came upon him** in his home and **he took** the cup and **gave it to them.** The case **came before Rabba,** and Rabba **exempted him** from payment.

The *gemara* next presents a case: *hahu gavra dahave mufkad leh kasa dekaspa* — a man had been entrusted with a silver goblet. Thieves broke in (*sliku ganvei ilavye*), and he — reading the text carefully — took the goblet himself and handed it over to them (*shakle vehavneih nialei*). This is genuinely a case of *nasav ve-natan beyad*: he physically transferred the object. The matter came before Rabba, who exempted him. The man was protecting his own life with his friend's property — *matzil atzmo bemamon chavero*.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: This** individual **is saving himself with another's property,** and he should therefore be liable.

Abaye challenges this exemption: *ha-matzil atzmo bemamon chavero hu* — is it not the case that one who saves himself with another's property is liable? As the *gemara* has established from other contexts, *assur lehatzil atzmo bemamon chavero*. While this phrase cannot literally mean one should allow himself to be killed rather than sacrifice another's property, it means at minimum that the act of self-preservation does not extinguish the legal obligation to compensate the owner.

### Wealthy vs. Non-Wealthy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חָזֵינַן; אִי אִינִישׁ אֲמִיד הוּא – אַדַּעְתָּא דִידֵיהּ אֲתוֹ, וְאִי לָא – אַדַּעְתָּא דְכַסְפָּא אֲתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said,** in explanation of Rabba's ruling: **We look** at his financial status: **If** the bailee **is a wealthy man,** the thieves **came with the intent** to steal **his** property, and he is therefore liable to pay, as he saved himself from financial loss by handing over another's property. **And if** he is **not** wealthy, the thieves presumably **came with the intent** to steal **the silver** cup, and he is therefore exempt from liability.

Rav Ashi introduces a distinction: *ee inish amid hu* — if the man is wealthy, then the thieves came because they knew he had his own money; the fact that he handed over the silver goblet was his own doing and in some sense attributable to the attention he attracted. In that case, presumably he bears liability. *Ve-ilo ada'ata de-kaspa hu* — if he is not wealthy, the thieves came specifically because they knew he was holding that silver goblet. The invasion was, in other words, entirely occasioned by the object itself rather than by the guardian personally. The difficulty, however, remains: even in that scenario, this is a case of *nasav ve-natan beyad*, which in the earlier discussion about pointing out property to the government was cited as the factor that *increases* liability. How then does the object being the thieves' target exempt the guardian?

### Tosafot on Shomer

Tosafot addresses this tension. Even granting that *nasav ve-natan beyad* ordinarily imposes liability, the case of a *shomer* is distinguishable. As Tosafot explains in the name of the Ri: *shelo lada'at ken hifkidu sheyatzil atzmo bo im yavo elav ada'ata de-kaspa* — the implicit terms of the bailment arrangement are such that by entrusting the object to this guardian, the owner effectively caused the guardian to become a target on his behalf. The object's presence in the guardian's home is what drew the danger, and so the entrustor bears some degree of responsibility for placing the guardian in that position. Tosafot acknowledges this is not entirely satisfying given the earlier analysis, and the tension between this ruling and the preceding discussion is not fully resolved.

### Pidyon Shvuyim Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה מִפְּקִיד גַּבֵּיהּ אַרְנְקָא דְּפִדְיוֹן שְׁבוּיִים. סְלִיקוּ גַּנָּבֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ, שַׁקְלַהּ יַהֲבַהּ נִיהֲלַיְיהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, פַּטְרֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא מַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ הוּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין לְךָ פִּדְיוֹן שְׁבוּיִים גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain man with whom the purse** containing funds collected **for the redemption of captives was deposited. Thieves came upon him** and **he took** the purse and **gave it to them.** The case **came before Rabba,** and Rabba **exempted him** from payment. **Abaye said to him: But this** individual **is saving himself with another's property,** and he should therefore be liable to pay. Rabba **said to him: You have no greater redemption of captives than this.**

The *gemara* then presents a parallel case: *hahu gavra de-hava mafkid gabei arneka de-pidyon shvuyim* — a man was entrusted with a wallet filled with money designated for the redemption of captives. Thieves came, and again he took the pouch and handed it over to them. Rabba again exempted him. Abaye again challenges: *ha-matzil atzmo bemamon chavero*. Rabba responds with a striking argument: *ein lecha pidyon shvuyim gadol mizeh* — there is no greater act of redeeming captives than this, for the money was designated to save Jewish lives, and here it was used precisely to save a Jewish life.

One might ask: why could he not have used his own money? Tosafot clarifies: even if the man was wealthy, the thieves came specifically because of the *pidyon shvuyim* money, and in any case, at that moment he did not have his own money available. When a person faces a genuine threat to his life or liberty and has no personal funds at hand, using money designated for *pidyon shvuyim* to save himself is itself regarded as *pidyon shvuyim*. Tosafot is careful to limit this: it applies only if he truly lacked access to his own funds at that moment.

The more fundamental question — why the fact that the thieves came specifically because of that particular object should exempt the holder, as contrasted with the earlier case where someone pointed out another's property to the government and was held liable even though the government came specifically for that property — remains unresolved. The distinction based on the *shomer* relationship and the entrustor's implicit role in placing the guardian at risk is the closest the *gemara* comes to an answer, but the fit is imperfect.

## Donkey on Boat

### Donkey Thrown Overboard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַקְדֵּים וְאַסֵּיק חֲמָרָא לְמַבָּרָא, קַמֵּי דִּסְלִיקוּ אִינָשֵׁי בְּמַבָּרָא. בָּעֵי לְאַטְבּוֹעֵי, אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא, מְלַח לֵיהּ לַחֲמָרָא דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא וְשַׁדְיֵיהּ לְנַהֲרָא, וּטְבַע. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה, פַּטְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: There was **a certain man who hastened and brought his donkey** onboard **a ferry [*lemavra*] before** other **people boarded the ferry.** The donkey began to move around and **was about** to cause the boat **to sink. A certain** other **man came** and **pushed the donkey of that** first **man into the river, and it drowned.** The case **came before Rabba,** and Rabba **exempted him** from payment.

The *gemara* presents the classic case: *hahu gavra de-atzin ve-asi chamra le-mavra* — a certain man brought his donkey onto a ferry boat. There is a textual question here about the *girsa*: the standard reading has him bringing the donkey onto the boat before the other passengers boarded (*kamei de-sliku inshi be-mavra*), while other versions simply have him bringing it on along with everyone else. The *gemara* continues: *baei le-atvuei* — the boat was in danger of sinking. A certain bystander came and pushed the donkey overboard (*umalakh lei le-chamra de-hahu gavra va-shadya le-nahr*), and the donkey drowned. The matter came before Rava, who exempted the bystander.

### Rava Exempt, Abaye Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא מַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ הוּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַאי – מֵעִיקָּרָא רוֹדֵף הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But this** individual **is saving himself with another's property,** and he should therefore be liable to pay. Rabba **said to him: This** owner of the donkey **was** considered **a pursuer from the outset,** as he endangered the other travelers.

Abaye objects once more: *ve-ha-matzil atzmo be-mamon chavero* — the people on the boat were saving themselves with another's property. Why should the one who pushed the donkey be exempt?

### Rodef Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה: רוֹדֵף שֶׁהָיָה רוֹדֵף אַחַר חֲבֵירוֹ לְהוֹרְגוֹ, וְשִׁיבֵּר אֶת הַכֵּלִים, בֵּין שֶׁל נִרְדׇּף בֵּין שֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם – פָּטוּר, שֶׁהֲרֵי מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **Rabba** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rabba says:** In the case of **a pursuer who was chasing after another** in order **to kill him and** the pursuer **broke vessels** during the chase, **whether** they **belonged to the pursued** party **or to anyone** else, he is **exempt** from reimbursing the owner of the vessels. This is **because he is liable to** receive the **death** penalty for attempted murder and is consequently exempt from any monetary liability he incurs simultaneously.

Rava responds: *hai me'ikara rodef hava* — this donkey was from the outset a *rodef*, a pursuer threatening the lives of all aboard. Rava then invokes his own principle: *rodef she-haya rodef achar chavero le-horgo*, a pursuer chasing a victim to kill him, who in the course of that pursuit breaks vessels — *bein shel nirdaf bein shel kol adam*, whether those vessels belong to the victim or to any third party — is exempt (*patur*). The reason is *she-harei mitchayev be-nafsho*: since the pursuer may lawfully be killed to stop him, a form of the *kim leh* principle applies. Because during the act of pursuit the *rodef* is subject to being killed, he does not simultaneously incur monetary liabilities.

### Rodef & Nirdaf Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִרְדׇּף שֶׁשִּׁיבֵּר אֶת הַכֵּלִים שֶׁל רוֹדֵף – פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא מָמוֹנוֹ חָבִיב עָלָיו מִגּוּפוֹ. אֲבָל שֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם – חַיָּיב, דְּאָסוּר לְהַצִּיל עַצְמוֹ בְּמָמוֹן חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And a pursued** individual **who broke the vessels of the pursuer** is also **exempt** from payment, **as the pursuer's property shall not be cherished more than his body,** i.e., his life. Since it is permitted to kill the pursuer in order to save his intended victim, it is permitted to destroy his property for that purpose. **But** if he destroyed property **belonging to anyone** else, he is **liable** to reimburse them, **as it is prohibited** for him **to save himself with another's property.**

The *nirdaf* — the pursued victim — who breaks the *rodef*'s vessels in the course of saving his own life is likewise exempt: *shelo yehei mamono chaviv alav mi-gufo* — the *rodef*'s property is not more precious than the *rodef*'s own life, and if the *rodef* may be killed to save the victim, his property may certainly be destroyed for the same purpose. But if the *nirdaf* breaks the vessels of a third party, he is *chayav*, for *assur le-hatzil atzmo be-mamon chavero*. This is, significantly, Rava's own ruling — and this is the key to understanding the preceding cases. Abaye, Rava's student, was in each of the earlier cases throwing Rava's own principle back at him. When Abaye said *ha-matzil atzmo be-mamon chavero*, he was reminding Rava of Rava's own teaching and asking why Rava was exempting the parties in each case.

### Good Samaritan Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרוֹדֵף שֶׁהָיָה רוֹדֵף אַחַר רוֹדֵף לְהַצִּיל, וְשִׁיבֵּר כֵּלִים, בֵּין שֶׁל נִרְדׇּף בֵּין שֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם – פָּטוּר. וְלֹא מִן הַדִּין, אֶלָּא שֶׁאִם אִי אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן – אֵין לְךָ אָדָם שֶׁמַּצִּיל אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ מִן הָרוֹדֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **a pursuer who was chasing after** another **pursuer** in order **to save** the latter's intended victim, **and** he **broke vessels** during the chase, **whether** they **belonged to the pursued** individual, i.e., the individual attempting murder, **or to anyone** else, he is **exempt** from payment. The Gemara notes: **And this is not** the *halakha* **by** Torah **law, but if you do not say so, you** will **not have** any **person that saves another from a pursuer.** In order to encourage people to attempt to save the lives of others, the Sages instituted that one who damages another's property in the process of saving a life is exempt from payment.

The *gemara* then discusses a third-party intervener — one who is neither the *rodef* nor the *nirdaf* but steps in to stop the *rodef*, and in the process breaks someone else's vessels. This person is exempt, though the *gemara* acknowledges openly: *lo min ha-din* — strictly speaking, he has no legal basis for exemption. The exemption is a policy ruling: *she-im ata omer kein ein lecha adam she-matzil chaveiro min ha-rodef* — if you impose liability on the intervener, no one will ever step in to save another from a pursuer. This is the *gemara*'s version of good Samaritan law: the one who intervenes to prevent a murder is not required to pay for property damaged in the course of doing so. The *nirdaf* himself needs no such policy exemption, since he will do whatever necessary to save his own life regardless.

The application of the *rodef* framework to the donkey case raises its own difficulties. Who exactly is the *rodef*? If the donkey is the *rodef*, the people on the boat are all victims (*nirdafim*), which should mean they can destroy the donkey without any question. If instead the owner of the donkey is deemed the *rodef* — and the note in our *girsa* that he boarded first and brought extra weight aboard may be relevant, positioning him as having violated the terms of the vessel — then the donkey is treated as the vessels of the *rodef*, and the bystander who pushes it overboard functions as a third-party intervener. The identity of the *rodef* and why the donkey's owner in particular is singled out, when all passengers contribute weight, is not fully unpacked by the *gemara*.

What is clear from the whole section is that in all the prior cases, Rava consistently exempted the party who saved himself with another's money, and Abaye consistently challenged him with Rava's own doctrine. The general answers Rava gives are: where the situation itself constitutes a *rodef* scenario, you are exempt; where the money used was *pidyon shvuyim* funds and the person had no alternative, you are exempt; and where the thieves came specifically because of the entrusted object and the *shomer* relationship is relevant, there may be an exemption — though the last case is the least clearly resolved.

## Karka Ein Nigzelet

### Stolen Field Swamped

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁטָפָהּ נָהָר, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: "הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ".
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a river flooded** a misappropriated field, the robber **may say to** its owner: **That which is yours is before you,** and no compensation is required.

The *gemara* transitions to the next *mishna*: if someone steals a field and it is subsequently swamped by an overflowing river (*sh'tafa nahar*), the thief may say *harei shelcha lefanecha* — here is your property before you — and walk away without replacing it. This reflects the foundational principle of *karka eina nigzelet*: real property cannot be stolen in the halakhic sense, and therefore the theft of land does not carry the liabilities that accompany theft of movable property.

### R. Elazar vs. Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַגּוֹזֵל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וּשְׁטָפָהּ נָהָר – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ שָׂדֶה אַחֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: "הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ".
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** In the case of **one who robbed a field from another and a river** then **flooded it,** the robber is **liable to provide** the field's owner with **a different field.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Elazar. And the Rabbis say:** He is exempt from doing so, as **he** can **say to** the owner: **That which is yours is before you.**

A *baraita* records the dispute: *ha-gozel sadeh me-chaveiro ve-shatafa nahara* — one who steals a field and it is swamped. Rabbi Elazar says the thief is obligated to provide a replacement field (*chayav le-ha'amid lo sadeh acheret*). The Sages say he may simply say *harei shelcha lefanecha*. The question is: on what do they disagree?

### Ribui Miut Ribui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דָּרֵשׁ רִיבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי; "וְכִחֵשׁ בַּעֲמִיתוֹ" – רִיבָּה, "בְּפִקָּדוֹן" – מִיעֵט, "כֹּל אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׁבַע עָלָיו לַשֶּׁקֶר" – חָזַר וְרִיבָּה. רִיבָּה וּמִיעֵט וְרִיבָּה – רִיבָּה הַכֹּל; וּמַאי רַבִּי? רַבִּי כֹּל מִילֵּי. וּמַאי מִיעֵט? מִיעֵט שְׁטָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this dispute: **With regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Elazar interpreted** the verses: "If any one sin, and commit a trespass against the Lord, and deal falsely with his neighbor in a matter of deposit…or anything about which he has sworn falsely, he shall restore it in full" (Leviticus 5:21–24), according to the hermeneutical principle of **amplifications and restrictions.** The phrase: "If any one sin, and commit a trespass against the Lord, **and deal falsely with his neighbor,"** is **an amplification.** When the verse states: **"In a matter of deposit,"** it has **restricted** the *halakha* to the case of a deposit. When the verse then states: **"Or anything about which he has sworn falsely,** he shall restore it in full," **it** has **then amplified** the *halakha* again. Accordingly, as the Torah **amplified and** then **restricted and** then **amplified** again, it has **amplified** the *halakha* to include **everything,** with only a single exception. **And what** is included due to the fact that the verse **has amplified** the *halakha*? The verse **has amplified** the *halakha* to include **everything** that one steals. **And what** is excluded due to the fact that the verse **restricted** the *halakha*? **It restricted** the *halakha* in order to exclude financial **documents,** which are dissimilar to a deposit in that their value is not intrinsic, but rather due to their function.

Rabbi Elazar employs the hermeneutical method of *ribui u-miut* — a broad formulation, then a narrow one. The verse *ve-chi yechahesh be-amito* (when one denies something of his fellow) is a *ribui*, a broad inclusive statement. *Be-fikadon* (in an entrusted object) is a *miut*, a limiting concrete example. *U-ve-chol asher yishava alav la-sheker* (and anything he swore falsely about) is again a *ribui*, broadening the scope. Applying the rule of *ribui miut ve-ribui*, the result is that everything is included except whatever is most unlike the concrete example. What is the *miut* excluding? *Miet shtarot* — documents, which lack intrinsic value (*ein gufan mamon*). Everything else that has intrinsic value, including land, falls within the scope of *ve-heshiv et ha-gezeilah asher gazal*, the obligation to return what was stolen.

### Rabanan Kelal U'Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן דָּרְשִׁי כְּלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי; "וְכִחֵשׁ" – כָּלַל, "בְּפִקָּדוֹן" – פָּרַט, "אוֹ מִכֹּל" – חָזַר וְכָלַל. כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְעֵין הַפְּרָט; מָה הַפְּרָט – דָּבָר הַמִּיטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן, אַף כֹּל דָּבָר הַמִּיטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן. יָצְאוּ קַרְקָעוֹת – שֶׁאֵין מִטַּלְטְלִין; יָצְאוּ עֲבָדִים – שֶׁהוּקְּשׁוּ לְקַרְקָעוֹת; יָצְאוּ שְׁטָרוֹת – שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמִּטַּלְטְלִין, אֵין גּוּפָן מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis interpreted** these verses according to the hermeneutical principle of **generalizations and details.** The phrase: **"And deal falsely** with his neighbor," is **a generalization,** while the subsequent phrase: **"In a matter of deposit,"** is **a detail.** When the verse then states: **"Or anything** about which he has sworn falsely, he shall restore it in full," **it** has **then generalized** again. In a case of **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization, you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail.** Accordingly, **just as the detail,** i.e., a deposit, **is movable property and** has **intrinsic monetary** value, **so too,** the verse includes **anything that is movable property and** has **intrinsic monetary** value. Consequently, **land has been excluded, as it is not movable property. Slaves have been excluded, as they are compared to land** with regard to many areas of *halakha*. Financial **documents have been excluded because, although they** are **movable property, they do not** have **intrinsic monetary** value.

The Sages, by contrast, apply *kelal u-prat u-chelal* — general, particular, general. Using this hermeneutic, one does not include everything except the most dissimilar; rather, one includes only what resembles the concrete case. *Mah ha-prat davar ha-mitaltel ve-gufo mamon* — the particular case, *pikadon*, is both movable and possessed of intrinsic value. The obligation of *ve-heshiv et ha-gezeilah* therefore extends only to things that are both chattel and intrinsically valuable. *Yatzu karkaot she-einan mitaltelin* — land is excluded because it is not movable. *Yatzu avadim she-hukshu le-karkaot* — slaves are excluded by their analogy to land. *Ve-yatzu shtarot* — documents are excluded because, though movable, they lack intrinsic value. Hence, according to the Sages, *karka eina nigzelet*.

### Cow on Stolen Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָדְתַנְיָא: הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת הַפָּרָה וּשְׁטָפָהּ נָהָר – חַיָּיב לְהַעֲמִיד לוֹ פָּרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, אוֹמֵר לוֹ: "הֲרֵי שֶׁלְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ". הָתָם בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** there is **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who robbed** another of **a cow and a river washed it away,** the robber is **liable to provide** the owner with another **cow;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Elazar. And the Rabbis say:** The robber can **say to** the owner: **That which is yours is before you,** and he would consequently be exempt. The Gemara asks: **There,** in the *baraita*, **with regard to what do they disagree?**

A *baraita* seems to complicate the picture: *ha-gozel sus parah ve-shatafa nahara chayav le-ha'amid lo parah divrei Rabbi Elazar* — Rabbi Elazar says that if one steals a cow and it is swamped, he must replace it, while the Sages again say *harei shelcha lefanecha*. But this appears to be a dispute about an animal, not about land. How does it fit the framework of *karka ein nigzelet*?

### Rav Papa Land Seizure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָתָם בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁגָּזַל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וְהָיְתָה פָּרָה רְבוּצָה בּוֹ, וּשְׁטָפָהּ נָהָר; דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְטַעְמֵיהּ, וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: There, with what are we dealing?** We are dealing with a case **where he** had **robbed another of a field, and there was a prone cow in it, and a river washed it** away. **Rabbi Elazar** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning,** as he maintains that land is included in the category of items that are not subject to the *halakhot* of robbery. Consequently, the robber acquires the land and simultaneously becomes liable to return it. Since he acquires the land, he acquires the cow that is on the land as well, and is liable to compensate its owner when it gets washed away by the river. **And the Rabbis** conform **to their** standard line of **reasoning,** as they hold that land was excluded by the verse. Therefore, the robber does not acquire the land and also does not acquire the cow.

Rav Papa resolves this: *kegon she-gazal sadeh me-chaveiro u-parah revutza bah* — the case involves one who seized a field from its owner and expelled him, while a cow was resting on that field. The thief never physically picked up the cow; he simply seized the land. The dispute thus turns entirely on whether *karka* is considered *nigzelet*. If, as Rabbi Elazar holds, one can *halakhically* steal land — meaning the act of seizure imposes upon the thief a degree of quasi-ownership — then, since a chattel found on one's land can be acquired through the land itself (*kinyan agav karka*), the thief is considered to have acquired the cow through his seizure of the field, and with that acquisition comes liability. The Sages, who hold *karka eina nigzelet* and therefore deny that any halakhic acquisition occurs through the seizure, deny that the cow was acquired at all, and therefore the thief has no liability for it. *Rabbi Elazar le-ta'ameih ve-rabanan le-ta'ameihu* — each opinion follows its own reasoning consistently.

## Return Location Rules

### Mishna Return Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ אוֹ שֶׁהִלְוָה הֵימֶנּוּ אוֹ שֶׁהִפְקִיד לוֹ, בַּיִּשּׁוּב – לֹא יַחֲזִיר לוֹ בַּמִּדְבָּר. עַל מְנָת לָצֵאת בַּמִּדְבָּר – יַחֲזִיר לוֹ בַּמִּדְבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who robs another or** who **borrowed** money **from him, or** one **with whom** another had **deposited** an item, if any of these interactions took place **in a settled area, he may not return** the item **to him in an unsettled area,** where it is of little benefit to the owner and he cannot safeguard it. If the loan or deposit was given **on the condition** that the recipient **may go out** and return it to the owner **in an unsettled area, he may return** it **to him in an unsettled area.**

The *mishna* addresses the location of return: *ha-gozel et chaveiro o she-hilva heimennu o she-hifkid lo be-yishuv* — one who stole from, borrowed from, or received an object in trust from his fellow in a settled area (*be-yishuv*). *Lo yachzir lo ba-midbar* — he may not return it to him in the wilderness. However, *al menat la-tzet ba-midbar*, if he stipulates at the outset that he intends to travel to the wilderness and may return it there, and the owner agrees, then *yachzir lo ba-midbar* — he may. One cannot of course stipulate when stealing, but this is relevant to the loan and *pikadon* cases.

### Milve vs. Aveidah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהוּ: מִלְוָה מִשְׁתַּלֶּמֶת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, אֲבֵידָה וּפִקָּדוֹן אֵין מִשְׁתַּלְּמִין אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹמָן! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִלְוָה נִיתְּנָה לִיתָּבַע בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, אֲבֵידָה וּפִקָּדוֹן לֹא נִיתְּנוּ לִיתָּבַע אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹמָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara **raises a contradiction** to the mishna from a *baraita*: **A loan may be repaid in any location,** while **a lost item and a deposit are returned only in their location,** i.e., the same type of place, settled or unsettled, where they were found or received. **Abaye said: This** is what the *baraita* **is saying: A loan may be claimed** by the creditor **in any location.** Yet, if the borrower initiates the process of repaying the loan in an unsettled area, the creditor may refuse to accept the repayment until they reach a settled area. Conversely, **a lost item and a deposit may be claimed only in their location.**

The *gemara* raises a contradiction: *milve mi-shtalemet be-chol makom* — a loan can be repaid anywhere; but *aveida u-pikadon ein mishtalmin ella bi-mekomam* — a lost object or an entrusted object can only be returned in their proper place. Does this mean that if a lost object was found in the wilderness and the owner lives in the city, it must be returned in the wilderness? No — the phrase means returned where it properly belongs. But the tension remains: may the *milve* be repaid in the middle of the wilderness?

Abaye clarifies: *milve nitenet li-tava be-chol makom* — a loan can be *demanded* anywhere. The creditor who encounters his debtor in the wilderness may demand payment on the spot. He has the right to ask, even if the debtor has nothing to give. But the debtor cannot *force* the creditor to accept repayment in the wilderness if the creditor prefers to receive it in town and during normal circumstances. An *aveida* or *pikadon*, by contrast, being a concrete object rather than a fungible debt, cannot be demanded in an inappropriate location; *aveida u-pikadon lo nitnu li-tava ella bi-mekomam*. The distinction is between the right to make a demand (*li-tava*) and the right to force acceptance of a return.

### Implicit Stipulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת לָצֵאת בַּמִּדְבָּר. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: "לֶיהֱוֵי הַאי פִּקָּדוֹן גַּבָּךְ, דַּאֲנָא לְמִדְבָּר נָפֵיקְנָא", וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ אִיהוּ: "אֲנָא לְמִדְבָּר נָמֵי בָּעֵינָא לְמִיפַּק. אִי בָּעֵינָא לְאַהְדֹּרִינְהוּ לָךְ, הָתָם מַהְדַּרְנָא לָךְ".
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if the loan or deposit was given **on the condition** that the recipient **may go out** and return it to the owner **in an unsettled area,** he may do so. The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that an explicit stipulation to this effect is binding? The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary, as** the mishna is discussing a case **where** the depositor **said to** the bailee: **Let this deposit be with you, as I am going out to an unsettled area, and** the bailee **said to him: I also need to go out to an unsettled area,** so **if I need to return** the deposit **to you there,** I will **return it to you** there.

As for the stipulation allowing wilderness return: the *gemara* observes this would be obvious if the agreement were explicit, and therefore the novelty lies in a subtler case. The owner says: watch this object — *de-le-midbar nafikna* — because I am going out to the wilderness. The guardian replies: *ana nami le-midbar ba'ina le-mifak* — I also may be heading out to the wilderness; *adreina lach* — I will return it to you if I happen to meet you out there. The owner makes no explicit affirmative response, but neither does he object. The *gemara* treats this as a case of implicit stipulation, since the owner's evident reason for the bailment was that he would be in the wilderness and presumably understood the possibility of return there.

## Admission of Liability

### Admit but Uncertain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: "גְּזַלְתִּיךָ"; "הִלְוִיתַנִי"; "הִפְקַדְתָּ אֶצְלִי, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם הֶחְזַרְתִּי לָךְ אִם לֹא הֶחְזַרְתִּי לָךְ" – חַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם. אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: "אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם גְּזַלְתִּיךָ"; "אִם הִלְוִיתַנִי"; "אִם הִפְקַדְתָּ אֶצְלִי" – פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who says to another: I robbed you,** or: **You lent me** money, or: **You deposited** an item **with me, and I do not know if I returned** your property **to you** or **if I did not return** it **to you,** he is **liable to pay** the sum or item in question. **But if he said to him: I do not know if I robbed you,** or: I do not know **if you lent me money,** or: I do not know **if you deposited** an item **with me,** he is **exempt from paying** the sum or item in question.

The *mishna* presents the following case: *ha-omer le-chaveiro gezalticha hilvitani hifkadta etzli ve-eini yodeia im hichzarti lach im lo hichzarti lach* — one who says to his fellow: I stole from you, or you lent me money, or you entrusted an object to me, and I do not know whether I ever returned it or paid it back. In such a case he is *chayav le-shalem*: he admits that he incurred liability, but cannot establish that he was ever discharged from it. By contrast, *im amar lo eini yodeia im gezalticha* — if he says he does not know whether he ever stole from the person or incurred the obligation in the first place, he is *patur mi-leshalem*.

### Bari ve-Shema Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר: "מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ", וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: "אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ" – רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמְרִי: חַיָּיב, וְרַב נַחְמָן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרִי: פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara cites a related dispute: It **was stated** that if one individual says to another: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other says: I do not know** whether or not this is true, **Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda say** that he is **liable** to pay the plaintiff, **and Rav Naḥman and Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that he is **exempt** from paying.

The *gemara* connects this to the classic dispute: *maneh li be-yadcha ve-halla omer eini yodeia* — Reuven says to Shimon, you owe me a hundred dollars, and Shimon responds, I don't know. Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda rule *chayav* on the principle of *bari ve-shema bari adif*: a definite claim prevails over a doubtful one, even though Shimon is *muchzak* — in possession of the money. Rav Nachman and Rabbi Yochanan rule *patur*: *ve-okei mamona be-chezkat marei* — we leave the money in the possession of its current holder, and a definite claim without evidence is insufficient to extract it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמְרִי חַיָּיב – בָּרִי וְשֶׁמָּא, בָּרִי עָדִיף. רַב נַחְמָן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרִי פָּטוּר – אוֹקִי מָמוֹנָא בְּחֶזְקַת מָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the reason for each opinion: **Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda say** that he is **liable** to pay, because when there is **a certain** claim **and an uncertain** claim, **the certain** claim is **superior. Rav Naḥman and Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that he is **exempt** from paying because of the principle: **Establish the money in the possession of its owner,** i.e., money is not taken from one's possession without clear proof that he is liable to pay it.

### Uncertain Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, אֲבָל אִם אָמַר לוֹ: "אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם הִלְוִיתַנִי" – פָּטוּר. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא קָא תָבַע לֵיהּ – רֵישָׁא נָמֵי דְּלָא קָא תָבַע לֵיהּ; אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? אֶלָּא דְּקָתָבַע לֵיהּ, וְקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: פָּטוּר מִלְּשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to derive a proof: **We learned** in the mishna: **But if he said to him: I do not know if you lent me money,** he is **exempt** from paying. **What are the circumstances? If we say** that the mishna is discussing a case where the lender **did not claim** the money from **him, the first clause** must **also** discuss a situation where the lender **did not claim** the money from him. If so, **why is** the borrower **liable** to pay in the case discussed in the first clause? **Rather,** the mishna must be discussing a case where the lender **did claim** the money from **him, and the latter clause** nevertheless **teaches** that the borrower is **exempt from paying,** even though his claim is uncertain and that of the lender is certain. This supports the opinion of Rav Naḥman and Rabbi Yoḥanan.

The *gemara* then asks how to situate our *mishna* within this debate: is a claim being made against the admitting party or not? If no claim is being made (*d'lo katva lei*), then even the first case of the *mishna* — I admit I stole but don't know if I returned it — should be *patur*, since no one is demanding payment. If a claim is being made, then even the second case — I don't know if I was ever liable — should be *chayav* according to the view that *bari adif*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; לְעוֹלָם דְּלָא קָא תָבַע לֵיהּ, וְרֵישָׁא – בְּבָא לָצֵאת יְדֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No, actually** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the lender **did not claim** the money from **him, and the first clause** is discussing a case **where** the borrower **comes to fulfill** his obligation **to Heaven.**

The *gemara* resolves this: the *mishna* consistently speaks in a situation where no formal claim is being made. The party is coming forward voluntarily. In the first case — I know I was liable but don't know if I was ever discharged — the ruling of *chayav* is not a legal compulsion but an expression of moral obligation: *reisha le-tzet yedei shamayim*. He wants to do the right thing, and the right thing is to pay. In the second case — I don't even know if I incurred liability — even the moral obligation does not apply, since one cannot be required, even morally, to pay for a debt whose very existence is uncertain.

### Tzeat Yedei Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ "מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ", וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר "אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ" – חַיָּיב, בְּבָא לָצֵאת יְדֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that it **was also stated: Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of **one who says to another: I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other** individual **says: I do not know** whether or not this is true, the latter is **liable** to pay. This applies **where he comes to fulfill** his obligation **to Heaven** even though he cannot be forced to pay.

This introduces an important distinction between categories. *Patur be-dinei adam ve-chayav be-dinei shamayim* — exempt by human law but obligated by divine law — is a concept the *gemara* invokes at several points throughout Bava Kamma. But *latzet yedei shamayim* is not quite the same as *lifnim mi-shurat ha-din*. *Lifnim mi-shurat ha-din* represents going beyond the letter of the law, an act of exceptional piety or generosity. *Latzet yedei shamayim* is doing what is morally right even when the law does not compel it — occupying the space between legal exemption and ethical obligation. The question of whether one who fails to act *le-tzet yedei shamayim* is actually *chayav bi-dei shamayim* is left open.

This is confirmed by the parallel statement of Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ha-omer le-chaveiro maneh li be-yadecha ve-halla omer eini yodeia chayav latzet yedei shamayim* — the same Rabbi Yochanan who holds *patur* in the formal legal dispute (*bari ve-shema, bari lo adif*, the *chazakah* of possession controls) nonetheless rules that the respondent is *chayav latzet yedei shamayim*. If one wants to act ethically, one must pay the person who made a definite claim against him, even though the law would leave the money in his pocket.

## Returning Stolen Animal

### Sheep Returned Dies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹנֵב טָלֶה מִן הָעֵדֶר וְהֶחְזִירוֹ, וּמֵת אוֹ נִגְנַב – חַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ. לֹא יָדְעוּ בְּעָלִים לֹא בִּגְנֵיבָתוֹ וְלֹא בַּחֲזִירָתוֹ, וּמָנוּ אֶת הַצֹּאן וּשְׁלֵימָה הִיא – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who stole a lamb from a flock and returned it** without informing the owner that he had done so, **and** then **it died or was stolen,** the thief is **liable** to pay **restitution** for **it.** If **the** lamb's **owners did not know** about the entire incident, i.e., they did **not** know **that it was stolen and** they did **not** know **that it was returned, and they counted the** flock of **sheep and** found **it whole,** the thief is **exempt** from paying.

The *mishna* establishes: *ha-gonav taleh min ha-tzon ve-hechziro* — one who steals a sheep from the flock and secretly returns it, after which it dies or is stolen again — *chayav be-achrayuto*: he has not fulfilled his obligation and remains liable. The owner was not aware of either the theft or the return. However, if *lo yadu ba'alim lo bi-gnevato ve-lo be-hachzirato* — the owners knew of neither the theft nor the return — and *u-manu et ha-tzon* — they counted the sheep and the number was complete — the thief is *patur*.

### Rava: Da'at vs. Minyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: לְדַעַת – צָרִיךְ דַּעַת. שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת – מִנְיָן פּוֹטֵר. וְכִי קָתָנֵי "וּמָנוּ אֶת הַצֹּאן וְהִיא שְׁלֵימָה" – אַסֵּיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara presents several ways to understand the mishna: **Rav says:** In a case where the owners **had knowledge** of the fact that their lamb was stolen, the thief is **required** to return it with the **knowledge** of the owners in order to be exempt from liability if the lamb is subsequently harmed. In a case where the owners **had no knowledge** that the lamb was taken, their **count** of the flock after the thief returned it **exempts** the thief from further liability. **And when** the mishna **teaches: And they counted the** flock of **sheep and** found **it whole,** it is referring specifically **to the latter clause,** i.e., the case where the owners were unaware that the lamb was stolen, as it is specifically in that case that the count exempts the thief from liability.

Rava reads the *mishna* straightforwardly: *lada'at tzarich da'at* — if stolen knowingly, the return requires explicit *da'at* of the owner. *Shelo lada'at minyan poter* — if stolen without the owner's awareness, counting (*minyan*) suffices. The phrase *u-manu et ha-tzon* in the *mishna* accordingly belongs to the final clause dealing with the *shelo lada'at* scenario.

### Shmuel: Always Minyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בֵּין לְדַעַת בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת – מִנְיָן פּוֹטֵר, וְכִי קָתָנֵי: "וּמָנוּ וְהִיא שְׁלֵימָה, פָּטוּר" – אַכּוּלַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: Whether** the owners **had knowledge** of the theft **or whether** they did **not have knowledge** of it, their **count** of the flock **exempts** the thief from payment. **And when** the mishna **teaches: And they counted** the flock of sheep **and** found **it whole,** the thief is **exempt,** it is referring **to the entire** mishna, as this exempts the thief from liability in both cases mentioned in the mishna.

Shmuel takes a different position: *bein lada'at bein shelo lada'at minyan poter* — counting always suffices. The owner's awareness of the counting exempts the thief regardless of whether the owner knew of the theft. This reading imposes significant strain on the *mishna*, since the *mishna* seems to clearly distinguish cases, but Shmuel's principle is that *minyan* represents adequate knowledge in all circumstances.

### R. Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: לְדַעַת – מִנְיָן פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת – אֲפִילּוּ מִנְיָן נָמֵי לָא צְרִיךְ. וְכִי קָתָנֵי: "וּמָנוּ אֶת הַצֹּאן וְהִיא שְׁלֵימָה" – אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In a case where the owners **had knowledge** of the theft, their **count** of the flock **exempts** the thief, and in a case where the owners **had no knowledge** of the theft, **even counting is not required** for the thief to be exempt. **And when** the mishna **teaches: And they counted the** flock of **sheep and** found **it whole,** it is referring specifically **to the first clause** of the mishna, as it is particularly in that case that the count is relevant.

Rabbi Yochanan shifts the entire framework one step down: *lada'at minyan poter* — when the owner knew of the theft, counting suffices to establish that the animal is back. *Shelo lada'at afilu minyan nami lo tzarich* — if the owner was entirely oblivious, not even counting is required; the thief's quiet return of the animal to where he took it from is sufficient. The *mishna*'s phrase about counting therefore belongs, on this reading, to the first case (*risha*) — the *lada'at* scenario — and in the *shelo lada'at* case, even less is required.

### R. Chisda Reversed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: לְדַעַת – מִנְיָן פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת – צָרִיךְ דַּעַת. וְכִי קָתָנֵי: "וּמָנוּ אֶת הַצֹּאן וְהִיא שְׁלֵימָה" – אַרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says:** In a case where the owners **had knowledge** of the theft, their **count** of the flock **exempts** the thief. In a case where the owners **had no knowledge** of the theft, the thief is **required** to return the lamb with their **knowledge** in order to become exempt from liability for future damage. **And when** the mishna **teaches: And they counted the** flock of **sheep and** found **it whole,** it is referring specifically **to the first clause** of the mishna, where the owners were aware of the theft, as it is only in that case that counting the flock is sufficient to exempt the thief from further liability.

Rav Chisda takes the position that is most counterintuitive: *lada'at minyan poter* — if the owner knew of the theft, counting suffices. *Shelo lada'at tzarich da'at* — if the owner did not know of the theft, explicit *da'at* of the return is required. This inverts the apparent logic: one would expect that the higher the owner's awareness of the wrong, the more demanding the standard for correction. Yet Rav Chisda holds that it is specifically when the owner was unaware of the theft that more is required.

### Animal Neglect Justifi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא? הוֹאִיל וְאַנְקְטַהּ נִגְרֵי בָּרָיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: What is the reasoning of Rav Ḥisda,** who holds that the requirement to return the animal with the owner's knowledge is in the case where the owners did not know about the theft? **Since** the thief **taught** the lamb to take **steps outside,** i.e., to leave the owners' property, it will now require greater supervision, which the owners can provide only if they are aware of the situation.

The *gemara* explains Rav Chisda's reasoning: *ho'il ve-anketina nagrei baraita* — since the theft itself has encouraged the animal to stray (*chinuch ha-lahal*). A stolen animal that has been removed from the flock once is now more likely to wander off on its own. The owner needs to know that this particular animal was taken away, because if he is unaware, he will not take extra precautions to supervise it. Therefore, the thief is not discharged of responsibility until he informs the owner — not merely as a matter of acknowledging wrongdoing, but to ensure that the owner can now protect the animal properly. When the owner knew of the theft and sees through the counting that his flock is complete, he implicitly draws the conclusion that the missing animal has returned and will naturally be more alert to its welfare. But when he never knew, the mere counting does not trigger this heightened awareness.

## Tanaim: Return Methods

### R. Yishmael Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַגּוֹנֵב טָלֶה מִן הָעֵדֶר, וְסֶלַע מִן הַכִּיס – לִמְקוֹם שֶׁגָּנַב יַחֲזִיר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: צָרִיךְ דַּעַת בְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the amoraic dispute cited previously is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: **One who steals a lamb from a flock or a *sela* from a purse** must **return** it **to the place** from where **he stole** it; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says:** The thief is **required** to return it with the **knowledge of** its **owners.**

The *gemara* asks whether the *amora'im*'s debate maps onto an earlier *tanna'im* dispute. *Ha-ganav taleh min ha-eder ve-sela min ha-kis* — one who steals a sheep from the flock or money from a wallet. Rabbi Yishmael rules: *le-makom she-ganav yachzir* — return it to where you took it from, and that suffices.

### R. Akiva: Inform Owner

Rabbi Akiva rules: *tzarich da'at ba'alim* — the owner must be made aware. The *gemara* assumes that all opinions agree with the principle of Rabbi Yitzchak — *adam asui le-mashmesh be-kiso be-chol sha'ah* — a person is constantly checking his wallet. The wallet case is therefore inherently one of *lada'at*: because the owner always monitors his funds, any theft is quickly discovered. The debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva about the wallet is thus a debate about whether *minyan* alone suffices when the theft is *lada'at*, or whether explicit *da'at* of the return is also required. This corresponds to the *amora'im*'s dispute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: אָדָם עָשׂוּי לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּכִיסוֹ בְּכׇל שָׁעָה. מַאי, לָאו בְּסֶלַע לְדַעַת – וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages discussing the matter **assumed that everyone accepts** the opinion **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: A person is prone to feel his purse constantly,** and therefore becomes aware of the theft soon after it takes place. **What, is it not** a case **where a *sela*** was stolen with the owner's **knowledge, and** the dispute of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is parallel **to the dispute of Rav and Shmuel?**

## Tanaim: Return Methods (Continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; בְּטָלֶה שֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת, וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** they disagree **in** the case of **a lamb that** was stolen **without the knowledge** of its owners, i.e., they did not know that it was stolen, **and** the dispute of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva is parallel **to the dispute of Rav Ḥisda and Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Rav Ḥisda rules in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva that the lamb must be returned with the owner's knowledge, and Rabbi Yoḥanan rules in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael that the owners do not need to become aware of the lamb's return.

### Wallet Case Analysis

The flock case, by contrast, involves a scenario of *shelo lada'at*: owners of large flocks do not immediately notice when an animal goes missing. The debate there — whether simply returning the animal to the flock suffices, or whether the owner must be explicitly informed — corresponds to the dispute between Rav Chisda and Rabbi Yochanan about the *shelo lada'at* scenario.

### Shomer Took Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: בְּשׁוֹמֵר שֶׁגָּנַב מֵרְשׁוּת בְּעָלִים – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא; וְהָכָא בְּשׁוֹמֵר שֶׁגָּנַב מֵרְשׁוּתוֹ, שֶׁיַּחֲזִיר לִמְקוֹם שֶׁגָּנַב קָמִיפַּלְגִי – רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: כָּלְתָה לוֹ שְׁמִירָתוֹ, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל סָבַר: לֹא כָּלְתָה לוֹ שְׁמִירָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said in the name of Rava: With regard to a bailee who stole** a lamb **from the owner's domain, everyone,** including Rabbi Yishmael, **agrees** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ḥisda,** and the owner must be aware of the animal's return in order for the thief to become exempt from further liability. **And here it is with regard to** whether **a bailee who stole from** within **his** own **domain may return** what he stole **to the place from** where **he stole** it that **they disagree. Rabbi Akiva holds: His safeguarding was terminated** when he stole the lamb and he must therefore return it with the owner's knowledge. **And Rabbi Yishmael holds: His safeguarding was not terminated** and he may return it to his own domain.

Rav Zavid in the name of Rava introduces a further refinement. If a *shomer* appropriated the object he was entrusted to guard — not by removing it from the owner's premises but by deciding internally to keep it — the question arises of whether his *shmirah* has lapsed. Rabbi Akiva holds *kalu lo shmirato*: the moment he mentally appropriated it and removed it from the agreed storage location, even briefly, he ceased to be a *shomer*. Returning it to the original location is therefore a return *shelo lada'at*, and according to Rabbi Akiva (following the principle of Rav Chisda), explicit *da'at* of the owner is required. Rabbi Yishmael holds *lo kalu lo shmirato*: since the *shomer* never removed it from the owner's purview in any meaningful sense, he is still acting as the owner's representative, and returning the object to the location where he was watching it is considered a return *lada'at* — he is, in effect, returning it to himself in his capacity as guardian.

### Overpaying Repayment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מִנְיָן פּוֹטֵר – תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וְהִבְלִיעַ לוֹ בְּחֶשְׁבּוֹן – תָּנֵי חֲדָא: יָצָא, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let** us **say** that with regard to the dispute over whether the owner's **counting** of his flock **exempts** a thief who returned a stolen lamb without notifying the owner, it **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who robbed another and** then **included** the money he owed **him** for the robbery **in the calculation** of the payment for a different transaction, i.e., he compensated the owner by overpaying him for a different transaction, **it is taught** in **one** *baraita*: **He** has **fulfilled** his obligation to pay, **and it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **He** has **not fulfilled** his obligation to pay.

The *gemara* turns to a further case: *ha-gozel et chaveiro ve-hivlio lo be-cheshbon* — one who stole from his fellow and, feeling guilty but unwilling to confess, overpays him when making a purchase, embedding the stolen amount in the payment. One *baraita* says *yatza* — this constitutes valid return. Another *baraita* says *lo yatza* — it does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם עָשׂוּי לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בְּכִיסוֹ בְּכׇל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה. מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי – דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר יָצָא, סָבַר: מִנְיָן פּוֹטֵר; וּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא יָצָא, סָבַר: מִנְיָן אֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Sages discussing the matter **assumed that everyone accepts** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says: A person is prone to feel his purse constantly,** and therefore becomes aware that he has been compensated soon after the payment is made. **What, is it not with regard to this** following point that **they disagree: The one who says** that the robber has **fulfilled** his obligation **holds** that the **counting** of the property **exempts** a robber from further liability, **and the one who says** that the robber has **not fulfilled** his obligation **holds** that the **counting does not exempt** a robber from further liability.

The *gemara* initially assumes that both *baraitot* agree with Rabbi Yitzchak's principle that one always checks one's wallet, and that overpaying would therefore quickly be noticed — analogous to *minyan*. The debate would then be whether *minyan* alone is sufficient to discharge the obligation (*yatza*) or whether explicit *da'at* of the return is required (*lo yatza*). The *gemara* then offers an alternative: perhaps the debate is actually about whether people always check their wallets (*d'Rabbi Yitzchak*). One opinion holds they do — *minyan* is therefore achieved quickly and suffices. The other holds they do not always do so, and therefore there is no reliable *minyan* here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: אִי סְבִירָא לַן כְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּמִנְיָן פּוֹטֵר; אֶלָּא בִּדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק קָמִיפַּלְגִי – מָר אִית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, וּמָר לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: One can **say** that **if we held in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Yitzḥak,** it would follow that **everyone agrees that** the **counting** of the property **exempts** a robber from further liability. **Rather,** it is **with regard to** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak** itself that **they disagree:** One **Sage accepts** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, and** one **Sage does not accept** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דִּמְנִי וּרְמָא לֵיהּ בְּכִיסֵיהּ, וְהָא דִּמְנִי וּרְמָא לִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that **everyone accepts** the statement **of Rabbi Yitzḥak, and** it **is not difficult: This** first *baraita*, which rules that the robber fulfilled his obligation, is discussing a case **where** the robber **counted** the money **and placed it** directly **in** the owner's **purse,** which the owner will soon open and count. **And that** other *baraita*, which rules that the robber did not fulfill his obligation, is discussing a case **where he counted** the money **and placed it in** the owner's **hand.** Consequently, it is possible that the owner will put the money directly into a container with other coins in it and will never realize that he was compensated for the theft.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דִּמְנִי וּרְמָא בְּכִיסֵיהּ; הָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ זוּזֵי אַחֲרִינֵי בְּכִיסֵיהּ, הָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ זוּזֵי אַחֲרִינֵי בְּכִיסֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are discussing a case **where** the robber **counted** the money **and placed it in** the owner's **purse. This** *baraita* discusses a case **where he has other dinars in his purse,** but he did not know how many. Therefore, when he counts the money, he will not realize that the robber inserted an additional sum. Whereas **that** *baraita* is discussing a case **where he does not have other dinars in his purse,** so that when he counts the money he will realize that an additional sum has been included in the payment.

A final alternative distinguishes by circumstance: *ha d'mani ve-ramayei be-kiseih* — where the repayer counted out the money and placed it directly into the recipient's wallet, the recipient will notice the discrepancy and achieve *minyan*. *Ve-ha d'manyei ve-ramayei be-yadeih* — where the money was counted and placed into the recipient's hand, the recipient will simply pocket it without recounting, and *minyan* will not occur. Thus even under the universally-accepted principle of Rabbi Yitzchak, specific circumstances may prevent the *de facto* *minyan* that the principle assumes.

## Buying from Shepherds

### Prohibited Purchases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִן הָרוֹעִים – צֶמֶר וְחָלָב וּגְדָיִים, וְלֹא מִשּׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת – עֵצִים וּפֵירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not purchase wool, milk, and kids from the shepherds** who tend the flocks of others, due to the concern that they have stolen these items from the owners of the flocks. **And** similarly, one may **not** purchase **wood and produce from produce watchmen.**

The *mishna* rules: *ein lokhin min ha-ro'im tzemar ve-chalav u-gediyim* — one may not purchase from shepherds wool, milk, or kids. Similarly, one may not purchase from produce-watchmen (*shomrei peirot*) wood or fruit. These are items the shepherds and watchmen were entrusted to guard on behalf of the owners; purchasing from them creates the presumption of theft and constitutes enabling it.

### Permitted from Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מִן הַנָּשִׁים – כְּלֵי צֶמֶר בִּיהוּדָה, וּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן בַּגָּלִיל, וַעֲגָלִים בַּשָּׁרוֹן. וְכוּלָּן שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְהַטְמִין – אָסוּר. וְלוֹקְחִין בֵּיצִים וְתַרְנְגוֹלִין מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **But one may purchase from women woolen goods in Judea, and linen goods in the Galilee, and calves in the Sharon,** as women in these locations often work with those commodities and it can be assumed that they are selling the items with the owner's consent. **And** with regard to **all these** items, in a case **where** the seller **told** the buyer **to conceal** the purchase, **it is prohibited,** as there is good reason to suspect that the items are stolen. **And one may purchase eggs and chickens from everywhere,** as it is unlikely that one would steal and sell these commodities.

However, *aval lokhin min ha-nashim klei tzemar bi'Yehudah u-klei pishtan ba-Galil* — from women one may purchase wool garments in Judea and linen garments in the Galilee. This reflects the established local practice: in Judea, women maintained wool-working businesses; in the Galilee, linen was the norm. Though the ordinary presumption might be that a married woman lacks independent authority to sell her husband's property, these were recognized industries conducted by women either on their own account or as their husbands' agents, and there was therefore no suspicion. Similarly, *agalim ba-Sharon* — calves could be purchased in the Sharon region, where this was an accepted trade.

### Transformed Goods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין לוֹקְחִין מִן הָרוֹעִים לֹא עִזִּים וְלֹא גְּדָיִים וְלֹא גִּיזִּין וְלֹא תְּלוּשִׁין שֶׁל צֶמֶר, אֲבָל לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן תְּפוּרִין – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן שֶׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 11:9): **One may purchase from shepherds neither goats, nor kids, nor fleeces, nor torn** pieces **of wool, but one may purchase sewn** garments **from them, because they are** presumed to be **theirs.** Since the act of weaving constitutes a *shinui* (transformation), which halakhically transfers ownership to the craftsman even if the raw materials were stolen, the final product is presumed to belong to the person offering it for sale.

The *baraita* elaborates: one may not purchase from shepherds goats, kids, shorn wool, or plucked wool. However, *aval lokhin mehem tefurim* — woven garments may be purchased, *mipnei she-hen shelahem* — because even if the raw material was stolen, the act of weaving constitutes a *shinui* (transformation), which halakhically transfers ownership to the thief. This is noted as an interesting proposition, since purchasing goods acquired through another's transformation indirectly encourages theft — a separate conversation — but the ruling stands.

### Milk & Cheese Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן חָלָב וּגְבִינָה בַּמִּדְבָּר, וְלֹא בַּיִּשּׁוּב.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may purchase milk and cheese from them in an unsettled area, but not in a settled area.**

*Ve-lokhin mehem chalav u-gevina ba-midbar ve-lo ba-yishuv* — milk and cheese may be purchased from shepherds in the wilderness but not in the town. The apparent logic: in the wilderness, owners likely give shepherds greater latitude to sell dairy products, since they cannot easily market them directly. Alternatively, in the town the owner himself would be the natural vendor, so a shepherd selling dairy products there raises suspicion. One might add the practical consideration that milk spoils quickly — in the wilderness, the shepherd may have no reasonable alternative.

### Quantity Thresholds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ צֹאן אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ גִּיזִּין; אֲבָל לֹא שְׁתֵּי צֹאן וְלֹא שְׁתֵּי גִיזִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may purchase from them four or five sheep** or **four or five fleeces** at a time, because it is unlikely that a shepherd would risk stealing such a significant quantity at once. **But** one may **not** purchase **two sheep, and** similarly, one may **not** purchase **two fleeces** at a time, as it is reasonable to assume that the shepherd would attempt to steal this amount from the owner.

*Ve-lokhin mehem arba tzon arba gizin* — one may purchase four sheep or four bundles of wool. *Aval lo shtei tzon ve-lo shtei gizin* — but not two. The principle is that a large quantity will inevitably be noticed by the owner, and therefore if the shepherd is selling a large amount, he must have received authorization. Two animals, by contrast, could go unnoticed — explained away as a natural loss or a stray.

### House vs. Wilderness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בַּיָּיתוֹת לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן, מִדְבָּרִיּוֹת אֵין לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says: One may purchase domesticated** animals **from them,** as it is unlikely that the shepherds would steal them from their owner, who would notice if they did not return home. Conversely, **one may not purchase desert,** i.e., non-domesticated, animals **from them,** as it is more likely that a shepherd would steal these animals.

Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes by type of animal: *bayitot lokhin mehem* — house animals may be purchased, *bariot ein lokhin mehem* — wilderness animals may not. House animals are kept close to the owner and their absence is immediately noticed, so a shepherd selling them must have authorization. Animals kept in distant pastures can disappear without the owner's awareness, and their sale by the shepherd is therefore suspect.

### Owner Awareness Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: כֹּל שֶׁהָרוֹעֶה מוֹכֵר וּבַעַל הַבַּיִת מַרְגִּישׁ בּוֹ – לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן, אֵין מַרְגִּישׁ בּוֹ – אֵין לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states the general principle: **The principle of the matter** is that with regard to **anything that the shepherd sells and the owner** would **perceive its** absence if it were stolen, **one may purchase** it **from them.** But if the owner **would not perceive its** absence, one **may not purchase** it **from them.**

The *mishna* states the general principle: *kol she-ha-ro'eh moker u-va'al ha-bayit margish bo lokhin mehem* — anything whose sale would be noticed by the owner may be purchased from the shepherd, since the owner's implicit awareness guarantees authorization or at least accountability. *Kol she-ein margish bo ein lokhin mehem* — anything the owner would not notice is missing may not be purchased.

### Rav Chisda Numbers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶן אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ צֹאן, אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ גִּיזִּין. הַשְׁתָּא יֵשׁ לוֹמַר אַרְבַּע זָבְנִינַן, חָמֵשׁ מִיבַּעְיָא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אַרְבַּע מִתּוֹךְ חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines the *baraita*: **The Master said** that **one may purchase from them four or five sheep** or **four or five fleeces** at a time. **Now** that **it can be said** that **we** may **buy four** sheep, as it is unlikely that the shepherd would steal such a large quantity at one time, **is** it **necessary** to state that we may buy **five?** The Gemara answers that **Rav Ḥisda said:** The meaning of the *baraita* is that one may purchase **four** sheep **out of** a flock consisting of **five** sheep, as it is reasonable to assume that the shepherd would not attempt to steal four-fifths of the owner's flock.

The *gemara* notes an apparent redundancy in the ruling: if four sheep may be purchased, five certainly may be purchased, since the larger the quantity the more conspicuous the transaction. The *gemara* therefore cites Rav Chisda's significant limitation: *arba mitoch hamisha* — four sheep out of a flock of five. The ruling is not a general permission to buy four sheep; it means one may purchase four when that represents eighty percent of the flock, a quantity so conspicuous that authorization is virtually certain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אַרְבַּע מִתּוֹךְ קָטָן וְחָמֵשׁ מִתּוֹךְ גָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that **Rav Ḥisda said** as follows: **Four** sheep may be purchased **from** the shepherd of **a small flock, and five** may be purchased **from** the shepherd of **a large flock.**

A second version of Rav Chisda's ruling, however, is less restrictive: *arba'a mitoch katan ve-chamisha mitoch gadol* — four from a small flock, five from a large one, without requiring the eighty-percent ratio.

### Healthy vs. Scrawny

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא – אָמְרַתְּ: "אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ צֹאן, אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ גִיזִּין", אַרְבַּע וְחָמֵשׁ – אִין, אֲבָל שָׁלֹשׁ – לָא; אֵימָא סֵיפָא: "אֲבָל לֹא שְׁתֵּי צֹאן" – הָא שָׁלֹשׁ זָבְנִינַן! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא בְּבָרְיָיתָא, הָא בִּכְחִישָׁתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question with regard to the *baraita*: **This matter itself is difficult,** as **you said** in the *baraita*: One may purchase **from them four or five sheep** or **four or five fleeces** at a time, which indicates that purchasing **four or five, yes,** it is permitted, **but** purchasing **three, no,** it is prohibited. **Say the latter clause: But** one may **not** purchase **two sheep.** This indicates **that we may purchase three** sheep at a time. The Gemara answers that this is **not difficult: This** clause, which permits the purchase of three sheep, is discussing a case **where** the sheep are **robust,** and therefore the owner is more attentive to them and would notice if three were missing. Conversely, **that** clause, which prohibits the purchase of three sheep, is discussing a case **where** the sheep are **frail.** Consequently, the owner does not care about them as much, and it is possible that he would not notice if only three of them went missing.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent inconsistency between the ruling permitting four and five and the ruling prohibiting two: the threshold is context-dependent. *Ha be-briata ha be-kachishata* — the case permitting purchase refers to healthy, robust animals, whose absence will be immediately noticed. The case prohibiting purchase of even three refers to scrawny animals, whose disappearance might easily go undetected. Whether a given quantity triggers the owner's awareness depends on the animals' condition and visibility. The *masechet* will continue from here tomorrow.
