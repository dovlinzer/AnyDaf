# Shekalim 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 13 Prostrations & Gates

### Intro: 13 Lists

The *mishna* on which we now focus appears in the context of a broader enumeration: thirteen *shofarot* — the containers used to collect donated funds — thirteen *shulchanot*, and thirteen *hishtachavayot*, prostrations. The *mishna* proceeds in a kind of inverted order, addressing these three topics in reverse sequence. Having just concluded the discussion of a fourteenth *hishtachavaya* and the spot where the *aron* was hidden, the *mishna* now returns to explain the thirteen prostrations, then the tables, and will eventually arrive at the *shofarot*. We thus follow a structure of ABC followed by CBA.

### Prostration Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: הֵיכָן הָיוּ הַהִשְׁתַּחֲוִיּוֹת הָאֵילּוּ. אַרְבַּע בַּצָּפוֹן וְאַרְבַּע בַּדָּרוֹם שָׁלשׁ בַּמִּזְרָח וּשְׁתַּיִם בַּמַּעֲרָב כְּנֶגֶד שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שְׁעָרִים
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: Where were these prostrations? Four in the North, four in the South, three in the East, and two in the West, corresponding to the thirteen gates.

A question arises naturally here: who precisely was performing these prostrations, and where were they standing when they did so? The *mishna* earlier mentions Chananya *segan ha-kohanim* as performing fourteen prostrations, and Rabban Gamliel is mentioned in that context as well. The ordinary *yisrael* would not have free access throughout the *azarah*, and access deeper into the compound was restricted to *kohanim*. The precise choreography of where one stood while performing the *hishtachavaya* remains, on the face of the *mishna*, unclear.

### South Gates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁעָרִים דְּרוֹמִיִּים סְמוּכִין לַמַּעֲרָב שַׁעַר הָעֶלְיוֹן שַׁעַר הַדֶּלֶק שַׁעַר הַבְּכוֹרוֹת שַׁעַר הַמָּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Southern gates close to the West: the Upper Gate, the Fuel Gate, the Firstborns' Gate, the Water gate.

Moving through the gates themselves, the *mishna* lists the southern gates from west to east — that is, beginning at the southwest corner: *Sha'ar ha-elyon*, the upper gate; *Sha'ar hadelek*, the gate of fuel or fire; *Sha'ar habechorot*, the gate of the firstborns; and *Sha'ar hamayim*, the gate of water.

According to the *Korban ha-edah*, *Sha'ar hadelek* was located opposite the *lishkat ha-etzim* — the chamber where the wood for the altar was stored — and the wood would be brought through there. *Sha'ar habechorot* was used because the *bechorot* were slaughtered in the south of the *azarah*. But a genuine difficulty presents itself: we normally understand that a *yisrael* bringing a *korban* would enter through *Sha'ar Nikanor* in the east, wait in the *ezrat Yisrael*, and the *kohen* would receive the animal and bring it slightly north of the *mizbeach* for *kodshei kodashim* or to the south for *bechorot*. The notion that an ordinary Israelite would enter through a side gate and hand off the animal directly to a *kohen* at that location is not the standard picture we carry. Perhaps one could suggest that the *yisrael* never fully entered the *azarah* but stood in the gateway itself; perhaps there was a narrow passage allowing movement around the courtyard's perimeter. The choreography remains genuinely puzzling.

Similarly, the *Sha'ar hadelek* is distinct from the *lishkat hadelek* — the chamber for firewood — which was located in the *ezrat nashim*. Why then a separate gate for firewood on the south wall of the *azarah* itself? These questions do not find easy resolution.

### Sha'ar Hamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ שַׁעַר הַמַּיִם שֶׁבּוֹ מַכְנִיסִין צְלוֹחִית שֶׁל מַיִם שֶׁל נִסּוּךְ חַמַּיִם בֶּחָג. רִבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר בּוֹ הַמַּיִם מְפַכִּים עֲתִידִין לִהְיוֹת יוֹצְאִין מִתַּחַת מִפְתַּן הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** And why was it called Water Gate? For there they bring in the phial of water for the water libation on Tabernacles. Rebbi Eliezer ben Jacob said, the water will drizzle in the future from under the threshold.

The *mishna* asks: *velamah nikra shemo Sha'ar hamayim?* — why was it called the gate of water? The answer: *shebo machnisim tzluchit shel mayim be-chag* — it is through this gate that they would bring in the flask of water for *nisuch hamayim* on *Sukkot*. The *mishna* in Tractate *Sukkah* describes the elaborate ceremony of *Simchat Beit ha-Shoevah* involving a procession through the *ezrat nashim*, yet here we have the water entering through a side gate in the south wall of the *azarah*. Once again, the exact topography of the ceremony does not resolve neatly.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov adds: *bo ha-mayim mifachim* — from there the waters will pour, like from a *pach*, a jug. *Ve-atidin lihyot yotzin mitachat miftan ha-bayit* — and they are destined to emerge from beneath the threshold of the *bayit*. This is an allusion to a *pasuk* in *Yechezkel* that the *gemara* will spend most of the rest of the *daf* elaborating, concerning a miraculous river that will flow forth from the *Beit Hamikdash le-atid lavo*.

### North Gates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוּמָּתָן בַּצָּפוֹן סְמוּכִין בַּמַּעֲרָב שַׁעַר יְכָנְיָה שַׁעַר הַקָּרְבָּן שַׁעַר הַנָּשִׁים שַׁעַר הַשִּׁיר. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ שַׁעַר יְכָנְיָה שֶׁבּוֹ יָצָא יְכָנְיָה בְּגָלוּתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Opposite them in the North, close to the West, the Jechoniah gate, the Sacrifice gate, the Women's gate, the Song's gate. And why was it called the Jechoniah gate? For through it Jechoniah left in his deportation.

From the south we turn to the northern gates, listed from northwest to southeast: *Sha'ar Yechanyah*, *Sha'ar hakorban*, *Sha'ar hanashim*, and *Sha'ar hashir*.

*Sha'ar Yechanyah* was named for king *Yechanyah*, who exited through it when he was taken into exile. Though the *bayit* was destroyed and rebuilt, the name was preserved — and the *gemara* will offer its own version of the events surrounding his departure.

*Sha'ar hakorban* is explained by the *Korban ha-edah* as the gate through which the *kodshei kodashim* were brought in, those offerings that required *shechitah* in the north. Again, the same topographical puzzle applies: does this mean that the owner of the *korban* would approach this side gate and present the animal there, while we normally assume entry through *Sha'ar Nikanor* and the animal being walked northward past the *mizbeach*? The *mishna* seems to indicate functional use for these lateral gates, but precisely how they were incorporated into the normal flow of *avodah* remains an open question.

*Sha'ar hanashim* is explained in the *Korban ha-edah* as the gate through which women would enter to perform *smicha* on their *korbanot*, or at least to accompany them — there being a well-known dispute about whether women perform *smicha* at all. Yet we generally understand that women entered through *Sha'ar Nikanor*, since the *ezrat nashim* was the outermost roofed courtyard accessible to them. A separate women's gate on the north wall of the *azarah* itself is an unusual datum.

*Sha'ar hashir* was presumably the gate adjacent to the chamber where the Levitical instruments were kept. The *gemara* also associates that area with the *Beit Hamoked*, where the *kohanim* would warm themselves by the fire kept burning there, and where a *lishka* of sorts was maintained.

### East Gates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבַּמִּזְרָח שַׁעַר נִיקָנוֹר וּשְׁנֵי פִשְׁפְּשִׁין הָיוּ לוֹ אֶחָד בִּימִינוֹ וְאֶחָד בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In the East the Nikanor gate, which had two small entrances, one to the right and one to the left.

In the east there was *Sha'ar Nikanor* — the great gate — and two smaller side gates, *pashpashim*, one on each side of it. These three together account for the three prostrations on the eastern side of the *azarah*. Significantly, the *mishna* here refers unmistakably to the main entrance to the *azarah* proper — the *sha'ar* through which *yisrael* would enter the *ezrat Yisrael* — which strongly suggests that the entire enumeration is indeed tracing a circuit of the *azarah* and not some outer precinct.

### West Gates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁנַיִם בַּמַּעֲרָב וְלֹא הָיָה לָהֶם שֵׁם:
>
> **Translation:** And two in the West which had no names.

Finally, behind the *Kodesh Kodashim*, on the western wall, were two gates — *u-shenayim ba-ma'arav lo hayah lahem shem* — and these two had no name at all. In the land of Israel, where virtually every stream and hilltop carries multiple names accumulated through layers of history, it is striking that the two westernmost gates of the holiest courtyard in the world went unnamed.

## Gemara: 13 vs 7

### Abba Yose Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: מַתְנִיתָה דְּאַבָּא יוֹסֵה בֶן חָנִין. דְּאַבָּא יוֹסֵה בֶן חָנִין אָמַר. כְּנֶגֶד שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שְׁעָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: The Mishnah is Abba Yose ben Ḥanin's, since Abba Yose ben Ḥanin said, corresponding to the thirteen gates.

The *gemara* opens by identifying whose view the *mishna* represents: *Abba Yose ben Yochanan hi de-amar k'neged yud-gimmel she'arin* — this is the position of Abba Yose ben Yochanan, who holds that the thirteen prostrations corresponded to thirteen gates. Whether *k'negedan* means literally "directly in front of" those gates or more loosely "corresponding to them in number" remains somewhat ambiguous — though the word *keneged* elsewhere in this very tractate implies physical positioning opposite a specific location.

### Rabbis Disagree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרַם כְּרַבָּנִן שִׁבְעָה שְׁעָרִים הָיוּ בָעֲזָרָה. עַל דָּעְתּוֹן דְּרָבָּנִן אֵיכָן הָיוּ הִשְׁתַּחֲוִיּוֹת הַלָלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** But according to the Rabbis there were seven gates in the *azarah*. In the Rabbis' opinion, where were the thirteen prostrations?

The Rabbis, however, dissent: *shiva she'arim hayu ba-azarah* — there were only seven gates in the *azarah*. The *gemara* specifies *ba-azarah*, which confirms that the entire discussion concerns the *azarah* proper. How one reduces thirteen to seven is not fully worked out, but possibilities include: not counting the two unnamed western gates, not counting the two side gates of *Sha'ar Nikanor*, and disputing the existence of certain others. The Rabbis thereby face the obvious question: if there were only seven gates, what accounts for thirteen prostrations?

### Breaches & Chashmonaim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּהִיא דְתַנִּינָן תַּמָּן. שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר פְרָצוֹת הָיוּ בוֹ שֶׁפְּרָצוּם מַלְכֵי יָוָון. חָזְרוּ וּגְדָרוּם וְגָזְרוּ כְּנֶגְדָּן שְׁלשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה הִשְׁתַּחֲוָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** As we have stated there, "There were thirteen breaches, were the Greek kings breached it; they came back and closed them and decided correspondingly thirteen prostrations."

The answer is historically charged: *yud-gimmel peratzot hayu bah she-peratzum malchei Yavan* — there were thirteen breaches made in the wall of the *azarah* by the Greek kings. *Ve-chazru u-gedarum benei Chashmonai* — the *Chashmonaim* came and repaired them. *U-begazru k'negdan yud-gimmel hishtachavuyot* — and they established, opposite those thirteen sites, thirteen prostrations.

The word *k'negdan* here may well mean physically in front of those repaired breaches. The symbolism is striking: prostrations specifically at the sites of the violation. Rather than simply forgetting the breaches, the *Chashmonaim* enshrined the memory of persecution and perseverance in the choreography of the *avodah* itself. The places that had been broken became the very spots at which one bowed in gratitude and awe.

## Living Waters

### Waters Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יֵצְאוּ מַיִם חַיִּים מִירוּשָׁלַם וגו׳.
>
> **Translation:** It is written, *on that day, fresh water will flow out from Jerusalem*, etc.

Because the *mishna* mentioned *Sha'ar hamayim* and introduced the phrase *bo ha-mayim mifachim* — the waters pour forth — the *gemara* now embarks on an extended treatment of the prophecy of water flowing from the *Beit Hamikdash*. The *pasuk* in *Zechariyah* states: *ve-hayah ba-yom ha-hu yetze'u mayim chayim mi-Yerushalayim* — on that day, living waters will flow out from Jerusalem. This sets the stage for the *gemara*'s interpretation of the extended vision in *Yechezkel*, which will occupy most of the remainder of the *daf*.

### Water Growth Pattern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. בֵּית קוֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים עַד הַפָּרָכוֹת כְּקַרְנֵי סִילַיי וּבִילַיי. מִן הַפָּרָכוֹת עַד מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב כְּקַרְנֵי חֲגָבִים. מִמִּזְבַּח הַזָּהָב וְעַד הָעֲזָרוֹת כְּחוּט שֶׁל שְׁיתִי. מִן הָעֲזָרוֹת וְעַד מִפְתַּן הַבַּיִת כְּחוּט שֶׁל עֶרֶב. מִיכָּן וָהֵילַךְ כְּמִפִּי הַפַּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: From the Holiest of Holies to the gobelins like the feelers of *silai* and *bilai*. From the gobelins to the golden altar like the horns of locusts. From the golden altar to the courtyards like a shut-thread, from the courtyards to the threshold of the Temple like woof-thread, from there onwards like the pouring from a jug.

A *baraita* is cited describing the progressive growth of the stream in extraordinary detail. The water originates in the *Kodesh Kodashim* itself and begins in the most minute quantities, growing larger and more powerful as it flows outward through successive zones of the *Beit Hamikdash*.

From the *Kodesh Kodashim* to the *parochet*, the stream is *ke-karnei silei ve-chilei* — like the antennae of small insects, specifically snails, the tiniest possible trickle. From the *parochet* to the golden altar — which stood in the *kodesh*, directly opposite the *menorah* and the *shulchan* — the stream grows to *ke-karnei chagavim*, like the antennae of grasshoppers, somewhat thicker. From the golden altar to the *azarah* (understood here to encompass the *ezrat nashim* as well, as the water is about to exit the structure), the stream is *ke-chut shel sheti*, like a thread of warp — the thinner of the two components of the weave. From the *azarah* to the *miftan ha-bayit*, the threshold, it becomes *ke-chut shel erev*, the woof, slightly thicker still. And from that point outward, *ke-fi ha-pach* — pouring like a jug. This is the very word, *mefachim*, used in the *mishna*.

The symbolism here runs counter to what one might expect. We normally think of *kedushah* as most concentrated at its source — the *Kodesh Kodashim* — and attenuating outward. But the stream of living water begins infinitesimally small at its origin and becomes a roaring river as it moves outward into the world. This is reminiscent of *kol demamah dakkah* — the still small voice — where the most profound divine presence arrives in the quietest form. Rivers in nature follow the same pattern: beginning from a small source and growing mightier as tributaries join.

### Yechezkel Vision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִנֵּה מַיִם מְפַכִּים מִן הַכָּתֵף הַיְמָנִית. כְּתִיב בְּצֵאת הָאִישׁ קָדִים וְקָו בְּיָדוֹ וַיָּמָד אֶלֶף בָּאַמָּה וַיַּעֲבִירֵנִי בַמַּיִם מֵי אָפְסָיִם. עַד קַרְסוּלֵהּ. וַיָּמָד אֶלֶף וַיַּעֲבִירֵנִי בַמַּיִם מַיִם בִּרְכָּיִם. עַד בִּרְכַּיָיא. וַיָּמָד אֶלֶף וַיַּעֲבִירֵנִי מֵי מָתְנָיִם. עַד מָתְנַיָיא. מִיכָּן וְהֵילַךְ וַיָּמָד אֶלֶף נַחַל אֲשֶׁר לֹא אוּכַל לַעֲבֹר.
>
> **Translation:** *behold, water drizzled from the right hand side*. It is written, *when the man left towards the East, with a measuring rod in his hand, he measured a thousand cubits and made me cross the water, ankle water*. Up to his ankles. *Then he measured another thousand cubits and made me cross the water, knee water.* Up to his knees. *Then he measured another thousand cubits and made me cross the water, hip water.* Up to his hips. From there on, *then he measured another thousand, a river which cannot be crossed.*

The *gemara* now turns to the text of *Yechezkel*'s vision directly, providing the underlying *pesukim*. *Ve-hinei mayim mifachim min ha-katef ha-yemanis* — behold, water pouring from the right side, that is the south, since one orients oneself facing east, making the right hand the south. *Va-yavod elef ba-ammah* — he measured a thousand *amot* eastward. *Mei afsayim* — *ad karsula*, water up to the ankles.

Another thousand: *mei birkayim* — *ad arkuba*, up to the knees. Another thousand: *mei masnayim* — *ad masnya*, up to the loins. And after yet another thousand *amot*, the stream becomes a *nachal asher lo uchal la-avor* — a river I could not cross.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילוּ לִיבֻּרְנִין גְּדוֹלָה אֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לַעֲבוֹר בּוֹ. מַה טַעֲמָא וְצִי אַדִּיר לֹא יַעַבְרֶנּוּ. מִפְּנֵי מַה כִּי גָאוּ הַמַּיִם מֵי שָׂחוּ. מָהוּ מֵי שָׂחוּ. מִלְּשׁוֹט. אָמַר רִבִּי חוּנָה. בְּאַתְרִין צְוָוחִין לְשַׁיְיטָא סְחוּנָא. וּפֵרַשׂ יָדָיו בְּקִרְבּוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר יְפָרֵשׂ הַשּׂוֹחֶה לִשְׂחוֹת מָהוּ לִשְׂחוֹת. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵה בֵּירִבִּי בּוּן. מַיִין דְּמִתְמַלְּלִין בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** Even a large Liburnian could not cross it. What is the reason? *No mighty ship may cross it*; why? *The water was high, swimming water*. What is "swimming water"? Where one swims. Rebbi Ḥuna said, in our place one calls the swimmer *seḥuna*. *He moves his hands in himself, as a swimmer would move to swim*. What means "to swim"? Rebbi Yose ben Rebbi Abun said, in unruly water.

The *gemara* interprets: *afilu le-virani gedolah einah yecholah la-avor bo* — even the largest ship cannot navigate it. The *gemara* cites a *pasuk* from *Yeshayahu* — *ve-tzi adir lo ya'avrenu* — no great vessel shall cross it — and then asks *mipnei mah*, why? *Ki ga'u ha-mayim mei sachu* — because the waters have risen, *waters to swim in*. What does *sachu* mean? Rabbi Chuna notes that in his locale swimmers were called *sachuna*, confirming that *sachu* means to swim.

A second *derasha* on the same word is then offered: Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Bun reads *sachu* not from *lischot* (to swim) but from *siach* (to speak). The waters are *mayim de-mistalin be-alma* — waters that will be spoken about throughout the world — because of their miraculous character.

### Zechariyah Wellspring

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִהְיֶה מָקוֹר נִפְתָּח לְבֵית דָּוִיד וּלְיֹוֹשְׁבֵי יְרוּשָׁלָם לְחַטַּאת וּלְנִידָּה. רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹנָתָן. מִבֵּית דָּוִיד וְעַד יוֹשְׁבֵי יְרוּשָׁלִָם כְּשֵׁרִים לְנִדָּה וּלְחַטָּאת. מִיכָּן וְהֵילַךְ מֵי תַעֲרוּבוֹת הֵם. כְּשֵׁירִים לְנִידָּה וּפְסוּלִים לְמֵי חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** It is written, *On this day, a water-source will be opened for the House of David and the dwellers in Jerusalem, for purification and impurity.* Rebbi Samuel bar Naḥman in the name of Rebbi Jonathan: From the House of David to the dwellers in Jerusalem it is qualified for impurity and purification. Farther away it is mixed water, qualified for impurity but disqualified for purification.

The *gemara* now cites a *pasuk* in *Zechariyah*: *ba-yom ha-hu yihiyeh makor niftach le-veit David u-le-yoshvei Yerushalayim le-chata't u-le-niddah* — on that day a wellspring will be opened for the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, for *chatat* and for *niddah*.

The *gemara* understands *chatat* here not as a sin-offering in the ordinary sense but as a reference to the *parah adumah*, which is called a *chatat* and whose preparation requires *mayim chayim* — naturally flowing spring water. A *niddah* may also immerse in *mayim chayim*. Thus the *pasuk* is read as speaking directly about the ritual uses of this miraculous stream.

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonasan elaborates: from the house of David and throughout the inhabitants of Jerusalem — that is, as the waters flow from the *Beit Hamikdash* through the royal quarter and into the broader city — *kesherim le-niddah ve-la-chatat*. The waters remain pure *mayim chayim* throughout that range and may be used for both purposes.

### Mixed Waters Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי לָעְזָר. מִבֵּית דָּוִיד וְעַד יוֹשְׁבֵי יְרוּשָׁלִָם כְּשֵׁירִים לְנִדָּה וּלְחַטָּאת. מִיכָּן וְהֵילַךְ מֵי קַטֵּיפְרֵיסוֹת הֵן. פְּסוּלִין לְנִדָּה וּלְחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Eleazar said, from the House of David to the dwellers in Jerusalem it is qualified for impurity and purification. Farther away it is water running down, disqualified for impurity and purification.

From that point onward, however, *mei ta'arovot heim* — they are mixed waters. Rainwater and other sources have joined the stream, and it can no longer be considered pure *mayim chayim*. The practical consequence: *kesherim le-niddah, pesulim le-mei chatat*. A *niddah* may still use these waters for *mikveh* purposes, since the admixture of rainwater does not disqualify a *mikveh* as such, but the water is no longer valid for preparing *mei chatat* for *parah adumah*, which requires *mayim chayim* specifically.

Rabbi Elazar offers a different position. He agrees that from the house of David and throughout Jerusalem the waters are *kesherim le-niddah ve-la-chatat*. But from that point on, he describes the waters not as *mei ta'arovot* but as *mei katafresot* — waters flowing down an incline, a *katafres* being a sloped surface. His ruling: *pesulim le-niddah ve-la-chatat* — invalid for both.

Why should an incline invalidate the water even for *niddah*? The *mefarshim* assume that Rabbi Elazar also presupposes that rainwater has mixed in. If so, the water is predominantly rainwater — and running rainwater, where the majority is not *mayim chayim*, is invalid for *mikveh* since a *mikveh* of predominantly rainwater must be still rather than flowing. Moreover, the question of *katafres* and *chibur* — whether water running down a slope is still considered connected to the body of water above — is a distinct issue in the *gemara* elsewhere. If the sloped section is not considered *mechubar* to the main stream, it would be treated as an independent body of rainwater runoff, invalid entirely.

### Flow to Seas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי הַמַּיִם הָאֵלֶּה יוֹצְאִים אֶל הַגְּלִילָה הַקַּדְמוֹנָה זֶה יַם שֶׁלְסַמְכו. וְיָרְדוּ אֶל הָעֲרָבָה זֶה יַם שֶׁלְטִיבֵּרִיָּא. וּבָאוּ הַיָּמָּה זֶה יַם הַמֶּלַח. אֶל הַיָּמָּה הַמּוּצָאִים זֶה הַיָּם הַגָּדוֹל. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמוֹ מּוֹצָאִים. כְּנֶגֵד שְׁנֵי פְעָמִים שֶׁיָּצָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is written, *He said to me, these waters flow out to the Eastern district*, this is Lake Samchonitis, *and descends to the lowlands*, this is Lake Tiberias, *and comes to the sea,* this is the Salt Sea, *the sea of outgoing water*, this is the Great Sea. And why is it called *the sea of outgoing water*? For the two times it came out.

Returning to *Yechezkel*: *ha-mayim ha-eileh yotzim el ha-Gelilah ha-Kadmonah* — these waters flow toward the eastern country. The *gemara* identifies the *Gelilah ha-kadmonah* as *yam shel Samcho* — the lake of Samcho, which Rashi identifies as the Hula Lake in the northern *Galil*. *Ve-yaredu el ha-aravah* — they descend to the *aravah* — this is *yam shel Tiveria*, the Sea of *Kinneret*. *U-va bo ha-yamah* — and they enter the ocean — this is *yam ha-melach*, the Dead Sea. *El ha-yamah ha-mutzaim* — toward the sea that issues forth — this is *ha-yam ha-gadol*, the Mediterranean.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי לָעְזְר בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי חֲנִינָה. בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה יָצְא עַד קַלַבְּרִיאָה וּבַשְׁנִיָיה יָצָא עַד קֵפֵי בֶּרְבֶּרִיָה. רִִבִּי אָחָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי חֲנִינָה. בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה יָצְא עַד קֵפֵי בֶּרְבֶּרִיָא בַשְׁנִיָיה יָצָא עַד עַכּוֹ וְעַד יָפוֹ. עַד פֹּה תָבוֹא וְלֹא תוֹסִיף. עַד עַכּוֹ תָבוֹא וְלֹא תוֹסִיף. וּפֹא יָשִׁית בִּגְאוֹן גַּלֶּיךָ. עַד יָפוֹ אָשִׁית גְּאוֹן גַּלֶּיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Eleazar in the name of Rebbi Ḥanina: The first time it went out up to Calabria, the second time it went to the Capes of Barbary. Rebbi Aḥa in the name of Rebbi Ḥanina: The first time it went out to the Capes of Barbary, the second time it came to Acco and Jaffa. *Up to here you shall come but no more*; up to Acco you shall come but not add. *And there you stop the might of your waves*, at Jaffa.

Why is the Mediterranean called *mutzaim*, meaning the one that issued forth or overflowed? Because it overflowed its boundaries twice in history. Once in the generation of *Enosh*, when *avodah zarah* prevailed and the Mediterranean overflowed and submerged a third of the inhabited world. And once in the generation of *haflagah*, the Tower of Bavel. Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Chanina says: the first time it went as far as *Kalbareia* — apparently a region the *Korban ha-edah* identifies with southern Italy, while others suggest it refers to Calabria specifically. The second time it reached the shores of *Barbaria*, the Barbary Coast of North Africa. Rabbi Yaakov in the name of Rabbi Chanina reverses the geography: the first time to *Barbaria*, the second time only as far as *Akko* and *Yafo* on the coast of Israel itself. The *gemara* then cites *Iyov*: *tavo ve-lo tosif* — until here you shall come and no further — reading *poh* as an allusion to *Yafo* and *ad poh* as *Akko*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיחָא יָמָּא רַבָּא יַמָּא דְמִילְחָא. בִּשְׁבִיל לְמִיתְקָן. יַמָּה דְטִיבֵּרִיָּא יַמָּה דְסַמְכוֹ. לְרַבּוֹת דְּגָתָם. לְמִינָה תִּהְיֶה דְגָתָם. לְמִינֵי מִינִים תִּהְיֶה דְגָתָם. תַּנֵּי. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָלַכְתִּי לְצַיְידָן וְהֵבִיאוּ לְפָנַיי יוֹתֵר מִשְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת מִינֵי דָגִים בְּתַמְחוּי אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** One understands the Great Sea and the Salt Sea, to make them sweet. Lake Tiberias and Lake Samchonitis, To increase their fish; *their fish shall be in kinds*; in great number shall be the kinds of their fish. It was stated: Rabban Simeon ben Gamliel said, it happened that I went to Sidon and they brought before me more than 300 kinds of fish on one tray.

The *gemara* then asks: it makes sense for the miraculous river to flow into the Mediterranean and the Dead Sea — *bishvil le-mitkan*, in order to sweeten their waters, as the *pasuk* itself says the salty waters will be healed. But why does the river flow into the *Kinneret* and the lake of *Samcho*, which are already fresh water? The answer: *le-rabot dagatam* — to increase their fish. *Le-minei minei tihyeh dagatam* — there will be multitudes of species of fish in those waters. This is followed by a striking supporting report: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel testifies that when he went to *Tzeidan*, they brought before him more than three hundred species of fish in a single dish.

### River Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִרְפּאוּ הַמָּיִם. בְּצֹּאתָיו וּגְבָאָיו וְלֹא יֵרָפְאוּ לְמֶלַח נִיתָּנוּ. כְּתִיב וְנִרְפּאוּ הַמָּיִם וְאַתְּ אָמַרְתָּ. וְלֹא יֵרָפְאוּ הַמַּיִם. מָקוֹם הוּא שֶׁשְּׁמוֹ וְלֹא יֵרָפְאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** *The waters will be healed*. *Its ponds and its puddles will not be healed, they will be given for salt*. It is written, *the waters will be healed,* and you are saying, *they will not be healed?* There is a place called "will not be healed."

Back to the *pasuk* in *Yechezkel*: *ve-nirpe'u ha-mayim* — the waters will be healed. *Be-tzeitav u-gevav ve-lo yirpe'u le-melach nittanu* — the swamps and marshes will not be healed, for they are given over to salt. The *gemara* notes an apparent internal contradiction: the verse opens by saying the waters will be healed and closes by saying they will not be healed. The resolution: *makom hu u-shemo velo yirpe'u* — *velo yirpe'u* is not a statement of outcome but a place name. The location by the edge of the river is called "Velo Yirpe'u."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב וְעַל הַנַּחַל יַעֲלֶה עַל שְׂפָתוֹ מִזֶּה וּמִזֶּה כָּל עֵץ מַאֲכָל לֹא יִבֹּל עָלֵהוּ וְלֹא יִתֹּם פִּרְיוֹ לָחֳדָשָׁיו יְבַכֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** It is written, *on the banks of the river on both sides all kinds of fruit tree will grow; its leaf shall not wilt, and its fruit not stop; in its months it shall produce first fruits.*

On both banks of the river grow all manner of food-bearing trees: *lo yibol alehu* — their leaves will not wither; *ve-lo yitom piryo* — their fruit will never cease; *le-chadashav yivaker* — they will blossom and bear forth each month; *pe'riyo le-ma'achal ve-alehu li-trufa* — the fruit is for food, and the leaves are for medicine.

### Future Fruit Cycles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי אָמַר רִבִּי יְהוּדָה. לְפִי שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה תְּבוּאָה עוֹשָׂה לְשִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים וְאִילָן עוֹשֶׂה לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ. אֲבָל לְעָתִיד לָבוֹא הַתְּבוּאָה עוֹשָׂה לְחוֹדֶשׁ אֶחָד וְאִילָן עוֹשֶׂה לִשְׁנֵי חֳדָשִׁים. מַה טַעֲמֵיהּ. לָחֳדָשָׁיו יְבַכֵּר. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵי. לְפִי שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם הַַזֶּה הַתְּבוּאָה עוֹשָׂה לְשִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים וְהָאִילָן עוֹשֶׂה לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ. אֲבָל לְעָתִיד לָבוֹא הַתְּבוּאָה עוֹשָׂה לַחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר יוֹם וְאִילָן עוֹשֶׂה לְחוֹדֶשׁ אֶחָד. שֶׁכֵּן מָצָאנוּ שֶׁעָשָׂת הַתְּבוּאָה בִימֵי יוֹאֵל לַחֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר יוֹם וְקָרֵב מִמֶּנָּה הָעוֹמֶר. מַה טַעֲמֵיהּ. וּבְנֵי צִיּוֹן גִּילוּ וְשִׂמְחוּ בַּיי אֱלֹהֵיכֶם כִּי נָתַן לָכֶם אֶת הַמּוֹרֶה לִצְדָקָה וַיּוֹרֶד לָכֶם גֶּשֶׁם יוֹרֶה וּמַלְקוֹשׁ בָּרִאשׁוֹן. מַה מְקַיֵים רִבִּי יוֹסֵי לָחֳדָשָׁיו יְבַכֵּר. בְּכָל חוֹדֶשׁ וָחוֹדֶשׁ יְהֵא מְבַכֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated: Rebbi Jehudah said, in the present world grain grows in six months and trees produce every twelve months, but in the future grain will grow in a month and trees will produce in two months. Rebbi Yose said, in the present world grain grows in six months and trees produce every twelve months, but in the future grain will grow in fifteen days and trees will produce in a month, for so we find that grain grew in the days of Joel in fifteen days, and the '*Omer* was brought from it. What is the reason? *The people of Zion shall enjoy and be happy in the Eternal, your God, for He in truth gave you strong rain, early rain and late rain in the first {month}*. How does Rebbi Yose satisfy *in its months it shall produce first fruits*? Every month it shall produce first fruits.

A *baraita* is cited with a debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosi concerning the production cycles of the future:

Rabbi Yehuda reasons from the present: in this world, grain takes six months to ripen and trees take twelve months. In the future, applying a consistent ratio of one-to-two, *tvua osa le-chodesh echad* — grain will ripen in one month, and *ilan osa li-shnei chodashim* — trees in two months. His reading of *le-chadashav yivaker* is that the tree produces every two months.

Rabbi Yosi goes further. In this world the same cycle applies, but in the future, *ha-tvua osa le-tet-vav yom* — grain will ripen in fifteen days, and *ilan osa le-chodesh echad* — trees in one month. He reads *le-chadashav* as one month per fruit cycle, not two. His proof: in the days of *Yoel* there was a miracle in which grain grew in fifteen days, and the *omer* was brought from it — as the *pasuk* states: *ki natan lachem et ha-moreh li-tzedakah ve-yored lachem geshem moreh u-malkosh ba-rishon* — the early and late rain came in the first month, which is *Nissan*, meaning that from the rain in *Nissan* the grain ripened in time for the *omer*, a span of fifteen days.

The *gemara* then asks: if Rabbi Yosi holds that trees produce every month, what does he make of the plural *le-chadashav*, "its months"? He reads it as *be-chol chodesh ve-chodesh yihiyeh meivaker* — every single month the tree will be producing; the plural simply indicates monthly recurrence.

### Healing Properties

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָלֵהוּ לִתְרוּפָה. רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר. תַּרְפֵּיהּ מְצַץ עָלֵיהּ וֹתָרַף מְזוֹנֵהּ. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל. חַד אָמַר. לְהַתִּיר פֶּה שֶׁלְמָעֲלָן. וְחוֹרָנָה אָמַר. לְהַתִּיר פֶּה שֶׁלְמַטָּן. רִבִּי חֲנִינָה וְרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי. חַד אָמַר לְהַתִּיר פֶּה עֲקָרוֹת. וְחוֹרָנָה אָמַר. לְהַתִּיר פֶּה אִילְּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** *And its leaf as medicine*. Rebbi Joḥanan said, its healing is that one looks at it and food is turned into medicine. Rav and Samuel, one says, to open the upper mouth, but the other one says, to open the lower mouth. Rebbi Ḥanina and Rebbi Joshua ben Levi, one says, to open the mouth of sterile women, but the other one says, to open the mouth of the dumb.

The *gemara* then addresses the verse's statement that the leaves will be *li-trufa*, for healing. Rabbi Yochanan interprets: *tarfei matzitz aleh ve-taraf mezona* — reading the word with a *tes*, as *taraf*, meaning food — so that the leaves can even serve as food. Why reinterpret the verse when fruit for food and leaves for medicine already seems a sufficiently generous arrangement? The *gemara* does not explain.

Rav and Shmuel offer two readings of *trufa le-hatir peh*: one says it means *le-hatir peh she-le-mala* — to open the mouth above, that is, to cure the mute and restore their speech; the other says *le-hatir peh she-le-mata* — to open the mouth below, meaning to cure the barren or to relieve constipation. Rabbi Chanina and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi make the same distinction more explicitly: one says *le-hatir peh akarot*, to make barren women fertile; the other says *le-hatir peh ilmim*, to restore speech to the mute.

## Yehoyachin Story

### Nebuchadnezzar Siege

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְּ מוֹצֵא. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעָלָה נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר לְכָאן בָּא וְיָשַׁב לוֹ בְדִיפְנֵי שֶׁלְאַנְטִוֹכִיָּה וְיָצָאָה סַנְהֶדְרִין גְּדוֹלָה לִקְרָאתוֹ וְאָמְרָה לוֹ. הִגִּיעַ זְמַן הַבַּיִת הַזֶּה לִיחָרֵב. אָמַר לָהֶן. אוֹתוֹ שֶׁהִמְלַכְתִּי אוֹתוֹ עֲלֵיכֶם תְּנוּהוּ לִי וַאֲנִי הוֹלֵךְ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** You are finding that when Nebuchadnezzar campaigned here, he came and sat in Daphne of Antiochia. The Great Synhedrion went to him and said to him, did the time come that this House be destroyed? He said to them, give me the one which I made king over you and I will depart.

Having mentioned *Sha'ar Yechanyah* and the reason for its name, the *gemara* now narrates the events leading to *Yechanyah*'s exile. *At motza be-sha'ah she-ala Nevuchadnetzar le-chan* — when Nebuchadnezzar came up to this place, *ba ve-yashav lo be-dafni shel Antochia* — he came and encamped at the walls of Antioch. This is strikingly far from Jerusalem, and yet the *Sanhedrin* went out to meet him there — a preemptive act reminiscent of *Yochanan ben Zakkai*'s famous capitulation to Vespasian in the period of the *Bayit Sheni*. The *Sanhedrin* said to him: *he-izman ha-bayit ha-zeh le-hacharev?* — Has the time come for the *Beit Hamikdash* to be destroyed? Take care of it and proceed.

Nebuchadnezzar replied: *oso she-himlachti aleichem* — the king I appointed over you — *teneihu li ve-ani holech* — give him to me and I will leave. Which is, in fact, precisely what transpired historically: he deposed *Yechanyah* and took him into exile.

### Keys Returned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּאוּ וְאָמְרוּ לִיהוֹיָכִין מֶלֶךְ יְהוּדָה. נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר בָּעֵי לָךְ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁמַע מֵהֶן כָּךְ נָטַל מַפְתֵּיחוֹת שֶׁלְבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ. עָלָה לְגַגּוֹ שֶׁלְהֵיכָל. אָמַר לָפָנָיו. רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁלְעוֹלָם. לִכְשֶׁעָבַר הָיִינוּ נֶאֱמָנִין לָךְ וְהָיוּ מַפְתֵּיחוֹתֵיךָ מְסוּרִין לָנוּ. עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאֵין אָנוּ נֶאֱמָנִין הֲרֵי מַפְתֵּחוֹתֵיךָ מְסוּרִין לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** They came and said to Jehoiachin, the king of Judea, Nebuchadnezzar wants you. When he heard this from them, he took the keys of the Temple and climbed to the roof of the Temple. He said before Him: Master of the world! In the past we were trustworthy for You and Your keys were handed to us. Now that we are not trustworthy for You, Your keys are handed to You.

The *Sanhedrin* went and informed *Yehoyachin melech Yehuda*: Nebuchadnezzar wants you.

Upon hearing this, *Yehoyachin* took the keys of the *Beit Hamikdash*, ascended to the roof of the *heichal*, and addressed God: *le-she-avar hayinu ne'emanim lach* — in the past we were trusted by You; *va-hayu maftechotecha mesurim lanu* — Your keys were entrusted to us. *Achshav she-einanu ne'emanim* — now that we are no longer found trustworthy — *harei maftechotecha mesurim lach* — here are Your keys returned to You. It is a remarkable act — part resignation, part theological statement, a formal acknowledgment that the stewardship has ended.

### Keys Fate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרֵין אֲמוֹרִין. חַד אָמַר. זְרָקָן וְעוֹד לֹא יָרְדוּ. וְחוֹרָנָה אָמַר. רָאָה כְמִין יָד נוֹטַלְּתַן מִיָּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Two Amoraim, One said, he threw them upwards and they never returned. The other said, He saw that something like a hand came and took them from him.

A dispute among *amora'im* follows: one says *zarkan ve-od lo yardu* — he threw the keys upward and they have not come down yet. The other says *ba ke-min yad ve-natalta mi-yado* — a hand emerged from heaven and took them. The two versions carry different theological valences: in the first, the keys hang suspended — unreturned, unclaimed, awaiting *redemption*; in the second, heaven actively receives them back, a gesture of acknowledgment from above.

### Nobles' Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁרָאוּ כָּל חוֹרֵי יְהוּדָה כֵן עָלוּ לְרֹאשׁ גַּגּוֹתֵיהֶן וְנָפְלוּ וָמֵתוּ. הָדָא הוּא דִכְתִיב מַשָּׂא גֵּיא חִיזָּיוֹן מַה לָּךְ אֵיפוֹא כִּי עָלִית כּוּלָּךְ לַגַּגּוֹת. תְּשׁוּאוֹת מְלֵאָה עִיר הוֹמִיָּה וגו׳.
>
> **Translation:** When all the freeholders of Jehudah heard this, they climbed to the tops of their roofs, fell down, and died. That is what is written, *Saying about the valley of vision: Why are you doing this, that you climbed on all roofs, the noisy, full, humming city*.

*Kivan she-ra'u kol chorei Yehuda ken* — when all the nobles of Judah witnessed this, *alu le-rosh gagotseihem ve-naflu ve-meitu* — they ascended to their rooftops and threw themselves off, dying. The *gemara* identifies this in the *pasuk* of *Yeshayahu*: *massa gei chizayon mah lach eifo ki alita kulech le-gagosei* — what has happened to you that you have all gone up to the rooftops? The city is in tumult.

This account is not without halakhic resonance. These *midrashim* describing mass suicide in moments of religious catastrophe were cited by the *Chachmei Ashkenaz* in the medieval period — particularly in the context of the Crusades — as precedent for martyrdom, demonstrating that this mode of response to persecution had classical roots in *aggadic* tradition.

## 13 Tables

### Tables Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר שׁוּלְחָנוֹת הָיוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: There were thirteen tables in the Temple.

The *mishna* now proceeds to the thirteen *shulchanot* — the second item in the inverted ABC-CBA structure. *Yud-gimmel shulchanos hayu ba-Mikdash* — thirteen tables were in the *Mikdash*.

### 8 Marble Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוֹנֶה שֶׁל שַׁיִישׁ בְּבֵית הַמִּטְבְּחַיִם שֶׁעֲלֵיהֶן מְדִיחִין אֶת הַקְּרָבַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** There were eight marble ones in the slaughterhouse on which one was soaking the intestines.

*She-monah shel shayish be-veit ha-mitbachayim* — eight made of marble in the *Beit Hamitbachayim*, the slaughterhouse. *She-aleihem medichim et ha-kirvayim* — on these they would wash out the innards and organs of the slaughtered animals, removing refuse. Marble was chosen because it keeps the meat cold, preventing spoilage.

### West Ramp Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁנַיִם בְּמַעֲרַב הַכֶּבֶשׁ אֶחָד שֶׁל שַׁיִישׁ וְאֶחָד שֶׁל כֶּסֶף עַל שֶׁל שַׁיִישׁ נוֹתְנִין אֶת הָאֵיבָרִין וְעַל שֶׁל כֶּסֶף כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** Two West of the ramp, one of marble and one of silver. On the marble one they put the limbs, on the silver one the vessels of service.

*U-shtayim be-ma'arav ha-kevesh* — two on the west of the ramp of the *mizbeach*: one of marble and one of silver. The marble one was for placing the *eivarim* — the limbs of the offerings — again to keep them cool before they were carried up to the *mizbeach*. The silver one was for the *klei shares*, the service vessels, which could be kept on silver without the same concern about temperature.

### Ulam Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁנַיִם בָּאוּלָם מִבִּפְנִים עַל פֶּתַח הַבַּיִת אֶחָד שֶׁל שַׁיִישׁ וְאֶחָד שֶׁל זָהָב. עַל שֶׁל שַׁיִישׁ נוֹתְנִים לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים בִּכְנִיסָתוֹ וְעַל שֶׁל זָהָב בִּיצִיאָתוֹ שֶׁמַּעֲלִין בַּקֹּדֶשׁ וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** And two in the vestibule inside, near the entrance to the Temple Hall, one of marble and one of gold. On the marble one they put the shew-bread when it was brought in, and on the golden one when it was brought out, for one increases in holiness and does not decrease.

*U-shtayim ba-ulam mi-bifnim al petach ha-bayit* — two in the *ulam*, just inside the entrance to the *heichal*. One was of marble, the other of gold. *Al shel shayish notnin lechem ha-panim bi-chnisaso* — on the marble table they would rest the *lechem ha-panim* upon its arrival, as it was brought in for placement. *Ve-al shel zahav be-yetziaso* — on the gold table it rested upon removal. *She-maalin ba-kodesh ve-lo moridin* — because one ascends in *kedushah* and does not descend. The bread would thus move from a lesser to a greater material on its way out, culminating on gold.

### Gold Showbread Table

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד שֶׁל זָהָב מִבִּפְנִים שֶׁעָלָיו לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים תָּמִיד:
>
> **Translation:** And a golden one inside on which the shew-bread was permanently.

And of course there was the famous thirteenth: *echad shel zahav mi-bifnim she-alav lechem ha-panim tamid* — the one golden table inside, on which the *lechem ha-panim* rested continuously. This is the *shulchan* known from the *Torah* itself — one of the original vessels of the *Mikdash* — and its presence among the thirteen is what completes the count.

## Tables Gemara

### Silver vs Marble

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: תַּנֵּי. עַל שֶׁלְכֶּסֶף. רִבִּי יוֹסֵי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק. רִבִּי חֲנַנְיָה מַטֵּי בָהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. לֵית כָּאן שֶׁלְכֶּסֶף מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַרְתִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: It was stated, "on the silver one". Rebbi Yose in the name of Rebbi Samuel bar Rav Isaac; Rebbi Ḥananiah brought it in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan: There was none of silver because it heats.

A *baraita* variant surfaces: rather than *shel shayish* — marble — for the table on which the *lechem ha-panim* rested upon entry, it says *shel kesef* — silver. Rabbi Yosi in the name of Rabbi Shmuel bar Rav Yitzchak, and Rabbi Chanina citing Rabbi Yochanan, rule: *leit kan shel kesef* — silver was not used. *Mipnei she-hu martiach* — because silver would heat the bread, causing it to spoil. Some have a variant *girsa* in which the concern was the opposite — that marble would cool the bread too much — but in our text the concern is that silver retains heat in a way that would be damaging.

### Showbread Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא כֵן תַּנֵּי. זֶה אֶחָד מִן הַנִּיסִּים שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ בְבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ. שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁהָיוּ מַנִּיחִין אוֹתוֹ חוֹם כָּךְ הָיוּ מוֹצִאִין אוֹתוֹ חוֹם. [שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר] לָשׁוּם לֶחֶם חוֹם כְּיוֹם הִלָּקְחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** But did we not state, this is one of the wonders which happened in the Temple, that just as they were putting it down hot, so they were removing it hot, [as it is written], *to put hot bread as on the day it was removed*.

At this point the *gemara* raises an obvious objection: surely there were sufficient miracles operative in the *Beit Hamikdash* that such practical concerns are irrelevant? Is it not taught in a *baraita*: *zeh echad min ha-nissim she-na'asu be-Beit HaMikdash* — this is one of the miracles that occurred in the *Beit Hamikdash* — *keshem she-hayu manichim oto cham, kach hayu motzi'im oto cham* — just as they placed the *lechem ha-panim* on the table while it was hot, so too they removed it a full week later and it was still hot, still completely fresh. The verse is cited: *lasum lechem cham be-yom hilakcho* — bread as hot as the day it was taken. If a miracle of this magnitude was in place, why concern ourselves with whether the table was marble or silver?

### No Relying on Miracles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר. אֵין מַזְכִּירִין מַעֲשֶׂה נִיסִּים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joshua ben Levi said, one does not mention wonders.

The response: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states, *ein mazkirin ma'aseh nissim* — one does not invoke miracles. The parallel formulation in the *Bavli* is: *ein somchin al ha-nes* — we do not rely on the miracle. The practical arrangements of the *Mikdash* were to be made responsibly, as though no miraculous intervention could be counted upon. If God then chooses to perform a miracle, all the better; but the design of the *avodah* must not be predicated on the assumption that a miracle will occur.

### Old Bread Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹן קוֹמֵי רִבִּי אִילָא. לֹא הָיָה שָׁם לֶחֶם מָהוּ לְהַנִּיחוֹ לַשַּׁבָּת הַבָּאָה. אֲמַר לוֹן. כְּתִיב וְנָתַתָּה עַל הַשּׁוּלְחָן לֶחֶם פָּנִים לְפָנַיי תָּמִיד. לֶחֶם פָּנִים אֲפִילוּ פָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** They asked before Rebbi Ila: If they did not have bread, may they leave it for the coming week? He said to them, it is written: *you shall put on the table the shew-bread before Me permanently.* The shew-bread, even disqualified.

A question is posed before Rabbi Ami: *lo hayah sham lechem, mahu le-hanicho le-Shabbat ha-ba'ah?* — if in some unusual circumstance there was no new *lechem ha-panim* ready on *Shabbat*, could the old loaves remain on the *shulchan* for an additional week — two weeks in total? The verse requires *lechem ha-panim lefanai tamid* — the showbread must always be before Me — and perhaps that *tamid* allows for continuous presence even beyond the normal weekly cycle.

### Continuous Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל שֶׁל זָהָב בִּיצִיאָתוֹ שֶׁמַּעֲלִין בַּקֹּדֶשׁ וְלֹא מוֹרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** and on the golden one when it was brought out, for one increases in holiness and does not decrease.

Rabbi Ami responds: *ve-nasata al ha-shulchan lechem panim lefanai tamid* — the verse says "place bread before Me always." *Lechem panim afilu pasul* — even if the bread is technically invalid. The precise meaning of *pasul* here is somewhat unclear — does the bread become invalid by remaining more than a week? — but the operative ruling is that it must remain *lefanai tamid*, before God continuously. The bread therefore may remain in place beyond the standard week if no new bread is ready. Additionally, Rabbi Ami's comment implies: *ve-ein lo notar* — there is no prohibition of *notar* applicable here, consistent with the principle that an offering on top of the *mizbeach* is not subject to *notar*, and a similar logic apparently applies to the *shulchan*.

## Solomon's 10 Tables

### Solomon's Tables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה שׁוּלְחָנוֹת עָשָׂה שְׁלֹמֹה. וַיַּעַשׂ שׁוּלְחָנוֹת עֲשָׂרָה וַיַּנַּח בַּהֵיכָל חֲמִשָּׁה מִיָּמִין וַחֲמִשָּׁה מִשְּׂמֹאל.
>
> **Translation:** Solomon made ten tables: *Solomon made ten tables and deposited them in the Temple Hall, five to the right and five to the left*.

The *mishna* now raises an additional dimension: *asarah shulchanos asah Shlomo* — Solomon made ten additional *shulchanot* beyond the original one, replicating the golden *shulchan* of *Moshe*. The biblical source: *va-ya'as shulchanos asarah va-yanach ba-heichal chamisha mi-yamin ve-chamisha mi-smol* — he made ten tables and placed five on the right and five on the left within the *heichal*.

### Placement Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין תֵּימַר. חֲמִשָּׁה בַדָּרוֹם וַחֲמִשָּׁה בַצָּפוֹן. וַהֲלֹא אֵין הַשּׁוּלְחָן כָּשֵׁר אֶלָּא בַצָּפוֹן. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר וְאֶת הַשֻּׁלְחָן תִּתֵּן עַל צֶלַע צָפוֹן. מַה תַלָמוּד לוֹמַר. חֲמִשָּׁה מִיָּמִין וַחֲמִשָּׁה מִשְּׂמֹאל. אֶלָּא חֲמִשָּׁה מִיָּמִין שֻׁלְחֲנוּ שֶׁלְמֹשֶׁה וַחֲמִשָּׁה מִשְּׂמאֹלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If you would say, five to the North and five to the South, but the table is only qualified in the North, as it is said, *and the table put on the North side*. Why does the verse say, *five to the right and five to the left*? Five to the right of Moses's table, and five to its left.

The ordinary reading of "right" and "left" when facing east would yield south and north. But this creates an immediate problem: *ein ha-shulchan kasher ela be-tzafon* — the *shulchan* is only valid when placed on the northern side, as the verse commands *ve-et ha-shulchan titen al tzela tzafon* — place the table on the northern side. If Solomon's additional tables were arranged north and south of the original, some of them would be more northerly than the original, violating the requirement that it occupy the northern position.

The resolution: *chamisha mi-yamin shulchano shel Moshe ve-chamisha mi-smolo* — the "right" and "left" do not mean north and south of the room, but east and west relative to *Moshe*'s original *shulchan*. The ten tables flanked the original on either side, meaning they were arrayed east and west of it, not north and south. The original *shulchan* thus remained in the northernmost position as required.

### Moses' Table Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי כֵן לֹא הָיָה מְסַדֵּר אֶלָּא שֶׁלְמֹשֶׁה בִּלְבַד. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָן אֲשֶׁר עָלָיו לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Nevertheless, only Moses's alone was used, as it is said, *the table on which is the shew-bread.*

The standard ruling: *af al pi chein lo hayah mesader ela be-shel Moshe bilvad* — even so, the *lechem ha-panim* was arranged only on *Moshe*'s original table, not on any of Solomon's ten additions. The verse is cited: *et ha-shulchan asher alav lechem ha-panim* — "the table upon which the *lechem ha-panim* rests" — the definite article indicating one specific table.

### All Tables Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוֹסֵי בֵּירִבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר. עַל כּוּלָּם הָיָה מְסַדֵּר. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר אֶת הַשּׁוּלְחָנוֹת וַעֲלֵיהֶם לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose ben Rebbi Jehudah says, all of them were used, for it is said, *the tables on which the shew-bread is*.

Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehudah takes the striking minority position: *al kulan hayah mesader* — the showbread was arranged on all eleven tables. He derives this from the verse *et ha-shulchanos va-aleihem lechem ha-panim* — using the plural *ha-shulchanos* and *aleihem*. This is, as the *gemara* does not hesitate to note, quite shocking. Not only did Solomon add vessels to the inner *heichal* — itself already a bold act, given the Torah's particularity about the sacred vessels — but according to Rabbi Yosi son of Rabbi Yehudah, an entirely separate *avodah* was established, with additional loaves baked and arranged on all ten new tables as well. The *gemara* simply records this view without further analysis, leaving it to stand in its remarkable solitude.
