# Zevachim 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slaughtering at Altar Top

### Perek Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כְּאִילּוּ נִשְׁחֲטוּ בַּצָּפוֹן. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵחֲצִי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלַדָּרוֹם – כַּדָּרוֹם, מֵחֲצִי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלַצָּפוֹן – כַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It was taught in the previous chapter that offerings of the most sacred order are to be slaughtered in the northern section of the Temple courtyard. With regard to **offerings of the most sacred order that one slaughtered atop the altar, Rabbi Yosei says:** Their status is **as though they were slaughtered in the north,** and the offerings are therefore valid. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** The status of the area **from the halfway point of the altar and to the south** is **like** that of **the south,** and offerings of the most sacred order slaughtered in that area are therefore disqualified. The status of the area **from the halfway point of the altar and to the north** is **like** that of **the north.**

Daf 58 opens a new *perek*, *Kodshei Kodashim*. Having devoted the previous *perek* to laying out the rules for each *korban* — where it is slaughtered, where its blood is applied — the Talmud now turns to the consequences of deviation from those rules. One might have wished that every *masechet* began this way: one *perek* establishing the basic rules, followed immediately by a *perek* exploring what happens when they are not followed. As it happens, we had to wait until the fifth *perek* for this opening statement, and now the sixth *perek* takes up the question of deviation.

### North Slaughter Rule

The foundational rule with which this *perek* begins is well established: *kodshei kodashim* — sacrifices of the highest sanctity — must have their *shechita* performed in the north. This was set out in the previous *perek*. The present *perek* asks: what happens when the rules are not followed perfectly?

### Top of Altar Issue

The first case the *mishnah* takes up is: what if the *kodshei kodashim* were slaughtered not in the north of the *azarah* opposite the altar, but on top of the altar itself? The issue here is not whether the top of the altar counts as valid ground for the purpose of *shechita* — one can assume the altar, as an extension connected to the floor, carries the same status as the ground below, especially given that *kohanim* perform their *avodah* while standing on the altar. The real question is one of geometry: if the *shechita* must be performed *al yerech hamizbeach tzafonah* — on the north side of the altar — does "north" refer to the northern half of the *mikdash* or to the north side of the altar itself? And when the animal is brought to the top of the altar, which portions of that top surface count as valid?

## Yosi Positions

### Yosi: Whole Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹמֵר הָיָה ר׳ יוֹסֵי, כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ בְּצָפוֹן קָאֵי. וּמַאי ״כְּאִילּוּ״? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא בָּעֵינַן ״עַל יָרֵךְ״ – וְלֵיכָּא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Yosei used to say: The entire altar stands in the north** section of the Temple courtyard. The Gemara asks: **And what** is the meaning of Rabbi Yosei's statement that if one slaughtered offerings of the most sacred order atop the altar it is **as though** they were slaughtered in the north, which indicates that they were not actually slaughtered in the north? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yosei said this **lest you say** that **we require** that the offering be slaughtered **"on the side** of the altar northward" (Leviticus 1:11), i.e., on the ground beside the altar, **and** that requirement **is not** fulfilled when it is slaughtered on top of the altar. Therefore, Rabbi Yosei **teaches us** that the offering is still valid.

*Rabbi Yosi omer: ke'ilu nishchetu batzafon* — Rabbi Yosi says that if *kodshei kodashim* were slaughtered on top of the altar, it is as if they were slaughtered in the north. The phrasing is immediately striking: he says "as if" (*ke'ilu*) they were slaughtered in the north — not that they actually were. This invites the question of what the qualification is. If Rabbi Yosi holds that the entire altar is situated in the north of the *mikdash*, why would he describe slaughter on top of it as merely "as if" in the north? One would expect him simply to say it is in the north.

### Yosi bar Yehuda: Half/Ha…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מֵחֲצִי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלַדָּרוֹם – כַּדָּרוֹם, מֵחֲצִי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלַצָּפוֹן – כַּצָּפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** The status of the area **from the halfway point of the altar and to the south** is **like** that of **the south,** and offerings of the most sacred order slaughtered in that area are therefore disqualified. The status of the area **from the halfway point of the altar and to the north** is **like** that of **the north.**

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda offers a different ruling: *mechatzi hamizbeach uledarom kadarom, mechatzi hamizbeach uletzafon katzafon* — from the midpoint of the altar southward, it is treated as the south; from the midpoint northward, it is treated as the north. This position reflects the intuitive assumption that the altar is centered in the *azarah*, straddling the dividing line between north and south. On that reading, the northern half of the altar lies in the northern hemisphere of the *mikdash* and is valid for *kodshei kodashim*, while the southern half is not.

### As If Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַב אַסִּי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הָכִי נָמֵי דְּחֶצְיוֹ בְּצָפוֹן וְחֶצְיוֹ בַּדָּרוֹם?! וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי; וְהָא אַתְּ הוּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁאִם שְׁחָטָן כְּנֶגְדָּן בַּקַּרְקַע – פְּסוּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rav Asi:** Rabbi Yoḥanan apparently understands that the reason Rabbi Yosei holds that an offering of the most sacred order slaughtered on the altar is valid is because the entire altar is in the northern section of the Temple courtyard. **If that is so,** shall one **also** say **that according to Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, half of** the altar was situated **in the north** of the Temple courtyard **and half of it** was situated **in the south?** **And if you would say** that **indeed** that is so, **wasn't it you who said in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan** that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, concedes that if one slaughtered** offerings of the most sacred order **on the ground opposite** the northern half of the altar, the offering is **disqualified?** Accordingly, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, apparently maintains that the altar is not located in the north at all.

The tension in Rabbi Yosi's language now becomes the *gemara*'s first point of inquiry. Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, reports that Rabbi Yosi held *kulo mizbeach batzafon kai* — the entire altar stands in the north. On that understanding, the phrase *ke'ilu* — "as if" — is not conceding that the top of the altar falls short of the northern designation. Rather, it addresses a different concern: one might have thought that *al yerech hamizbeach* requires the *shechita* to take place specifically on the side of the altar, not on its top surface — much as a hole-in-one is not the same as getting the ball closest to the hole. The word *ke'ilu* therefore comes to teach us that slaughtering on the top of the altar itself is sufficient and counts as if it were performed along the side, provided the location falls within the northern zone of the *mikdash* opposite the altar (*kineged hamizbeach*).

## Yochanan's Approach

### Altar in North

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – שְׁנֵיהֶם מִקְרָא אֶחָד דָּרְשׁוּ: ״וְזָבַחְתָּ עָלָיו אֶת עֹלֹתֶיךָ וְאֶת שְׁלָמֶיךָ״ – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי סָבַר: כּוּלּוֹ לְעוֹלָה וְכוּלּוֹ לִשְׁלָמִים. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: חֶצְיוֹ לְעוֹלָה וְחֶצְיוֹ לִשְׁלָמִים;
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi **said to** Rabbi Zeira: Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement with regard to Rabbi Yosei's opinion is an independent statement rather than an inference from the mishna. And with regard to the dispute in the mishna, **this** is what **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Both of them derived** their opinions **from one verse:** "An altar of earth you shall make for Me, **and you shall slaughter upon it your burnt offerings and your peace offerings"** (Exodus 20:21). **Rabbi Yosei maintains** that the verse teaches that **all of it,** i.e., the entire altar, is fit **for** slaughtering **a burnt offering, and all of it** is also fit **for** slaughtering **a peace offering. And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, maintains** that the verse teaches that **half of it** is fit **for** slaughtering **a burnt offering and half of it** is fit **for** slaughtering **a peace offering.**

The tradition reported by Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is therefore that Rabbi Yosi's position — that the whole top of the altar is valid — reflects his view that the entire altar stands in the north. The *ke'ilu* of the *mishnah* is not concessive but pedagogic: it comes to forestall the misreading that *al yerech* demands contact with the side rather than the top.

### Zeira Challenge

Rabbi Zeira challenges Rav Assi. His challenge is reasonable: if Rabbi Yosi's ruling that the whole altar top is valid reflects his view that the whole altar is in the north, then by the same logic, Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that only the northern half of the altar top is valid must reflect his view that the altar is positioned half in the north and half in the south. The debate in the *mishnah*, on this reading, is simply a debate about where the altar is situated. Rabbi Zeira presses this point: *v'chi teima hachi nami* — isn't that exactly the right inference?

### Below Altar Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתְּךָ כּוּלּוֹ לְעוֹלָה כָּשֵׁר, הַשְׁתָּה כּוּלּוֹ לְעוֹלָה – כָּשֵׁר, כּוּלּוֹ לִשְׁלָמִים מִיבַּעְיָא?! וְאִידָּךְ – אִיצְטְרִיךְ; סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךָ אָמֵינָא: עוֹלָה הוּא דִּדְחִיק לֵיהּ מָקוֹם; אֲבָל שְׁלָמִים, דְּלָא דְּחִיק לֵיהּ מָקוֹם – אֵימָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how does** **the other** Sage, Rabbi Yosei, respond to this reasoning? The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** for the verse to mention peace offerings. Otherwise, it could **enter your mind to say** that the verse allows one to slaughter only **a burnt offering** atop the altar, **as the location** where **it** may be slaughtered on the ground is **narrow. But** with regard to **peace offerings, whose location** for slaughter on the ground is **not narrow, say** that **no,** one may not slaughter them atop the altar. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that peace offerings as well may be slaughtered atop the altar.

Rav Assi responds by pointing to a tradition he himself had cited in Rabbi Yochanan's name: *modei Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda she'im shachtan kenegdan bakarka pesulah* — Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda concedes that if one slaughtered *kodshei kodashim* directly below the northern half of the altar at ground level, the *shechita* is invalid.

The challenge posed by this ruling is conceptual. To understand the ruling, one must first understand what scenario is being described, but let us begin with its implication. If Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda held that the altar is situated half in the north and half in the south — that is, if the north half of the altar is genuinely in the northern zone — then slaughtering directly beneath the northern half, at ground level, should be valid: one would be in the north. The fact that it is invalid suggests that the ground directly beneath the altar's northern half is not, in fact, situated in the northern zone. That can only be the case if the altar is not half-north, half-south at all, but is entirely situated in the south. The northern half of the altar top is valid only by virtue of a special scriptural dispensation — not because the altar occupies the north. And what is below the altar, absent any such dispensation, remains in the south and is therefore invalid.

One might have proposed a different explanation: that ground-level space directly beneath the north of the altar is invalid not because it is not in the north, but because it is not *al yerech hamizbeach* — it is neither the altar itself nor north of the altar in any meaningful sense. The airspace extends upward, but not downward. The *gemara*, however, does not accept this logic. It reads the invalidity of ground-level slaughter directly below the northern half as definitive evidence that that space is not in the northern zone — implying that the altar, for Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, is situated entirely in the south.

### Verse Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא – אָמַר רַב אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מוֹדֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁאִם שְׁחָטָן כְּנֶגְדָּן בַּקַּרְקַע – פְּסוּלוֹת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: מַאי כְּנֶגְדָּן בַּקַּרְקַע? אִילֵימָא אַמָּה יְסוֹד אַמָּה סוֹבֵב – הַאי גּוּפֵיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ הוּא! וְעוֹד, מַאי ״כְּנֶגְדָּן בַּקַּרְקַע״?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself: Rav Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, concedes that if one slaughtered** offerings of the most sacred order **on the ground opposite** the northern half of the altar, the offering is **disqualified. Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: What** is the meaning of the phrase: **On the ground opposite** the northern half of the altar? **If we say** it means that the offering was sacrificed upon the **cubit**-wide **base** of the altar or upon the **cubit**-wide **surrounding ledge** of the altar, **this itself is** part of the **altar. And furthermore, what** is the meaning of the phrase: **On the ground opposite** the northern half of the altar? The base and ledge are not on the ground.

With this, the *gemara* recasts the entire *mishnah* debate. Rabbi Yochanan's conclusion: *shneihem mikra echad darshu* — both Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda are debating the interpretation of a single verse, not staking out positions about where the altar is physically situated.

The verse in question is *vezavachta alav et olotecha ve'et shelamecha* — "and you shall slaughter upon it your burnt offerings and your peace offerings." The word *alav* — "upon it" — is read with unusual literalism: one may slaughter directly on top of the altar. This is striking, and Tosafos on the first *tosafos* of this *perek* notes explicitly that according to this verse, *lechatchila* one is permitted to perform *shechita* on top of the altar from Torah law. The only possible rabbinic restriction, Tosafos suggests, is that one should not bring a live animal to the top of the altar because of the concern that it will defecate there — but from the Torah itself, the top of the altar is a valid location.

The debate between Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda is then about how to read this verse. Rabbi Yosi holds *kulo le'ola v'kulo lishlamim*: the entire top of the altar may be used for an *olah* and the entire top for a *shelamim*. The verse permits using the full surface for either. The fact that it mentions both *olotecha* and *shelamecha* is not to divide them; it is simply because one might have thought the dispensation was granted only for the *olah*, which has limited space in the *azarah* during busy periods, and not for the *shelamim*, which has ample space elsewhere. The verse comes to teach that the *shelamim* may also be slaughtered anywhere on top.

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, reads the verse as dividing the altar top: *chatzi le'ola v'chatzi lishlamim* — half for the *olah* and half for the *shelamim*. The northern half is designated for the *olah*, the southern for the *shelamim*. This is structurally analogous to the debate about *Yom Tov* — whether it is entirely for God, entirely for oneself, or divided.

The upshot of Rabbi Yochanan's reformulation is decisive: the positions in the *mishnah* are about scriptural interpretation for this particular *halachah* of the altar top. They say nothing about where the altar is actually situated. In principle, Rabbi Yosi could hold that the entire altar is in the south and still permit the entire altar top by virtue of the verse. And Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda could hold that the altar is in the south and still divide the top in half based on the same verse. The question of where each *tanna* holds the altar to be situated must come from elsewhere — from some independent source or tradition — not from their positions in the *mishnah*. The tradition that for Rabbi Yosi the entire altar stands in the north must be grounded in some Tannaitic text.

## Below Altar Scenarios

### Ground Below Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּעָבֵיד מְחִילּוֹת בַּקַּרְקַע וְשָׁחֵיט בְּהוּ – וְכִי הַאי גַּוְונָא מִי הָוֵי מִזְבֵּחַ?! וְהָתַנְיָא: ״מִזְבַּח אֲדָמָה תַּעֲשֶׂה לִּי״ – שֶׁיְּהֵא מְחוּבָּר (מֵאֲדָמָה) [בַּאֲדָמָה]; שֶׁלֹּא יִבְנֶנּוּ לֹא עַל גַּבֵּי מְחִילּוֹת וְלֹא עַל גַּבֵּי כִּיפִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the case is **where one dug tunnels in the ground** beneath the altar, **and slaughtered** the offerings **in them, in a case like this would** the **altar** itself **be** fit for use so that according to Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, one may slaughter the offerings of the most sacred order on the altar but not on the ground? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"An altar of earth you shall make for Me"** (Exodus 20:21)? This verse indicates **that** the altar **must be attached to the earth,** so **that one may not build it on top of tunnels nor on top of arches.**

Having established that the invalidity of slaughtering directly beneath the northern half of the altar at ground level is part of Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's position, the *gemara* turns to a pointed practical question: *mai kenegdan bakarka* — how does one actually get to ground level directly beneath the altar? The altar is sitting there. One cannot simply stand on the ground beneath it. Is this a hovering altar? A theoretical case with no real-world scenario?

### Sovev Solution

The first possibility: perhaps what the *gemara* means by "below the altar at ground level" is not the earth beneath the altar itself, but rather a position on the *sovev* — the walk-around ledge — or on the *yesod* — the base course. One would be at ground level in the sense of standing on an outer structural element rather than on the top of the altar. This is rejected: *hai gufa mizbeach hu* — that is itself the altar. The *sovev* and the *yesod* are integral parts of the *mizbeach*. A dispensation for slaughtering on the altar would encompass them as well. The case of slaughtering "below the altar at ground level" must refer to actual earth, not to architectural features of the altar structure. Moreover, the phrasing *kenegdan bakarka* — "opposite them on the ground" — plainly indicates actual ground, not a structural platform.

### Tunnels Rejected

The next possibility: perhaps one excavates tunnels (*mechilot*) or vaulted underground spaces (*kippin*) directly beneath the northern half of the altar, creating a cavity in which a person could stand on the ground while physically positioned beneath the altar's northern portion. But this is ruled out by a *beraita*: *mizbeach adama ta'aseh li* — "you shall make for Me an altar of earth" — is interpreted to mean the altar must be connected to solid ground. Specifically, *she-lo yivnennu agav mechilot ve-lo agav kippin* — one may not build the altar over tunnels or over vaulted underground spaces. The altar must rest on uninterrupted compressed earth; any cavity beneath it would compromise its status. The *gemara* takes this requirement to mean that there can be no hollow space anywhere beneath the altar's footprint — a stringent reading of *adama* as demanding that the altar be truly integrated with the ground beneath it.

### Reconfiguration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּבַצְּרֵיהּ בַּצּוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** to have the phrase: On the ground opposite the northern half of the altar, in order to teach the *halakha* in a case **where one minimized** the dimensions of the altar and slaughtered the offerings on the ground where the northern half of the altar had previously stood.

The *gemara* settles on a third scenario: *de-batzrei bitzurei* — the altar was reconfigured and reduced in size. In some circumstances it was apparently possible to alter the dimensions of the altar (the dimensions specified as *halachah le-Moshe mi-Sinai* are not the dimensions of the Second Temple altar), and if the altar was made smaller, a strip of the ground that previously lay beneath the northern part of the altar would be exposed. A person could stand there on actual ground, directly opposite where the northern half had been. The *gemara* confirms this is the intended scenario. And crucially, on this reading, the ruling that slaughtering there is invalid carries a clear implication: the ground that was formerly under the northern half of the altar, once exposed, is not in the northern zone — it is in the south. This means the altar in Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's view is situated entirely in the south, with the northern half of its top permitted only by scriptural dispensation, not because it occupies the northern zone.

## Second Pyre Tannaim

### Tamid Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אֶפְשָׁר אִיתָא לְהָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְלָא תְּנֵינָא לַהּ בְּמַתְנִיתִין?! נְפַק, דַּק וְאַשְׁכַּח – דִּתְנַן: בֵּירְרוּ מִשָּׁם עֲצֵי תְאֵינָה יָפִים לְסַדֵּר מַעֲרָכָה שְׁנִיָּה שֶׁל קְטֹרֶת; כְּנֶגֶד קֶרֶן מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית, מָשׁוּךְ מִן הַקֶּרֶן כְּלַפֵּי צָפוֹן אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת; בְּאוֹמֶד חָמֵשׁ סְאִין גֶּחָלִים. וּבְשַׁבָּת – בְּאוֹמֶד שְׁמוֹנֶה סְאִין גֶּחָלִים, שֶׁשָּׁם הָיוּ נוֹתְנִין שְׁנֵי בְּזִיכֵי לְבוֹנָה שֶׁל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to discuss Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that according to Rabbi Yosei the entire altar was located in the northern section of the Temple courtyard. The Gemara had mentioned that Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement is an independent statement, not based on the mishna. **Rabbi Zeira said:** Is it **possible** that **this** statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan is** correct **and we did not learn it in** any **mishna?** Rabbi Zeira **went out, examined** the matter, **and discovered** a mishna that alludes to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Tamid* 2:5): The priests **selected fine wood of a fig tree from** the chamber of firewood, with which **to lay out a second arrangement** of wood on the altar so that coals from this arrangement could be used **for** burning the **incense.** This second arrangement was located **opposite** the **southwest corner** of the altar, **distanced from** the **corner northward** by **four cubits.** They would arrange enough wood which, when burned, would produce **approximately five *se'a*** of **coals. And on Shabbat,** there was enough wood to produce **approximately eight *se'a*** of **coals, as they would place there the two bowls of frankincense of the shewbread.**

Rabbi Zeira, recognizing that the tradition of Rabbi Yochanan must have some basis in Tannaitic literature, goes and searches and finds the following *mishnah* in tractate Tamid. The *mishnah* there describes the selection of wood to place on the *ma'aracha*, the arrangement of logs on the altar fire. Specifically, the priests would select fine fig wood for the *ma'aracha shniya shel ktoreth* — the second pyre designated for the purpose of burning the incense. This second fire on the altar top was distinct from the main central fire on which the *korbanot* were consumed. From the second pyre, coals would be taken to the inner golden altar for the daily burning of the *ketoreth*. Rabbi Rashi notes that fig wood may have been preferred for its quality, and also suggests a connection to the fig leaves with which Adam covered himself after his sin, giving the wood a kind of redemptive association. Regardless, this second arrangement of wood was maintained specifically for the incense service.

### Four Amot Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי סִימָנָא? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה סִימָן – כׇּל הַנִּיטָּל בִּפְנִים לִינָּתֵן בַּחוּץ, אֵינוֹ נוֹתֵן אֶלָּא בְּסָמוּךְ שֶׁאֵין לִפְנִים; וְכׇל הַנִּיטָּל בַּחוּץ לִינָּתֵן לִפְנִים, אֵינוֹ נִיטָּל אֶלָּא בְּסָמוּךְ שֶׁאֵין לִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the** significance of the mishna's **defining** the precise location of the arrangement and the fact that this is where the frankincense is burned? The Gemara embarks on a lengthy discussion to answer this question: This mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who holds that this arrangement of wood and the burning of the frankincense must be in that precise location, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says: This** is the **principle** of where sacrificial items are placed on the inner and outer altars: **Any** sacrificial item **that is taken** from the altar located **inside** the Sanctuary in order **to be placed** on the altar **outside** the Sanctuary, in the Temple courtyard, **may be placed only on** the area of that altar that is **near** the Sanctuary, so **that there is no** area closer to the **inside** of the Sanctuary. It must therefore be placed on the part of the external altar closest to the entrance to the Sanctuary. **And any** sacrificial item **that is taken** from the altar **outside** the Sanctuary, in the Temple courtyard, in order **to be placed** on the altar located **inside** the Sanctuary, **may be taken only from** the area of the external altar that is **near** the Sanctuary, so **that there is no** area closer to the **inside** of the Sanctuary, i.e., from the part of the external altar closest to the entrance to the Sanctuary.

The *mishnah* in Tamid specifies where on the altar this second pyre was situated: *keneged keren maaravit dromit* — opposite the southwestern corner — *mashuch min hakeren klapei tzafon arba amot* — set back from the corner four *amot* in the direction of the north. In other words, at the southwestern corner of the altar top, four *amot* removed northward from the southernmost edge.

The pyre was sized to produce *be-omed chamesh se'in gechalim* — approximately five *se'in* of coals, sufficient for the coal-taking required for the daily incense offering. On *Shabbat*, the fire was built larger to produce approximately eight *se'in* of coals, since on *Shabbat* coals were also required to burn the frankincense of the *lechem hapanim* — the show-bread — on the outer altar.

The *mishnah* in Tamid adds: *ve-sham hayu notnim shenei bezikhei levonah shel lechem hapanim* — and it was there that they would place the two scoops of frankincense of the *lechem hapanim*. The fire at the southwestern corner therefore served a dual purpose: it supplied the coals for the daily *ketoreth*, and it was the place where the *levonah* from the *shulchan* was burned on *Shabbat*.

### Inside/Outside Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הַנִּיטָּל בִּפְנִים לִינָּתֵן בַּחוּץ – מַאי נִיהוּ? אִילֵּימָא שִׁירַיִם, בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״אֶל יְסוֹד מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה אֲשֶׁר פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״! וְתוּ, כֹּל הַנִּיטָּל בַּחוּץ לִינָּתֵן בִּפְנִים – מַאי נִיהוּ? אִילֵּימָא גֶּחָלִים שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בְּהוּ: ״וְלָקַח מְלֹא הַמַּחְתָּה גַּחֲלֵי אֵשׁ וְגוֹ׳״! אֶלָּא כׇּל הַנִּיטָּל בִּפְנִים לִינָּתֵן בַּחוּץ – שְׁנֵי בְּזִיכֵי לְבוֹנָה שֶׁל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, דְּגָמְרִי מִשִּׁירַיִם. הַנִּיטָּל בַּחוּץ לִינָּתֵן בִּפְנִים – גֶּחָלִים דְּכֹל יוֹמָא וְיוֹמָא, דְּגָמְרָן מִגֶּחָלִים שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: With regard to the statement concerning **any** sacrificial item **that is taken** from the altar located **inside** the Sanctuary in order **to be placed** on the altar **outside** the Sanctuary, **what are these** items? **If we say** they are the **remaining** blood of the sin offerings whose blood is presented on the inner altar, there is no reason for Rabbi Yosei to formulate his principle, as **it is explicitly written concerning them:** "And all the remaining blood of the bull he shall pour out **at the base of the altar of the burnt offering, which is at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 4:7). This verse describes how the blood of the inner sin offerings that remained after the sprinkling was to be poured out on the base of the west side of the altar, which is the side closest to the entrance to the Sanctuary. **And furthermore,** with regard to the statement about **any** sacrificial item **that is taken** from the altar **outside** the Sanctuary, in the Temple courtyard, in order **to be placed** on the altar located **inside** the Sanctuary, **what are these** items? **If we say** these are the **coals of** the **Yom Kippur** service, which must be taken from the western side of the altar, **it is explicitly written concerning them: "And he shall take a coal pan full of coals of fire** from off the altar before the Lord" (Leviticus 16:12). The expression "before the Lord" is a reference to the western side of the altar, which is the side closest to the Sanctuary. The Gemara continues: **Rather,** Rabbi Yosei's statement with regard to **any** sacrificial item **that is taken** from the altar located **inside** the Sanctuary in order **to be placed** on the altar **outside** the Sanctuary is referring to the **two bowls of frankincense of the shewbread.** According to Rabbi Yosei, they must be burned on the western side of the altar, **as he derives** this *halakha* **from** the location on the base of the altar where the **remaining** blood of the inner sin offerings is poured. Rabbi Yosei's next statement, that any sacrificial item **that is taken** from **the** altar **outside** the Sanctuary in order **to be placed on** the altar located **inside** the Sanctuary may be taken only from the area of the external altar that is near the Sanctuary, is referring to the **coals that** are taken from the external altar **each and every day** and placed on the inner altar in order to burn the incense. According to Rabbi Yosei these coals must be taken from the western side of the altar, **as we derive** this *halakha* **from** the location on the altar from where the **coals of** the **Yom Kippur** service must be taken.

The *mishnah* in Tamid then poses the question: *u-mah simanah* — what is the identifying marker? That is, what is the *siman* that pins down this location? *Rabbi Yosi hi* — the *mishnah* is in accordance with Rabbi Yosi. And the *gemara* cites the relevant *beraita*: *kol ha-nital bifnim lenatan bachutz eino nital ela besamuch* — anything taken from inside (the *heichal*) to be placed outside (in the *azarah*) is taken out only adjacent to the exit. Symmetrically, anything taken from outside to be brought inside is taken only from the place nearest the entrance of the *heichal*.

This principle is attributed to Rabbi Yosi, and it is a principle rather than an explicit verse — which is why it serves as a *siman* rather than a derivation from *pesukim*. The *gemara* clarifies what kinds of acts fall under each half of this rule. One might have thought that the "inside to outside" half refers to the spilling of the remaining blood (*shirayim*) at the base of the altar — but that is explicitly stated in a verse: *el yesod mizbeach ha-olah asher petach ohel moed* — "at the base of the burnt offering altar which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting." No *siman* of Rabbi Yosi is needed there. Similarly, the "outside to inside" half might have referred to the coals taken on *Yom Kippur*, but that too is explicit: *velakach melo hamachtah gechalei esh me'al hamizbeach milifnei Hashem* — "and he shall take a pan full of burning coals from the altar before God" — meaning from the place nearest the *heichal*.

### Frankincense Evidence

The *gemara* therefore identifies the actual subjects of Rabbi Yosi's rule. For "inside to outside," the application is *shenei bezikhei levonah shel lechem hapanim* — the two scoops of frankincense from the *shulchan*. These are taken from inside the *heichal* and brought out to be burned on the outer altar, and no verse specifies where on the outer altar this is done. Rabbi Yosi's principle establishes that it must be done adjacent to where one exits — that is, at the western portion of the altar, the side nearest the *heichal* entrance. For "outside to inside," the application is *gechalim d'chol yoma v'yoma* — the coals taken daily for the burning of the *ketoreth*. These are taken from the outer altar to be brought inside to the golden altar, and while *Yom Kippur* coals are explicitly required to come from *milifnei Hashem* — the front of the altar, near the *heichal* — no verse specifies this for the daily coal-taking. Rabbi Yosi derives it by analogy.

The second pyre thus stands at the western side of the altar because both the frankincense and the daily coals must be handled adjacent to the *heichal* entrance, and the western face of the altar is the face nearest that entrance. It is specifically the southwest because when ascending the altar — which one does from the south — the southwestern corner is the first part of the western face that one reaches, making it the nearest practicable point. This *mishnah* in Tamid therefore reflects Rabbi Yosi's principle and is attributed to him.

## Four Amot Calculation

### All South Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ בְּדָרוֹם קָאֵי, עֶשְׂרִים וְשֶׁבַע בָּעֵי לְמֵיתֵי! וְאִי נָמֵי קָסָבַר קְדוּשַּׁת הֵיכָל וְאוּלָם חֲדָא הִיא, עֶשְׂרִים וְתַרְתֵּי בָּעֵי מֵיתֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses Rabbi Yosei's opinion that the second arrangement of wood was placed four cubits north of the southwest corner of the altar: **And what does** Rabbi Yosei **hold** about the placement of the altar in the Temple courtyard? **If he holds** that **the entire altar stands in the south** side of the Temple courtyard, then only the five northernmost cubits of the altar are opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary. Accordingly, in order for the arrangement of wood to be opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary, one is **required to move** it **twenty-seven** cubits north of the southwest corner of the altar. The Gemara continues: **And even if** Rabbi Yosei **holds** that the level of **sanctity of** the **Sanctuary and** the **Entrance Hall is the same,** so that the arrangement of wood can be opposite the Entrance Hall, which is ten cubits wider than the Sanctuary, his opinion is still difficult. In order for the arrangement of wood to be opposite the Entrance Hall, one is **required to move** it **twenty-two** cubits north of the southwest corner of the altar.

Now that the *mishnah* in Tamid has been attributed to Rabbi Yosi, the *gemara* asks what the measurement of four *amot* from the southern edge tells us about where Rabbi Yosi held the altar to be situated. The logic is as follows: the second pyre must be positioned *keneged* — opposite — the opening of the *heichal*. It must fall within the zone of the doorway. The *gemara* considers three scenarios.

If Rabbi Yosi held that the entire altar stands in the south, the center of the *mikdash* and the center of the altar would not coincide. The door of the *heichal* is in the center of the *mikdash*, spanning ten *amot* — five to each side of the midline. If the entire thirty-two-*amah* altar is in the southern half, then only five *amot* of the altar's width fall within the zone of the door. To get the fire on the altar top to a position opposite the door, one would have to move it at least twenty-seven *amot* northward from the southernmost edge — and at that point one would not describe it as being at the southwest corner but at the northwest corner. Alternatively, if the sanctity of the *heichal* and the *ulam* are considered a single continuous unit, the door of the *ulam* was twenty *amot* wide, giving ten *amot* in the southern zone — but even then, one would need to move the fire at least twenty-two *amot* from the edge. In neither case does "four *amot* from the south" make sense.

### Half/Half Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי קָסָבַר חֶצְיוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן וְחֶצְיוֹ בַּדָּרוֹם, חַד סְרֵי בָּעֵי לְמֵיתֵי! וְאֶלָּא קָסָבַר קְדוּשַּׁת הֵיכָל וְאוּלָם חֲדָא מִילְּתָא הִיא? שֵׁית בָּעֵי לְמֵיתֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** even **if** Rabbi Yosei **holds** that **half of** the altar was located **in the north** side of the Temple courtyard **and half in the south,** in order for the arrangement of wood to be opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary one is still **required to move** it **eleven** cubits to the north of the southwest corner of the altar. **And** if one suggests that, **rather, he holds** that **the sanctity of** the **Sanctuary and** the **Entrance Hall is one matter,** i.e., equal, one is still **required to move** the arrangement of wood **six** cubits from the southwest corner in order to be opposite the Entrance Hall.

If the altar is centered — half in the north, half in the south — then the midline of the altar aligns with the midline of the *mikdash*. The door of the *heichal* (ten *amot* wide) gives five *amot* in each hemisphere. The altar is sixteen *amot* into the southern hemisphere from its southernmost point to center. To reach the zone of the door, one would need to move at least eleven *amot* from the southern edge. If the *ulam* door suffices (twenty *amot* wide, ten in each hemisphere), one would need to move at least six *amot* from the edge. Still not four.

### All North Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ בְּצָפוֹן קָאֵי? וְהָנֵי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – אַמָּה יְסוֹד, וְאַמָּה סוֹבֵב, וְאַמָּה מָקוֹם קְרָנוֹת, וְאַמָּה מְקוֹם רַגְלֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים; דְּכִי מְסַגּוּ לְקַמֵּיהּ טְפֵי – תּוּ לֵיכָּא פֶּתַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** is it **not** that Rabbi Yosei's opinion that the arrangement of wood was located four cubits from the altar's southwest corner is **due to** the fact **that he holds** that **the entire altar stands in the north** side of the Temple courtyard? Accordingly, only the five southernmost cubits of the altar were opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary. **And** of **these four cubits** from which the arrangement of wood was distanced from the southwest corner of the altar, one **cubit** was the **base** of the altar; **and** one **cubit** was **the surrounding ledge** of the altar; **and** one **cubit** was the **place** where the **corners** of the altar were located; **and** another **cubit** was **the place of the feet of the priests,** i.e., space for the priests to walk around the perimeter of the surface of the altar in order to perform the sacrificial rites. The arrangement of wood was located specifically in that location, **as if** one were to **move** it **farther away** from the southwest corner of the altar, it would **no longer** be opposite the **entrance** to the Sanctuary. This proves that according to Rabbi Yosei, the entire altar was located in the northern side of the Temple courtyard, as stated by Rabbi Yoḥanan.

If the entire altar is in the north, the problem is reversed. The door of the *heichal* lies in the center of the *mikdash*, but since the altar is entirely in the northern hemisphere, the fire must stay as far south as possible — within the five *amot* of the door that fall into the northern zone. One may not push the fire any further north than that without departing from the zone of the door entirely.

### Base Components

The critical question, then, is: how much of the altar's vertical structure must one traverse before reaching the surface where the fire can be placed? Counting from the base of the altar upward: one *amah* for the *yesod*, one *amah* for the *sovev*, one *amah* for the space of the corner projections, and one *amah* for the walkway space used by the *kohanim* to move around the altar top. That totals four *amot*. Before one has any open surface available to place the fire, four *amot* of the altar's southern face have already been accounted for. The fire therefore sits at exactly the first available position — the immediate southernmost opening on the top surface — which is four *amot* above the base. At five *amot* above the base, the zone of the door would end; anything beyond that would fall outside the range of the *heichal* opening. So the measurement of four *amot* is actually a maximum: put the fire at the first available spot on the surface, and you are exactly within the five-*amah* range of the door.

This reading only works if the entire altar is in the north. Any other configuration forces the fire further northward — well beyond four *amot* from the base — to reach the zone of the door. The attribution of this *mishnah* in Tamid to Rabbi Yosi thus provides the Tannaitic grounding for Rabbi Yochanan's claim that Rabbi Yosi held *kulo mizbeach batzafon kai*.

## Adda Bar Ahava View

### Yehuda Centered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִזְבֵּחַ מְמוּצָּע וְעוֹמֵד בְּאֶמְצַע הָעֲזָרָה; ושְׁלֹשִׁים וּשְׁתַּיִם אַמּוֹת הָיוּ לוֹ – עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת כְּנֶגֶד פִּתְחוֹ שֶׁל הֵיכָל, אַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה מִיכָּן, וְאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה מִיכָּן; נִמְצָא מִזְבֵּחַ מְכוָּּון כְּנֶגֶד הֵיכָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this proof by suggesting an alternative explanation of the mishna in *Tamid*: **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: The altar was centered and standing** precisely **in the middle of the** Temple **courtyard, and it was thirty-two cubits** wide. **Ten cubits** were **opposite the entrance to** the **Sanctuary, eleven cubits** were **to this** side of the entrance to the Sanctuary, **and eleven cubits** were **to that** side of the entrance to the Sanctuary. It **turns out** that the length of the **altar was aligned opposite** the width of the **Sanctuary,** which itself was thirty-two cubits wide.

Rav Adda bar Ahava challenges this attribution. He proposes that the *mishnah* in Tamid belongs not to Rabbi Yosi but to Rabbi Yehuda. *Detanya: Rabbi Yehuda omer: mizbeach memutza ve-omed be-emtza azara* — Rabbi Yehuda explicitly states that the altar is centered in the *azarah*, positioned exactly halfway between north and south. Furthermore, *u-sheloshim u-shtayim amah hayu lo* — and its measure was thirty-two *amot*. And Rabbi Yehuda adds: *eser amot keneged pitcho shel heichal, achat esrei amah mikan ve-achat esrei amah mikan* — ten *amot* opposite the door of the *heichal*, and eleven *amot* to each side. The result is that the altar is precisely aligned with the *heichal*: *nimtza mizbeach mechuvvan keneged heichal* — the altar is found to be perfectly opposite the *heichal*. Rashi notes that this alignment is exact in both directions: the inner width of the *heichal* was twenty *amot*, and with six-*amah* thick walls on each side, the total structure was thirty-two *amot* — identical to the altar — and both are centered on the same axis.

### Top Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה חַד סְרֵי בָּעֵי לְמֵיתֵי; וְאִי קָסָבַר קְדוּשַּׁת הֵיכָל וְאוּלָם חֲדָא מִילְּתָא הִיא, שֵׁית בָּעֵי לְמֵיתֵי! מִי סָבְרַתְּ הָנֵי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – בַּהֲדֵי אַמָּה יְסוֹד וְאַמָּה סוֹבֵב?! אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בַּר מֵאַמָּה יְסוֹד וְאַמָּה סוֹבֵב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, according to Rabbi Yehuda,** one is still **required to move** the arrangement of wood **eleven** cubits from the southwest corner in order for it to be opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary. **And** even **if he holds** that **the sanctity of** the **Sanctuary and** the **Entrance Hall is one matter,** i.e., equal, one is still **required to move** the arrangement of wood **six** cubits in order to be opposite the Entrance Hall. Therefore, the mishna, which states that the arrangement of wood is four cubits north of the southwest corner, cannot be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. The Gemara responds: **Do you hold** that **these four cubits include** the **cubit** of the **base** of the altar **and** the **cubit** of the **surrounding ledge** of the altar? Actually, the **four cubits** are **aside from** the **cubit** of the **base** of the altar **and** the **cubit** of the **surrounding ledge** of the altar. Accordingly, the arrangement of wood was actually a total of six cubits from the southwest corner of the altar, and the mishna can be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

On the assumption that the altar is centered and half in the north, one would need to move the fire at least eleven *amot* northward from the southern edge to reach the zone of the *heichal* door, or at least six *amot* to reach the zone of the *ulam* door. How, then, does Rabbi Yehuda's position accommodate the "four *amot*" of the Tamid *mishnah*?

The *gemara* answers with a clarification that represents a much more natural reading of the measurement: *mi kasavrat hani arba amot bahadi amah yesod ve-amah sovev* — who told you we are measuring from the base of the altar? The four *amot* are not counted from the ground up. They are counted from the top surface of the altar downward — or rather, from the edge of the top surface as one's starting point, moving inward. *Arba amot bar mimmah yesod ve-amah sovev* — the four *amot* are in addition to, and separate from, the *yesod* and *sovev*. The base and walk-around structure add two *amot* below the top surface. So four *amot* from the edge of the top surface equals six *amot* from the base of the altar. And six *amot* inward from the southern edge, on a centered altar, brings the fire exactly to the zone of the *ulam* door — which is the minimum required, assuming that the *heichal* and *ulam* are treated as a unified sacred precinct for this purpose.

This is, as noted, a more intuitive way of reading the measurement. A priest ascending to place the fire would naturally count from the surface he is standing on, not from the ground far below. Four *amot* from the surface edge is six *amot* from the base — and six *amot* from the southern edge of a centered, thirty-two-*amah* altar puts the fire at the entry zone of the *ulam* door.

### Attribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹקְמַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, וּבִמְמוּצָּע! מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה שְׁמַעְנָא לֵיהּ מְמוּצָּע בְּהֶדְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks why Rav Adda bar Ahava interpreted the mishna to be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda: **But let him interpret it** to be **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei and** explain that the altar is located **in the center** of the Temple courtyard. The Gemara explains: Rav Adda bar Ahava interpreted the mishna to be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda **because we heard that Rabbi Yehuda explicitly** said that the altar was positioned **in the center** of the Temple courtyard, whereas we did not hear that Rabbi Yosei maintains such an opinion.

The *gemara* now presses Rav Adda bar Ahava: having shown that the Tamid *mishnah* works for Rabbi Yehuda's position of a centered altar, why not then challenge Rabbi Yochanan's attribution entirely? One could argue that the *mishnah* in Tamid is consistent with Rabbi Yosi as well — that Rabbi Yosi holds the altar is centered, not entirely in the north, and the four *amot* is measured from the surface, yielding the same arithmetic. Why attribute the *mishnah* specifically to Rabbi Yehuda rather than simply saying that Rabbi Yosi holds it is half and half?

### Yehuda Explicit

The answer given: *mishum de-Rabbi Yehuda shamana lei mimutza behedya* — because we hear explicitly from Rabbi Yehuda that he holds the altar is centered. Rabbi Yehuda states this position outright in his own words. Once Rabbi Yehuda has explicitly articulated the position of a centered altar, and the Tamid *mishnah* fits that position, it is most natural to assign the *mishnah* to Rabbi Yehuda. But this answer concedes a limitation: there is now no compelling proof that Rabbi Yosi holds the entire altar is in the north. If Rabbi Yosi could be read to hold the altar is centered — just as Rabbi Yehuda explicitly does — then Rabbi Yochanan's assertion that Rabbi Yosi holds *kulo batzafon* remains without direct Tannaitic support from this *mishnah*.

## Yosi HaGalili

### HaGalili Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנָתַתָּ אֶת הַכִּיּוֹר בֵּין אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ וְגוֹמֵר,
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sherevya said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna in *Tamid*, which holds that the entire altar was located in the northern part of the Temple courtyard? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: Since it is stated: "And you shall set the Basin between the Tent of Meeting** and the altar" (Exodus 40:7), and another verse states: **"And**

Rav Sharvaya proposes yet another attribution: *ha mani Rabbi Yosi HaGlili hi* — the *mishnah* in Tamid belongs to Rabbi Yosi HaGalili. This is a distinct *tanna* from the Rabbi Yosi discussed throughout — the latter being Rabbi Yosi bar Chalafta, the standard referent of the unqualified name — and also distinct from Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosi HaGalili is a *tanna* of an earlier generation, and the *gemara* now introduces a *beraita* in his name that takes the position that the entire altar is in the north.

### Kiyor Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִזְבַּח הָעוֹלָה וְגוֹ׳״ – מִזְבֵּחַ בְּפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד, וְלֹא כִּיּוֹר בְּפֶתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד. הֵיכָן הָיָה נוֹתְנוֹ? בֵּין הָאוּלָם וְלַמִּזְבֵּחַ, מָשׁוּךְ קִימְעָא כְּלַפֵּי הַדָּרוֹם. מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר כּוּלֵּיהּ מִזְבֵּחַ בְּדָרוֹם קָאֵי – נוֹקְמֵיהּ מִכּוֹתֶל הֵיכָל וְלַדָּרוֹם, בֵּין אוּלָם לַמִּזְבֵּחַ! וְאִי נָמֵי קָסָבַר קְדוּשַּׁת הֵיכָל וְאוּלָם חֲדָא הִיא – נוֹקְמֵיהּ מִכּוֹתֶל אוּלָם וְלַדָּרוֹם, בְּבֵין אוּלָם לַמִּזְבֵּחַ! וְאִי נָמֵי קָסָבַר חֶצְיוֹ בַּצָּפוֹן וְחֶצְיוֹ בַּדָּרוֹם – נוֹקְמֵיהּ מִכּוֹתֶל הֵיכָל וְלַדָּרוֹם, בֵּין אוּלָם וְלַמִּזְבֵּחַ! וְאִי נָמֵי קָסָבַר קְדוּשַּׁת הָאוּלָם וְהֵיכָל חֲדָא הִיא – נוֹקְמֵיהּ מִכּוֹתֶל אוּלָם וְלַדָּרוֹם, בֵּין אוּלָם וְלַמִּזְבֵּחַ! אָמַר קְרָא: ״צָפוֹנָה״ – שֶׁיְּהֵא צָפוֹן פָּנוּי מִכֵּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **the altar of the burnt offering** he set at the entrance to the Tabernacle of the Tent of Meeting." (Exodus 40:29), indicating that no object was allowed to be located between the altar and the Tent of Meeting, whose parallel, in the Temple, was the Sanctuary. Rabbi Yosei HaGelili derives from these verses that only the **altar** stood **at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting, but** the **Basin** did **not** stand **at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting. Where would** they **place** the Basin? It was placed **between the Entrance Hall and the altar, extended slightly toward the south.** Therefore, although the Basin was closer to the Sanctuary than the altar was, it did not actually stand between the altar and the Sanctuary. The Gemara seeks to clarify: **What does** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **hold? If he holds** that **the entire altar stands in the south** section of the Temple courtyard, **let him stand** the Basin anywhere **from** where **the wall of** the **Sanctuary** begins **and southward,** so that it is not opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary but it is **between** the **Entrance Hall and the altar.** This would allow for the optimal fulfillment of both verses (Exodus 40:29; Leviticus 4:7), as the Basin would be located between the altar and the Sanctuary without interposing between the altar and the entrance to the Sanctuary. The Gemara continues: **And even if he holds** that **the** level of **sanctity of** the **Sanctuary and** the **Entrance Hall is the same,** in which case the Basin could not be located opposite the entrance to the Entrance Hall as this too would be considered a violation of the second verse, **let him stand** the Basin anywhere **from** where **the wall of** the **Entrance Hall** begins **and southward,** so that it is not opposite the entrance to the Entrance Hall but is **in between** the **Entrance Hall and the altar.** From the fact that Rabbi Yosei HaGelili did not say that, it is clear he held that the altar was not located in the southern section of the Temple courtyard. **Alternatively,** if Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **holds** that **half of** the altar was located **in the north** section of the Temple courtyard **and half of it** was located **in the south, let him stand** the Basin anywhere **from** where **the wall of** the **Sanctuary** begins **and southward,** so that it is not opposite the entrance to the Sanctuary but **between** the **Entrance Hall and the altar.** Since the entrance to the Sanctuary was five cubits wide and stood in the middle of the courtyard, and the altar was thirty-two cubits long, there would have been eleven cubits to both the north and the south of the entrance to the Sanctuary where the Basin could be placed in order to be located between the altar and the Sanctuary without interposing between the altar and the entrance to the Sanctuary. **And even if** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **holds** that **the** level of **sanctity of** the **Entrance Hall and** that of the **Sanctuary is the same,** in which case the Basin could not be located opposite the entrance to the Entrance Hall because this too would be considered a violation of the second verse, **let him stand** the Basin anywhere **from** where **the wall of** the **Entrance Hall** begins **and southward,** so that it is not opposite the entrance to the Entrance Hall but **in between** the **Entrance Hall and the altar.** Why does Rabbi Yosei HaGelili require that the Basin be placed south of the altar? The Gemara concludes: **Rather, is it not due to** the fact **that** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **holds** that **the entire altar** stood **in the north** section of the Temple courtyard? The Gemara asks: Even if this is the case, why does Rabbi Yosei HaGelili require that the Basin be located to the south of the altar so that it is not actually in between the altar and the Sanctuary? **Even if you stand it** anywhere **from** where **the wall of** the **Sanctuary** begins **and northward,** it would not interpose between the altar and the entrance to the Sanctuary but **it would** actually **be** located in **between** the **Entrance Hall and the altar.** **And** even if Rabbi Yosei HaGelili holds that the sanctity of the Entrance Hall is equal to that of the Sanctuary, and that the Basin cannot interpose between the altar and the entrance to the Entrance Hall, **stand** the Basin anywhere **from** where **the wall of** the **Entrance Hall** begins **and northward,** so that it is not opposite the entrance to the Entrance Hall but it is located **in between** the **Entrance Hall and the altar.** That suggestion is technically not feasible, as **the verse states:** "And he shall slaughter it on the side of the altar **northward [*tzafona*]"** (Leviticus 1:11). This verse indicates **that the north** section of the Temple courtyard must **be vacant of** all **vessels,** including the Basin. This concludes the Gemara's explanation of Rav Sherevya's assertion that the mishna in *Tamid*, which holds that the altar was located in the northern part of the Temple courtyard, is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.

*Detanya: Rabbi Yosi HaGalili omer: mitoch shenemar ve-natata kiyor bein ohel moed u-vein hamizbeach* — as it is written: "and you shall place the laver between the Tent of Meeting and the altar." Rabbi Yosi HaGalili derives the position of the altar from the placement of the *kiyor* — the laver. The verse specifies that the laver stands between the entrance of the Tent of Meeting and the altar. The analysis of exactly where this places the altar — and how the *kiyor*'s location implies that the entire *mizbeach* stands in the north — will be taken up in the next session.
