# Zevachim 58 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Slaughtering at Altar Top

### Perek Opening

Daf 58 opens a new *perek*, *Kodshei Kodashim*. Having devoted the previous *perek* to laying out the rules for each *korban* — where it is slaughtered, where its blood is applied — the Talmud now turns to the consequences of deviation from those rules. One might have wished that every *masechet* began this way: one *perek* establishing the basic rules, followed immediately by a *perek* exploring what happens when they are not followed. As it happens, we had to wait until the fifth *perek* for this opening statement, and now the sixth *perek* takes up the question of deviation.

### North Slaughter Rule

The foundational rule with which this *perek* begins is well established: *kodshei kodashim* — sacrifices of the highest sanctity — must have their *shechita* performed in the north. This was set out in the previous *perek*. The present *perek* asks: what happens when the rules are not followed perfectly?

### Top of Altar Issue

The first case the *mishnah* takes up is: what if the *kodshei kodashim* were slaughtered not in the north of the *azarah* opposite the altar, but on top of the altar itself? The issue here is not whether the top of the altar counts as valid ground for the purpose of *shechita* — one can assume the altar, as an extension connected to the floor, carries the same status as the ground below, especially given that *kohanim* perform their *avodah* while standing on the altar. The real question is one of geometry: if the *shechita* must be performed *al yerech hamizbeach tzafonah* — on the north side of the altar — does "north" refer to the northern half of the *mikdash* or to the north side of the altar itself? And when the animal is brought to the top of the altar, which portions of that top surface count as valid?

## Yosi Positions

### Yosi: Whole Altar

*Rabbi Yosi omer: ke'ilu nishchetu batzafon* — Rabbi Yosi says that if *kodshei kodashim* were slaughtered on top of the altar, it is as if they were slaughtered in the north. The phrasing is immediately striking: he says "as if" (*ke'ilu*) they were slaughtered in the north — not that they actually were. This invites the question of what the qualification is. If Rabbi Yosi holds that the entire altar is situated in the north of the *mikdash*, why would he describe slaughter on top of it as merely "as if" in the north? One would expect him simply to say it is in the north.

### Yosi bar Yehuda: Half/Ha…

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda offers a different ruling: *mechatzi hamizbeach uledarom kadarom, mechatzi hamizbeach uletzafon katzafon* — from the midpoint of the altar southward, it is treated as the south; from the midpoint northward, it is treated as the north. This position reflects the intuitive assumption that the altar is centered in the *azarah*, straddling the dividing line between north and south. On that reading, the northern half of the altar lies in the northern hemisphere of the *mikdash* and is valid for *kodshei kodashim*, while the southern half is not.

### As If Analysis

The tension in Rabbi Yosi's language now becomes the *gemara*'s first point of inquiry. Rav Assi, citing Rabbi Yochanan, reports that Rabbi Yosi held *kulo mizbeach batzafon kai* — the entire altar stands in the north. On that understanding, the phrase *ke'ilu* — "as if" — is not conceding that the top of the altar falls short of the northern designation. Rather, it addresses a different concern: one might have thought that *al yerech hamizbeach* requires the *shechita* to take place specifically on the side of the altar, not on its top surface — much as a hole-in-one is not the same as getting the ball closest to the hole. The word *ke'ilu* therefore comes to teach us that slaughtering on the top of the altar itself is sufficient and counts as if it were performed along the side, provided the location falls within the northern zone of the *mikdash* opposite the altar (*kineged hamizbeach*).

## Yochanan's Approach

### Altar in North

The tradition reported by Rav Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is therefore that Rabbi Yosi's position — that the whole top of the altar is valid — reflects his view that the entire altar stands in the north. The *ke'ilu* of the *mishnah* is not concessive but pedagogic: it comes to forestall the misreading that *al yerech* demands contact with the side rather than the top.

### Zeira Challenge

Rabbi Zeira challenges Rav Assi. His challenge is reasonable: if Rabbi Yosi's ruling that the whole altar top is valid reflects his view that the whole altar is in the north, then by the same logic, Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's ruling that only the northern half of the altar top is valid must reflect his view that the altar is positioned half in the north and half in the south. The debate in the *mishnah*, on this reading, is simply a debate about where the altar is situated. Rabbi Zeira presses this point: *v'chi teima hachi nami* — isn't that exactly the right inference?

### Below Altar Proof

Rav Assi responds by pointing to a tradition he himself had cited in Rabbi Yochanan's name: *modei Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda she'im shachtan kenegdan bakarka pesulah* — Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda concedes that if one slaughtered *kodshei kodashim* directly below the northern half of the altar at ground level, the *shechita* is invalid.

The challenge posed by this ruling is conceptual. To understand the ruling, one must first understand what scenario is being described, but let us begin with its implication. If Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda held that the altar is situated half in the north and half in the south — that is, if the north half of the altar is genuinely in the northern zone — then slaughtering directly beneath the northern half, at ground level, should be valid: one would be in the north. The fact that it is invalid suggests that the ground directly beneath the altar's northern half is not, in fact, situated in the northern zone. That can only be the case if the altar is not half-north, half-south at all, but is entirely situated in the south. The northern half of the altar top is valid only by virtue of a special scriptural dispensation — not because the altar occupies the north. And what is below the altar, absent any such dispensation, remains in the south and is therefore invalid.

One might have proposed a different explanation: that ground-level space directly beneath the north of the altar is invalid not because it is not in the north, but because it is not *al yerech hamizbeach* — it is neither the altar itself nor north of the altar in any meaningful sense. The airspace extends upward, but not downward. The *gemara*, however, does not accept this logic. It reads the invalidity of ground-level slaughter directly below the northern half as definitive evidence that that space is not in the northern zone — implying that the altar, for Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, is situated entirely in the south.

### Verse Interpretation

With this, the *gemara* recasts the entire *mishnah* debate. Rabbi Yochanan's conclusion: *shneihem mikra echad darshu* — both Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda are debating the interpretation of a single verse, not staking out positions about where the altar is physically situated.

The verse in question is *vezavachta alav et olotecha ve'et shelamecha* — "and you shall slaughter upon it your burnt offerings and your peace offerings." The word *alav* — "upon it" — is read with unusual literalism: one may slaughter directly on top of the altar. This is striking, and Tosafos on the first *tosafos* of this *perek* notes explicitly that according to this verse, *lechatchila* one is permitted to perform *shechita* on top of the altar from Torah law. The only possible rabbinic restriction, Tosafos suggests, is that one should not bring a live animal to the top of the altar because of the concern that it will defecate there — but from the Torah itself, the top of the altar is a valid location.

The debate between Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda is then about how to read this verse. Rabbi Yosi holds *kulo le'ola v'kulo lishlamim*: the entire top of the altar may be used for an *olah* and the entire top for a *shelamim*. The verse permits using the full surface for either. The fact that it mentions both *olotecha* and *shelamecha* is not to divide them; it is simply because one might have thought the dispensation was granted only for the *olah*, which has limited space in the *azarah* during busy periods, and not for the *shelamim*, which has ample space elsewhere. The verse comes to teach that the *shelamim* may also be slaughtered anywhere on top.

Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, reads the verse as dividing the altar top: *chatzi le'ola v'chatzi lishlamim* — half for the *olah* and half for the *shelamim*. The northern half is designated for the *olah*, the southern for the *shelamim*. This is structurally analogous to the debate about *Yom Tov* — whether it is entirely for God, entirely for oneself, or divided.

The upshot of Rabbi Yochanan's reformulation is decisive: the positions in the *mishnah* are about scriptural interpretation for this particular *halachah* of the altar top. They say nothing about where the altar is actually situated. In principle, Rabbi Yosi could hold that the entire altar is in the south and still permit the entire altar top by virtue of the verse. And Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda could hold that the altar is in the south and still divide the top in half based on the same verse. The question of where each *tanna* holds the altar to be situated must come from elsewhere — from some independent source or tradition — not from their positions in the *mishnah*. The tradition that for Rabbi Yosi the entire altar stands in the north must be grounded in some Tannaitic text.

## Below Altar Scenarios

### Ground Below Query

Having established that the invalidity of slaughtering directly beneath the northern half of the altar at ground level is part of Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's position, the *gemara* turns to a pointed practical question: *mai kenegdan bakarka* — how does one actually get to ground level directly beneath the altar? The altar is sitting there. One cannot simply stand on the ground beneath it. Is this a hovering altar? A theoretical case with no real-world scenario?

### Sovev Solution

The first possibility: perhaps what the *gemara* means by "below the altar at ground level" is not the earth beneath the altar itself, but rather a position on the *sovev* — the walk-around ledge — or on the *yesod* — the base course. One would be at ground level in the sense of standing on an outer structural element rather than on the top of the altar. This is rejected: *hai gufa mizbeach hu* — that is itself the altar. The *sovev* and the *yesod* are integral parts of the *mizbeach*. A dispensation for slaughtering on the altar would encompass them as well. The case of slaughtering "below the altar at ground level" must refer to actual earth, not to architectural features of the altar structure. Moreover, the phrasing *kenegdan bakarka* — "opposite them on the ground" — plainly indicates actual ground, not a structural platform.

### Tunnels Rejected

The next possibility: perhaps one excavates tunnels (*mechilot*) or vaulted underground spaces (*kippin*) directly beneath the northern half of the altar, creating a cavity in which a person could stand on the ground while physically positioned beneath the altar's northern portion. But this is ruled out by a *beraita*: *mizbeach adama ta'aseh li* — "you shall make for Me an altar of earth" — is interpreted to mean the altar must be connected to solid ground. Specifically, *she-lo yivnennu agav mechilot ve-lo agav kippin* — one may not build the altar over tunnels or over vaulted underground spaces. The altar must rest on uninterrupted compressed earth; any cavity beneath it would compromise its status. The *gemara* takes this requirement to mean that there can be no hollow space anywhere beneath the altar's footprint — a stringent reading of *adama* as demanding that the altar be truly integrated with the ground beneath it.

### Reconfiguration

The *gemara* settles on a third scenario: *de-batzrei bitzurei* — the altar was reconfigured and reduced in size. In some circumstances it was apparently possible to alter the dimensions of the altar (the dimensions specified as *halachah le-Moshe mi-Sinai* are not the dimensions of the Second Temple altar), and if the altar was made smaller, a strip of the ground that previously lay beneath the northern part of the altar would be exposed. A person could stand there on actual ground, directly opposite where the northern half had been. The *gemara* confirms this is the intended scenario. And crucially, on this reading, the ruling that slaughtering there is invalid carries a clear implication: the ground that was formerly under the northern half of the altar, once exposed, is not in the northern zone — it is in the south. This means the altar in Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda's view is situated entirely in the south, with the northern half of its top permitted only by scriptural dispensation, not because it occupies the northern zone.

## Second Pyre Tannaim

### Tamid Source

Rabbi Zeira, recognizing that the tradition of Rabbi Yochanan must have some basis in Tannaitic literature, goes and searches and finds the following *mishnah* in tractate Tamid. The *mishnah* there describes the selection of wood to place on the *ma'aracha*, the arrangement of logs on the altar fire. Specifically, the priests would select fine fig wood for the *ma'aracha shniya shel ktoreth* — the second pyre designated for the purpose of burning the incense. This second fire on the altar top was distinct from the main central fire on which the *korbanot* were consumed. From the second pyre, coals would be taken to the inner golden altar for the daily burning of the *ketoreth*. Rabbi Rashi notes that fig wood may have been preferred for its quality, and also suggests a connection to the fig leaves with which Adam covered himself after his sin, giving the wood a kind of redemptive association. Regardless, this second arrangement of wood was maintained specifically for the incense service.

### Four Amot Location

The *mishnah* in Tamid specifies where on the altar this second pyre was situated: *keneged keren maaravit dromit* — opposite the southwestern corner — *mashuch min hakeren klapei tzafon arba amot* — set back from the corner four *amot* in the direction of the north. In other words, at the southwestern corner of the altar top, four *amot* removed northward from the southernmost edge.

The pyre was sized to produce *be-omed chamesh se'in gechalim* — approximately five *se'in* of coals, sufficient for the coal-taking required for the daily incense offering. On *Shabbat*, the fire was built larger to produce approximately eight *se'in* of coals, since on *Shabbat* coals were also required to burn the frankincense of the *lechem hapanim* — the show-bread — on the outer altar.

The *mishnah* in Tamid adds: *ve-sham hayu notnim shenei bezikhei levonah shel lechem hapanim* — and it was there that they would place the two scoops of frankincense of the *lechem hapanim*. The fire at the southwestern corner therefore served a dual purpose: it supplied the coals for the daily *ketoreth*, and it was the place where the *levonah* from the *shulchan* was burned on *Shabbat*.

### Inside/Outside Principle

The *mishnah* in Tamid then poses the question: *u-mah simanah* — what is the identifying marker? That is, what is the *siman* that pins down this location? *Rabbi Yosi hi* — the *mishnah* is in accordance with Rabbi Yosi. And the *gemara* cites the relevant *beraita*: *kol ha-nital bifnim lenatan bachutz eino nital ela besamuch* — anything taken from inside (the *heichal*) to be placed outside (in the *azarah*) is taken out only adjacent to the exit. Symmetrically, anything taken from outside to be brought inside is taken only from the place nearest the entrance of the *heichal*.

This principle is attributed to Rabbi Yosi, and it is a principle rather than an explicit verse — which is why it serves as a *siman* rather than a derivation from *pesukim*. The *gemara* clarifies what kinds of acts fall under each half of this rule. One might have thought that the "inside to outside" half refers to the spilling of the remaining blood (*shirayim*) at the base of the altar — but that is explicitly stated in a verse: *el yesod mizbeach ha-olah asher petach ohel moed* — "at the base of the burnt offering altar which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting." No *siman* of Rabbi Yosi is needed there. Similarly, the "outside to inside" half might have referred to the coals taken on *Yom Kippur*, but that too is explicit: *velakach melo hamachtah gechalei esh me'al hamizbeach milifnei Hashem* — "and he shall take a pan full of burning coals from the altar before God" — meaning from the place nearest the *heichal*.

### Frankincense Evidence

The *gemara* therefore identifies the actual subjects of Rabbi Yosi's rule. For "inside to outside," the application is *shenei bezikhei levonah shel lechem hapanim* — the two scoops of frankincense from the *shulchan*. These are taken from inside the *heichal* and brought out to be burned on the outer altar, and no verse specifies where on the outer altar this is done. Rabbi Yosi's principle establishes that it must be done adjacent to where one exits — that is, at the western portion of the altar, the side nearest the *heichal* entrance. For "outside to inside," the application is *gechalim d'chol yoma v'yoma* — the coals taken daily for the burning of the *ketoreth*. These are taken from the outer altar to be brought inside to the golden altar, and while *Yom Kippur* coals are explicitly required to come from *milifnei Hashem* — the front of the altar, near the *heichal* — no verse specifies this for the daily coal-taking. Rabbi Yosi derives it by analogy.

The second pyre thus stands at the western side of the altar because both the frankincense and the daily coals must be handled adjacent to the *heichal* entrance, and the western face of the altar is the face nearest that entrance. It is specifically the southwest because when ascending the altar — which one does from the south — the southwestern corner is the first part of the western face that one reaches, making it the nearest practicable point. This *mishnah* in Tamid therefore reflects Rabbi Yosi's principle and is attributed to him.

## Four Amot Calculation

### All South Theory

Now that the *mishnah* in Tamid has been attributed to Rabbi Yosi, the *gemara* asks what the measurement of four *amot* from the southern edge tells us about where Rabbi Yosi held the altar to be situated. The logic is as follows: the second pyre must be positioned *keneged* — opposite — the opening of the *heichal*. It must fall within the zone of the doorway. The *gemara* considers three scenarios.

If Rabbi Yosi held that the entire altar stands in the south, the center of the *mikdash* and the center of the altar would not coincide. The door of the *heichal* is in the center of the *mikdash*, spanning ten *amot* — five to each side of the midline. If the entire thirty-two-*amah* altar is in the southern half, then only five *amot* of the altar's width fall within the zone of the door. To get the fire on the altar top to a position opposite the door, one would have to move it at least twenty-seven *amot* northward from the southernmost edge — and at that point one would not describe it as being at the southwest corner but at the northwest corner. Alternatively, if the sanctity of the *heichal* and the *ulam* are considered a single continuous unit, the door of the *ulam* was twenty *amot* wide, giving ten *amot* in the southern zone — but even then, one would need to move the fire at least twenty-two *amot* from the edge. In neither case does "four *amot* from the south" make sense.

### Half/Half Theory

If the altar is centered — half in the north, half in the south — then the midline of the altar aligns with the midline of the *mikdash*. The door of the *heichal* (ten *amot* wide) gives five *amot* in each hemisphere. The altar is sixteen *amot* into the southern hemisphere from its southernmost point to center. To reach the zone of the door, one would need to move at least eleven *amot* from the southern edge. If the *ulam* door suffices (twenty *amot* wide, ten in each hemisphere), one would need to move at least six *amot* from the edge. Still not four.

### All North Theory

If the entire altar is in the north, the problem is reversed. The door of the *heichal* lies in the center of the *mikdash*, but since the altar is entirely in the northern hemisphere, the fire must stay as far south as possible — within the five *amot* of the door that fall into the northern zone. One may not push the fire any further north than that without departing from the zone of the door entirely.

### Base Components

The critical question, then, is: how much of the altar's vertical structure must one traverse before reaching the surface where the fire can be placed? Counting from the base of the altar upward: one *amah* for the *yesod*, one *amah* for the *sovev*, one *amah* for the space of the corner projections, and one *amah* for the walkway space used by the *kohanim* to move around the altar top. That totals four *amot*. Before one has any open surface available to place the fire, four *amot* of the altar's southern face have already been accounted for. The fire therefore sits at exactly the first available position — the immediate southernmost opening on the top surface — which is four *amot* above the base. At five *amot* above the base, the zone of the door would end; anything beyond that would fall outside the range of the *heichal* opening. So the measurement of four *amot* is actually a maximum: put the fire at the first available spot on the surface, and you are exactly within the five-*amah* range of the door.

This reading only works if the entire altar is in the north. Any other configuration forces the fire further northward — well beyond four *amot* from the base — to reach the zone of the door. The attribution of this *mishnah* in Tamid to Rabbi Yosi thus provides the Tannaitic grounding for Rabbi Yochanan's claim that Rabbi Yosi held *kulo mizbeach batzafon kai*.

## Adda Bar Ahava View

### Yehuda Centered

Rav Adda bar Ahava challenges this attribution. He proposes that the *mishnah* in Tamid belongs not to Rabbi Yosi but to Rabbi Yehuda. *Detanya: Rabbi Yehuda omer: mizbeach memutza ve-omed be-emtza azara* — Rabbi Yehuda explicitly states that the altar is centered in the *azarah*, positioned exactly halfway between north and south. Furthermore, *u-sheloshim u-shtayim amah hayu lo* — and its measure was thirty-two *amot*. And Rabbi Yehuda adds: *eser amot keneged pitcho shel heichal, achat esrei amah mikan ve-achat esrei amah mikan* — ten *amot* opposite the door of the *heichal*, and eleven *amot* to each side. The result is that the altar is precisely aligned with the *heichal*: *nimtza mizbeach mechuvvan keneged heichal* — the altar is found to be perfectly opposite the *heichal*. Rashi notes that this alignment is exact in both directions: the inner width of the *heichal* was twenty *amot*, and with six-*amah* thick walls on each side, the total structure was thirty-two *amot* — identical to the altar — and both are centered on the same axis.

### Top Measurement

On the assumption that the altar is centered and half in the north, one would need to move the fire at least eleven *amot* northward from the southern edge to reach the zone of the *heichal* door, or at least six *amot* to reach the zone of the *ulam* door. How, then, does Rabbi Yehuda's position accommodate the "four *amot*" of the Tamid *mishnah*?

The *gemara* answers with a clarification that represents a much more natural reading of the measurement: *mi kasavrat hani arba amot bahadi amah yesod ve-amah sovev* — who told you we are measuring from the base of the altar? The four *amot* are not counted from the ground up. They are counted from the top surface of the altar downward — or rather, from the edge of the top surface as one's starting point, moving inward. *Arba amot bar mimmah yesod ve-amah sovev* — the four *amot* are in addition to, and separate from, the *yesod* and *sovev*. The base and walk-around structure add two *amot* below the top surface. So four *amot* from the edge of the top surface equals six *amot* from the base of the altar. And six *amot* inward from the southern edge, on a centered altar, brings the fire exactly to the zone of the *ulam* door — which is the minimum required, assuming that the *heichal* and *ulam* are treated as a unified sacred precinct for this purpose.

This is, as noted, a more intuitive way of reading the measurement. A priest ascending to place the fire would naturally count from the surface he is standing on, not from the ground far below. Four *amot* from the surface edge is six *amot* from the base — and six *amot* from the southern edge of a centered, thirty-two-*amah* altar puts the fire at the entry zone of the *ulam* door.

### Attribution Question

The *gemara* now presses Rav Adda bar Ahava: having shown that the Tamid *mishnah* works for Rabbi Yehuda's position of a centered altar, why not then challenge Rabbi Yochanan's attribution entirely? One could argue that the *mishnah* in Tamid is consistent with Rabbi Yosi as well — that Rabbi Yosi holds the altar is centered, not entirely in the north, and the four *amot* is measured from the surface, yielding the same arithmetic. Why attribute the *mishnah* specifically to Rabbi Yehuda rather than simply saying that Rabbi Yosi holds it is half and half?

### Yehuda Explicit

The answer given: *mishum de-Rabbi Yehuda shamana lei mimutza behedya* — because we hear explicitly from Rabbi Yehuda that he holds the altar is centered. Rabbi Yehuda states this position outright in his own words. Once Rabbi Yehuda has explicitly articulated the position of a centered altar, and the Tamid *mishnah* fits that position, it is most natural to assign the *mishnah* to Rabbi Yehuda. But this answer concedes a limitation: there is now no compelling proof that Rabbi Yosi holds the entire altar is in the north. If Rabbi Yosi could be read to hold the altar is centered — just as Rabbi Yehuda explicitly does — then Rabbi Yochanan's assertion that Rabbi Yosi holds *kulo batzafon* remains without direct Tannaitic support from this *mishnah*.

## Yosi HaGalili

### HaGalili Intro

Rav Sharvaya proposes yet another attribution: *ha mani Rabbi Yosi HaGlili hi* — the *mishnah* in Tamid belongs to Rabbi Yosi HaGalili. This is a distinct *tanna* from the Rabbi Yosi discussed throughout — the latter being Rabbi Yosi bar Chalafta, the standard referent of the unqualified name — and also distinct from Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosi HaGalili is a *tanna* of an earlier generation, and the *gemara* now introduces a *beraita* in his name that takes the position that the entire altar is in the north.

### Kiyor Evidence

*Detanya: Rabbi Yosi HaGalili omer: mitoch shenemar ve-natata kiyor bein ohel moed u-vein hamizbeach* — as it is written: "and you shall place the laver between the Tent of Meeting and the altar." Rabbi Yosi HaGalili derives the position of the altar from the placement of the *kiyor* — the laver. The verse specifies that the laver stands between the entrance of the Tent of Meeting and the altar. The analysis of exactly where this places the altar — and how the *kiyor*'s location implies that the entire *mizbeach* stands in the north — will be taken up in the next session.
