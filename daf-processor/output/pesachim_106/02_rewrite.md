# Pesachim 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## One Cup Dilemma

### One Cup Setup

The daf opens in the midst of an extended discussion about *kiddush* and *havdalah*. The scenario under examination involves a person who arrives home on *motzaei Shabbat* with only a single *kos* of wine. He faces a dilemma: that cup is needed for *birkat hamazon*, yet he also needs it for *havdalah*. Tosafot permits him to eat his meal first — eating before *havdalah* — precisely so that he may have the cup available at the conclusion of *birkat hamazon*. He then proceeds to use that one cup for both *birkat hamazon* and *havdalah*.

### Eight Lessons

The Gemara derives a series of lessons from this *baraita*. Among the most significant: first, *birkat hamazon* requires a cup of wine, for if it did not, the scenario would present no difficulty in the first place. Tosafot notes that the plain reading implies this applies even when an individual recites *birkat hamazon* alone, without the presence of three or ten participants — though this is not standard contemporary practice.

Second, there must be a minimum required amount of wine — a *revi'it* — because if there were no minimum, one could simply divide the single cup into two cups and use each for a separate purpose.

Third, taking even a small sip of the wine from a cup renders the remainder *pagum* — unfit for ritual use — because otherwise one could simply sip a little for *birkat hamazon* and use the remainder for *havdalah*.

Fourth, the person making the blessing must actually drink from the cup, since if drinking were not required, the problem of *pagum* would never arise.

The Gemara further derives that two *kedushot* may be recited over a single cup — meaning *birkat hamazon* and *havdalah* may share one *kos* — and while the principle of *mitzvot chavilot chavilot* (bundling separate ritual acts onto a single vessel) is generally a concern, when no alternative exists, a single cup may serve both. Additionally, the Gemara identifies the *baraita*'s author as representing the position of Beit Shammai according to Rabbi Yehuda, on the basis of the order in which the *havdalah* blessings appear — fire (*aish*) before spices (*besamim*) — which is the reverse of our practice and corresponds to the Beit Shammai position in the earlier dispute about that order.

Finally, the *baraita* teaches that *ta'am mavdil* — the fact that he ate before *havdalah* — does not disqualify him from making *havdalah* afterward, at least in this permitted scenario.

### Rav Ashi's Logic

Rav Ashi raises a pointed observation. The Gemara had suggested that two separate lessons could be derived from the *baraita*: (1) that tasting wine renders the remainder *pagum* even if a sufficient quantity remains, and (2) that a minimum *shiur* of wine is required for a *kos shel beracha*. Rav Ashi argues that these are not two independent teachings but rather one unified concern — *chada milta hi*. The *baraita* may simply be describing a case where there is precisely a *revi'it* and nothing more. If so, when the person takes a sip, the remainder falls below the required *shiur*. There is no proof that the act of tasting creates any independent *pagum* status on wine that exceeds the minimum amount.

Tosafot clarifies that Rav Ashi does not necessarily dispute the underlying *halacha* of *pagum* — the Rashbam believes he does — but his point is that the *baraita* alone cannot establish it. The *baraita* does not indicate that the cup contained more than exactly a *revi'it*, so the only principle proven is the requirement for a minimum amount.

## Pagum Wine

### Stringent Authorities

The Gemara proceeds to discuss the practical scope of *pagum*. Rav Yaakov bar Idi was careful not to use a *chatzra* — a pitcher — that had been drunk from. Rav Idi bar Shisha was careful about a *kasa* — a cup — that had been drunk from. Mar bar Rav Ashi was careful even about a *chavita* — a barrel — that had been drunk from.

### What Pagum Means

The concept of *pagum* requires careful definition. At first glance one might think this concerns the principle of *mitzvot chavilot chavilot* — that once a vessel has been used for one *mitzvah* it cannot be used for another. But that is not the correct understanding. The issue is specifically that someone drank directly from the container. Once a person drinks directly from a pitcher, cup, or barrel, whatever remains in that container cannot be used for a ritual act, even if the quantity remains sufficient and even if the initial drinking had nothing whatsoever to do with any *mitzvah*. A person who tastes wine from a barrel to assess its quality — entirely outside any ritual context — has nonetheless rendered the remaining contents *pagum*.

The critical distinction is between drinking directly from a container and pouring from it. If one pours wine from a bottle into a cup and drinks from the cup, only the wine remaining in the cup becomes *pagum*; the wine in the bottle is entirely unaffected. Similarly — as other passages in the Gemara make clear — if before drinking one recites *borei pri hagafen* and pours into a smaller cup, the original container does not become *pagum*. The concern is therefore localized entirely to the container from which one drank directly.

The cases involving a pitcher and a barrel do present an imaginable scenario: one might come to a wine cellar, recite *kiddush* over a barrel, then open a tap and taste a bit. Even in that case, Mar bar Rav Ashi holds that what remains in the barrel has been rendered *pagum*.

The wine does not become prohibited for drinking by others — it may be consumed normally — but it may not be used by anyone, neither the original drinker nor anyone else, for *kiddush* or *havdalah*.

### Tosafot Bedieved

The Rashbam notes that the language used for these authorities is *kapid* — "was careful" — which sounds like a personal stringency rather than a firm ruling. Tosafot cites the Rashbam's view that this stringency applies only *lechatchila*, and that *bedieved* a *pagum* cup may be used. Tosafot then challenges this: if it is only a *lechatchila* concern, why did the Gemara need to permit eating the entire meal before *havdalah* to ensure the cup remained unused? Tosafot ultimately concludes that the concern is genuinely *bedieved* in nature, not merely a *lechatchila* preference — and that the Rashbam's reading is therefore difficult to maintain. Nevertheless, Tosafot adds a striking caveat: *mihu ra'uy Rashbam lismoch alav b'sha'at hadechak* — in a time of pressing need, it is appropriate to rely on the Rashbam's position. This is a remarkable statement about a near-contemporary authority: not a full agreement, yet a recognition that his view may serve as a legitimate basis in difficult circumstances.

### Yerushalmi Fix

A solution to the problem of a *pagum* cup appears not in the Bavli but in the Yerushalmi, quoted by the Rosh in *siman* 11. The Rosh writes: *uv'Yerushalmi mashma dekos pagum marbeh alav kol shehu umachshiro va'afilu b'mayim yachol letakno* — from the Yerushalmi it appears that a *pagum* cup may be restored by adding even a small amount to it, and even water suffices to fix it.

The logic is that by adding even a tiny amount, the cup is no longer one that was directly drunk from — it has received something new, and its status is thereby restored as fresh wine. Notably, one need not add back the precise quantity that was removed; any addition suffices to reconstitute the cup's identity.

This immediately raises a question: why did the Gemara not suggest this solution in the original scenario? Why not allow the person to drink a sip for *birkat hamazon*, add a drop of liquid, and then use the restored cup for *havdalah*? The Rosh addresses this: the wine in that case was already fully diluted to the maximum permissible ratio, and no additional water could be added without disqualifying it as wine. With no other wine available to add, the fix was unavailable.

### Practical Method

The Yerushalmi's principle is the basis for standard practice. After making *havdalah* and drinking a portion of the cup, one adds a drop of wine to the remaining contents and pours it back into the bottle. This restores the cup's status, so the returned wine is not considered *pagum*.

A subtle point must be observed, however. The logical direction of the restoration matters. One must add wine *into* the *kos* — bringing new wine to the container that was drunk from — and then pour the combined contents back into the bottle. One may not simply pour the *pagum* remnant from the *kos* back into the bottle, because that would be moving the *pagum* wine into the bottle rather than restoring the *kos* itself. Pouring *pagum* wine into a bottle creates a question of *rov* — if the bottle's wine constitutes the majority, the mixture may be acceptable *bedieved*, but *lechatchila* this approach is not followed. The proper practice is the reverse: pour fresh wine into the *kos*, thereby restoring it, and then return everything to the bottle.

The question of dilution that arose in antiquity — the Gemara's presumption that wine was routinely mixed with water in a three-to-one ratio — should not be confused with questions about alcohol percentage. The relevant measure is the proportion of original wine content relative to added water, not the strength of the drink.

## Sources & Foundations

### Zachor Source

A *baraita* now addresses the biblical basis for *kiddush* on wine. The verse *Zachor et yom haShabbat l'kadsho* — Remember the Sabbath day to sanctify it — is interpreted as *zachrehu al hayayin b'knisato*: declare and commemorate it over wine at its entry. From this the *baraita* derives the obligation to recite *kiddush* over wine.

### Day vs. Night

The *baraita* initially reads the verse as establishing a daytime obligation — *yom haShabbat* — and asks how we know the obligation applies at night as well. The Gemara then reverses course: the primary logic actually points to *kiddush* at night, because it is at the entry of *Shabbat*, the moment of sanctification, that one declares its identity. The verse's reference to "the day" provides a secondary, additional source for the daytime *kiddush*.

Rav Yehuda states that the daytime *kiddush* consists solely of *borei pri hagafen* — nothing more. The elaborate text we know from Friday night does not apply during the day.

### Rav Ashi Unsure

The Gemara records a remarkable incident involving Rav Ashi. He arrived in Mechuza, and those present asked him to make for them *Kiddusha Rabba* — the daytime *Shabbat* *kiddush*. Rav Ashi did not know what was meant by this phrase, nor apparently did he know the practice. A cup of wine was placed before him. He reasoned: any ritual act involving wine begins with *borei pri hagafen*, so he would begin there. He extended the recitation of *borei pri hagafen*, waiting for some cue, until he observed an elderly man leaning forward to drink — signaling that the *kiddush* was complete. Rav Ashi then quoted to himself the verse from *Kohelet*, *hechacham einav b'rosho* — the wise man has his eyes in his head — congratulating himself for having had the foresight to start with *borei pri hagafen* regardless.

The episode is striking on multiple levels. Here is Rav Ashi — one of the latest and most authoritative *amoraim* — apparently unacquainted with the very existence of daytime *Shabbat* *kiddush* as a distinct practice, and navigating a public ritual by reading the crowd rather than by announcing his uncertainty. It underscores just how much of the *halacha* of this period was still in flux and how heavily practices were observed and transmitted through direct observation of teachers and community customs.

### Vayechulu Origins

Tosafot raises a significant question about the nature of *Vayechulu*. The plain reading of various Gemaras is that *Vayechulu* is never recited as part of *kiddush* at home — it belongs exclusively to *tefillat maariv* of *Shabbat*. The Gemara establishes the practice of saying *Vayechulu* in *tefilla*, but there is no corresponding institution of *Vayechulu* over the *kos*.

Why then do we say *Vayechulu* at home at all? Tosafot explains: it is recited at home not as part of *kiddush* but as a substitute for those family members who did not daven *maariv* and therefore did not say *Vayechulu* within *shmoneh esrei*. The *takana* is that they should fulfill this recitation.

Why is *Vayechulu* said aloud in *shul* after *shmoneh esrei*? Because when *yom tov* falls on *Shabbat*, the *maariv* service uses *Atah Vechartanu* and *Vayechulu* is omitted from *shmoneh esrei*. To ensure that *Vayechulu* is said by everyone even in that scenario, the rabbis instituted saying it publicly after *shmoneh esrei*. And so that no distinction should be drawn between one *Shabbat* and another — since most weeks *Vayechulu* is said within *shmoneh esrei* — the practice was extended to every *Shabbat*.

The upshot is striking: *Vayechulu* is fundamentally part of *tefillat maariv*, not of *kiddush*. Its recitation at home serves an independent function of ensuring family members fulfill their *maariv* obligation. *Kiddush* itself is constituted by the *brachos* that *Chazal* instituted — *borei pri hagafen* and *mekadesh haShabbat* — not by the recitation of *psukim*. Just as the *mitzvah* of *sippur yetziat Mitzrayim* is fulfilled through the rabbinic *seder* rather than by simply reading the *psukim* in *Shemot*, so too the *mitzvah* of *zachrehu al hayayin* is fulfilled through the rabbinic *brachos*, not through the reading of *Vayechulu*.

Whether the wine itself is required *mid'oraita* is a separate question. Tosafot suggests that the entire requirement to recite *kiddush* on wine may be only an *asmachta*, though he entertains the possibility that the wine is genuinely a biblical requirement while the obligation to drink may be only rabbinic.

### Rambam View

The Rambam, in *perek* 29 of *Hilchot Shabbat*, rules unambiguously that the *mitzvat aseh* of *kiddush* is biblical: *zachor et yom haShabbat l'kadsho* — meaning one must remember it with words of praise and sanctification, *zechirat shevach v'kiddush*. Crucially, the Rambam extends this equally to *havdalah*: the verse requires remembrance *b'knisato b'kiddush hayom uv'yetziato b'havdalah* — at its entry through *kiddush*, and at its exit through *havdalah*. Thus the Rambam presents *kiddush* and *havdalah* as perfectly coequal *d'oraita* obligations, both derived from *Zachor et yom haShabbat*.

The Rambam further states that *kiddush al hayayin* and *havdalah al hayayin* are *midivrei sofrim* — rabbinic in origin — clearly the consensus position, even as the Rambam emphasizes the biblical status of the verbal obligations themselves.

Regarding *Vayechulu*, the Rambam describes its recitation before *kiddush* as *minhag pashut b'chol Yisrael* — the universal Jewish custom. Unlike Tosafot, who treats *Vayechulu* as categorically independent of *kiddush*, the Rambam's formulation integrates it closely: the custom is to recite *Vayechulu*, then *borei pri hagafen*, then *mekadesh haShabbat*, then drink a *m'lo lugmav*. The Rambam does not treat *Vayechulu* as a mere appendage; rather, the universal practice incorporates it as part of the *kiddush* experience, through which one may also fulfill one's *d'oraita* obligation.

## Postponing Havdalah

### Havdalah Timeline

A *baraita* quoted by the sons of Rabbi Chiya establishes that one who failed to make *havdalah* on *motzaei Shabbat* may make it up later in the week. Rabbi Zeira states that this window extends through Tuesday — *ad revii b'Shabbat*, meaning through the end of Tuesday — since Sunday, Monday, and Tuesday are halachically associated with the preceding *Shabbat*, while Wednesday, Thursday, and Friday are associated with the coming *Shabbat*. This is not merely an abstract framework; it has practical grounding in the laws of conditional *gittin*, where stipulations framed as "after *Shabbat*" are understood to apply through Tuesday and "before *Shabbat*" applies from Wednesday onward.

The idea that one may formally demarcate the conclusion of *Shabbat* on a Tuesday, after presumably having performed *melacha* and eaten normally for two days, is admittedly striking. Yet the halachic principle holds that the formal act of *havdalah* still carries significance as an act of demarcation even then.

### Fire Exception

Rabbi Yaakov bar Idi limits the above: the make-up *havdalah* applies to all the standard components except the blessing over fire. The *beracha* over *aish* may not be recited later in the week. The reason is that the kindling of fire is associated with a specific historical moment — the original creation of fire at the close of the first *Shabbat*. The blessing is meant to commemorate *techilat yetzirah shel ha'ur* at the moment it occurred, and that moment is *motzaei Shabbat* itself. While the other elements of *havdalah* function as acts of demarcation that remain meaningful throughout those early days of the week, the fire blessing is tied to a singular historical commemoration and cannot be separated from its original context.

### Hand Washing

Rav Bruna cites a ruling in the name of Rav: *hanotel yadav lo yekadesh* — one who has already washed his hands for a meal should not then make *kiddush*. According to the Rashbam, the concern is *hefsek*: having washed for *hamotzi*, one must proceed directly to bread, and reciting *borei pri hagafen* for *kiddush* in the interval would constitute an interruption. The Rashbam therefore holds that in this situation one should ideally hear *kiddush* from someone else; if no one else is available, one may have no choice but to wash again afterward.

## Washing & Kiddush

### Rav Yitzchak Challenge

Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta challenges Rav Bruna's ruling sharply. He declares: Rav is barely gone and we have already forgotten his teachings! He then testifies from his own extensive direct observation of Rav's practice.

The challenge is also an occasion to note how unusually rich the discussions on this daf are in *ma'aseh rav* — actual observed practices — as opposed to theoretical argumentation. Because *kiddush* and *havdalah* occur every single *Shabbat*, Sages had constant opportunity to observe what their teachers actually did, and those observations saturate these sugyas in a way that is far less common in other areas of *halacha*.

### Rav's Flexibility

Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta reports: *zimnin dechaviva leih rifta mekadesh arifta, zimnin dechaviva leih chamra mekadesh achamra* — sometimes when Rav was inclined toward bread he made *kiddush* on bread, and sometimes when he was inclined toward wine he made *kiddush* on wine. This testimony has a double significance: it apparently introduces the idea that *kiddush* may be made on bread — a notion that seems to contradict everything previously discussed about *kiddush* on wine — and it bears on the *hefsek* question.

### Rashbam Hefsek

The Rashbam reads the passage literally: Rav would sometimes wash his hands in preparation for bread, then change his mind and make *kiddush* on wine. The point of the testimony is that Rav would change course midway — washing for bread, then interposing *kiddush* on wine before proceeding to *hamotzi* — and this demonstrates that doing so is not an impermissible *hefsek*, at minimum *bedieved*. The Rashbam's reading preserves the concern of *hefsek* in principle while showing that Rav's practice establishes that it is not fatal.

### Tosafot B'Makom

Tosafot rejects both the literal reading and the Rashbam's explanation. Rabbeinu Tam argues that *kiddush* on bread is simply not possible — one cannot fulfill the obligation of *kiddush* with *hamotzi*. What then does the passage mean?

Tosafot reframes the entire episode by invoking a teaching from the very beginning of the *perek*: Rav held that *kiddush* does not require *makom seudah* — one may make *kiddush* without eating a meal immediately afterward. The phrase "made *kiddush* on bread" does not mean he recited the *kiddush* text over a loaf; it means that when Rav intended to have a meal with bread he would structure his *kiddush* in connection with that meal, and when he preferred wine alone he would make *kiddush* without the meal context.

More importantly, Tosafot argues that the concern raised by Rav Bruna was never about *hefsek* at all. Since for Rav one could make *kiddush* divorced from the meal, there was a genuine concern that after washing, a person might make *kiddush*, then decide to skip the meal entirely — rendering his *netilat yadayim* a *beracha l'vatala* (a blessing recited in vain). The testimony of Rav Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta responds to that concern: Rav himself would wash and make *kiddush* without worrying that he might abandon the meal afterward. The issue was never *hefsek*.

### Ashkenazi Practice

Once the concern of *hefsek* is removed entirely, the practical ramification becomes clear. Tosafot states explicitly: for those who hold *ein kiddush ela b'makom seudah* — that *kiddush* must be in the context of a meal — the connection between the washing, the *kiddush*, and the bread is so tight that there is no conceivable *hefsek* problem at all, and no risk that the meal will be abandoned. Indeed, Tosafot identifies the *minhag* of the *Ashkenazim* — meaning specifically the German-Jewish *Yekke* custom — as washing *davka* first, then making *kiddush*, then reciting *hamotzi*. For those raised in a different custom, this sequence may seem counterintuitive or even problematic, but Tosafot's reading demonstrates its impeccable logic: *kiddush bimkom seudah* means that the hand washing, *kiddush*, and *hamotzi* form a single unified act, all bound together around the meal.

## Alt. Beverages

### Chamar Medina

Tosafot raises the question of *chamar medina* — a regional beverage. The Gemara elsewhere states that *havdalah* may be made over *chamar medina*, a drink consumed socially rather than merely for thirst. Beer or a similar beverage serves as the paradigmatic example. The defining characteristic is that people drink it in a social setting even when not thirsty — it is a beverage of social significance in its locale. The question Tosafot poses is whether this latitude extends to *kiddush* as well, given the Gemara's apparent flexibility in allowing bread for *kiddush* on one hand and *chamar medina* for *havdalah* on the other.

### Bread Asymmetry

Tosafot's analysis produces an asymmetry. Bread may be used for *kiddush* — at least according to those who permit it — but not for *havdalah*. The reason is rooted in the concept of *kiddush bimkom seudah*: *kiddush* is defined by its connection to the meal, and bread is the meal itself. The loaf over which one recites *hamotzi* and the *kiddush* function belong to the same act. *Havdalah*, by contrast, does not require *makom seudah*; it stands independently. Because bread's eligibility derives entirely from its role in defining the meal, it has no analogous function in *havdalah* and cannot serve there.

The converse holds for *chamar medina*: it may serve for *havdalah* but its use for *kiddush* is uncertain. The connection of *kiddush* to the meal may demand something more substantial — wine or bread — rather than a mere social beverage.

### Practical Shiur

Whatever beverage is used for *kiddush* or *havdalah*, the required *shiur* remains a *revi'it* — approximately three and a half ounces. The argument that the *shiur* should be proportional to what is normative for that particular beverage is very late and finds no support in the Gemara or *rishonim*, where the assumption throughout is a standard *revi'it*. A practical solution for those wishing to use *chamar medina* — particularly in scenarios such as the nine days when wine is restricted — is to prepare a mixed drink, such as juice and spirits. Three and a half ounces of a mixed drink is far more manageable than drinking that quantity of undiluted liquor.

### Grape Juice Issue

The status of commercially available pasteurized grape juice is genuinely problematic. While the Gemara does acknowledge that unfermented wine — even wine pressed that same day — retains the status of *yayin*, such wine remains capable of fermentation. Pasteurized grape juice has had that capacity eliminated. It may therefore fall entirely outside the halachic category of *yayin*. The analogy to *mevushal* wine is suggestive. A responsum of the *Tzitz Eliezer* famously points out that one should first ask whether grape juice qualifies for *borei pri hagafen* before asking whether it qualifies for *kiddush* — let alone whether reconstituted grape juice (produced by removing water content, shipping, and reconstituting) would qualify.

The practical upshot: one should be considerably more *makpid* regarding the use of grape juice on Friday night, when *kiddush* carries its primary weight. On *Shabbat* day, where *chamar medina* is in any case widely relied upon and the halachic standards are less stringent, grape juice may be more readily accepted.

## Eating Before

### Ta'am Dispute

The final topic of the daf concerns eating or tasting food before *kiddush* or *havdalah*. Rav Huna, citing Rav, rules: *ta'am eino mekadesh* — if one has tasted food, one may no longer make *kiddush*. The question posed to Rav Huna is whether *havdalah* follows the same rule: *ta'am mahu sheyavdil* — does tasting food preclude *havdalah* as well?

Rav Huna himself answers that *ta'am mavdil* — tasting does not prevent one from making *havdalah*. Rav Assi disagrees and holds that tasting disqualifies one from *havdalah* just as from *kiddush*.

The underlying logic of the debate tracks the structural differences between the two obligations. *Kiddush* is intimately connected to the meal through *kiddush bimkom seudah*: it defines the opening of the meal, and once eating has already begun, the significance of that demarcating function is lost. *Havdalah*, which carries no *makom seudah* requirement, has a looser connection to eating, and it may therefore remain valid even if one has incidentally tasted something.

### Yirmiah Case

The dispute plays out in a vivid anecdote. Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba came to the home of Rav Assi as a guest. Rabbi Yirmiyah absentmindedly ate something after nightfall — *ishtali v'ta'im midei* — and then *havdalah* was brought before him, which he proceeded to recite. Rav Assi's wife observed this and pointed out to her husband: but you, Master, do not do this — you hold that one who has tasted may not make *havdalah*. Rav Assi replied: leave him alone — *shavkei* — he follows his own *rebbe*, who is Rav Huna, and Rav Huna holds that *ta'am mavdil*. He is acting in accordance with his own tradition and that is entirely acceptable.

The detail that Rav Assi's wife was familiar with her husband's ruling — and recognized the discrepancy immediately — reflects just how often this situation must have arisen in practice and how closely domestic life tracked halachic reasoning.

### Final Rulings

Rav Yosef cites Shmuel: *ta'am eino mekadesh, ta'am eino mavdil* — tasting disqualifies for both *kiddush* and *havdalah*. Rabba cites Rav Nachman citing Shmuel — a different tradition from the same authority — as ruling: *ta'am mekadesh v'ta'am mavdil* — tasting does not disqualify for either. Rava then rules definitively: *hilchesa ta'am mekadesh v'ta'am mavdil* — the *halacha* follows the lenient position in both cases. *Bedieved*, if one has tasted food, one may still make *kiddush* or *havdalah* after the fact.

The daf thus concludes by returning to the structural tension that has animated much of the discussion: *kiddush* and *havdalah* share the prohibition against prior eating, yet their different relationships to the meal — *kiddush* as meal-defining through *bimkom seudah*, *havdalah* as independent of the meal — generate distinct analytical frameworks. That same distinction explains why bread serves *kiddush* but not *havdalah*, and why the question of *ta'am* generates the disputing traditions that Rava ultimately harmonizes in a single lenient ruling.
