# Moed Katan 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Third Day Erev Regel

### No Bathing Until Nightfall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הַכֹּל מוֹדִין כְּשֶׁחָל שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁלּוֹ לִהְיוֹת עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל — שֶׁאָסוּר בִּרְחִיצָה עַד הָעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: Everyone,** including Abba Shaul, who says that part of a day is treated like a full day, **agrees** that **when his third** day of mourning **occurs on the eve of the Festival,** the mourner **is prohibited from bathing** his body **until the evening.**

The context here is critical. The *mishna* operates on the assumption — which will later be challenged — that *regel* does not interrupt *shiva* unless the mourner has observed a full three days. Given that baseline, one might have thought that since *regel* is about to interrupt *shiva*, the mourner could prepare by bathing early, just as we saw that a mourner nearing *shloshim* is permitted to get a haircut before *regel* in anticipation of the interruption. Rav Huna rules that this analogy does not hold for bathing on the third day: the mourner must wait until nightfall, when *yom tov* actually begins, before bathing.

The distinction between bathing and the haircut case is significant. Bathing is something a person can reasonably do once *yom tov* begins, and the prohibition of bathing belongs squarely to the core observances of *shiva*. Some authorities read *ad ha'erev* not as requiring one to wait until the actual onset of *yom tov*, but as permitting bathing once the late afternoon arrives, when one is sufficiently close to *yom tov*. Others limit the ruling specifically to bathing — that is, to prohibitions that can be readily remedied once *yom tov* begins — rather than extending it to all *shiva* practices. In either reading, the *l'halacha* application is not so sweeping.

### Rav Pappi & Rav Pappa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נְחֶמְיָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְרַב פַּפֵּי וּלְרַב פָּפָּא דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Neḥemya, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: I** once **found Rav Pappi and Rav Pappa sitting** together **and saying: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua.**

Rav Nechemia son of Rav Yehoshua reports that he found Rav Pappi and Rav Pappa sitting together and declaring: *halacha k'Rav Huna brei d'Rav Yehoshua* — the ruling follows Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua. No one disputes this ruling, and it stands as settled *halacha*.

### Three-Authority Girsa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַב נְחֶמְיָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְרַב פַּפֵּי וּלְרַב פָּפָּא וּלְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם חָל שְׁלִישִׁי שֶׁלּוֹ לִהְיוֹת עֶרֶב הָרֶגֶל — שֶׁאָסוּר בִּרְחִיצָה עַד הָעֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** a different version of this tradition: **Rav Neḥemya, son of Rav Yosef, said: I** once **found Rav Pappi, Rav Pappa, and Rav Huna, son of Yehoshua, sitting** together **and saying: Everyone concedes** that **when the third** day of mourning **occurs on the eve of a Festival,** the mourner **is prohibited from bathing** his body **until the evening.**

An alternative version of this tradition — *ika d'amri* — reports that it was not Rav Huna who stated the ruling first and was then affirmed by Rav Pappi and Rav Pappa, but rather that all three — Rav Pappi, Rav Pappa, and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua — were sitting together and jointly declaring: *hakol modim k'shechal shlishi shelo liyos erev haregel, she'assur birchitza ad ha'erev*. Either way, the substance of the ruling is the same and appears undisputed.

## Regel & Shloshim

### Regel Count Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵרָבָא: קְבָרוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, רֶגֶל עוֹלֶה לוֹ לְמִנְיַן שְׁלֹשִׁים, אוֹ אֵין רֶגֶל עוֹלֶה לוֹ לְמִנְיַן שְׁלֹשִׁים? לְמִנְיַן שִׁבְעָה לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לִי, דְּלָא נָהֲגָא מִצְוַת שִׁבְעָה בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye inquired of Rava:** If **one buried** his dead relative **on the Festival** itself, **does the Festival count toward his thirty-**day period of mourning, **or does the Festival not count toward his thirty-**day period of mourning? Abaye elaborated on his question: **I do not ask** whether or not the Festival counts toward his **seven-**day period of mourning because **the obligation** to observe **seven** days of mourning **does not apply** at all **during the Festival.**

The *mishna* had already established that *regel* is *mafsik v'eino oleh* — it interrupts *shiva* but does not count toward it. The rationale is that *regel* is so fundamentally antithetical to *aveilus* that the period of *simcha* cannot accumulate toward the most intensive mourning period. But the question here is not about *shiva* — it is about *shloshim*.

### Shiva vs Shloshim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי לְמִנְיַן שְׁלֹשִׁים, דְּקָא נָהֲגָא מִצְוַת שְׁלֹשִׁים בָּרֶגֶל, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **What I am asking is** with regard to the **thirty-**day period of mourning, because certain aspects of the **mitzva of** the **thirty-**day mourning period **do** in fact **apply during the Festival,** e.g., the prohibitions to launder clothes and cut hair. **What,** then, is the *halakha*:

Abaye himself clarifies the parameters of the question: *l'minyan shiva lo ka mibaya li* — he has no question about *shiva*. Since none of the distinctive practices of *shiva* can be observed during *regel*, there is no mechanism by which the *regel* period could count. Moreover, at a conceptual level, *shiva* is so profoundly at odds with the spirit of *regel* that it is impossible for the *regel* days to be counted. *Delo nahaga mitzvas shiva baregel* — the obligation of *shiva* simply is not practiced during *regel*.

The question concerns *shloshim* specifically, and it arises for two reasons. First, at the conceptual level, *shloshim* represents a less intensive degree of *aveilus* — it may not be entirely incompatible with the *regel* period in the way *shiva* is. Second, and more practically striking, *shloshim* practices — principally the prohibition against taking a haircut and against laundering clothes — happen to be observed by everyone during *chol hamoed* in any case, albeit for entirely different reasons. Since those practices are in effect during *regel* regardless, there is a basis to argue that the *regel* days might count toward the mourner's *shloshim*.

This is also the broader reason the question appears in this *masechta*: the overlap between *chol hamoed* restrictions and *shloshim* restrictions is what makes the *regel* period potentially relevant to the count.

### Rava: No Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינוֹ עוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** The Festival **does not count** toward the thirty days.

Rabba — or in the alternative reading, Rava — responds initially: *eino oleh* — the *regel* period does not count. The days of *yom tov* do not deduct from the mourner's obligation of *shloshim*.

## Tosefta Case

### Five Days Postponed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: הַקּוֹבֵר אֶת מֵתוֹ שְׁנֵי יָמִים קוֹדֶם הָרֶגֶל — מוֹנֶה חֲמִשָּׁה יָמִים אַחַר הָרֶגֶל, וּמְלַאכְתּוֹ נַעֲשֵׂית עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים, וַעֲבָדָיו וְשִׁפְחוֹתָיו עוֹשִׂים בְּצִינְעָא בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וְאֵין רַבִּים מִתְעַסְּקִין עִמּוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rava's opinion from the following *baraita*: If **one buries his dead** relative **two days before a Festival, he must count five days** of mourning **after the Festival, and** during this period **his work is performed** for him **by others. And his menservants and maidservants do** this work **in private inside his house, and the public need not occupy themselves with him** and come to console him.

The Gemara immediately challenges this ruling. A *tosefta* teaches: if a person buried a relative two days before *regel* — that is, with fewer than three days of *shiva* observed, so that on the assumption of the *mishna* the *regel* does not interrupt — then the mourner must complete *shiva* after *regel*, observing five additional days following *yom tov*.

This scenario creates a curious middle situation. The seven-day *shiva* period technically began before *regel*, but since fewer than three days were observed, the remaining five days are postponed until after *yom tov*. During the *regel* itself, the mourner does not observe the personal practices of *shiva*. Yet the seven days from the burial still span the *regel*, and the Gemara will now articulate how communal and personal obligations diverge across this period.

### Work by Others

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכְּבָר נִתְעַסְּקוּ בּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** for they already occupied themselves with him **during the Festival.**

During the five postponed days after *regel*, the *tosefta* teaches that *melachto naasit al yedei acherim* — work may be performed for the mourner by others. Furthermore, *avadav v'shifchotav osim betzinah betoch beito* — his servants may perform work privately within his home. Neither would be permitted during a standard, uninterrupted *shiva*, but the postponed character of these five days renders the prohibition more lenient. The mourner's own personal restrictions remain in force, but the communal and delegated dimensions of the *melacha* prohibition are relaxed because the period is, in a sense, already compromised by its interrupted nature.

### No Nichum Aveilim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם אֵבֶל — רֶגֶל מַפְסִיקוֹ. וְכׇל שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם עִסְקֵי רַבִּים — אֵין רֶגֶל מַפְסִיקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The **principle with regard to the matter** is as follows: **Any** activity **that** is prohibited to the mourner **because it is** an expression of **mourning is interrupted by the Festival** and remains prohibited afterward. **And anything that involves the public's occupation** with the mourner, e.g., coming to the mourner to offer him comfort and condolence, **is not interrupted by the Festival,** for people console the mourner during the Festival as well.

In a ruling that is genuinely striking, the *tosefta* states: *v'ein rabim mitaskin imo* — the masses do not involve themselves with the mourner during those postponed five days — *shekevar nitasku vo baregel* — because they already did so during *regel*. The community had already performed *nichum aveilim* during *chol hamoed*.

This is a remarkable *halacha*. One might have assumed that *nichum aveilim* — which is so public an expression of communal engagement with mourning — would be among the first practices suspended during *regel*. Yet the Gemara's position is that even during *regel*, when the mourner is not personally observing the practices of *shiva*, the community does engage in *nichum aveilim*. The mourner's need for consolation does not pause simply because the *regel* has interrupted the formal *shiva* structure. Indeed, this is consistent with the broader principle that one may perform *nichum aveilim* on *Shabbat* as well — something reflected in the practice of welcoming mourners before *Kabbalat Shabbat* with *HaMakom yenachem etchem*, though the actual *halacha* permits the *nichum* on *Shabbat* itself.

### Personal vs Communal

The Gemara articulates the underlying principle: *kol davar mishum aveilus regel mafsiko* — anything that involves the mourner's personal observance of *aveilus* is interrupted by *regel*. *V'chol shehu mishum iskei rabim ein regel mafsiko* — but anything rooted in communal practice, such as *nichum aveilim*, is not interrupted by *regel*. The community's obligation to comfort the mourner continues across the *regel* period, and consequently, by the time the postponed five days arrive, the community has already fulfilled that role and does not resume it.

It should be noted that even in the scenario of a burial during *chol hamoed* — where everything seems postponed — the mourner still observes *devarim shebetzina*, the private practices of *aveilus* such as abstaining from bathing and conjugal relations. The postponement is not total; it is the public, personal-observance dimension that is suspended, not the entirely private one.

## Burial During Regel

### Full Postponement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְבָרוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים בְּסוֹף הָרֶגֶל — מוֹנֶה שִׁבְעָה אַחַר הָרֶגֶל, אַרְבָּעָה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים — רַבִּים מִתְעַסְּקִין בּוֹ, שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָאַחֲרוֹנִים — אֵין רַבִּים מִתְעַסְּקִין בּוֹ, שֶׁכְּבָר נִתְעַסְּקוּ [בּוֹ] בָּרֶגֶל, וְרֶגֶל עוֹלֶה לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **he** buries his relative **three days** before **the end of the Festival,** the mourner **must count seven** days of mourning **after the Festival** because his mourning never began. On the **first four days** of his mourning, **the public must occupy themselves with him** and come to console him. On **the three last days,** however, **the public need not occupy themselves with him, as they already occupied themselves with him during the Festival.** That is to say, they certainly came to console him during the Festival, although the period of mourning did not actually begin until afterward. **And the Festival counts for him** toward the thirty-day mourning period.

The *tosefta* continues with a second scenario: if the burial occurred in the last three days of *regel* — during *chol hamoed* — then the entire *shiva* is postponed until after *regel*: *moneh shiva achar haregel*. Since the mourner had no opportunity to begin personal *shiva* observance before *regel*, everything is deferred.

### Community First Four Days

Even though the mourner's personal *shiva* is entirely postponed, the community's engagement is not. The burial took place, and the community begins its *nichum aveilim* from that moment. Accordingly, *arba'ah yamim harishonim* — during the first four days after *regel* — *rabim misaskin bo*, the masses continue to involve themselves with the mourner.

### No Mourning Last Three

For the final three of the seven post-*regel* days, however, *ein rabim misaskin bo shekevar nisasku vo baregel* — the community no longer continues its engagement, because it had already performed *nichum aveilim* during *regel* itself, beginning immediately after the burial. The three-day overlap between the community's post-burial engagement during *regel* and the subsequent seven days explains why the last three days of the postponed *shiva* receive no communal attendance.

This structure is remarkable. Even when the burial occurred during *chol hamoed* and the mourner's own practices are entirely deferred, the community's *nichum aveilim* begins at burial and runs continuously for seven days — spanning *regel* itself. The practice of deferring the mourner's personal *shiva* does not suspend the community's responsibility toward the mourner.

## Regel & Shloshim…

### Braita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: רֶגֶל עוֹלֶה לוֹ לְמִנְיַן שְׁלֹשִׁים, כֵּיצַד? קְבָרוֹ בִּתְחִילַּת הָרֶגֶל — מוֹנֶה שִׁבְעָה אַחַר הָרֶגֶל, ומְלַאכְתּוֹ נַעֲשֵׂית עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים, וַעֲבָדָיו וְשִׁפְחוֹתָיו עוֹשִׂין בְּצִנְעָא בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וְאֵין רַבִּים מִתְעַסְּקִין בּוֹ, שֶׁכְּבָר נִתְעַסְּקוּ בּוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, וְרֶגֶל עוֹלֶה לוֹ. תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rava's opinion from another *baraita*, in which it was taught: **A pilgrimage Festival counts toward the thirty-**day period of mourning. **How so?** If one **buried** his dead relative **at the beginning of a pilgrimage Festival, he must count seven** days of mourning **after the Festival, and** during this period **his work is performed by others. And his menservants and maidservants perform** this work **in private inside his house, and the public need not occupy themselves with him** and come to console him, **for they already occupied themselves with him during the Festival. And the Festival counts for him.** Here it is explicitly stated that the Festival counts toward the thirty-day period of mourning, even when the deceased was buried during the Festival itself. The Gemara concludes: This is indeed **a conclusive refutation** of Rava's opinion.

The Gemara returns to the question of whether *regel* counts toward *shloshim* and directly challenges Rava's initial ruling. A *braita* states explicitly: *regel oleh lo l'minyan shloshim* — *regel* counts toward *shloshim*. The *braita* describes the case: *kavro b'tchilat haregel* — the burial occurred at the beginning of *regel*, meaning during *regel* itself — and yet it states that *regel oleh lo*, the *regel* period counts. The Gemara had earlier attempted to limit the counting to a case where the mourner had begun *shiva* before *regel*; the *braita* now closes off that escape, applying the counting even to a burial during *regel* when the entire *shiva* is postponed.

The Gemara accepts this as a refutation of Rava's position: *tiyufta* — a valid challenge stands.

### Confirmation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ קְבָרוֹ בָּרֶגֶל, וְכֵן אוֹרִי לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לְרַבִּי פְּדָת בְּרֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ קְבָרוֹ בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** if **one buried** his dead relative **during the Festival,** the Festival counts toward his thirty-day period of mourning. **And, similarly, Rabbi Elazar ruled for Rabbi Pedat, his son: Even if one buried** his dead relative **during the Festival** it counts towards his thirty days.

The position is further confirmed by a teaching of Rabbi Yochanan, transmitted by Ravin upon his return from Eretz Yisrael: *afilu kavro baregel* — even if the burial was during *regel*, the *regel* period counts to deduct from *shloshim*. Rabbi Elazar similarly told Rabbi Pedat his son: *afilu kavro baregel*, the *regel* counts toward *shloshim*. This is the established *halacha*.

The *halacha* becomes even more striking in the case of Sukkot. If a person is buried before Sukkot, the *regel* first annuls *shiva*, deducting seven days from the thirty; then Shemini Atzeret, which counts as its own independent *regel*, deducts another seven days — functioning as a single-day holiday that nonetheless counts for a full seven. A person buried just before Sukkot may therefore find themselves with only nine remaining days of *shloshim* to observe after the *regel*. Even in a simpler case such as a burial on *erev Pesach*, the seven days of Pesach deduct from the twenty-three remaining days of *shloshim*, leaving only sixteen days of observance after *yom tov*.

By contrast, when the death occurs during *regel*, the impact is considerably smaller. Since *shiva* is not annulled — it is only postponed — the mourner begins with a full thirty days. If four days of *yom tov* remain at the time of burial, the mourner has twenty-six days of *shloshim* left afterward, rather than benefiting from the double reduction that applies when burial precedes *regel*.

## Shammai vs Hillel

### Three Days Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קִיֵּים כְּפִיַּית הַמִּטָּה שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים קוֹדֶם הָרֶגֶל — אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לִכְפּוֹתָהּ אַחַר הָרֶגֶל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד, אֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: If **one fulfilled the** obligation of **overturning the bed for three days before the pilgrimage Festival, he is no** longer **required to overturn it after the Festival;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. But the Rabbis say: Even** if one overturned his bed for only **one day, or even for** only **one hour,** he is not required to overturn it after the Festival.

The Gemara now turns to the foundational question of how many days of *shiva* must be observed before *regel* interrupts it. A *braita* states: if a person observed *kefiyat hamitah* — the overturning of the bed — for three days before *regel*, then *eino tzarich likfoso achar haregel* — the *regel* annuls the remaining *shiva* and one need not overturn the bed afterward. The choice of *kefiyat hamitah* as the paradigmatic practice is instructive: unlike most *shiva* prohibitions, which are essentially abstentions — from bathing, wearing shoes, and the like — the overturning of the bed is a demonstrative, affirmative act of *aveilus*. It is not the withholding of something but the active performance of a visible mourning practice. As such, it serves as a paradigm for what it means to *observe* *shiva* in a full sense.

This position — requiring three days — is attributed to Rabbi Eliezer: *divrei Rabbi Eliezer*. The *chachamim*, however, rule: *afilu yom echad, afilu sha'ah achas* — even one day, even one hour of *shiva* observance before *regel* is sufficient for the *regel* to interrupt and annul the remaining *shiva*.

### Beis Shammai vs Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הֵן הֵן דִּבְרֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי, הֵן הֵן דִּבְרֵי בֵּית הִלֵּל. שֶׁבֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, said: This is the statement of Beit Shammai,** and **that is the statement of Beit Hillel.** In other words, Rabbi Eliezer and the Rabbis disagreed about a matter that was the subject of an earlier dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, **for Beit Shammai say: Three days, and Beit Hillel say: Even one day.**

Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Shimon identifies the debate: *hen hen divrei Beis Shammai, hen hen divrei Beis Hillel* — this is in fact the dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel. *She'Beis Shammai omrim shlosha yamim u'Beis Hillel omrim afilu yom echad* — Beis Shammai requires three days; Beis Hillel says even one day suffices. This identification is significant. The entire body of *tannaitic* material up to this point had assumed three days as the requirement, suggesting that the three-day position had a notably dominant presence in the tradition — it was not an isolated minority view but one with considerable momentum. Yet once it is identified with Beis Shammai, the direction of the ruling becomes clear.

### One Hour Sufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא וּלְרַב הוּנָא: אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד, אֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said, and some say** a different version of this line of transmission: **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba and to Rav Huna: Even one day, even one hour.**

Rav Huna, citing Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — or, in an alternative version, Rabbi Yochanan himself said this to Rabbi Chiya bar Abba or Rav Huna — rules: *afilu yom echad, afilu sha'ah achas*. Even one hour is sufficient. Rabbi Yochanan here, as elsewhere in this sugya, takes the lenient position.

### Rava: Three Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּתַנָּא דִּידַן דְּאָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **the *tanna*** of **our** mishna, who **said three** days.

Rava rules: *halacha k'tana didan, de'amar shlosha* — the *halacha* follows our *tanna*, who requires three days. This places Rava in the stricter position, against Rabbi Yochanan's leniency, a recurring pattern in this sugya. One might ask whether this reflects a difference between Babylonian and Eretz Yisrael practice, though that remains speculative.

### Ravina: One Hour

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אִיקְּלַע לְסוּרָא דִפְרָת, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חֲבִיבָא לְרָבִינָא: הִלְכְתָא מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד, וַאֲפִילּוּ שָׁעָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina happened** to come to the city of **Sura on the** banks of the **Euphrates** River. **Rav Ḥaviva said to Ravina: What is** the ***halakha*** with regard to this issue? **He said to him: Even one day, and even one hour.**

Ravina came to Sura on the Perat river, where Rav Chaviva asked him: *hilchasa mai* — what is the practical ruling? Ravina answered: *afilu yom echad, afilu sha'ah achas* — even one day, even one hour. The ruling in practice follows the lenient position: any *shiva* observance before *regel*, even a single hour, is sufficient for the *regel* to annul the remaining *shiva*.

## Seven Days Source

### Holiday Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא וְרַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אַקִּילְעָא דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, נְפַק מִילְּתָא מִבֵּינַיְיהוּ: מִנַּיִן לַאֲבֵילוּת שִׁבְעָה — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָפַכְתִּי חַגֵּיכֶם לְאֵבֶל״, מָה חַג שִׁבְעָה — אַף אֲבֵילוּת שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § It was related that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, Rabbi Ami, and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa were** once **sitting in the pavilion of Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Elazar** and were conversing. **A matter emerged from among them: From where** is it derived that the rites of **mourning** are observed for **seven** days? **As it is written: "And I will turn your Festivals into mourning"** (Amos 8:10). **Just as a Festival** lasts for **seven** days, **so too mourning** lasts for **seven** days.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, Rabbi Ami, and Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha were sitting on the porch of Rabbi Yitzchak ben Elazar when a consensus emerged among them on the following question: *minayin l'aveilus shiva* — what is the source for the seven-day period of *aveilus*? The answer: *dichsiv, v'hafachti chageichem l'evel* — as it is written, "I will turn your holidays into mourning." Just as a standard *chag* is seven days, so too *aveilus* is seven days.

### Shemini Atzeret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא עֲצֶרֶת, דְּחַד יוֹמָא! הָהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ. דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה: מִנַּיִן לִשְׁמוּעָה רְחוֹקָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָפַכְתִּי חַגֵּיכֶם לְאֵבֶל״, וְאַשְׁכְּחַן עֲצֶרֶת דְּאִיקְּרִי חַד יוֹמָא חַג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that perhaps mourning is like ***Shavuot*, which is** only **one day.** The Gemara rejects this argument: **That** derivation, from the one day of *Shavuot*, **is required for** what was stated by **Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia: From where** is it derived **that** mourning in the case of **distant tidings,** i.e., when one hears that one of his relatives died a long time ago, **applies for only one day? As it is written: "And I will turn your Festivals into mourning," and we find** with regard to ***Shavuot* that one day is** also **called a Festival.**

The Gemara raises an objection: *v'eima atzeret d'chad yoma* — one might suggest using *Shavuot*, which is a one-day *chag*. The Gemara responds: *hahu mibayei lei l'ched'Resh Lakish* — that verse is needed for what Resh Lakish teaches, in the name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia.

Resh Lakish teaches: *minayin l'shmu'ah rechokah she'eino noheig ella yom echad* — what is the source that a distant report, one heard long after the death, requires only one day of mourning? *Dichsiv, v'hafachti chageichem l'evel* — from the same verse. *V'ashkechan atzeret d'ikri chag yoma* — and we find that *Shavuot* is called a *chag* of one day, yielding the model for a one-day *aveilus*.

The concept of *shemu'ah rechokah* reflects a principle with significant psychological and halachic nuance. When a person hears of a relative's death long after it occurred, the immediacy and subjective impact differ from a report received promptly. At the same time, the question of whether *aveilus* practices serve primarily the mourner's own experience or the honor of the deceased complicates the picture: from the mourner's subjective standpoint, even a distant report may feel immediate. Nevertheless, the objective distance from the death reduces the scope of required observance to a single day.

The *tosafot* note that the *Yerushalmi* raises a further objection: should one perhaps derive eight days, corresponding to the eight days of Sukkot? The *Yerushalmi* answers that *Shemini Atzeret* is its own *regel* — *shmini regel bifnei atzmo hu* — which, as noted above, carries its own *halachic* implications for counting against *shloshim*. The *tosafot* also observe that the *Yerushalmi* raises a question about deriving from narratives predating *mattan torah*, such as the seven days of mourning observed at the death of Yaakov Avinu, and answers that one cannot derive from pre-*mattan-torah* practice. Yet the *Yerushalmi* does cite many other sources for these institutions. It is striking how limited the Gemara's prooftexts are here — including the derivation of *shloshim* from the *nazir* case — when the Torah's own narratives reflect mourning practices of seven and thirty days quite explicitly.

## Shmuah Kerovah/Rechokah

### Close Report Full

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁמוּעָה קְרוֹבָה — נוֹהֶגֶת שִׁבְעָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים, שְׁמוּעָה רְחוֹקָה — אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: In the case of **recent tidings** of a relative's death, mourning **applies for seven- and thirty-**day periods. In the case of **distant tidings, it applies only for one day.**

The Gemara now presents a *tannaitic* teaching on the laws of *shemu'ah kerovah* and *shemu'ah rechokah* directly. *Shemu'ah kerovah noheget shiva u'shloshim* — a close report requires the full observance of *shiva* and *shloshim*.

### Distant One Day

The teaching continues: *Shemu'ah rechokah eino noheget ella yom echad* — a distant report requires only one day of mourning.

### Thirty Day Boundary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזוֹ הִיא קְרוֹבָה וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא רְחוֹקָה? קְרוֹבָה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים, רְחוֹקָה לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַחַת שְׁמוּעָה קְרוֹבָה וְאַחַת שְׁמוּעָה רְחוֹקָה — נוֹהֶגֶת שִׁבְעָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **What are** considered **recent tidings and what are** considered **distant tidings? Recent** tidings are news that arrives **within thirty** days of the person's death. **Distant** tidings are news that arrives **after thirty** days; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say: Both** in the case of **recent tidings and** in the case of **distant tidings,** mourning **applies for seven- and thirty-**day periods.

The boundary is defined: *kerovah betoch shloshim* — a report received within thirty days of the death is *kerovah*; *rechokah l'achar shloshim* — beyond thirty days, it is *rechokah*. This is the position of Rabbi Akiva. The *chachamim* disagree: they hold that whether the report arrives close or distant, the mourner observes the full *shiva* and *shloshim*. From the *chachamim*'s perspective, the mourner is experiencing the death at the moment of hearing, regardless of objective elapsed time.

### Lenient Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא יָחִיד מֵקֵיל וְרַבִּים מַחְמִירִין — הֲלָכָה כָּרַבִּים. חוּץ מִזּוֹ, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מֵקֵיל וַחֲכָמִים מַחְמִירִין — הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמֵּקֵיל בְּאֵבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Wherever you find an individual being lenient and the majority being stringent, the *halakha* is in accordance with the majority, except for this** case, **for even though Rabbi Akiva is lenient and the Rabbis are stringent, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva.** This is in keeping with the principle **stated by Shmuel: The *halakha* follows the statement of the** more **lenient** authority **in** matters relating to **mourning.**

Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan articulates a cardinal principle: *kol makom she'atah motze yachid meikel v'rabim machmirim, halacha k'rabim* — wherever you find a single authority being lenient and the majority being strict, we rule with the majority. *Chutz mizu* — with the exception of this case. Even though Rabbi Akiva is a lone voice being lenient against the strict majority of *chachamim*, we rule like Rabbi Akiva. *De'amar Shmuel: halacha k'divrei hameikel b'avel* — as Shmuel teaches, in matters of *aveilus* we follow the lenient opinion.

This principle governs *halachos aveilus* even today. It applies with unusual force in this domain: ordinarily, even in rabbinic matters, a majority opinion against a minority lenient view would carry the day. Here, uniquely, we adopt the minority leniency. The reason may relate to the recognition that observing *halachos aveilus* is both profoundly meaningful and profoundly demanding — many who would otherwise not observe *halacha* find great power in the structure of *aveilus* — and some balance must be struck. It is also worth noting that beyond the first day, all authorities agree that *aveilus* is rabbinic in character, which may further inform the consistent deference to lenient positions.

## Distant Report Cases

### Rabbi Chanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חֲנִינָא אַתְיָא לֵיהּ שְׁמוּעָה דַאֲבוּהּ מִבֵּי חוֹזָאֵי, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁמוּעָה רְחוֹקָה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** It was related that **Rav Ḥanina received** distant **tidings of his father's** death **from Bei Ḥozai. He came before Rav Ḥisda** to ask what he should do. Rav Ḥisda **said to him:** In the case of **distant tidings,** mourning **applies for only one day.**

Rabbi Chanina received word — *shemu'ah* — of his father's death from Bei Chuzai. He came before Rav Chisda to ask what to observe. Rav Chisda ruled: *shemu'ah rechokah eino noheget ella yom echad* — since enough time had passed, only one day of observance is required.

### Rav Natan Bar Ami

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נָתָן בַּר אַמֵּי אֲתָא לֵיהּ שְׁמוּעָה דְּאִימֵּיהּ מִבֵּי חוֹזָאֵי, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ, שְׁמוּעָה רְחוֹקָה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** It was similarly related that **Rav Natan bar Ami received a report about his mother's** death **from Bei Ḥozai. He came before Rava,** and Rava **said to him: They said** that in the case of **distant tidings,** mourning **applies for only one day.**

Similarly, Rav Natan bar Ami received word of his mother's death from Bei Chuzai. He came before Rava. Rava ruled: *harei amru shemu'ah rechokah eino noheget ella yom echad bilvad* — only one day.

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: בַּמָּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בַּחֲמִשָּׁה מֵתֵי מִצְוָה, אֲבָל עַל אָבִיו וְעַל אִמּוֹ שִׁבְעָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Natan **raised an objection to** Rava's opinion based on what was taught in a *baraita*: **In what** case **is this statement** that mourning applies for only one day **said?** In the case of **the** other **five close relatives,** over whose **death** it is **a mitzva** to mourn, i.e., son, daughter, brother, sister, and spouse. **But for one's father or mother,** one is required to mourn for **seven and thirty** days, even when the report of the parent's death is received some time after the event.

The Gemara challenges both rulings. A *braita* states: *bameh devarim amurim* — when do we apply the one-day rule for *shemu'ah rechokah*? *B'chamishah misei mitzvah* — for five of the seven relatives. But *aval al aviv v'al imo* — for one's father and mother, the mourner observes *shiva v'shloshim* in full, regardless of when the report is received. Since both of the above cases involved a parent, why were Rav Chisda and Rava ruling for only one day?

At this point it is worth pausing to note the distinction the Gemara is drawing. The determination of who qualifies as a relative for *aveilus* derives from the Torah's list of those to whom a *kohen* may become *tamei*: father, mother, son, daughter, brother, and sister — with the wife added by rabbinic interpretation of *lo yitama ba'al b'amov*. The special weight given to parents in this *braita* is framed not primarily in terms of the subjective emotional difficulty of parental loss — though the death of a child is in many ways harder — but rather in terms of *kibbud av va'eim*. The year of mourning for a parent, with its distinctive practices including *kaddish*, is understood as an expression of honor for the parent rather than simply a reflection of the depth of grief.

### Minority Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יְחִידָאָה הִיא, וְלָא סְבִירָא לַן כְּווֹתֵיהּ. דְּתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה וּמֵת אָבִיו שֶׁל רַבִּי צָדוֹק בְּגִינְזַק, וְהוֹדִיעוּהוּ לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, וּבָא וְשָׁאַל אֶת אֱלִישָׁע בֶּן אֲבוּיָה וּזְקֵנִים שֶׁעִמּוֹ, וְאָמְרוּ: נְהוֹג שִׁבְעָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים. וּכְשֶׁמֵּת בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֲחִיָּיה בַּגּוֹלָה, יָשַׁב עָלָיו שִׁבְעָה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him:** This **is an individual** opinion, **and we do not hold in accordance with his** opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: There was **an incident and the father of Rabbi Tzadok died in** the city of **Ginzak, and they informed him** of his father's passing only **after three years. He came and asked Elisha ben Avuya and the Elders with him** what he should do. **And** Elisha ben Avuya **said** to him: **Observe** the rites of mourning for **seven and thirty** days. **And when the son of Rabbi Aḥiyya died in the Diaspora,** and Rabbi Aḥiyya was informed about his passing a long time afterward, he **sat** in mourning for **seven and thirty** days. Therefore, it is clear that this opinion was held only by a few individual Sages, but it was not generally accepted.

Rava responds to the challenge: *yechidah hi* — that *braita* represents a minority opinion. *V'lo svira lan k'vasei* — we do not rule accordingly. A *braita* is cited in the name of Issi, the father of Rabbi Zeira, or according to others, the brother of Rabbi Zeira, who taught before Rabbi Zeira: a person who had no garment on which to perform *kria* — because all his clothes were borrowed, and one may not perform *kria* on borrowed garments — and then obtained his own garment during *shiva*, performs *kria*. But if he obtains his own garment *after shiva*, he does not perform *kria*. This seems to confirm that *kria* is confined to the context of *shiva*.

However, the *braita* identifies this as a *da'at yachid* that the mainstream does not follow. Notably, the *braita* does not confine its ruling to parents specifically — the second case involves a son. The *ashmeta* of the *braita* seems to be that for any relative, there is no concept of *shemu'ah rechokah*. Since we reject this view as a minority opinion, *shemu'ah rechokah* applies uniformly, including for parents — and the rulings of Rav Chisda and Rava stand.

## Rabbi Chiya Story

### Exchange Dialog

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא רַב, בַּר אֲחוּהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא דְּהוּא בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא, כִּי סְלֵיק לְהָתָם אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַבָּא קַיָּים? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִימָּא קַיֶּימֶת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִימָּא קַיֶּימֶת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַבָּא קַיָּים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** that this is Rabbi Aḥiyya's opinion? **But Rav was the son of Rabbi Ḥiyya's brother and** also **the son of Rabbi Ḥiyya's sister,** for Rav's father, Aivu, was Rabbi Ḥiyya's paternal half brother, and Rav's mother was Rabbi Ḥiyya's maternal half sister. **When** Rav **went there,** Eretz Yisrael, his uncle Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to him: Is** your **father,** Aivu, still **alive? Not wishing to deliver unfortunate news, Rav said to him: Is** my **mother** still **alive? In other words, why do you not ask me about my mother, who is your sister, whether she is still among the living? Rabbi Ḥiyya said to him: Is** your **mother** still **alive?** Rav **said to him: Is** my **father** still **alive?**

The Gemara presents an account involving Rav and Rabbi Chiya. Rav was the nephew of Rabbi Chiya on two sides simultaneously: Rabbi Chiya had a half-sibling on his father's side and a half-sibling on his mother's side, and those two half-siblings — not related to each other — had married one another, making Rav Rabbi Chiya's nephew twice over. When Rav traveled to Eretz Yisrael, he asked Rabbi Chiya: *abba kayam* — is father alive? Rabbi Chiya replied by asking whether Rav's mother was alive. Rav deflected again by asking about his father. The exchange went back and forth, with neither party willing to state directly that the other's parent had died. The *p'shat*, as Rashi and Tosafot discuss, is that Rabbi Chiya was conveying indirectly that his brother and sister — Rav's parents — had both died, without wishing to say so outright.

### Demonstration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: חֲלוֹץ לִי מִנְעָלַי, וְהוֹלֵךְ אַחֲרַי כֵּלַי לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥiyya immediately **said to his servant: Remove my shoes** as a sign of mourning, and afterward **carry my clothes behind me,** as I am going **to the bathhouse.**

Having understood that his brother and sister had died, Rabbi Chiya turned to his attendant and said: *chalotz li min'ali* — remove my shoe, as a mourner does not wear shoes — *v'holeich acharai keilai l'veit hamerchatze* — but take my garments to the bathhouse, for I will be going to bathe there later today. It was not that Rabbi Chiya had any particular urgent need for the bathhouse at that moment; he arranged this deliberately in order to demonstrate the *halacha*.

### Three Inferences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ — אָבֵל אָסוּר בִּנְעִילַת הַסַּנְדָּל, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ — שְׁמוּעָה רְחוֹקָה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ — מִקְצָת הַיּוֹם כְּכוּלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Learn three** *halakhot* **from this** incident with Rabbi Ḥiyya. **Learn from this** that **a mourner is prohibited from wearing shoes,** and therefore Rabbi Ḥiyya removed his shoes upon hearing of the deaths of his brother and sister. **And learn from this** that in the case of **distant tidings** of a relative's death, the mourning rites **apply for only one day** and no more. **And learn from this** that the legal status of **part of the day is like** that **of an entire** day, as immediately following the removal of his shoes, Rabbi Ḥiyya went to the bathhouse.

*Shma mina tlas* — three *halachos* may be inferred from Rabbi Chiya's conduct. First, *avel assur b'neilat hasandal* — a mourner is prohibited from wearing shoes. Second, *shemu'ah rechokah eino noheget ella yom echad* — a *shemu'ah rechokah* requires only one day of mourning, since by that afternoon he was already going to the bathhouse. Third, *miktzas hayom k'kulo* — part of the day counts as the full day, since even one partial day of *aveilus* satisfies the one-day requirement of *shemu'ah rechokah*.

## Regel/Shabbat Impact

### Regel Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין: שָׁמַע שְׁמוּעָה קְרוֹבָה בָּרֶגֶל, וּלְמוֹצָאֵי הָרֶגֶל נַעֲשֵׂית רְחוֹקָה — עוֹלָא לוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yosei bar Avin said:** If **one received recent tidings** of a relative's death **during a Festival,** when it is prohibited to mourn, **and after the Festival they became distant** tidings, as after the Festival thirty days had already elapsed since the relative's passing, the Festival **counts for him** toward the number of days that make it a belated report. **And,** consequently, after the Festival **he observes only one day** of mourning.

Rabbi Yosi bar Avin teaches: if a person heard a *shemu'ah* within the first thirty days — meaning it was technically *kerovah* — but the news arrived during *regel*, and by the time *regel* ended more than thirty days had elapsed since the death, the *regel* period counts and the *shemu'ah* is now treated as *rechokah*: *oleg lo v'eino noheig ella yom echad* — and the mourner observes only one day.

### Shabbat Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבִּי אַדָּא דְּמִן קֵסָרִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שָׁמַע שְׁמוּעָה קְרוֹבָה בְּשַׁבָּת, וּלְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת נַעֲשֵׂית רְחוֹקָה — אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabbi Adda of Caesarea taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If **one received recent tidings** of a relative's passing **on Shabbat, and at the conclusion of Shabbat it became distant tidings, he observes only one day** of mourning.

Rabbi Adda from Kisari taught the same principle before Rabbi Yochanan, extending it to *Shabbat*: if a person heard the report on day thirty on *Shabbat* — within the thirty days — but could not observe *aveilus* on *Shabbat*, and by *motzaei Shabbat* it was already day thirty-one, the *shemu'ah* is treated as *rechokah* and only one day is observed.

### Practice Timing

What emerges from both rulings is a consistent principle: the determinative factor is not when the mourner *heard* the news, but when the mourner is able to *practice* the *aveilus*. Even if the hearing fell within the thirty-day window, if objective circumstances — *regel* or *Shabbat* — prevented observance until after thirty days had passed, the moment of practice defines the *shemu'ah* as *rechokah*. The reasoning is that the practices of *aveilus* derive their meaning from their connection to the death. If the practices are being performed long after the death in objective terms, they cannot carry the full significance of *shiva u'shloshim*, regardless of the mourner's subjective experience of immediacy at the moment of hearing. The focus is on when the practices occur, not when the awareness was acquired.

## Kria Question

### Tear Clothes Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוֹרֵעַ אוֹ אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵעַ? רַבִּי מָנִי אָמַר: אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵעַ, רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אָמַר: קוֹרֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: Upon receiving a belated report of a close relative's passing, **does one rend** his garment **or does he not rend** it? **Rabbi Mani said: He does not rend** it, whereas **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** He does **rend** it.

The Gemara raises a final question: *koreah o eino koreah* — in the case where *shemu'ah kerovah* was heard during *regel* or *Shabbat* and thus treated as *rechokah* after *regel* or *Shabbat*, does the mourner perform *kria* or not? As Tosafot clarify, the general rule that *shemu'ah rechokah* does not require *kria* is taken as given; the specific question is whether this transitional case — where the hearing was technically within thirty days but the observance falls after — changes anything. Rabbi Mani rules: *eino koreah* — no *kria*. Rabbi Chanina rules: *koreah* — *kria* is performed.

### Kria in Shiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי מָנִי לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי דְּאָמֵינָא אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵעַ, הַיְינוּ דְּלָא אִיכָּא אֲבֵילוּת שִׁבְעָה. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ קוֹרֵעַ — קְרִיעָה בְּלֹא שִׁבְעָה מִי אִיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Mani said to Rabbi Ḥanina: Granted,** it makes sense **that according to my** position this is consistent, **as I say** that **he does not rend** his garment, and **this is because there is no seven-**day period of **mourning. But according to you, who says** that **he does rend** his garment, **is there rending without a seven-**day period of **mourning** afterward?

Rabbi Mani challenges Rabbi Chanina's position: *bishlama l'didi d'amina eino koreah, hainu d'lo ika aveilus shiva* — my ruling makes sense, since if a person is not observing *shiva*, there is no occasion for *kria*. But according to Rabbi Chanina, who says *kria* is performed, *kria belo shiva mi ika* — is there ever *kria* without the context of *shiva*? The implied principle is that *kria* belongs intrinsically to the context of *shiva* and cannot exist in isolation from it.

### Parents Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא אִיסִי אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אֲחוּהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא: מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ חָלוּק לִקְרוֹעַ, וְנִזְדַּמֵּן לוֹ בְּתוֹךְ שִׁבְעָה — קוֹרֵעַ. לְאַחַר שִׁבְעָה — אֵינוֹ קוֹרֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is there **not? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* reported by **Isi, father of Rabbi Zeira, and some say** it was taught by **the brother of Rabbi Zeira before Rabbi Zeira:** If at first **one did not have** his own **garment to rend, and he acquired** one **during the seven-**day period of mourning, he should **rend** it then. But if he acquired it only **after the seven-**day period of mourning, **he does not rend** it.

The Gemara responds: *v'lo* — is that truly the case? A *baraita* is cited in the name of Issi, the father of Rabbi Zeira, or according to others, the brother of Rabbi Zeira, who taught before Rabbi Zeira: a person who had no garment on which to perform *kria* — because all his clothes were borrowed, and one may not perform *kria* on borrowed garments — and then obtained his own garment during *shiva*, performs *kria*. But if he obtains his own garment *after shiva*, he does not perform *kria*. This seems to confirm that *kria* is confined to the context of *shiva*.

### Parents Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָנִי רַבִּי זֵירָא בָּתְרֵיהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בַּחֲמִשָּׁה מֵתֵי מִצְוָה, אֲבָל עַל אָבִיו וְעַל אִמּוֹ — קוֹרֵעַ וְהוֹלֵךְ! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִי, לִכְבוֹד אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira explained** the *baraita* **after him,** filling in a missing element: **In what** case **is this statement** that one does not rend his garment after the seven-day period of mourning **said?** In the case of **the** other **five close relatives** over whose **death** it is **a mitzva** to mourn, i.e., son, daughter, brother, sister, and spouse. **But for one's father or mother, one continues** to be obligated **to rend** his garment if he receives the garment after the seven-day mourning period as well. When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is referring to a symbolic act meant **to honor his father or mother.**

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction between this exception and the general principle: *ki tanya hahee lichvod aviv v'imo* — the ruling that permits *kria* after *shiva* is specific to the honor of father and mother. The general rule remains that *kria* is not performed outside the context of *shiva*, but *kibbud av va'eim* creates an exception for parents, allowing *kria* as an expression of honor even when the *shiva* period has ended.

This exception raises a further question: what if the *shemu'ah rechokah* itself involved the death of a parent — would the mourner perform *kria* as an expression of *kibbud av va'eim* even without a full *shiva*? The Gemara does not resolve this, and it becomes a subject of extended discussion in Tosafot.
