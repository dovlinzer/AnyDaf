# Beitzah 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Digging & Blood Cover

### Beit Shammai/Hillel

The sugya opens two lines from the bottom of 7b, where Rav Zreika cites a tradition concerning the debate between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* regarding *kisuy hadam* — the obligation to cover the blood of a *chayah* or *of* (a deer or chicken) slaughtered on *Yom Tov*. The earlier discussion had established that the debate is framed *lechatchila*: *Beit Shammai* permits one to slaughter *lechatchila* and then dig and cover the blood, while *Beit Hillel* holds that one should not do so *lechatchila*, though both agree that *bedi'avad* — if one went ahead and slaughtered — one may dig and cover.

The question that now arises is how digging and covering could be permitted at all. Covering blood after *shechita* is not *tzorech ochel nefesh*; the animal is already *kosher* to eat without it. It fulfills a *mitzvat aseh*, but it is not necessary to render the food edible. So how is this permitted on *Yom Tov*?

### Muktzeh & Shovel

Two distinct problems immediately present themselves. The first concerns *muktzeh*. Rav Yehuda teaches: *vehu she'yesh lo dikar na'utz miba'od yom* — this is only permitted when one has a shovel already stuck into the dirt from before *Yom Tov*. The dirt in question was not loose but packed into the ground, and dirt under normal circumstances has no designated use and would be *muktzeh*. Having the shovel pre-positioned from *erev Yom Tov* addresses this: it signals that the dirt was already contemplated for use before *Yom Tov* began.

### Melacha Problem

The second problem runs deeper. Even with the *muktzeh* issue resolved, digging on *Yom Tov* raises an independent concern: *ve'ha ka'avid tichsha* — you are grinding. Taking clumps of compacted earth and pulverizing them into loose granular material is a form of *tochen*, the *melacha* of grinding. That is a biblical prohibition. The Gemara answers: *be'afar tichuach* — we are speaking of loose dirt, where one is simply scooping out material that is already granular, not transforming a hard clod into powder.

But then a further problem surfaces: *ve'ha ka'avid gumma* — even if the dirt is already loose, you are making a hole. Making a hole can constitute one of two *melachot*: *boneh*, constructing something usable, or *choreish*, plowing, if done in soil suited for cultivation. The Gemara resolves this by invoking the principle of Rabbi Abba: *hachofer gumma be'Shabbat ve'ein tzarich ela la'afarah* — one who digs a hole on *Shabbat* but is interested only in the extracted dirt, not in the hole itself, is *patur*, because the hole is merely a byproduct of the act. The purpose of the *melacha* of digging a hole is the hole itself; here, the hole is incidental. This is the principle of *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* — a *melacha* performed for a purpose other than the intrinsic function of that *melacha*. While such an act is minimally forbidden *d'rabbanan*, it is exempt from the biblical prohibition.

### Rashi & Tosafot

Rashi adds a further consideration: beyond *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*, there is an element of *kilkul* — the act is in a sense destructive to the ground — which provides additional grounds for leniency. Tosafot takes a different approach. Tosafot acknowledges that the issue is at minimum a *d'rabbanan* violation, one that is *patur aval assur lechatchila*, and argues that it is permitted here *mishum simchas Yom Tov* — for the sake of the joy of *Yom Tov*, the Rabbis granted latitude even *lechatchila*. This framing is, as should be noted, somewhat curious, since according to *Beit Hillel* one should not have slaughtered the animal in the first place; framing the allowance of covering blood *bedi'avad* in terms of *simchas Yom Tov* is a somewhat strained rationale.

Tosafot himself anticipates a difficulty: if *simchas Yom Tov* provides the basis for the allowance, why does the Gemara require that the shovel be pre-positioned from before *Yom Tov*? His answer is that notwithstanding the broader latitude for *Yom Tov* joy, the *muktzeh*/*muchan* problem must still be independently addressed. We will override a *d'rabbanan* concern of digging a hole that one does not need, but we will not override the requirement that items be properly *muchan* before *Yom Tov*. This is, on its face, somewhat counterintuitive: one might expect the law to be stricter about what more closely resembles a *melacha* than about a purely rabbinic concern of *muktzeh*. Nevertheless, the principle holds: one must address the *muchan* issue through the pre-positioned shovel, and once that is done, the residual issue of digging a hole — reduced to a *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* — can be permitted.

This also surfaces a terminological distinction worth noting. The discussion moves between the language of *muktzeh* and *muchan*/*eino min hamuchan*. These are related but not identical concepts. The terminology of *muchan* and *eino min hamuchan* appears to be earlier, rooted in the period of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*, while *muktzeh* as a term seems to emerge from the generation of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yehuda. Conceptually they point in opposite directions: *muchan* requires affirmatively designating what is usable, while *muktzeh* requires identifying what is excluded. In the present *sugya* the operative language is that of *muchan*, which is a somewhat stricter framing.

### Muchan Status

The Gemara then shifts to the matter of covering material and introduces the case of *eifer kera* — ashes of an oven. A student asking why this is suddenly mentioned prompts the clarification that Rabbah explains the earlier statement as teaching two things: first, that one may dig with a pre-positioned shovel, and second, that if one has ashes from an oven already present, those ashes are considered *muchan* and may be used to cover the blood. The ashes of a *kirah* are not an item one would simply discard; they have enough potential household uses that they do not fall into the category of *eino min hamuchan*.

## Ashes & Dirt

### Oven Ashes

Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav teaches: *lo shanu ela shehusak me'erev Yom Tov* — this applies only where the fire was lit and the wood reduced to ashes before *Yom Tov* began. Ashes that existed at the onset of *Yom Tov* are considered *muchan* because ashes are the sort of item one might foreseeably use. They are not garbage; they are something one might use around the house for any number of purposes. But if the fire was kindled and the ashes created on *Yom Tov* itself, those ashes are *assur* — forbidden for use.

### New Ashes Exception

The reason newly created ashes are forbidden follows the same logic as the *beitzah* of the tractate's name: even though one anticipated using the wood and knew it would become ashes, the ashes did not yet exist when *Yom Tov* began and cannot be considered *muchan*. However, there is an exception: *ve'im ra'uy litzlot bo beitzah* — if the ashes are still hot enough and substantial enough that one could bake an egg in them, they are *mutar*. The reasoning is that such ashes have not yet completed their transformation. They began *Yom Tov* as wood, and wood is used for making fire, holding heat, and cooking. While somewhat transformed, ashes that can still bake an egg are still functionally performing the role of the original wood. They are not yet an entirely new entity. Once the fire is truly dead and the ashes are cold and inert, they become something genuinely new, and if created on *Yom Tov* they are not *muchan*.

### Daitey Ilavey

Rashi adds a comment suggesting that one must have had in mind from before *Yom Tov* to use the ashes for covering blood. This raises the question of what *muchan* actually requires by way of conscious intention. The answer is that *muchan* does not require explicit, conscious designation. The operative concept here is *daitey ilavey* — that one's mind was upon it. This does not mean that one actually sat down before *Yom Tov* and said, "I hereby designate these ashes for potential future use." Rather, it means that if one were asked before *Yom Tov* whether one might conceivably have a use for these ashes, one would answer yes. *Daitey ilavey* is a general background awareness — the kind of item that, as a matter of ordinary life, one anticipates one might need. The Gemara nowhere requires an affirmative conscious act of designation; the standard is whether this is the kind of thing people generally expect they might use.

A *braita* confirms this: *keshe'amru efer kera muchan hu* — when the Sages said ashes of a *kirah* are *muchan*, they meant only where the fire was kindled before *Yom Tov*. If kindled on *Yom Tov*, those ashes are *assur*, unless they can still bake an egg, in which case they remain effectively part of the original wood.

### House Dirt

The *braita* continues: *hichnis afar leginato* — if one brought dirt into one's garden or yard, *mutar lechasot bah* — one may use it to cover blood. This dirt is genuinely all-purpose. In a world where dirt had many uses — filling in holes, covering odors, any number of household needs — a pile of dirt brought into one's yard was clearly *muchan* for any need that might arise. One does not need a pre-positioned shovel in such a case; the dirt is already loose, already accessible, and already clearly *muchan* in every relevant sense.

### Rav Yehuda Dirt

Rav Yehuda adds an independent teaching: *machnis adam melo kupato afar* — a person may bring in a basketful of dirt and use it for *kol tzorcho* — any purpose. The novelty here relative to the yard scenario is twofold. First, the quantity is smaller — a basket as opposed to a large pile — and Rashi explains that the pile in the yard, by virtue of its size, self-evidently declares itself as general-purpose, whereas a basket of dirt requires the affirmative statement that it is *kol tzorcho*. Second, the context is more unusual: bringing a basket of dirt *into the house* is an odd act, and one might think it designates the dirt only for some particular purpose. The *chiddush* of *kol tzorcho* is that even in the house, even in a small quantity, such dirt is available for any conceivable use.

Mar Zutra adds a qualification: *vehu she'yichad lo keren zavit* — this applies only if the dirt has been given its own corner. If it is just a small pile lying on the floor without a distinct space, it risks being absorbed into the floor and losing its identity as a designated object. By assigning it a corner, one makes clear that it exists as a discrete, usable quantity.

## Koy Problem

### Koy Status

With this framework in place — ashes of a *kirah* are *muchan*, a pile of dirt in the yard is *muchan*, a basket of dirt brought into the house for *kol tzorcho* is *muchan* — the Gemara raises the case of the *koy*. The *koy* is an animal of indeterminate taxonomic status: it is unknown whether it belongs to the category of *chayah* (wild animal, like a deer) or *behemah* (domesticated animal, like a cow). This distinction carries two significant *halachic* consequences. A *chayah*'s blood must be covered after *shechita*, and one may eat the *chelev* (fat) of a *chayah*, whereas the *chelev* of a *behemah* is forbidden because that fat is normally brought on the *mizbeach*. If the *koy* is a *chayah*, its blood must be covered and its fat is permitted; if it is a *behemah*, covering is not required and the fat is forbidden.

The question: may one slaughter a *koy* on *Yom Tov*? The answer given is no — *ein shochtin oso b'Yom Tov* — because doing so places one in the situation of not knowing whether one is obligated to cover the blood. And *bedi'avad*, if one already slaughtered it, one may not cover the blood either, because covering blood requires some form of *Yom Tov* latitude that we will grant for a *vadai* obligation but not for a *safek*.

### Gemara Challenge

The Gemara immediately challenges this: given Rav Yehuda's teaching that a basket of dirt brought in for *kol tzorcho* is *muchan* for anything, why not simply use such dirt to cover the *koy*'s blood? If dirt is available for every purpose without qualification, there is no issue of *muktzeh*, no issue of digging a hole, and therefore no basis for prohibiting the covering of the *koy*'s blood. Surely this is a common scenario, and Rav Yehuda's rule should resolve it entirely.

The Gemara presses further: even setting aside Rav Yehuda's ruling, there is still the *eifer kira* — ashes of the *kirah* from before *Yom Tov* — which pose no *muktzeh* problem, require no hole to be dug, and should be available for covering. So why should slaughtering a *koy* be prohibited? (Tosafot excises the mention of the pre-positioned shovel here for independent reasons, noting that the shovel involves the problematic *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* of digging a hole, and therefore cannot serve as a clean counterexample. The ashes, however, are unproblematic.) If covering is possible without any *Yom Tov* latitude whatsoever, the prohibition on slaughtering the *koy* seems inexplicable.

The Gemara resolves the challenge by clarifying the case: *ela d'leis lei* — we must be speaking about a situation where one has neither ashes nor a pile of dirt, and the only option for covering is to dig the hole. Similarly: *hacha nami d'leis lei* — the case of the *koy* is also one where no ashes and no pile of dirt are available. In that scenario, covering would require digging the hole, and it is precisely there that the distinction between *vadai* and *safek* applies.

But this response immediately generates a further problem: *mai irya safek?* — if neither ashes nor prepared dirt are available, why single out the *koy* for prohibition? Even a *vadai chayah* should be forbidden *lechatchila* according to *Beit Hillel*, since slaughtering it would also require digging the hole. The Gemara answers with a *lo mibaya* construction: of course one should not slaughter a *vadai chayah lechatchila* if the only option is digging the hole — that goes without saying. The novel teaching of the *braita* addresses a different scenario: someone comes before the *posek* and says, "I have a *koy*. Let me slaughter it on *Yom Tov* — and then I simply won't cover the blood, since my obligation is only a *safek*. The *tircha* involved doesn't apply because I don't intend to cover anyway." The *braita* teaches: even that is not permitted. One may not slaughter a *koy* intending to leave the blood uncovered, because leaving it uncovered when it might be a *chayah* is itself unacceptable.

This leaves the following picture: *lechatchila*, neither a *vadai chayah* nor a *koy* should be slaughtered if one lacks ashes or prepared dirt. *Bedi'avad*, if one slaughtered a *vadai chayah*, one may dig the hole and cover; if one slaughtered a *koy*, one may not cover.

### Rava's Response

One might ask: the *braita* itself, in stating that *bedi'avad* one may not cover the *koy*'s blood, implies that a mechanism for covering exists — otherwise, why say one may not cover? *Ein mechassin* presupposes *yesh*. Rashi reads the Gemara here as objecting to a reading of the *braita* that assumes absolutely no covering option exists, not even a hole. But regardless, the Gemara treats this objection as grounds to pivot to a different explanation, introduced by Rava.

Rava states: *eifer kira muchan l'vadai ve'ein muchan l'safek*. Ashes of a *kirah* are *muchan* for uses that are definite needs, but they are not *muchan* for uses that are doubtful. This is, on its face, a striking claim. One might argue: the practical need to cover the *koy*'s blood is a *vadai* need. Whether the *halachic* obligation is a *safek* is a legal question, but the immediate practical situation is that the blood is there and may need to be covered. Why should the ashes' *muchan* status be calibrated to the *halachic* certainty of the obligation rather than the practical certainty of the need?

The answer lies in what *muchan* actually tracks. Ashes, unlike a basket of dirt designated *kol tzorcho*, are an item with a narrow range of uses. There are very few things one regularly uses ashes for. Because of this, ashes are *muchan* only for the specific uses one would reliably expect to have — not for generic anything-that-comes-up. Among those expected uses is slaughtering a *chayah* and covering the blood; that is a foreseeable, regular use. But using ashes for the blood of a *koy* is less foreseeable — it is in a *safek* category — and therefore the ashes were never mentally designated for that purpose. They are *muchan* for the *vadai* use but not for the *safek* use.

This creates a fascinating internal structure to the concept of *muchan*. It is not binary — either *muchan* or *muktzeh*. An item can be *muchan* for a defined set of anticipated uses and effectively *muktzeh* relative to other uses, even if those other uses are not unreasonable. The ashes are available for things one clearly expects to need them for; for things one only doubtfully expects to need them for, they are not available.

### Dirt Purpose Compare

The Gemara then records a *memra* attributed to Rava: *hichnis afar le'chasot bo tzo'ah mutar le'chasot bo dam tzippor* — if one brought dirt into the house to cover excrement, one may also use it to cover the blood of a bird (*dam tzippor*). Conversely, *dam tzippor assur le'chasot bo tzo'ah* — if one brought the dirt specifically to cover bird blood, one may not use it to cover excrement.

The first half of this ruling appears intuitively backwards. If one brought dirt to cover excrement — a *safek* use, since one does not know whether the baby will defecate in an inconvenient place — surely one may also use it for covering bird blood, which is a *vadai* use. The answer is indeed that: *safek* includes *vadai*. If you have designated dirt for a less certain use, it is certainly *muchan* for the more certain use as well. But the reverse is not true: if dirt was brought for *dam tzippor*, a *vadai* use, it is not *muchan* for the covering of excrement, which is a *safek* use.

This places excrement coverage firmly in the *safek* category — something that may or may not come up, depending on whether the child soils a place where it is bothersome. The *vadai* use is *dam tzippor* — the bird is being slaughtered on *Yom Tov*, the obligation to cover is certain. This establishes the hierarchy: *dam tzippor* is the definite use; *tzo'ah* is the uncertain use; and *koy* is an even more doubtful case in terms of the *halachic* obligation.

The Gemara also works through whether the Gemara's analysis of the *koy* case is really grounded in the hole-digging problem or in the *muchan* problem. When the Gemara asks *lesafek mai ta'ama lav deka'avid gumma* — perhaps the problem with scooping out ashes is that it makes a hole — it notes that scooping ashes for a *vadai* also makes a hole. The response *k'deRabbi Abba* — that it is *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa* — applies equally in both cases. And here Rabbi Abba's principle applies identically whether the need is *vadai* or *safek*, undermining any attempt to ground the distinction in the hole-making concern.

The Gemara also considers whether *asei doche lo ta'asei* — a positive *mitzvah* overriding a negative prohibition — could permit even a more significant *Yom Tov* violation when the obligation is certain. But the Gemara rejects this avenue. *Asei doche lo ta'asei* applies only when the very act that fulfills the positive commandment is simultaneously the act that uproots the prohibition — as in the case of *milah* in *tzara'at* or *tzitzit* with *shatnez*. Here, the grinding of a clod of earth occurs before the covering of the blood; the two acts are sequential, not simultaneous. Even the suggestion that one could pound the clod directly over the blood, so that grinding and covering happen at once, does not fully resolve the problem. More decisively, *Yom Tov* is both a *lo ta'aseh* and an *aseh* — *lo ta'aseh melachah* and *shabbaton*. Since *Yom Tov* involves both a positive and a negative commandment, the principle *ein asei docheh lo ta'aseh ve'aseh* means that the *asei* of *kisuy hadam* cannot override *Yom Tov* even in the simultaneous-act scenario.

The Gemara is thus left without a satisfying account of why more latitude is given in a *vadai* case than in a *safek* case when the concern is the possible violation itself. If there is a real risk of violating *tochen* by accidentally grinding a clod, that risk exists equally for the *chayah* and for the *koy*. The Gemara cannot simply say we are less worried about it in the *vadai* case because we care more about the *mitzvah* — because the concern, if real, should apply in both cases.

This impasse returns the Gemara to Rava's answer: *kira da'ato le-vadai ve'ein da'ato le-safek*. The ashes — and, by extension, similar items — carry an implicit *da'at* for *vadai* uses, not for *safek* uses. This is not primarily about the hole; it is about *muchan* status. And this is what *ve'azda Rava le'ta'amei* — Rava is consistent with his own reasoning — confirms through the ruling about dirt brought in for *tzo'ah* being usable for *dam tzippor*, but not vice versa.

### Koy-Tzo'ah Dispute

The Nahardelai, identified in another Gemara as Rami bar Berabi (or bar Bruchi), take a more expansive position: *amri Nahardelai afilu hichnis afar le'chasot bo dam tzippor mutar le'chasot bo tzo'ah* — even if one brought dirt specifically for the bird blood, one may use it to cover excrement. Whether this represents a principled disagreement with the *vadai/safek* framework — asserting, like Rav Yehuda, that *kol tzorcho* means genuinely anything — or whether the Nahardelai simply classify excrement coverage as close enough to *vadai*, is a genuine question.

The issue is debated in *Eretz Yisrael* between Rabbi Yossi bar Chama and Rabbi Zeira (or, in another version, Rava the son of Rabbi Yosef bar Chama and Rabbi Zeira): *chad amar koy harei hu k'tzoa* — one says the *koy* is comparable to excrement; *ve'chad amar koy eino k'tzoa* — the other says it is not comparable.

The debate can be mapped along a spectrum. At one end, clearly *vadai*, stands *dam tzippor*: one is definitively slaughtering the bird, one is definitively obligated to cover. Moving inward toward *safek*, covering excrement is a high-likelihood but not certain need. The *koy* sits further out still — the need to cover may be *vadai* in a practical sense (the blood is there), but the *halachic* obligation is genuinely uncertain. The question of *koy k'tzoa* is whether covering excrement and covering *koy* blood are close enough in their degree of likelihood/certainty to be treated the same way.

There is a subtlety here that deserves acknowledgment: the *safek* of the *koy* is not the same type of *safek* as the *safek* of the excrement. The excrement is a *safek* of need — one does not know whether the baby will soil a bothersome spot. The *koy* is a *vadai* need in a practical sense but a *safek* obligation — one definitely has *koy* blood on the ground, but one does not know whether one is legally required to cover it. These are structurally different types of uncertainty, and it is worth noting that the Gemara's framing of both as *safek* relative to *vadai* glosses over this asymmetry.

The Gemara tentatively identifies Rava as the one who says *koy harei hu k'tzoa*, based on his ruling that dirt brought for *tzo'ah* is usable for *dam tzippor* but not vice versa. If Rava places *tzo'ah* in the *safek* category (as the less definite use), and if *koy* is to be placed in the same category as *tzo'ah*, then under Rava's framework both would be inaccessible from dirt designated for *dam tzippor*. The *koy*, like the *tzo'ah*, is a *safek* use, and ashes or specifically designated dirt are not *muchan* for *safek* uses.

## Girsa Gezeirah

### Rav Yeiva Girsa

Rav Yeiva offers an entirely different explanation for why one may not cover the blood of a *koy* on *Yom Tov*: *gezeirah mishum hataras chelbo*. This has nothing to do with digging holes, nothing to do with *muktzeh* or *muchan*, and nothing to do with *melacha she'eina tzricha legufa*. Even granting Rav Yehuda's principle that a basket of loose dirt is available for anything, and even assuming no *tircha* whatsoever in the covering act, one still may not cover the *koy*'s blood on *Yom Tov*. The reason is that observers will see the blood being covered and conclude that the animal must have been a *chayah* — and therefore that its *chelev* is permitted. The *koy* faces the worst-case combination: it might be a *chayah*, requiring *kisuy hadam*, while simultaneously being a *behemah* whose *chelev* is forbidden. If people observe the blood being covered and conclude it is a *chayah*, they will come to eat the *chelev*, which may in fact be *assur* as *chelev behemah*.

### Weekday/YomTov

One might ask: this concern about misinterpretation should apply equally on a weekday. If someone sees a person covering the *koy*'s blood on a weekday, would they not also conclude that it is a *chayah* and the *chelev* is permitted? The Gemara responds: *b'chol amri l'naker chatzero hu d'tzarich* — on a weekday, observers will assume the person is merely cleaning up his yard, removing a bloodstain from the ground. They will not assume the act is a deliberate *mitzvah* performance. On *Yom Tov*, by contrast, the context is different: one does not casually clean one's yard on *Yom Tov*. If a person is observed digging and covering on *Yom Tov*, it is immediately clear that the act is purposeful and legally motivated. The conclusion observers will draw is that the person *must* cover the blood — and that presupposes the animal is definitively a *chayah*.

### Exceptions

The Gemara introduces two scenarios that might challenge this reasoning. First: what if on a weekday one covers the blood in the middle of a dung pile — clearly not cleaning one's yard — where observers will understand the act is a *mitzvah*? Second: what if one comes to the *beis din* and asks whether to cover, and they instruct one to do so — would that not equally broadcast that it is a *mitzvah* performance? The Gemara responds by refining the distinction. On a weekday, even if it is clear that the act is Rabbinically mandated, observers understand that the Rabbis sometimes instruct *safek* behavior. People know that *halacha* sometimes requires one to act under doubt. They will interpret the covering as a precautionary measure — *dilma* this animal is a *chayah*, so the Rabbis told him to cover just to be safe — and will not conclude that the *chelev* is definitively permitted.

On *Yom Tov*, however, that reasoning breaks down: *mi amrei lei rabbanan zil tarch v'chasei* — would the Rabbis really tell someone to expend effort on *Yom Tov* for a *safek*? The observer's reasoning will be: if he is covering on *Yom Tov*, the Rabbis must be certain it is a *chayah*, because otherwise they would not impose this *tircha* on him during *Yom Tov*. The conclusion follows naturally: it is a *chayah*, the *chelev* is permitted.

### Mixed Blood Tircha

This reasoning immediately raises a question: if the entire problem is that covering on *Yom Tov* signals certainty of *chiyuv*, then the framing around misinterpretation might actually be a derivative concern — the underlying issue may simply be *tircha*. The Gemara seems to confirm this when it records the case of Rabbi Zeira: *lo koy be'vadai amru* — not only may one not cover the *koy*'s blood, but even if one slaughtered a *behemah*, a *chayah*, and an *of* and their blood became mixed together, one may not cover the combined blood on *Yom Tov*. The concern here is that covering the mixed blood requires additional effort on account of the *behemah* blood, which does not require covering. The *tircha* issue is explicit: effort expended on behalf of a non-required act (*behemah* blood) piggybacks on the obligatory act (*chayah* blood) and cannot be permitted.

### One Shovel Rule

Rabbi Yossi bar Yasinaia refines this ruling: *lo shanu ela she'ein yachol le'chasoto be'dkirah achat* — the prohibition applies only when one cannot cover the blood with a single shoveling. If the mixed blood requires multiple rounds of shoveling — necessarily including some extra effort for the *behemah* blood — the prohibition applies. But *aval yachol le'chasoto be'dkirah achat mutar* — if it can all be covered with one scooping, it is permitted. The Gemara objects: surely this is obvious? The response: one might have thought that we should prohibit even the single-shovel case as a *gezeirah* against the double-shovel case — lest one come to allow even the extra effort when it is necessary. Rabbi Yossi bar Yasinaia teaches that no such *gezeirah* is imposed.

The *sugya* thus concludes by returning to *tircha* as a central organizing concern. Three explanations for the prohibition of covering *koy* blood on *Yom Tov* have emerged. The first — rejected by the Gemara but arguably the most straightforward — was that digging a hole is problematic, and while we extend latitude for that in a *vadai* case, we do not for a *safek*. The second, developed at length by Rava, is the *muchan* framework: ashes and designated dirt are *muchan* for *vadai* uses but not for *safek* uses, and *koy* blood coverage falls into the *safek* category. The third, introduced by Rav Yeiva, is a *gezeirah* against misinterpretation: covering on *Yom Tov* signals that the Rabbis are certain of the obligation, which will lead people to permit the *chelev*. And underlying the third explanation, as the final sugya about mixed blood makes clear, is *tircha* — the concern about expending effort on *Yom Tov* for uncertain or non-obligatory acts — which may in fact have been the cleanest distinction between *vadai* and *safek* all along.
