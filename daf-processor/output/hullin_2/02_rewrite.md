# Hullin 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Hullin

### Name & Scope

We begin Masechet Chullin, daf 2a. The masechet is traditionally called Shechitas Chullin, in contrast to Zevachim, which is Shechitas Kodshim. The name reflects its primary subject matter, though the masechet is by no means limited to shechita alone.

### Masechet Topics

The masechet is extremely well organized, covering all the steps involved in eating meat that is not a korban. It opens with two perakim on shechita, then treats treifos — the examination for punctures and wounds. It then addresses the mitzvos relating to kisuy hadam, covering the blood. Notably, this obligation applies only to birds and chayos such as deer, but not to behemos, since the blood of domesticated animals normally goes on the mizbeach. The masechet continues with the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its mother on the same day, and then addresses the removal of blood, gid hanasheh, and forbidden fats, concluding with the prohibition of cooking meat with milk. The masechet thus traces the entire sequence of what one must do in order to eat meat.

## Hakol Shochtin Mishnah

### Anyone May Slaughter

Our perek opens with the mishnah: *hakol shochtin* — anyone may slaughter — *veshechitasan kesheira* — and their shechita is valid.

### Cheresh Shoteh VeKatan

The mishnah then provides an exception: *chutz mecheresh shoteh vekatan* — excepting the cheresh, shoteh, and katan. This is the standard triad of individuals who, in halachah, are considered to lack da'as — the mature, functioning intellect required to effect halachically significant acts. The cheresh is a deaf-mute; the shoteh is one who is functionally unaware of his surroundings or the significance of his actions (with the technical parameters discussed in Masechet Chagigah); and the katan is a minor, a boy under thirteen or a girl under twelve.

Typically, such individuals are categorically excluded from acts that are not merely physical but require intent and awareness of the act's legal significance — a business transaction being the classic example, where even if an observer could confirm that all the technical steps were performed, the transaction still would not take effect, because the transaction is constituted by the parties' knowledge and intent, not merely by the physical exchange.

### Not Categorical Exclusion

What is striking here is that the mishnah does not treat the exclusion of cheresh shoteh vekatan as categorical. The mishnah explicitly spells out the reason: *shema yekalkelu es shechitasan* — lest they ruin their shechita. The concern is not that they are constitutionally incapable of effecting a halachic act, but rather that they may not execute the physical act correctly — they may not have the sufficient attention or awareness to ensure proper technical performance. This is therefore not a categorical exception but a concern about performance.

### Observed = Valid

Accordingly, the mishnah concludes: *vechullan sheshachatu ve'acherim ro'im osam, shechitasan kesheira* — if any of them slaughter while being observed by others, their shechita is valid. Since the exclusion was never categorical but only a concern about technical execution, the presence of an observer who can verify proper performance suffices to render the shechita valid.

This contrasts sharply with cases where cheresh shoteh vekatan are categorically excluded — such as performing a mitzvah on someone else's behalf, or executing a sale. In those contexts, even a perfectly observed performance would not suffice, because the deficiency is not in the physical act but in the legal capacity of the actor.

### Cheresh in Modern Law

One might ask: why should a cheresh, whose physical coordination is entirely intact, lack the requisite awareness for shechita? The answer lies in the assumption, operative in the Talmudic period when there were no means of communication with a deaf-mute, that such a person was effectively outside the normal frameworks of society — unable to receive information or to communicate in any meaningful way — and therefore lacked the focused awareness required to perform the act reliably.

Contemporary poskim have engaged seriously with whether this assumption still applies. One approach is to argue that the reason for the exclusion no longer obtains in the modern world. This approach is often considered halachically problematic, since halacha tends to focus on the defined parameters of a category rather than the underlying reasons, which would otherwise allow for too much flexibility in redefining categories. The more common approach, therefore, is to argue at the level of the parameters themselves: the cheresh was excluded because he could not speak. Today, a person who communicates through writing or sign language may well be considered to speak through those means and therefore might not fall within the technical definition of cheresh at all. There is a substantial body of contemporary teshuvos addressing this question.

## Hakol: L'chatchila?

### L'chatchila vs B'diavad

The Gemara opens with an observation about the mishnah's language. *hakol shochtin* — anyone may slaughter — implies that *lechatchila* anyone may do so. Yet the subsequent phrase *ushechitasan kesheira* — their shechita is valid — sounds like a post-facto endorsement, implying that certain people should not have done it *lechatchila* but that *bediavad* it is acceptable. The two halves of the mishnah appear to be in tension.

Rav Acha brei deRav poses this to Rav Ashi: *vechol hakol lechatchila hu?* — Is it always the case that the word *hakol* implies *lechatchila*? Perhaps *hakol* simply means that whoever does it produces a valid result, without implying that all such people are equally preferred *lechatchila*.

The Gemara then surveys a range of mishnayot that open with the word *hakol*, in order to determine whether *hakol* normally implies *lechatchila* or only *bediavad*. It should be noted that in Masechet Erchin a parallel discussion examines *hakol* from a different angle entirely — asking whom the word is coming to include. Here in Chullin, the Gemara takes a different tack, asking not who is included but whether their inclusion is *lechatchila* or merely *bediavad*.

### Temura Challenge

The first challenge comes from Masechet Temura: *hakol memirin* — anyone can effect a temura. Temura is the act of attempting to transfer the kedusha from one korban to another. The Torah explicitly prohibits this: *lo yachlifenu velo yamir oso, tov bera o ra betov* — do not exchange it, do not transfer it, good for bad or bad for good. If you attempt it, both animals become kadosh — the kedusha does not leave the original, but the replacement animal also acquires sanctity. *hakol memirin* means that everyone who attempts temura succeeds in effecting it. But can this mean that it is permitted *lechatchila*? Clearly not, since doing so is a biblical prohibition.

### Temura Answer

The Gemara answers: *hatam kidekatani ta'ama* — there, the mishnah in Temura itself explicitly qualifies the statement. The mishnah says *lo she'adam rashai lehamir* — this is not to say that a person is permitted to perform temura — *ela she'im hemir mumar* — but that if he did, it takes effect. And it adds *vesofeg es ha'arba'im* — and he receives the forty lashes. The fact that the mishnah in Temura had to explicitly state that *hakol memirin* does not mean *lechatchila* is itself proof that *hakol* normally does mean *lechatchila*. Here in Chullin, where no such qualification appears, *hakol shochtin* should indeed be read as *lechatchila*.

### Erchin Challenge

The Gemara continues with a challenge from the opening mishnah of Masechet Erchin: *hakol maarichin vene'erachin, nodrim venidrim* — everyone can pledge an erech and be the subject of an erech; everyone can make a neder and be the subject of one.

To understand the challenge, it is necessary to explain the two modes of valuation described at the end of Sefer Vayikra. If one says *dmei ploni alai* — the monetary value of that person is upon me — one obligates oneself to give the market value of that person to the Beis Hamikdash. Since a person has no market value in ordinary circumstances, this is determined by asking what that person would be worth if sold as a slave. If one instead uses the technical term *erech* — which also means value but functions as a terminus technicus — the obligation is fixed by the schedule the Torah itself provides: male or female, within various age ranges, each carries a specified amount. The word *erech* thus bypasses market value entirely and substitutes a Toraitic list.

The word *hakol* in that mishnah comes to include, among others, a *mufla samuch le'ish* — a minor within a year of adulthood who understands the significance of vows, whose vows are binding. *vene'erachin* — being the subject of an erech — includes even one who is terminally ill or unable to do any labor and thus has no market value: when the word *erech* is used, one pays the fixed Toraitic rate regardless. *nidrim* — being the subject of a neder of market value — includes even a newborn, who has no erech since the Toraitic list begins at one month, but who may have a market value.

### Vow Debate: Meir/Yehuda

The Gemara now challenges the inference that *hakol* implies *lechatchila* from this mishnah in Erchin: *vehaktiv vechi techdal lindor lo yihiyeh vecha chet* — the verse says that if you cease to make vows there will be no sin in you, implying that vowing is something to be avoided. And the famous verse in Kohelet: *tov asher lo tiddor mishetiddor velo tishalem* — better not to vow than to vow and not to fulfill. So how can *hakol maarichin* imply that vowing is *lechatchila* permitted?

This opens the celebrated debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda. A baraisa teaches: *tov mizeh umizeh she'eino noder kol ikar* — better than either of them is not to vow at all. This is the position of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: *tov mizeh umizeh noder umeshalem* — the best is to vow and to fulfill. Rabbi Meir's position is clear: even if you intend to fulfill your vow, you should not vow, because you are opening yourself to the risk of violation. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that vowing and fulfilling is praiseworthy.

### Yehuda Read Narrowly

One might think that even according to Rabbi Yehuda, the mishnah in Erchin could be read as *lechatchila*. The Gemara, however, chooses to read Rabbi Yehuda very narrowly: *ve'afilu Rabbi Yehuda lo ka'amar ella be'omer harei zeh* — even Rabbi Yehuda's lenient position applies only when one actually sanctifies a specific object, saying "this animal" or "this sum of money" is hereby donated. In that case there is no personal obligation: one has sanctified the object itself, not taken upon oneself a personal duty to produce a payment. If the object is lost without negligence, there is no personal liability, since the obligation resided in the object rather than in the person. What Rabbi Yehuda would not permit, under this reading, is a personal vow — one where the person takes upon themselves an obligation to pay — since that always carries the risk of non-fulfillment. Under this reading, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda converge: both agree that a personal vow is always potentially problematic. Rabbi Yehuda's permissive ruling applies only to the sanctification of an object, not a personal commitment.

With this, the challenge to *hakol* meaning *lechatchila* is reinstated: according to both Rabbi Meir and this narrow reading of Rabbi Yehuda, there is never a straightforward *lechatchila* basis for making a personal vow. So *hakol maarichin* cannot mean *lechatchila*.

### Why Make Vows?

This naturally raises the question of why the Torah describes vowing as a category at all, if it is always better not to vow. The answer lies in the psychological reality that people are sometimes seized by intense religious motivation — at a moment of inspiration or thanksgiving, they want to give expression to that feeling and to commit themselves to acting on it. A vow channels that motivation and creates a binding obligation that makes it more likely the person will actually follow through. Without the vow, the impulse may simply dissipate: one thinks, "I should give some money," and by the time one returns home, the feeling has passed and nothing happens. The vow is the mechanism by which the feeling translates into action.

The tension, then, is that the same mechanism that spurs action also opens the door to sin if the vow is not fulfilled. The Gemara's concern is with that downside risk, and that is why — at least according to Rabbi Meir and the narrow reading of Rabbi Yehuda — a personal vow is never straightforwardly recommended *lechatchila*.

It is also worth noting that the Torah never formulates a mitzvah to donate money to the Beis Hamikdash. It describes the phenomenon of such donations and legislates the laws of vows at the end of Vayikra, but it does not command it. The analogous commandments are tzedakah and support for the Levi'im. Capital donations to the sanctuary were clearly a major and ancient phenomenon — ancient synagogues bear inscriptions attesting to donors — but they are not themselves a mitzvah in the technical sense. The institution of vows taps into the deep religious motivation people feel to give, while the Gemara's restraint reflects the real risks that accompany formal commitment.

### Smicha Challenge

The Gemara continues. Having argued back and forth, it now tries the opposite position: perhaps *hakol* never means *lechatchila*. To test this, the Gemara cites: *hakol somchin* — everyone may perform smicha, the act of pressing one's hands on a korban. The verse says *vesamach yado venirtzah* — and he shall press his hand and it will be desired for him — which clearly establishes smicha as an obligation, something one should do *lechatchila*. Can *hakol somchin* be read as only *bediavad*? Surely not.

### Hakol: Either Way

The Gemara therefore concludes: *ika hakol lechatchila ve'ika hakol bediavad* — sometimes *hakol* means *lechatchila* and sometimes it means *bediavad*, depending on context. Since *hakol* can mean either, the original question dissolves: even if *ushechitasan kesheira* implies *bediavad*, *hakol shochtin* need not mean *lechatchila*. Perhaps the entire mishnah is speaking *bediavad*.

### Manufactured Problem

At this point the Gemara introduces what can only be described as a deliberately manufactured tension. It says: the real difficulty for me was the phrase *shechitasan kesheira* — since that implies *bediavad*, the opening *hakol shochtin* must mean *lechatchila*, for otherwise why use two phrases both meaning the same *bediavad* thing? Simply say *shechitasan hakol kesheira* and be done with it. Since the mishnah employs both phrases, the first must be conveying something different — *lechatchila* — which then creates a contradiction with the *bediavad* implication of the second phrase.

This is frankly a strange piece of reasoning. Having already established that *hakol* can mean *bediavad*, the simplest reading would be to accept the mishnah as consistent throughout. The Gemara instead insists on reading the two halves as saying different things in order to introduce a tension — and it is through resolving that tension that the Gemara will now read a host of additional cases into the mishnah's few words. The manufactured quality of the problem is the point: the Gemara is using it as an opportunity to expand what the mishnah is teaching.

### Smicha & Women MSS

Before moving on, a textual point regarding the smicha case. The Gemara here reads *hakol somchin, echad hanashim ve'echad ha'anashim* — whether men or women. This is striking, because a marginal note in the printed text — attributed to the scholar responsible for the cross-references, Yehoshua Boaz — states: *chipasnu bechol hamakomot velo matzanu ken beshum makom bashas* — we searched everywhere and found this formulation nowhere else in the entire Talmud. On the contrary, *ve'adaraba ipecha matzanu* — we found the opposite: that women are excluded from smicha. Even Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon, who permitted women to perform smicha, held only that it was *reshus* — permissible if they wished — not that women are obligated at the same level as men. The inclusion of *echad hanashim ve'echad ha'anashim* in the context of smicha, which in every other place in the Talmud is described as not obligatory for women, is an outlier. A check of the *dikdukei soferim* confirms that this phrase does not appear in some manuscripts, making it quite plausible that it entered the text as a copyist's error.

## Tamei Shochet Reading

### Tamei in Chullin

Rabbah bar Ula now proposes a reading of the mishnah that resolves the manufactured tension and simultaneously introduces new material. He says: *hachi katani* — this is how the mishnah should be read. *hakol shochtin* — anyone may slaughter — means that even a tamei person may slaughter *lechatchila*. Why would one have thought otherwise? Because the mishnah is referring to chullin shena'asu al taharat hakodesh — non-sanctified food that is being treated with the purity standards of kodesh.

This practice had a historical reality. There was a group known as the *chaverim* who were scrupulous about maintaining purity even for their ordinary food, treating their chullin with the same level of taharah ordinarily required for kodshim. This practice existed both during and after the period of the Beis Hamikdash. The question therefore arises: if you are part of such a group and you are tamei, may you slaughter your meat?

One might assume that since you are treating your food like kodesh, you ought not slaughter it while tamei, just as you would not handle actual kodshim while tamei. The mishnah therefore tells us: *hakol shochtin* — even a tamei person may slaughter *lechatchila*. The Gemara's initial objection — *tamei bechullin mai le'meimra*, why would we need to be told that a tamei person may slaughter ordinary chullin? — is answered precisely by this specification: we are not talking about ordinary chullin, but chullin al taharat hakodesh, where the tamei status is a relevant concern.

### Long Knife Solution

Even for chullin al taharat hakodesh, however, the tamei shochet must take care: *mevi sakkin arucha veshochet bah, kedei shelo yigga babasar* — he brings a long knife and slaughters with it, in order not to touch the meat. Tosafot raises a question about the mechanics of this concern, noting that for food to contract tumah it must have first been in contact with a liquid — and the blood of shechita does not serve as the requisite *machshir* to render the meat susceptible to tumah. Tosafot raises this question in the context of chullin al taharat hakodesh, though the full resolution of that question is not pursued here.

### Tamei Kodshim B'diavad

While a tamei person may slaughter chullin al taharat hakodesh with a long knife *lechatchila*, the case of actual kodshim is different: *uvemukdashim lo yishchot shema yigga babasar* — a tamei person should not slaughter a korban *lechatchila*, lest he touch the meat. However, *ve'im shachat ve'omer bari li shelo nagati, shechitatho kesheira* — if he did go ahead and slaughter, and declares with certainty that he did not touch the meat, the shechita is valid *bediavad*.

### VeChullan Referent

With this framework, the full mishnah takes on a more complex reading. *hakol shochtin* — everyone may slaughter *lechatchila*, including even a tamei person slaughtering chullin al taharat hakodesh with a long knife. *ushechitasan kesheira* — and *bediavad* it is valid — refers to the tamei person who slaughtered actual kodshim: even though *lechatchila* he should not have, if he did and did not touch the meat, the korban is valid.

*Chutz mecheresh shoteh vekatan* — excepting the cheresh, shoteh, and katan — means that even *bediavad*, if they slaughtered even ordinary chullin, their shechita is not valid: *shema yishhu, shema yidresu, shema yachlidu* — lest they have paused, applied pressure, or displaced the simanim. These are categorical concerns about technical performance that apply to the plainest case of chullin.

Then *vechullan sheshachatu ve'acherim ro'im osam* — if anyone slaughtered while being observed — does not refer back solely to the cheresh shoteh vekatan, but primarily to the tamei person slaughtering kodshim. If he was observed, even if he cannot himself report that he did not touch the meat, the observation suffices to establish that he did not, and the shechita is valid. This reading explains why the mishnah uses the broad *kol* rather than simply saying "and if cheresh shoteh vekatan slaughtered and were observed": the phrase is carrying a different referent, namely the tamei shochet of kodshim.

### Tosafot: Where Standing?

Tosafot raises a pointed practical question about the scenario of a tamei person slaughtering kodshim. A tamei person is prohibited from entering the Beis Hamikdash. Now, a person who is tamei meis — who contracted tumah through contact with a corpse — is permitted, according to biblical law, to enter the Ezras Nashim; only the Mikdash proper is off-limits. One could imagine him standing at the threshold of the Ezras Yisrael, with the korban just inside, and slaughtering it with a long knife. However, the problem is that by rabbinic enactment, even a tamei person was not permitted to be anywhere in the Ezras Nashim. And the Ezras Nashim is 135 amos — approximately 200 feet — in length. It is difficult to imagine a knife 200 feet long. Tosafot therefore suggests two possibilities: either the scenario refers to shechita on the bamos, the altars used outside of Jerusalem before the Beis Hamikdash was built, rather than to the Beis Hamikdash proper; or alternatively, it might depend on precisely where one was standing relative to the various entrances to the Temple complex.

### Women Shecht L'chatchila

On this point Tosafot cites a ruling from a Geonic work called Hilchos Eretz Yisrael to the effect that women should not perform shechita because *daatan kalos* — they are of insufficiently serious and focused mind. Tosafot rejects this categorically. In Tosafot's view, not only may women slaughter chullin, they may slaughter even kodshim *lechatchila*. The verse in Zevachim, to be discussed shortly, explicitly lists women among those who may perform shechita on sacrifices, and the word *lechatchila* is appropriate. *hakol shochtin* thus clearly includes women — and not merely in a *bediavad* sense, but fully *lechatchila*, even for kodshim. There are in fact one or two known historical cases of women who served as shochtos, and there is contemporary interest among women in studying Hilchos Shechitah. The halachic sources are unambiguous that gender is no barrier to shechita; only cheresh shoteh vekatan are excluded.

### Why Repeat Here?

The Gemara now raises an objection to Rabbah bar Ula's reading: *hai tamei bemukdashim me'hacha nafka?* — is the law about a tamei person and kodshim derived from here? It is derived from the explicit mishnah in Masechet Zevachim: *kol hapsulin sheshachatu shechitasan kesheira* — any invalid persons who slaughtered, their shechita is valid — *she'shechitah kesheira bezarim, banashim, uba'avadim, uvetmei'im, ve'afilu bekodshei kodashim* — for shechita is valid when performed by non-kohanim, by women, by slaves, and by tamei persons, even with the most holy sacrifices — *uvilvad shelo yihyu tme'im nogim babasar* — provided that the tamei persons do not touch the meat. This mishnah in Zevachim already teaches everything Rabbah bar Ula reads into our mishnah. Why would the law need to be repeated here?

### Zevachim Primary Source

The Gemara offers two answers. The first: *hacha ikar* — the primary teaching is actually here, with Zevachim's inclusion being secondary. The second, which the Gemara identifies as the better answer: *hasam ikar* — the primary teaching is indeed in Zevachim, where the context is kodshim and the ruling fits naturally. *debekodshim kai* — since Zevachim is speaking about korbanot, that is where the ruling about a tamei shochet properly belongs. Our mishnah, however, since it is discussing tamei and chullin, also implicitly brings in the case of tamei and kodshim alongside it. The case is not the primary teaching of our mishnah; it is carried along by the discussion. What makes this exchange remarkable is that the discussion in our Gemara is a lengthy elaboration of two short phrases — *hakol shochtin ushechitasan kesheira* — from which an entire framework regarding tumah and shechita has been constructed, including then an inquiry into why that which was never stated needed to have been stated.

## Ramban on Vows

### Yaakov's Vow

Before closing, it is worth pausing on the issue of vows. The Ramban poses a difficulty: the very Torah figures held up as models of piety made vows. *vayidor yaakov neder leimor* — Yaakov made a vow. *vayidor yisrael* — Yisrael made a vow. And we have the exhortation *nidru veshalmu la'Hashem Elokeichem kol sevivav* — make vows and fulfill them to Hashem your God, all those around Him. How is all of this reconciled with the discouragements of vowing found in Kohelet and in the Gemara?

### Vow in Distress

One answer the Ramban cites is *dibe'shaat tzarah shani* — in times of distress it is different, and vowing is specifically permitted. This fits within Ramban's broader framework, in which prayer too is commanded specifically in times of distress rather than as a daily obligation. In moments of crisis, making a vow is a legitimate expression of one's appeal to and reliance on God.

### Vows for Tzedakah OK

The Ramban then adds his own view, which he finds the more fundamental point: *veli yeira'eh shelo asru ella bekodshei mizbeach* — it seems to me that the prohibition applies specifically to vows concerning korbanot brought on the mizbeach — *shema yavo lidei takalah* — lest one come to violate the vow — *ve'ein bekorbanot tzorekh gavoah* — and because God has no need of your korbanot, as the verse states, *hinei shemoa mizevach tov* — behold, obedience is better than sacrifice. Since the korban is ultimately for the benefit of the person bringing it, not for God, and since there is the risk of non-fulfillment, vowing to bring korbanot carries particular danger.

However, *aval litzedakah ulevahapish issar la'aniyim* — if you are making a vow to give charity or to give money to the poor — *ve'ein tzorekh lomar lekayem*, and certainly to fulfill other mitzvot — *nishba'in venodrin* — it is appropriate to make such vows. Vows in service of genuine good in the world — giving to the poor, supporting those in need, fulfilling important obligations — are not subject to the same critique. The problem was always vowing in the context of korbanot, where the utility was personal and the risk of sin was real. Vowing in order to do good in the world is another matter entirely.

This is a fascinating Ramban, offering what amounts to a critique of korbanot as a category — not that they were wrong, but that they served human needs rather than divine ones — in a way that resonates with the conclusion of Masechet Zevachim itself, where the Gemara similarly notes that God does not need our korbanot. Vows that actually produce benefit in the world, that spur us to give tzedakah and to do mitzvot, stand on different ground entirely.
