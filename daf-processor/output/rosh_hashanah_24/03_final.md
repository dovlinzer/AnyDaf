# Rosh Hashanah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Moon Directionality

### Before/After Sun

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד בּוֹדְקִין אֶת הָעֵדִים? זוּג שֶׁבָּא רִאשׁוֹן — בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ רִאשׁוֹן. וּמַכְנִיסִין אֶת הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶם, וְאוֹמְרִין לוֹ, אֱמוֹר כֵּיצַד רָאִיתָ אֶת הַלְּבָנָה: לִפְנֵי הַחַמָּה, אוֹ לְאַחַר הַחַמָּה? לִצְפוֹנָהּ, אוֹ לִדְרוֹמָהּ? כַּמָּה הָיָה גָּבוֹהַּ, וּלְאַיִן הָיָה נוֹטֶה, וְכַמָּה הָיָה רָחָב. אִם אָמַר לִפְנֵי הַחַמָּה — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How do they examine the witnesses** who come to testify about the new moon? They deal with them in order, as **the pair** of witnesses **that arrives first they examine first. They bring in the greater of** the two witnesses, **and they say to him: Say how you saw the moon.** Was it **in front of the sun or behind the sun? To its north or to its south? How high was** the moon over the horizon, **and in which direction did it tilt? And how wide was** it? **If,** for example, **he said** that he saw the moon **in front of the sun, he has not said anything** of substance, as this is impossible and therefore he is either mistaken or lying.

The *gemara* opens by examining the astronomical framework used to test moon witnesses, picking up at the very bottom of 23b. The *mishnah* had already established that witnesses who claim to have seen the new moon before the sun — *lifnei hachammah* — have said nothing valid, *lo amar klum*. The *gemara* now unpacks why, and extends the analysis to a second axis of questioning: north versus south.

The underlying astronomy is as follows. The moon moves counterclockwise relative to the sun. When the moon is in exact conjunction with the sun — perfectly aligned — it is completely dark, since only its far side is illuminated. This is the *molad*. The moon becomes visible only some hours later, once it has moved sufficiently away from the sun that it is no longer blinded by solar glare. At that point, the new moon trails just behind the sun. The old moon, by contrast, sits just ahead of the sun — it is visible in the final moments before sunrise and then sets before the sun does. The new moon is the mirror image: invisible during the day because the sun is too bright, it becomes visible after sunset because the sun has already set and the moon is trailing just behind. This is why the new moon must be seen *after* the sun, *leachar hachammah*, and any witness who says otherwise is simply describing something that cannot be true.

### North/South Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַיְינוּ לִפְנֵי הַחַמָּה, הַיְינוּ לִצְפוֹנָהּ; הַיְינוּ לְאַחַר הַחַמָּה, הַיְינוּ לִדְרוֹמָהּ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פְּגִימָתָהּ — לִפְנֵי הַחַמָּה, אוֹ לְאַחַר הַחַמָּה? אִם אָמַר לִפְנֵי הַחַמָּה — לָא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **In front of the sun is** the same as **to its north,** and **behind the sun is** the same as **to its south.** Why would the court ask the witnesses both sets of questions? **Abaye said:** The first question does not refer to the moon's position relative to the sun, but to **the concave side** of the moon, whether it was **before the sun,** pointing toward it, **or behind the sun,** pointing away from it. **If he said** that it was **before the sun, he has said nothing.**

The second axis of questioning — *litzfonah* or *lidromah*, to the north or to the south — requires a more careful treatment of the sun's path. In the northern hemisphere, the sun does not travel directly east to west but arcs toward the south: rising in the east, curving southward, and setting in the west. Given this trajectory, something that is *lifnei hachammah*, in front of the sun as it moves, lies more to the north; something that is *leachar hachammah*, behind the sun, lies more to the south. The *gemara* therefore equates *lifnei hachammah* with *litzfonah* (north) and *leachar hachammah* with *lidromah* (south).

## Moon Indentation

### Pegima Definition

Abaye, however, reframes the question entirely. The question of *lifnei* versus *leachar*, he argues, is not simply asking whether the moon leads or trails the sun — that question is already settled, and the answer must be that it trails. Rather, Abaye understands the question as: *pegimata lifnei hachammah o leachar hachammah* — is the indentation, the lacking side of the moon, facing toward the sun or away from it?

This is physically straightforward once stated. The moon is illuminated by the sun; whichever portion faces the sun is lit, and whichever portion faces away is dark. The *pegimah*, the lacking or indented side, is therefore always the side facing away from the sun. A witness who claims that the indented side was facing toward the sun has described an optical impossibility. *Im amar pegimata lifnei hachammah, lo amar klum* — if he says the indentation faced the sun, he has said nothing.

### Moon Embarrassment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״הַמְשֵׁל וָפַחַד עִמּוֹ עוֹשֶׂה שָׁלוֹם בִּמְרוֹמָיו״, מֵעוֹלָם לֹא רָאֲתָה חַמָּה פְּגִימָתָהּ שֶׁל לְבָנָה, וְלֹא פְּגִימָתָהּ שֶׁל קֶשֶׁת. פְּגִימָתָהּ שֶׁל לְבָנָה — דְּחָלְשָׁה דַּעְתַּהּ. פְּגִימָתָהּ שֶׁל קֶשֶׁת — דְּלָא לֵימְרוּ עוֹבְדֵי הַחַמָּה
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is** the meaning of that **which is written: "Dominion and fear are with Him, He makes peace in His high places"** (Job 25:2)? It means that **the sun has never seen the concave** side **of the** new **moon, nor** has it ever seen **the concave** side **of a rainbow,** both of which always face away from the sun. It has never seen **the concave** side **of the moon, as** the moon would be **distressed** by a sense of inadequacy if its concave side faced the sun. Therefore the sun sees only the full roundness of its convex side. In this way He makes peace in His high places between the sun and the moon. Furthermore, the sun has never seen **the concave** side **of a rainbow, so that the worshippers of the sun not say,** as though the sun is a god,

This physical law is anchored in a *midrashic* teaching. Rabbi Yochanan derives it from the verse in Job: *hamshal ufachad imo oseh shalom bimromav* — dominion and fear are with Him, He makes peace in His celestial heights. The peace referred to is this: *me'olam lo ra'ata chammah pegimata shel levanah* — the sun has never seen the indentation of the moon. The reason given is *dechalsha da'atah* — so that the moon would not become despondent. This evokes the well-known *midrash* in which the moon complained that two luminaries of equal size cannot share the sky, and God diminished it. Given that humiliation, it would be an additional cruelty to allow the sun to see the moon's lacking side — its nakedness, as it were — and so the *pegimah* always faces away.

### Rainbow Symbolism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גִּירֵי קָא מְשַׁדְּיָיא.
>
> **Translation:** that the sun **is shooting arrows** at those who deny its divinity, using the rainbow as its bow. The concave side of the rainbow always faces away from the sun, so that it does not look like a bow held by the sun.

The same verse teaches that the sun has also never seen the indentation of the rainbow, *pegimata shel keshet*, for a different reason: *delo limru ovdei chammah girei ka mashdaya* — lest the sun-worshippers say that God is shooting arrows. A rainbow, viewed as an archer's bow, has its concave side facing the direction from which arrows are shot. The Ramban notes that the rainbow's significance as a covenantal sign lies precisely in this reversal: God holds the bow with its concave side facing upward and away, the posture of one who surrenders his weapon, as if to say He will not shoot down upon the earth. The raindrops of the *mabul* may be understood as the arrows — a connection the *gemara* surfaces here, though the precise astronomical relationship between the sun's position and the arc of the rainbow remains a matter for further investigation.

## Moon Directionality (II)

### Summer/Winter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הָיָה גָּבוֹהַּ, וּלְאַיִן הָיָה נוֹטֶה כּוּ׳. תָּנָא חֲדָא: לִצְפוֹנָהּ — דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין, לִדְרוֹמָהּ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. וְהָתַנְיָא אִיפְּכָא: לִדְרוֹמָהּ — דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין, לִצְפוֹנָהּ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that the examination of the witnesses included the following questions: **How high was** the moon over the horizon, **and in which** direction **did it tilt? It was taught** in **one** *baraita*: If the witness testifies that he saw the moon **to the north** of the sun, **his statement is valid.** However, if he says that he saw it **to its south, he has not said anything** of significance, as this is impossible. The Gemara asks: **Isn't the opposite taught** in a different *baraita*: If he testifies that he saw the moon **to the south** of the sun, **his statement is valid.** However, if he says he saw it **to its north, he has not said anything.**

Returning to the north-south question, the *gemara* presents an apparent contradiction. One *baraita* states that a witness who says the moon was to the north, *litzfonah*, has given valid testimony, while one who says it was to the south, *lidromah*, has said nothing. A second *baraita* says precisely the opposite: *lidromah devarav kayamin*, south is valid, while *litzfonah lo amar klum*, north is invalid. This second formulation, at least, coheres with the analysis above, in which the new moon trailing the sun would be to the south.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן, בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה. כָּאן, בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** in the second *baraita* mentioned above, it is referring **to the summer,** when the moon is to the south of the sun; **there,** in the first *baraita* mentioned above, it is referring **to the rainy season,** when the moon is to the north of the sun.

The *gemara* resolves: *lo kashya*, there is no difficulty. *Kan bimot hachammah, kan bimot hageshamim* — one *baraita* refers to summer and the other to winter. The explanation that best captures the astronomical reality, as the *Meiri* articulates it, is as follows. In winter, the sun's arc is shorter: it rises in the southeast and sets in the southwest. In summer, the arc is longer: it rises in the northeast and sets in the northwest. The question being posed to the witnesses is where on the horizon the moon set. In winter, the correct answer is that it set in the south; in summer, in the north. The apparent contradiction dissolves once one distinguishes between the two seasons. Rashi offers a different explanation that is considerably more difficult to unpack astronomically, and the *Meiri*'s reading is to be preferred.

A further contextual note: the earlier *mishnah* mentions that these examinations took place in a great courtyard in Jerusalem — *chatzer gedolah hayta biYerushalayim* — in the time of Rabban Gamliel the Elder, when the *Beit HaMikdash* still stood. But the proceedings we are now reading about involve Rabban Gamliel of Yavneh, post-destruction, and the seat of the court had moved to Yavneh. Rabbi Akiva, as discussed in an earlier *sugya*, detained witnesses in Lod on their way to Yavneh. The astronomical calculations thus properly apply to wherever the testimony is being accepted — which, after the *churban*, was Yavneh.

## Witness Height Testimony

### Two vs Three Marda'ot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶחָד אוֹמֵר גָּבוֹהַּ שְׁתֵּי מַרְדְּעוֹת, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ — עֵדוּתָן קַיֶּימֶת. אֶחָד אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר חָמֵשׁ — עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה. אֲבָל מִצְטָרְפִין לְעֵדוּת אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **one** witness **says** that he saw the moon **two plow handles high** above the horizon, **and** the other **one** says it was **three** plow handles high, **their testimony is valid,** as a small discrepancy of this kind is reasonable. However, if **one says** that he saw the moon **three** plow handles above the horizon, **and the** other **one says** it was **five** plow handles high, **their testimony is void,** as that discrepancy is unacceptable. **However,** this does not mean that the witnesses themselves are disqualified, as either of them may **join with another testimony,** i.e., he may combine his account with that of another witness who testifies likewise.

The *gemara* turns to a different dimension of witness testimony: the height of the moon above the horizon. The *baraita* states that if one witness says the moon was two *marda'ot* — some unit of measurement, roughly understood as saddle-lengths — above the horizon, and another says three, their testimony is considered consistent: *edutan kayamet*. A modest imprecision in observation is accepted; we do not expect witnesses to be perfectly precise instruments.

### Three vs Five Marda'ot

However, if one says three and the other says five, that is too great a discrepancy: *edutan beteila*, their combined testimony is nullified, since one cannot say they witnessed the same thing. Nevertheless, *aval mitztarfin le'edut acheret* — they may combine with other testimony. The reason is that we do not assume either witness is lying; each retains his *chazakah* of being *kasher*. Rashi explains that the witness who said three can pair with another who says three, and the one who said five with another who says five. The parallel in tractate *Sanhedrin* regarding discrepancies in testimony about timing is instructive, though the precise mechanics interact with other discussions there.

## Indirect Sightings

### Water/Glass/Clouds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רְאִינוּהוּ בְּמַיִם, רְאִינוּהוּ בַּעֲשָׁשִׁית, רְאִינוּהוּ בֶּעָבִים — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו. חֶצְיוֹ בְּמַיִם, חֶצְיוֹ בֶּעָבִים, חֶצְיוֹ בַּעֲשָׁשִׁית — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in another *baraita* that if the witnesses say: We did not actually see the moon, but **we saw it** reflected **in the water,** or **we saw it** reflected **in** a glass **lantern,** or **we saw it through** thin **clouds, they** may **not testify about it,** as only a direct sighting of the moon is acceptable. If they say: We saw **half of** the moon's reflection **in the water,** or we saw **half of it through the clouds,** or we saw **half of it in a lantern, they** still may **not testify about it.**

The *gemara* now addresses several forms of compromised or indirect sightings. If witnesses say *re'inuhu bamayim* — we saw its reflection in the water — or *ba'ashashit* — through a glass or lantern — or *be'avim* — in the clouds — *ein me'idim alav*, such testimony is insufficient. The concern is that what was seen may have been a glowing patch mistaken for the moon.

### Partial Visibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא כּוּלּוֹ אָמְרַתְּ לָא, חֶצְיוֹ מִבַּעְיָא?! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: חֶצְיוֹ בְּמַיִם חֶצְיוֹ בָּרָקִיעַ, חֶצְיוֹ בֶּעָבִים חֶצְיוֹ בָּרָקִיעַ, חֶצְיוֹ בַּעֲשָׁשִׁית חֶצְיוֹ בָּרָקִיעַ — אֵין מְעִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **Now,** if when one sees **the entire** moon in this manner, **you said** that this is **not** valid testimony, **is it necessary** to teach that one may not testify when he sees only **half of it? Rather, this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** If the witnesses saw **half of** the moon's reflection **in water** and **half of it** directly **in the sky,** or **half of it through the clouds** and **half of it in the sky,** or **half of it in a lantern** and **half of it in the sky,** although they saw half of the moon directly, **they** may **not testify** about it until they see the entire moon in the sky.

The *mishnah* also addresses the case of *chetzyo bamayim chetzyo barakia* — half in reflection and half directly visible in the sky — or half obscured by clouds and half directly seen. The *gemara* notes a logical puzzle: if a witness who claims to have seen the entire moon through a reflection or cloud has said nothing, why does the *mishnah* need to tell us that seeing only half under such conditions is also invalid? Of course it is. The *gemara* resolves that the *mishnah* is not speaking of a witness who saw only half; rather, he saw half of the moon directly in the sky and the other half only as reflected in water, or only through cloud cover. Even that partial directness is insufficient. The entirety must be seen without any obstructing or distorting medium.

### Episodic Sighting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: רְאִינוּהוּ וְשׁוּב לֹא רְאִינוּהוּ — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו. כׇּל הָכִי חָזוּ לֵהּ וְאָזְלִי?!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in another *baraita* that if the witnesses say: One moment **we saw** the new moon, **but we did not see it again, they** may **not testify about it.** The Gemara asks: **Must they go on watching it to such** an extent, i.e., why should they have to see it for a long period of time?

Another *baraita* addresses the case of witnesses who say *re'inuhu v'shuv lo re'inuhu* — we saw it and then could no longer see it. Such testimony is also invalid. One might object: the new moon is visible for only a few minutes after sunset before it sets; is it not inevitable that witnesses saw it and then it was gone? Must they have watched it continuously?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: רְאִינוּהוּ מֵאֵלֵינוּ, וְשַׁבְנוּ לִרְאוֹתוֹ מִדַּעְתֵּנוּ, וְלֹא רְאִינוּהוּ — אֵין מְעִידִין עָלָיו, מַאי טַעְמָא — אֵימוֹר כּוֹבִיתָא דְעֵיבָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּחָזֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** If the witnesses say: **We saw** the moon **on our own** accord, i.e., by chance, without looking for it, **and** then **we returned to look for it on purpose,** to deliver precise testimony about it, **but we did not see it** again, **they** may **not testify about it. What is the reason?** Because one can **say** that the first time it was **merely a small round** white **cloud that they saw,** which they mistook for the moon, and that is why when they looked for it again they could not find it.

Abaye clarifies. The *baraita* is not describing a case where the moon simply set in the ordinary course of events. Rather, *re'inuhu me'aleinu* — they saw it incidentally, in passing — *v'shavnu lir'oto mida'ateinu* — and then returned deliberately, intending to examine it carefully enough to testify — *velo re'inuhu*, and at that point could no longer see it. *Mai ta'ama* — what is the legal concern? *Kuvisha de'eiva b'alma hu d'chaza* — perhaps what they originally glimpsed was merely a rounded cloud. The fact that they could not relocate it when they looked intentionally suggests that the initial sighting was not reliable. Notably, the *gemara* does not require that a witness intend to testify at the moment of sighting — that is a separate discussion arising in *Makkot* and elsewhere. What it does require is that when a witness harbors enough doubt to go back and verify, and verification fails, the original sighting cannot serve as testimony.

## Sanctification Ritua (II)

### Mekudash Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רֹאשׁ בֵּית דִּין אוֹמֵר: ״מְקוּדָּשׁ״, וְכׇל הָעָם עוֹנִין אַחֲרָיו: ״מְקוּדָּשׁ מְקוּדָּשׁ״. בֵּין שֶׁנִּרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ, בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא נִרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ — מְקַדְּשִׁין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: After the witnesses have been examined and their testimony accepted, **the head of the court says:** It is **sanctified. And all the people respond after him:** It is **sanctified;** it is **sanctified. Whether** the moon **was seen at its** anticipated **time,** on the thirtieth day of the previous month, **or whether** it **was not seen at its** anticipated **time,** in which case witnesses are not necessary to establish the following day as the New Moon, the court **sanctifies it** and formally proclaims the day as the New Moon.

The *gemara* now arrives at the ritual of formal sanctification. *Rosh Beis Din omer mekudash, vechol ha'am onim acharav mekudash mekudash* — the head of the court declares *mekudash*, sanctified, and all those present respond: *mekudash, mekudash*. The significance of this public assent is considerable. The entire thrust of the tractate has been that *Beis Din* holds the power to fix the calendar, rooted in the principle of *asher tikre'u osam* — it is you, the people, who call and designate the festivals. The verbal affirmation of the assembled community is not mere ceremony; it is the enactment of that authority.

## Sanctification Debat (II)

### Tanna Kamma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר: אִם לֹא נִרְאָה בִּזְמַנּוֹ — אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁכְּבָר קִידְּשׁוּהוּ שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, says: If** the new moon **was not seen at its** anticipated **time,** the court **does not sanctify** the New Moon on the following day, **as** the celestial court in **Heaven has already sanctified it,** precluding the need for the additional sanctification by the earthly court.

The *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah* holds that the sanctification declaration is performed regardless of whether the moon was seen in its time — that is, whether the previous month was a short twenty-nine-day month or a long thirty-day month. Even when the thirtieth day arrives and it is self-evidently *Rosh Chodesh* without any sighting of the moon, the act of communal declaration is still performed. The analogy offered is the *mitzvah* of *veKidashtem et shenat hachamishim shanah* — the *yovel* year must be formally sanctified even though its identity is perfectly clear after forty-nine years of counting. The act of sanctification is not merely informational; it is constitutive.

## Sanctification Ritua (II)

### Moshe Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רֹאשׁ בֵּית דִּין וְכוּ׳. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר גַּמְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בֶּן שָׁאוּל אָמַר רַבִּי: אָמַר קְרָא ״וַיְדַבֵּר מֹשֶׁה אֶת מוֹעֲדֵי ה׳״ — מִכָּאן שֶׁרֹאשׁ בֵּית דִּין אוֹמֵר ״מְקוּדָּשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that **the head of the court** says: It is sanctified. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** that the court must sanctify the New Moon, derived? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Gamda said** that **Rabbi Yosei ben Shaul said** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said** that **the verse states: "And Moses declared** to the children of Israel **the appointed seasons of the Lord"** (Leviticus 23:44). **From here** it is derived **that the head of the court says:** It is **sanctified,** as it is evident from the verse that Moses, whose status was equivalent to that of the head of the Great Sanhedrin, declared the appointed times of the Festivals and New Moons in a formal manner.

The *gemara* seeks biblical grounding for the declaration formula. Chizkiyah bar Gamda in the name of Rabbi Yossi ben Shaul in the name of Rabbi derives from the verse *Vayidaber Moshe et mo'adei Hashem el Bnei Yisrael* — and Moshe declared the festivals of God to the children of Israel. Moshe, as the representative of the people and head of the court, is the archetype for the *rosh beis din* who pronounces *mekudash*. The word *vayidaber*, with its connotation of authoritative declaration rather than mere communication, is the source.

### People's Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הָעָם עוֹנִין אַחֲרָיו ״מְקוּדָּשׁ מְקוּדָּשׁ״. מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אָמַר קְרָא ״אֲשֶׁר תִּקְרְאוּ אוֹתָם״, קְרִי בֵּיהּ אַתֶּם. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: ״אֵלֶּה הֵם מוֹעֲדֵי״, הֵם יֹאמְרוּ מוֹעֲדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further teaches that after the head of the court says: It is sanctified, **all the people respond after him:** It is **sanctified;** it is **sanctified.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rav Pappa said** that **the verse states: "Which you shall proclaim them [*otam*]** to be sacred convocations" (Leviticus 23:2). Instead of *otam*, **read into** the verse: **You [*atem*],** as though the verse stated: Which you shall proclaim, you. This superfluous word you teaches that the month must be proclaimed not only by the head of the court, but also by you, the people. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that it is derived from the phrase: **"These are [*hem*] My appointed seasons"** (Leviticus 23:2). The term *hem* can also mean: They, which indicates that **they,** the people, **should** likewise **say** and announce **My appointed seasons.**

The response of the people — *mekudash, mekudash* — is derived by Rav Pappa from the verse *asher tikre'u osam* — which you shall proclaim. The verb *tikre'u* is in the plural, addressing the entire community. The *gemara* additionally reads it as *asher tikre'u atem* — you, the people, shall call. Notably, as a student points out, the letter *vav* is in fact absent from the text of *atem* in the verse, making this not merely a homiletical play but a genuine textual variant reading. The power of establishing the calendar is thus explicitly vested in the community, not only in its representative. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak derives from *eileh heim mo'adai* — these are My festivals — that the people themselves must pronounce *mo'adai*, "My appointed times."

### Double Affirmation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מְקוּדָּשׁ מְקוּדָּשׁ״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי לְמָה לִי? דִּכְתִיב: ״מִקְרָאֵי קוֹדֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **Why do I** need the people to declare: It is **sanctified;** it is **sanctified, twice?** The Gemara answers that the reason is **that it is written: "Sacred convocations"** (Leviticus 23:2) in the plural, which indicates that they must announce it twice.

Why is *mekudash* said twice? The *gemara* derives this from the plural form *mikra'ei kodesh* — holy convocations, in the plural — implying that the affirmation must itself be plural. The double *mekudash* also functions similarly to the double *amen* known elsewhere: a heightened, emphatic assent that internalizes and reinforces the reality being declared. An analogy is drawn to the *birkat hachodesh* recited in the synagogue before *Rosh Chodesh*, in which *chaverim kol Yisrael* and *venomar amen* may represent these two layers of communal affirmation, though the parallel is inexact since that announcement precedes the new month by several days.

## Sanctification Debat (II)

### R. Elazar Tzadok

Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok holds that the declaration is performed only when the moon was not seen in its time — that is, only on a twenty-nine-day month. If it was already a thirty-day month, with *Rosh Chodesh* having arrived automatically, the declaration is not performed.

### Plimo Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, פְּלֵימוֹ אוֹמֵר: בִּזְמַנּוֹ — אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא בִּזְמַנּוֹ — מְקַדְּשִׁין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites other opinions in this regard: **It is taught** in a *baraita* that the Sage **Palaimo says:** If the new moon was seen **at its** anticipated **time,** the court **does not sanctify** the New Moon, as the new moon appeared as expected and there is no need for any special sanctification. However, if the new moon was seen **not at its** anticipated **time,** the court must **sanctify** the New Moon.

Plimo takes the opposite view, and it is a striking one: *bizmano ein mekadeshin oso* — precisely when the month is a twenty-nine-day month, you do *not* make the declaration; *shelo bizmano mekadeshin oso* — only when it is a thirty-day month do you declare it. Rashi offers a strained explanation — *she'aino tzarich chizuk*, it does not need reinforcement — which is difficult to understand. The position seems nearly inexplicable, and the lecturer acknowledges it as such.

### R. Elazar Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אוֹתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְקִדַּשְׁתֶּם אֵת שְׁנַת הַחֲמִשִּׁים״ — שָׁנִים אַתָּה מְקַדֵּשׁ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְקַדֵּשׁ חֳדָשִׁים. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: In either case,** the court **does not sanctify** the New Moon formally, **as it is stated: "And you shall sanctify the fiftieth year"** (Leviticus 25:10), from which it is derived: **You** must formally **sanctify years** in court, **but you do not** have to formally **sanctify months,** as they are sanctified automatically by Heaven. Rather, it is enough merely to announce to the public that a certain day was established as the New Moon. **Rabbi Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok,** that if the new moon was seen at its anticipated time, the court sanctifies the new month; however, if it was not seen at its anticipated time, they do not sanctify it.

Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon holds that neither situation requires the declaration: *bein kach uvein kach ein mekadeshin oso* — in either case, no formal *mekudash* is pronounced. His textual basis is the verse *veKidashtem et shenat hachamishim shanah* — from which he derives that *shanim ata mekadesh*, one sanctifies years, but *ve'i ata mekadesh chodashim*, one does not formally sanctify months. The acceptance of the witnesses' testimony is sufficient; no ritual act of declaration accompanies it.

This position is surprising given the entire thrust of the tractate. The masechet consistently emphasizes that *Beis Din* actively and authoritatively creates *Rosh Chodesh*, not merely identifies it. The absence of a formal *mekudash, mekudash* could suggest, on the contrary, that *Beis Din* is simply adjudicating the astronomical facts rather than exercising a constitutive power — that *Rosh Chodesh* is made by the astronomical reality and *Beis Din* merely clarifies what that reality is. But that reading stands in tension with, for instance, the power of *Beis Din* to fix the calendar even against witness testimony, or to establish a different day if it chooses. We are left with four distinct positions: the *tanna kamma* (always), Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok (only on a short month), Plimo (only on a long month), and Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon (never).

### Ruling

Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok* — the law follows Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok, and the declaration is made only on a twenty-nine-day month. Abaye notes that this ruling is supported by a later *mishnah*, which he introduces to buttress the *halakhic* conclusion.

## Missed Window

### Me'ubar Outcome

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: רָאוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל, נֶחְקְרוּ הָעֵדִים וְלֹא הִסְפִּיקוּ לוֹמַר ״מְקוּדָּשׁ״ עַד שֶׁחָשֵׁיכָה — הֲרֵי זֶה מְעוּבָּר. מְעוּבָּר — אִין, מְקוּדָּשׁ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We, too, learn** in a mishna: If **the court and all of the Jewish people saw** the new moon, and **the witnesses were interrogated, but** the court **did not manage to say: Sanctified, before nightfall,** the previous month **is** rendered **a full,** thirty-day month. This indicates that **full, yes;** i.e., in this case, it is necessary to extend the previous month. However, **sanctified, no;** there is no need to formally sanctify the month, as the New Moon does not occur on its anticipated date in this case.

The *mishnah* Abaye cites states: if the *Beis Din* saw the moon — indeed, all of Israel saw it — the witnesses were examined, and yet they did not manage to pronounce *mekudash* before nightfall, *harei ze me'ubar* — the month is thereby automatically a thirty-day month. The opportunity has passed; the month is *me'ubar*.

From this language Abaye infers: *me'ubar in, mekudash lo* — the *mishnah* says it is *me'ubar*, implying it is not *mekudash*, meaning one does not go back the next day and make the declaration retroactively. Had the *mishnah* wanted to indicate that the *Beis Din* could simply try again the next day, it would have said *harei ze yekadshuhu lemachar* — let them sanctify it tomorrow. The fact that it instead says *me'ubar* closes off that possibility.

### Me'ubar Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעוּבָּר אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְרָאוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל, אִיפַּרְסְמָא וְלָא לִיעְבְּרוּהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: There is no proof from here, as **it was necessary for** the mishna to mention that the month is rendered **a full,** thirty-day month. The reason is that it might **enter your mind to say** that **since the court and the entire Jewish people saw** the new moon, **it has become public** knowledge **and** therefore there is **no** need **to** render the previous month a **full,** thirty-day month. Consequently, the mishna **teaches us** that even in this case the previous month must be extended. Nevertheless, it is still possible that the New Moon must be formally sanctified on the following day.

The *gemara* pushes back: perhaps the *mishnah* had no choice but to use the language *harei ze me'ubar*. Why? Because one might have thought that since the moon was seen by *Beis Din* and by all of Israel — *isparsema*, it was publicly known — perhaps the *Beis Din* would be compelled not to allow it to become *me'ubar*, and even having missed the moment of declaration, would be required to maintain it as a twenty-nine-day month. *Kamashmalan* — the *mishnah* informs us that no, the opportunity is lost and the month is *me'ubar*. The *mishnah*'s choice of words was thus dictated by the need to exclude that possibility, not necessarily to exclude retroactive sanctification on a later day.

### Retroactive Option

Rashi, however, derives from this textual choice a further conclusion: the reason the *mishnah* cannot say *yekadshuhu lemachar* is that such a formulation might have implied declaring the previous day as *Rosh Chodesh* retroactively, and the *mishnah* intends to rule that out. *Kamashmalan* — you cannot go back and retroactively sanctify an earlier day. Yet the Rambam, as will emerge from a later *mishnah*, actually rules that retroactive sanctification is possible — that on day thirty-one, *Beis Din* could retroactively sanctify day thirty. This represents an important dispute in how the *mishnah*'s language is understood, and the question is not definitively resolved in this *sugya*.

## Moon Images

### Gamliel's Aids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דְּמוּת צוּרוֹת לְבָנָה הָיוּ לוֹ לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּטַבְלָא וּבְכוֹתֶל בַּעֲלִיָּיתוֹ, שֶׁבָּהֶן מַרְאֶה אֶת הַהֶדְיוֹטוֹת, וְאוֹמֵר: הֲכָזֶה רָאִיתָ אוֹ כָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabban Gamliel had** a diagram of the different **forms of the moon** drawn **on a tablet** that hung **on the wall of his attic, which he would show to the laymen** who came to testify about the new moon but were unable to describe adequately what they had seen. **And he** would **say** to them: **Did you see** a form **like this or like this?**

The *gemara* now returns to the examination of witnesses, introducing a famous detail about Rabban Gamliel of Yavneh. *Demut tzurot levana hayu lo l'Rabban Gamliel b'tavla u'vekotel aliyato* — Rabban Gamliel had images of the moon's various phases on a tablet and on the wall of his upper chamber. *Shebahen mareh et hedyotot* — with these he would show the laypeople: *hakezeh ra'ita o kazeh*, did it look like this or like this? Much as an eye examination presents options to the patient, Rabban Gamliel used visual aids to clarify and verify the witnesses' testimony, since a picture communicates what words cannot easily convey.

### Lo Ta'asun Iti

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי״ — לֹא תַּעֲשׂוּן כִּדְמוּת שַׁמָּשַׁיי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And is it permitted** to create these types of forms? **Isn't it written: "You shall not make with Me** gods of silver, or gods of gold" (Exodus 20:20), which is interpreted as teaching: **You shall not make images of My attendants,** i.e., those celestial bodies that were created to serve God, including the sun and the moon?

The *gemara* immediately raises an objection: *u'mi shari*? Is this even permitted? The verse says *lo ta'asun iti* — you shall not make with Me — which the *gemara* interprets as *lo ta'asun kidmut shamshai*, do not make likenesses of those things that attend to God. Celestial bodies, as attendants of the divine, would appear to fall within this prohibition. How then could Rabban Gamliel maintain images of the moon?

### Abaye Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא שַׁמָּשִׁין שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לַעֲשׂוֹת כְּמוֹתָן, כִּדְתַנְיָא: לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם בַּיִת תַּבְנִית הֵיכָל, אַכְסַדְרָה תַּבְנִית אוּלָם, חָצֵר כְּנֶגֶד עֲזָרָה, שֻׁלְחָן כְּנֶגֶד שֻׁלְחָן, מְנוֹרָה כְּנֶגֶד מְנוֹרָה. אֲבָל עוֹשֶׂה
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye said: The Torah prohibited only** the images of those **attendants** with regard to **which it is possible to reproduce** an item in **their likeness.** Since it is impossible for anyone to reproduce the sun and the moon, the prohibition does not apply to these objects. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A person** may **not construct a house** in the exact **form of the Sanctuary,** nor **a portico in** the exact **form of the Entrance Hall** of the Sanctuary, nor **a courtyard corresponding to** the Temple **courtyard,** nor **a table corresponding to the table** in the Temple, nor **a candelabrum corresponding to the candelabrum** in the Temple. **However, one may fashion**

Abaye responds: *lo asra Torah ela shamashim she'efshar la'asot kemotan* — the Torah only prohibited making likenesses of those celestial attendants that are genuinely replicable by human hands. One can make a model of the moon, but one cannot actually produce the moon itself — the celestial luminaries are not truly replicable and thus do not fall under this prohibition. What, then, is prohibited? The *gemara* introduces a *baraita* that specifies.

## Prohibited Images

### Temple Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל חֲמִשָּׁה וְשֶׁל שִׁשָּׁה וְשֶׁל שְׁמוֹנָה. וְשֶׁל שִׁבְעָה — לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה, אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁל שְׁאָר מִינֵי מַתָּכוֹת. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף שֶׁל עֵץ לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁעָשׂוּ מַלְכֵי בֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי.
>
> **Translation:** a candelabrum **of five or of six or of eight** lamps. **But one may not fashion** a candelabrum with **seven** lamps **even** if he constructs it **from other kinds of metal** rather than gold, as in exigent circumstances the candelabrum in the Temple may be fashioned from other metals. **Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda says: Also, one may not fashion** a candelabrum **of wood, in the manner that the kings of the Hasmonean monarchy fashioned** it. When they first purified the Temple they had to prepare the candelabrum out of wood, as no other material was available. Since this candelabrum is fit for the Temple, it is prohibited to fashion one of this kind for oneself.

The *baraita* enumerates several categories of prohibited replicas. *Lo ya'aseh adam bayit tavnit heichal* — a person may not construct his house in the likeness of the *heichal*. He may not make his *achsadra*, his portico, in the form of the *ulam*, the entrance hall. He may not make his courtyard corresponding to the dimensions of the *azarah*. He may not make a table corresponding to the *shulchan* of the *Beit HaMikdash*, and he may not make a *menorah* corresponding to the *menorah* therein.

### Menorah Specifics

However, there is an important qualifier regarding the *menorah*. *Aval oseh shel chamisha v'shel shisha v'shel shemonah* — one may make a *menorah* of five, six, or eight branches. *Shel shiva lo ya'aseh* — a seven-branched *menorah* one may not make, even of other metals, since the seven-armed form itself is the prohibited shape, not merely a golden one. The question of what it means to "replicate" the *menorah* becomes more nuanced when one considers the position of *Tosafot*, who notes that the obligation for the *menorah*'s full ornamentation — the *gevi'im*, *kaftorim*, and *perachim* described in the Torah — applies only to a gold *menorah*. For any other material, such ornamentation is not required. This means the threshold for creating a prohibited "replica" is actually lower than one might think, since even a simple seven-armed structure without elaborate ornamentation could qualify.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָיה?! שַׁפּוּדִין שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל הָיוּ וְחִיפּוּם בְּבַעַץ. הֶעֱשִׁירוּ — עֲשָׂאוּם שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, חָזְרוּ הֶעֱשִׁירוּ — עֲשָׂאוּם שֶׁל זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** The other Sages **said to** Rabbi Yosei bar Yehuda: **From there** you seek to bring **a proof?** There the branches of the candelabrum **were** comprised of **spits [*shippudin*] of iron and they covered them with tin.** Later, when **they grew richer** and could afford a candelabrum of higher-quality material, **they fashioned them from silver.** When **they grew even richer, they fashioned them from gold.** Still, Abaye proves from this *baraita* that the prohibition against forming an image applies only to items that can be reconstructed in an accurate manner. Since this is not possible in the case of the moon, Rabban Gamliel's forms were permitted.

Rabbi Yossi the son of Rabbi Yehuda extends the prohibition further: *af shel eitz lo ya'aseh* — even a wooden *menorah* may not be made, *k'derech she'asu Beis Chashmonai* — in the manner of the Hasmoneans, who made a *menorah* from wooden spears when they recaptured the Temple and had no metal *menorah* available. This proves that a wooden *menorah* can qualify as a valid *menorah*. The sages respond to Rabbi Yossi: *misham ra'aya*? You cite that as a proof? *Shfudin shel barzel hayu* — those were actually iron spears, *vehifum b'va'atz* — covered with a dull metal alloy resembling lead. They were not truly wooden, and in any case were the best available substitute under emergency conditions. The *gemara* adds that as the Hasmoneans grew wealthier they replaced the wooden *menorah* with silver and eventually with gold.

The practical implications of this *baraita* are significant and contested. The question of whether Orthodox synagogues may have seven-armed *menorot* arises naturally. Some *acharonim* note that the prohibition is specifically against a replica functional for use as the *menorah* itself; others focus on the shape alone. The Temple Institute's claim that their *menorah* is not a replica because they intend to use it is a bold position. These questions are not resolved by the *gemara* but follow directly from it.

### Human Faces

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁמָּשִׁין שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לַעֲשׂוֹת כְּמוֹתָן מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי״ — לֹא תַּעֲשׂוּן כִּדְמוּת שַׁמָּשַׁיי הַמְשַׁמְּשִׁין לְפָנַי בַּמָּרוֹם! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא דְּמוּת אַרְבָּעָה פָּנִים בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is it** really **permitted** to form images of **those attendants** concerning **which it is impossible to reproduce their likeness? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that the verse: **"You shall not make with Me** gods of silver" (Exodus 20:19), comes to teach: **You shall not make images of My attendants that serve before Me on high.** Apparently, this includes the sun and the moon. **Abaye said:** This does not include the sun and the moon, as **the Torah prohibited only** the fashioning of **an image of** all **four faces** of the creatures of the Heavenly Chariot **together** (see Ezekiel, chapter 1). However, all other images, which are not the likeness of the ministering angels, are permitted.

The *gemara* returns to Abaye's initial limitation: the Torah only prohibits making things genuinely replicable and connected to God. The *baraita* being read identifies the prohibited categories as those of the Temple. But then a further challenge arises. The *gemara* cites another *baraita*: *kol hapartzufot mutarin chutz mipartzuf adam* — all forms are permissible except the human face. If Abaye's principle is that only what is truly replicable is prohibited, why should the human face be prohibited at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה פַּרְצוּף אָדָם לְחוֹדֵיהּ תִּשְׁתְּרֵי? אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: כׇּל הַפַּרְצוּפוֹת מוּתָּרִין חוּץ מִפַּרְצוּף אָדָם! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: מִפִּרְקֵיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי שְׁמִיעָא לִי: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי״ — לֹא תַּעֲשׂוּן אוֹתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **However, if** that is **so, let** the fashioning of an image of **a human face [*partzuf* ] alone be permitted. Why,** then, **is it taught** in a *baraita*: **All faces are permitted** for ornamental purposes, **except for the face of a person? Rav Huna, son of Rav Idi, said: From a lecture of Abaye I heard** that there is a different reason why one may not form an image of a human face, as the verse states: **"You shall not make with Me [*iti*]"** (Exodus 20:19). This can be read as: **You shall not make Me [*oti*].** Since man is created in the image of God, it is prohibited to form an image of a human being.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Idi reports: I heard from Abaye's teaching that a separate *derashah* covers this case. The same verse *lo ta'asun iti* is read as *lo ta'asun oti* — do not make Me. Why would making a human face constitute making God? Because of *tzelem Elokim* — the human being is created in God's image, and the human face is therefore a representation of God. This is a striking statement: the prohibition on making a human face is grounded not in the fear of idolatry toward another god, but in the recognition that the human visage itself embodies the divine image, and replicating it is therefore too close to attempting to represent God Himself.

One might then ask: if making a human face alone is already prohibited, why does the *gemara* need a separate prohibition against the four-faced angel, one of whose faces is a human face? *Tosafot* addresses this: even if someone else had already made the human face, one would still be prohibited from completing the other three faces around it. The prohibition on the four-faced figure is thus not entirely superfluous.

### Angels List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁאָר שַׁמָּשִׁין מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָא תַּנְיָא ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן אִתִּי״ — לֹא תַּעֲשׂוּן כִּדְמוּת שַׁמָּשַׁיי הַמְּשַׁמְּשִׁין לְפָנַי בַּמָּרוֹם, כְּגוֹן אוֹפַנִּים וּשְׂרָפִים וְחַיּוֹת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ וּמַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא אָסְרָה תּוֹרָה אֶלָּא שַׁמָּשִׁין שֶׁבַּמָּדוֹר הָעֶלְיוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And is it permitted** to form images of **other attendants? Isn't it taught** in another *baraita* that the verse: **"You shall not make with Me** gods of silver" (Exodus 20:19), teaches that **you shall not make images of My attendants that serve before Me on high, for example, *ofanim* and seraphim and the sacred *ḥayyot* and the ministering angels. Abaye said: The Torah prohibited only** those **attendants that are** found **in the upper Heaven,** i.e., the supreme angels in the highest firmament, but not the celestial bodies, e.g., the sun and the moon, despite the fact that they too are located in heaven.

The *baraita* extends further: *lo ta'asun kidmut shamashai hameshamshim lefanai b'marom* — do not make images of those that serve before Me in the celestial heights. The *gemara* enumerates: *ofanim, serafim, chayot hakodesh, malachei hasharet* — the celestial *ofanim*, the *serafim*, the *chayot hakodesh*, and the ministering angels. All images of this class of heavenly beings are prohibited.

Abaye responds: *lo asra Torah ela shamshai shebimador ha'elyon* — the Torah only prohibited the attendants of the highest celestial sphere. Those in the lower spheres, such as the planetary bodies, the sun, and the moon, are not included. The *gemara* then challenges even this: since when are the celestial bodies of the lower sphere permitted? A *baraita* cites the verse *asher bashamayim mimaal* — that which is in the heavens above — *lerabot chamah ulevanah kochavim umazalot*, to include the sun, moon, stars, and constellations; and *mimaal lerabot malachei hasharet*, the heavenly angels. The *gemara* responds: *ki tanya hahee l'ovdam* — that *baraita* speaks of making them for the purpose of worshipping them. If the purpose is worship, then obviously even a small worm is forbidden — as the verse continues to include everything: mountains, valleys, rivers, oceans, and even the tiniest creature.

So there are two distinct concerns operating in parallel throughout this discussion. The first, rooted in the *aseret hadibrot*, is the prohibition against making any image for the purpose of worshipping it — whether it be another god or a representation of God Himself. This is the *avodah zarah* concern in its most direct form, and it applies to literally everything. The second, rooted in *lo ta'asun iti*, is a prohibition against making things that are too closely connected to God even without any intent to worship — because such representations are an inappropriate embodiment of the divine. This second prohibition applies to: replicas of the Temple and its vessels; the human face, on account of *tzelem Elokim*; and all categories of celestial angels. The question that remains is whether the sun, moon, and stars fall under this second prohibition as well.

## Celestial Reps

### Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁבַּמָּדוֹר הַתַּחְתּוֹן מִי שְׁרֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״אֲשֶׁר בַּשָּׁמַיִם״ — לְרַבּוֹת חַמָּה וּלְבָנָה כּוֹכָבִים וּמַזָּלוֹת, ״מִמַּעַל״ — לְרַבּוֹת מַלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא — לְעׇבְדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **And is it permitted** to form images of **those** bodies found **in the lower heaven? Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: "You shall not make for yourself any graven image, nor any manner of likeness, of anything that is in heaven above, or that is in the earth beneath, or that is in the water under the earth" (Exodus 20:3). The phrase **"that is in heaven"** comes **to include** the **sun,** the **moon,** the **stars, and** the **constellations.** The term **"above"** serves **to include the ministering angels.** Apparently, it is prohibited to form an image even of the celestial bodies found in the lower Heaven. The Gemara answers: **When that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is in reference to the prohibition **against worshipping them.** However, there is no prohibition against forming an image in their likeness.

The *gemara* presses the question directly: *ve'asiya gredata misharay* — is making celestial bodies simply permitted, without any purpose of worship? A *baraita* states explicitly: *lo ta'asun kidmut shamashai hameshamshim lefanai, k'gon chamah ulevanah kochavim umazalot* — do not make likenesses of those that attend to Me, such as the sun, moon, stars, and constellations. How then could Rabban Gamliel have maintained such images?

### Gamliel Loophole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דַּאֲחֵרִים עָשׂוּ לוֹ. וְהָא רַב יְהוּדָה, דַּאֲחֵרִים עָשׂוּ לוֹ, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, סַמִּי עֵינֵיהּ דְּדֵין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes an alternative resolution: The case of **Rabban Gamliel is different, as others,** i.e., gentiles, **fashioned** those images **for him,** and it is prohibited only for a Jew to fashion such images; there is no prohibition against having them in one's possession. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** there is the case of **Rav Yehuda, as others fashioned for him** a seal in the form of a human being, **and Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda,** who was his student: **Sharp-witted one, blind this one's eyes,** i.e., disfigure the image, as it is prohibited even to have the image of a human being in one's possession.

The *gemara* answers: *shani Rabban Gamliel, de'acherim asu lo* — Rabban Gamliel is different because others made them for him. Rashi understands "others" as non-Jews. The prohibition is on making; owning an object made by someone else is a separate matter.

### Signet Ring

The *gemara* then raises a parallel case. Rav Yehuda had a signet ring that featured a human face, and it too had been made by others. Yet Shmuel said to him: *Rav Yehuda, shinina* — sharp one — *samei einei didei*, rub out its eyes. Even though someone else had made it, Rav Yehuda could not retain it in its complete form as a human image.

This seems to present a problem: if the prohibition is on making, and Rav Yehuda did not make it, why did he have to deface it? The answer introduces the rabbinic concern of *chashad* — suspicion. Even if one has not made a prohibited image, owning a three-dimensional human figure raises the concern that observers will suspect one of worshipping it.

### Signet Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, חוֹתָמוֹ בּוֹלֵט הֲוָה, וּמִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא. כִּדְתַנְיָא: טַבַּעַת, חוֹתָמוֹ בּוֹלֵט — אָסוּר לְהַנִּיחָהּ וּמוּתָּר לַחְתּוֹם בָּהּ. חוֹתָמוֹ שׁוֹקֵעַ — מוּתָּר לְהַנִּיחָהּ וְאָסוּר לַחְתּוֹם בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of Rav Yehuda, **his was a protruding seal,** i.e., the image projected from the ring, and Shmuel prohibited it **due to** the potential **suspicion** that he had an object of idol worship in his hand. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a ring,** if **its seal protrudes it is prohibited to place it** on one's finger, due to the suspicion of idol worship, **but it is permitted to seal** objects **with it.** In this case, the act of sealing creates an image that is sunken below the surface, which is not prohibited. However, if **its seal is sunken, it is permitted to place it** on one's finger, **but it is prohibited to seal** objects **with it,** as that creates a protruding image.

The *gemara* elaborates through a *baraita* distinguishing between protruding and indented seals. *Tabaat chotamo bolet* — if the signet ring's seal is protruding, three-dimensional — *assur lehanichah*, it is forbidden to wear, because the image stands out and people will suspect idolatrous intent; however, *mutar lachtom bah*, it may be used to make an impression, since the resulting impression will be indented. Conversely, *chotamo shokeia* — if the seal is indented — *mutar lehanichah*, one may wear it, since no three-dimensional image is displayed; but *assur lachtom bah*, one may not use it to make an impression, since doing so produces a protruding, three-dimensional image. The concern of *chashad* thus attaches specifically to the three-dimensional nature of a display, not to the mere ownership of the object.

## Context & Intent

### Public Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי חָיְישִׁינַן לַחֲשָׁדָא? וְהָא הָהִיא בֵּי כְנִישְׁתָּא דְּשַׁף וִיתֵיב בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא, דַּהֲוָה בֵּיהּ אִנְדְּרָטָא, וַהֲווֹ עָיְילִי רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל וַאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל וְלֵוִי וּמְצַלּוּ הָתָם, וְלָא חָיְישִׁי לַחֲשָׁדָא! רַבִּים שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And are we concerned about** arousing **suspicion** in a case of this kind? **But** what about that **certain synagogue that had been** destroyed in Eretz Yisrael and its stones were **relocated and** it was rebuilt so that it **sat in Neharde'a,** and **there was a statue [*andarta*]** of the king **in it. And** nevertheless **Rav and Shmuel and Shmuel's father and Levi would** all **enter and pray there and they were not concerned about** arousing **suspicion.** The Gemara answers: When **many** Jews are present it **is different,** as a large group is not suspected of having idolatrous intentions. Rather, it is assumed that the statue is there exclusively for purposes of ornamentation.

The *gemara* now asks: is the concern for *chashad* really so robust? In Nehardea there was a famous synagogue — *bei kenishta de-Shaf veYasiv* — which contained a statue, *andarta*. Some understand the name to mean that the congregation had cleared rubble and rebuilt, or even that stones from the *Beit HaMikdash* had been transported there and used in its construction. Despite the presence of this statue, Rav, Shmuel, Shmuel's father, and Levi all prayed there, *velo chaishi le-chashada* — with no concern about the appearance of suspicion. How can we say *chashad* is a serious concern if these great *amoraim* paid it no heed?

The *gemara* answers: *rabbim shani* — a public place is different. Where masses of people gather, no one suspects idolatrous intent, because a person who genuinely wished to worship an idol would not do so publicly. The *chashad* concern is reserved for private contexts.

### Gamliel as Nasi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל יָחִיד הוּא? כֵּיוָן דְּנָשִׂיא הוּא — שְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים גַּבֵּיהּ. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דִּפְרָקִים הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't Rabban Gamliel an individual?** According to this reasoning, his images of the moon should have been prohibited, as they would have aroused suspicion. The Gemara answers: **Since he is the *Nasi*,** the head of the Great Sanhedrin, **many** people **were** always **found with him,** and therefore there was no room for suspicion. The Gemara suggests an alternative answer: **If you wish, say** that these images were not whole; rather, they **were** formed **from pieces** of images that had to be put together. Only complete images are prohibited.

This resolution creates a new problem for Rabban Gamliel. His use of moon images was apparently in a private setting — he met with witnesses in his upper chamber. Why was he not subject to the concern of *chashad*? The *gemara* answers: *keivan de-nasi hu, shechichi rabbim gabei* — since he was the *Nasi*, his home functioned as a public forum. People streamed to him constantly, making his household effectively a public space even when the meeting was nominally private.

### Disassembled

Alternatively, the *gemara* offers: *de-prakim hava* — perhaps Rabban Gamliel's images were composed of separate components that were assembled and disassembled as needed. An object that exists only in assembled form for functional use, and is otherwise kept in pieces, does not carry the appearance of a cultic object and therefore raises no concern of *chashad*.

### Educational Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְהִתְלַמֵּד עֲבַד, וּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תִּלְמַד לַעֲשׂוֹת״ — אֲבָל אַתָּה לָמֵד לְהָבִין וּלְהוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests yet another answer: **If you wish, say:** Rabban Gamliel **did** this **to teach himself,** which is not prohibited, as **it is written: "You shall not learn to do** after the abominations of those nations" (Deuteronomy 18:9), which indicates: **However, you may learn to understand and to teach.** In other words, it is permitted to do certain things for the sake of Torah study which would otherwise be prohibited.

A third resolution: *le-hitlamad avid* — he did it for educational purposes, *dikhtiv lo tilmad la'asot* — as the verse teaches, do not learn how to do as the nations do, *aval ata lamed lehavin ulehorot* — but you may learn in order to understand and to teach. This principle, *lemaaseh*, that learning for educational understanding is distinct from learning to practice, may resolve either the biblical concern — that educational purpose removes the prohibition of making outright — or only the rabbinic concern of *chashad*, depending on how the *gemara*'s resolution is read. The text does not definitively adjudicate between these two possibilities.

## Modern Issues

### Modern Ownership

The concern of *chashad* and its application to ownership raises questions that extend well beyond antiquity. Rav Ovadia Yosef, addressing the question of why Jews may own busts and human figurines, notes that in Iraq and throughout the Sephardic world, wealthy Jewish families routinely commissioned portrait busts of themselves without any rabbinic objection. He argues more broadly that the *chashad* concern is contextual: if nobody would reasonably suspect that you are worshipping a G.I. Joe or a decorative bust, the rabbinic prohibition grounded in *chashad* may not apply in contemporary circumstances.

### Art Definition

What remains unresolved is the more fundamental question of what constitutes a prohibited image in the first place — particularly the distinction between two-dimensional and three-dimensional representations. *Tosafot* raises the possibility that because the sun and moon are perceived as two-dimensional, even a two-dimensional depiction of them might be prohibited. For the human face, by contrast, some argue that because the human form is three-dimensional in reality, a two-dimensional painting does not constitute a true representation and might therefore be permitted. The practical history is somewhat the reverse: the traditional community has been considerably more reluctant to depict human faces, even in two dimensions, than it has been to draw a picture of the sun. Yet as the *gemara* makes clear, a child's crayon drawing of the sun raises the same underlying question of *lo ta'asun iti*. The precise contours of what constitutes a prohibited representation — in terms of dimensionality, completeness, medium, and intent — remain genuinely open questions that the *sugya* surfaces but does not resolve.
