# Shevuot 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## God's Name & Oath Types

### Opening & Transition

We begin the fourth *perek* of Masechet Shevuot, moving on from the mechanics of how *shevuos* take effect — how one oath operates on top of another, what constitutes *sheker* versus *shav*, and related questions — to the use of a *shevua* in a legal context. The most immediate application is between litigants, but this *perek* also addresses the use of a *shevua* to compel testimony, functioning as something like a subpoena.

Before turning to the new *perek*, however, it is worth revisiting a point from the previous discussion concerning the *Ra'avad's* position on *shevuos* — a position that was not fully presented earlier due to the limitations of working without the *Ra'avad's* glosses in the app being used.

### Shevua vs. Neder

The fundamental distinction between a *shevua* and a *neder* is that a *shevua* involves an implicit or explicit invocation of God's name. This is rooted in two prohibitions: *lo tisa shem Hashem Elokecha lashav* and *lo tishavu bishmi lashaker*. This is precisely why there is a prohibition — and indeed *malkus* — for a *shvuas shav*, but not for a *neder shav*. A *neder shav* means an attempt to make something forbidden that simply did not take effect — no great harm done. But a *shvuas shav* means invoking God's name in vain, and that is inherently serious. The reason *shevuos* concern past events and specific actions is precisely because they all revolve around invoking God's name.

This raises the question: what if one did not invoke God's name at all, but simply said "I swear I will not do such and such"?

### Rambam & Ra'avad

The *Rambam* rules (*Hilchos Shevuos*, *perek* 2, *halacha* 4) that if someone takes an oath *velo hizkir shem velo kinuy* — without mentioning God's name or any divine epithet — *harei zeh assur bedavar shenishba alav*, the *shevua* is binding, but *ein lo malkus velo meivi korban*: there is neither *malkus* nor a *korban*. The practical consequence is thus entirely absent, and it may even be the case that such an oath is merely rabbinic in force.

The *Ra'avad* disagrees, at least partially. His disagreement is significant and worth reading carefully.

### Ra'avad's Two Components

The *Ra'avad* begins by noting that the *Rambam's* formulation may apply in certain contexts — specifically *ella be'edus uvifikadon*, in cases involving an oath of testimony or an oath of a *shomer* regarding the fate of an entrusted object. He notes that it is specifically in these contexts that we find the Torah's emphasis on God's name, and he marshals the relevant *Gemaras* in support.

But *aval bishvuas bituy* — when it comes to an oath about future actions — and even *shvuas shav*, everything studied until now — *afilu mifta o issur shvua*, even if one said *mifta* or *shevua* without invoking God's name, and *af al pi shelo amar lo shem velo kinuy*, even without stating God's name or any of its substitutes, the *Gemara* treats it as a full-fledged *shevua*. The *Ra'avad* enumerates additional formulations: *lav lav vehen hen* — saying "yes, yes" or "no, no" — which the *Gemara* recognizes as oath-language; *vechen yamin usmol*, invoking the right and left hand of the Creator by implication. In all these cases, the *Gemara* appears to treat the oath as fully binding.

The *Rambam* would agree that these are binding *shevuos*, but would maintain there is no *malkus* and no *korban*. The *Ra'avad*, however, argues that the *Gemara* treats them as real *shevuos* in every sense.

Yet the *Ra'avad* does not completely part ways with the *Rambam*. His final line is critical: *v'hani milei lekorban* — all of the above is in terms of the *korban* obligation and the prohibition of *bal yachal devaro*. One does not need the concept of *shem Hashem* to create an obligation, violate it, and thereby transgress *lo yachal devaro*. This is precisely how a *neder* works: a *neder* has nothing to do with *shem Hashem*, it creates a metaphysical reality about the object's status, and violating it constitutes *lo yachal devaro*. A *shevua* similarly creates a self-imposed obligation upon the person — independent of the invocation of God's name. Violating that self-imposed obligation is *lo yachal devaro*, and brings with it the obligation of a *korban*.

Where the *Ra'avad* does agree with the *Rambam* is regarding *malkus*. To incur lashes, one must transgress *lo tisa es shem Hashem Elokecha lashav* — the prohibition of *shvuas shav* — or *lo tishavu bishmi lashaker* — the prohibition of *shvuas sheker*. Both of these are explicitly tied to God's name. *Aval le-malkus be'inan shem, de-ksiv lo sisa es shem Hashem Elokecha lashav, ve-shvuas sheker nami lo tishavu bishmi lashaker*. Without God's name, there is no *malkus*. This is why *shvuas shav* incurs *malkus* while *neder shav* does not: the former involves God's name taken in vain, the latter does not.

The *Ra'avad* thus presents a two-component analysis of *shevua*. The first component is the invocation of God's name — this is what generates the prohibitions of *shvuas shav* and *shvuas sheker* and their attendant *malkus*. The second component is the creation of a self-imposed metaphysical obligation, analogous to a *neder*, which does not require God's name and which generates the obligation of a *korban* and the transgression of *lo yachal devaro* upon violation. Importantly, the *Ra'avad* adds that when it comes to *shvuas ha-eidus* specifically, he agrees with the *Rambam* — God's name is required there even for the *korban*.

With this understanding of *shevua* and the role of God's name firmly established, we turn to the new *perek* and its central subject: *shvuas ha-eidus*.

## Shevuas Ha-Eidus

### Parameters & Scope

The *Mishnah* opens: *shvuas ha-eidus noheges ba-anashim velo ve-nashim* — the oath of testimony applies to men and not women. This is because the *shvuas ha-eidus* in the *Torah* comes into play when a person is asked to come testify on someone's behalf and denies knowing any relevant testimony. It operates only in contexts where testimony would be legally relevant. Since women are not eligible witnesses in a *beis din*, the *shvuas ha-eidus* does not apply to them.

Likewise: *bi-rechokim velo bi-kerovim* — it applies only when one is adjuring someone who is not a relative; *bi-kesherim velo bi-pesulim* — only someone who is halachically qualified to testify, not, for example, one who has been disqualified as a *gazlan*; *ve-eino noheges ela be-re'uyin le-ha'id* — it applies only to those fit to testify. This final catch-all phrase adds something beyond what was already stated — presumably it would include a minor or someone not of sound mind, who may not have been captured by the earlier categories.

### Beit Din Requirement

The *Mishnah* then states: *bifnei beis din ve-shelo bifnei beis din*. The *Mishnah* is presenting *Rabbi Meir's* position: the *shvuas ha-eidus* applies even if the oath was administered outside of court. And *mi-pi atzmo* — even if the person imposed it upon himself outside of court — *Rabbi Meir* holds it would be binding.

As with the previous *perek*, the primary frame of reference throughout is the *korban* that the *Torah* prescribes for a false oath of testimony. The question of whether a person who acted *be-mazid* in such a case would also incur *malkus* — since this involves a *shvuas sheker* done willfully — is a point worth considering, but the basic framework of the *Mishnah* is the *korban* framework.

### No Subpoena Function

*Rabbi Meir* holds that a *shevua* outside of court is binding if the person imposed it upon himself. But if it was imposed by others: *u-vein mi-pi acherim, eino chayav ad she-yichpor be-veis din* — then it must be in court. One recalls from the previous discussion that if someone said *amen* outside of court, even in response to an oath imposed by another, *Rabbi Meir* would treat it as though one took the oath oneself. So *Rabbi Meir's* position is that a self-imposed oath outside of court creates a valid *shvuas ha-eidus*.

*Chachamim* disagree: *bein mipi atzmo uvein mipi acherim, einam chayavim ad sheyichperu b'veis din*. Regardless of who imposed the oath and where, liability only attaches when the denial takes place in court. The reasoning is that *shvuas ha-eidus* requires a context in which the testimony would be immediately relevant — where the witness is, as it were, on the stand and the testimony is needed right there. Outside of court, even if someone knows testimony, denying it represents a lesser degree of suppression. Yes, the person is still needed in court, but the denial is a step removed from the context in which it would be consequential.

This has an important practical implication: according to *Chachamim*, and to a significant degree even according to *Rabbi Meir*, the *shevua* cannot function as a subpoena. Imagine a litigant who says he borrowed money and has two witnesses, but one of them is reluctant to appear. The *beis din* must use whatever tools it has to bring that witness to court. The litigant cannot go to the witness outside of court, say *mashbi'acha ani*, and thereby legally compel him — as long as the witness is smart enough not to say *amen*, the oath has no binding force under *Rabbi Meir*, and the whole mechanism would fail to get the witness through the door in the first place.

### Liability Framework

The *Mishnah* continues: *ve-chayavin al zadon hashvua ve-al shiggasah im zadon ha-eidus*. This is a striking feature of *shvuas ha-eidus*: unlike most *korbanos* which require *v'ne'elam mimenu* — that the transgression occurred through some degree of inadvertence or unawareness — the *Torah* makes no such requirement here. A person who swears falsely that he does not know testimony, knowing full well what he is doing, still brings a *korban*. That is *zadon hashvua*.

Alternatively, there is the case of *shiggat hashvua im zadon ha-eidus*: the person knows he is lying about the testimony — *zadon ha-eidus* — but is *shogeg* with respect to the *shevua* itself, meaning he does not realize that this constitutes a prohibited oath or does not realize that a *korban* is associated with such an oath. That too triggers the obligation. One might ask: what would a simple *shogeg* in this context look like? The most obvious example would be that the person actually forgot the testimony and genuinely believed he was telling the truth. But that is not a false oath at all — we established earlier that if one believes one is telling the truth, no prohibition is violated regardless of the objective facts. So the *Mishnah* cannot offer that as its paradigmatic case of *shiggah*. The case it focuses on instead is: you know you are lying, but you do not know it is prohibited — or you know it is prohibited but do not know there is a *korban*. That constitutes *shegagah* with respect to the *shevua*, and still obligates a *korban*.

What one does not bring a *korban* for: *ein chayavin ela al shigegatah*, a full *shogeg* — meaning one who genuinely believed one was telling the truth.

For the *zadon* case: *mah hein chayavin al hazadon?* What does one bring when willfully lying? *Korban oleh veyored* — the sliding-scale *korban* calibrated to the person's means. Notably, there appears to be no *malkus* even in the fully willful case.

### Perjury vs. Denial

A useful comparison is to perjury in civil law: perjury requires intent to deceive — if one is simply mistaken, there is no crime. That maps remarkably well onto the *halachic* framework here, where liability for *shvuas ha-eidus* requires that one actually know the testimony and is consciously denying it.

One might also ask: what about giving false testimony? There is a distinction between *shvuas ha-eidus* — falsely claiming in the presence of a court, through an oath, that one does not know testimony — and actually taking the stand and testifying falsely. These are different acts. Notably, witnesses in a *halachic* court do not take an oath before testifying. If someone does get on the stand and testifies falsely, the applicable category is *eidim zomemin* — not *shvuas ha-eidus*. The question of whether false testimony per se carries *malkus* independently is a complex one discussed in Maseches Makkos, since establishing the falsity of testimony without the specific mechanism of *hazamah* is difficult.

As for the person who hires witnesses to testify falsely: the witnesses themselves may be subject to the laws of *eidim zomemin*, while the instigator — though *patur bidinei adam* — is *chayav bidei shamayim*. The *Rambam* goes so far as to call such a person a *rotzeach*. One who passively withholds testimony bears a lesser but still real degree of moral responsibility when his silence leads to an unjust outcome.

## Women & Witnesses

### Pasuk Interpretation

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei?* Of all the elements of the *Mishnah*, the *Gemara* chooses to focus first on *anashim velo nashim* — the exclusion of women. The *Gemara* cites a *drashah*: *ve-amdu shnei ha-anashim asher lahem hariv lifnei Hashem* — "and the two men who have the dispute shall stand before God." The plain reading would be that these are the two litigants, but the *Gemara* argues *be-eidim hakatuv medaber* — the verse is speaking of witnesses. And once we identify them as witnesses, the word *ha-anashim* — men — teaches that only men may serve as witnesses.

### Three Braitot

Three *Braitot* are presented, each working through a different derivation, reflecting an awareness that the initial argument can be challenged.

The first *Braita* reads the phrase as two parts: the second half, *asher lahem hariv*, describes the litigants, and the first half, *ve-amdu shnei ha-anashim*, must therefore describe the witnesses. Since witnesses are described as *ha-anashim*, testimony is restricted to men. The challenge: perhaps the entire verse — *ve-amdu shnei ha-anashim asher lahem hariv* — is one continuous description of the litigants, since there is no *vav* separating the clauses. If so, the phrase about "the men" cannot be separated out to refer to witnesses. In response: *ne'emar kan shnei vene'emar lehalan shnei* — there is a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *shnayim* here to *al pi shnei eidim yumat hamet*, where *shnei* explicitly accompanies witnesses. *Mah lehalan be-eidim af kan be-eidim* — just as *shnei* there means witnesses, so too *shnei* here refers to witnesses, and they must be men.

The second *Braita* offers a different approach. If the verse were about litigants, why would it say *two*? The number of litigants in a case is not fixed at two — there could be many parties. *Vechi shnayim ba'im ladin shlosha ein ba'im ladin?* Do only two come to court — can three not come? By contrast, witnesses have two as their fundamental number. True, one might argue that *conceptually* there are always two parties — the plaintiff and the defendant — even if each side is composed of many individuals. And one could say: *betove'a venitba hakatuv medaber* — the verse means the two fundamental roles. To resolve this, the *Braita* again falls back on the *gezeirah shavah* of *shnei*.

The third *Braita* takes yet another approach. *Amarta vechi anashim ba'im ladin nashim einam ba'im ladin?* If the word *anashim* is what teaches us to restrict to men, and if this verse is talking about litigants — does that mean women can never be litigants or defendants? Surely women have occasion to bring cases and to be sued. And there is a broader question: do we wish as a matter of law to exclude women from the ability to participate in court as parties? Obviously not. Therefore, the word *anashim* cannot be functioning as a legal restriction on the role of litigant — it must be describing witnesses, and from the witnesses the legal restriction flows.

### Tosafot's Question

*Tosafot* raises a penetrating question: even if we accept that the verse is describing witnesses rather than litigants, how do we know that the word *anashim* is functioning as a *legal exclusion* of women rather than a *descriptive* statement? Perhaps the *Torah* uses the word *anashim* because it is simply the normal case that witnesses are men — not because women are legally barred, but because *kol kvuda bat melech pnima*, the honor of the king's daughter is inward, women are more in the domestic sphere and less commonly found in public roles such as testifying in court. Just as the third *Braita* suggested that *anashim* may be descriptive when applied to litigants because women would typically avoid appearing in court, perhaps the same logic applies even to witnesses.

### Tosafot's Resolution

*Tosafot* resolves the question by drawing a critical distinction between the roles of witness and litigant. *Gabi eidim al korchach tzrichin lavo de-ein ed mipi ed kasher* — witnesses, if they have relevant testimony, are obligated to come to court and testify. There is no halachically valid substitute; hearsay testimony is not acceptable. A woman who witnessed something relevant cannot simply stay home and relay what she saw through her husband or any other intermediary. If she were an eligible witness, she would have no choice but to come.

A litigant, by contrast — at least on the side of the plaintiff — *yechola limsor ta'anoteha b'yad acher*, can empower a representative to argue her case. She can give power of attorney and have someone else present her claims in court. She need not appear herself.

Therefore: if the verse used the word *anashim* and the verse were describing litigants, one could explain *anashim* as merely descriptive — it is talking about the normal case where men appear in court, since women can and typically would choose to send a representative. But if the verse is describing witnesses, where women would be just as compelled to appear as men if they were eligible, then using the word *anashim* cannot be explained away as descriptive. It must be a legal term. *Tosafot's* conclusion is thus that the use of *anashim* in the context of witnesses establishes a genuine legal exclusion.

## Court Procedure

### Standing Requirement

Having established from the verse *ve-amdu shnei ha-anashim* that it refers to witnesses, the *Gemara* now turns to the *peshat* of the verse as applied to court procedure more broadly. *Mitzvah le-ba'alei dinin she-ya'amodu* — it is proper for litigants to stand. The judges sit and the litigants stand.

*Rabbi Yehuda* qualifies: *shamati she-im ratzu le-hoshiv et shneihem moshivin* — if the court wishes to have both litigants sit, that is permissible. There is no absolute technical requirement for standing. What is forbidden: *shelo yehei echad omed ve-echad yoshev* — one standing and one sitting. Similarly: *echad medaber kol tzorcho ve-echad omer lo katzer devarecha* — one litigant is allowed to speak at length while the other is told to cut his remarks short.

### Fair Treatment

The prohibition against differential treatment is derived not from *ve-amdu* itself but from a separate verse: *b'tzedek tishpot amitecha* — with righteousness shall you judge your fellow. The prohibition of one standing and one sitting, and one speaking freely while the other is rushed, is derived from this verse.

The word *b'tzedek* is worth pausing over, because it points to something subtler than mere formal equality. There is a difference between fairness and equality. If one litigant needs two hours to say what he needs to say and the other needs ten minutes, the court cannot cut the first one off after ten minutes in the name of equal treatment. The requirement is *tzedek* — justice — which means giving each person what they actually need, even if that results in different treatment. Equality of outcome is not always justice; sometimes justice requires accounting for different circumstances and different needs. This is the distinction between treating people equally and treating people fairly.

### L'Chaf Zechut

A second interpretation of the same verse: *davar acher, b'tzedek tishpot amitecha, havei dan et chavercha l'chaf zechut* — judge your fellow favorably. Here *amitecha* is read not in the judicial context but in the context of ordinary social interactions. Everyone judges others in daily life — the question is in which direction one tips that judgment. The instruction is *l'chaf zechut*: when you see something that could be interpreted critically or sympathetically, tip the scale toward the sympathetic interpretation, toward the side of merit. This is not a legal presumption of innocence in a court case; it is a social and moral directive about how to perceive other people's actions in everyday life.

### Emitecha & Preference

*Rav Yosef* offers a third reading: *b'tzedek tishpot amitecha* — *amitecha* means *im she'itcha b'Torah u'v'mitzvot*, one who is your fellow in Torah and *mitzvos*. The instruction *hishtadel l'dono yafe* — judge him well — is then explained by *Rashi*: if two cases come before you simultaneously, you may hear the case of a *talmid chacham* first.

This seems troubling at first — is this not favoritism? But *Rashi* and *Tosafot* limit the scope carefully: only if the two cases arrived at precisely the same time. If another case arrived first, a *talmid chacham* may not jump the line simply by virtue of his status. The principle applies only to a genuine tie, where there is no prior claim on the court's attention.

As an illustration: *Rav Ulla brei de-Rav Ilai* had a tort case before *Rav Nachman*. *Rav Yosef* sent word: *Ulla chaveirenu, amit batorah u'mitzvos* — Ulla is our colleague in Torah and *mitzvos*. *Rav Nachman* responded with some suspicion: *lama shalach li — lachanfeyei lei?* Why is he telling me this — just to flatter the litigant? He then reconsidered: the message must have been sent *lemishra d'kitrei* — to move Ulla along, to help expedite his case, presumably in a situation where Ulla's case and another had arrived simultaneously.

The *Gemara* adds a further dimension: *ai naami leshuda de-dayni* — or in a case that is entirely at the judge's discretion, with no factual basis for resolution, preferring a *talmid chacham* is legitimate. If, say, a gift was left in a will to "Reuven ben Shimon" and there are two such people in the city with no way of determining which was intended, one approach is to award it to the *talmid chacham*. This is a genuine form of preference — but it is confined to the narrow category of cases where judgment is truly indeterminate and discretion is entirely vested in the court.

## Witnesses Standing

### Standing Rules

*Rav Ulla* clarifies: *machloket be-ba'alei dinin* — the debate about whether litigants must stand applies to litigants. *Aval be-eidim, divrei hakol be-amida* — when it comes to witnesses, everyone agrees they must stand. This is derived from *ve-amdu shnei ha-anashim*, which, as established, refers to witnesses.

*Rav Huna* adds a further distinction: the debate about litigants applies *besha'at masa umatan* — during the deliberations. *Aval besha'at gmar din, divrei hakol dayanim biyishivah u'va'alei din be'amida* — when the verdict is actually delivered, everyone agrees that the judges sit and the litigants stand. This is derived from the verse describing Moshe: *vayeishev Moshe lishpot et ha-am vaya'amdu ha-am al Moshe* — Moshe sat and the people stood. A parallel formulation derives the same rule from the idea that witnesses are like *gmar din*: accepting testimony has a formal, almost ritual quality — *kabbolas eidus* — with a compelling and concrete effect on the proceedings. For that reason, witnesses must stand when testifying.

### Rav Huna's Widow

These principles create a practical dilemma illustrated by the following case. The widow of *Rav Huna* had a court case before *Rav Nachman*. *Rav Nachman* faced a genuine conflict: *heichi na'avid?* How should he handle her entrance? On one hand, *akum mikama* — he has a *halachic* obligation to rise in her presence. The *kavod ha-Torah* accorded to a *talmid chacham* extends even to his widow after his passing. On the other hand, *ei avidna hachi mistatmin ta'antei de-ba'al dina* — if he rises for her, the claims of her opposing litigant will be "stopped up." The other party will see that the judge honors one litigant and not the other, and however one explains the *halachic* basis for it, the psychological damage to that litigant's ability to present his case freely is real. And conversely, *lo akum mikama — eshet chaver harei hi ke-chaver* — he is not entitled to simply ignore the obligation to rise: the wife of a *chaver* — even the widow — is treated as a *chaver*.

### The Goose Ruse

*Rav Nachman's* solution was to instruct his attendant: *tzei ve-afrach alai bar avza* — go release a goose into the court. As the widow enters, the goose would fly at him, and in the commotion of dealing with the goose, he would naturally stand up — and thus fulfill his obligation to rise in her presence without the opposing litigant understanding that he had done so out of honor for her.

*Tosafot* raises a sharp question: the *halacha* of rising in honor requires a rising that communicates respect. A rising accompanied by a wild goose attack does not obviously communicate respect. *Tosafot* offers two resolutions. First: the widow herself understood the ruse perfectly — she knew he was honoring her — and the obligation of *kiyum kavod* was satisfied by her recognition of the intent, while the deception protected the opposing litigant from feeling disadvantaged. Second: the requirement to stand does not apply if one is already standing. The goose ruse caused *Rav Nachman* to rise *before* she entered, so that by the time she walked in he was already on his feet — technically not rising *for* her at all.

## Witnesses Standing…

### Half-Sitting Solution

A further complication: *Rav Huna* himself had ruled that at the time of *gmar din* the judges must sit and the litigants must stand. But *Rav Nachman* is obligated to rise for the widow, who is also a litigant in this very proceeding. How does he sit in judgment while also fulfilling his obligation to stand for her? The *Gemara* resolves this: *deyotiv keman de-shrei masanei* — he sits in a manner as if he were tying his shoes, half-risen from his chair, not fully seated. In that posture he both issues the ruling — making clear the judicial authority — and is not in a position of full dignified sitting. It is a compromise that attempts to honor both imperatives simultaneously.

## Kavod vs. Equality

### Voluntary Standing

*Rabbah bar Rav Huna* addresses the scenario of a *talmid chacham* and an *am ha'aretz* appearing together as opposing litigants. *Motvina lei le-tzurba me-rabbanan* — the court seats the *talmid chacham*. *Ve-la'am ha'aretz nami amrinan lei tiv* — the *am ha'aretz* is also told he may sit. The two are treated formally equally in this respect, though the manner differs slightly: the *talmid chacham* is actively seated by the court, while the *am ha'aretz* is merely invited to sit.

*Ve-i kai leit lan bah* — if the *am ha'aretz* chooses to stand nonetheless, presumably out of deference to his learned opponent, the court does not insist that he sit to enforce formal equality. One might have thought that the court should proactively enforce parity — if they are both supposed to sit, make them both sit. But the *halacha* is that the *am ha'aretz* may make this choice voluntarily, even though it places him in a psychologically weaker position.

### Forced Standing

A more troubling incident: *Rav bar Sharvaya* had a case before *Rav Pappa*. Both litigants had been seated. A court attendant arrived and struck the non-*talmid chacham* litigant, telling him he had no business sitting in the presence of a *talmid chacham*. *Ve-lo amar lei Rav Pappa tiv* — and *Rav Pappa* said nothing to counteract this. This is far worse than the voluntary case: the person is not choosing to stand out of deference; he is being physically compelled to stand by a court officer. *Heichi avid hachi?* How could *Rav Pappa* allow this?

*Rav Pappa* explained: the litigant could reason for himself that the judge had already given him permission to sit. When the court officer struck him, the litigant should understand that this reflected some personal grudge on the officer's part, not the court's position. The court officer, in other words, was acting on his own, not as an agent of the court's judgment. This seems to walk a fine line — a person standing while his opponent sits is in a disadvantaged position regardless of the explanation — but this is *Rav Pappa's* justification.

### Appearance of Bias

*Rabbah bar Avuha* raises an additional concern about the appearance of bias. *Hai tzurba me-rabbanan ve-am ha'aretz de-it lehu dina bahadi hadadei* — when a *talmid chacham* and an *am ha'aretz* have a case before a court, *lo lijkam tzurba me-rabbanan ve-leysev* — the *talmid chacham* should not go into the courtroom early and sit down. If he does, the opposing litigant will walk in and see him already settled there, perhaps exchanging pleasantries with the rabbinic judges, and the impression will be one of prior collusion and established social familiarity. *Mishum de-meichzei ke-man de-sader lei le-dinei* — it will look as if he had already been arranging matters in advance. This is an issue of *mar'it ayin* — the appearance of improper behavior — and it is significant enough to require the *talmid chacham* to stay out of the room before the proceeding begins.

This concern is not merely theoretical. There are documented situations in which a woman accompanying a party to a *beis din* for a *get* witnessed precisely this dynamic: the husband walks in, recognizes rabbinic acquaintances among the judges, and a wave of warm greetings and yeshiva reminiscences follows. Whatever the actual fairness of the proceeding, the other party's confidence in receiving equal treatment has been effectively undermined.

### Fixed Learning Time

There is an important exception: *ve-lo amaran ela de-lo kavia lei idnei* — the concern about entering the courtroom early applies only if the judge and the *talmid chacham* litigant do not have a fixed regular learning session together. *Aval kavia lei idnei, leis lan bah* — if they have an established *chavrusa* or regular class, the litigant's presence before the proceeding begins raises no concern, since his opponent can attribute their interaction to the ongoing learning relationship.

One might argue — and it is worth noting — that an ongoing *rebbe-talmid* relationship between a judge and a litigant raises its own serious questions about the ability of the judge to be impartial. But the *Gemara* does not pursue that concern here.

## Kavod & Sin Prevention

### Honor & Testimony

*Rabbah bar Avuha* raises yet another dimension: *hai tzurba me-rabbanan de-yada be-sahaduta* — a *talmid chacham* who knows relevant testimony, but *zila bei milta le-meizal le-vei dina de-zuta minei* — it would be beneath his dignity to appear before a court of judges who are his students or his students' students. *Lo leizil* — he need not go. His honor takes precedence, and he is not required to demean himself by presenting as a witness before judges of far lower status.

### Found Objects

*Rav Sheshet brei de-Rav Idi* cites a parallel: *matza sak u-kupa ve-ein darko litol harei zeh lo yitol* — if one finds an abandoned object in the street but it is beneath his dignity to bend down and pick it up — the test being whether he would bother to pick it up if it were his own — he is not obligated to take it in order to return it to its owner. Even though his inaction means the owner will not recover his property, one's personal dignity permits passive non-action even when it comes at a monetary cost to another. This is the principle of *kavod ha-briyot* — human dignity — taking precedence over certain monetary obligations where the individual's role is passive.

### Money vs. Sin

*V'hani mili b'mamona* — but all of the foregoing applies only in the monetary domain. *Aval b'issura* — when it comes to actual transgressions, *ein chochma v'ein tvuna v'ein etza l'neged Hashem* — there is no wisdom, no understanding, no calculation that can stand against God's honor. *Kol makom she-yesh chilul Hashem ein cholkin kavod le-rav* — wherever there is a *chilul Hashem*, honor is not accorded even to a great Torah scholar. The principle of dignity and honor yields entirely when God's honor is at stake.

### Rashi's Reading

What exactly is the case? One possibility is that the *talmid chacham* knows testimony about a capital matter, and his silence would mean that someone is wrongly convicted or wrongly acquitted. But *Rashi* reads the case differently: a *talmid chacham* is present in a judicial proceeding — perhaps as a court clerk — and his *rebbe*, who is the judge, is about to issue a ruling that would permit a woman to remarry when in fact her husband may still be alive. The *talmid chacham* knows this ruling is wrong. Despite the prohibition of contradicting or overriding one's *rebbe*, he must speak up. *Kavod ha-Torah* and deference to one's teacher do not apply when silence would lead to an *aveira* being committed.

### Ex Post vs. Ex Ante

There are thus three distinct scenarios nested within this discussion. *Rashi's* scenario — the most straightforward — involves preventing an *aveira* from being committed in real time: speak up now or an *aveira* occurs. A second scenario involves testimony that would exonerate someone from an undeserved punishment: here the motivation is protecting human life and bodily integrity, which is plainly compelling regardless of *kavod* considerations. The most complex scenario is the one most directly on point: must a *talmid chacham* lower himself to appear before inferior judges in order to ensure that a sinner receives his due punishment?

There is something qualitatively different about ex post punishment versus ex ante prevention. Preventing an *aveira* from occurring is an immediate and direct protection of God's honor. Ensuring that punishment is administered after the fact feels like a different order of claim — it is still about *kavod Hashem*, but at one remove. Whether the obligation to override one's personal dignity is equally strong in that case is the question with which this discussion concludes.
