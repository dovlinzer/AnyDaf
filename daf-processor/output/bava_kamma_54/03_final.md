# Bava Kamma 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vessel Exemption

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר וְכֵלָיו, וְנִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ כּוּ׳. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב עַל נִזְקֵי כֵלִים בְּבוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **an ox and its accoutrements,** i.e., the vessels it was carrying, **fell into** the pit **and** the vessels **were broken,** or if a donkey and its accoutrements fell in and the accoutrements were torn, the owner of the pit is liable for damage to the animal caused by the pit, but he is exempt from liability for damage caused to the vessels, by Torah edict. The Gemara notes: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda obligates** a person to pay **for damage** caused **to vessels in** cases of **Pit.**

The daf opens by returning to a principle that has been invoked since the beginning of the masechta but never fully analyzed: the exemption of a *bor* from liability for *keilim* damaged within it. The *mishna* states that if an ox and vessels fell into a well — *nafal l'tocho shor ve-kelav venishbaru* — the owner of the well is liable for the ox but exempt for the broken vessels. The *gemara* immediately notes that our *mishna* follows the *rabbanan* and not Rabbi Yehuda: *matnisin d'lo k'Rebbi Yehuda*. A *braita* specifies the point of dispute: Rabbi Yehuda holds the owner of the well liable for damage to *keilim* as well — *Rebbi Yehuda mechayev al nizkei kelim ba-bor*.

### Hermeneutics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָפַל שָׁמָּה שּׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר״; ״שׁוֹר״ – וְלֹא אָדָם, ״חֲמוֹר״ – וְלֹא כֵּלִים. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – ״אוֹ״, לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַכֵּלִים. וְרַבָּנַן – ״אוֹ״, מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְחַלֵּק. וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – לְחַלֵּק מִ״וְּנָפַל״ נָפְקָא. וְרַבָּנַן – ״וְנָפַל״, טוּבָא מַשְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the reasoning of the Rabbis? Since the verse states: "And an ox or a donkey fall therein"** (Exodus 21:33), it is inferred that it is specifically **an ox, but not a person,** for whose death the owner of the pit is liable. Moreover, one is liable for **a donkey, but not vessels.** By contrast, **Rabbi Yehuda** expounds the word **"or"** in the expression "an ox or a donkey" **to include vessels.** The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis** explain the word "or"? The word **"or"** is **necessary to separate** the cases of the ox and donkey. This indicates that if either falls in individually, the owner of the pit is liable. **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** derive the *halakha* **to separate** the cases in the verse? He derives it **from** the use of the singular form of the verb: **"And** an ox or donkey **fall [*venafal*],"** indicating that he is liable if even one animal falls. **And the Rabbis** maintain that the term **and fall** can also **indicate many** animals, as the use of the singular form of a verb does not prove that the subject is necessarily singular.

What is the *rabbanan*'s reasoning? The verse states *ve-nafal shammah shor o chamor* — an ox or a donkey falls therein. The *rabbanan* apply the hermeneutical principle: *shor v'lo adam, chamor v'lo kelim* — "ox" (excluding a person) and "donkey" (excluding vessels). Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, reads the word *o* — "or" — as coming to include *keilim*: *o l'rabot et ha-keilim*. In other words, the disjunctive "or" in the verse is not merely distinguishing between ox and donkey, but extending liability to things carried by the animal, namely vessels.

It is worth noting that Rabbi Yehuda's resistance to categorical exemptions in the *arba avot nezikin* is a broader pattern: in the next chapter on *esh*, he similarly rejects the exemption for *tamun* — objects that were concealed and thus not exposed — a position parallel to his rejection here of the *keilim* exemption.

The *rabbanan* respond that the word *o* is not available to perform that inclusion, because *o* is necessary for a different purpose: *l'chaleik* — to establish that the two animals, ox and donkey, are treated as independent cases. You need not have both an ox and a donkey fall in to incur liability; either one suffices. Rabbi Yehuda counters that this function could have been served by the word *ve-nafal* alone — written in the singular, the verse implies each animal independently — and so the *o* is free to include *keilim*. The *rabbanan* respond that *ve-nafal* written in the singular can nonetheless refer to multiple objects falling simultaneously, and Rashi provides the support: *kemo ve-ata Betzalel v'Oholiav... u-va ha-ot v'ha-mofet* — instances where the Torah uses a singular verb with multiple subjects. Therefore, without the word *o*, we would not know to read the verse disjunctively, and *o* is indispensable for that purpose, leaving nothing to include *keilim*.

### Rationale

Before proceeding to the *gemara*'s deeper analysis, it is worth pausing on the underlying logic of the *keilim* exemption. The exemption for *adam* — a person who falls into the well and dies — is in some ways not even a special *halakha* of *bor*. As Tosafot has noted from early in the masechta, there is a fundamental principle that civil payments are not assessed for the death of a human being; the verse *ha-meit yihyeh lo* speaks of property, not persons. Rabbi Yehuda does not dispute this aspect of the law.

The more genuinely puzzling exemption is that for *keilim*. One way to understand it is through a degree of causal indirectness: the well constitutes a direct hazard to anything moving autonomously through its vicinity — animals, people — but it is only an indirect hazard to vessels, which do not come to the well on their own; they must be brought there by an animal. The owner of the well has created a *michshol* for moving beings; the *keilim* are, so to speak, one step removed from the hazard. This is not to say there is some independent reason to be *chayav* that must be overcome — the Torah's exemption speaks for itself — but this framing at least gives some conceptual texture to why the *rabbanan* might have read the verse as excluding *keilim*.

### Klal u'Prat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא ״וְנָפַל״ – כָּלַל, ״שׁוֹר וַחֲמוֹר״ – פָּרַט; כְּלָל וּפְרָט – אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט; שׁוֹר וַחֲמוֹר – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא! אָמְרִי: ״בַּעַל הַבּוֹר יְשַׁלֵּם״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל. כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט; מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים, אַף כֹּל בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now challenges both opinions: **Say** that the term **"and fall"** is **a generalization,** whereas the phrase **"an ox or a donkey"** is **a detail.** According to the principles of halakhic exegesis, when the Torah presents **a generalization and a detail, the generalization** includes **only what** is contained **in the detail.** Therefore, **an ox and a donkey** are **indeed** included, but **anything else** is **not** included. The Sages **said** in response: The following verse: **"The owner of the pit shall pay"** (Exodus 21:34), means that **it then generalized** again. The verses are structured according to the principle of halakhic exegesis of: **A generalization, and a detail, and a generalization.** According to this principle, **you may deduce** that the verses are referring **only** to items that are **similar to the detail.** In this case, **just as the explicit detail** is referring to **animals, so too, all** items included in the generalization must be **animals.**

At the top of 54a, the *gemara* raises a prior question: before we even get to the debate over *keilim*, how do we know that the *bor* creates liability for anything beyond an ox and a donkey? One might read the verse as a *klal u'prat* — *ve-nafal* as a general term, *shor v'chamor* as the specific — and conclude: *ein ba-klal ela mah she-ba-prat*, only an ox and a donkey.

The *gemara* answers: *ba'al ha-bor yeshalem* — "the owner of the well shall pay" — constitutes a *chazarah v'chalal*, a return to the general. We thus have a *klal u'prat u'klal*: a general, a specific, and then a general again. The rule for such a hermeneutical structure is *klal u'prat u'klal ee ata dan ela k'ein ha-prat* — you include anything similar to the specific. Since the specific (*shor v'chamor*) is *ba'alei chayim* — living creatures — we include all living animals. This in itself explains the exclusion of *keilim* through a more familiar hermeneutical path, without requiring the technical readings of *shor v'lo adam, chamor v'lo kelim*.

But the *gemara* presses further: even within the *klal u'prat u'klal* framework, should we not limit liability to animals whose carcass conveys *tumah* by touching and carrying — *davar she-neveilatah metama b'maga u-v'masa* — since that is what *shor v'chamor* share? This would exclude birds, since the carcass of a bird does not convey *tumah* in the same way. The *gemara* then counters: if so, why write both *shor* and *chamor*? Had the Torah written only *shor*, one might have thought liability applies only to animals fit for sacrifice; had it written only *chamor*, one might have limited it to animals that have the sanctity of a firstborn (*kadosh bivchora*). The need to write both *shor* and *chamor* tells us we are not dealing with such narrow categories. Yet even granting this, birds remain potentially excluded. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the broadening phrase is *ha-meit yihyeh lo* — "the dead animal shall be his" — which includes any creature that can die. That is the foundational basis for liability.

### Breaking = Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי, מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָש, דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, אֲבָל עוֹפוֹת לֹא! אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא חַד פְּרָטָא! הֵי נִכְתּוֹב? אִי כְּתַב ״שׁוֹר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – אִין, שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֲמוֹר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קָדוֹשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה – אִין, שֶׁאֵין קָדוֹשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה – לָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – כֹּל דְּבַר מִיתָה. בֵּין לְרַבָּנַן דְּקָא מְמַעֲטִי לְהוּ לְכֵלִים, וּבֵין לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּקָא מְרַבֵּי לְהוּ לְכֵלִים – כֵּלִים בְּנֵי מִיתָה נִינְהוּ?! אָמְרִי: שְׁבִירָתָן זוֹ הִיא מִיתָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, just as the explicit detail,** i.e., the ox and donkey, is referring to **items whose carcass renders** a person ritually **impure by contact or by carrying, so too, all items,** i.e., all animals, **whose carcass renders** a person ritually **impure through contact or by carrying,** should be included in the *halakhot* of damage classified as Pit. **But birds,** whose carcasses do not render a person impure through contact or by carrying, would **not** be included. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the Merciful One write** only **one detail,** from which the exclusion of birds could be derived. The Gemara asks: **Which** detail **should the Torah write? If** it **writes** only **an ox, I would say** that an item that **is sacrificed on the altar,** such as an ox, **indeed** renders the owner of the pit liable, whereas an item **that is not sacrificed on the altar** does **not** render him liable. **And if the Merciful One writes** only **a donkey, I would say:** Those animals whose **firstborn** is **sanctified,** such as a donkey, as described in the Torah (Exodus 13:13), do **indeed** render their owners liable, but animals whose **firstborn** is **not sanctified** do **not** render them liable. Therefore, the Torah states both an ox and a donkey. **Rather,** the previous method of deriving liability for a pit must be rejected, and instead it should be derived as follows: The **verse states: "And the carcass shall be for him"** (Exodus 21:34), from which it may be inferred that **anything subject to death** is included. The Gemara asks: If this is the basis for liability for a pit, then **both according to the Rabbis, who exclude vessels** from liability based on another verse, **and according to Rabbi Yehuda, who has amplified** the *halakha* to include **vessels,** one could ask: **Are vessels subject to death?** Clearly, vessels cannot be subject to death. Why, then, is a separate source necessary to derive their halakhic status? The Sages **said** in reply: Vessels may also be considered subject to death in the sense that **their shattering is** tantamount to **their death.**

This leads to a pointed question: if *ha-meit yihyeh lo* is the ultimate source of liability — covering everything that dies — why did the *rabbanan* need a special *derashah* to exclude *keilim*? And why did Rabbi Yehuda need the word *o* to include them? *Keilim* do not die. They should be categorically excluded from *ha-meit yihyeh lo* without any additional derivation, and there would then be nothing for the word *o* to include.

The *gemara* answers: *shvirasam zo hi misasam* — the breaking of vessels is considered, in this legal framework, equivalent to their death. And this is consistent with what Rashi explains throughout: the exclusion of *shor v'lo adam* applies only to *mita* — death — since the operative phrase is *ha-meit yihyeh lo*. For injury to a person, one remains *chayav*, as injury is not covered by that exclusion. Vessels, however, are excluded even for damage short of total destruction, because any damage to a *keli* is conceptually its *mita*. Hence *keilim* fall within the scope of *ha-meit yihyeh lo* and do require active exclusion by the *rabbanan* and active inclusion by Rabbi Yehuda.

### Hevel Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַב דְּאָמַר: בּוֹר שֶׁחִיְּיבָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה – לְהֶבְלוֹ וְלֹא לַחֲבָטוֹ; בֵּין לְרַבָּנַן בֵּין לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – כֵּלִים בְּנֵי הַבְלָא נִינְהוּ?! אָמְרִי: בְּחַדְתֵי, דְּמִיפַּקְעִי מֵהַבְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to challenge this explanation: **But according to Rav, who says:** With regard to **Pit, for which the Torah deems** a person **liable,** it is only **for** damage caused by **its** lethal **fumes, but not for** damage caused by **the impact** of the fall, the following question may be raised **both according to the Rabbis and according to Rabbi Yehuda: Are vessels capable** of being broken by the lethal **fumes,** such that a verse is necessary to exclude them? The Sages **said** in reply: There is such a case concerning **new** vessels, **which crack from the fumes.**

A further complication arises from the position of Rav, who holds that the *bor* obligation established by the Torah is *l'hevelo v'lo l'chavato* — for the noxious air (*hevel*) within the well, not for the physical impact of striking the ground (*chavata*). According to Rav, the real hazard is inhalation of the bad air. But how could *keilim* be harmed by *hevel*? One cannot speak of a vessel inhaling toxic air. If *keilim* are simply not susceptible to *hevel*, they would have been automatically excluded without any need for a *derashah*, and there would have been nothing for Rabbi Yehuda's *o* to include.

The *gemara* answers: *b'chadti d'mifka'ei mehavla* — new, freshly-made vessels can actually shatter from the concentrated *hevel* within a deep well. New vessels, not yet hardened by use or time, are susceptible to bursting when exposed to the compressed noxious air. This is surprising — one might have expected old vessels to be more fragile — but the *gemara* apparently understands that newly fired pottery, for instance, retains a kind of internal sensitivity before it has fully set. This explains why the *keilim* exemption was necessary even according to Rav: new vessels genuinely could be affected by *hevel*, so without the *derashah* of *chamor v'lo keilim*, one might have been *chayav* for them.

### General Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: שׁוֹר פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין שֶׁנָּפַל לְבוֹר – פָּטוּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהַמֵּת יִהְיֶה לוֹ״ – בְּמִי שֶׁהַמֵּת שֶׁלּוֹ, יָצָא זֶה שֶׁאֵין הַמֵּת שֶׁלּוֹ. אֶלָּא אָמַר קְרָא: ״כֶּסֶף יָשִׁיב לִבְעָלָיו״ – לְרַבּוֹת כֹּל דְּאִית לֵיהּ בְּעָלִים. אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ כֵּלִים וְאָדָם נָמֵי! אָמַר קְרָא: ״שׁוֹר״ – וְלֹא אָדָם, ״חֲמוֹר״ – וְלֹא כֵּלִים. וּלְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּקָא מְרַבֵּי לְהוּ לְכֵלִים, בִּשְׁלָמָא ״שׁוֹר״ – מְמַעֵט בֵּיהּ אָדָם, אֶלָּא ״חֲמוֹר״ מַאי מְמַעֵט בֵּיהּ? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: ״חֲמוֹר״ דְּבוֹר לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְ״שֶׂה״ דַּאֲבֵידָה לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל – קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: But isn't **this** verse: **"And the carcass shall be for him," necessary for that which Rava** ruled, **as Rava says:** With regard to **a disqualified consecrated ox that fell into a pit,** the owner of the pit is **exempt, as it is stated: "And the carcass shall be for him."** It is referring **to** a person to **whom** the **carcass belongs,** thereby **excluding this** person, to **whom** the **carcass** does **not belong.** Once a *halakha* has already been derived from this verse, it cannot be used as the basis for deriving a second *halakha*. **Rather,** the *halakhot* of liability for Pit must be derived from another source. The **verse states** concerning Pit: **"He shall recompense money to its owners"** (Exodus 21:34), thereby **including any** item **that has an owner** within the scope of liability. The Gemara challenges this answer: **If so,** then **even vessels and people** should be included **as well,** which presents a difficulty for all opinions. The Gemara answers: This is why **the verse states: "An ox," but not a person; "a donkey," but not vessels.** The Gemara asks: **And according to Rabbi Yehuda, who amplifies** the scope of liability to include **vessels, granted** that the term **"an ox"** is necessary, as he **excludes people based on it,** for which he is not liable. **But** with regard to the term **"a donkey," what** does he **exclude based on it?** **Rather, Rava said:** The term **"donkey"** stated **with regard to Pit, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** the term **"sheep"** (Deuteronomy 22:1), stated **with regard to a lost** item, **according to** the opinion of **everyone** (see *Bava Metzia* 27a) are **difficult.** There is no explanation for why they are stated.

The *gemara* now determines which verse provides the comprehensive basis for liability. One candidate is *ha-meit yihyeh lo*, but Rava's teaching forecloses this: *shor psulei ha-mukdashin she-nafal l'bor pattur*, an animal that was consecrated as a sacrifice, became blemished, was redeemed, but whose carcass may not be used by the owner — for such an animal the owner of the *bor* is exempt, because *ha-meit yihyeh lo* applies only *l'mi she-ha-meit shelo*, where the carcass belongs to the owner. If *ha-meit yihyeh lo* is reserved for Rava's ruling, what is the source for the general principle of broad liability?

The answer is *kesef yashiv liv'alav* — "he shall pay money to its owners" — which is read inclusively: *l'rabot kol d'isa ba'olam*, to include anything in the world that has owners. This general verse establishes the scope of liability, and then *shor v'lo adam, chamor v'lo keilim* carves out the exemptions. For Rabbi Yehuda, the word *o* then restores *keilim* to liability, but it remains unclear what work the word *chamor* itself is doing in his reading. The *gemara* acknowledges the difficulty by raising a parallel: the word *seh* in the context of *aveidah* — lost property — appears similarly redundant given the phrase *al kol aveidah*. The *gemara* concludes: *kashya* — both difficulties stand unresolved.

## Animal Intelligence

### Mishna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – חַיָּיב. מַאי ״שׁוֹר חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן״? אִילֵימָא שׁוֹר שֶׁל חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹר שֶׁל שׁוֹטֶה, שׁוֹר שֶׁל קָטָן – הָא שׁוֹר שֶׁל פִּקֵּחַ פָּטוּר?!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If **a *ḥeresh* ox** or **a *shoteh*** ox **or a *katan*** ox **fell into** the pit, the owner of the pit is **liable.** The Gemara clarifies: **What** is meant by the phrase: **A *ḥeresh* ox** or **a *shoteh*** ox **or a *katan*** ox? **If we say** that it means: **An ox belonging to a deaf-mute [*ḥeresh*], an ox belonging to an imbecile [*shoteh*],** or **an ox belonging to a minor [*katan*],** then it can be inferred that if it was **an ox** belonging to a halakhically **competent** adult, the owner of the pit would be **exempt.** What would be the reason for this exemption?

The *mishna* teaches a seemingly peculiar rule: *nafal l'tocho shor cheresh shoteh v'katan chayav* — if a *cheresh* (deaf-mute), *shoteh* (imbecile), or *katan* (minor) ox fell in, the owner of the *bor* is liable. The *gemara* immediately challenges the formulation: what exactly is a "*shor cheresh shoteh v'katan*"?

If it means an ox *owned by* a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan*, the *gemara* asks: why would anyone think you would be *patur* for damaging the property of such owners? Their property is no less protected. And more startlingly, the *gemara* observes, does this imply that if the ox belonged to a person of full capacity — *shor shel pikeach* — you would be *exempt*? That seems counterintuitive to the point of being absurd.

### Animal Not Owner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא שׁוֹטֶה, שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Instead, it should be interpreted as: **An ox that is** impaired by being **deaf,** or **an ox that is an imbecile,** or **an ox that is** very **young.**

Rabbi Yochanan therefore explains: *shor she-hu cheresh, shor she-hu shoteh, shor she-hu katan* — the *mishna* is speaking not about who owns the ox, but about the ox itself, which displays the behavioral characteristics associated with a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan*. The animal itself is, in some sense, deficient in its capacity to perceive and navigate its environment.

### Braita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא פִּקֵּחַ – פָּטוּר? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר; לָא מִבַּעְיָא שׁוֹר שֶׁהוּא פִּקֵּחַ – דְּחַיָּיב; אֲבָל שׁוֹר חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, אֵימָא חֵרְשׁוּתוֹ גָּרְמָה לוֹ, קַטְנוּתוֹ גָּרְמָה לוֹ, וְלִיפְּטַר; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** can it be inferred that if **an ox that is of** standard **intelligence** fell into a pit, the owner of the pit would be **exempt?** **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** The mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary,** and should be understood as follows: **It is not necessary** to state that if **an ox that is of** standard **intelligence** fell into a pit, **that** the pit's owner is **liable. But** with regard to **an ox** that is **deaf, an imbecile, or** very **young, say** that it was not due to the pit alone that it fell in, but **its deafness caused it** to fall in or **its young age caused it** to fall in, **and** the owner of the pit **should be exempt.** Therefore, it **teaches us** that he is liable in these cases as well.

This explanation, however, immediately generates a difficulty from a *braita*: *nafal l'tocho bar da'at pattur* — if an intelligent being fell in, you are exempt. If *bar da'at* describes the animal itself, this would mean that an ox possessing full perception is *patur*. That is indeed striking: one of the primary cases of *bor* liability — an animal falling in — would be exempt when the animal is a fully functioning creature in daylight.

### Bar Da'at

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא לְרָבִינָא, וְהָתַנְיָא: נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ בַּר דַּעַת – פָּטוּר. מַאי, לָאו שׁוֹר בַּר דַּעַת? אָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, אָדָם. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אָדָם בֶּן דַּעַת הוּא דְּפָטוּר, הָא לָאו בֶּן דַּעַת הוּא – דְּחַיָּיב? ״שׁוֹר״ – וְלֹא אָדָם כְּתִיב! אֶלָּא מַאי ״בֶּן דַּעַת״ – מִין בֶּן דַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa said to Ravina: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If a living being that is **mentally competent fell into** a pit, the owner is **exempt. What, is it not** referring to **an ox** that is **mentally competent?** Ravina **said to him: No,** it is referring to **a person.** Rav Aḥa challenges this response: **If that is so,** you should then infer that it **is** only for the injuries of a **person** who is **mentally competent that** he is **exempt, but** if one is **not mentally competent,** is the *halakha* **that** he would be **liable?** But the term **"an ox" is written** in the Torah as causing liability, **and not a person.** Rava responded to Rav Aḥa: **Rather, what** does the term **mentally competent** mean? It is referring to **a species** that is **mentally competent,** and thereby includes all humans in the exemption from liability.

The *gemara* offers one resolution: *bar da'at* in the *braita* simply means a human being — *min bar da'at*, a species characterized by intelligence — and thus the *braita* is merely restating the established *halakha* that the owner of the *bor* is exempt for the death of a person who falls in. The term is categorical, not descriptive of the individual's mental state. This is confirmed by the verse *shor v'lo adam ketiv* — the exclusion of persons from *bor* liability is absolute, not dependent on whether any given person happened to be alert or mentally competent.

### Rava Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שׁוֹר וְהוּא חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹר וְהוּא שׁוֹטֶה, שׁוֹר וְהוּא קָטָן – דַּוְקָא; אֲבָל שׁוֹר וְהוּא פִּקֵּחַ – פָּטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּבָעֵי לֵיהּ עַיּוֹנֵי וּמֵיזַל. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: נָפַל לְתוֹכוֹ שׁוֹר חֵרֵשׁ, שׁוֹטֶה, וְקָטָן, וְסוֹמֵא, וּמְהַלֵּךְ בַּלַּיְלָה – חַיָּיב. פִּקֵּחַ וּמְהַלֵּךְ בַּיוֹם – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that Rabbi Yirmeya's explanation should be rejected, and the mishna is referring **specifically** to **an ox that is deaf, an ox that is an imbecile, and an ox that is** very **young,** for which he is liable; **but** for **an ox that is** of standard **intelligence,** he is **exempt. What is the reason?** The reason is **that** the ox **should have looked carefully** while **walking.** If it did not, it is responsible for its own injury. The Gemara notes: **That** idea **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to an **ox** that is **deaf, an imbecile, or** very **young, blind, or** that is **walking at night** and unable to see, if it **fell into** a pit, the pit's owner is **liable.** But if the ox is of standard **intelligence** and is **walking during the day,** the pit's owner is **exempt,** since normal behavior for oxen is that, ordinarily, they watch where they are going and avoid pits.

Rava, however, accepts the more radical reading directly. He rules: *shor ve-hu cheresh, shor ve-hu shoteh, shor ve-hu katan davka* — the *mishna* is precise: you are *chayav* only for an ox that was itself a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan*. But if the ox is a *pikeach* — a fully aware animal — you are *patur*. The reasoning is *d'ba'ei lei ayyunei u-mazal* — a normal, perceptive animal in an exposed, visible well should have seen the hazard and avoided it. The well was not, in legal terms, a hazard to such an animal. It only constitutes a genuine hazard to animals that lack the perceptual or developmental capacity to recognize and sidestep the danger.

This is a striking *gemara*. The owner of the *bor* creates a public hazard, and yet his liability for the paradigmatic case — a normal animal in daylight — is exempt. The law functions only at the edges: animals that cannot see, cannot perceive, or have not reached maturity are the ones whose fate is attributed to the *bor*.

### Examples

The *gemara* confirms this with a *braita*: *shor cheresh shoteh v'katan chayav* — plus a blind animal (*suma*) or one traveling at night — these are cases of *chiyuv*. But a *pikeach* traveling in the daytime — *patur*. These cases where the animal's natural capacities would have been adequate to detect and avoid an exposed well yield exemption.

### Rambam

The Rambam, apparently troubled by the growing narrowness of liability, limits the exemption to the case of death (*mita*). His reasoning: *mipnei she'derech ha-behema lirot velasur min ha-michsholim* — it is the nature of a normal animal to see and avoid obstacles. Only for death — where we presume the animal walked directly to its end despite full capacity to detect the danger — would we say the *bor* was insufficient a hazard to hold the owner accountable. But if the animal was merely injured, even a *pikeach* animal in daylight can be injured by a *bor*, and the owner remains liable: *ve'im huzak bah adam o ha-behema ha-pikchit chayav nezek shalem*. This also resolves the question about persons: an intelligent adult person injured by the *bor* — the owner is *chayav* for that injury, since injury does not require the same degree of reckless inattention as death would. The Rambam thus preserves a meaningful scope of liability: all injuries, and deaths of animals that were incapacitated in some way, while exempting only the death of a fully perceptive animal in conditions where it plainly should have seen the danger.

## All Animals

### All Animals Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶחָד שׁוֹר וְאֶחָד כׇּל בְּהֵמָה – לִנְפִילַת הַבּוֹר, וּלְהַפְרָשַׁת הַר סִינַי, וּלְתַשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְלַהֲשָׁבַת אֲבֵידָה, לִפְרִיקָה, לַחֲסִימָה, לַכִּלְאַיִם, וְלַשַּׁבָּת. וְכֵן חַיָּה וָעוֹף כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן. אִם כֵּן, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״שׁוֹר אוֹ חֲמוֹר״? אֶלָּא שֶׁדִּבֵּר הַכָּתוּב בַּהוֹוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The *halakha* is the same **whether** concerning **an ox or whether** concerning **any** other **animal with regard to** liability for **falling** into **a pit, and with regard to** keeping its **distance** from **Mount Sinai** at the time of the receiving of the Torah, when it was forbidden for any animal to ascend the mountain, **and with regard to the payment of double** the principal by a thief, **and with regard to** the mitzva of **returning a lost item, and with regard to unloading** its burden, and **with regard to** the prohibition of **muzzling** it while threshing, and **with regard to** the prohibition of **diverse kinds, and with regard to** the prohibition against its working on **Shabbat.** **And similarly, undomesticated animals and birds** are subject to the same *halakhot* **as** domesticated animals. **If so,** why **are** all of the above *halakhot* **stated** in the Torah only in reference to **an ox or a donkey? Rather,** the reason is **that the verse speaks of a common** scenario, from which the other cases may be derived.

The next *mishna* opens with the inclusive declaration: *echad shor ve'echad kol behema* — not only an ox but all animals are subject to the laws of the *bor*, as well as to *hafrashat Har Sinai*, *tashlumei kefel*, *hashavat aveidah*, *perika*, *chasimah*, *kilayim*, and *shabbat*. Wild animals (*chaya*) and birds (*of*) are included as well. One might have expected that wherever the Torah specifies *shor v'chamor*, the law is limited to those two species. The *mishna* teaches otherwise.

The *mishna* acknowledges the question directly: *im ken lama ne'emar shor v'chamor?* — if so, why does the Torah say "ox and donkey" in these contexts? The answer: *shediber ha-katuv be-hoveh* — the Torah speaks of common cases. The specification of ox and donkey is descriptive, not restrictive.

This is, however, in evident tension with the earlier *gemara*, which read *shor v'lo adam, chamor v'lo keilim* as active exclusions derived from those very words. The *mishna* here appears to treat the same words as merely illustrative. The *gemara* will need to work out how these two approaches coexist.

### Multiple Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לִנְפִילַת הַבּוֹר – ״כֶּסֶף יָשִׁיב לִבְעָלָיו״ כְּתִיב – כֹּל דְּאִית לֵיהּ בְּעָלִים, כְּדַאֲמַרַן. לְהַפְרָשַׁת הַר סִינַי – ״אִם בְּהֵמָה אִם אִישׁ לֹא יִחְיֶה״, וְחַיָּה בִּכְלַל בְּהֵמָה הָוְיָא; ״אִם״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָעוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains the sources for all of the *halakhot* enumerated in the mishna that apply to all animals. **With regard to falling** into **a pit,** it is **written: "He shall recompense money to its owners"** (Exodus 21:34), which means that **any** animal **that has owners** is included, **as we mentioned** (see 54a). **With regard to** the requirement that animals keep their **distance** from **Mount Sinai** before the giving of the Torah, this is derived from the verse: **"Whether it be animal or person, it shall not live"** (Exodus 19:13), **and an undomesticated animal [*ḥayya*] is** also **included in** the term **animal [*behema*].** In addition, the word **"whether"** serves **to include birds.**

The *gemara* now traces the individual sources for inclusion across the various laws. For the *bor*: *kesef yashiv liv'alav ketiv kol d'it lei be'alim* — "he shall pay money to its owners" is the general verse, as established at the end of the previous *sugya*, and it encompasses any creature or object with an owner.

For *hafrashat Har Sinai*: the verse says *im behema im ish lo yichyeh* — the word *behema* in its broad sense includes wild animals, and *im* serves to include birds. For *kilayim* in the sense of crossbreeding: *behemtecha lo tarbi'a kilayim* — the generic *behemtecha* encompasses all animals. For *kefel*, the double payment: *al kol devar pesha* — all matters of transgression — is naturally inclusive, and requires no special derivation for animals since the verse does not limit itself to living creatures at all. Similarly, *hashavat aveidah*: *lechol aveidat achicha* — all lost property of your fellow — is broadly inclusive, even beyond living creatures.

### Shabbat Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִפְרִיקָה – יָלֵיף ״חֲמוֹר״–״חֲמוֹר״ מִשַּׁבָּת. לַחֲסִימָה – יָלֵיף ״שׁוֹר״–״שׁוֹר״ מִשַּׁבָּת. לְכִלְאַיִם – אִי כִּלְאַיִם דַּחֲרִישָׁה, יָלֵיף ״שׁוֹר״–״שׁוֹר״ מִשַּׁבָּת; אִי כִּלְאַיִם דְּהַרְבָּעָה, יָלֵיף ״בְּהֶמְתְּךָ״–״בְּהֶמְתֶּךָ״ מִשַּׁבָּת. וְגַבֵּי שַׁבָּת מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to** the *halakha* of **unloading** a burden, **derive** it **from** a verbal analogy **from** the context of **Shabbat,** as the word **donkey** is employed in the context of unloading a burden (see Exodus 23:5), and the word **donkey** is employed in the context of the prohibition against having one's animal perform labor on Shabbat (see Deuteronomy 5:14), and that verse is clearly referring to any animal. **With regard to** the prohibition against **muzzling** an animal, mentioned in the verse: "You shall not muzzle an ox while it is threshing" (Deuteronomy 25:4), **derive** it **from** a verbal analogy **from** the context of **Shabbat,** as the word **"ox"** is employed in the context of muzzling, and the word **"ox"** is employed in the context of the prohibition against having one's animal perform labor on Shabbat (see Deuteronomy 5:14), and that verse is clearly referring to any animal. **With regard to** the prohibition of **diverse kinds, if** it is referring to the prohibition against using **diverse kinds for plowing,** due to which it is forbidden to plow with an ox and a donkey together (see Deuteronomy 22:10), **derive** it **from** a verbal analogy **from** the context of **Shabbat,** as the word **"ox"** is employed in the context of plowing with diverse kinds, and the word **"ox"** is employed in the context of the prohibition against having one's animal perform labor on Shabbat (see Deuteronomy 5:14), and that verse is clearly referring to any animal. **If** it is referring to the prohibition of **diverse kinds for crossbreeding, derive** it **from** a verbal analogy **from** the context of **Shabbat,** as the term **"your cattle"** is employed in the context of crossbreeding animals (see Leviticus 19:19), and the term **"your cattle"** is employed in the context of the prohibition against having one's animal perform labor on Shabbat (see Exodus 20:10), and that verse is clearly referring to any animal. The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **concerning Shabbat** itself that the words "ox" and "donkey" are referring to all types of animals?

For *perika* (unloading a collapsing animal) and *chasimah* (muzzling): the *gemara* derives these through a *gezerah shavah* from *shabbat*. *Perika* is stated with the word *chamor* — *ki tir'eh chamor achicha rovetz tachat mas'o* — and is linked to *shabbat* where *chamor* is shown to include all animals. *Chasimah* uses the word *shor* — *lo tachsom shor bedisho* — and is likewise linked to *shabbat* where *shor* includes all animals. Once both *shor* and *chamor* in the context of *shabbat* are read broadly, the same broadening applies wherever those words appear throughout the Torah.

### Klal u'Prat 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, בְּדִבְּרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת נֶאֱמַר: ״עַבְדְּךָ וַאֲמָתְךָ וּבְהֶמְתֶּךָ״, וּבְדִבְּרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנוֹת נֶאֱמַר: ״וְשׁוֹרְךָ וַחֲמֹרְךָ וְכׇל בְּהֶמְתֶּךָ״. וַהֲלֹא שׁוֹר וַחֲמוֹר בִּכְלַל כׇּל בְּהֵמָה הָיוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצְאוּ? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה שׁוֹר וַחֲמוֹר הָאָמוּר כָּאן – חַיָּה וָעוֹף כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן, אַף כֹּל חַיָּה וָעוֹף כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: In the first** version of the Ten **Commandments** it **is stated: "Your manservant, your maidservant, nor your cattle"** (Exodus 20:10), whereas **in the second** version of the Ten **Commandments** it **is stated: "Nor your ox, nor your donkey, nor any of your cattle"** (Deuteronomy 5:14). Now, **aren't an ox and a donkey** already **included in** the category of: **All animals,** which are included in the term "cattle"? **Why,** then, were they **specified? To teach you** that **just as** with regard to the terms **ox and donkey that are stated here, undomesticated animals and birds** have the same *halakha* **as them, so too,** everywhere that an ox and donkey are mentioned, **all** types of **undomesticated animals and birds** have the same *halakha* **as them.**

The source for this broad reading in the laws of *shabbat* itself comes from Rabbi Yose in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, who notes the contrast between the two sets of the *Aseres Ha-Dibros*. The first *dibros* mention *avdecha va'amatecha u'vehemtecha* — your servant, maidservant, and animal (general). The second *dibros* mention *ve-shorecha va'chamorecha ve-chol behemtecha* — your ox, your donkey, and all your animals (both specific and more broadly general). The question then arises: *vehalo shor v'chamor bi-khlal kol behema hayu* — ox and donkey were already included within "all your animals." Why were they singled out? The answer: *lomar lach mah shor v'chamor ha'amur kan chaya ve'of kayotzei vahen* — to teach that wherever the Torah specifies ox and donkey, we should understand it as including all living creatures, just as it does here.

But can we read the two *dibros* together as a *klal u'prat u'klal*? The *gemara* explores this possibility: *behema* of the first *dibros* as a *klal*, *shor v'chamor* of the second as a *prat*, and *ve-chol behemtecha* of the second as a return to the *klal*. The rule of *klal u'prat u'klal* would then extend liability to all creatures similar to the *prat*. The *gemara* again raises the challenge: perhaps limit it only to animals whose carcass conveys *tumah* — *davar she-nevilatam metame be-maga u-ve-masa* — which would exclude birds. And the response mirrors the earlier discussion: had the Torah written only *shor*, one might have limited rest on *shabbat* to animals eligible for sacrifice; had it written only *chamor*, one might have limited it to animals sanctified as firstborns. The pairing of *shor v'chamor* prevents both over-restrictions. Yet even so, birds might remain excluded under a *klal u'prat u'klal* analysis.

### Kol Inclusive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: ״בְּהֵמָה״ דְּדִבְּרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת – כָּלַל, ״שׁוֹרְךָ וַחֲמֹרְךָ״ דְּדִבְּרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנוֹת – פָּרַט; כְּלָל וּפְרָט – אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט; שׁוֹר וַחֲמוֹר – אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא! אָמְרִי: ״וְכׇל בְּהֶמְתֶּךָ״ דְּדִבְּרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנוֹת – חָזַר וְכָלַל. כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט; מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים, אַף כֹּל בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים. וְאֵימָא: מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁנְּבָלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא; אֲבָל עוֹפוֹת – לָא! אָמְרִי: אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא חַד פְּרָטָא! הֵי נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא? אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״שׁוֹר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – אִין, שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – לָא; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֲמוֹר״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֲמוֹר״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: קָדוֹשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה – אִין, שֶׁאֵין קָדוֹשׁ בִּבְכוֹרָה – לָא; כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״שׁוֹר״. אֶלָּא ״וְכׇל בְּהֶמְתֶּךָ״ – רִיבּוּיָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: But why not **say** that the verses be expounded as follows: The term **"cattle"** used **in the first** version of the Ten **Commandments** is **a generalization,** and the phrase **"your ox and your donkey"** used **in the latter** version of the Ten **Commandments** is **a detail.** In the case of **a generalization and a detail,** the principles of halakhic exegesis dictate that **the generalization includes only what** is specified **in the detail.** Therefore, with regard to this subject, **an ox and a donkey** should **indeed** be included, but **anything else** should **not** be included. The Sages **said** in reply: In the phrase **"nor any of your cattle"** stated **in the latter** version of the Ten **Commandments** following the phrase "your ox and your donkey," **it then generalized** again. Therefore, the verse is structured as **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization.** According to the principles of halakhic exegesis, **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail.** Consequently, **just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as animals, so too, all** items included in the general term must be **animals.** The Gemara challenges the answer: **But say** instead that **just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as an item,** i.e., an animal, **whose carcass renders** a person ritually **impure through contact or through carrying, so too, all items whose carcass renders** a person ritually **impure through contact or through carrying** should be included. **But birds,** whose carcasses do not render one impure through contact or through carrying, should **not** be included. The Sages **said** in reply: **If so, let the Merciful One write** only **one detail** from which this could be derived. The Gemara asks: **Which** detail **should** the **Merciful One write? If the Merciful One writes** only **an ox, I would say:** An animal that **is sacrificed on the altar,** such as an ox, **indeed** renders the owner of the pit liable, whereas an animal **that is not sacrificed on the altar** does **not** render him liable. **And if the Merciful One writes** only **a donkey, I would say:** An animal whose **firstborn** is **sanctified,** such as a donkey, as described in the Torah (Exodus 13:13), does **indeed** render him liable, but an animal whose **firstborn** is **not sanctified** does **not** render him liable. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes** both **an ox** and a donkey. Consequently, there is no redundancy, and each detail is required for correctly deriving other *halakhot*. If so, the previous question resurfaces: Why are the verses not expounded such that the prohibition does not include birds? **Rather,** the term: **"Nor any [*vekhol*] of your cattle,"** should be understood as **an amplification,** encompassing any item that is even partially similar to the detail.

Tosafot draws the distinction: *ba-kol* (as in the phrase used at *maasar sheni*) functions as a normal *klal*, while *ve-chol* — with the preceding *vav* — functions as a *ribuy*, an affirmatively inclusive term. This is the better reading of the *gemara*'s point: the *ve-chol* was not needed, since the *dibros rishonot* had already said *u'vehemtecha*; the extra *ve-chol* in the *dibros acharonot* is thus a *ribuy*, explicitly extending the category beyond what a *klal u'prat u'klal* would yield, to include birds as well.

The *gemara* puts it plainly: had the Torah wished merely to include domesticated and wild animals, it could have written *u'vehemtecha*, as in the first *dibros*. The choice of *ve-chol behemtecha* in the second *dibros* is a deliberate expansion. *Shema minah ribuyah* — learn from this that it is an inclusive formulation.

### Shor Chamor Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֹל הֵיכָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״כֹּל״ – רִיבּוּיָא הוּא?! וְהָא גַּבֵּי מַעֲשֵׂר דִּכְתִיב ״כֹּל״, וְקָא דָרְשִׁינַן לֵיהּ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט! דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְנָתַתָּה הַכֶּסֶף בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר תְּאַוֶּה נַפְשְׁךָ״ – כָּלַל, ״בַּבָּקָר וּבַצֹּאן וּבַיַּיִן וּבַשֵּׁכָר״ – פָּרַט, ״וּבְכֹל אֲשֶׁר תִּשְׁאׇלְךָ נַפְשֶׁךָ״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל; כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט, מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע, אַף כֹּל פְּרִי מִפְּרִי וְגִידּוּלֵי קַרְקַע! אָמְרִי: ״בְּכֹל״ – כְּלָלָא, ״כֹּל״ – רִיבּוּיָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: ״כֹּל״ נָמֵי כְּלָלָא הוּא, מִיהוּ הַאי ״כֹּל״ דְּהָכָא – רִיבּוּיָא הוּא; מִדַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִכְתַּב: ״וּבְהֶמְתֶּךָ״ כְּדִכְתִיב בְּדִבְּרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת, וּכְתַב: ״וְכׇל בְּהֶמְתְּךָ״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ רִיבּוּיָא. הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: ״כֹּל״ – רִיבּוּיָא הוּא; ״בְּהֶמְתֶּךָ״ דְּדִבְּרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת, וְ״שׁוֹר וַחֲמוֹר״ דְּדִבְּרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנוֹת – לְמָה לִי? אָמְרִי: ״שׁוֹר״ – לְאַגְמוֹרֵי ״שׁוֹר״–״שׁוֹר״ לַחֲסִימָה, ״חֲמוֹר״ – לְאַגְמוֹרֵי ״חֲמוֹר״–״חֲמוֹר״ לִפְרִיקָה, ״בְּהֶמְתֶּךָ״ – לְאַגְמוֹרֵי ״בְּהֶמְתֶּךָ״–״בְּהֶמְתְּךָ״ לְכִלְאַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** is it the case that **anywhere that the Merciful One states** the word **"any [*kol*]"** it **is** intended as an **amplification? But concerning tithes, where it is written "any [*kol*]," and** yet **we expound** the verses **with** the method of **a generalization and a detail,** rather than using the method of amplification and restriction. This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* concerning second-tithe money brought to Jerusalem: The verse states: **"And you shall give the money for anything [*bekhol*] your soul desires"** (Deuteronomy 14:26), which is **a generalization,** followed by: **"For oxen, or for sheep, or for wine, or for strong drink,"** which is **a detail,** and concludes with: **"Or for anything [*uvkhol*] your soul asks of you,"** where **it then generalized** again. Therefore, the verse is structured as **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization.** According to the principles of halakhic exegesis, **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail.** Consequently, **just as** the items mentioned in **the detail** are clearly **defined as produce of produce,** i.e., they grow from a parent organism, such as agricultural produce or animals, **and** they are **grown from the ground,** i.e., their sustenance comes from the ground, **so too,** it includes **all** things that are **the produce of produce and are grown from the ground.** It is evident from here that the method of a generalization and a detail is used in connection with the term "kol," as opposed to interpreting it as an amplification. The Sages **said** in response: A distinction may be drawn between the term: **"For anything [*bekhol*],"** which is **a generalization,** and the term **"any [*kol*],"** as in: "Any of your cattle," which is **an amplification.** And **if you wish, say** instead that the word **"any [*kol*]" is a generalization as well,** in addition to "for anything [*bekhol*]." **But this** word **"any,"** written **here, is an amplification.** This is the case **since it could have written: And your cattle, as it is written in** the **first** version of the Ten **Commandments, and** instead **it writes: "Nor any of your cattle." Learn from** this that it is **an amplification** and encompasses other animals as well. The Gemara continues to analyze this matter: **Now that you** have **said** that the term **"any [*kol*]" is an amplification, why do I** need the words **"your cattle,"** stated **in the first** version of the Ten **Commandments, and** the terms **"an ox or a donkey,"** stated **in the latter** version of the Ten **Commandments?** The phrase "and any of your cattle" already includes any animal. The Sages **said** in response: Each of these terms is required. The term **"ox"** can be used **to derive** the *halakha* **with regard to** the prohibition against **muzzling,** as the word **"ox"** is employed in the context of muzzling, and the word **"ox"** is employed in the context of the prohibition against having one's animal perform labor on Shabbat. The term **"donkey"** can be used **to derive** the *halakha* **with regard to** the *halakha* of **unloading** a burden, as the word **"donkey"** is employed in the context of unloading a burden (see Exodus 23:5), and the word **"donkey"** is employed in the context of the prohibition against having one's animal perform labor on Shabbat (see Deuteronomy 5:14). The term **"your cattle"** can be used **to derive** the *halakha* **with regard to** the prohibition of **diverse kinds,** as the term **"your cattle"** is employed in the context of crossbreeding animals (see Leviticus 19:19), and the term **"your cattle"** is employed in the context of the prohibition against having one's animal perform labor on Shabbat (see Exodus 20:10).

Given that *ve-chol behemtecha* already does the work of including all animals, the *gemara* asks: what then is the purpose of specifying *shor v'chamor* in the second *dibros*, since they were already included? The answer: each word carries a function beyond the *shabbat* context itself. *Shor* is specified in order to link the *shabbat* ruling to *chasimah* elsewhere in the Torah — teaching that "ox" in *lo tachsom shor bedisho* is not limited to an ox. *Chamor* is specified to link to *perika* — the obligation to unload a collapsing donkey is not limited to donkeys. *U'vehemtecha* of the *dibros rishonot* is specified to link to *kilayim* — the prohibition of crossbreeding applies not only to domesticated animals but to *chaya* and *of* as well. In each case, the specification enables a *gezerah shavah* to the corresponding law elsewhere, extending inclusion.

### Human Inclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ אָדָם לִיתְּסַר! אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: אָדָם מוּתָּר עִם כּוּלָּן לַחְרוֹשׁ וְלִמְשׁוֹךְ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: פַּפּוּנָאֵי יָדְעִי טַעְמָא דְּהָא מִילְּתָא – וּמַנּוּ? רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב; אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְמַעַן יָנוּחַ עַבְדְּךָ וַאֲמָתְךָ כָּמוֹךָ״ – לְהַנָּחָה הִקַּשְׁתִּיו, וְלֹא לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that this redundancy is the source for extending the prohibition of diverse kinds, then it **should be prohibited even** for **a person** to plow together with an ox or another animal. **Why,** then, **did we learn** in a mishna (*Kilayim* 8:6): It is **permitted** for **a person to plow and pull with any of them,** indicating that the prohibition relates only to animals? **Rav Pappa said:** The Sages of **Paphunya know the reason for this matter, and who** are these Sages? **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov,** who resided in Paphunya. The reason is that the **verse states:** "You shall not perform any labor, neither you, your son, your daughter, your slave, your maidservant, your ox, your donkey, any of your animals, or the stranger residing within your city gates, **that your manservant and your maidservant may rest as well as you"** (Deuteronomy 5:14), as if to say: **I have equated** people with animals only **concerning resting** on Shabbat, **but not with regard to another matter.**

This raises a sharp question: if *shabbat* links all the laws to all animals, and human beings are also included in the *shabbat* prohibition — indeed, *shabbat* says *lema'an yanuach avdecha va'amatecha ka-mocha*, and servants and laborers are explicitly mentioned alongside animals — then should human beings also be included in *kilayim*? Should it be forbidden for a person to plow together with an animal, since the same inclusionary logic that extends animal laws to all species should extend them to humans? The *mishna*, in fact, explicitly states the opposite: *adam mutar im kulam lachrosh v'limshoch* — a person may plow alongside any animal.

Rav Papa resolves this by citing Rav Acha bar Yaakov of Pappuna. The verse in *shabbat* states: *lema'an yanuach avdecha va'amatecha ka-mocha* — "so that your servant and maidservant may rest like you." Rav Acha bar Yaakov reads this precisely: *le'hanacha hikishtiv v'lo l'davar acher* — the Torah linked human beings to animals exclusively for the purpose of rest (*hanacha*). For all other purposes — *kilayim*, and by extension the other laws of the *mishna* — human beings remain categorically distinct from animals. The inclusion of human laborers in *shabbat* does not draw them into the broader framework of animal-related prohibitions.

## Tablets & Tet

### Tov Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאַל רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן עָגֵיל אֶת רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: מִפְּנֵי מָה בְּדִבְּרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת לֹא נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶם ״טוֹב״, וּבְדִבְּרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנוֹת נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶם ״טוֹב״?
>
> **Translation:** Having discussed some differences between the two versions of the Ten Commandments, the Gemara now discusses a related matter: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Agil asked Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: For what reason is** the word **good not stated in the first** version of the Ten **Commandments,** whereas **in the latter** version of the Ten **Commandments,** [the word good is stated there?]

A striking *aggadic* passage follows, occasioned — as Tosafot notes — by the *gemara*'s discussion of the two versions of the *Aseres Ha-Dibros*. There is indeed a second set of *luchos*, and the *shivre luchos* carries its own significance. The specific question posed here: *mipnei ma ba-dibros ha-rishonos lo ne'emar bahem tov u-va-dibros ha-acharonos ne'emar bahem tov*? Why does the word *tov* not appear in the first *dibros*, while it does appear in the second? The reference is to *Kibbud Av Va'em*, which in the second *dibros* contains the phrase *lema'an yetav lach*, but not in the first.

### Dismissal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: עַד שֶׁאַתָּה שׁוֹאֲלֵנִי לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶם ״טוֹב״, שְׁאָלֵנִי אִם נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״טוֹב״ אִם לָאו – שֶׁאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ אִם נֶאֱמַר בָּהֶן ״טוֹב״ אִם לָאו; כְּלָךְ אֵצֶל רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי, שֶׁהָיָה רָגִיל אֵצֶל רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי – שֶׁהָיָה בָּקִי בְּאַגָּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** in the context of the mitzva to honor one's parents, the word **good is stated there:** "In order that it shall be good for you" (Deuteronomy 5:16)? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: Before you ask me why** the word **good is stated, ask me if** the word **good is** actually **stated there or not, since** I am not sufficiently proficient in my knowledge of the biblical verses to remember the precise wording, and **I do not know if** the word **good is stated there or not. Go to Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai, who was** commonly **found at** the academy of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who was an expert in *aggada*.** Perhaps he heard something from him on this matter and can answer your question.

Rabbi Chanina's response to the questioner is itself memorable. Rather than answering, he says: before you ask me *why* it says *tov* in one place and not the other, ask me *whether* it says *tov* in the first *dibros* at all — *she'eini yodea im ne'emar bahem tov im lav*. I don't even know that! The point is not feigned ignorance but principled demarcation: this is *aggadata*, and that is not my field. Go to Rabbi Tanchum ben Chanilai — *she'ragil etzel Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, she'hayah baki ba'aggadah* — who spent time with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and is expert in *aggada*. The exchange is the mirror image of the famous case where Rabbi Akiva offered an *aggadic* explanation and was told to return to *Nega'im ve'Ohalos* — his proper domain is *halakha*, not *aggada*. Here the dynamic is reversed: a *halachic* authority declines to venture into *aggada* and directs the questioner to a specialist.

### Breaking Tablets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל לְגַבֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִמֶּנּוּ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי, אֶלָּא כָּךְ אָמַר לִי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחוּם אֲחִי אִמּוֹ שֶׁל רַב אַחָא בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אֲבִי אִמּוֹ שֶׁל רַב אַחַי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: הוֹאִיל וְסוֹפָן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר. וְכִי סוֹפָן לְהִשְׁתַּבֵּר מַאי הָוֵי? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם, פָּסְקָה טוֹבָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Ḥanina ben Agil **went to him** and asked him. Rabbi Tanḥum **said to him: I did not hear** anything on this matter **from** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **himself. But this** is what **Shmuel bar Naḥum, the brother of the mother of Rav Aḥa, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said to me, and some say** it was **the father of the mother of Rav Aḥai, son of Rabbi Ḥanina:** It does not mention the word good in the first tablets, **since they** were **ultimately** destined **to be broken** after the Jews made the Golden Calf. The Gemara asks: **And** even **if** it had mentioned the term good, and **they were ultimately** destined **to break, what of it? Rav Ashi said:** If this term had been mentioned in the first tablets, all **good** would have, **God forbid, ceased from Israel** once they were broken. Therefore, only the second version, which was written after the breaking of the tablets, contains the word good, so that there would always be good for the Jewish people.

The questioner proceeds to Rabbi Tanchum ben Chanilai, who reports not his own explanation but one he heard from Shmuel bar Nachum — described as a relative of Rav Acha the son of Rabbi Chanina, though the precise relationship is uncertain in the *gemara*'s transmission. The explanation: *ho'il ve-sofan lishhtaber* — since the first tablets were destined to be broken, it would not have been fitting for the word *tov* — "good" — to appear in them. The *gemara* challenges: *ve-chi mishhtaber mai havi?* So they were broken — what of it? Many momentous things were contained in the first tablets, including *Anochi Hashem Elokecha*, and their breakage was of cosmic significance.

Rav Ashi explains the concern: *chas ve-shalom paskah tovah mi-Yisrael* — God forbid that *tov* — goodness — should have been severed from Israel. The breakage of the other commandments, however tragic, was one thing. But had the word *tov* itself broken, it would have symbolized symbolically that good had ceased among Israel. That symbolic rupture would have been too severe a portent. So *tov* was held back from the first tablets, preserved for the second ones, where it could endure unbroken.

### Tet in Dreams

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הָרוֹאֶה טֵית בַּחֲלוֹמוֹ – סִימָן יָפֶה לוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״טוֹב״, אֵימָא: ״וְטֵאטֵאתִיהָ בְּמַטְאֲטֵא הַשְׁמֵד״! חַד טֵית קָאָמְרִינַן. אֵימָא: ״טֻמְאָתָהּ בְּשׁוּלֶיהָ״! טֵית בֵּית קָאָמְרִינַן. אֵימָא: ״טָבְעוּ בָּאָרֶץ שְׁעָרֶיהָ״! אֶלָּא הוֹאִיל וּפָתַח בּוֹ הַכָּתוּב לְטוֹבָה תְּחִילָּה – שֶׁמִּ״בְּרֵאשִׁית״ עַד ״וַיַּרְא אֱלֹהִים אֶת הָאוֹר״, לָא כְּתִיב טֵית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** If **one sees** the letter ***tet* in his dream,** it is **a good sign for him.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? If we say** that it is **because** the word **good [*tov*] is written** in the Torah and begins with the letter *tet*, then one could **say** instead that it is an allusion to the verse: **"And I will sweep it with the broom [*vetetetiha bemate'ateh*] of destruction"** (Isaiah 14:23), which also contains the letter *tet* several times but is referring to punishment. The Gemara answers: **We mean** that when someone sees **one *tet*** in his dream, it is a good sign, but this latter verse contains several. The Gemara asks further: According to this, **say** that it alludes to the verse: **"Her filthiness [*tumatah*] is in her skirts"** (Lamentations 1:9), which begins with the letter *tet*. The Gemara answers: **We mean** that when one sees the letter ***tet*** together with the letter ***bet*** in his dream, it is a good sign for him, as the word *tov* is written with both. The Gemara asks further: According to this, **say** that it alludes to the verse: **"Her gates are sunk [*tave'u*] into the ground"** (Lamentations 2:9), which begins with the letter *tet* followed by the letter *bet*. **Rather,** it is not merely because it is the first letter of the word good [*tov*] that it is considered a good omen. **Since the Torah initially introduces** the letter *tet* in a context **of good,** with the word good [*tov*] itself, it is a good omen. **As from** the word ***bereshit*,** the first word in the Torah, **until** the verse: **"And God saw that the light** was good [*tov*]" (Genesis 1:4), the letter ***tet* is not written** anywhere.

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi then teaches a related teaching: *ha-ro'eh tes be-chalomo siman yafeh lo* — one who sees the letter *tet* in a dream has a favorable omen. The *gemara* asks: is this simply because *tet* appears in the word *tov*? But surely there are many words beginning with *tet* that carry negative connotations — *ve-tet'ei'ah be-matatei ha-shmad* — "I will sweep them with the broom of destruction" — or *tum'atah be-shuleiha* — her defilement is on her skirt — both *pesukim* associated with destruction and *churban*. The *gemara* clarifies: seeing a single *tet* in a dream refers to the word *tov*, specifically; these other verses contain multiple instances of *tet*, which reads differently.

The deeper reason for the association: *ho'il u-patach bo ha-katuv le-tovah techilah* — the *tet* first appears in the Torah in a context of goodness. From the very beginning of *Bereishit* until *vayar Elokim es ha'or ki tov* — "and God saw the light, that it was good" — the letter *tet* does not appear in the Torah at all. Its inaugural appearance is in the word *tov*. Therefore, the letter *tet* is fundamentally associated with goodness; when it appears in a dream, the omen is favorable regardless of other possible associations.

### First Tet

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi also teaches a final teaching:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הָרוֹאֶה הֶסְפֵּד בַּחֲלוֹמוֹ – חָסוּ עָלָיו מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם, וּפְדָאוּהוּ. הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּכְתָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** If **one sees a eulogy [*hesped*] in his dream,** it is an allusion that **in Heaven they had pity [*ḥasu*] on him and saved him [*peda'uhu*]** from actually being eulogized. The Gemara notes: **This statement** applies specifically when he actually saw the word: Eulogy [*hesped*], **in writing.**

*ha-ro'eh hesped be-chalomo, chasu alav min ha-shamayim u-fduhu* — one who sees a eulogy in a dream was subject to a heavenly decree that was then commuted through divine compassion. This applies, according to Rashi, specifically when the word is seen written, as if on a page — *be-chtava*. The daf concludes here, with the parameters of *kilayim* to be resumed on the following day.
