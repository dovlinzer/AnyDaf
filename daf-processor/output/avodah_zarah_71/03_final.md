# Avodah Zarah 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wine & Non-Jews Intro

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּרְמִינְהִי: עִיר שֶׁכְּבָשׁוּהָ כַּרְקוֹם — כׇּל כֹּהֲנוֹת שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ פְּסוּלוֹת! אָמַר רַב מָרִי: לְנַסֵּךְ אֵין פְּנַאי, לִבְעוֹל יֵשׁ פְּנַאי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to the assumption that soldiers during wartime do not have time to commit transgressions from that which is taught in another mishna (*Ketubot* 27a): With regard to **a city that was conquered by** an army laying **siege, all the women** married to **priests** located **in** the city **are unfit** and forbidden to their husbands, due to the concern that they were raped. **Rav Mari** resolved the contradiction and **said: They do not have time to pour** wine for **libations,** as their passion for idolatry is not pressing at that time, but **they have time to engage in intercourse,** because their lust is great even during wartime.

Today's *daf* is 71a. The preceding *dappim* were occupied with the question of wine left unattended with a non-Jew: under what circumstances may one assume, with sufficient confidence, that the non-Jew did not touch the wine or otherwise handle it? Now, with the *mishna* at the top of 71a, the discussion returns to an earlier cluster of themes — when does wine become the property of the non-Jew, and when is it still considered the Jew's wine? Related to this is the question of at what stage one is deemed to have derived benefit (*hana'ah*) from *yayin nesech*. These are the central concerns of the current *daf*.

## Workers Refuse Wine

### Non-Jew Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוּמָּנִין שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁשָּׁלַח לָהֶם נׇכְרִי חָבִית שֶׁל יֵין נֶסֶךְ בִּשְׂכָרָן — מוּתָּר לוֹמַר: ״תֵּן לָנוּ אֶת דָּמֶיהָ״, מִשֶּׁנִּכְנְסָה לִרְשׁוּתָן — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **Jewish craftsmen to whom a gentile sent a barrel of wine** used for **a libation in** lieu of **their wage,** it is **permitted** for them **to say** to him: **Give us its monetary value** instead. But **once it has entered into their possession,** it is **prohibited** for them to say so, as that would be tantamount to selling the wine to the gentile and deriving benefit from it.

The *mishna* presents the following case: Jewish workers are employed by a non-Jewish owner. When the time comes to pay their wages, the employer has no cash on hand and offers them a barrel of wine instead. The *mishna* terms this *yayin nesech*, though as a practical matter, the non-Jew would not typically be setting aside wine dedicated to *avodah zarah*. Even *stam yeinam* — the ordinary wine of a non-Jew — is forbidden for benefit, and so the workers cannot simply accept this wine as payment.

## Wine & Non-Jews Intro…

### Chametz Tie-In

A notable feature of the upcoming analysis is how it connects to *chametz*-related discussions that have already appeared in this tractate. The question of how to define *bittul* — in particular, whether *chametz* is nullified like other prohibited substances or treated more stringently — has come up before, and the current *daf* will offer another such point of contact. Given that *Pesach* is imminent, this *chametz* tie-in is both appropriate and timely.

## Workers Refuse Wine…

### Request Cash

What are the workers permitted to do? The *mishna* rules: *mutar lomar lo ten lanu et dameha* — they may say to the employer, "Do not give us the wine; give us the cash equivalent instead." This is permitted because at the moment they make this request, the wine has not yet entered their possession. They are not selling *yayin nesech* or deriving benefit from it; they are simply declining the wine as payment and requesting an alternative.

### Possession Final

However, *mishe-nichnas le-reshutan assur* — once the wine has entered their possession, they may no longer reverse the arrangement by handing the wine back and demanding money in its place. At that point the wine is theirs, and returning it to the owner in exchange for cash constitutes selling *yayin nesech* and thereby deriving benefit from it. The rule is therefore straightforward: the request for cash must come before possession is established.

## Tax Payment via Non-Jew

### King's Tax Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מוּתָּר לָאָדָם לוֹמַר לְגוֹי: ״צֵא וְהָפֵס עָלַי מְנָת הַמֶּלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It is **permitted for a person to say to a gentile: Go and placate** the collectors of **the governmental tax** on wine **for me,** and I will reimburse you subsequently, even if he pays the tax with wine used for a libation.

The *Gemara* does not pause to comment on the *mishna* directly but instead addresses a parallel case. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: it is permitted for a person to say to a non-Jew, *tzei va-hafes alai menat ha-melech* — "Please go and pay the king's tax on my behalf." If the non-Jew then chooses to discharge this obligation using his own wine, that is the non-Jew's autonomous decision. The Jew never instructed him to pay with wine. The effect is simply that the non-Jew has covered the Jew's tax liability, and the Jew now owes the non-Jew the equivalent sum. The relationship between them is purely one of repaying a debt — the Jew is not using the *yayin nesech* in any meaningful sense.

This reasoning echoes an earlier discussion: if one tells a storekeeper to feed his workers and the storekeeper gives them wine, and then the storekeeper bills the Jew for the transaction, the Jew's only obligation is to pay the monetary debt. He did not direct the use of wine and is not considered to have derived benefit from it.

### Language Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אַל יֹאמַר אָדָם לְגוֹי ״עוּל תַּחְתַּי לָעוֹצֵר״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב: ״עוּל תַּחְתַּי לָעוֹצֵר״ קָאָמְרַתְּ? הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לְהָא, אֲבָל אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״מַלְּטֵנִי מִן הָעוֹצֵר״.
>
> **Translation:** One of the Sages **raised an objection** from a *baraita*: **A person may not say to a gentile: Go in my stead to the commissary [*la'otzer*]** to pay the wine tax for me, if he pays it in wine used for a libation. **Rav said to him: You say** that the case I am referring to is similar to one who says to a gentile: **Go in my stead to the commissary?** In that case, since he says: In my stead, whatever the gentile gives the commissary is considered as though the Jew gave it himself. **This** case that I am referring to **is comparable only to that** which is taught in the *baraita*: **But** the Jew **may say to** a gentile: **Save me from the commissary.**

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from a *braita*: *al yomar adam le-oved kochavim amod tachtai la-otzar* — one may not say to a non-Jew, "Stand in my stead" with respect to the tax official. Rashi explains *otzar* as a reference to a tax collector or similar officer. The question is obvious: why is this formulation forbidden? If, even in the permitted case, the non-Jew might pay with wine, why does the phrasing make a difference?

Rav responds: *amod tachtai la-otzar ka-amrat?* — note the language you used. "Stand in my stead" is not the same as "please pay my taxes." The language of standing in one's stead carries the connotation of being a *shaliach*, a representative acting in the Jew's place. When the Jew frames the instruction as "be my representative," and the non-Jew then pays with wine, it is as though the Jew himself chose to pay with wine. The wine belongs to the non-Jew, but the Jew has effectively deputized the non-Jew to act on his behalf, and thus the non-Jew's choice becomes, in a legal and conceptual sense, the Jew's choice. By contrast, the permitted formulation — *matzileni min ha-otzar*, "save me from the tax collector" — leaves the non-Jew full discretion, and his decision to use wine remains his own.

### Rashi Tosafot Debate

This is how Rashi reads the passage. Tosafot, however, is uncomfortable with that analysis. Tosafot argues that if the entire debt were a cash debt — if the Jew owed the king ten thousand dollars — then even the language of "stand in my stead" should not be problematic, because the non-Jew is ultimately paying off a monetary debt. Whether he uses cash or wine to discharge it, the Jew is left owing the non-Jew a monetary sum, not wine. Tosafot finds it difficult to say that a mere choice of phrasing transforms a straightforward debt-payment scenario into a case where the Jew is considered to have paid with wine.

### Tosafot Leniency

Tosafot therefore proposes a more substantive reading. The problematic case, says Tosafot, is one where the Jew actually owes *wine* to the king — perhaps because the tax is assessed as a percentage of his annual wine production. In such a case, if the Jew says "stand in my stead" to the non-Jew, and the non-Jew then pays the king with his own forbidden wine, it genuinely does look as though the Jew is discharging a wine debt using *yayin nesech*. The non-Jew is paying wine for wine, and the Jew is in effect using that forbidden wine for his own benefit.

And yet Tosafot goes further with a remarkable *chiddush*: even if the debt is a wine debt, and even if the non-Jew ultimately pays it with wine, it remains permitted — provided the Jew framed his request as *matzileni min ha-otzar*, "save me from my obligation." The Tosafot explains: that phrasing implies "do whatever you can to get me out of this — bribe the official, pay in cash, negotiate, or pay in wine if necessary." It leaves full discretion to the non-Jew and signals that the Jew is not specifically directing the payment of wine. That degree of distancing is sufficient to ensure that even if wine is ultimately paid, the Jew is not deemed to have paid with wine himself.

The contrast between Rashi and Tosafot here is significant. According to Rashi, the entire analysis involves a cash debt, and the only variable is the language used to delegate the task. According to Tosafot, the more striking case involves an actual wine debt, and even there, a subtle shift in framing — from "be my agent" to "save me from my obligation" — is enough to sever the legal and conceptual connection between the Jew and the forbidden wine used to discharge it.

## Selling Wine Details

### Fix Price First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר יֵינוֹ לְנׇכְרִי, פָּסַק עַד שֶׁלֹּא מָדַד — דָּמָיו מוּתָּרִין, מָדַד עַד שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַק — דָּמָיו אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a Jew **who sells his wine to a gentile,** if **he fixed** a price **before he measured** the wine into the gentile's vessel, deriving benefit from **the money** paid **for** the wine is **permitted.** It is not tantamount to selling wine used for a libation, as the gentile purchased the wine before it became forbidden, and the money already belonged to the Jew. But if the Jew **measured** the wine into the gentile's vessel, thereby rendering it forbidden, **before he fixed** a price, **the money** paid **for** the wine is **forbidden.**

A new *mishna* now addresses the case from the opposite direction: not the non-Jew's wine being offered to the Jew, but the Jew's wine being sold to the non-Jew. The concern here is that the non-Jew must not come into contact with the wine — or be in a position to do so — before the sale is finalized. Once the sale is complete and the wine belongs to the non-Jew, whatever he does with it is no longer the Jew's concern. The question is therefore: what finalizes the transaction?

The *mishna* states: *pasak ad she-lo madad damav mutarin* — if the price was fixed before the wine was measured out, the payment is permitted. The wine entered the non-Jew's possession after the transaction was already concluded. But the *mishna* does not mention any exchange of money. The simple reading is that *meshicha* — the physical act of drawing the item into one's possession — or, in this case, its entering the non-Jew's vessel, is what finalizes the sale; and the role of price-fixation is to ensure that there is genuine *gemar da'at*, a settled intention, at the moment possession transfers.

### Two Cases

The two cases of the *mishna* are as follows. First: *pasak ad she-lo madad damav mutarin* — if you fixed the price first and then measured the wine into his vessel, the money is permitted. The moment the wine enters the non-Jew's vessel, the sale is final; the non-Jew gains access only after ownership has transferred to him, so whatever he subsequently does to the wine is irrelevant — it is now his wine. Second: *madad ad she-lo pasak damav asurin* — if you first measured the wine into his vessel before fixing the price, the money is forbidden. At the moment the wine enters his vessel, the sale is not yet final; the wine is still yours, and it is now in a vessel that the non-Jew can access. Once it becomes *stam yeinam*, you cannot then finalize the sale, because you would be selling forbidden wine and receiving payment for it.

## Meshicha vs. Kesef

### Ameimar Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי קוֹנָה, תִּדַּע, דְּהָנֵי פָּרְסָאֵי מְשַׁדְּרִי פַּרְדָּשְׁנֵי לַהֲדָדֵי וְלָא הָדְרִי בְּהוּ. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ, מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי אֵינָהּ קוֹנָה, וְהַאי דְּלָא הָדְרִי בְּהוּ — דְּרָמוּת רוּחָא הוּא דִּנְקִיטָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ameimar says:** The legal act of **acquiring** an object **by pulling** it applies **to a gentile. Know** that it is so, **as those Persians send gifts [*pardashnei*] to one another and do not retract them,** which shows that they acquire one from another by pulling the object alone, even without paying for it. **Rav Ashi says: Actually, I will say to you** that **pulling** an object **does not acquire** it **in** a transaction involving **a gentile, and** the fact **that they do not retract their** gifts is not due to the *halakhot* of acquisition but **because they are taken over by haughtiness,** and they consider it shameful to retract a gift.

Ameimar draws a direct conclusion from the *mishna*: *meshicha be-oved kochavim koneh* — the act of *meshicha*, of the non-Jew drawing the item into his physical domain or taking physical control of it, effects a valid *kinyan* for a non-Jew, just as it would in transactions involving two Jews. This is the natural reading of the *mishna*, which makes no mention of monetary payment as the operative act of acquisition.

As a supporting observation, Ameimar notes that among Persians, the convention when exchanging goods or sending samples is that once the recipient takes physical possession, the transaction is not reversed. Rashi understands this as referring to cases where a sample is sent ahead of a larger delivery: once the buyer receives and accepts the sample, the sale is considered binding. Whether this is a formal legal norm or a matter of commercial etiquette, it at minimum reflects the understanding that physical taking of possession is the operative moment of finalization. Tosafot qualifies this by noting that the practices of Persians among themselves do not constitute a formal proof of what *halacha* recognizes, but the observation carries some weight as a supporting consideration.

### Rav Ashi Position

Rav Ashi disagrees. He asserts: *le-olam eima lach meshicha be-oved kochavim eino koneh* — I maintain that *meshicha* does not effect a *kinyan* for a non-Jew. As for the Persian practice of not reversing a transaction after physical possession is taken, Rav Ashi dismisses this as a matter of social convention or politeness — a form of *derech eretz* — rather than a legally operative norm.

The underlying debate here connects to the famous dispute in *Bava Metzia* between Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish regarding which act of *kinyan* the Torah itself prescribes for Jews. One position holds that according to the Torah, it is *kesef* — monetary payment — that effects acquisition; the Rabbis subsequently instituted *meshicha* for Jews and negated *kesef* for certain practical reasons. The other position holds the reverse. The relevance for non-Jews is as follows: whatever the Torah prescribes for Jews, the opposite applies to non-Jews. Thus if *kesef* is the Torah's *kinyan* for Jews, then *meshicha* is what works for non-Jews; and if *meshicha* is what the Torah prescribes for Jews, then *kesef* is the operative *kinyan* for non-Jews.

This debate has immediate practical implications, most notably for *mechirat chametz*, the sale of *chametz* to a non-Jew before *Pesach*. Because the *maskana* of this *Gemara* is not entirely clear, the practice emerged of performing every conceivable form of *kinyan* — *kesef*, *meshicha*, *chalipin*, *chatzer*, *dina demalchuta dina*, and more — in order to cover all possibilities.

Tosafot in this *sugyah* takes up the question of what our *psak halacha* should be with respect to sales to non-Jews, focusing primarily on the case of selling an interest in an animal to a non-Jew in order to exempt its offspring from the laws of *bechor*. The mechanism is that if the non-Jew holds even partial ownership of the mother at the time of birth, the *halacha* of *bechor* does not apply to the offspring. To accomplish this, Tosafot rules that one should ideally have the non-Jew both pay money (*kesef*) and perform an act of *meshicha* — and *meshicha* must be done either in a *simta* (a semi-public side alley that does not constitute a full public domain) or within the non-Jew's own domain. If the non-Jew has no domain of his own, Tosafot suggests a creative solution: first sell the non-Jew a room in the Jew's house, complete with its own *kinyan*, and then have the non-Jew draw the animal into that room. That establishes both the domain and the *meshicha* in a single sequence.

As for how much of the animal the non-Jew must own, Tosafot cites a debate among *amoraim* in the first chapter of *Bechorot*: some hold the non-Jew must own a portion significant enough that its removal would render the animal a *tereifa*; others hold even the ear suffices. Tosafot leans toward the more stringent position, recommending that one sell the non-Jew something like the lungs or the head. And as for the price: the non-Jew need not pay the full market value of what he is acquiring. Paying a *perutah* is sufficient, even though the portion of the animal is worth many times that amount, because — as Tosafot explains — everyone understands that the Jew's true intention is to transfer ownership for the purpose of exempting the offspring from *bechor*, and that sincere intention (*gemar ve-aknei*) supports the validity of the sale despite the token price. This is, of course, directly parallel to the structure of *mechirat chametz*, where similarly symbolic payment is combined with multiple forms of *kinyan*.

### Rav's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? מִדְּאָמַר לְהוּ רַב לְהָנְהוּ סָבוֹיָתָא: כִּי כָיְילִיתוּ חַמְרָא לְגוֹיִם, שִׁקְלוּ זוּזֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ וַהֲדַר (כָּיְילָן) [כַּיְילוּ] לְהוּ, וְאִי לָא נְקִיטוּ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ זוּזֵי, אוּזִיפוּנְהוּ וַהֲדַר שְׁקִילוּ מִינַּיְיהוּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתִיהְוֵי הַלְוָאָה גַּבַּיְיהוּ, דְּאִי לָא עָבְדִיתוּ הָכִי, כִּי קָא הָוֵי יֵין נֶסֶךְ — בִּרְשׁוּתַיְיכוּ קָא הָוֵי, וְכִי שָׁקְילִתוּ דְּמֵי יֵין נֶסֶךְ קָא שָׁקְילִתוּ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי קוֹנָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: From where do I say that** acquisition by pulling does not apply to gentiles? It is **from** that **which Rav said to certain wine shopkeepers: When you measure wine for gentiles, take** the **dinars from them and then measure** the wine **for them. And if they do not have dinars with them** readily available, **lend them** dinars **and then take** those dinars back **from them, so that it will be a loan** provided **to them** that they are repaying. **As if you do not do so, when it becomes wine** used for **a libation it becomes** so **in your possession, and when you take** the money it will be **payment for wine** used for **a libation** that **you are taking.** Rav Ashi concludes his proof for his opinion: **And if it enters your mind** that **pulling** an object **acquires** it **in** a transaction involving **a gentile,**

Rav Ashi now seeks to ground his position — that *kesef*, not *meshicha*, is the operative *kinyan* for non-Jews — in a teaching of Rav. Rav addressed wine merchants and instructed them: when you measure out wine for non-Jews, *shaklu zuzei mineihu ve-hadar haylu lehu* — first collect payment, and only then measure out the wine. If they do not have cash on hand, lend them the money and then take it back from them as payment, so that the purchase is actually transacted with cash before the wine changes hands.

The rationale Rav provides is explicit: *ki ka havei yayin nesech bi-reshuteichu ka havei* — if you measure out the wine before receiving payment, then at the moment the non-Jew's vessel receives the wine, the sale is not yet final, the wine is still yours, and it becomes *yayin nesech* while in your possession. Then, *vechi sheklitu dmei yayin nesech ka sheklitu* — when you subsequently take the money, you are taking payment for *yayin nesech*, which is forbidden.

The emphasis on receiving money upfront appears to support Rav Ashi's position that *kesef* is the operative *kinyan* for non-Jews. If *meshicha* were sufficient, one might argue there should be no problem: from the moment the wine enters the non-Jew's vessel, it is his, and he does not gain physical access to it until a moment later, by which time the sale is already complete. So why does Rav insist on payment upfront?

### Forbidden Drops

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּמַשְׁכֵהּ גּוֹי קַנְיֵיהּ, יֵין נֶסֶךְ לָא הָוֵי עַד דְּנָגַע בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **from** the moment **that he pulled it,** the **gentile acquired it,** whereas **it did not become wine** used for **a libation until he touched it.** Therefore, the seller can receive payment for the wine he sold, because at the time of the acquisition the wine was permitted.

The *Gemara* offers an answer that attempts to preserve the possibility that *meshicha* also works, while still explaining Rav's instruction. The answer focuses on the physical mechanics of pouring wine into a vessel. We are not dealing with the case of pouring into a Jewish flask; the problem arises specifically when the wine is being poured into the non-Jew's own vessel. And here there is a complication: at the bottom of the non-Jew's vessel, there are residual drops of wine — *stam yeinam* — left over from prior use. The concern is that as the Jew's kosher wine is poured in, it mixes with those drops before the sale is finalized.

*Yayin nesech* is *asur be-mashehu* — even the smallest trace renders the mixture forbidden. So at the very moment the wine enters the vessel and mingles with those drops, it becomes *yayin nesech*, and this happens a millisecond before — or simultaneously with — any act of *meshicha* that might vest ownership in the non-Jew. The result is that the wine becomes forbidden while it is still the Jew's, and the subsequent "sale" is a sale of *yayin nesech*.

This is why Rav insisted on payment upfront: cash paid in advance means the non-Jew already owns the wine before any pouring takes place, and thus whatever happens when it enters his vessel is happening to his wine, not the Jew's.

### Vessel on Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּקָא כָיֵיל וְרָמֵי לְמָנָא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, הָכִי נָמֵי. לָא צְרִיכָא דְּקָא כָיֵיל וְרָמֵי לְמָנָא דְּגוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **If** it is a case **where** the seller **measures** the wine **and pours** it **into the vessel of a Jew,** it is **indeed** permitted to do so without receiving payment first. Rav's ruling **is not necessary** except in a case **where he measures** the wine **and pours** it **into the vessel of** the **gentile,** which contains wine used for a libation, and the wine is rendered forbidden upon contact with the vessel even before the gentile acquires it by pulling it.

The *Gemara* presses further. Even granting the drops of wine at the bottom, why does the non-Jew not take possession the moment the wine enters the airspace of his vessel? The principle of *kinyan kli* — acquisition through one's vessel — should operate from the point at which the wine enters the vessel's airspace. At that moment the non-Jew would own it, and the wine would only reach the forbidden drops at the very bottom a fraction later.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, כִּי מְטָא לְאַוֵּירָא דְּמָנָא קַנְיֵיהּ, יֵין נֶסֶךְ לָא הָוֵי עַד דְּמָטֵי לְאַרְעִיתֵיהּ דְּמָנָא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ נִצּוֹק חִבּוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **Ultimately,** even in this case, **when** the wine **reaches the** interior **airspace of the** gentile's **vessel, he acquires it,** as this too is a mode of acquisition. And **it does not become wine** used for **a libation until it reaches the bottom of the vessel,** making contact with it, so the acquisition occurs before the wine becomes forbidden. Can one **conclude from** this that in the opinion of Rav **a stream** of liquid serves as **a connection** between two bodies of liquid? If so, when the Jew pours the wine into the gentile's vessel, the flow of wine that is in contact with the forbidden wine at the bottom of the vessel would render all the wine being poured into the vessel forbidden.

The *Gemara* considers whether the answer lies in the concept of *nitzok chibbur* — the idea that liquid connected by a stream of pouring is considered a single unit. If *nitzok chibbur* applies, then even though the wine at the top of the stream has not yet reached the drops at the bottom, it is halachically connected to them and thus forbidden. But the *Gemara* is reluctant to conclude *nitzok chibbur* simply in order to resolve this case — that principle remains contested and should not be treated as an established ruling merely to explain this difficulty.

An alternative resolution is therefore offered: the *Gemara* distinguishes between a vessel that the non-Jew is holding and one that is resting on the ground. If the non-Jew were holding his vessel, then *hagbahah* — lifting — would apply, since by holding the vessel he suspends its contents above the ground and is effectively *magbi'ah* the wine. But if the vessel is sitting on the ground, as is typically the case in a shop, then *hagbahah* does not apply. And the *Gemara* further establishes that the wine is not acquired through *kinyan kli* until it reaches the bottom of the vessel — it does not suffice for it to enter the airspace alone if the vessel is on the ground. Thus, acquisition through *kinyan kli* occurs only at the moment the wine reaches the bottom and mingles with the forbidden drops, at which precise moment the wine has already become *yayin nesech*. This is why Rav's instruction to collect payment first is necessary regardless of whether *meshicha* also works as a *kinyan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, אִי דְּנָקֵיט לֵיהּ גּוֹי לִכְלִי בִּידֵיהּ — הָכִי נָמֵי; לָא צְרִיכָא דְּמַנַּח אַאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this conclusion: **No,** a stream of liquid does not serve as a connection, and therefore **if** the **gentile is holding the vessel in his hand,** the gentile **indeed** acquires the wine before it becomes forbidden, and therefore the money paid for it is permitted. Rav's ruling **is not necessary** unless the gentile's vessel **is set on the ground,** so that there is no act of acquisition before the wine becomes forbidden.

### Kinyan Kli Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיקְּנֵי לֵיהּ כִּלְיוֹ! שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ: כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל לוֹקֵחַ בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר — לֹא קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let the vessels of** the gentile **acquire** the wine from the moment it enters the interior airspace. Can one **conclude from** this that if the **buyer's vessels** are **in the domain of the seller, the buyer does not acquire** the merchandise once it is placed in his vessels? This is an issue that is subject to a dispute between the Sages, which remains unresolved.

At this point the *Gemara* raises a further question: even without *meshicha* or *hagbahah*, why does *kinyan kli* not take effect while the vessel is in the seller's domain? This is a general question about *kinyan kli*: does a purchaser's vessel effect a *kinyan* while it is situated in the domain of the seller? The concern is that if *kinyan kli* does not work in the seller's domain, that would itself explain why the non-Jew does not acquire the wine before it becomes forbidden, without needing to invoke either the drops at the bottom or the specific mechanics of *hagbahah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: קָנָה לוֹקֵחַ, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא עַכֶּבֶת יַיִן אַפּוּמַּיהּ דְּכוּזַנְתָּא, דְּקַמָּא קַמָּא אִינְּסֵיךְ לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **No, actually, I** could **say to you** that the **buyer acquires** the merchandise in such a case. **But here,** where the wine is rendered forbidden before being acquired by the gentile, **we are dealing with** a case **where there is a remnant of wine** remaining **on the mouth of the** gentile's **jug [*kuzanta*],** which renders the wine poured into the jug forbidden, **as each** bit of wine **becomes libation** wine when it comes into contact with the remnant of wine on the mouth as it is poured into the jug. This is why Rav told the wine shopkeepers to take the money before measuring the wine into the gentile's vessel.

The *Gemara* declines to establish that *kinyan kli* fails in the seller's domain as a general principle, and instead reverts to a more concrete physical explanation. The real problem, the *Gemara* concludes, is that there is wine adhering to the lip of the vessel — the rim — left from previous use. As the Jew pours his wine into the vessel, the stream of wine contacts those forbidden drops on the lip before it ever enters the airspace of the vessel at all. Thus, the wine becomes mixed with *yayin nesech* at the very moment of pouring, before any *kinyan kli* can take effect. This explains, without recourse to either *kinyan kli* failing in the seller's domain or the contested principle of *nitzok chibbur*, why Rav required that payment be received before any measuring takes place.

### Wine Mixture Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְמַאן? דְּלָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, דְּאִי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, הָאָמַר: יִמָּכֵר כּוּלּוֹ לְגוֹיִם חוּץ מִדְּמֵי יֵין נֶסֶךְ שֶׁבּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **And in accordance with whose** opinion does Rav say this? It is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel; as, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, doesn't he say** that libation wine that became mixed with other wine **may all be sold to gentiles** for the monetary value of the entire mixture **except for the value of the wine** used for **a libation that is in** the mixture? Accordingly, it is permitted to accept payment for wine that was poured into a gentile's vessel, and the *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

Once this scenario is established, a further question arises. The wine that becomes forbidden is predominantly the Jew's kosher wine; only a minute quantity of *yayin nesech* from the residual drops is present in the mixture. The bulk of what is in the vessel is permissible wine. Why then may not the Jew simply accept payment for his own kosher wine, arguing that he is not being paid for and is not deriving benefit from the forbidden drops, only from his own portion?

This question opens onto a dispute between Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Sages. When *yayin nesech* becomes mixed with permitted wine, and the entire mixture is thereby forbidden (*asur be-mashehu*), may one nonetheless sell the mixture to a non-Jew — charging only for the permitted portion and not for the forbidden drops? One view permits this: the Jew is not receiving benefit from the *assur* wine, only from his own. The other view holds that once the mixture is uniformly forbidden, one cannot selectively claim payment only for the permitted portion; the entire mixture must be treated as *yayin nesech* and none of it may be sold.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי הוּא טַעְמָא, אֶלָּא לְרַב, הָאָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל — חָבִית בְּחָבִית, אֲבָל לֹא יַיִן בְּיַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The explanation** of **this** ruling can be **only according to** the opinion of **Rav,** who issued the ruling. And **doesn't Rav say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** only in the case of **a barrel** of permitted wine that became intermingled **with** another **barrel** of libation wine, **but not** in the case of **wine** that became mixed **with** other **wine** in the same barrel? Therefore, explaining Rav's statement as contradicting the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is not problematic; consequently, it cannot be proven from his statement that a gentile does not acquire an item by pulling it.

The *Gemara* notes that if this case is to be considered a genuine prohibition, it must not accord with the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who would appear to permit collecting payment for the permitted portion of a mixture. But Rav himself ruled: *halacha ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel chavis be-chavis* — the rule follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel specifically when sealed barrels are mixed up with one another but not when the liquids themselves are blended.

The distinction is significant. If ten barrels of kosher wine become intermixed with one barrel of *yayin nesech* and no one can identify which is which, Rav permits selling them all to a non-Jew and charging for ten barrels — because in principle the forbidden barrel remains conceptually separate and one can say "I am not selling you that one, whichever it is." But when the liquids themselves are physically combined into a single undifferentiated mass, that conceptual separation is impossible. The forbidden drops are inseparable from the permitted wine in any physical or practical sense, and the entire mixture must be treated as uniformly forbidden. Accordingly, one may not sell any of it, and payment for the permitted portion cannot be extracted separately. This is why Rav's instruction holds even in Rav Ashi's reading: the mixture is all one forbidden unit, and the entire justification for selling it falls away.

## Scrap Metal w/ Idol

### Idol Discovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַלּוֹקֵחַ גְּרוּטָאוֹת מִן הַגּוֹיִם וּמָצָא בָּהֶן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יַחֲזִיר, אִם מִשֶּׁנָּתַן מָעוֹת מָשַׁךְ — יוֹלִיךְ לְיָם הַמֶּלַח. אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי קוֹנָה, אַמַּאי יַחְזִיר? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִשּׁוּם דְּמִיחֲזֵי כִּי מִקָּח טָעוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises** another **objection** to the statement of Ameimar that a gentile can acquire an object by pulling it. It is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who buys broken vessels** made of gold or silver **from the gentiles and finds among them** an object of **idol worship, if he pulled** the object of idol worship, thereby performing an act of acquisition, **before he gave** the **money** to the gentile, **he may return** the object of idol worship to the gentile. But **if he pulled** it **after he gave** the **money** to the gentile, he may not return it. Since the idol's status was not revoked, he must **take** it and cast it **into the Dead Sea.** In that case, **if it enters your mind** that **pulling** an object **acquires** it **in** a transaction with **a gentile,** as Ameimar maintains, then **why may he return** the object once he has pulled it, since it is his? **Abaye said:** He may return it **because it appears to be a mistaken transaction,** since he clearly did not intend to purchase an object of idol worship.

The *Gemara* now examines a *braita* about a different category of forbidden object in order to derive further conclusions about the operative *kinyan* for non-Jews. The case: a Jew purchases scrap metal from a non-Jew, and upon inspecting his purchase he discovers that some of the metal pieces are small discarded idols — *avodah zarah* — which are *asur be-hana'ah*. The *braita* distinguishes between two scenarios. *Ad she-lo natan ma'os mashach* — if he performed *meshicha* and took physical possession before paying — he may return the idol. *Im mi-she-natan ma'os mashach* — if he first paid and then performed *meshicha* — he may not return it but must cast it into the sea (*yolic le-yam ha-melach*), because returning the idol to the non-Jew in exchange for a refund would constitute selling *avodah zarah* and thereby deriving benefit from a forbidden object.

The question is whether this *braita* teaches us something about what finalizes the sale: is it payment (*kesef*) or physical possession (*meshicha*)?

### Abaye's Answer

On the surface, the *braita* seems to suggest that payment is the operative act, since it is the payment of money that makes the return of the idol impermissible. Abaye, however, resists this conclusion. He explains that *meshicha* does work as a *kinyan* — the Jew took possession of all the scrap metal through *meshicha* — but the idol is an exception because the entire purchase was a *mekach ta'ut*, a mistaken transaction. The Jew never intended to acquire an idol, and therefore *meshicha* cannot be said to have effected a valid acquisition of the forbidden item specifically. That is why, as a matter of principle, he can return it.

### Rava's Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רֵישָׁא מִקָּח טָעוּת, סֵיפָא לָאו מִקָּח טָעוּת? אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא מִקָּח טָעוּת, וְרֵישָׁא דְּלָא יָהֵיב זוּזֵי — לָא מִיתְחֲזֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל, סֵיפָא דְּיָהֵיב זוּזֵי — מִיתְחֲזֵי כַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה בְּיַד יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Then why, in the second instance, where he paid the money, may he not return it? Is the purchase in **the first clause a mistaken transaction** but the purchase in **the latter clause not a mistaken transaction? Rather, Rava said:** The purchase in **the first clause and** the purchase in **the latter clause** are both **a mistaken transaction, but** in the case presented in **the first clause, where he did not pay him the dinars, it does not appear as** though there is an object of **idol worship in the possession of a Jew,** and so he may return it. By contrast, in **the latter clause, where he did pay him the dinars, it appears as** though there is an object of **idol worship in the possession of a Jew,** and therefore he must cast away the object rather than return it.

But this creates a problem, as Rava immediately points out: if the entire justification for returning the idol is *mekach ta'ut* — that the purchase was a mistake — then that should apply equally whether or not money has been paid. *Mekach ta'ut* nullifies the transaction with respect to the idol regardless of the sequence of payment and *meshicha*. Why then does the *braita* distinguish between having paid money and not having paid money?

Rava therefore refines the analysis. He accepts that in both cases — whether money was paid before or after *meshicha* — the situation is a *mekach ta'ut* with respect to the idol specifically, and the Jew does not genuinely own it in either case. The distinguishing factor is not whether a *kinyan* was made on the idol, but whether returning the idol to the non-Jew *looks like* a sale.

### Money Paid Effect

When money has not yet been paid: the Jew agreed on a price of, say, one thousand dollars for the scrap metal. The idol is worth ten dollars' worth of metal. He returns the idol to the non-Jew and when payment is eventually made, he simply deducts ten dollars and pays nine hundred and ninety. No money changes hands in connection with the idol itself; it simply never becomes part of the sale. This does not look like selling *avodah zarah* — it looks like correcting a mistaken purchase.

But when money has already been paid in full: to return the idol now, the Jew would hand back the object and demand ten dollars in return. That looks exactly like a sale — an idol is being transferred to the non-Jew in exchange for money. That appearance itself is prohibited: *seifa de-yahiv zuzei mitchazei ke-oved kochavim be-yad Yisrael* — it has the appearance of a Jew selling an idol.

The *kinyan*, on this reading, takes place at the time of *meshicha* in both cases. The only variable is whether the mechanics of returning the idol will carry the appearance of a forbidden sale. Thus, the *braita* does not prove that payment is the operative act of *kinyan*; rather, it turns on the distinct question of how the return of an idol appears in each case.

## Prepayment Analysis

### Measuring Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, הַמּוֹכֵר יֵינוֹ לְנׇכְרִי, פָּסַק עַד שֶׁלֹּא מָדַד — דָּמָיו מוּתָּרִים. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי אֵינָהּ קוֹנָה, אַמַּאי דָּמָיו מוּתָּרִין? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — דְּאַקְדֵּים לֵיהּ דִּינָר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a claim against Rav Ashi's opinion that a gentile cannot acquire an item by pulling it: **Mar Kashisha, son of Rav Ḥisda, said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** an objection to your opinion from the mishna: In the case of a Jew **who sells his wine to a gentile,** if **he fixed** a price **before he measured** the wine into the gentile's vessel, deriving benefit from **the money** paid **for** the wine is **permitted,** as the wine was acquired by the gentile before he touched it. **And if you say** that **pulling** an item **does not acquire** it **in** a transaction involving **a gentile, why is the money** paid **for it permitted?** Rav Ashi replied: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the gentile **paid him a dinar in advance,** before the measuring, thereby acquiring the wine with money.

Mar Kashisha the son of Rav Chisda raises a challenge to Rav Ashi directly: come and prove your position from our *mishna* itself — *hamocher yeino le-oved kochavim*, you sell wine to a non-Jew. *Pasak ad she-lo madad damav mutarin* — the price was fixed before it was measured, the money is permitted. Now, if *meshicha* does not work as a *kinyan* for a non-Jew, why would fixing the price before measuring be sufficient? The non-Jew has not yet paid money, so how is the wine his before he can touch it?

### Price Fixation Intent

Rav Ashi responds: *hacha be-mai askinan de-akdim lei dinar* — the case of the *mishna* involves a situation where the non-Jew prepaid. He gave money before the measuring took place. This is, admittedly, a difficult reading of the *mishna*, which never mentions money being paid. But Rav Ashi maintains that in the background of the *mishna*, money was already given; and without the price having been fixed, even prepayment does not finalize the sale, because *gemar da'at* — a fully settled intention — requires that both parties know the exact terms. Until the price is set, no amount of prior payment can close the transaction.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: מָדַד עַד שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַק — דָּמָיו אֲסוּרִין, וְאִי דְּקָדֵים לֵיהּ דִּינָר, אַמַּאי דָּמָיו אֲסוּרִין?
>
> **Translation:** One of the Sages raised an objection: **If that is so, say the last clause:** But if the Jew **measured** the wine into the gentile's vessel, thereby rendering it forbidden, **before he fixed** a price, **the money** paid **for** the wine is **forbidden. And if he paid him a dinar in advance, why** should **the money** paid **for it** be **forbidden?**

The *Gemara* then examines the second case of the *mishna* — *madad ad she-lo pasak damav asurin*, measured before the price was fixed, the money is forbidden — and asks: if money was already prepaid, why should this case be a problem?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּלְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי קוֹנָה, אַמַּאי רֵישָׁא דָּמָיו מוּתָּרִין, וְסֵיפָא דָּמָיו אֲסוּרִין?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: And according to you, as you say** that **pulling acquires** items **in** a transaction involving **a gentile, why** in **the first clause** is **the money** paid **for it permitted, and** in **the latter clause the money** paid **for it** is **forbidden?**

Rav Ashi's answer is the same: without *psak*, the sale remains open regardless of prior payment, because the buyer's *da'at* — his firm intention to complete the acquisition — is not settled until he knows the price.

### Kinyan Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר? פָּסַק — סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ; לֹא פָּסַק — לָא סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what have you to say** to explain the distinction between the two cases? You say that when the Jew **fixed** a price, the gentile consequently **relied** on him that the sale would not be canceled, and so the sale was concluded by the pulling. But in a case where **he did not fix** a price, the gentile **did not rely** on him that the sale would not be canceled, and therefore the sale was not concluded.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִידִי נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דִּקְדֵים לֵיהּ דִּינָר — פָּסַק, סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ; לֹא פָּסַק, לָא סָמְכָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **According to my** opinion **as well, even though** the gentile **gave him a dinar in advance,** if **he fixed** a price, the gentile **relied** on him that the sale would not be canceled, and if **he did not fix** a price, the gentile **did not rely** on him that the sale would not be canceled.

The *Gemara* then poses the question symmetrically to the other side. If, by contrast, we read the *mishna* according to the view that *meshicha* is the operative *kinyan* — meaning that *madad* itself is the act of *kinyan* — why does the presence or absence of *psak* make any difference? In both cases the *mishna* describes the same act of measuring wine, yet in one case the money is permitted and in the other forbidden. The answer, applicable on either reading, is that *psak samkha da'atei* — fixing the price settles the buyer's intention and renders the *kinyan* effective. *Lo pasak lo samkha da'atei* — without fixing the price, even a completed act of *meshicha* or payment does not achieve a final acquisition, because the requisite *gemar da'at* is absent.

The upshot is that the *mishna* cannot be read as a decisive proof for either position. Whether *madad* is the operative *kinyan* (on the *meshicha* reading) or whether it merely marks the moment of the non-Jew's physical access to the wine (on the *kesef* reading), the role of *psak* is structural in both cases: it is the precondition for any *kinyan*, whatever its form, to be finalized. The debate between Ameimar and Rav Ashi therefore remains unresolved by the text of the *mishna* itself.

## Ben Noach Theft

### Theft Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בֶּן נֹחַ נֶהֱרָג עַל פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְלֹא נִיתָּן לְהִישָּׁבוֹן, וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי אֵינָהּ קוֹנָה, אַמַּאי נֶהֱרָג?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** another proof against your opinion, **as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: A descendant of Noah,** i.e., a gentile, **is executed** by the court **for** theft of even **less than the value of one *peruta*; but** if he stole less than the value of one *peruta* from a Jew, **it is not subject to restitution,** i.e., he is not obligated to return it, as the Jew presumably waived the debt. **And if you say** that **pulling** an item **does not acquire** it **in** a transaction involving **a gentile, why** should **he** be **executed?** He did not commit a legally significant act by stealing the item, as it remains in the possession of the owner.

Ravina now cites a teaching in an attempt to settle the debate. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a *ben Noach* — a non-Jew subject to the seven Noahide laws — is executed even for stealing less than a *perutah*, and there is no possibility of restoring the situation through return of the object, since an amount less than a *perutah* is below the threshold of monetary claims in Jewish law.

### Theft Completion

The relevance to our debate: if *meshicha* does not effect a *kinyan* for non-Jews, and if *geneiva* — theft — is only legally completed when the thief has performed a *ma'aseh kinyan*, then by what act is the theft completed here? Where there is no payment of money — since one does not pay money when stealing — how is the theft ever legally consummated, so as to make the *ben Noach* liable for execution?

### Anguish Suffices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּצַעֲרֵיהּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: He is executed **because he caused a Jew distress** by stealing the item, even though by the *halakhot* of acquisition the thief did not acquire it.

Rashi explains: even without a formal *ma'aseh kinyan*, a *ben Noach* who physically deprives a Jew of access to his property has committed a sufficient act of *geneiva* by virtue of the anguish (*tza'ar*) and deprivation caused to the owner. The transgression of the Noahide prohibition against theft does not require the identical formal conditions of *kinyan* that apply in intra-Jewish property law. One can lock a person out of his own house and prevent him from accessing his property without technically completing a *kinyan*, yet that is sufficient to constitute a violation of the prohibition against theft for *bnei Noach*.

### Second Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״לֹא נִיתַּן לְהִישָּׁבוֹן״? דְּאֵינוֹ בְּתוֹרַת הִישָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** does it mean that the stolen object **is not subject to restitution?** It means **that it is not** subject **to the *halakhot* of restitution,** because it is not technically a theft.

The *Gemara* then addresses the second case in the same source: if a second non-Jew steals the object from the first, he too is executed. This implies that non-Jew number one has genuinely taken possession of the object, such that non-Jew number two can steal from him. But non-Jew number two did not harm the Jewish owner at all — only non-Jew number one. If the liability of non-Jew number one rested purely on the *tza'ar* caused to the Jew, then non-Jew number two, who caused the Jew no anguish whatsoever, should incur no liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בָּא חֲבֵירוֹ וּנְטָלָהּ מִמֶּנּוּ — נֶהֱרָג עָלֶיהָ. בִּשְׁלָמָא רֵישָׁא, מִשּׁוּם דְּצַעֲרֵיהּ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, אֶלָּא סֵיפָא, מַאי עָבֵיד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, say the latter clause** of that halakhic statement: If **another** descendant of Noah **came and took** the stolen item **from him,** he is also **executed for it. Granted,** with regard to **the first clause** it can be explained that although there is no obligation to return the item, the gentile is executed **because he caused the Jew distress; but** with regard to **the latter clause, what did he do?** He did not cause the Jew distress, and since the item is worth less than one *peruta*, one is not obligated to return it unless he stole it from a gentile. Consequently, the second theft should not be considered a legally significant act.

### Meshicha Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, מְשִׁיכָה בְּגוֹי קוֹנָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** should one not **conclude from it** that one **pulling** an object **acquires** it **in** a transaction performed by **a gentile?** Accordingly, the actions of both the first and second gentiles are legally significant acts of theft, for which they are liable to be executed. The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that this is so.

The conclusion is therefore that non-Jew number one must have genuinely acquired the stolen object — through an act of *meshicha*, of physical taking of possession — so that non-Jew number two is stealing from a genuine owner, and that is itself a Noahide violation. *Shema mina meshicha be-oved kochavim koneh* — conclude from this that *meshicha* effects a *kinyan* for a non-Jew.

### Tosafot Caveat

Tosafot, however, adds an important qualification. Even granting that *meshicha* works as a *kinyan* for non-Jews in the context of theft, this does not necessarily prove that *meshicha* is sufficient in a transactional sale. Tosafot's position is that there is no dispute about *meshicha* functioning for non-Jews in non-transactional contexts — gifts and theft both operate through physical possession regardless of payment. The contested question is specifically whether, in a sale between a Jew and a non-Jew, *meshicha* alone can finalize the transaction without any payment of *kesef*. The proof from *ben Noach* theft therefore does not resolve the central debate between Ameimar and Rav Ashi regarding the mechanics of commercial *kinyan* for non-Jews. The *Gemara*'s apparent conclusion — *shema mina* — applies in the narrow context of non-transactional acquisition, and the question of which *kinyan* governs a sale of *yayin nesech* to a non-Jew, with all its implications for *mechirat chametz* and related matters, remains a matter of ongoing dispute.
