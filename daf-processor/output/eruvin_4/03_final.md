# Eruvin 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Amah Measurements

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּת סוּכָּה וְאַמַּת מָבוֹי בְּאַמָּה בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה, אַמַּת כִּלְאַיִם בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said in the name of Rav Naḥman: The cubit** mentioned with regard to the *halakhot* **of *sukka* and the cubit** mentioned in connection with the *halakhot* **of an alleyway** is **a** small **cubit** consisting **of five** handbreadths. In contrast, **the cubit of** a forbidden mixture of **diverse kinds** of seeds is **a** large **cubit** consisting **of six** handbreadths.

The daf opens by resuming a discussion introduced at the bottom of 3b concerning the standardization — or lack thereof — of the *amah* as a unit of measure. Abaye, in the name of Rav Nachman, maintained that there are two distinct types of *amot*: one measuring five *tefachim* and one measuring six *tefachim*. The operative principle is that one always applies whichever measure produces the stricter result — a measurement system notable for its deliberate lack of standardization.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: כׇּל אַמּוֹת בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה, אֶלָּא: הַלָּלוּ שׂוֹחֲקוֹת, וְהַלָּלוּ עֲצֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rava said in the name of Rav Naḥman: All cubits** mentioned in measurements by the Sages consisted of a large **cubit of six** handbreadths. **However, these,** the cubits mentioned with regard to diverse kinds of seeds, **are** measured with **expansive** handbreadths, with the fingers spread apart, **whereas those,** the cubits mentioned with regard to an alleyway and a *sukka*, **are** measured with **compressed** handbreadths, with the fingers held together.

Rava, also citing Rav Nachman, takes a less extreme position. According to Rava, whenever the Sages speak of an *amah*, they always mean a six-*tefach* *amah*. The variance he acknowledges is far narrower: *hallalu sochakos v'hallalu atzevos* — some measurements are "laughing," that is, taken with a loosely held hand, and others are "distressed," meaning tightly compressed. This amounts to a minor variance, something on the order of half an inch, rather than the dramatic difference between five and six *tefachim*. Here too, one always applies whichever direction constitutes a *chumra* — using the larger measure when a larger measurement is stricter, and the smaller measure when that is stricter.

### Braita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כׇּל אַמּוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מְכֻוּוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **All cubits that were mentioned by the Sages are cubits of six** handbreadths, **provided that they are not precisely** a cubit.

The *gemara* now introduces a *braita* as a challenge: *kol amos she'amru chachamim b'amah bat shishah u'bilvad she'lo ye'u mechuvannos* — all *amot* mentioned by the Sages refer to a six-*tefach* *amah*, provided they are not precisely measured. This formulation fits Rava's position perfectly. For Rava, "not precise" simply means the measurements vary slightly between *sochakos* and *atzevos*, always in the direction of stringency. For Abaye, however, this *braita* poses a serious difficulty: it states that all *amot* of the Sages are six-*tefach* measurements, which appears to contradict his view that the five-*tefach* *amah* plays a significant role.

### Abaye's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרָבָא — כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיהֶוְיָין הַלָּלוּ שׂוֹחֲקוֹת וְהַלָּלוּ עֲצֵבוֹת. אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי קַשְׁיָא! אָמַר לָךְ אַבָּיֵי, אֵימָא: אַמַּת כִּלְאַיִם בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, according to Rava,** the *baraita* means: **So that these,** the cubits of diverse kinds of seeds, **should be** measured with **expansive** handbreadths, **and those,** the cubits of *sukka*, should be measured with **depressed** handbreadths. **However, according to Abaye,** it is **difficult. The Gemara answers: Abaye** could have **said to you:** Emend the *baraita* and **say: The cubit of diverse kinds** of seeds mentioned by the Sages **is** measured **with a cubit of six** handbreadths, not the other cubits.

Abaye responds by reinterpreting the *braita*: the opening clause should be read not as applying to all *amot* universally, but specifically to *amot* in the context of *kilayim*. In *kilayim*, since the required measurement functions to permit or qualify something — you need a certain distance to be allowed to plant in that field — one always uses the larger (stricter) measure. Hence, the six-*tefach* *amah* is invariably applied there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל אַמּוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים בְּכִלְאַיִם בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מְצוּמְצָמוֹת, מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא כׇּל אַמּוֹת קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **However, from** the fact **that it is taught** in **the latter clause** of the *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: All the cubits that the Sages mentioned with regard to diverse kinds** of seeds are measured **with cubits of six** handbreadths, **provided that they are not** measured with **exact** handbreadths? This proves **by inference** that **the** anonymous **first *tanna* is speaking of all cubits,** and not only those in the case of diverse kinds of seeds.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this reading. The end of the same *braita* contains a statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *kol amos she'amru chachamim b'kilayim b'amah bat shishah u'bilvad she'lo ye'u m'tzumtzamos* — all the *amot* by *kilayim* are six *tefachim* and not tightly measured. If, as Abaye claims, the opening clause of the *braita* already refers exclusively to *kilayim*, then Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would merely be repeating what the *tanna kamma* already said. The *gemara* therefore infers that the *tanna kamma* must be speaking about all *amot* generally — not just *kilayim* — and that the debate between him and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is precisely whether the six-*tefach amah* applies universally or only to *kilayim*. This understanding clearly supports Rava over Abaye.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ אַבָּיֵי: וְלָאו מִי אִיכָּא רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל דְּקָאֵי כְּווֹתִי, אֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara answers that Abaye** could have **said to you: Isn't there Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds in accordance with my** opinion? **I stated** my opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

Abaye acknowledges the force of this argument. He concedes that the *tanna kamma* appears to hold that all *amot* are six *tefachim*, which runs counter to his own view. Nevertheless, he points out that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel sides with him, at least to the extent of insisting on the six-*tefach amah* specifically in the *kilayim* context — implying that elsewhere, the five-*tefach amah* is operative. *Ana de'amari k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — Abaye aligns himself with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and accepts that his position is a minority one among the *tannaim*.

### Rava's Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַבָּיֵי וַדַּאי תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. לְרָבָא מִי לֵימָא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? אָמַר לָךְ רָבָא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָא אֲתָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן: אַמַּת כִּלְאַיִם לֹא יְצַמְצֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **According to Abaye,** the issue of large and small cubits **is certainly** subject to a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as his ruling can only be in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. **According to Rava,** however, **must it be said** that **this is** subject to a dispute between ***tanna'im*? The Gemara answers: This is not necessarily the case, as Rava** could have **said to you: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** does not dispute the basic teaching of the anonymous first *tanna* that all the cubits mentioned by the Sages are cubits of six handbreadths. Rather, he **came to teach us this: One** should **not reduce the cubit of diverse kinds** of seeds, i.e., one should not measure it with depressed handbreadths.

The *gemara* now asks: according to Rava, is there necessarily a *tannaitic* dispute here? Rava's position is that all *amot* are always six *tefachim*, varying only in whether they are measured loosely or tightly. But Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's statement — that *kilayim amot* are six *tefachim* — seems to imply that only by *kilayim* is the six-*tefach amah* used, suggesting a five-*tefach amah* applies elsewhere. This would appear to undermine Rava.

Rava responds that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is not introducing the six-*tefach amah* as something specific to *kilayim*. Rather, *that* is taken for granted — everything is always six *tefachim*. What Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is adding is the qualification *lo yetzamtzem*: do not measure the *kilayim amah* tightly. Since in *kilayim* we need a certain distance to permit planting, we apply the larger, looser six-*tefach* measure. His *chiddush* lies in the word *m'tzumtzamos*, not in the six *tefachim* themselves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא: "אַמַּת כִּלְאַיִם לֹא יְצַמְצֵם", בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי אַמַּת סוּכָּה וְאַמַּת מָבוֹי?
>
> **Translation:** **And** if that is the case, **let him say: One must not reduce the cubit of diverse kinds** of seeds. **What does** the phrase: **A cubit** consisting **of six** handbreadths come **to exclude? Does it not** come **to exclude the cubit of a *sukka* and the cubit of an alleyway,** which are measured with cubits of five handbreadths?

The *gemara* presses: if that is all he means, why not simply say "by *kilayim*, do not measure tightly"? Why specify "a six-*tefach amah*"? The phrase *b'amah bat shishah l'ma'utei mai* — what is the six-*tefach* specification coming to exclude? Does it not imply that by other things — such as a *sukkah* or a *mavoi* — one uses a five-*tefach amah*? This would again favor Abaye.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְמַעוֹטֵי אַמָּה יְסוֹד וְאַמָּה סוֹבֵב,
>
> **Translation:** **No,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's formulation comes **to exclude the cubit of the base** of the altar, which is the bottom level of the altar, one cubit high with a ledge one cubit wide, **and the cubit of the surrounding ledge** of the altar, which is five cubits above the base, six cubits above the ground, and one cubit wide.

Rava's final answer is decisive: *lo, l'ma'utei ammat yesod v'ammat sovev* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's emphasis on the six-*tefach amah* by *kilayim* is not meant to exclude *sukkah* or *mavoi* from the six-*tefach* standard. Rather, it is meant to draw a contrast with a single specific domain — the dimensions of the *mizbe'ach* in the *Beit Hamikdash* — where even Rava concedes that a five-*tefach amah* is used for certain measurements. In every practical area of *halacha* encountered today, whether *sukkah*, *mavoi*, or *kilayim*, all authorities agree that the operative unit is the six-*tefach amah*. The five-*tefach amah* is confined to the realm of the *Beit Hamikdash* and its *mizbe'ach*, a domain remote from contemporary practical application.

## Five Tefach Amah

### Exception Introduced

With Rava's reinterpretation established, the *gemara* turns to explain where exactly the five-*tefach amah* does apply. Both Abaye and Rava agree — whatever their broader dispute — that for certain measurements of the *mizbe'ach* in the *Beit Hamikdash*, a five-*tefach amah* is employed. This is the concession that even Rava makes, and it is the referent of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's implied exclusion.

### Yechezkel Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: "וְאֵלֶּה מִדּוֹת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ בָּאַמּוֹת אַמָּה אַמָּה וָטֹפַח וְחֵיק הָאַמָּה וְאַמָּה רֹחַב וּגְבוּלָהּ אֶל שְׂפָתָהּ סָבִיב זֶרֶת הָאֶחָד וְזֶה גַּב הַמִּזְבֵּחַ". "חֵיק הָאַמָּה" — זֶה יְסוֹד, "וְאַמָּה רֹחַב" — זֶה סוֹבֵב, "וּגְבוּלָהּ אֶל שְׂפָתָהּ סָבִיב" — אֵלּוּ הַקְּרָנוֹת, "וְזֶה גַּב הַמִּזְבֵּחַ" — זֶה מִזְבַּח הַזָּהָב.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is written: "And these are the measures of the altar by cubits; the cubit is a cubit and a handbreadth, the bottom shall be a cubit, and the breadth a cubit, and its border by its edge round about shall be a span: And this shall be the higher part of the altar"** (Ezekiel 43:13). And the Sages explained this verse as follows: **"The bottom shall be a cubit," this is the base** of the altar; **"and the breadth a cubit," this is the surrounding ledge** of the altar; **"and its border by its edge round about," these are the horns** of the altar, i.e., extensions of the corners of the altar; **"and this shall be the higher part of the altar,"** this refers to **the golden altar** that stood inside the Sanctuary and was also measured by small cubits.

The scriptural basis for this comes from the book of Yechezkel. It is worth noting that Yechezkel's descriptions of the *Beit Hamikdash* and its *avodah* present well-known difficulties, containing apparent contradictions with the explicit *Torah* text — to the point where the *gemara* elsewhere records that the Sages considered suppressing the book. Nevertheless, we do derive *halachic* principles from it.

The relevant *pasuk* states: *ve'eileh midos hamizbeiach ba'amos, ammah ammah vatefach* — "these are the measurements of the *mizbe'ach* in *amot*, an *amah* and an *amah* and a *tefach*." The *gemara* reads this as indicating that the measurements preceding this verse, which described various structural elements of the *mizbe'ach*, were given in a larger *amah* — specifically, a six-*tefach amah*, which is equivalent to a five-*tefach amah* plus one additional *tefach*. Thus, the phrase "an *amah* and a *tefach*" conveys that the large *amah* equals one small *amah* plus a *tefach* — thereby establishing the existence of both a five-*tefach* and a six-*tefach amah*.

### Yesod & Sovev

The *pasuk* continues: *vecheik ha'ammah ve'ammah rochav ugvulah el sefasah saviv zeires ha'echad vezeh gav hamizbeiach*. The *gemara* identifies the components measured in the smaller, five-*tefach amah*: *cheik ha'ammah* — the "belt" of the *amah* — refers to the *yesod*, the foundation of the *mizbe'ach*. *Ve'ammah rochav* — "an *amah* of width" — refers to the *sovev*, the walkway that circled the *mizbe'ach*. *Ugvulah el sefasah saviv* — "the border at its edge all around" — refers to the *karnos*, the horns at the four corners of the *mizbe'ach's* top. *Vezeh gav hamizbeiach* — "and this is the top of the *mizbe'ach*" — refers to the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, the golden altar. All of these structural elements, measured at one *amah* in some dimension, employ the five-*tefach amah*.

### Rashi Explains

The precise application of the five-*tefach amah* to these structures is not uniform across all directions. Rashi, in his commentary — and he notes that a fuller treatment appears in the chapter *Shtei HaLechem* — clarifies that it is not the *width* of the *yesod*'s projection that is measured in the small *amah*, but rather the *height* of its rise. Similarly, by the *sovev*, the inverse obtains: its height is measured in the standard six-*tefach amah*, but the width of the ledge is measured in the five-*tefach amah*. As for the *karnos*, they are one *amah* by one *amah* in both directions using the five-*tefach amah*. This is admittedly a complex and somewhat surprising picture — different dimensions of the same structure employ different scales of *amah* — but that is how Rashi presents it.

In summary, Rava's position emerges intact: in all practical *halachic* contexts, the six-*tefach amah* is universally operative, with minor variance between tightly and loosely measured *tefachim*, always in the direction of stringency. The five-*tefach amah* is reserved for specific structural measurements of the *mizbe'ach* in the *Beit Hamikdash*. And even according to Abaye, the five-*tefach amah* plays a broader role, though he concedes that the *tanna kamma* of the *braita* does not support him.

## Shiurim & Halacha LeMoshe

### Intro to Traditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: שִׁיעוּרִין חֲצִיצִין וּמְחִיצִין, הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: The measures** relating to mitzvot in the Torah, and the *halakhot* governing **interpositions** that invalidate ritual immersions, and the *halakhot* of **partitions are** all ***halakhot*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.** These *halakhot* have no basis in the Written Torah, but according to tradition they were orally transmitted by God to Moses together with the Written Torah.

From the discussion of measurements, the *gemara* transitions to a broader and famous statement attributed to Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi in the name of Rav: *shiurin chatzitzin umechitzin halacha leMoshe miSinai* — measurements, blockages, and partitions are all *halacha leMoshe miSinai*, traditions transmitted orally from Moshe at Sinai. The three terms carry a certain mnemonic ring in their grouping, and the *gemara* proceeds to unpack each one in turn. This discussion is highly apt at the opening of *Eruvin*, a tractate deeply concerned with measurements and the operative parameters of partitions.

### Seven Species

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁיעוּרִין?! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא! דִּכְתִיב: "אֶרֶץ חִטָּה וּשְׂעוֹרָה וְגוֹ׳", וְאָמַר רַב חָנָן: כׇּל הַפָּסוּק הַזֶּה לְשִׁיעוּרִין נֶאֱמַר —
>
> **Translation:** **Are measures a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai? They are** written **in the Torah, as it is written: "A land of wheat, and barley,** and vines, and figs, and pomegranates, a land of olive oil and honey" (Deuteronomy 8:8), **and Rav Ḥanan said: This entire verse was stated for** the purpose of teaching **measures** with regard to different *halakhot* in the Torah.

Beginning with *shiurim*, the *gemara* notes that the *pasuk* in Devarim enumerating the seven species of *Eretz Yisrael* — *eretz chitah u'se'orah v'gefen u'se'enah v'rimon eretz zeis shemen u'devash* — is, according to Rav Chanan, entirely devoted to providing the basis for *halachic* measurements. Each species in the verse serves as the source or vehicle for a particular *shiur*. The *gemara* works through each one.

### Wheat Bread Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "חִטָּה", לִכְדִתְנַן: הַנִּכְנָס לַבַּיִת הַמְנוּגָּע וְכֵלָיו עַל כְּתֵיפָיו, וְסַנְדָּלָיו וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּיָדָיו — הוּא וְהֵם טְמֵאִין מִיָּד. הָיָה לָבוּשׁ כֵּלָיו, וְסַנְדָּלָיו בְּרַגְלָיו, וְטַבְּעוֹתָיו בְּאֶצְבְּעוֹתָיו הוּא טָמֵא מִיָּד, וְהֵן טְהוֹרִין עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁהֶא בִּכְדֵי אֲכִילַת פְּרָס — פַּת חִיטִּין וְלֹא פַּת שְׂעוֹרִין, מֵיסֵב וְאוֹכֵל בְּלִיפְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Wheat** was mentioned as the basis for calculating the time required for one to become ritually impure when entering a house afflicted with leprosy, **as that which we learned** in a mishna: **One who enters a house afflicted with leprosy** of the house (see Leviticus 14), **and his clothes** are draped **over his shoulders, and his sandals and his rings are in his hands,** both **he and they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, **immediately** become **ritually impure.** However, if **he was dressed in his clothes, and his sandals were on his feet, and his rings were on his fingers, he immediately** becomes **ritually impure, but they,** the clothes, sandals, and rings, **remain pure until he stays** in the house **long enough to eat half a loaf** of bread. This calculation is based on **wheat bread,** which takes less time to eat, **and not** on **barley bread,** and it relates to one who is **reclining and eating** it together **with relish,** which hastens the eating. This is a Torah measurement connected specifically to wheat.

*Chitah*, wheat, teaches the standard of *kdei achilas pras* — the time it takes to eat half a standard loaf of bread. The *shiur* is of wheat bread, not barley bread, because wheat bread is the referent of the verse, and this time measurement figures importantly not only in the specific law to be discussed, but across a wide range of *halachic* contexts, including the laws of eating on Yom Kippur, the requirements for eating *matzah* on Pesach, and various issues of transgression where the act of eating must occur within this defined window. The *shiur* of *kdei achilas pras* is measured by wheat bread eaten while reclining and while dipping the bread into some type of sauce.

### Tzaraas Mishnah

The *gemara* illustrates this with a *mishnah* from the laws of *tzaraas*. When one enters a house afflicted with *tzaraas* — one of the severe ritual impurity states described in the *Torah* — the general rule is that anything that enters becomes *tamei* automatically, with one exception: garments actually being worn on the body do not become *tamei* immediately upon entry. Rather, they acquire *tumah* only if the person remains in the house long enough to eat a meal.

The *mishnah* articulates this distinction precisely. If someone enters the house carrying garments on his shoulders or holding his sandals and rings in his hands — rather than wearing them — then *hu v'heim tamei'im miyad*: both the person and those items become *tamei* immediately, since they are treated like any other object brought into the house. But if he enters while wearing his garments, with his sandals on his feet and his rings on his fingers, then he himself is *tamei* immediately, while *heim tehorim ad sheyis'ha'eh kdei achilas pras* — the garments remain *tahor* until he has spent within the house the time it takes to eat a *pras*. That *pras* is specifically a half-loaf of wheat bread, eaten while reclining and dipping, which represents the standard pace of a complete and deliberate meal. Rashi understands that all these criteria — wheat bread, reclining, and dipping — serve to *shorten* the time, since wheat bread is easier to eat than barley, reclining and dipping allow one to eat at the most natural and focused pace.

The *shiur* of *kdei achilas pras* is of great practical importance beyond *tzaraas*. Because it defines what constitutes a single act of eating for *halachic* purposes, it sets the outer boundary within which one must consume a *kezayis* for either a prohibition or a positive commandment. Thus, when eating *matzah* on Pesach, one must consume a *kezayis* within this timeframe. This generates the well-known stringency in practice of measuring the *kezayis* of *matzah* by compressing it to remove air, while measuring the *kdei achilas pras* against the time it would take to eat ordinary bread — resulting in a demanding combination. Whether one should measure *kdei achilas pras* relative to the particular food being eaten or against a fixed standard remains a subject of discussion.

### Barley Bean Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "שְׂעוֹרָה", דִּתְנַן: עֶצֶם כִּשְׂעוֹרָה מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, וְאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא בְּאֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Barley** is also used as a basis for measurements, **as we learned** in a mishna: **A bone** from a corpse the size **of a** grain of **barley imparts** ritual **impurity through contact and by being carried,** but **it does not impart impurity** by means **of a tent,** i.e., if the bone was inside a house, it does not render all the articles in the house ritually impure.

*Se'orah*, barley, teaches the *shiur* of a barley bean for bone-related *tumah*. A bone from a human corpse, even in the absence of any flesh, causes *tumah* through contact and carrying if it is at least the size of a barley bean. While causing *tumah* under a roof requires much larger quantities of bone — most of the skeleton or significant structural bones — a bone the size of a single barley grain suffices to convey *tumah* through touch or carrying. This is the *shiur* derived from *se'orah*.

### Wine, Fig, Pomegranate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "גֶּפֶן": כְּדֵי רְבִיעִית יַיִן לְנָזִיר. "תְּאֵנָה": כִּגְרוֹגֶרֶת לְהוֹצָאַת שַׁבָּת. "רִמּוֹן", כְּדִתְנַן: כׇּל כְּלֵי בַּעֲלֵי בָתִּים שִׁיעוּרָן כְּרִימּוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The halakhic measure determined by a **vine is the quantity of a quarter-*log* of wine for a nazirite.** A nazirite, who is prohibited to drink wine, is liable to be flogged if he drinks that measure. **Fig** alludes to the measure of **a dried fig-bulk with regard to** the *halakhot* of **carrying out** on **Shabbat.** One is liable for carrying food fit for human consumption on Shabbat, provided that he carries a dried fig-bulk of that food. **Pomegranate** teaches the measure, **as that which we learned** in a mishna: **All** ritually impure wooden **vessels belonging to** ordinary **homeowners** become pure through being broken, as broken vessels cannot contract or maintain ritual impurity, and they are considered broken if they have holes **the size of pomegranates.**

*Gefen*, the vine, teaches that for a *nazir* who drinks wine, the prohibited quantity is a *revios*. The *gemara* notes a difficulty with this particular derivation: unlike wheat, which provides a standard for measuring by wheat bread, and barley, which provides a concrete size, wine does not obviously yield a measurement for the amount a *nazir* must drink. The *shiur* of a *revios* seems to come from elsewhere. Rashi and Tosafot address this, with the essential answer turning on the fact that the measurement of volume is accomplished by water displacement, and the question arises whether one uses water or wine as the displacement medium. Tosafot maintains that wine has greater surface tension than water, so that displacing a *revios* of wine requires a slightly larger volume of the substance in question. This is admittedly a difficult piece to unpack precisely.

*Te'enah*, the fig, teaches the *shiur* of *kegrogeres* — the size of a pressed fig — which is the minimum quantity that renders one liable for carrying on *Shabbat*. This is familiar from the extensive treatment in *Shabbat*.

*Rimon*, the pomegranate, teaches the *shiur* for vessels of homeowners. A household vessel with a hole the size of a pomegranate loses its status as a vessel and is therefore no longer capable of contracting *tumah*. The *gemara* explains the logic: a storekeeper discards even a slightly damaged vessel since it affects its value, but a homeowner progressively repurposes a vessel as it develops holes — using it first for finer goods, then progressively for coarser items. Only when the hole reaches the size of a pomegranate does even a homeowner abandon it, at which point it ceases to function as a vessel.

### Olive Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "אֶרֶץ זֵית שֶׁמֶן (וּדְבָשׁ)": אֶרֶץ שֶׁכׇּל שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ כְּזֵיתִים. כׇּל שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! וְהָאִיכָּא הָנֵי דַּאֲמַרַן! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אֶרֶץ שֶׁרוֹב שִׁיעוּרֶיהָ כְּזֵיתִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages interpreted: **A land of olive oil and honey,** as: **A land, all of whose measures are olive-bulks.** The Gemara poses a question: **Does it enter your mind** that it is a land **all of whose measures** are olives-bulks? **Yet aren't there those** measures **that we** just **mentioned** above, which are not olive-bulks? **Rather, say: A land, most of whose measures are olive-bulks,** as most measures, e.g., those relating to forbidden foods and to impurity imparted by a corpse in a tent and by contact with an animal carcass, are olive-bulks.

*Eretz zeis shemen*, the land of olive oil, yields the principle that most *halachic* measurements are determined by the *kezayis*, an olive-sized measure. The *gemara* notes that the verse does not say "all" measurements are *kezayis*-based, as that would be inaccurate in light of the other *shiurim* just enumerated; rather, the *majority* of *halachic* measurements follow the *kezayis*. Rashi provides a representative list: prohibited consumptions such as *cheilev*, *dam*, *nosar*, *piggul*, *tamei*, *neveilah*, and *beheimah teme'ah* are all measured by a *kezayis*, and so too positive obligations to eat.

### Honey Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "דְּבַשׁ", כְּכוֹתֶבֶת הַגַּסָּה לְיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Honey,** i.e., dates from which date honey is extracted, also determines a measure, as with regard **to** eating on **Yom Kippur,** one is liable only if he eats **a large date-bulk** of food. Clearly, the measurements pertaining to mitzvot are explicitly written in the Torah and were not transmitted to Moses from Sinai.

*Dvash*, the date honey, teaches the *shiur* of *kokeves hagassah*, a large date, for *Yom Kippur*. This is the measure of food consumption that renders one liable for eating on *Yom Kippur*. It differs from the *kezayis* standard because the prohibition on *Yom Kippur* is not an eating prohibition in the conventional sense — it is an obligation of self-affliction, *inuy* — and thus the relevant quantity is one that constitutes a meaningful satiation rather than a minimal act of eating.

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיסְבְּרָא שִׁיעוּרִין מִיכְתָּב כְּתִיבִי?
>
> **Translation:** **And can you hold** that all these **measures are** explicitly **written** in the Torah with regard to each of the *halakhot* mentioned above?

At this point the *gemara* poses a sharp question: *utisbra*? Does it really make sense to say these *shiurim* are *halacha leMoshe miSinai* if they are derived from a *pasuk*? After all, the verse about the seven species is right there in the *Torah*. If the measurements are grounded in Scripture, in what sense are they oral traditions from Sinai? Moreover, the derivations involve considerable interpretive inference — how does one know that barley refers to bone, that wheat refers to the time to eat bread, or that the other species map onto the *shiurim* assigned to them? None of this is explicit in the text.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הִלְכְתָא נִינְהוּ, וְאַסְמְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן אַקְּרָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, they are *halakhot*** that were transmitted to Moses from Sinai, **and the Sages based them on verses** in the Torah.

The *gemara* resolves this directly: *ella hilchesa ninhu, v'asmikhinhu rabanan akrai* — they are in truth all *halacha leMoshe miSinai*, and the Sages merely connected them to the *pesukim* as an *asmachta*. One might have suggested a middle position — that the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* consisted of the tradition of how to read the *pasuk* — but the *gemara* does not take that route. The *shiurim* are fully oral traditions; the scriptural connections are rabbinic anchors, not the actual source.

This teaching is introduced with particular aptness at the opening of *Eruvin*, a tractate whose *halachot* are pervaded by measurements — dimensions of a *sukkah*, a *mavoi*, and the like — many of which are rabbinic in origin, while the underlying category of biblical measurement is here established as rooted in *halacha leMoshe miSinai*.

## Chatzitzin Rules

### Chatzitzin Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲצִיצִין, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא נִינְהוּ! דִּכְתִיב: "וְרָחַץ אֶת כָּל בְּשָׂרוֹ (בַּמַּיִם)", שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר חוֹצֵץ בֵּין בְּשָׂרוֹ לַמַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Are interpositions a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai? They are** written **in the Torah, as it is written: "And he shall bathe all his flesh in the water"** (Leviticus 15:16), and the Sages derived **that nothing should intervene between his flesh and the water.**

Turning to *chatzitzin*, the *gemara* immediately challenges the characterization of these rules as *halacha leMoshe miSinai* by pointing out that there is an explicit biblical source: the verse *verachatz es kol besaro* — "he shall wash his entire body" — implies that nothing may interpose between the body and the water. If the prohibition against *chatzitzah* is written in the *Torah*, in what sense is it an oral tradition from Sinai?

### Mikvah Requirements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "בַּמַּיִם" — בְּמֵי מִקְוֶה. "כׇּל בְּשָׂרוֹ" — מַיִם שֶׁכׇּל גּוּפוֹ עוֹלֶה בָּהֶן, וְכַמָּה הֵן — אַמָּה עַל אַמָּה בְּרוּם שָׁלֹשׁ אַמּוֹת. וְשִׁיעֲרוּ חֲכָמִים מֵי מִקְוֶה אַרְבָּעִים סְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The definite article in the phrase **"in the water"** indicates that this bathing is performed in water mentioned elsewhere, i.e., specifically **in the water of a ritual bath,** and not in just any water. And the phrase **"all his flesh"** indicates that it must be in **water into which all of his body can enter,** i.e., in which a person can immerse his entire body at once. **And how much** water **is that?** It is **a cubit by a cubit by the height of three cubits. And the Sages calculated** the volume of a ritual bath of this size and determined that **the waters of a ritual bath** measure **forty *se'a*.** As this is derived from the Written Torah, what need is there for a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai?

The *gemara* develops the implications of the phrase *kol besaro bamayim*. "All of his body" means the immersion must be in water sufficient to contain the entire body at once; one cannot immerse limb by limb. Furthermore, *bamayim* is interpreted as *bemei mikvah* — not any water, but specifically *mikvah* water. And how large must that *mikvah* be? *Amah al amah berum gimmel amot* — one *amah* by one *amah* (approximately one and a half by one and a half feet) by three *amot* in height (roughly four and a half feet). This corresponds to the height of a person standing — though perhaps slightly crouching — and represents the minimum volume of water required to submerge an entire body. The Sages calculated this volume as equaling forty *seah*: *veshiaru chachamim mikvah mem seah*. This is the minimum *shiur* of a *kosher mikvah*.

The practical importance of this *shiur* extends well beyond the rare case of an extremely minimal *mikvah*. Contemporary *mikvaot* typically contain far more than forty *seah* of water, but in practice they consist of a large reservoir of piped-in water connected through an opening to a smaller hidden chamber of collected rainwater — which constitutes the *halachically* valid *mikvah*. It is the forty-*seah* threshold that determines the minimum required size of that rainwater chamber, which in turn lends *halachic* validity to the connected water.

The *gemara* therefore notes that the verse teaches a great deal: the prohibition of *chatzitzah*, the requirement of full-body immersion, and the minimum *shiur* of the *mikvah* itself. Given this, the claim that *chatzitzin* are *halacha leMoshe miSinai* seems undermined — they appear to be directly biblical.

### Yitzchak's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתַאי הִילְכְתָא לְרוּבּוֹ וּלְמִיעוּטוֹ וּלְמַקְפִּיד וּלְשֶׁאֵין מַקְפִּיד, וְכִדְרַבִּי יִצְחָק. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה רוּבּוֹ וּמַקְפִּיד עָלָיו חוֹצֵץ, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד עָלָיו אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** The ***halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **comes** to teach the details of interpositions on the body **with regard to its majority and its minority, and with regard to one who is particular and one who is not particular, in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak. As Rabbi Yitzḥak said: By Torah law,** if there is an interposition between a person and the water, and it covers **the majority** of **his** body, **and he is particular** and wants the interposing substance removed, only then **is it** considered **an interposition** that invalidates immersion in a ritual bath. **However, if he is not particular about** that substance, **it is not** considered **an interposition.**

The *gemara* provides the answer: *ki ata hilchata lerubo ulemie'uto ulemakpid uleshe'eino makpid* — the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* is needed not to establish the concept of *chatzitzah* but to define precisely the parameters that determine when an obstruction rises to the level of a *halachic chatzitzah*. These parameters are articulated by Rabbi Yitzchak: *devar Torah rubo umakpid alav chotzetz, ushe'eino makpid alav eino chotzetz* — biblically, a *chatzitzah* is only operative when it covers the *majority* of the body *and* the person is *makpid* — that is, the person objects to its presence and wishes it removed. If the person is not *makpid* — if they are indifferent to the substance remaining on their body — there is no biblical *chatzitzah*, even if it covers the majority. This is a striking limitation: the *Torah* by itself might have been understood to prohibit any obstruction whatsoever, regardless of quantity or attitude. The *halacha leMoshe miSinai* narrows this considerably, requiring both *rov* and *makpid* simultaneously.

### Rabbinic Extensions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְגָזְרוּ עַל רוּבּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד מִשּׁוּם רוּבּוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד, וְעַל מִיעוּטוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד מִשּׁוּם רוּבּוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages, however, **issued a decree** prohibiting substances covering **the majority** of **one's** body with regard to which **he is not particular, due to** substances covering **the majority** of **one's** body with regard to which **he is particular. And,** they issued a decree prohibiting substances covering **the minority** of **his** body with regard to which **one is particular, due to** substances covering **the majority** of **his** body with regard to which **one is particular.**

Rabbinically, however, the Sages extended the prohibition beyond this biblical core. *V'gazru al rubo she'eino makpid* — the Sages decreed that a *chatzitzah* covering the majority of the body is rabbinically problematic even when one is *eino makpid*, lest it lead to the biblical case of majority combined with *makpid*. Similarly, *v'gam mi'ut hamakpid* — a *chatzitzah* covering a minority of the body is rabbinically prohibited when one is *makpid*, again as a precaution against the biblical case. Thus, the rabbinic rule is that either criterion alone — majority alone, or *makpid* alone — is sufficient to create a rabbinic *chatzitzah*.

### Gezeirah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיגְזוֹר נָמֵי עַל מִיעוּטוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד מִשּׁוּם מִיעוּטוֹ הַמַּקְפִּיד, אִי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם רוּבּוֹ שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַקְפִּיד! הִיא גּוּפַהּ גְּזֵירָה, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִיגְזוֹר גְּזֵירָה לִגְזֵירָה?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question: Then **let us also issue a decree** deeming substances covering **the minority** of **one's** body with regard to which **he is not particular** an interposition **due to** substances covering **the minority** of **his** body with regard to which **one is particular, or alternatively, due to** substances covering **the majority** of **his** body with regard to which **he is not particular. The Gemara answers: We do not issue that decree, because the *halakha* that deems both an interposition covering the minority of his body about which one is particular and an interposition covering the majority of his body about which one is not particular an interposition is itself a decree. Shall we** then **rise up and issue** one **decree to** prevent violation of another **decree?** In any case, these details with regard to interpositions are neither written nor alluded to in the Torah; rather, they are *halakhot* transmitted to Moses from Sinai.

The *gemara* then asks: why not go further and decree against *mi'ut she'eino makpid* — a minority covering on which one is not *makpid*? This category, while two degrees removed from the biblical case, can lead through intermediate steps to both other categories. *V'ligzar nami al mi'ut she'eino makpid*: one could argue this should be prohibited, since it can lead to the case of *mi'ut hamakpid* or to *rubo she'eino makpid*, both of which are themselves already rabbinic prohibitions.

The *gemara* responds: *hi gufa gezeirah, v'anan neiku v'nigzor gezeirah l'gezeirah?* — those intermediate categories are themselves already *gezeiros*, and one does not make a rabbinic decree upon a rabbinic decree. *Gezeirah l'gezeirah* is a well-established principle limiting the reach of rabbinic legislation. Crucially, it is the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* that establishes this boundary. The oral tradition from Sinai informs us that the biblical *chatzitzah* requires both *rov* and *makpid*, and thereby implicitly defines the upper limit of rabbinic extension: we can extend by one degree, but not by two.

This represents an interesting application of *halacha leMoshe miSinai* functioning as a leniency. Left to the plain reading of the *Torah*, one might have concluded that any interposition at all constitutes a *chatzitzah*. The *halacha leMoshe miSinai* narrows the biblical criterion, which in turn constrains how far the Sages could reasonably extend their decrees.

### Modern Minhag

In practice, the prevailing *minhag* is to remove even a *mi'ut she'eino makpid* before immersion in a *mikvah*, even though this category is neither biblically nor rabbinically prohibited. This is simply the accepted stringency. However, if there is a genuine reason that something cannot be removed, and the person is genuinely *eino makpid* — not merely reluctantly tolerant but truly indifferent — the immersion is valid. An illustrative case: a woman who wore a red *bendel*, a string associated with warding off *ayin hara*, and refused to remove it before immersion. Since it was a *mi'ut* and she was genuinely *eino makpid*, the immersion was valid despite the departing from *minhag*.

The most consequential and frequently encountered category in practice is *mi'ut hamakpid*. Covering the majority of the body is rarely at issue. But many practical situations involve minor coverings that may or may not rise to the level of *makpid*: nail polish residue, stitches, bandages, casts, braces, medical adhesives, and similar items. The definition of *makpid* is itself subject to dispute. Rashi, explaining *eino makpid*, says *havi kegufo* — when one is not *makpid*, the substance is treated as part of the body itself. This sets a demanding threshold for *eino makpid*: one must effectively have adopted the substance as part of oneself. Others understand *makpid* differently — as defined by the positive desire to have the substance removed. Under this view, anything one is indifferent to, or that one wants to remain, falls into the *eino makpid* category. This interpretive dispute generates wide practical variance in the *halachic* treatment of many common substances and medical devices.

### Disputed Categories

The final category, *rov she'eino makpid*, is difficult to encounter in practice when it comes to the body surface. The question becomes more tractable when one considers hair. Rashi, along with other *rishonim*, understands that the hair is treated as a distinct domain: just as *rov* and *makpid* apply to the body, *rov* of the hair independently constitutes a *chatzitzah*. This means that a substance covering the majority of one's hair could constitute a *chatzitzah* even if it does not cover the majority of one's body overall. Hair dye was raised as a potential example, though in practice hair dye penetrates and chemically bonds with the hair rather than sitting atop it, and therefore is not considered a *chatzitzah* regardless.

Rashi's ultimate position, clarified by the *gemara's* flow, is that the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* provides the *parameters* of the biblical *chatzitzah* rule — namely, that only *rov* combined with *makpid* is biblically prohibited. The rabbinic extensions to *rov she'eino makpid* and *mi'ut hamakpid* follow from there. A minority view among *rishonim* reads the *gemara* differently, holding that *rov umakpid* is itself derived from the *pasuk*, while the rabbinic categories of *mi'ut hamakpid* and *rov she'eino makpid* constitute the *halacha leMoshe miSinai*. Rashi objects to this reading: rabbinic enactments are not *halacha leMoshe miSinai*. Yet the objection is complicated by the fact that the term *halacha leMoshe miSinai* is occasionally applied in a looser sense to certain rabbinic enactments — creating some ambiguity in the precision of the categorization.

## Mechitzin Rules

### Mechitzin Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְחִיצּוֹת, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא נִינְהוּ! דְּאָמַר מָר: אָרוֹן תִּשְׁעָה וְכַפּוֹרֶת טֶפַח, הֲרֵי כָּאן עֲשָׂרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Partitions, they are** written **in the Torah,** as the fundamental principle that a partition ten handbreadths high establishes a separate domain is derived from the Torah. **As the Master said:** The Holy **Ark** in the Tabernacle was **nine** handbreadths high, as the verse states that its height was a cubit and a half. A cubit contains six handbreadths, so its height totaled nine handbreadths. **And the cover** atop the Ark was one **handbreadth, which** total **ten.** There is a tradition that the Divine Presence does not descend into the domain of this world, which is derived from the verse that states that the Divine Presence would reveal itself from above the cover of the Ark. Apparently, a partition of ten handbreadths creates a separate domain.

The *gemara* now addresses *mechitzin* — the rules governing partitions. Are these too biblical? The *gemara* says yes, and sources this in the *aron*: *aron tisha ve-chaporet tefach harei kan asara* — the *aron* stood nine *tefachim* high (as an *amah* and a half, comprising nine *tefachim* in a six-*tefach amah*), and the *kapores*, the lid, added a *tefach*, yielding a total height of ten *tefachim*. From this the rule of ten *tefachim* as the minimum height for a valid *mechitzah* is derived.

### Ten Tefachim Standard

The theological underpinning of this derivation is the famous *midrash* that God's presence never descended within ten *tefachim* of the ground, and Moshe never ascended within ten *tefachim* of the heavens. The *aron*, whose presence bridged the divine and human realms, was positioned at exactly ten *tefachim* — the threshold marking the separation of zones. From this geometry one infers that ten *tefachim* constitutes a distinct and complete vertical space, sufficient to divide two realms. A *mechitzah* that reaches ten *tefachim* therefore fully partitions the space below it.

The ten-*tefach* standard applies across a wide range of *halachic* domains: the minimum height of walls designating a *reshus hayachid* for *Shabbat*, the walls of a *sukkah*, the minimum height required for a *mezuzah* obligation, and many other contexts where a partition or enclosure is *halachically* significant.

### Rabbi Yehudah's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: אַמַּת בִּנְיָן בְּאַמָּה בַּת שִׁשָּׁה. אַמַּת כֵּלִים בְּאַמָּה בַּת חֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **The *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai is necessary only** according to the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who said: The cubit** mentioned with regard to **the building** of the Tabernacle and the Temple **was a** large **cubit of six** handbreadths, whereas **the cubit** mentioned with regard to the sacred **vessels was a cubit of five** handbreadths. According to this opinion, the Ark, which was a cubit and a half, and its cover, which was a handbreadth, measured eight and a half handbreadths. Therefore, nothing can be derived with regard to a partition of ten handbreadths.

The *gemara* notes that the derivation from the *aron* is specifically required for the position of Rabbi Yehudah. Rabbi Yehudah holds that the vessels of the *Mishkan*, including the *aron*, were measured with a five-*tefach amah*. Under his view, the *aron* at one and a half *amot* would be only seven and a half *tefachim* high, and together with the *kapores* would reach eight and a half *tefachim* — short of ten. This would eliminate the *aron* as a source for the ten-*tefach* standard. Hence, for Rabbi Yehudah, the ten-*tefach* minimum requires an independent *halachic* source.

### Rabbi Meir's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר: כׇּל הָאַמּוֹת הָיוּ בְּבֵינוֹנִית, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, כִּי אֲתַאי הִילְכְתָא — לְגוּד וּלְלָבוּד וּלְדוֹפֶן עֲקוּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: All the cubits were medium** ones, regular cubits of six handbreadths; **what can be said?** Apparently, according to his opinion, the laws governing partitions are explicitly stated in the Torah. **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** the *halakha* with regard to a partition of ten handbreadths is indeed written in the Torah. However, the ***halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **comes** to teach other *halakhot* concerning partitions, e.g., the *halakhot* of **extending** [***gode*],** according to which an existing partition is extended upward or downward to complete the requisite measure; **and** the *halakhot* of **joining [*lavud*],** according to which two solid surfaces are joined if they are separated by a gap of less than three handbreadths; **and** the *halakhot* of **the curved wall** of a *sukka*. A *sukka* is valid even if there are up to four cubits of invalid roofing, provided that this roofing is adjacent to one of the walls of the *sukka*. In that case, the invalid roofing is considered a bent extension of the wall. These concepts are certainly not written in the Torah.

Rabbi Meir, by contrast, holds that all *amot* in the *Mishkan* were of the standard (six-*tefach*) size, yielding a nine-*tefach aron* plus a *tefach kapores* equaling ten — which provides the derivation directly. For Rabbi Meir, then, one might ask: if ten *tefachim* can already be derived from the *aron*, what is the role of *halacha leMoshe miSinai* for *mechitzin*?

The *gemara* answers: *l'Rabbi Meir tisha hilchesa l'gud u'l'lavud u'l'dofen akuma* — according to Rabbi Meir, the *halacha leMoshe miSinai* is not needed for the ten-*tefach* minimum height itself, which is derived from the *aron*, but is required for the rules governing how a *mechitzah* operates conceptually. Three such rules are specified.

### Mechitza Operations

*Gud* (*gud achis* or *gud asik*) is the principle that a wall can be conceptually extended beyond its physical termination — either downward to the ground if it stops short, or upward if it does not reach the required height. A wall that physically covers only part of the required span is treated *as if* it continues. This principle was discussed in tractate *Shabbat* in the context of standing atop a pillar, where the side walls are considered as if extended upward to enclose the person.

*Lavud* is the principle that objects within three *tefachim* of one another are treated as if joined. If *s'chach* is within three *tefachim* of a wall, it is treated as connected to the wall. Similarly, if a partition does not quite reach the ground but leaves a gap of less than three *tefachim*, the gap is treated as closed.

*Dofen akuma* — the "bent wall" — is the rule by which invalid *s'chach* positioned alongside valid *s'chach* can be treated as a conceptual extension of the wall, "bending" toward the center to join the kosher area. This was discussed in relation to *sukkah* construction.

All three of these operative rules — *gud*, *lavud*, and *dofen akuma* — are *halacha leMoshe miSinai*. They define not the minimum dimensions of a *mechitzah* but the principles by which a *mechitzah's* spatial effect is determined.

## Lowering Mavoi Height

### Mishnah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה גָּבוֹהַּ מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה וּבָא לְמַעֲטוֹ, כַּמָּה מְמַעֵט?
>
> **Translation:** If the cross beam spanning the entrance to an alleyway **was higher than twenty cubits** from the ground **and one comes to diminish its** height, **how much must he diminish** it?

The *gemara* now returns to the *mishnah* that opened this section of *Eruvin*. The *mishnah* addresses a *mavoi* — an alleyway — whose opening is higher than twenty *amot*. Since the beam (*korah*) marking the entrance to the *mavoi* must be visible to those passing beneath it in order to fulfill its function as a *halachic* marker, a beam positioned higher than twenty *amot* is presumed to go unnoticed by passersby and is therefore invalid. The *mishnah* rules that in such a case one must lower the effective height of the opening.

### Lowering Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה מְמַעֵט?! כַּמָּה דִּצְרִיךְ לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא: רׇחְבּוֹ בְּכַמָּה? רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: טֶפַח, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is surprised by the question: **How much must he diminish** it? **The amount that he needs** in order to render its height less than twenty cubits. **Rather,** the space between the cross beam and the ground must, of course, be reduced to twenty cubits. However, when one raises the alleyway, **how much** must **the width** of the raised section be in order to render the alleyway fit for carrying within it? **Rav Yosef said:** One **handbreadth. Abaye said: Four** handbreadths.

The *gemara* now asks the obvious question: *kama mema'it?* — how much does one need to lower it? The answer, of course, is: enough to bring the opening to twenty *amot* or below — as much as necessary. But the *gemara* is asking a more specific question, expressed as *rochvo bekama* — how *wide* must the lowered area be? The scenario under discussion involves raising the floor of the *mavoi* near the entrance rather than physically lowering the beam itself. The Rif is of the view that one should simply lower the beam, and that is certainly the more straightforward approach. However, Rashi, Tosafot, and the mainstream view of the *rishonim* understand the *gemara* to be discussing the alternative approach of raising the floor — specifically, creating a raised platform directly beneath the beam's position. The practical question then becomes: how wide must this raised area be?

Rav Yosef rules that the raised area need only be a *tefach* wide — the width of the beam itself. Since the beam is assumed to have a minimum *halachic* width of one *tefach*, a raised platform of one *tefach* directly beneath it suffices to establish the effective height of the opening as measured from the raised area to the beam.

Abaye disagrees and requires a raised area of four *tefachim* in width. The dispute apparently concerns the minimum width necessary for a surface to constitute a meaningful, *halachically* significant floor. The *gemara* breaks off here, and the analysis of this dispute between Rav Yosef and Abaye — whether one *tefach* or four *tefachim* of width is required, and whether the raised area must extend the full length of the *mavoi* or only a portion of it — will continue in the next daf.
