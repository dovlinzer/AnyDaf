# Zevachim 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Onen at Night Setup

### Introduction

The daf begins at the bottom of 99b, with the words *rav mari amar* — Rav Mari's statement — serving as the springboard for the lengthy discussion that follows on 100a. The subject under examination is whether an *onen* (one who has lost a close relative and not yet buried them) may eat *korbanot* at night. The Gemara had inferred from the Mishnah that an *onen* is prohibited from eating *korbanot* only during the day, implying that on the night following the day of death, eating would be permitted. But the Gemara challenged this inference, noting that such leniency seemed to apply only to the *korban Pesach* and not to *korbanot* generally.

### Yom Misa/Kvura Distincti…

One of the central distinctions that emerged in the preceding discussion is the difference between *yom misa* — the day of death itself — and *yom kvura* — the day of burial, when that falls on a separate day. The Gemara established that whether the night following an *onen*'s day is forbidden depends on which of these two days is at issue. On the day of death, the following night is included in the prohibition; on the day of burial alone, when the death occurred on a prior day, the status may be different. This distinction will drive much of the analysis that follows.

### Shimon's Proof

The discussion also engaged with the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *aninus layla* — the mourning status at night — is not *de'oraisa*. His proof is that an *onen* may immerse in a *mikveh* and eat his *korban Pesach* at night, something that would be impossible if night-time *aninus* were biblical in force. This demonstrates, in Rabbi Shimon's view, that whatever prohibition applies at night is only rabbinic.

### Shimon's Contradiction

Yet Rabbi Shimon himself appears elsewhere to say the opposite — that one may not bring a *korban* on behalf of an *onen*, because only *keshe-hu shalem*, when a person is in a state of wholeness, may a *korban* be offered on their behalf. The word *shalem* alludes to *shelamim*, and Rabbi Shimon includes even the *korban Pesach* in this rule. So in one place he seems to say that a *Pesach* can be brought for an *onen* who will then eat it at night, and in another place he says it cannot be brought at all. The Gemara proceeds to work through several attempts to resolve this internal tension.

## Rav Mari's Answer

### Death/Burial Same Day

Rav Mari offers the first resolution at the top of 100a: *lo kashya* — the contradiction is not difficult. The key is precisely the distinction between *yom misa* and *yom kvura*. His answer turns on two different scenarios: one in which death and burial both occur on the fourteenth of Nisan, the day the *Pesach* is brought, and one in which only the burial takes place on the fourteenth while the death occurred on the thirteenth.

### Same Day Case

In the first case — *meis lo meiso be-yud daled ve-kivro be-yud daled* — the fourteenth is both the day of death and the day of burial. Because it is *yom misa*, the *aninus* is *de'oraisa*, and the Gemara states *yom misa tofes leilo mi-de'oraisa*: the day of death draws the following night into its prohibition on a biblical level. Since this *onen* will not be able to eat the *korban Pesach* that night, there is no point in bringing it on his behalf during the day. This, says Rav Mari, is where Rabbi Shimon rules that you do not bring the *Pesach* on behalf of the *onen*.

### Different Days Case

In the second case — *meis lo meiso be-yud gimmel ve-kivro be-yud daled* — the death occurred on the thirteenth and burial on the fourteenth, making the fourteenth only *yom kvura* and not *yom misa*. In this case, *aninus* is only *mi-derabbanan*, and *eino tofes leilo ela mi-derabbanan*: the night following is only rabbinically restricted. Since the night-time prohibition is merely rabbinic, we override it to permit eating the *korban Pesach*, and therefore we do permit bringing the *Pesach* on this person's behalf during the fourteenth. This is where Rabbi Shimon says the *Pesach* may be brought and eaten at night.

It should be noted that Rav Mari's answer focuses entirely on whether the *onen* will be able to eat the *korban Pesach* at night as the operative reason for permitting or forbidding its offering. This effectively sets aside the distinct concern raised by Rabbi Shimon in an earlier *baraita* — the idea of *shlemut*, that a *korban* cannot be brought for one who lacks wholeness — which has nothing inherently to do with nighttime eating. Tosafos raises this tension, and it may find its resolution in the answers that follow. For the moment, the discussion proceeds entirely on the question of whether eating at night is possible.

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

Rav Ashi challenges Rav Mari directly. The *baraita* quoted in support of Rabbi Shimon's position reads: *teda she-amru onen tovel ve-ochel es picho aval lo be-kodashim* — "know that they said an *onen* immerses and eats his *Pesach* but not other *kodashim*." This was Rabbi Shimon's proof to Rabbi Yehuda that *aninus layla* is not *de'oraisa*. But now Rav Mari has restricted Rabbi Shimon's proof to a case of *yom kvura* alone.

Rav Ashi presses: why couldn't Rabbi Yehuda simply respond that Rabbi Shimon's proof is irrelevant? The debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon about whether *aninus layla* is *de'oraisa* concerns *yom misa* — the day of death — as we know from the underlying derivation, which relates to the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, where the day of death and day of burial were one. When Rabbi Yehuda argues that the night is biblically forbidden, he is speaking of the night following *yom misa*. And when Rabbi Shimon brings his proof from the *onen* eating the *korban Pesach* at night, Rabbi Yehuda could simply retort: *kamina lach ana yom misa de'oraisa ve-amarta li at yom kvura derabbanan* — "I am speaking of *yom misa* and you are answering me with *yom kvura*!" The cases are not equivalent, and Rabbi Shimon's proof would fail to address Rabbi Yehuda's position. The Gemara concedes: *kashya* — this is indeed a difficulty. Rav Mari's answer does not fully resolve the contradiction.

## Abbaye's Answer

### Status Priority Principle

Abbaye offers the next resolution: *lo kashya* — it is not difficult. His answer accepts that we are indeed discussing *yom misa* throughout, directly engaging the scenario that Rav Ashi showed Rav Mari's answer could not handle. Abbaye's principle is this: when two competing statuses come into conflict, whichever took hold first takes precedence. The question of whether the *onen* status or the *korban Pesach* obligation governs this person depends on which one attached to him first.

### Death Before Chatzos

If the relative died before *chatzos* — before midday on the fourteenth of Nisan — then this person became an *onen* before any *Pesach* obligation existed for him. The obligation to bring the *korban Pesach* does not crystallize until midday on the fourteenth. In this case, *kodem chatzos she-lo echazi le-Pesach, chailei alei aninus* — before *chatzos* he was not yet fit for the *Pesach* obligation, and the *aninus* status took hold first. Consequently, the *Pesach* cannot be brought on his behalf; the *aninus* dominates, and Rabbi Shimon's rule applies: *keshe-hu shalem hu meivi*, only when he is in a state of wholeness.

### Death After Chatzos

If the relative died after *chatzos*, the situation reverses. By the time *chatzos* arrived, this person was already fit for and obligated in the *korban Pesach*. Then the relative died, and he became an *onen*. In this case, *lo chayla alei aninus* — the *aninus* does not take hold so as to push off the *Pesach* obligation that had already attached. The *korban Pesach* may still be brought on his behalf, and at night he eats it, since the night-time *aninus* is only *derabbanan* and yields to *achilas Pesach*.

This is Abbaye's elegant principle: it is not the intrinsic strength of *aninus* versus *Pesach* that determines the outcome, but rather the temporal sequence — which came first? Abbaye then proceeds to demonstrate this principle from *baraitot* dealing with *tumah* and the *Pesach* obligation, bringing proof that the same logic governs competing obligations generally.

### Yosef Story

The *baraita* opens with the general rule that a *kohen* has a *mitzvah* — indeed an obligation — to become *tamei* for his immediate relatives at death, even against his will. The *baraita* states *lo ratza, metamin oto al korcho*: if he refuses, his fellow *kohanim* force him. The story of Yosef the *kohen* illustrates this dramatically: his wife died on *erev Pesach*, and he did not wish to become *tamei*, both out of regard for the sanctity of his *kehuna* and because it would prevent him from bringing the *korban Pesach*. Nevertheless, his fellow *kohanim* voted and forcibly made him *tamei*, and he was therefore unable to bring the *Pesach*. This *baraita* implies that the obligation to become *tamei* for one's relative overrides even the obligation of *korban Pesach*.

### Nazir Baraita

Yet a second *baraita*, dealing with a *nazir*, seems to say the opposite. A *nazir* cannot become *tamei* even for immediate relatives. The *baraita* layers on additional statuses — a *kohen* and *nazir*, then a *kohen gadol* and *nazir* — teaching that even the combined weight of these stringent restrictions against *tumah* does not exempt one from the obligation of *meis mitzvah*, a body left without anyone to bury it. All of these layers were added precisely to show that *meis mitzvah* overrides everything, even the most severe prohibitions against *tumah*.

But crucially, the Gemara reads the mention of *korban Pesach* in this *baraita* — where the text describes someone on his way to *shecht* his *Pesach* and give *bris milah* to his son — as implying that the *Pesach* obligation by itself would prevent one from becoming *tamei* for a relative, even absent *nazir* and *kohen gadol* statuses. This is the reading the Gemara adopts, though Tosafos notes it is not straightforwardly the *pshat* of the *baraita*, since giving a *bris milah* to one's son does not generally override the obligation to bury a relative. The Gemara brackets that difficulty and focuses on the implication regarding *Pesach*.

So the contradiction stands: the story of Yosef the *kohen* says the obligation to become *tamei* for a relative overrides the *Pesach* obligation, while this *baraita* implies that the *Pesach* obligation prevents one from becoming *tamei* for a relative. Which is it?

The Gemara suggests perhaps the contradiction reflects the debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yishmael holds that becoming *tamei* for a relative is a *reshus* — a permission, not an obligation — while Rabbi Akiva holds it is a *chova*, a genuine obligation. Perhaps the *baraita* favoring *Pesach* is Rabbi Yishmael, and the story of Yosef is Rabbi Akiva. But the Gemara rejects this: the very *baraita* that seemed to favor *Pesach* — the one about the *nazir* going to *shecht* his *Pesach* — begins explicitly with Rabbi Akiva's view, stating there is an obligation to become *tamei* for a relative. So both *baraitot* represent Rabbi Akiva, and the contradiction remains.

### Baraita Reconciliation

Abbaye resolves it as follows: *shema mina kan kodem chatzos, kan achar chatzos* — it must be that the two *baraitot* describe different temporal scenarios. The story of Yosef the *kohen*, where the obligation to become *tamei* for his wife overrode the *Pesach*, occurred in the morning of *erev Pesach*, before *chatzos*. At that point the *Pesach* obligation had not yet attached, and so the prior obligation of burial took precedence. The *baraita* about the *nazir* going to *shecht* his *Pesach*, where the *Pesach* overrides *tumah* for a relative, describes a situation after *chatzos*, when the *Pesach* obligation had already crystallized — and therefore the *Pesach* holds its ground.

### Inference to Onen

If this principle governs the relationship between the *Pesach* obligation and *tumah* — whichever comes first prevails — then by the same logic it governs the relationship between the *Pesach* obligation and *aninus* status. If one became an *onen* before *chatzos*, the *aninus* came first, and Rabbi Shimon rules that the *Pesach* is not brought on his behalf. If one became an *onen* after *chatzos*, the *Pesach* obligation came first, and the *aninus* does not push it off — the *Pesach* is brought, and at night, since *aninus layla* is only *derabbanan*, it is overridden and he eats. This is Abbaye's complete answer.

## Rava's Answer

### Bring vs. Eat

Rava offers a third and independently elegant resolution. *Idi ve'idi achar chatzos* — in both cases of Rabbi Shimon's seemingly contradictory statements, we are speaking of after *chatzos*. Even after *chatzos*, Rava holds, if one becomes an *onen*, the *aninus* would ordinarily override the *Pesach* obligation. The distinction Rava draws is not temporal but procedural: it is the difference between the stage before *shechita* and *zerika* and the stage after.

### Before Shechita

*Kodem she-shachtu ve-zarku alav* — before the *korban Pesach* has been slaughtered and its blood sprinkled, if one is an *onen*, the *Pesach* is not brought on his behalf. He is not *shalem*, and this is where Rabbi Shimon says one does not bring a *korban* on behalf of an *onen*. The *shechita* and *zerika* should not take place.

### After Shechita

*Le-achar she-shachtu ve-zarku alav* — after the *korban Pesach* has already been slaughtered and its blood sprinkled, the question shifts. Can this *onen* eat the *Pesach* that night? The answer, according to Rabbi Shimon, is yes. Where Rabbi Shimon says an *onen* immerses and eats his *Pesach* at night, he is speaking of a case where the *Pesach* was already brought — perhaps improperly, but the *korban* is *kasher* after the fact — and now the only remaining question is whether he may eat it that night. Since *aninus layla* is only *derabbanan* for Rabbi Shimon, and eating serves the fulfillment of the *mitzvah*, we allow it. This reading is quite beautiful in its precision: Rabbi Shimon's two statements address two entirely different questions, and there is no contradiction at all.

### Rav Adda's Challenge

Rav Adda bar Matna challenges Rava: once the *shechita* and *zerika* have already been performed, *mah de-hava hava* — what was done was done. The offering is complete. Why should we then permit this *onen* to continue down a problematic path by eating at night? Let him return to his *aninus* status and not eat, since the major acts of the *korban* have already been accomplished.

### Eating Me'akeiv

Ravina responds: *achilas pesachim me'akeiv* — eating the *korban Pesach* is essential and constitutive of fulfilling the *mitzvah*. One is not *yotzei* the obligation of *korban Pesach* merely by virtue of the blood being sprinkled. The eating itself is required for fulfillment of the *mitzvah*. This is based on the teaching of Rabba bar Rav Huna, which will be cited shortly. Rava affirms this response to Rav Adda bar Matna, telling him: *tzayis mah de-kamar lach rabach* — pay close attention to what your teacher Rav Huna is teaching in defense of this position.

### Pesach Eating Centrality

This focus on eating as *me'akeiv* reflects a fundamental feature of the *korban Pesach* that sets it apart from all other *korbanot*. In every other *korban*, the central act is *zrikas ha-dam*, the sprinkling of the blood; eating and burning of the fats are secondary. The *korban Pesach*, by contrast, is brought *for the purpose of being eaten*. This is why, for example, *tumah hutra be-tzibur* — when a communal *korban* is brought despite the community being *tamei* — the dispensation normally permits the offering but not the eating. The *korban Pesach* is unique: because it is fundamentally an offering brought in order to be eaten, the dispensation of *tumah hutra be-tzibur* for the *Pesach* extends even to permit the eating. Within this framework, there is a debate about whether, *be-di'eved*, failing to eat would nonetheless leave the *korban* as having been validly offered. The Gemara here, taken at face value, indicates the answer is no: eating is truly indispensable. Therefore, once the *Pesach* has been brought for this *onen*, we must permit him to eat it at night, overriding the rabbinic *aninus layla*, because the eating is essential to the fulfillment of the *mitzvah*.

## Yom Shmuah Baraita

### Baraita Text

The Gemara now introduces the *baraita* from which Rabba bar Rav Huna derives that *achilas pesachim me'akeiv*. The *baraita* states: *yom shmua ke-yom kvura le-mitzvas shiva u-shloshim* — the day on which one hears news of a relative's death (within the first thirty days) is treated like the day of burial for the purposes of *shiva* and *shloshim*. That is the day from which the seven and thirty days of mourning begin for someone who did not learn of the death at the time it occurred.

However, *ve-le-achilas pesachim ke-yom likut atzamot* — for the purpose of eating the *korban Pesach*, *yom shmua* is treated not like *yom kvura* but like *yom likut atzamot*, the day of the gathering of bones. The practice of *likut atzamot* refers to the secondary burial: after the body had decomposed, the bones would be collected and interred in a small niche within a burial cave. That day carried a secondary *yom kvura* status, though of lighter weight than the original burial. The *baraita* thus places *yom shmua* in this lighter category for purposes of eating the *korban Pesach*.

The implicit logic is revealing. On *yom kvura* proper, the *baraita* implies, one cannot eat the *korban Pesach* that night. On *yom shmua* and *yom likut atzamot*, which are of lesser *aninus* weight, one can. This teaches something important about the relative force of *aninus* and *Pesach* eating in different configurations. The Gemara then spells out the inference: *miklal de-yom kvura afilu erev nami lo achil* — from the fact that the *baraita* had to invoke the lighter category of *likut atzamot*, we learn that on *yom kvura* itself, even the night following, one cannot eat the *korban Pesach*. This is a departure from what had been suggested earlier — that *yom kvura* without *yom misa* would be only rabbinic and would yield to *Pesach* — but it reflects a different tannaitic position.

The *baraita* then continues: *echad ze ve-echad ze tovel ve-ochel be-kodashim la-erev* — whether it is the case of *yom shmua* or of *yom likut atzamot*, one may immerse in a *mikveh* and eat *kodashim* that night.

### Internal Contradiction

This creates a sharp internal tension. The first half of the *baraita* implied that on *yom kvura* one cannot even eat the *korban Pesach* at night; the second half, with its *echad ze ve-echad ze*, appears to say that whether you are speaking of *yom kvura* or *yom likut atzamot*, you may eat *kodashim* generally that night. But *kodashim* are subject to even stricter standards than *korban Pesach* — if you cannot eat the more leniently treated *Pesach*, you certainly cannot eat *kodashim* generally. So how can the second half permit *kodashim* at night in what seems to include *yom kvura*?

### Rav Chisda's Answer

Rav Chisda offers the first resolution: *tannai hi* — this is a dispute between *tannaim* that has been incorporated into a single *baraita*. The first half of the *baraita* and the second half represent two different tannaitic opinions regarding the night following *yom kvura*, and they were simply compiled together without marking the transition. One *tanna* holds that *yom kvura* extends into the night and forbids even the *Pesach*; the other holds it does not, and one may eat *kodashim* generally that night.

### Rabba Bar Rav Huna

Rabba bar Rav Huna offers a different resolution: *lo kashya* — there is no real contradiction. *Kan she-shama shmuah al meto samuch le-shkiat ha-chamma, ve-chen she-liktu lo atzamot samuch le-shkiat ha-chamma, ve-chen she-mes lo mes u-kvaro samuch le-shkiat ha-chamma* — the first half of the *baraita*, which restricts eating, addresses cases where the *shmuah*, *likut atzamot*, or *kvurah* occurred close to nightfall — that is, when these events happened at night, after the sun had already set on the fourteenth. The second half of the *baraita*, permitting *kodashim* that night, addresses the standard case: when the burial, *shmuah*, or *likut atzamot* happened during the day of the fourteenth. In that case, the night follows the day but does not carry the full force of the day's *aninus* into it, and one may eat *kodashim* that night.

To work through the cases: when the burial or *likut atzamot* or *shmuah* happened during the fourteenth — meaning it was still daytime — then at nightfall one may eat *kodashim*. When these events happened at night — meaning the night of the fourteenth itself was when the *aninus* first took effect — then the *aninus* is current and active during the very time of eating, which changes the analysis. On *yom kvura* occurring at night, one cannot eat even *korban Pesach*. On *yom shmuah* or *yom likut atzamot* occurring at night, the *Pesach* obligation overrides, and one may eat it, because these are lighter *aninus* statuses; but general *kodashim* one may not eat.

### Rav Ashi's Reading

Rav Ashi offers yet another reading of the *baraita*. He suggests that when the *baraita* says *echad ze ve-echad ze tovel ve-ochel be-kodashim la-erev*, the two cases of "this and that" refer not to *yom kvura* and *yom likut atzamot*, but only to *yom shmuah* and *yom likut atzamot*. On that reading, the *baraita* never claims *yom kvura* permits eating *kodashim* at night. The *baraita* first establishes that *yom shmuah* is like *yom likut atzamot* for *Pesach*, and then concludes that both *yom shmuah* and *yom likut atzamot* permit eating *kodashim* at night. *Yom kvura* is never included in the permissive ruling of the second clause.

### Rejection of Rav Ashi

The Gemara rejects Rav Ashi's reading. Since the *baraita* had already equated *yom shmuah* and *yom likut atzamot* in the first clause, if the second clause merely wanted to say that both of these allow eating *kodashim* at night, it should have said *zeh ve-zeh*, "both of them," which would simply be confirming a previously established equivalence. The language *echad ze ve-echad ze* — "whether this one or that one" — is inclusive and expansive, drawing in all the cases previously discussed, including *yom kvura*. The Gemara therefore concludes that Rav Ashi's reading is *bidusa* — untenable — and the resolution of Rabba bar Rav Huna, distinguishing between whether the *aninus*-triggering event occurred during the day or after nightfall, remains as the accepted answer. And from that resolution, the Gemara affirms: *shema mina achilas pesachim me'akeves* — eating the *korban Pesach* is constitutive and essential, for it is only on account of that that we allow the *onen* to eat when the *aninus* triggered at night but was of a lighter variety, such as *yom shmuah* or *yom likut atzamot*.

## Tanna Kamma/Rabbi Debate

### Duration of Onen

The Gemara now turns to a *baraita* that directly addresses the duration of *aninus* status: *ad masai misonen alav* — until when does one remain in the status of *onen* for a relative who has died?

### Tanna Kamma vs. Rabbi

The *tanna kamma* says: *kol ha-yom* — the entire day. Rabbi says: *kol zman she-lo nikvar* — as long as the person has not been buried. On the surface, Rabbi's formulation seems to shorten the period: once burial takes place, even on the day of death itself, the *aninus* ends.

### Initial Problem

The Gemara raises an immediate difficulty. If both opinions are discussing *yom misa*, neither view makes sense in context. Everyone agrees that *aninus* extends into the night at least rabbinically — *mi ika de-leis lei de-yom misa tofes leila mi-derabbanan*? And furthermore, Rabbi's formulation — that *aninus* ends at burial — cannot mean that burial on the day of death itself would terminate *aninus* immediately, for the verse states that the day following a death is like a day of bitterness, implying the entire first day is included. So how can this *baraita* be understood?

### Rav Sheshes' Attempt

Rav Sheshes proposes that the *baraita* is not addressing *yom misa* at all, but rather *yom kvura* when it does not coincide with *yom misa*. The debate between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi is about how long *aninus* lasts on such a day: the *tanna kamma* says the day, Rabbi says only until the burial is complete.

### Rav Yosef's Challenge

Rav Yosef challenges this reading. The *baraita* discussed earlier — the one about *yom shmuah* and *likut atzamot* — implied, by limiting its permission to eat *kodashim* at night to those two lighter categories, that on *yom kvura* proper one cannot eat *kodashim* even at night. If that is so, then there must be a tannaitic position that *yom kvura* does extend into the night for the prohibition of *kodashim*. But Rav Sheshes' reading of this *baraita* would have both the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi agreeing that *yom kvura* does not extend into the night, since the *tanna kamma* says "the day" and Rabbi says "until burial," and neither explicitly includes the night. That would leave us without any tannaitic position that extends *yom kvura* into the night — contradicting the inference from the earlier *baraita*.

### Reinterpreted Debate

The Gemara therefore restructures the *baraita*. *Ela taritz hachi* — read it as follows: the *tanna kamma* says *kol oso ha-yom belo leilo* — the entire day, but without the night. The words *ve-leilo* are implicitly present in the *tanna kamma*'s position, even though not explicitly stated. The *tanna kamma* thus holds that *yom kvura*, when not coinciding with *yom misa*, carries *aninus* only for the day and not the night. Rabbi, by contrast, says *kol zman she-lo nikvar* — and extends this to: once buried, *tofes leilo*, it includes the following night. On this reading, Rabbi is the *machmir*: he holds that *yom kvura* draws the night into the *aninus* prohibition for *kodashim*.

This restructuring is necessitated because the Gemara also introduces a later *baraita* that shows Rabbi holding an even more expansive position: *kol zman she-eino nikvar, afilu mikan ve-ad asara yamim* — as long as the person has not been buried, even if that takes ten days, the *aninus* status continues, *divrei Rabbi*. The *chakhamim*, by contrast, say *aninus* applies only on the day of death. This second *baraita* reveals that Rabbi is consistently the *machmir* in extending *aninus*, which confirms that in the first *baraita* as well, Rabbi should be understood as the stringent position, not the lenient one. The Gemara therefore inverts the plain reading of the debate to make Rabbi the one extending *aninus* into the night of *yom kvura*.

### Final Position

The daf concludes with the following picture. Rabbi Shimon holds that *aninus layla* is *derabbanan*, that the night following the day of death is only rabbinically restricted. Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and holds it is *de'oraisa*. Their debate concerns *yom misa*. As for *yom kvura* that is not *yom misa*, the Gemara has now established that there is itself a tannaitic dispute: the *tanna kamma* holds that *yom kvura* does not extend into the night for purposes of the prohibition of *kodashim*, while Rabbi holds that it does. When it comes to *likut atzamot* and *yom shmuah*, both are acknowledged to be of lighter *aninus* weight and do not extend into the night for *kodashim*, though for *korban Pesach* specifically, the eating — which is *me'akeiv* — can override even the *aninus* status of *yom kvura* at night, and certainly the lighter statuses.
