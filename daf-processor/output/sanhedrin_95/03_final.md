# Sanhedrin 95 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chizkiyahu & Torah

### Chizkiyahu Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה עָשָׂה? נָעַץ חֶרֶב עַל פֶּתַח בֵּית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, וְאָמַר: כׇּל מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה יִדָּקֵר בְּחֶרֶב זוֹ. בָּדְקוּ מִדָּן וְעַד בְּאֵר שֶׁבַע, וְלֹא מָצְאוּ עַם הָאָרֶץ. מִגְּבָת וְעַד אַנְטִיפְרַס, וְלֹא מָצְאוּ תִּינוֹק וְתִינוֹקֶת, אִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה, שֶׁלֹּא הָיוּ בְּקִיאִים בְּהִלְכוֹת טוּמְאָה וְטׇהֳרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **What did** Hezekiah **do** to ensure Torah study? **He inserted a sword at the entrance of the study hall and said: Anyone who does not engage in Torah** study **shall be stabbed with this sword.** As a result, **they searched from Dan** in the north **to Beersheba** in the south, **and did not find an ignoramus.** They searched **from Gevat to Antipatris and did not find a male child, or a female child,** or **a man, or a woman who was not expert** even **in the** complex ***halakhot* of ritual purity and impurity.**

Today's *daf* is 95, and we are picking up at the bottom of 94b, in the middle of the *aggadetot* concerning *Chizkiyahu HaMelech* and the well-known teaching that he might have been the *Mashiach* had he only sung *shira*. The *Gemara* then raises the possibility that there may have been other reasons, known only to God, why he was not the *Mashiach*. What follows is the *Gemara's* own elaboration of why *Chizkiyahu* came so close to that distinction — and the answer it emphasizes is not merely that he was a righteous king, but that he returned the entire people to *Torah*. This aligns with the *Rambam's* conception of Messianic times, wherein the king *Mashiach* returns the people to righteousness, to the *mitzvot*, and to the worship of God. Seen through this lens, the *Gemara* is laying out what *Chizkiyahu HaMelech* actually accomplished: a universal return to *Torah* study. Everybody — man and woman, boy and girl — knew even the most obscure areas of *Torah*, including all of the laws of *tuma v'tahara*.

### Universal Torah Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל אוֹתוֹ הַדּוֹר הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יְחַיֶּה אִישׁ עֶגְלַת בָּקָר וּשְׁתֵּי צֹאן וְגוֹ׳״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִהְיֶה כׇל מָקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה שָּׁם אֶלֶף גֶּפֶן בְּאֶלֶף כָּסֶף לַשָּׁמִיר וְלַשַּׁיִת יִהְיֶה״. אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֶלֶף גֶּפֶן בְּאֶלֶף כָּסֶף, לַשָּׁמִיר וְלַשַּׁיִת יִהְיֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it is **about that generation** that the prophet **says: "And it shall come to pass in that day, that a man shall nourish a young calf and two sheep.** And it shall come to pass, from the abundance of milk that they produce, he shall eat butter, for butter and honey shall everyone eat, everyone who remains in the midst of the land" (Isaiah 7:21–22). **And** the prophet continues and **says: "And it shall come to pass in that day that every place where there were one thousand vines for one thousand silver coins, it shall be for briars and thorns"** (Isaiah 7:23). **Although one thousand vines are worth one thousand silver coins** and one could earn substantial profits through agricultural labor, the fields **will** grow **briars and thorns** due to neglect. The people of that generation were devoted to the study of Torah and engaged in labor only minimally to sustain themselves.

The *Gemara* paints a remarkable picture of *Chizkiyahu's* generation. The universal mastery of *Torah* was not confined to scholars; it was a society-wide phenomenon. Even the most recondite areas of law were known by all.

### Abundance & Neglect

The *Gemara* then cites the verse (Isaiah 7:21): *v'haya bayom hahu yichye ish eglat bakar u'shtei tzon* — on that day a person will live from a single calf and two sheep. And further (Isaiah 7:23): *v'haya bayom hahu yihye kol makom asher yihye sham elef gefen b'elef kessef l'shamir v'lashayit yihye* — any place where there had been a thousand vines worth a thousand pieces of silver will be given over to the worm. At first glance this hardly sounds like a blessing. The *Gemara* explains: even though the streets were lined with gold and the vines were worth fortunes, the people simply left it all to be consumed by bugs and worms, because they were entirely absorbed in sitting in the *Beis Medrash*. They had no interest in material or monetary pursuits. The single calf and two sheep is similarly read: people were not raising whole flocks because they were wholly uninterested in material accumulation. All they were doing — and one must be careful here not to project this forward to Messianic times, since the *Gemara* is reading these verses about *Chizkiyahu's* own era — was sitting and learning *Torah*.

## Spoils & Acquisition

### Spoils Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֻסַּף שְׁלַלְכֶם אֹסֶף הֶחָסִיל״. אָמַר לָהֶם נָבִיא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: אִסְפוּ שְׁלַלְכֶם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לִבְזוֹז אוֹ לַחְלוֹק? אָמַר לָהֶם: ״כְּאֹסֶף הֶחָסִיל״. מָה אֹסֶף הֶחָסִיל – כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד לְעַצְמוֹ. אַף שְׁלַלְכֶם – כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד לְעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"And your spoils shall be gathered like the gathering of the locusts;** as the advance of the locusts shall he advance" (Isaiah 33:4). **The prophet said to the Jewish people: Gather your spoils** from the army of Sennacherib. **They said to him:** Are we **to pillage** the spoils, each person for himself, **or** are we **to divide** the spoils with the monarchy? **He said to them:** Gather the spoils **like the gathering by the locusts. Just as** in **the gathering by the locusts, each and every one** of the locusts takes food **for itself, so too,** in gathering **your spoils, each and every one** of you shall take spoils **for himself.**

The *Gemara* transitions now to the aftermath of *Sancheriv's* defeat, citing the verse: *v'atshav shalchem osef hachasil* — you will gather in the booty as the gathering of the locust. The *navi* said to Israel: *isfu shalchem* — go take in the booty, strip the fallen soldiers, take in the wealth of the troops. The people asked: *livoz o lachlok* — is this to spoil freely or to distribute? *Rashi* explains: the question was whether each person could keep what he took for himself, or whether the booty had to be divided equally — or perhaps distributed to the poor. Could they simply keep it?

### Locust Metaphor

The *navi* answered: *k'osef hachasil* — like the gathering of the locust. *Mah osef hachasil kol echad v'echad l'atzmo* — every locust for itself. *Af shalchem kol echad v'echad l'atzmo* — so too with your booty, every man for himself, keep what you take.

### Ten Tribes Spoils

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: וַהֲלֹא מָמוֹן עֲשֶׂרֶת הַשְּׁבָטִים מְעוֹרָב בּוֹ? אָמַר לָהֶם: ״כְּמַשַּׁק גֵּבִים שׁוֹקֵק בּוֹ״. מָה גֵּבִים הַלָּלוּ מַעֲלִין אֶת הָאָדָם מִטּוּמְאָה לְטׇהֳרָה, אַף מָמוֹנָם שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל – כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּפַל בְּיַד אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם, מִיָּד טִיהַר. כִּדְרַב פָּפָּא, דְּאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עַמּוֹן וּמוֹאָב טָהֲרוּ בְּסִיחוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to him:** Since the army of Sennacherib came to Jerusalem after its conquest of the kingdom of Israel, **isn't the property of the ten tribes intermingled with it,** and therefore, gathering the spoils would be robbery? **He said to them: "As the advance of the locusts [*gevim*] shall he advance"** (Isaiah 33:4). **Just as these pools** of water **elevate a person up from** a state of **ritual impurity to** a state of **purity** through immersion, **so too the property of the Jewish people, once it falls into the hands of gentiles, it immediately purifies** the property, in the sense that it is no longer considered robbery to take it, as its owners despair of its recovery. This is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Pappa, as Rav Pappa says:** The property of **Ammon and Moab was purified through** the conquest of **Sihon.** Although the Torah rendered it prohibited to conquer the land of Ammon and Moab, once Sihon conquered their land, it was permitted for the Jewish people to conquer it.

The people then raised a further objection: *halo mammonot shel shvatim ur'vavu* — but *Sancheriv* had exiled the ten tribes, and some of the wealth he was carrying had originally belonged to those Israelites. Could they really take what rightfully belonged to their fellow Jews, wherever they might be?

### Conquest Nullifies

The *navi* responded with the image of *geivim*: *k'meshek geivim shokek bo* — like the roar of water rushing into pools. Just as a pool of water transforms a person from *tamei* to *tahor*, so too the money of Israel that had fallen into the hands of the nations has been purified — *mityaher* — and is free to be taken.

What does this mean practically? The parenthetical note in the *Gemara* — which appears to be a later gloss, as indicated by its placement in parentheses — invokes *Rav Papa's* principle: *Ammon u'Moav ki'aharu b'Sichon*. The lands of *Ammon* and *Moav* could not ordinarily be taken by Israel. Yet once *Sichon* had conquered them, those lands were no longer considered lands of *Ammon* and *Moav* — they became *Sichon's* lands, and Israel could take them. This is how nations always acquire territory: by conquest. Once a conquering army takes over, the land stops belonging to the original nation.

### Yeush & Property

The principle here is not simply that a non-Jew taking from a Jew automatically severs the Jew's ownership. The *Gemara* in *Bava Kamma* makes clear that normal laws of property ownership apply regardless of who is doing the taking. If a non-Jew steals something from a Jew, the original owner remains the owner unless certain conditions are met — a combination of *yeush* (the owner's relinquishment of hope of recovering the item) and *shinui reshus* (a change of legal domain). The same analysis applies whether the thief is Jewish or not.

The specific context that does sever ownership is when property is taken by a conquering army or by a *sikarikon* — a strongman operating entirely outside the law — from whom there is genuinely no legal recourse. In that situation the reality is so total, the loss so complete, that one truly stops owning the property. A prayer said on behalf of recovering it would be a *brachah levatalah*. The point the *Gemara* is making, then, is not narrowly about non-Jews taking from Jews; it applies equally to nations taking from nations. Here, once *Sancheriv* conquered and carried off the wealth of the ten tribes, those tribes lost their monetary claims, and Israel could legitimately take it.

## Sancheriv's Campaign

### Ten Stages Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: עֶשֶׂר מַסָּעוֹת נָסַע אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בָּא עַל עַיַּת, עָבַר בְּמִגְרוֹן, לְמִכְמָשׂ יַפְקִיד כֵּלָיו. עָבְרוּ מַעְבָּרָה, גֶּבַע מָלוֹן לָנוּ, חָרְדָה הָרָמָה, גִּבְעַת שָׁאוּל נָסָה. צַהֲלִי קוֹלֵךְ בַּת גַּלִּים, הַקְשִׁיבִי לַיְשָׁה, עֲנִיָּה עֲנָתוֹת. נָדְדָה מַדְמֵנָה, יֹשְׁבֵי הַגֵּבִים הֵעִיזוּ. עוֹד הַיּוֹם בְּנֹב לַעֲמֹד, יְנֹפֵף יָדוֹ הַר בַּת צִיּוֹן גִּבְעַת יְרוּשָׁלִָם״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says: That wicked** Sennacherib **traveled ten journeys on that day, as it is stated: "He is come to Aiath, he is passed through Migron; at Michmas he deposited his baggage. They passed [*averu*] Mabara; they have taken up their lodging at Geba; Ramah trembles; Gibeath Shaul has fled. Cry with a shrill voice, daughter of Gallim; hearken, Laish; poor Anathoth. Madmenah is in flight; the inhabitants of Gebim flee to cover. This very day shall he halt at Nov; he shall shake his hand against the mountain of the daughter of Zion, the hill of Jerusalem"** (Isaiah 10:28–32). He traveled to all these places on the same day.

*Amar Rav Huna eser masa'ot nasa Sancheriv b'oso hayom* — Rav Huna teaches that on the very day *Sancheriv* was approaching *Yerushalayim* to lay siege to it, he made ten journeys. The *Gemara* then enumerates these stages based on the verses in Isaiah 10: he came to *Ayas* (one); he passed through *Migron* (two); at *Michmash* he left his weapons (three); they passed through the pass (four); they lodged at *Geva* (five); *Ramah* trembled (six); *Givas Shaul* fled (seven). Then, accounting for the *navi's* address to the Jewish people — *tzahali koleich Bas Gallim* — this is not counted as a stage; it is the prophet speaking to *Knesset Yisrael*, calling her *Bas Gallim*, daughter of those who performed *mitzvot* like the waves of the sea.

### Lion's Six Names

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם אֲרִי, הָכָא לַיִשׁ! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שִׁשָּׁה שֵׁמוֹת יֵשׁ לַאֲרִי, אֵלּוּ הֵן: אֲרִי, כְּפִיר, לָבִיא, לַיִשׁ, שַׁחַל, שַׁחַץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Are these** matters **comparable? There,** in Jeremiah, Nebuchadnezzar is called ***ari*,** as the verse refers to him with the term *arye*, and **here,** in Isaiah, Nebuchadnezzar is called ***laish*.** The Gemara explains that the verses are not contradictory, as **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There are six** Hebrew **terms for the lion,** and **they are: *Ari*, *kefir*, *lavi*, *layish*, *shaḥal*, *shaḥatz*.**

The *Gemara* asks: *mi dami?* — how is this consistent? When speaking of *Nevuchadnetzar*, the verse uses the word *aryeh* for lion, but here the verse uses *layish*. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan shisha shemot yesh la'ari* — Rabbi Yochanan teaches that a lion has six names: *ari*, *kfir*, *lavi*, *layish*, *shachal*, and *shachatz*. These are simply synonyms, and there is therefore no contradiction.

### Nov's Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״עוֹד הַיּוֹם בְּנֹב לַעֲמֹד״? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר מֵעֲוֹנָהּ שֶׁל נוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **"This very day shall he halt at Nov"** (Isaiah 10:32)? **Rav Huna says: That** was the final **day** that **remained from** the punishment that the Jewish people received from **the sin of Nov** (see I Samuel, chapter 22).

*Amar Rav Huna*: the verse *od hayom b'Nov la'amod* — still this day he will stand at *Nov* — refers to the very same day on which *Sancheriv* made his ten journeys toward *Yerushalayim*. That day was the one on which punishment for the sin of *Nov* was still outstanding. *Nov ir hakohanim* had been destroyed in the days of *Shaul*, and that sin had not yet been fully accounted for. The anniversary of that destruction was the day *Sancheriv* drew close. *Oto hayom nishtayer me-avona shel Nov* — that day still carried the weight of the sin of *Nov*.

### Astrologers' Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ כַּלְדָּאֵי: אִי אָזְלַתְּ הָאִידָּנָא – יָכְלַתְּ לַהּ, וְאִי לָא – לָא יָכְלַתְּ לַהּ. אוֹרְחָא דִּבְעָא לְסַגּוֹיֵי בְּעַשְׂרָה יוֹמֵי, סַגֵּא בְּחַד יוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The astrologers said to** Sennacherib: **If you go** and conquer them **now, you will overcome** the Jewish people; **and if not, you will not overcome** the Jewish people. The Gemara relates: **He walked** and traversed **in one day a road** upon **which one must walk for ten days** in order to traverse it.

*Amru leh Kalda'ei* — the Chaldean astrologers told *Sancheriv*: *ei azlat ha'idna yachlat lah* — if you go today, you will be victorious. *V'ei la, lo yachlat lah* — if not, not. The journey normally required ten days. *Saga b'chad yoma* — he traveled it in a single day, so intent was he on seizing the propitious moment. This is what accounts for the ten journeys of a single day: it was a ten-day march compressed into one.

### Sancheriv at Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְטוֹ לִירוּשְׁלֶם, שְׁדוֹ לֵיהּ בִּיסְתַּרְקֵי, עַד דִּסְלֵיק וִיתֵיב מֵעִילָּוֵי שׁוּרָא, עַד דְּחַזְיַוהּ לְכוּלַּהּ יְרוּשְׁלֶם. כִּי חַזְיַיהּ, אִיזּוּטַר בְּעֵינֵיהּ. אָמַר: הֲלָא דָּא הִיא קַרְתָּא דִּירוּשְׁלֶם דַּעֲלַהּ אַרְגֵּישִׁית כֹּל מַשְׁירְיָתַי, וַעֲלַהּ כַּבֵּשִׁית כֹּל מְדִינָתָא? הֲלָא הִיא זְעֵירָא וְחַלָּשָׁא מִכֹּל כַּרְכֵי עַמְמַיָּא דְּכַבֵּשִׁית בִּתְקוֹף יְדַי? עֲלַהּ וְקָם וּמָנֵיד בְּרֵישֵׁיהּ, מוֹבֵיל וּמַיְיתֵי בִּידֵיהּ עַל טוּר בֵּית מַקְדְּשָׁא דִּבְצִיּוֹן וְעַל עֲזַרְתָּא דְּבִירוּשְׁלֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **When they arrived in Jerusalem they cast mats [*bistarkei*] for** Sennacherib and piled them high **until he ascended and sat above the wall, to the extent that he** was able to **see the entire** city of **Jerusalem. When he saw it, it** seemed **small in his eyes** and **he said: Is this the city of Jerusalem for which I have disrupted all my camps and for which I have conquered all** these **countries? Is it smaller and weaker than all the cities of the nations that I have conquered with my might? He went up and shook his head** in contempt, and dismissively **waved his hand at the Temple Mount that is in Zion and at the courtyard that is in Jerusalem.**

When *Sancheriv* arrived at *Yerushalayim*, his attendants piled up mattresses upon mattresses and raised him aloft so that he could survey the entire city. *Ki chazya, zutra b'eineih* — when he saw it, it was trivial in his eyes. He declared: *halo da hi karta d'Yerushalayim?* — is this the city of Jerusalem? *D'alah argishit kol mashiryai* — for this I have mobilized all my forces? *V'al da kavshit kol medinata* — for this I have conquered every province? *Halo hi ze'ira* — it is such a small city, *v'chalsha mikal karkei ammaya* — weaker than all the cities of the other nations that I have conquered with the strength of my hand. He stood up and shook his head in derision, waving his hand back and forth contemptuously — over the *Har HaBayit* and over the *Azarot* of *Yerushalayim*. The *Gemara's* description here is vivid and almost literary in quality; one senses it may draw on some older source.

### Postponement Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: נִישְׁדֵּי בֵּיהּ יְדָא הָאִידָּנָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: תָּמְהִיתוּ, לִמְחַר אַיְיתוֹ לִי כֹּל חַד וְחַד מִינַּיְיכוּ גּוּלְמוֹ הֲרַג מִינֵּיהּ. מִיָּד: ״וַיְהִי בַּלַּיְלָה הַהוּא וַיֵּצֵא מַלְאַךְ ה׳ וַיַּךְ בְּמַחֲנֵה אַשּׁוּר מֵאָה שְׁמוֹנִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה אָלֶף וַיַּשְׁכִּימוּ בַבֹּקֶר וְהִנֵּה כֻלָּם פְּגָרִים מֵתִים״. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: בָּת דִּינָא – בְּטַל דִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** His officers **said** to him: **Let us attack now** and begin the conquest of Jerusalem. Sennacherib **said to them: You are weary. Tomorrow, each and every one of you will bring me a piece** of a stone **from** the wall equivalent in size to the **seal [*gulmo harag*]** of a letter, and this will suffice to breach the wall and vanquish the city. The verse recounts that **immediately: "And it came to pass that night, that the angel of the Lord went forth and smote in the camp of the Assyrians one hundred and eighty-five thousand; and when men arose in the morning, behold, they were all dead corpses"** (II Kings 19:35). **Rav Pappa says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say:** When **quarrel lies** and is delayed overnight, **the quarrel is nullified.** Since Jerusalem was not conquered that day, the decree was voided.

The astrologers urged him: *mishkei yadei ha'idna* — let us conquer it now. But precisely because *Sancheriv* had now trivialized *Yerushalayim*, he no longer felt the urgency. *Amar lehu: tamhu lemachar* — wait until tomorrow. Give yourselves a rest; we have rushed here, everyone is exhausted. This is nothing; we will deal with it tomorrow. He commanded each of his soldiers to take a stone from the city wall — *Rashi* suggests this was as a token of the imminent conquest, to throw the stones at the city on the morrow. They made camp and slept.

### Annihilation Night

Before *Sancheriv* could act on his plan, that very night: *vayehi balaylah hahu vayetzei malach Hashem vayach bemachaneh Ashur me'ah ushemonim vachamishah elef* — the angel of God went out and struck 185,000 in the Assyrian camp. *Vayashkimu vaboker vehinei kulam pegarim meitim* — when they arose in the morning, they were all dead carcasses.

### Justice & Sleep

Justice & Sleep The *Gemara* cites the verse: *pash dina bat dina* — if the judgment sleeps over, it is nullified. This aphorism is evocative of the *Sanhedrin's* own principle of *linat hadin* — the requirement that a capital case be deliberated overnight — where part of the rationale is precisely to avoid hasty execution. But here the point is the converse: by letting the opportunity sleep, *Sancheriv* discarded it. *Pash dina* — the ruling passes, the moment dissolves. If you postpone something, you lose it. Do not procrastinate.

There is a deeper dimension here as well. The description of *Sancheriv* trivializing *Yerushalayim* — seeing nothing there worth conquering — reflects the perspective of someone entirely without love for the city. For one who has no emotional connection, *Yerushalayim* is simply an unremarkable provincial town. And yet, looking at the broader narrative, the day carried within it a genuine metaphysical vulnerability for Israel — the unresolved weight of *Nov's* sin. The passage invites us to read all of these elements together as the unfolding of a divine plan: the propitious day, the rush, the trivialization, the delay, and ultimately the miraculous annihilation — all working toward a divinely orchestrated conclusion. The parallel is apt: *Haman* cast the *pur* on the day that seemed most dangerous for Israel, and that very day was turned around. Just as a parent who watches a child stumble toward punishment does not wish it, and yet the outcome is ultimately guided by a larger design.

## David & Nov Sin

### Yishbi & Causality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיִשְׁבִּי בְּנֹב אֲשֶׁר בִּילִידֵי הָרָפָה וּמִשְׁקַל קֵינוֹ שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת מִשְׁקַל נְחֹשֶׁת וְהוּא חָגוּר חֲדָשָׁה וַיֹּאמֶר לְהַכּוֹת אֶת דָּוִד״. מַאי ״וְיִשְׁבִּי בְּנוֹב״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אִישׁ שֶׁבָּא עַל עִסְקֵי נוֹב. אֲמַר לֵיהּ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְדָוִד: עַד מָתַי יִהְיֶה עָוֹן זֶה טָמוּן בְּיָדְךָ? עַל יָדְךָ נֶהֶרְגָה נוֹב עִיר הַכֹּהֲנִים, וְעַל יָדְךָ נִטְרַד דּוֹאֵג הָאֲדוֹמִי, וְעַל יָדְךָ נֶהֶרְגוּ שָׁאוּל וּשְׁלֹשֶׁת בָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the massacre of Nov, the Gemara relates: **"And Ishbibenob, who was of the sons of the giant, the weight of whose spear was three hundred shekels of brass; and he was girded with new armor and planned to slay David"** (II Samuel 21:16). The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **Ishbibenob? Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** This is **a man [*ish*] who came** to punish David **over matters of Nov. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to David: Until when will this sin be concealed in your hand** without punishment? **Through your** actions the inhabitants of **Nov, the city of priests, were massacred, and through your** actions, **Doeg the Edomite was banished** from the World-to-Come, **and through your** actions **Saul and his three sons were killed.**

The word *Nov* having appeared, the *Gemara* now pivots to the earlier and more fundamental episode from which *Nov's* sin originated — the time of *David HaMelech*. The verse (Samuel II 21:16) reads: *v'Yishbi b'Nov asher b'yelidei haRafah* — and *Yishbi b'Nov*, who was among the descendants of *Rafah*, a Philistine giant whose spear weighed three hundred talents of copper and who was newly girded and intent on killing *David* while *David* was in the land of the *Plishtim*.

*Amar Rabbi Yehuda amar Rav*: the name *Yishbi b'Nov* means *ish sheba al iskei Nov* — a man who came on account of the affair of *Nov*. This is a striking *Gemara*. Even long before the time of *Sancheriv*, the sin of *Nov* was already being repaid — in the time of *David* himself.

*Amar lei HaKadosh Baruch Hu l'David*: God said to *David*: *ad masai yehei avon zeh tamun b'yadecha* — how long will this sin remain buried in your hands? *Al yadecha neherag Nov ir kohanim* — through you, *Nov ir kohanim* was destroyed. *David* had not personally destroyed *Nov*; he had fled there, knowing that *Shaul* was pursuing him, no doubt believing that *Shaul* would never dare destroy an entire city of *kohanim*. Nevertheless, the choice *David* made jeopardized them, and through that choice they were wiped out. The *Gemara* continues: *v'al yadecha nitrad Doeg HaEdomi* — and through you, *Doeg HaEdomi* was drawn in; *Doeg* was the one who incited *Shaul's* jealousy and carried out the slaughter. And *v'al yadecha nehergu Shaul v'shloshet banav* — through you, *Shaul* and his three sons were killed, for *Shaul's* sin of destroying *Nov* brought punishment upon him and his children.

This is genuinely troubling, and it deserves to be said plainly: *David* was the victim. *Doeg* was the instigator, *Shaul* the perpetrator. Yet the *Gemara* still says *al yadecha* — through you. Note that it does not say *mipanecha* — because of you, or that it was *David's* fault. The language is causal, not one of blame. And that itself raises a profound distinction: the difference between fault and causality. Even when one is a victim, one must anticipate the consequences of one's choices. That logic is at least intelligible when it comes to *David's* decision to shelter at *Nov* — he could have anticipated the risk he was placing that city under. It becomes far harder to understand with respect to *Doeg* and *Shaul*, who were the actual perpetrators. Perhaps the point is that there is enough in history to go around — not that *Shaul* and *Doeg* are exonerated, but that *David's* indirect role in setting in motion a chain of events is not invisible.

### David's Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְצוֹנְךָ יִכְלֶה זַרְעֶךָ, אוֹ תִּמָּסֵר בְּיַד אוֹיֵב? אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מוּטָב אֶמָּסֵר בְּיַד אוֹיֵב וְלֹא יִכְלֶה זַרְעִי.
>
> **Translation:** God said to David: Your arrival in Nov and your misleading Ahimelech the priest generated the chain of events, and therefore you must be punished. You may choose the punishment. **Is it your desire** that **your descendants will cease** to exist **or** that **you will be handed to the enemy?** David **said before Him: Master of the Universe, it is preferable** that **I will be handed to the enemy and my descendants will not cease** to exist.

*Birtzoncha yichlu zar'acha* — God offered *David* a choice: either your descendants will be wiped out, or you will be handed over to an enemy. *Amar lefanav: Ribbono Shel Olam, mutav emaser beyad oyev v'lo yichleh zar'i* — Master of the Universe, I would rather be handed over to the enemy than have my descendants destroyed.

### Demon Deer Trap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד נְפַק לִשְׁכוֹר בָּזָאֵי, אֲתָא שָׂטָן וְאִידְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּטַבְיָא. פְּתַק בֵּיהּ גִּירָא וְלָא מַטְיֵיהּ. מַשְׁכֵיהּ עַד דְּאַמְטְיֵיהּ לְאֶרֶץ פְּלִשְׁתִּים. כִּדְחַזְיֵיהּ יִשְׁבִּי בְּנוֹב אֲמַר: הַיְינוּ הַאי דְּקַטְלֵיהּ לְגׇלְיָת אֲחִי. כַּפְתֵיהּ, קַמְטֵיהּ, אוֹתְבֵיהּ וְשַׁדְיֵיהּ תּוּתֵי בֵּי סַדְיָיא. אִתְעֲבִיד לֵיהּ נִיסָּא, מָכָא לֵיהּ אַרְעָא מִתּוּתֵיהּ. הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״תַּרְחִיב צַעֲדִי תַחְתָּי וְלֹא מָעֲדוּ קַרְסֻלָּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **One day** David **went to hunt** with **a falcon [*liskor bazzai*]. Satan came and appeared to him as a deer. He shot an arrow at** the deer, **and** the arrow **did not reach it.** Satan **led** David to follow the deer **until he reached the land of the Philistines. When Ishbibenob saw** David **he said: This is that** person **who killed Goliath, my brother. He bound him, doubled him over, and placed him** on the ground, **and** then he **cast him under** the beam **of an olive press** to crush him. **A miracle was performed for him, and the earth opened beneath him** so he was not crushed by the beam. **That is** the meaning of that **which is written: "You have enlarged my steps beneath me, that my feet did not slip"** (Psalms 18:37).

One day *David* went out hunting to a place called *Biza*. *Ata shatan v'idmei lei k'tavya* — the *Satan* appeared to him in the form of a deer. *David* shot an arrow but could not reach it. The deer kept drawing him further, until *Patak beih geira, v'lo mata, mashchei, ad d'amtayei l'eretz Plishtim* — he kept pursuing it until he found himself in the land of the *Plishtim*. The image is reminiscent of the arrow that fell short in the story of *David* and *Yehonatan*, though here the purpose is entrapment.

When *Yishbi b'Nov* spied *David*, he recognized him: *hainu hai d'katlei le'Golyat achi* — this is the one who killed *Golyat* my brother. *Kafteih* — he bound *David* up; *kamteih* — he folded him; *otveih v'ramei tutei bei badya* — and placed him underneath an olive press to crush him to death. The image is almost comic in retrospect — rather than simply killing him, he subjects him to an elaborate contraption, reminiscent of those theatrical villains of fiction who construct elaborate death-traps rather than acting directly. A miracle intervened: *macha lei ar'a* — the ground beneath *David* sank slightly, and he was not crushed. The verse captures this: *tarchiv tza'adi tachtai* — You have broadened my steps below me; *v'lo ma'adu karsulai* — and my ankles did not slip. *Hahu yoma apanya de'ma'alei Shabta hava* — that day was *erev Shabbat*.

### Avishai's Rescue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבִישַׁי בֶּן צְרוּיָה הֲוָה קָא חָיֵיף רֵישֵׁיהּ בְּאַרְבְּעָא גַּרְבֵי דְּמַיָּא. חֲזִינְהוּ כִּתְמֵי דְּמָא. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲתָא יוֹנָה אִיטְּרִיף קַמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר: כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְיוֹנָה אִימְּתִילָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כַּנְפֵי יוֹנָה נֶחְפָּה בַכֶּסֶף״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דָּוִד מַלְכָּא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּצַעֲרָא שְׁרֵי. אֲתָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ וְלָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ. אֲמַר, תְּנַן: אֵין רוֹכְבִים עַל סוּסוֹ וְאֵין יוֹשְׁבִים עַל כִּסְאוֹ וְאֵין מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁים בְּשַׁרְבִיטוֹ. בִּשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה מַאי? אֲתָא שְׁאֵיל בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: בִּשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **That day at dusk on Shabbat eve, Abishai ben Zeruiah shampooed his hair with four jugs of water** in preparation for Shabbat. **He saw four bloodstains. There are** those **who say: A dove came and fluttered** its wings **before him.** Abishai **said: The congregation of Israel is likened to a dove, as it is stated: "You shall shine as the wings of a dove covered with silver** and her pinions with yellow gold" (Psalms 68:14); **conclude from it** that **David, king of Israel, is in a state of distress. He came to** David's **house and did not find him.** Abishai **said** that **we learned** in a mishna (22a): **One may not ride on** the king's **horse, and one may not sit on his throne, and one may not use his scepter. In a period of danger, what** is the *halakha*? **He came** and **asked** in **the study hall** what the ruling is in that situation. **They said to him: In a period of danger** one may **well** do so.

*Avishai ben Tzeruiah* — one of *David's* generals and the brother of *Yoav ben Tzeruiah* — was that same *erev Shabbat* washing his hair, in four buckets of water, when he noticed drops of blood in the water. Some say a dove came and began flapping and moaning in distress in his presence.

*Amar*: the Jewish people are compared to a dove, as it says *kanfei yona nechpasa bakesef*. If a dove is in distress, it must represent the king of Israel. *Shma mina David malka d'Yisrael betzara* — from this I conclude that *David* is in danger. He went to *David's* home and found him gone. He then asked a *halachic* question that is itself revealing: the *Mishnah* rules that one may not ride a king's horse, sit on his throne, or use his scepter. *Besha'as hassakana mai?* — what is the law when the king is in danger? He went and posed the question to the *Beis Medrash*, and they ruled: *besha'as hassakana shapir dami* — in a time of danger, of course it is permitted. He rode *David's* mule and set out — the land foreshortening for him, as we will discuss shortly. Why specifically *David's* mule rather than a faster horse? The *Gemara* does not explain; perhaps riding the royal animal carried some practical benefit of free passage, or perhaps the episode simply reports what occurred.

On the way, *Bahadi deka masgei, chazayei l'Orpah imei d'hahu* — as he rode, he came upon *Orpah*, the mother of *Yishbi b'Nov*. *Imei d'hahu dahavas navla* — she was knitting. When she saw *Avishai* coming, understanding that he was pursuing her son, she broke off her spindle and hurled it at him, intending to kill him. She missed. She then called out to him: *Alem, ayisei li pilchi* — young man, could you bring me back my spindle? He picked it up and *patkyei beresh mocha v'katlah* — drove it into her forehead and killed her. A notable detail here is the identification of this *Orpah* with the *Orpah* of the Book of Ruth, sister-in-law to *Ruth* and thus sister-in-law to *Naomi*. The *Gemara* in *Sotah* makes this identification explicit: the mother of *Yishbi b'Nov*, and of *Golyat*, was none other than that same *Orpah* — an inglorious end for a figure who also appears in a context of loyalty and kindness.

### Bound Prisoner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּד חַזְיֵיהּ יִשְׁבִּי בְּנוֹב, אֲמַר: הַשְׁתָּא הָווּ בֵּי תְרֵין וְקָטְלִין לִי. פַּתְקֵיהּ לְדָוִד לְעֵילָּא, וְדָץ לֵיהּ לְרוּמְחֵיהּ. אֲמַר: נִיפּוֹל עֲלֵהּ וְנִקְּטַל. אֲמַר אֲבִישַׁי שֵׁם, אוֹקְמֵיהּ לְדָוִד בֵּין שְׁמַיָּא לְאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ishbibenob saw him, he said: Now they are two,** David and Abishai, **and they will kill me. He threw David up** in the air, **and stuck his spear** into the ground. **He said: Let** David **fall upon it and die. Abishai recited** a sacred **name** of God and **suspended David between heaven and earth** so that he would not fall.

*Kad chazei Yishbi b'Nov Avishai*: when *Yishbi b'Nov* saw *Avishai* approaching, he calculated that now there were two against one. Despite the fact that *David* was still bound, he did not trust the odds. So *patkeih l'David l'eila* — he hurled *David* upward into the air, and *v'datz leh romchei* — thrust his spear below so that *David* would fall directly onto it. The *Gemara* notes that this could raise the question from earlier in *Sanhedrin* as to whether one who merely throws another upward is liable as a *rotzeach* if the victim falls onto a weapon. *Neipul v'niktal* — let him fall and be killed.

### Hovering Between

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנֵימָא לֵיהּ אִיהוּ? אֵין חָבוּשׁ מוֹצִיא עַצְמוֹ מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let** David **himself recite** the name of God and save himself. Why did he need Abishai? The Gemara answers: **A prisoner does not release himself from a prison** but requires someone else to release him. Similarly, one in danger is incapable of rescuing himself.

*Amar Avishai shem* — *Avishai* invoked one of the divine names, *u'kmei l'David bein shamaya l'ara* — and this stopped *David's* fall, leaving him hovering between heaven and earth. One might ask: why could *David* not invoke the divine name himself? The *Gemara* answers with one of its most resonant principles: *ein chavush matir atzmo mi-beis ha-asurim* — a bound prisoner cannot free himself from prison. *Rashi* explains: *ein da'ato mechuvvenet lomar ha-shem* — his mind is not properly focused to invoke the name. There is an unmistakably psychological dimension to this line. When one is the one in crisis, in anguish, the very mental state that the crisis creates prevents one from finding one's own way out. Under other circumstances, that same person might have helped another in identical danger. But for oneself, in one's own distress, the capacity is not there.

### Reverse Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי בָּעֵית הָכָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אֲמַר לִי קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא, וְהָכִי אַהְדַּרִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵפֹךְ צְלוֹתָיךְ! בַּר בְּרָךְ קִירָא לִיזְבּוֹן, וְאַתְּ לָא תִּצְטַעַר.
>
> **Translation:** Abishai **said to** David: **What do you seek here** and why did you fall into Ishbibenob's hands? David **said to him: This** is what **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to me, and this** is what **I responded to Him;** the time to submit to my enemy has arrived. Abishai **said to him: Reverse your prayer** and pray that your descendants will cease to exist rather than that you will be handed to the enemy, in accordance with the adage that people say: **Let your son's son** be a poor peddler and **sell wax, and you will not suffer.** Do not limit your expenses to leave an inheritance for your descendants.

*Amar Avishai* to *David* as he hovered: *mai ba'at hacha?* — what are you doing here? How did this happen? *David* told him: this is what God had said to me, and this is how I had responded — I chose to be handed over to the enemy rather than have my descendants destroyed.

*Amar lei Avishai: hafich tzlutach* — reverse your prayer. Ask that your descendants be destroyed instead. *Dar brich kira lizbun v'at lo titzta'er* — let your grandchildren be beggars as long as you yourself live comfortably. The *Gemara* cites this as an apparently common folk expression: let your grandchildren be beggars as long as you yourself live comfortably. *Avishai* was giving *David* practical advice to revise his prayer. Whether the *Gemara* is endorsing this view or merely reporting what *Avishai* counseled is unclear, and the advice sits uncomfortably given *David's* characteristic acceptance of responsibility. *David* responded: *ee hachi, sayyatan* — if that is what you counsel, then help me — either to descend safely or to pray the reverse prayer. *Hainu d'ichtiv: vayazor lo Avishai ben Tzeruiah* — and that is the verse: *Avishai ben Tzeruiah* came to *David's* aid. *Rav Yehuda amar Rav: she'azro bi-tfilah* — he assisted him through prayer, reversing the original petition.

### Avishai's Second Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר אֲבִישַׁי שֵׁם וְאַחֲתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abishai recited another sacred **name** of God **and caused** David to **land** safely after being suspended between heaven and earth, and they fled.

*Amar Avishai shem* — *Avishai* then invoked another divine name — *v'achsei* — and brought *David* all the way down safely to the ground, away from the spear.

### Taunting Strategy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה קָא רָדֵיף בָּתְרַיְיהוּ. כִּי מְטֹא קוּבֵּי, אָמְרִי: קוּם בֵּיהּ. כִּי מְטֹא בֵּי תְרֵי, אָמְרִי: בִּתְרֵי גּוּרְיָוָן קַטְלוּהּ לְאַרְיָא. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: זִיל אִשְׁתְּכַח לְעׇרְפָּה אִימָּיךְ בְּקִיבְרָא. כִּי אַדְכַּרוּ לֵיהּ שְׁמָא דְּאִימֵּיהּ, כְּחַשׁ חֵילֵיהּ וְקַטְלוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Ishbibenob **pursued them,** intending to kill them. **When they reached** the place named **Kuvi they said:** The name of the place is an abbreviation for the phrase meaning: **Stand** and battle **against him [*kum beih*]. When they reached** a place called **Bei Terei,** David and Abishai **said: With two [*bitrei*] cubs they killed the lion,** meaning they expected to be successful. **They said to** Ishbibenob: **Go find Orpah, your mother, in the grave. When they mentioned his mother's name to him** and told him she died, **his strength diminished, and they killed him.**

Even after *David* was rescued, *Yishbi b'Nov* continued pursuing both of them. They came first to a place called *Kubi*, where they considered making a stand. They came then to *Bei Trei*, where they said: *bitrei guryan katlohu l'arye* — can the two of us kill the lion? The reading *b'timyah* — in astonishment rather than resolve — may actually be the correct one: they were not certain they could overcome him by force alone. So instead they taunted him.

### Mother's Memory

*Amrei lei: zil ishtakach l'Orpah imach bekivra* — go find your mother in the grave. *Ki idkaru lei shma d'imeih, kechesh cheileih* — when they mentioned his mother's name, his strength left him, *v'katlohu* — and they were able to kill him. The mention of his mother — whose inglorious death *Avishai* had brought about on the road — was the instrument that broke him.

### Oath of Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״אָז נִשְׁבְּעוּ אַנְשֵׁי דָוִד לוֹ לֵאמֹר לֹא תֵצֵא עוֹד אִתָּנוּ לַמִּלְחָמָה וְלֹא תְכַבֶּה אֶת נֵר יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** ** It is** after this **that it is written: "Then David's men took an oath to him saying: You shall not go with us to war anymore and you will not douse the lamp of Israel"** (II Samuel 21:17).

*Az nishbe'u anshei David lo leimor lo teitzei od itanu lamilchama v'lo techabeh et ner Yisrael* — then *David's* men made him swear that he would no longer go out to war with them. He was too precious, the light of Israel, and they had come too close to losing him.

## Land Jumping

### Eliezer's Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה קָפְצָה לָהֶם הָאָרֶץ – אֱלִיעֶזֶר עֶבֶד אַבְרָהָם, וְיַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ, וַאֲבִישַׁי בֶּן צְרוּיָה. אֲבִישַׁי בֶּן צְרוּיָה – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. אֱלִיעֶזֶר עֶבֶד אַבְרָהָם – דִּכְתִיב: ״וָאָבֹא הַיּוֹם אֶל הָעָיִן״, לְמֵימְרָא דְּהָהוּא יוֹמָא נְפַק.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to land contracting to shorten a journey: **For three** individuals **the land contracted,** and each one miraculously reached his destination quickly: **Eliezer, servant of Abraham, and Jacob our forefather, and Abishai, son of Zeruiah.** The Gemara elaborates: The case of **Abishai, son of Zeruiah,** is **that which we said.** The case of **Eliezer, servant of Abraham,** is **as it is written: "And I came that day to the well"** (Genesis 24:42). His intention was **to say** to the members of Rebecca's family **that on that day he left** Canaan and on the same day he arrived, to underscore the miraculous nature of his undertaking on behalf of Abraham.

*Tanu rabanan: lishlosha kaftza lahem ha'aretz* — the *Rabbanan* taught that the land foreshortened its journey for three righteous figures. The first is *Eliezer eved Avraham*: *d'ichtiv va'avo hayom el ha'ayin* — I came today to the wellspring — demonstrating that the very day he departed he had already arrived at his destination in *Charan* to find a wife for *Yitzchak*.

### Avishai's Speed

*Avishai ben Tzeruiah ha d'amran* — *Avishai ben Tzeruiah* we have just discussed. Despite the vast distance between *Yerushalayim* and *Eretz Plishtim*, he traversed it with miraculous speed on *David's* mule. It is notable that *Sancheriv*, who also had a foreshortened journey — traveling ten days' worth of road in a single day — is not included in this list of three, though the *Gemara* had mentioned him just before.

### Jacob's Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יַעֲקֹב אָבִינוּ - דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּצֵא יַעֲקֹב מִבְּאֵר שָׁבַע וַיֵּלֶךְ חָרָנָה״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּפְגַּע בַּמָּקוֹם וַיָּלֶן שָׁם כִּי בָא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״. כִּי מְטָא לְחָרָן אֲמַר: אֶפְשָׁר עָבַרְתִּי עַל מְקוֹם שֶׁהִתְפַּלְּלוּ בּוֹ אֲבוֹתַי וַאֲנִי לֹא הִתְפַּלַּלְתִּי בּוֹ? בָּעֵי לְמֶיהְדַּר. כֵּיוָן דְּהַרְהַר בְּדַעְתֵּיהּ לְמִיהְדַּר, קְפַצָה לֵיהּ אַרְעָא. מִיָּד – ״וַיִּפְגַּע בַּמָּקוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** is **as it is written: "And Jacob departed from Beersheba and went to Haran"** (Genesis 28:10), **and it is written** thereafter, ostensibly after he arrived in Haran: **"And he encountered [*vayyifga*] the place, and he slept there, for the sun had set"** (Genesis 28:11). This means that **when** Jacob **arrived at Haran, he said: Is it possible that I bypassed a place where my forefathers prayed and I did not pray there? He sought to return** to Beit El. **Once he contemplated in his mind to return, the land contracted for him,** and **immediately: "And he encountered the place,"** indicating that he arrived there unexpectedly, sooner than he would have arrived without a miracle.

The third is *Yaakov Avinu*: *d'ichtiv vayeitzei Yaakov miBe'er Sheva vayelech Charanah* — and *Yaakov* left *Be'er Sheva* and went to *Charan* — and immediately: *vayifga bamakom* — he encountered the place. The juxtaposition implies that the moment he left *Be'er Sheva* he had already arrived at *Charan*. But then *Beis El* enters the picture. *Ki mata l'Charan, amar: efshar avarti al makom shehitpallu bo avotai va'ani lo hitpalalti bo?* — when he came to *Charan*, he thought: is it possible that I passed by the place where my forefathers prayed and I did not pray there? The framing is striking: it is not primarily about the place's inherent sanctity, but about the practice of the *avot* — he wants to follow in their custom.

*Ba'ei l'mehadar* — he decided to return. *Keivan d'hirher bedaiteih l'mehadar, kaftza lei ar'a, miyad vayifga bamakom* — the moment he formed the intention to return, the land jumped, and he was immediately at *Beis El*. So the first *kfitzat haderech* was from *Be'er Sheva* to *Charan*, and the second was the return to *Beis El*.

### Prayer & Encounter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּבָר אַחֵר: אֵין פְּגִיעָה אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַתָּה אַל תִּתְפַּלֵּל בְּעַד הָעָם הַזֶּה וְאַל תִּשָּׂא בַעֲדָם רִנָּה וּתְפִלָּה וְאַל תִּפְגַּע בִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively, encounter** means **nothing other than prayer, as it is stated: "And you, do not pray on behalf of this nation, and do not raise on their behalf song and prayer, and do not encounter [*tifga*] Me,** for I do not hear you" (Jeremiah 7:16).

*Davar acher: ein pegia ela tefila* — and additionally, the word *pegia* connotes prayer, as in *v'al tifga bi* — do not intercede with me. This dovetails naturally with the *Gemara's* point, since the entire purpose of *Yaakov's* return was to pray — so *vayifga bamakom* signifies both his encounter with the place and his prayer there. The *Gemara* also identifies this as the basis for *Yaakov* being the *mataken* of *tefillat arvit*.

### Sunset Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיָּלֶן שָׁם כִּי בָא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״. בָּתַר דְּצַלִּי בָּעֵי לְמִיהְדַּר, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: צַדִּיק זֶה בָּא לְבֵית מְלוֹנִי, יִפָּטֵר בְּלֹא לִינָה? מִיָּד בָּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ. וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּזְרַח לוֹ הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ״. וְכִי לוֹ בִּלְבַד זָרְחָה? וַהֲלֹא לְכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ זָרְחָה! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: שֶׁמֶשׁ שֶׁבָּאָה בַּעֲבוּרוֹ, זָרְחָה בַּעֲבוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"And he slept there because the sun had set"** (Genesis 28:11). **After** Jacob **prayed** and **he sought to return** to his travels, **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: This righteous** man **came to My inn. Will** I allow **him to take leave without sleeping** here? **Immediately the sun set,** not at the proper time, **and that is** the meaning of that **which is written** with regard to Jacob: **"And the sun shone for him** when he passed Penuel" (Genesis 32:32). The Gemara asks: **And did** the sun **shine only for him? But didn't** the sun **shine for the entire world? Rather, Rabbi Yitzḥak says: The sun that set** not at the proper time exclusively **for him shone** not at the proper time exclusively **for him** in order to rectify the disparity created by the premature sunset.

*Vayalon sham ki va hashemesh* — and he slept there because the sun had set. *Rashi* reads the verse to indicate that the sun set suddenly and unexpectedly — it is not *vayavo hashemesh vayalon sham* (the sun came and he slept) but *vayalon sham ki va hashemesh* (he slept because the sun had come) — the sequence implies that the sunset was unexpected, as though the sun had set prematurely.

*Amar HaKadosh Baruch Hu: tzaddik zeh ba l'veis maloni v'yiftar belo linah?* — this righteous one has come to My inn and shall he leave without sleeping over? The image of God calling *Beis El* His inn is charged with meaning. There is also a parallel to the law that one who brings a *korban* and whose *tefilla* or *paschal* offering is involved must sleep over and not rush away. The narrative itself shifts: it begins with *Yaakov's* motivation — following the practice of his forefathers — and then pivots to God's perspective — *My house, you've arrived, stay.* From *Yaakov's* angle, the place is where the *avot* prayed; from God's angle, it is His home and *Yaakov* is His guest.

*Miyad ba hashemesh* — so too the sun set before its time. The *Gemara* then notes the parallel: *vayizrach lo hashemesh v'hu tzoleia al yereicho* — the sun shone for him after his struggle with the angel. *V'chi lo bilvad zaracha? V'halo l'chol ha'olam kulam zaracha?* — did the sun shine for him alone? Does it not shine for everyone? *Ella amar Rabbi Yitzchak: shemesh sheba'ah ba'avuro zaracha ba'avuro* — the same sun that had set for him when he returned to pray at *Beis El*, that same sun shone for him again after his nightlong struggle with the angel.

### David's Descendants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דִּכְלָה זַרְעֵיהּ דְּדָוִד? דִּכְתִיב ״וַעֲתַלְיָה אֵם אֲחַזְיָהוּ רָאֲתָה כִּי מֵת בְּנָהּ וַתָּקׇם וַתְּאַבֵּד אֵת כׇּל זֶרַע הַמַּמְלָכָה״. וְהָא אִשְׁתְּיַיר לֵיהּ יוֹאָשׁ? הָתָם נָמֵי אִשְׁתְּיַיר אֶבְיָתָר, דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּמָּלֵט בֵּן אֶחָד לַאֲחִימֶלֶךְ בֶּן אֲחִיטוּב וּשְׁמוֹ אֶבְיָתָר״. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אִלְמָלֵא לֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר אֶבְיָתָר לַאֲחִימֶלֶךְ בֶּן אֲחִיטוּב, לֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר מִזַּרְעוֹ שֶׁל דָּוִד שָׂרִיד וּפָלִיט.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos David's prayer that his descendants cease, the Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that David's descendants ceased** to exist? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: "And Athaliah the mother of Ahaziah saw that her son was dead, and she arose and destroyed all the royal descendants"** (II Kings 11:1). The Gemara asks: **But didn't Joash remain** alive, and therefore, not all of David's descendants ceased to exist? The Gemara answers: **There too,** in the massacre in Nov, **Abiathar,** one of the priests, **remained** alive, **as it is written: "And one of the sons of Ahimelech, son of Ahitub, named Abiathar, escaped"** (I Samuel 22:20). **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Were it not for** the fact that **Abiathar remained** alive **for Ahimelech, son of Ahitub, there would have been no remnant or refugee remaining from the descendants of David.**

The *Gemara* then addresses whether *David's* descendants were in fact destroyed, given that his original prayer was reversed. The verse (Kings II 11:1) says: *v'Asalya em Achazyahu ra'asah ki mes benah vatakam vatabad et kol zera hamamlacha* — *Atalya* saw that her son had died and she rose and destroyed the entire royal family. Yet *va'ishtayeir Yoash* — *Yoash* was left over and became the next king, from whom further descendants came. *Hacha nami ishtayeir Avyasar* — similarly, from *Nov ir hakohanim*, one person escaped: *v'yimaleit ben echad l'Achimelech ben Achituv u'shmo Avyasar*. *Amar Rav Yehuda Amar Rav: v'ilmalei lo nishayar Avyasar l'Achimelech ben Achituv, lo nishayar mizar'o shel David afilu pali* — had *Avyasar* not survived from *Nov*, not a single remnant of *David's* line would have remained, and there would have been no *Malchei Yehuda* and no *Mashiach*. The precariousness of history, viewed through the *Gemara's* lens, is deeply unsettling: entire chains of consequence, the continuation of the messianic line, hanging by the survival of a single individual.

## Army Composition

### Officer Breakdown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בָּא עֲלֵיהֶם סַנְחֵרִיב הָרָשָׁע בְּאַרְבָּעִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה אֶלֶף אִישׁ בְּנֵי מְלָכִים, יוֹשְׁבִים בִּקְרוֹנוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב, וְעִמָּהֶן שִׁגְלוֹנוֹת וְזוֹנוֹת, וּבִשְׁמֹנִים אֶלֶף גִּבּוֹרִים לְבוּשֵׁי שִׁרְיוֹן קְלִיפָּה, וּבְשִׁשִּׁים אֶלֶף אֲחוּזֵי חֶרֶב רָצִים לְפָנָיו, וְהַשְּׁאָר פָּרָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos Sennacherib, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Sennacherib the wicked came upon them with forty-five thousand men, sons of kings, sitting in carriages of gold, and with them** were **consorts and prostitutes, and with eighty thousand warriors** each **wearing a coat of mail, and with sixty thousand sword-bearers running before him, and the rest were cavalry.**

*Amar Rav Yehuda Amar Rav*: *ba'aleihem Sancheriv harasha b'arba'im vachamishah elef ish* — *Sancheriv* came against them with 45,000 men who were *bnei melachim*, princes, *yoshvim bikronot shel zahav* — seated in golden covered wagons — accompanied by *shiglonot v'zonot*, queens and concubines. A good point arises here: the word *shiglonot* is one of those words in *Tanach* that is customarily read as *tishkavena* rather than as written, since the written form implies something unseemly; it is noteworthy that it appears here juxtaposed with *zonot*. Presumably *Rashi* renders it as *princesses*, though the parallel to *zonot* may suggest something closer to concubines.

Then *ushmonim elef giborim levushei shiryon klipa* — 80,000 valorous soldiers clad in plate armor. *Uvshishim elef achuzei cherev ratzim lefanav* — and 60,000 sword-fighters running before him. The arithmetic: 45,000 plus 80,000 plus 60,000 equals 185,000 — precisely the number the verse records as having been slain by the angel. But this was not *Sancheriv's* entire army; these were the elite, the *rashei gavsot*. *V'hashar parashim v'agronim v'raglim* — the rest were cavalry, supply troops, and infantry.

### Total Army Size

The *Gemara* then notes that a comparable force came against *Avraham* when he fought the four kings, and that the same number will come in the war of *Gog uMagog* — a connection that reinforces the *Gemara's* earlier thread: *Chizkiyahu*, who was nearly the *Mashiach*, anticipates Messianic times, and the parallels with *Avraham* as well as with the future war of *Gog uMagog* reflect the principle of *ma'aseh avot siman l'banim*.

### Camp Description

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: אוֹרֶךְ מַחֲנֵהוּ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת פַּרְסָה, רֹחַב צַוַּאר סוּסָיו אַרְבָּעִים פַּרְסָה. סַךְ מַחֲנֵהוּ מָאתַיִם וְשִׁשִּׁים רִיבּוֹא אֲלָפִים חָסֵר חַד. בָּעֵי אַבָּיֵי: חָסֵר חַד רִיבּוֹיָא, אוֹ חָסַר חַד אַלְפָּא, אוֹ חָסַר מְאָה, אוֹ חָסַר חַד? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It **was taught in a *baraita*: The length of the camp** of Sennacherib **was four hundred parasangs and the width of the necks of his horses** side by side **was forty parasangs.** The **total** number of soldiers in **his camp was 260 ten thousands,** i.e., 2,600,000, **minus one. Abaye asks:** Is the *tanna* saying **minus one** unit of **ten thousand, or minus one** unit of **one thousand, or minus one** unit of **one hundred or minus one?** The Gemara concludes: The question **shall stand** unresolved.

*U'v'matnita tana*: a *Baraita* teaches that the length of *Sancheriv's* camp was 400 parasangs, and the total number of his troops was 260 myriads (*ribuv*) of thousands (*alafim*) minus one. The *Gemara* asks whether the "minus one" refers to one myriad, one thousand, one hundred, or one individual — and leaves the question as *teyku*. The figure, depending on how it is read, ranges from roughly two and a half million to two billion six hundred million — numbers that exceed even the world population of that era, and which the *Gemara* itself does not attempt to rationalize. What it does convey is the sheer magnitude of the force bearing down on *Yerushalayim*.

### River Crossing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: רִאשׁוֹנִים עָבְרוּ בִּשְׂחִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְחָלַף בִּיהוּדָה שָׁטַף וְעָבַר״. אֶמְצָעִיִּים עָבְרוּ בְּקוֹמָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַד צַוָּאר יַגִּיעַ״. אַחֲרוֹנִים הֶעֱלוּ עָפָר בְּרַגְלֵיהֶם וְלֹא מָצְאוּ מַיִם בַּנָּהָר לִשְׁתּוֹת, עַד שֶׁהֵבִיאוּ מַיִם מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר וְשָׁתוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֲנִי קַרְתִּי וְשָׁתִיתִי מָיִם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** It was **taught: The first** set of soldiers in the army of Sennacherib **crossed by swimming** across the Jordan, **as it is stated: "And he shall sweep through Judea; he shall overflow and pass"** (Isaiah 8:8). Since the water level diminished with their crossing, **the middle** group of soldiers **passed while standing upright, as it is stated: "It shall reach even the neck"** (Isaiah 8:8). **The last** soldiers already **kicked up dust with their feet, and they did not find water in the river to drink,** and they had no water **until they brought water from another place and drank** it, **as it is stated: "I dug and drank water"** (Isaiah 37:25).

The *Baraita* also describes the army's passage through a river: the first wave had to swim through (*besichi*), so deep was the water. So many men then passed through that the middle group could walk with the water only up to their necks — *ad tzavar yagia*. By the time the last group crossed, the river had been so depleted that they walked on dry land and there was no water left in the river to drink, requiring water to be brought from elsewhere — *ani karti v'shatiti mayim*.

### Annihilation Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּצֵא מַלְאַךְ ה׳ וַיַּכֶּה בְּמַחֲנֵה אַשּׁוּר מֵאָה וּשְׁמוֹנִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה אָלֶף, וַיַּשְׁכִּימוּ בַבֹּקֶר וְהִנֵּה כֻלָּם פְּגָרִים מֵתִים״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: הַלָּלוּ רָאשֵׁי גְיָיסוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "Then the angel of the Lord went forth and smote in the camp of the Assyrians one hundred and eighty-five thousand. And when they arose early in the morning, behold, they were all dead corpses"** (Isaiah 37:36)? Apparently, there were many fewer soldiers than the number listed in the *baraita*. **Rabbi Abbahu says: These** listed here **are regiment leaders,** who commanded numerous soldiers.

The *Gemara* then asks: *bameh hikam?* — with what did the angel strike them? Various *Amoraim* offer different readings.

### Hand, Finger, and Blade

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה הִכָּם? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בַּיָּד הִכָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְא יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הַיָּד הַגְּדֹלָה״. הַיָּד שֶׁעֲתִידָה לִיפָּרַע מִסַּנְחֵרִיב. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בְּאֶצְבַּע הִכָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ הַחַרְטֻמִּים אֶל פַּרְעֹה אֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים הִיא״. הִיא אֶצְבַּע שֶׁעֲתִידָה לִיפָּרַע מִסַּנְחֵרִיב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְגַבְרִיאֵל: מַגָּלְךָ נְטוּשָׁה? אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, נְטוּשָׁה וְעוֹמֶדֶת מִשֵּׁשֶׁת יְמֵי בְּרֵאשִׁית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״[מִפְּנֵי חֶרֶב נְטוּשָׁה מִפְּנֵי קֶשֶׁת דְּרוּכָה מִפְּנֵי כּוֹבֶד מִלְחָמָה] (וְגוֹ)״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With what** did the angel **strike them? Rabbi Eliezer says: He struck them by hand, as it is stated: "And Israel saw the great hand"** (Exodus 14:31), where the term for hand, *yad*, is preceded by the definite article *ha*, indicating that this was **the hand that was destined to exact retribution from Sennacherib. Rabbi Yehoshua says: He struck them with** His **finger, as it is stated: "And the sorcerers said to Pharaoh: This is the finger of God"** (Exodus 8:15), **indicating** that **it is** the **finger that was destined to exact retribution from Sennacherib. Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, says** that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Gabriel:** Is **your scythe honed?** Gabriel **said before Him: Master of the Universe, it is standing honed from the six days of Creation, as it is stated: "For they have fled from the swords,** from the honed sword" (Isaiah 21:15).

*Rabbi Eliezer omer*: *beyad hikam* — with a hand. *Shene'emar vayar Yisrael et hayad hagedolah* — the same great hand that struck *Paroh* and Egypt was the hand that would pay back *Sancheriv*.

*Rabbi Yehoshua omer*: *be'etzba hikam* — only with a finger. *Shene'emar vayomru hachartumim el Paroh etzba Elokim hi* — it is the finger of God, says the verse by *Paroh*; that same finger would pay back *Sancheriv*.

*Rabbi Eliezer beno shel Rabbi Yossei HaGlili omer*: God said to *Gavriel*: *magalecha metushah?* — is your scythe sharpened? *Gavriel* responded: *Ribbono Shel Olam, metushah v'omedet misheshet yemei vereishit* — it has been sharpened and standing ready since the six days of creation. The point here is not merely that the same power that punished *Paroh* punished *Sancheriv*, but that the very instrument was prepared at the dawn of creation — it was always part of the plan.

### Seasonal Task

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: אוֹתוֹ הַפֶּרֶק זְמַן בִּישּׁוּל פֵּירוֹת הָיָה. אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְגַבְרִיאֵל: כְּשֶׁאַתָּה יוֹצֵא לְבַשֵּׁל פֵּירוֹת, הִזָּקֵק לָהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִדֵּי עׇבְרוֹ יִקַּח אֶתְכֶם כִּי בַבֹּקֶר בַּבֹּקֶר יַעֲבֹר בַּיּוֹם וּבַלָּיְלָה וְהָיָה רַק זְוָעָה הָבִין שְׁמוּעָה וְגוֹ׳״. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי, אַגַּב אוֹרְחָךְ, לִבְעֵל דְּבָבָךְ אִישְׁתְּמַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: That period was the season of the ripening of the fruit. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Gabriel: When you emerge to ripen** the **fruits, attend to** the army of Assyria and destroy **them, as it is stated: "As often as it passes through, it shall take you; for morning by morning shall it pass through, by day and by night; and the mere understanding of the report shall bring terror"** (Isaiah 28:19), indicating that Gabriel was to kill them while engaged in another activity. **Rav Pappa says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: While on your path, appear before your enemy** and afflict him.

*Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai omer*: that was the season when fruits were ripening, and *Gavriel's* assigned role was to oversee that ripening. God said to him: *keshetehei yotzei levashel peirot, hizakek lahem* — when you go out to ripen the fruits, take care of *Sancheriv's* troops along the way. *Shene'emar midei avro yikach etchem* — as he passes through, he will take you. *Ki vaboker vaboker ya'avor bayom uvalailah* — he passes by day and by night. *Amar Rav Papa: hainu d'amri inshi: agav urchach leba'al devavach ishtama* — that is what people say: along your way, make yourself known to your enemy. On your way past, settle an account.

One might raise the question from elsewhere that a *malach* is not supposed to perform two tasks simultaneously. The *Gemara* does not address this directly here.

### Death Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: בְּחוֹטְמָן נָשַׁף בָּהֶן וָמֵתוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְגַם נָשַׁף בָּהֶם וַיִּבָשׁוּ״. רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: כַּפַּיִם סָפַק לָהֶם וָמֵתוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְגַם אֲנִי אַכֶּה כַפִּי אֶל כַּפִּי וַהֲנִחֹתִי חֲמָתִי״. רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: אׇזְנַיִם גָּלָה לָהֶם וְשָׁמְעוּ שִׁירָה מִפִּי חַיּוֹת וָמֵתוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מֵרוֹמְמוּתֶךָ נָפְצוּ גּוֹיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are those who say:** The angel **blew into** the soldiers of the Assyrian army **through their noses and they died, as it is stated: "When he blows upon them, and they wither"** (Isaiah 40:24). **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says:** The angel **clapped his hands to them and they died, as it is stated: "I will also strike My hands together and I will assuage My anger"** (Ezekiel 21:22). **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa says: He revealed ears for them and they** were able to **hear the songs** of praise **from the mouths of the** celestial **creatures** drawing the chariot of God, **and they died, as it is stated: "From Your exaltation the nations were scattered"** (Isaiah 33:3), indicating that their death was caused by the revelation of God's greatness.

*V'yesh omrim*: *bechotmam nashaf bahem u'metu* — God blew into their nostrils and they died. *Shene'emar v'gam nashaf bahem vayivashu* — and they dried up.

*Rabbi Yirmeyah bar Abba amar*: *kapayim sipek lahem* — God clapped His palms, producing something like a great sonic concussion. *Shene'emar vegam ani akeh chapi el kapi vahanichoti chamati* — I will strike palm against palm and My fury will abate.

*Rabbi Yitzchak amar*: *oznayim gilah lahem* — God made their ears hear. *V'sham'u shirah mipi chayot u'metu* — they heard the *shira* sung by the heavenly *chayot*, and they died. This is the most striking opinion among those offered. Rather than a violent image — a divine hand, a finger, a blade — the troops were annihilated by hearing something beautiful. Their souls, confronted with the transcendent, departed. It is a very different conception of divine power. And it is worth noting that this ties back to the *shira* theme that runs throughout the *daf*: *Chizkiyahu* did not sing *shira*; the land sang *shira* in his place; *Nevuchadnetzar* sought to humiliate the *shirot* of *David HaMelech*; and now here, the angels' *shira* becomes itself the instrument of divine retribution against *Sancheriv's* host.

### Survivor Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה נִשְׁתַּיֵּר מֵהֶם? רַב אָמַר: עֲשָׂרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁאָר עֵץ יַעְרוֹ מִסְפָּר יִהְיוּ וְנַעַר יִכְתְּבֵם״. כַּמָּה נַעַר יָכוֹל לִכְתּוֹב? עֲשָׂרָה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: תִּשְׁעָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִשְׁאַר בּוֹ עוֹלֵלֹת כְּנֹקֶף זַיִת שְׁנַיִם שְׁלֹשָׁה גַּרְגְּרִים בְּרֹאשׁ אָמִיר אַרְבָּעָה חֲמִשָּׁה בִּסְעִפֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And how many of** the soldiers **remained** alive? **Rav says: Ten** soldiers, **as it is stated: "And the rest of the trees of his forest shall be few, that a child may write them"** (Isaiah 10:19). **And what number can a child** easily **write?** It is the number **ten,** represented by the letter *yod*, which is the smallest letter. **And Shmuel says: Nine** remained, **as it is stated: "And gleanings shall be left of him, as in the beating of an olive tree, two or three berries in the top of the uppermost bough, four or five in the branches of a fruitful tree"** (Isaiah 17:6). Four and five total nine.

How many survived? *Rava amar asara* — Rava says ten, based on the verse *ushear etz yaaro mispar yihyu v'naar yichtivem* — the remnant will be so few that a young lad could write down their number. How many can a lad write? Ten — the number of his fingers, or alternatively the number *yud* formed by a single drop of ink.

*Shmuel amar tisha* — nine, from the verse about the remnant of the olive-picking: *shnayim u'shlosha gargarim berosh amir v'arba'a v'chamisha biseifia* — two and three at the top, four and five at the branches: 2 plus 3 is 5, plus 4 plus 5 is 9.

### Remaining Totals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי אָמַר: אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״שְׁנַיִם שְׁלֹשָׁה [וְכוּ׳] אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: Fourteen** remained, **as it is stated: "Two or three…four or five,"** which total fourteen.

*Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi amar arba'a asar* — fourteen, because adding all terms: 2 plus 3 plus 4 plus 5 equals 14.

### Five Survivors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: חֲמִשָּׁה – סַנְחֵרִיב וּשְׁנֵי בָּנָיו, נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר וּנְבוּזַּרְאֲדָן. נְבוּזַרְאֲדָן – גְּמָרָא. נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרֵוֵהּ דִּי רְבִיעָאָה דָּמֵה לְבַר אֱלָהִין״, וְאִי לָאו דְּחַזְיֵיהּ מְנָא הֲוָה יָדַע? סַנְחֵרִיב וּשְׁנֵי בָּנָיו – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי הוּא מִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה בֵּית נִסְרֹךְ אֱלֹהָיו וְאַדְרַמֶּלֶךְ וְשַׂרְאֶצֶר בָּנָיו הִכֻּהוּ בַחֶרֶב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Five** remained: **Sennacherib, and his two sons,** and **Nebuchadnezzar, and Nebuzaradan,** the captain of the guard. The fact that **Nebuzaradan** was among them is learned through **tradition. Nebuchadnezzar** was among them, **as it is written** that Nebuchadnezzar said when he saw an angel with Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah: **"And the appearance of the fourth is like that of an angel"** (Daniel 3:25). **And if he had not seen** the angel that smote Sennacherib's army, **from where would he have known** that the fourth person looked like an angel? **Sennacherib and his two sons** were among them, **as it is written** later in their regard: **"And it came to pass as he was worshipping in the house of Nisroch his god that Adrammelech and Sarezer, his sons, smote him with the sword"** (II Kings 19:37). Evidently, the three of them survived.

*Rabbi Yochanan amar chamisha* — five. These five were: *Sancheriv u'shnei banav* — *Sancheriv* and his two sons (who, as already cited, ultimately killed him in the house of his god *Nisroch*) — and *Nevuchadnetzar v'Nevuzaradan*. The implication is sobering: fine, *Sancheriv's* army was annihilated; but *Nevuchadnetzar* and his general *Nevuzaradan* walk away, and they will return for *Yerushalayim*.

### Angel Recognition

*Nevuchadnetzar d'chtiv* — the textual proof for *Nevuchadnetzar* being among the survivors comes from the Book of *Daniel*, where *Nevuchadnetzar*, upon seeing the four figures in the furnace, declares that the fourth *damei l'var elohin* — resembles a divine being, an angel. How did *Nevuchadnetzar* know what an angel looked like? *V'ee lav d'chaza* — if he had not seen one before, *mena hava yada* — how would he have known? The answer is that he had seen the destroying angel at *Sancheriv's* camp, and that encounter remained with him. The five survivors carried with them the memory — and the consequences — of what they had witnessed.
