# Sanhedrin 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tax Collectors & Shevi'is

### Rabbi Zeira's Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא עֲבַד גַּבְיוּתָא תְּלֵיסַר שְׁנִין. כִּי הֲוָה אָתֵי רֵישׁ נַהֲרָא לְמָתָא, כִּי הֲוָה חָזֵי רַבָּנַן אֲמַר לְהוּ: ״לֵךְ עַמִּי בֹּא בַחֲדָרֶיךָ״. כִּי הֲוָה חָזֵי אִינָשֵׁי דְּמָתָא אָמַר: רֵישׁ נַהֲרָא אֲתָא לְמָתָא, וְהָאִידָּנָא נָכֵיס אַבָּא לְפוּם בְּרָא וּבְרָא לְפוּם אַבָּא, וּמִגַּנְזוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא. כִּי אָתֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִמַּאן נִבְעֵי? כִּי נִיחָא נַפְשֵׁיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׁקוּלוּ תְּלֵיסַר מָעֵי דְּצַיְירִי לִי בִּסְדִינַאי, וְאַהְדַּרוּ לֵיהּ לִפְלָנְיָא, דִּשְׁקַלְתִּינְהוּ מִינֵּיהּ וְלָא אִיצְטְרִיכוּ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The father of Rabbi Zeira collected** taxes for **thirteen years. When the head** tax collector of the **river** region **would come to the city,** Rabbi Zeira's father would prepare the residents ahead of time. **When he would see the rabbis, he would say to them** as a hint: **"Come, my people, enter into your chambers,** and shut your doors behind you; hide yourself for a little moment until the indignation has passed" (Isaiah 26:20). He said this so that the head tax collector would not see the rabbis, and it would be possible to lower the taxes of the city. **When he would see** the ordinary **people of the city, he would say** to them: Beware, as **the head** tax collector of the **river** region **is coming to the city, and will now slaughter the father,** i.e., take one's money, **before the son, and the son before the father. And everyone would hide. When** the head tax collector **would arrive,** Rabbi Zeira's father **would say to him: From whom shall I request** taxes? The city is scarcely populated, and only a small sum of taxes should be imposed on it. **When he was dying, he said to** those standing around his deathbed: **Take thirteen *ma'a* that are tied up in my sheet and return them to so-and-so, as I took them from him but I did not need them** to pay the tax.

The *daf* opens, properly speaking, three lines from the bottom of 25b, with a story offered as an exemplar of what a truly righteous tax collector looks like. The Gemara relates that the father of Rabbi Zeira served as a tax collector for the government for thirteen years. As we learned in the preceding discussion, a *stam* tax collector — one about whom nothing is known — is *kosher* until there is evidence that he is pocketing more than his due.

The story illustrates how this particular man managed to protect the townspeople he served. Whenever the regional official — the *reish nahara*, the head of the locale — came to town on a surprise visit, the father of Rabbi Zeira understood that this official would press him to collect higher taxes. He had apparently been reporting very low yields and demanding very little from the people. So what would he do when he saw that a visit was imminent? When he saw the rabbis out in the street, he would say to them cryptically: go back into your rooms. Let no one see you lest the visiting official notice all these people who might be exempt from taxation — or at least begin demanding more from them. His message to the rabbis was deliberately indirect; he did not want to be caught giving explicit instructions to hide. By contrast, when he saw the ordinary townspeople, he was somewhat more explicit, announcing: the boss is coming, and a father will be slaughtered in the presence of his son and a son in the presence of his father — meaning, the official will be furious and will impose crushing taxes upon everyone. The result in both cases was the same: *u-miglaz kulei alma*, everyone scattered and hid.

When the official finally arrived and demanded to know why so few taxes had been collected, the father of Rabbi Zeira replied: from whom should I collect? The town has been abandoned; there is no one here. And so the excuse held.

When he was on his deathbed, the father of Rabbi Zeira made a final request: take these thirteen *ma'ah* that I have bound up in my kerchief and return them to so-and-so, for I collected them from him as a tax payment, and in the end I did not need them — I managed to protect the people without using the money. The Gemara presents this as the portrait of a truly upright tax collector: someone who not only avoids personal enrichment but who goes to extraordinary lengths to protect those under his charge, and who in his final moments ensures that not a single coin was wrongfully retained.

### Otzrei vs. Sochrei Shevi'is

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בַּתְּחִילָּה הָיוּ קוֹרְאִין אוֹתָן ״אוֹסְפֵי שְׁבִיעִית״. מַאי קָאָמַר? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכִי קָאָמַר, בַּתְּחִילָּה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אוֹסְפֵי שְׁבִיעִית – כְּשֵׁרִין, סוֹחֲרִין – פְּסוּלִין. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ מַמְצִיאֵי מָעוֹת לַעֲנִיִּים, וְאָזְלִי עֲנִיִּים וְאָסְפִי לְהוּ וּמַיְיתוּ, חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה פְּסוּלִין. קָשׁוּ בָּהּ בְּנֵי רַחֲבָה: הַאי ״מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָאַנָּסִים״? ״מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הַתַּגָּרִין״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא, בַּתְּחִילָּה הָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה פְּסוּלִין. מִשֶּׁרַבּוּ הָאַנָּסִין – וּמַאי נִינְהוּ? אַרְנוֹנָא – כִּדְמַכְרִיז רַבִּי יַנַּאי: ״פּוּקוּ וּזְרַעוּ בַּשְּׁבִיעִית מִשּׁוּם אַרְנוֹנָא״. חָזְרוּ לוֹמַר: אוֹסְפִין כְּשֵׁרִין, סוֹחֲרִין פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Shimon said: Initially** people **would call them: Gatherers** of the produce **of the Sabbatical** Year. The Gemara asks: **What is he saying?** **Rav Yehuda says this** is what **he is saying: Initially** the Sages **would say** that **gatherers** of the produce **of** the **Sabbatical** Year, i.e., those who gather a large quantity of produce of the Sabbatical Year for themselves, **are fit** to bear witness, but **merchants** who trade in the produce of the Sabbatical Year are **disqualified. Once those who would offer money to the poor** to gather produce for them **grew abundant, and the poor would go and gather** the produce **for** those who hired them **and bring** it to them, then gathering a large amount of produce of the Sabbatical Year was considered a business. The Sages **then said** that **both this** category, gatherers of the produce of the Sabbatical Year, **and that** category, merchants who trade in the produce of the Sabbatical Year, are **disqualified. The children of** the Sage **Raḥava had a difficulty with this** explanation, as accordingly **this** phrase: **Once the tax collectors grew abundant,** is inaccurate; the mishna **should have** stated: **Once the merchants grew abundant. Rather,** Rabbi Shimon's statement should be explained differently, as follows: **Initially** the Sages **would say** that **both this** category, gatherers of the produce of the Sabbatical Year, **and that** category, merchants who trade in the produce of the Sabbatical Year, are **disqualified. Once the tax collectors grew abundant** it was permitted to gather the produce of the Sabbatical Year. **And what** tax did **they** collect? ***Arnona*,** which was a heavy tax on property collected both during the Sabbatical Year and in other years, placing pressure on those observing the Sabbatical Year. **As Rabbi Yannai proclaimed: Go out and sow** the fields **during the Sabbatical** Year **due to** the ***arnona*** that you must pay. Once this happened, the Sages **then said: Gatherers** of the produce of the Sabbatical Year **are fit** to bear witness, as they were no longer viewed as transgressors, but **merchants** who trade in this produce **are** still **disqualified.**

The Gemara now turns to explain a shift in terminology that appeared in the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* originally referred to those disqualified from testimony in connection with *shevi'is* as *otzrei shevi'is* — those who gather and hoard *shevi'is* produce — and then later referred to them as *sochrei shevi'is*, those who conduct commerce in *shevi'is* produce. The Gemara asks: what does this shift mean?

Rav Yehuda explains it as follows. Originally, the rule was that *otzrei shevi'is* — those who merely gathered the produce for personal use — were *kosher*, while *sochrei shevi'is* — those who actually conducted commerce in it — were *pasul*. The reasoning was that while gathering up one's own produce and keeping it, even past the *zman habiyur*, was not ideal, it did not constitute the kind of commercial violation that disqualifies a witness. However, people then discovered a workaround: they would pay poor people to go out and gather the *shevi'is* produce on their behalf. The poor people could argue they were merely gathering for personal use, but effectively they were collecting on behalf of someone engaged in commerce. Once this practice spread, the ruling changed: *chazru lomar echad zeh v'echad zeh psulim* — both the gatherer and the merchant are now *pasul*.

The *b'nei Rechav* objected to this reading of the *Mishnah*. The *Mishnah* uses the phrase *mishe-rabu ha-annasim*, "when the forceful ones multiplied," which they argued should have read *mishe-rabu ha-tagari*, "when the merchants multiplied," if the concern was simply commercial expansion. So Rava offers a different reading. Originally, the rule was that both gatherers and merchants were *pasul*. Then, *mishe-rabu ha-annasim* — when the strong-arm of the government increased its demands — the ruling changed. This refers to *arnona*, the government tax burden. At that point, as Rabbi Yannai publicly proclaimed: *puku v'ziru b'shevi'is* — go out and plant during *shevi'is*, because of the necessity of paying taxes. If people were being compelled to work their fields and harvest their crops in order to meet government demands, one could no longer declare that a gatherer is *pasul*. So *chazru lomar oskim ksheirim* — those who plant and gather are now permitted, even though this would ordinarily constitute a direct violation of *shemitah*. However, *sochrim psulim* still stands: if one is actually conducting commerce in *shevi'is* produce, one remains *pasul*.

### Arnona & Permissibility

This ruling raises an important question. How could *arnona* justify what would otherwise be a *d'oraysa* prohibition? *Tosafos* addresses this directly. The question is: *mishum arnona hitiru lachrosh velizro'a dehavai issur d'oraysa* — did they really permit a biblical violation merely because of tax demands?

*Tosafos* offers two answers. The first: perhaps this discussion concerns *shevi'is* in the present era, which may be only *d'rabbanan* in force, depending on various considerations regarding the sanctity of the land. If so, there would be room to permit it under significant economic pressure. The second answer: *ee nami, pikuach nefesh hu* — there was an element of mortal danger involved. The king sent a tax demand, they had nothing with which to pay, and they faced imprisonment at the king's hands, which could lead to death. *Tosafos* adds that the *Yerushalmi* states this explicitly in terms of *chayei nefesh*, preserving life.

It is striking that the *Bavli* includes no such explicit reference to life-threatening danger, whereas the *Yerushalmi* makes the mortal stakes clear. This creates two quite different interpretive frameworks: either this was a matter of *pikuach nefesh*, which would justify even a *d'oraysa* violation, or it was a *d'rabbanan* violation that was set aside because of severe and genuine economic pressure even without an immediate threat to life.

This *Gemara* becomes significant in the later discussions of *heter mechira*. Rav Kook invokes it substantially, because if we accept *Tosafos*'s first reading — that *shemitah* in the present era is *d'rabbanan* and that they permitted direct violation because of economic compulsion — then that framework can be applied to argue that under similar pressures, and given the rabbinic nature of the prohibition, other mechanisms such as the sale of land are certainly no less permissible. In fact, the argument would run: here they permitted the violation directly; a workaround like a land sale is even more lenient.

## Ibur Shana Story

### Encounters & Excuses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר זַרְנוֹקֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק הֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי לְעַבֵּר שָׁנָה בְּעַסְיָא. פְּגַע בְּהוּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אִיטַּפַּל בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ. אָמַר: אֵיזִיל אִיחְזֵי הֵיכִי עָבְדִי עוֹבָדָא. חַזְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקָא כָרֵיב. אָמַר לָהֶן: כֹּהֵן וְחוֹרֵשׁ! אָמְרוּ לוֹ, יָכוֹל לוֹמַר: ״אַגִּיסְטְוָן אֲנִי בְּתוֹכוֹ״. תּוּ חַזְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה כָּסַח בְּכַרְמֵי. אָמַר לָהֶן: כֹּהֵן וְזָמַר! אָמְרוּ לוֹ: יָכוֹל לוֹמַר ״לְעֵקֶל בֵּית הַבַּד אֲנִי צָרִיךְ״. אָמַר לָהֶם: הַלֵּב יוֹדֵעַ אִם לְעֵקֶל אִם לַעֲקַלְקַלּוֹת. הֵי אֲמַר לְהוּ בְּרֵישָׁא? אִילֵימָא הָא קַמַּיְיתָא אֲמַר לְהוּ בְּרֵישָׁא, הָא נָמֵי לֵימְרוּ: ״אַגִּיסְטְוָן אֲנִי בְּתוֹכוֹ״! אֶלָּא, הָא אֲמַר לְהוּ בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר אֲמַר לְהוּ הָךְ. מַאי שְׁנָא כֹּהֵן? מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲשִׁידִי אַשְּׁבִיעִית. דִּתְנַן: סְאָה תְּרוּמָה שֶׁנָּפְלָה לְמֵאָה סְאִין שֶׁל שְׁבִיעִית – תַּעֲלֶה. פָּחוֹת מִיכֵּן – יֵרָקְבוּ. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: אַמַּאי יֵרָקְבוּ? יִמְכְּרֶנּוּ לַכֹּהֵן בִּדְמֵי תְרוּמָה, חוּץ מִדְּמֵי אוֹתָהּ סְאָה. וְאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, נֶחְשְׁדוּ כֹּהֲנִים אַשְּׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara recounts an incident connected tangentially to the discussion concerning the Sabbatical Year: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Zarnokei and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak were going to intercalate the year in Asya,** as circumstances did not enable them to perform the intercalation of the year in Eretz Yisrael. **Reish Lakish met them** and **joined with them. He said: I will go see how they do the deed,** i.e., how the intercalation is performed practically. Reish Lakish **saw a certain man plowing** a field. **He said to** the other Sages: Look at this **priest** who is **plowing** during the Sabbatical Year. **They said to him** that he **could say** in his own defense: **I am a hired worker [*agiston*] in** the field, which belongs to a gentile. Therefore, it is permitted. Reish Lakish **further saw a certain man pruning** vines **in** the **vineyards. He said to** the other Sages: Look at this **priest** who is **pruning** vines during the Sabbatical Year. **They said to him** that he **could say: I need** the branches **to** make **a bale [*ekel* ]** for my **olive press,** i.e., a basket to hold olives for pressing. Since he is not pruning the vines for agricultural purposes but to use the branches, it is permitted for him to do so in the Sabbatical Year. Reish Lakish **said to them,** in a play on words: **The heart knows whether** he is doing so **for a bale [*ekel* ], or** whether he is saying this **as deceit [*la'akalkalot*].** The Gemara asks: **Which did he say to them first?** Which incident occurred first? **If we say** that **first he said to them that first** exclamation, that a priest was plowing the field, **let them say** to him in **this** latter incident **too,** in which someone was pruning vines, that he could claim in his defense: **I am a hired worker in** the field. This would have been a better justification than claiming he needed the branches. **Rather,** apparently **first he said to them this** exclamation, that a priest was pruning vines, **and** only **then he said to them that** a priest was plowing the field. The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **a priest?** Why did Reish Lakish assume the man was a priest? The Gemara answers: It is **because** priests **are suspected of** desecrating the **Sabbatical** Year. **As we learned** in a *baraita*: **A *se'a* of *teruma*,** the portion of the produce designated for the priest, **that fell into a hundred *se'a* of Sabbatical** Year produce and was indistinguishable, **is nullified,** i.e., it no longer has the status of *teruma*, and can be eaten until the time that Sabbatical Year produce must be removed from one's possession. If it fell into **a lesser** quantity of Sabbatical Year produce, the entire mixture **must** be left to **rot,** as it is prohibited for non-priests to partake of *teruma*. **And we discussed it: Why must** the produce **rot? Let** its owner **sell it to a priest in accordance with** its **value as *teruma*,** which is less expensive than regular produce because it can be eaten only by priests, and **except for the value of that *se'a*** of *teruma* that fell in, which the owner is required to give to a priest for free. The priest may then partake of the produce, treating the entire mixture as though it has the sanctity of *teruma*, and both the priest and the owner will benefit. **And Rav Ḥiyya said in the name of Ulla: That is to say,** i.e., one can infer from the fact that one is not permitted to sell the mixture to a priest, that **priests are suspected of** desecrating **the Sabbatical** Year by partaking of its produce after the time it must be removed from one's possession. They did so because they considered it theirs, the same as *teruma*, as both mitzvot pertain to the produce of Eretz Yisrael. Therefore, produce of the Sabbatical Year should not be given or sold to priests. Similarly, Reish Lakish assumed that those who desecrated the Sabbatical Year were priests.

With the halachic background established, the Gemara introduces a narrative that bears directly on the question of *shevi'is* and the fitness of those who tolerate its violation to serve in rabbinic roles. Rabbi Chiyya bar Zarnuki and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak were traveling to perform an *ibur shana* — to add an intercalary month to the year — in Asya, which is outside of *Eretz Yisrael*. *Tosafos* raises the question that one is generally not supposed to extend a *shemitah* year, and also that *ibur shana* is not supposed to be performed outside of *Eretz Yisrael* — and indeed, it may be that the intercalation was being calculated for the following year, not the current one.

Reish Lakish encountered them on the road and asked to accompany them, saying he wished to observe how such a proceeding was conducted. As they traveled — presumably still within *Eretz Yisrael* — he saw a man plowing. He exclaimed to the two rabbis: this man is plowing during *shemitah*! They replied that he could say he was an *agiston*, an imperial worker — meaning either that he was working land belonging to a non-Jew (which raises its own questions about whether a Jew may work a non-Jew's land in *Eretz Yisrael* during *shemitah*), or, as *Tosafos* prefers, that he was effectively conscripted — compelled to work the land as a government laborer in order to pay the *arnona*. Under those circumstances, as we have seen, the violation might be condoned.

Continuing on, Reish Lakish saw a *kohen* pruning a vineyard. He exclaimed: a *kohen*, and he is pruning on *shemitah*?! They replied that he could say he was pruning not to tend the vines but to obtain the wood for his olive press. To this, Reish Lakish responded: *ha-lev yodeia im l'akel im l'akalkalot* — the heart knows whether you are genuinely in need of the wood or whether this is simply a pretext.

The Gemara then asks: which of these two incidents occurred first? If one thinks it was the plowing incident, a difficulty arises: once they had given Reish Lakish the answer about the *agiston*, he should have realized — or they should have known to tell him — that the same kind of justification could apply to the pruning. The fact that they did not suggests the pruning came first. Indeed the Gemara concludes: the vineyard incident happened first, and then came the plowing, at which point they offered the *agiston* excuse.

The Gemara then asks: why did Reish Lakish specifically note that the pruner was a *kohen*? One might assume it was simply to add moral emphasis — a *kohen* is expected to be particularly scrupulous, so the violation was all the more striking. But the Gemara offers a different answer: *mishum d'chashidei a'shvi'is* — *kohanim* as a group were specifically suspected of laxity regarding *shemitah*. Rashi explains that they rationalized their behavior: since they were permitted to use *terumah* — produce with a degree of sanctity — they concluded that they were entitled to work the land on *shemitah* and benefit from its produce as well. If the *Torah* grants us access to sanctified produce, they reasoned, surely we may also work the sacred soil.

The Gemara cites a *tanna* in support of this inference. If a *seah* of *terumah* falls into one hundred *seah* of *shevi'is* produce, it is *battel*. But if there is less than one hundred, the entire mixture must be allowed to rot. The Gemara asks: why must it rot? One might suggest selling the whole mixture to a *kohen* at the price of *terumah*, since he must treat it all as *terumah* — he must be *tahor* and careful with it, but at least it would not go entirely to waste. Why not do that? Rabbi Chiyya in the name of Ulla answers: *nechsh'du kohanim a'shvi'is* — *kohanim* are not trusted with *shevi'is* produce. If you sell it to a *kohen*, he will eat it past the time when it is permitted. From this we learn that *kohanim* were under general suspicion regarding *shevi'is*.

It should be noted that while the suspicion in this source concerns eating *shevi'is* produce past the time of *biyur*, the incident in the story involves a *kohen* actually working the land — a far more overt violation. Yet Rashi's explanation ties it all together: the underlying rationalization — that the *Torah*'s allowance of sanctified produce extends to *shemitah* — led *kohanim* to be lax across the range of *shevi'is* prohibitions.

*Tosafos* raises a related question: how could one even consider selling the mixture to a *kohen*, given the prohibition against doing business with *shevi'is* produce? *Tosafos* answers by distinguishing between commercial activity — ongoing, profit-driven trade in *shevi'is* — and a one-time, incidental sale. If someone gathered *shevi'is* produce for personal use, consumed most of it, and then sold a small remainder, that does not constitute doing business with *shevi'is*. It was a chance occurrence, not a commercial enterprise. The prohibition on *sechorah* with *shevi'is* produce targets systematic commercial activity, not isolated transactions.

### Reish Lakish's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ: טָרוֹדָא הוּא דֵּין. כִּי מְטוֹ לְהָתָם, סְלִיקוּ לְאִיגָּרָא, שַׁלְּפוּהּ לְדַרְגָּא מִתּוּתֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּנֵי אָדָם הַחֲשׁוּדִין עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית, כְּשֵׁרִין לְעַבֵּר שָׁנָה? הֲדַר אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא לִי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַשְּׁלֹשָׁה רוֹעֵי בָקָר, וְרַבָּנַן אַחוּשְׁבָּנַיְיהוּ סְמוּךְ. וַהֲדַר אָמַר: לָא דָּמֵי. הָתָם, הֲדוּר אִימְּנוֹ רַבָּנַן וְעַבְּרוּהָ לְהָהִוא שַׁתָּא. הָכָא, קֶשֶׁר רְשָׁעִים הוּא, וְקֶשֶׁר רְשָׁעִים אֵינוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דָּא עָקָא. כִּי אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: קָרֵי לַן ״רוֹעֵי בָקָר״, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר וְלָא מִידֵּי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: וְאִי קָרֵי לְכוּ ״רוֹעֵי צֹאן״, מַאי אָמֵינָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** After Reish Lakish continually bothered them with his comments, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Zarnokei and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak **said: This one is a nuisance.** In order to rid themselves of him, **when they reached** the location where they intended to intercalate the year, **they went up to the roof** and **took the ladder out from under them** so that he would stay below and would not be able to join them. Sometime later, Reish Lakish **came before Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Reish Lakish **said to him:** Are **people who are suspected of** desecrating **the Sabbatical** Year **fit to intercalate the year?** He believed that the two Sages who were sent to intercalate the year treated the sanctity of the Sabbatical Year with disrespect. Reish Lakish **then said** in response to his own question: This is **not difficult for me; just as** the Sages once intercalated the year in accordance with the assessment of **three cattle herders** (see 18b), which was permitted, as **the Sages depended** not on the assessment of the cattle herders but **on their** own **calculation,** perhaps here too the Sages depended on their own calculations and not on the decision of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Zarnokei and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak. Reish Lakish changed his mind **and then said: It is not similar. There,** in the case of the cattle herders, **afterward the Sages were counted,** i.e., they made a decision, **and intercalated that year** themselves. **Here,** by contrast, **it is a conspiracy of wicked people,** as the Sages who intercalated the year are disqualified from doing so; **and a conspiracy of wicked people is not counted. Rabbi Yoḥanan said: This** problem you raise **is troublesome.** **When** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Zarnokei and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak subsequently **came before Rabbi Yoḥanan,** after hearing about his discussion with Reish Lakish, **they said to him** bitterly: Reish Lakish **referred to us as cattle herders, and the Master said nothing to him** in response? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to them** dismissively: **And** even **if he had referred to you as shepherds,** who are disqualified from bearing witness in addition to being disqualified from serving as judges, **what should I have said to him?** He is correct.

When they finally arrived at their destination, Rabbi Chiyya bar Zarnuki and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak went up to the attic to conduct the *ibur shana* proceedings, and they arranged to leave Reish Lakish out. They had decided he was *turda* — a nuisance, a headache — for holding them to such a rigorous standard while they were trying to navigate the realities of the time.

Reish Lakish went to Rabbi Yochanan and asked: are people who are *chashudim* on *shevi'is* fit to perform *ibur ha-shanah*? This question resonates directly with the *Mishnah* under discussion, since among those disqualified are *sochrei shevi'is*. These rabbis were not themselves violating *shemitah*, but they were passively condoning violations they witnessed on the road — and Reish Lakish argued that this compromised their fitness for so significant a rabbinic function.

Reish Lakish then attempted to walk back his own critique. Perhaps, he suggested, they could be like the cowherds in the famous story where three cowherds were observed talking about agricultural conditions and their observations were used by the rabbis to determine whether the year warranted intercalation. Those cowherds were not themselves rabbinic authorities, yet the rabbis relied on what they said. Similarly, perhaps Rabbi Chiyya bar Zarnuki and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak could be said to serve in a merely evidentiary role. This echo is pointed: the *Mishnah* mentions *ro'ei tzon* — shepherds — and *ro'ei bakar* — cowherds — among those who are *pasul*, and here the comparison is being made deliberately.

But Reish Lakish rejected his own analogy. *Lo dami* — it is not comparable. In the case of the cowherds, the rabbis heard what they said and then convened separately and performed the *ibur shana* themselves, based on their own *cheshbon*. The cowherds were merely a source of information, not participants in the proceeding. Here, the very rabbis who tolerated the violations of *shemitah* are themselves conducting the *ibur shana*. That is categorically different: *kesher resha'im hu* — it is a band of wicked people. *Ve-kesher resha'im eino min ha-minyan* — a band of wicked people cannot be counted toward the convocation required to perform the intercalation.

Rabbi Yochanan's response was pointed in a different direction: *da'aka* — that hurts. To call these rabbis *resha'im* goes too far. That was his only objection.

When Rabbi Chiyya bar Zarnuki and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak subsequently came before Rabbi Yochanan, they complained: Reish Lakish called us *ro'ei bakar* — cowherds — and you said nothing. You only defended us when he called us *resha'im*. Rabbi Yochanan replied: if he had called you *ro'ei tzon* — shepherds, which the tradition considers worse than cowherds, as we saw in the preceding material — what would I have said? He would have been right. You were tolerating violations of *shemitah*. You are not *resha'im*, but you bear responsibility for not doing more.

What is striking here is the contrast between the *Yerushalmi* and the *Bavli* in their handling of this reality. The *Yerushalmi*, living within the circumstances of *Eretz Yisrael*, presents the situation as an unambiguous matter of *pikuach nefesh* and raises no hint of criticism toward those who tolerated the violations. The *Bavli*, by contrast, contains no suggestion that lives were at risk — which is why *Tosafos* raises the alternative explanation that *shemitah* may have been *d'rabbanan* at the time. And the *Bavli's* narrative concludes with sharp, if nuanced, criticism: Reish Lakish's critique of the rabbis for their passivity is vindicated at least in part, even if calling them *resha'im* was a step too far. This distance from the circumstances — being outside the land, outside the immediate pressures — makes it easier to hold a more demanding standard.

## Shevna Narrative

### Shevna's Plot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קֶשֶׁר רְשָׁעִים? שֶׁבְנָא הֲוָה דָּרֵישׁ בִּתְלֵיסַר רִבְּוָותָא, חִזְקִיָּה הֲוָה דָּרֵישׁ בְּחַד סַר רִבְּוָותָא. כִּי אֲתָא סַנְחֵרִיב וְצַר עֲלַהּ דִּירוּשְׁלֶם, כְּתַב שֶׁבְנָא פִּתְקָא שְׁדָא בְּגִירָא: ״שֶׁבְנָא וְסִיעָתוֹ הִשְׁלִימוּ, חִזְקִיָּה וְסִיעָתוֹ לֹא הִשְׁלִימוּ״. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי הִנֵּה הָרְשָׁעִים יִדְרְכוּן קֶשֶׁת כּוֹנְנוּ חִצָּם עַל יֶתֶר״. הֲוָה קָא מִסְתְּפֵי חִזְקִיָּה, אֲמַר: דִּילְמָא חַס וְשָׁלוֹם נָטְיָה דַּעְתֵּיהּ דְּקוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא בָּתַר רוּבָּא, כֵּיוָן דְּרוּבָּא מִימַּסְרִי אִינְהוּ נָמֵי מִימַּסְרִי. בָּא נָבִיא וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״לֹא תֹאמְרוּן קֶשֶׁר לְכֹל אֲשֶׁר יֹאמַר הָעָם הַזֶּה קָשֶׁר״, כְּלוֹמַר, קֶשֶׁר רְשָׁעִים הוּא, וְקֶשֶׁר רְשָׁעִים אֵינוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן. הָלַךְ לַחְצֹב לוֹ קֶבֶר בְּקִבְרֵי בֵּית דָּוִד. בָּא נָבִיא וְאָמַר לוֹ: ״מָה לְּךָ פֹה וּמִי לְךָ פֹה כִּי חָצַבְתָּ לְּךָ פֹּה קָבֶר... הִנֵּה ה׳ מְטַלְטֶלְךָ טַלְטֵלָה גָּבֶר״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What** is the source of the *halakha* that **a conspiracy of wicked people** is not counted as part of a group? The Gemara answers: **Shebna,** a steward and a minister in King Hezekiah's court, was a prominent and influential figure. **He would teach** Torah **to** an audience of **130,000** followers, whereas King **Hezekiah would teach** Torah **to** an audience of merely **110,000** followers. **When Sennacherib came and besieged Jerusalem, Shebna wrote a note and shot** it over the wall **with an arrow.** It read: **Shebna and his camp have appeased** Sennacherib and are ready to surrender; **Hezekiah and his camp have not appeased** Sennacherib. **As it is stated** in allusion to this incident: **"For behold, the wicked bend the bow, they have made ready their arrow upon the string"** (Psalms 11:2). **Hezekiah was afraid. He said: Perhaps, God forbid, the opinion of the Holy One, Blessed be He, will follow the majority;** and **since the majority have submitted** to the Assyrians, even **those** who have not submitted **will also be submitted** into their hands. **The prophet** Isaiah then **came and said to him: "Say not: A conspiracy, concerning all of which this people say: A conspiracy"** (Isaiah 8:12). **Meaning, it is a conspiracy of wicked people, and a conspiracy of wicked people is not counted.** Therefore, although they are many, they are not considered the majority. Shebna **went to carve out a grave for himself among the graves of the house of David,** as he thought that the kingship would be given to him. **The prophet** Isaiah then **came and said to him: "What have you here, and whom have you here, that you have carved out a grave for yourself here?** You, who have carved yourself out a grave on high, and hollowed a habitation for yourself in the rock? **Behold, the Lord will make you wander like the wandering of a man;** and he will wind you round and round" (Isaiah 22:16–17).

The discussion of *kesher resha'im* — the band of wicked people who cannot be counted toward a *minyan* — now prompts an *aggadic* digression into the source of this principle. Where does the idea come from that a *kesher resha'im eino min ha-minyan*?

The Gemara introduces Shevna, who served as a senior official in the court of King Hezekiah. Shevna would teach *Torah* publicly, and his classes drew thirteen myriads — 130,000 attendees. Hezekiah himself commanded only eleven myriads. On the strength of his larger following, Shevna regarded himself as the more consequential figure.

When Sennacherib besieged Jerusalem, Shevna wrote a note, fired it out of the city on an arrow to Sennacherib's camp, and in it declared: Shevna and his faction have already made peace with you and are prepared to open the gates of Jerusalem and submit. Hezekiah and his faction refuse to surrender. The Gemara derives this from the verse in Psalms: *ki hinei ha-resha'im yidrechun keshet* — the wicked bend the bow; *konennu hitzam al yeter* — they aim their arrow at *yeter*, which Shevna read as a reference to his superior numbers. He was betting on his majority.

Hezekiah was frightened. He worried that God might, as it were, side with the majority — invoking the principle of *acharei rabim le-hatot*, following the majority. If Shevna commanded more followers, perhaps the divine calculus would follow the larger number.

The prophet came to Hezekiah and reassured him with the verse: *lo tomrun kesher le-chol asher yomar ha-am ha-zeh kesher* — do not call it a band in the way this people calls it a band. In other words: yes, they may be numerically larger, but *kesher resha'im hu* — it is a band of wicked people. *Ve-kesher resha'im eino min ha-minyan* — and the wicked do not count toward the majority. Hezekiah need not fear: the true majority is on his side.

The Gemara then continues the story of Shevna's downfall. Thinking himself the equal of kings, Shevna had gone ahead and carved out a sepulchre for himself among the tombs of the Davidic dynasty. The prophet confronted him: *ma lecha po u-mi lecha po* — what gives you the right to be here, among the graves of kings? And then God's punishment is described: *hinei Hashem metaltelecha taltela gaver* — God will send you into exile with the exile of a man. Rav observes from the word *gaver* that exile is harder for a man than for a woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: טִלְטוּלָא דְּגַבְרָא קָשֵׁי מִדְּאִיתְּתָא. ״וְעֹטְךָ עָטֹה״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁפָּרְחָה בּוֹ צָרַעַת. כְּתִיב הָכָא ״וְעֹטְךָ עָטֹה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״וְעַל שָׂפָם יַעְטֶה״. ״צָנוֹף יִצְנׇפְךָ צְנֵפָה כַּדּוּר אֶל אֶרֶץ רַחֲבַת יָדָיִם וְגוֹ׳״. תָּנָא: הוּא בִּיקֵּשׁ קְלוֹן בֵּית אֲדֹנָיו, לְפִיכָךְ נֶהְפַּךְ כְּבוֹדוֹ לְקָלוֹן. כִּי הֲוָה נָפֵיק אִיהוּ, אֲתָא גַּבְרִיאֵל אַחְדֵּיהּ לְדַשָּׁא בְּאַפֵּי מַשְׁרְיָיתֵיהּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַשְׁירְיָיתָךְ הֵיכָא? אֲמַר: הֲדַרוּ בִּי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, אַחוֹכֵי קָא מַחְיְיכַתְּ בַּן! נְקָבוּהוּ בַּעֲקָבָיו וּתְלָאוּהוּ בְּזַנְבֵי סוּסֵיהֶם, וְהָיוּ מְגָרְרִין אוֹתוֹ עַל הַקּוֹצִים וְעַל הַבַּרְקָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara mentions tangentially: **Rav says** that **the wandering of a man is more difficult than that of a woman.** He derives it from the expression "like the wandering of a man." With regard to the phrase: **"And he will wind you round and round," Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, says:** This **teaches that** Shebna **developed leprosy; it is written here: "And he will wind you round and round [*ve'otkha ato*]," and it is written there,** in the Torah, with regard to a leper: "And the leper in whom the plague is, his clothes shall be torn, and the hair of his head shall go loose, **and he shall cover his upper lip [*ya'te*]"** (Leviticus 13:45). The Hebrew word for upper lip is from the same root as the verb in the verse cited from Isaiah. **"He will violently roll and toss you like a ball into a large country;** there you shall die, and there shall be the chariots of your glory, you shame of your lord's house" (Isaiah 22:18). A *baraita* **taught: He,** Shebna, **desired shame for his master's house; therefore his glory turned to shame.** This is what happened to him: **When he was going out** of the gate of Jerusalem to submit to the Assyrians, the angel **Gabriel came** and **held the gate in front of his camp** so they could not follow him. Consequently, he went out by himself. The Assyrians **said to him: Where is your camp?** Shebna **said: They backed out on me. They said to him: If so, you are mocking us;** you led us to believe that behind you stands a large camp of supporters. **They punched holes in his heels and hung him by the tails of their horses, and dragged him on the thorns and on the bristles.**

Furthermore, *va-atecha ata* — you will be covered with a covering. Rabbi Yossi bar Rabbi Chanina interprets this through a *gezerah shavah*: just as the *metzora* covers his upper lip, so too Shevna was struck with *tzara'as*.

The verse concludes: *tzanof yitznefecha tznifa ka-dur el eretz rachavat yadayim* — you will be hurled like a ball across a wide land. *Tosafos* explains this as being rolled like a ball across the open country.

The Gemara also states: *hu bikesh kalon beis adonav* — he sought to bring shame upon the house of his master, Hezekiah, and therefore *kvodo le-kalon nehpach* — his own honor was turned to disgrace.

When Shevna finally tried to go out with his 130,000 followers to surrender to Sennacherib, the angel Gabriel appeared and closed the gate, cutting him off from his entire entourage. He went out alone. When Sennacherib's troops asked him where his people were, he claimed they must have abandoned him. They replied: then you have made a mockery of us — you promised us 130,000 people and you appear alone. They pierced his heels, suspended him from the tails of their horses, and dragged him across thorns and thistles.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שֶׁבְנָא בַּעַל הֲנָאָה הָיָה. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״לֶךְ בֹּא אֶל הַסֹּכֵן הַזֶּה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַתְּהִי לוֹ סֹכֶנֶת״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Shebna was a hedonist. It is written here: "Go, get yourself to this steward [*hasokhen*],** to Shebna, who is over the house" (Isaiah 22:15), **and it is written there** with regard to Abishag the Shunammite: **"And let her be a companion [*sokhenet*] to him;** and let her lie in your bosom, that my lord the king may get heat" (I Kings 1:2).

Rabbi Eliezer notes that Shevna was a man driven by sensual pleasure — the Talmud reads the description of his role through a verbal parallel to Avishag the Shunamite, who served King David. The verse *lech bo el ha-sochen* is linked by the word *sochen* to *va-tehi lo sochenes*, associating Shevna's position with physical indulgence. He was, in a word, a hedonist.

### Foundations & Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״כִּי הַשָּׁתוֹת יֵהָרֵסוּן צַדִּיק מַה פָּעָל״. רַב יְהוּדָה וְרַב עֵינָא, חַד אָמַר: אִילּוּ חִזְקִיָּה וְסִיעָתוֹ נֶהֱרָסִים, צַדִּיק מַה פָּעָל? וְחַד אָמַר: אִילּוּ בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ יַהָרֵס, צַדִּיק מַה פָּעָל? וְעוּלָּא אָמַר: אִילּוּ מַחְשְׁבוֹתָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע אֵינָן נֶהֱרָסוֹת, צַדִּיק מַה פָּעָל? בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִילּוּ מַחְשְׁבוֹתָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי הַשָּׁתוֹת יֵהָרֵסוּן״. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, נָמֵי – דִּתְנַן: אֶבֶן הָיְתָה שָׁם מִימוֹת נְבִיאִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, וּשְׁתִיָּיה הָיְתָה נִקְרֵאת. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה וְסִיעָתוֹ, הֵיכָא אַשְׁכְּחַן צַדִּיקֵי דְּאִיקְּרוּ ״שָׁתוֹת״? דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי לַה׳ מְצֻקֵי אֶרֶץ וַיָּשֶׁת עֲלֵיהֶם תֵּבֵל״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא מֵהָכָא: ״הִפְלִיא עֵצָה הִגְדִּיל תּוּשִׁיָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of "For behold, the wicked bend the bow, they have made ready their arrow upon the string. When the foundations are destroyed, what has the Righteous One done?"** (Psalms 11:2–3). **Rav Yehuda and Rav Eina** interpret this verse. **One says: If Hezekiah and his camp are destroyed, what has the Righteous One done? And one says: If the Temple should be destroyed, what has the Righteous One done? And Ulla says: If the intentions of that wicked person,** Sennacherib, **are not destroyed, what has the Righteous One done?** The Gemara asks: **Granted, according to the one who says** that the verse means: **If the intentions of that wicked person** are not destroyed, what has the Righteous One done, **this is** the reason **that it is written: "When the foundations [*hashatot*] are destroyed,"** i.e., the intentions of Sennacherib are destroyed, as intentions are called *shatot*. **And according to the one who says** it is referring to **the Temple,** the word *hashatot* is **also** clear, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Yoma* 53b): **There was a stone** in the Holy of Holies **from the days of the early prophets,** David and Samuel, who laid the groundwork for construction of the Temple, **and** this stone **was called** the **Foundation [*shetiyya*]** Stone. **But according to the one who says** that the reference is to **Hezekiah and his camp, where do we find that righteous people are referred to as *shatot*?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written:** "He raises up the poor out of the dust, He lifts up the needy from the dung hill, to make them sit with princes and inherit the throne of glory; **for the pillars of the earth are the Lord's, and He has set [*vayyashet*] the world upon them"** (I Samuel 2:8). Since the righteous are considered the foundations of the world, this verse is interpreted in reference to them. **And if you wish, say** instead that it is derived **from here: "Wonderful is His counsel, and great His wisdom [*tushiyya*]"** (Isaiah 28:29). Accordingly, the wise, righteous people are called *shatot*.

The Gemara now turns to a verse that connects this narrative to a broader theological and philosophical reflection: *ki ha-shatos yeharesun tzaddik mah pa'al* — if the foundations are destroyed, what will the righteous one have done? What does this verse mean in this context?

Rav Yehuda and Rav Ina offer two interpretations. One says: the verse is asking — had Hezekiah and his circle been destroyed, had Shevna succeeded in handing over the city, what would God have had left to give Hezekiah for his righteousness? The word *shatos*, foundations, refers here to Hezekiah and his followers, who are the foundational pillars upon which the world rests. The other says: the verse refers to the *Beis Ha-Mikdash*. Had Sennacherib entered and destroyed the Temple — with Shevna's treachery enabling this — where would God's promises have stood? What would have remained of divine providence?

Ulla offers a third interpretation that runs in the opposite direction: the *shatos* that needed to be destroyed were the schemes of Shevna himself. *Eilu machshavosav shel oso rasha einam neharasos* — had the plot of that wicked man not been destroyed, *tzaddik mah pa'al* — where would have been God's intervention on behalf of the righteous?

The Gemara explains the linguistic connections. For those who understand *shatos* as referring to Shevna's schemes, Rashi explains the word as related to *lo shas libo* — placing one's heart, i.e., one's plans and intentions. For those who understand *shatos* as referring to the *Beis Ha-Mikdash*, the connection is to the *even shesiyah* — the foundation stone — as recorded in the *Mishnah* about the stone that stood in the Temple from the days of the early prophets and was called *shesiyah*. For those who understand it as referring to Hezekiah and his circle, the Gemara asks: where do we find the righteous described as *shatos*? From the verse *ki l'Hashem metzukei eretz va-yashes aleihem tevel* — God has the pillars of the earth and places the world upon them. The righteous and the scholars of Torah are those pillars; the world is sustained upon them. And there is a further proof-text: *hifli eitzah higdil tushiyah* — the counsel is great, the *tushiyah* is great — where *tushiyah* refers to Torah. Torah is the foundation. And therefore Hezekiah and those who study and uphold Torah are themselves the foundations upon which the world stands.

### Machshavah & Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָנָן: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ תּוּשִׁיָּה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא מַתֶּשֶׁת כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם. דָּבָר אַחֵר: תּוּשִׁיָּה – שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה בַּחֲשַׁאי מִפְּנֵי הַשָּׂטָן. דָּבָר אַחֵר: תּוּשִׁיָּה – דְּבָרִים שֶׁל תּוֹהוּ שֶׁהָעוֹלָם מְשׁוּתָּת עֲלֵיהֶם. אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַחְשָׁבָה מוֹעֶלֶת אֲפִילּוּ לְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מֵפֵר מַחְשְׁבוֹת עֲרוּמִים וְלֹא תַעֲשֶׂינָה יְדֵיהֶם תּוּשִׁיָּה״. אָמַר רַבָּה: אִם עֲסוּקִין לִשְׁמָהּ, אֵינָהּ מוֹעֶלֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רַבּוֹת מַחֲשָׁבוֹת בְּלֶב אִישׁ וַעֲצַת ה׳ הִיא תָקוּם״. עֵצָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ דְּבַר ה׳ הִיא תָּקוּם לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the latter verse, **Rabbi Ḥanan says: Why is** the Torah **called *tushiyya*? Because it weakens [*matteshet*] the strength of a person** who engages in its study. **Alternatively, *tushiyya*** can be interpreted as an abbreviation: **That it was given in secret [*shenittena beḥashai*].** This was done **because of the Satan,** lest he claim that the Jewish people are not worthy of it. **Alternatively, *tushiyya*** can be interpreted as an abbreviation for **amorphous [*tohu*] matters that** seem foreign and strange, but nevertheless **the world is founded [*meshotat*] on them. Ulla says: Thought,** i.e., concern, **is effective [*mo'elet*] in** disturbing **even** the study of **statements of Torah, as it is stated: "He frustrates the thoughts of the crafty, so that their hands can perform nothing [*velo…tushiyya*]"** (Job 5:12), and *tushiyya* is a reference to Torah. **Rabba says: If** people **engage** in Torah study **for its own sake,** concern **is not effective; as it is stated: "There are many devices in a man's heart; but the counsel of the Lord, that shall stand"** (Proverbs 19:21). Rabba interprets this to mean that **a counsel that has in it the statement of the Lord shall rise forever** and cannot be disturbed.

This leads the Gemara to a series of reflections on *tushiyah* — the term the *Torah* uses to describe understanding or wisdom — and why the *Torah* itself might be called by this name.

Rav Chanan asks: *lamah nikra shemah tushiyah* — why is Torah called *tushiyah*? *Mipnei she-hi masheshes kocho shel adam* — because it weakens a person. The pursuit of Torah study is demanding; it taxes one's physical and mental strength.

Another interpretation: *tushiyah she-nisnah be-chashai* — it was given quietly, in private, not with the thunderous public revelation one might expect. This is because of *ha-satan*, the prosecutorial force in the divine realm. The second giving of the *Torah*, in particular, is associated with this quality of quiet transmission, echoed in the *kol demamah dakkah*, the still small voice.

A third interpretation is the most philosophically striking. *Tushiyah devarim shel tohu she-ha-olam mishtates aleihem* — Torah consists of words that are, physically speaking, of the category of *tohu*, nothingness. Words have no material form; they are, in a physical sense, nothing. Air. And yet the world is sustained upon them. The very thing that seems most insubstantial — the spoken and studied word of Torah — is what underlies all of reality. As one might observe: if God Himself, whose word has no physical form, created the entire world through speech, it is of a piece that the words of Torah, equally incorporeal, should be the foundation upon which the world stands.

Finally, Ulla makes a practical observation: *machshavah mo'eles afilu le-divrei Torah* — troublesome thoughts have an effect even upon words of Torah. As wonderful and foundational as Torah study is, a person who is perpetually anxious and preoccupied with worries will find that those thoughts impair even their ability to learn. The proof-text: *mefir machshavos arumim velo ta'asena yedeihem tushiyah* — God undoes the schemes of the clever, and their hands cannot achieve *tushiyah*, cannot reach Torah wisdom. Anxiety and preoccupation can foreclose the very engagement with Torah that gives the world its foundation.

Rava qualifies this: *osukim lishmah einam mo'eles* — if one is learning Torah *lishmah*, for its own sake, then the troublesome thoughts will not prevail. *Rabos machshavos be-lev ish va'atzas Hashem hi sakum* — a person may have many thoughts in their heart, but it is God's counsel — *etzas Hashem*, that which is dedicated entirely to God — that will endure. If one's learning is genuinely oriented toward the divine purpose and not toward other ends, then the distractions and anxieties of the world will not succeed in disrupting it. The Torah, studied *lishmah*, remains standing.
