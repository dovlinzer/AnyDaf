# Beitzah 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Days Yom Tov

### Egg Permissibility

The daf opens mid-discussion at the bottom of 4b, three lines before the text widens, beginning with the word *itmar*. The subject is *shnei yamim tovim shel galuyos* — the two-day *yom tov* of the Diaspora — examined through the lens of the egg question that has occupied the preceding sugyot.

*Itmar*: Rav and Rav Ashi dispute the status of an egg laid on the first day of *yom tov*. Rav holds — *nolda b'zeh muteres b'zeh* — that an egg born on one day of *yom tov* is permitted on the second. Rav Ashi holds the opposite: *nolda b'zeh asura b'zeh* — born on the first day, it is forbidden on the second.

### One Kedusha

The *Gemara* immediately asks: what is the underlying reasoning of Rav Ashi? *Kasavar Rav Ashi kedusha achas hi* — Rav Ashi appears to hold that the two days of *yom tov* constitute one continuous *kedusha*. The logic is as follows: the standard conceptual framework for two-day *yom tov* is that we do not know which of the two days is the actual *yom tov*, owing to uncertainty about which day was *Rosh Chodesh*. If the days are genuinely independent of one another — one *yom tov* and one *chol*, even if we are uncertain which is which — then by the time day two begins, the *muktzah* considerations of day two ask only whether the egg existed before day two commenced. Since it did, it should be permitted. The only basis to forbid it is to treat both days as a single, unified unit of *kedusha*, making an egg born on day one analogous to an egg born mid-*yom tov* — forbidden for the duration.

### Havdalah Contradiction

But the *Gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: *v'ha Rav Ashi mavdil miyoma tava lachavrei* — Rav Ashi himself makes *havdalah* from the first day of *yom tov* to the second. Rashi adds the striking detail that Rav Ashi would recite *hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol*, treating the second day as *chol* for purposes of the *havdalah* formula. Whatever the precise text of his *havdalah*, the act itself clearly reflects a view that the two days are distinct — not a single continuous unit. If so, why would the egg born on day one be forbidden on day two?

### Safek Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves this apparent contradiction by explaining that Rav Ashi did not in fact hold a definitive position on the nature of the two days. Rather, *safoka mesafeka lei* — he was genuinely uncertain. And so he adopted *chumra* on both fronts: *hacha l'chumra v'hacha l'chumra*. On the one hand, perhaps the two days are one continuous *kedusha* — and therefore he forbids the egg born on day one on day two. On the other hand, perhaps they are two distinct *kedushos*, or even a *kedusha* and a *chol* — and therefore he makes *havdalah* between them. Both positions reflect a stringent response to doubt. (One might naturally ask how he could recite a *bracha* in a state of uncertainty; presumably he made *havdalah* without the associated *brachot*, saying only the formula of *hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol* or *bein kodesh l'kodesh*.)

The upshot of this opening section is that the underlying question — one *kedusha* or two *kedushos* — is now the explicit subject of the *Gemara's* attention. Rav holds *shtei kedushos*; Rav Ashi's ruling reflects uncertainty but leads practically to treating them as *kedusha achas*.

## Minhag vs. Takana

### Modern Calendar Logic

Rav Zeira enters the discussion: *k'vasei de'Rav Ashi mistabra* — the position of *kedusha achas* makes good logical sense. His reasoning: *de'hashta yedinan b'keviya d'yarcha* — today we know when *Rosh Chodesh* is. The calendar has been fixed in advance and there is no genuine uncertainty. Yet *v'ka avdinan trei yomei* — we nevertheless keep two days. If there were genuine doubt about which day is *yom tov*, each day would carry its own independent weight. But since we keep two days not out of doubt but because the Rabbis told us to do so, the two days form a single legislated unit — one *kedusha* with both days carrying equal weight.

### Historical Minhag

Abaye disagrees: *k'vatei d'Rav mistabra* — Rav's position of two independent *kedushos* is the more logical one. And he cites a *Mishnah* in support.

### Beacon System

*Tnan: barishona hayu masin masa'ot* — originally there was a remarkably efficient system for communicating the establishment of *Rosh Chodesh* to communities in the Diaspora. Bonfires would be lit on mountaintops in a chain, each peak visible to the next, transmitting the information across great distances almost instantaneously. The entire Jewish world could know when *Rosh Chodesh* had been declared in time for *yom tov*.

But *mishe'kilkalu ha'Kutim* (or, according to some versions, *ha'minim*) — when sectarians began to sabotage the system by lighting false fires on the wrong days — the chain could no longer be trusted. The sabotage was not merely mischievous; it was ideologically motivated. The Sadducees, for instance, disputed the interpretation of the verse counting fifty days *mimacharat haShabbat* — whether *Shabbat* meant the literal Sabbath or the first day of *Pesach* — and had reason to manipulate when the fires were lit so that *Shavuot* would always fall on the day after a literal Shabbat. The response was to shift to a system of *shluchim* — official messengers dispatched by the *beit din*, who could carry verifiable credentials. But messengers travel slowly, and so communities in the Diaspora would often find that *yom tov* arrived before the messengers did — hence the genuine uncertainty that gave rise to two-day *yom tov*.

### Regional Flexibility

Abaye now draws the critical inference. *V'ilu bitlu Kutim* — had the sabotage of the beacon system never occurred and the fires continued, *adinan chad yoma* — communities in the Diaspora would have kept only one day. Moreover, even within the period of the messenger system, *v'heicha d'matu shluchim* — areas close enough to Israel that the messengers could arrive in time for *Sukkot* and *Pesach* — those areas kept only one day. The practice of two days is therefore not a flat, uniform rabbinic legislation. It is flexible: it depends on geography, on circumstances, on what would have been necessary at the time.

### Minhag Continuity

This is the point Rashi crystallizes. The debate here is whether two-day *yom tov* is a *takana* — an active rabbinic ordinance — or a *minhag* — the continuation of a historical practice. If it is a *takana*, the Rabbis have told us: keep two days of *yom tov*. That makes it a unified, two-day unit — *kedusha achas*. If it is a *minhag*, then we are preserving the way things were actually practiced during the period of uncertainty. And during that period, each day was independent: one was *yom tov* and one was *chol*, nobody knew which was which, and each stood on its own. If our practice is the continuation of that reality, then each day retains its independent status — *shtei kedushos*.

The flexibility Abaye points to — areas close to Israel keeping one day, areas far away keeping two, the theoretical possibility that improved communication could reduce the practice — all of this is inconsistent with the character of a *takana*. A rabbinic *takana* does not draw those kinds of geographic distinctions, nor would it dissolve if circumstances changed. The flexibility reflects the organic nature of *minhag*: we continue to practice what would have been done had we been living through those original circumstances.

### Chacham Tzvi

This distinction has direct contemporary relevance. The question of what a resident of *Chutz La'aretz* visiting *Eretz Yisrael* for *yom tov* should do — one day or two — was for many decades answered instinctively with two days. The Chacham Tzvi, however, argues for a fundamentally different framework. If one thinks of two-day *yom tov* as a *takana* — a rule binding on those from *Chutz La'aretz* — then a person from *Chutz La'aretz* takes that obligation wherever he goes. But the Chacham Tzvi says the correct framework is to ask: what would you have done at the time of the *Beis HaMikdash*? At that time, a resident of *Chutz La'aretz* visiting *Eretz Yisrael* for *yom tov* would have known the calendar — the *beit din* had declared *Rosh Chodesh* and the information was available — and would have kept one day. That is therefore what such a visitor should do today. This is precisely Abaye's position brought to its practical conclusion: two-day *yom tov* reflects how things would have been done in the time of the *Beis HaMikdash*, not a flat halachic obligation attached to one's status as a *ben Chutz La'aretz*.

### Future Persecution

The *Gemara* then poses the obvious question: given that we now have a fixed calendar and know exactly when *Rosh Chodesh* is, why do we still keep two days? The answer, transmitted from *Eretz Yisrael*, is *hizaharu beminhag avoteichem b'yadeichem* — be scrupulous about the practice of your forefathers. But then the *Gemara* — as indicated by the shift from Hebrew to Aramaic — adds an additional layer: *zimnin degazra hamalchut gezeira v'atei leakalkulei* — there may come a time when government persecution will disrupt access to the traditions of the calendar, and communities will genuinely not know which day is *yom tov*. Rashi explains that this refers to the possibility that under persecution the chain of transmission of the calendar could be lost, creating a real *safek* once again. This explains why the *minhag* is treated with the full gravity of a *yom tov* obligation and not merely as a quaint historical preservation. The two-day practice is rooted in the concept of *safek* — the uncertainty of which day is the real one — and may again become a genuine *safek*. That is Abaye's position, and it is in service of Rav's ruling that the two days constitute *shtei kedushos*, each independent.

## Rosh Hashanah

### RH One Kedusha

The *Gemara* now turns to two days of *Rosh Hashanah*, which occupy a fundamentally different category. Rabba and Shmuel bar Ami both agree — *modu bazei asuru bazei* — that even Rav, who ruled *shtei kedushos* for normal two-day *yom tov*, concedes that two days of *Rosh Hashanah* are *kedusha achas*. An egg born on day one of *Rosh Hashanah* is forbidden on day two. The question is why.

### Witness Rules

The *Mishnah* provides the historical background. *Barishona hayu mekabelin edut hachodesh kol hayom* — originally, *beit din* would accept testimony about the new moon throughout the entire day of the thirtieth of *Elul*, the potential first day of *Rosh Hashanah*. This created an inherent uncertainty: witnesses might appear at any point during the day to testify that they had seen the new moon the previous night. Until they appeared, no one knew with certainty whether that day would be declared *Rosh Chodesh Tishrei* — and hence *Rosh Hashanah* — or whether *Rosh Hashanah* would fall the following day.

### Levim Mistake

One year, *nishtahu eidim lavo* — the witnesses came very late. Assuming no witnesses would arrive, those at the *Beis HaMikdash* proceeded with the afternoon service on the assumption that it was an ordinary weekday. *V'nitkalkelu haLevi'im bashir* — the *Levi'im* sang the wrong *shir*. The *shir shel yom* recited during the *tamid shebein ha'arbayim* — the afternoon daily offering — was supposed to be the *yom tov* one, since witnesses were still within the window of acceptance. Instead, the *Levi'im* sang the weekday *shir*, believing the day would remain *chol*.

This error had layered consequences. Rashi notes that at *Shacharit*, it was always too early to know whether the day would be declared *Rosh Hashanah* — the witnesses had not yet arrived — so the weekday *shir* was always sung in the morning by design. By *Musaf* and *Mincha* time, however, the determination should have been possible, and the appropriate *yom tov* *shir* should have been sung. Tosafot raises an additional complication: if the *Musaf* offerings had not yet been brought — because everyone assumed it was a weekday — then when the witnesses did arrive late and *Rosh Hashanah* was declared, the *Musaf* korbanot would have had to be brought after the afternoon *tamid*, which is normally prohibited. This too is alluded to in the tractate of *Rosh Hashanah*. In short, the late arrival of witnesses created a cascade of halachic disruption.

### Mincha Cutoff

The response was a *takana*: *hitkinu shelo yehu mekabelin et ha'eidim ela ad haMincha* — witnesses would no longer be accepted after *Mincha* time, meaning after the time of the afternoon *tamid*. Once the *tamid* was to be brought, the *beit din* closed its doors. If witnesses had not appeared by then, the *Levi'im* would sing the weekday *shir*, the *Musaf* would not be brought (since it was being treated as a weekday), and if witnesses subsequently arrived, they would be turned away.

### Post-Mincha Kedusha

This *takana* had a striking consequence. Once *Mincha* time arrived and witnesses could no longer be accepted, everyone knew with certainty that the following day — not the current one — would be the calendar's *Rosh Hashanah*. And yet the ruling was: *nogin oso hayom kodesh u'l'machar kodesh* — they would continue treating that day with the sanctity of *Rosh Hashanah*, not resuming *melacha* until the end of the day, and the following day would be kept as *Rosh Hashanah* as well. If people were permitted to begin performing *melacha* partway through a day they had begun keeping as *Rosh Hashanah*, the concern was that in future years they would begin doing *melacha* from the morning itself. The integrity of the day required that once it was begun as *Rosh Hashanah*, it be completed as such.

The halachic implication is critical. In cases where witnesses arrived after *Mincha*, there would be two days of *Rosh Hashanah* — and the second portion of day one was kept not out of *safek* but out of *takana*. There was certainty that the official *Rosh Hashanah* was tomorrow, yet the day continued to be treated as sacred. That is not the character of *shtei kedushos* — independent days, each kept because it might be the real one. That is the character of *kedusha achas* — a single extended unit of sanctity that the Rabbis have mandated regardless of what is actually known. This is why two days of *Rosh Hashanah* are categorically different from normal two-day *yom tov*, and why they are called *yoma arichta* — one long day. This also has implications beyond the egg question: it bears on the recitation of *Shehecheyanu* on the second night of *Rosh Hashanah*. For ordinary two-day *yom tov*, one says *Shehecheyanu* on the second night because perhaps yesterday was *chol* and today is the real *yom tov*. For *Rosh Hashanah*, the logic is different — the two days are one extended unit, and the *Shehecheyanu* on the second night is accommodated through legal fictions such as a new fruit.

## Post-Churban Reform

### Rava's View

Rava now introduces a new position: *mitakanas Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai va'eilach, beitzah muteres* — from the time of Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's reform onward, the egg should be *permitted*, meaning the two days of *Rosh Hashanah* revert to *shtei kedushos*. The reasoning draws on a *Mishnah* in tractate *Rosh Hashanah*: *mishecharav Beis HaMikdash, hiskin Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai she'mekablin eidus hachodesh kol hayom* — after the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*, Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai ordained that witnesses could be accepted throughout the entire day once again. With the *Beis HaMikdash* gone, there were no more sacrificial consequences — no danger of singing the wrong *shir*, no problem of bringing *Musaf* after the *tamid*. The old *takana* restricting witnesses to before *Mincha* had no remaining rationale, and Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai lifted it.

Rava's logic follows directly: if witnesses can now be accepted all day, then the element of non-*safek* that had characterized part of the first day of *Rosh Hashanah* — the portion after *Mincha* when one kept the day as sacred even though one knew it was not *Rosh Hashanah* — disappears. The situation reverts to one of pure *safek*, just like ordinary two-day *yom tov*. And pure *safek* means *shtei kedushos*, not *kedusha achas*. Therefore, born on day one, permitted on day two.

### Abaye Objects

Abaye challenges Rava: *v'ha Rav u'Shmuel de'amri tarvaihu beitzah asurah* — but Rav and Shmuel both hold that the egg remains forbidden, meaning they hold *kedusha achas* even after Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's reform. How can Rava simply assert that the reform of accepting witnesses changes everything about the nature of the two days?

Rava's response is striking: *amina lach ana Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai v'at amrat li Rav u'Shmuel?* — I bring you Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai himself, an explicit *Mishnah* showing that the witness reality changed, which logically entails a shift to *shtei kedushos*, and you counter with Rav and Shmuel? The weight of the *Mishnah* overrides the Amoraic opinion.

### EY vs. CL

The *Gemara* acknowledges the force of Rava's argument: *lo kashya, ha lan ha lehu* — it is not difficult; the ruling differs by location. The *Gemara* now draws a distinction between *Eretz Yisrael* and *Chutz La'aretz*.

### Practice Divergence

In *Eretz Yisrael*, once Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's *takana* took effect and witnesses could be accepted all day, the practice of two-day *Rosh Hashanah* became variable. When witnesses came early on the first day, it would be a one-day *Rosh Hashanah*. When they came late and the first day had been kept out of doubt — uncertainty as to whether witnesses would arrive — and then witnesses came on day two, there would be two days, but those two days would now be characterized by *safek*, not by the old *takana*-based *kedusha achas*. In *Eretz Yisrael*, then, the reform genuinely changed the halachic character of two-day *Rosh Hashanah* into *shtei kedushos*, consistent with Rava's position. This is what *ha lan* — "this is for them [in *Eretz Yisrael*]" — refers to.

In *Chutz La'aretz*, however, Rashi explains that the situation is different. Two-day *yom tov* always remained a universal practice; there was never any year in which a community in *Chutz La'aretz* kept only one day of *Rosh Hashanah*. Because the two-day practice continued without interruption, and because it had begun under the character of *kedusha achas* — shaped by the old *takana* of not accepting witnesses — that character was never dislodged. The practice continued with the same halachic character it always had. Even though the underlying logic shifted, the unbroken continuity of practice preserved the original status. That is *ha lehu* — "this is for us [in *Chutz La'aretz*]." In *Chutz La'aretz*, two days of *Rosh Hashanah* remain *kedusha achas*, and an egg born on day one is forbidden on day two.

Tosafot in *Sukkah* offers a somewhat different explanation for why the distinction between *Eretz Yisrael* and *Chutz La'aretz* holds, but the essential point is the same: in *Chutz La'aretz*, because the practice of two days was never interrupted and never shifted to a variable reality, the *kedusha achas* framework persists.

## Davar She'beminyan

### Legislation Permanence

Rav Yosef now enters with a different explanation for why the egg remains forbidden — and in doing so advances one of the most consequential halachic principles in this entire chapter. *Af mishtakanat Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai va'eilach beitzah asurah* — even after the reform, the egg is forbidden. Why? *Havei davar she'be'minyan, v'chol davar she'be'minyan tzarich minyan acher l'hatiro* — it is a *davar she'be'minyan*: a matter that was established by legislative vote, and any such matter requires another act of legislation to retract it.

The principle is this: when the Rabbis officially enact a *takana*, a change in the underlying circumstances does not automatically void the legislation. The old law remains on the books until it is actively repealed by a new legislative act. In our case, the *takana* that established the two days of *Rosh Hashanah* as *kedusha achas* — with all its consequences, including the prohibition of an egg born on day one — was never explicitly retracted. Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai changed the *takana* about accepting witnesses. He did not issue a new *takana* about the nature of the two days. Therefore, the halachic character of *kedusha achas* remains in force.

One might ask: is this not already obvious from the earlier *Mishnah* in *Eduyot*, which states that no *beit din* can overturn the ruling of a previous *beit din* unless it surpasses it in wisdom and in number? Rav Yosef appears to be making a sharper point: not merely that you need a greater *beit din* to overturn a ruling, but that even when the circumstances that gave rise to the ruling have entirely disappeared, the ruling does not lapse by itself. The circumstances changing is not equivalent to legislation reversing. This has broad practical implications — as, for example, in the debate over *cholov stam*: if the *takana* requiring Jewish supervision of milk was enacted because of concern about *treif* milk being mixed in, does the *takana* lapse automatically in an environment where that concern is demonstrably absent? Rav Yosef's principle says no: the *takana* remains until it is actively repealed, unless the circumstances were explicitly built into the *takana* itself.

### Biblical Proofs

Rav Yosef proceeds to demonstrate this principle from biblical sources. *Dichtiv: heyu nechonim lishloshes yamim, al tigshu el isha* — God commanded the Jewish people at Sinai to abstain from marital relations for three days in preparation for *Ma'amad Har Sinai*. Once *Ma'amad Har Sinai* was concluded, one might think the command automatically lapsed — the reason had been fulfilled. Yet the verse tells us that God subsequently commanded: *lechu imru lahem shuvu lachem l'ohaleichem* — go tell them to return to their tents, meaning to resume marital relations. *Shema mina*: even though the reason for the original command had clearly passed, it required an active divine act to retract it.

The *Gemara* anticipates an objection: perhaps *shuvu lachem l'ohaleichem* was not a retraction but a new *mitzva* of *onah* — the obligation to resume marital relations after a period of separation. If so, it proves nothing about the need to retract the original prohibition. The *Gemara* therefore adduces a second biblical proof: *bimshoch hayovel heimah ya'alu vahar* — when the great blast of the *shofar* sounds, they may ascend the mountain. The mountain had been forbidden to approach during the *Sinai* experience: *gam hatzon vehabakar al yiru el mul hahar hahu*. Rashi cites a *Midrash* understanding *hahar hahu* as the mountain suffused with divine presence — and clearly, once the divine presence had departed, the prohibition on approaching should have lapsed automatically. Yet the Torah still had to say explicitly: when the *shofar* sounds, they may ascend. *Shema mina: kol davar she'be'minyan tzarich minyan acher l'hatiro* — even when the logical basis for a prohibition has been removed, an active retraction is required.

### Kerem Revai

The *Gemara* continues to shore up the principle. *V'chi teima hani milei de'oraisa* — perhaps one might say this applies only to biblical commands. By contrast, *d'rabbanan* legislation might not require active retraction when circumstances change. The *Gemara* therefore cites a case from rabbinic law.

The *Mishnah* teaches: *kerem revai haya oleh l'Yerushalayim mahalakh yom echad l'chol tzad* — produce of a fourth-year vineyard (*kerem revai*) had to be brought up to Jerusalem to be eaten there. The ordinary practice of redeeming the produce and bringing up its monetary equivalent was not permitted for those living within a day's journey of Jerusalem in any direction — they had to bring the actual fruit. The boundaries: Lod to the west, the Jordan to the east, and two northern/southern markers according to the *girsa*. The stated reason for this requirement was *kedei le'ater shukei Yerushalayim b'peirot* — to adorn the marketplaces of Jerusalem with fruit, to create an atmosphere of vibrant Jewish life in the holy city.

The *Gemara* then relates: Rabbi Eliezer had a *kerem revai* east of Lod, within a day's journey of Jerusalem, near the village of Tebi. He wished to declare it *hefker* — ownerless — so that the poor could take it to Jerusalem themselves and eat it there, sparing him the obligation to bring it personally. His students told him: *nimnu alecha chaveirecha v'hitiru* — your colleagues have already taken a vote and retracted the *takana*. This occurred after the *churban habayit*, when Jerusalem was no longer a vibrant Jewish center and the whole rationale for insisting on bringing the actual fruit no longer applied. And yet the *takana* did not lapse automatically when the *Beis HaMikdash* was destroyed. *Ta'ama deninmnu* — the reason Rabbi Eliezer was now exempt was that a new vote had been taken to retract it. *Halo nimnu lo, lo* — had they not taken a new vote, the *takana* would have remained in force despite the changed circumstances. This is the *d'rabbanan* proof for the principle of *davar she'be'minyan*.

### Tosafot Refinement

At this point Tosafot raises a subtle but important refinement, which has significant practical implications. One might ask: if a community enacts a *cherem* — a ban backed by excommunication — for a defined period (say, "until *Pesach*" or "for thirty days"), does that *cherem* require a formal vote to dissolve when the period expires? By analogy to the *Sinai* case — where abstention was commanded for three days and God still needed to actively permit its cessation — one might argue yes.

Tosafot answers no. The *Sinai* command was not formulated as "do not have relations for three days." It said: *al tigshu el isha* — abstain from relations. The *reason* one could infer a time limit of three days, but it was never explicitly built into the command. Therefore, an explicit act was needed to retract it. But if the time limit is itself written into the *takana* — "this prohibition lasts until *Pesach*" — then the expiration is part of the legislation itself, and the *takana* self-expires without need of a new vote.

This distinction is enormously consequential for many *d'rabbanan* questions. The classic case is *cholov Yisroel*. The *takana* prohibiting milk without Jewish supervision was enacted because of concern that *treif* milk might have been mixed in. If the *takana* was formulated flatly — milk without Jewish supervision is forbidden — then it remains in force regardless of changed circumstances, and one needs an active new legislation to permit *cholov stam* in an environment where the concern is absent. If, however, the *takana* was formulated conditionally — when there is concern about *treif* milk being mixed in, milk without Jewish supervision is forbidden — then the circumstances are built into the *takana*, and it would self-expire when those circumstances change. This is precisely the question that underlies the debates about *cholov stam* in modern *halacha*. Rav Yosef's principle, refined by Tosafot, is thus one of the central tools for analyzing a vast range of rabbinic legislation.

### Takana Application

Returning now to the case at hand: the *Gemara* establishes that the *takana* forbidding acceptance of witnesses after *Mincha* was retracted by Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai. But a separate question is whether the halachic status of two days of *Rosh Hashanah* as *kedusha achas* was itself ever the subject of a legislative enactment. Rav Yosef's claim is that *kedusha achas* was itself a *davar she'be'minyan*, independently established, and not merely a consequence of the witness *takana*. Therefore, even when the witness *takana* was retracted, *kedusha achas* remained — it would require its own separate retraction. *Ki nimnu a'eidus* — when Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai and his *beit din* voted, they voted about accepting witnesses. *A'beitza lo nimnu* — they never voted to change the status of the egg. That separate question was never revisited, and so it remains.

## Emergent Reality

### Dependent Reality

Abaye offers a sharp retort to Rav Yosef's entire framework. *Atu beitzah be'minyan mi havay?* — when exactly was there ever a formal legislative vote about the egg? No *beit din* ever sat down and enacted a *takana* saying: an egg born on day one of *Rosh Hashanah* is forbidden on day two. That was never a standalone piece of legislation that now needs to be separately retracted.

### Automatic Shift

Rather, says Abaye, *beitzah be'eidus talya milsa* — the egg question is entirely dependent on the witness question. The prohibition of the egg — or more precisely, the status of *kedusha achas* that generates that prohibition — was not an independent enactment. It was an emergent consequence of the fact that witnesses were not accepted after *Mincha*, creating moments of non-*safek* sanctity that unified the two days into a single extended *kedusha*. *Issur eidus, itsa issura d'beitzah* — the witness *takana* brought about the egg prohibition. *Istari eidus, istari beitzah* — once the witness *takana* was lifted, the egg prohibition dissolves automatically, because there is no longer any independent basis for it. It was always a derivative reality, not a legislated one, and a *davar she'be'minyan* principle does not apply to something that was never itself the subject of a *minyan*. Therefore, says Abaye, the logic of *davar she'be'minyan* is not a sufficient explanation for why *kedusha achas* persists after Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's reform.

## Maintaining Kedusha

### Rebuilding Concern

The *Gemara* now presents two further explanations for why *kedusha achas* is maintained even after the reform. Rav Adda bar Ahava — and both he and his colleague from the house of Kluchei — say: *af midirshat Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai va'eilach beitzah asurah*. Even after the reform, the egg remains forbidden. Why? *Mehera yibaneh Beis HaMikdash* — may the *Beis HaMikdash* be rebuilt speedily. *V'yomru: ishtakad milo achlanu beitzah b'Yom Tov Sheini?* — and people will say: last year, when an egg was born on day one, we ate it on day two. *Hashta nami neichol* — let us do the same now. *V'lo yad'u* — and they will not know *de'ishtakad shtei kedushos hein v'hashta kedusha achas hi* — that last year, without a *Beis HaMikdash*, there was genuine *safek* and *shtei kedushos*, whereas now, with the *Beis HaMikdash* restored and witnesses not accepted after *Mincha*, it is *kedusha achas*.

To prevent that confusion, we proactively maintain *kedusha achas* even in the post-*churban* period. This is not that we are stuck with a *takana* that was never retracted; it is that we are making a new, forward-looking decision to preserve the old practice in anticipation of the rebuilt *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Authority Distinction

The *Gemara* then raises an objection: if we are so worried about post-*churban* practice being confused with post-*churban* times once the *Beis HaMikdash* is rebuilt, why did Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai allow witnesses to be accepted all day at all? Should we not have maintained the restriction on witnesses as well, lest people after the rebuilding assume they can accept witnesses all day?

*Hachi hashta?* — the comparison is misplaced. *Eidus mesura l'veit din* — the acceptance of witnesses is entrusted to the *beit din*. The *beit din* knows the *halacha*. When the *Beis HaMikdash* is rebuilt, the *beit din* will know that witnesses are no longer accepted after *Mincha* time, and they will not make the mistake of assuming otherwise based on what was done in the post-*churban* period. *Beitzah l'chol mesura* — but the egg question is something every farmer faces. A farmer who saw eggs eaten on day two last year will eat them on day two this year, without knowing that the halachic regime has changed. Therefore the concern about popular confusion applies to the egg but not to the witness practice.

### Deliberate Preservation

Rava offers a third and somewhat different formulation: *af midirshat Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai va'eilach beitzah asurah*. Even Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai himself — *mi lo modeh Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai she'im ba'u eidim min haMincha u'l'ma'ala she'nohagim oto yom kodesh u'l'machar kodesh* — would acknowledge that when witnesses arrive after *Mincha* time, we continue treating that day with *kedusha* and the next day as well. This principle was not abrogated.

Rava's point, as Rashi and Tosafot both work to articulate, is that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's intervention was targeted and surgical. He used a scalpel, not a cleaver. He changed the specific *takana* about accepting witnesses. But the broader halachic reality of *kedusha achas* — the idea that once a day of *Rosh Hashanah* is begun, it must be completed as sacred, and that two days form a single extended unit — that he deliberately left intact. One cannot infer from his reform of the witness rules that he intended to alter the fundamental character of the two days.

### Final Ruling

The *Gemara* concludes with Rava's ruling: *v'amar Rava: hilchata k'vatei de'Rav be'hani telat, bein l'kula bein l'chumra* — the *halacha* follows Rav in all three cases, whether toward leniency or toward stringency.

The three cases are as follows. First, the case of *Shabbat* and *yom tov* back-to-back: there, *halacha* follows Rav strictly — an egg born on *Shabbat* is forbidden on *yom tov*, and vice versa, because they are treated as a continuous unit (or alternatively because of *hachana d'rabbah*). Second, normal two-day *yom tov* of *Chutz La'aretz*: there, *halacha* follows Rav leniently — the two days are *shtei kedushos*, each independent, and an egg born on day one is permitted on day two. Third, the case of two days of *Rosh Hashanah*: even after Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's reform, *kedusha achas* is maintained, and an egg born on day one remains forbidden on day two — this too follows Rav.

## Rishonim Dispute

### Rif Always Two

Having established that the *halacha* treats two days of *Rosh Hashanah* as *kedusha achas* — a single extended sanctity — the Rif draws a further conclusion. Quoting the entire *Gemara* and its rulings, he states that we therefore always keep two days of *Rosh Hashanah*, even in *Eretz Yisrael*. The maintenance of *kedusha achas* implies, in his reading, the perpetual observance of two days. This is how *Rosh Hashanah* is observed in *Eretz Yisrael* today: two days, treated as *kedusha achas* — the only *yom tov* on which *Eretz Yisrael* and *Chutz La'aretz* observe the same number of days.

### Rishonim One Day

It is striking, however, that this conclusion is not explicit in the *Gemara* itself. The Baal HaMaor and other *Rishonim* dispute the Rif's ruling. In their view, the *Gemara's* analysis of *kedusha achas* addresses only the halachic character of those years when two days of *Rosh Hashanah* are kept — not whether two days must always be kept in *Eretz Yisrael*. According to these *Rishonim*, when witnesses would have arrived on the first day — or, equivalently, when the fixed calendar places *Rosh Hashanah* on the thirtieth day of *Elul* (which, since *Elul* is never given thirty days, is effectively always) — only one day would be kept in *Eretz Yisrael*. Two days would apply only in years when witnesses arrived late, which created the two-day reality of keeping the first day out of doubt and the second day as the actual *Rosh Hashanah*.

### Modern Practice

In practice, the universal observance of two days of *Rosh Hashanah* — including in *Eretz Yisrael* — reflects the Rif's position. But it is important to appreciate that this universality is not self-evident from the *Gemara* and that a significant body of *Rishonim* held otherwise. The question of whether *Eretz Yisrael* should keep one or two days of *Rosh Hashanah* is a genuine dispute among the *Rishonim*, even though the *Gemara* is the source from which all parties argue.
