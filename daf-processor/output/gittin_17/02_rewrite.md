# Gittin 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Witnesses Abroad

### Rabba bar bar Chana

The sugya opens by recalling the unresolved question from the previous amud: when two witnesses bring a *get* from *medinat hayam*, must they declare *bifnei nechtav uvifnei nechtam*, or not? Two versions of the ruling had been presented, and it was unclear which one to follow.

The Gemara now continues with a narrative set approximately six lines from the bottom of the prior daf. Rabba bar bar Chana fell ill, and Rabbi Yehuda and Rabba came to visit him — a *bikur cholim* call. They posed the question directly: if two witnesses bring a *get* from abroad, must they state *bifnei nechtav uvifnei nechtam* or not? Rabba bar bar Chana answered them: they need not.

### Edei Mesirah Logic

His reasoning is a clean logical argument. He said: if these two witnesses were to say simply, "*bifneinu geireshah*" — "in our presence he divorced her" — would they not be believed? Of course they would. If they were present at the actual act of delivery, they would function as *edei mesirah*, witnesses to the divorce itself, and their testimony would be fully accepted. All the more so, then, their failure to recite the formal declaration of *bifnei nechtav uvifnei nechtam* creates no disqualification.

### Chaveirim Incident

At that point the Gemara interpolates an incident that, as the text itself signals, has little to do with the halachic ruling just stated — yet it is colorful enough to record. The story continues: *ad hachi ata hahu chavara* — while this was going on, a group of *chaveirim* arrived. Here *chaveirim* does not refer to the learned scholars who earned that honorific in certain circles, but rather to Persian ruffians. They seized the candle from before the rabbis. As Rashi explains, it happened to be one of their festivals, during which only the temples were permitted to have light; the *beit midrash* was suddenly plunged into darkness.

Rabba bar bar Chana then cried out: *Rachmana, o vetulach o vetula d'var Eisav* — "Merciful One, either in Your shade or in the shade of the son of Esau." In other words: Master of the Universe, either protect us Yourself, or — implausible as it sounds — we would rather be living under Roman rule than under these Persians.

The Gemara challenges this immediately: are the Romans better than the Persians? Was there not a *baraita* in which Rabbi Chiyya expounded the verse, "*Elokim hevin darkah v'hu yada et mkomah*" — God understood its ways and knew its place — to mean that God, knowing Israel could not survive under the Romans, exiled them to Babylonia? That clearly implies Babylonian exile was preferable to Roman subjugation. Yet here, Rabba bar bar Chana was wishing they were under Rome?

The Gemara resolves the tension: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. One statement refers to the period before the *chaveirim* descended upon Babylonia; the other refers to after. In other words, the original Persian rulers were benign enough that exile there was preferable to Roman domination — but once this particular faction of Persian ruffians took hold, Rome was the better option. With that, the Gemara closes the historical digression and returns to the halachic analysis.

### Get Yotzei Mitachat

Rabbi Ami then cites Rabbi Yochanan with a significant qualification: *lo shanu ela sheget yotzei mitachat yedei edei chtivah* — the ruling that one witness saying *bifanai nechtav* combined with two witnesses saying *bifaneinu nechtam* is valid applies specifically when the *get* comes from the hands of the witness to writing — that is, when the one who declared *bifanai nechtav* is the primary bearer of the *get*.

The background here is the case from the Mishnah at the close of the prior discussion: one says *bifanai nechtav* and two say *bifaneinu nechtam*, which was declared valid. Rabbi Yochanan now explains why. That first witness, the one who declared *bifanai nechtav*, serves as the primary *shliach*; because he is the one in whose hands the *get* rests, he is elevated — functioning, as the Gemara puts it, *k'shnayim al zeh* — equivalent to two witnesses for that element. Combined with the two who declared *bifaneinu nechtam*, the result is effectively two-and-two, satisfying the requirement on both counts.

However, *aval mitachat yad edei chatima pasul* — if the *get* comes from the hands only of the signing witnesses, with that single witness merely a secondary figure rather than the primary bearer, it is invalid. In such a case you have only two and one, not two and two, and the requirement is not met.

The implication, as the Gemara draws it, is that two witnesses bringing a *get* from *medinat hayam* do need to declare *bifaneinu nechtav uvifaneinu nechtam*. This appears to contradict the lenient ruling of Rabba bar bar Chana.

### Rav Assi's Challenge

Rav Assi presses the point. He confronts Rabbi Ami (or the authority behind this ruling) with a challenge from the *reisha* of the Mishnah: the case where two say *bifaneinu nechtav* and one says *bifanai nechtam* — the *machloket* between the *Tanna Kamma*, who declares it invalid, and Rabbi Yehuda, who validates it.

Rav Assi's logic runs as follows: if you hold that two witnesses bringing a *get* from abroad *must* declare the full formula — and thus the requirement is strict — then even the case where the *get* comes from the hands of both of them would be disqualified by the Rabbanan, since you still only have two and one. But on the other hand, the Gemara elsewhere records an alternate version of the same teaching in which Rabbi Ami ruled more leniently — that even a *get* coming from the hands of the signing witnesses (*edei chatima*) is valid. If that is your position, then plainly you hold that two witnesses from abroad need *not* state the full formula, and in that case, why would the Rabbanan declare the Mishnah's case of two-and-one invalid? If two witnesses together are sufficient without any declaration, then even if the *get* rests primarily in their hands, the Rabbanan should validate it.

Rav Assi thus confronts Rabbi Ami with the inconsistency: you appear to have stated two irreconcilable positions.

### Final Resolution

Rabbi Ami's reply is crisp: *amar leh in* — yes, that is so — and then, regarding his earlier stricter formulation: *b'ha zimna lo amarta hachi* — on that occasion you did not say it this way. He had in effect reversed himself, and now he states clearly: *yated hi shelo tamut* — this is a peg that shall not be moved. The definitive position is the lenient one: two witnesses who bring a *get* from abroad need not recite *bifaneinu nechtav uvifaneinu nechtam*.

With this resolution in hand, Rav Assi's challenge dissolves. Why does the Rabbanan's ruling in the Mishnah case render it invalid? Because in that case, the *get* does not come from the hands of both of them — *she'ein haget yotzei mitachat yedei shneihem* — the two are not the primary bearers. It is that factor alone, not any failure to recite the formula, that creates the problem. But when two witnesses come together as the primary bearers of a *get* from abroad, they need not declare anything at all, because their joint presence would have sufficed even as *edei mesirah* — witnesses to the delivery — and no formal attestation is required.

## Get Mukdam

### Intro: Predating

With the foregoing questions settled, the Gemara opens a new Mishnah and a new topic of great practical importance: *get mukdam* — the predated *get*. The Mishnah lays out a grid of possibilities based on the timing of writing (*ketivah*) and signing (*chatimah*):

A *get* written by day and signed by day is valid. Written at night and signed at night is valid. Written at night and signed the following day is likewise valid — this presents no difficulty because the natural progression of time moves forward from night into the following day, so there is no discrepancy. The problematic case is the fourth combination: written by day and signed at night. Here the Mishnah presents a *machloket*.

To appreciate why this fourth case is contentious, one must understand the general problem with any document that bears two different dates — whether the gap is a day, a month, or a year. In ordinary loan documents (*shtarot chov*), the concern is this: a creditor holding a *shtar* has the power not only to collect from the debtor's own assets but also from land he has since sold to third parties, provided the sale took place after the *shtar* took effect. This is because the signing of the *shtar* creates a lien (*shiabud*) from its effective date. If a document purports to have taken effect on a date earlier than it actually did, the creditor can unjustly pursue property that was legitimately sold during the intervening period. The purchaser (*Jonathan*, in the lecturer's running example) bought the land fairly and had no way to know of any prior claim — yet if the document is predated, he loses his property when he should not have.

The same structural problem arises with a *get*, albeit with different stakes. The *get* involves the husband's rights to the produce (*peirot*) of his wife's property. From some point in the process — the precise moment being subject to dispute — those rights cease. If the *get* is predated, the apparent termination of those rights is pushed back artificially, potentially harming purchasers or creating other legal distortions.

### Mishnah Dispute

The Mishnah presents the following *machloket*. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that a *get* written by day and signed at night (*nichtav bayom venichtam balaila*) is invalid — *pasul*. Rabbi Shimon, however, is *machshir* — he validates it.

But Rabbi Shimon's position requires care. The Mishnah states: *kol hagittin shenichtevu bayom venichtamu balaila pesulin* — all *gittin* written by day and signed at night are invalid — and then immediately carves out the exception: *chutz migittei nashim*, with the exception of bills of divorce. So in the very breath in which he appears to disqualify such documents generally, Rabbi Shimon explicitly exempts *gittin* — the divorce documents for women — and validates them. Understanding the logic of this exemption is the central task of the ensuing Talmudic discussion.

### Yochanan: Bas Achoto

The Gemara then introduces a famous *machloket* between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish about the foundational reason the Rabbanan required a date in a *get* at all.

Rabbi Yochanan says: the reason is *mishum bas achoto* — on account of his niece. The concern is *shema yechapeh al bas achoto* — lest a husband, married to his own niece (which is permitted by Torah law), seek to cover up her adultery by producing a *get* antedated to before the act of infidelity occurred. Without a date, or with a manipulable date, she could claim to have been divorced before she committed adultery and thus escape punishment. To prevent this kind of cover-up, the Rabbanan required a clear and immovable date on every *get*.

### Lakish: Peirot

Reish Lakish gives an entirely different reason: *mishum peirot* — on account of produce. The husband, during the marriage, retains the right to benefit from his wife's property — specifically its yield, the *peirot*. Those rights do not persist indefinitely after the *get* process begins. The date in the *get* establishes precisely when those rights were severed. Without a date, the husband could claim to have retained his rights over the produce all along, or conversely the wife could claim he had none when in fact he did. The *zman* in the *get* thus functions as the halachic boundary of the husband's property entitlements.

### Lakish Rejects Bas Achoto

The Gemara then systematically asks why each of these Amoraim does not adopt the other's reasoning.

Why does Reish Lakish not hold like Rabbi Yochanan — that is, why does he reject the *bas achoto* rationale? His answer: *znus lo shechicha* — adultery is not common. The Rabbanan would not institute a universal requirement of dating every *get* throughout all of Israel for the rare scenario in which a man married his niece, she subsequently committed adultery, and he then sought to cover it up through a predated *get*. Such a chain of circumstances is too attenuated to serve as the foundation of a broad *takkanah*. Reish Lakish therefore insists that the reason must be something that arises routinely in every divorce: the *peirot* question.

### Yochanan: Until Netina

Conversely, why does Rabbi Yochanan not hold like Reish Lakish — why does he not ground the *zman* requirement in *peirot*?

Rabbi Yochanan's answer: *kasavar yesh l'baal peirot ad sha'at nesina* — he holds that the husband retains his rights to the *peirot* all the way until the moment of delivery (*netinah*), the final stage of the *get* process. If the husband's entitlement runs until the very moment the *get* is placed in his wife's hands, then the date inscribed in the document — which records only the writing or signing — is irrelevant to the *peirot* question. No matter what date appears in the *get*, the husband was legitimately exercising his rights until the last possible moment. There is thus no potential for injustice with respect to *peirot*, and no need to institute a *zman* for that reason. The only reason that remains, and the one Rabbi Yochanan therefore does invoke, is *bas achoto*.

### Shimon via Lakish

Now the Gemara turns to explaining the *machloket* between Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma* through the lens of each Amora.

According to Reish Lakish, who holds that the entire institution of *zman* in a *get* is about *peirot*, what is the basis of the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma*? The answer is elegant. They disagree about the precise moment at which the husband's rights to *peirot* are extinguished.

Rabbi Shimon holds *ein lo mizman ketivah* — the husband has no rights from the moment of writing. The instant the *get* begins to be written, the husband loses all entitlement to *peirot*. With this understanding, a predated *get* is unobjectionable. Suppose the *get* was written in the beginning of 2015 and signed and delivered at the end of 2015. The *get* is dated from the writing — the beginning of 2015. From that moment, says Rabbi Shimon, the husband's rights ceased. So if the wife comes to claim that the husband had no right to sell produce or encumber property after that date, she is correct, and anyone who purchased from him during that intervening period — even a legitimate third-party buyer — cannot claim to have acquired it free of her claim. Rabbi Shimon is therefore comfortable with a predated *get*: the date reflects the moment of writing, and from that moment all rights were already gone.

The *Tanna Kamma*, by contrast, holds that the husband retains rights to *peirot* until *sha'at chatimah* — the moment of signing. If the *get* is predated to the writing but the husband's rights actually ran until signing, then the wife can unjustly reach back into the period between writing and signing and dispossess legitimate purchasers. The predated *get* thus creates a false impression that the husband had no rights during a period when he actually did. The *Tanna Kamma* therefore declares such a *get* invalid.

To crystallize this in concrete terms: the produce that accrues between *sha'at ketivah* and *sha'at chatimah* is what stands between Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma*. That is precisely what the Gemara states: *peirei d'vishaat ketiva ve'ad sha'at chatima ika beineihu* — the produce of that intervening period is the fulcrum of their dispute.

### Shimon via Yochanan

According to Rabbi Yochanan, however, the entire institution of *zman* has nothing to do with *peirot* — it is about *bas achoto*. So what, according to Rabbi Yochanan, is the basis of the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma*?

This is more complex, and the Gemara addresses it directly. Rabbi Yochanan essentially says: *aliba d'Rabbi Shimon lo kamina, kamina d'Rabbanan* — I am not speaking according to Rabbi Shimon's view; I am speaking according to the Rabbanan, i.e., the *Tanna Kamma*. In other words, Rabbi Yochanan's *bas achoto* rationale explains why the *Tanna Kamma* instituted a *zman* in the *get*, but Rabbi Shimon himself does not accept this rationale. Rabbi Shimon does not share the concern about covering up adultery — whether because he considers it too uncommon, or for some other reason — and therefore he does not require the *zman* for that purpose.

But one might press the point: Rabbi Shimon presumably does agree that the husband has *peirot* rights that need to be protected by a date. Why then does he validate a predated *get* — does that not expose the buyer to an unjust claim? The answer, consistent with the framework already established, is that Rabbi Shimon holds the husband loses *peirot* rights from *zman ketivah*. So from his perspective, the predated *get* does not create any injustice with respect to *peirot*: the husband's rights ended when the writing began, and the date in the *get* accurately reflects that cutoff.

For the *Tanna Kamma*, by contrast, the husband retains *peirot* until *sha'at netinah* — the moment of delivery. Because rights persist until delivery, the *zman* in the *get* cannot be the writing date, since that would mislead buyers into thinking the husband's rights ended earlier than they did. But this *peirot* concern is not, on Rabbi Yochanan's account, the reason the *Tanna Kamma* requires a date; that reason is *bas achoto*. Rather, it is a separate, background consideration that runs through the whole system.

### Peirot Timing

With both accounts now laid out, the Gemara crystallizes the underlying positions. According to Reish Lakish, the entire *machloket* between Rabbi Shimon and the *Tanna Kamma* rests on when the husband's *peirot* rights terminate: Rabbi Shimon holds from *sha'at ketivah*, the *Tanna Kamma* holds from *sha'at chatimah*. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the *Tanna Kamma*'s rationale for requiring a *zman* is *bas achoto*, while Rabbi Shimon does not share that concern.

The broader framework that emerges is this: there are three temporally distinct moments in the *get* process — *ketivah* (writing), *chatimah* (signing), and *netinah* (delivery). Different authorities anchor the husband's rights to *peirot* at different points along this timeline, and those anchoring decisions determine whether a predated *get* is valid or creates a legal fiction that harms third parties.

### Peirot Sources

The Gemara then presents an alternate version of the dispute — *ve'ika d'amri*, there are those who state it differently. In this formulation, the question is not why the Rabbanan required a date in the *get*, but rather: from when does the wife begin to collect *peirot*? That is, from which moment do the husband's entitlements cease and the wife's property rights become fully operative?

Rabbi Yochanan says: *mishat ketivah* — from the moment of writing. Reish Lakish says: *mishat netinah* — from the moment of delivery.

This alternate version inverts the positions relative to what was stated earlier, and the Gemara notes that a *baraita* supports the *netinah* reading for one of them. The resolution is *efoch* — reverse the attribution. The precise assignment of which Amora holds which position in this alternate version requires the flip, bringing the positions back into alignment with the *baraita*'s evidence.

The sugya thus concludes with a rich, multi-layered dispute about the halachic timeline of a *get* — a dispute whose ramifications extend to the rights of buyers, the protection of wives, and the fundamental question of when a divorce takes legal effect in its various dimensions.
