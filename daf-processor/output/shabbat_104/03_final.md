# Shabbat 104 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Open/Closed Mem

### Shin-Mem Word Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מָצִינוּ שֵׁם קָטָן מִשֵּׁם גָּדוֹל. מִי דָּמֵי? מֵ״ם דְּ״שֵׁם״ סָתוּם, מֵ״ם דְּ״שִׁמְעוֹן״ פָּתוּחַ!
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda said: We found** that one is liable for writing even if he did not complete what he was writing and wrote **a small name** that constituted part **of a longer name,** e.g., Shem from Shimon. The Gemara asks: **Is it similar?** The ***mem*** in **Shem is closed and the *mem*** in **Shimon is open.**

The daf opens by returning to a question raised at the bottom of 103b. The governing principle for *melacha* of *ketivah* on *Shabbos* is that one must write two letters — and not merely any two letters, but two letters that together constitute a word or meaningful name. The case under discussion is someone who sets out to write the full name *Shimon* and, having written *shin-mem*, stops. Since *shem* is itself a complete word, that person is *chayav*, even though the *mem* he wrote was part of a longer intended word.

The *gemara*'s difficulty is immediate: when one writes *shin-mem* in the middle of writing *Shimon*, the *mem* is open — it is a *mem* appearing in the middle of a word — whereas the word *shem* requires a closed *mem sofit*. How then can it be considered as though one wrote a complete, valid word?

### Braita Letter Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת סָתוּם וַעֲשָׂאוֹ פָּתוּחַ — כָּשֵׁר. מֵיתִיבִי: ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ — שֶׁתְּהֵא כְּתִיבָה תַּמָּה, שֶׁלֹּא יִכְתּוֹב אַלְפִין עַיְינִין, עַיְינִין אַלְפִין. בֵּיתִין כָּפִין, כָּפִין בֵּיתִין. גַּמִּים צָדִין, צָדִין גַּמִּים. דַּלְתִין רֵישִׁין, רֵישִׁין דַּלְתִין. הֵיהִין חֵיתִין, חֵיתִין הֵיהִין. וָוִין יוֹדִין, יוֹדִין וָוִין. זַיְינִין נוּנִין, נוּנִין זַיְינִין. טֵיתִין פֵּיפִין, פֵּיפִין טֵיתִין. כְּפוּפִין פְּשׁוּטִין, פְּשׁוּטִין כְּפוּפִין. מֵימִין סָמְכִין, סָמְכִין מֵימִין. סְתוּמִין פְּתוּחִין, פְּתוּחִין סְתוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: That is to say** that a **closed** letter that **one rendered open is valid** even in writing a Torah scroll, and it is not considered an irregularity in the writing. Therefore, one is liable for writing an open letter instead of a closed one. The Gemara **raised an objection** from a *baraita* that interprets the verse: **"And you shall write them** on the doorposts of your house and on your gates" (Deuteronomy 6:9). "And you shall write them [*ukhtavtam*]" means **that it should be perfect writing [*ketiva tamma*]** with no mistakes, and clear writing. This means that **one should not write** an ***alef* as** an ***ayin*,** an ***ayin* as** an ***alef*,** a ***beit* as** a ***kaf*,** a ***kaf* as** a ***beit*,** a ***gimmel* as** a ***tzadi*,** a ***tzadi* as** a ***gimmel*,** a ***dalet* as** a ***reish*,** a ***reish* as** a ***dalet*,** a ***heh* as** a ***ḥet*,** a ***ḥet* as** a ***heh*,** a ***vav* as** a ***yod*,** a ***yod* as** a ***vav*,** a ***zayin* as** a ***nun*,** a ***nun* as** a ***zayin*,** a ***tet* as** a ***peh*,** a ***peh* as** a ***tet*. Similarly, one should not write **bent** letters like *kaf* and *nun* found in the middle of a word as **straight** letters like *kaf* and *nun* found at the end of a word, nor should one write **straight** letters as **bent** letters. A final ***mem*** should not be written like a ***samekh*,** and a ***samekh*** should not be written like a ***mem*.** A **closed** *mem* should not be written **open,** and an **open** one should not be written **closed.**

The *gemara* considers whether an open *mem* might simply count as equivalent to a closed *mem* — and specifically whether, for purposes of a *sefer torah*, one would be valid in place of the other. This possibility is immediately foreclosed by a *braita* that enumerates letter-distinctions critical to the validity of a *sefer torah*: one must not write a *dalet* so that it resembles a *reish*, nor a *chaf* so that it resembles a *beis*, nor may one substitute an open *mem* for a closed one or vice versa. The *braita* makes clear that these forms are not interchangeable in *sefer torah* standards.

### Nisuch Hamayim Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר בַּשֵּׁנִי ״וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם״. בַּשִּׁשִּׁי ״וּנְסָכֶיהָ״, בַּשְּׁבִיעִי ״כְּמִשְׁפָּטָם״, הֲרֵי מֵ״ם יוֹ״ד מֵ״ם — ״מַיִם״, מִכָּאן רֶמֶז לְנִיסּוּךְ מַיִם מִן הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Ḥisda **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** While on the rest of the days of Sukkot the verse employs the phrase: "And its libation [*veniska*]," on the **second** day **it is stated: "And their libations [*veniskeihem*]"** (Numbers 29:19) with an extra letter *mem*; **on the sixth day, it is stated: "And its libations [*unsakhe'ah*]"** (Numbers 29:31) with an extra letter *yod*. **On the seventh** day, instead of "according to the law [*kamishpat*]" employed on the other days, **it is stated: "According to their laws [*kemishpatam*]"** (Numbers 29:33) with an extra letter *mem*. Together these additional letters, ***mem*, *yod*,** and ***mem*, form the word *mayim*,** which means water. **This is an allusion to the water libation from the Torah.** On *Sukkot*, a water libation was poured onto the altar in addition to the wine libation that accompanied sacrifices throughout the year. However, here, the closed *mem* at the end of the word *veniskeihem* is interpreted as if it were an open *mem* and used as the first *mem* in *mayim*.

Before resolving the contradiction, the *gemara* identifies the *mishna*'s authorship. It says the author of the *mishna* holds like the following *tanna*, and cites a *braita* in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira observes that across the various days of *Sukkot* where the *korbanot* are listed, the Torah writes *ve-nisko* in most places, but in three places something additional appears: on the second day, *ve-niskei-hem* — the *mem* is extra; on the sixth day, *ve-niskhe-ha* — the *yud* is extra; and on the seventh day, *ke-mishpatam* instead of *ke-mishpat* — again a *mem* is extra. The letters *mem-yud-mem* spell *mayim*, and this is the *remez* from the Torah for *nisuch hamayim* — the water libation performed in the *Beit Hamikdash* on *Sukkot*.

The significance for our question lies in this: the first *mem* in *ve-niskei-hem* is a *mem sofit*, yet Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira uses it to represent the open *mem* of *mayim*. This suggests that for the purposes of such a *drasha*, a *mem sofit* can stand in for an open *mem* — and perhaps more broadly that the two forms of the letter carry some halakhic equivalence.

### Mem Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּפָתוּחַ וַעֲשָׂאוֹ סָתוּם — כָּשֵׁר, סָתוּם נָמֵי סָתוּם וַעֲשָׂאוֹ פָּתוּחַ — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** from the fact **that an open** letter **that one rendered closed is valid,** in the case of a **closed** letter, **too, a closed letter that one rendered open is valid.** This homiletic interpretation supports Rav Ḥisda's opinion.

The *gemara* presses forward. *U-midipatuach ve-asao satum kasher* — if what should have been open one instead made closed, it is *kasher*, as demonstrated by that very *drasha* of *mayim*. And by the same logic, *satum nami* — the reverse should equally hold: *u-ma satum ve-asao patuach kasher*, if something that should be closed is rendered open, it too is *kasher*. Therefore, writing *shem* with an open *mem* counts as having written a complete word.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַלּוֹיֵי קָא מְעַלֵּי לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מֵ״ם וְסָמֶ״ךְ שֶׁבַּלּוּחוֹת בְּנֵס הָיוּ עוֹמְדִין. אֶלָּא סָתוּם וַעֲשָׂאוֹ פָּתוּחַ — גָּרוֹעֵי קָא מְגָרַע לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: מַנְצְפַךְ צוֹפִים אֲמָרוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **he elevates its** status, as **Rav Ḥisda said:** The letters ***mem* and *samekh* that were in the tablets were standing miraculously.** Each letter was chiseled all the way through the tablets. In that case, the segment of the tablets at the center of the *samekh* and final *mem*, letters that are completely closed, should have fallen. Miraculously, they remained in place. Consequently, rendering an open *mem* closed elevates its status. **However,** if **one rendered a closed** letter **open, he diminishes its** status, as **Rabbi Yirmeya said, and some say** that it was **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** who said: **The prophets instituted** the difference between the open and closed forms of the letters ***mem*, *nun*, *tzadi*, *peh*, *kaf*.** Since the closed letters date back to the Ten Commandments, apparently the prophets introduced the open versions of the letters, which are therefore less significant.

The *gemara* then interjects: *mi dami*? One cannot compare so simply. Making something open into closed — *iluye kama'ali* — is actually an improvement, an elevation of the letter. But making something closed into open — *geruay kama gara lei* — is a diminishment. Why is a closed letter considered superior? Because, as Rav Chisda teaches, the forms of the closed letters are original, and the open forms came later. To which the *gemara* now turns.

It is worth pausing to note the interesting methodological question lurking here: when evaluating *ketivah* on *Shabbos*, to what extent do we apply the halakhic standards of *sefer torah* writing, and to what extent do we simply look at what ordinary people recognize as writing? It is obvious that one need not write letters with their full scribal precision — their crowns and ornamental strokes — in order to be *chayav* on *Shabbos*. Yet here the *gemara* is explicitly importing the standards of *sefer torah* to resolve a question about *Shabbos* liability. This tension will resurface below.

## Script Origins

### Luchos Miracle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיסְבְּרָא?! וְהָכְתִיב: ״אֵלֶּה הַמִּצְוֹת״ — שֶׁאֵין הַנָּבִיא רַשַּׁאי לְחַדֵּשׁ דָּבָר מֵעַתָּה! אֶלָּא מִיהְוָה הֲוַאי, מִידָּע לָא הֲוָה יָדְעִין הֵי בְּאֶמְצַע תֵּיבָה הֵי בְּסוֹף תֵּיבָה, וַאֲתוֹ צוֹפִים תַּקְּנִינְהוּ. וְאַכַּתִּי ״אֵלֶּה הַמִּצְוֹת״ — שֶׁאֵין הַנָּבִיא רַשַּׁאי לְחַדֵּשׁ דָּבָר מֵעַתָּה! אֶלָּא, שְׁכָחוּם וְחָזְרוּ וְיִסְּדוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **And is that reasonable? Isn't it written: "These are the commandments** that the Lord commanded Moses to tell the children of Israel at Mount Sinai" (Leviticus 27:34). The word "these" underscores **that a prophet is not permitted to introduce any** new **element** related to the Torah and its mitzvot **from here on. Rather,** the prophets did not innovate these forms. Both the open and closed versions **existed** before then. However, people **did not know which** form appeared **in the middle of a word** and **which** form **at the end of a word. And the prophets came** and **instituted their** set positions. The Gemara asks: **And still** the question remains: Didn't the Sages derive from the verse: **"These are the commandments," that a prophet is not permitted to introduce any** new **element from here on?** How could they institute the position of the letters? **Rather,** over the course of time, the people **forgot their** positions in the words **and** the prophets **then reestablished their** positions. Apparently, closed letters are no more significant than the open ones.

To explain why the closed letter is the elevated, original form, the *gemara* cites Rav Chisda: *mem ve-Samech she-ba-luchos b'nes hayu omdim* — the *mem* and the *samech* on the *luchos* stood miraculously. The *luchos* were carved through completely, from one side to the other. But if the carving passed entirely through the stone, the interior block of a *mem sofit* or a *samech* — which is enclosed on all sides — would have no physical connection to the surrounding stone and would simply fall out. That central piece was therefore suspended in mid-air by miracle.

This teaching establishes that the closed *mem* — the *mem sofit* — existed already at the time of the *luchos*. The letter form we see at the end of words is thus ancient, original.

### Yerushalmi Ayin

The *Yerushalmi* presents an alternative teaching: it is the *ayin*, not the *mem* and *samech*, that stood miraculously on the *luchos*. The reason is that in ancient Hebrew script (*Ktav Ivrit*), the *ayin* — shaped like an eye — actually resembles the *samech*. The *Yerushalmi* apparently assumes that the *luchos* were written in ancient Hebrew script, not *Ktav Ashurit*, and therefore a different letter would have posed the miraculous challenge. This stands in contrast to the *Bavli*, which in *Megillah* grapples with the question of which script was used — arguing, somewhat strain­ingly, that *Ktav Ashurit* was the original script, was temporarily replaced by ancient Hebrew, and then restored. The *Yerushalmi* has no such difficulty: it simply assumes the *luchos* were in the ancient script.

### Prophets & Letters

Returning to the *Bavli*'s argument: the *gemara* now adduces a teaching of Rabbi Yirmiyah — some say Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba — that *manatzpach*, i.e., the letters *mem, nun, tzadi, peh, chaf*, those five letters that have distinct forms in the middle and at the end of words, were established by the *tzofim* — the prophets. The name *manatzpach* itself is a play on *min tzofayich*, "from your seers."

If the end letters already existed at the time of the *luchos* — as proven by the miracle of the *mem sofit* — and the prophets later introduced the distinction between middle and final forms, then the prophets must have introduced the *middle* letters. The open *mem*, the open *nun*, and their counterparts would be the later innovation. This explains why making an open letter closed is an elevation (returning to the original) while making a closed letter open is a diminishment (introducing the later form where it does not belong).

### Bavli Rejection

The *gemara* resists this conclusion sharply. *Ve-tisbera*? Can we really accept that the prophets introduced a new orthography? The verse states *eileh ha-mitzvos she-ein navi rashai le-chadesh davar me-atah* — these are the commandments, and no prophet is permitted to introduce anything new from this point forward. Whether because introducing a new script constitutes a change in the *mitzvos* themselves — since the laws of writing *sifrei torah*, *mezuzos*, and *tefillin* are tied to the form of the letters — or because of a general conservatism about any post-Mosaic innovation, the *gemara* cannot accept that the prophets genuinely introduced something that did not previously exist.

A first attempted resolution suggests that both forms always existed, but people did not know which belonged in the middle and which at the end — the two forms were used interchangeably. The prophets then regulated the usage and assigned each form to its proper position. But this too the *gemara* rejects with the same verse: even introducing a new restriction — even moving from interchangeable to regulated — is a form of *chidush* that prophets are not permitted to make. It would invalidate *mezuzos* that had previously been written in the reverse order.

### Resolution

The *gemara* arrives at its final resolution: *she-kichaum ve-chazru ve-yisdum* — both forms always existed, with clear rules about which belongs in the middle and which at the end; but at some point this knowledge was forgotten, usage became confused, and the prophets came and re-established the original practice. They were *machzir atara le-yoshnah* — restoring the crown to its original form, not introducing anything new.

This resolution is characteristic of the *Bavli*'s deeply conservative approach to questions of halakhic continuity. Not only is there no room for new *mitzvos* after *Moshe Rabbeinu*, but even changes as subtle as the regularization of letter forms cannot be admitted as genuine innovations. The prophets can only remind; they cannot originate.

With this established, the *gemara* can close the loop on the original question. Since it has always been the case that there were two forms and they were associated with specific positions, and since we see from the *drasha* of *mayim* that an end letter can represent a middle letter, the reverse is equally true. Writing *shem* with an open *mem*, while not valid for a *sefer torah*, is sufficiently recognizable as the equivalent of a closed *mem* to constitute *ketivah* for purposes of *Shabbos* liability.

## Luchos Reading Both Ways

### Bidirectional Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מֵ״ם וְסָמֶ״ךְ שֶׁבַּלּוּחוֹת בְּנֵס הָיוּ עוֹמְדִין. וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: כְּתָב שֶׁבַּלּוּחוֹת נִקְרָא מִבִּפְנִים וְנִקְרָא מִבַּחוּץ, כְּגוֹן: ״נְבוּב״ — ״בובן״, ״רַהַב״ — ״בָּהָר״, ״סָרוּ״ — ״ורס״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss **the matter itself. Rav Ḥisda said:** The letters ***mem* and *samekh* that were in the tablets were standing miraculously. And** furthermore, **Rav Ḥisda said:** The **writing on the tablets was read from the inside,** from one side of the tablets, **and read from the outside,** the other side of the tablets, in reverse order. The Gemara cites words that appear elsewhere in the Bible: ***Nevuv*** was read as ***bet*, *vav*, *bet*, *nun*; *rahav*** as ***beit*, *heh*, *reish*;** and ***saru*** as ***vav*, *reish*, *samekh*.**

The *gemara* continues with a second teaching of Rav Chisda: *kesav she-ba-luchos nikra mi-p'nim ve-nikra mi-bachutz* — the writing on the *luchos* could be read from the front and could be read from the back, because the carving passed entirely through the stone. As examples of what such bidirectional reading would look like, the *gemara* offers: *nevuv* read backwards becomes *buvan*; *Elokim* reversed produces *saru*; *rahav bahar* reversed becomes *bahar rahav*. The letters would not merely appear in reverse order — each individual letter would also be mirror-flipped.

*Tosafot* raises the question: why did the *gemara* choose these examples rather than words that actually appear in the *luchos*? The observation is left without a fully satisfying resolution, though words like *nevuv* and *saru* do appear in the relevant biblical contexts.

### Mystical Codes

The Maharsha offers an interpretation of the point of this teaching. One might think, he suggests, that the miracle of the *luchos* included the wondrous detail that when viewed from behind they read exactly the same as from the front — a kind of palindromic miracle. Rav Chisda is correcting that impression: no, viewed from behind they read backwards, as one would expect from any object carved through. But the Maharsha reads a deeper implication into the *gemara*'s choice to make this point here, at this moment in the discussion: he sees it as a prelude to the next passage, which explores the hidden, mystical dimensions of the Hebrew letters. The backwards reading of the *luchos* was not a failed miracle but a deliberate feature — carrying its own layer of meaning, its own hidden code embedded in the divine text. Whether or not this is what the *gemara* explicitly states, the Maharsha sees in it a thematic bridge to what follows.

## Aleph-Bet Drasha

### Aleph-Beis Start

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אֲתוֹ דַּרְדְּקֵי הָאִידָּנָא לְבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא וַאֲמַרוּ מִילֵּי דַּאֲפִילּוּ בִּימֵי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן לָא אִיתְּמַר כְּווֹתַיְיהוּ: אָלֶ״ף בֵּי״ת — אֲלַף בִּינָה. גִּימֶ״ל דָּלֶ״ת — גְּמוֹל דַּלִּים. מַאי טַעְמָא פְּשׁוּטָה כַּרְעֵיהּ דְּגִימֶ״ל לְגַבֵּי דָּלֶ״ת — שֶׁכֵּן דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל גּוֹמֵל חֲסָדִים לָרוּץ אַחַר דַּלִּים. וּמַאי טַעְמָא פְּשׁוּטָה כַּרְעֵיהּ דְּדָלֶ״ת לְגַבַּי גִּימֶ״ל — דְּלַימְצְיַהּ לֵיהּ נַפְשֵׁיהּ. וּמַאי טַעְמָא מְהַדַּר אַפֵּיהּ דְּדָלֶ״ת מִגִּימֶ״ל — דְּלִיתֵּן לֵיהּ בְּצִינְעָה, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיכְּסִיף מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: Young students came** today **to the study hall and said things** the likes of **which were not said** even **in the days of Joshua bin Nun.** These children who only knew the Hebrew alphabet interpreted the letters homiletically. ***Alef beit*** means **learn** [***elaf***] the **wisdom** [***bina***] of the Torah. ***Gimmel dalet*** means **give to the poor** [***gemol dalim***]. **Why is the leg of the *gimmel* extended toward** the ***dalet*? Because it is the manner of one who bestows loving-kindness to pursue the poor. And why** is the **leg of** the ***dalet* extended toward** the ***gimmel*?** It is so **that** a poor person **will make himself available to him** who wants to give him charity. **And why does the *dalet* face away from** the ***gimmel*?** It is to teach **that one should give** charity **discreetly so that** the poor person **will not be embarrassed by him.**

The *gemara* relates that the *rabbanim* said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: some schoolchildren came to the *beit midrash* today and said things the like of which had not been said even from the days of *Yehoshua bin Nun*. What they presented was an elaborate *drasha* on the *alef-beis* — a systematic reading of moral and spiritual meaning into each letter and letter-pair. The *drasha* itself is in Hebrew; inserted into it in Aramaic are later *gemara*-layer expansions that elaborate each point further.

The *drasha* opens: *alef-beis* — *alef binah*, learn wisdom. The *alef* represents *alifah*, learning; the *beis* represents *binah*, understanding. The combined message: learn, and you will gain wisdom. *Gimmel-dalet* — *gemol dalim*, give kindness to the poor. The *gimel* evokes *gemilut chassadim*; the *dalet* evokes *dalim*, the impoverished.

### Gimmel-Dalet Shapes

The *gemara* then presses deeper into the shapes of the letters themselves. *Mai ta'ama peshuta kara'ei de-gimel le-gabei dalet?* Why does the leg of the *gimel* extend forward, pointing toward the *dalet*? *She-ken darko shel gomeil chassadim larutz achar dal* — because it is the way of a genuine *gomeil chassadim* to run after the poor person, not to wait passively until approached. One who truly gives with love and kindness actively pursues the opportunity to give.

*U-mai ta'ama peshuta kara'ei de-dalet le-gabei gimel?* Why is the foot of the *dalet* angled back slightly toward the *gimel*? *De-limtze leh nafshei* — so that the poor person can make himself available, not fleeing too quickly from the one who wishes to give. He holds back just enough.

And *mai ta'ama mahadar apei de-dalet mi-gimel?* Why is the face of the *dalet* — the head of the letter — turned away from the *gimel*? *De-litei leh be-tzin'a* — so that the poor person need not look directly at his benefactor while receiving, sparing him the shame and embarrassment of facing the one who gives. *Ki heichi de-lo likhsof mineh* — so that he should not be made to feel ashamed.

### Hey-Vav Zecharya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ה״ו — זֶה שְׁמוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. ז״ח ט״י כ״ל — וְאִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא זָן אוֹתְךָ, וְחָן אוֹתְךָ, וּמֵטִיב לְךָ, וְנוֹתֵן לְךָ יְרוּשָּׁה, וְקוֹשֵׁר לְךָ כֶּתֶר לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. מֵ״ם פְּתוּחָה, מֵ״ם סְתוּמָה — מַאֲמָר פָּתוּחַ, מַאֲמָר סָתוּם. נוּ״ן כְּפוּפָה, נוּ״ן פְּשׁוּטָה — נֶאֱמָן כָּפוּף, נֶאֱמָן פָּשׁוּט.
>
> **Translation:** The children continued to interpret the letters. ***Heh vav*: That is** the principal **name of the Holy One, Blessed be He.** ***Zayin ḥet*, *tet yod*, *kaf lamed*: And if you do so, the Holy One, Blessed be He, feeds [*zan*] you, and shows you favor [*ḥan*], and bestows goodness [*meitiv*] upon you, and gives you an inheritance [*yerusha*], and ties a crown [*keter*] for you in the World to Come [*la'olam haba*].** The **open *mem* and closed *mem*** indicate that the Torah contains **an open statement,** understood by all, and **an esoteric statement.** The **bent *nun*** and the **straight *nun*** at the end of a word refer to **a faithful person who is bent [*ne'eman kafuf*]** and is modest now, who will ultimately become a **well-known faithful person [*ne'eman pashut*].**

The *drasha* continues: *hey-vav* — that is one half of the divine Name. Then *zayin, chet, tet, yud, chaf, lamed*: the *gemara* reads these letters as a divine promise to one who learns Torah and performs *gemilut chassadim* and *tzedakah*. The *zayin* — *zan otcha*, God will sustain you. *Chet* and *chaf* — *ve-chonecha otcha*, He will show you kindness. *Tet* — *u-metiv lecha*, He will give you goodness. *Yud* — *ve-noten lecha yerusha*, He gives you an inheritance. *Chaf* and *lamed* — *ve-kosher lecha keter le-olam haba*, He binds for you a crown in *olam haba*. The letters trace out a complete arc of reward for righteous conduct.

### Mem Nun Teachings

*Mem petucha, mem setuma* — an open *mem* and a closed *mem* — teach: *ma'amar patuach, ma'amar satum*. Rashi explains that some words of Torah are open, meant to be taught; others are closed, esoteric, meant to be hidden. The careful speaker knows which is which.

*Nun kefufah, nun peshutah* — a bent *nun* and a straight *nun* — teach: *ne'eman kafuf, ve-ne'eman pashut*. The trustworthy, righteous person is bent in this world and straight in the next. Rashi reads this as humility in *olam ha-zeh* and exaltation in *olam haba*. The Maharsha connects this to the two opinions about the *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah* — one opinion that it should be bent (evoking humility and submission before God), and the other that it should be straight (evoking aspiration and reaching upward toward God). Both dimensions coexist: bodily bent, spirit reaching upward.

### Samekh-Tzaddi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ס״ע — סְמוֹךְ עֲנִיִּים. לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא: סִימָנִין עֲשֵׂה בַּתּוֹרָה וּקְנֵה אוֹתָהּ. פ׳ כְּפוּפָה, פ׳ פְּשׁוּטָה — פֶּה פָּתוּחַ, פֶּה סָתוּם. צָדִ״י כְּפוּפָה וְצָדִ״י פְּשׁוּטָה — צַדִּיק כָּפוּף, צַדִּיק פָּשׁוּט. הַיְינוּ: נֶאֱמָן כָּפוּף, נֶאֱמָן פָּשׁוּט! הוֹסִיף לְךָ הַכָּתוּב כְּפִיפָה עַל כְּפִיפָתוֹ, מִכָּאן שֶׁנִּתְּנָה הַתּוֹרָה בִּמְנוֹד רֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** ***Samekh ayin*: Support the poor [*semokh aniyyim*]** to prevent them from falling further. **Another version: Make mnemonic** signs **[*simanim aseh*]** to remember **the Torah and acquire it.** The **bent *peh*** and the **straight *peh*:** Sometimes one needs to have **an open mouth [*peh patuaḥ*]** and speak, and sometimes one needs to have **a closed mouth [*peh satum*].** The **bent *tzadi*** and the **straight *tzadi*** indicate that **a righteous** person who is **bent** and humble **[*tzaddik kafuf*]** now will ultimately become **a well-known righteous** person **[*tzaddik pashut*]** whose righteousness is apparent to all. The Gemara asks: **That is identical** to the interpretation of the bent and straight *nun*: ***Ne'eman kafuf*, *ne'eman pashut*.** The Gemara explains: **The verse added** the **bending** of the righteous person **to the bending of** the faithful person. **From here** it is derived **that the Torah was given in** an atmosphere of **gravity.** One must receive the Torah with a sense of awe and extreme humility.

*Samech-ayin* — *semokh aniyim*, support the poor. An alternative reading: *simanim aseh ba-Torah*, make mnemonic signs for yourself in the Torah, so that you can retain what you have learned. And therefore you will *u-knei* — acquire Torah. The distinction between *gimmel-dalet* as *gemol dalim* and *samech-ayin* as *semokh aniyim* is worth noting: *gemilut chassadim* and *tzedakah* are not identical, and the *gemara* may intentionally repeat the theme of supporting the poor in two distinct formulations.

*Peh kefufah, peh peshutah* — a bent *peh* (the middle form) and a straight *peh* (the final form) — teach: *peh patuach, peh satum*. Parallel to the teaching of the *mem*: there are things one speaks about openly, and things one keeps closed. Guard the mouth.

*Tzaddi kefufah, tzaddi peshutah* — a bent *tzaddi* and a straight *tzaddi* — teach: *tzaddik kafuf, tzaddik pashut*, the righteous person bowed and the righteous person upright. The *gemara* notes this appears to duplicate what was already said about the *nun*: *hainu ne'eman kafuf ne'eman pashut*. The response: *hosif lecha ha-katuv kefifah al kefifato* — the Torah by repeating the lesson adds another measure of bowing, teaching that one must strive for *ever greater* humility. *Mikan she-nitna Torah be-mandud rosh* — from here we learn that the Torah was given with the lowering of the head. The Maharsha quotes the famous line from *Pirkei Avot*, *me'od me'od hevei shefal ruach* — be exceedingly lowly of spirit — as a counterpoint to the Greek ideal, which considered humility shameful. Here *Chazal* insist on the opposite.

### Kuf-Reish Teshuvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קוּ״ף — קָדוֹשׁ. רֵי״שׁ — רָשָׁע. מַאי טַעְמָא מְהַדַּר אַפֵּיהּ דְּקוּ״ף מֵרֵי״שׁ — אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אֵין אֲנִי יָכוֹל לְהִסְתַּכֵּל בָּרָשָׁע. וּמַאי טַעְמָא מְהַדַּר תָּגֵיהּ דְּקוּ״ף לְגַבֵּי רֵי״שׁ — אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אִם חוֹזֵר בּוֹ אֲנִי קוֹשֵׁר לוֹ כֶּתֶר כְּמוֹתִי. וּמַאי טַעְמָא כַּרְעֵיהּ דְּקוּ״ף תַּלְיָא — דְּאִי הָדַר בֵּיהּ, לִיעַיֵּיל.
>
> **Translation:** The children continued: ***Kuf*: Holy [*kadosh*],** referring to God. ***Reish*: A wicked person [*rasha*]. Why is the *kuf* facing away from** the ***reish*?** This question was phrased euphemistically, as it is the *reish* that is facing away from the *kuf*. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I am unable look at a wicked person,** i.e., the wicked person does not want to look toward God. **And why is the crown of** the letter ***kuf* turned toward** the ***reish*? The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: If** the wicked person **repents** his evil ways **I will tie a crown for him like My own. And why is the leg of** the ***kuf* suspended** and not connected to the roof of the letter? **Because if** the wicked person **repents he can enter** through this opening if he so desires.

*Kuf* is *kadosh*, holy — it represents God. *Reish* is *rasha*, the wicked one. Now the *drasha* must reckon with the co-existence of the righteous world and the *rasha*. God stands adjacent to the *rasha* in the alphabet: what is the relationship between them?

*Mai ta'ama mahadar apei de-kuf mei-reish?* Why does the *kuf* not face the *reish* — why is God's face, as it were, turned away from the *rasha*? The *gemara* answers in the name of the Holy One: *ein ani yakhol le-histakel ba-rasha* — I cannot look upon the *rasha*. He turns away.

But then: *u-mai ta'ama mahadar tagei de-kuf le-gabei reish?* Why does the little crown — the *tag* — on top of the *kuf* lean toward the *reish*? *Amar Ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu: im chozer bo, ani kosher lo keter kemoti* — if he returns in *teshuvah*, I will bind for him a crown like my own. Even while God does not face the *rasha* directly, God reaches out toward him through the crown atop the letter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵיעוּל בְּהָךְ! מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אִם לַלֵּצִים הוּא יָלִיץ וְלַעֲנָוִים יִתֶּן חֵן״? בָּא לִיטַמֵּא — פּוֹתְחִין לוֹ, בָּא לִיטָּהֵר — מְסַיְּיעִים אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let him enter through that** opening, as the *kuf* is open on both sides at the bottom. The Gemara answers: This **supports** the statement of **Reish Lakish, as Reish Lakish said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "If it concerns the scorners, He scorns them, and unto the humble He gives grace"** (Proverbs 3:34)? One who **comes** in order **to become impure,** i.e., to sin, **they,** in Heaven, **provide him with an opening** to do so, and he is not prevented from sinning. However, if he **comes** in order **to become purified,** not only is he allowed to do so, but **they,** in Heaven, **assist him.**

*U-mai ta'ama kura de-kuf tluyah?* Why is the leg of the *kuf* suspended — detached from the body of the letter rather than connected to its base? *De-i hadar bei layeil* — so that if he wishes to return, there is an opening for him to enter. The door is not shut.

The *gemara* then asks: *u-layeil be-hach?* Why not just have him enter from the other, obvious side? The answer is that this supports Reish Lakish, who taught: *im latsim hu yalits, ve-la-anavim yiten chen* — if someone chooses to mock and sin, God opens the way for him (*ba le-tamei potchin lo*); but if someone comes to purify himself, God actively assists him (*ba le-taher mesayin oto*). The opening in the *kuf*'s leg is not merely passive — it represents God actively facilitating the return of the *rasha* who wishes to do *teshuvah*, not just leaving a door ajar but providing a direct path back.

### Shin-Tav Truth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שִׁי״ן — שֶׁקֶר. תָּי״ו — אֱמֶת. מַאי טַעְמָא שֶׁקֶר מְקָרְבָן מִילֵּיהּ, אֱמֶת מְרַחֲקָא מִילֵּיהּ? — שִׁיקְרָא שְׁכִיחַ, קוּשְׁטָא לָא שְׁכִיחַ. וּמַאי טַעְמָא שִׁיקְרָא אַחֲדָא כַּרְעֵיהּ קָאֵי, וֶאֱמֶת מְלַבַּן לַבּוֹנֵי — קוּשְׁטָא קָאֵי, שִׁיקְרָא לָא קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** They further taught: ***Shin*: Falsehood [*sheker*]. *Tav*: Truth [*emet*].** **Why are the letters of** the word ***sheker* adjacent** to one another in the alphabet, while **the letters of *emet* are distant** from one another? That is because while **falsehood is** easily **found, truth is found** only with great difficulty. **And why do** the letters that comprise the word ***sheker*** all **stand on one foot, and** the letters that comprise the word ***emet*** stand on bases that are wide like **bricks?** Because the **truth stands** eternal and **falsehood does not stand** eternal.

The *drasha* reaches its conclusion. *Shin* is *sheker*, falsehood; *tav* is *emes*, truth. The world the *drasha* has described is not perfectly righteous — the *rasha* is real, and the letters must reckon with him. But even here the letter-shapes carry a message.

*Mai ta'ama sheker mekarvin milei, emes merachaka milei?* Why are the letters of *sheker* — *shin, kuf, reish* — close together in the alphabet, while the letters of *emes* — *alef, mem, tav* — are spread evenly across its beginning, middle, and end? Because *shikra shchiach* — falsehood is common, densely packed in this world; *kushta lo shchiach* — truth is rare, widely dispersed.

*U-mai ta'ama shikra achada krei kaei?* Why does *sheker* stand on a single pointed leg? The *shin* comes to a point, the *kuf*'s leg is narrow, the *reish* tapers — the entire word balances precariously on one thin base. *Ve-emes melaben libunei* — while *emes*, like bricks, rests on a flat, solid foundation. *Kushta kaei* — truth stands firm, truth is what establishes and upholds the world. *Shikra lo kaei* — falsehood has no lasting foundation, even if, as the real world sometimes demonstrates painfully, it can travel far before truth catches up.

## Atbash Drasha

### Atbash Folding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** א״ת ב״ש: — אוֹתִי תִּעֵב אֶתְאַוֶּה לוֹ ב״ש בִּי לֹא חָשַׁק, שְׁמִי יָחוּל עָלָיו? ג״ר — גּוּפוֹ טִימֵּא, אֲרַחֵם עָלָיו ד״ק דַּלְתוֹתַי נָעַל, קַרְנָיו לֹא אֲגַדֵּעַ? עַד כָּאן מִדַּת רְשָׁעִים,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another midrash that also deals with the letters of the alphabet. This one uses a code in which the first letter is paired with the last letter, the second letter with the penultimate one, and so on **[*alef tav*, *beit shin*].** *Alef tav*, God said: If **he despised Me [*oti ti'ev*]** would **I desire [*etaveh*] him? *Beit shin*:** If **he does not desire** to worship **Me [*bi*],** shall **My name [*shemi*] rest upon him? *Gimmel reish*:** He **defiled his body [*gufo*];** shall **I have mercy [*araḥem*] on him?** The word comprised of the letters *gimmel* and *reish* in Aramaic means licentiousness. ***Dalet kuf*:** He **locked My doors [*daltotai*],** shall **I not cut off his horns [*karnav*]? To this point,** the Gemara interpreted the letters as referring to **the attribute of the wicked.**

Having completed the *alef-beis* in sequence, the *gemara* now performs letter-substitution *drashos*, the most famous of which is *atbash*. The method: fold the 22-letter alphabet in half, and pair each letter of the first half with the corresponding letter from the second half in reverse. *Alef* pairs with *tav*, *beis* with *shin*, *gimel* with *reish*, and so on down to *kaf* pairing with *lamed*. The resulting letter-pairs produce new words that carry their own meaning.

### Wicked Response

Because the *alef-beis drasha* concluded by introducing the *rasha* — and because *sheker* stands opposite *emes* — the *atbash* now reads out a response to the *resha'im*. *Oti te'ev* — if you despise Me, God; *bi lo chashak* — you have no desire for Me; will My Name rest upon you? *Gufo tamei* — if a person has made himself impure; *aracham alav?* — shall I have compassion on him? *Daltosai na'al* — he has locked My doors, he has not entered My house. *Karnav lo agade'a* — should I not cut off his horns? *Ad kan midat resha'im* — this is the measure applied to the wicked.

### Righteous Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל מִדַּת צַדִּיקִים: א״ת ב״ש — אִם אַתָּה בּוֹשׁ. ג״ר ד״ק — אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן, גּוּר בְּדוֹק. ה״ץ ו״ף — חֲצִיצָה הָוֵי בֵּינְךָ לָאַף. ז״ע ח״ס ט״ן — וְאֵין אַתָּה מִזְדַּעְזֵעַ מִן הַשָּׂטָן. י״ם כ״ל — אָמַר [שַׂר שֶׁל] גֵּיהִנָּם לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, לַיָּם כֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** with regard to **the attribute of the righteous** it is taught differently. ***Alef tav*, *beit shin*: If you have shame [*ata bosh*], *gimmel reish*, *dalet kuf*: If you do so,** you will **reside [*gur*] in** the **heavens [*bedok*],** as the verse says: "Who stretches out the Heavens like a curtain [*dok*]" (Isaiah 40:22). ***Heh tzadi*, *vav peh*: There is a partition [*ḥatzitza havei*] between you and anger [*af*]. *Zayin ayin*, *ḥet samekh*, *tet nun*: And you will not be shaken [*mizdaze'a*] by the Satan. *Yod mem*, *kaf lamed*: The minister of Gehenna said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe,** send the righteous as well into the **sea** to which **all** go **[*yam kol*],** Gehenna.

But the same *atbash* can be read for the *tzaddikim* as well, since they too appear in the *alef-beis drasha*. *Oti te'ev* — if you despise Me, God; *bi lo chashak* — you have no desire for Me; will My Name rest upon you? *Gufo tamei* — if a person has made himself impure; *aracham alav?* — shall I have compassion on him? *Daltosai na'al* — he has locked My doors, he has not entered My house. *Karnav lo agade'a* — should I not cut off his horns? *Ad kan midat tzaddikim*.

### Gehenom Dialogue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אח״ס בט״ע גי״ף — אֲנִי חָס עֲלֵיהֶם מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבָּעֲטוּ בְּגִיף: דכ״ץ — דַּכִּים הֵם, כֵּנִים הֵם, צַדִּיקִים הֵם. הל״ק — אֵין לְךָ חֵלֶק בָּהֶן. ומרז״ן ש״ת — אָמַר גֵּיהִנָּם לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מָרִי, זֻנֵינִי מִזַּרְעוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The interpretation of the alphabet continues with other combinations of letters. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: *Alef ḥet samekh*, *bet tet ayin*, *gimmel yod peh*: I have mercy on them [*Ani ḥas aleihem*] because they spurned [*ba'atu*] adultery [*gif*].** The Gemara continues with this combination of the letters: ***Dalet kaf tzadi*: They are pure [*dakkim*], they are honest [*kenim*], they are righteous [*tzaddikim*]. *Heh lamed kuf*: You have no portion [*ḥelek*] with them,** based on the interchange of the letters *ḥet* and *heh*. ***Vav mem reish zayin nun*, *shin tav*:** The minister of **Gehenna said [*amar*],** based on *vav mem reish*, **before Him: Master of the Universe, my Master [*Mari*], sustain me [*zaneini*] with the seed of Seth [*Shet*],** which refers to all humankind, including the Jewish people.

The *atbash* concludes with an image of the sea — *yam kol*, let all enter the deep. This prompts a dramatized exchange: the *sar shel Gehenom*, the angel of *Gehinom*, addresses God: *Ribbono shel Olam*, let all come to me, let all souls descend into the sea of fire. God responds with compassion: these are *tzaddikim*, they are not your portion.

### Seven-Letter Code

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: א״ל ב״ם ג״ן ד״ס — לְהֵיכָן אוֹלִיכֵן, לְגַן הֲדַס. ה״ע ו״ף — אָמַר גֵּיהִנָּם לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, עָיֵף אָנֹכִי. ז״ץ ח״ק — הַלָּלוּ זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל יִצְחָק. ט״ר י״ש כ״ת — טֹר יֵשׁ לִי כִּיתּוֹת כִּיתּוֹת שֶׁל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם שֶׁאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Holy One, Blessed be He, **said to him** using another configuration of the alphabet: ***Alef lamed*, *beit mem*: Not with them [*al bam*],** i.e., you will have no portion of them. ***Gimmel nun*, *dalet samekh*: To where will I lead them?** I will lead them **to the garden of myrtle [*gan hadas*],** i.e., the Garden of Eden. ***Heh ayin*, *vav peh*:** The minister of **Gehenna said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, I am tired [*ayef anokhi*]** and thirsty and need people to care for me. The Holy One, Blessed be He, responded: ***Zayin tzadi*, *ḥet kuf*: These are the descendants [*zaro*] of Isaac [*Yitzḥak*]. *Tet reish*, *yod shin*, *kaf tav*: Wait [*tar*], I have groups** upon **groups [*yesh li kittot kittot*] of** other **nations that I will give you** instead.

A second letter-substitution system is now introduced, operating not by folding the alphabet in half but by cycling through it in groups of seven. The *gemara* works through the system of *achas ba-tagif* — taking letters seven positions apart and pairing them. God responds to *Gehinom*'s demand: *alef* — *ani chas aleihem*, I will have compassion upon them; *bet-tet* — *bo'etu*, they may have kicked away, they may have stumbled; but *dalet-kaf-tzadi* — *dachinim hem, tzaddikim hem*, they are lowly, they are righteous. And therefore *hey-lamed-kuf* — *ein lecha chelek bahem*, you have no portion among them. *Gehinom* receives nothing of the *tzaddikim*.

*Gehinom* then speaks again: *Ribbono shel Olam, Mari, mar zanini* — feed me, I am hungry, I need sustenance, give me from *mizaro shel Shet*, from the seed of *Shet* — meaning the Jewish people.

### Al-Bam Code

God responds to *Gehinom*: *le-ikhan olichem* — where shall I bring them? *le-gan hadas* — to the garden of myrtle, meaning *Gan Eden*. They are not for you. *Gehinom* protests: *ayef anochi*, I am weary, I need sustenance. God replies with the letters that follow: *halalu zaro shel Yitzchak*, these are the seed of *Yitzchak* — you do not receive them. And at the end: *yesh li kitot kitot*, I have many, many groups — *she-ovdei kochavim she-ani noten lach*, the nations who do not keep the Torah, those I will give you. You will not go hungry.

The sequence of *atbash*, seven-letter cycling, and *al-bam* thus traces a complete theological arc: the consequences for the *rasha* who despises God, the protection of the *tzaddik* who trembles before sin, and God's ultimate defense of *Yisrael* against the claims of *Gehinom*.

## Mystical Implications

### Musar & Kabbalah

What is the point of all this? On one level, it is an extraordinarily rich vehicle for teaching *mussar*. The letter-games provide a memorable, emotionally resonant way to communicate fundamental moral truths: give generously, pursue the poor to help them, guard your speech, cultivate humility, know that falsehood is unstable and truth endures, understand that God never closes the door on *teshuvah* completely. These are not trivial messages, and the form in which they are delivered makes them unforgettable.

But one must also wonder whether something more is being asserted. The very fact that the Hebrew *alef-beis*, approached from multiple angles and through multiple substitution systems, consistently yields coherent and theologically meaningful content — this itself seems to be making a claim. It reinforces the idea that the Hebrew alphabet possesses an inherent, mysterious organization, that the order of its letters is not arbitrary but reflects something deep about the structure of reality. This would connect back to what the Maharsha suggested about the backwards reading of the *luchos*: the *luchos* could be read both ways not because that was incidental to the miracle of the carving, but because every configuration of those letters carried embedded meaning. And this in turn has profound implications for the world of *kabbalah* and Jewish mystical tradition, where the combinatorial power of the Hebrew letters is not merely a homiletical device but a description of the structure of creation itself.

### Yehoshua ben Nun

The *gemara* introduced these *drashos* by saying they had not been uttered since the days of *Yehoshua bin Nun*. Why specifically *Yehoshua bin Nun*? The Maharsha suggests that because these *drashos* incorporate the *mem sofit* and *nun sofit* and their open counterparts — and because we established earlier that the proper distinction between middle and final forms was at one point forgotten and had to be restored by the prophets — the last period in which these *drashos* could have been articulated in their full integrity was before the forgetting occurred, which would place it in the era of *Yehoshua bin Nun*. It is also worth noting that the name *Nun* appears in the *gemara* written in its full spelling — *nun-vav-nun* — since the letter *nun* is itself one of the letters at the center of these discussions, and there may be an additional layer of wordplay in the choice of *Yehoshua bin Nun* specifically, whose very name contains the letter whose bent and straight forms carry such weighty meaning.

## Ketivah Mishnah

### Two Letter Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכּוֹתֵב שְׁתֵּי אוֹתִיּוֹת בְּהֶעְלֵם אֶחָד — חַיָּיב. כָּתַב בִּדְיוֹ בְּסַם בְּסִיקְרָא בְּקוֹמוֹס וּבְקַנְקַנְתּוֹם וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא רוֹשֵׁם. עַל שְׁנֵי כּוֹתְלֵי זָוִיּוֹת וְעַל שְׁנֵי לוּחֵי פִינְקָס וְהֵן נֶהֱגִין זֶה עִם זֶה — חַיָּיב. הַכּוֹתֵב עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ — חַיָּיב. הַמְסָרֵט עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְחַיֵּיב חַטָּאת וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who writes two letters** on Shabbat **during one lapse of awareness is liable.** The following substances used as ink are explained in the Gemara. One is liable if **he wrote with *deyo*, with *sam*, with *sikra*, with gum [*komos*], or with copper sulfate [*kankantom*] or with any substance that makes a mark.** If one wrote **on two walls** of a house that form **a corner, or on two parts of a writing tablet, and** the two items **are read together, he is liable. One who writes on his flesh** on Shabbat **is liable.** If **one** unwittingly **scratches** letters **on his flesh** on Shabbat, **Rabbi Eliezer deems** him **liable** to bring **a sin-offering and the Sages deem** him **exempt.**

The *gemara* now turns the page, and we arrive at the *mishna* governing the *melacha* of *ketivah*. *Ha-kosev shtei osiyos be-helem echad chayav* — one who writes two letters in a single lapse of awareness is *chayav*. The *mishna* then lists the qualifying materials: *kotev be-dyo* — with standard ink — *bi-sam, be-sikra, be-kumos, be-kankanatum* — various other permanent pigments and marking substances — *u-ve-khol davar she-hu roshem*, and with anything that makes a meaningful, permanent mark.

### Location Requirement

The *mishna* extends this to unusual surfaces and configurations. Writing on two walls that meet at a corner, or on two sections of a ledger, can still constitute *ketivah* — provided the letters are readable together, *ve-hen nehagin ze im ze*. If one letter appears on one wall and another letter appears around the corner on the adjacent wall, and they can be read continuously as one word, one is *chayav*. Similarly for two sections of a ledger read continuously. But if one letter is on one wall and the other on an opposite wall, or on two non-adjacent ledger sections, such that the letters cannot be read as a single continuous word, one is *patur*.

### Skin & Scratching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתַב בְּמַשְׁקִין בְּמֵי פֵּירוֹת, בַּאֲבַק דְּרָכִים בַּאֲבַק הַסּוֹפְרִים וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים — פָּטוּר. לְאַחַר יָדוֹ בְּרַגְלוֹ בְּפִיו וּבְמַרְפְּקוֹ, כָּתַב אוֹת אַחַת סָמוּךְ לַכְּתָב, וּכְתָב עַל גַּבֵּי כְּתָב, נִתְכַּוֵּין לִכְתּוֹב חֵי״ת וְכָתַב שְׁתֵּי זַיְינִין, אַחַת בָּאָרֶץ וְאַחַת בַּקּוֹרָה, כָּתַב עַל שְׁנֵי כּוֹתְלֵי הַבַּיִת, עַל שְׁנֵי דַּפֵּי פִנְקָס וְאֵין נֶהֱגִין זֶה עִם זֶה — פָּטוּר. כָּתַב אוֹת אַחַת נוֹטָרִיקוֹן — רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן בְּתִירָא מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** If **one wrote with liquids** or **with fruit juice,** or if one drew letters **with road dust, with scribes' dust** that they use to dry the ink, **or with any substance** with **which** the writing **does not endure, he is exempt.** Similarly, if one wrote by holding the pen on **the back of his hand, with his foot, with his mouth,** or **with his elbow;** if **one wrote** only **a single letter,** even if it was **adjacent to** other preexisting **writing;** or if **one wrote over** other **writing;** if **one meant to write** the letter ***ḥet*** and instead **wrote** the two halves of the *ḥet* as **two** instances of the letter ***zayin*;** if one wrote **one** letter **on the ground and one on a rafter;** if **one wrote** one letter **on two walls of a house,** or **on two parts of a writing tablet that are not read together, he is exempt.** If **one wrote one letter** as **an abbreviation** representing an entire word, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Beteira deems** him **liable** to bring a sin-offering, **and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.**

*Ha-kotev al besaro chayav* — one who writes on one's own flesh is liable. The question of permanence arises here: ink on skin is not indefinitely permanent, but it lasts long enough not to be dismissed as *eino mitkayem*. Permanence, as the *mishna* makes clear, does not mean irreversibility — something written in pencil is not automatically exempt merely because it can be erased. *Eino mitkayem* refers to writing that has no natural staying power whatsoever, not to writing that could in principle be reversed.

*Ha-mesaret al besaro* — one who scratches into one's flesh — is subject to a dispute. Rabbi Eliezer holds one is *chayav*; the *Chachamim* hold one is *patur*. The basis for the exemption may be either that the scratches fade too quickly (a permanence argument) or that scratching simply is not the *derech* of writing and constitutes a *shinuy*.

### Non-Permanent

*Kotev be-mashkin, be-mei peirot* — if one writes with liquids or fruit juice — *be-avak derakhim* — in road dust — *be-avak ha-sofrim* — in scribal dust — *u-ve-khol davar she-eino mitkayem, patur*. Here the *mishna* is explicit: anything that does not last is exempt. *Eino mitkayem* in this context means genuinely impermanent — materials that naturally fade, dissipate, or lose their form without any intervention.

### Shinuy Methods

*Le-akhar yado* — if one wrote with the back of the hand — *be-raglo* — with the foot — *be-fiv* — holding the instrument in the mouth — *u-ve-marpeko* — with the elbow or underarm — these are all forms of *shinuy*, abnormal methods of writing, analogous to carrying in an unusual manner on *Shabbos*. One is *patur* in all such cases.

### Single Letter Add

*Katav ot achat samukh li-khtav* — if one wrote a single letter next to an already-written word, completing it, such as adding a *mem* to the letters *shin-nun-yud* to create *shnayim* — one is *patur*. Although a complete meaningful word now exists, only one letter was written, which is below the minimum.

### Re-Inking

*Ve-katav al gabei ktav* — if one re-inked letters that were already written and readable — one is *patur*, since no new writing was produced. *Niskavein likhtov chet ve-khasav shenei zayinin* — if one intended to write a *chet* but instead produced two *zayinin* (since a *chet* in a *sefer torah* is written somewhat like two *zayinin* connected by a bridge, and one forgot the bridge) — one is *patur*, since the result does not correspond to the intended letter.

### Single Letter Acronym

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ דְּיוֹ — דְּיוֹתָא. סַם — סַמָּא. סִקְרָא — אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: סְקַרְתָּא שְׁמָהּ. קוֹמוֹס — קוֹמָא. קַנְקַנְתּוֹם — אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חַרְתָּא דְאוּשְׁכָּפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara defines the terms used in the mishna. ***Deyo*** is ***deyota*** made from soot. ***Sam*** is ***samma*,** which is yellow-tinged arsenic. ***Sikra*, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said: It is called *sikreta*** in Aramaic and is a lead-based red paint. ***Komos*** is ***koma*** in Aramaic, and it is an ink made with gum Arabic from the sap of a tree. ***Kankantom*, Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Shmuel said:** This is **the black** substance **used by cobblers,** copper sulfate.

*Kosev ot achat nutrikon* — what if one writes a single letter intended as an acronym representing an entire word, a common form of shorthand? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Beteira holds one is *chayav*: the single letter constitutes a meaningful inscription representing a complete word, and its significance exceeds even writing one letter that completes an already existing word. The *Chachamim* disagree and hold one is *patur*: there is an absolute minimum of two letters, regardless of how meaningful a single letter might be.

This debate connects back to the broader question raised by the opinion that defines the *melacha* as *roshem* — making a meaningful mark — rather than strictly as *ketivah*. Under a *roshem*-based framework, a single letter that meaningfully represents a complete word might well suffice for *chiyuv*. Under a strict *ketivah* framework, the formal requirement of two letters cannot be overridden by intent or significance.

## Ketivah Materials

### Ink Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא רוֹשֵׁם. לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא: כְּתָבוֹ בְּמֵי טַרְיָא וְאַפְצָא — כָּשֵׁר. תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כְּתָבוֹ בַּאֲבָר בִּשְׁחוֹר וּבְשִׁיחוֹר — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** we learned in the mishna that one who writes **with any substance that makes a mark** is liable. The Gemara asks: **What** does this statement come **to include?** The Gemara answers: It comes **to include that which Rabbi Ḥananya taught** with regard to writing a bill of divorce: If **one wrote it with the juice of** the fruit called ***teriya*, or** with **gallnut juice** instead of ink, **it is valid.** Similarly, **Rabbi Ḥiyya taught:** If **one wrote** a bill of divorce **with lead, with soot** (*ge'onim*), **or with shoeblack, it is valid.** Since those substances leave a permanent mark, one who writes with them on Shabbat is liable.

The *gemara* now examines the specific materials listed in the *mishna*. *Dyo* is identified as *diyuta*, standard ink. *Sam* — Steinsaltz identifies this as a yellow pigment of some kind. *Sikra* — Rabbah bar bar Chanah explains this as *sikrasa*, a red pigment or marking substance. *Komus* — some form of gum-based pigment. *Kankanatum* — Rabbah bar bar Chanah in the name of Shmuel identifies this as *chartah de-ushkafi*, the black used by shoemakers.

### Get Validity Test

*U-ve-khol davar she-hu roshem l-atuyei mai* — what does the phrase "anything that makes a mark" come to include? The *gemara* answers from a *braita* of Rabbi Chanina concerning the writing of a *get*: if the *get* was written with *mei Tarya* — which Rashi explains is some type of substance that creates a genuinely permanent stain (perhaps a dark fruit juice) — and with *aftza*, another type of black pigment, the *get* is *kasher*. Since a *get* requires permanent ink, if a substance qualifies for *get*, it is permanent, and writing with it on *Shabbos* renders one *chayav*. A *braita* of Rabbi Chiyya similarly lists *be-shchor, be-shichor* — various black pigments — as qualifying materials.

### Scratching Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְסָרֵט עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לַחֲכָמִים: וַהֲלֹא בֶּן סָטָדָא הוֹצִיא כְּשָׁפִים מִמִּצְרַיִם בִּסְרִיטָה שֶׁעַל בְּשָׂרוֹ? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: שׁוֹטֶה הָיָה, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין רְאָיָה מִן הַשּׁוֹטִים.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: If **one** unwittingly **scratches** letters **on his flesh** on Shabbat, Rabbi Eliezer deems him liable to bring a sin-offering and the Sages deem him exempt. **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer said to the Rabbis: Didn't** the infamous **ben Stada take** magic **spells out of Egypt in a scratch on his flesh? They said to him: He was a fool, and you cannot cite proof from a fool.** That is not the way that most people write. Incidentally, the Gemara asks: Why did they call him **ben Stada,** when **he was the son of Pandeira? Rav Ḥisda said:** His mother's **husband,** who acted as his father, was named **Stada,** but the **one who had relations** with his mother and fathered him was named **Pandeira.** The Gemara asks: Wasn't his mother's **husband Pappos ben Yehuda? Rather, his mother** was named **Stada** and he was named ben Stada after her. The Gemara asks: But wasn't **his mother Miriam, who braided women's hair?** The Gemara explains: That is not a contradiction. **Rather,** Stada was merely a nickname, **as they say in Pumbedita: This** one **strayed [*setat da*] from her husband.**

The *gemara* returns to the dispute about *ha-mesaret al besaro*. A *braita* records Rabbi Eliezer's argument to the *Chachamim*: how can you say that scratching into one's flesh is not a normal form of writing?

### Ben Stada Story

Rabbi Eliezer marshals a proof: did not *Ben Stada* smuggle witchcraft out of Egypt by scratching it into his flesh? If scratching were not a recognized form of writing, Ben Stada's method would have been ineffective and pointless. The *Chachamim* respond: *shoteh hayah* — he was a fool, driven by obsession. Rashi clarifies: this does not mean he lacked intelligence but that he was so consumed by his desire to obtain these *kishufim* that he was willing to resort to any method, however abnormal. *Ein mevi'in ra'ayah min ha-shotim* — one does not bring proof from a person acting irrationally. The very fact that Ben Stada resorted to scratching indicates it was *not* a normal method of writing but an extreme measure taken under extraordinary compulsion.

## Ben Stada Identity

### Genealogy

The *gemara* pauses to examine the identity of *Ben Stada* himself. A question arises: he is called *Ben Stada*, but elsewhere he is known as *Ben Pandira*. How can he have two patronymics? Rav Chisda resolves: *ba'al* — his mother's husband, the man who raised him — was *Pappos ben Yehuda*. But his biological father — the man his mother had actually been with — was *Pandira*. Hence two names: one for the household father, one for the biological father.

But this raises a further question: if the household father was *Pappos ben Yehuda*, why is he called *Ben Stada* rather than *Ben Pappos*? The *gemara* answers: *imo Stada* — his mother's name was *Stada*, and it is from his mother that he takes the name *Ben Stada*.

### Mother Identity

*Imo Miryam megadla sa'ar nashaya hava* — his mother was a *Miryam* who braided women's hair. And what does *Stada* mean as a name? *Kid'amri be-Pumbedita: stat da mi-ba'alah* — as they say in *Pumbedita*, she "sat away from" — that is, she strayed from — her husband. The name *Stada* thus encodes the fact of her adultery, identifying her by the act that made her notorious.

### Jesus Theory

This passage has attracted intense scholarly attention because many — both Jewish and non-Jewish scholars — have read it as a veiled reference to Jesus. The elements are suggestive: a *Miryam* (Mary) who is identified as *megadla sa'ar nashaya* (which some have read as echoing "Mary Magdalene," though that would confuse two distinct figures, since Mary Magdalene was a disciple of Jesus, not his mother); an accusation that the mother committed adultery rather than conceiving miraculously; a biological father named *Pandira*, which some scholars beginning about a hundred and twenty years ago proposed is a wordplay on the Greek *parthenos* (παρθένος), meaning virgin — the claim being that the *gemara* is mocking the doctrine of the virgin birth by etymologizing "virgin" into a proper name for the alleged biological father.

Against this identification, however, *Tosafot* cites *Rabbeinu Tam*: *Ben Stada de-amrinan hacha hu de-hava bi-mei Pappos ben Yehuda de-hava bi-mei Rabbi Akiva* — Ben Stada, as the *gemara* here presents him, lived in the time of *Pappos ben Yehuda*, who was a contemporary of Rabbi Akiva. The chronology is entirely wrong for Jesus. One may argue that *Chazal* could have conflated figures from different periods and that exact chronology was not always preserved with precision. But the discrepancy is significant. Additionally, the story says *Ben Stada hotzi kishufim mi-Mitzrayim bi-serita* — he smuggled witchcraft out of Egypt by scratching it into his flesh. If this is about Jesus, the trip to Egypt is at least plausible from the perspective of those who wished to explain away the miracle stories — but the *Rabbeinu Tam* era identification with *Rabbi Akiva*'s time makes that identification deeply strained.

Other scholars have argued the identification is not merely mistaken but was itself introduced polemically by those who wished to read anti-Christian mockery into the *gemara*, and that this passage has nothing to do with Jesus at all. The debate among scholars remains unresolved.

### Censorship

What is not in dispute is the history of censorship. Later printed editions of the *Talmud*, under pressure from ecclesiastical censors in Christian Europe, removed this passage entirely. The Steinsaltz edition restores it. Uncensored manuscripts of the *Talmud* that have been examined by scholars are more explicit still: in those versions, where the printed *gemara* leaves the identity ambiguous, the manuscripts name the figure directly as *Yeshu*, Jesus. Whether the identification is historically accurate, the censors clearly understood what they believed was being said, and removed it accordingly.
