# Nedarim 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Vows & Torah

### Neder vs. Shvuah

Masechet Nedarim concerns the laws of vows, and it is worth pausing at the outset to clarify what a *neder* is and how it differs from a *shvuah* before examining how the tractate understands these concepts.

In the Torah, there are two principal modes by which a person undertakes a binding verbal commitment: the *shvuah* and the *neder*. A *shvuah* is an oath. It involves calling upon God to witness a statement about something one has done or will do — whether a monetary claim, a past action, or a future undertaking. "I will give you a hundred dollars," "I will not eat bread tomorrow" — these are the kinds of statements that constitute a *shvuah*. Central to the concept of *shvuah* is the invocation of God's name: it is, at its core, a declaration made with God as witness, and swearing falsely in that context constitutes *lo tissa shem Hashem lashav* or *v'lo tishavu bishmi lashaker*.

A *neder*, on first reading of the biblical text, does not appear to be categorically distinct from a *shvuah*. Yaakov Avinu's famous vow — *vayidar Yaakov neder lemor im yihiyeh elohim imadi ushmarani baderech hazeh... v'chol asher titten li asser a'assrenu lach* — sounds very much like an oath about future conduct: if God does this for me, I will do such-and-such. The distinguishing feature might be the absence of direct invocation of God's name as a witness, since a *shvuah* classically entails calling upon God in precisely that way. But the difference is not sharply delineated in the *pesukim* themselves.

### Torah Examples

Several biblical contexts employ the word *neder*. In addition to Yaakov's vow, there is the vow of Israel before the battle with Canaan: *vayidar Yisrael neder lemor im nason titten es ha'am hazeh b'yadi v'hacharamti es areihem* — if You deliver this nation into my hand, I will dedicate their cities to God. That language of dedication, of giving something over to God, seems to be a common thread running through many Torah uses of *neder*. Similarly, the category of *korban* includes the possibility of a *neder* or a *nedavah*: *im neder o nedava zevach korbano* — the *neder* in that context is an obligation upon oneself to bring an offering.

Then there is Parshat Matos, which becomes the paradigmatic locus for this entire tractate. The verse reads: *ish ki yidor neder l'Hashem o hishava shvuah le'esor issar al nafsho lo yachel devaro* — a man who takes a *neder* or a *shvuah* to make something forbidden upon himself, he must not violate his word. Here the *neder* is explicitly about making something forbidden, and although the verse connects it to a *shvuah* as well, it is this formulation — the *neder* as an act that renders an object prohibited — that Chazal take as the defining model for the entire tractate.

### Chazal's Paradigm

What Chazal identify as the defining character of a *neder* is a focus on the *object* rather than on the *person*. A *shvuah* is person-oriented: "I swear that I will not eat this bread" — the person undertakes an obligation, and the object remains unchanged. A *neder*, by contrast, changes the status of the object itself: this bread becomes a forbidden object, much like other objects whose status is transformed by a verbal act. The object is the locus of the prohibition.

This understanding requires some reframing of the more prominent biblical examples. The cases of Yaakov and of Israel, where a *neder* sounds like a personal undertaking contingent on divine assistance, are reconceptualized by Chazal as *nidrei mitzvah* — vows to perform a *mitzvah* — a subcategory distinct from the standard *neder* discussed in this tractate. Similarly, the *neder* of a *korban*, while an obligation the person takes upon themselves, involves the verbal transformation of an object's status into the realm of the sacred. It is that element — the speech act that changes the object — which Chazal identify as the essential feature of a *neder*.

The question naturally arises: is there a unifying conceptual thread connecting all these cases? Even where the *neder* begins with a personal obligation, there is always an object whose status is being transformed. When Yaakov says he will give a *ma'aser*, he is verbally designating an object for God. When Israel vows to dedicate the cities as *cherem*, those objects are being given over through a declaration. When a *korban* is designated, the animal's status changes through words. The common denominator, then, is the power of verbal declaration to transform the identity and status of an object — *motzi b'peh* as the engine of change.

The contrast with a *shvuah* sharpens this point. In a *shvuah*, God's name is the key: you invoke God as witness to your action or commitment. In a *neder*, the key is not God's name but rather the object and your power — through your words — to alter its status. A *neder* draws upon a recognized domain in which human speech has transformative force.

### Korban Model

This leads to the concept of *ein nodrin ela bedavar hanadur* — one may only make a *neder* using the model of a *davar hanadur*, something whose forbidden status was itself produced by a human verbal act. If one says "this object should be like *chazir*," one has accomplished nothing, because the forbidden status of *chazir* derives from God's decree and its own nature, not from any human verbal transformation. One cannot, through speech, make something into *chazir*, because that domain is entirely outside the scope of human declarative power.

By contrast, if one says "this object should be like a *korban*," one is invoking a domain where human speech unambiguously transforms object-status. Just as one can make an animal a *korban*, or designate produce as *ma'aser*, or declare something *cherem*, one can render an object forbidden by likening it to these categories. The *korban* serves as the paradigmatic *davar hanadur* — the exemplary case of an object made forbidden through human declaration — and it is from this model that the standard vocabulary of *nedarim* is derived.

Importantly, a statement such as "this food is like *basar v'chalav*" or "like *chametz b'Pesach*" or "like *chelev*" would similarly accomplish nothing as a *neder*, even though those things are forbidden. The reason is the same: those prohibitions are divinely ordained categories, not the product of human verbal transformation. The valid model is exclusively the *davar hanadur* — something whose prohibited status flows from a human speech act of the same type.

### Konam

The classic term for this type of *neder* is *konam*, which is a variation — or euphemism — of the word *korban*. When one says *konam davar zeh alay* — "this thing should be like a *korban* to me" — one is drawing on that domain of human verbal transformation of object-status. *Konam* is thus not merely a substitute word; it is a word whose very form signals the conceptual underpinning of the *neder*: this object is to be treated like a sacrificial offering, whose status has been changed by my words.

It is worth noting in passing that even today, *bezman hazeh* when the *Beis HaMikdash* does not stand, one can still make a *neder* using this model. If something is declared like a *korban*, it becomes sanctified — it cannot be used, must be left to graze if it is an animal, and so forth — even without the possibility of actual sacrifice. The mechanism of declaring something like a *korban* remains operative.

### Speech Acts

All of this points to the broader category of *haplaa* — the speech act that transforms status. The Torah itself uses the root in *nazir ki yafli lindor neder nazir*, where *yaflia* means to enunciate or articulate, and in the rabbinic understanding it denotes specifically a speech act with transformative legal force. This is the category under which *nedarim*, *nezirut*, *ma'aser*, *hekdesh*, and *cherem* all fall: verbal declarations that change the ontological status of an object. A *shvuah* does not belong to this category in the same way — it does not change the status of an object but rather commits the person to a course of action or attests to a fact. The tractate before us is, at its heart, a sustained treatment of *haplaa* as applied to the domain of making objects forbidden.

## Masechet Nedarim

### Lashon Yerushalmi

Before beginning the *masechet* itself, it is worth noting several unusual features that set Masechet Nedarim apart from the rest of the Babylonian Talmud. The language of this tractate — its grammar, certain technical terms, and stylistic patterns — differs noticeably from the standard Babylonian Aramaic of the rest of the *Bavli*. This was already observed by Tosafot and the *rishonim* generally; Rashi designates this distinctive idiom in several places as *lashon Yerushalmi*, Palestinian Aramaic.

According to the scholarly literature, five tractates — Nedarim, Nazir, Meilah, Keritot, and Tamid — share a range of linguistic and grammatical peculiarities that distinguish them from the rest of the Babylonian Talmud. Notably, parts of Masechet Temurah exhibit the same phenomenon through what are labeled there as *lishna achrina* passages: one section of the text is in the familiar Babylonian Aramaic, and then an alternate version of the same passage appears written in this different idiom.

### Geonic Approach

Connected to this linguistic distinctiveness is a remarkable testimony concerning Geonic practice. The work *Otzar HaGeonim*, which collects and organizes Geonic responsa by tractate, records very little material on Nedarim. The reason given is telling: the *Geonim* who arose after the sealing of the Talmud and devoted themselves to clarifying its *halachot* — *hiziru atzmam mimasechet Nedarim* — they kept away from Masechet Nedarim. Within their *yeshivot* they formally announced: *de'anan la gersinan Nedarim* — "we do not study Nedarim." And further: *velo yediana le'asur ulehateir bah lo neder velo shevuah* — "we do not know how to prohibit or permit in this domain, neither by *neder* nor by *shevuah*." Consequently, they did not engage in *hataras nedarim* either on Rosh Hashanah or on Yom Kippur.

The text notes, however, that *be'Eretz Yisrael hayu nohagin bekach kol hayamim* — in the Land of Israel they maintained the practice throughout. One of the reasons cited for the Babylonian Geonic abstention is instructive: if *nedarim* are widely studied and discussed, people become aware of them as a readily available religious instrument and begin making them, sometimes inadvertently or inappropriately. Keeping the tractate out of the religious consciousness reduces the incidence of *nedarim*. This is analogous to the concern one might have about teaching certain sections of Masechet Kiddushin in a context where students might experiment with what they learn.

This warrants reflection. We tend not to appreciate how central the practice of making *nedarim* was to the religious lives of earlier generations. Vows were a standard mechanism for bringing God into one's daily world, for giving expression to one's religious impulses, for adding personal *chumrot* or taking on additional commitments. The *Gemara* elsewhere describes making a *neder* as analogous to building a *bamah* — with all the ambivalence that image carries. It can be an expression of genuine religious seriousness, but it also risks introducing a personal religiosity that operates outside communal norms, and the transgression risks involved in making and violating *nedarim* are considerable. The Geonic decision to deliberately abstain reflects a pastoral judgment about the balance of those risks.

### Pumbedita Theory

The question of the tractate's linguistic distinctiveness connects naturally to questions of provenance. Those who follow Rashi in calling the language *lashon Yerushalmi* have argued that the tractate ultimately reflects Palestinian usage and may be closer in origin to the *Yerushalmi* tradition than to the standard *Bavli*. This view is reinforced, they argue, by the fact that the Babylonian *Geonim* essentially did not engage with the tractate, while it remained part of living practice in Eretz Yisrael throughout the Geonic period.

However, this view is significantly contested in the scholarly literature. The more widely accepted position today is that the standard *Bavli* reflects the Babylonian usage of Sura — the academy where Rav Ashi edited the Talmud — while the distinctive idiom of Nedarim and the related tractates reflects the Babylonian usage of Pumbedita, the other great Babylonian academy, whose Aramaic differed in precisely these ways. On this account, there is no need to posit a Palestinian origin; the difference reflects a difference between the two great Babylonian centers of learning.

The further questions of relative dating — whether the Pumbeditan material is earlier, contemporaneous, or later than the Suran redaction, and whether it was re-worked or left in its original form — remain matters of scholarly debate, with multiple positions advanced. But the emerging consensus appears to favor the Pumbedita theory of origin rather than a Palestinian one.

### Textual Sources

Given the unusual character of the tractate, the question of which *rishonim* to rely on for commentary deserves attention. The commentary printed with the designation "Rashi" in standard editions of Nedarim is, by general scholarly acknowledgment, not actually Rashi's. The attribution is uncertain, and the commentary itself is considerably less rigorous and less precise than authentic Rashi — less consistent, less clear, and less analytically sharp. In many *yeshivot* that study this tractate, the pseudo-Rashi commentary is not treated as a primary learning resource.

The central commentary is instead the *Ran* — Rabbeinu Nissim of Gerona — who lived in the thirteenth century. The *Ran* is among the clearest of the *rishonim*, though not necessarily the most creatively innovative. He synthesizes the positions of earlier *rishonim*, presents the *Gemara*'s flow with admirable clarity, and brings in contradictions, difficulties, and resolutions in a manner that makes him function as something of a *rashi-tosafot* combined for this tractate. He is expansive, as the size of his commentary makes evident, but consistently lucid.

Tosafot and the Rosh are also present in the standard *Vilna* edition, appearing in their usual positions. The Tosafot here tend toward more straightforward commentary rather than the intensive *shakla vetarya* one finds in tractates like Bava Kamma or Gittin. Notably, despite the Geonic abstention, the *Rif* did write *Halachot* on Nedarim — his characteristic extraction of bottom-line *halachic* rulings — and the Ramban likewise composed *Hilchot HaRamban* on Nedarim, printed in the back of the *Gemara* parallel to the Rif. A range of *acharonim* have also written commentaries. The tractate is thus not without *rishonic* resources, even if they differ in character from those found in the more centrally studied *masechtot*.

## Kinuyim

### Kinuyim Rule

The first *mishna* of Masechet Nedarim opens with a broad principle: *kol kinnuyei nedarim kenedarim* — all *kinuyim* of *nedarim* have the status of *nedarim*. A *kinnuy* is a substitute word, a variation or euphemism — a term that is not exactly the standard vocabulary of a *neder* but is used in its place. The *mishna* establishes that such substitute language is legally effective and counts as if the standard term itself were used.

### Other Categories

The same principle is then extended in parallel formulations: *vacharamim kacharamim* — variations of *cherem* language carry the force of an actual *cherem*; and *ushvuot keshvuot* — variations of *shvuah* language are binding as a *shvuah*. The tractate also includes *nezirut kenezirut*: language that approximates or echoes the terminology of *nezirut* is binding as if one had formally declared oneself a *nazir*.

The *Ran* notes, in the context of *cherem*, that the tractate's treatment focuses on *cherem* understood primarily as a form of *neder* — that is, as a declaration making an object forbidden to oneself — rather than as the full category of dedicating objects to God or to the *kohanim*, which would belong more properly in the realm of *kodshim*.

### Konam Variations

The *mishna* does not immediately provide examples of these *kinuyim*; the examples appear only several *mishnayot* later, on daf 10. There the *mishna* states: *ha'omer konam konach konas harei elu kinnuyim l'korban* — one who says *konam*, *konach*, or *konas* has used a *kinnuy* for *korban*. One who says *cherek*, *cherech*, or *cheref* has used a *kinnuy* for *cherem*. One who says *nazik*, *naziach*, or *paziach* has used a *kinnuy* for *nezirut*. And terms like *shvuta* or *shkuka* are *kinuyim* for *shevua*. These words are recognizable echoes of their standard counterparts, with key letters altered or substituted.

### Purpose of Kinuyim

The *Gemara* addresses the origin and purpose of these invented terms. One possibility is that they derive from some other language — Aramaic or another Semitic tongue — that happens to sound similar to the Hebrew with slight variations. But the more straightforward explanation, and the one the *Gemara* endorses, is *lashon shebadu lahem chachamim* — it is a language the rabbis invented, a kind of technical terminology deliberately constructed to serve a purpose.

That purpose, as the *Gemara* explains, is to prevent the inadvertent utterance of *shem Hashem levatala*. If one uses the standard terms — *neder l'Hashem*, *korban l'Hashem*, *shvua l'Hashem* — one naturally appends God's name. By training people to use these unusual technical terms instead, the rabbis reduced the chance of God's name being used vainly. One might add that the strange and specialized nature of these terms also serves to make the speech act more conscious and deliberate — a person using *konam* knows they are engaging in a formal legal act, and the unfamiliarity of the word may introduce a moment of hesitation that the ordinary vocabulary would not.

Whatever the precise rationale, the *mishna* establishes that using these substitute terms is fully effective: *kol kinnuyei nedarim kenedarim*.

## Yadot

### Modrani Mimcha

The *mishna* then turns to a second concept, distinct from *kinuyim*: the *yad*. The *mishna* states: *ha'omer l'chavero modrani mimcha* — one who says to his fellow "I am vowed from you," *mufrashani mimcha* — "I am separated from you," or *meruchakani mimcha* — "I am distanced from you" — with respect to eating or tasting of his fellow's food, is *assur*, forbidden, from doing so. These formulations, though not standard *neder* vocabulary and not *kinuyim* in the sense of corrupted euphemisms, are nonetheless effective.

### Variations

The terms *mufrashani* and *meruchakani* are essentially translations or paraphrases of the concept of a *neder* — expressing the idea of separation or distance from the other person or their property. One might initially think these are additional *kinuyim*. The *Gemara*, however, distinguishes them: *kinuyim* are corrupted or variant phonetic forms of the standard words, while *modrani*, *mufrashani*, and *meruchakani* are ordinary, grammatically correct Hebrew expressions. The *Gemara* understands them instead as examples of *yadot* — abbreviated or elliptical statements.

There is also a textual question about whether the *mishna*'s language should be read as *she'ani ochel lach* — "those things that I eat of yours" — or *she'eini ochel lach* — "that I will not eat of yours." The distinction matters because a *neder* is object-focused: what becomes forbidden is the object, the food. If the formulation is "I will not eat," that sounds more like a *shvuah*, which is person-focused. The *Gemara* will engage with this question, and the precise text will bear on how we understand these abbreviated formulations.

### Menudeh Ani Lach

The *mishna* further records a case involving the phrase *menudeh ani lach* — "I am *menudeh* to you" — which borrows the language of *niduy*, Rabbinic excommunication. This usage is problematic as a *neder* formulation because it does not clearly invoke the model of a *davar hanadur*, nor does it straightforwardly transform the status of an object. Rabbi Akiva, as the *mishna* records, was inclined to rule stringently in such a case — not certain that it qualifies but not prepared to permit it — given the difficulty of fitting this language into the standard model of a *neder*.

### Yad Concept

The *Gemara* explains the concept underlying the cases of *modrani*, *mufrashani*, and *meruchakani*: these are examples of *yadot nedarim*, literally "handles" of *nedarim*. A *yad* is a handle in the sense of an abbreviated or elliptical statement — one that gestures toward a *neder* without articulating all of its required elements. The *Gemara* elsewhere discusses this concept in the context of *nezirut* as well: if someone is walking by and says "I will be" — stopping there — and the context makes clear that he meant "I will be like that *nazir*," the statement may be binding. The degree to which context must supply the missing elements distinguishes between *yadayim mochiachos* — handles that are self-evident from context — and *yadayim she'einam mochiachos* — handles where the intended meaning is not obvious and might be understood otherwise.

### Two Yad Models

The *Ran* explains precisely why *modrani mimcha* and similar formulations qualify as *yadot* rather than complete *nedarim*. He offers two distinct analyses. The first: *mishum delo masik diburei lemeimar kikorban* — the statement is abbreviated because the speaker did not complete it by saying "like a *korban*." On this understanding, the fundamental formulation of a *neder* requires the invocation of the *korban* model — *davar zeh assur kikorban* or *konam davar zeh alay* — and when that element is absent, the statement is only a *yad*, effective because we understand the *korban* reference to be the implicit completion of the sentence.

The second analysis: *i nami delo amar issura beheya* — alternatively, the statement is abbreviated because the speaker did not even say that something is *assur*, forbidden. A *neder* must change the status of an object; it must predicate *issur* of something. "I am separated from you" does not explicitly declare anything forbidden. Even setting aside the *korban* model, the very predication of prohibited status is missing. On this reading, the *yad* is effective because we understand the intent to create a prohibition, even though neither the language of prohibition nor the *korban* model was articulated.

These two analyses correspond to two different questions about what is strictly necessary for a complete *neder*: is explicit reference to the *korban* model required, or is an explicit statement of *issur* required? If one said *davar zeh assur alay beneder* — "this thing is forbidden to me as a *neder*" — without invoking the *korban* language, is that a complete *neder* or itself a *yad*? The two positions in the *Ran* reflect that underlying question.

## Object vs. Person

### Listing Order

The *Gemara* opens with an apparent discrepancy between our *mishna* and the opening *mishna* of Masechet Nazir. Our *mishna* provides a full list: *kol kinnuyei nedarim kenedarim, vacharamim kacharamim, ushvuot keshvuot, unezirut kenezirut* — all four categories. The opening *mishna* of Nazir, by contrast, states only *kol kinnuyei nezirut kenezirut* and lists nothing else. The *Gemara* asks: *mai shna gabei Nazir* — what is different about Nazir that it does not give the full list? And conversely, what is different about Nedarim that it does give the full list?

The *Gemara* answers: *neder* and *shvuah* appear together in the same *pasuk* — *ish ki yidor neder o hishavei shvua* — and therefore when discussing *neder*, one naturally includes *shvuah* as well. Once those two are mentioned together, one might as well fill out the complete list by adding *cherem* and *nezirut*. In Masechet Nazir, where the natural focus is *nezirut* alone, there is no comparable reason to list all the others.

### Heftzah vs. Nafsha

But this answer immediately generates a further question. If *neder* and *shvuah* are paired because they appear together in the *pasuk*, then the *mishna* should have listed them adjacently: *neder*, then *shvuah*, then the others. Instead, the *mishna* lists *neder*, then *cherem*, and only then *shvuah*. Why is *cherem* inserted between *neder* and *shvuah*?

Here the *Gemara* articulates one of the most fundamental conceptual distinctions of the tractate: *neder* and *cherem* both operate by making the *heftz* — the object — forbidden: *demis'ar heftza alei*. A *shvuah*, even when it concerns behavior toward an object, operates differently — *de'kasar nafshia min hacheftza* — it binds the person, not the object. Therefore, *neder* and *cherem* are grouped together as members of the same conceptual category, and *shvuah* is listed separately, after them. The Torah pairing of *neder* and *shvuah* explains why *shvuah* appears at all in this list; the conceptual distinction between *heftz*-focused and *nefesh*-focused prohibitions explains why *cherem* is placed between them. The *Gemara* thus uses this seemingly minor question of listing order to articulate what will prove to be the defining conceptual axis of the entire tractate.

### Kinuyim First

The *Gemara* raises a second structural question about the *mishna*: having established that the *mishna* discusses both *kinuyim* and *yadot*, why does it first mention the rule of *kinuyim* and then proceed directly to give examples of *yadot* — without first offering examples of *kinuyim*?

This is genuinely puzzling. The *mishna* opens with the general principle that *kinuyim* are effective. One would expect it then to illustrate that rule with examples of *kinuyim*, and only afterwards introduce the separate concept of *yadot* with its examples. Instead it introduces the rule of *kinuyim* and jumps immediately to examples of *yadot* — examples that won't be given until several *mishnayot* later.

### Rashi's Solution

Rashi's solution is that the *mishna* is a case of *chasorei mechasra* — the text is incomplete and must be supplemented. Reading the *mishna* as it stands is impossible without inserting a line that was implied but not written. The corrected reading should be: *kol kinnuyei nedarim kenedarim, v'yadot nedarim kenedarim* — all *kinuyim* of *nedarim* are like *nedarim*, and all *yadot* of *nedarim* are like *nedarim*. Once that missing line is understood to be present — at least implicitly — the *mishna* follows the chiastic ABBA structure: it states the rules for A (*kinuyim*) and B (*yadot*), and then provides examples beginning with B (the *yadot* examples, which follow immediately), returning to A (*kinuyim*) only in the later *mishna*. It chose to discuss B first in the examples because it had, in its implicit final clause, most recently mentioned *yadot*.

The irony that the *Gemara* acknowledges is that the entire argument for ABBA structure depends on an inserted line that was not actually there — it is a *yad*, one might say, in the *mishna* itself.

## Chiastic Patterns

### ABBA vs. ABAB

Following Rashi's invocation of the ABBA chiastic structure, the *Gemara* explores this pattern more broadly. It asks whether the rule is that a *mishna* always gives examples beginning with the item most recently mentioned — the ABBA pattern — or whether sometimes it begins with the first item mentioned, producing an ABAB structure. The *Gemara* brings a series of examples from across the *mishnah* to examine both patterns.

### Avot Examples

The three opening chapters of Masechet Shabbat provide the clearest examples of ABBA. *Bameh Madlikin u'vameh ein Madlikin* — with what may one light Shabbat candles and with what may one not? The *mishna* then begins its discussion with *ein madlikin*, the "may not" category — that is, B before A in the examples, having stated A then B in the rule. Similarly, *Bameh Tomnin u'vameh ein Tomnin* — with what may one insulate food and with what not? — followed by examples of what one may not use, again B first. And *Bameh isha yotzah u'vameh einah yotzah* — what may a woman wear in the public domain on Shabbat and what may she not — again followed by examples of what she may not wear, the second-mentioned category.

### Nochlim Example

Against these examples, the *Gemara* brings cases that follow the ABAB pattern. The *mishna* in Bava Batra: *yesh nochlin u'manchilim* — some inherit and bequeath — *nochlin velo manchilim* — some inherit but do not bequeath. The examples then begin with *v'elu nochlin u'manchilim* — those who both inherit and bequeath — the first-mentioned category. That is ABAB. An example of someone who inherits but does not bequeath would be a son relative to his mother: a son inherits from his mother, but the mother does not inherit from the son. Meanwhile a father and son illustrate the first category: a son inherits from his father, and if the son predeceases his father without other heirs, the father inherits from the son.

### Wife Examples

A parallel structure appears in Yevamot: *yesh mutarot l'va'aleihen va'asurot l'yivmeihen* — some wives are permitted to their husbands but forbidden to their brothers-in-law for *yibum* — *mutarot l'yivmeihen va'asurot l'va'aleihen* — and some are the reverse. A widow (*almanah*) married to a *kohen* whose husband's brother is a *kohen gadol* illustrates the first: she was permitted to her husband but the *kohen gadol* cannot perform *yibum*. A widow married to a *kohen gadol* illustrates the second: the marriage binds but she is forbidden to her husband (as a *kohen gadol* may not marry a widow), yet his ordinary-*kohen* brother can perform *yibum*. The *mishna* then begins its examples with the first category — again ABAB.

### Mincha Example

From the laws of *menachot*: *yesh te'unah shemen u'levonah* — some *menachot* require both oil and frankincense — *shemen v'lo levonah* — some require oil but not frankincense. The *mishna* then provides examples of those requiring both — ABAB again.

### Korban Example

Similarly, *yesh te'unah hagashah v'ein te'unah tenufah* — some offerings require *hagashah*, bringing them to the corner of the altar, but not *tenufah*, waving — *tenufah v'lo hagashah* — and some require waving but not *hagashah*. The examples begin with the first category — again ABAB.

### Bechor Example

From the laws of the firstborn: *yesh bechor l'nachala v'ein bechor l'Kohen* — some are considered firstborn for inheritance purposes but not for *pidyon haben* — *bechor l'Kohen v'ein bechor l'nachala* — and some are firstborn for *pidyon haben* but not for inheritance. A boy born after a miscarriage is the first viable son, so he receives the double inheritance portion, but since he did not breach his mother's womb he does not require *pidyon haben*. Conversely, a *kohen* who already has a firstborn son and then has another son by a different wife: that second son is the *peter rechem* of his mother and requires *pidyon haben*, but since he is not the firstborn of his father he does not receive a double portion. The *mishna* begins its examples with the first category — ABAB once more.

### Length-Based Rule

Facing this array of contradictory cases, the *Gemara* proposes a rule: *hanei mishum d'avshei lehu mefaresh hahu depatach berisha* — those that follow ABAB do so because the discussion of each category is long. In a chiastic ABBA structure, by the time you have given the rule for A, then B, and then a lengthy discussion of B examples, the reader will have lost track of what A was. The ABBA structure only works when the discussion is compact enough that the final return to A does not come so late as to be disorienting. When the treatment of B is going to be extensive, the *mishna* must restate A before going on to B, producing ABAB. The cases from Shabbat — the candles, the insulation, the woman's garments — are all relatively brief and thus can sustain the ABBA structure.

### No Fixed Rule

However, the *Gemara* notes a counterexample: the *mishna* regarding what animals may wear on Shabbat (*yotzei gamal* and similar) also follows ABAB despite involving only a brief list. This undermines the proposed rule, since brevity alone does not determine the choice of structure.

The *Gemara* concludes accordingly: *ella lav davka* — there is in fact no fixed rule. *Zimnin de-mafarash de-patach be-reisha* — sometimes the *mishna* gives examples beginning with the first-mentioned category, producing ABAB — *zimnin hahu de-sayeim mafarash be-reisha* — and sometimes it gives examples beginning with the last-mentioned category, producing ABBA. The *mishna* of Nedarim chose to present its examples in ABBA fashion, beginning with *yadot* before *kinuyim* — even though, as noted, the clause about *yadot* was implicit rather than explicit — and both patterns are legitimate. With that, the *Gemara*'s opening structural digression concludes, and the tractate will proceed to substantive analysis of *yadot* and *kinuyim* beginning tomorrow.
