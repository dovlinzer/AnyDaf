# Kiddushin 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mitzvot haben al av

### Overview & context

Daf 29 of Kiddushin marks a significant transition in the sugya. We have been moving through an extended discussion of *kinyanim* — the various modes of acquisition — organized with the systematic, categorical precision that characterizes these early *mishnayot*. Having covered *kol hana'aseh damim be'acher* and *reshut gavoah*, the Mishnah now pivots entirely, bringing the same organizational rigor to bear on a different domain: *mitzvot* and the categories of obligation that define who must perform them.

### Mishnah categories

The Mishnah on 29a opens with a characteristically compressed formulation — the sort of strange, almost elliptical locution one finds in particularly early *mishnayot*. It presents four categories, each defined by the intersection of the type of obligation and the gender of the obligated party.

The first category: *kol mitzvot haben al ha'av, anashim chayavin venashim peturot* — all the *mitzvot* of the child upon the father, men are obligated and women are exempt. The second: *vechol mitzvot ha'av al haben, echad anashim ve'echad nashim chayavin* — all the *mitzvot* of the father upon the child, whether men or women are equally obligated. The third: *vechol mitzvot aseh shehazman grama, anashim chayavin venashim peturot* — all positive *mitzvot* caused by time, men are obligated and women are exempt. The Rambam already notes that this rule has approximately as many exceptions as it has clear instances; whether it is a genuinely descriptive principle derived from the particulars or a prescriptive rule that dictates the particulars is itself a major question. The fourth category: *vechol mitzvot aseh shelo hazman grama, echad anashim ve'echad nashim chayavin* — non-time-bound positive *mitzvot* obligate men and women equally. And finally: *vechol mitzvot lo ta'aseh, bein shehazman grama bein shelo hazman grama, echad anashim ve'echad nashim chayavin* — all negative commandments, whether time-bound like *Shabbat* or not time-bound like the prohibition of *chazir*, obligate men and women equally, with three exceptions: *mibal takif*, *u'val tashchit*, and *u'val titama lametim*, the last of which applies specifically to male *kohanim*.

### Gemara interprets

The Gemara immediately asks: what does *kol mitzvot haben al ha'av* mean? If it means all the *mitzvot* that a child is obligated to perform toward a parent — that is, obligations running from child to parent — then why would women be exempt? That cannot be right, because a *braita* explicitly teaches otherwise. The verse states *ish immo ve'aviv tirau* — each man shall fear his mother and father — and the *Midrash Tannaim* derives: I know only that a man is obligated; how do I know a woman is as well? Because *tirau* is in the plural, implying two — both men and women are equally obligated in fearing and honoring their parents.

Rav Yehuda therefore reframes the Mishnah: *kol mitzvot haben hamutallot alav* — the *mitzvot* of the child that are laid upon the parent, meaning parental obligations directed *toward* children. Those, says the Mishnah, are the obligations of fathers toward sons: *anashim chayavim venashim peturot*, men are obligated and women are exempt.

### Kibbud av va'em

With this clarification in place, the structure becomes clear. *Kibbud av va'em* — the child's obligation toward parents — falls under the second category: *echad anashim ve'echad nashim chayavin*. Both men and women, both boys and girls, adult children of all kinds, are equally obligated to honor both father and mother. The asymmetry in the Mishnah applies solely to the parental side: it is fathers, not mothers, who bear the specific duties toward sons that the Gemara is about to enumerate.

## Braita: paternal duties

### Five obligations

The Gemara now turns to a *braita* that spells out concretely what these paternal obligations consist of. *Ha'av chayav bivno* — the father is obligated with respect to his son — in five specific ways: *lemolo*, to circumcise him; *ulfidoso*, to redeem him (*pidyon haben*); *ulilamedo Torah*, to teach him Torah; *ulehashio ishah*, to arrange his marriage; and *ulilamedo ummanus*, to teach him a trade. Some add a sixth: *af lehashito bamayim*, to teach him how to swim — a basic survival skill, the sort of competency that in earlier eras was genuinely life-saving.

It is worth noting that the *braita* does not mention the general obligation of *chinuch* — training a child to perform *mitzvot*, to recite *brachot*, to shake a *lulav*, to light candles, to observe negative commandments. Yet other sources make clear that *chinuch* is also primarily the father's obligation, though there is some discussion as to whether the mother shares in it as well.

### R. Yehuda on trade

Rabbi Yehuda sharpens the obligation of teaching a trade with a striking formulation: *kol she'eino melamed es beno ummanus melamdo listus* — anyone who does not teach his son a trade is as if he teaches him banditry. The Gemara immediately clarifies: surely no one actually teaches their child banditry. Rather, *ke'ilu melamdo listus* — it is *as if* he did, because by failing to provide his son with a legitimate livelihood, he leaves him with no honest means of sustaining himself. The hyperbole carries a moral charge — apparently the obligation to teach one's son a trade was not being taken seriously enough, and Rabbi Yehuda found it necessary to dramatize the stakes.

## Brit Milah analysis

### Father's obligation

The Gemara now unpacks each obligation in turn. *Lemolo* — where do we know that circumcision is the father's obligation? From the verse *vayamol Avraham es Yitzchak beno* — Abraham circumcised Isaac his son. Abraham's act establishes the paradigm. As for how we know this applies to future generations, the school of Rabbi Ishmael will provide the answer shortly.

### Fallback obligations

The Gemara then establishes a hierarchy of fallback obligations. *Veheicha d'la malye avuha mechayavei bei dina lemimalye* — if the father has not circumcised his son, the *beis din* becomes obligated, because the verse says *himmol lachem kol zachar* — you shall have circumcised for you all males — addressing the community collectively. The community bears responsibility to ensure that all Jewish males are circumcised; if the father fails, that obligation reverts to the public.

If *beis din* likewise fails to act and the child reaches adulthood uncircumcised, *mechayev ihu lemimhal nafshei* — he is now obligated to circumcise himself, derived from *ve'arel zachar asher lo yimmol besar orlaso venichresa* — an uncircumcised male who does not circumcise his foreskin shall be cut off. The very fact that he bears punishment for failure to circumcise demonstrates that he carries a personal obligation to do so.

A notable feature of this hierarchy is that it moves from father to community to self — the mother is not named as an intermediate or backup obligant. This stands in contrast to the story of Tzipporah, who performed the *bris milah* herself. It is important to distinguish between two separate questions: who is capable of executing the act (which the Tzipporah story addresses and which the Gemara in Avodah Zarah discusses), and who bears the halakhic obligation to ensure that the act is performed (which is our question here). On the latter question, the mother appears to be written out of the hierarchy entirely. There is, however, a Rashi elsewhere and a *Mareh Or Zarua* suggesting that the mother does bear an obligation after the father — which raises interesting practical questions, such as whether a single mother performing the *milah* for her son may recite the *bracha* of *lehachniso bivriso shel Avraham Avinu* in her own right as mother, or perhaps only as a representative of the community standing in place of *beis din*.

### Women excluded

How do we know that the mother bears no obligation? *D'ksiv kaasher tziva oso Elohim* — because the verse says as God commanded *him*, Abraham — *oso v'lo osa*: him and not her, not Sarah.

### Tanaic proof

But this is the verse about Abraham and Sarah in their own generation. How do we know it serves as the binding model for future generations? The school of Rabbi Ishmael taught: *kol makom shenemar tzav eino ela zeirus miyad ul'doros* — whenever the Torah uses the root *tzav* (command), it signals both urgency — encouraging alacrity in performance — and applicability to all future generations. This is demonstrated by two verses: *v'tzav es Yehoshua v'chazkeihu v'amtzeihu* (command Joshua, strengthen and encourage him) illustrates the urgency component, and *min hayom asher tziva Hashem v'halah l'doroseichem* (from the day God commanded and onward, for your generations) establishes the multigenerational dimension. Since the verse about Abraham's circumcision of Isaac uses *tziva*, the exclusion of the mother encoded there — *oso v'lo osa* — carries forward to all generations.

## Pidyon HaBen

### Hierarchy of duty

The Gemara turns to *pidyon haben*. The source is *kol bechor banecha tifdeh* — all the firstborn of your sons you shall redeem. As with *bris milah*, a hierarchy applies: the father redeems the son, and if the father does not, *michayiv ihu l'mifreikeih l'nafsheih* — the son must redeem himself as an adult, derived from *padoh tifdeh* — you shall surely redeem, implying a personal obligation if the parental one goes unfulfilled.

One significant difference from the *bris milah* hierarchy is worth noting: unlike circumcision, there is no communal obligation that all firstborn sons be redeemed. *Pidyon haben* is an obligation running between father and son; if the father fails, the obligation passes directly to the son himself, without an intermediate stage of communal responsibility.

### Bidirectional logic

How do we know that a woman is not obligated to redeem her son? The Gemara reads *tifdeh tipadeh* — you shall redeem, you shall be redeemed — as establishing a bidirectional principle: *kol shemitzveh lifdos es atzmo mitzveh lifdos es acherim* — whoever is obligated to redeem themselves is obligated to redeem others; *v'chol she'eino mitzveh lifdos es atzmo eino mitzveh lifdos acherim* — whoever is not obligated to redeem themselves is not obligated to redeem others. Since a woman is not subject to redemption herself, she is therefore not obligated to redeem her son.

### Logical deduction

But this raises a prior question: how do we know a woman is not obligated to redeem herself? The Gemara applies the same principle in the other direction: *kol she'acherim mtzuvim lifdoso mitzveh lifdos es atzmo* — if others are obligated to redeem you, you are obligated to redeem yourself. Conversely, *v'chol she'ein acherim mtzuvim lifdoso eino mitzveh lifdos es atzmo* — if no one else is obligated to redeem you, you are not obligated to redeem yourself. The child's obligation to self-redeem exists precisely because the parent's obligation went unfulfilled; where no parental obligation existed in the first place, no self-redemption obligation can arise.

### Daughters excluded

This brings us to the foundation of the entire chain: how do we know that a daughter's parents are not obligated to redeem her? From the verse itself — *kol bechor banecha tifdeh* — *banecha v'lo bnosecha*: your sons and not your daughters. Daughters are not subjects of *pidyon haben*; therefore no one is obligated to redeem them; therefore they are not obligated to redeem themselves; and therefore they are not obligated to redeem others, including their own sons.

The logic works neatly from the end backward. *Banecha v'lo bnosecha* establishes that daughters are not redeemed. *Tifdeh tipadeh* then establishes, in one direction, that those not obligated to be redeemed are not obligated to redeem themselves, and in the other direction, that those not obligated to redeem themselves are not obligated to redeem others. The entire structure of exclusion rests on the initial textual exclusion of daughters from the category of those who must be redeemed.

## Self vs. son debate

### Self first

The Gemara now presents a new *braita*: *hu lifdot u'vno lifdot, hu kodem livno* — if a man must redeem himself (because his father did not do so) and must also redeem his son (as a father), and he lacks the resources to do both, he redeems himself first. This position rests on the principle that one's own personal obligation takes precedence: *mitzva degufei adifa* — an obligation relating to one's own body or status comes first. There is also a chronological logic: presumably the obligation to redeem oneself predates the birth of one's son.

### R. Yehuda: son first

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *beno kodmo* — his son comes first. The conceptual argument is elegant. The father's personal obligation to be redeemed was *originally* on his father; his father failed to fulfill it. The obligation that falls *natively* on this man — the one that is properly his own — is his obligation to his son. Thinking in generational terms: Abraham's obligation was to Isaac; Isaac's obligation was to Jacob. If we are speaking of Isaac, his primary obligation as a father is to Jacob, and his obligation to himself exists only as a derivative of his own father's failure.

### Five sela'im case

Rav Yirmiyahu then reframes the entire dispute, revealing that it is more limited in scope than it initially appears. He says: *hakol modim* — everyone agrees — that if there are only five *sela'im* available (enough for only one *pidyon haben*), *hu kodem livno* — he redeems himself first. *Mitzva degufei adifa* — one's personal obligation takes precedence. This is not what the Gemara seemed to be saying before, but that is the resolution: the dispute between the first opinion and Rabbi Yehuda does not concern the case where there is only enough for one payment.

### Ten sela'im lien

The dispute, Rav Yirmiyahu explains, concerns a case where there are ten *sela'im* — five in cash and five in encumbered property (property upon which creditors hold a lien). The question turns on a fundamental halakhic principle: *milveh haktuva baTorah kaktuva bishtar damya* — does a Torah obligation create a lien on property, just as a written loan document (*shtar*) does?

Rabbi Yehuda holds that it does. On this view, from the moment this man became obligated to redeem himself (say, from Bar Mitzvah), the *kohanim* collectively acquired a lien on his property for five *sela'im*. He subsequently borrowed money from another creditor, Reuven, who also acquired a lien on the same property — but later in time. Rabbi Yehuda therefore says: use the five *sela'im* in cash to redeem your son (fulfilling that obligation first), and the *kohen* will then collect the five *sela'im* from the encumbered property, displacing Reuven's later lien, thus satisfying both obligations. As Rashi explains, the father is entitled to direct his liquid assets to his son's redemption because his son's obligation is primary; it then works out that the *kohanim* can enforce their earlier lien against the encumbered property, leaving Reuven, unfortunately, unable to collect. Rashi's reading, as Tosafot notes, creates some tension — the rationale invoked to justify directing cash to the son (son's obligation is primary) seems to cut against the very premise that self comes first — and Tosafot offers an alternative reading of the Gemara accordingly.

The Rabbis hold that *milveh haktuva baTorah lav kikesuvah bishtar damya* — a Torah obligation does not create a property lien. Therefore the *kohanim* have no prior lien, the five encumbered *sela'im* are of no help to them, and the man must simply choose between redeeming himself and redeeming his son with whatever cash he has. In that situation, *mitzvas degufei adif* — his own obligation takes precedence.

## Pidyon vs. Regel

### Son redeemed first

The Gemara presents another case of competing obligations: *lifdos es beno v'la'alos laregel* — if one must choose between redeeming his son and making the pilgrimage (*aliyah laregel*) to the Temple. The majority view: *podeh es beno v'achar kach oleh laregel* — redeem your son first, then make the pilgrimage.

### R. Yehuda rationale

Rabbi Yehuda, in what at first looks like a reversal of his earlier position (where he prioritized the son), now says the opposite: *oleh laregel v'achar kach podeh es beno* — go on pilgrimage first, then redeem your son. But here there is no contradiction with his earlier ruling, because this is not a question of self versus son but of two distinct obligations. His reasoning: *shezu mitzvah overes v'zu mitzvah einah overes* — *aliyah laregel* is a time-bound obligation that will pass; if you miss the *regel*, you cannot make it up. *Pidyon haben* can be performed later when you acquire the means.

### Verse order logic

The Gemara is satisfied with Rabbi Yehuda's rationale. But what is the Rabbis' rationale for prioritizing *pidyon haben*? *De'amar kra kol bechor banecha tifdeh, v'hadar v'lo yera'u fanai reikam* — the Torah places the verse of redemption before the verse commanding the pilgrimage offering. The sequential order of the *pesukim* itself dictates the priority: first take care of your son's redemption, then address the pilgrimage.

## Multiple firstborns

### Plural sons proof

A new *braita* asks: how do we know that if a man has five firstborn sons from five different wives, he is obligated to redeem all of them? From *kol bechor banecha tifdeh* — the word *kol* (all) indicates that the obligation extends to multiple firstborn sons.

### Peter rechem logic

One might think this is obvious — surely if you have five sons each of whom is a *bechor*, you redeem all five. But there is a genuine question here. *Peter rechem tala Rachmana* — the Torah defines *bechor* status by reference to the opening of the womb, making it a status belonging to the mother's womb, not the father's lineage. So each of these five sons is indeed a *bechor* to his respective mother. The question is whether one also applies the principle of *reishis ono* — the first of his strength — drawn from the laws of inheritance, where *bechor* status is determined by the father. If both conditions were required, a man who already has a son from one wife could never have another *bechor* from a subsequent wife. The Gemara teaches: *ka mashma lan* — no, *bechor* for purposes of *pidyon haben* is determined solely by the mother. Hence a man may have multiple sons who each independently carries *bechor* status, and he is obligated to redeem each of them.

## Talmud Torah obligation

### Father teaches son

The Gemara now turns to the obligation of *Talmud Torah*. The father is obligated to teach his son Torah, derived from *v'limadtem osam es beneichem* — you shall teach your sons. If the father does not teach his son, *mechayav ihu lemigmar nafsheih* — the son, upon becoming an adult, is obligated to learn on his own. This is derived from *ulimadtem* — you shall learn — read not as *v'limadtem* (you shall teach) but as an obligation to learn, implying that if no one taught you, you must teach yourself.

### Sons not daughters

How do we know the obligation is specifically toward sons and not daughters? From *v'limadetem osam es beneichem* — *banecha v'lo bnosecha*: your sons and not your daughters.

### Learn-teach logic

But this only establishes that there is no obligation to teach daughters. How do we derive the mother's exemption from the obligation to teach Torah altogether? The Gemara constructs the same bidirectional logic used for *pidyon haben* and *bris milah*. *Kol shemetzuveh lilmod metzuveh lelamed* — whoever is obligated to learn is obligated to teach. *V'chol she'eino metzuveh lilmod eino metzuveh lelamed* — whoever is not obligated to learn is not obligated to teach.

### Women's exemption

And how do we know a woman is not obligated to learn Torah herself? The same structure applies in the other direction: *kol she'heim metzuvim lelamdo az metzuveh lelamed es atzmo* — if others are obligated to teach you, you are obligated to teach yourself. *Kol she'ein heim metzuvim lelamdo ein metzuveh lelamed es atzmo* — if others are not obligated to teach you, you are not obligated to teach yourself. And since *v'limadetem osam es beneichem* — *banecha v'lo bnosecha* — others are not obligated to teach daughters Torah, daughters are therefore not obligated to teach themselves, and therefore not obligated to teach others.

### Modern discourse

This is, of course, the foundational *Gemara* for one of the most consequential halakhic debates of the modern era. Beginning in the 1950s — with Rav Soloveitchik on one side and the Lubavitcher Rebbe among others on another — and continuing through decades of responsa and scholarly discussion, the question of women's exemption from *Talmud Torah* has generated an enormous body of literature. Many approaches have been proposed: drawing distinctions between *Torah she'bichsav* and *Torah she'be'al peh*; arguing that even if women are exempt from the pure *mitzvah* of *limud Torah* for its own sake (*Torah lishmah*), they are obligated to learn all the Torah necessary for the observance of the many *mitzvot* they do perform — which amounts to a great deal of Torah; and arguing further that learning Torah is an integral part of one's basic relationship with *Hakadosh Baruch Hu* and religious life generally, leaving women's de facto learning obligation not so different in scope from men's. One area where the tension becomes particularly acute is when both spouses wish to devote themselves entirely to full-time Torah study — at which point the technical contours of the exemption become practically significant.

### Sinai context

It is worth noting the broader *midrashic* and *peshat* context that complicates any simple application of this technical exemption. The Torah in *Devarim* repeatedly emphasizes that at *Ma'amad Har Sinai* everyone was present: men, women, and children. *Chazal* themselves underscore this: *koh tomar l'veis Yaakov v'sageid livnei Yisrael* — the *Midrash* reads *l'veis Yaakov* as referring specifically to the women, who were addressed at Sinai first. Similarly, the requirement of *Hakhel* explicitly gathers *anashim nashim v'hataf* — men, women, and children — to hear the Torah read. These sources create a picture of women's deep and primary engagement with Torah that sits in genuine tension with the formal technical exemption, and it is this tension that animates the ongoing debate. The statement in *Sotah* about a father who teaches his daughter Torah — *k'ilu melamdah tiflus* — has also been subject to extensive analysis, with many authorities drawing careful distinctions about what precisely that statement prohibits and what it permits or even encourages.

## Self vs. son Torah

### Self first

The Gemara now applies the same self-versus-son framework to *Talmud Torah*. If a man has not been fully educated in Torah and must choose between studying himself and providing for his son's Torah education, *hu kodem livno* — he comes before his son.

### R. Yehuda condition

Rabbi Yehuda refines this: *im beno zariz ummulach u'tamudo mitkayem beyado, beno kodmo* — if his son is sharp, subtle, and retains what he learns, then his son's learning takes precedence. The underlying principle here is not simply a question of whose obligation is primary but of which investment will yield the greater result. When father and son are equivalently capable, the father's own obligation to himself comes first. But when the son clearly has superior capacity — when his learning will be more productive and enduring — then directing resources toward the son's education takes priority.

### Rav Yaakov story

The Gemara illustrates this with the story of Rav Yaakov, the son of Rav Acha bar Yaakov. Rav Acha sent his son Rav Yaakov to learn from Abaye. When Rav Acha subsequently came to visit, *chazi d'lo havi mechadidun shmatei* — he saw that his son's learning was not sharp; the material was not crisp and precise in his grasp. Rav Acha said to him: *ana adif minach, tuv at d'eizal ana* — I can learn better than you; you stay, I will go learn. The story illustrates that the default assumption had been to send the son — but once the father assessed that his own learning would be more productive, he reasserted his own priority. This is somewhat different from the pure formulation of Rabbi Yehuda's position: rather than saying "I will send my son unless I find I am better," Rav Acha defaulted to sending his son and reversed course only upon discovering his son's deficiency.

### Demon episode

The Gemara then appends a narrative connected to the same setting. *Shama Abaye d'ka havi ati* — Abaye heard that Rav Acha the father was coming. There was a *mazik* — a malevolent spirit — in Abaye's *beit midrash*, *d'chi havi ailei bitrei afilu biyemama havi mitzkei* — such that even when two people entered together, even during the daytime (when such spirits are normally constrained), they were harmed. Abaye instructed that no one should provide Rav Acha with lodging, *efshar d'mitracheish nisa* — perhaps a miracle would occur. Rav Acha was accordingly forced to sleep in the *beit midrash*. The *mazik* appeared to him in the form of a seven-headed serpent. With each prostration that Rav Acha made in prayer, one of the heads fell off — *kol kria d'kara, nassar chad reishei* — until the spirit was vanquished. In the morning he told the students: *ilo lo itrachish nisan sakintan* — had a miracle not occurred, you would have endangered me by forcing me to sleep there.

## Summary

### Duties recap

The sugya of 29a covers, with remarkable density, the core obligations a father bears toward his son: *bris milah*, *pidyon haben*, *Talmud Torah*, arranging marriage, and teaching a trade. In each case, the obligation is directed specifically from father to son — not from mother to son, not from father to daughter, and not from mother to daughter.

### Gender patterns

The textual grounds for excluding women and daughters follow a consistent pattern throughout the *sugya*. For *bris milah*, the exclusion derives from *kaasher tziva oso* — commanding him alone — extended to all generations by the principle that *tzav* implies future applicability. For *pidyon haben*, the chain runs through *banecha v'lo bnosecha* to *tifdeh tipadeh* and back, locking women out on both sides of the redemption relationship. For *Talmud Torah*, *banecha v'lo bnosecha* generates an analogous chain excluding women from the obligation to be taught, to teach themselves, and to teach their sons. There exist minority opinions — a Rashi and the *Mareh Or Zarua* — suggesting that the mother may carry at least a backup or secondary obligation in *bris milah*, which opens interesting questions for edge cases; but the dominant framework throughout this *sugya* is one of consistent textual exclusion.

### Balancing rules

Running through all of these obligations is a second layer of analysis: how to balance competing duties when resources are limited. The tension between one's personal obligation and one's filial obligation is resolved differently in different cases. For *pidyon haben*, the baseline rule is that one's personal obligation (*mitzva degufei adifa*) comes first — though Rabbi Yehuda's position introduces the generational framing that complicates this, and Rav Yirmiyahu's reconciliation confines the dispute to the case of ten *sela'im* with encumbered property. For *Talmud Torah*, personal study generally comes first, but Rabbi Yehuda introduces the aptitude condition — if the son's capacity is superior, the son's learning takes precedence — illustrated concretely by the story of Rav Acha and his son. And when *pidyon haben* competes not with personal obligation but with *aliyah laregel*, the majority prioritizes the non-repeatable obligation of redemption, while Rabbi Yehuda prioritizes the time-sensitive *mitzvah overes* of the *regel*.
