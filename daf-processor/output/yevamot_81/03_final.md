# Yevamot 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Saris & Androgynos

### Saris Wife Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ סְרִיס חַמָּה כֹּהֵן שֶׁנָּשָׂא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — מַאֲכִילָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a priest who is a eunuch by natural causes married an Israelite woman,** he **enables her to eat *teruma*.**

The mishnah on this daf returns us to the question of the *saris* — one who is incapable of procreation — and its implications for *chalitzah* and *yibum*. Having established in earlier discussion that a *saris chama* (one whose condition is congenital, from the sun, as it were) cannot perform *yibum* or *chalitzah* according to Rabbi Akiva — whereas a *saris adam* (one castrated by human intervention) had at one point been capable and is therefore not categorically excluded — the mishnah now turns to a distinct question: the *saris chama*'s wife and her eligibility to eat *terumah*.

The mishnah states: a *kohen* who is a *saris chama* and who married a *bat Yisrael* — she may eat *terumah* on his behalf. The distinction is critical. A *saris adam* is a *patzua daka*, whose marriage is forbidden, rendering the woman a *chalalah*. But a *saris chama* has committed no *issur* in marrying her; the marriage is fully valid, and she accordingly enjoys the rights of a *kohen*'s wife, including eating *terumah*.

### Androgynos Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס כֹּהֵן שֶׁנָּשָׂא בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל — מַאֲכִילָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** If **a priest who is a hermaphrodite,** possessing both male and female genitals, **married an Israelite woman,** he **enables her to eat *teruma*.**

Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon introduce the case of an *androgynos* — a person with both male and female genitalia — who is a *kohen* and has married a *bat Yisrael*. They rule that she may eat *terumah* on his behalf. Before examining the ruling itself, it is worth considering the three possible halakhic frameworks for understanding an *androgynos*. The intuitive assumption might be *safek* — perhaps we simply do not know whether to categorize this person as male or female. But there are two other possibilities as well. One is that the *androgynos* is a *berya bifnei atzmo*, a creature of a third category entirely, neither male nor female but something unto itself — a recognition that the binary division of the world into male and female may not be exhaustive. The third possibility is that the *androgynos* is definitively male, on the reasoning that maleness is defined by the presence of male genitalia, and since this person possesses male genitalia, he is male regardless of any other characteristics present.

### Androgynos Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס נוֹשֵׂא אֲבָל לֹא נִישָּׂא. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו סְקִילָה כַּזָּכָר.
>
> **Translation:** A hermaphrodite may marry** a woman **but he may not be married** by a man, as he is considered a man. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If one had intercourse with **a hermaphrodite,** he **is liable to** receive the punishment of **stoning on his account as if** he had had relations with **a male.**

The ruling of Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon — that an *androgynos kohen*'s wife may eat *terumah* — presupposes that the *androgynos* is halakhically male. For the marriage to confer *terumah* rights upon his wife, he must be definitively her husband, which requires him to be definitively male. Were he a *safek* or a *berya bifnei atzmo*, it is difficult to see how the marriage would unambiguously transfer *terumah* eligibility.

The mishnah further states that an *androgynos* can marry a woman but cannot be married by a man — again consistent with the position that he is definitively male. Rabbi Eliezer goes further, ruling that if an *androgynos* marries a man, the act is treated as an act of male homosexuality and carries the penalty of *sekilah*, again treating him as fully male. Whether the earlier positions in the mishnah require us to view the *androgynos* as definitively male, or whether they are compatible with a *safek* reading, remains to be examined in the *gemara*.

### Tumtum Chalitzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם שֶׁנִּקְרַע וְנִמְצָא זָכָר — לֹא יַחְלוֹץ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּסָרִיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If **a *tumtum*,** whose external sexual organs are indeterminate, **was torn** open so that his genitals were exposed, **and he was found** to be **a male,** he **must not perform *ḥalitza*, because he is** treated **like a eunuch.**

Rabbi Yehuda adds that a *tumtum* — a person whose genitalia were covered and whose sex was initially indeterminate — who was later found upon tearing of the skin covering to be male, may nonetheless not perform *chalitzah*, because he is considered like a *saris*. The presumption is that someone born in this condition will be unable to father children, rendering him a *saris chama* from birth and thus excluded from the entire framework of *yibum* and *chalitzah*.

## Yochanan vs Lakish

### Saris Wife Terumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: מוֹלִיד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹלִיד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara questions the mishna's teaching concerning a priest who was sexually impotent from birth: This **is obvious;** why should such a priest not enable his wife to partake of *teruma*? The Gemara answers: This *halakha* is necessary **lest you say** that since the verse states: "And such as are born in his house, they eat of his bread" (Leviticus 22:11), the allowance to eat *teruma* depends on the priest's capacity to father children, i.e., that only **one who can father** children **enables** his wife **to eat** *teruma*, but **one who cannot father** children **does not enable** his wife **to eat** *teruma*. Therefore, the *tanna* **teaches us** that the priest's capacity to have children is irrelevant.

The *gemara* opens by questioning whether the first ruling — that a *saris chama*'s wife may eat *terumah* — is even in need of stating. *Peshita* — it seems obvious. He is a valid *kohen*, the marriage is valid, she is not *pesulah* in any way. Why would we think otherwise? The *gemara* answers: one might have thought that the verse *yelid beito hem yochlu belachmo* — members of his household eat of his bread — is tied specifically to the context of a marriage that produces progeny. One might reason that only *molid ma'achil*, one who can have children causes his wife to eat *terumah*, and one who cannot, *she'eino molid*, does not. The mishnah teaches us that we do not apply this reasoning; the wife of a *saris chama* is his wife in every sense, and she eats *terumah*.

A related question arises as to whether the *saris chama* and the *tumtum she-nikra* — the *tumtum* who was found to be male — are treated identically. In both cases, the person is male but incapable of procreation; the mishnah simply addressed each in the context of a different *halakha* — *terumah* for the former, *chalitzah* for the latter. But these are not contradictory rulings; both *halakhot* apply to both cases. The distinction that does emerge from earlier discussions is between a *saris* and an ordinary *akar* — a man who is simply infertile. An *akar* does perform *chalitzah*, while a *saris* does not, and it is the *saris*'s categorical status, not merely his infertility, that excludes him.

### Lakish Safek View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מַאֲכִילָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה, וְאֵין מַאֲכִילָהּ בְּחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna that **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** If a priest who is **a hermaphrodite** married an Israelite woman, he enables her to eat *teruma*. **Reish Lakish said: He enables her to eat *teruma*, but he does not enable her to eat the breast and thigh** of peace-offerings.

The *gemara* now turns to the case of the *androgynos*. Reish Lakish states: the wife of an *androgynos kohen* may eat *terumah*, but she may not eat *chazeh v'shok* — the breast and thigh portions of *kodashim* that a *kohen*'s wife is entitled to receive. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and holds that she may even eat *chazeh v'shok*.

The logic of Reish Lakish's position flows from his view that an *androgynos* is a *safek* — possibly male, possibly not. If there is genuine doubt as to whether this woman is legally married to a *kohen*, then she cannot eat *chazeh v'shok*, which is biblical. Why then can she eat *terumah*? Because, the *gemara* explains, the *gemara* is speaking of *terumah bizman hazeh* — *terumah* in our times, which is only rabbinic in force, presumably because following the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash* the land lost its sanctification for purposes of *terumah* and *ma'asros*. In a case of *safek*, we are lenient in matters of rabbinic law, and so she may eat *terumah* even though the marriage is uncertain.

### Yochanan Male View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אוֹמֵר: אַף מַאֲכִילָהּ בְּחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** However, **Rabbi Yoḥanan** disagrees and **says** that **he even enables her to eat the breast and thigh** of peace-offerings.

Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, holds that the *androgynos* is definitively male, the marriage is fully valid, and the woman may eat *terumah* and *kodashim* alike, with no restriction.

### Terumah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מַאי שְׁנָא חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק — דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא? תְּרוּמָה נָמֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בִּתְרוּמָה בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Reish Lakish, what is different** about **the breast and thigh** of peace-offerings? If you say it is that they are **by Torah** law, ***teruma*** is **also by Torah** law. Why, then, is it permitted for her to eat *teruma*, but not the breast and thigh of peace-offerings? The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with *teruma* in the present,** after the destruction of the Temple, when *teruma* is in effect only **by rabbinic** law.

At this point the *gemara* shifts from the primary dispute — the status of the *androgynos* — to challenge the secondary premise underlying Reish Lakish's position, namely that *terumah* today is rabbinic. Rabbi Yochanan presses: do you really hold that *terumah bizman hazeh* is *derabbanan*?

Reish Lakish confirms this and offers a proof: he teaches a *baraita* in which an *igul* — a round of pressed figs — of *terumah* that becomes mixed with ordinary rounds of pressed figs is *oleh b'echad u'me'ah*, nullified in a ratio of one to a hundred. Now, an *igul* is a *davar she-biminyan* — an item sold by count — and the general rule is that things counted individually are *chashuv* and cannot be subject to *bittul*. The fact that *bittul* works here, Reish Lakish argues, must be because *terumah* nowadays is only rabbinic, and in rabbinic matters we are lenient enough to allow *bittul* even for a *davar she-biminyan*.

The *gemara* raises the obvious objection: *terumah* is itself a biblical prohibition. Reish Lakish acknowledges this but maintains the distinction between *terumah* in Temple times and *terumah* today, given the absence of the *Beis HaMikdash*.

The *gemara* then points out: if Reish Lakish wishes to contrast *terumah* as rabbinic with *chazeh v'shok* as biblical, he need not reach for *chazeh v'shok* at all. He could simply distinguish within *terumah* itself — rabbinic *terumah* today versus biblical *terumah* at the time of the *Beis HaMikdash*. The *gemara* accepts that this is indeed Reish Lakish's intent. When he says she eats *terumah* but not *chazeh v'shok*, he means: she eats today's *terumah*, which is rabbinic. And at a time when the *Beis HaMikdash* stood and *terumah* was biblical, she would not eat even that. Moreover, even rabbinic *terumah* — such as *terumah* of fruit, which is rabbinic even in Temple times — would be prohibited to her at the time of the *Beis HaMikdash*, because *dilma asi l'ochla b'terumah d'oraisa*: one might come to eat from the biblical *terumah* as well, and we are strict across the board when biblical *terumah* coexists with rabbinic *terumah*. In our times, however, when there is no biblical *terumah* at all, there is no such concern, and she may eat rabbinic *terumah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים מַאי — לָא? אַדְּתָנֵי ״אֵין מַאֲכִילָהּ בְּחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק״, לִיפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּתְרוּמָה דְּרַבָּנַן, אֲבָל בִּתְרוּמָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But when the Temple is standing, what** is the *halakha*? He does **not** enable his wife to eat *teruma*. But if so, there is a difficulty. **Instead of teaching** that **he does not enable her to eat the breast and thigh** of peace-offerings, **let him distinguish and teach** it **within** the case of *teruma* **itself** as follows: **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to *teruma*** that is in effect only **by rabbinic** law, **but with regard to *teruma*** that is in effect **by Torah** law this ruling does **not** apply.

## Bittul Evidence

### Igul Batel Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: כְּשֶׁהוּא מַאֲכִילָהּ — מַאֲכִילָהּ בִּתְרוּמָה בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה דְּרַבָּנַן, וְאֵין מַאֲכִילָהּ בִּזְמַן חָזֶה וָשׁוֹק, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּתְרוּמָה דְּרַבָּנַן, דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאוֹכֹלַהּ בִּתְרוּמָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That is also what he is saying.** In other words, this is actually what Reish Lakish means, as his statement should be understood as follows: **When he enables her to eat, he enables her to eat *teruma* in the present,** when *teruma* is in effect only **by rabbinic** law, **but he does not enable her to eat** *teruma* **at a time** that **the breast and thigh** are given to the priests, i.e., when the Temple is standing, not **even *teruma*** that is in effect only **by rabbinic** law. This is due to the concern that **perhaps he will bring her to eat *teruma*** that is in effect **by Torah** law.

Reish Lakish's proof that *terumah* today is rabbinic rests on the *igul* case: a round of pressed figs of *terumah* that becomes mixed with other rounds is *batel* in a hundred, despite being a *davar she-biminyan*. This requires some background on the principles of *bittul*.

The mechanism of *bittul* — halakhic nullification — operates on the logic that when a prohibited item is mixed into a much larger quantity of permitted items, the prohibited item loses its independent identity and becomes absorbed into the whole. This works when the items are undifferentiated and can be viewed collectively. When, however, items are individually significant — *chashuv* — each one retains its independent identity and cannot simply be dissolved into the collective. Such items are not subject to *bittul*.

One basis for this individual significance is the principle of *davar she-biminyan* — items sold by count. Things one counts by number, rather than by weight or volume, are treated as individually significant, and they are therefore generally not *batel*. The intuition is straightforward: a chair, a house, a barrel of wine — these are things one counts individually, each retaining its separate existence. They are not viewed as an undifferentiated mass.

Now, Reish Lakish argues that an *igul* — sold by count — should not be *batel* under this principle. Yet the *baraita* says it is *batel*. This can only be because, Reish Lakish reasons, *terumah* today is rabbinic, and in rabbinic domains we relax the strictures of *bittul* sufficiently to allow *bittul* even of a *davar she-biminyan*.

### Et vs Kol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף מַאֲכִילָהּ בְּחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ תְּרוּמָה בַּזְּמַן הַזֶּה דְּרַבָּנַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, שֶׁאֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה: עִיגּוּל בְּעִיגּוּלִים עוֹלֶה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וַהֲלֹא אֲנִי שׁוֹנֶה: חֲתִיכָה בַּחֲתִיכוֹת — עוֹלָה. מִי סָבְרַתְּ ״כׇּל שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִימָּנוֹת״ שָׁנִינוּ? ״אֶת שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִימָּנוֹת״ שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** However, **Rabbi Yoḥanan** disagrees and **says** that **he even enables her to eat the breast and thigh** of peace-offerings. With respect to this dispute, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to Reish Lakish:** Since you distinguish between *teruma* and the breast and thigh, **do you maintain** that ***teruma* in the present** is mandated only **by rabbinic** law? **He said to him: Yes,** and the proof is **that I teach** that **a cake** of dried figs that became intermingled **with** other **cakes is nullified.** If a cake of *teruma* figs became intermingled with one hundred ordinary cakes, the cake is nullified and it is not necessary to treat them all as *teruma*. If the cake, which is a food of importance in its own right, is nullified, this must be because the *teruma* is only by rabbinic law. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: But don't I teach** that even **a piece** of a sin-offering that became intermingled **with** other **pieces** of meat **is nullified,** as I maintain that the *halakha* of nullification applies even to Torah prohibitions? **Do you maintain** that **we learned** that **any** object **that it is usual to count,** i.e., any object that is even occasionally sold by unit, rather than by weight or measure, is considered to be important and therefore cannot be nullified? This is not so, as in fact **we learned** that only **that which it is usual to count,** i.e., an object that is always sold by unit and in no other manner, is considered to be important and is therefore not subject to nullification; and cakes of dried figs are not always sold by unit.

Rabbi Yochanan rejects this inference entirely. The proof fails, he says, because Reish Lakish has misread the *baraita* about *davar she-biminyan*. Reish Lakish understood the rule to be *kol she-darko limanot* — everything that is ever sold by count — is not *batel*. Rabbi Yochanan corrects him: the proper text of the *baraita* is *et she-darko limanot* — only that which is sold *exclusively* by count is not *batel*. The word *et* rather than *kol* narrows the category considerably.

The distinction is meaningful. Some items are *sometimes* sold by count and *sometimes* sold by weight or volume. Eggs, for instance, may be purchased individually or by the basket. Under Reish Lakish's reading, anything that is ever counted — even if it is also sometimes sold otherwise — would be *chashuv* and thus not *batel*. Under Rabbi Yochanan's reading, only that which is *exclusively* counted — always and only sold by number, never by weight or volume — is considered sufficiently individuated to resist *bittul*.

An *igul* of pressed figs, Rabbi Yochanan explains, is sometimes sold by count and sometimes not. It therefore falls outside the narrow category of *et she-darko limanot*, and accordingly it *can* be *batel*. This has nothing whatsoever to do with *terumah* being rabbinic. The *igul* is *batel* simply because it is not a *davar she-biminyan* in the strict sense Rabbi Yochanan defines. Reish Lakish's inference collapses.

### Kilaim Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא — דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ חֲבִילֵי תִלְתָּן שֶׁל כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם — יִדְלְקוּ. נִתְעָרְבוּ בַּאֲחֵרוֹת — כּוּלָּן יִדָּלְקוּ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יַעֲלוּ בְּאֶחָד וּמָאתַיִם. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִימָּנוֹת — מְקַדֵּשׁ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ מְקַדֵּשׁ אֶלָּא שִׁשָּׁה דְּבָרִים בִּלְבַד. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this *halakha* to which Rabbi Yoḥanan alludes? **As we learned** in a mishna (*Orla* 3:6–7): In the case of **one who had bundles of clover,** a type of legume, **of** a forbidden mixture of **food crops in a vineyard,** i.e., clover plants that grew in a vineyard, these bundles **must be burned,** as it is prohibited for one to derive benefit from a forbidden mixture of food crops in a vineyard. If the forbidden bundles **became intermingled with others** that are permitted, **they must all be burned;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: They are nullified in** a mixture of **one** part forbidden food **to two hundred** parts permitted food. **As Rabbi Meir would say: Any** object **that it is usual to count renders** a mixture **prohibited.** In other words, objects that are counted and sold by the unit, rather than by weight or estimation, are considered of special importance, and so they cannot be nullified by any majority and therefore must be burned. **But the Rabbis say: Only six objects** are important enough that they cannot be nullified and therefore **render** their mixtures **forbidden. Rabbi Akiva says:** There are **seven** such objects.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵלּוּ הֵן: אֱגוֹזֵי פֶרֶךְ, וְרִמּוֹנֵי בָּדָן, וְחָבִיּוֹת סְתוּמוֹת, וְחִלְפֵי תְרָדִין, וְקוּלְחֵי כְּרוּב, וְדַלַּעַת יְוָנִית. וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מוֹסִיף אַף כִּכָּרוֹת שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת. הָרְאוּיִם לְעׇרְלָה — עׇרְלָה. לְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם — כִּלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **They are** as follows: **Perekh nuts,** high-quality nuts from a place called Perekh; **Badan pomegranates,** pomegranates from a place called Badan; **sealed barrels** of wine; **shoots of beet; cabbage stalks; and Greek gourd. Rabbi Akiva adds,** as his seventh item, **a homeowner's loaves.** Different prohibitions apply to these seven items: **Those that are fit for** the prohibition of ***orla*,** fruit that grows in the first three years after a tree has been planted, i.e., the nuts and pomegranates, render the entire mixture ***orla*. Those that are fit** for the prohibition proscribing a mixture of **food crops in a vineyard,** i.e., the beets, cabbage, and gourd, render the entire mixture a mixture of **food crops in a vineyard.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: ״אֶת שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִימָּנוֹת״ שָׁנִינוּ. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ סָבַר: ״כֹּל שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ לִימָּנוֹת״ שָׁנִינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And it was stated that *amora'im* disagreed about the precise wording of this mishna: **Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that **we learned: That which it is usual to count,** i.e., Rabbi Meir's stringent ruling is limited to objects that are sold exclusively by unit. **And Reish Lakish holds** that **we learned: Any** object **that it is usual to count,** i.e., even items that are only sometimes sold by unit are considered important and cannot be nullified.

The underlying dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — *et she-darko limanot* versus *kol she-darko limanot* — finds its source in a *baraita* about *kilaim*. The *mishnah* teaches: one who possesses bundles of fenugreek (*tiltan*) that are *kilei hakerem* — produce that grew intermixed with a vineyard and is therefore forbidden — must burn them. If these bundles become mixed with other bundles of ordinary fenugreek, Rabbi Meir says all of them must be burned, because the forbidden bundles are not *batel*. The *chachamim*, however, say they are *batel* in one part in two hundred.

The basis of Rabbi Meir's stringency is his rule that a *davar she-biminyan* is not *batel* — and the text attributed to Rabbi Meir is *kol she-darko limanot*, everything that is counted. The *chachamim* reject this broad category and instead limit the category of items that are not *batel* to a specific list of six things: nuts of Parach, pomegranates from Badan, sealed barrels of wine, shoots of beets, heads of cabbage, and Greek gourd. Rabbi Akiva adds a seventh: home-baked loaves.

These six or seven items, whatever their particular significance in their time and context, represent a much narrower definition of *chashuv* than Rabbi Meir's. The *chachamim* are not saying that these items are the only things ever counted; they are saying that the category of things that resist *bittul* by virtue of individual significance is far more restricted than anything-that-is-ever-counted. The *Rambam*, in codifying this *halakha*, frames the list in terms of what is *chashuv* in a given time and place, rather than as a fixed eternal enumeration.

The *gemara* assumes that the discussion between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish operates within the framework of Rabbi Meir's view, and their dispute concerns the correct reading of Rabbi Meir's own formulation — *et* versus *kol*.

### Chatichah Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חֲתִיכָה — דְּתַנְיָא: חֲתִיכָה שֶׁל חַטָּאת טְמֵאָה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבָה בְּמֵאָה חֲתִיכוֹת שֶׁל חַטָּאוֹת טְהוֹרוֹת, וְכֵן פְּרוּסָה שֶׁל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים טְמֵאָה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבָה בְּמֵאָה פְּרוּסוֹת שֶׁל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים טְהוֹרוֹת — תַּעֲלֶה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא תַּעֲלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further explains: **What is** the case of **a piece,** referred to by Rabbi Yoḥanan? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **a piece of a ritually impure sin-offering became intermingled with one hundred pieces of ritually pure sin-offerings, and similarly,** if **a slice of ritually impure shewbread became intermingled with one hundred slices of ritually pure showbread,** the impure piece of a sin-offering or slice of shewbread **is nullified** in its respective mixture. **Rabbi Yehuda says: It is not nullified.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל חֲתִיכָה שֶׁל חַטָּאת טְהוֹרָה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבָה בְּמֵאָה חֲתִיכוֹת שֶׁל חוּלִּין טְהוֹרוֹת, וְכֵן פְּרוּסָה שֶׁל לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים טְהוֹרָה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבָה בְּמֵאָה פְּרוּסוֹת שֶׁל חוּלִּין טְהוֹרוֹת — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא תַּעֲלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** if **a piece of a ritually pure sin-offering became intermingled with one hundred pieces of ritually pure non-sacred** meat, **and similarly,** if **a slice of ritually pure shewbread became intermingled with one hundred slices of ritually pure non-sacred bread, everyone agrees that** the pure piece of sin-offering or slice of shewbread **is not nullified** in its respective mixture.

Rabbi Yochanan's decisive argument against Reish Lakish is a *baraita* about a *chatichah* of *kodashim*. A *chatichah* — a chunk of meat, a steak — of *chatat* that became *tamei* and became mixed with a hundred chunks of *tahor chatat* meat: the *baraita* says *ta'aleh* — it is *batel*, and the mixture may be distributed and eaten by *kohanim*. Similarly, a slice of *lechem hapanim* — the showbread — that became *tamei* and was mixed with a hundred *tahor* slices: again, *ta'aleh*, it is *batel*.

Rabbi Yehuda, however, says *lo ta'aleh* — it is not *batel* in these cases. The force of Rabbi Yochanan's proof comes from the view of the *chachamim*, who hold it is *batel*. A *chatichah* is something sold by count — you do not buy meat in an undifferentiated mass, you buy portions, individual pieces. Under Reish Lakish's reading of *kol she-darko limanot*, a *chatichah* would be a *davar she-biminyan* and not *batel*. Yet *kodashim* is unquestionably biblical. If *bittul* works for a *chatichah* of *kodashim*, it cannot be because we are lenient due to rabbinic status. It must be because a *chatichah*, while sometimes counted, is not *exclusively* counted — and therefore, under Rabbi Yochanan's rule of *et she-darko limanot*, it falls outside the category of things that resist *bittul*. Reish Lakish's inference from the *igul* case is therefore unfounded; the *bittul* of an *igul* proves nothing about *terumah* being rabbinic.

The *baraita* then presents a contrasting case: a *tahor* chunk of *chatat* that became mixed with a hundred *tahor* chunks of ordinary *chulin* — and a *tahor* slice of *lechem hapanim* mixed with a hundred *tahor* slices of ordinary bread. In these cases, *divrei hakol lo ta'aleh* — by universal agreement it is not *batel*. Rashi explains this result by reference to a combination of factors: the items are somewhat *chashuv* — quasi-counted — and, critically, the consequences of non-*bittul* are not severe: one simply needs to arrange for a *kohen* to eat the mixture. Since *bittul* is not strictly necessary and the item has some individual weight, we do not apply it here.

## Nimucha Answer

### Ground Meat Batel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת רֵישָׁא תַּעֲלֶה! אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: בְּנִימּוֹחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In any event, the first clause** of this *baraita* **teaches** that a piece of ritually impure sin-offering **can be nullified.** This poses a difficulty with respect to the opinion of Reish Lakish, as such a piece of meat is an item that is sometimes counted and considered important in its own right, and it is forbidden by Torah law, but nevertheless it can still be nullified. **Rabbi Ḥiyya, son of Rav Huna, said:** This *baraita* is referring not to a whole piece of meat but **to one that had been crushed** and broken into small parts. Once it is no longer a whole piece, it loses its importance and can be nullified.

How does Reish Lakish respond to Rabbi Yochanan's proof from the *chatichah*? Reish Lakish, speaking through Rav Assi son of Rav Huna, answers: *binimucha* — we are speaking of a case where the *chatichah* has been dissolved, ground up into what one might describe as hamburger meat. The *baraita*'s ruling that the *chatichah* is *batel* does not refer to a case where five distinct steaks sit before you. It refers to a case where the meat has been ground together into an indistinct mass. In that state, there is no longer an independent *chatichah* — each piece has lost its separate identity — and *bittul* accordingly operates without obstacle.

This is how Reish Lakish preserves his view. He agrees that a *chatichah* is something sometimes sold by count, and that it would normally be covered by his principle of *kol she-darko limanot* and thus not *batel*. But once it is ground, there is no longer any *chatichah* in existence — the pieces no longer retain their individual identity — and the factor that prevented *bittul* no longer applies.

One must consider whether this reading does justice to the *baraita*'s language, which speaks of a *chatichah* becoming mixed with other *chatichot*. On its face, the text seems to describe distinct pieces mixed with distinct pieces, and the ruling that it is *batel* appears to be a ruling about that very situation. This is precisely what Rabbi Yochanan takes it to mean. Reish Lakish, however, insists that the underlying case must be one of ground meat, since only that would explain why *bittul* applies.

A related question touches on the role of *min b'mino* — same-kind mixtures — in this discussion. When meat is mixed with meat of the same kind, the analysis can cut in two directions. On one view, because the items were never distinct from one another, they never lose their individual identity, and they are always present — and therefore, according to Rabbi Yehuda, *min b'mino lo batel*, a same-kind mixture is never *batel*. On the other view, because the items were never distinct, *bittul* is immediate, even without the need for *shishim*. The stringent outcome in certain cases — requiring one in a hundred or one in two hundred rather than the ordinary threshold — actually applies specifically to *min b'mino* mixtures, since it is only there that we say each item retains its presence and requires reduction to trivial quantity. For *aino mino* — different-kind mixtures — once the prohibited item can no longer be tasted it has lost its independent identity entirely and is *batel* on that basis alone.

This is also why Rashi explains that Rabbi Yehuda's ruling of *lo ta'aleh* in the *chatichah* case is attributable to his general position that *min b'mino lo batel*. Tosafot offers a different explanation, but the *min b'mino* framework is essential for understanding why *bittul* operates asymmetrically across these cases.

### Challenge Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is **so** that the piece has been crushed, **what is the reason for Rabbi Yehuda's** opinion that the piece is not nullified?

The *gemara* now challenges Reish Lakish's answer directly. The *baraita* presents both a *reisha* — where *bittul* does apply — and a *seifa* — where it does not. Reish Lakish reads the *reisha* as a case of ground meat, which is why *bittul* works. But if that is so, the *seifa* — where everything is *tahor* but *chatat* is mixed with *chulin* — must also be a case of ground meat (since the *baraita* deals with comparable scenarios throughout). Why then does *bittul* not work in the *seifa*?

Moreover, the *gemara* notes a structural problem: if the way to show that *bittul* does not apply is to describe a non-ground-meat case, why did the *baraita* not make that distinction *within the same scenario* — ground meat versus non-ground meat — rather than switching to an entirely different scenario involving *chulin*? According to Reish Lakish, one could simply have said: when dissolved into ground meat, *batel*; when still in distinct pieces, not *batel*. The fact that the *baraita* instead switches cases requires explanation.

The *gemara* answers: *tahor b'tahor adif lei* — the *baraita* prefers to give you the scenario where everything is *tahor*, because that is a bigger *chiddush*. One might have thought that when everything is *tahor*, the only question being whether a *kohen* or a non-*kohen* may eat it, we would readily say it is *batel* and allow anyone to eat freely. The *baraita* teaches that even in this easier case, *bittul* does not apply — a more surprising ruling, and therefore a more instructive one to state.

## Tumah & Severity

### Rabbinic vs Biblical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: רֵישָׁא, בְּטוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין דְּרַבָּנַן. סֵיפָא, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to Reish Lakish,** who establishes the *baraita* as referring to a piece of meat that has been crushed, **what is different** in **the first clause** of the *baraita* **and what is different** in **the latter clause?** Why is nullification possible in the case of impure pieces, but not in the case of pure ones? **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said: The first clause** is dealing with a piece of a sin-offering that contracted **ritual impurity due to liquids,** which is imparted **by rabbinic** law. Since the sacred food contracted impurity that is only rabbinic in origin, it may be nullified rather than allowed to go to waste. **The latter clause,** on the other hand, deals with sacred food that became intermingled with ordinary non-sacred food. The sacred food is forbidden to non-priests **by Torah** law, and therefore it is not nullified.

Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi offers a different resolution to the *reisha*/*seifa* asymmetry. The *reisha* — where *bittul* applies — involves a *chatichah* that became *tamei* through *tum'at mashkin derabbanan*: the piece of *chatat* became impure because it came into contact with a liquid that had itself been rendered *tamei* through a secondary chain of rabbinic upgrade. Because the *tumah* is only rabbinic in origin, the prohibition against eating this piece is sufficiently attenuated that we allow *bittul* to function.

The *seifa*, by contrast — where *bittul* does not apply — involves the mixing of *tahor chatat* with *tahor chulin*. Here the concern is not *tumah* at all, but rather that if one declares the *chatat* *batel*, a *tamei* person or a non-*kohen* will eat it, and eating *tahor kodashim* while *tamei* is an *issur d'oraisa*. Because the consequence of applying *bittul* is a biblical violation, we do not allow *bittul* to function.

### All-Pure Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל טוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ מַאי — לֹא תַּעֲלֶה, אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֲבָל חֲתִיכָה שֶׁל חַטָּאת טְהוֹרָה שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבָה בְּמֵאָה חֲתִיכוֹת שֶׁל חוּלִּין טְהוֹרוֹת לֹא תַּעֲלֶה, נִיפְלוֹג וְלִיתְנֵי בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּטוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְאַת שֶׁרֶץ — לֹא! טְהוֹרָה בִּטְהוֹרוֹת עֲדִיף לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** had the piece of meat mentioned in the first clause of the *baraita* contracted **ritual impurity through** contact with **a creeping animal,** which is imparted by Torah law, **what** would the *halakha* be? Presumably, it would **not** be **nullified.** If so, there is a difficulty, as **instead of teaching** a case involving non-sacred meat in the **latter clause** of the *baraita*, as follows: **However,** if **a piece of a ritually pure sin-offering became intermingled with one hundred pieces of ritually pure non-sacred** meat, the impure piece of a sin-offering **is not nullified; let** the *tanna* **distinguish and teach** it **in** the case involving only sacred meat, as follows: **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** the piece of meat contracted **ritual impurity due to liquids, but where** it contracted **ritual impurity through a creeping animal** it is **not** nullified. The Gemara answers: Even so, the *tanna* **prefers** to address the novel case of an intermingling of **ritually pure** items **with** other **ritually pure** items.

As before, the *gemara* asks: if this is the distinction, why did the *baraita* not simply demonstrate the non-*batel* case by introducing a biblical *tumah* — say, contact with a *sheretz* — within the same framework, rather than switching to the *chulin* scenario? The answer is again *tahor b'tahor adif lei*: the *baraita* finds it more instructive to present the case where everything is *tahor*, because in that case one might most readily assume there is no reason not to apply *bittul* — nothing is actually impure, no one has done anything wrong — and the teaching that *bittul* nonetheless does not apply is the more striking and informative ruling.

### Lav vs Karet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר: רֵישָׁא — אִיסּוּר לָאו, סֵיפָא — אִיסּוּר כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** that an alternative explanation may be suggested for the difference between the two clauses of the *baraita*: In **the first clause** of the *baraita*, which deals with the intermingling of impure sacred meat with pure sacred meat, there is concern about the violation of **a standard prohibition,** i.e., the prohibition against eating impure sacred meat; therefore, the piece of meat is nullified. In **the latter clause** of the *baraita*, which deals with the intermingling of sacred meat and non-sacred meat, there is concern about the violation of **a prohibition** that bears the punishment **of *karet*,** which bars a non-priest from eating pure sacrificial food; therefore, the piece of meat is not nullified.

Rava offers yet another resolution. The distinction between *reisha* and *seifa* is not *derabbanan* versus *d'oraisa*, but severity: the *reisha* involves an *issur lav* — a negative prohibition — while the *seifa* involves an *issur karet* — a violation punishable by excision. In the *reisha*, where the *chatat* became *tamei*: if the piece itself is *tamei*, the *kodashim* is degraded, and eating *tamei kodashim* is severe but falls at the level of a *lav* in this context. In the *seifa*, where the *kodashim* is *tahor* but a *tamei* person eats it, that is an *issur karet*. Rava reasons that we allow *bittul* for an *issur lav* but not for an *issur karet*.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this on the basis of an apparently contradictory ruling elsewhere in which Rava himself states that when it comes to biblical matters, we do not differentiate between an *issur lav* and an *issur karet* for purposes of *bittul*. This creates a direct contradiction within Rava's own positions, and the *gemara* marks it as a *kashya* — a difficulty left without resolution.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַבָּה הוּא דְּאָמַר: כֹּל בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא, לָא שְׁנָא אִיסּוּר לָאו, וְלָא שְׁנָא אִיסּוּר כָּרֵת! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But wasn't it Rabba** himself **who said:** With regard to the nullification of **any** item forbidden **by Torah** law that became intermingled with a permitted substance, there is **no difference between** that which is forbidden due to a **standard prohibition** and that which is forbidden due to **a prohibition** that entails the punishment **of *karet*.** The Gemara concludes: This is **difficult** for Rabba.

### Matirin Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: סֵיפָא — מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵי לֵיהּ דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין — אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶלֶף לֹא בָּטֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** yet another explanation: In **the latter clause,** where ritually pure sacred meat became intermingled with ritually pure non-sacred meat, the sacred meat does not become nullified **because it is an object whose prohibition is temporary.** The piece of sacred meat is not absolutely forbidden, as it is permitted for a priest to eat it. **And** the *halakha* is that **any object whose prohibition is temporary cannot be nullified,** no matter how great the amount of permitted substance, **even in** a mixture of one to **one thousand.**

Rav Ashi offers a final attempt. The *seifa*, he argues, involves a *davar she-yesh lo matirin* — a matter that has a permissible pathway. The concept of *davar she-yesh lo matirin* holds that when a prohibited item has an available route to becoming permitted — or, in a related formulation, when the need to eat it can be satisfied through a permitted pathway — we do not allow *bittul*, even if the quantity would technically be sufficient to *mevatel*. The classic case is an egg born on *Yom Tov*: it is prohibited today, but will become permitted tomorrow; we therefore do not apply *bittul*, since there is no pressing need to declare the egg nullified when one can simply wait.

Rav Ashi applies this reasoning to the *chatat*: one need not declare it *batel* and allow a non-*kohen* to eat the mixture, because there is a permissible route — give it to a *kohen*, who can eat it lawfully. This is analogous to waiting until tomorrow in the *Yom Tov* egg case. Since there is a *heter*, a permitted means of disposition, *bittul* is not invoked.

### Rejection Matirin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי בְּדוּתָא הִיא. לְמַאן? אִי לְכֹהֵן — מִישְׁרָא שְׁרֵי. אִי לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — לְעוֹלָם אָסוּר! אֶלָּא הָא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי בְּדוּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Now, that** teaching ascribed **to Rav Ashi is** certainly **a mistake [*beduta*],** as the application of this principle to the case at hand can easily be shown to be erroneous. With regard **to whom** is the prohibition temporary? **If** one would say that it is **for a priest,** the sacred meat **was** always **permitted** to him, even before it became intermingled, as a priest may eat both sacred and non-sacred food, and therefore it was never a forbidden mixture with respect to him. And **if** one would say that it is **for an Israelite,** the meat will **always** be **forbidden** to him. **Rather, that** teaching attributed **to Rav Ashi is** clearly **a mistake.**

The *gemara* immediately dismisses this argument as *dedusay* — foolish, or at minimum untenable. The category of *davar she-yesh lo matirin* refers to something that is *assur* for this person now but will become *muttar* for that same person later. The *Yom Tov* egg is forbidden to you today and will be permitted to you tomorrow — it is the same person, the same item, with a change in time. Here, the *chatat* is never *muttar* for a *Yisrael* — it is *le-olam assur*. And for a *kohen* it was never *assur* — *mi sharei*, it was always permitted. There is no entity for whom this item is currently *assur* and will later become *muttar*. The fact that a *kohen* can eat it is irrelevant to whether *bittul* should apply for the *Yisrael* who wishes to eat it. Thus the *davar she-yesh lo matirin* framework simply does not fit, and Rav Ashi's suggestion is withdrawn.

## Unresolved Bittul

### Three Answers

The *sugya* ends in a state of genuine unresolution. We are left with three proposed answers to the question of why *bittul* does not work in the *seifa*: (1) Rav Sheshet's answer — the *reisha* is *derabbanan* tumah, the *seifa* is *d'oraisa*; (2) Rava's answer — the *reisha* is an *issur lav*, the *seifa* is an *issur karet*; and (3) Rav Ashi's answer — *davar she-yesh lo matirin*. Of these, the third has been explicitly rejected by the *gemara* itself as untenable. The second is left as a *kashya*, contradicted by Rava's own ruling elsewhere. The first — the distinction between rabbinic and biblical *tumah* — stands as the most plausible of the three, though the underlying difficulty remains: even granting that the *seifa* is more severe, why should *bittul* not operate? *Bittul* routinely applies to biblical prohibitions — a drop of *chazir* mixed into a pot is *batel b'shishim* even though the prohibition is biblical. What makes this case different remains genuinely unclear.

Beyond the *bittul* analysis, the *sugya* leaves open three intertwined debates. First: the status of the *androgynos* — is he definitively male, as Rabbi Yochanan holds, or a *safek*, as Reish Lakish maintains? Second: is *terumah* today biblical or rabbinic? And third: what are the precise parameters of *davar she-biminyan* — does *et she-darko limanot* (Rabbi Yochanan's narrow reading) or *kol she-darko limanot* (Reish Lakish's broader reading) correctly describe when an item's countability prevents *bittul*? These questions are interlocking: the dispute about the *androgynos*'s status generates the question about *terumah*'s status today, which in turn generates the dispute about *bittul* and what kinds of items can be nullified. The *daf* thus traverses a remarkable range of halakhic terrain — from the status of an *androgynos kohen*'s wife to the fine structure of *hilkhot bittul* — without arriving at a clean resolution to any of them.
