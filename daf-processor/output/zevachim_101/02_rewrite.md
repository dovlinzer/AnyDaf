# Zevachim 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Aninus: Yom Misa vs. Yom…

### Rabbi's Night Position

The *gemara* opens by presenting a debate between Rabbi and Rabbi Yose regarding the duration of *aninus*. As established in the preceding discussion at the bottom of 100b, Rabbi holds that *aninus* extends not merely through *yom misa* — the day of death — but through *yom kvura*, the day of burial, while Rabbi Yose maintains it is limited to the day of death. The *gemara* further unpacks this dispute: the real point of contention is whether *aninus* extends into the night. Rabbi Yose holds it applies only during the day, while Rabbi holds it applies even into the night following *yom kvura*.

The *gemara* then records that this position was reported before Rava: since Rabbi Yose holds that *yom kvura* "takes hold" of the night (*tofes layla*) — meaning the night following burial also carries *aninus* status — and this extension of *yom kvura* into the night is only rabbinic (*mi-drabbanan*), it must follow that *yom misa* itself extends into the night at the level of *doraisa* (*mi-doraisa*). After all, we would not extend a rabbinic category further than the Torah-level category on which it is modeled.

### Yom Kvura Night Inference

The *gemara* now challenges whether Rabbi truly maintains that *aninus layla* is a Torah-level obligation. The question is sharpened: if the rabbinically enacted *yom kvura* encompasses the night, this implies Rabbi holds the Torah-ordained *yom misa* likewise encompasses the night.

### Braisa: Hayom vs. Layla

To test this assumption, the *gemara* cites a *braisa* rooted in Aharon's words at the time of the deaths of Nadav and Avihu. Aharon's statement is read as: "*hayom* — today I was forbidden, and at night (*layla*) I would have been permitted." This becomes the basis for examining whether *aninus layla* carries Torah-level force or is merely rabbinic.

### Rabbi Yehuda vs. Rabbi Sh…

The *braisa* records a dispute: Rabbi Yehuda holds that *aninus layla* is *doraisa* — even in future generations an *onein* may not eat a *korban* at night. Rabbi, by contrast, states explicitly: *aninus layla ein mi-divrei Torah ela mi-divrei sofrim* — the night dimension of *aninus* is only rabbinic in origin. This creates a tension with the earlier inference, which assumed Rabbi held *aninus layla* to be Torah-based.

### Yom Kvura Extension

The *gemara* resolves the tension. One might have thought that since *yom misa* is only a daytime prohibition at the Torah level, the rabbinic extension to *yom kvura* should likewise be limited to the daytime alone. The *gemara* answers: *le-olam drabbanan hi* — yes, *aninus layla* by *yom misa* is indeed only rabbinic. Even so, when the Sages extended *aninus* to encompass *yom kvura*, they went further than the Torah's own template. As the *gemara* states at the top of 101a: *ve-chachamim asu chizuk le-divreihem yoter mi-shel Torah* — the Sages reinforced their own enactments with even greater stringency than the Torah itself requires.

In summary: the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi (together with Rabbi Yose) concerns whether *aninus layla* by *yom misa* is *doraisa* or *drabbanan*. The separate question of *yom kvura* — universally acknowledged as rabbinic — is then extended into the night as well, representing a case where the rabbinic legislation exceeds the Torah's own standard.

## Nadav & Avihu Story

### Ki Chen Tzuveisi

The *gemara* now turns to the narrative surrounding the deaths of Nadav and Avihu and the burning of the *chatas*. The relevant *braisa* focuses on the phrase *ki chen tzuveisi* — "because I have been commanded" — which appears three times in the exchange between Moshe and Aharon. Each instance carries a distinct meaning.

The first occurrence, *ki chen tzuveisi ba-aninus yochluha*, indicates that Moshe told Aharon and his sons that they were commanded to eat the *korban* even though they were *onenim*. Moshe is saying: despite the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, God instructed me to tell you that you must eat the offering.

The second occurrence, *ka-asher tziveisi be-sha'at ma'aseh*, refers to the instruction Moshe conveyed at the time of the event itself. As Rashi explains — four lines from the top of his commentary — when the tragedy first occurred, Moshe told them that this day was singular, that God had revealed to him a distinction: *aninus hayom* in relation to *kodashim* differs from other days. He had been told explicitly that they could eat even as *onenim*. Subsequently, when Moshe discovered the *chatas* had been burned, he said: "You should have eaten it as I commanded you at the time — why did you not do so?"

The third occurrence, *ka-asher tziva Hashem*, represents Moshe's reaffirmation that this was not his own initiative but a Divine command — he was not speaking on his own authority.

Rashi's reading thus presents the following sequence: before the tragedy, Moshe conveys the license to eat as *onenim*; after discovering the burning, Moshe presses them by saying they should have followed what God had commanded through him at the time.

### Reconciling Sources

A contradiction now emerges. One source indicates that Moshe explicitly commanded them to eat even as *onenim* — meaning the license was given and the reason they did not eat must be something other than *aninus*. Yet another source states directly: *mipnei aninus nisrefah* — "it was burned on account of *aninus*." How can these two be reconciled?

### Shmuel's Resolution

Shmuel resolves the contradiction by identifying it as a dispute between Tannaim: the first source reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda (and Rabbi Shimon), while the second reflects Rabbi Nechemia.

### Aninus vs. Tumah Debate

The *braisa* makes the division explicit. Rabbi Nechemia holds *mipnei aninus serafuha* — they burned it because they were *onenim*. According to Rabbi Nechemia, Moshe had not conveyed to them a license to eat as *onenim*, and therefore the reason for the burning was simply their *aninus* status.

Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, both hold *mipnei tumah nisrefah* — it was burned due to ritual impurity. In their reading, Moshe had indeed given them a license to eat as *onenim*, which is why the *ki chen tzuveisi* verses explicitly mention that license. The burning, therefore, must be attributed to some entirely separate cause — *tumah* — unconnected to the deaths of Aharon's sons.

### Three Challenges to Anin…

The *gemara* presents three arguments on behalf of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon's position — three reasons why the burning cannot be attributed to *aninus*:

The first: *she-im atah omer mipnei aninus nisrefah hayu li-shloshan she-yisrefuha* — if the reason was *aninus*, all three *chatasot* should have been burned. As will be explained, there were three *chatas* offerings present: one associated with Nachshon, one from the eighth day of the *miluim*, and one for *Rosh Chodesh*. *Aninus* would have applied equally to all three, yet only one was burned. *Tumah*, by contrast, could have affected just one.

The second: *hayu ra'uy le-achlah la-erev* — if the problem was merely *aninus*, they could have waited and eaten the offering at night. Rashi notes this argument is more straightforwardly applicable according to Rabbi Shimon, who holds *aninus layla* is only rabbinic, in which case night would have been permitted. According to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *aninus layla* is *doraisa*, Rashi suggests there would have been a special exception in this particular case that would have permitted eating at night. Either way, *aninus* alone does not explain why they burned it outright rather than waiting.

The third: *v'lo Pinchas haya imahem* — Pinchas was present with them. Pinchas was not a close relative of Nadav and Avihu in the halakhically relevant sense — one is only an *onein* for seven relatives — and therefore he was not an *onein*. Had the sole obstacle been *aninus*, Pinchas could simply have eaten the *korban* himself. The fact that it was burned instead indicates the problem was something beyond *aninus* — namely, a *tumah* that rendered the offering unfit for anyone to eat.

## Kodshei Sha'ah vs. Dorot

### Rava's Explanation

Having established that Shmuel attributed the debate to Rabbi Nechemia on one side and Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon on the other, the *gemara* introduces Rava's alternative resolution. Rava proposes that *idei v'idei d'Rebbi Nechemia* — both *braisot* can in fact reflect the position of Rabbi Nechemia alone. There is no contradiction, because Rabbi Nechemia can maintain simultaneously that they had a license to eat as *onenim* and that the burning was due to *aninus*. The resolution lies in distinguishing between types of *korbanos*: *kodshei sha'ah* — offerings unique to that moment in history — and *kodshei dorot* — offerings that apply to all generations.

According to Rava's reading of Rabbi Nechemia, the license to eat as an *onein* applied exclusively to the *kodshei sha'ah*, the exceptional offerings brought only during that singular inaugural period. The *chatas* of *Rosh Chodesh*, however, was a standard offering applicable in all generations, and no special license extended to it. This immediately answers the first of the three challenges: only one *chatas* was burned — the *Rosh Chodesh* one — because only for that one did *aninus* remain operative. The other two were covered by the license.

### Three Chatasim Identified

The three *chatasot* referenced throughout this sugya are: the *seir* (goat) offering of Nachshon, the nasi of Yehuda, who brought the inaugural dedication offering on behalf of his tribe; the *chatas* of the eighth day of the *miluim*, the dedication of the Mishkan; and the *seir* of *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*. A *braisa* later in the discussion maps these onto the three words of the verse: *va-yidaresh darosh Moshe* — *va-yidaresh* refers to the *seir* of Nachshon, *chatat* refers to the *chatas* of the eighth day of the *miluim*, and *darosh* refers to the *seir* of *Rosh Chodesh*.

### Special vs. General Korb…

The conceptual divide is this: the *kodshei sha'ah* were offerings with no permanent counterpart in the ongoing sacrificial order. They existed as part of the singular, unrepeatable consecration of the Mishkan. For these, exceptional rules could be applied — including permitting an *onein* to partake. The *Rosh Chodesh* offering, however, was a paradigmatic *korban* of all generations, and no such special license could be extended to it.

## Moshe-Aharon Dialogue

### Nechemia's Reading

The *gemara* now asks how each school reads the actual *psukim* in Vayikra 10. Starting with Rabbi Nechemia's interpretation, the *gemara* reconstructs the implicit dialogue between Moshe and Aharon that underlies the verses. Moshe opens by asking Aharon: *madua lo achaltem* — why did you not eat the *chatas*?

### Blood & Kodesh Details

Moshe first probes whether there was a technical disqualification. He asks: *shema nichnas dama lifnai v'lifnim* — perhaps the blood of this *chatas* was brought into the inner sanctum, which would require the offering to be burned? Aharon answers: *hein lo huva dama* — no, the blood was not brought inside. Moshe then suggests the opposite: perhaps it was taken outside the boundaries of the Mishkan? Aharon answers: *ba-kodesh hayta* — it remained within the *kodesh*. Moshe then asks: *v'dilma ba-aninus hikravtem u-pesaltem otah* — perhaps you offered it while in a state of *aninus*, thereby disqualifying it? Aharon responds: *v'chi heim hikrivu* — did my sons bring it? *Ani hikravti* — I brought it. And a *kohen gadol* performs the *avodah* even as an *onein*.

This back-and-forth eliminates every possible ground for disqualification. The blood was not brought inside; it did not leave its proper area; it was not offered by an *onein* in a manner that would invalidate it. All the technical conditions were met.

### Aharon's Inference

Having cleared away all technical objections, Moshe then tells Aharon: *achol tochlu osah ka-asher tziveisi ba-aninus yochlu* — you should have eaten it as I commanded; eat it even in your state of *aninus*. Moshe is pressing: I gave you an explicit license to eat as *onenim*; why did you burn it?

At this point Aharon responds with his decisive argument: *hein hayom hikrivu et korbanam ve-et chatatam lifnei Hashem, va-tikrena oti ka-eleh, va-achalti chatat hayom ha-yitav be-einei Hashem?* — "Today my sons have died. Given that such a tragedy has befallen me, should I have eaten the *chatat hayom*? Would that have been good in God's eyes?"

Aharon's argument is substantive. He tells Moshe: perhaps your license was limited to *kodshei sha'ah*. I applied a *kal va-chomer*: if by *ma'aser*, which is a far less weighty matter than *korbanos*, the Torah states *lo achalti be-oni mimenu* — "I did not eat of it in my *aninus*" — then certainly with respect to *kodshei dorot*, offerings that apply for all generations, an *onein* should not eat. Therefore, even though you gave me a license, I understood that license to be restricted to the two exceptional *korbanos* of the *kodshei sha'ah*. For the *chatas* of *Rosh Chodesh*, which is a standard offering of all generations, I concluded that the license could not apply.

### Moshe's Concession

The *gemara* then states: *va-yishma Moshe va-yitav be-einav* — Moshe heard and it was good in his eyes. The *gemara* explains: *hoda v'lo bosh Moshe lomar lo shamati* — Moshe was not too proud to admit that he had not recalled this correctly. He acknowledged: *shamati v'shachachti* — I had heard this ruling and forgotten it. Aharon was right: the license applied only to the *kodshei sha'ah*, and the *Rosh Chodesh chatas* was correctly burned.

### Tumah Version

The *gemara* then presents how Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon read the same *psukim*. The initial exchange follows the same structure — Moshe asking whether the blood was brought inside, Aharon denying it, Moshe asking whether it left its boundary, Aharon denying that — but the final point of contention differs entirely.

In this version, once Moshe has eliminated the technical disqualifications, he presses a different challenge: *v'dilma agav meriraichu pashatem behu ve-itmu* — perhaps in your grief and bitterness you were negligent and the offering became *tamei*? Aharon responds with indignation: *kach ani be-einecha?* — Is that what you think of me? That I would show contempt for *kodshei shamayim*? *Va-tikrena oti ka-eleh, afilu ele ve-cha-eleh, eini mevaze kodshei shamayim* — even if tragedies far beyond this were to strike me, I would not fail in my protection of the *korbanos*. In other words, Aharon's defense here is not about *aninus* at all, but about rejecting any suggestion of negligence toward *tumah*. He is affirming that no matter the circumstances, he would not have permitted the offering to become *tamei* through carelessness.

There is a certain irony in this: Aharon ultimately defends himself against the accusation of negligence by saying he would never let *tumah* occur, and yet in the end *tumah* is precisely what happened. The resolution, as the *gemara* will clarify, is that the *tumah* came about through pure accident — *be-ones* — and was not the result of any failure on Aharon's part.

### Day vs. Night Alternative

The *gemara* then notes a parallel alternative reading within the Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon school. In this version, Aharon's pushback to Moshe's license is not about *kodshei sha'ah* versus *kodshei dorot*, but rather about day versus night. Aharon says to Moshe: *shema lo shamata ela ba-layla* — perhaps the license you received applied only to the night, not to the day. And during the day, he argues, a *kal va-chomer* from *ma'aser* applies: just as *ma'aser*, which is a lesser matter, is forbidden to an *onein*, how much more so *kodashim* during the day. Therefore, Aharon tells Moshe, I understood the license to mean: eat it at night, not during the day.

### Night Question & Tumah

The *gemara* immediately raises the obvious follow-up: *ibayalu li-shvuyei u-lemichlah be-orsah* — if that was Aharon's understanding, why didn't they simply wait and eat it at night? The answer is: *tumah be-ones basta* — an accidental *tumah* occurred to the offering, through no fault of anyone's, and therefore it had to be burned.

This version — the Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon approach — thus presents the following reconstruction: Moshe gave a license to eat as *onenim*; Aharon understood this license to apply only at night; they intended to eat it at night; but before they could do so, an accidental *tumah* rendered the one *chatas* unfit, and it was burned. The *aninus* remains part of the story, but it functions not as the cause of the burning but as the backdrop to the delay that led to the accidental *tumah*.

The *gemara* then shows how the words *hayom* and *hen hayom* are parsed differently by each school. According to Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, Aharon's statement *va-achalti chatat hayom* means: "Had I eaten this *chatat* during the day" — the word *hayom* specifies the time, and Aharon is explaining he chose not to eat during the day. According to Rabbi Nechemia, *chatat hayom* does not mean "the *chatat* today" but rather "the *chatat* of the day" — that is, the *Rosh Chodesh chatat*, the ordinary daily-type offering. Similarly, the phrase *hen hayom hikrivu* — according to Rabbi Nechemia — means: did they bring this offering? Implying that Aharon was clarifying that it was he, not his sons, who brought it, and he did so as a *kohen gadol* who is an *oveid onein*.

## Which Chatas Burned

### Three Sources Identified

The *gemara* now focuses on the three *chatasot* in detail, drawing on the *braisa* that expounds the verse *va-yidaresh darosh Moshe et se'ir ha-chatat* (Vayikra 10:16). The three words — *se'ir*, *chatat*, and *darosh* — are each taken as a reference to one of the three offerings: *se'ir* refers to the *seir* of Nachshon; *chatat* refers to the *chatas* of the eighth day of the *miluim*; and *darosh* refers to the *seir* of *Rosh Chodesh*.

### Singular Burned

The *gemara* proves from the text that only one of the three was burned. The verse states *v'hinei soraf* — "and behold it was burned" — using the singular, indicating that only one, not all three, was burned.

### Two Questions

Moshe's investigation, signaled by the double language *darosh darash* — two acts of searching — corresponds to two distinct questions. The first: *mipnei mah chatat zu nisrefah* — why was this one *chatas* burned? The second: *v'eilu munachos* — why are the other two still sitting there uneaten? In other words, Moshe is pressing not only on the burning of one, but also on the delay in eating the other two.

### Rosh Chodesh Burned

The *gemara* then asks: which of the three was the one that was burned? The verse elsewhere identifies the *Rosh Chodesh* offering with the phrase *l'chatat la-Hashem* — "as a *chatat* for God" — and on this basis the *gemara* concludes: *hevei omer zeh se'ir shel Rosh Chodesh* — it was the *seir* of *Rosh Chodesh* that was burned.

### Nechemia's Answer

Rabbi Nechemia's answer is now fully coherent: *aninus* disqualified only the *Rosh Chodesh chatas*, because that was a *korban* of all generations for which no special license had been granted. The other two — Nachshon's *seir* and the *chatas* of the eighth day of the *miluim* — were *kodshei sha'ah*, and for those, the license to eat as *onenim* applied. That is why only one was burned and not all three.

## Pinchas's Kohen Status

### Pinchas Challenge

The *gemara* returns to the second and third challenges against Rabbi Nechemia. We already saw that the question of why they did not wait until night is resolved by Rabbi Nechemia's position that *aninus layla* is *doraisa* — even at night, the *Rosh Chodesh chatas* could not be eaten. But the third challenge remains: why couldn't Pinchas have eaten it? If the problem was *aninus* applying to Aharon and his sons, Pinchas was not a close enough relative to be an *onein*. He should have been available to eat the *chatas* and thereby avoid burning it.

### Pinchas Becomes Kohen

The *gemara* answers that Rabbi Nechemia holds in accordance with the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who said in the name of Rabbi Chanina: *lo niskahein Pinchas ad she-harag l'Zimri* — Pinchas did not attain the status of *kohen* until he killed Zimri. This is a striking ruling. Although Pinchas was the son of Elazar ben Aharon and thus a direct patrilineal descendant of Aharon, there is a technical reason why he would not automatically have inherited *kehunah* at birth. The *kehunah* was conferred specifically on Aharon, his sons Elazar and Itamar, and Nadav and Avihu. Any children born to those sons after the *kehunah* was conferred would be born into *kehunah*. But Pinchas had already been born before the *kehunah* was given, and his father becoming a *kohen* did not retroactively make Pinchas one. Therefore, at the time of the deaths of Nadav and Avihu, Pinchas was not yet a *kohen* and could not have eaten from the *korbanos*.

### Peace Mission

Rav Ashi disagrees and pushes Pinchas's attainment of *kehunah* even later. According to Rav Ashi, Pinchas did not become a *kohen* until he made peace between the tribes. This refers to the episode after the entry into the land, when the tribes of Gad and Reuven erected an altar and the other tribes feared a schism was imminent. Pinchas led a diplomatic delegation and averted what might have become a civil war. According to Rav Ashi, it was only at that point — the fulfillment of the *brit shalom* in its fullest sense — that Pinchas truly attained the status of *kohen*.

The *gemara* notes the beauty of this reading: the *brit kehunat olam* began with an act of violence at the Zimri incident, yet its realization came through an act of peace-making. The warrior becomes the diplomat.

### Verse Evidence

The textual basis for Rav Ashi's position is the verse *va-yishma Pinchas ha-kohen u-nesi'ei ha-eidah* — "And Pinchas the *kohen* and the princes of the congregation heard." This is the first time in the Torah that Pinchas himself is called *ha-kohen*, rather than merely *Pinchas ben Elazar ben Aharon ha-kohen*, where the title *kohen* refers to Aharon rather than to Pinchas. The opponent of Rav Ashi's view points out that the verse *ve-haysah lo u-le-zaro acharav brit kehunat olam* already conferred *kehunah* in the Zimri story. Rav Ashi replies: that verse is a *berachah*, a promise and blessing for the future, which was only actualized at the later episode of the peace mission.

And conversely, to the challenge that the verse *va-yishma Pinchas ha-kohen* is used merely to establish the *yichus* — the genealogical pedigree — of the *kohanim gedolim* who would descend from Pinchas, Rav Ashi responds that the direct attribution of *kehunah* to Pinchas in that verse goes beyond mere lineage and marks the moment of his own entry into *kehunah*.

## Moshe's Kohen Status

### Moshe Kohen Gadol

The discussion of Pinchas's *kehunah* naturally raises the broader question of who else was present and might have eaten the *korban*. Rav makes a sweeping statement: *Moshe Rabbeinu Kohen Gadol hayah* — Moshe our teacher was a *kohen gadol*. Having served during the period of the *miluim* and performed the *avodah*, Moshe retained a status of *kehunah*. Rav further states that Moshe *ve-choleik be-kodshei shamayim hayah* — he had a portion in all the heavenly offerings. This is derived from the verse *me-eil ha-miluim le-Moshe hayah le-manah* — from the ram of the *miluim*, Moshe had a portion.

### Moshe Eating Challenge

This creates an immediate difficulty for the claim that the *chatas* had to be burned because the *kohanim* were *onenim*. If Moshe was a *kohen gadol* and he was not an *onein*, why couldn't he simply have eaten the *korban*? Just as the challenge was raised about Pinchas — *v'lav Pinchas imahem hayah*, wasn't Pinchas there with them — so too one might ask: *v'lav Moshe Rabbeinu hayah imahem*, wasn't Moshe Rabbeinu there with them?

### Moshe Occupied

The *gemara* answers: *de-tarid ba-Shechinah* — Moshe was completely preoccupied with the Divine Presence. He was constantly in communication with God, ascending and descending from *Har Sinai* first thing in the morning and returning first thing in the morning, as the *gemara* records: *Moshe be-hashkamah alah, be-hashkamah yarad*. Although this specific detail relates to the period of *Har Sinai*, the principle holds here as well: Moshe was entirely absorbed in his prophetic and administrative engagement with the Shechinah. It was simply not possible for them to hold the meat and rely on Moshe to eat it. He was unavailable.

### Ba'al Mum Eating

The *gemara* then cites a verse regarding a *ba'al mum* — a physically blemished *kohen* who may not perform the *avodah* but retains the right to eat *korbanos*: *lechem Elohav mi-kodshei ha-kodashim u-min ha-kodashim yokhal* — "from the most holy offerings and from the lesser holy offerings shall he eat." This verse establishes that a *ba'al mum* may eat both *kodshei kodashim* and *kodashim kalim*.

### Why Both Mentioned

A *braisa* raises the question of why the verse had to specify both categories. One might think that mentioning *kodshei kodashim* suffices, since it is the weightier category — and the same *kal va-chomer* would include *kodashim kalim*. Conversely, mentioning *kodashim kalim* alone might seem sufficient. The *braisa* explains: had the verse stated only *kodshei kodashim*, one might conclude that *kodshei kodashim* are permitted to the *ba'al mum* because they were once permitted to a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — but *kodashim kalim* might not be, since they were never similarly opened to a *zar*. And had it stated only *kodashim kalim*, one might conclude the *ba'al mum* may eat the lighter offerings but not the heavier *kodshei kodashim*. Therefore both had to be stated explicitly.

### Zar Eating Analysis

The *braisa*'s argument rests on the premise that *kodshei kodashim* were once permitted to a *zar*. The question is: which *zar* ate *kodshei kodashim*? The obvious candidate would be Moshe Rabbeinu during the period of the *miluim*. If Moshe was a *zar* — a non-*kohen* — and nonetheless ate *kodshei kodashim*, this would explain why *kodshei kodashim* have a certain precedent of openness beyond the strictly *kohen*-exclusive framework.

But this reading would suggest that Moshe was a *zar*, not a *kohen* — in direct contradiction to Rav's statement that Moshe was a *kohen gadol*. This is the *gemara*'s challenge: *katani mi-hat she-harei hutar le-zar v'lahem, lav Moshe* — the *braisa* implies it was a *zar* who ate *kodshei kodashim*, which would indicate Moshe was a *zar*, not a *kohen*.

### Rav Sheshet Clarifies

Rav Sheshet resolves the difficulty. He explains: *lo, ba-bamah le-zar* — the reference is not to Moshe at all, but to a *zar* eating at a *bamah*, a private altar used before the centralization of worship. The principle being invoked is: according to the opinion that a *minchah* may be brought at a *bamah*, such a *minchah* — which is normally *kodshei kodashim* and eaten exclusively by male *kohanim* — would be eaten by a *zar* at a *bamah*, since the laws of *bamah* operate differently. That is the precedent for *kodshei kodashim* having been permitted to a *zar*: not Moshe's eating, but the *zar* who eats a *minchah* at a *bamah*.

The reason the *minchah* specifically is invoked is as follows: *kodshei kodashim* at a *bamah* — what might that look like? An *olah* is burned entirely; a *chatat* and *asham* are obligatory offerings not brought at a private *bamah*. The only *kodshei kodashim* that could practically be brought as a voluntary *nedavah* at a *bamah* and be eaten there by a non-*kohen* would be a *minchah*. That is the case the *braisa* envisions. And as for *kodashim kalim* — there is no *kohen*'s portion of *kodashim kalim* that a *zar* ever ate at a *bamah*, which is why the verse needed to explicitly extend the *ba'al mum*'s permission to that category as well.

The question of whether Moshe retained *kohen* status will continue in the subsequent discussion.
