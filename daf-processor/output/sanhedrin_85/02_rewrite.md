# Sanhedrin 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wounding vs. Medical Care

### Wound Requirement

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion concerning *makei av ve'imo* — one who strikes a parent — and the requirement that the act produce a *chaburah*, a wound. The prohibition is not triggered by a mere blow; there must be actual physical injury.

### Yerushalmi: Permanent vs…

An interesting debate in the Yerushalmi concerns whether this wound requirement demands a *permanent* injury or whether any drawing of blood — even the minimal sort that would constitute a wound for purposes of *Shabbos* — suffices. From the Bavli's treatment of the subject, it is clear that the latter, more lenient threshold applies: any meaningful blood loss qualifies.

### Bavli: Medical Procedures

The Bavli raises a related and more practically charged question: what about drawing a parent's blood for medical purposes, such as therapeutic blood-letting, or accidentally causing bleeding while removing a thorn or splinter? At one point the Bavli seems to permit such procedures; yet elsewhere it records that certain *Amoraim* refused to allow their sons to perform even thorn removal on them, fearing that the son might inadvertently draw blood and thereby commit what would be a *shogeg* on an *issur sekilah*. If it is a third party rather than a parent, the concern is somewhat lesser — a *shogeg* on an *issur malkus* — but a concern nonetheless.

### Tosafos: Psik Reisha

This raises an immediate difficulty. If the son does not *intend* to draw blood, why should there be any concern at all? The general principle is that an *eino miskavein* — an unintended act — is not prohibited, provided the result is not a *definite* consequence. Tosafos addresses this directly: the cases under discussion must involve circumstances where the drawing of blood would be a *psik reisha* — a certain, inevitable outcome of the procedure being performed. Once the result is inevitable, the actor cannot claim lack of intent to shield himself from liability.

### Surgical Intent Question

Even granting the *psik reisha* analysis, one would have thought that the *context* of a medical procedure fundamentally redefines the nature of the act. The *havah amina* — the intuitive preliminary assumption — of the Gemara seems to be that performing a procedure for health purposes, or invoking the principle of *ve'ahavta l're'acha kamocha*, ought to remove the act entirely from the category of *makeh*. A physician who wounds in order to heal is not, conceptually, in the same category as one who wounds to harm.

### Third Party Surgery

What makes the Gemara's concern all the more striking is that it extends even to cases involving a *third party* — not a parent at all. Surely one cannot argue that surgery on a stranger is conceptually forbidden as an act of striking. The Gemara appears to grant that for a third party the context ought to reshape the legal category entirely; yet the concern persists, at least formally.

### Mekalkel and Tikkun

The Gemara resolves the related *Shabbos* dimension by invoking the concept of *mekalkel* — a destructive act that achieves no constructive purpose. For *Shabbos*, a prohibited labor (*melachah*) requires a *tikkun*, a constructive outcome; an act that is purely destructive (*mekalkel*) does not trigger the Shabbos prohibition. This is precisely why removing a splinter on Shabbos, even if blood is drawn, does not violate the Shabbos prohibition — the act is *mekalkel*, not constructive.

### Shabbat vs. Parent

However, this *mekalkel* reasoning cannot be imported into the domain of interpersonal injury. By a parent — or by any friend — the entire *problem* is precisely that one is injuring them, that one is *mekalkel* the other person's body. The *mekalkel* character of the act is not a defense; it is the very essence of the offense. Thus the resolution that works for *Shabbos* offers no relief in the context of striking a parent or friend.

### Rabbi Shimon: Wounding

The Gemara then raises Rabbi Shimon's position, which holds that wounding is inherently *mekalkel* — not because of contextual framing but by its very nature — and therefore would never constitute a forbidden *melachah* on *Shabbos* on those grounds. At most, it could be characterized as a *melachah she'einah tzerichah l'gufah* — a labor performed without need of the labor itself. The large *Tosafos* in *Hilchos Shabbos* works through the implications of *mekalkel* and *melachah she'einah tzerichah l'gufah* according to Rabbi Shimon's view, though those details are peripheral to our immediate topic.

### Strange Conclusion

The Gemara's ultimate conclusion is genuinely strange: by *Shabbos*, the act is permissible because it is either *mekalkel* or *eino miskavein* — unintended — and *Shabbos* has specific requirements of intent and constructive purpose. But with respect to a parent, or even with respect to a friend, the act fundamentally remains problematic. The *Rif* records the same ruling, noting that the *Amoraim* would not allow their sons to remove thorns because of the risk of drawing blood — without offering a further explanation. One would have expected that context of medical care or repair would entirely preclude the category of *makeh*; yet the Gemara declines to draw that conclusion.

## Son as Shaliach Beis Din

### Rav Sheshes' Question

The Gemara now turns to a question posed to Rav Sheshes: what if the *beis din* were to appoint a son as its *shaliach* — its agent — to administer lashes to a father who has been condemned, or to pronounce a *cherem* upon him? This question was raised earlier in the daf as a hypothetical; here it surfaces as a formal legal inquiry. In the case of cursing, one would need to assess whether the precise formulation used in a rabbinically administered *cherem* would even technically constitute the biblical prohibition of a son cursing a father — a question that depends on the separate debate regarding whether *shem hameforash* is required. But putting those details aside, the core question is: does court appointment provide the son legal cover to perform acts that would otherwise render him liable?

### General vs. Specific Lic…

Rav Sheshes' interlocutor first challenges the premise: since when does *anyone* have permission to hit or curse a fellow Jew? The general prohibition against striking and cursing another person applies universally. The answer is that the agent of the court operates under a specific mandate — the court's appointment provides the necessary license. For a normal person acting as *shaliach beis din*, this license is real and operative. The more interesting question — and the one the Gemara is really pressing — is whether such a license would function when the agent is the *son* of the condemned, and whether *beis din* appointment could, in principle or in fact, grant the son that standing. One might frame the distinction not merely as a question of whether the court *would* make such an appointment, but whether such an appointment would be halachically effective — whether the halacha could ever recognize the son in that role vis-à-vis his own father.

### Kvod Shamayim Override

The Gemara's answer invokes *kvod shamayim* — the honor of Heaven. Just as a normal person may perform acts in defense of Heaven's honor that would otherwise be prohibited (the Gemara alludes to the principle of *ein chochma ve'ein tevuna ve'ein eitza neged Hashem*, and to related contexts such as *kana'in pog'in bo*), so too the son, when acting in the capacity of defending Heaven's honor through his court mandate, may be permitted. This framing is noteworthy. Rather than grounding the permission in the formal legal mechanism of *shelichut* — agency — the Gemara frames it in terms of *purpose*: what is the actor trying to accomplish? This parallels the earlier discussion about medical procedures, where the Gemara likewise focused on the purposive context of the act. Equally noteworthy is that the Gemara draws no distinction between a son and a third party in this framework: just as a stranger may act for *kvod shamayim*, so may a son.

### Mechilta Derivation

The Gemara then raises a challenge from a *Mechilta*. The *Mechilta* addresses the question of how we know there is a prohibition against hitting at all — specifically, against hitting a parent. The source it identifies is the verse *lo yosif lehakoso maka raba* — the court-appointed lash-giver is commanded not to give extra lashes beyond the prescribed amount. From this, the *Mechilta* reasons: if even one who has explicit license to administer lashes is commanded not to administer *extra* lashes, how much more so is someone who had no license to strike in the first place absolutely prohibited from doing so?

### Mechilta Interpretation

The initial reading of the *Mechilta* suggests a specific application: the person who is "commanded to give lashes" refers to the general *shaliach beis din*, while "the person who is never commanded to give lashes" refers specifically to a son vis-à-vis his father — someone who would never be assigned that role. This would directly answer Rav Sheshes' question: a son is never given the court's license to strike a father, and therefore remains under an absolute prohibition.

### General Prohibition

The Gemara rejects this reading. *Aidei va'aidei lo shna b'no v'lo shna acher* — the *Mechilta* is not drawing a distinction between a son and a third party at all. Rather, it is establishing the *general* prohibition against hitting any fellow Jew. The reasoning is straightforward: when a court-sanctioned lash-giver may not give extra strikes, we derive by *kal va'chomer* that one with no such sanction at all may not strike anyone. The relevant distinction is not son versus stranger, but authorized strike versus unauthorized strike. *Kan b'makom mitzvah, kan shelo b'makom mitzvah* — the distinction is between a case where there is a *mitzvah* to administer lashes and one where there is none.

Parenthetically, this is worth noting: the same generic prohibition that forbids any Jew from hitting another Jew — derived from *lo yosif lehakoso* — also serves as the source for the specific prohibition against a child striking a parent. The two are grounded in the same textual derivation.

## Execution & Gavra Katila

### Father at Execution

The Gemara introduces a new case: a father is being led out to execution — *hayotzei leihareg* — and his son comes and strikes him, and also curses him. The son's motivation is understandable: he may wish to publicly dissociate from his father's crime, or the sin may have been perpetrated against the family itself. Regardless of motivation, the ruling is that the son is *chayav* — liable.

### Third Party Exempt

By contrast, if a third party — a stranger — strikes and curses this condemned man, he is *patur* — exempt. Even though striking and cursing a fellow Jew would ordinarily be prohibited and punishable (by lashes or monetary compensation, depending on the circumstances), in this case the stranger incurs no liability.

### Son vs. Third Party

This asymmetry demands explanation. If the condemned man has lost some legal status — if his impending execution changes his standing in any significant way — why should that change affect the third party but not the son? And conversely, if the condemned man retains sufficient legal standing to protect him from a stranger's blow, why should the son not benefit from the same protection?

### Rav Chisda: Resistance

Rav Chisda offers the first answer. The case under discussion is one where the condemned man is *mesarvin bo latzeit* — actively resisting, refusing to cooperate in being led to his execution. He is grabbing onto things, fighting back, making the process impossible. The court's agents need to use force to move him, and in the course of doing so they may strike him and possibly shout at him in terms that technically constitute a *kelalah*. A stranger acting as a court agent has explicit license to use such force. The son, however, is never assigned that agency role — and therefore lacks the license. This neatly answers our earlier question: the son cannot function as the court's agent in such matters, and that is precisely what distinguishes him from a third party here.

### Rav Sheshes: No Resistan…

Rav Sheshes, however, re-reads the case. He interprets it as involving a situation where there is *no* resistance — the condemned man is cooperating fully and is being led quietly to his execution. In that scenario, there is no court mandate to use force, and the third party is not acting as a court agent. So why, on Rav Sheshes' reading, is the stranger *patur*? The answer must be something more fundamental about the condemned man's legal status itself.

Rav Sheshes invokes the concept of *gavra katila* — "a dead man walking." The condemned man is legally regarded as already dead, and this status fundamentally alters the legal consequences of acts committed against him. Just as a pot hurtling toward the ground has, in some sense, lost its value before it even reaches the floor (an analogy the Gemara in *Bava Kamma* explores), so too a man hurtling toward execution may have lost the legal protections normally afforded a living body.

### Exemption from Payment

But the Gemara pauses before fully accepting this. If the person is *gavra katila*, one might exempt the third party from *nezek* — physical damage — since there is no longer monetary value in a body about to be destroyed. But what of *boshet* — embarrassment — and *tza'ar* — pain? Those are real and present even if the body is about to be forfeited. Rav Sheshes himself elsewhere rules that if one embarrasses a person who is sleeping and the person then dies before waking up, the perpetrator is still *chayav* to pay *boshet* — because the embarrassment falls upon the family. Here, too, the condemned man is alive and does experience pain and shame; the family remains present to collect. So the *gavra katila* status does not automatically eliminate all monetary exposure.

### Minor Injuries & Lashes

The Gemara then focuses the discussion. Rabbi Ami says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol hakah she'ein bah shaveh prutah lokeh* — any blow that causes less than a *perutah*'s worth of damage results in *malkus* rather than monetary payment, since there is nothing to compensate. This raises the question: if the third party is *patur*, and the act was a blow causing less than a *perutah*'s worth of damage, should the stranger not receive lashes for the underlying prohibition of striking a fellow Jew?

### Damages vs. Transgression

The Gemara clarifies: *mai patur dekamar patur mimamon* — when the text says the third party is *patur*, it means *patur* from monetary payment. This seems like a surprisingly deflated reading of "exempt," but the Gemara's apparent logic is as follows: *boshet* and *tza'ar* may well be payable; *nezek* — physical damage — is not, because the body has, in the relevant legal sense, no ongoing monetary value. This parallels the dropped-pot scenario: damage to something already in the process of destruction has no compensable value.

But this raises an acute problem: if the stranger is merely exempt from *mamon* — monetary payment — while the son is described as *chayav*, does this mean the son is *chayav b'mamon*? That seems absurd — if the body has no monetary value for the third party, why would it have monetary value for the son? The Gemara resolves this: *ella b'dino* — when the son is called *chayav*, it means *chayav misa*, liable to the death penalty for striking his father. The stranger is *patur* not merely from money but from *malkus* — and the reason requires further elaboration.

### Oseh Ma'aseh Amcha

The Gemara now introduces the governing principle. The stranger is *patur* because the verse states *ve'nasi b'amcha lo ta'or* — do not curse a *nasi* among your people — from which is derived the general prohibition against cursing any Jew. The operative word is *b'amcha* — "among your people," which the Gemara reads as *b'oseh ma'aseh amcha* — one who acts in accordance with the conduct of your people. This condemned man, having committed a sin severe enough to warrant execution, is no longer within the category of *oseh ma'aseh amcha*. The prohibitions against cursing and striking, which are grounded in the shared community of Israel, no longer protect him.

This principle raises deep questions about its scope and application. What degree of transgression removes a person from the category of *oseh ma'aseh amcha*? Is it limited to someone literally being led to execution — *mekuyam she'b'amcha*, in the narrower formulation of Rav Mari, meaning someone who has lost their legal right to remain alive — or does it extend to someone who is simply not observant? These questions will prove consequential.

With respect to *kelalah* — cursing — the exemption flows directly from the *nasi b'amcha* verse. With respect to *haka'ah* — striking — the Gemara derives the parallel exemption by way of analogy: *d'akish haka'ah l'kelalah*, hitting is linked to cursing. The two acts — verbal and physical assault — stand or fall together when the question is whether the victim falls within the protected community.

### Death & Cursing

But if the justification for exempting the stranger is that it is "as if" the condemned man is already dead — and Rav Sheshes' entire framework rests on this — then one must ask: does the same not apply to the son? The Gemara cites the upcoming *mishnah*, which teaches that cursing a parent *after death* remains prohibited and that the son is *chayav*. If the condemned father is legally "as if dead," the stranger is exempt because it is as if he is cursing a dead man — a dead man not being under the protection of *nasi b'amcha*. But the son remains *chayav* for cursing even a genuinely dead parent. The *gavra katila* equivalence to death therefore does not help the son: for the son, the prohibition against cursing and striking a parent extends even into death.

The Gemara attempts an answer: perhaps the father did *teshuvah*, in which case he reverts to the status of *oseh ma'aseh amcha* and the stranger would also be prohibited. But this creates the inverse problem — if the father did *teshuvah*, why is the stranger exempt? Any answer that works globally to explain the stranger's exemption undoes the son's liability, and any answer that explains the son's liability seems to pull the stranger back in. The Gemara cycles through these possibilities.

### Blood = Nefesh

Rashi provides the critical resolution regarding the hitting question. The *mishnah* states that striking a parent *after death* is *patur*. If the condemned father is legally treated as already dead, why is the son *chayav* for striking him? Rashi explains: the reason striking a parent after death is *patur* is specifically because the definition of a punishable strike requires *chaburah* — a wound — and a wound is defined by the drawing of blood. Blood represents *nefesh*, the life force. After genuine death, blood drawn from the body no longer carries that quality of *nefesh* — it is no longer "living blood." Therefore, there is no *chaburah* in the halachic sense, and no liability.

### Son Still Liable

Here, however, the condemned father is still biologically alive. The characterization of *gavra katila* is a legal fiction for certain purposes — but it does not change the physiological reality. The blood still pumps. Drawing blood from this man still constitutes a *chaburah* in the full halachic sense, because it is still taking living blood — *nefesh* — from a living body. The legal fictions of death that exempt the stranger from the general prohibitions of *nasi b'amcha* do not dissolve the biological reality that makes the son's act a genuine *chaburah*. The son therefore remains liable for both striking and cursing his condemned father: for cursing, because the prohibition applies even to a dead parent; for striking, because the strike is still a *chaburah* in the full sense since the blood is still biologically alive.

Rashi states this explicitly: *hayotzei leihareg it beh chaburah dechai hu ela desofo lamus* — the one being led to execution still has the quality of *chaburah* because he is alive; it is only that his end is death.

The conclusion, then, is two-fold. One reading of this entire passage — the reading based on Rav Chisda's approach, where the condemned is *resisting* — yields a clean answer to our earlier question: a son cannot serve as the court's agent to strike or curse his father, even under explicit mandate. The court's appointment simply does not create operative *shelichut* for a son in this domain. The other reading — Rav Sheshes' approach, where there is no resistance — yields a more complex picture grounded in the *gavra katila* status and the son's unique continuing obligation toward a parent that survives even after death.

Finally, the Gemara records: *amar Rabba bar Rav Huna vechen tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael — lechol ein haben na'aseh shaliach l'aviv l'hakoto ul'kallelo*, chutz m'meisit. In general, a son cannot be made a court agent to strike or curse his father — except in the case of a *meisit*, one who seduces others to idolatry, regarding whom the Torah says *lo tachmol velo techaseh alav* — show no compassion, do not cover for him. In that case there is such an overriding mandate to eradicate the threat that even the son could legitimately fill that role. This implies that where the son genuinely serves as a recognized court agent — as in the *meisit* case — he would indeed be exempt. The general rule is simply that in virtually all other cases, the son will never be a recognized agent of the court for such purposes against his own father.

A parenthetical observation: the question of *oseh ma'aseh amcha* and how severely a parent's conduct must compromise their standing before it affects the obligations of *kibbud av va'em* and *shiva* is not merely theoretical. Some authorities have applied the *rasha* category to contemporary situations — for instance, ruling that a child need not sit *shiva* for a parent who was not *shomer Shabbos*. This application is highly questionable given the concept of *tinok shenishba* and the need to redefine who falls into the category of *rasha* in the modern context. A more straightforward approach to these situations is grounded in the general principle of *chayecha vechayei chaveirecha chayecha kodmin* — your own life takes precedence. Even within the framework of *kibbud av va'em*, the *poskim* note that while a child is expected to endure a remarkable degree of personal humiliation (as illustrated by the famous story of a mother publicly humiliating a rabbi who remained silent), this applies after the fact; a child is permitted — and perhaps obligated — to protect himself *in advance* from harm, even if doing so would cause embarrassment to a parent. The prior obligation to oneself is operative even within the parent-child relationship.

## Mishna Distinctions

### Mishna Overview

The *mishnah* now states explicitly what has been discussed throughout: *hamakeh aviv ve'imo eino chayav ad she'ya'aseh bahem chaburah* — one who strikes a parent is not liable unless he produces a wound. It then notes: *ze chomer bimekalel mibemakeh* — this is a stringency of cursing over striking. *Shehamekalel le'achar mita chayav, vehamakeh le'achar mita patur* — if one curses a parent after the parent's death, one is liable; if one strikes a parent after death, one is exempt, for the reasons Rashi explained: striking requires *chaburah*, and a wound requires living blood.

### Textual Excess

The Gemara now explains the source for the rule that cursing applies even after a parent's death. The verse in *Vayikra* (20:9) reads: *ki ish ish asher yekalel et aviv ve'et imo mot yumat aviv ve'imo kilel damav bo*. The verse concludes with a repetition — *aviv ve'imo kilel* — that appears redundant: we already know from the earlier part of the verse that one who curses a parent is put to death. What does this repetition add? The answer is: it extends the prohibition *le'achar mita* — even after the parent's death.

### Inferential Logic

The Gemara builds the inferential logic as follows. We know that striking a parent — *haka'ah* — is limited to cases where the parent is alive; the nature of the act requires a living body. One might therefore reason: cursing, too, should be limited to a living parent. Indeed, if anything, cursing should be *narrower* than striking in certain respects, since striking applies regardless of whether the parent is an *oseh ma'aseh amcha*, whereas cursing is limited by the *nasi b'amcha* requirement. Since striking — the apparently broader prohibition — does not apply after death, perhaps cursing — in certain ways the narrower prohibition — should also not apply after death.

### Textual Override

The Gemara rejects this inference. The extra repetition of *aviv ve'imo kilel* overrides the logical deduction. Precisely because one might have argued *kal va'chomer* that if striking does not apply after death, cursing — more limited in some ways — certainly should not, the Torah repeats the phrase to teach that cursing does apply after death. The logical inference is overridden by the explicit textual expansion.

### Rabbi Yoshiyah: Either P…

But wait — can the repetition of *aviv ve'imo kilel* simply be deployed for the *le'achar mita* derivation? This depends on whether the verse is "available" for that purpose. The Gemara notes that according to Rabbi Yoshiyah, the repetition is not available, because Rabbi Yoshiyah uses it for something else entirely. Rabbi Yoshiyah holds: when the verse says *asher yekalel et aviv ve'et imo*, one might have thought that the son is only liable if he curses *both* parents simultaneously. The repetition of *aviv ve'imo kilel* teaches otherwise — *aviv kilel* is enough, and *imo kilel* is enough — either parent alone suffices to incur liability.

### Rabbi Yonatan: Both Pare…

Rabbi Yonatan disagrees. He holds that a conjunctive *vav* can be read as either "and both" or "or either one," depending on context. Unless the text explicitly says *yachdav* — together (as in *sha'atnez*, where the Torah specifies *tzemer u'fishtim yachdav*) — the default reading is that either one suffices. Therefore, no special repetition is needed to teach that cursing one parent alone is sufficient. The repetition of *aviv ve'imo kilel* remains "available" according to Rabbi Yonatan, who uses it to teach the *le'achar mita* extension.

### After-Death Derivation

According to Rabbi Yoshiyah, who has used the repetition for the "either parent" derivation, where does the *le'achar mita* extension come from?

### Oseh Ma'aseh Amcha Intro

The *braita* in the course of this discussion also passes through the *oseh ma'aseh amcha* principle — the limitation that cursing applies only to one who acts as a member of the covenant community. This emerges as a kind of byproduct of the textual analysis, but it will become central in the subsequent discussion.

### Verse Allocation

The Gemara resolves the question of textual economy as follows. Rabbi Yoshiyah derives the *le'achar mita* extension from a different verse — the verse in *Shemot*, which states independently: *mekalel aviv ve'imo mot yumat*. This verse, which appears separately from the main passage in *Vayikra*, is available to teach the *le'achar mita* application. Meanwhile, the verse in *Vayikra* is, for Rabbi Yoshiyah, deployed to teach that each parent individually suffices. For Rabbi Yonatan, the *Shemot* verse is used to teach that daughters, *tumtum*, and *androgynos* are included among those who can incur liability — since no gender-specific language appears in that verse. And the repetition in *Vayikra* — *aviv ve'imo kilel* — teaches the *le'achar mita* extension. The Gemara thus works through the allocation of verses across the various derivations, ensuring that each *tanna* has a coherent source for each rule.

## Mishna Distinctions…

### Mishna Silence

A *braita* presses a question: if hitting is, in one dimension, *broader* than cursing — specifically because hitting applies regardless of whether the parent is an *oseh ma'aseh amcha*, whereas cursing does not — why does the *mishnah* fail to state this as a distinct ruling? The *mishnah* notes that cursing is more stringent than hitting in the dimension of *le'achar mita*. But it says nothing about hitting being more stringent than cursing in the dimension of *oseh ma'aseh amcha*. Why the asymmetric presentation?

### Linking Makeh & Kalal

The Gemara answers: the author of the *mishnah* holds *makkishin hakah l'kelalah* — hitting and cursing are linked by analogy. Since the general prohibition against cursing is bounded by *b'amcha* — *nasi b'amcha lo ta'or* — and since the prohibition against hitting is derived by linking it to cursing, both prohibitions share the same scope. Just as cursing applies only when the victim is an *oseh ma'aseh amcha*, so too hitting applies only when the victim is an *oseh ma'aseh amcha*. The two are not distinct in this dimension; therefore there is no asymmetry to report, and the *mishnah* is silent on this point because its author sees the two prohibitions as coextensive in scope.

## Oseh Ma'aseh Amcha Debate

### Kuti Braita 1

The Gemara now brings two conflicting *braitot* concerning a Samaritan — a *Kuti*. The first *braita* teaches: *Kuti atah metzuveh al haka'ato ve'ei atah metzuveh al kilelato* — you are prohibited against striking a Samaritan, but you are not prohibited against cursing him. Hitting remains forbidden; cursing does not.

### Kuti Braita 2

The second *braita* teaches the opposite allocation: *ei atah metzuveh lo al kilelato velo al haka'ato* — you are not prohibited against either cursing or striking a Samaritan. Both acts are permitted.

### Gerim Debate

The initial assumption is that this debate turns on a factual question about the status of Samaritans: are they *geirei emet* — genuine converts whose conversion was valid — or *geirei arayot* — "converts of the lions," whose forebears converted under duress out of fear of lions (as described in *Melachim II*) and whose Jewishness is therefore questionable? If they are *geirei emet*, they are Jews, and the prohibitions of striking and cursing would be operative — at least in principle. If they are *geirei arayot*, they are not Jews, and those prohibitions would not apply.

### Why Distinguish?

Even granting that the two *braitot* debate their Jewish status, it remains puzzling why the first *braita* would distinguish between hitting (prohibited) and cursing (not prohibited). If they are genuinely Jewish, both should be prohibited; if not, neither should be. What would justify the asymmetric result? The answer, one might suggest, lies in the nature of the two acts. Cursing — public denunciation — may serve a social purpose, reinforcing communal norms and boundaries, and therefore even if the Samaritan is halakhically Jewish, publicly condemning someone who does not follow rabbinic law may be permissible or even warranted as an expression of communal standards. Physical injury, however, cannot be justified on such grounds: regardless of a person's level of observance, inflicting bodily harm remains a moral and legal transgression in a way that verbal condemnation does not.

### Shor Evidence

The Gemara refutes the initial assumption. The *braita* itself indicates that both *braitot* treat Samaritans as halakhically Jewish: *hainu dekatni alah shor keyisrael* — the *braita* concludes that the Samaritan's ox is treated like a Jewish ox for purposes of tort law. Under the *mishnah* in *Bava Kamma*, a non-Jewish ox that gores a Jewish ox is liable for full damages, while a Jewish ox that gores a non-Jewish ox is entirely exempt — because the non-Jewish ox lacks the legal standing of its Jewish counterpart. If the Samaritan's ox is treated as a Jewish ox, it means both *braitot* assume that Samaritans are considered Jews. The debate between them cannot therefore be about whether they are *geirei emet* or *geirei arayot*.

### Real Dispute

The true dispute, the Gemara concludes, is not about the Samaritans' Jewish status — both *braitot* agree they are halakhically Jewish — but about the scope of the *oseh ma'aseh amcha* limitation and whether it applies equally to hitting and cursing.

### Linked View

One view holds *makkishin hakah l'kelalah* — hitting and cursing are linked. Since Samaritans, though Jewish, are not *oseh ma'aseh amcha* in the relevant sense (they follow different laws and belong to a distinct communal group), the *nasi b'amcha* limitation exempts from cursing — and since hitting is linked to cursing, it also exempts from hitting. Both acts are therefore permitted.

### Unlinked View

The opposing view holds that hitting and cursing are not linked in this respect. The exemption from cursing flows from the specific language of *b'amcha* in the cursing verse, which limits the prohibition to those who act as genuine members of the covenant community. But hitting does not share that textual limitation; its prohibition is grounded in a different source, and there is no reason to extend the *b'amcha* qualification to it. Physical injury therefore remains prohibited regardless of whether the victim is *oseh ma'aseh amcha*.

### Cursing Non-Observant

The principle now crystallizes: cursing a Jew who is not *oseh ma'aseh amcha* does not transgress the prohibition of *nasi b'amcha lo ta'or*. The person has placed themselves, through their conduct, outside the scope of that protection.

### Parent Application

The same principle extends to parents. If a parent is not *oseh ma'aseh amcha*, cursing that parent does not trigger liability under the *nasi b'amcha* verse. Whether striking such a parent is also exempt depends on the debate just outlined: those who link hitting and cursing would say yes, those who do not link them would say no.

### Logical Question

This generates a striking conclusion when read against the opening discussion of the daf. There, we found that even performing a medical procedure on a parent — even removing a thorn — was treated by the Gemara as problematic because of the potential for blood-drawing, and the rabbis in question would not allow their sons to perform such procedures. Yet here, at the close of the daf, we arrive at the position that if the parent is not *oseh ma'aseh amcha*, the prohibition against cursing that parent may not apply at all — and the prohibition against striking may also not apply, depending on one's view. The scope of these prohibitions, so severe at their core that they reach into medical procedures and past death itself, is nonetheless bounded by the community of observant practice in ways that are genuinely consequential.
