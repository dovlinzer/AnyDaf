# Berakhot 19 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## 24 Places of Niddui

### R. Yehoshua ben Levi

The daf opens approximately five lines down on the wide column of 19a. The preceding context had involved a discussion of how to behave in a cemetery — whether *tzitzis* should drag on the floor and whether the deceased is aware of what transpires — which led into a pair of *aggadic* passages about speaking ill of *talmidei chachamim* after their deaths, including the story of someone who spoke disparagingly of Shmuel and was struck by a falling beam. With that backdrop, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi now makes the following statement: *b'esrim v'arba mekomos beis din menadin al kvod harav* — in twenty-four places, *beis din* will place someone in *niddui*, a form of excommunication, on account of disrespect shown to a *rav*. Moreover, all twenty-four of these cases can be found within the *Mishnah*.

When Rabbi Elazar asks where these twenty-four cases appear, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi responds simply: go figure it out yourself. Rabbi Elazar did precisely that — *nafak dak v'ashkach tlas* — he searched carefully and found three.

### Three Explicit Cases

The three cases Rabbi Elazar uncovered are: (1) one who denigrates *netilas yadayim*, hand-washing — *hamizulzel b'netilas yadayim*; (2) one who speaks disparagingly after the questionable conduct of *talmidei chachamim* — *hamespaper acharei mitasan shel talmidei chachamim*, where *mitasan* here does not carry its literal meaning but rather refers to a kind of divergence or deviation from the proper path; and (3) one who acts with arrogance or entitlement toward God — *hamigis da'ato klapei ma'alah*.

### Akavya ben Mahalalel

The *Gemara* addresses the second case — speaking ill of *talmidei chachamim* — somewhat out of order, since it was the subject most recently discussed. The proof text is a *Mishnah* in *Eduyos* involving Akavya ben Mahalalel. Akavya used to say: *im ein m'shakin lo es hageyores v'lo es hamshuchreres* — a woman who is a convert or a freed slave cannot be made to drink the *sotah* waters, because the verse uses the phrase *B'nei Yisrael*, which he interpreted narrowly to exclude converts and freed slaves. Rashi explains that *B'nei Yisrael* in this context refers not necessarily to the woman herself but potentially to her husband — meaning a woman married to a convert or freed slave would not undergo the *sotah* ritual. The precise scope of the exclusion involves some interpretive complexity.

The *chachamim* disagreed and held that she may drink. They cited a precedent: was there not the story of Karkamis, a freed slave woman in Jerusalem, whom Shemaya and Avtalyon ruled must drink? Akavya responded: *dugma hishkuha* — they only gave her a kind of facsimile, a mock procedure. This response is read two ways. *Tosafos* suggests the procedure was performed with plain water, effectively a placebo, which raises its own questions: why do it at all, and could a placebo even produce the *sotah* effect if she were guilty? Rashi reads it differently: because Shemaya and Avtalyon were themselves converts (*gerim*), they were personally invested in permitting the procedure, and therefore their ruling was partial and should not be relied upon.

Either way, Akavya was making an unflattering claim about Shemaya and Avtalyon — either that they fabricated a procedure, or that they ruled improperly due to personal bias. As a result, *veniduhu* — they placed him in *niddui*. *Umes beniduyo* — he died while still under that *niddui*, never having recanted. Consequently, *vesakal beis din es arono* — *beis din* placed a stone on his coffin, which is understood as a virtual act of stoning, a particularly harsh expression of censure.

One might ask whether it is ever legitimate to criticize a rabbi's conduct. The *Gemara* had previously indicated that even if a sage was seen to act improperly, one should assume he has done *teshuva*. Here, the deeper issue is that Akavya interpreted what happened in a way that reflected badly on Shemaya and Avtalyon — and from the perspective of the *chachamim*, that interpretation was tendentious. The *chachamim* felt they had to defend Shemaya and Avtalyon's honor. The *Gemara* itself does not hesitate to criticize where genuine criticism is warranted, but the concern here is that Akavya was reading the events in a way that served his own legal position while disparaging his predecessors.

Rabbi Yehudah, in response to this account, says: *chas veshalom she'Akavya ben Mahalalel nisnadah* — God forbid that someone of Akavya's stature was actually placed in *niddui*. When the Temple courtyard was sealed shut on Pesach and all of Israel gathered, no one among all of them equaled Akavya ben Mahalalel in wisdom, *taharah*, and *yirat chet*. He could not have said such a thing; it could not have been him. *Ela mi nihu?* — Who then was it? The answer is Elazar ben Chanoch.

### Elazar ben Chanoch

Elazar ben Chanoch *she-pikpek b'netilas yadayim* — he treated hand-washing with contempt, dismissing it as unworthy of observance. For this he was placed in *niddui*. When he died, *shalchu beis din v'henichu even gedolah al arono* — *beis din* sent word and placed a large stone on his coffin. The *Gemara* derives from this that whoever is placed in *cherem* and dies in that state, *beis din* stones his coffin.

This case makes clear that the issue is not merely violating a rabbinic law — everyone violates rabbinic laws from time to time. The problem is *zilzul*, a dismissive attitude, a posture of "why should I bother, it is only rabbinic." That kind of fundamental disrespect for rabbinic authority is what merits *niddui*. Two paradigms now emerge for how one can show disrespect to the *rabbis*: making a disparaging claim about specific rabbis' conduct, and treating a rabbinic enactment as beneath consideration.

### Choni HaMa'agel

The third case — *hamigis da'ato klapei ma'alah*, acting with haughtiness toward God — is illustrated by the famous story of Choni HaMa'agel, the circle-drawer. When Choni prayed for rain and received only a drizzle, he declared that was not what he wanted; when it came in torrents, he declared that was too much; he drew a circle and announced he would not step out until God provided exactly what was needed. The *Gemara* records that Shimon ben Shetach sent him this message: *tzarich atah le-hitnadot* — you deserve to be placed in *cherem*. *Ve-ilmalei Choni atah gozrani alecha nidui* — were you not Choni, I would excommunicate you. But *mah e'eseh she-ata mitchateh lifnei ha-Makom* — what can I do? You behave insolently before God, and He loves you so much that He acts on your wishes nonetheless. *K'ven ha-mitchateh lifnei aviv* — like a child who misbehaves and whose father still indulges him. *Yismach avicha ve-imecha ve-tagel yoledtecha* — your father and mother will rejoice over you.

It is notable that Shimon ben Shetach ends with a verse of praise even while declaring Choni deserving of *cherem*. Rashi understands *mitchateh* as a kind of naughtiness or stepping over the line before God. The dominant quality being illustrated here is less arrogance in the classic sense and more a kind of *entitlement* — acting as though one has a personal claim on God that overrides normal boundaries. The conceptual move then is that this same quality of overstepping toward God serves as an analogical model for overstepping toward rabbinic authority.

### Tudos of Rome

An additional case is introduced: *d'tanya Rav Yosef Tudos Ish Romi hinhig es b'nei Romi le'echol gedayim mekulasim b'leilei Pesachim* — Tudos of Rome established a practice among the Jews of Rome to eat whole roasted goats — roasted entire on a spit — on Seder night. The background to this requires some orientation. The *Mishnah* in the chapter *Makom She'nahagu* records that in places where the custom was to eat roasted meat on Seder night, they ate. There are Sephardic communities that to this day eat roasted lamb on Seder night as a *zecher l'korban Pesach*. The Ashkenazic practice, however, opposes this, since it too closely resembles actually bringing a *korban Pesach*. Tudos went further: not merely eating roasted meat, but roasting a whole goat over a spit in deliberate simulation of the Temple offering.

Shimon ben Shetach sent him the same message he had sent Choni: *ilmalei Tudos atah gozrani alecha nidui* — were you not who you are, I would excommunicate you. *She'atah ma'achil es Yisrael kodashim bachutz* — because you are making Israel eat *kodashim* outside the Temple, in the sense that you are enacting something so close in form to the *korban Pesach* that it blurs the line entirely.

This raises an interesting conceptual question. We began by saying that *niddui* is imposed in twenty-four places *al kvod harav*, on account of disrespect to a rabbi. Yet the cases accumulate in ways that strain that original framing. The case of Choni is about entitlement toward God; the case of Tudos is about a practice that the rabbis found objectionable, though it was not uniformly prohibited — there were communities that maintained such practices. How these become instances of *kvod harav* in any direct sense requires some stretching. Regarding questions about who has authority to impose *niddui*: Shimon ben Shetach was the *Nasi* and *Av Beis Din*, and presumably acted with the backing of other rabbis even if they are not mentioned. There are also certain predefined violations for which *niddui* takes effect automatically — the *Rambam* rules this way regarding a few cases, though the *Ra'avad* disagrees — while other cases require a formal decision by *beis din*.

### Analogies to 24 Cases

The *Gemara* now accounts for how one arrives at twenty-four cases when only three appear explicitly in the *Mishnah*. A *baraisa* is cited mentioning the famous dispute about the oven of *Achnai*, in which Rabbi Eliezer ruled the sectioned oven טהור and the *chachamim* ruled it טמא. The word *Achnai* is explained by Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel: *melamed she-hikifuhu halachos ke'achna* — it teaches that they surrounded the oven with *halachos* like a snake constricting its prey, debating all sides. Rabbi Eliezer refused to concede even when the walls of the *beis medrash* bent inward and a heavenly voice spoke in his defense. Eventually, the rabbis took all the items Rabbi Eliezer had declared *tahor* and burned them before him, and ultimately placed him in *niddui*. This is a genuine case of *kvod harav*: it is one thing to maintain a position in debate; it is another to refuse to submit to the majority ruling even after the fact, which is the classic case of *zaken mamre*.

However, the *Gemara* notes that while the case of the oven dispute itself appears in the *Mishnah*, the episode of the *niddui* does not. So strictly speaking, in terms of what the *Mishnah* explicitly records about *niddui*, there are still only three cases. The resolution is: *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi medamei milsa lemilsa* — Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi draws analogies from case to case, while Rabbi Elazar does not. From the three explicit paradigmatic cases, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi extended the logic — if *niddui* was imposed for *netilas yadayim*, then similar cases must also warrant *niddui* — and so analogically derived twenty-four total cases. It remains somewhat striking to speak of twenty-four definite places in the *Mishnah* when the actual enumeration depends on analogy, but that is the *Gemara*'s resolution.

## Burial & Osek b'Mitzvah

### Coffin Near Shema

Returning to the central topic of *Krias Shema*, the *Gemara* cites a *baraisa*: *ein motzi'in es hameis samuch l'Krias Shema* — one does not take out the coffin to begin the burial procession close to the time of *Shema*, because this would create complications regarding *osek b'mitzvah*. Once everyone is occupied with the burial, the principle of *osek b'mitzvah patur min hamitzvah* — one who is engaged in one mitzvah is exempt from another — would apply, and people might lose the opportunity to say *Shema* entirely. Better to say *Shema* first, then begin the procession. But *v'im hitchilu ein mafsikin* — if the procession has already begun, one does not interrupt it, because at that point one has become *osek b'mitzvah*.

The *Gemara* challenges this from a report that Rav Yosef's coffin was taken out precisely during the time of *Shema*, without waiting. The response: *adam chashuv shani* — an exceptionally important person is different; one does not delay the funeral proceedings of so distinguished a figure even for this consideration. This raises a genuine question about how one chooses between *mitzvos* when both compete for priority. The *Gemara*'s initial answer is: do the one you may not get another opportunity to do — if you begin with the *meis*, you may lose *Shema*. But when another overriding concern enters, such as the dignity of a great person, that calculus can shift.

### Before/Behind Coffin

The *Gemara* continues with those walking before and behind the coffin — *shel lifnei hamita v'shel acharei hamita*. Rashi's reading is that even those behind the coffin, if they are not as essential as those in front, should slip away to say *Shema*. *Tosafos* offers a simpler reading of the *Mishnah*: the only operative question is whether you are needed for the proceedings. If you are needed, you remain and do not say *Shema*; if you are not needed, you slip away and say *Shema*.

### Eulogizers Saying Shema

A *baraisa* addresses those engaged in delivering the eulogy — *ha'oskin b'hesped*. If the body is lying before them — *bizman she-hameis mutal lifneihem* — they slip away one at a time and recite *Shema*, because not all of them need to be present simultaneously. This raises an interesting question about the scope of *osek b'mitzvah*: can we say that the audience assembled at a *hesped* is also engaged in the mitzvah? The *Mishnah* earlier spoke of those in the row beside the mourners being *osek b'mitzvah*, while those on the outside could slip away individually to recite *Shema*. It would seem that crowd presence is in some way part of the *osek b'mitzvah* status — hence the requirement to leave only one at a time rather than all at once.

If the body is *not* lying before them, *hein yoshvin v'korin* — they sit and recite *Shema* as a group, and the *onen* sits in silence. The presence of the body thus functions as a marker of the degree to which one is genuinely *osek b'mitzvah*: the more immediate the *meis*, the more fully one is engaged in the mitzvah, and the less latitude there is to step away.

### Onen & Tziduk HaDin

While the eulogizers and participants say *Shema* and then stand in prayer, the *onen* — one who has suffered a loss but the burial has not yet taken place — is exempt from prayer and *Shema* and sits in silence. *V'hu omeid u'matzdik alav es hadin* — and he stands and recites *tziduk hadin*, acknowledging the justice of God's judgment. The text of *tziduk hadin* cited here is: *Ribono Shel Olamim, chatasi lefanecha, v'lo nifrati mimeni echad m'elef alfei alafim* — Master of the Universe, I have sinned greatly before You, and I have not received even one one-thousandth of the punishment I deserve. *Yehi ratzon milfanecha Hashem Elokeinu shetigdor pirtzoseinu u'firtzos kol amcha Beis Yisrael b'rachamim* — may it be Your will that You close our breaches and the breaches of all Your people Israel with compassion.

### Abaye's Critique

Abaye objects: *lo miba'ei lei l'einish l'meimar hachi* — a person should not say this. He cites Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish and a parallel teaching in the name of Rabbi Yosi: *l'olam al yiftach adam piv l'Satan* — one should never open one's mouth to the Satan. To declare that one has not received even a fraction of deserved punishment is to invite further punishment. Rav Yosef adds the proof text: when Yeshayahu said *k'meat k'Sedom hayinu* — we were almost like Sodom — the prophet responded by calling them *ktzinei Sedom*, officers of Sodom. You said "almost like Sodom," now I will call you Sodom. The principle: when you open the door to such declarations, worse follows.

### Tziduk HaDin Meaning

Beyond the practical concern about *piv l'Satan*, there is a deeper question about the theological framing embedded in this text. The language of *chatasi lefanecha v'lo nifrati mimeni* frames the death as punishment *directed at the speaker* — I have sinned, and I have not yet received what I deserve. This is a recognizable way that many people think in times of loss: what did I do to deserve this? Yet *tziduk hadin* in its essential meaning — and as expressed in the liturgical text we actually recite, *hatzur tamim po'alo ki chol drachav mishpat* — is not about the mourner's punishment but about vindicating God's justice with respect to the person who died. The declaration is: whatever God has done, to whoever suffers as a result, it is just — even if incomprehensible.

One possible reading of *chatasi lefanecha v'lo nifrati mimeni* that avoids the punishment-toward-me framing is: I recognize how small I am, how vast is the gap between my deeds and what I deserve, and therefore I have no standing to protest that this death is unjust. It is an expression of humility rather than a claim of personal punishment. Still, the *Gemara*'s very discomfort with this text — as Abaye reads it — seems to be precisely that it is framed in terms of the speaker's own reckoning. Rav Soloveitchik's understanding of the *onen* provides an important counterbalance: the *onen* is not merely exempt from *mitzvos* in the technical sense of *osek b'mitzvah*, but is positively entitled to remain in a state of existential questioning — of *what does this mean, what is life about, how is this just?* — before moving into the affirmative world of *mitzvos*. *Tziduk hadin* does not demand that questioning disappear; it holds both the questioning and the affirmation of God's justice simultaneously.

### Return from Burial

After the burial, *kavru es hameis v'chazru* — they buried the dead and turned back. The rule is: if they are able to begin and complete *Shema* — even *perek echad o pasuk echad*, a single section or a single verse — before they must take their places in the *shura*, the comforting line, they do so; if not, they do not begin. The *Gemara* initially reads the *Mishnah* as requiring the entire *Shema* to be completed, then harmonizes: the meaning is that if they can get even one *perek* or one *pasuk* in before the line forms, they should, because that unit constitutes some meaningful fulfillment of the obligation.

This connects to the broader question of how much of *Shema* is biblically required. Whether the biblical minimum is a *perek* or a *pasuk* — a question raised earlier in the tractate — bears on whether it is worthwhile to recite a partial *Shema* when the full text cannot be completed. It might also be argued that even less than the biblical minimum should be recited for the sake of *kabalas ol malchus shamayim*, but the *Gemara* does not pursue that line and focuses on the question of fulfilling the *mitzvah*.

### Shura Lines

The *Gemara* now addresses those standing in the *shura*, the line through which the mourners pass to receive consolation. A *baraisa* states: *shurah ha-ro'ah penimah peturah* — the row that faces inward toward the mourners is *patur*, exempt from *Shema* and prayer, as they are directly engaged in the mitzvah of comforting the mourners. *V'she'einah ro'ah penimah chayeves* — those who are not facing inward are obligated.

### R. Yehudah on Intent

Rabbi Yehudah offers a different and broader criterion: *haba machmas ha'avel patur, machmas atzmo chayav* — whoever came for the sake of the mourner is exempt; whoever came for his own reasons is obligated. This fundamentally reframes the question. Rather than asking about one's physical position relative to the inner or outer row, Rabbi Yehudah asks about intent and purpose. If you are genuinely there for the mourner, you are *osek b'mitzvah* regardless of where you stand. If you are there for your own reasons — to be seen, out of curiosity, or any other personal motive — then even standing in the inner row does not make you *osek b'mitzvah*. This raises a fascinating question: according to Rabbi Yehudah, what of someone in the inner row who is saying *hamakom yenachem* — is he *osek b'mitzvah* if his intent is self-serving? The framing of *osek b'mitzvah* as defined by purpose rather than by action alone is a meaningfully different conception.

## Kevod HaBriyes

### Shatnez Removal

The *Gemara* now pivots to what becomes one of the most important halakhic discussions in the daf. Rav Yehudah citing Rav states: *hamotzi kilayim b'vigdo poshtan afilu bashuk* — if someone discovers while walking in the street that his garment contains *shatnez* — a prohibited mixture of wool and linen — he must remove it immediately, even in the middle of the public street, even if doing so causes embarrassment. The reasoning: *ein chochmah v'ein tevunah v'ein eitzah leneged Hashem* — there is no wisdom, no understanding, no counsel that stands before God. You do not make calculations; if it involves a violation, you act. And this is tied directly to the principle stated on 19a: *kol makom she-yeish chillul Hashem ein cholkin kavod le-rav* — wherever there is a desecration of God's Name, one does not show deference even to a *rav*. The *kavod* that was discussed so extensively in the first section of the daf — the same *kavod* for whose sake people were placed in *niddui* — becomes entirely irrelevant when *chillul Hashem* is at stake.

### Two Paths Case

The *Gemara* then challenges this principle. A *baraisa* states: after burying the dead and turning back, if there are two paths before them — one *tahor* and one *tamei*, one pure and one impure — and another person goes down the *tamei* path, one should follow him even if one is a *kohen*, *mishum kvodo* — out of deference to that person's honor. One does not wish to leave him or to embarrass him by conspicuously taking the other path. But how can this be? Should we not say *ein chochmah v'ein tevunah v'ein eitzah leneged Hashem* — that no calculation of human dignity overrides the prohibition?

### Beis HaPras

The resolution, *targemah Rabbi Abba b'veis ha-pras d'rabbanan*, is that this case does not involve a path that is biblically *tamei*. Rather, it involves a *beis ha-pras* — an area where a grave has been plowed over, scattering small bone fragments throughout the field. As Rav Yehudah citing Shmuel teaches, *menapeiach adam beis ha-pras v'holech* — one may blow the dust before each step to clear away any possible bone fragments and then walk through, implying that the concern about *tumah* in a *beis ha-pras* is rabbinic in nature. The chips of bone may be too small to convey biblical *tumah*; the rabbis were stringent as an added precaution. Since the prohibition is rabbinic rather than biblical, it can be overridden for the sake of *kevod habriyos*. The *Gemara* also notes that a *beis ha-pras* that has been heavily trampled is *tahor*, as the fragments would have been ground down beyond concern.

The emerging principle: *kevod habriyos* — human dignity — overrides rabbinic law, but not biblical law.

### R. Elazar bar Tzadok

Further support: Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok testified that they used to leap over the coffins of the dead — *medalgin hayinu al gabei aronos shel meisim* — in order to greet Jewish kings, and not only Jewish kings but even non-Jewish kings, because one who has seen non-Jewish royalty will better appreciate Jewish kingship when it comes. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok was a *kohen*, and yet he describes leaping over coffins, which would ordinarily convey *tumah*. How could he do this?

### Coffin Exception

The answer comes from Rava: *devar Torah ohel she-yeish bo challal tefach chotzeitz bifnei ha-tumah* — biblically, if there is a handbreadth of open space between the lid of the *aron* and the body inside, that space constitutes a *roof* that stops the *tumah* from passing upward. *V'she-ein bo challal tefach eino chotzeitz* — if there is no handbreadth, the *tumah* passes through without obstruction. *V'rov aronos yesh bahen challal tefach* — most coffins do have this handbreadth. Nevertheless, *v'gazru al she-yesh bahen mishum she-ein bahen* — the rabbis decreed that even coffins with the handbreadth should be treated as if they do not have it, as a precautionary measure. *U-mishum kvod melachim lo gazru bahen* — but because of the honor of kings, the rabbis did not apply this decree in that context. So once again: what appears to be a biblical concern resolves into a rabbinic *gezeira*, which can be waived for *kevod habriyos*.

This requires a note, because the impression one has from standard halakhic discourse is that a *kohen*'s prohibition against contracting *tumah* from the dead is entirely biblical — he walks over a grave and immediately becomes *tamei* according to Torah law. How then can the *Gemara* treat any aspect of coffin-*tumah* as merely rabbinic? *Tosafos* explains: there is a separate rule that a sealed coffin — one sealed on all sides — is treated as an extension of the *meis* itself, and therefore does not create the *tefach* airspace that would stop the *tumah*. The case in the *Gemara* apparently involves a coffin that is not completely sealed; in such a case, the lid creates the requisite airspace and the biblical *tumah* does not pass through. The rabbinic decree extends the prohibition even to such cases — and it is that extension, that rabbinic overlay, that is waived for *kevod melachim*. This explains why in Eretz Yisrael, where coffins are not used, the biblical *tumah* issue is even more acute, since there is nothing at all to create the *tefach* space.

### Kvod Overrides

The *Gemara* then cites the principle in its broadest formulation: *gadol kvod ha-briyos she-docheh lo ta'aseh she-ba-Torah* — great is human dignity, for it overrides a biblical prohibition. This seems to contradict everything the *Gemara* has just established. The question follows immediately: should we not say *ein chochmah v'ein tevunah v'ein eitzah leneged Hashem*?

### Lo Tasur Framework

Rav bar Shava explained before Rav Kahana: *b'lav d'lo tassur* — the principle means not literally every biblical prohibition, but specifically the biblical prohibition encoded in *lo tassur min ha-davar*, the verse prohibiting departure from rabbinic instruction. Every rabbinic law has, standing behind it, this biblical injunction to obey the rabbis. When we say *kevod ha-briyos* overrides a "*lo ta'aseh she-ba-Torah*," we mean it overrides *lo tassur* — that is, we mean rabbinic law, not the full range of biblical prohibitions.

### Rav Kahana's Response

Those hearing this explanation *achiku alei* — laughed at him. Their objection: *lav d'lo tassur d'oraissa hi* — *lo tassur* is itself a biblical prohibition! If you are genuinely violating *lo tassur*, that is a biblical violation. Rav Kahana replied: *gavra raba amar milta, la techiku alei* — when a great person says something, do not laugh at him. The explanation he provides: *kol mili d'rabbanan asmichinhu al lo tassur, u-mishum kvodo sharu rabbanan* — all rabbinic laws are anchored in *lo tassur*, and the rabbis themselves have the authority to waive compliance with their own enactments when *kevod ha-briyos* requires it. Therefore when *kevod ha-briyos* is at stake, one is not actually transgressing *lo tassur*, because the rabbis themselves sanction the deviation.

This requires further unpacking. The violation of *lo tassur* is not simply doing something the rabbis prohibited. Rather, *lo tassur* is violated through an act of rejecting rabbinic authority — acting in defiance of the rabbis because one does not accept their jurisdiction. When one violates a rabbinic law because of *yetzer ha-ra*, or mistake, or *kevod ha-briyos*, one is not rejecting rabbinic authority in that substantive sense, and therefore does not violate *lo tassur*. We call it a biblical-adjacent concern only because *lo tassur* stands somewhere in the background. But the operative rule remains: *kevod ha-briyos* overrides rabbinic law, not biblical law in the direct sense.

### Hashavat Aveidah

The *Gemara* brings one more challenge: *ve-hisalamta mehem* — the verse regarding returning a lost object. The *Gemara* reads this verse both as a command (*do not turn away from it*) and as a permission (*there are times you do turn away*). The cases where one need not return the *aveidah*: if the finder is a *kohen* and the object is in a cemetery; if returning it would cost more in wages than the object is worth; and — most relevant here — *hayah zaken v'eino le-fi kvodo*, if one is an elderly or distinguished person for whom stooping down and carrying the object would be beneath his dignity. In that case, *l'chach ne'emar v'hisalamta mehem* — the verse itself permits non-return.

This appears to be a case where *kevod ha-briyos* — specifically, the dignity of the finder — overrides a biblical *mitzvah*. Should this not serve as a paradigm for a broader principle? The *Gemara* responds: *shani hasam, de-chtiv v'hisalamta mehem* — that case is different, because the Torah itself explicitly wrote the permission. Could we not derive a general rule from it? *Issura mi-mamona lo yalfinan* — we do not derive prohibitions from monetary matters. Returning a *aveidah* is a *mitzvah*, but it is fundamentally a monetary issue. Monetary considerations are evaluated by their own standards: *hamotzi mei-chaveiro alav ha-rayah*, and similar rules. One's dignity outweighs another person's monetary interest. But we do not say one's dignity outweighs a prohibition that is evaluated first and foremost in terms of the *issur* rather than in terms of money.

### Money vs. Prohibition

This distinction between monetary and prohibitive frameworks is significant for the overall structure of *kevod ha-briyos*. At this stage of the *sugya*, the *Gemara* has established that *kevod ha-briyos* overrides rabbinic law — and has worked to bracket or explain away all apparent cases of it overriding biblical law: the *beis ha-pras* case is rabbinic; the coffin case involves a rabbinic *gezeira*; the *lo tassur* framing shows that even the "biblical" formulation ultimately means rabbinic; and the *aveidah* case is limited to monetary matters. The conclusion of this *sugya* — examining precisely when *kevod ha-briyos* does and does not override — will be taken up in the next session.
