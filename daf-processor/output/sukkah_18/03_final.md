# Sukkah 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Neharde'a vs. Sura

### Two versions intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרָא אָמְרִי לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא בְּהַאי לִישָּׁנָא. בִּנְהַרְדְּעָא מַתְנוּ: אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: סְכָךְ פָּסוּל בָּאֶמְצַע — פּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבָּעָה. מִן הַצַּד — פּוֹסֵל בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וְרַב אָמַר: בֵּין מִן הַצַּד בֵּין בָּאֶמְצַע — בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **In Sura, they stated this *halakha* in that language** cited above. **In Neharde'a,** however, **they taught** it as follows: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Unfit roofing in the center** of the *sukka* **renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** a measure of **four** handbreadths of unfit roofing. **Along the side** of the *sukka*, **it renders** the *sukka* **unfit with** a measure of **four cubits** of unfit roofing. **And Rav said: Both along the side and in the center,** it renders the *sukka* unfit **with** a measure of **four cubits** of unfit roofing.

The daf opens in the middle of 17b, where the Gemara presents two distinct traditions regarding the Rav-Shmuel debate on *pasul schach*. The two academies — Sura, the yeshiva of Rav, and Neharde'a, the yeshiva of Shmuel — transmitted the debate in fundamentally different terms, and the practical ruling depends on which version one accepts.

### Sura version

According to the Sura version, both Rav and Shmuel agree on the underlying distinction: the threshold for *pasul schach* to be problematic depends on where it is located. If the *pasul schach* is on the side of the *sukkah*, the relevant measure is *arba amot*, because anything less than that can be incorporated into the wall via *dofen akumah*. If the *pasul schach* is in the middle of the *sukkah*, the relevant measure is *arba tefachim*, because even that amount creates its own distinct zone, effectively dividing the *schach* in half. As discussed in connection with Tosafot, this division does not invalidate the entire *sukkah* at once; rather, it cuts the *schach* into two sections, sometimes leaving one *kasher* half and one *pasul* half, and sometimes leaving two *pasul* halves.

One might ask whether it is possible to be left with two *kasher* halves. The Gemara assumes a three-walled *sukkah*. In a four-walled *sukkah*, if the *pasul schach* runs down the middle between two stretches of *kasher schach*, the result would indeed be two separate *kasher sukkot*, each with three walls — and one would simply be unable to sit in the middle. But the Gemara's analysis proceeds on the basis of the three-walled case.

### Neharde'a version

According to the Neharde'a version, this distinction between side and middle was not a shared position of Rav and Shmuel but rather the very point of their disagreement. In Neharde'a, the tradition was transmitted as follows: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel* — *schach be'emtza pasul be'arba'ah; min hatzad pasul be'arba amot*. Shmuel is the one who distinguishes between the two locations, holding that *arba tefachim* suffices to divide the *schach* in the middle, while *arba amot* are required on the side. Rav, by contrast — *bein min hatzad uvein be'emtza be'arba amot* — holds that the measure of *arba amot* applies uniformly, whether the *pasul schach* is on the side or in the middle.

### Practical ruling

This generates a striking ruling. In almost all disputes between Rav and Shmuel, the *halacha* follows Rav. Yet here, as in one or two other places in this *masechet*, we rule like Shmuel. Practically speaking, this means that *arba tefachim* of *pasul schach* in the middle of one's *sukkah* is already a problem — a conclusion few would have assumed on their own. According to the Sura version, this is the consensus of both Rav and Shmuel; according to the Neharde'a version, we are departing from our usual practice of following Rav in favor of Shmuel's more stringent position in the middle.

## Textual proofs

### Plank mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נָתַן עָלֶיהָ נֶסֶר שֶׁהוּא רָחָב אַרְבָּעָה טְפָחִים — כְּשֵׁרָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב דְּאָמַר בֵּין בָּאֶמְצַע בֵּין מִן הַצַּד בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי כְּשֵׁרָה. אֶלָּא לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר בָּאֶמְצַע בְּאַרְבָּעָה — אַמַּאי כְּשֵׁרָה? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna: **If one placed a board that is four handbreadths wide atop** the *sukka*, the *sukka* **is fit.** And the Gemara asks: **Granted, according to Rav, who said** that **both along the side and in the center,** a *sukka* is rendered unfit **with** a measure of **four cubits** of unfit roofing, it is **due to that** reason that the *sukka* **is fit. However, according to Shmuel, who said** that **in the center** of the *sukka*, the *sukka* is rendered unfit **with** a measure of **four** handbreadths of unfit roofing, **why is** the *sukka* **fit?** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** It is with a case where he placed the beam **along the side;** but had he placed it in the center, then according to Shmuel the *sukka* would indeed be unfit.

Having established the two versions of the debate through logical argument, the Gemara now turns to textual proofs — examining *mishnayot* and *baraitot* to see which position they support. The first source is the *mishnah* that teaches: *natan aleha neser sherochav arba'ah tefachim* — if one placed on the *sukkah* a plank four *tefachim* wide, the *sukkah* remains *kesherah*. This *mishnah* operates on the assumption, held by Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Yehuda, that such planks constitute *pasul schach*. Yet the *mishnah* rules that the *sukkah* is valid — though one may not eat or sleep beneath the plank.

This fits effortlessly with Rav's position. Rav holds that *pasul schach*, whether on the side or in the middle, requires *arba amot* to invalidate the *sukkah*. A plank of four *tefachim* falls well short of that threshold and therefore does not compromise the *sukkah's* validity, even though it is significant enough to prohibit sitting beneath it.

### Shmuel's reinterpretation

The difficulty is with Shmuel's position. If four *tefachim* of *pasul schach* in the middle suffices to divide the *sukkah*, why does the *mishnah* say the *sukkah* remains *kesherah*? The Gemara answers: *hacha b'mai askinan — min hatzad*. The *mishnah* is not discussing a plank placed in the middle of the *sukkah* but rather one placed on the side. There, since we can invoke *dofen akumah*, anything less than *arba amot* does not invalidate — and the *mishnah* is simply describing such a case. This reading is forced on Shmuel; the *mishnah* reads far more naturally according to Rav.

### Sheets vs. planks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנֵי סְדִינִין — מִצְטָרְפִין. שְׁנֵי נְסָרִים — אֵין מִצְטָרְפִין. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: נְסָרִים כִּסְדִינִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof with regard to Rav's opinion. **Come** and **hear: Two sheets** placed over the roofing of the *sukka* **join together** to render the *sukka* unfit. However, **two boards** placed on the *sukka* **do not combine** to render the *sukka* unfit. **Rabbi Meir says: Even boards** have the same legal status **as sheets,** and they combine to render the *sukka* unfit.

The Gemara presses further with a *baraita*: *shnei sadinim mitztarfin* — two sheets combine to produce enough *pasul schach*. If one sheet is three *tefachim* wide and another is one *tefach* wide, together they constitute four *tefachim* (or, if needed, they combine toward *arba amot*). By contrast, *shnei nesarin ein mitztarfin* — two planks do not combine. The reason, according to this view, is that planks do not constitute genuine *pasul schach* and therefore there is no problem to aggregate. Rabbi Meir dissents: *nesarin k'sadinim* — planks are like sheets and do combine.

To understand this *baraita*, one must first clarify what size planks are under debate. Recall the earlier discussion of whether Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda dispute planks in the range of three to four *tefachim*, or planks of four *tefachim* and above. The Gemara will now test both versions of Rav against this *baraita*.

### Rav's approach tested

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר רַב בֵּין בָּאֶמְצַע בֵּין מִן הַצַּד בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, מַאי ״מִצְטָרְפִין״ — מִצְטָרְפִין לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. אֶלָּא לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר רַב בָּאֶמְצַע בְּאַרְבָּעָה, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאִית בְּהוּ אַרְבָּעָה, לְמָה לְהוּ אִצְטְרוֹפֵי? אִי דְּלֵית בְּהוּ אַרְבָּעָה, קַנְיָא בְּעָלְמָא נִינְהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted, according to that version** from Neharde'a **that Rav said: Both along the side and in the center,** a *sukka* is rendered unfit **with** a measure of **four cubits** of unfit roofing, **what** is the meaning of **join together?** It means that the two unfit objects **join together to** comprise **four cubits. However, according to this version** from Sura, **in which Rav said:** A *sukka* is rendered unfit **with** a measure of **four** handbreadths of unfit roofing in the center, **what are the circumstances? If** each of the boards **has four** handbreadths in its width, **why must they join together** to render the *sukka* unfit? If each board is four handbreadths wide, each is capable of rendering the *sukka* unfit on its own, and if each of the boards **does not have four** handbreadths in its width, **why** would Rabbi Meir prohibit their use; **they are merely reeds?**

According to the Neharde'a version of Rav — that *pasul schach* requires *arba amot* everywhere, middle and side alike — the debate of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerns planks of four *tefachim* and above. These planks are individually large enough to be *pasul* even according to Rabbi Yehuda. If so, why does Rabbi Meir say they must combine? The answer is that *mitztarfin l'arba amot*: they must combine until one has *arba amot*, because that is the threshold Rav sets even in the middle. A single four-*tefach* plank does not invalidate; only a collection adding up to *arba amot* does. This works elegantly with Rav's position as transmitted in Neharde'a.

### Sura Rav tested

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית בְּהוּ אַרְבָּעָה, וּמַאי ״מִצְטָרְפִין״ — מִצְטָרְפִין לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is a case **where** each of the boards **has four** handbreadths in its width, **and what** is the meaning of **join together?** It means **they join together to** constitute **four cubits along the side.** This understanding fits both versions of Rav's opinion.

But the Sura version of Rav creates a problem. There, Rav holds that even in the middle, *pasul schach* invalidates at *arba tefachim*. And Rav there also maintains that the debate of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerns planks of four *tefachim* and above. If so — *ei d'is behu arba*, if the plank is four *tefachim*, it already invalidates on its own; *lama lehu l'tzerufei* — why would you need to combine? And *ei d'les behu arba* — if the plank is less than four *tefachim*, then *kanya b'alma ninhu* — it is merely a stick, not even considered a plank, and there is nothing to combine.

The Gemara resolves this: *le'olam de'is behu arba* — indeed the planks are four *tefachim* wide, and the need to combine (*mitztarfin*) applies not to produce four *tefachim* but to produce *arba amot* — because we are discussing planks placed on the *side* of the *sukkah*. There, the threshold is *arba amot*, and the planks must accumulate to that measure. In other words, once again, anything that seems to indicate that four *tefachim* is insufficient, the Gemara redirects to the case of *min hatzad*, where the *amot*-measure applies.

The pattern that has now emerged is significant: every time a *mishnah* or *baraita* seems to imply that four *tefachim* is insufficient to invalidate, the Gemara answers that the source is speaking *min hatzad*. This allows Shmuel's position — and the Sura version of Rav's position, which is effectively identical regarding the middle — to survive the textual challenges, since any scenario appearing to require more than four *tefachim* can be attributed to the side case.

A related question was raised regarding *gud assik* and *dofen akumah* — whether one can combine the two principles, extending a wall upward via *gud assik* and then bending it horizontally through *dofen akumah*. This may be possible, as there is apparently a *Ran* addressing that question, but one cannot use *dofen akumah* at the very top of the *schach* layer itself.

### Cedar planks baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: סִכְּכָהּ בִּנְסָרִין שֶׁל אֶרֶז שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן אַרְבָּעָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פְּסוּלָה. אֵין בָּהֶן אַרְבָּעָה — רַבִּי מֵאִיר פּוֹסֵל וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** proof from another *baraita*: If **one roofed** the entire *sukka* **with cedar beams that have four** handbreadths in **their** width, **everyone agrees** that the *sukka* is **unfit.** If **they do not have four** handbreadths in **their** width, **Rabbi Meir deems** the *sukka* **unfit and Rabbi Yehuda deems it fit.**

A further *baraita* is adduced: *sikcha bensarim shel erez* — if one covered a *sukkah* with cedar planks. *Sheyesh bahen arba* — if they are four *tefachim* wide, *divrei hakol pesulah* — universally invalid. This indicates that below four *tefachim*, the debate of Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda is live, but once a plank is four *tefachim* wide, even Rabbi Yehuda concedes invalidity. And conversely, *ein bahen arba* — if the planks are between three and four *tefachim*, Rabbi Meir rules invalid and Rabbi Yehuda permits.

### Rabbi Meir concedes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר שֶׁאִם יֵשׁ בֵּין נֶסֶר לְנֶסֶר כִּמְלֹא נֶסֶר — שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ פְּסָל בֵּינֵיהֶם וּכְשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir concedes that if** there is **between** one **board** and another **board** a gap the **complete** width **of a board,** then **one places** fit roofing from the **waste** of the threshing floor and the winepress, **and** the *sukka* **is fit.**

At the top of 18a comes a particularly instructive ruling: *u'modeh Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir himself concedes — *she'im yesh bein neser leneser kemlo neser* — that if between one plank and the next there is a gap equal in width to a plank, *she'maniach pesal beineihem u'kesherah* — and one fills that gap with valid *schach* (*pesal*, in the unusual usage of the Gemara for *kasher schach*), the *sukkah* is *kosher*.

This requires explanation. If four *tefachim* of *pasul schach* invalidates in the middle, how can alternating planks with *kasher schach* render the *sukkah* valid? One plank is already four *tefachim* of *pasul schach* — it should divide the *sukkah*. The Gemara presents this as a *gotcha*: in all previous cases, the Gemara escaped by saying we are speaking *min hatzad*, but here, the planks alternate across the entire roof. One cannot claim it is a side case — the *pasul schach* runs through the middle as well.

### Rav Huna's solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הָכָא בְּסוּכָּה דְּלָא הָוְיָא אֶלָּא שְׁמֹנֶה מְצוּמְצָמוֹת עָסְקִינַן, וְיָהֵיב נֶסֶר וּפְסָל וְנֶסֶר וּפְסָל וְנֶסֶר וּפְסָל מֵהַאי גִּיסָא, וְנֶסֶר וּפְסָל וְנֶסֶר וּפְסָל וְנֶסֶר וּפְסָל מֵהַאי גִּיסָא, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ שְׁנֵי פְסָלִין בָּאֶמְצַע, וְאִיכָּא הֶכְשֵׁר סוּכָּה בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: Here, we are dealing with a *sukka* that is exactly eight** cubits, i.e., forty-eight handbreadths, wide, and one began placing the roofing from the side. **And he places** a four-handbreadth **board and** then four handbreadths of **waste, and** another **board and waste, and a board and waste, from this side,** so that the total measure of roofing from that side is twenty-four handbreadths. **And** then **a beam and waste, a beam and waste, and a beam and waste, from that side,** so that the total measure of roofing from that side is twenty-four handbreadths. The result is **that** the *sukka* **has two** four-handbreadth stretches of **waste in the middle** of the *sukka*, totaling eight handbreadths. In that case, **there is** the minimum measure of fit roofing required for **fitness of a *sukka* in the middle,** and everyone agrees that the unfit roofing in the rest of the *sukka* cannot render it unfit. Since the unfit roofing measures less than four cubits on either side, the *sukka* is fit both according to the principle of curved wall and according to the opinion that unfit roofing renders the *sukka* unfit with four cubits.

The resolution comes from Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua: *hacha be'sukkah de'la havya ela shemoneh metzumtzamot askinan* — we are dealing with a *sukkah* that is precisely eight *amot* wide. The planks and *kasher schach* alternate in the following manner: starting from one side, one lays a plank, then valid *schach*, then a plank, and so on — proceeding inward from each side simultaneously, meeting in the middle. The result is that from each wall, the stretch of *pasul* material never reaches *arba amot*. Each side, taken as a unit, is less than *arba amot* of *pasul schach* from the wall, which means *dofen akumah* can incorporate it. The middle zone of *kasher schach*, where the two sides converge, is thereby protected.

What Rav Huna has done is demonstrate that even this seemingly devastating case reduces to a *min hatzad* scenario: the *pasul schach* from each side never exceeds the *dofen akumah* limit, so it is treated as part of the wall's bend, and the valid *schach* in the middle is kosher. It is worth noting that *dofen akumah* can incorporate *kasher schach* — not only *pasul schach* — within four *amot* of the wall. These principles — *gud assik*, *levud*, and *dofen akumah* — were explicitly included in the *halacha le'Moshe mi'Sinai* listed by the Gemara at the top of 5b, by the *Tosafot* there, in the context of partitions.

## Abaye's small sukkah

### Three tefachim rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲוִיר שְׁלֹשָׁה בְּסוּכָּה גְּדוֹלָה וּמִיעֲטוֹ, בֵּין בְּקָנִים בֵּין בְּשַׁפּוּדִין — הָוֵי מִיעוּט. בְּסוּכָּה קְטַנָּה, בְּקָנִים — הָוֵי מִיעוּט, בְּשַׁפּוּדִין — לָא הָוֵי מִיעוּט.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** If there is **space** measuring **three** handbreadths **in a large *sukka*,** which is defined as one larger than seven by seven handbreadths, **and one diminished** the space, **whether** he did so **with branches,** fit for roofing, or **whether** he did so **with** metal **skewers,** unfit roofing, **it is** an effective **diminution,** as there is neither sufficient space nor sufficient unfit roofing to render the *sukka* unfit. However, **in a small *sukka*,** if one diminished the space **with branches it is** an effective **diminution;** if he diminished the space **with skewers, it is not** an effective **diminution** and the *sukka* is unfit. The three handbreadths of skewers, while insufficient to render the *sukka* unfit, diminish the fit area of the *sukka* to the point that the measure that remains does not constitute a fit *sukka*.

Having concluded the extended analysis of textual proofs, the Gemara states that the ruling follows Shmuel — four *tefachim* in the middle invalidates — without dwelling on a formal declaration. The Gemara then transitions to a new ruling: *Amar Abaye: oviya shlosha besukkah gedolah umiato, bein bekanim bein bishpudin havei miyut* — if one has a *sukkah* that was diminished by three *tefachim* of some material, both valid *schach* (reeds) and *pasul schach* (metal skewers) constitute a reduction in a large *sukkah*. *Besukkah ketana* — but in a minimum-size *sukkah* of exactly seven by seven *tefachim* — *bekanim havei miyut* — valid *schach* that fills the gap solves the problem (and one can even invoke *levud* if not all is filled), *bishpudin lo havei miyut* — but *pasul schach* placed there does not solve the problem.

### Space definition

Abaye's reasoning introduces a genuinely new principle. It is not that three *tefachim* of *pasul schach* divides the *sukkah* in the way that four *tefachim* does in a larger *sukkah* — that threshold has already been established. Rather, Abaye holds that once a *sukkah* is at its minimum size, every *tefach* matters in a way it does not for a larger structure. Three *tefachim* of *pasul schach* in this context establishes its own zone — one that cannot simply be folded into the dimensions of the *sukkah*. Even if it does not formally divide the *schach*, it cannot be incorporated into the count of the *sukkah's* usable space.

A loose analogy: in a spacious apartment, one might count even auxiliary spaces when measuring floor area, but in a tiny studio where every square foot is precious, one cannot treat a non-living zone as part of the living space. The point is that in a minimum-size *sukkah*, even three *tefachim* of *pasul schach* is enough to render that zone dimensionally excluded — leaving the *sukkah* below the minimum size. This was implicit in Abaye's assumption from the previous day's discussion; here he makes it explicit.

## Levud in middle

### Levud question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי מִן הַצַּד. אֲבָל בָּאֶמְצַע — פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא, חַד אָמַר: יֵשׁ לָבוּד בָּאֶמְצַע, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵין לָבוּד בָּאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And this applies only** if the space is **along the side** of the *sukka*, in which case the principle of *lavud* applies. **However,** if the space is **in the center** of the *sukka*, **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree** with regard to the ruling. **One said:** The principle of ***lavud* is** applied even **in the center** of the *sukka*. **And one said:** The principle of ***lavud* is not** applied **in the center** of the *sukka*. Even if one diminished the space, the two sides of the roofing are not considered joined.

The Gemara pivots from discussing the threshold for *pasul schach* to a related question: does *levud* — the principle that a gap of less than three *tefachim* is treated as closed — apply only at the edge of a roof, or equally in the middle? The previous discussion has shown that the laws of *pasul schach* can differ depending on whether the material is at the side or in the center of the *sukkah*. Now the Gemara asks whether *levud*, applied to gaps of air, follows the same logic.

The phrase *ve'hani mili min hatzad* — "this applies only on the side" — serves as the transition, signaling, as Rashi explains, that we are reverting to the more general question about *levud* and not continuing Abaye's ruling about minimum-size *sukkot*. The Gemara notes that *levud* has always been applied without apparent geographic restriction; it is a foundational principle by which objects less than three *tefachim* apart are considered connected. Yet perhaps, at least for roofs, the middle presents different considerations.

### Rav Acha position

Rav Acha holds *yesh levud be'emtza* — *levud* applies even in the middle of a roof. This is the more intuitive position: *levud* is a general principle, and there is no obvious reason to restrict it to the edges of any structure.

### Ravina position

Ravina holds *ein levud be'emtza* — *levud* does not apply in the middle of a roof. This is a striking claim. Tosafot is sensitive to the apparent novelty: we apply *levud* all the time, including in contexts where gaps occur far from any edge — for instance, constructing a virtual wall from posts spaced less than three *tefachim* apart throughout their entire length, not just at the sides. Tosafot therefore restricts Ravina's view specifically to roofs: something about the geometry or legal function of a roof surface may make *levud* inapplicable in its center.

### Beam proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ לָבוּד בָּאֶמְצַע — דְּתַנְיָא: קוֹרָה הַיּוֹצְאָה מִכּוֹתֶל זֶה וְאֵינָהּ נוֹגַעַת בְּכוֹתֶל אַחֵר, וְכֵן שְׁתֵּי קוֹרוֹת, אַחַת יוֹצְאָה מִכּוֹתֶל זֶה וְאַחַת יוֹצְאָה מִכּוֹתֶל אַחֵר וְאֵינָן נוֹגְעוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ — פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה, אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא קוֹרָה אַחֶרֶת. שְׁלֹשָׁה — צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא קוֹרָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What is the rationale** for the opinion **of the one who said:** The principle of ***lavud* is** applied even **in the center** of the *sukka*? It is **as it is taught** in the *Tosefta*: With regard to a cross **beam** of the merging of alleyways **that projects from this wall** of an alleyway **but does not touch the other** opposite **wall, and similarly,** with regard to **two** cross **beams, one projecting from this wall and one projecting from the other** opposite **wall and they do not touch each other,** if there is a gap of **less than three** handbreadths between the beam and the wall or between the two beams respectively, **one need not bring another** cross **beam** to render the alleyway fit for one to carry within it, as they are considered joined based on the principle of *lavud*. However, if there is a gap of **three** handbreadths, **one must bring another** cross **beam.** Apparently, the principle of *lavud* is applied even in the center.

The Gemara seeks textual support for each position. In favor of *yesh levud be'emtza*, a *baraita* is cited concerning the laws of *Shabbat* and carrying in a *chatzer*: *kora ha'yotze mi'kotel ze ve'eina noga'at be'kotel acher* — a beam (*kora*) that projects from one wall but does not reach the opposite wall; or alternatively, two beams, *achat yotze mi'kotel ze ve'achat yotze mi'kotel acher ve'einan nogot zu be'zo* — one from each wall, meeting in the middle without touching. The rule: *pachot mi'shlosha eino tzarich le'havi kora acheret* — if the gap is less than three *tefachim*, no additional beam is needed to validate the *eruv* arrangement; *shlosha — tzarich le'havi kora acheret* — at three *tefachim*, a supplementary beam is required. This shows *levud* being invoked where two beams nearly meet in the middle — a case that is emphatically not about the edges. It thus supports the position that *levud* applies in the middle.

There is an interesting nuance here: one opinion holds *kora mishum mechitza* — the functional rationale for a *kora* is that it creates a virtual wall beneath it. Even so, the *baraita* applies *levud* to it, suggesting no restriction to edges.

### Skylight proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין לָבוּד בָּאֶמְצַע — דִּתְנַן: אֲרוּבָּה שֶׁבַּבַּיִת וּבָהּ פּוֹתֵחַ טֶפַח, טוּמְאָה בַּבַּיִת — כּוּלּוֹ טָמֵא, מַה שֶּׁכְּנֶגֶד אֲרוּבָּה — טָהוֹר. טוּמְאָה כְּנֶגֶד אֲרוּבָּה — כָּל הַבַּיִת כּוּלּוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for the opinion **of the one who said:** The principle of ***lavud* does not** apply **in the center?** It is **as we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **a skylight in the** roof of a **house whose opening is one** square **handbreadth,** if there is a source of **ritual impurity** imparted by a corpse **inside the house,** all the objects in **the entire** house become **ritually impure,** as the legal status of the roof is that of a tent over a corpse. However, the objects **that are** directly **opposite the skylight are ritually pure,** as the roof does not cover that part of the house. If the source of **ritual impurity** is itself situated **aligned with the skylight,** all the objects in **the entire house are ritually pure,** as there is no roof over the source of impurity.

Against *levud be'emtza*, the Gemara cites a *mishnah* in the laws of *tumah ve'tahara*: *aruba she'be'bayit* — a skylight in a house. If the skylight is a *tefach* wide and there is *tumah* in the house, the entire house is *tamei* but what is directly beneath the skylight remains *tahor*, since there is no roof over that column of space. Conversely, if the *tumah* is beneath the skylight, the rest of the house is *tahor*. Now, if we applied *levud* in the middle of a roof — and the skylight is less than three *tefachim* — we should close the gap and treat the roof as whole. But the *mishnah* clearly treats the skylight as creating a distinct, unroofed zone, even when it is small. This suggests that in the middle of a roof, *levud* does not apply.

### Exception response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ? שָׁאנֵי הִלְכוֹת טוּמְאָה דְּהָכִי גְּמִירִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the other** Sage, who holds that *lavud* applies in the center, how would he explain the mishna? The Gemara answers: **The *halakhot* of ritual impurity are different, as that** is the way **they learned them** through tradition. The *halakhot* of tents and ritual impurity are *halakhot* transmitted to Moses from Sinai. Therefore, their details are unique, and other areas of *halakha* cannot be derived from them.

The Gemara dismisses this proof: *shani hilchos tumah, ve'hachi gmiri lah* — the laws of *tumah* constitute their own independent category, transmitted as a specific tradition, and one cannot generalize from them to other legal domains. The *tumah* rules regarding roofs and skylights reflect a particular received tradition, not the general operative principle of *levud*. Thus the general rule remains that *levud* applies everywhere, and the *tumah* case is treated as exceptional. As one formulation puts it: for physical structures, the gap is closed; for the metaphysical operation of *tumah*, the opening remains open.

## Public teachings

### Roof hole teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִלְעַאי: בַּיִת שֶׁנִּפְחַת וְסִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבָּיו — כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: רַבִּי, פָּרֵישׁ, כָּךְ פֵּירַשׁ אַבָּא: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת פְּסוּלָה, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai taught: A house that was breached and one roofed over it** is a **fit** *sukka*. **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said to him: My teacher, explain** your opinion. Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai said that **this is how** my **father explained** it: If the ceiling between the wall and the breach is **four cubits** long, the *sukka* is **unfit.** If it is **less than four cubits,** the *sukka* is **fit.**

The Gemara now records a series of public rulings delivered by Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Ilai. The use of the term *darash* — he expounded publicly — signals that these were teachings given in an open forum, apparently before *Sukkot*, not private transmissions. The first: *bayis shenifchat vesichach al gabav kesheira* — a house whose roof partially collapsed, and one covered the gap with valid *schach*, is a *kosher sukkah*. This is a restatement of the *dofen akumah* principle in its simplest form: if the gap in the middle of the existing roof is filled with *kasher schach*, the existing walls of the house serve as the *sukkah's* walls.

### Four amot qualification

Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi was present and challenged the ruling: *Rabbi, parish* — Rabbi, you must qualify what you are saying. He then transmits his father Rabbi Yosi's formulation: *arba amot pesulah, pachot mi'arba amot keshera* — if the *kasher schach* in the middle of the roof is *arba amot* or more removed from the wall, the *sukkah* is invalid, since *dofen akumah* cannot extend that far; if it is less than *arba amot* from the wall, the *sukkah* is valid. A public ruling offered without this qualification is incomplete, even if technically correct in many cases.

### Avruma fish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר אִלְעַאי: אַבְרוּמָא שַׁרְיָא. אֲמַר לְפָנָיו רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: רַבִּי, פָּרֵישׁ, כָּךְ אָמַר אַבָּא: שֶׁל מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי — אֲסוּרָה, שֶׁל מָקוֹם פְּלוֹנִי — מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai taught:** With regard to the **abramis [*avroma*],** it is **permitted** to eat it, despite the fact that it is a very small fish that is typically caught in a net with many similar, non-kosher, fish, and it is difficult to distinguish between them. **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, said to him: My teacher, explain** your opinion. Rabbi Yehuda bar Elai said that **this is how** my **father explained it:** The abramis found in the rivers **of place so-and-so,** where there are also non-kosher fish, is **prohibited;** however, the abramis **of** a different **place so-and-so,** where there are no non-kosher fish, is **permitted.**

In the same public setting, Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Ilai delivered a second ruling: *avruma sharya* — the *avruma* fish is permissible. As Rashi explains two lines down, *avruma* is a species of small fish in which tiny parasites may become embedded in the skin in a manner not immediately visible: *min dagim ktanim ve'ein shratzim nikarim bahem*. The question was whether one must be concerned about these nearly imperceptible organisms.

### Location dependency

Once again, Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yosi intervened: *Rabbi, parish* — you must be more precise. His father's teaching was location-dependent: *she'makom ploni asura, she'makom ploni mutara* — fish of a specific place are forbidden, while those of another are permitted.

### Modern prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי צַחַנְתָּא דְּבָב נַהֲרָא שַׁרְיָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּרְדִיפִי מַיָּא, וְהַאי דָּג טָמֵא כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ חוּט הַשִּׁדְרָה לָא מָצֵי קָאֵים — וְהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּקָאֵי! אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם דִּמְלִיחִי מַיָּא, וְהַאי דָּג טָמֵא, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ קִילְפֵי לָא מָצֵי קָאֵי — וְהָא קָא חָזֵינַן דְּקָאֵי! אֶלָּא: מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מְרַבֵּה טִינַיְיהוּ דָּג טָמֵא. אֲמַר רָבִינָא: וְהָאִידָּנָא, דְּשָׁפְכִי נְהַר אֵיתָן וּנְהַר גַּמְדָּא לְהָתָם, אֲסִירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that this is **similar** to **that which Abaye said: These small fish [*tzaḥanta*] of the Bav River are permitted.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that Abaye unequivocally permitted eating these fish and was not concerned about the potential presence of non-kosher fish among them? **If we say** that it is **due to** the fact **that the water flows rapidly, and these non-kosher fish, since they do not have a spinal cord, are not able to exist** in that water, as the current carries the non-kosher fish out of the Bav River, and consequently all the remaining fish are kosher, that is not the case. **Don't we see that** non-kosher fish **exist** in rivers with strong currents? **Rather,** perhaps Abaye permitted it **because the water is salty, and these non-kosher fish are not able to exist** in that water **because they do not have scales.** This, too, is not the case, as **don't we see that** non-kosher fish **exist** in salty water? **Rather,** Abaye permitted the small fish in the Bav River **because the mud in that** river **is not** suited for **non-kosher fish to reproduce.** The conditions in the river render it an unproductive habitat for non-kosher fish. **Ravina said: And today, since** the government built canals between the rivers, and the **Eitan River and** the **Gamda River spill into** the Bav, it is **prohibited** to eat the small fish without thorough inspection.

Abaye had previously explained this distinction by reference to a river called Bav: fish from the river Bav (*sakanta de'Bav nahara*) were permitted because the conditions of that river did not support the proliferation of the relevant parasites. The question of whether rushing water, salty water, or the absence of particular mud accounts for this was debated, with the Gemara noting — in a striking appeal to empirical evidence — that some proposed explanations were contradicted by direct observation. The conclusion was that the mud of that particular river simply did not sustain the reproduction of the relevant organisms, analogous to how a particular municipal water source may or may not contain certain microorganisms. Ravina adds a practical epilogue: *ve'idna, de'shafchi Nahar Eitan ve'Nahar Gamda le'hasam* — nowadays, the rivers Eitan and Gamda have diverted and empty into what was once the Bav river, and therefore *assira* — all such fish are currently forbidden, as the ecological conditions that once made them safe no longer pertain.

## Portico sukkah

### Portico case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: סִיכֵּךְ עַל גַּבֵּי אַכְסַדְרָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ פַּצִּימִין — כְּשֵׁרָה. שֶׁאֵין לָהּ פַּצִּימִין, אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: כְּשֵׁרָה, וְרָבָא אָמַר: פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disagree: **If one roofed a portico that has posts** on its open side, the *sukka* is **fit.** If one roofed a portico **that does not have posts** on its open side, **Abaye said:** The *sukka* is **fit,** and **Rava said:** The *sukka* is **unfit.**

The Gemara introduces a new case: *sikcha al gabei achsadra she'yesh la patzimin* — one placed *schach* over a portico (*achsadra*) that has posts (*patzimin*). The visual setting is a yard with a covered walkway running along one side, supported by columns. If the columns are spaced close enough together — within three *tefachim* of one another — they can function as a valid wall through the principle of *levud*, and the *sukkah* is valid. The harder case, taken up next, is when the posts cannot serve as walls.

---

## Portico sukkah (continued)

### Abaye allows it

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר כְּשֵׁרָה — אָמְרִינַן פִּי תִקְרָה יוֹרֵד וְסוֹתֵם. רָבָא אָמַר פְּסוּלָה — לָא אָמְרִינַן פִּי תִקְרָה יוֹרֵד וְסוֹתֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Abaye said:** The *sukka* is **fit,** as **we say** that **the edge of the roof descends and seals** the opening. The edge of the roof itself is considered as though it were a small partition that extends downward and forms a wall. **Rava said:** This *sukka* is **unfit,** as **we do not say** that **the edge of the roof descends and seals.**

When the posts cannot be utilized as walls — whether because they are too far apart or otherwise inadequate — Abaye rules *kesheira*: the *sukkah* is still valid. His basis: *amrinan pi tikra yored ve'sotem* — we invoke the principle that the edge of a roof descends and closes off the space below.

### Rava forbids

Rava rules *pesula*: the *sukkah* is invalid. *Lo amrinan pi tikra yored ve'sotem* — we do not apply this principle here.

### Roof edge as wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אַבָּיֵי, לְדִידָךְ דְּאָמְרַתְּ פִּי תִקְרָה יוֹרֵד וְסוֹתֵם, אֲפִילּוּ הִפְחִית דּוֹפֶן אֶמְצָעִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מוֹדֵינָא לָךְ בְּהָהִיא, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כְּמָבוֹי הַמְפוּלָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Abaye: According to you, who said: The edge of the roof descends and seals** the opening like a wall, then in a case where the roofing of the *sukka* consists of straight beams, **even if one removed the middle wall,** leaving the *sukka* with only two parallel walls, the *sukka* would nevertheless be fit. Since the edge of the roof descends and seals, the legal status of that *sukka* is the same as one that has walls on all sides. Abaye **said to him: I concede to you** that **in that** particular case the principle: The edge of the roof descends and seals, does not apply, **as it is** considered **like an open alleyway,** through which the multitudes pass on two opposite sides. In other cases, the principle applies.

The principle of *pi tikra yored ve'sotem* is a genuinely new concept, distinct from everything previously discussed. *Dofen akumah* bends the wall upward and then inward across the *schach*. *Gud assik* projects a physical wall upward to a full height. *Levud* closes a gap between nearby objects. *Pi tikra yored ve'sotem* does something different: it treats the squared-off edge of a roof as the beginning of a virtual wall descending downward from that edge. If the portico has solid, square-edged beams, the outer face of those beams can be treated as the top of a wall that descends and encloses the space beneath.

This principle is not limited to *sukkah*; it appears in the context of *eruv* as well. On the question of whether the roof edge must be a *tefach* wide to qualify, this would need to be verified, but three *tefachim* is certainly not required.

A further clarification follows from Rashi and Tosafot's extensive discussion in the *tosafot* here. Rashi reads Abaye as permitting *pi tikra yored ve'sotem* to supply all three walls of the *sukkah*. Tosafot finds this difficult and argues that no one is suggesting the concept can substitute for more than one wall; the scenario discussed by Abaye already involves two genuine perpendicular walls, and *pi tikra* is invoked only to supply the third.

Reading the Gemara according to Tosafot: Abaye's case involves a *sukkah* with two legitimate walls already in place, and he uses *pi tikra* to close the third. Rava denies that *pi tikra* can accomplish even this.

## Abaye-Rava in Shabbat

### Parallel debate?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּאִתְּמַר: אַכְסַדְרָה בְּבִקְעָה, רַב אָמַר: מוּתָּר לְטַלְטֵל בְּכוּלּוֹ, דְּאָמְרִינַן פִּי תִּקְרָה יוֹרֵד וְסוֹתֵם. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בָּהּ אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, דְּלָא אָמְרִינַן פִּי תִקְרָה יוֹרֵד וְסוֹתֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara suggests: Let us say** that **Abaye and Rava disagree with regard to** the same issue that was the subject **in the dispute of Rav and Shmuel;** they are merely elaborating on a fundamental dispute between other *amora'im*. **As it was stated:** *Amora'im* disagree with regard to **a portico,** which has a roof and no walls or incomplete walls, located **in a field,** which is a *karmelit*. **Rav said: It is permitted to move** an object **throughout the entire** portico, **as we say** that **the edge of the roof descends and seals** the opening, rendering the portico a private domain, as it is effectively surrounded by partitions. **And Shmuel said: One may move** an object **in** the portico **only within four** cubits, **as we do not say** that **the edge of the roof descends and seals** the opening. Therefore, the portico's legal status is that of the surrounding field. Ostensibly, the basis of the dispute between Abaye and Rava is identical to the basis of the dispute between Rav and Shmuel.

The Gemara asks whether the dispute between Abaye and Rava here reflects and maps onto the earlier dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning *Shabbat*. This is not merely an academic observation; if the mapping holds, the analysis of the *Shabbat* case sheds light on the limits and rationale of each position.

### Shabbat parallel

The *Shabbat* case involves an *achsadra b'bik'a* — a portico in an open valley. Carrying in a valley is normally prohibited (*karmelit*), but an enclosed space within it would permit carrying. The question is whether a portico — again, Tosafot reads this as already possessing two walls — can be considered sufficiently enclosed to permit carrying throughout.

### Rav on Shabbat

Rav holds *mutar letaltel b'kulo* — one may carry throughout the entire portico. His rationale: *d'amrinan pi tikra yored ve'sotem* — we apply the principle that the roof edge descends and encloses.

### Shmuel on Shabbat

Shmuel holds *ein metaltelin bah ela b'arba amot* — one may carry only within four *amot*, as in any open *karmelit*. His rationale: *delo amrinan pi tikra yored ve'sotem* — we do not apply the principle.

### Shmuel consistent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison and says: **According to** the opinion **of Shmuel, everyone,** even Abaye, **agrees** that one does not apply the principle: The edge of the roof descends and seals, to the case of a *sukka*.

The Gemara notes that according to Shmuel, who rejects *pi tikra yored ve'sotem* entirely, there is no debate: he would apply the same position to *sukkah*. He consistently rejects the concept, and his position aligns with Rava's ruling that the *sukkah* without usable posts is *pesulah*. The interesting question is whether Rava's position in *sukkah* necessarily mirrors Rav's position in *Shabbat*, and whether Abaye necessarily mirrors Rav.

### Two-wall concession

The Gemara reports Abaye's concession in response to a challenge from Rava. Rava asked: if you accept *pi tikra yored ve'sotem*, would you apply it even to supply the middle (back) wall of a *sukkah* — that is, where you have only two parallel side walls and no perpendicular back wall at all? Abaye concedes: *modina lach b'hahi — d'hava lah k'mavoy hamefulash* — in that case, I agree it does not work. Two parallel walls without a connecting back wall resemble a *mavoy hamefulash*, a thoroughfare open at both ends, which is not perceived as an enclosed space at all. *Pi tikra* can complete a third wall only when one already begins with a configuration that reads as an enclosed space — a corner, a designated area. Without that, the concept has nothing to work with.

This concession by Abaye is what drives Tosafot to read all the cases in this *sugya* as involving two pre-existing perpendicular walls: since Abaye himself concedes the *mavoy hamefulash* scenario, the simple reading of the Gemara that Abaye permits *pi tikra* to supply all walls must be wrong. He must be speaking only of cases where two proper walls already define a space.

### Roofed portico

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַב. אַבָּיֵי כְּרַב, וְרָבָא אָמַר לָךְ: עַד כָּאן לָא אָמַר רַב הָתָם אֶלָּא דִּמְחִיצוֹת לְאַכְסַדְרָה הוּא דַּעֲבִידִי, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָאו לְהָכִי עֲבִידִי, לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When they disagree** is **according to** the opinion of **Rav. Abaye** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav:** The edge of the roof descends and seals both in the portico in the field and in the portico that one roofed as a *sukka*. **And Rava** could have **said to you: Rav stated** his opinion **only there,** with regard to a portico in the field, because **the partitions** formed by the descent of the edge of the roof **are** partitions **established for the portico. However, here,** in the case of a *sukka*, **where** the partitions formed by the descent of the edge of the roof **are not** partitions **established for the** portico, **no,** Rav would not say that the edge of the roof descends and seals.

Rava then refines the comparison to the *Shabbat* case. He says: even I — who reject *pi tikra yored ve'sotem* for *sukkah* — would concede the *Shabbat* ruling of Rav. Why the asymmetry? Because in the *Shabbat* case, the *achsadra* in question is a *covered* portico. It has an actual roof above it. *Ed kan lo amar Rava hatam ela demchitzot le'achsadra hu de'avidi* — the walls and roof of that portico were built to serve the enclosed space beneath them. When the walls are constructed for the sake of the space below, using the roof edge to seal a third wall is coherent: the roof is organically connected to the space it encloses.

### Roof serves space

The critical distinction Rava articulates is this: *hatam demchitzot le'achsadra hu de'avidi, aval hacha delav le'hachi avidi lo* — there, the partitions serve the *achsadra*; here, they do not. In the *sukkah* case, the edge one wishes to use as a virtual wall is the edge of a roof external to the *sukkah* itself — the overhanging roof of the adjacent building, for instance. The edge of a roof that exists to serve a different space cannot function as the defining border of a new space. For *pi tikra yored ve'sotem* to apply, the roof from which the virtual wall descends must be one that was constructed to serve the enclosed area in question.

The Gemara does not make explicit in its running text the assumption that two walls are already in place; this emerges only from Abaye's concession regarding the *mavoy hamefulash* case. But Tosafot, integrating this concession with other *Gemarot*, concludes that the *pi tikra* concept throughout this entire *sugya* operates against the background of two legitimate existing walls — serving, at most, to supply the third.
