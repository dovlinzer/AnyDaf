# Makkot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father & Son Exile

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאָב גּוֹלֶה עַל יְדֵי הַבֵּן, וְהַבֵּן גּוֹלֶה עַל יְדֵי הָאָב. הַכֹּל גּוֹלִין עַל יְדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹלִין עַל יְדֵיהֶן, חוּץ מִגֵּר תּוֹשָׁב. וְגֵר תּוֹשָׁב אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The father is exiled** to a city of refuge **due to** his unintentional murder of his **son. And the son is exiled due to** his unintentional murder of his **father. Everyone is exiled due to** their unintentional murder of **a Jew, and a Jew is exiled due to** his unintentional murder of any of **them, except for** the unintentional murder of **a gentile who resides in Eretz Yisrael and observes the seven Noahide mitzvot [*ger toshav*]. And a *ger toshav* is exiled only due to** his unintentional murder of **a *ger toshav*.**

The second chapter of Makkot, *Elu Hen HaGolin*, concerns those who are exiled for unintentionally killing another person. The Mishnah on this daf reads that the father is exiled on account of his unintentional murder of his son, and the son is exiled on account of his unintentional murder of his father. Everyone — a category we will unpack shortly — is exiled for killing a *Yisrael*, and a Jew is exiled for killing any member of this group, except when the victim is a *ger toshav*. The *ger toshav* himself is exiled only when he has killed another *ger toshav*.

Three distinct categories appear in the background of this Mishnah. The *ger toshav* is someone who is not Jewish but who fulfills the seven Noahide laws and resides among the Jewish people. The *oved kochavim* is a non-Jew who has accepted none of these obligations. The *eved Kena'ani* is a Canaanite servant living within a Jewish household. These three, together with *Yisrael*, form the spectrum of persons whose status the Mishnah addresses.

### Why Father & Son?

The Mishnah's decision to single out the father and son is immediately striking. After all, any person who kills another unintentionally is subject to exile — why must the Mishnah specify this particular relationship? The Gemara will probe precisely this question, alongside the broader issue of who exactly is included in *hakol* and on what basis any member of that group might be excluded.

### Father Striking Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָאָב גּוֹלֶה עַל יְדֵי הַבֵּן. וְהָאמְרַתְּ: יָצָא הָאָב הַמַּכֶּה אֶת בְּנוֹ! דִּגְמִיר. הָאמְרַתְּ אַף עַל גַּב דִּגְמִיר מִצְוָה קָעָבֵיד! בִּשְׁוַלְיָא דְּנַגָּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: **The father is exiled** to a city of refuge **due to** his unintentional murder of his **son.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** in the previous mishna: **To exclude a father who strikes his son,** who is not exiled? The Gemara answers: This mishna is referring to a son **who is learned,** and there is no mitzva to strike him; therefore, since the striking is optional, the father is exiled. The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say: Even if** the son **is learned,** the father **performs a mitzva** by striking his son? The Gemara answers: This ruling of the mishna is stated **with regard to a carpenter's apprentice [*bishevaleya*].** Since the father is teaching his son carpentry, not Torah, there is no mitzva to strike him to spur him to study.

The Gemara opens with a challenge to the first clause: *ha'av goleh al yedei haben*. The father is exiled for the unintentional killing of his son — yet earlier we were told that a father who strikes his son and thereby unintentionally kills him is *yatza*, excluded from exile. How do we reconcile the two statements? The cases must differ in some meaningful way.

### Teaching Torah & Craft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁוַלְיָא דְּנַגָּרֵי – חַיּוּתֵאּ הִיא דְּלַמְּדֵיהּ! דִּגְמִיר אוּמָּנוּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: In the case of **a carpenter's apprentice,** his father **is teaching him a livelihood,** which is also a mitzva. The Gemara answers: The reference is to a son **who has** already **learned another craft,** and since he is able to earn his livelihood there is no mitzva to teach him a second craft.

The Gemara's answer turns on the context of *degarim* — the son has already learned. The question, then, is what exactly the father was teaching when the fatal blow occurred. The Mishnah's specification applies to a father teaching his son a profession, *shulya deNagrai* — an apprenticeship in carpentry, for instance — rather than Torah. One might object that teaching a son a trade is itself obligatory, since the verse *ur'eh chayim im ha-ishah asher ahavta* implies that providing one's child with a livelihood is a religious duty. The Gemara's resolution is that the case concerns a son who has already acquired both Torah learning and a professional skill, and the father is now teaching him an entirely different craft, *umanuta achrita*. This specificity is what the Mishnah is highlighting.

The discussion then turns to the second clause, *vehaben goleh al yedei ha'av*. The Gemara raises a *remini*: we already know from a general principle that anyone who kills another unintentionally is exiled, but there is an explicit exclusion — *prat lemakeh aviv* — for one who kills his father, who receives a more severe punishment rather than exile. So in what sense does the Mishnah say the son is exiled *on account of* the father?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַבֵּן גּוֹלֶה עַל יְדֵי הָאָב כּוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהִי, ״מַכֵּה נֶפֶשׁ״ – פְּרָט לַמַּכָּה אָבִיו. אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְהָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the son is exiled due to** his unintentional murder of his **father. And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*. It is written: "And a murderer shall flee there, one who strikes a person unintentionally" (Numbers 35:11), from which it is derived that **one who strikes a person** is exiled. This serves **to exclude one who strikes his father,** whose punishment for doing so intentionally is more severe than that of the standard intentional murderer, and therefore the son is not subject to exile. **Rav Kahana said:** This is **not difficult; this** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, and that** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis disagree (*Sanhedrin* 50a) whether a son who kills his father intentionally is executed by strangulation or by the sword.

Rav Kahana proposes a resolution: *lo kashya* — this is not a contradiction. The two statements reflect the positions of Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis respectively, and their disagreement hinges on the hierarchy of capital punishments. Rabbi Shimon maintains that *chenek* (strangulation) is more severe than *sayaf* (beheading by sword), while the Rabbis hold the reverse — *sayaf* is more severe than *chenek*. The principle at stake is that unintentional commission of an act punishable by a given mode of execution can be atoned through exile, but only if that mode of execution falls below a certain threshold of severity. According to Rabbi Shimon, for whom strangulation is more severe, the son who unintentionally kills his father — whose intentional act would be punishable by strangulation — cannot be atoned through exile. According to the Rabbis, who rank beheading as more severe, the unintentional commission of an act punishable by the sword *can* be atoned through exile. Hence the Mishnah's statement that the son is exiled reflects the Rabbinic position, while the exclusion reflects Rabbi Shimon's.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר חֶנֶק חָמוּר מִסַּיִיף, שִׁגְגַת סַיִיף נִיתְּנָה לְכַפָּרָה, שִׁגְגַת חֶנֶק לֹא נִיתְּנָה לְכַפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **According to Rabbi Shimon, who says: Strangulation** is more **severe than** execution by the **sword,** which is why a son who kills his father is liable to be executed by strangulation, one who commits the **unwitting** violation of a prohibition generally punishable by execution by the **sword,** i.e., intentional murder of one who is not a parent, **is eligible for atonement** by means of exile; one who commits the **unwitting** violation of a prohibition generally punishable by the more severe death of **strangulation,** i.e., intentional murder of a parent, **is not eligible for atonement** by means of exile.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי סַיִיף חָמוּר מֵחֶנֶק, הוֹרֵג אָבִיו [בְּשׁוֹגֵג] שִׁגְגַת סַיִיף הוּא, וְשִׁגְגַת סַיִיף נִיתְּנָה לְכַפָּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, **according to the Rabbis, who say:** Execution by the **sword** is more **severe than strangulation,** which is why a son who kills his father is liable to be executed by the sword, a son who **unintentionally kills his father** has performed **an unwitting** violation of a prohibition generally punishable by execution by the **sword. And** one who commits **an unwitting** violation of a prohibition generally punishable by execution by the **sword,** i.e., intentional murder of one who is not a parent, **is eligible for atonement** by means of exile.

Why exactly the severity of the mode of execution determines eligibility for exile remains difficult to fully unpack. It appears to rest on *gezeirat hakatuv* — a scriptural decree that simply establishes the categories without compelling logical derivation. The *ir miklat* itself is in many respects a *gezeirat hakatuv*, and the same applies to the categories of who may enter it.

### Son Killing Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: פְּרָט לְעוֹשֶׂה חַבּוּרָה בְּאָבִיו בְּשׁוֹגֵג. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כֵּיוָן דִּבְמֵזִיד בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא, בְּשׁוֹגֵג נָמֵי לִיגְלֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** That which was derived in the *baraita* that one who strikes a person is exiled, to exclude one who strikes his father, who is not exiled, is not referring to a son who unintentionally kills his father, as in that case the son is certainly exiled. Rather, it is **to exclude one** who **inflicts a wound on his father unwittingly.** The novel element of this derivation is that **it enters your mind to say: Since** in a case **where** he wounded his father **intentionally, he is liable to be executed** like a murderer (see Exodus 21:15), if he did so **unwittingly, let him also be exiled** like an unintentional murderer. Therefore, this derivation **teaches us** that exile is administered specifically to an unintentional murderer, but it is not administered to the unwitting performer of any other transgression.

Rava offers an alternative explanation that focuses on the nature of the son's act itself. He argues that when a son kills his father, even unintentionally, two transgressions are necessarily involved: he must have struck him (*chavurah*) and the blow must have been fatal. This double dimension lends the act a quality that borders on intentionality. Since had the act been intentional (*bemezid*) the son would be liable to execution (*bar katla*), unintentionally (*beshogeg*) he is exiled. The Mishnah therefore specifically mentions the son's exile in relation to the father to teach us that despite the compounded nature of the transgression, exile remains the appropriate atonement in the unintentional case. As the *Aruch LaNer* notes, a scriptural verse is still needed, since the logical argument alone is not fully compelling.

## Categories of Killers

### Hakol Golin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל גּוֹלִין עַל יְדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְכוּ׳. ״הַכֹּל גּוֹלִין עַל יְדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵיתוֹיֵי עֶבֶד וְכוּתִי. תְּנֵינָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֶבֶד וְכוּתִי גּוֹלֶה וְלוֹקֶה עַל יְדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹלֶה וְלוֹקֶה עַל יְדֵי כּוּתִי וְעֶבֶד.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Everyone is exiled due to** their unintentional murder of **a Jew,** and a Jew is exiled due to his unintentional murder of any of them. The Gemara asks: **Everyone is exiled due to** their unintentional murder of **a Jew: What** case that was not already specified does this phrase serve **to add?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to add** a Canaanite **slave and a Samaritan.** The Gemara notes: **We learn** by inference from this mishna **that which the Sages taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: A Canaanite **slave and a Samaritan are exiled and flogged due to a Jew, and a Jew is exiled and flogged due to a Samaritan and** a Canaanite **slave.**

The Mishnah's phrase *hakol golin al yedei Yisrael* — everyone is exiled for the killing of a Jew — prompts the Gemara to ask: *le'ituyei mai*? What is the Mishnah adding with the word *hakol*? The answer: *le'ituyei eved ve'Kuti* — it comes to include the Canaanite servant and the Samaritan (*Kuti*).

### Servants & Samaritans

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא עֶבֶד וְכוּתִי גּוֹלֶה עַל יְדֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלוֹקֶה, גּוֹלֶה – דְּקַטְלֵיהּ, וְלוֹקֶה – דְּלַטְיֵיהּ. אֶלָּא יִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹלֶה וְלוֹקֶה עַל יְדֵי כּוּתִי? בִּשְׁלָמָא גּוֹלֶה – דְּקַטְלֵיהּ, אֶלָּא לוֹקֶה, אַמַּאי? דְּלַטְיֵיהּ? ״וְנָשִׂיא בְעַמְּךָ לֹא תָאֹר״ – בְּעוֹשֶׂה מַעֲשֵׂה עַמְּךָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **Granted, a slave and a Samaritan are exiled and flogged due to a Jew;** either of them is **exiled** in a case **where he killed** a Jew unintentionally, **and flogged** in a case **where he cursed** the Jew, thereby violating a Torah prohibition. **But** with regard to the *halakha* that **a Jew is exiled and flogged due to a Samaritan, granted,** the Jew is **exiled** in a case **where** he **killed** the Samaritan unintentionally, **but** the Jew **is flogged for** violating **what** prohibition? **For cursing** the Samaritan? Isn't that prohibition derived from the verse: **"And a leader in your people you may not curse"** (Exodus 22:27), from which it is inferred that the prohibition is in effect only **with regard to** one who **performs an action** characteristic **of your people,** who comports himself as an observant, God-fearing Jew? Even if the conversion of the Samaritans was valid, they regularly violate numerous prohibitions, and they do not comport themselves as observant Jews.

A *baraita* elaborates: *eved ve'Kuti goleh velokeh al yedei Yisrael, ve'Yisrael goleh velokeh al yedei Kuti ve'eved*. A Canaanite servant and a Samaritan are exiled for the unintentional killing of a Jew, and are also flogged on account of a Jew. Conversely, a Jew is exiled for the unintentional killing of a *Kuti* or servant, and is likewise flogged on their account. The Gemara immediately distinguishes the two penalties: exile applies for killing (*de'katlei*), and lashes apply for cursing (*de'lateih*).

### Cursing Non-Jews

This is where the analysis becomes complicated, particularly regarding lashes for cursing. The verse states *venasi be'amcha lo ta'or* — you shall not curse a leader among your people — and the Gemara understands *be'amcha* to refer specifically to one who acts as part of your people, *oseh ma'aseh amcha*. How then does a Jew incur lashes for cursing a *Kuti* or non-Jew? The lines are murky, and the Gemara acknowledges the difficulty.

### False Testimony Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהֵעִיד בּוֹ וְהוּזַם. דִּכְווֹתֵיהּ גַּבֵּי עֶבֶד, שֶׁהֵעִיד בּוֹ וְהוּזַם, עֶבֶד בַּר עֵדוּת הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** This *baraita* is not referring to lashes administered for cursing; rather, one is flogged in a case **where** a Jew **testified against** a Samaritan and a Samaritan testified against a Jew **and he was rendered a conspiring witness.** The Gemara asks: Does that mean that **in the corresponding** situation **with regard to** a Canaanite **slave,** a slave is flogged in a case **where** he **testified against** a Jew **and was rendered a conspiring witness? Is a slave eligible to** give **testimony?**

Rav Acha bar Yaakov attempts to resolve this by suggesting the case involves false testimony followed by *hazama* — refutation. If someone testified falsely and was subsequently subjected to *hazama*, the *edim zomemim* receive the punishment that would have befallen the defendant. Perhaps the lashes derive from this mechanism. The Gemara immediately challenges this: is a *eved* (Canaanite servant) even eligible to testify? The answer is that he is not, so this framing cannot stand either.

### Insignificant Blow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִכָּהוּ הַכָּאָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הִכָּהוּ הַכָּאָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said: What are we dealing with here?** The Jew is flogged due to a slave and a Samaritan, and the slave and the Samaritan are flogged due to the Jew in a case **where one struck** the other with **a blow that does not have** the capacity to cause damage **worth one *peruta*, as Rabbi Ami says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Although one who strikes another pays damages and is not flogged, if **he struck him** with **a blow that does not have** the capacity to cause damage **worth one *peruta*,** since there is no payment, **he is flogged** for violating a Torah prohibition.

A more refined resolution is then offered by Rav Acha son of Rav Ika. The specific case under discussion involves *haka'ah she'ein bah shaveh prutah* — a blow that does not cause damage worth even one *prutah*. Rav Ami citing Rabbi Yochanan teaches: one who strikes another with a blow worth less than a *prutah* — since there is no monetary payment due — incurs lashes for violating the Torah prohibition. The case of lashes in the *baraita* is thus this precise scenario: a blow too minor to generate financial liability, but serious enough to constitute a violation warranting corporal punishment.

## Ger Toshav Status

### Ger Toshav Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מֵעַל יְדֵי גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב וְכוּ׳. אַלְמָא גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב גּוֹי הוּא. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב גּוֹלֶה עַל יְדֵי גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב! אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּגֵר תּוֹשָׁב שֶׁהָרַג גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב, כָּאן בְּגֵר תּוֹשָׁב שֶׁהָרַג יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Everyone is exiled due to their unintentional murder of a Jew, and a Jew is exiled due to all of them, **except for** when it is **due to a *ger toshav*.** And a *ger toshav* is exiled due to his unintentional murder of a *ger toshav*. The Gemara comments: **Apparently,** one may conclude that **a *ger toshav* is a gentile,** and therefore he is not exiled when he unintentionally kills a Jew. **Say the latter clause** of the mishna: **A *ger toshav* is exiled due to** his unintentional murder of **a *ger toshav*,** indicating that his halakhic status is not that of a gentile, as gentiles are not liable to be exiled. There is an apparent contradiction between the two clauses in the mishna. **Rav Kahana said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** in the latter clause of the mishna, it is **in** the case of **a *ger toshav* who killed a *ger toshav*** that he is exiled; **there,** in the first clause, it is **in** the case of **a *ger toshav* who killed a Jew.** In the case described in the first clause he is not exiled, as his halakhic status is not that of a Jew, for whom the sin of unintentional murder of a Jew can be atoned through exile.

The Mishnah states that a Jew is exiled for unintentional killings across the board *chutz miger toshav* — except with respect to the *ger toshav*. Some commentaries note that the text as it stands is ambiguous — is the exception about a *ger toshav* being the victim, or the *ger toshav* as the perpetrator? The Gemara itself flags the difficulty, with some suggesting the correct reading should be *chutz miger toshav* rather than *chutz me'al yedei ger toshav*.

What is at stake is a fundamental question: is the *ger toshav* categorically equivalent to the *oved kochavim*, or does his acceptance of the Noahide laws and his residence among the Jewish people grant him a distinct intermediate status? *Alma ger toshav oved kochavim hu* — is the *ger toshav* simply a non-Jew? The Gemara poses this as an open question.

### Two Pesukim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּרָמֵי קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי, כְּתִיב: ״לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלַגֵּר וְלַתּוֹשָׁב בְּתוֹכָם תִּהְיֶינָה שֵׁשׁ הֶעָרִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיוּ לָכֶם הֶעָרִים לְמִקְלָט״, ״לָכֶם״ – וְלֹא לְגֵרִים!
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who raised a contradiction between** two **verses. It is written: "For the children of Israel and for the stranger and for the resident [*velatoshav*] among them, shall** these **six cities be** for refuge" (Numbers 35:15), indicating that a *ger toshav* is exiled. **And it is written** in a previous verse: **"And the cities shall be for you for refuge"** (Numbers 35:12), from which it is inferred: A refuge **for you but not for a *ger*** *toshav*, indicating that a *ger toshav* is not exiled. The contradiction is direct.

Two verses in Bamidbar seem to point in opposite directions. Bamidbar 35:15 reads *livnei Yisrael vela-ger vela-toshav betocham tihyena sheish ha-arim* — the six cities of refuge shall be for the children of Israel, and for the *ger*, and for the *toshav* among them. This verse, using the formulation *la-ger vela-toshav* (reminiscent of Avraham's *ger ve-toshav anochi*), implies that the *arei miklat* are available to all residents of the land. Yet Bamidbar 35:12 states *vehayu lachem ha-arim lemiklat* — the cities shall be for *you* — from which we derive *lachem velo lagerim*: for you and not for the strangers. The contradiction is direct.

### Rav Kahana's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּגֵר תּוֹשָׁב שֶׁהָרַג גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב, כָּאן בְּגֵר תּוֹשָׁב שֶׁהָרַג יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** The contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita* is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is referring to a case **where he killed him in a downward** motion; **there,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he killed him in an upward** motion. Rav Ḥisda elaborates: If one *ger toshav* killed another *ger toshav* **in a downward** motion, which is a case **where** if the perpetrator were **a Jew,** he would **be exiled,** the *ger toshav* **also suffices with exile.** But if the *ger toshav* killed his counterpart **in an upward** motion, which is a case **where** if the perpetrator were **a Jew,** he would **be exempt** from exile, the *ger toshav* **is executed.**

Rav Kahana resolves the tension: *lo kashya* — this is not a contradiction. One verse applies to a *ger toshav* who killed another *ger toshav*, and the other applies to a *ger toshav* who killed a *Yisrael*. As a resident of the land, the *ger toshav* is eligible for the *ir miklat* when he has killed one of his own kind. But if he kills a Jew, exile cannot serve as atonement and he faces execution. The two verses thus address distinct scenarios rather than conflicting rules.

### Baraita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: לְפִיכָךְ גֵּר וְגוֹי שֶׁהָרְגוּ – נֶהֱרָגִין. קָתָנֵי גֵּר דּוּמְיָא דְּגוֹי: מָה גּוֹי לָא שְׁנָא דִּקְטַל בַּר מִינֵּיהּ, וְלָא שְׁנָא דִּקְטַל דְּלָאו בַּר מִינֵיהּ – נֶהֱרָג, אַף גֵּר, לָא שְׁנָא דִּקְטַל בַּר מִינֵּיהּ וְלָא שְׁנָא קְטַל דְּלָאו בַּר מִינֵיהּ – נֶהֱרָג!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** to the *halakha* of the mishna from a *baraita*, which teaches: **Therefore, a *ger*** *toshav* **and a gentile who killed** a person **are killed,** even if they did so unintentionally. The Gemara infers based on the juxtaposition of a *ger toshav* and a gentile in the *baraita*: The *tanna* **teaches** the case of **a *ger*** *toshav* in a manner **similar** to the case of **a gentile: Just as** with regard to **a gentile** it is **no different** in a case **where he killed one of his kind,** a gentile, **and** it is **no different** in a case **where he killed one not of his kind,** a Jew, as in either case **he is executed** and is not exiled, **so too,** with regard to **a *ger*** *toshav*, it is **no different** in a case **where he killed one of his kind,** a *ger toshav*, **and** it is **no different** in a case **where he killed one not of his kind,** a Jew, as in either case **he is executed** and is not exiled.

The Gemara raises a fresh contradiction from a *baraita*: *lefichach ger ve-oved kochavim she-haregu neheragin* — therefore, a *ger* and an *oved kochavim* who kill are executed. The *baraita* groups the *ger* together with the *oved kochavim*, and argues by analogy: just as with the *oved kochavim* it makes no difference whether he killed someone of his own kind or someone of a different status — he is executed regardless — so too the *ger* should be executed regardless of whom he killed. This undercuts Rav Kahana's distinction, which depended on *whom* the *ger toshav* killed determining the outcome.

### Downward vs Upward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן שֶׁהֲרָגוֹ דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה, כָּאן שֶׁהֲרָגוֹ דֶּרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה. דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל גָּלֵי – אִיהוּ נָמֵי סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּגָלוּת. דֶּרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל פָּטוּר – הוּא נֶהֱרָג.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** The contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita* is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is referring to a case **where he killed him in a downward** motion; **there,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **where he killed him in an upward** motion. Rav Ḥisda elaborates: If one *ger toshav* killed another *ger toshav* **in a downward** motion, which is a case **where** if the perpetrator were **a Jew,** he would **be exiled,** the *ger toshav* **also suffices with exile.** But if the *ger toshav* killed his counterpart **in an upward** motion, which is a case **where** if the perpetrator were **a Jew,** he would **be exempt** from exile, the *ger toshav* **is executed.**

Rav Chisda proposes a different resolution: *lo kashya — kan she-harago derech yerida, kan she-harago derech aliya*. The distinction is not about whom was killed, but *how* the killing occurred — whether it was in a downward motion (*derech yerida*) or an upward motion (*derech aliya*). A downward killing, such as dropping an object that falls onto someone below, is understood as carrying a lesser degree of culpability — more plausibly accidental. An upward killing, such as throwing something that strikes someone, implies a more active and directed motion. The rule, as Rav Chisda formulates it, is: *derech yerida de-Yisrael gali ihu nami sagi lei be-galut* — in a downward-motion case, if a Jew would be exiled, the *ger toshav* similarly suffices with exile. *Derech aliya de-Yisrael patur ihu neherag* — in an upward-motion case, where a Jew would be exempt from exile (*patur* here meaning exempt from *galut* but liable to some other consequence), the *ger toshav* is executed.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְלָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא? וּמָה דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל גָּלֵי, אִיהוּ נָמֵי סַגִּי לֵיהּ בְּגָלוּת, דֶּרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל פָּטוּר, אִיהוּ נֶהֱרָג?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: And is it not** derived by means of **an *a fortiori*** inference to the contrary? **If** one unintentionally kills another in **a downward** motion, which is a case **where** if the perpetrator were **a Jew,** he would **be exiled,** the *ger toshav* **also suffices with exile** and nothing more, but if he killed in **an upward** motion, which is a case **where** if the perpetrator were **a Jew,** he would **be exempt** from exile, is **he killed?**

Rava challenges Rav Chisda's framework by means of a *kal vachomer*. If in the downward-motion case — where the Jew would be exiled — the *ger toshav* is also only exiled, fine. But in the upward-motion case — where a Jew would be exempt from exile — shouldn't the *ger toshav* also at most be exempt from exile, rather than being executed? The *kal vachomer* seems to run in exactly the opposite direction from Rav Chisda's conclusion. The argument resists easy resolution, and the Gemara shifts course.

### Permitted Killing Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בְּאוֹמֵר מוּתָּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אוֹמֵר מוּתָּר אָנוּס הוּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר, אוֹמֵר מוּתָּר – קָרוֹב לְמֵזִיד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The case in the *baraita* where a *ger toshav* is killed rather than exiled is **where** the *ger toshav* who killed another *ger toshav* **says** that **it is permitted** to kill the victim. If he killed him unintentionally he is exiled, in accordance with the ruling in the mishna. **Abaye said to him:** One who **says** that **it is permitted** to kill the victim **is a victim of circumstances beyond his control,** as he was unaware of the prohibition. Why, then, should he be executed? Rava **said to him:** That is not a problem, **as I say** that with regard to **one who says** that **it is permitted,** since he intended to kill the other, his action **borders on the intentional.**

Rava proposes an alternative framing: *be'omer mutar* — the case concerns a *ger toshav* who killed another *ger toshav* believing that the killing was permitted. This introduces a new axis of analysis, one that goes beyond the physical mechanics of the killing and focuses on the killer's state of mind.

### Mutar Anuss Category

Abaye challenges Rava on precisely this point. He distinguishes between *mutar anuss* — one who believed the act was permitted due to circumstances genuinely beyond his control — and *mutar mezid* — one whose belief that the act was permitted itself reflects a kind of recklessness or quasi-intentionality. Rava's response is that one who says *mutar* — that the killing is permitted — is *karov lemezid*, close to intentional. Even within the domain of *shegaga*, there is gradation: unintentional killing can range from the truly coerced and unavoidable to the nearly deliberate.

This distinction maps roughly onto the difference between negligence and recklessness in legal reasoning: a driver exercising every caution who is struck by another vehicle occupies an entirely different moral and legal category from one driving recklessly in poor conditions after drinking. It is the same external outcome — an unintentional death — but the internal quality of the act differs fundamentally.

### Mistaken Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאִיתְּמַר: כְּסָבוּר בְּהֵמָה וְנִמְצָא אָדָם, גּוֹי וְנִמְצָא גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב. רָבָא אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיב, אוֹמֵר מוּתָּר קָרוֹב לְמֵזִיד הוּא. רַב חִסְדָּא אוֹמֵר: פָּטוּר, אוֹמֵר מוּתָּר אָנוּס הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara observes: **And** Rava and Rav Ḥisda **follow their** standard line of **reasoning, as** is indicated by the fact that **it was stated** that they disagree in the case of a *ger toshav* who killed a person. If **he thought** he was killing **an animal and it was discovered** that it was **a person,** or if he thought he was killing **a gentile and it was discovered** that he was **a *ger toshav*, Rava says** he is **liable** to be executed, as with regard to **one who says** that **it is permitted,** his action **borders on the intentional. Rav Ḥisda says** he is **exempt, as** one who **says** that **it is permitted** to kill the victim **is a victim of circumstances beyond his control.**

The Gemara observes that Abaye and Rava are consistent with their respective positions elsewhere: *ve'azdu letameihu de'itmar kesavor behemah venimtza adam, Kena'ani venimtza ger toshav*. Another mode of *shegaga* — one that operates independently of the physical mechanics — is mistaken identity. The killer thought he was killing an animal (*behemah*) but it turned out to be a human being (*adam*); or he thought he was killing a *Kena'ani* servant and it turned out to be a *ger toshav*. Like the famous image in *The Little Prince* — what adults see as a hat is in fact a snake that has swallowed an elephant — the same act can be entirely different in nature depending on what the actor perceived.

Rava rules that in the case of *omer mutar* — believing the killing to be permitted — the person is *chayav*, liable, because his actions border on intentional. Rav Chisda rules that such a person is *patur* in the capital sense, treating him as a victim of circumstances beyond his control, an *omer mutar anuss*. The tension between the two positions — one emphasizing the culpable proximity to intentionality, the other emphasizing the involuntary nature of the error — remains unresolved.

## Avimelech Story

### Avimelech & Sarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: ״הִנְּךָ מֵת עַל הָאִשָּׁה אֲשֶׁר לָקַחְתָּ״. מַאי לָאו בִּידֵי אָדָם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda** from that which is written with regard to Abimelech, king of Gerar, who took Sarah, Abraham's wife: "And God came to Abimelech in a dream of the night and said to him: **You shall die for the woman you took,** as she is a man's wife" (Genesis 20:3). When Abimelech took Sarah he was under the impression that she was unmarried, as Abraham said that she was his sister. Despite the fact that Abimelech was one who says that it is permitted, he was liable to be executed for his action. **What, is it not** that he was liable to be executed **at the hand of** a court composed of **people,** indicating that one who says that it is permitted is liable?

Rava now brings a proof from the story of Avimelech and Sarah to support his position that *omer mutar* borders on intentional. The Gemara cites the verse spoken by God to Avimelech in a dream: *hincha met al ha-ishah asher lakachta* — you are going to die on account of the woman you have taken. Avimelech had taken Sarah from Avraham, believing she was Avraham's sister — a paradigmatic case of acting in error, under circumstances he believed to be legitimate.

The Gemara asks: what does *hincha met* mean here? Is this a death penalty to be administered *bidei adam* — by a human court? The Gemara considers this and concludes: *bidei shamayim* — the judgment is from heaven. The proof comes from the verse in which God says *mehato li*, you have transgressed against Me. The Gemara then draws a parallel with Yosef, who when resisting Potiphar's wife declared *vehatati lelohim* — I would sin against God. The Gemara asks: was Yosef not equally sinning against a man? It answers: *ella dino masur le'adam* — yes, but the judgment for such an act is ultimately rendered by people, by a human court. And the same applies here: *hacha nami dino masur le'adam* — in Avimelech's case too, his judgment is given over to human reckoning. This is Rava's point: even though Avimelech acted in genuine ignorance of the full situation — he was told she was a sister — God declares him deserving of death. The *omer mutar* does not escape liability simply by virtue of his error.

### Return of Sarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם. דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵחֲטוֹא לִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that conclusion: **No,** it means that he was liable to be executed **at the hand of Heaven,** but not by a court. The Gemara adds: The language of the verses **is also precise** in this regard, **as it is written:** "And I also prevented you **from sinning against Me"** (Genesis 20:6), indicating that it was a sin vis-à-vis God and was not in the jurisdiction of a court of men.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, ״וְחָטָאתִי לֵאלֹהִים״ – לֵאלֹהִים וְלֹא לְאָדָם? אֶלָּא, דִּינוֹ מָסוּר לְאָדָם, הָכָא נָמֵי דִּינוֹ מָסוּר לָאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to your reasoning** that based on the formulation of the verse one concludes that it was a sin exclusively vis-à-vis God, where Joseph says to Potiphar's wife: "And how can I perform this great evil, **and sin to God"** (Genesis 39:9), may one also infer that it was a sin **vis-à-vis God and not vis-à-vis man?** Joseph is referring to adultery, which is punishable by execution according to the Noahide laws as well. **Rather,** in that case it means that although he performed a sin vis-à-vis God, **his judgment is given over to** the ruling of **man; here too,** in the verse about Abimelech, it can be explained that although he performed a sin vis-à-vis God, **his judgment is given over to** the ruling of **man.** There is no proof from this verse with regard to the status of one who says that it is permitted to perform a transgression.

Abaye raises an objection to this reading. Avimelech himself protested: *hagoy gam tzadik taharog* — will You slay a nation even though it is righteous? And God did not simply reject his plea outright. Does this not imply divine acceptance that Avimelech was, to some degree, innocent?

The Gemara responds that the return of Sarah was mandated regardless — *mikol makom* — on multiple grounds. God's instruction *ve'atah hashev eshet ha'ish ki navi hu* — now return the wife of this man, for he is a prophet — does not mean, as a superficial reading might suggest, that Sarah must be returned *only because* Avraham is a prophet and that she could have been retained had he been an ordinary man. That reading is immediately rejected as absurd. Rather, as Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani says in the name of Rabbi Yonatan, God's message to Avimelech was this: return the wife unconditionally. And as for your claim of righteousness — *halo hu amar li achoti hi*, he told me she was his sister — consider that claim more carefully.

### Ben Noach Lesson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן שֶׁבֶּן נֹחַ נֶהֱרָג, שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לִלְמוֹד וְלֹא לָמַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **From here** it is derived **that a descendant of Noah,** i.e., a gentile, **is executed** for a capital offense even if he says that it is permitted, **as he should have learned** that it is prohibited **and he did not learn.**

The Gemara then shifts to an examination of how Avraham knew that Avimelech's intentions were not pure. The teaching is: *achsanai hu sheba la'ir al iskei achila ushtiya shoalin oto* — when a guest comes to town, the host's proper concern is food and drink, asking after the traveler's wellbeing and the journey. *Klum shoalin oto ishtecha zo achotecha zo* — would any proper host immediately ask a guest, "Is that your wife or your sister?" The fact that Avimelech asked precisely this question revealed the nature of his interest.

From this the Gemara derives: *mikan sheben Noach neherag shehaya lo lilmod velo lamad* — from here we learn that a *ben Noach* who should have learned and did not learn is liable to execution. Avimelech, as a *ben Noach* — analogous to the *ger toshav* who lives among the Jewish people — had the obligation to learn proper norms from those around him. His failure to internalize basic ethical conduct, revealed by the very nature of his inquiry, strips him of the defense of pure ignorance. Avraham intuited from Avimelech's question alone that his surroundings were dangerous — which is precisely why he had instructed Sarah beforehand. The *ger toshav* who fails to learn from the Jewish community around him bears moral responsibility for what follows.

## Blind & Hater

### Blind Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַסּוֹמֵא אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: גּוֹלֶה. הַשּׂוֹנֵא אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הַשּׂוֹנֵא נֶהֱרָג, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּמוּעָד. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ שׂוֹנֵא גּוֹלֶה וְיֵשׁ שׂוֹנֵא שֶׁאֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כֹּל שֶׁהוּא יָכוֹל לוֹמַר לְדַעַת הָרַג – אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה, וְשֶׁלֹּא לְדַעַת הָרַג – הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A blind person** who unintentionally murdered another **is not exiled;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** He is **exiled. The enemy** of the victim **is not exiled,** as presumably it was not a completely unintentional act. **Rabbi Yosei says:** Not only is **an enemy** not exiled, but he **is executed** by the court, **because** his halakhic status is **like** that of one who is **forewarned** by witnesses not to perform the action, as presumably he performed the action intentionally. **Rabbi Shimon says: There is an enemy who is exiled and there is an enemy who is not exiled. This is the principle:** In **any** case **where** an observer **could say he killed knowingly,** where circumstances lead to the assumption that it was an intentional act, the enemy **is not exiled,** even if he claims that he acted unintentionally. **And** if it is clear that **he killed unknowingly,** as circumstances indicate that he acted unintentionally, **he is exiled,** even though the victim is his enemy.

A new Mishnah introduces two further special cases. *Hasuma eino goleh divrei Rabbi Yehuda; Rabbi Meir omer goleh* — the blind person does not go into exile; this is the view of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says that the blind person does go into exile.

### The Hater

The Mishnah then addresses the *soneh* — the hater. It is important to note that the Hebrew *soneh* is not the same as *oyev*, an enemy. An enemy is someone openly opposed to you; a *soneh* is someone who harbors hatred toward you, potentially while maintaining outward relations. The verse we recite when opening the *aron* — *vayehi binsoa ha'aron vayomer Moshe: kumah Hashem veyafutzu oyvecha vayanusu mesanecha* — distinguishes between *oyvecha* (your enemies) and *mesanecha* (those who hate you), two distinct categories. The Mishnah's ruling concerns the latter. *Hasoneh eino goleh* — the hater does not go into exile.

### Hater Executed

Rabbi Yosi goes further: *hasoneh neherag mipnei shehu kemuad* — the hater is executed because he is considered like one who has already been warned. The logic is that a person harboring hatred toward the victim possesses an internal disposition that makes the killing quasi-intentional. The *soneh* does not require formal *hatrah* (prior warning) because the animus itself functions as the operative warning.

### Shimon's Distinction

Rabbi Shimon draws a more nuanced line: *yesh soneh goleh ve'yesh soneh she'eino goleh* — there is a hater who is exiled, and there is a hater who is not exiled. The operative principle: *kol shehu yachol lomar lada'at harag eino goleh, veshelo lada'at harag harei zeh goleh* — anyone of whom it can be said that he killed knowingly does not receive exile; one who killed without that knowledge of intent goes into exile. Rabbi Shimon thus distinguishes within the category of *soneh* based on whether the particular killing evidenced deliberate direction of the hateful impulse.

### Belo Re'ot Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ – פְּרָט לְסוֹמֵא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַסּוֹמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Apropos the dispute in the mishna between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir with regard to a blind person, **the Sages taught:** It is written that one is exiled to a city of refuge if he killed another **"without seeing"** (Numbers 35:23), indicating that the reference is to one who has the capacity to see. This serves **to exclude a blind person;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says:** On the contrary, the term **"without seeing"** serves **to include a blind person.**

The Gemara seeks the textual basis for these positions in the phrase *belo re'ot* — "without seeing" — which appears in the context of the *ir miklat*. *Tanu Rabanan: belo re'ot perat lesuma divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — the phrase "without seeing" excludes the blind person from the *ir miklat*, according to Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir, however, reads *belo re'ot* in the opposite direction — as specifically including the blind person. The verse in question describes someone who enters a forest to chop wood (*va'asher yavo et re'ehu baya'ar lachtov etzim*), swings the axe, and the iron head flies off and kills someone nearby — a paradigmatic case of unintentional killing. It is in this context that *belo re'ot* appears.

### Mi'ut Principles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״וַאֲשֶׁר יָבֹא אֶת רֵעֵהוּ בַיַּעַר״ – אֲפִילּוּ סוֹמֵא. אֲתָא ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״, מַעֲטֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda?** It is **as it is written** with regard to those who are exiled: **"And as one who goes with his neighbor into the forest"** (Deuteronomy 19:5), which is stated in general terms, applying **even** to **a blind person.** The phrase **"without seeing" comes** and **excludes** a blind person from this *halakha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר: ״בְּלֹא רְאוֹת״ – לְמַעֵט, ״בִּבְלִי דַּעַת״ – לְמַעֵט, הָוֵי מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט, וְאֵין מִיעוּט אַחַר מִיעוּט אֶלָּא לְרַבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Meir** interprets the verses differently: **"Without seeing"** serves **to exclude** a blind person, and **"without knowledge"** (Deuteronomy 19:4) also serves **to exclude** a blind person. **This is** an example of **a restrictive expression following a restrictive expression, and** there is a hermeneutical principle that **a restrictive expression following a restrictive expression** serves **only to amplify** the *halakha* and include additional cases. In this case, it includes a blind person in the *halakhot* of exile as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? ״בִּבְלִי דַּעַת״ – פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda** respond to that derivation? The Gemara answers that Rabbi Yehuda says that the phrase **"without knowledge" comes to exclude one with intent,** e.g., one who had intent to kill an animal and who killed a person, from the *halakha* of exile. Rabbi Yehuda interprets the two restrictive expressions as excluding two unrelated cases from the *halakha* of exile, and therefore the principle of: A restrictive expression following a restrictive expression serves only to amplify, is not relevant in this case.

The dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir turns on a technical principle of *mi'ut* (exclusion). Rabbi Yehuda reads *belo re'ot* as a *mi'ut* — an exclusion — that removes the blind person from eligibility for the *ir miklat*. Rabbi Meir counters that we also have the phrase *bivli da'at* (without knowledge) in the same context, which would be a second exclusion. The hermeneutical rule is that *ein mi'ut achar mi'ut ela lerabbot* — two consecutive exclusions in the same passage do not compound each other but rather expand the scope; the second exclusion comes to include rather than exclude. Therefore, Rabbi Meir concludes that the blind person is included in the *ir miklat*.

Rabbi Yehuda responds that *bivli da'at* is not functioning as a second *mi'ut* parallel to *belo re'ot* — it addresses a distinct category, namely the one who acts without any awareness at all, as opposed to one who acts with directed intent (*perat lemitkaven veda'at*). The two phrases operate on different axes entirely and do not trigger the double-exclusion rule.
