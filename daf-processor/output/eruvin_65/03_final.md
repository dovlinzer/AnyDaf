# Eruvin 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Drunkenness & Kavana

### Non-Jew Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כׇּל הַמֵּפִיק מָגֵן בִּשְׁעַת גַּאֲוָה, סוֹגְרִין וְחוֹתְמִין צָרוֹת בַּעֲדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״גַּאֲוָה אֲפִיקֵי מָגִנִּים סָגוּר חוֹתָם צָר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Whoever passes a shield** over himself **at a time of arrogance,** i.e., whoever suppresses his evil inclination as though it were covered with a shield when he is arrogant, e.g., when he is intoxicated or the like (Rabbeinu Ḥananel), **troubles will be closed and sealed from him, as it is stated: "The channels of [*afikei*] his scales are his pride, closed together as with a tight [*tzar*] seal"** (Job 41:7). The verse is interpreted homiletically: When at a time of arrogance a person passes a shield [*mapik*] over his evil inclination, his troubles [*tzarot*] will be closed and sealed before him.

The daf begins in the middle of a digression that arose from the discussion of how to handle a non-Jew's ownership in a shared courtyard. The *Gemara* had noted that if a non-Jew has Jewish hired hands working on his property, those workers may be treated as owners and can thereby participate in an *eruv*. This observation prompted Rav Nachman to remark approvingly about the quality of the *psak*, which in turn opened a lengthy digression about the conditions under which a ruling may or may not be issued — and specifically about the problem of issuing rulings while drunk.

### Rav Nachman on Psak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״אָפִיק״ לִישָּׁנָא דְעַבּוֹרֵי הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״אַחַי בָּגְדוּ כְּמוֹ נָחַל כַּאֲפִיק נְחָלִים יַעֲבוֹרוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **From where may it be inferred that** the meaning of **this** word ***afik* is a formulation** denoting **passing [*aborei*]?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "My brothers have dealt deceitfully like a wadi, like the channel [*afik*] of brooks that pass by [*ya'avoru*]"** (Job 6:15). This implies that the term *afik* is synonymous with the verb *ya'avoru*, which refers to something that travels and passes by.

The digression was set in motion by Rav Nachman's observation connecting the pleasure of a fine *psak* with the warning against *paskening* while drunk. The *Gemara* had already established that issuing *halachic* rulings in a state of intoxication is inappropriate, and that observation serves as the launching point for the extended discussion that follows throughout this section of the daf.

### Drunk & Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: שִׁיכּוֹר מִקָּחוֹ מִקָּח, וּמִמְכָּרוֹ מִמְכָּר. עָבַר עֲבֵירָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ מִיתָה — מְמִיתִין אוֹתוֹ, מַלְקוֹת — מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: הֲרֵי הוּא כְּפִיקֵּחַ לְכׇל דְּבָרָיו, אֶלָּא שֶׁפָּטוּר מִן הַתְּפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this argument from the following *baraita*: With regard to one who is **intoxicated, his acquisition is a** binding **acquisition;** that is, he cannot retract the transaction when he is sober, **and** similarly, **his sale is a** binding **sale.** Moreover, **if he committed a transgression for which** he is liable to receive the **death** penalty, **he is executed;** and if the offense is punishable by **lashes, he is flogged. The principle is** that **he is like a sober person in all matters, except that he is exempt from prayer.**

As reviewed from the previous session: a drunk person remains fully liable for his actions and any business transaction he enters into is binding — unless he has reached the extreme state of intoxication comparable to Lot, where he has literally no awareness of what he is doing. At the same time, even a lesser degree of drunkenness suffices to exempt a person from *davening*, because prayer requires the capacity to stand before God as one would stand before a king — a standard of composure that intoxication undermines.

### Magein Avraham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא הִגִּיעַ לְשִׁכְרוּתוֹ שֶׁל לוֹט, אֲבָל הִגִּיעַ לְשִׁכְרוּתוֹ שֶׁל לוֹט — פָּטוּר מִכּוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: They taught** that an intoxicated person is responsible for all his actions **only** in a case where **he did not reach the** state of **intoxication of Lot; however, if he reached the** state of **intoxication of Lot,** so that he is altogether unaware of his actions, **he is exempt from all** liability.

The *Gemara* now presents a statement by Rabbi Chanina: *kol hamaphsik magein b'sha'at ga'ava, sogrin v'chosmin tzarot ba'ado* — anyone who *maphsik* (passes over, or alternatively, does not reveal) *magein* at the time of *ga'ava*, his enemies are handed over and sealed away from him.

Rashi explains that *magein* here refers to *Magein Avraham*, the first *berachah* of the *Amidah* — the one for which *kavanah* is most essentially required. The word *ga'ava* is understood in connection with drunkenness, carrying the connotation of self-indulgence or self-absorption. Rashi draws support from a *Gemara* in *Berachot* that interprets the *pasuk* *v'oti hishlachta acharei gevecha* — "you have cast Me behind your back" — with the play *al tikrei gevecha ela ge'echa*, meaning: after a person has attended to his own bodily needs through eating and drinking, turning to *daven* is an act of placing God second. The concept of *ga'ava* is thus set in opposition to the proper disposition for prayer. The reward for passing over *Magein Avraham* under such circumstances is, fittingly, that the protection promised in that *berachah* — the divine shielding of Avraham from his enemies — is instead granted to the one who abstains. The *pasuk* cited is *ga'ava afikei m'ginim, sagur chotam tzar* — the *ga'ava* of shields, with the enemy closed and sealed.

### Afik Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵפִיק״ אִתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is not the correct interpretation; rather, **it was stated** that **whoever does not** cover, but **draws out [*mapik*]** a shield at a time of arrogance, troubles will be closed and sealed from him. In other words, a person must draw his weapons and shield in order to fight his evil inclination when it tries to overpower him (Rabbeinu Ḥananel).

The *Gemara* now subjects the key word *afik* to linguistic scrutiny. On what basis does the term *aphik* mean "to pass over"? The proof is from the *pasuk* *k'aphik nechalim ya'avoru* — "like the rushing channels of wadis they will pass by." The *afikim* here are those dramatic seasonal watercourses familiar from *Shir HaMa'alot*'s image of *k'aphikim ba'Negev*: dry gulches in the Negev that erupt into powerful flash floods when rain falls. The term *aphik* is thus connected lexically to *ya'avoru*, the root meaning to pass over or through.

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different reading. He agrees that the one who is drunk should not say *Magein Avraham*, but he understands *maphsik* in the opposite etymological direction: not "passes over" but "reveals." Thus according to Rabbi Yochanan, the phrase means that one does not *reveal* or *articulate* *Magein Avraham* — one does not say it aloud. His proof text is *vayeira'u afikei mayim* — the *afikim* of water were *revealed* — where the cognate word *afikei* is linked to the verb *vayiglu*, "and they were revealed." The practical upshot, however, is the same in both readings: the person who is drunk should not recite *Magein Avraham*. The *Gemara* itself acknowledges this near-equivalence: *michdi, kra mashma bein lomar bein lomar* — the *pasuk* implies more or less the same thing either way. So what, then, is the practical difference between the two formulations?

### Rav Sheshet Sleep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי, קְרָאֵי מַשְׁמַע בֵּין לְמָר וּבֵין לְמָר, מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת. דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת מָסַר שִׁינְתֵּיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ. מָר אִית לֵיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת, וּמָר לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now, since the verses may be interpreted both in accordance with** the opinion **of** this **Master and in accordance with** the opinion of the other **Master, what is** the practical difference **between them?** The Gemara answers: **The** practical difference **between them is** with regard to the following practice of **Rav Sheshet, as Rav Sheshet gave** the responsibility for monitoring **his sleep to his attendant,** instructing the attendant to wake him when the time for prayer arrived. One **Sage,** Rabbi Ḥanina, **is of** the opinion that the practice of **Rav Sheshet** is correct, as Rabbi Ḥanina maintains that if one is in great need of sleep, it is better to nap for a while and then wake up with renewed vigor. **And one Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **is not of** the opinion that the practice of **Rav Sheshet** is correct. He holds that a person must marshal his strength and pray, rather than succumb to the need for sleep.

The *Gemara* answers: *ika beinaihu d'Rav Sheshet* — the practical difference emerges in the case of Rav Sheshet. When Rav Sheshet was drunk, he would hand over his sleep to his attendant — that is, he would instruct his attendant to wake him after a set amount of time, such as an hour, so that he could sleep off the intoxication and then proceed to *daven*.

Rashi explains the dispute as follows. If the obligation is framed in the positive — one must *pass over* the drunkenness, allowing it to fully run its course — then merely setting an alarm, so to speak, is insufficient. One must wait until one wakes naturally. But if the obligation is framed in the negative — one must simply refrain from *actively revealing* (i.e., reciting) *Magein Avraham* while consciously intoxicated — then one need not proactively wait out every last effect. Waking after a period of sleep arranged in advance is sufficient. The difference between stating the rule in the positive (pass it over) versus the negative (do not reveal it) thus translates precisely into this question about whether Rav Sheshet's practice is valid.

The *MAHARSHA* further notes the connection to an earlier discussion in which the *Gemara* distinguished between drinking a *revi'it* of wine versus more: a small amount of wine might be ameliorated by a little sleep, while a larger amount is worsened by exertion. Now the question is refined further — does waking naturally differ from waking by an attendant's call? These are subtle but internally consistent distinctions.

### Chananel & Yetzer

*Tosafot* cites Rabbeinu Chananel, who offers a strikingly different reading of the entire passage. He explains that *ga'ava* here does not refer to drunkenness at all, but to the *yetzer hara* — the evil inclination. On this reading, the *Gemara* is raising a classic question in the psychology of moral struggle: when battling one's *yetzer hara*, should one attempt to sublimate and suppress it (the "pass over" approach, in which the desire is pushed down without being directly confronted), or should one expose and confront it directly (the "reveal" approach)? This is a question of moral strategy — do you deal with inner drives by suppression or by direct engagement?

*Tosafot* then works to connect this alternative framing back to the question about Rav Sheshet: if the issue at stake is how to handle the state of drunkenness itself, does one sleep it off passively (an act of suppression, allowing the condition to resolve on its own), or does one actively work through it, as the *Gemara* earlier suggested by the strategy of walking? Rabbeinu Chananel's reading opens the *Gemara* to a broader discussion of how human beings should deal with their various drives and distractions — not only the specific case of *Magein Avraham*.

### Unsettled Mind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין דַּעְתּוֹ מְיוּשֶּׁבֶת עָלָיו אַל יִתְפַּלֵּל, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּצָר אַל יוֹרֶה״. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בְּיוֹמָא דְּרָתַח לָא מְצַלֵּי, אָמַר: ״בְּצָר אַל יוֹרֶה״ כְּתִיב. מָר עוּקְבָא בְּיוֹמָא דְשׁוּתָא לָא הֲוָה נָפֵיק לְבֵי דִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: Anyone whose mind is unsettled should not pray, as it is stated: When distressed, one should not issue decisions.** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Ḥanina, on a day that he was angry,** would **not pray,** as **he said that it is written: When distressed, one should not issue decisions.** The Gemara similarly relates that **Mar Ukva, on a day of a south wind, would not venture out to the court,** for this hot and harsh wind would disturb his usual clarity of mind.

The *Gemara* now transitions from drunkenness to a related but broader concern: the requirement of *da'as meyushevet* — a settled mind — as a precondition for *davening*. Rav Chiya bar Ashi said in the name of Rav: *kol she'ein da'ato meyushevet alav al yitpalel* — anyone whose mind is not settled upon him should not pray. The proof text offered is *batzar al yoreh* — in anguish, do not rule. This is a striking application: the *pasuk* speaks of issuing legal rulings, yet the *Gemara* maps it onto prayer. Rashi himself notes that he searched for this *pasuk* throughout all of Scripture and could not find it, suggesting it may originate in *Sefer Ben Sira*, which the *Gemara* sometimes quotes with the formula *dechtiv*, treating it with a quasi-canonical standing. *Tosafot* offers a series of alternative suggestions, including the possibility that it is a paraphrase of another *pasuk* rather than a verbatim quotation.

The connection between *paskening* and *davening* is itself worth examining. The requirement to stand before God in prayer presupposes an awareness of Whom one is addressing, and there are at least two distinct components to this awareness: the appropriateness of one's general state (as with drunkenness, where one's very condition is unseemly before God), and the more cognitive requirement of being able to focus on the words one is saying. The *Gemara* does not formally subdivide these, but the transition from drunkenness to *da'as meyushevet* suggests a movement from the former concern toward the latter.

The *Gemara* also notes, earlier in the amud, the expression *shikhura v'lo miyayin* — drunk but not from wine — as a description of the entire period since *galut* began. This had already introduced the idea that distractedness, not only literal intoxication, is the operative concern. That theme now becomes explicit.

Rav Chanina, we are told, would not *daven* on a day when he became angry — *beyoma dertach lo matzli*. He too cited *batzar al yoreh*: in anguish, do not rule — and again translating the language of *psak* into the context of *davening*. The *Gemara* notes the curiosity of applying a formulation about *paskening* to the act of prayer, particularly given that the source text may not even be canonical Scripture.

Mar Ukva, for his part, would not go to the *beit din* to serve as a *dayan* on days of *shusah* — a hot, dry, windswept day that made concentration difficult. Here, uniquely, the concern is explicitly about *paskening*, not about *davening*. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak crystallizes the principle: *hilchasa ba'ya tzilusa* — halachic ruling requires clarity, like a day of northern wind, a cool, clear, focused day. Rashi adds that the *MAHARSHA* understands *k'yoma d'istana* not necessarily as a literal north wind but as a metaphor for an ideal, cloudless spring day: the kind of mental clarity that one should bring to the seat of judgment.

### Clarity for Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: הִלְכְתָא בָּעֲיָא צִילּוּתָא כְּיוֹמָא דְאִסְתָּנָא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִי אָמְרָה לִי אֵם קָרֵיב כּוּתָּחָא — לָא תְּנַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** The study of ***halakha* requires clarity, as** on **a day** when **a north wind** blows and clears the skies. **Abaye said** similarly that **if my stepmother says to me: Bring** me a dish of ***kutaḥ*, I can no** longer **study** Torah in my usual fashion, as even a simple task such as this troubles me and distracts me from my Torah study.

This observation about *hilchasa ba'ya tzilusa* points to a genuine asymmetry between the standards for *davening* and for *paskening*. One might initially suppose that judgment demands more rigorous clarity than prayer — and in terms of consequences, that may well be true. But prayer, unlike a postponable court case, carries a time-bound obligation: if one does not *daven*, one may forfeit the *mitzvah* entirely. The *Gemara* nonetheless indicates, consistently across this entire passage, that the preferred course of action when one lacks the required state of mind is to refrain from *davening* rather than to *daven* improperly.

A further asymmetry worth noting is that *paskening* can be delegated or postponed — another *dayan* can hear the case, or it can wait for a better day. The forfeiture of prayer, by contrast, is final. Yet the *Gemara* maintains that an improper *tefillah* is worse than no *tefillah*. At the same time, there is a *machlokes* among the *Rishonim* — the *Rambam* maintains a biblical obligation to pray daily, while the *Ramban* holds that the only biblically mandated prayer is *be'eis tzarah*, in a time of affliction. Interestingly, the *Gemara* here says the opposite: it is specifically in a state of anguish or agitation that one should *not* pray. The resolution is that the *Ramban* refers to external affliction that generates a desire to turn toward God in supplication, whereas the *Gemara*'s exemption concerns internal distraction or agitation that prevents focused *davening*. The former is the condition par excellence for meaningful prayer; the latter is its enemy.

### Distractibility Tales

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִי קְרַצְתַּן כִּינָּה לָא תְּנַאי. מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא עֲבַדָה לֵיהּ אִמֵּיהּ שִׁבְעָה מָנֵי לְשִׁבְעָה יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rava said: If I am bitten by a louse, I can no** longer **learn** in my usual manner. The Gemara relates that **the mother of Mar, son of Ravina, would prepare seven garments for him for the seven days** of the week, so that he would not be bitten by the lice found in old clothes (Rabbeinu Ḥananel).

The *Gemara* now moves from institutional standards to personal anecdotes, offering a series of vivid illustrations of the distractibility of even the greatest *Amoraim*. Abaye said: if his mother — the nursemaid who raised him, frequently quoted in the *Gemara* on matters of healing and medicine — would say to him in the middle of learning, *karei kucha* (bring me the dip), *lo tanoei*: he could no longer learn. Whether the interruption threw him entirely off his stride or left him too preoccupied until the request was fulfilled, the point is the same: even a minor external demand shattered his concentration.

Rava said — and some say it was Ravina — *itratztin kina, lo tanoei*: if a louse bit him, he could not learn. It is difficult not to find this humanizing. These are figures of towering intellectual achievement, and yet a mosquito bite — or its ancient equivalent — was enough to derail them entirely. The *Gemara* is making a serious point about the conditions required for genuine learning: distraction is not a minor inconvenience to be pushed through but a substantive impediment to the kind of clarity that the tradition demands.

The *Gemara* then adds that the mother of Mar the son of Ravina — and if the previous statement was attributed to Ravina, perhaps it is no coincidence — made for him seven garments, one for each day of the week. Fresh garments, being less likely to harbor lice, would remove that particular source of distraction from her son's learning.

### Night Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לָא אִיבְּרִי לֵילְיָא אֶלָּא לְשִׁינְתָּא. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: לָא אִיבְּרִי סֵיהֲרָא אֶלָּא לְגִירְסָא. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: מְחַדְּדָן שְׁמַעְתָּךְ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: דִּימָמֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: Night was created only for sleep. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: The moon was created only for** Torah **study** by its light. When people **said to Rabbi Zeira: Your teachings are** exceedingly **sharp, he said to them: They were** formulated **during the daytime** hours. This teaches that Torah study during the day is most beneficial to clarity of the mind.

The discussion of optimal conditions for learning naturally raises the question of timing. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *lo ivri leilya ela leshinta* — the night was created for nothing other than sleep. The implication is that serious learning belongs to the daylight hours, and attempting to learn at night is working against the natural order. Reish Lakish takes the opposite view: *lo ivri saira ela legirsa* — the moon was created for nothing other than study. Night, illuminated by the moon, is itself an invitation to learn.

*Tosafot* is troubled by the absolute formulation of Rav Yehuda's position and qualifies it: the statement refers specifically to the long summer nights, when sixteen or more daylight hours are available for learning and sleep can reasonably fill the nighttime. But in winter, when nights are long and days short, nighttime learning is not only permitted but expected. The *MAHARSHA* suggests a harmonization: the two opinions are not really in dispute, since moonlit nights are well-suited for learning while pitch-dark nights favor sleep. He also notes, with some wit, that Reish Lakish — who had spent part of his earlier life as a bandit — may have been more naturally at home in the night hours.

### Learning Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ בַּרְתֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: לָא בָּעֵי מָר מֵינַם פּוּרְתָּא? אֲמַר לַהּ: הַשְׁתָּא אָתוּ יוֹמֵי דַּאֲרִיכֵי וְקַטִּינֵי, וְנֵינוּם טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda's daughter said to** her father, **Rav Ḥisda,** who would spend his nights in study: **Doesn't the Master wish to sleep a little?** He **said to her: Days that are long** in quantity **but short** in the opportunity to study Torah and perform mitzvot **will soon arrive, and we will sleep a lot.** After I die, there will be more than enough time for sleep.

Rabbi Zeira was asked why his teachings were so sharp and penetrating. He replied: *deymamei ninhu* — those are things I learned during the day. He did not claim to learn exclusively by day, but he attributed his depth of insight specifically to his daytime study.

The daughter of Rav Chisda once asked her father whether he did not need to sleep at all, given that he learned straight through the night. He replied: *hashta atu yomei de'arichi veketini veninum* — soon enough, the days that are long and short will come, and there will be plenty of time to sleep. Rashi explains that these are the days of death, which are long for sleeping and which offer no opportunity whatsoever for *mitzvot*. The exchange is reminiscent of the famous story of the Vilna Gaon, who, upon a rare visit from his sister after twenty years of separation, greeted her briefly and returned to his learning, saying they would have time to talk in *Olam HaBa*. This *Gemara* is arguably the source of that ethos. It is worth noting, however, that here Rav Chisda is simply declining to sleep — he is not neglecting a person to whom he has concrete obligations. The *Gemara* elsewhere does engage with the tension between total immersion in learning and one's responsibilities to family and community, a tension that different authorities resolve quite differently.

### Day Workers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אֲנַן פּוֹעֲלֵי דִּימָמֵי אֲנַן. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב יָזֵיף וּפָרַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: We,** Torah scholars, **are day workers,** as our study is performed primarily during the day. The Gemara relates that **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov** would **borrow and repay,** i.e., if for some reason he neglected to study during the day, he would use the night hours to compensate for the missed time.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak declares: *ana poalei d'yimami anan* — we are day workers. The phrase evokes the teaching of *Pirkei Avot*: *hayom katzar v'hamelacha merubah v'hapoalim atzelim u'va'al habayit dochek* — the day is short, the work is great, the workers are lazy, and the Master of the house is pressing. Here the emphasis falls on the day: we work when there is daylight, and the night is our time off — for sleep.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov took a more pragmatic approach: *yazif u'para* — he would borrow and pay back. The *MAHARSHA* explains that his primary learning was conducted during the day, but if something came up and disrupted his daytime schedule, he would make up the lost time at night. Nighttime study was not his preferred mode but served as a reserve against unavoidable interruptions to his daytime regimen.

## Prayer Conditions

### Travel Recovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הַבָּא מִן הַדֶּרֶךְ, אַל יִתְפַּלֵּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאֶקְבְּצֵם אֶל הַנָּהָר הַבָּא אֶל אַחֲוָא וַנַּחֲנֶה שָׁם יָמִים שְׁלֹשָׁה וָאָבִינָה בָּעָם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: One who returns** home **from a journey should not pray for three days** while recovering from the hardship of being on the road, **as it is stated: "And I gathered them together at the river that runs to Aḥava, and we encamped there for three days, and I inspected the people"** (Ezra 8:15), after which it is stated: "Then I proclaimed a fast there, at the river of Aḥava, that we might afflict ourselves before our God, to seek of Him a safe journey for us" (Ezra 8:21), which teaches that they rested three days before praying.

The *Gemara* turns from internal states to external circumstances as conditions affecting prayer. Rabbi Elazar rules: *haba min haderech al yitpalel shelosha yamim* — one who returns from a journey should not pray for three days. For the *Amoraim*, a journey was an arduous undertaking, far removed from the minimal inconvenience of modern travel. The proof text is from Ezra: *va'ekbetzem el hanahar haba el Ahua vanachana sham yamim shelosha va'avina ba'am* — I gathered them by the river and we rested there for three days, and then I understood. The *MAHARSHA* notes that the very next *pasuk* references prayer, implying that only after three days of recovery does one attain the clarity needed to pray properly.

Practical application: the father of Shmuel, *avuha d'Shmuel*, would not pray for three days after returning from a journey — *lo matzli tlata yomei* — following this ruling precisely.

### Environmental Avoidance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל לָא מְצַלֵּי בְּבֵיתָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ שִׁיכְרָא. רַב פָּפָּא לָא מְצַלֵּי בְּבֵיתָא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ הַרְסָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel** himself **would not pray in a house that contained an alcoholic beverage,** as the scent of the alcohol would disturb his concentration during prayer. Similarly, **Rav Pappa would not pray in a house that contained small** fried **fish,** due to their smell.

Shmuel himself would not pray in a house in which drinking had been taking place — *lo matzli b'veita d'is bei shichra*. Whether the smell of alcohol, the ambient atmosphere, or some other associative quality made such a setting incompatible with prayer, this was Shmuel's personal standard, though the *Gemara* does not present it as an objective *halachah* applicable to all.

Rav Pappa would not pray in a house containing *harsana* — a particularly pungent fried-fish dish — *lo matzli b'veita d'is bei harsana*. The overpowering odor made concentration impossible for him. This example is perhaps easier to relate to than the drinking case, as a truly noxious smell is an immediate and visceral impediment to *kavanah*. One might note the anecdote of a student who asked a *rabbi* whether it was permissible to pray near the smell of cattle on a *kibbutz*, only to receive the reply: "What smell?" — a reminder that what constitutes a distraction is, at least in part, a function of habituation.

## Wine Aggada

### Wine & Appeasement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כׇּל הַמִּתְפַּתֶּה בְּיֵינוֹ — יֵשׁ בּוֹ מִדַּעַת קוֹנוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּרַח ה׳ אֶת רֵיחַ הַנִּיחוֹחַ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: Whoever is appeased by his wine,** i.e., whoever becomes more relaxed after drinking, **has in him** an element of **the mind-set of his Creator,** who acted in a similar fashion, **as it is stated: "And the Lord smelled the sweet savor,** and the Lord said in His heart, I will not again curse the ground any more for man's sake" (Genesis 8:21). As it were, God acted more favorably toward His creatures after He was appeased with the smell of the burnt offerings. Smell can be as potent as drinking or eating itself.

Rabbi Chanina opens the *aggadic* passage on wine with the following teaching: *kol hamitpateh b'yaino yesh bo midas kono* — anyone who can be appeased through wine possesses a quality of his Creator. The proof text is *vayaryach Hashem es reiach hanichoach* — God smelled the pleasing aroma of the sacrifice and declared that He would no longer curse the earth. It is, as the *Gemara* presents it, a strikingly anthropomorphic *pasuk*, one of the more unusual *pesukim* in this regard. The idea seems to be that when a person waits for an opportune moment — when his counterpart is relaxed and in good spirits — and at that point seeks reconciliation, he is engaged in a kind of *imitatio Dei*.

The *MAHARSHA* adds a nuance: the key phrase is *b'yaino*, in his own wine. If someone takes you out and wines and dines you in order to soften you up, that is a different matter. But if a person is drinking his own wine, and you approach him at that moment seeking to make amends, and he is generous enough to be appeased — that parallels the divine acceptance of a sacrifice. After all, when one brings a sacrifice, one is offering back to God what is already God's. Yet God accepts it graciously. So too here: the one drinking his own wine who allows himself to be appeased is demonstrating a generous spirit that mirrors the divine quality.

## Prayer Conditions…

### Modern Thresholds

The *Gemara*'s extended discussion of conditions that excuse one from *davening* — travel, environmental distraction, anger, distractedness — raises the question of how these exemptions apply in later generations. Many *Rishonim* take the position that these exemptions are effectively moot in practice, because we no longer have the *kavanah* even under ideal conditions that would justify such a high standard. Our threshold for what counts as acceptable *kavanah* has been substantially lowered over the generations — *yeridas hadoros* — and therefore we do not apply these exemptions. The *halacha* as *paskened* in the *Shulchan Aruch* reflects this position.

This approach does raise a genuine question: if someone truly is in a state of extreme distraction — grieving, overwhelmed, unable to focus at all — should we in fact advise them not to *daven*? The *Gemara* is unambiguous that an improper *tefillah* is worse than no *tefillah*. Yet we do not, as a practical matter, apply these exemptions today, on the theory that our baseline *kavanah* is already sufficiently minimal that the gap between our normal state and an agitated state is not significant enough to trigger the exemption.

The case of a *chatan* on his wedding day is instructive here as well: the *Gemara* debates whether his exemption from *Shema* is a function of *tirda* (distraction) or *osek b'mitzvah* (engagement in a *mitzvah*). These are distinct conceptual categories, and the *Rishonim* who limit the distraction-based exemptions still acknowledge the *osek b'mitzvah* category as operative.

## Wine Aggada…

### Wine & Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: כׇּל הַמִּתְיַישֵּׁב בְּיֵינוֹ — יֵשׁ בּוֹ דַּעַת שִׁבְעִים זְקֵנִים. ״יַיִן״ נִיתַּן בְּשִׁבְעִים אוֹתִיּוֹת, וְ״סוֹד״ נִיתַּן בְּשִׁבְעִים אוֹתִיּוֹת. נִכְנַס יַיִן — יָצָא סוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya said: Anyone who remains settled** of mind **after** drinking **wine,** and does not become intoxicated, **has** an element of **the mind-set of seventy Elders.** The allusion is: **Wine [*yayin*** spelled *yod*, *yod*, *nun*] **was given in seventy letters,** as the numerological value of the letters comprising the word is seventy, as *yod* equals ten and *nun* equals fifty. Similarly, the word **secret [*sod*** spelled *samekh*, *vav*, *dalet*] **was given in seventy letters,** as *samekh* equals sixty, *vav* equals six, and *dalet* equals four. Typically, when **wine entered** the body, **a secret emerged.** Whoever does not reveal secrets when he drinks is clearly blessed with a firm mind, like that of seventy Elders.

Rabbi Chiya teaches: *kol hamisyashev b'yaino yesh bo da'as ayin zekeinim* — anyone who is settled and composed when he drinks wine possesses the wisdom of the seventy elders. The reasoning is *gematriah*: *yayin*, wine, has a numerical value of seventy (*yud-yud-nun* = 10+10+50), and *sod*, secret, likewise has a value of seventy (*samech-vav-dalet* = 60+6+4). Hence the saying: *nichnas yayin yatza sod* — wine enters, secrets emerge. A person who can drink and yet remain composed — who does not let secrets spill out — has demonstrated the self-possession associated with the wisdom of the Sanhedrin.

Rashi reads this as: one who holds his secrets in is as weighty as the *Sanhedrin*. There is also a complementary reading: *sod* here carries the sense of wisdom and *Torah* depth, and one who drinks and yet retains the *Torah*'s wisdom within him — who does not lose his inner world to the wine — is himself a repository of that seventy-fold wisdom.

### Wine Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָנִין: לֹא נִבְרָא יַיִן אֶלָּא לְנַחֵם אֲבֵלִים וּלְשַׁלֵּם שָׂכָר לָרְשָׁעִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּנוּ שֵׁכָר לְאוֹבֵד וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanin said: Wine was created only in order to comfort mourners** in their distress, **and to reward the wicked** in this world so they will have no reward left in the World-to-Come, **as it is stated: "Give strong drink to him that is ready to perish,** and wine to the bitter of soul. Let him drink, and forget his poverty, and remember his misery no more" (Proverbs 31:6). "Him that is ready to perish" refers to the wicked, who will perish from the world, while "the bitter of soul" denotes mourners.

Rabbi Chanin teaches: *lo nivra yayin ela lenachem avelim u'leshalem sachar la'resha'im* — wine was created only to console mourners and to pay back the wicked. In a world in which wine has so many potential negative effects, one might ask what justifies its existence. The answer: it has a legitimate therapeutic role in comforting those in mourning — and for the wicked, it functions as their reward in this world (since they will receive none in the next). The *MAHARSHA* notes a play on words between *shechar* (strong drink) and *sachar* (reward): *tenu shechar la'aved* — give *shechar* to the one who is lost — is the reward (*sachar*) of the *rasha* in this world. The verse concludes *v'yayin l'marei nafesh* — and wine to those who are embittered of soul — affirming its consolatory function.

### Wine Abundance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חָנִין בַּר פָּפָּא: כׇּל שֶׁאֵין יַיִן נִשְׁפָּךְ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ כַּמַּיִם — אֵינוֹ בִּכְלַל בְּרָכָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבֵרַךְ אֶת לַחְמְךָ וְאֶת מֵימֶיךָ״. מָה לֶחֶם שֶׁנִּיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר — אַף מַיִם שֶׁנִּיקָּח בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — יַיִן, וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ מַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanin bar Pappa said: Anyone in whose house wine does not flow like water is not** yet **included** in the Torah's **blessing, as it is stated: "And He shall bless your bread and your water"** (Exodus 23:25). The water mentioned in this verse actually refers to wine, as learned in the following manner: **Just as bread** is something **that may be purchased with** second-**tithe money,** i.e., one is permitted to buy bread with money used to redeem second-tithe, **so** too the word **water** in the verse is referring to a liquid **that may be purchased with** second-**tithe money. And what is that?** It is **wine,** as one may buy wine with second-tithe money, but one may not buy water; **and** nevertheless, the verse **calls it "water."**

Rabbi Chanin bar Pappa offers yet another perspective: *kol she'ein yayin nishpach betoch beito kemayim eino bichlal bracha* — anyone whose wine is not as plentiful in his house as water is not in the category of blessing. The proof text is *u'verach et lachmecha v'et meimecha* — He will bless your bread and your water. The *Gemara* asks: in what sense could wine be described as water? The answer: the same way that bread in this *pasuk* refers to items purchased with *Ma'aser Sheni* money, so too *meimecha* (your water) must refer to a liquid that can be purchased with *Ma'aser Sheni* money — which rules out literal water and points to wine. The verse calls wine "water" to convey that when wine is as freely available in one's home as water, *that* is the sign of true blessing.

### Character Revelation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעַאי, בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים אָדָם נִיכָּר: בְּכוֹסוֹ, וּבְכִיסוֹ וּבְכַעְסוֹ. וְאָמְרִי לֵיהּ אַף בְּשַׂחֲקוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elai said: In three matters a person's** true character is **ascertained; in his cup,** i.e., his behavior when he drinks; **in his pocket,** i.e., his conduct in his financial dealings with other people; **and in his anger. And some say:** A person **also** reveals his real nature **in his laughter.**

The famous teaching of Rabbi Ilai closes this *aggadic* passage: *bishlosha devarim adam nikar* — a person's character is revealed through three things: *b'choso u've'chiso u've'chaso* — in his cup, in his purse, and in his anger. When drunk, a person's self-control is reduced and more of his true self emerges — *nichnas yayin yatza sod*. In business dealings, money is at stake, and how a person behaves when there is genuine financial pressure says much about who he really is. And in anger, as with drunkenness, the usual restraints weaken and character is exposed.

*Ve'amrei lah af bischoko* — some add: and in his joking around. Much truth is said in jest, and the content and manner of a person's humor can reveal aspects of character that more guarded social interaction conceals.

## Two Courtyards Eruv

### Configuration Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי בַּפְּנִימִית, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בַּחִיצוֹנָה. בָּא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי רַבִּי, וְאָסַר, וְלִפְנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא, וְאָסַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the topic of *eiruvin*: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** It once happened that there were two courtyards, one within the other, with **a Jew and a gentile** living in the **inner** courtyard, **while** a single **Jew** lived **in the outer one.** The **case came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi for a decision as to whether carrying in the outer courtyard could be permitted without renting from the gentile, **and he prohibited** it. The case then came before **Rabbi Ḥiyya, and he** too **prohibited** it.

The *Gemara* returns to *halacha*, specifically the question of two connected courtyards — an inner and an outer — in which both Jews and a non-Jew reside. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *Yisrael ve'nochri bifnimit, Yisrael bechitzona* — a Jew and a non-Jew in the inner courtyard, and only a Jew in the outer courtyard. This case was brought before Rebbe, who ruled it forbidden (*asar*), and before Rabbi Chiya, who likewise ruled it forbidden.

The basic structure of the problem: the inner courtyard (call it A) is accessible only through the outer courtyard (B). Anyone wishing to exit from A to the street must pass through B. The question is: does A's presence affect B? And specifically, given that a non-Jew lives in A, does this create a situation in which B faces a *eruv* problem?

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה כָּךְ הָיָה, וְהָא בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב: פְּנִימִי בִּמְקוֹמוֹ מַהוּ? וְאָמַר לָהֶן: מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** even **if you say** that indeed this is the *halakha*, that the Jew in the outer courtyard is of no consequence, and that he is only mentioned because **the incident that took place, took place in this way,** and those who came to ask the question provided all the details without knowing whether they were relevant, this is still difficult. **Wasn't a dilemma raised before Rav** himself with regard to this very issue: **What is** the *halakha* governing a Jew living in **the inner** courtyard **with regard to his** own **place?** Can he carry in the inner courtyard? **And he said to them: It is permitted** for him to carry there. Therefore, according to Rav, a gentile does not render it prohibited for a single Jew to carry, which is actually contrary to Rabbi Meir's opinion.

When Rav was asked about the inner courtyard standing alone — *penimi bi'mkomo* — he ruled: *mutar* — it is permitted. The problem lies in the outer courtyard, B. Why? Because in B, the configuration effectively brings together the Jew from A, the Jew of B, and the non-Jew of A — all three interacting in B's space. It is this aggregation in B, not A's internal configuration, that creates the *eruv* problem. A by itself contains only one Jew and one non-Jew, and where there is only one Jewish resident, the non-Jew does not generate an *eruv* issue.

### Rabbi Eliezer & Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי, כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב? הָאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ שְׁנֵי יִשְׂרְאֵלִים אוֹסְרִין זֶה עַל זֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Rather, what** else can you say? Can you say that he ruled **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov? Didn't** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov **say:** The gentile does not render it prohibited to carry **unless there are two Jews** living in the same courtyard who themselves render it **prohibited for one another** to carry without an *eiruv*? In this case they do not render it prohibited for each other to carry without an *eiruv*, as they do not live in the same courtyard.

After Rav's ruling is established, a discussion breaks out at the end of Rav Sheshet's *shiur*, where Rabbah and Rav Yosef had remained to continue the analysis. Rav Sheshet suggested that Rav's ruling follows the position of Rabbi Meir, who holds that even a single Jew creates an *eruv* problem when sharing space with a non-Jew. Rav Yosef objected sharply: *trei gavrei ravrevei ke'rabbanan lito behai milta?* — would two great scholars make such an elementary error? If this were Rabbi Meir, who holds one Jew is already problematic, then why mention the Jew in the outer courtyard at all? The outer courtyard detail would be entirely superfluous. Moreover, the *Gemara* records explicitly that when asked about the inner courtyard by itself, Rav said it is *mutar* — permitted. That ruling is incompatible with Rabbi Meir's position, since Rabbi Meir would find a problem even in A alone (one Jew with a non-Jew).

The better attribution, therefore, is to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who holds that *two* Jews with a non-Jew create a problem, but *one* Jew with a non-Jew does not. His formulation: *ad she'yesh sheni Yisrael osrin zeh al zeh* — the prohibition applies only where two Jews are making one another forbidden (i.e., where they are effectively in the same shared space). This is consistent with Rav's ruling that A alone is fine.

### Akiva's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: רֶגֶל הַמּוּתֶּרֶת בִּמְקוֹמָהּ — אוֹסֶרֶת שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** you might say that he ruled **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who said:** The **foot** of one **who is permitted in his** own **place** nonetheless **renders it prohibited not in its** own **place.** The Jew in the inner courtyard is permitted to carry in his own courtyard. However, in order to leave his courtyard, he passes through the outer one, in which it is prohibited for him to carry. Therefore, he renders it prohibited for the resident of the outer courtyard as well.

If the operative position is Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov — two Jews with a non-Jew is a problem, one Jew with a non-Jew is not — then why is B a problem? B has its own Jew, and A has a Jew and a non-Jew. The Jew of A is not literally living in B. The answer lies in the position of Rabbi Akiva: *regel ha'muteret bi'mkomah oseret she'lo bi'mkomah* — a *regel* (foot, or presence) that is permitted in its own domain nonetheless creates a prohibition in a domain where it is not located.

This principle operates as follows: according to Rabbi Akiva, because A must pass through B to reach the street, A's residents are in a meaningful sense also present in B. If this were an all-Jewish scenario, B would therefore be required to make an *eruv* together with A — it cannot make an independent *eruv* that ignores A. The *Rabbanan* disagree: since A is *mutar bi'mkomo* (permitted in its own space), it does not impose any obligation on B, and B can make its own *eruv* independently.

The *chiddush* in the case before us is not simply that Rabbi Akiva requires B to include A in its *eruv* — that would apply even in an all-Jewish scenario. The *chiddush* is that once B is required (per Rabbi Akiva) to make an *eruv* together with A, the combined configuration now consists of two Jews (B's Jew and A's Jew) together with A's non-Jew. That constitutes the problematic two-Jews-plus-non-Jew configuration of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, which is forbidden. The ruling against B thus flows from the combination of Rabbi Akiva's rule (B must include A) and Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's rule (two Jews with a non-Jew is a problem).

### Reversed Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מֵרַב: יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹי בַּחִיצוֹנָה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל בַּפְּנִימִית מַהוּ? הָתָם טַעְמָא מִשּׁוּם דִּשְׁכִיחַ דְּדָיַיר, דְּמִירְתַת גּוֹי, וְסָבַר: הַשְׁתָּא אָתֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָמַר לִי: יִשְׂרָאֵל דַּהֲוָה גַּבָּךְ הֵיכָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Eliezer raised a dilemma before Rav** as follows: If **a Jew and a gentile** live together **in the outer** courtyard, **and a Jew** lives alone **in the inner one, what is** the *halakha*? May they carry in the outer courtyard without renting from the gentile? One could argue as follows: **There,** in the case where the Jew and the gentile share the inner courtyard, **the reason** the Sages prohibited carrying is **because it is common** for a Jew and a gentile **to live** together in such a fashion. Ordinarily a single Jew would not live together in the same courtyard as a gentile, for fear that the gentile might kill him. However, here, the Jew living in the inner courtyard believes **that** the **gentile would be afraid** to kill him, as the gentile **thinks** to himself: **Now,** were I to kill my neighbor, the **Jew** living in the outer courtyard **might come and say to me:** The **Jew who used to** live **by you, where is** he? The gentile would not be able to offer as an excuse that the Jew left, for the other Jew from the outer courtyard would know whether or not he passed through his courtyard. Therefore, since that living arrangement is common, the decree applies, and the gentile's residence in the courtyard renders it prohibited to carry there.

The *Gemara* now considers the reverse scenario: *Yisrael ve'nochri be'chitzona, Yisrael be'pnimis* — a Jew and a non-Jew in the outer courtyard, and only a Jew in the inner one. By the same logic, since B (now the outer courtyard containing the Jew and the non-Jew) is the passage through which A's resident must travel, the analysis should be symmetrical: A must make an *eruv* together with B, creating the two-Jews-plus-non-Jew configuration.

The *Gemara* asks whether the two cases are truly equivalent or whether some distinction might apply. The argument for a distinction: perhaps the first case (Jew and non-Jew on the inside) is more common, because the Jew in the outer courtyard would be more likely to intimidate the non-Jew. The non-Jew, aware that the Jew from the outer courtyard could at any moment pass through and demand to know the whereabouts of the Jew from the inner courtyard — *Yisrael dehavi gabach heicha?* — would be deterred from any act of violence. In the reversed configuration, the non-Jew might calculate that he could simply claim his neighbor had gone out to the market, since the outer courtyard opens directly to the street and no one would necessarily know otherwise. On this reasoning, the concern about the non-Jew's presence (which underlies the *eruv* regulation) is less acute when the Jew is in the inner courtyard.

The *Gemara*, however, rejects this distinction:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִילְמָא, הָכָא נָמֵי מִירְתַת, דְּסָבַר: הַשְׁתָּא אָתֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְחָזֵי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** one would say that **here, too,** the gentile **would be afraid** to kill his Jewish neighbor, **as he thinks** to himself: **Now,** were I to kill my neighbor, **the Jew** living in the inner courtyard **might come** at any moment **and see me** in the act of killing his friend. Since the gentile does not know when the resident of the inner courtyard will pass through the outer courtyard, there is a chance his crime might be witnessed. In that case, it would not be uncommon for a Jew and a gentile to live together in such a fashion, and the Sages' decree that the gentile's residence renders carrying prohibited would apply.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״תֵּן לְחָכָם וְיֶחְכַּם עוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to** Rabbi Eliezer the following verse: **"Give to a wise man, and he will be yet wiser"** (Proverbs 9:9), i.e., it is proper to be stringent even in such a case. Consequently, carrying is prohibited in the outer courtyard unless the Jews rent from the gentile.

## Landlord Rental

### Inn Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ וְתַלְמִידֵי דְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא אִיקְּלַעוּ לְהָהוּא פּוּנְדָּק, וְלָא הֲוָה שׂוֹכֵר, וַהֲוָה מַשְׂכִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Reish Lakish and the students of Rabbi Ḥanina happened** to come on Shabbat **to a certain inn** that had at least three permanent residents, two Jews and a gentile who rented their quarters from the gentile innkeeper. Although **the** gentile **tenant was not** present on that Shabbat, the gentile **landlord was** present. Concerned that the gentile tenant might return during Shabbat and render it prohibited for them to carry, Rabbi Ḥanina's students wondered whether the gentile landlord can rent out the gentile's room again for the purpose of an *eiruv*.

The *Gemara* presents a practical case that has direct implications for the construction of *eruvin* in later times. Reish Lakish and the students of Rabbi Chanina arrived at a certain inn. The inn had multiple rooms, one of which was rented by a non-Jew. The group needed to make an *eruv* for the inn, which would normally have required renting the rights from the non-Jew tenant. However, that tenant was absent.

### Landlord Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ: מַהוּ לְמֵיגַר מִינֵּיהּ? כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּלָא מָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק לֵיהּ — לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ דְּלָא אָגְרִינָא. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי — הֵיכָא דְּמָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **They said: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to renting from him?** The Gemara clarifies: **Anywhere that** the landlord **cannot remove** the tenant, **you** need **not raise the dilemma, for they** clearly **cannot rent** it from him. If the landlord is unable to expel the tenant, the residence temporarily belongs completely to the tenant, and only he can rent it out. **Where you** need to **raise the dilemma** is with regard to a situation **where he can remove him.**

The question posed: *mahvei lei minei* — can we rent from the landlord, even though the one who actually holds the current lease on the room, and therefore has rights to it, is not present?

### Eviction Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי? כֵּיוָן דְּמָצֵי מְסַלֵּיק — אָגְרִינָא. אוֹ דִילְמָא, הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא הָא לָא סַלְּקֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the *halakha*? Does one say that **since** the landlord **can remove** the tenant, **they can rent** the residence from him, as the landlord retains a measure of control over it, and therefore he can rent it out again for the purpose of an *eiruv*? **Or perhaps now, in any case he has not** actually **removed him,** which means the residence is still entirely under the tenant's jurisdiction?

The *Gemara* structures the question carefully. If the landlord has no power to move or reassign the tenant — if the tenant could not be displaced even by the landlord — then clearly the landlord has no meaningful authority over the room and cannot transfer any rights. In that case, there is no question: one cannot rent from the landlord, *d'lo agrinan*. The operative question arises only where the landlord *does* have the power to remove or reassign the tenant. Even in that case, one might argue: right now the tenant has not been removed, and he currently holds rights to the room. Can a merely potential power to remove him translate into a presently actionable right to rent from the landlord?

### Practical Decision

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: נִשְׂכּוֹר, וְלִכְשֶׁנַּגִּיעַ אֵצֶל רַבּוֹתֵינוּ שֶׁבַּדָּרוֹם, נִשְׁאַל לָהֶן. אֲתוֹ שַׁיַּילוּ לְרַבִּי אַפָּס, אָמַר לָהֶן: יָפֶה עֲשִׂיתֶם שֶׁשְּׂכַרְתֶּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish said to them: Let us rent** it now, as the principle is that one may act leniently in a case of doubt involving a rabbinic prohibition, **and when we arrive at our Sages in the South we shall ask them** whether we acted properly. Later **they came and asked Rabbi Afes,** who **said to them: You acted well when you rented** it from the landlord.

As Shabbat approached with the question unresolved, Reish Lakish decided to act: *niskor* — let us rent from the landlord. He added: *u'keshenagia etzel raboteinu shebadarom nishal lahem* — and when we reach our masters in the south, we will ask them. Rashi explains that this approach was reasonable because the matter is *de-rabbanan* in character, and the general principle *halacha kedivrei hameikel b'Bavel be'eiruv* — that in cases of *eruv*, we rule leniently — justified acting on the assumption that the rental would prove valid, with the intention of seeking confirmation afterward.

### Rav Afes Approval

When Reish Lakish's group came before Rav Afes, one of the masters of the south, they reported what they had done. *Rav Afes amar lahem: yafe asitem shesechartem* — Rav Afes said to them: you did well to rent from the landlord. The ruling is thus confirmed: where a landlord retains the power to remove or reassign a tenant, that power is sufficient to enable the landlord to serve as the party from whom one rents *eruv* rights — even if the tenant is currently in place and present in his lease.

The implications of this ruling extend well beyond the immediate case. The foundational principle that one can rent *eruv* rights from a landlord who has the power to displace a tenant — rather than necessarily from the tenant himself — underlies the structure of many communal *eruvin* in later periods, including urban *eruvin* that involve renting rights from municipal authorities rather than from every individual property owner. The case of the inn is thus not a curiosity but a precedent of considerable practical consequence, to be developed further in the *sugyot* that follow.
