# Eruvin 73 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sleep vs. Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְקוֹם פִּיתָּא, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מְקוֹם לִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The place** where he eats his **bread, and Shmuel said:** His **place** of **sleep.**

### Brothers' Eating vs. Sleep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מְקוֹם לִינָה גּוֹרֵם. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בִּמְקַבְּלֵי פְרָס שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments on the statement in the mishna that a separate contribution to the *eruv* must be made by each of the brothers if they sleep in their own houses: **Learn from it** that one's **place of sleep determines** the location of his residence. The Gemara rejects this conclusion. **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: They taught** this mishna **with regard to** brothers who **receive a portion** from their father. The mishna is not referring to brothers who actually eat at their father's table, but rather to brothers whose father supplies them with food that they eat in their own homes.

The discussion resumes at the bottom of 72b, picking up a theme introduced in the *mishna*: brothers who eat at their father's table but sleep in separate homes are considered distinct units for *eruv* purposes, each required to contribute separately. The *mishna* had also drawn a distinction based on the direction of the *eruv*'s movement — when brothers are the recipients rather than the contributors, they may be treated as a single household. The *gemara* now probes the underlying principle: does the definition of primary domicile follow where one sleeps or where one eats?

This question has broad practical implications beyond *eruv chatzeros* alone. It recurs in laws of *ner Chanukah*, and it arises regularly in contemporary settings — dormitory students who sleep in individual rooms but eat in a common dining hall, for instance, face precisely this ambiguity. The *mishna*'s plain reading seems to privilege sleep: the brothers eat together yet are still classified as separate units, implying that where one sleeps is the operative factor. However, the *gemara* challenges this reading by suggesting that the *mishna* may not describe brothers literally eating together but rather brothers who receive their food from the father and eat separately. The debate thus remains open.

### Rav Yehuda's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ בֵּית שַׁעַר, אַכְסַדְרָה, וּמִרְפֶּסֶת בַּחֲצַר חֲבֵירוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה אֵין אוֹסֵר עָלָיו. (אֶת) בֵּית הַתֶּבֶן (וְאֶת) בֵּית הַבָּקָר בֵּית הָעֵצִים וּבֵית הָאוֹצָרוֹת — הֲרֵי זֶה אוֹסֵר עָלָיו. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר אֶלָּא מְקוֹם דִּירָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who has a gatehouse, porch,** or **balcony in his friend's courtyard does not render** the owner of the courtyard **prohibited** from carrying there without an *eiruv*, as these locations are not considered residences. However, if he has a **storeroom of straw, a cattle shed, a woodshed, or a storehouse** in his friend's courtyard, **he renders it prohibited** for his friend to carry there without an *eiruv*. **Rabbi Yehuda says: Only a place of** actual **dwelling renders** carrying **prohibited,** but a building that is not designated for residence does not render carrying without an *eiruv* prohibited for another resident of the courtyard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּבֶן נַפָּחָא שֶׁהָיוּ לוֹ חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵרוֹת בְּאוּשָׁא, וּבָא מַעֲשֶׂה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים, וְאָמְרוּ: אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר אֶלָּא בֵּית דִּירָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said: There was an incident with ben Nappaḥa, who had** houses in **five courtyards in Usha,** only one of which served as his own residence. **And the case came before the Sages** to decide whether an *eiruv* must be made for all of them, **and they said: Only a house of residence renders** carrying **prohibited.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית דִּירָה סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: מְקוֹם דִּירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses surprise at the wording of the *baraita*: **Does it enter your mind** that the correct reading is **a house of residence?** He has a house in each of the five courtyards. **Rather, say: A place of residence,** i.e., it is prohibited to carry in the place where he actually lives, but nowhere else.

Six lines from the bottom of the page, the *gemara* cites a ruling of Rav Yehuda: *eino osel ela mekom dira bilvad* — only a place of actual habitation creates a problem for *eruv*. A granary, a stable, or similar structures do not. Rav Yehuda further illustrates this with a precedent: Ben Nafcha had five separate properties in Usha, and when the matter came before the Sages, they ruled that his ownership of those properties did not impede the residents from making an *eruv*, since those properties were not places of domicile.

The *gemara* then refines the terminology. The phrase *beis dira* — literally "a house of dwelling" — might be read too literally, as if any place where someone happens to sleep or eat would qualify. One of the commentaries explains that the *gemara*'s concern is with fitness for habitation: a gatehouse, for example, does not count even if someone happens to be sleeping there, because it is not designed as a place of human dwelling. The *gemara* therefore emends the formulation to *mekom dira* — a place suited for habitation. This sets the stage for the central debate about what constitutes such a place.

### Rava vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְקוֹם דִּירָה? רַב אָמַר: מְקוֹם פִּיתָּא, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: מְקוֹם לִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** considered one's **place of residence? Rav said: The place** where he eats his **bread, and Shmuel said:** His **place** of **sleep.**

The *gemara* now frames the core dispute explicitly. *Mai mekom dira?* — What is the place of domicile? *Rava amar: mekom pita* — Rava says it is the place where one eats one's bread. *Shmuel amar: mekom lina* — Shmuel says it is the place where one sleeps.

It is worth noting that two distinct questions are in play here. The first is whether a given location qualifies as a *beis dira* at all. If someone sleeps in one courtyard but takes all his meals in another, does the eating-location rise to the level of a *beis dira* that requires participation in the *eruv*? The second question concerns priority: when two locations are in competition — one where a person sleeps and one where he eats — which takes precedence in determining his primary domicile? Both questions may be answered by the same principle, but they are conceptually distinct.

### Shepherds' Domicile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הָרוֹעִים, וְהַקַּיָּיצִין וְהַבּוּרְגָּנִין וְשׁוֹמְרֵי פֵירוֹת, בִּזְמַן שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לָלִין בָּעִיר — הֲרֵי הֵן כְּאַנְשֵׁי הָעִיר. בִּזְמַן שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לָלִין בַּשָּׂדֶה — יֵשׁ לָהֶם אַלְפַּיִם לְכׇל רוּחַ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to Rav's opinion from a *baraita*: With regard to **shepherds; fig watchmen,** who guard figs spread out in the field; **guardsmen** who sit in small guardhouses; **and produce watchmen; when they customarily sleep in the city** in addition to eating there, **they are like the residents of the city** with regard to their Shabbat limit, even though they were in the field when Shabbat began. However, **when they customarily sleep in the field,** even though they eat in the town, **they have** only **two thousand** cubits **in each direction** from the places where they sleep. This seems to contradict the opinion of Rav, who maintains that a person's place of dwelling is determined by where he eats, not by where he sleeps.

The *gemara* raises a challenge from a *baraita*: shepherds, harvesters, wanderers, and fruit-watchers — people who spend their days out in the fields — if they customarily return to the city to sleep, *harei hen k'anshei ha'ir*, they are considered city dwellers. For purposes of *eruv techumin*, the 2,000-*amah* Sabbath boundary is measured from the city's edge, giving them the full benefit of the city's expanse. However, if they customarily sleep in the field, *yesh lo alpayim l'chol ruach*, the 2,000 *amah* is measured from their sleeping location in the field — a far less advantageous starting point. This *baraita* appears to settle the question decisively in favor of Shmuel: domicile follows where one sleeps.

### Eating Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם אֲנַן סָהֲדִי דְּאִי מַמְטוּ לְהוּ רִיפְתָּא הָתָם, טְפֵי נִיחָא לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of the people in the field, **we are witnesses,** i.e., it is clearly evident, **that if** people would **bring them bread there,** to the place where they sleep, **it would be more convenient for them.** Fundamentally, however, a person's dwelling place is determined by where he eats, rather than where he sleeps.

The *gemara* deflects the challenge: *anan sahadi* — it is self-evident — that these field workers, had they been brought their bread where they sleep, would have preferred to eat there. In other words, the *baraita* does not actually disprove Rava's position that domicile follows the place of eating. Rather, since the workers eat in the field only out of necessity — because they work far from home and have no practical alternative — their field eating does not define their genuine *mekom pita*. Where one would choose to eat, given full freedom of choice, is the operative criterion, not where one is compelled to eat by circumstance.

This introduces a notably abstract dimension to the concept of *mekom pita*. The place of eating is no longer simply where one in fact eats, but where one would prefer to eat in an idealized situation. The *gemara* thus makes it difficult to pin down a clean, empirical rule. Both Rava and Shmuel can accommodate most cases by appealing to what the person would choose if circumstances were otherwise. The pattern continues in subsequent passages as the *gemara* applies this contextual reasoning to further scenarios.

## Household Units

### Five Wives & Servants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ חָמֵשׁ נָשִׁים מְקַבְּלוֹת פְּרָס מִבַּעֲלֵיהֶן, וַחֲמִשָּׁה עֲבָדִים מְקַבְּלִין פְּרָס מֵרַבֵּיהֶן — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה מַתִּיר בַּנָּשִׁים, וְאוֹסֵר בָּעֲבָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who has five wives** who **receive a portion from their husband** while each living in her own quarters in the courtyard, **and five slaves** who **receive a portion from their master** while living in their own lodgings in the courtyard, **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira permits in** the case of **the wives,** i.e., they do not each have to contribute separately to the *eiruv*, as they are all considered to be residing with their husband. **And he prohibits in** the case of **the slaves,** meaning that he holds that as they live in separate houses, each is considered as residing on his own.

The *gemara* now turns from the question of what constitutes a *mekom dira* to the related question of household unity: when are multiple individuals considered a single domestic unit for *eruv* purposes? A *baraita* presents a case of a man married to five wives, each living in her own home within the courtyard and receiving her food from her husband — *mekablot pras miba'aleihen* — and five servants similarly living in separate quarters and receiving food from their master — *mekablim pras mirabeihen*. Crucially, the *baraita* explicitly states that each party receives food, corresponding precisely to Rav's interpretation of the *mishna* regarding the brothers: the father provides food, but they eat separately. Neither sleeping together nor eating together in the same house occurs. The question is whether wives or servants in this situation constitute a single unit with the head of household or are independent units requiring separate participation in the *eruv*.

### Ben Beseira's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא מַתִּיר בָּעֲבָדִים, וְאוֹסֵר בַּנָּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava permits in** the case of **the slaves,** as a slave necessarily follows his master, **and he prohibits in** the case of **the wives,** as each woman is significant in her own right, and is not totally dependent on her husband.

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira rules that wives are one unit with the husband and do not require separate participation (*matir benashim*), but servants must participate separately (*oser ba'avadim*). This position is intuitive: husband and wife form a family unit even when occupying separate quarters on the estate, and marital unity is sufficient to subsume the wife's dwelling into the husband's household. Servants, however, are a distinct category — employees rather than family — and their separate quarters constitute separate *batim*.

### Ben Baba's View

Rabbi Yehuda ben Baba takes the opposite position: servants are considered united with their master (*matir ba'avadim*) and do not require separate participation, while wives living in separate homes are considered independent units (*oser benashim*). This is the more surprising ruling and requires explanation.

### Rav's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְדָנִיֵּאל בִּתְרַע מַלְכָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: What is the rationale** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava? As it is written: "But Daniel was in the gate of the king"** (Daniel 2:49). The verse refers to Daniel's function rather than to an actual location, indicating that wherever Daniel went, it was as though he was in the king's gate. The same applies to any slave vis-à-vis his master.

Rav provides the rationale for Rabbi Yehuda ben Baba's position. A *pasuk* states that Daniel was always present at the king's gate — *deDaniel bitra malka* — signifying that a servant-attendant is conceptually always in attendance upon the master, never truly separate. The servants' quarters are therefore an extension of the master's domain; they are always his, as it were, in a functional sense. Wives, by contrast, are independent persons. Even within a patriarchal structure, a wife who has her own home is considered to have established her own domain, and the marital bond alone does not subsume her residence into his.

Tosafot notes a play on words here: *tira* means a gate, and the name *Baba* likewise means a gate, making it fitting that it is Rabbi Yehuda ben Baba who derives his ruling from this verse about the gate.

A question arises as to why the analysis is framed with the husband as the center and the wives as satellites. Women bear independent obligations in *halacha*, so why should a wife not simply be viewed as responsible for her own *eruv*? The answer lies in the nature of the question: the issue is not personal obligation but household classification. A household, however large, requires only one contribution to the *eruv*. If the wife's home is an extension of her husband's household, the single household-unit is obligated once. If she is an independent unit, she — or rather, her dwelling — creates a separate obligation. In a context where one man is the head of a household providing food for multiple dwellings, the question of whether all those dwellings constitute one *reshus* is the operative inquiry.

### Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא, בֵּן אֵצֶל אָבִיו, כְּדַאֲמַרַן. אִשָּׁה אֵצֶל בַּעְלָהּ וְעֶבֶד אֵצֶל רַבּוֹ, פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָה וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא. תַּלְמִיד אֵצֶל רַבּוֹ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to clarify various aspects of this issue, starting with a summary of what has already been stated. The *halakha* is **obvious** in the case of **a son with his father, as we stated** it above the mishna. **A wife with her husband and a slave with his master** are subject to the **dispute** between **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava.** With regard to **a student** who lives **with his master** in the same courtyard and receives his sustenance from him, **what** is his status with regard to *eiruv*?

The *gemara* draws together the different categories. A son vis-à-vis his father — *pita ben etzel aviv* — is the case established in the *mishna*: adult children are separate units, as stated explicitly. The relationship of a wife to her husband and a servant to his master — *isha etzel ba'alah ve'eved etzel rabo* — is the debate between Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira and Rabbi Yehuda ben Baba: one of these relationships creates a tighter unit than a father and adult children, but which one is disputed. Interestingly, the *gemara* does not include the case of the man with five wives in this list, perhaps because the father-children case was established explicitly in the *mishna* at one extreme, and the Rav-student relationship — apparently agreed upon — represents the other.

### Students at Yeshiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּרַב בֵּי רַבִּי חִיָּיא אָמַר: אֵין אָנוּ צְרִיכִין לְעָרֵב, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָנוּ סוֹמְכִין עַל שׁוּלְחָנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי חִיָּיא. וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בֵּי רַבִּי אָמַר: אֵין אָנוּ צְרִיכִין לְעָרֵב, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָנוּ סוֹמְכִין עַל שׁוּלְחָנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this question: **As Rav,** when he was **in the school of Rabbi Ḥiyya, said: We do not need to establish an *eiruv*, as we are dependent upon the table of Rabbi Ḥiyya. And** similarly, **Rabbi Ḥiyya** himself, when he was **in the school of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **said: We do not need to establish an *eiruv*, as we are dependent upon the table of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

This brings the *gemara* to ask: *talmid etzel rabo mai?* — What is the status of a student in relation to his Rebbe? A student boarding on the same courtyard, dependent on the Rebbe's household for sustenance, might be expected to form a tighter unit than an adult child, since the relationship of dependence persists rather than diminishing with maturity.

The *gemara* cites a precedent: when Rav studied in the *yeshiva* of Rabbi Chiya, he declared that the students need not make their own *eruv*, since they all relied on Rabbi Chiya's table — *she'anu somchin al shulchano shel Rabbi Chiya* — even if they did not physically eat there. And Rabbi Chiya himself, when studying with Rebbi, similarly declared that the students need not make an *eruv* because they relied on Rebbi's table. The chain of precedent indicates that students at a *yeshiva* whose sustenance is provided by the Rebbe are considered extensions of the Rebbe's household, forming a single unit that does not require a separate *eruv*.

## External Contributions

### Five Contributors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵרַבָּה: חֲמִשָּׁה שֶׁגָּבוּ אֶת עֵירוּבָן, כְּשֶׁמּוֹלִיכִין אֶת עֵירוּבָן לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר, עֵירוּב אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן, אוֹ צְרִיכִין עֵירוּב לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עֵירוּב אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised a dilemma before Rabba:** With regard to **five** people **who** live in the same courtyard and **collected their *eiruv*, when they take their *eiruv* elsewhere** in order to merge their courtyard with a different one, is **one** contribution to the ***eiruv*** sufficient **for all of them, or do they need** a separate contribution to the ***eiruv* for each and every one** of them? Rabba **said to him: One** contribution to the ***eiruv*** suffices **for all of them.**

The *gemara* now shifts to a related question posed by Abaye to Rava. If five people in a courtyard have already collected an *eruv* — *chamisha she'gavu es eruvon* — and now wish to contribute their *eruv* to another courtyard, can they do so as a single collective unit, or must each person contribute separately? Rava answers: one *eruv* suffices for all of them — *eruv echad lekulan*.

### Brothers' Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַחִין, דְּכִי גָּבוּ דָּמוּ, וְקָתָנֵי: צְרִיכִין עֵירוּב לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא דָּיוֹרִין בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ. דְּמִגּוֹ דְּהָנֵי אָסְרִי, הָנֵי נָמֵי אָסְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye asked: **But** in the case of **brothers, who are comparable** to people **who collected** their *eiruv*, the mishna nonetheless **teaches: They require a** separate ***eiruv* for each and every one** of them. Rabba responded: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where there are** other **residents,** in addition to the father and his sons, living **with them.** In that case, **since these** additional residents **render** carrying in the same courtyard **prohibited** unless they join in an *eiruv*, **those** brothers **also render it prohibited** for one another to carry in the other courtyard unless each of them contributes to the *eiruv*.

The *gemara* challenges this from the case of the brothers in the *mishna*. The brothers, when the *eruv* remains in their home, are treated as a single unit and need not contribute separately. This resembles a situation in which they have already made an *eruv* among themselves. Yet the *mishna* states that when they go out to contribute their *eruv* elsewhere, they must each contribute separately. Should the same not apply to the five people in Rava's case?

### Strangers' Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁמּוֹלִיכִין אֶת עֵירוּבָן בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר, אֲבָל אִם הָיָה עֵירוּבָן בָּא אֶצְלָם, אוֹ שֶׁאֵין דָּיוֹרִין עִמָּהֶן בֶּחָצֵר — אֵין צְרִיכִין לְעָרֵב, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** to understand, **as** the mishna **teaches: When** do they state this *halakha*? **When they bring their *eiruv* elsewhere** in the courtyard. **But if their *eiruv* was coming to them, or if there are no** other **residents with them in the courtyard, they do not need to establish an *eiruv*,** as they are considered like a single individual living in a courtyard. **Learn from this** that the preceding ruling refers to a situation where they shared the courtyard with other residents.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *hacha bemai askinan kegon de'ika diyarin bahadeihu* — the *mishna*'s case involves strangers also living in the courtyard. It is precisely the presence of these outsiders that compels separate contribution. The *gemara* articulates the principle: *demigo dehanei asri hanei nami asri* — because the strangers create a prohibition for the brothers' side, this in turn establishes the brothers as a distinct unit as well. Once a stranger's presence demands that the courtyard be treated as composed of multiple units, the brothers can no longer claim to be a single cohesive household. They too become individual units, and thus must contribute separately when going out.

### Receiving vs. Contributing

By contrast, when they are receiving the *eruv* — when the bread is simply deposited in their home — they are not actively asserting anything about the structure of the courtyard. There is no active participation that underscores the multiplicity of units. The *gemara* therefore permits them to be treated as a single unit in that context. But when they actively contribute, they are acknowledging and participating in a system that recognizes separate units, and that very act of participation makes them individually distinct. This asymmetry — the same brothers being one unit when receiving and separate units when contributing — is explained entirely by the presence of strangers, which creates the condition in which active participation forces individual accountability.

In Rava's case, by contrast, everyone actively made an *eruv*, uniting them into a genuine single unit. Nothing disrupts that unity. When they go to contribute, there is no stranger's presence to disaggregate them, so one contribution on behalf of the whole suffices. The *gemara* confirms this reasoning: *shma mina* — we derive from here that it is the introduction of strangers that creates the disruption. Without them, the active *eruv* holds them together as one.

## Techumin Shabbos

### Students' Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בְּנֵי בֵי רַב, דְּאָכְלִי נַהֲמָא בְּבָאגָא, וְאָתוּ וּבָיְיתִי בְּבֵי רַב, כִּי מָשְׁחִינַן לְהוּ תְּחוּמָא, מִבֵּי רַב מָשְׁחִינַן לְהוּ אוֹ מִבָּאגָא מָשְׁחִינַן לְהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָשְׁחִינַן מִבֵּי רַב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara addresses a similar issue with regard to a joining of Shabbat boundaries: **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet:** With regard to **students in their master's house who eat** their **bread in** their houses **in the field [*baga*] and** then **come and sleep in** their **master's house, when we measure** their Shabbat **limit for them, do we measure** it **for them from** their **master's house,** where they sleep, **or do we measure** it **for them from the field,** where they eat? **He said to him: We measure** it **from** their **master's house.**

The *gemara* now presents a question from Rav Chiya bar Avin to Rav Sheshet. The case concerns *bnei bei Rav* — *yeshiva* students — who eat their bread in a house or hall some distance away (*baguei*) and return to sleep in the *beis medrash* of the *yeshiva*. The question is where to measure the *techum Shabbos* for these students: from the *yeshiva* where they sleep, or from the place where they eat — the classic scenario of dormitory and dining hall in different locations.

### Bread Location Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי נוֹתֵן אֶת עֵירוּבוֹ בְּתוֹךְ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה, וְאָתֵי וּבָיֵית בְּבֵיתֵיהּ, דְּמָשְׁחִינַן לֵיהּ תְּחוּמָא מֵעֵירוּבֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin asked: **But** in the case of **one who deposits his *eiruv*,** which establishes the location of his meal, **within two thousand cubits, and** then **goes** back **and sleeps in his house, we measure his** Shabbat **limit from his *eiruv*.** This implies that the determining factor is where he eats, rather than where he sleeps.

Rav Sheshet answers: *mishchina mi-bei Rav* — measure from the *yeshiva* itself, i.e., from where they sleep. The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty: this seems to contradict the very logic of *eruv techumin*. When one deposits an *eruv techumin* — placing bread at a designated point to establish a Sabbath domicile there — one measures from where the bread is, not from where one returns home to sleep that night. Does this not demonstrate that the place of eating (or at least the place of the bread) takes priority over the place of sleep?

### Contextual Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהָהוּא — אֲנַן סָהֲדִי, וּבְהָדָא — אֲנַן סָהֲדִי. בְּהָהוּא אֲנַן סָהֲדִי — דְּאִי מִיתְּדַר לֵיהּ הָתָם נִיחָא לֵיהּ, וּבַהֲדָא אֲנַן סָהֲדִי — דְּאִי מַיְיתוּ לְהוּ רִיפְתָּא לְבֵי רַב, נִיחָא לְהוּ טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **In that** case **we are witnesses, and in this** case **we are witnesses,** i.e., in both cases the person's intentions regarding his place of residence are clearly evident. **In that** case, where the person deposits his *eiruv*, **we are witnesses that if he** could **reside there,** at the site of his *eiruv*, **it would be better for him,** i.e., if he could spend the night there he would do so, since he wishes to continue from that place onward on the following day. **And in this** case of the students in their master's house, **we are witnesses that if** people **would bring them bread in their master's house,** enabling them to eat there, **it would be better for them.** Consequently, it is considered their place of residence.

The *gemara* resolves this with the same contextual reasoning deployed earlier: *anan sahadi* — it is self-evident — in each case what the person would prefer. Regarding *eruv techumin*: someone who places the *eruv* to the north, intending to travel in that direction on Shabbos, demonstrably would prefer his domicile to be there. He has to schlep out to the *eruv* location in any case, and he would rather begin from there. So it is evident that given full choice, he would prefer to eat and live at the *eruv* location — his effective preference places him there.

In the case of the *yeshiva* students, by contrast, *anan sahadi de-maisu le-rifta le-bei Rav nicha lehu* — it is self-evident that they would rather have their food brought to them where they sleep in the *yeshiva*. The fact that the dining hall is elsewhere is a circumstantial arrangement, not a reflection of genuine preference. Therefore, the *yeshiva* — their sleeping place — is their true domicile. The *gemara* thus continues to resist any rigid rule of "always follow sleep" or "always follow eating," instead calibrating each case according to where the person would genuinely prefer to be located.

## Father & Children

### Rami's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: אָב וּבְנוֹ, הָרַב וְתַלְמִידוֹ, כְּרַבִּים דָּמוּ אוֹ כִּיחִידִים דָּמוּ? צְרִיכִין עֵירוּב, אוֹ אֵין צְרִיכִין עֵירוּב? מָבוֹי שֶׁלָּהֶן נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה, אוֹ אֵין נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda:** With regard to **a father and his son,** or **a master and his student, are they considered as many** people **or as individuals?** The practical import of the question is as follows: If they lived together in a courtyard that was within another courtyard, are they considered as many people, who **require an *eiruv*** in order to render it permitted to carry in the outer courtyard, **or do they not require an *eiruv*,** as they are treated as an individual, who does not render carrying in the outer courtyard prohibited? Is **their alleyway** rendered **permitted** for carrying **through a side post and** a cross **beam,** like one that has multiple residents, **or is it not** rendered **permitted** for carrying **through a side post and** a cross **beam?**

The *gemara* records a further question posed by Rami bar Chama to Rav Chisda: *av u-veno, ha-Rav ve-talmido* — a father and his son, or a Rav and his student — what is their status in *eruv* law? This appears at first glance to be a recapitulation of the discussions already conducted. According to Rashi, it largely is. Tosafot suggests the new question may involve a scenario where they eat at separate tables rather than together, which would constitute a slight variation on the earlier cases. In either reading, the question touches on the same essential inquiry about household unity.

### Unit Classification

The question is posed in two parts: *tzrichim eruv o ein tzrichim eruv* — do they need an *eruv* at all? And a second question: are they classified as many or as one? The first question presupposes that if they are the sole occupants of a courtyard, perhaps no *eruv* is required at all. The second question concerns how they are treated vis-à-vis others.

### Mavoi Requirements

The second part of the question takes on particular halachic significance with respect to *lechi* and *kora*. A *mavoi* — a dead-end alleyway — does not require a full wall to permit carrying. A *lechi* (side post) or *kora* (crossbeam) suffices. However, this leniency applies only to a structure that qualifies as a genuine *mavoi*, and the definition of *mavoi* requires at minimum two *chatzeros* each containing at least two *batim*. If the father and sons, or the Rav and students, constitute a single household, then their collective dwellings might be classified as one *bayit* rather than multiple *batim*, which would mean the courtyard fails to meet the minimum threshold of a proper *chatzer*, and the *mavoi* would not qualify for the *lechi*-and-*kora* leniency.

### No Strangers Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: אָב וּבְנוֹ, הָרַב וְתַלְמִידוֹ, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין עִמָּהֶן דָּיוֹרִין הֲרֵי הֵן כִּיחִידִים, וְאֵין צְרִיכִין לְעָרֵב, וּמָבוֹי שֶׁלָּהֶן נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: You have** already **learned this** in the following *baraita*: With regard to **a father and his son** or **a master and his student, when there are no** other **residents with them, they are** considered **like individuals, and they do not need to establish an *eiruv*, and their alleyway** becomes **permitted** for carrying **through a side post and** a cross **beam** without a merging of alleyways.

Rav Chisda responds by citing a *baraita*: *av u-veno, ha-Rav ve-talmido, bizman she'ein imahem diyurin* — a father and his son, or a Rav and his student, when there are no other residents in the courtyard with them — *harei hen ke-yachidim* — they are considered like a single individual. This directly parallels what was established earlier: it is the introduction of strangers that fragments the unity. Without strangers, they are a coherent single unit, and *ein tzrichim le'arev* — they need not make an *eruv* among themselves.

### Lechi & Kora Leniency

Nevertheless, the *baraita* continues: *u-mavoi shelahen nitar be-lechi ve-korah* — their *mavoi* is still permissible with a *lechi* or *kora*. This is the crucial resolution: although they are classified as one unit for purposes of *eruv* requirements, they retain enough of a distinct character that their courtyard counts as a legitimate *chatzer* and their *mavoi* qualifies for the lesser leniency. The answer, in effect, is that these categories resist a clean binary. The father-and-children or Rav-and-student household is one unit in some respects and several in others. When strangers enter, they fully fragment into distinct units; in their absence, they function as one — yet even then, they retain enough structural distinctness to support the *lechi*-and-*kora* leniency for the *mavoi*.

## Eruv vs. Shituf

### Five Courtyards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָמֵשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת פְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ וּפְתוּחוֹת לְמָבוֹי, עֵירְבוּ בַּחֲצֵירוֹת וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בַּמָּבוֹי — מוּתָּרִין בַּחֲצֵירוֹת וַאֲסוּרִין בְּמָבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **five courtyards open into one another and** also **open into an alleyway,** the following distinctions apply: If the residents of the courtyard **established an *eiruv* in the courtyards and did not merge the** courtyards that open into the **alleyway, they are permitted** to carry **in the courtyards and they are prohibited** to carry **in the alleyway.** The *eiruv* they established cannot also serve as a merging of the courtyards that open into the alleyway.

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishna* concerning five *chatzeros* that are open to one another and open into a common *mavoi*. The *mishna* explores the interplay between *eruv chatzeros* and *shituf meva'ot*, asking whether each can substitute for the other.

### Eruv Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בַּמָּבוֹי — מוּתָּרִין כָּאן וְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if they merged** the courtyards of **the alleyway, they are permitted** to carry both **here,** in the alleyway, and **there,** in the courtyards.

If the residents of the courtyards made an *eruv* among their *chatzeros* but did not make a *shituf* in the *mavoi* — *eirvu ba-chatzeros velo nishtatfu ba-mavoi* — then the *chatzeros* are permitted for carrying but the *mavoi* remains forbidden. Making an *eruv* has unified the courtyards into a coherent residential unit, but that unification has not extended to the street. The *mavoi* remains a separate domain with no mechanism to permit carrying within it.

### Shituf Alone

Conversely, if they made a *shituf* in the *mavoi* without making *eruv chatzeros* — *ve'im nishtatfu ba-mavoi mutarin kan u-van* — then carrying is permitted both in the courtyards and in the *mavoi*. The logic here is that when the residents unify at the level of the street, emerging from their respective courtyards and sharing the *mavoi*, that unification radiates inward and encompasses the courtyards as well. The *shituf* thereby accomplishes what the *eruv* could not do in reverse.

### Asymmetry

The *mishna* thus presents a clear asymmetry: *shituf* can substitute for *eruv*, but *eruv* cannot substitute for *shituf*. The lecturer notes that this asymmetry is intuitively compelling. The *eruv chatzeros* is an inward-facing act of unification; it consolidates the private domain of the *chatzer* but does not engage the street. The *shituf*, by contrast, operates at the level of the *mavoi*, the quasi-public corridor through which all the courtyards communicate — and so it naturally draws together both the street and the courtyards feeding into it. The asymmetry flows from the structural relationship between the domains.

### Gemara's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵירְבוּ בַּחֲצֵירוֹת וְנִשְׁתַּתְּפוּ בַּמָּבוֹי, וְשָׁכַח אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חָצֵר וְלֹא עֵירַב — מוּתָּרִין כָּאן וְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** If **they established an *eiruv* in the courtyards** and also **merged** the courtyards of **the alleyway, and one of the residents of the courtyard forgot and did not** contribute to the ***eiruv*** in his courtyard, but did participate in the merging of the courtyards in the alleyway, **they are permitted** both **here and there,** as the merging of courtyards in the alleyway serves as an effective *eiruv* for the courtyards as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבְּנֵי מָבוֹי וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּתֵּף — מוּתָּרִין בַּחֲצֵירוֹת וַאֲסוּרִין בַּמָּבוֹי, שֶׁהַמָּבוֹי לַחֲצֵירוֹת כֶּחָצֵר לַבָּתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** However, if one **of the residents of the alleyway** forgot **and did not** participate in the **merging** of courtyards that open into the alleyway, **they are permitted** to carry **in the courtyards and prohibited** from carrying **in the alleyway, as** the principle is: **An alleyway is to** its **courtyards as a courtyard is to** its **houses.**

The *gemara*, however, refuses to accept this asymmetry. It argues that the *mishna* must be internally consistent: either one can always substitute for the other, or neither can substitute. The *gemara* raises a further complication from the *mishna*'s own continuation: if everyone made both an *eruv* and a *shituf* and then one person from a *chatzer* forgot to contribute to the *eruv*, it is still permitted. And if one person forgot to contribute to the *shituf*, carrying in the *mavoi* is forbidden. The *gemara* proceeds to test whether ביטול *bitul* (nullification of rights) can explain these outcomes under a single, internally consistent rule — whether that of Rabbi Meir or the *rabanan* — and finds that no such clean explanation resolves the apparent contradictions. It attempts to attribute the beginning and end of the *mishna* to Rabbi Meir and the middle to the *rabanan*, then rejects this patchwork as untenable.

## Rabbi Meir's Rule

### Need Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַנִּי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בָּעִינַן עֵירוּב וּבָעִינַן שִׁיתּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? The Gemara answers: **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: We require an *eiruv* and we** also **require a merging** of the courtyards in an alleyway, and one is not sufficient without the other.

Having failed to resolve the *mishna* by splitting it between authorities, the *gemara* asserts that the entire *mishna* follows Rabbi Meir — *kulla Rabbi Meir* — who holds that one always needs both an *eruv* and a *shituf*: *ba'inan eruv u-va'inan shituf*.

### One Forgot Eruv

Under this reading, the first clause of the *mishna* — stating that a *shituf* permits both the courtyards and the *mavoi* — means that they performed both a *shituf* and an *eruv*, and it was the combination of the two that achieved full permission. The apparently asymmetric statement was not about *shituf* alone sufficing but about the case where both were in place. Then the *mishna* moves to the scenario where everyone made an *eruv* and a *shituf* but one person forgot to contribute to the *eruv*: the result is still permitted. The *gemara* explains that in this case the *shituf* serves as a backup.

### One Forgot Shituf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: רַב לָא תָּנֵי ״פְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ״. וְכֵן אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: רַב לָא תָּנֵי ״פְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ״. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, רַב כָּהֲנָא גּוּפֵיהּ לָא תָּנֵי ״פְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: Rav did not teach** the mishna as stating that the five courtyards **open into one another,** but rather that each courtyard opens into the alleyway, and each established its own *eiruv*. **And so too, Rav Kahana said: Rav did not teach** the mishna as stating that the courtyards **open into one another. Some say** that **Rav Kahana himself did not teach** the mishna as stating that the courtyards **open into one another.**

By contrast, if one courtyard's residents forgot to participate in the *shituf* of the *mavoi*, then while carrying within the courtyards (covered by the *eruv*) remains permitted, the *mavoi* is forbidden. Here, the *eruv* cannot serve as a backup for the missing *shituf*. The problem exists even though everyone has an *eruv*, because the *eruv* does not encompass the *mavoi*.

### Shituf Backup Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, וְטַעְמָא מַאי אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר בָּעִינַן עֵירוּב וּבָעִינַן שִׁיתּוּף? שֶׁלֹּא לְשַׁכֵּחַ תּוֹרַת עֵירוּב מִן הַתִּינוֹקוֹת, וְהָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּרוּבַּהּ עֵירְבוּ — לָא מִשְׁתַּכְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In fact, **it is all** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. And what is the reason** that **Rabbi Meir said we require an *eiruv* and we** also **require a merging** of alleyways? It was only so **as not to cause the** halakhic **category of *eiruv* to be forgotten by the children.** If people would only merge courtyards, the *halakha* of establishing an *eiruv* for a courtyard would gradually be forgotten. **And here,** where only one person forgot to contribute to the *eiruv*, **since most of** them **established an *eiruv*** for the courtyards, the *halakha* of an *eiruv* **will not be forgotten.** Therefore, there is room to be lenient after the fact and to permit carrying in both places.

The *gemara* explains the asymmetry between these two cases under Rabbi Meir's framework. Rabbi Meir's reason for requiring both *eruv* and *shituf* is not that each is independently necessary in principle, but that he wishes to prevent people from forgetting that two distinct *halachos* exist — *shelo lishakeach toras eruv min ha-tinokos*. In a case where most or nearly all participants actively made both an *eruv* and a *shituf*, and only a single person inadvertently forgot the *eruv*, the *halacha* of *eruv* is not at risk of being forgotten. Therefore Rabbi Meir tolerates the *shituf* functioning as a backup in that narrow circumstance. This is a strikingly situation-dependent ruling, assessing each case on the basis of how likely the underlying *halacha* is to be forgotten. The lecturer acknowledges that the ruling has an ad hoc quality to it, and notes that the plain reading of the *mishna* — simple asymmetry between *shituf* and *eruv* — remains the more natural interpretation. The *gemara* does not appear to even consider this possibility, which is itself a striking feature of the sugya.

## Rav's Text

### Text Variant

Having failed to resolve the *mishna* by splitting it between authorities, the *gemara* now pursues a different approach: perhaps Rav's textual version of the *mishna* differs from the one we have. The idea is that Rav may have read the *mishna* without the phrase "open to one another," which would fundamentally change how the case should be understood and resolve many of the apparent contradictions.

### Why Eruv Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַב, דְּקָא סָבַר: אֵין מָבוֹי נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ בָּתִּים וַחֲצֵירוֹת פְּתוּחִים לְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** we must retract the previous explanation and say that **the reason Rav** did not teach the mishna as stating that the courtyards opened into one another is **that he holds** that **an alleyway cannot** be **rendered permitted** for carrying **through a side post and** a cross **beam unless there are houses and courtyards opening into it.** If, however, the courtyards open into one another, they are considered like a single courtyard, in which case they cannot be rendered permitted for carrying through a side post or a cross beam, and the merging of the alleyway is ineffective.

This textual variant has a substantive halachic payoff. If the courtyards are not open to one another, then an *eruv* made among their residents is not a joint *eruv chatzeros* in the ordinary sense but rather a collection of individual *eruvin* made separately by each household. Individual *eruvin* that have not merged the courtyards into a unified domain cannot substitute for a *shituf* of the *mavoi*, because they have not achieved any collective unification. The *shituf*, which does unify at the *mavoi* level, can then substitute for the *eruv* — because it draws everyone together — but not vice versa. Rav's *girsa* thus resolves the asymmetry that troubled the *gemara*: it is not that *shituf* is inherently more powerful in some abstract sense, but that separate individual *eruvin* simply have no capacity to unify anyone for purposes of the shared street.

## Shituf Requirements

### Entry & Exit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּלָא תָּנֵי פְּתוּחוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ. קָסָבַר כׇּל שִׁיתּוּף שֶׁאֵין מַכְנִיסוֹ וּמוֹצִיאוֹ דֶּרֶךְ פְּתָחִים בַּמָּבוֹי — לָאו שְׁמֵיהּ שִׁיתּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: What is the reason he did not teach** the mishna as stating that the five courtyards **open into one another?** Rav Yosef replied: Because **he holds** that **any merging** of alleyways **that is not brought in and taken out by way of the entrances** that open **into the alleyway,** i.e., which is not brought from each courtyard into the alleyway and then taken from the alleyway into the courtyard where it will be deposited, **is not considered** a valid **merging** of the alleyway. If the food used for the merging of alleyways is transferred directly from one courtyard to another, it seems as though it is being used to establish an *eiruv*. It is therefore ineffective as a merging of alleyways. Here too, if the courtyards open into one another, the merging of alleyways is invalid, due to a concern that the residents of the courtyard will transfer the food directly from one courtyard to another.

The *gemara* now introduces a different approach, attributed to Rav Kahana. He holds that a valid *shituf* for a *mavoi* requires that residents physically enter and exit each courtyard through the *mavoi* — *machniso u-motzio derech ptachim ba-mavoi* — in the course of making the *shituf*. The act of traversing the street while gathering or depositing the *eruv* bread is what gives the *shituf* its unifying quality. The street must actively be used as the conduit through which the partnership is forged.

### Direct Passage Problem

This requirement creates a problem when the courtyards are open to one another. If residents can pass directly from one courtyard to another without ever entering the *mavoi*, they can complete the entire process of assembling the *shituf* without using the street at all. Under Rav Kahana's view, such a *shituf* would be defective.

### Not a True Shituf

If the collection is conducted through internal passageways rather than through the *mavoi*, then even though bread has been gathered and shared, *lo shmeh shituf* — it is not called a *shituf* in the halachic sense. The act of physically unifying the parties through the shared public space is constitutive of the *shituf*, not merely incidental to it.

### Business Partner Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁהָיָה שׁוּתָּף לִשְׁכֵנָיו, לָזֶה בְּיַיִן וְלָזֶה בְּיַיִן, אֵין צְרִיכִין לְעָרֵב. הָתָם, דְּאַפְּקֵיהּ וְעַיְּילֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He raised an objection to him** based upon the following mishna: **A homeowner who was a partner of his neighbors, with this one in wine and with that one in wine, they do not need to establish an *eiruv*.** This indicates that it is not actually necessary to transfer the food used for the merging of alleyways from one place to another. For example, it is sufficient to have a jointly owned barrel of wine in one courtyard even if it did not pass through the alleyway. The Gemara rejects this proof and explains the mishna as follows: **There,** it is referring to a case **where they took** the wine **out** into the alleyway and subsequently **brought it in** to the courtyard where it was to be kept.

The *gemara* tests this principle against an earlier *mishna* about a homeowner who was in a wine partnership with his neighbor — each having contributed to a shared barrel. The *mishna* had treated that business partnership as constituting a *shituf*. But surely, the *gemara* objects, those business partners did not go in and out of each other's courtyards through the *mavoi* as part of their commercial arrangement. The *gemara* responds that they did in fact do so — *d'afkei ve'ayeilei* — when transporting the wine. This is a strained *ukhimta*, and the *gemara* presses further.

### Shared Meal Bread

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* (אֵיתִיבֵיהּ:) כֵּיצַד מִשְׁתַּתְּפִין בַּמָּבוֹי וְכוּ׳. הָתָם נָמֵי: דְּאַפְּקֵיהּ וְעַיְּילֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He raised** another **objection to him** from a different mishna: **How does one merge** courtyards that open **into alleyways?** The mishna continues and says that it is sufficient for one person to acquire the food used for the merging on behalf of all the other residents of the alleyway. This indicates that the food does not need to pass through all the courtyards in the alleyway. The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **There too,** it is referring to a case **where they** first **took** the food **out** from each of the courtyards into the alleyway **and** from there **brought it into** the courtyard where it was to be deposited.

A similar challenge arises from the rule that bread shared at a communal meal — where a group was eating together when Shabbos arrived unexpectedly — can serve as an *eruv* or, according to some, as a *shituf*. Surely these people did not traverse the *mavoi* together while eating their meal. Once again, the *gemara* insists that they did go in and out through the street.

### Location Matters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבָּה בַּר חָנָן: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, הִקְנָה לוֹ פַּת בְּסַלּוֹ הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא הָוֵי שִׁיתּוּף?! וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּנֵי חֲבוּרָה שֶׁהָיוּ מְסוּבִּין וְקִדֵּשׁ עֲלֵיהֶן הַיּוֹם — הַפַּת שֶׁעַל שֻׁלְחָן סוֹמְכִים עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם עֵירוּב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ מִשּׁוּם שִׁיתּוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ḥanan strongly objects to this: However, if** that **is so,** if **he transferred ownership of bread in his basket to** another person, **so too, it would not be** considered a valid **merging. And if you say** that **this is** indeed **so, didn't Rav Yehuda say that Rav said:** With regard to **members of a group who were dining** together on Shabbat eve, **and the day became sanctified for them,** i.e., Shabbat began while they were eating, **they may rely upon the bread on the table as an *eiruv*** for the courtyard, **and some say, as a merging** of the alleyway.

Rabbah offers a resolution that sidesteps the question of street-traversal entirely. The debate over whether the shared meal bread constitutes an *eruv* or a *shituf* depends simply on where the meal was held. *Kan be-mesubin ba-bayis* — if they were eating in a house, the bread serves as an *eruv chatzeros*, the deposit being in the house. *Ve-kan be-mesubin ba-chatzer* — if they were eating in the courtyard, the bread serves as a *shituf* of the *mavoi*, the deposit being in the *chatzer*. Crucially, in neither case did the participants walk through the *mavoi* during the course of their meal. Rabbah's resolution thus effectively refutes the premise of Rav Kahana's requirement: since a shared meal can constitute a valid *shituf* without any traversal of the street, street-traversal cannot be the operative factor. The *gemara* concedes: the approach requiring active passage through the *mavoi* must be abandoned.

## Rav's Alternative

### One Courtyard Result

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב: אֵין מָבוֹי נִיתָּר בְּלֶחִי וְקוֹרָה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ בָּתִּים וַחֲצֵירוֹת פְּתוּחִין לְתוֹכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now examines **the matter itself** cited in the previous discussion. **Rav said: An alleyway cannot** become **permitted** for carrying **through a side post and** a cross **beam,** unless there are houses and courtyards opening into it.

Having set aside Rav Kahana's traversal requirement, the *gemara* returns to Rav's actual reason for excluding the phrase *petuchot zu la-zu* from the *mishna*. The issue is not the manner of making the *shituf* but a consequence of making the *eruv*. If all five courtyards make an *eruv* together, they are transformed into a single unified *chatzer*. What had been five separate courtyards becomes, legally, one.

### Lechi Ineligibility

This transformation creates a problem. A *mavoi* is only eligible for the leniency of *lechi* or *kora* if it services at least two *chatzeros*, each containing at least two *batim*. If the five courtyards have merged into one through the *eruv*, the *mavoi* now opens onto a single *chatzer* — and a single *chatzer* does not satisfy the minimum threshold. The *mavoi* therefore loses eligibility for the *lechi*-or-*kora* leniency and would require a full wall to permit carrying.

### Transformation Problem

This is the reason Rav excises the phrase "open to one another" from his text. If the courtyards are open to each other and the residents make a combined *eruv*, they genuinely unify into one courtyard — and that unification, while achieving permission within the courtyards, undermines the *mavoi*'s status. Rav's concern is structural: the very success of the *eruv* destroys the prerequisite for the *mavoi*'s leniency. There is a paradox here — getting things right in one domain creates a problem in another — and Rav resolves it by insisting that the *mishna*'s case not involve courtyards open to each other at all.
