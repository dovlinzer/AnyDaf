# Pesachim 80b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tzitz & Tamei Meat

### Pesach Tamei Overview

The *mishnah* near the top of 80b introduces a principle that has been building throughout the preceding discussion: the *tzitz* — the golden forehead-plate worn by the *Kohen Gadol* — has the power to render acceptable a *korban* that would otherwise be invalidated by *tumah*. The specific case here is that of an individual's *korban Pesach*, a *korban yachid*, where the *pesach* itself became *tamei* while the owner remained *tahor*. The *mishnah* states: *hapesach shenizrak damo v'achar kach noda shehu tamei — hatzitz meratzeh* — if the blood of the *pesach* was sprinkled and only afterward did it become known that the offering had been *tamei*, the *tzitz* renders it acceptable.

The underlying assumption here, following the view that we do not rule like *Rebbi Natan*, is that if the meat is *tamei* the owner does not fulfill his obligation of *korban Pesach*. Ordinarily — and certainly *lechatchila*, and likely even *b'di'eved* — if the meat or blood is *tamei* and one is aware of this, the *pesach* is invalid. But when the person was ignorant of the *tumah* at the time, the *tzitz* operates to make it acceptable. The condition of ignorance, as we will see, is central to the entire discussion that follows.

### Tzitz: Objects vs. Person

The *mishnah* draws a sharp distinction between two types of *tumah*. The *tzitz* is effective when the objects involved — the meat or the blood — became *tamei*. These are the *kodshim* themselves, and the *tzitz* atones, so to speak, for the *avon hakodashim*, the sin inherent in the *tumah* of the sanctified matter. However, if it is the *guf* — the person himself — who became *tamei*, the *tzitz* does not apply: *nitma haguf ein hatzitz meratzeh*. This is stated in the *mishnah* as an established tradition: the *tzitz* is *meratzeh* for the *tumah* of the blood and meat in the case of the *nazir* and the one bringing the *korban Pesach*, but it does not render acceptable *tumat haguf*.

It is worth pausing on why the *mishnah* specifically mentions the *nazir* alongside the one bringing the *pesach*. The reason will become clearer as the discussion proceeds, but the common thread is that these are the two *korbanot* for which the *tumah* of the person is uniquely consequential: with other *korbanot*, a *tamei* owner can simply arrange for someone else to perform the *zerikat hadam*, and the *korban* remains valid. With the *pesach* and the *nazir*, however, the person's own *tumah* undermines the very possibility of bringing the *korban* at all.

### Tumat HaTehom

The *mishnah* then introduces a remarkable exception: *nitma tumat hatehom — hatzitz meratzeh*. Even with respect to *tumat haguf*, where the *tzitz* normally does not operate, if the *tumah* is of the category known as *tumat hatehom* — literally the *tumah* of the depths — the *tzitz* does render it acceptable.

*Tumat hatehom* is not merely a situation where the person happened to be unaware. It refers to a *tumah* that is objectively unknowable — a doubt that exists not simply in the mind of the individual but in the very nature of the situation. The paradigmatic case is a corpse buried underground, unknown to anyone, whose *tumah* one inadvertently incurred. The *halacha* accepted here, transmitted as *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*, is that such an unknowable *tumah* does not disqualify the individual when the *tzitz* is present, even though ordinarily *tumat haguf* would not be remedied by the *tzitz*.

### Pesach & Nazir Rules

This special dispensation — that the *tzitz* works even for *tumat haguf* in a case of *tumat hatehom* — is specifically limited to the *pesach* and the *nazir*. These are precisely the two cases where personal *tumah* is most consequential. A *tamei* person seeking to bring the *korban Pesach* is entirely displaced to *Pesach Sheini*. A *nazir* who becomes *tamei* must restart his *nezirut* from the beginning and cannot complete his *korban* to conclude the *nezirut*. In both cases, the person's *tumah* fundamentally prevents the *korban* from being brought at all.

For other *korbanot*, the question of the owner's *tumah* is largely irrelevant — someone else can perform the *zerikat hadam* regardless. But for *pesach* and *nazir*, we have a special tradition (*halacha l'Moshe miSinai*) that if the *tumah* was truly unknowable — *tumat hatehom* — then the *tzitz* makes it acceptable even for *tumat haguf*. The *mishnah*'s pairing of *pesach* and *nazir* is thus not incidental; they represent the only two cases where this particular dispensation is even relevant.

## Ignorance Requirement

### Timing of Ignorance

The *gemara* opens by analyzing the precise condition of ignorance required for the *tzitz* to operate in the case of *tamei* blood or meat. The *mishnah*'s phrasing — *nizrak damo v'achar kach noda*, the blood was sprinkled and then it became known — implies that what matters is the state of awareness at the moment of *zerikat hadam*. From this the *gemara* derives: *ta'ama d'nizrak v'achar kach noda* — the *tzitz* works because one sprinkled while still unaware. The corollary is clear: *aval noda v'achar kach nizrak — lo meratzeh* — if one already knew the *dam* was *tamei* before the *zerikat hadam* was performed, the *tzitz* does not help.

### Ravina vs. R. Shila

This straightforward reading of the *mishnah* is immediately challenged by a *beraita*. The *beraita* asks: for what does the *tzitz* render things acceptable? For blood, meat, or fat that became *tamei* — *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid*, whether unintentionally or intentionally; *bein b'oness bein b'ratzon*, whether against one's will or willingly; *bein b'yachid bein b'tzibur*, whether an individual's *korban* or a communal one. The *beraita* seems to say explicitly that the *tzitz* operates even if the *tumah* or the *zerikat hadam* was done *b'meizid* — deliberately and knowingly.

*Ravina* resolves the contradiction as follows. When the *beraita* says *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid*, it is referring to how the *dam* or *basar* became *tamei* in the first place. Whether the *tumah* of the object came about intentionally or unintentionally is irrelevant — the *tzitz* works either way. What does matter, however, is the state of mind at the time of *zerikat hadam*: *zrikato b'shogeg hurtzah, b'meizid lo hurtzah* — if the *zerikat hadam* itself was performed in ignorance, it is accepted; if it was performed with full knowledge of the *tumah*, it is not.

*Rabbi Shila* takes the opposite position. He accepts the *beraita* at face value and argues that even if one knew about the *tumah* and proceeded to perform the *zerikat hadam* deliberately, the *tzitz* still makes it acceptable: *zrikato bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid hurtzah*. What matters for *Rabbi Shila* is not the intentionality of the *zerikat hadam* but the manner in which the *tumah* itself occurred. If the *tumah* came about *b'shogeg* — unintentionally — then the *tzitz* works regardless of how the *zrika* was performed. But if the *tumah* itself was *b'meizid* — if the object was deliberately rendered *tamei* — then the *tzitz* does not help.

The logic of *Rabbi Shila*'s position, while striking, has a certain internal coherence: an object that was deliberately made *tamei* bears a qualitative stain that makes it undeserving of the *tzitz*'s atonement. An object that became *tamei* inadvertently can more naturally be "papered over" by the *tzitz*, even if one subsequently proceeds knowingly with the *zerikat hadam*. This is admittedly a counterintuitive reading and not the simple sense of the *mishnah*, but it is how *Rabbi Shila* understands the law.

### Braita Reconciliation

The *gemara* then confronts the question of how to reconcile each position with the other's primary text. For *Rabbi Shila*, who holds that the *tumah* must have arisen *b'shogeg* and that the *zrika* may be *b'meizid*, how does he read the *beraita*'s statement *bein b'shogeg bein b'meizid*? The *gemara* explains that, according to him, the *beraita* should be read as: when the *tumah* arose *b'shogeg*, I do not care whether the subsequent *zrika* was *b'shogeg* or *b'meizid* — the *tzitz* works either way.

The more pressing question is how *Ravina* — who holds that the *zrika* must be *b'shogeg* — can accommodate the *mishnah*'s seemingly restrictive phrasing. After all, the *mishnah* says *nizrak v'achar kach noda*, implying that one must have been unaware at the time of *zrika*. The *gemara* offers an elegant resolution: the *mishnah* chose to present the case of ignorance at the time of *zrika* not because that is the only scenario where the *tzitz* works, but because of the *mishnah*'s literary structure. The *mishnah* needed to contrast the first clause — where the *tzitz* works — with the second clause, where *nitma haguf* and the *tzitz* does *not* work. In the second clause, the *gemara* tells us, the *tzitz* fails to operate *even* when one was ignorant at the time. So to maintain the parallel structure, the *mishnah* presented the first clause using the case of ignorance at the time of *zrika* as well — *tani reisha nami nizrak v'achar kach noda* — even though in reality, according to *Ravina*, one could know of the *tumah* at the time and the *tzitz* would still be effective, as long as it was the *tumah* of the object and not the person.

In summary, the *gemara* yields two positions. *Ravina* focuses on the *act* of *zrika*: it must be performed without knowing the *dam* is *tamei* for the *tzitz* to work. *Rabbi Shila* focuses on the *origin* of the *tumah*: if the *tumah* arose inadvertently, the *tzitz* operates regardless of how the *zrika* was performed. Both agree that some degree of unintentionality is required — they disagree about where that unintentionality must be located.

## Tumat HaTehom Query

### Kohen vs. Owner

Having clarified the *tzitz*'s operation for *tamei* objects, the *gemara* turns to the domain in which the *tzitz* does work for *tumat haguf* — namely, *tumat hatehom*. *Rami bar Chama* poses the following question: when we say that *tumat hatehom* is permitted, that the *tzitz* renders the *korban* acceptable even for *tumat haguf* in cases of unknowable *tumah* — does this dispensation extend only to the *baalim*, the owners of the *korban Pesach* or the *nazir*, or does it extend as well to the *kohen* who performs the *avodah*?

The question has obvious practical significance. For most *korbanot*, it matters greatly whether the *kohen* performing the service is *tamei*. For the owner of a *pesach* or a *nazir*'s *korban*, the unique concern is that their personal *tumah* disqualifies them from having the *korban* brought at all. But is the *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* of *tumat hatehom* formulated around the *baalim* specifically — because they are the ones whose *tumah* uniquely prevents the *korban* — or is it formulated around the *korban* itself, such that any person involved, including the *kohen*, benefits from the dispensation?

Put differently: *mi amrinan ki gmeiri tumat hatehom b'baalim* — perhaps the tradition was transmitted specifically for the owners — *b'kohen lo gmeiri* — and we have no such tradition for the *kohen*? Or *dilma b'zivcha gmeiri* — the tradition was transmitted with reference to the offering itself, and therefore applies equally to the *kohen* and the *baalim*?

### Braita Proof

*Rava* attempts to resolve this question by citing the *beraita* of *Rabbi Chiya*, which states: *lo amru tumat hatehom ela l'meis bilvad* — the principle of *tumat hatehom* was said only with respect to a corpse, not to other forms of *tumah*. The *gemara* then asks: *le-mes l'mautei mai* — what is this exclusion designed to rule out? The most natural candidate is *tumat hatehom d'sheretz* — a case where a dead rodent was buried underground, unknowable to anyone, and someone inadvertently incurred its *tumah* in a way that could not have been known.

If such a case were to arise, *Rabbi Chiya*'s *beraita* tells us that the *tzitz* would *not* render it acceptable. But the *gemara* immediately asks: in what scenario would this exclusion even be necessary? For whom would it matter?

### Owner or Kohen

The *gemara* works through the possibilities. If the person who stepped on the buried rodent was the owner bringing his *pesach*, the question becomes: does being *tamei sheretz* even prevent one from bringing a *korban Pesach*? This depends on a dispute in the *gemara* elsewhere. According to the view that *ein shochtin v'zorkin al tmei sheretz* — that one may not perform the *shechita* and *zerikat hadam* while the owner is *tamei* even with a one-day *tumah* — the case is meaningful. The *chiddush* would be that even though the *tumah* was of the *tumat hatehom* variety, since it arose from a *sheretz* rather than a corpse, the *tzitz* does not save the *pesach*. But according to the view that *shochtin v'zorkin al tmei sheretz* — that one *may* bring the *korban Pesach* even while the owner has a one-day *tumah* — then the owner does not need the *tzitz* at all. Even if we *knew* he had touched a *sheretz*, the *pesach* would still be valid. *A fortiori*, if the *tumah* was unknowable, there would be no reason to speak of the *tzitz* at all.

Similarly, if the owner were a *nazir*, there would be no issue. A *nazir* is only concerned with *tumas meis* — *tumah* from a human corpse. Touching a *sheretz* has no bearing on his *nezirut* whatsoever: *ki hames mealoto amar Rachmana*. So the *nazir* case does not require the *tzitz* in a *sheretz* scenario either.

That leaves the *kohen* as the only person for whom the exclusion would be practically relevant. For every *korban*, the *kohen* performing the *avodah* must be *tahor*. If the *kohen* unknowingly touched a dead rodent buried in a manner consistent with *tumat hatehom* — a truly unknowable *tumah* — the *beraita* tells us the *tzitz* would not remedy this. But the fact that we need to *exclude* this case implies that for the *tumat hatehom* of a corpse, the *tzitz* would indeed work for the *kohen*. As the *gemara* puts it: *u-shma mina hutrah lo tumat hatehom* — we may infer from this that *tumat hatehom* operates to permit the *kohen* as well, not only the owner.

*Rav Yosef*, however, objects that this inference is not proven. The proof rests on two unstated assumptions: first, that the excluded case involves a *sheretz*; and second, that there is no scenario involving the *baalim* in which the *sheretz* exclusion would be necessary. But if the excluded case is something entirely different — namely, *tumat hatehom d'ziva*, the unknowable *tumah* of a *zav* or *zava* flow — then the *beraita*'s exclusion could still be referring entirely to the owners, and no inference about the *kohen* can be drawn. *Rav Yosef* therefore says: *le'olam b'baalim* — we are always talking about the owners — and the excluded case is not *sheretz* but *ziva*.

## Ziva & Unknowable Status

### Shomeres Yom k'Neged Yom

The abstraction of *tumat hatehom* beyond its literal meaning of "buried underground" is essential to understanding *Rav Yosef*'s counter-proposal. *Tumat hatehom* is not a topographical concept; it refers to any *tumah* whose status is genuinely unknowable at the time the *korban* is brought. And *Rav Yosef* proposes that it includes the *tumah* of a *zava* — a woman experiencing irregular uterine bleeding — in the following scenario.

A woman had a vaginal flow on the first day — let us say Monday. Under the laws of *ziva*, having bled once, she must observe the following day, Tuesday, without any further bleeding; this is the *shomeres yom k'neged yom*. If she does not bleed on Tuesday, she is *tehorah*. If Tuesday happens to be *Erev Pesach*, she arises in the morning, has not yet bled, and is currently *tehorah*. She brings her *korban Pesach*. But later on Tuesday she bleeds again.

At this point the question arises: was she *tamei* retroactively from the beginning of Tuesday, meaning she was *tamei* when her *korban Pesach* was brought? Or does the new bleeding only create *tumah* from the moment it begins? At the time of bringing the *korban*, her status was genuinely unknowable — it would only be determined by whether she bled later in the day. This is the *tumat hatehom* of *ziva*: not a buried corpse, but an objectively unknowable *tumah* at the time of the *korban*.

*Rav Yosef* says that this is what *Rabbi Chiya*'s *beraita* is excluding when it says *tumat hatehom* applies only to a corpse. It is excluding this case — *tumat hatehom d'ziva* — and saying the *tzitz* does not work for that. And since the excluded case is one involving the *baalim* of the *pesach* (not the *kohen*, who is entirely unaffected by *ziva* in this context), the *beraita* proves nothing about whether the *kohen* is included in *tumat hatehom* at all.

The *gemara* challenges this by citing *Rabbi Yosei*'s ruling in a *beraita*: a *shomeres yom k'neged yom* for whom the *korban Pesach* was slaughtered and sprinkled on her second day, and who subsequently bled — she may not eat the *korban Pesach* that evening (she is now *tamei*), but she is *patur* from the obligation to bring a *Pesach Sheini*. This suggests that the *korban* was retroactively acceptable. And the most natural explanation would be that the *tzitz* operated — because at the time of the *korban* it was a genuine *tumat hatehom*, and the *tzitz* rendered it acceptable.

### New vs. Retroactive Tumah

The *gemara*, however, pushes back: perhaps the reason *Rabbi Yosei* exempts her from *Pesach Sheini* is not because the *tzitz* worked, but because of a completely different principle regarding the nature of her *tumah*. Specifically, the *gemara* proposes that *Rabbi Yosei* holds *mikan u'l'haba hi mitameia* — her *tumah* operates only from this point forward, not retroactively.

The scenario unfolds as follows: the woman bled on Monday, immersed in a *mikvah* Tuesday morning, and began her day of watching in a state of *tahara*. The *korban Pesach* was brought during the daytime on Tuesday. Later on Tuesday, she began bleeding again. According to *Rabbi Yosei*, that new bleeding makes her *tamei* from that moment onward — it is a new *tumah*, not a continuation of Monday's. Therefore, when the *korban* was brought earlier in the day, she was genuinely *tehorah*, and the *pesach* was fully valid. The reason she cannot eat the meat that evening is simply that she has since become *tamei* again. And the reason she is exempt from *Pesach Sheini* is not that the *tzitz* covered over some retroactive *tumah*, but that there was no *tumah* at the time of the *korban* at all.

If this is *Rabbi Yosei*'s reasoning, then his ruling provides no proof that *tumat hatehom d'ziva* is a genuine category recognized by the *tzitz*. The *gemara* thus leaves the original question of *Rami bar Chama* — whether *tumat hatehom* extends to the *kohen* as well as the owners — unresolved, to be continued in the discussion on the following *daf*. The deeper question of what precisely constitutes *tumat hatehom* in the case of *ziva*, and whether the *tzitz* operates for such cases, remains the axis around which the *gemara* will continue to turn.
