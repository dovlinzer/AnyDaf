# Avodah Zarah 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sale/Rental Restrictions

### Trees & Produce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן אִילָן עַל מְנָת לָקוֹץ וְקוֹצֵץ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן אֶלָּא קְצוּצָה. שַׁחַת עַל מְנָת לִגְזוֹז וְגוֹזֵז, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לָהֶן אֶלָּא גְּזוּזָה. קָמָה עַל מְנָת לִקְצוֹר וְקוֹצֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹכְרִין אֶלָּא קְצוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One may sell to them a tree on the condition that he cut it down, and** the buyer **cuts it down;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says: One may sell to them only** a tree that has actually been **cut down. Similarly, one may sell to them fodder,** i.e., produce that has grown stalks but is not yet ripe, **on the condition to cut it down, and he cuts it down;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says: One may sell to them only** fodder that has been **cut down. So too, one may sell to them standing grain on the condition to harvest it, and he harvests it;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says: One may sell to them only harvested** grain.

The present daf picks up at the bottom of 20b, completing a *tana rabbanan* that addresses the core prohibition discussed in the preceding *mishna*: the restriction on selling items that are *mechubar lekarka* — attached to the ground — to non-Jews in the Land of Israel. This prohibition has obvious implications for contemporary debates about Jews selling land to non-Jews in Israel, though those debates must be understood in their proper context: the original prohibition was framed within the reality of a minority community struggling to maintain its foothold in the land, a context that differs substantially from present circumstances. The tradition, moreover, limits the scope of *matnat chinam* considerably — there is almost never a genuine case of *matnat chinam* — and allows for transactions rooted in *darchei shalom* and similar considerations. The preceding *gemara* had also addressed the question of *lo techonem* and when it does or does not apply. With those threads gathered, the *gemara* now returns to the *mishna*'s debate about selling something attached to the ground on the condition that the non-Jew immediately severs it.

### Yehuda vs. Meir

The *braita* records the dispute as follows. Rabbi Yehuda permits selling a tree (*ilan*) to a non-Jew on the condition that the purchaser chop it down — and the purchaser must then follow through and actually chop it down. Rabbi Meir is stricter: one may sell only what is already severed (*katzutz*). The same dispute repeats itself with *shachat* — the pre-grain stalks used for animal fodder — where Rabbi Yehuda permits selling it on the condition that the non-Jew cuts it himself (*vegozez*), while Rabbi Meir requires that it already be cut. And the same applies to *kama*, standing grain: Rabbi Yehuda permits the sale on condition of immediate harvest (*vekotzer*), while Rabbi Meir permits only what is already harvested.

### Three Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן אִילָן, בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא פָּסֵיד מְשַׁהֵי לֵיהּ, אֲבָל הַאי דְּכִי מְשַׁהֵי לֵהּ פָּסֵיד, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it is necessary** to state the *halakha* in each of these different scenarios. **As, had** the *baraita* **taught us** the dispute only with regard to **a tree,** I might have said that it is only **in that** case that **Rabbi Meir says** it must be cut down before being sold. The reason is that **since** the gentile **does not lose out** by keeping the tree in the soil, he might **keep it** in the ground. **But** in **this** case of standing grain, **since** if **he keeps it** in the ground **he will lose out,** one might **say** that Rabbi Meir **concedes to Rabbi Yehuda** that it may be sold before being harvested, on the condition that the gentile will harvest it, because the gentile would not leave the grain in the soil to spoil.

The *gemara* asks: why does the *braita* need all three cases? Surely one would suffice, and the others could be inferred. The *gemara* answers: *utzricha* — each case is independently necessary.

Had the *braita* taught only the case of the tree, one might have said that Rabbi Meir's stringency applies specifically there because leaving a tree standing involves no loss to the owner — it just stands there indefinitely — and therefore the non-Jew has every incentive to delay severing it, prolonging his attachment to the land. But in the case of *shachat*, where delay actually causes a loss — the stalks spoil and become unfit for fodder — perhaps Rabbi Meir would concede to Rabbi Yehuda that the non-Jew's own self-interest guarantees prompt severance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּהָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא יְדִיעַ שְׁבָחַיְיהוּ, אֲבָל שַׁחַת דִּידִיעַ שְׁבָחַיְיהוּ — אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the *baraita* **taught us** only **these two** *halakhot*, one might have said that Rabbi Yehuda permits selling these items on the condition that they be cut down **because the improvement** to the tree or grain **is not recognizable** when it is left in the ground. **But** in the case of **fodder, whose improvement is recognizable,** as it would continue to grow and ripen if left in the ground, one might **say** that Rabbi Yehuda **concedes to Rabbi Meir** that we are concerned that the gentile will not cut down the fodder, and therefore one may sell it only once it has been cut down.

Had the *braita* taught only the tree and the *kama* (standing grain), one might have concluded that Rabbi Yehuda permits in both those cases precisely because in neither is there a clear, obvious gain to the non-Jew from delay. The tree simply sits; the grain will rot. But *shachat*, where leaving it a while longer actually improves the yield, might be different — perhaps even Rabbi Yehuda would agree with Rabbi Meir that such a sale is impermissible, since the non-Jew has a positive incentive to drag out the arrangement. And conversely, had only the *shachat* been taught, one would not know whether Rabbi Meir's ruling there (where there is a tangible gain from delay) also extends to cases where there is no such gain. All three cases are therefore needed.

### Animals Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּהֵמָה עַל מְנָת לִשְׁחוֹט, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to the sale of large livestock, which is forbidden due to the concern that the gentile might use them to perform labor (see 14b), if such **livestock** are sold **on the condition that** the gentile will **slaughter** them, **what is** the *halakha*?

The *gemara* then raises a question: does Rabbi Yehuda's leniency — permitting a conditional sale before severance — extend analogously to the sale of an animal (*behema*) on condition of immediate slaughter? This question relates back to an earlier *mishna* (14b) about the prohibition on selling large or small livestock to non-Jews. The crux of the question is whether the rationale underlying Rabbi Yehuda's leniency in the land-connected cases applies equally here.

One might argue that Rabbi Yehuda's leniency by the tree and grain rests on the fact that the land itself remains in Jewish possession — the seller retains practical control and can enforce the condition. But once an animal is sold and passes into the non-Jew's domain (*reshus*), the seller loses any ability to ensure that the stipulation is honored. Perhaps Rabbi Yehuda would therefore agree with Rabbi Meir that a conditional sale of an animal is impermissible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: בְּהֵמָה עַל מְנָת לִשְׁחוֹט וְשׁוֹחֵט, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹכְרִין לוֹ אֶלָּא שְׁחוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a resolution: **Come** and **hear, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One may sell large **livestock** to a gentile **on the condition that** he **slaughter** it, **and he slaughters** it; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Meir says: One may sell to** a gentile **only a slaughtered** animal.

The *gemara* resolves the question with a citation: a *braita* teaches explicitly that Rabbi Yehuda permits selling an animal on condition of immediate slaughter, and the non-Jew may perform the *shechita* himself. Rabbi Meir, consistently, permits sale only of an already-slaughtered animal. The *gemara*'s concern was well-founded — one certainly cannot control what happens once the animal enters the non-Jew's possession — yet Rabbi Yehuda's more permissive general approach to these prohibitions holds even here.

## Geographic Scope

### Israel Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מַשְׂכִּירִין לָהֶם בָּתִּים בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר שָׂדוֹת, וּבְסוּרְיָא מַשְׂכִּירִין לָהֶם בָּתִּים אֲבָל לֹא שָׂדוֹת, וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ מוֹכְרִין לָהֶם בָּתִּים וּמַשְׂכִּירִין שָׂדוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מַשְׂכִּירִין לָהֶם בָּתִּים אֲבָל לֹא שָׂדוֹת, וּבְסוּרְיָא מוֹכְרִין בָּתִּים וּמַשְׂכִּירִין שָׂדוֹת, וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ מוֹכְרִין אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not rent a house to** a gentile **in Eretz Yisrael, and needless to say** one may not rent **fields** to them, as explained in the Gemara. **And in Syria one may rent houses to** gentiles, **but** one may **not** rent **fields. And outside of Eretz** Yisrael **one may sell houses and rent fields to** gentiles; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yosei says: In Eretz Yisrael one may rent houses to** gentiles **but** one may **not** rent **fields. And in Syria one may sell houses to them and rent fields, and outside of Eretz** Yisrael **one may sell** both **these,** houses, **and those,** fields.

The *mishna* now makes explicit what was implicit in the earlier material: not only may one not sell land-connected items to non-Jews in the Land of Israel, one may not even rent homes to them. The *mishna* states: *ein maskirin lahem batim b'eretz yisrael* — one does not rent homes to them in the Land of Israel — and this is *ve-ein tzarich lomar sadot* — it goes without saying that fields are even more restricted.

The underlying prohibition is *lo techonem* in the sense of *chaniya bekarka* — granting them a foothold in the land. However, the *gemara* understands that rental alone does not rise to the level of this biblical violation; true *chaniya bekarka* requires an outright sale. Rental is prohibited by rabbinic enactment as a safeguard. One can trace the logic to the episode of Avraham purchasing the *me'arat ha-machpela* from the *Bnei Chet* — the entire elaborate transaction was necessary precisely because acquiring a burial plot signals genuine permanence and rootedness in the land. Rental carries no such permanence, and therefore the biblical prohibition does not apply to it directly.

### Fields vs. Homes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״אֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר שָׂדוֹת״? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִית בַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי: חֲדָא, חֲנָיַית קַרְקַע; וַחֲדָא, דְּקָא מַפְקַע לַהּ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the meaning of the mishna's statement: **Needless to say** one may not allow gentiles to rent **fields?** Why is the *halakha* with regard to fields more obvious than the *halakha* of houses? **If we say** that it is **because** allowing a gentile to rent a field **entails two** problems, **one** of which is aiding gentiles in **encamping** in the **land, and** the other **one** is **that** doing so **releases** the land **from** the mitzva of separating **tithe,** this cannot be correct.

Why are fields more restricted than homes? The *gemara* identifies two distinct issues that attach to a field. The first is *chaniyat karka* — the presence and dwelling concern discussed above. The second is that renting a field to a non-Jew exempts the produce grown there from *terumos u-ma'asros*: once a non-Jew owns or effectively controls the land in the Land of Israel, the grain that grows there loses its obligatory status in tithes.

The *Yerushalmi* frames the distinction somewhat differently, in terms of *yispared* — the field is the source of livelihood and prosperity in an agricultural society, whereas the home is merely where one lives. A field therefore represents a more substantial and weighty presence in the land than a home, and restrictions around fields are correspondingly more severe. Whichever framing one adopts, the asymmetry is clear: a field carries a double weight of concern that a home does not.

### Syria Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּסוּרְיָא מַשְׂכִּירִין בָּתִּים כּוּ׳. מַאי שְׁנָא מְכִירָה דְּלָא? מִשּׁוּם מְכִירָה דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל. אִי הָכִי, מִשְּׂכִירוּת נָמֵי נִגְזוֹר! הִיא גּוּפַהּ גְּזֵרָה, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִיגְזוֹר גְּזֵרָה לִגְזֵרָה?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **In Syria one may rent** houses but not fields to gentiles. The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **selling** houses **that** it is **not** permitted to sell houses in Syria? The Gemara answers that it is prohibited **due to** a concern that this will ultimately result in the **selling** of houses **in Eretz Yisrael.** The Gemara challenges: **If that is so, let us also issue a decree** prohibiting **renting** houses to gentiles in Syria, lest it lead to renting to gentiles in Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara explains: The prohibition against renting houses to gentiles in Eretz Yisrael **is itself** a rabbinic **decree** lest one come to sell the houses, **and shall we arise and issue** one **decree to** prevent the violation of another **decree?**

*Surya* — Syria, or the territory conquered by King David beyond the borders of *Eretz Yisrael* proper — occupies an intermediate halakhic status. The *mishna* reflects this: in *Surya*, one may rent homes to non-Jews but may not rent fields.

The *gemara* asks: why can one not sell homes in *Surya*? If the concern is merely rabbinic — a safeguard against conducting such transactions in *Eretz Yisrael* proper — then even renting homes in *Surya* should be prohibited as a further safeguard. The *gemara* responds: *hi gufa gezeira* — renting in *Eretz Yisrael* is itself already a rabbinic safeguard (the real prohibition being sale). To then prohibit renting in *Surya* as a safeguard to that safeguard would be *gezeira ligezeira* — which the rabbis generally do not impose.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא שְׂכִירוּת שָׂדֶה דִּבְסוּרְיָא, דִּגְזֵרָה לִגְזֵרָה הִיא, וְקָא גָזְרִינַן! הָתָם לָאו גְּזֵרָה הוּא, קָסָבַר: כִּיבּוּשׁ יָחִיד שְׁמֵיהּ כִּיבּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** the prohibition against **renting a field that is in Syria is** also **a decree** whose purpose is **to** prevent the violation of another **decree,** as the prohibition against allowing a gentile to rent one's field in Eretz Yisrael is a rabbinic decree, **and** yet **we** still **issue the decree.** The Gemara explains: According to Rabbi Meir, **there,** with regard to selling houses and fields in Syria to gentiles, the prohibition **is not** merely **a decree** intended to prevent the violation of the decree with regard to Eretz Yisrael. Rather, Rabbi Meir **holds** that the **conquest of an individual is called a conquest.** Once Syria was conquered by King David, who is considered an individual in this regard, the sanctity of Eretz Yisrael applied to it.

But this answer raises a further difficulty: renting a field in *Surya* is also prohibited by Rabbi Meir. If renting a field even in *Eretz Yisrael* is only a rabbinic safeguard against selling, then prohibiting the rental of a field in *Surya* would seem to be *gezeira ligezeira* as well. The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that *Surya* is not treated as one degree removed from *Eretz Yisrael* in the first place — it is treated as functionally equivalent to *Eretz Yisrael*. The *tanna* who authored this position — Rabbi Meir — holds *kibbush yachid shmei kibbush*, that David's individual conquest of *Surya* constitutes a genuine annexation. Therefore *Surya* is halakhically part of the Land of Israel. Prohibiting the rental of a field in *Surya* is thus not a *gezeira ligezeira* at all — it is a straightforward prohibition applying within what is, in effect, *Eretz Yisrael*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׂדֶה דְּאִית בֵּיהּ תַּרְתֵּי — גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן, בָּתִּים דְּלֵית בְּהוּ תַּרְתֵּי — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **concerning a field, which has two** problems, as one releases the land from the mitzva of separating tithes and aids gentiles in acquiring land in Eretz Yisrael, **the Sages issued a decree** as a preventative measure, prohibiting the renting of fields just as in Eretz Yisrael. But **concerning houses, which do not have two** problems, **the Sages did not issue** such **a decree.**

Yet since *Surya* is not *Eretz Yisrael* proper, the rabbis are more lenient with some of the ancillary safeguards. Fields in *Surya* carry the double weight (*tartei*) — the *chaniyat karka* concern and the exemption from *terumos u-ma'asros* — so the rabbis maintain a safeguard there. But homes, which carry only a single concern, are not subject to the same rabbinic stringency in *Surya*, and renting homes is therefore permitted.

### Outside Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ וְכוּ׳. שָׂדֶה דְּאִית בֵּיהּ תַּרְתֵּי — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן, בָּתִּים דְּלֵית בְּהוּ תַּרְתֵּי — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that according to Rabbi Meir, **outside of Eretz** Yisrael one may sell houses and rent fields, but one may not sell fields to gentiles. The Gemara explains: **Concerning a field, which has two** problems when it is in Eretz Yisrael, **the Sages issued a decree** preventing its sale even outside of Eretz Yisrael. **Concerning houses, which do not have two** problems, **the Sages did not issue a decree** prohibiting their sale.

In *chutz la'aretz* — fully outside the Land of Israel — the *mishna* permits even selling homes and renting fields. The logic follows the same graduated structure: homes, which carry only one concern, receive no rabbinic restriction at all outside Israel, and one may sell them outright. Fields, which always carry the heavier double concern, remain subject to a rabbinic safeguard even in *chutz la'aretz* — one may rent but not sell them. The *gemara* explains this restriction as a *gezeira*: if we permit selling fields outside of Israel, we might come to permit selling fields in the Land of Israel itself. This may strike one as somewhat far-fetched — the fields of Babylonia seem quite remote from the fields of *Eretz Yisrael* — but the safeguard is maintained nonetheless, perhaps limited in its rationale to cases where one owns fields in both places simultaneously, though the *halacha* is not formally restricted to such cases.

### Neighborhood Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂנָּה שְׁכוּנָה. וְכַמָּה שְׁכוּנָה? תָּנָא: אֵין שְׁכוּנָה פְּחוּתָה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה בְּנֵי אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** In conclusion, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** that it is permitted to sell houses outside of Eretz Yisrael to gentiles. **Rav Yosef says: And** this is the *halakha* **provided that one does not make it** into a gentile **settlement. And how many** people constitute **a settlement?** The Sage **taught:** There is **no settlement** that consists of **fewer than three people.**

Even in *chutz la'aretz*, where transactions are broadly permitted, Rav Yosef adds an important qualification: *u-vilvad shelo ya'asenu shechunah* — provided one does not turn the area into a non-Jewish neighborhood. A *shechunah* begins when there are at least three households. One therefore may not sell to enough non-Jews that a distinct non-Jewish enclave forms within the Jewish neighborhood. The concern here is not merely formal halakhic — it reflects the broader communal interest in maintaining the Jewish character of residential areas.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא אָזֵיל הַאי יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמְזַבֵּין לְחַד גּוֹי, וְאָזֵיל הַאיְךְ וּמְזַבֵּין לַהּ לִתְרֵי! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אַ״לִּפְנֵי״ מִפַּקְּדִינַן, אַ״לִּפְנֵי״ דְּ״לִפְנֵי״ לָא מִפַּקְּדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let us be concerned** that **perhaps this Jew will go and sell to one gentile, and the other** owners of the adjacent houses **will go and sell to two** other gentiles, resulting in a gentile settlement. **Abaye said: We are commanded about** placing a stumbling block **before** the blind (see Leviticus 19:14), but **we are not commanded about** placing a stumbling block **before** someone who may place it **before** the blind. In other words, this prohibition applies only when one causes another to sin by his direct action, not in a situation such as this, where the prohibition is two stages removed from the Jew's action.

The *gemara* raises a practical concern: what if a Jew sells to one non-Jew, and that non-Jew subdivides his home and sells portions to two more families, creating three non-Jewish households without the original Jewish seller having directly sold to three? Abaye responds that the prohibition extends only to one's direct act — *a-lifnei mifakdinan, a-lifnei dilifnei lo mifakdinan* — we are enjoined against the direct enabling but not against the indirect consequences two steps removed.

## Exceptions

### Domicile Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁאָמְרוּ לְהַשְׂכִּיר, לֹא לְבֵית דִּירָה אָמְרוּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַכְנִיס לְתוֹכוֹ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תָבִיא תוֹעֵבָה אֶל בֵּיתֶךָ״, וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם לֹא יַשְׂכִּיר לוֹ אֶת הַמֶּרְחָץ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נִקְרָא עַל שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Even in a place** with regard to **which** the Sages **said** that it is permitted for a Jew **to rent** a house to a gentile, **they did not say** that one may rent it **for** use as **a residence, because** the gentile will **bring** objects of **idol worship into it, as it is stated: "You shall not bring an abomination into your house"** (Deuteronomy 7:26), and this is still considered the house of a Jew. **And** for the same reason, **in every place, one may not rent a bathhouse to** a gentile, **since it is called by the name of** the owner, and onlookers will think that the Jew is operating it on Shabbat.

Even in places where renting is permitted — whether to Rabbi Yossi's leniency allowing rental in *Eretz Yisrael* itself, or Rabbi Meir's allowance in *Surya*, or the broader permissions in *chutz la'aretz* — the *mishna* qualifies: *lo l'veit dirah amru*. The permission to rent does not extend to renting as a domicile. One may rent for a storehouse or other non-residential purpose, but not as a place of actual dwelling. The reason: *mipnei shemachnis l'tocho avodah zarah* — because the non-Jew will bring his idols into the home. The verse invoked is *lo tavi to'evah el beitecha* — do not bring an abomination into your house. Since a rented property is still halakhically considered the owner's house — as the *gemara* demonstrated earlier in the tractate when discussing the rental of animals on *Shabbat* — the non-Jew's worship within it constitutes a violation of this verse.

### Bathhouse Ban

The *mishna* adds a further restriction: in every location — even *chutz la'aretz* — one may not rent a bathhouse to a non-Jew. The reason is that the bathhouse continues to be associated with its Jewish owner by name, and when the non-Jew operates it on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, the public perceives it as the Jewish owner's establishment running on *Shabbat*. Unlike the *beit dira* concern, which is rooted in the prohibition of *lo tavi to'evah el beitecha*, the bathhouse concern is distinctly a *Shabbat* violation concern — elaborated upon in the *Tosefta* and in the *gemara*'s subsequent analysis. These two exceptions thus rest on categorically different foundations, a distinction that will be important in the *gemara*'s discussion.

## Field/Home Analysis

### Two-Issue Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״אֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר שָׂדוֹת״? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דְּאִית בַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי: חֲדָא, חֲנָיַית קַרְקַע; וַחֲדָא, דְּקָא מַפְקַע לַהּ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the meaning of the mishna's statement: **Needless to say** one may not allow gentiles to rent **fields?** Why is the *halakha* with regard to fields more obvious than the *halakha* of houses? **If we say** that it is **because** allowing a gentile to rent a field **entails two** problems, **one** of which is aiding gentiles in **encamping** in the **land, and** the other **one** is **that** doing so **releases** the land **from** the mitzva of separating **tithe,** this cannot be correct.

The *gemara* now examines the *mishna*'s statement that fields are more restricted than homes. The question is: *mai itztarich lemeimar sadot* — why is it obvious that fields are more restricted? The answer lies in the two-issue framework already introduced: *chada haniyat karka* — the presence-in-the-land concern — and *chada dekamfaka lah mi-ma'aser* — the field's exemption from *terumos u-ma'asros* once it passes to non-Jewish control. Fields carry both concerns; homes carry only one.

### Mezuzah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בָּתִּים נָמֵי אִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי: חֲדָא, חֲנָיַית קַרְקַע; וַחֲדָא, דְּקָא מַפְקַע לַהּ מִמְּזוּזָה! אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: מְזוּזָה חוֹבַת הַדָּר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why that cannot be the reason: **If that is so,** the same can be said about the prohibition against renting **houses,** as it **also involves two** problems: **One** is aiding gentiles in **encamping** in the **land, and** the other **one** is **that** it **releases** the house **from** the mitzva of ***mezuza*. Rav Mesharshiyya says** in response: Affixing **a *mezuza* is the obligation of the resident,** rather than an obligation that applies to the house. Therefore, if no Jew lives in a house, it is not subject to the mitzva of *mezuza*. This means that by renting a house to a gentile one is not removing the right to perform the mitzva from the house.

The *gemara* presses the point: homes too should have a second concern alongside *chaniyat karka*. Just as renting a field to a non-Jew removes it from the obligation of *terumos u-ma'asros*, renting a home to a non-Jew removes it from the obligation of *mezuzah*. Why then is a home treated as having only one issue? The *gemara* answers through Rav Mesharshia: *mezuzah chovas hadar hi* — the *mezuzah* is the obligation of the one who dwells in the home, not a status intrinsic to the land or structure.

### Tosafot Mezuzah

Tosafot elaborates this distinction with care. By the field, we speak of *kedushat eretz yisrael* — the land itself carries a halakhic status that renders its produce obligated in *terumos u-ma'asros*. When a non-Jew acquires that land, the land's status changes, and even a Jew who subsequently rents it and plants crops on it will find those crops exempt. This is the subject of the debate about *yesh kinyan l'akum l'hafki'ah mi-terumah u-ma'aser*, and this *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that non-Jewish ownership does indeed exempt the produce.

By the house, the situation is structurally different. We do not say that the house is obligated in *mezuzah* — we say that the person who lives in his own home is obligated. If one owns a house and is not living in it, there is no obligation to affix a *mezuzah*. More precisely, according to Tosafot, if one owns a house but rents it to another Jew, the owner is technically not obligated *mid'oraita* in *mezuzah* — because the obligation derives from *beitecha*, your house, and it is not the renter's house. The renter has a rabbinic obligation as the one dwelling there, but not a full biblical one. The non-Jew's entry into the home therefore does not change a status that inhered in the structure itself — there was no independent *mezuzah* status attached to the building to be lost. This is the fundamental asymmetry: fields have an intrinsic land-based sanctity that changes upon non-Jewish ownership; homes do not.

This distinction also bears on the practical question of *mezuzah* obligations in contemporary contexts. A rented apartment, according to Tosafot's framework, carries a rabbinic but not a biblical obligation. And the placement of *mezuzot* on commercial premises — retail stores, office buildings — where no one actually dwells, is without halakhic compulsion, whatever other reasons may motivate the practice.

## Kibbutz Yachid

### Syria Safeguard

Returning to the structure of Rabbi Meir's position, the *gemara* works through why *Surya* follows the rules it does. As established above, Rabbi Meir holds that *Surya* has the same halakhic status as *Eretz Yisrael* proper by virtue of *kibbush yachid shmei kibbush* — David's individual conquest counts as a genuine annexation. This means that selling a home in *Surya* is, in principle, a biblical violation of *lo techonem*, just as in *Eretz Yisrael* proper. Yet the rabbis chose not to impose a safeguard against renting homes in *Surya*, even though renting homes in *Eretz Yisrael* itself is prohibited as a safeguard against selling.

The *gemara*'s reasoning is that one cannot impose a *gezeira ligezeira* — a safeguard to a safeguard. Renting in *Eretz Yisrael* is itself already a rabbinic fence around the biblical prohibition of selling. To then prohibit renting in *Surya* as a further fence would be building a fence upon a fence, which the rabbis decline to do. Fields, however, are treated differently: since they carry the double concern, the rabbinic safeguard is maintained for them even in *Surya*.

There is an interesting subtlety here. If *Surya* were truly halakhically identical to *Eretz Yisrael* in every respect — if it were simply *lo techonem* applied equally — then any distinction between the two would be inexplicable. The *gemara*'s graduated treatment suggests that something beyond the pure *lo techonem* calculus is operating: the practical, communal, and quasi-political concern about where Jewish settlement most needs to be protected. *Eretz Yisrael* proper is treated with maximum stringency; *Surya*, even granting its biblical equivalence, receives somewhat more lenient rabbinic safeguards; and *chutz la'aretz* is treated most leniently of all.

### David's Conquest

The concept of *kibbush yachid* — individual conquest — requires explanation. Rashi understands it as follows: David waged his wars without the authority of the *Sanhedrin* and without the participation of *shishim ribo*, the six hundred thousand who represent *Bnei Yisrael* collectively. The question is whether a king's conquest is his private military achievement or whether it constitutes a conquest in the name of the entire Jewish people. If the land was not conquered as a national enterprise — if David was acting on his own initiative rather than as the representative of the collective — then that land does not become the patrimony of the Jewish people. It is land conquered by a king of Israel, not land conquered by the people of Israel.

Tosafot cites the *Sifrei* and offers a different framework. According to Tosafot, the problem with David's conquest of *Surya* was that he undertook it before *Eretz Yisrael* proper had been fully conquered. The conceptual structure of extending *kedushah* beyond the original borders requires that the base first be complete. If the territorial foundation of *Eretz Yisrael* has not yet been fully secured and settled, one cannot legitimately extend beyond it. There is something inherently problematic — from a priorities standpoint — in expanding outward while the core is not yet consolidated. David's preoccupation with these further territories, before *Eretz Yisrael* proper was fully in hand, renders the conquest structurally defective, and the *kedushah* of *Eretz Yisrael* cannot be extended upon it. Both Rashi and Tosafot agree on the bottom line — *kibbush yachid* does not establish full *kedushah* — but their conceptual accounts of *why* differ substantially and illuminate different dimensions of what makes a conquest nationally valid.

## Rabbi Yossi Lenient

### Yossi in Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מַשְׂכִּירִין לָהֶם בָּתִּים וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? שָׂדוֹת דְּאִית בְּהוּ תַּרְתֵּי — גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן, בָּתִּים דְּלֵית בְּהוּ תַּרְתֵּי — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that **Rabbi Yosei says: In Eretz Yisrael one may rent houses** but not fields **to** gentiles. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** that one may rent houses but not fields? **Concerning fields, which have two** problems, **the Sages issued a decree** as a preventive measure prohibiting the renting of fields in Eretz Yisrael. But **concerning houses, which do not have two** problems, **the Sages did not issue a decree** prohibiting renting houses to gentiles.

The *gemara* now considers the opposing position, that of Rabbi Yossi, who rules more leniently at every level. The relevant ruling is recorded: *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel halacha ke-Rabbi Yose* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yossi.

In *Eretz Yisrael* itself, Rabbi Yossi permits renting homes. His reasoning follows the same internal logic as Rabbi Meir applied by *Surya*: homes carry only one concern (*chaniyat karka*), while fields carry two. Where there is only one concern, the rabbis did not impose a safeguard. Fields, carrying two concerns, remain restricted — one may not rent a field even in *Eretz Yisrael*. There may also be a pragmatic dimension to Rabbi Yossi's leniency: the economic realities of a community mean that restricting the rental of homes is simply not sustainable, and perhaps one cannot require people to bear that burden.

### Yossi in Syria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְסוּרְיָא מוֹכְרִין וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא? קָסָבַר: כִּיבּוּשׁ יָחִיד לָא שְׁמֵיהּ כִּיבּוּשׁ, וְשָׂדֶה דְּאִית בַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי — גְּזַרוּ בַּהּ רַבָּנַן, בָּתִּים דְּלֵית בְּהוּ תַּרְתֵּי — לָא גְּזַרוּ בְּהוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that Rabbi Yosei rules: **And in Syria one may sell** houses and rent fields to gentiles, but one may not sell fields. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that one may sell houses but not fields? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yosei **holds** that the **conquest of an individual is not called a conquest,** and therefore there is no prohibition by Torah law against selling houses in Syria. **And concerning a field, which has two** problems, **the Sages issued a decree** as a preventive measure prohibiting the sale of fields in Syria. **Concerning houses, which do not have two** problems, **the Sages did not issue a decree** prohibiting their sale.

Rabbi Yossi's position on *Surya* diverges sharply from Rabbi Meir's, and this divergence reveals the deeper doctrinal disagreement between them. Rabbi Yossi permits selling homes in *Surya* outright. This is possible only if *Surya* is not treated as *Eretz Yisrael* — because if it were, selling a home there would be a biblical violation. Rabbi Yossi therefore holds *kibbush yachid lo shmei kibbush*: David's individual conquest does not constitute a valid annexation, and *Surya* does not carry the halakhic status of *Eretz Yisrael*.

Since *Surya* is not *Eretz Yisrael*, the biblical prohibition of *lo techonem* does not apply there. The rabbis impose safeguards nonetheless. Fields — carrying the double concern — may be rented but not sold, as a rabbinic safeguard against selling fields in *Eretz Yisrael* itself. Homes — carrying only one concern — are fully permitted, both for sale and rental. The rabbis do not feel that permitting the sale of homes in *Surya* will create a realistic risk of selling homes in *Eretz Yisrael*, and so no safeguard is imposed. In *chutz la'aretz*, Rabbi Yossi permits everything: *keivan demirchak lo gazrinan* — since the distance is great, we do not fear any slippage toward prohibited conduct in the Land of Israel itself.

## Exceptions (II)

### Tosafot Idols

The *mishna*'s exception prohibiting rental as a domicile — on the grounds that the non-Jew will bring his idols into the home — raised a pressing practical question for Tosafot: on what basis did Jews of their era rent and sell homes to non-Jews, including as places of actual residence? The *Tosefta* states that in both *Eretz Yisrael* and *Surya*, one should not rent a home to a non-Jew because it is known that they bring their idol into it — yet then qualifies that one may rent them barns, storehouses, and inns even so. Tosafot grapples with how to reconcile this with actual practice.

One reading of the *Tosefta* is that the distinguishing principle is permanence: when an idol is kept in the home on a fixed, permanent basis (*bikvi'ut*), the prohibition of *lo tavi to'evah el beitecha* applies. But when an idol is brought in only temporarily — as when a traveler stays at an inn — this does not constitute the forbidden "bringing an abomination into your house." On this reading, Tosafot suggests that the non-Jews of their time did not actually maintain idols in their homes on a permanent basis. They might bring in an icon when someone was dying or already dead, but even then they did not perform an act of actual worship; the statue was present for protective or ritualistic reasons, not as an object of formal veneration. Under these circumstances, the domicile restriction would not apply.

Tosafot then considers whether the *Tosefta*'s distinction might instead hinge on the word *beitecha* — "your house" — understood as a place where you yourself dwell. Inns, barns, and storehouses are not *beitecha* in that sense, and the prohibition therefore does not extend to them. But this interpretation offers no relief for the actual practice of renting residential homes, which would still seem to be prohibited.

Tosafot then cites Rabbeinu Chaim Kohen, who brings a proof from the *Yerushalmi*: the implication of the ruling itself — that even where renting is permitted, it is not permitted for a domicile — suggests that in places where selling is permitted, one may sell even for a domicile. One reading of this is that *beitecha* refers only to your genuine home, which is located in *Eretz Yisrael* — a house in *chutz la'aretz* is never truly *beitecha*. Tosafot finds this position difficult: does it mean there is no *mezuzah* obligation in *chutz la'aretz* because it is never truly *beitecha*? Ultimately, Tosafot's final resolution leans on the point that *beitecha* with its full biblical force applies only when one is actually living in the house. When the owner rents out the home and vacates, the biblical prohibition may not apply — it becomes only a rabbinic extension — and in that space the rabbis permitted more latitude, particularly in *chutz la'aretz*. As throughout this tractate, the tension between treating Christianity as *avodah zarah* and the practical realities of daily commerce in Christian Europe persisted well into the era of the *Shulchan Aruch* and the *Rama*, with various authorities proposing that perhaps Christians' idolatrous practice carries different weight for non-Jews than for Jews, or that their attachment to their icons is not sufficiently sincere to trigger these restrictions — all ultimately attempts to accommodate lived reality within the halakhic framework.

## Kibbutz Yachid (II)

### Rashi/Tosafot

The debate between Rashi and Tosafot on *kibbush yachid* repays further attention. For Rashi, the issue is one of representational legitimacy: did the king act in his own name or in the name of the nation? A conquest carried out without the *Sanhedrin*'s authorization and without the participation of *shishim ribo* — the full complement that embodies the national will — is a king's private military venture, not a national act. Since the Jewish people did not collectively conquer *Surya*, they have no collective claim to it, and it cannot carry the sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael* which was conquered and settled as a national inheritance.

For Tosafot, the issue is sequential rather than representational: *Eretz Yisrael* proper had not yet been fully conquered and settled when David turned his attention to *Surya*. The conceptual model is one of concentric completion — you must finish the core before the extension can take hold. The *kedushah* of *Eretz Yisrael* cannot spread outward until it has fully saturated the territory God designated as the Jewish homeland. David's premature expansion therefore lacks the foundation upon which *kedushah* can be built, and *Surya* consequently cannot participate in the sanctity of the Land. There is almost a rebuke embedded in Tosafot's reading: the question arises why David was concerning himself with these external territories at all when *Eretz Yisrael* itself had not yet been fully consolidated. That misordering of priorities itself disqualifies the conquest from generating genuine territorial *kedushah*.

## Bathhouse/Field

### Shimon ben Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם לֹא יִשְׂכּוֹר וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא: רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא יִשְׂכּוֹר אָדָם מֶרְחָצוֹ לְגוֹי, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּקְרָא עַל שְׁמוֹ, וְגוֹי זֶה עוֹשֶׂה בּוֹ מְלָאכָה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וּבְיָמִים טוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And in every place, one may not rent** a bathhouse to a gentile, as it is called by the name of the owner. The Gemara notes that **it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A person may not rent his bathhouse to a gentile, because it is called by the name of** the owner, **and this gentile uses it for performing** prohibited **labor on *Shabbatot* and on Festivals.**

A *braita* records the position of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: one may not rent one's bathhouse (*merchatz*) to a non-Jew, because the bathhouse continues to be identified by the owner's name, and the non-Jew will perform labor there on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*. The *gemara* notes that the *braita* is formulated as a prohibition specifically with respect to a non-Jew (*oved kochavim*), implying that renting to a *Kuti* (Samaritan) would be permissible, since a *Kuti* would observe *Shabbat*. The *gemara* then asks whether one need be concerned that the *Kuti* will operate the bathhouse on *chol ha-mo'ed*, since *Kutim* do not recognize the *issur melacha* of *chol ha-mo'ed*. The *gemara* dismisses this concern: we ourselves (*anan nami avdinan*) are permitted to heat water for bathing on *chol ha-mo'ed*, so there is nothing problematic about the *Kuti* doing so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לְכוּתִי, מַאי? שְׁרֵי. כּוּתִי אֵימַר עָבֵיד בֵּיהּ מְלָאכָה בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד! בְּחוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד אֲנַן נָמֵי עָבְדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: It is prohibited to rent one's bathhouse to a gentile, **but** with regard **to a Samaritan, what** is the *halakha*? It can be inferred from the lack of mention of a Samaritan that it is **permitted.** The Gemara asks: Why not **say** that **a Samaritan** will **perform work in** the bathhouse **during the intermediate days of a Festival?** The Gemara answers: **During the intermediate days of a Festival we too perform work** and heat bathhouses.

### Sharecropping Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל שָׂדֵהוּ לְגוֹי, מַאי? שְׁרֵי. מַאי טַעְמָא? אֲרִיסָא אֲרִיסוּתֵיהּ קָעָבֵיד. מֶרְחָץ נָמֵי אָמְרִי: אֲרִיסָא אֲרִיסוּתֵיהּ קָעָבֵיד? אֲרִיסָא דְּמֶרְחָץ לָא עָבְדִי אַנְשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further infers: It is prohibited to rent one's bathhouse to a gentile; **but** with regard to renting **one's field to a gentile, what** is the *halakha*? Evidently, it is **permitted. What is the reason** for this? People know that a gentile **sharecropper works for his tenancy,** not for the Jewish owner. The Gemara asks: But if so, in the case of **a bathhouse as well,** why not **say** that the gentile **sharecropper works for his tenancy,** and therefore a Jew should be permitted to rent them to gentiles. The Gemara answers: **People do not** usually **make** this type of arrangement whereby a second party works as **a sharecropper of a bathhouse.** Consequently, it will be assumed that the gentile is a hired worker who is working for the Jew.

A more substantial question follows: if renting a bathhouse is prohibited because the non-Jew's labor on *Shabbat* is associated with the Jewish owner's name, why is renting a field permitted? When a non-Jew is seen working a Jewish-owned field on *Shabbat*, surely the same concern applies — people will assume the Jewish owner directed him to work on *Shabbat*. The *gemara* answers: *arisuta arisuteih ka-avid* — people understand that work in a field is typically done under a sharecropping (*arisus*) arrangement. Sharecropping is sufficiently common in agricultural settings that observers naturally assume the non-Jew working the field on *Shabbat* is a *arisa* — a sharecropper — who works his own schedule, makes his own decisions, and keeps a portion of the harvest. The Jewish landowner's role is that of a passive beneficiary, not an employer directing the worker's Shabbat activity. The critical interpretive lens through which people see the situation is therefore the correct one.

### No Sharecropping

If the field's permissibility rests on the *arisus* presumption, the *gemara* asks, why not apply the same logic to the bathhouse? One could argue that the non-Jew has rented the bathhouse or entered a profit-sharing arrangement, and observers should assume accordingly. The *gemara* responds: *arisuta de-merchatz lo avdi inshi* — profit-sharing arrangements are simply not common for bathhouses. A bathhouse is a highly profitable enterprise, and owners do not typically give up those profits to a *arisa*-style partner. Consequently, there is no established cultural expectation through which observers will naturally read the non-Jew's operation of the bathhouse as anything other than employment by the Jewish owner. When the bathhouse runs on *Shabbat*, people will simply assume that Dov is running his business on *Shabbat*, collecting the fees, and having his workers heat the water — which presents the public appearance of a Jewish enterprise openly operating on *Shabbat*. This is more than a concern about suspecting the owner of a technical *issur de-rabbanan*; it violates the communal experience of *Shabbat* as a whole, analogous to *mechalel Shabbat be-farhesya*.

## Tosafot Framework

### Property Ownership

This *gemara* opens into one of the most practically significant discussions in the tractate for post-Talmudic communities. Tosafot here constructs a comprehensive framework for distinguishing permitted from prohibited arrangements involving non-Jewish labor on *Shabbat*. The *gemara*'s ruling about the bathhouse versus the field raises an obvious question: what about all the other contexts in which non-Jews perform labor on *Shabbat* in association with Jewish-owned enterprises or property — most prominently, the building of a Jewish-owned house?

The foundational distinction Tosafot draws is between work performed on the non-Jew's own property or in his own place of business versus work performed on the Jewish owner's property. When the non-Jew takes the job back to his own workshop — as in the paradigmatic case of *Beit Hillel* permitting one to drop off laundry before *Shabbat*, with the laundryman then working it on his own schedule at his own establishment — there is no visible association with the Jewish owner's name or property, and no *maris ayin* concern arises. When, however, the non-Jew is working visibly on land or property identified with the Jewish owner, the public association becomes the key variable.

### Contractor Logic

Within the category of work performed on Jewish property, Tosafot distinguishes between a day-laborer arrangement and a contractor (*kablan*) arrangement. A day-laborer is paid for each day of work and is presumptively under the Jewish employer's direction — people will naturally assume the owner told him to come on *Shabbat*. A contractor, by contrast, is hired to complete a project for a fixed sum and operates on his own timetable; the Jewish owner has no say in which days the contractor works. The public, sufficiently familiar with how contracting works, will understand that the non-Jew working on a Jewish-owned building on *Shabbat* is doing so on his own decision, not at the direction of the Jewish owner.

This is the basis on which Tosafot rules that a Jew may give a contract to a non-Jew to build his house, even though the work will be performed on *Shabbat*: *maskirim lahem urvot v'otzarot u'fundakaot* — the public interpretation will run through the correct lens of a contractor arrangement, not through the lens of directed Shabbat employment. The bathhouse is disanalogous precisely because no such *arisus* or contractor expectation exists in that context.

### Rabbeinu Tam

Tosafot records that Rabbeinu Tam ruled leniently on the contractor arrangement as a matter of practical *halacha* — *paskening* like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel that the *arisus* logic applies, and that a contracted arrangement avoids the *Shabbat* concern even on Jewish-owned property. Nevertheless, Tosafot notes that when it came to his own affairs, Rabbeinu Tam maintained a personal stringency: when he was having his own house built, he did not permit the non-Jewish workers to build on *Shabbat*, even though the arrangement was structured as a contract. He ruled permissively for others, but for himself he would not rely on the leniency. The *sugya* thus closes with the image of Rabbeinu Tam navigating precisely the tension this entire passage has explored — between what is formally permissible under the *arisus* and contractor framework, and what the halakhic intuition of a leading *posek* demands of himself in practice.
