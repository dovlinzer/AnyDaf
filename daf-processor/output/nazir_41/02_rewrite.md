# Nazir 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Metzora Shaving Requirem…

### Three Categories Shaving

The daf opens by situating the current discussion within a broader framework: there are three categories of people who are obligated to perform *giluach* — ritual shaving. The first is the *Levi'im* at the time of their original initiation in the wilderness, where the Torah states explicitly *vehe'eviru ta'ar al kol besaram*, "they shall pass a razor over all their flesh" — a formulation that leaves no doubt that a razor is required. The second category is the *metzora*, the person afflicted with the skin condition described in the Torah, where the verse instructs *veyigalach et kol se'aro*, "he shall shave all his hair," but makes no mention of a *ta'ar*, a razor. The third is the *nazir*, the nazirite, where the Torah says both *ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho*, "no razor shall pass over his head" — establishing a prohibition — and separately legislates the positive *mitzvah* of shaving, *vegilach hanazir petach ohel mo'ed et rosh nizro*, without again specifying the razor by name.

The underlying problem the *gemara* is working through is this: how do we know that the *mitzvah* shaving of the *metzora* and the *nazir* must be performed specifically with a razor, given that neither verse says so explicitly? Two *braisas* approach this question from opposite directions — one takes the *metzora*'s razor requirement as a given and tries (without success) to extend it to the *nazir*, while the other takes the *nazir*'s razor requirement as a given and tries to extend it to the *metzora*. The *gemara* now explains why each *braisa* started where it did, and that explanation turns out to depend on the broader dispute between the *Rabbanan* and Rabbi Eliezer.

### Rabbanan Beard Prohibiti…

The *Rabbanan* hold that the prohibition against shaving the beard applies only with a *ta'ar mamash* — literally a razor and nothing else. They derive this from the combination of two verses. The verse concerning *kohanim* states *ufat zekanam lo yegalechu*, which could encompass scissors — that is, any blade that cuts. The verse concerning Israelites states *velo tashchit et pe'at zekanecha*, where *tashchit* implies destruction at the root, which could encompass plucking but not necessarily a blade. Taken together, the prohibition requires both qualities simultaneously: a blade that destroys at the root. This defines the prohibited implement as, essentially, a razor. It is for this reason that many observe the practice of using electric shavers — on the view that an electric shaver operates through a scissor-like action and thus does not constitute a *ta'ar mamash*.

### Rabbanan Learning Metzora

This position of the *Rabbanan* on the beard prohibition is precisely what allows them to derive that the *metzora*'s shaving must be performed with a razor. The verse concerning the *metzora*'s *giluach* states *veyigalach et kol se'aro et rosho ve'et zekano* — it includes the beard in what the *metzora* must shave. Now, if the *metzora* is a *kohen*, shaving the beard would transgress both a *lo ta'aseh*, the prohibition *lo yegalechu*, and an *aseh*, the injunction *kedoshim tihyu*. For an ordinary Israelite, too, shaving the beard is prohibited. The Torah's inclusion of *et zekano* teaches that the *metzora*'s *mitzvah* overrides even these prohibitions.

The critical inference for the *Rabbanan* is this: since according to them the prohibition of shaving the beard applies *only* with a razor, the Torah could only need to declare an override if the *metzora*'s shaving is itself performed with a razor. Were the *metzora* permitted to shave the beard by some other means — scissors, for instance — the prohibition would not be triggered and no override would be necessary. The very fact that a *derashah* is required to permit it demonstrates that this shaving is done with a razor. Thus the *Rabbanan* learn the *metzora*'s requirement of a razor from *et zekano*, without any recourse to the laws of *nazir* at all.

### R. Eliezer Beard View

Rabbi Eliezer disputes the *Rabbanan*'s understanding of the beard prohibition. In his view, the prohibition of shaving the beard is broader than the *Rabbanan* acknowledge — it is not limited to a razor alone. Exactly how broad Rabbi Eliezer's position extends is the subject of the surrounding discussion, but the key point for the present *sugyah* is that for Rabbi Eliezer the beard prohibition applies in a wider range of cases than just the literal razor. This means the inference the *Rabbanan* draw from *et zekano* — that the *metzora*'s shaving overrides the beard prohibition, and therefore the *metzora*'s shaving must be with a razor — does not work for Rabbi Eliezer. Yes, the verse tells us the *metzora* overrides the beard prohibition, but since that prohibition is broader than just the razor, the override would need to apply across a wider range of implements. The fact that the *metzora* overrides the beard shaving does not, on Rabbi Eliezer's view, prove that the *metzora*'s shaving is specifically and exclusively with a razor.

### R. Eliezer Learning Metz…

Rabbi Eliezer must therefore find a different source to establish that the *metzora*'s *giluach* requires a razor, and he does so by using *et rosho* rather than *et zekano*. A *braisa* asks: what does the word *rosho* — "his head" — in the *metzora* verse come to teach? It answers: since the *nazir* is told *ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho*, the *nazir*'s prohibition of shaving is explicitly and exclusively a prohibition of the *ta'ar*. One might have thought that if a *nazir* becomes a *metzora*, he cannot fulfill the *metzora mitzvah* with respect to his head, since the razor is precisely what is prohibited to the *nazir*. *Talmud lomar rosho* — the word *rosho* teaches us that even the head of a *metzora* who is also a *nazir* must be shaved.

Now the same logical structure applies here as applied for the *Rabbanan* regarding the beard. Rabbi Eliezer knows that the *nazir*'s prohibition is specifically and only a prohibition of the razor. If the verse tells us that the *metzora* overrides even that razor prohibition — that even the *nazir*'s head must be shaved — then, applying the principle that an override is only invoked when there is no alternative means of fulfilling the obligation, the *metzora mitzvah* must require a razor. If the *metzora* could have used scissors or tweezers and thereby avoided the conflict with the *nazir*'s prohibition entirely, the Torah would never have needed to say that the *metzora* overrides this prohibition. The *gemara* makes this argument explicit: since the general rule — articulated by Reish Lakish — is that an *aseh* overrides a *lo ta'aseh* only when the two must inevitably conflict, the Torah's announcement that the *metzora* overrides the *nazir*'s *ta'ar* prohibition proves that the *metzora mitzvah* can only be fulfilled with a razor.

Thus the two *braisas* map onto the two positions. The *braisa* that begins by assuming the *metzora* requires a razor and then attempts (unsuccessfully) to extend this to *nazir* reflects the *Rabbanan*'s position — they already know the *metzora* requires a razor from *et zekano*, without involving *nazir* at all. The *braisa* that begins by assuming the *nazir* requires a razor and then attempts to extend this to *metzora* reflects Rabbi Eliezer's position — he cannot learn the *metzora*'s razor requirement from the beard, so he learns it instead from *rosho*, i.e., from the fact that the *metzora* overrides the razor prohibition of the *nazir*.

One might ask whether this reasoning proves only that the *metzora* is *permitted* to use a razor even against a *nazir*'s prohibition, rather than proving that the *metzora* is *required* to use a razor. The *gemara* addresses this directly. One could have imagined that the verse is merely telling us something like *hutrah* — once you are engaged in the *metzora*'s shaving, the razor prohibition no longer applies at all, even though alternative methods exist. The *gemara* rejects this precisely by invoking Reish Lakish's principle: we never say an *aseh* overrides a *lo ta'aseh* when the two can be reconciled. Therefore, if the *metzora* could have avoided the conflict, we would never allow him to use a razor. Since the verse does allow it — indeed requires it — the conclusion is that the *metzora mitzvah* must be performed with a razor and with nothing else.

### Aseh Docheh Lo Ta'aseh

With the question of how the *metzora*'s razor requirement is derived now resolved, the *gemara* turns to a broader structural question: what is the source for the general principle of *aseh docheh lo ta'aseh* — that a positive commandment overrides a prohibition — which the entire preceding discussion has been relying upon?

The answer comes from the juxtaposition of two verses concerning *tzitzit* and *shatnez*. The Torah states *lo tilbash sha'atnez* — one may not wear a garment of mixed wool and linen — immediately followed by *gedilim ta'aseh lach* — you shall make for yourself twisted fringes. The *techeiles*, the blue thread of *tzitzit*, must be spun from wool, and if one is wearing a linen garment, attaching *techeiles* means placing wool on linen — the very combination prohibited as *shatnez*. Yet the Torah permits it; the *aseh* of *tzitzit* overrides the *lo ta'aseh* of *shatnez*.

*Tosafot* observes that this instance is, in fact, a case of *hutrah* — a complete release from the prohibition rather than a mere *dechiyah*, a forced override — and that as a consequence one may wear the *tzitzit* garment even at night, when the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* does not apply, without transgressing the *shatnez* prohibition. This illustrates that there are indeed cases where the *parsha* of one *mitzvah* so fundamentally reconstitutes the act that the prohibition is simply inapplicable to it.

For the present discussion, however, the more immediately operative point is this: the *gemara* notes that for Rabbi Eliezer, both the override of the beard prohibition — which involves the *kohanim*'s *lo ta'aseh* of *lo yegalechu* together with the *aseh* of *kedoshim yihyu* — and the override of the *nazir*'s prohibition — which involves the *lo ta'aseh* of *ta'ar lo ya'avor* together with the *aseh* of *kadosh yihyeh gadel pera* — are cases of overriding a *lo ta'aseh* reinforced by an *aseh*, which represents a more difficult obstacle than a bare *lo ta'aseh* alone. *Tosafot* points out that precisely because Rabbi Eliezer uses both of his *drashos* to overcome the harder case, he must already know the basic principle from *tzitzit* and *shatnez* that an *aseh* overrides even a simple *lo ta'aseh*. The *drashos* by *rosho* and *zekano* then extend that principle to the harder cases — where both a prohibition and a positive commandment stand on the other side.

## Rosho and Zekano Analysis

### Rosho Sideburns Issue

Having established what each camp learns from *zekano* — that the *metzora*'s *giluach* overrides the prohibition of shaving the beard — the *gemara* turns to the question of what the *Rabbanan* do with *rosho*. For Rabbi Eliezer, *rosho* is already accounted for: it teaches that the *metzora* overrides the *nazir*'s razor prohibition applicable to the head. But the *Rabbanan* do not need *rosho* for that purpose, since they learn the *metzora*'s razor requirement from *zekano* alone. So what does *rosho* come to teach them?

The *gemara* answers: the *Rabbanan* need *rosho* to tell them that the *metzora*'s *giluach* overrides the prohibition of *lo takifu pe'at roshchem* — the prohibition of shaving the sideburns. Shaving all the hair of the head necessarily includes the area of the *pe'ot*, the sideburns, and without an explicit override, one might have thought that even a *metzora* could not remove the sideburns. A *braisa* states this explicitly: *lo takifu pe'at roshchem* — could one think this applies even to a *metzora*? *Talmud lomar rosho* — the verse says *rosho*, to teach that even the *metzora*'s sideburns must be shaved.

### Zekano Override Lesson

The *gemara* now examines why both *rosho* and *zekano* are needed. Once the *metzora* is told to shave his beard, overriding the prohibitions incumbent upon *kohanim* and ordinary Israelites alike, would it not follow automatically that the *metzora* also overrides the sideburn prohibition? The sideburn prohibition is, after all, less severe in certain respects — it applies to all Israelites but is not specifically reinforced by an additional *aseh* the way the *kohen*'s beard prohibition is. If *zekano* already teaches us that the *metzora* overrides the harder obstacles, surely we could infer that it also overrides the easier ones.

Similarly, *zekano* is introduced to override the beard prohibition with respect to the *metzora*. A *braisa* makes this explicit: *ufat zekanam lo yegalechu* — could one think this applies even to a *metzora*? *Talmud lomar zekano* — the verse includes his beard. And then the *gemara* asks: given that we have *zekano*, why do we also need *rosho*? And given that we have *rosho*, why do we also need *zekano*? The answer is *tzrichi* — both are necessary — and the *gemara* proceeds to explain precisely why each is indispensable.

### Beard vs. Sideburns Scope

The *gemara* works through the *tzrichi* argument with care. Suppose the Torah had written only *zekano* and not *rosho*. In that case, I know the *metzora* overrides the beard prohibition, but I might have thought that the prohibition of *hakafat kol harosh* — shaving all the hair of the head including the sideburns — is not actually prohibited at all in the general case. The argument would run as follows: the verse says *lo takifu*, from the root of *hakafah*, going around in a circle. The primary understanding of this prohibition is that one may not create a straight line from in front of the ear to behind it, leaving the sideburns cut. But if one shaves off *all* the hair of the head, including the *pe'ot* — perhaps that is not called *hakafah* at all, since one never created the configuration of a circle with the remaining hair stripped away. This is the question of *hakafat kol harosh shemah hakafah* — does shaving the entire head count as *hakafah*?

Had the Torah written only *zekano*, I would have concluded that *hakafat kol harosh lav shemah hakafah* — it is not called *hakafah*, and there is therefore no prohibition to override in the first place. The *metzora*'s shaving of the head would then be self-evidently permitted, but only because it was never prohibited. Therefore the Torah writes *rosho* to establish an override, and from the fact that it requires an override, I can infer that as a general rule *hakafat kol harosh shemah hakafah* — shaving the entire head is included in the prohibition, and the *metzora* therefore requires an explicit *derashah* to permit it.

### Why Two Verses Needed

Now suppose the reverse: the Torah had written only *rosho* and not *zekano*. From *rosho*, I would have learned two things: first, that the *metzora* overrides the sideburn prohibition, and second — from the very fact that an override is needed — that *hakafat kol harosh shemah hakafah*. I might have then assumed that by the same logic, once the *metzora* overrides the prohibition of the sideburns, the *mitzvah* of *metzora* overrides the beard prohibition as well, simply by inference. But there is a third thing I would not have learned: that the *metzora*'s *giluach* must be performed specifically with a razor.

This is the crucial asymmetry. For the *Rabbanan*, the reason that *zekano* proves the *metzora* must use a razor is that the beard prohibition is specifically and exclusively a razor prohibition. When the Torah declares that the *metzora* overrides the beard prohibition, and we know that prohibition exists only in the case of a razor, then the override can only be invoked if there is no alternative — which means the *metzora mitzvah* must be performed with a razor. But the sideburn prohibition, as we shall see, is broader than just the razor. If I had only *rosho* and tried to infer the *metzora*'s razor requirement from the override of the sideburns, I would fail — because the sideburn prohibition encompasses more than the razor, the override of it does not prove that the *metzora* must use a razor. Hence *zekano* is indispensable for establishing the razor requirement. And *rosho* is indispensable for establishing both that the sideburns themselves must be shaved and that *hakafat kol harosh* is a real prohibition. *Tzrichi* — both are needed.

## Rosho and Zekano Analy…

### Sideburns Broader Scope

Embedded in the *gemara*'s argument is a striking implicit claim: the prohibition of shaving the sideburns is broader in scope than the prohibition of shaving the beard. The beard prohibition, as the *Rabbanan* hold, is limited exclusively to a *ta'ar mamash* — a literal razor. The sideburn prohibition, however, applies across a wider range of implements. This is precisely why the override of the sideburn prohibition by the *metzora* cannot serve to establish that the *metzora mitzvah* requires a razor: an override of a prohibition that applies even without a razor does not prove that the act overriding it must be done with a razor.

## Sideburns Practical Hala…

### Tosafot Head Prohibition

This implicit *gemara* is the starting point for *Tosafot*'s analysis of the practical *halakhah* of sideburns. *Tosafot*, in the *dibbur hamatchil* beginning *hashta*, notes explicitly that our *sugyah* implies that the prohibition of *hakafat harosh* — shaving the sideburns — applies even without a razor. The *gemara*'s logic compels this conclusion: if the prohibition were limited to a razor just like the beard, then the override of sideburns would have been just as informative about the *metzora*'s razor requirement as the override of the beard, and the *gemara*'s distinction between the two overrides would collapse.

### Three Sources Sideburns

*Tosafot* proceeds to marshal three sources that bear on the scope of the sideburn prohibition, and the picture they present is not perfectly harmonious. The first source is our *gemara* itself, which, as just described, implies that sideburns are prohibited with a broader range than the razor alone. The second source is the *Toras Kohanim* — the *Sifra* — which discusses the *metzora* verse and states explicitly: *sheharosh assur bemisparayim vehazakan muttar* — the head is prohibited even with scissors, while the beard is permitted with scissors. This sounds unambiguous: ordinary scissors applied to the sideburns are prohibited. The third source is the *Tosefta*, which discusses the sideburn prohibition and states that one is *chayav* only if one uses a *ta'ar* — a razor. This appears to pull in the opposite direction from the *Sifra*.

### Tosafot K'ein Ta'ar

*Tosafot* resolves the tension between the *Sifra* and the *Tosefta* by proposing that *ke'ein ta'ar* — scissors that function like a razor — is what is prohibited. The *Tosefta*'s phrase "razor" is to be read as encompassing scissors that cut as close to the skin as a razor would; the *Sifra*'s mention of scissors refers to precisely this type of close-cutting scissors. The prohibition of sideburns thus extends beyond the *ta'ar mamash* to include *misparayim ke'ein ta'ar* — scissors that approximate the effect of a razor. In practical terms, this would include an electric shaver, which is understood to operate through a scissor-like mechanism that can cut very close to the skin. An instrument that cuts hair close to the root but through a scissor action — *ke'ein ta'ar* — is prohibited at the sideburns even according to those who permit it at the beard.

*Tosafot* then adds a further caution: *tzarich lehizaher shelo yitol pe'as harosh afilu bemisparayim* — one must be careful not to remove the sideburns even with ordinary scissors, since scissors generally can be characterized as *ke'ein ta'ar*. And the practice, *Tosafot* notes, is that when children get haircuts, one leaves a significant amount of hair at the temples — *leshayer betziddehen harbe se'ar*.

### Shulchan Aruch Ruling

The *Shulchan Aruch* in *Yoreh De'ah*, siman 181, addresses all these prohibitions together. On the sideburns, the *Shulchan Aruch* begins by stating *eino chayav ela bataar* — one is only *chayav* with a razor — but immediately adds *veyesh osrim bemisparayim ke'ein ta'ar veyesh lachush ledivrehem* — there are those who prohibit with scissors that are like a razor, and one should be concerned for their opinion. The ruling is thus not definitive in one direction; it leans toward the *ta'ar*-only position while acknowledging the stricter view.

By contrast, when the *Shulchan Aruch* addresses the beard prohibition, it states unambiguously: *eino chayav al hashchasas pe'as hazakan ela bataar aval bemisparayim muttar afilu ke'ein ta'ar* — one is only *chayav* for destroying the beard with a razor; scissors, even if they cut like a razor, are permitted for the beard. The asymmetry is complete: for the beard, the prohibition is limited to the literal razor, and even *ke'ein ta'ar* is permitted. For the sideburns, the *Shulchan Aruch* at minimum acknowledges a significant opinion that *ke'ein ta'ar* is prohibited.

### Contemporary Practice

The practical consequence of this analysis is familiar to anyone who has observed traditional Jewish practice: one may use an electric shaver on the beard — relying on the view that the beard prohibition is limited to a *ta'ar mamash* and that a shaver operates through a scissor-like mechanism — while leaving the sideburns entirely unshaved by anything that could be characterized as *ke'ein ta'ar*. The implicit teaching of the *gemara*, developed by *Tosafot* and reflected in the *Shulchan Aruch*, is that these are not the same prohibition with the same scope, and they should not be treated identically in practice.

## Complete Hair Removal

### Invalid Shaving Methods

The *mishnah*'s ruling is stated plainly: *vekhulan shegilchu shelo beta'ar o sheshiyeru shtei se'aros lo asu velo klum* — if any of these three categories — *nazir*, *metzora*, or *Levi'im* — shaved without using a razor, or if they left two hairs unshaved, they have not fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *giluach* at all. Both conditions must be met: the instrument must be a razor, and the shaving must be essentially complete.

### Rubo Kekulo Principle

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika draws a broader principle from this ruling: *zos omeres rubo k'kulo m'deoraisa* — this tells us that as a general Torah principle, a majority counts as the whole. His reasoning is that since the *gemara* will show that the requirement of complete shaving is derived from a specific *drasha* — with one hair remaining still counting as "all" but two hairs remaining not counting — the *gemara* treats this stringency as an exception that proves the rule. In the ordinary case, *rubo k'kulo* would apply and a majority would suffice. Here, the Torah explicitly demands totality, and that explicit demand is itself evidence that the default would have been otherwise.

### Yigalchenu Source

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika points to the source for the requirement of complete hair removal: *deichtiv uvayom hashevii yigalchenu* — "on the seventh day he shall shave it entirely." The word *yigalchenu*, with its third-person object suffix, implies the full entirety of the hair, not merely a majority. This *pasuk*, however, appears in the context of the *nazir tamei* — the nazirite who has contracted ritual impurity — and his shaving on the seventh day of purification. The *gemara* will return to the implications of this.

### One Hair Tolerance

Even within the requirement of complete shaving, the *gemara* acknowledges a kind of definitional tolerance: leaving one hair is still considered "all," but leaving two hairs is not. One hair is apparently within the margin of what can be characterized as complete; two hairs cross a threshold into something that cannot be so characterized. This is reminiscent of a point made elsewhere about precision in *halakhah* — that even when the Torah demands exactness, the *halakhah* operates with a working definition of exactness that does not demand mathematical perfection. Just as the *gemara*'s measurement of the diagonal of a square uses 1.4 rather than the precise irrational value, so too here: one remaining hair does not defeat the characterization of "complete."

### Nazir Tamei vs. Tahor

Rav Yosef son of Rabbi Chanina challenges the derivation: the verse *yigalchenu* is written with respect to a *nazir tamei*, an impure nazirite — how can one use it as a source for the requirement of complete shaving that applies to a *nazir tahor*, a pure nazirite completing his term? This challenge meets with what the *gemara* describes as laughter from the scholars of *Eretz Yisrael* — and the reason, as *Machaku alah bema'arava* implies, is that the question collapses under examination. The *gemara* responds: we know that the *nazir tamei*'s shaving must use a razor because we learn it from the *nazir tahor*. Given that we learn the *nazir tamei* from the *nazir tahor* in one direction, we can equally well learn from the *nazir tamei* back to the *nazir tahor* in the other direction. *Mah tamei ki shayar shtei se'arot velo klum avid* — just as by the *tamei* shaving, *yigalchenu* means total shaving and leaving two hairs invalidates it, *af hacha nami* — the same applies here. The two cases of shaving illuminate each other bidirectionally.

*Tosafot* notes a curious irony: in *Sanhedrin*, it is Rav Yosef son of Rabbi Chanina who is described as one of those who *machaku* — who laughed — at a question posed in *Eretz Yisrael*. Here, the laughter is directed at his own question. As the *gemara* might put it: if you laugh at other people's questions, other people will laugh at yours.

## Edge Cases Giluach

### Rava's Question

The *gemara* moves from the establishment of the requirement to a series of edge cases designed to test the outer limits of the principle. Rava poses the following question: a *nazir* who shaved at the conclusion of his *nezirut* but left two hairs. He has not yet fulfilled the *mitzvah*. Now hair grows back, and after a period his head is once again full of hair — but the original two hairs that he left are still identifiable. He then shaves those two specific hairs while his head remains full of all the regrown hair. Has he fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *giluach*? This is something like the philosophical puzzle of whether an action that targets only the two remaining original hairs constitutes a fulfillment, given that the totality of hair on his head is nowhere near completely shaved.

### Single Hair Not Signific…

The more refined version of Rava's question — as clarified in the text — is actually different: a *nazir* who shaved and left two hairs, then one of those hairs fell out on its own and he deliberately shaved the remaining one. Has he fulfilled the *mitzvah*? At first glance, one might say that since the final remaining hair has now been removed, the *giluach* is complete. The question, however, is whether the deliberate cutting of a single hair when no other hairs remain to be cut constitutes a meaningful act of *giluach* at all.

### Giluach as Process

The answer given is *giluach ein kan se'ar ein kan* — there is no *giluach* here and there is no hair here. The *gemara* initially takes this to mean: if there is no hair, how can there be *giluach*? But it clarifies: the point is that even though the person is rendered bald — *af al pi shese'ar ein kan* — the *mitzvah* of *giluach* has not been fulfilled — *mitzvas giluach ein kan*. The act of cutting one hair when there is no second hair to accompany it is not defined as a significant act of shaving. *Giluach*, as a *mitzvah*, is understood to be a process involving multiple hairs; cutting a single hair in isolation, without others being cut alongside it, does not rise to the level of an act of *giluach* in the halakhic sense.

### Baldness Without Mitzvah

The final inference is stark: the person ends up completely bald — the last hair has been removed — yet the *mitzvah* has not been fulfilled. The act of rendering someone bald and the act of performing *mitzvas giluach* are not identical. Baldness is a physical outcome; *giluach* as a *mitzvah* requires a meaningful act of shaving involving multiple hairs as part of a coherent process. When the final act consists of cutting only one hair with no others present to be cut, the result may be baldness, but not the fulfillment of the *mitzvah*.

## Hair Care Permitted Acts

### Hand Straightening Allow…

The next *mishnah* introduces a series of rulings about what a *nazir* may and may not do with respect to his hair during the period of *nezirut*. The *mishnah* states: *nazir chofef umefaspes aval lo sorek* — a *nazir* may run his fingers through his hair and use his hands to straighten and separate the hairs, but he may not comb. The prohibition on pulling out hair during *nezirut* applies not only to a razor but — as the *gemara* has noted — to any deliberate act of hair removal, including by hand. The question, then, is why using the hands to straighten hair is permitted.

### Unintentional Loss Allow…

The answer the *gemara* provides is that this *mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *davar she'eino miskaven mutar* — an action that is not intended to achieve the prohibited outcome is permitted. When a *nazir* runs his fingers through his hair or uses his hands to smooth and separate the hairs, his intention is to straighten them, not to pull them out. If a hair comes loose incidentally in the process, Rabbi Shimon regards this as *davar she'eino miskaven* and therefore permitted.

### Combing Prohibited

If the first part of the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Shimon's leniency, why does the *mishnah* then prohibit combing? If unintended hair removal is permitted, why should combing be any different? The *gemara* answers through Rabbah: *kulla Rabbi Shimon hi* — the entire *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Shimon's view — but combing is different because *kol hasorek lehassir nimi meduldlos miskaven* — anyone who combs their hair is, in the process of combing, intending to remove tangled, loosened, or knotted hairs. A comb encounters stuck or matted hairs and the act of drawing it through is inherently directed at removing those obstructed hairs. It is not merely an incidental consequence — it is part of the purpose of the combing action itself. Therefore, even Rabbi Shimon would agree that combing is prohibited: it is not a pure case of *davar she'eino miskaven*.

### Combing Shabbat Issues

This *gemara* carries significant implications beyond *nezirut*. The *Shulchan Aruch* and the *Beis Yosef* discuss the prohibition of combing hair on *Shabbos*, particularly in the context of women preparing for the *mikvah* on *Shabbos*. The concern is that combing on *Shabbos* involves the inevitable removal of hairs, either as a *pesik reisha* — an inevitable consequence — or, per this *gemara*, as an act of partial *kavanah*. Notably, the *gemara* here does not frame the prohibition of combing in terms of *pesik reisha* — it could have done so, relying on the principle that Abaye and Rava introduce limiting Rabbi Shimon's leniency. Instead, the *gemara* frames it as *miskaven*, as intentional removal, which is arguably a more fundamental objection. This distinction is itself noteworthy: *pesik reisha* as a concept limiting *davar she'eino miskaven* is associated with Abaye and Rava and is not something that appears explicitly in Rabbi Shimon's own formulation.

The question of what exactly constitutes *pesik reisha* in the context of combing is itself complex. If in the course of a three-minute combing session one will inevitably remove three hairs, but no single stroke of the comb is individually a *pesik reisha*, does the overall act qualify? The analogy to dragging a bench through a garden is instructive: each inch of movement may not be a *pesik reisha* for creating a furrow, but the full traversal of the garden is. Whether one aggregates across a process or evaluates each discrete act independently is a genuine question in the *poskim*.

### R. Ovadia Modern Combing

A striking position on this question is attributed to Rav Ovadia Yosef, who is reported to have ruled that modern combing does not necessarily constitute *pesik reisha*. His reasoning apparently turns on the character of modern combs, modern hair care, or some other relevant variable — though one should consult the *teshuvah* directly rather than relying on a summary. The ruling is noted here precisely because it is exceptional: the *gemara*'s reasoning, and the assumption of the *Shulchan Aruch* and the *poskim*, has generally been that combing inevitably involves hair removal, whether framed as *pesik reisha* or as *kavanah*. The suggestion that contemporary conditions alter this analysis is a significant departure, whatever its ultimate *halakhic* standing.

## Hair Cleansing Agents

### Mineral Cleanser Ban

The concluding section of the daf introduces a ruling of Rabbi Yishmael: *lo yachof b'adama* — a *nazir* may not wash his hair with a type of earth-based mineral substance. The reason given is *mipnei she'hi mesharet et hase'ar* — because this substance loosens and ultimately causes the hair to fall out. In the ancient world, mineral substances such as natron (a naturally occurring sodium carbonate), bentonite clay, and related compounds served as cleansing agents, some of which could weaken the hair and cause it to shed. Rabbi Yishmael prohibits these substances to a *nazir* because using them would lead to hair loss, implicating the prohibition against removing hair during *nezirut*.

### Specific vs. General Ban

The *gemara* raises a question about the precise scope of this prohibition: does the *mishnah*'s phrase *mipnei she'hi mesharet* — "because it loosens" — mean that Rabbi Yishmael is prohibiting specifically this type of mineral substance because of its hair-loosening properties? Or does it mean that the category of mineral cleansing agents is prohibited because such agents characteristically cause hair to loosen? The question turns on whether the prohibition targets the particular causal mechanism — the *mesharetut*, the loosening — of this specific agent, or whether it targets the broader category of mineral cleansing agents as a *gezeirah* on account of those within the category that do loosen hair.

### Harmless Mineral Use

The practical difference emerges clearly: suppose one identifies a mineral substance that is entirely harmless to hair — one that cleanses without loosening or causing any hair to fall out. If the prohibition is targeted at the specific harmful properties of the prohibited agent — *mipnei she'hi mesharet* in the narrow sense — then a harmless mineral would be permitted. If, on the other hand, the prohibition is a categorical *gezeirah* against all mineral cleansing agents, enacted because of those within the category that do cause hair loss, then even a demonstrably harmless mineral would be prohibited.

### Teiku Unresolved

The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved — *teiku*. This is more than a technical loose end: it reflects a genuinely open question about the structure of Rabbi Yishmael's prohibition and whether it is grounded in the specific mechanism of harm or in the categorical identity of the substance. The practical implication — whether a *nazir* may use a mineral-based cleansing agent if it is known not to cause hair loss — remains without a definitive answer in the *gemara*.
