# Yoma 46 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Coals & Milfnei Hashem

### Previous Setup

The daf opens by picking up from where we left off: a discussion of the coals taken from the outer altar and the derivation — from the *pesukim* at the beginning of *Parshat Tzav* — of the different fires maintained in the *Mikdash*. The basic framework is that everyone agrees on a minimum of two fires: one for the *korbanot* on the outer altar and one for the daily *ketoret* offered on the inner gold altar. Beyond that, there is universal agreement that a special additional fire was maintained for *Yom Kippur*. The further question is how we know that wherever else fire was needed — both for the *Yom Kippur* service in the *Kodesh Kodashim* and on a daily basis to light the *menorah* — the source was the outer altar rather than the inner gold altar, which would have been more proximate.

This question is resolved through various *pesukim*. The last derivation discussed was the source for the *machtah* — the fire pan used on *Yom Kippur*. The verse states: *ve-lakach melo ha-machtah gachalei esh me'al ha-mizbe'ach* — "and he shall take a full fire pan of coals from upon the altar." The difficulty is: which altar? One might reasonably read *mizbe'ach* as referring to the inner gold altar, which is closer to the divine presence. The resolution comes from the continuation of the verse, which adds *mi-lifnei Hashem* — "from before God."

### Two Verses Needed

The *gemara* now explains why both phrases are necessary — why the verse must say both *me'al ha-mizbe'ach* ("from the altar") and *mi-lifnei Hashem* ("from before God"). Had the Torah stated only *me'al ha-mizbe'ach*, one would have concluded that *mizbe'ach* refers to the inner altar. Had it stated only *mi-lifnei Hashem*, one might have read that phrase as requiring a very precise location — specifically the point directly opposite the entrance to the *Heichal* — and concluded that the coals must come exclusively from that one spot, with the sides to the north and south excluded. Each phrase is therefore indispensable, and the combination of the two is what the *gemara* terms *tzricha* — both are needed.

### Western Side & Entrance

The final resolution clarifies what *mi-lifnei Hashem* actually requires. The western face of the outer altar is considered closer to the divine presence, as it faces toward the *Heichal* and, by extension, toward the *Aron*. However, *mi-lifnei Hashem* might be understood even more narrowly — not merely the western side in general, but specifically the portion directly opposite the entrance, to the exclusion of the sections slightly to the north or south of the doorway. The verse therefore states *me'al ha-mizbe'ach* to expand the permissible range: one may take the coals from either side of the entrance — *me-hai gissa u-me-hai gissa de-innatza de-pitcha* — from this side and that side of the opening. Both expressions together define both the correct altar (the outer one) and the permissible range of positions along its western face.

## Rabbi Meir on Leftovers

### Special Fire on Shabbat

The *gemara* now transitions to a related but distinct topic that will carry us through the remainder of the chapter. *Amar Rabbi Elazar mishum Bar Kappara*: Rabbi Meir used to say that the limbs of an *olah* that were leftover — *eivarei olah she-nitotru* — are given their own special wood arrangement. According to Rabbi Meir, one of the additional fires at the altar was designated specifically for the fat and limbs that had not finished burning through the night. Offerings brought during the day were meant to be consumed through the following night, but if they remained unburned by morning, Rabbi Meir held that they would be transferred to a separate *ma'aracha* — a separate arrangement of wood — while the primary fire was reserved for the new day's offerings. The *gemara* adds: *ve-afillu be-Shabbat* — even on *Shabbat*.

This is a genuine *chiddush*. If a *korban* was brought on Friday and its limbs did not finish burning overnight, one is actually required on *Shabbat* morning to make a special fire, arrange the wood, and transfer those limbs — even though this involves active labor on *Shabbat* to address the remnants of a Friday *korban*. This is not at all like the *olat Shabbat be-shabbato*, which is a *Shabbat korban* brought in its own right. Here one is handling the leftover work of Friday's offering on *Shabbat* itself, and that constitutes a striking *chiddush*.

The *gemara* then questions: why is this a *chiddush*? We already know from our *Mishna* that every day there were four fires according to Rabbi Meir (*bechol yom hayu sham arba ma'arachot, divrei Rabbi Meir*), and that statement of "every day" would seem to include *Shabbat* implicitly. So what is this *beraita* adding?

### Psulim & Char Requirement

*Amar Rav Avin*: the *chiddush* is *lo nitzrecha ela li-fsulin* — it is necessary only to teach us that this practice applies even to *korbanot* that are *pasul*, invalid. Many categories of invalid offerings follow the rule of *keivan she-alu lo yerdu* — once they have been placed on the altar, they are not removed. But that rule does not automatically entail that they receive the same special treatment as valid offerings. One might have thought that invalid offerings are simply left where they are, perhaps pushed back onto the main fire if they fall, but not accorded the dignity of being transferred to a dedicated secondary *ma'aracha*. The *beraita* therefore informs us that even *psulim* receive this treatment.

However, this ruling comes with a crucial qualification: *ve-davka she-mashla bahen ha-or* — specifically when the fire has already taken dominion over them, when they have been substantially charred. If the fire has not yet taken hold — *aval lo mashla bahen ha-or* — one does not move them. The distinction is especially significant with respect to *Shabbat*: once an offering has been sufficiently consumed by fire, it has in a sense already been transformed. *Rashi* evokes the language of another *gemara* — *na'asa lachmo shel mizbe'ach* — it has become, as it were, the bread of the altar, akin to firewood. At that point it may be handled on *Shabbat*, even if it was *pasul*, even if it was yesterday's offering. If it has not yet reached that state, one leaves it where it is and addresses it after *Shabbat*.

### Mashla Bahen Ha'ur

There is a question as to whether the *gemara* at this stage is focused primarily on the *Shabbat* dimension or whether the discussion of *psulim* and *mashla bahen ha-or* also carries practical force on a weekday. A weekday *chiddush* can be identified as well: even on a regular day, one might have assumed that *pasul* offerings are simply ignored or left on the main fire, and that the transfer to a secondary *ma'aracha* would be too great a mark of distinction for something invalid. The *beraita* teaches that even *psulim* are so transferred — but only after *mashla bahen ha-or*. The logic is that the primary function of the main fire is to burn. Once that burning has been substantially accomplished, it is appropriate to move the remains to the secondary fire. The qualification therefore applies on weekdays as well, even apart from *Shabbat* concerns.

It is worth noting what is meant physically. The top of the *mizbe'ach* was a large, flat stone surface — not hollow throughout but stone-covered on top — upon which multiple separate piles of wood burned simultaneously. The various *ma'arachot* were distinct pyres on this surface, and the limbs of the *korbanot* were laid directly upon them. A limb could shift position, partly fall off, or simply fail to ignite fully. The question of whether it has been sufficiently consumed — *mashla bahen ha-or* — determines how it is subsequently handled.

### Altar Structure

The physical layout of the altar is relevant to understanding the discussion. Looking at the *mizbe'ach* from above as a kind of bird's-eye view, one sees a flat surface hosting several distinct wood piles and fires. According to Rabbi Meir, there were four fires daily and five on *Yom Kippur*. One of those four was the primary fire for the *korbanot*, and another was specifically for the leftover limbs from the previous day. The others served distinct functions discussed elsewhere. The arrangement matters here because when a limb from Friday's *korban* has not fully burned, the question is whether it remains on the main pile, is pushed back toward it, or is transferred to the dedicated secondary fire. Rabbi Meir's ruling is that it is actively transferred to its own *ma'aracha* — and one even makes a fresh fire for this purpose — *oseh lahen ma'aracha*, as the *beraita* states. This is not merely a passive leaving-in-place but an affirmative action.

The complexity of doing this on *Shabbat* includes not only the handling of the limbs but the very construction of the fire. *Oseh lahen ma'aracha* means building a new wood arrangement and kindling it — real *melacha* — on *Shabbat*, to address what remains of Friday's *korban*. The fact that this is permitted, and that it is permitted even for *psulim* once *mashla bahen ha-or* applies, is the full scope of the *chiddush*.

### Kosher & Invalid Alike

The *gemara* records a second position: *ika de-amri echad kesherim ve-echad pesulim im mashla bahen ha-ur in, i lo, lo* — some say that the qualification of *mashla bahen ha-or* applies to both valid and invalid offerings alike. According to this view, even for *kosher* offerings, one only transfers them to the secondary fire once the fire has substantially consumed them. If they have not yet reached that state, one does not transfer them — not even if they are valid.

This reading shifts the *chiddush* somewhat. The first opinion located the novelty specifically in the treatment of *psulim*, since valid offerings were presumably already known to receive this treatment regardless of their state. The second opinion makes *mashla bahen ha-or* a universal threshold. On a weekday, this teaches the correct timing of the transfer: only after substantial burning. On *Shabbat*, it teaches that even valid offerings from the previous day are subject to this principle — one waits until they have been substantially consumed before handling them on *Shabbat*, at which point they may be moved, since they have already essentially become like the wood-fuel of the altar. Until that point, they are left in place.

## Yesterday's Korban on Sh…

### Yom Kippur Proof

The *gemara* then asks: is the ruling *ve-afillu be-Shabbat* — even on *Shabbat* — actually a *chiddush*, given that the *Mishna* teaches it implicitly? The *Mishna* states *ve-ha-yom chamesh* — on *Yom Kippur* there were five fires. If one fire is the dedicated fire for leftover limbs, and the *Mishna* says this fire exists even on *Yom Kippur* — which has the same *halachot* as *Shabbat* — then surely it is already implicit that yesterday's *korbanot* are handled even on a day that functions like *Shabbat*.

### Achar Shabbat Caveat

*Amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov*: this inference is not as straightforward as it appears. The *beraita*'s statement of *ve-afillu be-Shabbat* is still necessary, because one might have limited the *Mishna*'s case of five fires on *Yom Kippur* to a specific calendar scenario — *heicha de-Yom HaKippurim lihavei achar Shabbat* — when *Yom Kippur* falls on a Sunday, immediately following *Shabbat*. In that case, one could justify burning *Shabbat*'s leftover *korbanot* on *Yom Kippur* because *de-chelvei Shabbat krayvin be-Yom HaKippurim* — the fats of *Shabbat* may be brought on *Yom Kippur*, since the two share the same *kedusha*. That cross-sanctity would suffice to explain the fifth fire. But if *Yom Kippur* fell mid-week, one might have said no such fifth fire exists. The *beraita*'s teaching of *ve-afillu be-Shabbat* is therefore still necessary to establish that yesterday's *korbanot* are always handled, regardless of such calendar considerations.

### Rava's Challenge

*Amar Rava*: this reasoning reflects poorly on the quality of analysis — *man hai de-lo chayish le-kimchei*, who is this person unconcerned with the quality of the flour they are producing? The metaphor invoked here echoes the famous *gemara* in which the storehouse of knowledge is compared to wheat and the process of analysis to grinding: *hakol tzrichin le-marei chittaya* — everyone needs the seller of wheat, meaning one must have the data before one can analyze it. Rava's pointed remark is that if you have the data — *ha-bechol yom tannan*, the *Mishna* says *every day* — then surely every day means every day, including *Shabbat*. Why construct this elaborate caveat about *Yom Kippur* following *Shabbat*? The *Mishna* itself renders the *beraita* redundant.

The *gemara* acknowledges the force of Rava's objection — *kashya* — but notes that *bechol yom* might be read as a contrast to the exceptional case of *Yom Kippur* rather than as a literal assertion that the practice applies every single day, including *Shabbat*. Dov's observation is apt here: *bechol yom* frequently functions in the *Mishna* as a rhetorical counterpoint to an exception, not as a substantive claim about every day without qualification.

All of this, however, is a debate about whether the *chiddush* was already known. There is universal agreement among those discussed so far that the *halacha* is as stated: yesterday's leftover *korbanot* are handled on *Shabbat*. The dissenting voice comes now.

## Beginning & End Override

### Tamid Overrides

*U-peliga de-Rav Huna* — all of the foregoing is in tension with the position of Rav Huna. *De-amar Rav Huna*: the *tamid* — *techilato docha ve-sofo eino docha* — the beginning of its process overrides, but its end does not override. On the plain reading as we first encounter this statement, Rav Huna appears to hold that while a *korban* may be brought on *Shabbat* — the bringing of a *korban* overrides *Shabbat* — the conclusion of that *korban*'s process cannot be carried over and handled on *Shabbat*. One may bring a *Shabbat* offering on *Shabbat*, but one may not handle the mop-up work of Friday's *korban* on *Shabbat*. This position is in direct tension with what we have been saying.

The *gemara* then asks: *mai eino docha* — with respect to which domain does Rav Huna's principle distinguish between beginning and end? The *tamid*, after all, overrides two things: *Shabbat* and *tumah*. The phrase *docha* is ambiguous. This sets up the debate between Rav Chisda and Rava (or Rabbah).

### Shabbat vs. Tumah

*Amar Rav Chisda*: the correct reading of Rav Huna's principle is *dochat ha-Shabbat ve-eino dochat ha-tumah* — the *tamid* overrides *Shabbat* in both its beginning and its end, but it overrides *tumah* only at the beginning, not at the end.

Regarding *Shabbat*, Rav Chisda holds that both the commencement of the *korban* and its completion override *Shabbat*. Friday's leftover limbs may be handled on *Shabbat* morning; the end of the process follows the beginning. But regarding *tumah*, the end of the process does not override. *Rashi* suggests two interpretations of what "the end" means here. One possibility is that the burning of limbs does not override *tumah*, while the sprinkling of the blood does. *Tosafot* contests this, arguing there is ample evidence that the burning of fats does override *tumah*. According to *Tosafot*, the more defensible position is the following: the question is whether, when a *tamid* was brought in a state of *tumah* on Wednesday, one may on Thursday morning continue to give special treatment to whatever limbs remain, transferring them to a secondary fire and arranging a new wood pile on their behalf. The parallel to the *Shabbat* question is evident: on *Shabbat*, the issue is whether Friday's unfinished burning may be addressed the following morning; here, the issue is whether Wednesday's *tumah*-korban may receive continued handling the following morning. Rav Chisda says: by *Shabbat*, yes — even the end overrides; by *tumah*, no — only the beginning overrides.

### Tumah Override Only

*Ve-Rabbah amar*: the reverse. *Docha et ha-tumah* — the *tamid* overrides *tumah* in both beginning and end. Even Thursday morning, one may continue handling Wednesday's *tumah*-korban, transferring its remaining limbs to the secondary fire. *Ve-eino docha et ha-Shabbat* — but the end of the process does not override *Shabbat*. Even though Friday's *korban* may be brought on *Shabbat*, one may not on *Shabbat* morning address the unfinished burning of Friday's offering. This contradicts the earlier consensus position and aligns with what Rav Huna's principle states on the simpler reading.

Each position thus agrees that the beginning of the *korban* overrides both *Shabbat* and *tumah*; they dispute only which of those two domains continues to be overridden by the end of the process.

## Abaye's Difficulty

### Both Inconsistent

*Abaye* then challenges both Rav Chisda and Rava (or Rabbah), arguing that each is internally inconsistent. *Le-didach kashya u-le-Rav Chisda kashya* — for you it is difficult, and for Rav Chisda it is difficult. The argument runs as follows. Each of you extends the override in one domain to the end of the process while restricting it in the other domain to the beginning only. But what is the basis for this asymmetry? Both *Shabbat* and *tumah* are overridden by the *tamid* through the same scriptural mechanism — *bemo'ado*, "in its time," from which we derive that the *korban* is brought in its time even against *Shabbat* (*bemo'ado va-afillu be-Shabbat*) and even against *tumah* (*bemo'ado va-afillu be-tumah*). If the same textual override applies to both, why should the end of the process be treated differently in each domain?

For Rava: if *tumah* may be overridden at the end because of *bemo'ado*, then by the same logic *Shabbat* should be overridden at the end as well.

For Rav Chisda: if *Shabbat* may be overridden at the end because of *bemo'ado*, then by the same logic *tumah* should be overridden at the end as well.

The challenge seems powerful: the two domains appear logically equivalent.

### Sofo Lo Ke'Tchilaso

Rava (or Rabbah) responds: *le-didi lo kashya* — for me there is no difficulty. His underlying principle is *sofo lo ke-tchilaso* — the end of the process is not treated like the beginning. Each stage is evaluated on its own independent merits, not by reference to the beginning.

Applying this: on Thursday, one faces a *tumah*-korban's remaining limbs. Evaluated independently, can one handle a *tamid* in a state of *tumah* today? Yes — *tumah* is always overridden for the *tamid*, regardless of which day it is. It is not a day-specific override. So even the end of the process, evaluated independently, involves a valid override of *tumah*. Hence one handles it on Thursday.

On *Shabbat* morning, one faces the remaining limbs of Friday's *korban*. Evaluated independently, can Friday's *korban* be handled on *Shabbat*? No — it is a Friday *korban*, and Friday *korbanot* never override *Shabbat*. Only *Shabbat* offerings override *Shabbat*. So the end of the process, evaluated independently, does not justify the override. Hence one does not handle it on *Shabbat*.

The asymmetry is thus entirely coherent: *tumah* is not day-specific and its override is available even the next morning, while *Shabbat* is day-specific and the override belongs only to today's *Shabbat* offerings, not to yesterday's work.

### Sofo Ke'Tchilaso

Rav Chisda responds from the opposite conceptual starting point: *sofo ke-tchilaso* — the end of the process is treated like the beginning. He does not evaluate each stage independently; instead, the beginning of the process carries the end along with it.

On this view, once a *korban* was legitimately commenced with a valid override, the conclusion of that process inherits the same override. This explains Rav Chisda's position regarding *Shabbat*: the *tamid* began on Friday with a full license, and *Shabbat* presents no real obstacle — the end, shlepped along by the beginning, also carries that license. But it does not explain, on first pass, why *tumah* is different. If the beginning overrides *tumah* and the end follows the beginning, why doesn't the end also override *tumah*?

### Hutra vs. Dechuya

The answer requires introducing a crucial conceptual distinction: *hutra* versus *dechuya*. When we say an obligation overrides a prohibition, there are two fundamentally different ways to understand what is happening. *Hutra* — the prohibition is simply not in force in this context; there is no violation at all. *Dechuya* — the prohibition remains, but we override it because a greater obligation demands it.

These are not merely terminological differences. Conceptually, if we see every instance of a *korban* brought on *Shabbat* as an ongoing violation that is merely permitted, we will naturally be more restrictive about what ancillary acts are permitted in its wake. If, on the other hand, we understand there to be no *Shabbat* violation at all in the *Mikdash* context, the latitude is correspondingly broader.

### Shabbat Hutra

According to Rav Chisda, *Shabbat de-hutra hi be-tzibur* — with respect to communal *korbanot*, *Shabbat* is *hutra*, not merely *dechuya*. When the *kohanim* are engaged in the *avodah* of *Shabbat*, they are not violating *Shabbat* and being permitted to do so — they are simply performing the *Shabbat* service, which is entirely consonant with *Shabbat*. There is no *chillul Shabbat* taking place, even conceptually. The *Mikdash* service on *Shabbat* is not an override of a prohibition; the prohibition simply does not apply in this domain.

Consequently, *sofo nami dachi* — the end of the process also carries through. Since there is no *Shabbat* tension to begin with, there is nothing for the end of the process to overcome. Even Friday's leftover *korban*, when it arrives on *Shabbat*, is within a domain where *Shabbat* is simply not an obstacle. *Sofo ke-tchilaso* works here because the beginning established that there is no *Shabbat* problem, and the end operates in that same problemless space.

### Tumah Dechuya

By contrast, *tumah de-dechuya hi be-tzibur* — with respect to *tumah*, the principle is *dechuya*, not *hutra*. When the community is *tamei* and we say the *korban* may be brought anyway, we are not saying there is no *tumah* problem. There is a problem — a *korban* is being brought in a state of *tumah* — and we are overriding it because the obligation to bring the *korban* in its time is paramount. This override requires ongoing justification.

And here is where *sofo ke-tchilaso* becomes unavailable for *tumah*: *techilato de-ikar kappara* — the beginning of the process, which constitutes the essential *kappara* — the blood-throwing, the primary acts of the *avodah* — that is what justifies the override. *Sofo de-lav ikar kappara* — the end, the remaining limbs, the mop-up work, is not the essential act of atonement. Without a sufficient justification to continue overriding *tumah*, one does not do so. The *dechuya* requires case-by-case justification, and the following morning that justification is simply absent.

The result is an elegant and internally consistent pair of positions. Rava holds that *Shabbat* cannot be overridden at the end because Friday *korbanot* never override *Shabbat*, period, and each stage must justify itself independently. Rav Chisda holds that *Shabbat* is overridden at the end because it was *hutra* at the start and the end inherits that license — but *tumah* is not overridden at the end because it was only *dechuya* at the start and the end cannot inherit an override that requires constant fresh justification. Both positions are logically coherent once their respective foundational principles are understood.

## Extinguishing Altar Fire

### Lo Tichbeh

The *gemara* now introduces a final new topic, which carries us to the end of the chapter. The Torah states in *Parshat Tzav*: *esh tamid tukad al ha-mizbe'ach lo tichbeh* — "a perpetual fire shall burn on the altar, it shall not go out." While the text is grammatically ambiguous — it could be read as a stative description ("it shall not go out") or as a command addressed to the *kohanim* ("you shall not extinguish it") — *Chazal* read it as the latter: an active prohibition against extinguishing any piece of the altar's fire. Even taking a single burning coal and extinguishing it constitutes a violation of this *lav* of *lo tichbeh*. The *Sefer HaChinuch* records this as commandment 132.

The question now raised is: what is the status of altar fire once it has been removed from the altar itself? The verse describes the fire as *tukad al ha-mizbe'ach* — burning *on* the altar. When a coal is taken from the altar to fill the *machtah* for *Yom Kippur*, or a flame is transferred to light the *menorah*, and one then extinguishes that fire while it is no longer on the altar — does the prohibition of *lo tichbeh* still apply?

### Identity After Detach

*Itmar*: it was stated regarding one who extinguishes the fire of the *machtah* or the fire of the *menorah* — *abaye amar chayav*, Abaye holds one is liable; *Rava amar patur*, Rava holds one is exempt.

The *gemara* immediately qualifies this debate by noting a point of consensus. *De-kibya be-reisho shel mizbe'ach* — if one extinguished it while still at the top of the altar, having just scooped the coals into the fire pan but not yet descended — *de-kulei alma lo peligi de-chayav* — everyone agrees one is liable. At the top of the altar, the coal is still unambiguously within the context of the *mizbe'ach*, and it retains its identity as *esh ha-mizbe'ach*.

The debate concerns the case where *de-achtei a-ra ve-tabya* — one brought it down to the ground and then extinguished it. *Abaye amar chayav* — Abaye says liable, *esh ha-mizbe'ach hu* — it is still the fire of the altar. The location is irrelevant; the identity of the fire is that of the altar, and that identity persists wherever it goes. Rava says *patur* — exempt. *Kivun de-nafka nafka* — once it has been detached, it has been detached. It no longer carries the identity of altar fire; it has become the fire of the *machtah* and assumes a new identity upon detachment.

The conceptual issue, as the *gemara* makes clear, is one of identity rather than merely location. Rava does not simply say the prohibition applies only when the fire is physically on the altar. Rather, he argues that the fire's identity has genuinely changed upon removal. It began as *esh ha-mizbe'ach*, but it is now *esh ha-machtah* — the fire of the fire pan.

### Rav Nachman's Case

This raises an apparent difficulty from a teaching of Rav Nachman. *Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabbah bar Bar Channah*: one who brings coals down from the altar and extinguishes them is liable. This *stam* case — where a *kohen* simply takes a coal, descends, and extinguishes it on the ground — seems to contradict Rava, who holds that once the coal is on the ground it has lost its altar identity and extinguishing it incurs no liability.

### Top of Altar Debate

The *gemara* asks *ke-man* — according to whom is Rav Nachman's ruling? Is it only consistent with Abaye? The *gemara* answers: *afilu tima ke-Rava* — it can even be consistent with Rava.

### New Purpose Identity Shi…

The distinction the *gemara* draws is this: *hosam lo iscalah le-mitzvato* — there, in Rav Nachman's case, the coal was not detached for a new purpose. A *kohen* simply took a coal off the altar and brought it to the ground without any *mitzvah* designation. That coal does not belong on the ground; it has no new role or identity. If you ask what should be done with it, the answer is: put it back. Its physical displacement does not change what it is. It is still, conceptually, the altar's coal — merely misplaced. Therefore, even according to Rava, extinguishing it renders one liable.

Here, however — in the case of the *machtah* — *iscalah le-mitzvah* — the coal was detached specifically for a new *mitzvah*, transferred to serve a new purpose in a new context. That act of purposeful detachment is what transforms its identity. It is no longer *esh ha-mizbe'ach* but rather the fire of the *machtah*, serving its new role. Accordingly, Rava holds that extinguishing it does not violate *lo tichbeh*.

There is thus a beautiful three-way conceptual distinction: a coal that belongs on the altar and remains there retains its full identity; a coal brought down purposelessly, though physically displaced, still retains its altar identity because it has assumed no new role; but a coal brought down for a designated new *mitzvah* acquires a new identity upon that purposeful detachment.

The *gemara* also presents an alternative version of the Abaye-Rava dispute in which their positions are reversed regarding which case is the point of disagreement: some say that when the coal is on the ground, everyone agrees it is exempt, and the debate concerns the case where the coal is still at the top of the altar but already inside the fire pan. Abaye holds that even then one is liable — the coal is still on the *mizbe'ach* and has not lost its identity. Rava holds that *kivun de-nafka nafka* — the moment the coal was separated from the altar's fire and placed into the fire pan, even while still physically at the top of the altar, its identity changed. The same principles govern both versions; only the precise location of the decisive moment differs.

### Tosafot on Swept Coals

*Tosafot* raises a further question worth noting. When the *kohen* transfers coals from one of the four *ma'arachot* to another — in the process of managing the multiple fires on top of the altar — some coals inevitably spill and fall to the ground. The *kohen* would then sweep them into the drainage channel, the *amah amah*. Why is the *kohen* not liable for extinguishing *esh ha-mizbe'ach* when these spilled coals go out?

*Tosafot* considers one answer: perhaps the *kohen* waits for them to extinguish on their own and only then sweeps them in. But *Tosafot* then offers a more conceptually interesting resolution. The coals that spill during this transfer were, from the very outset, destined to be waste. They are the overage — the portion that was always going to fall off and be discarded. A coal that is, from the moment of its detachment, already in the process of being discarded may well have already ceased to carry the identity of *esh ha-mizbe'ach* at the moment it hits the ground. Unlike a coal purposelessly removed, which still belongs back on the altar and has not assumed any new role, and unlike a coal removed for a designated *mitzvah*, which takes on a new *mitzvah* identity — a coal that is discarded waste may occupy a third category: it never had a new role and it no longer belongs to the old one. Once it hits the ground as designated refuse, perhaps everyone would agree that it has lost the identity of altar fire entirely.

This yields three distinct conceptual categories: fire retained on the altar in its original role; fire purposefully transferred to a new *mitzvah* context; fire that is simply discarded overage. The *sugya* as a whole is a remarkably rich meditation on the nature of identity — when objects carry their original character with them, when a new purpose transforms what they are, and at what precise moment that transformation takes effect.
