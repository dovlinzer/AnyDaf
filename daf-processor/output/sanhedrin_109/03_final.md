# Sanhedrin 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dor HaMabul & Ark

### Husband & Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִכֹּל הַבְּהֵמָה הַטְּהוֹרָה תִּקַּח לְךָ שִׁבְעָה שִׁבְעָה אִישׁ וְאִשְׁתּוֹ״, אִישׁוּת לִבְהֵמָה מִי אִית לַהּ? אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מֵאוֹתָם שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֶבְדָה בָּהֶם עֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"Of every kosher animal you shall take to you by sevens, husband and wife"** (Genesis 7:2), the Gemara asks: **Is there marriage for animals? Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** The reference is to **those** animals **with which** the **transgression** of relations with another species **was not performed.** Therefore, the Torah underscores that the animals that entered the ark were husband and wife.

The Gemara opens on 108b with a striking question about the *pasuk* describing the animals that entered the ark: *mi-kol ha-behemah ha-tehorah tikach lekha shiv'ah shiv'ah ish ve-ishto* — "of every clean animal you shall take seven and seven, a man and his wife." The question is obvious and almost comic: *ish ve-ishto le-behemah mi itei?* — since when do animals have marriages? The answer given by Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan is that the phrase does not mean they were actually married in any formal sense, but rather that the animals taken were those that had maintained a kind of fidelity — *me-otam she-lo ne-evdah bahem averah*, those upon whom no *averah* of interspecies copulation had been committed.

### Gate Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא יְדַע? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: שֶׁהֶעֱבִירָן לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה, כֹּל שֶׁהַתֵּיבָה קוֹלַטְתּוֹ – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֶבְדָה בָּהֶם עֲבֵירָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין הַתֵּיבָה קוֹלַטְתּוֹ – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בָּהּ עֲבֵירָה. רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר: מֵאוֹתָן הַבָּאִים מֵאֵילֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where did** Noah **know** which animals were not involved in that transgression? **Rav Ḥisda says: He passed them before the ark. All** animals **that the ark accepted, it was known that a transgression had not been performed with them. And any** animal **that the ark did not accept, it was known that a transgression had been performed with it. Rabbi Abbahu says:** Noah took onto the ark only **from those** animals **that came on their own,** as it appeared that they were sent from Heaven, and they were certainly fit for this purpose.

This immediately raises the question: *mi-na yada* — how was Noach supposed to know which animals qualified? Rav Chisda answers that Noach passed the animals before the *tevah*: *she-he-eviru lifnei ha-tevah*. Any animal that the *tevah* admitted — *kol she-hatevah koletato* — was thereby known to have been faithful, while any animal the *tevah* rejected was known to have sinned. It is not entirely clear from the Gemara's language whether this involved some kind of magnetic or divine pull, but the principle is that the ark itself served as the mechanism of selection.

Rabbi Abahu offers an alternative: *me-otam ha-ba-im me-eleihen* — the animals simply came of their own accord. This reading draws on the *pasuk* that says the animals *came to* Noach, using the active voice, implying that God arranged for them to present themselves rather than Noach having to select them.

### Ark Wood Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עֲשֵׂה לְךָ תֵּבַת עֲצֵי גֹפֶר״, מַאי גּוֹפֶר? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא: אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא, זוֹ מַבְלִיגָה. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: גּוּלְמֵישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **"Make you an ark of gopher wood"** (Genesis 6:14), the Gemara asks: **What** is **gopher** wood? **Rav Adda says** that **they say in the school of Rabbi Sheila: This** is wood from the ***mavliga*** tree; **and some say** that it is wood from the **willow [*gulamish*]** tree.

The *pasuk* instructs Noach to make the ark from *atzei gofer*. The Gemara asks: *mai gofer* — what is this wood? Rav Adda citing the school of Rabbi Shila says it refers to a type called *mavliga*; others say it is called *gulmish*. Both are understood to belong to the cedar family.

### Tzohar Light

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״צֹהַר תַּעֲשֶׂה לַתֵּבָה״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְנֹחַ, קְבַע בָּהּ אֲבָנִים טוֹבוֹת וּמַרְגָּלִיּוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּהְיוּ מְאִירוֹת לָכֶם כַּצׇּהֳרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **"A *tzohar* you shall make for the ark"** (Genesis 6:16), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Noah: Set precious stones and jewels in** the ark **so that they will shine for you as the afternoon [*tzohorayim*]** sun.

The *pasuk* instructs: *tzohar ta'aseh la-tevah* — make a *tzohar* for the ark. Rabbi Yochanan explains that God told Noach to set in it precious stones and gems — *avanim tovot u-margaliyot* — so that they would illuminate the interior like the midday sun: *kedei she-yihyu me'irot lakhem ke-tzohorayim*. As Rashi notes, there are two interpretations of *tzohar*: some understand it as a window, others as a luminous jewel. The Gemara here follows the jewel interpretation, with the sunlight presumably refracting off the stones to spread light throughout the ark's interior.

### Ark Design

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶל אַמָּה תְּכַלֶּנָּה מִלְמַעְלָה״, דִּבְהָכִי [הוּא] דְּקָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **"And to a cubit you shall finish it above"** (Genesis 6:16), the Gemara explains **that in that manner,** having been built wide at its base and narrow at its top, the ark would **stand** upright and would not capsize.

The *pasuk* continues: *ve-el amah tekhalenah milma'lah* — bring it to a cubit at the top. The Gemara explains: *de-be-hachi hu de-kaima* — that is simply good engineering. A tapered, angled roof that comes to a peak at the top distributes structural pressure in a way that makes the vessel more durable. The principle is not unlike the architectural logic of an arch or a dam built at an angle, where the incoming force actually strengthens the structure rather than breaking it.

### Three Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תַּחְתִּיִּם שְׁנִיִּם וּשְׁלִשִׁים תַּעֲשֶׂהָ״ – תָּנָא: תַּחְתִּיִּים לְזֶבֶל, אֶמְצָעִיִּים לַבְּהֵמָה, עֶלְיוֹנִים לָאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **"With lower, second and third stories shall you make it"** (Genesis 6:16), it was **taught** in a *baraita*: **The bottom** story was **for manure, the middle** story was **for animals, and the top** story was **for people.**

The verse also mandates three levels: *tachtiyim sheniyim u-shelishiyim ta'aseha*. A *tanna* teaches: *tachtiyim le-zevel* — the lowest level was designated for dung and excrement; *emtza'iyim le-behemah* — the middle level for the animals; *ve-elyonim le-adam* — and the upper level for the people. While this allots a great deal of space to the human inhabitants, the tapered design of the roof may account for the narrowing of that level.

### Raven Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְשַׁלַּח אֶת הָעֹרֵב״, אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: תְּשׁוּבָה נִיצַּחַת הֱשִׁיבוֹ עוֹרֵב לְנֹחַ. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבְּךָ שׂוֹנְאֵנִי וְאַתָּה שְׂנֵאתָנִי. רַבְּךָ שׂוֹנְאֵנִי – מִן הַטְּהוֹרִין שִׁבְעָה, מִן הַטְּמֵאִים שְׁנַיִם, וְאַתָּה שְׂנֵאתָנִי – שֶׁאַתָּה מַנִּיחַ מִמִּין שִׁבְעָה וְשׁוֹלֵחַ מִמִּין שְׁנַיִם. אִם פּוֹגֵעַ בִּי שַׂר חַמָּה אוֹ שַׂר צִנָּה, לֹא נִמְצָא עוֹלָם חָסֵר בְּרִיָּה אַחַת? אוֹ שֶׁמָּא לְאִשְׁתִּי אַתָּה צָרִיךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **"And he sent forth the raven,** which went forth to and fro, until the waters were dried up from the earth" (Genesis 8:7), **Reish Lakish says: The raven** provided **a convincing response to Noah;** when it did not wish to leave the ark the raven **said to him: Your Master,** God, **hates me, and you hate me. Your Master hates me,** as He commanded to take **from the kosher** species **seven** and **from the non-kosher** species **two. And you hate me, as you disregard** those **from the species of seven,** i.e., the kosher birds, **and** instead **dispatch** one **from the species of two,** i.e., the non-kosher birds. **If the angel of heat or the angel of cold harms me** and kills me, **will the world not be lacking one** species of **creature,** as there was only one pair of ravens? **Or perhaps** you are sending me because it is **my wife** that **you need,** in order to engage in intercourse with her.

The Gemara takes up the episode of the raven. Resh Lakish observes: *teshuvah nitzachit heshiv orev le-Noach* — the raven delivered to Noach a *teshuvah nitzachit*, an argument so compelling it could not be refuted. This is the same concept encountered earlier in the Gemara, where an argument is called *nitzachit* not necessarily because it is correct, but because no response can be mounted against it.

The raven's argument was as follows: *rabkha soneini ve-atah snatani* — your Master hates me, and you hate me. God took seven pairs of the clean animals and only two of the unclean, and now you are sending me — one of only two ravens in the world — on this mission. *Im pogea bi sar chama o sar tzina* — if the angel of heat or cold encounters me, *lo nimtza olam chasar briyah achat* — the world will be left lacking an entire species. Why not send from a species with seven, where the loss of one would still leave six? The raven then adds, somewhat absurdly: *o shema le-ishti atah tzarikh* — or perhaps you have designs on my wife?

### No Intercourse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: רָשָׁע! בַּמּוּתָּר לִי נֶאֱסַר לִי, בַּנֶּאֱסָר לִי לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** Noah **said to** the raven: **Wicked one!** If **with** the woman **who is** generally **permitted to me,** my wife, intercourse **is forbidden to me,** then **with** regard to domesticated and undomesticated animals, **which are** generally **forbidden to me,** is it **not all the more so** the case that they are forbidden to me?

Noach responds to the last point, calling the raven *rasha* — wicked. *B'mutar lach ne'esar* — even what is permitted to me, namely my own wife, is forbidden to me on the ark. *U'v'ne'esar li lo kol shekein* — certainly an animal, of the very type of transgression that brought about the destruction of the world through interspecies mixing, is unthinkable. Noach answers the silly point while leaving the stronger argument technically unanswered — a feature the Gemara implicitly acknowledges.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּנֶאֶסְרוּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבָאתָ אֶל הַתֵּבָה אַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ וְאִשְׁתְּךָ וּנְשֵׁי בָנֶיךָ אִתָּךְ״, וּכְתִיב: ״צֵא מִן הַתֵּבָה אַתָּה וְאִשְׁתְּךָ וּבָנֶיךָ וּנְשֵׁי בָנֶיךָ אִתָּךְ״. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִיכָּן (אָמְרוּ) שֶׁנֶּאֶסְרוּ בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that it was prohibited** for them to engage in intercourse while in the ark? The Gemara answers: It is derived from that **which is written: "And you shall come into the ark, you, and your sons, and your wife, and your sons' wives with you"** (Genesis 6:18); **and it is written: "Emerge from the ark, you and your wife, and your sons and your sons' wives with you"** (Genesis 8:16). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From here,** the Sages derived and **said that it was prohibited** to engage **in intercourse** while in the ark, as when Noah and his family entered, the husbands and wives were listed separately, and when they emerged, the husbands were listed with their wives.

The Gemara then asks: *u'mei'heichan ne'asru* — from where do we derive that intercourse was forbidden on the ark? The answer comes from a careful reading of two *pesukim*. When they entered the ark, the verse lists them in the order: *atah u-vanecha v'ishtecha u-nshei vanecha itacha* — you, your sons, your wife, and your sons' wives — men and women listed separately. But when they left, the verse reverses the pairings: *atah v'ishtecha u-vanecha u-nshei vanecha* — you and your wife, your sons and their wives — couples reunited. Rabbi Yochanan derives from this conspicuous structural difference that *neasru b'tashmish hamitah* — they were forbidden conjugal relations during the entire time on the ark.

### Three Violators

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה שִׁמְּשׁוּ בַּתֵּיבָה, וְכוּלָּם לָקוּ. כֶּלֶב וְעוֹרֵב וְחָם. כֶּלֶב נִקְשַׁר, עוֹרֵב רָק, חָם לָקָה בְּעוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Three** violated that directive and **engaged in intercourse** while **in the ark, and all of them were punished** for doing so. They are: The **dog, and** the **raven, and Ham,** son of Noah. The **dog** was punished in that it **is bound;** the **raven** was punished in that it **spits,** and **Ham was afflicted in** that **his skin** turned black.

A *baraita* teaches: *shlosha shimshu b'teivah* — three violated this prohibition on the ark, *ve-chulam laku* — and all three were punished. These three were: the dog, the raven, and Ham.

*Kelev nikshar* — the dog is now leashed, tied up, as its punishment. *Oreiv rak* — the raven spits; Rashi explains that this refers to the raven's mode of reproduction, though this explanation is difficult to understand as a punishment. *Ve-Cham laka b'oro* — Ham was smitten in his skin. Rashi identifies this as the origin of the dark-skinned descendants of Ham, specifically *sheyatza mimenu Kush*, the Ethiopians. While never made explicit in the *pasuk* itself, this has been a long-held reading within rabbinic tradition, though it carries the obvious difficulty that the Gemara uses the word *laka* — implying something negative — while any sensitivity to the topic requires care. One might also note that this provides a possible retrospective explanation for why Noach sent the raven first: having violated the prohibition, perhaps it was sent out of the ark as a form of consequence.

### Dove's Preference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְשַׁלַּח אֶת הַיּוֹנָה מֵאִתּוֹ לִרְאוֹת הֲקַלּוּ הַמַּיִם״. אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מִכָּאן שֶׁדִּירָתָן שֶׁל עוֹפוֹת טְהוֹרִים עִם הַצַּדִּיקִים.
>
> **Translation:** **"And he sent forth the dove from him, to see if the waters abated"** (Genesis 8:8), **Rabbi Yirmeya says: From here** it is derived **that the dwelling place of kosher birds** in the ark was **with the righteous** people, as the verse emphasizes that Noah dispatched the dove from his place.

The Gemara turns to the dove. Rabbi Yirmiyahu notes that the verse says *vayishlach et ha-yonah me-ito* — he sent the dove *from with him* — and derives from the word *me-ito* that *diratan shel ofot tehorim im ha-tzadikim* — the kosher birds prefer to dwell together with the righteous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהִנֵּה עֲלֵה זַיִת טָרָף בְּפִיהָ״. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָמְרָה יוֹנָה לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, יִהְיוּ מְזוֹנוֹתַי מְרוֹרִים כְּזַיִת וּמְסוּרִים בְּיָדְךָ, וְאַל יִהְיוּ מְתוּקִים כִּדְבַשׁ וּמְסוּרִים בְּיַד בָּשָׂר וָדָם. מַאי מַשְׁמַע דְּהַאי ״טָרָף״ לִישָּׁנָא דִּמְזוֹנֵי הוּא? דִּכְתִיב: ״הַטְרִיפֵנִי לֶחֶם חֻקִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **"And in her mouth was an olive branch plucked off [*taraf* ]"** (Genesis 8:11), **Rabbi Elazar says:** The **dove said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, may my sustenance be bitter as the olive and dependent on Your hands, and not sweet as honey and dependent on the hands of flesh and blood.** The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred that *taraf* is a term** that indicates **sustenance?** The Gemara answers: It is inferred from that **which is written: "Feed me [*hatrifeni*] with my allotted portion"** (Proverbs 30:8).

The verse then reports that the dove returned with *aleih zayit taraf b'fiha* — an olive leaf torn in its mouth. Rabbi Elazar expounds: the dove said before God: *Ribono Shel Olam, yihyu mezonosai merurin k'zayit umasurin b'yadcha* — let my food be as bitter as an olive but dependent upon You, *v'al yihyu metukin k'dvash umasurin b'yad basar v'dam* — rather than sweet as honey but dependent on human beings. The dove preferred the hardship of foraging freely under divine providence over the comfort of domestication and reliance on people. The words *taraf* meaning food and *b'fiha* meaning "in its mouth" are thus read as the dove's spoken declaration. The proof that *taraf* means food comes from the verse *hatrifeni lechem chuki* — provide me with the bread of my portion.

### Dove Metaphor

This *drasha* is best understood not merely as a general lesson about self-reliance, but as a metaphor for the Jewish people. The dove represents Israel: even when our sustenance in this world is bitter as an olive, we prefer a direct dependence on God to the easier path of dependence on foreign powers. That interpretation gives the entire passage its depth and purpose — the dove's declaration is Israel's declaration about its own condition in exile.

### Animals' Families

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לְמִשְׁפְּחֹתֵיהֶם יָצְאוּ מִן הַתֵּבָה״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״לְמִשְׁפְּחוֹתָם״ וְלֹא ״הֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** **"After their kinds [*lemishpeḥoteihem*], they emerged from the ark"** (Genesis 8:19), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: After their kinds [*lemishpeḥotam*]** the animals emerged, **but not them [*hem*]** themselves, as some of the animals that entered the ark died during that year and it was their descendants who emerged.

The Gemara notes that the verse states *le-mishpechoseihem yatze'u min ha-teivah* — they came out by their families. Rabbi Yochanan comments: *bi-mishpechosam ve-lo hem* — by their families but not themselves. The *Maharsha* acknowledges that no one is entirely certain how to parse this teaching. One possibility is that the verse emphasizes they emerged in family groupings rather than individually, but the precise import of the contrast remains unclear.

## Life on Ark

### Eliezer Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא: אָמַר לֵיהּ אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְשֵׁם רַבָּא, כְּתִיב: ״לְמִשְׁפְּחֹתֵיהֶם יָצְאוּ מִן הַתֵּבָה״. אַתּוּן הֵיכָן הֲוֵיתוּן? אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: צַעַר גָּדוֹל הָיָה לָנוּ בַּתֵּיבָה. בְּרִיָּה שֶׁדַּרְכָּהּ לְהַאֲכִילָהּ בַּיּוֹם – הֶאֱכַלְנוּהָ בַּיּוֹם, שֶׁדַּרְכָּהּ לְהַאֲכִילָהּ בַּלַּיְלָה – הֶאֱכַלְנוּהָ בַּלַּיְלָה. הַאי זְקִיתָא לָא הֲוָה יָדַע אַבָּא מָה אָכְלָה. יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה יָתֵיב וְקָא פָאלֵי רִמּוֹנָא, נְפַל תּוֹלַעְתָּא מִינַּהּ אֲכַלָה. מִיכָּן וְאֵילָךְ הֲוָה גָּבֵיל לַהּ חִיזְרָא, כִּי מַתְלַע אָכְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥana bar Bizna says: Eliezer,** servant of Abraham, **said to Shem the Great,** son of Noah: **It is written: "After their kinds, they emerged from the ark,"** indicating that the different types of animals were not intermingled while in the ark. **Where were you** and what did you do to care for them while they were in the ark? Shem **said to him: We** experienced **great suffering in the ark** caring for the animals. Where there was **a creature that one typically feeds during the day, we fed it during the day,** and where there was a creature **that one typically feeds at night, we fed it at night.** With regard to **that chameleon, my father did not know what it eats. One day,** my father **was sitting and peeling a pomegranate. A worm fell from it** and the chameleon **ate it. From that point forward** my father **would knead bran** with water, and **when it became overrun with worms,** the chameleon would **eat** it.

Rav Chana bar Bizna reports that Eliezer, the servant of Avraham, posed a question to Shem the elder — *Shem rabba* — invoking the verse *le-mishpechoseihem yatze'u min ha-teivah* as a springboard: *atun heichi avisun* — what was it like for you on the ark?

### Feeding Schedule

Shem replied: *tzar gadol haya lanu ba-teivah* — the hardship we endured on the ark was immense. Every creature had to be fed according to its natural cycle: *birya she-darkah le-ha'achilah ba-yom he'echelanu ba-yom, she-darkah le-ha'achilah ba-laylah he'echelanu ba-laylah* — daytime animals were fed by day, nighttime animals by night. The care was round the clock and without respite.

### Chameleon Diet

There was a particular difficulty: *hai ziksa lo hava yada abba mai achal* — father did not know what the chameleon ate. One day Noach was sitting and splitting open pomegranates — *hava yaseiv ve-pali rimona* — and a worm fell out of the pomegranate, which the chameleon promptly ate. From that point on, *hava gaval lei chizra* — he would knead or prepare for it some kind of porridge or mixture, and when it became wormy the chameleon would eat it.

### Lion's Fever

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְיָא – אֶישָּׁתָא זַנְתֵּיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב: לָא בְּצִיר מִשִּׁיתָּא וְלָא טְפֵי מִתְּרֵיסַר זָיְנָא אִישָּׁתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The lion, a fever sustained it,** since when it suffered from a fever, it did not need to eat; **as Rav said:** For **no fewer than six** days **and no more than twelve** days, **fever sustains** a person; he need not eat and is sustained from his own fats.

The lion presented a different challenge: how does one feed a lion on an ark full of the very animals one is trying to preserve? The answer was providential: *ishata zinsei* — God afflicted the lion with a fever for the duration of the voyage, so that it had no appetite for food. As Rav teaches: no less than six and no more than twelve months will a fever sustain and nourish — presumably meaning that the fever sustained the lion for the full twelve months of their time on the ark.

### Phoenix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּרְשִׁינָה – אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ אַבָּא דְּגָנֵי בְּסַפְנָא דְּתֵיבוֹתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא בָּעֵית מְזוֹנֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזֵיתָיךְ דַּהֲוֵת טְרִידַתְּ, אָמֵינָא לָא אֲצַעֲרָךְ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יְהֵא רַעֲוָא דְּלָא תְּמוּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאֹמַר עִם קִנִּי אֶגְוָע וְכַחוֹל אַרְבֶּה יָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the **phoenix [*avarshina*], my father found it lying in** its compartment on **the side of the ark. He said to** the bird: **Do you not want food?** The bird **said to him: I saw that you were busy,** and **I said I would not trouble you** by requesting food. Noah **said to** the bird: **May it be** God's **will that you shall not die,** and through that bird the verse was fulfilled, **as it is stated: "And I said, I shall die in my nest, and I shall multiply my days as the phoenix"** (Job 29:18).

There was also a creature called *orchina* — understood to be a phoenix or a bird of extraordinary longevity. Shem recounts that Noach found it resting quietly in one of the ark's compartments and asked: *lo ba'at me-zoney* — don't you need food? The bird replied: *chazisach de-hava trida* — I saw how busy you were with all the other animals, *amina lo atzrachach* — so I thought, I will not burden you. Noach blessed it: *yehei ra'ava de-lo samus* — may it be God's will that you never die. The verse cited in support is from *Iyov*: *ve-cha-chol arbeh yamim* — like the sand I will multiply days — understood as a reference to this immortal bird.

### Kings' Battle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנָה בַּר לֵוַאי: אֲמַר שֵׁם רַבָּא לֶאֱלִיעֶזֶר, כִּי אֲתוֹ עֲלַיְיכוּ מַלְכֵי מִזְרָח וּמַעֲרָב, אַתּוּן הֵיכִי עֲבְידִיתוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְאַבְרָהָם וְאוֹתְבֵיהּ מִיַּמִּינֵיהּ, וַהֲוָה שָׁדֵינַן עַפְרָא וְהָווּ חַרְבֵי, גִּילֵי וְהָווּ גִּירֵי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד נְאֻם ה׳ לַאדֹנִי שֵׁב לִימִינִי עַד אָשִׁית אֹיְבֶיךָ הֲדֹם לְרַגְלֶיךָ״, וּכְתִיב: ״מִי הֵעִיר מִמִּזְרָח צֶדֶק יִקְרָאֵהוּ לְרַגְלוֹ יִתֵּן לְפָנָיו גּוֹיִם וּמְלָכִים יַרְדְּ יִתֵּן כֶּעָפָר חַרְבּוֹ כְּקַשׁ נִדָּף קַשְׁתּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥana bar Leva'ei says** that **Shem the Great said to Eliezer,** servant of Abraham: **When** the four great **kings of the east and the west came upon you** to wage war with Abraham, **what did you do?** Eliezer **said to him: The Holy One, Blessed be He, brought Abraham and placed him to His right, and we would throw dust and it became swords,** and we threw **straw and it became arrows, as it is stated: "A Psalm of David. The Lord says to my master: Sit to My right, until I make your enemies your footstool"** (Psalms 110:1), **and it is written: "Who has raised up one from the east at whose steps victory attends? He gives nations before him, and makes him rule over kings; his sword makes them as the dust, his bow as driven straw"** (Isaiah 41:2).

Shem then turned the conversation around, addressing Eliezer directly: *ki asun aleichon melachim mizrach u-maarav* — when the kings of the east and west came against you, *atun heichi avisun* — how did you manage?

### Abraham Miracle

Eliezer answered: God brought Avraham, and me at his right hand, and the two of them would throw *afra* — dirt — at the enemy armies, and it would turn into swords; they threw chaff, and it turned into arrows. The *pasuk* from Tehillim is invoked: *Ne'um Hashem l'Adoni shev li-mini* — sit at my right hand. And the *pasuk* from Yeshayahu, familiar from the *haftarah* of *Lech Lecha*: *mi he'ir mi-mizrach* — who stirred from the east? — *yiten ke-afar charbo ke-kash nidaf kashto* — his sword is like dust, his bow like driven chaff. Thus the *pshat* of the *pasuk* itself encodes the miracle: the dust became swords, the chaff became arrows.

## Nachum & Crown

### Gam Zu Letovah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַחוּם אִישׁ גַּם זוֹ הֲוָה רְגִיל, דְּכֹל דַּהֲוָה סָלְקָא לֵיהּ אֲמַר: ״גַּם זוֹ לְטוֹבָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Abraham's miraculous weapons, the Gemara relates: **Naḥum of Gam Zo was accustomed that** in response to **any** circumstance **that arose in his** regard, he **would say: This too [*gam zo*] is for the best.**

The Gemara now introduces Nachum Ish Gamzu, about whom it was said that *kol d'havei sareik lei amar gam zu letovah* — whatever happened to him, he would say: this too is for the good. One might observe that the truest test of the *gam zu letovah* attitude is not when bad things are miraculously transformed into good ones, but precisely when the difficulty remains difficulty — and yet one maintains that disposition. Nevertheless, what follows is a story in which the bad indeed turns good, through a miracle tied directly to the very tradition Eliezer just described.

### Crown Mission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד בְּעוֹ [יִשְׂרָאֵל] לְשַׁדּוֹרֵי דּוֹרוֹן לְקֵיסָר. אָמְרִי: בַּהֲדֵי נַחוּם אִישׁ גַּם זוֹ, דִּמְלוּמָּד בְּנִסִּים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** One day the Jewish people sought to send a gift [*doron*] to the emperor. They said: With **Naḥum of Gam Zo, as he is experienced in miracles.**

One day the Jewish people wished to send a precious gift to the *Keysar* — the Roman emperor. *Nishdar behadei Nachum Ish Gamzu* — they decided to send it through Nachum Ish Gamzu, *de-melumad be-nissim hu* — because he was known to have miracles happen to him. This itself is a revealing detail: the confidence placed in Nachum was not because he was wise or diplomatic, but because his way of living — his *gam zu letovah* posture — made him someone through whom things worked out.

### Theft & Dirt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְטָא לְהָהוּא דִּיּוּרָא, בְּעָא לְמֵיבַת. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מַאי אִיכָּא בַּהֲדָךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: קָא מוֹבֵילְנָא כְּרָגָא לְקֵיסָר. קָמוּ בְּלֵילְיָא, שְׁרוֹנְהוּ לְסִיפְטֵיהּ, וּשְׁקַלוּ כֹּל דַּהֲוָה גַּבֵּיהּ וּמַלּוֹנְהוּ עַפְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When he reached a certain lodging, he sought to sleep** there. The residents of that lodging **said to him: What** do you **have with you?** Naḥum **said to them: I am taking the head tax to the emperor. They rose in the night, opened his chest and took everything that was in it, and** then **filled** the chest **with dirt.**

When Nachum arrived at an inn and disclosed that he was carrying a crown of precious jewels as tribute to the emperor, the innkeepers rose in the night, *sharinu le-sifteih* — opened his luggage — *ve-shaklu kol d'havei gabeih* — took everything inside — *u-malinu afra* — and refilled the box with dirt so that Nachum would be none the wiser. He continued on his journey, entirely unaware of the substitution.

### Caesar Suspects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְטָא לְהָתָם, אִישְׁתְּכַח עַפְרָא. אֲמַר: אַחוֹכֵי קָא מְחַיְּיכִי בִּי יְהוּדָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **When he arrived there,** in Rome, **earth was discovered** in the chest. The emperor **said: The Jews are mocking me** by giving me this gift.

When he arrived and presented the box to the *Keysar*, and the emperor opened it to find only dirt, he concluded: *achuchei kamachaichu bi Yehuda'ei* — the Jews are mocking me. Nachum was taken out to be executed.

### Eliyahu Appears

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַפְּקוּהּ לְמִקְטְלֵיהּ. אֲמַר: גַּם זוֹ לְטוֹבָה. אֲתָא אֵלִיָּהוּ וְאִידְּמִי לְהוּ כְּחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: דִּילְמָא הַאי עַפְרָא מֵעַפְרָא דְּאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ הוּא, דַּהֲוָה שָׁדֵי עַפְרָא הָווּ חַרְבֵי, גִּילֵי הָווּ גִּירֵי. בְּדוּק וְאַשְׁכַּחוּ הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **They took** Naḥum **out to kill him.** Naḥum **said: This too is for the best. Elijah** the prophet **came and appeared to them as one of** Naḥum's traveling party. Elijah **said to them: Perhaps this earth is from the earth of Abraham our forefather, who would throw dust** and **it became swords,** and who would throw **straw** and **it became arrows. They examined** the dust **and discovered** that it was indeed the dust of Abraham.

He said: *gam zu letovah* — this too is for the good. At that moment Eliyahu appeared — *admi l'hu*, disguised as one of the Roman officers — and suggested to them: *dilma hai afra me-afra d'Avraham Avinu hu* — perhaps this dirt is the very dirt of Avraham our patriarch, the miraculous dirt of which it was said *d'hava shadi afra havei charvei* — he threw dirt and it became swords — *gila havei girei* — he threw chaff and it became arrows.

### Dirt Tests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוָה מָחוֹזָא דְּלָא הֲווֹ קָא יָכְלִי לֵיהּ לְמִיכְבְּשֵׁיהּ. שְׁדוֹ מֵהָהוּא עַפְרָא עֲלֵיהּ וְכַבְשׁוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There was a province that** the Romans **were unable to conquer. They threw** some **of this earth upon** that province **and they conquered it.**

They tested it: *badku ve-ishtakach hachi*. There was a certain fortified city that had resisted all Roman conquest until that point. They threw some of this dirt at it, and the city fell. The miracle was confirmed.

### Gold Reward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַיְּילוּהּ לְבֵי גִנְזָא, אָמְרִי: שְׁקוֹל דְּנִיחָא לָךְ. מַלְּיֵיהּ לְסִיפְטָא דַּהֲבָא. כִּי הֲדַר אֲתָא, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ הָנָךְ דָּיּוֹרֵי: מַאי אַמְטֵית לְבֵי מַלְכָּא? אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאי דִּשְׁקַלִי מֵהָכָא אַמְטַאי לְהָתָם. שָׁקְלִי אִינְהוּ אַמְטוֹ לְהָתָם. קַטְלִינְהוּ לְהָנָךְ דָּיוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** In appreciation for the gift that Naḥum of Gam Zo had brought on behalf of the Jewish people, **they brought him into the treasury** and **said: Take** that **which is preferable to you. He filled his chest** with **gold. When he returned** to that lodging, **those residents said to him: What did you bring to the king's palace?** Naḥum **said to them: What I took from here, I brought to there.** The residents concluded that the earth with which they had filled the chest had miraculous properties. **They took** earth and **brought it to** the emperor. Once the Romans discovered that the earth was ineffective in battle, **they executed those residents.**

Nachum was brought into the royal treasury and told to take whatever he wished. He filled his box with gold. On his return journey, the innkeepers who had stolen the original contents asked him what he had brought to the emperor that merited such reward. He told them simply: *mai d'shakli me-hacha ampeiti l'hasam* — whatever I took from here is what I brought there. Taking him at his word, the innkeepers gathered dirt and brought it to the emperor as a gift — and were executed for their trouble. The story has the structure of a folk tale, reminiscent of the Chelm story in which a man brings back gold for his onions, and his neighbor, trying to replicate the success, brings garlic and receives only onions in return.

## Dor HaPlaga

### No Share

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דּוֹר הַפְּלַגָּה אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא וְכוּ׳. מַאי עֲבוּד?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the members of **the generation of the dispersion have no share in the World-to-Come.** The Gemara asks: **What** sin **did they perform?**

The Gemara moves from *Dor HaMabul* to *Dor HaPlaga* — the generation of the dispersion. The *Mishnah* has established that they too have no share in *Olam HaBa*. The question is: *mai avdu* — what exactly did they do wrong?

### Tower Motive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי שֵׁילָא: נִבְנֶה מִגְדָּל וְנַעֲלֶה לָרָקִיעַ, וְנַכֶּה אוֹתוֹ בְּקַרְדּוּמּוֹת כְּדֵי שֶׁיָּזוּבוּ מֵימָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Their sin is not explicitly delineated in the Torah. **The school of Rabbi Sheila say** that the builders of the Tower of Babel said: **We will build a tower and ascend to heaven, and we will strike it with axes so that its waters will flow.**

The school of Rabbi Shila offers the classic answer: *nivneh migdal ve-na'aleh la-rakia* — they sought to build a tower and ascend to heaven — *ve-nakeh oto be-kardumot* — and strike God with axes. The motivation, seemingly, was control over rain: since rain descends from the heavens and is the one resource entirely dependent on divine will, they wished to seize control of that mechanism.

### Valley Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחֲכוּ עֲלַהּ בְּמַעְרְבָא: אִם כֵּן, לִיבְנוֹ אַחַד טוּרָא!
>
> **Translation:** **They laughed at** this explanation **in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, and asked: **If** that was their objective, **let them build** a tower **on a mountain;** why did they build it specifically in a valley (see Genesis 11:2)?

The objection raised in Eretz Yisrael is sharp: *im ken yivnu echad be-tura* — if they wanted to reach heaven, why does the verse say *va-yisu bik'ah* — they traveled to a valley? One does not build a tower to heaven in a valley. This is acknowledged as a formidable question — a *kushiya nitzchit* of sorts — and the Gemara never provides a satisfying answer to it, even as it continues to work within the framework that reaching heaven was indeed the goal.

### Three Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אֶלָּא) אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אֶלְעָזָר: נֶחְלְקוּ לְשָׁלֹשׁ כִּיתּוֹת, אַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: נַעֲלֶה וְנֵשֵׁב שָׁם, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: נַעֲלֶה וְנַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאַחַת אוֹמֶרֶת: נַעֲלֶה וְנַעֲשֶׂה מִלְחָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yirmeya bar Elazar says: They divided into three factions; one said: Let us ascend** to the top of the tower **and dwell there. And one said: Let us ascend** to the top of the tower **and engage in idol worship. And one said: Let us ascend** to the top of the tower **and wage war.**

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Elazar resolves part of the difficulty by noting that the builders were not a monolithic group: *necheliku l'shalosh kitot* — they divided into three factions, each with distinct motivations. A unified construction project can harbor diverse intentions, and indeed the Gemara suggests that what united them on the surface concealed fundamental disagreements about purpose.

### Dwelling Group

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ שֶׁאוֹמֶרֶת נַעֲלֶה וְנֵשֵׁב שָׁם – ״הֱפִיצָם ה׳״,
>
> **Translation:** **That** faction **that said: Let us ascend** to the top of the tower **and dwell there, God dispersed them.**

The first group said: *na'aleh ve-neshev sham* — let us ascend and take up residence there. Whether to protect themselves from a future flood or to consider themselves somehow elevated beings, they did not seek to make war against God. Their punishment was accordingly proportional: *hepitzam Hashem* — they were scattered. Their offense was about where they wished to live, so the response addressed precisely that.

### War Group

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ שֶׁאוֹמֶרֶת נַעֲלֶה וְנַעֲשֶׂה מִלְחָמָה – נַעֲשׂוּ קוֹפִים וְרוּחוֹת וְשֵׁידִים וְלִילִין,
>
> **Translation:** **And that** faction **that said: Let us ascend** to the top of the tower **and wage war, became apes, and spirits, and demons, and female demons.**

The second group said: *na'aleh ve-na'aseh milchamah* — let us ascend and wage war against God. They wished to surpass the divine. Their punishment, as *midah keneged midah*, was to be made subhuman: *na'asu kofim ve-ruchot ve-shedim ve-lilin* — they were turned into monkeys, spirits, demons, and *lilin*. They aspired to divinity; they were stripped of their humanity — or more precisely, as one might add, they were made *be-tzelem Elohim* and chose to read that image as actual divinity, so God removed even that *tzelem* from them.

### Idolatry Group

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ שֶׁאוֹמֶרֶת נַעֲלֶה וְנַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – ״כִּי שָׁם בָּלַל ה׳ שְׂפַת כׇּל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **that** faction **that said: Let us ascend** to the top of the tower **and engage in idol worship,** it is written: **"Because there the Lord confounded the language of all the earth"** (Genesis 11:9).

The third group said: *na'aleh ve-na'avdu avodah zarah* — let us ascend and worship idols. Their punishment was *balal Hashem sephat kol ha'aretz* — God confounded the language of all the earth. The *midah keneged midah* here is not immediately obvious. Rashi explains that the confusion of language prevented them from coordinating their *avodah zarah*. A deeper reading would suggest that this group's sin was the worship of intermediaries — not outright denial of God but the insertion of celestial middlemen between Israel and the divine, which distorts and corrupts the very concept of monotheism. In response, God took the means of communication — itself a conduit — and made it an obstacle. Just as they turned access into obstruction by interposing intermediaries, God turned language from a vehicle of unity into a barrier of confusion.

### Natan View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּם לְשֵׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה נִתְכַּוְּונוּ. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״נַעֲשֶׂה לָנוּ שֵׁם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וְשֵׁם אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים לֹא תַזְכִּירוּ״. מָה לְהַלָּן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אַף כָּאן עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says: All of** those factions **intended** to build the tower **for the sake of idol worship. It is written here: "And let us make a name for us"** (Genesis 11:4), **and it is written there: "And make no mention of the name of the other gods"** (Exodus 23:13). **Just as there,** the connotation of "name" **is idol worship, so too here,** the connotation of "name" **is idol worship.**

A *baraita* adds the view of Rabbi Natan: *kulam le-shem avodat kochavim nitkavnu* — all three groups intended idolatry. He derives this through a *gezerah shavah*: the verse here says *ve-na'aseh lanu shem*, and elsewhere the Torah says *ve-shem Elohim acherim lo tazkiru* — the word *shem* links both passages to idolatry.

### Tower Fate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִגְדָּל שְׁלִישׁ נִשְׂרַף, שְׁלִישׁ נִבְלַע, שְׁלִישׁ קַיָּים. אָמַר רַב: אֲוִיר מִגְדָּל מְשַׁכֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The** uppermost **third** of the **tower was burned,** the lowermost **third** of the tower **was swallowed** into the earth, and the middle **third remained** intact. **Rav says: The atmosphere of the tower causes forgetfulness;** anyone who goes there forgets what he has learned.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches regarding the fate of the tower itself: *migdal shlish nisraf* — one third was burned; *ve-shlish nivla* — one third was swallowed into the ground; *ve-shlish kayam* — one third still stands and can theoretically be visited.

### Tower Hidden

If a third of it remains, why has no one found it? Rav answers: *avir migdal meshake'ach* — the air around the tower causes forgetfulness. Anyone who comes near it and then departs cannot remember where they were or what they saw. It is a kind of divinely imposed amnesia that renders the surviving structure effectively invisible to human experience.

### Bad Omens

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: בָּבֶל וּבוֹרְסִיף סִימָן רַע לַתּוֹרָה. מַאי בּוֹרְסִיף? אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: בּוֹר שָׁאפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** As a result of the building of the tower, forgetting was introduced into the world. **Rav Yosef says: Babylonia and** the adjacent place, **Bursif, are** each **a bad omen for Torah,** i.e., they cause one to forget his knowledge. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **Bursif? Rabbi Asi says:** It is an abbreviation of **empty pit [*bor shafi*].**

Rav Yosef then notes that *Bavel u-Borsif* — Babylon and its neighboring locale of Borsif — are *siman ra le-Torah*. Since the tower stood there and caused forgetting, the very air of that region is associated with the loss of Torah memory. This is deeply ironic, of course, given that Babylon ultimately became the center of the greatest Torah tradition in the diaspora. Rav Assi interprets *Borsif* as *bor shapi* — a pit that draws out, that pulls away — presumably a reference to the sapping of Torah retention.

## Anshei Sedom

### Sedom No Share

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַנְשֵׁי סְדוֹם אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא וְכוּ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **The people of Sodom have no share in the World-to-Come.**

Having treated *Dor HaMabul* and *Dor HaPlaga*, the Gemara turns to *Anshei Sedom*. Notably, throughout this entire discussion the Gemara has not paused to explain the underlying logic of the hierarchy articulated in the *Mishnah* — why some groups are *omdin ba-din* but lack *Olam HaBa*, why others receive neither, and what distinguishes one category from another. The Gemara simply moves forward with what each group did and what their fate is.

### Wicked & Sinners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַנְשֵׁי סְדוֹם אֵין לָהֶן חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַנְשֵׁי סְדֹם רָעִים וְחַטָּאִים לַה׳ מְאֹד״. ״רָעִים״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וְחַטָּאִים״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: The people of Sodom have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the men of Sodom were wicked and sinners before the Lord exceedingly"** (Genesis 13:13). **"Wicked"** indicates **in this world; "and sinners"** indicates **for the World-to-Come.**

The verse in Bereishit describes the people of Sodom as *ra'im ve-chata'im la-Hashem me'od* — wicked and sinners before God greatly. The Gemara expounds the double language.

### Two Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: רָעִים בְּגוּפָן, וְחַטָּאִים בְּמָמוֹנָם. רָעִים בְּגוּפָן – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאֵיךְ אֶעֱשֶׂה הָרָעָה הַגְּדֹלָה הַזֹּאת וְחָטָאתִי לֵאלֹהִים״, וְחַטָּאִים בְּמָמוֹנָם – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה בְךָ חֵטְא״. ״לַה׳״ – זוֹ בִּרְכַּת הַשֵּׁם. ״מְאֹד״ – שֶׁמִּתְכַּוְּונִים וְחוֹטְאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says: "Wicked"** is referring to sins they committed **with their bodies; "and sinners"** is referring to sins they committed **with their money. "Wicked"** is referring to sins they committed **with their bodies, as it is written** with regard to Joseph and the wife of Potiphar: **"And how can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God"** (Genesis 39:9). **"And sinners"** is referring to sins they committed **with their money, as it is written:** "And your eye is wicked against your poor brother, and you give him nothing…**for it shall be reckoned to you as a sin"** (Deuteronomy 15:9). **"Before the Lord"; this** is referring to **blessing,** a euphemism for cursing, **God. "Exceedingly"** means **that they had intent and sinned** and did not sin unwittingly or driven by lust.

Rav Yehuda interprets: *ra'im be-gufam* — wicked with their bodies; *ve-chata'im be-mamonam* — and sinners with their money. An alternative *baraita* inverts the mapping of the words but preserves the same two categories: bodily transgression and monetary transgression.

The bodily sin is identified through the *pasuk* spoken by Yosef when he refused the wife of Potifar: *ve-eich e'eseh ha-ra'ah ha-gedolah ha-zot ve-chatati l'Elohim* — and how shall I commit this great evil and sin against God? That *pasuk* uses the root *ra'ah* for sexual transgression. The monetary sin is derived from *ve-hayah becha chet* — there will be a sin upon you — which the Torah uses in the context of failing to give to the poor. These two sins — sexual immorality and neglect of the poor — are in fact the same two identified as the core failures of *Dor HaMabul*, where both *chamas* and *hishchit kol basar* were central to their *gezar din*.

The verse continues: *la-Hashem* — against God — which the Gemara reads as *birchat Hashem*, blasphemy. And *me'od* — greatly — is interpreted either as doing these sins with full deliberate intent rather than through *yetzer ha-ra*, or in the *baraita* as *shefichus damim* — the shedding of blood — supported by the verse about Menashe: *gam naki shafach Menashe harbeh me'od*.

### Sedom Abundance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אַנְשֵׁי סְדוֹם לֹא נִתְגָּאוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל טוֹבָה שֶׁהִשְׁפִּיעַ לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וּמָה כְּתִיב בָּהֶם? ״אֶרֶץ מִמֶּנָּה יֵצֵא לָחֶם וְתַחְתֶּיהָ נֶהְפַּךְ כְּמוֹ אֵשׁ. מְקוֹם סַפִּיר אֲבָנֶיהָ וְעַפְרֹת זָהָב לוֹ. נָתִיב לֹא יְדָעוֹ עָיִט וְלֹא שְׁזָפַתּוּ עֵין אַיָּה. לֹא הִדְרִיכוּהוּ בְנֵי שָׁחַץ לֹא עָדָה עָלָיו שָׁחַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: The people of Sodom became haughty** and sinned **due only to the** excessive **goodness that the Holy One, Blessed be He, bestowed upon them. And what is written concerning them,** indicating that goodness? **"As for the earth, out of it comes bread, and underneath it is turned up as it were by fire. Its stones are the place of sapphires, and it has dust of gold. That path no bird of prey knows, neither has the falcon's eye seen it. The proud beasts have not trodden it, nor has the lion passed thereby"** (Job 28:5–8). The reference is to the city of Sodom, which was later overturned, as it is stated thereafter: "He puts forth His hand upon the flinty rock; He overturns the mountains by the roots" (Job 28:9).

The Gemara then explains the origin of Sodom's wickedness in a way that exactly parallels the Gemara's earlier treatment of *Dor HaMabul*: *lo nisga'u ela bishvil tovah she-hishpia lahem ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — they became arrogant only because of all the goodness God lavished upon them. The verses from *Iyov* are marshaled to describe this abundance: *eretz mimena yatza lechem* — bread came forth from the land; *ve-tachteha nehepach ke-mo esh* — beneath it turned like fire; *makom sapir avaneha ve-afros zahav lo* — its stones were like sapphires and it had gold dust. It was a land of extraordinary natural wealth.

The verse continues: *nasiv lo yedau ayit ve-lo shezafaso ein ayah* — the path was unknown to the vulture and unseen by the eye of the hawk — understood metaphorically to mean that no bandits or enemies ever attacked this blessed land. *Lo hidrichuhu benei shachatz* — the sons of pride did not trample it; *lo adah alav shachal* — the lion did not gather against it. The land was uniquely protected and bountiful.

### Prevent Strangers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ: וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁ״אֶרֶץ מִמֶּנָּה יֵצֵא לָחֶם וְעַפְרֹת זָהָב לוֹ״, לָמָּה לָנוּ עוֹבְרֵי דְרָכִים? שֶׁאֵין בָּאִים אֵלֵינוּ אֶלָּא לְחַסְּרֵינוּ [מִמָּמוֹנֵנוּ]. בּוֹאוּ וּנְשַׁכַּח תּוֹרַת רֶגֶל מֵאַרְצֵנוּ! שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פָּרַץ נַחַל מֵעִם גָּר הַנִּשְׁכָּחִים מִנִּי רָגֶל דַּלּוּ מֵאֱנוֹשׁ נָעוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The people of Sodom **said: Since** we live in **a land from which bread comes and has the dust of gold,** we have everything that we need. **Why do we need travelers, as they come only to divest us of our property? Come, let us cause the** proper **treatment of travelers to be forgotten from our land, as it is stated: "He breaks open a watercourse in a place far from inhabitants, forgotten by pedestrians, they are dried up, they have moved away from men"** (Job 28:4).

Their response to this abundance was: *lama lanu ovrei drachim* — why do we need passersby? *She'ein ba'im etzlenu ela lachasrenu mi-mamonenu* — they only come to take from our wealth. *Bo'u ve-nishkach torat regel me'artzenu* — let us eliminate the right of passage from our land.

The *pasuk* from *Iyov* is cited: *peretz nachal me'am gar ha-nishkachim mini regel* — they burst forth a river from the people who dwell there, causing passersby to be forgotten from their land. The interpretation offered is that God responded by diverting a stream of fire and brimstone from the heavenly course — *peretz nachal* as the divine punishment directed against those who made the foot-traveler forgotten in their midst.

### Leaning Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״עַד אָנָה תְּהוֹתְתוּ עַל אִישׁ תְּרׇצְּחוּ כֻלְּכֶם כְּקִיר נָטוּי גָּדֵר הַדְּחוּיָה״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיוּ נוֹתְנִים עֵינֵיהֶן בְּבַעֲלֵי מָמוֹן, וּמוֹשִׁיבִים אוֹתוֹ אֵצֶל קִיר נָטוּי, וְדוֹחִים אוֹתוֹ עָלָיו, וּבָאִים וְנוֹטְלִים אֶת מָמוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "How long will you seek to overwhelm a man? You will all be murdered like a leaning wall or a tottering fence"** (Psalms 62:4)? This **teaches that** the people of Sodom **set their sights on property owners.** They would take one **and place him alongside an inclined,** flimsy **wall** that was about to fall, **and push it upon him** to kill him, **and** then **they** would **come and take his property.**

Rabba expounds a verse in Tehillim: *ad ana tehotetu al ish teratzechu chulchem ke-kir natui gader ha-dechuyah* — how long will you oppress a person, will you all murder like a leaning wall, like a pushed-over fence? The *Midrash* is: the people of Sodom would identify wealthy individuals, invite them to walk beside a leaning wall, and then push the wall onto them — *ve-dochin oto alav* — and after killing them they would raid their property. The desire for wealth, which one might think would be satisfied given their extraordinary abundance, instead became self-consuming — generating covetousness and ultimately murder.

### Tunnels & Perfume

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״חָתַר בַּחֹשֶׁךְ בָּתִּים יוֹמָם חִתְּמוּ לָמוֹ לֹא יָדְעוּ אוֹר״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיוּ נוֹתְנִים עֵינֵיהֶם בְּבַעֲלֵי מָמוֹן, וּמַפְקִידִים אֶצְלוֹ אֲפַרְסְמוֹן, וּמַנִּיחִים אוֹתוֹ בְּבֵית גִּנְזֵיהֶם. לָעֶרֶב בָּאִים וּמְרִיחִין אוֹתוֹ כְּכֶלֶב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יָשׁוּבוּ לָעֶרֶב יֶהֱמוּ כַכָּלֶב וִיסוֹבְבוּ עִיר״, וּבָאִים וְחוֹתְרִים שָׁם וְנוֹטְלִים אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "In the dark they dig through houses; by day they shut themselves up; they know not the light"** (Job 24:16)? This **teaches that they would set their sights on property owners.** They would take one **and** they would **give him balsam,** whose smell diffuses, **and** the property owner would **place it in his treasury. In the evening,** the people of Sodom **would come and sniff it** out **like a dog** and discover the location of the property owner's treasury, **as it is stated: "They return at evening; they howl like a dog, and go round about the city"** (Psalms 59:7). **And** after discovering the location **they would come and dig there, and they would take that property.**

Rabba expounds a second verse: *chatar ba-choshech batim* — they dig through houses in the darkness — *yomam chatmu lamo* — in the daytime they seal themselves in. The Gemara explains: the people of Sodom would identify wealthy homeowners and approach them with a seemingly generous request — *ha-yu notnin eineihem be-ba'alei mamon u-mafkidim etzlo afarsamon* — would you mind storing some of our precious *afarsamon* perfume in your treasure house? The wealthy person obliged. Then at night — *ba'in u-merichim oto ke-kelev* — they would come and sniff it out like a dog, following the scent of the perfume precisely to the storehouse, *ve-bain ve-chotrin sham* — and dig tunnels right to it — *ve-notlin et mamono* — and steal the contents. The verse from Tehillim is cited: *yashuvu la'erev yehemu che-kelev vi-sovvu ir* — they return at evening, they howl like dogs and circle the city.

### Tzipori 300

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּצִיפּוֹרִי. אִחַתְרַין הָהִיא לֵילְיָא תְּלָת מְאָה מַחְתְּרָתָא בְּצִיפּוֹרִי. אֲתוֹ וְקָא מְצַעֲרִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: יְהַבְיתְּ אוּרְחֵיהּ לְגַנָּבֵי! אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִי הֲוָה יָדַעְנָא דְּאַתּוּ גַּנָּבֵי? כִּי קָא נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, שָׁפְעִי מַרְזְבֵי דְצִיפּוֹרִי דְּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei taught in Tzippori** the methods of theft employed in Sodom. **That night three hundred tunnels were excavated in Tzippori** in order to employ those methods. Homeowners **came and harassed him; they said to him: You have given a way for thieves** to steal. Rabbi Yosei **said to them: Did I know that thieves would come** as a result of my lecture? The Gemara relates: **When Rabbi Yosei died, the gutters of Tzippori** miraculously **overflowed** with **blood** as a sign of his death.

Rabbi Yose expounded this *drasha* publicly in Tzipori, recounting how Sodom's thieves would deposit perfume in wealthy homes to mark them for nighttime tunnel-breaking. That very night, three hundred people in Tzipori attempted to replicate the technique and tunneled into homes across the city. The townspeople confronted Rabbi Yose angrily: *yahavitu orcha le-ganve* — you have given a method to thieves. He replied: *mi hava yadana de-atun ganve* — I did not know I was delivering my *shiur* to a room full of thieves. The episode raises a genuine question: what was novel about the technique? Tunneling was not new; presumably the *chiddush* was the use of perfume as a locator. The number three hundred is likely an exaggeration, but the anecdote points to the real dangers of public exposition of criminal methodology, even in the context of Torah study.

### Yose's Death

The Gemara then notes: *ki nach nafshi de-Rabbi Yose* — when Rabbi Yose died — *shafu marzeiva de-Tzipori dama* — the gutters of Tzipori ran with blood. The image is arresting. On one level it speaks to the magnitude of communal mourning for a great sage. On another level, following immediately upon a story about theft, murder, and the abuse of wealth, it functions as a subtle commentary on the city of Tzipori itself — a wealthy, cosmopolitan city not unlike the Rome whose corruptions the Gemara has been cataloguing. The blood flowing in the streets of Tzipori upon the death of its great teacher may be the Gemara's quiet critique of what that city stood to lose.

## Sedom Laws

### Ox Tax

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: דְּאִית לֵיהּ חַד תּוֹרָא – מַרְעֵי חַד יוֹמָא, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ – לִירְעֵי תְּרֵי יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The people of Sodom **would say:** Anyone **who has one ox shall herd** the city's oxen **for one day.** Anyone **who does not have** any oxen **shall herd** the city's oxen **for two days.**

The Gemara now catalogs the perverse legal and social structures of Sodom. Their communal regulation for watching the town's oxen ran as follows: *de-is lei tora marei chad yoma, de-leis lei marei trei yoma* — whoever owns an ox watches for one day; whoever owns no ox watches for two days. The logic is inverted from anything fair: the burden falls heaviest on those who have least stake in the outcome. This is a paradigm of regressive social policy, designed to oppress the poor under the guise of civic duty.

### Orphan Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יַתְמָא בַּר אַרְמַלְתָּא, יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ תּוֹרֵי לְמִרְעֵיה. אֲזַל, שַׁקְלִינְהוּ וְקַטְלִינְהוּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: דְּאִית לֵיהּ תּוֹרָא – נִשְׁקוֹל חַד מַשְׁכָּא, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ תּוֹרָא – נִשְׁקוֹל תְּרֵי מַשְׁכֵּי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לְהוּ: סוֹף דִּינָא כִּתְחִילַּת דִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They gave oxen to a certain orphan, son of a widow, to herd. He went** and **took them and killed them.** The orphan **said** to the people of Sodom: **Let** anyone **who has one ox take one hide** and **let** anyone **who does not have an ox take two hides.** The people of Sodom **said to** the orphan: **What** is the reason for **this?** The orphan **said to them: The ultimate rule is parallel to the initial rule;** just as the initial rule was that anyone with an ox watches one day and anyone without watches two days, so too the final rule is that anyone with an ox takes one hide and anyone without takes two.

Into this system stepped an orphan, the son of a widow — someone at the very bottom of Sodom's social hierarchy — who was given the oxen to watch. He proceeded to slaughter them all, and then announced his own counter-regulation: *de-is lei tora nishkal chad mashka, de-leis lei nishkal trei mashkei* — whoever owns an ox takes one hide; whoever owns no ox takes two hides. The Sodomites protested: *mai hai* — what is this absurdity? He replied: *sof dina ke-techilat dina* — the end of the law mirrors the beginning of the law. Your own principle, applied consistently, yields exactly this. They had no answer.

### Bridge Toll

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּעָבַר בְּמַבָּרָא – נִיתֵּיב חַד זוּזָא, דְּלָא עָבַר בְּמַבָּרָא – נִיתֵּיב תְּרֵי. דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ דָּרָא דְּלִבְנֵי, אָתֵי כֹּל חַד וְחַד שָׁקֵיל חֲדָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא חֲדָא דִּשְׁקַלִי. דַּהֲוָה שָׁדֵי תּוּמֵי אוֹ שַׁמְכֵי, אֲתוֹ כֹּל חַד וְחַד שָׁקֵיל חֲדָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא חֲדָא דִּשְׁקַלִי.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, they declared in Sodom: **Let** one **who crosses on a ferry give one dinar** as payment; **let** one **who does not cross on a ferry,** but walks in the river, **give two** dinars. In addition, when there was anyone **who had a row of bricks, each and every one** of the people of Sodom **would** come and **take one** brick and **say to him: I am taking** only **one,** and you are certainly not particular about so inconsequential an item, and they would do this until none remained. And when there was anyone **who would cast garlic or onions** to dry, **each and every** one of the people of Sodom **would** come and **take one** and **say to him: I took** only **one** garlic or onion, and they would do this until none remained.

The taxation of passage was similarly structured to extract maximum payment from those least able to resist. Anyone who crossed by the bridge paid one *zuz*. Anyone who crossed through the river — to avoid the toll — paid two *zuz*. There was no way to pass through or around Sodom without paying, and the attempt to avoid one fee simply generated a higher one. Similarly, if a person had a pile of bricks or a spread of garlic and onions to sell, every passerby would help themselves to one item — *ana chada de-shakeil, I'm only taking one* — until by day's end the entire stock was gone, with each individual thief technically having taken almost nothing.

### Four Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבַּע דַּיָּינֵי הָיוּ בִּסְדוֹם, שַׁקְרַאי וְשַׁקְרוֹרַאי זַיְיפַי וּמַצְלֵי דִּינָא.
>
> **Translation:** **There were four judges in Sodom** and they were named for their actions: **Shakrai,** meaning liar, **and Shakrurai,** habitual liar, **Zayfai,** forger, **and Matzlei Dina,** perverter of justice.

The four judges of Sodom bore names that announced their function: *Shakrai* — Liar; *Shakrurai* — Great Liar; *Zaifi* — Forger; *Matzli Dina* — Perverter of Justice. Their court operated in deliberate inversion of every principle of justice.

### Wife & Donkey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמָחֵי לֵיהּ לְאִיתְּתָא דְּחַבְרֵיהּ וּמַפְּלָא (לֵיהּ), אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: יַהֲבַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ דְּנִיעַבְּרַהּ נִיהֲלָיךְ. דְּפָסֵיק לֵיהּ לְאוּדְנָא דַּחֲמָרָא דְּחַבְרֵיהּ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הֲבַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ עַד דְּקָדְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** These were the judgments that they rendered: In a case of **one who strikes the wife of another and causes her to miscarry, they would say to** the woman's husband: **Give** the woman **to** the **one** who struck her, **so that she will be impregnated for you** again. In a case of one **who severed the ear of another's donkey, they would say to** the owner of the donkey: **Give** the donkey to the one who caused the damage, **until** the ear **grows** back.

If someone struck his neighbor's pregnant wife and caused her to miscarry, the judges would rule: *hav niyalah* — give your wife to him, so he can impregnate her and thereby restore what was lost. If someone cut off the ear of his neighbor's donkey, the ruling was: give your donkey to the person whose donkey you maimed, *ad di-ka'atya* — until the ear grows back, during which time the maimer gets use of the whole animal.

### Launderer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּפָדַע לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ, אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: הַב לֵיהּ אַגְרָא דִּשְׁקַל לָךְ דְּמָא. דְּעָבַר בְּמַבָּרָא – יְהֵיב אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי, דְּעָבַר בְּמַיָּא – יָהֵיב תְּמָנְיָא זוּזֵי. זִימְנָא חֲדָא אֲתָא הָהוּא כּוֹבֵס, אִיקְּלַע לְהָתָם. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הַב אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי. אָמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא בְּמַיָּא עֲבַרִי. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, הַב תְּמָנְיָא דַּעֲבַרְתְּ בְּמַיָּא. לָא יְהַב. פַּדְיוּהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּדַיָּינָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לֵיהּ אַגְרָא דִּשְׁקַל לָךְ דְּמָא, וּתְמָנְיָא זוּזֵי דַּעֲבַרְתְּ בְּמַיָּא. אֱלִיעֶזֶר עֶבֶד אַבְרָהָם אִיתְרְמִי הָתָם, פַּדְיוּהּי. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דַּיָּינָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לֵיהּ אַגְרָא דִּשְׁקַל לָךְ דְּמָא. שְׁקַל גְּלָלָא, פַּדְיֵוהּ אִיהוּ לְדַיָּינָא. אֲמַר: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַגְרָא דִּנְפַק לִי מִינָּךְ, הַב נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְהַאי, וְזוּזֵי דִּידִי כִּדְקָיְימִי קָיְימִי.
>
> **Translation:** In a case of one **who wounds another, they would say** to the injured party: **Give** the one who wounded you **a fee, as he let your blood.** And they instituted an ordinance: One **who crossed** the river **on a ferry gives four dinars,** and one **who crossed** the river **in the water gives eight dinars. One time a certain launderer came** and **arrived there.** The people of Sodom **said to him: Give four dinars** as payment for the ferry. **He said to them: I crossed in the water. They said to him: If so, give eight** dinars, **as you crossed in the water. He did not give** the payment, and they struck him and **wounded him. He came before the judge** to seek compensation. The judge **said to him: Give** your assailant **a fee, as he let your blood, and eight dinars, as you crossed** the river **in the water. Eliezer, servant of Abraham, happened** to come **there,** and **they wounded him. He came before the judge** to seek compensation. The judge **said to him: Give** your assailant **a fee, as he let your blood. He took a stone** and **he wounded the judge.** The judge **said: What** is **this?** Eliezer **said to him: The fee that is** to be **paid to me by you, give it to that** person who wounded me, **and my money will remain where it remains.**

If someone wounded his neighbor and caused bleeding, the judges ruled that the wounded party owed the assailant a medical fee — *agra de-shakeil lach dama* — because he had performed a service by letting blood, as a professional bloodletter would. The launderer who refused to pay the bridge toll, was beaten for his refusal, came before the judges, and was told he owed eight *zuzei* for having crossed through the water — plus the bloodletting fee to the man who beat him. Eliezer, the servant of Avraham, happened to pass through Sodom on one of his journeys and was similarly beaten. He came before the judge and proceeded to smash a stone on the judge's head, then coolly informed him: the bloodletting fee you now owe me — pay it to this man who beat me. And my own money I shall keep. With that, he bested the entire system at its own game.

## Eliezer Sodom

### Procrustes Bed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָוְיָא לְהוּ פּוּרְיְיתָא דַּהֲווֹ מַגְנִי עֲלַהּ אוֹרְחִין. כִּי מַאֲרֵיךְ – גָּיְיזִי לֵיהּ, כִּי גוּץ – מָתְחִין לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the sins of the people of Sodom: **They had beds on which they would lay their guests; when** a guest **was longer** than the bed **they** would **cut him,** and **when** a guest **was shorter** than the bed **they would stretch him.**

Sodom maintained a guest bed for travelers — *mi-ttat Sodom* — which operated on a grotesque principle of uniformity. If a visitor was too long for the bed, *gayzei lei* — they would cut off his legs to make him fit. If he was too short, *matchilei* — they would stretch him until he fit. The phrase *mittat Sodom* has entered the language as the paradigm for forcing others to conform to one's own structure, regardless of the violence it does them.

### Bed Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלִיעֶזֶר עֶבֶד אַבְרָהָם אִקְּלַע לְהָתָם. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: קוּם גְּנִי אַפּוּרְיָא. אֲמַר לְהוֹן: נִדְרָא נְדַרִי מִן יוֹמָא דְּמִיתַת אִמָּא לָא גָּנֵינָא אַפּוּרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Eliezer, servant of Abraham, happened** to come **there. They said to him: Come lie on the bed. He said to them: I took a vow** that **since the day my mother died I do not lie on a bed.**

When Eliezer came to Sodom and was offered the use of this bed, he declined with an ingenious excuse: *nidra nadini mi-yoma de-miset imi* — I took an oath on the day my mother died that I would never sleep on a bed. He thus escaped by invoking an oath rather than by revealing that he knew what the bed was for.

### Starving Poor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה מִתְרְמֵי לְהוּ עַנְיָא, יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ כֹּל חַד וְחַד דִּינָרָא, וּכְתִיב שְׁמֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ. וְרִיפְתָּא לָא הֲווֹ מַמְטֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When a poor person would happen** to come **to** Sodom, **each and every** person **would give him a dinar, and the name of** the giver **was written on** each dinar. **And they would not give** or sell **him bread,** so that he could not spend the money and would die of hunger.

When a poor person somehow found his way into Sodom, the city's response was a masterpiece of performative generosity masking lethal cruelty. Every resident would give the poor man a *dinar* — and write his own name on it. But no one would sell him food. The poor man thus accumulated money he could not spend, surrounded by people whose names were inscribed on the coins they had given him and who would not permit him to use them.

### Coin Recovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה מָיֵת, אָתֵי כֹּל חַד וְחַד שָׁקֵיל דִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **When he would die, each and every person would come and take his** dinar.

When he died of starvation — *kad hava mayit* — each resident came and took back his coin, identified by the name written on it. The cruelty was layered: the appearance of charity, the agony of wealth without recourse, and the final retrieval of every coin from the corpse.

### Wedding Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי אַתְנוֹ בֵּינַיְיהוּ: כֹּל מַאן דְּמַזְמֵין גַּבְרָא לְבֵי הִילּוּלָא, לְשַׁלַּח גְּלִימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **This** is what the people of Sodom **stipulated among themselves: Whoever invites a man to a wedding, his cloak will be removed.**

Sodom also enacted a law that anyone who invited a guest to a wedding feast would be stripped of his garments as punishment, since outsiders at communal celebrations were considered intolerable.

### Eliezer Crashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲוִי הַאי הִילּוּלָא, אִקְּלַע אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְהָתָם וְלָא יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ נַהֲמָא. כִּי בָּעֵי לְמִסְעַד, אֲתָא אֱלִיעֶזֶר וִיתֵיב לְסֵיפָא דְּכוּלְּהוּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאן אַזְמְנָךְ לְהָכָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְהָהוּא [דְּיָתֵיב]: אַתְּ זַמֵּנְתַּן. [אֲמַר: דִילְמָא שָׁמְעִי בִּי דַּאֲנָא אַזְמֵינְתֵּיהּ וּמְשַׁלְּחִי לֵיהּ מָאנֵיהּ דְּהַאי גַּבְרָא.] שְׁקַל גְּלִימֵיהּ הָהוּא דְּיָתֵיב גַּבֵּיהּ וּרְהַט לְבָרָא. וְכֵן עֲבַד לְכוּלְּהוּ עַד דְּנָפְקִי כּוּלְּהוּ וְאַכְלַאּ אִיהוּ לִסְעוּדְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **There was this wedding,** and **Eliezer,** servant of Abraham, **arrived there and they did not give him bread. When he sought to dine, Eliezer came and sat at the end,** behind **everyone. They said to him: Who invited you to here? He said to the one sitting** next to him: **You invited me.** That man **said** to himself: **Perhaps they will hear that I invited him and they will remove the garment of that man,** referring to himself. **The one who sat next to him took his cloak and ran outside. And likewise,** Eliezer **did** the same **for all of them until they all left, and he ate the meal.**

Eliezer arrived in Sodom during a wedding feast and was denied food. He planted himself at the end of the table and when the person next to him asked who had invited him, Eliezer told him: you did. The man, terrified that others would hear him accused of inviting an outsider and strip him of his clothes, fled. Eliezer moved to the next seat and told that person the same thing. He worked his way around the entire table in this manner — *ve-chen avid le-chulehu* — until everyone had fled and he sat and ate the entire feast alone.

### Woman Righteous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַוְיָא הָהִיא רָבִיתָא דַּהֲוָת קָא מַפְּקָא רִיפְתָּא לְעַנְיָא בְּחַצְבָּא. אִיגַּלַּאי מִלְּתָא, שַׁפְיוּהָ דּוּבְשָׁא, וְאוֹקְמוּהָ עַל אִיגַּר שׁוּרָא. אֲתֹא זִיבּוּרֵי וְאַכְלוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **There was a young woman who would take bread out to the poor people in a pitcher** so the people of Sodom would not see it. **The matter was revealed,** and **they smeared her with honey and positioned her on the wall** of the city, and **the hornets came and consumed her.**

There was one young woman in Sodom who secretly brought bread to a poor person, hiding it in her water jug as she went to draw water. When this was discovered, the people of Sodom covered her with honey — *shafyuha devash* — placed her atop a wall — *ve-okmuha al igra de-shura* — and left her there until the bees came and consumed her.

### Outcry Sealing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ זַעֲקַת סְדֹם וַעֲמֹרָה כִּי רָבָּה״. וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עַל עִיסְקֵי רִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And that is** the meaning of that **which is written: "And the Lord said: Because the cry of Sodom and Gomorrah is great [*rabba*]"** (Genesis 18:20). **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** *Rabba* is an allusion to the **matter of the young woman [*riva*]** who was killed for her act of kindness. It is due to that sin that the fate of the people of Sodom was sealed.

Rav Yehuda teaches that it was this act — *al iskei riva* — the affair of this young woman — that sealed Sodom's fate. The verse *va-yomer Hashem za'akat Sedom va-Amorah ki rabbah* — the outcry of Sodom is great — refers to the cry of this single righteous woman, the last act of goodness in the city, extinguished with honey and bees.

## Korach & Tribes

### Korach Fate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲדַת קֹרַח אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתְּכַס עֲלֵיהֶם הָאָרֶץ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַיֹּאבְדוּ מִתּוֹךְ הַקָּהָל״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The members of **the assembly of Korah have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the earth closed upon them"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in this world,** and also: **"And they perished from among the assembly"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in the World-to-Come.**

The Gemara concludes the chapter with two brief entries. *Adat Korach ein lahem chelek la-Olam HaBa* — the congregation of Korach has no share in the World to Come. The verse is invoked: *va-techas aleihem ha-aretz* — the earth covered them — in this world; *va-yovdu mi-toch ha-kahal* — they were lost from the assembly — in the World to Come. In this world they were swallowed by the earth; in the next world they are excluded from the *kahal*.

### Tribes Exile

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: עֲדַת קֹרַח אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתְּכַס עֲלֵיהֶם הָאָרֶץ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַיֹּאבְדוּ מִתּוֹךְ הַקָּהָל״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

Similarly: *aseret ha-shevatim ein lahem chelek la-Olam HaBa* — the Ten Tribes have no share in the World to Come. The verse: *va-yashlichem el eretz acheret* — He cast them to another land — in this world; *va-yovdu mi-toch ha-kahal* — lost from the assembly — in the next world. These are the words of Rabbi Akiva.

### Akiva Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: עֲלֵיהֶם אָמַר הַכָּתוּב ״ה׳ מֵמִית וּמְחַיֶּה מוֹרִיד שְׁאוֹל וַיָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: About** the assembly of Korah, **the verse states: "The Lord kills and makes alive; He lowers to the grave, and raises"** (I Samuel 2:6), indicating that the assembly of Korah has a share in the World-to-Come.

Rabbi Akiva's position is the stricter view. He holds that just as they were cast to another land and lost from the community in this world, so too they are lost from *Olam HaBa*. The verse's double language — exile in this world and loss from the *kahal* for the next — seals their fate on both planes.

Rabbi Eliezer offers a more expansive reading. He cites: *va-yomer Hashem Hashem memit u-mechayeh morid she'ol va-ya'al* — God brings death and life, brings down to the grave and raises up. Since God has the power to resurrect even those brought to death and to the grave, the Ten Tribes can still be revived and redeemed. This presumably applies to *dor ha-midbar* as well, among whom we would otherwise have assumed *chelek la-Olam HaBa* was more secure.

### Beteira Compassion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲדַת קֹרַח אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתְּכַס עֲלֵיהֶם הָאָרֶץ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַיֹּאבְדוּ מִתּוֹךְ הַקָּהָל״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הֵן כַּאֲבֵידָה הַמִּתְבַּקֶּשֶׁת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תָּעִיתִי כְּשֶׂה אֹבֵד בַּקֵּשׁ עַבְדֶּךָ כִּי מִצְוֹתֶיךָ לֹא שָׁכָחְתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 13:9): The members of **the assembly of Korah have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the earth closed upon them"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in this world,** and also: **"And they perished from among the assembly"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in the World-to-Come;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** Although it says that they perished, **they are like a lost item that is sought,** ultimately found, and rehabilitated, **as it is stated: "I have gone astray like a lost sheep; seek out your servant, for I do not forget your mitzvot"** (Psalms 119:176).

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira takes an even more compassionate stance regarding *adat Korach* specifically: *harei hen ka'aveida ha-mitbakeshet* — they are like a lost object that is being sought. God has not abandoned them; God is actively looking for them. The verse cited is from Tehillim: *ta'iti ke-seh oved bakesh avdecha ki mitzvotecha lo shachachti* — I have strayed like a lost sheep; seek your servant, for I have not forgotten Your commandments. Even the congregation of Korach, in all their rebellion, did not forget God's *mitzvot* — and God, for His part, has not stopped seeking them. The session concludes here, with the exposition of the *pesukim* about Korach's congregation to continue in the next *shiur*.
