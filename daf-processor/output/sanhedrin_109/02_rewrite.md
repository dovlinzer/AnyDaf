# Sanhedrin 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dor HaMabul & Ark

### Husband & Wife

The Gemara opens on 108b with a striking question about the *pasuk* describing the animals that entered the ark: *mi-kol ha-behemah ha-tehorah tikach lekha shiv'ah shiv'ah ish ve-ishto* — "of every clean animal you shall take seven and seven, a man and his wife." The question is obvious and almost comic: *ish ve-ishto le-behemah mi itei?* — since when do animals have marriages? The answer given by Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan is that the phrase does not mean they were actually married in any formal sense, but rather that the animals taken were those that had maintained a kind of fidelity — *me-otam she-lo ne-evdah bahem averah*, those upon whom no *averah* of interspecies copulation had been committed.

### Gate Test

This immediately raises the question: *mi-na yada* — how was Noach supposed to know which animals qualified? Rav Chisda answers that Noach passed the animals before the *tevah*: *she-he-eviru lifnei ha-tevah*. Any animal that the *tevah* admitted — *kol she-hatevah koletato* — was thereby known to have been faithful, while any animal the *tevah* rejected was known to have sinned. It is not entirely clear from the Gemara's language whether this involved some kind of magnetic or divine pull, but the principle is that the ark itself served as the mechanism of selection.

### Animals Volition

Rabbi Abahu offers an alternative: *me-otam ha-ba-im me-eleihen* — the animals simply came of their own accord. This reading draws on the *pasuk* that says the animals *came to* Noach, using the active voice, implying that God arranged for them to present themselves rather than Noach having to select them.

### Ark Wood Type

The *pasuk* instructs Noach to make the ark from *atzei gofer*. The Gemara asks: *mai gofer* — what is this wood? Rav Adda citing the school of Rabbi Shila says it refers to a type called *mavliga*; others say it is called *gulmish*. Both are understood to belong to the cedar family.

### Tzohar Light

The *pasuk* instructs: *tzohar ta'aseh la-tevah* — make a *tzohar* for the ark. Rabbi Yochanan explains that God told Noach to set in it precious stones and gems — *avanim tovot u-margaliyot* — so that they would illuminate the interior like the midday sun: *kedei she-yihyu me'irot lakhem ke-tzohorayim*. As Rashi notes, there are two interpretations of *tzohar*: some understand it as a window, others as a luminous jewel. The Gemara here follows the jewel interpretation, with the sunlight presumably refracting off the stones to spread light throughout the ark's interior.

### Ark Design

The *pasuk* continues: *ve-el amah tekhalenah milma'lah* — bring it to a cubit at the top. The Gemara explains: *de-be-hachi hu de-kaima* — that is simply good engineering. A tapered, angled roof that comes to a peak at the top distributes structural pressure in a way that makes the vessel more durable. The principle is not unlike the architectural logic of an arch or a dam built at an angle, where the incoming force actually strengthens the structure rather than breaking it.

### Three Levels

The verse also mandates three levels: *tachtiyim sheniyim u-shelishiyim ta'aseha*. A *tanna* teaches: *tachtiyim le-zevel* — the lowest level was designated for dung and excrement; *emtza'iyim le-behemah* — the middle level for the animals; *ve-elyonim le-adam* — and the upper level for the people. While this allots a great deal of space to the human inhabitants, the tapered design of the roof may account for the narrowing of that level.

### Raven Argument

The Gemara takes up the episode of the raven. Resh Lakish observes: *teshuvah nitzachit heshiv orev le-Noach* — the raven delivered to Noach a *teshuvah nitzachit*, an argument so compelling it could not be refuted. This is the same concept encountered earlier in the Gemara, where an argument is called *nitzachit* not necessarily because it is correct, but because no response can be mounted against it.

The raven's argument was as follows: *rabkha soneini ve-atah snatani* — your Master hates me, and you hate me. God took seven pairs of the clean animals and only two of the unclean, and now you are sending me — one of only two ravens in the world — on this mission. *Im pogea bi sar chama o sar tzina* — if the angel of heat or cold encounters me, *lo nimtza olam chasar briyah achat* — the world will be left lacking an entire species. Why not send from a species with seven, where the loss of one would still leave six? The raven then adds, somewhat absurdly: *o shema le-ishti atah tzarikh* — or perhaps you have designs on my wife?

### No Intercourse

Noach responds to the last point, calling the raven *rasha* — wicked. *B'mutar lach ne'esar* — even what is permitted to me, namely my own wife, is forbidden to me on the ark. *U'v'ne'esar li lo kol shekein* — certainly an animal, of the very type of transgression that brought about the destruction of the world through interspecies mixing, is unthinkable. Noach answers the silly point while leaving the stronger argument technically unanswered — a feature the Gemara implicitly acknowledges.

The Gemara then asks: *u'mei'heichan ne'asru* — from where do we derive that intercourse was forbidden on the ark? The answer comes from a careful reading of two *pesukim*. When they entered the ark, the verse lists them in the order: *atah u'vanecha v'ishtecha u'nshei vanecha itacha* — you, your sons, your wife, and your sons' wives — men and women listed separately. But when they left, the verse reverses the pairings: *atah v'ishtecha u'vanecha u'nshei vanecha* — you and your wife, your sons and their wives — couples reunited. Rabbi Yochanan derives from this conspicuous structural difference that *neasru b'tashmish hamitah* — they were forbidden conjugal relations during the entire time on the ark.

### Three Violators

A *baraita* teaches: *shlosha shimshu b'teivah* — three violated this prohibition on the ark, *ve-chulam laku* — and all three were punished. These three were: the dog, the raven, and Ham.

*Kelev nikshar* — the dog is now leashed, tied up, as its punishment. *Oreiv rak* — the raven spits; Rashi explains that this refers to the raven's mode of reproduction, though this explanation is difficult to understand as a punishment. *Ve-Cham laka b'oro* — Ham was smitten in his skin. Rashi identifies this as the origin of the dark-skinned descendants of Ham, specifically *sheyatza mimenu Kush*, the Ethiopians. While never made explicit in the *pasuk* itself, this has been a long-held reading within rabbinic tradition, though it carries the obvious difficulty that the Gemara uses the word *laka* — implying something negative — while any sensitivity to the topic requires care. One might also note that this provides a possible retrospective explanation for why Noach sent the raven first: having violated the prohibition, perhaps it was sent out of the ark as a form of consequence.

### Dove's Preference

The Gemara turns to the dove. Rabbi Yirmiyahu notes that the verse says *vayishlach et ha-yonah me'ito* — he sent the dove *from with him* — and derives from the word *me'ito* that *diratan shel ofot tehorim im ha-tzadikim* — the kosher birds prefer to dwell together with the righteous.

The verse then reports that the dove returned with *aleih zayit taraf b'fiha* — an olive leaf torn in its mouth. Rabbi Elazar expounds: the dove said before God: *Ribono Shel Olam, yihyu mezonosai merurin k'zayit umasurin b'yadcha* — let my food be as bitter as an olive but dependent upon You, *v'al yihyu metukin k'dvash umasurin b'yad basar v'dam* — rather than sweet as honey but dependent on human beings. The dove preferred the hardship of foraging freely under divine providence over the comfort of domestication and reliance on people. The words *taraf* meaning food and *b'fiha* meaning "in its mouth" are thus read as the dove's spoken declaration. The proof that *taraf* means food comes from the verse *hatrifeni lechem chuki* — provide me with the bread of my portion.

### Dove Metaphor

This *drasha* is best understood not merely as a general lesson about self-reliance, but as a metaphor for the Jewish people. The dove represents Israel: even when our sustenance in this world is bitter as an olive, we prefer a direct dependence on God to the easier path of dependence on foreign powers. That interpretation gives the entire passage its depth and purpose — the dove's declaration is Israel's declaration about its own condition in exile.

### Animals' Families

The Gemara notes that the verse states *le-mishpechoseihem yatze'u min ha-teivah* — they came out by their families. Rabbi Yochanan comments: *bi-mishpechosam ve-lo hem* — by their families but not themselves. The *Maharsha* acknowledges that no one is entirely certain how to parse this teaching. One possibility is that the verse emphasizes they emerged in family groupings rather than individually, but the precise import of the contrast remains unclear.

## Life on Ark

### Eliezer Questions

Rav Chana bar Bizna reports that Eliezer, the servant of Avraham, posed a question to Shem the elder — *Shem rabba* — invoking the verse *le-mishpechoseihem yatze'u min ha-teivah* as a springboard: *atun heichi avisun* — what was it like for you on the ark?

### Feeding Schedule

Shem replied: *tzar gadol haya lanu ba-teivah* — the hardship we endured on the ark was immense. Every creature had to be fed according to its natural cycle: *birya she-darkah le-ha'achilah ba-yom he'echelanu ba-yom, she-darkah le-ha'achilah ba-laylah he'echelanu ba-laylah* — daytime animals were fed by day, nighttime animals by night. The care was round the clock and without respite.

### Chameleon Diet

There was a particular difficulty: *hai ziksa lo hava yada abba mai achal* — father did not know what the chameleon ate. One day Noach was sitting and splitting open pomegranates — *hava yaseiv ve-pali rimona* — and a worm fell out of the pomegranate, which the chameleon promptly ate. From that point on, *hava gaval lei chizra* — he would knead or prepare for it some kind of porridge or mixture, and when it became wormy the chameleon would eat it.

### Lion's Fever

The lion presented a different challenge: how does one feed a lion on an ark full of the very animals one is trying to preserve? The answer was providential: *ishata zinsei* — God afflicted the lion with a fever for the duration of the voyage, so that it had no appetite for food. As Rav teaches: no less than six and no more than twelve months will a fever sustain and nourish — presumably meaning that the fever sustained the lion for the full twelve months of their time on the ark.

### Phoenix

There was also a creature called *orchina* — understood to be a phoenix or a bird of extraordinary longevity. Shem recounts that Noach found it resting quietly in one of the ark's compartments and asked: *lo ba'at me-zoney* — don't you need food? The bird replied: *chazisach de-hava trida* — I saw how busy you were with all the other animals, *amina lo atzrachach* — so I thought, I will not burden you. Noach blessed it: *yehei ra'ava de-lo samus* — may it be God's will that you never die. The verse cited in support is from *Iyov*: *ve-cha-chol arbeh yamim* — like the sand I will multiply days — understood as a reference to this immortal bird.

### Kings' Battle

Shem then turned the conversation around, addressing Eliezer directly: *ki asun aleichon melachim mizrach u-maarav* — when the kings of the east and west came against you, *atun heichi avisun* — how did you manage?

### Abraham Miracle

Eliezer answered: God brought Avraham, and me at his right hand, and the two of them would throw *afra* — dirt — at the enemy armies, and it would turn into swords; they threw chaff, and it turned into arrows. The *pasuk* from Tehillim is invoked: *Ne'um Hashem l'Adoni shev li-mini* — sit at my right hand. And the *pasuk* from Yeshayahu, familiar from the *haftarah* of *Lech Lecha*: *mi he'ir mi-mizrach* — who stirred from the east? — *yiten ke-afar charbo ke-kash nidaf kashto* — his sword is like dust, his bow like driven chaff. Thus the *pshat* of the *pasuk* itself encodes the miracle: the dust became swords, the chaff became arrows.

## Nachum & Crown

### Gam Zu Letovah

The Gemara now introduces Nachum Ish Gamzu, about whom it was said that *kol d'havei sareik lei amar gam zu letovah* — whatever happened to him, he would say: this too is for the good. One might observe that the truest test of the *gam zu letovah* attitude is not when bad things are miraculously transformed into good ones, but precisely when the difficulty remains difficulty — and yet one maintains that disposition. Nevertheless, what follows is a story in which the bad indeed turns good, through a miracle tied directly to the very tradition Eliezer just described.

### Crown Mission

One day the Jewish people wished to send a precious gift to the *Keysar* — the Roman emperor. *Nishdar behadei Nachum Ish Gamzu* — they decided to send it through Nachum Ish Gamzu, *de-melumad be-nissim hu* — because he was known to have miracles happen to him. This itself is a revealing detail: the confidence placed in Nachum was not because he was wise or diplomatic, but because his way of living — his *gam zu letovah* posture — made him someone through whom things worked out.

### Theft & Dirt

When Nachum arrived at an inn and disclosed that he was carrying a crown of precious jewels as tribute to the emperor, the innkeepers rose in the night, *sharinu le-sifteih* — opened his luggage — *ve-shaklu kol d'havei gabeih* — took everything inside — *u-malinu afra* — and refilled the box with dirt so that Nachum would be none the wiser. He continued on his journey, entirely unaware of the substitution.

### Caesar Suspects

When he arrived and presented the box to the *Keysar*, and the emperor opened it to find only dirt, he concluded: *achuchei kamachaichu bi Yehuda'ei* — the Jews are mocking me. Nachum was taken out to be executed. He said: *gam zu letovah* — this too is for the good.

### Eliyahu Appears

At that moment Eliyahu appeared — *admi l'hu*, disguised as one of the Roman officers — and suggested to them: *dilma hai afra me-afra d'Avraham Avinu hu* — perhaps this dirt is the very dirt of Avraham our patriarch, the miraculous dirt of which it was said *d'hava shadi afra havei charvei* — he threw dirt and it became swords — *gila havei girei* — he threw chaff and it became arrows.

### Dirt Tests

They tested it: *badku ve-ishtakach hachi*. There was a certain fortified city that had resisted all Roman conquest until that point. They threw some of this dirt at it, and the city fell. The miracle was confirmed.

### Gold Reward

Nachum was brought into the royal treasury and told to take whatever he wished. He filled his box with gold. On his return journey, the innkeepers who had stolen the original contents asked him what he had brought to the emperor that merited such reward. He told them simply: *mai d'shakli me-hacha ampeiti l'hasam* — whatever I took from here is what I brought there. Taking him at his word, the innkeepers gathered dirt and brought it to the emperor as a gift — and were executed for their trouble. The story has the structure of a folk tale, reminiscent of the Chelm story in which a man brings back gold for his onions, and his neighbor, trying to replicate the success, brings garlic and receives only onions in return.

## Dor HaPlaga

### No Share

The Gemara moves from *Dor HaMabul* to *Dor HaPlaga* — the generation of the dispersion. The *Mishnah* has established that they too have no share in *Olam HaBa*. The question is: *mai avdu* — what exactly did they do wrong?

### Tower Motive

The school of Rabbi Shila offers the classic answer: *nivneh migdal ve-na'aleh la-rakia* — they sought to build a tower and ascend to heaven — *ve-nakeh oto be-kardumot* — and strike God with axes. The motivation, seemingly, was control over rain: since rain descends from the heavens and is the one resource entirely dependent on divine will, they wished to seize control of that mechanism.

### Valley Problem

The objection raised in Eretz Yisrael is sharp: *im ken yivnu echad be-tura* — if they wanted to reach heaven, why does the verse say *va-yisu bik'ah* — they traveled to a valley? One does not build a tower to heaven in a valley. This is acknowledged as a formidable question — a *kushiya nitzchit* of sorts — and the Gemara never provides a satisfying answer to it, even as it continues to work within the framework that reaching heaven was indeed the goal.

### Three Groups

Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Elazar resolves part of the difficulty by noting that the builders were not a monolithic group: *necheliku l'shalosh kitot* — they divided into three factions, each with distinct motivations. A unified construction project can harbor diverse intentions, and indeed the Gemara suggests that what united them on the surface concealed fundamental disagreements about purpose.

### Dwelling Group

The first group said: *na'aleh ve-neshev sham* — let us ascend and take up residence there. Whether to protect themselves from a future flood or to consider themselves somehow elevated beings, they did not seek to make war against God. Their punishment was accordingly proportional: *hepitzam Hashem* — they were scattered. Their offense was about where they wished to live, so the response addressed precisely that.

### War Group

The second group said: *na'aleh ve-na'aseh milchamah* — let us ascend and wage war against God. They wished to surpass the divine. Their punishment, as *midah keneged midah*, was to be made subhuman: *na'asu kofim ve-ruchot ve-shedim ve-lilin* — they were turned into monkeys, spirits, demons, and *lilin*. They aspired to divinity; they were stripped of their humanity — or more precisely, as one might add, they were made *be-tzelem Elohim* and chose to read that image as actual divinity, so God removed even that *tzelem* from them.

### Idolatry Group

The third group said: *na'aleh ve-na'avod avodah zarah* — let us ascend and worship idols. Their punishment was *balal Hashem sephat kol ha'aretz* — God confounded the language of all the earth. The *midah keneged midah* here is not immediately obvious. Rashi explains that the confusion of language prevented them from coordinating their *avodah zarah*. A deeper reading would suggest that this group's sin was the worship of intermediaries — not outright denial of God but the insertion of celestial middlemen between Israel and the divine, which distorts and corrupts the very concept of monotheism. In response, God took the means of communication — itself a conduit — and made it an obstacle. Just as they turned access into obstruction by interposing intermediaries, God turned language from a vehicle of unity into a barrier of confusion.

### Natan View

A *baraita* adds the view of Rabbi Natan: *kulam le-shem avodat kochavim nitkavnu* — all three groups intended idolatry. He derives this through a *gezerah shavah*: the verse here says *ve-na'aseh lanu shem*, and elsewhere the Torah says *ve-shem Elohim acherim lo tazkiru* — the word *shem* links both passages to idolatry.

### Tower Fate

Rabbi Yochanan teaches regarding the fate of the tower itself: *migdal shlish nisraf* — one third was burned; *ve-shlish nivla* — one third was swallowed into the ground; *ve-shlish kayam* — one third still stands and can theoretically be visited.

### Tower Hidden

If a third of it remains, why has no one found it? Rav answers: *avir migdal meshake'ach* — the air around the tower causes forgetfulness. Anyone who comes near it and then departs cannot remember where they were or what they saw. It is a kind of divinely imposed amnesia that renders the surviving structure effectively invisible to human experience.

### Bad Omens

Rav Yosef then notes that *Bavel u-Borsif* — Babylon and its neighboring locale of Borsif — are *siman ra le-Torah*. Since the tower stood there and caused forgetting, the very air of that region is associated with the loss of Torah memory. This is deeply ironic, of course, given that Babylon ultimately became the center of the greatest Torah tradition in the diaspora. Rav Assi interprets *Borsif* as *bor shapi* — a pit that draws out, that pulls away — presumably a reference to the sapping of Torah retention.

## Anshei Sedom

### Sedom No Share

Having treated *Dor HaMabul* and *Dor HaPlaga*, the Gemara turns to *Anshei Sedom*. Notably, throughout this entire discussion the Gemara has not paused to explain the underlying logic of the hierarchy articulated in the *Mishnah* — why some groups are *omdin ba-din* but lack *Olam HaBa*, why others receive neither, and what distinguishes one category from another. The Gemara simply moves forward with what each group did and what their fate is.

### Wicked & Sinners

The verse in Bereishit describes the people of Sodom as *ra'im ve-chata'im la-Hashem me'od* — wicked and sinners before God greatly. The Gemara expounds the double language. Rav Yehuda interprets: *ra'im be-gufam* — wicked with their bodies; *ve-chata'im be-mamonam* — and sinners with their money. An alternative *baraita* inverts the mapping of the words but preserves the same two categories: bodily transgression and monetary transgression.

### Two Sins

The bodily sin is identified through the *pasuk* spoken by Yosef when he refused the wife of Potifar: *ve-eich e'eseh ha-ra'ah ha-gedolah ha-zot ve-chatati l'Elohim* — and how shall I commit this great evil and sin against God? That *pasuk* uses the root *ra'ah* for sexual transgression. The monetary sin is derived from *ve-hayah becha chet* — there will be a sin upon you — which the Torah uses in the context of failing to give to the poor. These two sins — sexual immorality and neglect of the poor — are in fact the same two identified as the core failures of *Dor HaMabul*, where both *chamas* and *hishchit kol basar* were central to their *gezar din*.

The verse continues: *la-Hashem* — against God — which the Gemara reads as *birchat Hashem*, blasphemy. And *me'od* — greatly — is interpreted either as doing these sins with full deliberate intent rather than through *yetzer ha-ra*, or in the *baraita* as *shefichus damim* — the shedding of blood — supported by the verse about Menashe: *gam naki shafach Menashe harbeh me'od*.

### Sedom Abundance

The Gemara then explains the origin of Sodom's wickedness in a way that exactly parallels the Gemara's earlier treatment of *Dor HaMabul*: *lo nisga'u ela bishvil tovah she-hishpia lahem ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — they became arrogant only because of all the goodness God lavished upon them. The verses from *Iyov* are marshaled to describe this abundance: *eretz mimena yatza lechem* — bread came forth from the land; *ve-tachteha nehepach ke-mo esh* — beneath it turned like fire; *makom sapir avaneha ve-afros zahav lo* — its stones were like sapphires and it had gold dust. It was a land of extraordinary natural wealth.

The verse continues: *nasiv lo yedau ayit ve-lo shezafaso ein ayah* — the path was unknown to the vulture and unseen by the eye of the hawk — understood metaphorically to mean that no bandits or enemies ever attacked this blessed land. *Lo hidrichuhu benei shachatz* — the sons of pride did not trample it; *lo adah alav shachal* — the lion did not gather against it. The land was uniquely protected and bountiful.

### Prevent Strangers

Their response to this abundance was: *lama lanu ovrei drachim* — why do we need passersby? *She'ein ba'im etzlenu ela lachasrenu mi-mamonenu* — they only come to take from our wealth. *Bo'u ve-nishkach torat regel me'artzenu* — let us eliminate the right of passage from our land.

The *pasuk* from *Iyov* is cited: *peretz nachal me'am gar ha-nishkachim mini regel* — they burst forth a river from the people who dwell there, causing passersby to be forgotten from their land. The interpretation offered is that God responded by diverting a stream of fire and brimstone from the heavenly course — *peretz nachal* as the divine punishment directed against those who made the foot-traveler forgotten in their midst.

### Leaning Wall

Rabba expounds a verse in Tehillim: *ad ana tehotetu al ish teratzechu chulchem ke-kir natui gader ha-dechuyah* — how long will you oppress a person, will you all murder like a leaning wall, like a pushed-over fence? The *Midrash* is: the people of Sodom would identify wealthy individuals, invite them to walk beside a leaning wall, and then push the wall onto them — *ve-dochin oto alav* — and after killing them they would raid their property. The desire for wealth, which one might think would be satisfied given their extraordinary abundance, instead became self-consuming — generating covetousness and ultimately murder.

### Tunnels & Perfume

Rabba expounds a second verse: *chatar ba-choshech batim* — they dig through houses in the darkness — *yomam chatmu lamo* — in the daytime they seal themselves in. The Gemara explains: the people of Sodom would identify wealthy homeowners and approach them with a seemingly generous request — *ha-yu notnin eineihem be-ba'alei mamon u-mafkidim etzlo afarsamon* — would you mind storing some of our precious *afarsamon* perfume in your treasure house? The wealthy person obliged. Then at night — *ba'in u-merichim oto ke-kelev* — they would come and sniff it out like a dog, following the scent of the perfume precisely to the storehouse, *ve-bain ve-chotrin sham* — and dig tunnels right to it — *ve-notlin et mamono* — and steal the contents. The verse from Tehillim is cited: *yashuvu la'erev yehemu che-kelev vi-sovavu ir* — they return at evening, they howl like dogs and circle the city.

### Tzipori 300

Rabbi Yose expounded this *drasha* publicly in Tzipori, recounting how Sodom's thieves would deposit perfume in wealthy homes to mark them for nighttime tunnel-breaking. That very night, three hundred people in Tzipori attempted to replicate the technique and tunneled into homes across the city. The townspeople confronted Rabbi Yose angrily: *yahavitu orcha le-ganve* — you have given a method to thieves. He replied: *mi hava yadana de-atun ganve* — I did not know I was delivering my *shiur* to a room full of thieves. The episode raises a genuine question: what was novel about the technique? Tunneling was not new; presumably the *chiddush* was the use of perfume as a locator. The number three hundred is likely an exaggeration, but the anecdote points to the real dangers of public exposition of criminal methodology, even in the context of Torah study.

### Yose's Death

The Gemara then notes: *ki nach nafshi de-Rabbi Yose* — when Rabbi Yose died — *shafu marzeiva de-Tzipori dama* — the gutters of Tzipori ran with blood. The image is arresting. On one level it speaks to the magnitude of communal mourning for a great sage. On another level, following immediately upon a story about theft, murder, and the abuse of wealth, it functions as a subtle commentary on the city of Tzipori itself — a wealthy, cosmopolitan city not unlike the Rome whose corruptions the Gemara has been cataloguing. The blood flowing in the streets of Tzipori upon the death of its great teacher may be the Gemara's quiet critique of what that city stood to lose.

## Sedom Laws

### Ox Tax

The Gemara now catalogs the perverse legal and social structures of Sodom. Their communal regulation for watching the town's oxen ran as follows: *de-is lei tora marei chad yoma, de-leis lei marei trei yoma* — whoever owns an ox watches for one day; whoever owns no ox watches for two days. The logic is inverted from anything fair: the burden falls heaviest on those who have least stake in the outcome. This is a paradigm of regressive social policy, designed to oppress the poor under the guise of civic duty.

### Orphan Logic

Into this system stepped an orphan, the son of a widow — someone at the very bottom of Sodom's social hierarchy — who was given the oxen to watch. He proceeded to slaughter them all, and then announced his own counter-regulation: *de-is lei tora nishkal chad mashka, de-leis lei nishkal trei mashkei* — whoever owns an ox takes one hide; whoever owns no ox takes two hides. The Sodomites protested: *mai hai* — what is this absurdity? He replied: *sof dina ke-techilat dina* — the end of the law mirrors the beginning of the law. Your own principle, applied consistently, yields exactly this. They had no answer.

### Bridge Toll

The taxation of passage was similarly structured to extract maximum payment from those least able to resist. Anyone who crossed by the bridge paid one *zuz*. Anyone who crossed through the river — to avoid the toll — paid two *zuz*. There was no way to pass through or around Sodom without paying, and the attempt to avoid one fee simply generated a higher one. Similarly, if a person had a pile of bricks or a spread of garlic and onions to sell, every passerby would help themselves to one item — *ana chada de-shakeil, I'm only taking one* — until by day's end the entire stock was gone, with each individual thief technically having taken almost nothing.

### Four Judges

The four judges of Sodom bore names that announced their function: *Shakrai* — Liar; *Shakrurai* — Great Liar; *Zaifi* — Forger; *Matzli Dina* — Perverter of Justice. Their court operated in deliberate inversion of every principle of justice.

### Wife & Donkey

If someone struck his neighbor's pregnant wife and caused her to miscarry, the judges would rule: *hav niyalah* — give your wife to him, so he can impregnate her and thereby restore what was lost. If someone cut off the ear of his neighbor's donkey, the ruling was: give your donkey to the person whose donkey you maimed, *ad di-ka'atya* — until the ear grows back, during which time the maimer gets use of the whole animal.

### Launderer

If someone wounded his neighbor and caused bleeding, the judges ruled that the wounded party owed the assailant a medical fee — *agra de-shakeil lach dama* — because he had performed a service by letting blood, as a professional bloodletter would. The launderer who refused to pay the bridge toll, was beaten for his refusal, came before the judges, and was told he owed eight *zuzei* for having crossed through the water — plus the bloodletting fee to the man who beat him. Eliezer, the servant of Avraham, happened to pass through Sodom on one of his journeys and was similarly beaten. He came before the judge and proceeded to smash a stone on the judge's head, then coolly informed him: the bloodletting fee you now owe me — pay it to this man who beat me. And my own money I shall keep. With that, he bested the entire system at its own game.

## Eliezer Sodom

### Procrustes Bed

Sodom maintained a guest bed for travelers — *mi-ttat Sodom* — which operated on a grotesque principle of uniformity. If a visitor was too long for the bed, *gayzei lei* — they would cut off his legs to make him fit. If he was too short, *matchilei* — they would stretch him until he fit. The phrase *mittat Sodom* has entered the language as the paradigm for forcing others to conform to one's own structure, regardless of the violence it does them.

### Bed Refusal

When Eliezer came to Sodom and was offered the use of this bed, he declined with an ingenious excuse: *nidra nadini mi-yoma de-miset imi* — I took an oath on the day my mother died that I would never sleep on a bed. He thus escaped by invoking an oath rather than by revealing that he knew what the bed was for.

### Starving Poor

When a poor person somehow found his way into Sodom, the city's response was a masterpiece of performative generosity masking lethal cruelty. Every resident would give the poor man a *dinar* — and write his own name on it. But no one would sell him food. The poor man thus accumulated money he could not spend, surrounded by people whose names were inscribed on the coins they had given him and who would not permit him to use them.

### Coin Recovery

When he died of starvation — *kad hava mayit* — each resident came and took back his coin, identified by the name written on it. The cruelty was layered: the appearance of charity, the agony of wealth without recourse, and the final retrieval of every coin from the corpse.

### Wedding Law

Sodom also enacted a law that anyone who invited a guest to a wedding feast would be stripped of his garments as punishment, since outsiders at communal celebrations were considered intolerable.

### Eliezer Crashes

Eliezer arrived in Sodom during a wedding feast and was denied food. He planted himself at the end of the table and when the person next to him asked who had invited him, Eliezer told him: you did. The man, terrified that others would hear him accused of inviting an outsider and strip him of his clothes, fled. Eliezer moved to the next seat and told that person the same thing. He worked his way around the entire table in this manner — *ve-chen avid le-chulehu* — until everyone had fled and he sat and ate the entire feast alone.

### Woman Righteous

There was one young woman in Sodom who secretly brought bread to a poor person, hiding it in her water jug as she went to draw water. When this was discovered, the people of Sodom covered her with honey — *shafyuha devash* — placed her atop a wall — *ve-okmuha al igra de-shura* — and left her there until the bees came and consumed her.

### Outcry Sealing

Rav Yehuda teaches that it was this act — *al iskei riva* — the affair of this young woman — that sealed Sodom's fate. The verse *va-yomer Hashem za'akat Sedom va-Amorah ki rabbah* — the outcry of Sodom is great — refers to the cry of this single righteous woman, the last act of goodness in the city, extinguished with honey and bees.

## Korach & Tribes

### Korach Fate

The Gemara concludes the chapter with two brief entries. *Adat Korach ein lahem chelek la-Olam HaBa* — the congregation of Korach has no share in the World to Come. The verse is invoked: *va-techas aleihem ha-aretz* — the earth covered them — in this world; *va-yovdu mi-toch ha-kahal* — they were lost from the assembly — in the World to Come. In this world they were swallowed by the earth; in the next world they are excluded from the *kahal*.

### Tribes Exile

Similarly: *aseret ha-shevatim ein lahem chelek la-Olam HaBa* — the Ten Tribes have no share in the World to Come. The verse: *va-yashlichem el eretz acheret* — He cast them to another land — in this world; *va-yovdu mi-toch ha-kahal* — lost from the assembly — in the next world. These are the words of Rabbi Akiva.

### Akiva Redemption

Rabbi Akiva's position is the stricter view. He holds that just as they were cast to another land and lost from the community in this world, so too they are lost from *Olam HaBa*. The verse's double language — exile in this world and loss from the *kahal* for the next — seals their fate on both planes.

### Eliezer View

Rabbi Eliezer offers a more expansive reading. He cites: *va-yomer Hashem Hashem memit u-mechayeh morid she'ol va-ya'al* — God brings death and life, brings down to the grave and raises up. Since God has the power to resurrect even those brought to death and to the grave, the Ten Tribes can still be revived and redeemed. This presumably applies to *dor ha-midbar* as well, among whom we would otherwise have assumed *chelek la-Olam HaBa* was more secure.

### Beteira Compassion

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira takes an even more compassionate stance regarding *adat Korach* specifically: *harei hen ka'aveida ha-mitbakeshet* — they are like a lost object that is being sought. God has not abandoned them; God is actively looking for them. The verse cited is from Tehillim: *ta'iti ke-seh oved bakesh avdecha ki mitzvotecha lo shachachti* — I have strayed like a lost sheep; seek your servant, for I have not forgotten Your commandments. Even the congregation of Korach, in all their rebellion, did not forget God's *mitzvot* — and God, for His part, has not stopped seeking them. The session concludes here, with the exposition of the *pesukim* about Korach's congregation to continue in the next *shiur*.
