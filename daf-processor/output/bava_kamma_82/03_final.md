# Bava Kamma 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ten Conditions

### Joshua's Takanos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עֲשָׂרָה תְּנָאִים הִתְנָה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּשָׁעַת כְּנִיסָתָם לָאָרֶץ: שֶׁיְּהוּ קוֹרִין בַּשַּׁבָּת, וּמְהַלְּכִין בִּשְׁבִילֵי הָרְשׁוּת עַד שֶׁתֵּרֵד רְבִיעָה שְׁנִיָּה, וּמְסַלְּקִין לְצִידֵּי הַדְּרָכִים, מִפְּנֵי יְתֵידוֹת הַדְּרָכִים, וּנִפְנִין לַאֲחוֹרֵי הַגָּדֵר, וְהַתּוֹעֶה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים, מְפַסֵּיג וְיוֹרֵד מְפַסֵּיג וְעוֹלֶה, וּמֵת מִצְוָה קָנָה מְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: [This appears at the end of 81b] **The ten conditions that Joshua stipulated with Israel at the time of their entry into the land:** That they should read [the Torah publicly] on Shabbat, and they should walk in permitted paths until the second rainfall [when crops sprout], and they should veer to the sides of roads because of hard protrusions of the road, and they should relieve themselves behind a fence, and one who becomes lost among the vineyards may cut down branches and descend [and then] cut down branches and ascend, and **a corpse with no one to bury it acquires its place** [and may be buried where it is found].

The daf opens in the middle of the list of *eser takanos* — the ten conditions that Yehoshua stipulated with the Jewish people upon their entry into the land. These *takanos* represent a compromise of individual property rights for the benefit of the larger community. The list appears at the bottom of 81b, just before the lines widen on the page.

### Mes Mitzva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: הַמּוֹצֵא מֵת מוּטָל בְּאִיסְרַטְיָא, מְפַנֵּהוּ לִימִין אִיסְרַטְיָא אוֹ לִשְׂמֹאל אִיסְרַטְיָא. שְׂדֵה בוּר וּשְׂדֵה נִיר – מְפַנֵּהוּ לִשְׂדֵה בוּר. שְׂדֵה נִיר וּשְׂדֵה זֶרַע – מְפַנֵּהוּ לִשְׂדֵה נִיר. הָיוּ שְׁתֵּיהֶן בּוּרוֹת; שְׁתֵּיהֶן נִירוֹת; שְׁתֵּיהֶן זְרוּעוֹת – מְפַנֵּהוּ לְמָקוֹם שֶׁיִּרְצֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **One who finds a corpse laid out on a main street [*isratya*] evacuates it** for burial either **to the right of the street or to the left of the street,** but it may not be buried under the main street itself. If one can move the corpse either to **a fallow field or** to **a plowed field, he evacuates it to the fallow field. If the choice is between a plowed field and a sown field, he evacuates it to the plowed field. If both fields were fallow, or if both were plowed, or if both were sown, he evacuates it to any side where he wishes** to move it.

The final condition on the list is *mes mitzva koneh mekomo*: wherever one finds an unattended corpse with no obvious family, it may be buried in that very spot. The *Gemara* then raises a contradiction from another source: *hamotzei mes mutal ba'esratya* — if one finds a corpse lying across an *esratya*, a major road (the word is Greek), one does not bury it right in the middle of the road. Instead, one moves it to the right or left, to a nearby field. The *Gemara* then sets out a hierarchy: between an unplowed lot (*sadeh bor*) and a plowed-but-unplanted lot (*sadeh nir*), one brings it to the *sadeh bor*; between a *sadeh nir* and a planted field (*sadeh zera*), one brings it to the *sadeh nir* so as to cause the least possible damage. If both sides offer equivalent options, one simply chooses whichever seems best.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי: בְּמוּטָּל עַל הַמֵּיצַר; מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנִּיתָּן לְפַנּוֹתוֹ, מְפַנֵּהוּ לְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁיִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai said:** The ruling of this *baraita* is stated **with regard to** a corpse **laid out on the pathway.** Were the corpse buried there, it would prohibit passage by priests. **Since permission was** already **granted to evacuate it** from there, **one may evacuate it to any place he wishes.** If, however, the corpse was in a field, it would be prohibited to move it.

Rav Bibi resolves the contradiction: the case of the *esratya* involves a corpse lying stretched across the full width of the road — *mutal al hametzar* — which makes burial in place impossible regardless. Since the body must be moved in any case, one makes a considered choice about where to bring it, and if the options are equivalent, one uses whatever sensible principle is available. That explains how this condition of *mes mitzva* coheres with the *braita* about the road.

### Counting Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: עֲשָׂרָה?! הָנֵי חַד סְרֵי הָוְיָין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **says:** Are there really only **ten? These** conditions enumerated in the *baraita* **are** actually **eleven.**

Having completed the list, the *Gemara* observes: *hanei chad asar havyen* — if one counts the items, there are eleven, not ten. The *Gemara* therefore must account for the discrepancy.

### Shlomo's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְהַלְּכִין בִּשְׁבִילֵי הָרְשׁוּת – שְׁלֹמֹה אַמְרַהּ. כִּדְתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁכָּלוּ פֵּירוֹתָיו מִן הַשָּׂדֶה, וְאֵינוֹ מַנִּיחַ בְּנֵי אָדָם לִיכָּנֵס בְּתוֹךְ שָׂדֵהוּ – מָה הַבְּרִיּוֹת אוֹמְרוֹת עָלָיו? מָה הֲנָאָה יֵשׁ לִפְלוֹנִי, וּמָה הַבְּרִיּוֹת מַזִּיקוֹת לוֹ? עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב אוֹמֵר: ״מִהְיוֹת טוֹב אַל תִּקָּרֵי רָע״. וּמִי כְּתִיב: ״מִהְיוֹת טוֹב אַל תִּקָּרֵי רָע״? אִין, כְּתִיב כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא: ״אַל תִּמְנַע טוֹב מִבְּעָלָיו, בִּהְיוֹת לְאֵל יָדְךָ לַעֲשׂוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers: The condition that **one may walk in permitted paths** that cut through a private field in the summer was not instituted by Joshua; rather, King **Solomon said it. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one's produce was completely** harvested **from the field, but he does not allow people to enter into his field** to shorten their route, **what do people say about him?** They say: **What benefit does so-and-so have** by denying entry into his field? **And what harm are people causing him** by traversing his field? **Concerning him, the verse says: Do not be called wicked** by refraining **from being good. Is it** really **written: Do not be called wicked** by refraining **from being good?** There is no such verse in the Bible. **Yes,** an idea **like this** is found in the Bible, albeit in a slightly different form, as **it is written like this: "Withhold not good from him to whom it is due, when it is in the power of your hand to do it"** (Proverbs 3:27).

The *Gemara* answers that one of the items — the rule that people may walk through a neighbor's field via its footpaths (*shvilei ha-reshus*) during the off-season when nothing is growing — is not actually a *takanas Yehoshua* at all. Rather, it derives from a principle associated with Shlomo, and so it does not count toward the ten. A *braita* elaborates: if someone's produce has already been harvested and he nonetheless refuses to allow people to cut through his field, people will say of him, *mah hana'ah l'ploni u-mah habriyot mazikos lo* — what benefit does he get from it, and in what way are people harming him? Standing on one's legal rights in a case of *zeh ne'eneh v'zeh eino chaser* — where others benefit and you lose nothing — is technically permissible but morally indefensible.

The *Gemara* quotes a verse to that effect: *al timna tov miba'laiv biheyos le'el yadecha* — do not withhold good from those who deserve it when you have the power to give (Mishlei). One might have thought this was a different verse, perhaps from *Ben Sira*, but the *Gemara* identifies a proper *pasuk* from Mishlei that conveys the same sentiment.

### Additional Takanos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיכָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּשְׁעַת הוֹצָאַת זְבָלִים, אָדָם מוֹצִיא זִבְלוֹ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְצוֹבְרוֹ כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים; כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא נִישּׁוֹף בְּרַגְלֵי אָדָם וּבְרַגְלֵי בְהֵמָה. שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָאָרֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further questions the statement of the *baraita* that Joshua instituted ten stipulations: **And there is** also the stipulation mentioned **by Rabbi Yehuda. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: During the time of manure removal, a person may remove his manure** from his property **into the public thoroughfare and pile it up** there for **a full thirty** days, **so that it should be trodden by the feet of people and by the feet of animals,** thereby improving its quality, as it was **on this condition** that **Joshua apportioned Eretz** Yisrael **to the Jewish people.**

The *Gemara* then asks whether there are yet additional conditions beyond the ones enumerated. Several are introduced.

Rabbi Yehuda teaches that during the season of removing dung, one has the right to bring fertilizer out into the public domain and leave it there for thirty days — *b'sha'at hotza'at z'valim* — so that it can be trodden upon and turned into usable material. This too was a condition of Yehoshua.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיכָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: תְּנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא שֶׁיְּהֵא זֶה יוֹרֵד לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ וְקוֹצֵץ שׂוֹכוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ לְהַצִּיל נְחִיל שֶׁלּוֹ, וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ דְּמֵי שׂוֹכוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ; וּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא שֶׁיְּהֵא זֶה שׁוֹפֵךְ יֵינוֹ וּמַצִּיל דּוּבְשָׁנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנוֹטֵל דְּמֵי יֵינוֹ מִתּוֹךְ דּוּבְשָׁנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ; וּתְנַאי בֵּית דִּין הוּא שֶׁיְּהֵא זֶה מְפָרֵק אֶת עֵצָיו וְטוֹעֵן פִּשְׁתָּנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וְנוֹטֵל דְּמֵי עֵצָיו מִתּוֹךְ פִּשְׁתָּנוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ; שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָאָרֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues this line of questioning: **And** furthermore, **there are** the stipulations mentioned **by Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: It is a stipulation of the court,** i.e., it is an automatic right even when not explicitly granted, **that this** owner of a bee colony **may enter the field of another and chop off the other's branch** in order **to save his** bee **colony, and** afterward **he gives him** the **value of the other's** severed **branch.** That is, if a beekeeper finds that one of his colonies has relocated to a tree in a neighboring field, he may bring it back to his own property together with the branch, provided that he later reimburses the owner of the tree. **And it is** also **a stipulation of the court that this** bearer of wine **pours out his wine** from his barrel **and** uses the barrel **to save another's** spilling **honey,** which is more valuable than the wine, **and** then **he takes** the **value of his wine from the** saved **honey of the other,** as reimbursement. **And it is** likewise **a stipulation of the court that this** owner of wood **unloads his wood** from his donkey **and loads another's flax,** which is more valuable than wood, if the load of flax is stranded on the road due to a mishap, **and** later **he takes** the **value of his wood from the** saved **flax of that other** individual. Once again, the reason is that it was **on this condition** that **Joshua apportioned Eretz** Yisrael **to the Jewish people.**

Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka teaches three further conditions, all framed as *tnai beit din* — a stipulation of the court. First, if my bees swarm onto a branch in my neighbor's property, I may cut off that branch to retrieve the swarm, paying my neighbor for it. Second, if I spill my wine to save your honey when your barrel is breaking, you must compensate me — you cannot claim you never asked me to do so. Third, if I unload my firewood from my donkey to transport your flax when your animal breaks down, you must pay me for my wood. A question naturally arises: why do these require a formal *tnai beit din* at all? Surely simple logic would demand reimbursement. *Tosafot* notes a tension with another *Gemara* that seems to limit the obligation to paying a fee rather than the full value of the wine. *Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing a case where the honey-owner himself physically compelled the other person to spill the wine — in that case full reimbursement is owed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּיחִידָאֵי לָא קָאָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why the stipulations mentioned by Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yishmael are omitted by the earlier *baraita*: In the *baraita*, **we are not speaking of individual** opinions, but only of those that are accepted by all the Sages.

The *Gemara* deflects these additional teachings by noting *be-yachidai lo ka-amina* — the list of ten *takanos* claims only conditions taught unanimously, not those transmitted in the name of individual authorities. Whether this means others actually dispute these conditions or merely that they lack unanimous transmission is not entirely clear.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כִּי אֲתָא רַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֶחָד אִילָן הַנּוֹטֶה לְתוֹךְ שְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ, וְאֶחָד אִילָן הַסָּמוּךְ לַמֶּצֶר – מֵבִיא וְקוֹרֵא, שֶׁעַל מְנָת כֵּן הִנְחִיל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת הָאָרֶץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further questions the number of Joshua's stipulations: **But when Rabbi Avin came** from Eretz Yisrael he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **both a tree that leans into the field of another and a tree that is close to a boundary** with another field, the owner of the tree **brings** the first fruits of the tree **and recites** the accompanying declaration, as described in Deuteronomy 26:5–10, **as** it was **on this condition** that **Joshua apportioned Eretz** Yisrael **to the Jewish people.** This is an additional stipulation by Joshua, which means that there are more than ten.

The *Gemara* then cites Ravin in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a tree that leans over a neighbor's field or stands near a boundary line — one may still bring *bikkurim* from it. This is because there is an opinion in the *Gemara* that such a tree, since it damages a neighbor's field, has no legal standing in its current location, and therefore one should not be able to bring *bikkurim* from it. Rabbi Yochanan rules that Yehoshua's conditions explicitly permitted this. Rashi notes that this teaching is a *baraita*, raising the question of why it is not included in the canonical list of ten. The *Gemara* resolves this by revealing that the original list of ten conditions is not an anonymous *baraita* at all. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאן תָּנָא עֲשָׂרָה תְּנָאִין שֶׁהִתְנָה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הוּא. רַב גְּבִיהָה מִבֵּי כְתִיל מַתְנֵי לַהּ בְּהֶדְיָא: רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם וְרַבִּי בִּרְיָיס אָמְרִי מִשּׁוּם זָקֵן אֶחָד, וּמַנּוּ – רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי; עֲשָׂרָה תְּנָאִין הִתְנָה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Rather, who** is the one who **taught** the *baraita* that deals with the **ten conditions that Joshua stipulated? It is Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi,** an *amora*. Therefore, Rabbi Yoḥanan, another *amora*, can disagree with it. **Rav Geviha from Bei Katil teaches** this **explicitly** in his version of the *baraita*: **Rabbi Tanḥum and Rabbi Berayes say in the name of a certain elder, and who is that** elder? It is **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: Joshua stipulated ten conditions.**

*S'fuyai* — it is in fact the teaching of an *amora*, Yehoshua ben Levi specifically, and therefore other *amoraim* or *tannaim* may well offer different or additional formulations. Rav Geviah of Bei Kasil teaches this explicitly, and Rav Tanchum and Rav Beris transmit it in the name of a certain *zakein* — identified as Yehoshua ben Levi — that he listed exactly ten conditions. So the list of ten is a specific formulation of that authority, not a universally binding enumeration.

## Ezra's Takanos

### Leadership Shift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה תַּקָּנוֹת תִּיקֵּן עֶזְרָא: שֶׁקּוֹרִין בַּמִּנְחָה בַּשַּׁבָּת; וְקוֹרִין בְּשֵׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי; וְדָנִין בְּשֵׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי; וּמְכַבְּסִים בַּחֲמִישִׁי בַּשַּׁבָּת; וְאוֹכְלִין שׁוּם בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת; וְשֶׁתְּהֵא אִשָּׁה מַשְׁכֶּמֶת וְאוֹפָה; וְשֶׁתְּהֵא אִשָּׁה חוֹגֶרֶת בְּסִינָר; וְשֶׁתְּהֵא אִשָּׁה חוֹפֶפֶת וְטוֹבֶלֶת; וְשֶׁיְּהוּ רוֹכְלִין מַחֲזִירִין בָּעֲיָירוֹת; וְתִיקֵּן טְבִילָה לְבַעֲלֵי קְרָיִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ezra** the Scribe **instituted ten ordinances:** He instituted **that** communities **read** the Torah **on Shabbat in the afternoon; and they** also **read** the Torah **on** every **Monday and Thursday; and** the courts convene and **judge** every **Monday and Thursday; and one does laundry on Thursday; and one eats garlic on Shabbat eve. And** Ezra further instituted **that a woman should rise early and bake** bread on those days when she wants to bake; **and that a woman should don a breechcloth; and that a woman should** first **comb** her hair **and** only then **immerse** in a ritual bath after being ritually impure; **and that peddlers** of cosmetics and perfumes **should travel around through** all **the towns. And** Ezra further **instituted** the requirement of **immersion for those who experienced a seminal emission.**

The *Gemara* transitions from the *takanos* of Yehoshua to those of Ezra. The shift is thematically significant. Yehoshua's conditions were framed as *hinchil Yehoshua et ha'aretz* — he bequeathed the land — reflecting his role as political sovereign, setting up the economic and social structure of Jewish life in the land. Ezra, by contrast, operated under foreign rule; the Jews in his time had no political sovereignty, returning to the land only because the Persian king Koresh granted permission. The first taste of genuine sovereignty would come only later, with the Chashmonaim. Ezra's leadership was religious rather than political — he was, in a sense, the first rabbi — and his *takanos* reflect that character entirely.

### Torah Readings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיְּהוּ קוֹרְאִים בַּמִּנְחָה בַּשַּׁבָּת – מִשּׁוּם יוֹשְׁבֵי קְרָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes these ordinances, the first of which is **that** communities **shall read** the Torah **on Shabbat afternoon.** This Gemara explains that this ordinance was instituted **due to those who sit** idly on street **corners,** who do not attend the synagogue during the week.

Two of Ezra's ten *takanos* concern the public reading of the Torah. The *Gemara* lists them together: *shekorei b'mincha b'shabbos*, that there should be a Torah reading at Shabbos Mincha, and *shekorei b'sheni u'v'chamishi*, that there should be readings on Mondays and Thursdays.

The reason for the Shabbos Mincha reading, as Rashi explains, is *mishum yoshvei chanuyos kol yemei hachol* — for the sake of the shopkeepers who are occupied with business throughout the week and have no opportunity to hear the Torah reading on market days. Rather than reading for those who are already present on Monday and Thursday, this gives the storekeepers an additional opportunity. It also provides anticipation of the coming week's *parsha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁיְּהוּ קוֹרְאִים בְּשֵׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי – עֶזְרָא תִּיקֵּן?! וְהָא מֵעִיקָּרָא הֲוָה מִיתַּקְנָא! דְּתַנְיָא: ״וַיֵּלְכוּ שְׁלֹשֶׁת יָמִים בַּמִּדְבָּר וְלֹא מָצְאוּ מָיִם״ – דּוֹרְשֵׁי רְשׁוּמוֹת אָמְרוּ: אֵין מַיִם אֶלָּא תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹי כׇּל צָמֵא לְכוּ לַמַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the second of Ezra's ordinances: **And that they should read** the Torah **on** every **Monday and Thursday.** The Gemara asks: **Did Ezra institute** this practice? **But it was instituted from the beginning,** i.e., long before his time. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And Moses led Israel onward from the Red Sea, and they went out into the wilderness of Shur; **and they went three days in the wilderness, and found no water"** (Exodus 15:22). **Those who interpret verses** metaphorically **said** that **water** here is referring to **nothing other than Torah, as it is stated** metaphorically, concerning those who desire wisdom: **"Ho, everyone who thirsts, come for water"** (Isaiah 55:1).

Regarding the Monday-Thursday reading, the *Gemara* asks: was this Ezra's institution at all? A *braita* reveals that it goes back much further — to the generation that left Egypt. The *pasuk* records that they walked three days in the desert and found no water — *vayelechu shloshet yamim bamidbar v'lo matzu mayim*. The *Gemara* interprets this *midrashically*: *ein mayim ela Torah* — water here means Torah. They had gone three days without Torah and had become spiritually weary, *nil'u*. The Maharsha connects this to the verse *vayorehu Hashem etz* — the wood thrown to sweeten the water — as an allusion to *etz chayim*, the Torah. At that moment, *sham sam lo chok u'mishpat*, a standing ordinance was established: one should never go three days without Torah. The prophets among them — Moshe, Aharon, and Miriam — therefore instituted reading on Shabbos, Monday, and Thursday, skipping Sunday, Tuesday, Wednesday, and Friday, so that the gap between readings never exceeds two days.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁהָלְכוּ שְׁלֹשֶׁת יָמִים בְּלֹא תּוֹרָה, נִלְאוּ, עָמְדוּ נְבִיאִים שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶם וְתִיקְּנוּ לָהֶם שֶׁיְּהוּ קוֹרִין בַּשַּׁבָּת, וּמַפְסִיקִין בְּאֶחָד בַּשַּׁבָּת, וְקוֹרִין בְּשֵׁנִי, וּמַפְסִיקִין שְׁלִישִׁי וּרְבִיעִי, וְקוֹרִין בַּחֲמִישִׁי, וּמַפְסִיקִין עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָלִינוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים בְּלֹא תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The verse means that **since** the Jews **traveled for three days without** hearing any **Torah they became weary,** and therefore the **prophets among them arose and instituted for them that they should read** from the Torah each **Shabbat, and pause** on **Sunday, and read** again on **Monday, and pause** on **Tuesday and Wednesday, and read** again on **Thursday, and pause** on **Shabbat eve, so they would not tarry three days without** hearing the **Torah.** Evidently this practice predates Ezra.

### Ancient Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעִיקָּרָא תִּקְּנוּ חַד גַּבְרָא – תְּלָתָא פְּסוּקֵי, אִי נָמֵי תְּלָתָא גַּבְרֵי – תְּלָתָא פְּסוּקֵי, כְּנֶגֶד כֹּהֲנִים לְוִיִּם וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִים; אֲתָא הוּא, תַּיקֵּן תְּלָתָא גַּבְרֵי וְעַשְׂרָה פְּסוּקֵי, כְּנֶגֶד עֲשָׂרָה בַּטְלָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Initially they instituted** that **one man** read **three verses;** or **alternatively,** that **three men** read **three verses.** Either way, the number three **corresponds to** the three types of Jews: **Priests, Levites, and Israelites.** Ezra later **came** and **instituted** that **three men** always read, **and** that **ten verses** altogether be read by them, **corresponding to the ten idlers** in a city, i.e., the ten men who are paid to spend their time dealing with synagogue and communal matters.

This ancient institution addresses the concern that Ezra could not have "instituted" something already in place from the time of the Exodus. The *Gemara* clarifies the nature of Ezra's contribution.

### Ezra Expansion

Originally, the institution consisted of one person reading three *pesukim*, or alternatively three people each reading one *pasuk* — a total of three *pesukim* — corresponding to *kohen*, *Levi*, and *Yisrael*. Ezra came and expanded this: three people should receive *aliyos* and together they should read a total of ten *pesukim* — *k'neged asara batlanim*, corresponding to the ten *batlanim*, the community members who are regularly present in *shul* to ensure a *minyan* and attend to communal needs. Rashi explains these *batlanim* as people of good standing who set aside their own affairs to be available for *tzorchei tzibur* and to be present in the *beit knesset*. So Ezra's contribution was both the expansion of the number of *pesukim* and the institutionalization of the *asara batlanim* — the concept of a standing *minyan* of ten. One wonders whether Ezra's reform also reflects the emergence of the synagogue as a more ritualized institution, with a formal structure of being called up to the Torah and a *minyan* of ten.

*Tosafot* asks why specifically Monday and Thursday were chosen; other day combinations could have served equally well. *Tosafot* cites a *midrash* that when Moshe ascended to receive the final *luchot*, he went up on a Thursday and came down on a Monday — making those days an *et ratzon*, a time of divine favor. *Tosafot* also notes the connection to fast days, which were historically observed on Mondays and Thursdays as days of divine receptivity — and naturally, if people are gathering to read the Torah, those same days would be chosen for fasting.

### Court & Laundry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָנִין בְּשֵׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי – דִּשְׁכִיחִי, דְּאָתוּ לְמִקְרֵא בְּסִיפְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The next ordinance of Ezra is: **And** the courts convene and **judge** every **Monday and Thursday.** The Gemara explains that the reason for this ordinance is **that** many people are **found** in a city on these days, **as they come** from the countryside **for the reading of the** holy **book,** the Torah, which is performed on Mondays and Thursdays, as stated above.

The *Gemara* records that courts sat on Mondays and Thursdays — *danin b'sheni u'v'chamishi* — because that is when people gathered in town for the Torah reading. Whether the courts preceded the Torah readings or vice versa is a kind of chicken-and-egg question, but the practical integration is clear: by concentrating communal activities on the same days, attendance at one event ensures presence for the other. *Tosafot* raises the question that another *Gemara* suggests that before Ezra, *beit din* sat every day. *Tosafot* resolves this by proposing that Ezra specifically instituted the practice for smaller towns and villages, while major centers may already have had daily courts. Alternatively, focused, less frequent sessions can actually be more productive than daily sittings — just as designating a specific laundry day ensures the task gets done.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁיְּהוּ מְכַבְּסִין בַּחֲמִישִׁי בַּשַּׁבָּת – מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **And that one should do laundry on Thursday.** This was instituted **due to** the need to have clean garments in **deference to Shabbat.**

The laundry ordinance — *mechabsin b'chamishi b'shabbat*, doing laundry on Thursday — is *mishum kevod Shabbat*, in honor of Shabbos, so that fresh clothes are ready for the holy day.

### Garlic & Onah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁיְּהוּ אוֹכְלִין שׁוּם בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת – מִשּׁוּם עוֹנָה; דִּכְתִיב: ״אֲשֶׁר פִּרְיוֹ יִתֵּן בְּעִתּוֹ״, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב נַחְמָן, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב כָּהֲנָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: זֶה הַמְשַׁמֵּשׁ מִטָּתוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the next listed ordinance: **And that one should eat garlic Shabbat eve.** This is **due to** the fact that garlic enhances sexual potency, and Friday night is an appropriate time for **conjugal relations. As it is written** concerning the righteous: "And he shall be like a tree planted by streams of water, **who brings forth his fruit in his season"** (Psalms 1:3); **and Rabbi Yehuda says, and some say** it was **Rav Naḥman, and some say** it was **Rav Kahana, and some say** it was **Rabbi Yoḥanan** who said: **This** is referring to **one who engages in sexual intercourse every Shabbat eve.**

Another *takana* of Ezra is eating garlic on *erev Shabbat* — *mishum onah*, in order to fulfill the *mitzvah* of marital relations. A *braita* lists five properties of garlic: it satisfies, it warms the body internally, it gives a pleasant glow to the complexion, it increases semen, and it destroys intestinal parasites. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֱמְרוּ בַּשּׁוּם: מַשְׂבִּיעַ, וּמַשְׁחִין, וּמַצְהִיל פָּנִים, וּמַרְבֶּה הַזֶּרַע, וְהוֹרֵג כִּנִּים שֶׁבִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִם. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מַכְנִיס אַהֲבָה וּמוֹצִיא אֶת הַקִּנְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that **five matters were stated with regard to garlic: It satisfies; it warms** the body; **it causes** one's **countenance to shine; it increases** one's **sperm, and it kills lice that are in the intestines. And some say** that it also **instills love** into those who eat it **and removes jealousy** from them.

Some add that it fosters love and dispels jealousy, contributing to the general wellbeing that makes garlic something of an ancient superfood. The *Gemara* derives the connection to Friday night from the verse *asher piryo yiten b'ito* — he gives his fruit in the proper time — which is applied to one who fulfills *onah* from *erev Shabbat* to *erev Shabbat*. The *Gemara* presents this teaching through a chain of possible attributions: Rav Yehudah, or alternatively Rav Nachman, or alternatively Rav Kahana, or alternatively Rabbi Yochanan. It is worth noting that certain sects during the *bayis sheni* period considered marital relations on Shabbos to be forbidden, viewing the holy day as incompatible with physical pleasure. The ruling of *Chazal* is precisely the opposite: *Oneg Shabbat* includes the *mitzvah* of marital relations, representing an explicitly anti-ascetic religious posture.

### Women's Takanos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁתְּהֵא אִשָּׁה מַשְׁכֶּמֶת וְאוֹפָה – כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא פַּת מְצוּיָה לַעֲנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The next ordinance is: **And that a woman should rise early and bake** bread on those days when she bakes. This Gemara explains that this was instituted **so that bread should be available for poor people,** who go begging for bread in the mornings.

Three of Ezra's *takanos* concern women specifically. First, a woman who bakes should bake early in the morning — *isha mashkemet v'ofah* — so that bread will be available for the poor who pass through during the day. Bread being the staff of life, early baking ensures that the poor have access to food. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁתְּהֵא אִשָּׁה חוֹגֶרֶת בְּסִינָר – מִשּׁוּם צְנִיעוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches: **And that a woman should don a breechcloth [*sinar*].** This ordinance was instituted **due to** reasons of **modesty.**

Second, a woman should wear a *sinar* — some type of undergarment, though the word more literally means an apron — *mishum tzniutah*, for the sake of modesty. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁתְּהֵא אִשָּׁה חוֹפֶפֶת וְטוֹבֶלֶת – דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds: **And that a woman should** first **comb** her hair **and** only then **immerse** in a ritual bath. This is to ensure that there is no dirt or other substance in the hair that would invalidate the immersion. The Gemara questions this: **This is** required **by Torah law,** Ezra did not institute this.

Third, a woman should comb her hair before immersing in the *mikvah* — *isha chofefet v'tovelet*.

A question arises: why does this *takana* specifically address women when men too were immersing regularly in that era, before the temple service became inaccessible to most? The answer, as a student suggests, is that the concern about hair pertains to those with long hair, and that describes women in the typical case. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְרָחַץ אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ בַּמַּיִם״ – שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא דָּבָר חוֹצֵץ בֵּין בְּשָׂרוֹ לַמַּיִם. ״אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ״ – אֶת הַטָּפֵל לִבְשָׂרוֹ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ – שֵׂעָר!
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*, concerning a verse that discusses one who must undergo ritual immersion: **"And he shall bathe his flesh [*et besaro*] in water"** (Leviticus 14:9). This verse teaches **that no substance should interpose between his flesh and the water.** When the verse states this in the expanded form of **"*et* his flesh,"** using the term "*et*," this teaches that the water must come into contact even with **that which is subordinate to his flesh. And what is that?** It is one's **hair.** Accordingly, the Torah itself states that there may not be any interposing substance in the hair at the time of immersion. What, then, did Ezra add?

The *Gemara* presses further: is not combing the hair before immersion a biblical obligation in any event? The verse says *v'rachatz et besaro bamayim* — one's flesh must be in direct contact with the water, and the extra word *et* includes *hatafel l'besaro*, what is attached to the body, namely the hair. Thus hair must also be in direct contact with water. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – לְעַיּוֹנֵי דִּלְמָא מִיקְּטַר, אִי נָמֵי מְאַוַּס מִידֵּי, מִשּׁוּם חֲצִיצָה; וַאֲתָא אִיהוּ תַּיקֵּן חֲפִיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **By Torah law** one is required **to inspect** his or her hair before immersion, as **perhaps** some hairs are **knotted** together, preventing contact with water at that spot, **or** perhaps there is some **repulsive substance** in his hair. One must perform this inspection **because** these would constitute **an interposition. And** Ezra **came** and added to the Torah's minimal obligation. He **instituted** the requirement of **combing** the hair even when it is known that it is not knotted and contains no repulsive substance.

The *Gemara* responds: *d'oraisa*, the biblical requirement is satisfied by mere inspection — running one's fingers through the hair or visually checking for obstructions, since the concern is *chatzitza*, an interposition preventing contact between the hair and water. What Ezra added was the requirement of actual combing — *chafifa* — rather than simple inspection.

*Tosafot* raises an interesting point here: the elaborate bathing and full-body preparation that women perform before *mikvah* immersion today appears to be an ancient practice with roots in the *Gemara*, but it does not seem to have been formally part of Ezra's *takana*. The *takana* addressed specifically the hair, since hair is harder to inspect and prone to knotting; the full body, being more readily visible, was not given the same formal ordinance, though the practice of thorough bodily preparation developed later. Even regarding the hair, a question remains: does the obligation require combing alone, or combing together with washing?

### Peddlers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁיְּהוּ רוֹכְלִין מְחַזְּרִין בָּעֲיָירוֹת – מִשּׁוּם תַּכְשִׁיטֵי נָשִׁים, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִתְגַּנּוּ עַל בַּעֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the next of Ezra's ordinances: **And that peddlers should circulate through** all **the towns.** This Gemara explains that this is **because** peddlers supply **women's cosmetics,** and therefore Ezra instituted this practice **so that** women **should not become unattractive to their husbands.**

Ezra further ordained that fragrance sellers and peddlers should have the right to travel through the various towns and villages — *rochlin machzirin ba'ayarot* — and communities may not bar them. The purpose is *mishum tachshitei nashim*, to enable women to purchase jewelry and fragrances, *k'dei shelo yitganu al ba'aleihen* — so that they not become unattractive to their husbands. This *takana* is less obviously religious in character, but it connects to the same concern for domestic harmony that underlies the *sinar* ordinance. The running theme in Ezra's women's *takanos* is the stability and quality of family life.

### Tevilah Ba'alei Keri

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיקֵּן טְבִילָה לְבַעֲלֵי קְרָיִין – דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא! דִּכְתִיב: ״וְאִישׁ כִּי תֵצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ שִׁכְבַת זָרַע, וְרָחַץ אֶת בְּשָׂרוֹ בַּמַּיִם״! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא לִתְרוּמָה וְקָדָשִׁים; אֲתָא הוּא, תִּיקֵּן אֲפִילּוּ לְדִבְרֵי תוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the last of the ten ordinances: **And he instituted** the requirement of **immersion for those who experienced a seminal emission.** The Gemara asks: But this **is** required **by Torah law, as it is written: "And if the flow of seed go out from a man, then he shall bathe** all **his flesh in water"** (Leviticus 15:16). The Gemara answers: **By Torah law** immersion **is** required only if one wishes to partake **of *teruma* or sacrificial** meat. Ezra **came** and further **instituted** that immersion is necessary **even for** reciting or studying **matters of Torah.**

Finally, Ezra ordained immersion in the *mikvah* for a *ba'al keri* — a man who has had a seminal emission. The *Gemara* notes that *d'oraisa*, such a man is already obligated to immerse — the Torah says *v'ish ki tetze mimenu shichvat zera v'rachatz bamayim et kol besaro*. The biblical obligation, however, concerns only his status with regard to *terumah* and *kodashim* — without immersion he may not eat *terumah* or participate in sacrificial service. What Ezra added — *afilu l'divrei Torah* — was that a *ba'al keri* is prohibited from learning Torah or *davening* until he has immersed. He was not formally reinstituting the immersion itself but rather extending its implications to Torah study and prayer.

This is the source of the practice among certain *chassidim* of immersing in the *mikvah* every morning. Since a man who had relations the night before would be required to immerse before *davening*, and since attendance at the *mikvah* each morning would advertise what transpired the previous night, the practice developed of immersing every single morning, removing any inference. *Tosafot*, however, rules that we do not follow Ezra's *takana* in this regard. Rather, we rule in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira — *divrei Torah ein mikablin tumah*, words of Torah cannot become impure — and Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira effectively overrode Ezra's extension. A question naturally arises about the authority of a later *amora* to abrogate a *takana* of Ezra, but the practical factor was that the community was simply unable to abide by it. The *takana* thus effectively fell away, though certain communities preserved it — giving rise to the morning *mikvah* practice that persists in some circles today.

## Jerusalem Laws

### Jerusalem Property

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲשָׂרָה דְּבָרִים נֶאֱמְרוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם: אֵין הַבַּיִת חָלוּט בָּהּ; וְאֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה; וְאֵינָהּ נַעֲשֵׂית עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת;
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages taught that one may not raise chickens in Jerusalem. The Gemara cites a *baraita* that contains a list of other *halakhot* that are unique to Jerusalem. **Ten matters were stated with regard to Jerusalem: A house** situated **in** Jerusalem does **not** become **irredeemable** one year after its sale. Those who sell houses in other walled cities have the right to buy back their property for one year after the transaction. If they fail to do so, the house becomes the permanent possession of the buyer (see Leviticus 25:29–30). This *halakha* does not apply to houses in Jerusalem. **And** its Elders do **not bring a heifer whose neck is broken** as required when a murder victim is found near a city and the murderer is unknown (see Deuteronomy 21:1–9); **and** it **cannot become an idolatrous city** (see Deuteronomy 13:13–19).

The *Gemara* now presents ten laws specific to Jerusalem. The first is *ein bayis chalut bah*: a house sold in Jerusalem cannot become permanently transferred to the buyer. In all other walled cities, if a house is not repurchased within the year, the buyer acquires it permanently. In Jerusalem, the seller retains the right to repurchase until the *yovel*. The second is *ein mevia egla arufa*: if a murdered victim is found outside Jerusalem, the *egla arufa* — the calf whose neck is broken in a special ceremony — is not brought for Jerusalem. Third, *v'eino na'asit ir hanidachas*: if the entire city worships idolatry, the law of *ir hanidachas* that mandates total destruction does not apply to Jerusalem.

### Urban Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָהּ מִטַּמְּאָה בִּנְגָעִים; וְאֵין מוֹצִיאִין בָּהּ זִיזִין וּגְזוּזְטְרָאוֹת; וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ אַשְׁפַּתּוֹת; וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ כִּבְשׁוֹנוֹת; וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ גַּנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסוֹת – חוּץ מִגַּנּוֹת וורָדִין שֶׁהָיוּ מִימוֹת נְבִיאִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים; וְאֵין מְגַדְּלִים בָּהּ תַּרְנְגוֹלִים; וְאֵין מְלִינִים בָּהּ אֶת הַמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues its list: **And** a house in Jerusalem **cannot become ritually impure** with the impurity **of leprous sores; and one may not build out projections or balconies [*gezuztraot*]** from houses that are **in** Jerusalem. **And one may not establish garbage dumps in** Jerusalem; **and one may not build kilns in it; and one may not plant gardens and orchards [*pardesot*] in it, except for** the **rose gardens that were** already there **from the times of the early prophets; and one may not raise chickens in it; and** finally, **one may not leave a corpse overnight in** Jerusalem.

The *Gemara* continues: *ein motzi'in bah zizin u'gezustra'ot* — no beams or balconies may protrude over Jerusalem's streets. *Ein osin bah ashpatot* — no garbage dumps may be made in Jerusalem. *Ein osin bah kivshonot* — no kilns for firing pottery may operate in Jerusalem. *Ein osin bah gannot u'fardesot* — no gardens or orchards may be planted in Jerusalem.

The prohibition on protruding beams is explained on two grounds: *mipnei ohel hatuma*, because a protruding beam above a street would spread *tumah* from a corpse to people far removed from the body, and Jerusalem's proximity to the temple service makes any expansion of *tumah* particularly undesirable; and *mishum olei regalim*, because the pilgrims who flood Jerusalem for the festivals would risk striking their heads on protruding structures. The prohibition on garbage dumps is *mishum shekatzim* — garbage attracts rodents, and dead rodents are themselves a source of *tumah*. The kilns are prohibited *mishum kutra* — because of smoke and air pollution. The gardens and orchards, counterintuitively, are prohibited *mishum sircha* — because they emit unpleasant odors, particularly rotting fallen fruit; apple orchards are especially notable in this regard, and the requirement for fertilizer adds to the problem.

### Animal & Death Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין הַבַּיִת חָלוּט בָּהּ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְקָם הַבַּיִת אֲשֶׁר לוֹ חוֹמָה לַצְּמִתֻת לַקֹּנֶה אֹתוֹ לְדֹרֹתָיו״, וְקָסָבַר לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה יְרוּשָׁלַיִם לִשְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses these ten *halakhot* pertaining to Jerusalem, one by one: **A house** situated **in** it does **not** become **irredeemable** one year after its sale. The reason is **that it is written:** "And if it is not redeemed within the space of a full year, then **the house** that is in the **walled** city **shall be made sure in perpetuity to him who bought it, throughout his generations"** (Leviticus 25:30). **And** the *tanna* who taught this *baraita* **maintains** that **Jerusalem was not apportioned to** any single one of **the tribes** of Israel; rather, it is considered common property. Since no one has ancestral ownership of any house in Jerusalem, its houses cannot be sold permanently.

*Ein migdalin bah tarnegolim* — no chickens may be raised in Jerusalem. This is *mishum kodashim*: chickens forage freely and might pick up small pieces of bone and deposit them on someone's *terumah* or *kodashim*, rendering them *tamei*. *Ein malinin bah et ha-meis* — a corpse may not remain overnight in Jerusalem. The *Gemara* notes that no formal textual source is cited for this; it is simply a tradition, though it evidently connects to the *kedushah* and *taharah* of Jerusalem.

### Scriptural Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה – דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי יִמָּצֵא חָלָל בָּאֲדָמָה אֲשֶׁר ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ נוֹתֵן לְךָ לְרִשְׁתָּהּ״, וִירוּשָׁלַיִם לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה לִשְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the next *halakha*: **And** its inhabitants do **not bring a heifer whose neck is broken.** The reason is **that it is written: "If one is found slain in the land that the Lord your God gives you to possess it"** (Deuteronomy 21:1). **And,** again, the *tanna* who taught this *baraita* **maintains** that **Jerusalem was not apportioned to** any one of **the tribes** of Israel. Therefore, it is not included in the description: "The land that the Lord your God gives you to possess it."

The *Gemara* then provides the scriptural basis for those laws that derive from the principle *lo nitchalka Yerushalayim l'shvatim* — Jerusalem was not divided among the tribes and therefore does not belong to any single tribe.

For *ein bayis chalut bah*, the verse reads *vekama habayis asher lo chomah lakone oto l'dorotav* — the house in a walled city passes to the buyer *l'dorotav*, for his generations, implying permanent ancestral inheritance. But Jerusalem, having never been apportioned to a tribe, lacks that sense of owned ancestral land being transferred.

For *ein mevia egla arufa*, the verse reads *ki yimatze chalal ba'adamah asher Hashem Elokecha noten lecha lerishtah* — "the land which Hashem gives you to inherit." The word *lerishtah* carries the connotation of inheritance and private possession. Since Jerusalem was never "given" in that tribal-inheritance sense, it falls outside the ordinance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָהּ נַעֲשֵׂית עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת – דִּכְתִיב: ״עָרֶיךָ״, וִירוּשָׁלַיִם לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה לִשְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states: **And** it **cannot become an idolatrous city.** The reason is **that it is written,** in the introduction of the passage dealing with the *halakha* of an idolatrous city: "If you shall hear tell concerning one of **your cities,** which the Lord your God gives you to dwell there" (Deuteronomy 13:13). **And** the *tanna* who taught this *baraita* maintains that **Jerusalem was not apportioned to** any one of **the tribes** of Israel. It is therefore not included in the description "one of your cities, which the Lord your God gives you to dwell there."

For *v'eino na'asit ir hanidachas*, the relevant term is *irecha* — your city — implying a city that is possessed and owned. Jerusalem was not parceled out to be anyone's possession in that sense.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינָהּ מִטַּמְּאָה בִּנְגָעִים – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְנָתַתִּי נֶגַע צָרַעַת בְּבֵית אֶרֶץ אֲחֻזַּתְכֶם״, וִירוּשָׁלַיִם לֹא נִתְחַלְּקָה לִשְׁבָטִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches: **And** a house in Jerusalem does **not become ritually impure** with the impurity **of leprous sores.** The reason is **that it is written: "And I put the plague of leprosy in a house of the land of your possession"** (Leviticus 14:34). **And** the *tanna* who taught this *baraita* maintains that **Jerusalem was not apportioned to** any one of **the tribes** of Israel. It is therefore not included in the description "a house of the land of your possession."

For *v'einah metame'ah b'nega'im*, the verse says *venatati nega tzara'at beveit eretz achuzatchem* — I will place the affliction of *tzara'at* in a house of the land of your *achuzah*, your holding. Since Jerusalem was not apportioned as anyone's *achuzah*, the law of a house afflicted with *tzara'at* does not apply there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מוֹצִיאִין בָּהּ זִיזִין וּגְזוּזְטְרָאוֹת – מִפְּנֵי אֹהֶל הַטּוּמְאָה, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּלָא לִיתַּזְקוּ עוֹלֵי רְגָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the next *halakha*: **And one may not build out projections or balconies** from houses that are **in** Jerusalem. The Gemara provides two reasons for this prohibition. First, it is **due to** the danger of contracting **ritual impurity** by being in **the** same **tent** as a corpse, i.e., under the same roof, in which case the impurity spreads to all items under the roof. If even a small part of a corpse is under a balcony, everyone who passes under that balcony is rendered impure. Many people come to Jerusalem to sacrifice offerings, and they must maintain a state of ritual purity. The other reason is **so that those** great crowds of **pilgrims not be injured** by colliding with the projections.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ אַשְׁפַּתּוֹת – מִשּׁוּם שְׁקָצִים.
>
> **Translation:** The next *halakha* pertaining to Jerusalem is: **And one may not establish garbage dumps in it.** The Gemara explains that the reason is **due to** the **repugnant creatures** that are attracted to such heaps and impart ritual impurity upon their death.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ כִּבְשׁוֹנוֹת – מִשּׁוּם קוּטְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states: **And one may not build kilns in** Jerusalem. The reason is **due to the** unsightly **smoke** produced by kilns. The Sages sought to preserve the beauty of Jerusalem and the Temple.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ גַּנּוֹת וּפַרְדֵּסִין – מִשּׁוּם סִירְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **And one may not plant gardens and orchards in it.** This is **due to the odor** emitted by these places, either from discarded weeds or from fertilizer.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְגַדְּלִים בָּהּ תַּרְנְגוֹלִים – מִשּׁוּם קֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The next *halakha* on the list is: **And one may not raise chickens in** Jerusalem. The Gemara explains that this is **due to the sacrificial** meat that is consumed in Jerusalem. Since chickens peck in the garbage, they are likely to pick up items that impart ritual impurity and bring them into contact with the consecrated food, which may not be eaten in an impure state.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְלִינִים בָּהּ אֶת הַמֵּת – גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the last *halakha*: **And one may not leave a corpse overnight in it.** The Gemara notes that this prohibition is **a tradition;** there is no known explanation for it.

## Pig Raising Ban

### Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְגַדְּלִין חֲזִירִים בְּכׇל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that **one may not raise pigs anywhere.**

The *Gemara* now departs from the structured list of Jerusalem laws and returns to the broader *mishnah*, which lists prohibitions that apply more generally. The *mishnah* had already discussed restrictions on grazing animals in the land of Israel, but the next prohibition is more sweeping: *ein migdalin chazirim b'chol makom* — one may not raise pigs anywhere at all.

### Siege Account

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּשֶׁצָּרוּ בֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי זֶה עַל זֶה, הָיָה הוּרְקָנוֹס מִבִּפְנִים וַאֲרִיסְטוֹבְּלוּס מִבַּחוּץ. וּבְכׇל יוֹם הָיוּ מְשַׁלְשְׁלִים לָהֶם בְּקוּפָּה דִּינָרִין, וְהָיוּ מַעֲלִים לָהֶם תְּמִידִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* the background for this *halakha*: **When** the members of **the house of Hasmonean** monarchy **were at war with each other, Hyrcanus,** one of the parties to this war, **was inside** the besieged Jerusalem, **while** his brother **Aristobulus,** the other contender to the throne, was **on the outside. And every day** the people inside **would lower down money in a box** from the Temple walls, to purchase sheep to sacrifice, **and** those on other side **would** take the money and **send up** sheep **to them** over the wall for the **daily offerings.**

A *baraita* narrates the historical event that generated this prohibition. During the period of the Chashmonaim, the royal house fell into civil war — *beit Chashmonai zeh al zeh*, one faction against another. Horkanus was inside Jerusalem and Aristobulus was besieging it from outside. Despite the hostility between the factions, both parties observed Jewish practice: each day the besieged city would lower a basket of *dinarim* — coins — to the besiegers, who would in return send up animals for the daily *tamid* sacrifice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה שָׁם זָקֵן אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה מַכִּיר בְּחׇכְמַת יְווֹנִית, אָמַר לָהֶם: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בָּעֲבוֹדָה – אֵין נִמְסָרִים בְּיֶדְכֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **There was a certain elder there who was familiar with Greek wisdom,** and **he said** to those besieging Jerusalem: **As long as they occupy themselves with the** Temple **service, they will not be delivered into your hands.**

Among the besiegers was an elder who possessed *chochmat yevanit* — Greek wisdom. What this means is somewhat puzzling, since what this elder is about to say seems essentially religious in content. A note in the edition suggests it refers to a kind of coded or allusive language, perhaps cryptic speech used within the royal household that would not be understood by the masses. Some at the shiur suggest it may indicate a Hellenist perspective on divine protection through temple worship — an interesting possibility, though the details of the historical conflict between the religious and Hellenist factions within the Chashmonaim would require more research to confirm. This elder warned the besiegers: *kol zeman she'oskin ba'avodah ein nimtsarim beyadechem* — as long as those inside are able to perform the divine service, you will not be able to conquer them.

### Pig Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָחָר שִׁלְשְׁלוּ דִּינָרִין בְּקוּפָּה, וְהֶעֱלוּ לָהֶם חֲזִיר. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לַחֲצִי הַחוֹמָה, נָעַץ צִפׇּרְנָיו בַּחוֹמָה, וְנִזְדַּעְזְעָה אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת פַּרְסָה עַל אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת פַּרְסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The next day they lowered down money in a box** as usual, **but** this time **they sent up to them a pig. When the pig reached to the midpoint of the** Temple **wall it stuck its hooves into the wall, and Eretz Yisrael quaked** over an area of **four hundred parasangs by four hundred parasangs.**

The next day, instead of lowering the usual animals in exchange for the coins, the besieged city received back a pig. As the pig was being hauled up partway along the wall — *keivan shehigia lachatzi hachomah* — it dug its claws into the stones, because, as Rashi notes, that is what pigs do when forcibly dragged somewhere against their will. At that moment, *venizda'aza'ah Eretz Yisrael arba me'ot parsa al arba me'ot parsa* — the entire land of Israel, assumed to span four hundred *parsangs* by four hundred *parsangs*, shook with the sacrilege of the act.

### Curse Enacted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה אָמְרוּ: אָרוּר הָאִישׁ שֶׁיְּגַדֵּל חֲזִירִים, וְאָרוּר הָאָדָם שֶׁיְּלַמֵּד אֶת בְּנוֹ חׇכְמַת יְווֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** **At that time** the Sages **said: Cursed be the man who raises pigs, and cursed be the man who teaches his son Greek wisdom.**

At that moment a declaration was made: *arur ha'ish sheyegadel chazirim* — cursed be anyone who raises pigs. And alongside it: *arur ha'adam sheyelamed et beno chochmat yevanit* — cursed be anyone who teaches his son Greek wisdom.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַל אוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה שָׁנִינוּ: מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבָּא עוֹמֶר מִגַּנּוֹת הַצְּרִיפִין, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם מִבִּקְעַת עֵין סוֹכֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** it was **concerning that time** of siege that **we learned** in a mishna: There was **an incident** in which the barley for the ***omer* offering came from the gardens of Tzerifin,** far from Jerusalem, **and** the wheat for **the two loaves** of *Shavuot* was brought **from the valley of Ein Sokher.** Barley and wheat could not be brought from any nearer because the besiegers had destroyed all the produce around Jerusalem. This concludes the *baraita*.

The *Gemara* records that the *mishnah* in *Menachot* notes a consequence of the siege: the *omer* offering had to be brought from the gardens of *Tzrifin* — a distant location — and the *shtei halechem* from the valley of *Ein Socher*, because the besieged city had insufficient local wheat for these sacrifices.

### Tosafot Questions

The essay explains that *Tosafot* raises two significant questions about this narrative. The first concerns the prohibition on pig-raising itself. We already have a general principle that one may not engage in business with non-kosher items — *asur la'asot schora b'chol devarim hateme'im*. If so, why was a special *takana* needed? *Tosafot* responds that the general prohibition on commerce in non-kosher items applies only when those items are being dealt with as food. One might raise pigs for other purposes — their hides, their fat (such as lard), and other by-products — and in such a case the business prohibition would not apply. Hence the need for the specific *gezerah* against raising pigs at all. An alternative answer is that the general rabbinic prohibition on such commerce had not yet been enacted at the time, though by the era of the *mishnah* it was already in place, which raises its own question about why the *mishnah* still needed to state the pig prohibition separately.

The second question *Tosafot* raises is temporal: the curse against *chochmat yevanit* was proclaimed at this moment of the Chashmonaim, yet another *Gemara* records a prohibition against learning *chochmat yevanit* that was enacted at the time of the destruction of the *beit hamikdash*. *Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing between a curse — which was declared in the Chashmonaean period and left individual compliance voluntary, if morally censured — and a formal rabbinic prohibition, which was enacted only at the time of the *churban*. The earlier declaration was an imprecation; the later enactment was binding legislation. *Tosafot* acknowledges that there remains some apparent overlap and redundancy in these formulations.
