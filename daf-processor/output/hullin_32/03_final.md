# Hullin 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kavana Requirements

### Intro: General Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, נְהִי דְּלָא בָּעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה לִזְבִיחָה, לַחֲתִיכָה בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Natan and hold that slaughter of non-sacred animals requires intent would say: **Although we do not require intent to slaughter** non-sacred animals, **we require** intent **to cut** the neck of the animal. Throwing the knife down is not sufficient.

Our discussion picks up on 31b, where the *gemara* had been addressing whether *shechita* is valid without intention and had broadened this into a larger question about whether non-sacred, non-*kodshim* acts require *kavana* at all. The *gemara* had extended the analysis to *tevilah*, raising the question of how far such a generalization ought to reach. It had established, moreover, that even those who require *kavana* for *tevilah* concede that *shechita* is governed by a distinct verse, and that verse indicates *kavana* is not required there. We now turn to a more precise question: what *type* of intention is, or is not, being discussed?

### Rabbis' Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁחֲטָה בְּהֵמָה אַחֶרֶת עִמָּהּ, לְרַבִּי נָתָן – פָּרָה פְּסוּלָה בְּהֵמָה כְּשֵׁרָה, לְרַבָּנַן – פָּרָה כְּשֵׁרָה בְּהֵמָה פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** But if **another animal was** inadvertently **slaughtered** together **with** the red heifer in the same action, **according to Rabbi Natan,** who holds that the slaughter of non-sacred animals without intent is valid, the red **heifer is disqualified,** because an additional labor was performed with its slaughter, and the other **animal is fit** for consumption, as its slaughter was valid. **According to the Rabbis,** who hold that slaughter of non-sacred animals without intent is not considered slaughter, the red **heifer is fit** for use in the purification rite because no other labor was performed with its slaughter, and **the** other **animal is unfit** for consumption.

The passage opens with the position of the *rabbanan*, who hold that intention *is* required for *shechita*. They were the ones who ruled that throwing knives at a wall does not constitute valid *shechita*. How, then, do they account for the Torah's implicit message that *kodshim* demand *kavana* while non-sacred acts do not? Their response is precise: *nechee delo ba'inan kavana lizevicha* — granted, one need not intend for the act to constitute *zevichah*, formal ritual slaughter — *l'chatichah ba'inan*, but one must at least intend the act of cutting.

The scope of this requirement for *kavana l'chatichah* is itself a matter of discussion. It might mean no more than a general intention to cut *something* — so that if one believed one was cutting a gourd and instead cut a chicken's neck, that would suffice. Or, as *tosafot* suggests, it might mean that one must know one is cutting the *simanim* — the trachea and the esophagus — even without intending that this act will carry the *halachic* force of *shechita* and render the meat permissible.

### Kavana Levels


This range of *kavana* levels parallels the *gemara*'s broader treatment of *mitzvos tzrichos kavana*. The classic illustration involves someone walking behind a *shul* who hears a *shofar* being blown. If he intended, he has fulfilled his obligation. But the *gemara* clarifies: this may mean only that he recognized the sound as a *shofar* and not as the braying of a donkey — not that he directed his mind toward fulfilling the *mitzvah*. So too with eating *matzah*: the minimal requirement might be only that one be aware one is eating *matzah* and not a cracker, without any conscious intention to discharge an obligation.

At the furthest extreme lies the category of *mitasek* — acting in complete incidental unawareness of the physical nature of what one is doing. A person in this state does not even know at the physical level what is occurring; the act is entirely disconnected from any conscious engagement. Beyond that threshold, one knows physically what one is doing but need not grasp its *halachic* import. The *rabbanan* stake out a middle position: full *kavana* — intending that this act serve as *shechita* — is not required, but one must at least be consciously performing the act of cutting, or perhaps specifically cutting the *simanim*. Below that level lies *mitasek*, and *mitasek* is insufficient.

### Rabbi Natan Extreme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּהָא זְכָנְהוּ רַבִּי נָתָן לְרַבָּנַן, מִי כְּתִיב ״וְחָתַכְתָּ״? ״וְזָבַחְתָּ״ כְּתִיב, אִי בָּעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה לַחֲתִיכָה – אֲפִילּוּ לִזְבִיחָה נָמֵי לִיבְעֵי, אִי לָא בָּעֵינַן כַּוָּונָה לִזְבִיחָה – לַחֲתִיכָה נָמֵי לָא לִיבְעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that it was **with this** contention that **Rabbi Natan overcame the Rabbis:** He said: **Is it written** with regard to the slaughter of non-sacred animals: **And you shall cut?** It **is written: "And you shall slaughter"** (Deuteronomy 12:21). The Torah does not distinguish between cutting and slaughtering; **if we require intent for cutting, we** should **require** intent **even for slaughtering.** Conversely, **if we do not require intent for slaughtering, we should also not require** intent **for cutting.**

The striking position belongs to *rabbi natan*, whom the *gemara* ruled we follow. He maintained that even completely incidental action is valid — if one accidentally drops a knife and it performs a *shechita*, or if one is throwing darts and happens to sever the *simanim*, the *shechita* is good. These are precisely the cases we would ordinarily classify as *mitasek*, where what occurred was wholly incidental to any intention of the actor. And yet *rabbi natan*, whom the *halacha* apparently follows, holds that such an act is valid. This is a tremendous *chiddush* — that for *shechita*, *zero* intention is required, not even intention for the physical act.

### Rava's Challenge


*Rava* now advances a sharp logical challenge. *mi ksiv vechatachta?* — the Torah never wrote "and you shall *cut*." *vezavachta ktiv* — it wrote "and you shall *slaughter*." The verse demands a full act of *shechita*. So one should either require *kavana* for the complete *halachic* act of *shechita* — *kavana* that one's cutting is intended to render the meat permissible — or one should require no *kavana* at all. What is the justification for this intermediate position of *kavana l'chatichah*? Either the verse's demand for a *zevichah* imports full intentionality, in which case the *rabbanan* should require *kavana* even for the *shechita's* legal effect; or it does not, in which case there is no basis for requiring *kavana* even for the physical act of cutting.

The implicit response — consistent with the *gemara*'s broader treatment — is that without any *kavana* for even the physical act, the deed does not connect to the actor at all. One has become entirely *mitasek*. It is entirely coherent to say: at minimum, the actor must have performed *some* act; perhaps he did not define it as *shechita*, did not think about *shechita*, but he consciously performed the cutting. The *gemara*'s challenge, however, is that once we concede the Torah's demand is an act of *shechita*, there is no principled stopping point short of full *kavana* for the *halachic* act.

### Tevilah Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי נִדָּה שֶׁנֶּאֶנְסָה וְטָבְלָה? אִילֵימָא דְּאַנְסַהּ חֲבִירְתַּהּ וְאַטְבְּלַהּ, כַּוָּונָה דַּחֲבִרְתַּהּ כַּוָּונָה מְעַלַּיְיתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara revisits the matter of immersion without intent. **What are the circumstances of a menstruating woman who,** after the menstrual flow ended, **was compelled** against her will **and immersed** in a ritual bath? **If we say that another** woman **compelled her and immersed her** in a ritual bath, the immersion should be valid even according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as **the intent of another** woman **is full-fledged intent.**

The *gemara* now returns to the case of a *niddah* who immersed without intention. The question is raised: what exactly is this case? If a friend physically grabbed her and threw her into the *mikveh*, the *gemara* suggests that the friend's *kavana* substitutes for hers — *kavana dechavarta kavana mealya hi*. This is not self-evidently true. If the Torah demands that *she* perform a conscious act of *tevilah*, it is not obvious that someone else's intention to immerse her satisfies that requirement, since she has gone from being the subject of the act to being its object. Yet the *gemara* operates on the assumption that *kavana* can be transferred in this way, analogous to the earlier discussion in which one person's *kavana* was said to apply to another's vessels.

The *gemara* supports this from a *mishnah* listing a woman who is a *chereshet*, a *shoteh*, or *suma* — deaf-mute, unsound of mind, or blind — as well as a woman whose mind became temporarily disturbed. The *mishnah* states that if they have sound companions, those companions can assist them and arrange for them to eat *terumah*. The *gemara* reads this as establishing that another's intention and assistance can render the *tevilah* valid on behalf of someone whose own *kavana* is insufficient.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, בִּתְרוּמָה נָמֵי אָכְלָה, דִּתְנַן: הַחֵרֶשֶׁת, וְהַשּׁוֹטָה, וְהַסּוֹמָא, וְשֶׁנִּטְרְפָה דַּעְתָּהּ, אִם יֵשׁ לָהֶן פִּקְּחוֹת מְתַקְּנוֹת אוֹתָן – אוֹכְלוֹת בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** in that case the immersion enables her to **partake of *teruma* as well, as we learned** in a mishna (*Nidda* 13b): In the case of a woman who is **a deaf-mute, or an imbecile, or blind, or who went insane,** and is therefore unable to examine herself reliably, **if** one of these women **has a competent** friend, that friend **prepares her** by examining her and immersing them in a ritual bath. And on that basis the incompetent woman **may partake of *teruma*.**

The *gemara* then turns to *rav pappa*'s formulation for *rabbi natan*'s position: the paradigmatic case of *tevilah* without *any* intention — not even for the physical act — is a woman who fell off a bridge into a river that would have qualified as a *mikveh*. She did not intend to enter the water at all. And for the *rabbanan*, the parallel case is a woman who went into a river to cool off — she intended the physical act of entering the water but had no *kavana* for *tevilah* as such.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְרַבִּי נָתָן, שֶׁנָּפְלָה מִן הַגֶּשֶׁר, וּלְרַבָּנַן, שֶׁיָּרְדָה לְהָקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: According to Rabbi Natan,** who does not require intent for the slaughter of non-sacred animals, immersion against her will that renders it permitted for a woman to engage in intercourse with her husband is in a case **where she fell from a bridge** into a river, with no intent at all. **According to the Rabbis,** who require intent to cut for slaughter to be valid and intent to enter the water for immersion to be valid, it is referring to a case **where she descended** into the water **to cool** herself, with no thought of purification.

This elegantly demonstrates how the *gemara* abstracts and generalizes the debate. The argument over *kavana* in *shechita* is understood to reflect a broader dispute about what level of intention is necessary for acts that change status in the non-*kodshim* realm. For *rabbi natan*, zero intention suffices — the act may be entirely incidental, what we would ordinarily classify as *mitasek* — and this is a position of enormous scope. For the *rabbanan*, intention for the physical act is the minimum threshold.

## Parah Adumah Case

### Rule: One Animal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁחַט פָּרָה וְשָׁחַט בְּהֵמָה אַחֶרֶת עִמָּהּ, לְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of the dispute between Rabbi Natan and the Rabbis. **Rava said:** If one **slaughtered** a red **heifer and** in the same action **slaughtered another animal** together **with it, everyone agrees** that the red heifer is **disqualified.**

*Rava* now introduces a distinct application that, while not strictly a *kavana* question, illuminates the *kavana* debate in a revealing way. The case involves the *shechita* of the *parah adumah*. *rashi* notes that there are two independent problems that can disqualify the *shechita* of the *parah adumah*. The first is performing another *melachah* simultaneously — the principle of *v'shachat osah*, she must be slaughtered alone. The second, and the one operative here, is the requirement of *v'lo osah v'chavertah* — one may not slaughter it together with another animal at the same time.

*Rashi* clarifies, based on the subsequent *gemara*, that the problem of doing another *melachah* simultaneously applies only when one consciously intends that other activity — because then one's full focus is not on the *parah*. But the problem of *v'lo osah v'chavertah* is independent of intention: even if one is entirely focused on the *parah* and the second animal's *shechita* is wholly incidental, the question remains whether that incidental act constitutes a valid *shechita* — and if so, the *parah* is invalidated.

### Accidental Second

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! נִשְׁחֲטָה בְּהֵמָה אַחֶרֶת לְרַבִּי נָתָן אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתָהּ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא אוֹתָהּ וַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי – כְּגוֹן שֶׁשָּׁחַט שְׁתֵּי פָּרוֹת בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה דְּחוּלִּין אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: That is **obvious;** why did Rava have to teach that? The Gemara answers: It **was necessary for** Rava to teach the *halakha* **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan** in the case where **another animal was** inadvertently **slaughtered** in the same action with the red heifer. It may **enter your mind to say: The Merciful One states** with regard to the slaughter of the red heifer: "And you shall give it to Elazar the priest…**and he shall slaughter it** before him" (Numbers 19:3), from which it is inferred: One may slaughter it, **but not it and another** animal simultaneously. **And what are the circumstances** of that prohibition? It is a case **where one slaughtered two** red **heifers at the same time. But** in a case where one slaughtered a red heifer together with **a non-sacred animal, say no,** that it does **not** disqualify the red heifer. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that even slaughter with a non-sacred animal disqualifies the red heifer.

The real *chiddush* lies in the case where the second animal's *shechita* was entirely unintended. One was focused solely on the *parah*, and another animal happened to be in the path of the knife. The debate between *rabbi natan* and the *rabbanan* now maps directly onto this case.

According to *rabbi natan*, *shechita* without intention is fully valid. Therefore, the second animal was *shechted* validly — and since a valid *shechita* of another animal occurred simultaneously with the *parah*, the *parah* is *psulah*. *l'rabbi natan*: *parah psulah, behemah kesherah*. For the *rabbanan*, by contrast, the second animal's *shechita* lacked even the minimal *kavana l'chatichah* — one was not intending to cut it at all. Without that threshold of intention, the act is insufficient, the second animal was not validly *shechted*, and the *parah* therefore remains *kesherah*. *l'rabbanan*: *parah kesherah, behemah psulah*.

It is worth noting that even the *rabbanan's* position here is itself a *chiddush*. The *rabbanan* require *kavana l'chatichah* — intention to cut. Here, there was certainly a *kavana* to cut *something* — namely, the *parah*. Yet the *rabbanan* hold that this is insufficient: one needs *kavana* specifically for cutting the thing one is cutting, not merely a general intention to cut that happens to encompass another object simultaneously. One's *kavana* must be directed at the particular item being cut.

### Intent vs. Side Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָתַךְ דַּלַּעַת עִמָּהּ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פְּסוּלָה, נֶחְתְּכָה דַּלַּעַת עִמָּהּ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rava adds: If one slaughtered a red heifer and in the same action **cut a gourd** together **with it, everyone agrees** that the red heifer **is disqualified.** If one slaughtered a red heifer and **a gourd was** inadvertently **cut** together **with it** in the same action, **everyone agrees** that the red heifer is **fit** for use in the purification rite, as that is not labor that disqualifies a red heifer and it is also not excluded by the derivation from the verse "And he shall slaughter it."

The Gemara notes that this ruling is not self-evident and was indeed necessary. One might have thought that *v'lo osah v'chavertah* applies only when one *shechts* two *paros adumos* simultaneously. *kamashma lan* — the ruling teaches that even if the second animal is an ordinary *chullin* animal, the prohibition of *v'lo osah v'chavertah* still applies when *rabbi natan's* position renders that second *shechita* valid.

The final application distinguishes between a *melachah* one intends and one that occurs incidentally. If while *shechting* the *parah adumah* one deliberately used the opportunity to cut a gourd placed alongside — *chatach d'la'as imah* — this is a case of intending another *melachah*, and that renders the *parah* invalid by the separate disqualification of engaging in other work simultaneously.

However, if a gourd happened to get cut incidentally — *nechtechah d'la'as imah* — without any intention, then *divrei hakol kesherah*: by universal agreement, the *parah* remains valid. This is so for two reasons: first, one did not intend another *melachah*, so the problem of distracted focus does not apply; second, cutting a gourd is not an act of *shechita*, so the problem of *v'lo osah v'chavertah*, which concerns only the simultaneous *shechita* of another animal, is likewise inapplicable. What happened incidentally to the gourd creates no disqualification whatsoever.

The overarching *chiddush* of all these cases, to reiterate, is *rabbi natan's* extreme ruling — upheld as *halacha* — that *shechita* can be valid even as pure *mitasek*, an act entirely incidental to any intention of the actor. The one requirement that *shechita* always demands is *koach gavra* — the physical energy of a person; a knife that falls off a table on its own does not suffice. But beyond that, according to *rabbi natan*, no *kavana* whatsoever is required.

## Shehiya (Pausing)

### Intro: Shehiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָפְלָה סַכִּין וְהִגְבִּיהָהּ, נָפְלוּ כֵּלָיו וְהִגְבִּיהָן, הִשְׁחִיז אֶת הַסַּכִּין וְעָף, בָּא חֲבֵירוֹ וְשָׁחַט, אִם שָׁהָה כְּדֵי שְׁחִיטָה – פְּסוּלָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם שָׁהָה כְּדֵי בִיקּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If, when one was in the middle of slaughtering an animal, **the knife fell and he lifted it** and then completed the slaughter, or if **his garments fell and he lifted them** and then completed the slaughter, or if **he had honed the knife and grew weary** before completing the slaughter and **another came and slaughtered** the animal, **if he interrupted** the slaughter in one of these ways or in a different way for an interval **equivalent** to the duration of an act of **slaughter,** the slaughter is **not valid. Rabbi Shimon says:** The slaughter is not valid **if he interrupted** the slaughter for an interval **equivalent** to the duration of **an examination.**

We now turn to a new *mishnah* and a different category of *psul*: *shehiya*, the invalidation of *shechita* due to pausing in the middle of the act. The *mishnah* presents several scenarios in which a pause may have occurred. The *shochet* dropped the knife mid-*shechita* and had to retrieve it. Or he stopped to pick up fallen objects. Or he had been sharpening his knife just before *shechita* — a physically taxing activity — and grew faint mid-way through, requiring a colleague to complete the act.

The *mishnah* states: *im shah k'dei shechitah, psulah* — if one paused for the duration it takes to perform another *shechita*, the act is invalid. *rabbi shimon* dissents: the measure is not the time to perform another *shechita* but the time to check (*bikur*) the knife.

It is worth noting that *shehiya* is not a *halacha* unique to *shechita*. It reflects a general principle applicable wherever pausing can be said to break a continuous process. When one pauses mid-*Megillah* reading, mid-*Shemoneh Esrei*, or mid-*Shema*, the question is similarly whether the pause was long enough to constitute a break. The standard measure throughout these contexts is typically the time it would take to perform the entire activity from the beginning. A pause shorter than that is not considered a true interruption. *rabbi shimon's* measure — the time to check the knife — is therefore an exceptional departure from this general framework.

### Time Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי כְּדֵי שְׁחִיטָה? אָמַר רַב: כְּדֵי שְׁחִיטַת בְּהֵמָה אַחֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of an interval **equivalent** to the duration of an act of **slaughter? Rav said:** It means an interval **equivalent** to the duration of an act of **slaughter of another animal,** not the duration required to complete the act of slaughter that was interrupted.

*Mai k'dei shechitah?* — How precisely does one calculate the duration of another *shechita*? *rav* answers: *k'dei shechitas beheima acheres* — the time it takes to *shecht* a different animal, meaning a complete act of *shechita*, not the remaining portion of the current one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לֵיהּ רַב כָּהֲנָא וְרַב אַסִּי לְרַב: כְּדֵי שְׁחִיטַת בְּהֵמָה לִבְהֵמָה, וְעוֹף לְעוֹף, אוֹ דִלְמָא אַף בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹף?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana and Rav Asi said to Rav:** Is the interruption that invalidates the slaughter an interval **equivalent** to the duration of an act of **slaughter of** another **animal for an animal,** i.e., in a case where an animal was being slaughtered, **and** an interval equivalent to the duration of an act of slaughter **of** another **bird for a bird? Or perhaps** it is an interval equivalent to the duration of an act of slaughter **of an animal even for a bird,** and if the interruption is any shorter it does not invalidate the slaughter?

*Rav Kahana* and *rav ashi* pressed *rav* with a further question: does this mean the time to *shecht* an animal of the same type as the one being *shechted* — *beheima* compared against *beheima*, *of* compared against *of* — or does it mean a fixed standard, always the time it takes to *shecht* a large animal (*beheima*), even when one is in the middle of *shechting* a bird (*of*)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא הֲוָה בְּדִיחְנָא בֵּיהּ בְּחַבִּיבִי, דֶּאֱישַׁיְּילֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav **said to them:** When we studied this topic **I** did **not** feel sufficiently **intimate with my beloved** uncle, Rabbi Ḥiyya, such **that I** could **ask him** that question. Rav was able only to transmit that which he heard from Rabbi Ḥiyya, but was unable to resolve their dilemma.

*Rav* gave a remarkable response: he said he had not been comfortable enough (*lo havni bedichna*) with his teacher, his uncle *rabbi chiya*, to press him with this question at the time he learned this *halacha*. He therefore did not know the answer. Nevertheless, the *gemara* records that *rav* himself did arrive at a position.

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: אָמַר רַב: כְּדֵי שְׁחִיטַת בְּהֵמָה לִבְהֵמָה, וְעוֹף לְעוֹף, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹף, וְכֵן כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that there is an amoraic dispute with regard to this matter. **Rav said:** An interval **equivalent** to the duration of an act of **slaughter of** another **animal for an animal, and** an interval equivalent to the duration of an act of slaughter **of** another **bird for a bird. And Shmuel said:** An interval equivalent to the duration of an act of slaughter **of an animal even for a bird. And likewise, when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** An interval equivalent to the duration of an act of slaughter **of an animal even for a bird.**

*Rav* ruled strictly: *k'dei shechitas beheima le-beheima ve-of le-of* — the measure is calibrated to the type of animal being *shechted*. If one is *shechting* a bird, the permissible pause is only as long as it takes to *shecht* another bird, which is a shorter standard. *shmuel*, by contrast, ruled leniently: *afilu beheima le-of* — even when *shechting* a bird, the measure is the time it takes to *shecht* a large animal, which is a fixed and more generous standard. *rabbi yochanan* likewise ruled leniently, as transmitted by *ravin* upon his arrival from *eretz yisrael*.

### Rabbi Chanina Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא אָמַר: כְּדֵי שֶׁיָּבִיא בְּהֵמָה אַחֶרֶת וְיִשְׁחוֹט. יָבִיא אֲפִילּוּ מֵעָלְמָא? נָתַתָּ דְּבָרֶיךָ לְשִׁיעוּרִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥananya said:** An interval **equivalent** to the period in **which one can bring another animal and slaughter** it. The Gemara asks: Does that mean the time in which **one can bring** an animal **even from anywhere else,** regardless of the distance? Occasionally, there is no animal available in close proximity. If so, **you have rendered your statement** subject **to circumstances;** it does not apply uniformly to all cases.

*Rabbi Chanina* went further still, ruling that the measure is *k'dei she-yavi behemah acheres ve-yishchot* — the time it takes to bring another animal and *shecht* it. The *gemara* immediately objects: *afilu me-alma?* If one must include the time to bring an animal from wherever it might be — a nearby market or one miles away — then no fixed standard can be established, and one has given over the measurement to infinite variability. *natasata devareicha leshiurim* — you have rendered your words without a defined quantity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עוֹמֶדֶת לְהַטִּיל אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** The practical difference **between** the opinion of Rabbi Ḥananya and that of the other Sages is in a case where there is an animal **standing** before the slaughterer and it is necessary **to cast** it to the ground in order to slaughter it. Even Rabbi Ḥananya disregards the time required to bring the animal from elsewhere. But Rabbi Ḥananya holds that the slaughter is invalidated only if the interruption lasts an interval equivalent to the duration of the act of casting the animal to the ground and slaughtering it. The Rabbis invalidate the slaughter even if an interval has elapsed that is equivalent to the duration of the act of slaughtering the animal, without casting it to the ground.

*Rav Pappa* resolves this: *rabbi chanina* does not mean bringing from the marketplace. Rather, the dispute concerns a cow that is already standing before you — *omedes lehachil ita beinaihu*. The question is whether the preparatory process of getting the animal into position for *shechita* — lifting it and causing it to crouch so that the neck is exposed — is included in the measure of "the time it takes to *shecht*." According to *rabbi chanina* as clarified by *rav pappa*, this preparation time is included, making his standard considerably more lenient than the others.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: כְּדֵי שֶׁיַּגְבִּיהֶנָּה וְיַרְבִּיצֶנָּה וְיִשְׁחוֹט, דַּקָּה לְדַקָּה וְגַסָּה לְגַסָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites an additional opinion with regard to the duration of the interruption that invalidates slaughter. **They said in the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **in the name of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina:** The duration of the interruption that invalidates slaughter is **equivalent** to the period in **which he can lift** the animal from the ground **and** then **lay it** back on the ground **and slaughter** it. The interval is calculated based on the size of the animal in question. The duration of the interruption that invalidates slaughter of **a small** animal is equivalent to the period necessary **to** lift, lay down, and slaughter **a small** animal. **And** the duration of the interruption that invalidates slaughter of **a large** animal is equivalent to the period necessary **to** lift, lay down, and slaughter **a large** animal.

*Rabbi Yose berabbi Chanina* offered a formulation from *eretz yisrael* that combines elements of both the more lenient and more strict approaches: the measure includes lifting the animal and causing it to crouch before *shechting* — *k'dei she-yagbihena ve-yarbitzena ve-yishchot* — but simultaneously, the standard is calibrated to the type of animal: *dakah le-dakah ve-gasah le-gasah* — small animal measured against small animal, large animal measured against large animal.

The *gemara* does not reach a definitive conclusion on these competing formulations. *rashi* rules strictly, holding that since *rav* himself was ultimately strict — comparing like to like rather than always using the generous *beheima* standard — and given the unresolved nature of the debate, one must follow the stricter position. In practice, *rashi* rules that when *shechting* a bird, the permissible pause is only the time to *shecht* another bird, and the preparatory time is not included.

### Dull Knife Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּסַכִּין רָעָה, אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava said:** In the case of **one who slaughters with a blunt knife, even** if the completion of the slaughter lasts **the entire day,** the slaughter is **valid** provided there is no interruption in the midst of the slaughter.

A separate but illuminating ruling is offered by *rava*: if one *shechts* with a dull knife — *ha-shochet be-sakin ra'ah* — the *shechita* is valid even if it takes the entire day. It bears noting that a dull knife is distinct from a nicked knife: the problem of a nicked blade is that it tears rather than slices, but a dull knife that slices slowly without a nick does not share that problem. The *shechita* may be agonizingly slow, but it is continuous. *shehiya* is a measure of *interruption*, not of duration. If the *shochet* never pauses — if his knife moves back and forth in continuous motion, however slowly — there is no *shehiya*, regardless of how long the act takes.

This point also raises the broader question of *tzar ba'alei chayim* — the suffering of animals — which is central to the underlying rationale of many *shechita* laws, yet rarely if ever translates into a concrete *halachic* disqualification in the technical formulations. The relationship between the *ta'amei hamitzvot* and the technical *halachic* criteria is a larger question this case vividly brings to the surface.

### Unresolved Issues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: שְׁהִיּוֹת, מַהוּ שֶׁיִּצְטָרְפוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** If there were several short **interruptions** during a single act of slaughter, **what is** the *halakha* in terms of whether **they join** together to invalidate the slaughter if the sum of the durations of all the interruptions is greater than the maximum permitted interruption?

*Rava* raises two questions that remain unresolved. The first: *shehiyot mahu sheyitzterfu?* — if one had multiple brief pauses, each shorter than the *shiur* for *shehiya*, do they combine? If the disqualifying pause must be 10 seconds, and one paused for 1 second, then 4 seconds, then 3 seconds, then 2 — do these aggregate to a disqualifying break, or must the interruption be a continuous 10 seconds? The *gemara* asks whether one can resolve this from the case of the dull knife, since continuous back-and-forth motion over a long period presumably involves many tiny interruptions. But the *gemara* rejects this: in that case there was never a pause at all — the motion was continuous. Whether tiny discrete pauses aggregate remains unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִפְשׁוֹט לַהּ מִדִּידֵיהּ! הָתָם בִּדְלָא שְׁהָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **And let** Rava **resolve** the dilemma **from his own** statement, as he permitted an act of slaughter that lasts the entire day, during which there were presumably brief interruptions throughout. Apparently, the interruptions do not join together. The Gemara responds: **There,** Rava was referring to a case **where there was no interruption,** as the slaughterer drew the knife back and forth throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן: שָׁהָה בְּמִיעוּט סִימָנִין, מַהוּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, raises a dilemma:** If one cut the majority of the *simanim* and then **interrupted** the slaughter before proceeding to cut the **minority of the *simanim*, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara answers: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The second question, raised by *rav huna son of rav natan*: *shaha bemiut simanim mahu?* — what is the law if one paused while cutting the minority (*miut*) of the *simanim*? The *gemara* answers *teiku* — the question stands unresolved.

### Rashi vs. Rabbeinu Tam

This question about a pause in the minority of the *simanim* parallels the earlier *gemara* that asked about *chalada* in the minority of the *simanim*. *rashi* reads both *gemaros* in what he considers the straightforward sense: even after one has already validly cut the majority of both *simanim* — at which point the *shechita* would have been complete had one stopped — if one continues and finishes in a defective manner, those later defects become incorporated into the act.

The principle at stake is that *rov kekulo* — majority is as the whole — might mean only that if one stops at the majority, it counts as if one had completed the act. But if one chooses to continue and complete the act improperly, that completion is incorporated into the overall *shechita* and can retroactively compromise it. This gives rise to the practical ruling that if an animal is dying slowly after a valid *shechita* and the *shochet* wishes to hasten its death, he should *not* complete the *shechita* in the normal fashion — for resuming the *shechita* after a pause would constitute *shehiya*. Instead, he should cut from behind, or sever the arteries and veins, in a manner that does not constitute completing the *shechita* itself.

*Rabbeinu Tam* disagrees in principle, holding that once a valid *kosher shechita* has been completed — once the majority of both *simanim* have been properly cut — any subsequent act cannot retroactively compromise it. The question of how exactly *rabbeinu tam* reads this *gemara* remains to be worked out more precisely, as the simple reading appears to favor *rashi's* position.

What makes *rashi's* position conceptually striking on an additional level is this: even if one resumed the *shechita* after a full hour's pause, *rashi* still considers it the *same act* — now compromised by *shehiya* — rather than a wholly separate and independent act. The pause has not severed the connection between the earlier and later cutting; it has merely introduced a defect into the continuous act. This too is a significant *chiddush*.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם שָׁהָה [כְּדֵי בִיקּוּר]. מַאי כְּדֵי בִיקּוּר? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּדֵי בִּיקֻּרוֹ שֶׁל חָכָם. אִם כֵּן, נָתַתָּ דְּבָרֶיךָ לְשִׁיעוּרִין! אֶלָּא, כְּדֵי בִּיקּוּר טַבָּח חָכָם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon says:** The slaughter is not valid **if he interrupted** the slaughter for an interval equivalent to the duration of an examination. The Gemara asks: **What** is an interval **equivalent** to the duration of **an examination? Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is an interval **equivalent** to the duration of **an examination** of the knife **by a Sage** prior to the slaughter of the animal. The Gemara objects: **If so, you have rendered your statement** subject **to circumstances,** as sometimes the Sage is near and sometimes the Sage is far, and the time required for examination varies accordingly. The Gemara responds: **Rather,** the reference is to an interval **equivalent** to the duration of **an examination** performed by **a slaughterer** who is **a Sage.** In that case, travel time is not factored in; only the time of the examination itself is considered, and this does not vary.

The *mishnah's* minority position is that of *rabbi shimon*, who measures the disqualifying pause not by the time to perform another *shechita* but by *k'dei bikur* — the time to check the knife. *rabbi yochanan* clarifies: this means *k'dei bikur shel chacham* — the time it takes to show the knife to a *chacham* for inspection. The *gemara* objects that this introduces the same problem of variability raised against *rabbi chanina*: how far away is the sage? The *gemara* resolves this: it means *k'dei bikur tabach chacham* — the *shochet* himself is a *chacham*, and the measure is how long it would take *him* to inspect his own knife.

## Uprooting & Treifa

### Three Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שָׁחַט אֶת הַוֶּושֶׁט, וּפָסַק אֶת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת, אוֹ פָסַק אֶת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁחַט אֶת הַוֶּושֶׁט, אוֹ שָׁחַט אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְהִמְתִּין לָהּ עַד שֶׁמֵּתָה, אוֹ שֶׁהֶחְלִיד אֶת הַסַּכִּין תַּחַת הַשֵּׁנִי וּפְסָקוֹ – רַבִּי יְשֵׁבָב אוֹמֵר: נְבֵלָה, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one cut the gullet** in the standard manner of slaughter with a back-and-forth movement, **and he severed the windpipe** not in the standard manner, **or** if **one severed the windpipe and thereafter cut the gullet, or** if **one cut one of** the *simanim* **and waited until** the animal **died, or** if one cut one *siman* and **concealed the knife beneath the second** *siman* **and severed it** from below, **Rabbi Yeshevav says:** The animal is **an unslaughtered carcass** and imparts ritual impurity through contact with it and carrying it. **Rabbi Akiva says:** The animal is **a *tereifa*,** and although eating it is prohibited, it does not transmit ritual impurity.

The *mishnah* now presents three categories of defective *shechita*. In the first, one *shechted* the esophagus and then the trachea was *pasak* — severed or uprooted — without a proper act of *shechita*; this is the *psul* of *ikur*. In the second, the reverse: the trachea was uprooted first and then the esophagus was *shechted*. In the third, one *shechted* only one *siman* and waited until the animal died without ever cutting the second — effectively dying without a complete *shechita*. The *mishnah* also includes the case of *chalada* — where after *shechting* one *siman*, the knife was burrowed beneath the tissue and the second *siman* was cut in the opposite direction, constituting an act of *chalada* rather than proper *shechita*. In all these cases, the *shechita* was defective.

### Akiva vs. Rabbis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָל אָמַר רַבִּי יְשֵׁבָב מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כֹּל שֶׁנִּפְסְלָה בִּשְׁחִיטָתָהּ – נְבֵלָה, כֹּל שֶׁשְּׁחִיטָתָהּ כָּרָאוּי וְדָבָר אַחֵר גָּרַם לָהּ לִיפָּסֵל – טְרֵפָה, וְהוֹדָה לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yeshevav stated a principle in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua: Any** animal **that was rendered unfit during its slaughter** because the slaughter was not performed properly **is an unslaughtered carcass; any** animal **whose slaughter was** performed **properly and another matter caused it to become unfit is a *tereifa*. And Rabbi Akiva conceded to his** opinion.

*Rav Yeshevav* in the name of *rabbi yehoshua* rules: all such cases produce a *neveila*. Since the animal died without a valid *shechita*, it is a *neveila* — an animal that died without being properly *shechted*.

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: these cases produce a *treifa*. His reasoning is that one did not simply do nothing — one wounded the animal in the course of a *shechita* attempt. The wounds inflicted constitute *treifa*-level damage. And since this occurred in the context of a *shechita* act, it would be inappropriate to classify the animal as a *neveila*, even though the *shechita* was never completed. In *rabbi akiva's* view, the proximity to a *shechita* process is sufficient to shift the classification from *neveila* to *treifa*.

*Rav Yeshevav* responds with a principled distinction in the name of *rabbi yehoshua*: *kol she-nipsala bishchitasah, neveila* — any animal whose *shechita* itself was compromised and rendered invalid is a *neveila*. *kol she-shchitasah k'rauy, v'davar acher garam lah le-hipasel, treifa* — if the *shechita* itself was performed correctly and some external factor caused the animal to be forbidden — such as a punctured organ — then it is a *treifa*. The distinguishing criterion is not when or how the damage occurred in relation to the slaughter process, but whether the *shechita* itself was executed properly. If yes, any resulting *psul* is a *treifa*. If no, the animal is a *neveila*.

And remarkably, *rabbi akiva* concedes: *v'hoda lo Rabbi Akiva*. He acknowledges that this is the correct way to draw the line.

### Gemara Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שָׁחַט אֶת הַוֶּושֶׁט וְכוּ׳. וְהוֹדָה לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וּרְמִינְהִי: אֵלּוּ טְרֵפוֹת בַּבְּהֵמָה
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: If **one cut the gullet…and Rabbi Akiva conceded to his** opinion. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna at the beginning of the next chapter (42a): **These** wounds constitute ***tereifot* in an animal:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְקוּבַת הַוֶּושֶׁט וּפְסוּקַת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** An animal that has **a perforated gullet,** where the perforation goes through the wall of the gullet, **or** one with **a severed windpipe.** According to this mishna, the severed windpipe renders the animal a *tereifa* and not an unslaughtered carcass. If Rabbi Akiva reconsidered his opinion and conceded to Rabbi Yeshevav, in accordance with the opinion of which *tanna* is that mishna?

The *gemara* now raises a pointed difficulty against this consensus. The following *perek's Mishnah*, which catalogues the *trefos* of an animal, lists *nekuvat ha-veshet* — a perforated esophagus — and *psukat ha-gargeres* — a severed majority of the trachea — among the *trefos*. But we have just established that damage to the *simanim* means the *shechita* was never valid, producing a *neveila*. How, then, does the *mishnah* call damage to the trachea and esophagus a *treifa*?

The apparent answer — which the *gemara* eventually reaches — is that one must distinguish between cases where the *shechita* was performed *at* the site of the damage versus *away* from it. But the *gemara* has difficulty reaching this answer immediately because it seems to be working with the assumption that any pre-existing cut in the *simanim* renders any subsequent *shechita* meaningless: if the esophagus already has a hole, one cannot truly perform a valid *shechita* on an already-compromised structure, and therefore the result should always be *neveila*, never *treifa*.

The practical difference between *neveila* and *treifa* is significant. Both are biblically prohibited for consumption, and both may be sold to a non-Jew. The key distinction is *tumah*: a *neveila* conveys ritual impurity; a *treifa* does not.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן שֶׁשָּׁחַט וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּסַק, כָּאן שֶׁפָּסַק וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁחַט. שָׁחַט וּלְבַסּוֹף פָּסַק – נִפְסֶלֶת בִּשְׁחִיטָה הִיא, פָּסַק וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁחַט – כִּי דָּבָר אַחֵר גָּרַם לָהּ לִיפָּסֵל דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is referring to a case **where one cut** the gullet **and ultimately severed** the windpipe not in the standard manner. **There,** the mishna in the next chapter is referring to a case **where he severed** the windpipe not in the standard manner, **and ultimately cut** the gullet. If he **cut** the gullet **and ultimately severed** the windpipe, the animal **was rendered unfit during its slaughter;** therefore, it assumes the status of an unslaughtered carcass, according to the principle of Rabbi Yeshevav. But if **he severed** the windpipe **and ultimately cut** the gullet, it is **as** though **another matter caused** the animal **to become unfit;** therefore, it assumes the status of a *tereifa*.

*Rava* attempts a resolution based on sequence. *shachat ulebasof pasak* — if the *shechita* began correctly and the cut in the *siman* arose afterward, during the *shechita* process — then *nifsela bishchitasah hi*, the *shechita* was compromised, and the result is *neveila*. *pasak ulebasof shachat* — if the cut pre-existed and the *shechita* was then performed on already-damaged *simanim* — then *kidavar acher garam lo lipasel damya*, it is analogous to an external cause of *psul* rather than a failure of the *shechita* itself, making it a *treifa*.

However, this resolution is challenged. *rav acha bar rav huna* points out that the *mishnah* presents both orders — first cutting the esophagus and then the trachea becoming uprooted, and first the trachea being uprooted and then shechting the esophagus — and in both cases rules *neveila*. *rava's* distinction based on sequence would predict different outcomes in the two orders, but the *mishnah* treats them identically. An attempt is made to reread the *mishnah* as presenting only one scenario in different formulations, but the *gemara* rejects this as inconsistent with the *mishnah's* plain language, which says *v'achar kach* — and then afterward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר הוּנָא לְרָבָא: שָׁחַט אֶת הַוֶּושֶׁט וּפָסַק אֶת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת, פָּסַק אֶת הַגַּרְגֶּרֶת וְאַחַר כָּךְ שָׁחַט אֶת הַוֶּושֶׁט – נְבֵלָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Huna raised an objection to Rava** from a *baraita*: If one **cut the gullet and** then **severed the windpipe,** or if one **severed the windpipe and cut the gullet thereafter,** the animal is **an unslaughtered carcass.** Apparently, contrary to Rava's statement, the order is irrelevant, and even if the windpipe is severed first, the animal assumes the status of an unslaughtered carcass.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: וּכְבָר שָׁחַט אֶת הַוֶּושֶׁט מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: **Say** that the latter case in the *baraita* means not that he cut the gullet thereafter; rather, it is a case where one severed the windpipe **and had already cut the gullet at the outset.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁתֵּי תְּשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר, חֲדָא דְּהַיְינוּ קַמַּיְיתָא, וְעוֹד הָא תְּנַן ״וְאַחַר כָּךְ״!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa bar Huna **said to him:** There are **two refutations of the statement,** i.e., of your attempt to answer the difficulty from the *baraita*: **One** is that if the second case in the *baraita* is one where he initially cut the gullet, then **that is** identical to **the first** case in the *baraita*. **And furthermore, didn't we learn** explicitly in the *baraita*: Or if one severed the windpipe and cut the gullet **thereafter,** the animal is an unslaughtered carcass?

### List Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אֵלּוּ אֲסוּרוֹת קָתָנֵי, וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן נְבֵלוֹת, וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן טְרֵפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The use of the term: *Tereifot*, in the mishna in the next chapter is not to the exclusion of unslaughtered carcasses; rather, the *tanna* **teaches** that term in a general sense, meaning: **These** wounds serve to render an animal **forbidden; and there are** some **of them** that are **unslaughtered carcasses,** e.g., an animal with a severed windpipe, **and there are** some **of them** that are ***tereifot*.**

*Rava* therefore abandons his earlier distinction and offers a different reading of the *mishnah* that lists *trefos*. He concedes that the list does not literally mean that damage to the trachea and esophagus always produces a *treifa*. Rather, *elu asurot ketani* — the *mishnah* is listing things that are *forbidden*, and among them, *yesh mehem neveilot ve-yesh mehem treifot* — some are *neveilos* and some are *treifos*. A damaged *siman*, according to this reading, would be classified as *neveila*, while the *mishnah* lists it simply as a category of forbidden animal without specifying which type of *psul* applies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחְשׁוֹב נָמֵי דְּחִזְקִיָּה, דְּאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: עֲשָׂאָהּ גִּיסְטְרָא – נְבֵלָה, וְלִיחְשׁוֹב נָמֵי דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: נִטְּלָה יָרֵךְ וְחָלָל שֶׁלָּהּ – נְבֵלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to that interpretation: If the mishna is listing those circumstances that render the animal an unslaughtered carcass, **let** the *tanna* **consider** the circumstance **of Ḥizkiyya as well, as Ḥizkiyya says:** If **one rendered** the animal like **a shard** by cutting it into two widthwise, its halakhic status is that of **an unslaughtered carcass** even while it is convulsing before its death. **And let** the *tanna* **consider** the circumstance **of Rabbi Elazar as well, as Rabbi Elazar said:** If **the thigh,** the hind leg of the animal, **was removed and its recess** is obvious (see 21a), it is **an unslaughtered carcass.**

The *gemara* objects to this reading as well. If the *mishnah* is cataloguing all types of *neveilos* alongside *treifos*, it should have listed many other categories of *neveila* that it does not mention — such as *chizkiyah's* ruling that an animal split in half is a *neveila*, or *rabbi eliezer's* ruling that an animal from which the thigh together with its socket was removed is a *neveila*. Why are those omitted?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָתָנֵי נְבֵלָה, דְּלָא מְטַמְּאָה מֵחַיִּים, אֲבָל נְבֵלָה דִּמְטַמְּאָה מֵחַיִּים – לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **When** the mishna **teaches** the case of **an unslaughtered carcass,** it is a case where the animal **does not** assume that status and **impart impurity while alive. But** the *tanna* **does not teach** the case of **an unslaughtered carcass** where the animal assumes that status and **imparts impurity while alive,** e.g., the cases of Ḥizkiyya and Rabbi Elazar.

*Rava* responds with a distinction: the *mishnah* does not list *neveilos* that convey *tumah* while the animal is still alive. *neveila demetamya mechayim* — cases such as an animal split in half or missing its thigh-socket, which are classified as *neveila* even while the animal is technically still alive — are not included in this list. The cases the *mishnah* does mention become *neveila* only upon death. And within that category, a damaged trachea or esophagus belongs as well — not as a true *treifa*, but as a *neveila* that takes effect upon death.

This is a noteworthy point in its own right: the *gemara* here explicitly acknowledges that *neveila* status can attach to an animal that is still alive — alive in the biological sense, still breathing, blood still pumping — yet already classified as a *neveila halachically*. This interacts in a complex way with the earlier discussions of whether *neveila* and death are coextensive concepts.

### Resh Lakish Resolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כָּאן – שֶׁשָּׁחַט בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ, כָּאן – שֶׁשָּׁחַט שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ. שָׁחַט בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ – נִפְסְלָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה הִיא, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם חֲתָךְ – כִּי דָּבָר אַחֵר גָּרַם לָהּ לִיפָּסֵל דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish** resolved the apparent contradiction between the mishna here and the mishna on 42a, and **said: Here,** the *tanna* is referring to a case **where one cut** the gullet **in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe. **There,** on 42a, the *tanna* is referring to a case **where he cut** the gullet **not in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe. The reason for the distinction is that if **one cut** the gullet **in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe, it is an animal that **was rendered unfit during its slaughter** and it assumes the status of an unslaughtered carcass. By contrast, if he cut the gullet **not in** the same **place** as the initial **cut** in the windpipe it is **like** a case where **another matter caused** the animal **to become unfit.** Therefore, cutting the gullet renders it a *tereifa* and prevents it from imparting ritual impurity.

*Resh Lakish* provides what the *gemara* presents as the resolution, and indeed the one that the overall thrust of the discussion points toward. *kan sheshachat bimkom chatach, kan sheshachat shelo bimkom chatach* — the critical variable is whether the *shechita* was performed *at* the site of the pre-existing cut or *away* from it.

If the animal had a hole in its trachea or esophagus, and the *shochet* performed the *shechita* at a different location along the neck — not at the site of the hole — then the *shechita* itself was executed properly. The act of *shechita* was valid; the hole in the *simanim* is simply a separate condition that renders the animal a *treifa*. *ki davar acher garam lah lipasel* — a different problem, external to the *shechita* itself, caused the disqualification.

If, however, the *shechita* was performed *at* the site of the hole — *shachat bimkom chatach* — then the cut that produced the *shechita* incorporated the pre-existing tear. In that case, *nifsala bishchita hi* — the *shechita* itself was compromised by the defect. What one did was not a valid *shechita* followed by an external *psul*; the defect was woven into the *shechita* act. The result is *neveila*.

This resolution is clean and intuitive: the distinction between *neveila* and *treifa* depends not on the sequence of events — which order the cuts occurred — but on the simple question of whether, at the moment of *shechita*, the act was performed on an intact section of the *simanim* or on one already compromised by a pre-existing cut.
