# Kiddushin 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Return from Exile

### Opening & Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּהֲנֵי לְוִיֵּי וְיִשְׂרְאֵלֵי. מְנָלַן דִּסְלִיקוּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּשְׁבוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַלְוִיִּם וּמִן הָעָם וְהַמְשֹׁרְרִים וְהַשּׁוֹעֲרִים וְהַנְּתִינִים בְּעָרֵיהֶם וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּעָרֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna included in its list of types of lineage **priests, Levites, and Israelites.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that they ascended?** The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "So the priests, and the Levites, and some of the people, and the singers, and the gatekeepers, and the Gibeonites dwelt in their cities, and all of Israel in their cities"** (Ezra 2:70). The verse specifies priests, Levites, and all of Israel.

Today's daf is daf 70. We pick up in the middle of 69b, approximately fifteen lines down in the wide lines, at the word *minalan* — "from where do we know?" — where the text begins its discussion of *Kohani Leviei VeYisraeli*, the various groups that came up with Ezra from the Babylonian exile.

### Three Groups

The *mishnah* enumerates the different groups that returned with Ezra, and the *gemara* now proceeds to demonstrate that most of what the *mishnah* records is grounded in scriptural testimony. In this sense, the *mishnah* reflects a fairly accurate historical record, even if not every detail is made explicit in the verses. The *gemara* asks: *minalan disliku* — how do we know that members of the *kohanim*, *levi'im*, and *Yisrael* all came up with Ezra?

### Scriptural Proof

The answer comes from the verse: *Vayeshvu hakohanim vehaleviim umin ha'am vehameshorerim vehasho'arim vehanetinim be'areihem vechol Yisrael be'areihem* — "And the *kohanim* and the *levi'im* and from the people and the *meshorerim* and the *sho'arim*" — understood to refer to the *levi'im* who perform the singing or instrumental music and those who guard the gates — "and the *netinim* in their cities, and all of Israel in their cities." The verse thus attests to *kohen*, *levi*, and *Yisrael*. The *netinim* — the Gibeonites — appear here as well, though their discussion comes later in the *mishnah*. Moving forward.

## Chalalim

### Gedola Chazaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲלָלֵי גֵּירֵי וַחֲרוֹרֵי. חֲלָלֵי מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גְּדוֹלָה חֲזָקָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִבְּנֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים בְּנֵי חֳבַיָּה בְּנֵי הַקּוֹץ בְּנֵי בַרְזִלַּי אֲשֶׁר לָקַח מִבְּנוֹת בַּרְזִלַּי הַגִּלְעָדִי אִשָּׁה וַיִּקָּרֵא עַל שְׁמָם. אֵלֶּה בִּקְשׁוּ כְתָבָם הַמִּתְיַחְשִׂים וְלֹא נִמְצָאוּ וַיְגֹאֲלוּ מִן הַכְּהֻנָּה. וַיֹּאמֶר הַתִּרְשָׁתָא לָהֶם אֲשֶׁר לֹא יֹאכְלוּ מִקֹּדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים עַד עֲמֹד כֹּהֵן לְאוּרִים וּלְתֻמִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further states that ***ḥalalim*, converts, and emancipated slaves** ascended from Babylonia. The Gemara clarifies: **From where do we** derive that ***ḥalalim*** ascended? The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei says: Great** is the importance of **a presumptive status, as it is stated: "And of the children of the priests: The children of Habaiah, the children of Hakkoz, the children of Barzillai, who took a wife of the daughters of Barzillai the Gileadite, and was called after their name. These sought the registry of their genealogy, but it was not found. Therefore, they were deemed polluted and put out from the priesthood. And the Tirshatha said to them that they should not eat of the offerings of the most sacred order until there arose a priest with the *Urim VeTummim*"** (Ezra 2:61–63).

The *gemara* turns to *chalalim*, *geirim*, and *charurim* — disqualified *kohanim*, converts, and freed slaves. How do we know *chalalim* — *kohanim* born from forbidden unions who therefore lack *kedushat kehuna* — came up with Ezra? A *braita* is cited: Rabbi Yosi says, *gedola chazaka* — the principle of presumed status is great — as we learn from the following passage in Ezra.

### No Genealogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר לָהֶם: הֲרֵי אַתֶּם בְּחֶזְקַתְכֶם. בַּמֶּה הֱיִיתֶם אוֹכְלִים בַּגּוֹלָה, בְּקׇדְשֵׁי הַגְּבוּל? אַף כָּאן נָמֵי, בְּקׇדְשֵׁי הַגְּבוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: This shows that there was uncertainty whether specific descendants of priests were fit for the priesthood or were *ḥalalim*, **and** Ezra **said to them: You have** retained **your presumptive status.** In other words, despite their failure to provide proof that they were fit priests, they maintained their prior status. Ezra said: **Of what** priestly gifts **did you partake** when you were **in exile?** You partook only **of the consecrated** gifts of **the boundaries,** i.e., from *teruma*, which may be eaten anywhere. **Here too,** in Eretz Yisrael, you may partake only **of the consecrated** gifts of **the boundaries.** You may not, however, partake of anything that must be eaten inside Jerusalem, as indicated by the verse "They should not eat of the offerings of the most sacred order." In any event, since the verse writes that they were "put out from the priesthood," it can be seen that there were *ḥalalim* who came with Ezra to Eretz Yisrael.

The verse reads: *Umibnei hakohanim bnei Chavayah bnei haKotz bnei Barzillai asher lakach mibnot Barzillai haGil'adi ishah vayikarei al shemam* — these are the priestly families that went up from the exile, the sons of Chavayah, Kotz, and Barzillai, named after Barzillai the Gileadite because they had married his daughters and were identified by that name even though they were really his daughters' children. The verse continues: *Eleh bikshu ksavam hamityachasim velo nimtze'u* — these sought their records of lineage and could not find them. The word *yichus* appears here directly: they could not produce documentation to establish themselves as legitimate *kohanim*. As a result, *vayig'alu min hakehuna* — they were pushed out from the *kehuna*, though the word *vayig'alu* is read with the sense of being removed or rejected rather than redeemed.

### Kodshei Kodashim

*Vayomer HaTirshatha lahem asher lo yochlu mikodesh hakodashim ad amod kohen le'urim vetumim* — Nehemiah, who is called by the title *HaTirshatha*, said to them: since you cannot document your *kehuna*, you may no longer eat from the *kodshei hakodashim* — from the sacrificial offerings — until a *kohen gadol* arises with an *urim vetumim*. Since there was no *urim vetumim* in the Second *Beit Hamikdash*, Rashi understands this to mean, in effect, until the coming of Mashiach. They cannot prove their *yichus*, and so they cannot eat from the *korbanot*.

### Terumah Exemption

But the *gemara* then introduces the principle of *gedola chazaka*. He said to them: *harei atem bechezkatchem* — you retain your presumed status. The question is: what were you eating in exile? *Bekodshei hagvul* — you were eating things sanctified outside the *Mikdash*, meaning *terumah*, which is what you had been eating until now. And so, *af kan nami bekodshei hagvul* — here too you may continue eating *terumah*; your *chazaka* is retained.

This is the *chiddush* of Rabbi Yosi: although these *kohanim* could not document their *kehuna* and could not eat from the *korbanot*, they could continue eating *terumah* on the basis of their established presumptive status. There had been no contrary evidence disproving their *kehuna* — only an absence of supporting documentation. All *kohanim* today lack documentation of their lineage, yet we extend them the same *chazaka*. The distinction between *korbanot* and *terumah* here is notable and will be developed further.

### Terumah Jurisdiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר: מַעֲלִים מִתְּרוּמָה לְיוּחֲסִין? הָנֵי דַּאֲכוּל בִּתְרוּמָה אָתוּ לְאַסּוֹקִינְהוּ! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּרִיעַ חֶזְקָתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says** that **one elevates** a priest **to lineage,** i.e., one attributes the lineage of a priest to an individual on the basis of observing him partaking of ***teruma*,** how could they be allowed to partake of *teruma*? **Those who partake of *teruma* will** subsequently **be elevated** to the full status of priests. The Gemara answers: **There it is different, as their presumptive status had been weakened.** Since they did not partake of offerings, as did all other priests, all knew that they were not regular priests.

A complication arises, however. There is an opinion — *ma'alin mitrumah le'yichus* — that one who is seen eating *terumah* is thereby presumed to hold full *yichus*, including fitness to bring *korbanot* in the *Beit Hamikdash*. If we allow these *kohanim* to eat *terumah* now that they are in Eretz Yisrael, observers may presume that they are fully qualified *kohanim* and draw incorrect inferences about their status vis-à-vis the *Beit Hamikdash*.

The *gemara* answers: *shani hasam de-rei'a chezkateihu* — their case is different, because their *chazaka* has been weakened. Rashi explains that since a public declaration was made distinguishing those permitted to bring *korbanot* from those who are not, the distinction is already known. People understand that these particular *kohanim* are eating *terumah* on a different basis and will not draw the wrong conclusion. Tosafot adds that since it was publicly known that they had sought their genealogical records and failed to find them, no one would be confused into thinking they held full *yichus*. Either way, the historical event attached a clear and known label: they eat *terumah* but may not be *makriv korbanot*.

This still raises the question: what exactly is *gedola chazaka*? A *chazaka* simply means that the status quo continues in the absence of contrary evidence — so what is so great about this one? The *gemara* answers:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי גְּדוֹלָה חֲזָקָה? דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא אֲכוּל בִּתְרוּמָה דְרַבָּנַן, וּלְבַסּוֹף אֲכוּל בִּתְרוּמָה דְאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **what** is the meaning of Rabbi Yosei's claim that **great** is **a presumptive status?** They received nothing extra by virtue of this presumption. The Gemara responds: They have gained in **that initially they would partake of *teruma*** only of Babylonian produce, which is *teruma* **by rabbinic law, and afterward they would partake of** produce that is ***teruma* by Torah law.** The separation of *teruma* in Eretz Yisrael is a Torah obligation, and the produce is fully consecrated.

*Demeikara achal biterumah derabbanan ulevesof achal biterumah de'oraita* — initially, in exile, *terumah* was only rabbinic in force, since *terumah* outside Eretz Yisrael has no biblical standing. But now that they are coming into Eretz Yisrael, their *chazaka* as *kohanim* in the domain of *terumah* carries over into *terumah de'oraita* as well. That is genuinely remarkable: within the world of *terumah*, once a *chazaka* of *kehuna* is established, it applies across all levels of *terumah* obligation.

An alternative answer is then offered:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי בִּדְרַבָּנַן – אֲכוּל, בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – לָא אֲכוּל, וְכִי מַסְּקִינַן מִתְּרוּמָה לְיוּחֲסִין – מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מִדְּרַבָּנַן לָא מַסְּקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers an additional defense of the opinion that one elevates a priest to lineage based on observing him partake of *teruma*: **And if you wish, say: Actually, now also,** in Eretz Yisrael, **they would partake** only **of** produce that is *teruma* **by rabbinic law,** such as vegetables, while **they would not partake of** produce that is *teruma* **by Torah law,** such as grains. **And when we elevate** a priest **to lineage** on the basis of observing him partake of ***teruma*,** that is only when he was observed partaking of produce that is *teruma* **by Torah law,** but if someone partakes of produce that is *teruma* **by rabbinic law, we do not elevate** him. Therefore, they could continue partaking of *teruma* based on their presumptive status, and there is no concern that they might be elevated to the status of full-fledged priests.

But then what is *gedola chazaka*? The *gemara* replies:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי גְּדוֹלָה חֲזָקָה? דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לֵיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם תְּרוּמָה דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, לְבַסּוֹף – אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם תְּרוּמָה דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, בִּדְרַבָּנַן – אֲכוּל, בִּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא – לָא אֲכוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the question remains: **What** is meant by the phrase: **Great** is **a presumptive status?** The Gemara answers: It means **that initially, there was no** reason **to decree** and prohibit them from partaking of produce that was *teruma* by rabbinic law **due to** the possibility that they may partake of produce that is ***teruma* by Torah law,** as there was no produce that was *teruma* by Torah law in Babylonia. **Afterward,** when they came to Eretz Yisrael, **although there was reason to decree** and prohibit them from partaking of produce that was *teruma* by rabbinic law **due to** the possibility that they may partake of produce that was ***teruma* by Torah law,** as that kind of *teruma* was also present, their presumptive status was nevertheless strong enough to allow them to continue **to partake of** produce that was ***teruma* by rabbinic law,** although they could **not eat** produce that is ***teruma* by Torah law.**

The *gemara* also addresses a textual difficulty. The verse says *lo yochlu mikodshei hakodashim* — they shall not eat from *kodshei hakodashim*. Does this not imply that they *may* eat everything else, including *terumah de'oraita*? The *gemara* resolves this:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב ״וַיֹּאמֶר הַתִּרְשָׁתָא לָהֶם אֲשֶׁר לֹא יֹאכְלוּ מִקֹּדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים״ – בְּקֹדֶשׁ הַקָּדָשִׁים הוּא דְּלֹא יֹאכְל[וּ], הָא כֹּל מִידֵּי נֵיכוֹל! הָכִי קָאָמַר: לָא מִידֵּי דְּמִיקְּרֵי קֹדֶשׁ, וְלָא מִידֵּי דְּמִיקְּרֵי קָדָשִׁים. לָא מִידֵּי דְּמִיקְּרֵי קֹדֶשׁ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכׇל זָר לֹא יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ״, וְלָא מִידֵּי דְּמִיקְּרֵי קָדָשִׁים, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וּבַת כֹּהֵן כִּי תִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ זָר הִיא בִּתְרוּמַת הַקֳּדָשִׁים לֹא תֹאכֵל״, וְאָמַר מָר: ״בַּמּוּרָם מִן הַקֳּדָשִׁים לֹא תֹאכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "And the Tirshatha said to them that they should not eat of the offerings of the most sacred order [*kodesh hakodashim*]"** (Ezra 2:63)? This indicates that **it is from the offerings of the most sacred order that they may not eat, but anything** else, i.e., offerings not of the most sacred order, **they may eat,** including produce that is *teruma* by Torah law. The Gemara answers: **This** is what the Tirshatha **is saying:** They may **not** partake of **anything that is called sacred [*kodesh*], nor anything that is called most sacred [*kodashim*].** The Gemara clarifies. The words **not anything that is called sacred** are referring to *teruma*, **as it is written** with regard to *teruma*: **"No non-priest may eat of the sacred"** (Leviticus 22:10). And the words **nor anything that is called most sacred** are referring to offerings, **as the verse states: "And a daughter of a priest, if she is married to a non-priest, she may not eat of that which is set apart from the sacred things"** (Leviticus 22:12). **And the Master says:** What is the meaning of "that which is set apart from the sacred things"? It means **from** the portions **separated from the offerings,** i.e., the breast and thigh of a peace-offering,

According to Rashi, *terumah de'oraita* applies only to grain, oil, and wine, so any other produce would remain *terumah derabbanan*. Under this reading, they are limited to *terumah derabbanan* in Eretz Yisrael as well, and there is no concern about the *ma'alin mitrumah le'yichus* problem, since that inference applies only when one is observed eating *terumah de'oraita*.

### Chazaka Strength

One might ask: is this not anomalous? The same person is simultaneously a *kohen* for the purposes of *terumah* and not a *kohen* for the purposes of *korbanot*. The entire point of Rabbi Yosi's teaching is precisely that — *gedola chazaka*: a *chazaka* established within a defined domain is operative within that domain even when it cannot be extended to an adjacent one. The *chazaka* was established through the practice of eating *terumah*; there is no parallel practice in the realm of *korbanot* that would generate a corresponding *chazaka* there.

This principle has important practical analogues. A *kohen* today has established a *chazaka* of *kehuna* through his ongoing observance of the laws of *tumah* — avoiding cemeteries, hospitals, and the like — in a way that he has not necessarily established through his marriage conduct. Accordingly, when a leniency is sought in a *tumah* case on the grounds that "perhaps he is not really a *kohen*," such an argument carries much less weight, because his actions have established him as a *kohen* in that domain. In the world of marriage, by contrast, no comparable practice has built up the same *chazaka*, so there may be somewhat more room to introduce doubt. The point, as the *gemara* illustrates here, is that *chazaka* operates through the lens of a particular legal domain, and one cannot automatically transfer it from one domain to another.

It also bears noting what kind of *chalalim* the *gemara* has actually established here. These are not definitively identified *chalalim* — they are *safek chalalim*, *kohanim* who could not prove they had a clean priestly lineage and may therefore have been *chalalim*. They were restricted from the *korbanot*, permitted to continue with *terumah*, and it is this group — those unable to establish their genealogical fitness — that represents the *chalalim* who came up with Ezra.

## Gerim & Slaves

### Gerim & Freedmen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גֵּירֵי וַחֲרוֹרֵי״. מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכׇל הַנִּבְדָּל מִטֻּמְאַת גּוֹיֵי הָאָרֶץ אֲלֵהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **converts and emancipated slaves** ascended from Babylonia. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rav Ḥisda says: As the verse states** with regard to the eating of the Paschal offering upon the return to Eretz Yisrael: "And the children of Israel who had come back from the exile ate, **and all such as had separated themselves to them from the impurity of the nations of the land** to seek the Lord, the God of Israel, did eat" (Ezra 6:21), indicating that converts and emancipated slaves who had abandoned "the impurity of the nations of the land," i.e., idolatry, joined Ezra.

The *gemara* turns to *geirim* and *charurim* — converts and freed slaves. How do we know they came up with Ezra? Rav Chisda says, from the verse at the top of 70a: *vechol hanivdal mitum'at goyei ha'aretz aleihem* — all those who separated themselves from the impurity of the nations of the land joined them. These were people who had previously been non-Jews, and their joining the community could only come about through conversion or manumission.

### Intermarriage Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַמְזֵרֵי. מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּשְׁמַע סַנְבַלַּט הַחֹרֹנִי וְטֹבִיָּה הָעֶבֶד הָעַמּוֹנִי״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי רַבִּים בִּיהוּדָה בַּעֲלֵי שְׁבוּעָה לוֹ כִּי חָתָן הוּא לִשְׁכַנְיָה בֶן אָרַח וִיהוֹחָנָן בְּנוֹ לָקַח אֶת בַּת מְשֻׁלָּם בֶּן בֶּרֶכְיָה״, קָסָבַר נׇכְרִי וְעֶבֶד הַבָּא עַל בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל – הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: הַוָּלָד מַמְזֵר, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? וְתוּ: מִמַּאי דַּהֲווֹ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי? דִּילְמָא לָא הֲווֹ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי! וְתוּ: מִמַּאי דְּהָכָא הֲווֹ לְהוּ וּסְלִיקוּ? דִּילְמָא הָתָם הֲווֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that ***mamzerim*** were among those who ascended from Babylonia. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And Sanballat the Horonite and Tobiah the servant, the Ammonite, heard"** (Nehemiah 2:19), **and** elsewhere **it is written** with regard to Tobiah the Ammonite: **"For there were many in Judah sworn to him because he was the son-in-law of Shecaniah the son of Arah; and his son Jehohanan had taken the daughter of Meshullam the son of Berechiah"** (Nehemiah 6:18). The Gemara proceeds to explain: This *tanna* **holds** that in the case of **a gentile or a slave who engaged in sexual intercourse with a Jewish woman, the offspring is a *mamzer*.** Since Tobiah the Ammonite, a gentile, married a Jewish woman, as did his son, there were clearly *mamzerim* among those who ascended. The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that in that case **the offspring is a *mamzer*. But according to the one who says** that the lineage of **the offspring is unflawed** and has the status of the mother, **what can be said? And furthermore, from where** is it clear **that** Jehohanan **had offspring** from this wife? **Perhaps he did not have offspring,** and it is possible that there were no *mamzerim*. **And furthermore,** even if they did have offspring, **from where** is it clear **that they had** offspring **here,** in Babylonia, who then **ascended** to Eretz Yisrael? **Perhaps they were there,** in Eretz Yisrael, all the time, as they may have been one of the families that was not exiled to Babylonia, and therefore they cannot be used as the proof that *mamzerim* ascended from Babylonia.

The question of *mamzeirim* follows. The *gemara* cites the verse: *vayishma Sanbalat haChoroni veTuviyah ha'eved ha'Ammoni* — Sanbalat and Tuviyah the Ammonite slave heard. The verse then states: *ki rabbim biYehudah ba'alei shevuah lo* — many in Judah had taken an oath to this Tuviyah. *Ki chatan hu liShechaniah ben Arach* — because he was a son-in-law to Shechaniah ben Arach, a Jew. So this slave had married a Jewish woman. And further: *viYehochanan beno lakach et bat Meshullam ben Berechiah* — and Tuviyah's son Yehochanan had also married the daughter of another Jewish man. These slaves, then, had married Jewish women and presumably had children from those unions — children who would have been *mamzeirim*.

But the *gemara* challenges this on several grounds. First, is a child born of a slave and a *bat Yisrael* really a *mamzer*? The *gemara* acknowledges that this holds only according to the opinion that such a child is a *mamzer* — on the grounds that *kiddushin* cannot take effect between them, and a child of a union where *kiddushin* is invalid is a *mamzer*. We do not rule that way; the accepted *halacha* is that such a child is *kasher*, not a *mamzer*.

### Mamzeirim

Even setting that aside, the *gemara* raises further objections. How do we know they actually had children from these marriages? And even if they did, how do we know those children were born in Babylon and then came up to Eretz Yisrael? The verses being cited describe the situation *in Judah* — *biYehudah* — meaning those marriages may have taken place in the land itself. You cannot use those verses to prove that *mamzeirim* from Babylon made the journey with Ezra's group.

The *gemara* therefore derives the point from a different source entirely.

## Tel Melach Groups

### Genealogy Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״וְאֵלֶּה הָעוֹלִים מִתֵּל מֶלַח תֵּל חַרְשָׁא כְּרוּב אַדּוֹן וְאִמֵּר, וְלֹא יָכְלוּ לְהַגִּיד בֵּית אֲבֹתָם וְזַרְעָם אִם מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל הֵם״, ״תֵּל מֶלַח״ – אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁדּוֹמִים מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם לְמַעֲשֵׂה סְדוֹם שֶׁנֶּהֶפְכָה לְתֵל מֶלַח. ״תֵּל חַרְשָׁא״ – זֶה שֶׁקּוֹרֵא ״אַבָּא״ וְאִמּוֹ מְשַׁתַּקְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the proof that *mamzerim* were among those who ascended from Babylonia is **from here: "And these were they that ascended from Tel Melah, Tel Harsha, Cherub, Addon, and Immer; but they could not tell their fathers' houses, nor their offspring, whether they were of Israel"** (Nehemiah 7:61). The Gemara explains that these names are to be interpreted as follows: **"Tel Melah"; these are people whose** licentious **actions were similar to the act of Sodom, which was turned into a mound of salt [*tel melaḥ*]. "Tel Harsha"; this** is referring to one **who calls** a man **father, and his mother silences him,** as the word *ḥarsha* is similar to *maḥarishto*, meaning: Silences him. In any event, the statement that there were those who acted licentiously, as did the people of Sodom, means that there were *mamzerim* among them.

The *gemara* turns to the verse: *ve'eleh ha'olim miTel Melach, Tel Charsha, Keruv, Addon ve'Immer* — these are those who came up from Tel Melach, Tel Charsha, and other locations. *Velo yachlu lehagid beit avotam vezar'am im Yisrael hem* — they could not say who their paternal household was, or, as Rashi explains, even from whom they were descended on their mother's side. They could not prove they were Jews at all. So already from the plain meaning of the verse, an entire contingent of the returning exiles could not establish their Israelite identity. They did not claim to be non-Jews; they simply had no way to prove they were Jewish.

### Tel Melach Derasha

Now the *gemara* offers its *derasha*. *Tel Melach* — the name means "mound of salt." These are people whose deeds resembled those of Sodom, which was overturned into a region of salt. According to certain *midrashim*, Sodom was associated with adultery; and so those compared to Sodom had committed adultery, which means their children were *mamzeirim*. So on the level of *derasha*, the first group — those from Tel Melach — represents *mamzeirim*, children of adulterous unions who cannot demonstrate legitimate Jewish descent.

### Shtukim

*Tel Charsha* — *charsha* evokes *cheresh*, silence. This refers to someone who calls out "Abba, Abba!" — who calls for his father — and his mother silences him. She cannot or will not reveal who the father is. That is a *shtuki* — someone whose paternity cannot be established, though his mother is known to be Jewish.

### Asufim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְלֹא יָכְלוּ לְהַגִּיד בֵּית אֲבֹתָם וְזַרְעָם אִם מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל הֵם״ – זֶה הוּא אֲסוּפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱסַף מִן הַשּׁוּק. ״כְּרוּב אַדּוֹן וְאִמֵּר״ – אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אָמַר אָדוֹן: אֲנִי אָמַרְתִּי יִהְיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל לְפָנַי חֲשׁוּבִים כִּכְרוּב וְהֵם שָׂמוּ עַצְמָם כְּנָמֵר. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אָמַר אָדוֹן: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁשָּׂמוּ עַצְמָם כְּנָמֵר – הֵן חֲשׁוּבִים לְפָנַי כִּכְרוּב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues with its explication of the verse: **"But they could not tell their fathers' houses, nor their offspring, whether they were of Israel"; this** is referring to **a foundling who is gathered from the marketplace.** Such a person does not even know if he is Jewish, as he has no knowledge of his parents. With regard to the names **"Cherub, Addon, and Immer," Rabbi Abbahu says** that these terms should be expounded as follows: **The Master [*Adon*],** God, **said: I said** that **the Jewish** people **shall be as important before Me as a cherub, but they made themselves** impudent **as a leopard [*namer*]. There are** those **who say** a different version: **Rabbi Abbahu said: The Master [*Adon*] said** that **although they made themselves as a leopard [*namer*], they are as important before Me as a cherub.**

The verse continues: *velo yachlu lehagid beit avotam vezar'am im Yisrael hem* — they could not even establish whether they were from Israel. *Zehu ha'asufi* — this is the foundling, *shene'esaf min hashuk*, one gathered from the marketplace. A *shtuki* does not know his father but knows he is Jewish through his mother. The *asufi* does not even know who his mother was, and so cannot establish Jewish identity at all.

The *netinim*, it should be noted, appeared explicitly in an earlier verse in the passage, so their presence in the returning group is already accounted for. With these derivations, the *gemara* has now covered the complete list from the *mishnah*: *kohanim*, *levi'im*, *Yisrael*, *chalalim*, *geirim*, *charurim*, *mamzeirim*, *shtukim*, *asufim*, and *netinim*.

## Intermarriage Ethics

### Leopard Metaphor

The *gemara* now transitions into a more *aggadic* register, directly confronting the social reality described by the *mishnah*. Rabbi Abbahu expounds the phrase *Keruv Addon ve'Immer* from the same verse. *Amar Adon* — the Master, God, said: *yehu Yisrael lefanai chashuvim ke-Keruv* — Israel should be as precious to Me as the cherubim, as the angels. *Vehem samu atzmam ke-namer* — but they made themselves like the leopard, like an untrustworthy, spotted animal. They committed adultery and intermarried inappropriately.

An alternative version of the same *derasha* is also recorded: even though they made themselves like the leopard and acted in this disloyal way, *hen chashuvim lefanai ke-Keruv* — they are still precious before Me as the cherubim. God's love for Israel is not extinguished by their failures. Two readings of the same words, two very different theological endpoints.

### Unequal Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: כׇּל הַנּוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ, מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ חֲרָשׁוֹ לְכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ וּזְרָעוֹ מֶלַח, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵלֶּה הָעוֹלִים מִתֵּל מֶלַח תֵּל חַרְשָׁא״.
>
> **Translation:** § Explicating the same verse, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** In the case of **anyone who marries a woman who is not suited for him** to marry due to her lineage, **the verse ascribes him** blame **as though he plowed [*ḥarash*] all** of **the entire world and sowed it with salt [*melaḥ*], as it is stated** with regard to those of flawed lineage who ascended from Babylonia: **"And these were they that ascended from Tel Melah, Tel Harsha."**

Rabba bar bar Chana then cites a teaching with sharp moral force: *kol hanosei ishah she'einah hogenet lo* — anyone who marries a woman who is not fitting for him — here the context makes clear that "fitting" refers to social and genealogical standing, *yichus* — it is as though he plowed the entire world and sowed it with salt. The imagery is of destruction: you plow the earth to prepare it for seeds, then pour in salt to render it permanently barren. The marriage produces not growth but ruin. The *gemara* finds this reading in the same verse: *ve'eleh ha'olim miTel Melach* — *charsha*, the plowing; *melach*, the salt.

### Marrying for Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב אַדָּא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַנּוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה לְשׁוּם מָמוֹן – הָוַיִין לוֹ בָּנִים שֶׁאֵינָן מְהוּגָּנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּה׳ בָּגָדוּ כִּי בָנִים זָרִים יָלָדוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Adda says** that **Rav says:** In the case of **anyone who marries a woman** of flawed lineage only **for the sake of money, he will have offspring** who will act **inappropriately, as it is stated: "They have dealt treacherously against the Lord, for they have begotten strange children;** now shall the new moon devour them with their portions" (Hosea 5:7).

Rabba bar Rav Adda citing Rav then extends this: *kol hanosei ishah leshum mamon* — anyone who marries a woman for money, that is, who accepts a lower *yichus* in exchange for wealth, *havyu lo banim she'einam hogenin* — will have children who are unworthy. The verse cited: *baHashem bagadu ki banim zarim yaladu* — they betrayed God because they bore foreign children. The word *zarim* points to the estrangement from proper lineage and character that results.

One might think the wealth at least is preserved even if the children turn out poorly. Not so:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר מָמוֹן פָּלֵט, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״עַתָּה יֹאכְלֵם חֹדֶשׁ אֶת חֶלְקֵיהֶם״. וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר: ״חֶלְקוֹ״ – וְלֹא חֶלְקָהּ, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חֶלְקֵיהֶם״. וְשֶׁמָּא תֹּאמַר לִזְמַן מְרוּבֶּה – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״חֹדֶשׁ״. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: חֹדֶשׁ נִכְנַס וְחֹדֶשׁ יָצָא וּמָמוֹנָם אָבַד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Rav Adda explains the verse: **And lest you say** that at least the **money** that they received as dowry **was spared,** although they suffer from the acts of their offspring, **the verse states: "Now shall the new moon devour them with their portions,"** meaning their property shall be consumed in a single month. **And lest you say his portion** will be lost **but not the portion** of his wife, **the verse states "their portions"** in the plural. **And lest you say** this will occur **after a long time,** but in the interim he will benefit from the money, **the verse states: "The new moon."** The Gemara asks: **From where may** it **be inferred** that their money will be lost immediately? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: A month comes and a month goes, and their money is** already **lost.** In any event, the fact that the punishment they receive is the loss of their portions indicates that the sin in this case was marrying for the sake of money.

*Atah yochlem chodesh et chelkeihem* — in a month their portion will be consumed. And lest one imagine only the man's portion is lost: *chelkeihem* — both their portions, his and hers. And lest one think the destruction takes a long time: *chodesh* — a month. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains: one month comes in and another goes out — in a single month cycle, everything is gone.

### Divine Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב אַדָּא וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי סַלָּא אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: כׇּל הַנּוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ אֵלִיָּהוּ כּוֹפְתוֹ וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא רוֹצְעוֹ. וְתָנָא: עַל כּוּלָּם אֵלִיָּהוּ כּוֹתֵב וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא חוֹתֵם: אוֹי לוֹ לַפּוֹסֵל אֶת זַרְעוֹ, וְלַפּוֹגֵם אֶת מִשְׁפַּחְתּוֹ, וְלַנּוֹשֵׂא אִשָּׁה שֶׁאֵינָהּ הוֹגֶנֶת לוֹ, אֵלִיָּהוּ כּוֹפְתוֹ וְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא רוֹצְעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba bar Rav Adda says, and some say Rabbi Salla says** that **Rav Hamnuna says:** In the case of **anyone who marries a woman who is not suited for him** to marry due to her lineage, **Elijah binds him** in the manner that those liable to receive lashes are bound, **and the Holy One, Blessed be He, straps him. And** a Sage **taught: Concerning all of them, Elijah writes and the Holy One, Blessed be He, signs** the following: **Woe to he who disqualifies his offspring, and who brings a flaw to his family** lineage, **and who marries a woman who is not** halakhically **suited for him** to marry. **Elijah binds him and the Holy One, Blessed be He, straps him.**

The teaching escalates further. Rabba bar Rav Adda citing Rav Sala citing Rav Hamnuna: *kol hanosei ishah she'einah hogenet lo, Eliyahu kofato veHakadosh Baruch Hu rotze'ahu* — whoever marries a woman who is not fitting for him, Eliyahu binds him to the post and God Himself administers the lashes. A *braita* adds: on all such people, Eliyahu writes and God seals — *oi lo leposel et zar'o uvehapogem et mishpachto ulehanosei ishah she'einah hogenet lo* — woe to him who stigmatizes his offspring and degrades his family and marries a woman not fitting for him.

The choice of Eliyahu as the one who writes this writ is significant. Eliyahu is alive in heaven; he is the one who tells God, when asked what he is doing there, that he alone has remained faithful. He is the guardian of fidelity to the covenant. That it is Eliyahu who writes and God who signs reflects a cosmic seriousness attached to this offense.

### Kol Haposel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל הַפּוֹסֵל – פָּסוּל. וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר בִּשְׁבָחָא לְעוֹלָם, וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּמוּמוֹ פּוֹסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** He further said: **And anyone who disqualifies** others by stating that their lineage is flawed, that is a sign that he himself **is** of **flawed** lineage. Another indication that one's lineage is flawed is that **he never speaks in praise** of others. **And Shmuel says:** If one habitually claims that others are flawed, **he disqualifies** himself **with his** own **flaw.** The flaw he accuses them of having is in fact the one that he has.

The *gemara* continues: *vechol haposel pasul ve'eino medaber bishvacho le'olam* — and anyone who stigmatizes others is himself *pasul*, and is the sort of person who only ever criticizes, never praises. And Shmuel sharpens this into the maxim: *bemumo posel* — when someone invalidates another, it is because that very flaw is his own. The classic formulation that emerges is *kol haposel bemumo posel*: whoever disqualifies others shares their disqualification.

A reasonable question arises: does this mean that rabbis who investigate *yichus* and declare certain families *pesulim* are thereby themselves suspect? The answer is clearly not. The teaching addresses the layperson who spreads unfounded slurs, not the rabbinic authority who investigates and adjudicates. Ezra himself identified and categorized all these groups — such inquiry is legitimate and necessary. The *gemara* is concerned with the world in which status-anxiety is high and defamation is easy: someone who casually calls others *pasul* without evidence is revealing something about himself. But that is altogether different from the structured process of communal genealogical investigation.

## Nehardea Visitor

### Butcher Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּמִנְּהַרְדְּעָא דְּעָל לְבֵי מַטְבְּחַיָּא בְּפוּמְבְּדִיתָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַבוּ לִי בִּישְׂרָא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: נְטַר עַד דְּשָׁקֵיל (לְ)שַׁמָּעֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה בַּר יְחֶזְקֵאל וְנִיתֵּיב לָךְ. אֲמַר: מַאן יְהוּדָה בַּר שְׁוִיסְקֵאל דְּקָדֵים לִי, דְּשָׁקֵל מִן קַמַּאי?! אֲזַלוּ אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה. שַׁמְּתֵיהּ. אֲמַרוּ: רְגִיל דְּקָרֵי אִינָשֵׁי ״עַבְדֵי״. אַכְרֵיז עֲלֵיהּ דְּעַבְדָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara recounts a related incident: There was **a certain man from Neharde'a who entered a butcher shop in Pumbedita. He said to them: Give me meat. They said to him: Wait until the servant of Rav Yehuda bar Yeḥezkel has taken** his meat, **and** then **we will give** it **to you.** The man **said** to them in anger: **Who is** this **Yehuda bar Sheviske'el,** a derogatory name for a glutton for meat, **that he should precede me, that he should take before me? They went** and **told Rav Yehuda** what the man had said. Rav Yehuda **excommunicated him,** in accordance with the *halakha* of one who disparages a Torah scholar. **They** also **said** to him that the same man **was in the habit of calling people slaves.** Rav Yehuda **proclaimed about him that he is a slave** and may not marry a Jew.

The *gemara* now tells a vivid story that will wind its way back to *kol haposel bemumo posel*. A certain man from Nehardea came to Pumbedisa and went to the butcher to purchase meat. He was told to wait — they needed first to serve the attendant of Rav Yehuda bar Yechezkel. The visitor was outraged. He demanded to know who this "Yehuda bar Sheviskel" was to take precedence over him — mocking the name *Yechezkel* by substituting a derogatory term, which Rashi suggests may also carry the meaning of a glutton. When the people reported this to Rav Yehuda, Rav Yehuda placed the man in *nidui* — excommunication — for showing disrespect toward a *talmid chacham*.

They then told Rav Yehuda something further: this man is in the habit of calling people *avdi* — slaves — whenever he is offended. On the basis of this, Rav Yehuda proclaimed: this man is a slave. This declaration rests directly on the principle of *kol haposel bemumo posel*: if he constantly calls others slaves, it is because that stigma is his own.

### Court Summons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל הָהוּא אַזְמְנֵיהּ לְדִינָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. אַיְיתִי פִּיתְקָא דְהַזְמָנָא. אֲזַל רַב יְהוּדָה לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵיזִיל אוֹ לָא אֵיזִיל? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵיזָל לָא מִיבְּעֵי לָךְ לְמֵיזַל, מִשּׁוּם דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה אַתְּ, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם יְקָרָא דְּבֵי נְשִׂיאָה קוּם זִיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the story: **That** man **went and summoned** Rav Yehuda **to judgment before Rav Naḥman,** who was a judge in Neharde'a. When the **summons arrived** in Pumbedita, **Rav Yehuda went before Rav Huna** to seek his council. Rav Yehuda **said to him: Should I go or should I not go?** Rav Huna **said to him:** As for the obligation **to go, you are not required to go, since you are a great man** and therefore are not under the jurisdiction of Rav Naḥman's court. **But due to the honor of the Exilarch's house,** as Rav Naḥman was the son-in-law of the Exilarch, **get up** and **go.**

The visitor, now placed in *nidui* and declared a slave, was not prepared to let the matter rest. He summoned Rav Yehuda to stand before Rav Nachman's court in Nehardea. A writ of subpoena — a *pitka de'azmana* — was delivered to Rav Yehuda.

### Huna's Advice

Rav Yehuda brought this subpoena to Rav Huna and asked: *eizal o lo eizal* — should I go or not? Rav Huna replied that as a matter of principle, Rav Yehuda had no need to go — *mishum degavra raba at* — because Rav Yehuda was a greater authority than Rav Nachman, and arguably Rav Nachman was outside his proper jurisdiction. However, Rav Nachman was a son-in-law of the house of the *nasi*, and given the honor owed to that family, Rav Huna advised: go. The whole episode had begun as a dispute about respect for authority and precedence, and now that tension was being played out on a larger stage.

### Yehuda Arrives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דְּקָעָבֵיד מַעֲקֶה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר לְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר אִידִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּתְמַנָּה אָדָם פַּרְנָס עַל הַצִּבּוּר אָסוּר בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה בִּפְנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: פּוּרְתָּא דְגוּנְדְּרִיתָא הוּא דְּקָא עָבֵידְנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סְנֵיא ״מַעֲקֶה״ דִּכְתִיב בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אוֹ ״מְחִיצָה״ דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yehuda **arrived** in Neharde'a and **found** Rav Naḥman **constructing a parapet.** Rav Yehuda **said to** Rav Naḥman: **Does the Master not hold in accordance with that** *halakha* that **Rav Huna bar Idi says** that **Shmuel says: Once a person has been appointed a leader of the community, he is prohibited from performing labor before three** people, so that he not belittle the honor of his position? Rav Naḥman **said to him: It is** merely **a little fence [*gundarita*] that I am constructing.** Rav Yehuda **said to him: Is** the term ***ma'akeh*, which is written in the Torah, or** the corresponding term ***meḥitza*, which the Sages said, distasteful** to you? Why do you use a term that is used by neither the Torah nor the Sages?

Rav Yehuda arrived and found Rav Nachman building a *ma'aka* — a fence. Immediately, Rav Yehuda challenged him: does the Master not know the teaching of Rav Huna bar Idi citing Shmuel, that once a person is appointed *parnas al hatzibur* — a communal leader — it is forbidden for him to perform labor in the presence of three people? Rav Yehuda, famously a devoted transmitter of Shmuel's teachings, was wasting no time in demonstrating that Rav Nachman was not only of lower stature but was actively violating a ruling Rav Yehuda knew firsthand. Rav Nachman replied that he was only repairing a small break, minimizing what he was doing.

### Terminology Dispute

But Rav Yehuda immediately pivoted to something else. The word Rav Nachman had used for "fence" was *gundirisa* — an unusual, elevated-sounding term. Rav Yehuda asked: what is wrong with the Torah's word *ma'aka*, or the rabbinic term *mechitza*? Why use this strange word?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יֵתִיב מָר אַקַּרְפִּיטָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִי סְנֵי ״סַפְסָל״ דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן, אוֹ ״אִיצְטְבָא״ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵיכוֹל מָר אֶתְרוּנְגָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״אֶתְרוּנְגָּא״ תִּילְתָּא בְּרָמוּת רוּחָא – אוֹ ״אֶתְרוֹג״ כִּדְקַרְיוּהּ רַבָּנַן, אוֹ ״אֶתְרוֹגָא״ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִישְׁתֵּי מָר אַנְבָּגָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סְנֵי ״אִיסְפָּרָגוֹס״ דְּקַרְיוּהּ רַבָּנַן אוֹ ״אַנְפַּק״ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי?
>
> **Translation:** During their meeting, Rav Naḥman **said to him: Let the Master sit on the bench [*karfita*].** Rav Yehuda **said to him: Is** the term ***safsal*, which the Sages said, or** the word ***itzteva*, which** common **people say, distasteful** to you? Why are you using uncommon terms? Rav Naḥman then **said to him: Let the Master eat a citron [*etronga*].** Rav Yehuda **said to him: This is what Shmuel said: Anyone who says *etronga*** demonstrates **one-third of a haughtiness of spirit.** Why? He should **either** say ***etrog*, as the Sages called it, or *etroga*, as** common **people say** in Aramaic. Saying *etronga* is a sign of snobbery, as it was employed by the aristocratic class. He subsequently **said to him: Let the Master drink a cup [*anbaga*]** of wine. Rav Yehuda **said to him: Is** the term ***ispargus*, as the Sages called it, or *anpak*, as** common **people say, distasteful** to you?

Rav Nachman then invited Rav Yehuda to sit, using the word *karpisa* for stool. Rav Yehuda responded: why not use *safsal*, which is the rabbinic term, or *itztaba*, which is what people commonly say? Rav Nachman offered fruit, calling it *estrunga*. Rav Yehuda shot back: Shmuel himself taught that anyone who says *estrunga* has a third of haughtiness in him — use either *esrog*, as the rabbis say, or *esroga*, as people say. Rav Nachman then offered a drink using the word *anvaga*. Rav Yehuda asked: why not *ispargos*, the rabbinic term, or *anfak*, the common word?

The pattern is consistent: Rav Yehuda is showing that at every turn, Rav Nachman is using inflated, affected language — putting on airs — while simultaneously conducting himself in ways unbecoming of his claimed authority. One listener suggested the opposite reading, that Rav Nachman was using common vernacular. But the *gemara*'s logic moves clearly in the other direction: Shmuel's statement that *estrunga* reflects haughtiness confirms that the vocabulary was the problem, not the simplicity.

### Women's Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תֵּיתֵי דּוֹנַג תַּשְׁקֵינַן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁים בְּאִשָּׁה. קְטַנָּה הִיא. בְּפֵירוּשׁ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁים בְּאִשָּׁה כְּלָל, בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה בֵּין קְטַנָּה. נְשַׁדַּר לֵיהּ מָר שְׁלָמָא לְיַלְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: קוֹל בָּאִשָּׁה עֶרְוָה. אֶפְשָׁר עַל יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין שׁוֹאֲלִין בִּשְׁלוֹם אִשָּׁה. עַל יְדֵי בַּעְלָהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין שׁוֹאֲלִין בִּשְׁלוֹם אִשָּׁה כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** Later on, Rav Naḥman **said to him: Let** my daughter **Donag come** and **pour us drinks.** Rav Yehuda **said to him: This is what Shmuel says: One may not make use of a woman** for a service such as this. Rav Naḥman replied: **She is a minor.** Rav Yehuda retorted: **Shmuel explicitly says: One may not make use of a woman at all, whether** she is **an adult or a minor.** Later on, Rav Naḥman suggested: **Let the Master send** greetings of **peace to** my wife **Yalta.** Rav Yehuda **said to him: This is what Shmuel says: A woman's voice is** considered **nakedness,** and one may not speak with her. Rav Naḥman responded: It is **possible** to send your regards **with a messenger.** Rav Yehuda **said to him: This is what Shmuel says: One may not send greetings to a woman** even with a messenger, as this may cause the messenger and the woman to relate to each other inappropriately. Rav Naḥman countered by suggesting that he send his greetings **with her husband,** which would remove all concerns. Rav Yehuda **said to him: This is what Shmuel says: One may not send greetings to a woman at all.**

Rav Nachman then called for his daughter *Dunag* to pour wine for them. Rav Yehuda stopped him: Shmuel explicitly taught that one may not make use of a woman's services. Rav Nachman protested — she is a minor! Rav Yehuda replied: Shmuel said explicitly, *bein gedolah bein ketannah* — whether a grown woman or a minor.

Rav Nachman then tried a different tack. Perhaps Rav Yehuda would like to send his respects to Rav Nachman's wife, Yalta? Rav Yehuda replied: Shmuel taught *kol be'ishah ervah* — the voice of a woman is *ervah*. If she responds to my greeting I will hear her voice. Rav Nachman then suggested it could be done through an intermediary. Rav Yehuda: Shmuel taught one does not ask after the welfare of a woman even through an intermediary. Through her husband then? Rav Yehuda: Shmuel taught one does not ask after a woman's welfare at all, *klal* — categorically.

This last ruling prompted a challenge from Tosafot: the *malachim* asked *Ayeh Sarah ishtecha* — where is Sarah your wife? Tosafot's distinction, which the lecturer acknowledges is not entirely clear, appears to be between asking how a woman is doing (*she'ilat shalom*) and actively dispatching a message of good wishes — the former being permitted, the latter being what Shmuel prohibits. However this distinction is parsed, the *gemara* treats *kol be'ishah ervah* here as referring to the speaking voice. The *rishonim* note that this is not absolute: in a context where men and women are accustomed to speaking with one another, the speaking voice is not categorically *ervah* — which underscores the broader principle that these categories are contextually sensitive.

### Yalta's Intervention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחָה לֵיהּ דְּבֵיתְהוּ: שְׁרִי לֵיהּ תִּגְרֵיהּ דְּלָא נִישַׁוְּויָךְ כִּשְׁאָר עַם הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** Yalta, **his wife,** who overheard that Rav Yehuda was getting the better of the exchange, **sent** a message **to him: Release him** and conclude your business with him, **so that he not equate you with another ignoramus.**

At this point Yalta, Rav Nachman's wife, sent a message to her husband: dismiss the case and let this man go — *delo nishvach kishar amei ha'aretz* — otherwise Rav Yehuda would reduce Rav Nachman to the level of an ordinary *am ha'aretz*. Rav Yehuda was making Rav Nachman look incompetent at every exchange, and Yalta was sharp enough to see that this needed to end.

### Nachman's Capitulation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי שְׁיָאטֵיהּ דְּמָר הָכָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: טַסְקָא דְהַזְמָנוּתָא שַׁדַּר מָר אַבָּתְרַאי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא שׁוּתָא דְמָר לָא גְּמִירְנָא, טַסְקָא דְהַזְמָנוּתָא מְשַׁדַּרְנָא לְמָר?! אַפֵּיק דִּיסְקָא דְהַזְמָנוּתָא מִבֵּי חָדֵיהּ וְאַחְזִי לֵיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא גַּבְרָא וְהָא דִּסְקָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוֹאִיל וַאֲתָא מָר לְהָכָא לִישְׁתַּעֵי מִילֵּיהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לֵימְרוּ מְחַנְּפִי רַבָּנַן אַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Desiring to release Rav Yehuda, Rav Naḥman **said to him: What is the reason** that **the Master is here?** Rav Yehuda **said to him: The Master sent me a summons.** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Now** that **I have not** even **learned the Master's** form of **speech,** as you have demonstrated your superiority to me by reproving me even over such matters, **could I** have **sent a summons to the Master?** Rav Yehuda **removed the summons from his bosom and showed it to him.** While doing so, Rav Yehuda **said to him: Here is the man and here is the document.** Rav Naḥman **said to him: Since the Master has come here, let him present his statement, in order that** people **should not say: The Sages flatter one another** and do not judge each other according to the letter of the law.

Rav Nachman now attempted to recover. He asked Rav Yehuda, *mai shayata demar hacha* — what brings you here? Rav Yehuda was taken aback: you sent me a subpoena! He produced the writ from his jacket and showed it. Rav Nachman responded: *hashta deshuta demar lo gamirna* — I cannot even follow your ordinary conversation, you are so far above me; do you think I would ever send you a subpoena to a *beis din*? *Chas veshalom*, I would never presume to do such a thing!

Nonetheless, since Rav Yehuda had already made the journey, Rav Nachman said they should hear the case — *ki heichi delo lemru machanfe rabbanan ahadadi* — so people should not say that rabbis merely flatter and show favoritism to one another. Rav Nachman asked: why did you put this man in *nidui*? Because he caused pain to a messenger of the rabbis. Why not lashes? Rav responded to similar offenses with lashes; Rav Yehuda explained that *nidui* is actually a more significant and lasting censure. Why did you declare him a slave?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא אַכְרֵיז מָר עֲלֵיהּ דְּעַבְדָּא הוּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּרְגִיל דְּקָרֵי אִינָשֵׁי ״עַבְדֵי״, וְתָנֵי: כׇּל הַפּוֹסֵל – פָּסוּל, וְאֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר בִּשְׁבָחָא לְעוֹלָם. וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּמוּמוֹ פּוֹסֵל. אֵימַר דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל לְמֵיחַשׁ לֵיהּ, לְאַכְרוֹזֵי עֲלֵיהּ מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman further inquired: **What is the reason** that **the Master proclaimed about him that he is a slave?** Rav Yehuda **said to him:** Because he **is in the habit of calling people slaves, and** it **is taught: Anyone who disqualifies** others by stating that their lineage is flawed, that is a sign that he himself **is** of **flawed** lineage. Another indication of his lineage being flawed is that **he never speaks in praise** of others. **And Shmuel said: He disqualifies with his** own **flaw.** Rav Naḥman retorted: You can **say that Shmuel said** this *halakha* only **to** the degree that one should **suspect him** of being of flawed lineage. But **did he** actually **say** this **to** the extent that one could **proclaim about him** that he is of flawed lineage?

*Deragil dekari inshi avdei* — because he habitually calls others slaves. *Vetani kol haposel pasul ve'eino medaber bishvacha le'olam, ve'amar Shmuel bemumo posel* — and the teaching of Shmuel is: when someone disqualifies others, it is because that flaw is his own.

Rav Nachman pressed back: Shmuel's teaching, one might say, means only to be *suspect* of such a person — not to *proclaim* him a slave outright. Is actually declaring someone a slave not taking things too far?

### Hasmonean Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי (אֲתָא הַהוּא בַּר דִּינֵיהּ מִנְּהַרְדָּעֵי). אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא בַּר דִּינֵיהּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה: לְדִידִי קָרֵית לִי ״עַבְדָּא״, דְּאָתֵינָא מִבֵּית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי מַלְכָּא?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֹּל דְּאָמַר מִדְּבֵית חַשְׁמוֹנַאי קָאָתֵינָא – עַבְדָּא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the story: **Meanwhile, that litigant arrived from Neharde'a. That litigant said to Rav Yehuda: You call me a slave? I, who come from the house of the Hasmonean kings?** Rav Yehuda **said to him: This is what Shmuel says: Anyone who says: I come from the house of the Hasmonean** kings, **is a slave.** As will be explained, only slaves remained of their descendants.

While they were still debating this very point, the litigant himself stepped forward and said to Rav Yehuda: you had the audacity to call me a slave? *De'atina mibeit Chashmonai malka* — I descend from the royal Hasmonean house!

### Slave Verdict

Rav Yehuda replied without hesitation: Shmuel taught — *kol de'amar mibeit Chashmonai ka'atina avda hu* — anyone who claims descent from the Hasmonean house is a slave. The entire Hasmonean line was wiped out; the one who assumed their name and perpetuated it was Herod — *Hordos* — who was a slave. So by claiming Hasmonean descent, the man has only confirmed the verdict.
