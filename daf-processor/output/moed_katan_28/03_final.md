# Moed Katan 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Traveling Mourner

### Holech Mimakom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הוֹלֵךְ מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, אִם יָכוֹל לְמַעֵּט בַּעֲסָקָיו — יְמַעֵּט, וְאִם לָא — יְגַלְגֵּל עִמָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita*: **If** an *avel*, a mourner, **is traveling from place to place,** he should limit his business dealings if possible. **If he can minimize his business dealings, he should do so, and if not, he should "roll with them."**

The Gemara picks up at the very bottom of 26b with a *baraita*: *holech mimakom lemakom* — if an *avel* must travel from place to place, *im yachol lema'et be'askaya yema'et* — if he can limit his business dealings, he should do so, *ve'im lav yegalgel imahen* — and if not, he should "roll with them." This is a terse and somewhat cryptic formulation. Two questions immediately arise: first, isn't an *avel* prohibited from business dealings altogether? And second, what precisely does *yegalgel imahen* mean?

### Yegalgel Imahen

The Rambam's reading, which aligns in part with Tosafot's approach, understands the passage as addressing not serious commercial activity but rather the ordinary provisions and transactions unavoidable during travel — stopping to buy food, arranging lodging, and the like. In that context, the *avel* should still try to minimize even these incidental dealings. As for *yegalgel imahen*, the Rambam seems to read it as limiting oneself to the basic necessities of the journey and no more, though precisely how that meaning is derived from the word *yegalgel* is not entirely clear. The passage remains ambiguous regarding the broader parameters of *melachah* for a traveling *avel*.

## Kfiyat Hamitah

### Me'ematai Kofin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מֵאֵימָתַי כּוֹפִין אֶת הַמִּטּוֹת? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִשְּׁיָצָא מִפֶּתַח בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From what point do** the *avel* **upturn the beds?** (This is the *halakha* concerning a particular practice of mourning) **Rabbi Eliezer says: From when** the deceased **has left the house.**

The Gemara now turns to *kefiyat hamitot* — the practice of upturning beds during mourning — which is one of the most symbolically charged expressions of *aveilut*. The question posed is: *me'ematai kofin et hamitot* — from what point does the *avel* upturn the beds? *Kefiyat hamitah* functions not merely as an ongoing personal practice but as a kind of declarative act marking the commencement of mourning status. The question is thus tantamount to asking: when does the mourning begin?

Rabbi Eliezer holds: *misheyatza mippetach beito* — from when the deceased has left the house, presumably being taken toward the cemetery. This ruling raises an interesting question: is *kefiyat hamitah* a status of the house, or a personal practice of the mourner? If it is a status of the house, the house could in principle enter that state even before the mourners themselves have begun their individual practices. The phrasing *misheyatza mippetach beito* might be making precisely that statement — that the departure of the *met* from the house triggers a changed status for the house, even before the mourners' own practices formally begin.

### R. Eliezer vs Yehoshua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוֹשׁוּעַ אוֹמֵר: מִשְּׁיִסְתַּם הַגּוֹלֵל. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בִּרְבַּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן שִׁמְעַת שֶׁכְּשׁוּם שֶׁיָּצָא הַמִּטָּה מִן הַחָצֵר, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְהַנִּשְׁמָרִים: כְּפוּ מִטּוֹתֵיכֶם. וְשׁוּם שֶׁנִּסְתַּם הַגּוֹלֵל, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשׁוּעַ לִישְׂרָאֵל: כְּפוּ מִטּוֹתֵיכֶם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: כְּבָר כְּפִינוּ עַל פִּי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The beds should be upturned only **from when the coffin is sealed.** And there is a related **incident involving Rabban Gamliel the Elder.** When the coffin **left the courtyard, Rabbi Eliezer said to those guarding** the house: **Upturn your beds.** And when the coffin **was sealed, Rabbi Yehoshua** said **to** the people: **Upturn your beds.** The people **said to him: We have already upturned** our beds **on the authority of Rabbi Eliezer.**

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees, holding that *kefiyat hamitah* begins only *misheyistam hagolel* — once the deceased is buried and the coffin is sealed. A famous *ma'aseh* is brought to illustrate the dispute. When Rabban Gamliel Hazaken died, as the body left the house, Rabbi Eliezer said to those who remained: *kefu mitoteichem* — upturn your beds. Later, once the burial was complete, Rabbi Yehoshua likewise told the mourners to upturn their beds. They replied: we have already done so on the authority of Rabbi Eliezer. The dispute is thus preserved in practice, but not resolved by this episode.

### Halacha Mikel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲלָכָה כִּדְבָרֵי הַמֵּקִיל בַּאָבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The** ruling **follows the principle of leniency in matters of mourning.**

The ruling follows the principle of *halacha kedivrei hamikel be'avel* — in matters of mourning we rule leniently. Accordingly, the *halacha* is like Rabbi Yehoshua: *kefiyat hamitah* does not begin until the *met* has actually been buried. This holds whether the question concerns the mourners' individual practices or the status of the house. Either way, the upturning of beds — and the mourning it inaugurates — begins only after *kevurah*.

## Zokfin Hamitot

### Be'erev Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאֵימָתַי זוֹקְפִין אֶת הַמִּטּוֹת בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת? מִן הַמִּנְחָה וּלְמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From what point on *erev Shabbat* may** the beds **be stood upright again?** The answer is **from *minchah* time onward.**

The Gemara next addresses when the beds may be restored. *Me'ematai zokfin et hamitot be'erev Shabbat?* — from what point on *erev Shabbat* may the beds be stood upright again? The answer is *min haminchah ulemalah* — from *minchah* time onward, one may restore the beds in preparation for *Shabbat*. Tosafot raise the question whether this refers to *minchah ketanah* or *minchah gedolah* — a significant practical difference of approximately three hours.

### Lo Yoshev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי כֵן, אֵינוֹ יוֹשֵׁב עֲלֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **However, even though the beds may be stood upright for *Shabbat*,** the *avel* **may not sit on them.**

Rav Bar Huna introduces an important distinction: *af al pi chen eino yoshev* — even though the beds may be stood upright for *Shabbat*, the *avel* may not sit on them. This reveals a meaningful conceptual division: the act of restoring the beds for *Shabbat* is permitted, but the *avel*'s personal practice of *aveilut* — which includes not sitting on a normal, upright piece of furniture — remains in force. The act of setting the house right for *Shabbat* is a distinct matter from the mourner's bodily engagement with those furnishings.

This invites a deeper question about the nature of *kefiyat hamitah*: is it primarily a status of the house — the way a house of mourning looks — or is it a practice of the *avel* as an individual, concerning where he or she sits and sleeps? Here we see both dimensions operating simultaneously, and on *erev Shabbat* they are decoupled. The beds may be restored as part of preparing the house for *Shabbat*, but the *avel*'s personal obligation to avoid sitting upon them continues.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יוֹם אֶחָד, חוֹזֵר וְכוֹפֵה.
>
> **Translation:** **On *motzai Shabbat*, even if** the *avel* **has only one day remaining** in *shiva*, **he** must **re-upturn all the beds** throughout the house **for that final day.**

The Gemara also rules: *umotzai Shabbat af al pi she-eino ela yom echad chozer ve-kofeh* — on *motzai Shabbat*, even if the *avel* has only one day remaining in *shiva*, he must re-upturn all the beds throughout the house for that final day.

## Kol Mitot

### Hakofeh Mitato

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַכּוֹפֵה אֶת מִטָּתוֹ — לֹא מִטָּתוֹ בִלְבַד הוּא כוֹפֵה, אֶלָּא כׇּל הַמִּטּוֹת שֶׁיֶּשׁ לוֹ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ הוּא כוֹפֵה. וַאֲפִילּוּ יֶשׁ לוֹ עֶשֶׂר מִטּוֹת בַּעֲשָׂרָה מְקוֹמוֹת, וְכוֹפֵה אֶת כׇּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** a *baraita*: **When an *avel* upturns his couch, he must upturn not only his own but every couch** in the **entire house he upturns. Even if he has ten couches in ten different places, he upturns all of them.**

A *baraita* clarifies the scope of the practice: *ha-kofeh mitato lo mitato bilvad hu kofeh ela kol mitot she-yesh lo b'toch beito hu kofeh* — when an *avel* upturns his couch, he must upturn not only his own but every couch in the entire house. *Ve-afilu yesh lo eser mitot b'asara mekomot* — even if he has ten couches in ten different rooms, *ve-kofeh et kulan* — he upturns all of them. This extends even to a situation of five brothers mourning together: each one, in his or her own house, upturns all the beds there. The practice is thus not merely about where the *avel* personally reclines, but about the entire state of the household. The parallel to covering mirrors throughout the house is apt — it is the full domestic environment that is transformed.

The term *mitah* here should not be translated narrowly as "bed." It encompasses couches, divans, and any furniture upon which people would sit or recline — in essence, the standard seating of the household. Just as we cover mirrors throughout the house today, the *avel* then upturned every such piece of furniture, creating an environment where neither the mourner nor any visitor had a normal place to sit.

### Meyuchedet Lekelim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיְתָה הַמִּטָּה מְיוּחֶּדֶת לִכְלִים — אֵין צְרִיכָה לִכְפּוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **If a particular couch or bench was designated exclusively for storing vessels,** it **does not need to be upturned.**

An exception is introduced: *ve-im hayta ha-mitah meyuechedet l'keilim* — if a particular couch or bench was used exclusively for storing vessels, functioning more like a dresser or a storage shelf, *ein tzarich lichpotah* — it does not need to be upturned. Even though we established that beds must be upturned even when the *avel* does not intend to sit on them, the underlying principle is that only furnishings designed for sitting or reclining require upturning.

### Dargash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּרְגָּשׁ אֵין צְרִיכוֹ לִכְפּוֹתוֹ, אֶלָּא זוֹקְפוֹ. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: שׁוֹלֵף אֶת יִתְרוֹ וְנוֹפֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **A *dargash* likewise does not need to be fully upturned; it is merely stood upright.** And **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: For a *dargash*, one releases its straps and it collapses on its own.**

The *baraita* introduces a further case: *dargash ein tzarich lichpoto ela zokfo* — a *dargash* likewise does not need to be fully upturned, but is merely stood upright. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel adds: for a *dargash*, one releases its straps and it collapses on its own. The Gemara will now investigate the nature of this *dargash*.

## Dargash Definition

### Arsa D'gada

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי דַּרְגָּשׁ? אָמַר עוּלָּא: אַרְסָא דְּגָדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **What is a *dargash*?** Ulla **says: It is a couch of good luck.**

*Mai dargash?* — what is a *dargash*? Ulla says: *arsa de-gada* — it is a couch of good luck. The word *gad* appears in *Tanach* with connotations of fortune or luck, and Ulla identifies the *dargash* as some sort of ceremonial chair associated with good fortune — not apparently an *avodah zarah* concern, but a recognized cultural artifact.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: וְהוּא אָמַר מַלְכָּא בַּשְׁבִּיתָא בִּדְרַגְשָׁא קָא יִתְכְּלִי, הַיְינוּ אַרְסָא דְּגָדָא דְּלָא קָא יִתְכְּלִי בָּהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba immediately challenges this:** He says that when a **king sits *shiva* he reclines on the *dargash*.** But **is a couch of good luck** that no one ordinarily sits on **something that a king would recline upon?**

Rabba immediately challenges this: if the *dargash* is a couch of good luck that nobody would ordinarily sit upon — rather like the *kos shel Eliyahu*, present but untouched — how can we say that a king sitting *shiva* would recline upon it? The *mishnah* teaches that when a king sits *shiva*, all the people recline on the ground while the king reclines on the *dargash*. If it is a ceremonial luck-couch that no one is ever supposed to sit on, the *dargash* would be an entirely inappropriate accommodation for the king.

### Kaviti

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשִׁי: מַאי קוּשְׁיָא?! הֲדָדֵי מִילֵּי הֵן. בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מָצוּי, אַנְ מַלְכָּא לָא קָא מִתְחַזְיָא לֵיהּ. וּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא מָצוּי, אַנְ מַלְכָּא קָא מִתְחַזְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi deflects this challenge:** Such things are **analogous** to one another. **When food is not available, we would not presume to feed the king, but when it is available, we do provide him food.** Similarly, **the *dargash* is the compromise** — we would not have the king recline on a normal bed, which would violate the practice of *aveilut*, but since he is a king, he uses this ceremonial couch as a middle ground.

Rav Ashi deflects this challenge: *mai kushya* — why is this difficult? It is analogous to eating and drinking. During ordinary times, we would not presume to feed the king, but when the king sits *shiva* we do provide him food and drink. Similarly, the *dargash* is the compromise — we would not have the king recline on a normal bed, which would violate the practice of *aveilut*, but since he is a king, he uses this ceremonial couch as a middle ground.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: אִי לָא קָא יִתְכְּלִי בִּידֵיהּ, אִי צַרִיךְ לִכְפּוֹתוֹ?! הֲרֵי כׇּל הַמִּטּוֹת כּוֹפִין אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה לִשְׁכֹּב עֲלֵיהֶן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba responds:** If **no one sits on it**, **why does it not need to be upturned?** We just established that **all beds in the house must be upturned, even those the *avel* does not intend to sit upon.**

The Gemara, however, presses forward with a stronger objection: *amai eino tzarich lichfoto* — if the *dargash* is a luck-couch that no one sits on, why does it not need to be upturned? We just established that *all* beds in the house must be upturned, even those the *avel* does not intend to sit upon. The Gemara responds: *midi dehavei a'mita ha'meyuechedet l'keilim* — it is comparable to the bench designated for storing vessels, which also does not require upturning. Only items made for sitting require upturning; a luck-couch that no one sits on is exempt on the same grounds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: אֶלָּא אִי קוּשְׁיָא הָא קוּשְׁיָא: דְּאָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל שׁוֹלֵף אֶת יִתְרוֹ וְנוֹפֵל — וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אַרְסָא דְּגָדָא, מַאי יִתְרוֹ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba insists:** If there is a question, **here it is.** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that for a *dargash*, one releases its straps and it collapses.** But **if it is a luck-couch, what are these straps?**

But the Gemara insists there remains a real difficulty: *ela i kushya ha kushya* — if there is a question, here it is. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that the *dargash* is treated by releasing its straps so that it collapses on its own. *Ve-i salka da'tach arsa de-gada* — but if it is a luck-couch, *mai kaviti it leih* — what are these straps? Ordinary couches of that era, however constructed, did not have straps. This physical detail — the straps — proves that the *dargash* is something structurally distinct, and that Ulla's identification as a luck-couch does not adequately account for its physical description.

### Arsa D'tzalla

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אָתָא רָבִּין, אָמַר: מַאי דַּרְגָּשׁ? אַרְסָא דְּצַלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael,** a Sage **reported: What is a *dargash*? A *dargash* is a couch or seat made of leather.**

*Ki ata Ravin* — when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, one of the Sages, identified in some versions as Rav Tachlifa bar Ma'arava, who was known in the marketplace of the leather-workers (*gilda'ei*), reported: *mai dargash — arsa d'tzalla* — a *dargash* is a couch or seat made of leather. This explains the straps at once. The construction was a frame with a piece of leather stretched across it, held in place by straps — something like what we might envision as a director's chair or a tent-bed, where a flat piece of leather serves as both mattress and surface, affixed to the frame by straps that, when released, cause the whole thing to collapse. The identification of the source as someone who frequented the leather-workers' marketplace lends the report particular credibility.

This explains the straps entirely. What remains somewhat unclear is precisely *why* this leather construction exempts the *dargash* from the requirement of full inversion. It is not a classic couch — which would have a wooden frame, straw filling, a cloth covering — and apparently that structural difference classifies it as categorically distinct from the furniture that must be upturned.

### Serugah Mitokho

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה: דַּרְגָּשׁ סְרוּגָה מִתּוֹכוֹ, מִטָּה סְרוּגָה עַל גַּבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **An alternative formulation is also cited: Rav Yirmiyah said:** The weaving that holds the *dargash* together **runs through the frame from within,** whereas **a regular bed has its roping woven over the top of the frame.**

An alternative formulation is also cited: *Rav Yirmiyah amar, dargash serugah mitokho* — the weaving that holds the *dargash* together runs through the frame from within, *mitah serugah al gabah* — whereas a regular bed has its roping woven over the top of the frame. For a standard bed of the period, ropes would be woven in a crisscross pattern over and around the frame, creating a surface analogous to what we might call box springs. For the *dargash*, by contrast, leather straps would be threaded through holes in the frame and knotted on the outside — running *through* rather than *over* the frame. This is an additional distinguishing characteristic, though how it alone would explain the *dargash*'s exemption from upturning is not entirely clear.

### Halakha Ke-Rabban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר אָחָא אָמַר רַב יְהוֹשׁוּעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The practical ruling is:** *Rav Yaakov bar Acha said in the name of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi, the *halacha* follows **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

The practical ruling is given: *amar Rav Yaakov bar Acha amar Rav Yehoshua ben Levi, halakha ke-Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the *halacha* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who says one releases the straps of the *dargash* and allows it to collapse on its own. Notably, this is actually the more stringent position: rather than simply leaving the *dargash* standing as-is, the ruling requires a positive act of releasing the straps. It is curious that the ruling follows the more demanding view in this case.

## Mitah Nekliftyah

### Zokfah Ve'dayo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר אָחָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מִטָּה שֶׁנִּקְלַפְתָּהּ יוֹצָא, זוֹקְפָהּ וְדַיּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yaakov bar Acha further reports in the name of Rav Assi:** A **bed whose posts extend upward,** that is, a four-poster bed, **it is sufficient merely to stand it upright; full inversion is not required.**

Rav Yaakov bar Acha further reports in the name of Rav Assi: *mitah she-naklitiah yotzin* — a bed whose posts extend upward, that is, a four-poster bed, *zokfah ve-dayo* — it is sufficient merely to stand it upright; full inversion is not required.

The phrase *zokfah ve-dayo* is interesting because it sounds as though one is actually doing something, whereas in practice it amounts to a permission to leave the bed as it stands. The reason becomes clear upon reflection: if you turn a four-poster bed completely upside down, the tall posts will actually elevate it *higher* than it was in its normal position, which defeats the symbolic purpose of lowering the furniture as an expression of mourning. Accordingly, one simply stands the bed on its side — *zokfah*, to make it vertical — and that suffices.

This point also helps clarify the earlier use of *zokfo* in the context of the *dargash*. The *Orchot Chaim* explains *zokfo* as meaning to stand the object upright — to turn it 90 degrees rather than 180 degrees — as distinct from the full inversion normally required. For the four-poster bed, this makes perfectly good sense structurally. The application to the *dargash* may similarly indicate that some partial act of disruption is required, even if a full *kefiyah* is not.

## Yashan Al Gabi

### Udini Gedolah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָשַׁן עַל גַּבִּי כִּסֵּא, עַל גַּבִּי דִּיוָן גָּדוֹל, עַל גַּבִּי קַרְקַע — לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **A *baraita* addresses:** If someone **sleeps on a chair or on a large couch, or** even **directly on the ground,** he **has not fulfilled his obligation.**

A *baraita* addresses what happens when the *avel* is not sleeping on an upturned bed at all. *Yashan al gabi kisse al gabi udaiyni gedolah* — if someone sleeps on a chair or on a large couch, *al gabi karka* — or even directly on the ground — *lo yatza yedei chovato* — he has not fulfilled his obligation. It is not enough to avoid sitting on normal furniture; the obligation requires actually sitting or sleeping on an upturned couch. Sleeping on the ground, while unusual, does not achieve the distinctive symbolic quality of the upturned bed. The particular experience of using furniture that has been inverted — rendered strange and inhospitable — is itself the practice.

### Shelo Kiyam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁלֹּא קִיֵּם כְּפִיַּת הַמִּטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yochanan states:** Such a person **has not fulfilled the obligation of *kefiyat hamitah*.**

Rabbi Yochanan states this sharply: *shelo kiyam kefiyat hamitah* — such a person has not fulfilled the obligation of *kefiyat hamitah*. What is notable about this formulation is how it frames the shortcoming. One might expect the language to say that the *avel* has not properly discharged his personal obligation. Instead, Rabbi Yochanan says that the *kefiyat hamitah* itself has not been fulfilled — as though the deficiency is in the status of the act, not merely in the person's compliance with it. This picks up on the broader question animating this entire *sugya*, beloved to the Briskers: is *kefiyat hamitah* a *chovat gavra* — a personal obligation of what the *avel* does — or is it about the objective status of the household, the reality of upturned beds? Rabbi Yochanan's phrasing suggests the latter: by not sitting on the upturned bed, the person has undermined whether the beds can truly be considered *kofin* at all.

## Mekhabdin

### Umarbitzin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְׁנָה: מְחַבְּדִין וּמַרְבִּיצִין בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל. מַדִּיחִין כְּעָרוֹת וְכוֹסוֹת וּצְלוֹחִית וּקִיתוֹנִית בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may sweep and wash the floors in the *avel*'s house. One may wash the dishes, cups, flasks, and small jugs in the house of mourning.**

The Gemara turns to the cleanliness and presentation of the house of mourning. *Mechabdin umarbitzin beveit ha'avel* — one may sweep and wash the floors in the *avel*'s house. *Umadichin kearot vekosot utzlochiot vekitoniot beveit ha'avel* — one may wash the dishes, cups, flasks, and small jugs in the house of mourning. The house may be kept clean and orderly. This is noteworthy, given how much the practice of *kefiyat hamitah* emphasizes the disruption of the household's normal state. The disruption is specific — upturned couches — and does not extend to general disorder or uncleanliness.

### Mugmar Vebasamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְבִיאִים אֶת הַמּוּגְמָר וְאֶת הַבְּשָׂמִים בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **What is not permitted, however, is bringing incense and aromatic spices into the house of mourning.**

What is not permitted, however, is bringing incense and aromatic spices into the house of mourning: *ve'ein mevi'in et hamugmar vehabsamim beveit ha'avel*. This is presumably because such things make the environment too pleasant and festive, which is inappropriate for a house in mourning.

### Beit Hamenachamin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מְבָרְכִים עַל הַמּוּגְמָר וְעַל הַבְּשָׂמִים בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara questions this from a *baraita* of Bar Kappara:** One **does not recite a *berachah* over incense and spices in the house of mourning.**

The Gemara questions this from a *baraita* of Bar Kappara: *ein mevarchin al hamugmar ve'al habsamim beveit ha'avel* — one does not recite a *berachah* over incense and spices in the house of mourning. This implies they are brought there but no *berachah* is made — which would seem to contradict the *baraita* prohibiting bringing them altogether.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא קָשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל, הָא — בְּבֵית הַמְּנַחֲמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara resolves the contradiction:** The two sources **address different locations. The prohibition on bringing incense applies to the *avel*'s own house; the rule about not reciting a *berachah* applies in the *beit hamenachamin*, the house where the comforters gather.**

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya — ha beveit ha'avel ha beveit hamenachamin* — the two sources address different locations. The prohibition on bringing incense applies to the *avel*'s own house; the rule about not reciting a *berachah* applies in the *beit hamenachamin*, the house where the comforters gather. What exactly constitutes a *beit hamenachamin* and why it would have this intermediate status is not fully spelled out. One suggestive explanation: the *beit hamenachamin* might refer specifically to the room where the *avel* and visitors are together, as distinct from rooms where the *avel* sits alone.

## Ein Molichin

### Tavla Veaskutla

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מוֹלִיכִין בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל לֹא בְּתַבְלָא וְלֹא בְּאַסְקוּתְלָא וְלֹא בְּקָנוֹן, אֶלָּא בַּסַּלִּים. וְאֵין אוֹמְרִים בִּרְכַּת אָבְלִים בַּמּוֹעֵד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One does not bring food to the *avel*'s house in fancy platters** (*tavla*, *askutla*, or *kanon* — various types of ornate trays), **but only in simple wicker baskets. One does not recite the special blessing for mourners on *chol hamoed*.**

The Gemara presents a *mishnah* governing how food is brought to the house of mourning: *ein molichin beveit ha'avel lo betavla velo be'askutla velo bekanon ela besalim* — one does not bring food to the *avel*'s house in fancy platters (*tavla*, *askutla*, or *kanon* — various types of ornate trays), but only in simple wicker baskets. The practice of bringing food to the house of mourning — especially the *seudat havra'ah* — is well established; what the *mishnah* regulates is the manner of presentation, emphasizing simplicity and humility appropriate to the setting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל עוֹמְדִים בִּשְׁעוּרִים וּמְנַחֲמִים וּפוֹטְרִים אֶת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Nevertheless,** one **does stand in the rows upon the mourners' return from burial, gives words of consolation, and then dismisses the assembled crowds.**

The *mishnah* also states: *ve'ein omrim birchat avelim b'moed* — one does not recite the special blessing for mourners on *chol hamoed*. Nevertheless, *aval omdim b'shurah umenachamim u'potrim et harabbim* — one does stand in the rows upon the mourners' return from burial, gives words of consolation, and then dismisses the assembled crowds. This reflects the delicate balance maintained during *chol hamoed*: burial does take place, *keriah* and *chalitzah* are performed, *seudat havra'ah* is provided, but the formal practices of *aveilut* are largely restricted to *devarim she'betzina*, the private observances, and those practices that would in any case not be publicly visible during *chol hamoed*, such as not laundering or cutting hair.

## Ein Mani'im

### Nashim Leolam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מַנִּיחִים אֶת הַמִּטָּה בָּרְחוֹב, שֶׁלֹּא לְהַרְגִּיל אֶת הַהֶסְפֵּד. וְלֹא שֶׁל נָשִׁים לְעוֹלָם מִפְּנֵי הַכָּבוֹד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One does not leave the bier in the street to enable a *hesped*,** so as not to encourage extended eulogizing on *chol hamoed*. **However,** women's **biers are never left in the street** even on *chol hamoed*, **out of respect.**

The *mishnah* rules: *ein manichin et hamitah barechov* — one does not leave the bier in the street to enable a *hesped*, *shelo l'hargil et ha-hesped* — so as not to encourage extended eulogizing on *chol hamoed*. However, *v'shel nashim l'olam mipnei hakavod* — women's biers are never left in the street even on *chol hamoed*, out of respect. Rabbeinu Gershom suggests this has to do with concerns about bodily fluids if the woman was a *niddah*, but the more general sense is one of modesty and dignity around women — a theme already encountered in connection with the practices of *keriah*. The precise concern is not spelled out explicitly.

## Ein Molichin (II)

### Salei Nitzarin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מוֹלִיכִים בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל, עָשִׁירִים בְּקַלְתוֹת שֶׁל כֶּסֶף וְשֶׁל זָהָב, וַעֲנִיִּים בַּסַּלֵּי נִצָּרִים. וְהָיוּ עֲנִיִּים מִתְבַּיְּישִׁין. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ הַכֹּל מְבִיאִים בַּסַּלֵּי נִצָּרִים שֶׁל אֲרָבָה קְלוּפָה, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל עֲנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages traced the history behind the regulation:** Originally, **the wealthy would bring food to the house of mourning on silver and gold trays,** *while* **the poor would bring it in wicker baskets of peeled willow.** *As a result,* **the poor were embarrassed.** The Sages therefore **instituted that everyone should bring food in the same type of simple wicker basket,** *out of* **respect for the poor.**

The Gemara traces the history behind the regulation about simple baskets. Originally, *barishona hayu molichim b'veit ha'avel ashirim b'kaltot shel kesef v'shel zahav* — the wealthy would bring food to the house of mourning on silver and gold trays, *va'aniyim b'salei nitzarin* — and the poor would bring it in wicker baskets of peeled willow. *Ve-hayu aniyim mitbayeshim* — the poor were embarrassed, both when they themselves were in mourning and received such unimpressive contributions, and when their offerings as comforters looked so modest beside those of the wealthy. The Sages therefore enacted: *hitkinu she-yehu ha-kol mevi'im b'salei nitzarin shel arava klufa* — everyone should bring food in the same type of simple wicker basket, *mipnei kevodan shel aniyim* — out of respect for the poor. Death becomes an equalizer.

## Mashkim Zechuchit

### Tzevuah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מַשְׁקִים בְּבֵית הָאָבֵל, עָשִׁירִים בִּזְכוּכִית לְבָנָה, וַעֲנִיִּים בִּזְכוּכִית צְבוּעָה. וְהָיוּ עֲנִיִּים מִתְבַּיְּישִׁין. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ הַכֹּל מַשְׁקִים בִּזְכוּכִית צְבוּעָה, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל עֲנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **A parallel reform governed drinking vessels.** Originally, **the wealthy were served in clear glass,** *while* **the poor** were served **in colored glass.** *As a result,* **the poor were embarrassed.** The Sages **instituted that everyone would drink from the same colored glass,** *out of* **respect for the poor.**

A parallel reform governed drinking vessels. Originally, *barishona hayu mashkim b'veit ha'avel ashirim b'zechuchit levana* — the wealthy were served in clear glass, *va'aniyim b'zechuchit tzevuah* — and the poor in colored glass. In that era, clear glass was the superior product; colored glass, being less refined, was associated with poverty. The poor were embarrassed. The Sages reformed the practice: *hitkinu she-yehu ha-kol mashkim b'zechuchit tzevuah mipnei kevondan shel aniyim* — everyone would drink from the same colored glass, out of respect for the poor.

## Megalim Panei

### Pnei Aniyim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מְגַלִּים פְּנֵי עָשִׁירִים וּמְכַסִּים פְּנֵי עֲנִיִּים, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהָיוּ מֻשְׁחָרִים פְּנֵיהֶם מִפְּנֵי בַצֹּרֶת. וְהָיוּ עֲנִיִּים מִתְבַּיְּישִׁין. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מְכַסִּים פְּנֵי הַכֹּל, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל עֲנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **A further reform addressed burial customs.** Originally, **the faces of the wealthy deceased were uncovered, while the faces of the poor were covered.** The **reason:** *since* **the faces of the poor would be darkened from famine.** *As a result,* **the poor were embarrassed** even in death. The Sages **instituted that** all **faces would be covered,** *out of* **respect for the poor.**

Originally, *barishona hayu megalim pnei ashirim u-mekhasim pnei aniyim* — the faces of the wealthy deceased were uncovered, while the faces of the poor were covered. The reason: *mipnei she-hayu mushkharim pneihem mipnei batzoret* — the faces of the poor would be darkened from famine, since in an era of frequent food scarcity, death often came from hunger, and the physical signs were painfully visible. The poor were embarrassed even in death. The Sages enacted: *hitkinu she-yihyu mekhasim pnei ha-kol mipnei kevondan shel aniyim* — all faces would be covered, for the sake of the dignity of the poor.

### Khalkalah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מוֹצִיאִים עָשִׁירִים בְּדַרְגָּשׁ, וַעֲנִיִּים בִּכְלִיכָה. וְהָיוּ עֲנִיִּים מִתְבַּיְּישִׁין. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ הַכֹּל מוֹצִיאִים בִּכְלִיכָה, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל עֲנִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly with the mode of conveyance:** Originally, **the wealthy were carried out on a *dargash*,** and **the poor on a** more ordinary **bier.** *As a result,* **the poor were embarrassed.** The Sages **instituted that everyone would be** brought out **on** the same simple **bier,** *out of* **respect for the poor.**

Similarly with the mode of conveyance: *barishona hayu motzi'im ashirim be-dargash* — originally the wealthy were carried out on a *dargash* — here the *dargash* reappears, this time as a mark of honor — *va'aniyim be-khalkalah* — and the poor on a more ordinary bier. *Ve-hayu aniyim mitbayeshim.* The Sages reformed the practice: *hitkinu she-yihyu ha-kol motzi'im be-khalkalah mipnei kevondan shel aniyim* — everyone would be brought out on the same simple bier, for the dignity of the poor.

## Matbilin Hakelim

### Niddot Metot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיוּ מַטְבִּילִין אֶת הַכֵּלִים עַל גַּבֵּי נִדּוֹת מֵתוֹת, וְהָיוּ נִדּוֹת חַיּוֹת מִתְבַּיְּשׁוֹת. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַטְבִּילִין עַל גַּבֵּי כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל נִדּוֹת חַיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **A related reform involved ritual vessels.** Originally, **the vessels of women who had been *niddot* when they died were immersed in a *mikveh*.** *As a result,* **women who were *niddot* while alive were embarrassed,** because their status was being effectively publicized by the special treatment accorded to their vessels after death. The Sages **instituted that** all **women's vessels would be immersed** after death, *so that* **those with menstrual impurity would not be treated differently.**

*Barishona hayu matbilin et ha-kelim al gabei niddot metot* — originally the vessels of women who had been *niddot* when they died were immersed in a *mikveh*. The phrase *al gabei* ("on the backs of" or "on account of") is admittedly unusual, but the meaning is clear: when a woman who was a *niddah* passed away, those wishing to use the vessels she had left behind in the household would immerse them in a *mikveh* to address questions of ritual impurity. This singled out *niddot* in a very public way. *Ve-hayu niddot khayot mitbayshot* — women who were *niddot* while alive were embarrassed, because their status was being effectively publicized by the special treatment accorded to their vessels after death. A woman immersing her own vessels privately is one thing; being marked as a class requiring communal attention is quite another. The Sages enacted: *hitkinu she-yihyu matbilin al gabei kol ha-nashim mipnei kevondan shel niddot khayot* — all women's vessels would be immersed after death, so that *niddot* would not be treated differently.

### Zavim Metim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה מַטְבִּילִין עַל גַּבֵּי זָבִים מֵתִים, וְהָיוּ זָבִים חַיִּים מִתְבַּיְּישִׁין. הִתְקִינוּ שֶׁיְּהוּ מַטְבִּילִין עַל גַּב הַכֹּל, מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹדָן שֶׁל זָבִים חַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly,** originally, **the vessels of men who had died while in a state of *zivah*** were **immersed.** *As a result,* **men who were *zavim* in life were embarrassed.** The Sages **instituted that** all **men's vessels would be immersed,** *out of* **respect for those with abnormal discharge who were alive.**

*Barishona matbilin al gabei zavim metim* — originally, the vessels of men who had died while in a state of *zivah* — an abnormal bodily discharge rendering them ritually impure — were similarly immersed. *Ve-hayu zavim khayim mitbayeshim* — men who were *zavim* in life were embarrassed by this public posthumous distinction. The Sages enacted accordingly: *hitkinu she-yihyu matbilin al gabei ha-kol mipnei kevondan shel zavim khayim* — all men's vessels would be immersed, for the dignity of *zavim* who were alive.

## Hotza'at HaMet

### Kasheh Lekrovav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיְתָה הוֹצָאַת הַמֵּת קָשָׁה לִקְרוֹבָיו יוֹתֵר מִמִּיתָתוֹ, עַד שֶׁהָיוּ קְרוֹבָיו מַנִּיחִים אוֹתוֹ וּבוֹרְחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **The situation was extraordinarily difficult:** Originally, **the expense of burial was harder on the relatives of the deceased than the death itself,** to the point that **relatives would simply abandon the body** rather than face the costs.

*Barishona haytah hotza'at ha-met kashah li-krovav yoter mi-mitato* — originally the expense of burial was harder on the relatives of the deceased than the death itself. The financial burden associated with funerals was so crushing that, as the Gemara states starkly, *ad she-hayu krovav manikhin oto u-vorkhin* — relatives would simply abandon the body rather than face the costs. This is striking testimony to how economically devastating funeral practices had become.

### Klei Pishtan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְנָהַג קַלּוּת רֹאשׁ בְּעַצְמוֹ, וְיָצָא בִּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן. וְנָהֲגוּ הָעָם אַחֲרָיו לָצֵאת בִּכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Until Rabban Gamliel intervened:** He **treated himself with deliberate lightness** in this regard, and **was buried in simple linen garments.** **Thereafter, people followed his example and were buried in linen.**

The situation persisted *ad she-ba Rabban Gamliel* — until Rabban Gamliel the *Nasi* intervened. *Ve-nahag kalut rosh be-atzmo* — he treated himself with deliberate lightness in this regard, *ve-hotzi'uhu bi-khlei pishtan* — and was buried in simple linen garments. *Ve-nahagu acharav latzeit bi-khlei pishtan* — and thereafter, people followed his example and were buried in linen. It is striking to realize that what we now think of as quintessentially Jewish burial practice — the *tachrichim*, the linen garments, the *kittel* — evolved not from some ancient mystical imperative but from a social reform driven by concern for the poor and the economically burdened. What began as a ruling Rabban Gamliel applied to himself became universal practice precisely because the *Nasi*'s personal example carried moral authority.

### Tzerda De'bar Zuza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְהָאִידָּנָא נְהוּג עָלְמָא אֲפִילּוּ בְּצַרְדָּא דְּבַר זוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Papa adds:** Even **a cloak costing as little as a single *zuza*,** something utterly inexpensive, **is now acceptable** and indeed standard **for burial.**

Rav Papa adds: *afilu b'tzerda de'bar zuza* — even a cloak costing as little as a single *zuza*, something utterly inexpensive, is now acceptable and indeed standard for burial. This represents the full arc of the reform: what had been an occasion for ruinous expenditure had been reduced to the most minimal of expenses.

## Ein Mani'im (II)

### Biraḥov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מַנִּיחִים אֶת הַמִּטָּה בָּרְחוֹב, שֶׁלֹּא לְהַרְגִּיל אֶת הַהֶסְפֵּד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara returns to the ruling:** **One does not leave the bier in the street,** so as not to encourage *hespedim* **during** a **time when they are restricted.**

The Gemara returns to the ruling of the *mishnah*: *ein manichin et hamitah barachov* — one does not leave the bier in the street on *chol hamoed*, in order not to encourage *hespedim* during a time when they are restricted.

### Ein Moed Talmid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֵין מוֹעֵד בִּפְנֵי תַּלְמִיד חָכָם, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן חֲנוּכָּה וּפוּרִים. וְהַנִּי מִילֵּי בְּפָנָיו, אֲבָל שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו — לָא. אִינִי?! וְהָא רַב כָּהֲנָא סַפְדֵיהּ לְרַב זְבִיד מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא בְּפוּם נַהֲרָא! אָמַר רַב פַּפֵּי: יוֹם שְׁמוּעָה הֲוָה, וְכִבְפָנָיו דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Papa rules:** **The restrictions of *chol hamoed* on *hespedim* do not apply in the presence of a *talmid chacham*; a eulogy is given for a Torah scholar even on *chol hamoed*.** **And this applies, however, *in the presence of the body itself*.** The Gemara immediately challenges this: Did not **Rav Kahana eulogize Rav Zvid of Neharde'a in Pum Nahara** — a **different location, far from where the body was?** **Rav Pappi explains:** That was **the day** on which Rav Kahana received the **news** of the death, **and** a eulogy in such a situation **is considered as** if it is **in his presence.**

Rav Papa rules: *ein moed bifnei talmid chacham* — the restrictions of *chol hamoed* on *hespedim* do not apply in the presence of a *talmid chacham*; a eulogy is given for a Torah scholar even on *chol hamoed*. *Ve-chol sheken Chanukah u'Purim* — and certainly during Chanukah and Purim. This applies, however, *bifanav* — in the presence of the body itself. *Aval shelo bifanav lo* — but not when the body is absent.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: Rav Kahana eulogized Rav Zvid of Neharde'a in Pum Nahara — a different location, far from where the body was. Rav Pappi explains: *yom shmu'ah havah, u'k'bifanav dami* — that was the very day Rav Kahana received the news of the death, and on the day one first hears of a death, it is considered subjectively equivalent to being in the presence of the body. This is a striking answer. The ruling that *ein moed bifnei talmid chacham* is grounded in the objective honor due to the *chacham* and the objective reality of *kavod hachacham* overriding *chol hamoed* restrictions. If so, one might expect the override to require the physical presence of the body itself. Yet the Gemara allows the subjective reality — the day of hearing — to suffice. This is an unusual interplay between the objective rationale and a subjective criterion.

### Yom Shmu'ah

The upshot of Rav Pappi's ruling is that *yom shmu'ah* — the day one first hears of the death of a *talmid chacham* — is treated as equivalent to being in the presence of the body, and a *hesped* may be delivered even on *chol hamoed*.

## Hesped Halev

### Sopfim Al Shadayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: הֶסְפֵּד עַל לֵב, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַל שָׁדַיִם סוֹפְדִים״. טִיפּוּחַ בַּיָּד. קִילּוּס בָּרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla teaches:** There is **a eulogy of the heart.** He derives this **from the verse:** *"Striking [*sofedim*] the breasts"** (Isaiah 32:12). He also identifies **a kind of clapping or beating with the hand;** and **a stamping of the feet.**

Ulla teaches: *hesped halev* — there is a eulogy of the heart. He derives this from the verse *al shadayim soptim* — "upon the breasts they eulogize." This reframes what a *hesped* is. Throughout the earlier discussion, *hesped* appeared primarily as a verbal art — poetic speech in praise of the deceased. Here we see its physical and performative dimensions. *Kipuach beyad* — a kind of clapping or beating with the hand; *kilus b'regel* — a stamping of the feet. These were not merely peripheral gestures but integral components of the public ritual of mourning. When we understand the *hesped* as this full-bodied communal event — the wailing, the beating of chests, the stamping — it becomes much clearer why *hespedim* are restricted during times such as *chol hamoed* and the entirety of *chodesh Nissan*. Nowadays, when the *hesped* has become a dignified verbal tribute, its restriction feels almost incongruous. The restrictions were designed for a phenomenon that was expansive, loud, and intensely communal in a way quite foreign to contemporary practice. This helps explain why today those restrictions are often observed more in letter than in spirit — people will note that it is *Nissan* and offer "a few words" — but one can appreciate that such a practice is not a severe violation if one understands that the ancient phenomenon being restricted was categorically different.

### Hamakaless

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְקַלֵּס — לֹא יְקַלֵּס בְּסַנְדָּל, אֶלָּא בְּמִנְעָל, מִפְּנֵי הַסַּכָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** Those who **stomp their feet during the *hesped*** **should not do so in a sandal, but rather in a proper shoe, because of the danger.**

Those who stamp their feet during the *hesped* — *hamakaless lo yakaless b'sandal* — should not do so in a sandal, *ela b'min'al*, but rather in a proper shoe, *mipnei hasakanah* — because of the danger. Rabbeinu Gershom explains that a sandal provides insufficient support and can easily twist the foot during forceful stamping. Rabbeinu Chananel suggests that going barefoot at least makes one more careful about where one places one's foot; either way, the sandal is the most dangerous option.

## Niyana Rosho

### Shov Ein Menachamin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָבֵל, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּיעְנַע רֹאשׁוֹ — שׁוּב אֵין מְנַחֲמִים רַשָּׁאִים לֵישֵׁב אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yochanan teaches:** **Once the mourner has nodded his head,** the **comforters may no longer sit beside him.**

Rabbi Yochanan teaches: *avel kivan she-niana rosho shuv ein menachamin rashaim liyeshev etzlo* — once the mourner has nodded his head, the comforters may no longer sit beside him. This is a signal from the mourner that he has reached a point where he wishes to be left alone — whether because he has momentarily found a degree of inner equilibrium and needs space to be with himself, or simply because he is withdrawing inward and needs solitude. Rabbeinu Gershom suggests that it is a sign that he is consoling himself.

This ruling carries a significant practical lesson. Comforting mourners requires attention not only to what one says but to what the mourner actually needs. Too often the visit becomes about the comforter — what they said, whether they said the right thing, whether they stayed long enough — rather than about reading where the mourner is. Here the *halacha* explicitly tells us: when the mourner signals that he needs to be left alone, leave. The nod of the head is enough.

### Hakol Chayabim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל חַיָּיבִים לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי נָשִׂיא, חוּץ מֵאָבֵל וְחוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yochanan further rules:** **All are obligated to stand when the *Nasi* is present,** *except* **for a mourner and someone who is ill.**

Rabbi Yochanan further rules: *ha-kol chayabim la'amod bifnei nasi* — all are obligated to stand when the *Nasi* is present, *chutz me'avel va-choleh* — except for a mourner and someone who is ill. The exemption of the sick is self-evident. The exemption of the *avel* is more conceptually interesting: the mourner is permitted to remain within his own private universe, not required to respond to social hierarchies or public formalities. He is, in a sense, in a different realm.

### Lekal Omrim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לַכֹּל אוֹמְרִים לָהֶם ״שֵׁבוּ״, חוּץ מֵאָבֵל וְחוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The corollary is also stated:** For **everyone who stood for the *Nasi*,** you **tell them to sit back down,** *except* **for a mourner and someone ill.**

The corollary is also stated: *le-khol omrim lahem shevu* — for everyone who stood for the *Nasi*, you tell them to sit back down, *chutz me'avel va-choleh* — except for a mourner and someone ill. You do not say *shevu* to them, because they never had to stand in the first place. One interpretation adds: the word *shevu* can also connote "remain" or "stay," and one would not want to suggest such a connotation to an *avel* or a *choleh*.

## Léchem Mishlo

### Yom Rishon Assur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אָבֵל, יוֹם רִאשׁוֹן אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל לֶחֶם מִשֶּׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Rav:** **A mourner on the first day is forbidden to eat from his own bread; he must be fed by others.**

Rav Yehuda reports in the name of Rav: *avel yom rishon assur le'echol lechem mishelo* — a mourner on the first day is forbidden to eat from his own bread; he must be fed by others. This is the underlying rationale for the practice of *seudat havra'ah*.

### Yechezkel Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא לִיחֶזְקֵאל: ״וְלֶחֶם אֲנָשִׁים לֹא תֹאכֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The source is a verse in *Yechezkel*,** where **God tells the prophet:** *"And the bread of people you shall not eat."* The verse is read as revealing what is normative for a mourner: since **God commands *Yechezkel* specifically not to eat others' bread** — implying that doing so would be the natural expectation — this shows that a normal *avel* is precisely supposed to be fed from others' food.

The source is a verse in *Yechezkel*, where God tells the prophet: *ve-lechem anashim lo tochal* — "and the bread of people you shall not eat." The verse is read as revealing what is normative for a mourner: since God commands *Yechezkel* specifically not to eat others' bread — implying that doing so would be the natural expectation — this shows that a normal *avel* is precisely supposed to be fed from others' food. One must be careful here, as *Yechezkel* is often a source for *aveilut* through inversion — learning from what he was told *not* to do what a normal mourner is obligated to do. The verse thus establishes that on the first day of mourning, the *avel* depends on others for sustenance.

### Machalfi Seudataihu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מְחַלְּפִי סְעוֹדָתַיְיהוּ לַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef reports:** Rabbah and **Rav Yosef** would **exchange their meals with one another when both were mourners simultaneously.**

Rav Yosef reports: *machalfi seudatayhu le-hadadi* — Rabbah and Rav Yosef exchanged their meals with one another when both were mourners simultaneously. By each giving the other food from his own supply, each was being fed by the other's bread, satisfying the requirement that the *avel* be fed by others. This exchange is reminiscent of a similar arrangement reportedly made regarding *mishloach manot* on *Purim* — two Sages without means would exchange their *seudot* so that each fulfilled the obligation without either going without. Here too, the exchange may reflect both limited means and a creative halakhic solution to a situation where two mourners found themselves in *aveilut* at the same time.

## Met Be'ir

### Kol Bnei Hair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מֵת בְּעִיר — כׇּל בְּנֵי הָעִיר אֲסוּרִין בַּעֲשִׂיַּית מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda reports further in the name of Rav:** **If someone has died in the city,** all **the residents of the city are forbidden from doing *melachah*.** The scope of this ruling is striking — not merely the immediate family or community of the deceased, but the entire population of the town.

Rav Yehuda reports further in the name of Rav: *met be'ir — kol bnei ha'ir asurim ba'asiyat melachah* — if someone has died in the city, all the residents of the city are forbidden from doing *melachah*. The scope of this ruling is striking — not merely the immediate family or community of the deceased, but the entire population of the town.

### Rav Hamnuna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הַמְנוּנָא אִיקְּלַע לְדֵרוּ מָתָא, שְׁמַע קוֹל שִׁיפּוּרָא דְשָׁכְבָא. חֲזָא הָנָךְ אִינָשֵׁי דְּקָא עָבְדִי עֲבִידְתָּא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לֶיהְווֹ הָנָךְ אִינָשֵׁי בְּשַׁמְתָּא. לָא שָׁכְבָא אִיכָּא בְּמָתָא?! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: חֲבוּרָתָא אִיכָּא בְּמָתָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי הָכִי, שַׁרְיָא לְכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara illustrates this with a story.** **Rav Hamnuna** once **came to a certain town. He heard the sound** of the *shofar* **blowing to signal that someone had died.** He then **saw people going about their work,** apparently immediately after hearing the announcement. **He said to them:** "Let **these people be under an excommunication. Is there not a body in the city that needs to be buried?"** They **replied:** "There **is a *chevra*, a society, in the city** to handle the burial." **Rav Hamnuna responded:** If that **is so,** then **I release you** from the *nidduy*."

The Gemara illustrates this with a story. Rav Hamnuna *ikla l'darumsa* — came to a certain town. He heard the sound of the *shofar* blowing to signal that someone had died — *shama kali shappura d'shichva*. He then saw people going about their work — *haza hanakh inishi d'ka avdi avidata* — apparently immediately after hearing the announcement. *Amar lehu* — he said to them: *lehavei hanakh inishi b'shamta* — let these people be in a state of *nidduy*. Is there not a body in the city that needs to be buried?

They replied: *chavrusas ika b'mata* — there is a *chevra*, a society, in the city to handle the burial. Rav Hamnuna responded: if that is so, *sharya l'cho* — I release you from the *nidduy*. Rabbeinu Gershom explains: *chavurot hen she'elu kovrin meiseihen levadan ve'elu kovrin meiseihen levadan* — these were distinct societies in which each group takes responsibility for burying its own. When such an organized society exists to take charge of the burial, the general prohibition on *melachah* for the rest of the town is lifted. The existence of an organized *chevra kadisha* changes the communal obligation.

## Hamiskasheh

### Al Meis Acher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַמִּתְקַשֶּׁה עַל מֵתוֹ יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי — עַל מֵת אַחֵר הוּא בּוֹכֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda teaches in the name of Rav:** "Whoever **grieves over his deceased excessively** must in fact be **weeping for another dead.**"

Rav Yehuda teaches in the name of Rav: *kol ha-miskasheh al meito yoter midai — al meis acher hu bocheh* — whoever grieves over his deceased excessively must in fact be weeping for another dead. The tone of this teaching shifts from a psychological observation to something more ominous: the "other dead" may not be someone already gone, but someone yet to come.

### Hahi Itta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּהֲוָת בִּשְׁיָבָבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, הֲווֹ לַהּ שִׁבְעָה בָּנִים. מִית חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ. הֲווֹת קָא בָּכְיָא בִּיתִירֻתָא עֲלֵיהּ. שְׁלַח לַהּ רַב הוּנָא: לָא תַּעַבְדִי הָכִי! לָא אַשְׁגְּחָה בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara brings a story to illustrate.** *There **was a woman in the neighborhood of Rav Huna. She had seven sons. One of them died. She was crying excessively.** Rav Huna sent word to her: Do not do this. She ignored him.

The Gemara brings a story to illustrate. *Hahi itta d'havas bishvavusei d'Rav Huna* — there was a woman in the neighborhood of Rav Huna. *Havu lah shiva banin* — she had seven sons. One of them died. *Havas kabbakhya beyaserata* — she was crying excessively. Rav Huna sent word to her: do not do this. She ignored him.

### Tzavit Zavda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לַהּ: אִי צָיְיתַתְּ — מוּטָב, וְאִי לָא — צְבִית זְוַודְתָּא לְאִידַּךְ מִית, וּמִיתוּ כּוּלְּהוּ. לְסוֹף אֲמַר לַהּ: תִּימוּשׁ זְוַודְתָּא לְנַפְשִׁיךְ, וּמִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **He sent again:** "If **you listen** it **will be good, and if not, prepare the shrouds for the next one to die.** But she would **not listen, and they all died. Finally he said to her:** Now prepare the shrouds for yourself, and she died."

He sent again: *ee tzaish mutav* — if you listen it will be good, *ve'i la — tzavis zavda l'idakh meis* — and if not, prepare the shrouds for the next one to die. She did not listen, *u-meisu kuleihu* — and all her sons died. Finally he said to her: *tzavis zavda l'nafsheikh* — now prepare the shrouds for yourself. And she died.

This is an extraordinarily harsh story, and it is difficult to read without discomfort — particularly the image of a mother who has already lost one child being warned about, and then experiencing, the successive deaths of all her others. One might understand Rav Huna's concern: in a world of highly demonstrative public mourning, there may have been a question of whether excessive grief could become disconnected from genuine feeling, or whether such consuming mourning caused one to lose sight of the living family members who still needed care. Rav Yehuda's earlier teaching suggests this very concern — that the one who grieves excessively may be forgetting the others who remain. The image of *Yaakov Avinu* is invoked implicitly: consumed for decades by mourning for *Yosef*, he arguably neglected the rest of his children and household. Still, the harshness of the story remains striking, and whether Rav Huna's words were prediction or causation is a question the Gemara leaves open.

## Al Tivku Lamet

### Shlosha Yamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִרְמְיָהוּ: ״אַל תִּבְכּוּ לַמֵּת וְאַל תָּנוּדוּ לוֹ יוֹתֵר מִכְּשִׁיעוּר״. הָא כֵּיצַד? שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים לִבְכִי, וְשִׁבְעָה לְהֶסְפֵּד, וּשְׁלוֹשִׁים לְגִיהוּץ וּלְתִסְפּוֹרֶת. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אִי אַתֶּם רַחְמָנִים בּוֹ יוֹתֵר מִמֶּנִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **A verse in *Yirmiyahu* is cited as the biblical basis for prescribed limits on mourning:** *"Do not weep excessively for the dead and do not wail for him beyond the proper measure."* **The limits are then specified:** The **first three days are for weeping, and the full seven days for *hesped*, and thirty days for laundry and haircuts.** **After that, the Gemara has God declare:** "Do not have more compassion for him than I do."

A verse in *Yirmiyahu* is cited as the biblical basis for prescribed limits on mourning: *al tivku lamet yoter midai veal tanudu lo yoter mikhishiur* — do not weep excessively for the dead and do not wail for him beyond the proper measure. The limits are then specified: *shlosha yamim livchi* — the first three days are for weeping, *ve-shiva* — and the full seven days for *hesped* — eulogizing and the physical expressions of mourning.

*Ve-shloshim le-gihutz u-letisporet* — and thirty days for laundry and haircuts. After that, the Gemara has God declare: *ee atem rachmanim bo yoter mimeni* — you should not have more compassion than I do. What is striking here is that the practices of *shloshim* — not laundering, not cutting hair — are presented in this context not merely as halakhic categories but as expressions of grief, continuous with the weeping and eulogizing of the earlier days. The transition from emotive expressions to behavioral restrictions frames those restrictions as belonging to the same continuum of mourning.

## Becho Becho

### Laholeich Belo Vanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּכוּ בָכוֹ לַהוֹלֵךְ״, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לַהוֹלֵךְ בְּלֹא בָּנִים. רַבִּי יְהוֹשׁוּעַ בֶּן לֵוִי לָא אָזַל לְבֵי אִבְלָא אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָזֵיל בְּלָא בְּנֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּכוּ בָכוֹ לַהוֹלֵךְ כִּי לֹא יָשׁוּב עוֹד וְרָאָה אֶת אֶרֶץ מוֹלַדְתּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse is interpreted:** *"Cry, cry for the one going,"* **Rav Yehuda says:** **Cry for the one who leaves without children.** Such a death is especially deserving of grief because the person has left nothing behind — no descendants, no continuity. **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** followed this interpretation: **He would only go to a house of mourning when the deceased had died without children,** as **the verse** teaches: *"Cry, cry for the one who will not return again, who will not see the land of his heritage** and of his progeny."

The verse *becho becho la-holeich* — "cry, cry for the one going" — is interpreted. Rav Yehuda says: cry for the one who leaves without children. Such a death is especially deserving of grief because the person has left nothing behind — no descendants, no continuity. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi went further in practice: *lo azil le-vei avla ela le-man de'azil belo vnei* — he would only go to a house of mourning when the deceased had died without children. The verse supports this: *becho becho la-holeich ki lo yashuv od ve-ra'ah et eretz moladeto* — cry for the one who will not return again, who will not see the land of his heritage and of his progeny.

### She'avar Aveira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: זֶה שֶׁעָבַר עֲבֵירָה וְשָׁנָה בָּהּ, וְלֹא יָשׁוּב עוֹד. רַב הוּנָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כֵּיוָן שֶׁעָבַר אָדָם עֲבֵירָה וְשָׁנָה בָּהּ — הוּתְּרָה לוֹ. הוּתְּרָה לוֹ סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ כְּהֶיתֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna offers a different reading.** *"This refers to one* **who sinned and repeated the sin, and will no longer return,** *because he has descended into a moral state from which there is no *teshuvah*.* Rav Huna goes according to his own broader principle: **Once a person commits a sin and repeats it, it becomes permitted to him.** The Gemara immediately qualifies: **Does it literally become permitted?** *No, rather* **it becomes as if it were permitted in his own mind.** The person has rationalized the behavior to the point where he no longer perceives it as a transgression, and from that point, return becomes nearly impossible.

Rav Huna offers a different reading. *Zeh she'avar aveira ve-shanah bah* — this refers to one who sinned and repeated the sin, *velo yashuv od* — and will no longer return, because he has descended into a moral state from which there is no *teshuvah*. Rav Huna goes according to his own broader principle: *keivan she'avar adam aveira ve-shanah bah, hutra lo* — once a person commits a sin and repeats it, it becomes permitted to him. The Gemara immediately qualifies: *hutra lo salka da'tach?* — does it literally become permitted? *Ela eima na'asit lo keheter* — no, rather it becomes as if it were permitted in his own mind. The person has rationalized the behavior to the point where he no longer perceives it as a transgression, and from that point, return becomes nearly impossible. This is the teaching that Rabbi Yisrael Salanter sharpened into the observation that if one does something three times, it can come to feel like a *mitzvah*.

## Sheloshah Yamim

### Cherev Munachat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: אָבֵל, שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים יִרְאֶה אֶת עַצְמוֹ כְּאִילּוּ חֶרֶב מוּנַּחַת לוֹ בֵּין שְׁתֵּי יַרְיכוֹתָיו. מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה עַד שִׁבְעָה — כְּאִילּוּ מוּנַּחַת לוֹ כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בְּקֶרֶן זָוִית. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — כְּאִילּוּ עוֹבֶרֶת כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בַּשּׁוּק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi describes the inner emotional world of the *avel* across the seven days of *shiva*.** During the **first three days, the mourner should feel as though a sword is hovering between his sides.** **Between the third and seventh days,** the sword **is still present** in the room, **but it has moved to a corner.** **From the seventh day onward,** it **is as though** the sword **passes by in the street.**

Rabbi Levi describes the inner emotional world of the *avel* across the seven days of *shiva*. *Shlosha yamim harishonim yireh atzmo ke'ilu cherev munachat lo bein shtei yarchotav* — during the first three days, the mourner should feel as though a sword is hovering between his sides. This is not merely grief but a kind of existential alarm — an awareness that death has come close, that the one who died was part of a group to which the mourner belongs, and that mortality has drawn near. The Sages elsewhere teach that when one member of a group dies, the others should feel concern for themselves. The sword at the sides captures precisely this.

*Mishlosha ad shiva ke'ilu munachat lo kenegdo be-keren zavit* — between the third and seventh days, the sword is still present in the room, but it has moved to a corner. The acute terror has receded somewhat, but the presence of death remains immediate.

*Mikan va'eilach ke'ilu overet kenegdo ba-shuk* — from the seventh day onward, it is as though the sword passes by in the street. It is no longer in the room, but it is still out there. The sense of mortality does not disappear; it simply distances itself from the person's immediate experience. This three-stage progression captures something true about the arc of acute grief — the initial overwhelming intensity, the gradual but still-present weight through the *shiva*, and the lingering background awareness that persists thereafter.
