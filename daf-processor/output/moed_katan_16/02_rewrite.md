# Moed Katan 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Practices Comparison

### Intro: Practices List

We pick up in the middle of 15b, continuing through a list of practices that apply to an *avel* and then examining whether those same practices apply to a *metzora* and a *menudeh* — mostly yes, but with notable exceptions. The *gemara* proceeds category by category, asking in each case whether the rule that governs the *avel* extends to these other two figures.

### Ne'ilat Hasandal

The *gemara* establishes that an *avel* is forbidden in *ne'ilat hasandal* — wearing a shoe. The source is the verse in which God instructs Yechezkel: *u'na'alecha tasim beraglecha* — "put your shoes on your feet" (Yechezkel 24:17). The inference, as the *gemara* explains, is *miklal d'chulei alma assur*: from the fact that Yechezkel required a special dispensation to wear shoes, we derive that the ordinary rule is prohibition. The *gemara* here follows its recurring pattern of learning *avel* practices from Yechezkel — God commanding Yechezkel to do the opposite of the usual practice.

The *gemara* then asks: *menudeh mahu bine'ilat hasandal* — what is the rule for a *menudeh* regarding shoes? Rav Yosef cites a *baraita* concerning the practices of a *ta'anit* (public fast day): when they said one may not wear a *sandal*, they said so only *be'ir* — within the city — but on the road one may wear shoes. If one sets out on a journey, he puts his shoe on; when he enters the city, he removes it. The *baraita* then continues: *vechen atta motzeh bemenudeh u've'avel* — and so too you find with a *menudeh* and an *avel*. This seems like fairly strong evidence that the prohibition of *ne'ilat hasandal* applies to a *menudeh* as well.

The *gemara*, however, offers its characteristic deflection: *lo a'shara* — the statement that the same applies to a *menudeh* and an *avel* refers to *other* practices shared between *ta'anit* and *avel*, such as the prohibition against bathing or haircuts, but not necessarily to the specific practice of shoes just mentioned. So although the surface reading strongly suggests that a *menudeh* does not wear shoes, the *gemara* stops short of a definitive ruling.

As for a *metzora*, the *gemara* simply says *teiku* — the question of *ne'ilat hasandal* for a *metzora* remains unanswered. This is noteworthy when read alongside the earlier unresolved question of *rechitzah* (bathing). In both cases the plain sense of the *baraita* — *vechen atta motzeh bemenudeh u've'avel* — suggests that the practice does extend to a *menudeh*, but the *gemara* does not confirm this conclusively.

From a conceptual standpoint, *ne'ilat hasandal* fits comfortably into a category of externally visible practices that mark a person as separated from normal social participation — disheveled clothes, wild hair, shoes removed. These are the outward signs of someone who has been, in some sense, cast outside the ordinary rhythms of communal life. This is distinct from the practices unique to the *avel* that reflect the inner state of mourning — *tefillin*, *Torah* study, and *kefiyat hamittah* — which express the loss of joy and the embrace of sadness rather than social separation.

It is worth pausing on what distinguishes an *avel* from a *menudeh* in this regard. The *avel* has not been expelled from society; the community comes to him in the form of *nichum avelim*. Yet he, too, withdraws from the normal rhythms of life — he does not leave the house during *shiva*, he does not engage in ordinary interaction — precisely to make space for the internal work of grief, to fully absorb the reality of what has occurred. The *metzora*, by contrast, is explicitly commanded in the Torah to call out *tamei tamei*, to let his garments hang loose, to dwell alone outside the camp — the marks of one who is genuinely kept apart. And the *menudeh* is indeed a case of societal censure: this person has acted in unacceptable ways, and the *halacha* imposes upon him outwardly antisocial behavior as a form of communal pressure until he conforms. Most of the demands of *niduy* fall on the *menudeh* himself — how he must behave — even as society is simultaneously required to give him the cold shoulder.

There is also the matter of *asiyat melachah*. Recall that the *avel* is forbidden to work, but this prohibition is not primarily about social interaction. Rather, the *gemara* elsewhere identifies it as *mesiach da'at min ha'aveilus* — work distracts from the interior focus that mourning demands. It is thus a prohibition oriented inward. Tellingly, the *menudeh* is not only permitted to do his own work, but may hire laborers and be hired out. The *menudeh* is not fully cut off from society in that economic sense — only from its more intimate registers.

### Tashmish HaMitah

An *avel* is forbidden in *tashmish hamittah* — marital relations. The source is the verse *vayenachem David et Batsheva ishto* (II Shmuel 12:24), which describes David consoling Batsheva and coming unto her. The context makes clear this occurred after the period of *aveilus* for the infant had ended, from which we infer — *miklal d'me'ikara assur* — that during *aveilus* it is forbidden.

The *gemara* asks: *menudeh mahu betashmish hamittah*? Rav Yosef attempts a proof: *kol otan shanim shehayu Yisrael bamidbar menudin hayu* — throughout all the years Yisrael were in the *midbar*, they were in a state of rejection by God, yet they clearly had marital relations, as evidenced by the children born during that period. Abaye immediately challenges this: *d'dilma menudeh lashamayim shani d'kil* — perhaps the case of being rejected by heaven is fundamentally different, and perhaps more lenient. But then, the *gemara* notes, in the previous discussion Abaye had implied the opposite — that *menudeh lashamayim* might be *chamur*, more severe. The *gemara* concludes that *sefuki mesapka lei* — Rav Yosef simply does not know which way the status of *menudeh lashamayim* cuts: *zeil hacha kamidachi lei vezeil hacha kamidachi lei* — whichever direction you try to prove from *menudeh lashamayim*, he will push you away. It is genuinely unclear whether being rejected by heaven is more stringent or more lenient than being rejected by one's community.

This uncertainty has a striking implication: the *gemara* is suggesting that *menudeh labriyot* — being cast out by people — might actually carry harsher practical consequences than *menudeh lashamayim*. This is conceptually fascinating. If the practices of *niduy* are understood as expressions of one's relationship to society, then the fact that one's behavior was found unacceptable *by society* might translate into more severe societal practices than a rejection rooted in one's relationship to God. God's rejection carries its own consequences, but *niduy* as an institution is a human communal mechanism, and perhaps it is most fully activated when the breach is a human communal one.

For a *metzora*, a *baraita* teaches: *veyashav michutz le'ahalo* — he shall sit outside his tent — followed by the ruling that he is *ke'menudeh ve'chaver ve'assur betashmish hamittah*, forbidden in marital relations. The proof that *ahalo* refers to his wife is from the verse *shuvu lachem le'ohaleichem* — go back to your tents, said to Bnei Yisrael after Matan Torah — and the surrounding context of *al tigshu el ishah*, which establishes that "tent" is a euphemism for one's wife. So *shema minah*, the *metzora* is clearly forbidden in *tashmish hamittah*. 

One might then try to derive: *venifshot nami lemenudeh* — learn it out for a *menudeh* as well, since the *baraita* says the *metzora* is *ke'menudeh*. But Rav Huna the son of Rav Pinchas, citing Rav Yosef, resists: the *baraita* does not necessarily say the *metzora* is forbidden in *tashmish hamittah* because he is *like a menudeh*. Rather, he is forbidden in *tashmish hamittah* as a separate ruling, and separately he is compared to a *menudeh* regarding *other* practices — *bemilei achrayita*. So again, the proof is blocked.

What is worth noting here is that *tashmish hamittah* is the most private of all the practices on this list. It has nothing to do with one's public presentation, with how one appears in the marketplace, or with how one interacts with neighbors. It takes place entirely within the intimate domestic space. This complicates any clean framework that would divide *avel* practices into "expressions of mourning" on one hand and "expressions of social separation" on the other. After all, on *Shabbat* and *Yom Tov*, there is the rule of *devarim shebetzina noheig* — that the *avel* still observes the intimate, hidden practices of *aveilus*. *Tashmish hamittah* is precisely such a practice: totally hidden from society, yet still governed by the rules of *aveilus*. This does raise real questions about a purely sociological reading of these practices. There may be an additional dimension — the idea that certain physical pleasures are simply inappropriate for one in a state of grief, or in a state of *niduy*, regardless of whether they are visible to anyone.

### Shiluach Korbanos

An *avel* may not send his *korbanos* to be brought in the *Beis Hamikdash* on his behalf. The source is Rabbi Shimon's teaching that the *shelamim* offering is called *shelamim* because it is brought *bizman shehu shaleim* — when one is whole — and not *bizman shehu onen*, when one is an *onen*. Even extending this to the full period of *aveilus*, the idea fits the category of *tefillin* and *Torah* — affirmative expressions of religious engagement and completeness that are inappropriate for one in a state of acute loss.

For a *menudeh*, Rav Yosef again cites the case of Bnei Yisrael in the *midbar*: they were in a state of divine rejection yet apparently continued to bring *korbanos* throughout. Abaye again deflects: *dilma menudeh lashamayim shani dekil* — perhaps that is more lenient. And again the contradiction arises: the *gemara* previously seemed to suggest the opposite. *Speiki mesapka lei*, Rav Yosef remains uncertain, and whichever side he attempts to argue from, Abaye deflects him in the other direction.

What makes this case particularly striking is that *korbanos* — unlike shoes or marital relations — are obviously about one's relationship to God. One might have thought that *menudeh lashamayim* would be more constrained here than *menudeh labriyot*, since bringing an offering is an act directed toward heaven. Yet the *gemara* appears to entertain the possibility that even in this domain, *menudeh labriyot* is more severe — a truly counterintuitive suggestion. One way to understand this is that *korban* offering in the *Beis Hamikdash* was not merely a private, spiritual act; it was deeply communal and participatory. Being *meshale'ach korbanot* involves sending to a public, national religious space. In that sense, it belongs partly to the category of communal participation, which a *menudeh labriyot* is precisely cut off from.

For a *metzora*, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* interpreting a verse in Yechezkel (chapter 44): *ve-acharei taharaso*, after his purification, implying that before purification he may not bring. Rabbi Shimon reads the verse as establishing that *bizman sheraui levi'ah raui le-hakravah* — when he is permitted to enter the Temple precinct, he may bring; *bizman she-eino raui levi'ah eino raui le-hakravah* — when he cannot enter, he cannot bring. Rabbi Yehuda disputes this reading entirely, maintaining the verse refers only to a *kohen*'s inaugural offering. So this remains a *machloket* between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon, and the question of *shiluach korbanos* for a *metzora* is not definitively settled.

One might ask why the *gemara* derives *metzora* rules from Yechezkel at all, given the well-known difficulties with using Yechezkel as a halachic source. The *gemara* elsewhere addresses this: before Yechezkel made it explicit, we already had a *masorah* for this practice; Yechezkel's verse serves as confirmation rather than the sole derivation.

### Conceptual Framework

Stepping back across all these practices, one can observe a general pattern, even if it does not hold perfectly. The practices most clearly exclusive to the *avel* — *tefillin*, *Torah* study, *kefiyat hamittah* — concern the internal, affirmative, and joyful dimensions of religious life. The practices shared more broadly — disheveled appearance, shoes, perhaps bathing — concern external markers of separation from normal social existence. But the case of *tashmish hamittah* sits uncomfortably in either column. It is neither a public marker nor an affirmative religious expression; it is the most intimate and private of practices, and yet it appears on the shared side of the ledger.

The uncertainty that permeates the *gemara*'s analysis of *menudeh lashamayim* versus *menudeh labriyot* is itself deeply illuminating. The *gemara* cannot commit to a consistent view of whether rejection by heaven is stricter or more lenient than rejection by people, and it acknowledges this openly by noting that Rav Yosef is pushed away from either direction. The most coherent resolution is simply that the two dimensions — one's status before God and one's status within the community — operate on parallel tracks that do not map cleanly onto one another.

## Court Powers Sources

### Summons from Korach

With the list of practices complete, the *gemara* turns to a new topic: how does someone come to be placed in *niduy* in the first place? This requires establishing the procedural and legal foundations of rabbinic court authority — the power to summon, to censure, to excommunicate, and if necessary to compel through more forceful means. Rava opens with a question: *menalan de'meshadrinan shlicha de'vei dina u'mezamnan leih le'dina* — from where do we know that the court sends a messenger to issue a summons requiring a person to appear for judgment?

The source is the verse *vayishlach Moshe likro le'Datan vela'Aviram bnei Eliav* (Bamidbar 16:12): Moshe sent and called to Datan and Aviram. This is the foundational precedent for the summons procedure: just as Moshe dispatched a messenger to call the parties in the Korach dispute to appear, so too a *beis din* sends its official messenger to compel appearance.

### Summons Elements

The *gemara* now elaborates on the specific content of a proper summons, deriving each element from the Korach narrative. The summons must identify the judge or court before whom the defendant must appear: *likmi gavra raba* — appear before the great person — derived from the verse's continuation *lifnei Hashem*, which functions here as a reference to the judicial authority, since judges represent divine law. The summons must also identify the opposing litigant: *atah u'planya* — you and so-and-so — derived from *atah ve'heim ve'Aharon*, "you and they and Aharon." Thus a complete summons states: you are to appear before such-and-such a court, together with your opponent in this matter.

The *gemara* further derives that the summons must specify a time to appear: *dekhtiv machar* — "appear tomorrow" — and that a period is thus set. Each of these procedural requirements is rooted in the Moshe-Datan-Aviram episode, making that narrative a kind of template for rabbinic judicial procedure.

### Multiple Summons

If the defendant fails to appear at the first summons, the *gemara* asks: *zimna batar zimna* — from where do we know a second summons is issued rather than immediately imposing sanctions? The source is the verse in Yirmiyahu (46:17): *karu sham Paroh melech Mitzrayim sha'on he'evir hamo'ed* — they have called Pharaoh king of Egypt a noise; he has let the appointed time pass. Rashi reads *sham* here as *shamta* — the Aramaic term for *niduy* — suggesting that Pharaoh was eventually declared in excommunication for having let the time pass. The implication is that one missed court date is not sufficient; the defendant is called again, and only after repeated non-compliance does the court escalate to *shamta*.

### Contempt Grounds

The *gemara* now turns to contempt: *menalan de'i mispaker bishlicha de'vei dina* — from where do we know that if a person acts disparagingly toward the court's messenger there are consequences? The verb *mispaker* shares a root with *apikores*, and the connection is not coincidental. The *gemara* in *Sanhedrin* understands an *apikores* as someone who acts with contempt toward *talmidei chakhamim*. The principle is central: rabbinic authority depends on recognition. When someone treats the messenger of the court with contempt — dismissing not just the individual messenger but the authority he represents — this fundamentally undermines the court system. The *gemara* adds that the messenger who is treated this way may report it back: *de'ati ve'amar lo mit'chazi ke'lishna bisha* — when he comes back and tells us what was said, this does not constitute *lishna bisha*, prohibited speech. The proof: by the Moshe narrative, Moshe learned what Datan and Aviram said in response — meaning the messenger brought that report back. Implicit in the narrative is that this reporting was appropriate.

Such behavior — contemptuous dismissal of the court's messenger — constitutes what we would call contempt of court, a concept that captures precisely the idea that rejecting the messenger is rejecting the authority of the institution.

### Escalation Sanctions

From where do we know that the court then places the offender in *shamta*, a state of *niduy*? The verse *oru Meroz* from *Shirat Devorah* (Shoftim 5:23) is invoked: *oru* is read as cognate with *arur* — cursed — and Meroz is taken as the name of a person (or place, or according to some, a star) who was called to participate in the war against Sisera and refused. The parallel: one called to appear in court refuses or acts contemptuously; just as Meroz was cursed for not appearing for battle when summoned, so too the non-compliant defendant is put in *niduy*.

The *gemara* continues through the entire escalation framework. For *cherem* — the more complete form of excommunication — the verse *oru arur* provides the source, with the doubling of the curse language indicating a higher-level sanction following upon a failed *niduy*. The rules governing what *cherem* entails in practice are derived as well: *de'achil veshatei behadeih* — one may not eat or drink with the person in *cherem* — and *veka'ei bearba amot dideih* — one may not stand within his *arba amot*. The source for these is *oru arur yosheveiha* — cursed are those who dwell with him, meaning one may not be in close proximity with such a person.

The *gemara* further derives that the court announces publicly why the person is being placed in this state: *ki lo ba'u le'ezrat Hashem bagiborim* — because they did not come to the aid of God among the warriors — enumerating the offense before the community. Ulla adds the dramatic detail: *be'arba mei'a shipurei shamteih Barak leMeroz* — Barak excommunicated Meroz with four hundred *shofarot*. The *shofar* blast is thus the instrument by which *niduy* is proclaimed publicly — and with full communal fanfare, not merely a private administrative act. As for who Meroz was, there is a dispute: some say he was a great person who did not show up to fight; others say *kochava hava* — it was a star, since *min shamayim nilchamu hakochavim*, the stars fought from heaven, and this star refused.

### Cherem & Property

Having established the escalating sanctions of *niduy* and *cherem*, the *gemara* asks: *menalan demafkerinan nichsei* — from where do we know that the court may seize and declare ownerless the property of someone who refuses to comply? Here the model shifts from *Shirat Devorah* to the narrative of Ezra, which serves as the source for the principle of *hefker beis din hefker* — a court's declaration of ownerlessness is legally effective. The verse reads: *vechol asher lo yavo lishloshes hayamim ba'atzat hasarim vehazekenim yocharam kol rechusho vehu yibadel mikahal hagola* — whoever does not appear within three days at the counsel of the officers and elders, all his property shall be put in *cherem* and he shall be separated from the community of the exile (Ezra 10:8).

It is worth noting the overlap in vocabulary: *cherem* in the Torah can apply to property made taboo and destroyed, as in the case of Achan and *mo'el bacherem*, but also to property made off-limits to its owner and effectively made available to others — which is the sense operative here. The verse combining property *cherem* with communal separation is precisely the template for the court's dual power: to confiscate and to ostracize.

The passage from Nechemya then provides the further escalation: *va'ariv imam va'akalalem va'akeh mehem anashim va'emretem va'ashbi'em* — I fought with them, cursed them, struck some of them, pulled their hair, and made them swear an oath (Nechemya 13:25). The *gemara* reads this as a list of additional enforcement tools available to the court: verbal rebuke, physical force, hair-pulling, and oath imposition. It should be noted that this is qualitatively different from the earlier sanctions. *Niduy*, *cherem*, and property seizure are all forms of social and economic pressure. Physical compulsion is a different category entirely — a shift from communal mechanisms to direct bodily enforcement.

### Corporal Authority

The *gemara* continues: *menalan d'kaftinan* — from where do we know that if necessary a person can be bound and physically restrained? And from where do we know of *hardafah*? The verse in Ezra 7:26 provides: *hen le'maves hen le'shashu hen le'onash nechasin vela'asurin* — whether to death, whether to *shashu*, whether to punishment of property, or to imprisonment. The *gemara* asks: *mai le'shashu*? A chain of tradition — Adda Mari citing Nechemya bar Baruch citing Rav Chiya bar Avin citing Rav Yehuda — identifies *le'shashu* with *hardafah*. And then Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilas in the name of Rav clarifies: *hardafah* means *menadin le'altar* — the authority to impose *niduy* immediately, without the ordinary procedural delays, when circumstances demand it.

This verse from Ezra, incidentally, represents one of the final historical events recorded in *Nach* — and yet the community was still struggling with non-compliance in the matter of foreign wives.

The broader point is significant: we have moved from a focused discussion of *niduy* and *cherem* to a comprehensive account of rabbinic and judicial authority more generally. The *beis din* functions not only as a court in the narrow legal sense but as a comprehensive governing institution — religious authority, judicial authority, and enforcement authority combined. In a system where there is no separation between the rabbinic and the political, the court both declares the law and enforces it. The tools at its disposal range from social ostracism at one end to physical compulsion and capital authority at the other, each calibrated to the severity of the non-compliance and the needs of the moment. Emergency powers — *sha'ata tzricha lahem* — allow the court to act outside its ordinary procedural constraints when the situation demands it, a concept familiar from the discussions in *Sanhedrin* of *beis din makin ve'onshin shelo min hadin*.

## Niduy Timeline

### Niduy Timing

The *gemara* now addresses the practical timeline of *niduy* imposition. The rule regarding *hardafah* — immediate *niduy* — applies specifically to cases of *afkeiruta*, contempt. However, the general rule for civil matters is different: *maz'harin be-Sheni va-Chamishi ve-Sheni* — you warn the person on Monday, Thursday, and Monday before imposing any sanction. This Monday-Thursday-Monday sequence represents a formal warning period that precedes the first *niduy*, giving the recalcitrant party ample opportunity to comply before sanctions are applied. Only after this warning period passes without compliance does the court impose *niduy*, whereas contemptuous behavior toward the court or its messenger triggers *niduy* immediately.

### Escalation Sequence

Once *niduy* is imposed, the escalation sequence runs as follows: the initial period of *niduy* lasts thirty days. If the person still does not comply, a second thirty-day period is imposed. If compliance remains absent after sixty days total, the person is elevated to *cherem*, the more severe form of excommunication. The principle is a graduated one — increasing pressure at each stage while holding open the possibility of compliance and release.

### Butcher Case

A vivid illustration of these principles appears in the case of a certain butcher who acted with contempt — *de'ishtapkha* — toward Rabbi Tuvi bar Masna. Abaye and Rava convened a *beis din* and placed him in *niduy*. The butcher then went and made peace with the party with whom he had been in dispute — presumably resolving the underlying matter and asking forgiveness from Rabbi Tuvi bar Masna as well, though the text is not entirely explicit on this last point.

At this juncture Abaye asks: *heichi le'avid* — what should we do? The question has two sides. On one hand: *lishrei leih* — should we release him now? After all, he has complied and made peace. On the other hand: *lo chal shamta le'talsin yomin* — the *niduy* has not run for thirty days, and perhaps the full thirty-day period must be endured regardless of subsequent compliance, since the sanction represents a punishment for the contemptuous act, not merely a tool to extract compliance. On the other hand: *lo lishrei leih* — should we not release him? The problem is that the *rabbanim* need to leave; the *beis din* is about to disband. The concern is that once the court disperses, it may not be possible to reconstitute it, and then no one will be able to release the butcher.

Abaye turns to Rav Idi bar Avin: have you heard a ruling on this? Rav Idi bar Avin cites in the name of Rav Tachlifa bar Avin in the name of Shmuel: *tut asar ve'tut sharei* — the same blow of the *shofar* that bound him can release him. That is, the same formal act that imposed the *niduy* can, if the court sees fit, dissolve it. The court that imposed it has the authority to release it immediately.

### Early Release Debate

But immediately this ruling is qualified: *hani mili mamona* — that applies when the *niduy* was imposed in the context of a civil matter. *Aval afkeiruta* — but when the offense was contempt of court, perhaps the full thirty-day period must be completed regardless: *ad de'chayel shamta le'talsin yomin* — until the *niduy* has run its thirty days. Contempt is a graver offense, and perhaps it demands a fixed period of consequence that cannot be abbreviated simply because the person has since conformed. This question is left unresolved.

The *gemara* then draws an implicit inference from the entire episode: Abaye's urgency to act before the court dispersed suggests he holds that *hani bei talsa de'shamitu lo asu talsa achrinei ve'sharei leih* — the three who imposed the *niduy* cannot be replaced by a different three for purposes of releasing it. The same court that acted must act again to release. This inference sets up the next discussion.

## Niduy Authority

### Rav vs. Talmid

The *gemara* now articulates the hierarchy of *niduy* authority. *Menudeh le'rav menudeh le'talmid* — if a *rav* has placed someone in *niduy*, his students must observe that *niduy* and treat the person accordingly. *Menudeh le'talmid eino menudeh le'rav* — but if a *talmid* placed someone in *niduy*, the *rav* is not obligated to observe it. This asymmetry makes sense once one understands the context: these rules apply to personal *niduy*, *niduy l'chavodo*, where someone imposes *niduy* because his own honor or dignity was treated with contempt. In such a case, those below the person who imposed it must follow suit — the *talmid* defers to the *rav*'s exercise of his personal authority — but those above him, like the *rav*'s own *rebbe*, are not required to.

A similar principle applies geographically: *menudeh le'iro menudeh le'ir acheret* — if a city places someone in *niduy*, other cities must respect it. *Menudeh le'ir acheret eino menudeh le'iro* — but if another city placed him in *niduy*, his own city is not necessarily bound. *Menudeh le'nasi menudeh le'chol Yisrael* — if the *nasi* places someone in *niduy*, all of Israel follows it. *Menudeh le'chol Yisrael eino menudeh le'nasi* — but the reverse does not follow. The hierarchy flows in one direction: those subordinate to the authority that issued the *niduy* are bound; those above it need not be.

It is also worth noting what the *gemara* establishes about the practical content of *niduy*. The person in *niduy* may not be eaten or drunk with, and people may not remain within his *arba amot*. This is a serious form of social isolation — eating together being one of the most powerful expressions of communal belonging and intimacy. Yet *niduy* is not complete *cherem*: the *menudeh* may still work, hire, be hired, teach, and be taught. He is excluded from the intimate circles of community — including, according to the *Rambam*, being counted toward a *minyan* — but not from all interaction. *Cherem*, by contrast, severs all social and commercial ties entirely.

### Rabban Gamliel

Rabban Gamliel introduces a crucial ruling: *echad min hatalmidim shenidah u'mes chelko eino mufar* — if one of the students placed someone in *niduy* — meaning for personal reasons, because his own honor was violated — and then that student dies, his portion of the *niduy* is not automatically released. The person in *niduy* is not freed simply by the death of the one who imposed it. This ruling carries several important implications.

### Three Inferences

The *gemara* derives *shalosh* — three inferences — from Rabban Gamliel's ruling. First, *shema mina talmid shenidah l'chavodo niduyo niduy*: this confirms the concept of personal *niduy*, *niduy l'chavodo* — a student may impose *niduy* on someone who treated his honor with contempt, and such a *niduy* is halachically valid even without a formal institutional mandate. Second, *kol echad ve'echad mefer chelko*: each party to the original *niduy* releases his own portion, suggesting that each member of the *beis din* must participate in the release for it to take effect. Third, *shema mina hanei bei tlasa de'shamisu lo asu tlasa achrina ve'sharei leih*: the original three who imposed the *niduy* cannot simply be replaced by a different three for the release — the original court must be the releasing authority.

### Personal vs. Institution…

This third inference is particularly important for distinguishing between personal *niduy* and institutional *niduy*. A personal *niduy*, *l'chavodo*, is tied to the individual who imposed it. Because it is rooted in a personal violation, the person who was wronged must personally participate in the release. An institutional *niduy* — imposed because of a broader violation of communal norms, court procedures, or religious standards — belongs to the community as a whole and is not tied to any particular individual. In that case, presumably any competent authority could release it.

### Court Release Dispute

Amemar rules as the practical *halacha*: *hanei bei tlasa de'shamisu asu bei tlasa achrina ve'sharei leih* — a different court of three may indeed release a *niduy* imposed by the original three. The practical necessity is obvious: if only the original three could ever release, then the *menudeh* could be permanently trapped if the original court disbanded or its members died. Rav Ashi challenges Amemar from Rabban Gamliel's ruling: *mai lav eino mufar klal* — does it not mean that after the student's death the *niduy* simply cannot be released at all?

### Death & Release

The *gemara* resolves this: *lo* — the ruling does not mean the *niduy* can never be released. Rather, it means release is not automatic upon death. One does not go free simply because the person who imposed the *niduy* has died. Instead, *ad d'asu bei tlasa achrina ve'sharei leih* — a new court of three must be constituted and must formally release him. This is the resolution: death does not dissolve the *niduy*, but another properly constituted body of three can authorize release. The death of the original imposing party simply means that release requires a new formal process — it does not mean perpetual imprisonment in one's status.

This resolution is necessary on both sides: without it, the *menudeh* would indeed be permanently trapped, which is untenable; but simply granting automatic release upon death would empty the institution of its force. The middle path — continued *niduy* after death, but releasable through a new three-person body — maintains both the seriousness of the sanction and the possibility of eventual restoration.
