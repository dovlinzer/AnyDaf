# Shevuot 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inedible vs. Non-Kosher

### Setting Up Contradiction

The *gemara* on 23b opens with a discussion of the oath "*shevua shelo ochal*" — an oath not to eat. The *mishnah* rules that if a person takes such an oath and then consumes food that is not fit for eating (*ochalin she'ein reuyin la'achilah*) or drink that is not potable (*mashkin she'ein reuyin lishtiyah*), he is *patur* — exempt from liability. Yet later in the same *mishnah*, the ruling shifts: if someone takes an oath not to eat and then eats *nevelos u'treifos*, carcasses and non-kosher animals, *shekatzim u'remasim*, creeping things and the like — he is *chayav*, liable.

The *gemara* flags this as an internal contradiction: *hagufa kashya*. The opening clause appears to treat halachically unfit food as equivalent to physically unfit food, exempting a person who eats it; yet the closing clause imposes liability for eating non-kosher food, which is also halachically unfit for consumption. *Mai sh'na reisha u'mai sh'na seifa* — what distinguishes the first case from the last?

The *gemara* offers an initial resolution: *reisha bistam, seifa bimfaresh* — the opening clause speaks of an unspecified oath ("I will not eat"), while the closing clause speaks of one who was explicit ("I will not eat non-kosher food"). When a person issues a generic oath not to eat, non-kosher food falls outside its scope because it is not considered fit for eating; but when he explicitly names non-kosher food, the oath takes effect directly on it. Of course, this reading is not explicit in the *mishnah* itself, and it is complicated by the position of *Reish Lakish*, who holds that even an explicit oath cannot take effect on something already forbidden — the principle of *ein issur chal al issur*.

### Rava's Answer: Dirt

Rava's resolution, which will emerge more fully later, is that the *mishnah*'s exemption in the opening clause refers to something that is physically inedible, like dirt (*afar*). When the *mishnah* says a person is *patur* for eating something "not fit for eating," it is not referring to *treif* food — which, though halachically forbidden, is still entirely edible in a physical sense — but to substances that no person would eat under ordinary circumstances, such as dirt or the like. This distinction will become central to the *gemara*'s ultimate resolution.

### Oath on Top of Torah

Even granting that the closing clause of the *mishnah* involves an explicit oath concerning non-kosher food, the *gemara* presses further: *amai? mushba ve'omed me'Har Sinai hu* — why should such an oath take effect at all? The person is already bound from Sinai not to eat *treif* food. This is the problem of *mushba ve'omed*: a pre-existing oath or prohibition blocks a subsequent one from taking hold. Since every Jew is already, in effect, sworn at Sinai to observe the dietary laws, a personal oath to abstain from non-kosher food should be redundant and legally inoperative — it cannot take effect on top of an existing prohibition.

## Issur Kolel Solution

### Broader Prohibition Works

*Rav*, *Shmuel*, and *Rabbi Yochanan* all offer the same solution to the *mushba ve'omed* problem: *bekolel devarim hamutorim im ha'asurim* — the oath encompasses both permitted and forbidden foods simultaneously. When a person swears that he will eat neither kosher food nor non-kosher food, the oath takes effect on the kosher food, which is otherwise permitted. Since the oath has a broader scope — *issur kolel* — it "grasps" the permitted food and, by extension, pulls the already-forbidden food into its orbit as well.

This is worth pausing over. The underlying concern is that a new prohibition cannot ordinarily take effect on something already prohibited. But *issur kolel* supplies an exception: when the new prohibition is wider in scope — encompassing objects not previously forbidden — it takes effect on those objects, and simultaneously reinforces its hold on the object that was already forbidden. The mechanism is that the oath's broader reach gives it independent footing.

It is worth noting a tension here with what has been discussed in preceding *dapim*: when a person says "I will not eat X and Y," we generally treat that as two separate *shevuos*, requiring two separate *korbanos* for violation. If that is so, one might have expected an oath about kosher food and an oath about non-kosher food to be evaluated independently — in which case the kosher-food oath takes effect, but the non-kosher-food oath does not, without any *kolel* mechanism. Presumably the *gemara* here envisions a case where all the food stands before the person and the oath sweeps over everything at once as a single undivided declaration — though this tension deserves attention.

### Reish Lakish Disagrees

*Reish Lakish* disagrees. He holds that *issur kolel* does not solve the problem when the second prohibition is a *shevua* — a self-imposed oath. According to *Reish Lakish*, the only way an oath can take effect on non-kosher food is by targeting something that is presently permitted by Torah law. He therefore offers a different mechanism: *ee bimfareish chatzi shiur v'aliba d'Rabbanan* — the oath explicitly forbids even less than a *kezayis*. Since the Torah only prohibits consuming a *kezayis* of forbidden food, a lesser amount is technically permitted on a biblical level. An oath forbidding even a *chatzi shiur* — less than the full prescribed amount — takes effect precisely because it addresses something the Torah has not prohibited. Alternatively, according to *Rabbi Akiva*, who holds that an oath forbids even the smallest quantity, the problem is solved in a different way — but *Reish Lakish*'s primary solution works by targeting the gap below the biblical threshold.

Here a sharp and elegant inner logic emerges: *Reish Lakish* himself is the one who holds that a *chatzi shiur* is not biblically forbidden. His own solution thus perfectly coheres with his own position. *Tosafos* notes this and raises the converse: the reason *Rabbi Yochanan* does not avail himself of *Reish Lakish*'s *chatzi shiur* solution may be that *Rabbi Yochanan* holds a *chatzi shiur* is itself biblically prohibited — in which case an oath targeting it still runs into the *mushba ve'omed* problem. The *gemara* does not state this explicitly, but *Tosafos* treats it as the elegant underlying logic. *Tosafos* further raises the question of how firmly something must be biblically prohibited before a *shevua* cannot take effect on top of it — whether a prohibition derived from a positive commandment, or the attenuated kind inferred from a subtle textual hint, suffices to block a *shevua*.

### Self vs. Automatic

The *gemara* pursues the question of why *Reish Lakish* refuses to accept *Rabbi Yochanan*'s *issur kolel* solution. The *gemara* puts the question directly: *ki amrinan issur kolel* — when do we apply the principle of *issur kolel*? The answer *Reish Lakish* would give is: *b'issur haba me'elav* — only when the broader prohibition arrives on its own, without being self-generated. If the second prohibition is externally and automatically imposed by the Torah, then when it encompasses both permitted and forbidden things, it takes hold of the forbidden things too. But if the second prohibition is generated by the person himself through a *shevua*, it cannot achieve this effect.

### Already Sworn Status

The distinction *Reish Lakish* draws between automatic and self-imposed prohibitions deserves careful examination. One way to understand it is that he views *ein shevua chala al shevua* — the rule that one oath cannot take effect on top of another — as a distinct principle, separate from the general rule of *ein issur chal al issur*. Even if *issur kolel* can override the general *ein issur chal al issur* when the second prohibition is broad enough, the specific rule governing oaths operates differently: a *shevua* is categorically incapable of taking hold of something already bound by a prior oath, including the foundational oath of Sinai.

*Tosafos* adds a different dimension: it is not merely that the person created the second prohibition, but that the *shevua* is inherently subjective — it applies only to the individual who utters it. Compare this to *Yom Kippur*, which imposes an objective, world-facing prohibition on all food. Because *Yom Kippur* objectively changes the forbidden status of the apple, it simultaneously changes the forbidden status of the *treif* hamburger. But a *shevua* is a private, personal declaration. Its effect on the apple does not carry the same objective weight, and therefore it lacks the power to add another layer of prohibition to something already objectively forbidden. The example clarifies this: when *Yom Kippur* arrives, all food — kosher and non-kosher alike — becomes forbidden. The *treif* hamburger is now doubly forbidden, once as *treif* and again as *Yom Kippur* food. Everyone agrees that *Yom Kippur*, as an automatic and objective prohibition, achieves this. But according to *Reish Lakish*, a personal self-generated oath cannot replicate that result, because its subjective nature renders it too weak to impose an additional layer of prohibition on something already objectively off-limits.

The question is also raised whether the same reasoning applies to a *neder* followed by a *shevua* on the same item — and presumably, yes, once something is already forbidden to the person, even by a prior self-generated prohibition, the subsequent *shevua* cannot take hold.

The *gemara* now notes a further elegance: this explains why *Rabbi Shimon* exempts a person who violates such an oath. According to *Reish Lakish*, the *tanna kamma*'s ruling that one is liable can only be explained by the *chatzi shiur* mechanism — the oath took effect specifically on a quantity below the biblical threshold. *Rabbi Shimon* rules that even a *chatzi shiur* carries lashes (*kol shehu lemakot*), meaning that even less than a *kezayis* was already prohibited. In that case, there was no gap for the oath to fill, and so the oath cannot take effect at all — explaining *Rabbi Shimon*'s exemption perfectly.

According to *Rabbi Yochanan*, by contrast, the reason the *tanna kamma* rules one is liable is *issur kolel* — the broader oath sweeps in the non-kosher food alongside the kosher. *Rabbi Shimon* then rejects this reasoning because he rejects *issur kolel* entirely. The *gemara* cites the *baraisa*: *Rabbi Shimon omer ha-ochel neveila be-Yom HaKippurim patur* — one who eats a carcass on *Yom Kippur* is exempt from the *Yom Kippur* sin offering. The *Yom Kippur* prohibition, though broader in scope, cannot take effect on something already forbidden. *Rabbi Shimon* denies *issur kolel* root and branch, with the result — as one student correctly observed — that many of the *gemara*'s famous exercises in stacking multiple violations onto a single act would simply not apply according to his view.

## Lav v'Hein Requirement

### Reversible Oaths

The *gemara* now introduces a requirement for an oath that has not yet been explicitly presented in this tractate, though the *gemara* here takes it largely for granted: the principle of *lav v'hein* — an oath must be capable of being taken in both directions. This is derived from the *pasuk* (*Vayikra* 5:4) that speaks of an oath *l'hareia o l'heitiv* — to do harm or to do good — which the *gemara* understands to mean that a binding oath must be reversible: one must be able to swear to do the thing or to refrain from doing it. If the reverse formulation would itself be invalid — for instance, an oath to commit a transgression (*shevua l'vatel et hamitzva*) — then the original oath also cannot take effect.

This requirement creates a new problem in our discussion. If a person swears not to eat non-kosher food, one might ask: can he validly swear to eat non-kosher food? Surely not — that would be a *shevua* to perform a *mitzvah*-violation. Without the valid reverse, the original oath cannot take effect either.

### Half-Measure Solution

According to *Reish Lakish*, this is not a problem. Since his mechanism involves the *chatzi shiur* — forbidding less than the minimum biblical amount — the reverse is entirely coherent. One can validly swear to eat less than a *kezayis* of non-kosher food, since that quantity is not biblically prohibited. Thus the oath forbidding even that amount satisfies the *lav v'hein* requirement: both directions of the oath are theoretically valid.

### Generic Oath Solution

According to *Rabbi Yochanan*, whose mechanism is *issur kolel*, the *lav* direction — swearing not to eat — is entirely valid: the broader oath not to eat anything takes effect on both kosher and non-kosher food. But what of the *hein* — the positive direction? Swearing that one *will* eat non-kosher food is a *shevua* to transgress, which is invalid. The *gemara* asks: *ela hein heichi mishkachat lah* — where do you find the *hein*?

The answer, which the *gemara* now articulates, resolves the difficulty cleanly. The *gemara* invokes *Rava*'s core ruling: *shevua shelo ochel v'achal afar patur* — if one swears not to eat and then eats dirt, he is exempt. What this teaches is that *treif* food is not, in fact, classified as inedible. The original assumption — that a generic oath not to eat exempts one from eating non-kosher food because it is "unfit" — was incorrect. *Treif* food is entirely edible in the physical sense; only something like dirt is genuinely inedible. Therefore, a generic oath not to eat — without any specification — encompasses *treif* food automatically. It is not necessary to name non-kosher food explicitly.

### Back to Rava's Answer

This resolves the *lav v'hein* difficulty as well. When a person says simply "I will not eat today," the oath encompasses all food — kosher and non-kosher alike. *Treif* food is considered edible; dirt is not. The *issur kolel* operates naturally: since the oath takes effect on the apple, it takes effect on the hamburger as well. And the *lav v'hein* requirement is satisfied: the reverse oath — "I will eat today" — is perfectly valid, because it covers all food, and if the person swears to eat today and eats *treif* food, he has fulfilled his oath. There is no problem of swearing to commit a transgression, because the oath is not directed specifically at the non-kosher food; it is a generic oath about eating in general.

By contrast, if a person were to say explicitly, "I swear not to eat non-kosher food," the reverse would be "I swear to eat non-kosher food" — which is invalid, and so the oath would fail the *lav v'hein* test entirely.

This also dissolves the earlier need to suppose that the *mishnah*'s closing clause involves an explicit naming of non-kosher food. The person simply says a generic oath not to eat, and since *treif* food is physically edible, it is included. The *issur kolel* mechanism then accounts for how this oath takes effect on top of the pre-existing Torah prohibition — the oath covers the apple, and because it is broader, it also covers the hamburger.

*Rav Mari* adds that this conclusion is also implied by the *mishnah* itself in a different case: if a man declares that his wife will be forbidden to him *konam*-style if he has eaten anything today (*im achalti hayom*), and it emerges that he had earlier eaten non-kosher food — his wife is forbidden. This shows that eating non-kosher food counts as eating. The *gemara*, however, qualifies this: *hatam keivan d'mei-ikara achal* — in that case, the man had already eaten the non-kosher food before the declaration. Having already eaten it, he has demonstrated through his actions (*achshavei*) that the food is edible to him. This is the principle of *achshavei*: a person's act of consumption can elevate an item's classification into the category of food, at least for purposes of a vow or oath. The *gemara* therefore cautions that this case cannot serve as proof for ours — where the oath precedes any act of eating. Before a person has eaten *treif* food, it may still be considered outside the category of edible things as far as a generic oath is concerned. Nevertheless, the *gemara* maintains its ruling: *treif* food is considered edible even prospectively, the only true exclusion being something like dirt.

## Kolel vs. Mosif

### Rava's Mechanism

*Rava* now asks a foundational question: *mai ta'ama d'man d'is lei issur kolel* — what is the underlying rationale for *issur kolel*? Why should an oath or prohibition that takes effect on a permitted object simultaneously reinforce its hold on an already-forbidden object? His answer: *midi de'hava a'issur mosif* — it works just like *issur mosif*. The mechanism of *issur kolel* is analogous to — and can be understood through — the already-established mechanism of *issur mosif*.

*Rava* continues: the one who exempts (*man d'patar*) — i.e., *Rabbi Shimon*, who rejects *issur kolel* — would say that *issur mosif* works when dealing with a single object (*b'chada chaticha*), but not when dealing with two different objects (*b'shtei chatichos*). The distinction is critical.

### Sister-to-Wife Example

The clearest illustration is the case of a man's sister who subsequently marries another man. Call the man *Reuven* and the sister *Leah*. Before the marriage, *Leah* is forbidden to *Reuven* as a sister. When *Leah* then marries *Mordechai*, a new prohibition takes effect — the prohibition of *eshet ish*, a married woman. Does *Reuven* now violate two prohibitions if he has relations with her: both the prohibition of a sister and the prohibition of a married woman?

This is *issur mosif* — and everyone agrees it works. The marriage prohibition is not an *issur kolel* in the technical sense; it does not expand the number of *objects* that are forbidden. Rather, it changes the status of *Leah herself*. Before the marriage, she was forbidden to one person (*Reuven*) as a sister. After the marriage, she is forbidden to the entire world as a married woman. Her own status has been transformed, and that transformation affects even *Reuven*, to whom she was already forbidden. The new prohibition took hold of *Leah* as an object — changing what she is — and because her status changed globally, it changed vis-à-vis *Reuven* as well.

### What is Kolel

*Issur kolel*, by contrast, works differently. Here the new prohibition does not change the status of the forbidden object itself. Instead, it expands the range of objects or actions that are forbidden. The paradigm case: *Yom Kippur* arrives. Before *Yom Kippur*, the apple was permitted and the *treif* hamburger was forbidden. *Yom Kippur* makes the apple forbidden. *Yom Kippur* is now broader (*kolel*) — it encompasses the apple, which was previously permitted. And because it encompasses a new set of things, it takes hold simultaneously on the hamburger, adding a second layer of prohibition there.

The key point is that the hamburger's own status has not changed. *Issur kolel* does not transform what the hamburger *is*; it simply means that the new prohibition, by sweeping in more objects, also re-prohibits the hamburger. This is a weaker mechanism than *issur mosif*, which changes the object's own status — and this is why *Rabbi Shimon* accepts *issur mosif* but rejects *issur kolel*: he holds that merely adding more forbidden objects does not supply sufficient grounds to stack a second prohibition on what was already forbidden.

### What is Mosif

*Issur mosif* thus has a precise and distinct definition: it changes the status of a single object or person, expanding the scope of who is forbidden access to that object, or the ways in which the object is itself prohibited. The *notar* example — leftover sacrificial matter — illustrates this well with an object rather than a person. The *chelev* (forbidden fat) of a sacrifice that has become *notar* (leftover past its permitted time) now cannot even be placed on the *mizbe'ach* — something that was previously possible with that fat. The object's own prohibited status has expanded. That is *mosif*.

When a person becomes *tamei* (ritually impure), by contrast, the *chelev* itself has not changed. What has changed is the person's relationship to an entire world of *korbanos*: all sacrificial food is now forbidden to him. Because more objects are now forbidden to him, the prohibition adds another layer onto the *chelev* as well. That is *kolel* — expanding the universe of forbidden things, not transforming the status of the object itself.

Similarly, when *Yom Kippur* arrives, the *chelev* has not become more forbidden on its own terms. But the *tamei* person's world of forbidden food has expanded enormously — all food is now prohibited — and that expansion generates yet another layer of prohibition on the *chelev*. Another *kolel*.

The test question: when an animal is *makdish*-ed — consecrated as a *korban* — does that represent *kolel* or *mosif* with respect to the *chelev* already inside the animal? The consecration does not expand the *chelev*'s own prohibition; rather, it makes new objects — the meat of the animal — forbidden, thus expanding the number of forbidden objects. That is *kolel* with respect to the *chelev*.

### Rabbi Shimon Rejects

*Rabbi Shimon* rejects *issur kolel* entirely. The *gemara* cites the *baraisa*: *Rabbi Shimon omer ha-ochel neveila be-Yom HaKippurim patur* — one who eats a carcass (*neveila*) on *Yom Kippur* is not liable for the *Yom Kippur* prohibition. Precisely because the *neveila* was already forbidden before *Yom Kippur*, the *Yom Kippur* prohibition — even though it is broader in scope — cannot take effect on it a second time. *Rabbi Shimon* holds that the *kolel* mechanism, no matter how broad the encompassing prohibition, cannot stack a new *issur* onto what was already prohibited.

The consequence of *Rabbi Shimon*'s position is wide-ranging. The famous *gemara* constructions in which a single act generates multiple *chatat* obligations depend on both *issur mosif* and *issur kolel*. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, cases built on *issur kolel* collapse: you cannot get a second liability for *Yom Kippur* eating if the food was already *treif*. *Issur mosif*, by contrast, *Rabbi Shimon* does accept — because there the object itself genuinely changes status, which is a qualitatively different kind of stacking.

## Five-Prohibition Meat

### The Setup

The *gemara* now turns to a dramatic illustration of these principles. *Yesh ochel achila achas ve'chayav aleha arba chatat ve'asham echad* — there is a case where one act of eating a single piece of food generates liability for four *chatat* sin-offerings and one *asham* guilt-offering. The case involves a person who is *tamei* (ritually impure), who eats *chelev* (forbidden fat) that had the additional status of *notar* (leftover sacrifice), that came from a *mukdashim* (sanctified animal), on *Yom Kippur*.

### Forbidden Fat

The first prohibition is *chelev* itself. From the moment an animal is born, the forbidden fat within it carries an inherent prohibition. This is the baseline *issur*, independent of anything the person does or any subsequent status the animal acquires.

### Made into Sacrifice

The second prohibition arises when the animal is consecrated — made a *korban*. The consecration creates an *issur hekdesh* on the entire animal. With respect to the *chelev* specifically, this is *issur kolel*: the consecration does not change the *chelev*'s own forbidden status, but it makes additional objects (the meat of the animal) forbidden. Because the scope of the new prohibition encompasses more objects — and the *chelev* is among them — a second layer of prohibition attaches to the *chelev* as well.

### Leftover Status

The third prohibition arises from the *notar* status: the sacrifice was not consumed or burned in its permitted time, and the remaining fat is now *notar*. This changes the status of the *chelev* itself — it was previously capable of being burned on the *mizbe'ach*, and now even that is foreclosed. Because the *notar* status expands the ways in which the *chelev* is prohibited — no longer merely forbidden to human consumption, but now forbidden even to the altar — this is *issur mosif*. The object's own status has been altered, and a new prohibition layers onto it as a result.

### Impurity Adds

The fourth prohibition comes from the person's *tumah*. When the person becomes *tamei*, the *chelev* itself does not change. But the *tamei* person's forbidden world expands enormously: all *korbanot* and sanctified foods are now forbidden to him. Because the *tumah* prohibition encompasses the *chelev* alongside a whole range of other sanctified items, this is *issur kolel* — expanding the universe of prohibited objects for that person, thereby adding another layer onto the already-forbidden *chelev*.

### Yom Kippur Layer

The fifth prohibition is *Yom Kippur*. When *Yom Kippur* arrives, the prohibition expands even further: now all food — not just sanctified foods — is forbidden. The *chelev* was already forbidden on multiple grounds, but *Yom Kippur* adds yet another layer via *issur kolel*, because the *Yom Kippur* prohibition encompasses even the apple and the bread that were previously permitted.

The offerings thus generated are: four *chatat* offerings (for the *chelev*, the *notar*, the *tamei* person eating sanctified food, and *Yom Kippur*) and one *asham* for the *me'ilah* — the violation of sanctified property.

### Shabbat Carrying

*Rabbi Meir* proposes a sixth prohibition: if the person carried the *chelev* in a public domain on *Shabbat* while eating it — *hotzio chayav* — he adds a *Shabbat* violation to the count. The *chachamim* respond: *eino min hashem* — carrying is not part of the act of eating; it is entirely incidental to the consumption and therefore cannot be bundled into this framework. *Rashi* further notes that *Rabbi Meir* does not raise the same point for *Yom Kippur* because he understands there to be no prohibition of carrying on *Yom Kippur* as such.

## Why Not Oaths?

### Oath Objection

If *issur kolel* works — and the *gemara* has been building toward affirming that it does, at least according to *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rava* — one might ask why an oath (*shevua*) was not added to the five-prohibition example. Before *Yom Kippur*, the person could have declared a *shevua* not to eat figs and *chelev*. Since the oath takes effect on the figs (which are kosher and not otherwise forbidden), and since it is a broader oath — *issur kolel* — it would also take effect on the *chelev*. One act of eating the *chelev* should then generate an additional *chatat* for the *shevua*, bringing the total to five *chatat* offerings and one *asham*. So why does the *mishnah* stop at four?

The *gemara* answers — and does so several times, each with a slightly different rationale — that the *shevua* is simply not within the scope of the discussion. In each case, the *gemara* affirms: *ein hachi nami* — the *halacha* would indeed hold true, and an oath taken in this configuration would add another layer of prohibition via *issur kolel*. The exclusion is purely a matter of which types of prohibitions the *tanna* chose to enumerate.

### Variable Offering

The first answer: *ki tanya korban kavua* — the *mishnah* was discussing only prohibitions that carry a fixed-value *korban*. All four *chatat* offerings enumerated are fixed in value. A *shevua*, by contrast, generates a *korban oleh ve'yored* — a variable-value offering whose cost depends on the financial means of the violator. The *tanna* simply chose not to include prohibitions of that type in the discussion.

The *gemara* challenges this: *ve'harei tamei she'achal es ha'hekdesh de'korban oleh ve'yored hu* — the *korban* for a *tamei* person who ate sanctified food is itself a variable-value offering. The *gemara* responds: *bi'nasi rebbi eliezer hi* — the case in the *mishnah* involves a *nasi* (king), for whom *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that the *korban* in this case is a fixed *sa'ir* (goat), making it a fixed-value offering after all.

### No Minimum Amount

*Rabbi Ashi* offers a different answer: *ki tanya midi de'chayil ki'shiur* — the *mishnah* discusses only prohibitions that take effect relative to a minimum prescribed amount (*shiur*). A *shevua*, by contrast, can take effect even on less than the minimum, since one can formulate an oath in terms of *mashu* — any amount whatsoever. The *tanna* did not wish to include prohibitions with this characteristic in the enumeration.

The *gemara* again challenges: *ve'harei hekdesh* — the violation of *hekdesh* (*me'ilah*) also does not require a *kezayis*; all one needs is a *perutah*'s worth of benefit. The *gemara* responds: even so, *me'ilah* does have a minimum — a *perutah* — and thus it still falls within the category of prohibitions with a *shiur*. A *shevua* can be formulated with literally no minimum, which places it in a categorically different class.

### No Kares Requirement

*Rav Zeira* (cited by *Rav Ashi*) offers yet another answer: *ki tanya zedono kares* — the *mishnah* discusses only prohibitions whose willful violation carries the penalty of *kares*, divine excision. All four *chatat* violations listed carry *kares* for deliberate transgression. A *shevua* does not: its willful violation incurs a standard penalty and a *korban*, but not *kares*. Therefore the *tanna* excluded it.

The *gemara* challenges: *ve'harei asham de'zedono lav* — the *asham* for *me'ilah* does not carry *kares* for willful violation; it is merely a standard negative prohibition. How can *kares* be the criterion if *me'ilah* is already in the list without it? The *gemara* responds: *be'chataos ka'amina* — the *kares* criterion applies specifically to the *chatat* offerings in the list. Within the category of *chataot*, only standard ones backed by *kares* were considered. The *asham* is a separate category and is not bound by that criterion.

### Non-Physical Acts

*Ravina* offers the final answer: *ki tanya midi de'chaila a'midi de'var achila hu* — the *mishnah* discusses prohibitions that take effect on physical food objects. A *shevua*, by contrast, can take effect on non-physical actions — one can swear to sleep or not to sleep, to stand or not to stand. Its scope extends beyond the domain of physical objects. The *tanna* did not wish to include a category of prohibition so untethered from the object-based framework of the discussion.

The *gemara* challenges: *hekdesh de'chayil nami a'etzim va'avanim* — *hekdesh* can also take effect on wood and stones, things that are not food. The *gemara* responds: that is true, but when we say *hekdesh* appears in our list, we are referring to its characteristic mode of operation in relation to physical consumables. The *shevua*, however, characteristically operates even on purely non-physical acts — *kegon she'ishan ve'shelo yishan* — such as swearing to take a nap or not to take a nap. That distinguishing characteristic of the *shevua* is what placed it outside the scope of the *mishnah*'s enumeration.

### Oath Affirmed

In every case, the *gemara*'s refrain is *ein hachi nami*: yes, the *halacha* is true. Had a *shevua* been included, it would indeed have added an additional layer of prohibition via *issur kolel*. Even though a *shevua* is self-generated — a fact that led *Reish Lakish* to reject *issur kolel* for *shevuos* — the accepted position (that of *Rabbi Yochanan*, *Rava*, and the *tanna kamma*) is that a self-imposed oath of broader scope does follow the *issur kolel* mechanism and can take effect on top of a pre-existing prohibition. The *mishnah*'s silence about *shevuos* in the five-prohibition example reflects only the *tanna*'s choice of scope, not a rejection of the principle.
