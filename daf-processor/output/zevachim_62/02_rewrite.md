# Zevachim 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Locating the Altar

### Question: Locating Mizbe…

The *gemara* on 62b opens with a question that follows naturally from a discussion a few lines earlier, where the *gemara* had been addressing how the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* knew how to construct the Second *Beit Hamikdash* and in what respects they modified the *mizbe'ach* from the one built by Moshe. The *gemara* now narrows its focus: *bishlama bayit minkar tzurato* — it is understandable how the *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* knew where to locate the structure of the *Beit Hamikdash* itself, since the foundations of the first *Beit Hamikdash* were still visible. *Ela mizbe'ach mena yad'ei* — but how did they know where to place the *mizbe'ach*? The *mizbe'ach* itself was gone, and there was apparently nothing obvious to mark its precise location.

### R. Elazar: Michael's Vis…

Rabbi Elazar answers: *ra'u mizbe'ach banuy u-Michael haSar haGadol omeid u-makriv alav* — they had some form of prophetic vision in which they saw the *mizbe'ach* already built, and Michael, the great angelic *sar*, standing there and offering a sacrifice upon it. This is a striking passage. The image of Michael as *sar melitz* — the angelic advocate — bringing *korbanot* invites the question of what kind of offering an angel would bring, and to what end. One natural inference is that these were *olot*, which are not brought for sin, consistent with Michael's role as *melitz yosher*. More broadly, the vision suggests that when the earthly *Beit Hamikdash shel mata* does not exist, some sort of spiritual *Beit Hamikdash shel ma'ala* continues to operate at the level of the *malachim*, and the *Maharsha* connects this to the well-known idea that the heavenly Jerusalem is directly above its earthly counterpart, as discussed in *Taanit*. When *Yisrael* returns and reconstructs the *Beit Hamikdash shel mata*, they fill in the gap below. The *Anshei Knesset HaGedolah* were shown the heavenly *mizbe'ach* as a kind of template — a divine blueprint — so they would know exactly where to build on earth. The analogy is evocative: what could not be recovered archaeologically was instead revealed spiritually.

This also bears on the relationship between *ruach hakodesh* and the act of building the *Beit Hamikdash*. One recalls that Moshe himself, when confronted with the construction of the *menorah*, did not know precisely how it should be made, and the Torah says *kazeh* — God showed him the form. Something similar seems to be at work here: when human understanding reaches its limit, divine assistance bridges the gap.

### R. Yitzchak: Ashes of Yi…

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha offers a second answer: *afro shel Yitzchak hu she-munach b'oto makom* — the ashes of Yitzchak are deposited in that very spot. This opinion draws on the imagery of the *Akeida* and the *olah*, connecting the *mizbe'ach* in Jerusalem to the binding of Yitzchak on that same site. The precise nature of the ashes is worth noting: the *efer* mentioned here is presumably from the ram that was sacrificed in Yitzchak's place, though certain *midrashim* develop this idea further, suggesting — in ways that may reflect later martyrological contexts — that Yitzchak himself was in some sense sacrificed and then revived. The ashes, whatever their precise nature, served as a physical marker — a trace of the primordial sacrifice that identified the exact location of the future *mizbe'ach*.

### R. Shmuel: Smell Distinc…

Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani provides a third answer, which operates on an entirely different register: *mi-kol ha-bayit kulo herichu rei'ach ketoret, mi-sham herichu rei'ach evarim* — throughout the entire area of the *Beit Hamikdash*, which was recognizable from its foundations, the air smelled of *ketoret*, the incense. But from that one spot, the smell was different — it was the smell of *evarim*, of burnt animal limbs. The location of the *mizbe'ach* was thus distinguished by a distinct olfactory signature, the residual smell of centuries of animal sacrifice, as against the pervasive fragrance of *ketoret* that characterized the rest of the *chatzer*. This answer, like the others, points to a form of knowledge unavailable through ordinary architectural or archaeological investigation — whether visionary, physical, or sensory, the location had to be revealed.

### Three Prophets from Exile

Rabba bar bar Chana, citing Rabbi Yochanan, introduces a complementary tradition: *sheloshah nevi'im alu imahem min ha-golah* — three prophets came up with the people from the exile to rebuild the *Beit Hamikdash Sheni*. The first testified to them about the *mizbe'ach* — that is, regarding its permitted dimensions, namely that the *mizbe'ach* could be built as large as sixty *amot*, larger than either Moshe's or Shlomo's. The second testified about the *mekom ha-mizbe'ach* — where the *mizbe'ach* should be located, which stands as a fourth answer to the question with which the *gemara* opened. The third testified that sacrifices may be offered even when there is no *bayit* — *makrivin af al pi she-ein bayit*. This third ruling, as Rashi notes, is connected to the principle of *kedushat ha-bayit kidsheha l'shata v'kidsheha l'atid lavo* — that the sanctity of the *Beit Hamikdash* consecrated the site both for its time and for the future. This is, of course, a *machloket* that runs throughout *Shas*, and the *gemara* here does not develop it further; the *rishonim* are similarly brief on the matter.

### R. Elazar ben Yaakov

A parallel *baraita* is then cited: *Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov omer*, similarly recording that three *nevi'im* came up from the *golah*. In this version, however, the three testimonies are configured differently. One prophet testified about both the location of the *mizbe'ach* and its permitted dimensions, combining what the first *baraita* split across two figures. The second testified that sacrifice may be offered even without the *bayit*. The third testified about something entirely outside the world of the *Mikdash* — *she-tikatev Ashurit*, that the Torah should be written in the *Ashurit* script. This refers to the transition, associated with the time of Ezra, from the ancient *Ketav Ivri* to the *Ketav Ashuri*, the square script familiar today. According to one view in the *gemara* in Sanhedrin 21b, discussed by both Rashi and Rambam, the *Ketav Ashuri* was the original script, subsequently lost, and restored by Ezra. The inclusion of this testimony alongside the testimonies about the *mizbe'ach* is striking: it suggests that the prophetic charge of those who came from exile encompassed not only the physical rebuilding of the *Beit Hamikdash* but also the transmission and accessibility of Torah itself — the dual revolution of Ezra's era.

## Mizbeach Components

### Me'akvin Components

The *gemara* now turns to a *baraita* enumerating what invalidates the *mizbe'ach*. *Keren v'kevesh v'yesod v'ribu'a me'akvin* — the four *keranot* at the top corners of the *mizbe'ach*, the *kevesh* or ramp, the *yesod* or foundation, and the *ribu'a*, meaning that the *mizbe'ach* must be a perfect square — all of these are *me'akvin*: if any of them is absent, the *mizbe'ach* is *pasul*. By contrast, *midato orko midato rochvo midato komato ein me'akvin* — the precise measurements of length, width, and height are not *me'akvin*. The *mizbe'ach* may deviate from its specified dimensions without being rendered *pasul*, as long as these four essential structural features are present.

### Pesuk Derivation

The *gemara* asks *mena hanei milei* — what is the scriptural basis for this distinction? Rav Huna points to the use of the definite form *hamizbe'ach* in several verses in Vayikra. The rule he derives is: *kol makom she-ne'emar hamizbe'ach le-akev* — wherever the Torah uses the form *hamizbe'ach*, it indicates that the element mentioned alongside it is *me'akev*. Rashi identifies three such verses: *keren hamizbe'ach* in Vayikra 4, making the *keranot* *me'akev*; *el yesod hamizbe'ach*, also in Vayikra 4, making the *yesod* *me'akev*; and *el pnei hamizbe'ach*, where *panim* — the face of the *mizbe'ach* — is understood to refer to the approach to it, i.e., the *kevesh* or ramp.

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge: if *hamizbe'ach* wherever it appears renders the adjacent element *me'akev*, then the verse *ve-natata oto tachat karchov hamizbe'ach mi-lemata* would imply that the *karchov* is likewise *me'akev*. What is the *karchov*? The *gemara* cites a *machloket tannaim*: *Rabbi omer ze kiyor* — Rabbi holds it is the *kiyor*, a decorative element at the top of the *mizbe'ach*; *Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Yehuda omer ze ha-sovev* — Rabbi Yose holds it is the *sovev*, a surrounding strip. The *gemara* accepts that yes, the *karchov*, whatever it is, is *me'akev*, since the verse uses *hamizbe'ach* in connection with it.

### Karchov Debate

The *machloket* between Rabbi and Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Yehuda about what the *karchov* actually is thus has direct practical consequence: according to Rabbi, it is a decorative feature — *kiyor*, a kind of ornamental element — placed atop the *mizbe'ach*; according to Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Yehuda, it is the *sovev*, a structural surrounding strip. Both agree it is essential to the *mizbe'ach's* validity.

### Broken Horn Story

The *gemara* supports its ruling with a *baraita* that describes a historically famous episode: *oto ha-yom nifgema keren hamizbe'ach* — on that day a *keren* of the *mizbe'ach* was broken. The story, elaborated in *Sukka*, involves the practice of *nisuch ha-mayim*, the water libation on *Sukkot*. This *mitzvah* is not stated explicitly in the Torah and was disputed by the *Tzadokim*, who were more literalist in their reading of scripture. On this occasion, a *Tzadoki Kohen* refused to pour the water onto the *mizbe'ach* — or poured it instead on his feet. The people, enraged, pelted him with their *etrogim* and also, according to the text, with stones. The debris struck the *mizbe'ach* and broke off one of its *keranot*. The *gemara* records that they brought a *bul shel melach* — a lump of salt — and stuffed it into the broken corner as a temporary repair, not because this made the *mizbe'ach* fit for use, but to preserve the dignity of the *mizbe'ach* so that it would not be seen as defective: *she-lo yeira'eh mizbe'ach pagum*. The *baraita* then concludes with the ruling that underlies the entire discussion: *she-kol mizbe'ach she-ein lo keren ve-kevesh ve-yesod ve-ribu'a pasul* — any *mizbe'ach* lacking a *keren*, *kevesh*, *yesod*, or *ribu'a* is *pasul*. Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Yehuda adds: *af ha-sovev* — also the *sovev*, consistent with his view that the *sovev* is the *karchov* which the verse renders *me'akev*.

### Two Karchov Functions

The *gemara* then refines our understanding of the *karchov* further, with a *baraita* that asks *eizehu karchov* — what is the *karchov*? The answer given is: it is the space *bein keren le-keren*, and in addition, *mekom hiluch raglei hakohanim amah* — a place one *amah* wide where the *kohanim* walk. The *gemara* objects: the *kohanim* could not literally walk *between* the *keranot*, since the corners would impede movement. The *baraita* must therefore mean that the *karchov* designates the space between the corners as one element, and the *amah* walkway for the *kohanim* as a separate, additional feature.

A further difficulty arises from the verse *tachat karkuvo mi-lemata ad chetzyo*, which implies the *karchov* was below — on the side — while the discussion of the *karchov* as an architectural feature placed it near the top. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this by saying *trei havu* — there were two *karkovim*. *Chad le-noy*, one was decorative, corresponding to the feature described by Rabbi (the *kiyor*) or Rabbi Yose be-Rabbi Yehuda (the *sovev*). *Ve-chad le-kohanim de-la nishtakedu*, the other served as a safety feature for the *kohanim* so they would not slip. Rashi explains this second *karchov* not as a fence projecting upward, but as a groove or indentation carved into the surface along the top of the *mizbe'ach*, giving the *kohanim's* feet traction. Whether one imagines it as a lattice engraving or as a narrow channel cut into the stone, the effect is the same: a recessed strip that prevented slipping, while the decorative *karchov* on the exterior side served an aesthetic and structural purpose. The two functions — beauty and safety — thus coexist in two distinct features, both sharing the name *karchov*.

## Mizbeach Dimensions

### Dimensions Not Me'akvin

Having established the four *me'akvin* elements, the *gemara* returns to the other side of the *baraita*: *midato orko u-midat komato ein me'akvin* — the precise length and height of the *mizbe'ach* do not constitute *me'akvin*. That is to say, variations in the overall dimensions of the *mizbe'ach* do not render it *pasul*, as long as the essential structural features are present.

### Rabbi Yosi's Size Limit

Rabbi Mani qualifies this ruling with an important caveat: *u-bilvad shelo yifchatenu mi-mizbe'ach she-asa Moshe* — provided one does not make it smaller than the *mizbe'ach* that Moshe built. While the upper bound extends to sixty *amot*, there is a floor below which the *mizbe'ach* may not be reduced.

### R. Yosef's Confusion

The *gemara* then asks how large that floor is: *kamah*? Rabbi Yosef answers *amah* — one *amah*. His colleagues laughed at this, and cited the explicit verse: *chamesh amot orech ve-chamesh amot rochav ravu'a yihiyeh ha-mizbe'ach* — the *mizbe'ach* should be five *amot* by five *amot* square. How could Rabbi Yosef say one *amah*? Rashi notes at the top of the *daf* that Rabbi Yosef suffered from memory difficulties in his old age, which explains why his students thought he had erred.

Abaye, his devoted student, intervened: *dilma mekom ha-ma'arachah ka'amar mar* — perhaps the master was referring to the middle square, the *mekom ha-ma'arachah*, the space where the actual pyre was arranged, which was indeed one *amah* by one *amah*. Rabbi Yosef responded gratefully: you, a great man, understood what I was saying. He then turned on those who had laughed at him and called them *bnei Keturah* — implying they were peripheral to the tradition, like the sons of Avraham who were sent away and did not inherit the covenant, almost sons of Avraham, but not quite.

This same epithet, *bnei Keturah*, appears shortly afterward in a story involving Rabbi Tarfon. The nephews of Rabbi Tarfon were sitting before him studying. He opened his mouth and deliberately misstated a *pasuk*, substituting a different name for Keturah. When they corrected him — *Keturah ketiv*, it says Keturah — he called them *bnei Keturah*. The technique was apparently a classic: a teacher feigns a mistake to test whether the students are paying attention. Rabbi Tarfon's use of the name Keturah in the deliberate misquotation is itself a kind of jest: the word he used to catch his sleeping students was the very word they had to supply to correct him, and his rebuke was then that they were *bnei Keturah* — knowing the surface but not the depth, or as one might put it, all fragrance and no substance.

### Gezirin Measurements

The *gemara* next addresses the dimensions of the *gezirin*, the wooden logs used as the base of the *ma'arachah*. Abaye bar Huna, citing Rav Chama bar Guria, states: *gezirin she-asah Moshe orchan amah ve-rochban amah ve-ovyan ke-machak godesh se'ah* — the wooden logs that Moshe made were one *amah* long, one *amah* wide, and as thin as the implement used to level a *se'ah* measure of grain. These two flat wooden squares, stacked one atop the other, formed the platform on which the *atzei ha-ma'arachah* and the fire rested. The thinness of the *gezirin* is notable: they were substantial in area but minimal in thickness, functioning essentially as a base rather than as a structural element.

### Amah Kduma Discussion

Rabbi Yirmiyah adds that the *amah* in question is an *amah gedumah* — a smaller *amah* of five *tefachim* rather than the standard six, appropriate for the constrained space at the top of the *mizbe'ach* where the *kohanim* were walking in a one-*amah* strip. Since that strip was already not wide, the middle *amah* of the *ma'arachah* should be minimally sized.

Rav Yosef questions whether Rabbi Yirmiyah's deduction was even necessary, pointing to a *baraita* that derives the same principle from a verse: *al ha-etzim asher al ha-esh asher al ha-mizbe'ach* — the layered description of wood upon fire upon *mizbe'ach* teaches *she-lo yehu etzim yotzim min ha-mizbe'ach klum*, that the logs must not protrude beyond the *mizbe'ach* at all. They must fit exactly within the *mizbe'ach's* dimensions.

Rashi's commentary clarifies that Rabbi Yirmiyah and Rav Yosef may in fact be referring to two different types of reduced measurement — the *amah kedumah* of five *tefachim* on the one hand, and what Rashi calls *amah metumtemet* on the other — though the precise distinction between them is referenced elsewhere, in *Menachot*.

## Ramp Specifications

### Kevesh Dimensions

A *mishna* in *Masechet Midot* records the dimensions of the *kevesh*, the ramp ascending to the *mizbe'ach*: thirty-two *amot* long, sixteen *amot* wide, and nine *amot* high. The *gemara* now proceeds to derive and analyze the various specifications of the *kevesh*.

### Kevesh North Location

The *gemara* asks *mena hanei milei* — not about the measurements, but about the location: how do we know that the *kevesh* was on the south side of the *mizbe'ach*? Rav Huna cites the verse: *ve-shachat oto al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafonah* — one must slaughter the offering on the *yerech* of the *mizbe'ach* to the north. He derives from this: *she-yehe yerech ba-tzafon u-fanav ba-darom* — the *yerech*, the thigh or flank, should face north, and the face should be toward the south. The image evoked is of a person lying down, with his legs toward the north and his face toward the south. Since the *kevesh* is the face — the front — of the *mizbe'ach*, it follows that the *kevesh* was on the south side. The *mizbe'ach* is thirty-two *amot* in length (the long dimension), and sixteen *amot* wide; the person in this analogy is lying with his head to the south, his legs — his *yerech* — pointing north.

### Lying vs. Sitting Debate

The *gemara* challenges this interpretation. The verse says the *yerech* should be toward the north — but why does that require the face to be toward the south? One could equally imagine the *kevesh* on the north side, with a person sitting upright facing north, so that both the *yerech* and the face point in the same direction. In that case, the legs would still face north, satisfying the verse, yet the *kevesh* would be on the north, not the south.

Rava resolves this by invoking the word *ravu'a* — the verse says the *mizbe'ach* shall be *ravu'a*, and Rava understands this to imply lying down, consistent with the image of the *kevesh* being at the southern foot of the reclining figure. Abaye objects: *ravu'a* does not mean lying down; it teaches that the *mizbe'ach* must be square. Using *ravu'a* to also indicate a lying position is an overextension.

Rava responds: *mekhdiv meruba* — the word used is *ravu'a*, not *meruba*; had the Torah written *meruba*, that would only teach squareness. *Ravu'a* is deliberately chosen to convey both squareness and the orientation of the *kevesh*, since it carries connotations of both flatness and geometric perfection. Abaye counters: if *ravu'a* implied lying down, the Torah would have written *ravutz*. Rava's final answer is that *ravu'a* is the precisely correct word because it means both things simultaneously — *d'mashma hach u'mashma hach* — it teaches squareness and it conveys the image of a figure lying flat, which in turn establishes that the *kevesh* is on the south.

### Right Turns East

A second source for the *kevesh's* southern position comes from a *baraita* citing Rabbi Yehuda: the verse *u-ma'alotav pnot kadim* — the steps should turn to the east — establishes the rule that *kol pinot she-ata poneh lo yehu ela derech yamin la-mizrach*, all turns one makes should be right turns toward the east. If the *kevesh* is on the south, a *kohen* ascending and then turning would make a right turn that takes him eastward — toward the *kedushah*, toward the *kadim*. If the *kevesh* were on the north, the turn eastward would be a left turn. The requirement to turn right toward the east thus necessitates that the *kevesh* be on the south.

The *gemara* asks: perhaps the rule should be left turns to the east? The answer is derived from the *Yam shel Shlomo*, the great basin constructed by Shlomo. The verse describing the twelve cattle supporting the *yam* says that three face north, three face west, three face south, and three face east. The description proceeds from north to east by going around clockwise — right turns. The repeated use of *ponim* for each group of cattle is not merely to describe the basin; it establishes a general halakhic principle: *kol pinot she-ata poneh lo yehu ela derech yamin la-mizrach*. When approaching the *mizbe'ach*, one should always turn right to face east.

### Air Space Requirement

A question is posed to Rabbi Yosi in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *avir yesh bein kevesh la-mizbe'ach* — there is an air space between the *kevesh* and the *mizbe'ach*. They were not flush against one another. Rabbi Yosi asks whether the questioner himself accepts this. He then offers a *pasuk*-based proof: *ve-asita olotecha ha-basar ve-ha-dam* — the verse juxtaposes *basar* (meat) and *dam* (blood) in connection with *olot*. The inference is *mah dam be-zrika af basar be-zrika* — just as the blood is applied through *zrika*, throwing, so too the meat must be thrown. Throwing implies that the *kohen* stands at some remove and sends the *basar* through the air onto the *mizbe'ach*, which implies an air space between the *kevesh* and the *mizbe'ach*.

The respondent pushes back: one could say the *kohen* stands on the top of the *ma'arachah* itself and throws from there — the air space traversed is within the *mizbe'ach*, not between the *kevesh* and the *mizbe'ach*. The reply: this argument fails because it is obvious that the *kohen* must throw, since the fire on the *ma'arachah* is already burning — no one places meat directly onto live flames with their hands. The throwing action is forced by the fire regardless. If the analogy between *basar* and *dam* is to teach us something beyond the self-evident, it must teach us that there is also an air gap between the *kevesh* and the *mizbe'ach* itself.

Rav Papa offers an alternative derivation that does not rely on the throwing analogy. He reasons logically: *ki dam mah dam avir karka mafsiko af basar avir karka mafsiko* — just as the *dam*, when thrown, traverses air space over the ground before reaching the *mizbe'ach*, so too the *basar* must traverse actual ground-level air space, not merely air space above the metal surface of the *mizbe'ach*. The analogy thus teaches that the *kohen* must stand on the ground — or equivalently, on the *kevesh* — and the *basar* must fly through open air between the *kevesh* and the *mizbe'ach*.

### Two Small Ramps

Rav Yehuda teaches that in addition to the main *kevesh*, there were *shnei kvashim ketanim yotzim min ha-kevesh* — two small ramps branching off from the main *kevesh*. One led to the *sovev*, and one led to the *yesod*, allowing the *kohanim* to access these elements of the *mizbe'ach* for the relevant *avodot*. Even these smaller ramps were *muvdalim min ha-mizbe'ach melo nima* — separated from the *mizbe'ach* by the width of a hair, not physically connected to it. The reason: *mishum she-ne'emar saviv* — because the verse says the *mizbe'ach* must be *saviv*, surrounding or encircled, which implies that the *mizbe'ach* must be a self-contained entity, completely surrounded by open space, even if only by a hair's breadth.

Rabbi Abahu provides an alternative derivation for the same gap, using the word *ravu'a* — the requirement that the *mizbe'ach* be square implies that it stand as an independent, self-contained structure. The *gemara* notes that both *limudim* are necessary. Had the Torah only written *saviv*, one might have thought the *mizbe'ach* could be round, merely requiring separation from adjacent structures; the word *ravu'a* teaches that it must be square. And had the Torah only written *ravu'a*, one might have thought it could be rectangular — elongated rather than perfectly square; the word *saviv* contributes the sense of perfect encircling, reinforcing the requirement for a proper square.

The *mishna* in *Midot* then records that the *kevesh* and the *mizbe'ach* together measured sixty-two *amot*. The *gemara* challenges: the *mizbe'ach* is thirty-two *amot* and the *kevesh* is thirty-two *amot* — that should total sixty-four, not sixty-two. The resolution is that the *yesod* and the *sovev* each consumed one *amah* of the *mizbe'ach's* base dimension, so the effective external length of the *mizbe'ach* was thirty *amot* rather than thirty-two, giving the correct total of sixty-two.

### Elevation Ratios

Rami bar Chama concludes the discussion with a general principle about ramp construction: *kol kivshei kvashim shalosh amot le-amah* — for all ramps generally, the standard elevation ratio is one *amah* of height for every three *amot* of horizontal distance. The *mizbe'ach* ramp, however, was constructed to a different ratio: *shalosh amot va-chatzi ve-etzba vi-shlish etzba be-zigufeta* — three and a half *amot* of horizontal distance, plus one *etzba* and a third, for every *amah* of elevation. How is this derived? The height of the *mizbe'ach* — excluding the *keranot* — was nine *amot*, and the length of the *kevesh* was thirty-two *amot*. Dividing thirty-two by nine yields three and a half with a remainder. The remaining half *amah* equals twelve *etzba'ot* (since one *amah* equals six *tefachim* and each *tefach* equals four *etzba'ot*, giving twenty-four *etzba'ot* per *amah*, and thus twelve *etzba'ot* per half-*amah*). Dividing those twelve *etzba'ot* among the nine *amot* of elevation gives one and one-third *etzba'ot* per *amah*, for a total ratio of three *amot* and one-third plus one *etzba* — consistent with the figure Rami bar Chama states. The *mizbe'ach* ramp was thus somewhat less steep than a standard ramp, reflecting the need for the *kohanim* to ascend comfortably and safely while performing the *avodah*.
