# Shevuot 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oath Classifications

### Nedarim vs Shvuot

The sugya of Daf 21 opens against the backdrop of a fundamental conceptual distinction that pervades the entire masechta: the difference between a *neder* and a *shvuah*. A *neder* changes the status of an object — the prohibition created by vow inheres in the thing itself, not in the person. The *shvuah*, by contrast, relates to the person; it is a declaration about what one will or will not do. Given this distinction, the governing prohibition for a *neder* is *lo yachel devaro* — one may not allow the words to be negated by acting in a manner that disregards the status one has created in the object. But when it comes to a *shvuah*, the relevant prohibition depends on which type of oath is at issue, and that question is precisely what the Gemara is working through.

### Past vs Future Oaths

The question the Gemara has been navigating is what prohibition one transgresses when taking a false *shvuah*. There are two classic categories. First, one may take a false oath about events in the past — the paradigmatic case being *achalti velo achalti*, swearing that one ate when one did not, or that one did not eat when one did. Second, one may take an oath to do something in the future and then fail to fulfill it.

### First Opinion

The first opinion in the Gemara holds that a past-oriented false oath constitutes a *shvuat shav* — an oath in vain — on the grounds that such words have no operative impact; one has lied, but the *shvuah* did not bind one to any future course of action, rendering it essentially purposeless. A future-oriented oath that one fails to keep, according to this view, transgresses *lo tishavu bishmi lashaker* — the prohibition against taking a false oath in God's name — because one initially made a binding commitment and subsequently falsified it.

### Second Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ – שֶׁקֶר, וְאַזְהַרְתֵּיהּ מִ״לֹּא תִשָּׁבְעוּ בִשְׁמִי לַשָּׁקֶר״. ״אוֹכַל״ וְ״לֹא אוֹכַל״ – עוֹבֵר בְּ״לֹא יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ״. וְאֵי זוֹ הִיא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא? נִשְׁבָּע לְשַׁנּוֹת אֶת הַיָּדוּעַ לְאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rabin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he reported that **Rabbi Yirmeya says** that **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** If one takes an oath, saying: **I ate,** or: **I did not eat,** it is **a false** oath if it is not true. **And its prohibition** in the Torah is **from:** "And **you shall not take an oath by My name falsely,** so that you profane the name of your God; I am the Lord" (Leviticus 19:12). If one takes an oath, saying: **I will eat, or: I will not eat,** and breaks his oath, he **violates** the prohibition: "When a man vows a vow to the Lord, or takes an oath to bind his soul with a bond, **he shall not break his word"** (Numbers 30:3). **And which** oath is **an oath** taken **in vain?** It is when one **takes an oath to deny that which is known to people** to be true.

The second opinion, which the Gemara now takes up, is arguably more intuitive. According to this view, using an oath to lie about the past is a *shvuat sheker* — a false oath — because one is straightforwardly lying under oath. A future-oriented oath that one violates, however, is governed by *lo yachel devaro*, precisely as a *neder* is. The logic is that a future oath creates an obligation, and when one disregards that obligation, one has negated the binding force of one's words — the same conceptual structure that underlies *lo yachel devaro* for vows.

### Rambam's View

It is worth noting that both of these positions are contested by the Rambam, who takes the simpler and perhaps most straightforward approach: whether the false oath concerns the past or the future, it is classified as a *shvuat sheker*. If one lies about the past under oath, that is *sheker*; if one swears to do something in the future and fails to keep it, one has retroactively made one's oath false, which is equally *sheker*. This, however, is not the view being developed in the sugya at this point.

## Source Derivation

### Rav Pappa's Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָא דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ – לָאו בְּפֵירוּשׁ אִיתְּמַר, אֶלָּא מִכְּלָלָא אִיתְּמַר; דְּאָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם, אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: כֹּל לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה – לָאו שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה לוֹקִין עָלָיו, וְשֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו; חוּץ מִנִּשְׁבָּע וּמֵימֵר וּמְקַלֵּל אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בַּשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: This** statement **of Rabbi Abbahu,** i.e., conveying the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, **was not stated explicitly** by Rabbi Abbahu; **rather, it was stated by inference.** It was inferred from **that** which **Rav Idi bar Avin says** that **Rav Amram says** that **Rav Yitzḥak says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili:** With regard to **any prohibition in the Torah,** if it is a **prohibition that involves an action, one is flogged for** violating **it. But** with regard to a prohibition **that does not involve an action,** one **is not flogged** for violating it, except in the cases of one who **takes an oath, and** one who **substitutes** a different animal for one that is consecrated to be sacrificed (see Leviticus 27:10), **and** one who curses **another using the** Divine **Name** (see Leviticus 19:14).

*Amar Rav Pappa*: the attribution of the second opinion — that past oaths are *shvuat sheker* and future oaths fall under *lo yachel devaro* — to Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan was not an explicit statement, but an inference (*mikhlala itmar*). The tradition was transmitted through a chain: Rabbi Idi bar Avin said in the name of Rav Amram, who said in the name of Rav Yitzchak, who said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — and Rabbi Yochanan cited Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Yose HaGlili.

### Lavin Without Action

That tradition reads: any prohibition (*lo ta'aseh*) in the Torah that involves an action (*lav sheyesh bo ma'aseh*) incurs lashes; one that involves no action (*lav she'ein bo ma'aseh*) does not incur lashes. The exceptions are taking an oath (*nishba*), performing *temurah* — attempting to transfer the sanctified status of one *korban* onto another animal — and cursing another person in God's name. All three of these exceptions are speech acts, which ordinarily would not be classified as "actions" for the purpose of the rule. Yet the Torah prescribes lashes for them. The rule thus stands: speech acts generally do not generate lashes, but these three are uniquely treated as actionable.

### Lashav Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׁבָּע מְנָלַן? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי, אָמַר קְרָא: ״לֹא תִשָּׂא אֶת שֵׁם ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ לַשָּׁוְא כִּי לֹא יְנַקֶּה״ – בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה אֵין מְנַקִּין אוֹתוֹ, אֲבָל בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ וּמְנַקִּין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that one who **takes an oath** that is not true is flogged? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: The verse states: "You shall not take the name of the Lord your God in vain; for** the Lord **will not absolve of guilt** he that takes His name in vain" (Exodus 20:7). It is the **heavenly court** that **does not absolve him; but the earthly court flogs him, and** in doing so, **absolves him of guilt.**

The Gemara now asks: where is the source for lashes in the case of a *shvuat shav*? Rabbi Yochanan, in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, derives it from the verse *lo tisa shem Hashem Elokecha lashav, ki lo yenakeh Hashem* — do not take God's name in vain, for God will not cleanse the one who does so. The *derashah* runs as follows: the heavenly court (*beit din shel ma'alah*) will not cleanse such a person, but the earthly court (*beit din shel matah*) will administer lashes — and through those lashes, cleansing is achieved. This is a striking inversion of what one might initially understand: lashes here function not merely as punishment but as a vehicle for atonement. The verse's declaration that God will not forgive is read not as an absolute negation of all forgiveness, but as a limitation on *divine* clemency — leaving open the path of human-administered punishment as the mechanism of cleansing.

### Pappa-Abaye Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: דִּלְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: ״לֹא יְנַקֶּה״ כְּלָל? אִי כְּתִיב ״כִּי לֹא יְנַקֶּה״ – כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ; הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי לֹא יְנַקֶּה ה׳״ – ה׳ הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ מְנַקֶּה, אֲבָל בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ וּמְנַקִּין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: Perhaps this** is what **the Merciful One is saying: He will not be absolved of guilt at all.** Abaye answered: **If it were written: For he will not be absolved of guilt,** it would be **as you say. Now that it is written: "For the Lord will not absolve of guilt,"** the verse teaches that **it is the Lord Who will not absolve** one who takes His name in vain; **but the earthly court flogs him, and** in doing so **absolves him of guilt.**

Rav Pappa challenges this reading: perhaps the verse means precisely what it says — *lo yenakeh klal*, that there is absolutely no forgiveness for one who takes God's name in vain? Abaye responds: if the verse had simply said *ki lo yenakeh*, that would support Rav Pappa's reading. But the verse specifically reads *ki lo yenakeh Hashem* — *God* will not cleanse. The emphasis on God as the one who withholds cleansing implies that *He* specifically will not do so, but the earthly court may administer lashes and thereby achieve cleansing. This is the textual basis for the rule that a *shvuat shav* incurs lashes (*malkot*).

### Double Lashav Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא, שְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר מְנָלַן? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: ״לַשָּׁוְא״ ״לַשָּׁוְא״ שְׁתֵּי פְּעָמִים; אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לִשְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן לִשְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for receiving lashes for **an oath** taken **in vain,** despite the fact that no physical action was performed. **From where do we** derive that this is also the *halakha* with regard to **a false oath? Rabbi Yoḥanan himself said:** The verse states: **"In vain…in vain," twice. If** the second mention **is not** necessary for the **matter of an oath** taken **in vain,** since it was already stated, **apply it** to the **matter of a false oath.**

Having established the source for lashes regarding *shvuat shav*, the Gemara asks: what is the source for lashes regarding *shvuat sheker* — a *false* oath? Rabbi Yochanan, speaking in his own name this time, answers: the word *lashav* appears twice in the passage. The verse says *lo tisa shem Hashem Elokecha lashav*, and then the verse concludes *ki lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa et shemo lashav*. Why the repetition? One mention of *lashav* suffices to address *shvuat shav*; the second is therefore redirected (*taneihu inyan*) to teach that lashes are also incurred for *shvuat sheker*. The doubled *lashav* functions as an extension from the known case — an oath in vain — to the adjacent case of a false oath.

## Which Oath Gets Lashes

### Abahu's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: הַאי שְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל – לָאו שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא! וְאֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאוֹכַל״, וְלֹא אָכַל – הַאי מִי לוֹקֶה?! וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאוֹכַל כִּכָּר זוֹ הַיּוֹם״, וְעָבַר הַיּוֹם וְלֹא אֲכָלָהּ – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Abbahu discusses this:** With regard to **this false oath** referred to by Rabbi Yoḥanan, **what are the circumstances** under which one who takes it is flogged? **If we say** it is when he says: On my **oath I will not eat, and** he then **ate, that is a prohibition that involves an action. And** if one would **rather** say **that he says:** On my **oath I will eat, and he does not eat, is** one who breaks his oath like **that flogged? But wasn't** it **stated** that with regard to one who says: On my **oath I will eat this loaf today, and the day passed and he did not eat it, Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish both say he is not flogged?**

Following this derivation, Rabbi Abahu poses a sharp analytic question: the verse teaches that *shvuat sheker* incurs lashes — but which case of *shvuat sheker* are we actually talking about? The point of the derivation was to demonstrate that even a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh*, a prohibition transgressed without an action, can incur lashes. So what case fits that description?

### Future Active Eliminated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה – מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה, וְכׇל לָאו שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה אֵין לוֹקִין עָלָיו. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה – מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה הַתְרָאַת סָפֵק, וְהַתְרָאַת סָפֵק לֹא שְׁמָהּ הַתְרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says he is not flogged because it is a prohibition that does not involve an action, and** concerning **any prohibition that does not involve an action, one is not flogged for** violating **it. And Reish Lakish says he is not flogged because** his violation of the prohibition necessarily involves **an uncertain forewarning, and an uncertain forewarning is not considered a forewarning** at all. Only if one is forewarned immediately prior to violating a prohibition does he receive lashes. Since not fulfilling his oath is a sin of omission, whenever the forewarning is offered, it remains uncertain whether one will fulfill the oath or not.

The first candidate — swearing that one will not eat and then actively eating — is immediately eliminated. If one swore not to eat and proceeded to eat, this is a *lav sheyesh bo ma'aseh*, a prohibition transgressed through a physical act. This case needs no special derivation; it follows the standard rule. The verse's teaching would be superfluous if directed at this case.

### Future Passive Eliminated

The second candidate — swearing that one *will* eat and then passively failing to do so — would qualify as a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh*, since the violation consists of simply not eating. But does one actually incur lashes in this case? The Gemara notes that this very question was addressed explicitly.

### Eating Case Analysis

The case is stated: if one swears to eat a particular loaf of bread that day (*shvua ochel kikar zu hayom*), and the day passes without one having eaten it (*ve'avar hayom velo achlah*), Rabbi Yochanan and Resh Lakish both rule that one does not incur lashes — though they disagree about the reason.

### Yochanan's Rationale

Rabbi Yochanan holds that one is exempt because this is a *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh* — a prohibition violated without action — and the general rule is that such a prohibition does not incur lashes (*kol lav she'ein bo ma'aseh ein lokin alav*).

### Resh Lakish's Rationale

Resh Lakish offers a different reason: *hatra'at safek*, a doubtful warning. For lashes to be administered, there must be a valid *hatra'ah* — a warning delivered immediately before the transgression. In this case, if one warns the person at the end of the day not to violate the oath, one cannot know at that moment whether the person will ultimately eat or not. Since the transgression will only be realized when the day ends without eating, any warning delivered beforehand is inherently uncertain as to whether it will prove to have been necessary. Rashi emphasizes that a *hatra'ah* must be delivered immediately before the transgression, and in this case the moment of warning is never one of certainty. A *hatra'at safek* — a doubtful warning — is not a valid *hatra'ah*.

### Past Oath Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: תְּהֵא בְּ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״. וּמַאי שְׁנָא? אָמַר רָבָא: בְּפֵירוּשׁ רִיבְּתָה תּוֹרָה שְׁבוּעַת שֶׁקֶר דּוֹמָה לְשָׁוְא, מָה שָׁוְא לְשֶׁעָבַר, אַף שֶׁקֶר נָמֵי לְשֶׁעָבַר.
>
> **Translation:** In response to his own question, **Rabbi Abbahu says:** The case where one who takes a false oath is flogged **will be where** he takes an oath saying: **I ate, or: I did not eat.** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about oaths relating to the past, for which one is liable to receive lashes even though he did not perform an action, and oaths relating to the future that one violates by omission, and for which one is therefore exempt from lashes according to Rabbi Yoḥanan because it is a prohibition that does not involve an action? **Rava said: The Torah explicitly extended** the liability to receive lashes to one who takes **a false oath** that **is similar to an oath** taken **in vain. Just as** an oath taken **in vain** refers **to the past, so too,** one is liable for **a false** oath that refers **to the past.** This discussion of Rabbi Abbahu's is the source of his inference as to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan mentioned by Rav Pappa.

With future-oriented violations thus excluded — whether the violation is active (covered by the standard rule) or passive (exempt from lashes in any case) — Rabbi Abahu concludes that the only case of *shvuat sheker* for which the verse needs to teach that lashes are incurred is the past-oriented case: *achalti velo achalti*. One swears about what one has or has not done, and swears falsely. That is the case. The violation is immediate, it is accomplished through speech, and it is precisely for this case that the derivation from the doubled *lashav* is necessary.

## Rava's Explanation

### Torah's Doubling

Having concluded that lashes for *shvuat sheker* apply specifically to the past-oriented case, the Gemara asks what distinguishes this case conceptually from a future-oriented false oath. *Amar Rava*: the Torah deliberately doubled the word *lashav* to signal that the *shvuat sheker* which incurs lashes is the one that resembles a *shvuat shav* — an oath in vain. *Mah shav l'she'avar, af sheker nami l'she'avar* — just as *shvuat shav* pertains to the present or past (something obviously true, obviously false, or entirely purposeless), so too the *shvuat sheker* that incurs lashes pertains to the past.

### Conceptual Parallel

The deeper conceptual point is this: both *shvuat shav* and a past-oriented *shvuat sheker* share the same structural feature — the transgression is constituted by the speech act itself. When one swears by something patently true or patently false, or swears that one ate when one did not, the very act of uttering the oath is the violation. One is using God's name either purposelessly or to deceive in that very moment. The oath itself — the words spoken — *is* the sin.

### Immediate Violation

This stands in sharp contrast to a future-oriented oath. When one swears to do something and then fails to do it, the moment of oath-taking is not itself the transgression. Part of the structure of the violation depends on what happens afterward: whether one keeps or does not keep what one said. Even according to the Rambam, who classifies this as *shvuat sheker*, the falsification occurs when one fails to fulfill the oath — which means the violation is not fully realized at the moment of speech. It is, in a sense, a two-stage transgression: the oath creates an obligation, and the future failure to uphold it turns the oath into a lie. The conceptual question — whether the violation is in the original speech act or in the subsequent non-compliance — is precisely what determines whether lashes apply and in what circumstances. For a past-oriented oath, the lie is instantaneous and complete at the moment of speaking. That is why it earns *malkot* even without any subsequent physical act.

## Mishna Shvuat Bituy

### Multiple Oaths Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל כִּכָּר זוֹ״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכְלֶנָּה״, וַאֲכָלָהּ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. זוֹ הִיא שְׁבוּעַת בִּטּוּי, שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ קׇרְבָּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Abbahu** from a mishna (27b): If one says: On my **oath I will not eat this loaf,** and then says: On my **oath I will not eat it,** and then again: On my **oath I will not eat it, and he** subsequently **ate it, he is liable only once. This is the oath on an utterance for which** one who violates the prohibition **intentionally is liable** to receive **lashes and** one who violates it **unwittingly** is liable to bring **a sliding-scale offering.**

The Gemara now raises a challenge to Rabbi Abahu's conclusion from a *mishnah*. The *mishnah*'s case involves someone who takes a series of oaths not to eat a particular loaf: *shvuah shelo ochal kikar zu*, then again *shvuah shelo ochlena*, then again. He then eats it. The ruling is *eino chayav ela achas* — he is liable for only one, because *ein shvuah chala al shvuah* — one oath cannot take effect upon a pre-existing oath. Since he was already bound by the first oath, the second and third add nothing.

### Shvuat Bituy Definition

The *mishnah* then identifies this as the case of *shvuat bituy* — an oath of enunciation, future-oriented — and declares: *zo hi shvuat bituy shechayavin al zedonah malkot ve'al shegagasah korban* — this is the oath of enunciation for which one incurs lashes if one willfully violates it and brings a *korban olah veyored* if one violates it unintentionally. The *korban olah veyored* is a sliding-scale offering — the specific type prescribed for inadvertent violation of *shvuat bituy*. The phrasing *zo hi* — "this is" — appears to be exclusive: this is the case, implying that other cases are not included.

One might ask: why does the *mishnah* need to specify *zo hi* if the case is obvious? The exclusionary force of the phrase is the point. What is it excluding? The most natural candidate is the past-oriented case, *achalti velo achalti*. If the *mishnah* says "this" future-oriented oath is where one incurs lashes and brings a *korban*, one might infer that a past-oriented false oath does not.

### Shvuat Shav Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא סֵיפָא: זוֹ הִיא שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ פָּטוּר. זוֹ הִיא לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? מַאי לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ – דְּלָא לָקֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna (29a): Which oath is an oath taken in vain? It is when one takes an oath to deny that which is known to people to be true, like one says with regard to a stone column that it is made of gold. **This is an oath** taken **in vain, for** which one is **liable** to receive **lashes** if he takes the oath **intentionally, and for** which he is **exempt** if he takes it **unwittingly.** When the mishna says: **This is** an oath taken in vain, it is **to exclude what? What, isn't** that phrase added to the mishna **to exclude** one who takes an oath saying: **I ate, or: I did not eat,** teaching **that he is not flogged?**

The *mishnah* then turns to *shvuat shav* and similarly uses the phrase *zo hi* — "this is the *shvuat shav* for which one incurs lashes for willful violation but is exempt from bringing a *korban* for inadvertent violation." The contrast with *shvuat bituy* is instructive: both incur *malkot* for intentional transgression, but *shvuat shav* carries no *korban* at all, even if violated unintentionally.

### Exclusion Question

This raises a structural question. The *mishnah* covers two cases — future oaths and oaths in vain — and uses the phrasing *zo hi* for each. The only case the *mishnah* does not explicitly address is *achalti velo achalti*, the past-oriented false oath. Both clauses of the *mishnah* seem to exclude this case, but do they exclude it for the same reason, or for different reasons? And what exactly is each clause excluding it *from*?

## Past Oaths Resolution

### First Clause: No Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; זוֹ הִיא דְּעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ פָּטוּר מִקׇּרְבָּן, אֲבָל ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ עַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ חַיָּיב קׇרְבָּן; וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דִּמְחַיֵּיב לְשֶׁעָבַר כִּלְהַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** the mishna adds: **This is** an oath, to teach that **for** violating the prohibition against taking an oath in vain **unwittingly,** one is **exempt from** bringing **an offering; but** one who takes an oath saying: **I ate, or: I did not eat, is liable** to bring **an offering for an unwitting** violation. **And this is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who deems** one **liable** to bring an offering for oaths referring **to the past** just **like** one is liable for oaths referring **to the future.**

Rabbi Abahu proposes a reading. The first clause — which identifies the future-oriented *shvuat bituy* as the one for which an unintentional violation brings a *korban* — is excluding the past-oriented case from the *korban* requirement. That is, *zo hi* in the first clause is saying: it is the future oath that generates both lashes and a *korban*; the past-oriented case does not generate a *korban*. This exclusion reflects the position of Rabbi Yishmael, who holds that only future-oriented oaths carry the *korban* obligation — the two explicit cases in the Torah that require a *korban olah veyored* are both future-oriented, and Rabbi Yishmael limits the *korban* liability to *asid lavo*, the future.

### Second Clause Tension

If the second clause — the one discussing *shvuat shav* — is similarly using *zo hi* to exclude the past-oriented case, one might infer that it is excluding *achalti velo achalti* from *malkot* as well. This would create a significant problem, because Rabbi Abahu has argued that the past-oriented false oath is precisely the case that *does* incur lashes. For that to be what the *mishnah* implies — that this most egregious case, swearing falsely about the past, carries no lashes — would be deeply counterintuitive. As one might observe: of all the categories of false oath, the deliberate use of God's name to deceive about a matter of fact is the most brazen, and yet we would be contemplating the possibility that it carries no penalty at all?

### Both Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא! רֵישָׁא רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְסֵיפָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא?! כּוּלַּהּ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְרֵישָׁא לָאו לְמַעוֹטֵי ״אָכַלְתִּי״ וְ״לֹא אָכַלְתִּי״ מִקׇּרְבָּן, אֶלָּא לְמַעוֹטֵי ״אוֹכַל״ וְלֹא אָכַל מִמַּלְקוֹת, אֲבָל קׇרְבָּן מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say** that **the first clause** of that mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael?** How can **the first clause** follow **Rabbi Yishmael's** opinion and **the latter clause** follow **Rabbi Akiva's** conflicting opinion? The Gemara answers: The **entire** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, and the first clause** does **not** serve **to exclude** one who took an oath saying: **I ate, or: I did not eat, from** the liability to bring **an offering. Rather,** it serves **to exclude** one who took an oath saying: **I will eat, and** who subsequently **did not eat, from** the liability to receive **lashes. But he is** still **liable** to bring **an offering.**

The Gemara considers whether both clauses might reflect the same position and, if so, whose. If the first clause follows Rabbi Yishmael — past oaths exclude *korban* — and the second clause follows Rabbi Akiva — who holds that past oaths *do* incur a *korban*, the same as future oaths — then the two clauses are internally inconsistent: one says no *korban* for past oaths, the other implies there is. The Gemara therefore proposes: *kula Rabbi Akiva* — the entire *mishnah* follows Rabbi Akiva, who holds that past false oaths carry the same liability as future ones.

### Correct Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא? מִסְתַּבְּרָא, קָאֵי בִּלְהַבָּא – מְמַעֵט לְהַבָּא; קָאֵי בִּלְהַבָּא – מְמַעֵט לְשֶׁעָבַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** about this way of understanding the mishna that it is to be preferred? The Gemara answers: **It stands to reason** that when the mishna **is addressing** an oath referring **to the future,** it **excludes** an oath referring **to the future,** in accordance with the reading that the entire mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. Does it makes sense that when **addressing** an oath referring **to the future,** it **excludes** an oath referring **to the past,** as the reading of the first clause in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yishmael would have it? Therefore, the added sentence: This is an oath on an utterance, in the first clause, serves to exclude one who took an oath saying: I will eat, and who subsequently did not eat, from the liability to receive lashes; and the whole mishna can be understood as being in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva that one is liable to bring an offering even for oaths relating to the past.

Under this reading, the first clause's exclusion is not of past oaths from lashes, but of a different case of future oath from *korban* and *malkot*. Specifically, the first clause contrasts the future oath violated through *action* — one swears not to eat and eats — with a future oath violated *passively* — one swears to eat and fails to. The former incurs both lashes and a *korban*; the latter incurs a *korban* but not lashes, since it is a passive violation. The phrase *zo hi* in the first clause is pointing to the active violation as the paradigm.

### Future Oaths Focus

The second clause, similarly, is not excluding past-oriented oaths from lashes — on the contrary, under Rabbi Akiva's view, they *do* incur lashes. Rather, both clauses are oriented around future oaths and are distinguishing between active and passive modes of violation. The *mishnah* is thus internally consistent: throughout, it is Rabbi Akiva who holds that past false oaths incur both lashes and a *korban*, and the *mishnah*'s exclusions are all drawn from within the category of future oaths, distinguishing the active violator (who gets lashes and is liable for a *korban*) from the passive one (who brings a *korban* but does not receive lashes). This is the settled conclusion the Gemara reaches.

## Kezayit Requirement

### Tiny Amount Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״ וְאָכַל כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, חַיָּיב כּוּ׳. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ – כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דִּמְחַיֵּיב בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁהוּא לְמַכּוֹת, וְלֹא אָמְרוּ כְּזַיִת אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן קׇרְבָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one says: On my **oath I will not eat, and he** then **ate any amount,** even less than an olive-bulk, he is **liable** according to Rabbi Akiva. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **Does Rabbi Akiva in the entire Torah,** i.e., in general, **hold** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who deems** one **liable** for eating **any amount** of forbidden food? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says:** One who eats **any amount** of forbidden food is liable **to** receive **lashes, and** the Sages **stated** the measure of **an olive-bulk only in the matter of** determining liability to bring **an offering.**

The Gemara now shifts to a related question: if one takes an oath not to eat and then eats only a tiny amount (*kol shehu*) — less than a *kezayit* — has one transgressed?

### Akiva: Words Violation

Rabbi Akiva holds that even a tiny amount constitutes a transgression. His reasoning, stated in reply to the *Chachamim*, is striking: "Since when do we find that a person incurs lashes merely for speaking?" — meaning, it is already unusual that a *shvuah* generates lashes through speech alone, without any physical act. Since the entire structure of this prohibition is grounded in the violation of one's words, the act of eating is relevant not as an act of eating per se but as the act that falsifies one's statement. And falsifying one's statement requires no minimum measure: even the smallest amount of eating means one has violated what one said.

### Rabbis: Kezayit Standard

The *Chachamim* disagree. Their position is that a *kezayit* — the standard olive-sized minimum for eating-related prohibitions — is required here as well. The violation is still defined by eating, and eating is halachically defined by the *kezayit* threshold.

### Oath Concepts

The conceptual stakes of this dispute are significant and are best articulated through the analysis of Rav Soloveitchik, who draws on Rashi's framework. The question is: what is the nature of the transgression when one violates a *shvuah* about eating? One approach — reflected in *Tosafot* — treats the *shvuah* as particularly weighty precisely because it generates liability through speech alone, and argues that the severity of the prohibition implies one must have intended even the smallest amount. A different approach, articulated more precisely by Rashi and developed by Rav Soloveitchik, locates the issue structurally: the *shvuah* creates an obligation through words, and therefore the violation is of the *words* rather than of the physical act of eating. When the violation is constituted by falsifying a speech act, there is no minimum measure for the falsification. A small lie is still a lie.

## Akiva's General Position

### General Tiny Bit Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּבְדִין הוּא דְּבָעֵי אִיפְּלוֹגֵי בְּעָלְמָא, וְהַאי דְּקָא מִיפַּלְגִי הָכָא – לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כּוֹחָן דְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: הוֹאִיל וּמְפָרֵשׁ חַיָּיב, סְתָם נָמֵי חַיָּיב; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּפָטְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if in fact Rabbi Akiva agrees with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon, then **by right,** the mishna **should have** taught that Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis **disagree in general. And** the reason **that** it states only **here** that **they disagree** is **to convey to you the far-reaching nature** of the opinion **of the Rabbis.** The Rabbis hold **that even though it is** possible **to say** that **since** one who eats less than an olive-bulk after taking an oath in which he **specifically** forbids himself any amount is **liable,** one who eats less than an olive-bulk **without** so **specifying** in his oath will **also** be **liable,** as that is included in his oath not to eat, that reasoning is not accepted. The mishna therefore states the dispute in the context of an oath that he will not eat in order to **teach us that,** according to the Rabbis, as long as he did not specify that his oath includes any amount, he is **exempt** from receiving lashes.

The Gemara raises a broader question about Rabbi Akiva's position: does he hold, throughout the Torah, that even a tiny amount (*kol shehu*) suffices to transgress eating prohibitions? This would align him with the view of Rabbi Shimon.

### Shimon's Position

Rabbi Shimon explicitly holds that for *malkot*, any amount of forbidden food suffices — a *kol shehu* transgresses and incurs lashes. However, for bringing a *korban*, one still needs a *kezayit*. The *kezayit* threshold remains operative for the *korban*, but not for the *malkot*.

### Logical Extension

The Gemara reasons: if Rabbi Akiva holds like Rabbi Shimon generally — that a *kol shehu* suffices to incur lashes throughout the Torah — then his argument to the *Chachamim* in the case of *shvuot* would be trivially easy. He could simply say: I hold that even a tiny bit always violates eating prohibitions everywhere in the Torah. The *Chachamim* would have no particular difficulty answering him on the grounds of the uniqueness of *shvuot*. The fact that the debate is framed specifically in terms of *shvuot* suggests that something unique about oaths is driving his position.

### Oath Uniqueness

The resolution is that the debate is indeed uniquely about *shvuot*. Rabbi Akiva does not necessarily hold Rabbi Shimon's general position; his argument is specific to the structure of *shvuah* obligations. The *Chachamim*'s position is similarly extreme on their side: even though there is a theoretical basis for saying that *shvuot* require no minimum measure — given that the violation concerns one's words — they maintain the standard *kezayit* threshold even here. The debate thus illuminates a point of genuine principle about the nature of oath-obligations rather than a simple application of a general rule.

## Kezayit Evidence

### Nazirite Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: נָזִיר שֶׁשָּׁרָה פִּתּוֹ בְּיַיִן, וְיֵשׁ בָּהּ כְּדֵי לְצָרֵף כְּזַיִת – חַיָּיב. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בְּעָלְמָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, לְמָה לִי לְצָרֵף?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents another attempt to resolve the dilemma. **Come** and **hear** a mishna (*Nazir* 34b): **Rabbi Akiva says: A nazirite who soaked his bread in wine** and ate it, **and** the two together **contain enough to combine to an olive-bulk,** is **liable,** even though there is less than the minimal measure of wine. **And if it should enter your mind that he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon in general,** then **why do I** need the wine and bread **to combine** to the measure of an olive-bulk? Consumption of any amount of wine should be sufficient for him to be liable.

The Gemara brings a textual proof. The rabbis cite Rabbi Akiva's own ruling in a *nazir* case: if a *nazir* dips his bread in wine and eats a *kezayit* — even if the *kezayit* is a combination of wine and bread rather than wine alone — he is liable, since *heter mitztaref le'issur*, permitted food combines with the forbidden food to reach the requisite measure. This principle, unique to *nazir*, allows combining the bread (permitted) with the wine (forbidden) to constitute the full *kezayit*.

### Kezayit Inference

The critical inference: Rabbi Akiva himself requires combining to reach a *kezayit* in the *nazir* case. If he held generally that any amount transgresses — like Rabbi Shimon — there would be no need to combine at all. Even a tiny drop of wine would suffice. The fact that he demands a *kezayit* even through combination demonstrates that he does not hold Rabbi Shimon's general position.

### Unique Oath Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְדִבְרֵיהֶם דְּרַבָּנַן קָאָמַר לְהוּ: לְדִידִי, בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לִי. לְדִידְכוּ, אוֹדוֹ לִי מִיהָא, הוֹאִיל וּמְפָרֵשׁ חַיָּיב, סְתָם נָמֵי חַיָּיב! וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן: לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The fact that Rabbi Akiva did not answer the Rabbis in that way does not necessarily indicate that he does not agree with Rabbi Shimon in general. Perhaps he **is speaking to the Rabbis in accordance with their** own **statement,** and this is how he would respond to their question: **As far as my opinion** is concerned, **in the entire Torah I hold** that the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** that one is liable for all prohibitions involving eating when one eats any amount. **According to you, admit to me at least** that **since** one who **specifically** forbids himself any amount is **liable,** one who eats less than an olive-bulk **without** so **specifying** in his oath will **also** be **liable. And the Rabbis said to him: No,** we will not admit that.

The conclusion is thus established: Rabbi Akiva holds the standard *kezayit* requirement for eating prohibitions generally. His position that a *kol shehu* suffices for *shvuot* is specific to the unique nature of oath-violations, grounded in the principle that the transgression is one of words rather than of eating per se.

## Oath on Forbidden Foods

### Issur on Issur Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תְּנַן: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, וְאָכַל נְבֵילוֹת וּטְרֵיפוֹת, שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים – חַיָּיב. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר. וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: אַמַּאי חַיָּיב? מוּשְׁבָּע מֵהַר סִינַי הוּא! רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בְּכוֹלֵל דְּבָרִים הַמּוּתָּרִין עִם דְּבָרִים הָאֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And additionally, we learned** in a mishna (22b): If one says: On my **oath I will not eat, and** then **ate** the meat of unslaughtered **carcasses, or animals with a wound that will cause them to die within twelve months [*tereifot*],** or **repugnant creatures, or creeping animals,** he is **liable; and Rabbi Shimon deems** him **exempt. And we discussed it: Why** is he **liable** for breaking his oath when he eats non-kosher food? **He is** already **under oath from Mount Sinai** not to eat forbidden food, and an oath cannot take effect to render a matter forbidden that is already forbidden. **Rav and Shmuel and Rabbi Yoḥanan both,** i.e., all, **say** that this is a case **where he incorporates** into the oath that he will not eat some **permitted items, along with** the statement concerning the **forbidden items.** Since the oath takes effect with regard to the permitted items, it extends also to the forbidden ones.

The *mishnah* now addresses a further question: can a *shvuah* take effect on foods that are already biblically prohibited? The general principle of *ein issur chal al issur* — no new prohibition can attach to something already forbidden — would seem to prevent any *shvuah* from having operative effect on, say, forbidden meats. One is already prohibited from eating them; the oath would appear to add nothing.

### Case Description

The *mishnah*'s case: one takes an oath not to eat, and then eats *neveilot* (animals not slaughtered according to *halacha*), *tereifot* (animals with certain defects), *shkatzim* (creeping creatures), and *remasim* (crawling creatures) — all biblically forbidden foods.

### Mishna Ruling

The *mishnah* rules that one is liable. Despite the principle of *ein issur chal al issur*, the *shvuah* takes effect, and violating it generates liability.

### Shimon Exemption

Rabbi Shimon, however, exempts. For Rabbi Shimon, *ein issur chal al issur* applies absolutely: since these foods are already forbidden, a new *shvuah* cannot superimpose an additional prohibition upon them. Therefore, eating them does not constitute a violation of the oath — the oath never took effect on them to begin with.

## Oath Inclusive Language

### Inclusive Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אִי אַתָּה מוֹצֵא אֶלָּא אִי בִּמְפָרֵשׁ חֲצִי שִׁיעוּר – וְאַלִּיבָּא דְרַבָּנַן, אִי בִּסְתָם – וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּאָמַר: אָדָם אוֹסֵר עַצְמוֹ בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Reish Lakish says: You find** that one is liable for eating forbidden food as the result of an oath **only if** it is both an oath **where** he **specifies** that it includes **a half-measure,** in this case, less than an olive-bulk, **and in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis** that one is not liable for eating a half-measure unless it is specified in the oath. Since eating a half-measure is not prohibited by the Torah, the oath takes effect. Alternatively, you find that one is liable **if** he took the oath **without specifying** that the oath prohibits less than the usual measure **and in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva, who says** that **a person renders himself prohibited from** eating **any amount** by taking an oath not to eat.

The Talmud addresses how the majority position — that one is liable — is coherent in light of *ein issur chal al issur*. The first approach, attributed to Rav, Shmuel, and Rabbi Yochanan, is the principle of *becholel devarim hamutarim im devarim ha'asurim* — including permitted things together with forbidden things within the scope of the oath. If one swore not to eat anything, the oath takes effect on all the permitted foods in that category; since it takes effect on the permitted foods, it simultaneously takes effect on the forbidden foods as well. The oath is not directed solely at forbidden foods — it encompasses permitted and forbidden alike — and therefore it is operative across the entire scope. Rabbi Shimon rejects this principle and maintains that even under such inclusive language, the *shvuah* never generates a new prohibition for what was already forbidden.

### Resh Lakish's Approach

Resh Lakish offers a different approach. The oath takes effect on forbidden foods if one specifies a minimal measure — for example, stating that one will not eat even half a *kezayit*. The biblical prohibition on *neveilah* and similar foods attaches to a *kezayit*; below that threshold, there is no biblical transgression (though there may be a rabbinic one). When the *shvuah* explicitly prohibits an amount smaller than the minimum for the biblical prohibition, it creates a genuinely new restriction — the oath forbids what the Torah does not, and therefore the oath has operative effect at that level. Alternatively, even without explicit specification, the oath takes effect according to Rabbi Akiva's position that a *shvuah* forbids even a *kol shehu* — which goes beyond the biblical prohibition that only attaches at the *kezayit* level.

### Akiva's Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בְּעָלְמָא כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, כָּל שֶׁהוּא נָמֵי מוּשְׁבָּע וְעוֹמֵד מֵהַר סִינַי הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בְּעָלְמָא כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it enters your mind** that Rabbi Akiva **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon in general,** this is difficult, as one who consumes **any amount is also already under oath from Mount Sinai.** Why should the oath take effect according to Reish Lakish? **Rather,** must one **not conclude from it: In general, Rabbi Akiva holds like the Rabbis,** and it is only with regard to unspecified oaths that he deems one liable for eating any amount? The Gemara affirms: **Conclude from it** that Rabbi Akiva holds like the Rabbis.

Rabbi Akiva's position that a *shvuah* generates liability for any amount is thus directly relevant here. Since the biblical prohibition on forbidden foods is only violated by a *kezayit*, an oath that forbids even a *kol shehu* creates a new prohibition for amounts below the *kezayit* threshold. In this sense, the oath is not superimposing itself on an existing prohibition — it is creating a distinct, oath-based restriction at a level where the Torah's prohibition does not operate.

### Proof of Akiva

This analysis also provides a proof for the conclusion reached earlier — that Rabbi Akiva holds his *kol shehu* position specifically with respect to *shvuot* and does not hold like Rabbi Shimon generally. For if Rabbi Akiva held like Rabbi Shimon that a *kol shehu* of forbidden food *always* transgresses the biblical prohibition, then even the *kol shehu* level would be *mushba ve'omed me'Har Sinai* — already foresworn at Sinai, already covered by the biblical prohibition. The oath would add nothing. The fact that Resh Lakish can invoke Rabbi Akiva's position as the basis for the oath's effectiveness at the *kol shehu* level proves that Rabbi Akiva does not extend his *kol shehu* rule to forbidden foods generally. His position is unique to *shvuot*.

## Oath vs Vow Measure (III)

### Word vs Act

The conceptual framework underlying the *kezayit* debate now comes into fuller relief. For normal eating prohibitions, the transgression is defined by the act of eating — and the act of eating is halachically significant only at the *kezayit* level. For vows (*nedarim*), a similar logic applies: when one says this food is forbidden to me, violating the vow consists of eating it, and eating is quantified by the *kezayit*. For a *shvuah*, however, the violation is of one's word: one swore not to eat, and eating any amount at all falsifies that statement. The violation is not primarily the act of eating; it is the act of rendering false what one swore.

### Qualitative Significance

The Gemara's earlier discussion of *biryah* — a qualitatively whole unit, such as an ant — is relevant here. Normally, a quantitative threshold is required to give halachic significance to a small amount of food. But certain things carry qualitative significance independent of quantity: a *biryah* is a whole, complete organism, and its integrity as a unit gives it significance regardless of its size. Similarly, explicitly specifying a tiny amount in one's oath gives that tiny amount qualitative significance: one has singled it out and defined it as the threshold of one's commitment.

### Word Falsification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּסְתָם, אֲבָל בִּמְפָרֵשׁ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא. מַאי טַעְמָא? מְפָרֵשׁ נָמֵי כִּבְרִיָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis is with regard to **where** one took the oath **without specifying** that he is liable for eating any amount. **But** in a case **where he specifies** that his oath applies to any amount, **everyone agrees** that he is liable **for** eating **any amount. What is the reason** for this? **One who specifies** this renders any amount significant **like** a whole **entity.**

The underlying principle, then, is that the *kezayit* requirement is a function of the act of eating being the operative locus of the violation. Where the violation is instead of a spoken commitment — where the sin is that one's words have been rendered false — the minimum measure for eating is irrelevant. Any amount that falsifies the statement is sufficient. This is the coherent basis for Rabbi Akiva's position, and it rests on a fundamental distinction between prohibitions constituted by physical acts and prohibitions constituted by the breach of a verbal commitment.

## Oath vs Vow Measure (III)s (continued)

### Konamos Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּ״שֶׁלֹּא אוֹכַל״, אֲבָל בְּ״שֶׁלֹּא אֶטְעוֹם״ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לִיטְעוֹם נָמֵי – כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** The **dispute** is with regard to a case **where** one takes an oath saying: On my oath **I will not eat, but** in a case **where** one says: On my oath **I will not taste, all agree** that he is liable **for** tasting **any amount.** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that he is liable in that case? Tasting has no defined measure. The Gemara answers: Rava nevertheless taught it, **lest you say** that **even** if one takes an oath saying: I will not taste, he is liable only if he eats an olive-bulk, **as people say: To taste,** as a way of saying: To eat. Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that this is not the case.

This same logic extends to *konamos* — vows using the technical term *konam* (a paraphrase of *korban*, used to give an object a quasi-sanctified status). The Gemara takes for granted that a *konam*-vow is violated even by a *kol shehu*, with essentially universal agreement among the *rishonim*. The reason is precisely parallel to the analysis of *shvuot*: when one declares a food *konam* to oneself, the violation consists not of the act of eating per se but of transgressing the status one has conferred upon the object. Since the transgression is about the object's status rather than the act of consumption, the *kezayit* measure — which defines the act of eating — does not apply.

## Offering on False Oath

### Speech Act Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: הֵיכָן מָצִינוּ בִּמְדַבֵּר וּמֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן, שֶׁזֶּה מְדַבֵּר וּמֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן. וְלָא?! וַהֲרֵי מְגַדֵּף! מְדַבֵּר וְאוֹסֵר קָאָמְרִינַן, וְהַאי מְדַבֵּר וְחוֹטֵא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that Rabbi Akiva **said to** the Rabbis: **Where do we find one who speaks and** is liable to **bring an offering** for it, **as this** oath taker merely **speaks,** i.e., takes an oath, **and brings an offering** for it? The Gemara asks: **And** do we **not? But isn't a blasphemer** liable to bring an offering according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva (see *Karetot* 7a)? The Gemara answers: **We are speaking of one who speaks and** in doing so **generates a prohibition. And this one,** the blasphemer, **is** merely one who **speaks and sins** but does not bring an offering.

The Gemara next raises a fundamental question about the structure of oath-violations: is it truly the case that one brings a *korban* for a transgression accomplished entirely through speech? This is unusual. Generally, a *korban* is brought when one has committed a substantive physical act — eating forbidden food, performing a forbidden labor — not merely for the act of speaking.

### Counterexamples

Two counterexamples are considered. First, *megadef* — one who blasphemes God. Blasphemy is a speech act, yet one does not bring a *korban* for it; the prohibition is violated the moment one speaks, but it generates no offering. Second, a *nazir* who brings a *korban* upon completing his *nezirut*: this is a *korban* associated with a process initiated by speech (the vow of *nezirut*), but the *korban* at the end is not for *violating* the *nazir*'s vow — it is the prescribed offering that concludes the term of *nezirut* properly.

### Narrowed Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי נָזִיר! מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּנוֹ עַל דִּבּוּרוֹ קָאָמְרִינַן, וְהַאי מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן – לְאִשְׁתְּרוֹיֵי לֵיהּ חַמְרָא הוּא דְּקָא מַיְיתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But doesn't a nazirite** render wine forbidden to himself through speech, by making a vow? And he does bring an offering. The Gemara answers: **We are speaking of one who brings an offering** specifically **for his speaking, and this one,** the nazirite, **brings an offering** at the end of his naziriteship **in order to permit wine to himself.**

The Gemara progressively narrows the definition. The relevant category is not simply "speech acts" in general, but specifically: a speech act that creates a personal prohibition (*oser le'atzmo*) which is subsequently violated. Blasphemy creates no ongoing prohibition; the *nazir*'s concluding *korban* is not for violation. What is unique to *shvuot* is that the speech act establishes a binding personal obligation, and the *korban* is brought when that obligation is inadvertently breached.

### Hekdesh Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ! אוֹסֵר לְעַצְמוֹ קָאָמְרִינַן, וְהַאי אוֹסֵר עַל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But isn't consecrated** property a case where one renders an item forbidden via speech alone and brings an offering for its misuse? The Gemara answers: **We are speaking of one who,** by speaking, **generates a prohibition for himself,** and nevertheless brings an offering. **And this one,** who consecrates an item, **generates a prohibition for the whole world.**

The analogy to *hekdesh* — consecrated objects — is raised as particularly apt. When one consecrates an object, speech creates a new status that is subsequently binding; if one misuses that object, one violates *me'ila* and brings a *korban*. The structure is: words create a status; violation of that status generates a *korban*. This is structurally identical to *shvuot*: words create an obligation; inadvertent violation of that obligation generates a *korban olah veyored*.

### Vow Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי קוּנָּמוֹת! קָסָבַר אֵין מְעִילָה בְּקוּנָּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Aren't *konamot*** an example of a case where one renders an item forbidden to himself by speech alone and brings an offering for using it? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Akiva **holds** that **there is no** prohibition of **misuse** of consecrated property **with regard to *konamot*.**

Does the same apply to *konamos* — vows? If a *konam*-vow functions like *hekdesh*, then violating it would constitute *me'ila*, and one would bring a *korban* for its violation just as for misuse of consecrated property.

### Vow No Me'ila

The Gemara resolves this by ruling that *konamos* do not have the status of *hekdesh* for purposes of *me'ila* (*ein me'ila be'konamot*). A *konam*-vow creates a personal prohibition and generates *malkot* for willful violation, but it is not considered a form of consecration that triggers the *me'ila* framework. Therefore, there is no *korban* for violating a *konam*-vow — only *malkot*. The *korban olah veyored* for inadvertent oath-violation is thus a unique feature of *shvuot*, distinguishing them from all other speech-act prohibitions.

## Rava's Refinements

### Explicit Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בִּסְתָם, אֲבָל בִּמְפָרֵשׁ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא. מַאי טַעְמָא? מְפָרֵשׁ נָמֵי כִּבְרִיָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis is with regard to **where** one took the oath **without specifying** that he is liable for eating any amount. **But** in a case **where he specifies** that his oath applies to any amount, **everyone agrees** that he is liable **for** eating **any amount. What is the reason** for this? **One who specifies** this renders any amount significant **like** a whole **entity.**

*Amar Rava*: the debate between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* about whether a *kol shehu* suffices to violate a *shvuah* is limited to the case where one took the oath in general terms — *shvuah shelo ochal*, "I will not eat," without specifying a measure. In such a case, Rabbi Akiva holds that even a tiny amount constitutes a violation, while the *Chachamim* require a *kezayit*. But if one explicitly stated a tiny amount — "I will not eat even a *kol shehu*" — all agree that even a tiny amount suffices to violate the oath. This is simply because one has defined one's own threshold explicitly.

### Biryah Comparison

Rava compares an explicit specification to a *biryah*. Just as a *biryah* — a qualitatively whole unit — has inherent significance that exempts it from the *kezayit* requirement, an explicit declaration that a tiny amount is the threshold gives that amount a defined significance. When significance is established — whether through qualitative integrity or through explicit verbal designation — the ordinary quantitative threshold is set aside.

### Tasting Case

Similarly, if one swore not to *taste* (*shelo et'om*) rather than not to *eat*, all agree that any amount constitutes a violation. Tasting inherently refers to a small amount — that is the ordinary meaning of the word. One who swears not to taste something has defined the threshold by the nature of the word itself. The Gemara anticipates the objection that colloquially, people say "I'll just have a taste" when they mean to eat normally — but that vernacular usage does not override the precise halachic definition of *tasting* as a minimal amount.

## Oath vs Vow Measure (III)s (Conclusion)

### Debate Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בִּשְׁבוּעוֹת, אֲבָל בְּקוּנָּמוֹת – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל בְּכׇל שֶׁהוּא. מַאי טַעְמָא? קוּנָּמוֹת נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא קָא מַדְכַּר שְׁמָא דַּאֲכִילָה, כְּדִמְפָרֵשׁ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa says:** The **dispute** in the mishna is **with regard to oaths, but with regard to *konamot*, all agree** that one is liable for eating **any amount. What is the reason** for this? **Indeed,** with regard to ***konamot*, since** in the vow **he did not explicitly mention eating,** which has a defined measure, but only that the item is forbidden to him like an offering, it is **as if he specified** that he is liable for eating any amount.

*Amar Rav Pappa*: a further clarification. The dispute between Rabbi Akiva and the *Chachamim* regarding the *kol shehu* threshold applies specifically to *shvuot* — oaths, where one uses the language of a personal action ("I will not eat"). It does not apply to *konamos* — vows using the language of object-status.

### Vow Consensus

When one declares a food *konam* to oneself, all opinions agree — including the *Chachamim* — that even a tiny amount violates the vow. This is not limited to cases where one has explicitly specified a tiny amount; even a standard *konam* declaration is universally held to be violated by any measure.

### Vow Reason

The reason follows directly from the structural analysis developed throughout this sugya. A *shvuah* uses the language of eating: "I will not eat." The violation is therefore framed by the act of eating, and eating is normally defined by a *kezayit*. A *konam*-vow, by contrast, does not use the language of eating at all. One says this object is *konam* to me — one confers a status on the object without referencing the act of consumption. When the violation is of the object's status rather than of the act of eating, the *kezayit* threshold that defines a halachically significant act of eating is simply not relevant. Any engagement with the object — any amount at all — constitutes a transgression of its status. This is the conclusion that unifies the sugya's extended analysis of measures, speech acts, and the nature of oath-obligations.
