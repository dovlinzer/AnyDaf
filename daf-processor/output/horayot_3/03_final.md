# Horayot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Individual vs. Beis Din

### Yehuda: Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יָחִיד שֶׁעָשָׂה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: This** *halakha* in the mishna, which states that an individual who performs a transgression on the basis of a ruling issued by the court is exempt from liability to bring an offering, is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. But the Rabbis say: An individual who performs** a transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of** the **court** is **liable.**

The sugya opens with a ruling transmitted by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *zo divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — the position that if an individual follows *beis din* and acts in accordance with their erroneous ruling, he is exempt from bringing a *korban*, and *beis din* itself is likewise exempt from bringing its special *par* (bull) offering, because the majority of the *tzibbur* did not follow the ruling. This position has an intuitive logic to it: if *beis din* itself is not liable for their special *korban* because the majority of the community did not act on the ruling, why should the individual bear any responsibility? He did nothing wrong by deferring to the highest halachic authority.

### Chachamim: Liable

The *chachamim*, however, disagree. *Aval chachamim omrim: yachid she'asah al pi beis din chayav* — an individual who acted on the ruling of *beis din* in such a case is nevertheless obligated to bring a *korban*. This is a striking and counterintuitive position. The person is entirely blameless from any subjective moral standpoint: he is not a scholar, he relied on the greatest rabbinic authorities of his generation, and yet he must bring an atonement offering?

### Sin & Tumah

The answer lies in understanding what *korbanot* are actually about. The *korban* in the Torah, at least the *chatas*, is not fundamentally a mechanism for expressing personal guilt. It is a response to the objective reality that a sin was committed — that something was done which, had it been done willfully, would have incurred *karet*. That act creates a kind of *tuma*, an impurity in the world, and it requires rectification regardless of the subjective culpability of the person who committed it. The analogy is instructive: if someone walks into a store and breaks something, they must sweep up and pay for it regardless of whether it was their fault. The act creates the obligation. So too here — a sin was done, and since *beis din* is not bringing the *korban*, the person who committed the act must clean up the mess, so to speak. A comparable principle appears in the *Gemara* in Shabbat regarding a *tinok shenishba* — a child taken captive who has no awareness of being Jewish — who nevertheless brings certain *korbanot* for sins committed in complete innocence.

## Baraita Interpretati (II)

### Case Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא, עֲדַיִין אֲנִי אוֹמֵר: מִיעוּט קָהָל שֶׁחָטְאוּ – חַיָּיבִין, שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵיהֶן פַּר. רוֹב קָהָל שֶׁחָטְאוּ – יְהוּ פְּטוּרִין, שֶׁהֲרֵי בֵּית דִּין מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵיהֶם פַּר. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ – אֲפִילּוּ רוּבָּהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the opinion of **the Rabbis?** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Still I say:** Each member of **a minority of the congregation that sinned** based on the ruling of a court is **liable** to bring an offering for his unwitting transgression, **as** the **court does not bring a bull** for an unwitting communal sin **on the basis of** the minority's transgression. One might have thought that each member of **a majority of** the **congregation that sinned** based on the ruling of a court **would be exempt** from liability to bring an offering for his unwitting transgression, **as** the **court brings a bull** for an unwitting communal sin **on the basis of** the majority's transgression. To counter this, **the verse states** with regard to a sin-offering brought for an unwitting transgression: **"From among the common people,"** from which it is derived that **even** members of **the majority of** the congregation, **and even** members of **the entire** congregation, are not exempt because of the ruling of the court.

Having established the positions, the *Gemara* now asks: *mai Rabbi Yehuda?* Where exactly do we see this dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim*? The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* which begins: *adayin ani omer miyut kahal she-chatu chayavin she-ein beis din mevi'in aleihen par* — I would say that when a minority of the congregation sins, they are obligated, because *beis din* does not bring the special *par* offering for a minority. *Rov ha-kahal she-chatu hayu pturin* — but I might have thought that if the majority of the community sins, they should be exempt, *she-harei beis din mevi'in aleihen par*, because *beis din* would bring the *par* in that case. The *baraita* then derives from the verse *me'am ha'aretz* — "from the people of the land" — that even a majority, even the entire community, is still obligated.

### Logical Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי? אִילֵּימָא בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה [בֵּית דִּין שֶׁלֹּא בְּהוֹרָאָה], בֵּית דִּין מַאי עֲבִידְתַּיְיהוּ? שֶׁלֹּא בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין, בֵּית דִּין מִי מַיְיתוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּהוֹרָאָה? אֶלָּא בְּהוֹרָאָה. וְהָא כִּי כְּתִיב ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״ – בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה הוּא דִּכְתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what** circumstances did the majority of the congregation sin? **If we say** that the reference is to a transgression **with unwitting** performance of **an action** unrelated to a ruling of the court, why does the *tanna* say: As the court brings a bull for an unwitting communal sin on the basis of the majority's transgression? Given that this is a case where **a court** did **not** issue **a ruling, what action did** the **court** perform? Since it was **not on** the basis of **the ruling of** the **court, does** the **court bring** a bull for an unwitting communal sin that was performed **not on** the basis of its **ruling? Rather,** the reference in the *baraita* must be to a case where the majority of the congregation performed a transgression **on** the basis of the **ruling** of the court. If so, the question arises: **But when it is written** with regard to a sin-offering brought for an unwitting transgression: **"From among the common people,"** it is **with regard to** a transgression **with unwitting** performance **of an action that it is written.**

The *Gemara* immediately presses: what case are we actually discussing? If the case is one of simple *shiggas ma'aseh* — accidental sin without any ruling from *beis din* — then why is the *baraita* drawing a comparison to what *beis din* would or would not bring? *Beis din mai aviditaihu?* If there was no ruling of *beis din*, *beis din* is simply not a relevant factor. Of course individuals who sin on their own are obligated in a *korban*. And if, on the other hand, the *baraita* is discussing a case where the community followed a ruling of *beis din*, then the verse *me'am ha'aretz* — which speaks about ordinary individual sin — cannot serve as a source for a case involving a *horat beis din*. Either way, the *baraita* seems incoherent.

The difficulty deepens when we think it through more carefully. If we are speaking of a case where there was no ruling of *beis din*, then the entire comparison — "since *beis din* would bring a *korban* in the analogous case, maybe the *tzibbur* should be exempt" — makes no sense, because there is no analogous case involving *beis din* to compare to. And if we are speaking of the case of a ruling of *beis din*, then the verse *me'am ha'aretz*, which applies to plain unintentional sin, cannot tell us anything about liability when the community followed an erroneous judicial ruling.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו הָכִי קָאָמַר: מִיעוּט קָהָל שֶׁחָטְאוּ בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה חַיָּיבִין, שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין מְבִיאִין עַל יְדֵיהֶן פַּר בְּהוֹרָאָה. הָא הֵן חַיָּיבִין. יָכוֹל רוֹב צִבּוּר שֶׁעָשׂוּ בְּשִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה יְהוּ פְּטוּרִין, שֶׁהֲרֵי בֵּית דִּין מְבִיאִין עֲלֵיהֶם פַּר בְּהוֹרָאָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מֵעַם הָאָרֶץ״, אֲפִילּוּ רוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** can one **not** that **this** is what the *tanna* **is saying:** The *baraita* is incomplete and must be taught in this manner: Each member of **a minority of the congregation that sinned with unwitting** performance **of an action** is **liable** to bring an offering for his unwitting transgression, **as** the **court does not bring a bull** for an unwitting communal sin **on the basis of** the minority's transgression **in** a case where the court issued an erroneous **ruling. But** a minority of the congregation that sinned unwittingly on the basis of the ruling of the court is also **liable.** The *baraita* continues: One **might** have thought that **a majority of the congregation that** sinned **with unwitting** performance **of an action would be exempt, as** the **court brings a bull** for an unwitting communal sin **on the basis of** the majority's transgression **in** a case where the court issued an erroneous **ruling.** Therefore, **the verse states: "From among the common people,"** from which it is derived that **even** members of **the majority of** the congregation are liable.

The *Gemara* resolves this by proposing a different reading of the *baraita*. The *baraita* must be understood as follows: in a case of *shiggas ma'aseh* with no *horaat beis din*, when a minority of the congregation sins, they are clearly obligated — *she-ein beis din mevi'in aleihen par be-hora'ah*, meaning that in the comparable case of a ruling of *beis din* followed by a minority, *beis din* would not bring the *par*, and yet those individuals are obligated. So certainly when there is no ruling at all and a minority sins, they are obligated.

But one might have thought: if the majority of the congregation sins through *shiggas ma'aseh* with no ruling, perhaps they are exempt — because in the comparable case of *horaat beis din* followed by a majority, it is *beis din* that brings the *korban* and the individuals of the *tzibbur* are not obligated. Perhaps the same would apply here — when the majority sins on their own, they too should be exempt? The verse therefore teaches: *afilu rubah va-afilu kulah* — even the majority, even the entire community, is still obligated.

### Parallel Logic

What this structure reveals is that the *baraita* is built on a parallelism between cases of *shiggas ma'aseh* and cases of *horaat beis din*. The *baraita* uses the *horaat beis din* scenarios as the reference points for reasoning about the *shiggas ma'aseh* scenarios. And critically, the *baraita* takes for granted, without needing to prove it, that when an individual follows *beis din* and commits a sin, he brings a *korban*. It only needs to *prove* the case of the majority sinning by *shiggas ma'aseh*, because that is the non-obvious result. If the *baraita* held that even in the case of *horaat beis din* an individual brings nothing, there would be no reason to treat the individual case of *shiggas ma'aseh* as obviously obligated and leave it unproven. The very structure of the *baraita* — that it takes the individual's liability for granted and must argue only about the *tzibbur* — demonstrates that the *baraita* presupposes individual liability in the case of *horaat beis din*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הָעוֹלָה הִיא״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה מִיעוּטִין.
>
> **Translation:** It is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: "This is the law of the burnt-offering, it is** that which goes up on its pyre on the altar" (Leviticus 6:2); **these are three exclusionary** terms: "This," "the burnt-offering," and "it is," which serve to exclude three offerings concerning which the *halakha* is that even if they are placed on the altar they are subsequently removed: A burnt-offering slaughtered at night, a burnt-offering whose blood was spilled before it was sprinkled, and a burnt-offering whose blood was taken outside the courtyard. Apparently, it is Rabbi Yehuda who interprets multiple exclusionary terms.

This is the proof that there exists a position — call it that of the *chachamim* — that when an individual follows *beis din* and *beis din* does not bring the special *korban*, the individual is nonetheless *chayav*. And it is contrasted with Rabbi Yehuda's position, derived from the three *miutim* in the verse (*nefesh*, *bishgaga*, *ba'asotah*), each of which serves as an exclusion, among them the exclusion of one who acts under the direction of *beis din* rather than on his own initiative. The hermeneutical method of applying each *miyut* as a separate exclusion is itself a distinctively Rabbi Yehuda approach, as noted by both Rashi and Tosafos — since the standard rule is *ein miyut achar miyut ella lerabbos*, that a second *miyut* following another comes to expand rather than restrict. That Rabbi Yehuda applies each one independently as an exclusion is characteristic of his *derasha* technique, which is also attested elsewhere regarding the verse *zos toras ha-olah* at 3a, where he similarly enumerates three independent *miutim*.

## Reading Options (II)

### Rav Papa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יָחִיד שֶׁעָשָׂה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּין – פָּטוּר, אֶלָּא בֵּית דִּין מַשְׁלִים לְרוֹב צִבּוּר קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** There is no proof that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis relates to the mishna, as their dispute can be understood differently. Perhaps **everyone agrees** that **an individual who performed** a transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of** the **court** is **exempt. Rather,** the *tanna'im* **disagree** with regard to whether the members of the **court** combine with the members of the congregation to **complete a majority of** the **congregation.** The members of the congregation who sinned constitute less than a majority. When the judges of the court who sinned are added to that minority, the total of people who sinned constitute a majority.

Once the *Gemara* establishes that there are two *stam* baraitot — one that appears to exempt the individual following *beis din* and one whose structure presupposes his liability — it asks: *mimmai* — how do we know which is Rabbi Yehuda and which is the *chachamim*? Perhaps the attribution is reversed, or perhaps neither is Rabbi Yehuda's. The *Gemara* answers that the one employing the threefold *miyut* is demonstrably Rabbi Yehuda's, based on the known characteristic of his exegetical method.

Then a parallel tradition is introduced: Rav Nachman citing Shmuel frames the same controversy differently — *zo divrei Rabbi Meir, aval chachamim omrim yachid she'asah al pi horat beis din chayav*. So now the same structural debate is attributed to Rabbi Meir versus the *chachamim* rather than Rabbi Yehuda versus the *chachamim*.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai Rabbi Meir mai rabanan* — where do we see this as a debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim*? A *baraita* is cited: *horu ve'asu* — they ruled and they acted. Rabbi Meir exempts; the *chachamim* obligate. But who are the actors? If it is *beis din* itself that followed its own ruling, the question of *chiyuv* would concern *beis din*'s special *korban*, not a *korban* of individuals — and there is already a *baraita* establishing that *ma'aseh taluy ba-kahal*, the criterion of action belongs to the *tzibbur*, not *beis din*. *Beis din* only brings its special *korban* when the *tzibbur* acts on the ruling, not when *beis din* itself acts. So the case in the *baraita* must be that *beis din* ruled and a minority of the congregation followed. And the debate is whether *yachid she'asah be-horat beis din* is *patur* (Rabbi Meir) or *chayav* (the *chachamim*). That appears to be the most natural reading.

### Who Brings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר סָבַר: בֵּית דִּין מַשְׁלִים לְרוֹב צִבּוּר, וּמַר סָבַר: אֵין בֵּית דִּין מַשְׁלִים לְרוֹב צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** He explains: One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds:** The members of **the court** combine with the members of the congregation to **complete a majority of** the **congregation. And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds:** The members of the **court do not** combine with the members of the congregation to **complete a majority of** the **congregation;** therefore, those who sinned on the basis of the ruling of the court constitute less than a majority and the court is exempt from liability to bring an offering. The mishna is not only in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, as in the case of the mishna, but even the Rabbis agree.

The second alternative: the debate may concern not whether the individual is *chayav* or *patur* at all, but rather *who* brings the *korban* in the classic case where *beis din* ruled and the majority of the community followed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין וְעָשׂוּ רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל קָהָל, וּמַאן חֲכָמִים – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: צִבּוּר מַיְיתֵי וּבֵית דִּין מַיְיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is in a case where the **court issued a ruling and a majority of the congregation performed** a transgression on the basis of that ruling, and everyone agrees that the court is liable to bring a bull as an offering for an unwitting communal sin. Rabbi Meir deems the members of the congregation exempt from bringing an offering. **And who are the Rabbis** who deem them liable to bring an offering? **It is Rabbi Shimon, who said:** The **congregation brings** an offering **and** the **court brings** an offering.

Everyone agrees a *korban* must be brought; the question is whether it is *beis din* that brings it or the *tzibbur* as a collective. *Man chachamim? Rabbi Shimon hi — de-amar tzibbur maysei* — the *chachamim* position that the community is *chayav* would be Rabbi Shimon, who holds that it is the *tzibbur* as a corporate body that brings the *korban*, not *beis din*. This is admittedly a somewhat strained reading of the language of *chiyuv* and *ptur* in the *baraita*, but it is offered as one viable possibility.

### Tribal Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: שֵׁבֶט שֶׁעָשָׂה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּינוֹ, וּמַאן חֲכָמִים? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: שֵׁבֶט שֶׁעָשָׂה בְּהוֹרָאַת בֵּית דִּינוֹ, אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is in the case of **a tribe that performed** a transgression **on** the basis of **the ruling of its** tribal **court.** Rabbi Meir holds that there is no liability to bring a bull as an offering for an unwitting communal sin for a ruling issued by a tribal court; therefore, he deems the court exempt from bringing an offering. **And who are the Rabbis** who deem the tribal court liable to bring an offering? **It is Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a tribe that performed** a transgression **on** the basis of **a ruling of its** tribal **court, that tribe** is **liable.**

The third alternative is particularly interesting. Perhaps the debate is about whether a single *shevet* that followed the ruling of its own *beis din* — its tribal court — can trigger the special *korban* obligation. The picture here is something like a federal versus state court framework: the Torah speaks of a central *beis din* and a central *tzibbur*, but the tribes were themselves corporate entities with their own courts. If the majority of the tribe of Dan followed an erroneous ruling of Dan's tribal court, does that suffice to create a *chiyuv* for the special *korban*, or does the entire mechanism require the central *Sanhedrin* and *Klal Yisrael* as a whole?

### Majority Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁחָטְאוּ שִׁשָּׁה, וְהֵן רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל קָהָל, אוֹ שִׁבְעָה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָן רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל קָהָל, וּמַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר מִשְּׁמוֹ: חָטְאוּ שִׁשָּׁה וְהֵן רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל קָהָל, אוֹ שִׁבְעָה אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָן רוּבּוֹ שֶׁל קָהָל – חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis is in a case **where six** of the twelve tribes **sinned and,** although they do not constitute a majority of the number of the tribes, in terms of population **they** constitute **a majority of** the **congregation. Or seven** tribes sinned, and **even though** in terms of population **they are not a majority of** the **congregation,** they constitute a majority of the tribes. **And whose** opinion is expressed in **the *baraita*** as the opinion of the Rabbis? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says in the name of** Rabbi Meir: If **six** tribes **sinned and** in terms of population **they are a majority of** the **congregation, or** if **seven** tribes sinned **even though** in terms of population **they are not a majority of** the **congregation, they are liable.**

The fourth alternative is the most striking of all, and the *Gemara* frames it with explicit appreciation for the complexity. The debate may concern what constitutes a *rov* in the first place. Two criteria are conceivable: majority of population and majority of tribes. The scenarios map almost perfectly onto the structure of majority vote versus the Electoral College. In one scenario: six tribes sinned, which was not a majority of tribes, but those six happened to contain a majority of the total population. In the other scenario: seven tribes sinned, which is a majority of tribes, but those seven together represent a minority of the total population. Is either definition of *rov* alone sufficient, or do you need both? The *baraita* being discussed is attributed here to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar citing Rabbi Meir, who holds that either definition is sufficient — *chatu shisha ve-hein ruba shel kahal o shiv'ah af al pi she-einam ruba shel kahal, chayavin*. Rabbi Shimon holds the opposite — you need a stricter confluence of both criteria. And the *Gemara* notes that this may explain which position is the *chachamim* in the *baraita*. In any case, the *Gemara* has now offered four entirely different readings of what the ambiguous *baraita* debate is about, none of which require us to say that the explicit controversy concerns individual liability when following *beis din*.

## Majority in Israel

### Land Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: וּבְהוֹרָאָה, הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב יוֹשְׁבֵי אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַשׂ שְׁלֹמֹה בָעֵת הַהִיא אֶת הֶחָג וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עִמּוֹ קָהָל גָּדוֹל מִלְּבוֹא חֲמָת עַד נַחַל מִצְרַיִם לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים וְשִׁבְעַת יָמִים אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם״. מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״וְכׇל יִשְׂרָאֵל עִמּוֹ״, ״קָהָל גָּדוֹל מִלְּבוֹא חֲמָת עַד נַחַל מִצְרַיִם״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הָנֵי הוּא דְּאִיקְּרִי קָהָל, אֲבָל הָנָךְ לָא אִיקְּרִי קָהָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi says: And with regard to** the definition of the majority that establishes liability for performance of a transgression on the basis of the **ruling** of a court, **follow the majority of the residents of Eretz Yisrael, as it is stated: "And Solomon held the feast at that time, and all Israel with him, a great congregation, from the entrance of Hamath until the Brook of Egypt, before the Lord our God, seven days and seven days, fourteen days"** (I Kings 8:65). The Gemara clarifies the words of this verse: **Since it is written: "And all Israel with him," why do I** need to add: **"A great congregation, from the entrance of Hamath until the Brook of Egypt"? Conclude from it:** It is **these** residents of Eretz Yisrael **who are characterized** as **a congregation; but those** who reside outside Eretz Yisrael **are not characterized** as **a congregation.**

Having spent considerable discussion on how a *rov tzibbur* is defined and counted, the *Gemara* now introduces a ruling from Rav Assi that draws a sharp boundary around who counts toward the *tzibbur* in the first place. *U-ve-hora'ah, halech achar rov yoshvei Eretz Yisrael* — when determining whether you have the requisite majority of the community to trigger the special *korban* of *beis din*, you count only those who are living in the Land of Israel. Jews living outside the Land are not included in the calculation.

### Identity Concept

This ruling carries a significant conceptual implication. The entire structure of a central *beis din* issuing rulings for the community presupposes a particular model of Jewish existence — not Judaism as a portable religious identity uncoupled from any land, but rather as a national corporate identity rooted in sovereignty over a specific territory. The idea of a central court, a *Sanhedrin*, is fundamentally about the law of the land and the people who live within it. Those living outside, even if halachically Jewish in every respect, are viewed in this framework as something like expats — part of the *am*, the people, but not part of the *kahal*, the tightly constituted corporate community. And it is the concept of *kahal* — the bounded, territorially-defined community — that is the operative category for the special *korban* of *beis din*.

### Verse Support

The *Gemara* supports Rav Assi's ruling from a verse in Melachim: *va-ya'as Shlomo ba-et ha-hi et ha-chag ve-chol Yisrael imo kahal gadol mi-lavo Chamat ad Nachal Mitzrayim* — Shlomo made the festival and all of Israel was with him, a great *kahal*, from the approach of Chamat to the Egyptian wadi. The verse says *kol Yisrael*, all of Israel, but then specifies the geographic boundaries. Why specify the boundaries if it means *kol Yisrael*? *Shema mina*: only those within those borders are called *kahal* in the operative sense. *Aval hanach lo ikri kahal* — those outside are not designated *kahal*. The verse by the special *korban* of *beis din* uses the term *ha-kahal ve-asu* — and it is that tight, territorially-bounded *kahal* that is the referent. One may be part of the *am* without being part of the *kahal*, and only the *kahal* counts for the purpose of this *halacha*.

## Status Changes

### Numbers Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא: מְרוּבִּין וְנִתְמַעֲטוּ – הַיְינוּ פְּלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues defining the majority that establishes liability. It is **obvious** that the case of when those who performed a transgression on the basis of the ruling of the court were **numerous,** i.e., they constituted a majority, **and** their percentage **diminished,** e.g., if some sinners died and they now constitute a minority of the congregation; **that is the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis,** who disagree in a mishna (10b) with regard to an anointed High Priest or a king who performed an unwitting transgression before assuming his position. The Rabbis hold: Since they were laymen when they sinned, they are liable to bring the offering of a layman. Rabbi Shimon holds: If they became aware of their transgression while still laymen, they bring the offering of a layman. If they became aware of their transgression after they assumed their positions, they are exempt. Accordingly, in the case of a majority whose number diminished, the Rabbis hold that since it was a majority that sinned, they bring an offering. Rabbi Shimon holds that it depends on their status when they became aware they had unwittingly sinned.

The *Gemara* now turns to what happens when the numbers shift between the moment of sin and the moment that *beis din* becomes aware of its error. The case: at the time the erroneous ruling was followed, a majority — fifty-one percent — of the community sinned. But before *beis din* became aware of its mistake, some of those who had sinned died, such that at the moment of *yediat beis din*, only a minority of the living community had actually followed the ruling. Does *beis din* bring its special *korban*? *Harei eilu plugta de-Rabbi Shimon ve-rabanan* — this is precisely the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim*.

### Sin vs. Awareness

The underlying debate concerns the halakhic weight of the moment of sin versus the moment of awareness. Rabbi Shimon holds that the status conditions must align at both moments — the sin must occur under conditions of *chiyuv* and the awareness must arise under the same conditions of *chiyuv*. The *chachamim* hold that you go by the moment of sin alone.

### Status Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּעָטִין וְנִתְרַבּוּ, מַאי? מִי פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרַבָּנַן? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָזֵיל בָּתַר יְדִיעָה מְחַיַּיב, וְרַבָּנַן דְּאָזְלִי בָּתַר חַטָּאָת – פָּטְרִי [אוֹ לָא], מַאי? וְתִיסְבְּרָא? אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָזֵיל אַף בָּתַר יְדִיעָה [הֵיכִי דְּהָוֵי יְדִיעָה וַחֲטָאָה בְּחִיּוּב], יְדִיעָה דְּלָא חֲטָאָה מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ? דְּאִם כֵּן, לַיְיתֵי כִּי הַשְׁתָּא! אֶלָּא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן חֲטָאָה וִידִיעָה בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** If those who performed a transgression on the basis of the ruling of the court were **few,** i.e., they constituted a minority, **and** their percentage **increased,** e.g., if some non-sinners died and the sinners now constitute a majority of the congregation, **what** is the *halakha*? **Do Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis disagree** with regard to this matter as well? That would mean that **Rabbi Shimon, who follows** the status at the time of **awareness, deems** the court **liable, and the Rabbis, who follow** the status at the time of the **transgression, deem** the court **exempt. Or** do they **not** disagree? **What** is the conclusion? The Gemara clarifies: **And can you understand** that this case is contingent on the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the Rabbis? **Say that you heard that Rabbi Shimon follows** the status at the time of **awareness as well,** i.e., in a case **where** both the **awareness and** the **transgression** were **in** a period of **liability,** either liability as a layman or liability as a king or as an anointed High Priest, the sinner is liable to bring an offering. But in a case where the **awareness** was during a period of liability, but the **transgression** was **not, did you hear** Rabbi Shimon say the court is liable? The Gemara clarifies: **As, if** it is **so** that Rabbi Shimon holds that the period of awareness is the sole determining factor, **let** the High Priest and the king **bring** an offering **according to** their **present** status. If they became aware of the transgression after assuming their positions, let them bring the offering appropriate for a High Priest or a king. Why, then, does Rabbi Shimon say that in that case they are exempt? **Rather,** apparently **Rabbi Shimon requires** that both the **transgression and** the **awareness** take place in a period of liability. There is no resolution for the dilemma that was raised.

The clearest illustration of this dispute involves the *Kohen Gadol*. If a person sinned as an ordinary *kohen* — eating *chelev* or *chametz* on Pesach — and subsequently became *Kohen Gadol*, and only then became aware of his earlier sin, there is a mismatch: the sin occurred when he was a *kohen hedyot*, but the awareness came when he was *Kohen Gadol*. The *chachamim* say he brings the *korban* appropriate to his status at the moment of sin — as an ordinary individual. Rabbi Shimon says that since the awareness arose when he was in a different status, he brings nothing. The logic of *im kein leisei ke-hashata* — if it were all about the moment of awareness, Rabbi Shimon would say bring a *korban* now as a *Kohen Gadol* — makes clear that Rabbi Shimon does not simply follow the moment of awareness. He requires *both* the moment of sin and the moment of awareness to be aligned in terms of the level of obligation. *Rabbi Shimon chatas ve-yediah ba'i* — he requires both criteria to be met simultaneously.

### Application

The same principle applies to our case. When the sin occurred, *beis din* would have been *chayav* — the majority had sinned. According to Rabbi Shimon, since at the moment of awareness the majority no longer exists (some having died), there is no *chiyuv*. The *chachamim*, going by the moment of sin, would still obligate *beis din* to bring the *korban*.

The reverse case — *mu'atin ve-nisrabu*, a minority who sinned and then, before awareness, the tally shifts such that they become a majority (because people from the non-sinning side died off) — would work in the opposite direction. According to Rabbi Shimon, who goes by the moment of awareness, once there is now a majority at the moment *beis din* becomes aware, perhaps that would suffice. But the *Gemara* rejects this: *ve-tisbra?* Would you really think Rabbi Shimon holds that the moment of awareness alone creates an obligation when the moment of sin did not meet the threshold? *Rabbi Shimon chatas ve-yediah ba'i* — he needs both. If the original sin did not occur under conditions of *chiyuv*, the subsequent awareness under different conditions cannot create a *chiyuv* from nothing. The *chiyuv* must be rooted in both moments.

## Combining Rulings

### Same Sin Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין חֵלֶב מוּתָּר, וְעָשׂוּ מִיעוּט הַקָּהָל, וְחָזְרוּ בֵּית דִּין בָּהֶן, וְהוֹרוּ, וְעָשׂוּ מִיעוּט אַחֵר, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁתֵּי יְדִיעוֹת נִינְהוּ לָא מִצְטָרֵף, אוֹ דִּלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי חֵלֶב הוּא, מִצְטָרֵף?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the **court issued a ruling** that forbidden **fat** is **permitted, and a minority of the congregation performed** the transgression of eating forbidden fat on the basis of that ruling, **and the court reversed their** decision and then reversed their decision again **and issued a ruling** that forbidden fat is permitted, **and a different minority** of the congregation **performed** the transgression, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara elaborates: Is it that **since there are two** disparate experiences of **awareness,** the first minority **does not combine** with the second minority, even though the two minorities together would constitute a majority? **Or perhaps, since** both **this** transgression **and that** transgression are the same, eating forbidden **fat,** the first minority **combines** with the second minority, and the two minorities together constitute a majority.

The *Gemara* raises a series of nested questions about whether separate rulings of *beis din* can combine to produce the necessary *rov tzibbur*. The first scenario: *horu beis din she-cheilev mutar ve-asu miyut ha-kahal*, they ruled that a certain fat was permitted and forty percent of the community followed them. Then *beis din* retracted. Then they issued the same ruling again, and another twenty percent followed. Can the two groups — forty percent from the first ruling and twenty percent from the second — be combined to reach a majority? On one hand, *keivan de-shtei yediot ninhu*, there were two separate moments of awareness and two separate rulings, which might make them independent events that cannot be combined. On the other hand, *keivan de-haidi ve-haidi cheilev hu* — since it is the same sin both times, perhaps they do combine.

### Same Sin Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי חֵלֶב הוּא מִצְטָרֵף, מִיעוּט בְּחֵלֶב שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי הַקֵּבָה, וּמִיעוּט בְּחֵלֶב שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי דַּקִּין, מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי כֵּיוָן דְּבִתְרֵי קְרָאֵי קָאָתֵי – לָא מִצְטָרֵף, אוֹ דִּלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי חֵלֶב הוּא – מִצְטָרֵף?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** in that case: **Since** both **this** transgression **and that** transgression are the same, eating forbidden **fat,** the first minority **combines** with the second minority, then there is another dilemma: In a case where **a minority** performed a transgression on the basis of the first ruling of the court and ate forbidden **fat that is upon the maw, and a minority** performed a transgression on the basis of the second ruling of the court and ate forbidden **fat that is upon the small intestine, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara elaborates: **Here, certainly, since** these transgressions **come from two** different **verses,** the first minority **does not combine** with the second minority; **or perhaps, since** both **this** transgression **and that** transgression are the same, eating forbidden **fat,** the first minority **combines** with the second minority, and the two minorities together constitute a majority.

If you say that the same sin combines even across two separate rulings, then a further question arises: what if the two rulings concerned two different types of *cheilev* — one ruling permitted the fat over the stomach, and another permitted the fat over the intestines? These are technically different *issurim* derived from different verses. Does the fact that it is still called *cheilev* cause them to combine, or does the distinct textual derivation make them categorically separate — *keivan de-bitrei krai ka-asi, lo mitztaref*?

### Different Sins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר: שֵׁם חֵלֶב הוּא וּמִצְטָרֵף, מִיעוּט בְּחֵלֶב וּמִיעוּט בְּדָם, מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי (כֵּיוָן) דִּתְרֵי אִיסּוּרֵי נִינְהוּ, [וְכֵיוָן דְּאֵין אִיסּוּרָן שָׁוֶה] – לָא מִצְטָרֵף, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן (דְּאִיסּוּרָן שָׁוֶה) – דְּקׇרְבָּנָן שָׁוֶה, מִצְטָרֵף?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** in that case: **It is the name of** forbidden **fat** that they both have in common, **and** the first minority **combines** with the second minority, then there is another dilemma: In a case where **a minority** performed a transgression on the basis of the first ruling of the court and ate forbidden **fat, and a minority** performed a transgression on the basis of the second ruling of the court and ate forbidden **blood, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara elaborates: **Here, certainly, they are two** distinct **prohibitions, and since** the nature of **their prohibition is not identical,** the first minority **does not combine** with the second minority. **Or perhaps, since their offerings are identical,** as one is liable to bring a sin-offering for unwitting violation of either of these prohibitions, the first minority **combines** with the second minority.

And if you say that all types of *cheilev* combine, the *Gemara* asks about two entirely distinct prohibitions. *Beis din* first ruled erroneously about *cheilev*, thirty percent followed; then they ruled erroneously about *dam*, another thirty percent followed. Can those sixty percent be combined to constitute a *rov*?

### Korban Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּמְצֵי לוֹמַר: כֵּיוָן (דְּאִיסּוּרָן שָׁוֶה) דְּקׇרְבָּנָן שָׁוֶה מִצְטָרֵף, מִיעוּט בְּחֵלֶב וּמִיעוּט בַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, מַהוּ? הָכָא וַדַּאי אֵין אִיסּוּרָן שָׁוֶה וְאֵין קׇרְבָּנָן שָׁוֶה, אוֹ דִּלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּאִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי כָּרֵת הוּא, מִצְטָרֵף? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** in that case: **Since their offerings are identical,** the first minority **combines** with the second minority, then there is another dilemma: In a case where **a minority** performed a transgression on the basis of the first ruling of the court and ate forbidden **fat, and a minority** performed a transgression on the basis of the second ruling of the court and engaged **in idol worship, what is** the *halakha*? **Here, certainly, neither are their prohibitions identical nor are their offerings identical,** because an individual who unwittingly engages in idol worship may bring only a female goat for atonement. Therefore, the first minority does not combine with the second minority. **Or perhaps, since** for both **this** transgression **and that** transgression one **is** liable to receive **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*]** if he performs it intentionally, the first minority **combines** with the second minority. The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Here the analysis becomes particularly refined. *Trei issurei ninhu* — these are clearly two separate prohibitions. But *keivan de-korban shaveh* — since a person who accidentally ate *cheilev* and a person who accidentally consumed *dam* each brings the same type of *korban*, a *chatas*, perhaps any two *aveiros* that carry the same *korban* can be combined for the purpose of *rov tzibbur* following an erroneous ruling of *beis din*. Under this view, any two rulings of *beis din* — no matter how different the underlying prohibitions — would combine, as long as the individual violation of each carries the same *korban*.

### Unresolved Edge

And if that is so, then the only edge case that might not combine is a ruling about *cheilev* paired with a ruling about *avodah zarah*. Why single out *avodah zarah*? Because when an individual accidentally worships idolatry, he brings a different *korban* than the standard *chatas* brought for other violations. So the *korban* is not the same. Does that distinction prevent combination? Or does the fact that both *cheilev* and *avodah zarah* carry the penalty of *karet* create enough of a shared category to combine them for *rov tzibbur* purposes? *Teiku* — the *Gemara* leaves this unresolved.

## Court Continuity

### Court Succession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁחֵלֶב מוּתָּר, וְעָשׂוּ מִיעוּט הַקָּהָל, וּמֵת אוֹתוֹ בֵּית דִּין וְעָמַד בֵּית דִּין אַחֵר, וְחָזְרוּ וְהוֹרוּ, וְעָשׂוּ מִיעוּט אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If the **court issued a ruling that** forbidden **fat** is **permitted, and a minority of the congregation performed** the transgression of eating forbidden fat on the basis of that ruling, **and** the members of **that court died and another court stood** in their place, **and again issued** the same **ruling** that forbidden fat is permitted, **and a different minority performed** the transgression of eating forbidden fat on the basis of that ruling, what is the *halakha*?

The *Gemara* now addresses the question of whether the *beis din* that issues the ruling must be the same *beis din* that becomes aware of the error. The case: *horu beis din she-cheilev mutar ve-asu miyut ha-kahal* — *beis din* ruled, a minority followed. *Ve-meis oso beis din* — that *beis din* died. *Ve-amad beis din acher* — a new *beis din* arose, repeated the ruling, and another minority followed. Now, if these two minorities together constitute a majority, does the new *beis din*'s awareness of the error create the *chiyuv* for the special *korban*?

### Awareness Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בֵּית דִּין מַיְיתֵי – לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּהָא לֵיתַנְהוּ, אֶלָּא כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ – אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר צִבּוּר מַיְיתֵי, מַאי צִבּוּר? הָא קָאֵי, אוֹ דִּלְמָא [יְדִיעָה] דְּהָהוּא בֵּית דִּין דְּהוֹרוּ בָּעִינַן! תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **According to** the opinion **of the one who said** that only the **court brings** the offering, **do not raise the dilemma, as those** judges who issued the original ruling on the basis of which the minority performed a transgression **are no** longer alive. **Rather, when should you raise the dilemma?** Raise it **according to** the opinion **of the one who said** that the **congregation brings** the offering. **What** is the *halakha*? The Gemara elaborates: The **congregation exists,** as together the two minorities constitute a majority of the congregation who performed the transgression. **Or perhaps,** for liability **we require** the **awareness of the same court that issued the ruling,** and that court no longer exists. The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *Gemara* reasons as follows. *Aliba de-man de-amar beis din maysei* — according to the view that it is *beis din* that brings the *korban* (rather than the *tzibbur*): *lo tiba'ei lach* — this case needs no deliberation. *De-ha leisnhu* — because the original *beis din* no longer exists. A *beis din* that did not issue the ruling cannot be obligated to bring the special *korban* for that ruling, and the original *beis din* is gone. But this reasoning itself raises a deeper question: is *beis din* a collection of specific individuals, or is it an abstract institutional entity? If *beis din* is an institution, then the same court — even if entirely repopulated with new judges — should remain liable for the rulings of its institutional predecessors. The *Gemara* appears to treat the court more as a collection of individuals than as an abstract entity, at least when the ruling itself has been superseded by a fresh ruling from a new court.

### Identity vs. Entity

*Ki tiba'ei lach aliba de-man de-amar tzibbur maysei* — the genuine *teiku* is according to the view that it is the *tzibbur* that brings the *korban*. In that case, the *tzibbur* is still around — the community members who sinned on the basis of the original ruling are still alive. Does the awareness of the new *beis din* suffice to trigger their obligation? Or does the *chiyuv* require specifically *yediat beis din de-horu* — the awareness of the very *beis din* that issued the erroneous ruling? Underlying this is a profound conceptual point: what defines an erroneous ruling as objectively erroneous? Perhaps only the recognition of error by the court that issued it constitutes a genuine retraction, not a later court pronouncing that an earlier court was wrong. *Teiku* — the *Gemara* does not resolve this either.

## Unanimity & Dissent

### All Must Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מֵאָה שֶׁיָּשְׁבוּ לְהוֹרוֹת – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עַד שֶׁיּוֹרוּ כּוּלָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִם כׇּל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל יִשְׁגּוּ״, עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁגּוּ כּוּלָּן [עַד שֶׁתִּפְשׁוֹט הוֹרָאָה בְּכׇל עֲדַת יִשְׂרָאֵל] אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דִּבְכָל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ קַיְימָא לַן רוּבּוֹ כְּכוּלּוֹ, וְהָכָא כְּתִיב ״כׇּל הָעֵדָה״ – הוֹאִיל וְכָךְ, אֲפִילּוּ הֵן מֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yonatan says:** Even in the case of **one hundred** judges **who convened to issue a ruling** and erred, **they are not liable** to bring an offering **unless they all issue** that **ruling, as it is stated** with regard to liability to bring the offering: **"And if the entire assembly of Israel shall act unwittingly"** (Leviticus 4:13). From the term "entire" it is derived that the judges are not liable **until they all act unwittingly, and the ruling** must **disseminate** and be adopted **throughout the entire assembly of Israel,** i.e., the Sanhedrin. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Hoshaya, said: So too it is reasonable** to conclude this, **as throughout the entire Torah we maintain** the principle: The legal status of **the majority of** an entity is considered **like all of** that entity, **and here: "The entire assembly," is written. Since it is so,** a majority does not suffice. **Even** if **they are one hundred** judges, they are liable only if the erroneous ruling was unanimous.

The *Gemara* introduces a new topic regarding the internal workings of *beis din*. Rabbi Yonatan states: *meah she-yashevu le-horo, ein chayavin ad she-yoru kulan* — if a hundred people sat together to issue a ruling, they are not liable for the special *korban* unless all of them ruled. The plain reading would suggest two things: first, that you need a full attendance of all court members, and second, that the vote must be unanimous.

### Verse Language

The source is the verse *ve-im kol adat Yisrael yishgu* — "if the entire congregation of Israel errs." The *diyuk* is from *kol* — the word "entire" or "all." *Ad she-yishgu kulan* — they all have to be in error. *Ad she-tishveh hora'ah be-chol adat Yisrael* — the ruling has to be uniform across the entire *beis din*. This would seem to mean unanimous agreement, which would of course make it nearly impossible ever to trigger this *chiyuv*, since one dissenting member would always break unanimity. The *Gemara* also observes that if the rule of *rubo ke-kulo* — majority is treated as the whole — normally applies throughout the Torah, why does the verse need to say *kol ha-edah*? The inference would seem to be that here, unlike the norm, actual unanimity is required, and therefore even if the court had more than seventy-one members, you would need all of them.

### Silent Dissent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי: הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין, וְיָדַע אֶחָד מֵהֶן שֶׁטָּעוּ אוֹ תַּלְמִיד וְרָאוּי לְהוֹרָאָה. תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רָבָא: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי גְּמִיר וּסְבִיר, אֲבָל גְּמִיר וְלָא סְבִיר – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* is **cited:** If the **court issued** a ruling and one member **knew that they erred** or if he was a student **qualified to issue** halakhic **rulings** — **why do I** need both cases? **Rava said: It was necessary** to state both, as it may **enter your mind to say** that this statement that he is liable applies specifically to one who is **learned and analytical, but** that one who is **learned but not analytical, no,** he should not be liable to bring an offering.

A *baraita* is then cited: *horu beis din ve-yada echad mehen she-tatu* — *beis din* issued a ruling and one member knew they had erred, but said nothing. If that member then acted on the ruling — whether he acted alongside them, after them, or even if the others did not act and he acted alone — *harei ze chayav*, he is *chayav*. The reason: *she-lo talah be-beis din* — he did not rely on *beis din*, because he knew they were wrong.

### Verbal Dissent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [תְּנַן:] הוֹרוּ בֵּית דִּין, וְיָדַע אֶחָד מֵהֶן שֶׁטָּעוּ וְאָמַר לָהֶן ״טוֹעִין אַתֶּם״ – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פְּטוּרִים. טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר לָהֶן ״טוֹעִין אַתֶּם״ – דִּפְטוּרִים, הָא שָׁתֵיק מִישְׁתָּק – חַיָּיבִין, וְגָמַר לַהּ הוֹרָאָה, וְאַמַּאי? וְהָא לֹא הוֹרוּ כּוּלָּן! אָמְרִי: הָכִי נָמֵי כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִרְכִּין בְּרֹאשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If the **court issued a ruling, and one of** the judges **knew that they erred and he said to them: You are mistaken, they are exempt.** One may infer: **The reason that** they are **exempt** is **that** the judge **said to them: You are mistaken. But** if **he was silent,** they are **liable, and the ruling is complete. And why,** according to Rabbi Yonatan, would they be liable? **But isn't** it so that **not all of them issued** the same **ruling?** The Sages **say** in response: **So too** here, it is a case **where** the dissenting judge **bowed his head.**

A second *baraita* adds a different scenario: *horu beis din ve-yada echad mehen she-tatu ve-amar lahen — toin atem!* — one member explicitly told his colleagues: "You are mistaken!" In that case, *harei elu pturim* — the others are exempt. They relied on what appeared to be a ruling of *beis din*, and the verbal dissent of one member does not deprive the ruling of its status as a ruling of *beis din*.

But the *Gemara* notes: *ta'ama de-amar lahen toin atem de-pturim* — the reason they are *patur* is that he explicitly said *toin atem* (and then the conclusion: *ha shatik mashtik chayavin* — but if he merely remained silent and abstained, they would be *chayavin*). The implication appears to be that silent abstention, when it looks like a nod of acquiescence, does not prevent the ruling from being considered unanimous. But open verbal opposition does. And yet the *Gemara* asks: *ve-amai halo lo horu kulan* — but in the case of verbal dissent, why are they *patur*? Surely it was not a unanimous ruling! This is an apparent challenge to the idea that the vote must be unanimous, because even without unanimity (with a dissenter) it would seem the ruling is still valid.

### Community Burden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: סָמְכוּ רַבּוֹתֵינוּ עַל דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְעַל דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי צָדוֹק, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: אֵין גּוֹזְרִין גְּזֵירָה עַל הַצִּבּוּר אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן רוֹב הַצִּבּוּר יְכוֹלִין לַעֲמוֹד בָּהּ. וְאָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַבָּא: מַאי קְרָא? ״בַּמְּאֵרָה אַתֶּם נֵאָרִים וְאֹתִי אַתֶּם קֹבְעִים הַגּוֹי כֻּלּוֹ״. וְהָא הָכָא דִּכְתִיב ״הַגּוֹי כֻּלּוֹ״, וְרוּבָּא כְּכוֹלָּא דָּמֵי, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי (יוֹחָנָן) [יוֹנָתָן]. תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya raises an objection** to the statement of Rabbi Yonatan from a *baraita*: **Our Sages relied on the statement of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and on the statement of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, who would say: One does not issue a decree upon the congregation unless the majority of the congregation is able to withstand it.** **And Rav Adda bar Abba said: What is the verse** from which this principle is derived? It is derived from the verse: **"With the curse you are cursed, yet you rob Me, the entire nation"** (Malachi 3:9). The verse is referring to the oath taken by the entire people to observe the *halakhot* of tithes, and they violated those *halakhot*. **But here it is written: "The entire nation," and** yet, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Tzadok, relied on this verse to derive that the legal status of **the majority of** an entity is **like** that of **the entire** entity, and therefore if a majority of the congregation can withstand the decree the court may issue it. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The *Gemara* challenges the unanimity requirement from a different direction as well, citing a principle attributed to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok: one does not issue a *gezeira* upon the community unless the majority of the community can withstand it. Rav Ada bar Ahavah identifies the verse: *ba-me'erah atem ne'arim ve-osi atem kovim, ha-goy kulo* — you are cursed with a curse and you defraud Me, the entire nation. The *diyuk* from *ha-goy kulo* — just as the earlier verse used *kol* to suggest unanimity, this verse says *kol* and yet is interpreted to mean the majority. *Ve-ruva ke-kulo dami* — majority is treated as the whole. *Tiyuvta de-Rabbi Yochanan, tiyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of the unanimity requirement.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, מַאי ״כׇּל עֲדַת״ דְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא? הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִי אִיכָּא כּוּלָּם – הָוְיָא הוֹרָאָה, וְאִי לָא – לָא הָוְיָא הוֹרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather,** now that Rabbi Yonatan's opinion has been refuted, **what** is derived from the phrase **"the entire assembly" that the Merciful One states? This** is what it **is saying: If there are all** the judges present and they issue that ruling based on the majority, **it is a ruling, and if not, it is not a ruling.**

The *Gemara* concludes: *ve-ella mai kol ata kamma rachmana?* — if *kol* does not mean unanimous vote, what does it teach? *Ikka kullam, havya hora'ah; ve-lo, lo havya hora'ah* — the word *kol* requires that all members of *beis din* be present when the ruling is issued. Full attendance is required. But you do not need a unanimous yes-vote. If a member is silent, abstaining, or even quietly dissenting in his own mind while outwardly nodding, the ruling is valid. Only explicit verbal opposition — *toin atem* — disrupts the ruling. The resolution thus distinguishes two entirely different requirements: full attendance (*kullam*) is mandated by the verse; unanimous affirmative vote is not.

## Shared Liability

### Yoke Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: עֲשָׂרָה שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין בַּדִּין – קוֹלָר תָּלוּי בְּצַוַּאר כּוּלָּן. פְּשִׁיטָא! הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲפִילּוּ תַּלְמִיד בִּפְנֵי רַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a court session **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** When there are **ten** judges **who sit in judgment, the chain [*kolar*]** placed around the neck of the person taken to his punishment **is suspended around the neck of all of them,** i.e., they are all responsible for the decision. The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **This teaches us that even a student before his teacher** may not be silent in deference to his teacher, as he bears responsibility for an erroneous ruling.

Rabbi Yehoshua derives from this discussion a broader principle about judicial responsibility. *Yod she-yoshvin ba-din* — if ten people are sitting in judgment, even those who are technically superfluous to the *beis din* formation, *ha-kolar talui be-tzavar kullam* — the yoke of judgment hangs around all of their necks. Every member of the court, regardless of whether their presence was halachically necessary, shares in the responsibility for the ruling. The *Gemara* asks: *peshita* — is that not obvious? The answer: the novel teaching is *da-afilu talmid bifnei rabbo* — even a student sitting in the presence of his teacher, who in practice would simply follow the teacher's lead rather than exercising independent judgment, is nonetheless a participant in the court and shares in its collective responsibility.

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא כִּי הֲוָה נָפֵיק לְבֵי דִינָא, מַיְיתִי עֲשָׂרָה תַּנָּאֵי דְבֵי רַב לְקַמֵּיהּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִימְטְיַין שִׁיבָּא מִכְּשׁוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Huna, when he would go out to the court** to sit in judgment, would **bring ten *tanna'im*,** i.e., people **who** recited *mishnayot* and *baraitot* in the **study hall,** to sit **before him** and serve as partners in judgment with him. He said: I do this **so that we will** each **receive a splinter from the beam,** i.e., each of us will bear only a small part of the responsibility.

Rav Huna took this principle to heart in a practical and somewhat poignant way. *Ki havei nafik le-vei dina* — when he would go out to *beis din*, he would bring ten of the *tarna'im* from Rav's study hall — essentially young scholars who served as memorizers and assistants — and seat them before him at the court. *Ki heichi de-nimti shiva meikashura* — so that each would get a splinter from the beam. The metaphor is of a heavy beam that, if lifted alone, would crush one person, but distributed across many bearers, each only carries a manageable share. By assembling ten people at the court even when they were halachically unnecessary for the ruling, Rav Huna was distributing the moral and spiritual weight of judgment. If the ruling turned out to be wrong, no single person would bear the full burden of responsibility alone.

### Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי כִּי הֲווֹ מַיְיתִי טְרֵפְתָּא לְקַמֵּיהּ, מַיְיתֵי עֲשָׂרָה טַבָּחֵי מִמָּתָא מַחְסֵיָא וּמוֹתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ, אָמַר: כִּי הֵיכָא דְּנִימְטְיַין שִׁיבָּא מִכְּשׁוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates on a similar note: **Rav Ashi, when they would bring** a slaughtered animal **before him** to determine whether or not it was **a *tereifa*, would bring ten slaughterers from Mata Meḥasya and sit** them **before him** while rendering his decision. **He said:** I do this **so that we will** each **receive a splinter from the beam.**

Rav Ashi applied the same logic to a different domain. *Ki havei meisi treifa le-kamei* — when he had to rule on a *treifa* case — determining whether an animal was halachically valid for consumption — *meisi asara tivchei me-masa Machasya* — he would bring ten of the *shochtim* from the city of Masa Machasya and have them present at the decision. Their practical expertise in *shechita* and *treifot* was certainly relevant and may have informed the ruling. But beyond their expertise, the same principle applied: *ki heichi de-nimti shiva meikashura* — so that each would receive a splinter from the beam. The responsibility for a consequential halachic decision should never rest on one set of shoulders alone.
