# Horayot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Individual vs. Beis Din

### Yehuda: Exempt

The sugya opens with a ruling transmitted by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *zo divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — the position that if an individual follows *beis din* and acts in accordance with their erroneous ruling, he is exempt from bringing a *korban*, and *beis din* itself is likewise exempt from bringing its special *par* (bull) offering, because the majority of the *tzibbur* did not follow the ruling. This position has an intuitive logic to it: if *beis din* itself is not liable for their special *korban* because the majority of the community did not act on the ruling, why should the individual bear any responsibility? He did nothing wrong by deferring to the highest halachic authority.

### Chachamim: Liable

The *chachamim*, however, disagree. *Aval chachamim omrim: yachid she'asah al pi beis din chayav* — an individual who acted on the ruling of *beis din* in such a case is nevertheless obligated to bring a *korban*. This is a striking and counterintuitive position. The person is entirely blameless from any subjective moral standpoint: he is not a scholar, he relied on the greatest rabbinic authorities of his generation, and yet he must bring an atonement offering?

### Sin & Tumah

The answer lies in understanding what *korbanot* are actually about. The *korban* in the Torah, at least the *chatas*, is not fundamentally a mechanism for expressing personal guilt. It is a response to the objective reality that a sin was committed — that something was done which, had it been done willfully, would have incurred *karet*. That act creates a kind of *tuma*, an impurity in the world, and it requires rectification regardless of the subjective culpability of the person who committed it. The analogy is instructive: if someone walks into a store and breaks something, they must sweep up and pay for it regardless of whether it was their fault. The act creates the obligation. So too here — a sin was done, and since *beis din* is not bringing the *korban*, the person who committed the act must clean up the mess, so to speak. A comparable principle appears in the *Gemara* in Shabbat regarding a *tinok shenishba* — a child taken captive who has no awareness of being Jewish — who nevertheless brings certain *korbanot* for sins committed in complete innocence.

## Baraita Interpretati (II)

### Case Analysis

Having established the positions, the *Gemara* now asks: *mai Rabbi Yehuda?* Where exactly do we see this dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim*? The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* which begins: *adayin ani omer miyut kahal she-chatu chayavin she-ein beis din mevi'in aleihen par* — I would say that when a minority of the congregation sins, they are obligated, because *beis din* does not bring the special *par* offering for a minority. *Rov ha-kahal she-chatu hayu pturin* — but I might have thought that if the majority of the community sins, they should be exempt, *she-harei beis din mevi'in aleihen par*, because *beis din* would bring the *par* in that case. The *baraita* then derives from the verse *me'am ha'aretz* — "from the people of the land" — that even a majority, even the entire community, is still obligated.

### Logical Difficulty

The *Gemara* immediately presses: what case are we actually discussing? If the case is one of simple *shiggas ma'aseh* — accidental sin without any ruling from *beis din* — then why is the *baraita* drawing a comparison to what *beis din* would or would not bring? *Beis din mai aviditaihu?* If there was no ruling of *beis din*, *beis din* is simply not a relevant factor. Of course individuals who sin on their own are obligated in a *korban*. And if, on the other hand, the *baraita* is discussing a case where the community followed a ruling of *beis din*, then the verse *me'am ha'aretz* — which speaks about ordinary individual sin — cannot serve as a source for a case involving a *horat beis din*. Either way, the *baraita* seems incoherent.

The difficulty deepens when we think it through more carefully. If we are speaking of a case where there was no ruling of *beis din*, then the entire comparison — "since *beis din* would bring a *korban* in the analogous case, maybe the *tzibbur* should be exempt" — makes no sense, because there is no analogous case involving *beis din* to compare to. And if we are speaking of the case of a ruling of *beis din*, then the verse *me'am ha'aretz*, which applies to plain unintentional sin, cannot tell us anything about liability when the community followed an erroneous judicial ruling.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves this by proposing a different reading of the *baraita*. The *baraita* must be understood as follows: in a case of *shiggas ma'aseh* with no *horaat beis din*, when a minority of the congregation sins, they are clearly obligated — *she-ein beis din mevi'in aleihen par be-hora'ah*, meaning that in the comparable case of a ruling of *beis din* followed by a minority, *beis din* would not bring the *par*, and yet those individuals are obligated. So certainly when there is no ruling at all and a minority sins, they are obligated.

But one might have thought: if the majority of the congregation sins through *shiggas ma'aseh* with no ruling, perhaps they are exempt — because in the comparable case of *horaat beis din* followed by a majority, it is *beis din* that brings the *korban* and the individuals of the *tzibbur* are not obligated. Perhaps the same would apply here — when the majority sins on their own, they too should be exempt? The verse therefore teaches: *afilu rubah va-afilu kulah* — even the majority, even the entire community, is still obligated.

### Parallel Logic

What this structure reveals is that the *baraita* is built on a parallelism between cases of *shiggas ma'aseh* and cases of *horaat beis din*. The *baraita* uses the *horaat beis din* scenarios as the reference points for reasoning about the *shiggas ma'aseh* scenarios. And critically, the *baraita* takes for granted, without needing to prove it, that when an individual follows *beis din* and commits a sin, he brings a *korban*. It only needs to *prove* the case of the majority sinning by *shiggas ma'aseh*, because that is the non-obvious result. If the *baraita* held that even in the case of *horaat beis din* an individual brings nothing, there would be no reason to treat the individual case of *shiggas ma'aseh* as obviously obligated and leave it unproven. The very structure of the *baraita* — that it takes the individual's liability for granted and must argue only about the *tzibbur* — demonstrates that the *baraita* presupposes individual liability in the case of *horaat beis din*.

This is the proof that there exists a position — call it that of the *chachamim* — that when an individual follows *beis din* and *beis din* does not bring the special *korban*, the individual is nonetheless *chayav*. And it is contrasted with Rabbi Yehuda's position, derived from the three *miutim* in the verse (*nefesh*, *bishgaga*, *ba'asotah*), each of which serves as an exclusion, among them the exclusion of one who acts under the direction of *beis din* rather than on his own initiative. The hermeneutical method of applying each *miyut* as a separate exclusion is itself a distinctively Rabbi Yehuda approach, as noted by both Rashi and Tosafos — since the standard rule is *ein miyut achar miyut ella lerabbos*, that a second *miyut* following another comes to expand rather than restrict. That Rabbi Yehuda applies each one independently as an exclusion is characteristic of his *derasha* technique, which is also attested elsewhere regarding the verse *zos toras ha-olah* at 3a, where he similarly enumerates three independent *miutim*.

## Reading Options

### Rav Papa Challenge

Once the *Gemara* establishes that there are two *stam* baraitot — one that appears to exempt the individual following *beis din* and one whose structure presupposes his liability — it asks: *mimmai* — how do we know which is Rabbi Yehuda and which is the *chachamim*? Perhaps the attribution is reversed, or perhaps neither is Rabbi Yehuda's. The *Gemara* answers that the one employing the threefold *miyut* is demonstrably Rabbi Yehuda's, based on the known characteristic of his exegetical method.

Then a parallel tradition is introduced: Rav Nachman citing Shmuel frames the same controversy differently — *zo divrei Rabbi Meir, aval chachamim omrim yachid she'asah al pi horat beis din chayav*. So now the same structural debate is attributed to Rabbi Meir versus the *chachamim* rather than Rabbi Yehuda versus the *chachamim*.

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai Rabbi Meir mai rabanan* — where do we see this as a debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim*? A *baraita* is cited: *horu ve'asu* — they ruled and they acted. Rabbi Meir exempts; the *chachamim* obligate. But who are the actors? If it is *beis din* itself that followed its own ruling, the question of *chiyuv* would concern *beis din*'s special *korban*, not a *korban* of individuals — and there is already a *baraita* establishing that *ma'aseh taluy ba-kahal*, the criterion of action belongs to the *tzibbur*, not *beis din*. *Beis din* only brings its special *korban* when the *tzibbur* acts on the ruling, not when *beis din* itself acts. So the case in the *baraita* must be that *beis din* ruled and a minority of the congregation followed. And the debate is whether *yachid she'asah be-horat beis din* is *patur* (Rabbi Meir) or *chayav* (the *chachamim*). That appears to be the most natural reading.

---

## Baraita Interpretati (II)

### Underlying Assumption

However, Rav Papa challenges even this reading. He argues that the debate in the *baraita* may not necessarily be about whether the individual following *beis din* is *chayav* or *patur*. The *baraita* is sufficiently ambiguous that one could read it as reflecting a range of entirely different disputes. And the key observation that drives this point is the following: *ela lav — mi-de-lo mahader al miyuta l'chiyuvah bishgegas ma'aseh* — the *baraita* does not bother to first prove the case of the individual sinning by *shiggas ma'aseh*. It takes it for granted. If the premise of the *baraita* were that the individual following *beis din* is exempt — that there can be a sin with nobody obligated in a *korban* — then the *baraita* would have needed to prove both cases separately: first, why does the individual bring a *korban* when sinning by *shiggas ma'aseh* (since in the analogous *hora'ah* case no one brings), and then, why does the majority bring a *korban* by *shiggas ma'aseh* (since in the analogous *hora'ah* case *beis din* brings and the *tzibbur* does not). The fact that the *baraita* simply takes the individual case for granted — never arguing for it, going straight to the majority case as the open question — proves that the *baraita* assumes individual liability even in the *hora'ah* case. *Shema mina: miyuta be-hora'ah chayavin hein kisva us'ira* — the minority who follow *beis din* are still obligated in their own *korbanot*, and that is so self-evident to the *baraita* that it needs no proof. Only the *tzibbur* case, where in the analogous *hora'ah* scenario the individuals are exempt, requires a derivation.

This analysis is extremely important because it is precisely what the *Gemara* uses to assert that the *chachamim* position — that the individual following *beis din* is *chayav* — is baked into the very structure of the *baraita*, regardless of what specific controversy the *baraita* is ostensibly addressing. Whether we read the debate as Rabbi Yehuda versus *chachamim* or Rabbi Meir versus *chachamim*, and whether the explicit debate concerns one issue or another, the *baraita*'s assumption of individual liability in the *hora'ah* case is constant.

## Reading Options (II)

### Beis Din in Tally

With this established, Rav Papa proceeds to offer a series of alternative readings of what the explicit debate in the *baraita* might actually be about — alternatives that do not require us to say the dispute is over whether an individual following *beis din* is *chayav* or *patur*. The first alternative: *ela be-veis din mashlimu le-rov tzibbur ka-mifligi* — perhaps the debate is about whether the actions of *beis din* members themselves are counted toward the tally of whether a majority of the community has sinned. Imagine a scenario where the number of community members who followed the erroneous ruling falls just short of a majority, but if you add in the seventy-one members of *beis din* who acted on their own ruling, you reach the majority threshold. Does *beis din* count toward the *tzibbur* for this purpose, or are they always treated as a distinct entity whose personal conduct is categorically separate from that of the general community? The *chachamim* would hold that *beis din mashlimu le-rov tzibbur* — their actions do contribute to the tally — and Rabbi Meir would hold that *ein beis din mashlimu le-rov tzibbur*.

### Who Brings

The second alternative: the debate may concern not whether the individual is *chayav* or *patur* at all, but rather *who* brings the *korban* in the classic case where *beis din* ruled and the majority of the community followed. Everyone agrees a *korban* must be brought; the question is whether it is *beis din* that brings it or the *tzibbur* as a collective. *Man chachamim? Rabbi Shimon hi — de-amar tzibbur maysei* — the *chachamim* position that the community is *chayav* would be Rabbi Shimon, who holds that it is the *tzibbur* as a corporate body that brings the *korban*, not *beis din*. This is admittedly a somewhat strained reading of the language of *chiyuv* and *ptur* in the *baraita*, but it is offered as one viable possibility.

### Tribal Rulings

The third alternative is particularly interesting. Perhaps the debate is about whether a single *shevet* that followed the ruling of its own *beis din* — its tribal court — can trigger the special *korban* obligation. The picture here is something like a federal versus state court framework: the Torah speaks of a central *beis din* and a central *tzibbur*, but the tribes were themselves corporate entities with their own courts. If the majority of the tribe of Dan followed an erroneous ruling of Dan's tribal court, does that suffice to create a *chiyuv* for the special *korban*, or does the entire mechanism require the central *Sanhedrin* and *Klal Yisrael* as a whole? *Man chachamim? Rabbi Yehuda hi — de-tanya: shevet she'asah be-horat beis dino, oso shevet chayav* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that a tribe following its own court creates a *chiyuv*, while Rabbi Meir would hold it does not.

### Majority Definition

The fourth alternative is the most striking of all, and the *Gemara* frames it with explicit appreciation for the complexity. The debate may concern what constitutes a *rov* in the first place. Two criteria are conceivable: majority of population and majority of tribes. The scenarios map almost perfectly onto the structure of majority vote versus the Electoral College. In one scenario: six tribes sinned, which was not a majority of tribes, but those six happened to contain a majority of the total population. In the other scenario: seven tribes sinned, which is a majority of tribes, but those seven together represent a minority of the total population. Is either definition of *rov* alone sufficient, or do you need both? The *baraita* being discussed is attributed here to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar citing Rabbi Meir, who holds that either definition is sufficient — *chatu shisha ve-hein ruba shel kahal o shiv'ah af al pi she-einam ruba shel kahal, chayavin*. Rabbi Shimon holds the opposite — you need a stricter confluence of both criteria. And the *Gemara* notes that this may explain which position is the *chachamim* in the *baraita*. In any case, the *Gemara* has now offered four entirely different readings of what the ambiguous *baraita* debate is about, none of which require us to say that the explicit controversy concerns individual liability when following *beis din*.

## Majority in Israel

### Land Requirement

Having spent considerable discussion on how a *rov tzibbur* is defined and counted, the *Gemara* now introduces a ruling from Rav Assi that draws a sharp boundary around who counts toward the *tzibbur* in the first place. *U-ve-hora'ah, halech achar rov yoshvei Eretz Yisrael* — when determining whether you have the requisite majority of the community to trigger the special *korban* of *beis din*, you count only those who are living in the Land of Israel. Jews living outside the Land are not included in the calculation.

### Identity Concept

This ruling carries a significant conceptual implication. The entire structure of a central *beis din* issuing rulings for the community presupposes a particular model of Jewish existence — not Judaism as a portable religious identity uncoupled from any land, but rather as a national corporate identity rooted in sovereignty over a specific territory. The idea of a central court, a *Sanhedrin*, is fundamentally about the law of the land and the people who live within it. Those living outside, even if halachically Jewish in every respect, are viewed in this framework as something like expats — part of the *am*, the people, but not part of the *kahal*, the tightly constituted corporate community. And it is the concept of *kahal* — the bounded, territorially-defined community — that is the operative category for the special *korban* of *beis din*.

### Verse Support

The *Gemara* supports Rav Assi's ruling from a verse in Melachim: *va-ya'as Shlomo ba-et ha-hi et ha-chag ve-chol Yisrael imo kahal gadol mi-lavo Chamat ad Nachal Mitzrayim* — Shlomo made the festival and all of Israel was with him, a great *kahal*, from the approach of Chamat to the Egyptian wadi. The verse says *kol Yisrael*, all of Israel, but then specifies the geographic boundaries. Why specify the boundaries if it means *kol Yisrael*? *Shema mina*: only those within those borders are called *kahal* in the operative sense. *Aval hanach lo ikri kahal* — those outside are not designated *kahal*. The verse by the special *korban* of *beis din* uses the term *ha-kahal ve-asu* — and it is that tight, territorially-bounded *kahal* that is the referent. One may be part of the *am* without being part of the *kahal*, and only the *kahal* counts for the purpose of this *halacha*.

## Status Changes

### Numbers Change

The *Gemara* now turns to what happens when the numbers shift between the moment of sin and the moment that *beis din* becomes aware of its error. The case: at the time the erroneous ruling was followed, a majority — fifty-one percent — of the community sinned. But before *beis din* became aware of its mistake, some of those who had sinned died, such that at the moment of *yediat beis din*, only a minority of the living community had actually followed the ruling. Does *beis din* bring its special *korban*? *Harei eilu plugta de-Rabbi Shimon ve-rabanan* — this is precisely the dispute between Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim*.

### Sin vs. Awareness

The underlying debate concerns the halakhic weight of the moment of sin versus the moment of awareness. Rabbi Shimon holds that the status conditions must align at both moments — the sin must occur under conditions of *chiyuv* and the awareness must arise under the same conditions of *chiyuv*. The *chachamim* hold that you go by the moment of sin alone.

### Status Example

The clearest illustration of this dispute involves the *Kohen Gadol*. If a person sinned as an ordinary *kohen* — eating *chelev* or *chametz* on Pesach — and subsequently became *Kohen Gadol*, and only then became aware of his earlier sin, there is a mismatch: the sin occurred when he was a *kohen hedyot*, but the awareness came when he was *Kohen Gadol*. The *chachamim* say he brings the *korban* appropriate to his status at the moment of sin — as an ordinary individual. Rabbi Shimon says that since the awareness arose when he was in a different status, he brings nothing. The logic of *im kein leisei ke-hashata* — if it were all about the moment of awareness, Rabbi Shimon would say bring a *korban* now as a *Kohen Gadol* — makes clear that Rabbi Shimon does not simply follow the moment of awareness. He requires *both* the moment of sin and the moment of awareness to be aligned in terms of the level of obligation. *Rabbi Shimon chatas ve-yediah ba'i* — he requires both criteria to be met simultaneously.

### Application

The same principle applies to our case. When the sin occurred, *beis din* would have been *chayav* — the majority had sinned. According to Rabbi Shimon, since at the moment of awareness the majority no longer exists (some having died), there is no *chiyuv*. The *chachamim*, going by the moment of sin, would still obligate *beis din* to bring the *korban*.

The reverse case — *mu'atin ve-nisrabu*, a minority who sinned and then, before awareness, the tally shifts such that they become a majority (because people from the non-sinning side died off) — would work in the opposite direction. According to Rabbi Shimon, who goes by the moment of awareness, once there is now a majority at the moment *beis din* becomes aware, perhaps that would suffice. But the *Gemara* rejects this: *ve-tisbra?* Would you really think Rabbi Shimon holds that the moment of awareness alone creates an obligation when the moment of sin did not meet the threshold? *Rabbi Shimon chatas ve-yediah ba'i* — he needs both. If the original sin did not occur under conditions of *chiyuv*, the subsequent awareness under different conditions cannot create a *chiyuv* from nothing. The *chiyuv* must be rooted in both moments.

## Combining Rulings

### Same Sin Scenario

The *Gemara* raises a series of nested questions about whether separate rulings of *beis din* can combine to produce the necessary *rov tzibbur*. The first scenario: *horu beis din she-cheilev mutar ve-asu miyut ha-kahal*, they ruled that a certain fat was permitted and forty percent of the community followed them. Then *beis din* retracted. Then they issued the same ruling again, and another twenty percent followed. Can the two groups — forty percent from the first ruling and twenty percent from the second — be combined to reach a majority? On one hand, *keivan de-shtei yediot ninhu*, there were two separate moments of awareness and two separate rulings, which might make them independent events that cannot be combined. On the other hand, *keivan de-haidi ve-haidi cheilev hu* — since it is the same sin both times, perhaps they do combine.

### Same Sin Type

If you say that the same sin combines even across two separate rulings, then a further question arises: what if the two rulings concerned two different types of *cheilev* — one ruling permitted the fat over the stomach, and another permitted the fat over the intestines? These are technically different *issurim* derived from different verses. Does the fact that it is still called *cheilev* cause them to combine, or does the distinct textual derivation make them categorically separate — *keivan de-bitrei krai ka-asi, lo mitztaref*?

### Different Sins

And if you say that all types of *cheilev* combine, the *Gemara* asks about two entirely distinct prohibitions. *Beis din* first ruled erroneously about *cheilev*, thirty percent followed; then they ruled erroneously about *dam*, another thirty percent followed. Can those sixty percent be combined to constitute a *rov*?

### Korban Principle

Here the analysis becomes particularly refined. *Trei issurei ninhu* — these are clearly two separate prohibitions. But *keivan de-korban shaveh* — since a person who accidentally ate *cheilev* and a person who accidentally consumed *dam* each brings the same type of *korban*, a *chatas*, perhaps any two *aveiros* that carry the same *korban* can be combined for the purpose of *rov tzibbur* following an erroneous ruling of *beis din*. Under this view, any two rulings of *beis din* — no matter how different the underlying prohibitions — would combine, as long as the individual violation of each carries the same *korban*.

### Unresolved Edge

And if that is so, then the only edge case that might not combine is a ruling about *cheilev* paired with a ruling about *avodah zarah*. Why single out *avodah zarah*? Because when an individual accidentally worships idolatry, he brings a different *korban* than the standard *chatas* brought for other violations. So the *korban* is not the same. Does that distinction prevent combination? Or does the fact that both *cheilev* and *avodah zarah* carry the penalty of *karet* create enough of a shared category to combine them for *rov tzibbur* purposes? *Teiku* — the *Gemara* leaves this unresolved.

## Court Continuity

### Court Succession

The *Gemara* now addresses the question of whether the *beis din* that issues the ruling must be the same *beis din* that becomes aware of the error. The case: *horu beis din she-cheilev mutar ve-asu miyut ha-kahal* — *beis din* ruled, a minority followed. *Ve-meis oso beis din* — that *beis din* died. *Ve-amad beis din acher* — a new *beis din* arose, repeated the ruling, and another minority followed. Now, if these two minorities together constitute a majority, does the new *beis din*'s awareness of the error create the *chiyuv* for the special *korban*?

### Awareness Question

The *Gemara* reasons as follows. *Aliba de-man de-amar beis din maysei* — according to the view that it is *beis din* that brings the *korban* (rather than the *tzibbur*): *lo tiba'ei lach* — this case needs no deliberation. *De-ha leisnhu* — because the original *beis din* no longer exists. A *beis din* that did not issue the ruling cannot be obligated to bring the special *korban* for that ruling, and the original *beis din* is gone. But this reasoning itself raises a deeper question: is *beis din* a collection of specific individuals, or is it an abstract institutional entity? If *beis din* is an institution, then the same court — even if entirely repopulated with new judges — should remain liable for the rulings of its institutional predecessors. The *Gemara* appears to treat the court more as a collection of individuals than as an abstract entity, at least when the ruling itself has been superseded by a fresh ruling from a new court.

### Identity vs. Entity

*Ki tiba'ei lach aliba de-man de-amar tzibbur maysei* — the genuine *teiku* is according to the view that it is the *tzibbur* that brings the *korban*. In that case, the *tzibbur* is still around — the community members who sinned on the basis of the original ruling are still alive. Does the awareness of the new *beis din* suffice to trigger their obligation? Or does the *chiyuv* require specifically *yediat beis din de-horu* — the awareness of the very *beis din* that issued the erroneous ruling? Underlying this is a profound conceptual point: what defines an erroneous ruling as objectively erroneous? Perhaps only the recognition of error by the court that issued it constitutes a genuine retraction, not a later court pronouncing that an earlier court was wrong. *Teiku* — the *Gemara* does not resolve this either.

## Unanimity & Dissent

### All Must Rule

The *Gemara* introduces a new topic regarding the internal workings of *beis din*. Rabbi Yonatan states: *meah she-yashevu le-horos, ein chayavin ad she-yoru kulan* — if a hundred people sat together to issue a ruling, they are not liable for the special *korban* unless all of them ruled. The plain reading would suggest two things: first, that you need a full attendance of all court members, and second, that the vote must be unanimous.

### Verse Language

The source is the verse *ve-im kol adat Yisrael yishgu* — "if the entire congregation of Israel errs." The *diyuk* is from *kol* — the word "entire" or "all." *Ad she-yishgu kulan* — they all have to be in error. *Ad she-tishveh hora'ah be-chol adat Yisrael* — the ruling has to be uniform across the entire *beis din*. This would seem to mean unanimous agreement, which would of course make it nearly impossible ever to trigger this *chiyuv*, since one dissenting member would always break unanimity. The *Gemara* also observes that if the rule of *rubo ke-kulo* — majority is treated as the whole — normally applies throughout the Torah, why does the verse need to say *kol ha-edah*? The inference would seem to be that here, unlike the norm, actual unanimity is required, and therefore even if the court had more than seventy-one members, you would need all of them.

### Silent Dissent

A *baraita* is then cited: *horu beis din ve-yada echad mehen she-tatu* — *beis din* issued a ruling and one member knew they had erred, but said nothing. If that member then acted on the ruling — whether he acted alongside them, after them, or even if the others did not act and he acted alone — *harei ze chayav*, he is *chayav*. The reason: *she-lo talah be-beis din* — he did not rely on *beis din*, because he knew they were wrong.

### Verbal Dissent

A second *baraita* adds a different scenario: *horu beis din ve-yada echad mehen she-tatu ve-amar lahen — toin atem!* — one member explicitly told his colleagues: "You are mistaken!" In that case, *harei elu pturim* — the others are exempt. They relied on what appeared to be a ruling of *beis din*, and the verbal dissent of one member does not deprive the ruling of its status as a ruling of *beis din*.

But the *Gemara* notes: *ta'ama de-amar lahen toin atem de-pturim* — the reason they are *patur* is that he explicitly said *toin atem* (and then the conclusion: *ha shatik mashtik chayavin* — but if he merely remained silent and abstained, they would be *chayavin*). The implication appears to be that silent abstention, when it looks like a nod of acquiescence, does not prevent the ruling from being considered unanimous. But open verbal opposition does. And yet the *Gemara* asks: *ve-amai halo lo horu kulan* — but in the case of verbal dissent, why are they *patur*? Surely it was not a unanimous ruling! This is an apparent challenge to the idea that the vote must be unanimous, because even without unanimity (with a dissenter) it would seem the ruling is still valid.

### Community Burden

The *Gemara* challenges the unanimity requirement from a different direction as well, citing a principle attributed to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and Rabbi Elazar bar Rabbi Tzadok: one does not issue a *gezeira* upon the community unless the majority of the community can withstand it. Rav Ada bar Ahavah identifies the verse: *ba-me'erah atem ne'arim ve-osi atem kovim, ha-goy kulo* — you are cursed with a curse and you defraud Me, the entire nation. The *diyuk* from *ha-goy kulo* — just as the earlier verse used *kol* to suggest unanimity, this verse says *kol* and yet is interpreted to mean the majority. *Ve-ruva ke-kulo dami* — majority is treated as the whole. *Tiyuvta de-Rabbi Yochanan, tiyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of the unanimity requirement.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* concludes: *ve-ella mai kol ata kamma rachmana?* — if *kol* does not mean unanimous vote, what does it teach? *Ikka kullam, havya hora'ah; ve-lo, lo havya hora'ah* — the word *kol* requires that all members of *beis din* be present when the ruling is issued. Full attendance is required. But you do not need a unanimous yes-vote. If a member is silent, abstaining, or even quietly dissenting in his own mind while outwardly nodding, the ruling is valid. Only explicit verbal opposition — *toin atem* — disrupts the ruling. The resolution thus distinguishes two entirely different requirements: full attendance (*kullam*) is mandated by the verse; unanimous affirmative vote is not.

## Shared Liability

### Yoke Principle

Rabbi Yehoshua derives from this discussion a broader principle about judicial responsibility. *Yod she-yoshvin ba-din* — if ten people are sitting in judgment, even those who are technically superfluous to the *beis din* formation, *ha-kolar talui be-tzavar kullam* — the yoke of judgment hangs around all of their necks. Every member of the court, regardless of whether their presence was halachically necessary, shares in the responsibility for the ruling. The *Gemara* asks: *peshita* — is that not obvious? The answer: the novel teaching is *da-afilu talmid bifnei rabbo* — even a student sitting in the presence of his teacher, who in practice would simply follow the teacher's lead rather than exercising independent judgment, is nonetheless a participant in the court and shares in its collective responsibility.

### Rav Huna

Rav Huna took this principle to heart in a practical and somewhat poignant way. *Ki havei nafik le-vei dina* — when he would go out to *beis din*, he would bring ten of the *tarna'im* from Rav's study hall — essentially young scholars who served as memorizers and assistants — and seat them before him at the court. *Ki heichi de-nimti shiva meikashura* — so that each would get a splinter from the beam. The metaphor is of a heavy beam that, if lifted alone, would crush one person, but distributed across many bearers, each only carries a manageable share. By assembling ten people at the court even when they were halachically unnecessary for the ruling, Rav Huna was distributing the moral and spiritual weight of judgment. If the ruling turned out to be wrong, no single person would bear the full burden of responsibility alone.

### Rav Ashi

Rav Ashi applied the same logic to a different domain. *Ki havei meisi treifa le-kamei* — when he had to rule on a *treifa* case — determining whether an animal was halachically valid for consumption — *meisi asara tivchei me-masa Machasya* — he would bring ten of the *shochtim* from the city of Masa Machasya and have them present at the decision. Their practical expertise in *shechita* and *treifot* was certainly relevant and may have informed the ruling. But beyond their expertise, the same principle applied: *ki heichi de-nimti shiva meikashura* — so that each would receive a splinter from the beam. The responsibility for a consequential halachic decision should never rest on one set of shoulders alone.
