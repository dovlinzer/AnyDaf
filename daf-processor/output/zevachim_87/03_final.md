# Zevachim 87 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hardened Meat & Timing

### Meat Falls Off Fire

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁרִירֵי. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** **with regard to hardened** limbs that were dried by the fire but have not yet been reduced to ash. The mishna teaches that before midnight, such limbs should be returned to the altar. The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter** derived that midnight determines whether or not they shall be returned?

The *daf* opens by resuming a discussion carried over from the bottom of 86b, where a *mishnah* established that if sacrificial matter falls off the altar fire before *chatzos*, there remains a *mitzvah* to return it, and the laws of *me'ilah* apply; after *chatzos*, the *mitzvah* has lapsed, *me'ilah* no longer applies, and one need not return it to the fire.

### Three States of Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַלְּקֵיהוּ, חֶצְיוֹ לְהַקְטָרָה וְחֶצְיוֹ לַהֲרָמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav explains: **Divide** the night into two parts: **Half of** the night, i.e., until midnight, is designated **for** the mitzva of **burning,** and during this time, that which is dislodged from the altar shall be returned; **and half of** the night, i.e., after midnight, is designated **for removing.**

The *Gemara* initially challenged this rule: what, after all, does *chatzos* change? Either the matter has already turned to ash, in which case one is finished with it even before *chatzos*, or it is still meat, in which case even after *chatzos* one returns it. The *Gemara*'s resolution is that *chatzos* is decisive only for an intermediate case — meat that has hardened to the consistency of stone but has not yet become ash. In that middle state, before *chatzos* it retains the status of burning matter with an associated *mitzvah* and *me'ilah*; after *chatzos* that status lapses and it is considered consumed.

### Chatzos on Mizbe'ach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: פֵּירְשׁוּ קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת וְהֶחְזִירָן אַחַר חֲצוֹת – רַבָּה אָמַר: חֲצוֹת שֵׁנִי עוֹכַלְתָּן, רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר עוֹכַלְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara previously explained the mishna as saying that limbs that were hardened by the fire but not entirely consumed are not returned to the altar if they were dislodged from it after midnight, since the mitzva of burning has been performed and the limbs are considered to have been entirely consumed. It **was stated** that *amora'im* engage in a dispute concerning the following matter: In the case of limbs that **separated** from upon the altar **before midnight and** were **returned after midnight,** whereby the mitzva of burning was not completed by midnight, **Rabba says:** The **second midnight,** i.e., midnight of the following night, **renders them consumed,** and if they were dislodged from the altar thereafter they are not returned. **Rav Ḥisda says: Dawn** following the first evening **renders them consumed,** and if they were dislodged from the altar thereafter they are not returned.

The *Gemara* further assumes — and will shortly press this assumption — that what causes this hardened meat to be considered consumed is that it was atop the *mizbe'ach* at the moment of *chatzos*. The question then becomes: what if the meat was not on the *mizbe'ach* at *chatzos*?

### Off & Back Again

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֵּירְשׁוּ קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת, וְהֶחְזִירָן לְאַחַר עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר – רַבָּה אָמַר: חֲצוֹת שֵׁנִי עוֹכַלְתָּן, רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: אֵין בָּהֶן עִיכּוּל לְעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** These *amora'im* also dispute the *halakha* in a case where the limbs **separated** from the altar **before midnight and were returned** to the altar **after dawn: Rabba says** that the **second midnight renders them consumed,** and if they separated after this time they are not returned. **Rav Ḥisda says:** Since these limbs were not returned to the altar by dawn, **they are never subject to consumption** through the passage of time. Rather, they are returned to the altar and allowed to burn until they are reduced to ash. These two disputes between Rabba and Rav Ḥisda indicate that both agree that limbs not on the altar by midnight are not considered consumed.

The *Gemara* therefore addresses a case in which the pieces of meat (*pirshu kodem chatzos vehichziran achar chatzos*) fell off the *mizbe'ach* before midnight and were returned after midnight. Assuming the meat was replaced at the top of the altar but was never actually consumed by fire — it remains hardened but not yet ash — will its status ever change given that it missed the first *chatzos* while it was on the *mizbe'ach*?

### Rabbah: Chatzos Sheni

Rabbah answers: *Chatzos sheni ochaltan* — the second midnight, Monday night, will confer the status of "consumed" upon this hardened meat. In Rabbah's view, every *chatzos* carries the power to reclassify hardened meat as consumed, so if the meat missed Sunday midnight, Monday midnight will suffice.

### Rav Chisda: Alot Hashach…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא? וּמָה חֲצוֹת שֶׁאֵין עוֹשֶׂה לִינָה, עוֹשֶׂה עִיכּוּל; עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה לִינָה, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה עִיכּוּל?
>
> **Translation:** **They say** in **the school of Rav: What is the reasoning of Rav Ḥisda,** who says that dawn renders these limbs consumed? It is the following *a fortiori* inference: **And if midnight, which does not cause** the disqualification of being **left overnight** with regard to limbs that were left off the altar until that time, still **causes consumption,** i.e., limbs burned on the altar until midnight are considered entirely consumed, then certainly with regard to **dawn, which causes** the disqualification of being **left overnight** with regard to limbs that were not left off the altar until that time, **isn't it logical that it causes consumption?**

Rav Chisda disagrees: *Amud Hashachar ochaltan* — daybreak, rather than the following midnight, will change the status. For Rav Chisda, it is not necessary to wait until Monday midnight; the dawn of Monday morning renders the meat no longer fit for the *mizbe'ach*. This is not because it becomes classified as ash — rather, the meat becomes *notar*: with the arrival of the morning of the following day it is legally leftover and may not be returned to the altar.

### Rav Chisda's Logic

The reasoning attributed to Rav Chisda by the *Gemara* is a *kal vachomer*: midnight (*chatzos*) does not ordinarily produce *linah* — it does not render food that must be eaten or portions at the base of the *mizbe'ach* into *notar*. *Chatzos* has no operative significance for ordinary sacrificial matter in that respect. And yet midnight does have the power to reclassify hardened meat as consumed. How much more so, then, should *alot hashachar* — which does produce *linah*, causing all unconsumed matter to become *notar* — reclassify the hardened meat as consumed.

Stripped of the formal *kal vachomer*, Rav Chisda's point is straightforward: hardened meat sitting at the top of the *mizbe'ach* at daybreak should simply become *notar* along with everything else. One might suggest that *ha-mizbe'ach mekadesh* shields it even from *notar* status — but that complication is set aside for the moment. The underlying intuition is that it is incongruous to think this meat remains entirely unaffected by the arrival of daybreak.

### Scenario Two

The *Gemara* then introduces a second scenario: the meat came off before *chatzos* (say, at 11:50 PM Sunday night) and was not returned until after *alot hashachar* (say, at 5:10 AM Monday morning). In this case Rava holds that the meat's status still does not change, and one must wait until Monday midnight — *chatzos sheni* — to consider it consumed. Rav Chisda, consistently with his position, holds that since the meat missed both *chatzos* and *alot hashachar* while it was atop the *mizbe'ach*, its status never changes: *ein bein ichul le'olam* — it will never acquire the status of consumed.

### Location Matters?

Both Rava and Rav Chisda share a common assumption: the critical time periods confer the status of "consumed" only when the meat is actually atop the *mizbe'ach*. If the meat was elsewhere — whether at the foot of the altar or on the ground — neither midnight nor daybreak produces that reclassification while it is off the *mizbe'ach*.

Rav Chisda's consistent position is that only the time periods relevant to Sunday's sacrifices can affect Sunday's meat: Sunday midnight and Sunday *alot hashachar*. Monday's *chatzos* is relevant for Monday's meat; Monday's *alot hashachar* is relevant for Monday's *korbanot*. Once Sunday's windows have passed, no subsequent time has operative significance for Sunday's meat. Rava, by contrast, holds that each successive midnight retains the power to reclassify hardened meat, so there is always another opportunity.

On the question of why the meat does not simply become *notar* when it is lying at the foot of the *mizbe'ach* at daybreak: one possible answer is that it does in fact acquire *notar* status — but once it is placed atop the *mizbe'ach*, the principle of *mizbe'ach mekadesh* takes hold, and *notar* that has been brought to the top of the altar may remain there and be consigned to the fire. The sole thing that would definitively preclude returning it to the fire is if the meat were already classified as consumed. Whether an object that is wholly inedible but not yet ash would even acquire *notar* status is itself an interesting question, since *notar* presupposes the category of food — but in any event, once it is placed back atop the *mizbe'ach*, the *mekadesh* principle governs.

## Rav Yosef's Challenge

### Status Everywhere?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: וּמַאן לֵימָא לַן דַּחֲצוֹת בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ מְשַׁוְּיָא לְהוּ עִיכּוּל? דִּילְמָא כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּמַשְׁכְּחָא לְהוּ מְשַׁוְּיָא לְהוּ עִיכּוּל! שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: הִלְכְתָא כְּרַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this: And who shall say to us that midnight,** specifically when the limbs are **at the top of the altar, effects for them consumption? Perhaps anywhere that** the limbs **are found,** midnight **effects for them consumption.** The Gemara notes: **They sent from there,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yosef,** i.e., the passing of midnight renders all limbs consumed, regardless of their location at that time.

Rav Yosef presses on the shared assumption underlying the entire debate. Both Rava and Rav Chisda presuppose that *chatzos* changes the status of hardened meat only while it is atop the altar. But who says that is correct? *Man leima lan dechatzos beroash hamizbe'ach mashvei lei ikul?* — who told us that midnight reclassifies the meat only if it is at the top of the *mizbe'ach*? Perhaps — *deilma kol heicha d'mishtachach mashvei lei ikul* — wherever the meat happens to be, midnight confers the status of consumed upon it.

### Eretz Yisrael Rules

A ruling was then transmitted from Eretz Yisrael: *hilchesa keRav Yosef* — the *halacha* follows Rav Yosef. The status change occurs wherever the meat is located; the entire debate between Rava and Rav Chisda, which presupposed a location requirement, is built on a premise the *halacha* rejects.

### Tosafos Comment

Tosafos raises a pointed observation here. The *Gemara* records that "they sent from Eretz Yisrael" that the *halacha* follows Rav Yosef. One might have expected the *Gemara* to ask its standard question — why rule on matters relevant only at the time of the future Temple, since such rulings are purely theoretical? Tosafos explains: *perush yafeh amar* — the phrasing signifies that what Rav Yosef says is logically compelling, not that a formal practical ruling is being issued. Moreover, Tosafos notes, the *Gemara* will later reinterpret the positions in a way that shows they do not ultimately conflict with Rav Yosef's logic.

### Chiya & Bar Kappara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: פֵּירְשׁוּ קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת וְהֶחְזִירָן אַחַר חֲצוֹת – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין. וְכֵן תָּנָא בַּר קַפָּרָא: פֵּירְשׁוּ קוֹדֶם חֲצוֹת וְהֶחְזִירָן לְאַחַר חֲצוֹת – יוֹצְאִין מִידֵי מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** It **was also stated** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says:** In the case of limbs that **separated** from upon the altar **before midnight and** were **returned** to the altar **after midnight,** one **may not benefit** from them *ab initio*, **but** if one benefited from them after the fact he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, since the mitzva of burning is considered fulfilled after midnight has passed. **And bar Kappara also taught:** If **they separated** from upon the altar **before midnight and** were **returned** to the altar **after midnight,** the limbs **are removed from** being subject to liability for **misuse** of consecrated property.

The *Gemara* cites corroborating *Tannaitic* and *Amoraic* testimony. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba states: if the pieces fell off the *mizbe'ach* before midnight and were returned after midnight — *lo nehenin velo moalin* — midnight had the effect of removing *me'ilah* even though the meat was not on the *mizbe'ach* at the time. Bar Kappara teaches the same in a *baraita*: *pirshu kodem chatzos vehechzirun achar chatzos yotzin midei meilah* — if they came off before *chatzos* and were not returned until after, *me'ilah* no longer applies. Evidently, the status change effected by *chatzos* occurs wherever the meat is, consistent with Rav Yosef's position.

## Fatty Meat Exception

### Rav Papa Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְכִי מֵאַחַר דִּשְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם הִילְכְתָא כְּרַב יוֹסֵף, וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא וְכֵן תָּנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא – רַבָּה וְרַב חִסְדָּא בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּשְׁמֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: And since they sent from there** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yosef** that midnight effects consumption even for those items left off the altar, **and Rav Ḥiyya bar Abba says** this as well, **and bar Kappara also teaches** that this is the *halakha*, then **with regard to what do Rabba and Rav Ḥisda disagree?** Abaye **said to him:** They disagree **with regard to fatty** limbs, whose consumption is delayed due to their surrounding fat, and consequently midnight may not effect consumption for these limbs.

Rav Papa puts a sharp question to Abaye: if the ruling from Eretz Yisrael confirms Rav Yosef, and Rabbi Chiya bar Abba and Bar Kappara both attest that *chatzos* changes status regardless of location, what exactly remains as the subject of dispute between Rava and Rav Chisda? Their entire debate, compelling as it is, appears to have no operative case.

Abaye answers in a single word: *b'shmanim* — in fatty meat. Rashi explains that even after fatty meat has hardened on the outside, it is not yet considered consumed, because the fat retains some inner moisture that prevents it from reaching the status of ash. This creates a third intermediate category: harder than raw meat but not as fully desiccated as the standard hardened case.

### Clarified Scenario

The *Sfas Emes* and *Chazon Ish* offer a clarification that proves illuminating. The case is not simply one of fatty meat throughout; rather, the meat was still in a meaty, un-hardened state at the first midnight, and therefore midnight did not change its status at all — it was still too close to real meat. The meat was then returned to the altar and dried out further over the course of the night. By the time later critical hours arrive — the next *alot hashachar* or the following midnight — the meat has in fact reached the hardened state, and the question now is whether there is a subsequent time that will reclassify it as consumed. This resolves the conceptual difficulty: the first midnight was simply inapplicable because the meat had not yet hardened; the debate between Rava and Rav Chisda concerns what happens next.

### Debate Preserved

With this clarification, the positions of Rav Chisda and Rava remain live and meaningful. Rav Chisda holds that *alot hashachar* of the same night is the operative moment: if the meat was meaty throughout and had dried out only by dawn, that dawn renders it consumed — provided it is atop the *mizbe'ach* at that point. If it was not there at *alot hashachar*, no subsequent time applies. Rava, consistently, holds that the following midnight always retains the power to reclassify, and that one does not limit operative moments to those of the original night.

## Lina on Mizbe'ach

### Rabbah Asks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַבָּה: לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ, אוֹ אֵינָהּ מוֹעֶלֶת בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava raises a dilemma before Rabba:** Is the disqualification of **being left overnight effective** in disqualifying limbs that are situated **at the top of the altar** at dawn but were not placed on the pyre, **or is** it **not effective** in disqualifying limbs that are **at the top of the altar** but were not placed on the pyre? The Gemara clarifies: **What are the circumstances? If we say that** the limbs **have not descended** from the altar, then the *halakha* should be obvious: **Now** that with regard to limbs that were **left overnight in the Temple courtyard, you said** in the mishna (84a) **that** if they ascended upon the altar **they shall not descend,** then with regard to limbs left **on top of the altar,** is it **necessary** to teach that they shall not descend?

The *Gemara* opens a new inquiry: *ba'ei minei Rava mei'Rabbah* — Rava posed a question to Rabbah. *Lina mo'eles b'rosho shel mizbei'ach o einah mo'eles b'rosho shel mizbei'ach?* — does *linah* take effect at the top of the *mizbe'ach*, or does it not? The practical stakes are as follows. A *kohen* has meat that belongs to a *chatat* or an *olah*, sitting atop the *mizbe'ach* at daybreak. Does the arrival of daybreak render it *notar* even while it is physically on the *mizbe'ach*?

### Meat Falls Off

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא דְּיָרְדוּ לְשֻׁלְחָן מְדַמֵּינַן לַהּ; דִּתְנַן: אֲפִילּוּ הֵן עַל הַשֻּׁלְחָן יָמִים רַבִּים – אֵין בְּכָךְ כְּלוּם, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְקַרְקַע מְדַמֵּינַן?
>
> **Translation:** **But rather,** the dilemma is in a case **where** they were left overnight on top of the altar and **descended** from it after dawn. Do **we compare** limbs left overnight on top of the altar **to** the **Table** of the shewbread, and they are therefore returned, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Menaḥot* 100a): **Even** if loaves of shewbread, which are supposed to be replaced every Shabbat, remained **on the Table many days** beyond Shabbat, there is **nothing** wrong **with that,** and the loaves are not disqualified by being left overnight. **Or perhaps we compare** the limbs **to** flesh left on the **ground** of the Temple courtyard, which is disqualified when left there until dawn, and they are not returned.

The *Gemara* immediately sharpens the question. If the meat is still atop the *mizbe'ach* after daybreak, the question is less pressing — *mizbe'ach mekadesh* allows it to remain and be consigned to the fire in any event. The genuinely difficult case is where the meat was atop the *mizbe'ach* when daybreak arrived, and subsequently fell off. The question is: since it was on the *mizbe'ach* at daybreak, did the *mizbe'ach* somehow insulate it from acquiring *notar* status? If so, even after it falls off it may be returned and burned. If not — if *linah* applies even at the top of the *mizbe'ach* — then once it falls off, the rule of *yardu lo ya'alu* (what has descended does not go back up) would apply, and it could not be returned.

The *Gemara* explores an analogy to the *lechem hapanim* on the *shulchan*. There, the *mishnah* rules *afilu hen al shulchan yamim rabim ein bekach klum* — even if the loaves remain on the table for many days, it is of no consequence; they do not become *notar*. Rashi explains the scenario: if the *kohanim* failed to set out the *lechem hapanim* on *Shabbos* and only placed it on Sunday, the proper time for the *bizichin* offering has not yet arrived — it is *mechusar zman*, the loaves must be on the *shulchan* for a full seven days. The next opportunity to offer the *bizichin* and eat the bread would be the following *Shabbos*, by which point the loaves would have been on the *shulchan* for thirteen days — yet they do not become *notar*.

### Kli vs. Ground

The question underlying the analogy is whether the *mizbe'ach* is to be regarded as a *kli* — a vessel — in which case its sanctifying power might extend to preventing *linah*, or whether it is to be regarded as the ground itself, in which case it would have no such effect. A *kli sharet* in principle insulates from *notar*; the ground does not. The *mizbe'ach* occupies an uncertain middle position.

### Rabbah's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין לִינָה בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him: There is no** disqualification of limbs that are **left overnight at the top of the altar.**

Rabbah answers: *ein linah b'rosh hamizbayach* — *linah* does not take effect at the top of the *mizbe'ach*. Accordingly, if meat was atop the *mizbe'ach* when daybreak arrived and then fell off, it has not become *notar* and may be returned to the fire and burned. It is worth noting that the *Gemara* formulates this in terms of *linah*, distinct from the earlier formulation of *mizbe'ach mekadesh*. Whether Rabbah's ruling would extend to meat that is meant to be eaten by *kohanim* rather than burned on the altar is a separate question the *Gemara* does not resolve here.

### Baraita Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ, אוֹ לָא? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאִיתְּמַר: אֵיבָרִים שֶׁלָּנוּ בָּעֲזָרָה – מְקַטֵּר וְהוֹלֵךְ כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה. לָן בְּרֹאשׁוֹ שֶׁל מִזְבֵּחַ – מְקַטֵּר וְהוֹלֵךְ לְעוֹלָם. יָרְדוּ – רַבָּה אָמַר: יַעֲלוּ, רָבָא אָמַר: לֹא יַעֲלוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Did Rava **accept** this response **from** Rabba **or not?** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from that **which was stated:** With regard to **limbs that were left overnight in the Temple courtyard,** the priest may place them upon the altar to **burn** them **all night** long provided that they are placed there before dawn. With regard to a limb that was **left overnight at the top of the altar,** the priest may **burn** it **forever,** i.e., no matter how much time has passed. With regard to limbs that were left overnight on top of the altar and then **descended** from it, **Rabba says** that **they shall ascend,** while **Rava says** that **they shall not ascend. Conclude from it** that Rava **did not accept** the response **from** Rabba, as he holds here that limbs are disqualified when left overnight on top of the altar. The Gemara confirms: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is so.

A *baraita* addresses the case of *evarim she-lanu ba-azarah* — limbs of a *korban* that spent the night in the *azarah*. The *baraita* rules: if they remained at the top of the *mizbe'ach* — *lan b'rosh she-al mizbei'ach makater ve-holech le-olam* — they may be burned at any time, since *mizbe'ach mekadesh* applies continuously while they are atop the altar. If they descended — *yerdu* — this is precisely the case at issue: they were on the altar at daybreak and then came off.

### Rabbah vs. Rava

On this *baraita*, Rabbah says *ya'alu* — they may be returned and burned; Rava says *lo ya'alu* — they may not be returned. Rabbah holds that *linah* has no force at the top of the *mizbe'ach*, and so the meat never acquired *notar* status while it was there; having descended without becoming *notar*, it may go back up. Rava disagrees: *linah* does take effect even at the top of the *mizbe'ach*, and the meat is therefore *notar* upon descending, subject to the rule of *yardu lo ya'alu*. The *Gemara* notes that the *Rambam* rules in accordance with Rava on this question, against the intuitive reading.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לָא קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara confirms: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is so.

The *Gemara* concludes: *shema mina lo kibla minei* — it is clear that Rava did not accept Rabbah's position, for they debate it openly.

## Mekadesh Concept

### Principle Extended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַמִּזְבֵּחַ מְקַדֵּשׁ כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״הַנֹּגֵעַ בְּמִזְבֵּחַ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא מִזְבֵּחַ; כֶּבֶשׁ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל הַנֹּגֵעַ בָּהֶם יִקְדָּשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Just as the altar sanctifies** items, so too, the ramp and the service vessels sanctify items. With regard to this *halakha*, **the Sages taught:** The verse states: "Whatever **touches the altar** shall be sacred" (Exodus 29:37). From here **I have** derived **only** that the **altar** sanctifies items. **From where** is it derived that the **ramp** sanctifies items as well? **The verse states:** "And you shall anoint…**the altar [*et hamizbe'aḥ*]"** (Exodus 40:10), and the addition of the word *et* serves to include the ramp. With regard to **service vessels, from where** is it derived that they sanctify items? **The verse states** with regard to them: **"Whatever touches them shall be sacred"** (Exodus 30:29).

The *Gemara* now broadens the *mekadesh* principle beyond the *mizbe'ach* itself. Just as the altar sanctifies what comes upon it — enabling *pesulim* to remain and be burned — so too the *kevesh* (the ramp) and the *keilim sharet* (the service vessels) share in this sanctifying power. This extension is derived from explicit sources.

### Kevesh Derivation

The *baraita* states: *ha-nogea ba-mizbeach* — I know only that the *mizbe'ach* itself has this power. Whence do I derive the ramp? *Talmud lomar et ha-mizbeach* — the word *et* in the verse *umashachta et ha-mizbeach* ("and you shall anoint the *mizbe'ach*," which Rashi identifies as the verse in the passage concerning anointing, verse 37 of the relevant passage) serves as an expansive particle including the *kevesh*. There is a significant dispute among commentators as to precisely which verse is the source, but Rashi identifies it as the verse concerning the anointing that sanctifies.

### Keilim Sharet

For the *keilim sharet*, there is an explicit verse: *umashachta otam, kol ha-nogea ba-hem yikdash* — the verse that speaks about anointing the service vessels states explicitly that whatever touches them becomes sanctified. The same formulation — *kol ha-nogea yikdash* — that applies to the *mizbe'ach* applies to the *keilim sharet*, and so their sanctifying power is directly stated in the Torah.

### Reish Lakish Asks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת מַהוּ שֶׁיְּקַדְּשׁוּ אֶת הַפְּסוּלִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְהַכֶּבֶשׁ מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הָרָאוּי לָהֶן, כָּךְ כֵּלִים מְקַדְּשִׁין!
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish raises a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **whether service vessels sanctify disqualified** items? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: You learned** in the mishna that **just as the altar and the ramp sanctify** items **that are suited to them** even if those items are disqualified, **so too,** the service **vessels sanctify** items placed in them.

Reish Lakish posed a question to Rabbi Yochanan: *kol klei shares mahu sheyekadesh es hapsulin* — what exactly is the nature of the sanctification conferred by a *kli sharet* upon invalid items placed within it? Rabbi Yochanan initially replied that this is taught in our *mishnah*, since the *mishnah* states explicitly that just as the *mizbe'ach* and the *kevesh* sanctify what is fit for them, so too the *kelim* sanctify. But Reish Lakish pressed further.

### Valid L'Chatchila?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְכַתְּחִילָּה לִיקְרַב קָמִיבְּעֵי לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to him:** The mishna indicates that service vessels sanctify that which is placed in them in the sense that they may no longer be redeemed even if they become disqualified. **I raise the dilemma** with regard to whether service vessels sanctify disqualified items such that they may be **sacrificed *ab initio*.**

Reish Lakish clarifies his question: *lechatchila likarev kamiba'ei li* — what he wants to know is whether the sanctification by a *kli sharet* is powerful enough to permit *lechatchila* offering of otherwise invalid items. Concretely: if blood was caught in a *kli sharet* by a *pasul* person, the blood is invalid. May one now proceed *lechatchila* to throw that blood on the *mizbe'ach*? Does the *kli sharet* render the blood sufficiently sanctified that one may go forward as if the *psul* did not exist?

The analogy to the *mizbe'ach* itself is instructive: when something *pasul* is on the *mizbe'ach*, the rule is that it remains there and may be moved to the fire. But that involves no dislocation — it stays in place and is burned where it is. For a *kli sharet*, the proposed act of *mekadesh* would require actively transferring the contents to the *mizbe'ach* — a materially different step. Whether *kli sharet mekadesh* extends that far is the question.

### Rashi's View

Rashi approaches the question of what *kli sharet mekadesh* means if it does not mean permission to offer *pasul* material *lechatchila*. He identifies the operative *nafka mina* not as *me'ilah* (as one might initially suppose) but as the question of *pidyon* — redemption. Before an item is placed in a *kli sharet*, it may in principle be redeemed; once it is placed in a *kli sharet*, it can no longer be redeemed. This is the sense in which the *kli sharet* literally sanctifies — it raises the object's sanctity to a level that precludes redemption, without necessarily permitting its use for the *korban* if it is otherwise *pasul*. On Rashi's reading, *kli sharet mekadesh* means that the item is elevated in sanctity, not that the *psul* is thereby overridden for purposes of *avodah*.

### Psul Catching Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִים וְזָרְקוּ אֶת דָּמוֹ; מַאי, לָאו שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִים וְזָרְקוּ פְּסוּלִים?
>
> **Translation:** An offering **that** people **unfit** for performing the Temple service **collected and** then **sprinkled its blood** shall not descend from the altar if it ascended. **What, is it not** that the mishna means **that** people **unfit** for performing the Temple service **collected** the blood **and** people **unfit** for performing the Temple service **sprinkled** it as well, which disqualifies it from ascending the altar? But if the collection alone was performed by people unfit for performing the Temple service, although the offering becomes disqualified, those fit to perform the Temple service may sprinkle the blood and sacrifice the offering *ab initio*. The reason, apparently, is that service vessels sanctify disqualified blood such that it may be sprinkled *ab initio*.

The *Gemara* then cites a *baraita* case: *shekiblu psulim v'zarku es damo* — a *pasul* person caught the blood and threw the blood. The *mishnah* teaches that if this occurred, and the meat was subsequently placed on the *mizbe'ach*, the meat may remain, since *mizbe'ach mekadesh* applies.

### Both Actions Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, שֶׁקִּיבְּלוּ פְּסוּלִים. אִי נָמֵי שֶׁזָּרְקוּ פְּסוּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish rejects this proof: **No,** the mishna may be referring to two independent cases, i.e., **that** people **unfit** for performing the Temple service **collected** the blood, **or that** people **unfit** for performing the Temple service **sprinkled** the blood after it was collected by people who were fit for performing the Temple service. Accordingly, the mishna teaches only that if such offerings ascended the altar they shall not descend after the fact, but service vessels do not sanctify disqualified items such that they are offered *ab initio*.

Rabbi Yochanan reads the *vav* in the *baraita* as conjunctive — "and" rather than "or." A *pasul* person both caught the blood and threw it. This apparently redundant formulation has significance: if the *pasul* person only caught the blood, the blood is invalid — but since it is within a *kli sharet*, one might be permitted to proceed and throw it *lechatchila*. Only if the *pasul* person also threw it is the *avodah* definitively compromised. The implication of Rabbi Yochanan's reading is striking: catching invalid blood in a *kli sharet* might not disqualify the subsequent throwing; it is the invalid throwing itself that creates the *psul*.

### Unresolved Question

The *Gemara* pushes back on this reading. The word in the *baraita* is "or" — *shekiblu psulim ee nami shezarku psulim* — either catching or throwing suffices to render the meat *pasul* for this purpose. The attempt to derive from the *baraita* that blood caught by a *pasul* person in a *kli sharet* may be thrown *lechatchila* therefore fails as a textual proof. The larger question — whether *kli sharet mekadesh* is powerful enough to permit *lechatchila* offering of otherwise invalid matter, such that a *korban* would be entirely *kasher* — remains without a definitive *Gemara* resolution.

## Air Space of Altar

### Hovering Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲוִיר מִזְבֵּחַ – כְּמִזְבֵּחַ דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא? תָּא שְׁמַע: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁהַמִּזְבֵּחַ מְקַדֵּשׁ, כָּךְ כֶּבֶשׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara raises a dilemma: Is the **airspace** above the **altar considered as** the **altar** itself, whereby items that enter this airspace shall not descend from the altar, **or** is it **not** considered like the altar? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the mishna, which states: **Just as the altar sanctifies** items, **so too,** the **ramp sanctifies** items, and if they ascended upon it they shall be sacrificed upon the altar and shall not descend.

The *Gemara* opens a new question: *avir mizbe'ach k'mizbe'ach dami o lo* — does the air space above the *mizbe'ach* have the same legal status as the *mizbe'ach* itself? If one has *pasul* or *tamei* matter hovering in the airspace directly above the altar — say, thrown up and caught there without actually touching the surface — does *mizbe'ach mekadesh* apply to it? Is the *kohen* permitted to place it on the fire, or must he remove it?

### Moving Meat Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֲוִיר מִזְבֵּחַ לָאו כַּמִּזְבֵּחַ דָּמֵי – אֲוִיר כֶּבֶשׁ נָמֵי לָאו כְּכֶבֶשׂ דָּמֵי; הֵיכִי מַסֵּיק לֵיהּ מִכֶּבֶשׁ לְמִזְבֵּחַ? הָוֵה לֵיהּ יָרוּד!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that the **airspace** above the **altar** is **not considered as** the **altar** itself, then the **airspace** above the **ramp as well** should **not** be **considered as the ramp.** If so, **how** can one **elevate** the disqualified limbs of an offering **from** the **ramp to** the **altar?** The moment one elevates a limb from upon the ramp, **it is** considered to have **descended** from the ramp, and all disqualified items that descended shall not be returned.

The *Gemara* attempts to derive an answer from the established *halacha* that the *kevesh* too is *mekadesh*. Just as the altar sanctifies, so does the ramp. If the air space above the altar were not considered equivalent to the altar, then one would expect the same to be true of the ramp: the air space above the ramp would not be considered like the ramp. But this generates an immediate practical difficulty: *heichi masklei michevesh lemizbe'ach havei yerud?* — how could one physically transfer a piece of *pasul* meat from the ramp to the altar? The moment one lifts it up to carry it, it passes through the airspace. If the airspace is not considered part of the *mizbe'ach*, the act of lifting it constitutes removal from the *mizbe'ach* — and what has been removed may not be returned. The entire operation of bringing *pasul* matter from the ramp to the fire would be impossible.

### Dragging Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּנָגֵד לֵיהּ. וְהָא אֲוִיר יֵשׁ בֵּין כֶּבֶשׁ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ! רוּבּוֹ לַכֶּבֶשׁ – כַּכֶּבֶשׁ, רוּבּוֹ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ – כַּמִּזְבֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: The mishna is referring to a case **where** the priest **drags** the offering up the ramp, but it never enters its airspace. The Gemara challenges: **But** even if he drags it up the ramp, he must still lift it, as **there is a space between** the **ramp and the altar.** The Gemara responds: Since this gap is small, only a minority of the limb of an offering will be located over the gap as it passes from the ramp to the altar. Therefore, when **a majority of** the limb is **on the ramp,** the minority situated above the gap is considered **as** if it is on **the ramp.** Once **a majority of** the limb is **on the altar,** the minority situated above the gap is considered **as** if it is on **the altar.**

The *Gemara* proposes a solution: *denagdei* — one does not lift it at all, but rather drags it along the surface of the *kevesh* without ever lifting it, keeping it in continuous contact with the ramp's surface.

A new difficulty immediately presents itself: *veha avir yesh bein kevesh lemizbe'ach* — there is a small gap between where the *kevesh* ends and the *mizbe'ach* begins. When one drags the meat across this gap, part of it is suspended over empty space, not in direct contact with either the ramp or the altar surface. If the airspace is legally distinct from the altar, even this dragging would pass through a zone where the meat is, in some sense, off both surfaces simultaneously.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *rubo l'kevesh k'kevesh, rubo l'mizbe'ach k'mizbe'ach* — we apply the principle of majority. If the majority of the piece of meat is still on the ramp, the entire piece is considered to be on the ramp; if the majority is on the *mizbe'ach*, it is considered to be on the *mizbe'ach*. The small portion suspended over the gap is subsumed into the majority, and there is no problem. This resolution means the dragging scenario does not require the airspace to have the same status as the altar — the gap issue is resolved through *rubo*, not through *avir*.

### Chubur Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּפְשׁוֹט מֵהָא – הָא דְּבָעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא יֵשׁ חִיבּוּר לְעוֹלִין אוֹ לָא, תִּיפְשׁוֹט דְּיֵשׁ חִיבּוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **resolve from this** mishna **the dilemma that was raised by Rami bar Ḥama: Is there a connection of** limbs that **ascend** upon the altar, i.e., is an offering considered a unit such that even those parts that do not ascend the altar shall be considered as if they are upon it, **or** is there **not,** and each part is considered independent? Based on the previous answer, **resolve** the dilemma by saying **that there is a connection** of limbs.

The *Gemara* now notes a further implication: *tifshot meha d'ba'ei rami bar chama* — from this resolution one can perhaps answer the question Rami bar Chama raised elsewhere. Rami bar Chama asked: *yesh chibur l'olin o lo* — when bringing sacrificial matter up onto the *mizbe'ach*, do we treat it as a single connected unit, or do we evaluate each piece independently? The resolution through *rubo* presupposes that the piece of meat is treated as one unit — otherwise, the fragment suspended over the gap would need to be evaluated on its own, and the majority principle would be irrelevant. The implication, then, is that *yesh chibur l'olin* — matter being brought up is considered a single unit.

### Pigul Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא בַּר רַב חָנָן: וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ אֲוִיר מִזְבֵּחַ כַּמִּזְבֵּחַ דָּמֵי, עוֹלַת הָעוֹף דְּפָסְלָה בְּמַחְשָׁבָה הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Rav Ḥanan objects to** the assumption that the airspace above the altar is considered as the altar itself: **But if you say** that the **airspace** above the **altar** is **considered as** the **altar** itself, then with regard to **a bird burnt offering that** one **disqualified by** having the **intention** to burn it beyond its designated time, i.e., the day after it was pinched, **how can you find** the circumstances for such a disqualification to take effect?

Ravva bar Rav Chanan challenges from the opposite direction. If one maintains that the airspace of the *mizbe'ach* is equivalent to the *mizbe'ach* itself, a problem arises regarding *olas ha'of*: *olas ha'of de-paslah bemachshavah heichi mashkachat lah* — how could one ever bring about *pigul* through an improper thought while performing the *melikah* of a bird offering?

### Pigul While Hovering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קַלְטַהּ מִזְבֵּחַ!
>
> **Translation:** One's intent to burn an offering beyond its designated time disqualifies it only when he intended to burn it during a time that he may not do so. Since a bird burnt offering is pinched in the airspace above the altar, the **altar** has already **accepted it.** Therefore, even if one delayed burning it until the next day, it does not descend from the altar, like all other offerings that are disqualified by being left overnight.

The *Gemara* explains the difficulty. The *melikah* of a bird is performed at the top of the *mizbe'ach*. The *kohen* performing it is standing over the altar — and therefore, whatever he holds is in the airspace of the *mizbe'ach*. Now, the underlying principle of *pigul* is that a *machshavah* (thought) cannot be more severe than the act itself. If one actually followed through on the thought — for example, burning the meat two days hence — that act would not be problematic, since the meat is in the airspace of the *mizbe'ach*, which we are assuming is equivalent to the *mizbe'ach* itself, and *mizbe'ach mekadesh* would apply for as long as it remains there. If the actual deed is permitted, how can the *thought* of that deed constitute *pigul*?

### Rav Shimi Answers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: אַלְּמָה לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר: הֲרֵינִי מוֹלְקָהּ עַל מְנָת לְהוֹרִידָהּ לְמָחָר וּלְהַעֲלוֹתָהּ וּלְהַקְטִירָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi objects to** the objection raised by Rava bar Rav Ḥanan: **Why** is it **not** possible for a bird burnt offering to be disqualified through the intention to burn it beyond its designated time? **You find it** in a case **where he says: I am hereby pinching it in order to take it down** from the altar **tomorrow and** thereafter **sacrifice it and burn it.** If he takes it down from the altar the next day, he may not subsequently return it there for burning, as all disqualified items that have descended from the altar shall not be returned to it. Accordingly, even if the airspace above the altar is considered as the altar itself, a bird burnt offering can still be disqualified with such intention.

Rav Shimi bar Ashi answers: *alma lo* — the difficulty is misplaced. One can readily construct a scenario where a *pigul* thought would apply even to a bird offering performed in the airspace of the *mizbe'ach*. The scenario is: *hareini molek al menas lehoridah lemachar ulehaalosah lehaktarah* — the *kohen* performs the *melikah* with the explicit intention of removing the bird from the *mizbe'ach* after daybreak and then returning it to be burned thereafter. That act — bringing the bird down after daybreak and then returning it — would be *assur*, since it would have become *notar* in the interim. Since the thought envisions an actually impermissible act, it constitutes a valid *pigul* thought.

### Down & Up Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר: לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ; אֶלָּא לְרַבָּה, דְּאָמַר: אֵין לִינָה מוֹעֶלֶת בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – לֵיתָא לְמַחְשַׁבְתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the objection of Rav Shimi bar Ashi: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Rava, who says** that the disqualification of **being left overnight** is **effective** even when the offering is **at the top of the altar.** Accordingly, if he were to take the bird burnt offering down from the altar the next day, he may not restore it to the altar for burning. **But** this is difficult **according to** the opinion of **Rabba, who says:** The disqualification of **being left overnight** is **not effective** when the offering is **at the top of the altar,** and therefore even if the next day he removed the bird burnt offering from the altar, he must return it there to burn it, as it has not been disqualified. If so, **his intention is not** significant, i.e., he does not disqualify a bird burnt offering with such intention.

The *Gemara* notes that this solution works straightforwardly according to Rava, who holds that *linah* does take effect even at the top of the *mizbe'ach*: bring it down after daybreak, and it has become *notar*, so the thought of doing so later is a thought about something genuinely impermissible. According to Rabbah, who holds that *linah* does not take effect at the top of the *mizbe'ach*, the meat would not become *notar* if left there overnight — so the scenario requires refinement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבָּה נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ – כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר: הֲרֵינִי מוֹלְקָהּ עַל מְנָת לְהוֹרִידָהּ קוֹדֵם עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר, וּלְהַעֲלוֹתָהּ לְאַחַר עַמּוּד הַשַּׁחַר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shimi bar Ashi responds: **According to** the opinion of **Rabba as well, you find** a case of a bird burnt offering that is disqualified due to one's intention, **such as where he says: I am hereby pinching it in order to take it down** from the altar **before dawn and** to then **sacrifice it after dawn.** In such a case, where the offering is removed from the altar before dawn, even Rabba would agree that one's intention renders a bird burnt offering disqualified by being left overnight.

The answer is: *hareini molek al menas lehoridah kodem amud hashachar ulehaalosah le'achar amud hashachar* — the intention is to bring it down before daybreak (when it has not yet become *notar*) and return it after daybreak. That act too would be impermissible — since after *alot hashachar* it would then acquire *notar* status upon descent and could not be returned — and the thought of it therefore constitutes *pigul* even according to Rabbah.

### Birds as Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהָךְ גִּיסָא מִיהָא תִּיפְשׁוֹט – דַּאֲוִיר מִזְבֵּחַ כְּמִזְבֵּחַ דָּמֵי; דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אֲוִיר מִזְבֵּחַ לָאו כְּמִזְבֵּחַ דָּמֵי,
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shimi bar Ashi adds: Irrespective of the validity of the proof of Rava bar Rav Ḥanan, **in any event, resolve** the issue **to this side,** i.e., in favor of the claim **that** the **airspace** above the **altar** is **considered as** the **altar** itself. **As if** it **enters your mind** that the **airspace** above the **altar** is **not considered as** the **altar,**

The *Gemara* pivots. Having introduced the bird case, it observes that the birds can actually serve as positive evidence that the airspace of the *mizbe'ach* is indeed equivalent to the *mizbe'ach* itself. Specifically, the *Gemara* raises the case of a *chatat ha'of* that was *pasul* — performed *shelo lishma*, for instance — and asks how the blood-sprinkling (*haza'ah*) of such a *chatat* could ever be performed if one maintains that the airspace is not equivalent to the altar. The discussion of how this plays out will continue at the top of the following *daf*.
