# Bava Batra 114 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Inheritance as Din

### Overview & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבָּה בַּר חֲנִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: "וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו" – בַּיוֹם אַתָּה מַפִּיל נַחֲלוֹת, וְאִי אַתָּה מַפִּיל נַחֲלוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה. אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דְּשָׁכֵיב בִּימָמָא הוּא דְּיָרְתִי לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ, מַאן דְּשָׁכֵיב בְּלֵילְיָא לָא יָרְתִי לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ḥanina taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman:** A verse in the passage concerning the double portion inherited by a firstborn states: **"Then it shall be on the day that he causes his sons to inherit** that which he has" (Deuteronomy 21:16). The addition of the phrase "on the day" teaches that **it is** specifically **during the day** that **you may distribute inheritances, but you may not distribute inheritances at night. Abaye said to him:** That cannot be the *halakha*, as, **if that is so,** it ought to be **that** only **one who dies during the day is** the one from **whom his children inherit,** but with regard to **one who dies at night, his children do not inherit** from **him,** and this is not the case.

Daf 114 is a relatively brief daf, and it picks up from the bottom of 113b. We have shifted gears momentarily from the detailed tree of inheritance to some fundamental *halachot* about the concept of *nachalot* as a form of *din* and *mishpat*. The daf also contains a notable digression that has nothing directly to do with inheritance at all. Both threads are worth following carefully, as they illuminate the broader legal framework within which the laws of inheritance operate.

### Day vs Night Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבָּה בַּר חֲנִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: "וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו"
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ḥanina taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman:** A verse in the passage concerning the double portion inherited by a firstborn states: **"Then it shall be on the day that he causes his sons to inherit** that which he has"**

A *baraita* is taught before Rav Nachman: the verse *ve-hayah be-yom hanchilo et banav* — "and it shall be on the day he bequeaths to his sons" — is read to mean that the distribution of an estate takes place in the daytime and not at night.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דְּשָׁכֵיב בִּימָמָא הוּא דְּיָרְתִי לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ, מַאן דְּשָׁכֵיב בְּלֵילְיָא לָא יָרְתִי לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him:** That cannot be the *halakha*, as, **if that is so,** it ought to be **that** only **one who dies during the day is** the one from **whom his children inherit,** but with regard to **one who dies at night, his children do not inherit** from **him,** and this is not the case.

Abaye immediately exposes the absurdity of taking this statement too literally: does it mean that only if a person dies during the daytime do his children inherit? And if he dies at night, they do not? Clearly that cannot be the intent.

### Clarification: Din Nacha…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא דִּין נַחֲלוֹת קָא אָמְרַתְּ? דְּתַנְיָא: "וְהָיְתָה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לְחֻקַּת מִשְׁפָּט" – אוֹרְעָה כָּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כּוּלָּהּ לִהְיוֹת דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye suggests a different interpretation of Rabba bar Ḥanina's statement: **Perhaps you said** a distinction between day and night with regard to the **adjudication of inheritances,** as judges are permitted to sit only during the day. A proof for this distinction is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A verse in the passage concerning inheritance states: **"And it shall be for the children of Israel a statute of judgment"** (Numbers 27:11), teaching that **the entire portion was placed [*ure'a*]** together **to be** considered a matter of **judgment,** subject to the procedural rules of a court matter.

Abaye therefore redirects: perhaps what was meant is *din nachalot* — the adjudication of inheritance law. It is the legal proceeding, not the biological fact of death, that must take place during the daytime.

### Chukkat Mishpat

The *baraita* then supplies the textual basis. At the conclusion of the entire *parasha* of *Bnot Zelophehad*, the Torah states: *ve-hayetah li-venei Yisrael le-chukkat mishpat* — "this shall be for the children of Israel as an edict of judgment." The verse designates the entire *parasha* as *mishpat*, thereby establishing that *nachalot* constitutes a form of *din*.

### Rashbam's Chiddush

According to the Rashbam, the *chiddush* is simply that *nachalot* is classified as *dinei mamonot* — monetary adjudication. And since it is *dinei mamonot*, it follows the rule that all monetary adjudication must be initiated in the daytime. One might ask: what else could it possibly be? The Rashbam acknowledges the difficulty. Perhaps one might have imagined that the laws of inheritance are more a matter of clarifying facts — identifying the closest surviving heir — rather than a true legal dispute. But whenever there is a genuine contest, whether over the terms of a will or the fair distribution of assets among competing parties, it rises to the level of formal *din*. That, for the Rashbam, is the *chiddush*.

### Rishonim: Chok Status

Many *Rishonim*, however, go considerably further. The Rambam, the Rashba, and others focus not just on the word *mishpat* but on the full formulation: *chukkat mishpat*. The word *chok* evokes something closer to ritual law — a fixed, immutable ordinance that cannot be tampered with. This has immediate practical implications. A question that arises in every generation is whether secular inheritance law can govern the division of an estate, particularly in cases of intestacy, on the grounds of *dina d'malchuta dina* — the principle that the law of the sovereign governs monetary matters. The Rambam and Rashba answer emphatically: *chas ve-shalom*. Because *nachalot* is *chukkat mishpat*, it carries the weight of a *chok*, and *dina d'malchuta* has no purchase here.

The deeper reason they offer is structural. In ordinary *dinei mamonot*, two parties enter into a transaction with mutual intent; there is a meeting of minds, and that element of consent makes the legal framework somewhat malleable. Inheritance, by contrast, happens automatically at the moment of death. The deceased does not choose to transfer property — the transfer simply occurs by operation of Torah law. Since there is no underlying agreement between living parties, there is nothing subject to renegotiation by reference to secular convention. It is, in this sense, more like ritual law than like ordinary monetary law — and for that reason *dina d'malchuta dina* cannot override it.

## Rav Yehuda & Din Rul (II)

### Dinei Mamonot Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה, רָצוּ – כּוֹתְבִין, רָצוּ – עוֹשִׂין דִּין. שְׁנַיִם – כּוֹתְבִין וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **And** this is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Yehuda, as Rav Yehuda says:** In a case where there were **three** men **who entered** a room **to visit a sick person** and the sick person desired to write a will in order to distribute his property following his death, if the visitors **wish** to do so **they may write** his will and sign it as witnesses. And if **they wish, they may act** in **judgment,** i.e., they may act as a court in the matter, since there are three of them. Therefore, they can determine that the will has the validity of an act of court and transfer the property to the heirs in their capacity as a court. But if only **two** came to visit the sick person, **they may write** the will and sign it as witnesses, **but they may not act** in **judgment,** since three are required to form a court.

The *Gemara* now connects the principle of daytime adjudication to a more general rule articulated by Rav Yehuda. Any form of *din* must be initiated during the daytime. *Dinei mamonot* is the most lenient category: the proceedings must begin in the day but may continue and conclude at night. *Dinei nefashos* is the most stringent: both the opening and the conclusion of the case must take place during the day. The laws of inheritance, as *dinei mamonot*, therefore require at minimum that adjudication begin in the daytime.

### Three Visitors Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם;
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda says:** This *halakha* was **taught only** in a case where the three came to visit him **during the day,**

As an illustration of how this plays out in practice, Rav Yehuda introduces the following case: three men entered to visit a sick person — a *shochiv mera*, one who is on his deathbed — and in the course of that visit, the dying man gave some form of verbal distribution of his property. After his death, those three visitors must now somehow give legal effect to what they heard. The question is what role they may play.

## Matnat Shechiv Mera

### Rabbi Yochanan Position

Before continuing with Rav Yehuda's case, it is worth setting out the legal landscape governing deathbed transfers, which will occupy later *perakim* more fully. Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka takes the position that a dying person retains the power to redistribute his property among his heirs — though not to non-heirs. While the Torah's default rules dictate who inherits and in what proportions, Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka holds that the dying person has some latitude to reallocate shares among those who are already in the line of succession. Interesting questions flow from this: can he favor a daughter over a son? A brother over a child? How broadly is "heirs" to be defined?

### Shechiv Mera Definition

Setting aside Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka's position — which represents a minority view — the Rabbis developed their own institution of the *matnat shechiv mera*: a verbal bequest made on the deathbed. The conditions are specific: the person must be genuinely dying and anticipating death; he must give away his property by speech alone, without any formal *kinyan*; the bequest must cover all of his property; and he must not retract before he dies. If all conditions are met, the Rabbis declared this verbal instruction binding — *divrei shechiv mera ke-khtuvim u-kmesurim dami*, the words of a dying person are treated as if they have been written and delivered. This is an entirely rabbinic institution, but a robust one. The particular *chiddush* is that it takes effect not at the moment of speech but at the moment of death, and it applies even to property acquired between the time of the declaration and the time of death.

### Kinyan Bemiktzat

The third mechanism is not a rabbinic institution at all but rather the application of an existing legal tool — *kinyan* — to the deathbed context. The dying person performs a standard *kinyan sudar*, taking or giving a handkerchief (or similar object), thereby transferring a designated piece of property to the recipient. Crucially, the transfer can be structured to take effect at the moment before death. This is a classic *kinyan bemiktzat* — it operates only on property already in the person's possession at the time of the *kinyan*, not on property he might acquire afterward. And unlike *matnat shechiv mera*, it does not require giving away everything.

### Three Methods Summary

To summarize, there are three distinct mechanisms by which a person can control the distribution of his estate beyond the Torah's default rules: first, the redistribution among heirs according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, which is *de-oraita* in character and limited to those already in the line of inheritance; second, the *matnat shechiv mera*, which is a rabbinic institution based on verbal declaration and effective across all property including future acquisitions; and third, the *kinyan bemiktzat*, which uses the classic legal mechanism of *kinyan* and is effective only on existing property at the time of acquisition. In the case of the three visitors, it is not immediately clear which of these mechanisms the dying man employed. Most likely he gave a verbal *matnat shechiv mera*, though the possibility of a formal *maaseh kinyan* is also raised by the *Rishonim*.

## Rav Yehuda & Din Rul (II)

### Witnesses vs Beit Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בַּלַּיְלָה, אֲפִילּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה – כּוֹתְבִין וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין דִּין. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָווּ לְהוּ עֵדִים, וְאֵין עֵד נַעֲשֶׂה דַּיָּין.
>
> **Translation:** **but** if they came **at night, even** if **three** men came to visit the sick person, **they may write** the will and sign it as witnesses **but they may not act** in **judgment. What is the reason** that they may not act in judgment the next day? It is **because they are** already **witnesses** to the will of the deceased, **and** there is a principle that **a witness cannot become a judge,** i.e., one who acts as a witness in a particular matter cannot become a judge with regard to that same matter?

Returning to the case of the three visitors: after the dying man makes his distribution, those present must decide what role they are playing. The *Gemara* states that if they choose, they may function as witnesses — *ratzu kotvin* — writing up a *shtar* that records what they heard the man say. That *shtar* is then presented to a *Beit Din*, which adjudicates on the basis of it. Alternatively, if there are three of them, they may function directly as a *Beit Din* — *ratzu osim din* — and adjudicate the case themselves without requiring separate testimony, relying on the principle of *lo tehei shemia gedolah mire'iyah*: the direct perception of a *Beit Din* is no weaker than testimony presented through witnesses. If only two visitors were present, they may only serve as witnesses, and the case must be brought before a separate *Beit Din*.

### Daytime Requirement

Rav Chisda now introduces the critical qualification. The option of functioning as a *Beit Din* is available only if the visit took place during the daytime. *Lo shanu ela be-yom* — this was only taught with respect to daytime. If all three were there and it was daytime, they may choose to adjudicate directly.

### Night Visit Restriction

If the visit took place at night, then even if there were three of them, they may only serve as witnesses — *kotvin ve-ein osim din*. They cannot retroactively constitute themselves as a *Beit Din* that sat in judgment, because a *Beit Din* cannot open a case at night.

### Ein Ed Na'aseh Dayan

The reason is twofold. First, the case cannot be opened at nighttime — all *din* must begin in the day. Second, and relatedly, *ein ed na'aseh dayan* — one who has functioned as a witness cannot turn around and serve as a judge. If a person heard the dying man's declaration at night, his legal status at that moment was that of a witness; a *Beit Din* cannot be constituted from witnesses to whom the case has already been presented in their capacity as *edim*. This demonstrates concretely that *nachalot* is *din* — we have witnesses, we have a *Beit Din*, we care about whether there are two or three present, and we require that adjudication begin in the day.

This passage raises several important questions among the *Rishonim*. One concerns what actually designates someone as a witness. The Rashbam cites his grandfather Rashi, from a *teshuva*, who holds that if the dying man explicitly called the visitors in as witnesses — if he summoned them in that capacity — then they are labeled as witnesses, and *ein ed na'aseh dayan* applies even if there were three of them and they came in the daytime. Tosafot disagrees. For Rashi, the very act of designation as a witness is sufficient to preclude the role of judge; for Tosafot, the designation alone does not carry that legal consequence unless they have actually testified.

## Tosafot: Chok Nature

### No Forum Selection

Tosafot offers a different reading of the *chiddush* of *chukkat mishpat* — one that connects directly to Michael's question about whether the heirs can simply select their own *Beit Din*. Tosafot argues that the *chiddush* is not merely that *nachalot* is *dinei mamonot*, but that the heirs do not have the ordinary right of forum selection. In a standard *dinei mamonot* case, litigants may contest which *Beit Din* hears the matter, and there is established *halacha* about which venue governs. Here, there is room to argue that one heir might say, "I don't want those three men as my *Beit Din*; let's take the case to a different court."

### Imposed Jurisdiction

According to Tosafot, *chukkat mishpat* establishes that once a *Beit Din* has directly witnessed the bequest — once these three men were present and heard the declaration — they may judge the case even against the will of the heirs, even without the heirs' consent. The jurisdiction, in effect, is imposed. This is tied to the broader point that inheritance is not a classic transaction between identifiable litigants. When there is genuine uncertainty about who qualifies as an heir, who received a valid bequest, and what the dying man intended, the "parties" to the dispute cannot be neatly defined in advance. A *Beit Din* that witnessed the relevant facts may therefore step in and adjudicate regardless of the preferences of those who later assert claims.

### Rashi: Witness Label

Rashi's position, as cited by the Rashbam, is that the dying man's explicit designation of the visitors as witnesses — his having called them in to serve in that capacity — gives them the label of *ed*, witness, and from that point forward *ein ed na'aseh dayan* applies. Tosafot disagrees, and distinguishes between designation and actual testimony.

### Ed Types Discussion

This leads to a broader distinction among the *Rishonim* between two different applications of *ein ed na'aseh dayan*. The first is the case of *ed hame'id* — one who has actively come before a *Beit Din* and testified. Such a person cannot subsequently serve as a *dayan* in that same case; once testimony has been formally given, the roles of witness and judge are incompatible. The second is the case of *ed haro'eh* — one who merely witnessed the relevant facts but has not testified. This person may still serve as a *dayan*, since what the rule prevents is not his sitting in judgment, but his treating his mere observation as equivalent to the *Beit Din* having had the case formally presented. An *ed haro'eh* who witnessed a transfer at night is not prevented the following morning from sitting as a *dayan* if other *edim* come to testify before him. The key distinction is between having witnessed something and having functioned formally as a witness in a legal proceeding — and between whether the witnessing constitutes the opening of a case before a *Beit Din*.

## Kinyan Chalipin

### Retraction Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קִנְיָן, עַד אֵימָתַי חוֹזֵר? רַבָּה אָמַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בְּאוֹתוֹ עִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** § After mentioning Rav Yehuda's ruling the Gemara cites a related dispute. It **was stated:** With regard to **a transaction, until when** may one of the parties **renege** on the transaction? **Rabba says: As long as they are seated** in the same location they may renege on the transaction. **Rav Yosef says: As long as they are dealing with that matter,** i.e., they are still discussing that transaction, they may renege on it.

The *Gemara* now introduces a discussion that appears at first glance entirely unrelated to inheritance. Because one of the mechanisms for a deathbed transfer is the *kinyan bemiktzat* — which employs *kinyan chalipin*, the exchange of a *sudar* or similar object — the *Gemara* takes the opportunity to explore a question about *kinyan chalipin* more broadly: *ad ematai chozer* — how long after performing the *kinyan* may a party retract?

The scenario is straightforward: two parties have agreed on the sale of a car, say, and instead of performing *meshikha*, they do *kinyan chalipin* — one party takes a pen from the other, sealing the deal, in the presence of witnesses. The question is whether there is any window after this act during which a party may back out.

### Rabba's View

Rabba holds that one may retract *kol zman she-yoshvin* — as long as both parties remain seated together in the same room. Once they have risen and dispersed, the *kinyan* is final and irrevocable.

### Rav Yosef's View

Rav Yosef holds a more restrictive position: one may retract only *kol zman she-asukin be-oto inyan* — as long as the parties are still engaged in discussion of the same subject matter. Even if they remain seated in the same room, if they have moved on to other topics — discussing the weather, the ball scores — the *kinyan* is final and no retraction is possible.

### Gmirot Da'at Basis

The underlying principle that makes this question intelligible is the unique nature of *kinyan chalipin* among all the modes of *kinyan*. In all other forms of *kinyan* — *meshikha*, *hagbaha*, *chatzer* — the physical act itself carries symbolic weight: it demonstrates ownership, it reflects the reality of possession. There is a physical correlate to the legal transfer. In *kinyan chalipin*, by contrast, there is nothing demonstrated. The taking of a pen or a handkerchief does not show ownership of anything. The act is purely a signal — a signal that both parties have reached complete *gmirot da'at*, full finality of intent. The *kinyan* is valid not because of what the act symbolizes physically, but because the exchange is understood as an agreed-upon mechanism for expressing unconditional commitment to the deal.

This means that *kinyan chalipin* operates entirely in the domain of mental commitment. And if it operates entirely in the domain of intent, then the question of whether that intent is truly complete becomes live in a way that it is not with other forms of *kinyan*. With *hagbaha*, once the object is lifted, the physical reality has changed and the legal change follows necessarily. With *chalipin*, everything depends on the inner state of the parties — and it is therefore conceivable that for a brief moment after the act, the commitment has not fully crystallized. That window is what Rabba and Rav Yosef are debating.

### Application & Reconcilia…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּווֹתִי דִּידִי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה – רָצוּ כּוֹתְבִין, רָצוּ עוֹשִׂין דִּין. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין, לֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: It stands to reason in accordance with my opinion, as Rav Yehuda says:** In a case where there were **three** men **who entered** a room **to visit a sick person** and the sick person desires to write a will in order to distribute his property following his death, if the visitors **wish** to do so **they may write** his will and sign it as witnesses. And if **they wish, they may act** in **judgment. And if it enters your mind** that one may renege on a deal **as long as they are seated** in the same location, then **let us be concerned** that **perhaps he will renege on** his decision. How can the visitors act in judgment in a case where the matter has not been resolved, as the sick person may still change his mind?

The *Gemara* now ties this dispute back to the case of the three visitors. Rav Yosef himself argues that the *halacha* of the three visitors — namely, that they may function as a *Beit Din* and adjudicate the case right then and there — is actually evidence for his position over Rabba's. If, as Rabba holds, one may retract as long as the parties are still sitting in the same room, then why would the visitors be able to adjudicate immediately? The dying man performed his *kinyan* and is still in the same room — according to Rabba, he could retract at any moment. It would be premature and futile to open the case when he might still undo everything he just did.

Rav Yosef, however, can explain the case cleanly: once the dying man completes the *kinyan* and the conversation moves on to other matters — once they are no longer *asukin be-oto inyan* — the transfer is final. At that point the *Beit Din* can convene and adjudicate without any concern that the man will retract.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא; וּלְרַב יוֹסֵף מִי נִיחָא?! וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא הָדַר בֵּיהּ! אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר – דִּיסְלִיקוּ מֵעִנְיָנָא לְעִנְיָנָא; הָכִי נָמֵי, דְּקָמוּ וַהֲדַר יְתִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I stated this *halakha* before Rav Kahana** and I asked him: **And according to** the opinion of **Rav Yosef, does it work out well? But** even according to his opinion there **should be a concern** that **perhaps he will renege on** his decision, as Rav Yosef holds that one may renege on a transaction as long as the two parties are still dealing with that matter. **Rather, what have you to say?** That according to Rav Yosef, Rav Yehuda's ruling applies **where they withdrew from** discussing that **matter to** discuss a different **matter,** so he can no longer renege on his decision. **So too,** according to Rabba, one can explain that Rav Yehuda was referring to a case **where they arose and then sat** down again.

Rav Ashi reports that he presented this argument to Rav Kahana, who immediately challenged: does Rav Yosef really do better here? Even according to Rav Yosef, if they are sitting in judgment while still discussing the very same matter, the man could theoretically retract — they are still *asukin be-oto inyan*. Rav Kahana resolves this by saying that in the case of the visitors, the judgment takes place after the topic has shifted — *d'asliku me-inyana le-inyana* — so by the time they convene as a *Beit Din*, the *kinyan* is already irrevocable. And he offers a parallel resolution for Rabba: one can construct the case so that the visitors left the room — *kamu ve-hadar yasivu* — before reconvening to adjudicate. Once they have all risen and dispersed, even Rabba concedes that retraction is no longer possible, and the *Beit Din* can then sit to execute what was already finalized.

### Halakha Rules Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בְּשָׂדֶה, עִנְיָן וּמֶחֱצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Yosef in** the disputes concerning the division of **a field,** discussed above (12b), reneging while they are discussing the same **matter,** discussed here, **and a half** of one's property, discussed below (143a).

The *Gemara* rules *ve-hilkhata ke-vosei de-Rav Yosef* — the *halacha* follows Rav Yosef. And it specifies that this ruling applies in three cases within *Bava Batra*, remembered by the mnemonic *sadeh, inyan, u-mechtza*: the case of the division of a field from an earlier passage; our case of *asukin be-oto inyan*; and a later case of *mechtza*, where a person instructs that his property be given to his wife and his son, and the ruling is that this means half to each. Tosafot notes that the phrase "we rule like Rav Yosef in these three cases" does not necessarily imply that we rule like Rabba in all other disputes between them. The formulation might simply be identifying three specific cases within this *masechta* where Rav Yosef's position prevails — not establishing a general preference for Rabba.

## Chain of Inheritance

### Mishna Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה אֶת בְּנָהּ וְכוּ׳. הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא: הָאִישׁ אֶת אִמּוֹ, וְהָאִישׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **a woman** bequeaths to **her son,** her husband, and her maternal uncles, but she does not inherit from them. The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **this as well? But it is** already **taught** in **the former clause: A man** inherits from **his mother and a man** inherits from **his wife.** The *halakha* stated in this clause seems to be the same as that of the other clause.

With the various digressions concluded, the *Gemara* returns to the chain of inheritance and addresses what appears to be a redundancy in the *Mishna*. The *Mishna* on 108a stated: *ha-ish et imo ve-ha-ish et ishto nochalim ve-lo manchilim* — a man inherits from his mother and from his wife, but they do not inherit from him. His son inherits, his brother inherits, his father inherits — not his mother, not his wife. The *Mishna* then continued: *ha-ishah et benah ve-ha-ishah et ba'alah manchilim ve-lo nochalim* — a woman and her son, a woman and her husband, they cause the inheritance to pass on, but they themselves do not inherit in return.

### Redundancy Question

The *Gemara* asks: why state the second clause at all? It is the exact mirror image of the first. The first clause says these parties inherit going one direction but not the other; the second clause simply reverses the perspective and says the same thing from the other side. Why is this not mere redundancy?

### Bakever Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּאִשָּׁה אֶת בְּנָהּ, דּוּמְיָא דְּאִשָּׁה אֶת בַּעְלָהּ; מָה אִשָּׁה אֶת בַּעְלָהּ – אֵין הַבַּעַל יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בַּקֶּבֶר, אַף אִשָּׁה אֶת בְּנָהּ – אֵין הַבֵּן יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִמּוֹ בַּקֶּבֶר, לְהַנְחִיל לָאַחִים מִן הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This teaches us that** the *halakha* of **a woman** who bequeaths to **her son** is **similar to that** of **a woman** who bequeaths to **her husband: Just as** with regard to **a woman** who bequeaths to **her husband, the husband does not inherit** property through **his wife** while he is **in the grave,** i.e., if a husband predeceases his wife, then his relatives, such as children from another marriage, do not inherit the wife's property through him but rather the wife's own relatives inherit her property, **so too,** the same *halakha* applies with regard to **a woman** who bequeaths to **her son,** that **the son does not inherit** property through **his mother** while he is **in the grave** in order **to bequeath to his paternal brothers.** In both cases, the woman's own relatives inherit her property.

The *Gemara* answers: the second clause is not mere repetition. It comes to teach us that *ha-ishah et benah* — the relationship of a woman and her son — is analogous to *ha-ishah et ba'alah* — the relationship of a woman and her husband, with respect to the law of *bakever*, inheritance through the dead.

### Bakever Principle

What is the *bakever* principle? The husband does not inherit from his wife if he is dead — *ein ha-ba'al yoresh et ishto ba-kever*. That is, if the husband has already died and then his wife dies, we do not say: "Let us trace the estate as though it first passed to the husband and then from him to his heirs." Once the husband is dead, the marital relationship no longer exists. He was never a blood relative; the connection was created entirely through the institution of marriage. Death severs that bond. He and his wife are, in legal terms, as divorced — and if she died after a divorce, her estate would not pass to his family. So too, once he is dead, her estate does not pass through him to his relatives. He does not inherit *ba-kever*.

The practical consequence is this: suppose a woman has property and her husband has already died. He had a son from a previous wife. Should we say that the woman's estate notionally passes to the deceased husband, and from him to that son? No. *Ein ha-ba'al yoresh et ishto ba-kever*. The connection is severed. Her estate goes to her own family — her father, her brother, her son if she has one.

### Blood vs Marital Kinship

The contrast with blood relationships is instructive. If a man died leaving a son and a daughter, and the son subsequently died before the grandfather — so that the inheritance now passes from the grandfather to the grandchildren's generation — the deceased son's child would take priority over the surviving daughter. We apply *yoresh ba-kever* in that case: we trace the inheritance as though the son had lived to receive it and then ask who would succeed him. This is because the bond of blood (*korva*) persists; lineage is not severed by death. The son's child is the son's child regardless of whether the son is alive.

But the bond of marriage (*she'arut*) — the *basar echad* unity created by the marital relationship — exists only while the marriage endures. Death dissolves it entirely. And the *Gemara* now says: the same principle applies to the relationship of a woman and her son. If the son has died before the mother, we do not say that her estate passes notionally through the dead son to his paternal brothers — brothers who share a father with him but who are in no way related to her. He does not inherit *ba-kever* to take the property away from her family and deliver it to people she has no relationship with at all. *Yerushat ba-kever* operates where *korva* persists; it does not operate where the effect would be to transfer property entirely outside the relevant family.

## Mother Inherits (Radical)

### Rav Yochanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה – הָאָב יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת בְּנוֹ, וְאִשָּׁה יוֹרֶשֶׁת אֶת בְּנָהּ; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "מַטּוֹת" – מַקִּישׁ מַטֵּה הָאֵם לְמַטֵּה הָאָב, מָה מַטֵּה הָאָב – אָב יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת בְּנוֹ, אַף מַטֵּה הָאֵם – אִשָּׁה יוֹרֶשֶׁת אֶת בְּנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the *halakha* that a mother inherits from her son, the Gemara notes that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Rabbi Shimon: By Torah law a father inherits** from **his son, and a woman inherits** from **her son** if the father is no longer alive, **as it is stated** with regard to a woman receiving inheritance: "And every daughter who possesses an inheritance from the **tribes** of the children of Israel" (Numbers 36:8). Since the plural term "tribes" includes both her father's tribe and her mother's tribe, the verse **juxtaposes** the **tribe of the mother to the tribe of the father,** in that **just as** with regard to **the father's tribe a father inherits** from **his son, so too,** with regard to **the mother's tribe, a woman inherits** from **her son** if the father is deceased.

Having established the *bakever* principle, the *Gemara* now presents a position that is genuinely striking. Rav Yochanan, in the name of Rabbi Yehuda ben Rabbi Shimon, states: *dvar Torah ha-av yoresh et bno ve-ha-ishah yoreshes et bnah* — by Torah law, both a father and a mother inherit from a deceased son. This is a direct challenge to the ruling of the *Mishna*, which places the mother outside the line of inheritance entirely.

### Daughters of Z. Source

The source is the *parasha* of *Bnot Zelophehad*. The verse states *kol bat yoreshes nachala mi-matot* — every daughter who inherits an estate does so from the *matot*, the tribes. The *Gemara* reads this as establishing a parallel between the *mateh* of the father and the *mateh* of the mother: *mah mateh av yoresh et bno, af mateh em hi yoreshes et bnah* — just as the father's tribe inherits from a son, so too the mother's tribe inherits from a son. The *Bnot Zelophehad* precedent, which established that daughters could inherit in the absence of sons, is now extended upward: the same logic that recognizes female claimants in the descending generation is applied to the ascending generation as well, recognizing the mother as an heir to her son.

### Rashbam's Limitation

The Rashbam immediately limits the scope of this ruling. It cannot mean that the mother always inherits from her son — that would completely overturn the established structure. The Rashbam therefore reads it as applying only when the father is not alive. If the father is alive, the entire estate goes to him; only in the father's absence does the mother enter the picture as an heir.

### Tosafot's Objection

Tosafot is deeply troubled by the ruling even in its limited form, and articulates why. If a mother can inherit from a son whenever the father is deceased, the implications for the patriarchal structure of inheritance are severe. Consider: the entire system of inheritance is built around *mishpachat av* — family is defined through the father's line, and the estate is meant to circulate within the father's *shevet*. If the mother inherits, the estate passes into her family, her *shevet*, and it may never return to the father's brothers and paternal relatives who would otherwise be the next in line. The Torah itself was concerned about property passing from *shevet* to *shevet* — that was the entire reason the daughters of Zelophehad were told to marry within their tribe. And yet this ruling would allow property to migrate out of the patriarchal family with extraordinary ease: a man marries a woman from a different tribe, he dies, his property passes to the son, the son dies, and the property goes to the mother — to the other tribe — without any of the safeguards the Torah put in place. Tosafot notes that the concern about tribal transfer would apply at every generation and for men and women alike, making the concern over *Bnot Zelophehad* seem almost unnecessary if this principle were broadly operative.

### Tosafot: Ger Exception

So disturbed is Tosafot by this position that he — or rather Rabbeinu Tam and Ri — radically reinterprets it. They argue that Rav Yochanan's ruling applies only in the case of a *giyoret* — a female convert — who has a son. The convert has no halakhic relatives on her side; her family connections before conversion are severed. If her son dies, his estate would normally become *hefker* — ownerless — since a *ger* who dies without heirs leaves property that no one can claim. But since he does have one halakhic relative — his mother, the *giyoret* — the ruling is that she inherits. The *chiddush*, on this reading, is not a general principle that mothers inherit from sons, but a narrow rule that prevents the estate of a *ger* from becoming *hefker* when the only surviving relative is his converted mother. This interpretation is admittedly not *pshat* — it does not follow naturally from the text — but it shows how disruptive Tosafot considers the straightforward reading to be.

### Assessment of Radicality

Whatever one makes of Tosafot's reinterpretation, the position attributed to Rav Yochanan is genuinely radical. The reasoning from *Bnot Zelophehad* is applied not only downward — establishing that daughters can inherit in the absence of sons — but upward, establishing that a mother stands in a parallel position to a father in the ascending generation. This cuts against the foundational principle that *mishpachat av kruyah mishpachah, mishpachat em einah kruyah mishpachah* — family identity is traced through the father, and the mother's lineage does not define one's tribal or familial belonging. Rav Yochanan's view essentially challenges that asymmetry, insisting that the same equality extended to daughters by *Bnot Zelophehad* must be extended to mothers as well. How the *Gemara* will reckon with this position, and how it interacts with the *Mishna*'s explicit ruling to the contrary, will be the subject of the continuation.
