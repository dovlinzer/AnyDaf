# Bava Kamma 90 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mechila & Debt Markets

### Debt Sale Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי מָחֲלָה לְגַבֵּי בַעַל – לָא קָא מַפְסֵיד, דְּהַשְׁתָּה נָמֵי לָא מִידֵּי קָא יָהֲבָה לֵיהּ! סוֹף סוֹף, כֹּל לְגַבֵּי בַעַל – וַדַּאי מָחֲלָה, וְאַטְרוֹחֵי בֵּי דִינָא בִּכְדִי לָא מַטְרְחִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** so **that** even **if she** were to **forgive** the debt of her marriage contract **with regard to** her **husband,** the one she injured **would not lose** anything, **as now as well she is not giving him any** payment, one could respond that **ultimately,** in **any** matter that will engender a benefit **to** her **husband, she will certainly forgive** the debt with regard to him. **And we do not trouble the courts** to supervise the sale of the financial advantage **without cause.**

The daf under discussion is 90, though we begin by picking up on 89b. Before proceeding to the *Gemara* proper, it is worth pausing on a *Tosfos* that bears directly on a topic raised in the previous session: the problem of *mechila* as it applies to the sale of debt.

The underlying scenario is familiar from commercial life. If Reuven owes a thousand dollars through a *shtar*, and the holder of that *shtar* is uncertain whether he will be able to collect — or simply prefers liquidity — he may sell the debt at a discount to a third party. This is, of course, the basis of the modern debt market: credit card debt, bank debt, and countless other instruments are bought and sold in precisely this fashion. The buyer pays less than face value and hopes to collect the full amount.

### Market Destruction Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: וְכֵן הִיא שֶׁחָבְלָה בְּבַעְלָהּ – לֹא הִפְסִידָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, אַמַּאי? תְּזַבְּנִינַּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לִכְתוּבְּתַהּ לְבַעְלַהּ בְּטוֹבַת הֲנָאָה – בְּהָא חֲבָלָה, דְּאִי מָחֲלָה לְגַבֵּי בַעַל, לֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the reason she does not sell the financial advantage of her marriage contract to obtain money with which to pay the injured party is that she would then forgive the debt of the marriage contract, then with regard to **that which is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 9:22): **And similarly,** in a case where **she** was the one **who injured her husband she has not lost her marriage contract** as a result, as he does not collect compensation from the properties she brought into the marriage that are enumerated in the marriage contract, **why** does she not lose it? **Let her sell the financial advantage of her marriage contract to her husband** in order to obtain the money to compensate him **for that injury** that she caused him, **as, if she will forgive** the debt of her marriage contract **with regard to her husband there will be no loss** for the purchaser, i.e., her husband.

The Talmudic problem, however, is that the original creditor retains the legal power of *mechila* — the ability to forgive the debt. If Michael purchases a debt that Reuven owes to the lecturer, and the lecturer then approaches Reuven and says, "Give me a hundred dollars and I will forgive the debt," Reuven pays a nominal sum, the lecturer declares *mochel*, and Michael — who purchased the debt through the original creditor's standing — can no longer collect. As Shmuel rules, the buyer of a *shtar* collects only derivatively through the original holder, and a *mechila* by that original holder is effective even post-assignment.

The consequence, as one might immediately recognize, is devastating for any market in debt instruments. If the original creditor can always unilaterally extinguish the debt through *mechila*, no rational buyer would purchase a *shtar*. The entire enterprise of trading in debt collapses.

### Tosfos Solutions

*Tosfos* addresses this problem directly at the top of 89b, in the middle of the *Tosfos* beginning on the previous page. The *dibbur hamatchil* is not at the top of the amud; the relevant passage begins with the fourth line, the first full-width line: *v'im tomar*. *Tosfos* raises the objection: since we know that a woman would almost certainly be *mochel* her *kesubah* for her husband's sake — that is, she would forgive it rather than have him face the burden of payment — why does the *Gemara* bother assessing how much someone would pay for such a *kesubah*? No one would be foolish enough to purchase it, since *mechila* is all but certain.

*Tosfos* then offers the resolution. He writes: *v'yesh lomar de-hacha yechola la'asos kiyum be-mashkonot* — one can establish mechanisms that prevent *mechila* from occurring. *Tosfos* enumerates four such mechanisms: a *mashkon* (collateral), *arevut* (a cosigner), a *shvua* (oath), and a *neder* (vow). The *shvua* and *neder* are the most straightforward: the original creditor swears or vows not to forgive the debt. The precise operative mechanisms of the *mashkon* and *arevut* are not spelled out by *Tosfos*, but the basic concept is that some form of security arrangement or third-party guarantee ensures that even if *mechila* were attempted, the buyer of the debt would not be left without recourse.

*Tosfos* then wonders why, in the specific case before the *Gemara*, we do not simply require the woman to take a *shvua* or *neder* to protect the buyer of her *kesubah* — a question he leaves as *tzarich iyun*. But the main analytical contribution is clear: *Tosfos* recognizes that the *mechila* problem is not an insuperable barrier to debt markets, but can be addressed through contractual or legal instruments external to the *shtar* itself.

### Historical Significance

What makes this *Tosfos* historically remarkable is the context in which it was written. The sophisticated financial system that depends on the tradability of debt instruments — the system that underlies modern banking, capital markets, and the very concept of liquidity — would not be developed for centuries after *Tosfos*. Yet here, in the Middle Ages, *Tosfos* is already thinking through the structural problem and sketching the mechanisms necessary to make such markets function. The ability to have meaningful currency in a broader economic sense depends precisely on one's ability to deal with debt, and *Tosfos* saw that problem and its solutions with remarkable clarity.

## Takanas Usha Debate

### Woman's Sales Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תַּקָּנַת אוּשָׁא תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? דְּתָנֵי חֲדָא: עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג יוֹצְאִין בְּשֵׁן וָעַיִן לָאִשָּׁה, אֲבָל לֹא לָאִישׁ. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: לֹא לָאִישׁ וְלֹא לָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the earlier discussion and suggests: **Shall we say** that the issue whether or not there was **an ordinance of Usha** instituting that the wife cannot sell her usufruct property **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*? As it is taught** in **one** *baraita*: Canaanite **slaves** that the woman brought into the marriage as **usufruct** property **are emancipated by** having **a tooth** knocked out **or an eye** blinded **by the woman,** i.e., the wife, as is the *halakha* when the owner of a slave knocks out the slave's tooth or blinds his eye, **but** they are **not** emancipated by having a tooth knocked out or an eye blinded **by the man,** i.e., the husband, as he is not their owner. **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: The slave is **not** emancipated if his tooth was knocked out or his eye was blinded, **nor by the man nor by the woman.**

The *Gemara* now turns to the question of *takanas Usha* and the rights of a woman with respect to her *nichsei milugs*. The relevant issue is whether a woman can sell her *nichsei milugs* — property she owns within the marriage over which her husband enjoys the *peiros*, the usufruct. The husband's position is defined by *kinyan peiros*: he has the right to the produce or benefit of the property. The woman holds title, *kinyan haguf*. The question is whether *kinyan peiros kekinyan haguf dami* — whether ownership of the *peiros* is tantamount to ownership of the body of the property.

If *kinyan peiros lav kekinyan haguf dami*, as Reish Lakish holds, then in principle the woman should be able to sell her *nichsei milugs*, since she holds title and the husband's interest is merely usufructuary. But *takanas Usha* — an enactment attributed to the rabbinic convocation at Usha, shortly after the period of Kerem beYavneh — ostensibly grants the husband greater rights over his wife's property than he technically deserves under the law, potentially blocking the wife from such sales. The question driving the *Gemara* here is whether the disagreement we find in the sources is really a debate about *takanas Usha* or about the underlying principle of *kinyan peiros*.

### First Braita

The first *braita* teaches: *avdei milugs yotzin b'shein v'ayin l'isha* — slaves held as *nichsei milugs* go free if the woman knocks out their eye or tooth, but not if the husband does so. The implication is clear: the woman is considered the halachic owner of these slaves, and it is the owner's act of knocking out an eye or tooth — the *halacha* of *shein v'ayin* — that triggers manumission.

### Second Braita

A second *braita* teaches the opposite: *lo l'ish v'lo l'isha* — neither the husband nor the wife can free such slaves through *shein v'ayin*. This creates an apparent contradiction that the *Gemara* must resolve.

### Gemara Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי; מַאי, לָאו בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לָאִשָּׁה – לֵית לֵיהּ תַּקָּנַת אוּשָׁא, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא לָאִישׁ וְלֹא לָאִשָּׁה – אִית לֵיהּ תַּקָּנַת אוּשָׁא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its analysis: **They assumed that everyone** holds that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce,** which the husband has with regard to usufruct property, is **not like ownership of the item itself,** which is why the husband is not considered the owner of the slaves. **What, is it not** so that the two *baraitot* **disagree with regard to this: That the one who says** that the slave is emancipated if he was struck **by the woman** holds that **there is no ordinance of Usha?** Since she retains the right to sell her usufruct property, she is considered the full owner of the slaves with regard to the *halakha* of their being emancipated. **And the one who says** that the slave is **not** emancipated if he was struck **by the man and not** if he was struck **by the woman** holds that **there is an ordinance of Usha.** Since she cannot sell her usufruct property, she is not considered to be the full owner of the slaves with regard to the *halakha* of their being emancipated.

The *Gemara* begins its analysis with an assumption: *savruha de'kulei alma kinyan peiros lav kekinyan haguf dami* — let us assume that everyone agrees with Reish Lakish that *kinyan peiros* alone does not constitute ownership of the object itself. Given that starting point, what is the debate between the two *braisos* about?

The first suggestion is that this is a dispute about *takanas Usha* itself. The *braita* that allows the wife to free the slave through *shein v'ayin* represents the view that holds there is no *takanas Usha* — the wife has title, the husband has only *peiros*, and the wife may therefore exercise full ownership rights including the ability to free the slave. The *braita* that denies this power to both husband and wife represents the view that accepts *takanas Usha*, which grants the husband a blocking power over the wife's disposition of the property. Under *takanas Usha*, the wife cannot unilaterally free the slave because the husband can veto it, and the husband cannot free it because the title belongs to the wife. The result is that neither party can effect manumission.

The *Gemara* considers whether this debate might instead be attributed to different historical periods — one *braita* reflecting the law before *takanas Usha* and the other after. It further entertains the possibility that both *braisos* reflect the post-*takana* reality.

### Rava's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי לְאַחַר תַּקָּנָה, וְאִית לְהוּ תַּקָּנַת אוּשָׁא; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָאִשָּׁה וְלֹא לָאִישׁ – מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הֶקְדֵּשׁ, חָמֵץ וְשִׁחְרוּר – מַפְקִיעִים מִידֵי שִׁיעְבּוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers an alternative explanation: **And if you wish, say** instead that both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* are referring to the time **after the ordinance** was instituted, **and** the *tanna* in each *baraita* holds that **there is an ordinance of Usha. Rather, according to the one who says** that the slave is emancipated if he is struck **by the woman but not** if he is struck **by the man, what is the reason?** It is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rava, as Rava said: Consecration** of an item to the Temple, the advent of the prohibition of **leavened bread** taking effect upon a leavened food, **and** the **emancipation** of a slave **abrogate** any **lien** that exists upon them. Therefore, the emancipation of a slave resulting from the knocking out of his tooth or the blinding of his eye abrogates the lien that the husband has on the slave as usufruct property, leaving the woman as the solitary owner.

This leads to the key question: if we accept *takanas Usha* and the husband accordingly has the power to block the wife from alienating the property, how could the wife still free the slave through *shein v'ayin*? The *Gemara* answers: *k'de'Rava*, invoking Rava's principle that *hekdesh, chametz, ve-shichrur mafki'in midei shibud* — sanctification to the Temple, the transformation of *chametz* into something *assur behana'ah*, and the freeing of a slave each operate to sever a lien or encumbrance.

The principle requires elaboration. The ordinary rule is that a lien-holder's interest survives the transfer of property. If Michael holds a lien on my ox and I sell the ox to Dov, Michael can repossess it from Dov, just as he could have from me. If I donate the ox to the Temple treasury as *bedek habayis* — a straightforward transfer of ownership — Michael can likewise recover it from the Temple, since the Temple treasury acquired it subject to his lien. The transfer does not extinguish the lien.

However, if I consecrate the ox as a *korban* — as an *olah*, for instance — something fundamentally different has occurred. This is not merely a transfer of ownership but a transformation of identity. The ox is now a sanctified object of a distinct ontological category. To say that Michael can simply "take" it from this new status would be to say that his assertion of a proprietary lien can undo a *kedusha* — and that is not how liens work. The lien attaches to an object as it is; when the object undergoes a fundamental change of identity, the lien has no purchase on the new thing.

Similarly with *chametz*: if a non-Jew holds a lien on my *chametz* and it remains in my home over *Pesach*, becoming *assur behana'ah*, the non-Jew cannot simply assert his lien and take it, because the thing that now exists — something forbidden in all benefit — is a categorically different object from what he originally held a lien on. One qualification must be noted: as the *Yerushalmi* observes, the *assur behana'ah* argument cannot apply straightforwardly to the non-Jewish lien-holder, since *chametz* is not *assur behana'ah* to a non-Jew. The stronger version of the principle is the *hekdesh* case, where the identity transformation is absolute.

The most vivid illustration, however, is *shichrur* — the freeing of a slave. If Michael holds a lien on my slave and I free him, the slave becomes a *ben chorin*, a free person. How could Michael now assert his lien? To "take" the slave would require reducing a free person back to servitude. There is no mechanism by which asserting a monetary lien accomplishes that. The free person is a new legal and human reality; the lien, which attached to an *eved*, cannot follow him into his freedom.

### Shichru & Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרָבָא, וְהָכָא אַלְמוּהּ רַבָּנַן לְשִׁיעְבּוּדָא דְבַעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: **Shall we say** that this statement **of Rava is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*?** According to this explanation, the *baraita* that states that the slave is not emancipated when he is struck by the woman holds that the lien of the husband is not abrogated, which would run counter to the statement of Rava. The Gemara responds: **No;** it is the case **that everyone agrees with** the statement **of Rava** that a lien is abrogated, **and here** the reason why the *baraita* holds that the husband's lien on the slave is not abrogated is that **the Sages strengthened the lien of a husband** on his wife's property, and it is only this type of lien that is not abrogated by emancipation.

This is precisely the *Gemara*'s answer to our question. The woman is the halachic owner of the *nichsei milugs* slaves. When she knocks out the eye or tooth, the act of *shichrur* that follows is not a mere alienation of property — it is a transformation of identity. The husband's interest, however robust under *takanas Usha*, is in the nature of a lien or blocking power. But that blocking power cannot prevent what *shichrur* does, because *shichrur* does not merely change ownership — it changes the very being of the slave. The slave is no longer property at all; he is a free man. The husband's lien or veto right, like any *shibud*, is severed by the identity transformation.

A question arises: could the husband, by analogy, knock out the slave's eye and achieve the same result? The answer is no, because the husband is not the owner. The *Gemara* is careful to say that the mechanism of *shichrur mafki'a midei shibud* presupposes that one begins from the position of owner. The woman starts as owner; she knocks out the eye; the *halacha* is the slave goes free; the husband's interest cannot block that. But a random person — or the husband, who holds no title — cannot invoke the same principle because the identity-transforming act of *shichrur* can only be performed by the master.

The *Gemara* then asks whether Rava's principle is itself a subject of *tannaitic* debate — *leima de-Rava tanna'ei hi?* — since if everyone agrees with *takanas Usha* and the *braisos* diverge, the second *braita* must apparently reject the *shichrur* argument. The *Gemara* answers: no, everyone can agree with Rava. The second *braita* may hold that the Rabbis made the husband's interest under *takanas Usha* so powerful as to constitute something like partial ownership, not merely a lien — and if he is effectively a co-owner, the wife's act of *shichrur* might be blocked not as the assertion of a lien against an identity-change, but as the exercise of a co-owner's veto against a co-owner's decision. The exact parameters of that position — in particular, whether a co-owner who knocks out a slave's eye can effect manumission — would need to be explored separately.

### Kinyan Peiros Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – לֵית לְהוּ לְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי תַּקָּנַת אוּשָׁא, וְהָכָא – בְּקִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי קָמִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone agrees** that **these *tanna'im*** of the two *baraitot* **hold that there is no ordinance of Usha, and here, they disagree with regard to** the question whether **ownership of** the rights to use an item and to **its produce** is **like ownership of the item itself.** In this case, the husband enjoys the usage of the slave while the wife owns the slave himself. The *baraita* that rules that the slave is not emancipated when struck by the woman holds that the husband is also considered to be an owner, and the *baraita* that rules that the slave is emancipated when struck by the woman holds that the husband is not considered to be an owner.

The *Gemara* then offers yet another resolution of the two *braisos*: *kulei alma leit lehu lehani tanna'ei takanas Usha* — perhaps neither *Tanna* holds of *takanas Usha* at all. There is no *Usha*, no enactment, no blocking power granted to the husband beyond what he possesses under the basic law. Under this reading, what the *braisos* debate is the classic dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish: *be-kinyan peiros kekinyan haguf dami plugi* — whether *kinyan peiros* is equivalent to *kinyan haguf*.

Under this framework, *takanas Usha* is needed only if one accepts Reish Lakish's position that *kinyan peiros lav kekinyan haguf dami* — for only then does the husband lack ownership, and only then must some additional enactment shore up his rights. But if one holds like Rabbi Yochanan that *kinyan peiros kekinyan haguf dami*, then the husband is already, by the basic logic of the *halacha*, an owner. No *takanas Usha* is required to block the wife's disposition of the property — the husband's *kinyan peiros* already suffices.

This debate is then shown to parallel a *tannaitic* dispute of broader scope. The *braita* discusses the case of one who sells his slave to another but stipulates that the slave shall continue to serve the seller for thirty days. The seller, for those thirty days, holds *kinyan peiros* — he receives the service of the slave — while the buyer holds *kinyan haguf*, the title. Now suppose one of these parties beats the slave so severely that the slave dies. The *halacha* of *yom o yomayim* — the Torah's provision that a master who beats his slave is not executed if the slave survives for a day or two — applies only to the master. Who is the master in this divided arrangement?

Rabbi Meir holds that the first man — the seller who still has the slave's service for thirty days — is the one governed by *yom o yomayim*, because the slave is *tachasav*, directly under his control. The *Gemara* reads Rabbi Meir as formally holding *kinyan peiros kekinyan haguf dami*: his *kinyan peiros* makes him the full halachic master, more than the one who holds mere title. This is indeed a stronger version of the principle than encountered previously — here, *kinyan peiros* does not just give the *peiros*-holder equal standing with the title-holder; it actually displaces the title-holder entirely, rendering him irrelevant to the *yom o yomayim* calculus.

Rabbi Yehuda holds that the second man — the buyer who holds title — is subject to *yom o yomayim*, because *she-hu kaspo*, he is the one for whom the slave represents capital. The *Gemara* reads Rabbi Yehuda as holding *kinyan peiros lav kekinyan haguf dami*: the *peiros* rights are insufficient to constitute ownership; it is the title-holder who is the master.

Rabbi Yossi holds that both are subject to *yom o yomayim*. The *Gemara* declines to read this as Rabbi Yossi affirming that *kinyan peiros* creates a second, co-equal *kinyan haguf*; rather, the *Gemara* reads Rabbi Yossi as holding the matter *be-safek* — he is uncertain whether *kinyan peiros* rises to the level of *kinyan haguf*. Since we do not know who the master is, we would not execute either party. Crucially, the *Gemara* is not prepared to treat *kinyan peiros* as an equal form of *kinyan haguf* that simply adds a second owner; it insists the question is binary.

Rabbi Eliezer holds that neither is subject to *yom o yomayim*: the seller cannot invoke *yom o yomayim* because the slave is no longer *tachasav* — he does not hold title; the buyer cannot invoke it because the slave is not yet *kaspo* in the active sense — he is not yet working for him. The *Gemara* asks *mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Eliezer* — what is Rabbi Eliezer's source? The verse uses the language *ki kaspo hu* — because he is his property. From the extra word *hu*, the *Gemara* derives *kaspo ha-meyuchad lo* — property that is fully and exclusively his. For *yom o yomayim* to apply, one must have both title *and* active control. This mirrors the ruling of Rabbi Yochanan discussed elsewhere that neither the *gazlan* — who has possession but not title — nor the original owner — who has title but not possession — can be *makdish*, because the *halacha* requires both that the object be one's own and that it be within one's domain.

There is, it should be noted, something that feels somewhat artificial about making the *yom o yomayim* debate entirely dependent on the abstract *kinyan peiros* question rather than on the practical question of who has the right to discipline the slave at any given moment. The *Gemara*, however, insists on framing it formally, and this framing is itself significant.

### Ameimar Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר אַמֵּימָר: אִישׁ וְאִשָּׁה שֶׁמָּכְרוּ בְּנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג – לֹא עָשׂוּ וְלֹא כְּלוּם? כְּמַאן – כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose** opinion **is that which Ameimar says:** If there was **a man or a woman,** i.e., a husband or a wife, **who sold** the wife's **usufruct property,** which is owned by the wife but used by the husband, **they did not accomplish anything,** as the sale does not take effect? **In accordance with whose** opinion is his statement? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who holds that one is considered to be an owner only if he both owns the item itself and enjoys the usage of that item.

The *Gemara* then asks: *keman azla ha de-amar Ameimar* — whose view is reflected in the teaching of Ameimar? Ameimar rules: *ish ve-isha she-machru be-nichsei melog — lo asu ve-lo klum* — a husband and wife who jointly sell *nichsei milugs* have accomplished nothing; the sale is void.

One might have thought that since between them they hold all interests in the property — she has title, he has *peiros* — their combined signature would suffice to constitute a complete transfer. Why is this not the case? Rashi explains the distinction from ordinary *shutfin*: if two partners each own fifty percent of a property, each is the full and exclusive owner of his fifty percent. Either may independently sell his share, and together they can convey the whole. But in the case of *nichsei milugs*, the division of interests is not quantitative but qualitative. She holds title to the entire property; he holds *peiros* over the entire property. He cannot sell what he does not own — the *guf* of the property — and she cannot sell what would, absent a disposition of his *peiros* rights, leave the buyer with a property burdened by his usufruct. There is no mechanism by which two people, each lacking sufficient ownership standing, can pool their deficiencies to create a valid sale. For that to work, one party would need to first transfer his or her interest to the other — so that one person holds both *kinyan peiros* and *kinyan haguf* — and that person could then convey the whole.

### Full Ownership Req.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא לְהָא דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִי שֶׁחֶצְיוֹ עֶבֶד וְחֶצְיוֹ בֶּן חוֹרִין, וְכֵן עֶבֶד שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין – אֵין יוֹצְאִין בְּרָאשֵׁי אֵבָרִים שֶׁאֵינָן חוֹזְרִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי, הָכִי אָמְרִי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught that which the Sages taught: One who is a half-slave half-freeman, and similarly, a slave belonging to two partners, are not emancipated** when injured by one of their owners **in** one of their **extremities that do not regenerate,** i.e., the twenty-four extremities whose injury by an owner emancipates a slave? **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: This is what** the Sages **say in the name of Rava: It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer.**

This connects directly to the *shein v'ayin* analysis. A further *braita* is cited: *mi she-chetzio eved ve-chetzio ben chorin* — a slave who is half-free and half-enslaved — *ve-chen eved shel shenei shutafin* — or a slave owned by two partners — does not go free through *shein v'ayin*. The requirement is not only qualitative but quantitative: one must be one hundred percent the owner, not merely the qualitative form of owner appropriate to the *halacha* of *shein v'ayin*.

The *Gemara* asks whose view this represents, and Rav Mordechai cites in the name of Rava: *Rabbi Eliezer hi* — this is the position of Rabbi Eliezer, who derives from the same *pasuk* — *ki kaspo hu* — that full, exclusive ownership is required. Just as *yom o yomayim* requires a *kaspo ha-meyuchad lo*, so too *shein v'ayin* requires an *avdo ha-meyuchad lo* — his slave, fully and exclusively. Even if a partner owns one hundred percent of one half of the slave, that half-share of ownership does not suffice to free the slave through *shein v'ayin*. The *Gemara* acknowledges, through *Tosafos*, that this is a special *derashah* in the *pasuk* applicable to the specific *halachos* of *shein v'ayin* and *yom o yomayim*, not a general principle applicable to all areas of law, where ordinary partnership rules would of course apply.

## Boshet Damages

### Boshet Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל – הַכֹּל לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲפִילּוּ עֲנִיִּים שֶׁבְּיִשְׂרָאֵל, רוֹאִין אוֹתָם כְּאִילּוּ הֵם בְּנֵי חוֹרִין שֶׁיָּרְדוּ מִנִּכְסֵיהֶם; שֶׁהֵם בְּנֵי אַבְרָהָם יִצְחָק וְיַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle** of assessing payment for humiliation caused to another: It is **all** evaluated **in accordance with the honor of** the one who was humiliated, as the Gemara will explain. **Rabbi Akiva said: Even** with regard to the **poor among the Jewish people, they are viewed as though they were freemen who lost their property** and were impoverished. And their humiliation is calculated according to this status, **as they are the children of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob,** and are all of prominent lineage.

The *Gemara* now transitions to a new *mishna* dealing with *boshet* — the payment for embarrassment. The transition brings with it a striking irony. Throughout Bava Kamma, the framework for assessing damages has been inherently flexible and case-specific: one assesses the reduction in market value caused by an injury, the reasonable cost of medical treatment, the earning capacity lost, the pain and suffering that the victim would require compensation to endure. For *boshet*, the same logic would seem to demand a case-by-case assessment — how embarrassed was this particular person by this particular act? How much would he or she require to have willingly undergone this experience? The *mishna* immediately before our passage states the principle explicitly: *hakol lefi ha-mivayesh ve-ha-mitbayesh* — everything depends on the nature of the embarrassor and the one embarrassed.

Yet what we find, counterintuitively, is that *Chazal* attempted to set fixed amounts for *boshet*. Perhaps because subjective assessment of embarrassment was too difficult or too susceptible to manipulation, the tradition established a schedule of set payments for specific humiliating acts.

### Fixed Amounts List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַתּוֹקֵעַ לַחֲבֵירוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ סֶלַע. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: מָנֶה. סְטָרוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ מָאתַיִם זוּז. לְאַחַר יָדוֹ – נוֹתֵן לוֹ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז. צָרַם בְּאׇזְנוֹ; תָּלַשׁ בִּשְׂעָרוֹ; רָקַק – וְהִגִּיעַ בּוֹ רוּקּוֹ; הֶעֱבִיר טַלִּיתוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ; פָּרַע רֹאשׁ הָאִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק – נוֹתֵן לוֹ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who strikes another** must **give him a *sela*. Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** that he must give him **one hundred dinars.** If he **slapped** another on the cheek, he must **give him two hundred dinars.** If he slapped him on the cheek with **the back of his hand,** which is more degrading than a slap with the palm, he must **give him four hundred dinars. If he pulled his ear,** or **pulled out his hair,** or **spat** at him **and his spittle reached him,** or if **he removed** the other's **cloak from him,** or if **he uncovered the head of a woman in the marketplace,** in all of these cases, he must **give** the injured party **four hundred dinars.**

The *mishna* sets out the following: *ha-tokeia le-chaveiro* — one who strikes his friend on the ear — pays one *sela*, equivalent to four *zuz*. Rabbi Yehuda, citing Rabbi Yossi HaGlili, says the payment is a *maneh* — one hundred *zuz*, which is twenty-five times the *tanna kamma*'s figure.

To calibrate the magnitudes: a *kesubah* was two hundred *zuz*, an amount understood to represent roughly what was needed to sustain a person for a year. A *maneh* is therefore half that sum — a very substantial payment for a single act of striking someone's ear.

*Sotra* — slapping someone in the face — carries a payment of two hundred *zuz*, equal to the full value of a *kesubah*. This is for a slap in the face, presumably in public. Striking with the back of the hand — which carries a connotation of contemptuous dismissal — costs four hundred *zuz*, double the *sotra* payment. *Tzarmo b'ozno* — yanking him by the ear — is similarly enumerated. *Talash be-searo* — pulling his hair. *Rakak ve-higi'a bo roko* — spitting on him, with the spit reaching him. *He'evir taliso mimenu* — pulling off his cloak, leaving him exposed. And *para rosh isha ba-shuk* — uncovering a woman's hair in the marketplace — also carries a payment of four hundred *zuz*.

The *mishna* then appends: *ze ha-klal — hakol lefi kevodo* — this is the principle: everything depends on the person's honor.

### Rabbi Akiva's Dignity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל – הַכֹּל לְפִי כְּבוֹדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle** of assessing payment for humiliation caused to another: It is **all** evaluated **in accordance with the honor of** the one who was humiliated, as the Gemara will explain.

The tension is obvious. The *mishna* has just set fixed amounts, and then immediately declares that assessment depends on the individual's standing. The *Gemara* resolves this through Rabbi Akiva: *afilu aniyim she-be-Yisrael ro'im osam ke-ilu hem bnei chorin she-yardu min-nachaseihem she-bnei Avraham Yitzchak ve-Yaakov* — even the poor of Israel are viewed as if they were members of the free upper classes who have fallen from their wealth, being descendants of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov.

Rabbi Akiva is drawing a distinction between class and wealth. One may be financially impoverished while belonging, in terms of lineage and social standing, to the nobility. The analogy is apt: there exists such a thing as the impoverished aristocracy — people of distinguished lineage living in straitened circumstances. Their poverty does not diminish their *kavod*, their honor and standing. Rabbi Akiva argues that every Jew, as a descendant of the Patriarchs, is in exactly this position: even the poor are *bnei chorin she-yardu min-nachaseihem*, free people of noble lineage who have come upon hard times. Since the *boshet* assessment is keyed to *kavod* rather than to wealth, and since everyone's *kavod* is, as a matter of halachic principle, maximum, the fixed high amounts specified in the *mishna* apply universally. *Hakol lefi kevodo* and the fixed amounts are not in tension — the fixed amounts represent what *kavod* demands for everyone.

### Hair Uncovering Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁפָּרַע רֹאשׁ הָאִשָּׁה בַּשּׁוּק, בָּאת לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְחִיְּיבוֹ לִיתֵּן לָהּ אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, תֵּן לִי זְמַן. וְנָתַן לוֹ זְמַן. שְׁמָרָהּ עוֹמֶדֶת עַל פֶּתַח חֲצֵרָהּ, וְשָׁבַר אֶת הַכַּד בְּפָנֶיהָ, וּבוֹ כְּאִיסָּר שֶׁמֶן. גִּילְּתָה אֶת רֹאשָׁהּ, וְהָיְתָה מְטַפַּחַת וּמַנַּחַת יָדָהּ עַל רֹאשָׁהּ. הֶעֱמִיד עָלֶיהָ עֵדִים, וּבָא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא; אָמַר לוֹ: לָזוֹ אֲנִי נוֹתֵן אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת זוּז?!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna relates: **And an incident** occurred **involving one who uncovered the head of a woman in the marketplace,** and the woman **came before Rabbi Akiva** to request that he render the assailant liable to pay for the humiliation that she suffered, **and** Rabbi Akiva **rendered** the assailant **liable to give her four hundred dinars.** The man **said to** Rabbi Akiva: **My teacher, give me time** to pay the penalty, **and** Rabbi Akiva **gave him time. The man then waited for her** until **she was standing by the opening of her courtyard, and he broke a jug in front of her, and** there was the value of **about an *issar* of oil inside** the jug. The woman then **exposed her** own **head and she was wetting [*metapaḥat*]** her hand in the oil, **and placing her hand on her head** to make use of the oil. The man then **set up witnesses** to observe **her** actions, **and he came before Rabbi Akiva,** and **he said to him: Will I give four hundred dinars to this** woman for having uncovered her head? By uncovering her head for a minimal benefit, she has demonstrated that this does not cause her humiliation.

The *Gemara* illustrates this principle with a remarkable story. *U-maaseh be-echad she-para rosh isha ba-shuk* — there was an incident in which a man uncovered a woman's hair in the marketplace. The woman came before Rabbi Akiva, who obligated the man to pay her four hundred *zuz*. The man requested a week to pay, and Rabbi Akiva granted the extension.

The man then devised a scheme. He observed the woman standing at the entrance to her courtyard, the opening of her yard facing the *reshus ha-rabim*, the public domain. He contrived to drop a small flask of oil — worth about a *issar*, a trivial amount — in front of her. The woman, seeing the oil, uncovered her own hair, used her hand to collect the oil, and applied it to her hair right there in the public domain. The man had arranged witnesses to observe this. He then appeared before Rabbi Akiva: *l'zo ani nosen arba me'os zuz* — for this type of woman I am paying four hundred *zuz*? She uncovered her own hair for a dollar's worth of oil.

### Objective Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא אָמַרְתָּ כְּלוּם. הַחוֹבֵל בְּעַצְמוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי – פָּטוּר; אֲחֵרִים שֶׁחָבְלוּ בּוֹ – חַיָּיבִים. וְהַקּוֹצֵץ נְטִיעוֹתָיו, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי – פָּטוּר; אֲחֵרִים – חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: You did not say anything,** i.e., this claim will not exempt you. **One who injures himself, although it is not permitted for him** to do so, is nevertheless **exempt** from any sort of penalty, but **others who injured him** are **liable** to pay him. In this case as well, the man was liable to compensate the woman for shaming her, despite the fact that she did the same to herself. Similarly, **one who cuts** down **his** own **saplings, although it is not permitted for him** to do so, as this violates the prohibition of: "You shall not destroy" (see Deuteronomy 20:19), is **exempt** from any penalty, but **others** who cut down his saplings are **liable** to pay him.

Rabbi Akiva was unmoved. *Lo amarta klum* — you have said nothing. The response draws on an established legal principle: *ha-chovel be-atzmo — af al pi she-eino rashai — patur* — one who injures himself, even though he is not permitted to do so, is exempt from payment. *Acherim she-chavlu bo — chayavim* — others who injure him remain liable. Similarly: *ha-kotzetz netiotav — af al pi she-eino rashai — patur* — one who cuts down his own saplings is exempt, yet *acherim she-chavlu bo — chayavim*. The objective fact of the injury or degradation does not change based on whether the victim was willing to inflict it upon himself or herself.

The parallel is exact. The woman may have been willing to expose her hair for a trivial amount of oil — she may, in that moment, have not felt the shame to be significant. But *boshet* as assessed by *halacha* is not a purely subjective phenomenon. It has an objective value rooted in the person's inherent *kavod*, which is, in Rabbi Akiva's framework, always at the maximum. The same way a severed arm represents an objective injury regardless of whether the owner cut it off himself, an act of public humiliation represents an objective degradation of a person's dignity regardless of whether the victim was willing to participate in a similar act for personal benefit.

The deeper insight in this ruling — noted in the discussion — is its close relationship to the debate between Rabbi Akiva and Ben Azzai. Rabbi Akiva famously holds that *ve-ahavta le-reacha kamocha* is the *klal gadol ba-Torah* — love your neighbor as yourself is the great principle. Ben Azzai counters that *zeh sefer toldos adam* — this is the book of the generations of man — is a greater principle, because one's obligation to respect another person does not derive from self-love but from the other person's status as a human being. If you do not respect yourself, the *ve-ahavta le-reacha kamocha* principle might suggest your obligations to others are proportionally diminished. Ben Azzai insists this cannot be so. Interestingly, Rabbi Akiva here — in the case of the woman — is making a parallel argument: it does not matter that this woman does not respect herself in this moment. The objective *kavod* that belongs to every descendant of Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov remains intact, and the person who violated it must pay its full price.

## Witness as Judge

### Core Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מָנֶה צוֹרִי תְּנַן, אוֹ מָנֶה מְדִינָה תְּנַן? תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּתְקַע לֵיהּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אֲנָא, הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, הַב לֵיהּ מָנֶה צוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, one who strikes another must pay him one hundred dinars. The Gemara clarifies this opinion. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Did **we learn** in the mishna that he must pay **one hundred dinars** of **Tyrian** coinage, **or** did **we learn** in the mishna that he must pay **one hundred dinars** of **state** coinage, which is worth one-eighth of Tyrian coinage, i.e., twelve and a half dinars of Tyrian coinage? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a proof: It happened **that there was a certain man who struck another** man, and the latter **came before Rabbi Yehuda Nesia** to request an assessment for the humiliation he suffered. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to** the assailant: **This is I,** and **this is Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Give him one hundred dinars** of **Tyrian** coinage.

The *Gemara* has been discussing the payment of a *maneh* for striking an ear, and the question arises whether this is a *maneh Tzori* — the larger denomination — or a *maneh medinah*, one-eighth that value. A story is cited: a man who struck another's ear was brought before Rabbi Yehuda Nesia, the grandson of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The judge declared, *ha ana v'ha Rabbi Yossi HaGlili* — here am I, and here is Rabbi Yossi HaGlili — and ordered payment of a *maneh Tzori*. The *Gemara* derives from this that when the *mishna* states a *maneh*, it means *maneh Tzori*, the higher value.

But a deeper question immediately presents itself. The *Gemara* initially reads *ha ana v'ha Rabbi Yossi HaGlili* as the judge saying: "I witnessed what you did" — combining his personal testimony with Rabbi Yossi HaGlili's legal ruling to order payment. The difficulty: this would mean that the judge who witnessed the event is also adjudicating it. *Le-meimra de-eid na'aseh dayan* — does this imply that a witness may become a judge in the same case?

### Tarfon vs Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״הָא אֲנָא, הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי״? אִילֵּימָא הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אֲנָא – דַּחֲזֵיתָךְ, וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – דְּאָמַר מָנֶה צוֹרִי, זִיל הַב לֵיהּ מָנֶה צוֹרִי. לְמֵימְרָא דְּעֵד נַעֲשֶׂה דַּיָּין?! וְהָתַנְיָא: סַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁרָאוּ אֶחָד שֶׁהָרַג אֶת הַנֶּפֶשׁ – מִקְצָתָן נַעֲשׂוּ עֵדִים, וּמִקְצָתָן נַעֲשׂוּ דַּיָּינִין; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּם עֵדִים הֵם, וְאֵין עֵד נַעֲשֶׂה דַּיָּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What** did Rabbi Yehuda Nesia mean when he stated: **This is I,** and **this is Rabbi Yosei HaGelili? If we say** that **this is** what Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **said to** the assailant: **This is I, who saw you** strike the man, **and this is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says** that the penalty for this act is **one hundred dinars** of **Tyrian** coinage; therefore, **go** and **give him one hundred dinars** of **Tyrian** coinage, **is this to say that a witness can become a judge,** i.e., that one who witnessed an event can himself serve as a judge concerning the matter, and consequently Rabbi Yehuda Nesia was able to act as both witness and judge in the same case? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if there was **a Sanhedrin that saw one kill** another **person, some of them have become witnesses** and testify before the others, **and some of them have become judges** to issue a verdict; this is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Akiva says: They are all witnesses** to the act, **and a witness cannot become a judge.**

A *baraita* bears directly on this question. A *Sanhedrin* out walking witnessed a murder. Rabbi Tarfon rules: *miktzatan na'asu eidim u-miktzatan na'asu dayanim* — some of them become witnesses and some serve as judges; one cannot do both. Rabbi Akiva rules: *kulam eidim hein ve-ein eid na'aseh dayan* — they are all witnesses, and a witness cannot become a judge. For Rabbi Akiva, the mere act of seeing something — even if one never formally testifies — disqualifies one from adjudicating the case. The *Sanhedrin* would have to convene a different court. This is feasible since capital cases require only twenty-three judges rather than seventy-one.

### Day vs Night Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן – אֶלָּא דְּמִקְצָתָן נַעֲשׂוּ עֵדִים וּמִקְצָתָן נַעֲשׂוּ דַּיָּינִין; אֲבָל עֵד נַעֲשֶׂה דַּיָּין – לָא קָאָמַר! כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – כְּגוֹן שֶׁרָאוּ בַּלַּיְלָה, דְּלָא בְּנֵי מֶעְבַּד דִּינָא נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers from this *baraita* that even **Rabbi Tarfon says** his opinion **only** in a case **where some of** the members of the Sanhedrin **have become witnesses and some of them have become judges, but he did not say** that **a witness becomes a judge** without anyone else testifying. All the more so according to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva would it not be permitted for Rabbi Yehuda Nesia to act as a judge as well as serving as a witness. The Gemara rejects this: One could say that **when that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is taught in a case **where** the Sanhedrin **saw** the murder **at night,** at which time **it is not** permitted **for them to practice judgment,** since cases of capital law may be judged only during the daytime. They were therefore not functioning as judges at that time. On the following day, testimony must be heard from some, now acting as witnesses, in front of the others, now acting as judges. By contrast, in the case of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia, he was able to serve as both the witness and the judge, as he witnessed the event while functioning as a judge.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent tension as follows. Rabbi Tarfon does not say that the judge can use his own sighting as a basis for ruling — he says only that some members of the *Sanhedrin* may formally testify while others judge; being a witness does not inherently disqualify one from judging, but one cannot do both in the same proceeding. What the *Gemara* needs to establish is whether the judge's direct sighting can function like the receipt of testimony.

The resolution: *ki tanya hi ke-gon she-ra'u ba-layla* — the *baraita* deals with a case where they saw the murder at night, when a court cannot be in session. Since testimony can only be received during the daytime, a judge's nocturnal sighting cannot be treated as the equivalent of receiving testimony. The formal witnesses must testify in court during the day, and once those witnesses have testified, they cannot turn around and serve as judges.

But if the sighting occurred during the day, a judge who personally witnessed the event has effectively "received" the testimony directly. There is nothing epistemically inferior about direct observation compared to hearing witnesses; indeed, one might argue the opposite. If the judge saw it himself during the day — during the hours when a court would be in session — that sighting is analogous to testimony formally received, and he may then adjudicate.

### Alt Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: הָא אֲנָא – דִּסְבִירָא לִי כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּאָמַר מָנֶה צוֹרִי; וְהָא סָהֲדִי דְּמַסְהֲדִי בָּךְ; זִיל הַב לֵיהּ מָנֶה צוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers an alternative explanation: **And if you wish, say** instead that Rabbi Yehuda Nesia did not witness the incident, and **this is** what he **said to** the assailant: **This is I, that I hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says** that **one hundred dinars** of **Tyrian** coinage must be paid; **and these are witnesses who testify concerning you** that you struck the other. Therefore, **go** and **give him one hundred dinars** of **Tyrian** coinage.

The *Gemara* offers an alternative reading of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia's statement that requires no *ed na'aseh dayan* analysis at all. *Ve-i ba'it eima, hachi ka'amar leh* — perhaps *ha ana* means not "I witnessed you" but "here I am as *dayan*, holding like Rabbi Yosei HaGlili, who rules a *maneh Tzori*" — *ve-ha sahadi de-mesahadei bach* — and there are witnesses who have testified against you. Under this reading, the judge was not personally testifying; he was simply identifying his ruling with Rabbi Yosei HaGlili's position. The witnesses are separate persons who gave testimony in the ordinary fashion.

### Instrument Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְהִכָּה אִישׁ אֶת רֵעֵהוּ בְּאֶבֶן אוֹ בְאֶגְרֹף״ – שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי אוֹמֵר: מָה אֶגְרוֹף מְיוּחָד – שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים, אַף כֹּל שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים; פְּרָט לְשֶׁיָּצְתָה מִתַּחַת יַד הָעֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Sanhedrin* 12:3) with regard to what is stated in the Torah concerning injuries: "And if men contend, **and one smite the other with a stone, or with his fist"** (Exodus 21:18), that **Shimon HaTimni says: Just as a fist is unique** in **that it is submitted to the assembly** of judges to assess its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** who attest that it was the fist used to strike, as a fist remains attached to the assailant, **so too,** a ruling can be issued in the case of **any** item **that is submitted to the assembly** of judges to assess its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** who attest that this was the item used to strike? This serves to **exclude** a case **where** the stone that injured **left the possession of the witnesses** and is not available to be inspected by the court to assess if it is capable of causing the purported injury.

The discussion of *ed na'aseh dayan* concludes, and the *Gemara* transitions to a related topic: the requirement to assess the instrument of injury. Shimon HaTeimani derives from the verse — *ve-chi yakeh ish et re'ehu be-even o be-egrof* ("and if a man strikes his fellow with a stone or a fist") — that just as a fist can be examined and assessed by both the *eidah* (the court) and the *eidim* (the witnesses), so too any instrument used to inflict injury must be assessable by both. The *perat* — the exclusion — applies to instruments that left the possession of the witnesses: if the *eidim* can no longer produce and show the instrument, the defendant is acquitted.

### Akiva Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וְכִי בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין הִכָּהוּ – שֶׁיּוֹדְעִין כַּמָּה הִכָּהוּ, וְעַל מָה הִכָּהוּ – אִם עַל שׁוֹקוֹ אוֹ צִיפַּר נַפְשׁוֹ? וְעוֹד, הֲרֵי שֶׁדָּחַף אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ מֵרֹאשׁ הַגָּג אוֹ מֵרֹאשׁ הַבִּירָה וָמֵת – בֵּית דִּין הוֹלְכִין אֵצֶל בִּירָה, אוֹ בִירָה הוֹלֶכֶת אֵצֶל בֵּית דִּין? וְעוֹד, אִם נָפְלָה, חוֹזֵר וּבוֹנֶה?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to him: But** is it the case that in all incidents of injury the assailant **struck him in the presence of the court,** so **that they know** exactly **how** hard **he struck him and on what** part of the body **he struck him,** e.g., **if** he struck him **on his thigh** or **if** he struck him on **the lobe of his heart?** Rather, the court relies on witnesses to testify about the injury. Therefore, the witnesses should be able to testify about the item used as well. **And furthermore,** if **there** was one **who pushed another from the top of the roof or from the top of a building, and** the one who was pushed **died** as a result of the fall, do the members of **the court go to the building** to inspect how high it is, **or** does the **building go to the court?** Obviously neither, and the court relies upon the testimony of witnesses who state how tall the building is. The witnesses should then be able to testify about the item used as well. **And furthermore, if** the building **collapsed,** must the court **rebuild it** in order to assess its height?

Rabbi Akiva objects to Shimon HaTeimani's formulation. His objection is pointed: *ve-chi bifnei beis din hikah* — did the assailant strike in front of the *beis din*? The *beis din* can look at the defendant's fist, but it did not see how many times he struck, at what part of the body, with what force. Assessing a fist in the abstract tells you little. More strikingly: *harei she-dofahu me-chaveiru me-rosh ha-birah* — what if he pushed his friend off the top of a tower or castle and the victim died? Should *beis din* take a field trip to inspect the building? *Ha-birah holechet etzel beis din* — should the castle come to the court? *Ve-od im naflah ha-birah* — what if the castle has since fallen down? *Chozer u-voneh et ha-birah* — rebuild the building so the court can inspect it? The requirement that *beis din* directly assess the instrument is physically and practically impossible in many real cases.

### Witness Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מָה אֶגְרוֹף מְיוּחָד – שֶׁהוּא מָסוּר לָעֵדִים, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהוּא מָסוּר לָעֵדִים; פְּרָט לִכְשֶׁיָּצְתָה אֶבֶן מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ שֶׁל מַכֶּה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabbi Akiva interprets the verse differently: **Just as a fist is unique** in **that it is submitted to the witnesses** for them to testify about it, **so too,** a ruling can be issued in the case of **any** item **that is submitted to the witnesses** to testify about, despite the item's not being available for the court to assess. This serves to **exclude** a case **where** the **stone** that injured **left the possession of the assailant** and cannot be found, and even the witnesses were unable to see it. In such a case the assailant is **exempt** from payment, since even the witnesses cannot testify about whether the stone was capable of inflicting the purported injuries.

Rabbi Akiva therefore reformulates the principle: *ela mah egrof meyuchad she-masur le-eidim* — rather, a fist is paradigmatic of things that can be assessed by the *witnesses*. The requirement is not that *beis din* see and assess the instrument, but that the *eidim* be able to assess it. *Af kol she-masur le-eidim* — any instrument assessable by the witnesses suffices. *Perat she-yatza even mitachat yado shel makeh* — the exclusion is when the stone left the striker's hand before the witnesses had a good visual on it, such that even the *eidim* cannot assess it. In that case, the defendant goes free.

The relevance to the *ed na'aseh dayan* discussion is then made explicit. The *baraita* attributes to the court the question *ve-chi bifnei beis din hikah she-yod'im kamah hikah* — as if to say, if *beis din* had directly witnessed it, they would know the specifics. This implies that if *beis din* had witnessed the event directly, they could use that knowledge. But does this not contradict Rabbi Akiva's view that witnessing an event disqualifies one from judging? The *Gemara* answers: *aliba de-Rabbi Shimon HaTeimani ka'amrei leih ve-lo sevira lei* — Rabbi Akiva is merely arguing from within Shimon HaTeimani's own framework to expose its absurdity. He is showing that the *beis din*-assessment requirement leads to impossible demands. Rabbi Akiva himself does not hold that a *beis din* witnessing an event can ever serve as judge; as soon as they become witnesses, they must recuse themselves entirely.

## Shor Tam & Mu'ad

### Judgment Order Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שׁוֹר תָּם שֶׁהֵמִית וְהִזִּיק – דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת, וְאֵין דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת. מוּעָד שֶׁהֵמִית וְהִזִּיק – דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת, וְחוֹזְרִין וְדָנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת. קָדְמוּ וְדָנוּהוּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת – אֵין חוֹזְרִין וְדָנִין אוֹתוֹ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara quotes a related *halakha*. **The Sages taught:** In the case of **an innocuous ox that killed** a person **and** subsequently went and **caused damage,** the court **judges it** as a case of **capital law** and the ox is killed, **and** the court **does not judge it** as a case of **monetary law,** despite the damage that it caused. By contrast, in the case of **a forewarned** ox **that killed** a person **and** subsequently went and **caused damage,** the court **judges it** as a case of **monetary law,** and the owner is liable to pay for the damage it caused, **and** then the court **goes back and judges it** again as a case of **capital law,** and the ox is killed. But if the court **proceeded and judged it** first as a case of **capital law,** the court **does not go back and judge it** again as a case of **monetary law,** since it has already been sentenced to be killed.

The final *baraita* of our daf deals with the sequencing of judgments when an ox has both killed a person and inflicted property damage. The *baraita* rules: *shor tam she-hemit ve-hizik — danin oto dinei nefashot ve-ein danin oto dinei mammonot* — a *shor tam* that killed and caused damage is tried first for the capital offense (*dinei nefashot*), and the monetary case (*dinei mammonot*) does not proceed. Conversely, *mu'ad she-hemit ve-hizik — danin oto dinei mammonot ve-chozrin ve-danin oto dinei nefashot* — a *mu'ad* that killed and caused damage is tried first for the monetary obligation, and only then for the capital offense.

### Tam Ox Rule

The logic for the *shor tam* case is clear. A *shor tam* pays for damages from the body of the ox itself — *mi-guf ha-shor*. But an ox condemned as a *shor nesakel* becomes *assur be-hana'ah*: the stoned ox may not be used for any benefit. If the capital judgment is rendered first, the ox becomes a *shor nesakel* and there is nothing left from which to collect the monetary damages. The injured party loses his recovery. Hence, for the *shor tam*, the capital case takes priority and the monetary case simply cannot proceed; the order of judgment is not merely a sequencing preference — it is determinative of outcome.

### Mu'ad Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי קָדְמוּ וְדָנוּהוּ דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת מַאי הָוֵי? לִיהְדַּר וְלִידַיְינֵיהּ דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And if they proceeded and judged it** first as a case of **capital law, what of it? Let them go back and judge it** again **also** as a case of **monetary** law.

For the *mu'ad* ox, however, the question is less obvious. A *mu'ad* owner pays the full damages out of his own pocket, not from the body of the ox. Whether the ox becomes a *shor nesakel* before or after the monetary judgment is rendered, the owner can still be compelled to pay. Why should the order of proceedings matter at all? If the *dinei nefashot* case were adjudicated first, condemning the ox, why couldn't *beis din* then proceed to the monetary case and order the owner to pay?

### Rava's Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רָבָא: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּבֵי רַב, דְּיָתְבִי וְקָאָמְרִי: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַתִּימְנִי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: ״מָה אֶגְרוֹף מְיוּחָד – שֶׁמָּסוּר לָעֵדָה וְלָעֵדִים״, אַלְמָא בָּעֵינַן אוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי דִינָא; וְהָא, כֵּיוָן דִּגְמַר דִּינֵיהּ לִקְטָלָא – לָא מְשַׁהֵינַן לֵיהּ לְאוּמְדָּנָא דְּבֵי דִינָא, וְלָא מְעַנֵּינַן לְדִינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: I found the Sages of the school of Rav who were sitting and saying** to explain this: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon HaTimni, who says** that **just as a fist is unique** in **that it is submitted to the assembly** of judges to assess its ability to injure **and to the witnesses** to attest that this was the fist used to strike, so too, a ruling can be issued in the case of any item that is submitted to the assembly of judges to assess its ability to injure and to the witnesses to attest that this was the item used to strike. Apparently, we require the evaluation of the court** in order to render him liable to pay for the damage. **And** with regard to **this** ox that killed, **once the verdict** was given that it to be put **to death, we do not delay** the implementation of the verdict in order to wait **for the evaluation of the court, and we do not delay its judgment.** Therefore, the monetary case is not judged.

Rava found the scholars of the *beis medrash* debating this very question and offered his analysis. They proposed that the *baraita* follows the position of Shimon HaTeimani: *de-amar mah egrof meyuchad she-masur le-eidah u-le-eidim* — the *beis din* must assess the instrument of injury. For an ox, the ox *is* the instrument of injury. Before any judgment can be rendered in the monetary case, *beis din* must assess the ox — examine it and determine whether, under the circumstances, it had the capability and characteristics to inflict the damage that occurred. *Alma ba'inan umdana de-veis din* — we require a direct assessment by *beis din* of the instrument.

### Execution Delay

But here is the problem: *ve-ha keivan de-gamruhu le-dineih le-katla, lo mashghinan beih le-umdana de-veis din* — once the ox has been condemned to execution as a *shor nesakel*, one cannot delay the execution to allow *beis din* to conduct its assessment for the monetary case. The reason is *lo ma'aninan le-dineih* — we do not cause an affliction of justice by making the ox wait for its execution.

This principle is deeply parallel to the rules governing human capital punishment. Throughout Bava Kamma we have encountered the *shor nesakel* as an analogue to a human convicted of a capital offense — the ox is processed with many of the same procedural requirements as a human murderer. Here, too, the parallel holds: just as one does not delay the execution of a person convicted of a capital crime — because the certainty of impending death combined with an indefinite wait constitutes a form of torment — so too one does not delay the execution of a *shor nesakel*. Once the ox is condemned, it must be executed without delay. The *beis din*'s need to assess the ox for purposes of the monetary judgment cannot justify holding the condemned animal in suspension. Since the *umdana* required by Shimon HaTeimani's position cannot be performed post-condemnation without causing this unacceptable delay, the monetary case must precede the capital one.
