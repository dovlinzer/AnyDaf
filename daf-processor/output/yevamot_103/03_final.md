# Yevamot 103 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chalitza & Leather

### Slipper Invalidity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאַנְפִּילְיָא חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה כּוּ׳. לְמֵימְרָא דְּאַנְפִּילְיָא לָאו מִנְעָל הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If she performed *ḥalitza* using **a soft shoe [*anpileya*]** made of cloth for *ḥalitza*, **her *ḥalitza* is invalid.** The Gemara explains: **That is to say that an *anpileya* is not** considered **a shoe.**

The daf opens in the middle of the bottom of 102b, approximately fifteen lines from the end, at a new paragraph. The *Mishnah* establishes: *ba'anpalya chalitzatah pesulah* — if the *yevamah* performed *chalitza* using a slipper, the *chalitza* is invalid. It is worth recalling the earlier ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, which required the shoe used for *chalitza* to be made of leather, derived from a *pasuk*. That requirement, however, is by no means straightforwardly established from the *tannaitic* material. The *Gemara* will work to reconcile everything into a consistent requirement of leather, but as the discussion unfolds it becomes clear that multiple sources complicate, rather than confirm, that position.

### Slipper Not a Shoe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן נָמֵי: אֵין הַתּוֹרֵם נִכְנָס לֹא בְּפַרְגּוֹד חָפוּת וְלֹא בְּאַנְפִּילְיָא, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּמִנְעָל וְסַנְדָּל, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין נִכְנָסִין בְּמִנְעָל וְסַנְדָּל לָעֲזָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And we also learned** similarly in a mishna (*Shekalim* 3:2): **The one who collects the funds** of shekels donated to the Temple from the chamber and puts them into baskets in order to be used **may not enter** to collect the funds **wearing a garment [*pargod*] that is cuffed [*ḥafut*], nor wearing an *anpileya*, and needless to say** that he may not enter wearing **a shoe or a sandal, because one may not enter** the Temple **courtyard wearing a shoe or a sandal.** It is prohibited for the one collecting funds from the chamber to enter the chamber wearing a garment or footwear in which money could be hidden, lest people come to suspect that he hid in them funds collected from the chamber. In any case, the wording of the mishna indicates that an *anpileya* is not considered a type of shoe, since it is permitted to enter the Temple wearing an *anpileya* when there is no reason for suspicion, unlike a shoe or sandal, which can never be worn in the Temple.

From the *Mishnah*'s ruling, the *Gemara* infers: *danpalya lav min'al hu* — a slipper is not legally classified as a shoe. This inference sets up the broader discussion of what, precisely, constitutes a *min'al* for purposes of *chalitza* and related laws.

### Shekalim Parallel

The *Gemara* supports this inference by citing a parallel from *Shekalim*. When a person brings his *trumat halishka* — the annual contribution to the *Beit HaMikdash* — and goes in person to deposit it in the *pushka*, the *Mishnah* in *Shekalim* specifies: *ein hatorim nichnas* — the one who separates the funds may not enter *Har HaBayit* or the *Beit HaMikdash* with a cloak that has a hem (*lo bepargout chafut*), out of concern that he might conceal money in the hem, thereby appearing suspect (*nishchad*). Equally, he may not enter *v'lo b'anpalya* — with slippers — presumably, as Rashi explains, because slippers similarly create the appearance that money might be concealed within them.

### Temple Shoe Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: אֶחָד מִנְעָל וְסַנְדָּל וְאַנְפִּילְיָא, לֹא יְטַיֵּיל בָּהֶן, לֹא מִבַּיִת לְבַיִת וְלֹא מִמִּטָּה לְמִטָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita* concerning what footwear is permitted on Yom Kippur, which seems to indicate otherwise: The halakha is **the same for a** soft leather **shoe, and a** hard leather **sandal, and an *anpileya*, as one may not walk in them from one house to another, nor from one bed to another** on Yom Kippur, due to the prohibition against wearing shoes, indicating that at least as far as Yom Kippur is concerned, an *anpileya* is considered a shoe.

The *Mishnah* in *Shekalim* then adds: *v'ein tzarich lomar bemin'al vesandal* — and needless to say, not with a shoe or sandal. The reason here is entirely different: not the suspicion of hiding money, but the prohibition against entering the *azarah* in an ordinary shoe. This derives from the verse *shal ne'alecha me'al raglecha* — remove your sandal from upon your foot — and from the *pasuk*, *lamma zeh lachem remos chatzerai* — why do you trample My courtyards. Entering the Temple precinct while wearing regular shoes is considered disrespectful, a different category of concern than the slipper issue. Thus the *Mishnah* in *Shekalim* places *anpalya* in a legally distinct category from *min'al vesandal*: the slipper is addressed separately and for a separate reason, reflecting its distinct *halachic* status. The *Gemara* then raises a contradiction (*reminihu*): another *braita* groups *min'al*, *sandal*, and *anpalya* together in the prohibition of walking in them on *Yom Kippur* — *lo yitayel adam bahen lo mibayit lebayit velo mimita lemita*. If the slipper is in a separate legal category from shoes, why does this *braita* lump them together?

## Yom Kippur Debate

### YK Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּאִית בֵּיהּ כְּתִיתֵי, וּמִשּׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** There, with regard to Yom Kippur, it is referring to an *anpileya* **that has cushioning, and** this is forbidden **due to the pleasure** that one derives from cushioned footwear on a day when people are commanded to afflict themselves.

The *braita* states that one may not walk about — not from house to house and not from couch to couch — whether wearing a shoe, sandal, or slipper. This is the *Yom Kippur* context. The difficulty is immediate: if a slipper is not a shoe, why should it be forbidden on *Yom Kippur*, where the prohibition is specifically against wearing shoes?

Abaye offers an initial resolution: the *braita* is speaking of a slipper that contains *ketiti* — small stuffed pieces of cloth — giving it a pleasant, cushioned feel. The prohibition in this case, he suggests, is *mishum ta'anug* — because of the pleasure it provides, which is inappropriate on *Yom Kippur*.

### Rava Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וּמִשּׁוּם תַּעֲנוּג בְּלֹא מִנְעָל בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מִי אֲסִירִי? וְהָא רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא כָּרֵיךְ סוּדָרָא אַכַּרְעֵיהּ וְנָפֵיק!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: But** is footwear **that is not considered** to be **shoes forbidden on Yom Kippur due to** the **pleasure** one derives from wearing them? **But Rabba bar Rav Huna would wrap a scarf on his feet and go out** on Yom Kippur so his feet would not be injured, implying that there is no prohibition against wearing something comfortable on one's foot, as long as it is not defined as a shoe.

Rava immediately dismisses this reasoning. Since when, he asks, is pleasure that does not fall within one of the five formally prohibited categories of *Yom Kippur* a *halachic* problem? The *Yom Kippur* prohibition concerns wearing shoes, not deriving comfort for one's feet in general. To illustrate, Rava cites the well-known example: *v'ha Rabbah bar bar Chana karchis sudra akarei* — Rabbah bar bar Chana would wrap a cloth around his leg and go out on *Yom Kippur*. This cloth served the function of a shoe and provided comfort, yet it was entirely permissible, because halachically it was not a shoe. The point is clear: if it is not a shoe, it is not a shoe, regardless of the pleasure involved.

### Bar Chana Example

The case of Rabbah bar bar Chana's cloth wrapping serves as a compelling proof that the formal category matters, not the experiential comfort. Functional similarity to a shoe does not make something a shoe.

### Rava's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּאַנְפִּילְיָא שֶׁל עוֹר, כָּאן בְּאַנְפִּילְיָא שֶׁל בֶּגֶד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: This** is **not difficult. Here,** when they said that an *anpileya* has the status of a shoe, it is referring to **an *anpileya*** made **of leather. There,** when they do not consider it a shoe, it is referring to **an *anpileya*** made **of cloth.**

Rava therefore resolves the apparent contradiction differently: *kan be'anpileya shel or, kan be'anpileya shel beged* — here we are speaking of a leather slipper, there of a cloth slipper. The *braita* that forbids a slipper on *Yom Kippur* is referring to a leather slipper, which does count as a shoe; the *Mishnah* that renders *chalitza* with a slipper invalid is referring to a non-leather slipper, which is not a shoe at all. This solution reintroduces the leather criterion — not only as a *chalitza*-specific requirement derived from a *pasuk*, but as a general definitional principle in *halachah*: leather is what makes a slipper cross the threshold into being legally classified as a shoe.

## Leather: Form vs. Function

### Modern Shoes

This distinction carries direct practical implications. The question of whether one may wear a well-made, highly supportive non-leather shoe on *Yom Kippur* turns on precisely this issue. Is the requirement of leather a purely formal, definitional *halachah* — in which case even the most supportive canvas sneaker would be permitted — or does it reflect a functional criterion, leather being the material that most effectively protects and supports the foot? If it is purely formal, non-leather shoes of any quality remain permitted on *Yom Kippur* and the question is closed. If it is functional, the analysis may shift as materials and norms evolve.

The *Gemara* keeps returning to leather as though it is the dispositive criterion, which can certainly give the impression of a formal requirement. On the other hand, one might argue the other way: that the distinction between leather and non-leather maps onto the distinction between what does and does not provide meaningful protection, and that the *halachah* tracks the function rather than the material itself. The lecturer notes his personal practice not to wear even comfortable cloth slippers on *Yom Kippur* or *Tisha B'Av*, wearing only thin socks with minimal rubber traction, because experientially such an arrangement does not feel like it honors the spirit of the prohibition. Others hold that non-leather is simply not a shoe — pure formalism — and have no hesitation. Still others might argue that even Rava's formal leather distinction is less relevant in a time when non-leather shoes can be as protective and shoe-like as leather ones, though this approach would not apply to *chalitza*, where the *pasuk* anchors the requirement more firmly.

### Rashi: No Protection

Rashi's commentary on the *Shekalim* case illuminates his position. In the margin between Rashi and the *Gemara*, Rashi explains: *matnitin de'terumat halishka be'anpileya shel beged* — the *Mishnah* concerning the *trumat halishka* is speaking of a cloth slipper. *Delav magen hu* — because it is not protective. *Delo havei min'al* — it is not classified as a shoe. And: *u'legabei azara nami leka bizayon* — nor does it constitute a disrespect relative to the *azarah*. He adds: *ve'ein derech lehachnisah betit* — and it is not the normal way to enter with mud underneath, which seems to refer to the lack of a ridged sole that would trap dirt. The crucial phrase is Rashi's *delav magen hu*: the slipper fails the definitional threshold not because it is categorically non-leather, but because it does not protect. By contrast, Rashi explains of the *Yom Kippur* case: *dehai d'kippurim beshel or* — that prohibition concerns a leather slipper.

### Form or Function?

What emerges is a significant question: by *chalitza*, where a *pasuk* mandates the use of a shoe, the leather requirement appears to be an absolute formal criterion. But when the same distinction between leather and non-leather governs the laws of *Yom Kippur* and the *Beit HaMikdash*, is that too a formal definition — or is it grounded in the functional capacity of leather to protect and cover the foot, as Rashi's language of *magen* suggests? Rashi does not say that a non-leather slipper is definitionally not a shoe; he says it does not protect, and therefore is not a shoe. This functional reading has real consequences: if leather's relevance is protective function, then materials that provide equivalent protection might carry equivalent *halachic* weight, at least in non-*chalitza* contexts.

## Braita: Valid Objects

### Torn & Broken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: חָלְצָה בְּמִנְעָל הַנִּפְרָם שֶׁחוֹפֶה אֶת רוֹב הָרֶגֶל, בְּסַנְדָּל הַנִּפְחָת שֶׁמְּקַבֵּל אֶת רוֹב הָרֶגֶל, בְּסַנְדָּל שֶׁל שַׁעַם וְשֶׁל סִיב, בְּקַב הַקִּיטֵּעַ, בְּמוּק, בִּסְמִיכַת הָרַגְלַיִם, בְּאַנְפִּילְיָא שֶׁל עוֹר, וְהַחוֹלֶצֶת מִן הַגָּדוֹל,
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava:** If **she performed *ḥalitza* using a shoe whose seams were opened up, which** still **covered most of the foot;** or if she performed *ḥalitza* **with a sandal** whose sole **was** partially **opened that** still **held most of the foot;** or if she performed *ḥalitza* **with a sandal** made **of cork [*sha'am*], or of fibers** from a tree; or **with a prosthetic foot of an amputee;** or **with a felt shoe [*muk*];** or **with a leg blanket** that an amputee makes for his feet as a covering in which to put the stumps of his legs, which is not an actual shoe; or **with a leather *anpileya*; and** likewise, a woman **who performs *ḥalitza*** with her *yavam* when he is an **adult man,**

The *Gemara* now presents a *braita* enumerating cases of *chalitza* that are valid, at minimum *b'dieved*. The list begins: *chalitza b'min'al hanifram shechofeh rov haregel* — if she performed *chalitza* with a torn shoe, provided it covers most of the foot. Similarly, *basandal hanifchat she'mekabbel rov haregel* — a broken sandal that still supports most of the foot is valid. The structural integrity required is that the object cover or support the majority of the foot; so long as that threshold is met, damage does not invalidate it.

### Cork & Fiber

The *braita* continues: *u'v'sandal shel sha'am v'shel siv* — a sandal made of cork (*sha'am*) or of fiber (rush or bass, *siv*). These are self-evidently non-leather materials. This is significant: the *braita* is validating *chalitza* performed with sandals that are not made of leather. This stands in apparent tension with Rav Yehuda's statement in the name of Rav that leather is required. The *Gemara* will have to reckon with this.

### Prosthetic Shoe

The *braita* then introduces the case of *v'kav hakite'ah* — the prosthetic used by an amputee. This is the wooden peg-leg used by someone whose leg has been severed. According to Rashi's reading of the *Mishnah*, if the amputation is below the knee, the prosthetic functions as the person's shoe and its removal constitutes valid *chalitza*; if the amputation is above the knee, it does not. In Rashi's framework, the reasoning is that when the leg is amputated below the knee, the remaining stump of the *shok* — the calf — is considered the foot for *halachic* purposes, and the prosthetic that covers and protects it is classified as a shoe upon that foot.

The *braita* also mentions *b'mook* — using a felt covering. Rashi explains that *mook* denotes a hard felt, which does provide protection. So here we have another non-leather material being validated, this time even in the context of the prosthetic. This reinforces the growing picture that the list of valid *chalitza* objects includes numerous non-leather items.

The *braita* then mentions *u'v'smichat haraglayim* — the supports for the feet. Rashi describes this as the situation of a person whose legs are not functional and who moves by dragging himself along the ground, propelling with his arms, with a protective mat or covering on his feet to prevent them from being scraped. This object, worn on the dragging legs, is also included in the list of valid *chalitza* objects.

Finally, the list includes *u'v'anpilaya shel or* — a leather slipper. This confirms that for the slipper specifically, leather is the operative distinction.

### Invalid Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בְּמִנְעָל הַנִּפְרָם שֶׁאֵין חוֹפֶה אֶת רוֹב הָרֶגֶל, בְּסַנְדָּל הַנִּפְחָת שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל אֶת רוֹב הָרֶגֶל, וּבִסְמִיכַת הַיָּדַיִם, וּבְאַנְפִּילְיָא שֶׁל בֶּגֶד, וְחוֹלֶצֶת מִן הַקָּטָן — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if she performs *ḥalitza* when he is wearing **a shoe** that is so **torn that it does not cover most of the foot; or using a broken sandal that does not hold most of the foot; or using a hand blanket** that the amputee wears on his hands, similar to a leather shoe, in order to drag himself using them; **or using a soft shoe [*anpileya*]** made **of cloth; or** a woman **who performs *halitza* with** a *yavam* who is **a minor;** in all these cases **her *ḥalitza* is disqualified.**

The *braita* then turns to invalid cases. A torn shoe that does not cover most of the foot (*she'ein chofeh rov haregel*) is invalid. A broken sandal that does not support most of the foot is invalid. *U'vsmichat hayadayim* — the protective covering for the *hands* of the same person who drags himself — even though his hands are functionally his means of locomotion, we do not classify hand-coverings as shoes. *U'v'anpilya shel beged* — a cloth slipper is invalid. *V'choletzet min hakatan* — *chalitza* performed with a minor is invalid. *Chalitzatah pesulah* — all of these are invalid.

What this *braita* makes vividly clear is that the requirement of leather, while operative for the slipper, does not apply universally. Cork, fiber, felt, and wood all appear in the valid column without any qualification. This substantially complicates any claim that leather is a formal universal requirement for *chalitza* footwear. It appears that leather matters specifically — and perhaps only — in the case of the *anpilya*, where without leather the object functions more like a sock than a shoe. For genuinely shoe-like objects — sandals of cork or fiber, a prosthetic, a protective mat for dragging legs — leather is not demanded.

A question naturally arises regarding straps: the *Gemara* earlier mentioned undoing the straps as part of the *chalitza* act, and the *braita* there stated that if he undoes the straps and she removes the shoe, it is invalid — she must do both. But this should be read as: if the shoe happens to have straps, she must perform every component of the removal. It does not appear that straps are a mandatory feature of the *chalitza* shoe. The many cases in this *braita* that clearly involve no straps — a broken sandal, a prosthetic, a felt covering — bear this out. The *Gemara* never explicitly states that straps are required.

## Leather Contradiction

### Abaye: All Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַב הַקִּיטֵּעַ, מַנִּי — רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַקִּיטֵּעַ יוֹצֵא בְּקַב שֶׁלּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹסֵר. בְּאַנְפִּילְיָא שֶׁל בֶּגֶד, אֲתָאן לְרַבָּנַן! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּסֵיפָא רַבָּנַן, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי רַבָּנַן. וְרֵישָׁא, בִּמְחוּפֶּה עוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Who is** the *tanna* who holds that **an amputee's prosthetic** is considered a shoe? **It is Rabbi Meir, as we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 65b): **One with an amputated leg may go out** on Shabbat **with his wooden leg,** as it has the legal status of a shoe; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** He reasons that the prosthesis functions like the shoe of any other person, indicating that Rabbi Meir is not especially concerned about the material from which the shoe is made. And **Rabbi Yosei,** on the other hand, **prohibits** the amputee from going out on Shabbat with his wooden leg, as he does not consider it a shoe that is being worn, but rather a wooden object that is being carried. The Gemara asks how Rabbi Meir can be the *tanna* of the *baraita*, as the continuation of the *baraita* states *ḥalitza* is disqualified if performed **with an *anpileya*** made **of cloth,** as this cloth shoe is not to be considered a shoe. If so, have **we come to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who rule in accordance with Rabbi Yosei, and render it prohibited to use any shoes for *ḥalitza* that are not made of leather?

Having identified that the *braita*'s valid cases include non-leather materials alongside the leather requirement for the slipper, the *Gemara* asks: *kav kita mani?* — who is the *tanna* behind the prosthetic ruling? The answer is Rabbi Meir, based on the *Mishnah* in *Shabbat*: *hakita yotzei b'kav shelo divrei Rabbi Meir* — an amputee may go out on *Shabbat* with his prosthetic, for Rabbi Meir classifies it as a shoe. Rabbi Yossi forbids it, considering the prosthetic an external object rather than a shoe.

Thus the ruling that a prosthetic validates *chalitza* reflects Rabbi Meir's position — and implicitly, Rabbi Meir's position that leather is not required. The end of the *braita*, however, distinguishes between leather and non-leather slippers, which sounds like the position of the *Rabbanan*. How can the beginning of the *braita* follow Rabbi Meir (no leather requirement) while the end follows the *Rabbanan* (leather required)?

Abaye resolves this by insisting that the *braita* follows the *Rabbanan* throughout: *mide-seifa rabbanan, reisha nami rabbanan*. Since the end requires leather for the slipper, the beginning must also be read as requiring leather throughout. Accordingly, the prosthetic case must involve a prosthetic covered in leather — *v'reisha b'mechuppeh or*. The *braita* is not validating a plain wooden prosthetic; it is validating one that has been covered with a leather surface.

### Rava: Functional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אֲבָל אֵין מְחוּפֶּה עוֹר, מַאי — פָּסוּל? אִי הָכִי, אַדְּתָנֵי סֵיפָא בְּאַנְפִּילְיָא שֶׁל בֶּגֶד, לִיפְלוֹג בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים: בִּמְחוּפֶּה עוֹר, אֲבָל אֵין מְחוּפֶּה עוֹר — פָּסוּל! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מִדְּרֵישָׁא רַבִּי מֵאִיר, סֵיפָא נָמֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר: הַאי — מַגֵּין, וְהַאי — לָא מַגֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: But** according to your explanation, if the prosthesis is **not covered in leather, what** would its status be? It would be **unfit. If so, rather than teaching** in the **latter clause: *Anpileya*** made **of cloth** is invalid for *ḥalitza*, **let** it **distinguish within** the matter **itself** and say: **In what** case **is this statement** that a wooden prosthesis is fit **said?** It is **in** the case of a prosthetic leg **covered in leather, but** if it is **not covered in leather, it is unfit.** **Rather,** the fact that the *baraita* was not taught in that manner indicates that Abaye's explanation is incorrect. Therefore, **Rava said** that the contradiction should be reconciled in another way: **Since the first clause** of the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** that the shoes need not be made of leather, **the latter clause is also** taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir**, and the distinction between a wooden prosthetic and an *anpileya* of cloth is: **This** prosthesis **protects** the foot, **and that** soft shoe **does not protect** the foot, as it does not have a hard sole. Rabbi Meir does not require that the shoe be of leather, but he does require that it be protective footwear.

Rava challenges this reading. If we are going to require a leather-covered prosthetic, the *braita* should have said so explicitly — it should have distinguished between a leather-covered prosthetic and a non-leather one, just as it distinguished between a leather slipper and a cloth slipper. The fact that the *braita* draws no such distinction for the prosthetic undermines Abaye's interpretation.

Rava therefore inverts the approach: *mide-reisha Rabbi Meir, seifa nami Rabbi Meir* — both the beginning and the end of the *braita* follow Rabbi Meir. And Rabbi Meir does not require leather as a formal criterion. How, then, does Rava explain the distinction between a leather slipper (valid) and a cloth slipper (invalid)? The answer is functional: *hai magein ve-hai lo magein* — one protects and one does not.

### Protective Function

Rava's position, which Rashi's language in the *Shekalim* context foreshadowed, is that leather is never a formal, definitional requirement in its own right. It is a proxy for protection. When a non-leather material is genuinely protective — a cork sandal, a fiber sandal, a felt prosthetic covering, a wooden peg-leg — it qualifies as a shoe and validates *chalitza*. When a non-leather material is not protective — a cloth slipper, which functions more like a sock — it does not qualify. The cloth slipper requires leather precisely because without leather it fails the protective function entirely; with leather, even the slipper becomes sufficiently shoe-like.

This has significant *halachic* implications. If Rava's functional reading is correct, the leather requirement for *Yom Kippur* is likewise not a formal rule but a reflection of leather's historically superior protective and shoe-defining qualities. A non-leather shoe that genuinely protects and supports the foot might, on this reading, be considered a shoe for *Yom Kippur* purposes — though for *chalitza*, the *pasuk* may impose a more fixed formal standard regardless.

## Chalitza Requirements

### Planted Feet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַאי מַאן דְּחָלֵיץ, צָרִיךְ לְמִדְחֲסֵיהּ לְכַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement in the *baraita* that indicates that *ḥalitza* may be performed even if the *yavam* is not standing, **Ameimar said: The one who performs *ḥalitza*** by having his *yevama* remove his shoe **must press his foot** to the ground, and while in this position the *yevama* will remove his shoe.

Ameimar introduces a further requirement: *hai man de-chalitz tzarich le-midchasei le-char'ei* — the man performing *chalitza* must push his foot firmly to the ground. The reasoning, while not fully explicit, seems to be that the act of *chalitza* involves a transition — from foot firmly planted to foot unplanted as the shoe is removed. The moment of removal is defined against the prior state of the foot being fully planted.

### Position Flexibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר, וְהָתַנְיָא: בֵּין עוֹמֵד, בֵּין יוֹשֵׁב, בֵּין מוּטֶּה! אֵימָא: וּלְעוֹלָם דְּדָחֵיס לְכַרְעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: But isn't it taught** in the *baraita* above: She may perform *ḥalitza* **whether he was standing or sitting or leaning?** One who is leaning cannot easily press his foot into the ground. He answered him: **Say** that the man may perform *ḥalitza* while in any of these positions, but that **actually** this is true only if **he presses his foot** to the ground, which is admittedly more difficult to do while leaning.

Rav Ashi challenges Ameimar from the *braita*: *bein omed bein yoshev bein mutteh* — whether standing, sitting, or leaning, the *chalitza* is valid. If firm planting were essential, how could a reclining position qualify? Ameimar's response, as the *Gemara* understands it, is that these positions are not in conflict: whether sitting, standing, or leaning, the requirement is that the foot be firmly planted on the ground. Even someone reclining — much like sitting in a recliner — must have his feet pressing down on the ground. The position of the body is flexible; the grounding of the foot is not.

### Foot Disability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַאי מַאן דִּמְסַגֵּי עַל לִיחֲתָא דְּכַרְעֵיהּ, לָא חָלֵיץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Ameimar** also **said** about this issue: **Someone who walks on the backs of his feet,** meaning he is clubfooted and his foot is twisted upside down, **cannot perform *ḥalitza*.

Ameimar adds a second ruling: *hai man de-masgei al lachusta de-char'ei lo chalitz* — a person who walks on the front of his foot — whose foot is twisted such that the upper part of the foot drags along the ground rather than the sole — cannot perform *chalitza*. Even if such a person is wearing a shoe, it is not functioning as a shoe for him: the sole of the shoe is not what protects his foot in contact with the ground; rather, it is the upper portion of the shoe. Since the shoe does not functionally serve the purpose of a shoe for this individual, its removal does not constitute valid *chalitza*.

This connects to a famous incident in *Moed Katan*: two eulogizers who said something Rav Ashi found inappropriate received the evil eye from him, rendering them lame. Rav Ashi applies Ameimar's ruling to say that these individuals — *lav bar chalitz ve-lav bar kifaf chalitz* — cannot perform *chalitza* because their lameness prevents them from wearing shoes in a normal, functional way.

## Prosthetic Debate

### Support Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר, וְהָתַנְיָא: סְמִיכוּת הָרַגְלַיִם, לָאו דְּחָלֵיץ בַּהּ אִיהוּ? לָא, דְּיָהֵיב לֵיהּ לְאַחֵר וְחָלֵיץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Leg supports** can be used for *ḥalitza*. **Does this not mean that** this lame individual **performs *ḥalitza* using these** supports on his knees? This would indicate that even one with twisted feet can perform *ḥalitza*. The Gemara answers: **No,** the intention is that if **he gave these supports to another** whose foot is shaped normally **and he** wore them while **performing *ḥalitza*,** it is valid. That other one is allowed to perform *ḥalitza* while wearing these supports because they are also considered shoes, but one whose foot is misshapen may not perform *ḥalitza* with them, as it functions for him as a foot, not a shoe.

Rav Ashi raises a question against Ameimar's ruling about the person who walks on the front of his foot. The *braita* listed *smichat haraglayim* — the supports for the feet of the person who drags himself — as a valid *chalitza* object. In that case, the person's feet are also not contacting the ground through their soles; they are dragging. Is that not precisely the same functional situation?

The *Gemara* clarifies: no. The *braita* is not saying that this person himself uses those foot-supports for *chalitza*. Rather, *de-yahvi lei le-achrini ve-chalatz* — someone else wears those supports. The supports are valid *chalitza* objects, but only when used by a person whose feet function normally. The person who actually drags himself cannot perform *chalitza* with those supports, because for him they are not functioning as shoes on his feet. For a person with normal feet who wears the same object, it counts as a shoe and *chalitza* is valid.

### Rashi: Shok

Rashi addresses an apparent difficulty: if the person who walks on the front of his foot cannot perform *chalitza* because the shoe is not protecting the sole of his foot, why does the amputee below the knee fare differently? After all, the prosthetic is not covering the sole of a foot either — the foot is gone.

Rashi explains by invoking the principle that emerges from the *Mishnah*'s ruling: *uve'af al gav de-tenan min ha-arkuvah u-le-mata kesherah* — the *Mishnah* validates *chalitza* from below the knee. Rashi's reading: *chi nachis raglo kamei lei shok* — when the leg descends, what is there is the *shok*, the calf. *De-rachmana amar me-al raglo de-hainu shoko* — the Torah says *me-al raglo*, "above his foot," meaning above what now serves as his foot, which is the *shok*. The prosthetic that covers and protects the base of the *shok* is therefore protecting what is halachically considered the foot. This is fundamentally different from the person who walks on the front of his foot: that person's actual foot exists and is present, but the shoe does not protect its sole.

### Tosafot View

Tosafot takes a different approach entirely. Rather than distinguishing between the amputee and the front-foot walker on the grounds of what counts as the foot, Tosafot reads the prosthetic case in the *braita* identically to the dragging-foot case: in both instances, the *braita* is describing the object being used by someone other than the person who actually has the condition. The prosthetic, on this reading, is valid *chalitza* only when used by a person who does not have an amputated leg — perhaps by bending the leg underneath somehow. The amputee himself, just as the person who drags himself, cannot perform *chalitza* using his prosthetic.

This is a striking position and creates significant practical consequences. On Tosafot's reading, an amputee below the knee cannot perform *chalitza* with his own prosthetic, just as someone who walks on the front of his foot cannot perform *chalitza*. Tosafot supports this by explicitly citing the *Gemara*'s ruling about *smichat haraglayim* as the model: *yahvi le-achrini ve-chalatz, ke-de-amar somchos ha-reglayim*.

### Above Knee

Both Rashi and Tosafot agree that if the amputation is above the knee, *chalitza* is impossible. The *Gemara*'s distinction — *min ha-arkuvah u-le-mata kesherah* — limits validity to below-the-knee amputation. Beyond that, the only debate between the *rishonim* is about below-the-knee amputation: can the amputee himself use his prosthetic (*Rashi*), or only a non-amputee wearing that same prosthetic (*Tosafot*)?

This raises a profound and sobering question. Suppose a man has no brothers other than one who is severely lame — unable to wear shoes normally — or whose leg is amputated above the knee. The *yevamah* cannot obtain *chalitza*. Everyone wants to do the right thing, everything is known, and yet there is no halachic solution. This is categorically different from the *agunah* cases where the problem is evidentiary uncertainty. Here, the facts are clear, all parties are willing, and still *chalitza* may be legally unavailable. What the *poskim* say about such a case would require separate investigation.

## Regel: Foot or Leg

### Regel Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה וּלְמַטָּה כּוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהִי: רְגָלִים — פְּרָט לְבַעֲלֵי קַבִּין!
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that if one's leg was amputated **from the knee down** and his *yevama* performed *ḥalitza* with him, the *ḥalitza* is valid. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita* that comments on the pilgrimage one makes to Jerusalem during a Festival. The Torah states: "Three **Festivals [*regalim*]** you shall celebrate for Me in the year" (Exodus 23:14). The *baraita* comments on the verse: Festivals are referred to in the verse as *regalim*, which literally means feet, indicating that one must actually make the pilgrimage to Jerusalem by foot [*regel*] during the Festival, which comes to **exclude people who have prostheses.** This indicates that a prosthetic foot is not called a *regel*, which seems to contradict the mishna that allows *ḥalitza* on a prosthetic from the knee down.

The *Gemara* now addresses a foundational linguistic question that underlies all the above: does *regel* in the relevant *pasuk* — *v'chaltzah na'alo me'al raglo* — mean *foot* specifically, or does it mean *leg*? This matters enormously. If *regel* means leg, then amputation above the knee should still leave enough of the leg to satisfy *me-al raglo*, and *chalitza* should remain possible. If *regel* means only foot, then we must work our way up from the foot and the knee joint becomes the outer limit.

Rav Kahana raises a challenge: *beshelia yetzei mibein ragleha* — a miscarried fetus that comes out from between her *reglayim*. The *regel* in question here clearly refers to the thigh region, not the foot. This suggests *regel* can mean leg — even the upper leg.

### Euphemistic Usage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵעַל רַגְלוֹ״. אִי הָכִי, לְמַעְלָה מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה נָמֵי! ״מֵעַל״, וְלָא מֵעַל דְּמֵעַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is different here,** with respect to *ḥalitza*, **as it is written:** "She removes the shoe **from on his foot [*me'al raglo*]"** (Deuteronomy 25:9), which indicates that not only can his actual foot be used for performing *ḥalitza*, but also the part above it, i.e., the calf. The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** that one may use a part of his leg above his foot to perform *ḥalitza*, then if one's leg was amputated **from above the knee,** he should **also** be eligible for *ḥalitza*; and yet, the same mishna taught that only one with a leg amputated from below the knee is eligible for *ḥalitza*. The Gemara answers that the verse states: **"From on** his foot," meaning above his foot, **but not: From on that which is on** his foot; the wording indicates that it can be above his foot until the knee, but not any further above that.

Abaye responds to Rav Kahana's challenge: *bisha'a she'kar'a lileid, no'etzet ekeveha beyarcheha* — when a woman crouches to give birth, she digs her heels into her sides. At that moment, what comes out literally comes out from between her feet. Abaye is reading *ragleha* as feet, not thighs, because of the crouching posture.

The *Gemara* then marshals further proofs. *Lo asa raglav velo asa sefamo* — he did not do his *raglav* and did not trim his mustache. The *Gemara* understands this as referring to pubic grooming, where *raglav* is used as a euphemism for the private area. Similarly, *vayavo Shaul lehasech et raglav* — Saul went to relieve himself — where *raglav* again euphemistically refers to the area between the thighs, not to the feet themselves. And *ach masi'ach hu et raglav* — he is relieving himself in an inner chamber.

In each of these cases the *Gemara* gives the same answer: *lishna me'alya* — refined language. The word *regel* is being used euphemistically to avoid direct mention of the thighs or private areas. The underlying denotation of *regel* remains *foot*; it is only deployed as indirect speech when referring to intimate areas. The fact that it is pressed into service as a euphemism does not mean it actually denotes the entire leg.

### Foot Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, הַאי אִיסְתָּוִירָא עַד אַרְעָא נָחֵית. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מִיפְסָק פָּסֵיק, הָוֵה לֵיהּ אִיהוּ ״מֵעַל״, וְשׁוֹקָא מֵעַל דְּמֵעַל! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא מִיפְסָק פָּסֵיק, כֹּל דְּבַהֲדֵי כַּרְעָא — כְּכַרְעָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Learn from here that the heel bone [*istavira*] reaches to the ground** where it connects to the foot, **for if it enters your mind** to say **that it is separate and divided,** and there is another bone in between, **then that** ankle bone **is "from on** the foot" **and the calf** would be prohibited for *ḥalitza*, as it would be considered: **From on that which is on** his foot. **Rav Ashi said: Even** if **you say that it is separate and divided** from the calf, because **anything that is connected with the** sole of the **foot is considered like the foot,** then the ankle is certainly part of the foot, making the calf the area that is "from on the foot."

After working through all these cases — including *bein ragleha kara shachav* from the episode of Yael and Sisera, which the *Gemara* also answers with *lishna me'alya* — the conclusion is firm: *regel* means foot. The *Gemara* consistently deflects every apparent counterexample as euphemistic usage rather than evidence of a broader denotation. The consequence is maintained: *chalitza* requires the shoe to be removed from what is legally the foot, and amputation above the knee removes any possibility of meeting that requirement.

## Sisera & Yael

### Seven Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁבַע בְּעִילוֹת בָּעַל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֵּין רַגְלֶיהָ כָּרַע נָפַל שָׁכָב בֵּין רַגְלֶיהָ כָּרַע נָפָל בַּאֲשֶׁר כָּרַע שָׁם נָפַל שָׁדוּד״. וְהָא קָא מִתְהַנְיָא מֵעֲבֵירָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on what happened when Sisera was in Yael's tent. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: That wicked** man, Sisera, **had** sexual **intercourse** with Yael **seven times that day, as it is stated: "At her feet he sunk, he fell, he lay; at her feet he sunk, he fell; where he sunk, there he fell down dead"** (Judges 5:27). Each instance of the terms "sunk," "fell," or "lay" in the verse indicates an act of intercourse, as Yael sought to tire and weaken Sisera to enable her to kill him. The Gemara asks: **But** how could Yael do this even for the noble purpose of killing the wicked Sisera, as **she derived pleasure from** the **transgression** of licentious sexual relations with a gentile?

Having cited the *pasuk* from Yael and Sisera in the linguistic discussion, the *Gemara* pivots to an extended *aggadic* passage. Rabbi Yochanan states: *sheva be'ilot ba'al oto rasha be'oto hayom* — that wicked man had relations with Yael seven times that day. The source is a close reading of the *pesukim* in *Shoftim*: *bein ragleha kara nafal shachav, bein ragleha kara nafal, ba'asher kara sham nafal shadud*. Counting the verbs — *kara, nafal, shachav, kara, nafal, kara, nafal* — yields seven acts.

### Pleasure Problem

The *Gemara* immediately raises a serious theological and moral difficulty: *v'ha kamistanya mei'aveira?* — but was Yael not deriving pleasure from a transgression? This question would seem to undermine the moral heroism for which she is celebrated. If she experienced physical pleasure in the course of her sin, even if the sin was committed for a righteous purpose, how does the tradition valorize her act? The concern is not merely whether she *could* do it — the concept of *aveirah lishmah* addresses that — but whether an act from which one derives pleasure can be celebrated as self-sacrifice.

### Zuhama Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: כׇּל טוֹבָתָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים — רָעָה הִיא אֵצֶל צַדִּיקִים. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ פֶּן תְּדַבֵּר עִם יַעֲקֹב מִטּוֹב עַד רָע״, בִּשְׁלָמָא רַע — לְחַיֵּי, אֶלָּא טוֹב אַמַּאי לָא? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: טוֹבָתָן שֶׁל רְשָׁעִים — רָעָה הִיא אֵצֶל צַדִּיקִים. בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם, דִּלְמָא מַדְכַּר לֵיהּ שְׁמָא דַּעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. אֶלָּא הָכָא, מַאי רָעָה אִיכָּא? דְּקָא שָׁדֵי בָּהּ זוּהֲמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: Every** act that is a **benefit for the wicked** **is a disadvantage for the righteous,** as a righteous individual gains no pleasure from this so-called beneficial act. **As it is stated** by God to Laban: **"Take heed to yourself that you speak not to Jacob either good or bad"** (Genesis 31:24). **Granted,** speak no **bad; this is rightly so,** i.e., understandable. **But** speak no **good? Why not? Rather, learn from here** that even something that would be **a** good **benefit to the wicked** like Laban, **is a disadvantage for the righteous.** The Gemara asks: **Granted, there,** in Laban's words to Jacob, it is understandable that there could be a certain repulsive aspect to a wicked man speaking nicely to a righteous individual, as **perhaps he will mention to him the name of** the **idol** he **worships** and even though he means well, it still would repulse Jacob. **But here, what disadvantage is there** if she derives benefit from licentious relations with a wicked man? The Gemara answers: **He implants filth in her** and contaminates her, as her body accepts his semen.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the question in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *kol tovasan shel resha'im ra'ah hi etzel tzaddikim* — any good that the wicked have to offer is, from the perspective of the righteous, only evil. The verse cited is God's warning to Lavan: *hishamer lecha pen tedaber im Yaakov mitov ad ra'* — take care not to speak to Yaakov from good to bad. The *Gemara* understands: not speaking bad to Yaakov is obvious; why also not speak good? This demonstrates that whatever good a *rasha* would offer is experienced by the righteous only as harm.

But the *Gemara* does not accept this as a bare metaphysical principle and probes further: in Lavan's case, the explanation is clear — any blessing he would pronounce would invoke the name of his god (*Elohei Nachor*, etc.), which Yaakov would not want. But what is the harm in Sisera's case, where the concern seems to be purely physical pleasure? The *Gemara* answers: *de-ka shadei bah zuhama* — because Sisera cast into her a *zuhama*, a spiritual stench or contamination.

### Theology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁבָּא נָחָשׁ עַל חַוָּה הֵטִיל בָּהּ זוּהֲמָא. יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁעָמְדוּ עַל הַר סִינַי — פָּסְקָה זוּהֲמָתָן. גּוֹיִם, שֶׁלֹּא עָמְדוּ בְּהַר סִינַי — לֹא פָּסְקָה זוּהֲמָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rabbi Yoḥanan** also **said,** based on his understanding that the serpent seduced Eve into having sexual relations with him: **When the serpent came upon Eve, he infected her with** moral **contamination,** and this contamination remained in all human beings. When the **Jewish people stood at Mount Sinai their contamination ceased,** whereas with regard to **gentiles, who did not stand at Mount Sinai, their contamination never ceased.** Therefore, Yael was repulsed by the contamination that she allowed into her body, and she did not benefit from relations with Sisera.

This leads to a broader theological statement from Rabbi Yochanan: *besha'ah sheba nachash al Chava hitil bah zuhama* — when the snake had relations with Chava, he instilled in her a *zuhama*. *Yisrael she'amdu al Har Sinai, paska zuhamatan* — when Israel stood at Sinai, their *zuhama* ceased. *Ovdei kochavim shelo amdu al Har Sinai, lo paska zuhamatan* — the idolaters, who did not stand at Sinai, retain their *zuhama* to this day.

The implied polemical context is worth noting. This tradition appears to be a Jewish response to the Pauline Christian doctrine of original sin, reversing its terms entirely: the Jews are not burdened with original sin — they were cleansed of it at Sinai. It is the nations who never received the Torah who retain the primordial contamination of the snake's encounter with Chava. Far from sex being intrinsically sinful, as early Christian theology would have it, the Jewish people were specifically liberated from the condition that makes it so. Hence, when Sisera — a non-Jew carrying the full *zuhama* of the unredeemed nations — had relations with Yael, whatever physical sensation she experienced was accompanied by a deep spiritual contamination that entirely negated any pleasure. The good that a *rasha* offers the righteous is not merely neutral; it actively does harm.

Tosafot's engagement with this *aggadah* focuses on the *aveirah lishmah* dimension. Even granting that what Yael did was halachically justified as a great act done for the sake of *Klal Yisrael*, the *pesukim* celebrate her with a heroism that implies self-sacrifice. If she were experiencing genuine pleasure throughout, the celebration would be misplaced — there is no sacrifice in an act one enjoys. The answer, then, is not to deny the *aveirah lishmah* justification but to explain why it was also a genuine sacrifice: the *zuhama* ensured that what appeared to be pleasure was in fact causing her harm.

## Shoe Ownership

### Fit Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָלְצָה בְּמִנְעָל שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״נַעֲלוֹ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נַעֲלוֹ. נַעַל שֶׁל כׇּל אָדָם, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נַעַל״ — נַעַל מִכׇּל מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that **if she performed *ḥalitza* using a shoe that was not his,** the *ḥalitza* is valid. **The Sages taught:** From the verse "And remove **his shoe** from on his foot" (Deuteronomy 25:9), **I have** derived **only** that the *yavam* may wear **"his shoe,"** i.e., a shoe that belongs to him; **from where** do I derive that he may wear **any person's** shoe? **The verse states** the words **"shoe"** and **"shoe"** twice: "And remove his shoe" (Deuteronomy 25:9), and "The house of he who had his shoe removed" (Deuteronomy 25:10), to teach us that **in any case** it is acceptable, i.e., the shoes of another person may also be worn for *ḥalitza*.

The *Gemara* turns to the question of whether *chalitza* may be performed using a shoe that belongs to someone else. The *braita* derives from the word *na'alo* — his shoe — that the shoe must be *ra'ui lo*, appropriate for him.

### Borrowed Allowed

The derivation proceeds through *gezeirah shavah* or *ribbui*: the word *na'al* appears multiple times in the *pasuk*, and from the repetition the *braita* learns *na'al na'al mikol makom* — the word *shoe* in any context, meaning even a borrowed shoe is valid. The term *na'alo* thus does not require ownership but suitability.

### Shoe Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״נַעֲלוֹ״? נַעֲלוֹ הָרָאוּי לוֹ. פְּרָט לְגָדוֹל שֶׁאֵין יָכוֹל לַהֲלוֹךְ בּוֹ, וּפְרָט לְקָטָן שֶׁאֵינוֹ חוֹפֶה רוֹב רַגְלוֹ, וּפְרָט לְסַנְדָּל הַמְסוּלְיָים שֶׁאֵין לוֹ עָקֵב.
>
> **Translation:** But **if so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states: "His shoe,"** which seems to indicate that he must own the shoe that he is wearing? It teaches that one must wear **"his shoe,"** i.e., a shoe that is **fitting for him, excluding** a shoe so **large that he is unable to walk in it, and excluding** a shoe **so small it does not cover most of his foot, and** also **excluding a heelless sandal [*sandal hamesulyam*],** a sandal **that** has only a sole but **does not have a heel** and is not fit for walking.

From *na'alo hara'ui lo* — a shoe appropriate for him — the *braita* derives several exclusions. A shoe too large, such that he cannot walk in it normally (*perat legadol she'eino yachol lahaloch bah*), is invalid. A shoe too small, such that it does not cover most of his foot (*perat lekatan she'eino chofeh rov raglo*), is invalid. And *u'perat lesandal hamasulayin she'ein lo akeiv* — a sandal of the type that has no heel is excluded. This last exclusion shifts from a personal fitness requirement to an objective structural standard: regardless of fit, a heelless sandal does not qualify.

### Abaye Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה קָאֵי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲתַאי יְבָמָה לַחְלוֹץ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לֵיהּ סַנְדָּלָךְ, יְהַיב לֵיהּ סַנְדָּלָא דִשְׂמָאלָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימַר דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן, דִּיעֲבַד, לְכַתְּחִלָּה מִי אֲמוּר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Abaye was standing before Rav Yosef and a *yevama* came to perform *ḥalitza*.** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: **Give** the *yavam* **your sandal** so that the *ḥalitza* may begin. **He gave him his left sandal.** Rav Yosef **said to him:** Granted, one can **say that the Sages said** that it is permitted to perform *ḥalitza* with the left shoe **after the fact,** but **did they say that** it is also permitted ***ab initio*?**

The *Gemara* relates an incident involving Abaye and Rav Yosef. Abaye was standing before Rav Yosef when a *yevamah* came to perform *chalitza*. Rav Yosef instructed Abaye: *hav lei sandalach* — give the *yavam* your sandal. Perhaps the *yavam* had only a non-leather slipper or otherwise unsuitable footwear. Abaye, apparently wishing to make a point, deliberately gave the *yavam* his left sandal — knowing that the right sandal is the *lechatchila* requirement. Rav Yosef rebuked him: when did the rabbis say a left sandal is acceptable? Only *b'dieved*. They never said *lechatchila*.

### Transfer Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי הָכִי, סַנְדָּל שֶׁאֵין שֶׁלּוֹ נָמֵי: אֵימַר דַּאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן דִּיעֲבַד, לְכַתְּחִלָּה מִי אֲמוּר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ: הַב לֵיהּ וְאַקְנִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: If so, then** also **with regard to a sandal that does not belong to him, say that the Sages said** that it is permitted **after the fact;** however, **did they say** it is permitted as well to perform *ḥalitza* using another's shoe ***ab initio*?** Rav Yosef **said to** Abaye: **This is what I was saying to you: Give him** your sandal **and transfer ownership to him** by giving it to him as a temporary gift so that it will be his, and therefore the *ḥalitza* will be performed in an ideal manner, without any question as to its validity.

Abaye responded with equal sharpness: and when did you tell me to give him my sandal? When did the rabbis say a borrowed sandal is acceptable? Only *b'dieved*. So why are you asking me to give him a borrowed sandal *lechatchila* in the first place? My use of the left sandal is no more problematic than your instruction to lend him mine.

Rav Yosef clarified his original intent: *hav lei v'aknei lei* — give it to him and transfer possession to him. The instruction was not to lend the sandal but to make the *yavam* the owner of it for the duration of the *chalitza*, after which it would be returned. Since the sandal would be legally his at the moment of *chalitza*, the concern of borrowed footwear is entirely resolved.

## Wood & Disease

### Wooden Sandals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַנְדָּל שֶׁל עֵץ. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: רַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַקִּיטֵּעַ יוֹצֵא בְּקַב שֶׁלּוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹסֵר. אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אוֹמֵר: בִּמְחוּפֶּה עוֹר, וְדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught that if the *yevama* performed *ḥalitza* while the *yavam* was wearing **a wooden sandal** the *ḥalitza* is valid. The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who taught that it is permitted to use a wooden sandal? **Shmuel said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, as we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 65b): **One with an amputated leg may go out** on Shabbat **with his wooden leg,** as it has the legal status of a shoe; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** He reasons that the prosthesis functions like the shoe of any other person, indicating that Rabbi Meir is not especially concerned about the material from which the shoe is made. And **Rabbi Yosei prohibits** it, since he does not consider it to have the legal status of a shoe. Alternatively, **the father of Shmuel says:** Here the mishna is referring to a wooden sandal **that is covered in leather, and all agree.** This *halakha* was taught in accordance with all opinions, as even Rabbi Yosei agrees that the leather covering makes it a shoe.

The *Gemara* now asks about wooden sandals: *sandal shel etz, man tanna?* — who is the *tanna* that holds a wooden sandal is valid for *chalitza*? The question circles back to the leather issue. If wood is valid, that is in tension with any formal leather requirement.

Shmuel answers: it is Rabbi Meir, based on the same *Mishnah* — *hakiteia yotzei b'kav shelo divrei Rabbi Meir* — where Rabbi Meir classifies a wooden prosthetic as a shoe for purposes of *Shabbat*. Attributing the wooden sandal ruling to Rabbi Meir places this leniency in the same camp: Rabbi Meir does not require leather. Rabbi Yosei disagrees and forbids the prosthetic on *Shabbat* — for him, it is not a shoe.

### Shmuel: Covered

Shmuel then offers an alternative reading: *b'mechupah or divrei hakol* — the wooden sandal case is universally valid, because it is speaking of a wooden sandal covered in leather. On this reading, the permissibility of the wooden sandal is not evidence against a leather requirement; rather, the leather covering satisfies the requirement. This mirrors Abaye's earlier attempt to read leather covering into the prosthetic case, which Rava rejected. Here, though, it is Shmuel advancing the position, and the *Gemara* does not immediately challenge it.

### Tzara'at Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פַּפִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: סַנְדָּל הַמּוּסְגָּר — לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ בּוֹ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה. סַנְדָּל הַמּוּחְלָט — לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ בּוֹ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappi said in the name of Rava: One should not perform *ḥalitza*** on a *yavam* wearing **a quarantined sandal,** i.e., a sandal examined by a priest who found its signs of leprosy to be inconclusive, and places the sandal in isolation for a waiting period of up to two weeks to see if clear indications of leprosy develop. **But if she did perform *ḥalitza*** while the *yavam* was wearing it, **her *ḥalitza* is** nevertheless **valid** after the fact. On the other hand, if the sandal with leprosy is **a confirmed sandal,** i.e., a sandal that was definitively ruled to have leprosy, **one may not perform *ḥalitza* with it, and if she did perform *ḥalitza*** while the man was wearing it, **her *ḥalitza* is disqualified.** As an object with confirmed leprosy must be burned, it is considered halakhically as if it were already burnt, and is consequently considered to lack the qualities of a shoe necessary for *ḥalitza*.

The *Gemara* then presents the rulings of Rav Papa in the name of Rava regarding sandals afflicted with *nega tzara'at* — signs of ritual leprosy that appear in leather objects. The case of *sandal hamusgar* is a sandal that has been sealed (*musgar*) — placed in quarantine pending inspection after the first week, not yet definitively determined to be *tamei*. The case of *sandal hamuchlat* is a sandal definitively ruled to be *metzora* and therefore subject to destruction.

### Rav Papa Strict

According to one version of Rav Papa's ruling in the name of Rava: a *musgar* sandal — *lo tachlotz bo* — should not be used for *chalitza lechatchila*, but *v'im chaltza chalitzatah kesherah* — if she did, the *chalitza* is valid *b'dieved*. A *muchlat* sandal, however — *lo tachlotz bo, v'im chaltza chalitzatah pesulah* — must not be used, and if she did, the *chalitza* is invalid even *b'dieved*. The reason for this stricter position is the principle of *kitutei michtat shiurei* — an object that is going to be destroyed is considered as if already destroyed, or at minimum has its legally relevant thresholds undermined. A sandal that has been definitively ruled *metzora* and is headed for destruction cannot be considered a shoe in the full legal sense.

### Lenient View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: אֶחָד סַנְדָּל הַמּוּסְגָּר וְאֶחָד סַנְדָּל הַמּוּחְלָט — לֹא תַּחֲלוֹץ בּוֹ, וְאִם חָלְצָה — חֲלִיצָתָהּ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In contrast, **Rav Pappa said in the name of Rava:** With regard to **both a quarantined sandal and a confirmed sandal** the same *halakha* applies: **She may not perform *ḥalitza* with it** *ab initio*, **but if she did perform *ḥalitza*** with it, **her *ḥalitza* is valid.**

A second version of the ruling, attributed again to Rav Papa in the name of Rava, takes a more lenient position: *echad sandal hamusgar v'echad sandal hamuchlat* — both the sealed and the definitively afflicted sandal — *lo tachlotz bo lechatchila* — should not be used *lechatchila*; *v'im chaltza chalitzatah kesherah* — but if she did, the *chalitza* is valid in both cases. This view rejects the application of *kitutei michtat shiurei* to the *muchlat* sandal, holding that however undesirable its use, the sandal retains its status as a shoe sufficiently to validate *chalitza b'dieved*. The discussion of *kitutei michtat shiurei* and the resolution between these two positions will continue in the next *daf*.
