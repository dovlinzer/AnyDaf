# Yoma 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Backup Wife & Onen

### Backup Wife Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַפְרֵעַ דְּגִיטָּא דְּהָא לָאו גִּיטָּא הוּא, וְעָבֵיד לֵיהּ עֲבוֹדָה בִּשְׁנֵי בָתִּים! אֶלָּא, דְּאָמַר לַהּ: הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטֵּיךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתָּמוּת חֲבֶרְתִּיךְ. וְדִילְמָא מָיְיתָא חֲבֶרְתַּהּ, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ גִּיטָּא דְּהָא גִּיטָּא, וְקָם לֵיהּ בְּלֹא בַּיִת!
>
> **Translation:** **retroactively that the bill of divorce of this** second woman **is not a** valid **bill of divorce,** since the first wife died. In that case, it turns out retroactively that **he performed** part of **the service with two houses,** married to two wives. **Rather,** it is a case **where** the High Priest **said to** the second wife: **This is your bill of divorce on condition that your counterpart dies.** The Gemara asks: In this case, too, **perhaps her counterpart** will **die and the bill of divorce of this** second woman **is** a valid **bill of divorce, and** he will **remain without a house** at all.

The daf opens by returning to the conclusions reached in the previous session regarding how the *kohen gadol* could maintain his marital status in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda — always married to exactly one wife at precisely the right moment. The *gemara* had understood that the word *beito* requires not merely *kiddushin* but full *nissuim*, an actual lived reality of having established a household together. Had *kiddushin* alone sufficed, the solution would have been straightforward: one could simply stipulate that the *kiddushin* with the second wife takes effect the moment the first wife dies. But *nissuim* cannot be activated by a retroactive stipulation in that manner, since it reflects an existing domestic reality rather than a formal legal status. The *gemara* was therefore forced to construct a more complicated framework — not one in which the marriage takes effect upon some future event, but one in which a *get* takes effect retroactively.

This second complication was compounded by the *gemara*'s further assumption that the *kohen gadol* must be married to precisely one wife, no more and no fewer. That assumption was challenged from the case of *yibum*, where the word *bayis* is used and the obligation applies even with two wives and even after mere *erusin* before *nissuim*. Both of the *gemara*'s working assumptions were thus called into question by the *yibum* parallel, yet the *gemara* ultimately held its position and concluded that the *kohen gadol* would need to be married to two wives simultaneously, each of whom has received a *get* formulated under different conditional terms, arranged so that regardless of which wife dies, he will retroactively have been married only to the surviving one at any given moment. In this way, the *gemara* constructed what amounts to a mechanism for a non-married *agun* — a striking conceptual creation.

### Onen Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַקְרִיב אוֹנֵן, וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַיּוֹם. מַאי ״כׇּל הַיּוֹם״? אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לַהֲבִיאוֹ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the death of the wife of the High Priest, the Gemara cites an additional *baraita*. **The Sages taught: A High Priest sacrifices** offerings when he is an **acute mourner,** on the day of a relative's death, but **does not eat** from those offerings. **Rabbi Yehuda says: The entire day.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **The entire day?** Rava said: This phrase **is necessary only to bring him from his house.** Not only is it permitted for the High Priest to serve in the Temple when he is an acute mourner, but it is a mitzva to bring him from his house to serve in the Temple for the entire day to help ease his pain.

With that background established, the *gemara* now raises a further question: if the *kohen gadol*'s wife dies, would he not become an *onen*? The relevant *halacha* is that a *kohen gadol*, unlike a *kohen hedyot*, is permitted to continue performing the *avodah* even as an *onen*, as the verse states *u-min ha-mikdash lo yeitzei v'lo yechalel et mikdash elohav* — he does not leave the *beit ha-mikdash* even when an immediate relative dies. He therefore continues performing the *avodah*. It should be noted that as an *onen* he cannot eat from the *korbanot*, but since it is Yom Kippur and no one eats in any case, this would seem to present no difficulty.

A parenthetical point is worth raising here. The simple reading of the *pesukim* actually does not list a *kohen gadol*'s wife among the relatives to whom he would become *tamei*. The Torah lists his father, mother, siblings — *le-aviv u-le-imo, le-achiv u-le-achoto* — but the plain sense of the text does not mention a wife. Indeed, regarding a *kohen hedyot*, the verse *lo yitama ba'al be-amav le-hachalo* was understood by some as indicating that a husband-*kohen* cannot become *tamei* for his wife at all. Nevertheless, *chazal* derive from the phrase *ki im le-she'ero ha-karov elav* — "his flesh that is close to him" — that *she'ero zo ishto*, his wife is included in this category. The *Rambam* understands the *kohen hedyot*'s obligation regarding his wife to be Rabbinic in origin, and even so the Rabbis deemed it serious enough to override the *tumah* restrictions of *kehuna*. The assumption throughout, then, is that a *kohen* — whether *gadol* or *hedyot* — is an *onen* upon the death of his wife.

### Gezeirah Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַשְׁתָּא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אַפּוֹקֵי מַפְּקִינַן לֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: הָיָה עוֹמֵד וּמַקְרִיב עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְשָׁמַע שֶׁמֵּת לוֹ מֵת, מַנִּיחַ עֲבוֹדָתוֹ וְיוֹצֵא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יִגְמוֹר. וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מַיְיתִינַן לֵיהּ מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Now,** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, we remove** the High Priest from the Temple when he is an acute mourner, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: If a common priest **was standing and sacrificing** an offering **on top of the altar and heard that** a relative **of his died, he leaves his service** in the middle **and exits** the Temple; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: He completes** the service and then leaves. Rabbi Yehuda rules stringently in the case of a priest who is an acute mourner sacrificing an offering. Even though the *baraita* is referring to a common priest, it is reasonable to say that the same is true with regard to a High Priest as well. Rabbi Yehuda says that a High Priest who becomes an acute mourner exits the Temple, **and you say we bring** the High Priest who is an acute mourner **from his house** to serve?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹבֵד כׇּל הַיּוֹם, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאכַל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה לְרָבָא: וּמִי גָּזַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאכַל? וְהָתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אַף אִשָּׁה אַחֶרֶת מַתְקִינִין לוֹ, שֶׁמָּא תָּמוּת אִשְׁתּוֹ. וְאִי מָיְיתָא אִשְׁתּוֹ עָבֵיד עֲבוֹדָה, וְלָא גָּזַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאכַל!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehuda means **to say that** the High Priest **does not serve for the entire day** even though the Torah allows him to do so, due to a rabbinic **decree lest** he forget that he is an acute mourner and **eat** consecrated food forbidden to him. **Rav Adda bar Ahava said to Rava: And did Rabbi Yehuda issue a decree lest he eat? But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The Sages would **even designate another wife for him lest his wife die? And if his wife dies,** he nevertheless **performs** the Temple **service, and Rabbi Yehuda did not issue a decree lest he eat.** That contradicts the other statement by Rabbi Yehuda that a High Priest may not serve for the entire day that he is an acute mourner.

The potential problem arises not from the *avodah* itself but from a ruling of Rabbi Yehuda. The *gemara* at the top of 14a states: *she-eino oved kol hayom* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, a *kohen gadol* who is an *onen* may not perform the *avodah* for the entirety of that day. The reason is *gezeirah shema yochal* — a Rabbinic precautionary decree lest, if permitted to serve, he come to inadvertently eat from the *korbanot*, which an *onen* is forbidden to do. This is, it must be acknowledged, a striking piece of reasoning, since it overrides the explicit biblical verse *min ha-mikdash lo yeitzei* by means of a Rabbinic concern about a secondary violation.

Rav Adda bar Ahava posed a challenge to Rava: does Rabbi Yehuda truly prohibit the *kohen gadol* from performing the *avodah* out of concern he might eat from the *korbanot*? After all, the *mishna* itself, in articulating Rabbi Yehuda's position, states *af isha acheret matkinim lo shema tamut ishto* — we set aside a backup wife for him lest his primary wife die. The implication is that if his wife does die, his backup wife takes her place and he continues performing the *avodah* — and we do not appear concerned that he will come to eat from the *korbanot* as an *onen*.

### Yom Kippur Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים הוּא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא קָא אָכְלִי, הוּא נָמֵי לָא אָתֵי לְמֵיכַל, הָכָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אָכְלִי — הוּא נָמֵי אָתֵי לְמֵיכַל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the mishna, **since it is Yom Kippur, when everyone does not eat, he too** will **not come to eat.** However, **here,** during the rest of the year, **when everyone eats, he too** will **come to eat.** Therefore, a decree was issued.

The response is direct: *hachi hashta*? The cases are not comparable. In the situation described in our *mishna*, the *kohen gadol* is serving on Yom Kippur — *keivan de-Yom Kippurim hu, de-kulei alma lo achli* — since it is Yom Kippur, no one eats at all. Under such circumstances, *hu nami lo ati le-meichal* — he too will not come to eat; the concern simply does not apply. On a normal day, however, *de-kulei alma achli* — everyone is eating — and the *kohen gadol* as an *onen* might inadvertently come to eat from the *korbanot* as well. The *gezeirah* was never extended to Yom Kippur, where the entire environment of fasting renders it inapplicable.

### Onen Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הַאי גַּוְונָא מִי חָיְילָא עֲלֵיהּ אֲנִינוּת? וְהָא מִיגָּרְשָׁא! נְהִי דַּאֲנִינוּת לָא חָיְילָא עֲלֵיהּ, אִטְּרוֹדֵי מִי לָא מִיטְּרִיד?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question from a different perspective: **And** in a case **like this,** would the halakhic status of **acute mourning take effect on him,** considering that **she is divorced?** According to Rabbi Yehuda, the High Priest must give his wife a provisional divorce in which case she is no longer his wife and if she dies he is no longer obligated to mourn her. The Gemara answers: **Although** the status of **acute mourning does not take effect on him, is he not troubled** over the death of his wife? Therefore, according to Rabbi Yehuda, it is appropriate to prohibit his performance of the service on that day.

The *gemara* then raises a question that might have occurred to anyone following the previous day's discussion. It asks: *midi de-aninut lav chala alei* — but in what sense is he even an *onen* to begin with? The entire elaborate construction of conditional *gittin* was designed precisely so that if his wife dies, she would be considered retroactively divorced from him at that moment. *Ve-ha migarsha* — she is already divorced from him! He therefore would have no *onen* status at all, since the woman who died was not, retroactively, his wife.

The *gemara* responds: *tirda de-libeih mi la mitrad* — true, technically he is not an *onen*, but he will surely be emotionally distracted by his wife's death. This answer is, however, somewhat difficult to follow. Even granting that Rabbi Yehuda's original *gezeirah* was motivated by a concern lest he eat, it is not obvious why mere emotional distraction should generate the same concern. The apparent incompleteness of this resolution suggests that the question about *aninut* may have been raised from a position that did not accept the entire framework of conditional *gittin* and retroactive divorce developed in the previous *sugya*, and may have been operating with a different understanding of how the backup wife arrangement would function. In any case, the *gemara* brackets the retroactive-divorce solution in order to pursue the *aninut* question, and with this, the discussion of the first *mishna* — concerning the seven-day separation, the backup *kohen*, and the backup wife — is concluded.

## Seven-Day Preparation

### Seven Days Start

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל שִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים הוּא זוֹרֵק אֶת הַדָּם, וּמַקְטִיר אֶת הַקְּטוֹרֶת, וּמֵיטִיב אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת, וּמַקְרִיב אֶת הָרֹאשׁ וְאֶת הָרֶגֶל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: During **all seven days** of the High Priest's sequestering before Yom Kippur, **he sprinkles the blood** of the daily burnt-offering, **and he burns the incense, and he removes** the ashes of **the lamps** of the candelabrum, **and he sacrifices the head and the** hind **leg** of the daily offering. The High Priest performs these tasks in order to grow accustomed to the services that he will perform on Yom Kippur.

The *gemara* now moves to a new *mishna*, which addresses the substance of the *kohen gadol*'s seven-day stay in the *beit ha-mikdash* and what he would actually do during that time. The opening line of the *mishna* is: *kol shivat hayamim* — all seven days, referring to the period during which he is sequestered in the *beit ha-mikdash* before Yom Kippur.

### Daily Services

The *mishna* specifies the *avodot* the *kohen gadol* performs during these seven days. *Hu zorek et hadam* — he performs the *zerikat hadam* for the *tamid*, the daily offering. *U-maktir et haktoret* — he offers the *ketoret*, which was brought in two installments, half in the morning and half in the afternoon. *U-meitiv et haneirot* — he also tends to the *menorah*, cleaning out the wicks and lamps in the morning, since the cleaning takes place in the morning and the lighting in the evening.

### Service Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הַיָּמִים אִם רָצָה לְהַקְרִיב — מַקְרִיב, שֶׁכֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל מַקְרִיב חֵלֶק בָּרֹאשׁ, וְנוֹטֵל חֵלֶק בָּרֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **On all the other days** of the year, **if** the High Priest **wishes to sacrifice** any of the offerings, **he sacrifices them, as the High Priest sacrifices** any **portion** that he chooses **first and takes** any **portion** that he chooses **first.**

It is worth pausing to observe what the *mishna* is describing. The *zerikat hadam* and *ketoret* are directly relevant to the Yom Kippur *avodah*, though on Yom Kippur the *ketoret* will be offered in the *kodesh ha-kodashim* rather than on the outer *mizbeiach ha-chitzon*. The *meitiv et haneirot*, however, is not something specifically associated with the Yom Kippur *avodah* at all. This suggests that the seven-day period is not simply a rehearsal of Yom Kippur-specific rituals but rather a full immersion in the *avodah* of the *beit ha-mikdash* generally — a comprehensive bootcamp to get the *kohen gadol* acclimated to everything that takes place in the *mikdash*. This makes particular sense given that in the later period the position of *kohen gadol* was often purchased, meaning the officiant might have little prior experience performing the *avodah*. The parallel to the *miluim* — the seven-day inauguration period during which the *kohanim* were first initiated into their service in the *mishkan* — is apt. The seven-day structure serves as both practical preparation and a reenactment of that inaugural model.

The *mishna* further specifies *u-makriv et ha-rosh ve-ha-regel* — he would offer the head and the first leg of the *tamid* lamb onto the ramp of the *mizbeiach*, as part of the process by which the dismembered limbs were brought up in stages. Notably, he does not perform the *shechita* — which can be performed even by a non-*kohen* — but does perform the key *avodot* that constitute the heart of the *tamid* service.

There is something worth noting in the overall character of these seven days. The *gemara* elsewhere takes care to underscore that even tasks that might seem mundane, such as the *terumat ha-deshen* — the removal of ashes from the altar — carry genuine *avodah* significance and ought not be regarded as beneath the dignity of the service. There is no sense in the *gemara* that any of these daily *avodot* are peripheral. On the contrary, they represent the full range of what it means to serve in the *beit ha-mikdash*.

### Year-Round Rights

As for the rest of the year, the *mishna* states: *im ratzah le-hakriv makriv* — if the *kohen gadol* wishes to offer a *korban* on any given day, he may do so. He holds no fixed assignment outside the seven preparatory days and Yom Kippur itself, but should he appear on any ordinary day he retains the prerogative to perform whatever *avodah* he chooses. *She-kohen gadol makriv chelek be-rosh ve-notel chelek be-rosh* — the *kohen gadol* may offer first and receives the first portion. He may, in effect, step to the front of any line. Whether with respect to the performance of the *avodah* or the apportionment of the *korban*'s meat, the *kohen gadol* exercises a standing right of precedence on any day of the year he chooses to exercise it.

## Parah Adumah & Tumah

### Mishnah Authorship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דְּאִי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, הָא אָמַר: טָהוֹר שֶׁנָּפְלָה עָלָיו הַזָּאָה טִמְּאַתּוּ, הֵיכִי עָבֵיד עֲבוֹדָה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this mishna? **Rav Ḥisda said:** This mishna is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, as if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** it is difficult. **Didn't** Rabbi Akiva **say:** With regard to a **ritually pure** person **upon whom a sprinkling** of purification water **fell, it renders him impure?** This is based on the enigmatic principle with regard to the water of the red heifer: It purifies the ritually impure and renders impure the ritually pure. If so, **how** can the High Priest **perform the** Temple **service?** The High Priest is sprinkled with purification water on each of the seven days of his sequestering due to the possibility that he was impure with impurity imparted by a corpse. However, it is possible that he is ritually pure. If he is ritually pure, the sprinkling will render him impure.

The *gemara* now raises a question about the authorship of our *mishna*. *Amar Rav Chisda: d'lo ke-Rabbi Akiva* — Rav Chisda states that the *mishna* cannot be following the position of Rabbi Akiva. This requires explanation, since a *baraisa* taught earlier (not in our *mishna* but in a *baraisa* associated with the preceding discussion) established that during the seven days, the ashes of the *parah adumah* would be sprinkled upon the *kohen gadol* each day. The question is how this practice interacts with Rabbi Akiva's distinctive and radical position concerning the effect of the *parah adumah*.

### Rabbi Akiva Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְהִזָּה הַטָּהוֹר עַל הַטָּמֵא״. עַל הַטָּמֵא — טָהוֹר, וְעַל הַטָּהוֹר — טָמֵא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הַדְּבָרִים הַלָּלוּ אֲמוּרִין אֶלָּא בִּדְבָרִים הַמְקַבְּלִים טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that it is written: **"And the pure person will sprinkle it upon the impure person"** (Numbers 19:19); this emphasis that he sprinkles the water upon the impure person comes to teach that if he sprinkled **on the ritually impure** person, that person becomes **pure; but** if he sprinkled **on the pure** person, that person becomes **ritually impure.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. And the Rabbis say: These matters are stated** to teach that it is considered sprinkling **only** if it is performed **on items susceptible to impurity,** whereas if the water was sprinkled on items not susceptible to impurity, it is not considered sprinkling.

Rabbi Akiva's position is stated clearly: *tahor she-nafal alav haza'ah, tamei* — if the waters of the *parah adumah* are sprinkled upon a person who is already *tahor*, he becomes *tamei*. This is the full extension of the paradoxical logic inherent in the *parah adumah*: it makes *tamei* people *tahor*, and makes *tahor* people *tamei*. One might have assumed that this paradox — while true of those who handle or carry the ashes — does not extend to the person upon whom the sprinkling is performed. The plain sense of the Torah's description of the *parah adumah* says nothing about the sprinkled-upon *tahor* person becoming *tamei*; presumably, if you are already *tahor*, being sprinkled upon changes nothing for you. But Rabbi Akiva holds that the reversal is absolute and applies in both directions.

If Rabbi Akiva is correct, a severe practical problem emerges for the seven-day preparation period. Each day, ashes are sprinkled upon the *kohen gadol* to ensure his *tahara*. But if on any given day he happens to already be *tahor*, the sprinkling would render him *tamei* — not with a seven-day *tumah* requiring a new count, since this form of *tumah* only lasts until immersion and nightfall, but *tamei* nonetheless for the remainder of that day. The consequence would be that he cannot perform the *avodah* on any of the seven days, since each day's sprinkling might have rendered him *tamei*. The *mishna*, which envisions him performing the daily *avodot* throughout the seven-day period, therefore cannot accord with Rabbi Akiva's view.

The *baraisa* on which this discussion turns states: *veyizah ha-tahor al ha-tamei* — the *tahor* person sprinkles upon the *tamei*. *Al ha-tamei tahor* — if sprinkled upon the *tamei* person, the result is that the *tamei* person becomes *tahor*. *Ve-al ha-tahor* — and if upon a *tahor* person — *tamei* — he becomes *tamei*. This is the position of Rabbi Akiva.

The Sages, however, dispute this. Their position is: *ein ha-devarim ha-lalu amurim ela be-davar ha-mekabel tumah* — the operative rule of *al ha-tamei* is not about the *status* of the person (whether currently *tamei* or *tahor*), but rather about whether the thing being sprinkled upon is *capable* of receiving *tumah* at all. A valid sprinkling requires that the object sprinkled be of a type susceptible to *tumah*. The teaching addresses the case of one who intended to sprinkle upon an animal — which cannot become *tamei* — but whose sprinkling landed on a person, or vice versa. According to Rashi, what defines the act is not the intent but what actually occurred. If the sprinkling in fact landed on a person (who can become *tamei*), it constitutes a valid act of sprinkling and any water remaining on the hyssop may still be used. If it landed on an animal (which cannot become *tamei*), the act of sprinkling was invalid and the remaining water on the hyssop is disqualified.

### Rabbis Limit Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא — כְּדִתְנַן: נִתְכַּוֵּון לְהַזּוֹת עַל הַבְּהֵמָה וְהִזָּה עַל הָאָדָם, אִם יֵשׁ בָּאֵזוֹב — יִשְׁנֶה. נִתְכַּוֵּון לְהַזּוֹת עַל הָאָדָם וְהִזָּה עַל הַבְּהֵמָה, אִם יֵשׁ בָּאֵזוֹב — לֹא יִשְׁנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** the halakhic implication of that statement? It is **as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to one who mistakenly **intended to sprinkle** purification water **on an animal,** which does not become impure when alive, **but** happened to **sprinkle** it **upon** an impure **person, if** water remains **on the hyssop** that he used to sprinkle the water, **he** should **repeat** the action and sprinkle the purification water on the person to purify him. Since the first sprinkling was onto a person, who can become impure, the water remaining on the hyssop may be reused, and it is not disqualified by improper use. However, with regard to one who **intended to sprinkle** purification water **on a person but** happened to **sprinkle** it **upon an animal,** even **if** water remains **on the hyssop, he** should **not repeat** the action. Since the first sprinkling was onto an animal, which cannot become impure, the water is disqualified by improper use, and may not be used in a second sprinkling.

The Rabbis, then, are not endorsing Rabbi Akiva's radical extension of the paradox; they are limiting it. They insist that the *parah adumah*'s paradoxical reversal is to be constrained, not amplified. To this, Rabbi Akiva essentially replies: you are correct that it is logically strange, but that is precisely the point. *Amarti echkama ve-hi rechoka mimeni* — Shlomo HaMelech declared that he sought wisdom and found it distant from him; the *parah adumah* represents that category of divine statute that resists rational comprehension. The reversal is real, it is absolute, and it applies even to the *tahor* person who is sprinkled upon.

### Sprinkling Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְהִזָּה הַטָּהוֹר עָלָיו״, מַאי ״עַל הַטָּמֵא״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ עַל הַטָּמֵא — טָהוֹר, וְעַל הַטָּהוֹר — טָמֵא. וְרַבָּנַן: הַאי לִדְבָרִים הַמְּקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the basis of the dispute: **What is the reason for** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva?** Instead of writing: And the pure person will sprinkle it upon the impure person, **let the Merciful One write** in the Torah: **And the pure person will sprinkle it upon him,** and it would be clear that it is upon the aforementioned impure person. **What** is taught by the phrase: **Upon the impure person? Learn from it** that if he sprinkled **on the impure** person, that person becomes **pure; but** if he sprinkled **on the pure** person, that person becomes **impure. And the Rabbis** say with regard to **that** phrase: **It comes** to teach that it is only considered sprinkling if it is performed **on items susceptible to impurity.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר שְׁלֹמֹה ״אָמַרְתִּי אֶחְכָּמָה וְהִיא רְחוֹקָה מִמֶּנִּי״. וְרַבָּנַן? הַהוּא לַמַּזֶּה וְלַמַּזִּין עָלָיו — טָהוֹר, וְנוֹגֵעַ בָּהֶן — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva** would respond to that *a fortiori* inference: **That is what** King **Solomon said: "I said I would become wise, but it eludes me"** (Ecclesiastes 7:23). According to tradition, even Solomon in his great wisdom could not understand the contradictory nature of the sprinkling of purification water that purifies an impure person and impurifies a pure person. **And the Rabbis** ascribe Solomon's bewilderment to a different aspect of the *halakha*: **The one who sprinkles** the water **and the one upon whom one sprinkles** the water are **pure; but one who touches** the water unrelated to sprinkling is **impure.**

The *gemara* then asks: does the Rabbinic position truly hold that the one who performs the sprinkling — the *mazeh* — is *tahor*? The verse states explicitly: *u-mazeh mei ha-niddah yechabes bgadav ve-ha-nogea be-mei ha-niddah yitma ad ha-arev* — the one who sprinkles must launder his garments and becomes *tamei*, and the one who touches the water also becomes *tamei*. Not only does the Torah make the *mazeh tamei*, it imposes upon him the additional requirement of laundering his garments — a stringency beyond what applies to the *nogea*, who merely touches the water. Two distinct categories are thus explicitly delineated, both *tamei*, with the *mazeh* being treated more stringently than the *nogea*. How then can the Rabbis claim the *mazeh* is *tahor*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַזֶּה טָהוֹר? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וּמַזֵּה מֵי הַנִּדָּה יְכַבֵּס בְּגָדָיו״. מַאי ״מַזֶּה״ — נוֹגֵעַ. וְהָכְתִיב ״מַזֶּה״, וְהָא כְּתִיב ״נוֹגֵעַ״? וְעוֹד: מַזֶּה בָּעֵי כִּיבּוּס בְּגָדִים, נוֹגֵעַ לָא בָּעֵי כִּבּוּס בְּגָדִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is** the **one who sprinkles** the water actually **pure? Isn't it written: "He who sprinkles the purification waters will wash his clothes,** and he who touches the purification waters will be unclean until evening" (Numbers 19:21)? The Gemara responds: **What** is the meaning of the term: **He who sprinkles?** It means: **He who touches. But isn't it written: He who sprinkles? And isn't it written** in the same verse: And **he who touches? And furthermore,** in that verse, **one who sprinkles requires washing** of his **clothes,** indicating a more severe level of impurity, whereas **one who touches does not require washing** of his **clothes.** Apparently, when it is written: He who sprinkles, it is not referring to one who touches.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מַאי ״מַזֶּה״ — נוֹשֵׂא. וְנִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״נוֹשֵׂא״, מַאי טַעְמָא כְּתִיב ״מַזֶּה״? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּבָעֵינַן שִׁיעוּר הַזָּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Rabbis assert: **What** is the meaning of: **He who sprinkles?** It refers to **one who carries** the purification water. The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **let the Merciful One write: One who carries; what is the reason** that **he who sprinkles is written** if the reference is to carrying? The Gemara answers: **This** use of the term sprinkling to depict carrying **teaches us** that in order to become impure from carrying purification water, one **must** carry the **measure** required for **sprinkling.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: הַזָּאָה צְרִיכָה שִׁיעוּר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: הַזָּאָה אֵין צְרִיכָה שִׁיעוּר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר! אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַזָּאָה אֵין צְרִיכָא שִׁיעוּר, הָנֵי מִילֵּי אַגַּבָּא דְגַבְרָא, אֲבָל בְּמָנָא — צְרִיכָה שִׁיעוּר. דִּתְנַן: כַּמָּה יְהֵא בָּהֶן וִיהֵא כְּדֵי הַזָּאָה — כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּטְבּוֹל רָאשֵׁי גִבְעוֹלִין וְיַזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** works out **well** according **to the one who said** that **sprinkling requires a** minimum **measure** of water, as then the concept of a measure required for sprinkling has meaning. **However,** according **to the one who said** that **sprinkling does not require a** minimum **measure** of water, **what can be said?** There is no concept of a measure required for sprinkling. The Gemara answers: **Even** according **to the one who said** that **sprinkling does not require a** minimum **measure** of water, **that applies** only to the measure of purification water that must be sprinkled on **the back of the** impure **man;** any amount will suffice. **However, in the vessel** into which one dips the hyssop in order to sprinkle the water, a certain **measure** of water **is required, as we learned** in a mishna: **How much** water should **be in** the vessel so that it **will be equivalent** to the measure required for **sprinkling?** It must be **equivalent** to the measure required **to dip the tops of the stems** of the hyssop branch into the water **and sprinkle it.** Apparently, even according to the one who said that there is no minimum measure of water for sprinkling there is a minimum measure of water that must be in the vessel.

### Sunset Sprinkling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּעָבֵיד עֲבוֹדָה כּוּלֵּיהּ יוֹמָא, וּלְפַנְיָא מַדּוּ עֲלֵיהּ, וְטָבֵיל וְעָבֵיד הֶעֱרֵב הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** who said that the purification water impurifies the pure, the difficulty can be resolved: The High Priest **performs the** Temple **service** for **the entire day** on each of the seven days of sequestering, **and toward the evening** the priests **sprinkle** the purification water **upon him.** Even if he was pure and the sprinkling rendered him impure, he **immerses** immediately **and observes** the requirement to wait **until sunset,** at which point he is purified and prepared to serve the next day.

Returning to the original question about the *kohen gadol*'s seven-day preparation, Rav Chisda had said the *mishna* cannot accord with Rabbi Akiva. Abaye responds: *afilu teima Rabbi Akiva* — one can maintain the *mishna* even according to Rabbi Akiva, and it is simply a matter of scheduling. The *kohen gadol* performs the *avodah* throughout the entire day, *u-fanya* — and toward evening, just before sunset, one sprinkles upon him. He then immerses in the *mikvah*, and *ve-avid ha-erev shemesh* — nightfall arrives and he is fully *tahor*. Even if the sprinkling rendered him *tamei* in those final minutes before sunset, the immersion and the arrival of nightfall resolve his status before he needs to perform any further *avodah* the next morning. The entire problem, according to Abaye, is merely a logistical one of timing.

It is notable that the *gemara* does not entertain the possibility of simply omitting the daily sprinkling, which would sidestep the problem entirely. The practice of daily *haza'ah* appears to be a settled consensus — though as was noted in an earlier passage, there is an opinion that it was performed only on the third and seventh days rather than every day, which would at least have reduced the tension.

## Service Order Debate

### Mishnah Order

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַקְטִיר אֶת הַקְּטוֹרֶת וּמֵטִיב אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת. אַלְמָא קְטוֹרֶת בְּרֵישָׁא וַהֲדַר נֵרוֹת. וּרְמִינְהוּ: מִי שֶׁזָּכָה בְּדִישּׁוּן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, וּמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בַּמְּנוֹרָה, וּמִי שֶׁזָּכָה בַּקְּטוֹרֶת!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that on each of the seven days the High Priest **burns the incense and removes** the ashes from **the lamps. Apparently, incense** is burned **first, and then the lamps** are cleaned. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna in tractate *Tamid* that discusses the lottery in which the priests performing the various tasks that constitute the morning Temple service are selected. That mishna first mentions the one **who was privileged** to be selected to perform **removal of the ashes from the inner altar,** and then **the one who was privileged** to be selected to remove the ashes from the lamps of **the candelabrum,** and only then does it deal with **the one who is privileged** to be selected to burn **the incense.** According to that mishna, removing ashes from the candelabrum precedes the burning of the incense.

With the *parah adumah* discussion concluded, the *gemara* turns to the specific *avodot* enumerated in the *mishna* and examines their order. The *mishna* lists *u-maktir et ha-ketoret u-meitiv et ha-neirot* — the *kohen gadol* first offers the *ketoret* and then tends to the *neirot*. The *gemara* notes: *alma, ketoret be-reisha ve-hadar neirot* — it is apparent from our *mishna* that in the order of the morning *avodot* in the *beit ha-mikdash*, the *ketoret* comes first and the *neirot* afterward.

### Tamid Order

The *gemara* immediately juxtaposes this with a passage from *masechet Tamid*, which presents the order of the daily service through a sequence of three separate *mishnayot*, each describing the duties of a different *kohen*: first, the one who was assigned the cleaning of the inner *mizbeiach*; then the one who was assigned the *menorah*; and only thereafter the one who was assigned the *ketoret*. The sequence is thus: inner *mizbeiach*, *menorah*, *ketoret* — with the *menorah* clearly preceding the *ketoret*, the inverse of our *mishna*'s order.

### Different Authors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאן תָּנָא תָּמִיד, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִישׁ הַמִּצְפָּה הוּא. וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: בָּא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית — נוֹתֵן מִזְרָחָה צָפוֹנָה, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית — נוֹתֵן מַעֲרָבָה דָּרוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: Who is** the *tanna* who **taught** the *mishnayot* in tractate ***Tamid*? It is Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpa,** who disagrees with the *tanna* of the mishna here. The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn the opposite, as we learned** in the mishna in tractate *Tamid*: The priest sprinkles the blood of the daily offering on the altar. **He comes to the northeast corner** of the altar and **sprinkles** once on the **northeast** corner. From there he proceeds to the **southwest corner** and **sprinkles** once on the **southwest** corner.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִישׁ הַמִּצְפָּה מְשַׁנֶּה בַּתָּמִיד: מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית — נוֹתֵן מִזְרָחָה צָפוֹנָה. מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית — נוֹתֵן מַעֲרָבָה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹתֵן דָּרוֹמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught** in the *Tosefta* **concerning** this mishna: **Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpa changes the** sprinkling of the blood of the **daily offering** relative to the sprinkling of blood of all other burnt-offerings. He agrees with the first *tanna* with regard to the first sprinkling of the blood; the priest comes to the **northeast** corner and **sprinkles** once on the **northeast** corner. However, when he proceeds to the **southwest** corner he does not sprinkle once as he would when offering a burnt-offering; rather, **he sprinkles** on the **west** side of the altar **and then sprinkles** on the **south** side. Since the disputing opinion is that of Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpa, apparently the unattributed opinions in the *mishnayot* in tractate *Tamid* are not the opinions of Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpa.

To resolve this apparent contradiction, Rav Huna suggests that *masechet Tamid* was authored by Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpah. This is a surprising name to identify as the author of an entire tractate, but the reasoning follows. The *gemara* then challenges this attribution: *ve-ha ipcha shemia lan* — in fact, the opposite is the case, for Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpah is demonstrably a *contrary* voice within *masechet Tamid*, not its author. The evidence appears in a *baraisa* that presents the standard ruling regarding the *zerikat ha-dam* of the *tamid*: at the northeast corner, one throw of blood covers the north and east sides; at the southwest corner, one throw covers the south and west sides — two throws that address all four corners. The *baraisa* then records that *Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpah meshane ba-Tamid* — Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpah treats the *tamid* differently. He agrees about the northeast corner, but holds that at the southwest corner one performs two separate applications rather than a single throw: first the west, then the south. He thus explicitly diverges from the *stam* of *masechet Tamid*. One cannot therefore identify him as its author; he is its dissenting voice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאן תְּנָא סֵדֶר יוֹמָא — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִישׁ הַמִּצְפָּה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** there must be a different resolution to the contradiction between the mishna here and the one in tractate *Tamid*. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** the Yom Kippur service in **tractate *Yoma*? It is Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpa,** and tractate *Tamid* is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis who disagree with him.

Rabbi Yochanan therefore reformulates the resolution: *man tana seder Yoma Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpah hu* — it is the *seder* of *Yoma* that was authored by Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpah, not *Tamid*. The two tractates simply reflect different *tannaitic* opinions: the author of *Tamid* holds that the *menorah* precedes the *ketoret*; Rabbi Shimon Ish HaMitzpah, author of *Yoma*, holds that the *ketoret* precedes the *menorah*. Different authors, different rulings, no contradiction.

The *gemara*, however, is not satisfied. *U-remi seder Yoma a-seder Yoma* — there is a contradiction *within* the *mishna* of *Yoma* itself. A later *mishna* in our tractate discusses the four daily lotteries (*paysaot*) that determined which *kohanim* performed which *avodot*. The second lottery assigned specific tasks in sequence: *shechitah*, *zrikah*, cleaning of the inner *mizbeiach*, cleaning of the *menorah*, bringing the limbs of the *tamid* to the ramp. The third lottery then announced: *chadashim la-ketoret bo'u ve-hapisu* — anyone who has never yet had the privilege of offering the *ketoret* should come and participate in this lottery, since the *ketoret* was considered a *segulah* for wealth, and to prevent the same *kohen* from monopolizing this privilege, only first-time participants were eligible. The clear implication of this sequence is that the *menorah* comes before the *ketoret*, aligning with *masechet Tamid* — yet directly contradicting the earlier *mishna* of *Yoma* that placed the *ketoret* first.

### Abaye's Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִי סֵדֶר יוֹמָא אַסֵּדֶר יוֹמָא, דִּתְנַן: פַּיִיס הַשֵּׁנִי — מִי שׁוֹחֵט, מִי זוֹרֵק, מִי מְדַשֵּׁן מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, וּמִי מְדַשֵּׁן אֶת הַמְנוֹרָה, וּמִי מַעֲלֶה אֵבָרִים לַכֶּבֶשׁ. פַּיִיס הַשְּׁלִישִׁי — חֲדָשִׁים לַקְּטוֹרֶת בּוֹאוּ וְהָפִיסוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from one mishna in **tractate *Yoma* against** another mishna in **tractate *Yoma*, as we learned** in a mishna: **The second lottery** conducted daily among the priests determined the following: **Who slaughters** the daily morning offering; **who sprinkles** its blood; **who removes the ashes** from **the inner altar; and who removes the ashes** and burnt wicks from **the candelabrum; and who takes the limbs** of the daily offering **up to the ramp** to be burned later. Following the second lottery the priests dispersed, and they later reconvened for **the third lottery.** Before the third lottery, the appointee declared: Let only those priests who are **new to** offering **the incense** come and **participate in the lottery** for the incense. Apparently, removing the ashes from the lamps of the candelabrum preceded the burning of the incense, which contradicts the mishna here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בַּהֲטָבַת שְׁתֵּי נֵרוֹת, כָּאן בַּהֲטָבַת חָמֵשׁ נֵרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is discussing **the removal** of the ashes from **two lamps,** which is performed after the burning of the incense; **there,** the mishna of the lotteries is discussing **the removal** of the ashes from **five lamps,** which precedes the burning of the incense. As will be explained, the priest attends to five lamps first, and after a break, he attends to the final two lamps.

Abaye resolves the internal contradiction in *Yoma* with a solution that reframes the entire discussion: *kan be-tivat shtei neirot, kan be-tivat chamesh neirot* — the different *mishnayot* are not in fact describing different orderings of the same act, because the tending of the *menorah* was not performed as a single continuous act. It was divided into two stages: five lamps first, then two lamps afterward. When one *mishna* says the *menorah* comes before the *ketoret*, it refers to the first five lamps. When another says the *ketoret* comes before the *menorah*, it refers to the final two lamps. The *ketoret* was offered in between the two parts of the *menorah* service.

This elegant solution means that both statements are simultaneously correct: the *menorah* (in its first installment of five) *does* precede the *ketoret*, and the *ketoret* *does* precede the *menorah* (in its final installment of two). The apparent contradiction dissolves once the *menorah* service is understood as a split act rather than a unified one.

### Blood vs Incense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּבִקְטוֹרֶת מַפְסֵיק לְהוּ? וְהָא אַבָּיֵי מְסַדֵּר מַעֲרָכָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דִּגְמָרָא, בְּדַם הַתָּמִיד מַפְסֵיק לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that the** burning of the **incense interposes** between attending to the first five lamps and attending the last two? **But** when **Abaye related the order of the** daily **priestly functions in the name of tradition, didn't** he state that it was the sprinkling of **the blood of the daily offering that interposed between** the five lamps and the two, not the burning of the incense?

The *gemara* then notes a complication. Abaye himself, in a separate context, was known to teach the order of the morning *avodah* from a received tradition — *mesader maaracha mishmeih de-gemara* — and in that tradition it was the *dam ha-tamid*, the blood of the daily offering, that divided the two parts of the *menorah* service, not the *ketoret*. If Abaye's own tradition placed the *dam* as the divider, how can his resolution of the contradiction rest on the *ketoret* being the divider?

### What Divides Lamps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָהִיא לְאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, הָא לְרַבָּנַן. דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יֵיטִיב אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת וְאַחַר כָּךְ יַקְטִיר, אֶלָּא יַקְטִיר וְאַחַר כָּךְ יֵיטִיב. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: מֵטִיב, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַקְטִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: This is **not difficult. That** sequence cited by Abaye is in accordance with the opinion of **Abba Shaul,** whereas **this** sequence cited in the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One** should **not remove** the ashes from **the lamps and then burn** the incense; **rather one should burn** the incense **and then remove** the ashes from the lamps. **Abba Shaul says: One removes** the ashes from the lamps **and then burns** the incense. The different sources reflect the dispute cited in the *baraita*.

The response is that this, too, is a *tannaitic* dispute: *ha de-Abba Shaul, ha de-Rabbanan* — one position reflects the view of Abba Shaul, the other the view of the Rabbis. The *baraisa* makes this explicit: *lo yeitiv et ha-neiros ve-achar kach yaktir, ella yaktir ve-achar kach yeitiv* — one should not complete the *tivat ha-neirot* in full and *then* offer the *ketoret*; rather, one should offer the *ketoret* and then complete the *menorah*. This is the Rabbinic position, preserving Abaye's resolution: the *ketoret* interrupts the two parts of the *menorah*, with five lamps first, then *ketoret*, then two lamps. Abaye's own tradition about the *dam* as the divider follows Abba Shaul.

### Verse Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל? דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּבֹּקֶר בַּבֹּקֶר בְּהֵיטִיבוֹ אֶת הַנֵּרוֹת יַקְטִירֶנָּה״. וְרַבָּנַן? מַאי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion of **Abba Shaul?** It is **as it is written: "Every morning when he removes the ashes from the lamps,** he shall burn it"** (Exodus 30:7). First the lamps are cleaned, and only then is the incense burned. **And** how do **the Rabbis,** who hold that the incense was burned first, interpret this verse? They say: **What is the Merciful One saying?**

Abba Shaul's position is: *meitiv ve-achar kach maktir* — one completes the *menorah* fully and only then offers the *ketoret*. His reasoning derives from the verse: *ba-boker ba-boker be-heitivo et ha-neirot yaktirena* — in the morning, when he tends the lamps, he shall burn the *ketoret*. Abba Shaul reads this sequentially: first complete the *neirot* in their entirety, and *then* burn the *ketoret*. If the *menorah* must be divided at all, the interruption for Abba Shaul is the *dam ha-tamid*, not the *ketoret*, with the *ketoret* coming only after the *menorah* is finished.

The Rabbis read the same verse differently: *be-idan ha-tava tehei haktarat ketoret* — the phrase *be-heitivo et ha-neirot yaktirena* does not mean "after completing the lamps, then burn the *ketoret*," but rather "during the period of tending the lamps, burn the *ketoret*." The *ketoret* is thus offered in the *midst* of the *menorah* service, not after it. This is the reading that supports Abaye's resolution of the contradiction, and the debate between Abba Shaul and the Rabbis — over which act interrupts the two halves of the *menorah*, and over how to read the verse — will continue into the next session.
