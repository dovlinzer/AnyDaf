# Niddah 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kishuy Duration

### Kishuy Timeframe

The Gemara on 37b opens with a fundamental question concerning *kishuy*—the phenomenon of childbirth pains accompanying bleeding. Having established the circumstances under which such pains render blood non-problematic, the Gemara now asks: how far back in time can the pains still be attributed to the childbirth? In other words, how many days before birth can we say that bleeding accompanied by pain is related to the impending birth, such that it does not create *dam zivah*?

### Three Opinions

The Mishnah presents three positions. Rabbi Meir says forty or fifty days. Another opinion holds that one month suffices. A third limits it to two weeks.

The Gemara immediately notes a difficulty with Rabbi Meir's formulation: if fifty days is sufficient, is there any question about forty? The phrase "forty or fifty" would normally suggest an open-ended range—as in the first Mishnah in Sukkah, where Rabbi Yehuda says a *sukkah* may be forty or fifty *amot* tall, meaning there is no upper limit at all. Tosafot raises this point. But as the Gemara will demonstrate shortly, Rabbi Meir treats fifty as a precise number, offering an exact count of days. The Gemara therefore asks: if fifty is the precise figure, why does the Mishnah also cite forty?

### Sick vs. Healthy

Rav Chisda resolves this: *kan chola, kan bria*—the distinction turns on whether the woman is sick or healthy. Rashi explains that for a woman with a weaker constitution, bleeding can plausibly be attributed to the fetus at an earlier stage, up to fifty days before birth; for a healthy woman, the connection holds only up to forty days.

### Rabbi Levi

Rabbi Levi introduces a more restrictive principle: *ein hadam metaher ela yamim hare'uyim lihyot bahen zavah*—the *kishuy* renders blood pure only during those days when she is actually fit to become a *zavah*. The critical distinction underlying Rabbi Levi's position is that blood during the *niddah* period is problematic regardless of accompanying pain; *kishuy* operates exclusively to neutralize *dam zivah*. Consequently, only the eleven days of the *zavah* period—and no others—are affected by the *kishuy*.

### Rava's View

Rava extends this framework: *afilu biyamim hare'uyim lisfirat zivah*—the *kishuy* applies even during the days relevant to the *counting* of *zavah* status, not merely during the eleven days themselves. To illustrate: suppose a woman bled at some point during her pregnancy, say on the first of the month, making her a *niddah*. Starting on day eight, any subsequent bleeding would constitute *dam zivah*. Now, had she actually become a *zavah gedolah* during days eight through eighteen, she would have needed seven clean days afterward. Therefore, blood on any of the days running from day eight through day twenty-five is in some sense "related" to a potential *zavah* status—either as the *zavah* period itself or as the seven-day counting period that would follow it. Rava holds that *kishuy* renders blood pure throughout this extended range, through day twenty-five.

### Rav Adda

Rav Adda bar Ahava goes further still, following Rava's logic to its ultimate conclusion. According to Rava's reasoning, the *kishuy* applies not only through day twenty-five but potentially indefinitely. Once she has bled once and entered the *niddah* period, a *zavah* period opens on day eight. If she were to bleed three times during that period, she would become a *zavah gedolah* and would need seven clean days. But if bleeding interrupted those seven days, the count would be reset, and she could theoretically never complete the seven clean days. Thus, from day eight onward, every subsequent day is potentially a day relevant to the *zavah* counting process—an extension of the unresolved *zavah* status. Rav Adda therefore holds that any blood after the first seven days of the initial bleeding is considered potential *dam zivah*, and *kishuy* renders it pure. This is a striking *kula*: once a *niddah* period occurs, virtually any subsequent bleeding—no matter how far removed in time—falls within the scope of *kishuy* leniency.

### Reconciliation

The Gemara now asks how these positions square with Rabbi Meir's statement that *kishuy* applies for forty or fifty days. This formulation implies that for an entire stretch of forty or fifty days, bleeding accompanied by pain is rendered pure—but how is that consistent with Rabbi Levi, who limits *kishuy* to the eleven days of the *zavah* period?

The Gemara answers on behalf of Rabbi Levi: *mi katani tahorah b'kullan?*—the Mishnah never says she is *tehorah* for the entire forty or fifty days. Rather, *biyemei niddah niddah, biyemei zivah tehorah*: during the *niddah* days she remains a *niddah*, and during the *zavah* days she is *tehorah*. The statement that *kishuy* is relevant for forty or fifty days means only that this is the window within which the laws of *kishuy* are operative—the rules apply whenever, within that stretch, she happens to be in a *zavah* period. It does not guarantee that she is *tehorah* throughout.

The Gemara asks further about the second formulation attributed to Rabbi Levi: *ein hadam metaher ela yamim hare'uyim lihyot bahen zavah gedolah*—it renders blood pure only on days when she could become a *zavah gedolah*. Tosafot presents two readings of this stricter formulation. One reading holds that it excludes days one and two of the *zavah* period, since a *zavah gedolah* requires three consecutive days of bleeding and therefore cannot be initiated on those days. The other reading excludes days ten and eleven, since a *zavah gedolah* cannot be completed if the bleeding begins that late. Either way, Tosafot understands Rabbi Levi to be restricting *kishuy* to a subset of the eleven-day period.

Rashi reads the passage differently. In his view, the formulation "*zavah gedolah*" tells us not which days are covered, but what type of *zavah* status is neutralized. *Kishuy* prevents her from becoming a *zavah gedolah*, but it does not prevent her from becoming a *zavah ketanah*. If she bleeds once or twice during the *zavah* period with accompanying pain, she remains a *zavah ketanah* and still requires one clean day. *Kishuy* thus provides immunity only from *zavah gedolah* status, not from all forms of *zavah*.

In the subsequent formulation, *Abba Shaul* citing Rava holds: *afilu yamim hare'uyim lihyot bahen zavah ketanah*—*kishuy* renders blood pure even on days when she could become only a *zavah ketanah*. The *drashah* for *zavah ketanah* status derives from the same verse (*dameha yamim rabim*) used to establish that *kishuy* applies, and therefore the full eleven days are included. Both approaches ultimately limit the operative period to eleven days; the expansive reading of Rav Adda bar Ahava—where *kishuy* applies indefinitely after any initial bleeding—is what accounts for the forty or fifty days mentioned by Rabbi Meir.

## Bleeding Calculations

### 150 Days

The Gemara introduces a remarkable calculation in the name of Rabbi Meir: *yesh makshah kuf nun yom ve'ein zavah olah bahen*—there is a case in which a woman can bleed for 150 consecutive days and never become a *zavah*. The Gemara then works through exactly how this is achieved.

### Precise Count

The construction begins with a sequence of bleeding that precedes the fifty-day *kishuy* window. A woman bleeds for two days during the *zavah* period—she is a *zavah ketanah* but not a *zavah gedolah*. Then she bleeds for seven days as a *niddah*. Then she bleeds for two more days in the *zavah* period following the *niddah*—again a *zavah ketanah*, not a *zavah gedolah*. This totals eleven days of bleeding, and she has not yet become a *zavah gedolah*. On the very next day, she would be in the *zavah* period and a third day of bleeding would create *zavah gedolah* status—but this is precisely when the fifty-day *kishuy* period begins. The Gemara notes that this makes clear that Rabbi Meir treats fifty as a precise figure: he is counting exactly.

### Full 150 Count

For the next fifty days, she bleeds continuously, but since it is accompanied by *kishuy*, none of it constitutes *dam zivah*. She then gives birth—to a girl. The birth itself triggers *tumas yoledet*: two weeks of *niddah* status followed by sixty-six days of *toharah*, during which even normal bleeding does not render her a *zavah*. These eighty days of post-birth status add to the total: eleven plus fifty is sixty-one, plus eighty is one hundred forty-one. After the eighty days, she bleeds seven more days as a *niddah*, then two more days in the *zavah* period—again a *zavah ketanah* only. That adds nine more days, bringing the total to one hundred fifty. At no point has she become a *zavah gedolah*.

### Lifetime Bleeding

The Gemara raises a pointed objection: if this reasoning is accepted, one could construct a scenario in which a woman bleeds every single day of her life—continuously cycling through *kishuy* periods, births, and eighty-day post-birth periods—and never become a *zavah*. The scenario involves a woman who repeatedly gives birth, then during her eighty days of *toharah* miscarries, restarting the cycle indefinitely. The implication is that the entire structure of *zavah* law could be circumvented.

### No Kishuy Miscarriage

Rabbi Meir responds: *ein koshi linefalim*. The laws of *kishuy* apply only when the bleeding leads to a live birth. A miscarriage does not trigger the *kishuy* leniency. Therefore, any scenario dependent on miscarriage to extend the cycle will not work: if the birth is a miscarriage, then whatever bleeding preceded it—even if accompanied by pain—does not benefit from *kishuy*, and three days of *zavah*-period bleeding would render her a *zavah gedolah*.

The Gemara notes in passing that one could still construct a scenario of perpetual bleeding without *zavah* status by chaining together multiple live births without invoking *kishuy* at all—simply by becoming pregnant again during the eighty *toharah* days, giving birth, and repeating. But if one wants to include *kishuy* as part of the mechanism, that portion of the argument collapses wherever miscarriage is involved.

## 100 Days Baraita

### 100-Day Count

A *beraita* presents a more modest version of the calculation: *yesh ro'ah me'ah yom ve'ein zivah olah bahen*—there is a case in which a woman can bleed for one hundred days without ever becoming a *zavah*. The construction here does not invoke *kishuy* at all and involves only a single birth. She bleeds two days in the *zavah* period, seven days as a *niddah*, and two more in the *zavah* period—eleven days total, still not a *zavah gedolah*. She then gives birth to a girl, and the following eighty days of post-birth *toharah* contribute eighty more days of bleeding. After those eighty days, she bleeds seven days as a *niddah* and two more in the *zavah* period—nine additional days. The total: eleven plus eighty plus nine equals one hundred days of continuous bleeding, without *kishuy* and with a single birth, and she has never been a *zavah gedolah*.

### No Blood Requirement

The Gemara asks: *mai kamashma lan?*—what is the *beraita* teaching us that we do not already know? The answer is that the *beraita* comes to reject the position that *i efshar liftichat hakever belo dam*—it is impossible for the opening of the womb to occur without blood. In the scenario described, the woman bleeds two days as a *zavah ketanah*, then seven as a *niddah*, then two more—and then gives birth. One might suppose that the very process of the child exiting through the womb would itself cause blood to flow before the child had fully emerged, which would be a third day of *zavah*-period bleeding and render her a *zavah gedolah* before the *yoledet* status took hold.

The *beraita* teaches that we do not make that assumption. We do not presume that blood necessarily accompanies the opening of the womb. If blood is seen, it is noted; but absent actual evidence of bleeding, we do not presuppose it. Therefore, the scenario can in principle be completed—a woman exits the *zavah*-period bleeding and enters childbirth without the act of birth itself generating an additional day of *zavah* blood.

Rashi notes an apparent tension: in any real-world case, bleeding during the act of childbirth would be accompanied by the pains of *kishuy* and would thus not constitute *dam zivah* regardless. The scenario requires us to imagine—however hypothetically—that there were no childbirth pains, so that we can speak of the one-hundred-day count as independent of *kishuy*. The theoretical point, however, stands: *ptichat hakever* does not automatically entail blood.

## Ninth Month View

### Ninth Month Lenient

Rabbi Yehuda, citing Rabbi Tarfon, holds: *daiyah chodshah*—her month is sufficient. Rather than computing fifty days back or focusing solely on the eleven-day *zavah* period, Rabbi Yehuda focuses on whether the bleeding with pain occurred within the ninth month of pregnancy. A *beraita* elaborates that this position contains both a stringency and a leniency relative to other opinions.

### Eighth Month Strict

The stringent dimension: if a woman bled for two days at the end of the eighth month and one day at the beginning of the ninth, and gave birth even at the very beginning of the ninth month, she is a *yoledet bezov*—she gives birth having been a *zavah*. The reason is that the majority of the three days of potentially *zavah*-producing bleeding occurred in the eighth month. Since Rabbi Yehuda's rule looks to the month in which the bleeding predominantly occurred, the single day in the ninth month is treated as fundamentally belonging to the eighth-month bleeding episode, and *kishuy* provides no relief.

### Ninth Month Always OK

The lenient dimension is striking: if she bled one day at the end of the eighth month and two days at the beginning of the ninth, and gave birth at the very end of the ninth month—even without any continuous pain leading up to the delivery—she is not a *yoledet bezov*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, once the majority of the bleeding occurred within the ninth month, the entire episode is attributed to childbirth, and *kishuy* applies. There is no requirement that the pain be continuous and uninterrupted all the way to the moment of birth. This is a significant leniency beyond all other positions, which generally require the pain to connect the bleeding to the actual birth.

### Calendar Month

Rav Adda bar Ahava draws an inference from Rabbi Yehuda's formulation: *shema minah kasavar Rabbi Yehuda shipura garin*—Rabbi Yehuda holds that it is the *shofar*, the announcement of *Rosh Chodesh*, that is determinative. The "month" in question is not the woman's personal ninth month of pregnancy, counted from conception, but the ninth calendar month as established by *beit din*.

Both Rashi and Tosafot address this inference at length. Tosafot's key argument is as follows: Rabbi Yehuda draws a sharp distinction between two days in the ninth month and one in the eighth versus two in the eighth and one in the ninth. But how could such precision be applied to a personal pregnancy month? The day of conception cannot be known with certainty—semen can remain viable for up to three days, so the actual day of fertilization is unknown. Since Rabbi Yehuda's rule demands exactness about which day belongs to which month, the "month" must be the objective calendar month, not the imprecise personal month of pregnancy. The determination is therefore a formal, *halachic* one based on the calendar sanctified by *beit din*, rather than a purely biological calculation.

## Pregnancy Biology

### 271 Days

Shmuel states: *ein ishah mis'aberet veyoledet ela l'masayim veshivim ve'echad yom*—a woman does not conceive and give birth except at two hundred seventy-one days. That is, counting the day of conception as day one, pregnancy lasts through day two hundred seventy and birth occurs on day two hundred seventy-one. The Gemara adds that birth may also occur on day two hundred seventy-two or two hundred seventy-three—not because women vary, but because semen may remain viable for up to three days after intercourse. If fertilization occurs on the day of intercourse, birth will be on day two hundred seventy-one; if not until the second day, on two hundred seventy-two; if not until the third day, on two hundred seventy-three. The pregnancy itself is exactly two hundred seventy days from conception.

This precision appears to conflict with Rabbi Yehuda's view that birth can occur anywhere across the ninth month. The Gemara explains that Shmuel speaks in accordance with the practice of the early *chasidim*.

### Chasidim Wednes

The *beraita* reports: *chasidim harishonim lo hayu meshamshim mitoteihem ela b'revi'i b'Shabbat*—the early pious ones would only engage in marital relations on Wednesday, so that their wives would not be placed in a situation of giving birth on Shabbat and encountering issues associated with that circumstance. Their practice was premised on Shmuel's precise calculation: if the length of pregnancy from the day of intercourse is a fixed number of days, then the day of birth can be predicted from the day of intercourse, and one can choose accordingly.

### Day Calculation

The Gemara asks: why only Wednesday? If it is Wednesday and beyond, what about Thursday, Friday, and Shabbat itself? The Gemara clarifies: *m'revi'i ve'eilach*—from Wednesday onward. The reasoning is as follows. Since two hundred seventy-three is divisible by seven, the day of birth falls on the same weekday as the day of intercourse when 273 days are counted. If intercourse occurred on Sunday and birth is on day 273, she gives birth on Shabbat. If intercourse was on Monday and birth is on day 272, she gives birth on Shabbat. If intercourse was on Tuesday and birth is on day 271, she gives birth on Shabbat. Therefore, intercourse on Sunday, Monday, or Tuesday could result in a Shabbat birth. Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, or Shabbat will not, because none of the three possible day-counts—271, 272, or 273—from those days lands on Shabbat. The *chasidim* therefore restricted themselves to Wednesday onward.

### Gematria 271

The source for Shmuel's figure of 271 is a *drashah*: *vayiten Hashem lah herayon*—"and God gave her a pregnancy" (in the context of Ruth). The word *herayon*, pregnancy, has a *gematria* of two hundred seventy-one. The days of 272 and 273 are derivative of this base figure, accounting for the possible delay between intercourse and fertilization.

### Seventh Month Birth

Mar Zutra adds a distinction: even according to the opinion that a nine-month birth occurs only after a full nine months (*yoledet l'tisha einah yoledet l'mekutain*)—that is, a nine-month baby is always carried to term and born only after complete months—a seven-month birth is different. *Yoledet l'sheva yoledet l'mekutain*: a seven-month baby can be born even at the very beginning of the seventh month. The scriptural basis is the verse regarding Chana: *vayehi litkufat hayamim vatahar Chana vateled ben*—"and it was after the period of days that Chana conceived and bore a son." The Gemara derives: *miut tekufot shtayim*—the minimum number of *tekufot* (seasons of three months each) is two, equaling six months; *miut yamim shnayim*—the minimum number of additional days is two. Six months and two days places the birth at the very beginning of the seventh month, showing that a viable seventh-month birth can occur from the outset of that month.

## Two Weeks View

### Two Week Limit

Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon hold the most restrictive position: *ein koshi yoter mi'shtei shabbatot*—*kishuy* applies for no more than two weeks before the birth. Shmuel explains the reasoning behind this view.

### Two Week Verse

The derivation is from the verse regarding the *yoledet*: *v'tam'ah shvuayim k'nidatah*—she shall be *tamei* two weeks like her *niddah*. The analysis proceeds: *k'nidatah v'lo k'zivatah*—only like *niddah* status applies during this period, not *zavah* status. This implies that *zavah* blood during the period around childbirth is *tehorah*—because the verse specifies *niddah* and not *zavah*, we learn that *zavah* blood is set aside during this time. The question then becomes: for how long? The verse itself provides the answer—*shvuayim*, two weeks. The same verse from which we derive the leniency of *kishuy* also specifies its duration: two weeks.

### 25 Days Max

A *beraita* then works through the calculation under this view. A woman can bleed for twenty-five days without becoming a *zavah gedolah*: two days in the *zavah* period (*zavah ketanah*), seven days as a *niddah*, two more days in the *zavah* period (*zavah ketanah* again)—totaling eleven days. Then the two-week *kishuy* window applies, covering fourteen additional days. Eleven plus fourteen equals twenty-five days of bleeding without *zavah gedolah* status.

### 26 Days Zavah

If she were to bleed for twenty-six days before birth, she would inevitably become a *zavah gedolah*. The twenty-sixth day would fall within the *zavah* period and, combined with previous *zavah*-period days, would constitute a third consecutive day of *dam zivah*, making her a *zavah gedolah*. The *beraita* states this as a limit: one cannot have twenty-six days of pre-birth bleeding with *kishuy* and avoid *zavah* status. The Gemara adds the ruling of *ein koshi linefalim*—if there is a miscarriage rather than a live birth, the *kishuy* leniency does not apply at all, and even three days of *zavah*-period bleeding make her a *zavah gedolah* without any offset.

## Post-Birth Bleeding

### Two Positions

The Mishnah addresses a case of *hamakshah betoch shemonim shel nekevah kodem sheyeitzei havalad*—a woman who is experiencing *kishuy* during her eighty days of post-birth status following the birth of a girl, before the subsequent child has been born. The sixty-six days following the initial fourteen constitute a period of *toharah* in which even ordinary, painless bleeding does not render her a *niddah* or a *zavah*. The question is: what is the status of bleeding that occurs during these sixty-six days and is accompanied by *kishuy*—that is, pains attributable to a subsequent fetus?

The *tanna kamma* holds that all such blood remains *tahor* until the second birth. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees and rules that it is *tamei*.

### First Argument

The Rabbis confront Rabbi Eliezer with a *kal vachomer*. Before childbirth, the law is strict regarding ordinary bleeding (*dam shofei*)—it can create *zavah* status—and yet lenient regarding *dam koshi*, blood accompanied by childbirth pain. Here, during the sixty-six days of *toharah*, ordinary bleeding is already entirely *tahor*. How much more so, then, should bleeding with *kishuy* be *tahor*? If *kishuy* confers leniency even in a context where ordinary bleeding is *tamei*, surely it should confer leniency in a context where ordinary bleeding is already *tahor*.

### Dayo Principle

Rabbi Eliezer responds: *dayo lavo min hadin lihyot kenidon*—it is sufficient for the conclusion of a *kal vachomer* to be equal to its source, not greater than it. The premise of *kishuy*'s leniency is that it exempts her from *tumas zavah*. That is all. One cannot derive from *kishuy* a broader exemption from *tumas niddah*, because the source of the leniency—*kishuy*—speaks only of *zavah* status. Therefore, even if the *kal vachomer* is accepted, its conclusion is limited: she is not a *zavah*, but she can still be a *niddah* if the bleeding falls during *niddah* days.

The Rabbis' argument thus yields only that she is not a *zavah*—which Rabbi Eliezer does not dispute. He is perfectly willing to concede that *kishuy* exempts her from *zavah* status during these sixty-six days. His position is that the blood may still produce *niddah* status, and since the *kal vachomer* cannot exceed its source (*dayo*), the exemption from *zavah* does not carry over to an exemption from *niddah*.

### Second Argument

The Rabbis then reformulate: *amru lo harei anu meshivim lach lashon acher*—they reframe the *kal vachomer* entirely. Rather than comparing pain before birth to pain after birth, they now compare pain after birth to non-pain after birth. During the sixty-six days, ordinary bleeding—without any *kishuy*—is completely *tahor*, creating no status of *niddah* or *zavah*. Since bleeding with *kishuy* is always treated more leniently than bleeding without, and since bleeding without *kishuy* creates no status here at all, how much more so should bleeding with *kishuy* create no status during this period?

This is a stronger argument. The original *kal vachomer* compared two instances of pain in different contexts; Rabbi Eliezer could deflect it with *dayo*, saying the conclusion of *kishuy* is simply no *zavah*. But the reformulated argument does not compare two instances of *kishuy* at all. It compares *kishuy* after birth to ordinary bleeding after birth. Since ordinary bleeding after birth produces nothing, and since *kishuy* is always the more lenient category, blood with *kishuy* here must also produce nothing.

### Self vs. Fetus

Rabbi Eliezer holds firm: *afilu atem meshivim kol hayom kulo*—you may reformulate this all day long. The *dayo* principle still applies. However the *kal vachomer* is constructed, its premise remains the leniency of *kishuy*, and *kishuy* removes only *zavah* status. Nothing in the *kishuy* framework has ever addressed *niddah* status. Therefore, the conclusion can reach no further than the exemption from *zavah*, leaving *niddah* intact.

### Wellspring Limit

Rava offers a sharper formulation of Rabbi Eliezer's underlying logic. The Rabbis' basis for saying that *kishuy* exempts her from *zavah* is the verse *dameha machmat atzmah v'lo machmat valad*—the bleeding that creates *zavah* status must come from the woman herself, not as a consequence of the fetus. Since *kishuy* indicates that the blood is attributable to the fetus, it is not her own blood, and thus not *dam zivah*. Rava notes that by the same token, the Torah specifies regarding the post-birth *toharah* period: *tahorah mimkor dameha*—she is pure from the source of her blood. This refers to blood that emerges naturally from her own body during those sixty-six days. Blood that flows as a result of a subsequent fetus is *not* from her natural wellspring—it has a different cause—and the Torah's grant of *toharah* does not extend to it. Thus Rabbi Eliezer is on firm textual ground: the *toharah* of the post-birth period covers her own natural bleeding, not bleeding generated by a new fetus.

### No Niddah Either

The Gemara records the Rabbis' final rejoinder. Even granting Rabbi Eliezer's logic—that blood from the subsequent fetus is not covered by the *toharah* of the sixty-six days—the conclusion that she becomes a *niddah* during this period is untenable. *Amar kra teshev yeshiva achat lekulan*: the Torah states that she shall sit her singular sitting covering all cases. The structure of the post-birth *toharah* is unitary and consistent. Since she categorically cannot be a *zavah* during these sixty-six days—even Rabbi Eliezer does not contest this—she equally cannot be a *niddah*. The Torah's decree of *toharah* for the period is all-encompassing: once she is exempt from *zavah* status throughout, she is correspondingly exempt from *niddah* status as well. The two statuses rise and fall together within this period, and Rabbi Eliezer's attempt to maintain *niddah* liability while conceding the absence of *zavah* liability cannot be sustained.
