# Niddah 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kishuy Duration

### Kishuy Timeframe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה הִיא קִשּׁוּיָהּ? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳. הַשְׁתָּא חֲמִשִּׁים מְקַשְּׁיָא, אַרְבָּעִים מִיבַּעְיָא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לָא קַשְׁיָא — כָּאן לְחוֹלָה, כָּאן לִבְרִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** How long before birth is pain attributable to **her** labor **pains,** such that the blood is not considered blood of *ziva*? **Rabbi Meir says:** Even forty or fifty days before the birth. The Gemara asks: **Now** that you have said that even **fifty** days before the due date a woman can experience labor **pains, is** it **necessary** to teach that she can experience them **forty** days before? **Rav Ḥisda says:** This is **not difficult. Here,** where the mishna states that she can experience labor pains fifty days before birth, it is referring **to a sick woman; there,** where the mishna states that she can experience labor pains forty days before birth, it is referring **to a healthy woman.**

The Gemara on 37b opens with a fundamental question concerning *kishuy*—the phenomenon of childbirth pains accompanying bleeding. Having established the circumstances under which such pains render blood non-problematic, the Gemara now asks: how far back in time can the pains still be attributed to the childbirth? In other words, how many days before birth can we say that bleeding accompanied by pain is related to the impending birth, such that it does not create *dam zivah*?

### Three Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: כַּמָּה הוּא קִשּׁוּיָהּ? רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַרְבָּעִים וַחֲמִשִּׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a difficulty with regard to the opinion of Levi: **We learned** in the mishna: **How long** before birth **is** pain attributable to **her** labor **pains? Rabbi Meir says:** Even **forty or fifty days** before the birth. Rabbi Meir apparently maintains that a woman who experiences continuous labor pains accompanied by emissions of blood for forty or fifty days remains pure.

The Mishnah presents three positions. Rabbi Meir says forty or fifty days. Another opinion holds that one month suffices. A third limits it to two weeks.

The Gemara immediately notes a difficulty with Rabbi Meir's formulation: if fifty days is sufficient, is there any question about forty? The phrase "forty or fifty" would normally suggest an open-ended range—as in the first Mishnah in Sukkah, where Rabbi Yehuda says a *sukkah* may be forty or fifty *amot* tall, meaning there is no upper limit at all. Tosafot raises this point. But as the Gemara will demonstrate shortly, Rabbi Meir treats fifty as a precise number, offering an exact count of days. The Gemara therefore asks: if fifty is the precise figure, why does the Mishnah also cite forty?

### Sick vs. Healthy

Rav Chisda resolves this: *kan chola, kan bria*—the distinction turns on whether the woman is sick or healthy. Rashi explains that for a woman with a weaker constitution, bleeding can plausibly be attributed to the fetus at an earlier stage, up to fifty days before birth; for a healthy woman, the connection holds only up to forty days.

### Rabbi Levi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: אֵין הַוָּלָד מְטַהֵר אֶלָּא יָמִים הָרְאוּיִין לִהְיוֹת בָּהֶן זָבָה, וְרַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּיָמִים הָרְאוּיִין לִסְפִירַת זָבָה. אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: וּלְטַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַב
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the *halakha* that a woman who experiences labor pains does not contract the impurity of *ziva*, **Rabbi Levi says: The** birth of **a child renders** the mother **ritually pure** from *ziva* **only** if she experienced bleeding during the eleven **days that are fit** for her **to become a *zava*.** But if she experienced bleeding due to labor pains during the days of menstruation that precede or follow those eleven days, she is a menstruating woman. **And Rav says: Even** if she continued to experience bleeding **during** the **days that are fit for the counting of a *zava*,** i.e., in the seven days following the eleven days of *ziva*, which are also part of her days of menstruation, she remains pure. **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: And according to the reasoning of Rav,**

Rabbi Levi introduces a more restrictive principle: *ein hadam metaher ela yamim hare'uyim lihyot bahen zavah*—the *kishuy* renders blood pure only during those days when she is actually fit to become a *zavah*. The critical distinction underlying Rabbi Levi's position is that blood during the *niddah* period is problematic regardless of accompanying pain; *kishuy* operates exclusively to neutralize *dam zivah*. Consequently, only the eleven days of the *zavah* period—and no others—are affected by the *kishuy*.

### Rava's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ יָמִים הָרְאוּיִין לִסְפִירַת סְתִירַת זָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **even** if she continues to experience bleeding **during** the **days that are fit for the** new **counting** following **the negation** of the days counted by **a *zava*,** she remains pure. If a woman experiences bleeding during her seven clean days, she negates any days counted thus far, and must begin a new count of seven clean days. Accordingly, all the days that follow her days of *ziva* are effectively considered days that are fit for the counting of a *zava*. Therefore, blood emitted due to labor pains during these days is also ritually pure. Consequently, the blood that accompanies labor pains is ritually impure only if she begins to emit it during her days of menstruation.

Rava extends this framework: *afilu biyamim hare'uyim lisfirat zivah*—the *kishuy* applies even during the days relevant to the *counting* of *zavah* status, not merely during the eleven days themselves. To illustrate: suppose a woman bled at some point during her pregnancy, say on the first of the month, making her a *niddah*. Starting on day eight, any subsequent bleeding would constitute *dam zivah*. Now, had she actually become a *zavah gedolah* during days eight through eighteen, she would have needed seven clean days afterward. Therefore, blood on any of the days running from day eight through day twenty-five is in some sense "related" to a potential *zavah* status—either as the *zavah* period itself or as the seven-day counting period that would follow it. Rava holds that *kishuy* renders blood pure throughout this extended range, through day twenty-five.

### Rav Adda

Rav Adda bar Ahava goes further still, following Rava's logic to its ultimate conclusion. According to Rava's reasoning, the *kishuy* applies not only through day twenty-five but potentially indefinitely. Once she has bled once and entered the *niddah* period, a *zavah* period opens on day eight. If she were to bleed three times during that period, she would become a *zavah gedolah* and would need seven clean days. But if bleeding interrupted those seven days, the count would be reset, and she could theoretically never complete the seven clean days. Thus, from day eight onward, every subsequent day is potentially a day relevant to the *zavah* counting process—an extension of the unresolved *zavah* status. Rav Adda therefore holds that any blood after the first seven days of the initial bleeding is considered potential *dam zivah*, and *kishuy* renders it pure. This is a striking *kula*: once a *niddah* period occurs, virtually any subsequent bleeding—no matter how far removed in time—falls within the scope of *kishuy* leniency.

### Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ כְּרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה, אֶלָּא לְלֵוִי — קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the difficulty: **Granted,** the mishna is clear **according to Rav,** as **you find** that it is possible that she will remain pure for such a long period, i.e., **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav Adda bar Ahava** that Rav deems the blood pure even during the days that are fit for the new counting following the negation of the days counted by a *zava*. Accordingly, as long as the woman begins to experience labor pains during her days of *ziva*, she remains pure until birth, provided the labor pains continue without a twenty-four-hour period of resting. **But according to Levi,** who maintains that blood accompanying labor pains is pure only during the days of *ziva*, the mishna is **difficult,** as it is impossible for her blood to remain pure due to labor pains for fifty days.

The Gemara now asks how these positions square with Rabbi Meir's statement that *kishuy* applies for forty or fifty days. This formulation implies that for an entire stretch of forty or fifty days, bleeding accompanied by pain is rendered pure—but how is that consistent with Rabbi Levi, who limits *kishuy* to the eleven days of the *zavah* period?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ לֵוִי: מִי קָתָנֵי ״טְהוֹרָה בְּכוּלָּן״? בִּימֵי נִדָּה — נִדָּה, בִּימֵי זִיבָה — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Levi** could **say to you: Does** the mishna **teach** that she is **pure during all** these days, i.e., that she remains pure throughout the days of menstruation and *ziva*? Not so. Rather, if she emitted blood due to labor pains **during the days of menstruation** she has the status of **a menstruating woman,** whereas if she emitted the blood **during the days of *ziva*** she is **pure.** The mishna is teaching only that she will not be deemed a *zava* on account of this blood.

The answer on behalf of Rabbi Levi: *mi katani tahorah b'kullan?*—the Mishnah never says she is *tehorah* for the entire forty or fifty days. Rather, *biyemei niddah niddah, biyemei zivah tehorah*: during the *niddah* days she remains a *niddah*, and during the *zavah* days she is *tehorah*. The statement that *kishuy* is relevant for forty or fifty days means only that this is the window within which the laws of *kishuy* are operative—the rules apply whenever, within that stretch, she happens to be in a *zavah* period. It does not guarantee that she is *tehorah* throughout.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא אָמְרִי: אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: אֵין הַוָּלָד מְטַהֵר אֶלָּא יָמִים הָרְאוּיִין לִהְיוֹת בָּהֶן זָבָה גְּדוֹלָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דָּמָהּ ״יָמִים רַבִּים״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** Some Sages **state another version** of the above disagreement: **Rabbi Levi says: The** birth of **a child renders** the mother **ritually pure only** if she experienced bleeding during the eleven **days that are fit** for her **to become a greater *zava*,** i.e., if she experienced bleeding on three consecutive days during that time. But if she experienced bleeding on only one or two days she is rendered a lesser *zava*, and she must observe a clean day for each day she experiences a discharge. **What is the reason?** It **is written:** "And if a woman has an issue of her blood many days…all the days of the issue of her impurity she shall be as in the days of her menstruation: She is impure" (Leviticus 15:25). The verse is referring specifically to a greater *zava*, indicating that the *halakha* that a woman is not rendered a *zava* on account of an emission of blood caused by labor pains applies only to a greater *zava*.

The Gemara asks further about the second formulation attributed to Rabbi Levi: *ein hadam metaher ela yamim hare'uyim lihyot bahen zavah gedolah*—it renders blood pure only on days when she could become a *zavah gedolah*. Tosafot presents two readings of this stricter formulation. One reading holds that it excludes days one and two of the *zavah* period, since a *zavah gedolah* requires three consecutive days of bleeding and therefore cannot be initiated on those days. The other reading excludes days ten and eleven, since a *zavah gedolah* cannot be completed if the bleeding begins that late. Either way, Tosafot understands Rabbi Levi to be restricting *kishuy* to a subset of the eleven-day period.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ יָמִים הָרְאוּיִין לִהְיוֹת בָּהֶן זָבָה קְטַנָּה, מַאי טַעְמָא? ״יְמֵי״ וְ״כׇל יְמֵי״ הָתָם כְּתִיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abba Shaul says in the name of Rav:** The birth of a child renders the mother ritually pure **even** if she experienced bleeding on the **days that are fit** for her **to become a lesser *zava*. What is the reason? "Days" and "many days" are written there,** from which the impurity of a lesser *zava* is derived. Accordingly, the verse includes a lesser *zava* in this *halakha* as well.

Rashi reads the passage differently. In his view, the formulation "*zavah gedolah*" tells us not which days are covered, but what type of *zavah* status is neutralized. *Kishuy* prevents her from becoming a *zavah gedolah*, but it does not prevent her from becoming a *zavah ketanah*. If she bleeds once or twice during the *zavah* period with accompanying pain, she remains a *zavah ketanah* and still requires one clean day. *Kishuy* thus provides immunity only from *zavah gedolah* status, not from all forms of *zavah*.

In the subsequent formulation, *Abba Shaul* citing Rava holds: *afilu yamim hare'uyim lihyot bahen zavah ketanah*—*kishuy* renders blood pure even on days when she could become only a *zavah ketanah*. The *drashah* for *zavah ketanah* status derives from the same verse (*dameha yamim rabim*) used to establish that *kishuy* applies, and therefore the full eleven days are included. Both approaches ultimately limit the operative period to eleven days; the expansive reading of Rav Adda bar Ahava—where *kishuy* applies indefinitely after any initial bleeding—is what accounts for the forty or fifty days mentioned by Rabbi Meir.

## Bleeding Calculations

### 150 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ מְקַשָּׁה מֵאָה וַחֲמִשִּׁים יוֹם, וְאֵין זִיבָה עוֹלָה בָּהֶן. כֵּיצַד? שְׁנַיִם בְּלֹא עֵת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the above statement of Rabbi Meir, it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: There is** a scenario in which a woman **experiences** labor **pains** accompanied by emissions of blood for **one hundred and fifty days, and** the impurity of ***ziva* is not included in those** days, i.e., she will not be rendered a greater *zava*. **How** so? First, a woman emits blood for **two** days **not at the time** of her menstruation, e.g., she experiences bleeding on the tenth and eleventh days of *ziva*. She is not rendered a menstruating woman by these emissions, as they occurred during the days of *ziva*, nor does she become a greater *zava*, since she experienced bleeding on only two days.

The Gemara introduces a remarkable calculation in the name of Rabbi Meir: *yesh makshah kuf nun yom ve'ein zavah olah bahen*—there is a case in which a woman can bleed for 150 consecutive days and never become a *zavah*. The Gemara then works through exactly how this is achieved.

### Precise Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשִׁבְעָה נִדָּה, וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁל אַחַר הַנִּדָּה, וַחֲמִשִּׁים שֶׁהַוָּלָד מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir continues: **And** then she continues to experience bleeding throughout the **seven** days of **menstruation.** She is not considered to have experienced bleeding of *ziva* for three consecutive days, as the third emission occurred during her days of menstruation. **And** she then experiences bleeding for the first **two** days **after the** days of **menstruation,** i.e., on the first and second days of the eleven days of *ziva*. **And** on the third day of *ziva* she begins the period of **fifty** days before giving birth, during **which the child renders** her **pure.** This constitutes a period of sixty-one days during which she is not rendered a greater *zava*.

The construction begins with a sequence of bleeding that precedes the fifty-day *kishuy* window. A woman bleeds for two days during the *zavah* period—she is a *zavah ketanah* but not a *zavah gedolah*. Then she bleeds for seven days as a *niddah*. Then she bleeds for two more days in the *zavah* period following the *niddah*—again a *zavah ketanah*, not a *zavah gedolah*. This totals eleven days of bleeding, and she has not yet become a *zavah gedolah*. On the very next day, she would be in the *zavah* period and a third day of bleeding would create *zavah gedolah* status—but this is precisely when the fifty-day *kishuy* period begins. The Gemara notes that this makes clear that Rabbi Meir treats fifty as a precise figure: he is counting exactly.

### Full 150 Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוֹנִים שֶׁל נְקֵבָה, וְשִׁבְעָה נִדָּה, וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁל אַחַר הַנִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir continues: **And** this period is followed by **eighty** days **of** purity from *ziva* after giving birth to **a female,** for a total of 141 days during which she does not become a greater *zava* on account of blood emitted. **And** there are then **seven** days of **menstruation** following her days of purity, **and** another **two** days **after the** days of **menstruation,** i.e., the first and second days of *ziva*, during which she experiences bleeding, for a total of 150 days during which she does not become a greater *zava* on account of blood emitted.

For the next fifty days, she bleeds continuously, but since it is accompanied by *kishuy*, none of it constitutes *dam zivah*. She then gives birth—to a girl. The birth itself triggers *tumas yoledet*: two weeks of *niddah* status followed by sixty-six days of *toharah*, during which even normal bleeding does not render her a *zavah*. These eighty days of post-birth status add to the total: eleven plus fifty is sixty-one, plus eighty is one hundred forty-one. After the eighty days, she bleeds seven more days as a *niddah*, then two more days in the *zavah* period—again a *zavah ketanah* only. That adds nine more days, bringing the total to one hundred fifty. At no point has she become a *zavah gedolah*.

### Lifetime Bleeding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אִם כֵּן, יֵשׁ מְקַשָּׁה כׇּל יָמֶיהָ, וְאֵין זִיבָה עוֹלָה בָּהֶן!
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Meir: **If so, there is** also a scenario in which a woman **experiences** emissions of blood due to labor **pains** for **all the days of her** life, **and** the impurity of ***ziva* is not included in them.** For example, she gives birth to a female, and at the conclusion of her days of impurity engages in intercourse with her husband and becomes pregnant. She continues to experience bleeding throughout her days of purity, after which she experiences bleeding during the seven days of menstruation and the first two days of the next cycle of *ziva*. She then experiences labor pains accompanied by blood for fifty days, after which she miscarries a female. She subsequently becomes pregnant again within eighty days of the miscarriage, and the above process repeats itself. In this manner, she will never become a greater *zava*.

The Gemara raises a pointed objection: if this reasoning is accepted, one could construct a scenario in which a woman bleeds every single day of her life—continuously cycling through *kishuy* periods, births, and eighty-day post-birth periods—and never become a *zavah*. The scenario involves a woman who repeatedly gives birth, then during her eighty days of *toharah* miscarries, restarting the cycle indefinitely. The implication is that the entire structure of *zavah* law could be circumvented.

### No Kishuy Miscarriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: מַאי דַּעְתַּיְיכוּ? מִשּׁוּם נְפָלִים? אֵין קוֹשִׁי לִנְפָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Meir **said to them: What is your opinion,** that a woman is not rendered a *zava* **due to** the birth of **non-viable newborns?** The *halakha* that blood emitted due to labor **pains** does not render a woman a *zava* does **not** apply **to non-viable newborns.**

Rabbi Meir responds: *ein koshi linefalim*. The laws of *kishuy* apply only when the bleeding leads to a live birth. A miscarriage does not trigger the *kishuy* leniency. Therefore, any scenario dependent on miscarriage to extend the cycle will not work: if the birth is a miscarriage, then whatever bleeding preceded it—even if accompanied by pain—does not benefit from *kishuy*, and three days of *zavah*-period bleeding would render her a *zavah gedolah*.

The Gemara notes in passing that one could still construct a scenario of perpetual bleeding without *zavah* status by chaining together multiple live births without invoking *kishuy* at all—simply by becoming pregnant again during the eighty *toharah* days, giving birth, and repeating. But if one wants to include *kishuy* as part of the mechanism, that portion of the argument collapses wherever miscarriage is involved.

## 100 Days Baraita

### 100-Day Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יֵשׁ רוֹאָה מֵאָה יוֹם, וְאֵין זִיבָה עוֹלָה בָּהֶן. כֵּיצַד? שְׁנַיִם בְּלֹא עֵת, וְשִׁבְעָה נִדָּה, וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁל אַחַר הַנִּדָּה, וּשְׁמוֹנִים שֶׁל נְקֵבָה, וְשִׁבְעָה נִדָּה, וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁל אַחַר הַנִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **There is** a scenario in which a woman **sees** blood for **one hundred days, and** the impurity of greater ***ziva* is not included in them. How** so? She emits blood for **two** days **not at the time** of her menstruation, **and** then throughout the **seven** days of **menstruation, and** then for the first **two** days **after the** days of **menstruation. And** this period is followed by **eighty** days **of** purity from *ziva* after giving birth to **a female, and seven** days of **menstruation** following her days of purity, **and** another **two** days **after the** days of **menstruation.** This amounts to one hundred days.

A *beraita* presents a more modest version of the calculation: *yesh ro'ah me'ah yom ve'ein zivah olah bahen*—there is a case in which a woman can bleed for one hundred days without ever becoming a *zavah*. The construction here does not invoke *kishuy* at all and involves only a single birth. She bleeds two days in the *zavah* period, seven days as a *niddah*, and two more in the *zavah* period—eleven days total, still not a *zavah gedolah*. She then gives birth to a girl, and the following eighty days of post-birth *toharah* contribute eighty more days of bleeding. After those eighty days, she bleeds seven days as a *niddah* and two more in the *zavah* period—nine additional days. The total: eleven plus eighty plus nine equals one hundred days of continuous bleeding, without *kishuy* and with a single birth, and she has never been a *zavah gedolah*.

### No Blood Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר אִי אֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** this *baraita* **teaching us?** The Gemara responds: The *baraita* serves **to exclude** the opinion of **the one who said** that **it is impossible for** there to be **an opening of the womb [*hakever*] without** an emission of **blood.** According to this opinion, if a woman gives birth after experiencing bleeding on the first two days after the days of menstruation, it is certain she experienced bleeding on the third day as well, as every birth is accompanied by an emission of blood. Consequently, she has experienced bleeding on three consecutive days during the days of *ziva* and is rendered a greater *zava*. The *baraita* **teaches us that it is possible for** there to be **an opening of the womb without** an emission of **blood.** Therefore, if she gave birth on the third day of *ziva* and she did not experience bleeding, she is not rendered a greater *zava*.

The Gemara asks: *mai kamashma lan?*—what is the *beraita* teaching us that we do not already know? The answer is that the *beraita* comes to reject the position that *i efshar liftichat hakever belo dam*—it is impossible for the opening of the womb to occur without blood. In the scenario described, the woman bleeds two days as a *zavah ketanah*, then seven as a *niddah*, then two more—and then gives birth. One might suppose that the very process of the child exiting through the womb would itself cause blood to flow before the child had fully emerged, which would be a third day of *zavah*-period bleeding and render her a *zavah gedolah* before the *yoledet* status took hold.

The *beraita* teaches that we do not make that assumption. We do not presume that blood necessarily accompanies the opening of the womb. If blood is seen, it is noted; but absent actual evidence of bleeding, we do not presuppose it. Therefore, the scenario can in principle be completed—a woman exits the *zavah*-period bleeding and enters childbirth without the act of birth itself generating an additional day of *zavah* blood.

Rashi notes an apparent tension: in any real-world case, bleeding during the act of childbirth would be accompanied by the pains of *kishuy* and would thus not constitute *dam zivah* regardless. The scenario requires us to imagine—however hypothetically—that there were no childbirth pains, so that we can speak of the one-hundred-day count as independent of *kishuy*. The theoretical point, however, stands: *ptichat hakever* does not automatically entail blood.

## Ninth Month View

### Ninth Month Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: דַּיָּהּ חׇדְשָׁהּ, וְיֵשׁ בַּדָּבָר לְהָקֵל וּלְהַחֲמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** It is **sufficient** that a woman is not rendered a *zava* on account of blood that is emitted due to labor pains only from within one month of her due date. In this regard, it **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Tarfon:** It is **sufficient for her** not to be rendered a *zava* from within one **month** of **her** due date. **And there is** an aspect of **leniency and** an aspect of **stringency in the matter.**

Rabbi Yehuda, citing Rabbi Tarfon, holds: *daiyah chodshah*—her month is sufficient. Rather than computing fifty days back or focusing solely on the eleven-day *zavah* period, Rabbi Yehuda focuses on whether the bleeding with pain occurred within the ninth month of pregnancy. A *beraita* elaborates that this position contains both a stringency and a leniency relative to other opinions.

### Eighth Month Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? קִשְּׁתָה שְׁנַיִם בְּסוֹף שְׁמִינִי וְאֶחָד בִּתְחִלַּת תְּשִׁיעִי, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּתְחִלַּת תְּשִׁיעִי יָלְדָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: **How** so? If **she experienced** labor **pains** accompanied by an emission of blood for **two** days **at the end of** her **eighth** month of pregnancy, which are days in which she can be rendered a *zava*, **and** for **one** day **at the beginning of** her **ninth** month, then **even** if she **gave birth** at **the beginning of** the **ninth** month, the one day from the ninth month joins together with the two days from the eighth month, and **this** woman is considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*.** This is an aspect of stringency.

The stringent dimension: if a woman bled for two days at the end of the eighth month and one day at the beginning of the ninth, and gave birth even at the very beginning of the ninth month, she is a *yoledet bezov*—she gives birth having been a *zavah*. The reason is that the majority of the three days of potentially *zavah*-producing bleeding occurred in the eighth month. Since Rabbi Yehuda's rule looks to the month in which the bleeding predominantly occurred, the single day in the ninth month is treated as fundamentally belonging to the eighth-month bleeding episode, and *kishuy* provides no relief.

### Ninth Month Always OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל קִשְּׁתָה יוֹם אֶחָד בְּסוֹף שְׁמִינִי וּשְׁתַּיִם בִּתְחִלַּת תְּשִׁיעִי, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּסוֹף תְּשִׁיעִי יָלְדָה — אֵין זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if **she experienced** labor **pains** accompanied by an emission of blood for **one day at the end of** her **eighth** month, **and** for only **two** days **at the beginning of** her **ninth** month, then **even** if she **gave birth at the end of** the **ninth** month, **this** woman **is not** considered **one who gives birth as a *zava*,** and she is pure. This is an aspect of leniency.

The lenient dimension is striking: if she bled one day at the end of the eighth month and two days at the beginning of the ninth, and gave birth at the very end of the ninth month—even without any continuous pain leading up to the delivery—she is not a *yoledet bezov*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, once the majority of the bleeding occurred within the ninth month, the entire episode is attributed to childbirth, and *kishuy* applies. There is no requirement that the pain be continuous and uninterrupted all the way to the moment of birth. This is a significant leniency beyond all other positions, which generally require the pain to connect the bleeding to the actual birth.

### Calendar Month

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָסָבַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה שִׁיפּוּרָא גָּרֵים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says: Conclude from** the *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the blast of **the shofar** at the beginning of the ninth month **causes** the blood emitted during that month to be pure. That is to say, once the ninth months begins, any blood emitted does not render her a *zava*.

Rav Adda bar Ahava draws an inference from Rabbi Yehuda's formulation: *shema minah kasavar Rabbi Yehuda shipura grain*—Rabbi Yehuda holds that it is the *shofar*, the announcement of *Rosh Chodesh*, that is determinative. The "month" in question is not the woman's personal ninth month of pregnancy, counted from conception, but the ninth calendar month as established by *beit din*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין אִשָּׁה מִתְעַבֶּרֶת וְיוֹלֶדֶת אֶלָּא לְמָאתַיִם וְשִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד יוֹם, אוֹ לְמָאתַיִם וְשִׁבְעִים וּשְׁנַיִם יוֹם, אוֹ לְמָאתַיִם וְשִׁבְעִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** that the entire ninth month of pregnancy is suitable for giving birth, and therefore any blood seen during the ninth month is attributed to the imminent labor? **But doesn't Shmuel say** that **a woman becomes pregnant and gives birth only after 271 days,** which is a full nine months, **or after 272 days, or after 273** days? She will not, however, give birth during the ninth month itself.

Both Rashi and Tosafot address this inference at length. Tosafot's key argument is as follows: Rabbi Yehuda draws a sharp distinction between two days in the ninth month and one in the eighth versus two in the eighth and one in the ninth. But how could such precision be applied to a personal pregnancy month? The day of conception cannot be known with certainty—semen can remain viable for up to three days, so the actual day of fertilization is unknown. Since Rabbi Yehuda's rule demands exactness about which day belongs to which month, the "month" must be the objective calendar month, not the imprecise personal month of pregnancy. The determination is therefore a formal, *halachic* one based on the calendar sanctified by *beit din*, rather than a purely biological calculation.

## Pregnancy Biology

### 271 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כַּחֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, דְּתַנְיָא: חֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים לֹא הָיוּ מְשַׁמְּשִׁין מִטּוֹתֵיהֶן אֶלָּא בִּרְבִיעִי בְּשַׁבָּת, שֶׁלֹּא יָבוֹאוּ נְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן לִידֵי חִלּוּל שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Shmuel **said** his statement **in accordance with** the opinion of **the early** generations of **pious** men. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The early** generations of **pious** men **would engage in sexual intercourse only on a Wednesday, so that their wives should not come to a desecration of Shabbat.**

Shmuel states: *ein ishah mis'aberet veyoledet ela l'masayim veshivim ve'echad yom*—a woman does not conceive and give birth except at two hundred seventy-one days. That is, counting the day of conception as day one, pregnancy lasts through day two hundred seventy and birth occurs on day two hundred seventy-one. The Gemara adds that birth may also occur on day two hundred seventy-two or two hundred seventy-three—not because women vary, but because semen may remain viable for up to three days after intercourse. If fertilization occurs on the day of intercourse, birth will be on day two hundred seventy-one; if not until the second day, on two hundred seventy-two; if not until the third day, on two hundred seventy-three. The pregnancy itself is exactly two hundred seventy days from conception.

This precision appears to conflict with Rabbi Yehuda's view that birth can occur anywhere across the ninth month. The Gemara explains that Shmuel speaks in accordance with the practice of the early *chasidim*.

### Chasidim Wednes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּרְבִיעִי וְתוּ לָא? אֵימָא: מֵרְבִיעִי וְאֵילָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** If they were to engage in sexual intercourse on a Sunday, Monday, or Tuesday, their wives might give birth on Shabbat, either 271, 272, or 273 days after conception either. The Gemara asks: If this was indeed the concern of the early generations of pious men, why would they engage in intercourse only **on a Wednesday, and nothing more?** After all, if they engaged in intercourse on a Thursday, Friday, or Shabbat, they would likewise avoid any desecration of Shabbat. The Gemara answers: **Say** that the *baraita* means that the early generations of pious men would engage in intercourse each week only **from Wednesday onward.**

The *beraita* reports: *chasidim harishonim lo hayu meshamshim mitoteihem ela b'revi'i b'Shabbat*—the early pious ones would only engage in marital relations on Wednesday, so that their wives would not be placed in a situation of giving birth on Shabbat and encountering issues associated with that circumstance. Their practice was premised on Shmuel's precise calculation: if the length of pregnancy from the day of intercourse is a fixed number of days, then the day of birth can be predicted from the day of intercourse, and one can choose accordingly.

### Day Calculation

The Gemara asks: why only Wednesday? If it is Wednesday and beyond, what about Thursday, Friday, and Shabbat itself? The Gemara clarifies: *m'revi'i ve'eilach*—from Wednesday onward. The reasoning is as follows. Since two hundred seventy-three is divisible by seven, the day of birth falls on the same weekday as the day of intercourse when 273 days are counted. If intercourse occurred on Sunday and birth is on day 273, she gives birth on Shabbat. If intercourse was on Monday and birth is on day 272, she gives birth on Shabbat. If intercourse was on Tuesday and birth is on day 271, she gives birth on Shabbat. Therefore, intercourse on Sunday, Monday, or Tuesday could result in a Shabbat birth. Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, or Shabbat will not, because none of the three possible day-counts—271, 272, or 273—from those days lands on Shabbat. The *chasidim* therefore restricted themselves to Wednesday onward.

### Gematria 271

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דַּחֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים? דִּכְתִיב ״וַיִּתֵּן [ה׳] לָהּ הֵרָיוֹן״ — ״הֵרָיוֹן״ בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא מָאתַן וְשִׁבְעִים וְחַד הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said: What is the reasoning of the early** generations of **pious** men, who state that a woman does not give birth before 271 days have passed from the time of insemination? **As it is written** with regard to Boaz and Ruth: "And he went in unto her, **and the Lord gave her conception [*herayon*]"** (Ruth 4:13). The letters that constitute the word ***herayon* are 271 in numerical value.**

The source for Shmuel's figure of 271 is a *drashah*: *vayiten Hashem lah herayon*—"and God gave her a pregnancy" (in the context of Ruth). The word *herayon*, pregnancy, has a *gematria* of two hundred seventy-one. The days of 272 and 273 are derivative of this base figure, accounting for the possible delay between intercourse and fertilization.

### Seventh Month Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יוֹלֶדֶת לְתִשְׁעָה אֵינָהּ יוֹלֶדֶת לִמְקוּטָּעִים, יוֹלֶדֶת לְשִׁבְעָה יוֹלֶדֶת לִמְקוּטָּעִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיְהִי לִתְקוּפוֹת הַיָּמִים וַתַּהַר חַנָּה וַתֵּלֶד בֵּן״, מִיעוּט ״תְּקוּפוֹת״ — שְׁנַיִם, מִיעוּט ״יָמִים״ — שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra says: Even according to the one who said,** e.g., Shmuel and the early generations of pious men, that a woman **who gives birth at nine** months **does not give birth after an incomplete** number of months, i.e., she carries for a full nine months, nevertheless, a woman **who gives birth at seven months can give birth after an incomplete** number of months. **As it is stated** with regard to the birth of Samuel: **"And it came to pass, when the seasons of the days had come, that Hannah conceived, and bore a son"** (I Samuel 1:20). **The minimal** number of **"seasons"** is **two,** and as each season of the year is three months, this amounts to six months. And **the minimal** number of **"days" is two.** If so, Samuel was born in the seventh month of Hannah's pregnancy.

Mar Zutra adds a distinction: even according to the opinion that a nine-month birth occurs only after a full nine months (*yoledet l'tisha einah yoledet l'mekutain*)—that is, a nine-month baby is always carried to term and born only after complete months—a seven-month birth is different. *Yoledet l'sheva yoledet l'mekutain*: a seven-month baby can be born even at the very beginning of the seventh month. The scriptural basis is the verse regarding Chana: *vayehi litkufat hayamim vatahar Chana vateled ben*—"and it was after the period of days that Chana conceived and bore a son." The Gemara derives: *miut tekufot shtayim*—the minimum number of *tekufot* (seasons of three months each) is two, equaling six months; *miut yamim shnayim*—the minimum number of additional days is two. Six months and two days places the birth at the very beginning of the seventh month, showing that a viable seventh-month birth can occur from the outset of that month.

## Two Weeks View

### Two Week Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אֵין קוֹשִׁי יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁתֵּי שַׁבָּתוֹת. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְטָמְאָה שְׁבוּעַיִם כְּנִדָּתָהּ״ — כְּנִדָּתָהּ וְלֹא כְּזִיבָתָהּ, מִכְּלָל דְּזִיבָתָהּ טְהוֹרָה. וְכַמָּה שְׁבוּעַיִם?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** Labor **pains** do **not** occur **more than two weeks** before birth. **Shmuel said: What is the reasoning of the Rabbis,** i.e., Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon? **As it is written** with regard to a woman who gives birth to a female: **"And she shall be impure two weeks, as in her menstruation"** (Leviticus 12:5). The verse indicates that she is impure **as in her menstruation but not as in her *ziva*. By inference,** one may conclude **that her *ziva* is pure,** i.e., if she emits blood accompanied by labor pains she is not rendered a *zava*. **And** for **how** long is this the case? For **two weeks.**

Rabbi Yosi and Rabbi Shimon hold the most restrictive position: *ein koshi yoter mi'shtei shabbatot*—*kishuy* applies for no more than two weeks before the birth. Shmuel explains the reasoning behind this view.

### Two Week Verse

The derivation is from the verse regarding the *yoledet*: *v'tam'ah shvuayim k'nidatah*—she shall be *tamei* two weeks like her *niddah*. The analysis proceeds: *k'nidatah v'lo k'zivatah*—only like *niddah* status applies during this period, not *zavah* status. This implies that *zavah* blood during the period around childbirth is *tehorah*—because the verse specifies *niddah* and not *zavah*, we learn that *zavah* blood is set aside during this time. The question then becomes: for how long? The verse itself provides the answer—*shvuayim*, two weeks. The same verse from which we derive the leniency of *kishuy* also specifies its duration: two weeks.

### 25 Days Max

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יֵשׁ מְקַשָּׁה עֶשְׂרִים וַחֲמִשָּׁה יוֹם, וְאֵין זִיבָה עוֹלָה בָּהֶן. כֵּיצַד? שְׁנַיִם בְּלֹא עֵת, וְשִׁבְעָה נִדָּה, וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁלְּאַחַר נִדָּה, וְאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שֶׁהַוָּלָד מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakha* that blood emitted due to labor pains does not impart impurity of *ziva*, **the Sages taught: There is** a scenario in which a woman **experiences** labor **pains** accompanied by emissions of blood for **twenty-five days** before birth, **and** the impurity of greater ***ziva* is not included in them. How** so? She emits blood for **two** days **not at the time** of her menstruation, i.e., on the tenth and eleventh days of *ziva*, **and** then for the **seven** days of **menstruation, and** then for the first **two** days of *ziva* **that** come **after** the days of **menstruation. And** this period is followed by **fourteen** days during **which the** impending birth of **a child renders pure** the blood she sees.

A *beraita* then works through the calculation under this view. A woman can bleed for twenty-five days without becoming a *zavah gedolah*: two days in the *zavah* period (*zavah ketanah*), seven days as a *niddah*, two more days in the *zavah* period (*zavah ketanah* again)—totaling eleven days. Then the two-week *kishuy* window applies, covering fourteen additional days. Eleven plus fourteen equals twenty-five days of bleeding without *zavah gedolah* status.

### 26 Days Zavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר שֶׁתִּתְקַשֶּׁה עֶשְׂרִים וְשִׁשָּׁה יוֹם בְּלֹא וָלָד, וְלֹא תְּהֵא יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And it is impossible for** a woman **to experience** labor **pains** accompanied by emissions of blood for **twenty-six days without a child,** such that **she will not be** a woman **who gives birth as a *zava*.** Since a span of twenty-six days includes at least five days of *ziva*, there will inevitably be three consecutive days of *ziva* during which she experienced bleeding, therefore rendering her a greater *zava*.

If she were to bleed for twenty-six days before birth, she would inevitably become a *zavah gedolah*. The twenty-sixth day would fall within the *zavah* period and, combined with previous *zavah*-period days, would constitute a third consecutive day of *dam zivah*, making her a *zavah gedolah*. The *beraita* states this as a limit: one cannot have twenty-six days of pre-birth bleeding with *kishuy* and avoid *zavah* status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּלֹא וָלָד? בִּתְלָתָא נָמֵי סַגִּי! אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אֵימָא ״בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ וָלָד״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא ״בְּלֹא וָלָד״ קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: Is the *baraita* really dealing with a case **without a child,** i.e., where she is not even pregnant? If there is no child, then experiencing bleeding **on three** consecutive days after the days of menstruation **is also sufficient** to render her a greater *zava*. **Rav Sheshet said** one should **say** the *baraita* as follows: Twenty-six days **in a case where there is a child,** i.e., where she is pregnant. **Rava said to** Rav Sheshet: **But** the *baraita* explicitly **teaches: Without a child.**

The Gemara adds the ruling of *ein koshi linefalim*—if there is a miscarriage rather than a live birth, the *kishuy* leniency does not apply at all, and even three days of *zavah*-period bleeding make her a *zavah gedolah* without any offset.

## Post-Birth Bleeding

### Two Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' הַמְקַשָּׁה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים שֶׁל נְקֵבָה — כׇּל דָּמִים שֶׁהִיא רוֹאָה טְהוֹרִין עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא הַוָּלָד, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A woman **who experiences** labor **pains within eighty** days **of** giving birth to **a female,** e.g., she conceived during the sixty-six days of purity, or she initially conceived twins and she gave birth to a female and the birth of the second fetus was delayed, **all blood that she sees is ritually pure,** as she is currently within her days of purity. And this remains the *halakha* **until the child emerges** from the womb, at which point she is rendered impure as a woman who gives birth. **And Rabbi Eliezer deems ritually impure** the blood that occurs due to these labor pains.

The Mishnah addresses a case of *hamakshah betoch shemonim shel nekevah kodem sheyeitzei havalad*—a woman who is experiencing *kishuy* during her eighty days of post-birth status following the birth of a girl, before the subsequent child has been born. The sixty-six days following the initial fourteen constitute a period of *toharah* in which even ordinary, painless bleeding does not render her a *niddah* or a *zavah*. The question is: what is the status of bleeding that occurs during these sixty-six days and is accompanied by *kishuy*—that is, pains attributable to a subsequent fetus?

The *tanna kamma* holds that all such blood remains *tahor* until the second birth. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees and rules that it is *tamei*.

### First Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהֶחְמִיר בְּדַם הַשּׁוֹפִי, הֵיקֵל בְּדַם הַקּוֹשִׁי; מְקוֹם שֶׁהֵיקֵל בְּדַם הַשּׁוֹפִי, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנָּקֵל בְּדַם הַקּוֹשִׁי?
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to Rabbi Eliezer: And what,** if **in an instance where** the verse **was stringent with regard to blood** emitted while **resting,** namely if a pregnant woman emits blood after her days of purity without any labor pains she is rendered impure, the verse **was lenient with regard to blood** that accompanies **the** labor **pains;** then in **an instance where** the verse **was lenient with regard to blood** emitted while **resting,** i.e., during a woman's days of purity, **is it not right that we will be lenient with regard to blood** that accompanies **the** labor **pains?**

The Rabbis confront Rabbi Eliezer with a *kal vachomer*. Before childbirth, the law is strict regarding ordinary bleeding (*dam shofei*)—it can create *zavah* status—and yet lenient regarding *dam koshi*, blood accompanied by childbirth pain. Here, during the sixty-six days of *toharah*, ordinary bleeding is already entirely *tahor*. How much more so, then, should bleeding with *kishuy* be *tahor*? If *kishuy* confers leniency even in a context where ordinary bleeding is *tamei*, surely it should confer leniency in a context where ordinary bleeding is already *tahor*.

### Dayo Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן, מִמָּה הֵיקֵל עָלֶיהָ? מִטּוּמְאַת זִיבָה, אֲבָל טְמֵאָה טוּמְאַת נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: It is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source.** In other words, the status of blood emitted due to labor pains during her days of purity should not be more lenient than that of blood emitted due to labor pains after her days of purity. Rabbi Eliezer elaborates: **Concerning what** type of impurity **was** the verse **lenient with regard to** a woman who experiences an emission of blood due to labor pains? **Concerning the impurity of *ziva*. But she** may still be rendered **ritually impure** with **the impurity of a menstruating woman.** So too, if a woman experiences emissions of blood due to labor pains during her days of purity, she is rendered a menstruating woman.

Rabbi Eliezer responds: *dayo lavo min hadin lihyot kenidon*—it is sufficient for the conclusion of a *kal vachomer* to be equal to its source, not greater than it. The premise of *kishuy*'s leniency is that it exempts her from *tumas zavah*. That is all. One cannot derive from *kishuy* a broader exemption from *tumas niddah*, because the source of the leniency—*kishuy*—speaks only of *zavah* status. Therefore, even if the *kal vachomer* is accepted, its conclusion is limited: she is not a *zavah*—which Rabbi Eliezer does not dispute. He is perfectly willing to concede that *kishuy* exempts her from *zavah* status during these sixty-six days. His position is that the blood may still produce *niddah* status, and since the *kal vachomer* cannot exceed its source (*dayo*), the exemption from *zavah* does not carry over to an exemption from *niddah*.

### Second Argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הֲרֵי אָנוּ מְשִׁיבִין לְךָ לָשׁוֹן אַחֵר, וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁהֶחְמִיר בְּשׁוֹפִי שֶׁלִּפְנֵי הַוָּלָד — הֵיקֵל בְּקוֹשִׁי שֶׁעִמּוֹ. מָקוֹם שֶׁהֵיקֵל בְּשׁוֹפִי שֶׁלְּאַחַר הַוָּלָד — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנָּקֵל בְּקוֹשִׁי שֶׁעִמּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him: But we** will **respond to you** with **another version** of the *a fortiori* inference: **And what,** if **in an instance where** the verse **was stringent with regard to** blood that is emitted while **resting before** the birth of **the child** the verse **was lenient with regard to** blood that is emitted due to labor **pains that** come **with it,** i.e., which precede the birth of the child; then in **an instance where** the verse **was lenient with regard to** blood emitted while **resting,** namely during a woman's days of purity, **is it not right that we will be lenient with regard to** blood that accompanies **the** labor **pains that** come **with it?**

The Rabbis then reformulate: *amru lo harei anu meshivim lach lashon acher*—they reframe the *kal vachomer* entirely. Rather than comparing pain before birth to pain after birth, they now compare pain after birth to non-pain after birth. During the sixty-six days, ordinary bleeding—without any *kishuy*—is completely *tahor*, creating no status of *niddah* or *zavah*. Since bleeding with *kishuy* is always treated more leniently than bleeding without, and since bleeding without *kishuy* creates no status here at all, how much more so should bleeding with *kishuy* create no status during this period?

This is a stronger argument. The original *kal vachomer* compared two instances of pain in different contexts; Rabbi Eliezer could deflect it with *dayo*, saying the conclusion of *kishuy* is simply no *zavah*. But the reformulated argument does not compare two instances of *kishuy* at all. It compares *kishuy* after birth to ordinary bleeding after birth. Since ordinary bleeding after birth produces nothing, and since *kishuy* is always the more lenient category, blood with *kishuy* here must also produce nothing.

### Self vs. Fetus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: אֲפִילּוּ אַתֶּם מְשִׁיבִין כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן, מִמָּה הֵיקֵל עָלֶיהָ מִטּוּמְאַת זִיבָה, אֲבָל מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאַת נִדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: Even** if **you respond** by citing *a fortiori* inferences of that type throughout **the entire day,** the principle remains that **it is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source. Concerning what** type of impurity **was** the verse **lenient with regard to** a woman who experiences an emission of blood due to labor pains? **Concerning the impurity of *ziva*. But** an emission of blood **renders her impure** with **the impurity of a menstruating woman.**

Rabbi Eliezer holds firm: *afilu atem meshivim kol hayom kulo*—you may reformulate this all day long. The *dayo* principle still applies. However the *kal vachomer* is constructed, its premise remains the leniency of *kishuy*, and *kishuy* removes only *zavah* status. Nothing in the *kishuy* framework has ever addressed *niddah* status. Therefore, the conclusion can reach no further than the exemption from *zavah*, leaving *niddah* intact.

### Wellspring Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בְּהָא זַכִּינְהוּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְרַבָּנַן, לָאו אָמְרִיתוּ ״דָּמָהּ״ — דָּמָה מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָהּ וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת וָלָד, הָכִי נָמֵי ״וְטָהֲרָה מִמְּקוֹר דָּמֶיהָ״ — דָּמֶיהָ מֵחֲמַת עַצְמָהּ וְלֹא מֵחֲמַת וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: With this** response **Rabbi Eliezer triumphed over the Rabbis: Didn't you say** that the reason why a woman is not rendered a *zava* on account of blood that is emitted due to labor pains before birth is because the verse states: "And if a woman has an issue of her blood many days" (Leviticus 15:25)? The term **"her blood"** indicates that only **her blood** that comes **due to herself** is impure due to *ziva*, **but not** blood that comes **due to** her **child. So too,** one can say that as the verse states with regard to a woman who gives birth: **"And she shall be purified from the fountain of her blood"** (Leviticus 12:7), this indicates that only **her blood** that comes **due to herself** is pure from *ziva*, **but not** blood that comes **due to** her **child.**

Rava offers a sharper formulation of Rabbi Eliezer's underlying logic. The Rabbis' basis for saying that *kishuy* exempts her from *zavah* is the verse *dameha machmat atzmah v'lo machmat valad*—the bleeding that creates *zavah* status must come from the woman herself, not as a consequence of the fetus. Since *kishuy* indicates that the blood is attributable to the fetus, it is not her own blood, and thus not *dam zivah*. Rava notes that by the same token, the Torah specifies regarding the post-birth *toharah* period: *tahorah mimkor dameha*—she is pure from the source of her blood. This refers to blood that emerges naturally from her own body during those sixty-six days. Blood that flows as a result of a subsequent fetus is *not* from her natural wellspring—it has a different cause—and the Torah's grant of *toharah* does not extend to it. Thus Rabbi Eliezer is on firm textual ground: the *toharah* of the post-birth period covers her own natural bleeding, not bleeding generated by a new fetus.

### No Niddah Either

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: בִּימֵי נִדָּה — נִדָּה, בִּימֵי זִיבָה — טְהוֹרָה? אָמַר קְרָא ״תֵּשֵׁב״ — יְשִׁיבָה אַחַת לְכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** This Gemara raises a difficulty against the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer: If so, one can **say** that if she emits blood due to labor pains **during the days of menstruation** she is **a menstruating woman,** but if she emits blood **in the days of *ziva*,** i.e., after the days of menstruation, she is **pure.** The Gemara explains that **the verse states: "She shall observe** the blood of purity" (Leviticus 12:5), which indicates that there is **one observance for all** the days of her purity, i.e., all the blood she emits due to labor pains during her days of purity is either pure or impure.

The Gemara records the Rabbis' final rejoinder. Even granting Rabbi Eliezer's logic—that blood from the subsequent fetus is not covered by the *toharah* of the sixty-six days—the conclusion that she becomes a *niddah* during this period is untenable. *Amar kra teshev yeshiva achat lekulan*: the Torah states that she shall sit her singular sitting covering all cases. The structure of the post-birth *toharah* is unitary and consistent. Since she categorically cannot be a *zavah* during these sixty-six days—even Rabbi Eliezer does not contest this—she equally cannot be a *niddah*. The Torah's decree of *toharah* for the period is all-encompassing: once she is exempt from *zavah* status throughout, she is correspondingly exempt from *niddah* status as well. The two statuses rise and fall together within this period, and Rabbi Eliezer's attempt to maintain *niddah* liability while conceding the absence of *zavah* liability cannot be sustained.
