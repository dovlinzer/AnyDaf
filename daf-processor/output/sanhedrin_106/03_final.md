# Sanhedrin 106 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bilam's Reversed Curses

### Intro & Vayasem Davar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״וַיָּשֶׂם דָּבָר בְּפִי בִּלְעָם״, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר – מַלְאָךְ, רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אָמַר – חַכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And the Lord placed a matter in Bilaam's mouth"** (Numbers 23:5), **Rabbi Elazar says:** It was **an angel** that spoke from his mouth. **Rabbi Yonatan says:** It was **a hook** placed in his mouth to prevent him from saying anything else.

The daf begins roughly eleven lines from the bottom of the page, with the verse *vayasem davar b'fi Bilam* — "God placed a word in the mouth of Bilam." This serves as the launching point for a broader discussion of how exactly God controlled Bilam's speech, and what Bilam had actually intended to say.

### Angel vs. Hook Debate

*Rabbi Eliezer* understands *vayasem davar* to mean that an angel was given to Bilam to instruct and constrain what he said. *Rabbi Yochanan*, by contrast, reads it as *chaka* — a fishhook — meaning God dragged the words out of him. The *Gra* suggests these two positions are not truly in conflict: one describes the mechanism of preventing Bilam from saying what he wanted to say, while the other describes the mechanism of compelling him to say the right thing. One blocks the tongue; the other forces it.

### Reversing the Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִבִּרְכָתוֹ שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע אַתָּה לָמֵד מָה הָיָה בְּלִבּוֹ. בִּיקֵּשׁ לוֹמַר שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ לָהֶם בָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת – ״מַה טֹּבוּ אֹהָלֶיךָ יַעֲקֹב״. לֹא תִּשְׁרֶה שְׁכִינָה עֲלֵיהֶם – ״וּמִשְׁכְּנֹתֶיךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״. לֹא תְּהֵא מַלְכוּתָן נִמְשֶׁכֶת – ״כִּנְחָלִים נִטָּיוּ״. לֹא יְהֵא לָהֶם זֵיתִים וּכְרָמִים – ״כְּגַנֹּות עֲלֵי נָהָר״. לֹא יְהֵא רֵיחָן נוֹדֵף – ״כַּאֲהָלִים נָטַע ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From the blessing of that wicked person,** Balaam, **you** can **ascertain what was in his heart.** God transformed the curses that he planned into blessings. **He sought to say that they should not have synagogues and study halls,** and he said instead: **"How goodly are your tents, Jacob"** (Numbers 24:5), a blessing on their synagogues. He sought to say **that the Divine Presence [*shekhina*] will not rest upon them,** and he said instead: **"And your dwellings [*mishkenot*] Israel."** He sought to say that **the kingdom of Israel would not continue,** and he said instead that it would continue: **"Like the winding brooks"** (Numbers 24:6), which flow continuously. He sought to say that **they would have no olive trees and vineyards,** and he said instead: **"Like gardens by the river's side"** (Numbers 24:6). He sought to say that **their fragrance would not diffuse** from their fulfillment of mitzvot, and he said instead: **"Like aloes that the Lord has planted"** (Numbers 24:6).

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: from the blessings of that wicked person you can deduce — simply by reversing them — what he had wanted to curse Israel with, because God reversed his curses into blessings. The *pasuk* itself states *vayafoch Hashem Elokecha lach es hakelalah libracha* — "God reversed the curse into a blessing."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יִהְיוּ לָהֶם מְלָכִים בַּעֲלֵי קוֹמָה – ״כַּאֲרָזִים עֲלֵי מַיִם״. לֹא יִהְיֶה לָהֶם מֶלֶךְ בֶּן מֶלֶךְ – ״יִזַּל מַיִם מִדָּלְיָו״. לֹא תְּהֵא מַלְכוּתָן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּאוּמּוֹת – ״וְזַרְעוֹ בְּמַיִם רַבִּים״. לֹא תְּהֵא עַזָּה מַלְכוּתָן – ״וְיָרֹם מֵאֲגַג מַלְכּוֹ״. לֹא תְּהֵא אֵימַת מַלְכוּתָן – ״וְתִנַּשֵּׂא מַלְכֻתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** He sought to say that **they would not have kings of stature,** and he said instead: **"Like cedar trees beside the waters"** (Numbers 24:6). He sought to say that **they will not have a king the son of a king,** and he said instead: **"Water shall flow from his branches"** (Numbers 24:7). He sought to say that **their kingdom would not rule over the nations,** and he said instead: **"And his seed shall be in many waters"** (Numbers 24:7). He sought to say that **their kingdom would not be fierce,** and he said instead: **"And his king shall be higher than Agag."** He sought to say **that there will be no fear of their kingdom,** and he said instead: **"And his kingdom shall be exalted"** (Numbers 24:7).

Working through the blessings in sequence: Bilam had wanted to say that Israel should have no *batei knesiyos u'batei midrashot* — synagogues and study halls — and instead was forced to say *Ma Tovu Ohalecha Yaakov*, "How beautiful are your tents, O Jacob." He wanted to say that the *shechinah* should not rest upon them, and instead uttered *mishkenotecha Yisrael* — your dwelling places, drawing on the same root as *shechinah*. He wanted to say that their kingship should not be extended, and instead produced the image of *kenachalim nitayu* — rivers stretched out — a symbol of enduring dominion. He wanted to say they should have no olive trees or vineyards, and instead said *keganos alei nahar* — like gardens by the river. He wanted their aroma not to be fragrant, and instead gave them *k'ahalim nata Hashem* — like *ahalim* planted by God. The word *ohel* can denote both a tent and the aromatic substance *ahalot* mentioned in *mor ve'ahalot*, and so the blessing carries a double meaning. He wanted to say they should have no kings of stature, and instead said *kearazim alei mayim* — like cedar trees by the water. He wanted to say there should be no king son of a king — no dynastic continuity — and instead gave the image of *yizal mayim midalyav*, water flowing from one bucket to the next. He wanted to say their kingship should not dominate other nations, and instead said *vezaro bemayim rabim* — his descendants over many waters. He wanted to say their kingship should not be powerful, and instead gave *vayarom meAgag malko*. He wanted to say their kingship should not inspire fear, and instead declared *vetinesa malchuso*.

Every one of these metaphors, when reversed, reveals what Bilam had been reaching for — a comprehensive curse across the dimensions of divine presence, kingship, land, fragrance, and fear.

### Curses Reverted Except O…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא: כּוּלָּם חָזְרוּ לִקְלָלָה, חוּץ מִבָּתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּמִבָּתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּהֲפֹךְ ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ לְּךָ אֶת הַקְּלָלָה לִבְרָכָה כִּי אֲהֵבְךָ ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ״. קְלָלָה, וְלֹא קְלָלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Kahana says: All of** the blessings ultimately **reverted to** be fulfilled as the **curse** that he originally intended, as all of those circumstances befell the Jewish people, **except for** the destruction of **synagogues and study halls, as it is stated: "And the Lord your God transformed the curse into a blessing for you, because the Lord your God loved you"** (Deuteronomy 23:6). **A curse** in the singular, **not curses** in the plural, was transformed permanently.

*Amar Rabbi Abba bar Kahana*: in the end, *kulam chazru leklalah* — all of the blessings reverted to their originally intended curse. Whether this happened because of Bilam's original intent, or simply because of Israel's subsequent sins, is an open question, but the observation stands: the kingship is gone, the land with its olive trees and vineyards is gone, so much of what the blessings described has in fact been lost. *Chutz mi'batei knesiyos u'mibatei midrashot* — the sole exception is the very first blessing, *Ma Tovu Ohalecha Yaakov*. No matter the depth of exile, synagogues and study halls have persisted. The *pasuk* is then read carefully: *kelalah velo kalalot* — "the curse" in the singular, implying that only one of the curses remained permanently reversed into a blessing, while the others ultimately reasserted themselves.

### Beloved's Wounds vs Enem…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״נֶאֱמָנִים פִּצְעֵי אוֹהֵב וְנַעְתָּרוֹת נְשִׁיקוֹת שׂוֹנֵא״? טוֹבָה קְלָלָה שֶׁקִּילֵּל אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹתֵר מִבְּרָכָה שֶׁבֵּרְכָם בִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע. אֲחִיָּה הַשִּׁילוֹנִי קִילֵּל אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּקָנֶה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִכָּה ה׳ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל כַּאֲשֶׁר יָנוּד הַקָּנֶה בַּמַּיִם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Faithful are the wounds of a friend; but the kisses of an enemy are importunate"** (Proverbs 27:6)? **Better is the curse that Ahijah the Shilonite cursed the Jewish people than the blessing that Balaam the wicked blessed them. Ahijah the Shilonite cursed Israel with a reed, as it is stated: "For the Lord shall smite Israel as a reed is shaken in water"** (I Kings 14:15).

*Amar Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: what is meant by the verse *ne'emanim pitzei ohev vena'atarot neshikot soneh* — "trustworthy are the wounds of a beloved, but the kisses of an enemy are unreliable"? Better is the curse that *Achiyah HaShiloni* the prophet cursed Israel with than the blessings that *Bilam HaRasha* bestowed upon us. The wounds of a friend — even when painful — are rooted in genuine care, while an enemy's kindness conceals a hidden agenda. As one student aptly noted, this fits the verse precisely: Achiyah's curse came from genuine prophetic concern for Israel's welfare, while Bilam's blessing was rooted in an attempt to destroy.

### Reed vs Cedar Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה קָנֶה זֶה עוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹם מַיִם, וְגִיזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף, וְשׇׁרָשָׁיו מְרוּבִּין. וַאֲפִילּוּ כׇּל רוּחוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם בָּאוֹת וְנוֹשְׁבוֹת בּוֹ, אֵין מְזִיזוֹת אוֹתוֹ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ, אֶלָּא הוּא הוֹלֵךְ וּבָא עִמָּהֶן. כֵּיוָן שֶׁדּוֹמְמוּ הָרוּחוֹת, עָמַד קָנֶה בִּמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **just like a reed** that **stands in a place** near **water,** as the water sustains it, **and its stalk replenishes** itself, as if it is cut another grows, **and its roots are numerous. And even if all the winds that are in the world come and gust against it, they do not move it from its place** and uproot it. **Rather, it goes and comes with** the winds. And **once the winds subside the reed remains in its place.**

When *Achiyah HaShiloni* wanted to curse Israel, he used the image of a reed: *vehika Hashem et Yisrael kaasher yanud hakaneh bamayim* — "God will smite Israel as the reed sways in the water." On the surface this appears harsh — Israel will not be firm, they will sway. But consider what a reed in water actually is. No matter how many waters rush past it, the reed remains rooted where it was planted. Even if cut down, *vegizo machalif* — it grows back. Its roots are numerous and deep. Even if all the winds of the world blow against it, they do not uproot it from its place; rather, *hu holech uva imahem* — it sways along with them. And when the winds finally cease, *amad hakaneh bimkomo* — the reed stands exactly where it always stood. The curse is therefore a genuine blessing: we will suffer, we will sway, but we will endure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל בִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע בֵּרְכָן בְּאֶרֶז. מָה אֶרֶז זֶה? אֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹם מַיִם, וְשׇׁרָשָׁיו מוּעָטִין, וְאֵין גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף. אֲפִילּוּ כׇּל הָרוּחוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם בָּאוֹת וְנוֹשְׁבוֹת בּוֹ, אֵין מְזִיזוֹת אוֹתוֹ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ. כֵּיוָן שֶׁנָּשְׁבָה בּוֹ רוּחַ דְּרוֹמִית, מִיָּד עוֹקַרְתּוֹ וְהוֹפַכְתּוֹ עַל פָּנָיו. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁזָּכָה קָנֶה לִיטּוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ קוּלְמוֹס לִכְתּוֹב מִמֶּנּוּ סִפְרֵי תוֹרָה, נְבִיאִים וּכְתוּבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **But Balaam the wicked blessed them with a cedar.** There is an aspect of curse in that blessing, as he was saying they will be **just like a cedar** that **does not stand in a place** near **water, and its roots are few** relative to its height, **and its trunk does not replenish** itself, as if it is cut it does not grow back. And **even** if **all the winds that are in the world come and gust against it, they do not move it from its place** and uproot it; but **once a southern wind gusts it immediately uproots** the cedar **and overturns it on its face. Moreover,** it is **the reed that was privileged to** have **a quill [*kulmos*] taken from it to write scrolls of Torah, Prophets, and Writings.** Therefore, the curse of Ahijah is better than the blessing of Balaam.

*Bilam HaRasha*, by contrast, when he blessed Israel, compared us to a cedar tree — *kearazim alei mayim*. And what is a cedar? *Eino omed bimkom mayim* — it does not grow from water. Its roots are shallow. If cut down, it does not regenerate. True, because of its rigidity, ordinary winds do not even move it — it will not bend at all. But *keivan shenashva ruach dromit* — once a powerful southern wind strikes it, *miyad okarto vehofachto al panav* — it is immediately uprooted and overturned. Within the blessing lurks a hidden curse, and within the curse a hidden blessing.

There is a further dimension to the reed's virtue: *ela shezacha kaneh litol himenu kulmus lichtov mimenu Sefer Torah* — the reed merits being fashioned into a quill with which a *Sefer Torah* is written. The resilience is not merely physical but ultimately spiritual in its orientation.

## Keini & Yitro Story

### Bilam Addresses Keini

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיַּרְא אֶת הַקֵּינִי וַיִּשָּׂא מְשָׁלוֹ״. אָמַר לוֹ בִּלְעָם לְיִתְרוֹ: קֵינִי, לֹא הָיִיתָ עִמָּנוּ בְּאוֹתָהּ עֵצָה? מִי הוֹשִׁיבְךָ אֵצֶל אֵיתָנֵי עוֹלָם?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Balaam: **"And he looked at the Kenite and he took up his parable** and said: Though firm is your dwelling place, and though your nest be set in rock" (Numbers 24:21). **Balaam said to Yitro: Kenite,** were **you not in Egypt with us in that counsel** to drown the newborn males of Israel? **Who placed you alongside the mighty of the world?**

The *daf* continues: *vayar et haKeini vayisa meshalo vayomar* — he saw the Keini and lifted his parable and said, *eitan moshavcha vesim basela kinecha* — strong is your dwelling place, and you set your nest in the rock, with *kinecha* playing on the name *Keini*. The *Gemara* identifies the *Keini* here as *Yitro*. Bilam says to him rhetorically: *lo hayita imanu b'otah eitzah* — weren't you with us in that council? How did you merit such greatness? The question *ma hoshivcha eitan olam* — who caused you to dwell with the mighty ones of the world, i.e., Israel — is rhetorical, given that Yitro had been part of the original plot.

### Three Counselors Plot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי סִימַאי: שְׁלֹשָׁה הָיוּ בְּאוֹתָהּ עֵצָה, אֵלּוּ הֵן: בִּלְעָם, אִיּוֹב וְיִתְרוֹ. בִּלְעָם שֶׁיָּעַץ – נֶהֱרַג, אִיּוֹב שֶׁשָּׁתַק – נִידּוֹן בְּיִסּוּרִין, וְיִתְרוֹ שֶׁבָּרַח – זָכוּ בְּנֵי בָנָיו לֵישֵׁב בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַגָּזִית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִשְׁפְּחוֹת סוֹפְרִים יוֹשְׁבֵי יַעְבֵּץ תִּרְעָתִים שִׁמְעָתִים שׂוּכָתִים הֵמָּה הַקִּינִים הַבָּאִים מֵחַמַּת אֲבִי בֵית רֵכָב״, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְנֵי קֵינִי חֹתֵן מֹשֶׁה עָלוּ מֵעִיר הַתְּמָרִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And that is what Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Simai says: Three were** associates **in that counsel, and they are: Balaam, Job, and Yitro. Balaam, who advised** to drown the newborn males, **was killed. Job, who was silent** and was reluctant to express his opinion, **was sentenced to** suffer **afflictions. And Yitro, who fled** after he disagreed with that counsel and Pharaoh sought to kill him, **his descendants were privileged to sit** as scribes in session with the Sanhedrin **in the Chamber of Hewn Stone, as it is stated: "And the families of the scribes who dwelt in Jabez; the Tirathites, the Shimeathites, the Sucathites. These were the Kenites who came of Hammath the father of the house of Rechab"** (I Chronicles 2:55). **And it is written there** with regard to the identity of the Kenites: **"And the children of the Kenite, Moses' father-in-law, went up from the city of the palm trees"** (Judges 1:16).

This brings the *Gemara* to the *derasha* of *Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* [citing] *Rabbi Simai*: *shlosha hayu b'ota eitza* — there were three participants in the counsel against Israel at Pharaoh's court: Bilam, Iyov, and Yitro. *Bilam she'ya'atz neherag* — Bilam gave the advice and was killed. *Iyov she'shatak nidon b'yisurin* — Iyov was silent; he had not the courage to speak against Pharaoh, and so he suffered. This is presented as the *Gemara*'s explanation for all of Iyov's sufferings — silence in the face of evil is itself a moral failing. It is not merely about active participation; failing to stand against evil, remaining a silent bystander, carries its own consequence. There is a resonance here with Iyov's narrative as well, where the first rounds of suffering are met with silence, and eventually he does speak.

### Yitro's Escape & Reward

*Yitro she'barach* — Yitro fled. He could not speak up — perhaps he would have been killed — but he refused to remain in place as a passive participant. He separated himself from the evil. And because of that separation, *zachu b'nei vanav layshev b'Lishkat HaGazit* — his descendants merited to sit in the Chamber of Hewn Stone, where the *Sanhedrin* convened in the *Beit HaMikdash*. And that is the meaning of *eitan moshavcha*: you dwell among the mighty, because you were present at the counsel but then rejected it and fled.

The *pasuk* cited is *umishpachot sofrim yoshvei Yabetz* — the family of scribes who dwelt in *Yabetz*, which is read as an allusion to the *Lishkat HaGazit*. *Hema haKinim haba'im me'Chamat avi beit Rechav* — these are the Kenites who came from Chamat, father of Beit Rechav. The identification is then clinched by the verse *u'vnei Keini choten Moshe* — the sons of Keini, the father-in-law of Moshe — establishing that *Keini* is the *mishpachat sofrim*, the family of scribes, and that *yoshvei Yabetz* means dwelling in the *Lishkat HaGazit*.

The image of Yitro simply running away might seem like avoidance, but the *Gemara* treats it as genuine moral courage. When facing a tyrant, options are limited: one can speak up and be killed, or one can flee — but remaining silent and in place is complicity. Moshe himself fled when Pharaoh was about to apprehend him. Running away, in this context, is a form of moral self-preservation and an act of dissociation from evil.

## Bilam's Final Prophecies

### Keini Parable

The *daf* continues with Bilam's final utterances: *eitan moshavcha vesim basela kinecha* — strong is your dwelling place, and you set your nest in the rock. The full force of this parable, as the *Gemara* has developed it above in relation to Yitro, affirms the reward that comes to one who dissociates from evil even without the ability to actively resist it.

### Woe & Lion Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּשָּׂא מְשָׁלוֹ וַיֹּאמַר אוֹי מִי יִחְיֶה מִשֻּׂמוֹ אֵל״. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: אוֹי מִי שֶׁמְּחַיֶּה עַצְמוֹ בְּשֵׁם אֵל. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹי לָהּ לָאוּמָּה שֶׁתִּמָּצֵא בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עוֹשֶׂה פִּדְיוֹן לְבָנָיו. מִי מֵטִיל כְּסוּתוֹ בֵּין לָבִיא לִלְבִיאָה בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנִּזְקָקִין זֶה עִם זֶה?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And he took up his parable, and said: Alas, he who lives from what God has appointed him"** (Numbers 24:23), **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: Woe unto one who sustains himself** in an indulgent manner **in the name of God,** i.e., Balaam, whose livelihood was from speaking in the name of God. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Woe unto the nation that will be found** hindering the Jewish people **at the time when the Holy One, Blessed be He, redeems His children. Who places his garment between a male lion and a female lion when they are mating?** One who does so will certainly die.

*Vayisa meshalo vayomar oy mi yichye mi'shumo El* — he lifted his parable and said: woe, who will live from God's placing of these things? *Amar Rabbi Shimon ben Levi*: from the word *mi'shumo* — from His name — woe is the person who gives himself life through the name of God, i.e., *mi she'mechayeh atzmo b'shem El*, the one who makes himself into a god. *Rashi* offers two readings: one is the person who literally goes around claiming to be a divine figure; the other is the person who decides to live a godless life devoted entirely to sensory and physical pleasure, treating himself as the ultimate authority. The former reading likely carries the *pshat*, though *Rashi*'s second explanation has also been noted by scholars as a not-so-shielded critique of certain religious movements.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the verse means something closer to the plain sense — *oy lah l'uma she'timatze b'sha'ah she'HaKadosh Baruch Hu oseh pidyon l'vanav* — woe to the nation that finds itself in the way when God comes to redeem His children. The metaphor Rabbi Yochanan deploys is striking: *mi matil ksuto bein lavi l'lviyah b'sha'ah she'niska'akin zeh im zeh* — who would throw his garment between a lion and a lioness when they are copulating? Lions appear indolent most of the time, but when they act, their full power is unleashed. The nation that interposes itself at that moment of divine redemption faces total annihilation.

### Assyria Ever Kittim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְצִים מִיַּד כִּתִּים״, אָמַר רַב: לִיבּוֹן אַסְפִּיר. ״וְעִנּוּ אַשּׁוּר וְעִנּוּ עֵבֶר״ – עַד אַשּׁוּר קָטְלִי מִיקְטָל, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ מְשַׁעְבְּדִי שַׁיעְבּוֹדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And ships come from the coast of Kittim"** (Numbers 24:24), **Rav says:** This is **the Roman legion [*libbun aspir*]** that will attack Assyria. **"And they shall afflict Assyria, and they shall afflict Eber"** (Numbers 24:24). **Before** they reach **Assyria they will kill** the Jewish people; **from that** point **forward they will enslave them** and not kill them.

The *pasuk* continues: *v'tzim miyad Kittim* — legions from the hand of *Kittim*. *Amar Rav Libun Aspir* — this is an identification whose precise historical referent is debated. The Soncino commentary offers several possibilities: some read *Aspir* as deriving from the Persian *aswar*, meaning knight, yielding the sense of a cavalry legion. *Kittim* is generally taken to denote Rome. One reading, attributed to Jastrow, interprets the entire passage as a reference to Leo the Isaurian, the Byzantine emperor who led the iconoclastic movement and provoked a prolonged conflict between the eastern and western halves of the empire — which would explain the term *Libun* as a reference to the "white" faction. Whatever the precise historical identification, the thrust is of a powerful military formation arising from the coastal regions associated with Rome.

*V'inu Ashur v'inu Ever* — they will afflict Assyria and they will afflict Ever. The *Gemara* then makes a subtle distinction: up to Assyria they will kill (*ad asher katli miktal*), but beyond that point — *mikan v'eilach m'shabdi shibudei* — they will no longer kill but only subjugate.

### Bilam's Real Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִנְנִי הוֹלֵךְ לְעַמִּי לְכָה אִיעָצְךָ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה הָעָם הַזֶּה לְעַמְּךָ״. ״עַמְּךָ לְעָם הַזֶּה״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא: כְּאָדָם שֶׁמְּקַלֵּל אֶת עַצְמוֹ וְתוֹלֶה קִלְלָתוֹ בַּאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Balaam said to Balak: **"Behold, I go to my people; come therefore, and I shall advise you what this people shall do to your people"** (Numbers 24:14). Ostensibly, **he should have** said: What **your people** shall do **to this people. Rabbi Abba bar Kahana says:** Balaam spoke **like a person who curses himself** but does not wish to utter so awful a matter **and** instead **ascribes his curse to** take effect on **others.**

*Hineni holech l'ami* — I am going back to my people. Bilam says to Balak: *l'cha i'atzcha asher ya'aseh ha'am hazeh l'amcha b'acharit hayamim* — let me advise you what this nation will do to your nation in the end of days. On the surface, this refers to the fate of Moab at the hands of Israel. But the *Gemara* understands it as the verse in which Bilam gave Balak his advice to use the Moabite and Midianite women to seduce Israel — because a later verse states *hein heinah hayu l'vnei Yisrael al dvar Bilam*, they were against Israel through the word of Bilam.

This creates a textual difficulty: the verse should read *amcha l'am hazeh* — what your nation should do to this nation — not the other way around. *Amar Rabbi Abba bar Kahana*: this is the speech pattern of *adam she'mekalel atzmo v'toleh klilato b'acherim* — a person who curses himself but frames it as speaking about others. *Rashi* offers an alternative explanation: Bilam phrased it normally, intending to advise how to bring about Israel's downfall, but the *pasuk* as written inverted the formulation — a kind of euphemism, declining to write the destructive counsel directly. Either way, the *Gemara* reads this as the locus of Bilam's advice to Balak to seduce Israel into sin.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: אֱלֹהֵיהֶם שֶׁל אֵלּוּ שׂוֹנֵא זִימָּה הוּא, וְהֵם מִתְאַוִּים לִכְלֵי פִשְׁתָּן. בּוֹא וְאַשִּׂיאֲךָ עֵצָה: עֲשֵׂה לָהֶן קְלָעִים, וְהוֹשֵׁיב בָּהֶן זוֹנוֹת, זְקֵינָה מִבַּחוּץ וְיַלְדָּה מִבִּפְנִים, וְיִמְכְּרוּ לָהֶן כְּלֵי פִשְׁתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** Balaam **said to them: The God of these** Jewish people **despises lewdness, and they desire linen garments,** as they have no new garments; **come, and I will give you advice. Make for them** enclosures using wall **hangings and seat prostitutes in them,** with **an old woman outside** the enclosure **and a young woman inside, and** have the women **sell them linen garments.**

*Amar lahem eloheihem shel eilu sonei zimah hu* — the God of this people hates sexual immorality. *V'heim mitavin l'chlei pishtan* — and they happen to desire fine linen garments. Come, let me give you counsel. The convergence of these two observations forms the basis of the strategy that follows.

## Seduction Strategy

### Marketplace Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה לָהֶן קְלָעִים מֵהַר שֶׁלֶג עַד בֵּית הַיְשִׁימוֹת, וְהוֹשִׁיב בָּהֶן זוֹנוֹת, זְקֵינָה מִבַּחוּץ וְיַלְדָּה מִבִּפְנִים. וּבְשָׁעָה שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל אוֹכְלִין וְשׁוֹתִין וּשְׂמֵחִין וְיוֹצְאִין לְטַיֵּיל בְּשׁוּק, אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ הַזְּקֵינָה: אִי אַתָּה מְבַקֵּשׁ כְּלֵי פִשְׁתָּן? זְקֵינָה אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ בְּשָׁוֶה, וְיַלְדָּה אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ בְּפָחוֹת, שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Balak **made for them** enclosures using wall **hangings from the snow mountain,** the Ḥermon, **until Beit HaYeshimot, and he sat prostitutes in them,** with **an old woman outside and a young woman on the inside. And at the time when Jewish people were eating and drinking and were glad and going out to stroll in the marketplace, the old woman would say to** a Jew: **Aren't you seeking linen garments?** He would enter the enclosure and ask the price, **the old woman would quote him** a price **equal** to its value, **and the young woman would quote him** a price **less** than its value. That scenario would repeat itself **two or three times.**

*Asei lahen kla'im* — make curtains for them, set up a kind of open-air market or street fair. *Vehoshiv bahen zonot* — station prostitutes in the booths. *Zkeinah mibachutz veyaldah mibifnim* — the older woman on the outside, so it will not be immediately apparent that seduction is the purpose, and the younger, more attractive woman on the inside. *Veyimkru lahen klei pishtan* — and have these booths sell fine linen garments, which will draw Israelite men to browse.

The plan was executed on a large scale: *mehar sheleg ad beit hayeshimot* — from Mount Sheleg to Beit Hayeshimot, an enormous expanse was set up in this fashion, with prostitutes stationed throughout, the older women at the entrance and the younger women inside. It is worth noting that this setup appears to draw on a real social phenomenon familiar to the *Gemara*'s audience — there are parallel references in the opening of *Avodah Zarah* to Roman markets that included prostitutes among the commercial offerings.

### Gradual Seduction

*Uvesha'ah sheYisrael ochlin veshotin vesmeichim veyotzim letayel bashuk* — when Israel were eating and drinking and rejoicing and going out to stroll in the market, the older woman would call out: would you be interested in fine linen garments? He would ask the price; the older woman would give the fair market rate. Then he would be drawn toward the younger woman inside, *veyaldah omeret lo befachot* — and she would offer him a discount. This exchange happens two or three times, drawing him back repeatedly, each visit extending his time in the company of the younger woman, ostensibly in the course of a commercial negotiation.

### Idol Worship Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַחַר כָּךְ אוֹמֶרֶת לוֹ: הֲרֵי אַתְּ כְּבֶן בַּיִת, שֵׁב בְּרוֹר לְעַצְמְךָ. וְצַרְצוּרִי שֶׁל יַיִן עַמּוֹנִי מוּנָּח אֶצְלָהּ, וַעֲדַיִם לֹא נֶאֱסַר יַיִן שֶׁל עַמּוֹנִי וְלֹא יַיִן שֶׁל גּוֹיִם. אָמְרָה לוֹ: רְצוֹנֶךָ שֶׁתִּשְׁתֶּה כּוֹס שֶׁל יַיִן? כֵּיוָן שֶׁשָּׁתָה, בָּעַר בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And thereafter she would say to him: You are like a member of our household, sit** and **choose for yourself** the merchandise that you want. **And a jug of Ammonite wine was placed near her, and** neither **Ammonite wine nor gentile wine had been prohibited yet** for Jews. **She said to him: Is it your wish to drink a cup of wine? Once he drank** the wine, his evil inclination **burned within him.**

After multiple visits, the younger woman would say: *harei atah k'ven bayit* — you are practically family here. *Shev go rolei le'atzmecha* — sit, look through the wares yourself. And she would produce a flask of Ammonite wine — *utzlochit shel yayin Amoni munach etzlah*. The *Gemara* inserts a historical note: *ve'adayin lo ne'esar yayin shel nochrim* — the wine of non-Jews had not yet been forbidden at that time. This is not merely informational; it also hints at why the prohibition was eventually enacted, since social wine-drinking between Jewish men and non-Jewish women clearly created the conditions for exactly this kind of boundary erosion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לָהּ: הִשָּׁמֵיעִי לִי. הוֹצִיאָה יִרְאָתָהּ מִתּוֹךְ חֵיקָהּ, אָמְרָה לוֹ: עֲבוֹד לָזֶה! אֲמַר לָהּ: הֲלֹא יְהוּדִי אֲנִי? אָמְרָה לוֹ: וּמָה אִיכְפַּת לְךָ? כְּלוּם מְבַקְּשִׁים מִמְּךָ אֶלָּא פִּיעוּר? [וְהוּא אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁעֲבוֹדָתָהּ בְּכָךְ]. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵינִי מַנַּחְתְּךָ עַד שֶׁתִּכְפּוֹר בְּתוֹרַת מֹשֶׁה רַבְּךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הֵמָּה בָּאוּ בַּעַל פְּעוֹר וַיִּנָּזְרוּ לַבֹּשֶׁת וַיִּהְיוּ שִׁקּוּצִים כְּאׇהֳבָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **He** then **said to her: Submit to me** and engage in intercourse with me. **She** then **removed** the **idol** that **she worshipped from her lap and said to him: Worship this. He said to her: Am I not Jewish?** I am therefore forbidden from engaging in idol worship. **She said to him: And what is your concern? We are asking you to do nothing more than defecate** in its presence. **But he does not know that its worship** is conducted **in that** manner. Once he did so, she said to him: **Moreover, I will not leave you until you deny the Torah of Moses your teacher, as it is stated: "But when they came to Ba'al-Peor they separated themselves to the shameful item; and they became detestable like that which they loved"** (Hosea 9:10). They devoted themselves to the disgrace of defecation, and detested the name of God.

Once he had drunk the wine, *ba'ar bo* — his passion was inflamed — and he asked her to be intimate with him. At that point she reached into her bosom and produced her idol: *avod lazeh* — worship this first, and then I will agree. He recoiled: *halo Yehudi ani* — I am a Jew. And she responded: *u'ma ichpat lach* — what does it matter? *Kum u'vakesh mimcha iypur* — I am only asking you to defecate before it. And *hu eino yodea she'avodato be'kach* — he does not know that that is in fact the mode of worship of the *Baal Peor*.

The dynamic being described is one of incremental compromise. Each step is minimized, each line crossed is framed as trivial — until, as *Rashi* inserts, she then says: and now, if you truly want to be with me, you must deny the Torah of Moshe your teacher. The sequence illuminates how moral collapse occurs: not through a single dramatic fall, but through the accumulation of small steps, each of which seemed manageable in isolation, until one has traveled so far from one's original position that what was once unthinkable becomes the price of one more step. This is the pedagogical warning embedded in the narrative — the same logic by which the rabbis cautioned against the dangers of any context that begins eroding boundaries.

### Baal Peor Plague

*Shene'emar heima ba'u ba'al pe'or* — they came to *Baal Pe'or*, *vayinazru laboshet* — and they strayed to their shame, *vayihyu shikutzim be'ahavam* — and they became abominations through their desire. The seduction strategy succeeded, and Israel was drawn into the sin of *Baal Pe'or*. The consequences were the plague of 24,000 dead, as the *Gemara* will note shortly.

## Shittim Debates

### Shittim Name Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֵּשֶׁב יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּשִּׁטִּים״. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: שִׁטִּים שְׁמָהּ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: שֶׁנִּתְעַסְקוּ בְּדִבְרֵי שְׁטוּת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And Israel dwelt in Shittim"** (Numbers 25:1), **Rabbi Eliezer says: Shittim** is **the name of** the place. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is an allusion to the fact **that they were engaged in matters of nonsense [*shetut*],** i.e., prostitution and idol worship.

*Vayeshev Yisrael bashitim* — Israel dwelt in the *Shitim*. *Rabbi Eliezer omer shitim shemo* — that was simply the name of the place. *Rabbi Yehoshua omer she'nitasku b'divrei shtut* — no, the name reflects what happened there: they engaged in matters of foolishness, the word *shitim* being read as related to *shtut*. This debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* follows a consistent pattern: *Rabbi Eliezer* takes names at face value while *Rabbi Yehoshua* reads meaning into them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַתִּקְרֶאןָ לָעָם לְזִבְחֵי אֱלֹהֵיהֶן״, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: עֲרוּמּוֹת פָּגְעוּ בָּהֶן. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ כּוּלָּם בַּעֲלֵי קְרָיִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And they called [*vatikrena*] the people to the offerings of their gods"** (Numbers 25:2), **Rabbi Eliezer says: Naked women encountered them. Rabbi Yehoshua says: They all became those who experienced a seminal emission [*kerayin*]** resulting from the lust that they experienced.

The *Gemara* then records their dispute about the nature of the seduction itself. *Rabbi Eliezer omer arumot pagu bahen* — they encountered the women naked, a blatant and direct form of enticement — though it is not entirely clear which *pasuk* this is derived from. *Rabbi Yehoshua omer shenatan b'kulam ba'al keri* — the entire nation experienced seminal emission, which is associated with the *Shitim* and the *ba'al keri* condition.

### Refidim Weakness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לְשׁוֹן ״רְפִידִים״? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: רְפִידִים שְׁמָהּ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: שֶׁרִיפּוּ עַצְמָן מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא הִפְנוּ אָבוֹת אֶל בָּנִים מֵרִפְיוֹן יָדָיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the homiletic interpretation of the names of places, the Gemara asks: **What** is the connotation of the **term Rephidim** (see Exodus 19:2)? **Rabbi Eliezer says: Rephidim** is **the name of** the place. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It is an allusion to the fact **that they enfeebled [*rippu*] themselves with regard to** engaging in **matters of Torah, as it is stated: "The fathers do not look back to their children from feebleness [*rifyon*] of hands"** (Jeremiah 47:3). There too, the connotation of the name is dereliction in the study of Torah.

Because one dispute of *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* about a place name has been cited, a second is brought alongside it, concerning *Refidim* and the attack of Amalek. *Rabbi Eliezer omer Refidim shemo* — that was simply the name of the place where Amalek encountered Israel. *Rabbi Yehoshua omer sherifu atzman midivrei Torah* — no, their hands had grown weak from Torah, the word *rifyon* being embedded in *Refidim*, and it was that spiritual weakness that gave Amalek its opening. *Shene'emar lo hifnu avot el banim merifyon yadayim* — "the fathers did not turn to the sons from the weakness of the hands."

### Fornication Begins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיֵּשֶׁב״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְשׁוֹן צַעַר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֵּשֶׁב יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּשִּׁטִּים וַיָּחֶל הָעָם לִזְנוֹת אֶל בְּנוֹת מוֹאָב״. ״וַיֵּשֶׁב יַעֲקֹב בְּאֶרֶץ מְגוּרֵי אָבִיו בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן״, ״וַיָּבֵא יוֹסֵף אֶת דִּבָּתָם רָעָה אֶל אֲבִיהֶם״. וְנֶאֱמַר: ״וַיֵּשֶׁב יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּאֶרֶץ גֹּשֶׁן״, ״וַיִּקְרְבוּ יְמֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לָמוּת״. ״וַיֵּשֶׁב יְהוּדָה וְיִשְׂרָאֵל לָבֶטַח אִישׁ תַּחַת גַּפְנוֹ וְתַחַת תְּאֵנָתוֹ״, ״וַיָּקֶם ה׳ שָׂטָן לִשְׁלֹמֹה אֵת הֲדַד הָאֲדֹמִי מִזֶּרַע הַמֶּלֶךְ הוּא בֶּאֱדוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Everywhere that it is stated: And he dwelt, it is nothing other than an expression of pain,** of an impending calamity, **as it is stated: "And Israel dwelt in Shittim, and the people began to commit harlotry with the daughters of Moab"** (Numbers 25:1). It is stated: **"And Jacob dwelt in the land where his father had sojourned in the land of Canaan"** (Genesis 37:1), and it is stated thereafter: **"And Joseph brought evil report of them to his father"** (Genesis 37:2), which led to the sale of Joseph. **And it is stated: "And Israel dwelt** in the land of Egypt **in the land of Goshen"** (Genesis 47:27), and it is stated thereafter: **"And the time drew near that Israel was to die"** (Genesis 47:29). It is stated: **"And Judah and Israel dwelt safely, every man under his vine and under his fig tree"** (I Kings 5:5), and it is stated thereafter: **"And the Lord raised up an adversary to Solomon, Hadad the Edomite; he was of the king's seed in Edom"** (I Kings 11:14).

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *vayachel ha'am liznot b'vnot Moav* — the people began to fornicate with the daughters of Moav. The word *vayachel*, "they began," invites a broader reflection on the pattern in which the word *vayeshev* — dwelling, settling — appears in *Tanach* and is followed by trouble. Several examples are cited: *vayeshev Yaakov b'eretz megurei aviv* and then the episode of Yosef and his brothers; *vayeshev Yisrael b'eretz Goshen* and then *vayikrevu yemei Yisrael lamut*; *vayeshev Yehuda v'Yisrael lavetach ish tachat gafno v'tachat te'enato* and then — several chapters later — the rise of *Hadad ha'Adomi* against Shlomo. These examples are admittedly of varying force; the last requires going some seven chapters forward to find the adverse development. The point being made is that *vayeshev* functions as a kind of *lashon tza'ar*, a term of distress — settledness and comfort precede a fall — though the examples are not all equally compelling.

## Bilam's Death

### Bilam Seeks Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת מַלְכֵי מִדְיָן הָרְגוּ עַל חַלְלֵיהֶם וְגוֹ׳ וְאֶת בִּלְעָם בֶּן בְּעוֹר הָרְגוּ בֶּחָרֶב״. בִּלְעָם מַאי בָּעֵי הָתָם? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁהָלַךְ לִיטּוֹל שְׂכַר עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה אֶלֶף [שֶׁהִפִּיל מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל]. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה אָמַר רַב, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: גַּמְלָא אֲזַלָא לְמִיבְעֵי קַרְנֵי, אוּדְנֵי דַּהֲווֹ לֵיהּ גְּזִיזָן מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to Balaam, it is stated: **"And they slew the kings of Midian, with the rest of their slain…And Balaam, son of Beor, they slew with the sword"** (Numbers 31:8). The Gemara asks: **Balaam, what did he seek there;** what was his role in that war? He lived in Aram. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He went to collect payment for twenty-four thousand** members **of the Jewish people, whom he felled** with his advice. **Mar Zutra bar Toviya says** that **Rav says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: A camel goes to seek horns** and **the ears that it had are severed from it.** Not only was Balaam unsuccessful in collecting his fee, he also lost his life.

*V'et malchei Midyan hargu al chalaleihem* — they killed the kings of Midian along with their slain. And then: *v'et Bilam ben Be'or hargu bacharev* — they killed Bilam son of Be'or by the sword. What was Bilam doing in Midian? He had left Balak and returned to his own people. The *Gemara* asks *mah ba'ei hatam* — what was he doing there? *Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *shehalach litol schar esrim v'arba'ah elef she'naflu mi'Yisrael* — he had gone to collect payment for his advice, which had caused a plague that killed 24,000 of Israel. He went back to receive his reward — and it was there that he met his death.

### Camel's Horn Parable

*Amar Mar Zutra bar Tuvia amar Rav: hainu d'amri inshi* — that is what people say: *gamla azla lemib'ei karnei* — a camel went to ask for horns, and *udnei d'havu lei gezizi minei* — had the ears it had cut off. Not content with what it had, it reached for more, and in doing so lost what it already possessed. The application is transparent: Bilam was not satisfied with whatever he had received from Balak, went back to collect more, and was killed in the process.

The *Gemara*'s point is reinforced by a striking analogy. A man was paid to bring rain to San Diego. He performed his cloud-seeding procedure, it subsequently rained for many days, and the flooding caused some ten million dollars in damage. A year later he returned to collect his ten thousand dollar fee. The city presented him with a ten million dollar bill for the damages. If you want to take credit for the rain, you take responsibility for the flood as well. Bilam wanted payment for his deadly counsel — and the payment he received was death.

### Prophet to Magician

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת בִּלְעָם בֶּן בְּעוֹר הַקּוֹסֵם״. קוֹסֵם? נָבִיא הוּא! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִלָּה נָבִיא, וּלְבַסּוֹף קוֹסֵם. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא הַיְינוּ דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: מִסְּגָנֵי וְשִׁילְטִי הֲוַאי, אִיַּיזַן לְגַבְרֵי נַגָּרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated: **"And Balaam, son of Beor, the diviner,** did the children of Israel slay with the sword among the rest of their slain" (Joshua 13:22). The Gemara asks: Was he **a diviner? He is a prophet. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Initially** he was **a prophet, but ultimately,** he lost his capacity for prophecy and remained merely **a diviner. Rav Pappa says** that **this** is in accordance with the adage **that people say:** This woman **was** descended **from princes and rulers,** and **was licentious with carpenters.**

The *pasuk* in *Yehoshua* refers to Bilam as *hakosem* — the magician or diviner — rather than *navi*, prophet. This is noted as a deliberate demotion. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: bitechilah navi ulevasof kosem* — he began as a prophet but ended as a mere practitioner of magic. *Rashi* explains: *shenatan einav bemalchut venittela heimenu nevuah* — because he set his eyes on the kingship, God removed the gift of prophecy from him. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: hainu d'amrei inshi* — that is what people say: *misagnei v'shalti havai, izin legabi nagrei* — this woman descended from kings and ministers and wound up serving carpenters. The mighty have fallen.

### Four Deaths of Bilam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָרְגוּ בְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל [בַּחֶרֶב] אֶל חַלְלֵיהֶם״, אָמַר רַב: שֶׁקִּיְּימוּ בּוֹ אַרְבַּע מִיתוֹת – סְקִילָה, וּשְׂרֵיפָה, הֶרֶג, וָחֶנֶק.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the latter part of the verse: "And Balaam, son of Beor, the diviner, **did the children of Israel slay with the sword among the rest of their slain"** (Joshua 13:22), **Rav says:** It means **that they accomplished in him** all **four** means of court-imposed **executions: Stoning, and burning, beheading, and strangulation.**

*Hargu benei Yisrael becherev el chalaleihem* — the *Bnei Yisrael* killed him by sword upon their corpses. The phrase *el chalaleihem* is read as superfluous and carrying additional import. *Amar Rav*: *shekiyemu bo arba mitot sekilah sreifah hereg vechenek* — they inflicted upon Bilam all four forms of capital punishment: stoning, burning, slaying, and strangling. *Rashi* explains the derivation from the word *chalaleihem*, which implies multiple deaths. The practical sequence, as *Rashi* reconstructs it, involved hanging, burning on the stake to which he was suspended, decapitation, and strangulation.

### Bilam's Age at Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִי שְׁמִיעַ לָךְ בִּלְעָם בַּר כַּמָּה הֲוָה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִיכְתָּב לָא כְּתִיב, אֶלָּא מִדִּכְתִיב ״אַנְשֵׁי דָמִים וּמִרְמָה לֹא יֶחֱצוּ יְמֵיהֶם״, בַּר תְּלָתִין וּתְלָת שְׁנִין אוֹ בַּר תְּלָתִין וְאַרְבַּע. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרַתְּ, לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי פִּנְקָסֵיהּ דְּבִלְעָם, וַהֲוָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ: בַּר תְּלָתִין וּתְלָת שְׁנִין בִּלְעָם חֲגִירָא כַּד קָטֵיל יָתֵיהּ פִּנְחָס לִיסְטָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Ḥanina: Have you heard how old Balaam was** when he died? Rabbi Ḥanina **said to him: It is not written** explicitly in the Torah. **But from** the fact **that it is written: "Bloody and deceitful men shall not live half their days"** (Psalms 55:24), this indicates that he was **thirty-three or thirty-four years old,** less than half the standard seventy-year lifespan. The heretic **said to him: You have spoken well, I myself saw the notebook of Balaam and it was written therein: Balaam the lame was thirty-three years old when Pinehas the highwayman killed him.**

A *min* — a sectarian, quite possibly a Jewish Christian — approached *Rabbi Chanina* and asked: *mi shemia lach Bilam bar kamah havai* — have you heard how old Bilam was when he died? *Rabbi Chanina* replied that it is not explicitly written, but from the verse *anshei damim umirmah lo yechetzu yemeihem* — men of blood and deceit will not live to half their days — one can infer he was either thirty-three or thirty-four years old. The sectarian responded: *shappir ka'amrat* — you have spoken well. And then he added: *ledidi chazi li pinkasei deBilam* — I actually happened to see Bilam's ledger or diary, and it was written therein: *bar tlatin utlat shanin Bilam chagira* — thirty-three years old was Bilam the lame one. *Katilei Pinchas lista'ah* — Pinchas the bandit killed him. From the perspective of whoever wrote in Bilam's ledger, *Pinchas* is cast as the wicked one. The note about *Pinchas* leading the military campaign serves, from *Rashi*'s perspective, to explain how *Pinchas* is credited with the killing even without personally wielding the sword.

The likely subtext of this exchange is a veiled reference to Jesus, who died at approximately thirty-three years of age. The *min*'s knowing response — "you have spoken well" — and the elaboration from Bilam's supposed diary suggest that this was precisely the point being probed and implicitly addressed.

### Distancing from Bilam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא לִבְרֵיהּ: בְּכוּלְּהוּ לָא תַּפֵּישׁ לְמִדְרַשׁ, לְבַר מִבִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע, דְּכַמָּה דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ בֵּיהּ דְּרוֹשׁ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar, son of Ravina, said to his son:** With regard to **all of those** enumerated as not having a share in the World-to-Come, **do not extensively interpret** verses **homiletically** in order to denigrate them, **except** with regard to **Balaam the wicked, as any** negative element **that you discover in his** regard, continue to **interpret homiletically concerning him,** as it is appropriate to attribute wickedness to one so wicked.

*Amar Mar brei deRavina levrei*: in all of *Perek Chelek*, when studying the wicked figures who have no portion in the World to Come, one should not invest too much in elaborating their evil — *levar miBilam harasha*, with the sole exception of Bilam. Because Bilam is the one non-Jew on the list, *dechamah demishkachat bei derosh bei* — as much as you can expound about his wickedness, by all means do so.

## Doeg the Edomite

### Doeg's Name Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב ״דּוֹאֵג״, וּכְתִיב ״דּוֹיֵיג״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בַּתְּחִילָּה יוֹשֵׁב הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְדוֹאֵג, שֶׁמָּא יֵצֵא זֶה לְתַרְבּוּת רָעָה. לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּצָא, אָמַר: וַוי שֶׁיָּצָא זֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **It is written** in one verse: **"Doeg** the Edomite" (I Samuel 22:9), **and it is written** in another verse: "And the king said to **Doyeig"** (I Samuel 22:18). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** in explaining the discrepancy: **Initially, the Holy One, Blessed be He, sat and was concerned [*doeg*]** that **perhaps this** person **would emerge to** undertake **an evil path. After he emerged** on that path, God **said: Alas [*vai*], that** person **has emerged to** undertake an evil path.

The *daf* now turns to *Doeg ha'Adomi*. The name *Doeg* appears in two distinct spellings: one with the letters *dalet-vav-alef-gimmel* and another rendering. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: at the outset, when *Doeg* was righteous and on the correct path — but already showing his potential for evil — the Holy One Blessed be He was anxious, *do'eg* — worried — about him. That is the first spelling. After he ultimately actualized his destructive potential, God said *oy* — woe — that this one went forth in the direction he did, either lamenting his birth or lamenting his apostasy. That is the second spelling, with the *vav-yod-yod* rendering *dovay*, woe.

### Scholar Contradictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָן: גִּבּוֹר, רָשָׁע, וְצַדִּיק, חַיִל, וְסוֹפֵר).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **a mnemonic** for the statements of Rabbi Yitzḥak that follow: **Mighty, wicked, and righteous, riches, and counter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״מַה תִּתְהַלֵּל בְּרָעָה הַגִּבּוֹר חֶסֶד אֵל כׇּל הַיּוֹם״? אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְדוֹאֵג: לֹא גִּבּוֹר בַּתּוֹרָה אַתָּה? מַה תִּתְהַלֵּל בְּרָעָה? לֹא חֶסֶד אֵל נָטוּי עָלֶיךָ כׇּל הַיּוֹם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Why boast of your evil mighty one? The mercy of God endures continually"** (Psalms 52:3)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Doeg: Aren't you mighty in Torah? Why do you boast of evil? Isn't God's mercy extended over you continually** when you engage in His Torah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְלָרָשָׁע אָמַר אֱלֹהִים מַה לְּךָ לְסַפֵּר חֻקָּי״? אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְדוֹאֵג הָרָשָׁע: מַה לְּךָ לְסַפֵּר חֻקָּי? כְּשֶׁאַתָּה מַגִּיעַ לְפָרָשַׁת מְרַצְּחִים וּפָרָשַׁת מְסַפְּרֵי לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, מָה אַתָּה דּוֹרֵשׁ בָּהֶם? ״וַתִּשָּׂא בְרִיתִי עֲלֵי פִיךָ״. אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אֵין תּוֹרָתוֹ שֶׁל דּוֹאֵג אֶלָּא מִשָּׂפָה וְלַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "But to the wicked, God says: What have you to do to declare My statutes,** and that you have taken My covenant in your mouth" (Psalms 50:16)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Doeg the wicked: Why do you speak of My statutes** and My Torah? **When you reach** the Torah **portion of murderers and** the Torah **portion of slanderers, how do you teach them?** You have violated both. With regard to the end of that verse: **"And that you have taken My covenant in your mouth"** (Psalms 50:16), **Rabbi Ami says: Doeg's Torah is only** insincere **lip service,** as it is in his mouth but not in his heart. He does not have a profound understanding of the Torah and does not commit himself to the performance of its mitzvot.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: what is meant by the verse *mah tithallel be'ra'ah ha'gibor chesed El kol hayom* — why does the mighty one glorify himself in evil, with God's kindness extended to him all day? The Holy One Blessed be He said to *Doeg*: were you not a great and valorous person in Torah? How did you come to glorify yourself in evil? Was not God's kindness poured out upon you throughout your years of learning and success?

And then: *vela-rasha amar Elohim mah le'cha lesapper chukkai* — to the wicked one God says: what business do you have reciting My laws? The Holy One said to *Doeg the wicked*: when you reach the sections dealing with murderers — say you are teaching *Makkot* or *Sanhedrin* and you are discussing the laws of those who kill — how do you have the audacity to give a *shiur* on that after what you have done? *U-parshat mesapprei lashon hara* — and when you come to the sections dealing with *lashon hara* — how can you teach My Torah? You are a living contradiction to everything the Torah represents.

This is a point the *Gemara* has made before: Torah scholarship does not automatically produce a righteous person. One can be enormously knowledgeable, capable of delivering brilliant *shiurim*, and be a thoroughly corrupt individual. The verse *ki siftei kohen yishmeru da'at ve-Torah yevakshu mi-pihu ki malakh Hashem Tzeva'ot hu* — "for the lips of the priest shall preserve knowledge, and Torah shall be sought from his mouth, for he is an angel of God" — expresses the demand that a Torah teacher be a living embodiment of what Torah is about, not merely its intellectual conduit.

### Learning Without Heart

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וְיִרְאוּ צַדִּיקִים וְיִירָאוּ וְעָלָיו יִשְׂחָקוּ״? בַּתְּחִילָּה יִירָאוּ, וּלְבַסּוֹף יִשְׂחָקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And the righteous shall see, and fear, and shall laugh at him"** (Psalms 52:8)? **Initially, they will fear** Doeg due to his success, **and ultimately they will laugh** when they witness his downfall.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״חַיִל בָּלַע וַיְקִאֶנּוּ מִבִּטְנוֹ יוֹרִישֶׁנּוּ אֵל״? אָמַר דָּוִד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, יָמוּת דּוֹאֵג! אָמַר לוֹ: ״חַיִל בָּלַע וַיְקִיאֶנּוּ״. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: ״מִבִּטְנוֹ יוֹרִישֶׁנּוּ אֵל״!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "He has swallowed riches and he shall vomit them again; God shall cast them out of his belly"** (Job 20:15)? **David said before the Holy One, Blessed be He: Master of the Universe, Doeg shall die.** God **said to him: "He has swallowed riches and he shall vomit them again."** He is filled with Torah and wisdom; wait until he forgets what he has learned. David **said before Him: "God shall cast them out of his belly."** God can remove his Torah knowledge from him before he will forget it on his own.

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *ein Torato shel Doeg ela mi-sefatav u-lachutz* — Doeg's Torah never penetrated him. It remained only from the lips outward. It never transformed who he was. Whether this was true from the outset or whether he initially had genuine piety that was subsequently lost, the point stands: the possibility exists of someone who gives magnificent *shiurim* and is a moral anathema to everything those *shiurim* are about.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: what is meant by the verse *veyiru tzaddikim ve-yira'u ve-alav yischaku* — the righteous saw and were afraid, and then they laughed at him? Initially, *yira'u* — they feared — that people would follow *Doeg*'s bad example, because here was a *talmid chacham* who might lead others astray. But ultimately, *yischaku* — they laughed — because when *Doeg* met his downfall, it was clear that no one would be drawing lessons from his path.

### David's Petition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״גַּם אֵל יִתׇּצְךָ לָנֶצַח״? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְדָוִד: נֵיתֵי דּוֹאֵג לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: ״גַּם אֵל יִתׇּצְךָ לָנֶצַח״. מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״יַחְתְּךָ וְיִסָּחֲךָ מֵאֹהֶל וְשֵׁרֶשְׁךָ מֵאֶרֶץ חַיִּים סֶלָה״? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: לֵימְרוּ שְׁמַעְתָּא בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ. אָמַר לְפָנָיו: ״יַחְתְּךָ וְיִסָּחֲךָ מֵאֹהֶל״. לֶיהֱוֵי לֵיהּ בְּנִין רַבָּנַן: ״וְשֵׁרֶשְׁךָ מֵאֶרֶץ חַיִּים סֶלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "God shall likewise destroy you forever"** (Psalms 52:7)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said to David: Let Doeg enter the World-to-Come.** David **said before Him: "God shall likewise destroy you forever,"** i.e., let Doeg not have eternal life. **What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "He shall pluck you away, and pluck you from your tent, and root you out from the land of the living. Selah"** (Psalms 52:7)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said** to David: At least **let them state a *halakha* in the study hall in his name.** David **said before Him: "He shall pluck you away, and pluck you from your tent,"** i.e., let him be completely removed from the tents of Torah. God said to him: **Let him have sons who are Sages.** David said: **"And root you out from the land of the living. Selah,"** i.e., let Doeg be entirely uprooted.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: what is meant by the verse *gam El yitatzcha lanetzach* — even God will crush you forever? The Holy One Blessed be He wanted to give *Doeg* a portion in the World to Come — consistent with the position mentioned earlier that even these figures retain some access to *olam haba*. But David objected. There is tension here between two parties with legitimate claims: God, who would extend mercy even to *Doeg*, and David, who is the wronged party and refuses to concede. Neither is simply right or wrong; the passage is designed to embody that complexity.

God then said: fine, if not *olam haba*, then at least let Torah be said over in his name in the *Beit Midrash* — *limru shma'ta bei midrasha mishmeih*. David said: *yechat-cha ve-yisach-cha me-ohel* — let him be cut off even from the tent of Torah. Should he at least have descendants who are *talmidei chachamim*? *Ve-shoresh-cha me-eretz chayim sela* — let his root be cut off from the land of the living. No descendants, no Torah credit, nothing. This is a striking exchange, given that elsewhere the *Gemara* notes that even the descendants of those who sought to destroy Israel eventually converted and produced Torah scholars. The question of what to do with the Torah legacy of a morally corrupt genius remains genuinely unresolved.

### Lost Greatness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״אַיֵּה סֹפֵר אַיֵּה שֹׁקֵל אַיֵּה סֹפֵר אֶת הַמִּגְדָּלִים״? ״אַיֵּה סֹפֵר״ – כׇּל אוֹתִיּוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, ״אַיֵּה שֹׁקֵל״ – שֶׁשּׁוֹקֵל כָּל קַלִּים וַחֲמוּרִים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה, ״אַיֵּה סֹפֵר אֶת הַמִּגְדָּלִים״ – שֶׁהָיָה סוֹפֵר שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת הֲלָכוֹת פְּסוּקוֹת בְּמִגְדָּל הַפּוֹרֵחַ בָּאֲוִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Where is he that counts; where is he that weighs; where is he that counts the towers [*migdalim*]"** (Isaiah 33:18)? **Where is he that counts all the letters of the Torah? Where is he that weighs, who considers all the** elements of ***a fortiori* inferences in the Torah? Where is he who counts the towers?** This is Doeg, **who would count three hundred halakhic conclusions with regard to** the purity of **a cupboard [*migdal*] that floats in the air [*avir*].**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי: אַרְבַּע מְאָה בַּעְיֵי בְּעוֹ דּוֹאֵג וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל בְּמִגְדָּל הַפּוֹרֵחַ בָּאֲוִיר, (וְלָא אִיפְּשַׁט לְהוּ חַד). אָמַר רָבָא: רְבוּתָא לְמִבְעֵי בַּעְיֵי? בִּשְׁנֵי דְּרַב יְהוּדָה כּוּלֵּי תַּנּוֹיֵי בִּנְזִיקִין, וַאֲנַן קָא מַתְנֵינַן טוּבָא בְּעוּקְצִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Doeg and Ahithophel raised four hundred dilemmas with regard to** the purity of **a cupboard that floats in the air, and they did not resolve** even **one,** an indication of their great knowledge. **Rava says:** Is it **greatness to raise dilemmas?** That is not a barometer of greatness, as **in the years of Rav Yehuda all of their** Torah **study** was confined to the order of ***Nezikin*, and we study much** more than that, and are expert even **in** tractate ***Okatzin*,** the final tractate in the difficult order of *Teharot*.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: what is meant by the verse *ayeh sofer ayeh shokel ayeh sofer et ha-migdalim* — where is the counter, where is the weigher, where is the one who counts the towers? *Ayeh sofer* — where is the one *she-sofer kol otiyot she-ba-Torah* — who could count every letter in the Torah? *Doeg* possessed that capacity. How did someone with such mastery go so wrong? Or: how did such mastery come to be forgotten? *Ayeh shokel* — where is the one who could weigh all the *kal va-chomerim* in the Torah? *Ayeh sofer et ha-migdalim* — where is the one *she-hayah sofer shalosh me'ot halachot psukot be-migdal ha-poreiach ba-avir* — who could enumerate 300 decided laws regarding a tower floating in the air? *Rashi* explains this as a reference to a chest (*migdal*) positioned half inside and half outside a house and exposed to the air (*avir*), raising complex questions about whether *tumah* passes inward or outward — some of the most intricate territory in the tractate of *Ohalos*. That *Doeg* could navigate 300 decided *halachot* in that domain marks him as a scholar of the first order.

### Questions vs Answers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אַרְבַּע מְאָה בַּעְיֵי בְּעוּ דּוֹאֵג וְ אֲחִיתוֹפֶל בְּמִגְדָּל הַפּוֹרֵחַ בָּאֲוִיר, וְלָא אִיפְּשַׁט לְהוּ חַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami says: Doeg** and **Ahithophel asked four hundred questions about a tower floating in the air. Yet they could not resolve even one of them.**

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *arba me'ah ba'ei ba'u Doeg ve-Achitofel be-migdal ha-poreiach ba-avir* — *Doeg* and *Achitophel* together could ask 400 questions about a tower floating in the air. *Ve-lo ifshat lehu chad* — yet they could not resolve even one of them. This introduces a subtle qualification: before, *Doeg* was credited with 300 *halachot psukot* — decided law, actual conclusions. Here, the number rises to 400 but the result is only unresolved questions. Someone can be brilliant at generating questions without being able to reach conclusions.

### Heart Over Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכִי הֲוָה מָטֵי רַב יְהוּדָה ״אִשָּׁה שֶׁכּוֹבֶשֶׁת יָרָק בִּקְדֵירָה״, וְאָמְרוּ לַהּ ״זֵיתִים שֶׁכְּבָשָׁן בְּטַרְפֵיהֶן טְהוֹרִים״, אָמַר: הֲוָיוֹת דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל קָא חָזֵינָא הָכָא, וַאֲנַן קָא מַתְנֵינַן בְּעוּקְצִין תְּלָת סְרֵי מְתִיבָתָא! וְרַב יְהוּדָה שָׁלֵיף מְסָאנֵי וְאָתֵי מִטְרָא, וַאֲנַן צָוְחִינַן וְלֵיכָּא דְּמַשְׁגַּח בַּן. אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לִיבָּא בָּעֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַה׳ יִרְאֶה לַלֵּבָב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover, when Rav Yehuda would encounter** the mishna in tractate *Okatzin* that discusses the extent to which the stems of various fruits and vegetables are considered an integral part of the produce in terms of contracting ritual impurity, where the mishna discusses the *halakha* concerning **a woman who pickles a vegetable in a pot, and some say** when he would reach the mishna (*Okatzin* 2:1): **Olives pickled with their leaves are pure,** because after pickling, it is no longer possible to lift the fruit by its leaves, so they are no longer considered part of the fruit; he would find it difficult to understand. **He would say:** Those are **the discussions between Rav and Shmuel that we see here. And we,** by contrast, **teach** tractate ***Okatzin* in thirteen academies. But** nevertheless, **when Rav Yehuda would remove one of his shoes the rain would** immediately **fall,** whereas **we cry out and no one notices us. Rather, the Holy One, Blessed be He, seeks the heart,** and the barometer of greatness is devotion of the heart and not the amount of Torah that one studies, **as it is written: "But the Lord looks on the heart"** (I Samuel 16:7).

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *ein Torato shel Doeg ela mi-sefatav u-lachutz* — Doeg's Torah never penetrated him. It remained only from the lips outward. It never transformed who he was. Whether this was true from the outset or whether he initially had genuine piety that was subsequently lost, the point stands: the possibility exists of someone who gives magnificent *shiurim* and is a moral anathema to everything those *shiurim* are about.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: what is meant by the verse *veyiru tzaddikim ve-yira'u ve-alav yischaku* — the righteous saw and were afraid, and then they laughed at him? Initially, *yira'u* — they feared — that people would follow *Doeg*'s bad example, because here was a *talmid chacham* who might lead others astray. But ultimately, *yischaku* — they laughed — because when *Doeg* met his downfall, it was clear that no one would be drawing lessons from his path.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: what is meant by the verse *chayil bala vayaki'ennu, mibitno yorishenu El* — he swallowed great wealth and disgorged it, God drove it from his innards? *Amar David lifnei Hakadosh Baruch Hu*: Master of the Universe, let *Doeg* die. *Amar leih*: *chayil bala vayaki'ennu* — he has swallowed great Torah. I will not bring death upon him until he has disgorged it, until he has forgotten his Torah. David then said: *mibitno yorishenu El* — then You, God, drive it from him. If that is what it takes, cause him to forget his Torah, so that justice can be done.

*Amar Rabbi Yitzchak*: what is meant by *gam El yitatzcha lanetzach* — even God will crush you forever? The Holy One Blessed be He wanted to give *Doeg* a portion in the World to Come — consistent with the position mentioned earlier that even these figures retain some access to *olam haba*. But David objected. There is tension here between two parties with legitimate claims: God, who would extend mercy even to *Doeg*, and David, who is the wronged party and refuses to concede. Neither is simply right or wrong; the passage is designed to embody that complexity.

God then said: fine, if not *olam haba*, then at least let Torah be said over in his name in the *Beit Midrash* — *limru shma'ta bei midrasha mishmeih*. David said: *yechat-cha ve-yisach-cha me-ohel* — let him be cut off even from the tent of Torah. Should he at least have descendants who are *talmidei chachamim*? *Ve-shoresh-cha me-eretz chayim sela* — let his root be cut off from the land of the living. No descendants, no Torah credit, nothing. The question of what to do with the Torah legacy of a morally corrupt genius remains genuinely unresolved.

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *rabuta le-mib'ei ba'ei* — is it such a great feat to ask questions? Anyone can ask questions. This cuts deeper: the obsession with *Doeg* and *Achitophel*'s scholarly accomplishments misses the point. Torah scholarship is not equivalent to being a good person or a true servant of God.

*Bishnei de-Rav Yehudah kuli tanuyei bi-Nezikin* — in the years of *Rav Yehudah*, all they studied was *Nezikin*. They did not study *Nashim*, they did not study *Moed*, and certainly not *Taharos*. *Va'anan ka matninya tuva b'Uktzin* — and we know even *Uktzin*, the most obscure tractate in *Taharos*, with thirteen major *yeshivot* of discussion. By sheer breadth and depth of knowledge, our generation is far ahead of *Rav Yehudah*. And yet: *Rav Yehudah shalyif msanei v'ata mitra* — all *Rav Yehudah* had to do was remove his shoe as a gesture of preparatory prayer, and rain would come. *Va'anan tzavchinan ve-leika d'mashgiach ban* — we pray and cry out the whole day and no one pays attention to us.

*Ela Hakadosh Baruch Hu liba ba'ei* — God wants the heart. *D'chtiv v'Hashem yireh lalevav* — as it is written, "God sees to the heart." The phrase carries a play on words: *liba ba'ei*, wanting the heart, echoes the word *libay ba'ei*. God does not want brilliant Talmudic questions. He wants religious sincerity. The message connects directly back to everything said about *Doeg*: Torah that remains only from the lips outward, Torah that is never internalized, Torah that does not transform — that is Torah that produces a *Doeg*, not a servant of God. And as the verse states: *sod Hashem lireiaiv* — God discloses Himself to those who fear Him. The one whose Torah is purely intellectual, disconnected from genuine *avodas Hashem*, will not arrive at the correct *halachic* conclusion. The fear of God is not a supplement to Torah learning; it is a prerequisite for the Torah yielding its deepest truths.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: דּוֹאֵג וַאֲחִיתוֹפֶל לָא [הֲווֹ] סָבְרִי שְׁמַעְתָּא. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ מָר זוּטְרָא: מַאן דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ ״אַיֵּה סֹפֵר אַיֵּה שֹׁקֵל אַיֵּה סֹפֵר אֶת הַמִּגְדָּלִים״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ לָא הֲווֹ סָבְרִי שְׁמַעְתָּא?! אֶלָּא דְּלָא הֲוָה סָלְקָא לְהוּ שְׁמַעְתָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְהִלְכְתָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״סוֹד ה׳ לִירֵאָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiyya says: Doeg and Ahithophel did not comprehend halakhic discussions. Mar Zutra objects to this** statement: These are people **with regard to whom it is written: "Where is he that counts; where is he that weighs; where is he that counts the towers"** (Isaiah 33:18), **and you say** that **they did not comprehend halakhic discussions? Rather,** Doeg and Ahithophel **would not conclude halakhic discussions in accordance with halakhic** rulings, **as it is written: "The secret of the Lord is with those who fear Him"** (Psalms 25:14). Since they did not fear God, they did not arrive at halakhic conclusions despite their keen intellect.

*Amar Rav Mesharshiya*: *Doeg ve-Achitofel lo havu savri shma'ata* — they did not truly understand what they were learning. *Masiv Mar Zutra*: but it is written about such a person *ayeh sofer ayeh shokel ayeh sofer et ha-migdalim* — how can you say they didn't know how to learn? *Ela d'lo havu salki shma'ata aliba d'hilchasa* — rather, they could engage with the material but they could not arrive at the *halacha*. And why? *D'chtiv sod Hashem lireiaiv* — because God speaks to those who fear Him. Their learning, being a purely intellectual game and not genuine *avodas Hashem*, could not penetrate to the correct *halachic* conclusion.

### Three Destructive Angels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: לֹא מֵת דּוֹאֵג עַד שֶׁשָּׁכַח תַּלְמוּדוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוּא יָמוּת בְּאֵין מוּסָר וּבְרֹב אִוַּלְתּוֹ יִשְׁגֶּה״. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: נִצְטָרַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִצְמַתָּה כׇּל זוֹנֶה מִמֶּךָּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami says: Doeg died only when he forgot what he learned, as it is stated: "He shall die for want of instruction, and in his folly he shall go astray"** (Proverbs 5:23). **Rav Ashi says: He was afflicted with leprosy** before his death, **as it is stated: "Those that go far from You shall perish; You destroy [*hitzmatta*] all those who go astray from You"** (Psalms 73:27).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתִיב הָתָם: ״לַצְּמִתֻת״, וּמְתַרְגְּמִינַן: ״לַחֲלוּטִין״. וּתְנַן: אֵין בֵּין מוּסְגָּר וּמוּחְלָט אֶלָּא פְּרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה.
>
> **Translation:** Where is the allusion to leprosy in this verse? **It is written there:** "And the land shall not be sold **in perpetuity [*litzmitut*]"** (Leviticus 25:23), **and we translate** it into Aramaic as: ***Laḥalutin*. And we learned** in a mishna with regard to lepers (*Megilla* 8b): **The difference between a quarantined** leper, i.e., one examined by a priest who found his symptoms to be inconclusive, and who must therefore remain in isolation for a period of up to two weeks to see if conclusive symptoms develop; **and a confirmed [*muḥlat*]** leper, i.e., one whose symptoms were conclusive and the priest declared him a confirmed leper, **is only** with regard to **letting** the hair on one's head **grow and rending** one's garments. The derivation is based on the etymological similarity between *hitzmatta* and *litzmitut*; the translation of *litzmitut* as *laḥalutin*, and the etymological similarity between *laḥalutin* and *muḥlat*.

*Amar Rabbi Ami*: *lo mes Doeg ad she'shachach talmudo* — *Doeg* did not die until he had forgotten his Torah. *Shene'emar hu yamut b'ein musar u've-rov ivalto yishgeh* — he will die without instruction, and in his great foolishness he will err.

*Rav Ashi amar*: he became a *metzora*, a leper. The textual derivation is complex: the word *hatzamta* — you have cut off — is connected via the word *l'tzmisus* in the Torah, which the Targum renders *l'chalutin*, meaning permanent or full-status. And the *Mishnah* teaches: *ein bein musgar u'muchlat ella priah u'frima* — the only difference between a quarantined leper and a full *metzora* is the rending of garments and the growing of hair. The chain of verbal connections is intricate, working toward establishing that *Doeg* attained the status of a full *metzora*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה, רָאוּ, וְחָצוּ, וְקָרְאוּ)
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites **a mnemonic** for the *halakhot* that follow: **Three, saw, and half, and called him.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה מַלְאֲכֵי חַבָּלָה נִזְדַּמְּנוּ לוֹ לְדוֹאֵג, אֶחָד שֶׁשִּׁכַּח תַּלְמוּדוֹ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׂרַף נִשְׁמָתוֹ, וְאֶחָד שֶׁפִּיזֵּר עֲפָרוֹ בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבְבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Three angels of destruction encountered Doeg: One who caused him to forget his Torah** knowledge, **one who burned his soul, and one who dispersed the ashes of** his soul **in synagogues and in study halls** to be trampled beneath the feet of the righteous.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *shlosha malachei chavala nizdamnu lo l'Doeg* — three angels of destruction met *Doeg*. *Echad she'shikach talmudo* — one caused him to forget his Torah, consistent with *David*'s petition that *Doeg*'s learning be removed before his death. *V'echad she'saraf nishmato* — one burned his soul. *V'echad she'pizer afro b'vattei khnesiyos u'vattei midrashos* — and one scattered his ashes in synagogues and study halls. In this way, *Doeg* achieves a kind of presence in the *Beit Midrash* — through his ashes — even as he has been denied any living portion in its life and legacy.
