# Hullin 137 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trefah Exemption

### Trefah Not Listed

The *mishnah* enumerated several ways in which *zroa u'lchayayim u'va'keiva* — the priestly gifts of the arm, cheeks, and stomach — has a broader application than *reshit ha'gez*, the obligation to give the first shearings. The most obvious distinction is that *reshit ha'gez* applies only to sheep and only when one owns at least five of them. The *Gemara*, however, observes that additional distinctions could have been listed, and asks why the *mishnah* omitted the fact that *reshit ha'gez* applies to a *trefah* animal, whereas the priestly gifts do not.

The *Gemara* takes it for granted that the gifts do not apply to a *trefah*. Rashi explains this on the basis of the verse *v'nasan la'Kohen* — "and he shall give to the Kohen" — which implies the gift must be suitable for the *Kohen* to eat. Since a *trefah* cannot be eaten by the *Kohen*, the gifts do not apply. Given this, why does the *mishnah* not list the *trefah* exemption as a point of difference, noting that *reshit ha'gez* applies to a *trefah* while *matanos* do not?

Ravina answers: the *mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon. A *baraita* states explicitly that Rabbi Shimon exempts *trefos* from *reshit ha'gez* as well. From his perspective, there is no distinction to list: both *matanos* and *reshit ha'gez* are equally exempt from the *trefah*, so no contrast exists.

### Rabbi Shimon's Derivation

The *Gemara* asks: what is Rabbi Shimon's reasoning? The answer is that he employs a *gezeirah shavah*: he links the word *nesina* (giving) in the context of *reshit ha'gez* to the word *nesina* in the context of *ma'aser behemah*. Just as *ma'aser* does not apply to a *trefah*, so too *reshit ha'gez* does not apply to a *trefah*.

### Terumah Linkage Rejected

The *Gemara* immediately raises a challenge: if one is willing to derive a law through the shared word *nesina*, why not instead link *reshit ha'gez* to *terumah*? The consequence of such a linkage would be that just as *terumah* applies only in the Land of Israel and not outside it, so too *reshit ha'gez* would apply only in the Land of Israel — a position identified elsewhere with Rabbi Elazar, but not the position of the *mishnah*, which explicitly states that *reshit ha'gez* applies both in the Land of Israel and outside it.

The *Gemara* responds with a simple *ela*: that linkage is rejected precisely because of our *mishnah's* ruling that *reshit ha'gez* applies in *chutz la'aretz*. We therefore cannot learn from *terumah*. The reasoning of Rabbi Shimon must instead be grounded in the link to *ma'aser*.

The *Gemara* then explains the actual mechanism of Rabbi Shimon's derivation: he links *reshit ha'gez* to *ma'aser behemah* through the shared word *tzon* (flock). The verse establishing *ma'aser behemah* speaks of the flock, and *reshit ha'gez* likewise speaks of the flock. Just as *ma'aser* does not apply to a *trefah*, so too *reshit ha'gez* does not apply to a *trefah*.

The *Gemara* then asks: and how do we know that *ma'aser* itself does not apply to a *trefah*? After all, if the tenth animal that walks out of the pen has a *mum* (blemish), it does become sanctified as *ma'aser* and is eaten with its blemish rather than redeemed. Perhaps a *trefah* should be treated similarly? The *Gemara* answers from the verse *kol asher ya'avor tachas ha'shavet* — "whatever passes under the rod." Since a *trefah* cannot walk under the rod (some *trefos* are missing a limb), the verse excludes it. Tosafot notes a difficulty here: some animals that are not *trefos* also cannot walk under the rod, such as one whose hoof has been torn off. The *Gemara's* *drasha* thus focuses on the quality of being unable to walk as the operative exclusion, even though the category of *trefah* is broader than just those that literally cannot walk. That is the mechanics of the *drasha*, even if it is somewhat inexact.

### Ma'aser vs. Bechor

Having established the *tzon*-*tzon* link to *ma'aser*, the *Gemara* challenges: why not instead derive *reshit ha'gez* from *bechor* through the same shared word *tzon*? The verse regarding *bechor* also uses the word *tzon*. And since *bechor* does apply to a *trefah* — the firstborn is automatically sanctified by virtue of its birth, without any act on the owner's part — learning from *bechor* would lead to the conclusion that *reshit ha'gez* also applies to a *trefah*, reversing Rabbi Shimon's position.

### Reshit HaGez-Ma'aser Lin…

The *Gemara* responds that it is more logical to learn *reshit ha'gez* from *ma'aser* rather than from *bechor*, and proceeds to enumerate the points of connection between *reshit ha'gez* and *ma'aser*. First, *zecharim*: both *reshit ha'gez* and *ma'aser* are not limited to male animals, whereas *bechor* applies only to males. Second, *tmeim*: neither applies to non-kosher species, while *bechor* applies even to a donkey. Third, *bimrubim*: both require a minimum number of animals — *reshit ha'gez* requires five and *ma'aser* requires ten — whereas *bechor* applies even to a single animal. Fourth, *merachem*: neither *reshit ha'gez* nor *ma'aser* is sanctified from the moment of birth, unlike *bechor*, which is sanctified from the womb. Fifth, *adam*: neither applies to human beings, while *bechor* does. Sixth, *pashut*: crucially, both apply only to non-firstborns. An animal that is already a *bechor* is not subject to *ma'aser* and not subject to *reshit ha'gez*, since both apply only to ordinary, non-sanctified animals — the precise inverse of *bechor*. Seventh, *lifnei ha'dibur*: neither *reshit ha'gez* nor *ma'aser* existed as obligations before the Torah was given at Sinai, whereas *bechor* had a form of sacred status even in Egypt.

### Reshit HaGez-Bechor Cont…

The *Gemara* counters: *adrabah* — quite the reverse! Perhaps *reshit ha'gez* should be learned from *bechor*, since there are also numerous points of connection between them. The *Gemara* lists these in turn. *Yatom*: both *reshit ha'gez* and *bechor* apply to an orphan animal — one born after its mother died during delivery — whereas *ma'aser behemah* does not apply to such an animal, because *ma'aser* is bound by the rule that an animal must have been with its mother for seven days, a condition that cannot be fulfilled if the mother died at birth. *Shelakchu*: both apply to purchased animals. *Ma'aser behemah* applies only to animals born within one's own flock; if one purchases young sheep, one does not give *ma'aser*. But if one purchases sheep and shears them, one gives *reshit ha'gez*, and if one purchases a sheep and it gives birth to a firstborn, one gives *bechor*. *Beshutfut*: both apply in cases of partnership with another Jew, while *ma'aser behemah* applies only when the animals are solely owned. *Netano*: similarly, both apply to an animal received as a gift, which again is not born into one's own flock and thus not subject to *ma'aser*. *Bifnei Kohen* (or in the reading of Tosafot, *lifnei*): in the case of *ma'aser*, if one miscounts and designates the ninth or the eleventh animal as the tenth, those animals also acquire sanctity — *ma'aser* pulls in adjacent animals — whereas *bechor* and *reshit ha'gez* apply only to the specific animal itself, with no such spillover. *Kohen*: both *reshit ha'gez* and *bechor* are given to the *Kohen*, whereas *ma'aser behemah* is brought as a *korban* and eaten by the owner, not given to a *Kohen*. *Bikdushah*: neither *bechor* nor *reshit ha'gez* requires a formal act of sanctification, whereas *ma'aser* requires the act of designating and counting the tenth. *Mechira*: both *bechor* and *reshit ha'gez* may be sold, whereas the Torah states explicitly *lo yimacher* — *ma'aser behemah* shall not be sold.

### Pashut as Key Factor

Having presented a formidable list of connections to *bechor*, the *Gemara* acknowledges: *v'hani nifishei* — these points are even more numerous. Yet the conclusion remains that one should derive *reshit ha'gez* from *ma'aser* rather than from *bechor*. The decisive reasoning is: *pashut mipashut adif lei* — the connection of *pashut* (non-firstborn) is the most fundamental. The entire legal identity of *bechor* is that it is the firstborn; the entire legal identity of both *ma'aser* and *reshit ha'gez* is that they apply specifically to non-firstborns, to ordinary animals. *Bechor* is constitutively defined by being *bechor*; *ma'aser* and *reshit ha'gez* are constitutively defined by being everything that is not *bechor*. That structural correspondence outweighs the larger number of incidental shared features with *bechor*. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon correctly derives from *ma'aser* that *reshit ha'gez* does not apply to a *trefah*.

## Sheep Only

### Shearing Gezeirah

The *mishnah* teaches that *reshit ha'gez* applies only to *rechelim* — sheep — unlike the priestly gifts of *zroa u'lchayayim u'va'keiva*, which apply to both cattle and sheep. The *Gemara* at the top of 137a asks: from where is this derived?

Rav Chisda answers: we make a *gezeirah shavah* using the word *gez* (shearing). The Torah states *reishit gez tzonecha titen lo* — "the first shearing of your flock you shall give to him." And in the book of Iyov we find the verse *umigez kevasai yitchamam* — "and from the shearing of my sheep he warmed himself," where the same word *gez* appears specifically in the context of *kevasim*, sheep, not goats. The question naturally arises: why would one have thought to include goats in the first place, given that the verse mentions *tzon* (flock)? Tosafot addresses this by citing Bereishit 27, where Rivka tells Yaakov to go to the *tzon* and take *shenei g'deiyei izim* — two goats. There we see that goats can be included in the word *tzon*. Hence the need to exclude them through a comparison to the Iyov verse, which uses *gez* specifically by *kevasim*. Tosafot notes this is not a formal *gezeirah shavah* in the classical sense, but rather a textual inference that when the Torah speaks of shearing one's flock, it means shearing sheep, not goats.

### Goat Exclusion

The *Gemara* raises a further challenge: why not link the word *gez* (shearing) instead to the *bechor* context, where the Torah also uses the language of shearing flocks — *v'lo tagoz bechor tzonecha*, do not shear the firstborn of your flock. And the *baraita* there expands this prohibition to cattle as well, implying that shearing can be applied even to cattle. If shearing is broad enough to encompass cattle, perhaps the obligation of *reshit ha'gez* should likewise include goats and even cattle.

The *Gemara* responds with a verse: *titen lo* — "you shall give to him" — implies something suitable for the *Kohen* to wear personally, not merely for utilitarian purposes. This would exclude cattle hair, which is not used for clothing. But what of goat hair? Goat hair is used for certain textile purposes. The *Gemara* answers: *ba'inan geizah v'leika* — the Torah requires an act of *geizah*, actual shearing, and when one removes the hair of goats, one does not shear; rather, one plucks or removes it through a different process. Since the method of hair removal from goats differs from shearing, it does not qualify.

### Rabbi Yosi & Verbs

The *Gemara* then identifies the interpretive principle at stake and asks: who holds this position — that the word *geizah* means specifically an act of shearing and nothing analogous? This is the approach of Rabbi Yosi, who consistently interprets verbs in their narrow, precise sense and does not extend them abstractly to any act that achieves the same result.

However, the *Gemara* immediately complicates this: even according to Rabbi Yosi, who reads verbs narrowly, his principle is *modeh Rabbi Yosi b'midi d'orchei* — Rabbi Yosi concedes where the act in question is the normal, customary way of doing that thing for that object. Rabbi Yosi only excludes unusual or analogous methods when applied to the same object. But removing goat hair by plucking or by whatever method is standard for goats is the *normal* way of removing goat hair. It is not an unusual technique applied to goats — it is precisely the usual one. Therefore, Rabbi Yosi's narrow reading of *geizah* would not by itself suffice to exclude goats.

### Priestly Garment Test

The *Gemara* therefore invokes a different principle, attributed to the school of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi: the verse connecting *reshit ha'gez* to the *Kohen* uses the language *la'amod l'shareit* — to stand and serve — evoking the priestly service. This yields the requirement that the gift must be *davar harauy l'shirut* — something from which a garment suitable for the priestly service could potentially be made, i.e., *bigdei kehunah*. Wool meets this criterion, since wool was used in the *bigdei kehunah*. Goat hair was never used in the *bigdei kehunah* and therefore does not qualify. This principle — that the gift must be material suitable for priestly garments — will recur in the subsequent discussion to define both the type and the quantity of wool required.

### Hard Wool Exemption

With the *gezeirah shavah* to the Iyov verse no longer needed to exclude goats (since the *davar harauy l'shirut* principle accomplishes that independently), the *Gemara* asks: what purpose does the Iyov verse serve? It answers: *l'chadtani d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* — it comes to teach what was stated in the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: *k'vasim shetzamran kasheh peturin me'reishit ha'gez* — sheep whose wool is stiff and hard are exempt from *reshit ha'gez*. The basis is the very verse from Iyov: *umigez kevasai yitchamam* — "from the shearing of my sheep he warmed himself." The wool must be of the kind capable of producing warmth when fashioned into clothing. Wool that is too stiff and rigid to wrap around the body and provide genuine warmth does not qualify.

Rashi and the Rambam read this somewhat differently. Rashi appears to suggest a relative standard — that such wool is distinguished from ordinary sheep's wool — while the Rambam states the criterion in objective terms: wool that is categorically incapable of providing warmth as clothing is exempt.

This is a striking application. The thematic orientation of *reshit ha'gez* toward clothing and the *Kohen*'s personal use — an idea the Rambam himself introduces mid-*halacha* when he notes that the Torah provided the *Kohen* with grain through *terumah*, meat through *korbanot* and *matanos*, and clothing through *reshit ha'gez* — turns out to have concrete halachic consequences. It excludes goat hair, restricts the obligation to wool, and now further restricts it to wool that is genuinely fit for garment-making.

### Shearing vs. Bathing

The *Gemara* now addresses two apparently conflicting *baraitot*. The first *baraita* states: if one shears goats — which we have established is exempt — and if one *shotef* (rinses, passes through water) the sheep, one is exempt. The case of washing the sheep refers to the practice of driving flocks through a river or stream, which serves as a bath; in the course of this process, some wool may be torn off or separated from the sheep's body. The question is whether wool that detaches during bathing is subject to *reshit ha'gez*. The first *baraita* says no, this is exempt.

A second *baraita*, however, states: while shearing goats is exempt, *v'hashoteif et harechelim chayav* — one who bathes the sheep is *obligated*. The two *baraitot* thus appear to contradict each other directly regarding the wool that comes off sheep during bathing.

### Rabanan-Rabbi Yosi Debate

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. One *baraita* represents the position of the *Rabanan* and the other represents the position of Rabbi Yosi. The dispute is precisely about how narrowly one reads the verb *gez*. According to Rabbi Yosi, *reshit ha'gez* requires a formal act of shearing; wool that separates during bathing is not the product of *geizah* and is therefore exempt. According to the *Rabanan*, who read the verb more broadly, any process that removes wool from sheep — including bathing, if wool detaches as a result — creates an obligation.

The *Gemara* at this point cites a *baraita* to illustrate Rabbi Yosi's broader hermeneutic: the Torah says *leket ketzircha* — the gleanings of your harvesting. Rabbi Yosi says *ein leket ela haba machamat katzir* — *leket* applies only to what falls as a direct result of using a scythe in the normal harvest process, not to grain that falls when one merely plucks stalks by hand. The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yosi here seems to say the same thing as the *tana kama*, and concludes that the *baraita* reads as Rabbi Yosi elaborating on the *tana kama*'s position: *kuli Rabbi Yosi* — it is all Rabbi Yosi.

Yet Rabbi Yosi is not entirely literalistic. A subsequent *baraita* shows that he extends *leket* to uprooting onions and plucking tomatoes, deriving this from the repetition of harvest-language in the relevant verses. His principle is *modeh Rabbi Yosi b'midi d'orchei*: he includes analogous acts that are the normal, standard method for that particular crop or object, even if they differ from scything wheat. He excludes only acts that are unusual or indirect — such as wool dislodging during bathing, or grain falling when one plucks stalks by hand. The crucial distinction is between what is genuinely *orchei* (customary practice) for a given subject and what is not.

Rav Acha the son of Rava makes this explicit to Rav Ashi, and a further proof is adduced from the *mishnah* concerning *pe'ah* and *malbinot betzalim shebein hayerek* — fields of onions interspersed among non-obligated vegetables. Rabbi Yosi there rules *pe'ah mikol achat v'achat* (one gives *pe'ah* from each separate row of onions), which presupposes that uprooting onions qualifies for *pe'ah* even though the verse uses the word *katzircha*. Since uprooting is the standard way of harvesting onions, Rabbi Yosi includes it. This confirms that his standard is functional normalcy, not strict literal identity with the verb.

## Wool Amount

### Sheep Count

The *mishnah* records a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding the minimum number of sheep required to trigger the obligation of *reshit ha'gez*. Beit Shammai holds that two sheep suffice, while Beit Hillel holds that five are required. The *Gemara* notes that the position of Beit Shammai is not difficult: we know of a verse where two animals are called *tzon*, so two certainly constitute a flock. The difficulty is with Beit Hillel: there is also a verse where two are called *tzon*, so how can Beit Hillel require five and simply ignore that usage?

### Asuyyot Analysis

Rav Kahana answers by invoking the word *asuyot* in the verse cited by Beit Hillel, which describes five sheep as *asuyot*. The word *asuyot* — meaning "made" or "laden" — indicates, in the plural, that they perform (*osot*) two *mitzvot*: *reshit ha'gez* and *matanos*. When you own five sheep, you are positioned to fulfill both the obligation of giving first shearings and the obligation of giving the priestly gifts upon slaughter.

The *Gemara* challenges this: perhaps the two *mitzvot* are *bechorah* and *matanos* — the firstborn obligation and the gifts? The *Gemara* rejects this: one sheep is sufficient to be obligated in both the firstborn and the priestly gifts, so the number five would be irrelevant to that combination. The *Gemara* then turns the objection back: but similarly, one sheep suffices for *matanos*! Rav Ashi therefore reformulates the *drasha*: *asuyot* in the causative sense means that these five sheep impose an obligation on their owner — *she-ma'asot et ba'aleihem* — they compel him to perform the *mitzvah* of *reshit ha'gez*. The word *asuyot* thus signals that it is specifically when one owns five that this obligation is activated.

### Rabbi Yosi's Four

A third opinion is introduced from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: Rabbi Yosi's son cites his father as holding that four sheep are required, deriving this from the verse *v'arba tzon tachas ha'seh* — the Torah's requirement that one who steals and slaughters or sells a sheep repays four sheep in its place. That verse uses the word *tzon* (flock) to describe four animals, yielding the position that four constitutes a flock sufficient for the obligation.

### Rabbi Prefers Yosi

A *baraita* records that Rabbi (Rebbi Yehudah HaNasi) expressed a clear preference for the position of Rabbi Yosi. He states: even if Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai derived their positions from verses in the Torah, and Rabbi Yosi's position were derived only from a verse in *Nach*, the tradition (*kabbalah*) of Rabbi Yosi's school would be preferable. All the more so now, when in fact Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai are learning from verses in *Nach*, while Rabbi Yosi is learning from a verse in the Torah itself — how much more so should we follow Rabbi Yosi's ruling of four.

The *Gemara* asks what specifically attracts Rebbi to Rabbi Yosi's position. One might think he is drawn to it as a middle-ground compromise between the two and five of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. The *Gemara* rejects this reasoning, invoking the principle *ein hachra'ah shelishit machra'at* — a third opinion does not carry weight simply by virtue of situating itself between two others. Such weight applies only to a *da'at machria*, a position that actually splits its vote: where one *tanna* says a law applies in both Case A and Case B, and another says it applies in neither, a third who says it applies in A but not in B effectively sides with the first opinion in the dispute about A and with the second in the dispute about B, yielding a two-to-one majority in each direction. That is logically decisive. But a position that simply announces a new number — four, without any logical decomposition of the other positions — is merely a third opinion, not a tipping opinion.

Rabbi Yochanan explains that what drew Rebbi to Rabbi Yosi's position was not its middling character but its pedigree: *mipi ha'shemuah amrah* — he had it as a received tradition, *mipi Chaggai Zechariah u'Malachi*, from the last of the prophets. It should be noted that despite Rebbi's preference, the *Geonim* and the Rambam rule in accordance with Beit Hillel's position of five sheep, following the standard principle of *halacha* like Beit Hillel rather than privileging Rebbi's expressed preference, since a ruling in the *mishnah* does not in itself determine final *psak*.

## Amount to Give

### Dosetai's Position

The *mishnah* records the opinion of Dosetai ben Hurkenos, who holds that each of the five sheep must produce a *maneh u'pras* — one and a half *maneh* — of wool in order for the owner to be obligated. The *Chachamim* disagree and say *kol shehu* — even a small amount suffices. The *Gemara* immediately asks: how much is *kol shehu*?

### Rav's Unified Amount

Rav answers: *maneh u'pras* — the same figure as Dosetai ben Hurkenos, one and a half *maneh*. The difference is that whereas Dosetai requires one and a half *maneh* from each sheep individually, Rav requires one and a half *maneh* total from all five sheep combined. He adds the qualification *uvilvad sheyihyu mechumashot* — provided the total is equally distributed among the five sheep. This means each sheep must contribute its proportionate share: if one sheep produces its ninth of the total but another falls short, the obligation is not triggered. The floor per individual sheep is therefore one-fifth of one and a half *maneh*.

### Shmuel's Proportion

Shmuel states a different position: the flock must produce 60 *selaim* in total, and one *sela* is given to the *Kohen*. This introduces a proportional model — the gift is 1/60th of the total shearing, an idea recognizable from *terumah*. Shmuel's minimum of 60 *selaim* is likely derived by working backwards from the gift: since one must give something of significance — no less than one *sela* — and since the proportion is 1/60th, one must begin with at least 60 *selaim*. The minimum is generated by the logic of the proportion.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Minimum

Rabbi Yochanan, as reported by Rabbah bar bar Chana, disagrees with Shmuel on both points. He holds that the total shearing need only be six *selaim*, and the gift to the *Kohen* is five *selaim*. This reflects a fundamentally different framework: rather than a proportional obligation, Rabbi Yochanan posits an absolute minimum gift of five *selaim*. The basis for five as the minimum gift is presumably related to the *mishnah*'s language of *reishit* — the principle that *reishit sheyihyu she'reha nikarim*, that what remains after the gift is given must still be recognizable as a meaningful quantity. If the absolute minimum gift is five, then the minimum one must possess before giving is six: five to give plus something left over.

Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan are thus working from opposite ends. Shmuel fixes the proportion (1/60th) and derives the minimum from the smallest meaningful unit (one *sela*), arriving at 60. Rabbi Yochanan fixes the absolute minimum gift (five *selaim*) and derives the minimum holding from that floor, arriving at six.

### Conversion Clarified

The *Gemara* works with several units of measurement, and it is worth establishing the conversion table precisely. The correct equivalences are: one *maneh* equals 25 *selaim*, and one *maneh* also equals 100 *dinarim*, which means one *sela* equals four *dinarim*. With this corrected table, the positions may be restated: Dosetai ben Hurkenos requires one and a half *maneh* (= 37.5 *selaim*) per sheep, yielding a total of 187.5 *selaim* for five sheep — approximately eight pounds of wool. Rav requires 37.5 *selaim* total across all five sheep. Shmuel requires 60 *selaim* total. Rabbi Yochanan requires only six *selaim* total, which at the scale of roughly 100 *selaim* to four pounds amounts to approximately two-tenths of a pound — a very small quantity indeed. A useful shorthand for subsequent discussions: one *maneh* is approximately equivalent to one pound of wool.
