# Hullin 137 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trefah Exemption

### Trefah Not Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר אֶת הַטְּרֵפוֹת מֵרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? יָלֵיף ״נְתִינָה״ ״נְתִינָה״ מִמַּתָּנוֹת – מָה מַתָּנוֹת, טְרֵפָה – לָא, אַף רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז נָמֵי, טְרֵפָה – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon deems *tereifot* exempt from the first sheared wool. What is the reason for the ruling of Rabbi Shimon?** The reason is that he **derives a verbal analogy between the term giving mentioned with regard to the first sheared wool and giving from the gifts of the priesthood.** The Torah states: "Shall you give him" (Deuteronomy 18:4), with regard to the first sheared wool, and it states: "He shall give," with regard to the gifts of the priesthood. **Just as with regard to the gifts of the priesthood, one is not obligated to give them from a *tereifa* animal, so too, with regard to the first sheared wool, one is not obligated to give them from a *tereifa*.**

The *mishnah* enumerated several ways in which *zroa u'lchayayim u'va'keiva* — the priestly gifts of the arm, cheeks, and stomach — has a broader application than *reshit ha'gez*, the obligation to give the first shearings. The most obvious distinction is that *reshit ha'gez* applies only to sheep and only when one owns at least five of them. The *Gemara*, however, observes that additional distinctions could have been listed, and asks why the *mishnah* omitted the fact that *reshit ha'gez* applies to a *trefah* animal, whereas the priestly gifts do not.

The *Gemara* takes it for granted that the gifts do not apply to a *trefah*. Rashi explains this on the basis of the verse *v'nasan la'Kohen* — "and he shall give to the Kohen" — which implies the gift must be suitable for the *Kohen* to eat. Since a *trefah* cannot be eaten by the *Kohen*, the gifts do not apply. Given this, why does the *mishnah* not list the *trefah* exemption as a point of difference, noting that *reshit ha'gez* applies to a *trefah* while *matanos* do not?

Ravina answers: the *mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Shimon. A *baraita* states explicitly that Rabbi Shimon exempts *trefos* from *reshit ha'gez* as well. From his perspective, there is no distinction to list: both *matanos* and *reshit ha'gez* are equally exempt from the *trefah*, so no contrast exists.

### Rabbi Shimon's Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי יָלֵיף נְתִינָה נְתִינָה מִמַּתָּנוֹת, לֵילַף נְתִינָה נְתִינָה מִתְּרוּמָה, מָה תְּרוּמָה: בָּאָרֶץ – אִין, בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – לָא, אַף רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז נָמֵי: בָּאָרֶץ – אִין, בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – לָא. אַלְּמָה תְּנַן: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז נוֹהֵג בָּאָרֶץ וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If Rabbi Shimon derives a verbal analogy between giving mentioned with regard to the first sheared wool and giving from the gifts of the priesthood, let him also derive a verbal analogy between giving mentioned with regard to the first sheared wool and giving from *teruma*.** The Torah states: "The first fruits of your grain, of your wine, and of your oil, and the first sheared wool of your flock shall you give him" (Deuteronomy 18:4). **Just as with regard to *teruma*, produce grown in Eretz Yisrael, yes, it is obligated, whereas produce grown outside of Eretz Yisrael is not obligated, so too with regard to the mitzva of the first sheared wool: In Eretz Yisrael, yes, it applies, whereas outside of Eretz Yisrael it does not apply. Why, then, did we learn in the mishna that the mitzva of the first sheared wool applies both in Eretz Yisrael and outside of Eretz Yisrael?**

The *Gemara* asks: what is Rabbi Shimon's reasoning? The answer is that he employs a *gezeirah shavah*: he links the word *nesina* (giving) in the context of *reshit ha'gez* to the word *nesina* in the context of *ma'aser behemah*. Just as *ma'aser* does not apply to a *trefah*, so too *reshit ha'gez* does not apply to a *trefah*.

### Terumah Linkage Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּיָלֵיף ״צֹאן״ ״צֹאן״ מִמַּעֲשֵׂר – מָה מַעֲשֵׂר, טְרֵפָה – לָא, אַף רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז, טְרֵפָה – לָא. וְהָתָם מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב ״כֹּל אֲשֶׁר יַעֲבֹר תַּחַת הַשָּׁבֶט״ – פְּרָט לִטְרֵפָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עוֹבֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather, this is the reason for the ruling of Rabbi Shimon, as he derives a verbal analogy between the term "flock [*tzon*]" mentioned with regard to the first sheared wool and the term "flock [*tzon*]" from the animal tithe.** With regard to the first sheared wool, the verse states: "And the first sheared wool of your flock [*tzonekha*], shall you give him" (Deuteronomy 18:4), and with regard to the animal tithe it states: "And all the tithe of the herd or the flock [*tzon*], whichever passes under the rod, the tenth shall be holy unto the Lord" (Leviticus 27:32). **Just as with regard to the tithe, one is not obligated in the case of an animal that is a *tereifa*, so too, with regard to the first sheared wool, one is not obligated in the case of a *tereifa*.** The Gemara asks: **And there, with regard to the animal tithe, from where do we derive that it does not apply to a *tereifa*?** The Gemara answers **that it is written: "Whichever passes under the rod," which excludes a *tereifa*, which is not able to pass under the rod due to its physical state.**

The *Gemara* immediately raises a challenge: if one is willing to derive a law through the shared word *nesina*, why not instead link *reshit ha'gez* to *terumah*? The consequence of such a linkage would be that just as *terumah* applies only in the Land of Israel and not outside it, so too *reshit ha'gez* would apply only in the Land of Israel — a position identified elsewhere with Rabbi Elazar, but not the position of the *mishnah*, which explicitly states that *reshit ha'gez* applies both in the Land of Israel and outside it.

The *Gemara* responds with a simple *ela*: that linkage is rejected precisely because of our *mishnah's* ruling that *reshit ha'gez* applies in *chutz la'aretz*. We therefore cannot learn from *terumah*. The reasoning of Rabbi Shimon must instead be grounded in the link to *ma'aser*.

The *Gemara* then explains the actual mechanism of Rabbi Shimon's derivation: he links *reshit ha'gez* to *ma'aser behemah* through the shared word *tzon* (flock). The verse establishing *ma'aser behemah* speaks of the flock, and *reshit ha'gez* likewise speaks of the flock. Just as *ma'aser* does not apply to a *trefah*, so too *reshit ha'gez* does not apply to a *trefah*.

The *Gemara* then asks: and how do we know that *ma'aser* itself does not apply to a *trefah*? After all, if the tenth animal that walks out of the pen has a *mum* (blemish), it does become sanctified as *ma'aser* and is eaten with its blemish rather than redeemed. Perhaps a *trefah* should be treated similarly? The *Gemara* answers from the verse *kol asher ya'avor tachas ha'shavet* — "whatever passes under the rod." Since a *trefah* cannot walk under the rod (some *trefos* are missing a limb), the verse excludes it. Tosafot notes a difficulty here: some animals that are not *trefos* also cannot walk under the rod, such as one whose hoof has been torn off. The *Gemara's* *drasha* thus focuses on the quality of being unable to walk as the operative exclusion, even though the category of *trefah* is broader than just those that literally cannot walk. That is the mechanics of the *drasha*, even if it is somewhat inexact.

### Ma'aser vs. Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵילַף ״צֹאן״ ״צֹאן״ מִבְּכוֹר – מָה בְּכוֹר אֲפִילּוּ טְרֵפָה, אַף רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז אֲפִילּוּ טְרֵפָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But let Rabbi Shimon derive a verbal analogy between the term "flock [*tzon*]" mentioned in this context and the term "flock [*tzon*]" from the mitzva to consecrate the male firstborn animal.** In that context, the Torah states: "All the firstborn males that are born of your herd and of your flock [*tzonekha*] you shall sanctify to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 15:19). **Just as with regard to the consecration of the firstborn even a *tereifa* is consecrated, so too, with regard to the first sheared wool the obligation applies even in the case of a *tereifa*.**

Having established the *tzon*-*tzon* link to *ma'aser*, the *Gemara* challenges: why not instead derive *reshit ha'gez* from *bechor* through the same shared word *tzon*? The verse regarding *bechor* also uses the word *tzon*. And since *bechor* does apply to a *trefah* — the firstborn is automatically sanctified by virtue of its birth, without any act on the owner's part — learning from *bechor* would lead to the conclusion that *reshit ha'gez* also applies to a *trefah*, reversing Rabbi Shimon's position.

### Reshit HaGez-Ma'aser Linkage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִסְתַּבְּרָא מִמַּעֲשֵׂר הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן זְכָרִים טְמֵאִין. בִּמְרוּבִּין, מֵרֶחֶם. אָדָם, פָּשׁוּט, לִפְנֵי הַדִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **is reasonable that Rabbi Shimon should derive the *halakha* of the first sheared wool from the *halakha* of animal tithe rather than from the mitzva of the firstborn, as there are many *halakhot* that are common to both the first sheared wool and the animal tithe.** The Gemara enumerates these *halakhot*: First, the first sheared wool and the animal tithe apply to both **male** and female animals, while the mitzva of the firstborn applies only to males. Furthermore, the first sheared wool and the animal tithe do not apply to **non-kosher** animals, whereas the sanctity of the firstborn also applies to donkeys, which are not kosher. In addition, these two mitzvot both apply only in a case **of numerous** animals: The first sheared wool applies only if one shears no fewer than five sheep, and one must own at least ten animals to set aside the animal tithe, whereas the sanctity of the firstborn applies to a single animal. Another common feature is that unlike the firstborn, the first sheared wool and the animal tithe are not sanctified **from the womb,** but only once they are designated. Moreover, both the first sheared wool and the animal tithe apply only to animals, whereas the sanctity of the firstborn also applies to a firstborn son in the case of **a man.** Likewise, these two mitzvot apply not only to firstborn animals but also to **ordinary** non-firstborn animals, unlike the sanctity of the firstborn. Finally, the first sheared wool and the animal tithe did not apply **before the divine word was issued** at the giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai, whereas the sanctity of the firstborn already applied while the Jews were still in Egypt.

The *Gemara* responds that it is more logical to learn *reshit ha'gez* from *ma'aser* rather than from *bechor*, and proceeds to enumerate the points of connection between *reshit ha'gez* and *ma'aser*. First, *zecharim*: both *reshit ha'gez* and *ma'aser* are not limited to male animals, whereas *bechor* applies only to males. Second, *tmeim*: neither applies to non-kosher species, while *bechor* applies even to a donkey. Third, *bimrubim*: both require a minimum number of animals — *reshit ha'gez* requires five and *ma'aser* requires ten — whereas *bechor* applies even to a single animal. Fourth, *merachem*: neither *reshit ha'gez* nor *ma'aser* is sanctified from the moment of birth, unlike *bechor*, which is sanctified from the womb. Fifth, *adam*: neither applies to human beings, while *bechor* does. Sixth, *pashut*: crucially, both apply only to non-firstborns. An animal that is already a *bechor* is not subject to *ma'aser* and not subject to *reshit ha'gez*, since both apply only to ordinary, non-sanctified animals — the precise inverse of *bechor*. Seventh, *lifnei ha'dibur*: neither *reshit ha'gez* nor *ma'aser* existed as obligations before the Torah was given at Sinai, whereas *bechor* had a form of sacred status even in Egypt.

### Reshit HaGez-Bechor Counterargument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה, מִבְּכוֹר הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמֵילַף, שֶׁכֵּן: יָתוֹם, שֶׁלָּקְחוּ. בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת, נָתְנוּ; בִּפְנֵי כֹּהֵן, בִּקְדוּשָּׁה, וּמְכִירָה – וְהָנָךְ נְפִישִׁין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **On the contrary, Rabbi Shimon should derive the *halakha* of the first sheared wool from the sanctity of the firstborn rather than from the animal tithe, as there are many *halakhot* common to the first sheared wool and the firstborn animal.** The Gemara again enumerates the *halakhot* in common: Both apply to **an orphan** animal, i.e., one whose mother died before its birth, whereas the animal tithe does not apply to an animal of this kind. Furthermore, both mitzvot are in effect in the case of an animal **that one purchased,** whereas the animal tithe does not apply to a purchased animal. In addition, both the mitzva of the first sheared wool and the mitzva of the firstborn animal apply to an animal owned **in partnership,** unlike the animal tithe. Both apply as well to an animal that one **gave** another as a gift, whereas the animal tithe does not apply in the case of a gift. Likewise, both apply even when not **in the presence of the Temple, whereas the animal tithe is in effect only when the Temple is standing. Moreover, both the first sheared wool and the firstborn are given to **a priest, whereas the animal tithe is eaten by the owner.** The Gemara further states that the matter **of consecration** applies neither to the mitzva of the first sheared wool nor to the firstborn animal. The firstborn is consecrated from the womb, while the first sheared wool is non-sacred. By contrast, the animal tithe is consecrated by the owner when passed under the rod. **And finally, the option of sale by the priest applies to the first sheared wool and the firstborn animal after the priest receives them, whereas the animal tithe may not be sold. The Gemara adds: And these *halakhot* common to the first sheared wool and the firstborn are more numerous** than the *halakhot* shared by the first sheared wool and the animal tithe. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon should derive the verbal analogy between the first sheared wool and the firstborn.

### Pashut as Key Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּשׁוּט מִפָּשׁוּט עֲדִיף לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Even so, Rabbi Shimon **prefers** to derive the *halakhot* of the first sheared wool, which apply to **an ordinary** non-firstborn animal, **from** those of animal tithe, which also apply to **an ordinary** animal. Rabbi Shimon maintains that the shared factor that the first sheared wool and the animal tithe apply to all animals, not only the first to emerge from the womb, is decisive and overrides the other shared *halakhot*.

Having presented a formidable list of connections to *bechor*, the *Gemara* acknowledges: *v'hani nifishei* — these points are even more numerous. Yet the conclusion remains that one should derive *reshit ha'gez* from *ma'aser* rather than from *bechor*. The decisive reasoning is: *pashut mipashut adif lei* — the connection of *pashut* (non-firstborn) is the most fundamental. The entire legal identity of *bechor* is that it is the firstborn; the entire legal identity of both *ma'aser* and *reshit ha'gez* is that they apply specifically to non-firstborns, to ordinary animals. *Bechor* is constitutively defined by being *bechor*; *ma'aser* and *reshit ha'gez* are constitutively defined by being everything that is not *bechor*. That structural correspondence outweighs the larger number of incidental shared features with *bechor*. Therefore, Rabbi Shimon correctly derives from *ma'aser* that *reshit ha'gez* does not apply to a *trefah*.

## Sheep Only

### Shearing Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז אֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג אֶלָּא בִּרְחֵלִים. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אָתְיָא גִּיזָּה גִּיזָּה, כְּתִיב הָכָא ״רֵאשִׁית גֵּז צֹאנְךָ תִּתֶּן לוֹ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״וּמִגֵּז כְּבָשַׂי יִתְחַמָּם״, מָה לְהַלָּן כְּבָשִׂים, אַף כָּאן כְּבָשִׂים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **The first sheared wool is in effect only with regard to sheep.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters derived?** **Rav Ḥisda said:** It is **derived by means of a verbal analogy between shearing mentioned in this context and shearing mentioned elsewhere.** It is **written here: "The first sheared wool of your flock [*tzonekha*], shall you give him"** (Deuteronomy 18:4), **and** it is **written there** that Job says concerning the needy: **"And he was warmed with the shearing of my sheep [*kevasai*]"** (Job 31:20). **Just as there, the verse is referring specifically to the shearing of sheep, as the term *keves* is used only with regard to a sheep, so too here, with regard to the first sheared wool, the verse is referring to sheep, despite the fact that the term *tzon* can be interpreted as referring also to goats.**

The *mishnah* teaches that *reshit ha'gez* applies only to *rechelim* — sheep — unlike the priestly gifts of *zroa u'lchayayim u'va'keiva*, which apply to both cattle and sheep. The *Gemara* at the top of 137a asks: from where is this derived?

Rav Chisda answers: we make a *gezeirah shavah* using the word *gez* (shearing). The Torah states *reishit gez tzonecha titen lo* — "the first shearing of your flock you shall give to him." And in the book of Iyov we find the verse *umigez kevasai yitchamam* — "and from the shearing of my sheep he warmed himself," where the same word *gez* appears specifically in the context of *kevasim*, sheep, not goats. The question naturally arises: why would one have thought to include goats in the first place, given that the verse mentions *tzon* (flock)? Tosafot addresses this by citing Bereishit 27, where Rivka tells Yaakov to go to the *tzon* and take *shenei g'deiyei izim* — two goats. There we see that goats can be included in the word *tzon*. Hence the need to exclude them through a comparison to the Iyov verse, which uses *gez* specifically by *kevasim*. Tosafot notes this is not a formal *gezeirah shavah* in the classical sense, but rather a textual inference that when the Torah speaks of shearing one's flock, it means shearing sheep, not goats.

### Goat Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף ״גִּיזָּה״ ״גִּיזָּה״ מִבְּכוֹר, דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֹא תַעֲבֹד בִּבְכֹר שׁוֹרֶךָ וְלֹא תָגֹז בְּכוֹר צֹאנֶךָ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שׁוֹר בַּעֲבוֹדָה וְצֹאן בְּגִיזָּה. מִנַּיִן לִיתֵּן הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה, וְאֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא תַעֲבֹד וְלֹא תָגוֹז״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But derive instead a verbal analogy concerning shearing mentioned in this context from shearing mentioned in the case of the firstborn animal: Just as the prohibition against shearing the firstborn also applies to a firstborn ox, so too, the *halakhot* of the first sheared wool should also apply to oxen.** It is prohibited to shear a firstborn ox, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"You shall do no work with the firstborn of your ox, and you shall not shear the firstborn of your flock"** (Deuteronomy 15:19). From a straightforward reading of the verse, **I have derived only that a firstborn ox may not be used for labor and that a firstborn of an animal from the flock may not be used for shearing. From where is it derived to apply the prohibition that was stated about that animal to this one, and the prohibition that was stated about this animal to that one? The verse states: "You shall do no work…and you shall not shear."** The conjunction "and" indicates that the two parts of the verse apply to both animals.

The *Gemara* raises a further challenge: why not link the word *gez* (shearing) instead to the *bechor* context, where the Torah also uses the language of shearing flocks — *v'lo tagoz bechor tzonecha*, do not shear the firstborn of your flock. And the *baraita* there expands this prohibition to cattle as well, implying that shearing can be applied even to cattle. If shearing is broad enough to encompass cattle, perhaps the obligation of *reshit ha'gez* should likewise include goats and even cattle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר קְרָא: ״תִּתֵּן לוֹ״, וְלֹא לְשַׂקּוֹ. אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, נוֹצָה שֶׁל עִזִּים לִיחַיַּיב? בָּעֵינַן גִּיזָּה, וְלֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: One cannot say that oxen are subject to the mitzva of first sheared wool, as the **verse states** with regard to this mitzva: **"Shall you give him," which indicates that the shearing is given to the priest himself, i.e., for him to wear, and not for use as his sack.** The shearing of oxen is fit for use only as sackcloth, not as clothing. The Gemara objects: **If that is so, let one be obligated in the mitzva of the first sheared wool in the case of goats' hair, as it too can be used for clothing.** The Gemara explains: To be obligated in the first sheared wool **we require an act of shearing, and** goats' hair is plucked, **not sheared.**

The *Gemara* responds with a verse: *titen lo* — "you shall give to him" — implies something suitable for the *Kohen* to wear personally, not merely for utilitarian purposes. This would exclude cattle hair, which is not used for clothing. But what of goat hair? Goat hair is used for certain textile purposes. The *Gemara* answers: *ba'inan geizah v'leika* — the Torah requires an act of *geizah*, actual shearing, and when one removes the hair of goats, one does not shear; rather, one plucks or removes it through a different process. Since the method of hair removal from goats differs from shearing, it does not qualify.

### Rabbi Yosi & Verbs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ הַאי סְבָרָא? רַבִּי יוֹסֵי – הָא מוֹדֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּמִידֵּה דְּאוֹרְחֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who did you hear who accepts this reasoning that only an act of shearing renders one obligated in the mitzva of the first sheared wool?** This is the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, which the Gemara will soon cite.** But **Rabbi Yosei concedes that the obligation does exist without the act of shearing in a case where the usual manner of removing the wool is not by shearing.**

The *Gemara* then identifies the interpretive principle at stake and asks: who holds this position — that the word *geizah* means specifically an act of shearing and nothing analogous? This is the approach of Rabbi Yosi, who consistently interprets verbs in their narrow, precise sense and does not extend them abstractly to any act that achieves the same result.

However, the *Gemara* immediately complicates this: even according to Rabbi Yosi, who reads verbs narrowly, his principle is *modeh Rabbi Yosi b'midi d'orchei* — Rabbi Yosi concedes where the act in question is the normal, customary way of doing that thing for that object. Rabbi Yosi only excludes unusual or analogous methods when applied to the same object. But removing goat hair by plucking or by whatever method is standard for goats is the *normal* way of removing goat hair. It is not an unusual technique applied to goats — it is precisely the usual one. Therefore, Rabbi Yosi's narrow reading of *geizah* would not by itself suffice to exclude goats.

### Priestly Garment Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: ״לַעֲמוֹד לְשָׁרֵת״ – דָּבָר הָרָאוּי לְשֵׁירוּת, הָכָא נָמֵי – דָּבָר הָרָאוּי לְשֵׁירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the Gemara provides a different source for the mishna's statement.** The reason that the mitzva of first sheared wool applies only to sheep is **as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** It is stated in the verse following the source for the mitzva of first sheared wool: "For the Lord your God has chosen him out of all your tribes, **to stand to serve** in the name of the Lord, he and his sons forever" (Deuteronomy 18:5). The term "to serve" indicates that the first sheared wool that is given to the priest must consist of **a matter fit for the Temple service,** i.e., its size must be large enough for use for the priestly garments. **Here too,** the first sheared wool must be of **a matter fit for Temple service,** i.e., sheep's wool, from which the priestly garments are woven.

The *Gemara* therefore invokes a different principle, attributed to the school of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi: the verse connecting *reshit ha'gez* to the *Kohen* uses the language *la'amod l'shareit* — to stand and serve — evoking the priestly service. This yields the requirement that the gift must be *davar harauy l'shirut* — something from which a garment suitable for the priestly service could potentially be made, i.e., *bigdei kehunah*. Wool meets this criterion, since wool was used in the *bigdei kehunah*. Goat hair was never used in the *bigdei kehunah* and therefore does not qualify. This principle — that the gift must be material suitable for priestly garments — will recur in the subsequent discussion to define both the type and the quantity of wool required.

### Hard Wool Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״גִּיזָּה״ ״גִּיזָּה״ לְמַאי אֲתָא? לְכִדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: כְּבָשִׂים שֶׁצַּמְרָן קָשֶׁה פְּטוּרִים מֵרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּמִגֵּז כְּבָשַׂי יִתְחַמָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But if so, for what purpose does the verbal analogy between shearing mentioned with regard to the first sheared wool and shearing mentioned with regard to the flock come?** The Gemara answers: It is necessary **for that which was taught** in **the school of Rabbi Yishmael.** As **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** In the case of **sheep whose wool is hard,** the owner is **exempt from the first sheared wool, as it is stated: "And he was warmed with the shearing of my sheep"** (Job 31:20). Since hard wool does not provide warmth, the mitzva of the first sheared wool does not apply to this wool.

With the *gezeirah shavah* to the Iyov verse no longer needed to exclude goats (since the *davar harauy l'shirut* principle accomplishes that independently), the *Gemara* asks: what purpose does the Iyov verse serve? It answers: *l'chadtani d'bei Rabbi Yishmael* — it comes to teach what was stated in the school of Rabbi Yishmael. The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: *k'vasim shetzamran kasheh peturin me'reishit ha'gez* — sheep whose wool is stiff and hard are exempt from *reshit ha'gez*. The basis is the very verse from Iyov: *umigez kevasai yitchamam* — "from the shearing of my sheep he warmed himself." The wool must be of the kind capable of producing warmth when fashioned into clothing. Wool that is too stiff and rigid to wrap around the body and provide genuine warmth does not qualify.

Rashi and the Rambam read this somewhat differently. Rashi appears to suggest a relative standard — that such wool is distinguished from ordinary sheep's wool — while the Rambam states the criterion in objective terms: wool that is categorically incapable of providing warmth as clothing is exempt.

This is a striking application. The thematic orientation of *reshit ha'gez* toward clothing and the *Kohen*'s personal use — an idea the Rambam himself introduces mid-*halacha* when he notes that the Torah provided the *Kohen* with grain through *terumah*, meat through *korbanot* and *matanos*, and clothing through *reshit ha'gez* — turns out to have concrete halachic consequences. It excludes goat hair, restricts the obligation to wool, and now further restricts it to wool that is genuinely fit for garment-making.

### Shearing vs. Bathing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי חֲדָא: גּוֹזֵז אֶת הָעִזִּים וְשׁוֹטֵף אֶת הָרְחֵלִים – פָּטוּר, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַגּוֹזֵז אֶת הָעִזִּים – פָּטוּר, וְשׁוֹטֵף אֶת הָרְחֵלִים – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** § It **is taught** in **one baraita**: If one **shears goats, or washes sheep,** which causes some of their wool to be detached, he is **exempt** from the obligation of first sheared wool with regard to this wool. **And** it **is taught** in **another baraita**: **One who shears goats is exempt but** one who **washes the sheep** is **obligated** to separate the first sheared wool from the wool that was washed off.

The *Gemara* now addresses two apparently conflicting *baraitot*. The first *baraita* states: if one shears goats — which we have established is exempt — and if one *shotef* (rinses, passes through water) the sheep, one is exempt. The case of washing the sheep refers to the practice of driving flocks through a river or stream, which serves as a bath; in the course of this process, some wool may be torn off or separated from the sheep's body. The question is whether wool that detaches during bathing is subject to *reshit ha'gez*. The first *baraita* says no, this is exempt.

A second *baraita*, however, states: while shearing goats is exempt, *v'hashoteif et harechelim chayav* — one who bathes the sheep is *obligated*. The two *baraitot* thus appear to contradict each other directly regarding the wool that comes off sheep during bathing.

### Rabanan-Rabbi Yosi Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבָּנַן, וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. דְּתַנְיָא: ״לֶקֶט קְצִירְךָ״ – וְלֹא לֶקֶט קִיטּוּף. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵין לֶקֶט אֶלָּא הַבָּא מֵחֲמַת קָצִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves the contradiction: It is **not difficult,** as **this baraita,** which requires one to separate the first sheared wool from the washed-off wool, **is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, and that baraita,** which exempts the washed off wool, **is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei.** The Gemara cites the source of this dispute. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "And when you reap the harvest of your land, you shall not completely remove the corner of your field when you reap, and you shall not gather the gleaning of your harvest; you shall leave them for the poor" (Leviticus 23:22). The phrase **"the gleaning of your harvest" indicates that only such a gleaning is included, and not gleaning from picking.** If one picks the crop by hand rather than harvesting it using agricultural tools, he is exempt from the mitzva to leave the gleanings for the poor. **Rabbi Yosei says: Gleanings are only those that come due to harvesting** using agricultural tools.

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. One *baraita* represents the position of the *Rabanan* and the other represents the position of Rabbi Yosi. The dispute is precisely about how narrowly one reads the verb *gez*. According to Rabbi Yosi, *reshit ha'gez* requires a formal act of shearing; wool that separates during bathing is not the product of *geizah* and is therefore exempt. According to the *Rabanan*, who read the verb more broadly, any process that removes wool from sheep — including bathing, if wool detaches as a result — creates an obligation.

The *Gemara* at this point cites a *baraita* to illustrate Rabbi Yosi's broader hermeneutic: the Torah says *leket ketzircha* — the gleanings of your harvesting. Rabbi Yosi says *ein leket ela haba machamat katzir* — *leket* applies only to what falls as a direct result of using a scythe in the normal harvest process, not to grain that falls when one merely plucks stalks by hand. The *Gemara* notes that Rabbi Yosi here seems to say the same thing as the *tana kama*, and concludes that the *baraita* reads as Rabbi Yosi elaborating on the *tana kama*'s position: *kuli Rabbi Yosi* — it is all Rabbi Yosi.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא? כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: שֶׁרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר, אֵין לֶקֶט אֶלָּא הַבָּא מֵחֲמַת קָצִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara expresses puzzlement at this *baraita*: The opinion of **Rabbi Yosei is identical to the opinion of the first *tanna*.** The Gemara explains: The **entire baraita is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and this is what the baraita is teaching:** The phrase "the gleaning of your harvest" teaches that crops picked by hand are exempt from the mitzva, **as Rabbi Yosei says: Gleanings are only those that come due to harvesting.** The Gemara infers that just as Rabbi Yosei interprets the term "harvest" as exempting crops that were picked by hand, so too he derives from the term "shearing" that the mitzva of the first sheared wool does not apply to fleece that was washed off. The Rabbis, who disagree with Rabbi Yosei with regard to the gleanings, likewise disagree with regard to the first sheared wool.

Yet Rabbi Yosi is not entirely literalistic. A subsequent *baraita* shows that he extends *leket* to uprooting onions and plucking tomatoes, deriving this from the repetition of harvest-language in the relevant verses. His principle is *modeh Rabbi Yosi b'midi d'orchei*: he includes analogous acts that are the normal, standard method for that particular crop or object, even if they differ from scything wheat. He excludes only acts that are unusual or indirect — such as wool dislodging during bathing, or grain falling when one plucks stalks by hand. The crucial distinction is between what is genuinely *orchei* (customary practice) for a given subject and what is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּמִידֵּה דְּאוֹרְחֵיהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: ״קָצִיר״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא קָצִיר, עוֹקֵר מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: Rabbi Yosei concedes that the obligation applies to a case where the usual manner of harvesting is by picking, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse cited above **that Rabbi Yosei says:** From the term **"harvest" I have derived only** the obligation of gleanings with regard to **harvest. From where do I derive** the obligation of gleanings with regard to one who **uproots** the crop?

Rav Acha the son of Rava makes this explicit to Rav Ashi, and a further proof is adduced from the *mishnah* concerning *pe'ah* and *malbinot betzalim shebein hayerek* — fields of onions interspersed among non-obligated vegetables. Rabbi Yosi there rules *pe'ah mikol achat v'achat* (one gives *pe'ah* from each separate row of onions), which presupposes that uprooting onions qualifies for *pe'ah* even though the verse uses the word *katzircha*. Since uprooting is the standard way of harvesting onions, Rabbi Yosi includes it. This confirms that his standard is functional normalcy, not strict literal identity with the verb.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לִקְצוֹר״, תּוֹלֵשׁ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּקוּצְרֶךָ״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: מַלְבְּנוֹת בְּצָלִים שֶׁבֵּין הַיָּרָק, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: פֵּאָה מִכׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מֵאַחַת עַל הַכֹּל.
>
> **Translation:** **The verse states: "And when you reap the harvest of your land, you shall not completely remove the corner of your field by reaping"** (Leviticus 19:9). The superfluous term **"by reaping" includes uprooting the crop. From where is it derived** that the obligation extends to one who **picked the crop by hand? The verse states: "When you reap"** (Leviticus 23:22), which is also superfluous. It is derived from here that the mitzva applies to all ways of gathering the crop, provided they are the usual manner of harvesting that particular crop. **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: We learn as well in a mishna (*Pe'a* 3:4) that discusses the mitzva to leave produce in the corner of the field for the poor [*pe'a*]: If one has garden beds of onions that are among the vegetables, Rabbi Yosei says that one leaves separate *pe'a* from each and every one of the beds. And the Rabbis say that one leaves *pe'a* from one garden bed for all of them.** This indicates that Rabbi Yosei concedes that one is obligated to leave *pe'a* and gleanings from onions, despite the fact that they are picked by hand rather than harvested with tools. The reason is that this is the usual manner of gathering onions.

## Wool Amount

### Sheep Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה הוּא מְרוּבֶּה? בִּשְׁלָמָא לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי – תַּרְתֵּי נָמֵי אִיקְּרוֹ ״צֹאן״, אֶלָּא לְבֵית הִלֵּל – מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **And how many is numerous?** Beit Shammai say: It is at least two sheep, and Beit Hillel say: It is at least five sheep. The Gemara objects: **Granted, according to the opinion of Beit Shammai, two sheep are also called *tzon*,** as in the verse cited by Beit Shammai: "That a man shall rear a young cow and two sheep [*tzon*]" (Isaiah 7:21). Since the mitzva of the first sheared wool is written with regard to *tzon*, it is logical that it applies to two sheep. **But what is the reasoning of Beit Hillel,** who hold that the mitzva applies only to five sheep, due to the verse: "And five sheep [*tzon*] made" (I Samuel 25:18)? Why do they not apply the obligation using the broader criterion of two sheep, as the verse quoted by Beit Shammai clearly indicates that two sheep are also called *tzon*?

The *mishnah* records a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding the minimum number of sheep required to trigger the obligation of *reshit ha'gez*. Beit Shammai holds that two sheep suffice, while Beit Hillel holds that five are required. The *Gemara* notes that the position of Beit Shammai is not difficult: we know of a verse where two animals are called *tzon*, so two certainly constitute a flock. The difficulty is with Beit Hillel: there is also a verse where two are called *tzon*, so how can Beit Hillel require five and simply ignore that usage?

### Asuyyot Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״עֲשׂוּיוֹת״, שֶׁעוֹשׂוֹת שְׁתֵּי מִצְווֹת: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז וּמַתָּנוֹת. אֵימָא בְּכוֹרָה וּמַתָּנוֹת? בְּכוֹרָה, חֲדָא מִי לָא מִיחַיְּיבָא? וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, מַתָּנוֹת, חֲדָא מִי לָא מִיחַיְּיבָא? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: ״עֲשׂוּיוֹת״ – שֶׁמְּעַשּׂוֹת אֶת בַּעֲלֵיהֶן, וְאוֹמְרוֹת לוֹ: ״קוּם עֲשֵׂה מִצְוָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** The **verse states: "Five sheep made [*asuyot*]," in plural. The plural form indicates that two mitzvot have been made, i.e., performed, with them.** The two mitzvot are **the first sheared wool and the gifts of the priesthood,** a mitzva that applies to a single animal. If the two mitzvot have been performed only when there are five sheep, evidently the mitzva of the first sheared wool applies only to five sheep. The Gemara asks: Why can't one **say that the two mitzvot are the firstborn animal and the gifts of the priesthood?** The Gemara answers: The other mitzva cannot be the firstborn animal, as with regard to the **firstborn, isn't one animal obligated,** i.e., doesn't one animal render its owner obligated? Yet the verse states that one performs the two mitzvot only with five sheep. The Gemara asks: **And according to your reasoning, with regard to the gifts of the priesthood, isn't one animal obligated, i.e., doesn't one animal render its owner obligated?** **Rather, Rav Ashi says:** The term **"made" should be understood as meaning that five sheep make their owner** perform the first sheared wool **and figuratively say to him: Stand and fulfill a mitzva,** whereas less than five sheep do not obligate their owner in the mitzva of first sheared wool.

Rav Kahana answers by invoking the word *asuyot* in the verse cited by Beit Hillel, which describes five sheep as *asuyot*. The word *asuyot* — meaning "made" or "laden" — indicates, in the plural, that they perform (*osot*) two *mitzvot*: *reshit ha'gez* and *matanos*. When you own five sheep, you are positioned to fulfill both the obligation of giving first shearings and the obligation of giving the priestly gifts upon slaughter.

The *Gemara* challenges this: perhaps the two *mitzvot* are *bechorah* and *matanos* — the firstborn obligation and the gifts? The *Gemara* rejects this: one sheep is sufficient to be obligated in both the firstborn and the priestly gifts, so the number five would be irrelevant to that combination. The *Gemara* then turns the objection back: but similarly, one sheep suffices for *matanos*! Rav Ashi therefore reformulates the *drasha*: *asuyot* in the causative sense means that these five sheep impose an obligation on their owner — *she-ma'asot et ba'aleihem* — they compel him to perform the *mitzvah* of *reshit ha'gez*. The word *asuyot* thus signals that it is specifically when one owns five that this obligation is activated.

### Rabbi Yosi's Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: אַרְבַּע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַרְבַּע צֹאן תַּחַת הַשֶּׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** It **was taught** in a *baraita* **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei,** that he **says in the name of his father:** The obligation of the first sheared wool applies to one who owns **four sheep, since a verse calls four sheep *tzon*,** as it is stated: "If a man steals an ox or a sheep, and kills it or sells it, he shall pay five oxen for the ox, **and four sheep [*tzon*] for a sheep"** (Exodus 21:37).

A third opinion is introduced from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: Rabbi Yosi's son cites his father as holding that four sheep are required, deriving this from the verse *v'arba tzon tachas ha'seh* — the Torah's requirement that one who steals and slaughters or sells a sheep repays four sheep in its place. That verse uses the word *tzon* (flock) to describe four animals, yielding the position that four constitutes a flock sufficient for the obligation.

### Rabbi Prefers Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: אִלְמָלֵא דִּבְרֵיהֶן דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, וְדִבְרֵי בְּרִיבִּי קַבָּלָה – אֲנַן דִּבְרֵי בְּרִיבִּי שׁוֹמְעִין, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁדִּבְרֵיהֶם דִּבְרֵי קַבָּלָה, וְדִבְרֵי בְּרִיבִּי דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. וְהָאָמַר מָר: אֵין הַכְרָעָה שְׁלִישִׁית מַכְרַעַת!
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Had their statements,** i.e., the opinions of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, been based on **statements of the Torah, and the statement of the Distinguished One [*Beribbi*],** Rabbi Yosei, based on the texts of the **tradition [*kabbala*],** the Prophets and Writings, even so **we would listen to the statement of the Distinguished One,** Rabbi Yosei. **All the more so now that their statements are based on texts of the tradition,** as both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel derived their rulings from verses from the Prophets, while **the statement of the Distinguished One,** Rabbi Yosei, is based on **the statements of the Torah.** The Gemara objects: **But didn't the Master say: The decision of a third opinion that compromises between the first two opinions is not** considered **decisive** if it is based on considerations that were not mentioned by the other two opinions. Although Rabbi Yosei's opinion is a compromise between the opinions of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, it is based on a different source.

A *baraita* records that Rabbi (Rebbi Yehudah HaNasi) expressed a clear preference for the position of Rabbi Yosi. He states: even if Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai derived their positions from verses in the Torah, and Rabbi Yosi's position were derived only from a verse in *Nach*, the tradition (*kabbalah*) of Rabbi Yosi's school would be preferable. All the more so now, when in fact Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai are learning from verses in *Nach*, while Rabbi Yosi is learning from a verse in the Torah itself — how much more so should we follow Rabbi Yosi's ruling of four.

The *Gemara* asks what specifically attracts Rebbi to Rabbi Yosi's position. One might think he is drawn to it as a middle-ground compromise between the two and five of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. The *Gemara* rejects this reasoning, invoking the principle *ein hachra'ah shelishit machra'at* — a third opinion does not carry weight simply by virtue of situating itself between two others. Such weight applies only to a *da'at machria*, a position that actually splits its vote: where one *tanna* says a law applies in both Case A and Case B, and another says it applies in neither, a third who says it applies in A but not in B effectively sides with the first opinion in the dispute about A and with the second in the dispute about B, yielding a two-to-one majority in each direction. That is logically decisive. But a position that simply announces a new number — four, without any logical decomposition of the other positions — is merely a third opinion, not a tipping opinion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפִּי שְׁמוּעָה אָמְרָה, מִפִּי חַגַּי זְכַרְיָה וּמַלְאָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** in response: Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi accepted Rabbi Yosei's opinion not because it was a compromise, but rather because **he said it according to a tradition he had from the prophets Haggai, Zechariah, and Malachi.**

Rabbi Yochanan explains that what drew Rebbi to Rabbi Yosi's position was not its middling character but its pedigree: *mipi ha'shemuah amrah* — he had it as a received tradition, *mipi Chaggai Zechariah u'Malachi*, from the last of the prophets. It should be noted that despite Rebbi's preference, the *Geonim* and the Rambam rule in accordance with Beit Hillel's position of five sheep, following the standard principle of *halacha* like Beit Hillel rather than privileging Rebbi's expressed preference, since a ruling in the *mishnah* does not in itself determine final *psak*.

## Amount to Give

### Dosetai's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳. וְכַמָּה ״כׇּל שֶׁהֵן״? אָמַר רַב: מָנֶה וּפְרָס, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהוּ מְחוּמָּשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas says** that the mitzva of the first sheared wool applies to five sheep, each of whose shearing weighs one hundred and fifty dinars, whereas the Rabbis say: Five sheep, each of whose shearing is any amount. The Gemara asks: **And how much** is signified by the phrase: **Any amount?** **Rav says:** It is a total weight of **one hundred dinars [***maneh***] and half** of one hundred dinars [***peras***], **provided that they are divided equally between the five sheep.**

The *mishnah* records the opinion of Dosetai ben Hurkenos, who holds that each of the five sheep must produce a *maneh u'pras* — one and a half *maneh* — of wool in order for the owner to be obligated. The *Chachamim* disagree and say *kol shehu* — even a small amount suffices. The *Gemara* immediately asks: how much is *kol shehu*?

### Rav's Unified Amount

Rav answers: *maneh u'pras* — the same figure as Dosetai ben Hurkenos, one and a half *maneh*. The difference is that whereas Dosetai requires one and a half *maneh* from each sheep individually, Rav requires one and a half *maneh* total from all five sheep combined. He adds the qualification *uvilvad sheyihyu mechumashot* — provided the total is equally distributed among the five sheep. This means each sheep must contribute its proportionate share: if one sheep produces its ninth of the total but another falls short, the obligation is not triggered. The floor per individual sheep is therefore one-fifth of one and a half *maneh*.

### Shmuel's Proportion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: שִׁשִּׁים, וְנוֹתֵן סֶלַע אַחַת לְכֹהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says:** It is a total weight of **sixty *sela*, of which he gives** the weight of **one *sela* to the priest.**

Shmuel states a different position: the flock must produce 60 *selaim* in total, and one *sela* is given to the *Kohen*. This introduces a proportional model — the gift is 1/60th of the total shearing, an idea recognizable from *terumah*. Shmuel's minimum of 60 *selaim* is likely derived by working backwards from the gift: since one must give something of significance — no less than one *sela* — and since the proportion is 1/60th, one must begin with at least 60 *selaim*. The minimum is generated by the logic of the proportion.

### Rabbi Yochanan's Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֵׁשׁ – לְכֹהֵן חָמֵשׁ, וְאַחַת לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar bar Ḥana says that Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is a total weight of **six *sela*, of which he gives to the priest** the weight of **five *sela*, and** he leaves **one *sela* for himself.**

Rabbi Yochanan, as reported by Rabbah bar bar Chana, disagrees with Shmuel on both points. He holds that the total shearing need only be six *selaim*, and the gift to the *Kohen* is five *selaim*. This reflects a fundamentally different framework: rather than a proportional obligation, Rabbi Yochanan posits an absolute minimum gift of five *selaim*. The basis for five as the minimum gift is presumably related to the *mishnah*'s language of *reishit* — the principle that *reishit sheyihyu she'reha nikarim*, that what remains after the gift is given must still be recognizable as a meaningful quantity. If the absolute minimum gift is five, then the minimum one must possess before giving is six: five to give plus something left over.

Shmuel and Rabbi Yochanan are thus working from opposite ends. Shmuel fixes the proportion (1/60th) and derives the minimum from the smallest meaningful unit (one *sela*), arriving at 60. Rabbi Yochanan fixes the absolute minimum gift (five *selaim*) and derives the minimum holding from that floor, arriving at six.

### Conversion Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, וּלְרַב מִי נִיחָא? וְהָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז בְּשִׁשִּׁים! הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ דְּהַהִיא, רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ גִּיזִּין הַרְבֵּה עָסְקִינַן, וּמְבַקֵּשׁ לִיתְּנָן לְכֹהֵן, וְאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: כֹּל חַד וְחַד לָא תְּבַצַּר לֵיהּ מֵחֲמֵשֶׁת סְלָעִים. גּוּפָא: רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: רֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז בְּשִׁשִּׁים, תְּרוּמָה בְּשִׁשִּׁים, פֵּאָה בְּשִׁשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning, does it work out well** according **to the opinion of Rav?** But **Rav and Shmuel both say that the first sheared wool** given to the priest is one part **in sixty.** Rav holds that the weight of one hundred and fifty dinars renders one obligated in the first sheared wool, and one-sixtieth of this weight is less than one *sela*. Therefore, Rav's opinion is also not in accordance with the mishna. The Gemara answers: **It was stated with regard to that mishna that Rav and Shmuel both say:** The five *sela* stated in the mishna is not referring to the total amount given from the sheared wool. Rather, in the mishna **we are dealing with a Jew who has a large amount of shearing, and he wishes to give them to the priest,** i.e., to several priests. **And we say to him:** With regard to **each and every one of the priests, do not give him less than five *sela*.** But if one has only a small amount of shearing, he gives the priest one-sixtieth of the shearing, even if it is less than five *sela*. § The Gemara mentioned a ruling of Rav and Shmuel with regard to the amount that one is required to give as the first sheared wool. The Gemara discusses **the matter itself. Rav and Shmuel both say: The first sheared wool** given to the priest is one part **of sixty. Likewise, the amount one is required to separate as *teruma* is one part of sixty, and the amount one must leave in his field as *pe'a* is one part of sixty.**

The *Gemara* works with several units of measurement, and it is worth establishing the conversion table precisely. The correct equivalences are: one *maneh* equals 25 *selaim*, and one *maneh* also equals 100 *dinarim*, which means one *sela* equals four *dinarim*. With this corrected table, the positions may be restated: Dosetai ben Hurkenos requires one and a half *maneh* (= 37.5 *selaim*) per sheep, yielding a total of 187.5 *selaim* for five sheep — approximately eight pounds of wool. Rav requires 37.5 *selaim* total across all five sheep. Shmuel requires 60 *selaim* total. Rabbi Yochanan requires only six *selaim* total, which at the scale of roughly 100 *selaim* to four pounds amounts to approximately two-tenths of a pound — a very small quantity indeed. A useful shorthand for subsequent discussions: one *maneh* is approximately equivalent to one pound of wool.
