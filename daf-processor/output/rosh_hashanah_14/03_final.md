# Rosh Hashanah 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shliach & Kavanah

### Get Delivery Requirements

We learned in *Masechet Gittin* that if a man sends a *get* through a *shliach*, the *shliach* must declare *bifnei nichtav u'bifnei nechtam* — that the *get* was written and signed in his presence. This declaration ensures the *get* is valid. The *gemara* explains the reasons behind this requirement: whether the concern is to confirm the authenticity of the signatures, or to ensure that the writing of the *get* was performed *lishmah*, for the sake of the specific woman. This illustrates a vital *yesod* in *halacha* regarding the trust we place in a *shliach*.

### Intent in Mitzvot

This discussion leads to a broader principle: whenever we perform a *mitzvah*, we must ensure it is done with the proper *kavanah*. Even though we are in *galus*, our *avodah* is to refine ourselves and the world around us until we merit the *geulah* with the coming of *Mashiach*, *bimeherah beyameinu*, *amen*.

## Ice & Snow on Shabbat

### Melting Ice

If one simply leaves ice in a cup and allows it to melt on its own, there is no problem of *molid*, because no active *ma'aseh* is being performed — it happens *mimeila*, on its own. Accordingly, one may place ice in a drink, but one may not crush it. The *Mishnah Berurah* clarifies in *seif katan* 35 that the liquid resulting from the ice is *misbatel* into the water already present in the cup.

### Snow on Garments

The *Shulchan Aruch* discusses in *siman* 320, *seif* 10 what is to be done when snow lands on one's clothes. The *Mechaber* rules: *hayah sheleg al begadav menaran* — if there is snow on his garments, he may shake them off. The reasoning is that snow is not *nivla*, not absorbed into the fabric, and therefore there is no *issur* of *melaben*. However, the *Rema* notes that one should be *machmir* if there is no great *tzorech*.

### Walking on Snow

Walking on snow is *muttar*. The *gemara* explains that since the crushing of snow underfoot is incidental to the normal act of walking, it does not constitute the *melacha* of *risuk*. It is simply the ordinary *derech* of movement, and is therefore permitted on *Shabbos*.

## Terumah & Mishkan

### Terumah Definition

In this week's *parsha*, *Parshat Terumah*, we find the first instruction for building the *Mishkan*. The *Torah* says: *vayikchu li terumah me'eis kol ish asher yidvenu libo tikchu es terumasi* — take for Me an offering from every man whose heart motivates him; you shall take My offering. The word *terumah*, usually translated as contribution or offering, derives from the root *rom*, meaning to lift up. When a person gives with a full heart, he is not merely giving an object — he is elevating himself and the object along with him. The *Ramban* explains that the central purpose of the *Mishkan* was to be a place where the *Shechinah*, the Divine Presence, could rest among *Bnei Yisrael*. And how is that achieved? Through *asher yidvenu libo* — through the willingness of the heart.

### Keruvim & Shechinah

Later in the *parsha*, when describing the *keruvim* atop the *aron*, the *Torah* states that their faces were *ish el achiv*, one toward the other. *Rashi* notes that this was the case when *Bnei Yisrael* were doing the will of *Hashem*. When they were not, the *keruvim* turned away. Everything depends on the heart. When there is true *nedivus lev* — a genuine generosity of spirit — the physical structure becomes a home for the *Shechinah*. May we all merit to give from the heart and thereby create a place for *Hashem* in our lives.

## Traits & Yetzarim

### Teshuva Process

This is the path of perfect *teshuva*: to take every part of one's personality and bring it back to its source, back to *HaKadosh Baruch Hu*.

### Holy Boldness

Similarly with the trait of *azut*, boldness — a person should not be impudent toward other people, but he should possess *azut d'kedushah*, holy boldness, to stand up for truth and not be embarrassed before those who mock him in his service of *Hashem*.

### Divine Roots of Traits

Every trait, even those that appear negative on the surface, has a root in the divine. The work of a person is to elevate these traits and employ them according to the will of the *Borei*, the Creator. This is what it means to serve *Hashem* with both of one's *yetzarim* — both the *yetzer hatov* and the *yetzer hara*. When we take the energy of the *yetzer hara* and channel it into our passion for *Torah* and *mitzvos*, we are performing the highest level of *avodah*.

### Strong Drives & Mussar

This is why the holy *sefarim* teach that a person who has a very strong *yetzer* — a powerful internal drive — has the potential to reach far higher levels than someone who is naturally quiet and passive. The more fire a person carries within, the more he can ignite his soul to burn with a love for *Elokus*, for Godliness. But if he fails to work on his *middos*, that same fire will consume him in negative desires and anger. Therefore, the study of *mussar* and the refinement of character is the foundation of everything. Without it, even our holy actions can be tainted by ego. May we all merit to find the path of balance and to use every part of ourselves to bring *giluy Shechinah* — the revelation of the Divine Presence — into this world.

## Trumas Halishkah

### Preventing Suspicion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִשְׁמַר הַחַיִּל וּמִשְׁמַר הַמָּקוֹם, הַנִּכְנַס לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַמָּעוֹת, לֹא יִכָּנֵס בְּבֶגֶד שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כִּיסִים, וְלֹא בִּנְעָלַיִם, וְלֹא בַּנְּעָלִים וְלֹא בְּתִפִּלִּין וְלֹא בַּקָּמֵעַ, מִשּׁוּם תִּקּוּן הָעוֹלָם, שֶׁאִם הִתְעַשֵּׁר אַחַר כָּךְ — יֹאמְרוּ: מִן הַלִּשְׁכָה הִתְעַשֵּׁר. וְגוֹמֵר: לְפִי שֶׁאָדָם צָרִיךְ לָצֵאת יְדֵי הַבְּרִיּוֹת כִּדְרַךְ שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לָצֵאת יְדֵי הַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The two types of guards who watch the Temple, the **guard of the Temple court and the guard of the Holy of Holies, the one who entered to take out the money**, from the chamber of the Temple, **was forbidden to enter wearing a garment with pockets, and not with shoes, not with sandals, not with *tefillin*, not with an amulet**, for the sake of **public order**, as if **he became wealthy afterward, people would say: He became wealthy from the chamber.** The *baraita* **continues:** The reason for these restrictions is **because a person must satisfy the requirements of his fellow human beings just as he must satisfy the requirements of the Omnipresent.**

We can extend this concept to other areas of life as well. The *Mishnah* in *Masechet Shekalim*, chapter 3, *Mishnah* 2, discusses the process of *trumas halishkah* — the taking of money from the chamber of the *Beis HaMikdash* in order to purchase the *korbanos tzibbur*, the communal sacrifices. The *Mishnah* states that the person who entered to withdraw the coins was forbidden to wear a garment with sleeves that could conceal money, and was not permitted to wear shoes, sandals, *tefillin*, or a *kameia*, an amulet — because if he were to become wealthy afterward, people might suspect that he had secreted money from the *lishkah* in his clothing or footwear. The *Mishnah* concludes with the principle: *lefi she'adam tzarich latzeis yedei habriyos k'derech shetzarich latzeis yedei HaMakom* — because a person must satisfy the requirements of his fellow human beings just as he must satisfy the requirements of the Omnipresent.

### V'Heyitem Nekiyim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַיִּשְׁמְרוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶת־הַדֶּרֶךְ וּשְׁמָרוּ אֶת־הַדִּבְרִים אֲשֶׁר יְהוָה דִּבֵּר אֵלָיו וַיֵּצְאוּ מִמִּצְרַיִם בִּפְלוּאִים אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת אֶלֶף רַגְלִי מִן־הַמְּתִים. וִהְיִיתֶם נְקִיִּים מֵהַשֵּׁם וּמִישְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And you shall be clean before the Lord and before Israel.**

This is based on the *pasuk*: *v'heyitem nekiyim me'Hashem u'mi'Yisrael* — you shall be clean before *Hashem* and before Israel. A fundamental principle emerges from here: it is not sufficient for a person to know in his own heart that he is acting honestly. It is not enough to say, "I know I am honest, *Hashem* knows I am honest, and I do not care what people think." Of course, when it comes to performing *mitzvos*, one should not be swayed by those who mock him — as the *Rema* rules in the very first *siman* of the *Shulchan Aruch*: *v'lo yisbayesh mipnei bnei adam hamal'igim alav*, a person should not be embarrassed before those who mock him for his service of *Hashem*. However, when it comes to matters of integrity — to money and honesty — we bear a responsibility to be above suspicion, and to conduct ourselves in such a way that even someone seeking to find fault will be unable to do so.

## Trumas Halishkah…

### Kiddush/Chillul Hashem

This standard applies to anyone who holds a public position, to anyone who handles money belonging to others — whether a *gabbai tzedakah*, a treasurer of a *shul* or communal organization, or a person in business. Everything must be transparent, with no room for misunderstanding. This is part of the concept of *kiddush Hashem*: when people observe a *shomer Torah u'mitzvos* acting with the highest levels of integrity and scrupulously avoiding even the appearance of impropriety, great *kavod* is brought to *Hashem* and to the *Torah*. Conversely, if a person acts carelessly and invites suspicion — even if he is in fact innocent — this can lead to *chillul Hashem*, a desecration of the Name of Heaven. We must therefore keep this principle ever before our eyes: *v'heyitem nekiyim me'Hashem u'mi'Yisrael* — always striving to be clean and pure both in our relationship with *Hashem* and in our relationship with our fellow human beings.

## Shema Timing

### Kohanim & Shema Time

The *Mishnah* at the opening of *Masechet Berachot* asks: *me'eimatai korin et shema be'arvit* — from what time do we recite the *shema* in the evening? The answer given is: *mishe'ah shehakohanim nichnasim le'echol bitrumatan* — from the time that the *kohanim* enter to eat their *terumah*.

### Tana's Foundation

The *gemara* there raises a question: *tana heicha ka'i dekatani me'eimatai* — where did the *tana* stand before this that he is now asking "from when"? The *gemara* answers that he is proceeding from the *pasuk* of *beshachbecha uv'kumecha* — the *Torah* instructs us to recite *shema* when we lie down and when we rise. The *Mishnah* therefore asks, "from when" is the time of lying down? This is the fundamental starting point of *Shas*, of the entire *Talmud Bavli* — beginning with the precise timing of a *mitzvah*.

## Nishmat Prayer

### Nishmat Segulah

*Nishmat kol chai* carries this same light and the same power. The text of *Nishmat* is composed of language that can be found within *Birchat HaMazon*, and it is for this reason that many *Chasidishe sefarim* teach that the recitation of *Nishmat* is a *segulah* for *parnassah*. Just as *Birchat HaMazon* is a blessing for physical sustenance, *Nishmat* is a blessing for the soul — and in saying it, we tap into that same flow of abundance.

### Birchat HaShir

The *Nishmat* prayer is called the *Birchat HaShir*, the blessing of song, and it is the ultimate expression of gratitude. It reminds us that every breath is a gift from the Almighty — *kol haneshama tehallel Yah*, every soul shall praise God. When we say *Nishmat kol chai*, we acknowledge that our very life force comes from Him. The connection between *Nishmat* and *Birchat HaMazon* reveals that our physical needs and our spiritual essence are one — they must not be separated, for everything flows from the same source. May we always merit to say these words with a full heart and to recognize the miracles that surround us each and every day.

## Slave Sales

### Slave Freedom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת עַבְדּוֹ לַעֲכוּ״ם — יָצָא לְחֵרוּת. מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם. וּמִפְּנֵי שֶׁאַתָּה עֲשָׂאתוֹ לְהָפֵךְ עַבְדּוֹ שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **MISHNA:** **One who sells his slave to a non-Jew goes free.** This is **due to the institution for the betterment of the world, because you have made his slave of the Holy One, blessed be He,** unable to observe the commandments.

We are in *Masechet Gittin*, 44a. The *Mishnah* teaches: *hamocher avdo la'akum yatza lecheirut* — if a person sells his slave to a non-Jew, the slave goes free. This is a *knas*, a penalty, that the *Chachamim* imposed upon the owner because he has caused the slave to lose his ability to perform *mitzvos*.

### Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמַאי הִלְכְתָא אִי מָכַר לוֹ לִתְקוּפָה — כְּגוֹן שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם? אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲפִילּוּ יוֹם אֶחָד, יָצָא לְחֵרוּת, שֶׁכִּבַּר עֲשָׂאתוֹ לַעֲבוֹר עִישּׂוּרִים. וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לִמְכּוֹר עַבְדְּךָ שְׁנִית עַד שֶׁתִּקְנוֹ מִן הַנָּכְרִי, וּשְׁנִית מִיּוֹחָזוֹ בְּדִין. וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְחִיר גָּבוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA:** **What is the law** if he sold him only for a limited period, such as thirty days? **Rabbi Akiva said: Even if it is** only **one day, he goes free, because you have already caused him to forfeit** the performance of **commandments. And just as you cannot sell your slave a second time**, after you reclaim him from the non-Jew in accordance with court proceedings **until you redeem him from the non-Jew, he is reacquired to you through court** proceedings, **even at a high price.**

The *gemara* raises the question of what the *din* is if the slave is sold only for a limited period — for example, for thirty days. The *gemara* cites a *machloket* between *Rebbi Akiva* and the *Chachamim*. *Rebbi Akiva* holds that even if the slave is sold for a single day, *yatza lecheirut* — he goes free — because the owner has already caused him to forfeit *mitzvos*. Furthermore, the *gemara* makes clear that the owner cannot simply reclaim the slave; the *beis din* compels the owner to repurchase him from the non-Jew, even at a high price, after which the slave goes free. This mechanism ensures that owners are dissuaded from ever placing a slave in the hands of someone who will cause him to cease observing *Torah u'mitzvos*.

### Manumission Document

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבַּיֵי, וְהָתַנְיָא כַּאֲבַיֵי: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְעוֹבֵד כּוֹכָבִים יָצָא לְחֵרוּת, וְצָרִיךְ לִכְתּוֹב לוֹ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר וּמָכַר אוֹתוֹ. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּשֶׁלֹּא כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ. אֲבָל כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ — זֶה הוּא שִׁחְרוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** And it is **taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with Abaye:** If a person **sells his slave to a non-Jew, he goes free and** the master **is required to write him a bill of manumission and** has **sold him.** How so? **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what case** is this **said?** **When he did not write a formal bill of sale for the slave.** **But if he wrote a bill of sale for him, that document itself** constitutes the **manumission.**

*Amar Abaye* — and this ruling is corroborated by a *baraita* in accordance with *Abaye*'s position: *hamocher avdo l'oved kochavim yatza lecheirut v'tzarich lichtov lo get shichrur umacharav* — if a person sells his slave to a non-Jew, he goes free, and the master is required to write him a bill of manumission. *Amar Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*: *bameh devarim amurim?* When does this apply? *Shelo katav alav ono* — when he did not write a formal bill of sale for the slave. *Aval katav alav ono, zeh hu shichruro* — but if he did write a bill of sale, that document itself constitutes the slave's manumission.

### Terumah Disqualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְעוֹבֵד כּוֹכָבִים פוֹסְלוֹ מִלֶּאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה. וְאִם הָיָה רַבּוֹ כֹּהֵן — לֹא יַאֲכִלוֹ בִּתְרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who sells his slave to a non-Jew disqualifies him from eating *teruma*.** Even if the slave technically retains his status as a slave and would otherwise be eligible to eat *teruma* as a member of a *kohen*'s household, **and if his master was a *kohen, he may no longer feed him *teruma*.**

The *Mishnah* further states: *hamocher avdo l'oved kochavim poslo meloechol b'terumah* — one who sells his slave to a non-Jew thereby disqualifies the slave from eating *terumah*. Even if the slave technically retains his status as a slave and would otherwise be eligible to eat *terumah* as a member of a *kohen*'s household, once he has been sold to a non-Jew he is disqualified. *Ve'im hayah rabbo kohen, ma'achilo b'terumah* — if his master was a *kohen* and previously fed him *terumah*, he may no longer do so.

### Rebbi Yosei's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: בַּחֲלוּקָה זוֹ — לֹא אָמְרוּ יָצָא לְחֵרוּת, אֶלָּא שֶׁיִּכְתּוֹב לוֹ רַבּוֹ שִׁחְרוּר וְיֹאמַר לוֹ: הִנָּךְ בְּן חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said:** In this case — when the slave is disqualified from eating *teruma* — **they did not say he goes free, rather** that **his master should write him a formal manumission and tell him: You are hereby a free person.**

*Amar Rebbi Yosei*: in this case — *lo amru yatza lecheirut* — they did not say he goes free, *ela sheyichtov lo rabbo shichrur veyomar lo harei atah ben chorin* — unless his master writes him a formal manumission and declares to him: you are hereby a free person.

## Damages & Tikkun

### Land Quality Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שָׁמִין לָהֶם בְּעִידִית, וּבַעַל חוֹב בִּבֵּינוֹנִית, וִכְתוּבַת אִשָּׁה בְּזִבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **MISHNA:** When **assessing payment for damages, collection is made from the best quality land; a creditor collects from medium quality land; and a woman's *ketubah*** is **collected from the poorest quality land.**

With *hadran alach hamocher avdo*, we complete the fourth *perek* of *Masechet Gittin* and begin *Perek* 5, *Perek Hanizakin*. The *Mishnah* opens: *shamin lahem b'idit* — when assessing payment for damages, collection is made from the best quality land; *uva'al chov b'veinonit* — a creditor collects from medium quality land; *uchtuvas ishah b'ziburis* — and a woman's *ketubah* is collected from the poorest quality land.

### Rebbi Meir Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אַף כְּתוּבַת אִשָּׁה בִּבֵּינוֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says: Even a woman's *ketubah*** is **collected from medium quality land.**

*Rebbi Meir* disagrees with this last ruling and holds: *af chtuvas ishah b'veinonit* — even a woman's *ketubah* is collected from medium quality land.

### Free Property Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִפְרָעִים מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּבָּדִים בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ נְכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִים, אֲפִילּוּ הֵם זִבּוּרִים. שׁוּם בֶּצַע שֶׁלּוֹ וְנִשְׁמְרוּ לוֹ נְכָסִים זִבּוּרִים, נִפְרָעִים מִן הַזִּבּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **We do not collect from sold, encumbered property when free property exists, even if the free properties are of the poorest quality.** Thus, if a debtor sold his best land but retained poor-quality land, the creditor must collect from the free poor-quality land rather than pursue the *lokeach*, the purchaser of the encumbered property.

The *Mishnah* then enumerates several laws instituted *mipnei tikkun ha'olam*, for the betterment of the world. Among them: *ein nifrahin minechasim meshubadim bimkom sheyesh nechasim b'nei chorin* — we do not collect from sold, encumbered property when free property exists, *ve'afillu hen ziburis* — even if the free properties are of the poorest quality. Thus, if a debtor sold his best land but retained poor-quality land, the creditor must collect from the free poor-quality land rather than pursue the *lokeach*, the purchaser of the encumbered property.

The *gemara* asks for the reasoning behind this rule. The answer is *mipnei tikkun ha'olam*: if buyers knew their purchased property could be seized even when the seller retained other assets, no one would be willing to purchase land. Commercial stability requires that purchasers enjoy security in their acquisitions. Therefore, as long as *b'nei chorin* — free property — remains available, the creditor must proceed there first.

### Orphan Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִפְרָעִים מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים אֶלָּא מִן הַזִּבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **We do not collect from orphans' property except from the poorest quality land.**

The *Mishnah* further states: *ein nifrahin minechasei yetomim ela min haziburis* — we do not collect from orphans' property except from the poorest quality land.

### Tikkun Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וּמַה טַּעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִשּׁוּם חִנָּם, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּהְיֶה הַדָּלֶת נִשְׁמָרֶת בִּפְנֵי הַחִנָּם. וְהוּא שֶׁלֹּא הִיה בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **GEMARA:** The *gemara* **seeks the reason** for this special treatment of orphans. **Rabbi Yochanan said: This is for the sake of grace, meaning that we wish to encourage people to lend money** to orphans **and to engage in transactions with them, while simultaneously protecting the orphans' interests.**

The *gemara* seeks the reason for this special treatment of orphans. *Amar Rebbi Yochanan*: *mishum china* — for the sake of grace, meaning that we wish to encourage people to lend money to orphans and to engage in transactions with them, while simultaneously protecting the orphans' interests so that they do not lose their finest assets. An additional reason given is that orphans are not privy to the business dealings their father may have entered into, and they are therefore extended the benefit of the doubt, with collection limited to the poorest quality land. All of this falls under the overarching principle of *tikkun ha'olam*.

## Mah Nishtanah

### Four Questions

Why is this night different from all other nights? *Shebechol haleilos anu ochlin chometz umatzah halaila hazeh kulo matzah* — on all other nights we eat *chometz* and *matzah*; on this night only *matzah*. *Shebechol haleilos anu ochlin shear yerakos halaila hazeh maror* — on all other nights we eat other vegetables; on this night *maror*. *Shebechol haleilos anu ochlin basar tzali shaluk umvushal halaila hazeh kulo tzali* — on all other nights we eat meat roasted, boiled, or cooked; on this night only roasted. *Shebechol haleilos anu matbilin paam achas halaila hazeh shtei peamim* — on all other nights we dip once; on this night twice.

### Reclining

*Shebechol haleilos anu ochlin bein yoshvin uvein msubin halaila hazeh kulanu msubin* — on all other nights we eat either sitting or reclining; on this night we all recline. Reclining is the symbol of freedom and royalty.

### Rambam Version

The *Rambam* notes that the version of *mah nishtanah* differs slightly depending on whether it is recited *bizman habayis* or *bazman hazeh*. The question regarding roasted meat, for instance, was asked only *bizman habayis*, when the *korban Pesach* was offered. These distinctions reflect the different realities of the *seder* across the two eras.

## Haggadah Narrative

### Story Elaboration

After the questions are posed, the father responds: "We were slaves to Pharaoh in Egypt," and he proceeds to narrate the entire *Haggadah*. Anyone who elaborates further on the story of what happened to *Bnei Yisrael* in Egypt is considered praiseworthy. Moreover, in every generation a person is obligated to see himself as though he himself had just gone out from the slavery of Egypt.

### Symbolic Foods

One then recites the sections explaining the significance of the *Pesach*, the *matzah*, and the *maror*. The *Pesach* represents the Almighty's passing over the houses of our ancestors in Egypt. The *maror* recalls that the Egyptians embittered the lives of our ancestors. The *matzah* commemorates the redemption. We are therefore obligated to thank, to praise, to glorify, and to extol the One who performed all these miracles for our ancestors and for us.

### Hallel & Second Cup

One then begins *Hallel*, reciting through the end of the second chapter, concluding with the words *chalamish l'maino mayim*. He then recites the *beracha* of *asher ge'alanu* — "Who has redeemed us and redeemed our ancestors from Egypt" — and concludes "Blessed are You *Hashem*, *ga'al Yisrael*, Who has redeemed Israel." He then drinks the second cup while reclining on his left side.

## Seder Meal Order

### Hamotzi & Brachot

*Halacha* 2: After the second cup, one recites the *beracha* of *hamotzi lechem min ha'aretz* over the whole *matzah*, and then recites *al achilas matzah* over the broken piece.

### Matzah & Maror

One then takes a *kezayit* from the whole *matzah* and a *kezayit* from the broken piece and eats them together. He subsequently recites *al achilas maror*, dips the *maror* in *charoses*, and eats it — being careful not to leave the *maror* in the *charoses* for too long, lest the taste of the *maror* be nullified. He then wraps *matzah* and *maror* together, dips them in *charoses*, and eats them without an additional *beracha*.

### Afikoman

*Halacha* 3: He then eats his meal, eating and drinking as he wishes. At the conclusion of the meal he eats of the meat of the *Pesach* — at least a *kezayit* — and eats nothing after it. *Bazman hazeh*, in our times, he eats a *kezayit* of *matzah* at the meal's end and eats nothing afterward, so that the taste of the *matzah* — or, in Temple times, the taste of the *Pesach* — lingers in his mouth.

### Brachot & Cups

*Halacha* 4: He then recites *Birchat HaMazon* on the third cup and drinks it. They pour the fourth cup, he completes *Hallel* and recites *Birchat HaShir*, which is the *beracha* of *yehalelucha Hashem Elokeinu*. He then recites the *beracha* on the fourth cup and drinks nothing afterward.

## Limud & Kinyan

### Learning Modes

Returning to the *beis hamedrash* — to finding one's *limud*, one's *seder*, and one's proper place within the world of Torah study — whether one is learning an *amud* of *gemara* per day, a *daf*, or studying *b'iyun*, the essential goal remains the same: to make a *kinyan*.

### Kinyan Concept

When we think about what a *kinyan* means — *shtar*, *kessef*, and all the formal modes of acquisition — the most important dimension is that one truly owns the material, that one genuinely understands it. This means having a *biyur* on every single word one says, truly internalizing and possessing what one has learned.

## Chatzot Timing

### Moshe's Caution

*David HaMelech* would say: *ani me'orer hashachar ve'ein hashachar me'orer osi* — I awaken the dawn, and the dawn does not awaken me. The *gemara* asks: what was the difference between *Moshe Rabbeinu* and *David HaMelech*? Why did *Moshe Rabbeinu* say *kechatzos* — "approximately midnight" — whereas *David HaMelech* knew the precise moment of *chatzos*? The *gemara* answers that *Moshe Rabbeinu* in fact knew exactly when *chatzos* was. He said *kechatzos* because he was concerned that the *itztagninei Paroh*, the astrologers of *Paroh*, might miscalculate the moment and then declare that *Moshe* was a *badai* — a liar.

### David's Kinnor

*David HaMelech*, by contrast, possessed the *kinnor*. The *kinnor* informed him of the exact moment of *chatzos*.

### Torah Learning

With that precise knowledge, *David HaMelech* was able to rise at exactly *chatzos* and engage in *Torah* learning until the break of morning.
