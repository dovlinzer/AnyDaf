# Beitzah 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review: Melacha YT

### New Perek Intro

The present *daf* is 25. We back up slightly and pick up from the bottom of 24b in order to review two important points that arose over the preceding days at the opening of the new *perek*.

### Catching Fish/Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹחֲטִין מִן הַנְּגָרִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, אֲבָל לֹא מִן הָרְשָׁתוֹת וּמִן הַמִּכְמוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **One may slaughter** animals **from** pens containing **pools** of drinking water **on a Festival, but not from** those found caught in **nets or** in **traps,** as they may have been caught on the Festival itself.

The new *perek* introduces the principle that one may not catch fish or wild animals on *Yom Tov*. This immediately generates an important discussion among the *Rishonim*: if one is permitted to *shecht* animals on *Yom Tov*, why is catching forbidden? And this leads to the broader question of whether it is truly the case that any *melacha* associated with the preparation of food is permitted on *Yom Tov*.

### Rashi's Approach

*Rashi*'s basic answer is that the problem with catching is that one could have done it beforehand — one could have taken care of it from *erev Yom Tov*. This implies that only *melachos* that could not have been attended to before *Yom Tov* are permitted, or at least that performing a *melacha* which was deferrable raises a question of biblical prohibition.

### Rishonim Challenge

The *Rishonim* challenge this position. They argue that if something is directly related to food preparation — not in an indirect or merely preparatory sense, but genuinely direct — then even if one could have attended to it beforehand, it remains permitted on *Yom Tov*. There is also a broader difficulty with *Rashi*'s framework: it is not clear that deferrability alone would render a *melacha* biblically forbidden. After all, grinding spices is permitted on *Yom Tov* even though one could presumably have ground them the day before, because freshly ground spices are of superior quality — but that is a difference in degree, not kind. In any case, *Rashi*'s approach is effectively rejected by the *Rishonim* on the weight of evidence against it.

### Tosafot/Yerushalmi

*Tosafos* quotes the famous *Yerushalmi* that connects the laws of *melacha* on *Yom Tov* to the verse *ushmartem es hamatzos*, juxtaposing *melacha* with the duty to guard the *matzos* from becoming *chametz*. The point at which one must guard the dough is when the flour is added to the water — the kneading. The implication, as the *Yerushalmi* develops it, is that only *melachos* from the kneading (*lisha*) onward are permitted on *Yom Tov*. Trapping, which occurs far earlier in the food-production process, falls outside this range. The difficulty, however, is that we find in the *Gemara* that *borer* under certain immediate circumstances and grinding of spices are permitted — and these *melachos* occur in the flour-processing stage, before any analog to kneading. This creates a tension with the *Yerushalmi*'s framework and requires a reconceptualization of what *milisha va'eilach* actually means.

### Ramban Framework

The *Ramban* provides what is probably the most compelling resolution. His formulation appears to be stated at the level of *de'oraisa*. He notes that two distinct *pesukim* permit food-related activity on *Yom Tov*: one speaks of *ach asher ye'aseh lechol nefesh* — things done for the sake of food — and the other, in contexts where that explicit permission is absent, uses the phrase *meleches avodah* rather than the categorical *melacha* of *Shabbos*. The *Ramban* focuses precisely on this term *meleches avodah*.

His distinction is not a categorical list of prohibited *melachos* but a contextual one. *Meleches avodah* refers to work performed out in the field, on a large scale, over extended periods — *leyamim harbeh* — as a major processing operation. By contrast, work done in the home or kitchen in immediate preparation for eating is not *meleches avodah* and is therefore permitted. Thus grinding wheat into flour is *meleches avodah*, but grinding spices for freshly seasoned food tonight is a home-based, kitchen-based activity and is not. Catching wild animals belongs to that prior, large-scale category of field work — it is not the kind of thing one does in the immediate context of preparing a meal. Indeed, the *Ramban* adds that anyone living a domesticated life would plan to have meat caught days in advance rather than depend on a successful hunt the same day.

### Ran's View

The *Ran* finds himself sympathetic to the *Ramban*'s type of distinction but ultimately cannot accept it as a framework operating at the biblical level. His difficulty is that if the distinction were biblical, it ought to track clear categories of *melacha* rather than the context in which a given *melacha* is performed. He therefore concludes that all these distinctions are rabbinic — that biblically one would be permitted to go out and catch animals, harvest wheat, and grind it on *Yom Tov*, and that it is only rabbinic legislation that drew the line. The majority of *Rishonim*, however, hold that these distinctions do operate at the biblical level, and the *Ramban*'s conceptualization remains the most coherent account of where the line falls.

## Non-Jew Bringing Items

### Rav Pappa Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הִלְכְתָא: גּוֹי שֶׁהֵבִיא דּוֹרוֹן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּיוֹם טוֹב, אִם יֵשׁ מֵאוֹתוֹ הַמִּין בִּמְחוּבָּר — אָסוּר. וְלָעֶרֶב נָמֵי אֲסוּרִין בִּכְדֵי שֶׁיֵּעָשׂוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that **the *halakha*** in this regard **is** as follows: In the case of **a gentile who brought a gift to a Jew on a Festival, if there is of that species** still **attached** to a tree or the ground, **it is prohibited** to be eaten, as it may be assumed that the gentile picked it that same day. **And in the evening as well,** after the conclusion of the Festival, **it is prohibited for** the period of **time** needed **for its preparation,** i.e., the period of time necessary to detach it from the tree or the ground, as one may not derive benefit from a prohibited labor that was performed on a Festival on behalf of a Jew.

Turning now to the other important topic under discussion: the case of a non-Jew who brings items on *Yom Tov*. The *Gemara* records the ruling of Rav Pappa: *hilcheta*, *nochri sheheivi doron leyisrael beyom tov* — if a non-Jew brings a gift for a Jew on *Yom Tov* — *im yesh me'uto hamin bimechubar assur* — if that type of produce is currently in a state where it could have been harvested that day, it is forbidden.

### Muktzeh Concerns

The reason in this first case is *muktzeh*. Since we have already established that *safek muchan* is *assur*, one must assume the produce was attached to the ground and harvested on *Yom Tov* itself, rendering it *muktzeh*.

### Post-YT Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אֵין מֵאוֹתוֹ הַמִּין בִּמְחוּבָּר, תּוֹךְ הַתְּחוּם — מוּתָּר, חוּץ לַתְּחוּם — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if none of that species is** still **attached** to the ground, then if the gift was brought from **within the limit,** i.e., the distance one may travel on a Festival, **it is permitted,** as no prohibited labor has been performed. If, however, the gift was brought from **outside the limit, it is prohibited.**

The *Gemara* continues: *le'erev nami assurin* — even in the evening after *Yom Tov*, when *muktzeh* is no longer a concern, the produce remains forbidden. The reason is *bichdei sheye'aseh* — one must wait after *Yom Tov* the amount of time it would have taken to complete the work had it been done at that point.

### Bikhdei Sheya'asu

An interesting question arises as to how one measures *bikhdei sheya'asu* in practice. If the non-Jew brings a hundred bushels of apples, does one measure by how long it would take a crew to harvest them, or by the time for the actual *melacha* of picking a single apple, or by the time it would take to transport the goods to the recipient's location? This question of quantification is left open, but the operative principle is clear: one cannot benefit from these items until one has waited, after *Yom Tov*, the amount of time that would have been required to produce them.

### Techumim Concern

The second case: *ve'im ein me'uto hamin bimechubar* — if it is past the harvest season for that species, so there is no concern that the items were picked today. Here there is no *muktzeh* issue. On *Yom Tov*, the only remaining concern is *techumim* — the possibility that the non-Jew brought the items from beyond the *techum*. If he brought them from within the *techum*, they are permitted (*toch hatchum mutar*). If he brought them from beyond the *techum* (*chutz latchum*) — this appears at the top of 25a — they are forbidden, again with the same rule of *bichdei sheye'aseh*.

### Jew-to-Jew Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַבָּא בִּשְׁבִיל יִשְׂרָאֵל זֶה — מוּתָּר לְיִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** And an item **that came** from outside the limit **for one Jew is permitted to another Jew.** No prohibition applies to the second recipient, as the gentile's intention was not fulfilled. Since the *halakha* of limits is a rabbinic prohibition, the Sages decreed that the object is prohibited only to the one on behalf of whom it was brought, but not to others.

So even when *muktzeh* is not at issue, there remains a principle that one cannot immediately benefit from what a non-Jew did in violation of *Yom Tov* restrictions. There is, however, an exception: *ve'haba bishvil Yisrael zeh mutar leyisrael acher* — if the non-Jew brought the items for one particular Jew, another Jew is permitted to benefit from them. The significance of this exception will be clarified in the discussion of the underlying rationale below.

## Rashi: Neheneh

### Neheneh Concept

Two major approaches are offered to explain why a Jew cannot benefit from what a non-Jew brought for him. *Rashi* ties the case to the concept found in the laws of *Shabbos* of being *neheneh* from *melachet nochri* — deriving benefit from *melacha* performed by a non-Jew on *Shabbos* for a Jew. If a non-Jew cooks food for a Jew on *Shabbos* — even entirely of his own initiative, without being asked — the Jew is forbidden to benefit from it. This principle, says *Rashi*, applies equally when the non-Jew performs a *melacha* for a Jew on *Yom Tov*.

### Rashi Logic

This explains the extension of the prohibition into *motzaei Yom Tov* as well. Normally, once *Shabbos* ends, one may derive benefit from what a non-Jew did. *Rashi* explains that the waiting period of *bikhdei sheya'asu* is required because during that window one is still effectively deriving benefit from the *Shabbos* violation: had the *melacha* not been performed on *Shabbos*, one would have had to wait at least that long after *Shabbos* for it to be completed. By benefiting within that time, one is benefiting from the fact that it was done on *Shabbos*. Only once *bikhdei sheya'asu* has elapsed can one be said to derive no benefit from the violation itself.

By this logic, *Rashi* also explains the *Jew-to-Jew* exception: since in the *techum* case the violation is only rabbinic, the Sages were lenient and permitted another Jew to benefit.

### Ramban Refinement

The *Ramban* sharpens the conceptual analysis further. The principle of being *neheneh* from *melacha* on *Shabbos* applies, he argues, only where the *melacha* produced something new — a new reality that did not previously exist. Cooked food is a new reality; the *Shabbos* violation created something that was not there before.

### Carrying Exception

Carrying, by contrast, does not create a new reality. The object is exactly what it was; it has merely changed location. Accordingly, there is a genuine debate among the *poskim* as to whether the concept of *neheneh mimelachet Shabbat* applies to carrying at all. The item is now accessible to the beneficiary, which is something new for him — but the object itself is unchanged.

### Techumim Weakest

*Techumim* is an even weaker case than carrying, because the issue with *techumim* is not even the physical relocation of an object so much as a question of one's position relative to where the object was. Other Jews theoretically had access to the same items; it is merely a matter of the recipient's location versus the item's location. This, says the *Ramban*, is why the *Gemara* goes more lenient in the *techum* case and permits another Jew to derive benefit — particularly since hypothetically any Jew within range of the items could have had access to them.

## Tosafot Reject Rashi

### Non-Jew More Problem

*Tosafos*, the *Ramban*, and the other *Rishonim* reject *Rashi*'s approach entirely, and this rejection is the position accepted *le-halacha*. Their argument is that the concept of *neheneh mimelachet Shabbat* cannot be what is driving the ruling here. The reason is that this concept — benefiting from a *Shabbos* violation — applies equally when a Jew performs the *melacha* and when a non-Jew does, yet we find that *bikhdei sheya'asu* applies uniquely to the case of a non-Jew.

### Risk of Request

Specifically, when a Jew violates *Shabbos* and, say, picks an apple for another Jew, that apple is forbidden to the violator but *immediately* permitted to others after *Shabbos*. There is no requirement to wait *bikhdei sheya'asu*. *Bikhdei sheya'asu* applies only when a non-Jew performed the *melacha*. Why the difference? Because the concern in the case of the non-Jew is not merely conceptual — the worry that one has derived benefit from a violation — but entirely practical: if one is permitted to benefit immediately after *Yom Tov* from what the non-Jew did, one will arrange in advance for the non-Jew to perform *melacha* on *Yom Tov* so that the benefit is available immediately at *motzaei Yom Tov*. It is this proactive arrangement that the Rabbis sought to prevent.

### Modern Application

This concern has a practical dimension in later *halachic* discussion as well. Some *poskim* note, in connection with hospital Shabbos scheduling, that one should not specifically assign a non-observant Jewish doctor to a *Shabbos* shift on the reasoning that "he doesn't keep *Shabbos* anyway." On the contrary — an observant Jewish doctor taking the shift will be careful to perform only what *halacha* permits, while the other arrangement risks producing precisely the casual attitude toward *Shabbos melacha* that the Rabbis were concerned about.

### Non-Jew Only Rule

To summarize: the prohibition of *bikhdei sheya'asu* does not apply to *melacha* done by a Jew. It applies specifically to *melacha* done by a non-Jew, because only in that case is there a risk of proactively arranging for forbidden work to be done for one's benefit.

### Two-Day YT Debate

This analysis generates an important practical *nafka mina* regarding two-day *Yom Tov*. If the non-Jew performs *melacha* for a Jew on the first day of *Yom Tov*, may the Jew benefit from it on the second day within *bikhdei sheya'asu*?

### Rashi vs Tosafot

*Rashi* would say yes. His reasoning flows from *sefeika deyoma*: if yesterday were *chol*, the non-Jew was permitted to pick the apples; if yesterday were *Yom Tov*, then today is *chol* and one could hypothetically have had the apples picked during the hours since the second day began. Either way, one is not benefiting from a *Yom Tov* violation, because whichever day was truly *Yom Tov*, there exists a hypothetical scenario in which the work could have been done on the other day. Therefore, by *Rashi*'s logic, two hours into the second day one may already benefit.

*Tosafos* rejects this. For *Tosafos*, the concern has nothing to do with the conceptual question of whether one is benefiting from a violation. The concern is purely practical: will this arrangement lead you to ask the non-Jew to work for you? And the answer is clearly yes. If you are permitted to benefit on day two from what the non-Jew did on day one, you will arrange for exactly that next year. The *shakla ve'tarya* of *sefeika deyoma* is intellectually elegant but practically irrelevant to the concern at hand. One must therefore wait until after *Yom Tov* entirely before benefiting. The *halacha* follows the interpretation of *Tosafos*.

## Fish Channel Case

### Sluice Gate Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: הַסּוֹכֵר אַמַּת הַמַּיִם מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב, וּלְמָחָר הִשְׁכִּים וּמָצָא בָּהּ דָּגִים — מוּתָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion about trapping animals on a Festival. **Rabba bar Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** If **one dammed a water channel** that was used to irrigate a field **on the eve of a Festival, and the following day he arose and found fish in it, they are permitted.** These fish presumably arrived with the water before the Festival and were unable to escape, as the channel had been obstructed. Therefore, the fish are considered as having been prepared before the Festival.

The *Gemara* now presents the ruling of Rabbah bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *hasocher amat hamayim me'erev Yom Tov* — if one closed off an irrigation channel or waterway with sluice gates on *erev Yom Tov*, creating a closed system, and the next morning on *Yom Tov* one found fish in it — they are permitted.

### Fish Already Caught

The reasoning is that since the system was closed from before *Yom Tov*, anything inside is considered already caught. The fish were effectively secured from *erev Yom Tov* and are therefore *muchan* — prepared and designated from before *Yom Tov* — and may be used.

## Wild Animal Nesting

### Rav Chisda Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא, מִדִּבְרֵי רַבֵּינוּ נִלְמוֹד: חַיָּה שֶׁקִּנְּנָה בְּפַרְדֵּס — אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה זִמּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: From** this **statement of our teacher,** Rav, **we learn** that **an undomesticated animal that nested in an orchard** near one's house **does not require** special **designation** but is considered prepared.

Rav Chisda draws an inference from this ruling: *chaya shekinina bepardes* — a wild animal, say a deer, that has made its nest in a garden — *eina tzericha zimmun* — does not require prior verbal designation (*zimmun*). The reasoning by analogy is that just as the fish caught in a closed channel are considered *muchan* without requiring special designation, so too a young wild animal that has settled in one's garden is considered effectively caught and *muchan* without any act of *zimmun*.

### Rav Nachman Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: נְפַל חַבְרִין, בְּרַבְרְבָתָא. הָתָם לָא קָא עָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה, הָכָא קָא עָבֵיד מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** in response to this conclusion: **Our colleague,** Rav Ḥisda, **has fallen upon a great** matter, i.e., an issue that is not at all straightforward but is the subject of various disagreements. Rav Naḥman explains the difference between the cases: **There,** in the case where the undomesticated animal nested in an orchard, the person **performed no action** at all, as the animal came of its own accord, whereas **here,** in the case where he dammed the water channel, **he performed an action** when he blocked the water from leaving the channel.

Rav Nachman objects that Rav Chisda — or, according to the alternative version, Rabbah bar Rav Huna himself, in which case it is Rav Nachman who notes that the son of their colleague got himself into a debate of *gedolim* — has made an inference that does not hold. One cannot simply move from the fish case to the wild animal case.

### Active vs Passive

The distinction is clear: *hatam lo ka avid ma'aseh* — in the wild animal case, the owner did nothing. The animal simply wandered into the garden and nested on its own. Even if the animal is practically inaccessible and one is already thinking of using it, what has the owner actually done to establish that he intends to use it? There is no act that reflects any planning. *Hacha ka avid ma'aseh* — in the fish case, by contrast, the owner actively closed the sluice gates, performing a deliberate act that caught the fish. That active step both secures the fish and reflects the owner's intention to use them.

### Shmaya V'Abtalyon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא בָּעֲיָא זִמּוּן? וְהָתַנְיָא: חַיָּה שֶׁקִּנְּנָה בְּפַרְדֵּס — צְרִיכָה לְזַמֵּן, וְצִפּוֹר דְּרוֹר — צָרִךְ לִקְשׁוֹר בִּכְנָפֶיהָ כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְחַלֵּף בְּאִמָּהּ. וְזוֹ עֵדוּת שֶׁהֵעִידוּ מִפִּי שְׁמַעְיָה וְאַבְטַלְיוֹן. תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman concludes his challenge: **And does** the animal **not require** further **designation? Isn't it taught** explicitly in a *baraita*: **An undomesticated animal that nested in an orchard requires designation. And** as for **a free bird, one is required to tie its wings so that it will not be mistaken for its mother,** i.e., so that he not take a different bird, such as its mother, in its place. **And this** is **a testimony that was reported in the name of Shemaya and Avtalyon.** The Gemara comments: Indeed, this is **a conclusive refutation** of the Sages who drew an inference from Rav's statement.

The *Gemara* brings a *braita* as evidence that in the wild animal case, *zimmun* is indeed required: *chaya shekinina bepardes tzericha lezamen* — a wild animal that has taken up lodging in a garden must be verbally designated before *Yom Tov*. The *braita* also notes that a *tzippor dror* — a particular species of bird — must have its wings bound so as not to be confused with its mother, since the young of this species resemble the parent closely in size. This testimony is traced back to the authority of *Shmaya ve'Abtalyon*, the teachers of Hillel and Shammai. The *Gemara* declares this a *tiyufta* — a refutation of the inference that no *zimmun* is needed.

### Bird Wing Binding

The wing-binding requirement for the *tzippor dror* is not an act of *zimmun* in itself but rather a practical measure: *kedei shelo titchalef be'imah* — so that the designated bird not be confused with its mother. Even after one has performed *zimmun* on the young bird, without binding its wings one cannot reliably identify which bird was designated.

### Counter-Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי בָּעֲיָא זִמּוּן? וְהָתַנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: מוֹדִים בֵּית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל עַל שֶׁהִזְמִינָן בְּתוֹךְ הַקֵּן וּמָצָא לִפְנֵי הַקֵּן — שֶׁאֲסוּרִין. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּיוֹנֵי שׁוֹבָךְ וְיוֹנֵי עֲלִיָּה, וְצִפֳּרִים שֶׁקִּנְּנוּ בִּטְפִיחִין וּבְבִירָה. אֲבָל אֲווֹזִים וְתַרְנְגוֹלִים וְיוֹנֵי הַרְדָּסִיָּאוֹת וְחַיָּה שֶׁקִּנְּנָה בְּפַרְדֵּס — מוּתָּרִין, וְאֵין צְרִיכִין זִמּוּן. וְצִפּוֹר דְּרוֹר צְרִיכָה לְקַשֵּׁר בִּכְנָפֶיהָ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּתְחַלֵּף בְּאִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does** an undomesticated animal in an orchard really **require designation? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar said: Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel concede** with regard to doves and the like **that one had designated** on the eve of a Festival when they were **inside the nest, and** on the Festival itself **he found** them **in front of the nest, that** they **are prohibited,** as these doves might not be the same ones that he had designated but rather others that came from somewhere else? **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is **with regard to doves of a dovecote, doves of a loft, and birds nesting in pitchers or in a building. However, geese, chicken, domestic doves, and an undomesticated animal that nested in an orchard are permitted** in all cases, **and they do not require designation** in advance. In the case of **a free bird, one is required to tie its wings so that it is not mistaken for its mother.**

The *Gemara* immediately complicates this picture. A second *braita* records Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's statement that both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel agree that if one designated birds inside a nest and then found birds outside the nest, those outside birds are forbidden — because one cannot be certain they are the same ones that were designated. This rule applies to *yonei shovach* and *yonei aliyah* (birds of the coop and the loft) and birds that have nested in crevices in walls. These are species one would not automatically assume one plans to eat, and so they require *zimmun*.

However, the *braita* then distinguishes: *avazin ve'tarnigolin* — ducks and chickens — and *yonei hordisiyot* (Herodian pigeons kept regularly for eating), and *chayah shekinena bepardes* — a wild animal that has taken up lodging in one's garden — *muttarin*, they are permitted without any *zimmun*. Here, then, we have a direct contradiction: one *braita* in the name of *Shmaya ve'Abtalyon* requires *zimmun* for the nesting wild animal, while this second *braita* explicitly exempts it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְקוּשָּׁרִים וְהַמְנוּעֲנָעִין, בְּבוֹרוֹת וּבְבָתִּים וּבְשִׁיחִין וּבִמְעָרוֹת — מוּתָּרִין, וּבְאִילָנוֹת — אֲסוּרִין, שֶׁמָּא יַעֲלֶה וְיִתְלוֹשׁ. וְהַמְקוּשָּׁרִין וְהַמְנוּעֲנָעִין — בְּכׇל מָקוֹם אֲסוּרִין, מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to **those** birds **whose** wings **were tied** as a sign, **and** similarly, **those that were shaken** in advance in order to designate them for the Festival, if they were **in cisterns, in houses, in trenches, or in caves, they are permitted** on the Festival. However, if they were **in trees, they are prohibited, lest one climb up** the tree **and detach** something from it, which is prohibited. **And those** birds **whose** wings **were tied** by another, **and** similarly, **those that were shaken** by another **are prohibited in all places,** even not on a Festival, **due to** the prohibition against **stealing.** Tying or shaking is considered an act of acquisition, and therefore others may not take them.

The *braita* also reiterates that the *tzippor dror* must have its wings bound even after *zimmun*, so as not to be confused with its mother. It further notes that birds found bound or obviously handled (*keshurim u'vimnu'im*) — and birds found in wells, cisterns (*shichin*), or caves — are permitted, while those found in trees are forbidden even if previously designated, because accessing them would require climbing and potentially breaking branches, which constitutes *uvda d'chol*. And birds that have been tagged by others are forbidden everywhere on account of *gezel*.

### Mother vs Baby

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בַּהּ, הָא בְּאִמַּהּ. אִמַּהּ בְּזִמּוּן סַגִּי לַהּ? צֵידָה מְעַלְּיוּתָא בָּעֲיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which teaches that an animal nesting in an orchard is permitted even without designation, is referring **to it,** a young animal that cannot escape; whereas **that** *baraita*, which states that designation is required, is referring **to its mother,** which is larger and can escape. The Gemara asks: But is mere **designation sufficient for its mother?** Doesn't **it require complete capture,** as it is an undomesticated animal?

Rav Nachman initially suggests resolving the contradiction by distinguishing between the mother deer and the baby: it is the mother that requires *zimmun*, and the baby that does not. The *Gemara* presses him: how can *zimmun* alone suffice for the mother? It is not simply a *muktzeh* question — *tzeida me'alya ba'ya* — the mother is a fully mobile wild animal; one would need to actually trap her. A mere verbal designation does not address the fact that catching her requires a genuine act of *tzida*. Indeed, up to this point in the discussion one might have thought that the *kinyan chatzer* of the garden treats even the mother as effectively caught, but the *Gemara* now reveals that this assumption is untenable: the mother deer will not wait around to be caught simply because she has nested in a domesticated space. Only the young animal, which cannot easily escape, can be considered effectively caught by virtue of its presence in the owner's domain.

### Garden Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּדִידַהּ. הָא בְּגִנָּה הַסְּמוּכָה לָעִיר, הָא בְּגִנָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ סְמוּכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that **both this** *baraita* **and that** one are referring **to it,** a young animal that is unable to escape, and the difference between them is as follows: **This** *baraita*, which does not require designation, is referring **to a garden** situated **near the city,** so that one knows precisely where the animal is located and he can take it at any time. **That** *baraita*, which requires designation, is referring **to a garden that is not** located **nearby.**

The *Gemara* therefore accepts the resolution of Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *idi ve'idi bedida* — in both contradicting *braisos* we are discussing the young animal, not the mother. The question of *zimmun* is solely a *muktzeh* issue, not a trapping issue. The difference between the two *braisos* is one of location: *ha begina smucha le'ir ha begina she'eina smucha* — one *braita* addresses a garden close to the city (effectively the owner's backyard), and the other addresses a garden at some distance. If the young deer is in one's backyard, one may presume one was aware of it and planned to use it; no verbal *zimmun* is required. If it is in a more distant orchard or garden, *zimmun* is required.

## Mesukenet Animal

### Mesukenet Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּהֵמָה מְסוּכֶּנֶת — לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם לֶאֱכוֹל מִמֶּנָּה כְּזַיִת צָלִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ כְּזַיִת חַי מִבֵּית טְבִיחָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **an animal** is **in danger** of dying, in which case its meat would be prohibited as the animal had not been properly slaughtered, and one wishes to slaughter it in the hope that it will be found fit for eating and he will be spared a loss, **he may not slaughter** it on a Festival **unless there is** still **time in the day** for him **to eat an olive-bulk of roasted meat from** the animal, so that it is possible to say that he slaughtered the animal for the sake of the Festival. **Rabbi Akiva says:** There need not be enough time for him to roast it; rather, it is sufficient **even** if there is only time to eat **an olive-bulk of raw** meat **from the place where** the animal **is slaughtered,** i.e., from its neck, without going to the trouble of removing its hide and roasting it.

The *Mishnah* now takes up the case of a *behema mesukenet* — an animal in danger of imminent death, whether from old age or illness. Such an animal is not a *treifa* (which would render *shechita* ineffective), but if it dies before *shechita*, it becomes a *neveilah*. One therefore wishes to *shecht* it on *Yom Tov* not so much because one needs an extra portion of meat that day but in order to preserve the animal's *kashrus* status. The *Mishnah* rules: *lo yishchot ella im ken yesh shahut bayom le'echol mimena kezayit tzli* — one may not *shecht* the *mesukenet* unless there is sufficient time remaining in the day to eat at least one roasted *kezayit* from it on *Yom Tov*. Only then can the *shechita* be said to have been performed, at least in part, for the sake of *Yom Tov* itself.

Notably, the *Mishnah* does not require that one actually go ahead and eat that *kezayit*, but only that there be time to do so in theory. How much of a *lecha'tchila* requirement this imposes — and to what extent eating the *kezayit* is merely a formal pretext versus a genuine expression of *Yom Tov* need — remains a question.

### Rabi Akiva Raw

Rabbi Akiva offers a more lenient position: *afilu kezayit chai mibeis tevichaso* — even a raw *kezayit* of meat from the site of the *shechita* suffices. Since one could in principle eat raw meat, and one can immediately access meat from the neck of the animal right where the *shechita* was performed, this minimal possibility of consumption is enough to validate the *shechita* for *Yom Tov* purposes.

### Field Shechita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁחָטָהּ בַּשָּׂדֶה — לֹא יְבִיאֶנָּה בְּמוֹט וּבְמוֹטָה, אֲבָל מֵבִיא בְּיָדוֹ אֵבָרִים אֵבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **one slaughtered** an animal on a Festival **in the field, he may not bring it** to his house **on a pole or on a set of poles** carried by two people, as this appears similar to a weekday activity. **Rather,** he must alter his usual weekday manner of performing this action **and bring** it in **by hand, limb by limb.**

The *Mishnah* addresses a further case: *shachata basadeh* — if one *shecht* the animal out in a field. One may not bring it back using carrying beams or poles, in the manner of people transporting a whole slaughtered animal from the market — that would constitute *uvda d'chol*, weekday-like activity. Instead, one must bring it back section by section by hand (*evarim evarim*). This is more laborious (*tircha*) but avoids the appearance of a commercial weekday transaction.

## Flaying Dismember

### Torah Flaying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִתּוּחַ בְּעוֹלָה, וְהוּא הַדִּין לְקַצָּבִים. מִכָּאן לִמְּדָה תּוֹרָה דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ: שֶׁלֹּא יֹאכַל אָדָם בָּשָׂר קוֹדֶם הֶפְשֵׁט וְנִתּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rami bar Abba said:** The mitzva of **flaying and cutting** the animal into pieces is mentioned in the Torah with regard **to the burnt-offering, and the same is true for butchers.** That is to say, we learn from the *halakhot* of the burnt offering that a butcher should first remove the hide and cut the animal into pieces. **From here the Torah taught** proper **etiquette, that a person should not eat meat before flaying and cutting** the animal into pieces.

Rami bar Abba observes that the *Torah* mentions flaying (*hefsheit*) and dismemberment (*nituach*) in the context of the *korban olah* — *hiphshit es ha'olah ve'nitach osah linetacheha* — and states that the same principle applies to butchers: one should not eat meat before the animal has been flayed and dismembered. The teaching is introduced as: *mikan limdah Torah derech eretz shelo yochal adam basar kodem hefsheit venituach* — from here the *Torah* teaches proper conduct, that one should not eat meat before it has been flayed and cut up. The *Gemara* will consider whether this is purely a matter of *derech eretz* or whether it carries *halachic* implications.

### Derech Eretz Not Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (לְאַפּוֹקֵי) מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? אִילֵּימָא לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּהֵמָה בְּחַיֶּיהָ — בְּחֶזְקַת אִיסּוּר עוֹמֶדֶת, עַד שֶׁיִּוָּדַע לָךְ בַּמֶּה נִשְׁחֲטָה. נִשְׁחֲטָה — בְּחֶזְקַת הֶיתֵּר עוֹמֶדֶת, עַד שֶׁיִּוָּדַע לָךְ בַּמֶּה נִטְרְפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** new *halakha* **is** Rami bar Abba **teaching us? If we say** that he wishes **to exclude** the opinion **of Rav Huna,** there is a difficulty, **as Rav Huna said: An animal, while alive, retains its presumptive status of** being subject to the **prohibition** against eating a limb from a living creature, and it retains that status after slaughter **until it becomes known to you how it was slaughtered.** As long as it has not yet been clarified that the animal was slaughtered properly, it is presumed to be prohibited. However, once the animal has been **slaughtered** properly, **it retains its presumptive status of being permitted until it becomes known to you how it became a *tereifa*,** an animal suffering from a wound or illness that would cause it to die within twelve months, which is prohibited to be eaten even after ritual slaughter. There is no need to conduct a special examination of the animal to determine if it had a defect or illness, as it is presumed to be permitted. Even if a defect is found, this does not necessarily render the animal a *tereifa*, as one can say that it appeared only after the animal was slaughtered.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ka mashma lan* — what is this teaching us? Could it be that Rami bar Abba is coming to reject the position of Rav Huna? The answer, as the *Gemara* establishes, is that his teaching is not *halachic* in nature. Rather, as Rami bar Abba himself indicates, he is teaching *derech eretz* — proper conduct — not a *halacha*.

### Rav Huna Issur

Rav Huna's position is as follows: *behemah bechayeha bechezkas issur omedes ad sheyivada lecha bemah shechitah* — a living animal stands in a presumptive status of prohibition (*chezkas issur*) until one definitively knows that it was *shecht* properly. The significance of this goes beyond the obvious requirement to *shecht*: it means that in a case of *safek* — for instance, if there is uncertainty whether a nick in the knife occurred before or after the *shechita* — one cannot remove the *chezkas issur* without definitive knowledge that the *shechita* was valid. (*Tosafos* formulates a distinct prohibition called *eino zavuach* — the state of not having been *shecht*, derived from the positive commandment *ve'zavachta ve'achalta* — which applies whether the animal is alive or dead, further grounding the *chezkas issur* framework.)

### After Shechita Heter

Once one knows definitively that the *shechita* was performed correctly — the knife examined, the act observed and confirmed — the animal shifts to a *chezkas heter*: *ad sheyivada lecha bemah nitreifah* — it is presumptively permitted until one knows for certain that it is a *treifa*. This means one is not required to examine every organ for potential *treifos*; any doubt is resolved in favor of *heter* unless one has positive knowledge of a *treifa*.

### Meiyut Threshold

This is not, however, the full picture as the *halacha* ultimately stands. A distinction is drawn between *mi'ut she'eino matzui* — an uncommon defect occurring in fewer than approximately 5% of cases — and *mi'ut hamatzui* — a defect occurring at that threshold or above. For defects in the former category, the *chezkas heter* operates fully and no checking is required even when checking is possible. But once a defect occurs with sufficient regularity, if it is possible to check, one must check. This is why the lungs are examined: problems in the lungs occur often enough to qualify as *mi'ut hamatzui*, and since the lungs are accessible, they must be inspected. If the lungs are lost after *shechita*, however — for instance, if an animal made off with them — the animal remains permitted, because the *chezkas heter* holds in the absence of positive knowledge of *treifus*.

### Flaying Question

Given this framework, the *Gemara* considers whether Rami bar Abba's teaching about flaying and dismemberment might carry *halachic* content: perhaps requiring flaying and dismemberment before eating is meant to mandate checking for *treifos* before consuming the meat.

### No Checking Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: מְקוֹם טְבִיחָתָהּ מַמָּשׁ. אֶלָּא, רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא אוֹרַח אַרְעָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach: From the actual place where** the animal **is slaughtered? Rather,** the Gemara rejects the previous explanation and says that **Rami bar Abba** merely **teaches us** proper **etiquette,** even though no prohibition is involved.

The *Gemara* rejects this interpretation. The *Mishnah* itself supports Rav Huna's position that there is no *halachic* requirement to check before eating: *de-tenan*, Rabbi Akiva says one may eat even a raw *kezayit* from the site of the *shechita* — before any flaying, dismemberment, or inspection. (The *Gemara* briefly considers whether *mibeis tevichaso* means literally from the neck area, or — as one reading suggests — from the stomach meat, something easier to eat raw; Rabbi Chiya's *braita* confirms the literal reading: it means the actual place of *shechita*, the neck meat.) Thus there is no *halachic* objection to eating before checking.

### Ravenousness Point

Accordingly, the conclusion is that Rami bar Abba's teaching is precisely what it appeared to be at the outset: *orchis de-aretz kamashma lan* — he is imparting proper conduct. The point is not *halacha*; it is the admonition against ravenousness. One should not tear at the meat before it has been properly flayed and cut up, simply because one cannot restrain oneself.

## Derech Eretz

### Garlic Onion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא: לֹא יֹאכַל אָדָם שׁוּם וּבָצָל מֵרֹאשׁוֹ, אֶלָּא מֵעָלָיו. וְאִם אָכַל — הֲרֵי זֶה רְעַבְתָּן. כַּיּוֹצֵא בוֹ: לֹא יִשְׁתֶּה אָדָם כּוֹסוֹ בְּבַת אַחַת, וְאִם שָׁתָה — הֲרֵי זֶה גַּרְגְּרָן. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַשּׁוֹתֶה כּוֹסוֹ בְּבַת אַחַת — הֲרֵי זֶה גַּרְגְּרָן, שְׁנַיִם — דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, שְׁלֹשָׁה — מִגַּסֵּי הָרוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages teach proper manners unconnected to any prohibition, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A person** should **not eat garlic or onions** from the side of **its head,** i.e., its roots, **but** rather **from** the side of **its leaves. And if he did eat** in that manner, **he** gives the appearance of being **a glutton. Similarly, a person** should **not drink his cup** of wine all **at once, and if he did drink** in this manner, **he** gives the appearance of being **a** greedy **drinker. The Sages taught** in this regard: **One who drinks his cup** all **at once is a** greedy **drinker;** if he does so **in two** swallows, this is proper **etiquette;** in **three** swallows, he is **of haughty spirit,** as he presents himself as overly delicate and refined.

The *Gemara* continues with a series of teachings on restraint in eating and drinking. A *braita* rules: *lo yochal adam shum u'vatzal me'rosho ella me'alav* — one should not eat a garlic or an onion from the root end but rather from where the leaves emerge (what we would call the top). Eating from the root end is a sign of ravenousness — apparently because it suggests one cannot even be bothered to hold the vegetable in the normal way. *Ve'im achal harei ze ra'avtan* — if one does eat that way, one has shown oneself to be ravenous. The point in bringing this *braita* is to demonstrate that Rabbinic literature regularly addresses not *halachic* questions but matters of how not to conduct oneself in a gluttonous manner.

### Drinking Gulps

Similarly: *lo yishkeh adam koso be'vat achat* — one should not drink an entire cup of wine in a single gulp. *Ve'im shasah harei ze gargran* — if one does, one is a glutton. A further *braita* elaborates: drinking in one gulp is gluttonous; drinking in two gulps is *derech eretz* — the normal, proper manner; drinking in three or more very small sips, however, is *migasay haruach* — a mark of haughtiness. Proper comportment lies in the middle.

### Orlah Lesson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָמֵי בַּר אַבָּא: חֲצוּבָא — מְקַטַּע רַגְלֵיהוֹן דְּרַשִּׁיעַיָּא. נְטִיעָה — מְקַטַּע רַגְלֵיהוֹן דְּקַצָּבַיָּא וּדְבוֹעֲלֵי נִדּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the previous discussion, the Gemara notes that **Rami bar Abba** also **said: The sea squill,** a plant from the lily family whose roots project deep into the ground, will **cut off the feet of the wicked** in the future on the Day of Judgment. It was customary to plant sea squill on the edges of fields as boundary markers because their roots grow straight down without spreading out. Those who overstepped boundaries and infringed upon their neighbor's property should have heeded the markers and desisted. Similarly, **young trees will cut off the feet of butchers and those who have relations with menstruating women.** After a tree is planted, one must wait three years before eating its fruit. This should serve as a lesson for those butchers who hasten to eat of the animal's meat before removing its hide, and for those who have relations with their menstruating wives and do not wait for them to achieve ritual purification.

The same Rami bar Abba teaches: *neti'ah mekate'a ragleihen de-kasvaya ud'baalei niddot* — the principle of planting and waiting is what undercuts the legs of two groups who lack patience. The reference is to *orlah*: one invests years of care in a tree but must wait three full years before eating any of its fruit. This demands sustained patience and self-restraint, and the *Torah* holds it up as a lesson.

### Butchers Restraint

The lesson of *orlah* is directed at butchers and at *baalei niddot*. For butchers: even though *halachically* one may eat from meat before checking it for *treifos*, acting with some patience and restraint — not tearing at the meat the moment it is *shecht* — is the proper conduct. Who wants to discover a week later that by rushing one consumed *treifa* meat? The *orlah* model teaches that certain pleasures require patience. Similarly, *baalei niddot* — men who cannot restrain themselves from approaching their wives before *tehara* and immersion in the *mikvah* — should internalize the lesson of *orlah*: some of the deepest pleasures in life require waiting before they may be enjoyed.

### Turmus Bean

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוֹרְמוֹסָא — מְקַטַּע רַגְלֵיהוֹן דְּשָׂנְאֵיהוֹן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיּוֹסִיפוּ בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לַעֲשׂוֹת הָרַע בְּעֵינֵי ה׳ וַיַּעַבְדוּ אֶת הַבְּעָלִים וְאֶת הָעַשְׁתָּרוֹת וְאֶת אֱלֹהֵי אֲרָם וְאֶת אֱלֹהֵי צִידוֹן וְאֵת אֱלֹהֵי מוֹאָב וְאֵת אֱלֹהֵי בְנֵי עַמּוֹן וְאֵת אֱלֹהֵי פְלִשְׁתִּים וַיַּעַזְבוּ אֶת ה׳ וְלֹא עֲבָדוּהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The lupine [*turmus*],** an extremely bitter legume that is edible only after an extensive process, **will cut off the feet of the enemies of the Jewish people,** a euphemism for the Jewish people themselves. **As it is stated: "And the children of Israel continued to do evil in the eyes of the Lord, and served the Baalim and the Ashtaroth, and the gods of Aram and the gods of Zidon and the gods of Moab and the gods of the children of Ammon and the gods of the Philistines, and they forsook the Lord and did not serve Him"** (Judges 10:6).

*Tormusa mekate'a ragleihen de-soneihen de-Yisrael* — the *turmus* bean undercuts the legs of the enemies of the Jews, which idiomatically refers to the Jews themselves. The *turmus* is a type of bean that must be cooked seven times before it is edible, yet even after all that labor it is eaten only as a light dessert course, not as a main dish. The significance of this image is developed in the next section.

## Torah to Israel

### Seven Gods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וַיַּעַזְבוּ אֶת ה׳״, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁלֹּא עֲבָדוּהוּ?! וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְלֹא עֲבָדוּהוּ״ — אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אֲפִילּוּ כַּתּוֹרְמוֹס הַזֶּה שֶׁשּׁוֹלְקִין אוֹתוֹ שֶׁבַע פְּעָמִים וְאוֹכְלִין אוֹתוֹ בְּקִנּוּחַ סְעוּדָה לֹא עֲשָׂאוּנִי בָּנַי.
>
> **Translation:** **By inference from** that **which is stated: "And they forsook the Lord," do I not know that they did not serve Him? Rather,** for **what** purpose **does the verse state** the seemingly unnecessary words **"and did not serve Him"? Rabbi Elazar said: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: My children did not treat Me even like this lupine, which,** because it is inedible as it is, must be **cooked** in water **seven times** in order to temper its bitter taste **and** is eventually made so sweet that **one eats it as a dessert** after **a meal.** They worshipped all seven types of idolatry listed in the verse, and even after I punished them for each and every one of them, they still refused to repent from their evil ways. Instead, they remained rebellious and did not serve Me.

The verse concludes: *ve'et Hashem azvu ve'lo avaduhu* — they abandoned God and did not worship Him. Once the verse states they abandoned God, it is obvious they did not worship Him; what then is the additional import of the phrase *ve'lo avaduhu*? The *Gemara* answers: *amar Elazar amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu afilu k'tormusa hazeh shesholkin oto sheva pe'amim ve'ochlin oto be'kinuach seuda lo asuni banai* — even like this bean that is cooked seven times and eaten only as a dessert, My children did not make Me even that. *Rashi* offers two readings: either the Jewish people are the bean — subjected to seven punishments for their seven apostasies, yet still they strayed, making them harder to "cook" than the bean — or God Himself is the bean, and the point is that Israel did not even keep God around as a dessert, something one has alongside seven other dishes. They abandoned Him entirely, not even maintaining Him as an afterthought alongside their seven foreign gods. The *Maharsha* favors the second reading.

### Tough Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר: מִפְּנֵי מָה נִתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן עַזִּין. תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״מִימִינוֹ אֵשׁ דָּת לָמוֹ״. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: רְאוּיִין הַלָּלוּ שֶׁתִּנָּתֵן לָהֶם דָּת אֵשׁ. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: דָּתֵיהֶם שֶׁל אֵלּוּ — אֵשׁ. שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא (לֹא) נִתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — אֵין כׇּל אוּמָּה וְלָשׁוֹן יְכוֹלִין לַעֲמוֹד בִּפְנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara considers another aspect of the character of the Jewish people. **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in the name of Rabbi Meir: For what reason was the Torah given to the Jewish people?** It is **because they are impudent,** and Torah study will weaken and humble them. A Sage **of the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** the following with regard to the verse: **"From His right hand went a fiery law for them"** (Deuteronomy 33:2); **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said:** Based on their nature and character, **these** people, the Jews, are **fit to be given a fiery law,** a hard and scorching faith. **Some say** a different version of this *baraita*: **The ways** and nature **of these** people, the Jews, **are** like **fire, as, were it not for** the fact that **the Torah was given to the Jewish people,** whose study and observance restrains them, **no nation or tongue could withstand them.**

The sugya transitions to a *tana* who teaches in the name of Rabbi Meir: *mipnei mah nitna Torah le-Yisrael mipnei shehen azim* — why was the *Torah* given to Israel? Because they are hard and stubborn. The tradition from the school of Rabbi Yishmael derives this from the verse *mimino esh dat lamo* — from God's right hand, a fiery law for His people.

### Torah Fire

One reading of this verse is: *re'uyin elu she-tinaten lahem dat esh* — these people are worthy of receiving a law of fire, they are tough enough to handle it. An alternative reading: *datehem shel elu esh* — their very nature is fire. *She-ilmalei nitna Torah le-Yisrael ein kol uma ve-lashon yecholim la'amod bifneihem* — had the *Torah* not been given to Israel, no nation on earth could withstand them. The *Torah* is what tames and channels that fierce, unyielding quality; without it, Israel's intensity would be entirely destructive. The sugya thus attempts to present the attribute of *keshiyut* — stubbornness — in a dual light: it is what has led Israel to sin repeatedly, but it is also what enables Israel to hold fast to God and *Torah* when properly directed.

### Three Tough Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, שְׁלֹשָׁה עַזִּין הֵן: יִשְׂרָאֵל בָּאוּמּוֹת, כֶּלֶב בַּחַיּוֹת, תַּרְנְגוֹל בָּעוֹפוֹת. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף עֵז בִּבְהֵמָה דַּקָּה. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף צָלָף בָּאִילָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is** the same as **what Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said: There are three impudent ones: The Jewish people among the nations;** the **dog among animals;** and the **rooster among birds. And some say: Also** the **goat among small cattle. And some say: Also** the **caper bush among trees.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish concludes with a list of three that are exceptionally tough: *Yisrael be-umot* — Israel among the nations; *kelev be-chayot* — the dog among animals (not the fiercest, but the most tenacious and persistent); *tarnegol be-ofot* — the rooster among birds. Some add: *ez bi-vehemah daka* — the goat among small domesticated animals; and some further add *tzalaf be-ilanot* — the *tzalaf* among trees, though *Rashi* is uncertain of the precise significance of this last addition.
