# Bava Batra 137 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Achareicha Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״נְכָסַי לְךָ וְאַחֲרֶיךָ לִפְלוֹנִי״, וְיָרַד רִאשׁוֹן וּמָכַר וְאָכַל – הַשֵּׁנִי מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין לַשֵּׁנִי אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁשִּׁיֵּיר רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The *halakha* in this case **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*; as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who said: **My property** is given **to you, and after you, to so-and-so, and** the **first** recipient **entered** the property **and sold** it **and consumed** the profits, **the second** recipient **repossesses** the property **from the purchasers,** as the property belongs to him after the death of the first recipient; **this is the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: The second** recipient receives **only that which the first** beneficiary **left,** since his sale is valid.

### Rabbi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: ״נְכָסַי לְךָ וְאַחֲרֶיךָ לִפְלוֹנִי״ – יוֹרֵד רִאשׁוֹן וּמוֹכֵר וְאוֹכֵל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין לָרִאשׁוֹן אֶלָּא אֲכִילַת פֵּירוֹת בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from another *baraita* (*Tosefta* 8:4): If one says: **My property** is given **to you, and after you, to so-and-so, the first one may enter** the property **and sell** it **and consume** the profits; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: The first one has only** the right of **consumption of the produce.**

Both authorities agree that *lechatchila* Sarah should not attempt to sell the *karka* outright. But the disagreement surfaces when she actually does so. Rabbi holds that *kinyan peirot lav k'kinyan haguf* — the right to the produce does not constitute ownership of the body of the property — and therefore Sarah lacks the power to alienate the land.

### RSG's Position

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that she can sell it. His reasoning is either that *kinyan peirot k'kinyan haguf* — the usufruct right is tantamount to full ownership — or, alternatively, that *achareicha shani*: the *achareicha* formulation itself is distinctive and actually conveys the *guf* of the property to Sarah, with Rachel receiving it only thereafter. Under Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's view, Sarah holds genuine title and can therefore convey it to another.

### Rasha Arum Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵיזֶהוּ רָשָׁע עָרוּם? זֶה הַמַּשִּׂיא עֵצָה לִמְכּוֹר בִּנְכָסִים כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Who is a cunning wicked person? This** is **one who provides advice to sell property in accordance with** the ruling of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** Although Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that the sale is valid, he does not permit one to do so *ab initio*, as that would deprive the second designated recipient of the property. Therefore, one who advises the first recipient to do so is considered a cunning wicked person.

The *gemara* then raises a related question: what if Sarah, rather than attempting an outright sale, were advised to convey the property to a third party as a gift, thereby severing the chain and cutting Rachel out entirely? Anyone who counsels such a strategy is characterized as a *rasha arum* — a wickedly shrewd person — because they are engineering harm to Rachel without any benefit to Sarah herself. Sarah *lechatchila* should not pursue this course; and while if she actually did so we might not invoke the harshest terminology for her conduct, the advisor who orchestrated it bears that designation.

## Death Timing Stages

### Three Death Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. וּמוֹדֶה, שֶׁאִם נְתָנָן בְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע – לֹא עָשָׂה כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel; but** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **concedes that if** the first recipient **gave** the property to another **as the gift of a person on his deathbed,** in which case no formal act of acquisition is required, **he has done nothing;** the second recipient can repossess the property from the one to whom it was given.

A more sophisticated question then arises: what if Sarah, instead of making an outright gift, attempts to convey the property through a *matnat shchiv mera* — a deathbed gift — to a third party, thereby breaking the chain before it reaches Rachel? To analyze this, the *gemara* conceptualizes three distinct temporal stages. First, there is the period while Sarah is still alive. Second, there is the moment of death itself — *gmar misa*. Third, there is the period after death has already concluded.

### Achareicha vs. Matna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע לֹא קָנָה אֶלָּא לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וּכְבָר קְדָמוֹ ״אַחֲרֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason? Abaye says:** The recipient of **the gift of a person on his deathbed acquires** it **only after** the person's **death, and** in this case, the acquisition of the recipient resulting from the statement of: **After you,** i.e., the second designated recipient, **has already preceded** the acquisition of the first one.

The *gemara* reasons as follows. The *achareicha* transfer to Rachel takes effect at the moment of death — the instant Sarah is no longer alive. The classic formulation of a *matnat shchiv mera*, by contrast, is *mehayom ule'achar mita* — operative after death. Accordingly, if Sarah attempted a *matnat shchiv mera* to a third party, Rachel would prevail: the *achareicha* chain to Rachel kicks in at the moment of death, while the *matnat shchiv mera* to the third party would only take effect after death. The attempted conveyance does not succeed in breaking the chain.

### Bein Hashmashot

This analysis invites a conceptual observation that is worth pausing over. The framework requires us to distinguish not merely between "alive" and "dead" but to treat the moment of death as its own discrete category — almost like a *bein hashmashot*, a twilight zone that is neither the prior state nor the subsequent one. Death, of course, is experienced practically as a process rather than a binary switch. Yet the *gemara* conceptualizes it as two stages: the moment of death and what comes after. Whether the *gemara* means to posit a literally instantaneous sliver of time or is simply working with a conceptual distinction between two phases is not entirely clear, but the analytical framework depends on maintaining that distinction.

## Matna Timing Debate

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר אַבָּיֵי הָכִי? וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: מַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע, מֵאֵימָתַי קָנָה? אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: עִם גְּמַר מִיתָה, וְרָבָא אָמַר: לְאַחַר גְּמַר מִיתָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did Abaye** actually **say this,** that the gift of a person on his deathbed takes effect only after his death? **But wasn't it stated** in an amoraic dispute concerning the matter: **From when does** the recipient of **the gift of a person on his deathbed acquire** it? **Abaye says: With the completion of death,** i.e., at the moment he dies; **and Rava says: After the completion of death.**

The *gemara* quotes Abaye as the authority for the ruling that *matnat shchiv mera* takes effect *l'achar misa* — after death — and that therefore the *achareicha* to Rachel beats it out. But the *gemara* immediately challenges this: was that really Abaye's position? For it was taught elsewhere that Abaye and Rava disputed precisely this question. Abaye had said: *matnat shchiv mera lo kani ela im gmar misa* — it takes effect at the moment of death, not after. Rava had said: *l'achar gmar misa* — only after death has concluded.

What might have motivated Abaye's original position of *im gmar misa*? The underlying tension in *matnat shchiv mera* is that it is a rabbinic institution attempting to combine two incompatible legal mechanisms. On one hand, it seeks to function like an inheritance — something that passes upon death. On the other hand, it operates through the power of a living person to make a gift; one cannot give a gift to take effect after one's own death, since at that point one no longer has the legal capacity to convey anything. The compromise Abaye may have been reaching toward is to locate the operative moment as close to death as possible without crossing entirely into the post-death period — *im gmar misa*, at that threshold moment when death is completing.

### Rava's Position

Rava, by contrast, places the operative moment squarely after death — *l'achar gmar misa*. For Rava, the *matnat shchiv mera* fully approximates a *yerusha*, a natural inheritance, in its timing.

### Abaye Reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָדַר בֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵהַהִיא. מִמַּאי דְּמֵהַהִיא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ? דִּלְמָא מֵהָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Abaye retracted that** latter statement. The Gemara asks: **From where** is it known **that he retracted that** statement? **Perhaps he retracted this,** i.e., the statement that the recipient acquires it only after the person's death, and his conclusion is that he acquires it at the moment of his death.

The *gemara* reports that Abaye ultimately reversed himself — *hadar beih Abaye* — and came to agree with Rava that *matnat shchiv mera* takes effect *l'achar gmar misa*. The question the *gemara* then poses is: from which position exactly did Abaye retract? Perhaps, the *gemara* suggests, Abaye did not retract from his position of *im gmar misa* at all. Perhaps instead he retracted from his ruling in the *achareicha* case — and his revised view would be that both the *achareicha* and the *matnat shchiv mera* operate *im gmar misa*, splitting simultaneously at the moment of death. On that reading, the chain to Rachel and the deathbed gift to the third party would arrive at the same instant, and the question of which prevails would remain open.

### Get Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּתְנַן: ״זֶה גִּיטֵּךְ אִם מַתִּי״; ״זֶה גִּיטֵּךְ מֵחוֹלִי זֶה״; ״זֶה גִּיטֵּךְ לְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: That possibility **should not enter your mind, as we learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 72b): If a man on his deathbed says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce if I die,** or: **This is your bill of divorce** if I die **from this illness,** or: **This is your bill of divorce after I die, he has said nothing.** The divorce does not take effect after his death. Evidently, when one gives instructions on his deathbed, his intention is for them to be effective after he dies.

The *gemara* rejects this reading. *Lo salka datach* — it is inconceivable, the *gemara* argues, that *matnat shchiv mera* operates *im gmar misa*. The proof is from the laws of *get*. The *mishnah* presents three formulations a dying man might use when giving his wife a *get*: *ze gitecha im mati* — this is your *get* if I die; *ze gitecha mecholi ze* — this is your *get* of this illness; and *ze gitecha le'achar mita* — this is your *get* after my death. The last formulation, *le'achar mita*, renders the *get* entirely invalid — *velo amar klum*. From this the *gemara* infers that a deathbed *get* takes effect after death, and therefore that the equivalent mechanism for a *matnat shchiv mera* likewise operates after death. Accordingly, Abaye must have retracted from his *im gmar misa* position and now agrees with Rava that *matnat shchiv mera* is *l'achar gmar misa* — and thus Rachel's *achareicha* claim at the moment of death precedes it.

### Proof Analysis

This is a striking piece of reasoning that warrants careful scrutiny, because the *gemara* is making at least two significant assumptions that are not self-evident.

The first assumption is that the timing of a deathbed *get* is the same as the timing of a *matnat shchiv mera*. These are genuinely different legal instruments. A *matnat shchiv mera* is a special rabbinic institution with its own mechanics; it need not operate on the same clock as a conditional *get*. The *gemara* assumes they are equivalent in this respect, but the reason for that assumption is not made explicit.

The second, more fundamental question concerns how the *get* case actually demonstrates that the operative moment is *l'achar gmar misa* rather than *im gmar misa*. The *mishnah* tells us that *ze gitecha le'achar mita* is definitively invalid. But why does that prove that *im gmar misa* would also be invalid — and thus that the *get* must be operating *l'achar misa*? Two possibilities present themselves. One is that if the operative moment were *im gmar misa*, we would expect the *get* to be at least a *safek* — doubtfully valid — rather than definitively invalid. The *gemara* apparently assumes the *get* is *vadai* not effective, which means *im gmar misa* cannot be the operative moment either. The alternative possibility — which is worth taking seriously — is that the phrase *im gmar misa* does not in fact refer to a gray transitional instant at all. Perhaps *im gmar misa* simply means "a moment before death" — the instant just before the transition is complete, while the person is still halachically alive. If so, a *get* given *im gmar misa* would be perfectly valid, and the contrast the *gemara* is drawing is between the moment-before-death formulation (where the *get* works) and the after-death formulation (where it does not). The *gemara*, on this reading, is demonstrating that since *le'achar mita* fails, the operative moment must be placed earlier — and whatever label we apply to that earlier moment, it is the functional equivalent of what Rava calls *l'achar gmar misa* for *matnat shchiv mera*.

The upshot is that the *gemara* concludes both Abaye and Rava agree: *matnat shchiv mera* operates *l'achar gmar misa*. Consequently, in the original case, Rachel's *achareicha* claim ripens at the moment of death and Sarah's attempted *matnat shchiv mera* to the third party takes effect only afterwards — the chain remains unbroken and Rachel receives the property.

## RSG Halacha

### Slaves & Shrouds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָיוּ בָּהֶן עֲבָדִים וְהוֹצִיאָן לְחֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** that if the first recipient sells the property, the sale is valid, **and** this is the *halakha* **even if there were** Canaanite **slaves** included **in** the property **and** the first recipient **emancipated them.**

The *gemara* now returns to the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and extends it to a series of cases. Rabbi Zeira citing Rabbi Yochanan states: *halacha k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — we rule like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel that in an *achareicha* transfer the first recipient has genuine title and may convey the property. *V'afilu hayu bahen avadim v'hotzian lecheirus* — and this applies even if the gifted property included slaves and she freed them. Freeing the slaves would break the chain, since Rachel would have expected to receive them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְמֶיעְבַּד אִיסּוּרָא לָא יָהֲבִינַן לָךְ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that the giver **would have said to him: I did not give you** my Canaanite slaves **to transgress a prohibition,** as it is prohibited to emancipate Canaanite slaves, Rabbi Yoḥanan **teaches us** that he has the legal right to do so.

The *gemara* objects: *pshita* — is this not obvious? If she owns the property, of course she can free the slaves. The *gemara* responds: *mahu detaima* — one might have thought the original donor could argue that while he accepted she might redirect the property, he never contemplated she would commit an *issur* with it. According to the *gemara*, there is a prohibition against freeing a Canaanite slave — a position that requires acknowledging the conceptual difficulty of reading the Torah's extensive concern with ethical treatment of slaves and liberation from bondage as establishing such a prohibition. Regardless, the *gemara* rules: *ka mashma lan* — we are nonetheless taught she may do even this.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וַאֲפִילּוּ עֲשָׂאָן תַּכְרִיכִין לְמֵת. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לְשַׁוּוֹיִנְהוּ אִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה לָא יְהַבִי לָךְ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel even if he made** the gift into **shrouds for a corpse.** The Gemara asks: Isn't that **obvious?** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that the giver would have said to him: **I did not give you** my possessions **to render them into items from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited,** but rather so that you may use and enjoy them, and Rabbi Yoḥanan **teaches us** that he may do so.

Similarly, Rabbi Yosef citing Rabbi Yochanan rules *halacha k'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, and extends it to a case where the gifted property was cloth and the recipient turned it into *tachrichim* — burial shrouds. By becoming *assur b'hana'ah*, the shrouds are removed from Rachel's ability to benefit from them, effectively breaking the chain. Again the *gemara* raises a *hava amina* that the donor could protest: *leshabbinei issurei hana'ah lo yahavi lach* — I did not give it to you to transform into something from which benefit is forbidden. And again the *gemara* rules: *ka mashma lan*, she may do so.

These rulings might superficially seem strange. Once a gift is given, how can the original donor dictate subsequent use? The answer lies in a principle discussed earlier in the *gemara* that in unilateral gift transactions we are more attentive to the mindset of the giver — *da'at hanosein* — because the recipient risked nothing. In some circumstances, *devarim she'b'lev* — unstated intentions — carry halachic weight in the gift context. Nonetheless, in these cases the *gemara* concludes that such implicit restrictions do not override the recipient's full ownership rights. Had the donor wished to impose such restrictions, he would have needed to do so through explicit conditions.

### Esrog Yotzei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא: ״אֶתְרוֹג זֶה נָתוּן לְךָ בְּמַתָּנָה, וְאַחֲרֶיךָ לִפְלוֹנִי״, נְטָלוֹ רִאשׁוֹן וְיָצָא בּוֹ – בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman bar Rav Ḥisda taught:** If one said to another: **This *etrog* is given to you as a gift, and after you** die, **to so-and-so,** and **the first** recipient then **took it and fulfilled** his obligation of taking the four species on the first day of *Sukkot* **with it,** when one is obligated to take species that belong to him, in this case, **we have arrived at the dispute between Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** as to the nature of the ownership of the first recipient. According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's opinion that he cannot sell the *etrog*, it is not considered entirely his, and he does not fulfill his obligation by taking it. According to the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds that he can sell it, he fulfills his obligation.

The *gemara* now introduces a celebrated case. Rava bar Rav Chisda teaches: *esrog zeh nassun lach b'matanah v'acharecha li-ploni* — this *esrog* is given to you as a gift and after you it should go to so-and-so. Sarah received the *esrog* with an *achareicha* to Rachel. Sarah took the *esrog* and sought to fulfill the *mitzvah* of the Four Species. Was she *yotzei*?

At first glance this would simply replicate the debate between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, *achareicha shani* — she has genuine ownership — and she can certainly be *yotzei* with her own *esrog*. According to Rabbi, she has only *kinyan peirot*, not *kinyan haguf*. Since being *yotzei* with an *esrog* requires full ownership — *lachem mishelachem*, the *esrog* must be yours — it would seem she cannot fulfill the *mitzvah*.

### Intent Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּמָר סָבַר: קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: קִנְיַן פֵּירוֹת לָאו כְּקִנְיַן הַגּוּף דָּמֵי. אֶלָּא הָכָא, אִי מִיפָּק לָא נָפֵיק בֵּיהּ, לְמַאי יַהֲבֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak objects to this** statement, reasoning that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagree only there,** in the case of: After you, with regard to the nature of the recipient's ownership; **as** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and the **profits** it engenders **is considered like ownership of the item itself, and** the other **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **ownership of** the rights to use an item and the **profits** it engenders **is not considered like ownership of the item itself. But here,** with regard to the *etrog*, **if he does not fulfill** his obligation **with it, for what** purpose **did** the owner **give it to him?**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak raises the following objection. The debate between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel concerned whether *kinyan peirot* is equivalent to *kinyan haguf* for purposes of commercial transactions — can she sell the land? But here, with an *esrog*, what possible use does *kinyan peirot* afford her? *Im mafik lo nafik bei, lemai yahavis nihaleh* — if she cannot use it to be *yotzei* the *mitzvah*, why did the donor give it to her at all? With a field, *kinyan peirot* means she benefits from the produce. With a table, *kinyan peirot* means she can use the table. But with a single *esrog*, no natural use presents itself that would constitute *kinyan peirot* — she cannot eat it in any meaningful quantity, she cannot sell it. The only conceivable purpose for which the *esrog* was transferred to her was to enable her to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *netilas lulav*. Therefore, the *gemara* concludes, even Rabbi must agree that the conveyance was designed to enable her to be *yotzei* — and the *gemara* rules: *ela mafik, d'chulei alma modu denafik* — regarding fulfilling the *mitzvah*, everyone agrees she is *yotzei*.

### Everyone Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִיפָּק – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּנָפֵיק בֵּיהּ; מְכָרָהּ אוֹ אֲכָלָהּ – בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** with regard to **fulfilling** his obligation, **everyone agrees that he fulfills** his obligation **with it.** It is only in a case where the first recipient **sold it or consumed it,** that **we have arrived at the dispute between Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** According to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, if he sold it, the second recipient can repossess the *etrog* from the purchaser, and if he consumed it he must pay the second recipient its value, whereas according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, he owes nothing to the second recipient, as the *etrog* belongs to him.

The ruling is thus that on the question of being *yotzei* the *mitzvah*, Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel do not disagree. Both hold that she may fulfill her obligation with this *esrog*. The *gemara* grounds this not in an objective determination that *kinyan peirot* satisfies the ownership requirement of *lachem*, but rather in an assessment of the donor's intent: since there was no other conceivable purpose for this gift, we read the transfer as specifically designed to give her whatever title is necessary to be *yotzei*.

### Sale vs. Eat

The dispute between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel remains operative in the other direction. *Machrah achlah — banu lemachlokes Rabbi v'Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — if Sarah sold the *esrog* or ate it, thereby breaking the chain to Rachel, that is precisely where the classical debate resurfaces. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, she had *kinyan haguf* and was entitled to do so. According to Rabbi, she had only *kinyan peirot* and by consuming or selling it she violated the terms of the conveyance and Rachel is entitled to redress.

The *Rashbam*'s explanation of why being *yotzei* counts as a form of *kinyan peirot* deserves attention. He writes that being *yotzei* the *mitzvah* is the *peri* — the fruit — of this *esrog*, and that is what was granted to her. This framing is difficult to accept straightforwardly. The Torah's requirement of *lachem* demands ownership, not merely the benefit of use. Lending someone an *esrog* does not enable them to be *yotzei* — only genuine transfer of ownership does. If Sarah truly lacks *kinyan haguf*, then simply designating the fulfillment of the *mitzvah* as a form of *peri* does not resolve the underlying ownership deficiency.

A more satisfying account would be that the conveyance in this context actually does grant Sarah full *kinyan haguf* — but only for the limited domain of *hilchos esrog*. In other words, the transfer is not a global *kinyan peirot* that somehow extends to ritual purposes; rather, the donor's expressed intent creates a genuine, if circumscribed, ownership right in Sarah for precisely this purpose: she has title to the *esrog* for the *mitzvah*, and for that purpose alone. This is not unlike other cases in the *gemara* where an entity is considered an owner within one legal domain but not another. Just as ownership of a slave can be notionally transferred to a second party for limited purposes — such as determining liability if the slave is gored — so too here Sarah may be treated as the owner within the narrow compass of *mitzvah* fulfillment.

## Estate vs. Partners

### Estate Entity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: הָאַחִין שֶׁקָּנוּ אֶתְרוֹג בִּתְפוּסַת הַבַּיִת; נְטָלוֹ אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְיָצָא בּוֹ – אִם יָכוֹל לְאוֹכְלוֹ, יָצָא; וְאִם לָאו, לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna says:** In a case of **brothers who acquired an *etrog* with** money from the jointly **held** property **of the estate,** and **one of them took it** on *Sukkot* **and** attempted to **fulfill** his obligation **with it,** the *halakha* depends on the limits of his ownership of the *etrog*: **If he may eat it,** i.e., his brothers allow him to do so, **he has fulfilled** his obligation to perform the mitzva, **and if not, he has not fulfilled** his obligation.

The *gemara* introduces a related and analytically rich case. Rabba bar Huna states: *ha'achim shekanu esrog b'tfusas habayis* — brothers who jointly inherited an estate used estate funds to purchase an *esrog*. This *esrog* is thus collectively owned — but through the distinctive mechanism of *tufsat habayis*, estate ownership, rather than ordinary partnership.

The legal significance of *tufsat habayis* lies in the fact that it functions similarly to a corporate entity. In ordinary *shutfus*, partnership, each partner owns a defined fractional share — half mine, half yours. In an undivided estate, by contrast, no individual brother owns any specific fraction. The estate itself is the owner, and the brothers are beneficiaries or shareholders in that abstract entity until a formal division takes place. This makes *tufsat habayis* a remarkable precedent in halachic thinking for something approaching the modern concept of a legal person distinct from its human members.

The problem with using a jointly-owned *esrog* in ordinary *shutfus* is well established: one cannot be *yotzei* with a *lulav* and *esrog* belonging to partners, because *lachem* demands that the object be fully yours, and half-ownership is insufficient.

### Brother Yotzei

The question, then, is whether *tufsat habayis* is different. The *gemara* rules: *natlo echad meihem v'yatza bah* — if one of the brothers took it and sought to be *yotzei* — *im yachol lochlo yatza, v'im lav lo yatza* — if he could have eaten it without the other brother objecting, he was *yotzei*; if not, he was not.

The eating criterion invites analysis. One reading is that eating functions as a mere indicator of effective ownership: the fact that the brother could have consumed it without objection shows that the others implicitly ceded their interest to him — effectively allowing him to treat it as if he had extracted it from the estate for his own use. On this reading, the *esrog* has been constructively removed from the estate and he is effectively its sole owner at the moment he takes it, and the eating test is simply a diagnostic tool.

A deeper reading draws on a principle in *Sukkah* regarding the *esrog*: the *esrog* must be *rauy l'achilah* — fit for eating — in order to satisfy the requirement of *lachem*. The Rambam, in *Peirush Mishnayos*, explains that the Torah specifies *pri eitz hadar* — a fruit — and something that cannot be eaten does not fully qualify as such. Inability to eat might arise because the produce is *tevel* and hence halachically inaccessible, or because one's rights over it are incomplete. If the brother's interest in the estate is such that he cannot freely consume the *esrog* without objection, his claim on it is insufficiently robust to constitute the ownership the *mitzvah* demands.

The further question is whether the estate structure genuinely does something legally that plain partnership does not, or whether the operative principle is simply that wherever — whether in an estate or a partnership — the other party would not object to consumption, that is sufficient to treat the consumer as the functional owner. The *gemara*'s specification that the ruling applies *davka d'ika esrog l'chol chad u'chad* — only when each of the other brothers had his own *esrog* — suggests that the absence of objection is itself the operative fact: when Michael has his own *esrog*, his willingness to let his brother take the estate *esrog* without protest functions as an implicit concession, bringing the case within a framework similar to the conditional gift discussed below.

### Community Esrog

The *Rashbam* records an illuminating historical practice from his own community. The custom in his time was for the entire congregation to share a single *esrog*, since *esrogim* were difficult to obtain. The *Rashbam* explains the halachic basis: since the *esrog* is communally owned — which would be a form of *shutfus* and thus ordinarily problematic — the community operates on the understanding that whenever any individual's turn comes to take the *esrog* and make the *bracha*, every other member of the community implicitly grants that person, as a gift, their respective portion in the *esrog* at that moment. Thus the person holding the *esrog* owns it fully at the instant of the *mitzvah* — *kol echad ad she-yivareich alav*.

The *Rashbam* grounds this in the principle that even an unexpressed but universally understood intent is legally operative: *af al gav de-lo parish k'man de-parish dami* — even where it was not explicitly stated, it is treated as if it were. He connects this to the framework of *matanah al menat l'hachzir* — a gift on condition it be returned — since each member effectively transfers the *esrog* to the current holder for the duration of his use and it then reverts to the communal pool.

## Conditional Return

### Return Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: ״אֶתְרוֹג זֶה נָתוּן לְךָ בְּמַתָּנָה, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירֵהוּ לִי״, נְטָלוֹ וְיָצָא בּוֹ; הֶחְזִירוֹ – יָצָא, לֹא הֶחְזִירוֹ – לֹא יָצָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמַתָּנָה עַל מְנָת לְהַחֲזִיר – שְׁמָהּ מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** If one person said to another: **This *etrog* is given to you as a gift on the condition that you return it to me,** and the recipient **took it** on *Sukkot* **and** attempted to **fulfill** his obligation **with it,** if he ultimately **returned it, he has fulfilled** his obligation; if he **did not return it, he has not fulfilled** his obligation, as he did not fulfill the condition, thereby retroactively invalidating the gift. The Gemara explains that Rava **teaches us that a gift** given **on the condition that it be returned is considered** a valid **gift.**

The *gemara* now takes up the paradigmatic case of *matanah al menat l'hachzir* — a gift on condition that it be returned. Rava states: *esrog zeh nassun lach b'matanah al menat she-tachzireihu li* — this *esrog* is given to you as a gift on condition that you return it to me. Michael took it and was *yotzei*, and then returned it. He was *yotzei*. If Michael took it but did not return it — *lo yatza* — he did not fulfill his obligation, and moreover he now holds a stolen *esrog*, since the original transfer to him was conditional and the condition was never satisfied, meaning retroactively the *esrog* was never validly his.

A series of nuanced questions arise from this framework. First, what exactly constitutes *hachzarah* — returning? Does it require a formal act of *kinyan* transferring ownership back, or merely physically placing the object back in the original owner's hands? If the *beis din* compels Michael to return the *esrog* after he refused to do so voluntarily, has the condition been retroactively satisfied? Intuitively, *al menat she-tachzireihu li* implies a volitional act of return — and involuntary restitution would not seem to qualify. Intermediate cases — where Michael neither intended to keep it nor actively returned it, simply leaving it in the room for the original owner to retrieve — present their own difficulties regarding what constitutes fulfillment of the condition.

A separate question concerns giving the *esrog* to a minor child *al menat she-tachzireihu li*. A *katan* can acquire property *al yedei acher* but cannot himself transfer title back. If the condition requires a formal act of *makneh* by the child, it cannot be fulfilled. But if *she-tachzireihu* means only physical return rather than a formal legal act of transfer, a *katan* might be able to satisfy it.

### Valid Conditional Gift

The *gemara* derives from this case a significant conceptual ruling: *matanah al menat l'hachzir shma matanah* — a gift conditioned on return is nonetheless a legally valid gift. This is not self-evident. One might naturally reason that any gift destined to be returned is a mere fiction — the recipient was never truly the owner, only a temporary possessor. The *chiddush* of Rava is that the conditional gift confers genuine ownership during the period of possession, with a stipulation governing what must subsequently occur. The recipient is a full owner; the condition does not negate the *kinyan* but rather creates an obligation that runs alongside it.

## Palm Tree Case

### Tree Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּהֲוָה לַהּ דִּיקְלָא בְּאַרְעָא דְּרַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי. כׇּל אֵימַת דַּהֲוָת אָזְלָא לְמִיגְזְרֵיהּ, הֲוָה קָפֵיד עִילָּוַהּ. אַקְנִיתֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ כֹּל שְׁנֵי חַיָּיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman who had a palm tree on the land of Rav Beivai bar Abaye. Whenever she would go to harvest** the produce from **it, he would get angry at her** for trampling on his crops. Since she did not wish to anger him, **she** decided to temporarily waive her right to the tree's produce, and **transferred** ownership of **it to him** for **all the years of his life,** so that after his death she would once again be able to harvest the produce.

The *gemara* presents a case involving a woman who held a palm tree situated within the property of Rabibi bar Abaye. Whenever she wished to go and harvest the tree, Rabibi bar Abaye would object and impede her access — insisting the timing was inconvenient, that she was disturbing his land, and so forth. Eventually she transferred ownership of the tree to him for the duration of his lifetime, apparently calculating that after his death the ownership would revert to her and she could then access the tree without conflict.

### Transfer to Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל אִיהוּ אַקְנְיֵיהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לִבְנוֹ קָטָן.
>
> **Translation:** **He** then **went and transferred it to his minor son,** so that it would remain in his family even following his death.

Rabibi bar Abaye, however, proceeded to transfer the tree to his minor son. The choice of a minor son was deliberate: a *katan* can acquire property — *koneh* — but cannot himself transfer it — *eino yachol l'haknos*. By placing the tree in his minor son's hands, Rabibi bar Abaye ensured that no one could subsequently acquire it from the son, insulating it from alienation.

The question is whether this maneuver broke the chain. The woman was the *achareicha* — she was the one who had stipulated that ownership should revert to her upon his death. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, a recipient in an *achareicha* arrangement can sell or convey the property to another, thereby breaking the chain. If that is so, did Rabibi bar Abaye's transfer to his son sever the woman's reversion rights?

### RSG Allows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִשּׁוּם דְּאָתוּ מִמּוּלָאֵי, אָמְרִיתוּ מִילֵּי מוּלְיָאתָא? אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לָא קָאָמַר אֶלָּא לְאַחֵר, אֲבָל לְעַצְמוֹ – לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said** to Rav Beivai bar Abaye: Is it **because you descend from truncated [*mula'ei*]** people, as Rav Beivai's family traced their lineage to Eli, all of whose descendants were condemned to premature death (see I Samuel 2:31), that **you speak truncated [*mulyata*]** and unsound **matters? Even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** who holds that in a case where one says: My property is given to you, and after you, to so-and-so, if the first recipient sells the property the sale is valid, **says** so **only** in a case where it was to be given **to another** person; **but** if the giver said that it will return **to himself,** he did **not** say that the first recipient can sell it, as the giver clearly intended to maintain his ownership of the property. Accordingly, Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, held that Rav Beivai's sale did not take effect.

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua addressed Rabibi bar Abaye dismissively, saying — in a colorful idiom — that because he came from the place of Mamula, he offered Mamula-style arguments, an expression the *Rashbam* explains as meaning either crude, cut-down reasoning or perversely twisted logic; in any case, it was not intended as a compliment. The substance of Rav Huna's critique: even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who permits the first recipient to convey the property and thereby break the chain, *lo ka'amar ela l'acher* — said so only when the conveyance is to a genuinely independent third party. Conveying the property to one's own son does not constitute a chain-breaking transfer. The son remains within the domain of his father; after the father's death, the reversion to the woman remains operative, and the transfer to the son does not interrupt it.

## Ox & Hekdesh

### Hekdesh Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: ״שׁוֹר זֶה נָתוּן לְךָ בְּמַתָּנָה, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירֵהוּ לִי״; הִקְדִּישׁוֹ וְהֶחְזִירוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה מוּקְדָּשׁ וּמוּחְזָר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** If one said to another: **This ox is given to you as a gift on the condition that you return it to me,** and the recipient **consecrated it and** then **returned it** to him, **it is consecrated and returned.** The consecration takes effect, and although the owner may not use it, nevertheless it is considered returned and the condition is fulfilled.

The *gemara* concludes with a case that illuminates the boundaries of *matanah al menat l'hachzir*. Rava citing Rav Nachman states: *shor zeh natun lach b'matanah al menat she-tachzireihu li* — here is an ox as a gift, on condition that you return it to me. The recipient declared the ox *hekdesh* — consecrated to the Temple — and then returned it. The *gemara* initially suggests: *harei zeh mukdash umuchzar* — the ox is *hekdesh* and the condition has been satisfied. This would follow from the principle that *matanah al menat l'hachzir shma matanah*: the recipient was a genuine owner during his period of possession and therefore had the capacity to declare *hekdesh* with binding effect.

### L'Hachzir Variants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: מַאי אַהְדְּרֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמַאי חַסְּרֵיהּ? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חָזֵינַן; אִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירֵהוּ״ – הָא אַהְדְּרֵיהּ. אִי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירֵהוּ לִי״ – מִידֵּי דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ קָאָמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: What did he return to him?** The ox was not returned to the possession of the giver, as it is consecrated. Rav Naḥman **said to him: And what did he cause him to lose?** He returned the ox. **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** We must rule in this case based on what **we see. If** the giver **said to** the recipient: **On the condition that you return it,** it is considered returned, as **he returned it.** But **if he said to him: On the condition that you return it to me, he was** effectively **saying to him** that he must return **something fit for him** to use. Consequently, the consecration prevents the condition from being fulfilled, and both the gift and the consecration are retroactively nullified.

Rava, however, challenges Rav Nachman: *mai ahadrei* — what did he actually return? He returned an ox that the original owner cannot use, since it is now *hekdesh* and *assur b'hana'ah*. That does not satisfy the condition. Rav Nachman responds: *umai chasrei* — and what did the owner lose? The ox is physically intact. This exchange is bracketed by Rav Ashi, who resolves the matter through a linguistic distinction.

### Circular Logic

On the formulation *she-tachzirehu li*, a striking circularity emerges. The recipient declares the ox *hekdesh* and then returns it. But because the returned ox is *assur b'hana'ah* and thus unfit for the donor, the condition *li* is not satisfied. If the condition is not satisfied, the gift was never valid — the recipient never received genuine ownership. If he never owned it, he lacked the capacity to declare *hekdesh* in the first place. And if the *hekdesh* declaration is void, then the ox is not actually *hekdesh* — in which case one might think the return is valid, which would mean the gift was valid, which would mean the *hekdesh* was valid after all. The *gemara* does not continue this cycle. The conclusion stands: where the donor stipulated *li*, the *hekdesh* declaration fails, the condition is unmet, and the ox reverts to the donor's unencumbered ownership. Where the donor stipulated without *li*, the condition is satisfied and the *hekdesh* is operative.
