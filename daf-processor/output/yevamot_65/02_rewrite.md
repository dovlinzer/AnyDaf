# Yevamot 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three Marriages

### Marriage Setup

The daf under discussion is 65, and we pick up where the previous discussion left off. The sugya concerns a woman who has been married to a man for ten years without bearing children and is subsequently divorced. The situation carries an interesting internal tension: on one hand, there is a presumption that the infertility is not necessarily her fault, such that she may remarry a man who still needs to fulfill *peru u'revu*; on the other hand, there is a countervailing assumption that she may be a contributing factor, which is why the first husband is permitted — indeed encouraged — to take another wife.

### Tanu Rabbanan

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* beginning *tanu rabbanan*, found four lines from the top of 65a. The case runs as follows: she married the first man and had no children, married the second man and still had no children — that is two marriages without children, which, as we have seen, is the subject of the dispute between *Rebbe* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel regarding how many instances are required to establish a *chazakah*. The *baraita* then rules: *vishlishi lo tinasei ela lemi sheyeish lo banim* — she should not marry a third man unless that man has already fulfilled the *mitzvah* of *peru u'revu*. Following *Rebbe*'s position, after two such marriages we must at minimum be concerned that the problem lies with her. Therefore, if she does marry a man who has no children, *taitzei belo kesubah* — she is divorced without receiving her *kesubah*, since it has effectively been established that the infertility is her problem, and that husband must now pursue having children elsewhere.

### Third Marriage Query

An *ibaya* is then posed: suppose she proceeds to marry the third man, waits the requisite ten years, and still has no children. At this point, after three successive childless marriages, the case that the infertility lies with her seems firmly established. The question is whether the first two husbands — who did pay her *kesubah* at the time of their divorces, since the fault had not yet been conclusively identified as hers — may now return to her and demand the *kesubah* money back: *mahu delitve'u hanach kamaei, mi matzu amru lah, iglai milta de'at hu digramat* — it has now been revealed that you were the cause.

### Ketubah Return Claim

The thrust of this question is really about the burden of proof. Throughout these cases, the woman appears to be presumed entitled to her *kesubah*, placing the burden on the husband to establish otherwise. The question is whether newly available evidence — the failure of the third marriage — retroactively shifts that burden and allows the first two husbands to recover what they paid.

### Body Weakened Defense

The *Gemara* answers: *mistavra matzi amrah lehu hashta hu de'achash* — the more reasonable conclusion is that she can respond to them, "It is only now that my body has grown weaker." Perhaps twenty years have passed since the first marriage, ten since the second. Even if she was a partial cause earlier, or no cause at all, the passage of time and the weakening of her constitution may explain the current pattern. The *Gemara* accepts this defense: *mistavra matzi amrah lehu hashta hu de'achash*. She may say this to them, and they cannot recover the *kesubah*.

## Fourth Marriage

### Fertile Fourth

A second *ibaya* follows: suppose she marries a fourth man and now, with him, she does have children. So she was fertile all along. The question is now the mirror image of the previous one.

### Demand Third

May she go back to the third husband — who, recall, did not pay her *kesubah* precisely because by that point there was sufficient grounds to presume the infertility was hers — and demand her *kesubah* now that her fertility has been demonstrated?

### Shtikosayich Yafah

The *Gemara* answers with a striking formulation: *shtikosayich yafah midibura'yich* — your silence is better than your speech. The reason is that if she presses her claim against husband number three, asserting that she was always fertile and that the failure of that marriage was his fault, he can turn around and say: *ana ada'ata de'hachi lo garashtich* — had I known you were fertile, I never would have divorced you. If that argument succeeds, it would mean the *get* was given in error, invalidating it retroactively. And if the *get* is invalid, then the child she bore with husband number four is a *mamzer*. The stakes of pressing this claim are therefore catastrophic.

### Rav Pappa Raises

Rav Pappa objects: *ee ishtaka ishtakin? anan mi shatkinan? nimtza get batel u'vanim mamzerim* — if the concern about the *get*'s validity is real, how can we simply stay silent? We ourselves — the *beit din*, the *Gemara*, everyone — cannot simply keep quiet if there is a genuine question about whether the divorce was valid and the children are legitimate.

### Status Changed

The *Gemara* responds: *ella amrinan hashta hu d'avraya* — we say that it is only now that her condition has improved. That is, we adopt the position that her fertility is a new development, not a revealed truth about her condition at the time of the third marriage. This is consistent with the earlier principle that she could claim her body weakened over time; the converse applies here as well — we will say her body strengthened or her circumstances changed, and we will not revisit the factual premise of the original divorce.

### No Retroactive

The underlying principle is that we do not retroactively revise legal determinations based on later-emerging evidence. The divorce was valid on the facts as they were understood at the time, and we judge the case as it stood then. Subsequent developments are treated as changes in status, not revelations of hidden prior truth. This principle operates in everyone's best interest: the woman avoids the catastrophic outcome of her children being declared *mamzerim*, and the legal system avoids the destabilizing effect of constantly re-litigating settled matters whenever new evidence surfaces.

One might ask whether this principle extends to business or damages contexts — whether claims could be revisited whenever a counter-assumption cannot be articulated. The broader answer is grounded in the concept of *dvarim she-balev einam dvarim* and the general rule that legal determinations are made on the basis of the best available assumptions at the time, without regularly revising them based on facts that emerge only later.

## Infertility Source

### Mutual Blame

The *Gemara* now turns to the classic scenario: *hu amar minei, v'hi amra mineh* — he says the infertility problem is hers, and she says it is his. This is presumably the standard case underlying the entire discussion, and the *halacha* would seem to be that she receives her *kesubah* since the fault is not established. Tosafot raises a number of complicated questions about what precisely distinguishes this scenario from earlier cases, but the *Gemara* here takes a specific direction.

### Yora K'chetz

Rabbi Ami rules: *dvarim she-beino l'veina ne'emenes* — in matters that are between the two of them privately, she is believed. The *Gemara* then explains the mechanism: *hi kama leh b'yora k'chetz, hu lo kama leh b'yora k'chetz* — she is in a better position to know whether he ejaculates with the force of an arrow, *yora k'chetz*, than he is during the act itself. If she is claiming that the cause of the infertility is a weakness in his ejaculation, we credit her testimony because she has better access to that information than he does. This raises a natural question: why must this be the specific claim? Why isn't a general assertion that it may be his problem sufficient? We have already said that since it may be his problem, she receives her *kesubah* — the language *lo zachah l'hibanos mimenah* suggests the possibility is always open. This is a question that Tosafot grapples with, though the discussion there is complicated enough not to pursue here in full.

### Fertility Test

The husband then proposes: *eizal insiv iteta v'ivdok nafshai* — I will go ahead and marry another woman and see if I have children with her. If I do, that will prove the problem is hers and she should not receive her *kesubah*.

### Divorce First

Rabbi Ami responds: *be-zo yotzi ve-yitein ketubah* — if he wishes to pursue that course, he must first divorce his wife and give her the *kesubah* before marrying another woman. The *Gemara* frames this in terms of a broader principle: *she'ani omer kol hanosei isha al ishto yotzi v'yiten ketubah* — anyone who marries another woman while still married to his first wife, at least if the first wife protests, must give the first wife a *get* and pay her *kesubah*. The first wife has the right to object to a second marriage, and if the husband insists on proceeding, that objection entitles her to a compelled divorce with *kesubah*.

---

## Polygamy Debate

### Rabbi Ami Monogamy

Rabbi Ami's position thus treats the first wife's protest as legally effective: if she does not want him to take another wife, he cannot do so while remaining married to her. If he insists, he must divorce her with her *kesubah* first. This is not merely a moral preference but a legal entitlement.

### Rava Polygamy OK

Rava disagrees: *nosei adam kama nashim al ishto ve-hu d'is lei l'mizan ninhu* — a man is entitled to marry multiple women even alongside his existing wife, provided he has the means to support them all. On Rava's view, the first wife's protest does not carry legal force sufficient to prevent a second marriage; the only operative constraint is economic capacity.

### Bavli-Yerushalmi

This disagreement is not merely a technical *halachic* dispute but reflects a deeper question about whether polygamy is regarded as fundamentally incompatible with the first wife's rights or as a permissible institution subject only to practical constraints. As noted, this maps quite neatly onto the broader divide between *Bavel* and *Eretz Yisrael*, and historically between Sephardic and Ashkenazic practice, which in turn tracks whether these communities were embedded in polygamous or monogamous surrounding cultures. It is worth noting that even in polygamous cultures, multiple wives were practically possible only for wealthy men who could support them — which is precisely the limiting condition Rava articulates.

The *aguna* question hovers in the background here as well: in cases where the husband refuses to give a *get* while pursuing or maintaining another relationship, the question of whether *beit din* has the authority and will to actually compel the divorce and *kesubah* payment is distinct from what *halacha* says ought to happen. It is also worth noting — and this was confirmed in the course of discussion — that Rabbi Ami was from *Eretz Yisrael*, which tracks perfectly: Rabbi Ami's more restrictive view of polygamy aligns with the *Eretz Yisrael* tradition, while Rava's permissive view aligns with the Babylonian tradition.

## Women Sue Divorce

### Miscarriage Dispute

The *Gemara* turns to a new set of cases in which it is the woman who is suing for divorce. *Hu amar apalta b'go eser ve'hi amra lo apaliti* — he claims that she had a miscarriage during the ten-year period, such that he is not yet obligated to divorce her, since the ten-year clock has not run uninterrupted by any pregnancy. She denies having had a miscarriage and demands her divorce and *kesubah*. Note the curious posture here: she is the one pressing for divorce, presumably because she wishes to have the opportunity to remarry and have children.

Rabbi Ami rules: *af b'zu hi ne'emenes* — here too she is believed. The reason: *d'ei issa d'hapila nafshah b'akarta lo machzaka* — if it were true that she had not miscarried, she would not be claiming that she went the full ten years without even a miscarriage. By claiming there was no miscarriage, she is effectively establishing herself as infertile, which is *halachically* not a definitive ruling — she can still remarry — but practically is damaging to her prospects. As one participant noted with some wry understatement, it is not good for a *shidduch*. She would not fabricate a claim that stigmatizes her in this way if it were not true, and therefore we believe her even though the claim serves her interest in the immediate legal context by entitling her to a divorce with *kesubah*.

### Chazaka Miscarry

*Hapila v'chazra v'hapila v'chazra v'hapila* — what if she miscarried three times? In that case she does not have a *chazakah* of being unable to conceive, but she may well have a *chazakah* of being unable to carry to term. The *Gemara* rules: *huchzaka l'nefalim* — she has established a *chazakah* of miscarrying. Rashi explains: *yotzi v'yiten ketubah shema lo zachah libanos mimena* — he divorces her and gives the *kesubah*, because perhaps he has not been granted the merit of having children through her. The *kesubah* is still paid, because even with this established pattern it is not conclusively proven that the problem lies exclusively with her — it might be some combination — and so the *lo zachah l'hibanos mimena* framing keeps the possibility open that it is not solely her deficiency.

### Count Miscarriage

A dispute then arises: *hu amar hapila terei ve'hi amra telasa* — he claims she miscarried twice, not yet reaching the threshold for a *chazakah*, while she claims it was three times. This matters because three miscarriages triggers the *chazakah* and with it the obligation to divorce her and pay the *kesubah*.

### Negates Self

Rabbi Yochanan bar Elazar reports: *uvda hava b'bei midrasha de'amru hi mehemana* — a case came before the *beit midrash* and they ruled she is believed. The reasoning is the same as before: *d'ee issa d'lo aplah nafshah b'nefalim lo machzeka* — if it were false, she would not be willing to establish herself as a woman who regularly miscarries. The *beit midrash* credits her precisely because the claim, while advantageous to her in the immediate dispute, carries a broader self-stigmatizing dimension that she would not invoke falsely. Underlying all of these cases is a consistent pattern: she is believed over him across the full range of disputes, in each instance because her claim contains an element that works against her broader interests. This functions as something like a *migo* or an analogous credibility-enhancing consideration.

What is particularly striking is the uniformity of this pattern across such varied scenarios — the source of the infertility, the occurrence of a miscarriage, the count of miscarriages — and the interesting question this raises about what distinguishes these cases from earlier *mishnayot* in which the husband's claim was credited. Tosafot engages this question, though the details are sufficiently complicated as to require separate treatment.

## Peru u'Revu

### Mishnah Setup

The *Gemara* now transitions to the *mishnah* that concludes this *perek*: *haish metzuveh al peru u'revu aval lo haisha* — the man is commanded on *peru u'revu* and not the woman. This ruling is striking on its face, given that it is the woman who bears children.

### Both Commanded

Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka dissents, arguing that both are commanded, deriving this from the verse: *vayivarech osam Elokim vayomer lahem Elokim peru u'revu* — God blessed them and said to them, to both of them, be fruitful and multiply. This is a reasonable textual argument, and one that carries additional intuitive force given that it is the woman who carries and births children.

### Man Conquers

Rav Illa, in the name of Rabbi Elazar the son of Rabbi Shimon, derives the limitation to the man from the continuation of the same verse: *u'milu et ha'aretz v'chivshuha* — fill the land and conquer it. *Ish darko lichvosh v'ein isha darkah lichvosh* — it is the way of the man to conquer and not the way of the woman. Since the verse concludes with an act — conquest — that characterizes the man, the rabbis conclude that even the earlier command was addressed to the man alone.

### Plural Objection

An objection is raised: *adderaba, v'chivshuha tarti mashma* — on the contrary, the word *v'chivshuha* is in the plural form, implying *atem tichvishu osah* — you, in the plural, shall conquer it. How then can the verse be read as addressing only the man?

### Kri u'Kesiv

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves this: *v'chivshah ksiv* — the word is actually written in the text without the *vav*, making it singular rather than plural. The *kri* — the traditional reading — has it in the plural, but the *kesiv* — the written spelling — renders it singular. On the basis of this *kri u'chesiv*, the verse is read as addressing the man alone. It is quite striking that a verse which in its plain reading appears to address both husband and wife is restricted to the man alone on the basis of a defective spelling — but that is how the *Gemara* proceeds.

## Woman Exempt Why

### Lashevet

The *Gemara* having established the textual derivation, the question remains: what is the theological or conceptual rationale? One approach, grounded in the *Gemara* itself, connects *peru u'revu* to the broader principle of *lashevet* — settling and populating the world — derived from the verse *lo tohu b'ra'ah lashevet yetzarah*, God did not create the world to be void but to be inhabited. On this reading, *peru u'revu* is not merely about biological reproduction but about the larger project of *yishuv ha'olam*, building and sustaining civilization. That project, on a traditional division of roles, falls to the man as the one who goes out into the world to build society, while the woman remains in the domestic sphere. If this framing is correct, the *mitzvah* of *peru u'revu* as an aspect of settling the world naturally devolves upon the man. This is probably the most straightforward reading of the *Gemara*'s own logic in context, even if it carries its own set of assumptions.

### Patriarchy

A second explanation, noted by several *acharonim*, focuses on the verb *v'chivshuha* and reads it not as conquering the land but as the man's conquest, so to speak, in the act of sexual intercourse — connecting it to the *Gemara*'s broader framing of the man as the initiator and active party in relations, with the woman cast in a more passive role (*karka olam*). On this reading, since children come through the man's active role in the sexual act, the *mitzvah* is assigned to him as the agent. More broadly, the patriarchal structure of the family as understood throughout *Yevamot* — where children belong to the father's household, not the mother's — renders the father the primary party in the act of having children. This is reflected in the very language of *Tanach*: *Avraham holid et Yitzchak* — Abraham sired Isaac — where the woman, Sarah, is invisible in the formulation even though she is the one who gave birth. Within a social structure where children are born into and named for the father's household, it follows that the *mitzvah* of having them is primarily attributed to him.

### Feminist View

This dimension of the sugya raises what one might call the feminist critique: the woman is simultaneously burdened with the physical reality of pregnancy, labor, and childbirth, and excluded from receiving credit — *mitzvah* credit — for the very act she alone performs. She is assigned the traditional role of child-bearer by societal expectation, and then rendered invisible in the legal framework that assigns the corresponding *mitzvah* to the man. It is, as one might put it, the worst of both worlds: socially defined by her child-bearing role but not recognized *halachically* as the one obligated — or credited — in that act.

### Berman Freedom

Rabbi Saul Berman, in a well-known essay written approximately thirty years ago, proposed a reading that inverts this critique. On his account, the *halacha*'s exemption of the woman from *peru u'revu* is not a denial of her role but a liberation from a compelled one. By not obligating her in *peru u'revu*, the *halacha* leaves open the possibility that a woman may choose not to marry, or choose not to have children, and pursue a more independent path without violating a Torah obligation. The exemption thus functions as a grant of freedom rather than an erasure. Readers may evaluate which of these readings they find more persuasive — or reject both — but the question itself is one the sugya genuinely invites.

## Rule Like Broka

### Yochanan Dispute

The *Gemara* now turns to the *amoraic* dispute over the *halacha*: *itmar, Rabbi Yochanan v'Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — there is a recorded dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, one of whom says we rule like Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka that the woman is also commanded, and one of whom says we do not rule like him.

### Don't Rule Broka

The *Gemara* seeks to determine which *amora* held which position: *tistayayeim d'Rabbi Yochanan hu d'amar ein halacha* — let us conclude that it is Rabbi Yochanan who says we do not rule like Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka. The basis for this conclusion is discussed in the next segment.

---

## Practical Diff

### Stay Single

Before examining the evidence for how Rabbi Yochanan ruled, it is worth pausing to consider what practical difference this dispute makes — because beyond the abstract question of who receives *mitzvah* credit, the *halacha* has substantial real-world implications. If a woman is not commanded on *peru u'revu*, can she choose to remain unmarried without violating any Torah obligation? On the view that she is not commanded, the answer would appear to be yes.

### Demand Divorce

A second practical difference: suppose a man has already had children from a previous wife but his current wife has not. If the woman is commanded, she has the basis to demand a divorce so that she may remarry and attempt to have children with another man. If she is not commanded, it is less clear what legal standing she has to compel such a divorce, as we will see in the stories below.

### Birth Control

A third and particularly significant contemporary implication: if a woman is not commanded on *peru u'revu*, may she use contraception freely? Rav Moshe Feinstein raised precisely this question — if a woman is not commanded, perhaps she may use hormonal contraception without *halachic* objection, since she bears no obligation regarding *peru u'revu*. Of course, the residual question then becomes whether she is permitted to do so if it prevents her husband from fulfilling *his* obligation, since the context in which he can fulfill it is dependent on her. But the question is genuinely opened by the exemption, and the story of Yehudit later in this sugya is one of the primary *Talmudic* sources engaged in that discussion.

## Rule Like Broka…

### Abbahu/Chiya

Returning to the question of how Rabbi Yochanan ruled: *d'yativ Rabbi Abbahu v'ka'amar mishmeih d'Rabbi Yochanan: halacha* — Rabbi Abbahu sat and declared in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, meaning the woman is also commanded. There is then a textual variant: some say it was not Rabbi Abbahu but Rabbi Chiya bar Abba who transmitted this ruling in Rabbi Yochanan's name.

### Face Away

In either version: *hadreinh Rabbi Ami v'Rav Assi l'apeihu* — Rabbi Ami and Rav Assi turned their faces away from the speaker. They did not openly contradict him, but their gesture of disapproval made clear that they believed this ruling was incorrect and did not represent Rabbi Yochanan's actual position.

### Rav Pappa Why

Rav Pappa raises a question about the textual variant: *bishlama* if it was Rabbi Abbahu who said it, then one can understand why Rabbi Ami and Rav Assi merely turned away rather than explicitly contradicting him — *mishum kevod beit Keisar*, because Rabbi Abbahu had a close relationship with the imperial court and they did not want to publicly contradict someone of his standing; hence their silent expression of displeasure rather than open refutation. But *ella l'man d'amar* that it was Rabbi Chiya bar Abba who said it — who is Rabbi Chiya bar Abba that they could not simply tell him directly that Rabbi Yochanan did not rule this way? Why would they resort to turning their faces rather than saying *leimru lei lama amar Rabbi Yochanan hachi* — just tell him explicitly that Rabbi Yochanan did not say this?

The conclusion that emerges is that Rabbi Ami and Rav Assi clearly believed Rabbi Yochanan held that we do *not* rule like Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka, and that the *halacha* is therefore that the *mitzvah* of *peru u'revu* falls on the man alone.

## Not Commanded Cases

### Rabbi Ami

The *Gemara* now presents narrative episodes illustrating the practical stakes. *Ki ha de'asa l'kamei d'Rabbi Ami* — a woman came before Rabbi Ami. From the context, the man in question apparently already had children from another woman, so the case is not one where he is being compelled to divorce in order to fulfill *peru u'revu* himself. She, on the other hand, has not had children and wishes to be divorced so she can remarry. He wants to remain married. She demands a divorce with *kesubah*. Rabbi Ami says to her: *zil lo mifkadat* — go, you are not commanded on *peru u'revu*, and therefore you have no legal basis for compelling a divorce with *kesubah*. Without a commanded obligation, there is no cause of action, and a divorce without cause would not entitle her to the *kesubah*.

### Old Age Need

The woman responds: *l'siva d'la mai tehvei alah* — but what about her old age? Who will take care of her? The implicit claim is that regardless of any formal *mitzvah*, she has a fundamental human need and desire to have children — people who will support her in old age — and that this constitutes sufficient cause to compel a divorce with *kesubah*.

Rabbi Ami accepts the argument: *hi vadai kafinan* — in that case, we will certainly compel it. The woman's basic human interest in having children, quite apart from any *mitzvah* obligation, is recognized as legitimate cause. The practical import is significant: even without a formal *mitzvah*, the desire to have children — grounded in the very human need for support and continuity — gives her standing to sue for divorce with *kesubah*.

### Rav Nachman

The same argument plays out again before Rav Nachman. He too initially tells the woman she is not commanded and therefore cannot demand a *kesubah*.

### Grave Need

She responds: *lo ba'ya hach iteta chutra l'yadah u'mara l'kvurah* — does this woman not need a staff for her hand and a shovel for her grave? She needs someone to support her in old age and to bury her when she dies. Rav Nachman accepts this: *ki ha vadai kafinan* — in that case we will certainly compel the divorce. The story carries a wry edge: one might wonder why *beit din* should have to wait for the woman to articulate this claim herself, rather than assuming it from the outset. The fact that the judges do wait reveals something about how automatic or invisible these basic human needs were in the context of the legal discourse of the time.

## Yehudit

### Twin Birth

The *Gemara* introduces the story of Yehudit, the wife of Rabbi Chiya. *Yehudah v'Chizkiyah te'omei havu* — Yehudah and Chizkiyah were twins, one whose formation was completed after nine months and one after seven. This unusual pregnancy — two children forming at different intervals within the womb — caused their mother, Yehudit, *tza'ara d'leidah*, severe pain in childbirth.

### Disguise Query

*Shinatah manah* — she disguised herself, changing her clothes, and came before her husband Rabbi Chiya as if she were a different woman. *Amrah lei: iteta mifkeda apirya v'rivya?* — she asked him: is a woman commanded on *peru u'revu*?

### Chiya Answers No

*Amar lah: lo* — he answered: no, she is not.

### Sterilize

*Azlah ashtaya sama d'akrta* — she went and drank a sterilizing potion, a medical preparation that rendered her infertile, because she could not endure the prospect of more pregnancies like the last one, and her *posek* had told her she was not obligated to do so.

### Matter Revealed

*U'l'sof igli milta* — eventually the matter came to light. Rabbi Chiya discovered what she had done. He said to her: *eichu yaladt li chada karsa achriti* — I wish you had waited to give us one more brood of children. He was, apparently, already *yotzei peru u'revu* twice over: *d'amar mar, Yehudah v'Chizkiyah achi* — Yehudah and Chizkiyah, his sons, were his; and *Pazi v'Tavi achvata* — Pazi and Tavi, his daughters, were also his, apparently another pair of twins. So he had already fulfilled his obligation with two boys and two girls, even according to Beit Shammai's standard. His lament was not that she had deprived him of his *mitzvah* but that they could have had one more cohort of children.

The *halachic* question this story raises is whether it constitutes evidence that a woman who is not commanded on *peru u'revu* may use sterilizing contraception without restriction, leaving the consequences for the husband's fulfillment of his *mitzvah* as his concern alone. The complicating factor here is that the husband was already *yotzei*, so the story cannot straightforwardly be extended to cases where he has not yet fulfilled his obligation. Nevertheless, it is one of the central *Talmudic* sources in discussions of women and birth control.

## Half Slave

### Half Free Freed

*V'amar Rav Acha bar Katina amar Rabbi Yitzchak: ma'aseh b'ishah achat she-chatziyah shifchah v'chatziyah bat chorin v'kafu et rabah v'asa'ah bat chorin* — a case is cited in which a woman who was half slave and half free was compelled by *beit din* to be fully freed. The parallel case involving a man in this status has appeared earlier in *Yevamot*. One might infer from this ruling that the woman must be freed in order to be able to have children, which would in turn suggest she bears some form of obligation regarding *peru u'revu* — otherwise why compel her master to free her?

### Sex Protection

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak rejects this inference: *minhag hefker nahagu bah* — the compulsion was not because she had an obligation to have children, but because her intermediate status exposed her to sexual exploitation. A woman who can neither marry a free man nor a slave is left with no legitimate marital option, and as a result was vulnerable to being taken advantage of sexually. *Beit din* intervened to protect her from that situation, not to vindicate a *mitzvah* obligation. One cannot therefore derive from this case that the woman is commanded on *peru u'revu*.

## Conclusion

### Bavli View

The consistent weight of the *Bavli* is that the *mitzvah* of *peru u'revu* is not incumbent upon the woman. Case after case — the ruling of Rabbi Ami, the turning away of Rabbi Ami and Rav Assi from the claim that Rabbi Yochanan ruled otherwise, the resolution of the half-slave case by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — all point in the same direction.

### Yerushalmi

It should be noted, however, that the *Yerushalmi* treats this question in a more open way, and at least creates the impression that there is a genuine ongoing debate over the woman's obligation, or that the ruling may actually go in the direction of obligating her. The *Bavli* arrives at a firm conclusion; the *Yerushalmi* does not appear to foreclose the question in the same way.

---

## Woman Exempt Why…

### Childbirth Risk

One final explanatory approach to the woman's exemption is worth noting, even though it cannot be found in the *Gemara* itself: the *Meshech Chochma*, writing approximately 150 years ago, proposed that since childbirth involves genuine physical risk to the woman's life, the *Torah* could not impose it as a *mitzvah* obligation upon her. A *mitzvah* that mandates a person to undertake life-threatening danger as a positive obligation would be problematic, and therefore the *Torah* leaves *peru u'revu* as a free choice for the woman rather than a commanded act. This explanation resonates with many people, and one hears it cited frequently as though it were a traditional *Talmudic* position; in fact, as best one can determine, it originates with the *Meshech Chochma* and has no earlier source. Whether it captures the *Gemara*'s reasoning is another question entirely.

---

## Conclusion…

### Tosafot

Tosafot raises an interesting position that also deserves mention in this context: even if a woman is not technically commanded in *peru u'revu* in its formal sense, she may nonetheless be included in the broader mandate of *lashevet* — settling and populating the world, derived from *lo tohu b'ra'ah lashevet yetzarah*. This is one opinion in one Tosafot, and it is not codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*, but it receives serious attention in *halachic* discussion. The implication is that even without the technical form of the *mitzvah*, the woman partakes in its underlying value — not through strict obligation but through a kind of mandated orientation toward the project of *yishuv ha'olam*. Whether this constitutes *psak halacha* is genuinely debated, but it is an important position to be aware of.

## New Topic

### Perek Start

With the conclusion of this *perek* — *hadran alach HaBa Yevimto* — the *Gemara* opens the following chapter, which returns to the domain of *kehunah* and forbidden marriages. The opening *mishnah* establishes the cases: *almana l'Kohen Gadol, gerusha v'chalutza l'Kohen Hedyot* — a widow married to a *Kohen Gadol*, or a divorcée or *chalutza* married to a regular *kohen*. These are marriages that are *halachically* forbidden yet legally binding — the child is not a *mamzer*, but the woman, through the forbidden act of sexual intercourse with a *kohen*, becomes a *chalala*, disqualified from the benefits she would otherwise enjoy by virtue of being a *kohen*'s wife.

### Servants Brought

The *mishnah* addresses a specific scenario within these forbidden marriages: *hechnisa lo avdei melog v'avdei tzon barzel* — the woman brings into the marriage servants who belong to two different legal categories, *nichsei melog* or *nichsei tzon barzel*. The status of those servants with respect to eating *terumah* will turn on which category they belong to, and that in turn depends on how ownership and title are structured in each type.

---

## Property Types

### Tzon Barzel

*Nichsei tzon barzel* — literally "iron sheep property" — refers to property she brings into the marriage where title transfers entirely to the husband. Upon bringing in, say, a parcel of real estate, the deed is effectively signed over to him; he becomes the full legal owner. However, the value of that property is assessed at the time of entry into the marriage, and that assessed value constitutes a debt he owes her: if they divorce or if he predeceases her, she is entitled to recover that sum. This is precisely what is reflected in the standard *ketubah* text, where the groom accepts responsibility for all the property she brings in — *bein b'manei d'levusha bein b'manei d'arsa* — at an assessed value. The groom acquires full title but is liable for the value. With respect to slaves classified as *nichsei tzon barzel*, they thus become his slaves entirely during the marriage.

### Melog

*Nichsei melog* operates differently. The wife retains title to the property — it remains hers — but the husband enjoys the *kinyan peiros*, the right to benefit from the property's fruits, its *usufruct*, during the marriage. Rental income from her land, produce from her fields, the labor of her slaves — all of this goes to him during the marriage. Correspondingly, he bears the obligation to maintain those assets: *af al pi she-hu chayav b'mzonoson* — even though he is responsible for feeding her *melog* slaves. But the title remains hers. If the slaves die, the loss is hers; if they increase in value or produce children, the gain is hers. He has rights over the benefit but not the ownership.

---

## Terumah Rights

### Normal Both

In a normal, permitted marriage between a *kohen* and a woman he may marry — say, a *bat Yisrael* or *bat kohen* — both categories of servants eat *terumah*. The servants classified as *tzon barzel* eat by virtue of him, since title has passed to him and they are his slaves. The servants classified as *melog* eat by virtue of her, since she, as the wife of a *kohen*, is herself entitled to eat *terumah*, and her slaves eat through her.

### Chalala Block

Now the complication: when the marriage is a forbidden one and the woman thereby becomes a *chalala*, she loses her entitlement to eat *terumah*. She cannot eat it because she is now disqualified from the priestly benefits she would otherwise enjoy as a *kohen*'s wife. This disqualification flows from the forbidden union itself.

The question is what happens to her *melog* slaves. The *tzon barzel* slaves are straightforward: title passed to him, they are his slaves, they eat *terumah* through him, and his priestly status is unaffected — a *kohen* never loses his own priestly standing through a forbidden marriage, only the woman is degraded. So the *tzon barzel* slaves eat. But the *melog* slaves are still *her* slaves. Their right to eat *terumah* would have to flow through her, and since she herself cannot eat, the question arises whether they can.

A diagram may help clarify the issue. In a normal case, the *kohen* is at the top, his wife — whether *bat Yisrael* or *bat kohen* — eats *terumah* by virtue of the marriage, the *tzon barzel* slaves eat by virtue of him directly, and the *melog* slaves eat by virtue of her, which is legitimate because she herself is entitled. But when she becomes a *chalala*, she is cut off from *terumah*: she cannot eat it, and therefore the *melog* slaves, whose eating must flow through her, cannot eat either. The *tzon barzel* slaves, eating directly through him, are unaffected.

### Mishnah Rule

The *mishnah* states the rule explicitly: *v'avdei milug lo yochlu bitrumah v'avdei tzon barzel yochlu bitrumah* — the *melog* servants do not eat *terumah* and the *tzon barzel* servants do eat. The *mishnah* then defines each category: *melog* slaves are those for whom *im meisu meisu lah* — if they die, the loss is hers — and *im hosiru hosiru lah* — if they increase in value, the gain is hers — even though *af al pi she-hu chayav b'mzonoson*, he must feed them. *Tzon barzel* slaves are those for whom *im meisu meisu lo* — if they die, the loss is his — and *im hosiru hosiru lo* — if they appreciate, the gain is his — *ho'il v'hu chayav b'achrayusan*, since he bears full responsibility for them. In the normal case of a *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen*, both types eat *terumah*; in the case of a *bat kohen* married to a *Yisrael*, neither type eats, since she has lost her entitlement upon marrying a non-*kohen* and he never had it.

---

## Why Not Eat

### Indirect Query

The *Gemara* now asks: why should the *melog* slaves not eat *terumah*? The *kohen* does own something in relation to those slaves — he owns the *kinyan peiros*, the right of usufruct. Let the wife be regarded as *his* acquisition in some sense, and the slaves as her acquisition, so that we view the *melog* slaves as an indirect acquisition of his own: *lahavei kekinyano shekana kinyan* — let it be treated as his acquired property that itself has acquired additional property. If the *Gemara* elsewhere extends *terumah* eating rights through indirect ownership chains, why not here?

### Scripture

The *Gemara* answers from the verse: *v'Kohen ki yikneh nefesh kinyan kaspo hu yochal bo* — when a *kohen* acquires a soul through his money, that soul shall eat of it. From this the *Gemara* derives the right of a *kohen*'s wife and slaves to eat *terumah*, and extends it: *kinyano shekana kinyan* — if the *kohen*'s acquisition itself acquires another being, that being may also eat. So a wife can eat, her slaves can eat, his slaves' slaves can eat. The chain of indirect ownership can support *terumah* rights.

### Ochel Principle

But the *Gemara* then formulates a limiting principle: *kol ha'ochel ma'achil, kol she'eino ochel eino ma'achil* — whoever can eat herself enables her dependents to eat; whoever cannot eat herself cannot enable her dependents to eat. The *melog* slaves eat by virtue of the wife. If she can eat — if she is a legitimate *eishes kohen* entitled to *terumah* — then she enables them. If she cannot eat because she has become a *chalala*, the enabling link is broken, and they cannot eat through her.

### Areil Exception

An apparent exception is raised: what about a *kohen* who is an *areil* — uncircumcised, whether due to medical necessity or other reason — or who is *tamei*, ritually impure? Such a *kohen* may not eat *terumah* himself, yet his wife and slaves may still eat. If the principle is that only one who can eat enables others to eat, this seems to be a counterexample: here is one who cannot eat, yet he enables others to eat.

### Temporary vs Permanent

The *Gemara* resolves this by drawing a distinction based on the nature of the disqualification: *hasam pumayu ka'iv lehu* — there, their mouth is hurting them, meaning the impediment is a personal, temporary incapacity, not a fundamental change in status. An *areil* could in principle become circumcised — whatever the practical obstacles — and a *tamei* person can always become *tahor*. The disqualification is situational and reversible. A *chalala*, by contrast, is in a permanent state: the forbidden sexual union has categorically changed her status, and there is no mechanism by which she reverts. A permanent status-change severs the enabling link entirely; a temporary personal incapacity does not.
