# Shekalim 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bedek HaBayit Redemption

### Intro & Approach

Today's daf is 13, and we pick up at the very bottom of 12a. This passage proved difficult at the end of the previous shiur — and rightly so, as it is much debated among the commentators. The explanation presented here follows that of the *Tosafot HaRosh*, which is, on balance, the simplest available.

### Unblemished Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חוּנָה בְשֵׁם רַב רִבִּי אַבָּהוּ בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. קָדְשֵׁי בֶדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁפּוֹדִין תְּמִימִין יָצְאוּ לְחוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥuna in the name of Rav, Rebbi Abbahu in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan: *Sancta* given for the upkeep of the Temple which were redeemed unblemished become profane.

The *gemara* opens with a statement transmitted by Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav, citing Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kodshei bedek ha-bayit she-pe'd'an temimim yatze'u le-chullin* — animals sanctified for *bedek ha-bayit* that were redeemed while still unblemished exit entirely into the domain of the ordinary.

To understand this, one must recall the fundamental distinction between the two types of *kedushah*. *Kedushah* of *bedek ha-bayit* is essentially a sanctity of ownership — the Temple treasury acquires a proprietary interest in the animal. As such, even if one sanctifies an unblemished animal for *bedek ha-bayit* (which one is technically not supposed to do, yet if done — whether explicitly or through the generic *kiddush* discussed in our *mishnah* — the *kedushah* takes effect), and subsequently redeems it without a *mum*, one is entitled to do so. Since this *kedushah* is about ownership rather than purpose, the animal upon redemption has no residual *kedushah* whatsoever — it is *yatza le-chullin* in the fullest sense.

This stands in contrast to a *korban*, which, even after developing a *mum* and being redeemed, retains a residual sanctity: one may not shear it, work it, or benefit from its milk.

### Mizbe'ach Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתַא אָמְרָה כֵן. וּוְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן מוּתָּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָם. רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה בְשֵׁם רַב חִסְדָּא. תִּיפְתָּר שֶׁפּוֹדִין תְּמִימִין וְהוּמְמוּ. רִבִּי יּוֹסֵה בְשֵׁם רַב חִסְדָּא. מַתְנִיתַא אָמְרָה כֵן. וּוְלָדָן וַחֲלָבָן אָסוּר לְאַחַר פִּדְיוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** A Mishnah says so. The Mishnah states that blemished animals which were dedicated as sacrifices never become intrinsically holy; if they are redeemed they are fully profane, their offspring and their milk is profane (first quote). If they were validly dedicated and later developed a blemish, they have to be redeemed and used as profane food, but cannot totally lose their sacred status; they cannot be used for work nor are their offspring and their milk permitted (second quote, also *Temurah*7:1.), "their offspring and their milk are permitted after their redemption." Rebbi Ḥizqiah in the name of Rav Ḥisda: Explain it if they were redeemed unblemished and then became defective. Rebbi Yose in the name of Rav Ḥisda: Does the Mishnah say so? "Their offspring and their milk are forbidden after their redemption."

The *gemara* then invokes a *mishnah* — *matnitin amar ken* — noting that a *mishnah* in Chullin supports this. That *mishnah* addresses a case of *kodshei mizbe'ach* where a *mum* preceded the act of sanctification. The *mishnah* there distinguishes between two scenarios. If an animal is sanctified for a *korban* and subsequently develops a *mum*, the original *kedushah* was a full *kedushat korban*, and even after redemption some residual *kedushah* remains. However, if the animal already had a *mum* when sanctified, it was never truly fit for the *mizbe'ach*, and the *kedushah* it received is not a genuine *kedushat mizbe'ach* in the full sense.

### Chullin Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי חִזְקִיָּה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יּוֹסֵה. קָדְשֵׁי בֶדֶק הַבַּיִת שֶׁפָּדְיָין תְּמִימִים יָצְאוּ לְחוּלִין. אִין תֵּימַר. לֹא יָצְאוּ לְחוּלִין. הֵיאַךְ קָדְשֵׁי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ חָלִין עַל קָדְשֵׁי בֶדֶק הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ḥizqiah in the name of Rebbi Yose: *Sancta* given for the upkeep of the Temple which were redeemed unblemished become profane. If you are saying they did not become profane, how could the sanctity of the altar fall on *sancta* given for the upkeep of the Temple?

There is something analogous between sanctifying an already-blemished animal for the *mizbe'ach* and sanctifying something directly for *bedek ha-bayit* — in both cases the animal was never truly fit for the altar at the moment of sanctification. The *mishnah* in Chullin teaches that when a *mum* preceded the *kedushah* for the *mizbe'ach*, the ruling is *vladan va-chalavan mutar le-achar pidyonan* — the offspring and the milk are permitted after redemption. The *gemara* takes this to mean the animal is entirely *yatza le-chullin*. By analogy, the same ought to be true when the animal was sanctified directly for *bedek ha-bayit*, and perhaps even without any *mum* at all.

Similarly, a *mishnah* in Temurah articulates the contrast from the other direction: *kodshei mizbe'ach* that are sanctified and then develop a *mum* result in a ruling of *vladan va-chalavan asur* — the offspring and milk remain forbidden after redemption. The *mishnah* implies by contrast that *kodshei bedek ha-bayit* would yield *vladan va-chalavan mutar*. Again, however, the cases being contrasted all involve a *mum* at some stage, whether before or after sanctification.

### Chizkiyah's Challenge

At this point Rav Chizkiyah in the name of Rav Chisda raises a challenge — *hipatir* — essentially rejecting the proposed analogy. Following the reading of the *Gra*, the response is that one cannot draw a proof from the case of a *mum* to the case of an unblemished *bedek ha-bayit* animal. In the cases under discussion, the animal at least had a *mum*; it is not necessarily true that an unblemished *bedek ha-bayit* animal would be equally and completely desanctified upon redemption.

One might ask: is the comparison not compelling? If the *kedushah* never had the character of *kedushat mizbe'ach* — either because the animal was blemished at sanctification or because it was sanctified directly for *bedek ha-bayit* — the *kedushah* in both cases ought to be fully removable. Yet the *gemara* maintains the distinction. One possible rationale is practical: if one sees an animal being used freely after redemption but the animal is unblemished and known to have been sanctified, that creates a far more significant public impression than seeing the same with a visibly blemished animal. So even if the *kedushah* types are genuinely comparable, the presence of a *mum* is what in practice allows for complete *yetzi'ah le-chulin*, and one cannot extrapolate to the unblemished case.

Rabbi Yosei in the name of Rav Chisda then reasserts — *matnitin amrah ken* — still insisting the *mishnah* supports complete desanctification. The full arc of his argument draws on both the implicit contrast (from the Temurah *mishnah*) and the parallel (from the Chullin *mishnah*), but in every case the textual basis involves a *mum*. The *gemara*'s conclusion is therefore that while the analogies are suggestive, one cannot derive a definitive ruling about unblemished *bedek ha-bayit* redemption from these sources alone — since in all the supporting cases, a *mum* is present.

The argument from our own *mishnah* is ultimately the strongest. Rabbi Chizkiyah in the name of Rabbi Yosei insists: *kodshei bedek ha-bayit she-pe'd'an temimim yatze'u le-chullin*. The proof is that if they did not fully exit into *chullin*, one could never impose a *kedushat mizbe'ach* upon them afterward. Yet our *mishnah*, according to Rabbi Yehoshua, says that the unblemished animals from a generic *kiddush* — once belonging to *bedek ha-bayit* — are sold, the proceeds go to *bedek ha-bayit*, and the buyer goes on to bring an *olah*. How could that buyer bring the animal as a *korban* if it still retained any residual *kedushah*? One can only sanctify something for a *korban* if it is completely *chullin*. This is a powerful proof from our *mishnah* that unblemished *bedek ha-bayit* animals, once redeemed, are fully desanctified.

## Female Sanctity Status

### R. Shimon Gender

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין קְדוּשַׁת הַמִּזְבֵּחַ חָלָה עֲלֵיהֶן. לֵיְידְא מִילָּה. לַגִּיזָה וְלָעֲבוֹדָה. הִפְרִישׁ נְקֵיבָה לְעוֹלָתוֹ וּלְפִּסְחוֹ וּלְאֲשָׁמוֹ עוֹשֶׂה תְמוּרָה. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר. לְעוֹלָתוֹ עוֹשֶׂה תְמוּרָה. לְפִסְחוֹ וּלְאֲשָׁמוֹ אֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה תְמוּרָה. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. לְעוֹלָתוֹ. וּלְפִּסְחוֹ וּלְאֲשָׁמוֹ אֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה תְמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The sanctity of the altar falls on defective animals. In which respect? For shearing and work. "If somebody dedicated a female for his elevation offering, or his *Pesaḥ*, or his reparation offering, it effects substitution. Rebbi Simeon says, for his elevation offering it effects substitution; for his *Pesaḥ* or his reparation offering it does not effect substitution." "Rebbi Simeon ben Jehudah says in the name of Rebbi Simeon, neither for his elevation offering, nor his *Pesaḥ*, nor his reparation offering, does it effect substitution."

The *gemara* now turns to a new set of questions arising from a *mishnah*, asking what *kedushah* — if any — attaches to animals sanctified for the wrong purpose by reason of gender. The case: *hifrish nekevah le-olato u-le-fischo u-le-ashamo* — one set aside a female animal and sanctified it as an *olah*, or as a *pesach*, or as an *asham*, all of which require a male. The first position is that such an animal is *oseh temurah* — it possesses enough *kedushat korban* that were one to attempt an exchange (*temurah*), the *kedushah* would take effect on the substitute animal. The very act of *temurah* is a biblical prohibition, but the *halacha* is that although one transgresses, the second animal nonetheless becomes *kadosh* while the first retains its *kedushah*.

Rabbi Shimon, however, distinguishes: *le-olato oseh temurah* — for an *olah*, yes, the female achieves enough *kedushat korban* to make *temurah*; *le-fischo u-le-ashamo ein oseh temurah* — but for a *pesach* or an *asham*, no. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda then transmits in the name of Rabbi Shimon an even broader position: *le-olato le-fischo u-le-ashamo ein oseh temurah* — for none of them does it make *temurah*.

These two positions attributed to (or in the name of) Rabbi Shimon thus represent a tripartite range: the first *tanna* holds that all three achieve *kedushah* sufficient for *temurah*; Rabbi Shimon distinguishes *olah* from the others; and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda in Rabbi Shimon's name says none of them achieve such *kedushah*.

### Bird Precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. שֶׁכֵּן מָצָאנוּ נְקֵיבָה בָעוֹף כְשֵׁירָה לָבוֹא עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, the reason of Rebbi Simeon is that we find that a female is qualified as elevation offering of a bird.

Rabbi Yochanan explains the reasoning of the first Rabbi Shimon — the one who distinguishes *olah* from *pesach* and *asham*: *sheken matzinu nekevah be-of kesherah lavo olah* — since a female bird is entirely valid to be brought as an *olah*, the concept of "female" is not foreign to the *parshah* of *olah*. Consequently, when one sanctifies a female animal as an *olah*, that animal is sufficiently within the *parshah* to acquire genuine *kedushat korban*, and it therefore makes *temurah*. By contrast, a female is entirely foreign to the *parshah* of *pesach* and *asham*, and therefore acquires no such *kedushah*.

### R. Yochanan Compare

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. אִם מִין בְּמִינוֹ הוּא חָלוּק עָלָיו לֹא כָל שֶׁכֵּן מִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** [And]Rebbi Joḥanan said, the reason of Rebbi Simeon is, if there are differences in its own kind, so much more if it is not its own kind.

Rabbi Yochanan then explains the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, who says that even for an *olah* the female makes no *temurah*: *im min be-mino hu chaluk alav, kol sheken min she-eino* — if something of the same fundamental type but differing in a single characteristic (like age) is already so different as to not receive *kedushah*, how much more so something of the wrong gender entirely.

The illustrative case is from a *baraita*: an *asham* requiring a one-year-old for which one brought a two-year-old, or requiring a two-year-old for which one brought a three-year-old — *lo yatza*. One is not *yotzei* with the *korban*. And most *mefarshim* read the following line, *Rabbi Shimon omer kol atzman einam kedoshim*, to mean that the animals do not even become *kadosh* at all. Since the tradition of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda is attributed to Rabbi Shimon, the reasoning follows: if even a difference of one year in an animal that is otherwise entirely appropriate — right species, right gender, right in every other respect — suffices to prevent *kedushah* according to Rabbi Shimon, then certainly the wrong gender falls entirely outside the *parshah*.

## Sanctity Distinctions

### Sanctity Categories

At this point in the analysis it becomes necessary to introduce a more precise conceptual framework than the simple *mizbe'ach*/*bedek ha-bayit* dichotomy. The more exact categories are *kedushat haguf* and *kedushat damim*.

*Kedushat haguf* is intrinsic sanctity: the object itself is to be used — the animal itself is to be brought as a *korban*, the beam itself is to be incorporated into the *Beit HaMikdash*. *Kedushat damim* is a sanctity of value: the object itself will not be used, but will be sold and the proceeds used in its stead. These categories cross-cut the *mizbe'ach*/*bedek ha-bayit* distinction. A stone sanctified to be built into the *Beit HaMikdash* has *kedushat haguf* that is *le-bedek ha-bayit*. And there exists a category of *kedushat damim le-mizbe'ach* — a sanctity of proceeds designated for the *mizbe'ach* — which is the key to understanding several of the cases that follow.

## Female Sanctity Status…

### R. Abba Pesach/Asham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי אַבָּהוּ בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דַּבֵּר אֶל אַהֲרֹן וְאֶל בָּנָיו וגו׳. אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיב לַיי לְעוֹלָה. הַכֹּל קָרֵב לְעוֹלָה. לִרְצ‍וֹנְכֶם תָּמִים זָכָר. יָכוֹל אֲפִילוּ עוֹפוֹת. תַּלָמוּד לוֹמַר בַּבָּקָר. לֹא עוֹפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abbahu in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish. The reason of Rebbi Joshua: *Speak to Aaron and to his sons*, etc., *who would sacrifice to the Eternal as elevation sacrifice;* everything may be brought as elevation sacrifice, *by your volition, unblemished, male*. I could think that this includes birds. The verse says, *in cattle*, not birds.

Rabbi Abba clarifies why Rabbi Yochanan's original question — why not extend the reasoning to *asham* as well, since leftover *asham* funds go toward an *olah*, and an *olah* as a bird can be female — does not succeed. The difference is this: by a *pesach*, *gufo karev shelamim* — the animal body itself is brought as *shelamim* after *Pesach*. A female is therefore genuinely within the *parshah* of *pesach* because the animal itself can serve in that capacity. By contrast, by an *asham* and an *olah*, *ein gufo karev olah* — the *asham* animal itself is never brought as an *olah*; only its redeemed value is transferred. The animal body itself thus never serves as an *olah*, and the connection is too attenuated.

## Sanctity Distinctions…

### R. Yehoshua Female

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וְרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ שְׁנֵיהֶן אָמְרוּ דָבָר אֶחָד. כְּמַה דְרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר. נְקֵיבָה לְעוֹלָה לֹא קָדְשָׁה אֶלָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ דָּמִים. כֵּן רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמַר. נְקֵיבָה לְעוֹלָה לֹא קָדְשָׁה אֶלָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Joḥanan said, Rebbi Simeon and Rebbi Joshua said the same. As Rebbi Joshua said, a female as elevation offering is sanctified only for its money's worth, so Rebbi Simeon said, a female as elevation offering is sanctified only for its money's worth.

This distinction leads directly to an important analysis of Rabbi Yehoshua's position in the *mishnah*. Rabbi Yochanan states: *Rabbi Shimon ve-Rabbi Yehoshua shneihem amru davar echad* — Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehoshua effectively say the same thing. How so? Rabbi Yehoshua says in the *mishnah* that when one sanctifies all one's property generically, the unblemished female animals acquire *kedushat olah* — yet in practice one sells them and uses the proceeds for an *olah*, while the animals themselves go as *zivchei shelamim*. The implication is that the female animal acquires *kedushat olah* in a meaningful sense, yet one is permitted to sell it *without* a *mum*. How can an animal with *kedushat olah* be redeemed without a *mum*?

### Redemption Mechanism

The answer is that the female's unsuitability for an *olah* is itself a significant enough deficiency that it pushes the *kedushah* out of the domain of *kedushat haguf* and into the domain of *kedushat damim*. It was never truly sanctified in its body for use as an *olah*; its *kedushah* from the outset was only as proceeds — *kedushat damim*. This explains the apparent paradox: since the *kedushah* is *kedushat damim*, the animal can be sold without a *mum*. But since the *kedushah* is *le-mizbe'ach*, the proceeds must be used for an *olah*.

### Proceeds Purpose

This subtle category — *kedushat damim le-mizbe'ach* — is what enables this hybrid result: the animal may be redeemed without a *mum* because it was always about its proceeds rather than the thing itself; yet the proceeds are designated *le-mizbe'ach*, so they must go toward an *olah*. This explains both Rabbi Yehoshua in the *mishnah* and, by parallel, Rabbi Shimon's position that a female for an *olah* does not make *temurah* — since one who has only *kedushat damim* cannot make *temurah*.

## Generic Sanctification

### Generic Olah Creation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי זְעוּרָה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דַּבֵּר אֶל אַהֲרֹן וְאֶל בָּנָיו וְאֶל כָּל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל וְאָמַרְתָּ אֲלֵהֶם אִישׁ אִישׁ מִבֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיב לַיי לְעוֹלָה. הַכֹּל קָרֵב לְעוֹלָה. לִרְצ‍וֹנְכֶם תָּמִים זָכָר. מְנַיִין אֲפִילוּ נְקֵיבוֹת. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר בַּבָּקָר לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַנְּקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Ze`ira in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish. The reason of Rebbi Joshua: *Speak to Aaron, and his sons, and to all Children of Israel*, etc. *who will**offer an elevation sacrifice to the Eternal*, anything could be elevation sacrifice, *voluntarily from you, unblemished, male.* From where even females? The verse says, *in cattle*, to include females.

Rabbi Zeira in the name of Rabbi Shimon Lakish now turns to the textual basis for Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling. The verse cited is *daber el Aharon ve-el banav ve-el kol bnei Yisrael ve-amarta aleihem ish ish mi-beit Yisrael asher yakriv la-Hashem le-olah* — the general rule for *korbanot* is that a generic *kedushah* applied to animals creates *kedushat olah*. The verse's scope, *hakol kareiv le-olah*, makes the *olah* the default category.

The verse then specifies *tamim zachar* — unblemished male — for one's desire. The question arises: if only males are mentioned, from where does Rabbi Yehoshua include females in the *kedushat olah*? The answer is *talmud lomar ba-bakar* — the word "cattle" is generic and encompasses females as well, extending the *kedushah* to unblemished females.

### R. Elazar Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי אַבָּהוּ בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי לָעְזָר. דַּבֵּר אֶל אַהֲרֹן וְאֶל בָּנָיו וגו׳. אֲשֶׁר יַקְרִיב לַיי לְעוֹלָה. הַכֹּל קָרֵב עוֹלָה. לִרְצ‍וֹנְכֶם תָּמִים זָכָר. יָכוֹל אֲפִילוּ עוֹפוֹת. תַּלָמוּד לוֹמַר בַּבָּקָר. לֹא עוֹפוֹת. רִבִּי יִרְמְיָה וְרִבִּי בּוּן בַּר חִיָיה הֲווֹן יָתְבִין וְאָמְרִין. תַּמָּן אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. טַעֲמֵיהּ דְּרִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. שֶׁכֵּן נְקֵיבָה שֶׁבָּעוֹף כְשֵׁירָה לָבוֹא עוֹלָה. וְהָכָא הוּא אָמַר הָכֵן. אָמַר רִבִּי יוֹסֵה. קִיַימְתִּיהָ כָּהִיא דְאָמַר רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי זְעוּרָה. כָּל שֶׁאֵינָהּ יְכוֹלָה לִקָּרֵב לֹא הִיא וְלֹא דָמֶיהָ לֹא קָדְשָׁה אֶלָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abbahu in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Laqish. The reason of Rebbi Eleazar: *Speak to Aaron and to his sons*, etc., *who would sacrifice to the Eternal as elevation sacrifice;* everything may be brought as elevation sacrifice, *by your volition, unblemished, male*. I could think that this includes birds. The verse says, *in cattle*, not birds. Rebbi Jeremiah and Rebbi Abun bar Ḥiyya were sitting and saying, there said Rebbi Joḥanan said, the reason of Rebbi Simeon is that we find that a female is qualified as elevation offering of a bird. And here, he says so? Rebbi Yose said, I confirmed it following what Rebbi Samuel said in the name of Rebbi Ze`ira: Anything which could be sacrificed neither itself nor its money's worth is sanctified only as money's worth.

Rabbi Elazar (the text switches from Rabbi Eliezer, since Rabbi Eliezer's position in the *mishnah* is that everything goes to *bedek ha-bayit*, making the attribution there problematic; the *mefarshim* accordingly emend to Rabbi Elazar) goes further than Rabbi Yehoshua. He holds that the generic *kedushah* creates *kedushat olah* not only on unblemished cattle and sheep but on everything fit for the *mizbe'ach* — birds, flour, wine, oil. His proof text is the same verse: *asher yakriv la-Hashem le-olah — hakol kareiv olah*, everything that is *ra'uy le-mizbe'ach* receives *kedushat olah*.

Rabbi Abahu in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish explains: *ta'ama de-Rabbi Elazar* — the same verse that Rabbi Yehoshua used. Where Rabbi Yehoshua stops at cattle and sheep, Rabbi Elazar reads the entire opening phrase as universal in scope and understands *ba-bakar* not as a limitation on what receives *kedushah* but as a limitation on what may be brought *itself* as opposed to through its proceeds.

### Scripture Bakar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יִצְחָק בֵּירִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. כָּתוּב זָכָר. וְאַתְּ אָמַרְתָּ בַּבָּקָר לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַנְּקֵבוֹת. וְדִכְוָתָהּ כָּתָב תָּמִים. וְאַתְּ אָמַרְתָּ בַּבָּקָר לְרַבּוֹת בַּעֲלֵי מוּמֵין. מַה בֵינֵיהוּן. רַב אָמַר. שִׁיבְטָא דְכַרְכָדָא בֵינֵיהוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Isaac ben Rebbi Eleazar: It says *male*, and you are saying *in cattle*, to include females. Then similarly it is written *unblemished*, and you are saying *in cattle*, to include the blemished. What is between them? The stick of a weaver's shuttle is between them.

The question is then asked: if you use *ba-bakar* to include females (overriding the explicit *zachar*), why not also use it to include blemished animals (overriding the explicit *tamim*)? The *gemara* answers with a striking idiom — *shivta de-kadka beineihem*, the rod of the loom is between them, meaning there is a world of difference between the two extensions. Unblemished females are fundamentally fit to be brought as *korbanot* — just not as an *olah* specifically. Blemished animals, by contrast, are fundamentally unfit to be brought as *korbanot* at all. The *bakar* extension only reaches as far as animals that are, in principle, within the *parshah* of *korbanos*.

### Bird Redemption Trap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַרְתָּ יְאוּת. לְהַקְרִיבוֹ אֵין אַתְּ יָכוֹל. דִּכְתִיב בַּבָּקָר. לֹא עוּפוֹת. לִפְדוֹתוֹ אֵין אַתְּ יָכוֹל. שֶׁאֵין לָעוֹף פִּדְיוֹן. לְפוּם כֵּן צָרַךְ מֵימַר. לֹא קָדְשָׁה אֶלָּא קְדוּשַׁת דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** And you are saying, this is correct. You cannot sacrifice it, for it is written *in cattle*. You cannot redeem it since birds cannot be redeemed. Accordingly, one must say, it is sanctified only as money's worth.

This leads to an important question about birds in Rabbi Elazar's system. According to Rabbi Elazar, when one gives a generic *kedushah*, a bird receives *kedushat olah*. Yet the bird cannot be brought itself — *de-chtiv ba-bakar ve-lo ofot*, the verse specifies cattle and not birds as what may be brought directly. But a bird also cannot be redeemed, because *ein la-of pidyon* — there is no mechanism for redeeming a bird. So the bird has *kedushah* but can neither be used nor redeemed. 

Rabbi Yosei clarifies by citing a principle of Rabbi Shmuel in the name of Rabbi Zeira: *kol she-eino yachol likrov lo hu ve-lo dameha lo kidshah ela hekdesh tamid* — anything that can be neither brought nor redeemed through its proceeds receives only a standing *kedushah* and does not get *kedushat olah* through the generic *kedushah*. In Rabbi Elazar's system, therefore, the bird receives *kedushat damim* but cannot have it realized — it is a *kedushah* that, given the generic context, is trapped without *pidyon* or *korban*. The upshot is *lo kidshah ela kedushat damim* in this attenuated, unrealizable sense.

The question is raised: how does this square with the earlier statement of Rabbi Yochanan that a female bird's eligibility for an *olah* is precisely what makes the female animal's *kedushah* meaningful enough to make *temurah*? The answer appears to be that these are two different contexts. When one *explicitly* sanctifies a female animal as an *olah*, the bird precedent gives that act enough halachic traction that the animal acquires *kedushat haguf* and makes *temurah*. But when the *kedushah* arises from the generic *kiddush* of Rabbi Elazar's *mishnah*, the limiting verse (*ba-bakar ve-lo ofot*) intervenes and pushes the bird into a different category — one where the *kedushah* cannot be actualized either through the animal itself or through its proceeds.

## Temurah & Tameia

### Female Pesach Fit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי. אֲנִי רוֹאֶה אֶת דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּפֶסַח שֶׁמָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ פֶּסַח בָּא שְׁלָמִים. וְלֹא אֶרְאֶה אֶת דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בָּאָשָׁם שֶׁמָּא הֶקְדֵּשׁ אָשָׁם בָּא עוֹלָה. אָמַר רִבִּי אַבִּין. אִם הֶקְדֵּישׁ פֶּסַח בָּא שְׁלָמִים גּוּפוֹ קָרֵב שְׁלָמִים. אִם הֶקְדֵּשׁ אָשָׁם בָּא עוֹלָה אֵין גּוּפוֹ קָרֵב עוֹלָה. מַאי כְדוֹן. הָהֵין אָמַר. הֶקְדֵּשׁ דָּמִים קָדַשׁ. וְהָהֵין אָמַר. הֶקְדֵּשׁ גּוּף קָדַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi said, I agree with Rebbi Simeon about the *Pesah*; could the *Pesaḥ* be brought as a well being offering? But I do not agree with the words of Rebbi Simeon for reparation offerings; could a reparation offering be brought as an elevation offering?" Rebbi Abbin said, in case a *Pesaḥ* be brought as a well being offering, its body is brought as well-being offering; in case a reparation offering be brought as an elevation offering, its body cannot be brought as elevation offering. What about it? In one case one says, it is sanctified for its money's worth; in one case one says, it is sanctified in its body.

Returning to the dispute about whether a female sanctified for a *pesach* makes *temurah*, the *gemara* records that Rabbi objects to applying Rabbi Shimon's ruling to the *pesach* case: *Ein ani ro'eh divrei Rabbi Shimon be-Pesach she-mosar Pesach ba shelamim*. A female is genuinely within the *parshah* of *pesach* because if one fails to bring it as a *pesach*, it may subsequently be brought as *shelamim*. Since the animal itself can be brought in that capacity, the female is not so foreign to the *pesach* that it should be denied *kedushat korban*.

### Tameia Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲבֵרַיָיא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. רִבִּי אִיְיבוֹ בַּר נַגָּרִי אָמַר קוֹמֵי רִבִּי אִילָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹחָנָן. טַעֲמָא דְהֵין תַּנָּיָיא. וְאִם כָּל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵיאָה אֲשֶׁר לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ מִמֶּנָּה קָרְבָּן לַיי. מַה תַלְמוּד לוֹמַר טְמֵאָה. אֶלָּא אֲפִילוּ טְמֵיאָה בְאוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁם. וְקַשְׁיָא. בְּדָא כְתִיב וְהֶעֱמִיד וְהֶעֱרִיךְ. רִבִּי זְעוּרָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. לֹא אָמַר כֵן אֶלָּא וְאִם כָּל בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה אֲשֶׁר לֹא יַקְרִיבוּ מִמֶּנָּה קָרְבָּן לַיי. כָּל שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לִיקָּרֵב לֹא כָאן וְלֹא בְמָקוֹם אַחֵר אֵינָהּ עוֹשָׂה תְמוּרָה. יָצָאת נְקֵיבָה שֶׁבָּעוֹף שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה לִיקָּרֵב כָּאן רְאוּיָה לִיקָּרֵב בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The colleagues in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan; Rebbi Ayvo bar Nagari said before Rebbi Ila in the name of Rebbi Joḥanan. The reason of this Tanna: *But if any impure animal from which no sacrifice to the Eternal may be offered*. Why does the verse say, *impure*? But even impure for this denomination. This is difficult; about this is written, *he shall stand, he shall appraise*? Rebbi Ze`ira in the name of Rebbi Eleazar did not say so but, *but if any impure animal from which no sacrifice to the Eternal may be offered*; anything which could be sacrificed neither here nor at any other place does not effect substitution. This excludes the female of a bird which even though it cannot be sacrificed here is fit to be sacrificed at another place.

The *gemara* continues to probe the basis for denying *kedushat korban* to inappropriate animals. The verse invoked is *ve-im kol behemah teme'ah asher lo yakrivu mimenah korban la-Hashem* — the verse concerning a *teme'ah* animal (such as a camel) that one attempts to sanctify as a *korban*. The question is what falls into the category of *teme'ah* for this purpose. One approach, associated with the position of Rabbi Shimon, reads *teme'ah* broadly: *afilu teme'ah be-oto ha-shem* — even something inappropriate by reason of any disqualifying characteristic is in the category of *teme'ah*. This reading would consign a female sanctified for an *olah* to the *teme'ah* category and deny it *kedushah* sufficient for *temurah*.

Rabbi Zeira in the name of Rabbi Elazar, however, reads it more narrowly: *kol she-eino ra'uy likarev lo kan ve-lo be-makom acher* — only something that cannot be brought here or anywhere as a *korban* falls into the *teme'ah* category. A female, by contrast, while not fit for this specific *korban*, is fit *le-karev be-makom acher* — it can be brought as some other *korban*. Therefore *yatzat nekevah le-olah* — the case of a female for an *olah* is excluded from the *teme'ah* category, and it does make *temurah*.

### Bestiality Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי בּוּן וְרִבִּי בּוּן בְּעוֹן קוֹמֵי רִבִּי זְעוּרָה. הֲרֵי הַרוֹבַע וְהַנִּרְבַּע הֲרֵי אֵינָן רְאוּיִין לִיקָּרֵב לֹא כָאן וְלֹא בְמָקוֹם אַחֵר וַהֲרֵי הֵן עוֹשִׂין תְּמוּרָה. אָמַר לוֹן. אַף אֲנִי לֹא אָמַרְתִּי אֶלָּא טְמֵאָה מַמָּשׁ. וְקַשְׁיָא. בְּדָא כְתִיב וְהֶעֱמִיד וְהֶעֱרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Abbin and Rebbi Abun asked before Rebbi Ze`ira: Are there not the animals used for active or passive bestiality, which can be sacrificed neither here nor at any other place, and they effect substitution? He said to them, also I did speak only about really impure ones. This is difficult; about this is written, *he shall stand, he shall appraise*?

Rabbi Avun and Rabbi Bun raise a challenge: what about an animal that has been used in an act of bestiality (*haroveiah ve-hanirva*)? Such an animal is *she-einam reuyim likarev lo kan ve-lo be-makom acher* — completely unfit for any *korban*, anywhere, under any circumstances. Yet the *halacha* is that if one sanctifies such an animal, it does achieve enough *kedushat mizbe'ach* to make *temurah*. If the criterion for exclusion is that an animal cannot be brought anywhere, how does an animal involved in bestiality avoid that exclusion?

### Tameia Strict

Rabbi Zeira responds: *af ani lo amarti ela teme'ah mamash* — I only ever meant *teme'ah* in the strictest sense: a genuinely *tame* species, like a camel. The word *teme'ah* in the verse means precisely that — a non-kosher animal. It does not extend to animals that are disqualified for other reasons, even if they are entirely unfit for the altar. Consequently, animals involved in bestiality, though completely unfit, are not in the category of *teme'ah mamash* and therefore do make *temurah*. And similarly, a female sanctified for an *olah* — which is certainly not a *tame* species — is not in the *teme'ah* category, and would make *temurah*.

The practical consequence of this conclusion is that one cannot use the *teme'ah* verse to exclude wrong-gender animals from *temurah*. The category of *teme'ah* is reserved strictly for non-kosher species, and anything outside that narrow category retains sufficient *kedushat mizbe'ach* to make *temurah* — leaving the broader dispute about the *nature* of the *kedushah* acquired by a female animal (whether *kedushat haguf* or *kedushat damim*) unresolved by this textual route.

## Supplier Contracts

### Monthly Prices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: אַחַת לִשְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם מְשַׁעֲרִין אֶת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. כָּל הַמְקַבֵּל עָלָיו לְסַפֵּק סְלָתוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע עָמְדוּ מִשָּׁלֹשׁ יְסַפֵּק מֵאַרְבַּע. מִשָּׁלֹשׁ וְעָמְדוּ מֵאַרְבַּע יְסַפֵּק מֵאַרְבַּע שֶׁיַּד הֶקְדֵּשׁ עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנָה. אִם הִתְלִיעָה סוֹלֶת הִתְלִיעָה לוֹ וְאִם הֶחְמִיץ יַיִן הֶחְמִיץ לוֹ. אֵינוֹ מְקַבֵּל אֶת מְעוֹתָיו עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא הַמִּזְבֵּחַ מְרַצֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: Once every thirty days one sets the prices for the office. Anyone who accepts to supply fine flour at a rate of four, if the going rate goes up to three he has to supply at the rate of four; if at the rate of three and the going rate went down to four he has to supply at four since the hand of the sanctuary is privileged. If the flour turned out worm-infected, it was infected in his possession; and if the wine turned into vinegar it was vinegar in his possession. He is not paid until the altar did atone.

The *gemara* now arrives at the final *mishnah* of the *perek*, which is considerably more straightforward. *Achat li-shloshim yom meshari'in et ha-lishkah* — once every thirty days, the Temple administrators would fix prices for the commodities they were purchasing: flour, wine, oil, and similar supplies needed for the *korbanot*. The prices set at the beginning of the month governed all transactions for that month, until the next price-setting at *Rosh Chodesh*.

### Price Lock

The mechanism works as follows. Suppose the Temple agreed with a supplier to provide flour at four *se'ah* per *shekel*. If during the month market prices rise so that the going rate is now only three *se'ah* per *shekel* — *yaspik me-arba*, the supplier must nonetheless deliver four *se'ah* per *shekel* as agreed. The original contract price holds.

### Temple Benefit

Conversely, if the original agreement was three *se'ah* per *shekel* and the price drops so that four *se'ah* can be had for a *shekel* — *yaspik me-arba*, the supplier must deliver at the more favorable rate of four. *She-yad hekdesh al ha-elyonah* — the hand of *hekdesh* is always on top. In either direction of market movement, the Temple always benefits.

### Quality Returns

Beyond the price terms, there are quality guarantees. *Im hitli'ah* — if the flour became wormy, even after delivery and payment, the supplier bears responsibility: *solet hitli'ah lo*, it is the supplier's loss. Similarly, *im hichmitz yayin hichmitz lo* — if the wine turned sour, that too falls to the supplier.

The supplier's exposure continues until the moment of use: *eino mekabel ma'osav ad she-yehei mizbe'ach mirtze* — the supplier does not have an unqualified right to retain payment until the *mizbe'ach* has accepted the offering. Up until that point, the right of return remains in force.

### Payment Terms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הלכה: תַּנֵּי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. מִיַּד הָיוּ מְקַבְּלִין אֶת מָעוֹתָיו. וְהַכֹּהֲנִים זְרִיזִים הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** HALAKHAH: It was stated in the name of Rebbi Simeon: he receives his money immediately, for the priests are quick.

The final ruling, transmitted in the name of Rabbi Shimon, introduces a modification out of fairness to the supplier: *miyad hayu mekablin et ma'oseihen* — payment would be given immediately upon delivery. Why is the Temple not concerned that it is surrendering its leverage before the offering is made? *Ha-kohanim zerizim heim* — the *kohanim* are diligent and will use the materials promptly. Since in practice the gap between delivery and offering is negligible, there is no reason to impose an unfair payment delay on the supplier.

With that, the *perek* concludes.
