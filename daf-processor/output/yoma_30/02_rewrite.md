# Yoma 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kohen Gadol Tevilah

### Morning Preparations

Daf 30 opens by situating us at daybreak on Yom Kippur, as the *kohen gadol* is about to begin the day's service. We know that *shechitah* is coming, but the Mishnah first describes an earlier step: the *kohen gadol* is taken to the *mikvah*. We were previously told, in a general way, that people would immerse after defecating and wash their hands and feet after urinating — but we have not yet been given the reason why the *kohen gadol* specifically immerses on the morning of Yom Kippur. The next Mishnah provides the answer.

### Ein Adam Nichnas

The Mishnah articulates a general principle: *ein adam nichnas la'azarah la'avodah*, no person may enter the *azarah* — the Temple courtyard, accessible even to non-*kohanim* as far as *Sha'ar Nikanor* and encompassing the open courtyard where the *mizbe'ach* stood — *afilu tahor*, even if he is already in a state of purity, *ad sheyitbol*, without first performing a ritual immersion. This, then, is presumably why the *kohen gadol*, who has spent an entire week attaining *taharah*, still immerses on the morning of Yom Kippur: the requirement applies to everyone who enters the *azarah* for service.

### Chamesh Tevilot

The Mishnah continues: *chamesh tevilot v'asarah kiddushin tovel Kohen Gadol umekadesh bo bayom* — the *kohen gadol* immerses five times and sanctifies his hands and feet ten times on that day. Each time he goes to the *mikvah*, he washes his hands and feet both before and after, and the Gemara will spell out exactly when each immersion occurs and why. *V'chulan bakodesh al beit haparva* — all of these were performed in the *azarah* itself, on the roof of the *Beit Haparva*, a room we have encountered before in the layout of the *Beit Hamikdash*. The sole exception is the very first immersion of the day — which must, by the very logic of the Mishnah's principle, be performed outside, since one cannot enter the *azarah* without first immersing.

### Privacy Curtain

The Mishnah adds: *pirsu sadin shel butz beino l'vein ha'am* — a linen curtain was spread vertically to separate the *kohen gadol* from the people. Apparently the location where he immersed, atop a rooftop structure, was visible to those assembled, and a curtain was therefore held up to protect his privacy. The Mishnah then records *vekidesh yadav v'raglav*, though there is a textual question regarding whether this phrase belongs in the *girsa* at this point.

## Ben Zoma Kal V'Chomer

### Ben Zoma's Question

*Sha'alu et Ben Zoma tevilah zo lamah* — they asked Ben Zoma: what is the purpose of this *tevilah*? The question here is not specifically about the *kohen gadol's* Yom Kippur immersions, but rather about the general requirement that any person immerse before entering the *azarah*. Ben Zoma's answer works in an interesting direction: rather than deriving a rule for Yom Kippur from general Temple law, he uses what the *Torah* describes of the *kohen gadol's* Yom Kippur service to teach a rule applicable throughout the year. This reversal is worth noting.

### Biblical Immersion Requi…

The *Torah* in *Acharei Mot*, describing what Aharon does on Yom Kippur, repeatedly uses the phrase *vrachatz bamayim et besaro v'lavasham* — he immerses and then dons the garments. The underlying structure is as follows: the *kohen gadol* on Yom Kippur performs two categories of service. When performing the unique inner service in the *heichal* — the covered structure housing the *Shulchan* and *Menorah* — he wears the four simple white linen garments. When he performs the outer service, shared with the rest of the year, he wears the eight gold garments. Every time he transitions between these two categories — both in terms of physical space and in terms of the type of *avodah* — he removes one set of garments, immerses, and dons the other. This immersion between services within the *azarah* is the source from which Ben Zoma derives his argument.

### Kal V'Chomer Logic

Ben Zoma formulates a *kal vachomer*: *uma hamishaneh mikodesh l'kodesh*, if someone who is changing from one sanctified space to another sanctified space — and from a space whose unauthorized entry by a *tamei* person carries the penalty of *karet* to another space with the same penalty — is nevertheless required to immerse, *ta'un tevilah*, then *hameshaneh mechol lekodesh*, someone who is transitioning from an unsanctified space to a sanctified one, and from a space without *karet* to a space with *karet*, *eino din sheta'un tevilah* — how much more so must that person immerse?

One might object that we do not generally apply the rule of *tevilah* between spaces within the *Mikdash* to normal Kohanim in an ordinary year — a regular *kohen* moving from the *azarah* to the *heichal* does not immerse. Ben Zoma is not claiming otherwise. His point is narrower: at minimum, we can learn from the principle of *tevilah* between sanctified spaces that the transition *into* sanctified space requires something of an immersion. The *kal vachomer* establishes the requirement for ordinary entry, not between-space transitions in general.

## Rab Yehuda Serach Tuma

### Serach Tevilah

Rabbi Yehuda offers a fundamentally different explanation: *serach tevilah hee zo* — this is really a kind of vestigial or pseudo-*tevilah*. It is not a genuine, formally demanded *tevilah* in the halachic sense. Rather, it exists *k'dei sheyizkor tumah yeshanah shebeyado veyifrosh* — so that a person will recall any latent *tumah* he may be carrying and thereby refrain from entering.

### Reflection on Purity

The mechanism Rabbi Yehuda has in mind is experiential. When a person goes to the *mikvah*, the first thought that naturally arises is: am I *tamei*? The act of immersion triggers reflection on one's purity status. Before entering the *Mikdash*, this forced pause might cause someone to remember: "I actually touched something." If what he touched requires only immersion and nightfall, he will turn around and come back later. If he remembers he became a *zav*, or that a woman was a *niddah*, he will realize he requires seven days of preparation. The *mikvah* visit does not itself solve every problem of *tumah* — a *tvul yom*, someone who has immersed but not yet waited for nightfall, remains in a restricted state — but it generates the right kind of introspection. Rabbi Yehuda's model is therefore neither about real *tumah* remediation, which would require more than a simple immersion, nor about a formal ritual demand; it is about cultivating sensitivity and awareness before entering the *Mikdash*.

### Preparation Models

This parallel to the *kohen gadol's* seven-day preparation before Yom Kippur is striking. Recall that two primary models were offered for that preparation: the *har Sinai* model — preparing for the encounter with the divine presence — and the *miluim* model — preparing for the performance of *avodah*. A third model emerged as well: the *serach tumah* concern, the possibility that the *kohen gadol* might be *tamei* and that extended preparatory activity would help address that. Remarkably, those same three conceptual frameworks map onto the question of why any person immerses before entering the *Mikdash*. It might be about *tumah* sensitivity, as Rabbi Yehuda holds. It might be a ritual preparation for entering that sacred space — an encounter-centered model. Or it might be specifically a preparation for performing *avodah*. All three possibilities that animated the discussion of the seven-day preparation find their echo here.

### Tosafos Avodah Model

Tosafos raises a challenge to Ben Zoma's *kal vachomer*. The objection is *teima lei ika lemirach ma lehallan sheken rotzeh la'avod avodah* — the *kohen gadol* who immerses between garment changes does so because he is about to perform *avodah*, not merely because he is transitioning between spaces. The *kal vachomer* therefore proves only that someone entering the *Mikdash* in order to perform *avodah* must immerse: *tahor nichnas k'dei la'avod*. Tosafos resolves this by saying the biblical requirement applies only to one entering to perform *avodah*, while *mi'derabbanan gazru afilu benichnas shelo la'avod* — the Rabbis extended the requirement even to one entering without intending to perform service.

This analysis, Tosafos's distinction between entering to perform *avodah* and merely entering the space, maps cleanly onto the earlier pair of models: the *miluim* model was oriented around preparation for *avodah*, while the *har Sinai* model was about entering the divine presence as such. The *serach tumah* model, Rabbi Yehuda's position, stands apart from both as a third distinct rationale.

## Nafka Mina Debate

### Bemai Kamiflegi

At the top of 30b, the Gemara asks: *bemai kamiflegi* — what is the practical consequence of the dispute between Ben Zoma and Rabbi Yehuda? The Gemara identifies the first candidate: *be'achulei avodah kamiflegi* — they debate whether failure to immerse retroactively invalidates the service.

### Mechil Avodah

According to Ben Zoma, who derives the requirement from the *kohen gadol's* practice and treats it as a genuine biblical obligation, *mechil avodah* — the failure to immerse would desecrate and invalidate the service performed.

### Lo Mechil Avodah

According to Rabbi Yehuda, since the entire basis is rabbinic — a mnemonic device rather than a formal demand — *lo mechil avodah*, failure to immerse does not invalidate the service.

### Kohen Gadol Lo Taval

The Gemara challenges Ben Zoma's position from a *beraisa*: *Kohen Gadol shelo taval velo kidesh* — if the *kohen gadol* did not immerse and did not wash his hands when he was supposed to, *bein beged le-beged u-vein avodah le-avodah* — whether between the white garments and the gold garments, or between the inner and outer services — *avodato kesherah*, his service is valid *bedi'avad*. This applies both to a *kohen gadol* and to an ordinary *kohen hedyot*. So even on Ben Zoma's own model — which was derived from the *kohen gadol's* between-service immersions — failure to immerse does not actually invalidate the *avodah bedi'avad*.

### Kidush Yadayim Shacharit

The *beraisa* continues with a critical distinction: *shelo kidesh yadav ve-raglav shacharis ve-avad avodato pesulah* — if the *kohen*, whether *gadol* or *hedyot*, did not perform the initial sanctification of hands and feet at the beginning of the day, and then performed service, the service is *invalid*. This exception is crucial. The *Torah* in the passage commanding the construction of the *kiyor* states: *ve-natata shama mayim ve-rachatsu mimeno Aharon u-vanav et yadeihem ve-et ragleihem be-vo'am el Ohel Moed o be-gishtam el ha-mizbe'ach le-shareit velo yamutu* — they shall wash when approaching the *Ohel Moed* or the *mizbe'ach* for service, and if they do not, they shall die. Another Gemara derives from this that failure to wash hands at the beginning of the day renders the subsequent service invalid.

This is therefore the one act that everyone agrees is a biblical requirement, carrying the penalty of *chiyuv misah* and invalidating the *avodah* if omitted: the morning *kiddush yadayim ve-raglayim*. The *kohen gadol's* subsequent immersions between garments, or the subsequent hand-washings between services, are separate matters entirely and do not share this status.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

Returning to the *nafka mina*: since the *beraisa* itself establishes that even on Ben Zoma's model the *avodah* is valid *bedi'avad* when the between-garments immersion was skipped, the argument that the practical difference between Ben Zoma and Rabbi Yehuda lies in *mechil avodah* fails. Both agree, in effect, that the service is not retroactively invalidated. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ella lemakam be'aseh kamiflagi* — the practical difference is whether one has violated a biblical positive commandment. According to Ben Zoma, since the requirement is derived from the *Torah*, one who fails to immerse *ka'i be'aseh* — stands in transgression of a positive biblical obligation. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *lo ka'i be'aseh* — it is only a rabbinic requirement, and no biblical violation has occurred. The distinction is meaningful in terms of *sachar ve'onesh*, reward and punishment, even if it produces no operative halachic change in the validity of the *avodah* itself.

A notable observation: these two models of why one washes hands before beginning the day's service — Rabbi Yehuda's *serach tumah* rationale and Ben Zoma's ritual-preparatory model — parallel a well-known dispute among the *Rishonim* regarding the *takkanah* of washing hands in the morning. The Rashba holds that one washes because one may have touched parts of one's body during the night, requiring clean hands for *davening* — a direct parallel to the *serach tumah* rationale. The Rosh holds that just as the *kohen* washed to begin the *avodah* in the *Mikdash*, so too our own *avodat Hashem* in the broader world begins with that same act. The correspondence is precise and illuminating.

## Metzora Day Eight

### Metzora Gate Nikanor

The Gemara transitions to a new challenge: *u-mi it lei le-Rabbi Yehudah hai sevara* — does Rabbi Yehuda truly hold that the entire basis for *tevilah* is the *serach tumah* concern? A *beraisa* is cited: *metzora she-tovel ve-omed be-Sha'ar Nikanor* — a *metzora*, on the morning of the eighth day, immerses and then stands at *Sha'ar Nikanor*, the entrance to the *azarah*. He stands there because the blood of his sacrifice must be placed on his thumbs and toes, requiring him to present them at the threshold — but he himself may not enter the *azarah*, as he is still a *mechussar kippurim*, one who lacks full atonement because he has not yet brought his offering.

To understand the *beraisa's* relevance, a brief review of the *metzora's* purification process is necessary. On the seventh day, the *metzora* completes his purification rites and immerses in the *mikvah*. That night he becomes a *tvul yom*, and once nightfall passes he is no longer *tamei* in the full sense but remains *mechussar kippurim* until he brings his *korban* on day eight. On day eight, he comes to the *Mikdash* to bring that *korban*.

Now, *Rabbi Yehudah omer eino tzarich tevilah she-kevar taval me-emesh* — Rabbi Yehuda says the *metzora* does not need to immerse again on day eight, because he already immersed the previous day. On the surface, this appears to contradict the Mishnah's universal principle that anyone entering the *azarah* must immerse. But this is precisely the point: Rabbi Yehuda's position is entirely consistent with his stated rationale. Since the *tevilah* is only a mnemonic device to prompt awareness of possible *tumah*, and this *metzora* has just spent seven days in a rigorous purification process culminating in an immersion the previous day, the reminder is simply unnecessary. The Gemara confirms: *hai kidtani tana shek'var taval miba'od yom* — the *tanna* tells us precisely why, that he already immersed the day before. This not only does not challenge Rabbi Yehuda's position; it directly supports it. *D'kari lah mai kari lah* — the Gemara itself asks why the question was even raised in this form, answering that it was merely a prelude to a more substantive problem to follow.

### Lishkat Metzoraim

The more pressing challenge comes from another source. We have previously encountered the *Lishkat Metzoraim* in the context of the *Beit Hamikdash's* architectural layout — a chamber designated for *metzoraim*, *shesham metzoraim tovlin*, where *metzoraim* would immerse. The question is what this chamber's existence implies about Rabbi Yehuda's position. *Rabbi Yehudah omer lo metzoraim bilvad amru ella kol adam* — Rabbi Yehuda states that not only *metzoraim* would use this chamber; any person would immerse there. The Gemara asks: according to Rabbi Yehuda, why would *metzoraim* need to use it at all? They immersed the day before. And conversely, if Rabbi Yehuda grants that *metzoraim* immerse there, why does he exempt the *metzora* in the *beraisa* above from immersing on day eight?

Tosafos, it is worth noting, weighs in separately on the question of who was required to immerse: was the requirement limited to a *kohen* performing *avodah*, or did it extend to any person who merely entered the space? This is consistent with the earlier distinction between the *miluim* model and the *har Sinai* model, and Tosafos concludes that biblically the requirement tracks *avodah*, while rabbinically it was extended to all who enter.

### Tvul Yom Status

The Gemara's analysis depends on a careful understanding of the different gradations of purity status and which areas of the Temple complex each status permits entry to. The relevant categories here are *tvul yom* — one who has immersed but not yet waited for nightfall — and *mechussar kippurim* — one who has completed all purification requirements save for bringing the requisite *korban*.

On Har Habayis, the broader Temple Mount, a *mechussar kippurim* is permitted. However, a *tvul yom* is permitted on Har Habayis but is rabbinically excluded from the *Ezrat Nashim*. If one is still fully *tamei* — meaning one has not even immersed — one is excluded even from Har Habayis entirely. The *Lishkat Metzoraim* is located within this broader Temple Mount complex, in the vicinity of the *Ezrat Nashim*. For the *metzora* to reach this room on day eight in order to use its *mikvah*, he must have already been permitted to enter that space. And for that, he must not only have immersed on day seven but also have waited for nightfall, thereby exiting the status of *tvul yom*. If he has not immersed on day seven, he is not merely a *tvul yom* — he is fully *tamei*, and he cannot even ascend Har Habayis, let alone reach the *Lishkat Metzoraim*. The entire structure of the day-eight arrival presupposes a prior day-seven immersion.

### Day Seven Tevilah

The Gemara therefore refutes the suggestion that the *metzora* might arrive at the *Lishkat Metzoraim* without having immersed the previous day. *Ee d'lo taval he'erev shemesh ba'ei* — if he truly did not immerse on day seven, he would need to wait for nightfall after immersing before he could even enter that space, and the whole scenario dissolves. The only viable case is one where he definitely immersed on day seven. Given that, the question returns: why does Rabbi Yehuda require another immersion on day eight?

### Hesach Hadaat

The Gemara's first answer: *ha de-asach da'atei ha de-lo asach da'atei* — the distinction is whether he maintained vigilant mental attentiveness to his purity status between the day-seven immersion and the day-eight entry. If he was not paying attention — *asach da'atei* — he might have contracted another form of *tumah* in the interim, and therefore requires another immersion. If he was scrupulous, he need not immerse again.

But the Gemara immediately complicates this. *Ee asach da'atei hazo'ah shlishi v'shvi'i ba'ei* — if he truly was not paying attention, he might have come in contact with a *meit*, a corpse, in which case a simple *mikvah* visit is not sufficient; he would require sprinkling on the third and seventh days before he could reenter. *D'amar Rav Dostai bar Masun amar Rabbi Yochanan: hesach hada'at tzarich hazah shlishi v'shvi'i* — one who loses attentiveness to his purity status requires the full seven-day *hazah* preparation process. This would mean that for most people, the practical barrier to entering the *Mikdash* without extraordinary preparation is enormous — almost analogous in effect to the *kohen gadol's* seven-day preparation, since a person who has simply not been rigorously tracking his purity status would in principle need the full sprinkling process.

Tosafos raises a challenge: what about someone who became *tamei* from a *nevelah* or *sheretz*, forms of *tumah* that do not require the seven-day sprinkling? Could such a person simply immerse and come the next day? Tosafos resolves this by explaining that the Gemara's cases — those that do not require *hazah* — are specifically cases where the person was already *tamei* from such a source but maintained *da'at* throughout, meaning that even in his impure state he was continuously aware of his status and careful not to compound it by also touching a *meit*. The *hesach hada'at* that triggers the requirement for seven-day preparation is specifically the loss of consciousness that creates real fear of contact with *tum'at meit*.

### Mindset Intention

The Gemara concludes by rejecting the *hesach hada'at* answer entirely as the basis for the *metzora* debate, since an answer that invokes *hesach hada'at* requiring sprinkling would make the *mikvah* visit in the *Lishkat Metzoraim* insufficient anyway. The Gemara instead offers a final resolution: *lo kashya, ha d'taval l'da'at bias Mikdash, ha d'lo taval l'da'at bias Mikdash* — the distinction is whether, when the *metzora* immersed on day seven, he had in mind that he was preparing for entry into the *Mikdash* the following day.

According to Rabbi Yehuda, whose entire rationale is that *tevilah* serves to generate focused reflection on one's purity status before entering the sacred space, the day-seven immersion counts as a valid pre-entry *tevilah* only if the person was actively conscious at the time that he would be entering the *Mikdash* the next day. If that intention was present, the experiential purpose of the *tevilah* has been served — he thought it through then, he would have caught any additional *tumah* concerns in his mind at that moment. If he was not thinking about his imminent entry when he went to the *mikvah* — perhaps he immerses regularly and simply did not focus on the connection — then the purpose of the *tevilah* has not been fulfilled, and he must immerse again on day eight, at the threshold of entry.

This is a notably rigorous formulation of Rabbi Yehuda's position. The *tevilah* requirement is not satisfied merely by the physical act of immersion; it requires that the act be experienced as preparation for the *Mikdash*. A person who goes to the *mikvah* every morning as a matter of personal piety, without specifically directing his intention toward the entry into the *Beit Hamikdash* that will follow, has not discharged the requirement. The *tevilah* before *Mikdash* is constituted, on Rabbi Yehuda's model, by a particular kind of intentional reflection, not merely by the fact of having been immersed.

## Three Positions

### Abaye Rav Yosef

Abaye raises a question to Rav Yosef: *neima Rabbanan de-pligi alei de-Rabbi Yehuda ke-Ben Zoma sevira lehu* — should we say that the Rabbis who dispute Rabbi Yehuda's position here actually hold like Ben Zoma? Rashi explains the context: the earlier debate was over whether a *metzora* must immerse on day eight. The Rabbis require it; Rabbi Yehuda exempts him because he immersed the day before. If the Rabbis require the *metzora* to immerse on day eight, perhaps they do so because they hold with Ben Zoma that immersion before entry is a universal formal requirement, applying to everyone on the day of entry — and the *beraisa's* mention of the *metzora* is simply *le-odi'ach kocho de-Rabbi Yehudah*, to highlight the novelty of Rabbi Yehuda's lenient position, rather than to imply that normal people are exempt.

### Only Metzoraim

The Gemara then raises the alternative reading: *o dilma shani Metzora de-da'ish betuma* — or perhaps the Rabbis are making a more radical claim, not that everyone immerses before entry, but specifically that the *metzora* requires a day-eight immersion because he has been so steeped in *tumah*. Under this reading, the Rabbis' position is not an endorsement of Ben Zoma's universal requirement but rather a narrow exception applicable only to *metzoraim*. The Rabbis would be saying: ordinary people who are *tahor* need not immerse before entry; only the *metzora*, who has spent an extended period in a severe state of *tumah*, must immerse again even after having done so the prior day. *Kol adam lo da'ish betuma* — a normal person is not immersed in *tumah* in that same way, and no additional *tevilah* is required.

### Daish Betuma

The Rabbis' distinctive logic, on this reading, is rooted in the nature of the *metzora's* condition. A *metzora* has spent an extended period in a state of severe *tumah*, not merely incidentally but essentially — he has been, in a sense, dwelling within *tumah*, *da'ish betuma*. Even having immersed on day seven, the residual concern is enough to require another immersion when he actually comes to present himself at the *Mikdash*. A normal person entering the Temple, who has not been in that immersed-in-*tumah* state, is trusted to be *tahor*; no such additional step is demanded.

### Three Opinions

The Gemara thus arrives at a conclusion: there are genuinely three distinct positions on the question of *tevilah* before entry into the *Mikdash*. The first, Ben Zoma's position, holds that every person — regardless of prior immersion — must immerse on the day of entry, derived by *kal vachomer* from the *kohen gadol's* immersions between services. The second, Rabbi Yehuda's position, holds that immersion is a rabbinic mnemonic device, not a formal ritual requirement; a prior immersion with the right intention and maintained *da'at* can suffice, as in the case of the *metzora* who immersed on day seven with the *Mikdash* in mind. The third position, that of these Rabbis, is the most radical: ordinary persons entering the *Mikdash* are not required to immerse at all; the only individuals required to immerse before entry are *metzoraim*, whose prolonged immersion in severe *tumah* demands the extra step. The Daf leaves us positioned here, with these three distinct frameworks mapped out, to be continued in the next shiur.
