# Shekalim 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Communal Funds Overview

### Opening & Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: הַתְּרוּמָה מֶה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ. לוֹקְחִין בָּהּ תְּמִידִין וּמוּסָפִין וְנִסְכֵּיהֶם הָעֹמֶר וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים וְכָל קָרְבְּנוֹת הַצִּיבּוּר. שׁוֹמְרֵי סְפִחִים בַּשְּׁבִיעִית נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. רִבִּי יוֹסֵה אוֹמֵר אַף הָרוֹצֶה מִתְנַדֵּב שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ אַף אַתָּה אוֹמֵר שֶׁאֵין בָּאִין אֶלָּא מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: For what was the disbursement used? One buys from it daily and *musaf* sacrifices and their libations, the *`omer*, and the Two Breads, and the shew-bread, and all public offerings. The watchmen for aftergrowth in the sabbatical year take their wages from the disbursement from the lodge. Rebbi Yose says, he also may volunteer as unpaid trustee. They told him, would you not also say that they only come from the public?

We begin *daf* 10 with a new *perek*, picking up from the bottom of *daf* 9b. The preceding material described how the money was physically removed from the chamber — a procedure called *trumat halishka*, the taking of the *truma* from the *lishka*. That process, as we saw, was deliberately fashioned to resemble the taking of *truma*, with the remainder designated as *shirayim*. Questions surrounding what remained and whether it retained sanctity will be revisited briefly. This new *perek* turns our attention to a different question: what was actually done with the funds? We have been saying all along that the money went toward *korbanos tzibur*, communal sacrifices. Now the *mishna* gives us the specifics.

### Terumah Usage

The *mishna* opens with the question: *hatruma mah hayu osin bah* — what would they do with the *truma*? Note that the term *truma* is employed deliberately; it is not simply asking what was done with the *shekalim*. This framing continues to emphasize the *truma*-like character of the withdrawal. The answer: *lokchim ba temidin umusafin veniskeyhen* — they would purchase the *korbanos tzibur* with it: the *temidin*, the two daily sacrifices; the *musaf* offerings, which consist largely of *olos* together with a *chatas*; and the *nesachim*, meaning the wine, grain, and flour that accompanied them — all of the *korbanos tzibur* with their attendant offerings brought upon the *mizbe'ach*.

### Non-Animal Offerings

The *mishna* continues: *ha'omer ushtei halechem v'lechem hapanim* — the *omer*, the *shtei halechem* brought on *Pesach* and *Shavuos*, and the *lechem hapanim*, which also constitutes a *korban tzibur*. These are non-animal *minchos* that qualify as *korbanos tzibur* and are therefore purchased from the communal fund. The *mishna* concludes: *v'chol korbanos hatzibur* — and all *korbanos tzibur*. Were this the *Bavli*, one would expect the *gemara* to ask *kol l'rabos mai* — what does the inclusive word "all" come to add? The *Yerushalmi* does not raise that question, and, as we shall see, neither does the *gemara* that follows.

## Omer & Shemita

### Watchers Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַתְּרוּמָה מֶה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ כול׳. מָה רָאָה זְמַן עֲצֵי כֹהֲנִים וְהָעָם לְהִימָּנוֹת. אֶלָּא בְשָׁעָה שֶׁעָלוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִן הַגּוֹלָה וְלֹא מָצְאוּ עֵצִים בַּלִּישְׁכָּה וְעָמְדוּ אֵילּוּ וְנִתְנַדְּבוּ עֵצִים מִשֶּׁלְעַצְמָן וּמְסָרוּם לַצִּיבּוּר וְקָרְבוּ מֵהֶן קָרְבְּנוֹת צִיבּוּר. וְהִתְנוּ עִמָּהֶן הַנְּבִיאים שֶׁבֵּינֵיהֶן שֶׁאֲפִילוּ לִשְׁכָּה מְלֵיאָה עֵצִים וְעָמְדוּ אֵילּוּ וְנִתְנַדְּבוּ עֵצִים מִשֶּׁלְעַצְמָן שֶׁלֹּא יִהֵא קָרְבָּן מִתְקָרֵב אֶלָּא מִשֶּׁלָּהֶן תְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: "For what was the disbursement used," etc. For what reason were the times of wood by the priests and the people to be counted? Only that at the time when Israel returned from the Diaspora and did not find wood in the chamber, those came forward and volunteered wood from their own, donated it to the public, and used it to offer public sacrifices. The prophets among them stipulated that even if the chamber was full of wood and those came and offered and volunteered wood from their own, that the sacrifice should only be brought first from theirs.

The *mishna* then introduces an interesting case: *shomrey sefichin beshvi'is notnim sachar mitrumas halishka* — the people who watch the wild growth during *shemita* are paid from the *trumat halishka*. What is the background? During the *shemita* year, one does not plant the grain that will be harvested for the *omer*. Instead, one takes grain that has grown spontaneously from dropped seeds — the *sefichin*, the wild growth. But to ensure there is something available to cut, one must hire people to watch over designated fields, preventing others from taking the grain, while not acting in a proprietary manner that would itself violate *shemita*. The watching is done on behalf of the *beis din*, the *mikdash*, and *klal Yisrael*. Once the grain is ready, it is harvested and taken into the possession of the *beis hamikdash*. The key point is that you can pay these workers from the *trumat halishka* even though you are not paying for the *korban* object itself.

### R. Yosi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יוֹסֵה אוֹמֵר אַף הָרוֹצֶה מִתְנַדֵּב שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ אַף אַתָּה אוֹמֵר שֶׁאֵין בָּאִין אֶלָּא מִשֶּׁל צִיבּוּר:
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Yose says, he also may volunteer as unpaid trustee. They told him, would you not also say that they only come from the public?

*Rabbi Yosi* goes further: *af harotze mitnadve shomer chinam* — if someone wishes to volunteer as a watcher without compensation, watches the grain for the sake of the *beis hamikdash* without being hired, and then himself harvests and donates the grain to the *beis hamikdash*, that is also acceptable. Rabbi Yosi may have been more tentative about deploying the communal funds to cover secondary needs of the *korban*; his solution is to allow someone to donate their time freely and simply give over the grain as a gift.

### Tzorchei Korban

The underlying principle at work is *tzorchei korban k'korban* — the needs that enable a *korban* to be brought may be funded from the same source as the *korban* itself. This principle opens a broad range of possibilities, several of which we will explore as we proceed.

### Sages' Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רִבִּי אָחָא. דְּרִבִּי יוֹסֵה הִיא. דְּרִבִּי יוֹסֵה אָמַר. אַף הָרוֹצֶה מִתְנַדֵּב שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. רִבִּי יוֹסֵי בְשֵׁם רִבִּי אִילָא. דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא. מַה פְלִיגִין. בְּגוּפוֹ שֶׁלְקָרְבָּן. אֲבָל בְּמַכְשִׁירֵי קָרְבָּן כָּל עַמָּא מוֹדֵיי שֶׁהוּא מִשְׁתַּנֶּה קָרְבָּן יָחִיד לְקָרְבָּן צִיבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Aḥa said, this is Rebbi Yose's, since Rebbi Yose said, also he may volunteer as unpaid trustee. Rebbi Yose in the name of Rebbi Ila, it is the opinion of everybody. Where do they disagree? About the body of the offering. But for enablers of the offering everybody agrees that a private offering can be turned into public offering.

The *chachamim* respond: *amru lo, af ata omer she'ein ba'in ela mishel tzibur* — do you not yourself admit that the *korbanos* may only be brought from communal funds? This sets up the central debate of the *gemara*: may an individual make a private donation to the *beis hamikdash* for the purpose of a communal sacrifice? If I bring my own sheep and declare that I give it wholeheartedly to the *beis hamikdash* for use as a *tamid* on behalf of *klal Yisrael*, is that permissible?

The *chachamim*'s objection to Rabbi Yosi's model is that when a private individual watches the grain and then cuts and donates it, the wheat has been his personal property — and his personal property becoming a donation is not acceptable to the *chachamim* for a *korban tzibur*. We want communal sacrifices purchased with communal funds. Private donation, even for a communal purpose, is rejected. One might ask whether the concern is purely one of appearances — that no one should be able to say "that was my *korban*" when the whole point is that it is being brought on behalf of *klal Yisrael* — or whether there is an objective halachic deficiency: that the act of purchasing communally is constitutive of *korban tzibur* status, and a private donation, however sincerely given, cannot replicate it. As we shall see, the *gemara*'s own discussion suggests the concern is specifically about the *korban* itself, not about communal purpose in general.

## Private Donations

### Gemara Framing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנֵּי. [אִ]שָּׁה שֶׁעָשָׂת כֻּתּוֹנֶת לִבְנָהּ צְרִיכָה לִמְסוֹר לְצִיבּוּר. אָמַר רִבִּי אָחָא. דְּרִבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא. דְּרִבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר. אַף הָרוֹצֶה מִתְנַדֵּב שׁוֹמֵר חִנָּם. רִבִּי יוֹסֵה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי אִילָא. דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא. מַה פְלִיגִין. בְּגוּפוֹ שֶׁלְקָרְבָּן. אֲבָל בְּמַכְשִׁירֵי קָרְבָּן כָּל עַמָּא מוֹדֵיי שֶׁהוּא מִשְׁתַּנֶּה קָרְבָּן יָחִיד לְקָרְבָּן צִיבּוּר. מַתְנִיתָא פְלִיגָא עַל רִבִּי יוֹסֵי. אוֹתָן הַיָּמִים נוֹהֲגִין בִּשְׁעַת קָרְבָּן וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁעַת קָרְבָּן. רִבִּי יוֹסֵה אוֹמֵר. אֵינָן נוֹהֲגִין אֶלָּא בִּשְׁעַת קָרְבָּן. וְעוֹד מִן הָדָא דְתַנֵּי. אָמַר רִבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֵּירִבִּי צָדוֹק. אָנוּ הָיִינוּ מִבְּנֵי סְנָאָה בֶן בִּנְיָמִן וְחָל תִּשְׁעָה בְאָב לִהְיוֹת בַּשַּׁבָּת וְדָחִינוּ אוֹתוֹ לְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת וְהָיִינוּ מִתְעַנִּין וְלֻא מַשְׁלִימִין.
>
> **Translation:** It was stated, a woman who made a coat for her son has to surrender it to the public. Rebbi Aḥa said, this is Rebbi Yose's, since Rebbi Yose said, also he may volunteer as unpaid trustee. Rebbi Yose in the name of Rebbi Ila, it is the opinion of everybody. Where do they disagree? About the body of the offering. But for enablers of the offering everybody agrees that a private offering can be turned into public offering. A *baraita* disagrees with Rebbi Yose. "Those days are observed at the time of sacrifices and not at the time of sacrifices; Rebbi Yose says, they are observed only at the time of sacrifices." Also from the following: "Rebbi Eleazar ben Rebbi Ṣadoq said, we were of the descendants of Senaah ben Benjamin. When the Ninth of Av fell on a Sabbath, we postponed it to the end of the Sabbath and were fasting but not completing.

The *gemara* does something structurally interesting: it skips over the opening of the *mishna* and goes directly to this debate, planning to double back to the beginning later. It opens with the question *mah ra'u* — why was this practice established? This connects to a *mishna* in *Taanis*, which records that there were eight occasions, *zman atzei hakohanim ve'ha'am*, on which eight families each had a designated date for contributing wood to the *beis hamikdash*. The question immediately arises: since wood for the *mizbe'ach* must be communally owned, does the fact that these families contributed their own private wood not raise precisely the problem of private donations for communal purposes?

### Eight Families

Each of the eight families had its own date — a fact that, as we shall see, had historical roots. Although the *beis hamikdash* would normally purchase communal needs from the *shekel* funds, wood presents an interesting case: it is communally owned, yet it is not a *korban* proper. Where does it come from? That question will be addressed in later *mishnayos*. For now, the issue is whether the entire institution of family wood donations — individual property given over for communal use — is subject to the debate between *Rabbi Yosi* and the *chachamim*.

### Wood Donations History

The *baraisa* explains: *besha'ah she'alu Yisrael min hagolah* — when the Jewish people returned from exile in the days of *Ezra*, *velo matzu etzim balishkah* — there was no wood in the *lishka*. The temple was being rebuilt and the wood stores were empty. *Ve'amdu elu ve'nisnadvu etzim mishel atzmam* — these eight families rose and donated wood from their own resources, *u'mesarum latzibur* — and freely gave it over for communal use. *Ve'hikrivu mehem korbanos tzibur* — and *korbanos tzibur* were brought upon those fires. *Ve'hisnu imahem nevi'im shebeineihem* — the prophets among them — presumably *Chaggai*, *Zecharyah*, and *Malachi*, the last of the prophets — stipulated with them: *she'afillu lishkah meleiah etzim* — even in the future, when the storehouses are full of wood, *ve'amdu elu ve'nisnadvu etzim mishel atzmam* — if these families wish to continue donating their wood, we will not refuse on grounds that we have enough. Moreover, *shelo yehei korban miskarev ella mishelahem techilah* — we will use their donated wood first, as the first wood upon which offerings are brought that week.

This institution is striking. Not only do the *chachamim* accept private wood donations, but they ensure that the donated wood takes precedence, and the dates themselves become family holidays. If anything, this seems *more* problematic than the case of *Rabbi Yosi*, since the families' identity is permanently bound up with the practice.

### R. Yacha's Analysis

*Amar Rabbi Yacha d'Rabbi Yose* — Rabbi Yacha the son of Rabbi Yosi says: that entire institution follows the approach of *Rabbi Yosi*, as Rabbi Yosi holds that one may contribute one's own time and resources for the sake of the *beis hamikdash* for communal purposes. The wood donation institution thus reflects only *Rabbi Yosi*'s view, not the view of the *chachamim*.

### Core Distinction

The discussion continues to address the underlying principle: *Rabbi Yose b'shem Ila* — or, as others have it, *Rav Assi b'shem Rabbi Ila* — responds: *divrei hakol hi* — the *mishna* actually represents a unanimous position. *Mah peligan* — where does the disagreement between *Rabbi Yosi* and the *chachamim* lie? *Beguf d'korban* — concerning the *korban* itself. Can the actual object to be offered come from a private donation? That is contested. *Aval bemachshirei korban* — but for things that enable the *korban* to be brought, such as the wood, *kol ama modei* — everyone agrees that *shehi mishtaneh korban yachid lekorban tzibbur* — one can take something that was privately owned and dedicate it for communal purposes. Thus private donations are universally accepted for attendant matters; only for the *korban* itself do the *chachamim* resist.

What precisely is the concern with the *korban* itself? It does not appear to be a worry that the object never truly becomes communal property, since the *chachamim* readily accept private donations for other communal needs. The concern seems to be something more specific to the *ikar* of what is placed upon the *mizbe'ach* — the thing that most essentially represents the community before God. For that, no individual should be able to say "that is mine." Everything else is, as it were, a sideshow; but the *korban tzibur* itself must be purchased communally. Whether this is a policy sensitivity or an objective halachic requirement that communal purchasing constitutes *korban tzibur* status is not spelled out by the *Yerushalmi*.

## Kohen Gadol Garments

### Tunic Case

A *baraisa* is now cited: *isha she'asta kutonet livnah* — if a woman made a white tunic, *keshera* — it may be used for the *kohen gadol* on *Yom Kippur*, *uvilvad she'timserena letzibbur* — provided she freely gives it over to the community. The case involves the white linen garments worn by the *kohen gadol* during the *Yom Kippur* service.

### Consensus Position

*Rabbi Yacha d'Rabbi Yose* initially says this follows *Rabbi Yosi*'s position — that one may donate privately for communal purposes. But *Rabbi Yose b'shem Ila* responds again: *divrei hakol hi* — everyone agrees to this. *Mah peligan* — the debate is only *beguf d'korban*, concerning the *korban* itself. *Aval bemachshirei korban, kol ama modei* — for anything that enables the *avoda* to be performed, everyone agrees you may accept private donations and convert them to communal use. The white garments of the *kohen gadol* are *machshirei korban* — they enable the *avoda* — but they are not themselves the *korban*. This is admittedly somewhat counterintuitive, given the particular weight attached to the *kohen gadol*'s *bigdim* on *Yom Kippur*, but the formal classification is clear: the garments facilitate the *avoda*; they are not what is offered. The *Yerushalmi* does not explain precisely why the line is drawn exactly at the *korban* itself.

### Wood & Holiday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָעוֹמֶר וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים. מַתְנִיתָא דְרִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. דְרִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר . אֵין הָעוֹמֶר בָּא מִן הַסּוּרִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** "The *`omer*, and the Two Breads, and the shew-bread." The Mishnah is Rebbi Ismael's, since Rebbi Ismael said, the *`omer* is not brought from Syria.

The *gemara* now offers additional proof that even the *chachamim* accept private donations for communal purposes other than the *korban* itself. A *baraisa* teaches: *otam yamim nohagim beshuat korban ushelo beshuat korban* — those eight days are observed as family *yom tovs* whether it is the era of *korbanos*, i.e., the time of the *beis hamikdash*, or not. *Rabbi Yosi*, however, says: *einam nohagim ella b'shuat korban bilvad* — they are observed only during the era of *korbanos*.

It is worth noting the *Yerushalmi*'s different formulation: instead of the *Bavli*'s *bizman beis hamikdash* and *shelo bizman beis hamikdash*, the *Yerushalmi* uses *beshaat korban* and *shelo beshaat korban*. The point, of course, is the same.

Now, when the text says this *pliga d'Rabbi Yose* — that this *baraisa* argues with *Rabbi Yosi* — it does not refer to the *Rabbi Yosi* of the original *mishna*, but rather to the *amora* *Rabbi Yosi* (*Rav Assi b'shem Rabbi Ila*) who had argued that even the *chachamim* accept private wood donations. The proof runs as follows: if the *chachamim* debate *Rabbi Yosi* regarding whether the family holidays apply outside the *beis hamikdash* era, both sides presuppose that the institution of family wood donations exists and is legitimate. The *chachamim* themselves thus accept the institution. This contradicts *Rabbi Acha*'s position that the institution reflects only *Rabbi Yosi*'s view, and supports *Rabbi Yosi* the *amora*'s contention that the *chachamim* universally accept private donations for non-*korban* matters. The *gemara* accordingly adjusts either the *girsa* or the attribution.

### Holiday Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּמָּן תַּנִּינָן. כָּל קָרְבְּנוֹת הַיָּחִיד וְהַצִּיבּוּר בָּאִין מִן הָאָרֶץ וּמִחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. מִן הֶחָדָשׁ וּמִן הַיָּשָׁן. חוּץ מִן הָעוֹמֶר וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם. שֶׁאֵין בָּאִין אֶלָּא מִן הֶחָדָשׁ וּמִן הָאָרֶץ. רִבִּי חוּנָה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי יִרְמְיָה. דְרִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא. דְרִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אָמַר. אֵין הָעוֹמֶר בָּא מִן הַסּוּרִיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** There, we have stated: "All private and public sacrifices come from the Land and from outside the Land, from new or old [grain], except for `*omer* and the Two Breads, which only come from new grain and from the Land." Rebbi Ḥuna in the name of Rebbi Jeremiah, this is Rebbi Ismael's, since Rebbi Ismael said, the *`omer* is not brought from Syria.

The proof is reinforced by a second *baraisa*: *amar Rabbi Eliezer bei Rabbi Tzadok anu hayinu mi'bnei San'ah ben Binyamin* — we are descendants of the house of *San'ah ben Binyamin*, one of the eight families, whose designated date was the tenth of *Av*. Once, *Tisha b'Av* fell on *Shabbos* and was therefore postponed to *motza'ei Shabbos* — which coincided with this family's holiday. *V'hayinu mit'anim v'lo meshlimin* — we began the fast but did not complete it, because the family holiday was powerful enough to override even the completion of the *Tisha b'Av* fast. This demonstrates that the family holidays were observed *after* the destruction of the *beis hamikdash* — for one would not observe *Tisha b'Av* during the era of the *beis hamikdash*. Since *Rabbi Yosi* does not accept the holidays in that era, and yet the *chachamim* clearly do, the *chachamim* must accept the entire institution of private wood donations. The conclusion stands: the *chachamim* agree with private donations for all attendant matters, and their disagreement with *Rabbi Yosi* is confined to the *korban* itself.

## Omer Sources

### Geographic Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּמָּן תַּנִּינָן. עֶשֶׂר קְדוּשּׁוֹת הֵן. אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת מִכָּל הָאֲרָצוֹת. וּמַה הִיא קְדוּשָּׁתָהּ. שֶׁמְּבִיאִים מִמֶּנָּה הָעוֹמֶר וְהַבִּיכּוּרִים וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** There, we have stated: "There are ten levels of holiness. The Land of Israel is holier than other lands; and what is its holiness? That one brings from it the *`omer*, first fruits, and the Two Breads, which cannot be from other lands."

Having addressed the debate over private donations, the *gemara* doubles back to the opening of the *mishna* and its list of communal offerings, including the *omer*, the *shtei halechem*, and the *lechem hapanim*. The *gemara* now notes: *masnisin d'Rabbi Yishmael* — our *mishna* follows the position of *Rabbi Yishmael*, who holds that *ein omer ba min haSurya* — the *omer* cannot be brought from *Surya*. *Surya* refers to lands bordering *Eretz Yisrael* that were conquered by *Dovid HaMelech* — roughly corresponding to territories in present-day Syria and Jordan — whose status with respect to the sanctity of *Eretz Yisrael* was disputed. If the *omer* could come from *Surya*, there would be no need to hire watchers of wild growth in *Eretz Yisrael* during *shemita*; one could simply bring grain from *Surya*, where *shemita* either does not apply or at least does not apply biblically. The very fact that our *mishna* describes hiring watchers within *Eretz Yisrael* demonstrates that our *mishna* holds one must bring the *omer* from *Eretz Yisrael* proper — and that is the view of *Rabbi Yishmael*.

### Ten Holiness Levels

The *gemara* invokes the famous *mishna* in *Kelim*: *eser kedushos hein* — there are ten levels of holiness ordered by geography, each more sacred than the last. *Eretz Yisrael mekudeshes mikol ha'aratzos* — the land of Israel is holier than all other lands. *U'mah hi kedushasah* — what is the expression of its holiness? *Shemevi'im mimenah ha'omer v'habikurim u'shtei halechem* — only from produce grown in the land of Israel may one bring the *omer*, the *bikurim*, and the *shtei halechem*. The *gemara* here cites the relevant *pesukim*: by *shtei halechem* the verse says *mimoshvoseichem*; by the *omer* the verse says *v'ktzartem es ktzirah* — the cutting of its harvest, indicating specifically the land's own harvest. It is striking that the primary expression of *Eretz Yisrael*'s sanctity is framed not in covenantal or historical terms, but in terms of the land itself — what grows from its soil. *Rabbi Chiya b'shem Rabbi Yirmiya* states that this *mishna* in *Kelim* proceeds according to *Rabbi Yishmael*, who holds the *omer* cannot come from *Surya*, as the *mishna*'s identification of *Eretz Yisrael*'s holiness with the *omer* implies that other opinions might permit the *omer* from *Surya*.

### Syria Excluded

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר. מֶה חָרִישׁ רְשׁוּת אַף קָצִיר רְשׁוּת. יָצָא קְצִיר הָעֹמֶר. שֶׁהוּא מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** "Rebbi Ismael says, since sowing is a voluntary act, also harvesting is a voluntary act. This excludes harvesting the *`omer*, which is a commandment.

*Rabbi Yishmael*'s position is derived from a *drashot* in *Shvi'it*. The *pasuk* states *be-charish u-va-katzir tishbot* — you shall rest from plowing and from harvesting. *Rabbi Yishmael* reads this as follows: plowing is always a *reshus*, a discretionary act — there is no mitzvah to plow. Therefore, when the verse says to cease harvesting, it means harvesting that is likewise a *reshus*. But harvesting that is a *mitzvah* — specifically the cutting of the *omer*, of which the verse says *v'ktzartem es ktzirah* — is excluded. The consequence is twofold: one need not refrain from cutting the *omer* on account of *shemita*, and one may even cut it on *Shabbos*. This is the principle *yatza katzir ha-Omer she-hu mitzvah* — the cutting of the *omer* is exempted because it is a *mitzvah*.

### Mitzvah Connection

The *gemara* then notes: *Rabbi Yishmael ke-da'ateih* — *Rabbi Yishmael* here is consistent with his own reasoning, *de-amar Rabbi Yishmael ein Omer ba min ha-Surya* — that the *omer* cannot come from *Surya*. The connection is as follows: if the *omer* must come from *Eretz Yisrael* specifically, that specificity lends the act of cutting a genuine *mitzvah* character. But if one could bring it from *Surya*, the cutting would lack that essential character — there would be less ground to call it a *mitzvah* and therefore less basis for overriding *shemita* or *Shabbos*. The *Yerushalmi* at face value almost seems to argue that the geographic requirement itself creates the *mitzvah* quality. The *mefarshim*, however, explain it differently: if one could bring the *omer* from *Surya* where *shemita* does not apply, there would be no pressing need to permit cutting in *Eretz Yisrael* on *shemita*. The fact that *Rabbi Yishmael* permits it implies that *Eretz Yisrael* is the only option — *ke-da'ateih*, consistent with his position.

### Planting Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהֵן עוֹמֶר מָהוּ שֶׁיִּזָּרַע לָכֵן מִתְּחִילָּה. רִבִּי חִיָיה בַּר אָדָא בְעָא קוֹמֵי רִבִּי מָנָא. לֹא נִמְצָא כְקוֹמֵץ עַל הַשְּׁיֵרִיים שֶׁאֵינָן נֶאֱכָלִין. אָמַר לֵיהּ. נַעֲשֶׂה כַחֲמִשָּׁה דְבָרִים שֶׁהֵן בָּאִין בְּטוּמְאָה וְאֵינָן נֶאֱכָלִין בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** Could one sow from the start for the `*omer*? Rebbi Ḥiyya bar Ada asked before Rebbi Mana, would he not take the fistful for leftovers than cannot be eaten? He said to him, it has a status like the five kinds which can be brought in impurity but may not be eaten in impurity.

A further question is raised: *ha-hen omer mahu she-yizarei bi-tchila* — since we are discussing wild growth (*sefichin*), might one be permitted to actually plant the seeds during *shemita* for the purpose of the *omer*, given that harvesting the *omer* is itself a *mitzvah* that overrides *shemita*? *Rabbi Chiya bar Ada* posed this question before *Rabbi Mana*. The response: *lo nimtza ke-kometz al ha-kilayim she-einam ne'echalim* — this would render the grain forbidden, since what grows from seeds planted in violation of *shemita* would itself be *assur*. When one takes the *korban mincha*, the *kometz* is placed on the *mizbe'ach* and the remaining *shirayim* must be fit for eating. If the grain grew from prohibited planting, the *shirayim* would be *einam ne'echalim* — not fit for consumption. This parallels what the *Bavli* derives from a *pasuk* in *Yechezkel* — *mimashkeh Yisrael min hamuttar l'Yisrael* — that one may not bring a *korban* from *ma'achalos assuros*.

The response to this objection invokes a principle from what was recently learned in *Pesachim*: there are five *korbanos tzibur* that may be brought in a state of *tumah* but may not be eaten in a state of *tumah* — only the *Pesach* may be eaten *b'tumah*, since the point of the *Pesach* is specifically to be eaten. Nevertheless, those *korbanos tzibur* are brought despite being unfit for eating. If a *korban* may be brought even when the *shirayim* cannot be eaten — as in the case of *tumah* — perhaps the same logic could apply here. The difference, of course, is that *tumah hutra b'tzibur* is a special dispensation for communal sacrifice brought in *tumah*; whether the same principle could be extended to *shemita*-tainted grain is left unresolved. The *mishna*'s discussion of wild growth implicitly answers the question in the negative: wild growth, not planted grain, is the prescribed path.

## Sanctity Transfer

### Banker Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. נוֹטֵל מָעוֹת מִן הָשּׁוּלְחָנִי וְנוֹתֵן לַקּוֹצְרִין וּלְשׁוֹמְרִין עַד שֶׁלֹּא יַקְרִב הָעוֹמֶר. מֵבִיא מָעוֹת מִתְּרוּמַת הִַלִּשְׁכָּה וּמְחַלְלָן עָלָיו. וְטָבוּ כֵן.
>
> **Translation:** How does he do it? He takes coins from the money changer and gives them to the harvesters and the watchmen before the *`omer* is presented. He brings coins from the disbursement from the lodge and redeems them for it; then it is in order.

Returning to the *mishna*'s provision that those who watch the wild growth during *shemita* are paid from the *trumat halishka*, the *gemara* now addresses a fundamental technical problem: how does one actually pay workers with sanctified money? Normally, when sanctified funds are used to purchase an object, the *kedusha* transfers from the money to the object — you buy an animal, the *kedusha* moves to the animal, and you now have a sanctified *korban*. But when paying for labor, there is no object to which the *kedusha* can transfer. So how is this done without a violation of *me'ila*?

The *gemara* describes the mechanism: *notel ma'ot min hashulchani* — you go to the banker — a money changer who also functions as a lender with available funds — and the banker pays the workers and the harvesters from his own unsanctified funds. This happens *ad she-lo yikarev ha-Omer* — before the *omer* is brought. Then, *mevi ma'ot mi-trumat halishka* — you take the sanctified *trumat halishka* funds, and *mechallelan alav* — you transfer their *kedusha* onto the harvested grain. The grain that the worker brings is now sanctified. The worker has already been paid by the bank, so he turns over the grain. The sanctified money, now desanctified, is used to repay the banker. In short: the bank pays the worker; the worker freely gives the grain; the *kedusha* transfers from the communal funds to the grain; the bank is repaid with what is now unsanctified money.

### Retroactive Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטָבוּ כֵן. רִבִּי אָחָא בְשֵׁם רִבִּי בָּא. כָּל מַה שֶׁיִּתֵּן הֵן הֵן דָּמֶיהָ מִשָּׁעָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה. תַּנֵּי. אַף בְּפַתָּחֵי אֲבָנִים כֵּן. כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. נוֹטֵל מָעוֹת מִשּׁוּלְחָנִי וְנוֹתֵן לַחוֹצְבִין וּלְסַתָּתִין עַד שֶׁלֹּא תִינָּתֵן עַל גַּבֵּי הַדֹּימוֹס. [אַחַר כָּךְ] מֵבִיא מָעוֹת מִתְּרוּמַת הִַלִּשְׁכָּה וּמְחַלְלָן עָלֶיהָ. וְטָבָא הִיא כֵן. רִבִּי יוֹסֵה בֵּירִבִּי בּוּן בְּשֵׁם שְׁמוּאֵל. כָּל מַה שֶׁיִּתֵּן הֵן הֵן דָּמָיו מִשָּׁעָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** Then it is in order. Rebbi Aḥa in the name of Rebbi Abba: Any which he gives are its coins from the start. It was stated: The same is the case with the stone-masons. He takes coins from the money changer and gives them to the quarry workers and the stone-cutters before it is fixed in the row. Then he brings coins from the disbursement from the lodge and redeems them for it; then it is in order. Rebbi Yose ben Rebbi Abun in the name of Samuel: Any which he gives are its coins from the start.

The *gemara* raises a question: *v'tavo ken* — is this truly appropriate? The problem is that the grain itself may not be worth the amount paid to the workers. You paid a hundred *shekel* in labor costs to watch grain that, as a raw commodity, is worth far less. How can you transfer a hundred *shekel* of *kedusha* onto grain worth twenty? *Rabbi Yitzchak b'shem Rabbi Ba* answers: *kol mah she-yiten hein hein damav misha'ar rishona* — whatever you pay retroactively constitutes the value of the object. If *shemita* conditions drive up the cost of labor disproportionately, those labor costs become the assessed value of the grain. Just as the final cost of a product often reflects the labor embedded in it, so here the grain's value is retroactively set at the amount paid on its behalf.

### Stone Carvers

A *baraisa* then applies the same mechanism to a different case: *af l'pitchei avanim ken* — you do the same for those who carve stones for the *beis hamikdash*. *Notel ma'ot mi-shulchani ve-noten le-chotzvin ve-lasatatin* — you have the banker pay the hewers and the stone-carvers, *ad she-lo tinaten al gabei ha-dimos* — before the stone is placed on the *dimos*, the layer of stones already in place. *U-mi-she-nitanah al ha-dimos* — once the stone is set in the structure, *mevi ma'ot mi-trumat halishkah u-mechallel aleha* — you bring the sanctified funds and transfer the *kedusha* to the finished stone, then repay the banker with the now-desanctified money. The *gemara* asks the same question here — *v'tavo ken*? — noting that the cost of stone carving may not equal the simple commodity value of the stone. *Rabbi Yose be-reibi Bun b'shem Shmuel* gives the identical answer: *kol mah she-yiten hein hein damav misha'ar rishona* — retroactively, whatever was paid is the assessed value of the stone. The *mefarshim* explain the practical situation: the carvers may have been paid for ongoing labor including wasted stones and failed attempts, costs that would normally not factor into the market price of a single finished stone. Nevertheless, retroactively, that total outlay becomes the stone's assessed value.

One cannot help but note that this is a remarkably precise description of the *beis hamikdash* construction process — the *dimos*, the hewing, the cutting — very likely reflecting Herodian-era stonework.

## Korban Types

### Red Heifer & Scapegoat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** משנה: פָּרָה וְשָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ וְלָשׁוֹן שֶׁל זְהוֹרִית בָּאִין מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִשְׁכָּה. כֶּבֶשׁ פָּרָה וְכֶבֶשׁ שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ וְלָשׁוֹן שֶׁבֵּין קַרְנָיו וְאַמַּת הַמַּיִם וְחוֹמַת הָעִיר וּמִגְדְּלוֹתֶיהָ וְכָל צָרְכֵי הָעִיר בָּאִין מִשְּׁיֵרֵי הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNAH: The Cow, the he-goat which is sent away, and the shiny strip come from the money disbursed from the lodge. The ramp for the Cow, the ramp for the he-goat which is sent away, the strip bound on its horns, the water canal, the city wall and its towers, and all needs of the city, come from the leftovers in the lodge.

The next *mishna* lists further items purchased with the *shekel*, some of which might not be immediately obvious as falling into the category of *korban tzibur*. *Parah* — the *parah adumah*, the red heifer, is not a standard *korban*; it is slaughtered outside the *beis hamikdash*, though it is called a *chatas* and is treated in many respects like a *korban* — its blood is sprinkled toward the *beis hamikdash*. It may be purchased with the communal funds. *Ve-sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* — the goat sent to *Azazel* is not a *korban* at all. Why then may it be purchased from the *shekel*? Because one does not know in advance which of the two *Yom Kippur* goats will be designated for *Azazel* and which for the *mizbe'ach*. Since both must be purchased together, both are acquired with the communal funds.

### Ritual Items

*Lashon shel zehorit* — the crimson strand. This is not the strand tied to the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* at the cliff; rather, this is the strand thrown into the fire of the *parah adumah*, as the *Torah* prescribes adding hyssop and the crimson thread to the burning. This is therefore considered part of the *korban* itself, analogous to the way *nesachim* are considered part of the *korban tzibur*.

### Ramps

*Kevash para ve-kevash sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* — the ramps. When the *parah adumah* was led out to where it would be slaughtered, a special double-ramp structure was constructed — a ramp atop a ramp — to protect it from *tumah*. Similarly, a designated exit ramp was built for the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach*, because crowds would surround and attack the man leading the goat — pulling its hair, shouting at it to carry away their sins and leave. A protected passage was therefore required. *U-leshon shebein karnav* — and the crimson strand between its horns, another element of the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* ritual. All of these come from the *trumat halishka*.

### City Infrastructure

*Ve-amat hamayim ve-chomat ha'ir u-migdaloteha ve-chol tzorchei ha'ir* — the water channel, the walls of Yerushalayim, its towers, and all the city's needs. These obviously are not *korbanos*; accordingly, they do not come from the *trumat halishka* but rather from the *sheyarei halishka*. Recall that the previous material established that when the *truma* is taken from the *shekel* funds, what remains in the chamber is desanctified — except according to *Rabbi Meir*. That desanctified remainder, the *sheyarei halishka*, may be used for non-*korban* and even non-sacred purposes, such as reinforcing the walls of Yerushalayim and attending to the city's municipal needs.

## Leftover Funds

### Abba Shaul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר כֶּבֶשׁ פָּרָה כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ מִשֶּׁל עַצְמָן: מוֹתַר שְׁיֵרֵי לִשְׁכָּה מֶה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהֶן. לוֹקְחִין בָּהֶן יֵינוֹת שְׁמָנִים וּסְלָתוֹת וְהַשָּׂכָר לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ דִּבְרֵי רִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל רִבִּי עֲקִיבָה אוֹמֵר אִין מִשְׁתַּכְּרִין בְּשֶּׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ אַף לֹא מִשֶּׁל עֲנִיִּים:
>
> **Translation:** Abba Shaul says, the High Priests build the ramp for the Cow at their own expense. What did one do with the leftover in the lodge? One buys with it wine, oil, and fine flour, and the gain is the Temple's, the words of Rebbi Ismael. Rebbi Aqiba says, one gains neither for the Temple nor funds for the poor.

*Abba Shaul* adds a dissenting note: *kevash para kohanim gedolim osim mishel atzmam* — the ramp for the *parah adumah* was actually built from the private resources of the *kohanim gedolim*, without recourse to communal funds at all.

### Investment Strategy

The *mishna* then asks: *motar sheyarei lishkah mah hayu osim bahen* — what was done with the remainder of the *sheyarei halishka*? You had funds that were desanctified and used for the city's needs; now the year ends and even those funds have not been fully spent. What do you do with them? *Lokchim bahen yeinot shemanim ve-saltot ve-haschar lehekdesh* — you would purchase wine, oil, and flour, sell them to the people coming to the *beis hamikdash*, and the profit would go to the *hekdesh*. In essence, the *beis hamikdash* would invest its leftover funds by running a small commercial operation.

### R. Yishmael

This is the view of *Rabbi Yishmael*: use the money to generate returns. Whoever administered these funds — the *gizbar* and his staff — would put the money to work, potentially through subcontractors or a side business selling provisions to pilgrims.

### R. Akiva

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: *ein mistakrin beshel hekdesh* — one does not conduct business with *hekdesh* funds. It is not appropriate for the *hekdesh* to operate commercially. The concern is twofold: first, it undermines the sanctity of the institution — one does not want the *beis hamikdash* to appear focused on profit. Second, there is the risk: markets decline. One does not take risks with funds held in sacred or communal trust.

### Poor Funds

*Af lo mishel aniyim* — *Rabbi Akiva* applies the same principle to funds collected for the poor. If the *tzedaka* collectors have a surplus, they should not invest it commercially. Again, the concern may be appearance — those administering communal charity funds should not appear to be profiting from them — or it may be the principle of *ve-heyitem nekiyim*, ensuring transparency and cleanliness in the handling of others' money. Either way, *Rabbi Akiva* holds that neither *hekdesh* nor charitable funds should be put to work in commercial ventures.

## Terumah Remainder

### Gold Plating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתַר הַתְּרוּמָה מֶה הָיוּ עוֹשִׂין בָּהּ. רִקּוּעֵי זָהָב צִיפּוּי לְבֵית קָדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים. רִבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר מוֹתַר הַפֵּירוֹת קַיָיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת. רִבִּי עֲקִיבָה אוֹמֵר מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה קַיָיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. מוֹתַר נְסָכִים לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת. רִבִּי חֲנַנְיָה סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים אוֹמֵר מוֹתַר נְסָכִים קַיָיץ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ מוֹתַר תְּרוּמָה לִכְלֵי שָׁרֵת. זֶה וָזֶה לֹא הָיוּ מוֹדִין בַּפֵּירוֹת:
>
> **Translation:** What did they use the leftover of the disbursement for? Gold sheets coating for the Holiest of Holies. Rebbi Ismael said, the leftover of produce is for adornment of the altar, the leftover of the disbursement is for Service vessels. Rebbi Aqiba says, the leftover of the disbursement is for adornment of the altar, the leftover of the libations is for Service vessels. Rebbi Ḥanania the executive officer of the Cohanim says, the leftover of the libations is for adornment of the altar, the leftover of the disbursement is for Service vessels. Neither of them did agree about produce.

The *mishna* turns from the *sheyarei halishka* to the leftover *truma* itself. *Mosar hatruma ma hayu osim bah* — what was done with the *truma* funds that remained at year's end? The *mishna* answers: *rikuei zahav tzipui leveit kodesh hakodashim* — gold plating for the walls of the *kodesh hakodashim*. Why gold plating for the *kodesh hakodashim* of all things? It is difficult to explain why this in particular is the appropriate use for leftover *shekalim*, given that the *truma* was designated for *korbanos tzibur*.

### Kayitz Option

*Rabbi Yishmael* says: *mosar ha-peros kayitz hamizbe'ach* — the leftover from *peros* goes toward the *kayitz hamizbe'ach*. What is *peros*? The *gemara* will address this. The *kayitz hamizbe'ach* is the practice of bringing voluntary *olos* when the *mizbe'ach* would otherwise stand idle — ensuring that the *mizbe'ach* always appears active and not, as it were, neglected. *Rabbi Yishmael* says that any leftover would go to fund these voluntary burnt offerings. This at least makes intuitive sense: you are using the *shekel* for some form of *korban*, even if not an obligatory one.

*Mosar truma liklei shares* — what is done with the leftover *truma* proper? According to this view, it goes toward purchasing the *klei shares*, the vessels of the *beis hamikdash*. Why the *klei shares* and not something more directly related to *korbanos* is again not immediately obvious.

### R. Akiva Alt

*Rabbi Akiva* says: *motar truma le-kayitz hamizbe'ach* — the leftover *truma* goes to the *kayitz hamizbe'ach*. This is more readily understood: the *truma* was collected for *korbanos*; the *kayitz hamizbe'ach* provides *korbanos*; the usage is consistent with the original purpose of collection.

### Nesachim Option

*Mosar nesachim liklei shares* — the leftover from the *nesachim* fund goes to the *klei shares*. *Rabbi Chananya Segan HaKohanim* — the assistant *kohen*, who presumably had firsthand expertise — says: *mosar nesachim kayitz hamizbe'ach* — no, the leftover *nesachim* funds go to the *kayitz hamizbe'ach*, because the *nesachim* are offerings fully consumed upon the *mizbe'ach*, and the *kayitz ha-mizbe'ach* consists of *olos* similarly consumed in their entirety. The functional similarity suggests that usage. *Mosre hatruma likli shares* — and the leftover *truma* funds, those go for the *klei shares*. *Ve-ze ve-ze lo hayu modim be-peirot* — and both of them would not agree regarding *peiros*. What *peiros* means in this context remains to be addressed in the next *daf*.

The underlying principle seems to be that each type of leftover fund should be directed toward uses that are analogous or parallel to its original function, though the *mishna* and *gemara* do not spell out the reasoning with full clarity. It is also worth noting that the question of what *mosre nesachim* means is itself somewhat obscure: if the *nesachim* were purchased with *trumat halishka* funds, then leftover *nesachim* funds would simply be leftover *truma*. The more natural reading may be that *mosre nesachim* refers to private *nesachim* funds — an individual who set aside money for the *nesachim* of his personal *korban*, had a surplus, and donated that surplus to the *beis hamikdash*. In that case *mosre nesachim* would be a distinct category of private leftover, separate from the communal *truma*.

## String Weights

### Three Strings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳: פָּרָה וְשָׂעִיר כול׳. רִבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי יוֹנָתָן. שְׁלֹשָׁה לְשׁוֹנוֹת הֵן. שֶׁלְשָׂעִיר בְּסֶלַע. שֶׁלְמְצוֹרָע בְּשֶׁקֶל. שֶׁלְפָּרָה בִּשְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים. רִבִּי חוֹנִיָיה דִבְרַת חַוְורָן רִבִּי בָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא בְּשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן חַלְפוּתָא. שֶׁלְפָּרָה בִּשְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים וּמֶחֱצָה. וְאִית דְּמַפְקִין לִישְׁנָא . בָּעֲשָׂרָה (זִין) [זוּז] .
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: "The Cow," etc. Rebbi Samuel bar Naḥman in the name of Rebbi Jonathan: There are three strips. The one for the he-goat for a tetradrachma, for the sufferer from skin disease for a sheqel, for the Cow for two tetradrachmas. Rebbi Onias from Berat-Hauran in the name of Rebbi Abba bar Zavda in the name of Rebbi Simeon ben Ḥalaphta, for the Cow for two tetradrachmas and a half; but some express it in the formulation ten (*zin*) [*zuz*].

*Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachman b'shem Rabbi Yonasan* teaches: *gimel leshonos hen* — there are three strings. *Shel sa'ir ba-sela* — the one used in the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* ritual costs or weighs one *sela*. *Shel metzora be-shekel* — the one used in the purification ritual of the *metzora* costs half a *sela*, which is one *shekel*. *Shel para bishtei sela'im* — the one used for the *parah adumah* weighs two *sela'im*. *Rabbi Chunya bras chavrin* in the name of *Rabbi Ba bar Zavda b'shem Rabbi Shimon ben Chalafsa* says the string for the *parah adumah* was two and a half *sela'im*. Some formulate this as ten *zuz* — since there are four *zuz* to a *sela*, two and a half *sela'im* equals ten *zuz*; this is a trivial variant in expression rather than a substantive dispute.

### Weight Rationales

Why the different weights? The string for the *parah adumah* must be heavy because it is thrown into the fire, and it must be substantial enough to actually reach and burn in the flames. The string for the *sa'ir ha-mishtale'ach* is twice as heavy as that of the *metzora* because it was divided in half: one portion was left at the rock, and the other portion was tied between the horns of the goat. Thus it needed to begin at twice the size of a string used in its entirety.

## Staff Payments

### Sage Teachers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה בְשֵׁם שְׁמוּאֵל. תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַמְלַמְּדִין אֶת הַכֹּהֲנִים הִילְכוֹת שְׁחִיטָה הִילְכוֹת קַבָּלָה הִילְכוֹת זְרִיקָה נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Jehudah in the name of Samuel: The scholars who teach the priests the rules of slaughter, rules of receiving, rules of sprinkling, take their wages from the disbursement from the lodge.

*Rabbi Yehuda b'shem Rabbi Shmuel* teaches: *talmidei chachamim ha-melamdin es ha-kohanim hilchos shechita hilchos kabala hilchos zerika* — the *talmidei chachamim* who would teach the *kohanim* the laws of *shechita*, *kabala*, and *zerika* — the slaughter, reception of the blood, and sprinkling — *notlin secharan mi-trumas ha-lishka* — were paid from the *trumat halishka*. This reflects the assumption embedded in the *gemara* that rabbinic scholars held the relevant expertise and served as instructors to the *kohanim* in the performance of the *avoda*.

This ruling is surprising. The *trumat halishka* was designated for *korbanos tzibur*, and the whole thrust of the preceding discussion has been that even attendant matters connected to *korbanos* are not paid from the *truma* proper. How then can salaries for *talmidei chachamim* — teaching staff — come from this fund? There is no object to which the *kedusha* of the money could transfer. This question will recur with the cases that follow.

### Blemish Inspectors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי יִצְחָק בַּר רְדִיפָה בְשֵׁם אִימִּי. מְבַקְּרֵי מוּמֵי קֳדָשִׁים נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Isaac bar Redifa in the name of Immi: The inspectors of defects of *sancta* take their wages from the disbursement from the lodge.

*Rabbi Yifta bar Redifa b'shem Rabbi Ami* adds: *mevakrei mumei kodshim* — the inspectors who would examine sacrificial animals for disqualifying blemishes — *notlin sechar mi-trumas ha-lishka* — were also paid from the *trumat halishka*. Again, this is not the *korban* itself but rather an enabling function — ensuring the animals offered are *kasher*.

### Scribal Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִבִּי אָחָא רִבִּי תַנְחוּם בַּר חִיָיה בְשֵׁם רִבִּי שִׂמְלַאי. מַגִּיהֵי סֵפֶר הֶעֲזָרָה נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rebbi Aḥa, Rebbi Tanḥum bar Ḥiyya in the name of Rebbi Simlai: Those who correct the scroll of the Temple courtyard take their wages from the disbursement from the lodge.

*Rabbi Acha, Rabbi Tanchum bar Chiya b'shem Rabbi Simlai* teaches: *magihei Sefer HaAzara notlin secharam mi-trumat halishka* — the correctors of the *Sefer HaAzara* were also paid from this fund. What is the *Sefer HaAzara*? Some *mefarshim* identify it with the authoritative *Sefer Torah* kept in the *beis hamikdash*, which was produced by a process of comparing three separate manuscript versions of the *Torah* and following the majority where they differed — a procedure described in rabbinic literature. This *Sefer Torah* served as the master text against which all others were corrected. Some *mefarshim* read *Sefer HaAzara* as referring to the *Sefer Torah* from which the *kohen gadol* would read in the *azara* on *Yom Kippur* — which had to be proofread and corrected regularly. Others note a variant *girsa* of *Sefer Ezra*, identifying it with the text that *Ezra HaSofer* brought back from Babylonia, regarded as the most accurate version.

### In-House Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גִּידּוּל בַּר בִּנְיָמִן בְּשֵׁם אַסִּי. שְׁנֵי דַיָינֵי גְזֵילוֹת נוֹטְלִין שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Giddul bar Benjamin in the name of Assi: The two judges of robberies take their wages from the disbursement from the lodge.

*Gidul bar Binyamin b'shem Rav Assi* (some read *gezerot* instead of *gezelot*) adds: *shenei dayanei gezelot* — two judges who handled theft cases within the *beis hamikdash* and could take on no other occupation — *notlin secharam mi-trumat halishka*. These were the standing in-house *dayanim* of the *mikdash*, and they too were paid from the *truma* fund.

The cumulative problem is evident: all of these — teaching *kohanim*, inspecting animals, proofreading *sifrei Torah*, adjudicating disputes — are paid from the *trumat halishka*, which is supposed to be reserved for *korbanos tzibur*. There is no obvious *korban*-related object to which the *kedusha* of the payment could transfer.

## Parochet Weavers

### Payment Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל אַמַר. נָשִׁים הָאוֹרְגוֹת בַּפְּרָכוֹת נוֹטְלוֹת שְׂכָרָן מִתְּרוּמַת הַלִּשְׁכָּה. רַב חוּנָה אָמַר. מִתְּרוּמַת בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת. מַה וּפְלִיג. שְׁמוּאֵל עֲבֲד לָהּ כְּקָרְבָּן. רַב חוּנָה עֲבֲד לָהּ כְּבִּינְיָין.
>
> **Translation:** Samuel said, women who are weaving the gobelins take their wages from the disbursement from the lodge; Rav Ḥuna said, from disbursements for repair of the House. How do they disagree? Samuel makes it a sacrifice, Rav Huna makes it part of the building.

*Shmuel amar nashim haorgot baparochet* — women who weave the *parochet*, the great curtain of the *beis hamikdash* — whenever the *gemara* discusses weaving or sewing, it characteristically mentions women — *notlin secharam mi-trumat halishka* — they too are paid from the *trumat halishka*. *Rabbi Chuna* disagrees: *lo, mi-trumat Bedek HaBayit* — they are paid not from the *trumat halishka* but from the funds of *bedek habayit*, the fund designated for maintaining and repairing the physical structure of the *beis hamikdash*.

### Conceptual Debate

*Mah palig* — what is the underlying conceptual disagreement? *Shmuel avid lei kikorban* — *Shmuel* treats the *parochet* as analogous to a *korban*: it is essential to the *avoda* and its funding belongs with the *korban* funds. *Rav Huna avid lei kibinyan* — *Rav Huna* treats the *parochet* as part of the building's structure. Is a curtain part of the architectural structure, or is it a hanging appended to it? *Rav Huna* holds it is like the structure, and therefore its funding belongs with *bedek habayit*.

### Resolution

The broader and more fundamental question is how *any* of the payments in this entire section can come from the *trumat halishka*. The *trumat halishka* is sanctified money designated for *korbanos tzibur*, and throughout this *daf* we have insisted that even attendant matters — *machshirei korban* — do not come from it. How then does it pay for teachers, inspectors, scribes, judges, and weavers?

The answer that the *mefarshim* provide is admittedly somewhat deflating in its formalism: the *beis din* stipulates at the outset that if and when the *trumat halishka* funds are used for any of the following purposes, those funds retroactively never acquired sanctity for that use — they are treated as *chulin* for that purpose and may therefore be applied freely. The practical result is a movement from a tightly defined regime — sanctified funds usable only for *korbanos tzibur* — to a far more flexible arrangement in which the *beis din*, through a retroactive condition, can desanctify the funds for a wide range of communal and *mikdash*-related needs that extend well beyond the *korban* itself.
