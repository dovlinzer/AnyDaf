# Bava Metzia 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah Overview

### Four Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהָיְתָה שְׁאוּלָה מֵתָה. וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ – חַיָּיב. הַשּׂוֹכֵר אוֹמֵר: שְׂכוּרָה מֵתָה, בְּיוֹם שֶׁהָיְתָה שְׂכוּרָה מֵתָה, בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהָיְתָה שְׂכוּרָה מֵתָה. וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ – פָּטוּר. זֶה אוֹמֵר: שְׁאוּלָה, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: שְׂכוּרָה – יִשָּׁבַע הַשּׂוֹכֵר שֶׁשְּׂכוּרָה מֵתָה. זֶה אוֹמֵר: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ – יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **it died during the period in which it was being borrowed**, so that, according to his claim, the borrower is liable to pay for the cow, **and the other one, the borrower, says: I do not know** what happened, the borrower is **liable** to pay. If **the renter says: The rented cow is the one that died; or: It died on the day that it was being rented; or: It died during the period in which it was being rented, and the other one, the owner of the cow, says: I do not know** what happened, the renter is **exempt.** If **this** owner **says** with certitude: The **borrowed** cow is the one that died, **and that** renter **says** with certitude: The **rented** cow is the one that died, then **the renter takes an oath that the rented** cow is the one that **died,** and he is then exempt from liability. If **this** one **says: I do not know** what happened, **and that** one **says: I do not know** what happened, then **they divide** the disputed amount. The bailee is liable to pay for only half the value of the cow.

The *mishnah* at the opening of Bava Metzia 98 sits within a broader discussion of the obligations of a *sho'el* — one who borrows an object — but pivots into a distinct and richly complex domain: competing claims of liability when an animal was partly borrowed and partly rented. The scenario involves two parties, an owner and a borrower-renter, who dispute which arrangement was in effect when the animal died or was otherwise damaged in a manner that would render a *sho'el* liable but a *socher* exempt. The *mishnah* presents four cases.

### Case 1: Owner Claims

In the first case, the owner (*mash'il*) asserts definitively that it was the borrowed cow (*she'ula*) that died — meaning the borrower owes compensation — while the borrower responds that he does not know which animal died. The *mishnah* rules that the borrower must pay.

### Case 2: Borrower Claims

In the second case, the roles are reversed: the borrower claims definitively that it was the rented cow (*schura*) that died — freeing him from liability — while the owner says he does not know. The *mishnah* rules that the borrower is exempt.

### Case 3: Opposing Claims

In the third case, both parties make definitive but opposing claims: the owner insists it was the borrowed cow, while the borrower insists it was the rented one. Here the *mishnah* rules that the renter takes an oath (*yishava ha-socher she-schura meta*) that it was the rented cow that died, and he is thereby exempt.

### Case 4: Both Uncertain

In the fourth case, both parties say they do not know. The *mishnah* rules that they divide the liability between them.

## Bari ve-Shema

### Initial Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ: מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Conclude from** the mishna that in a comparable situation, where one says to another: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other one says: I do not know** whether or not I have your money, that the defendant is **liable** to pay.

The *gemara* opens its analysis on 97b by drawing an inference from the first case of the *mishnah*. If the owner makes a definite claim (*bari*) and the borrower responds with uncertainty (*shema* — "I don't know"), and the *mishnah* rules that the borrower must pay, it would seem to follow that a definite claim overcomes the possession of the one holding the money. The usual rule is *hamotzi mi-chaveiro alav ha-ra'aya* — the burden of proof lies with the claimant. And yet here, without any proof, the mere definiteness of the owner's claim appears sufficient to extract payment from the borrower who has *chezkat mamon*.

### Bari Adif Principle

This is the principle known as *bari adif* — the definite claim is stronger — and it would mean that *bari* prevails even over *chezkat mamon*, the presumption that money remains with whoever currently holds it.

### Amoraic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תִּהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב נַחְמָן, דְּאִיתְּמַר: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ. רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמְרִי: חַיָּיב. רַב נַחְמָן וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמְרִי: פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that it is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Naḥman. As it was stated** that the *amora'im* disagreed about the following case: With regard to one who approaches another and says: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other says: I do not know, Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda say:** The respondent is **liable** to pay, because he did not deny the claim. **Rav Naḥman and Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** He is **exempt** from payment.

The *gemara* immediately identifies a conflict with the view of Rav Nachman. As the *gemara* records: Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda hold that in a case where Reuven says "you owe me a hundred" and Shimon says "I don't know," Shimon is *chayav* — he must pay. Rav Nachman and Rav Yochanan, however, rule that he is *patur*, because *chezkat mamon* prevails in the absence of certainty. Our *mishnah*, which rules that the borrower pays in Case 1, seems to be a decisive challenge to Rav Nachman.

### Galgul Shvua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כְּגוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עֵסֶק שְׁבוּעָה בֵּינֵיהֶן. הָכָא נָמֵי – כְּגוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עֵסֶק שְׁבוּעָה בֵּינֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this contention: Just **as Rav Naḥman says** in that context: He is liable to pay only in a case **where there is a matter of an oath between them, here too,** it is a case **where there is a matter of an oath between them.** In that case, Rav Naḥman rules that he is liable to pay only if he is already liable to take an oath concerning his denial of part of the claim. Since he does not know if he owes this sum, and he is therefore unable to take the oath he is liable to take, he must pay. In this case as well, since the bailee does not know what occurred, he cannot take an oath, and must pay.

The *gemara* deflects this challenge by invoking a principle Rav Nachman applies in other contexts: the *mishnah* may be speaking of a case where there is already a *shvua* — an oath obligation — in play between the parties. Once a person is independently obligated to take an oath, the doctrine of *galgul shvua* allows additional oaths to be rolled onto that existing obligation. If, for example, the borrower was already a *modeh b'miktzat* — he partially admitted a separate debt — he would be obligated to take an oath about the remainder. The owner could then invoke *galgul shvua* to require him to also swear about which cow died. But since the borrower says he does not know, he cannot take that oath. The result is *mitoch she-eino yachol lehishava meshalem*: because he is unable to swear, he must pay. Thus, the *mishnah* need not be read as endorsing *bari adif* in a pure sense — it may operate entirely through the mechanism of an oath that cannot be taken.

## Modeh B'Miktzat

### Rava's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״מָנֶה לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא חֲמִשִּׁים, וְהַשְּׁאָר אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ. מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **as Rava says:** One who approaches another and says: **I** have **one hundred dinars in your possession, and the other says: You have in my possession only fifty** dinars that I am sure about, **and** as for **the rest, I do not know.** As one who admitted to part of a claim, he is liable, by Torah law, to take an oath that he does not owe the other fifty dinars. **Since he cannot take an oath** to that effect, as he is unsure if he owes it, **he must pay.**

At the top of 98a, Rava provides the concrete example that anchors this entire analysis. If a claimant says "you owe me a hundred," and the respondent says "I only owe you fifty — the other fifty I don't know whether I owe you or not," this creates a classic *modeh b'miktzat* situation. The respondent has admitted to half the claim and is uncertain about the other half. Had he flatly denied the second fifty, he would take a *shvua* about it in the standard *modeh b'miktzat* framework. Here, though, he cannot take that oath because he genuinely does not know.

### Chain of Logic

The chain of logic is precise: *modeh b'miktzat* generates an obligation to take a *shvua* on the disputed portion. The respondent is thus *chayav* in a *shvua*. Since he cannot fulfill that obligation — he has no knowledge to swear to — the principle of *mitoch she-eino yachol lehishava meshalem* applies: inability to swear converts into an obligation to pay. This, rather than the raw principle of *bari adif*, is the engine driving the ruling in Case 1 of the *mishnah*.

### Two Cows Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ רֵישָׁא בִּשְׁתַּיִם וְסֵיפָא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ. רֵישָׁא בִּשְׁתַּיִם – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁתֵּי פָרוֹת מָסַרְתִּי לָךְ, פַּלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא בִּשְׁאֵילָה, וּפַלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא בִּשְׂכִירוּת. אִי נָמֵי: חַד יוֹמָא בִּשְׁאֵילָה, וְחַד יוֹמָא בִּשְׂכִירוּת, וּמֵתוּ תַּרְוַיְיהוּ בְּעִידָּן שְׁאֵילָה. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ שׁוֹאֵל: חֲדָא – אִין, בְּעִידָּן שְׁאֵילָה מֵתָה, וְאִידַּךְ – לָא יָדַעְנָא אִי בְּעִידָּן שְׁאֵילָה מֵתָה וְאִי בְּעִידָּן שְׂכִירוּת מֵתָה, דְּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how the mishna can be interpreted as referring to a case in which the defendant makes a partial admission, thereby requiring him to take an oath: **You find it** in **the first clause,** i.e., in the first two cases of the mishna, in a situation **where** he took hold of **two** cows, **and** in **the latter clause,** i.e., in the third case, in a situation **where** he took hold of **three** cows. The Gemara explains: One can interpret **the first clause** to be referring to a case **where** one took hold of **two** cows, as follows: The case is **that** the owner **said to** the bailee: **I delivered two cows to you** under the agreement that one **half** of the **day** you would have them **through borrowing and** the other **half** of the **day through renting,** or, **alternatively, one day through borrowing and one day through renting. And** I claim that **both died during a period of borrowing,** so you are liable to pay for both of them. **And the borrower said to him:** With regard to **one** of them, **yes,** I admit that **it died during a period of borrowing.** But with regard to the **other one, I do not know whether it died during a period of borrowing or if it died during a period of renting.** Because he admits to part of the claim, he is required to take an oath in order to be exempt from the rest of the claim. As he concedes that he does not know what happened, he is unable to take such an oath. The *halakha* is that **since he is unable to take an oath, he must pay** for both cows.

The *gemara* then works to fold this mechanism directly into the *mishnah*'s own cow-based scenario, rather than importing a separate hundred-dollar debt from outside. For the first case of the *mishnah* (*reisha*), the *gemara* proposes reading it as involving two cows — *reisha b'veiz*. Reuven says to Shimon: "I gave you two cows, half the day borrowed and half the day rented" (or day one borrowed and day two rented). Both cows died. Reuven claims both died during the borrowed period. Shimon the *sho'el* responds: "The first one — I concede, it died when it was borrowed, I owe you for that cow." He has now admitted to one full cow's worth of liability. "The second — I don't know whether it died when borrowed or when rented." He cannot swear about the second cow. Since he is *modeh b'miktzat* — admitting to the first cow — he is obligated to take a *shvua* about the second. Unable to do so, *mitoch she-eino yachol lehishava meshalem*: he pays for the second cow as well.

### Three Cows Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסֵיפָא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁלֹשׁ פָּרוֹת נָתַתִּי לָךְ, שְׁתַּיִם בִּשְׁאֵילָה וְאַחַת בִּשְׂכִירוּת, וָמֵתוּ הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי דִּשְׁאֵילָה, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ שׁוֹאֵל: אִין, חֲדָא דִּשְׁאֵילָה מֵתָה, אִידַּךְ – לָא יָדַעְנָא אִי דִּשְׁאֵילָה מֵתָה וּדְקָיְימָא דִּשְׂכִירוּת הִיא, אִי דִּשְׂכִירוּת מֵתָה וְהָא דְּקָיְימָא דִּשְׁאֵילָה הִיא, וּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one can interpret **the latter clause** to be referring to a case **where** he took hold of **three** cows, as follows: The case is **that** the owner **said to** the bailee: **I gave three cows to you; two through borrowing and one through renting. And** I claim that **those two that** were being **borrowed** were the ones that **died. And the borrower said to him: Yes,** I admit that **one** of the cows **that** was **borrowed died.** But with regard to **the other** one that died, **I do not know if** it was the other cow that was being **borrowed** that **died, and** so the cow **that is** still **alive is** the one **that** was being **rented, or** if the one **that** was being **rented died, and this** one **that is** still **alive is** the one **that** was being **borrowed.** Because he admits to part of the claim, in order to be exempt from the rest of the claim he is required to take an oath. As he concedes that he does not know what happened, he is unable to take such an oath. And the *halakha* is that **since he is unable to take an oath, he must pay** for both cows.

For the final cases of the *mishnah* (*seifa*), where the scenario involves two cows of different status, the *gemara* adds yet another cow — *seifa b'gimmel*. Reuven says: "I gave you three cows, two borrowed and one rented. Two died, and I say both were the borrowed ones." Shimon responds: "I concede — one of the borrowed cows died, and I owe you for that." That is the *modeh b'miktzat*. "The other — I don't know. Was it the second borrowed cow that died, making the surviving one the rented cow and leaving me liable for both? Or was it the rented cow that died, making the surviving one the borrowed cow, in which case I owe you only for the first?" Again he cannot swear, and *mitoch she-eino yachol lehishava meshalem* compels payment.

### Resolution

The upshot of this entire sugya is that the *mishnah* need not be read as endorsing the position of Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda that *bari adif* suffices to overcome *chezkat mamon*. Rather, the *mishnah* is constructed — in the *gemara*'s reading — so that the payment obligation flows entirely from an inability to swear after having been rendered *chayav* in a *shvua* through *modeh b'miktzat*. This preserves Rav Nachman's position that *bari* alone does not win out over possession.

## Rami Bar Chama

### Shomrim Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, דְּאָמַר: אַרְבָּעָה שׁוֹמְרִים צְרִיכִין כְּפִירָה בְּמִקְצָת וְהוֹדָאָה בְּמִקְצָת, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ רֵישָׁא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ וְסֵיפָא בְּאַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how the mishna can be interpreted even according to the unique opinion of Rami bar Ḥama: **And** the mishna can be interpreted **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rami bar Ḥama, who says:** In order for any of the **four** types of **bailees** to be required to take an oath, **they need** to make both **a denial of part** of the owner's claim **and an admission of** another **part** of his claim. According to his opinion, **you find** the bailee is liable to take an oath in **the first clause,** i.e., in the first two cases of the mishna, in a situation **where** he took hold of **three** cows, **and** in **the latter clause,** i.e., in the third case, in a situation **where** he took hold of **four** cows.

The *gemara* now introduces a further complication. Rami Bar Chama derives from the verse *ki asher yomar ki hu zeh* — "it is this one" — a general principle governing all four categories of *shomrim* (guardians) in the Torah. Each of the four guardians — *shomer chinam* (unpaid guardian), *shomer sachar* (paid guardian), *socher* (renter), and *sho'el* (borrower) — is at times required to take a *shvuat ha-shomrim*, an oath about what befell the entrusted object.

### Hoda'ah + Kfira

Rami Bar Chama holds that *shvuat ha-shomrim* is only operative when the *shomer* is already standing within the framework of *modeh b'miktzat* — but critically, his version of *modeh b'miktzat* requires not only a partial admission (*hoda'ah b'miktzat*) but also a partial denial (*kfira b'miktzat*). The classic *modeh b'miktzat* is: "You claim I owe you a hundred — I admit to fifty and deny the other fifty." The denial is as essential as the admission. If the respondent merely admits to one portion and says he is uncertain about another, that does not satisfy Rami Bar Chama's requirement. He needs an active *kfira* — a genuine denial of one part. Only once both the *hoda'ah* and the *kfira* are in place can *shvuat ha-shomrim* be invoked.

### Three Cows (Rami)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא בְּשָׁלֹשׁ – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: שָׁלֹשׁ פָּרוֹת נָתַתִּי לְךָ, פַּלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא בִּשְׁאֵלָה וּפַלְגֵיהּ דְּיוֹמָא בִּשְׂכִירוּת, אִי נָמֵי חַד יוֹמָא בִּשְׁאֵלָה וְחַד יוֹמָא בִּשְׂכִירוּת, וּמֵתוּ תְּלָת כּוּלְּהוּ בְּעִידָּן שְׁאֵילָה, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ שׁוֹאֵל: חֲדָא – לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם. וַחֲדָא – אִין, בְּעִידָּן שְׁאֵילָה מֵתָה, וְאִידַּךְ – לָא יָדַעְנָא אִי בְּעִידָּן שְׁאֵילָה מֵתָה וְאִי בְּעִידָּן שְׂכִירוּת מֵתָה, דְּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: One can interpret **the first clause** to be referring to a case **where** he took hold of **three** cows, as follows: The case is **that** the owner **said to** the bailee: **I gave three cows to you** under the agreement that one **half of the day** you would have them **through borrowing and** the other **half of the day through renting,** or, **alternatively, one day through borrowing and one day through renting. And** I claim that **all three of them died during a period of borrowing,** and so you are liable to pay for all of them. **And the borrower said to him:** With regard to **one** of them, this **matter never occurred,** as I took only two cows from you. **And,** as for the two cows I did take, with regard to **one** of them, **yes,** I admit that **it died during a period of borrowing.** But with regard to the **other one, I do not know whether it died during a period of borrowing or if it died during a period of renting.** Because the bailee admits to part of the claim and denies another part of the claim, he is required to take an oath in order to be exempt from the rest of the claim. As he concedes that he does not know what happened, he is unable to take such an oath. The *halakha* is that **since he is unable to take an oath, he must pay** for all three cows.

This substantially raises the number of cows required to construct the *mishnah*'s scenario. For the first case (*reisha*), Rami Bar Chama requires three cows — *reisha b'gimmel*. Reuven says: "I gave you three cows, and half the day they were borrowed, half the day rented" (or one day borrowed, the next rented). All three died, and Reuven claims all three died during the borrowed period. Shimon responds: "The first — you're lying, I never borrowed that cow at all (*lo hayu devarim me'olam*)." That is the *kfira b'miktzat* — an outright denial of one unit of the claim. "The second — I concede, it died when it was borrowed, I owe you." That is the *hoda'ah b'miktzat*. Now Rami Bar Chama's conditions are met: there is both a partial denial and a partial admission. With *modeh b'miktzat* properly constituted in his view, *shvuat ha-shomrim* can attach. "The third — I don't know whether it died when borrowed or when rented." He cannot swear, and *mitoch she-eino yachol lehishava meshalem* requires payment.

### Four Cows (Rami)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֵיפָא בְּאַרְבַּע – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַרְבַּע פָּרוֹת נָתַתִּי לְךָ, שָׁלֹשׁ בִּשְׁאֵלָה, חֲדָא בִּשְׂכִירוּת, וּמֵתוּ הָנָךְ שָׁלֹשׁ דִּשְׁאֵלָה. וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ שׁוֹאֵל: חֲדָא – לֹא הָיוּ דְבָרִים מֵעוֹלָם. וַחֲדָא – אִין, דִּשְׁאֵלָה מֵתָה, וְאִידַּךְ – לָא יָדַעְנָא אִי דִּשְׂכִירוּת מֵתָה וּדְקָיְימָא דִּשְׁאֵלָה, אוֹ דִּשְׁאֵלָה מֵתָה וּדְקָיְימָא דִּשְׂכִירוּת, דְּמִתּוֹךְ שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לִישָּׁבַע – מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** One can interpret **the latter clause** to be referring to a case **where** he took hold of **four** cows, as follows: The case is **that** the owner **said to** the bailee: **I gave four cows to you, three through borrowing** and **one through renting. And** I claim that **those three that** were being **borrowed** were the ones that **died. And** he **said to him,** i.e., the **borrower** said to the owner: With regard to **one** of the cows that you claim, this **matter never occurred,** as I never took that cow from you. **And** as for the cows that I did take that died, with regard to **one** of them, **yes,** I admit that it was a cow **that** was being **borrowed** that **died; but the other** cow that died, **I do not know whether** it was the cow that was being **rented** that **died, and** so the cow **that is** still **alive** is the one **that** was being **borrowed, or** whether the cow **that** was being **borrowed died, and** so the cow **that is** still **alive is** the one **that** was being **rented.** Because the bailee admits to part of the claim and denies another part of the claim, he is required to take an oath in order to be exempt from the rest of the claim. As he concedes that he does not know what happened, he is unable to take such an oath. The *halakha* is that **since he is unable to take an oath, he must pay** for all three cows.

For the final cases of the *mishnah*, Rami Bar Chama requires four cows — *seifa b'arba*. Reuven says: "I gave you four cows — three borrowed and one rented — and three died, all during the borrowed period." Shimon responds: "The first — I never borrowed that cow (*lo hayu devarim me'olam*)." That is the *kfira*. "The second — I concede, it died when borrowed." That is the *hoda'ah*. Those two extra cows establish the required *modeh b'miktzat* with both its components. "The remaining two — I don't know which is which, whether it was the borrowed or the rented one that died." He cannot swear about those two, which replicate the original two-cow scenario of the *mishnah*, and so he must pay.

### Why Both Required

The reason Rami Bar Chama insists on both *kfira* and *hoda'ah* is that, in his view, *shvuat ha-shomrim* requires the full envelope of *modeh b'miktzat* as its gateway. A partial admission alone, accompanied only by uncertainty rather than denial, does not constitute the complete framework. The *kfira* and the *hoda'ah* together form the protective gate through which *shvuat ha-shomrim* must pass.

## Case 3: Opposing Claims

### Galgul Shvua Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה אוֹמֵר שְׁאוּלָה, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר שְׂכוּרָה – יִשָּׁבַע הַשּׂוֹכֵר שֶׁשְּׂכוּרָה מֵתָה. וְאַמַּאי? מַה שֶּׁטְּעָנוֹ – לֹא הוֹדָה לוֹ, וּמַה שֶּׁהוֹדָה לוֹ – לֹא טְעָנוֹ! אָמַר עוּלָּא: עַל יְדֵי גִּלְגּוּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: The bailee rented one cow and borrowed another one. **This** owner **says** with certitude: The **borrowed** cow is the one that died. **And that** renter **says** with certitude: The **rented** cow is the one that died. In this case, **the renter takes an oath that the rented** cow is the one that **died** and then he is exempt. The Gemara asks: **But why** should he take an oath? **That which** the owner **claimed from him, he did not admit to** at all, **and that which** the bailee **admitted to,** the owner **had not claimed from him.** In order to be required to take an oath, the bailee must admit to part of the owner's actual claim. **Ulla said:** The mishna is referring to a case where the owner required the bailee to take another oath **by extending** the oath that he had already required him to take.

The *gemara* now turns to the third case of the *mishnah*, in which both parties make definitive opposing claims — the owner says it was definitely the borrowed cow, the borrower says it was definitely the rented cow — and the *mishnah* rules that the *socher* takes an oath and is exempt. This is puzzling. If the *socher* simply denies the owner's claim outright, this is a case of *kofer ha-kol* — a complete denial — and a *kofer ha-kol* normally requires no oath at all; he simply goes free. Why does the *mishnah* impose an oath?

The *gemara* asks this directly: *ve-amai?* What he was claimed from he did not concede, and what he conceded was not what was claimed from him. There is no *modeh b'miktzat* here at all. Ulla answers: *al yedei gilgul* — through *galgul shvua*.

### Renter's Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִישְׁתְּבַע לִי אֵיזוֹ מִיהַת דִּכְדַרְכָּהּ מֵתָה, וּמִיגּוֹ דְּמִישְׁתְּבַע דִּכְדַרְכָּהּ מֵתָה, מִישְׁתְּבַע נָמֵי דִּשְׂכוּרָה מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: For example, this is a case **where** the owner **said to him: Take an oath to me, in any event, that** the cow **died naturally,** and not as a result of your negligence. The owner has a right to demand such an oath. **And since** the bailee **is made to take an oath that** the cow **died naturally,** that oath can be extended such that **he** can **also be made to take an oath that** it was the cow that was **rented** that **died.**

Unlike the first case, however, where the *gemara* had to introduce external circumstances to generate the base oath obligation, here the *socher* has an inherent, independent *shvua* he must take. The *shvuat ha-shomrim* naturally applies to his situation. Even if we were to accept the *socher*'s version entirely — that it was definitively the rented cow that died — he would still be obligated to take the classic *shvuat ha-shomrim*: an oath that the animal died in an ordinary way (*ke-darkah meta*), without any negligence on his part. This oath is not about which cow died; it is about the manner of death. As a *socher*, he is always subject to this obligation.

### Adding the Oath

Once that underlying *shvua* is in place, *galgul shvua* operates: since he is already taking an oath, the court can add onto it a further oath — that it was in fact the rented cow that died and not the borrowed one. This combined oath covers both the manner of death and the identity of the animal. This is far simpler than the first case, which required constructing *modeh b'miktzat* through additional cows. Here the basis for the oath is already built into the *socher*'s natural situation as a guardian, and *galgul shvua* then extends that oath to encompass the disputed question of which animal it was.

## Case 4: Both Uncertain

### Sumchus Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה אוֹמֵר אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ וְזֶה אוֹמֵר כּוּ׳. הָא מַנִּי – סוֹמְכוֹס הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק – חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna concludes: If **this** one **says: I do not know** what happened, **and that** one **says:** I do not know what happened, then they divide the disputed amount. The bailee is liable to pay for only half the value of the cow. The Gemara asks: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this?** It **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Sumakhos, who says:** When there is **property of uncertain ownership,** the parties **divide** it equally between them.

The fourth and final case of the *mishnah* — where both parties say *eini yodea*, "I don't know" — leads the *gemara* to identify whose opinion the *mishnah* reflects. The ruling that they divide the payment corresponds to the view of Sumchus, who holds *mammon ha-mutal b'safek cholkin*: when money is in a state of genuine doubt, the parties split it.

### Sumchus Debate

The counterposition is *hamotzi mi-chaveiro alav ha-ra'aya*: even in a situation of total uncertainty, if you wish to extract money from someone else's possession, you must prove your claim; absent proof, the money stays where it is. The *mishnah*'s ruling to divide, rather than leave the money with the current holder, follows Sumchus. The objective structure of the doubt here is concrete — it is known that one cow was borrowed and one was rented, both parties acknowledge they do not know which died — and it is precisely in such circumstances that Sumchus applies his principle of division.

## Ba'alim Factor

### Question 1

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: שְׁאָלָהּ בִּבְעָלִים, שְׂכָרָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּבְעָלִים, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן שְׁאֵילָה לְחוֹדַהּ קָיְימָא וּשְׂכִירוּת לְחוֹדַהּ קָיְימָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא שְׂכִירוּת בִּשְׁאֵלָה מֵישָׁךְ שָׁיְיכִי, דְּהָא מִיחַיַּיב בִּגְנֵיבָה וַאֲבֵידָה?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Abba bar Memel raises a dilemma:** If one **borrowed** a cow together **with** borrowing the services of its **owner,** and then, before returning the cow, **he rented** the cow from the owner **without** borrowing the services of its **owner, what is** the *halakha*? **Do we say:** The **borrowing stands by itself and** the **renting stands by itself,** i.e., they are two independent transactions, and so the bailee is liable for any mishap that occurs during the renting period, as the owner's services were not borrowed by him during that time? **Or, perhaps** the subsequent **renting is related to** the prior **borrowing** and is an extension of it. It could be said that the renting is related to the borrowing **because** a renter is **liable for theft and loss,** as is a borrower. Perhaps the liability resulting from renting that immediately follows a period of borrowing is a downgraded form of the liability undertaken at the start of the period of borrowing. If so, then in this case, since the bailee bore no liability during the borrowing period, as the cow was borrowed while the owner was providing his services to the borrower, the bailee will not bear liability during the rental period.

The *gemara* now opens a new set of questions raised by Rabbi Abba bar Mamal, concerning the interplay between different successive arrangements and the doctrine of *ba'alim* — whether the owner was working for the borrower at the time of the transaction. The rule is that if the owner was working for the borrower (*ba'alim*) at the time of the borrowing, the *sho'el* is exempt from liability even for *ones* (accidents). The question is how this exemption carries across transitions from one arrangement to another.

The first question: if a person borrowed a cow when the owner was working for him (*shala b'va'alim*), and then, in the middle of that period, he converted the arrangement into a rental without the owner being present (*sachar shelo b'va'alim*), what is the law? Two frameworks are possible. Perhaps each transaction — the *she'ila* and the *schirus* — stands independently, such that the rental, having been made without *ba'alim*, carries no exemption. Or perhaps the *schirus* is seen as a continuation of the *she'ila* (*schirus b'she'ila mishach shaichi*), since the liability for *gneiva va'aveida* that began during the *she'ila* continues into the *schirus* period without interruption. If so, the *ba'alim* exemption that protected against *gneiva va'aveida* during the *she'ila* may carry forward into the *schirus* as well.

### Continuity

The question turns on whether *she'ila* and *schirus* are to be treated as discrete transactions or as a single continuous engagement. In favor of continuity: the *socher*'s liability for *gneiva va'aveida* is the same as the *sho'el*'s, making the transition feel seamless. In favor of separation: the payment arrangement and the level of liability for *ones* differ fundamentally between the two.

### Exemption Carry-Over

The specific sub-question is whether the *ba'alim* exemption from *she'ila* — which applied to the full range of *she'ila* liabilities, including *ones* — continues to protect the *socher* against *gneiva va'aveida* during the *schirus* period. If the two arrangements are one continuous thing, it might. If they are separate, the *schirus* period lacks its own *ba'alim* basis and the exemption would not apply.

### Question 2

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר שְׂכִירָה בִּשְׁאֵלָה מֵישָׁךְ שָׁיְיכִי, שְׂכָרָהּ בִּבְעָלִים שְׁאָלָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּבְעָלִים, מַהוּ? שְׁאֵלָה בִּשְׂכִירוּת וַדַּאי לָא שָׁיְיכָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁיְיכָא בְּמִקְצָת, כְּמַאן דְּשָׁיְיכָא בְּכוּלַּהּ דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **If you say** that in such a case, the subsequent **renting is related to** the prior **borrowing** and is an extension of it, then in the reverse case, where **one rented** a cow together **with** borrowing the services of its **owner,** and then, before returning the cow, **he borrowed it** from the owner **without** borrowing the services of its **owner, what is** the *halakha*? Do we say that the subsequent **borrowing is certainly not related to** or an extension of the prior **renting,** as a borrower undertakes a higher level of liability than a renter? **Or perhaps, since** the subsequent borrowing is **partially related** to the prior renting, as the borrower is liable for theft and loss just as a renter is, **it is considered as though it were related entirely to it** and is an extension of it. Consequently, the exemption of using an item together with its owner's services also applies to the subsequent borrowing.

The second question is the reverse: the person rented the cow with the owner working for him (*sachar b'va'alim*) — exempting him from *gneiva va'aveida* — and then, before the rental ended, converted the arrangement into a borrowing without the owner present (*shala shelo b'va'alim*). Here the question is whether the *ba'alim* exemption from the original *schirus* continues into the *she'ila*.

### New Liabilities

This case is more complex than the first. When a *socher* becomes a *sho'el*, he does not merely continue with existing obligations — he takes on new ones. As a *socher*, he was liable for *gneiva va'aveida* but exempt from *ones*. As a *sho'el*, he becomes liable for *ones* as well. This expansion of liability is arguably a qualitative break. The concern is: since *ones* liability is entirely new and had no *ba'alim* exemption attached to it, perhaps the transition to *she'ila* is so significant a change that even the *gneiva va'aveida* liability is treated anew, without any carryover of the *ba'alim* exemption. Alternatively, one might argue that since the *ba'alim* exemption remains relevant to part of the new arrangement — the *gneiva va'aveida* component — it should be considered relevant to the whole (*keivan d'shaichi b'miktzat keman d'shaichi b'kula dami*), and the *sho'el* would be entirely exempt.

### Question 3

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר לָא אָמְרִינַן כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁיְיכָא בְּמִקְצָת כְּמַאן דְּשָׁיְיכָא בְּכוּלַּהּ דָּמֵי: שְׁאָלָהּ בִּבְעָלִים וּשְׂכָרָהּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּבְעָלִים, וְחָזַר וּשְׁאָלָהּ, מַהוּ? הֲדַר אָתְיָא לַהּ שְׁאֵלָה לְדוּכְתַּהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא אִפְּסִיקָא לַהּ שְׂכִירוּת בֵּינֵי וּבֵינֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **If you say:** In such a case, **we do not say that since** the subsequent borrowing is **partially related** to the prior renting, **it is considered as though it were entirely related to it** and is an extension of it, then in a case where **one borrowed** a cow together **with** borrowing the services of its **owner, and** then, before returning the cow, **he rented it** from the owner **without** borrowing the services of its **owner, and then** at the end of the renting period, **he** again **borrowed it** without the owner's services, **what is** the *halakha*? Do we say that the second period of **borrowing returns to its** original **place,** i.e., is the third period, in which one borrowed the cow, essentially a continuation of the first period? **Or, perhaps,** the period of **renting** serves as **an interruption in the middle** of the two periods of borrowing, such that the third period cannot be seen as a continuation of the first period?

The third question compounds the earlier ones: the person borrowed with *ba'alim*, then rented without *ba'alim*, and then borrowed again — also without *ba'alim* (*shala b'va'alim, sachar shelo b'va'alim, v'chazar v'shala*). The question is whether, when the arrangement cycles back to *she'ila* at the end, the original *ba'alim* status is restored (*hadar ati lei she'ila l'duchtah*).

### Status Return

This question seems to assume, as a premise, that when *she'ila* converted to *schirus*, the *ba'alim* exemption continued into the *schirus* for *gneiva va'aveida* — otherwise the *schirus* interlude would have no relevance to restoring anything. The question then is whether the *schirus* period is a full enough break that the return to *she'ila* does not recapture the original *ba'alim* status, or whether the continuity of the arrangement is such that cycling back to *she'ila* restores all of its original characteristics, including the *ba'alim* exemption even for *ones*.

### Question 4: Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂכָרָהּ בִּבְעָלִים וּשְׁאָלָהּ וְחָזַר וּשְׂכָרָהּ, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן אָתְיָא לַהּ שְׂכִירוּת לְדוּכְתַּהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא אִפְּסִיקָא לַהּ שְׁאֵלָה בֵּינֵי וּבֵינֵי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, in another case, **one rented** a cow together **with** borrowing the services of its **owner. And** then, before returning the cow, **he borrowed it** from the owner without borrowing the services of its owner. **And then,** at the end of the borrowing period, **he** again **rented it,** without borrowing the services of the owner. **What is** the *halakha*? **Do we say** that the second period of **renting returns** it **to its** original **place,** i.e., is the second period essentially a continuation of the first period? **Or, perhaps** the period of **borrowing** serves as **an interruption in the middle** of the two periods of renting, such that the third period cannot be seen as a continuation of the first period. The Gemara concludes: These dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

The fourth and final question is the most complex: the person rented with *ba'alim*, then borrowed without *ba'alim*, then rented again (*sachar b'va'alim, sha'al shelo b'va'alim, v'chazar v'sachar*). Here the transition from *schirus* to *she'ila* is, as the *gemara* has suggested throughout, a harder case for continuity — since the *she'ila* introduces genuinely new liabilities, it represents a more substantial break. The question is whether, after that intervening *she'ila*, the final return to *schirus* restores the *ba'alim* exemption that had applied to the original *schirus*, or whether the *she'ila* in the middle definitively severs the chain. The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved: *teiku*.
