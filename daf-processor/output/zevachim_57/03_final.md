# Zevachim 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodashim Kalim Categories

### Three Kodashim Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבְּכוֹר וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר וְהַפֶּסַח – קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים – שְׁחִיטָתָן בְּכׇל מָקוֹם בָּעֲזָרָה; וְדָמָן טָעוּן מַתָּנָה אֶחָת, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן כְּנֶגֶד הַיְסוֹד. שִׁינָּה בַּאֲכִילָתָן. הַבְּכוֹר – נֶאֱכָל לַכֹּהֲנִים, וְהַמַּעֲשֵׂר – לְכׇל אָדָם; וְנֶאֱכָלִין בְּכׇל הָעִיר, בְּכׇל מַאֲכָל, לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד. הַפֶּסַח – אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא בַּלַּיְלָה, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא עַד חֲצוֹת, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא לִמְנוּיָו, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא צָלִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The firstborn** offering, **the** animal **tithe** offering, **and the Paschal offering are offerings of lesser sanctity. Their slaughter is anywhere in the** Temple **courtyard, and their blood requires one placement, provided that** the priest **places it** so that the blood goes **on the base** of the altar. The *halakha* **differs with regard to their consumption. The firstborn** offering **is eaten by the priests, and the** animal **tithe** offering is eaten **by any person,** i.e., any ritually pure Jew. **And they are eaten throughout the city** of Jerusalem, prepared **in any** manner of **food** preparation, **for two days and one night. The Paschal offering is eaten only at night, and it is eaten only until midnight, and it is eaten only by its registrants,** i.e., those who registered in advance to partake of the offering, **and it is eaten only roasted,** not prepared in any other manner.

The present daf — the last of the fifth *perek* — opens its discussion of *kodashim kalim* by examining three remaining sacrificial categories: *bechor*, *ma'aser*, and *pesach*. All three share a common structural feature: a single application of blood *keneged hayesod*. Beyond that, however, they diverge considerably in the parameters governing their consumption. The *bechor* is eaten exclusively by *kohanim*; *ma'aser behema* is eaten by its owners and may be consumed anywhere within Yerushalayim; and the *pesach* is subject to a unique set of restrictions — it may only be eaten until *chatzot*, only roasted, and only by those who had been counted among its registered participants (*limnuyav*). Both *bechor* and *ma'aser* are eaten over two days and the intervening night, while the *pesach* is more severely limited. The Gemara now proceeds to derive the sources for each of these parameters.

## Bechor Two Days/Night

### Tannaim Bechor Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לַבְּכוֹר שֶׁנֶּאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לָּךְ כַּחֲזֵה הַתְּנוּפָה וּכְשׁוֹק הַיָּמִין״ – הִקִּישׁוֹ הַכָּתוּב לְחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק שֶׁל שְׁלָמִים; מָה שְׁלָמִים נֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד, אַף בְּכוֹר נֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **with regard to a firstborn** offering **that** it is **eaten for two days and** the intervening **night?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated** with regard to a firstborn offering: **"And their flesh shall be yours, as the breast of waving and as the right thigh"** (Numbers 18:18). **The verse juxtaposes** the firstborn offering **with the breast and thigh of peace offerings. Just as peace offerings are eaten for two days and one night, so too, a firstborn** offering is **eaten for two days and one night.**

The *Tanu Rabbanan* opens by asking: from where do we derive that a *bechor* may be eaten for two days and the intervening night? The *Torah* does not state this period explicitly for *bechor*. The answer lies in a verse that links the *bechor* directly to the *shelamim*: *uvesaram yihye lach kachazeh hatenufa ukeshok hayamin lach yihye* — "and their flesh shall be yours, like the breast of waving and like the right thigh" (Bamidbar 18:18). The verse compares the flesh of the *bechor* to the *chazeh* and *shok* of the *shelamim*. Just as *shelamim* is eaten for two days and one intervening night — a rule explicit in the *Torah* — so too *bechor* is eaten for two days and the intervening night. The reason the verse does not simply say "the *bechor* belongs to the *kohanim*" is precisely to create this *hekesh* and thereby teach the eating period.

### Yavneh Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְזוֹ שְׁאֵלָה נִשְׁאֲלָה לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים בַּכֶּרֶם בְּיַבְנֶה: בְּכוֹר לְכַמָּה נֶאֱכָל? נַעֲנָה רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְאָמַר: לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* relates: **And this question was asked before the Sages in the vineyard,** i.e., the academy, **in Yavne: For how many** days and nights is **a firstborn** offering **eaten? Rabbi Tarfon responded and said: For two days and one night.**

This question was put before the Sages in the *kerem* of Yavneh — the *yeshiva* that reconstituted Torah scholarship following the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The question posed was: for how long is a *bechor* eaten? Rabbi Tarfon answered directly: two days and the intervening night, though at this stage he did not explain the derivation.

### Yose HaGalili Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה שָׁם תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד שֶׁבָּא לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ לִפְנֵי חֲכָמִים תְּחִלָּה, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי שְׁמוֹ, אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, מִנַּיִן לָךְ? אָמַר לוֹ: בְּנִי, שְׁלָמִים קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, וּבְכוֹר קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים; מָה שְׁלָמִים נֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד, אַף בְּכוֹר נֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **There was one student there who came to the study hall before the Sages** for the **first** time, **and his name** was **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. He said to** Rabbi Tarfon: **My teacher, from where do you** derive this? Rabbi Tarfon **said to him: My son, peace offerings** are **offerings of lesser sanctity, and a firstborn** offering is **an offering of lesser sanctity. Just as peace offerings are eaten for two days and one night, so too, a firstborn** offering is **eaten for two days and one night.**

A student who was attending the *beit midrash* for the very first time — later to become the celebrated Rabbi Yose HaGalili — pressed Rabbi Tarfon for his source. Rabbi Tarfon responded with a *ma matzinu* argument: *shelamim* are *kodashim kalim* and *bechor* is *kodashim kalim*; just as *shelamim* is eaten for two days and one night, so too *bechor*. Rabbi Yose HaGalili immediately challenged this reasoning: one could equally well categorize the *bechor* not alongside *shelamim* but alongside *chatat* and *asham*, for *bechor matana lakohen vechatat ve'asham matana lakohen* — the *bechor*, like the *chatat* and the *asham*, is given as a gift directly to the *kohen*. Just as *chatat* and *asham* are eaten for only one day and the following night, perhaps *bechor* should likewise be eaten for only one day and night.

### Tarfon Responds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: נָדוּן דָּבָר מִתּוֹךְ דָּבָר, וְנִלְמַד דָּבָר מִתּוֹךְ דָּבָר; מָה שְׁלָמִים אֵין בָּאִין עַל חֵטְא – אַף בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ בָּא עַל חֵטְא, מָה שְׁלָמִים נֶאֱכָלִים לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד – אַף בְּכוֹר נֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Tarfon **said to him: Let us judge a matter from a matter** to which it is most similar, i.e., a firstborn offering is an offering of lesser sanctity, as are peace offerings, **and learn a matter from a matter,** i.e., there is another comparison to be made: **Just as peace offerings do not come** as atonement **for a sin, so too, a firstborn** offering **does not come** as atonement **for a sin. Just as peace offerings are eaten for two days and one night, so too, a firstborn** offering is **eaten for two days and one night.**

Rabbi Tarfon acknowledged the logic but pushed back: one should compare things to what they most resemble, and there is a compelling reason to place *bechor* alongside *shelamim* rather than *chatat* and *asham*. Neither *shelamim* nor *bechor* comes as a result of any sin — *shelamim ein ba'in al cheit af bechor eino ba al cheit*. They are both *kodashim kalim*, freely brought and not sin-related. On those grounds, the two-day period of *shelamim* should apply to *bechor* as well.

### Yose Counter-argument

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, נָדוּן דָּבָר מִדָּבָר, יִלְמַד דָּבָר מִדָּבָר; חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן, וּבְכוֹר מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן; מָה חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם אֵין בָּאִין בְּנֶדֶר וּנְדָבָה – אַף בְּכוֹר אֵינוֹ בְּנֶדֶר וּנְדָבָה, מָה חַטָּאת וְאָשָׁם נֶאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד – אַף בְּכוֹר נֶאֱכָל לְיוֹם אֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **said to him: My teacher,** I can answer you in a similar way. **Let us judge a matter from a matter** to which it is most similar, i.e., a firstborn offering is more similar to a sin offering and a guilt offering, and **learn a matter from a matter,** i.e., there is another comparison to be made: **A sin offering and a guilt offering** are each given as **a gift to the priest, and a firstborn** offering is given as **a gift to the priest. Just as a sin offering and a guilt offering do not come as a vow** offering **or** as **a gift** offering, i.e., they are brought only by one obligated to bring them, **so too, a firstborn** offering **does not** come **as a vow** offering **or as a gift** offering. **Just as a sin offering and a guilt offering are eaten for one day** and one night, **so too, a firstborn** offering should be **eaten for one day** and one night.

Rabbi Yose HaGalili was not persuaded. He countered that just as one should compare like to like, there remains an equally compelling reason to align *bechor* with *chatat* and *asham*: not only are all three given as gifts to the *kohen*, but there is an additional structural parallel — *chatat* and *asham* cannot be brought as a voluntary offering (*neder* or *nedava*); they arise from obligation. Similarly, *bechor* is not voluntary — the animal is automatically consecrated as *peter rechem*, and there is no act of will that creates it. Therefore, by this logic, *bechor* should be treated like *chatat* and *asham* and eaten for only one day and one night. The debate at this point reached an impasse, with two equally balanced analogical arguments on either side.

## Akiva-Yishmael Debate

### Akiva Explicit Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָפַץ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וְנִסְתַּלֵּק רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לָּךְ״ – הִקִּישָׁן הַכָּתוּב לְחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק שֶׁל שְׁלָמִים; מָה שְׁלָמִים נֶאֱכָלִין לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד, אַף בְּכוֹר נֶאֱכָל לִשְׁנֵי יָמִים וְלַיְלָה אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva jumped** into the discussion, **and Rabbi Tarfon left.** Rabbi Akiva **said to** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: **But** the verse **states: "And their flesh shall be yours,** as the breast of waving and as the right thigh" (Numbers 18:18). **The verse juxtaposes** firstborn offerings **with a breast and thigh of peace offerings. Just as peace offerings are eaten for two days and one night, so too, a firstborn** offering is **eaten for two days and one night.**

At this point Rabbi Akiva entered the discussion — and Rabbi Tarfon stepped aside. Rabbi Akiva argued that the dispute need not rest on inferential analogy at all, because the *Torah* itself provides an explicit textual link. The verse *uvesaram yihye lach* directly compares the *bechor* to the *chazeh* and *shok* — and those portions belong specifically to the *shelamim*. Since the text has already performed the comparison for us, *bechor* is equated to *shelamim*, and the two-day eating period follows.

### Yose Toda Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: הִיקַּשְׁתּוֹ לְחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק שֶׁל שְׁלָמִים, וַאֲנִי מַקִּישׁוֹ לְחָזֶה וָשׁוֹק שֶׁל תּוֹדָה – מָה תּוֹדָה נֶאֱכֶלֶת לְיוֹם וְלַיְלָה, אַף בְּכוֹר נֶאֱכָל לְיוֹם וְלַיְלָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **said to** Rabbi Akiva: **You juxtaposed it with a breast and thigh of peace offerings. But** since the Torah does not specify with which offering the firstborn is juxtaposed, **I juxtapose it with a breast and thigh of a thanks offering,** which are also given to a priest. **Just as a thanks offering is eaten for** one **day and** one **night, so too, a firstborn** offering should be **eaten for** one **day and** one **night.**

Rabbi Yose HaGalili, however, refused to concede. Yes, he agreed, the verse links the *bechor* to the *chazeh* and *shok* — but who says it is the *chazeh* and *shok* of a *shelamim* specifically? Perhaps it is the *chazeh* and *shok* of a *toda* (thanksgiving offering). And since a *toda* is eaten for only one day and one night, the *bechor* would correspondingly be limited to that shorter period as well.

### Akiva Lach Yihye

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לָּךְ״ – שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לְךָ יִהְיֶה״, וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לְךָ יִהְיֶה״? הוֹסִיף הַכָּתוּב הֲוָיָה אַחֶרֶת בַּבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: I concede that it is possible to juxtapose a firstborn offering with a thanks offering, **but** the verse **states: "And their flesh shall be yours,** as the breast of waving and as the right thigh, it shall be yours" (Numbers 18:18). **Since** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** a second time: **"It shall be yours," what** is the meaning when **the verse states** a second time: **"It shall be yours"? The verse adds another** day on which it is permitted **for a firstborn** offering to **be** eaten, i.e., a second day.

Rabbi Akiva offered a response: the verse begins *uvesaram yihye lach* and then concludes with *lach yihye* — the phrase "it shall be yours" appears twice. This repetition is superfluous if it merely restates the same point. The extra *lach yihye* at the end is therefore coming to teach something additional: the *bechor* is granted to the *kohanim* in an even more expansive way — *hosif hakatuv havaya acheret b'bechor* — pointing to the longer eating period of *shelamim* rather than the shorter period of *toda*.

### Yishmael Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁנֶּאְמְרוּ דְּבָרִים לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אָמַר לָהֶן: צְאוּ וְאִמְרוּ לוֹ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: טָעִיתָה. תּוֹדָה מֵהֵיכָן לָמְדָה – מִשְּׁלָמִים; [וְכִי] דָּבָר הַלָּמֵד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד בְּהֶיקֵּשׁ?! הָא אֵין עָלֶיךָ לִידּוֹן בְּלָשׁוֹן אַחֲרוֹן, אֶלָּא בְּלָשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **And when these matters were said before Rabbi Yishmael, he said to** his students: **Go out and tell Rabbi Akiva: You were mistaken** when you conceded to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili that the allotted time for eating a firstborn offering could have been derived from the allotted time for eating a thanks offering. Why? **From where is** it **derived** that the breast and thigh of **a thanks offering** are eaten by priests? It is derived **from** a juxtaposition with **a peace offering.** Nowhere is it stated explicitly that the breast and thigh of a thanks offering are eaten only by priests. **And** in the realm of consecrated matters, **can a matter derived via a juxtaposition then teach** its *halakha* **via a juxtaposition?** The Gemara (49b) demonstrated that it cannot. **Consequently, you should not infer from the latter formulation** of the verse, from the words "It shall be yours," **but** rather, **from the first formulation** of the verse, as you claimed that the Torah juxtaposes a firstborn offering with the breast and thigh of a peace offering.

When this entire exchange was reported to Rabbi Yishmael, he responded sharply: *ta'ita* — Rabbi Akiva had erred by conceding the initial point. The concession was that, absent the extra *lach yihye*, one might have thought the verse was linking *bechor* to the *chazeh* and *shok* of a *toda*. But this concession was fundamentally mistaken: from where do we know that a *toda* even *has* a *chazeh* and a *shok*? The *toda* is a form of *shelamim*, and it is only from the *shelamim* that we know the *toda* has these portions at all. The *toda*'s *chazeh* and *shok* are themselves derived through a *hekesh* connecting *toda* to *shelamim*.

### Heikesh Double-Apply

This is the principle of *davar halamad b'hekesh ein chozer u'melamed b'hekesh*: something that has been learned through *hekesh* cannot in turn go and teach through another *hekesh*. Since the existence of a *chazeh* and *shok* by a *toda* is itself known only through its connection to *shelamim*, one cannot use the *toda*'s *chazeh* and *shok* as the referent of the link in the *bechor* verse. The verse linking *bechor* to *chazeh* and *shok* must therefore be pointing to the *chazeh* and *shok* of *shelamim* directly — since only there does the rule exist independently. Rabbi Akiva should have maintained his original position from the outset, and the *lach yihye* is not needed for this purpose.

## Lach Yihye Mum

### Yishmael Mum Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, הַאי ״לְךָ יִהְיֶה״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? לִימֵּד עַל בְּכוֹר בַּעַל מוּם שֶׁהוּא מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֵן – שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what does Rabbi Yishmael do with this** extra phrase: **"It shall be yours"?** This **teaches about a blemished firstborn** offering, **that it is** also given as **a gift to the priest, as we have not found** it written explicitly anywhere **in the entire Torah** what should be done with a blemished firstborn.

The Gemara now asks: since Rabbi Yishmael does not need *lach yihye* to establish the two-day eating period — that follows directly from the unambiguous *hekesh* to *shelamim* — what does Rabbi Yishmael do with the extra phrase? The answer is that *lach yihye* teaches an entirely different *halacha*: even a *bechor ba'al mum* — a firstborn animal with a physical blemish — is given to the *kohen*, and the *kohen* eats it without bringing it as a *korban*. This is an unprecedented rule (*shelo matzinu bechol haTorah*): Rashi understands this to mean it is not stated explicitly anywhere else in the *Torah* that a blemished animal goes to the *kohen*; Tosafot, cited elsewhere, interpret it more broadly to mean there is nothing else like it in the entire *Torah* — an animal that is not redeemed, not brought as a sacrifice, but simply eaten with its *mum* intact.

### Akiva Besaram

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא יָלֵיף לֵיהּ מִ״בְּשָׂרָם״ – חַד תָּם וְחַד בַּעַל מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** from where does **Rabbi Akiva** derive that a blemished firstborn offering is also given as a gift to the priest? **He derives it from** the term **"their flesh,"** which is written in the plural. This teaches that **both an unblemished** firstborn offering **and a blemished** firstborn offering are given as gifts to the priest.

The Gemara asks: and where does Rabbi Akiva — who uses *lach yihye* to establish the two-day period — derive this rule about the *bechor ba'al mum*? Rabbi Akiva derives it from the plural form *besaram*, "their flesh." The word *besaram* (rather than *besaro*, "its flesh") indicates a plurality of cases: one for the *tam* (unblemished animal) and one for the *ba'al mum*.

### Yishmael Besaram

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, מִ״בְּשָׂרָם״ דְּהָנָךְ בְּכוֹרוֹת קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yishmael** understand the plural term? The Gemara answers that he understands it to be **saying** that the priest receives gifts **from the flesh of** all **these firstborn** offerings mentioned in the verse, i.e., the firstborn of cattle, sheep, and goats.

Rabbi Yishmael, by contrast, reads *besaram* as referring not to two categories of *bechor* (blemished and unblemished) but to the multiple species of *bechor* that have been mentioned — *shor*, *ez*, *kesev*, and so forth. For him, *besaram* simply aggregates the various animal types and does not extend to teach about the *ba'al mum*. That teaching comes instead from *lach yihye*, as stated above.

## Himenu v'Davar Acher

### Akiva Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? מָר סָבַר: הֵימֶנּוּ וְדָבָר אַחֵר – הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ; וּמָר סָבַר: לָא הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael both agree that a matter derived via a juxtaposition cannot then teach its *halakha* via a juxtaposition, **with regard to what** principle **do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: One **Sage,** Rabbi Yishmael, **holds** that a *halakha* derived both **from that** juxtaposition **and** from **another** principle **is** considered **a juxtaposition.** In this case, the specific detail that the breast and thigh of a thanks offering are given to a priest is derived from a juxtaposition with peace offerings. The length of time in which it may be eaten, one day and one night, is written explicitly. Since it is nevertheless considered a juxtaposition, it cannot be used to teach the *halakha* with regard to a firstborn offering. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that such a case **is not** considered to be **a juxtaposition,** and therefore it can be used to teach another *halakha* via a juxtaposition.

The Gemara now turns to the underlying disagreement between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael — specifically, why Rabbi Akiva initially accepted Rabbi Yose HaGalili's challenge about learning from the *toda*'s *chazeh* and *shok*, when in principle this should be ruled out by *davar halamad b'hekesh ein chozer u'melamed b'hekesh*. The answer is that Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael dispute a more nuanced application of this principle. The principle states that something learned through a *hekesh* cannot in turn be used to teach through another *hekesh*. But what if only *part* of the relevant law was learned through *hekesh*, while another part was already present in the intermediate category independently? In that scenario — referred to as *himenu v'davar acher* — Rabbi Akiva holds that the restriction does not apply. The reasoning can be modeled as a three-step chain: law X is introduced in category A; through a *hekesh*, A teaches B, so X now applies to B; and then, from B to C through another *hekesh*, one wishes to extend X to C. The concern is precisely this double-step. But if category B already contained part of X independently — apart from anything derived from A — then, according to Rabbi Akiva, linking B to C carries with it the part that was already in B, and the derived portion can travel along. The rule against double *hekesh* does not block this.

Applied to our case: the law we wish to derive for the *bechor* from the *toda* is that it is eaten for one day and one night. The one-day rule is explicitly stated by a *toda* in the *Torah* — that is the "part already present in B." What was learned through *hekesh* from *shelamim* to *toda* is only the existence of a *chazeh* and a *shok* by the *toda*. So when the *bechor* verse links to *chazeh* and *shok*, and one argues it refers to the *toda*'s *chazeh* and *shok*, what we would be deriving is the one-day rule — but that was already present in the *toda* independently. This is why Rabbi Akiva thought the argument from *toda* was legitimate, and why he felt compelled to rebut it using the double *lach yihye*.

### Yishmael View

Rabbi Yishmael disagrees: even in a case of *himenu v'davar acher* — even when part of the relevant law already exists in the intermediate category — the principle of *davar halamad b'hekesh ein chozer u'melamed b'hekesh* still applies in full. The reason is that nothing can actually happen through the link from B to C unless the *hekesh*-derived element (the existence of *chazeh* and *shok* in the *toda*) is operative. The fact that the one-day rule is independently present in *toda* is beside the point: the vehicle of transmission — the *chazeh* and *shok* — only exists in *toda* because of the *shelamim* connection. Therefore the double *hekesh* remains blocked, and Rabbi Akiva's concession was unwarranted.

### Pesach Kapparah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכֵן יַעֲשֶׂה לְאֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״ – כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמַּזֶּה לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים אַחַת לְמַעְלָה וְשֶׁבַע לְמַטָּה מִדַּם הַפָּר, כָּךְ מַזֶּה בַּהֵיכָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who says** that a matter derived via a juxtaposition and another principle **is not a juxtaposition, this is** the reason **that it is written** with regard to the rites performed by the High Priest on Yom Kippur: **"And so shall he do for the Tent of Meeting** that dwells with them in the midst of their impurity" (Leviticus 16:16). The High Priest sprinkles the blood of two offerings, the bull and the goat. He sprinkles their blood between the staves of the Ark and on the Curtain separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies. In each location he sprinkles their blood once above and seven times below. The verse does not write all of the details of these sprinklings with regard to each offering, and the details of each are derived from what is written explicitly in the other, as follows: **Just as** the High Priest **sprinkles in the innermost sanctum,** between the staves of the Ark, **once above and seven** times **below, with the blood of the bull, so he sprinkles** on the Curtain **in the Sanctuary.**

The Gemara now brings a different case to test these positions. On Yom Kippur, the *kohen gadol* sprinkles blood in the *Kodesh Kodashim* — once above and seven times below — from both the *par* (the ox) and the *se'ir* (the goat). The *Torah* states explicitly by the *par* that there are seven sprinklings below but does not specify how many above; by the *se'ir*, the word *oto* ("it") implies one sprinkling above, but the seven below are not stated. Since the verse links the two — *ve'asitem ka'asher asa ledam hapar* — each is derived from the other, giving both animals one above and seven below.

The question then becomes: how many sprinklings are done in the *heichal* (outside the *Kodesh Kodashim*), on the *parochet*? The verse says *vechen ya'aseh le'ohel mo'ed* — "so shall he do for the Tent of Meeting" — indicating that the same pattern of one above and seven below applies there as well, for both the *par* and the *se'ir*. But the "one above" for the *par* was only learned from the *se'ir* via *hekesh*. Extending that knowledge to the *heichal* appears to be a second application of that *hekesh*-derived rule — a double step that would be problematic for Rabbi Yishmael.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: חוּץ מִפְּנִים בְּחַד זִימְנָא גְּמִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And if you wish, say** there is a different resolution: The sprinkling **outside is derived from** the sprinkling **inside, all at once.** There are not two comparisons here, one derived from the other, but rather a single, complex juxtaposition, from which all of the relevant *halakhot* are derived.

The Gemara offers two resolutions for Rabbi Yishmael's position. First: *mekomos hu digemiri mehadadi* — the two *hekesh* applications here are not of the same type. The first *hekesh* links the *par* to the *se'ir*; the second links the *Kodesh Kodashim* to the *heichal*. These are distinct comparisons — one between animals, the other between locations — and that distinction means it does not constitute a problematic double-chain.

Second resolution, found at the top of 57b: *chutz mi'pnim b'chad zimna gamir* — the laws for the interior and the exterior are learned together at the same time, not sequentially. Rashi explains: the *par* already has seven sprinklings on the inside independently. The link from inside to outside carries the seven from the *par* on purely direct grounds — no *hekesh* is needed. Once that direct link is operative, one can piggyback and include the "one above" that was learned through the *par*-*se'ir* *hekesh*. This is crucially different from the *toda* case. In the *toda* case, the entire vehicle of transmission — the existence of *chazeh* and *shok* in the *toda* — depends on the *hekesh* from *shelamim*. Nothing moves without it. Here, by contrast, the seven-sprinkling rule travels on its own independent basis from inside to outside; the *hekesh*-derived element (the one above) merely rides along. For Rabbi Yishmael, this distinction is decisive: when there is a genuine independent anchor in the target category, the derived element may accompany it. It is only when *nothing at all* can move without first invoking the *hekesh*-derived component that the principle blocks the chain entirely.

## Shtei HaLechem

### Lechem Heikesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״מִמּוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם תָּבִיאוּ לֶחֶם תְּנוּפָה״ – שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תָּבִיאוּ״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״תָּבִיאוּ״? כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה מֵבִיא מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר הֲרֵי הוּא כָּזֶה; מַה לְּהַלָּן עִשָּׂרוֹן לְחַלָּה, אַף כָּאן עִשָּׂרוֹן לְחַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Granted, according to the one who says** that a matter derived via a juxtaposition and another principle **is not a juxtaposition, this is** the reason **that it is written** with regard to the offering brought on *Shavuot*: **"You shall bring out of your dwellings two wave loaves** of two-tenths of an ephah; they shall be of fine flour" (Leviticus 23:17). **Since** apparently there is **no** need for **the verse to state: "You shall bring,"** as it already states: "And you shall present a new meal offering to the Lord" (Leviticus 23:16), **why** must **the verse state: "You shall bring"?** It states this to teach that **anything that you bring from another place** that is similar to this offering, i.e., the leavened bread that accompanies a thanks offering, **shall be** prepared **like this. Just as there,** with regard to the two loaves, they must be prepared from **a tenth** of an ephah of flour **for** each **loaf, so too here,** each of the ten loaves of leavened bread that accompany a thanks offering must be prepared from **a tenth** of an ephah of flour **for** each **loaf.**

The Gemara introduces another test case — this time regarding the *shtei halechem* brought on Shavuot. The verse states: *mi'moshvoteichem tavi'u lechem tenufa* — "from your dwellings you shall bring a wave-bread offering." By the *shtei halechem*, the *Torah* specifies *shnei esronim* — two *esronim* of flour for the two loaves, meaning one *issaron* per loaf. We now wish to derive that the ten *chametz* loaves of a *toda* also require one *issaron* per loaf — that is, ten *esronim* total for the *chametz* portion of the *toda*. This derivation is performed by linking the *shtei halechem* (category A) to the *toda*'s *chametz* loaves (category B), establishing one *issaron* per *chametz* loaf wherever *chametz* is brought.

### Matza Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי – מָה לְהַלָּן שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנִים, אַף כָּאן שְׁנֵי עֶשְׂרוֹנִים?! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״תִּהְיֶינָה״. וְלָמַדְנוּ עֲשָׂרָה לְחָמֵץ; עֲשָׂרָה לְמַצָּה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַל חַלֹּת לֶחֶם חָמֵץ״ – נֶגֶד חָמֵץ הָבֵא מַצָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **If** they are compared, why not say that **just as there,** with regard to the two loaves, the total amount of flour is **two-tenths** of an ephah, **so too here,** with regard to the ten loaves that accompany a thanks offering, the total amount of flour for all ten should be **two-tenths** of an ephah? To counter this comparison, **the verse states: "They shall be,"** to serve as a restriction and teach that only these two loaves amount to a total of two-tenths of an ephah, but the bread accompanying the thanks offering does not amount to two-tenths of an ephah. The Gemara continues: The ten loaves accompanying a thanks offering must each be a tenth of an ephah, and this has been derived from two sources: A juxtaposition with the offering of *Shavuot* and another principle, which is that which the verse states concerning the thanks offering, that there must be ten leavened loaves. **And we have learned** that **ten**-tenths of an ephah is required **for** the ten loaves of **leavened bread. From where** is it derived that **ten**-tenths of an ephah are required **for** the ***matza*** which also accompanies a thanks offering? After stating that thirty *matzot* accompany a thanks offering, **the verse states: "With cakes of leavened bread"** (Leviticus 7:13), to juxtapose the *matza* with the leavened bread. This teaches that one must **bring *matza*** of an amount **corresponding to** the **leavened bread.** In any event, this derivation can be employed according to the one who says that a matter derived via a juxtaposition and another principle is not a juxtaposition.

But a *toda* also includes thirty *matzah* loaves. How do we know that the total flour for those thirty *matzah* loaves also equals ten *esronim*? The verse *al challat lechem chametz* teaches that the *matzah* corresponds proportionally to the *chametz* — *neged chametz havi matzah* — so the total weight of the *matzah* equals the total weight of the *chametz*, even though the *matzah* loaves are individually smaller (each being one-third of an *issaron*). The derivation thus runs: from the *shtei halechem* (A) to the *toda chametz* (B), one learns one *issaron* per loaf; then from the *toda chametz* (B) to the *toda matzah* (C), one learns ten *esronim* total. This is a two-step chain — and the question is whether it constitutes a prohibited *davar halamad b'hekesh chozer u'melamed b'hekesh*.

For Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *himenu v'davar acher* does not constitute a prohibited double-*hekesh*, this is straightforward: the number ten was already present in B (there were always ten *chametz* loaves), and only the *issaron*-per-loaf rule was learned through *hekesh*. Applying the total of ten *esronim* from *chametz* to *matzah* is anchored in that pre-existing element. But for Rabbi Yishmael, who prohibits even *himenu v'davar acher*, a problem remains — and the Gemara must explain how even his position permits this two-step learning.

### Taviu Direct Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא הָוֵי הֶיקֵּשׁ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? ״תָּבִיאוּ״ – יַתִּירָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to the one who says** that a matter derived via a juxtaposition and another principle **is a juxtaposition,** and therefore it cannot then teach its *halakha* via another juxtaposition, **what is there to say?** How can the measure of flour for the *matza* be derived from the measure of flour for the leavened bread? The Gemara answers: The first *halakha*, connecting the two loaves of *Shavuot* to the bread accompanying a thanks offering, is not derived via a juxtaposition, but rather via a superfluous word. The term **"You shall bring,"** written with regard to the two loaves, **is extra,** and is therefore considered as if it were written explicitly with regard to the leavened bread accompanying the thanks offering. Therefore, it is possible to derive the *halakha* concerning matza from the *halakha* concerning leavened bread via a juxtaposition.

The Gemara resolves this for Rabbi Yishmael's position: *taviu yeseira hu* — the word *tavi'u* in the verse is redundant. The verse did not need to say *tavi'u*; the text already established the *shtei halechem*. The extra *tavi'u* therefore functions not as a *hekesh* but as an independent, direct rule: whenever you bring *chametz* as a waved offering, one *issaron* per loaf applies — regardless of which sacrifice is involved. Because this rule is stated directly by the verse rather than derived through analogy, it is not subject to the restriction on double *hekesh*. The law applies to the *toda chametz* on its own textual authority, and from there the *matzah* derivation proceeds without any forbidden chaining.

## Pesach Chatzot

### Elazar ben Azarya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפֶּסַח אֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר [בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה] אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן ״בַּלַּיְלָה [הַזֶּה]״, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״וְעָבַרְתִּי בְאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״; מַה לְהַלָּן עַד חֲצוֹת, אַף כָּאן עַד חֲצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **The Paschal offering is eaten only** at night and it is eaten only until midnight. The Gemara asks: **Who is the *tanna*** who taught this mishna? **Rav Yosef said** that **it is Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And they shall eat of the flesh on that night" (Exodus 12:8). **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says: It is stated here: "On that night,"** without stating when the night ends. **And it is stated there,** with regard to the plague that afflicted the firstborn Egyptians: **"And I will pass through the land of Egypt on that night** and I will strike every firstborn in the land of Egypt" (Exodus 12:12). With regard to the death of the firstborns the Torah states: "So said the Lord: At about midnight, I will go out into the midst of Egypt and every firstborn in Egypt shall die" (Exodus 11:4–5). Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya continues: **Just as** in the verse **there,** the death of the firstborns occurred **until midnight,** as stated explicitly in the verse, **so too,** in the verse **here,** the mitzva to eat the Paschal offering continues **until midnight** but not beyond.

The discussion now turns to the final clause of the *mishnah*: the *pesach* is eaten only until *chatzot*. The Gemara identifies this position as that of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya — a figure familiar from the first *mishnah* of *Berachot*, where the same authority rules that the *afikoman* must be eaten before *chatzot* on *Pesach* night. The *baraita* records his argument: the verse states *balayla hazeh* regarding the eating of the *pesach*, and separately, God says *va'e'evor b'eretz Mitzrayim balayla hazeh* regarding the plague of the firstborn. Through a *gezerah shavah* on the phrase *balayla hazeh*, we link the eating of the *pesach* to the moment of *makat bechorot*, which occurred at *chatzot*. Just as the plague struck at midnight, so too the eating of the *pesach* is limited to until *chatzot*.

### Akiva Night Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״וַאֲכַלְתֶּם אוֹתוֹ בְּחִפָּזוֹן״ – עַד שְׁעַת חִפָּזוֹן! אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה״? שֶׁיָּכוֹל יְהֵא כְּכׇל הַקֳּדָשִׁים הַנֶּאֱכָלִים בַּיּוֹם; תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּלַּיְלָה״ – בַּלַּיְלָה יְהֵא נֶאֱכָל, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said to** Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: **But isn't it** already **stated:** "And so you shall eat it, with your loins girded, your shoes on your feet, your staffs in your hands, **and you will eat it in haste,** for it is the Paschal offering for the Lord" (Exodus 12:11)? This verse indicates that the Paschal offering may be eaten **until the time of haste,** i.e., until dawn, as the Jewish people left Egypt hastily the next morning. The Gemara asks: **If so, why** must **the verse state: "On that night,"** with regard to eating the Paschal offering? The Gemara explains: This phrase is necessary, **as** one **might** have thought that the Paschal offering is **like all the** other **offerings** in **that it should be eaten during the day,** on the day it is sacrificed. To counter this reasoning, **the verse states: "On** that **night,"** to emphasize that this particular offering **shall be eaten at night, but it shall not be eaten during the day.**

Rabbi Akiva disagrees. He cites the verse *va'achaltem oto bechipazon* — "you shall eat it in haste." The *chipazon* — the hurrying and rushing — refers to the actual departure from Egypt, which occurred in the morning. Therefore the permitted eating window extends throughout the entire night, until the time of actual departure. The *pesach* is eaten the whole night.

Given his position, Rabbi Akiva must explain what the phrase *balayla hazeh* comes to teach. He argues: one might have thought that just like other *kodashim*, which are brought and may begin to be consumed during the day, the *pesach* should similarly be eaten during the day of the fourteenth. The verse therefore states *balayla* — no, the *pesach* is eaten specifically at night, not during the preceding day. The phrase *balayla hazeh* is not connecting the *pesach* to *makat bechorot* to establish a *chatzot* deadline; rather, it is simply excluding daytime eating.

### Abaye Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וּמִמַּאי דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה, וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא? דִּלְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן, וּלְהַרְחִיק מִן הָעֲבֵירָה! אִם כֵּן, מַאי ״אֶלָּא עַד חֲצוֹת״? אֶלָּא כִּי הָתָם – מָה הָתָם דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אַף כָּאן נָמֵי דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **And from where** do you know **that** the mishna represents the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, and** is stating that the Paschal offering must be eaten by midnight **by Torah law? Perhaps** the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, and is stating that the Paschal offering must be eaten by midnight **by rabbinic law, and** the reason for the rabbinic decree was **to distance** one **from a sin.** Rav Yosef replied: **If so, what** does the mishna mean when it says: It is eaten **only until midnight?** With regard to the other offerings the mishna teaches: Until midnight, without specifying: Only. **Rather,** one must say that it is **like there,** with regard to the other *halakhot* of the Paschal offering stated in the mishna. **Just as there,** the requirements are **by Torah law, so too here,** with regard to the final time for eating the meat, the requirement is **also by Torah law.**

Abaye raises a pointed question directed at Rav Yosef's attribution of the *mishnah* to Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya. Why must we say the *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya's position — namely that the *chatzot* limit is *de'oraita*? Perhaps the *mishnah* accords with Rabbi Akiva's ruling that the *pesach* is permitted all night *de'oraita*, and the *chatzot* deadline stated in the *mishnah* is merely *derabbanan* — a rabbinic precaution to distance a person from potential transgression. This is precisely the principle stated in *Masechet Berachot*: even for *kodashim* that are permitted all night, the Sages decreed that they be completed by *chatzot* as a protective measure.

This is, as the lecturer acknowledges after a moment's reconsideration, actually a strong question — because in fact the *mishnah* in the earlier part of the *perek* does say *ad chatzot* regarding other *kodashim* such as *zivchei shalmei tzibur*, *ashamot*, *toda*, and *eil nazir*, which are technically eaten all night but are described with the *chatzot* deadline reflecting the rabbinic precaution. So when the *mishnah* says *chatzot* by *pesach*, why should that be treated differently — why not simply say it too is *derabbanan*?

### Deoraita Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמִּשְׁנָה קָאָמְרָה: ״וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא עַד חֲצוֹת״ – אֶלָּא לִמְנוּיָו, וְאֵינוֹ נֶאֱכָל אֶלָּא צָלִי. בְּאִיזֶה קָרָא? דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְשָׂרָם יִהְיֶה לָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara's answer is precise and telling. The *mishnah*'s phrasing regarding *pesach* is *ve'eino ne'echal ella ad chatzot* — "and it is not eaten except until *chatzot*." The critical word is *ella* — the formulation is one of absolute exclusion. Wherever else the *mishnah* says *ad chatzot*, it says simply *ne'echal ad chatzot*; here it says *eino ne'echal ella ad chatzot*. The *ella* construction indicates that *chatzot* is an absolute and definitive boundary — the language of something that simply cannot occur, not a rabbinic guideline reflecting an ideal cutoff. This phrasing marks the limit as *de'oraita*, not merely a precaution enacted *derabbanan*. The *mishnah* therefore follows the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, who holds that the *pesach* is forbidden *mi'de'oraita* after *chatzot*.
