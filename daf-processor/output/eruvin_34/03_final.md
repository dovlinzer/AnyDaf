# Eruvin 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv Accessibility

### Tosafos on Eruv Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? נֵימָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי אַמְטוֹיֵי מָצֵי מַמְטֵי לֵיהּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אַמְטְיֵיהּ — כְּמַאן דְּאַמְטְיֵיהּ דָּמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **Why** must one actually bring the *eiruv* to the place where he wishes to establish his residence? **Let us say: Since if he wished to bring** the *eiruv* there **he could bring it, even though he did not bring it, it is considered as though he did bring it** there.

The discussion on *daf* 34 opens by reviewing the *Gemara*'s extended treatment of whether an *eruv* must be physically and halachically accessible at the moment of *bein hashmashot* in order to be valid. The *Gemara* had been exploring a series of theoretical scenarios — what if the *eruv* is in a *reshut hayachid* and the person is in a *reshut harabim*? According to Rebbe, rabbinic restrictions (*derabbanan*) are not enforced during *bein hashmashot*, so even a configuration that would otherwise be problematic on a rabbinic level is still considered accessible. The *Gemara* had also discussed the possibility of staged transfers — taking the *eruv* from a *reshut hayachid* through a *karmelit* and then into a *reshut harabim* — and the case of a basket in a *reshut hayachid* that could be tipped so that its contents descend into a *karmelit*, thereby rendering the *eruv* accessible. After following this chain of what-ifs to its logical extreme, the *Gemara* threw up its hands: if one can always conjure a theoretical reconfiguration, why must one ever physically place the *eruv* at its intended location at all?

*Tosafot* raises a pointed objection to Rashi's reading of this passage. According to Rashi, the *Gemara*'s question applies even to a standard Friday *eruv* for *Shabbos*. *Tosafot* rejects this: in the ordinary case of an *eruv* placed on *Erev Shabbos*, the entire question of accessibility is measured at *bein hashmashot*, and at that moment the person is two thousand *amot* outside the city while the *eruv* is in the house. There is simply no way to bring it — it would take longer than *bein hashmashot* lasts, and the act of carrying would in any case constitute a biblical prohibition, not merely a rabbinic one. Rebbe's dispensation for *derabbanan* violations during *bein hashmashot* does not help when the problem is a *d'oraisha*. So *Tosafot* argues that the *Gemara*'s question never applied to a standard *Shabbos eruv* in the first place. The question pertains exclusively to a *Yom Tov eruv*: if one wishes to establish an *eruv* for a weekday *Yom Tov*, why does the *eruv* need to be physically placed anywhere? Since carrying is permitted on *Yom Tov*, one could always, even during *bein hashmashot Yom Tov*, have brought the *eruv* from wherever it was to wherever it needed to be.

### Yom Tov After Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם יוֹם טוֹב שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת אַחַר שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said:** The fact that one must bring his *eiruv* the day before to the spot that he wishes to establish as his place of residence, and the potential to bring it there does not suffice, is **a decree due to a Festival that occurs after Shabbat.**

This reframing renders the *Gemara*'s question and answer considerably more intelligible. The *Gemara*'s answer, attributed to Rabbi Zeira, is that the *eruv* must in fact be physically placed in its proper location — but the only reason this is ever required is for the case of *Yom Tov* that falls on Sunday, immediately following *Shabbos*. In that scenario, even though carrying would otherwise be permitted on *Yom Tov*, the *eruv* could not have been brought to its location during *Shabbos*, and by the time *Yom Tov* begins it is too late. To ensure the *eruv* is in place for that one case, the requirement to physically lay the *eruv* was established across the board.

The *Gemara* thus arrives at a somewhat remarkable conclusion: the general requirement to actually place one's *eruv* in the designated location is anchored in a single edge case — a *Yom Tov* that follows immediately after *Shabbos*. The *Gemara* does not shy away from this implication; rather, it articulates it directly.

## Wall Height & Access

### Eruv on Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: נִתְכַּוֵּון לִשְׁבּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהִנִּיחַ עֵירוּבוֹ בַּכּוֹתֶל, לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. נִתְכַּוֵּון לִשְׁבּוֹת בְּרֹאשׁ הַשּׁוֹבָךְ אוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּגְדָּל, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** Rav bar Shabba **raised** another **objection** from a different *baraita*: With regard to one who **intended to establish his Shabbat residence in the public domain and placed his *eiruv* in a wall** that is more than four cubits away from that location; if he placed the *eiruv* **below** a height of **ten handbreadths** above the ground, **his *eiruv* is a** valid ***eiruv*;** but if he placed it **above ten handbreadths, his *eiruv* is not a** valid ***eiruv*** because he is in a public domain while his *eiruv* is in a private domain. If **one intended to establish his Shabbat residence on top of a dovecote or on top of** a large **cupboard,** if he placed the *eiruv* in the dovecote or cupboard **above ten handbreadths** from the ground, **his *eiruv* is a** valid ***eiruv*** because both he and his *eiruv* are in a private domain; but if he placed it **below ten handbreadths,** the area in which he placed his *eiruv* is considered a *karmelit*, and **his *eiruv* is not a** valid ***eiruv*** because he cannot transport his *eiruv* from there to his own domain on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* now takes up a series of cases from the *Mishnah* in order to test the theoretical framework that had been established. The first: one wished to begin *Shabbos* in the *reshut harabim* and placed the *eruv* on a wall. If the wall is less than ten *tefachim* high — *lemata me'asara tefachim* — the *eruv* is valid. If the wall is higher than ten *tefachim* — and we also assume four by four *tefachim* wide, making it a *reshut hayachid* — the *eruv* is invalid, because the person is in a *reshut harabim* and the *eruv* is in a *reshut hayachid*.

### Tower/Coop Case

The second case: one wished to begin *Shabbos* on the roof of a chicken coop (*shovach*) or on the roof of a tower (*migdal*). In such a case, the rule is reversed: if the wall alongside it is higher than ten *tefachim*, the *eruv* placed on that wall is valid — both the person and the *eruv* are in a *reshut hayachid*, and the *eruv* is accessible. If the wall is lower than ten *tefachim*, the *eruv* is not valid.

Rashi offers two explanations for why the low-wall configuration fails. The first is that a wall lower than ten *tefachim* that is usable by the public becomes part of the *reshut harabim*, so the *eruv* would be in a *reshut harabim* while the person on the elevated roof is in a *reshut hayachid* — a prohibited configuration. The second explanation, which Rashi also advances, is more conceptual: the airspace of the *reshut harabim* extends up to ten *tefachim*. Anything above that threshold belongs to a different conceptual domain. If the person is above ten *tefachim* and the *eruv* is below ten *tefachim*, they are considered to occupy different zones, and the *eruv* is therefore not accessible. It should be noted that this second explanation does not fit easily with all the *Gemara*'s other discussions, and the *Mishnah* about the pole — to be discussed shortly — appears to contradict it.

### Tipping the Tower

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? הָכִי נָמֵי נֵימָא: הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לִנְטוֹתוֹ וְלַהֲבִיאוֹ לְתוֹךְ עֲשָׂרָה! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הָכָא בְּמִגְדָּל מְסוּמָּר עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Why** should this be so? **Here too, let us say** that his *eiruv* should be valid even if it was placed below ten handbreadths, **since one can tilt** the cupboard **and bring it to within ten** handbreadths from the ground. **Rabbi Yirmeya said: Here, we are dealing with a cupboard that is nailed** to the wall so that it cannot be tilted.

The *Gemara* then asks: given the logic established earlier about tipping the basket, why should the lower-wall case be a problem at all? In theory, could one not simply tip the *migdal* over, bringing the *eruv* below ten *tefachim* and thus into the same zone as the person? The *Gemara* is using this as a *reductio ad absurdum*: the theoretical reconfiguration argument, which was accepted in the basket case, would seem to apply here as well.

### Nailed vs. Tall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּמִגְדָּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְסוּמָּר, וְהָכָא בְּמִגְדָּל אָרוֹךְ עָסְקִינַן, דְּאִי מַמְטֵי לֵיהּ פּוּרְתָּא, אָזֵיל חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even if you say** that it is referring to **a cupboard that is not nailed** to the wall, **here, we are dealing with** a very **tall cupboard,** such **that were one to tilt it a little** in order to bring the top of the cupboard within ten handbreadths from the ground, the top of the cupboard **would project beyond** the **four cubits** that constitute one's Shabbat residence.

Two answers are given. Rabbi Yirmiah says: here we are dealing with a *migdal* that is nailed into the ground — *migdal mesumares* — and therefore physically cannot be tipped. Rava offers a second answer: even if it is not nailed down, we are dealing with a tall *migdal* — *migdal aroch* — such that if one were to tip it even slightly, the *eruv* would wind up outside of four *amot* from where the person intended to begin *Shabbos*. And that creates two problems: one cannot bring the *eruv* from four *amot* away in a *reshut harabim*, and moreover, one's intended location for *shevitah* would itself be displaced, since the tipped configuration would place it four *amot* from the original position.

### Window & Rope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאִיכָּא כַּוְּותָא וּמִתְנָא — לַיְיתֵיהּ בְּכַוְּותָא וּמִתְנָא! דְּלֵית לֵיהּ כַּוְּותָא וּמִתְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What,** exactly, **are the circumstances? If** it is referring to a case **where** the cupboard **has a window, and** one has **a rope** at hand, **let him bring it by means of the window and rope.** In other words, let him lower the rope through the cupboard's window and bring the *eiruv* with it, and he will not have to move the entire cupboard. The Gemara answers: Here we are dealing with a case **where it does not have a window, and** he does not have **a rope** at hand.

The *Gemara* continues pushing on this theme. It asks: what if there is a window and a rope? Could one not thread the rope through the window and pull the *eruv* out that way, making it accessible without tipping? The *Gemara* responds: the case we are discussing is precisely one where there is no window and no rope. But the *Gemara*'s willingness to entertain the rope scenario is itself instructive. *Tosafot* explains that if a rope were already attached to the *eruv*, the object would be considered partially extended toward the person even before it was fully retrieved, so that drawing it in fully would not constitute a complete biblical act of carrying — analogous to the principle of *eged kli* discussed elsewhere in *Shabbos*. The *Gemara* is not dismissing the what-if; it is simply ruling it inapplicable to the case at hand.

The overall conclusion of this entire section is that the principle of theoretical accessibility is real but bounded. Yes, one can invoke reconfiguration — tipping a basket, threading a rope — to establish that an *eruv* is accessible. But those considerations only apply when the reconfiguration is actually feasible: the tower is not nailed down, the reconfiguration does not push one outside of four *amot*, and there is actually a rope in place.

## Well Case

### Well Domains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנוֹ בְּבוֹר אֲפִילּוּ עָמוֹק מֵאָה אַמָּה וְכוּ׳. הַאי בּוֹר דְּקָאֵי הֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּקָאֵי בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — פְּשִׁיטָא! רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד עוֹלָה עַד לָרָקִיעַ, וְכִי הֵיכִי דְּסָלְקָא לְעֵיל, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּנָחֲתָא לְתַחַת. וְאֶלָּא דְּקָאֵי בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **If one placed** the *eiruv* **in a pit, even** if **it is a hundred cubits deep,** his *eiruv* is a valid *eiruv*. The Gemara asks: **This pit, where is it situated? If you say that it is situated in the private domain,** **it is obvious,** for **the private domain ascends to the sky, and just as it ascends upward, so** too, **it descends downward** to the bottom of the pit, even if it is more than ten handbreadths deep. **Rather,** we must say **that** the pit **is situated in the public domain.**

The *Gemara* turns to the *Mishnah*'s case of an *eruv* placed in a well, even one a hundred *amot* deep, and rules it valid. The *Gemara* asks where this well is located, in order to determine what the *chiddush* actually is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּנִתְכַּוֵּון לִשְׁבּוֹת הֵיכָא? אִי לְמַעְלָה — הוּא בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד וְעֵירוּבוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר הוּא. אִי לְמַטָּה, פְּשִׁיטָא — הוּא וְעֵירוּבוֹ בִּמְקוֹם אֶחָד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now clarifies: **Where** did one **intend to establish his Shabbat residence? If** he intended to establish his residence **above** the pit in the public domain, this is a case where **he is in one place and his *eiruv* is in another place,** i.e., in a private domain, and therefore his *eiruv* is not valid. Alternatively, **if** one intended to establish his Shabbat residence **below,** in the pit, **it is** also **obvious,** as **he and his *eiruv* are in one place.**

If the well is in a *reshut hayachid* — that is, surrounded by a private domain — the ruling is obvious. Just as a *reshut hayachid* extends upward to the heavens, it extends downward into the earth. The well is a *reshut hayachid*, and if the person is also in a *reshut hayachid*, the *eruv* is in the same domain and is clearly valid. No novelty there.

If the well is in a *reshut harabim*, then the bottom of the well, by virtue of being ten *tefachim* deep and enclosed, constitutes a *reshut hayachid*. If the person intends to begin *Shabbos* at ground level — above the well in the *reshut harabim* — the *eruv* is in a different domain and is inaccessible. If the person intends to begin *Shabbos* at the bottom of the well, then obviously the person and the *eruv* are in the same place, and again there is no novelty.

### Well in a Karmelit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּקָאֵי בְּכַרְמְלִית וְנִתְכַּוֵּון לִשְׁבּוֹת לְמַעְלָה, וְרַבִּי הִיא דְּאָמַר: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת לֹא גָּזְרוּ עָלָיו בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This ruling **is necessary only** in a case **where** the pit **is situated in a *karmelit*, and he intended to establish his Shabbat residence above** the pit in the *karmelit*. **And** with regard to the question of how this *eiruv* can be valid, as one cannot bring the *eiruv* from the pit to the *karmelit*, the answer is that the mishna was taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said:** With regard to **anything that is** prohibited on Shabbat **due to rabbinic decree [*shevut*], they did not issue the decree** to apply **during twilight.**

The *Gemara* therefore concludes: the *chiddush* arises specifically where the area surrounding the well is a *karmelit*. The bottom of the well is a *reshut hayachid*, and the person intends to begin *Shabbos* at a normal height just above the well, within the *karmelit*. The problem is that retrieving the *eruv* from the *reshut hayachid* while standing in a *karmelit* would involve a rabbinic violation. The *Mishnah*'s ruling that it is valid follows the position of Rebbe — *ve-Rabbi hi* — who holds that rabbinic prohibitions are not enforced during *bein hashmashot*, and therefore the *eruv* is considered accessible even across this domain distinction.

This case rounds out the *Gemara*'s treatment of the *Mishnah* cases from the previous *amud*. The underlying thread is consistent: accessibility is determined halachically, not merely physically. Rebbe's position that *derabbanan* restrictions do not apply during *bein hashmashot* transforms the halachic calculus of what counts as "accessible," with substantial ramifications across all of these cases.

## Pole & Reed

### Pole vs. Reed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נְתָנוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַקָּנֶה אוֹ בְּרֹאשׁ הַקּוּנְדָּס, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא תָּלוּשׁ וְנָעוּץ, אֲפִילּוּ גָּבוֹהַּ מֵאָה אַמָּה — הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one placed** his *eiruv* **on top of a reed or on top of a pole [*kundas*], when** the reed or pole **is detached** from its original place **and stuck** into the ground, **even** if it is **a hundred cubits high, it is a** valid ***eiruv*,** as one can remove the reed or pole from the ground and take his *eiruv*.

The *Gemara* moves to a new *Mishnah*: if one placed the *eruv* on top of a tall pole (*kaneh*) or a tall reed (*kundas*), the ruling depends on whether the object is *talush vena'utz* — cut and then thrust back into the ground — or whether it is still growing from the earth. If it is a detached pole replanted in the ground, the *eruv* is valid even if the pole is a hundred *amot* tall. This case is notable because it appears to contradict Rashi's earlier position that anything above ten *tefachim* is in a different conceptual zone from someone standing in the *reshut harabim* below — yet the *Mishnah* has no difficulty with an *eruv* atop a flagpole of any height. The *Mishnah* thus seems to take for granted that the person could, at least in theory, shake the pole until the *eruv* falls, and that there is no halachic barrier to doing so.

### Detached vs. Attached Reeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רָמֵי לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא לְרָבָא: תָּלוּשׁ וְנָעוּץ — אִין. לֹא תָּלוּשׁ וְנָעוּץ — לָא. מַנִּי? רַבָּנַן הִיא, דְּאָמְרִי: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבוּת גָּזְרוּ עָלָיו בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא רַבִּי! רֵישָׁא רַבִּי וְסֵיפָא רַבָּנַן?!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Adda bar Mattana raised a contradiction before Rava** concerning two tannaitic rulings: The mishna states that if the reed **was detached** from its place of growth **and** then **stuck** into the ground, **yes,** the *eiruv* is valid. That indicates that if it **was not detached and** then **stuck** back into the ground, **no,** the *eiruv* is not valid. In accordance with **whose** opinion is this mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say: Anything that is** prohibited on Shabbat **due to rabbinic decree [*shevut*],** such as using a tree on Shabbat, **they issued the decree** to apply even **during twilight.** Therefore, if the *eiruv* was on top of a reed that was still attached to the ground in its original place of growth, since it is prohibited by rabbinic decree to make use of trees on Shabbat, one cannot remove the *eiruv* from its place at the time that he must establish his Shabbat residence, and therefore his *eiruv* is invalid. But **didn't you say** that **the first clause,** i.e., the previous mishna, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? How can you say that **the first clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and the latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis?**

Rav Adda bar Masna then challenges Rava: the *Mishnah* specifies *talush vena'utz* — meaning if the reed is still attached to the ground, the *eruv* is not valid. Whose position does this reflect? It reflects the *Rabbanan* who disagree with Rebbe and hold that rabbinic restrictions *are* enforced during *bein hashmashot*. Since using something attached to the ground (*mechubar l'karka*) is rabbinically prohibited on *Shabbos*, the *eruv* placed on a growing reed would not be accessible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּבָר רָמֵי לֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרַב חִסְדָּא, וְשַׁנִּי לֵיהּ: רֵישָׁא רַבִּי, וְסֵיפָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Adda: **Rami bar Ḥama already raised this contradiction before Rav Ḥisda, and** Rav Ḥisda **answered him:** Indeed, **the first clause** is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and the latter clause** is in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis.

But this immediately raises a difficulty: the first *Mishnah* in the chapter explicitly discussed placing the *eruv* on a tree — which is also *mechubar l'karka* — and apparently validated it. Can the beginning of the *Mishnah* reflect Rebbe's more lenient position and the end reflect the *Rabbanan*'s stricter one? Rava responds that Rami bar Chama had already posed this exact question to Rav Chisda, and Rav Chisda answered that yes, the beginning of the *Mishnah* follows Rebbe and the end follows the *Rabbanan*. While this answer might seem weak at first glance, it is historically defensible: the *Mishnayos* were collated and redacted by Rebbe from diverse sources, and different sections may well originate from different schools of thought.

### Ravina's Resolution: Snapping Reeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי הִיא, וְסֵיפָא גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִקְטוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** You can, in fact, say that **it is all Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and the latter clause,** i.e., the mishna that insists that the reed be detached and inserted, is not based upon the prohibition of utilizing trees on Shabbat. Rather, in that case there is a unique **decree lest he break** it **off** from the ground if the reed is relatively soft.

Ravina, however, offers a more unified reading: the entire *Mishnah* follows Rebbe — *kula Rabbi* — and yet Rebbe would prohibit placing the *eruv* on a reed. The reason is not simply the rabbinic concern about *mechubar l'karka* that Rebbe otherwise overrides at *bein hashmashot*, but rather a more acute concern: one might come to cut the reed — *shema yiktom*. Since reeds are easily snapped, there is a realistic concern that one will break one off, which would constitute a biblical violation. This concern is severe enough that even Rebbe would not permit it, even though he would permit a comparably minor rabbinic restriction. The tree case and the reed case are therefore distinguishable, and the *Mishnah* is internally consistent on Rebbe's own terms.

### Rav Nachman Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא פּוּלְמוּסָא דַּאֲתָא לִנְהַרְדְּעָא. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב נַחְמָן: פּוּקוּ עֲבִידוּ כְּבוּשֵׁי כַּבְשֵׁי בְּאַגְמָא, וּלְמָחָר נֵיזִיל וְנִיתֵּיב עִלָּוַיְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates **that** a certain **army [*pulmosa*]** once **came to Neharde'a** and took quarters in the study hall, so that there was not enough room for the students. **Rav Naḥman said to** the students: **Go out and create** seats by **compressing** reeds **in the marshes, and tomorrow,** on Shabbat, **we will go and sit on them** and study there.

An incident further illuminates the distinction. When a military detachment was quartered in Nehardea, the students had to vacate the study hall and needed somewhere to gather on *Shabbos*. Rav Nachman instructed them: before *Shabbos*, go out to the marshy field and flatten (*kvushey kavshey*) some of the reeds, so that on *Shabbos* there will be a place to sit and learn. Rami bar Chama — and some attribute the challenge to Rav Ukva bar Abba — challenged Rav Nachman: the *Mishnah* implies that reeds attached to the ground are problematic, so how could one sit on flattened reeds on *Shabbos*, which would be using something *mechubar l'karka*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרַב נַחְמָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר אַבָּא לְרַב נַחְמָן: תָּלוּשׁ וְנָעוּץ אִין, לֹא תָּלוּשׁ וְלֹא נָעוּץ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama raised an objection to Rav Naḥman, and some say** that it was **Rav Ukva bar Abba** who raised the objection **to Rav Naḥman,** from the mishna that states that if the reed **was detached and** then **stuck** into the ground, **yes;** but if it **was not detached and not stuck** into the ground, **no.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם בְּעוּזְרָדִין. וּמְנָא תֵּימְרָא דְּשָׁנֵי לַן בֵּין עוּזְרָדִין לְשֶׁאֵין עוּזְרָדִין — דְּתַנְיָא: הַקָּנִין וְהָאֲטָדִין וְהַהִגִּין — מִין אִילָן הֵן, וְאֵינָן כִּלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַקָּנִים וְהַקִּידָן וְהָאוּרְבָּנִין — מִין יָרָק הֵן, וְהֵן כִּלְאַיִם בַּכֶּרֶם. קַשְׁיָא אַהֲדָדֵי!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: There,** in the mishna, we are dealing with **hard** reeds, which may not be bent and used on Shabbat, unlike soft reeds. He adds: **And from where do you say that we distinguish between hard** reeds **and** reeds **that are not hard? As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Reeds, boxthorn, and thistles are species of trees, and** therefore they **are not** included in the prohibition of **food crops in a vineyard,** which applies only to herbs planted among vines. **And it was taught in another** *baraita*: **Reeds, cassia, and bulrushes are species of herbs, and** therefore **they are** included in the prohibition of **food crops in a vineyard.** These two *baraitot* **contradict one another,** as one states that reeds are trees, while the other says that they are considered herbs.

Rav Nachman replied: *hatam be'ozardin* — that rule applies when the reeds have hardened. It is the hardened reeds, *ozardin*, that are categorized like trees and therefore subject to the rabbinic prohibition of *mechubar l'karka*. Soft, pliable reeds are more like grass or vegetables, and they are not subject to that prohibition. The *Mishnah*'s stringency about reeds applies to hardened reeds only. Moreover, the concern that one will snap off a piece — the concern underlying the prohibition — is more acute with hardened reeds, which snap easily, than with soft reeds, which are difficult to break off cleanly.

### Reeds in Kilayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כָּאן בְּעוּזְרָדִין, כָּאן בְּשֶׁאֵין עוּזְרָדִין. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, conclude from this** that we must distinguish between them as follows: **Here,** in the first *baraita*, it is referring **to hard** reeds, which are like trees; whereas **there,** in the second *baraita*, it is referring **to** reeds **that are not hard.** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **conclude from this** that our resolution of the contradiction is correct.

The *Gemara* then seeks a source for the distinction between hardened and unhardened reeds in terms of categorical classification. It turns to the laws of *kilayim*. One *baraita* states that stalks, thornbushes (*atadim*), and brambles (*higim*) are all *min ilan* — they belong to the tree category — and therefore do not constitute *kilayim* with a vineyard, since trees may be planted alongside vines. A separate *baraita*, however, lists reeds (*kanim*) together with other plants and classifies them as *min yerakin* — the vegetable category — in which case planting them near vines would constitute *kilayim*.

### Soft vs. Hard

The two *baraitot* appear to contradict one another. The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction by applying exactly the distinction Rav Nachman had invoked: hardened reeds (*ozardin*) belong to the tree category and are therefore not *kilayim* with a vineyard; soft reeds belong to the vegetable category and are subject to *kilayim* restrictions when planted near vines. The resolution — *kan be-zardin kan she-eino zardin* — confirms that the formal category of a reed as tree or vegetable depends on its state of hardness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקִידָּה מִין יָרָק הוּא?! וְהָתְנַן: אֵין מַרְכִּיבִין פֵּגָם עַל גַּבֵּי קִידָּה לְבָנָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא יָרָק בְּאִילָן! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: קִידָּה לְחוּד, וְקִידָּה לְבָנָה לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question with regard to the previously cited *baraita*: **Is cassia a type of herb? Didn't we learn** in a mishna: **One may not graft rue to white cassia, as this** involves the grafting of **herbs to a tree?** This proves that white cassia is a tree. **Rav Pappa said:** There is no difficulty, as **cassia is distinct** and is considered a type of herb, **and white cassia is distinct** and is considered a type of tree.

This is a methodologically significant moment in the *sugya*. The concern that motivates the prohibition of *mechubar l'karka* on *Shabbos* is that one might snap off a branch. In principle, one could ask whether the category of "tree" for *Shabbos* purposes should be defined by that motivating reason — whatever is likely to be snapped off — or by a formal, cross-halachic definition. The *Gemara* here takes the formalist approach: it imports the definition of "tree" from *kilayim* and applies it to *Shabbos*. This reflects the broader question of whether halachic categories are local (*din mesuyam*) or universal (*din klali*), a *chakira* that runs throughout the literature. Here the *Gemara* clearly treats the category as universal, drawing the definition from an entirely different area of *halacha*.

The *Gemara* also notes a further refinement regarding *kida*: one *baraita* lists it in the vegetable category, while the *Mishnah* treats *kida levana* (white *kida*) as belonging to the tree category, such that grafting rue — a vegetable — onto it would constitute *kilayim* of vegetable with tree. Rav Papa resolves the apparent contradiction: plain *kida* and white *kida* are distinct varieties, one in each category. The broader point is reinforced — classification as tree or vegetable can vary even within what appears to be a single species, whether by variety or by developmental stage.

## Locked Wardrobe

### Mishna Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נְתָנוֹ בְּמִגְדָּל וְאָבַד הַמַּפְתֵּחַ — הֲרֵי זֶה עֵירוּב. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהַמַּפְתֵּחַ בִּמְקוֹמוֹ — אֵינוֹ עֵירוּב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one put** the *eiruv* **in a cupboard** and locked it, **and the key was lost,** so that he is now unable to open the cupboard and access the *eiruv*, **it is** nonetheless a valid ***eiruv*. Rabbi Eliezer says: If he does not know that the key is in its place, it is not** a valid ***eiruv*.**

The final *Mishnah* of the *daf* addresses a scenario that shifts the question of accessibility from the halachic to the purely practical: one placed the *eruv* in a *migdal* — understood here as a wardrobe or chest — locked it, and then lost the key. The *Tanna Kamma* rules it valid. Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: if one does not know that the key is in its place (*im eino yodea she-ha-mafteiach bimkomo*), the *eruv* is invalid.

### The Question of Physical Accessibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאַמַּאי? הוּא בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד וְעֵירוּבוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And why** should the *eiruv* be valid if the key was lost? **He is in one place and his *eiruv* is in a different place,** since he cannot access the *eiruv*.

The *Tanna Kamma*'s position raises an immediate conceptual question. All of the earlier discussion about accessibility presupposed that halachic barriers are what render an *eruv* inaccessible, while Rebbe's dispensation for *derabbanan* violations at *bein hashmashot* is what restores accessibility. But in this case, there is no halachic barrier at all — the wardrobe is simply locked and the key is gone. It is a purely physical problem. Yet apparently even a physical barrier can be sufficient to render an *eruv* inaccessible. The *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling that it *is* valid seems to suggest that only a halachic barrier matters — that physical inaccessibility does not transform the two locations into separate *makomot* the way a domain boundary does.

### Brick & Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הָכָא בְּמִגְדָּל שֶׁל לְבֵנִים עָסְקִינַן וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: פּוֹחֵת לְכַתְּחִילָּה וְנוֹטֵל. דִּתְנַן: בַּיִת שֶׁמִּילְּאָהוּ פֵּירוֹת, סָתוּם וְנִפְחַת — נוֹטֵל מִמְּקוֹם הַפְּחָת, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: פּוֹחֵת וְנוֹטֵל לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was Rav and Shmuel who both said: Here, we are dealing with a cupboard** made **of bricks, and** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: One may create a breach** in a brick wall on Shabbat ***ab initio*, and take** produce from the other side. **As we learned** in a mishna: If **a house filled with produce** had been **sealed and was** then **breached, one may take out** produce **from the place of the breach. Rabbi Meir** disagrees and **says: One may** even **create a breach** in the wall of the house **and take** produce ***ab initio*.**

The *Gemara* declines to accept this reading and instead offers an explanation: *hacha bemigdal shel leveinim askinan* — the *Mishnah* is speaking of a wardrobe constructed from bricks laid without plaster. And the ruling follows the opinion of Rabbi Meir, who permits dismantling such a structure.

### Rabbi Meir on Bricks

The underlying principle derives from a *Mishnah* in *Beitzah* about a storehouse sealed with bricks that then sustained a breach: Rabbi Meir rules that one may even deliberately dismantle such a wall *lechatchilah* to retrieve the contents inside — *pocheis ve-notel lechatchilah*. Since the bricks are laid without mortar or plaster, there is no binding agent, and Rabbi Meir holds that undoing such a structure does not constitute the prohibited labor of *soser* — demolishing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר אַדָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּאַוֵּירָא דְלִיבְנֵי. הָכָא נָמֵי בַּאֲוֵירָא דְלִיבְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Didn't Rav Naḥman bar Adda say that Shmuel said** that that very mishna cited as a proof is referring **to** a structure built from **layers of bricks** piled one atop the other without cement or mortar between them, in which case making a hole cannot be considered dismantling a bona fide structure? The Gemara answers: **Here too,** we are dealing with a cupboard made from **layers of bricks.**

The application to the wardrobe case is as follows: since the brick construction can be taken apart, the *eruv* is considered physically accessible after all, and the *Tanna Kamma* is therefore justified in ruling it valid. However, Rav Zeira notes that Rabbi Meir's dispensation applies specifically on *Yom Tov* — *be-Yom Tov amru* — but not on *Shabbos*. The difference is likely connected to the *Yom Tov* value of *simchat Yom Tov* and the *Torah*'s special dispensation regarding food preparation on *Yom Tov*. Consequently, the *Mishnah*'s case of the locked wardrobe is specifically a *Yom Tov eruv* scenario: on *Shabbos*, even according to Rabbi Meir, the brick construction could not be dismantled, and the *eruv* would be inaccessible.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּיוֹם טוֹב אָמְרוּ, אֲבָל לֹא בְּשַׁבָּת! הָכָא נָמֵי בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **Didn't Rabbi Zeira say:** The Sages in the aforementioned mishna, who discussed the breaching of a wall, **spoke** only **with regard to a Festival, but not with regard to Shabbat?** Therefore, it cannot be derived from that mishna that it is permitted to breach the cupboard on Shabbat in order to access the food inside. The Gemara answers: **Here too,** the mishna is referring **to** a case where the person needed an *eiruv* for **a Festival** but not for Shabbat.

### Key Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי עֲלַהּ, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם בָּעִיר אָבַד — עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב, וְאִם בַּשָּׂדֶה אָבַד — אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ עֵירוּב. וְאִי בְּיוֹם טוֹב, מָה לִי עִיר מָה לִי שָׂדֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** it is **so** that the mishna is referring only to a Festival, there is a difficulty with **that which was taught about it** in the following *Tosefta*: **Rabbi Eliezer says: If** the key **was lost in a city, his *eiruv* is** a valid ***eiruv*; and if it was lost in a field, his *eiruv* is not** a valid ***eiruv*,** for within a city it is possible to carry the key by way of courtyards that have joined together in an *eiruv* or the like, but in a field it is impossible to carry it, as the field has the status of a *karmelit*. **And if** the mishna is referring **to a Festival, what** is the difference **to me** whether the key was lost in a **city** or a **field?** On a Festival there is no prohibition against carrying from a private to a public domain, and therefore if the key was lost even in a field, the *eiruv* should still be valid.

Once this framework is established, the *Gemara* turns to a *baraita* that elaborates on Rabbi Eliezer's position. Rabbi Eliezer states: if the key was lost in the city (*ba'ir*), the *eruv* is valid; if the key was lost in the field (*basadeh*), the *eruv* is invalid. The criterion Rabbi Eliezer applies is not the mere fact of loss but the practical consequence: if the key were found in the city, one could theoretically carry it through a series of *reshut hayachid* configurations — through walled courtyards, via rooftops — and eventually get it to the *eruv*. If the key is lost in the field, there is no way to get it to the *eruv* even once found, and so the *eruv* cannot be considered accessible.

### Found Key

The *Gemara* notes, however, that this *baraita* cannot be describing the same scenario as the *Mishnah* if the *Mishnah* is a *Yom Tov* case. On *Yom Tov*, carrying is generally permitted — so whether the key is lost in the city or the field, one could carry it directly. The distinction between city and field would be irrelevant. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *Mishnah* and *baraita* must be restructured, and offers a reading in which they address different aspects of the problem.

In the *Gemara*'s revised reading, the *Tanna Kamma*'s ruling that the locked wardrobe is a valid *eruv* applies when it is *Yom Tov* and the wardrobe is constructed of un-plastered bricks — which is why it is considered accessible. On *Shabbos* under those conditions, it would not be valid. And once the key is actually found, it is not automatically valid even in the city, because getting the key from one *reshut hayachid* to another is itself complicated: there is a dispute among authorities as to whether one can transfer an object from a rooftop to a courtyard or vice versa, since those are considered distinct sub-domains within the broader *reshut hayachid* category. Rabbi Eliezer, then, draws his city/field distinction not on the basis of whether the key was *lost* there, but on whether — once found — it can be gotten to the *eruv* through a chain of *reshut hayachid* transfers. The *Chachamim* hold that even finding it in the city is insufficient to establish accessibility, given the practical difficulties of executing such a transfer across different types of *reshut hayachid*. The *Gemara* leaves the full elaboration of this dispute to continue on the following *amud*.
