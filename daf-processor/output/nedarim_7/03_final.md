# Nedarim 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yad Sheino Mochiach

### Definition of Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: יֵשׁ יָד לְקִידּוּשִׁין, אוֹ לָא? הֵיכִי דָמֵי: אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר לַהּ לְאִשָּׁה ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״, וְאָמַר לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ ״וְאַתְּ נָמֵי״ — פְּשִׁיטָא הַיְינוּ קִידּוּשִׁין עַצְמָן! אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר לַהּ לְאִשָּׁה ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״, וְאָמַר לַהּ לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ ״וְאַתְּ״. מִי אָמְרִינַן ״וְאַתְּ נָמֵי״ אֲמַר לַהּ לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, וְתָפְסִי בַּהּ קִידּוּשִׁין לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, אוֹ דִּלְמָא: ״וְאַתְּ חֲזַאי״ אֲמַר לַהּ לַחֲבֶירְתַּהּ, וְלָא תָּפְסִי בָּהּ קִידּוּשִׁין בַּחֲבֶירְתַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma: Is there intimation for betrothal or not?** Does betrothal take effect via an incomplete statement? The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of such a case? **If we say** it is a case **where one said to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me, and he said to another** woman: **And you too, isn't** it **obvious** that **this is betrothal itself,** and it takes effect? **Rather,** it is a case **where one said to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me, and he said to another** woman: **And you. Do we say** that **he said the other** woman: **And you too** are betrothed, **and betrothal takes effect with regard to the other** woman, **or perhaps he said to the other** woman: **And you see** that I am betrothing this woman, **and betrothal does not take effect with regard to the other** woman?

We pick up at the very top of daf 7, at the concept of *yesh yad*. We are in the middle of a discussion that began with a debate between Rav and Rav Yehuda over whether *yad sheino mochiach* qualifies as a *yad* or not. Tosafot raises a preliminary definitional question: what exactly is *yad sheino mochiach*? It must be distinguished from something that is not a *yad* at all — that is, a case where the speaker's meaning is completely uncertain and open-ended. *Yad sheino mochiach*, as we discussed yesterday, is best understood as a case where it is fairly clear what the person means, but not entirely so. There is some ambiguity, and if there is a concept of *yad*, we go by the most reasonable presumed meaning while acknowledging that some other meaning remains possible. This is distinct from a case where the statement is so open-ended that it could mean any number of things. *Yad sheino mochiach* involves one or two or three reasonable possibilities — not an infinite range of interpretations.

### Speech Acts vs Implicit

The broader question running through this section of the *gemara* is where the concept of *yad* applies at all. The fact that the *gemara* asks this question presupposes that in these domains, we are not merely interested in *da'at* — in subjective intent — but in the precise words used: in the *speech act* itself. With *kiddushin*, as Tosafot notes by drawing on the language of *hekdesh*, the declaration *harei at* functions as a formal *hekdesh*-like consecration, and therefore there is a genuine speech-act dimension: the question arises whether one must be entirely explicit or whether an abbreviated, slightly equivocal expression can still be effective. That is the question of *yad* in the context of *kiddushin*.

The case of *pe'ah* is even more striking, because the classical form of *pe'ah* involves no speech whatsoever — one simply refrains from cutting the corner of the field. But Tosafot's analysis requires us to envision a context where one does perform a speech act of designation: one says "this is *pe'ah*" rather than merely leaving the corner. In such a case, the question of *yad* becomes relevant.

### Complete Sentence Rule

Tosafot raises a subtle but important distinction. Why does it matter whether there is some ambiguity in the expression? After all, even if the meaning were completely unambiguous, if there is no concept of *yad*, the person never fully articulated what they meant, and the designation should be ineffective regardless. Tosafot's answer is this: there is a difference between a case where a full sentence was never begun and a case where a full sentence was already stated. If someone has already said *harei at mekudeshet li* to one woman, and then turns and says "and this one" — if that second expression is completely unambiguous, then *and this one* simply applies the entire first sentence to the second woman. It is like saying *amen* after hearing a *berachah*: it is as if you have said the full thing yourself. The Rambam makes this precise point — *ha'omer amen k'mevarech hu dami* — the one who says *amen* has, in a sense, said the entire *berachah*.

Since in such a case the second designation would be fully explicit by virtue of the first sentence, there would be no *yad* in play at all. The *gemara* therefore introduces a degree of ambiguity into these cases in order to ensure that the second expression cannot be read as a simple repetition of the first sentence. And once there is that ambiguity — once we cannot say with certainty that the second utterance was meant to extend the first — then the person has not said the thing explicitly, and the question of *yesh yad o lav* becomes relevant.

The upshot is this: the concept of *yad* addresses whether an inexplicit statement can be effective even when the meaning is clear. *Yesh yad* says yes — even without full articulation, if the meaning is discernible, it counts. *Ein yad* says no — you must say it explicitly, and knowing what you meant is not sufficient.

## Tzedakah & Yad

### Multiple Units Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ יָד לִצְדָקָה, אוֹ אֵין יָד לִצְדָקָה? הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵימָא דְּאָמַר: ״הָדֵין זוּזָא לִצְדָקָה, וְהָדֵין נָמֵי״ — הָהוּא צְדָקָה עַצְמָהּ הִיא! אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר ״הָדֵין״ וְלָא אָמַר ״נָמֵי״, מַאי? ״הָדֵין נָמֵי צְדָקָה״ קָאָמַר, אוֹ דִּלְמָא [מַאי] ״וְהָדֵין״ (נָמֵי) — לְנַפְקוּתָא בְּעָלְמָא קָאָמַר, וְדִבּוּרָא הוּא דְּלָא אַסְּקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **Is there intimation for charity or is there no intimation for charity?** The Gemara clarifies the question: **What are the circumstances** of such a case? **If we say that** it is a case where **one said: This dinar is for charity and this also, that itself is** an explicit statement of donating to **charity. Rather,** it is a case **where he said: This, and did not say: Also. What** is his intention? Is he understood to be **saying: This is also charity, or perhaps what is the meaning of: And this?** He is **saying** that this coin is **merely for general use, and he did not complete his statement.**

The *gemara* turns to the question of *yad* in the context of *tzedakah*. What is the case? If someone says, "This dollar is for *tzedakah*," and then says, "and this one also" — that is no question at all. The word "also" in this context is entirely explicit: it applies the first sentence to the second dollar without any ambiguity. The Rambam's formulation applies perfectly here: saying *v'hadein nami* when it is entirely clear what is meant is equivalent to restating the first sentence. No *yad* is involved.

The genuine *yad* question arises when someone says "this dollar is for *tzedakah*" and then simply says "and this" — *v'hadein* — without adding "also for *tzedakah*." What does *v'hadein* mean here? Perhaps he means, "and this one too is for *tzedakah*." Or perhaps he means, "and this one I'll use for something else entirely." There is just enough ambiguity that the second utterance cannot be read as a transparent repetition of the first full sentence. It is precisely to introduce that ambiguity that the *gemara* frames the case this way. Without it, as we said, there would be no *yad* question — the second expression would simply extend the first.

### Korbanot Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: כֵּיוָן דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ לְקׇרְבָּנוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּפִיךְ״ — זוֹ צְדָקָה. מָה קׇרְבָּנוֹת יֵשׁ לָהֶן יָד, אַף צְדָקָה יֵשׁ לָהּ יָד. אוֹ דִלְמָא לְ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״ הוּא דְּאִיתַּקַּשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two sides of this dilemma: **Do we say** that **since** charity is **juxtaposed to offerings, as it is written** in a verse following the prohibition against delaying an offering: "That you have spoken **with your mouth"** (Deuteronomy 23:24) and the Sages expounded that **this is** a reference to **charity,** therefore, **just as there is intimation,** i.e., intimation is effective, **with regard to offerings, so too, there is intimation with regard to charity? Or perhaps** it is only with regard to the prohibition: **You shall not delay, that it is juxtaposed,** but not with regard to other *halakhot*?

The *gemara* then asks: *mi amrinan keivan d'itkesh l'korbanot d'chtiv b'ficha zu tzedakah* — since *tzedakah* is compared to *korbanot* through the *pasuk* which teaches that "that which passes your lips" applies to *tzedakah*, and there is a prohibition of *bal te'acher* — of failing to fulfill a verbal commitment to give *tzedakah*, particularly when one pledges at a communal appeal — does the comparison to *korbanot* extend also to the concept of *yad*? Just as *korbanot* was the original domain from which *yad* was derived, does *tzedakah*'s linkage to *korbanot* carry that over as well? Or is the comparison limited: *bal te'acher* applies to *tzedakah*, but the structural category of *yad* does not?

### Tzedakah as Neder

It is striking that the *gemara* introduces *tzedakah* at this point in the discussion, placed after *kiddushin* and *pe'ah*, which are both quite distant from the *neder* category. *Tzedakah*, by contrast, is in many ways very close to it. When someone designates a specific sum as *tzedakah*, that money acquires a *tzedakah* status. It is not exactly *hekdesh*, but it is modeled on that structure — one cannot simply redirect those funds to a different purpose and replace them with a different donation later. The object itself becomes earmarked. Furthermore, when someone accepts upon themselves a *tzedakah* obligation through speech, there is the prohibition of *bal te'acher* if they fail to fulfill it. This combination — the designation affecting the status of an object, and the spoken commitment creating a personal obligation — makes *tzedakah* closely parallel to *nedarim* in structure.

It is therefore interesting that the *gemara* raises this question alongside *kiddushin* and *pe'ah*, because *tzedakah* is more naturally in the *neder* mold than either of those. Much of what applies to *tzedakah* reflects the *korban*-*neder* model directly, and one would expect the answer to be that *yesh yad* applies here more readily. Much of this, of course, is a question between a person and God — the courts may have limited jurisdiction over many of these verbal commitments — but the halachic effect is real.

## Tzedakah vs Hefker

### Hefker Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ יָד לְהֶפְקֵר, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵין יָד לְהֶפְקֵר? הַיְינוּ צְדָקָה! אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר קָאָמַר. אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר יֵשׁ יָד לִצְדָקָה, דְּאֵין הֶיקֵּשׁ לְמֶחֱצָה. הֶפְקֵר מִי אָמְרִינַן הַיְינוּ צְדָקָה, אוֹ דִּלְמָא שָׁאנֵי צְדָקָה, דִּצְדָקָה לָא חַזְיָא אֶלָּא לַעֲנִיִּים, אֲבָל הֶפְקֵר בֵּין לַעֲנִיִּים בֵּין לַעֲשִׁירִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **Is there intimation for** rendering one's property **ownerless, or perhaps there is no intimation for** rendering one's property **ownerless.** Does an incomplete expression employed by an owner to relinquish property take effect or not? The Gemara notes: **This is** the same as the previous question with regard to **charity,** which is comparable to rendering one's property ownerless for the benefit of the poor. The Gemara responds: This question is **stated** in the style of: **If you say,** as follows: **If you say there is intimation for charity, as there is no partial** analogy based on **juxtaposition, do we say** that rendering one's property **ownerless is** the same as **charity; or perhaps charity is different, as charity is suitable only for the poor, but ownerless property** is suitable for **both the poor and the wealthy,** and therefore it cannot be derived from the *halakha* with regard to charity.

The *gemara* then asks: *ma shehu l'hefker* — is there a concept of *yad* when someone declares property *hefker*? If I take my old possessions and place them outside, renouncing ownership so that anyone may take them, does that act of renunciation admit of a *yad* parallel? The *gemara*'s initial response is: *haynu tzedakah* — this is the same as *tzedakah*, since both involve making something available for others to take. The question is whether they operate through the same mechanism.

### Beneficiary Scope

The *gemara* then distinguishes: *shani tzedakah*, *tzedakah* is different, because *tzedakah lo chazya ela l'aniyim* — *tzedakah* goes only to the poor. *Hefker*, by contrast, *mi aniyim u'mi ashirim* — is available to rich and poor alike. This difference in the scope of beneficiaries may constitute a meaningful distinction. The logic is not entirely explicit — it is not immediately obvious why the fact that *hefker* can be taken by wealthy people should make it less susceptible to the *yad* principle — but the *gemara* raises this as a genuine differentiating factor.

### Mechanisms Differ

Beyond the question of who can benefit, there is a deeper structural difference. *Tzedakah* functions, as we have noted, somewhat like a *neder*: one designates an object with a *tzedakah* status, or accepts upon oneself a *tzedakah* obligation. In either case, the verbal act creates or defines a status. *Hefker*, by contrast, is not really about designating a status to an object. It is about renouncing ownership. When I declare something *hefker*, I am removing my control over it — severing my relationship to the property. The paradigm is *kol chamira vachamiya d'ika birshuti* — a declaration of non-ownership, of disavowal. Whether such a mechanism of renunciation is naturally susceptible to a concept of *yad*, which seems to operate through verbal acts of designation, is itself a genuine *chiddush* to consider. The inclusion of *hefker* in this list is, accordingly, quite striking.

## Hazmanah Designation

### Toilet Designation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבִינָא: יֵשׁ יָד לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא, אוֹ לָא? הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵימָא דַּאֲמַר ״הָדֵין בֵּיתָא לֶיהֱוֵי בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא, וְהָדֵין נָמֵי״, הַהוּא בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא נָמֵי הָוֵה! אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר ״וְהָדֵין״ וְלָא אָמַר ״נָמֵי״. מַאי ״הָדֵין״ דְּאָמַר — ״וְהָדֵין נָמֵי בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא״, אוֹ דִלְמָא מַאי ״וְהָדֵין״ — לְתַשְׁמִישָׁא בְּעָלְמָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raised** another **dilemma: Is there intimation for** designating a location as **a bathroom or not?** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the case? **If we say that** it is a case where **one said: Let this structure be a bathroom and this** one **also, that** second structure **is** certainly **also a bathroom. Rather,** it is a case **where he said: And this, and he did not say: Also. What** is his intention? Is the expression: **And this, that he said,** understood to mean: **And this** shall **also be a bathroom? Or perhaps what** is the meaning of: **And this? He is saying** that it is designated **for general use** rather than as a bathroom.

Ravina now joins the discussion by asking a related question: *ve-yesh yad le-beit hakiseh o lo* — is there a concept of *yad* for a *beit hakiseh*? The *halakhic* relevance of designating a space as a toilet is that such a space acquires a status that forbids certain activities within it, such as Torah study or reciting *Kri'at Shema*. So what is the case? If someone says, "This house should be a *beit hakiseh*," and then says "and this one also" — that is fully explicit and presents no question. The question arises when he says "this house should be a *beit hakiseh*" and then simply says "and this" — *v'hadein* — leaving ambiguous whether he meant "this too should be a *beit hakiseh*" or "this one I'll use for something else."

### Hazmanah & Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דִּפְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ לְרָבִינָא דְּיֵשׁ זִימּוּן לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא? וְהָא מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ לְרָבִינָא: הִזְמִינוֹ לְבֵית הַכִּסֵּא מַהוּ? הִזְמִינוֹ לְבֵית הַמֶּרְחָץ מַהוּ? זִימּוּן מוֹעִיל, אוֹ אֵין זִימּוּן מוֹעִיל? רָבִינָא חֲדָא מִגּוֹ חֲדָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ: זִימּוּן מוֹעִיל אוֹ אֵין זִימּוּן מוֹעִיל. אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר יֵשׁ זִימּוּן: יֵשׁ יָד אוֹ אֵין יָד תִּיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Can it be derived **by inference that it is obvious to Ravina that there is designation for a bathroom,** i.e., that if one explicitly designates a location as a bathroom, it attains that status even before it is used for that purpose, so that one may not bring sacred items to that location? **Didn't Ravina raise this as a dilemma?** He asked: If one **designated** a particular location **as a bathroom, what is** the *halakha*? If one **designated it as a bathhouse, what is** the *halakha*? In other words, **is designation effective** to grant the location a particular status, **or is designation not effective?** The Gemara answers: **Ravina raised one dilemma within another** dilemma: **Is designation effective or is designation not effective?** And **if you say there is designation,** i.e., designation is effective, **is there intimation or is there not intimation,** i.e., is designation via intimation effective? The Gemara concludes: **The dilemma remains** unresolved.

The *gemara* notes that before Ravina can even get to the *yad* question, there is a prior issue: Ravina apparently takes for granted that *hazmanah* — verbal designation — gives a *beit hakiseh* its *halakhic* status even before the space has been used as such. But Ravina himself had asked elsewhere whether *hazmanah le-beit hakiseh mahu* and *hazmanah le-beit ha-merchatz mahu* — whether designating a space for use as a toilet or bathhouse, without yet having used it that way, confers the relevant status. Does *hazmanah milta hi o lav milta hi* — does designation create status or not?

The *gemara* resolves the apparent tension by explaining that Ravina asked one question because of the other: *chada migu chada ka mibaya lei*. First: does *hazmanah* work at all? And if you say it does, then second: is there a *yad* for *hazmanah*? The two questions are nested — the *yad* question is conditional on the *hazmanah* question.

### Teiku Conclusion

The *gemara* concludes: *tiba'ei lei* — let these questions stand, which is the formulation in *Masechet Nedarim* for what elsewhere appears as *teiku*. Tosafot notes this explicitly: *tiba'ei lei peirush k'mo teiku di-leshon Nedarim meshuneh* — *tiba'ei lei* means the same as *teiku*, but the language of *Masechet Nedarim* is distinctive, as we have noted before. All of these questions — *tzedakah*, *hefker*, *hazmanah*, *beit hakiseh* — are left unresolved.

## Ran's Framework

### Conditional Psak

What then is the *halakhah*? The Ran addresses this directly. He begins with a principle articulated by the Ramban: when the *gemara* poses a conditional question in the form of *im timtzei lomar* — "if you say A, then what about B?" — this structure implies that the *gemara* has already, implicitly, *pasak*'d the first premise. By building the question about *hefker* on the prior question about *tzedakah*, the *gemara* has effectively accepted that *yesh yad l'tzedakah*. One could argue that the conditional structure is merely a logical device for posing related questions and implies nothing about *psak*, but the Ramban maintains that *im timtzei lomar* carries an implicit resolution of the first issue.

### Safek Issurim

The second principle the Ramban invokes is the general rule that *kol heikha de-itamar teiku be-issurin le-chumra* — whenever a *teiku* arises in a matter of *issur*, we are *machmir*. Since the question of *yad* in *tzedakah* is left as a *teiku*, we treat it as binding.

But the Ran finds this startling. He notes that there is an explicit *sugya* at the end of *Perek Elu Metziot* that *safek mamona le-kula* — in doubtful monetary matters, we are lenient. And is not this precisely a monetary question? If it is uncertain whether these funds belong to the *aniyim*, then we should apply *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-reayah* — the one claiming to take from another bears the burden of proof. The poor person cannot prove that the money belongs to them, so the owner would retain it.

This raises a broader conceptual question, discussed by Reb Shimon Shkop in his *Sha'arei Yosher*: why do we not apply *safek issur* logic to monetary disputes? If I genuinely do not know whether money belongs to me or to another person, should I not be *machmir* regarding the *issur* of *gezel* and simply hand it over? Reb Shimon's answer is that monetary questions are governed first by civil law — by how the law determines property rights — and the question of whether one violates *lo tignov* flows from that legal determination, not from an independent objective inquiry into who "really" owns the object. The civil law determines ownership, and only then does the *issur* question arise.

### Tzedakah Status

But the Ran's resolution is precisely the point we have been developing throughout: *tzedakah* is not simply a monetary dispute. When someone says *harei zo tzedakah*, the money acquires an actual *tzedakah* status — it is not merely that the *aniyim* now have a claim on those funds, as in a standard monetary dispute, but that the object itself has been designated, in a manner structurally similar to *hekdesh*, with a *tzedakah* identity. If one then uses those funds for something else, the violation is not simply one of stealing from the poor — it is a violation of the *tzedakah* status itself. That is why the Rambam and the Rashba are *machmir*: they treat this as *safek issur*, not *safek mamona*. The money is not simply owed to the poor the way a debt is owed; it has been invested with a quasi-sacred status through the act of verbal designation, parallel to a *neder*. And precisely because *tzedakah* functions in this *neder*-like capacity — affecting both the status of the object and creating a personal obligation — it belongs in the *safek issur* category, where we are *machmir* in a *teiku*.

## Menudeh Ani Lach

### Rabbi Akiva Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנוּדֶּה אֲנִי לָךְ וְכוּ׳. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מוֹדֶה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְעִנְיַן מַלְקוֹת שֶׁאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. דְּאִם כֵּן, נִיתְנֵי ״רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַחְמִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that if one said: **I am ostracized from you,** Rabbi Akiva was uncertain about the *halakha* but was inclined to rule stringently about this. **Abaye said: Rabbi Akiva concedes with regard to flogging that one is not flogged** if he violates a vow that was expressed in this way. **As, if so,** if Rabbi Akiva held that one is liable to be flogged, **let** the mishna **teach: Rabbi Akiva is stringent.** The fact that it states: Rabbi Akiva was uncertain but was inclined to rule stringently, indicates that although Rabbi Akiva holds that one may not violate this vow, he concedes that one is not liable to be flogged if he does violate the vow.

We now move up to the top of 7a and into a new *mishnah*. The *mishnah* presents the expression *menudeh ani lach* — "I am distanced from you" — and records that Rabbi Akiva *chochech bezeh lehachmir*: he was conflicted but inclined toward stringency, treating this as a *lashon neder*. The question is why this expression should be treated any differently from clearly recognized *lashon neder* formulations such as *murchak mimcha*, *mechunak mimcha*, or *kanuy mimcha*, all of which are recognized as *lashon neder* without dispute.

The difficulty is that *menudeh* most naturally evokes *nidui* or *cherem* — excommunication — rather than the language of personal vow-making. *Cherem* is not exactly a *neder*: it is an objective status that a person acquires, not something done vis-à-vis a specific individual in the manner of a personal *neder*. That is why Rabbi Akiva does not rule with full confidence but rather is *chochech lehachmir* — he acknowledges the linguistic difficulty while leaning toward treating the expression as effective. And Abaye immediately concedes that even Rabbi Akiva agrees that one would not receive *malkot* for violating such a *neder*: it is at most a rabbinic *chumra* or a matter of *safek*.

### Nadina vs Mashmatena

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בִּ״נְדִינָא מִינָּךְ״ — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּאָסוּר. ״מְשַׁמַּתְנָא מִינָּךְ״ — לְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׁרֵי. בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי — בִּ״מְנוּדֶּה אֲנִי לָךְ״, דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: לִישָּׁנָא דְנִידּוּיָא הוּא, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לִישָּׁנָא דִמְשַׁמַּתְנָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: With** regard to a vow that one expressed with the phrase: **I am distanced [*nadeina*] from you, everyone agrees that he is prohibited** from deriving benefit from the other individual, as this is an intimation of a vow. If he employs the expression: **I am excommunicated [*meshamattena*] from you everyone agrees** that he is **permitted** to derive benefit from the other person, even though he meant to distance himself from the other individual, because this is not the terminology of a vow. **With regard to what do they disagree?** They disagree **with regard to** a case when the language one uses is: **I am ostracized from you, as Rabbi Akiva holds** that **it is a language of distancing** and therefore expresses a vow, **and the Rabbis hold** that **it is a language of excommunication,** and not the terminology with which people express vows.

Rav Pappa sharpens the analysis. He distinguishes between two formulations. If someone says *nadina minach* — "I am separated from you" — *kuli alma lo peligi de'asur*: all agree it is forbidden. The word *nadina* can be understood as "I am distancing myself from you," and this distancing — in the context of interpersonal relations — is the very mechanism of a *neder*: I am forbidding your possessions to me, thereby removing myself from you. *Minach* — "from you" — is the classic *lashon neder* of distancing. There is no dispute about this formulation.

By contrast, if someone says *mashmatena minach* — "I am in *cherem* from you" — *kuli alma sheru*: all agree it is permitted. The word *shamta* unambiguously means *cherem* in its technical sense. To say "I am in *cherem* from you" is meaningless as a *neder*: *cherem* is an objective communal status, not a relational prohibition between two individuals. It conveys nothing that functions as a *lashon neder*.

The debate between Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis, then, focuses on the intermediate case: *menudeh ani lach* — "I am *menudeh* to you." Rabbi Akiva holds that *lishna denidui hu* — the word *nidui* retains a connotation of distancing, akin to the root of separation. Even with the word *lach* ("to you"), it can be read as expressing a relational distancing from that person, which is the core of *lashon neder*. The Rabbis, by contrast, hold that *lishna deshamta hu* — the word *lach* transforms the meaning into something like "as far as you are concerned, I am in *cherem*," which is not *lashon neder* at all.

### Etymology Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּפְלִיגָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא. דְּהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאָמַר ״מְשַׁמַּתְנָא בְּנִכְסֵיהּ דִּבְרֵיהּ דְּרַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא״ אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵית דְּחָשׁ לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. קָסָבַר: בִּ״מְשַׁמַּתְנָא״ פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** Rav Pappa **disagrees with** the opinion of **Rav Ḥisda, as** demonstrated in the following incident: There was **a certain man who said: I am excommunicated** from **the property of the son of Rav Yirmeya bar Abba. He came before Rav Ḥisda** to ask whether this statement was effective in generating a prohibition or not. Rav Ḥisda **said to him: There is no one who,** in practice, **is concerned for that** opinion **of Rabbi Akiva.** Apparently, Rav Ḥisda **holds** that **they** also **disagree with regard to** the phrase: **I am excommunicated** from you. This indicates that the dispute between the *tanna'im* is not with regard to specific terms but with regard to the more general question of whether terms of ostracism or excommunication are terms that can also express vows.

Why should *nidui* carry a connotation of separation distinct from *cherem*? Tosafot suggests, somewhat hesitantly, that the root *nidui* may be related to *niddah* — a term that itself connotes separation or removal. Some hold that *niddah* derives from *nad*, meaning "moved" or "displaced," evoking the image of someone separated from the community, as a menstruating woman was, in certain ancient societies, removed to a separate space. In that etymological reading, *menudeh* means simply "separated" or "distanced" — without the specific technical connotations of *cherem* and *shamta*. If so, saying *menudeh ani lach* could naturally be read as "I am separating myself from you," which is precisely the mechanism of a *neder*: forbidding your possessions to me and thereby creating distance.

Abaye thus concludes: *nadina minach* — using the verbal form that conveys relational distancing — is clearly *lashon neder*. *Mashmatena minach* — using the technical *cherem* language — is clearly not. *Menudeh ani lach* falls in between, and that is where Rabbi Akiva and the Rabbis disagree.

### Rav Chisda Ruling

The *gemara* then notes that the ruling of the *mishnah* conflicts with the position of Rav Chisda. A certain man had said *mashlemna be-nichsei de-berei de-Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba* — expressing himself in explicit *cherem* language regarding the possessions of the son of Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba. He came before Rav Chisda asking whether he was now forbidden from those possessions. Rav Chisda ruled: *leit de-chash lah le-ha de-Rabbi Akiva* — nobody follows Rabbi Akiva on this point. You are permitted.

This ruling reveals something important about how Rav Chisda reads Rabbi Akiva's position. The fact that Rav Chisda said "we do not follow Rabbi Akiva" — in a case where explicit *cherem* language was used — implies that Rabbi Akiva would have said even explicit *shamta* language creates a *neder* obligation. Rav Chisda thus reads Rabbi Akiva as holding that *kasavar de-bi-mishamteina paligi* — even the explicit *cherem* formulation falls within Rabbi Akiva's view of *lashon neder*. This is indeed a far-reaching position: even if you say unambiguously "I am in *cherem*," Rabbi Akiva would treat that as generating a *neder* status. The practical *halakhah*, as stated by Rav Chisda and accepted by the *rishonim*, follows neither version of Rabbi Akiva — neither the one who says *menudeh ani lach* creates a *neder* nor certainly the one who says explicit *cherem* language does.

## Nidui Laws

### In-Person Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִילָא אָמַר רַב: נִדָּהוּ בְּפָנָיו — אֵין מַתִּירִין לוֹ אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו. נִדָּהוּ שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו — מַתִּירִין לוֹ בֵּין בְּפָנָיו בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Ila said** that **Rav said:** If **one ostracized** another individual **in his presence, one** may **dissolve** it **for him only in his presence.** If **one ostracized him not in his presence, one** may **dissolve it for him in his presence or not in his presence.**

Having discussed *cherem* and *nidui* as linguistic categories, the *gemara* now pivots into a substantive discussion of the *halachot* governing *nidui* itself. Rav Ila reports in the name of Rav: *niduho be-fanav ein matirin lo ela be-fanav* — if someone was placed in *cherem* in his presence, the *cherem* can only be reversed in his presence as well. Conversely, if the *cherem* was imposed in his absence, it may be reversed either in his presence or not.

The *rishonim* debate the reason. One explanation holds that the act of removal must match the force of the act of imposition — if the *cherem* was imposed in full formal fashion with the person present, then the reversal must carry equivalent gravity. A *she'lo be-fanav* reversal would be insufficient to fully undo what was done *be-fanav*. A second, more interpersonal explanation holds that since the person was publicly humiliated by being brought before a *beit din* and placed in *cherem* in his presence, the restoration of his dignity requires an equally public acknowledgment — a reversal *be-fanav* — so that the community knows his status has changed. According to this second view, if the reversal was done *she'lo be-fanav*, it would be valid *bediavad*, since the concern is honor and public knowledge rather than the formal act itself.

### Shem Shamayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָנִין אָמַר רַב: הַשּׁוֹמֵעַ הַזְכָּרַת הַשֵּׁם מִפִּי חֲבֵירוֹ צָרִיךְ לְנַדּוֹתוֹ, וְאִם לֹא נִידָּהוּ — הוּא עַצְמוֹ יְהֵא בְּנִידּוּי. שֶׁכׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהַזְכָּרַת הַשֵּׁם מְצוּיָה, שָׁם עֲנִיּוּת מְצוּיָה. וַעֲנִיּוּת, כְּמִיתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי מֵתוּ כׇּל הָאֲנָשִׁים״, וְתַנְיָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁ״נָּתְנוּ חֲכָמִים עֵינֵיהֶם״, אוֹ מִיתָה אוֹ עוֹנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanin said** that **Rav said: One who hears mention of the name** of God in vain **by another** individual **must ostracize him** for doing so. **And if he did not ostracize him, he himself,** the listener, **shall be ostracized, as wherever mention of God's name** in vain **is common, poverty is** also **common there.** **And poverty is** so harsh that it is considered **like death, as it is stated: "For all the men are dead** who sought your life" (Exodus 4:19). The Sages had a tradition that Dathan and Abiram had sought to have Moses killed in Egypt and that they were the men referred to in the quoted verse (see 64b). They were still alive at that time but had become impoverished. **And** additionally, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Wherever** it says that **the Sages set their eyes** on a particular individual, the result was **either death or poverty.** This also indicates that death and poverty are equivalent.

Rav Chanan then teaches in the name of Rav: *hashome'a azkarat hashem mipi chaveiro* — one who hears a fellow Jew utter God's name unnecessarily, *shelo letzorech*, is obligated to place that person in *cherem*. And if he fails to do so, *hu atzmo yehei venidui* — he himself deserves to be placed in *cherem*. The underlying authority is broad: this is not a matter limited to formal *batei din*. The Rabbis have effectively authorized anyone who witnesses such an act to impose *cherem* as a form of communal censure — a kind of citizen's enforcement on behalf of the halachic order.

### Name & Poverty

The *gemara* grounds this in a striking chain of reasoning. *Shechol makom she'azkarat hashem metzuya, aniut hi metzuya* — wherever God's name is treated too casually, poverty is found in its wake. And *aniut kemita* — poverty is like death, as we learn from the *pasuk* in which God tells Moshe that all those who sought to kill him have died. Since Datan and Aviram were still alive at that point, the *gemara* concludes they must have become poor, which demonstrates that poverty is considered equivalent to death.

Some *rishonim* connect this to the *pasuk*: *bechol hamakom asher azkir et shemi avo eilecha uverachticha* — wherever My name is properly invoked, I will come and bless you. By implication, where My name is invoked improperly, the opposite of *uverachticha* follows — poverty rather than blessing.

The chain is then completed: *kol makom shenatnu chachamim eineihem, o mita o oni* — wherever the Rabbis directed their displeasure toward someone, the result was either death or poverty. *Nidui* and *cherem* are the instruments of that rabbinic censure. Someone who treats God's name carelessly is bringing poverty and virtual death upon the community; the Rabbis respond with *nidui*, which itself carries the consequence of poverty or death. The *mida keneged mida* symmetry is striking.

The connection to *Masechet Nedarim* is also worth noting. The prohibition of *lo tisa et shem Hashem Elokecha lashav* is primarily associated with *shevuot*, not *nedarim*. But since *nedarim* and *shevuot* are closely linked, and since carelessness with language — whether of *nedarim* or of God's name — represents the same broader failure to honor the weight of words, this discussion finds its appropriate home here. The power of speech runs through all of it.

## Immediate Reversal

### Story Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, שַׁמְעַהּ לְהָךְ אִיתְּתָא דְּאַפִּקָה הַזְכָּרַת הַשֵּׁם לְבַטָּלָה, שַׁמְּתַהּ וּשְׁרָא לַהּ לְאַלְתַּר בְּאַפַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said: I was standing before Rav Huna,** and **he heard a certain woman utter a mention of the name** of God **in vain. He excommunicated her and immediately dissolved** the excommunication **for her in her presence.**

Rabbi Abba relates: he was standing before Rav Huna when Rav Huna overheard a woman utter God's name without necessity. *Shamteh v'altar shara lah* — he immediately placed her in *cherem* and then immediately reversed it. He imposed the censure and undid it in the same moment, as a demonstrative act of rabbinic displeasure, with no intention of allowing it to take lasting effect.

### Three Inferences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַשּׁוֹמֵעַ הַזְכָּרַת הַשֵּׁם מִפִּי חֲבֵירוֹ צָרִיךְ לְנַדּוֹתוֹ, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: נִידָּהוּ בְּפָנָיו, אֵין מַתִּירִין לוֹ אֶלָּא בְּפָנָיו, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֵין בֵּין נִידּוּי לַהֲפָרָה וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Learn three things from this. Learn from this** that **one who hears mention of the name** of God in vain **by another** individual **must ostracize him; and learn from this** that if **one ostracized** another **in his presence, one** may **dissolve** it **for him only in his presence; and learn from this** that **there is nothing,** i.e., no minimum time that must pass, **between ostracism and nullification** of the ostracism.

The *gemara* draws three *halakhic* inferences from this episode. First: *hashome'a shem shamayim mipi chaveiro tzarich l'nadoto* — one who hears God's name uttered needlessly is obligated to place the offender in *cherem*, as Rav Huna demonstrated by acting immediately. Second: *mendeh befanav va'afillu mendeh shelo befanav* — one can reverse a *nidui* whether in the person's presence or not; Rav Huna made a point of reversing it *befaneha*, in her presence, which indicates that had he chosen to reverse it *she'lo befaneha*, that too would have been valid. Third: *ein bein niddui l'hafara v'lo kelum* — there is no required waiting period between the imposition of *nidui* and its reversal; it may be undone immediately.

This appears to contradict other *gemarot* that require a thirty-day wait. The resolution offered by the *rishonim* is that the thirty-day requirement applies to cases of genuine, serious violation — where someone persistently showed disrespect to halachic authority or to the Rabbis in a grave manner. For minor infractions, such as casual utterance of God's name, the *cherem* is purely expressive. It is a momentary act of censure, demonstrating that the community does not condone what occurred, without any intention to impose lasting social consequences. In such a case, immediate reversal is not only permitted but appropriate. 

It is also worth noting that the reversal of *cherem* is called *hafara* rather than *hattarah*. *Hattarah* is the process by which a *neder* is undone through a *chacham*, by establishing that the original intent never encompassed the present situation. *Hafara* is the annulment exercised by a husband over his wife's vow — an act of administrative power without reference to original intent. That the release from *cherem* is described as *hafara* underscores its nature: it is not a finding that the *cherem* was inapplicable or mistaken, but a simple exercise of authority to annul what authority had imposed.

## Self-Nidui Authority

### Self-Reversal Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: תַּלְמִיד חָכָם מְנַדֶּה לְעַצְמוֹ, וּמֵיפֵר לְעַצְמוֹ. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״אֵין חָבוּשׁ מַתִּיר עַצְמוֹ מִבֵּית הָאֲסוּרִין״, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Giddel said** that **Rav said: A Torah scholar** can **ostracize himself, and he** can **nullify** the ostracism **for himself.** The Gemara asks: **Isn't** it **obvious** that he can nullify the ostracism for himself, just as he is able to do for others? The Gemara answers: It states this **lest you say,** as per the popular maxim: **A prisoner cannot free himself from prison,** and since he is ostracized he cannot dissolve the ostracism for himself; therefore it **teaches us** that he can do so.

Rav Gidel teaches in the name of Rav: *talmid chacham menade l'atzmo u'meifer l'atzmo* — a *talmid chacham* can place himself in *cherem* and can also release himself from that *cherem*. The Ran qualifies this: the principle applies specifically in cases like those we have been discussing, where the person does not genuinely deserve the *cherem* — the self-imposed *nidui* is a formal or expressive gesture rather than a deserved sanction.

### Imprisonment Analogy

The *gemara* challenges this: *pshita* — is this not obvious? And the reply is: *ein chavush matir atzmo m'beit ha'asurim* — a prisoner cannot free himself from prison. If a *talmid chacham* truly deserves a *cherem* — if he has genuinely acted in a manner requiring rabbinic censure — then he cannot unilaterally reverse that *cherem* on his own authority. The act of reversal requires an external authority. The self-reversal power exists only where the self-imposed *cherem* was never deserved in the first place, or was imposed as an expressive or procedural act.

### Mar Zutra Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי: כִּי הָא דְּמָר זוּטְרָא חֲסִידָא, כִּי מִחַיַּיב בַּר בֵּי רַב שַׁמְתָּא — מְשַׁמֵּית נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר מְשַׁמֵּת בַּר בֵּי רַב. וְכִי עָיֵיל לְבֵיתֵיהּ, שָׁרֵי לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ וַהֲדַר שָׁרֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** where a Torah scholar might ostracize himself? It is **like that** case involving **Mar Zutra Ḥasida. When a student** in **the academy was liable to** receive **excommunication,** Mar Zutra Ḥasida would **first excommunicate himself and then he would excommunicate the student** of Torah. **And when he would enter his home, he would dissolve** the excommunication **for himself and then dissolve** the excommunication **for** the student.

The paradigm case is that of Mar Zutra the Pious. When a *yeshiva* student was required to be placed in *cherem* for some misconduct, Mar Zutra would first place himself in *cherem* — *mashmat nafshia b'reisha* — and only then place the student. Two explanations are offered for this practice. The first is that even when the *cherem* is necessary, it is a necessary evil — putting a *talmid chacham* in *cherem*, even for genuine misbehavior, carries a weight of gravity, and Mar Zutra felt that doing so required him to share in some of that gravity by subjecting himself to it as well, a form of solidarity or humility before the act. The second is practical and almost mnemonic: since someone in *cherem* may not reside normally with his household, when Mar Zutra returned home at the end of the day he would be reminded that he himself was in *cherem* — and thus prompted to reverse both his own *cherem* and then the student's. The sequence of reversal was also deliberate: *shari lei l'nafshia* first, placing himself back in a state of full standing, and only then *v'hadar shari lei*, reversing the student's *cherem* — paralleling the principle of the *Kohen Gadol* who first performs *kaparah* for himself before doing so for others. The student would thus remain in *cherem* for several hours, which was the intended period of censure.
