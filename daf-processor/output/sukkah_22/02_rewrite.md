# Sukkah 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sukka Meduvlelet

### Mishna Overview

The Mishna at the top of 22a introduces the concept of a *sukka meduvlelet* — a sukka whose *schach* is in some way incomplete or irregular — and rules that it is *kesheira*. The Mishna further specifies the condition *v'tzillatah merubah michamattah* ("and its shade is greater than its sunlight"), and then addresses the opposite extreme: *hame'uvah k'min bayit*, schach as thick as the roof of a house. Even in that case, the Mishna declares, *af al pi she'ein hakochavim nir'im mitocha*, even if you cannot see the stars through it — it remains *kesheira*.

This last ruling is genuinely striking. The widespread intuition that a valid *sukka* must allow one to see the stars is almost certainly derived from this very Mishna, which acknowledges that standard while simultaneously ruling it is not the upper limit of validity. A *sukka* with extremely dense *schach* that entirely blocks one's view of the night sky is still kosher. And as the Rishonim note, the Gemara does not establish any upper bound on how thick the *schach* may be — that question will require further analysis.

### Rav's Case

The Gemara opens with a question: *mai meduvlelet?* — what exactly does *meduvlelet* mean? Rav answers: *sukka aniya*, a "poor" or thin *sukka*. The word *meduvlelet* may derive from *dal*, thin or impoverished. On this reading, Rav understands the Mishna as a single unified case: a *sukka* with sparse, thin *schach* is kosher provided its shade exceeds its sunlight — *tzillatah merubah michamattah*. As Rav reads it, this is not a particularly novel ruling; the requirement that shade exceed sunlight has already been established. The real *chiddush* of the Mishna, on this view, lies at the other end — in the ruling that even extraordinarily thick *schach* remains valid.

### Shmuel's Case

Shmuel offers a more striking interpretation. He reads *meduvlelet* as derived from *mevulbal* — mixed up or interleaved — and understands the Mishna as presenting two distinct cases. In Shmuel's scenario, the *schach* is not thin but rather arranged in two separate horizontal planes: a lower layer and an upper layer, where the pieces of the upper layer are positioned directly above the gaps of the lower layer, and vice versa. If one were to bring the two layers together and flatten them, they would constitute a complete, continuous roof. But as they stand at different heights, considerable sunlight may pass through.

Shmuel rules this *sukka* kosher — *sukka mebulbelet* is valid on its own — and the condition of *tzillatah merubah michamattah* in the Mishna is a second, independent case rather than a qualifier of the first.

### Virtual Roof

This is a genuinely significant *chiddush*. In Shmuel's case, the *schach* does not in practice provide more shade than sunlight to the interior. Yet Shmuel declares it kosher. What this implies is that the requirement of *tzillatah merubah michamattah* is not simply a description of the *sukka*'s actual functional performance — it is something more like a quality we ascribe to the *schach* itself. If we can view the two layers virtually as constituting a single unified roof, and that virtual roof would provide adequate shade, then the *schach* is considered shade-giving even when, in practice, it does not deliver that shade.

This connects to discussions earlier in the tractate. The Gemara has already grappled with cases where the *sukka* does not functionally provide shade — for instance, where surrounding mountains generate the shade, or where the *sukka* is so tall that its walls cast shade rather than the *schach*. In all these cases, the underlying question is whether the *schach* must actually be delivering shade at this moment, or whether the criterion is something more abstract — a quality of the *schach* such that, under ideal conditions, it would provide shade. Shmuel's ruling points strongly toward the latter. By invoking what we might call a virtual or conceptual flattening of the two layers, the Gemara allows us to evaluate the *schach* as it would function if it were consolidated, not as it actually functions given its dispersed arrangement.

## Combining Layers

### Abaye's Rule

Having established that two separate layers of *schach* at different planes can be treated as a single valid roof, Abaye introduces an important limitation. This principle of virtual combination — treating the two layers as one — operates only when the vertical distance between them is less than *sheloshah tefachim*. If the gap between the two layers is three *tefachim* or more, they can no longer be combined; each layer is treated as a separate entity, and since neither by itself constitutes valid *schach*, the *sukka* is *pesulah*. This invokes the well-known principle of *lavud*: elements separated by less than three *tefachim* are treated as if they are joined.

It is worth noting an interesting nuance here. Earlier in the tractate, in the context of *sukka tachat sukka*, the Gemara recognized that once two roofs are separated by even one *tefach*, they are considered distinct. There, however, each roof was independently valid. Here, where neither layer by itself suffices, the logic of *lavud* still applies, and we require that the gap be less than three *tefachim* in order to treat the layers as one.

### Rava's Rule

Rava extends the analysis in the opposite direction. Even where the gap between the two layers exceeds *sheloshah tefachim*, the *sukka* may still be valid — provided the pieces of the *upper* layer are each at least one *tefach* wide. In that case, says Rava, we invoke the principle of *chavut rami*: we imagine throwing the upper layer downward so that it fills the gap and merges with the lower layer, forming a unified roof. The upper layer, by virtue of its width of a *tefach*, has sufficient structural significance to be treated as though it has descended and covered the spaces below.

Rashi notes that *chavut rami* is conceptually analogous to *gud achit* — the principle by which a suspended wall is extended downward — though the parallel is imperfect. *Gud achit* and *pi tikra* operate by extending a vertical plane downward. *Chavut rami* does something distinct: it takes an entire horizontal element and virtually lowers it, filling the intervening space. What makes the width of a *tefach* crucial is that a piece of that width constitutes a meaningful structural unit — a kind of *ohel* in its own right — which is therefore amenable to this conceptual displacement.

The surprise here is considerable. When we apply these principles to walls — *mechitzot* — one can at least argue that a wall's function is to define a boundary, and imagining it extended still serves that definitional purpose. But *schach*'s function is to provide shade, not merely to define a boundary. Imagining the upper layer of *schach* as virtually descending does not, in any literal sense, cause it to provide more shade. And yet, as noted above, this reinforces the reading that what the Gemara demands from *schach* is not actualized shade but the quality of a shade-providing structure — evaluated virtually, not empirically.

### Chavut Rami Intro

Rava himself anticipates the need to ground this principle: *mena amina lah?* — from where do I derive this? He then articulates both dimensions of the rule: that *chavut rami* applies when there is a *tefach*, and does not apply when there is not. His source is a Mishna in Ohalot.

## Chavut Rami Source

### Ohalot Mishnah

Rava's source is a Mishna in Ohalot dealing with beams of a house. Suppose a house has two levels of crossbeams but no plaster or slats connecting them — only the parallel beams themselves. Rashi clarifies that the absence of *ma'azivah* means there are no planks running in the perpendicular direction; only the main beams remain. The Mishna in Ohalot discusses how *tumah* (ritual impurity) spreads in relation to these beams.

The first scenario presented is one in which the two layers of beams are exactly parallel — each upper beam sits directly above a lower beam, beam aligned with beam. The beams are assumed to be a *tefach* wide. If *tumah* is located beneath one of the lower beams, it spreads only directly beneath that beam (*tachteiha tamei*) and nowhere else, because the beam blocks its upward spread. If *tumah* is between the lower and upper beams and directly in the space framed by them, it is *tamei* in that space but cannot spread further because the upper beam blocks it above. If *tumah* is on top of one of the upper beams, everything above it extending to the heavens is *tamei*, but the space to the sides and below remains *tahor*. In each case, the beam functions as a roof — it stops the spread of *tumah* and confines it to the space directly beneath it.

### Alternating Beams

Now the Mishna turns to the case directly parallel to Shmuel's scenario of *schach*: the upper beams are positioned not above the lower beams but above the gaps between them — the two layers are interleaved. In this configuration, the Mishna rules: *tumah tachteihen, tachat kullan tamei* — if *tumah* is under any one of the beams, it is *tamei* beneath all of them. The reason is precisely *chavut rami*: since the upper and lower beams together cover the entire horizontal plane, we view them as though they have been lowered and flattened into a single continuous roof. A continuous roof spreads *tumah* in all directions beneath it. This is the principle Rava identifies as the source for the rule by *sukka*: when alternating elements together constitute a complete cover, we treat them as a unified structure.

### Braita Limitation

A *braita* then specifies precisely when this principle applies: *bizman sheyesh bahen tefach u'veineihen poteach tefach* — when each beam is a *tefach* wide and the space between adjacent beams is also a *tefach* wide. Only under these conditions do we invoke *chavut rami*. If the space between beams is less than a *tefach*, then even though the lower beams are individually a *tefach* wide and function as independent roofs, the upper structure is not significant enough to invoke *chavut rami*. The *tumah* beneath one of the lower beams is *tamei* under that beam only; it does not spread laterally, and the spaces between beams and above them remain *tahor*.

The dual requirement is thus: the beam itself must be a *tefach* wide, and the gap must also be at least a *tefach*. The *tefach* is the threshold of structural significance — below it, a beam is not meaningfully a roof; a gap smaller than a *tefach* does not rise to the level of a space in which we need to invoke virtual combination. Rava takes this rule from *Ohalot* and applies it directly to the laws of *sukka*, concluding that by *sukka* as well, *chavut rami* operates only when the upper element is a *tefach* wide.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

Rav Kahana was transmitting this discussion — the positions of Abaye and Rava regarding combining two layers of *schach* — when Rav Ashi challenged him. Rav Ashi questioned whether it is truly the case that when an element is less than a *tefach* wide, *chavut rami* cannot apply. He cited a *braita* from the laws of the *kora* of a *mavoi* in which it appeared that elements less than a *tefach* wide could still be combined. This challenge sets up the extended discussion in the following section.

## Beam Principles

### Mavoi Beam

In order to permit carrying in a *mavoi* — an alleyway open on one end — a *kora* (beam) spanning the opening is required. The *braita* establishes that a single beam emerging from one wall but not reaching the opposite wall is treated as valid if the gap is less than three *tefachim*, by the principle of *lavud*. Likewise, two beams emerging from opposite walls but not meeting in the middle are treated as a combined beam, again provided the gap between their tips is less than three *tefachim*. If the gap is three *tefachim* or more, another beam must be brought.

### R. Shimon

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees with the standard three-*tefachim* rule. He holds that *lavud* extends to four *tefachim*, and therefore a gap of less than four does not require a supplementary beam, while four or more does.

The *braita* then addresses a further scenario: two beams that are parallel to one another but each individually too narrow to support a brick (*ariach*), which requires a surface of at least a *tefach* width. If the two beams together can support a brick laid widthwise — spanning both beams — then they are treated as a single valid *kora* and no additional beam is needed. If they cannot support the brick even in this configuration, another beam is required. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel again takes the more lenient position: he would accept the arrangement even if the brick is laid lengthwise — spanning the three *tefachim* of the brick's length — between them. The principle in either case is the same: even when neither beam individually meets the requirement, if together they can perform the function, they are treated as a valid combined beam.

### Height Zones

The *braita* continues with the case most directly relevant to Rav Ashi's challenge: *hayu achat lema'ala ve'achat lemata* — the two beams are not merely parallel in the same horizontal plane but are positioned at different heights. Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda rules: *ro'im et ha'elyona ke'ilu hi lemata ve'et hatachtona ke'ilu hi lema'ala* — we view the upper beam as if it were lower and the lower as if it were higher; we bring them conceptually to the same plane. This sounds precisely like *chavut rami* applied to beams of a *mavoi*, and since neither beam by itself is a *tefach* wide (for if it were, there would be no need to combine them), this appears to be *chavut rami* operating without the *tefach* requirement.

There is, however, a critical limitation: the upper beam must be no higher than twenty *amot*, and the lower beam must be no lower than ten *tefachim*. These are the same parameters that govern the *sukka* and the *mavoi* beam generally. A beam outside the valid zone cannot be rehabilitated by imagining it brought into the valid zone — it is simply disqualified.

### Lavud Resolution

Rav Ashi's apparent proof that *chavut rami* applies even to elements less than a *tefach* wide is met with a response: *taretz mehacha nami* — one can resolve the difficulty from the text itself. The key lies in the limiting clause of Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda's ruling: *uvilvad shelo te'hei elyona lema'ala me'esrim ela betoch esrim* — the upper beam must be within twenty *amot*. But more specifically, the resolution is that *vehatachtona smucha lah bepachos mishlosha* — the lower beam must be within less than three *tefachim* of the upper one. In other words, what actually permits combining them is not *chavut rami* but *lavud*: the two beams are close enough vertically that they are treated as joined. Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda's ruling about beams at different heights, then, does not invoke *chavut rami* at all. It relies on *lavud*, and the clause about the valid zone simply means that neither beam may begin in an invalid position.

The Gemara thus preserves Rava's position intact: *chavut rami* operates only when the element in question is a *tefach* wide. When the elements are less than a *tefach*, combination at different planes is possible only if they fall within the range of *lavud* — less than three *tefachim* from one another. This reading of Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda may feel somewhat forced, since one might have expected him to state the *lavud* condition explicitly rather than framing the discussion in terms of height zones. Nevertheless, the Gemara accepts this resolution in order to maintain the principled consistency of *chavut rami* across the domains of *Ohalot*, *sukka*, and *mavoi*.

The deeper question the Gemara raises throughout this discussion is whether the same structural principles govern all three domains. Rabba's assumption is that they do — that a rule derived from *Ohalot* regarding the spread of *tumah* under a roof maps directly onto the laws of *schach*, and that the laws of the *kora* of a *mavoi* (even though that is a rabbinic enactment) can be read in light of the same principles. One could plausibly argue that each domain has its own logic: *mavoi* is entirely rabbinic and therefore more lenient; *Ohalot* concerns the pure conceptual structure of a covering; and *sukka* is a functional structure that must actually provide shade. But the Gemara assumes the overlap, and when the case of the *kora* seems to undermine the principle, it finds a way to reconcile rather than distinguish.

## Shade vs. Sunlight

### Two Standards

The Mishna closes its first section with the ruling *v'tzillatah merubah michamattah kesheira* — if the shade is greater than the sunlight, it is kosher. The Gemara notes that the Mishna in an earlier *perek* (chapter) stated the converse: *shechamattah merubah mitzillatah, pesulah* — if the sunlight exceeds the shade, it is invalid. Reading these two statements together produces an apparent contradiction. The first implies that fifty-fifty — equal shade and sunlight — is *pesul*, since only "more shade" is explicitly validated. The second implies that fifty-fifty is *kosher*, since only "more sunlight" is explicitly invalidated.

### Equal Measure

This tension is noted directly by the Gemara: *ha ki'hadadi?* — what about when they are exactly equal? One statement implies *pesulah*, the other implies *kesheira*. The Gemara resolves the tension with a distinction: *lo kashya — kan mil'ma'ala, kan mil'mata* — there is no difficulty: one statement measures from above, the other from below. These are not the same measurement.

### Rashi's View

Rav Papa introduces an explanatory principle drawn from popular speech: *k'zuza mil'eila, k'istera mil'tata* — what looks like a *zuz* from above looks like an *istera* from below. The *zuz* and the *istera* are coins of different sizes, and the debate between Rashi and Tosafot turns on which is larger.

Rashi holds that the *istera* is larger than the *zuz*. On this reading, the proverb means: what is measured as equal from above will appear greater below. Applied to the *sukka*: if the gaps and the *schach* appear equal when measured from above, the sunlight that reaches the floor will actually be greater than the shade — because sunlight disperses as it travels downward. Accordingly, when the Mishna says that a fifty-fifty arrangement at floor level is kosher, it implies that the *schach* above must actually exceed fifty percent, since the dispersion effect means equal coverage below requires more *schach* above. Conversely, when the Mishna says that more-sunlight-than-shade is *pesul*, measured at floor level, the *schach* above may have been equal — but the dispersion produced more sunlight below. The criterion, on Rashi's view, is ultimately about what reaches the interior, and the point is that more *schach* must be present above to compensate for the spreading of sunlight.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot disagrees with Rashi's reading of the proverb, holding that the *istera* is the smaller coin. On Tosafot's reading, something that looks like a *zuz* from above looks smaller — like an *istera* — when seen from below. The operative mechanism is not the physical dispersion of sunlight but an optical illusion: gaps in the *schach*, when viewed from below, appear smaller than they actually are. If you stand on the floor of a *sukka* and look upward, open spaces in the *schach* will seem disproportionately narrower than the covered areas.

This leads Tosafot to the following resolution: when the Mishna says that equal shade and sunlight is *kosher*, it refers to equality as measured from below — because in that case, the actual gaps above are larger than they look, meaning there is in fact more *schach* than the observer perceives. When the Mishna says that equal shade and sunlight is *pesul*, it refers to equality as measured from above — the accurate measure — in which case the *schach* does not actually predominate, and the gaps, though appearing smaller from below, truly do equal the *schach*.

The practical upshot of the debate is significant. According to Rashi, *kosher* status requires that even the sunlight reaching the floor be equaled or surpassed by shade — a demanding standard. According to Tosafot, one needs the *schach* to be at least equal as measured from above — with the assurance that it will appear even more dominant from below.

## Schach Thickness

### House Thickness

The Mishna rules that *schach* as thick as a house roof is valid. The Gemara presents this as *tanu rabbanan*: *hame'uvah k'min bayit, af al pi she'ein kochavim nir'im mitochah, kesheira* — schach thick as a house, even if the stars cannot be seen through it, is kosher. The Gemara does not impose an upper bound on thickness. As noted by Tosafot, the Gemara in this tractate does not identify any point at which *schach* becomes so thick as to constitute a permanent house structure and thereby lose its *sukka* validity — though Tosafot elsewhere suggests that if rain cannot penetrate at all, the structure may cross that threshold.

### No Sunlight

The Mishna then extends the case: if the *schach* is so dense that even *kochvei chamah* — the glare or bright light of the sun itself — cannot be seen through it, meaning no sunlight whatsoever penetrates, there is a dispute. *Beit Shammai* rules it *pesul* — at this level of density it has become indistinguishable from a house and can no longer be considered a *sukka*. *Beit Hillel* rules it *kosher* even in this extreme case. The ruling follows Beit Hillel, and the *sukka* remains valid even when no sunlight passes through at all.

## Mobile Structures

### Wagon & Boat

The Mishna opens a new topic: *ha'oseh sukkato be-rosh ha-agalah* — one who constructs a *sukka* on top of a wagon — *o be-rosh ha-sefinah*, or on a boat — *kesheira*, it is kosher, and one may go up into it (*olin lah*) on *Yom Tov*. A wagon and a boat, while mobile, do not raise the concerns associated with trees or animals. Entering such a *sukka* on *Yom Tov* involves no prohibited act.

### Tree & Animal

If, however, a *sukka* is constructed *be-rosh ha-ilan* — in the crown of a tree — or *al gabei gamal* — on the back of a camel — it is also valid (*kesheira*), but one may not ascend into it on *Yom Tov* (*ein olin lah be-Yom Tov*). Climbing a tree is prohibited on *Yom Tov*, as is making use of an animal. These prohibitions prevent entry even though the *sukka* itself is structurally kosher.

### Wall Combinations

The Mishna then discusses cases where some walls of the *sukka* are supported by or built into the tree while others are constructed by human hands (*bidei adam*). If two walls rest on the tree and one is built by human hands, or two are built by human hands and one rests on the tree, the *sukka* is valid but one may not enter on *Yom Tov* — since access necessarily involves using the tree. Only when three walls are *bidei adam* — and access can be arranged independently of the tree — may one enter on *Yom Tov*.

### Independence Test

The Mishna concludes with a *kol she-nital* — a general governing principle: *kol she-nital ha-ilan ve-yecholah la-amod bifnei atzmah* — if the tree were removed and the *sukka* could stand on its own, the *sukka* is valid and one may enter on *Yom Tov*. If its structural integrity depends on the tree — if removing the tree would cause it to collapse — then using the *sukka* constitutes using the tree, and it is prohibited on *Yom Tov*. The independence test operationalizes the rule about three walls: three walls built by human hands typically, though not in every imaginable configuration necessarily, suffice to ensure that the structure remains standing without the tree.
