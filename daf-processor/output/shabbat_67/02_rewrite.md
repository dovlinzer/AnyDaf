# Shabbat 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Healing Incantations

### One-Day Fever

The Gemara opens with *v'amar Abaye* — Abaye states that his mother (his nurse-maid, who raised him as an orphan) taught him the following. The *amud* begins ten lines from the bottom of 66b, at the words *mai teiku*, and the material picks up in the middle of a sequence of remedies and incantations Abaye received from her.

For a one-day fever (*eshta bas yoma*), Abaye's mother instructed: take a white coin (*zuza chivera*), go to where salt is dried out — as salt pans still function today near the Dead Sea — and take an equal weight of salt. Bind it in the hollow of the neck (the small depression where the neck meets the chest) with a string of hair (*nira barka*), and it will heal. If that cannot be done, one should sit at a crossroads (*parashas drachim*) — as Tamar went to *parashas einayim* — and when a large ant (*shumshumana gamla*) is seen carrying something on its back, take it, place it in a copper tube (*guvta d'nechasha*), seal the tube with lead, stamp it with sixty seals (Rashi notes that sixty is an exaggeration), shake it, carry it, and say to the ant: *tunach alai v'tunai alach* — your burden should be on me and my burden should be on you, so that the fever is transferred to the ant.

At this point *Rav Acha the son of Rav Huna* raised a question to *Rav Ashi*: perhaps this same ant was previously used by someone else to absorb his fever. In that case, the exchange would be counterproductive — one would be receiving back the first person's burden rather than shedding one's own. The response is therefore to revise the incantation: rather than a bilateral exchange, say *tunai v'tunach alach* — my burden and your burden are both on you. What is striking here is that they consider it entirely appropriate to raise a logical objection against an incantation and revise its wording accordingly. This implies that they understood there to be some operative mechanism — something being genuinely manipulated — and therefore a question could be posed and a correction made. This is not the posture of people treating these formulas as arbitrary magic.

If neither approach is available, take a new small flask (*kuza chadta*), go to the river, and say: *Nahara Nahara* — river, river, lend me (*oizfen*) a flask of water for a guest who has visited me, the fever being the guest. Fill the flask, spin it seven times around the head, throw it behind the back, and say to the river: take back the water you gave me, for the guest who came to me came in a day and shall leave in a day (*b'yoma asa b'yoma azal*). This is intended to cause the fever to depart.

### Three-Day Fever

*Rav Huna* teaches the remedy for a three-day fever (*eshta silsa* — Rashi notes it may refer to a fever that has returned three times). The treatment requires an elaborate collection of seven items from seven separate sources: seven thorns from seven palm trees, seven splinters from seven beams, seven pegs from seven bridges, seven portions of ash from seven ovens, seven measures of dust from seven door-sockets, seven scrapings of tar from seven boats, and seven seeds of cumin, together with seven hairs from the beard of an old dog (*dikna d'kalba sava*). All of these are bound in the hollow of the neck with a string of hair. One might wonder, practically, about the source of the seven pegs from seven bridges — whether one simply removes them. Perhaps, as one suggestion proposes, the pegs were surplus materials left over after construction, available to those who needed them for healing purposes.

### Intense Fever

*Rabbi Yochanan* teaches the treatment for *eshta tzmirtah*, an intense burning fever — Rashi describes it as one that sets the entire body on fire. Take a knife made entirely of iron, go to where there is a certain type of bush (Steinsaltz identifies it as a mulberry or thorn bush; the word *vardina* suggests something rose-adjacent), and tie a hair-string to it. On the first day, carve into the bush a little (*likhrok bei porta*) and say: *Vayera malach Hashem elav* — "And the angel of God appeared to him" (Shemos 3:2). The relevance is the verse's conclusion: *madua lo yivar hasneh* — why will the bush not be consumed? The invocation draws a parallel: as the bush was not consumed by fire, so too should the fever not consume the afflicted person. The second day, carve a bit more and say the next verse: *Vayomer Moshe asura na v'ereh madua lo yivar hasneh* — "And Moses said, let me turn and see why the bush is not burning." The third day, carve again and say: *Vayar Hashem ki sar lir'ot* — "God saw that he had turned aside to look" — the word *sar*, turned aside, is invoked to suggest that the fever too should turn aside and depart.

*Rav Aha the son of Rava* then proposed to *Rav Ashi* that a fourth verse would serve well: *Vayomer al tikrav halom* — "And He said, do not draw near here" — so that the fever should not draw near to the patient. The response is to restructure the sequence: on the first day, combine the first two verses (since the essential operative phrase is *madua lo yivar hasneh*, and both those verses contribute to it); on the second day say *Vayar Hashem ki sar lir'ot*; and on the third day say *Vayomer al tikrav halom*. Again, a question is posed and the incantation revised — a pattern that recurs throughout this passage.

When actually carving into the bush, one should bow down close to the ground (*leitatei*) and say: O bush, O bush (*hasneh hasneh*), it was not because you were taller than all other trees that the Holy One caused His *shechinta* to dwell upon you, but rather because you were lower than all trees (*mishum d'mikhkat mikol ilanei*) did God choose you. And just as the fire saw Hananya, Mishael, and Azarya in the furnace and fled from before them (*v'arkat min kadmohei*), so should the fire of the fever see Ploni the son of Plonis — the patient — and flee from before him.

The connection between the humility of the *sneh* and the incantation is not entirely transparent. One possibility is that the act of bowing down, making oneself humble, is intended to invoke divine compassion, echoing the theme of God choosing the lowest of the trees.

### Boils

For *simta* — which Rashi identifies as a type of boil — one recites: *baz bazya mas masya kas kasya*. Rashi does not attempt to explain these syllables. The incantation continues: *sharli v'amarli*, and then invokes *ilein malachei d'ishtalchu m'ara d'Sdom* — these are the angels sent from the land of Sodom. Rashi clarifies that they did not literally come from Sodom; it is simply part of the *lachash*. The purpose is healing: *u'l'asya shechinta d'kaivin* — to heal the painful boils.

Further doubled words follow: *bazach bizach bazbazach mas masach kamon kamoch*. The incantation then says: *einaich bich einaich bich* — your appearance is to you, your appearance is to you — meaning, remain contained, do not inflame the skin further. *Ashrichat bach* — stay in your place. Do not spread further. *Zarach k'chlot u'ch'farda d'lo para v'dlo ravya* — your seed should be like something that is contained, like a mule that is neither fruitful nor multiplies. *Kach lo tifrei v'lo tirbei b'gufei d'Ploni bar Plonis* — so should you neither spread nor multiply on the body of the patient.

### Demons & Shades

For *kifa* — which Rashi says may refer to pus-filled boils, or alternatively to a certain type of demonic shade — one says: *cherev shlufa v'chala netusha* — a drawn sword and an extended sling — *lo shmi yochav chulin machovin* — his name is not painful illnesses.

For a shade (*shida*) one says: *havayat d'pakik d'pakuk havayat* — you were bound and blocked up, you were. *Lita tavor u'mshumat* — cursed, broken, and excommunicated (like a *shamta*). *Bar Tit bar Tamya bar Tina* — calling the demon by these names, all variants of plaster or mud. The incantation continues with further phrases that Rashi does not explain.

For the shade of the bathroom (*shida d'veit hachisei*), one says: *akarkafei d'ari v'a'usi d'guryaisa* — on the head of a lion and on the nose of a lioness — *ashkachton l'shida bar shirkai panda b'meshera d'karti* — there can be found the demon whose name is *bar shirkai panda* in the valley of leeks. *Chavtei* — he should be struck, swallowed in the cheek of a donkey, and so banished.

One might ask why an individual addresses demons directly rather than praying to God for relief. This is a profound question, and as the Gemara will make clear within just a few lines, performing incantations of this kind is itself considered forbidden. The observation underscores how remarkable it is that these formulas are transmitted in the Gemara at all.

## Rationalism & Magic

### Questioning Incantations

What is perhaps most striking about this entire passage is the recurring pattern in which the Sages treat incantations as subject to rational scrutiny. As noted above, *Rav Acha the son of Rav Huna* asked a pointed logical question about the ant-incantation and proposed a revised formulation. Similarly, *Rav Aha the son of Rava* proposed a better verse for the fever-incantation. In each case, someone raises an objection and the text is actually revised. This suggests the Sages did not view these formulas as arbitrary, inviolable magic but rather as operating through some kind of real — if opaque — mechanism, such that logical questions could be posed and improvements made. They appear to have believed that something was genuinely being manipulated, and therefore the wording mattered.

### Rambam vs. Gra

This naturally raises the broader question of what to make of such material. The *Rambam* is explicit: all of this is nonsense, derived from the mistaken science of the ancient world. Notably, in his commentary on the *Mishnah* and elsewhere, the *Rambam* states that one may recite an incantation over a patient on *Shabbat* — not because it works — but because it is psychologically beneficial, and anything that calms an ill person's mind has value. The *Gra*, by contrast, argues that this entire framing of the *Rambam* is simply wrong, that the *Rambam* was misled by Greek philosophy, and that the incantations in the Gemara are entirely real and operative, as their prevalence in the Gemara itself demonstrates.

The question touches a deep nerve, because underlying it is the problem of authority. For some, it is essential to say the rabbis never erred even in matters of science — because if one concedes error in one domain, how can authority in *halacha* be trusted? The *Rambam* rejects this framing entirely. He argues that one cannot expect the Sages to have transcended the science of their time; that was never the source of their greatness. Their authority derives from their role as transmitters of the tradition and as those authorized to interpret the *Torah* — not from any claim to supernatural scientific foreknowledge. The Sages were limited by the knowledge available in their era, and there is no theological problem in acknowledging that.

### Three Approaches

This yields three distinct positions on how to handle apparent conflicts between Gemara statements and observed reality.

The first holds that we are simply wrong — the ancients got it right, and if the remedies no longer seem to work, that is our failure of knowledge or execution.

The second, associated with the *Rambam*, holds that the Sages were not scientists, that they shared the assumptions of their time, and that being mistaken about empirical matters in no way diminishes their religious authority.

The third — a position often invoked to preserve rabbinic dignity while conceding the empirical point — holds that the facts on the ground have genuinely changed: *nishtana hateva*, the natural order has shifted, and what once worked no longer does. This approach allows one to accept that the incantations no longer function without attributing error to the rabbis.

It is worth noting that the *Geonim* already ruled that one may not rely on the medical recommendations in the Gemara, given that they no longer accord with observed practice. That the *Geonim* themselves made this concession shows that the willingness to set aside Talmudic medical advice is ancient and not a product of modernity.

## Clothing on Shabbat

### Bells & Ornaments

With the discussion of incantations concluded, the Gemara turns to close the sixth *perek* with a few remaining points about what may be worn on *Shabbat*. The Gemara mentions that sons of princes (*vnei melachim*) could wear bells and similar ornaments. *Rabbi Yehoshua* identifies the opinion underlying this ruling as that of *Rabbi Shimon*, who holds that all of Israel are sons of kings (*kol Yisrael bnei melakhim hen*) — we are all descended from Avraham, Yitzchak, and Yaakov — and therefore whatever is appropriate to wear for royalty is appropriate for all Jews. *Rava* offers an alternative rationale: the discussion concerns a bell that is actually woven into the garment (*arig bi'ksusa*), and therefore everyone agrees it may be worn, since if it is integral to the garment there is no concern that one will remove it and come to carry it.

### Crutches & Canes

As the *perek* closes, it is worth recalling two pivotal points from the preceding discussion. First, the general rule: all these items that may not be worn on *Shabbat* in the *reshus harabbim* are prohibited out of concern that one will remove them and carry them. *Tosafot* addressed the striking fact that this concern does not seem to govern actual practice — women routinely wear jewelry in public spaces. *Tosafot* offered three answers, all of which were incorporated into the *Shulchan Aruch*: (1) perhaps the ruling follows the opinion that wearing ornaments is permitted even in a *chatzer*, and since we maintain that there is no *reshus harabbim* in the halachic sense today (a position we rely upon for *eruvin*), the concern does not apply; (2) the violation is only rabbinic in nature, people will not listen to a prohibition even if stated, and it is therefore better to remain silent (*mutav yehyu shogegin*); (3) the concern for removing and showing applied only in a social context where such behavior was common, and since that social norm no longer applies, the entire concern lapses.

Second, and of particular practical importance, is the *Tosafot* ruling regarding crutches and canes, which derives from the Gemara's discussion of prosthetic limbs. The Gemara debated whether a wooden leg is considered clothing (and therefore permissible to wear on *Shabbat*) or a burden (and therefore forbidden to carry). *Tosafot* drew a critical distinction: all of that discussion concerned items that are not strictly necessary for ambulation. But anything without which a person cannot walk at all — crutches, a cane, a wheelchair — is considered halachically part of the body rather than a carried object, and may be used even where there is no *eruv*. This ruling is *pasken'd l'halacha* and has significant practical implications for those who cannot walk without such aids.

### Amulets & Amorites

The *perek* closes with the *Mishnah* that bridges these concerns about what may be worn and the broader topic of *darchei emori*. Items worn for medical purposes that are actively functioning on the body do not constitute carrying. But the question arises: what if those items are of a type that seems not merely medical but superstitious? That is the subject of the final *mishnah* and the ensuing Gemara discussion.

## Amulets Debate

### Three Amulets

The *Mishnah* states: *yotzin b'veitzat hachargol* — one may go out wearing the egg of a locust or grasshopper (*chargol*, one of the species listed in the *Torah*). *U'v'shen shu'al* — and the tooth of a fox. *U'v'masmer min hatzaluv* — and a nail from a crucifixion. Crucifixion was not unique to any one figure; the Romans executed many people in this manner, and nails from crucifixions were believed to have medicinal properties. All of these may be worn because they serve healing purposes (*mishum refuah*), and an object worn on the body for a healing purpose is not considered carrying. This is the view of *Rabbi Meir*. The *Chachamim*, however, forbid these items even during the week, not because of carrying on *Shabbat*, but because of *darchei emori* — the ways of the Amorites, the prohibited practices of idolatrous peoples.

The Gemara then explains what each item treats. The locust egg (*beitzat hachargol*) is used for an earache (*shichla*). The fox tooth (*shen hasha'al*) is used for sleep disorders — the tooth of a living fox (*d'chaya*) for one who sleeps too much, and the tooth of a dead fox (*d'mita*) for one who cannot fall asleep. The crucifixion nail (*masmer min hatzaluv*) is used for *zirpa* — what Rashi describes as an infected wound, perhaps from metal contact, which one might today associate with tetanus.

### Healing Exemption

*Abaye and Rava* both state a general principle (*de'amri tarvaihu*): *kol davar sheyeish bo mishum refuah ein bo mishum darchei emori* — anything that genuinely has healing benefit is not subject to the prohibition of *darchei emori*. If something actually works medicinally, it cannot be classified as superstitious Amorite practice. The *Rambam* in his *Peirush HaMishnayot* explains the criterion: one determines whether something has *refuah* by observing whether it actually works.

The Gemara then presses the converse: does this mean that something with no healing benefit necessarily falls under *darchei emori*? The question is pressed by a *Baraisa* about a tree that is shedding its fruit prematurely. Two remedies are offered: weighting the branches with stones (*toano be'avanim*) and painting the trunk with a red dye (*sokro besikra*). The Gemara readily explains the rationale for the stones — they weaken the sap flow and thereby prevent the fruit from dropping (*ki heichi delichkush cheilah*). But what good does the painting do? *Mai refuah ka'avid* — what medical benefit does it provide?

The Gemara's answer is striking: *ki heichi delichzeih inish veliv'ei aleih rachamei* — so that people will see the tree is in distress, and will pray for its owner. They see the painted tree, recognize that there is a problem, and pray on behalf of the owner, whose prayers are then answered. This is grounded in the rule derived from the *metzora*, who is required to call out *tamei tamei* — Rashi notes that one might have assumed the announcement should come from others, but the verse specifies that even the *metzora* himself must declare his condition. The reason: *tzarich lehodiya tza'aro lerabbim*, one must publicize one's suffering to the public, *verabbim yevakshu alav rachamim*, so that the community will pray on the afflicted person's behalf.

What is profoundly significant about this Gemara is that the question is asked at all. The inquiry — what good does the painting do? — implies that there must be a rational, explicable mechanism for something to qualify as *refuah* and thereby escape the category of *darchei emori*. It is not enough that people believe it works or that they have seen it appear to work. The Gemara demands an explanation. This is a much narrower definition of *refuah* than one might have expected — and it creates an obvious tension with the lengthy catalogue of incantations reported earlier. If an explanation is required, then virtually everything in that earlier material would seem to fall short.

One response is that *Abaye* and *Rava* may have held the broader empirical standard — anything they believed had been demonstrated to work qualified as *refuah* — while the *stam* of the Gemara, in framing the question the way it does, adopts a narrower, more rationalist standard. Not every *sugya* need be a unified position. Additionally, there is a distinction between *Abaye's* own transmitted traditions from his nurse-maid and the legal framework he articulates jointly with *Rava* — the former being ethnographic and the latter normative.

The painting example also yielded a real-world insight: a kibbutz practice of painting trees turns out to have the genuine benefit of preserving moisture in the trunk — a mechanism the Gemara did not know but which fully vindicates the practice on rational grounds.

### Tree Remedy

*Ravina* then asks: according to whom do we hang dates on a palm tree that is not producing well? *Ki hai tana* — like the *tanna* just cited, meaning the practice is justified because it alerts passersby to pray for the tree's owner. *Tani tana b'perek Amorai kamei d'Rabbi Chiya bar Avin* — a *tanna* taught in the chapters of the *Amoraim* in the presence of *Rabbi Chiya bar Avin*. Rashi explains that this refers to the two chapters of the *Tosefta* on *Shabbat* — chapters 6 and 7 or 7 and 8 depending on the version — which contain the extensive list of what constitutes *darchei emori* and what does not.

## Darchei Emori List

### Allowed Incantations

*Rabbi Chiya bar Avin* states: *kulhu yesh bo mishum darchei emori levar mi'hani* — everything in that list falls under the prohibition of *darchei emori* except for these two cases. The first exception: if a bone is lodged in one's throat (*etzem begrono*), one brings another bone from the same species — if a chicken bone, then another chicken or chicken bone — places it on the forehead, and recites: *chad chad nachis bala, bala nachis chad chad* — one by one go down and swallow, swallow and go down one by one. This is not *darchei emori*. The second exception: for a fishbone lodged in the throat (*idra*), one says: *nin'atzta ke-makat, nin'alta ke-tris, shia shia* — you are stuck like a needle, you are locked like a shutter, go down, go down. This too is permitted.

These two incantations are noteworthy precisely because they are incantations — they are not treatments that are obviously explicable in rational terms. And yet they are explicitly exempted from *darchei emori*. This seems to support the view that the standard is not rationalistic explicability but rather some form of demonstrated or assumed effectiveness. Whether the *stam* of the Gemara is consistent on this point remains a genuine tension.

Regarding the overall structure of the *darchei emori* list: the *Yere'im* (*Rabbi Eliezer mi-Metz*, a student of *Rabbeinu Tam*) argues that the list of Amorite practices enumerated by the Sages reflects those known at the time of the *Torah* to be the practices of the Canaanite peoples, and that *assur lehosif aleihem* — one may not add to the list. Anything not on the list is therefore permitted. This is a significant limiting principle.

### Forbidden Incantations

The *darchei emori* list continues. *Ha-omer gad gadi u'snuk lo ashkei u'bushkei* — one who says "let my luck increase and not falter, day and night" — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*. This is a specific incantation for good fortune and falls under the prohibition. This is distinct from a prayer to God expressing the same wish, such as the *yehi ratzon* formulas recited at the *Rosh Hashanah* table with symbolic foods; those are prayers directed to God, not standalone incantations.

*Hu b'shmo v'hi b'shmah* — if a husband and wife in the bedroom refer to each other by the other's name — this too is *darchei emori*. *Donu doni* — an incantation for the strengthening of one's wine barrels — also falls under the prohibition.

### Divine Names

*Rabbi Yehuda* adds a further dimension to two of the above: not only are these practices *darchei emori*, they are actually worse — they invoke the names of real pagan deities. Regarding *gad gadi*: *ein Gad ela lashon avodah zarah* — the word *Gad* is the name of a deity, as the verse states (*Yeshaya* 65:11): *ha-orchim l'Gad shulchan* — those who set a table to the god Gad. Regarding *donu doni*: *ein Dan ela lashon avodah zarah* — *Dan* is also the name of a deity, as the verse states (*Amos* 8:14): *chai elohecha Dan* — by the life of your god *Dan*, associated with the worship at the city of Dan, where one of Jeroboam's two golden calves was erected.

It is striking that two of the names of the *shevatim* — *Gad* and *Dan* — also function as divine names in idolatrous contexts. One possibility is that the tribal names were appropriated by surrounding peoples for their deities, or alternatively that the city of Dan became so associated with idolatrous practice that the god worshipped there simply bore its name. One might similarly note *Tammuz* (*Tamuz*), a month name in the Jewish calendar that is also the name of a deity, as the verse describes: *mevakkot et ha-Tammuz* (Yechezkel 8:14).

### Animal Omens

*Ha-omer la-orev tzrach* — one who says to a raven, "screech," and to a female raven, "screech," and "turn your tail toward me for good" (*v'hachiziri li zenavech l'tova*) — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*. This was done upon seeing or hearing a raven, which was interpreted as a portent, and the incantation was meant to redirect the omen. *Ha-omer shachtu tarnegol ze she-kara arvit* — one who says to slaughter a rooster because it crowed after dark (when roosters should not crow), *v'tarnegolet she-kar'ah gavrit* — or a hen that crowed like a rooster — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*. The feeling that one must slaughter such an animal to ward off misfortune is itself the Amorite practice. Many readers will recognize in these passages echoes of folk superstitions that persisted long after the Talmudic period in various Jewish communities — the impulse to neutralize a bad omen by acting against it is ancient and widespread.

### Cooking Superstitions

*Eshteh ve-otir eshteh ve-otir* — reciting "I will drink and leave over, drink and leave over" as an incantation — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*. The intent to leave something over at the end of a meal can be a fine custom (*v'savanu v'hotarnu*), but articulating it as a ritual formula crosses into Amorite practice.

*Ha-mafka'at beitzim ba-kotel bifnei efrochim* — one who cracks eggs against a wall or places eggshells on a wall in front of chicks to protect them from dying — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*. *Ha-megis bifnei efrochim* — one who stirs a pot in front of chicks for the same purpose — also forbidden. *Ha-merakkedet v'ha-monah shiv'im ve-echad efrochim bishvil she-lo yamutu* — one who dances while counting seventy-one chicks so that they should not die — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*. The anxiety about losing one's chicks clearly prompted a variety of such practices.

*Ha-merakkedet le-kutach* — dancing when making *kutach* (a sour-milk based condiment) — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*. *Ve-ha-meshattaka le-adashim* — insisting on silence while cooking lentils — also forbidden. *Ve-ha-metzavachat le-grisim* — making loud noise while cooking *grisim* (a type of legume) — also forbidden. One might note that elsewhere in the Gemara it is said that the sounds made when grinding spices have a real effect on quality; this list, however, treats the analogous practices as pure superstition.

*Ha-mash-tenet bifnei kederatah bishvil she-titbashel mehera* — a woman who urinates in front of her pot to speed up cooking — *yesh bo mishum darchei emori*.

### Legitimate Methods

In contrast to the forbidden practices, certain methods of speeding up cooking or improving one's lamp are entirely permitted. Placing a twig of *tut* (mulberry) and a piece of glass in a pot will genuinely cause the contents to cook faster — this is not *darchei emori* but a real technique. The *Chachamim*, however, object to the piece of glass not on grounds of *darchei emori* but of *sakana* — physical danger, as glass fragments in food present an obvious hazard.

Placing a clump of salt into an oil lamp (*bul shel melach l'toch haner*) will make it burn more cleanly — this too is a real and permitted method. Placing pottery shards (*charsisa*) beneath a lamp will cause it to burn more slowly. *Rav Zutra* adds: one who covers an oil lamp or leaves a naphtha-burning lamp (*sheraga d'mishcha* or *nafta*) uncovered causes the fuel to burn faster than necessary and thereby transgresses *Bal Tashchit* — the prohibition against wasteful destruction.

### Wedding Toasts

The *Baraisa* then states: saying *chamra v'chayei l'fum rabbanan* — wine and life to the mouth of the rabbis — is *ein bo mishum darchei emori*, not a *darchei emori* problem. It is simply a standard toast.

A story is cited: *Ma'aseh b'Rabbi Akiva she-asah mishteh l'vno* — Rabbi Akiva made a wedding feast for his son, and at every cup of wine brought to the table he said *chamra v'chayei l'fum rabbanan* — wine and life to the mouth of the rabbis. One might ask whether this particular phrase, being a crystallized formula repeated at every cup, could be considered an incantation. The answer is no — it is simply a standard, recognized toast, not a ritual invocation. The version attributed to Rabbi Akiva extended the formula: *chayei v'chamra l'fum rabbanan u'l'fum talmideihem* — wine and life to the rabbis, to the rabbis, and to their students.

An interesting related question arises from the *Tosefta*: whether one may say *asuta* — that is, the equivalent of "gesundheit" — when someone sneezes. Depending on the textual version (*girsa*), some read *yesh bo mishum darchei emori* and others read *ein bo mishum darchei emori*, reflecting genuine dispute about whether so natural an expression crosses any line.

This concludes the sixth *perek*. *Hadran Alach Bameh Isha*.

## Seventh Perek Intro

### Six to Seventh

It is worth pausing to appreciate the structure of *Masechet Shabbat* as we move from the sixth *perek* to the seventh. The first six *perakim* have all been, in one way or another, about preparing for *Shabbat*. The tractate opened with the rules of carrying, then moved to lighting candles, putting food on the fire, insulating food, what one may wear — and whether one's animals may wear — as one dresses for *Shabbat*. Two entire *perakim* were devoted to the topic of carrying in its various dimensions — what people and animals may wear and what constitutes forbidden carrying. It is not coincidental that there are six *perakim* before the seventh: six days lead up to the seventh, and these six *perakim* lead up to the *perek* that addresses the *melachos* of *Shabbat* themselves. The *melachos* have been implicit throughout, but now we arrive at the heart of the matter. In a few *dappim* we will reach the famous *Mishnah* that enumerates the thirty-nine *avos melachos*; but first the tractate introduces the framework for liability.

### General Principle

The seventh *perek* opens with a *klal gadol* — a general principle: *kol ha-shoche'ach ikar Shabbat v'asah melachos harbeh b'Shabbatos harbeh* — one who entirely forgot the concept of *Shabbat* and performed many categories of labor on many *Shabbosos* is liable for only one *chatat*, one sin-offering. The reasoning is that only one fundamental error was made: the complete forgetting that *Shabbat* exists at all. No matter how many *Shabbosos* passed and how many types of work were performed, the person made a single mistake, and a single *chatat* is appropriate.

The second case: *ha-yode'a ikar Shabbat* — one who knows that *Shabbat* exists, but made an error about which day is *Shabbat* (perhaps mistaking Friday for *Shabbat* or losing count of the days). This person performed many labors on many *Shabbosos* — *chayav al kol Shabbat v'Shabbat* — and is liable for a separate *chatat* for each *Shabbat* on which the error occurred. The mistake was repeated twenty times if twenty *Shabbosos* passed; twenty sin-offerings are owed. The number of *melachos* performed on each *Shabbat* does not multiply the liability — it is the number of separate *Shabbosos* during which the error was operative.

An important question arises: if the person made one original error (say, miscounting the days by one), then all subsequent *Shabbat* violations flow from that same single error. Why then does the Gemara say one is liable for each *Shabbat* separately? The answer — which will be developed in the ensuing *sugya* — is that the intervening weekdays (*chol*) objectively separate the violations into distinct acts, even if the subjective mistake was one continuous miscalculation.

The third case: *ha-yode'a she-hu Shabbat* — one who knows that it is *Shabbat*, but was unaware that a particular act is forbidden. This is the most common type of error. Such a person is *chayav al kol av melacha u'melacha* — liable for a separate *chatat* for each category of forbidden labor (*av melacha*) about which he was mistaken. If someone never knew that watering plants was forbidden and did so every *Shabbat* for twenty weeks, he owes a single *chatat* — because there was one error, regarding one *av melacha*. But if he also did not know that writing was forbidden, and did that as well, he owes a separate *chatat* for that category. The unit of liability is the number of *avos melachos* misunderstood, not the number of *Shabbosos* on which the violation occurred.

The fourth case: *ha-oseh melachos harbeh me'ein melacha achat* — one who performed many variations of work all falling within a single category. Watering plants, pruning trees, and fertilizing — all are subcategories of encouraging plant growth, a single *av melacha*. Performing all of them yields only one *chatat*, because they all derive from a single root prohibition. This principle — that *toldos* (subcategories) of a single *av melacha* are grouped together for purposes of *chatat* liability — will be at the center of discussion in the pages to come.
