# Bava Batra 156 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Age & Judgment

### Shichiv Meyra Intro

The daf opens in the middle of an extended digression that arose from the Mishnah's discussion of a *shtar* whose validity was contested on the grounds that it was titled as a *matnat shechiv meyra*. The immediate trigger was a case in which a man sold land and his brother later claimed that the seller had been a *katan* — underage — at the time of the transaction. The Gemara clarifies that "underage" here does not simply mean under *bar mitzvah*; there is a distinct principle that to sell land belonging to one's father's estate, a person must be either eighteen or twenty years old. The Gemara had already explored various nuances: even if a person has reached twenty, if he is demonstrably irresponsible the sale may not stand; conversely, if he is under twenty but shows unusual savvy in business affairs, the sale may be upheld. The further question of whether there is any fixed cutoff for irresponsibility beyond age — whether, say, a thirty-year-old spendthrift would also be disqualified — is left contextual by the Gemara, without a clear bright line.

Underlying the entire framework is the reason why land is treated so differently from other property. Land represents the fundamental *yerusha*, the family patrimony, the continuity of the estate across generations — especially significant in an era when *yovel* was no longer operative and a sold plot would not automatically revert. The Rashbam and Tosafot both note that this restriction applies only to inherited land, not to land a person acquires through his own labor and purchase. One reason for this distinction is that inherited property carries a familial and historical weight that purchased property does not; another is the psychological reality that people tend to be less careful with what came easily to them.

### Rav Huna Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: וּלְעֵדוּת – עֵדוּתוֹ עֵדוּת. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא לְמִטַּלְטְלֵי, אֲבָל לִמְקַרְקְעֵי – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says: And with regard to** the **testimony** of one who is not old enough to sell property that he inherits from his father, **his testimony** is valid **testimony. Mar Zutra said: We said** that his testimony is valid **only with regard to movable property, but not with regard to land.**

Against this backdrop, Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua introduces a crucial distinction. Even though a person under twenty cannot sell his father's land, his *edut* — his testimony regarding a transaction involving land — remains valid. The reasoning is that the incapacity to sell is not a reflection of diminished intelligence or awareness; the young person understands perfectly well what is happening around him. The concern is not about comprehension but about *judgment* — specifically, the maturity of judgment required to decide that a particular sale is genuinely in one's long-term interest. Testifying about facts one has witnessed is a different cognitive act than making a consequential financial decision, and on the former the person under twenty is fully *kosher le'edut*.

### Mar Zutra Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְמָר זוּטְרָא: מַאי שְׁנָא מִטַּלְטְלִי – דִּזְבִינֵיהּ זְבִינֵי; אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, הָא דִּתְנַן: הַפָּעוֹטוֹת – מִקָּחָן מִקָּח וּמִמְכָּרָן מִמְכָּר בְּמִטַּלְטְלִין, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּעֵדוּתָן עֵדוּת?! אָמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם, בָּעֵינָא ״וְעָמְדוּ שְׁנֵי הָאֲנָשִׁים״, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Mar Zutra:** In **what** way **is movable property different** from land? It is different **because** with regard to movable property **his sale is** a valid **sale. But if that is so,** consider **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Gittin* 59a): **A purchase** made **by young children** is a valid **purchase, and a sale** made by them is a valid **sale.** These *halakhot* apply **to** transactions involving **movable property.** Is it possible **that also** in the case of young children **their testimony** is valid **testimony?** Isn't it an established *halakha* that minors are disqualified from bearing witness? Mar Zutra **said to** Rav Ashi: **There,** with regard to testimony, **I require** the witnesses to be men, as the verse states: **"Then the two men shall stand"** (Deuteronomy 19:17), which is interpreted as referring to witnesses, **and** this requirement is **not** fulfilled in the case of young children.

Mar Zutra, however, pushes back and introduces a distinction within *edut* itself. He holds that the leniency applies only to testimony involving *metaltelin* — movable property — but not to testimony involving *karka* — real property. The implicit logic Mar Zutra appears to be working with is that just as the person's capacity tracks with his ability to transact, and his transactions with *metaltelin* are valid while his transactions with *karka* are not, so too his testimony should be valid only in domains where his underlying transactions would be recognized. This represents a tighter integration of the two legal categories — testimonial capacity and transactional capacity — than Rav Huna had assumed.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

Rav Ashi challenges Mar Zutra by pressing this very logic to an uncomfortable conclusion. If testimonial capacity tracks transactional validity, then consider the ruling regarding *pi'utot* — young children of approximately six or seven years old — for whom the Sages permitted transactions in *metaltelin* to be valid, so that the ordinary commerce of daily life could function without requiring a parent present for every purchase. If Mar Zutra's principle holds, one would have to conclude that a six-year-old could serve as a valid *ed* for matters of *metaltelin* — a conclusion the Gemara finds untenable, since formal *edut* categorically excludes minors. The Gemara therefore resolves that there are two distinct criteria for valid testimony, and neither one alone is sufficient. The first is the formal, categorical requirement: the witness must be a male adult, as derived from the phrase *ve'amdu shenei ha'anashim* — "and the two men shall stand." The second is a contextual criterion of mature judgment, applicable within the category of formally qualified adults. So a person who is over *bar mitzvah* and thus formally qualified may still be found to lack the degree of judgment required for testimony about *karka*, where the age threshold is twenty. Both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously: formal adult status and the requisite level of judgment for the subject matter at hand.

### Two Criteria

This dual-criteria framework carries an important implication: the restrictions on selling land *benichsei aviv* — specifically the age-twenty requirement — apply expressly to *karka*. A person who has passed *bar mitzvah* but not yet reached twenty satisfies the formal criterion for testimony on *metaltelin* but not on *karka*.

### Tosafot Value

Tosafot does not accept this reading of Mar Zutra's position. Tosafot argues that if the only question were whether the person could testify about *whether* a sale of land occurred — straightforward factual testimony — it is hard to see why that would be limited by the same age restriction as the sale itself. Testifying about a fact does not implicate the same judgment concerns as making a decision. Tosafot therefore understands Mar Zutra's restriction to apply specifically to testimony in which the witness is called upon to assess the *value* of the land — for instance, in a case where there is a claim of overcharging, and the witness must declare what the land was worth at the time. In such a case, the concern is precisely the same as the concern underlying the prohibition on sales: the young person's judgment is distorted by the allure of money, so that he cannot reliably assess the true value of land as against a stated price. Tosafot thus ties the testimonial limitation directly to the same psychological dynamic that will be articulated momentarily in the Gemara's discussion of gifts — the seductive pull of ready cash on a young person who has not yet developed the mature appreciation for the long-term value of land.

One might note that this requires something like an expert assessment — appraising the fair market value of land — and it is far from obvious that any ordinary person, young or old, possesses that competence. Perhaps the Gemara is working within an agrarian context in which the value of a standard parcel of farmland was relatively transparent to any adult member of the community. In any event, Tosafot's reading has the virtue of making the testimonial restriction and the transactional restriction mirror-images of one another, governed by the same underlying logic.

## Gifts vs Sales

### Ameimar Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: וּמַתְּנָתוֹ מַתָּנָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: הַשְׁתָּא, וּמָה זְבִינֵי – דִּמְקַבֵּל זוּזֵי, אָמְרַתְּ דְּלָא – דִּלְמָא מוֹזֵיל וּמְזַבֵּין; כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן מַתָּנָה – דְּלָא מָטֵי לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar says:** With regard to one who is not old enough to sell property that he inherits from his father, if he gave a gift from the property, **his gift** is a valid **gift. Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: Now, if** with regard to **a sale,** in **which he receives money, you say** that the Sages instituted an ordinance **that** he is **not** authorized to sell, **lest he reduce** the price of the property and deplete his father's estate, **all the more so** in the case of **a gift** he is not authorized to give a gift, **as nothing at all comes into his** possession in exchange. Ameimar **said to** Rav Ashi:

The Gemara now presents a ruling that strikes as genuinely surprising. Ameimar states: *u'matnato matnah* — even though this person under twenty cannot sell his father's land, a gift he makes is valid. This is counterintuitive on its face. The earlier Gemara had flagged the freeing of slaves as a paradigm of irresponsible behavior toward one's estate, suggesting that the broader concern is with any gratuitous dissipation of inherited property. A sale at least returns something; a gift returns nothing. Why, then, would the gift be *more* permissible than the sale?

### Rav Ashi Logic

Rav Ashi presses exactly this challenge. If even selling — where the young person at least receives money in exchange — is prohibited, because we fear he may sell too cheaply, then *all the more so* a gift, where the estate receives nothing at all, ought to be invalid. The logic, as Rav Ashi frames it, seems to flow straightforwardly from lesser to greater: *dilma mozil u'mazvin*, perhaps he will undersell — and yet we prohibit the sale; *kol sheken matnah de'la mati leh ve'la midi*, certainly a gift, where nothing at all comes to him, should not be valid.

### Money Seduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא קִים לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּיָנוֹקָא מְקָרְבָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ גַּבֵּי זוּזֵי; וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ זְבִינֵיהּ זְבִינֵי, זִמְנִין דִּמְקַרְקְשִׁי לֵיהּ זוּזֵי, אָזֵיל מְזַבֵּין לְכוּלְּהוּ נִכְסֵי דַּאֲבוּהּ. אֲבָל גַּבֵּי מַתָּנָה, אִי לָאו דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ – לָא הֲוָה יָהֵיב לֵיהּ מַתָּנָה; אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן: תֶּיהְוֵי מַתְּנָתוֹ מַתָּנָה, דְּלִעְבְּידוּ לְהוּ מִילֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the Sages maintain that a child's inclination** is to be **attracted to money. And if you say** that **his sale is** a valid **sale,** there may be **times** that there are potential buyers **who rattle the dinars** before **him** in order to tempt him to sell, and **he will go and sell all of his father's property.** That is why the Sages ruled that all of his sales are not valid. **But with regard to a gift, if he did not** derive **benefit from** the recipient, **he would not give him a gift. The Sages** therefore **said: Let the gift** of an orphan **be** a valid **gift, so** that people **will perform** beneficial **matters for** the orphans, as the orphan can reciprocate by giving gifts.

The Gemara's response reframes the entire analysis. The Sages established as a fixed rule — a *lo pelug* — that a young person's mind is drawn toward cash: *yanuka mekarva da'ateh gabbei zuzei*. Young people are seduced by the immediacy of money. The specific fear the Gemara articulates is vivid: *mekarkeshi leh zuzei* — someone jingling coins at them — and then *ve'azil u'mazvin le'chulhu nichsei de'avuh*, they go ahead and sell off the entire family estate. The onomatopoeic force of *mekarkeshi* captures perfectly the sensory appeal, the sound of coins being shaken in a pouch, that might cloud a young person's assessment of what an acre of land is really worth. The Gemara is not making a case-by-case assessment; it has established a blanket rule because the moment you say that any specific sale will be valid once it appears to have been a good deal, you open the door to manipulation. A clever buyer can always make a particular transaction look like a favorable one. The prohibition is categorical precisely to foreclose that manipulation.

### Gift Logic

The reason gifts are treated differently now becomes clear. When it comes to a gratuitous transfer, the temptation the Gemara worries about is simply not present. There is no one dangling money in front of the young person; there is no immediate exchange in which the senses are engaged by tangible gain. *Aval gabei matnah i'lav de'hava leh hana'ah mineh lo hava yahiv leh matnah* — were it not that the person received some genuine benefit from the recipient, he would not have given a gift. The absence of an immediate monetary temptation means that the young person's judgment is not being overridden by the same seductive force. The Gemara acknowledges, implicitly, that this logic has limits — one could imagine scenarios in which a young person is manipulated through gifts of hospitality or flattery into transferring land — but the structural concern, the categorical fear of the jingling coin, is simply not present in the same way.

### Chachamim Rationale

Even granting this reasoning, one might still ask: once the Sages have restricted the sale for the young person's protection, why not simply extend the same protection to gifts? Whatever the difference in risk level, the estate is still being given away for nothing. The Gemara adds a further consideration: *amru rabbanan tehei matnato matnah dilaavdu lei mili* — the Sages wanted people to be willing to do favors for the young person. If it were known that no gift he gave could ever be valid, people would have no incentive to invest socially in him. The very *possibility* that he might gift land to someone who has been generous to him creates a social dynamic in which people shower him with kindness. The Rashbam sharpens this point: *ligmol lo chesed u'lheitiv lo bechol inyanim* — to perform acts of kindness for him in all matters. The practical implication the Gemara seems to have in mind is that since gifts of *karka* are relatively rare even among adults, and the young person could in any event make gifts of *metaltelin* freely, the real significance of allowing gifts of land is primarily this incentive structure — preserving his social support network during the years before he reaches full transactional capacity.

## When Check Needed

### Verification Need

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בּוֹדְקִין לְקִדּוּשִׁין, לְגֵרוּשִׁין, וְלַחֲלִיצָה, וּלְמֵיאוּנִין. וְלִמְכּוֹר בְּנִכְסֵי אָבִיו – עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **Shmuel says:** Children who have reached the age of majority, i.e., a boy who is thirteen years old and a girl who is twelve years old, **are examined** for signs indicating puberty if it is necessary to determine their adulthood **for** the purpose of **betrothal, for** the purpose of **divorce, for** the purpose of ***ḥalitza*, and** for the purpose of stating a girl's **refusal** to remain married. **But** in order **to sell from the property that** one inherited from **his father,** the seller must be older, and one cannot sell this property **until** the seller **is twenty** years **old.**

Having discussed the age thresholds for land transactions, the Gemara pivots to a broader discussion of when direct verification of adulthood is required. The triggering issue in the earlier narrative — the brother who proposed exhuming the body to check for pubic hair — raises the general question: when must one establish the presence of pubic hair through direct examination, and when may one rely on a presumption of adulthood based on age alone?

The framework here rests on what is known as *chazakah de'Rava* — Rava's presumption that once a boy reaches thirteen or a girl reaches twelve, we assume they have developed the requisite signs of adulthood. The halachic conclusion is that this presumption may be relied upon for matters that are *de'Rabbanan* in nature, but not for matters that are *de'Oraita*. This is why, at the rabbinic level, a thirteen-year-old boy may lead a congregation or receive an *aliyah*, since those functions rest on rabbinic frameworks. But for a *de'Oraita* obligation — such as *parashat zachor*, whose reading may be a Torah-level requirement — mere age is insufficient; one would need affirmative evidence of physical maturity. It should be noted, though not developed in this Gemara, that for boys, the presence of facial hair constitutes evidence of pubic hair and thereby avoids the need for more intrusive examination.

### Four Areas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דִּבְדַקְנָא לְקִדּוּשִׁין, לְגֵרוּשִׁין לְמָה לִי? לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְיִבּוּם – דִּתְנַן: בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד שֶׁבָּא עַל יְבִמְתּוֹ – קְנָאָהּ. וְאֵין נוֹתֵן גֵּט עַד שֶׁיִּגְדַּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But once I examined** the boy **for** the purpose of **betrothal, why do I** need to examine him again **for** the purpose of **divorce?** The Gemara answers: This **is necessary only with regard to** the **levirate marriage** of a minor, **as we learned** in a mishna (*Nidda* 45a): A boy who is **nine years and one day old who engaged in intercourse with his *yevama*,** i.e., his brother's widow, **acquired her** as his wife by means of engaging in the act of intercourse. Although a minor cannot betroth a woman under ordinary circumstances, in the case of levirate marriage the act of intercourse of a nine-year-old with his *yevama* effects acquisition. **But he cannot give** her **a bill of divorce until he reaches his majority.** It is therefore necessary to examine him at the time of the divorce.

Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel now provides a formal statement: *bodkin le'kiddusdin le'geirushin le'chalitzah u'le'miunin* — we verify physical adulthood for four areas: *kiddushin*, *geirushin*, *chalitzah*, and *miun*.

For *kiddushin*: suppose a boy who was thirteen gave *kesef kiddushin* to a girl who is known to be an adult. Is the marriage valid? The answer has consequences in both directions. Being stringent and treating the marriage as definitely valid would mean that if she subsequently accepts *kiddushin* from a second man, her acceptance from the first forecloses the second. But treating the first as merely a *safeik* means she would need a *get* from both. Where consequences attach in both directions, the question cannot be resolved by simple stringency alone; one must establish the facts. Similarly, if a girl who has reached age twelve but whose physical maturity has not been verified accepts *kesef kiddushin* directly — not through her father — one must verify whether the *kiddushin* was definitively valid.

For *geirushin*: only a halachic adult can issue a *get*; therefore the husband's adulthood must be verified. The Gemara notes that since verifying for *kiddushin* and verifying for *geirushin* cover overlapping ground — once you have established adulthood for one purpose you have established it for the other — the separate mention of *geirushin* must be doing independent work.

### Yibum Issue

That independent work, the Gemara explains, concerns *yibum*. The Mishnah elsewhere establishes that a boy of nine years and one day who engages in intercourse with his deceased brother's widow has thereby *acquired* her through *yibum* — the act of intercourse creates the marriage-bond of *yibum* in a way that is almost purely factual rather than formal. Even if the parties did not fully understand the legal significance of the act, the Gemara contemplates that intercourse can create this bond. The woman is thus *de'Oraita* married through *yibum*. But *veino oseh get ad sheyagdil* — the boy cannot issue a *get* until he is an adult. So here is a scenario in which the boy may be legally bound to her through *yibum* and yet his ability to issue a *get* requires separate verification of adulthood — hence the need to check specifically for purposes of *geirushin* even where *kiddushin* was never the question.

### Chalitzah Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לַחֲלִיצָה – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: ״אִישׁ״ כָּתוּב בַּפָּרָשָׁה, אֲבָל אִשָּׁה – בֵּין גְּדוֹלָה וּבֵין קְטַנָּה; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּמַקְּשִׁינַן אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ, דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why it is necessary to mention examining a boy **for** the purpose of ***ḥalitza*:** This is mentioned **to the exclusion of that which Rabbi Yosei** says, **as** Rabbi Yosei **says: "Man,"** i.e., an adult man, **is written in the** Torah **passage** with regard to *ḥalitza*, as the verse states: "And if the man does not wish to take his brother's wife" (Deuteronomy 25:7). **But a woman, whether** she is **an adult or a minor,** can be released by *ḥalitza*, as the verse does not indicate her age. To counter this, Rav Naḥman **teaches us that a woman is juxtaposed to a man** in this passage, indicating that the *yevama* must also have reached adulthood, and the *halakha* is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.**

The statement that one must verify adulthood for *chalitzah* is noted as rejecting the position of Rabbi Yose. The verse governing *chalitzah* uses the word *ha'ish* — the man — in requiring that the male party be an adult, but does not explicitly impose the same requirement on the woman. Rabbi Yose, noting this textual asymmetry, holds that the woman need not be an adult for *chalitzah* to be valid, drawing an analogy to *geirushin*, where the woman is essentially a passive recipient of the *get* and need not be an adult herself. Rabbi Yose reads *chalitzah* similarly: whatever active role the woman plays in removing the sandal, the essential legal actor is the man. The ruling here — that we check *both* parties — rejects Rabbi Yose by equating the woman to the man: *demakshinan isha le'ish, shelo kerabbi yose*.

### Meiun Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמֵיאוּנִין – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיִּרְבֶּה שָׁחוֹר; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why it is necessary to mention examining a person **for** the purpose of stating her **refusal.** This is mentioned **to the exclusion of that which Rabbi Yehuda** says, **as** Rabbi Yehuda **says** that a girl whose mother or brother married her off while she was a minor can nullify her marriage by refusing to remain married, and she can state this refusal **until** she reaches complete maturity, i.e., **when** the area covered by **black** pubic hairs **is greater** than the skin of the genital area. Rav Naḥman therefore **teaches us that** the *halakha* is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** and once a girl has developed two pubic hairs she cannot state her refusal.

The inclusion of *miun* in the list is noted as rejecting the position of Rabbi Yehuda. *Miun* is the right of a girl married off by her brothers or mother — in the absence of her father — to verbally reject her husband while she is still a minor, thereby dissolving the marriage without a *get*. Once she reaches adulthood and remains in the marriage, she requires a *get* to leave. Rabbi Yehuda holds that for purposes of *miun*, adulthood is defined not by the standard criterion of two pubic hairs but by a more developed standard: *ad sheyirbe shachor* — until the dark hair is abundant and, as another Gemara specifies, lies flat against the skin. By grouping the verification for *miun* alongside the standard verifications for *kiddushin* and *geirushin*, the ruling implicitly applies the ordinary definition of adulthood and thereby rejects Rabbi Yehuda's heightened standard: *delo kerabbi yehuda*.

### Age Twenty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִמְכּוֹר בְּנִכְסֵי אָבִיו עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בֶּן עֶשְׂרִים – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר בֶּן שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman states: **But** in order **to sell from the property that** one inherited from **his father,** the seller must be older, and he cannot sell the property **until he is twenty** years **old.** This is mentioned **to the exclusion of** the opinion of the **one who says** that the seller can be **eighteen** years **old.**

Finally, the statement concludes: *ulimkor benichsei aviv ad sheyehe ben esrim* — for selling the father's estate, one must be twenty, and this rejects the position mentioned earlier that eighteen suffices. This provides a clean summary: the verification framework covers not only the personal-status areas of marriage and divorce but also the transactional context of inherited land, and it specifies that the threshold for land sales stands at a higher and distinct level from the physical markers of adulthood.

## Summary Rulings

### Twentieth Year

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: תּוֹךְ זְמַן, כְּלִפְנֵי זְמַן. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּגִידֵּל בַּר מְנַשֶּׁה. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּמָר זוּטְרָא. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּאַמֵּימָר. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל – בְּכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that with regard to the age when a minor can sell property inherited from his father, **during** the **time,** i.e., during his twentieth year, is considered **as before** the **time** when it is permitted, and he cannot sell until the end of his twentieth year. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the ruling that Rava sent to **Giddel bar Menashe,** that a child who has reached his majority and understands the nature of business negotiations can sell land. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the statement of **Mar Zutra,** that one who is not fit to sell land is also not fit to bear witness with regard to land. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the statement of **Ameimar,** that an orphan under the age of twenty can bestow gifts from the property he inherited from his father. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the statement that **Rav Naḥman** says that **Shmuel says with regard to all** the matters that he mentioned.

The Gemara now delivers a concentrated series of final halachic rulings. *Toch zman kilfnei zman* — during the twentieth year, the person is still considered to be under age; one must actually complete the twentieth year and enter the twenty-first. This rules out any argument that once the twentieth year has begun, the person has in some sense "reached" twenty.

### Savvy Exception

The halacha follows Giddel bar Menashya, who held that a person who is demonstrably *yodea be'tiv massa u'mattan* — savvy in the ways of commerce — may have his transactions upheld even before reaching the age of twenty. The age threshold is the default rule; genuine demonstrated business acumen can override it.

### Testimony Split

The halacha follows Mar Zutra's distinction between *metaltelin* and *karka*. A person under twenty whose transactions are valid for *metaltelin* may testify about *metaltelin*, but may not testify about *karka*. This is the combined ruling of the two-criteria framework: formal adulthood via *bar mitzvah*, plus the contextual judgment threshold appropriate to the subject matter.

### Gift Valid

The halacha follows Ameimar: a gift made by a person under twenty is valid, even though his sale of the same property would not be. Whatever the lingering puzzlement about this ruling — and the Gemara does not fully resolve the intuitive difficulty — this is the operative law.

### Verification All

The halacha follows the comprehensive framework of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel across all the cases enumerated: verification of physical adulthood is required for *kiddushin*, *geirushin*, *chalitzah*, and *miun*, and for land sales the threshold is twenty.

## Deathbed Gift

### Rabbi Elazar Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמְחַלֵּק נְכָסָיו עַל פִּיו, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד בָּרִיא וְאֶחָד מְסוּכָּן; נְכָסִים שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהֶן אַחְרָיוּת – נִקְנִין בְּכֶסֶף וּבִשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה, וְשֶׁאֵין לָהֶן אַחְרָיוּת – אֵין נִקְנִין אֶלָּא בִּמְשִׁיכָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who divides his property** between various recipients **by** means of **verbal** instruction, **Rabbi Elazar says:** Both in the case of **one** who is **healthy and** in the case of **one** who is **dangerously ill,** the *halakha* is as follows: **Property that serves as a guarantee,** i.e., land, **is acquired by** means of **money, by a deed** of transfer, **or by taking possession** of it. **And that** which **does not serve as a guarantee,** i.e., movable property, **can be acquired only by pulling.**

The Gemara now arrives at the Mishnah that directly addresses the question that has been lurking throughout the tractate: does a *matnat shechiv meyra* require a formal *kinyan*, or does a verbal declaration suffice? This is, as the Gemara itself will make clear, the very foundation of the institution of *matnat shechiv meyra* as it is understood from the *yeshiva*-world perspective: the great *chiddush* is that *kechisuvim u'mesurim damu* — a *shichiv meyra's* verbal transfer is treated as if the property has already been written over and delivered. Rabbi Elazar, however, simply denies this. He holds that both a healthy person and a person on his deathbed are governed by the same rules: *echad bari ve'echad mesukan* — there is no special category.

### Property Types

According to Rabbi Elazar, the ordinary rules of *kinyan* apply without modification: *nechasim sheyeish lahem achrayut niknin bekesef beshtar uvachazakah* — property with *achrayut* (real property, which can serve as collateral) is acquired through money, a deed, or *chazakah*; and *she'ein lahem achrayut ein niknin ela bimshicha* — property without *achrayut* (movables) is acquired only through *meshicha*, drawing the object toward oneself. No verbal declaration, however sincere and however made on the threshold of death, substitutes for these formal modes of transfer. The consequence is clear: if the person distributes his estate verbally and then dies without a formal *kinyan* having been completed, the transfers are simply void.

This position is, from a traditional learning standpoint, almost heretical in its rejection of an institution the Sages are assumed to have established. The earlier Mishnah had addressed *matnat shechiv meyra* in terms of *chozer* — whether the gift reverts if the person recovers — but had taken for granted that a verbal transfer was at least effective in the first place. Rabbi Elazar rejects even that premise.

### Meroni Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶן רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לַחֲכָמִים: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּמָרוֹנִי אֶחָד שֶׁהָיָה בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְהָיוּ לוֹ מִטַּלְטְלִין הַרְבֵּה וּבִיקֵּשׁ לִיתְּנָם בְּמַתָּנָה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵין לָהֶם תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁיַּקְנֶה עַל גַּב קַרְקַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer said to the Rabbis:** There was **an incident involving a certain** man **of Meron who was in Jerusalem, and he had a lot of movable property. And he desired to give** the movable property **as gifts** to various individuals, but they could not be acquired by pulling. The Rabbis **said to him: There is no remedy for** transferring the property **unless he transfers** the movable property **by means of** transferring the ownership of **land.**

Rabbi Eliezer — and here the Gemara will debate whether this is indeed Rabbi Eliezer or Rabbi Elazar — attempts to support the requirement of a formal *kinyan* by citing a story about a man from Meron who was in Jerusalem with a large amount of movable property and wished to give it all away. The Sages told him: *ein lahem takkanah ad sheyiknem agav karka* — there is no way to effect the transfer properly unless he uses *kinyan agav*, the mechanism by which a person transfers movables along with a piece of land, so that the land serves as the anchor for the transfer. Since *meshicha* would be impractical for vast quantities of livestock and goods, *kinyan agav* was the recommended approach. He accordingly acquired a small plot of land near Jerusalem and transferred the northern portion to one person along with a hundred sheep and a hundred barrels of wine, the southern portion to another with the same, and so on until he had distributed his entire estate. After his death, the Sages upheld his distributions. Rabbi Eliezer reads this as proof that even in the case of a *shichiv meyra*, a formal *kinyan* was necessary.

### Bnei Rochel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּאִמָּן שֶׁל בְּנֵי רוֹכֵל שֶׁהָיְתָה חוֹלָה, וְאָמְרָה: ״תְּנוּ כְּבִינְתִּי לְבִתִּי, וְהִיא בִּשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מָנֶה״, וּמֵתָה, וְקִיְּימוּ אֶת דְּבָרֶיהָ! אָמַר לָהֶן: בְּנֵי רוֹכֵל – תִּקְבְּרֵם אִמָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Elazar: There was **an incident involving the mother of the sons of Rokhel, who was sick, and** who **said: My brooch shall be given to my daughter, and it is** valued **at twelve hundred dinars. And** this woman subsequently **died, and** the Sages **upheld her statement.** This indicates that a person on his deathbed can gift property without an act of acquisition. Rabbi Elazar **said to them:** That case was different; **the sons of Rokhel should be buried by their mother,** i.e., he cursed them. It is not possible to bring a proof from this incident, as these sons were wicked people. Consequently, when ruling in this matter the Sages did not act in accordance with the *halakha*, but allowed the mother of the sons of Rokhel to give this valuable piece of jewelry to their sister without an act of acquisition having been performed.

Against Rabbi Elazar's position, the Sages cite the case of the mother of the *Bnei Rochel* — the children of a man named Rochel. She was ill and said: "Give my necklace to my daughter." The necklace was worth twelve *maneh*, a substantial sum. She died, and the Sages upheld her words — despite the fact that no *kinyan* had been performed. When Rabbi Eliezer is confronted with this case, his response is striking: *bnei rochel tikberem imam* — let the sons of Rochel be buried by their mother. He does not accept the case as proof of a rule; instead, he suggests it was an exceptional outcome driven by the unworthiness of the sons. The necklace was given to the daughter not because the mother's verbal declaration was legally binding, but because the Sages chose not to allow it to go to sons who did not deserve it. The legal norm, Rabbi Eliezer maintains, has not changed.

## Meroni Debate

### Eliezer Version

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָלַךְ וְלָקַח בֵּית סֶלַע אֶחָד סָמוּךְ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאָמַר: צְפוֹנוֹ לִפְלוֹנִי, וְעִמּוֹ מֵאָה צֹאן וּמֵאָה חָבִיּוֹת; וּדְרוֹמוֹ לִפְלוֹנִי, וְעִמּוֹ מֵאָה צֹאן וּמֵאָה חָבִיּוֹת. וָמֵת, וְקִיְּימוּ חֲכָמִים אֶת דְּבָרָיו. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִשָּׁם רְאָיָה?! מָרוֹנִי בָּרִיא הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He went and acquired one** plot of **rocky** land **adjacent to Jerusalem, and** he **said:** I give **the north** part **of** this area **to so-and-so, and with it one hundred sheep and one hundred barrels. And** I give **the south** part **of** the area **to so-and-so, and with it one hundred sheep and one hundred barrels. And he died, and the Sages upheld his statement.** This indicates that a person on his deathbed cannot transfer property without an act of acquisition. The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Eliezer: Do you bring **proof from there?** The man **of Meron was healthy** at the time. This was not the gift of a person on his deathbed, and it could not be acquired by verbal instruction.

The Gemara now unpacks the Meroni case more carefully. Rabbi Eliezer's version of events treats the man from Meron as a *shichiv meyra* who nonetheless needed — and performed — a formal *kinyan*. The very fact that a *kinyan* was done, in Rabbi Eliezer's telling, is what made the distributions valid. This is his proof that even a *shichiv meyra* cannot rely on mere verbal declaration.

### Kinyan Agav

The mechanics of what was done are worth noting. The man used *kinyan agav* — transferring movables by bundling them with a transfer of land. Since performing *meshicha* on hundreds of animals and barrels would be logistically impossible, the *kinyan agav* method allowed the entire transfer to be effected through the formality of the land transaction, with the movables riding along by virtue of being designated together with the land.

### Chachamim Response

The Sages, however, reject Rabbi Eliezer's framing of the Meroni case. *Meroni bari hayah* — the man from Meron was healthy when he made the distributions. He was not a *shichiv meyra* at all. He performed a *kinyan* not because the law of *matnat shechiv meyra* requires it, but simply because as a healthy person making a *matnat bari*, he was subject to ordinary rules of *kinyan*. The reason his distributions were upheld is precisely that he performed a valid *kinyan*. This case therefore proves nothing about what a *shichiv meyra* requires; it is simply a case of an ordinary healthy-person gift that was properly effected. Rabbi Eliezer's version of the story — that the man was a *shichiv meyra* — is disputed by the Sages, who say he was *bari*, and the two sides are in effect arguing about what kind of case this was.

## Thorns Vineyard

### Thorns Kilayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: בְּנֵי רוֹכֵל – תִּקְבְּרֵם אִמָּן וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא קָא לָיֵיט לְהוּ? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְקַיְּימֵי קוֹצִים בַּכֶּרֶם הָיוּ, וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר לְטַעְמֵיהּ – דִּתְנַן: הַמְקַיֵּים קוֹצִים בַּכֶּרֶם, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: קִדֵּשׁ; וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא קִדֵּשׁ, אֶלָּא דָּבָר שֶׁכָּמוֹהוּ מְקַיְּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Rabbi Elazar **said to them:** That case was different; **the sons of Rokhel should be buried by their mother.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that **he was cursing them? Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: They were maintaining thorns in a vineyard** and did not uproot them, **and Rabbi Eliezer** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning, as we learned** in a mishna (*Kilayim* 5:8): With regard to **one who maintains thorns in a vineyard, Rabbi Eliezer says: He has proscribed** the vineyard, rendering it forbidden due to the prohibition against diverse kinds. **And the Rabbis say: Only** growing **a matter,** i.e., a crop, **the like of which** people usually **maintain, proscribes** a vineyard and renders it forbidden.

With the Meroni case resolved, the Gemara returns to Rabbi Eliezer's dismissal of the *bnei rochel* case with the phrase: *mekayamei kotzim bekarem hayu* — "they were those who maintained thorn bushes in their vineyard." This requires explanation. The prohibition of *kilayim* in a vineyard covers the planting or maintenance of mixed species together with grapevines. The question is whether thorn bushes fall within this prohibition at all. A Mishnah elsewhere records a debate: *hamekayem kotzim bekarem, Rabbi Eliezer omer kidesh* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that if one allows thorn bushes to remain in one's vineyard, they become *kadesh*, sanctified as *kilayim*, and become *assur behanaa*, forbidden for any use, constituting a full transgression. *Vehachamim omrim lo kidesh* — the Sages disagree. In their view, *kilayim* applies only to something that people actively cultivate and intend to use as a crop — *davar shekamoho mekayemim*. Thorn bushes, being essentially weeds, do not qualify.

### Arab Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא כַּרְכּוֹם, חֲזֵי; אֶלָּא קוֹצִים, לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? שֶׁכֵּן בַּעֲרַבְיָא מְקַיְּימִין קוֹצִים בַּשָּׂדוֹת לִגְמַלֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** if it were **saffron** that grew in the vineyard, it **is useful** for seasoning and other uses, and therefore it proscribes the vineyard. **But** with regard to **thorns, for what are they useful? Rabbi Ḥanina said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Eliezer?** It is **because in Arabia they maintain thorns in the fields for their camels.** Rabbi Eliezer holds that since thorns are maintained in one place, they are considered useful everywhere.

The Gemara asks what the basis for Rabbi Eliezer's position could be. *Kotzim lemai hazei* — what are thorns good for? If they serve no purpose, how can they be considered a cultivated species giving rise to *kilayim*? Rav Chanina provides the answer: *sheken ba'arvaya mekayemim kotzim besadot legamleihem* — the Arabs specifically maintain thorn bushes in their fields in order to feed them to their camels. Since camels do in fact eat thorns, and there are communities that intentionally cultivate them for this purpose, Rabbi Eliezer holds that thorn bushes are a legitimate crop and their maintenance in a vineyard constitutes *kilayim*.

### Sons Unworthy

With this background, the full logic of Rabbi Eliezer's dismissal of the *bnei rochel* case becomes clear. Rabbi Eliezer looks at the sons through the lens of his own ruling: maintaining thorn bushes in a vineyard is a violation of *kilayim*, making them *resha'im*. It was because of their wickedness — their halachic misconduct — that the Sages upheld the mother's verbal gift to the daughter. The mother's declaration was not legally binding under the normal rules; it was the sons' unworthiness that led the Sages to depart from ordinary legal procedure. The Sages, by contrast, follow their own ruling that thorn bushes do not constitute *kilayim*, and therefore see nothing wrong with the sons' conduct. In their view, the necklace was given to the daughter because the mother's verbal declaration was a valid *matnat shechiv meyra* — proof of the institution that Rabbi Eliezer denies.

## Verbal Kinyan

### Rav Levi Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: קוֹנִין קִנְיָן מִשְּׁכִיב מְרַע, אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. וְלֹא לָחוֹשׁ לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּא תִּטָּרֵף דַּעְתּוֹ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Levi says:** An act of **acquisition may be performed** in order to effect acquisition of property **from a person on his deathbed even on Shabbat,** even though transactions are not performed on Shabbat. **And** this, that an act of acquisition must be performed, is **not** stated in order **to take into consideration the statement of Rabbi Eliezer** that the gifts of any person on his deathbed require an act of acquisition. **Rather,** the reason for this is that if a person on his deathbed requests the performance of an act of acquisition, his request is fulfilled, **lest** his anxiety upon seeing that his will is not being carried out cause him to **lose control of his mind** due to his grief, exacerbating his poor physical state.

The Gemara now states affirmatively, as the prevailing position: *konin kinyan mishechiv meyra* — a *kinyan* can be effected from a *shichiv meyra*. The statement itself is somewhat compressed, but its implication is that the verbal declaration of a *shichiv meyra* functions as a binding *kinyan*; the Sages' position that no formal act is required is adopted as normative.

### Shabbat Kinyan

Rav Levi then extends this principle: *konin kinyan mishechiv meyra afilu be'Shabbat* — one may perform a *kinyan* for a *shichiv meyra* even on *Shabbat*. This is noteworthy because *Shabbat* normally prohibits commercial transactions, and the prohibition is not trivially set aside. The question is: under what circumstances would a *kinyan* even be needed if the Sages have already ruled that a verbal declaration suffices? Recall that the Sages' ruling applies to a *shichiv meyra bekulo* — one who distributes his entire estate. A *shichiv meyra bemiktzat* — one who distributes only part — does require a *kinyan* to make the transfer irrevocable. There is also the consideration that even where the *kinyan* is technically unnecessary, the dying person may insist on one for peace of mind. The Rashbam understands the ruling as applying in both scenarios: whether the *kinyan* is fully necessary (as in the case of *miktzat nechasim*) or only for reassurance (as in *kol nechasim*), it may be performed even on *Shabbat*.

### Tiruf Daas

Why does the concern for *Shabbat* yield? The answer is: *shema titrof daato alav* — lest his mind become disordered. The Gemara's concern is that if the *shichiv meyra* is anxious about whether his dispositions will be legally binding, and that anxiety goes unaddressed, the resulting *tiruf daas* — mental anguish and agitation — may hasten his death. To prevent this, the Sages permit even a rabbinic prohibition of *Shabbat* to be set aside. The *kinyan* is performed not because it is legally necessary for the transfer to work, but because the dying person's psychological state requires it. This is a remarkable instance of rabbinic law yielding to the concrete human need of a specific individual.

### Medical Ethics

Rav Moshe Feinstein extracts a significant principle from this *sugya* and applies it to contemporary medical ethics. Halacha does not ordinarily recognize a robust right of patient autonomy in medical decisions: if one is obligated to preserve life, it is not obvious that a patient may simply refuse treatment that doctors agree is necessary. However, there are cases — such as high-risk procedures with uncertain benefit for a terminal patient — where the calculus of what *pikuach nefesh* requires is itself unclear, and autonomy finds a natural place in that gap. More broadly, Rav Moshe argues that where a patient actively and persistently refuses treatment, forcing the treatment upon them creates a risk of *tiruf daas* that may itself become a *sakanat nefashos* greater than the underlying medical risk. The principle does not ground an absolute right of autonomy; it grounds a *halachic* reason to take the patient's will seriously as a factor in the medical calculus. The basis in the *shichiv meyra* Gemara is this: there, to prevent mental anguish, we override *derabbanans* of *Shabbat*. Rav Moshe extends the principle, while noting — as the Gemara itself leaves implicit — that the *tiruf daas* concern in the Gemara overrides a *derabbanan*, not a *de'Oraita*, and that any extension to Torah-level prohibitions would require additional analysis.

## Shabbat Weekday

### Eliezer Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בַּשַּׁבָּת – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין יָכוֹל לִכְתּוֹב; אֲבָל לֹא בַּחוֹל. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בַּשַּׁבָּת אָמְרוּ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר בַּחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Eliezer says: On Shabbat, the** verbal **statement** of a person on his deathbed **stands, as** he **cannot write,** and the Sages instituted that he can effect the transaction verbally lest the inability to do so exacerbate his condition. **But** a verbal instruction does **not** stand if stated **on a weekday. Rabbi Yehoshua says: With regard to Shabbat,** the Sages **stated** that his verbal instruction is sufficient, even though writing is prohibited. One can infer ***a fortiori*** that the same applies **with regard to a weekday,** when writing is permitted.

The final Mishnah of the daf introduces a position attributed to Rabbi Eliezer that creates a surprising internal tension with the opening Mishnah. Here Rabbi Eliezer says: *b'Shabbat devarav kayamim mipnei she'yachol likhtov, aval lo b'chol* — on *Shabbat* the *shichiv meyra's* verbal declarations are valid, because he cannot write a document; but on a weekday they are not valid. This is a significant qualification. It suggests that Rabbi Eliezer does not categorically reject the institution of *matnat shechiv meyra*; rather, he limits its verbal effectiveness to contexts where writing a document — and thereby performing a formal *kinyan* via *shtar* — is impossible. On *Shabbat*, writing is forbidden, so the verbal declaration must substitute. On a weekday, the option of writing is available, and therefore a verbal declaration alone will not suffice.

### Yehoshua Kal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ – זָכִין לַקָּטָן וְאֵין זָכִין לַגָּדוֹל; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לַקָּטָן אָמְרוּ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, one can acquire** property **on behalf of a minor, but one cannot acquire** property **on behalf of an adult,** since he can perform the act of acquisition himself; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The Sages **stated** this *halakha* **with regard to a minor,** and one may infer ***a fortiori*** that this also applies **with regard to an adult,** who is able to perform the act of acquisition himself.

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees by deploying a *kal vachomer*: *b'Shabbat amru, kal vachomer b'chol* — if the Sages permitted a verbal *kinyan* on *Shabbat*, when *all* forms of *kinyan* are unavailable, then certainly they would permit it on a weekday, when normal *kinyanim* are in full operation. The logic runs in the direction exactly opposite to Rabbi Eliezer's: the availability of formal *kinyanim* is not a reason to disallow the verbal alternative; rather, if the verbal alternative works even in an environment where *kinyanim* are categorically suspended, it works all the more in an environment where the ordinary legal framework is operational. The verbal *kinyan* of a *shichiv meyra* is not a last resort for when nothing else is possible; it is a valid institution in its own right.

### Zakhin Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ – זָכִין לַקָּטָן וְאֵין זָכִין לַגָּדוֹל; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לַקָּטָן אָמְרוּ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, one can acquire** property **on behalf of a minor, but one cannot acquire** property **on behalf of an adult,** since he can perform the act of acquisition himself; this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Yehoshua says:** The Sages **stated** this *halakha* **with regard to a minor,** and one may infer ***a fortiori*** that this also applies **with regard to an adult,** who is able to perform the act of acquisition himself.

The Gemara notes a parallel dispute. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *zakhin l'katan v'ein zakhin l'gadol* — one may acquire a gift on behalf of a minor who is absent, but not on behalf of an adult. His reasoning tracks his position in the *Shabbat* case: the special dispensation applies only where the normal option is unavailable. A minor cannot acquire for himself, so the Sages allow someone else to do it for him; an adult can acquire for himself, so the dispensation is unnecessary and inapplicable. Rabbi Yehoshua inverts this: *l'katan amru, kal vachomer l'gadol* — if the Sages allowed acquisition on behalf of a minor even though the minor himself cannot act, then certainly they would allow acquisition on behalf of an adult, who can perfectly well ratify the transaction and is in effect using the acquirer as his agent. In both disputes, Rabbi Eliezer understands the special rule as a *b'dieved* accommodation for impossible circumstances, while Rabbi Yehoshua understands it as a positive institution that applies most naturally where the principal is a capable legal actor.

### Eliezer Elazar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא – דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בַּחוֹל – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לִכְתּוֹב; אֲבָל לֹא בַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Whose** opinion is expressed in the **mishna?** The Gemara answers: **This is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Meir says** that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to a person on his deathbed who distributes his property by verbal instruction, **on a weekday, his statements stand, because he can write, but** his statement does **not** stand **on Shabbat,** because he cannot write.

This raises a textual problem. If the Rabbi Eliezer of this Mishnah accepts a verbal *matnat shechiv meyra* on *Shabbat*, is he the same person as the Rabbi Elazar of the opening Mishnah, who appeared to deny the institution categorically? Tosafot addresses this directly. Based on other Gemara sources, Tosafot concludes that the Rabbi Eliezer of the opening Mishnah does in fact categorically deny any verbal *matnat shechiv meyra* — whether on *Shabbat* or on a weekday. That absolute position cannot be reconciled with the nuanced *Shabbat*-versus-weekday distinction of this Mishnah. Tosafot therefore concludes that these must be two different Tannaim: the Rabbi Elazar of the opening Mishnah — spelled with an *ayin* — who absolutely denies the institution, and the Rabbi Eliezer of this Mishnah — spelled with an *alef* — who accepts it at least for *Shabbat*.

### Yerushalmi Version

The Yerushalmi preserves a different *girsah* — a different textual reading — of the Mishnah that distinguishes between *Shabbat* and weekdays. In the Yerushalmi's version, the position that verbal transfer is valid on *Shabbat* but not on a weekday is attributed not to Rabbi Eliezer at all, but to *vehachamim omrim* — the Sages. This creates a three-way picture. One view categorically denies verbal *matnat shechiv meyra* (Rabbi Elazar of the opening Mishnah, in Tosafot's reading). A second view accepts it on *Shabbat* but not on a weekday (the Sages in the Yerushalmi's reading, or Rabbi Eliezer in the Bavli's reading of this Mishnah). And the third, dominant view accepts it as a fully operative institution on *Shabbat* and weekday alike (Rabbi Yehoshua, and by implication the normative halacha). Whether this yields two distinct positions or three depends on how one reads the Tannaic attributions — a question the Gemara leaves open for the continuation of the sugya.
