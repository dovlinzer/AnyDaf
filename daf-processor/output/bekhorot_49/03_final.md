# Bekhorot 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Wives, One Husband

### Two wives, two males

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁלֹּא בִּכֵּרוּ וְיָלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — נוֹתֵן עֲשָׂרָה סְלָעִים לַכֹּהֵן. מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אִם לְכֹהֵן אֶחָד נָתַן — יַחֲזִיר לוֹ חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים, וְאִם לִשְׁנֵי כֹּהֲנִים נָתַן — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהוֹצִיא מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **two wives** of one man, both of **whom had not** previously **given birth, and they gave birth** to **two males,** i.e., each bore one male, and the sons were intermingled, the father **gives ten *sela*** coins **to the priest** even if it is unknown which son was born first, because it is certain that each is firstborn of his mother. In a case where **one of them dies within thirty days** of birth, **if he gave** all ten *sela* coins **to one priest,** the priest **must return five *sela* to him,** because the father was not obligated to redeem the son who then died. **And if he gave** the redemption payment **to two** different **priests, he cannot reclaim** the money **from the possession** of either priest, as each could claim that the money that he received was for the living child.

The daf begins with the mishnah on 48b, continuing the discussion of *pidyon haben* and the complications that arise when the identity of the *bechor* is uncertain. The mishnah opens with the case of *shtei nashim shelo bichru* — two wives of the same husband, neither of whom has yet given birth. Each wife now gives birth to a male; since each child is a potential firstborn, there would ordinarily be two obligations of *pidyon haben*. The husband gives ten *sela'im* to a *kohen* — five for each child, since a *sela* is equivalent to a *shekel*.

### Death within 30 days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה, אוֹ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבָה — נוֹתֵן חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים לַכֹּהֵן; שְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת וְזָכָר, אוֹ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּשְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת — אֵין כָּאן לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If one mother gave birth to **a male and** one gave birth to **a female,** or if between them they gave birth to **two males and** one **female,** and the children were intermingled, the father **gives five *sela*** coins **to the priest:** In the first case because the male might have preceded the female and in the second case because one of the males is certainly firstborn. If the children were **two females and a male, or two males and two females, the priest has nothing here,** as it is possible the female was born first to each mother.

The mishnah then introduces a tragic complication: one of the two boys dies within thirty days. Since the obligation of *pidyon haben* does not begin until the thirtieth day — as derived from the verse *ufduyav miben chodesh tifdeh* — a death within that window cancels the obligation for that child. The precise parameters of giving the money before day thirty will be addressed later in the daf, but the mishnah for now proceeds on the assumption that such early payment can be valid.

### One vs. two Kohanim

If the husband had given both payments to the same *kohen*, that *kohen* must return five *sela'im*, since only one child is now obligated. However, if he had given the payments to two separate *kohanim* without specifying which payment was for which child, he cannot recover from either one. Each *kohen* will simply claim that he received payment for the child who survived, not the one who died, and since the father himself did not know which payment corresponded to which child at the time of giving, neither *kohen* can be compelled to return anything. This outcome reflects the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*: in civil matters, the burden of proof lies with the one seeking to extract money from another.

### Mixed gender births

The mishnah continues with further scenarios of uncertainty. If the two wives gave birth to a boy and a girl — or to two boys and a girl — and it is unknown which mother bore which child, there may be only one *bechor*, or possibly none at all. In the case of a boy and a girl where identity is confused, the father gives only five *sela'im* to one *kohen*, designating the payment for whichever boy turns out to be the firstborn. In the case of two girls and either one or two boys, it is entirely possible that each mother gave birth to a girl first, yielding no *bechor* at all. In that case, *ein kan lakohen klum* — the *kohen* receives nothing.

The underlying principle here is striking: the obligation of *pidyon haben* is treated entirely as a civil liability. If the debt cannot be established through civil procedure, there is no residual obligation to perform the *mitzvah* on its own. One might raise the objection that a father ought to give the money *matanah al menat lehachzir* — as a gift on condition that it be returned — in order to fulfill the ritual act of *pidyon* even without sustaining a financial loss. Indeed, there is a *gemara* elsewhere that holds that *matanah al menat lehachzir* does satisfy the *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben*. If that is so, one might argue that even where no civil liability can be established, the ritual act itself ought to be performed. The answer implicit in the mishnah is that without an underlying debt, there is no *mitzvah* at all — not even the ritual act. The *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben* is entirely a function of the civil obligation, and where that obligation cannot be sued for, the *mitzvah* simply does not exist.

### One experienced wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַחַת בִּכֵּרָה וְאַחַת שֶׁלֹּא בִּכֵּרָה, וְיָלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — נוֹתֵן חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים לַכֹּהֵן, מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶן בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — הָאָב פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If **one** of his wives **had** previously **given birth and one had not** previously **given birth and** they **gave birth** to **two males** who became intermingled, the father **gives five *sela*** coins **to the priest,** as it is certain that one of them was born to the mother who had not yet given birth. If **one of them dies within thirty days** of birth **the father is exempt** from that payment, as it is possible that the one who died was born to the mother who had not yet given birth.

The mishnah closes this section with a case of *achat bichra v'achat shelo bichra* — one wife who has previously given birth and one who has not — both married to the same man. Even if the identities of the children are confused, the wife who has never given birth has definitively produced a firstborn male. Therefore, regardless of which child is biologically hers, the father is obligated in *pidyon*. He gives five *sela'im* to a *kohen* for whichever boy turns out to be the firstborn of the previously childless wife. If one of the two boys then dies within thirty days, the father may recover the money from the *kohen*, since the child who died may have been the firstborn of the previously childless wife, removing the obligation. In the case of a boy and a girl born to these two wives — where identity is confused — the *kohen* receives nothing, because it is entirely possible that the previously childless wife gave birth to the girl first.

The mishnah also addresses the scenario where the father dies while the sons survive. Drawing on the preceding discussion, if the boys themselves seek to claim a double portion of the estate, each will argue that he is the *bechor*; but when the obligation to pay *pidyon* arises, each will argue the opposite. The *gemara* had addressed this tension previously, noting that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda debate the conditions under which the lien on the estate enables collection even after the estate has been divided.

## Two Wives, Two Men

### Setup: mixed paternity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים שֶׁל שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים שֶׁלֹּא בִּכֵּרוּ, וְיָלְדוּ שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — זֶה נוֹתֵן חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים לַכֹּהֵן וְזֶה נוֹתֵן חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **two women who had not** previously **given birth,** who were married **to two** different **men, and they gave birth** to **two males** and the sons were intermingled, **this** father **gives five *sela*** coins **to a priest and that** father **gives five *sela*** coins to a priest, as each is certainly firstborn to his mother.

The mishnah now turns to a more complex scenario: *shtei nashim shel shenei anashim shelo bicharu* — two wives of two different men, neither of whom has previously given birth. Each wife bears a male child, so each father is separately obligated in *pidyon haben*. However, the children have become confused, so neither father knows which child is his. Despite this confusion, each father knows that his wife has given birth to a firstborn male, and therefore each gives five *sela'im* — *zeh noten chamesh sela'im vezeh noten chamesh sela'im*.

### Single Kohen payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶם בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים — אִם לַכֹּהֵן אֶחָד נָתָנוּ, יַחֲזִיר לָהֶם חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים. אִם לִשְׁנֵי כֹּהֲנִים נָתָנוּ — אֵינָן יְכוֹלִין לְהוֹצִיא מִיָּדָם.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **one of them dies within thirty** days of birth, **if** the fathers **gave** all ten *sela* coins **to one priest,** the priest **must return five *sela*** coins **to them. But if they gave** the redemption payment **to two** different **priests they cannot reclaim** the money **from the possession of** either priest, as each could claim that the money that he received was for the living child.

If one of the children then dies within thirty days, one of the fathers is no longer obligated. But since the children's identities are confused, neither father knows whether it is his child who died. If both fathers had given their payments to the same *kohen*, the *kohen* must return five *sela'im* — that much is clear, since he is holding ten and is only entitled to five. The difficulty is: which father receives the refund? The *kohen* can push off each father individually, saying to each that perhaps the other father is the one owed the refund.

### Power of attorney

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבָא בְּהַרְשָׁאָה.
>
> **Translation:** This is referring to a case **where** one of the fathers **comes with authorization** to act on behalf of the other father to state his claim for him, and therefore the priest cannot reject his claim. But if they gave the money to two different priests an authorization is of no effect, as each priest can claim the other took the redemption money of the son who died.

The *gemara* resolves this through the mechanism of *harsha'ah* — power of attorney. One father grants a power of attorney to the other, so that the second father can present himself to the *kohen* and say: you owe either me or the other father — and I hold the other father's power of attorney. Since the *kohen* indisputably owes five *sela'im* to one of the two, he cannot push off a claimant who holds power of attorney for both. This is the ruling of Shmuel: *be-bar harsha'ah* — the solution works through the instrument of power of attorney.

The introduction of *harsha'ah* prompts a broader discussion of its halakhic parameters. *Harsha'ah* is a challenging concept in *halacha* because *halacha* tends to operate with concrete, physical transfers rather than abstract legal instruments. The mechanism by which it functions is understood as a kind of transfer of title: when I give you my power of attorney, I am effectively transferring to you the ownership of the claims — the rights that others owe to me. Because this involves a conceptual transfer of title rather than a transfer of a physical object, there are limits.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי: לָא כָּתְבִינַן אַדְרַכְתָּא אַמִּטַּלְטְלִי! הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּכַפְרֵיהּ, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא כַּפְרֵיהּ — כָּתְבִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't** the Sages **of Neharde'a say: We do not write an authorization document [*adrakhta*] concerning movable property?** Therefore, in the case of redemption, where money, which has the status of movable property, is demanded from the priest, an authorization document may not be used. The Gemara answers: **This statement,** that one does not write authorization for movable property, applies only **when** the respondent, in this case the priest, already **denied** the claim against him. **But** in a case where the respondent **did not** yet **deny** the claim against him **we write** authorization even for movable property. In the case of redemption, although the priest claims the one issuing the claim against him is not the father of the son who died, he does not deny that he received the money.

The *Nehardei* — the scholars of Nehardea — rule: *lo katvinan adrachta a-metalteli* — we do not write a power of attorney (*adrachta* being the Aramaic equivalent of *harsha'ah*) on chattel; we write it only on real estate. The reason is as follows. If someone owes me money in the form of real estate — say, they are sitting on my land — I remain in conceptual control of that land even if they are physically present on it, because land cannot be hidden or moved. Chattel, however, can be taken away, concealed, and removed from my sphere of control. If a thief has taken my movable property and carried it off, I cannot sell it, I cannot consecrate it — it is outside my control. Since the *harsha'ah* mechanism depends on my being able to transfer something I control, I cannot transfer power of attorney over chattel that has left my possession.

The *gemara* then distinguishes: *hani mili heicha dechafrei* — this limitation applies where the other party is denying that he owes the money. If he denies the obligation, then the chattel in question is genuinely beyond my reach and I have no ability to transfer title to it. However, *aval heicha delo kafrei* — where the *kohen* is not denying the debt but only unclear as to whom he owes it, the situation is different. Here the *kohen* himself admits he owes five *sela'im* to one of the two fathers. Since he admits a debt exists — even if he does not know to whom — the money is considered conceptually accessible to the potential creditor, enough to enable a transfer of rights. Therefore, even though this involves chattel rather than real estate, the *harsha'ah* is effective.

*Tosafot* notes, both here and at length in Bava Kamma, that in practice the *Geonim* and *Rishonim* expanded the limits set by the *gemara*, developing the ability to write *harsha'ah* even where the other party denies the debt and even on chattel. This reflects a broader phenomenon in *dinei mamonot* where the economic realities of developing societies required *halacha* to adapt categories that had been formulated under more limited conditions. *Tosafot* attempts to explain on what grounds this expansion was halakhically justified. This is one illustration of a recurring tension in *halacha*: the tradition operates with concrete, physical categories, but modern economic and legal life requires instruments — corporations, intellectual property, abstract powers of attorney — that have no direct precedent in the *gemara*. How that gap is bridged in the post-*gemara* period is its own significant area of inquiry.

### Two Kohanim payment

If both fathers gave their payments to two separate *kohanim*, however, neither father can recover from either *kohen*. Each *kohen* will simply claim that the payment he received was for the surviving child, not the one who died, and since neither father can disprove this, neither *kohen* is compelled to return anything.

### Mixed genders, two men

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — הָאָבוֹת פְּטוּרִין, וְהַבֵּן חַיָּיב לִפְדּוֹת אֶת עַצְמוֹ; שְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת וְזָכָר, אוֹ שְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת וּשְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — אֵין כָּאן לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** If the women gave birth to **a male and a female** and the children became intermingled, **the fathers are exempt,** as each could claim that he is the father of the female, **but the son is obligated to redeem himself,** as he is certainly a firstborn. If **two females and a male** were born, **or two females and two males, the priest has nothing here,** as it is possible the female was born first to each mother.

The mishnah continues: if one wife bore a boy and the other a girl, and the identities are confused, the fathers are exempt — *ha'avot peturim* — because each father can say that perhaps his wife bore the girl, not the boy, and he has no *bechor*. However, the son who was born first to the wife who had never previously given birth knows that he is obligated in *pidyon haben*, even though he does not know who his father is. Since his paternity is in doubt but his status as a *bechor* is not, the *gemara* rules: *haben chayav lifdot et atzmo* — he must redeem himself when he reaches the appropriate age.

If there are two girls and one or two boys, with identity confused, then it is possible that neither mother bore a firstborn son — each may have given birth to a girl first. In that case the *kohen* receives nothing, the fathers give nothing, and the sons themselves cannot be certain they are firstborns.

This section of the mishnah is followed by a compressed presentation of the case where one of the two wives has previously given birth and the other has not. Since the mother who has never previously given birth has definitely produced a firstborn male, *ze shelo becherah ishto noten chamesh sela'im lakohen* — the husband of the previously childless wife gives five *sela'im* to the *kohen*. In the case of a boy and a girl where identity is confused, since it is possible the previously childless wife bore the girl first, *ein lakohen klum* — the *kohen* receives nothing.

A question arises regarding the sons themselves: if they reach the age of bar mitzvah and neither knows whether he is the *bechor*, can either be compelled to give *pidyon haben*? Since the *kohanim* cannot prove which son is obligated, neither can be sued, and therefore neither is obligated. This too reflects the governing principle: where the civil liability cannot be established, the *mitzvah* does not attach.

### Chattel vs. real estate

The preceding discussion of *harsha'ah* has already addressed the distinction between chattel and real estate in the context of transferring power of attorney. As noted, the *Nehardei* limit the writing of a formal *harsha'ah* to real estate precisely because real estate remains under the conceptual control of its owner even when another party occupies it, whereas chattel taken away or denied is beyond the owner's reach. The exception — where the other party admits a debt but disputes to whom it is owed — permits *harsha'ah* even on chattel, since the admission of the debt restores sufficient conceptual access to enable the transfer.

### Rav Huna's cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא רַב הוּנָא: שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבָה — אֵין כָּאן לַכֹּהֵן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this case **Rav Huna teaches:** If they gave birth to **two males and a female the priest has nothing here,** despite the fact that one of them is definitely a firstborn, as each father can claim that his firstborn is the female. In addition, the sons are exempt as well, since each can claim that the female was his sister and born first.

The mishnah had ended the section on two wives of two separate husbands by stating only that if a boy and a girl are born and identity is confused, *ein kan lakohen klum*. Rav Huna adds a further case: *shenei zacharim u'nekeivah ein kan lakohen klum* — even two boys and a girl yields a case where the *kohen* receives nothing. In this scenario — two wives of two different husbands, neither of whom has previously given birth — each husband can claim that his wife bore the girl first and he has no obligation. It remains possible that the two boys were born to one mother and the girl to the other, and that the girl was the firstborn of the wife with no prior children.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא דִּידַן, כֵּיוָן דְּבִשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים הוּא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד וּשְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — לָא מִתְּנֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the *tanna* of our** mishna, why does he not state this case? The Gemara answers: **Since you find** this ruling **that** they are entirely exempt in a case **where** the women are married to **two men,** but **you do not find it** in a case **of one man and two** of his **wives,** as a firstborn was definitely born to that man and he must give five *sela* coins to a priest, the *tanna* **does not teach** the case of two women and two men either. The reason is that stylistically, the *tanna* prefers to teach the ruling: The priest has nothing here, only when the *halakha* is identical in a case of two wives of two men and a case of two wives of one man.

The *gemara* then asks: why did the Tanna of the mishnah not include this case? The answer is that this case — two boys and a girl with *kohen* receiving nothing — is only applicable in the scenario of two wives of two different husbands. It does not arise in the case of one man and two wives, because if one man has two wives who each bore a child, one of them definitively bore a male firstborn — the obligation exists. The Tanna therefore omitted the two-boys-and-a-girl scenario because it has no parallel in the one-husband cases that preceded it, and the mishnah had already illustrated the principle with the simpler case of a boy and a girl.

## Death after 30 days

### Death before day 30

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵת הַבֵּן בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנָּתַן לַכֹּהֵן — יַחְזִיר. לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָתַן — יִתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **the** firstborn **son dies within thirty days** of birth, **although** the father **gave** five *sela* **to the priest,** the priest **must return** it. If the firstborn son dies **after thirty days** have passed, **even if** the father **did not give** five *sela* coins to the priest **he must give** it then.

The next mishnah states the rule in its clearest form: *meis haben betoch shloshim yom af al pi she'nasan lakohen yachzir* — if the son died within thirty days, even though the money has already been given to the *kohen*, the *kohen* must return it. The *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben* does not take effect until the thirtieth day, and a death before that point means no obligation ever crystallized.

### Death after day 30

Conversely: *le'achar shloshim yom af al pi shelo nasan yiten* — if the child died after thirty days, even if the father has not yet given the money, he must still give it. One might think that since the child is now gone, there is no longer a living firstborn on whose behalf the *mitzvah* could be performed. The mishnah rules otherwise: once day thirty-one arrived, the father became obligated in *pidyon haben* at that moment. The subsequent death of the child does not undo an obligation that already took effect. The *pidyon* is thus not contingent on the child being alive at the time of payment; it became an irrevocable debt at the moment the thirty-day threshold was crossed.

### Shehecheyanu paradox

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question in this connection. The practice among *Ashkenazim* is to recite the *bracha* of *shehecheyanu* at *pidyon haben* — unlike at *bris milah*, where *Ashkenazim* do not recite *shehecheyanu* (though *Sephardim* do, and the reason *Ashkenazim* omit it at *bris milah* is itself difficult to explain). *Tosafot* then considers the scenario described by this mishnah: the child has died after thirty days, the father is still obligated to perform the *pidyon haben*, and he would, following this ruling, presumably still recite *shehecheyanu* — a blessing of thanksgiving over a joyous occasion — even as he mourns his deceased child. *Tosafot* acknowledges the painful irony of this situation without resolving it away.

## Day 30 status

### Rav Gidel ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵת בְּיוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים — כְּיוֹם שֶׁלְּפָנָיו. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אִם נָתַן — לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם לֹא נָתַן — לֹא יִתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** If the firstborn **dies on the thirtieth day,** that day's halakhic status is **like** that of the **day that preceded it,** as the obligation takes effect only after thirty days have elapsed. **Rabbi Akiva says:** If the firstborn dies on the thirtieth day it is a case of uncertainty; therefore, **if** the father already **gave** the redemption payment to the priest **he cannot take** it back, **but if he did not** yet **give** payment **he does not** need to **give** it.

What is the status of day thirty itself? Rav Gidel rules: *meis be'yom shloshim ke'yom she'lefanav* — if the child dies on day thirty, it is treated like the previous day, meaning the obligation has not yet taken effect. The verse says *miben chodesh* — from a month old — which Rav Gidel understands to mean that the child must have entered his second month, i.e., day thirty-one has begun.

### Rabbi Akiva doubt

Rabbi Akiva is uncertain about the status of day thirty. He rules: *im nasan lo yital* — if the father has already given the money to the *kohen*, the *kohen* need not return it, since day thirty may already be considered "a month old." *Ve'im lo nasan, lo yitein* — if the father has not yet given, he need not give. Rabbi Akiva's position is thus a pure *safek*: since day thirty is of ambiguous status, whoever currently holds the money retains it.

### Bamidbar derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? גָּמְרִי ״חֹדֶשׁ״ ״חֹדֶשׁ״ מִמִּדְבָּר, מָה הָתָם ״וָמַעְלָה״ אַף הָכָא נָמֵי וָמַעְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of the Rabbis,** i.e., the first *tanna*, who hold that the mitzva of redemption applies only after thirty days have elapsed? The Gemara explains that **they derive** the meaning of the term **"month"** stated in this context by means of a verbal analogy **from** the meaning of the term **"month"** stated in the context of the redemption of the Israelite firstborn in **the wilderness** via the Levites. **Just as there,** with regard to the redemption through the Levites, it is stated: "Number all the firstborn males of the children of Israel from a month old **and upward"** (Numbers 3:40), i.e., after thirty days, **so too here,** with regard to the mitzva of redemption for future generations, where it states: "From a month old you shall redeem" (Numbers 18:16), the requirement: **And upward,** applies **as well,** i.e., only after thirty days.

The *gemara* asks: what is the basis for the rabbinic position? The answer is *gemiri chodesh chodesh mimisbar* — it is learned by analogy from the opening of Bamidbar, where it says *miben chodesh vama'alah*, counting from a month and above. The word "and above" indicates that the count begins only when the child has passed beyond a month — i.e., starting on day thirty-one. The same phrase appears in our context and therefore carries the same meaning.

### Two verses principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ, מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב ״וָמַעְלָה״ גַּבֵּי עֲרָכִין, וְלָא גָּמְרִי מִמִּדְבָּר — הָווּ לְהוּ שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְּתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד — אֵין מְלַמְּדִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva is uncertain** in this regard, as one could claim: **From** the fact **that it was necessary** for the verse **to write "and upward" with regard to** the mitzva of **valuations:** "And if it be from sixty years old and upward" (Leviticus 27:7), **and** it **is not derived from** the redemption of the Israelite firstborns in the **wilderness** that the phrase "from…years old" means "and upward," one can conclude the following: The redemption of the firstborn in the wilderness and valuations **are two verses that come as one,** i.e., to teach the same matter. **And** there is a principle that **any two verses that come as one do not teach** their common *halakha* to other cases.

Rabbi Akiva's uncertainty stems from a specific textual observation. The phrase "a month and above" appears not only in the context of the census in Bamidbar but also in the context of *erchin* — the system of valuations established in the Torah, where the value assigned to a person of one month and above is given. If the rule could have been derived from the Bamidbar census, why did the Torah need to repeat "and above" in the context of *erchin*? The principle *shnei kesuvim haba'im ke'echad ein melamdin* — two verses that come to teach the same thing do not generate a general rule — suggests that perhaps these two cases are exceptions rather than the norm. If they were the norm, one verse would suffice; the need for two verses implies they are anomalous, and the standard rule may therefore be that day thirty is already considered a full month.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא: כִּי אֵין מְלַמְּדִין — לְעָלְמָא, אֲבָל לְגוּפַיְיהוּ — מְלַמְּדִין, וּמִשּׁוּם הָכִי מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** one could say: **When** do two verses that come as one **not teach** their common *halakha*? That is **with regard to general** *halakhot*, i.e., entirely different areas of *halakha*. **But with regard to themselves,** i.e., similar cases, **they do teach.** If so, one should derive permanent *halakha* of redemption of firstborns from the redemption of the firstborn in the wilderness. **And** it is **due to that** reason that Rabbi Akiva is **uncertain** concerning a firstborn on his thirtieth day.

The *gemara* then raises the counter-possibility: *aval legufaihu melamdin* — the principle that two verses cannot extend a rule to other areas does not prevent each verse from informing cases closely related to its own subject matter. The Bamidbar census of *bechorim* for the purpose of redeeming them with the *Leviim* is structurally similar to our *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben*, even though it was a unique historical event. Perhaps the Bamidbar verse may inform our *mitzvah* precisely because they share the same fundamental subject. Rabbi Akiva is therefore genuinely uncertain: *u'mishum hachi misapkinan lehu* — this unresolved doubt is why he treats day thirty as ambiguous and leaves the money in the hands of whoever holds it.

One additional observation: the *gemara* throughout this discussion takes it for granted that a month means thirty days. Given that an average lunar month is approximately twenty-nine and a half days, one might have thought the threshold was twenty-nine days. The *gemara* consistently rounds upward, reflecting the assumption that a full month counts as thirty days. Whether one could technically perform *pidyon haben* after exactly thirty twenty-four-hour periods, rather than waiting until halakhic day thirty-one, is a question addressed in the later *poskim*.

## Viability & mourning

### Rava's consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים לְעִנְיַן אֲבֵילוּת, יוֹם שְׁלֹשִׁים כְּיוֹם שֶׁלְּפָנָיו. וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי הַמֵּיקֵל בְּאֵבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: All concede with regard to mourning** that **the thirtieth day is like the preceding day,** i.e., if the son died on the thirtieth day it is considered as though he died on the day before and he has the status of a stillborn, and the rites of mourning are not observed. **And** the reason is as **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of the** more **lenient** authority **in** matters relating to **mourning.**

Rava introduces an important clarification: *hakol modim le'inyan aveilus yom shloshim ke'yom she'lefanav* — all authorities agree that for the purposes of *aveilus*, day thirty is treated like day twenty-nine, i.e., as still within the first month. This is because the rule *halakha ke'divrei ha'meikel be'avel* — in matters of mourning, we follow the lenient opinion — means that even Rabbi Akiva, who is uncertain whether day thirty already qualifies as "a month old" for *pidyon haben*, would agree to leniency in the context of *aveilus*. A parent whose child dies on day thirty would not observe *shiva*.

### Full-term babies

Rava's statement is followed by discussion of viability and the status of day thirty in establishing whether a child is viable. The *gemara* draws a distinction regarding viability. If a baby is known to be full-term, it is considered viable from the moment of birth, and even if it dies on the first day, the parents observe *shiva* and all the laws of *aveilus* apply. If, however, there is uncertainty whether the baby was full-term — for instance, whether it may have been a *neifel* — then the child must survive thirty days for viability to be established. If such a child dies before thirty days have elapsed, the parents do not observe *shiva*. If it survives to day thirty-one, it is established as viable and all *aveilus* obligations apply. The status of day thirty in this context is the subject of Rava's statement: all agree it is treated like the previous day, and parents would not observe *shiva*.

### Viability vs. mitzva

*Rashi* on the mishnah suggests that the reason *pidyon haben* does not apply until thirty days is because of the *neifel* concern — the child may not have been viable. *Tosafot* disputes this. The thirty-day requirement for *pidyon haben* is not a function of establishing viability; it is an independent halakhic rule derived from the verse *miben chodesh*. The evidence for this is that even a child known with certainty to be full-term and healthy — where there is no *neifel* concern whatsoever — cannot have *pidyon haben* performed before day thirty-one. The thirty-day wait for viability determination and the thirty-day threshold for *pidyon haben* are therefore two distinct halakhic categories that happen to share the same number, but operate on entirely different principles.

This distinction has practical consequences. One might think that since Rabbi Akiva treats day thirty as potentially satisfying the *pidyon haben* requirement, and yet Rava says all agree day thirty is like the previous day for *aveilus*, there could be a case where a *kohen* retains money for *pidyon haben* even as the parents do not observe *shiva* — because the two *halakhot* track different criteria. The thirty-day period for *aveilus* is about establishing viability in cases of doubt, and we are lenient there; the thirty-day period for *pidyon haben* is about when the *mitzvah* becomes operative, and Rabbi Akiva thinks that may already be day thirty.

## Father dies

### Before day 30

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מֵת הָאָב בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים — בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדָּה, עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁנִּפְדָּה; לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — בְּחֶזְקַת שֶׁנִּפְדָּה, עַד שֶׁיֹּאמְרוּ לוֹ שֶׁלֹּא נִפְדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **the father** of the firstborn **dies within thirty** days of birth **the presumptive status of** the son is **that he was not redeemed, until** the son **will bring proof that he was redeemed.** If the father dies **after thirty days** have passed **the presumptive status of** the son is **that he was redeemed, until** people **will tell him that he was not redeemed.**

If the father dies before day thirty and the son, upon reaching adulthood, does not know whether his father performed *pidyon haben*, he operates under the presumption *be'chezkat shelo nifdah* — he should assume he has not been redeemed. It would be most unusual for a father to perform *pidyon haben* before day thirty, and in the absence of information to the contrary, the son must assume he is unredeemed and must redeem himself. This presumption holds *ad she'yavi ra'ayah she'nifdah* — until he can bring proof that he has been redeemed.

### After day 30

If the father dies after day thirty, the presumption reverses: *be'chezkat she'nifdah* — the son should assume that he has been redeemed. The father lived to see day thirty-one, the obligation took effect, and we assume he fulfilled it at the earliest opportunity. This assumption holds unless people inform him that he was not redeemed: *ad she'yomru lo shelo nifdah*.

### Types of proof

The mishnah uses different language in the two cases: in the first, the son must *bring proof* that he was redeemed; in the second, *people must tell him* he was not. Rashi explains the asymmetry: proof of the absence of an event is inherently difficult to produce. If a *kohen* says "I never received payment from your father," that is not necessarily conclusive evidence — there are many *kohanim*. Therefore, the mishnah does not ask for formal *ra'ayah* in the case of presumed redemption, but instead envisions a scenario such as the father having stated on his deathbed that he had never redeemed his son. That kind of testimony — not a formal evidentiary proof, but a credible statement about an absence — is what *yomru lo* encompasses.

## Son's redemption

### Dual obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּ לִפְדּוֹת וּבְנוֹ לִפְדּוֹת — הוּ קוֹדֵם לִבְנוֹ; רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּנוֹ קוֹדְמוֹ, שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ עַל אָבִיו וּמִצְוַת בְּנוֹ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** If one had both **himself to redeem and his son to redeem, his** own redemption **takes precedence over** that of **his son. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The redemption of **his son takes precedence, as the mitzva** to redeem the father is incumbent **upon his** own **father, and the mitzva** to redeem **his son** is incumbent **upon him.**

The mishnah presents a case that arises in practice more often than one might expect: *hu lifdot u'beno lifdot* — a man who was never himself redeemed by his father now has his own firstborn son. He faces two obligations simultaneously: to redeem himself and to redeem his son. The question is one of priority.

### Self comes first

The mishnah rules: *hu kodem li'veno* — he takes care of himself first. The implication seems to be that his own *mitzvah* of redemption is the primary one, and his obligation to his son is secondary.

### Child comes first

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *beno kodmo* — his son comes first. The reason is *she'mitzvato al aviv* — the *mitzvah* of redeeming him was his father's *mitzvah*, and therefore his own redemption is derived from his father's obligation, not his own. *U'mitzvat beno alav* — by contrast, his *mitzvah* to redeem his son is his own primary *mitzvah*. Rabbi Yehuda therefore holds that the father's primary *mitzvah* is to redeem his son, and his own redemption is something he inherits, as it were, from the unfulfilled obligation of his own father. His son's *pidyon* therefore takes priority.

### Practical memory issues

A significant practical difficulty underlies all these cases: who will remember? If a father dies young, leaving an infant son who has never been redeemed, who will recall thirteen years later, when the boy reaches bar mitzvah, that he still needs to redeem himself? The *Acharonim* discuss various solutions, including fashioning a piece of jewelry or a bracelet that the child wears bearing some indication of his unredeemed status, so that when he reaches maturity the matter will not be forgotten. Beyond this, the question arises whether a *beit din* can act on behalf of a minor child and arrange his *pidyon* before he comes of age. Two possible grounds exist: first, since the *pidyon* is in the child's own interest, perhaps an agent can be appointed for him even though he is a minor; second, perhaps the community at large bears some responsibility to ensure *pidyon haben* is performed. This remains a debated question among the *poskim*.

### Artificial insemination

These questions about communal obligation and *beit din* intervention arise acutely in contemporary cases involving artificial insemination. Where the sperm donor is unknown — typically assumed to be non-Jewish — and the halakhic status of the donor as father is in doubt, the practical result is that the husband of the mother may not be considered the child's halakhic father for purposes of *pidyon haben*. There is no father to perform the *mitzvah*, and no one to remember it in thirteen years. Some *poskim* permit the *beit din* to appoint the mother's husband as agent — not as father, but as representative of the *beit din* or of the child — to perform the *pidyon haben* while the child is still young, before it is forgotten. Where the husband donates his own sperm for his wife, the question of whether he is considered the halakhic father for purposes of *pidyon haben* is more complex, and there is considerably more reason to regard him as fully the child's father in that case.

## Early redemption

### Rav's view

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַפּוֹדֶה אֶת בְּנוֹ בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, רַב אָמַר: בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי. דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ — אֵין בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי, לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם וְאִיתַנְהוּ לְמָעוֹת — וַדַּאי בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It **was stated:** In the case of **one who redeems his** firstborn **son within thirty days** of his birth by giving a priest five *sela* coins, **Rav says his son is redeemed and Shmuel says his son is not redeemed.** The Gemara explains: **Everyone** agrees that if the father said: He is redeemed **from now,** that **his son is not redeemed,** as the obligation to redeem the son is not yet in effect. Likewise, if the father says the redemption should take effect **after thirty days, and the money is** still **there,** in the possession of the priest, after thirty days, **his son is certainly redeemed,** as the money is in the priest's possession when the obligation of redemption comes into effect.

The *gemara* now addresses the case — implicit in the mishnah throughout — of *hapodeh et beno betoch shloshim yom*: a father who gives the *pidyon haben* money to a *kohen* before day thirty. The mishnah has assumed in various cases that such early payment can be valid. Now the *gemara* considers the precise parameters. Rav rules: *beno paduy* — the son is redeemed; the early payment is effective.

### Shmuel's view

Shmuel disagrees: *ein beno paduy* — the early payment does not work. The *gemara* clarifies that all authorities agree on two extreme cases. If the father gives the money and says it should take effect *now*, on day two or day ten, everyone agrees it does not work — the *mitzvah* has not yet begun. If the father gives the money with the stipulation that it take effect after thirty days, and the money is still in the *kohen*'s possession on day thirty-one, everyone agrees it works — it is as if the *pidyon* were performed on day thirty-one itself.

### Money must exist

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וְנִתְעַכְּלוּ הַמָּעוֹת, רַב אָמַר: בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַקִּידּוּשֵׁי אִשָּׁה, הָתָם לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּנִתְעַכְּלוּ הַמָּעוֹת — הָווּ קִידּוּשֵׁי,
>
> **Translation:** **They disagree** in a situation **where** the father says the redemption should take effect **after thirty days but the money was squandered away** in the meantime. **Rav says his son is redeemed, just as is** the *halakha* **with regard to the betrothal of a woman** on the condition that it takes effect after thirty days. In that case **there,** is it **not** correct that **even though the money was squandered away** during the thirty days **it is** a valid **betrothal?**

The debate between Rav and Shmuel concerns a third scenario: the father gives the money within thirty days, designating it to take effect after thirty days, but by the time day thirty-one arrives, the money has already been spent. In this case, is there a valid *pidyon*? Rav says yes — the act was performed and the effect is merely postponed, and the fact that the money no longer exists on day thirty-one is irrelevant. Shmuel says no — if the money is gone by day thirty-one, it does not work.

### Kiddushin parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר לָךְ: הָתָם — בְּיָדוֹ לְקַדְּשָׁהּ מֵעַכְשָׁיו, הָכָא — אֵין בְּיָדוֹ לִפְדּוֹתוֹ מֵעַכְשָׁיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel** could **say to you: There,** with regard to betrothal, it is **in his power to betroth her from now,** when he gave her the coins, and therefore it is irrelevant that the money was spent. Conversely, **here,** in the case of redemption, it is **not in his power to redeem** his son **from now.**

Rav supports his position by analogy to *kiddushin*: a man can betroth a woman today and specify that the *kiddushin* take effect in thirty days, and even if the ring has been spent or lost, the betrothal is valid. If splitting the act from the effect is permissible in *kiddushin*, it should be permissible here as well. Shmuel responds by distinguishing the cases. In *kiddushin*, the man could, if he chose, perform the *kiddushin* with immediate effect today — the thirty-day delay is purely his preference. Because he *could* have performed the act and its effect simultaneously right now, he is considered to be in control of the transaction, and he may legitimately separate the act from the effect. In *pidyon haben*, however, the father cannot perform the *pidyon* with immediate effect today — the *mitzvah* is not yet active. Since he has no present ability to perform the act now, he cannot be said to be "doing the act now" in any meaningful sense. Without that present ability, there is no valid act at all, and the designation of future effect cannot save it.

### Shmuel accepted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקַיְימָא לַן, דְּכֹל הֵיכָא דִּפְלִיגִי רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל הִלְכְתָא כְּרַב בְּאִיסּוּרֵי וְכִשְׁמוּאֵל בְּדִינֵי, הָכָא הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the practical *halakha* in this dispute the Gemara comments: **And even though we maintain** as a principle **that wherever Rav and Shmuel disagree the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav in ritual matters and in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel in monetary matters, here the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Shmuel,** who holds that if one redeems his son within thirty days he is not redeemed.

The *gemara* rules: *hilchasa kevasei de'Shmuel* — the *halakha* follows Shmuel. This is notable because the general principle is *kol heicha de'pleigi Rav u'Shmuel hilchasa ke'Rav be'isuri u'ke'Shmuel be'dinei* — we follow Rav in matters of *issur* and Shmuel in civil matters. Since *pidyon haben* might appear to be a matter of *issur*, one would expect the ruling to follow Rav. Yet the *gemara* rules like Shmuel — either because Shmuel's reasoning was more persuasive on its merits, or because the consensus among later authorities developed in that direction. It is worth noting that there appears to be a second, possibly interpolated, statement of this ruling in the *gemara* — the *halakhta* appears to be repeated, which is itself a textual indication that it may be a *Geonic* gloss rather than original *gemara* text. This is consistent with the observation that many of the *halakhta* rulings in the *gemara* were originally found in *Hilkhot Gedolot*, written in Aramaic and following the flow of the *gemara*, and were subsequently incorporated into the *gemara* text itself. Notably, the Rambam, Ramban, and Rosh all rule according to Rav — which may indicate that they regarded the *halakhta* here as precisely such a later gloss rather than authoritative *gemara*.

### Mishnah clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מֵת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנָּתַן לַכֹּהֵן — יַחְזִיר לוֹ חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים. טַעְמָא דְּמֵת, הָא לֹא מֵת — בְּנוֹ פָּדוּי! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? דְּאִיתַנְהוּ לְמָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **We learned** in the mishna on the previous *amud*: If the firstborn son **dies within thirty days** of birth, **although** the father **gave** five *sela* coins **to the priest,** the priest **must return** the **five *sela*** coins **to him.** The Gemara infers: **The reason** he must return the money is **that** the son **died. But** if he did **not die** within thirty days **his son is redeemed** despite the fact that the father gave the priest the money prior to the proper time. This inference contradicts the opinion of Shmuel. The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the **money is** still **extant,** i.e., in the priest's possession.

The *gemara* then reconciles Shmuel's ruling with the various mishnah cases that appear to assume early payment can be valid. Each time the mishnah speaks of money given before day thirty that is effective, Shmuel's position is that the money was still present on day thirty-one. The statement that if the father died within thirty days the son is *be'chezkat shelo nifdah* is similarly consistent: the proof that the *pidyon* was valid even though paid early would consist of showing that the money was still with the *kohen* on day thirty-one. All the mishnah's cases are thus harmonized with Shmuel's requirement that the money physically exist at the moment the *mitzvah* becomes operative.

## Self vs. son priority

### Tannaitic dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הוּ לִפְדּוֹת וּבְנוֹ לִפְדּוֹת — הוּ קוֹדֵם לִבְנוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּנוֹ קוֹדְמוֹ, שֶׁמִּצְוָתוֹ עַל אָבִיו, וּמִצְוַת בְּנוֹ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one had both **himself to redeem and his son to redeem, his** own redemption **takes precedence over** that of **his son. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The redemption of **his son takes precedence, as the mitzva** to redeem the father is incumbent **upon his** own **father, and the mitzva** to redeem **his son** is incumbent **upon him.** Consequently, he should first fulfill the mitzva that is incumbent upon him by redeeming his son.

The *gemara* returns to the question of priority between self-redemption and son-redemption, now framing it as a full *baraita*: the Sages rule *hu kodem li'veno* — he redeems himself first; Rabbi Yehuda rules *beno kodmo* — his son comes first, since the *mitzvah* of his own redemption was his father's *mitzvah*, while the *mitzvah* of redeeming his son is his own primary *mitzvah*.

### Yirmiyah reframes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא אֶלָּא חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים — הוּ קוֹדֵם לִבְנוֹ, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּמִצְוָה דִּידֵיהּ עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says: Everyone concedes** that in a case **where there are only five *sela*** coins available and one has to redeem both himself and his son, **his** own redemption **takes precedence over** that of **his son,** despite the indication to the contrary from the mishna. **What is the reason?** The reason is **that his own mitzva is preferable** to one he performs on behalf of others.

Rabbi Yirmiyah reframes the debate in a way that significantly alters its character. Rather than presenting it as a pure dispute over whose *mitzvah* takes conceptual precedence, he reframes it as a question of what happens when funds are limited: *hakol modim heicha de'leika ela chamesh sela'im* — all agree that if only five *sela'im* are available, the person redeems himself first.

### Limited funds

The reason Rabbi Yirmiyah gives is: *mitzvah didei adif* — his own *mitzvah* takes priority. This raises a fundamental conceptual question that the *gemara* does not fully resolve. Does "his own *mitzvah*" mean that the *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben* is, at its conceptual root, the *mitzvah* of the *child*, with the father acting on the child's behalf — in which case when this person was unredeemed, it was fundamentally *his* *mitzvah* that went unfulfilled? Or does it mean that even if the *mitzvah* is fundamentally the father's, nevertheless since an unredeemed status directly affects this person's own being and standing, he is entitled — analogous to *chayecha kodmin* — to attend to his own status before attending to his son's? These are two very different conceptual frameworks, and Rabbi Yirmiyah's language supports either reading.

### Lien theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא חָמֵשׁ מְשׁוּעְבָּדוֹת וְחָמֵשׁ בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Where they disagree** is in a case **where there is** both land worth **five** *sela* coins that is **liened** property that had been previously sold to others **and** land worth **five** *sela* coins that is **unsold** property.

Rabbi Yirmiyah then identifies *eimas pligi* — when the Sages and Rabbi Yehuda actually disagree. The debate concerns a case where there are five *sela'im* in cash — *bnei chorin* — and a field sold by the father that carries a lien of five *sela'im* — *meshubadot*. The lien on the field exists because when this person was an infant, his father was obligated in *pidyon haben*, and *milveih hakesuvah ba'Torah k'kesuvah b'shtar damya* — a debt written in the Torah is treated like a debt recorded in a formal document, and therefore creates a lien on property the father then owned. That lien follows the property even after it was sold. However, the five *sela'im* cash was acquired after the father's death, so no lien from the son's *pidyon* obligation ever attached to it.

### No lien view

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: מִלְוָה הַכְּתוּבָה בַּתּוֹרָה — לָאו כִּכְתוּבָה בִּשְׁטָר דָּמְיָא, הִלְכָּךְ מִצְוָה דִּידֵיהּ עָדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis maintain: A loan that is written in the Torah is not considered as though it is written in a document,** since buyers will not know to guard against repossession if it is not written in a document. Therefore, the five *sela* coins that are liened property are not available for redemption, and consequently **his own mitzva is preferable.** He accordingly redeems himself with the land that is not liened.

The Sages dispute the premise: *milveh hakesuvah ba'Torah lav k'kesuvah b'shtar damya* — a Torah-mandated obligation does not create a lien equivalent to a written document. No lien ever attached to the sold field. Therefore, the only funds available are the five *sela'im* cash, and a choice must be made between self-redemption and son-redemption. In this scenario, all agree the person redeems himself first: *hilkhakh mitzvah didei adif*. 

This discussion reveals the deepest disagreement underlying the competing positions. Rabbi Yehuda believes a Torah obligation creates a binding lien much like a debt in a written document, making the liened property available for the father's own redemption. The Sages reject this classification, treating Torah obligations as less binding in their effect on property transfers. These differing views about the nature of Torah-based financial obligations shape the entire analysis of priority.

### Mitzva status

The deepest question running through this entire discussion is the fundamental nature of the *mitzvah*: is *pidyon haben* at its root the father's *mitzvah* — with the son, should the father fail to act, merely inheriting a derived obligation — or is it fundamentally the son's *mitzvah*, with the father acting as agent on his behalf, so that the "real" obligated party is always the child? Rabbi Yehuda's formulation in the mishnah leans strongly toward the latter: *she'mitzvato al aviv*, your own redemption was your father's *mitzvah*, implying that the *mitzvah* is yours by right but was discharged by your father. The Sages' position, as articulated by Rabbi Yirmiyah, may reflect either the same understanding — in which case the priority of self-redemption is grounded in the analogy to caring for one's own needs first — or a fundamentally different one, in which the *mitzvah* is the father's and a person's own obligation to redeem himself is purely derivative, yet still takes priority because of the direct connection to his personal status.
