# Keritot 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Categories

### Overview

Daf 21 continues the discussion of the *issur dam* — the prohibition against consuming blood — and the question of which categories of creatures fall within its scope. The broader agenda of the daf also includes a distinction between *dam hanefesh*, lifeblood, and other types of blood, which bears on whether one incurs *karet* or merely violates a negative commandment. For now, however, the *gemara* is focused on the foundational question: which categories are included in the prohibition and which are entirely excluded? The Torah mentions *oph u-vehemah* — bird and animal — and from that phrase several distinguishing characteristics emerge. These characteristics serve to exclude fish, grasshoppers, humans, and other creatures. The relevant distinguishing features involve whether a species has both a light and a heavy *tumah*, whether there is a distinction between *issur* and *heter* in consuming it, and whether it has flesh classified as meat. Each of these criteria will be developed in turn.

### לעוף ולבהמה

The *gemara* examines why the *pasuk* needed to state both *l'oph* and *l'vehemah* rather than either term alone. Had the *pasuk* stated only *l'oph*, one might have reasoned: birds have no *kilayim* — meaning no fabric material derived from birds can create a *shatnez* problem — and therefore the prohibition should apply only to animals that similarly lack *kilayim*, which would exclude sheep, whose wool is subject to *shatnez*. Hence it was necessary to also say *l'vehemah*. Conversely, had the *pasuk* stated only *l'vehemah*, one might have limited the prohibition to animals that do not carry the prohibition of *eima la-banim* — the commandment against taking the mother together with her young — which would have excluded kosher birds that are subject to that prohibition. Hence it was also necessary to say *l'oph*. Both terms together ensure the full range of birds and animals is covered.

### Klal u-Frat u-Klal

At this point the *gemara* raises a challenge: why not read *kol dam* as a *klal*, *l'oph v'l'vehemah* as a *prat*, and apply the rule of *klal u-frat* — in which case nothing beyond *oph u-vehemah* would be included? The *gemara* answers that there is a second *klal*: *nefesh asher tochal kol dam ve-nichresah ha-nefesh ha-hi me-ameha* — whoever eats any blood shall be cut off — which constitutes a general statement following the *prat*, giving us a *klal u-frat u-klal*. Under that rule the prohibition extends to things similar to *oph u-vehemah* but is not limited exclusively to them.

An objection is raised, however: the first *klal* concerns the prohibition of eating blood, while the second *klal* concerns the punishment of *karet*. Since they address different matters, perhaps they cannot be combined into a single *klal u-frat u-klal*. The *gemara* responds that this is the method of the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*, who does apply *klal u-frat u-klal* even when the concluding *klal* and the opening *klal* address different topics — one the prohibition and one the punishment — and even though the two *klalim* are not perfectly parallel.

### Defining Features

With the *klal u-frat u-klal* established, the *gemara* articulates the principle: whatever shares the defining features of *oph u-vehemah* is included, and whatever does not is excluded. The *gemara* enumerates these features: *oph* and *vehemah* possess both a light *tumah* — the capacity to receive *tumah* as food — and a heavy *tumah* — the capacity to be a source of *tumah* as *neveilah* or *nevelat oph*. They also have *issur ve-heter*, meaning they are forbidden unless properly slaughtered, which distinguishes them from fish and grasshoppers. And they have the quality of being flesh, *min basar*, which excludes things like eggs. Anything sharing these features falls within the prohibition; anything lacking them does not.

## Koy Category

### Koy Independence

Having established the abstract criteria for inclusion, the *gemara* asks what, at the end of the day, is actually being included beyond *oph u-vehemah* themselves. *Chayah* is assumed to be subsumed within *behemah*. So what additional creature requires this elaborate *klal u-frat u-klal* framework in order to be included? *Abba bar Avin* answers: *le-atuyei damo shel koy* — to include the blood of the *koy*, an animal whose status as either *chayah* or *behemah* is uncertain, or perhaps — as the *gemara* here maintains — is neither.

The *gemara* then asks: if the *koy* is merely a creature of uncertain status, either a *chayah* or a *behemah* but we cannot determine which, why would a *pasuk* be needed to prohibit its blood? Since its blood would be forbidden under either classification, the question is moot. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the *tanna* who required the *klal u-frat u-klal* held that a *koy* is *biryah bifnei atzmah* — a species entirely unto itself, neither *chayah* nor *behemah*. Because it falls into no existing category, an explicit derivation is necessary to include it. This also carries a practical consequence: according to this position, eating the fat of a *koy* is not merely a *safek issur* for which one might bring an *asham talui*; it is a *vadai issur* with direct biblical prohibition, and a definite *chatat* would apply.

### Koy Laws

Granting that the *koy* is its own category, the *gemara* proceeds to establish each prohibition that applies to it, since it cannot simply be treated as a *chayah* or *behemah*. The prohibition of *chelev*: derived from *mikol chelev* — from *any* fat — which includes even the fat of a *koy*. The prohibition of *neveilah*: derived from *mikol neveilah* — from *any neveilah* — which includes a creature that is neither *chayah* nor *behemah*. The prohibition of *gid hanasheh*: since the *pasuk* forbids eating the sinew that is on the *kaf ha-yarech* — the mound of the thigh — and the *koy* anatomically possesses such a thigh, the prohibition applies to it by virtue of having that body part, regardless of categorical classification.

### Koy Rulings

With *chelev*, *dam*, *neveilah*, and *gid hanasheh* all derived, the *gemara* turns to the related questions of *tumah* and *shechitah*. No independent *pasuk* is required for these, the *gemara* reasons: *midechol mili rabyei rachmana ke-vehemah* — since the Torah has assimilated the *koy* to a *behemah* in all other matters, its *tumah* and *shechitah* requirements follow by the same logic. One subtle but important point emerges from this discussion: the *koy* is treated like a *behemah* for purposes of *chelev*, not like a *chayah*. A *chayah*'s fat is permitted; a *koy*'s fat is forbidden as a *vadai issur*. This distinguishes the *koy*-as-independent-species view from the *koy*-as-doubt view in a concrete and practically significant way. The *gemara* notes a certain parallel between the *koy* and the *androginos* — the *androginos* likewise potentially constitutes a *min bifnei atzmo* — and the discussion of the *koy* here illustrates just how far-reaching that premise can be once it is accepted.

## Human Flesh

### Live Human Meat

The *gemara* addresses flesh cut from a living human. Such flesh is not subject to the prohibition of *basar min ha-chai*, which applies to animals, nor does it inherently carry *tumah* in the way *neveilah* does. However, the *gemara* establishes that human flesh from a living person *can* be classified as food — and therefore can receive *tumat ochlin* — but only if two conditions are met: *machshavah*, the intent to use it as human food, and *hechsher*, prior contact with liquid.

This is a striking ruling. The principle of *klal amru be-tumah* is that anything designated for human consumption is susceptible to *tumah* and remains so until it becomes unfit even for the consumption of dogs: *kol ha-meyuchad le-ochel adam tamei ad she-yipaseil me-achilat kelev*. The threshold to acquire food status requires designation for human consumption; the threshold to lose that status is more demanding, requiring that the item become completely inedible. This asymmetry — a lower bar to acquire food status, a higher bar to lose it — reflects a general principle that might be described as a kind of halachic stickiness: once a status is acquired, it adheres and is difficult to dislodge.

In the case of human flesh from a living person, however, no automatic food designation exists. One must affirmatively intend to treat it as food. And if the *machshavah* was for dog food rather than human food — *machshavah le-kelev lav machshavah hi* — it does not suffice. Only intent for human consumption counts.

There is an obvious question here: given the extreme abnormality of treating human flesh as food, should the principle of *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — that one's idiosyncratic intention is overridden when no normal person would act this way — not apply and categorically exclude it? The *gemara* notably does not invoke this principle here for live human flesh, which is striking. The apparent implication is that, however unusual, the possibility is not treated as so categorically beyond the pale that it nullifies the *machshavah*. A separate question arises regarding *hechsher*: whether human blood can serve as the activating liquid for this purpose is itself subject to discussion, since *dam nefesh* and *dam makah* are treated differently regarding whether they function as *machshir* liquids.

### Dead Human Status

Once a person dies, the analysis changes entirely. The *gemara* states: *hanei milei mechayim* — all of the above applies only to flesh from a living human. After death, the corpse transmits only *tumah chamurah* — specifically *tumas meis* — and the category of food *tumah* becomes entirely irrelevant. The reasoning offered is *batla da'ato etzel kol adam*: no normal person, under any circumstances, considers a human corpse as food.

This invites a question, however: the *gemara* had just acknowledged that flesh from a *living* human can, with appropriate *machshavah*, be considered food. Why does *batla da'ato* apply to the corpse but not to live human flesh? This is noted as genuinely difficult. One might have expected the principle to operate with equal force in both cases, or at least to have been applied first to the arguably more disturbing case of live human flesh. The *gemara* does not resolve this tension explicitly here.

## Neveilah Food

### Neveilah Types

The preceding discussion of human flesh raises a parallel question about *neveilah* of animals. When an animal dies, its flesh becomes a source of *tumah chamurah* as *neveilat behemah*. Why, then, does it matter whether such flesh is classified as food and can receive *tumat ochlin*? Is not the heavier *tumah* of *neveilah* simply dominant?

To address this, the *gemara* introduces a *mishnah* that distinguishes among different types of *neveilah* with respect to their food status. *Neveilas behemah teme'ah* — the carcass of a non-kosher animal — requires *machshavah* to be considered food, and this applies regardless of whether it is in a city or a village. Similarly, *neveilas oph tahor bikfarim* — the carcass of a kosher bird in a village — also requires *machshavah*. By contrast, *neveilas behemah tehorah* — the carcass of a kosher animal — and *neveilas oph tahor* in a city, along with *chelev* sold in marketplaces, do not require *machshavah*; they are presumed to be food.

Why does a *neveilah* not require *hechsher*? Because anything destined to carry a heavy *tumah* is exempt from the liquid-contact prerequisite, as derived from the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*: just as seeds require *hechsher* because they will not themselves become sources of heavy *tumah*, so too only things that will not become sources of heavy *tumah* require *hechsher*. Since *neveilah* itself will become a source of *tumah*, it needs no prior contact with liquid.

### No Hechsher

The principle is thus: meat of *neveilah* does not require *hechsher* because it will itself be a source of heavy *tumah*. But it may still require *machshavah*, since not every type of *neveilah* is automatically assumed to be desired for consumption. The fact that a human corpse requires neither — because *tumas meis* supersedes all food-status categories — is precisely what the earlier analysis demonstrated. The *neveilah* of an animal, by contrast, does not completely eliminate the relevance of food classification, as will be shown.

### Half-Kezayit

*Rav* poses the challenge directly: if *neveilah* is already *tamei* in its own right, why does it matter whether we call it *tumat ochlin*? *Rabbi Chiya* responds with a concrete scenario. Suppose one has less than a *kebaitza* of food — the minimum amount needed to receive *tumat ochlin* — and augments it with less than a *kezayit* of *neveilah*. Specifically: one has a *kebaitza* minus a half-*kezayit* of ordinary food, and combines it with a half-*kezayit* of *neveilah*. Together they form a *kebaitza*, enough to receive *tumah* as food. But the half-*kezayit* of *neveilah* is insufficient on its own to transmit *tumah* as *neveilah* — a full *kezayit* is required. Therefore in this case it matters enormously to classify the *neveilah* as food: as food, the combined mass can receive *tumah*; as *neveilah* alone, the sub-*kezayit* quantity cannot transmit it.

The *gemara* then asks: in this scenario — where the *neveilah* is only a half-*kezayit* — it will never become a source of heavy *tumah*, so why does it not require *hechsher* after all? The answer given is that the half-*kezayit* of *neveilah* remains fundamentally of the category of *neveilah*. If even half a *kezayit* more were added, it would become a full *kezayit* of *neveilah* and would be a source of *tumah*. Since it is structurally of the *neveilah* category — even if the present quantity falls short — it does not require *hechsher*. Ordinary foods that have no *neveilah* component at all require *hechsher* precisely because they will never become sources of heavy *tumah*; but anything with even a partial *neveilah* component retains the exemption from *hechsher*.

The *mishnah*'s distinction between *neveilas behemah teme'ah* and *neveilas behemah tehorah* with respect to the need for *machshavah* is puzzling. Both involve animals whose carcasses non-Jews might eat. One would expect non-Jews to be as inclined to eat dead non-kosher animals as dead kosher ones — indeed, in the ancient world, pork was an extremely common food source. The *gemara*'s approach may be that the relevant scenario involves animals that died of natural causes rather than those slaughtered without *shechitah*, in which case a natural death by disease might be presumed less appealing even in the non-Jewish market. But the precise basis for the distinction between kosher and non-kosher animals remains unclear.

### Encased Neveilah

*Rabbi Chanina* offers an alternative scenario for the relevance of food classification. Rather than a sub-*kezayit* of *neveilah*, suppose there is a full *kezayit* of *neveilah* enclosed within dough — the classic case of a *kreplach*-like structure — where the combined meat-and-dough is the size of a *kebaitza*. If something *tamei* touches the outside of the dough, it does not come into contact with the *neveilah* inside. Therefore the item cannot receive *tumah* simply by virtue of the *neveilah* it contains — touching the outer dough does not constitute touching the *neveilah*. However, by classifying the whole structure as food, the outer dough can receive *tumat ochlin* when touched by something *tamei*, since together they constitute a *kebaitza* of food.

The *gemara* challenges: if the enclosed *neveilah* is not transmitting *tumah* through touch, why does the structure not require *hechsher*? The answer is that the *neveilah* inside can still transmit *tumah* through *masa* — carrying — even if not through direct contact. Since the *neveilah*, when carried, does transmit *tumah*, the exemption from *hechsher* applies. The relevance of food classification is thus limited to cases of touch on the outer surface, where the carrier does not come into direct contact with the *neveilah* itself.

## Human Flesh…

### Corpse Tumah

The *gemara* now returns to the contrast between *neveilah* of animals and human corpse flesh, this time in light of the enclosed-dough scenario. One might have thought that even for a human corpse, the food-status question would be relevant in the *neveilah*-in-dough scenario — for there, the corpse flesh would not transmit *tumah* through touch of the outer dough. The *gemara* rejects this: *tumas meis* operates differently from other *tumah*. It is subject to the principle of *tumah boka ve-olah boka ve-yoredet* — the *tumah* of a corpse breaks through upward and downward. Unless there is a *tefach* of open space and a covering constituting a proper *ohel*, the *tumah* of the corpse radiates directly through whatever surrounds it. Therefore even dough encasing corpse flesh does not block the corpse's *tumah*; anyone above or below it is affected regardless of direct contact. Food classification is thus irrelevant for corpse flesh even in the enclosed scenario.

Interestingly, this second treatment of the corpse question does not invoke *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — the principle used earlier. Here the *gemara* reasons instead from the mechanics of *tumas meis*, which renders food classification structurally moot regardless of whether anyone would conceivably eat such flesh. This framing offers a more internally consistent treatment: from the standpoint of the *tumah* laws, live human flesh and dead human flesh are treated symmetrically as potential foods in principle — it is only the overpowering, penetrating nature of *tumas meis* that makes the designation irrelevant for the corpse, not a categorical exclusion from food status. This is admittedly still somewhat puzzling given the earlier appeal to *batla da'ato*, but at least the second passage does not compound the difficulty.

## Blood Sources

### Fish/Locust Blood

With the structural analysis of the *issur dam* complete, the *gemara* turns to a series of specific blood categories and their halachic status. A *baraita* has stated that the blood of fish and grasshoppers is *mutar* because they are *kulo heter* — entirely permissible. The *gemara* interrogates what this phrase means. It cannot mean that all their parts may be eaten, since a *chayah* likewise has no prohibited *chelev* and yet its blood is forbidden. Nor can it mean they have no *gid hanasheh*, since birds also lack a *gid hanasheh* prohibition yet their blood is forbidden. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *d'lo ba'ei shechitah* — they do not require *shechitah*. Fish and grasshoppers have no *issurim* that *shechitah* comes to address; they may be eaten live, and their entire status is one of unqualified permissibility. That is the characteristic that distinguishes them from birds and animals and justifies excluding their blood from the prohibition.

### Rodent Blood

The *gemara* then records a statement of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav*: *dam sheratzim lokim alav* — one who eats a *kezayit* of rodent blood receives lashes. A *baraita* is cited that appears to contradict this: it lists blood of bipeds, blood of *sheratzim* and *remasim* as forbidden, but specifies *ve-ein chayavin alav* — one does not incur the transgression. The *gemara* attempts to argue that *ein chayavin alav* means only that one does not incur *karet*, but not that there is no *lav*. Two responses are given. First, if there were a *lav* for rodent blood, the *baraita* would have listed it alongside the other categories subject to a *lav* but not *karet*, such as blood of internal organs and limbs — yet it does not. Second, the *baraita* that has occupied the entire daf explicitly excludes *sheratzim* from the prohibition verse, *yatza dam sheratzim she-ein bahem tumah chamurah*, indicating that the exclusion operates at the level of the prohibition itself, not merely at the level of *karet*.

*Rabbi Zeira* resolves the apparent ruling of *Rav*: *hisru bo mishum sheretz lokeh* — if someone was warned that the blood constitutes *sheretz* and then consumed it, he receives lashes, but not on account of the blood prohibition. Rather, blood of a rodent is treated as the meat of the rodent itself, and one transgresses the prohibition of eating *sheretz*, not the prohibition of blood. The principle at work here is that blood is treated as part of the meat of the creature from which it comes, at least for the purposes of applying other prohibitions. This connects to the earlier debate between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* regarding whether blood of *neveilah* transmits *tumah* like the meat of *neveilah* — the underlying logic is the same: blood is of a piece with the flesh.

### Human Blood

*Rav* states: *dam dagim she-kinso assur* — if one collects fish blood in a vessel, it is forbidden. The *gemara* immediately asks from the *baraita* that explicitly permits fish blood. The *gemara* resolves: the *baraita*'s permission applies when the blood was not deliberately collected; *Rav*'s prohibition applies when it was gathered together. The concern when collected is one of appearance — it resembles animal blood, and consuming it in that form creates a *marit ayin* issue.

### Fish vs. Human

With this distinction in place, the *gemara* raises a further question: the same *baraita* that permitted fish blood stated that human blood carries some prohibition. If fish blood is only problematic when collected, the *baraita*'s statement about human blood must also be referring to the uncollected case — which would mean that even uncollected human blood is forbidden. But this seems too strict. The *gemara* resolves by refining the fish-blood ruling: the *baraita*'s permission for fish blood applies specifically when the fish has its scales — *de-it bei kaskasin* — present together with the blood, even in a cup. The scales serve as an identifying marker, dispelling the appearance of animal blood and removing the *marit ayin* concern. *Rav*'s prohibition applies when the blood is collected and there are no scales present to identify it.

The comparison to human blood follows accordingly. Human blood collected in a cup and without any identifying feature is forbidden by rabbinic enactment. Uncollected human blood — such as blood that incidentally drips onto food — is not technically forbidden but falls under a norm of *mitzvot prosh*, of avoiding unseemly behavior rather than a formal *issur*.

### Gum Blood

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that illustrates the practical boundary: if one has bleeding gums and blood gets onto a piece of bread, one should scrape it off before eating. But blood that is still between one's teeth may simply be swallowed without concern. *Rashi* explains that the instruction to scrape away the blood on the bread reflects a norm of *mitzvot prosh* — conduct befitting propriety — rather than a formal rabbinic prohibition; the *baraita* conspicuously does not use the word *assur*. Blood that remains in the mouth, still on one's own body, is a fortiori unproblematic. Exactly where the line falls between the clearly permitted — blood still in the mouth — and the unseemly but not forbidden — blood on the bread — and the actually prohibited — collected human blood in a cup — is a question raised by *Tosafot* elsewhere, and one whose full elaboration goes beyond the scope of the present daf.
