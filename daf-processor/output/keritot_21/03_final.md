# Keritot 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Categories

### Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָכַל דַּם שְׁחִיטָה, בַּבְּהֵמָה בְּחַיָּה וּבָעוֹף, בֵּין טְמֵאִין בֵּין טְהוֹרִין, דַּם נְחִירָה, דַּם עִיקּוּר, דַּם הַקָּזָה שֶׁהַנְּשָׁמָה יוֹצְאָה בּוֹ – חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו. דַּם הַטְּחוֹל, דַּם הַלֵּב, דַּם בֵּיצִים, דַּם חֲגָבִים, דַּם הַתַּמְצִית – אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְחַיֵּיב בְּדַם הַתַּמְצִית.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one consumed** an olive-bulk of **blood** that spurted during the **slaughter of a domesticated animal, an undomesticated animal, or a bird, whether** it is **a kosher or non-kosher** species; or if one consumed **blood** that flowed after **stabbing** an animal or killing it in a manner other than by ritual slaughter, or **blood** that spurted after **ripping** the animal's windpipe or gullet, or **blood** that spurted during **bloodletting with which the soul departs, one is liable** to receive *karet* **for** consuming **it** intentionally or to bring a sin offering for consuming it unwittingly. But with regard to **blood of the spleen, blood of the heart, blood of eggs, blood of grasshoppers,** or **blood of exudate [*tamtzit*],** i.e., that oozes from the neck of the animal after the initial spurt of its slaughter concludes,**one is not liable for** consuming **it. Rabbi Yehuda deems** one **liable in** the case of **blood of exudate.**

Daf 21 continues the discussion of the *issur dam* — the prohibition against consuming blood — and the question of which categories of creatures fall within its scope. The broader agenda of the daf also includes a distinction between *dam hanefesh*, lifeblood, and other types of blood, which bears on whether one incurs *karet* or merely violates a negative commandment. For now, however, the *gemara* is focused on the foundational question: which categories are included in the prohibition and which are entirely excluded? The Torah mentions *oph u-vehemah* — bird and animal — and from that phrase several distinguishing characteristics emerge. These characteristics serve to exclude fish, grasshoppers, humans, and other creatures. The relevant distinguishing features involve whether a species has both a light and a heavy *tumah*, whether there is a distinction between *issur* and *heter* in consuming it, and whether it has flesh classified as meat. Each of these criteria will be developed in turn.

### לעוף ולבהמה

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״כָּל דָּם לֹא תֹאכְלוּ״, שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי אֲפִילּוּ דַּם מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם, דַּם בֵּיצִים, דַּם חֲגָבִים, דַּם דָּגִים, הַכֹּל בַּכְּלָל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לְעוֹף וְלִבְהֵמָה״, מָה עוֹף וּבְהֵמָה מְיוּחָדִין, שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן טוּמְאָה קַלָּה וְטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶן אִיסּוּר וְהֶיתֵּר, וְהֵן מִין בָּשָׂר – אַף כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן טוּמְאָה קַלָּה וְטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, וִיהִי בָּהֶן אִיסּוּר וְהֶיתֵּר, וִיהִי בָּהֶן מִין בָּשָׂר – בַּכְּלָל. אוֹצִיא דַּם מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם – שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, וְאֵין בָּהֶן טוּמְאָה קַלָּה. אוֹצִיא דַּם שְׁרָצִים – שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה. אוֹצִיא דַּם בֵּיצִים – שֶׁאֵין מִין בָּשָׂר, דַּם דָּגִים דַּם חֲגָבִים – שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ הֶיתֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall consume no manner of blood"** (Leviticus 7:26). **I** would **derive** from here that **even** with regard to the **blood of bipeds,** i.e., human beings, and the **blood of eggs,** the **blood of grasshoppers,** and the **blood of fish, all** these are **included** in the prohibition against consuming blood. Therefore, **the verse states: "Whether it is of bird or of animal"** (Leviticus 7:26). The *baraita* explains the derivation from the verse: **Just as birds and animals are unique** in **that they have** the capacity for both **a light** form of **ritual impurity,** if they become impure after they are slaughtered, in which case they are considered impure food, **and a severe** form of **impurity,** if they die without valid ritual slaughter, **and they have** the possibility of being **forbidden or permitted, and they are a type of meat, so too, everything** that has the capacity for both **a light** form of **impurity** and a severe form of ritual impurity, and has the possibility of being forbidden or permitted, and is a type of meat, is included in the prohibition. **I will** likewise **exclude** the **blood of creeping animals, which do not have a severe** form of **ritual impurity.** Impurity transmitted to a person by the carcass of a creeping animal does not render clothing impure, whereas impurity transmitted by the carcass of an animal or bird does render one's clothing impure. **I will** also **exclude the blood of eggs, as** they **are not a type of meat,** and finally I will exclude **the blood of fish** and **the blood of grasshoppers, as they are entirely permitted,** as explained on 21b.

The *gemara* examines why the *pasuk* needed to state both *l'oph* and *l'vehemah* rather than either term alone. Had the *pasuk* stated only *l'oph*, one might have reasoned: birds have no *kilayim* — meaning no fabric material derived from birds can create a *shatnez* problem — and therefore the prohibition should apply only to animals that similarly lack *kilayim*, which would exclude sheep, whose wool is subject to *shatnez*. Hence it was necessary to also say *l'vehemah*. Conversely, had the *pasuk* stated only *l'vehemah*, one might have limited the prohibition to animals that do not carry the prohibition of *eima la-banim* — the commandment against taking the mother together with her young — which would have excluded kosher birds that are subject to that prohibition. Hence it was also necessary to say *l'oph*. Both terms together ensure the full range of birds and animals is covered.

### Klal u-Frat u-Klal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: ״כׇּל דָּם״ – כָּלַל, ״עוֹף וּבְהֵמָה״ – פָּרַט, כְּלָל וּפְרָט, אֵין בַּכְּלָל אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁבַּפְּרָט, עוֹף וּבְהֵמָה – אִין, מִידִּי אַחֲרִינָא – לָא! ״נֶפֶשׁ אֲשֶׁר תֹּאכַל כׇּל דָּם״ – חָזַר וְכָלַל, כְּלָל וּפְרָט וְכָלַל, אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט. וְהָא לָא דָּמֵי כְּלָלָא בָּתְרָא לִכְלָלָא קַמָּא, כְּלָלָא קַמָּא לָאו, כְּלָלָא בָּתְרָא כָּרֵת! הַאי תַּנָּא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל [בְּרִיבּוּיֵי וּמִיעוּטֵי וּ]כְלָלֵי וּפְרָטֵי דָּרְשִׁינַן מִן הָדֵין גַּוְונָא, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא דָּמֵי כְּלָלָא בָּתְרָא לִכְלָלָא קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But say** that the verse should be interpreted as follows: The phrase **"no manner of blood"** is **a generalization,** and the mention of **"bird or animal" is a detail.** This is **a generalization and a detail,** and the hermeneutical principle in such cases is that **the generalization includes only what** is mentioned **in the detail.** Consequently, with regard to **a bird and an animal, yes,** it is prohibited to consume their blood, but with regard to **anything else,** the prohibition does **not** apply. The Gemara explains that when the next verse states: **"Whoever consumes any blood,** that soul shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 7:27), **it then generalized** again. Therefore, this is a case of **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization,** in which case the relevant hermeneutical principle dictates that **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail.** Accordingly, the verse includes everything that has the following three components, as stated above: The capacity for both a light form of ritual impurity and a severe form of ritual impurity; the possibility of being forbidden or permitted; and categorization as a type of meat. The Gemara objects: **But the last generalization is not similar to the first generalization. The first generalization,** where it states: "You shall consume no manner of blood," indicates a regular **prohibition,** punishable by lashes, whereas **the last generalization,** where it states: "That soul shall be cut off from his people," indicates that the prohibition is punishable by ***karet*.** Consequently, the hermeneutical principle for cases of a generalization, a detail, and a generalization should not apply. The Gemara responds: **This *tanna*,** whose opinion is recorded in this *baraita*, is of **the school of Rabbi Yishmael,** who maintains that **we expound generalizations and details** even **in a case** like **this, even though the last generalization is not similar to the first generalization.**

At this point the *gemara* raises a challenge: why not read *kol dam* as a *klal*, *l'oph v'l'vehemah* as a *prat*, and apply the rule of *klal u-frat* — in which case nothing beyond *oph u-vehemah* would be included? The *gemara* answers that there is a second *klal*: *nefesh asher tochal kol dam ve-nichresah ha-nefesh ha-hi me-ameha* — whoever eats any blood shall be cut off — which constitutes a general statement following the *prat*, giving us a *klal u-frat u-klal*. Under that rule the prohibition extends to things similar to *oph u-vehemah* but is not limited exclusively to them.

An objection is raised, however: the first *klal* concerns the prohibition of eating blood, while the second *klal* concerns the punishment of *karet*. Since they address different matters, perhaps they cannot be combined into a single *klal u-frat u-klal*. The *gemara* responds that this is the method of the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*, who does apply *klal u-frat u-klal* even when the concluding *klal* and the opening *klal* address different topics — one the prohibition and one the punishment — and even though the two *klalim* are not perfectly parallel.

### Defining Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְעֵין הַפְּרָט, מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ טוּמְאָה קַלָּה וְטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶ[ן] אִיסּוּר וְהֶיתֵּר וְיֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִין בָּשָׂר, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ טוּמְאָה קַלָּה וְטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further analyzes the *baraita*. **The Master said** that there is **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization,** and therefore **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail. Just as the detail is explicit** in that it is referring to **an item that has** the capacity for both **a light** form of **impurity and a severe** form of **impurity, and it has** the possibility of being **forbidden or permitted, and it is a type of meat, so too, every item that has** the capacity for both **a light** form of **ritual impurity and a severe form of ritual impurity,** and has the possibility of being forbidden or permitted, and is a type of meat, is included in the prohibition against consuming blood.

With the *klal u-frat u-klal* established, the *gemara* articulates the principle: whatever shares the defining features of *oph u-vehemah* is included, and whatever does not is excluded. The *gemara* enumerates these features: *oph* and *vehemah* possess both a light *tumah* — the capacity to receive *tumah* as food — and a heavy *tumah* — the capacity to be a source of *tumah* as *neveilah* or *nevelat oph*. They also have *issur ve-heter*, meaning they are forbidden unless properly slaughtered, which distinguishes them from fish and grasshoppers. And they have the quality of being flesh, *min basar*, which excludes things like eggs. Anything sharing these features falls within the prohibition; anything lacking them does not.

## Koy Category

### Koy Independence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַף כֹּל״ דְּקָתָנֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אָבִין: לְאֵתוֹיֵי דָּמוֹ שֶׁל כּוֹי. מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר כּוֹי סְפֵיקָא הוּא, אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמֵיסַר סְפֵיקָא? אֶלָּא קָסָבַר כּוֹי בְּרִיָּה בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does this phrase: **So too every** item, **which** the *baraita* **teaches,** serve **to include** beyond birds and animals, which are mentioned explicitly in the verse? After all, the blood of human beings, creeping animals, fish, and grasshoppers have already been excluded from the prohibition. **Rav Adda bar Avin said:** It serves **to include** the **blood of a *koy*,** an animal that is not definitively categorized as either a domesticated animal or an undomesticated animal. The Gemara asks: **What** does Rav Adda bar Avin **maintain** in this regard? **If he maintains** that **a *koy* is an uncertain** case, **is a verse necessary to teach** the *halakha* in a case of **uncertainty? Rather,** Rav Adda bar Avin **maintains** that the *koy* **is a distinct entity,** i.e., it is not in the category of domesticated animals or undomesticated animals, and therefore its *halakha* must be taught.

Having established the abstract criteria for inclusion, the *gemara* asks what, at the end of the day, is actually being included beyond *oph u-vehemah* themselves. *Chayah* is assumed to be subsumed within *behemah*. So what additional creature requires this elaborate *klal u-frat u-klal* framework in order to be included? *Abba bar Avin* answers: *le-atuyei damo shel koy* — to include the blood of the *koy*, an animal whose status as either *chayah* or *behemah* is uncertain, or perhaps — as the *gemara* here maintains — is neither.

The *gemara* then asks: if the *koy* is merely a creature of uncertain status, either a *chayah* or a *behemah* but we cannot determine which, why would a *pasuk* be needed to prohibit its blood? Since its blood would be forbidden under either classification, the question is moot. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the *tanna* who required the *klal u-frat u-klal* held that a *koy* is *biryah bifnei atzmah* — a species entirely unto itself, neither *chayah* nor *behemah*. Because it falls into no existing category, an explicit derivation is necessary to include it. This also carries a practical consequence: according to this position, eating the fat of a *koy* is not merely a *safek issur* for which one might bring an *asham talui*; it is a *vadai issur* with direct biblical prohibition, and a definite *chatat* would apply.

### Koy Laws

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן דָּמוֹ, חֶלְבּוֹ מְנָלַן? ״מִכׇּל חֵלֶב״. נִבְלָתוֹ מְנָלַן? ״מִכׇּל נְבֵלָה״. גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה מְנָלַן? בְּכַף הַיָּרֵךְ תַּלְיֵהּ רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא אִית לֵיהּ כַּף הַיָּרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We found** a source for the prohibition against consuming the **blood of** a *koy*. **From where do we** derive that **its fat** is also forbidden? The Gemara answers that this is derived **from** the verse: "You shall not eat **any [*kol*] fat"** (Leviticus 7:23). The additional word "*kol*" serves to include the fat of a *koy* as being forbidden. The Gemara inquires further: **From where do we** derive that **its carcass** is prohibited? The Gemara answers that this is derived **from** the verse: "You shall not eat **any [*kol*] animal carcass"** (Deuteronomy 14:21). Here too, the additional word "*kol*" serves to include the carcass of a *koy* as being prohibited. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that **the sciatic nerve** of a *koy* is prohibited? The Gemara answers that **the Merciful One rendered** the prohibition of the sciatic nerve **dependent on** the presence of **the spoon of the thigh,** as it is stated: "Therefore the children of Israel shall not eat the sciatic nerve which is upon the spoon of the thigh" (Genesis 32:33), **and this** *koy* **has a spoon [*kaf* ] of the thigh,** i.e., a round protrusion of flesh in its thigh that is shaped like a spoon.

Granting that the *koy* is its own category, the *gemara* proceeds to establish each prohibition that applies to it, since it cannot simply be treated as a *chayah* or *behemah*. The prohibition of *chelev*: derived from *mikol chelev* — from *any* fat — which includes even the fat of a *koy*. The prohibition of *neveilah*: derived from *mikol neveilah* — from *any neveilah* — which includes a creature that is neither *chayah* nor *behemah*. The prohibition of *gid hanasheh*: since the *pasuk* forbids eating the sinew that is on the *kaf ha-yarech* — the mound of the thigh — and the *koy* anatomically possesses such a thigh, the prohibition applies to it by virtue of having that body part, regardless of categorical classification.

### Koy Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוּמְאָתוֹ וּשְׁחִיטָה, מְנָלַן? סְבָרָא, מִדִּלְכֹל מִילֵּי רַבְּיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא כִּבְהֵמָה, טוּמְאָתוֹ וּשְׁחִיטָתוֹ נָמֵי כִּבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **From where do we** derive the **impurity** status of the carcass of a *koy*, **and** that ritual **slaughter** renders its meat permitted for consumption? The Gemara answers that these *halakhot* are derived by logical **reasoning: From** the fact **that in all** the **matters** mentioned above **the Merciful One included** the *koy* and rendered it **like a domesticated animal,** it is logical that the *halakhot* concerning **its impurity and its slaughter** are **also like** those concerning **a domesticated animal.**

With *chelev*, *dam*, *neveilah*, and *gid hanasheh* all derived, the *gemara* turns to the related questions of *tumah* and *shechitah*. No independent *pasuk* is required for these, the *gemara* reasons: *midechol mili rabyei rachmana ke-vehemah* — since the Torah has assimilated the *koy* to a *behemah* in all other matters, its *tumah* and *shechitah* requirements follow by the same logic. One subtle but important point emerges from this discussion: the *koy* is treated like a *behemah* for purposes of *chelev*, not like a *chayah*. A *chayah*'s fat is permitted; a *koy*'s fat is forbidden as a *vadai issur*. This distinguishes the *koy*-as-independent-species view from the *koy*-as-doubt view in a concrete and practically significant way. The *gemara* notes a certain parallel between the *koy* and the *androginos* — the *androginos* likewise potentially constitutes a *min bifnei atzmo* — and the discussion of the *koy* here illustrates just how far-reaching that premise can be once it is accepted.

## Human Flesh (II)

### Live Human Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יָצְאוּ שְׁרָצִים שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, וּרְמִינְהִי: הַחוֹתֵךְ מִן הָאָדָם – צָרִיךְ מַחְשָׁבָה וְהֶכְשֵׁר. וְקַשְׁיָא לַן: מַחְשָׁבָה לְמָה לִי? תֵּעָשֶׂה חֲתִיכָה שֶׁלּוֹ מַחְשָׁבָה! וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בְּחוֹתְכוֹ לַכֶּלֶב, וּמַחְשָׁבָה לַכֶּלֶב לָאו מַחְשָׁבָה הִיא. וְלָא? וְהָתְנַן, כְּלָל אָמְרוּ בְּטוּמְאָה: כֹּל הַמְיוּחָד לֶאֱכוֹל אָדָם – טָמֵא, עַד שֶׁיִּפָּסֵל מֵאֲכִילַת כֶּלֶב!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to analyze the *baraita* cited above (20b–21a). **The Master said:** The blood of **creeping animals** has been **excluded, as they do not have** a severe form of **impurity.** The Gemara asks: **And** do creeping animals **not** have a severe form of impurity? After all, by Torah law the carcass of **a creeping animal transmits impurity through contact.** The Gemara answers that even so, **it does not transmit impurity through carrying,** in the manner of an animal carcass. The *baraita* contrasts animals, which are susceptible to both light and more severe forms of impurity, and people, which the *baraita* had just acknowledged that flesh from a *living* human can, with appropriate *machshavah*, be considered food. The Gemara learns: **With regard to** one who **cuts** flesh **from** a living **person** for food, there are two conditions under which it becomes impure with the impurity of food: First, the flesh **requires intent** to eat it or feed it to others, **and** second, it must **be rendered susceptible** to impurity by coming into contact with water or one of the other six liquids that render food items susceptible to impurity. Before explaining the contradiction between this mishna and the *baraita* cited above, the Gemara seeks to clarify the mishna itself: **And** the following was **difficult for us: Why do I** need **intent? Let his** act of **cutting serve as intent** to eat the flesh. **And Reish Lakish said:** This is referring **to one who cuts** the flesh in order to feed it **to a dog, and intent** to feed it **to a dog is not** considered **intent** to use the flesh as food. The Gemara asks: **And** is intent to feed a dog **not** considered intent to use the flesh as food? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Teharot* 8:6): The Sages **stated a principle with regard to ritual impurity:** With regard to **any** food **that is** assumed to be **food designated** for **a person,** once it becomes impure with the impurity of food, it remains **impure until it is rendered unfit to be consumed by a dog?**

The *gemara* addresses flesh cut from a living human. Such flesh is not subject to the prohibition of *basar min ha-chai*, which applies to animals, nor does it inherently carry *tumah* in the way *neveilah* does. However, the *gemara* establishes that human flesh from a living person *can* be classified as food — and therefore can receive *tumat ochlin* — but only if two conditions are met: *machshavah*, the intent to use it as human food, and *hechsher*, prior contact with liquid.

This is a striking ruling. The principle of *klal amru be-tumah* is that anything designated for human consumption is susceptible to *tumah* and remains so until it becomes unfit even for the consumption of dogs: *kol ha-meyuchad le-ochel adam tamei ad she-yipaseil me-achilat kelev*. The threshold to acquire food status requires designation for human consumption; the threshold to lose that status is more demanding, requiring that the item become completely inedible. This asymmetry — a lower bar to acquire food status, a higher bar to lose it — reflects a general principle that might be described as a kind of halachic stickiness: once a status is acquired, it adheres and is difficult to dislodge.

In the case of human flesh from a living person, however, no automatic food designation exists. One must affirmatively intend to treat it as food. And if the *machshavah* was for dog food rather than human food — *machshavah le-kelev lav machshavah hi* — it does not suffice. Only intent for human consumption counts.

There is an obvious question here: given the extreme abnormality of treating human flesh as food, should the principle of *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — that one's idiosyncratic intention is overridden when no normal person would act this way — not apply and categorically exclude it? The *gemara* notably does not invoke this principle here for live human flesh, which is striking. The apparent implication is that, however unusual, the possibility is not treated as so categorically beyond the pale that it nullifies the *machshavah*. A separate question arises regarding *hechsher*: whether human blood can serve as the activating liquid for this purpose is itself subject to discussion, since *dam nefesh* and *dam makah* are treated differently regarding whether they function as *machshir* liquids.

### Dead Human Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מִילֵּי מֵחַיִּים, אֲבָל לְאַחַר מִיתָה – טָמֵא הוּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה. דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי בְּהֵמָה לְאַחַר מִיתָה, אִי בָּשָׂר – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, וְאִם דָּם – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, דִּתְנַן: דַּם נְבֵילוֹת – בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מְטַהֲרִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מְטַמְּאִין.
>
> **Translation:** Before explaining the contradiction between this mishna and the *baraita* cited above, the Gemara seeks to clarify the mishna itself: **This statement** of the mishna that human flesh is susceptible to impurity as a food item applies **while** the person **is alive. But after death** his corpse **is impure** by **a severe** form of **impurity** and is no longer susceptible to impurity as a food item, and that is the meaning of the *baraita*. The Gemara objects: The *baraita* contrasts animals, which are susceptible to both light and more severe forms of impurity, and people, which are susceptible only to severe ritual impurity, and the Gemara has explained the *baraita* as referring to a human being after death. But **in the corresponding** situation **with regard to an animal after death, if** one is referring to its **meat,** then it **transmits impurity** by **a severe** form of **impurity,** the impurity of a carcass, **and if** one is referring to its **blood,** it likewise **transmits impurity** by **a severe** form of **ritual impurity,** and not a light form of ritual impurity. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 5:1): With regard to **the blood of** animal **carcasses, Beit Shammai deem** it **pure, and Beit Hillel deem** it **impure** just like the carcass itself, which transmits severe ritual impurity. Why, then, does the *baraita* differentiate between people and animals and indicate that dead animals are susceptible to a light form of ritual impurity?

Once a person dies, the analysis changes entirely. The *gemara* states: *hanei milei mechayim* — all of the above applies only to flesh from a living human. After death, the corpse transmits only *tumah chamurah* — specifically *tumas meis* — and the category of food *tumah* becomes entirely irrelevant. The reasoning offered is *batla da'ato etzel kol adam*: no normal person, under any circumstances, considers a human corpse as food.

This invites a question, however: the *gemara* had just acknowledged that flesh from a *living* human can, with appropriate *machshavah*, be considered food. Why does *batla da'ato* apply to the corpse but not to live human flesh? This is noted as genuinely difficult. One might have expected the principle to operate with equal force in both cases, or at least to have been applied first to the arguably more disturbing case of live human flesh. The *gemara* does not resolve this tension explicitly here.

## Neveilah Food

### Neveilah Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לִכְדִתְנַן: נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְמֵאָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, נִבְלַת [הָ]עוֹף הַטָּהוֹר בַּכְּפָרִים – צָרִיךְ מַחְשָׁבָה, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר. נִבְלַת בְּהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וְנִבְלַת עוֹף טָהוֹר וְחֵלֶב בַּשְּׁוָקִים – אֵין צְרִיכִין לֹא מַחְשָׁבָה וְלֹא הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The statement of the *baraita* that animals are susceptible to a light form of impurity after death while people are not **is necessary only for that which we learned** in a mishna with regard to the impurity of food (*Okatzin* 3:3): **A carcass of a non-kosher animal,** e.g., a horse or donkey, found **in any location,** and likewise an unslaughtered **carcass of a kosher bird** found **in villages,** where there are not many people and it is unlikely to be eaten, **require** express **intent** to be eaten in order for them to be considered food and susceptible to impurity of food. But **they are not required** to come into contact with a liquid in order to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity. By contrast, **the carcass of a kosher animal anywhere, and the unslaughtered carcass of a kosher bird or** forbidden **fat** found **in marketplaces,** will presumably be eaten, and therefore they **require neither intent** to be eaten **nor** contact with liquid in order to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity.

The preceding discussion of human flesh raises a parallel question about *neveilah* of animals. When an animal dies, its flesh becomes a source of *tumah chamurah* as *neveilat behemah*. Why, then, does it matter whether such flesh is classified as food and can receive *tumat ochlin*? Is not the heavier *tumah* of *neveilah* simply dominant?

To address this, the *gemara* introduces a *mishnah* that distinguishes among different types of *neveilah* with respect to their food status. *Neveilas behemah teme'ah* — the carcass of a non-kosher animal — requires *machshavah* to be considered food, and this applies regardless of whether it is in a city or a village. Similarly, *neveilas oph tahor bikfarim* — the carcass of a kosher bird in a village — also requires *machshavah*. By contrast, *neveilas behemah tehorah* — the carcass of a kosher animal — and *neveilas oph tahor* in a city, along with *chelev* sold in marketplaces, do not require *machshavah*; they are presumed to be food.

### No Hechsher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא: מַחְשָׁבָה, לְמָה לִי לְטוּמְאָה קַלָּה? הִיא גּוּפַהּ טוּמְאָה הִיא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת נְבֵילָה, וְצֵרְפָהּ לְפָחוֹת מִכְּבֵיצָה אוֹכָלִין, דְּהַאי וְהַאי הָוֵי כְּבֵיצָה. אִי הָכִי, לִיבְעֵי נָמֵי הֶכְשֵׁר, דְּהָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״עַל כׇּל זֶרַע [זֵרוּעַ] אֲשֶׁר יִזָּרֵעַ״ – מָה זְרָעִים מְיוּחָדִין שֶׁאֵין סוֹפָן לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה וְצָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר, אַף כֹּל שֶׁאֵין סוֹפָן לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה צָרִיךְ הֶכְשֵׁר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בָּאוֹכָלִין דְּעָלְמָא, דְּלֵית בְּהוּ פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת נְבֵלָה, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּאִיכָּא בְּגַוֵּיהּ פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת נְבֵילָה, כֵּיוָן דְּאִילּוּ מְצָרֵף לֵיהּ כְּזַיִת – לָא בָּעֵי הֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav said to Rabbi Ḥiyya: Why do I** need **intent for** a carcass to be eaten so that it will be considered food and therefore susceptible to the impurity of food, which is **a light** form of **ritual impurity?** After all, the carcass **itself is** already impure with a severe form of **impurity.** Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to** Rav: It is necessary for a case **where there is less than an olive-bulk** of the **carcass,** which is not susceptible to the impurity of a carcass, **and one combined it with less than an egg-bulk** of **food,** so **that this and that** together **are an egg-bulk,** which is the minimum amount of food that is susceptible to food impurity. Rav raised a difficulty to Rabbi Ḥiyya: **If so,** that less than an olive-bulk of an animal carcass does not transmit impurity, **it should also require** contact with a liquid in order to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity as a food. **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that with regard to the impurity of foods, the verse states: **"Upon any sowing seed which is to be sown"** (Leviticus 11:37), which teaches: **Just as seeds are unique** in **that they will not ultimately become impure** with **a severe** form of **ritual impurity and** they **require** contact with liquid in order to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity, **so too, anything that will not ultimately become impure** with **a severe** form of **ritual impurity requires** contact with liquid in order to be **rendered susceptible** to impurity. Rabbi Ḥiyya **said to** Rav: **This statement** applies **to foods in general, which do not contain less than an olive-bulk of an animal carcass. But here, where** the food **does contain less than an olive-bulk of an animal carcass,** the *halakha* is different: **Since** it would be impure with a severe form of ritual impurity **if one were to add to** the amount of animal carcass so that there would be a full **olive-bulk,** it **does not require** contact with a liquid in order **to be rendered susceptible** to impurity. In any case, this *halakha* that less than an olive-bulk of a carcass combines with less than an egg-bulk of food to equal a full egg-bulk that is susceptible to food impurity, applies only to the flesh of an animal,

Why does a *neveilah* not require *hechsher*? Because anything destined to carry a heavy *tumah* is exempt from the liquid-contact prerequisite, as derived from the school of *Rabbi Yishmael*: just as seeds require *hechsher* because they will not themselves become sources of heavy *tumah*, so too only things that will not become sources of heavy *tumah* require *hechsher*. Since *neveilah* itself will become a source of *tumah*, it needs no prior contact with liquid.

The principle is thus: meat of *neveilah* does not require *hechsher* because it will itself be a source of heavy *tumah*. But it may still require *machshavah*, since not every type of *neveilah* is automatically assumed to be desired for consumption. The fact that a human corpse requires neither — because *tumas meis* supersedes all food-status categories — is precisely what the earlier analysis demonstrated. The *neveilah* of an animal, by contrast, does not completely eliminate the relevance of food classification, as will be shown.

### Half-Kezayit

The *gemara* notes a certain parallel between the *koy* and the *androginos* — the *androginos* likewise potentially constitutes a *min bifnei atzmo* — and the discussion of the *koy* here illustrates just how far-reaching that premise can be once it is accepted.

### Encased Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חֲנַנְיָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דְּהָוֵי כְּזַיִת, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁחִיפָּהוּ בְּבָצֵק. אִי הָכִי, הֶכְשֵׁר נָמֵי נִיבְעֵי! הָנֵי מִילֵּי בָּאוֹכָלִים אַחֲרִינֵי, דְּלָא מְטַמּוּ לָא בְּמַגָּע וְלָא בְּמַשָּׂא. אֲבָל הָכָא, נְהִי דְּלָא מְטַמֵּא בְּמַגָּע, דְּקָמְחַפֵּי בְּבָצֵק, בְּמַשָּׂא מִיהַת לִיטַמֵּא, דְּהָא טָעֵון לֵיהּ. לְאַפּוֹקֵי מֵת, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּחִיפָּהוּ בְּבָצֵק מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, דְּטוּמְאָה בּוֹקַעַת וְעוֹלָה בּוֹקַעַת וְיוֹרֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥananya said:** With regard to the assertion that the carcass of a non-kosher animal becomes susceptible to the impurity of food only if one intends to eat it, **you may even say that** the piece of the carcass **is an olive-bulk. What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where one covered it with** less than an egg-bulk of **dough.** Since it is covered with dough the carcass cannot come into contact with anything else in order to transmit impurity to it, and since there is less than an egg-bulk of dough, the dough itself is not susceptible to the impurity of food. But if the carcass and the dough combined are a full egg-bulk, they are susceptible to the impurity of food and can transmit that impurity to other food items. The Gemara objects: **If so, let it also require** contact with a liquid in order **to be rendered susceptible** to the impurity of food. The Gemara responds: **That statement** applies **with regard to other** kinds of **food, which do not transmit impurity** at all, **neither through** direct **contact nor through carrying. But here, granted that** the piece of animal carcass **does not transmit impurity through contact, as it is covered with dough, it should at least transmit impurity through carrying, as it is laden upon him,** despite the fact that he does not touch it. According to Rav Ḥananya, it is in this regard that a light form of impurity, the impurity of food, applies to an animal after death. This is **to the exclusion of** a human **corpse, as even if one covered it with dough it transmits a severe** form of **ritual impurity, as** the **impurity** of a corpse **breaks through and ascends,** and **breaks through and descends,** even when it is covered.

*Rabbi Chanina* offers an alternative scenario for the relevance of food classification. Rather than a sub-*kezayit* of *neveilah*, suppose there is a full *kezayit* of *neveilah* enclosed within dough — the classic case of a *kreplach*-like structure — where the combined meat-and-dough is the size of a *kebaitza*. If something *tamei* touches the outside of the dough, it does not come into contact with the *neveilah* inside. Therefore the item cannot receive *tumah* simply by virtue of the *neveilah* it contains — touching the outer dough does not constitute touching the *neveilah*. However, by classifying the whole structure as food, the outer dough can receive *tumat ochlin* when touched by something *tamei*, since together they constitute a *kebaitza* of food.

The *gemara* challenges: if the enclosed *neveilah* is not transmitting *tumah* through touch, why does the structure not require *hechsher*? The answer is that the *neveilah* inside can still transmit *tumah* through *masa* — carrying — even if not through direct contact. Since the *neveilah*, when carried, does transmit *tumah*, the exemption from *hechsher* applies. The relevance of food classification is thus limited to cases of touch on the outer surface, where the carrier does not come into direct contact with the *neveilah* itself.

Interestingly, this second treatment of the corpse question does not invoke *batla da'ato etzel kol adam* — the principle used earlier. Here the *gemara* reasons instead from the mechanics of *tumas meis*, which renders food classification structurally moot regardless of whether anyone would conceivably eat such flesh. This framing offers a more internally consistent treatment: from the standpoint of the *tumah* laws, live human flesh and dead human flesh are treated symmetrically as potential foods in principle — it is only the overpowering, penetrating nature of *tumas meis* that makes the designation irrelevant for the corpse, not a categorical exclusion from food status. This is admittedly still somewhat puzzling given the earlier appeal to *batla da'ato*, but at least the second passage does not compound the difficulty.

## Blood Sources

### Fish/Locust Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: יָצָא דַּם דָּגִים וְדַם חֲגָבִים שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ הֶיתֵּר. מַאי כּוּלּוֹ הֶיתֵּר? אִילֵימָא דְּחֶלְבּוֹ מוּתָּר – הֲרֵי חַיָּה דְּחֶלְבָּהּ מוּתָּר וְדָמָהּ אָסוּר! וְאֶלָּא: מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵית בְּהוּ אִיסּוּר גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה? וַהֲרֵי עוֹף דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ אִיסּוּר גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה, וְדָמוֹ אָסוּר! אֶלָּא מַאי כּוּלּוֹ הֶיתֵּר – דְּלָא בָּעֵי שְׁחִיטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said** in the *baraita*: The **blood of fish and** the **blood of grasshoppers** have been **excluded, as they are entirely permitted.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the clause: **They are entirely permitted? If we say** it is referring to the fact **that** all the **fat** of fish and grasshoppers is **permitted,** one may respond that all the **fat of an undomesticated animal** is also **permitted, and** yet **its blood is forbidden.And** if you say: **Rather,** fish and grasshoppers are referred to as entirely permitted **because the prohibition of the sciatic nerve does not apply to them,** this is also untenable; **but** in the case of **a bird there is no prohibition** against eating **the sciatic nerve and** even so, **its blood** is **forbidden. Rather, what** is the meaning of the statement that fish and grasshoppers **are entirely permitted?** This is referring to the *halakha* **that** fish and grasshoppers **do not require** ritual **slaughter** to permit their consumption.

With the structural analysis of the *issur dam* complete, the *gemara* turns to a series of specific blood categories and their halachic status. A *baraita* has stated that the blood of fish and grasshoppers is *mutar* because they are *kulo heter* — entirely permissible. The *gemara* interrogates what this phrase means. It cannot mean that all their parts may be eaten, since a *chayah* likewise has no prohibited *chelev* and yet its blood is forbidden. Nor can it mean they have no *gid hanasheh*, since birds also lack a *gid hanasheh* prohibition yet their blood is forbidden. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *d'lo ba'ei shechitah* — they do not require *shechitah*. Fish and grasshoppers have no *issurim* that *shechitah* comes to address; they may be eaten live, and their entire status is one of unqualified permissibility. That is the characteristic that distinguishes them from birds and animals and justifies excluding their blood from the prohibition.

### Rodent Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: דַּם שְׁרָצִים לוֹקִין עָלָיו בִּכְזַיִת. מֵיתִיבִי: דַּם הַטְּחוֹל, דַּם הַלֵּב, דַּם הַכְּלָיוֹת, דַּם אֵבָרִים – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה. דַּם מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם, דַּם שְׁרָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים – אָסוּר, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו! מַאי אֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו – כָּרֵת, אֲ[בָל] חֲדָא, דְּהַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא. וְעוֹד, תַּנָּא מִלָּאו נָמֵי קָא מְמַעֵט לֵיהּ, דְּקָתָנֵי: יָצָא דַּם שְׁרָצִים, שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה! אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: הִתְרוּ בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם שֶׁרֶץ – לוֹקֶה, מִשּׁוּם דָּם – אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to the **blood of creeping animals, one is flogged for** consuming **it in** the amount of **an olive-bulk.** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **blood of the spleen, blood of the heart, blood of the kidneys,** and **blood of limbs,** all **these are** forbidden for consumption **by** a regular **prohibition,** punishable by lashes, but their consumption is not punishable by *karet*. By contrast, the **blood of bipeds,** i.e., human beings, and the **blood of creeping animals and crawling things** are **forbidden but one is not liable for** their consumption. This indicates that one is not even flogged for consuming the blood of creeping animals. The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of the clause: **One is not liable for** its consumption? It means that one is not liable to receive ***karet*** for consuming it, but one **is liable for** violating **a prohibition.** The Gemara challenges this answer: **One** reason this is not a good explanation is **that this is** the same as **the first clause.** The first clause of the *baraita* lists types of blood whose consumption is a violation of a regular prohibition and is not punishable by *karet*. Clearly, the latter clause cannot also be referring to types of blood in the same category. **And furthermore, a *tanna* excludes** consumption of this blood **from** a regular **prohibition as well, as** the *baraita* cited earlier **teaches:** The **blood of creeping animals** has been **excluded, as it does not have a severe** form of **ritual impurity.** **Rabbi Zeira said:** When Rav said that one is flogged for consuming the blood of creeping animals, he meant that if witnesses **forewarned him** that if he consumes the blood he will be liable **due to** the prohibition of eating **a creeping animal,** then **he is flogged.** The reason is that the prohibition against eating a creeping animal includes its blood. But if they forewarned him that he will be liable **due to** the prohibition of consuming **blood, he is not flogged.**

The *gemara* then records a statement of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav*: *dam sheratzim lokim alav* — one who eats a *kezayit* of rodent blood receives lashes. A *baraita* is cited that appears to contradict this: it lists blood of bipeds, blood of *sheratzim* and *remasim* as forbidden, but specifies *ve-ein chayavin alav* — one does not incur the transgression. The *gemara* attempts to argue that *ein chayavin alav* means only that one does not incur *karet*, but not that there is no *lav*. Two responses are given. First, if there were a *lav* for rodent blood, the *baraita* would have listed it alongside the other categories subject to a *lav* but not *karet*, such as blood of internal organs and limbs — yet it does not. Second, the *baraita* that has occupied the entire daf explicitly excludes *sheratzim* from the prohibition verse, *yatza dam sheratzim she-ain bahem tumah chamurah*, indicating that the exclusion operates at the level of the prohibition itself, not merely at the level of *karet*.

*Rabbi Zeira* resolves the apparent ruling of *Rav*: *hisru bo mishum sheretz lokeh* — if someone was warned that the blood constitutes *sheretz* and then consumed it, he receives lashes, but not on account of the blood prohibition. Rather, blood of a rodent is treated as part of the meat of the rodent itself, and one transgresses the prohibition of eating *sheretz*, not the prohibition of blood. The principle at work here is that blood is treated as part of the meat of the creature from which it comes, at least for the purposes of applying other prohibitions. This connects to the earlier debate between *Beis Shammai* and *Beis Hillel* regarding whether blood of *neveilah* transmits *tumah* like the meat of *neveilah* — the underlying logic is the same: blood is of a piece with the flesh.

### Human Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: דַּם דָּגִים שֶׁכִּינְּסוֹ – אָסוּר. מֵיתִיבִי: דַּם דָּגִים, דַּם חֲגָבִים – מוּתָּר, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה! הָהִיא בְּשֶׁלֹּא כִּינְּסוֹ, כִּי קָא אָמַר רַב – בְּשֶׁכִּינְּסוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav says: Fish blood that one collected** in a receptacle is **prohibited** for consumption because it would look as though one is consuming the blood of an animal or bird. The Gemara **raises an objection** from the latter clause of the *baraita* cited above: The **blood of fish** and the **blood of grasshoppers are permitted, and** this indicates that they are permitted **even *ab initio*.** The Gemara answers: **That** *baraita* is referring to a case **where he did not collect** the blood in a receptacle; **when Rav said** that it is prohibited, he was referring to a situation **where he did collect** the blood in a receptacle.

*Rav* states: *dam dagim she-kinso assur* — if one collects fish blood in a vessel, it is forbidden. The *gemara* immediately asks from the *baraita* that explicitly permits fish blood. The *gemara* resolves: the *baraita*'s permission applies when the blood was not deliberately collected; *Rav*'s prohibition applies when it was gathered together. The concern when collected is one of appearance — it resembles animal blood, and consuming it in that form creates a *marit ayin* issue.

### Fish vs. Human

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְווֹתַהּ גַּבֵּי מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם – בְּשֶׁלֹּא כִּינְּסוֹ, מִי אָסוּר? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: דָּם שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי כִּכָּר – גּוֹרְרוֹ וְאוֹכְלוֹ, שֶׁל בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם – מוֹצְצוֹ וּבוֹלְעוֹ וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ! אֶלָּא: כִּי תַּנְיָא הַהִיא מַתְנִיתָא – דְּאִית בֵּיהּ קַשְׂקַשִּׂים, כִּי קָאָמַר רַב אָסוּר – דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ קַשְׂקַשִּׂים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this answer: **In the corresponding** situation **with regard to** the **blood of bipeds,** which the *baraita* states is forbidden, the case must also be one **where one did not collect** the blood in a receptacle. In such a case, **is** the blood really **forbidden? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If some human **blood was on a loaf** of bread, one **scrapes off** the blood **and** then he **may eat** the bread. But with regard to blood that is **between** one's **teeth, he may suck it and swallow it without concern,** as the blood is permitted if it has not been removed from the body. This indicates that even human blood that has not been collected in a receptacle is permitted. **Rather,** the *baraita* rules that fish blood is permitted even when it has been collected in a receptacle, and **when that *baraita* is taught,** it is referring to a case **where there are scales in it** and therefore it is clearly fish blood. By contrast, **when Rav said** that it is **prohibited**, he was referring to a case **where there are no scales in** the blood, and therefore it is not evident that it is fish blood.

With this distinction in place, the *gemara* raises a further question: the same *baraita* that permitted fish blood stated that human blood carries some prohibition. If fish blood is only problematic when collected, the *baraita*'s statement about human blood must also be referring to the uncollected case — which would mean that even uncollected human blood is forbidden. But this seems too strict. The *gemara* resolves by refining the fish-blood ruling: the *baraita*'s permission for fish blood applies specifically when the fish has its scales — *de-it bei kaskasin* — present together with the blood, even in a cup. The scales serve as an identifying marker, dispelling the appearance of animal blood and removing the *marit ayin* concern. *Rav*'s prohibition applies when the blood is collected and there are no scales present to identify it.

The comparison to human blood follows accordingly. Human blood collected in a cup and without any identifying feature is forbidden by rabbinic enactment. Uncollected human blood — such as blood that incidentally drips onto food — is not technically forbidden but falls under a norm of *mitzvot prosh*, of avoiding unseemly behavior rather than a formal *issur*.

### Gum Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: דַּם מְהַלְּכֵי שְׁתַּיִם – אֲפִילּוּ מִצְוַת פְּרוֹשׁ אֵין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says:** With regard to the **blood** of **bipeds, there is not even a command to abstain** from consuming it *ab initio*.

The *gemara* cites a *baraita* that illustrates the practical boundary: if one has bleeding gums and blood gets onto a piece of bread, one should scrape it off before eating. But blood that is still between one's teeth may simply be swallowed without concern. *Rashi* explains that the instruction to scrape away the blood on the bread reflects a norm of *mitzvot prosh* — conduct befitting propriety — rather than a formal rabbinic prohibition; the *baraita* conspicuously does not use the word *assur*. Blood that remains in the mouth, still on one's own body, is a fortiori unproblematic. Exactly where the line falls between the clearly permitted — blood still in the mouth — and the unseemly but not forbidden — blood on the bread — and the actually prohibited — collected human blood in a cup — is a question raised by *Tosafot* elsewhere, and one whose full elaboration goes beyond the scope of the present daf.
