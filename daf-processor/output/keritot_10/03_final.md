# Keritot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sequential Births

### Intro: Multiple Events

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁיָּלְדָה וְלָדוֹת הַרְבֵּה, וְהִפִּילָה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים נְקֵבָה, חָזְרָה וְהִפִּילָה בְּתוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים נְקֵבָה, וְהַמַּפֶּלֶת תְּאוֹמִים, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מְבִיאָה עַל הָרִאשׁוֹן וְאֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עַל הַשֵּׁנִי. מְבִיאָה עַל הַשְּׁלִישִׁי וְאֵינָהּ מְבִיאָה עַל הָרְבִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A woman who gave birth to several offspring.** This is a case where a woman gave birth to a daughter, after which she is ritually impure for fourteen days and then enters a period of sixty-six days of ritual purity, even if she experiences uterine bleeding. Nevertheless, during this interim period, she is still somewhat impure, and it is therefore prohibited for her to enter the Temple or to partake of consecrated food, and at the end of the period she must bring an offering. **And** during those days of ritual purity, **she** became pregnant again and then **miscarried a female** fetus **within** the **eighty** days, **and then** became pregnant **again and miscarried** another **female** fetus **within eighty** days of the first miscarriage. In this situation, when she ultimately completes her process of purification, she brings one single offering for all the births and miscarriages. **And** a similar *halakha* applies to a woman **who miscarries multiple** fetuses from a single pregnancy at different points in time, miscarrying each fetus before completing the purification period of forty days for a male or eighty days for a female for the previous fetus. When she finally completes her process of purification, she brings one single offering for all of the miscarriages. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In these cases, a single offering does not suffice for all the births or miscarriages. Rather, **she brings** an offering **for the first** birth or miscarriage and **does not bring** an offering **for the second** miscarriage, as it took place before the completion of the purification period for the first. **She** then **brings** an offering **for the third** miscarriage **and does not bring** an offering **for the fourth** fetus, as it was miscarried before the completion of the purification period for the third fetus.

The Mishnah at the bottom of 9b addresses the final case among a series dealing with bringing a single *korban* for multiple events — not for sins, but for *korbanot* related to *taharah*. Having already treated sequential *tzara'at* and sequential *nezirut*, the Mishnah now turns to sequential childbirths. The question is whether, when a second birth or miscarriage occurs within the eighty-day period of a female birth, a single *korban* suffices for all events at the end of the process.

According to the *Chachamim*, since there was never a moment at which the obligation to bring a *korban* for the first birth had matured, the second birth simply extends the process, and only one *korban* is required at the conclusion. Rabbi Yehuda, however, holds that while the second birth is incorporated into the first, this does not mean they merge into one seamless process. Rather, the second is absorbed into the first for *korban* purposes — with the result that even-numbered births are subsumed under the odd-numbered births that precede them (two is incorporated into one, four into three, and so on). The odd-numbered births remain independently obligating: one, three, five, and so forth each require their own *korban*.

### Incorporation Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּטוּמְאָה מָה לִי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה? מִי אָמְרִינַן: עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וָלָד שֵׁנִי כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן קׇרְבָּן, כֵּיוָן דְּלֹא יָצְאָה שָׁעָה שֶׁרְאוּיָה לְהַקְרִיב בָּהּ קׇרְבָּן, הוֹאִיל וְכֵן – וָלָד שֵׁנִי כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן טוּמְאָה וְטׇהֳרָה, אֵימָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּמַאן דְּאִיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, וּמַפְסְקָא טוּמְאָה דְּשֵׁנִי, וּמְמַלֵּא יוֹמֵי טׇהֳרָה דְּרִאשׁוֹן וַהֲדַר מָנֵי לֵיהּ יוֹמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Concerning ritual impurity, what does Rabbi Yehuda say? Do we say** that **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the **second offspring is** considered **as though it does not** exist **only with regard to the matter of an offering?** In other words, **since the time** in **which** it was **possible to sacrifice an offering** for the second fetus **had not** yet **emerged, since that is so,** the **second offspring is** considered **as though it does not** exist. **But concerning the matter of ritual impurity and purity,** we **say** that **he holds** that it is considered **as something that does** exist, **and** accordingly, the days of **impurity** brought on **by** the **second** birth **interrupt** the days of purity following the first birth. The woman must therefore observe the days of impurity for the second birth, **and** afterward **she completes the days of purity for** the **first** birth, **and then counts the days of purity** brought on **by the second** fetus.

The *Gemara* opens 10a with a *ba'aya*: *betuma mai amar Rabbi Yehuda* — what would Rabbi Yehuda say when the question is not one of *korban* obligation but of *tumah* and *taharah*? To appreciate the force of this question, consider the basic framework. After giving birth to a male, a woman is *tamei* for seven days and then *tehorah* for the subsequent thirty-three days, meaning that any bleeding during those thirty-three days does not render her a *niddah*. For a female birth, these periods are doubled: fourteen days of *tumah* and sixty-six days of *taharah*. Now suppose a second birth occurs during the *taharah* period of the first. The second birth creates a fresh period of *tumah*, interrupting the *taharah* days that had been accumulating from the first birth. The question is whether, according to Rabbi Yehuda, that interrupted *taharah* must then be "made up" before the *taharah* of the second birth commences.

### Interloper Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה חוּמְרָא הוּא דְּאִית לֵיהּ, אֲבָל הָכָא – קוּלָּא הוּא, וְקוּלָּא לֵית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps, according to Rabbi Yehuda,** the second offspring is considered as though it exists only where **there is a stringency** that results from that definition. **But here,** if the woman would complete the days of purity of the first birth after the days of impurity of the second, **this is a leniency,** as she is deemed pure even if she sees blood during these days, **and** therefore, perhaps Rabbi Yehuda **does not hold** that the offspring is considered existent where this results in **a leniency.**

The *Gemara* formulates the question precisely. One might argue: *hatam hu dekamar lei Rabbi Yehuda vlad sheni kemahn deleit damei inyan korban* — Rabbi Yehuda only says the second birth is "as if it were not present" for purposes of *korban* obligation, because there had never yet been a moment at which a *korban* could have been brought for the first birth. But regarding *tumah v'taharah*, perhaps he holds *vlad sheni kemahn de'it damei* — the second birth retains its independent presence, *umifsaka tuma desheni* — the *tumah* of the second birth interrupts the first, and *u'mimallei yomei tahara d'rishon* — the remaining *taharah* days of the first birth must be completed before the *taharah* period of the second birth begins.

This is the "interloper" framing. If the second birth is genuinely independent within the first birth's period, it interrupts that period and entitles the woman to recover the days that were taken from her — first completing the *taharah* of the first birth, and only then proceeding to the *taharah* of the second.

### Yehuda's Stringency

On the other hand, the *Gemara* presents a counter-framing: *o dilma l'Rabbi Yehuda chumra hu d'it lei* — perhaps Rabbi Yehuda's incorporation doctrine is driven entirely by stringency. He incorporates the second birth into the first in order to require a *korban* for every other birth in the sequence (one, three, five...), which is the more stringent outcome compared to the *Rabbanan*, who require only a single *korban* at the very end. But *aval hacha kula hu v'kula leit lei* — if incorporation would lead to a leniency, as it would here by giving the woman additional *taharah* days after the fact, Rabbi Yehuda would not apply it.

The *Gemara* thus frames the question not merely as a conceptual inquiry about whether two births form one process, but as a question about what principle drives Rabbi Yehuda's doctrine of incorporation: is it a coherent view that these births form a single unified event, or is it a *chumra*-driven rule that only applies when it produces greater stringency?

## Pesach Offering Case

### Problem Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא מִסּוּרָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: יוֹלֶדֶת, שׁוֹחֲטִין וְזוֹרְקִין עָלֶיהָ בְּיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים לַזָּכָר וּבְיוֹם שְׁמוֹנִים לַנְּקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna from Sura said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: In the case of **a woman after childbirth, one slaughters** the Paschal offering **and sprinkles** its blood **on her account** on the fourteenth of Nisan, if this occurs **on** the **fortieth day** after giving birth **to a male or on** the **eightieth day** after giving birth **to a female.** Although she is still impure at the time of the sacrifice, she will be able to partake of the Paschal offering on the night of the fifteenth of Nisan.

The *Gemara* brings a proof from a *baraita* cited by Rav Huna of Sura. The *baraita* states: *yoledes shochtin v'zorkin aleha b'yom arba'im l'zachar u'v'yom shemonim l'nekeiva* — one may slaughter and sprinkle the blood of a *korban Pesach* on behalf of a woman who gave birth, on day forty for a male birth or day eighty for a female birth. This is deeply relevant to the season, as the discussion concerns a woman in the final stages of her post-birth process on *erev Pesach*.

The difficulty is immediate. A *korban Pesach* is brought only for someone who will be able to eat it that night. Now consider this woman's status. After her seven or fourteen days of *tumah*, she immerses in the *mikvah* and enters a lengthy period that functions as a *tvul yom aroch* — a prolonged residual *tumah* state. Like an ordinary *tvul yom* who has immersed but must wait until nightfall to eat *terumah* and *korbanot*, this woman's thirty-three or sixty-six days of *taharah* represent a waiting period before she may eat *kodashim*. She is substantially *tahor* and may not be rendered a *niddah* by ordinary bleeding, but she remains restricted from *korbanot* until the completion of the forty or eighty days.

Furthermore, even at the close of day forty or eighty, her purification is not fully complete. She is now *mechussar kipurim* — lacking the final purification offering she must bring the following morning to be permitted to eat *korbanot*. So if day forty or eighty is *erev Pesach*, and she brings her *korban Pesach* that afternoon, how can she eat it that night, when she is still *mechussar kipurim*?

### Twin Births Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן? טְמֵאָה הִיא! וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּאָמַר: וָלָד שֵׁנִי כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages challenged this *baraita*: **Is that so?** But **she is** still **impure** on the night of the fifteenth of Nisan, and she may not partake of sacrificial meat until she brings her offering on the forty-first day for a male or the eighty-first day for a female. **And Rav Ḥisda said** in response: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that the **second offspring is deemed as though it does not** exist. The *baraita* is dealing with a case where the woman gave birth to two male offspring on two consecutive days, and the fourteenth of Nisan occurred on the fortieth day after the birth of the second offspring, which is the forty-first day following the birth of the first.

The *Gemara* initially answers with a citation from Rav Chisda: *ha mani Rabbi Yehuda, de'amar vlad sheni keman d'leisa dami* — this *baraita* follows Rabbi Yehuda, who holds the second birth is "as if not present." The scenario is one of twins, where a female was born one day and a male the next. When the *baraita* says "day forty," it does not mean day forty from the first birth (the female); it means day forty from the second birth (the male) — which is day forty-one from the first. Since forty-one days have elapsed from the female birth, her *taharah* process from that birth is complete and she may eat that night.

As for the male birth — day forty from him has arrived, but his period is not yet fully concluded. How can she be permitted? The *Gemara*'s answer implies that according to Rabbi Yehuda, the second birth is so thoroughly incorporated into the first that its completion is defined by the completion of the first birth. Once the first birth's period is over, the combined process is over, even if forty days have not elapsed from the second birth individually.

### Rejection: Twin Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּטוּמְאָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וָלָד שֵׁנִי כְּמַאן דְּאִיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, הֵיכִי שָׁחֲטִינַן עֲלַהּ בְּיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים? לְאוּרְתָּא נָמֵי לָא מָצְיָא אָכְלָה! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינָּה: לְטׇהֳרָה וּלְטוּמְאָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וָלָד שֵׁנִי כְּמַאן דְּלֵיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **concerning impurity, Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the **second offspring is** considered **as though it is** extant, which means that the days of purity following the first birth are delayed by the second birth and the mother does not become pure until the second term is complete, **how may one slaughter** the Paschal offering **on her account on the fortieth day?** After all, **in the evening** of the fifteenth **she also may not eat** the Paschal offering, because only forty days will have passed from the time of the second birth, and she would still be impure. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** it that **concerning ritual purity and impurity, Rabbi Yehuda holds** that the **second offspring is** considered **as** though **it does not** exist?

The *Gemara* rejects this answer sharply. *Ve'eima bituma sevira lei leRabbi Yehuda vlad sheni keman d'isa dami, heichi shachtinan aleih b'yom mem le'osa nami lo matzi achla* — if Rabbi Yehuda holds that for *tumah v'taharah* the second birth is independent, then "day forty" in the *baraita* cannot mean day forty from the second birth (which is day forty-one from the first), because on that day the second birth's own process has not concluded, and she cannot eat that night. The *Gemara* presses the point: *ve'i amarta betumah v'taharah l'Rabbi Yehuda vlad sheni keman d'iteih dami, heichi shachtinan aleih b'yoma?*

If the second birth is independent for *tumah* purposes — and if that independence means she may even be entitled to "make up" *taharah* days — then she certainly cannot bring her *korban Pesach* before that second process is complete. On the other hand, if the *baraita* is saying she *can* bring it on day forty from the second (day forty-one from the first), this would only make sense if the second birth is fully subsumed within the first, with no independent standing whatsoever. The *Gemara* does not want to commit to that conclusion prematurely, so it abandons the twin-births answer entirely. *Ela shema mina* could have resolved matters, but the *Gemara* instead offers a fresh approach: *le-olam eima lach... ve-chi tanya hi be-Pesach ha-ba b'tumah* — the case is one where the entire congregation is *tamei*, and the *korban Pesach* is therefore brought in a state of communal *tumah*.

### Pesach BeAla'im Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: לְטוּמְאָה וּלְטׇהֳרָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּוָלָד שֵׁנִי כְּמַאן דְּאִיתֵיהּ דָּמֵי, וְכִי תַנְיָא הַהִיא – בְּפֶסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **Actually, I** could **say to you:** Even **concerning impurity and purity, Rabbi Yehuda holds that** the **second offspring is considered as something that does** exist; **and when that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is dealing with a case of a woman who gave birth to one child, and it is referring to a case **of a Paschal offering that is brought in impurity.** When the majority of a community is impure, the Paschal offering is brought even in impurity.

When the majority of the congregation is *tamei* — typically *tamei meit* — the *korban Pesach* is offered and eaten *b'tumah*, with the entire community participating despite their *tumah*. In such circumstances, this woman, though not yet fully *tehorah*, could potentially eat as well. This resolves the eating question — but immediately raises another: the *baraita* specifies day forty or eighty; if the solution lies in communal *tumah*, why does the day matter at all? Let her participate from day eight or day fifteen, once she has immersed in the *mikvah*!

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָכְלָה? וְהָתְנַן: פֶּסַח הַבָּא בְּטוּמְאָה, לֹא יֹאכְלוּ מִמֶּנּוּ זָבִים וְזָבוֹת, נִדּוֹת וְיוֹלְדוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And** in that case, **does she partake** of it, as the *baraita* suggests? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Pesaḥim* 95b): With regard to **a Paschal offering brought in impurity, men who experience a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zavim*], women who experience a discharge of uterine blood after their menstrual periods [*zavot*], menstruating women, and women after childbirth may not partake of it?**

The *Gemara* raises a prior objection first. The *baraita* teaches: *Pesach haba b'tumah lo yochlu mimenu zavim v'zavos nidos v'yoldos* — even when the *Pesach* is brought in a state of communal *tumah*, those whose *tumah* originates from within their own bodies — *zavim*, *zavot*, *niddot*, and *yoldot* — may not eat. A woman who has just given birth falls squarely within this exclusion. How, then, can she eat at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא, לָא אָכְלָן כִּי לָא טְבִילָן. כִּי תַנְיָא הַהִיא דְּשׁוֹחֲטִין וְזוֹרְקִין עָלֶיהָ, דְּהָא טָבְלָה. אִי הָכִי, מִשְּׁמִינִי דִּילַיהּ הוּא דְּחַזְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In **that** case, **they do not partake** of it **because they did not immerse** themselves in a ritual bath. But **when that** mishna **is taught, that one slaughters** the Paschal offering **and sprinkles** its blood **on her account,** the reason is **that she** had already **immersed** herself on the eighth day after giving birth. The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** then **it is from** the **eighth** day following **his** birth **that** she **is fit** to eat a Paschal offering brought in impurity, and there is no reason for the *baraita* to specify that the offering is slaughtered on the fortieth day.

The *Gemara* answers: *ki lo tavlin* — the exclusion applies only when she has not yet immersed. *D'tavla* — in our case, she has already gone to the *mikvah*, and her *tumah* is therefore residual, not active. But this returns us to the earlier question: once she has immersed, why does it matter whether it is day eight or day forty?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשְּׁמִינִי לָא חַזְיָא, קָסָבַר: טְבוּל יוֹם דְּזָב – כְּזָב דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Actually, she **is not fit from** the **eighth day,** because the *tanna* **maintains** that **a *zav* who immersed that day** and is waiting for nightfall for the purification process to be completed **is considered** to have the status **of a *zav*** and may not partake of a Paschal offering brought in ritual impurity. Similarly, a woman who immerses after giving birth is considered like one who immersed that day, and therefore she cannot partake of sacrificial meat until the end of the forty-day period.

The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar tvul yom d'zav k'zav dami* — even after immersion, during the extended *tvul yom* period (her thirty-three or sixty-six days), her residual *tumah* retains the character of the original *tumah*. She is not merely a generic *tvul yom*; she is still considered a *yoledet* in a residual state, and that status excludes her even from *Pesach haba b'tumah*. This is why day eight or day fifteen would not suffice.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בְּיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים נָמֵי לָא חַזְיָא! לָאיֵי, בְּיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים חַזְיָא, קָסָבַר: מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים דְּזָב – לָאו כְּזָב דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** then **on** the **fortieth day she is also not fit** to eat the Paschal offering. The Gemara explains: This is **not so; on** the **fortieth day she is fit** to partake of the Paschal offering after nightfall, because the *tanna* **holds** that **a *zav*** who has immersed himself and waited for nightfall but is **lacking atonement,** i.e., he has not yet brought an atonement offering to complete the purification process, **is not considered** to have the status **of a *zav*.** Therefore, after completing the purification period, the woman may partake of the offering, despite the fact that she has not yet brought her atonement offering.

The *Gemara* then presses further: *ei hachi, b'yomam nami lo chazya* — if the residual *tumah* retains the character of the original, then even on day forty she cannot eat, because she still has not brought her *korban* and remains *mechussar kipurim* with the status of a *yoledet*! The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar mechusar kipurim d'zav lav k'zav dami* — at the final stage of the purification process, when one is merely *mechussar kipurim*, the residual *tumah* is no longer characterized by the original status. A *mechusar kipurim* is simply a person who needs to bring a *korban* — a generic, label-free deficiency. Since on the night after day forty she carries only this generic designation and is no longer classified as a *yoledet* in any meaningful sense, she may eat a *Pesach* that is brought *b'tumah*.

This produces a precise and carefully delimited ruling: not day eight, not day fifteen, but specifically day forty or eighty — because only at that stage does the original *tumah* character dissolve into the generic status of *mechusar kipurim*, permitting her to eat the communal *Pesach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר מְחוּסַּר כִּפּוּרִים דְּזָב כְּזָב דָּמֵי, הָא מַתְנִייתָא הֵיכִי מְתָרֵץ לַהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: רָבָא מְתָרֵץ לַהּ בְּיוֹם אַרְבָּעִים לִיצִירַת זָכָר וּבְיוֹם שְׁמוֹנִים לִיצִירַת נְקֵבָה, וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דְּאָמַר: לְזָכָר אַרְבָּעִים וְאֶחָד, לִנְקֵבָה שְׁמוֹנִים וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But according to Rava, who says** that **a *zav* who** is **lacking atonement is considered** to have the status **of a *zav*, how does he resolve** the problem in **this *baraita*? Rav Ashi said** that **Rava resolves it** as follows: The *baraita* is not referring to the fortieth day after the birth of a male, but to a miscarriage that occurred on the **fortieth day after the conception of a male, or** the **eightieth day after the conception of a female. And it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says** that it takes **forty-one** days **for a male** fetus to be formed, and **eighty-one** days **for a female** fetus to be formed. Since the miscarriage occurred before the forty-first or eighty-first day, it does not render the woman impure with the impurity of childbirth, and therefore she may partake of the Paschal offering.

The *Gemara* then raises the view of Rava, who holds *mechusar kipurim d'zav k'zav dami* — that even at the final *mechusar kipurim* stage, the original *tumah* character persists. According to Rava, she would still be classified as a *yoledet* and excluded even from *Pesach haba b'tumah*. How, then, does Rava explain the *baraita* about day forty and eighty?

### Yishmael Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, תִּיפּוֹק לִי דִּטְמֵאָה הִיא מִשּׁוּם נִדָּה! – בְּלֵידָה יַבִּישְׁתָּא. אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָה? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִי אֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לִפְתִיחַת הַקֶּבֶר בְּלֹא דָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But **ultimately,** one should **conclude that she is impure because** she has the status of **a menstruating woman.** The Gemara explains: The mishna was stated **with regard to** the case of **a dry birth,** in which no blood was expelled, and therefore no impurity imparted. The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** then **what** is the purpose **of stating it?** The ruling in such a case is obvious. The Gemara answers: It was necessary to state it, **lest you say** that it is **impossible for the womb to open without blood** being expelled. The *tanna* therefore **teaches us that it is possible for the womb to open without blood** being expelled. Accordingly, Rabbi Yehuda's opinion with regard to the impurity of a second birth cannot be deduced from this mishna.

Rav Ashi answers on Rava's behalf: *Rava metaretz lah b'yom mem l'yetzirat zachar u'v'yom shemonim l'yetzirat nekeivah, v'Rabbi Yishmael hi* — Rava resolves the *baraita* by invoking Rabbi Yishmael's principle that a fetus is only considered fully formed and viable from day forty-one for a male and day eighty-one for a female. When the *baraita* speaks of "day forty" or "day eighty," it refers not to day forty from birth but to day forty from conception — i.e., the day on which a potential pregnancy would first attain the status of a viable fetus.

If a woman miscarries on day forty of gestation (or day eighty for a female fetus), the fetus has not yet reached the threshold of viability under Rabbi Yishmael's principle. She is therefore not *temeiah leidah* — the miscarriage does not trigger the *tumah* of childbirth — and she may eat the *korban Pesach* that night.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges: *sof sof tipta d'temeiah hi mishum niddah* — even if there is no *tumah* of childbirth, there is certainly blood, and any uterine blood renders her a *niddah*. The *Gemara* answers: *b'vladah yaveshta* — the case involves a dry birth, a miscarriage without accompanying blood. This is admittedly difficult to imagine, but the *Gemara* treats it as a coherent legal scenario.

One might then ask: if it is a dry birth and there is no *tumah leidah* and no *niddah* blood, is this not simply an obvious application of Rabbi Yishmael's principle? What is the *chiddush*? The *Gemara* answers: *mahu d'teima ee efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* — one might have thought it is impossible for the opening of the womb to occur without blood, even if no blood was observed, and therefore we would presume blood even without evidence. The *baraita* teaches: *ka mashma lan d'efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* — it is indeed possible, and we do not presume blood that was not seen. Since the fetus had not yet reached the forty-one or eighty-one day threshold, it is not a viable birth, and absent visible blood, she is not *temeiah* and may eat the *korban Pesach* that night.

The *Gemara* takes note of the phrase *efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* — whether it is possible for the opening of the womb to occur without blood. The *Gemara* here uses the word *kever*, grave, to describe the womb — a usage that appears only in the context of miscarriage, where the womb has held a child that did not survive. Elsewhere in the *Gemara*, the womb is referred to as *makor* or *rechem*. The *poskim* who apply this principle to other scenarios, such as gynecological examinations, have been questioned by other authorities precisely because the *Gemara*'s language may have been calibrated specifically for the case of miscarriage — and some hold that the *halacha* of *ee efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* should not be extended beyond that context.

## Scriptural Proof

### Sixty Days Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמַעְיָה, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״שִׁשִּׁים״, יָכוֹל בֵּין רְצוּפִין בֵּין מְפוּזָּרִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יוֹם״, מָה יוֹם רָצוּף – אַף שִׁשִּׁים כּוּלָּן רְצוּפִין. מַנִּי? אִלֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, מִי אִית לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן מְפוּזָּרִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shemaya said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to a woman who gave birth to a female: "And she shall continue in the blood of purification **sixty** days and six days" (Leviticus 12:5). One **might** have thought that she counts the days **whether they are continuous or dispersed.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "Sixty **days** and six days," to teach that **just as a day is a continuous** time period, **so too all sixty** days **are continuous.** And **whose** opinion is this? **If we say** it is the opinion of **the Rabbis,** that cannot be correct, as **are the Rabbis of** the opinion that it is possible for a woman who gave birth to count **dispersed** days? This is not the case, as the Rabbis count the days of purification from the second birth.

The *Gemara* returns to the fundamental question raised at the outset: does Rabbi Yehuda fully incorporate the second birth into the first for all purposes, including *tumah v'taharah*, or does the second birth retain some independence in matters of purity? Rav Shemaya offers a proof from the verse *shishim yom v'sheshet yamim teshev bidmei taharah* — for sixty-six days she shall remain in the blood of her purity (following a female birth). The *drasha* proceeds: *shishim yachol bein metzufin bein mefuzarim* — I might have thought that her sixty *taharah* days (the majority of the sixty-six) could be accumulated whether continuously or in scattered fashion. The verse therefore says *yom* — "day" — in the singular: just as a single day is an indivisible unit, so too the sixty days must be one continuous unit.

### Rabbis Excluded

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mani* — who is the author of this *drasha*? If it is the *Rabbanan*, the entire premise of the *drasha* collapses. The *Rabbanan* would never have entertained the idea of "making up" *taharah* days in the first place. Their entire approach treats multiple sequential births as a single extended process with no interruptions and no recovery of lost time. They count from the end of the process and require only one *korban*. There is no conceptual space within their framework for scattered or discontinuous *taharah* days.

### Attribution Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וּמִדְּקָא יָהֵיב לַהּ שִׁשִּׁים רְצוּפִים בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, שְׁמַע מִינָּה חוּמְרָא אִית לֵיהּ, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קוּלָּא לֵית לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? And from** the fact that **he prescribes for her sixty continuous days** to be counted **together, conclude from it** that **Rabbi Yehuda maintains** that the second offspring is considered extant only where **a stringency** results, but where **a leniency** results, in that she continues counting the days of purity of the first birth after completing the days of impurity for the second birth, **he does not maintain** that the second offspring is considered as though it exists.

The *drasha* must therefore belong to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that each birth retains some degree of separateness — specifically, he holds that the second birth is not fully merged into the first but is rather subsumed under it for *korban* purposes while potentially remaining distinct for *tumah*. Since it is Rabbi Yehuda's framework that generates the *hava amina* of making up days (the "interloper" theory), it is Rabbi Yehuda who needs to be told: *yom*, the days must be continuous. You do not get to scatter them or make them up. Once a birth interrupts the *taharah* period, those days are not recovered at the end.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם רַבָּנַן הִיא. וְהָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן – לְיוֹלֶדֶת זָכָר מִתּוֹךְ שְׁמוֹנִים שֶׁל נְקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **No, actually,** the *baraita* **is** stated in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. And what are we dealing with here?** We are dealing **with a woman who gives birth to a male within** the **eighty-**day period following the birth **of a female,** so that the forty-day period for the male is entirely subsumed within the purification period for the female. In such a case, even the Rabbis would maintain that the second birth does not interrupt the term following the first. She would observe the seven days of impurity for the male, and then complete the days of purity for the female. Consequently, the verse teaches that the sixty-six days of purity following the first birth are observed continuously, and there is no need to observe additional days due to the intervening days of impurity.

The *Gemara* pushes back: perhaps the *drasha* is still the *Rabbanan*'s, and it addresses a specific case — *yoledet zachar mi-toch shmonim shel nekeivah* — a male birth occurring during the eighty-day period of a female birth, where the entire male *tumah* and *taharah* cycle is contained within the female's eighty days. In such a case, even the *Rabbanan* might have entertained the idea of making up the interrupted *taharah* days, since the female birth dominates and the male is an internal interruption. The verse would then be telling even the *Rabbanan* that the days must remain continuous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, שָׁלְמִין יוֹמֵי דְּקַדְמָאָה, וְעַד כָּאן דְּבָתְרָאָה לָא שָׁלְמִין, דְּהָא רַבָּנַן לְוָלָד שֵׁנִי מָנוּ! אֶלָּא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ לְרַבָּנַן – בְּיוֹלֶדֶת תְּאוֹמִים, נְקֵבָה קַדְמָיְיתָא וְזָכָר בָּתְרָאָה, וּכְגוֹן דִּילֵידְתֵּיהּ לְזָכָר בָּתְרָאָה בְּעֶשְׂרִים לְיוֹמֵי טׇהֳרָה, דְּבָעֲיָא מִינְקָט לֵיהּ שִׁבְעָה יוֹמֵי טוּמְאָה דְּלֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: But **ultimately,** this scenario is impossible; the reason is that **the days of the first** birth **are completed while the days of the latter** birth **are not completed, as the Rabbis count the days following the second birth,** and therefore the term relating to the first birth is abandoned. The Gemara offers a different explanation of the *baraita*: **Rather, you find** a case of counting dispersed days **according to the Rabbis** in a case **where a woman gives birth to twins, first a female and then a male, and for example, she gives birth to the latter** offspring, the **male, on** the **twentieth** day **of** the **days** of **purity** for the female, **as she is required to observe for him seven days of impurity for birth.**

The *Gemara* dismisses this: *sof sof shalmu yomei de-kamaitha ve-adayin de-bathraiyta lo shalmu* — in any scenario of sequential births, the first birth's period will expire before the second birth's period is concluded, creating a single long chain. The *Rabbanan* always view this as one extended process; they would never frame it as an interruption requiring compensation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: יָכוֹל הֵיכָא דְּיָלְדָה תְּאוֹמִים, נְקֵבָה מֵעִיקָּרָא וְזָכָר לְבַסּוֹף, תַּפְסֵיק טוּמְאָה דְּלֵידָה בְּמִיצְעֵי, וְנִימְנֵי לַהּ שִׁשָּׁה וְשִׁשִּׁים מְפוּזָּרִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יוֹם״, מָה יוֹם כּוּלּוֹ רָצוּף – אַף שִׁשִּׁים כּוּלָּם רְצוּפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **And this** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** One **might** have thought that **where she gave birth to twins, a female initially and a male afterward,** that the **impurity of** the second **birth interrupts** the term following the first birth **in the middle, and she counts sixty-six dispersed days.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "Sixty **days** and six days," to teach that **just as a day is an entirely continuous** time period, **so too all sixty** days **are** to be **continuous.** Since the *baraita* could be in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, no proof can be inferred with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

The resolution is to invoke the case of *yoledet te'omim* — twins. If a woman first delivers a female and then a male, and the male is born during the female's *taharah* days, the seven days of *tumah* for the male birth interrupt the female's *taharah* period. In this specific case, one might argue — even according to the *Rabbanan* — that since the female birth defines and dominates the overall process, the interruption caused by the male birth entitles the woman to make up those seven *taharah* days. The verse therefore tells us: *yom* — the days must be continuous; even in this case, you do not spread them out or recover them later.

This establishes: since it is even possible to read the verse as addressing the *Rabbanan* in the twins case, the proof that Rabbi Yehuda fully incorporates the second birth without any independent *tumah* standing remains contested.

### Thirty Days Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״שְׁלֹשִׁים״ – יָכוֹל בֵּין רְצוּפִין בֵּין מְפוּזָּרִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״יוֹם״, מָה יוֹם כּוּלּוֹ רָצוּף – אַף שְׁלֹשִׁים רְצוּפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* which discusses a woman who gave birth to a male. The verse states: "And she shall continue in the blood of purification three days and **thirty** days" (Leviticus 12:4). One **might** have thought that she counts the days **whether they are continuous or dispersed.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "Three **days** and thirty days," to teach that **just as a day is an entirely continuous** time period, **so too** all **thirty** days **are** to be **continuous.**

Abaye offers a second *drasha* that sidesteps this objection entirely. The verse dealing with a male birth reads *v'shloshim yom u'shloshet yamim teshev bidmei taharah* — for thirty-three days she shall remain in the blood of her purity. The same structure applies: *shloshim yachol bein retzufin bein mefuzarim* — I might have thought the thirty days need not be continuous; *talmud lomar yom* — the verse says "day," teaching that just as a day is one continuous unit, so too the thirty must be continuous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי הָא? אִלֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, וּמִי אִית לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן מְפוּזָּרִין? הָא אָמְרִי רַבָּנַן: לְוָלָד שֵׁנִי מָנְיָא! אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חוּמְרָא אִית לֵיהּ, קוּלָּא לֵית לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** And **whose** opinion is this? **If we say** it is **the Rabbis,** that cannot be correct, as **are the Rabbis** of the opinion that it is possible for a woman who gave birth to count **dispersed** days? No, as **the Rabbis say** that **she counts** the days from the birth **of** the **second fetus. Rather, is it not** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? And** therefore, may one **conclude from it** that **Rabbi Yehuda is of** the opinion that the second offspring is considered extant only where **a stringency** results; but where **a leniency** results, **he is not of** the opinion that the second offspring is considered to have existed.

The *Gemara* applies the same analysis: *mani*? If it is the *Rabbanan*, they have no concept of scattered days. It must be Rabbi Yehuda. *V'shma mina chumra it lei l'Rabbi Yehuda kula leit lei* — from this we derive that Rabbi Yehuda's incorporation principle is driven by stringency, and he would not extend it to yield a leniency. And crucially, the case of a male birth forecloses the possibility of a twins scenario creating the *hava amina*, because after a male birth, a second birth could not arrive quickly enough to be fully incorporated within the first. The scenario generating the question is necessarily one that belongs to Rabbi Yehuda's framework alone, not to the *Rabbanan*. This *drasha* therefore proves the point cleanly.

### Final Six Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי, תָּא שְׁמַע: ״וְשֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים״ – יָכוֹל בֵּין רְצוּפִין וּבֵין מְפוּזָּרִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שִׁשִּׁים״, מָה שִׁשִּׁים כּוּלָּן רְצוּפִין – אַף שִׁשָּׁה כּוּלָּן רְצוּפִין. מַנִּי? אִלֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, מִי אִית לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן מְפוּזָּרִין? הָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן: לְוָלָד שֵׁנִי מָנְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And further, Rav Ashi said: Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*, which discusses the verse: "Sixty days **and six days"** (Leviticus 12:5). One **might** have thought that she counts these six days **whether they are continuous or dispersed,** so that if, for example, she miscarried on the sixty-third day, she would complete the remaining three days of purity for the first fetus after completing the period of purification for the second. Therefore, **the verse states: "Sixty"** to teach that **just as the sixty days are all continuous, so too, the six days are all continuous. Whose** opinion is this? **If we say** it is the opinion of **the Rabbis,** that cannot be correct, as **are the Rabbis** of the opinion that it is possible for a woman who gave birth to count **dispersed** days? **Didn't the Rabbis say** that **she counts for** the **second offspring?**

Rav Ashi adds a third proof from the continuation of the verse: *v'shishim yom v'sheshet yamim* — sixty and six days. The same *drasha* is applied to the final six days: *yachol bein retzufin bein mefuzarin* — might I have thought these six days could be accumulated non-continuously? *Talmud lomar shishim* — the verse ties the six to the sixty, and just as the sixty must be continuous, so too the six must be continuous. If *tumah* interrupts during those final six days, the woman does not recover those days afterward.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חוּמְרָא אִית לֵיהּ, קוּלָּא לֵית לֵיהּ? שְׁמַע מִינָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, is it not** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda? And** therefore, may one **conclude from it** that **Rabbi Yehuda is of** the opinion that the second offspring is considered extant only where **a stringency** results; but where **a leniency** results, **he is not of** the opinion that the second offspring is considered to have existed? The Gemara rules: Indeed, **conclude from it** that this is the case.

Critically, a *tumah* that occurs specifically in the last six days of the sixty-six — as opposed to earlier in the process — could only be classified under Rabbi Yehuda's framework. It is far too late for a second birth to arrive that would be "fully incorporated" within the first. The *Rabbanan* would not have this conceptual problem, and no twins scenario applies at this late stage. The *drasha* must therefore be Rabbi Yehuda's, and it tells us unambiguously: *chumra it lei kula leit lei* — even at this final stage, Rabbi Yehuda does not allow the woman to recover her *taharah* days. The second birth is fully incorporated into the first in every dimension — for *korban*, for *tumah*, and for *taharah* — and the days must run continuously from start to finish.

## Oleh VeYored Offerings

### Overview Variable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ מְבִיאִין קׇרְבָּן עוֹלֶה וְיוֹרֵד: עַל שְׁמִיעַת קוֹל, וְעַל בִּטּוּי שְׂפָתָיִם, וְעַל טוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו, וְהַיּוֹלֶדֶת, וְהַמְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **These** are the five situations mentioned in the mishna (9a) in which one **brings a sliding-scale offering: For hearing the voice** of an oath, i.e., where one took a false oath that he does not have any testimony to provide on a given issue; **and for** the **utterance of the lips,** which is a case where one took a false oath about a different matter; **and for** the **defiling of** the **Temple,** by entering it while ritually impure, **or** defiling **its sacrificial** foods, by partaking of them while ritually impure; **and a woman after childbirth; and a leper** at the end of his purification process.

The Mishnah introduces the broader category of *korban oleh v'yored* — the offering of changing value, whose form depends on the financial means of the one obligated to bring it. Five cases are enumerated. Three appear together at the end of *Parshat Vayikra* and constitute the classical category: *shmiat kol*, *bitui sfatayim*, and *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*. The other two — *yoledet* and *metzora* — are treated independently in the Torah but share the same sliding-scale structure.

### Five Cases Listed

*Al shmiat kol* — one who was subpoenaed to testify, swore falsely that he had no knowledge of the matter, and thereby failed to testify. *V'al bitui sfatayim* — one who swore to perform or refrain from an action and violated that oath. *V'al tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — one who entered the *Beit HaMikdash* or ate *korbanot* while *tamei*. These three appear adjacently in the Torah. Then *yoledet* — a woman after childbirth — and *metzora* — one afflicted with *tzara'at*.

### Substitution Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יֵשׁ מֵבִיא בְּדַלּוּת וַעֲשִׁירוּת, וְיֵשׁ מֵבִיא בְּדַלּוּת וְיֵשׁ מֵבִיא בְּדַלֵּי דַּלּוּת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** a *baraita* with regard to the nature of a sliding-scale offering, which varies depending on the owner's financial status: **There is** a case in which **one brings** the offering in one of two possible states, either a bird offering if the individual is in a state **of poverty or** an animal offering if he is in a state of **wealth; and there is** a case in which **one brings** the offering in one of three possible states, of wealth, **of poverty,** or **of extreme poverty.**

A *baraita* categorizes the forms of substitution. Some bring one for one: a single bird in place of a single lamb. Some bring two for two: two birds in place of two lambs. Some bring two for one: two birds in place of a single lamb. And some bring one for two: a single *mincha* in place of two birds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹלֶדֶת – מְבִיאָה בְּדַלּוּת וַעֲשִׁירוּת. מְצוֹרָע – מֵבִיא בְּדַלּוּת. שְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל וּבִטּוּי שְׂפָתָיִם וְטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו – מְבִיאִין בְּדַלּוּת וְדַלֵּי דַּלּוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **A woman after childbirth brings** a sliding-scale offering either **in poverty,** i.e., two doves or two pigeons, one as a burnt offering and one as a sin offering, **or** in **wealth,** i.e., a lamb as a burnt offering and a dove or a pigeon as a sin offering (see Leviticus 12:1–8). **A leper brings** the offering either **in poverty** in the form of a lamb for a guilt offering, and two doves or two pigeons, one as a sin offering and one as a burnt offering, or in wealth in the form of a lamb for a guilt offering, a lamb as a burnt offering, and a female lamb as a sin offering (see Leviticus 14:10–22). For **the hearing of the voice,** i.e., taking a false oath that one cannot provide testimony, **or the utterance of the lips,** or the **defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, one **brings** an offering either in wealth, i.e., a female lamb or goat as a sin offering, **or in poverty,** i.e., two doves or two pigeons, one as a sin offering and one as a burnt offering, **or in extreme poverty,** i.e., a meal offering.

The *yoledet*, who normally brings one lamb and one bird (the bird being the substitution for the second lamb), substitutes one for one. The *metzora*, who normally brings two lambs for his *chatat* and *olah* and substitutes with two birds if poor, substitutes two for two. The classical *oleh v'yored* cases — *shmiat kol*, *bitui sfatayim*, *tumat mikdash* — require a single lamb as the *chatat* in the wealthy case; the poor substitute brings two birds (two for one); and the destitute substitute brings a single *mincha* of a tenth of an *eifah* (one for two).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: יֵשׁ מֵבִיא אֶחָד תַּחַת אֶחָד, יֵשׁ מֵבִיא שְׁנַיִם תַּחַת שְׁנַיִם, יֵשׁ מֵבִיא שְׁנַיִם תַּחַת אֶחָד, יֵשׁ מֵבִיא אֶחָד תַּחַת שְׁנַיִם, מִכָּאן אַתָּה לָמֵד לַעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה בִּפְרוּטָה.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: When offerings are brought in poverty rather than wealth, **there is** a case in which one **brings one** offering **instead of** a different **one; there is** a case in which one **brings two** offerings **instead of two** other offerings; **there is** a case in which one **brings two** offerings **instead of one** offering; and **there is** a case in which one **brings one** offering **instead of two** offerings. **From here you may derive** that the offering **of one-tenth of an ephah** may be worth as little **as one *peruta*.**

The Mishnah appends the observation: *mikan ata lamed she'asiris ha'eifah b'prutah* — from all of this we learn that a tenth of an *eifah* of flour is worth a single *prutah*. This appears to be a non-sequitur, and the *Gemara* undertakes to show that it is not.

## Oleh VeYored Offerings…

### Pricing Scripture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹלֶדֶת מְבִיאָה אַחַת תַּחַת אַחַת – פְּרִידָה אַחַת וָעוֹף תַּחַת כֶּבֶשׂ, מְצוֹרָע – שְׁתֵּי פְרִידִין תַּחַת שְׁנֵי כְבָשִׂים, שְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל וּבִטּוּי שְׂפָתָיִם וְטוּמְאַת מִקְדָּשׁ וְקָדָשָׁיו – מְבִיאִין שְׁתֵּי פְרִידִין תַּחַת כִּבְשָׂה אַחַת, וּמְבִיאִין בְּדַלֵּי דַּלּוּת עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה תַּחַת שְׁתֵּי פְרִידִין.
>
> **Translation:** **A woman after childbirth brings one** offering **instead of one** offering: **One young bird,** as a sin offering that she would bring even in wealth, **and** another **bird** as a burnt offering **instead of** the **lamb. A leper** brings **two young birds instead of two lambs.** For **the hearing of the voice and the utterance of the lips and the defiling of** the **Temple or its sacrificial** foods, one **brings two young birds instead of one female lamb, and in** a case **of extreme poverty** one **brings one** meal offering of **one-tenth of an ephah instead of two young birds.**

Before addressing the apparent non-sequitur, the *Gemara* establishes the pricing baseline. The *baraita* states: *ha'omer harei alai besela mizbeach, mevi keves, she'ein davar karev besela ela keves* — one who declares he will bring an offering worth a *sela* must bring a lamb, since the standard animal offered at the value of a *sela* is a lamb. The source: *mid'amar rachmana ayil asham bechesef shekalim* — the Torah requires a ram for the *asham* offering at a cost of silver *shekalim*. The standard silver coin unit — a *sela* — is the price of an *asham* ram, a two-year-old sheep. A one-year-old lamb, it is inferred, is worth half that: one *sela*. Thus the baseline is established: a lamb costs one *sela*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי: מִכָּאן אַתָּה לָמֵד לַעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה שֶׁהִיא בִּפְרוּטָה. מְנָלַן? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי בְּסֶלַע לַמִּזְבֵּחַ״ – מֵבִיא כֶּבֶשׂ, שֶׁאֵין דָּבָר קָרֵב בְּסֶלַע אֶלָּא כֶּבֶשׂ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* **teaches: From here you may derive** that the offering **of one-tenth of an ephah** may be worth as little **as one *peruta*.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** learn this *halakha*? The Gemara answers that it is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me to** bring an offering worth **one *sela* to the altar,** he **brings a lamb, as there is nothing that is sacrificed upon the altar** that is worth **one *sela* other than a lamb.**

## Ephah Value Calculation

### Non-Sequitur Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מִמַּאי? מִדְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא ״אֵיל אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים״, מִכְּלָל דְּכֶבֶשׂ בֶּן שָׁנָה בְּסֶלַע, מִדִּכְתִיב ״כֶּבֶשׂ בֶּן שְׁנָתוֹ״. וּתְנַן: עָמְדוּ קִינִּין בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם בְּרִיבְעָתַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is this *halakha* derived? It is derived **from** the fact **that the Merciful One states** in the Torah that the **ram** brought in its second year as **a guilt offering** must be "according to your valuation in **silver** by **shekels"** (Leviticus 5:15), i.e., it must be worth two *sela*. It may therefore be understood **by inference that a lamb,** i.e., a young ram, **in its** first **year is** worth **one *sela*.** And it is known that a lamb is brought when it is within its first year, **as it is written: "A lamb of the first year"** (Leviticus 12:6). **And we learned** in a mishna (8a): The price of **nests,** i.e., pairs of birds brought by an impoverished person instead of a lamb, **stood that day at one-quarter** of a silver dinar. A quarter-dinar is one-sixteenth of a *sela*, the price of a lamb.

The question is how the Mishnah's concluding statement — that a tenth of an *eifah* costs a *prutah* — follows from the discussion of substitution categories. The *Gemara* works through the mathematics by a chain of ratios derived from the Torah's pricing of offerings.

### Sela Baseline

As established, a lamb costs one *sela*. The *sela* equals four *dinarim* of silver, and each *dinar* subdivides further into smaller denominations. The *prutah* is the smallest monetary unit of halakhic significance.

### 1-to-16 Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִדְּחָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּדַלּוּת לְמִיהְוֵי חַד מִשֵּׁית עֲשַׂר בַּעֲשִׁירוּת, חָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּדַלֵּי דַּלּוּת לְמִיהְוֵי חַד מִשֵּׁית עֲשַׂר בְּדַלּוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And from** the fact **that the Merciful One has mercy upon** one who brings an offering **in poverty,** in that He permits him to bring an offering **that is** worth **one-sixteenth** of the value of the lamb that is brought **in wealth,** it may be inferred that **the Merciful One takes pity upon** one who brings an offering **in extreme poverty,** and consequently He permits him to bring an offering **which is** worth **one-sixteenth** of the value of the offering brought **in** moderate **poverty.** Accordingly, the offering brought in extreme poverty may be equal to a two hundred fifty-sixth part of the value of a *sela*.

The *Gemara* notes: *amdu kinim bo bayom beriv'asayim* — the standard price of a *kan* (a pair of birds) is a quarter of a *dinar*, which is a quarter of a quarter of a *sela*. Since a lamb is one *sela*, which is four *dinarim*, and a pair of birds is a quarter of a *dinar*, the ratio of two birds to one lamb is one to sixteen. This ratio is the Torah's permitted reduction for those who are *dal* — poor — in the context of *oleh v'yored*: instead of one lamb, they bring two birds at one-sixteenth the cost.

### Ratio Downward

Applying the same ratio downward from the birds to the *mincha*: *u'mid'chas rachmana lei dali dalut v'havei chad mishisar be'ashirus* — since the Torah permits the reduction of one-sixteenth from lamb to birds, the same ratio should apply from birds to *mincha*. One-sixteenth of a quarter-*dinar* (48 *prutos*) would be three *prutos*. This is more than a *prutah*, so the calculation does not yet arrive at the Mishnah's conclusion.

### Applying the Ratio Downward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, דַּלּוּת כְּמָה הָוְיָא – רִיבְעָא, וּכְמָה פְּרִיטֵי בְּרִיבְעָא – אַרְבָּעִים וּתְמָנְיָא פְּרִיטֵי, חַד מִשֵּׁית עֲשַׂר כְּמָה הָוֵי דְּדַלּוּת – תְּלָתָא פְּרִיטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara examines the mathematics necessary to determine that the value of the offering brought in extreme poverty is equal to one *peruta*: **If so, how much is** the offering brought in **poverty** worth? It is worth **one-quarter** of a dinar. **And how many *perutot*** are **in one-quarter** of a dinar? **Forty-eight *perutot*.** Accordingly, **how many** *perutot* **are** in the offering brought in extreme poverty, which is worth **one-sixteenth of** the offering brought in **poverty? Three *perutot*.**

The *Gemara* continues to apply the ratio and arrives at the problem: if the birds cost 48 *prutos* and the *mincha* is one-sixteenth of that, the result is three *prutos* — not one *prutah*. The Mishnah's claim that the *asirit ha'eifah* costs one *prutah* is not derivable from this ratio alone.

### 1-to-32 Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּקָתָנֵי: מִכָּאן אַתָּה לָמֵד לַעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה בִּפְרוּטָה, אַמַּאי פְּרוּטָה? הָאָמְרַתְּ: עֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה דַּלֵּי דַּלּוּת הוּא, וְאָמְרַתְּ חַד מִשֵּׁית סְרֵי דְּדַלּוּת הוּא, דְּחָשְׁבִינַן דִּתְלָתָא פְּרִיטֵי הָוַיִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** with regard to **that** which the *baraita* **teaches: From here you may derive** that the offering **of one-tenth of an ephah** may be worth as little **as one *peruta*, why** did the *baraita* teach that it may be worth **one *peruta*,** rather than three *perutot*? **Didn't you say** that **one-tenth of an ephah is** brought in **extreme poverty, and you** also **said** that this offering should be worth **one-sixteenth of** the offering brought in **poverty, which we calculated to be three *perutot*?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּא מִיּוֹלֶדֶת יָלֵיף, דְּמַתְיָא פְּרִידָה אַחַת תַּחַת כֶּבֶשׂ, דְּהָוֵי חַד מִתְּלָתִין וּתְרֵים דְּכֶבֶשׂ. וְעַד כָּאן, דַּלֵּי דַּלּוּת כְּמָה הָוֵי – חַד מִשֵּׁית עֲשַׂר בְּדַלּוּת, דְּמֵהֵיכָא יָלֵיף – מִכֶּבֶשׂ וְאַיִל. עַד כָּאן, פְּרוּטָה וּפַלְגָא הָוֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The *tanna* derives** the cost of an offering brought in poverty **from** the case of **a woman after childbirth, who brings one bird instead of a lamb, as** a bird **is** worth **one thirty-second** of the value **of a lamb.** The Gemara asks: **But until now, how much** was the offering brought in **extreme poverty** calculated to be worth? **One-sixteenth** of the value of the offering brought **in poverty.** And **from where** is this **derived?** It is derived **from** the value of **the lamb and** the **ram,** as explained earlier. If so, **until now,** the calculations lead to the conclusion that the offering brought in extreme poverty **is** worth **one and one-half *perutot*,** as one thirty-second of the value of a lamb is equal to one-eighth of a silver dinar rather than one-quarter, which means that the offering brought in poverty is worth twenty-four *perutot*, one-sixteenth of which is equal to one and one-half *perutot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כּוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא מִיּוֹלֶדֶת יָלֵיף, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: מִדְּחָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּדַלּוּת לְמִיהְוֵי חַד מִתְּלָתִין וּתְרֵים בַּעֲשִׁירוּת, וּמַאי הִיא – יוֹלֶדֶת, הָכִי חָס רַחֲמָנָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּדַלֵּי דַּלּוּת לְמִיהְוֵי חַד מִן תְּלָתִין וּתְרֵים בְּדַלּוּת.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **Rava said** a different answer: **The entire matter is derived from** the offering brought by **a woman after childbirth, and this** is what the *tanna* **is saying:** It is derived **from** the fact that **the Merciful One has mercy upon** one who brings an offering **in poverty, in** that he may bring an offering **which is** worth **one thirty-second** of the value of the offering brought **in wealth.** Rava adds parenthetically: **And** from **what** case **is this** principle derived? From the case of **a woman after childbirth. So too, the Merciful One has mercy upon** one who brings an offering **in extreme poverty, in** that he may bring an offering **that is** worth **one thirty-second** of the value of the offering brought in moderate **poverty.**

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the *yoledet*: *tana miyoledet yalif* — the ratio is learned from the case of *yoledet*. There, the poor substitute brings one bird in place of one lamb — a ratio of one to thirty-two, since a single bird (half a *kan*) is half of a quarter-*dinar* against one full *sela*. Rava states explicitly: *kula milta miyoledet yalif* — the entire framework of proportionality is imported from the *yoledet*. The Torah's mercy to the destitute (*dali dalim*) in the *oleh v'yored* context follows the same ratio as the Torah's mercy to the poor *yoledet*: one to thirty-two.

### Pruta Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, נְכֵי רִיבְעָא דִּפְרוּטָה הָוֵי! הָכִי נָמֵי, אֶלָּא דְּלָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא לְאֵיתוֹיֵי פָּחוֹת מִפְּרוּטָה לַמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** the value should **be one *peruta* minus one-quarter,** i.e., three-quarters of a *peruta*. The Gemara explains: **Indeed** that is so, **but** it is **not proper conduct to bring** an offering worth **less than one *peruta* before God.**

Applying a one-to-thirty-two ratio to a single bird (24 *prutos* — half of the pair's 48) yields twenty-four divided by thirty-two, which is three-quarters of a *prutah*. The *Gemara* acknowledges the math: *nechi riv'a depruta havei* — it comes out to a *prutah* minus a quarter. The *Gemara* accepts the calculation and rules: *ela delav orech ar'a le'atuyei pachot mipruta lemikdash* — it is simply not the practice to bring anything worth less than a *prutah* to the Temple. The minimum denomination for a Temple offering is a *prutah*; anything less is rounded up. Therefore, even though the arithmetic yields slightly under a *prutah*, in practice the *asirit ha'eifah* is valued at one *prutah* — and that is the source for the Mishnah's otherwise puzzling concluding statement. It is not a non-sequitur; it is the endpoint of a precise chain of ratios drawn from the Torah's graduated pricing of the *oleh v'yored* offerings.
