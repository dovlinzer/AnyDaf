# Keritot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sequential Births

### Intro: Multiple Events

The Mishnah at the bottom of 9b addresses the final case among a series dealing with bringing a single *korban* for multiple events — not for sins, but for *korbanot* related to *taharah*. Having already treated sequential *tzara'at* and sequential *nezirut*, the Mishnah now turns to sequential childbirths. The question is whether, when a second birth or miscarriage occurs within the eighty-day period of a female birth, a single *korban* suffices for all events at the end of the process.

According to the *Chachamim*, since there was never a moment at which the obligation to bring a *korban* for the first birth had matured, the second birth simply extends the process, and only one *korban* is required at the conclusion. Rabbi Yehuda, however, holds that while the second birth is incorporated into the first, this does not mean they merge into one seamless process. Rather, the second is absorbed into the first for *korban* purposes — with the result that even-numbered births are subsumed under the odd-numbered births that precede them (two is incorporated into one, four into three, and so on). The odd-numbered births remain independently obligating: one, three, five, and so forth each require their own *korban*.

### Incorporation Doctrine

The *Gemara* opens 10a with a *ba'aya*: *betuma mai amar Rabbi Yehuda* — what would Rabbi Yehuda say when the question is not one of *korban* obligation but of *tumah* and *taharah*? To appreciate the force of this question, consider the basic framework. After giving birth to a male, a woman is *tamei* for seven days and then *tehorah* for the subsequent thirty-three days, meaning that any bleeding during those thirty-three days does not render her a *niddah*. For a female birth, these periods are doubled: fourteen days of *tumah* and sixty-six days of *taharah*. Now suppose a second birth occurs during the *taharah* period of the first. The second birth creates a fresh period of *tumah*, interrupting the *taharah* days that had been accumulating from the first birth. The question is whether, according to Rabbi Yehuda, that interrupted *taharah* must then be "made up" before the *taharah* of the second birth commences.

### Interloper Framework

The *Gemara* formulates the question precisely. One might argue: *hatam hu dekamar lei Rabbi Yehuda vlad sheni kemahn deleit damei inyan korban* — Rabbi Yehuda only says the second birth is "as if it were not present" for purposes of *korban* obligation, because there had never yet been a moment at which a *korban* could have been brought for the first birth. But regarding *tumah v'taharah*, perhaps he holds *vlad sheni kemahn de'it damei* — the second birth retains its independent presence, *umifsaka tuma desheni* — the *tumah* of the second birth interrupts the first, and *u'mimallei yomei tahara d'rishon* — the remaining *taharah* days of the first birth must be completed before the *taharah* period of the second birth begins.

This is the "interloper" framing. If the second birth is genuinely independent within the first birth's period, it interrupts that period and entitles the woman to recover the days that were taken from her — first completing the *taharah* of the first birth, and only then proceeding to the *taharah* of the second.

### Yehuda's Stringency

On the other hand, the *Gemara* presents a counter-framing: *o dilma l'Rabbi Yehuda chumra hu d'it lei* — perhaps Rabbi Yehuda's incorporation doctrine is driven entirely by stringency. He incorporates the second birth into the first in order to require a *korban* for every other birth in the sequence (one, three, five...), which is the more stringent outcome compared to the *Rabbanan*, who require only a single *korban* at the very end. But *aval hacha kula hu v'kula leit lei* — if incorporation would lead to a leniency, as it would here by giving the woman additional *taharah* days after the fact, Rabbi Yehuda would not apply it.

The *Gemara* thus frames the question not merely as a conceptual inquiry about whether two births form one process, but as a question about what principle drives Rabbi Yehuda's doctrine of incorporation: is it a coherent view that these births form a single unified event, or is it a *chumra*-driven rule that only applies when it produces greater stringency?

## Pesach Offering Case

### Problem Posed

The *Gemara* brings a proof from a *baraita* cited by Rav Huna of Sura. The *baraita* states: *yoledes shochtin v'zorkin aleha b'yom arba'im l'zachar u'v'yom shemonim l'nekeiva* — one may slaughter and sprinkle the blood of a *korban Pesach* on behalf of a woman who gave birth, on day forty for a male birth or day eighty for a female birth. This is deeply relevant to the season, as the discussion concerns a woman in the final stages of her post-birth process on *erev Pesach*.

The difficulty is immediate. A *korban Pesach* is brought only for someone who will be able to eat it that night. Now consider this woman's status. After her seven or fourteen days of *tumah*, she immerses in the *mikvah* and enters a lengthy period that functions as a *tvul yom aroch* — a prolonged residual *tumah* state. Like an ordinary *tvul yom* who has immersed but must wait until nightfall to eat *terumah* and *korbanot*, this woman's thirty-three or sixty-six days of *taharah* represent a waiting period before she may eat *kodashim*. She is substantially *tahor* and may not be rendered a *niddah* by ordinary bleeding, but she remains restricted from *korbanot* until the completion of the forty or eighty days.

Furthermore, even at the close of day forty or eighty, her purification is not fully complete. She is now *mechussar kipurim* — lacking the final purification offering she must bring the following morning to be permitted to eat *korbanot*. So if day forty or eighty is *erev Pesach*, and she brings her *korban Pesach* that afternoon, how can she eat it that night, when she is still *mechussar kipurim*?

### Twin Births Answer

The *Gemara* initially answers with a citation from Rav Chisda: *ha mani Rabbi Yehuda, de'amar vlad sheni keman d'leisa dami* — this *baraita* follows Rabbi Yehuda, who holds the second birth is "as if not present." The scenario is one of twins, where a female was born one day and a male the next. When the *baraita* says "day forty," it does not mean day forty from the first birth (the female); it means day forty from the second birth (the male) — which is day forty-one from the first. Since forty-one days have elapsed from the female birth, her *taharah* process from that birth is complete and she may eat that night.

As for the male birth — day forty from him has arrived, but his period is not yet fully concluded. How can she be permitted? The *Gemara*'s answer implies that according to Rabbi Yehuda, the second birth is so thoroughly incorporated into the first that its completion is defined by the completion of the first birth. Once the first birth's period is over, the combined process is over, even if forty days have not elapsed from the second birth individually.

### Rejection: Twin Answer

The *Gemara* rejects this answer sharply. *Ve'eima bituma sevira lei leRabbi Yehuda vlad sheni keman d'isa dami, heichi shachtinan aleih b'yom mem le'osa nami lo matzi achla* — if Rabbi Yehuda holds that for *tumah v'taharah* the second birth is independent, then "day forty" in the *baraita* cannot mean day forty from the second birth (which is day forty-one from the first), because on that day the second birth's own process has not concluded, and she cannot eat that night. The *Gemara* presses the point: *ve'i amarta betumah v'taharah l'Rabbi Yehuda vlad sheni keman d'iteih dami, heichi shachtinan aleih b'yoma?*

If the second birth is independent for *tumah* purposes — and if that independence means she may even be entitled to "make up" *taharah* days — then she certainly cannot bring her *korban Pesach* before that second process is complete. On the other hand, if the *baraita* is saying she *can* bring it on day forty from the second (day forty-one from the first), this would only make sense if the second birth is fully subsumed within the first, with no independent standing whatsoever. The *Gemara* does not want to commit to that conclusion prematurely, so it abandons the twin-births answer entirely. *Ela shema mina* could have resolved matters, but the *Gemara* instead offers a fresh approach: *le-olam eima lach... ve-chi tanya hi be-Pesach ha-ba b'tumah* — the case is one where the entire congregation is *tamei*, and the *korban Pesach* is therefore brought in a state of communal *tumah*.

### Pesach BeAla'im Answer

When the majority of the congregation is *tamei* — typically *tamei meit* — the *korban Pesach* is offered and eaten *b'tumah*, with the entire community participating despite their *tumah*. In such circumstances, this woman, though not yet fully *tehorah*, could potentially eat as well. This resolves the eating question — but immediately raises another: the *baraita* specifies day forty or eighty; if the solution lies in communal *tumah*, why does the day matter at all? Let her participate from day eight or day fifteen, once she has immersed in the *mikvah*!

The *Gemara* raises a prior objection first. The *baraita* teaches: *Pesach haba b'tumah lo yochlu mimenu zavim v'zavos nidos v'yoldos* — even when the *Pesach* is brought in a state of communal *tumah*, those whose *tumah* originates from within their own bodies — *zavim*, *zavot*, *niddot*, and *yoldot* — may not eat. A woman who has just given birth falls squarely within this exclusion. How, then, can she eat at all?

The *Gemara* answers: *ki lo tavlin* — the exclusion applies only when she has not yet immersed. *D'tavla* — in our case, she has already gone to the *mikvah*, and her *tumah* is therefore residual, not active. But this returns us to the earlier question: once she has immersed, why does it matter whether it is day eight or day forty?

The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar tvul yom d'zav k'zav dami* — even after immersion, during the extended *tvul yom* period (her thirty-three or sixty-six days), her residual *tumah* retains the character of the original *tumah*. She is not merely a generic *tvul yom*; she is still considered a *yoledet* in a residual state, and that status excludes her even from *Pesach haba b'tumah*. This is why day eight or day fifteen would not suffice.

The *Gemara* then presses further: *ei hachi, b'yomam nami lo chazya* — if the residual *tumah* retains the character of the original, then even on day forty she cannot eat, because she still has not brought her *korban* and remains *mechussar kipurim* with the status of a *yoledet*! The *Gemara* answers: *kasavar mechusar kipurim d'zav lav k'zav dami* — at the final stage of the purification process, when one is merely *mechussar kipurim*, the residual *tumah* is no longer characterized by the original status. A *mechusar kipurim* is simply a person who needs to bring a *korban* — a generic, label-free deficiency. Since on the night after day forty she carries only this generic designation and is no longer classified as a *yoledet* in any meaningful sense, she may eat a *Pesach* that is brought *b'tumah*.

This produces a precise and carefully delimited ruling: not day eight, not day fifteen, but specifically day forty or eighty — because only at that stage does the original *tumah* character dissolve into the generic status of *mechusar kipurim*, permitting her to eat the communal *Pesach*.

The *Gemara* then raises the view of Rava, who holds *mechusar kipurim d'zav k'zav dami* — that even at the final *mechusar kipurim* stage, the original *tumah* character persists. According to Rava, she would still be classified as a *yoledet* and excluded even from *Pesach haba b'tumah*. How, then, does Rava explain the *baraita* about day forty and eighty?

### Yishmael Principle

Rav Ashi answers on Rava's behalf: *Rava metaretz lah b'yom mem l'yetzirat zachar u'v'yom shemonim l'yetzirat nekeivah, v'Rabbi Yishmael hi* — Rava resolves the *baraita* by invoking Rabbi Yishmael's principle that a fetus is only considered fully formed and viable from day forty-one for a male and day eighty-one for a female. When the *baraita* speaks of "day forty" or "day eighty," it refers not to day forty from birth but to day forty from conception — i.e., the day on which a potential pregnancy would first attain the status of a viable fetus.

If a woman miscarries on day forty of gestation (or day eighty for a female fetus), the fetus has not yet reached the threshold of viability under Rabbi Yishmael's principle. She is therefore not *temeiah leidah* — the miscarriage does not trigger the *tumah* of childbirth — and she may eat the *korban Pesach* that night.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges: *sof sof tipta d'temeiah hi mishum niddah* — even if there is no *tumah* of childbirth, there is certainly blood, and any uterine blood renders her a *niddah*. The *Gemara* answers: *b'vladah yaveshta* — the case involves a dry birth, a miscarriage without accompanying blood. This is admittedly difficult to imagine, but the *Gemara* treats it as a coherent legal scenario.

One might then ask: if it is a dry birth and there is no *tumah leidah* and no *niddah* blood, is this not simply an obvious application of Rabbi Yishmael's principle? What is the *chiddush*? The *Gemara* answers: *mahu d'teima ee efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* — one might have thought it is impossible for the opening of the womb to occur without blood, even if no blood was observed, and therefore we would presume blood even without evidence. The *baraita* teaches: *ka mashma lan d'efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* — it is indeed possible, and we do not presume blood that was not seen. Since the fetus had not yet reached the forty-one or eighty-one day threshold, it is not a viable birth, and absent visible blood, she is not *temeiah* and may eat the *korban Pesach* that night.

The *Gemara* takes note of the phrase *efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* — whether it is possible for the opening of the womb to occur without blood. The *Gemara* here uses the word *kever*, grave, to describe the womb — a usage that appears only in the context of miscarriage, where the womb has held a child that did not survive. Elsewhere in the *Gemara*, the womb is referred to as *makor* or *rechem*. The *poskim* who apply this principle to other scenarios, such as gynecological examinations, have been questioned by other authorities precisely because the *Gemara*'s language may have been calibrated specifically for the case of miscarriage — and some hold that the *halacha* of *ee efshar l'ptichat hakever belo dam* should not be extended beyond that context.

## Scriptural Proof

### Sixty Days Derasha

The *Gemara* returns to the fundamental question raised at the outset: does Rabbi Yehuda fully incorporate the second birth into the first for all purposes, including *tumah v'taharah*, or does the second birth retain some independence in matters of purity? Rav Shemaya offers a proof from the verse *shishim yom v'sheshet yamim teshev bidmei taharah* — for sixty-six days she shall remain in the blood of her purity (following a female birth). The *drasha* proceeds: *shishim yachol bein metzufin bein mefuzarim* — I might have thought that her sixty *taharah* days (the majority of the sixty-six) could be accumulated whether continuously or in scattered fashion. The verse therefore says *yom* — "day" — in the singular: just as a single day is an indivisible unit, so too the sixty days must be one continuous unit.

### Rabbis Excluded

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *mani* — who is the author of this *drasha*? If it is the *Rabbanan*, the entire premise of the *drasha* collapses. The *Rabbanan* would never have entertained the idea of "making up" *taharah* days in the first place. Their entire approach treats multiple sequential births as a single extended process with no interruptions and no recovery of lost time. They count from the end of the process and require only one *korban*. There is no conceptual space within their framework for scattered or discontinuous *taharah* days.

### Attribution Yehuda

The *drasha* must therefore belong to Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that each birth retains some degree of separateness — specifically, he holds that the second birth is not fully merged into the first but is rather subsumed under it for *korban* purposes while potentially remaining distinct for *tumah*. Since it is Rabbi Yehuda's framework that generates the *hava amina* of making up days (the "interloper" theory), it is Rabbi Yehuda who needs to be told: *yom*, the days must be continuous. You do not get to scatter them or make them up. Once a birth interrupts the *taharah* period, those days are not recovered at the end.

The *Gemara* pushes back: perhaps the *drasha* is still the *Rabbanan*'s, and it addresses a specific case — *yoledet zachar mi-toch shmonim shel nekeivah* — a male birth occurring during the eighty-day period of a female birth, where the entire male *tumah* and *taharah* cycle is contained within the female's eighty days. In such a case, even the *Rabbanan* might have entertained the idea of making up the interrupted *taharah* days, since the female birth dominates and the male is an internal interruption. The verse would then be telling even the *Rabbanan* that the days must remain continuous.

The *Gemara* dismisses this: *sof sof shalmu yomei de-kamaitha ve-adayin de-bathraiyta lo shalmu* — in any scenario of sequential births, the first birth's period will expire before the second birth's period is concluded, creating a single long chain. The *Rabbanan* always view this as one extended process; they would never frame it as an interruption requiring compensation.

The resolution is to invoke the case of *yoledet te'omim* — twins. If a woman first delivers a female and then a male, and the male is born during the female's *taharah* days, the seven days of *tumah* for the male birth interrupt the female's *taharah* period. In this specific case, one might argue — even according to the *Rabbanan* — that since the female birth defines and dominates the overall process, the interruption caused by the male birth entitles the woman to make up those seven *taharah* days. The verse therefore tells us: *yom* — the days must be continuous; even in this case, you do not spread them out or recover them later.

This establishes: since it is even possible to read the verse as addressing the *Rabbanan* in the twins case, the proof that Rabbi Yehuda fully incorporates the second birth without any independent *tumah* standing remains contested.

### Thirty Days Derasha

Abaye offers a second *drasha* that sidesteps this objection entirely. The verse dealing with a male birth reads *v'shloshim yom u'shloshet yamim teshev bidmei taharah* — for thirty-three days she shall remain in the blood of her purity. The same structure applies: *shloshim yachol bein retzufin bein mefuzarim* — I might have thought the thirty days need not be continuous; *talmud lomar yom* — the verse says "day," teaching that just as a day is one continuous unit, so too the thirty must be continuous.

The *Gemara* applies the same analysis: *mani*? If it is the *Rabbanan*, they have no concept of scattered days. It must be Rabbi Yehuda. *V'shma mina chumra it lei l'Rabbi Yehuda kula leit lei* — from this we derive that Rabbi Yehuda's incorporation principle is driven by stringency, and he would not extend it to yield a leniency. And crucially, the case of a male birth forecloses the possibility of a twins scenario creating the *hava amina*, because after a male birth, a second birth could not arrive quickly enough to be fully incorporated within the first. The scenario generating the question is necessarily one that belongs to Rabbi Yehuda's framework alone, not to the *Rabbanan*. This *drasha* therefore proves the point cleanly.

### Final Six Days

Rav Ashi adds a third proof from the continuation of the verse: *v'shishim yom v'sheshet yamim* — sixty and six days. The same *drasha* is applied to the final six days: *yachol bein retzufin bein mefuzarin* — might I have thought these six days could be accumulated non-continuously? *Talmud lomar shishim* — the verse ties the six to the sixty, and just as the sixty must be continuous, so too the six must be continuous. If *tumah* interrupts during those final six days, the woman does not recover those days afterward.

Critically, a *tumah* that occurs specifically in the last six days of the sixty-six — as opposed to earlier in the process — could only be classified under Rabbi Yehuda's framework. It is far too late for a second birth to arrive that would be "fully incorporated" within the first. The *Rabbanan* would not have this conceptual problem, and no twins scenario applies at this late stage. The *drasha* must therefore be Rabbi Yehuda's, and it tells us unambiguously: *chumra it lei kula leit lei* — even at this final stage, Rabbi Yehuda does not allow the woman to recover her *taharah* days. The second birth is fully incorporated into the first in every dimension — for *korban*, for *tumah*, and for *taharah* — and the days must run continuously from start to finish.

## Oleh VeYored Offerin (II)

### Overview Variable

The Mishnah introduces the broader category of *korban oleh v'yored* — the offering of changing value, whose form depends on the financial means of the one obligated to bring it. Five cases are enumerated. Three appear together at the end of *Parshat Vayikra* and constitute the classical category: *shmiat kol*, *bitui sfatayim*, and *tumat mikdash v'kodashav*. The other two — *yoledet* and *metzora* — are treated independently in the Torah but share the same sliding-scale structure.

### Five Cases Listed

*Al shmiat kol* — one who was subpoenaed to testify, swore falsely that he had no knowledge of the matter, and thereby failed to testify. *V'al bitui sfatayim* — one who swore to perform or refrain from an action and violated that oath. *V'al tumat mikdash v'kodashav* — one who entered the *Beit HaMikdash* or ate *korbanot* while *tamei*. These three appear adjacently in the Torah. Then *yoledet* — a woman after childbirth — and *metzora* — one afflicted with *tzara'at*.

### Substitution Categories

A *baraita* categorizes the forms of substitution. Some bring one for one: a single bird in place of a single lamb. Some bring two for two: two birds in place of two lambs. Some bring two for one: two birds in place of a single lamb. And some bring one for two: a single *mincha* in place of two birds.

The *yoledet*, who normally brings one lamb and one bird (the bird being the substitution for the second lamb), substitutes one for one. The *metzora*, who normally brings two lambs for his *chatat* and *olah* and substitutes with two birds if poor, substitutes two for two. The classical *oleh v'yored* cases — *shmiat kol*, *bitui sfatayim*, *tumat mikdash* — require a single lamb as the *chatat* in the wealthy case; the poor substitute brings two birds (two for one); and the destitute substitute brings a single *mincha* of a tenth of an *eifah* (one for two).

The Mishnah appends the observation: *mikan ata lamed she'asiris ha'eifah b'prutah* — from all of this we learn that a tenth of an *eifah* of flour is worth a single *prutah*. This appears to be a non-sequitur, and the *Gemara* undertakes to show that it is not.

## Oleh VeYored Offerin (II)

### Pricing Scripture

Before addressing the apparent non-sequitur, the *Gemara* establishes the pricing baseline. The *baraita* states: *ha'omer harei alai besela mizbeach, mevi keves, she'ein davar karev besela ela keves* — one who declares he will bring an offering worth a *sela* must bring a lamb, since the standard animal offered at the value of a *sela* is a lamb. The source: *mid'amar rachmana ayil asham bechesef shekalim* — the Torah requires a ram for the *asham* offering at a cost of silver *shekalim*. The standard silver coin unit — a *sela* — is the price of an *asham* ram, a two-year-old sheep. A one-year-old lamb, it is inferred, is worth half that: one *sela*. Thus the baseline is established: a lamb costs one *sela*.

## Ephah Value Calculation

### Non-Sequitur Posed

The question is how the Mishnah's concluding statement — that a tenth of an *eifah* costs a *prutah* — follows from the discussion of substitution categories. The *Gemara* works through the mathematics by a chain of ratios derived from the Torah's pricing of offerings.

### Sela Baseline

As established, a lamb costs one *sela*. The *sela* equals four *dinarim* of silver, and each *dinar* subdivides further into smaller denominations. The *prutah* is the smallest monetary unit of halakhic significance.

### 1-to-16 Ratio

The *Gemara* notes: *amdu kinim bo bayom beriv'asayim* — the standard price of a *kan* (a pair of birds) is a quarter of a *dinar*, which is a quarter of a quarter of a *sela*. Since a lamb is one *sela*, which is four *dinarim*, and a pair of birds is a quarter of a *dinar*, the ratio of two birds to one lamb is one to sixteen. This ratio is the Torah's permitted reduction for those who are *dal* — poor — in the context of *oleh v'yored*: instead of one lamb, they bring two birds at one-sixteenth the cost.

### Ratio Downward

Applying the same ratio downward from the birds to the *mincha*: *u'mid'chas rachmana lei dali dalut v'havei chad mishisar be'ashirus* — since the Torah permits the reduction of one-sixteenth from lamb to birds, the same ratio should apply from birds to *mincha*. One-sixteenth of a quarter-*dinar* (48 *prutos*) would be three *prutos*. This is more than a *prutah*, so the calculation does not yet arrive at the Mishnah's conclusion.

### Applying the Ratio Downward

The *Gemara* continues to apply the ratio and arrives at the problem: if the birds cost 48 *prutos* and the *mincha* is one-sixteenth of that, the result is three *prutos* — not one *prutah*. The Mishnah's claim that the *asirit ha'eifah* costs one *prutah* is not derivable from this ratio alone.

### 1-to-32 Ratio

The *Gemara* resolves this by invoking the *yoledet*: *tana miyoledet yalif* — the ratio is learned from the case of *yoledet*. There, the poor substitute brings one bird in place of one lamb — a ratio of one to thirty-two, since a single bird (half a *kan*) is half of a quarter-*dinar* against one full *sela*. Rava states explicitly: *kula milta miyoledet yalif* — the entire framework of proportionality is imported from the *yoledet*. The Torah's mercy to the destitute (*dali dalim*) in the *oleh v'yored* context follows the same ratio as the Torah's mercy to the poor *yoledet*: one to thirty-two.

### Pruta Minimum

Applying a one-to-thirty-two ratio to a single bird (24 *prutos* — half of the pair's 48) yields twenty-four divided by thirty-two, which is three-quarters of a *prutah*. The *Gemara* acknowledges the math: *nechi riv'a depruta havei* — it comes out to a *prutah* minus a quarter. The *Gemara* accepts the calculation and rules: *ela delav orech ar'a le'atuyei pachot mipruta lemikdash* — it is simply not the practice to bring anything worth less than a *prutah* to the Temple. The minimum denomination for a Temple offering is a *prutah*; anything less is rounded up. Therefore, even though the arithmetic yields slightly under a *prutah*, in practice the *asirit ha'eifah* is valued at one *prutah* — and that is the source for the Mishnah's otherwise puzzling concluding statement. It is not a non-sequitur; it is the endpoint of a precise chain of ratios drawn from the Torah's graduated pricing of the *oleh v'yored* offerings.
