# Pesachim 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prior Dispute Review

### Rabbi Meir Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מִכֵּלִים הוּא דַּהֲדַר בֵּיהּ, אֲבָל בָּאוֹכָלִין כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. אוֹ דִילְמָא: לִגְמָרֵי הֲדַר בֵּיהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Was **it** only **from** his ruling with regard to liquids that transmitted ritual impurity to **vessels** by Torah law **that** Rabbi Yehuda **retracted** his opinion, **but with regard to foods he** still **holds in accordance with** the opinions of **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon,** that liquids transmit impurity to foods by Torah law? **Or perhaps he completely retracted** his previous opinion, and Rabbi Yehuda in fact holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who rules that there is no Torah basis for the impurity of liquids.

The daf under discussion opens by returning to a dispute that had been established on 17b, concerning a case of *safek* — doubtful status — involving *mashkin tmayim*, defiled liquids, and whether those liquids then impart *tumah* to whatever they subsequently contact. Rabbi Meir takes the most lenient position: since *tum'at mashkin* is *mid'rabbanan* in his view, a *safek* arising from a rabbinic prohibition is treated leniently, and the item in question is *tahor*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָזַר בּוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: Rabbi Yehuda retracted** his previous statement in the *baraita* and accepted the ruling that the impurity of liquids is only by rabbinic decree.

Rabbi Yehuda initially occupies the opposite pole. In his opinion, *mashkin* are *metam'im mid'oraita*, and therefore a *safeika mid'oraita* is treated *l'chumra* — the item is *tamei*. If Rabbi Meir represents an extreme in leniency, Rabbi Yehuda represents an extreme in stringency. At some point, however, Rabbi Yehuda was said to have reversed himself — *chazar bah* — and the question now is the extent of that reversal.

### R. Yose & Shimon Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: לָאוֹכָלִין — טְמֵאִין, לְכֵלִים — טְהוֹרִים.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita* about uncertainty as to the impurity of liquids that **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** With regard **to foods** that came into contact with impure liquid, the foods **are impure.** However, with regard **to vessels** that came into contact with impure liquid, the vessels **are pure.**

Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon occupy a middle position, splitting their ruling between categories. They hold that in a case of *safek*, items of food (*ochlin*) are *tamei*, while vessels (*kelim*) are *tahor*. The implication of their position is that *mashkin* are *metam'im ochlin mid'oraita* — which is why they rule stringently regarding *ochlin* — while *tum'at kelim* through *mashkin* is only *mid'rabbanan*, and therefore *safek* is treated leniently when it comes to *kelim*.

### Extent of Reversal

With this framework in place, the question sharpens: when Rabbi Yehuda reversed himself, did he retreat only one step — aligning with Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon, meaning he now holds that *kelim* are *tahor* in a case of *safek* but *ochlin* remain *tamei* — or did he reverse himself entirely, becoming as lenient as Rabbi Meir and holding that these *mashkin* cannot render anything *tamei* at all? This question, *ibayu lehu mi-kelim hu d'hadar beh o b'ochlin k'Rabbi Yose v'Rabbi Shimon sevirah leh — o dilma legamrei hadar beh k'Rabbi Meir*, drives the analysis forward.

## Cow & Mei Chatat Case

### Mei Chatat Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, תָּא שְׁמַע: פָּרָה שֶׁשָּׁתְתָה מֵי חַטָּאת — בְּשָׂרָהּ טָמֵא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר:
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: Come** and **hear** a resolution to this dilemma from a mishna: With regard to **a cow that drank purification waters** in which the ashes of the red heifer were mixed and which were to be sprinkled on one who was ritually impure with impurity imparted by a corpse, and the cow was slaughtered before it digested the water, **its flesh is impure** due to contact with this water. Pure items that come in contact with the purification waters become impure, as derived from a verse. **Rabbi Yehuda says:**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes to resolve the question by examining a specific case. The case involves *mei chatat* — the waters prepared from the ashes of the *parah adumah*, the Red Heifer — which carry a well-known paradox: they purify the *tamei* and render *tamei* the *tahor*. These waters represent a rare and highly regulated substance. Under ordinary circumstances, *mei chatat* carry the status of *av hatumah* — a primary source of *tumah* — not merely a *rishon* or any secondary derivative.

### Slaughter Scenario

The constructed case is the following: a cow — an ordinary, non-consecrated animal — wanders to the vessel containing *mei chatat* and drinks from it. The cow is then slaughtered (*shechita* is performed) before the water has passed through its digestive system, so the liquid remains inside the animal. The question is: what is the status of the meat of this cow, which now contains within it what was, prior to being swallowed, an *av hatumah*?

### Meat Status Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּטְלוּ בְּמֵעֶיהָ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מִכֵּלִים הוּא דַּהֲדַר בֵּיהּ, אֲבָל בָּאוֹכָלִין כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, אַמַּאי בָּטְלוּ בְּמֵעֶיהָ לִגְמָרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The purification waters **are nullified in its innards** and do not impurify the meat of the cow. **And if it enters your mind** that it was **from** his ruling with regard to liquids transmitting impurity to **vessels** by Torah law **that** Rabbi Yehuda **retracted** his opinion, **but with regard to foods he holds in accordance with** the opinions of **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon** that liquids transmit ritual impurity to food by Torah law, **why** does he say that the purification waters **are nullified in its innards entirely** and no longer transfer impurity at all?

The ruling recorded is *besarah tamei* — the flesh is *tamei*. Rabbi Yehuda then offers a dissenting position: *batlu b'me'eha* — they were nullified within its intestines — implying that the meat is *tahor*. This appears to be a *machloket* between a *tana kama* and Rabbi Yehuda. Since this is a case in which *mashkin tmayim* are now in contact with food — namely, the flesh of the slaughtered animal — and Rabbi Yehuda says the flesh is *tahor*, this would seem to answer the earlier question about his *chazarah*: it appears he holds that *mashkin tmayim* cannot render *ochlin tamei* at all, which would place him entirely in the camp of Rabbi Meir.

### Chazarah Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהִי דְּטוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה לָא מְטַמְּאוּ, טוּמְאָה קַלָּה מִיהָא נִיטַמְּאוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **Although** these waters **do not transmit a severe** form of **ritual impurity** to a person or vessel that comes into contact with them, **in any event let them transmit a lesser** form of **impurity** to food that comes into contact with them. Rabbi Yehuda's ruling with regard to the purification waters indicates that he retracted his previous opinion entirely and he maintains that there is no impurity of liquids by Torah law at all. Since this impurity is by rabbinic law, the Sages did not extend their decree to the uncommon circumstance of the purification waters.

The *gemara* examines this reasoning carefully. If Rabbi Yehuda's reversal was only partial — that is, if he aligned with Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon and still holds that *mashkin* can render *ochlin tamei* — then why does he say *batlu b'me'eha* with no qualification? The objection is framed: *nehi d'tumah chamura lo metamu — tumah kala miha nitamu*? Even granting that the severe dimension of *tumah* might be nullified, surely some lighter degree of *tumah* should still apply?

### Tumah Gravity Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בָּטְלוּ בְּמֵעֶיהָ״ נָמֵי? בָּטְלוּ מִטּוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה, אֲבָל טוּמְאָה קַלָּה מְטַמְּאוּ. מִכְּלָל דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה נָמֵי מְטַמְּאוּ, הָא ״בְּשָׂרָהּ טָמֵא״ קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention: **What, too, is** the meaning of Rabbi Yehuda's phrase: **They are nullified in its innards? It** means that **they are nullified** only **from a severe** form of **impurity. However,** according to Rabbi Yehuda, the purification waters **transmit a lesser form of impurity.** This proves **by inference that the first *tanna* maintains** that the purification waters **also transmit a severe** form of **impurity** to people and vessels. This is a problematic conclusion, as the first *tanna* **teaches: Its flesh is impure,** which clearly indicates that its flesh alone is impure, whereas the purification waters swallowed by the cow do not transmit impurity to people or vessels. The result is that according to this approach, there is no difference between the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and the first *tanna*.

The *gemara* responds by reinterpreting the word *batlu*. Rather than meaning the *tumah* is completely annihilated, *batlu b'me'eha* means the liquid is no longer an *av hatumah* — the *tumah chamura*, the severe grade, is gone. However, it still retains *tumah kalah*, a lighter grade of *tumah*. So the meat is indeed *tamei* in the sense of *tumah kalah* — the water, once inside the animal, has been downgraded from an *av* to something more like a *rishon*, and a *rishon* can still render flesh *tamei*. The flesh becomes *tamei* through *tumah kalah*, but the liquid can no longer function as an *av hatumah* to render *kelim tamei*.

### Reconciling Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלֵּהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, וְחַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: פָּרָה שֶׁשָּׁתְתָה מֵי חַטָּאת — בְּשָׂרָהּ טָמֵא. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — טוּמְאָה קַלָּה, אֲבָל טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה — לֹא, שֶׁרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בָּטְלוּ בְּמֵעֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The **entire** mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and** the mishna **is incomplete and is teaching the following:** With regard to **a cow that drank the purification waters, its flesh is impure. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said with regard to **a lesser** form of **impurity, but** with regard to **a severe** form of **purity, no,** its flesh is not impure, **as Rabbi Yehuda says:** The waters **are nullified in its innards** and their status is no longer that of purification waters. Instead, their impurity is by rabbinic law, like any other liquid.

This reinterpretation raises a further question about the *tana kama*. If Rabbi Yehuda — the lenient one — still holds the meat is *tamei* by virtue of *tumah kalah*, then the *tana kama*, who is apparently the more stringent opinion, should hold that *tumah chamura* still applies — that the water remains an *av hatumah* even inside the animal. But if it is a full *av hatumah*, then why does the *tana kama* say only *besarah tamei*? An *av hatumah* would render *kelim tamei* as well, so why is the ruling limited to the flesh?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם בָּטְלוּ בְּמֵעֶיהָ לִגְמָרֵי, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ מַשְׁקֶה סָרוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually,** it is possible to explain that the waters **are nullified in its innards entirely** from any type of impurity, as this issue is unrelated to the question of whether the impurity of liquids is by Torah law or rabbinic law. Rather, this *halakha* is **due to** the fact that purification waters **become a foul liquid** when ingested, and the principle is that offensive liquid can neither be rendered impure itself nor transfer impurity to other items.

The *gemara* answers: *kula Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the entire passage reflects Rabbi Yehuda's opinion alone. There is no independent *tana kama* here; the text requires the principle of *chasorei mechasra*, meaning the passage as it stands is incomplete and must be supplemented. Reading it fully: the case of *parah she'shat'tah mei chatat* — *besarah tamei* — refers only to *tumah kalah*. The qualification *bameh devarim amurim* applies: this is specifically not *tumah chamura*, since Rabbi Yehuda himself says *batlu b'me'eha* — nullified with respect to *tumah chamura*, but retaining *tumah kalah*. It is all Rabbi Yehuda's own elaboration of his position.

Rav Ashi, however, offers an alternative reading. Rather than reinterpreting *batlu*, he maintains the plain meaning — *le'olam batlu b'mayim legamrei*, it truly is fully nullified. But this case does not resolve the question of Rabbi Yehuda's general position regarding *mashkin tmayim*, because this situation is categorically different: *mishum dehavai lei mashkeh saruach* — once the liquid has been swallowed by the animal and sits within its digestive tract, it is no longer considered a proper *mashkeh* at all. It has become disgusting, *saruach*, and just as a food that has become putrefied beyond the point of being *raui l'achilat kelev* loses its status under various laws, this liquid too loses its *tumah*-status. The *parah* case is therefore a special case, and Rabbi Yehuda's position in ordinary cases of *mashkin tmayim* remains unresolved.

## Yitma Yitma Derashah

### R. Yose Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: לָאוֹכָלִין — טְמֵאִין, לְכֵלִים — טְהוֹרִים. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא רַבּוֹ אֲמָרָהּ, דְּדָרֵישׁ ״יִטְמָא״ — יְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the *baraita* about uncertainty as to the impurity of liquids that **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say:** With regard **to foods** that came into contact with impure liquid, the foods **are impure.** However, with regard **to vessels** that came into contact with impure liquid, the vessels **are pure. Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Reish Lakish said: Rabbi Yosei said** this *halakha* **in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, his teacher, who interpreted** the term ***yitma*,** which is an intransitive verb in the simple conjugation meaning: **It shall be impure,** as though it were written ***yetamme*,** a transitive verb in the intensive conjugation meaning: **It shall render impure,** i.e., it transmits impurity to other items.

The *gemara* now turns to Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon's position — *l'ochlin tme'in ul'kelim tehorin* — that *mashkin* render *ochlin tamei* but not *kelim*. Rabbah bar bar Chana reports in the name of Reish Lakish that Rabbi Yose arrived at his position by following the *shitat* his teacher, Rabbi Akiva: *Rabbi Yose b'shitat Rabbi Akiva rabo amrah*. This attribution is significant: Rabbi Yose is presenting the underlying method of Rabbi Akiva, though he may not follow it to all of its conclusions. The *limmud* — the textual derivation — is from the doubled form *yitma yitma*.

### Torah Verses Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם דָּרַשׁ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: ״וְכׇל כְּלִי חֶרֶשׂ אֲשֶׁר יִפֹּל מֵהֶם וְגוֹ׳״, אֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר ״טָמֵא״ אֶלָּא ״יִטְמָא״ — יְטַמֵּא אֲחֵרִים, לִימֵּד עַל כִּכָּר שֵׁנִי שֶׁעוֹשֶׂה שְׁלִישִׁי בְּחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna: **On that day,** when they appointed Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya the *Nasi*, **Rabbi Akiva taught: "And every earthenware vessel** into which **any of them** falls, whatever is in it shall be impure [*yitma*], and you shall break it" (Leviticus 11:33). The verse **does not say: It is impure [*tameh*]; rather,** it says: **It shall be impure [*yitma*],** indicating that an item in an impure earthenware vessel **transmits impurity to other** items. **This** verse **teaches about a loaf** with **second-**degree ritual impurity status, i.e., ritual impurity imparted through contact with a vessel impurified by a creeping animal, **that** the loaf **renders** other items impure with **third-**degree ritual impurity, even **non-sacred** items.

The *gemara* turns to the relevant *pesukim* in *Parshat Shmini* (Vayikra). The first passage discusses what happens when a dead *sheretz* — one of the eight ritually unclean crawling creatures — falls upon various objects. The Torah states: *mikol kli eitz o veged o or o sak v'chol kli asher yeiaseh melachah bahem — bamayim yuva v'tamei ad haerev v'taher*. An object that a dead *sheretz* lands upon becomes *tamei* and requires immersion.

### Clay Vessel Case

The next *pasuk* presents a distinct scenario: *v'chol kli cheres asher yipol meihem el tocho — kol asher b'tocho yitma*. When a dead *sheretz* falls into a *kli cheres* — a clay vessel — everything inside it becomes *tamei*. The mechanism here is distinctive: a *kli cheres* conveys *tumah* through its airspace (*mei'aviro*). One need not have the *sheretz* touch the food directly; even dangling it within the interior of the clay vessel renders everything inside *tamei*. The clay vessel, unlike other vessels, cannot be purified — it must be broken. The food within it, however, must previously have been made susceptible to *tumah* through contact with liquid (*hechsher*); it need not be wet at the moment of contact, but it must have been wet at some prior point after being detached.

### Food vs. Liquid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא הֵיכִי דָּרֵישׁ? ״וְכׇל מַשְׁקֶה אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׁתֶה בְּכׇל כְּלִי יִטְמָא״ — יְטַמֵּא, לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין? אָמַרְתָּ: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **And here,** with regard to the ritual impurity of liquids, **how does** Rabbi Yosei **interpret** the verses? The Gemara cites the verse: "From all food which may be eaten, upon which water has come, shall be impure; **and all drink that may be drunk in every vessel, shall be impure [*yitma*]"** (Leviticus 11:34). Rabbi Yosei interprets the end of the verse as: **Shall render impure [*yetamme*],** indicating that liquid also **transmits impurity to foods.** The Gemara discusses this derivation: Do **you say** that this expression teaches that liquid **transmits ritual impurity to foods, or perhaps** the verse **is** teaching **only** that **it transmits impurity to** other **liquids,** but not to food? **You said** in response: **That was not** the correct interpretation.

The *gemara* then reads the next pair of verses: *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel asher yavo alav mayim yitma — v'chol mashkeh asher yeshateh b'chol kli yitma*. Two categories are presented: *ochel* — food upon which *mayim* have come — and *mashkeh* — liquid in a vessel. In each case the word *yitma* appears. The critical observation is that the Torah, which is written without vowel markings, uses *yitma* twice — once for *ochel* and once for *mashkeh*.

### Yitma Vocalization

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה״? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא מָצִינוּ טוּמְאָה שֶׁעוֹשָׂה כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders about the unusual expression. **What is** the meaning of the phrase: **That was not** the correct interpretation? How can this difficulty be dismissed so easily? **Rav Pappa said:** It means that **we did not find** any case of **ritual impurity that renders a similar** item impure. Therefore, it must be that the verse teaches that this liquid transmits impurity to food.

The word *yitma* as vocalized is passive: "shall become *tamei*." But the same consonantal text can be vocalized as *yetamei*, meaning active: "shall render *tamei*," i.e., shall make something else *tamei*. Rabbi Akiva's method — and Rabbi Yose's adoption of it — exploits this ambiguity. By reading *yitma* as *yetamei*, one derives that both the *ochel* and the *mashkeh* do not merely become *tamei* themselves but also impart *tumah* to other things.

### R. Yose Derashah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: מִגּוּפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא נָמֵי לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ ״יִטְמָא״ — לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ ״יִטְמָא״ דְּסֵיפָא לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין, ״יִטְמָא״ דְּרֵישָׁא, נָמֵי לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין. נִיעָרְבִינְהוּ וְנִיכְתְּבִינְהוּ: ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל אֲשֶׁר יָבוֹא עָלָיו מַיִם וְכׇל מַשְׁקֶה אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׁתֶה בְּכׇל כְּלִי יִטְמָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: From** an analysis of **the verse itself you also cannot say** that the term: **Shall be impure,** means that the liquid **transmits ritual impurity** only to **liquids. As, if it enters your mind** to say that the term: **Shall be impure, in the latter** portion of the verse means that **it transmits ritual impurity** only to **liquids,** then the term: **Shall be impure, in the first** portion of the verse, in reference to food, should **also** mean that **it transmits ritual impurity** only to **liquids.** And if that is so, **let** the verse **combine** the two cases **and write them** together as follows: **From all food which may be eaten, upon which water has come, and all drink that may be drunk in every vessel, shall be impure.**

The question then becomes: what precisely can the *mashkeh* render *tamei* — other *mashkin*, *ochlin*, or *kelim*? Rabbi Yose's conclusion — derived through the *yitma yitma* reading in the *shitat* Rabbi Akiva — is that *mashkin* can render *ochlin tamei* but cannot render *kelim tamei*. The *pasuk* uses the doubled *yitma* precisely to capture this asymmetry: the active dimension (*yetamei*) is present, but it is bounded. This is Rabbi Yose's *derashah*: *mashkin metame ochlin* but not *kelim*. That is the basis for his split ruling in the *safek* case — *l'ochlin tme'in, l'kelim tehorin*.

## Can Liquid Metame Liquid?

### Same Type Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרֵי ״יִטְמָא״ לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא: ״יִטְמָא״ דְּרֵישָׁא לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין, ״יִטְמָא״ דְּסֵיפָא לְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אוֹכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina concludes his proof: **Why do I** need the term: **Shall be impure, twice? Rather,** it must be that the term: **Shall be impure, in the first** portion of the verse teaches that food **transmits ritual impurity** to **liquids,** while the term: **Shall be impure, in the latter** portion of the verse teaches that liquid **transmits ritual impurity** to **food.**

Having established that *mashkin* can render *ochlin tamei*, the *gemara* raises a further question: can *tamei mashkin* render other *mashkin tamei*? The Talmud's answer is no, and the reasoning is articulated in two ways.

### Rav Papa Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא מַשְׁקִין מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲלוּלִין לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה, תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּלֵיכָּא מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why did** Rabbi Akiva mention **specifically** that food impurifies **liquids due to** the fact that **they are susceptible to contracting impurity? Let him derive** this proof from the simple fact **that there is no other item** that food could render impure. As food does not transmit impurity to food, the only remaining alternative is that food impurifies liquids.

Rav Papa states the principle: *lo matzinu tumah osah kayotzei bah* — we have never found a case in which a *tumah* renders something of its own kind *tamei*. An *ochel* does not make another *ochel tamei*; a *mashkeh* does not make another *mashkeh tamei*. Only entities of different categories act upon one another. This principle is striking and far-reaching. It implies that when *tamei* and *tahor* items of the same category become mixed — as in numerous earlier cases involving *terumah teme'ah* falling into *terumah tehorah* — the issue is not that the entire mixture becomes *tamei* through transmission; rather, the individual items retain their own status, and the *halachic* difficulty is simply one of *bitul* — one cannot identify which item is which. The *tamei* items do not actively render the *tahor* ones *tamei*. This reading of Rav Papa's principle, though surprising, appears to be uncontested in the *gemara*.

### Ravina Textual Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכִי תֵּימָא אוֹכֶל חָמוּר, דִּמְטַמֵּא מַשְׁקִין, נִיטַמְּיֵיהּ לִכְלִי. הַהוּא חוּמְרָא דְמַשְׁקִין הוּא, מִשּׁוּם דְּמַשְׁקִין עֲלוּלִין לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this is** what Rabbi Akiva **is saying: And lest you say** that the impurity of **food is severe,** as evidenced by the fact **that** it **transmits ritual impurity to liquids** despite the fact that liquids do not transfer impurity to other liquids, and therefore **let** food **transmit impurity to a vessel,** despite the fact that liquid does not transmit impurity to a vessel; therefore, the Gemara states **that** the fact that food transmits impurity to liquids **is** actually **a stringency** characteristic of **liquids,** not of food. The impurity of food is not more severe than that of liquids; rather, food transmits impurity to liquids **due to** the fact **that liquids** are **susceptible to contracting impurity.**

Ravina adds a textual proof from the very *pesukim* under discussion. If one were to claim that *mashkin* render other *mashkin tamei*, then the *yitma* of the *seifa* — applied to *mashkeh* — would mean: liquid renders liquid *tamei*. But then what would the *yitma* of the *reisha* — applied to *ochel* — mean? It could only mean: food renders liquid *tamei*. The result would be that both the *reisha* and the *seifa* are teaching the same thing — that some substance renders *mashkeh tamei*. If so, the Torah could have collapsed them into a single *pasuk*: whether food or liquid, it renders liquid *tamei*. The fact that the Torah uses *yitma* separately in each clause — once for *ochel* and once for *mashkeh* — demands that they be teaching two different things.

### Two Yitmas Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה הִיא עֲלִילָתָן — שֶׁמְּקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה שֶׁלֹּא בְּהֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in **what** manner **is their susceptibility** manifest? It is manifest in the fact **that they become ritually impure without** being first rendered **susceptible** to impurity. Foods can become impure only after first coming into contact with one of seven liquids. Liquids do not require any preparatory stage before becoming impure.

The two *yitmas* therefore teach two distinct *dinim*: the first *yitma*, applied to *ochel*, means that food renders *mashkin tamei* (*yitma de'reisha letamei tumah de'mashkin*); the second *yitma*, applied to *mashkeh*, means that liquid renders *ochlin tamei* (*yitma de'seifa letamei tumah de'ochlin*). Each *yitma* conveys an asymmetric relationship: food acts on liquid, and liquid acts on food. Neither acts on its own kind.

## Liquid & Vessels

### Why Not Vessels?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְטַמֵּא אֶת הַכֵּלִים! וְלָאו קַל וָחוֹמֶר הוּא, וּמָה כְּלִי שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא מַשְׁקֶה — אֵין מְטַמֵּא כְּלִי, מַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת כְּלִי — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְטַמְּאוּ אֶת הַכֵּלִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that the term teaches that liquid **transmits ritual impurity to vessels.** The Gemara rejects this contention: **And isn't it an *a fortiori*** inference? **Just as** an impure **vessel, which transmits impurity to liquid** that comes into contact with it, nevertheless **does not transmit impurity to** another **vessel,** so too, **liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with **a vessel, is it not right that they** should **not transmit impurity to vessels?**

Having resolved that *mashkin* render *ochlin tamei* but not other *mashkin tamei*, the *gemara* now asks why *mashkin* cannot also render *kelim tamei*. Why should *kelim* be excluded?

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: כִּי לָא מְטַמְּאוּ, מַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת כְּלִי. אֲבָל מַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ, הָכִי נָמֵי דִּמְטַמְּאוּ! מַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ מִי כְּתִיבִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And say that when** liquids **do not transmit ritual impurity** to a vessel, that is in the case of **liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with an impure **vessel,** as the vessel itself does not transmit impurity to another vessel. **However,** with regard to **liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with **a creeping animal,** a more severe form of impurity, **indeed, they** should **transmit impurity** even to vessels. The Gemara rejects this contention: **Are liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with **a creeping animal stated** explicitly in the Torah? The impure liquids mentioned in the verse became impure by contact with a vessel that came into contact with a dead creeping animal.

The attempt to include *kelim* proceeds by way of a *kal vachomer*. The original scenario involves a *sheretz* dangled in the airspace of a *kli cheres*: the *kli* becomes *tamei*, and as a result the *mashkin* inside it become *tamei*. These *mashkin* — which became *tamei* through the *kli* — are called *mashkin haba'in machmat kli*. Now, we know that a *kli* itself cannot render another *kli tamei*. If so, these *mashkin*, which derive their *tumah* from the *kli*, certainly should not be able to render a *kli tamei*: *eino din shelo yetamei et hakeilim*.

However, the *gemara* raises a second scenario: what if the *sheretz* does not fall into the airspace of the *kli* but falls directly into the *mashkeh* itself, touching the liquid? In that case, the *mashkin* derive their *tumah* directly from the *sheretz* (*mashkin haba'in machmat sheretz*), not through the *kli*. Should a *kal vachomer* not apply — if *mashkin haba'in machmat kli* cannot render *kelim tamei*, surely *mashkin haba'in machmat sheretz* — which are more severely *tamei*, since they were directly contaminated by an *av hatumah* — can render *kelim tamei*?

### Sheretz Direct Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָאו מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר קָאָתֵי: וּמָה מַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת כְּלִי — מְטַמְּאִין, מַשְׁקִין הַבָּאִים מֵחֲמַת שֶׁרֶץ — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And doesn't** the impurity of liquids that came into contact with a creeping animal **come from an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with **a vessel,** which itself became impure due to contact with a creeping animal, **transmit impurity,** with regard to **liquids that come to** a state of impurity **due to** contact with **a creeping animal, all the more so** is it **not** clear that they transmit impurity to a vessel?

The *gemara* thus considers whether the case of a *sheretz* falling directly into water creates a *halachic* difference. The question is pointed: if a *sheretz* is floating in a pool of water and the water touches a *kli*, what is the status of that *kli*? The case where the *sheretz* itself is in the liquid — rather than in the airspace of the vessel — is not explicitly covered by the *pesukim*; one would have to derive it. The *kal vachomer* suggests it should be *metame* the *kli* a fortiori from the case where the *kli* itself is the source.

### Dayo Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Since the basic *halakha* of these liquids is derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference, no stringencies are added to it, in accordance with the principle: **It is sufficient [*dayyo*]** for the conclusion that **emerged from the** *a fortiori* **inference** to **be like the source of the inference.** In other words, a *halakha* derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference is no more stringent than the source from which it is derived. In this case, liquid that became impure through contact with a creeping animal transmits impurity to the same extent that liquid that became impure through contact with a vessel does.

The *gemara* responds with the principle of *dayo l'va min hadin lihyot kenidon* — it is sufficient for the conclusion of a *kal vachomer* to equal the *din* of its source, and one may not extend it beyond. The Torah's case established that whatever is inside the *kli* — food or liquid — becomes *tamei*. The maximum that the *kal vachomer* may derive in the case of a *sheretz* directly in the water is exactly what was established in the original precedent: the liquid can render *ochlin tamei*. It cannot, by extension of the *kal vachomer*, be made to render *kelim tamei*, because the original case never established *tumah* for *kelim* through this mechanism. The *dayo* principle blocks the inference at that point.

## R. Akiva Derashah

### First Yitma Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יִטְמָא״ דְּרֵישָׁא הֵיכִי דָּרֵישׁ? ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל אֲשֶׁר יָבוֹא עָלָיו מַיִם יִטְמָא״, יִטְמָא — לְטַמֵּא אֶת הַמַּשְׁקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How does** Rabbi Akiva **interpret** the term: **Shall be impure, in the first** portion of the verse: **"From all food which may be eaten, upon which water has come, shall be impure"** (Leviticus 11:34)? He interprets the term: **Shall be impure,** as: Shall render impure. Food **transmits ritual impurity to liquids.**

Returning now to Rabbi Akiva's own *derashah* on the *yitma yitma*, the *gemara* examines how he himself reads each of the two instances. The first *yitma*, from *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel asher yavo alav mayim yitma*, Rabbi Akiva reads as teaching: the food renders *mashkin tamei* — *yitamei letamei et hamashkin*.

### Second Yitma Liquid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר לְטַמֵּא אֶת הַמַּשְׁקִין, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְטַמֵּא אֶת הַכְּלִי?
>
> **Translation:** Do **you say** that the term teaches that food **transmits impurity to liquids, or perhaps** the term teaches **only** that food **transmits impurity to a vessel?**

The second *yitma*, from *v'chol mashkeh asher yeshateh b'chol kli yitma*, he reads as teaching: the liquid renders *ochlin tamei* — *yitamei letamei et ha'ochlin*. These are the two directions of transmission established by the doubled language: *ochel* to *mashkeh*, and *mashkeh* to *ochel*.

### Food Cannot Metame Food

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מַשְׁקֶה שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא אוֹכֶל — אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא כְּלִי, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאֵין מְטַמֵּא אוֹכֶל — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְטַמְּאוּ כְּלִי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You** can **say an *a fortiori*** inference that negates this possibility: **Just as liquid, which transmits impurity to food, does not transmit impurity to a vessel,** with regard to **food, which does not transmit impurity to food, is it not right that it** should **not transmit impurity to a vessel?**

One might ask: if *ochel* is *metame mashkin*, why should we not say it is also *metame kelim*? Rabbi Akiva establishes this via *kal vachomer* in the negative. A *mashkeh* renders *ochlin tamei* but does not render *kelim tamei* — this was established above. An *ochel*, which cannot even render another *ochel tamei* (since like cannot make like *tamei*), is certainly not going to render *kelim tamei*: *ochel she'ein metamei ochel — eino din shelo yetamei kli*. The logic cuts in the negative direction: the weaker party (*ochel*, which cannot act on its own kind) certainly cannot exceed what the stronger party (*mashkeh*, which can act on a different category) is permitted to do.

### Mishkin Severity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״יִטְמָא״ — לְטַמֵּא אֶת הַמַּשְׁקִין שֶׁהֵן עֲלוּלִין לְקַבֵּל טוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **how** then **do I establish** the meaning of the term: **Shall be impure,** which in this context indicates that food impurifies other items? This term indicates that food **transmits impurity to liquids, which are susceptible to contracting impurity.**

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: perhaps *ochlin* should be considered more severe than *mashkin* — after all, *ochel* is *metame mashkin*, and perhaps that greater capacity warrants a conclusion that *ochel* should also be *metame kelim*. The *gemara* rejects this: the apparent severity of *ochlin* is not intrinsic to food itself. It is *chumra demashkin hu* — it is a stringency that flows from the nature of *mashkin*.

### Chumra Mishkin

The explanation is as follows: *mashkin* are more susceptible to receiving *tumah* than *ochlin* because *mashkin* can receive *tumah* without any prior *hechsher* — without any preparatory act. A food item must first have liquid come upon it (*hechsher b'mayim*) before it can receive *tumah*; without this preparation, even direct contact with an *av hatumah* leaves the food *tahor*. A *mashkeh*, by contrast, requires no such preparation — by its very nature it is susceptible to *tumah*. This greater receptivity of *mashkin* to *tumah* is what gives them their apparent force, so that when they contact *ochlin*, they render them *tamei*. But this *chumra* belongs to the *mashkeh*, not to the *ochel*. The *ochel*'s ability to render *mashkin tamei* is therefore a reflection of the *mashkeh*'s own susceptibility — *mishum demashkin dekabel tumah shelo behechsher* — not a sign that *ochlin* themselves occupy an elevated position in the hierarchy of *tumah*. Consequently, the *kal vachomer* that would have extended *ochel*'s reach to *kelim* is without foundation.
