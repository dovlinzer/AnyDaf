# Pesachim 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prior Dispute Review

### Rabbi Meir Position

The daf under discussion opens by returning to a dispute that had been established on 17b, concerning a case of *safek* — doubtful status — involving *mashkin tmayim*, defiled liquids, and whether those liquids then impart *tumah* to whatever they subsequently contact. Rabbi Meir takes the most lenient position: since *tum'at mashkin* is *mid'rabbanan* in his view, a *safek* arising from a rabbinic prohibition is treated leniently, and the item in question is *tahor*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Reversal

Rabbi Yehuda initially occupies the opposite pole. In his opinion, *mashkin* are *metam'im mid'oraita*, and therefore a *safeika mid'oraita* is treated *l'chumra* — the item is *tamei*. If Rabbi Meir represents an extreme in leniency, Rabbi Yehuda represents an extreme in stringency. At some point, however, Rabbi Yehuda was said to have reversed himself — *chazar bah* — and the question now is the extent of that reversal.

### R. Yose & Shimon Split

Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon occupy a middle position, splitting their ruling between categories. They hold that in a case of *safek*, items of food (*ochlin*) are *tamei*, while vessels (*kelim*) are *tahor*. The implication of their position is that *mashkin* are *metam'im ochlin mid'oraita* — which is why they rule stringently regarding *ochlin* — while *tum'at kelim* through *mashkin* is only *mid'rabbanan*, and therefore *safek* is treated leniently when it comes to *kelim*.

### Extent of Reversal

With this framework in place, the question sharpens: when Rabbi Yehuda reversed himself, did he retreat only one step — aligning with Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon, meaning he now holds that *kelim* are *tahor* in a case of *safek* but *ochlin* remain *tamei* — or did he reverse himself entirely, becoming as lenient as Rabbi Meir and holding that these *mashkin* cannot render anything *tamei* at all? This question, *ibayu lehu mi-kelim hu d'hadar beh o b'ochlin k'Rabbi Yose v'Rabbi Shimon sevirah leh — o dilma legamrei hadar beh k'Rabbi Meir*, drives the analysis forward.

## Cow & Mei Chatat Case

### Mei Chatat Setup

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes to resolve the question by examining a specific case. The case involves *mei chatat* — the waters prepared from the ashes of the *parah adumah*, the Red Heifer — which carry a well-known paradox: they purify the *tamei* and render *tamei* the *tahor*. These waters represent a rare and highly regulated substance. Under ordinary circumstances, *mei chatat* carry the status of *av hatumah* — a primary source of *tumah* — not merely a *rishon* or any secondary derivative.

### Slaughter Scenario

The constructed case is the following: a cow — an ordinary, non-consecrated animal — wanders to the vessel containing *mei chatat* and drinks from it. The cow is then slaughtered (*shechita* is performed) before the water has passed through its digestive system, so the liquid remains inside the animal. The question is: what is the status of the meat of this cow, which now contains within it what was, prior to being swallowed, an *av hatumah*?

### Meat Status Dispute

The ruling recorded is *besarah tamei* — the flesh is *tamei*. Rabbi Yehuda then offers a dissenting position: *batlu b'me'eha* — they were nullified within its intestines — implying that the meat is *tahor*. This appears to be a *machloket* between a *tana kama* and Rabbi Yehuda. Since this is a case in which *mashkin tmayim* are now in contact with food — namely, the flesh of the slaughtered animal — and Rabbi Yehuda says the flesh is *tahor*, this would seem to answer the earlier question about his *chazarah*: it appears he holds that *mashkin tmayim* cannot render *ochlin tamei* at all, which would place him entirely in the camp of Rabbi Meir.

### Chazarah Analysis

The *gemara* examines this reasoning carefully. If Rabbi Yehuda's reversal was only partial — that is, if he aligned with Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon and still holds that *mashkin* can render *ochlin tamei* — then why does he say *batlu b'me'eha* with no qualification? The objection is framed: *nehi d'tumah chamura lo metamu — tumah kala miha nitamu*? Even granting that the severe dimension of *tumah* might be nullified, surely some lighter degree of *tumah* should still apply?

### Tumah Gravity Levels

The *gemara* responds by reinterpreting the word *batlu*. Rather than meaning the *tumah* is completely annihilated, *batlu b'me'eha* means the liquid is no longer an *av hatumah* — the *tumah chamura*, the severe grade, is gone. However, it still retains *tumah kalah*, a lighter grade of *tumah*. So the meat is indeed *tamei* in the sense of *tumah kalah* — the water, once inside the animal, has been downgraded from an *av* to something more like a *rishon*, and a *rishon* can still render flesh *tamei*. The flesh becomes *tamei* through *tumah kalah*, but the liquid can no longer function as an *av hatumah* to render *kelim tamei*.

### Reconciling Positions

This reinterpretation raises a further question about the *tana kama*. If Rabbi Yehuda — the lenient one — still holds the meat is *tamei* by virtue of *tumah kalah*, then the *tana kama*, who is apparently the more stringent opinion, should hold that *tumah chamura* still applies — that the water remains an *av hatumah* even inside the animal. But if it is a full *av hatumah*, then why does the *tana kama* say only *besarah tamei*? An *av hatumah* would render *kelim tamei* as well, so why is the ruling limited to the flesh?

The *gemara* answers: *kula Rabbi Yehuda hi* — the entire passage reflects Rabbi Yehuda's opinion alone. There is no independent *tana kama* here; the text requires the principle of *chasorei mechasra*, meaning the passage as it stands is incomplete and must be supplemented. Reading it fully: the case of *parah she'shat'tah mei chatat* — *besarah tamei* — refers only to *tumah kalah*. The qualification *bameh devarim amurim* applies: this is specifically not *tumah chamura*, since Rabbi Yehuda himself says *batlu b'me'eha* — nullified with respect to *tumah chamura*, but retaining *tumah kalah*. It is all Rabbi Yehuda's own elaboration of his position.

Rav Ashi, however, offers an alternative reading. Rather than reinterpreting *batlu*, he maintains the plain meaning — *le'olam batlu b'mayim legamrei*, it truly is fully nullified. But this case does not resolve the question of Rabbi Yehuda's general position regarding *mashkin tmayim*, because this situation is categorically different: *mishum dehavai lei mashkeh saruach* — once the liquid has been swallowed by the animal and sits within its digestive tract, it is no longer considered a proper *mashkeh* at all. It has become disgusting, *saruach*, and just as a food that has become putrefied beyond the point of being *raui l'achilat kelev* loses its status under various laws, this liquid too loses its *tumah*-status. The *parah* case is therefore a special case, and Rabbi Yehuda's position in ordinary cases of *mashkin tmayim* remains unresolved.

## Yitma Yitma Derashah

### R. Yose Intro

The *gemara* now turns to Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon's position — *l'ochlin tme'in ul'kelim tehorin* — that *mashkin* render *ochlin tamei* but not *kelim*. Rabbah bar bar Chana reports in the name of Reish Lakish that Rabbi Yose arrived at his position by following the *shitat* his teacher, Rabbi Akiva: *Rabbi Yose b'shitat Rabbi Akiva rabo amrah*. This attribution is significant: Rabbi Yose is presenting the underlying method of Rabbi Akiva, though he may not follow it to all of its conclusions. The *limmud* — the textual derivation — is from the doubled form *yitma yitma*.

### Torah Verses Setup

The *gemara* turns to the relevant *pesukim* in *Parshat Shmini* (Vayikra). The first passage discusses what happens when a dead *sheretz* — one of the eight ritually unclean crawling creatures — falls upon various objects. The Torah states: *mikol kli eitz o veged o or o sak v'chol kli asher yeiaseh melachah bahem — bamayim yuva v'tamei ad haerev v'taher*. An object that a dead *sheretz* lands upon becomes *tamei* and requires immersion.

### Clay Vessel Case

The next *pasuk* presents a distinct scenario: *v'chol kli cheres asher yipol meihem el tocho — kol asher b'tocho yitma*. When a dead *sheretz* falls into a *kli cheres* — a clay vessel — everything inside it becomes *tamei*. The mechanism here is distinctive: a *kli cheres* conveys *tumah* through its airspace (*mei'aviro*). One need not have the *sheretz* touch the food directly; even dangling it within the interior of the clay vessel renders everything inside *tamei*. The clay vessel, unlike other vessels, cannot be purified — it must be broken. The food within it, however, must previously have been made susceptible to *tumah* through contact with liquid (*hechsher*); it need not be wet at the moment of contact, but it must have been wet at some prior point after being detached.

### Food vs. Liquid

The *gemara* then reads the next pair of verses: *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel asher yavo alav mayim yitma — v'chol mashkeh asher yeshateh b'chol kli yitma*. Two categories are presented: *ochel* — food upon which *mayim* have come — and *mashkeh* — liquid in a vessel. In each case the word *yitma* appears. The critical observation is that the Torah, which is written without vowel markings, uses *yitma* twice — once for *ochel* and once for *mashkeh*.

### Yitma Vocalization

The word *yitma* as vocalized is passive: "shall become *tamei*." But the same consonantal text can be vocalized as *yetamei*, meaning active: "shall render *tamei*," i.e., shall make something else *tamei*. Rabbi Akiva's method — and Rabbi Yose's adoption of it — exploits this ambiguity. By reading *yitma* as *yetamei*, one derives that both the *ochel* and the *mashkeh* do not merely become *tamei* themselves but also impart *tumah* to other things.

### R. Yose Derashah

The question then becomes: what precisely can the *mashkeh* render *tamei* — other *mashkin*, *ochlin*, or *kelim*? Rabbi Yose's conclusion — derived through the *yitma yitma* reading in the *shitat* Rabbi Akiva — is that *mashkin* can render *ochlin tamei* but cannot render *kelim tamei*. The *pasuk* uses the doubled *yitma* precisely to capture this asymmetry: the active dimension (*yetamei*) is present, but it is bounded. This is Rabbi Yose's *derashah*: *mashkin metame ochlin* but not *kelim*. That is the basis for his split ruling in the *safek* case — *l'ochlin tme'in, l'kelim tehorin*.

## Can Liquid Metame Liquid?

### Same Type Principle

Having established that *mashkin* can render *ochlin tamei*, the *gemara* raises a further question: can *tamei mashkin* render other *mashkin tamei*? The Talmud's answer is no, and the reasoning is articulated in two ways.

### Rav Papa Explanation

Rav Papa states the principle: *lo matzinu tumah osah kayotzei bah* — we have never found a case in which a *tumah* renders something of its own kind *tamei*. An *ochel* does not make another *ochel tamei*; a *mashkeh* does not make another *mashkeh tamei*. Only entities of different categories act upon one another. This principle is striking and far-reaching. It implies that when *tamei* and *tahor* items of the same category become mixed — as in numerous earlier cases involving *terumah teme'ah* falling into *terumah tehorah* — the issue is not that the entire mixture becomes *tamei* through transmission; rather, the individual items retain their own status, and the *halachic* difficulty is simply one of *bitul* — one cannot identify which item is which. The *tamei* items do not actively render the *tahor* ones *tamei*. This reading of Rav Papa's principle, though surprising, appears to be uncontested in the *gemara*.

### Ravina Textual Proof

Ravina adds a textual proof from the very *pesukim* under discussion. If one were to claim that *mashkin* render other *mashkin tamei*, then the *yitma* of the *seifa* — applied to *mashkeh* — would mean: liquid renders liquid *tamei*. But then what would the *yitma* of the *reisha* — applied to *ochel* — mean? It could only mean: food renders liquid *tamei*. The result would be that both the *reisha* and the *seifa* are teaching the same thing — that some substance renders *mashkeh tamei*. If so, the Torah could have collapsed them into a single *pasuk*: whether food or liquid, it renders liquid *tamei*. The fact that the Torah uses *yitma* separately in each clause — once for *ochel* and once for *mashkeh* — demands that they be teaching two different things.

### Two Yitmas Explained

The two *yitmas* therefore teach two distinct *dinim*: the first *yitma*, applied to *ochel*, means that food renders *mashkin tamei* (*yitma de'reisha letamei tumah de'mashkin*); the second *yitma*, applied to *mashkeh*, means that liquid renders *ochlin tamei* (*yitma de'seifa letamei tumah de'ochlin*). Each *yitma* conveys an asymmetric relationship: food acts on liquid, and liquid acts on food. Neither acts on its own kind.

## Liquid & Vessels

### Why Not Vessels?

Having resolved that *mashkin* render *ochlin tamei* but not other *mashkin tamei*, the *gemara* now asks why *mashkin* cannot also render *kelim tamei*. Why should *kelim* be excluded?

### Kal Vachomer

The attempt to include *kelim* proceeds by way of a *kal vachomer*. The original scenario involves a *sheretz* dangled in the airspace of a *kli cheres*: the *kli* becomes *tamei*, and as a result the *mashkin* inside it become *tamei*. These *mashkin* — which became *tamei* through the *kli* — are called *mashkin haba'in machmat kli*. Now, we know that a *kli* itself cannot render another *kli tamei*. If so, these *mashkin*, which derive their *tumah* from the *kli*, certainly should not be able to render a *kli tamei*: *eino din shelo yetamei et hakeilim*.

However, the *gemara* raises a second scenario: what if the *sheretz* does not fall into the airspace of the *kli* but falls directly into the *mashkeh* itself, touching the liquid? In that case, the *mashkin* derive their *tumah* directly from the *sheretz* (*mashkin haba'in machmat sheretz*), not through the *kli*. Should a *kal vachomer* not apply — if *mashkin haba'in machmat kli* cannot render *kelim tamei*, surely *mashkin haba'in machmat sheretz* — which are more severely *tamei*, since they were directly contaminated by an *av hatumah* — can render *kelim tamei*?

### Sheretz Direct Case

The *gemara* thus considers whether the case of a *sheretz* falling directly into water creates a *halachic* difference. The question is pointed: if a *sheretz* is floating in a pool of water and the water touches a *kli*, what is the status of that *kli*? The case where the *sheretz* itself is in the liquid — rather than in the airspace of the vessel — is not explicitly covered by the *pesukim*; one would have to derive it. The *kal vachomer* suggests it should be *metame* the *kli* a fortiori from the case where the *kli* itself is the source.

### Dayo Principle

The *gemara* responds with the principle of *dayo l'va min hadin lihyot kenidon* — it is sufficient for the conclusion of a *kal vachomer* to equal the *din* of its source, and one may not extend it beyond. The Torah's case established that whatever is inside the *kli* — food or liquid — becomes *tamei*. The maximum that the *kal vachomer* may derive in the case of a *sheretz* directly in the water is exactly what was established in the original precedent: the liquid can render *ochlin tamei*. It cannot, by extension of the *kal vachomer*, be made to render *kelim tamei*, because the original case never established *tumah* for *kelim* through this mechanism. The *dayo* principle blocks the inference at that point.

## R. Akiva Derashah

### First Yitma Food

Returning now to Rabbi Akiva's own *derashah* on the *yitma yitma*, the *gemara* examines how he himself reads each of the two instances. The first *yitma*, from *mikol ha'ochel asher ye'achel asher yavo alav mayim yitma*, Rabbi Akiva reads as teaching: the food renders *mashkin tamei* — *yitamei letamei et hamashkin*.

### Second Yitma Liquid

The second *yitma*, from *v'chol mashkeh asher yeshateh b'chol kli yitma*, he reads as teaching: the liquid renders *ochlin tamei* — *yitamei letamei et ha'ochlin*. These are the two directions of transmission established by the doubled language: *ochel* to *mashkeh*, and *mashkeh* to *ochel*.

### Food Cannot Metame Food

One might ask: if *ochel* is *metame mashkin*, why should we not say it is also *metame kelim*? Rabbi Akiva establishes this via *kal vachomer* in the negative. A *mashkeh* renders *ochlin tamei* but does not render *kelim tamei* — this was established above. An *ochel*, which cannot even render another *ochel tamei* (since like cannot make like *tamei*), is certainly not going to render *kelim tamei*: *ochel she'ein metamei ochel — eino din shelo yetamei kli*. The logic cuts in the negative direction: the weaker party (*ochel*, which cannot act on its own kind) certainly cannot exceed what the stronger party (*mashkeh*, which can act on a different category) is permitted to do.

### Mishkin Severity

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: perhaps *ochlin* should be considered more severe than *mashkin* — after all, *ochel* is *metame mashkin*, and perhaps that greater capacity warrants a conclusion that *ochel* should also be *metame kelim*. The *gemara* rejects this: the apparent severity of *ochlin* is not intrinsic to food itself. It is *chumra demashkin hu* — it is a stringency that flows from the nature of *mashkin*.

### Chumra Mishkin

The explanation is as follows: *mashkin* are more susceptible to receiving *tumah* than *ochlin* because *mashkin* can receive *tumah* without any prior *hechsher* — without any preparatory act. A food item must first have liquid come upon it (*hechsher b'mayim*) before it can receive *tumah*; without this preparation, even direct contact with an *av hatumah* leaves the food *tahor*. A *mashkeh*, by contrast, requires no such preparation — by its very nature it is susceptible to *tumah*. This greater receptivity of *mashkin* to *tumah* is what gives them their apparent force, so that when they contact *ochlin*, they render them *tamei*. But this *chumra* belongs to the *mashkeh*, not to the *ochel*. The *ochel*'s ability to render *mashkin tamei* is therefore a reflection of the *mashkeh*'s own susceptibility — *mishum demashkin dekabel tumah shelo behechsher* — not a sign that *ochlin* themselves occupy an elevated position in the hierarchy of *tumah*. Consequently, the *kal vachomer* that would have extended *ochel*'s reach to *kelim* is without foundation.
