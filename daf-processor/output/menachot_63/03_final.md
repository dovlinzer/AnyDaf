# Menachot 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Vessels

### Opening Remarks

Daf 63 of Menachot marks the final daf of this perek — a perek that was among the first to focus directly and systematically on describing the various types of *menachot*, how they are made, and the constituent steps of their preparation. The full step-by-step process of kneading, baking, and so forth has not yet been exhaustively covered, but we are drawing close, and the relevant stages are coming into view one by one.

### Hagasha & Tenufa

The preceding material addressed which *menachot* require *hagasha* and which require *tenufa*. Now the discussion narrows further to focus on the specific types of *mincha* described distinctly in Vayikra. The Torah's baseline assumes a *mincha* that is unbaked — flour mixed with oil: *ki takriv korban mincha la-Hashem, solet yihiyeh korbano, v'yatzak aleha shemen v'natan aleha levonah*. But the Torah then describes several baked variations: *v'chi takriv mincha ma'afeh tanur* — if you bring one baked in an oven; *v'im mincha al ha-machavat korbanecha* — if it is in a flat pan; *v'im mincha marcheshet korbanecha* — if it is in a deep pan. The *mishnah*'s organizing question, then, is: given that a person may take a personal *neder* to bring a *korban*, and the nature of that *neder* determines what must be brought, what are the legal consequences of specifying one vessel type over another?

## Machavat/Marcheshet

### Mishna Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי בְּמַחֲבַת״ – לֹא יָבִיא בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת, ״בְּמַרְחֶשֶׁת״ – לֹא יָבִיא בְּמַחֲבַת. מָה בֵּין מַחֲבַת לְמַרְחֶשֶׁת? מַרְחֶשֶׁת יֵשׁ לָהּ כִּיסּוּי, מַחֲבַת אֵין לָהּ כִּיסּוּי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מַרְחֶשֶׁת עֲמוּקָּה וּמַעֲשֶׂיהָ רוֹחֲשִׁין (רַכִּין), מַחֲבַת צָפָה וּמַעֲשֶׂיהָ קָשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who** takes a vow to bring a meal offering to the Temple and **says: It is** incumbent **upon me** to bring a meal offering prepared **in a *maḥavat*, may not bring** one prepared **in a *marḥeshet*.** Similarly, if he says: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering prepared **in a *marḥeshet*, he may not bring** one prepared **in a *maḥavat*.** The mishna clarifies: **What is** the difference **between a *maḥavat* and a *marḥeshet*? A *marḥeshet* has a cover,** whereas **a *maḥavat* does not have a cover;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says: A *marḥeshet* is deep, and** due to the large amount of oil, **its product** is soft because it **moves about [*roḥashin*]** in the oil. **A *maḥavat*** is flat, as the sides of the pan are **level** with the pan, **and** due to the small amount of oil, **its product is hard.**

The *mishnah* opens: *ha-omer harei alai ba-machavat* — one who declares "behold, I take upon myself to bring a *mincha* in a *machavat*." The ruling is *lo yavi ba-marcheshet* — he may not bring it in the *marcheshet*. Conversely, if he specified a *marcheshet*, he may not bring it in the *machavat*. These are two distinct obligations that cannot be interchanged.

### Vessel Distinction

The *mishnah* then asks: *mah bein machavat le-marcheshet?* — what is the difference between them? The *marcheshet yesh lah kisui* — the *marcheshet* has a cover, *u-machavat ein lah kisui* — while the *machavat* does not. In practical terms, the *machavat* functions like a flat frying pan, and the *marcheshet* more like a saucepan or deep pan. These are the words of Rabbi Yose HaGelili.

Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel adds a complementary distinction: *marcheshet amuka* — the *marcheshet* is deep — *u-ma'aseh rochashin* — and what is prepared in it moves, bubbles, and churns. When flour and oil are cooked in a deeper vessel with more liquid, the result is something softer and more fluid in texture. The *machavat*, by contrast, is flat — *tzafah* — the contents rest on the surface without being immersed in oil, and *ma'aseh kashin* — what emerges is harder and more solid, like something fried rather than simmered.

### Rabbi Yose HaGelili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? אִילֵּימָא מַרְחֶשֶׁת, דְּאָתְיָא אַרִחוּשֵׁי הַלֵּב, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״רָחַשׁ לִבִּי דָּבָר טוֹב״, וּמַחֲבַת, דְּאָתְיָא אַמַּחְבּוֹאֵי הַפֶּה, כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: ״מְנַבַּח נַבּוֹחֵי״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara inquires: As the Torah does not describe the different vessels, **what is the reason for** the interpretation of **Rabbi Yosei** HaGelili, explaining that a *marḥeshet* has a cover and a *maḥavat* does not have a cover? **If we say** that the term ***marḥeshet*** indicates **that** the offering **comes** to atone **for** the sinful **musings [*raḥashei*] of the heart, as it is written: "My heart muses [*raḥash*] on a goodly matter"** (Psalms 45:2), and therefore this meal offering must be prepared in a covered vessel just as the thoughts of the heart are hidden, this interpretation is insufficient. **And** if we say that the term ***maḥavat*** indicates **that** the offering **comes** to atone **for** transgressions committed with the **corners of [*ammaḥavo'ei*] the mouth, as people say** with regard to someone who speaks loudly: He **is barking [*minbaḥ nevuḥei*],** and therefore this meal offering must be prepared in an open vessel, this interpretation is also insufficient.

The *gemara* probes the reasoning behind Rabbi Yose HaGelili's distinction. Why would it matter to a person vowing to bring a *mincha* whether it is made in a covered or uncovered vessel, especially if the baking is done in the *mikdash* by the *kohanim*? The *gemara*'s answer reveals how distant we are from that world: the two vessel types carried distinct symbolic associations that, while no longer intuitively obvious to us, would have been meaningful to those bringing these offerings.

The *gemara* proposes that the word *marcheshet* is connected to *rachush* — the movements or stirrings of the heart. Rashi explains this as *hirhur aveira*, sinful thought. The textual support is the verse *rachash libi davar tov* — "my heart stirred with a good thing" — where *rachash* connotes inner emotional movement. Since the heart is internal and concealed within the body, the vessel that represents it would naturally be a covered one, with contents that move and bubble unseen beneath the lid. On this reading, one who brings a *marcheshet* is atoning for the hidden movements of the heart — for sinful thoughts.

The *machavat*, the open and flat vessel, would then correspond to speech. The word *machavat* is linked to *mach'bu'ei*, meaning utterances or spoken words. As people say, *minvach nivuchei* — he barked or voiced aloud — suggesting speech. Speech is by nature uncovered and externally expressed, just as the *machavat* is open. Rashi associates this with *rechilut* and *lashon ha-ra*, which, while technically speech rather than action, can be far more destructive than many physical transgressions. The *mincha* offered in an open *machavat* would thus symbolize an atonement connected to what one has spoken.

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא אִיפְּכָא: מַחֲבַת, דְּאָתְיָא אַמַּחְבּוֹאֵי הַלֵּב, דִּכְתִיב ״לָמָּה נַחְבֵּאתָ לִבְרֹחַ״; מַרְחֶשֶׁת, דְּאָתְיָא אַרִחוּשֵׁי [הַפֶּה], כִּדְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: ״הֲוָה מְרַחֲשָׁן שִׂיפְוָותֵיהּ״. אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The reason these interpretations are insufficient is that one can also **say the opposite,** and suggest that the name ***maḥavat*** indicates that the offering must be prepared in a closed vessel, **as it comes** to atone **for the secret** musings **of the heart, as it is written** that Laban said to Jacob: **"Why did you flee secretly [*naḥbeita*]"** (Genesis 31:27). Likewise, with regard to ***marḥeshet*,** one can say that it must be prepared in an open vessel, **as it comes** to atone **for whispers [*reḥushei*]** of the mouth which are heard and revealed, **as people say: His lips were whispering [*meraḥashan*].** Therefore, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili cannot derive the meanings of the terms *marḥeshet* and *maḥavat* from the verses; **rather,** his interpretation **is learned as a tradition.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this symbolic association: *eima ipcha* — perhaps it is precisely the reverse. Perhaps *machavat* connects to *mach'vo* — concealment — the *hidden* thoughts of the heart. The supporting verse is Lavan's complaint to Yaakov, *lama nachbeita livro'ach* — "why did you hide your intention to flee?" — where the same root conveys concealment. On this reading, the covered *machavat* represents the hidden inner life of the heart, and the *marcheshet* — whose root *rachash* means to move — refers to the movement of the lips, i.e., speech: *hava merachshan sifvosei*, his lips were moving and muttering.

The symbolic reasoning could plausibly run in either direction. The *gemara* therefore concludes: *ella gemara gemiri lah* — the distinction is not derived through symbolic inference at all, but is received as a tradition. Rabbi Yose HaGelili knew that the *machavat* is uncovered and the *marcheshet* is covered through received knowledge, not through the kind of symbolic analysis the *gemara* had been attempting. What the attempted analysis reveals, however, is precisely how removed we are from this world: associations that once felt natural and obvious to those bringing these offerings are no longer self-evident to us.

It should also be noted that Rabbi Yose HaGelili and Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel need not be seen as arguing. One describes the distinction in terms of covered versus uncovered; the other in terms of deep versus flat. Both characteristics may simultaneously be true of the same vessels.

### Rabbi Chanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר כּוּ׳. מַרְחֶשֶׁת עֲמוּקָּה – דִּכְתִיב ״וְכׇל נַעֲשָׂה בַמַּרְחֶשֶׁת״, מַחֲבַת צָפָה – דִּכְתִיב ״וְעַל מַחֲבַת״.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says** that a *marḥeshet* is deep, whereas a *maḥavat* is flat. The Gemara explains the reason for this opinion: **A *marḥeshet*** is **deep, as it is written** with regard to this meal offering: **"And all that is made in the *marḥeshet*"** (Leviticus 7:9). The use of the term "in" indicates that this meal offering is prepared inside a vessel, i.e., a deep container. Conversely, **a *maḥavat*** is flat, with the sides of the pan **level** with the pan, **as it is written** with regard to this meal offering: **"And on the *maḥavat*"** (Leviticus 7:9). The use of the term "on" indicates that it is prepared on the vessel, not inside it. Therefore, a flat vessel is required.

Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel's distinction — that the *marcheshet* is deep — is supported more directly from the *pesuk* itself. The verse says *ve-chol na'aseh ba-marcheshet* — "all that is made *in* the *marcheshet*" — using the preposition *be-*, indicating genuine interior depth. By contrast, the verse says *ve-al machavat* — "on the *machavat*" — using *al*, "upon," just as one speaks of grilling *on* a grill or frying on a completely flat skillet. The prepositions themselves, *be-* versus *al*, encode the distinction between a vessel with real inner depth and one that is essentially a flat surface.

## Beit Shammai vs Hillel

### Beit Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: הָאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מַרְחֶשֶׁת״ יְהֵא מוּנָּח עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא אֵלִיָּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one vows: It is incumbent upon me to bring a meal offering prepared in a *marḥeshet*, he is obligated to bring a meal offering of that type. With regard to this, **the Sages taught** that **Beit Shammai say:** With regard to **one who says: It is** incumbent **upon me** to bring **a *marḥeshet*,** without using the term: Meal offering, or the preposition: In, in such a case the money for the meal offering **should be placed** in a safe place **until** the prophet **Elijah comes** heralding the Messiah, and clarifies what should be done.

A *baraita* records a dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Beit Shammai says: one who declares *harei alai marcheshet* — "behold, upon me is a *marcheshet*" — the matter *yehei munach ad she-yavo Eliyahu* — should be set aside until Eliyahu comes. This formulation, familiar from monetary disputes, places the matter in permanent escrow when no determination can be made. In the classic monetary case, two people each deposited funds — one two hundred, one one hundred — and the custodian forgot which depositor gave which amount. Since each claimant insists on the larger sum, the custodian returns a hundred to each and the disputed remainder is left *ad she-yavo Eliyahu*. Here too, Beit Shammai rules that the vow is indeterminate and cannot be resolved.

It is worth noting that Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel lived during the time of the *mikdash*, or at least spoke in terms of what the *halacha* would be in that setting, imagining it as fully operative. Without the explicit qualifier *ba-zman ha-zeh*, we do not read their rulings as addressed to a post-*mikdash* reality.

### Ambiguity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַפְּקָא לְהוּ, אִי עַל שׁוּם כְּלִי נִקְרְאוּ, אוֹ עַל שׁוּם מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: Beit Shammai **are uncertain** with regard to the source of the terms *marḥeshet* and *maḥavat*, **whether** the offerings **are called** these names **due to** the specific **vessel** in which each meal offering is prepared, **or** whether they are called these names **due to** the manner of **their preparation.** The significance of this distinction is that if the term *marḥeshet* is referring to a specific type of vessel, then if one takes a vow: It is incumbent upon me to bring a *marḥeshet*, he must bring an actual vessel of that type, whereas if the term is referring to the manner of preparation of the meal offering then he is obligated to bring that type of meal offering. Since Beit Shammai are uncertain which is the correct interpretation, they rule that he must wait until the prophet Elijah comes.

Why is the vow indeterminate? The *gemara* explains: *mesapka lehu ei al shum kli nikre'u o al shum ma'aseihem* — they are uncertain whether the word *marcheshet* is the name of the vessel — a specific type of cooking pan — or whether it functions as a descriptive adjective characterizing a type of *mincha*, i.e., a bubbling or moving one.

The distinction matters practically. If *marcheshet* is the name of a vessel and one declares *harei alai marcheshet*, one is donating the vessel itself to the *beit ha-mikdash*, not undertaking to bring a *korban*. If, however, *marcheshet* is an adjective describing the character of a *mincha*, then *harei alai marcheshet* is understood as a vow to bring a *minchat marcheshet*. Without knowing which usage applies, we cannot determine what the person has obligated himself to bring — or whether he has obligated himself to bring a *korban* at all.

### Tosafot

Tosafot raises a pointed question: why not simply ask the person what he meant? The tension here runs deeper than mere inquiry. In legal contexts, our general principle is *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* — unexpressed intention has no legal standing. This principle applies most strictly to bilateral transactions such as commerce and contracts, where the only accessible reality is what was actually said. But for one-sided transactions — gifts, and especially ritual vows — there is far more room to factor in what the person actually intended. In the domain of *nedarim*, we additionally invoke *piv ve-libo shavin* — the alignment of heart and speech — as a condition for a valid vow, which gives inner intention significant purchase.

Tosafot considers a scenario in which the person has already designated money or flour *le-marcheshet*, leaving it ambiguous whether the funds are destined for the vessel or for the *korban*. He asks: why not instruct the person to purchase a *marcheshet* vessel with separate non-sacred funds, and then declare that if he originally meant the vessel, the *kedushah* transfers to this newly purchased one, and if he meant the *korban*, then the original funds go toward the *mincha* and the new vessel goes to the *beit ha-mikdash*? This covering-of-bases approach is normally what we would demand of someone who created an ambiguous situation.

Tosafot's answer is that the case involves someone who declared that the funds should go *le-marcheshet* in whatever sense the linguists determine the word to mean — *l'mah she-yidreshu chachmei ha-lashon*. The person deliberately abdicated the determination of meaning to an external authority. In that case, the resolution does not depend on what the person meant but on what the word objectively means — and that remains undetermined, hence *yehei munach ad she-yavo Eliyahu*.

This connects to a broader principle running through the Beit Shammai–Beit Hillel debates on *hekdesh*: Beit Shammai generally holds that *hekdesh bi-ta'ut* — a mistaken dedication — remains *hekdesh*, whereas Beit Hillel holds it does not. Beit Shammai thus attends more rigorously to the speech act itself, giving it binding force independent of the speaker's actual intention. Beit Hillel, by contrast, gives more weight to what the speaker genuinely had in mind. The ruling of *yehei munach* is thus consistent with Beit Shammai's characteristic posture: the words were said, we cannot determine their objective meaning, and the speaker's private intent does not resolve the impasse.

### Beit Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: כְּלִי הָיָה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וּ״מַרְחֶשֶׁת״ שְׁמוֹ, וְדוֹמֶה כְּמִין כַּלְבּוֹס עָמוֹק, וּכְשֶׁבָּצֵק מוּנָּח בְּתוֹכוֹ – דּוֹמֶה כְּמִין תַּפּוּחֵי הַבָּרָתִים וּכְמִין בִּלְוָטֵי הַיְּוָונִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Beit Hillel say** that there is no uncertainty about this matter, as **there was** a particular **vessel in the Temple, and its name** was ***marḥeshet*. And** this vessel **resembled a type of deep *kelabus*,** which is a vessel with indentations, **and when dough is placed inside it,** it gets pressed against the indentations and takes their shape. The dough **resembles a type of apple of *berotim*** trees, **or a type of acorn [*balutei*]** of **the Greek** oak trees. Therefore, one who takes a vow: It is incumbent upon me to bring a *marḥeshet*, must bring this type of vessel to the Temple as a donation.

Beit Hillel rules otherwise: *kli hayah ba-mikdash u-marcheshet shemah* — there was a vessel in the *mikdash* and *marcheshet* was its name. For Beit Hillel, the term *marcheshet* unambiguously denotes a specific vessel. Accordingly, one who says *harei alai marcheshet* is straightforwardly donating that vessel to the *beit ha-mikdash*. No uncertainty remains, because the meaning of the word is settled: it refers to the *kli*, not to a type of *mincha*. Beit Hillel does not need to inquire into the speaker's intent, because the language itself is clear.

### Marcheshet Description

Beit Hillel then describes this vessel: *v'domeh ke-min kalvos amuk* — it resembles a deep *kalvos*, a specific type of container. Rashi's description, reflected also in the diagram in the *Shita Mekubetzes*, depicts a vessel with indented interior compartments — something like a muffin pan — though another rendering shows a single central deep space. The common feature is that the vessel has distinct interior recesses. *U-khe-sheha-batzeik nach be-tocho* — when the dough settles into these indentations — *domeh ke-min tapuchei ha-bartin* — it resembles tree growths or knots, and *ke-min blutei ha-yevanim* — like the acorns of the Greek oak. That is, the baked dough would protrude from the vessel's compartments in rounded forms.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְכׇל נַעֲשָׂה בַמַּרְחֶשֶׁת וְעַל מַחֲבַת״, אַלְמָא עַל שׁוּם הַכֵּלִים נִקְרְאוּ, וְלֹא עַל שׁוּם מַעֲשֵׂיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the verse **states** two different prepositions with regard to these vessels: **"And all that is made in the *marḥeshet* and on the *maḥavat*"** (Leviticus 7:9). It does not state simply: And all that is made in the *marḥeshet* and the *maḥavat*. Since it seems from the verse that when using the *marḥeshet* the meal offering is prepared inside the vessel and when using the *maḥavat* it is prepared on the vessel, **evidently they are called** these names **due to** the **vessel** in which the meal offering is prepared, **not due to** the manner of **their preparation.**

The *gemara* concludes by citing the verse *ve-chol ha-na'aseh ba-marcheshet u-va-machavat* — using *be-* for the *marcheshet* (in) and *al* for the *machavat* (on) — confirming that these names belong to the vessels themselves, *al shem ha-keilim nikre'u ve-lo al shem ha-ma'asim*.

## Baked Mincha

### Mincha Be-Tanur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הֲרֵי עָלַי בְּתַנּוּר – לֹא יָבִיא מַאֲפֵה כוּפָּח, וְלֹא מַאֲפֵה רְעָפִים, וּמַאֲפֵה יוֹרוֹת הָעַרְבִיִּים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: רָצָה – מֵבִיא מַאֲפֵה כוּפָּח.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one says: **It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring a meal offering baked **in an oven, he may not bring** a meal offering **baked** on **a small oven [*kupaḥ*], nor a** meal offering **baked** on roofing **tiles, nor** a meal offering **baked** in the baking **pits** of **the Arabs. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he so **wishes,** he may **bring** a meal offering **baked** on **a *kupaḥ*.**

The *mishnah* turns to the *ma'afeh tanur*, the oven-baked *mincha*. If one declares *harei alai ba-tanur* — behold, I undertake to bring the *mincha* made in an oven — the *mishnah* specifies what does and does not fulfill that vow.

### Prohibited Vessels

The *mishnah* rules: *lo yavi ma'aseh kupach* — one may not bring something baked in a *kupach*, a small individual oven. The *kupach* was a compact, teepee-like structure in which fire burned at the base and heat concentrated near the top — an oven for a single person's use, as opposed to the larger communal *kira*. Nor may one bring *ma'aseh re'afim* — something baked on tiles, a flat surface rather than an enclosed oven. Nor *ma'aseh yorot ha-arviyim* — something baked in Arab pots, a method in which the pot itself serves as the baking chamber rather than an oven. Each of these is disqualified because the vow specified a *tanur*, and these alternatives do not constitute a genuine *tanur*.

### Home Preparation

One detail that emerges clearly from the *mishnah*'s framing is that the person is understood to be preparing the *mincha* at home and then bringing it. The formulation *lo yavi* — do not bring — and the specification of various household baking vessels all suggest that the vower is engaged in the baking process personally, outside the *mikdash*, before presenting the finished product. This is a significant assumption that will continue to require attention as the discussion develops.

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מַאֲפֵה תַנּוּר״, וְלֹא מַאֲפֵה כוּפָּח, וְלֹא מַאֲפֵה רְעָפִים, וְלֹא מַאֲפֵה יוֹרוֹת הָעַרְבִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: "And when you bring a meal offering **baked in an oven"** (Leviticus 2:4), this emphasizes that it must be prepared in an oven, **and not baked** on **a *kupaḥ*, nor baked** on roofing **tiles, nor baked** in the baking **pits** of **the Arabs,** in accordance with the opinion of the first *tanna* in the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״תַּנּוּר״ ״תַּנּוּר״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים, לְהַכְשִׁיר מַאֲפֵה כוּפָּח. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״תַּנּוּר״ ״תַּנּוּר״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים, אֶחָד שֶׁתְּהֵא אֲפִיָּיתָן בְּתַנּוּר, וְאֶחָד שֶׁיְּהֵא הֶקְדֵּישָׁן בַּתַּנּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** In this verse it states **"oven,"** and it also states **"oven"** in another verse: "And every meal offering that is baked in the oven" (Leviticus 7:9). Since it is written **two times,** and these terms are restrictions, one follows the hermeneutical principle that a restrictive expression following a restrictive expression serves only to amplify the *halakha* and include additional cases. Consequently, this derivation serves **to render fit** a meal offering **baked** on **a *kupaḥ*,** and it too is deemed an oven. **Rabbi Shimon says:** The terms **"oven"** and **"oven,"** which are written a total of **two times,** serve to teach two *halakhot*: **One** instance teaches **that their baking should be in an oven, and** the other **one** teaches **that their consecration is in an oven,** i.e., meal offerings are not consecrated in service vessels but rather in the oven.

Rabbi Yehuda holds that one who vowed to bring a *minchat ma'afeh* may not bring a mixture of both types, or may bring something baked in a *kupach*. The *korban* must be of a single type or prepared in a full-sized oven. His source: *talmud lomar korban mincha* — the Torah uses the singular *korban mincha*, implying *korban echad amarti lecha ve-lo shnayim u-sheloshah korbanos* — I told you one *korban*, not two or three. Bringing half of one type and half of another would constitute, in effect, partial fulfillment of two separate categories rather than a single cohesive *korban*.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon reads the doubled *tanur* differently. The two mentions do not expand which vessels qualify; rather, they teach two distinct requirements: *echad she-tehei afiyatam ba-tanur* — the baking must take place in an oven — *ve-echad she-tehei hekdesham ba-tanur* — and the *mincha* must also become sanctified in the oven. The *tanur* in the *mikdash* itself functioned as a *kli sharet*, a sacred vessel, and it is through placement in that oven that the *mincha* acquires its *kedushah*. This sets the stage for the *gemara*'s subsequent probing of this position.

## Kedusha Timing

### Yotzei Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַאי סְבָרָא? וְהָתְנַן: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם הֱוֵי רָגִיל לוֹמַר שְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים כְּשֵׁרוֹת בָּעֲזָרָה, וּכְשֵׁרוֹת בְּבֵית פָּאגֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Shimon hold** in accordance with **this** line of **reasoning? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (95b) that **Rabbi Shimon says:** One should **always be accustomed to say** that **the two loaves and the shewbread are valid** if they are kneaded, shaped, or baked **in** the Temple **courtyard, and** that they **are** also **valid** if they are prepared **in** the place called **Beit Pagei,** which is outside the walls of the Temple Mount? As these offerings are not disqualified by being taken outside the Temple, evidently they are not consecrated in the oven.

The *gemara* challenges Rabbi Shimon's position sharply. If the oven sanctifies the *mincha*, then any *mincha* removed from the *mikdash* after baking would be *pasul be-yotzei* — invalidated by having left the Temple precincts in a state of *kedushah*. Yet we know from a later *mishnah* that Rabbi Shimon himself says the *shtei ha-lechem* and *lechem ha-panim* are valid even when prepared in *Beit Pagi* — an area just outside the wall of the *azarah*. If these offerings had already acquired *kedushat ha-guf* in the oven, how could they be brought outside without becoming *pasul*?

### Mincha Kedusha

This question highlights a striking and fundamental difference between a *mincha* and an animal *korban*. When one designates an animal as an *olah* — *harei zu le-olah* — it immediately acquires *kedushat ha-guf*; *me'ilah* applies from that moment, and no *kli sharet* is required to achieve this sanctity. A *mincha*, by contrast, progresses through stages. The initial declaration is more like *kedushat damim*, and full *kedushat ha-guf* requires additional steps — the *kli sharet*, and in many cases further ritual acts — before it reaches its full sanctity.

### Tosafot on Kedusha

Tosafot raises the question in a still sharper form. The *gemara* elsewhere establishes that a *mincha* accompanying an animal offering — such as the *shtei ha-lechem* — does not achieve full *kedushat ha-guf* until the *zerikat ha-dam* of the accompanying animals. This means it can be taken outside the *mikdash* before that point without being rendered *pasul*. But is this a special rule limited to *menachot* that accompany animals, or does it apply to all *menachot*? Tosafot entertains the possibility that even after *kedushat kli* — sanctification through a sacred vessel — a *mincha* that stands alone may still be removed from the *mikdash* without being invalidated. This would distinguish *menachot* fundamentally from animal *korbanot*, where *kedushat ha-guf* renders removal immediately problematic.

### Rava's Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֵימָא, שֶׁיְּהֵא הֶקְדֵּישָׁן לְשׁוּם תַּנּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in response: Rabbi Shimon maintains that the oven does not consecrate meal offerings, and as for his statement in the *baraita* concerning the two derivations, one should **say** that the other derivation from the term "oven" teaches **that their consecration** by the owner **must be** explicit, i.e., from the outset he must say that he is sanctifying his meal offering **for the sake** of a meal offering baked in **an oven.**

To resolve the difficulty, Rava offers a radical reinterpretation of Rabbi Shimon's ruling. *Eima she-yehei hekdesham le-shum tanur* — what Rabbi Shimon means is not that the oven itself confers *kedushah*, but that at the time of sanctification, one must explicitly designate the *mincha* as a *minchat ma'afeh tanur* — specifying the type of *mincha* it is to become. The requirement is not about the oven as a sanctifying instrument, but about the precision of the owner's declaration at the moment of dedication.

This is admittedly a significant re-reading of the plain meaning. But bracketing the hermeneutic move, what the discussion illuminates is that the *kedushah* of a *mincha* does not crystallize at the moment of baking, even when baked in the *mikdash's* own *kli sharet*. The *mincha* retains a degree of legal fluidity well past the point of its physical preparation — which, as noted, further supports the possibility that baking and preparation may take place outside the *mikdash* entirely. The level of sanctity that would lock the *mincha* within the Temple precincts comes later in the process, not at the point of cooking.

## Chalos/Rekikin Mix

### Vow Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחַת מַאֲפֶה – לֹא יָבִיא מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין; רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַתִּיר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** If one says: **It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a baked meal offering,** without specifying loaves or wafers, **he may not bring half** the required offering in the form of **loaves and** the other **half** in the form of **wafers;** rather, they must all be of one form or the other. **Rabbi Shimon deems** this **permitted, due to** the fact **that** both loaves and wafers are written with regard to this meal offering, which indicates that **it is one offering** of two possible forms.

The *mishnah* addresses the case of one who vows *harei alai minchat ma'afeh* — to bring a baked *mincha*. The Torah describes this offering in Vayikra: *solet chalos matzot belulot ba-shemen u-rekikei matzot meshuchim ba-shemen* — fine flour loaves unleavened mixed with oil, and wafers unleavened anointed with oil. The question is whether one who vowed to bring a *minchat ma'afeh* may bring a mixture of both types, or must choose one.

### Rabbi Yehuda

The Gemara now examines these positions in detail.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְכִי תַּקְרִיב״ – כְּשֶׁתַּקְרִיב, לַעֲשׂוֹת דְּבַר רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And when you bring a meal offering baked in an oven" (Leviticus 2:4). The phrase: **"And when you bring,"** indicates that this offering is not obligatory. Rather, **when you** wish you may **bring,** i.e., the verse teaches how **to perform** the meal offering baked in an oven as **a voluntary matter.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קׇרְבַּן מִנְחָה״ – אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מִנַּיִן לְאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחַת מַאֲפֶה״, שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״קׇרְבָּן מִנְחָה״ – קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד אָמַרְתִּי לָךְ, וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה קׇרְבָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the term: **"A meal offering," Rabbi Yehuda says: From where** is it derived with regard **to one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a baked meal offering, that he must not bring half** of the bread in the form of **loaves and half** in the form of **wafers? The verse states: "A meal offering,"** which indicates: **I told you** to bring **one offering,** i.e., all ten loaves from one type, **and not two or three offerings** of different types, as allowed by Rabbi Shimon.

Rabbi Yehuda holds that one who vowed to bring a *minchat ma'afeh* may not bring half *chalos* and half *rekikin*. The *korban* must be of a single type. His source: *talmud lomar korban mincha* — the Torah uses the singular *korban mincha*, implying *korban echad amarti lecha ve-lo shnayim u-sheloshah korbanos* — I told you one *korban*, not two or three. Bringing half of one type and half of another would constitute, in effect, partial fulfillment of two separate categories rather than a single cohesive *korban*.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי נֶאֱמַר ״קׇרְבָּן״ ״קׇרְבָּן״ שְׁנֵי פְּעָמִים? וַהֲלֹא לֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא קׇרְבָּן אֶחָד, וְנֶאֱמַר בּוֹ חַלּוֹת וּרְקִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon said to** Rabbi Yehuda: **And is it stated** with regard to a meal offering baked in an oven: **"Offering,"** and again: **Offering,** for a total of **two times,** once in connection to a meal offering of loaves and once in connection to a meal offering of wafers? If that were the case, it would indicate that these are two types of offerings. But **doesn't it** actually **say "offering" only one** time: "And when you bring a meal offering baked in an oven, it shall be unleavened loaves of fine flour mixed with oil, or unleavened wafers spread with oil" (Leviticus 2:4)? **And it is stated with regard to this** meal offering both **loaves and wafers,** which indicates that these are two varieties of the same offering.

Rabbi Shimon permits bringing half *chalos* and half *rekikin*, arguing that both fall under a single *korban*. His textual argument is elegant: the word *korban* appears only once in the relevant verse, covering both *chalos* and *rekikin* together. Had the Torah intended two distinct *korbanot*, it would have repeated the word *korban* — one for each type. Since it uses *korban* once and then specifies both variations under that single header, the two forms are not separate *korbanot* but rather two options within a single category. One may therefore choose all loaves, all wafers, or a mixture of both.

### Mixing Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵעַתָּה, רָצָה לְהָבִיא חַלּוֹת – מֵבִיא, רְקִיקִין – מֵבִיא, מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין – מֵבִיא, וּבוֹלְלָן וְקוֹמֵץ מִשְּׁנֵיהֶם, וְאִם קָמַץ וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ מֵאֶחָד עַל שְׁנֵיהֶם – יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon continues: **From now** it may be inferred that if one **wants to bring** ten **loaves he may bring** ten loaves, and if he prefers to bring ten **wafers, he may bring** ten wafers, and if he decides that **half** of them should be **loaves and half** of them **wafers, he may bring** it in this manner. **And** if he brings part as loaves and part as wafers, how does he proceed? He **mingles** all of **them and removes a handful from both of them. And** if **he removed a handful and** it happened that only part **of one** type, either loaves or wafers, **came up in his hand for both of them,** he has **fulfilled** his obligation, as they are both part of a single offering.

A practical question arises: if bringing both types, how is the *kemitzah* performed? The *mincha* must have a *kometz* — a handful — taken from it. Rabbi Shimon's answer is that one bakes both the *chalos* and the *rekikin*, then breaks them into pieces, mixes the pieces together — *ubolelan* — and takes the *kometz* from the mixture — *v'komaitz mi-sheneihem* — so that the handful represents both types. Moreover, *im kamatz ve-alah be-yado me-echad al sheneihem* — if the handful happened to contain only pieces of one type, one is nonetheless *yotzei*. Since the two types are fundamentally one *korban*, the *kemitzah* of any portion of it is representative of the whole.

## Rabbi Yose's Drasha

### Different Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי מִנְחַת מַאֲפֶה״, שֶׁלֹּא יָבִיא מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה אֲשֶׁר תֵּאָפֶה בַּתַּנּוּר״, ״וְכׇל נַעֲשָׂה בַמַּרְחֶשֶׁת וְעַל מַחֲבַת לַכֹּהֵן הַמַּקְרִיב אֹתָהּ לוֹ תִהְיֶה״, ״וְכׇל מִנְחָה בְלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן וַחֲרֵבָה לְכׇל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda says: From where** is it derived with regard **to one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a baked meal offering, that he may not bring half** of the offering as **loaves and half** as **wafers?** He answers: **The verse states: "And every meal offering that is baked in an oven, and every one that is made in the deep pan, and on the shallow pan, shall belong to the priest who sacrifices it. And every meal offering, mixed with oil, or dry, shall belong to all the sons of Aaron,** one as well as another" (Leviticus 7:9–10).

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda reaches the same practical conclusion as his father — that one may not bring half *chalos* and half *rekikin* — but derives it from a different verse. Rather than citing the verse about the *minchat ma'afeh tanur* itself, he turns to a passage in *Tzav*.

### Kol Mincha Drasha

The verse in *Tzav* reads: *ve-chol mincha asher te'afeh ba-tanur ve-chol na'aseh ba-marcheshet ve-al machavas* — listing the three baked types of *mincha* — *le-chohen ha-makriv otah lo tihyeh*. Then the verse continues: *ve-chol mincha belulah ba-shemen va-charevah* — and every *mincha* mixed with oil, and every dry one — *le-chol bnei Aharon tihyeh*. The two unbaked categories are *belulah ba-shemen* — the standard flour offering — and *charevah* — the dry one, used for the *minchat chote* or *sotah*, which contains no oil. These are completely distinct from one another in character, purpose, and legal status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה ״וְכׇל״ הָאָמוּר לְמַטָּה שְׁנֵי מִינִין חֲלוּקִים, אַף ״וְכׇל״ הָאָמוּר לְמַעְלָה שְׁנֵי מִינִין חֲלוּקִים.
>
> **Translation:** The verses juxtapose the meal offering baked in an oven to the meal offering prepared on the pan and the meal offering prepared in the deep pan, and similarly to the meal offering brought as a gift, alluded to by the phrase: "Mixed with oil," and to the meal offering of a sinner, which is called: "Dry." This teaches that **just as** the term: **"And every"** (Leviticus 7:10), **stated below** with regard to those meal offerings, is referring to **two different types** of meal offering, **so too,** the term: **"And every"** (Leviticus 7:9), **stated above,** with regard to the two forms of meal offering baked in an oven, is referring to **two different types** of meal offering, and therefore one may not bring part as loaves and part as wafers.

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda reasons: *mah ve-chol ha-amor le-mata shnei minin chalukin, af ve-chol ha-amor le-mala shnei minin chalukin*. Just as the *ve-chol* mentioned below — covering the two unbaked *menachot* — refers to two wholly separate categories, so too the *ve-chol* mentioned above — *ve-chol mincha asher te'afeh ba-tanur* — implies that the *ma'afeh tanur* encompasses two wholly separate categories: *chalos* and *rekikin*. And since they are categorically separate, they cannot be combined.

### Shemen Shemen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר לָךְ: כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב ״בַּשֶּׁמֶן״ ״בַּשֶּׁמֶן״, כְּמַאן דִּכְתִיב ״קׇרְבָּן״ ״קׇרְבָּן״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that loaves and wafers are two different types of meal offerings baked in an oven, respond to Rabbi Shimon's proof? After all, **Rabbi Shimon is saying well** when he points out that the verse mentions "offering" only once. The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yehuda could have **said to you: Since it is written: "With oil,"** and: **"With oil,"** in the verse: "It shall be unleavened loaves of fine flour mixed with oil, or unleavened wafers spread with oil" (Leviticus 2:4), it is considered **as though it is written "offering"** and **"offering,"** and therefore these are deemed two different types of meal offering.

The *gemara* now returns to evaluate Rabbi Yehuda's position in light of Rabbi Shimon's response. The key is the verse's repetition of the word *shemen*: *chalos matzot belulot ba-shemen u-rekikei matzot meshuchim ba-shemen*. The word *shemen* appears twice. The *gemara* argues that this double mention functions *ke-man de-khtiv korban korban* — as if the Torah had written *korban* twice — marking the two sub-types as genuinely distinct options. This is what prevents Rabbi Shimon from reading *chalos* and *rekikin* as interchangeable components of a single undifferentiated offering.

### Rabbi Shimon Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, אִי לָא כְּתִיב ״בְּשֶׁמֶן״ ״בְּשֶׁמֶן״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא דַּוְוקָא מֶחֱצָה חַלּוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה רְקִיקִין, אֲבָל חַלּוֹת לְחוֹדַיְיהוּ וּרְקִיקִין לְחוֹדַיְיהוּ אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rabbi Shimon** respond to this claim? The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon maintains that the repetition of the term "with oil" teaches a different *halakha*. **If it were not written "with oil,"** and again **"with oil," I would say** that a meal offering baked in an oven must be **specifically** brought **half** as **loaves and half** as **wafers, and** if he wanted to bring only **loaves alone or wafers alone,** I would **say** that he may **not** bring a meal offering in this manner. The repetition of the term "with oil" **teaches us** that a meal offering baked in an oven can comprise ten loaves, or ten wafers, or a combination of both types.

Rabbi Shimon, however, accounts for the doubled *shemen* differently. Had the Torah not written *ba-shemen* twice — *ee lo ketiv ba-shemen ba-shemen* — Rabbi Shimon says he would have thought the *only* valid option was half and half: *davka mechtza chalos u-mechtza rekikin*, which one might argue is actually the plain reading of the verse. *Aval chalos le-chudaihu ve-rekikin le-chudaihu — eima lo*: one might have thought that all-loaves or all-wafers alone would not fulfill the vow. The double *shemen* comes to teach that one may bring entirely one type or entirely the other. But the single *korban* header still permits bringing of both — so the full range of options remains: all *chalos*, all *rekikin*, or a mixture of both.

### B'Diavad Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַיְינוּ אֲבוּהּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאִי עֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further inquires: The *baraita* states that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, holds that loaves and wafers are two different types of meal offerings. The opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, is** the same as that of **his father,** Rabbi Yehuda, whose opinion is also mentioned in the *baraita*. Why is it necessary to cite Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda's opinion separately? The Gemara explains: It is necessary to cite the opinion of Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda, because there is a practical difference **between** his ruling and that of his father; **as, if** someone transgressed and **performed** the sacrifice of a meal offering baked in an oven by bringing a mixture of loaves and wafers, according to Rabbi Yehuda the offering is valid after the fact, whereas Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda deems it not valid even after the fact.

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda and his father Rabbi Yehuda arrive at the same *lechatchilah* ruling — do not bring half and half — but differ in a crucial *be-diavad* implication. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the prohibition on mixing is a formal requirement, but if one nevertheless brought half *chalos* and half *rekikin*, one would still be *yotzei*: the mixture still constitutes a valid *minchat ma'afeh tanur*, just not the preferred way to bring it. For Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, however, since he analogizes *chalos* and *rekikin* to the completely distinct unbaked categories — which are as different from each other as, say, a *minchat machavat* is from a *minchat marcheshet* — the categories are genuinely separate. Bringing a mixture would be like bringing part of one type of *mincha* for another, and one would not be *yotzei* even *be-diavad*.

With this, the *gemara* closes the perek:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** 

This was a perek devoted to the practical reality of what a *mincha* is.

## Perek Rabbi Ishmael

### Omer Introduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: עוֹמֶר הָיָה בָּא בַּשַּׁבָּת מִשָּׁלֹשׁ סְאִין, וּבַחוֹל מֵחָמֵשׁ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד שַׁבָּת וְאֶחָד חוֹל מִשָּׁלֹשׁ הָיָה בָּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yishmael says:** When the day of the sacrifice of the ***omer*** meal offering **would** occur **on Shabbat,** the labors performed that would otherwise be prohibited were kept to a minimum, and the one-tenth of an ephah of flour that was **brought** as an offering was processed **from three *se'a*** of reaped barley. **And** if it occurred **during the week,** the flour was processed **from five** *se'a* of reaped barley. **And the Rabbis say: Both on Shabbat and** during the **week,** the *omer* offering **would come from three** *se'a* of reaped barley.

The new perek begins with the words *Rabbi Yishmael omer* — and appropriately so, as the entire perek is devoted to the *omer*, the sheaf offering brought on the sixteenth of Nisan, together with the laws of *sefirat ha-omer* that follow from it.

### Starting Amounts

The *mishnah* opens with a dispute about how much grain one must begin with in order to produce the required *issaron* — one tenth of an *eifah* of fine flour — for the *omer*. Rabbi Ishmael rules: if the sixteenth of Nisan falls on *Shabbat*, one begins with three *se'im* of grain; on a weekday, one begins with five *se'im*. The *chachamim* disagree and hold there is no distinction: whether *Shabbat* or weekday, the *omer* was always produced from three *se'im*. On their view, three *se'im* is both sufficient and the consistent standard.

## Chananya's Cutting Rules

### Shabbat Procedure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא סְגַן הַכֹּהֲנִים אוֹמֵר: בַּשַּׁבָּת הָיָה נִקְצָר בְּיָחִיד, וּבְמַגָּל אֶחָד, וּבְקוּפָּה אַחַת, וּבַחוֹל – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, בְּשָׁלֹשׁ קוּפּוֹת, וְשָׁלֹשׁ מַגָּלוֹת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד שַׁבָּת וְאֶחָד חוֹל – בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, בְּשָׁלֹשׁ קוּפּוֹת, וּבְשָׁלֹשׁ מַגָּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina, the deputy High Priest, says: On Shabbat** the barley **was reaped by an individual and with one sickle and with one basket** into which the barley was placed; **and during the week,** it was reaped **by three** people **with three baskets and three sickles. And the Rabbis say: Both on Shabbat and** during the **week,** it was reaped **by three** people **with three baskets and with three sickles.**

Rabbi Chananya Segan HaKohanim describes how the *omer* grain was cut, and his account distinguishes sharply between *Shabbat* and weekday. On *Shabbat*, *be-yachid u-ve-magal echad u-ve-kupah achat* — a single person was sent out, using one scythe and one basket. The procedure was deliberately minimized and conducted without public display.

### Weekday Procedure

On a weekday, the cutting was done *be-sheloshah u-vi-shalosh kupot u-vi-shalosh magalot* — by three people, with three scythes and three baskets. The procedure was carried out openly and with conspicuous communal participation.

### Chachamim

The *chachamim* disagree with Rabbi Chananya's differentiation. In their view, the procedure was the same on *Shabbat* and weekday alike: three people, three scythes, three baskets, regardless of when the sixteenth fell.

### Sadducee Refutation

An important motivating factor underlies this dispute. The cutting of the *omer* overrides *Shabbat* — if the sixteenth falls on *Shabbat*, the grain must still be cut that night. This position was contested by the Sadducees, who disputed both when the *omer* was to be brought and whether it could override *Shabbat*. The public, conspicuous procedure — three people, three scythes, three baskets — served *le-hotzi mi-liban shel Tzadukim*, to refute the Sadducee position and demonstrate that the *omer* indeed overrides *Shabbat*, openly and without apology. Rabbi Chananya Segan HaKohanim's differentiation suggests a different balance: minimize the incursion into *Shabbat* on *Shabbat* itself, while maximizing public display on a weekday. As *Segan HaKohanim* — deputy to the *Kohen Gadol* — Rabbi Chananya was presumably speaking from direct experience of the Temple's procedures.

## Grain Amount Analysis

### Isaron Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבָּנַן קָא סָבְרִי: עִשָּׂרוֹן מוּבְחָר (בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה) [מִשָּׁלֹשׁ] סְאִין אָתֵי, וְלָא שְׁנָא בְּחוֹל וְלָא שְׁנָא בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael and the Rabbis disagree in the mishna with regard to how many *se'a* of barley were reaped for the *omer* meal offering on Shabbat. According to Rabbi Yishmael three *se'a* were reaped when the offering was brought on a Shabbat, and five *se'a* were reaped when the offering was brought on a weekday. The Rabbis maintain that both during the week and on Shabbat, three *se'a* were reaped. The Gemara asks: **Granted** the opinion of **the Rabbis** is clear, as they maintain that **a select tenth** of an ephah of flour **comes from three *se'a*** of reaped barley, **and** therefore **there is no difference** whether the barley is reaped **during the week or** whether it is reaped **on Shabbat,** as a select tenth is required.

The *gemara* questions Rabbi Ishmael's position directly. If on a weekday one uses five *se'im* and on *Shabbat* only three, the implication is that the quality or ease of producing the *issaron* differs based on starting quantity. But what exactly does Rabbi Ishmael hold? If a choice *issaron* can only be obtained from five *se'im*, then *Shabbat* should pose no obstacle — the *omer* overrides *Shabbat*, so use five *se'im* even then. And if three *se'im* suffices for a choice *issaron*, then why use five on a weekday?

### Rava's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: קָסָבַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, עִשָּׂרוֹן מוּבְחָר בְּלָא טִירְחָא אָתֵי מֵחָמֵשׁ, בְּטִירְחָא אָתֵי מִשָּׁלֹשׁ. בַּחוֹל מַיְיתִינַן מֵחָמֵשׁ, דְּהָכִי שְׁבִיחָא מִילְּתָא. בְּשַׁבָּת – מוּטָב שֶׁיַּרְבֶּה בִּמְלָאכָה אַחַת בְּהַרְקָדָה, וְאַל יַרְבֶּה בִּמְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Yishmael holds** that **a select tenth** of an ephah of flour can come **without exertion from five** *se'a*, and **with exertion from three.** Therefore, **on a weekday we** reap and **bring** flour **from five** *se'a*, **as this** produces **a better** final **product,** since only the highest-quality flour of each *se'a* is selected. **On Shabbat,** it is **preferable that one** should **increase** the effort involved **in one prohibited labor,** that **of sifting** the flour numerous times, **and one** should **not increase** the number of prohibited acts involved in performing **many prohibited labors,** such as reaping and winnowing, which are required for the processing of five *se'a*.

Rava resolves this: Rabbi Ishmael holds that a choice *issaron* can indeed be obtained from three *se'im*, but it requires significantly more labor. From five *se'im*, the same quality is achieved more easily — *belo tircha ati me-chamesh, be-tircha ati mi-shalosh*. The reason is straightforward: when starting with a larger quantity, one can pass the grain through a coarser sieve and still obtain a sufficient yield of fine flour from the first pass, without repeated sifting. With only three *se'im*, achieving the same fineness demands more intensive sifting effort. 

### Shabbat Minimization

On a weekday, one uses five *se'im* for two reasons: it is more praiseworthy — one can say that five full *se'im* were processed to yield this single *issaron* — and it requires less effort, making the process smoother. On *Shabbat*, by contrast, one uses only three *se'im* — despite the extra labor involved — because the guiding principle is to minimize *Shabbat* violation.

### One vs. Many Melachot

The specific form of minimization is instructive. *Mutav she-yarbeh be-melacha achat be-harkadah ve-al yarbeh be-melachot harbeh* — it is better to perform one *melacha* many times over than to perform many distinct *melachot*. Performing repeated acts of a single category of labor — even many times — is preferable to involving multiple different categories of labor. While the *halachic* difference may not be fully expressed in terms of formal violations, there is an experiential and perhaps deeper sense in which performing many *melachot* of different kinds constitutes a greater rupture of *Shabbat* than repeating a single *melacha* many times. This principle has resonance in other *Shabbat* contexts as well — for instance, the rules governing how multiple unintentional violations are counted and what *korbanot* they require.

### Five Melachot

Rashi specifies the *melachot* involved in producing flour from grain: *she-yiktzor* — cutting; *ve-yizreh* — winnowing; *ve-yivror* — sorting (*borer*); *ve-yitchan* — grinding; *ve-yirkod* — sifting. These are five distinct *melachot*. Each additional *se'ah* of grain that must be harvested and processed requires engaging all five. By contrast, additional rounds of sifting alone — which is what the smaller quantity requires — involves only the single *melacha* of *harkadah* (sifting), performed repeatedly. On *Shabbat*, therefore, the calculus favors fewer categories of *melacha*, even at the cost of greater physical effort within that single category.

## Minimizing Violation

### Same Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד, דְּתַנְיָא: אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת – מַפְשִׁיט אֶת הַפֶּסַח עַד הֶחָזֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עַד שֶׁיַּפְשִׁיט אֶת כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba said: Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, said the same thing. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **the fourteenth** of Nisan **occurs on Shabbat,** when the Paschal offering is sacrificed but not roasted until Shabbat ends, **one flays the Paschal offering up to the breast,** to enable removal of the parts of the animal that are sacrificed upon the altar on Shabbat, and flays the rest of the animal after Shabbat. Further skinning is only to facilitate eating the animal and therefore it does not override Shabbat. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka. And the Rabbis say:** One has not performed the obligation properly **unless he flays it in its entirety.**

Rava observes that Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka expressed essentially the same principle. Both are concerned with limiting *Shabbat* violation — or at least the scope of incursion into *Shabbat* — even in contexts where such violation is halachically permitted.

### Effort or Deeper

The question is whether their shared concern is merely about effort and convenience, or whether it reflects something more substantive — a principled view that even permitted *Shabbat* violations should be kept as narrow and contained as possible, not just for practical reasons but as an expression of the sanctity of *Shabbat* itself.

### Korban Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי לָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הָתָם, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאֶפְשָׁר לָא טָרְחִינַן? הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאֶפְשָׁר – לָא טָרְחִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains why the statements of Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, are the same. **Didn't Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, say there** that **anywhere that it is possible** to perform the necessary task without an additional action, **we do not exert** ourselves on Shabbat? **Here, too, since it is possible** to perform the necessary task without the extra flaying, **we do not exert** ourselves.

The case of Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka illustrates this. A *baraita* teaches: when the fourteenth of Nisan falls on *Shabbat* and the *korban Pesach* must be brought, the animal is flayed — *mapshit es ha-Pesach ad ha-chazeh* — up to the chest. The chest area is the anatomical threshold: once that point is reached, the belly is sufficiently exposed to remove the *eimurim*, which must be burned on the altar even on *Shabbat*. Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka rules that one flays only to that point — no further. The *chachamim* disagree and hold that one flays the entire animal.

The logic of Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka is that once the religious purpose has been achieved — the *eimurim* can be removed — there is no warrant for continuing to flay. Additional flaying serves no sacred function and represents an unnecessary extension of *Shabbat* violation. *Kol heicha de-efshar lo tarchinan* — wherever possible, we do not exert effort beyond what is required.

### Religious Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הָכָא, אֶלָּא דְּלֵיכָּא בִּזְיוֹן קָדָשִׁים, אֲבָל הָתָם דְּאִיכָּא בִּזְיוֹן קֳדָשִׁים,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison: **From where** is this conclusion reached? **Perhaps Rabbi Yishmael states** his ruling **only here,** in the case of reaping three *se'a* on Shabbat, **where there is no degradation of consecrated** items. **But there, where there is degradation of consecrated** items, as the animal will be left half-flayed overnight,

The *gemara* raises a subtle challenge, however, to the equation of Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka. Perhaps their reasoning differs in a critical way. Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka may limit the flaying not merely because it involves extra *melacha*, but specifically because of *bizayon kodshim* — the degradation of a sacred object. A partially flayed animal, left in that state, does not present a respectful appearance for a *korban*. This specific concern — that the sanctified object should not be treated disrespectfully — may be what limits him, not a general principle of minimizing *Shabbat* incursion. In a case without *bizayon kodshim*, he might hold like the *chachamim* and permit doing the full task.

### Omer Warranted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא לָךְ כְּרַבָּנַן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **I** could **say to you** that **he holds** in accordance with the opinion **of the Rabbis** that the entire animal should be flayed.

Conversely, Rabbi Ishmael's ruling about the *omer* may not be fully analogous to Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka either. When Rabbi Ishmael says to use three *se'im* rather than five on *Shabbat*, the two extra *se'im* are not merely gratuitous — they genuinely improve the process and make it easier to achieve a fine *issaron*. There is a legitimate religious benefit to using more grain. Perhaps in such a case, where the extra effort actually serves the *mitzvah* itself, Rabbi Shimon b'R. Yochanan ben Beroka would agree with the *chachamim* — permitting or even mandating the fuller procedure — because the additional *melacha* is genuinely warranted for the *mitzvah*. The *gemara* leaves open whether the two positions truly cohere or whether the relevant circumstances differ enough to keep them distinct.
