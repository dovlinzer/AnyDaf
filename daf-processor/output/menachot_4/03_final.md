# Menachot 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelo Lishmo Invalidation

### Mincha Shelo Lishmo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מִמִּנְחַת חוֹטֵא וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת. בִּשְׁלָמָא מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא – ״חַטָּאת״ קַרְיַיהּ רַחֲמָנָא, ״לֹא יָשִׂים עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא יִתֵּן עָלֶיהָ לְבוֹנָה כִּי חַטָּאת הִיא וְגוֹ׳״, אֶלָּא מִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **except for the meal offering of a sinner and the meal offering of jealousy.** The Gemara asks: **Granted, the meal offering of a sinner** is disqualified when a handful is removed from it not for its own sake, as **the Merciful One calls it a sin offering,** in the verse: **"He shall put no oil upon it, neither shall he put any frankincense upon it, for it is a sin offering.** And he shall bring it to the priest, and the priest shall take his handful" (Leviticus 5:11–12). This verse indicates that just as a sin offering is disqualified when sacrificed not for its own sake, so too, the meal offering of a sinner is disqualified when a handful is removed from it not for its own sake. **But** with regard to **the meal offering of jealousy, from where do we** derive that this is the *halakha*?

The *mishna* establishes that *shelo lishmo* operates by *mincha* the same way it does by animal sacrifices: by most sacrifices it renders the offering *kasher* but the owners do not receive credit. However, by two specific *minchot* — the *minchat choteh*, the *mincha* standing in place of a *chatat* for a sinner, and the *minchat kenaot*, the *mincha* brought for a *sotah* — the law follows the *chatat*, and a *shelo lishmo* intent renders the entire offering invalid, not merely insufficient for the owner's obligation.

### Minchat Choteh Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: מִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת מוֹתָרָהּ נְדָבָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמְרַתְּ, ״מַזְכֶּרֶת עָוֹן״ כְּתִיב בָּהּ, וּבְחַטָּאת כְּתִיב: ״וְאֹתָהּ נָתַן לָכֶם לָשֵׂאת אֶת עֲוֹן הָעֵדָה״, מָה חַטָּאת מוֹתָרָהּ נְדָבָה – אַף מִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת מוֹתָרָהּ נְדָבָה. וְכַחַטָּאת, מָה חַטָּאת פְּסוּלָה שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ – אַף מִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת פְּסוּלָה שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this *halakha* may be derived from a *baraita*, **as a *tanna* taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman:** With regard to money that was designated for **a meal offering of jealousy, its surplus,** i.e., the money remaining after the purchase of the meal offering, is used to purchase communal **gift** offerings. Rav Naḥman **said to him: You are saying well,** as **it is written with regard to** a meal offering of jealousy: **"Bringing iniquity to remembrance"** (Numbers 5:15), **and it is written with regard to a sin offering: "And He has given it you to bear the iniquity of the congregation"** (Leviticus 10:17). A verbal analogy is drawn between the two uses of the term "iniquity" in these verses. This teaches that **just as** in the case of **a sin offering, its surplus** is used to purchase communal **gift** offerings, **so too,** with regard to **a meal offering of jealousy, its surplus** is used to purchase communal **gift** offerings. **And** a meal offering of jealousy is also **like a sin offering** in another aspect: **Just as a sin offering is disqualified** when sacrificed **not for its own sake, so too, a meal offering of jealousy is disqualified** when a handful is removed from it **not for its own sake.**

The *gemara* addresses the two cases in turn. Regarding the *minchat choteh*, the derivation is straightforward: the *Torah* itself calls it a *chatat*. The *pasuk* reads, *lo yasim aleha shemen velo yiten aleha levona ki chatat hi* — "he shall not place oil upon it nor put frankincense upon it, for it is a *chatat*." Since it is called a *chatat* and also functions as a substitute for a *chatat* in those offerings whose form varies according to what the owner can afford, the rule that *shelo lishmo* fully invalidates a *chatat* applies here as well.

### Gezeirah Shavah Avon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אָשָׁם יְהֵא פָּסוּל שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמוֹ, דְּגָמַר ״עָוֹן״ ״עָוֹן״ מֵחַטָּאת. דָּנִין ״עָוֹן״ מֵ״עָוֹן״, וְאֵין דָּנִין ״עֲוֹנוֹ״ מֵ״עָוֹן״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that the *halakha* of a meal offering of jealousy is derived from a verbal analogy to a sin offering based on the word "iniquity," then **a guilt offering should** also **be disqualified** if it was sacrificed **not for its own sake, as** a similar verbal analogy may be **derived from** the verse that states: "The **iniquity [*avon*]** of the congregation" (Leviticus 10:17), with regard to **a sin offering,** and the verse that states: "And shall bear his **iniquity"** (Leviticus 5:17), in connection with a guilt offering. The Gemara responds: **One derives** a verbal analogy based on the word **"iniquity" from** a verse that likewise uses the term **"iniquity," but one does not derive** a verbal analogy based on the term **"his iniquity [*avono*]" from** a verse that uses the term **"iniquity."**

The *gemara* then asks: where do we know this *halacha* for a *minchat kenaot*? A *tanna* taught in the presence of Rav Nachman that if money is left over after designating funds for a *minchat kenaot*, the surplus goes for a *nedava* — a free-will *olah* offering, contributed to one of the collection boxes in the *Beis Hamikdash* from which communal *olos* were funded on slow days.

Rav Nachman responded: *shappir kamarta* — that teaching makes sense. By the *mincha* of the *sotah*, the *Torah* uses the word *mazkeret avon* — it causes sin to be remembered. And by the standard animal *chatat*, it says *v'osah nasan lachem laseis es avon ha-edah* — it was given to carry the sin of the congregation. The shared word *avon* links the two. Since extra money designated for a *chatat* goes toward an *olah* — derived from the *pasuk kessef asham ve-kessef chatat la-kohanim yihiyu* — the *gezeirah shavah* of *avon-avon* teaches that the same rule applies to a *minchat kenaot*: *mah chatat mosara nidava, af minchat kenaot mosara nidava*. And once that link is established, it also carries the *halacha* that *shelo lishmo* invalidates: *mah chatat pesula shelo lishmo, af minchat kenaot pesula shelo lishmo*.

### Asham Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? וְהָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְשָׁב הַכֹּהֵן״ ״וּבָא הַכֹּהֵן״ – זוֹ הִיא שִׁיבָה זוֹ הִיא בִּיאָה. וְעוֹד, לִיגְמַר ״עֲוֹנוֹ״ ״עֲוֹנוֹ״ מֵעָוֹן דִּשְׁמִיעַת הַקּוֹל, דִּכְתִיב: ״אִם לֹא יַגִּיד וְנָשָׂא עֲוֹנוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What difference is** there? **Didn't the school of Rabbi Yishmael teach** the following verbal analogy with regard to leprosy of houses? The verse states: **"And the priest shall return [*veshav*]** on the seventh day" (Leviticus 14:39), and another verse concerning the priest's visit seven days later states: **"And the priest shall come [*uva*]** and look" (Leviticus 14:44). **This returning** and **this coming** have the same meaning, and one can therefore derive by verbal analogy that the *halakha* that applies if the leprosy had spread at the conclusion of the first week applies if it had spread again by the end of the following week. All the more so should a less pronounced difference of one letter between *avon* and *avono* not prevent the teaching of a verbal analogy. **And furthermore, let one derive** a verbal analogy through the term **"his iniquity"** stated with regard to a guilt offering, and the term **"his iniquity" from** the verse concerning the sin offering brought for the **iniquity for hearing the voice,** i.e., the sin offering of one who takes a false oath that he does not have any information relevant to a matter when another requests that he testify about it, **as it is written: "If he does not utter it, then he shall bear his iniquity"** (Leviticus 5:1).

The *gemara* immediately presses: if the derivation rests on the shared word *avon* linking the *minchat kenaot* to the *chatat*, and thereby importing the *halacha* that *shelo lishmo* is fully invalid, then the same reasoning should extend to an *asham*. By an *asham*, too, the *Torah* uses the word *avon* — as Rashi notes, *velo yada ve-ashem ve-nasa avono*. So one should say: *asham yehei passul shelo lishmo, degamar avon avon mi-chatat*.

The *gemara* responds that the link is not precise: by the *chatat* and *minchat kenaot* the word is *avon*, while by the *asham* the word is *avono* — with a possessive suffix. You cannot draw a *gezeirah shavah* from *avon* to *avono*: *donin avon me-avon ve-ein donin avono me-avon*.

But this is quickly challenged. The *gemara* asks *mai nafka mina* — why should the imprecision matter? After all, it is taught in the school of Rabbi Ishmael that a *gezeirah shavah* need not use identical words: *ve-shav ha-kohen u-va ha-kohen* — "the *kohen* shall return" and "the *kohen* shall come" — are applied to the *metzora* and linked by *gezeirah shavah* even though *shiva* and *bia* are entirely different words. So *zeh hi shiva ve-zoh hi bia* — they mean essentially the same thing and the link holds. Ordinarily, one might say that a precise link is preferable when a choice exists, but here there is no choice between competing links: one should simply extend the *chatat*'s rule to the *minchat kenaot* and, by the same reasoning, to the *asham*.

The *gemara* adds a further point: *ve-od* — even if one insists on a precise match, the word *avono* does appear by the very *chatat* relevant here, the sliding-scale *korban*, as the *pasuk* reads *im lo yagid ve-nasa avono*. So if the *gezeirah shavah* is based on *avono-avono*, it would explain why a *minchat kenaot shelo lishmah* is invalid, yet it would still not explain why the same conclusion should not apply to the *asham*.

### Limited Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כִּי גָמְרִי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, לְמוֹתַר נְדָבָה הוּא דְּגָמְרִי. וְכִי תֵימָא: אֵין גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לְמֶחֱצָה – גַּלִּי רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי חַטָּאת, ״וְשָׁחַט אוֹתָהּ לְחַטָּאת״ – אוֹתָהּ לִשְׁמָהּ כְּשֵׁירָה, שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ פְּסוּלָה. אֲבָל כׇּל קָדָשִׁים, בֵּין לִשְׁמָן בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – כְּשֵׁרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that **when the verbal analogy was derived, it was derived** only with regard **to** the *halakha* that the **surplus** from the money designated for a meal offering of jealousy is used to purchase communal **gift** offerings, and not with regard to the *halakha* that a meal offering of jealousy from which a handful was removed not for its own sake is disqualified. **And if you would say** that there is a principle that **there is no partial verbal analogy,** that principle does not apply in this instance. As **the Merciful One revealed with regard to a sin offering** that the *halakha* of other offerings may not be derived from this case, as the verse states: **"And slaughter it for a sin offering"** (Leviticus 4:33). This verse indicates that **it,** i.e., a sin offering, when slaughtered **for its own sake** is **valid,** and when slaughtered **not for its own sake** is **disqualified. But all** other **sacrificial** animals, **whether** sacrificed **for their sake or not for their sake, are valid.**

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty: *ki gamar gezeirah shava le-mosar nedavah hu degamir*. The *gezeirah shavah* operates only to teach the *halacha* of surplus funds — that leftover money designated for a *minchat kenaot* goes toward an *olah*, just as leftover money for a *chatat* does. The *halacha* that *shelo lishmo* invalidates is not derived from the *gezeirah shavah* at all.

One might object: once you make a *gezeirah shavah*, you cannot apply it selectively. If the link of *avon-avon* connects the *minchat kenaot* to the *chatat*, then the full *chatat* rules — including the invalidity of *shelo lishmah* — must follow automatically. The *gemara* responds: that principle holds when the *chatat* rule in question must apply across the board. But here, the *Torah* itself includes a limiting word by the *chatat*: *gali rachmana gabei chatas ve-shachat osah le-chatat* — the *Torah* reveals that by a *chatat* it is slaughtered specifically as a *chatat*, and the word *osah* narrows this, teaching *osah lishmah kesheira, shelo lishmah pesulah*. That word *osah* contains within it the force that this stringency is limited to the *chatat* itself: *aval kol kodashim bein lishman bein shelo lishman ksherim* — all other *kodashim*, whether done with the right or wrong intent, remain *kasher*. Therefore, you cannot insist that the *gezeirah shavah* must carry the *shelo lishmah* stringency to the *minchat kenaot*, because you have an explicit limitation preventing that rule from automatically spreading.

The upshot: the *gezeirah shavah* cannot serve as the source for the *halacha* that *shelo lishmah* invalidates a *minchat kenaot*. And so the *gemara* returns to the fundamental question — *minchat choteh u-minchat kenaot de-pasul shelo lishman, me-nalan?* — where do we actually learn this?

### Word Hi Teaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת דִּפְסוּלִין שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן, מְנָלַן? חַטָּאת טַעְמָא מַאי, מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב בַּהּ ״הִיא״, הָכִי נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב בְּהוּ ״הִיא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if the Merciful One revealed that one cannot derive the *halakha* of other offerings that were sacrificed not for their sake from a sin offering, then **from where do we** derive the *halakha* **that the meal offering of a sinner and the meal offering of jealousy are disqualified** when a handful is removed from them **not for their sake?** The Gemara explains: With regard to **a sin offering, what is the reason** that it is disqualified when sacrificed not for its own sake? It is **because it is written with regard to** this offering: **"It,"** in a verse discussing the sin offering of the *Nasi*: "It is a sin offering" (Leviticus 4:24). This indicates that a sin offering is valid only when it is sacrificed for its own sake. **So too, it is written with regard to them,** i.e., the meal offering of a sinner and the meal offering of jealousy: **"It."** In the case of the meal offering of a sinner the verse states: "It is a sin offering" (Leviticus 5:11), and with regard to the meal offering of jealousy it is written: "It is a meal offering of jealousy" (Numbers 5:15).

The answer lies within the texts of the *minchat choteh* and *minchat kenaot* themselves — not from any link to the *chatat*. The *gemara* first establishes why a *chatat shelo lishmah* is fully invalid: *mishum de-chesiv bah hi* — because the *pasuk* uses the word *hi*, "it is." As was discussed in Zevachim, the *derasha* is: *hi lishmah kesheira, shelo lishmah pesulah* — "it" must be done for its own purpose; any deviation invalidates it. The word *hi* signals that this *korban* must maintain its identity at every stage; any derailing renders it fully invalid.

Crucially, the word *hi* appears not only by the *chatat* but within the *pesukim* of the *minchat choteh* and *minchat kenaot* themselves. The *pasuk* for the *minchat choteh* reads: *lo yasim aleha shemen velo yiten aleha levona ki chatat hi*. And for the *minchat kenaot* the *Torah* says: *ki minchat kenaot hi*. Since the word *hi* appears in each of these contexts — and it appears early in the process of the *korban* — the same *derasha* applies: any derailing at that early stage invalidates the offering outright. The invalidity of *shelo lishmah* for these two *minchot* is thus derived internally, from the word *hi* within their own *pesukim*, not from any link to the *chatat*.

### Asham Hu Differs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָשָׁם נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״הוּא״, הָהוּא ״הוּא״ לְאַחַר הַקְטָרַת אֵימוּרִין הוּא דִּכְתִיב. כִּדְתַנְיָא: אֲבָל אָשָׁם לֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ ״הוּא״ אֶלָּא לְאַחַר הַקְטָרַת אֵימוּרִין, הוּא עַצְמוֹ שֶׁלֹּא הוּקְטְרוּ אֵימוּרָיו כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: But if so, concerning **a guilt offering as well, it is written about** this offering: **"It,"** as the verse states: "It is a guilt offering" (Leviticus 7:5). Accordingly, a guilt offering should likewise be disqualified if it is slaughtered not for its own sake. The Gemara responds: **That** term **"it" is written with regard to** the stage **after the burning of** the **sacrificial portions [*eimurin*]** of a guilt offering, which are intended for burning upon the altar. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: One derives from the word "it" that if the offering was slaughtered not for its own sake it is disqualified only in the case of a sin offering. **But** concerning **a guilt offering, it is stated about** this offering: **"It** is a guilt offering," **only** with regard to the stage **after the burning of** the **sacrificial portions.** The *baraita* adds: One cannot derive that if these portions were burned not for the sake of a guilt offering then the offering is disqualified, since the guilt offering **itself** is **valid** if **its sacrificial portions were not burned** upon the altar at all.

The *gemara* immediately challenges: the same word appears by an *asham* — *asham hu asham l'Hashem* — so should the same *derasha* apply, rendering *asham shelo lishmo* invalid?

The *gemara* says no. The word *hu* by the *asham* is different in a critical way: *hahu hu, achar haktaras emurim hu de-chesiv* — it is written in the *Torah* after the *pasuk* describes the offering of the entrails, *ve-hiktir osam ha-kohen ha-mizbeacha isheh l'Hashem asham hu* — "the *kohen* shall offer them upon the altar as a fire-offering to Hashem; it is an *asham*." Since the word *hu* appears at a stage when it is no longer possible to invalidate the *asham* — the *gemara* and *braisa* confirm *aval asham lo ne'emar bo hu ela l'achar haktaras emurim, ve-hu atzmo shelo huktru imurav kasher* — even if the *eimurim* were never offered, the *asham* would still be *kasher* — the word *hu* at that late stage cannot be teaching that deviating intent invalidates. Since the stage at which *hu* is written is one where invalidation is already impossible, it cannot be carrying the *derasha* that *shelo lishmo* is *pasul*. By contrast, the word *hi* by the *chatat*, *minchat choteh*, and *minchat kenaot* appears earlier in the process, at a stage when invalidation is still possible, and therefore it bears the full force of that limiting *derasha*.

### Hu Sticky Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא ״הוּא״ לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אָשָׁם שֶׁנִּיתַּק לִרְעִיָּיה, וּשְׁחָטוֹ סְתָם – כָּשֵׁר לְשׁוּם עוֹלָה. נִיתַּק – אִין, לֹא נִיתַּק – לָא. אָמַר קְרָא: ״הוּא״, בַּהֲוָיָיתוֹ יְהֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **why do I** need the word **"it"** stated with regard to a guilt offering? The Gemara answers: It is necessary **for that which Rav Huna** says that **Rav says:** With regard to **a guilt offering** whose owner died or whose transgression was otherwise atoned for, and **that was** therefore **consigned** by the court **to grazing** until it develops a blemish so that it can be sold and the proceeds used to purchase a burnt offering, if, before it developed a blemish, someone **slaughtered it without specification** of its purpose, it is **fit** if it was sacrificed **as a burnt offering.** The Gemara infers: If **it was consigned** to grazing, **yes,** it is fit if it was sacrificed as a burnt offering if slaughtered. By inference, if **it was not consigned** to grazing, it is **not** fit. What is the reason for this? **The verse states: "It** is a guilt offering," indicating that **it shall** remain **as it is,** i.e., as a guilt offering, unless it is consigned by the court to another purpose.

If the word *hu* by the *asham* cannot be teaching the *shelo lishmo* rule, what does it teach? The *gemara* explains: *le-chide-Rav Huna amar Rav* — as Rav Huna said in the name of Rav: *asham she-nitak le-ra'ayah u-shechato stam kasher le-shem olah* — an *asham* that has been transferred to pasture (*nitak le-ra'ayah*) and was subsequently slaughtered without specific intent is valid as an *olah*.

The context is as follows. When an *asham* can no longer be brought — for instance, because the owner already brought another one — the animal must eventually be converted to an *olah*. The procedure is to send it out to pasture and wait for it to acquire a blemish, at which point it is redeemed and the funds used for an *olah*. If, however, one were to slaughter it in the *Beis Hamikdash* at that stage without specific intent — *stam* — it would function as an *olah*, since it was already set on that path. But this rule applies only *nitak in, lo nitak lo*: only once the animal has been actively sent out to pasture. If the *asham* had already lost its purpose but had not yet been transferred to pasture, and it was slaughtered, the slaughter would be invalid.

The principle: why does the animal not automatically become an *olah* from the moment it can no longer function as an *asham*, given that we know its eventual destination? The *gemara* says: *amar kra hu be-havyaso yehei* — the word *hu* teaches that the *asham* retains its original identity in a "sticky" sense. *Asham hu* means: it remains in its status. Because the *Torah* uses *hu* to underscore its ongoing identity, the animal cannot simply drift into a new status. Something active must be done — specifically, transferring it to pasture — to remove it from that *hu* identity. Only once that concrete step has been taken does the *shechita* count as an *olah*. This is the teaching carried by the word *hu* by an *asham*: not the *shelo lishmo* rule, but the persistence of the *asham*'s original identity until affirmatively changed.

## Minchat Ha'omer Rule

### Rav's Innovation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר שֶׁקְּמָצָהּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ – פְּסוּלָה, הוֹאִיל וּבָאת לְהַתִּיר וְלֹא הִתִּירָה. וְכֵן אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בַּאֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – פְּסוּלִין, הוֹאִיל וּבָאוּ לְהַכְשִׁיר וְלֹא הִכְשִׁירוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that all the meal offerings from which a handful was removed not for their sake are fit for sacrifice but they do not fulfill the owner's obligation. Concerning this, **Rav says:** With regard to **the *omer* meal offering,** i.e., the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan (see Leviticus 23:9–14), if the priest **removed a handful** from it **not for its own sake** it is **disqualified.** It is disqualified **since** an *omer* meal offering **came** for a specific purpose, namely, **to permit** the consumption of the new crop, **and** this meal offering **did not permit** the consumption of the new crop because its rites were performed not for its own sake. **And so you say with regard to the guilt offering of a nazirite** who became ritually impure, whose proper sacrifice enables the nazirite to restart his naziriteship afresh in purity, **and** so you say with regard to **the guilt offering of a leper,** whose proper sacrifice enables the leper to enter the Israelite camp and to partake of offerings of sanctity, **that** if one **slaughtered** these offerings **not for their sake, they are disqualified.** They are disqualified **since** their sacrifice **came to render** the nazirite and leper **fit, and** these guilt offerings **did not render** them **fit.**

Having established the *halachos* of *shelo lishmo* as enumerated in the *mishna*, the *gemara* now expands the list. Rav states: *minchat ha-omer she-kimtza shelo lishmo pesulah* — the *minchat ha-omer*, the *mincha* offered on the second day of Pesach to render the new grain permissible, is invalid if the *kemitza* was performed *shelo lishmo*, even though the *mishna* mentioned only the *minchat choteh* and *minchat kenaot* as exceptions.

Rav's reasoning is entirely logical rather than textual: *ho'il u-va'ah le-hatir ve-lo hitira* — since it came to permit and did not permit. The *minchat ha-omer* has a single defined function: to make the new grain permissible for consumption. Rav's reasoning is that once the offering is performed *shelo lishmo*, it detaches from its owners — or, in this case, from the *tzibbur* — and loses its meaningful identity as a *minchat ha-omer*. Since it is no longer functioning as a *minchat ha-omer*, it cannot render the new grain permissible. And if it is not doing that function, it is entirely without purpose — and therefore *pasul* in the full sense: one cannot eat the remainder, it has no standing as a valid *korban*.

This reasoning introduces a new principle: any *korban* that has a specific function to perform is fully invalid when *shelo lishmo* prevents it from performing that function. The obvious question this raises is: why limit the principle to the *minchat ha-omer*? A *chatat* also has a function — to achieve *kapparah* for a sin. An *asham* also has a function. If it is *shelo lishmo* and thus cannot achieve *kapparah*, should it not also be invalid by the same logic?

The *gemara* presses exactly this point. Rav himself extends the principle immediately: *ve-chen ata omer be-asham nazir u-ve-asham metzora* — the same applies to the *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*. A *nazir* who becomes *tamei* brings an *asham* in order to restart his *nezirus* through the process of *tahara*. A *metzora* brings an *asham* as part of the *tahara* process to change his status. Their job is *le-hachshir* — to change status — just as the *minchat ha-omer*'s job was to change the status of the new grain. Therefore, *shelo lishmam psulin ho'il u-va'u le-hachshir ve-lo hichshiru* — since they came to change status and did not do so, they are invalid.

Note that this extends *shelo lishmo* invalidity even to private *korbanos* — the *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* are private — as long as the *korban*'s defining purpose is a specific status change. But the lingering question is: if the logic is that a *korban* not performing its function is invalid, why does this not equally apply to an *asham* for theft (*asham gezeilt*) or *meilah*, which are also not performing their *kapparah* function when done *shelo lishmo*?

### Textual Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: כׇּל הַמְּנָחוֹת שֶׁנִּקְמְצוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן – כְּשֵׁירוֹת, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה, חוּץ מִמִּנְחַת חוֹטֵא וּמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת. וְאִם אִיתָא, לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי: ״חוּץ מִמִּנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We learned** in the mishna that **all the meal offerings** from **which a handful was removed not for their sake are fit** for sacrifice **but they did not satisfy the obligation of the owner,** except for the meal offering of a sinner and the meal offering of jealousy. **And if it is so** that an *omer* meal offering from which a handful was removed not for its own sake is disqualified, then **let** the mishna **also teach: Except for the *omer* meal offering.**

Before addressing that conceptual question, the *gemara* first asks a textual one: the *mishna* at the beginning of Menachot lists only the *minchat choteh* and *minchat kenaot* as exceptions to the general rule that *shelo lishmo* is *kasher*. If Rav's rule is correct, why does the *mishna* not mention the *minchat ha-omer* as an additional exception?

### Private vs. Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָתָנֵי בָּאָה יָחִיד, בָּאָה צִבּוּר לָא קָתָנֵי; כִּי תָנֵי בָּאָה בִּגְלַל עַצְמָהּ, בָּאָה בִּגְלַל זֶבַח – לָא קָתָנֵי. כִּי קָתָנֵי הָנָךְ שֶׁאֵין קָבוּעַ לָהֶן זְמַן, הָא דְּקָבוּעַ לָהּ זְמַן – לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **When** the mishna **teaches** this *halakha*, it teaches it only with regard to meal offerings that **come** on behalf of **an individual.** The mishna **does not teach** the *halakha* with regard to those meal offerings that **come** on behalf of the **community.** Additionally, **when** the mishna **teaches** this *halakha*, it is only with regard to a meal offering that **comes on account of itself,** i.e., as an independent offering. The mishna **does not teach** the *halakha* with regard to a meal offering that **comes on account of,** i.e., together with, **a slaughtered offering,** e.g., the *omer* meal offering, which is brought along with two sheep. The Gemara adds: Furthermore, **when** the mishna **teaches** this *halakha*, it is only with regard to **those** meal offerings **whose time is not set,** i.e., they may be sacrificed on any date. The mishna **does not teach** the *halakha* with regard to **this** *omer* meal offering, **whose time** for offering **is set** on the sixteenth of Nisan.

The *gemara* offers three answers to reconcile Rav's ruling with the *mishna*'s apparent silence. First: *ki katani be-yachid, be-tzibur lo katani* — the *mishna* is dealing with private *korbanos* and therefore did not discuss the *minchat ha-omer*, which is a public *korban*. Second: *ba'ah biglal atzma, ba'ah biglal zevach lo katani* — the *mishna* addresses *minchot* that come on their own; the *minchat ha-omer* comes together with an animal *korban* and was therefore excluded from the *mishna*'s scope. Third: *she-ein kavua lahen zman, ha-kavua lahen zman lo katani* — the *mishna* discusses *minchot* that may be brought at any time, while the *minchat ha-omer* comes at a fixed time tied to the *mo'ed* cycle. In each of these readings, the *mishna*'s silence does not disprove Rav's ruling; the *mishna* simply was not dealing with that category of case and therefore did not bother to enumerate it as an exception.

## Status-Change Ashamos

### Textual Gap Extended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כְּשֵׁרִין, אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָלוּ לַבְּעָלִים לְשׁוּם חוֹבָה, חוּץ מִפֶּסַח וְחַטָּאת. וְאִם אִיתָא – לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי: חוּץ מֵאֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע, דְּבָאוּ לְהַכְשִׁיר וְלֹא הִכְשִׁירוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **We learned** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 2a): **All slaughtered offerings that one slaughtered not for their sake are fit, but they did not satisfy the obligation of the owner.** This is the *halakha* with regard to all offerings **except for the Paschal offering and the sin offering,** which are disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake. **And if it is so** that the *halakha* is in accordance with the statement of Rav, **let** the mishna **also teach: Except for the guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper, since they came to render** one **fit and they did not render** one **fit.**

A parallel textual question is then raised about the *asham nazir* and *asham metzora*. The *gemara* cites the first *mishna* of Zevachim: *kol ha-zvachim she-shachtam shelo lishmam ksherim ela she-lo alu le-va'alim le-shem chova* — all animal *korbanos* done *shelo lishmo* are *kasher* but the owners do not receive credit, *chutz mi-pesach ve-chatat* — except for the *pesach* and the *chatat*. If the *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* are also *pasul shelo lishmo*, why were they not listed there as additional exceptions?

### Asham Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא אֲשַׁם גְּזֵילוֹת וַאֲשַׁם מְעִילוֹת, דִּלְכַפָּרָה אָתוּ – לָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Since there** are other guilt offerings, i.e., **the guilt offering for robbery,** which one brings for taking a false oath denying an accusation of robbery, **and the guilt offering** brought **for misuse** of consecrated property, **that come for atonement,** which do not render one fit and are fit for sacrifice if they were slaughtered not for their sake, the *tanna* of the mishna could **not** state the *halakha* with regard to guilt offerings in **an absolute** manner, and therefore he refrains from mentioning guilt offerings at all.

Here, the answers given for the *minchat ha-omer* do not apply. The *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* are private, they come on their own, and they come at no fixed time. The *gemara* therefore gives a different answer: *kivan de-ika asham gezeilt ve-asham meilot de-le-chapara atu, lo psika lei* — since there exist *ashamos* that are for *kapparah* — the *asham gezeilt* and *asham meilah* — which are perfectly valid even when done *shelo lishmo*, the category of *asham* is not monolithic. The rule that *shelo lishmo* invalidates applies only to some *ashamos* and not others. Because the *mishna* in Zevachim was speaking in terms of entire categories — *pesach* as a category is always invalid *shelo lishmo*, *chatat* as a category is always invalid *shelo lishmo* — it could not cleanly state that *asham* is an exception, since some *ashamos* are and some are not. The category would have had to be subdivided, which was outside the *mishna*'s framework. Therefore the *mishna* omitted *asham* entirely and let it fall under the general rule, without flagging the sub-category distinction.

### Status vs. Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא אֲשַׁם נָזִיר וַאֲשַׁם מְצוֹרָע, דְּבָאוּ לְהַכְשִׁיר וְלֹא הִכְשִׁירוּ? הָנֵי נָמֵי בָּאוּ לְכַפָּרָה וְלֹא כִּפְּרוּ! אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: מָצִינוּ שֶׁחִלֵּק הַכָּתוּב בֵּין מְכַפְּרִים וּבֵין מַכְשִׁירִין, מְכַפְּרִין אִית בְּהוּ דְּאָתוּ לְאַחַר מִיתָה, מַכְשִׁירִין לֵית בְּהוּ דְּאָתוּ לְאַחַר מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **the guilt offering of a nazirite and the guilt offering of a leper?** Why are they disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake **since they came to render fit and they did not render fit? These,** i.e., the guilt offerings for robbery and for misuse of consecrated property, should **likewise** be disqualified when slaughtered not for their sake, since they **came for atonement and they did not atone.** **Rabbi Yirmeya said** in response: **We find that the Torah differentiates between** those guilt offerings that **atone and** those that **render fit,** and the *halakha* is more stringent with regard to those that render fit. Rabbi Yirmeya elaborates: With regard to those guilt offerings that **atone, there are among them** offerings **that come after death,** i.e., they are sacrificed after the death of their owners, whereas with regard to those that **render fit, there are none among them that come after death.**

Now the *gemara* arrives at the underlying conceptual question it has been building toward: *mai shana asham nazir ve-asham metzora de-va'u le-hachshir ve-lo hichshiru?* — what distinguishes the *asham nazir* and *asham metzora* which, since they came to change status and did not, are *pasul*? The follow-up: *hani nami ba'u le-chaper ve-lo kiper* — by the other *ashamos*, they came to atone and they did not atone; by your logic, they too should be *pasul*.

Rabbi Yirmiyah's answer is: *matzinu she-chilek ha-katuv bein mechaperim u-vein machshirin* — we find that the *Torah* itself distinguishes between *korbanos* that effect *kapparah* and those that effect *hechsher*. The distinction is demonstrated by the following: there are cases where *korbanos* related to *kapparah* can be brought after the owner dies (*mechaperim atis behu de-asu le-achar misa*), while one never finds a *korban* that is exclusively about status-change being brought after the owner dies (*machshirin leis behu le-achar misa*). This asymmetry in the *Torah*'s treatment of the two categories proves that the *Torah* treats them differently in a fundamental way.

### Yoledes Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהֵבִיאָה חַטָּאתָהּ וּמֵתָה – יָבִיאוּ יוֹרְשִׁין עוֹלָתָהּ, עוֹלָתָהּ וּמֵתָה – לֹא יָבִיאוּ יוֹרְשִׁין חַטָּאתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Kinnim* 2:5): With regard to **a woman** after childbirth **who brought her sin offering** for her ritual purification **and died, the heirs shall bring her burnt offering,** which comes to atone. If she set aside **her burnt offering and died, the heirs shall not bring her sin offering,** as it comes to render her fit to partake of offerings.

The *mishna* cited to demonstrate this is the case of a *yoledes* — a woman who gave birth. A *yoledes* is obligated to bring both a *chatat* and an *olah*. The *mishna* teaches: if she already brought her *chatat* and then died, the heirs bring her *olah*. If she brought her *olah* and then died, the heirs do not bring her *chatat*.

The *gemara* uses this case to reveal a conceptual point. A *yoledes*'s *chatat* is not primarily a sin-offering in the usual sense — the *yoledes* has not committed a sin in the ordinary way. Rather, her *chatat* is *le-hachshir*: it is part of the process by which she exits her state of *tumah* and becomes permitted to eat *korbanos*. It is a status-change offering. And consistent with Rabbi Yirmiyah's principle, you do not bring it after she dies — since the status-change it was meant to achieve is no longer relevant.

Her *olah*, by contrast, is brought after she dies. And since we have established that her *chatat* is *le-hachshir*, we infer that her *olah* is *le-chaper* — it carries the character of atonement. As Rashi notes, *echad le-chatat ve-echad le-olah* — one is for the *chatat* function and one is for the *olah* function, and since one is *le-hachshir*, the other is inferred to be *le-chaper*. The fact that the *olah* is brought even after the woman's death — when its stated purpose of *kapparah* can no longer be achieved for her — demonstrates that a *korban* whose identity is *le-chaper* can be brought even when it is not fulfilling its function.

This is Rabbi Yirmiyah's proof. A *korban* fundamentally about *kapparah* is, at its core, an offering to God; even if it cannot achieve *kapparah* in the immediate case — because the person is dead, or because it is *shelo lishmo* — it retains its identity as an offering and can therefore still be brought. A *korban* fundamentally about status-change, however, is essentially a ritual mechanism whose entire purpose is to change a status. Once it cannot serve that function, there is nothing left of its identity. One might tentatively understand it this way: the *le-chaper korban* begins with the act of offering and achieves *kapparah* through it, whereas the *le-hachshir korban* is primarily defined by its functional outcome, and without that outcome it has no remaining standing.

## Status After Death

### Nazir Funds Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: הַמַּפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת לִנְזִירוּתוֹ – לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרְאוּיִים לָבֹא כּוּלָּן שְׁלָמִים. מֵת וְהָיוּ לוֹ מָעוֹת סְתוּמִין – יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה; מְפוֹרָשִׁין: דְּמֵי חַטָּאת – יוֹלִיךְ לְיָם הַמֶּלַח, לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין; דְּמֵי עוֹלָה – יָבִיא בָּהֶן עוֹלָה, וּמוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן. דְּמֵי שְׁלָמִים – יָבִיא בָּהֶן שְׁלָמִים, וְנֶאֱכָלִין לְיוֹם אֶחָד, וְאֵין טְעוּנִין לֶחֶם. וְהָא עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים דְּנָזִיר דְּמַכְשִׁירִים נִינְהוּ, וְקָא אָתוּ לְאַחַר מִיתָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 11a): In the case of **one who separates funds for** the offerings of **his naziriteship,** i.e., his sin offering, burnt offering, and peace offering, a person **may not derive benefit** from them *ab initio*, **but** if one benefited from them after the fact, he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, i.e., he is not required to bring a guilt offering and is not obligated to repay the principal and an additional fifth. This is **because** the coins **are all fit for bringing peace offerings,** and there is no misuse of consecrated property with regard to funds fit for a peace offering. The mishna continues: If the nazirite **died and he had undesignated funds** that he set aside to pay for his nazirite offerings without specifying how much money should be allocated toward each offering, **they will be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings. If he left behind **allocated** funds, then with regard to **the money for a sin offering,** one must **take** it and cast it **into the Dead Sea; one may not benefit** from it *ab initio*, **but** if one benefited from it after the fact, he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. With regard to **the money for a burnt offering, one brings with it a burnt offering, and** one is liable for **misusing them.** The mishna concludes: With **the money for a peace offering, one brings a peace offering with it, and** these offerings **are eaten for one day,** like the peace offering brought by a nazirite, **and do not require** the **loaves** that are normally bought with the peace offering of a nazirite. Rabbi Yehuda explains his question: **But** the **burnt offering and peace offering of a nazirite are** offerings **that render** the nazirite **fit** to drink wine, **and** yet **they come after death.**

The *gemara* challenges Rabbi Yirmiyah's principle from a *mishna* in the laws of *nezirus*. If a *nazir* sets aside funds for his three *korbanos* — *chatat*, *olah*, and *shelamim* — without designating which pile is for which, the *halacha* is *lo nehenin ve-lo mo'alin*: one may not derive personal benefit from the funds, but there is no transgression of *meilah* in doing so. The reason: *mipnei she-re'uyin lavo kullan shelamim* — since it is possible that all the funds might go toward *shelamim* (the *nazir* could use all the money for *shelamim* and bring the *chatat* and *olah* from other funds), and by *shelamim* the owner eats the meat, so there is only *meilah* on the *eimurim* after they are sacrificed, not on the funds in advance. Since the identity of the funds has not been solidified, there is no *meilah*.

Now, if this *nazir* dies, *yipplu le-nedava* — the undesignated funds are contributed to the communal collection box for *olos*. But if the *nazir* had explicitly designated his funds — this pile for a *chatat*, this for an *olah*, this for a *shelamim* — and then died, each pile is treated differently. The funds designated for a *chatat* go to the sea — *demei chatat yelechu le-yam ha-melach* — destroyed, with no *meilah* but no permitted use, analogous to the *chatat ha-meisa* rule that an animal *chatat* whose owner dies must die. No benefit may be derived, but there is no *meilah* since the money is no longer going toward a sacred purpose. The *olah* funds are used to bring an *olah*, with *meilah* applying. The *shelamim* funds are used to bring a *shelamim*, eaten for one day rather than two (reflecting the *shelamim* of a *nazir*), and without the accompanying bread, since the loaves require an actual living *nazir* to bring them.

### Olah Shelamim Alive

The critical point: when the *nazir* has died and his heirs bring his *olah* and *shelamim*, those *korbanos* were part of the process that was meant to complete his *nezirus* and change his status. The *olah* and *shelamim* of a *nazir* at the conclusion of his *nezirus* are *machshirin* — they are the *korbanos* that, together with the *chatat*, complete the status change. Yet they are brought even after the *nazir*'s death, when they cannot possibly change anyone's status.

### Challenge to Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: מַכְשִׁירִין נָמֵי, מִי לֵית בְּהוּ דְּאָתוּ לְאַחַר מִיתָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, objects to this:** With regard to offerings that **render fit as well, are there not among them** offerings **that come after death?**

This presents a direct challenge to Rabbi Yirmiyah's principle. If *machshirin* — status-change *korbanos* — cannot be brought after death, why are the *olah* and *shelamim* of a *nazir* brought after his death? They are precisely the *korbanos* that effect the *hechsher* of his completed *nezirus*.

## Sole Function Principle

### Rav Pappa Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכִי קָא אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לֹא מָצִינוּ הֶכְשֵׁר קָבוּעַ דְּבָא לְאַחַר מִיתָה, וּדְנָזִיר הֶכְשֵׁר שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָבוּעַ הוּא, דְּאָמַר מָר: גִּילַּח עַל אַחַת מִשְּׁלׇשְׁתָּן יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** in response that **this is what Rabbi Yirmeya is saying: We do not find** an instance of **a fixed** manner of **rendering fit that comes after death,** i.e., there is no instance where the only offering that will render one fit to act in a manner previously prohibited to him may be sacrificed after death. **And** the offerings **of a nazirite** are examples of a means of **rendering fit that is not fixed,** **as the Master said** about the nazirite (*Nazir* 45a): And if he **shaved after** the sacrifice of any **one of the three of them,** i.e., after sacrificing his sin offering, burnt offering, or peace offering, **he has fulfilled** his obligation after the fact, i.e., he has successfully completed his term of naziriteship. Accordingly, there is no specific offering that renders him fit.

Rav Pappa resolves the difficulty by clarifying what Rabbi Yirmiyah actually meant to say: *hachi ka'amar Rabbi Yirmiyah, lo matzinu hechsher kavua de-va achar misa* — we have never found a *hechsher kavua* — a *korban* that is the sole and fixed vehicle for status change — that is brought after death. Rabbi Yirmiyah's principle was never that all *machshirin* cannot be brought after death, but only those that are the exclusive means of effecting the status change.

### Sole vs. Multiple

The distinction Rav Pappa draws is precise. In the cases Rav and Rabbi Yirmiyah discussed — the *asham nazir*, the *asham metzora*, and the *minchat ha-omer* — that specific *korban* is the only thing that effects the relevant status change. The entire *hechsher* rests on that single offering. Its entire identity is therefore constituted by that function, and when it cannot fulfill the function, it has no identity remaining and is *pasul*. But by the *nazir* at the end of his *nezirus* with his three *korbanos*, no single one of them is indispensable to the status change.

### Any Korban Works

The *gemara* supports this by citing the rule: *gile'ach al achat mi-shalosh yatza* — if a *nazir* brought only one of his three *korbanos* and then performed the shaving, he has fulfilled the essential requirement and his status changes. He remains obligated in the other two *korbanos*, but any one of the three can independently change his status. Therefore, none of the three individually bears the exclusive identity of being the *hechsher*. Since the *olah*, for instance, does not have to be the one that changes the status — the *chatat* or *shelamim* might serve that role instead — its identity is not wholly constituted by *hechsher*. It can function independently of the status-change goal. And therefore it remains valid and is brought even after the *nazir*'s death, even though it cannot now accomplish the status change, because that never had to be its identity in the first place.

This resolves the challenge elegantly. The *asham nazir*, the *asham metzora*, and the *minchat ha-omer* are each the sole possible vehicle for their respective status change. When *shelo lishmo* prevents them from functioning, they lose their entire identity and are *pasul*. The *olah* and *shelamim* of a *nazir* completing his *nezirus*, by contrast, are each one of three possible *machshirin*, none of which is individually indispensable. Their identity is not locked entirely into the *hechsher* function, and they therefore remain valid even when that function cannot be performed.
